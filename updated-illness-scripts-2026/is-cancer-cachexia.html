Cancer Cachexia
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Cancer Cachexia"][/sc]
  • Cancer cachexia is a multifactorial syndrome characterised by severe weight loss, muscle wasting, and loss of adipose tissue despite adequate caloric intake.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise cancer features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.

  • Risk factors: Risk of cancer cachexia rises with relevant predisposition, comorbidity, and exposure profile.
  • Higher-risk or missed groups: Cancer Cachexia may be missed when presentations are atypical or comorbidity obscures key features.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Cancer Cachexia"][/sc]
  • Cancer cachexia is driven by a complex interaction between tumour-derived factors and host responses. Tumours secrete pro-inflammatory cytokines (e.g., TNF-α, IL-6) and proteolysis-inducing factors, leading to systemic inflammation and breakdown of skeletal muscle and adipose tissue. Increased muscle protein degradation via the ubiquitin-proteasome pathway and reduced protein synthesis result in muscle wasting. Simultaneously, fat loss occurs due to lipolysis driven by catecholamines and other tumour factors. These metabolic abnormalities are compounded by anorexia, insulin resistance, and alterations in energy expenditure.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Cancer Cachexia"][/sc]
  • Cancer cachexia typically develops over months and is progressive. Early stages may involve subtle weight loss and fatigue, which can progress to severe muscle wasting, profound weakness, and functional decline in advanced stages. It often parallels the progression of the underlying malignancy.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Cancer Cachexia"][/sc]
  • Ask about unintentional weight loss - Most common and defining symptom, occurring due to increased catabolism of fat and muscle driven by systemic inflammation.
  • Ask about muscle weakness - Results from loss of muscle mass and decreased protein synthesis, leading to functional decline.
  • Ask about loss of appetite (anorexia) - Driven by tumour-induced changes in metabolic signalling and inflammatory cytokines that suppress appetite.
  • Ask about fatigue - Caused by muscle wasting, metabolic dysregulation, and systemic inflammation, leading to reduced energy availability.
  • Ask about early satiety - Occurs due to impaired gastric emptying and reduced gastrointestinal motility associated with cancer and systemic inflammation.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Cancer Cachexia"][/sc]
  • Check muscle wasting - Visible loss of muscle mass, especially in the temporal, clavicular, and thigh regions, due to increased proteolysis and reduced protein synthesis.
  • Check weight loss - Significant, rapid weight loss despite normal or even increased caloric intake, caused by tumour-induced catabolic processes.
  • Check pallor - May result from underlying malignancy-related anaemia or overall poor nutritional status.
  • Check tachycardia - Often seen as a result of anaemia or systemic inflammation.
  • Check peripheral oedema - Can occur due to hypoalbuminaemia from malnutrition or the cancer’s effects on liver function.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Cancer Cachexia"][/sc]
  • Rapid, unexplained weight loss - Suggests advanced cancer with systemic involvement and warrants urgent evaluation.
  • Severe muscle wasting - Can indicate late-stage cachexia and is associated with poor prognosis.
  • Anorexia and early satiety - May signify advanced cachexia or gastrointestinal involvement of the malignancy.
  • Refractory fatigue - Could indicate systemic progression or poor nutritional status, leading to severe functional decline.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Cancer Cachexia"][/sc]
  • Absence of weight loss - Some patients may maintain weight due to oedema or tumour mass, masking the underlying muscle wasting.
  • Normoalbuminaemia - Patients with cachexia may have normal serum albumin levels despite significant malnutrition, making diagnosis more challenging.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features or comorbidity can mask the underlying diagnosis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Cancer Cachexia"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Serum albumin - Often low due to malnutrition or inflammation, reflecting the severity of the catabolic state; C-reactive protein (CRP) .
  • Targeted and monitoring: Full blood count (FBC) - May show anaemia, reflecting underlying malignancy, inflammation, or malnutrition; CT scan - Can demonstrate muscle wasting.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Cancer Cachexia"][/sc]
  • An elderly male with advanced lung cancer presenting with progressive weight loss, muscle wasting, fatigue, and anorexia over 6 months, refractory to nutritional support.
  • Adult with symptom pattern and objective findings suggestive of cancer cachexia, requiring focused confirmation and risk stratification.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Cancer Cachexia"][/sc]
  • Chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD) - Can cause muscle wasting and weight loss, but usually without systemic inflammation and more.
  • Congestive heart failure (CHF) - Differentiate from cancer cachexia using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.
  • Chronic liver disease - Can lead to muscle wasting and anorexia, but more often presents with jaundice, ascites.
  • Alternative serious diagnosis - Distinguishing history, examination, and targeted investigations do not support cancer cachexia.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Cancer Cachexia"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Assess physiological stability and urgent complications at presentation, and initiate early stabilisation where required.
  • Core care: Deliver condition-specific treatment for cancer cachexia, address underlying drivers, and involve appropriate specialist support.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Reassess symptom trajectory, objective markers, and treatment response over time to detect complications early.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Worsening symptoms, physiological deterioration, or failure to improve should trigger urgent reassessment for severe or alternative pathology in cancer cachexia.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Cancer Cachexia"][/sc]
  • Severe functional impairment - Progressive muscle wasting leads to weakness and immobility, significantly reducing quality of life.
  • Increased mortality - Cachexia is a direct cause of death in 20-30% of cancer patients.
  • Recurrent infections - Immunosuppression and malnutrition increase the risk of infections, complicating the patient's overall prognosis.
