Disuse Atrophy
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Disuse Atrophy"][/sc]
  • Disuse atrophy refers to the gradual loss of muscle mass and strength due to prolonged inactivity or reduced use of skeletal muscles.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise disuse features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.

  • Higher-risk or missed groups: Disuse Atrophy may be missed when presentation is atypical or obscured by comorbidity.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Disuse Atrophy"][/sc]
  • Disuse atrophy occurs when skeletal muscles are not subjected to regular contraction or loading. Lack of use leads to a reduction in muscle protein synthesis and an increase in muscle protein degradation. Key mechanisms include:
  • Decreased mechanical load and neural stimulation lead to the downregulation of pathways involved in muscle growth, such as the insulin-like growth factor (IGF-1) signalling pathway.
  • Activation of the ubiquitin-proteasome pathway increases protein degradation.
  • Over time, muscle fibres shrink, particularly type I (slow-twitch) fibres, resulting in reduced muscle mass and strength.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Disuse Atrophy"][/sc]
  • Acute Phase: Disuse atrophy can begin within days of immobility. Noticeable muscle loss may occur within two weeks.
  • Subacute Phase: Over 4-6 weeks, there is significant reduction in muscle size and strength, particularly in bedridden patients.
  • Chronic Phase: After 3-6 months of disuse, the muscle atrophy may become severe, and recovery requires prolonged rehabilitation.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Disuse Atrophy"][/sc]
  • Ask about muscle Weakness - The most common symptom, due to reduced muscle fibre size and loss of contractile proteins.
  • Ask about reduced Muscle Mass - Patients often notice visible muscle wasting in affected limbs or areas, due to loss of muscle bulk.
  • Ask about fatigue - Due to reduced muscle endurance, even light physical activities may become tiring.
  • Ask about joint Stiffness - Immobility often leads to stiffness in joints due to a lack of movement, further limiting mobility.
  • Ask about immobility - In severe cases, the affected muscles are unable to support movement or weight-bearing activities.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Disuse Atrophy"][/sc]
  • Check muscle Wasting - Visible reduction in muscle bulk, especially in unused or immobilised limbs.
  • Check decreased Muscle Tone - Muscles may feel flaccid or soft upon palpation due to loss of contractile proteins.
  • Check weakness on Examination - Reduced strength on manual muscle testing (graded using MRC scale).
  • Check impaired Mobility - Difficulty or inability to perform simple movements or maintain posture due to muscle weakness.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Disuse Atrophy"][/sc]
  • Rapid Muscle Wasting - Can indicate an underlying neuromuscular disorder or malignancy rather than simple disuse atrophy.
  • Loss of Reflexes - This could suggest a neurological cause for atrophy, such as motor neuron disease or peripheral neuropathy.
  • Severe Pain - Unexplained or severe pain alongside atrophy may point to a different cause, such as complex regional pain syndrome (CRPS) or osteomyelitis.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Disuse Atrophy"][/sc]
  • Asymptomatic Early Stages - In some cases, muscle atrophy may occur without noticeable symptoms until significant weakness develops.
  • Disuse Atrophy in Bedridden Elderly Patients - Often attributed to "old age" rather than a reversible condition, leading to under-treatment.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask disuse atrophy.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Disuse Atrophy"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Muscle Strength Testing - Manual muscle testing using the Medical Research Council (MRC) scale to assess degree of weakness; Electromyography (EMG).
  • Targeted and monitoring: Targeted investigations to confirm diagnosis, assess complications, and monitor response in disuse atrophy.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Disuse Atrophy"][/sc]
  • Sarcopenia - Similar age-related loss of muscle mass, but with more diffuse and gradual progression compared.
  • Motor Neurone Disease - Presents with muscle atrophy and weakness, but typically involves fasciculations, hyperreflexia, and a progressive.
  • Myopathy - Weakness is more proximal, often associated with inflammatory markers or muscle enzymes like creatine.
  • Peripheral Neuropathy - Accompanied by sensory loss, reflex changes, and distal muscle wasting.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Disuse Atrophy"][/sc]
  • Contractures: Prolonged disuse can lead to irreversible joint contractures due to shortening of muscles and tendons.
  • Bone Loss: Chronic immobility can result in reduced bone density and increased risk of fractures (disuse osteoporosis).
  • Pressure Ulcers: In bed-bound patients, muscle atrophy can exacerbate the risk of pressure sores due to reduced cushioning over bony prominences.
