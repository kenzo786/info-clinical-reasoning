Childhood Anaemia
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Childhood Anaemia"][/sc]
  • Childhood anaemia is a condition characterised by a decrease in the number of red blood cells (RBCs) or haemoglobin concentration below the normal range for the child’s age and sex.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise childhood features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.

Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Childhood Anaemia"][/sc]
  • Anaemia in children can arise from three main mechanisms:
    • Decreased RBC production: This can be due to nutritional deficiencies (e.g., iron, vitamin B12, folate), bone marrow suppression, or chronic disease.
    • Increased RBC destruction: Haemolytic anaemias such as sickle cell disease or G6PD deficiency result in premature destruction of red blood cells.
    • Blood loss: Acute or chronic blood loss, such as gastrointestinal bleeding or heavy menstrual periods in adolescent girls, can lead to anaemia.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Childhood Anaemia"][/sc]
  • Acute anaemia: Sudden onset due to significant blood loss or acute haemolysis can lead to rapid development of symptoms.
  • Chronic anaemia: More gradual onset, often due to nutritional deficiencies or chronic disease, with subtle initial symptoms that may progress over weeks to months.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Childhood Anaemia"][/sc]
  • Ask about fatigue - Due to reduced oxygen delivery to tissues, causing decreased energy levels.
  • Ask about pallor - Reduced RBC count leads to less haemoglobin, making the skin and mucous membranes appear pale.
  • Ask about shortness of breath - The body compensates for hypoxia by increasing respiratory effort.
  • Ask about dizziness - Decreased oxygen to the brain can cause light-headedness or fainting.
  • Ask about difficulty concentrating - Hypoxia can impair cognitive function in school-aged children.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Childhood Anaemia"][/sc]
  • Check pallor of skin and conjunctiva - Due to decreased haemoglobin and reduced blood flow to peripheral tissues.
  • Check tachycardia - The heart compensates for low oxygen-carrying capacity by increasing cardiac output.
  • Check koilonychia (spoon-shaped nails) - A sign of iron deficiency, which is a common cause of anaemia.
  • Check systolic murmur - Resulting from increased blood flow velocity through the heart in response to anaemia.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Childhood Anaemia"][/sc]
  • Severe fatigue and weakness - May indicate significant anaemia requiring urgent intervention.
  • Chest pain or dyspnoea at rest - Suggests the heart is struggling to compensate for hypoxia and may signal heart failure or severe anaemia.
  • Jaundice - Could indicate haemolytic anaemia where red blood cells are being destroyed rapidly.
  • Haematuria - Suggests underlying haemolytic or renal causes of anaemia, requiring urgent evaluation.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Childhood Anaemia"][/sc]
  • Failure to thrive - In infants, chronic anaemia may present as poor weight gain and developmental delay.
  • Pica - Unusual cravings for non-food substances like dirt or ice, often seen in iron deficiency anaemia.
  • Behavioural problems - School-aged children may present with irritability or attention problems.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features or comorbidity can mask the underlying diagnosis.

  • A 4-year-old girl presents with 3 months of increasing fatigue, pallor, and poor appetite. Physical examination reveals conjunctival pallor and a systolic murmur. FBC shows microcytic anaemia with low ferritin.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Childhood Anaemia"][/sc]
  • Thalassaemia - Presents with microcytic anaemia, but iron studies are normal. Haemoglobin electrophoresis confirms the diagnosis.
  • Sickle Cell Disease - May present with haemolytic anaemia, jaundice, and pain crises. Blood smear shows sickled cells.
  • Anaemia of chronic disease - Usually normocytic, associated with chronic inflammatory conditions, normal or increased ferritin, and reduced TIBC.

Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Childhood Anaemia"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Assess physiological stability and urgent complications at presentation, and initiate early stabilisation where required.
  • Core care: Deliver condition-specific treatment for childhood anaemia, address underlying drivers, and involve appropriate specialist support.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Reassess symptom trajectory, objective markers, and treatment response over time to detect complications early.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Worsening symptoms, physiological deterioration, or failure to improve should trigger urgent reassessment for severe or alternative pathology in childhood anaemia.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Childhood Anaemia"][/sc]
  • Heart failure - Severe anaemia can lead to high-output heart failure due to the increased cardiac workload.
  • Developmental delay - Chronic anaemia in early childhood can impair cognitive and physical development.
  • Recurrent infections - Children with severe anaemia may have impaired immune function, increasing susceptibility to infections.
