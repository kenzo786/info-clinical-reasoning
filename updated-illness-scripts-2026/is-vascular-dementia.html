Vascular Dementia
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Vascular Dementia"][/sc]
  • Vascular dementia is a form of dementia caused by chronic, reduced blood flow to the brain, often due to small vessel disease or multiple strokes.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise vascular features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.

  • Higher-risk or missed groups: Vascular Dementia may be missed when presentation is atypical or obscured by comorbidity.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Vascular Dementia"][/sc]
  • Vascular dementia arises from cerebrovascular damage, such as small or large vessel strokes, chronic ischaemia, or haemorrhages. Key mechanisms include:
  • Ischaemic Damage: Reduced blood flow from blocked or narrowed vessels causes neuron death, leading to areas of brain infarction.
  • Small Vessel Disease: Chronic hypertension and diabetes can cause arteriosclerosis of small vessels, impacting white matter and deep brain structures.
  • Haemorrhagic Lesions: Less common, but microbleeds in the brain due to amyloid angiopathy or hypertensive bleeding can contribute to vascular dementia.
  • These processes result in cognitive deficits due to impaired communication between brain regions and neuronal loss.
    • Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Vascular Dementia"][/sc]
    • Initial Phase: May begin with mild cognitive impairment (MCI), often asymptomatic or with subtle issues in planning or concentration.
    • Progression: Vascular dementia can progress in a stepwise manner, with noticeable declines following cerebrovascular events (e.g., small strokes).
    • Late Phase: Severe cognitive and physical disability, with impaired daily functioning, memory loss, and behavioural changes.
    Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Vascular Dementia"][/sc]
    • Ask about executive Dysfunction: Difficulty with planning, organisation, and multitasking, due to frontal lobe and white matter involvement.
    • Ask about slowed Thinking: Reduced processing speed due to disrupted brain networks.
    • Ask about short-Term Memory Loss: Less common initially but worsens over time, reflecting hippocampal and cortical ischaemic damage.
    • Ask about behavioural Changes: Apathy, irritability, or depression due to frontal-subcortical circuit disruption.
    • Ask about gait Abnormalities: Small vessel disease often affects basal ganglia, leading to slower, shuffling gait.
    Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Vascular Dementia"][/sc]
    • Check bradyphrenia (Slowness of Thought): Often noticeable in early stages, due to frontal and white matter involvement.
    • Check gait Disturbances: Unsteady, shuffling gait resulting from basal ganglia damage and white matter ischaemia.
    • Check focal Neurological Deficits: Symptoms such as hemiparesis or dysarthria if past strokes affected motor pathways.
    Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Vascular Dementia"][/sc]
    • Rapid Cognitive Decline: May indicate concurrent Alzheimer’s pathology or progressive stroke risk.
    • Recurrent Falls: Associated with progressive gait and balance issues, and can signify worsening small vessel disease.
    • Behavioural Changes with Agitation: May suggest frontal lobe involvement or coexisting psychiatric conditions.
    Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Vascular Dementia"][/sc]
    • Isolated Memory Loss: Atypical in vascular dementia and may delay diagnosis, resembling Alzheimer’s disease more closely.
    • Sudden Onset with Stepwise Decline: Presents like acute cognitive impairment following each stroke.
    • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask vascular dementia.
    Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Vascular Dementia"][/sc]
    • Baseline and bedside: Brain MRI: Shows ischaemic changes, small vessel disease, or lacunar infarcts in affected areas.
    • Targeted and monitoring: CT Head: Used if MRI is unavailable; reveals cortical atrophy and white matter changes; Cognitive Testing: Neuropsychological assessments focus on executive.
    Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Vascular Dementia"][/sc]
    • A 78-year-old male with a history of hypertension and multiple strokes presents with a gradual decline in planning and organisation abilities, slowed thinking, and a shuffling gait over the last.

    Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Vascular Dementia"][/sc]
    • Alzheimer’s Disease - Progressive memory loss is more pronounced than executive dysfunction in early stages.
    • Dementia with Lewy Bodies - Features visual hallucinations, fluctuating cognition, and Parkinsonian motor symptoms.
    • Frontotemporal Dementia - Early changes in behaviour or language without significant memory loss initially.
    • Related alternative diagnosis - Distinguish from vascular dementia using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.
    Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Vascular Dementia"][/sc]
    • Immediate priorities: Assess severity, physiological stability, and immediate complications.
    • Core care: Deliver condition-specific management for vascular dementia, treat underlying drivers, and coordinate appropriate specialist input.
    • Monitoring and follow-up: Reassess symptom trajectory, examination findings, and objective markers to confirm response in vascular dementia.
    • Escalation and safety-netting: Escalate urgently for clinical deterioration, evolving red flags, or failure to improve in suspected vascular dementia.
    Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Vascular Dementia"][/sc]
    • Progressive Disability: Impaired functioning in daily life, requiring caregiver support.
    • Depression and Anxiety: Frequently arise due to cognitive and physical limitations.
    • Falls and Fractures: Associated with gait and balance issues, leading to increased injury risk.
    • Prognosis: Variable; cognitive decline may progress slowly or rapidly depending on risk factors and stroke recurrence.
