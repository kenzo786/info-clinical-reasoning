Bell's Palsy
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Bell's Palsy"][/sc]
  • Bell's palsy is an acute, unilateral lower motor neurone facial nerve (cranial nerve VII) paralysis of unknown aetiology.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise bell features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.

  • Higher-risk or missed groups: Bell's Palsy may be missed when presentations are atypical or comorbidity obscures key features.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Bell's Palsy"][/sc]
  • Bell's palsy involves inflammation of the facial nerve, likely triggered by viral infections (e.g., herpes simplex virus). The inflammation causes compression of the nerve as it travels through the narrow facial canal. This compression leads to ischaemia and demyelination, resulting in the loss of nerve function.
  • The exact cause remains unknown, but it is often linked to viral reactivation, autoimmune responses, or vascular ischaemia.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Bell's Palsy"][/sc]
  • Onset: Rapid, usually within 48-72 hours.
  • Acute phase: Maximum weakness is often reached within one week of onset.
  • Recovery phase: Spontaneous recovery typically begins within 3 weeks, with full recovery by 3-6 months in 85% of cases.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Bell's Palsy"][/sc]
  • Ask about unilateral facial weakness - Sudden inability to move the muscles on one side of the face due to lower motor neurone lesion of the in this clinical context.
  • Ask about inability to close the eye - Weakness of the orbicularis oculi muscle, leading to difficulty closing the eyelid.
  • Ask about loss of facial expressions - Paralysis of muscles involved in smiling, frowning, and raising the eyebrows due to facial nerve involvement.
  • Ask about ear pain or discomfort - Often due to inflammation or irritation of the facial nerve near its origin close to the ear canal.
  • Ask about altered taste - Loss of taste sensation in the anterior two-thirds of the tongue, as the chorda tympani branch of the facial nerve is.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Bell's Palsy"][/sc]
  • Check unilateral facial droop - Classic sign of facial nerve palsy, involving sagging of the mouth and flattening of nasolabial folds.
  • Check inability to raise eyebrows or close the eye - Upper and lower facial muscles are equally affected due to lower motor neurone lesion.
  • Check decreased blinking - Loss of motor control over eyelid muscles leads to reduced blink reflex, increasing the risk of corneal dryness or ulceration.
  • Check hyperacusis - Increased sensitivity to sound due to paralysis of the stapedius muscle (also innervated by the facial nerve).
  • Check loss of taste - Due to chorda tympani nerve involvement affecting taste sensation on the anterior two-thirds of the tongue.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Bell's Palsy"][/sc]
  • Bilateral facial paralysis - This could suggest more serious conditions such as Guillain-Barré syndrome, Lyme disease, or sarcoidosis.
  • Progressive neurological deficits - Could indicate a central cause such as a brainstem lesion or stroke.
  • Severe headache, neck stiffness, or fever - May indicate an infectious process such as meningitis.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Bell's Palsy"][/sc]
  • Slow onset of symptoms - Typically, Bell's palsy has a rapid onset. Gradual progression may indicate a tumour compressing the facial nerve.
  • Bilateral weakness - More indicative of conditions like Guillain-Barré syndrome or Lyme disease.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features or comorbidity can mask the underlying diagnosis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Bell's Palsy"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Clinical diagnosis: Based on history and characteristic findings. No specific laboratory or imaging tests are required unless atypical features are present.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Imaging: MRI may be indicated if the presentation is atypical, to exclude tumours or strokes; Nerve conduction studies (NCS): Occasionally used.

  • A 35-year-old female presents with a 48-hour history of sudden, painless, unilateral facial weakness. She reports difficulty closing her left eye and a slight reduction in taste sensation. Examination reveals.
  • Adult with symptom pattern and objective findings suggestive of bell's palsy, requiring focused confirmation and risk stratification.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Bell's Palsy"][/sc]
  • Stroke (ischaemic/haemorrhagic) - Sudden onset, typically spares the upper face (forehead), often associated with other neurological deficits.
  • Ramsay Hunt syndrome - Herpetic vesicles in the ear canal or on the tongue, associated with facial nerve.
  • Guillain-Barré syndrome - Often bilateral, may be associated with limb weakness or sensory changes, and may progress.
  • Lyme disease - Differentiate from bell's palsy using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Bell's Palsy"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Assess physiological stability and urgent complications at presentation, and initiate early stabilisation where required.
  • Core care: Deliver condition-specific treatment for bell's palsy, address underlying drivers, and involve appropriate specialist support.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Reassess symptom trajectory, objective markers, and treatment response over time to detect complications early.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Worsening symptoms, physiological deterioration, or failure to improve should trigger urgent reassessment for severe or alternative pathology in bell's palsy.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Bell's Palsy"][/sc]
  • Corneal ulceration: Due to incomplete eye closure and reduced blink reflex.
  • Permanent facial weakness: Seen in up to 15% of patients, especially those with complete paralysis at onset.
  • Synkinesis: Involuntary facial movements during voluntary action, caused by aberrant nerve regeneration.
