Corns and Calluses
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Corns and Calluses"][/sc]
  • Corns and calluses are localised areas of hyperkeratosis (thickened skin) caused by excessive pressure or friction.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise corns features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Corns and Calluses"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Corns and Calluses"][/sc]
  • Risk factors: Risk factors for corns and calluses include relevant predisposition, comorbidity, and exposure history.
  • Higher-risk or missed groups: Corns and Calluses may be missed when presentation is atypical or obscured by comorbidity.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Corns and Calluses"][/sc]
  • Prolonged mechanical stress stimulates hyperproliferation of keratinocytes in the stratum corneum.
  • The thickened epidermis forms a protective barrier against friction and pressure.
  • Corns develop when pressure localises to a small area, creating a central core, while calluses result from diffuse stress.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Corns and Calluses"][/sc]
  • Acute phase: Corns or calluses may form within days to weeks of repeated mechanical irritation.
  • Chronic phase: Lesions persist or recur if the underlying cause is not addressed, often becoming symptomatic over months or years.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Corns and Calluses"][/sc]
  • Ask about pain or tenderness - Results from pressure on nerve endings beneath the hyperkeratotic lesion, especially in corns.
  • Ask about burning sensation - Occurs in areas of friction due to irritation of superficial nerve endings.
  • Ask about discomfort with walking - Pathophysiology is related to biomechanical stress disrupting normal gait.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Corns and Calluses"][/sc]
  • Check thickened, hardened skin - The hallmark of hyperkeratosis, with corns appearing more centralised and calluses diffuse.
  • Check yellowish or grey appearance - Due to accumulated keratin in the stratum corneum.
  • Check visible fissures or cracks - Indicate advanced callus formation, which can lead to secondary infection.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Corns and Calluses"][/sc]
  • Ulceration - Suggests underlying diabetic neuropathy or vascular disease.
  • Infection (erythema, purulence) - Indicates secondary bacterial involvement, often requiring urgent treatment.
  • Rapid progression or recurrence - May signify an undiagnosed structural abnormality or malignancy (rare).
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Corns and Calluses"][/sc]
  • Painless corns or calluses in patients with neuropathy.
  • Lesions on unusual sites, potentially mimicking warts or other dermatoses.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask corns and calluses.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Corns and Calluses"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Clinical examination - Key for diagnosis; no additional tests typically required.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Dermatoscopy - Helps differentiate corns and calluses from warts; Imaging (X-ray) - Indicated in cases of suspected bony deformities.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Corns and Calluses"][/sc]
  • Plantar warts - Often painful with pinprick bleeding on shaving, unlike the even keratosis of corns/calluses.
  • Fungal infections - Associated with erythema and scaling, unlike isolated thickening.
  • Psoriasis - Diffuse hyperkeratosis with scaling, often associated with nail or joint changes.
  • Related alternative diagnosis - Distinguish from corns and calluses using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.

  • Immediate priorities: Assess severity, physiological stability, and immediate complications.
  • Core care: Deliver condition-specific management for corns and calluses, treat underlying drivers, and coordinate appropriate specialist input.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Reassess symptom trajectory, examination findings, and objective markers to confirm response in corns and calluses.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Escalate urgently for clinical deterioration, evolving red flags, or failure to improve in suspected corns and calluses.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Corns and Calluses"][/sc]
  • Ulceration - Particularly in diabetic or vascular disease patients.
  • Infection - Secondary bacterial infection requiring antibiotics.
  • Structural changes - Persistent mechanical stress can worsen deformities over time.
