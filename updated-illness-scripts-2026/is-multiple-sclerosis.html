Multiple Sclerosis (MS)
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Multiple Sclerosis (MS)"][/sc]
  • Multiple sclerosis (MS) is a chronic, autoimmune, inflammatory disorder affecting the central nervous system (CNS), characterised by the formation of sclerotic plaques in the brain and spinal cord.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise multiple features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.

  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Multiple Sclerosis (MS)"][/sc]
  • Gender: MS is more common in women than men (3:1 ratio).
  • Age: Typically diagnosed between 20-40 years.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Multiple Sclerosis (MS)"][/sc]
  • MS is characterised by immune-mediated demyelination of the CNS. T cells and B cells abnormally target myelin antigens, triggering inflammation. This leads to myelin destruction and axonal damage. Over time, astrocyte proliferation causes sclerotic plaques. The demyelination slows or blocks nerve conduction, leading to the clinical manifestations of MS.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Multiple Sclerosis (MS)"][/sc]
  • Relapsing-Remitting MS (RRMS): Characterised by periods of acute attacks (relapses) followed by recovery (remissions). The majority of patients initially present with this form.
  • Secondary Progressive MS (SPMS): RRMS can transition to SPMS, where patients experience steady progression of symptoms with or without relapses.
  • Primary Progressive MS (PPMS): Around 10-15% of patients present with steadily worsening symptoms without relapses or remissions from the onset.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Multiple Sclerosis (MS)"][/sc]
  • Ask about fatigue: One of the most common symptoms. Caused by central nervous system inflammation and demyelination, leading to inefficient nerve conduction.
  • Ask about visual disturbances (e.g., optic neuritis): Frequently an early symptom. Inflammation of the optic nerve leads to unilateral vision loss, pain on eye movement, with associated clinical features.
  • Ask about muscle weakness: Demyelination affecting motor pathways leads to weakness, commonly in the legs, causing walking difficulties.
  • Ask about numbness or tingling (paraesthesia): Due to sensory pathway disruption, presenting as abnormal sensations, usually in the limbs.
  • Ask about bladder dysfunction: Spinal cord involvement affects the bladder, leading to urgency, frequency, or retention.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Multiple Sclerosis (MS)"][/sc]
  • Check optic disc pallor: Seen in optic neuritis, indicating past or present inflammation of the optic nerve.
  • Check hyperreflexia: Due to upper motor neuron involvement, patients may demonstrate brisk reflexes.
  • Check spasticity: Chronic demyelination of motor pathways leads to muscle stiffness and spasticity, especially in the legs.
  • Check ataxia: Demyelination in the cerebellum or its connections results in uncoordinated movements, causing ataxia and poor balance.
  • Check uhthoff’s phenomenon: Temporary worsening of neurological symptoms, particularly visual symptoms, triggered by increased body temperature.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Multiple Sclerosis (MS)"][/sc]
  • Rapid neurological deterioration: May indicate a severe MS relapse or progressive form requiring urgent intervention.
  • Bilateral optic neuritis: While optic neuritis is common in MS, bilateral involvement could suggest other conditions, such as neuromyelitis optica (NMO).
  • Respiratory failure: Can occur in severe, advanced MS due to diaphragmatic weakness, requiring urgent management.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Multiple Sclerosis (MS)"][/sc]
  • Trigeminal neuralgia in younger patients: Although trigeminal neuralgia is more common in older adults, its presence in young patients could be a sign of MS.
  • Unilateral hearing loss: Rare in MS, but can occur due to involvement of the auditory pathways.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask multiple sclerosis (ms).

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Multiple Sclerosis (MS)"][/sc]
  • Neuromyelitis optica (NMO) - Bilateral optic neuritis and longitudinally extensive transverse myelitis are more characteristic of NMO.
  • Cerebrovascular disease - Stroke can present with focal neurological deficits, but typically the onset is more acute.
  • Lupus or other connective tissue diseases - Can mimic MS with neurological symptoms, but typically associated with systemic signs like arthritis.
  • Related alternative diagnosis - Distinguish from multiple sclerosis (ms) using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Multiple Sclerosis (MS)"][/sc]
  • Secondary progressive MS: Many patients with relapsing-remitting MS eventually develop progressive disability.
  • Immobility: Chronic disease can lead to severe motor impairment, contributing to deconditioning, pressure sores, and infections.
  • Cognitive impairment: Progressive MS may cause significant memory and cognitive difficulties, impacting daily functioning.
