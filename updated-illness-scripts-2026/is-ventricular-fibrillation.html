Ventricular Fibrillation (VF)
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Ventricular Fibrillation (VF)"][/sc]
  • Ventricular fibrillation (VF) is a life-threatening cardiac arrhythmia characterised by rapid, erratic electrical impulses in the ventricles.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise ventricular features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.

  • UK GP population: VF is rare in primary care settings but accounts for a significant proportion of sudden cardiac deaths.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Ventricular Fibrillation (VF)"][/sc]
  • VF results from disorganised electrical activity in the ventricles, often triggered by myocardial ischaemia, electrolyte imbalances, or underlying heart disease. Normally, the ventricles contract in a coordinated manner to pump blood. In VF, multiple electrical circuits form within the ventricular myocardium, causing chaotic electrical impulses that prevent coordinated contraction. The ventricles "quiver" rather than pump blood, leading to a sudden drop in cardiac output and perfusion, ultimately causing cardiovascular collapse and death without immediate intervention.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Ventricular Fibrillation (VF)"][/sc]
  • Initial phase: VF can occur abruptly, often in patients with a history of ischaemic heart disease, myocardial infarction, or structural heart abnormalities.
  • Progression: VF rapidly leads to loss of consciousness and cardiovascular collapse within seconds to minutes, with brain death occurring in 4-6 minutes without intervention.
  • Recovery phase: If defibrillation and resuscitation are successful, normal sinus rhythm may be restored, but patients remain at high risk for recurrence and complications like anoxic brain injury.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Ventricular Fibrillation (VF)"][/sc]
  • Ask about sudden collapse - Occurs due to the immediate loss of cardiac output, resulting in cerebral hypoperfusion and loss of consciousness.
  • Ask about loss of consciousness - Caused by cerebral hypoxia from inadequate blood flow during VF.
  • Ask about no pulse - As the heart is unable to pump blood effectively, no pulse can be detected.
  • Ask about preceding chest pain or palpitations - These may occur if VF is triggered by myocardial ischaemia or arrhythmias.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Ventricular Fibrillation (VF)"][/sc]
  • Check unresponsiveness - Due to loss of cerebral perfusion.
  • Check no detectable pulse - Reflects the absence of effective cardiac contraction during VF.
  • Check agonal breathing or absent respiration - Brainstem-driven reflex breathing patterns may occur, but normal respiration is absent due to lack of blood flow.
  • Check absent heart sounds - There is no coordinated myocardial contraction, so no heart sounds are audible.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Ventricular Fibrillation (VF)"][/sc]
  • Loss of consciousness - Immediate collapse without warning is a red flag indicating cardiac arrest, necessitating immediate intervention.
  • No pulse - Signifies no effective blood circulation, an emergency that indicates ongoing cardiac arrest.
  • Absent respiration - Suggests severe cardiovascular collapse with cessation of ventilation, requiring urgent resuscitation to prevent brain injury.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Ventricular Fibrillation (VF)"][/sc]
  • Intermittent syncope - In some patients, VF may manifest as sudden syncope episodes that revert spontaneously, especially if VF is brief and converts back to sinus rhythm spontaneously.
  • Brief palpitations followed by collapse - This may occur if VF is preceded by other arrhythmias like ventricular tachycardia.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask ventricular fibrillation (vf).

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Ventricular Fibrillation (VF)"][/sc]
  • Ventricular tachycardia - Distinguish from ventricular fibrillation (vf) using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.
  • Pulseless electrical activity (PEA) - There is electrical activity on ECG, but no pulse. PEA is different from VF.
  • Asystole - Complete absence of electrical activity (flatline) on ECG, whereas VF shows chaotic electrical activity.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Ventricular Fibrillation (VF)"][/sc]
  • Anoxic brain injury - Prolonged lack of cerebral perfusion can result in permanent brain damage, especially if defibrillation is delayed.
  • Recurrence of VF - Without addressing the underlying cause, patients remain at high risk for recurrent VF.
  • Heart failure - Extensive myocardial damage from recurrent VF can lead to long-term ventricular dysfunction.
