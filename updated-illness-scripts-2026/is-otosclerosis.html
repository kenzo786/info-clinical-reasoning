Otosclerosis
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Otosclerosis"][/sc]
  • Otosclerosis is a primary bone disorder of the otic capsule, characterised by abnormal bone remodelling in the middle and inner ear.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise otosclerosis features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.

Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Otosclerosis"][/sc]
  • Otosclerosis begins with abnormal bone resorption in the otic capsule, specifically around the stapes footplate and the oval window.
  • This bone resorption is followed by disorganised bone remodelling, with replacement by sclerotic bone. This process leads to fixation of the stapes, impairing its ability to transmit sound vibrations to the inner ear.
  • In cochlear otosclerosis, the process spreads to the cochlea, resulting in damage to the sensory cells and nerve pathways, causing sensorineural hearing loss.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Otosclerosis"][/sc]
  • Early stage: Slow, progressive hearing loss that may be noticed over several months to years. Often starts in the third or fourth decade of life.
  • Progressive stage: The hearing loss worsens over time, potentially becoming bilateral. Some patients may develop tinnitus or vertigo as the disease progresses.
  • Advanced stage: Complete conductive hearing loss may occur, with possible involvement of the cochlea, leading to mixed or sensorineural hearing loss.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Otosclerosis"][/sc]
  • Ask about conductive hearing loss - Most common symptom, often starts in one ear and may become bilateral. Caused by stapes fixation, preventing sound transmission.
  • Ask about tinnitus - Perceived as a buzzing or ringing sound, occurs in up to 75% of patients, likely due to inner ear involvement.
  • Ask about vertigo or imbalance - Uncommon, but may occur in advanced cases when the cochlea is affected.
  • Ask about paracusis of Willis - Patients may hear better in noisy environments, due to conductive hearing loss.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Otosclerosis"][/sc]
  • Check rinne test negative - Bone conduction is better than air conduction, indicating conductive hearing loss.
  • Check weber test lateralises to affected ear - Indicates conductive hearing loss on the side of lateralisation.
  • Check normal tympanic membrane - Otosclerosis typically does not cause visible abnormalities of the eardrum.
  • Check schwartz's sign - Reddish hue visible on the promontory behind the tympanic membrane, seen in some cases due to increased vascularisation in active disease.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Otosclerosis"][/sc]
  • Rapidly progressive hearing loss - May indicate cochlear otosclerosis or other underlying pathologies, such as tumours or autoimmune disorders.
  • Severe vertigo or imbalance - Could suggest involvement of the vestibular system, or an alternative diagnosis like Ménière's disease.
  • Sudden hearing loss - Uncommon in otosclerosis, but warrants urgent evaluation for potential alternative causes, such as sensorineural hearing loss from other aetiologies.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Otosclerosis"][/sc]
  • Otosclerosis may present with predominantly sensorineural hearing loss, which can obscure the diagnosis, particularly in cases with cochlear involvement (cochlear otosclerosis).
  • In rare instances, it may present as unilateral hearing loss with no significant progression over time, leading to confusion with other causes of conductive loss.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask otosclerosis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Otosclerosis"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Audiometry - Typically reveals conductive hearing loss with an air-bone gap, especially in the lower frequencies. Sensorineural loss may be evident.
  • Targeted and monitoring: CT scan of temporal bone - Can confirm the diagnosis by visualising the sclerotic changes around the stapes and otic capsule.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Otosclerosis"][/sc]
  • Chronic otitis media - Unlike otosclerosis, this presents with a history of recurrent ear infections, perforated tympanic membrane.
  • Ossicular chain discontinuity - May present similarly but usually follows trauma or infection, and tympanometry often shows abnormal.
  • Cholesteatoma - Distinguish from otosclerosis using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Otosclerosis"][/sc]
  • Sensorineural hearing loss - Occurs in advanced cases with cochlear involvement, leading to a mixed hearing loss that may be more difficult to treat.
  • Complete deafness - Rare, but may occur if the disease severely affects both cochlear and conductive pathways.
  • Vestibular symptoms - Although uncommon, involvement of the balance organs can lead to vertigo and chronic imbalance.
