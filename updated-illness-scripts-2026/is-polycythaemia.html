Polycythaemia
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Polycythaemia"][/sc]
  • Polycythaemia is a haematological condition characterised by an increased concentration of red blood cells (RBCs) in the blood, leading to an elevated haematocrit and haemoglobin level.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise polycythaemia features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.

  • Higher-risk or missed groups: Polycythaemia may be missed when presentation is atypical or obscured by comorbidity.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Polycythaemia"][/sc]
  • Primary polycythaemia: In polycythaemia vera, a JAK2 mutation leads to abnormal signalling in haematopoietic stem cells, causing excessive RBC production independent of erythropoietin (EPO).
  • Secondary polycythaemia: Chronic hypoxia (e.g., due to lung disease, high altitude) or elevated EPO levels (e.g., renal tumour) stimulates increased RBC production as a compensatory response to low oxygen levels.
  • Hyperviscosity: Increased RBC mass raises blood viscosity, which can impair blood flow, increasing the risk of thrombosis.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Polycythaemia"][/sc]
  • Slow progression: Polycythaemia often develops over months to years, with symptoms emerging gradually.
  • Chronic phase: If untreated, primary polycythaemia can lead to thrombotic complications or transition to a myelofibrotic phase with bone marrow fibrosis.
  • Acute progression: Acute thrombotic events can occur, leading to strokes or myocardial infarction.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Polycythaemia"][/sc]
  • Ask about fatigue - Commonly reported due to increased blood viscosity leading to poor tissue oxygenation.
  • Ask about headache - Occurs from hyperviscosity-related reduced cerebral perfusion.
  • Ask about pruritus, especially after warm showers - Believed to be linked to histamine release from excess basophils in polycythaemia vera.
  • Ask about dizziness and light-headedness - Due to poor oxygen delivery in highly viscous blood.
  • Ask about dyspnoea - Occurs in advanced cases due to decreased pulmonary oxygen exchange efficiency.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Polycythaemia"][/sc]
  • Check plethoric complexion (ruddy appearance) - Due to increased RBC mass and blood flow to the skin.
  • Check splenomegaly - Common in polycythaemia vera, resulting from increased blood cell production.
  • Check hypertension - Raised blood viscosity increases peripheral resistance.
  • Check thrombotic signs (e.g., deep vein thrombosis, stroke) - Caused by hyperviscosity and increased clotting.
  • Check hepatomegaly - Occasionally present due to extramedullary haematopoiesis.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Polycythaemia"][/sc]
  • Severe headache with visual changes - Indicates possible cerebral thrombosis, requiring urgent assessment.
  • Chest pain or shortness of breath - May signify myocardial infarction or pulmonary embolism.
  • Sudden weakness or difficulty speaking - Suggests a thrombotic stroke.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Polycythaemia"][/sc]
  • Mild headaches or fatigue without other symptoms - Common in early or mild cases and may be misdiagnosed as general fatigue.
  • Isolated hypertension - May be mistaken for essential hypertension without recognition of underlying polycythaemia.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask polycythaemia.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Polycythaemia"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Full blood count - Elevated haematocrit (>52% in men, >48% in women) and haemoglobin levels indicate polycythaemia; Serum erythropoietin (EPO) .
  • Targeted and monitoring: JAK2 mutation analysis - Positive in ~95% of polycythaemia vera cases, helping confirm primary polycythaemia; Arterial blood gases (ABG) - May.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Polycythaemia"][/sc]
  • Chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD) - Distinguished by chronic hypoxia but with associated lung findings.
  • Chronic kidney disease - May cause secondary polycythaemia due to raised EPO from renal hypoxia or tumours.
  • Dehydration - Can mimic polycythaemia but shows elevated haematocrit with normal RBC mass.
  • Related alternative diagnosis - Distinguish from polycythaemia using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Polycythaemia"][/sc]
  • Thrombosis - Common due to increased blood viscosity, leading to strokes or myocardial infarctions.
  • Myelofibrosis - Progression to fibrosis in polycythaemia vera, causing bone marrow scarring and cytopenias.
  • Acute leukaemia - Rare but increased risk in polycythaemia vera, particularly with long-term treatment.
