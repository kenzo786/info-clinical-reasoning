Medication-Induced Anaemia
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Medication-Induced Anaemia"][/sc]
  • Medication-induced anaemia is a form of anaemia caused by the administration of certain drugs that either impair red blood cell production, increase red blood cell destruction, or interfere with vital.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise medication features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.

Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Medication-Induced Anaemia"][/sc]
  • Bone marrow suppression: Drugs like chemotherapy agents suppress the bone marrow’s ability to produce red blood cells, leading to anaemia.
  • Haemolysis: Certain drugs can cause immune-mediated or direct damage to red blood cells, leading to premature destruction. For example, antibiotics like penicillin can cause immune haemolytic anaemia.
  • Nutrient interference: Drugs like methotrexate interfere with folate metabolism, essential for DNA synthesis in red blood cell precursors, resulting in megaloblastic anaemia.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Medication-Induced Anaemia"][/sc]
  • Acute: Haemolytic reactions can present within hours to days of drug administration.
  • Subacute: Bone marrow suppression due to medications (e.g., chemotherapy) typically presents within weeks of starting the drug.
  • Chronic: Interference with vitamin metabolism (e.g., methotrexate) often results in anaemia over several months of therapy.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Medication-Induced Anaemia"][/sc]
  • Ask about fatigue - Common in anaemia due to reduced oxygen-carrying capacity of the blood. Reduced haemoglobin levels mean that tissues receive less oxygen, leading, with associated clinical features.
  • Ask about pallor - Caused by reduced red blood cell count, particularly noticeable in the conjunctiva, due to decreased blood perfusion.
  • Ask about dyspnoea on exertion - Due to decreased oxygen delivery to tissues, patients often experience shortness of breath during physical activity.
  • Ask about dizziness - Caused by reduced cerebral oxygenation secondary to decreased haemoglobin levels.
  • Ask about jaundice (in haemolytic anaemia) - Occurs when red blood cells are destroyed, releasing haemoglobin which is then converted into bilirubin, leading to yellowing, with associated clinical features.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Medication-Induced Anaemia"][/sc]
  • Check pallor - Visible in the skin and mucous membranes due to decreased haemoglobin.
  • Check tachycardia - The body compensates for anaemia by increasing heart rate to maintain oxygen delivery to tissues.
  • Check systolic ejection murmur - Common in severe anaemia due to hyperdynamic circulation, as the heart pumps harder to meet oxygen demand.
  • Check jaundice - Indicates haemolysis, as discussed, resulting from excess bilirubin in the blood.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Medication-Induced Anaemia"][/sc]
  • Severe jaundice - Suggests massive haemolysis and could indicate a life-threatening haemolytic crisis, often requiring immediate cessation of the offending drug and possible blood transfusion.
  • Haemoglobin <7 g/dL - Severe anaemia, which may necessitate urgent transfusion and could lead to cardiac failure or ischaemia.
  • New or worsening chest pain - Suggests reduced oxygen supply to the myocardium, indicating possible acute coronary syndrome in anaemic patients.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Medication-Induced Anaemia"][/sc]
  • Asymptomatic: Some patients, particularly those with chronic mild anaemia, may not exhibit noticeable symptoms, especially if they are elderly or have adapted physiologically to the anaemia over time.
  • Mild cognitive impairment: Older adults may present with confusion or lethargy as their primary symptom due to decreased cerebral oxygenation.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask medication-induced anaemia.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Medication-Induced Anaemia"][/sc]
  • Aplastic anaemia - Differentiated by pancytopenia and hypocellular bone marrow without clear drug association.
  • Iron deficiency anaemia - Low serum ferritin and microcytosis, as opposed to drug-induced anaemia, which can show macrocytosis.
  • Pernicious anaemia - Similar macrocytic anaemia, but with anti-parietal cell or intrinsic factor antibodies, and often no.
  • Related alternative diagnosis - Distinguish from medication-induced anaemia using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.

  • Core care: Deliver condition-specific management for medication-induced anaemia, treat underlying drivers, and coordinate appropriate specialist input.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Reassess symptom trajectory, examination findings, and objective markers to confirm response in medication-induced anaemia.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Escalate urgently for clinical deterioration, evolving red flags, or failure to improve in suspected medication-induced anaemia.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Medication-Induced Anaemia"][/sc]
  • Heart failure: Severe anaemia can precipitate heart failure, particularly in elderly or vulnerable patients, due to the heart’s increased workload to supply oxygen.
  • Infection risk: Medications like chemotherapy not only cause anaemia but can also lead to neutropenia, increasing infection susceptibility.
  • Death: If left untreated, severe anaemia, particularly haemolytic types, can be fatal due to multi-organ failure or cardiac complications.
