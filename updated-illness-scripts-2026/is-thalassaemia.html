Thalassaemia
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Thalassaemia"][/sc]
  • Thalassaemia is a group of inherited blood disorders caused by defects in the synthesis of haemoglobin chains, leading to ineffective erythropoiesis and varying degrees of anaemia.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise thalassaemia features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.

Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Thalassaemia"][/sc]
  • Thalassaemia results from mutations in the genes responsible for alpha or beta globin chains. In alpha thalassaemia, there is decreased production of alpha chains, while beta thalassaemia is characterised by reduced or absent beta-globin synthesis. The imbalance between globin chains leads to ineffective erythropoiesis, haemolysis, and anaemia. The excess chains precipitate in red blood cells (RBCs), leading to destruction of developing erythroblasts in the bone marrow and shortened lifespan of mature RBCs in circulation.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Thalassaemia"][/sc]
  • Neonatal period: Most patients are asymptomatic at birth because of the predominant production of fetal haemoglobin (HbF), which does not require beta globin.
  • Infancy to early childhood: Symptoms such as anaemia, growth retardation, and failure to thrive develop as fetal haemoglobin declines and adult haemoglobin production begins.
  • Adolescence and adulthood: Without appropriate treatment, chronic anaemia, organ damage from iron overload (secondary to transfusions), and skeletal deformities can develop.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Thalassaemia"][/sc]
  • Ask about fatigue and weakness - Due to chronic anaemia from ineffective erythropoiesis, patients often present with these symptoms.
  • Ask about pallor - Reduced haemoglobin levels cause decreased oxygen delivery to tissues, resulting in pale skin.
  • Ask about jaundice - Excessive haemolysis leads to increased bilirubin production, causing yellowing of the skin and sclera.
  • Ask about shortness of breath - Anaemia leads to decreased oxygen-carrying capacity, manifesting as dyspnoea, especially during exertion.
  • Ask about bone pain and deformities - The body attempts to compensate for the anaemia by expanding the bone marrow, leading to skeletal changes, particularly, with associated clinical features.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Thalassaemia"][/sc]
  • Check pallor - Indicates anaemia due to low haemoglobin levels.
  • Check splenomegaly - The spleen is enlarged as it filters out abnormal erythrocytes and extramedullary haematopoiesis occurs.
  • Check hepatomegaly - Due to extramedullary haematopoiesis and iron overload from frequent blood transfusions.
  • Check growth retardation - Chronic anaemia and ineffective erythropoiesis impair normal growth and development.
  • Check skeletal deformities - "Chipmunk facies" and frontal bossing result from expanded bone marrow to increase RBC production.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Thalassaemia"][/sc]
  • Severe anaemia - Sudden worsening of anaemia may indicate an aplastic crisis, commonly triggered by parvovirus B19 infection.
  • Iron overload - Occurs due to frequent transfusions, leading to endocrine complications (e.g., diabetes), liver cirrhosis, and cardiac failure.
  • Heart failure - A result of chronic severe anaemia or iron overload, requiring urgent intervention.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Thalassaemia"][/sc]
  • Patients with thalassaemia minor or trait may remain asymptomatic or present with mild anaemia that can be mistaken for iron-deficiency anaemia. They typically present with microcytosis without significant clinical symptoms.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask thalassaemia.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Thalassaemia"][/sc]
  • Iron overload - A common complication due to transfusions, leading to cardiac, hepatic, and endocrine dysfunction.
  • Cardiac failure - Due to chronic anaemia or iron deposition in cardiac tissue.
  • Bone deformities - Untreated or inadequately transfused patients may develop skeletal abnormalities due to bone marrow expansion.
  • Infections - Especially in post-splenectomy patients or those on iron chelation therapy.
