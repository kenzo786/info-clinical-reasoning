Referred Neck Pain
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Referred Neck Pain"][/sc]
  • Referred neck pain refers to pain originating from another anatomical region, such as the shoulders, upper back, or even visceral organs, that is perceived in the neck.

Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Referred Neck Pain"][/sc]
  • Referred neck pain occurs due to the convergence of sensory input from different tissues (somatic or visceral) in the dorsal horn of the spinal cord, which leads to misinterpretation of the pain's true source. For example, pain from myocardial ischaemia may be referred to the neck due to the overlap in nerve supply (C3-C5 dermatomes). Other common mechanisms include nerve root irritation, inflammation, or muscle tension that transmits pain signals to areas distant from the original source.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Referred Neck Pain"][/sc]
  • Acute phase: Immediate pain referral following injury, inflammation, or acute visceral events like myocardial infarction. Pain is sudden and sharp, often without trauma to the neck.
  • Subacute phase: Persistent pain over weeks to months, often following resolution of the primary cause, like a shoulder injury, with ongoing referred pain due to continued neural sensitisation.
  • Chronic phase: Referred pain persisting for more than 3 months, often associated with chronic conditions like cervical spondylosis or gallbladder disease. Pain may become less localised, and neurological symptoms like paraesthesia may emerge.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Referred Neck Pain"][/sc]
  • Ask about neck pain without trauma - Pain felt in the neck despite the primary pathology being elsewhere, such as the shoulder or heart. This occurs.
  • Ask about radiating arm or shoulder pain - Due to cervical radiculopathy, where nerve compression leads to referred pain down the arm.
  • Ask about chest pain or discomfort - When neck pain is referred from myocardial ischaemia, it may be accompanied by chest pressure or tightness.
  • Ask about headaches - Particularly occipital headaches can occur when upper cervical nerves (C1-C2) are involved.
  • Ask about pain exacerbated by certain movements - Movements that compress nerve roots or put pressure on muscles, like looking upwards or sideways, may worsen, with associated clinical features.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Referred Neck Pain"][/sc]
  • Check tenderness in the neck muscles - Muscle spasm or tension may occur as a secondary response to the referred pain source, causing palpable tenderness.
  • Check reduced range of motion - Limited movement in the neck, especially with flexion and extension, may suggest nerve involvement or muscular compensation.
  • Check positive Spurling's test - If the pain is referred from cervical radiculopathy, this test (neck extension and lateral rotation with axial compression) may reproduce.
  • Check neurological deficits - In cervical radiculopathy, weakness, paraesthesia, or reflex changes may occur in the corresponding dermatome or myotome.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Referred Neck Pain"][/sc]
  • Chest pain with exertion - This may indicate referred pain from myocardial ischaemia, a medical emergency.
  • Severe unrelenting pain at night - May suggest a malignancy (e.g., Pancoast tumour) referring pain to the neck.
  • Neurological deficits - Progressive motor weakness or sensory loss may indicate severe cervical radiculopathy, cord compression, or spinal tumour.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Referred Neck Pain"][/sc]
  • Right-sided neck pain and shoulder pain without overt chest pain - Referred pain from a silent myocardial infarction, more common in diabetics or elderly patients, may present without typical chest symptoms.
  • Unilateral headache and neck pain - Can occur in cervical facet syndrome or C2-C3 referred pain, which may be misdiagnosed as migraine or tension-type headache.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask referred neck pain.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Referred Neck Pain"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Cervical spine X-ray - May show degenerative changes if cervical spondylosis is the cause of referred pain; MRI of the cervical.
  • Targeted and monitoring: ECG and cardiac biomarkers - If cardiac referred pain is suspected, these tests can help rule out myocardial infarction; Nerve conduction.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Referred Neck Pain"][/sc]
  • Cervical spondylosis - Differentiated by chronic neck pain, stiffness, and radiographic evidence of degenerative changes.
  • Shoulder impingement - Presents with shoulder-specific pain and limitation of movement, improving with shoulder injections.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Referred Neck Pain"][/sc]
  • Chronic pain - May develop due to persistent nerve sensitisation or untreated underlying conditions.
  • Myelopathy - If cervical compression is not treated, it can progress to spinal cord damage.
  • Cardiac complications - Untreated myocardial ischaemia can lead to myocardial infarction or heart failure.
