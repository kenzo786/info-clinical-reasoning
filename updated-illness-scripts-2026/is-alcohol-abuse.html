Alcohol Abuse
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Alcohol Abuse"][/sc]
  • Alcohol use disorder is a harmful pattern of alcohol consumption with impaired control, persistent use despite harm, and risk of dependence and withdrawal.
  • UKMLA essentials: loss of drinking control, delirium tremens or seizure risk, assess withdrawal and alcohol-related harm.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Alcohol Abuse"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Alcohol Abuse"][/sc]
  • Alcohol Abuse is encountered across acute and specialist care, with burden shaped by age, comorbidity, and exposure context.
  • Risk increases in patients with relevant predisposing disease, treatment exposures, or delayed access to care.
  • Missed or late recognition is more likely when presentations are atypical or overlap with common alternative diagnoses.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Alcohol Abuse"][/sc]
  • Alcohol abuse affects the brain's reward and stress systems, particularly altering dopamine and gamma-aminobutyric acid (GABA) signalling. Chronic exposure leads to neuroadaptation, where increased alcohol tolerance and withdrawal symptoms develop due to desensitisation of GABA receptors and upregulation of excitatory neurotransmitters. Over time, this disrupts executive control and increases the compulsive need for alcohol, reinforcing addiction. Chronic alcohol use also impacts other organs, contributing to liver disease (cirrhosis), cardiovascular problems, and neuropsychiatric complications.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Alcohol Abuse"][/sc]
  • Early Phase: Initial occasional binge drinking or regular use with few consequences. Tolerance begins to build.
  • Middle Phase: Drinking becomes more frequent, and withdrawal symptoms (e.g., tremors) may occur. Social, occupational, and health consequences emerge.
  • Late Phase: Severe physical dependence, chronic health complications, and severe social or occupational impairment. Withdrawal symptoms become more pronounced, requiring medical intervention.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Alcohol Abuse"][/sc]
  • Ask about Cravings for alcohol - A powerful desire to drink driven by dopamine dysregulation in the brain's reward pathways.
  • Ask about Inability to control drinking - Loss of executive function due to prefrontal cortex damage, making it difficult to stop drinking despite a desire to do so.
  • Ask about Withdrawal symptoms - Including tremors, sweating, and nausea, resulting from neuroadaptation and imbalance between inhibitory and excitatory neurotransmission.
  • Ask about Increased tolerance - The need to consume more alcohol to achieve the same effect due to downregulation of GABA receptors.
  • Ask about Gastrointestinal symptoms - Such as nausea, vomiting, and diarrhoea, due to alcohol's irritant effect on the gastric mucosa.
  • Ask about Depression and anxiety - Mood disturbances result from chronic alcohol-induced neurochemical imbalances, particularly affecting serotonin and dopamine pathways.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Alcohol Abuse"][/sc]
  • Check Tremors - Particularly in the hands, due to withdrawal-induced autonomic hyperactivity.
  • Check Jaundice - Yellowing of the skin and eyes, indicating advanced liver damage and reduced liver function (bilirubin build-up).
  • Check Alcohol odour on breath - A common sign during or after a recent drinking episode.
  • Check Palmar erythema - Reddening of the palms due to altered oestrogen metabolism in chronic liver disease.
  • Check Ascites - Fluid accumulation in the abdomen, associated with cirrhosis and portal hypertension in chronic alcohol abuse.
  • Check Spider naevi - Small, spider-like blood vessels on the skin, due to elevated oestrogen levels from liver dysfunction.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Alcohol Abuse"][/sc]
  • Severe withdrawal (delirium tremens) - Includes confusion, agitation, hallucinations, and seizures, potentially life-threatening due to autonomic hyperactivity.
  • Upper gastrointestinal bleeding - Haematemesis or melena may indicate oesophageal varices, which can rupture in cirrhosis.
  • Altered mental status - Suggestive of hepatic encephalopathy or Wernicke-Korsakoff syndrome, both of which can progress to coma if untreated.
  • Jaundice with ascites - Indicates advanced liver failure and portal hypertension.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Alcohol Abuse"][/sc]
  • High-functioning alcoholics - Individuals who maintain employment and social relationships but have heavy drinking patterns. They may present late with advanced liver disease.
  • Isolated symptoms of depression or anxiety - Alcohol use can mask itself as psychiatric symptoms, especially in younger patients.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Alcohol Abuse"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Full blood count (FBC) - May show macrocytic anaemia due to folate deficiency, elevated mean corpuscular volume (MCV), and thrombocytopenia (low platelets) associated with liver dysfunction.
  • Targeted and monitoring: LFTs and synthetic liver function to assess alcohol-related liver injury; GGT or carbohydrate-deficient transferrin where chronic heavy use confirmation is needed; abdominal ultrasound for steatosis, cirrhosis features, or portal-hypertension complications.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Alcohol Abuse"][/sc]
  • A 45-year-old male with a history of daily alcohol consumption presents with tremors, nausea, and jaundice. He reports difficulty controlling his drinking and experiences withdrawal symptoms when attempting to stop. LFTs show elevated liver enzymes, and ultrasound reveals hepatic steatosis.
  • Adult with dependent alcohol use, withdrawal symptoms on cessation, and evidence of alcohol-related organ harm, consistent with alcohol use disorder.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Alcohol Abuse"][/sc]
  • Non-alcoholic fatty liver disease - Metabolic risk factors with minimal alcohol exposure and a non-alcoholic liver-injury pattern suggest this diagnosis.
  • Viral hepatitis - Presents with jaundice and liver enzyme elevation, but serological markers for hepatitis A, B, or C will be positive.
  • Benzodiazepine or barbiturate withdrawal - Similar withdrawal symptoms, but often without the same pattern of liver enzyme derangement.
  • Depressive disorder - Low mood and anhedonia may coexist but do not account for physiological withdrawal or alcohol-specific biochemical harm.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Alcohol Abuse"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Assess withdrawal severity, thiamine status, and acute medical risk, and provide supervised withdrawal support when dependence is present.
  • Core care: Combine relapse-prevention medication options with structured psychosocial interventions and community alcohol-service engagement.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Review drinking pattern, withdrawal recurrence, mental health, liver function, nutrition, and social risk at planned follow-up.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Escalate urgently for delirium tremens, withdrawal seizures, suicidal ideation, gastrointestinal bleeding, jaundice progression, or encephalopathy features.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Alcohol Abuse"][/sc]
  • Cirrhosis - Progressive scarring of the liver due to chronic alcohol-induced liver damage. Complications include portal hypertension, ascites, and oesophageal varices.
  • Hepatic encephalopathy - Mental status changes due to the build-up of ammonia and other toxins in liver failure.
  • Wernicke-Korsakoff syndrome - A neurological condition due to thiamine deficiency, presenting with confusion, ataxia, and ophthalmoplegia (Wernicke's) or memory impairment (Korsakoff's).
  • Cardiomyopathy - Alcohol-induced weakening of the heart muscle, leading to heart failure.
