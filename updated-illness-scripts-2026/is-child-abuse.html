Child Abuse
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Child Abuse"][/sc]
  • Child abuse is a broad term encompassing physical, emotional, sexual harm, or neglect inflicted upon a child under 18 by a caregiver or parent.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise child features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Child Abuse"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Child Abuse"][/sc]
  • Risk factors: Risk of child abuse rises with relevant predisposition, comorbidity, and exposure profile.
  • Higher-risk or missed groups: Child Abuse may be missed when presentations are atypical or comorbidity obscures key features.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Child Abuse"][/sc]
  • Physical Abuse: Repetitive trauma leads to physical injuries such as bruises, fractures, and head trauma. Mechanisms of harm vary by injury but generally result from blunt force or impact.
  • Emotional Abuse: Chronic verbal abuse or neglect disrupts emotional development, potentially impairing social, cognitive, and mental health.
  • Sexual Abuse: Sexual contact or exploitation leads to physical trauma and psychological distress, often resulting in PTSD and anxiety disorders.
  • Neglect: Insufficient nutrition, healthcare, and supervision cause developmental delays, poor growth, and recurrent infections.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Child Abuse"][/sc]
  • Acute phase: Injuries may present as isolated events but often reflect a pattern. Psychological impacts, such as fear or withdrawal, can manifest shortly after abusive incidents.
  • Chronic phase: With ongoing abuse, physical signs of repeated trauma emerge, alongside developmental delays, emotional distress, and potential regression in behaviour.
  • Long-term phase: If unresolved, abuse may lead to chronic mental health disorders, interpersonal difficulties, and increased risk of future abuse.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Child Abuse"][/sc]
  • Ask about withdrawal or Fearfulness - Children may avoid adults or display heightened anxiety due to ongoing fear of abuse.
  • Ask about frequent Unexplained Injuries - Recurrent bruising, fractures, or burns, particularly in unusual areas (e.g., upper arms), suggest physical abuse.
  • Ask about developmental Delays - Lack of appropriate social, cognitive, or emotional milestones can result from neglect or emotional abuse.
  • Ask about chronic Somatic Complaints - Headaches, abdominal pain, or other symptoms without a clear physical cause may indicate psychological distress from abuse.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Child Abuse"][/sc]
  • Check bruises or Burns in Distinct Patterns - Injuries matching the shape of an object (belt, cigarette burn) indicate inflicted trauma.
  • Check fractures, Particularly in Infants - Spiral fractures or fractures in non-ambulatory children raise concern for abuse.
  • Check poor Hygiene or Malnutrition - Poor growth, dental caries, and inadequate clothing may indicate neglect.
  • Check fearful or Aggressive Behaviour - Emotional signs such as heightened aggression or extreme compliance may reflect trauma response.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Child Abuse"][/sc]
  • Multiple Injuries of Different Ages - Suggests recurrent trauma and possible ongoing abuse.
  • Delay in Seeking Care for Serious Injuries - Caregivers delaying treatment may indicate intent to hide the nature of injuries.
  • Fractures or Intracranial Injuries in Non-ambulatory Infants - Such injuries in non-mobile infants are highly suspicious for abuse.
  • Sexually Transmitted Infections in Prepubertal Children - Raises strong suspicion for sexual abuse and requires immediate investigation.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Child Abuse"][/sc]
  • Absence of Physical Signs with Emotional Disturbance - Emotional abuse may lack visible physical signs but still cause developmental and psychological harm.
  • Somatic Complaints without Clear Medical Cause - Complaints like recurrent abdominal pain may be misinterpreted as gastrointestinal issues rather than signs of psychological distress.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features or comorbidity can mask the underlying diagnosis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Child Abuse"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Full Blood Count - To assess for anaemia or other signs of malnutrition in neglected children; X-rays or Bone Surveys .
  • Targeted and monitoring: Targeted investigations to confirm diagnosis, assess complications, and monitor response in child abuse.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Child Abuse"][/sc]
  • A 6-year-old child presents with multiple bruises in various stages of healing, delayed development, and withdrawn behaviour. The caregiver provides inconsistent explanations for the injuries.
  • Adult with symptom pattern and objective findings suggestive of child abuse, requiring focused confirmation and risk stratification.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Child Abuse"][/sc]
  • Osteogenesis Imperfecta - May present with fractures; differs by presence of blue sclerae, family history.
  • Coagulopathies - Haemophilia or other bleeding disorders may cause bruising; diagnosis confirmed with coagulation studies.
  • Accidental Trauma - Injuries typically match the history and have consistent explanations.
  • Münchausen Syndrome by Proxy - Involves induced illness by the caregiver, presenting with incongruent history and repeated healthcare presentations.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Child Abuse"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Assess physiological stability and urgent complications at presentation, and initiate early stabilisation where required.
  • Core care: Deliver condition-specific treatment for child abuse, address underlying drivers, and involve appropriate specialist support.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Reassess symptom trajectory, objective markers, and treatment response over time to detect complications early.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Worsening symptoms, physiological deterioration, or failure to improve should trigger urgent reassessment for severe or alternative pathology in child abuse.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Child Abuse"][/sc]
  • Psychological Disorders - High risk of PTSD, depression, anxiety, and behavioural disorders.
  • Chronic Health Conditions - Increased risk of chronic pain, somatisation, and developmental delays.
  • Future Abuse - Risk of recurring abuse in the absence of intervention, contributing to intergenerational trauma.
  • Poor Social and Educational Outcomes - Lower academic achievement and difficulties in interpersonal relationships are common.
