Medication Overuse Headache (MOH)
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Medication Overuse Headache (MOH)"][/sc]
  • Medication Overuse Headache (MOH) is a secondary headache disorder caused by the excessive or inappropriate use of acute headache medications.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise medication features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.

Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Medication Overuse Headache (MOH)"][/sc]
  • MOH occurs due to the chronic overuse of acute pain medications, which alters pain modulation mechanisms in the brain. Overuse of medications such as triptans, opioids, or simple analgesics can lead to central sensitisation, reduced pain thresholds, and increased headache frequency.
  • Repeated use of these medications may also cause neurochemical changes, particularly in serotonin and dopamine pathways, further exacerbating headache patterns.
  • This leads to a 'rebound' phenomenon, where the cessation of medication worsens the headache, prompting further use, creating a vicious cycle.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Medication Overuse Headache (MOH)"][/sc]
  • Early Phase: Episodic headaches initially respond to acute medication.
  • Chronic Phase: With repeated use, headaches become more frequent, eventually occurring daily or almost daily.
  • Post-treatment Phase: Following medication withdrawal, headaches may worsen temporarily but improve within weeks to months.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Medication Overuse Headache (MOH)"][/sc]
  • Ask about daily or near-daily headaches – Often persistent and of varying intensity. This occurs due to central sensitisation and neurochemical changes in the brain.
  • Ask about headache upon waking – Common as medication wears off overnight, leading to 'rebound' headaches.
  • Ask about headache worsening with medication use – The very medication intended to relieve headaches triggers and perpetuates them.
  • Ask about restlessness, nausea, or irritability – Due to fluctuating levels of medication in the system and withdrawal effects.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Medication Overuse Headache (MOH)"][/sc]
  • Check no specific neurological signs – Neurological examination is usually normal, helping differentiate MOH from other causes of secondary headaches.
  • Check signs of medication overuse – Patients may carry medications or report frequent use of over-the-counter drugs.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Medication Overuse Headache (MOH)"][/sc]
  • Sudden-onset, severe headache – May indicate a subarachnoid haemorrhage or other life-threatening causes.
  • Neurological deficits – Such as weakness or vision changes, which suggest alternative diagnoses like stroke, brain tumour, or giant cell arteritis.
  • Fever and stiff neck – Suggests meningitis or encephalitis.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Medication Overuse Headache (MOH)"][/sc]
  • Patients denying overuse – Some patients may underreport medication use due to unawareness of its overuse.
  • Headache without 'overuse' amounts – In rare cases, headaches may occur even when medications are not used in typical overuse quantities.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask medication overuse headache (moh).
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Medication Overuse Headache (MOH)"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: No definitive diagnostic tests – MOH is diagnosed clinically based on patient history and the pattern of medication use.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Targeted investigations to confirm diagnosis, assess complications, and monitor response in medication overuse headache (moh).
