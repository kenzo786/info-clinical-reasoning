Pulmonary Hypertension
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Pulmonary Hypertension"][/sc]
  • Pulmonary hypertension (PH) is a pathophysiological disorder characterised by elevated pressure in the pulmonary arteries.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise pulmonary features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.

Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Pulmonary Hypertension"][/sc]
  • PH occurs due to increased pulmonary vascular resistance (PVR), which raises pressure in the pulmonary arteries. This increase in PVR can be due to narrowing of the small pulmonary arteries, vascular remodelling, or obstruction (e.g., due to emboli in chronic thromboembolic PH). In group 2 PH (related to left heart disease), the elevated pressures are secondary to increased left atrial pressure.
  • Over time, elevated pressure in the pulmonary circulation leads to right ventricular hypertrophy and eventual right heart failure, as the right ventricle struggles to pump against the increased resistance.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Pulmonary Hypertension"][/sc]
  • Early Phase: Symptoms may be minimal or absent; patients might report mild shortness of breath or fatigue with exertion.
  • Progressive Phase: Symptoms like dyspnoea, chest pain, and syncope become more pronounced as right ventricular failure develops.
  • Late Phase: Severe right heart failure with symptoms such as oedema, ascites, and significant functional decline.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Pulmonary Hypertension"][/sc]
  • Ask about exertional dyspnoea - Due to increased pulmonary pressures limiting cardiac output during physical activity.
  • Ask about fatigue - Caused by reduced oxygen delivery due to decreased cardiac output.
  • Ask about chest pain - Likely related to right ventricular ischaemia as it struggles to pump against increased pulmonary pressures.
  • Ask about syncope - Occurs during exertion due to reduced cerebral perfusion secondary to low cardiac output.
  • Ask about peripheral oedema - A consequence of right heart failure leading to venous congestion.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Pulmonary Hypertension"][/sc]
  • Check loud P2 (accentuated pulmonary component of the second heart sound) - Reflects increased pressure in the pulmonary artery.
  • Check right ventricular heave - Palpable impulse on the left parasternal border, indicating right ventricular hypertrophy.
  • Check jugular venous distension (JVD) - Due to elevated right atrial pressure from right heart failure.
  • Check peripheral oedema - Caused by systemic venous congestion due to right ventricular failure.
  • Check ascites - Also a result of systemic venous congestion.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Pulmonary Hypertension"][/sc]
  • Syncope - Indicates advanced disease with poor prognosis, as it reflects a severely compromised cardiac output.
  • Worsening dyspnoea - May suggest progression to right heart failure, which carries a high risk of mortality.
  • New onset chest pain - Could signal right ventricular ischaemia or acute worsening of PH due to pulmonary embolism.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Pulmonary Hypertension"][/sc]
  • Patients may present with vague symptoms such as generalised fatigue, which could be misattributed to other causes like deconditioning or depression. Dyspnoea might be subtle or occur only with significant exertion, delaying diagnosis.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask pulmonary hypertension.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Pulmonary Hypertension"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Right heart catheterisation - Gold standard for diagnosing PH, showing mPAP ≥25 mmHg. This reflects increased pressure in the pulmonary circulation.
  • Targeted and monitoring: CT pulmonary angiography - To exclude chronic thromboembolic pulmonary hypertension (CTEPH) by visualising pulmonary arteries; Pulmonary function tests (PFTs) - Help.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Pulmonary Hypertension"][/sc]
  • Left heart failure - Typically presents with crackles on lung auscultation and evidence of pulmonary oedema on imaging.
  • Chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD) - Distinguish from pulmonary hypertension using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.
  • Interstitial lung disease - Diffuse lung crackles and reduced lung volumes on PFTs help differentiate from PH.
  • Related alternative diagnosis - Distinguish from pulmonary hypertension using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Pulmonary Hypertension"][/sc]
  • Right heart failure - Most common and life-threatening complication, occurring due to the increased pressure load on the right ventricle.
  • Arrhythmias - Atrial fibrillation and other arrhythmias can occur due to right atrial dilatation and strain.
  • Thromboembolism - Both venous and pulmonary thromboembolism can complicate PH, particularly in patients with underlying conditions like CTEPH.
