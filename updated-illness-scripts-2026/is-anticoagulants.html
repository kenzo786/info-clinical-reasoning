Anticoagulants
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Anticoagulants"][/sc]
  • Anticoagulants are a class of medications that reduce the blood's ability to form clots, aiming to prevent or manage thromboembolic events.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise anticoagulants features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Anticoagulants"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Anticoagulants"][/sc]
  • Risk factors: Risk of anticoagulants rises with relevant predisposition, comorbidity, and exposure profile.
  • Higher-risk or missed groups: Anticoagulants may be missed when presentations are atypical or comorbidity obscures key features.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Anticoagulants"][/sc]
  • Anticoagulants work by targeting various components of the coagulation cascade to inhibit clot formation. For instance:
  • Warfarin: Inhibits vitamin K-dependent clotting factors (II, VII, IX, X).
  • Direct oral anticoagulants (DOACs): Apixaban and rivaroxaban inhibit factor Xa, while dabigatran inhibits thrombin (factor IIa).
  • Heparins: Enhance antithrombin III activity, inhibiting thrombin and factor Xa.
  • The reduction in clotting factor activity decreases thrombin generation and fibrin clot formation, reducing thrombus extension or new clot formation.
    • Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Anticoagulants"][/sc]
    • Immediate effect: Heparins and DOACs start acting within hours.
    • Delayed onset: Warfarin requires 3-5 days to achieve full anticoagulation due to inhibition of existing clotting factor synthesis.
    • Duration of action varies by agent, from hours (unfractionated heparin) to days (warfarin).
    Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Anticoagulants"][/sc]
    • Ask about bleeding: Most common, ranging from minor (bruising, gum bleeding) to severe (gastrointestinal or intracranial haemorrhage). Caused by reduced clot formation capacity.
    • Ask about haematoma formation: Localised bleeding into tissues, often due to trauma exacerbated by anticoagulation.
    • Ask about signs of anaemia: Fatigue, pallor, and dyspnoea, secondary to blood loss.
    Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Anticoagulants"][/sc]
    • Check bruising: Easily visible, often larger or more frequent than normal due to impaired haemostasis.
    • Check petechiae: Small pinpoint haemorrhages, often indicating platelet dysfunction or very thin clotting reserve.
    • Check hypotension: In severe bleeding, hypovolaemia can lead to shock.
    Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Anticoagulants"][/sc]
    • Severe headache: Suggestive of intracranial haemorrhage.
    • Abdominal pain with distension: May indicate retroperitoneal or gastrointestinal bleeding.
    • Black or tarry stools (melaena): Indicates gastrointestinal bleeding.
    Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Anticoagulants"][/sc]
    • Minor bleeding in unusual locations, such as subconjunctival haemorrhages.
    • Silent anaemia due to chronic low-level bleeding, leading to nonspecific fatigue and pallor.
    • How it gets missed: atypical features or comorbidity can mask the underlying diagnosis.
    Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Anticoagulants"][/sc]
    • Baseline and bedside: Prothrombin Time (PT)/INR: Elevated in warfarin use.
    • Targeted and monitoring: Anti-factor Xa level: Monitors efficacy of low molecular weight heparin and some DOACs; Complete Blood Count (CBC): Identifies anaemia and thrombocytopaenia.
    Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Anticoagulants"][/sc]
    • A 72-year-old male on warfarin for atrial fibrillation presents with fatigue, dark stools, and a prolonged INR, consistent with gastrointestinal bleeding.
    • Adult with symptom pattern and objective findings suggestive of anticoagulants, requiring focused confirmation and risk stratification.
    Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Anticoagulants"][/sc]
    • Thrombocytopaenia - Distinguished by reduced platelets on CBC.
    • Coagulopathy from liver disease - Elevated PT and aPTT, with abnormal liver function tests.
    • Vitamin K deficiency - Prolonged PT, resolves with supplementation.
    • Alternative serious diagnosis - Distinguishing history, examination, and targeted investigations do not support anticoagulants.
    Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Anticoagulants"][/sc]
    • Immediate priorities: Assess physiological stability and immediate high-risk complications at presentation, with prompt stabilisation where required.
    • Core care: Provide condition-specific management for anticoagulants, treat underlying drivers, and coordinate multidisciplinary care.
    • Monitoring and follow-up: Reassess symptoms, objective markers, and functional impact over time to confirm response and detect complications early.
    • Escalation and safety-netting: Urgent reassessment is required for worsening symptoms, physiological deterioration, or failure to improve with initial management of anticoagulants.
    Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Anticoagulants"][/sc]
    • Intracranial haemorrhage: Life-threatening, requires rapid reversal of anticoagulation.
    • Massive gastrointestinal bleeding: Can lead to hypovolaemic shock and organ dysfunction.
    • Heparin-induced thrombocytopaenia (HIT): Rare but serious complication of heparin therapy.
