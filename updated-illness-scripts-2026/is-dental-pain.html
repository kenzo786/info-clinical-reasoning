Dental Pain
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Dental Pain"][/sc]
  • Dental pain, also known as odontalgia, refers to discomfort or pain originating from the teeth or surrounding structures (periodontium).
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise dental features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.

Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Dental Pain"][/sc]
  • The pathophysiology of dental pain largely depends on the underlying cause:
  • Caries: Bacterial infiltration of enamel and dentin leads to demineralisation and exposure of the dental pulp, resulting in inflammatory responses and pain.
  • Pulpitis: Inflammation of the dental pulp due to infection or trauma causes increased pressure in the confined pulp chamber, leading to nerve irritation and pain.
  • Periodontitis: Inflammation of the periodontal ligament and alveolar bone due to bacterial infection results in pain, often associated with tooth mobility.
  • Trauma: Direct injury to the tooth or supporting structures can damage the nerve or blood supply, leading to pain and sensitivity.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Dental Pain"][/sc]
  • Acute: Sudden onset, often linked to infection (e.g., acute pulpitis, abscess). The pain may be sharp, severe, and exacerbated by thermal stimuli or chewing.
  • Chronic: Long-standing pain that may fluctuate in intensity, often due to underlying chronic conditions such as periodontitis or unresolved caries.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Dental Pain"][/sc]
  • Ask about pain on chewing or biting - Due to pressure on the inflamed periodontal or pulp tissue, commonly seen in dental caries or cracked teeth.
  • Ask about sensitivity to hot or cold - A result of exposed dentin or pulp from caries or enamel erosion, which irritates the dental nerve.
  • Ask about constant throbbing pain - Indicative of pulpitis or dental abscess, where inflammation leads to continuous nerve irritation.
  • Ask about swelling or tenderness of the gums - Often associated with periodontitis or abscess formation, due to the localised infection and inflammation.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Dental Pain"][/sc]
  • Check localised tenderness on palpation - Suggests inflammation or infection in the affected tooth or surrounding structures.
  • Check visible dental caries or fractured tooth - Common cause of pain, seen on inspection as darkened, decayed areas or cracks in the enamel.
  • Check swollen, erythematous gums - Indicates gingivitis or periodontitis, often associated with localised infection or plaque buildup.
  • Check fistula or draining abscess - A sign of severe infection where pus has created a sinus tract to the surface, often with associated swelling.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Dental Pain"][/sc]
  • Facial swelling - May indicate spread of infection beyond the tooth into surrounding tissues (e.g., cellulitis or Ludwig’s angina), which can compromise airway patency.
  • Trismus (difficulty opening mouth) - Can signify deep space infection (e.g., peritonsillar abscess) or involvement of the muscles of mastication.
  • Fever - Suggests systemic involvement of infection, indicating a possible dental abscess or spreading odontogenic infection.
  • Severe, unremitting pain - May point to severe infection, malignancy, or non-dental causes like trigeminal neuralgia.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Dental Pain"][/sc]
  • Referred pain to the jaw or ear - Dental pain can present atypically as referred pain due to shared nerve pathways between the teeth and jaw or ear (trigeminal nerve).
  • Non-localised pain - In some cases, especially with early infection or trauma, pain may not localise to a specific tooth, making diagnosis challenging.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask dental pain.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Dental Pain"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Focused bedside assessment and first-line testing to assess severity and immediate risk in suspected dental pain.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Pulp vitality testing (cold test) - Helps assess pulp health; a non-responsive tooth may indicate necrosis, whereas an exaggerated response may.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Dental Pain"][/sc]
  • Trigeminal neuralgia - Sudden, intense facial pain, often triggered by minor stimuli, differs from the more continuous.
  • Sinusitis - Distinguish from dental pain using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Dental Pain"][/sc]
  • Dental abscess: If untreated, dental infections can progress to an abscess, which may require drainage and antibiotics.
  • Osteomyelitis of the jaw: A rare but severe infection of the jawbone, often requiring surgical debridement and prolonged antibiotic therapy.
  • Systemic spread of infection: In cases of severe odontogenic infection, there is a risk of sepsis, cellulitis, or life-threatening conditions such as Ludwig’s angina.
