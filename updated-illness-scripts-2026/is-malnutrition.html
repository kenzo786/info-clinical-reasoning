Malnutrition
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Malnutrition"][/sc]
  • Malnutrition refers to an imbalance between nutrient intake and the body's requirements, leading to either undernutrition (inadequate intake of energy and nutrients) or overnutrition (excessive intake).
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise malnutrition features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Malnutrition"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Malnutrition"][/sc]
  • Risk factors: Risk factors for malnutrition include relevant predisposition, comorbidity, and exposure history.
  • Higher-risk or missed groups: Malnutrition may be missed when presentation is atypical or obscured by comorbidity.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Malnutrition"][/sc]
  • Malnutrition develops when there is a chronic imbalance between nutrient intake and the body’s demands. In undernutrition, insufficient intake of calories, proteins, and essential vitamins leads to catabolism of fat stores and muscle mass. This triggers metabolic, hormonal, and immune dysfunctions, reducing muscle strength, impairing wound healing, and increasing vulnerability to infection. In overnutrition, excessive intake of nutrients, especially fats and sugars, leads to obesity, metabolic syndrome, and cardiovascular complications.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Malnutrition"][/sc]
  • Acute phase: Occurs over days to weeks, often triggered by illness or injury, leading to rapid weight loss and nutrient depletion.
  • Chronic phase: Develops over months or years due to gradual inadequacy in dietary intake, often exacerbated by chronic diseases like cancer or COPD. Symptoms may be subtle initially but worsen over time, leading to progressive weight loss and muscle wasting.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Malnutrition"][/sc]
  • Ask about unintentional weight loss - Occurs due to a deficit in caloric intake and increased catabolism of fat and muscle.
  • Ask about fatigue and weakness - A result of decreased energy stores and muscle wasting.
  • Ask about loss of appetite - Often due to underlying conditions such as infections, cancer, or gastrointestinal issues that reduce the desire or ability to eat.
  • Ask about reduced immune function (frequent infections) - Malnutrition impairs the immune response due to protein-energy deficiencies, making individuals more susceptible to infections.
  • Ask about cold intolerance - Loss of fat stores leads to decreased insulation and thermoregulation.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Malnutrition"][/sc]
  • Check muscle wasting - Visible loss of muscle mass, particularly in the temples, shoulders, and limbs, due to protein catabolism.
  • Check oedema - Peripheral oedema, especially in the ankles, due to low serum protein levels causing decreased oncotic pressure.
  • Check dry, flaky skin - Indicative of deficiencies in essential fatty acids and vitamins such as vitamin, with associated clinical features.
  • Check brittle hair or hair loss - Results from protein and micronutrient deficiencies.
  • Check glossitis (swollen, inflamed tongue) - Suggestive of vitamin B deficiencies, particularly B12 and folate.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Malnutrition"][/sc]
  • Severe weight loss (>10% body weight in 3-6 months) - Indicates significant nutritional deficiency, often seen in advanced disease or acute severe illness (e.g., cancer cachexia).
  • Recurrent or severe infections - Suggests immune compromise due to protein-energy malnutrition, increasing the risk of complications like sepsis.
  • Altered mental status or confusion - May indicate severe vitamin or electrolyte deficiencies (e.g., thiamine deficiency in Wernicke’s encephalopathy).
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Malnutrition"][/sc]
  • Obesity with micronutrient deficiency - Individuals may have an excess intake of calories but remain deficient in essential vitamins and minerals, particularly in overconsumption of processed foods.
  • Subtle or no weight loss in early chronic disease - Especially in elderly patients or those with chronic illness, weight loss may be gradual or absent initially, masking malnutrition.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask malnutrition.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Malnutrition"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Body mass index (BMI) - A BMI <18.5 kg/m² suggests undernutrition. Severe malnutrition is classified as BMI <16 kg/m²; Serum albumin.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Prealbumin (transthyretin) - A more sensitive marker of acute malnutrition, as its half-life is shorter than albumin; Serum electrolytes - Electrolyte.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Malnutrition"][/sc]
  • Cachexia - Differs from malnutrition by being driven by systemic inflammation (e.g., cancer) with marked muscle.
  • Anorexia nervosa - Psychological disorder with extreme food restriction leading to severe undernutrition, often with distinct psychological.
  • Chronic gastrointestinal disease (e.g., Crohn's disease) - Malabsorption of nutrients due to intestinal inflammation, presenting with weight loss, diarrhoea, and anaemia.
  • Related alternative diagnosis - Distinguish from malnutrition using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Malnutrition"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Assess severity, physiological stability, and immediate complications.
  • Core care: Deliver condition-specific management for malnutrition, treat underlying drivers, and coordinate appropriate specialist input.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Reassess symptom trajectory, examination findings, and objective markers to confirm response in malnutrition.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Escalate urgently for clinical deterioration, evolving red flags, or failure to improve in suspected malnutrition.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Malnutrition"][/sc]
  • Infection risk - Frequent infections occur due to impaired immune response, potentially leading to sepsis in severe cases.
  • Refeeding syndrome - Rapid reintroduction of nutrition can cause severe electrolyte disturbances (e.g., hypophosphataemia), leading to arrhythmias and multi-organ failure.
  • Pressure ulcers - Malnourished patients are at increased risk of skin breakdown and pressure ulcers due to immobility and poor tissue repair.
