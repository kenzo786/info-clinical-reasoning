Aortic Aneurysm
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Aortic Aneurysm"][/sc]
  • An aortic aneurysm is a pathological dilation of the aorta, typically greater than 1.5 times its normal diameter.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise aortic features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.

Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Aortic Aneurysm"][/sc]
  • The development of an aortic aneurysm results from a combination of genetic, environmental, and mechanical factors. Chronic inflammation and degeneration of the aortic wall’s elastic fibres lead to a weakened vessel wall. This is typically due to risk factors such as atherosclerosis, hypertension, and smoking, which promote mechanical stress and inflammatory changes. As the vessel wall weakens, the aorta dilates, creating an aneurysm. Over time, this dilation increases the risk of rupture due to high-pressure blood flow, especially if the aneurysm exceeds 5.5 cm in diameter.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Aortic Aneurysm"][/sc]
  • Aortic aneurysms typically develop and enlarge slowly over many years, often asymptomatically. Small aneurysms (<5.5 cm) may remain stable for years, while larger aneurysms grow more rapidly and are at higher risk of rupture. The average rate of growth for AAA is 2-3 mm/year. Rupture, when it occurs, is often sudden and catastrophic.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Aortic Aneurysm"][/sc]
  • Ask about asymptomatic - Most aortic aneurysms are asymptomatic and detected incidentally during imaging for unrelated reasons.
  • Ask about abdominal pain (AAA) - Diffuse or focal, often in the lower back or flank. The pain arises from the mechanical stretching of the aneurysm.
  • Ask about chest pain (TAA) - Common with thoracic aneurysms. Pain is often described as deep, constant, and may radiate to the back. It reflects the in this clinical context.
  • Ask about pulsatile abdominal mass (AAA) - Sometimes described by patients, this may be felt as a throbbing sensation in the abdomen.
  • Ask about hoarseness (TAA) - Pressure on the recurrent laryngeal nerve can cause a change in voice.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Aortic Aneurysm"][/sc]
  • Check pulsatile abdominal mass (AAA) - Palpable pulsation in the abdomen, typically above the umbilicus. This reflects the large, dilated aorta.
  • Check hypotension (ruptured AAA) - Due to massive internal haemorrhage, a drop in blood pressure is a sign of aneurysmal rupture.
  • Check grey Turner’s sign - Flank ecchymosis indicating retroperitoneal haemorrhage from a ruptured AAA.
  • Check distal embolisation - Signs of emboli (e.g., blue toes or sudden limb ischaemia) may arise from thrombus in the aneurysm sac breaking off and in this clinical context.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Aortic Aneurysm"][/sc]
  • Severe, sudden abdominal or back pain - Suggests impending or actual rupture of an AAA, an emergency condition requiring immediate intervention.
  • Hypotension with abdominal pain - Indicates aneurysmal rupture, leading to massive haemorrhage and shock.
  • Syncope - May indicate severe blood loss from rupture.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Aortic Aneurysm"][/sc]
  • Renal colic-like pain - A leaking AAA can irritate the retroperitoneum, mimicking symptoms of kidney stones.
  • Heart failure - A large thoracic aneurysm may compress the heart or great vessels, leading to symptoms resembling heart failure.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features or comorbidity can mask the underlying diagnosis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Aortic Aneurysm"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Abdominal Ultrasound (AAA) - Initial screening test. A dilated aorta ≥3 cm confirms the presence of an aneurysm; CT Angiography (CTA).
  • Targeted and monitoring: Targeted investigations to confirm diagnosis, assess complications, and monitor response in aortic aneurysm.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Aortic Aneurysm"][/sc]
  • A 70-year-old male presents with sudden-onset severe back pain and hypotension. He has a known 5.5 cm abdominal aortic aneurysm, and his symptoms are consistent with a ruptured aneurysm.
  • Adult with symptom pattern and objective findings suggestive of aortic aneurysm, requiring focused confirmation and risk stratification.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Aortic Aneurysm"][/sc]
  • Renal colic - May present with flank pain, but lacks the pulsatile mass or cardiovascular collapse seen.
  • Acute myocardial infarction - Can cause severe chest or abdominal pain, but does not typically feature the pulsatile.
  • Gastrointestinal perforation - Presents with acute abdomen and hypotension but no pulsatile mass or back pain radiating.
  • Alternative serious diagnosis - Distinguishing history, examination, and targeted investigations do not support aortic aneurysm.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Aortic Aneurysm"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Assess physiological stability and urgent complications at presentation, and initiate early stabilisation where required.
  • Core care: Deliver condition-specific treatment for aortic aneurysm, address underlying drivers, and involve appropriate specialist support.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Reassess symptom trajectory, objective markers, and treatment response over time to detect complications early.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Worsening symptoms, physiological deterioration, or failure to improve should trigger urgent reassessment for severe or alternative pathology in aortic aneurysm.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Aortic Aneurysm"][/sc]
  • Rupture - The most severe complication, with high mortality rates (up to 80%). It leads to massive internal haemorrhage.
  • Thromboembolism - Clots within the aneurysm can break off and lead to distal embolic events, such as stroke or limb ischaemia.
  • Infection - Rare but can occur, particularly with prosthetic grafts used in repair. Known as mycotic aneurysm.
