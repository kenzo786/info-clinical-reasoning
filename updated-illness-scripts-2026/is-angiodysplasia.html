Angiodysplasia
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Angiodysplasia"][/sc]
  • Angiodysplasia is a vascular malformation of the gastrointestinal (GI) tract, characterised by dilated and fragile blood vessels that can lead to chronic or recurrent gastrointestinal bleeding.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise angiodysplasia features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Angiodysplasia"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Angiodysplasia"][/sc]
  • Risk factors: Risk of angiodysplasia rises with relevant predisposition, comorbidity, and exposure profile.
  • Higher-risk or missed groups: Angiodysplasia may be missed when presentations are atypical or comorbidity obscures key features.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Angiodysplasia"][/sc]
  • Angiodysplasia arises due to intermittent low-grade obstruction of submucosal veins in the gastrointestinal tract. This leads to dilatation and eventual formation of abnormal, fragile blood vessels.
  • The chronic low oxygen state, often exacerbated by ageing or comorbid conditions (e.g., heart failure, aortic stenosis), promotes neovascularisation (formation of new, fragile vessels), which can rupture, causing GI bleeding.
  • The condition is particularly common in the caecum and ascending colon, where the largest concentration of these malformations is often found.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Angiodysplasia"][/sc]
  • Chronic course: Angiodysplasia often presents with recurrent, chronic GI bleeding over months to years, resulting in iron-deficiency anaemia.
  • Acute exacerbations: Some patients may present with episodes of significant GI bleeding, though life-threatening haemorrhage is rare.
  • Asymptomatic cases: Many patients may have subclinical disease, which is incidentally discovered during endoscopy or imaging for other reasons.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Angiodysplasia"][/sc]
  • Ask about occult gastrointestinal bleeding - Often presents with iron-deficiency anaemia without overt signs of blood loss. This occurs due to slow, intermittent bleeding from fragile.
  • Ask about haematochezia - Fresh blood in the stool, typically a sign of lower GI bleeding, which may occur with more substantial bleeding from angiodysplasia lesions.
  • Ask about fatigue and pallor - Symptoms related to chronic anaemia caused by long-standing blood loss.
  • Ask about melena - Black, tarry stools due to digested blood, often seen in angiodysplasia of the upper GI tract or more proximal lesions.
  • Ask about asymptomatic - In some cases, angiodysplasia is asymptomatic and discovered incidentally during colonoscopy or imaging for other conditions.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Angiodysplasia"][/sc]
  • Check pallor - A common sign of chronic anaemia caused by ongoing blood loss from angiodysplasia.
  • Check tachycardia - Reflects compensatory mechanisms in response to blood loss and anaemia.
  • Check hypotension - A sign of more significant acute blood loss, though rare, and typically seen in more severe cases.
  • Check positive faecal occult blood test (FOBT) - Can indicate the presence of occult GI bleeding.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Angiodysplasia"][/sc]
  • Severe haematochezia or melena with hypotension - May signify large-volume bleeding and risk of haemodynamic instability.
  • Refractory anaemia despite iron therapy - Suggests ongoing occult blood loss that is not being managed effectively and may necessitate endoscopic evaluation.
  • Syncope or near-syncope - Indicates significant blood loss with hypovolaemia, requiring urgent evaluation.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Angiodysplasia"][/sc]
  • Asymptomatic presentation is common, particularly in elderly patients undergoing colonoscopy for other reasons.
  • Patients with aortic stenosis may have a syndrome called Heyde’s syndrome, where angiodysplasia and aortic stenosis coexist, increasing the risk of bleeding.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features or comorbidity can mask the underlying diagnosis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Angiodysplasia"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Focused bedside assessment and first-line investigations to define immediate risk in suspected angiodysplasia.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Capsule endoscopy - Used when colonoscopy is non-diagnostic, particularly to evaluate the small bowel; Faecal occult blood test (FOBT) - May.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Angiodysplasia"][/sc]
  • A 75-year-old woman presents with recurrent painless lower gastrointestinal bleeding and iron-deficiency anaemia, with colonic vascular ectasia seen on endoscopy.
  • Adult with symptom pattern and objective findings suggestive of angiodysplasia, requiring focused confirmation and risk stratification.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Angiodysplasia"][/sc]
  • Diverticular disease - Unlike angiodysplasia, diverticular bleeding is often more acute and massive, usually associated with known.
  • Colorectal cancer - Colorectal malignancy presents with GI bleeding but often with associated weight loss and change.
  • Haemorrhoids - Differentiate using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.
  • Alternative serious diagnosis - Distinguishing history, examination, and targeted investigations do not support angiodysplasia.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Angiodysplasia"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Assess physiological stability and immediate high-risk complications at presentation, with prompt stabilisation where required.
  • Core care: Provide condition-specific management for angiodysplasia, treat underlying drivers, and coordinate multidisciplinary care.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Reassess symptoms, objective markers, and functional impact over time to confirm response and detect complications early.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Urgent reassessment is required for worsening symptoms, physiological deterioration, or failure to improve with initial management of angiodysplasia.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Angiodysplasia"][/sc]
  • Recurrent GI bleeding - This is the most common complication, leading to chronic anaemia and the need for repeated interventions.
  • Iron-deficiency anaemia - Chronic blood loss results in anaemia, necessitating long-term iron supplementation and monitoring.
  • Hypovolaemic shock - Rare but can occur in the setting of massive bleeding, particularly in older patients with comorbid conditions.
