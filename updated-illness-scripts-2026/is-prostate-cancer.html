Prostate Cancer
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Prostate Cancer"][/sc]
  • Prostate cancer is a malignancy originating in the prostate gland, commonly characterised by the uncontrolled growth of abnormal glandular cells.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise prostate features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.

Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Prostate Cancer"][/sc]
  • Prostate cancer generally originates from glandular epithelial cells in the prostate, typically in the peripheral zone.
  • Mutations in oncogenes and tumour suppressor genes (e.g., PTEN, TP53) lead to dysregulated cell proliferation.
  • Growth is often driven by androgens, which bind to androgen receptors and stimulate tumour growth. Advanced stages may become androgen-independent.
  • As the tumour progresses, it can invade local structures, spread to pelvic lymph nodes, and metastasise to distant sites, primarily bones and lymph nodes.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Prostate Cancer"][/sc]
  • Early Stage: Often asymptomatic, slow-growing, may remain undiagnosed for years.
  • Localised Disease: Growth within the prostate gland, with potential urinary symptoms or incidental detection through screening.
  • Locally Advanced Disease: Spread to surrounding tissues, increasing urinary and systemic symptoms.
  • Advanced/Metastatic Stage: Metastasis to bones and other organs; prominent systemic symptoms and pain.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Prostate Cancer"][/sc]
  • Ask about asymptomatic in Early Stages - Commonly asymptomatic in localised stages, detected incidentally through screening.
  • Ask about urinary Hesitancy/Weak Stream - Due to prostate enlargement compressing the urethra.
  • Ask about nocturia - Increased frequency of urination at night from bladder outlet obstruction.
  • Ask about haematuria - Prostate gland invasion causing bleeding into the urinary tract.
  • Ask about bone Pain (in metastatic disease) - Often due to metastases to the pelvis, spine, or femur causing pain and sometimes fractures.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Prostate Cancer"][/sc]
  • Check prostate Enlargement on Digital Rectal Exam (DRE) - The prostate may feel asymmetrically enlarged, nodular, or firm, suggesting malignancy.
  • Check cachexia in Advanced Disease - Weight loss due to advanced malignancy and systemic involvement.
  • Check bone Tenderness - Painful areas upon palpation, indicating bony metastasis.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Prostate Cancer"][/sc]
  • New-Onset Bone Pain - Indicates possible bony metastasis, commonly seen in the spine, pelvis, or ribs.
  • Unintentional Weight Loss - May suggest systemic disease spread or aggressive cancer subtype.
  • Lower Limb Weakness or Paraesthesia - Could signal spinal cord compression due to vertebral metastases.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Prostate Cancer"][/sc]
  • Young Patients with Aggressive Cancer - Though rare, younger patients may present with advanced disease and rapid progression.
  • Isolated Bone Pain without Urinary Symptoms - Some patients with metastatic disease may first present with skeletal pain without urinary complaints.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask prostate cancer.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Prostate Cancer"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: PSA Test: Elevated prostate-specific antigen levels (typically >4 ng/mL in prostate cancer); helps in screening and monitoring but may have false.
  • Targeted and monitoring: MRI of Prostate: Multi-parametric MRI assesses tumour location, extent, and invasion; Prostate Biopsy: Core needle biopsy confirms diagnosis with histopathological grading.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Prostate Cancer"][/sc]
  • Metastasis to Bone: Common in advanced disease, often leads to pain and pathological fractures.
  • Spinal Cord Compression: Due to vertebral metastases, causing neurological deficits or paralysis if untreated.
  • Urinary Incontinence: May occur post-treatment (especially post-surgery or radiotherapy).
  • Outlook: Localised prostate cancer has high survival rates, while metastatic prostate cancer has a 5-year survival rate below 30%.
