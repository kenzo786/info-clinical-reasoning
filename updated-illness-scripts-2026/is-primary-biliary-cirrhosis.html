Primary Biliary Cirrhosis (PBC)
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Primary Biliary Cirrhosis (PBC)"][/sc]
  • Primary biliary cirrhosis (PBC), also known as primary biliary cholangitis, is a chronic autoimmune liver disease characterised by the progressive destruction of the small intrahepatic bile ducts, leading to cholestasis.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise biliary features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Primary Biliary Cirrhosis (PBC)"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Primary Biliary Cirrhosis (PBC)"][/sc]
  • Predominantly affects women, with a female-to-male ratio of around 9:1.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Primary Biliary Cirrhosis (PBC)"][/sc]
  • Autoimmune mechanism: An immune-mediated attack occurs against the biliary epithelial cells lining the small intrahepatic bile ducts, possibly triggered by environmental factors in genetically predisposed individuals.
  • Role of anti-mitochondrial antibodies (AMAs): Present in 90-95% of PBC patients, these antibodies target mitochondrial enzymes in biliary cells, initiating an inflammatory response.
  • Cholestasis: As small bile ducts are destroyed, bile flow is disrupted (cholestasis), leading to the accumulation of toxic bile acids in the liver, which damages hepatocytes and promotes fibrosis.
  • Progression to fibrosis and cirrhosis: Chronic cholestasis promotes liver fibrosis and, eventually, cirrhosis with portal hypertension.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Primary Biliary Cirrhosis (PBC)"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Often asymptomatic, or presents with mild symptoms such as fatigue and pruritus. Disease progression can be gradual, spanning years to decades.
  • Progressive phase: Development of more specific liver-related symptoms, with worsening pruritus and potential signs of cholestasis.
  • Advanced phase: Cirrhosis develops, leading to hepatic decompensation and complications, such as portal hypertension and liver failure.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Primary Biliary Cirrhosis (PBC)"][/sc]
  • Ask about fatigue - Common and can be severe; likely due to systemic inflammation and cholestasis impacting energy metabolism.
  • Ask about pruritus - Widespread itching often worsens at night; caused by bile acid accumulation in the skin due to cholestasis.
  • Ask about right upper quadrant discomfort - Mild pain due to hepatic inflammation or hepatomegaly.
  • Ask about jaundice - Late symptom, indicates significant cholestasis or cirrhosis with hyperbilirubinaemia.
  • Ask about dry eyes and mouth (sicca syndrome) - Autoimmune-related symptom, commonly associated with PBC.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Primary Biliary Cirrhosis (PBC)"][/sc]
  • Check hepatomegaly - Palpable liver enlargement, typically due to inflammation and fibrosis.
  • Check hyperpigmentation - Darkening of the skin, particularly over areas exposed to sunlight, due to chronic cholestasis and vitamin D deficiency.
  • Check excoriations - Skin scratches due to pruritus.
  • Check jaundice - Yellowing of the skin and sclerae, an indicator of significant liver dysfunction.
  • Check signs of cirrhosis (ascites, spider naevi) - Indicate advanced disease with portal hypertension and liver decompensation.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Primary Biliary Cirrhosis (PBC)"][/sc]
  • Jaundice onset - Sudden jaundice may indicate hepatic decompensation and advanced cirrhosis.
  • Ascites - Suggests portal hypertension due to cirrhosis, increasing risk for spontaneous bacterial peritonitis.
  • Hepatic encephalopathy - Altered mental status suggests liver failure with inability to detoxify ammonia.
  • Weight loss and fatigue - Can indicate progression to liver failure or hepatocellular carcinoma.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Primary Biliary Cirrhosis (PBC)"][/sc]
  • Asymptomatic presentation: Many patients are diagnosed during routine testing due to elevated liver enzymes.
  • Non-specific systemic symptoms: Including arthralgia and myalgia, which may be mistaken for other autoimmune conditions.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask primary biliary cirrhosis (pbc).
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Primary Biliary Cirrhosis (PBC)"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Liver function tests (LFTs): Elevated alkaline phosphatase (ALP) and gamma-glutamyl transferase (GGT) are common, reflecting cholestasis. Bilirubin may rise in advanced.
  • Targeted and monitoring: IgM levels: Often elevated, indicative of autoimmune disease; Ultrasound: May show hepatomegaly but usually rules out other causes of cholestasis; Liver.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Primary Biliary Cirrhosis (PBC)"][/sc]
  • Primary sclerosing cholangitis (PSC) - Differentiated by larger bile duct involvement and association with inflammatory bowel disease; AMA usually.
  • Autoimmune hepatitis (AIH) - Similar autoimmune nature, but ANA or SMA may be positive, and ALT/AST elevation predominates.
  • Non-alcoholic fatty liver disease (NAFLD) - Presents with elevated liver enzymes but lacks AMA positivity and typically presents with metabolic.
  • Cholestatic drug reaction - Identifiable through drug history; symptoms and liver tests resolve upon discontinuation.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Primary Biliary Cirrhosis (PBC)"][/sc]
  • Cirrhosis and liver failure: Due to progressive fibrosis and loss of liver function; common in advanced disease.
  • Portal hypertension: Secondary to cirrhosis, leading to ascites, varices, and splenomegaly.
  • Osteoporosis: Due to chronic cholestasis and vitamin D malabsorption.
  • Hepatocellular carcinoma: Increased risk in those with cirrhosis, though rare in PBC compared to other cirrhotic conditions.
