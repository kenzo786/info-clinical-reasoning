Trauma-Induced Mouth Pain
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Trauma-Induced Mouth Pain"][/sc]
  • Trauma-induced mouth pain refers to acute or chronic pain in the oral cavity resulting from direct injury to the tissues, teeth, or bones of the mouth.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise trauma features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.

Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Trauma-Induced Mouth Pain"][/sc]
  • The injury leads to direct damage to the mucosal lining, teeth, or bones, causing inflammation, pain, and sometimes disruption of nerve endings. This inflammatory response triggers swelling, redness, and potential disruption of oral function. In cases of severe trauma, bones may fracture or teeth may dislodge, leading to further complications like infection or tissue necrosis.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Trauma-Induced Mouth Pain"][/sc]
  • Immediately post-trauma: Acute pain, swelling, and possible bleeding. The severity of symptoms depends on the extent of injury.
  • 1-3 days post-trauma: Swelling and inflammation peak. If the injury is minor, healing begins, with pain subsiding after this period. Severe injuries may show signs of infection.
  • 7-10 days post-trauma: Most soft tissue injuries begin healing with pain and inflammation gradually reducing.
  • Chronic phase: In cases of severe or untreated trauma, chronic pain, non-healing wounds, or complications like abscesses may develop.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Trauma-Induced Mouth Pain"][/sc]
  • Ask about pain - Acute, localised pain due to nerve irritation and inflammation.
  • Ask about swelling - Inflammatory response to tissue damage leading to oedema in the affected area.
  • Ask about bleeding - Immediate after trauma due to ruptured capillaries in soft tissues.
  • Ask about difficulty chewing - Pain and swelling may impair normal oral function.
  • Ask about tooth sensitivity or fracture - Dental trauma can lead to exposed dentin or fractures, resulting in heightened sensitivity or sharp pain.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Trauma-Induced Mouth Pain"][/sc]
  • Check swelling of oral mucosa - Visible oedema as a result of inflammation from trauma.
  • Check ecchymosis or bruising - Submucosal bleeding manifesting as purple or blue discolouration of oral tissues.
  • Check loose or fractured teeth - Physical signs of dental injury often visible or detected upon examination.
  • Check bleeding - Fresh blood or dried clots may be seen, depending on the timing of the trauma.
  • Check restricted jaw movement - In cases of mandibular trauma or severe swelling, jaw movement may be impaired.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Trauma-Induced Mouth Pain"][/sc]
  • Persistent or worsening pain - May indicate infection, abscess formation, or deeper tissue injury like a fracture.
  • Systemic signs of infection - Fever, malaise, or lymphadenopathy suggest a secondary infection, which can progress to cellulitis or sepsis.
  • Numbness or paraesthesia - Could suggest nerve damage, particularly the inferior alveolar nerve in mandibular trauma.
  • Difficulty breathing or swallowing - May indicate airway compromise or retropharyngeal haematoma, which is a medical emergency.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Trauma-Induced Mouth Pain"][/sc]
  • Chronic, dull pain - Instead of acute pain, some individuals may present with persistent, dull pain from undetected fractures or chronic inflammation.
  • Delayed swelling or infection - In some cases, swelling or signs of infection may occur several days after the initial trauma due to delayed bacterial colonisation.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask trauma-induced mouth pain.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Trauma-Induced Mouth Pain"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Dental X-ray - Used to identify fractures, dislocations, or tooth root damage. A panoramic radiograph may show mandibular fractures.
  • Targeted and monitoring: CT scan - For severe trauma, especially involving the jaw or facial bones, to assess for fractures or deep tissue damage.

  • Temporomandibular joint dysfunction (TMD) - TMD typically presents with jaw pain and clicking, rather than direct oral trauma.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Trauma-Induced Mouth Pain"][/sc]
  • Infection - Trauma can lead to bacterial invasion and abscess formation.
  • Chronic pain - Due to nerve damage or persistent inflammation.
  • Tooth loss - Severe trauma may result in avulsed or non-recoverable teeth.
  • Malocclusion - Resulting from untreated dental or jaw fractures.
