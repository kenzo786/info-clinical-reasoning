Hypoxia
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Hypoxia"][/sc]
  • Hypoxia is a medical condition characterised by insufficient oxygen supply at the tissue level despite adequate blood flow.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise hypoxia features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Hypoxia"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Hypoxia"][/sc]
  • Risk factors: Risk factors for hypoxia include relevant predisposition, comorbidity, and exposure history.
  • Higher-risk or missed groups: Hypoxia may be missed when presentation is atypical or obscured by comorbidity.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Hypoxia"][/sc]
  • Hypoxia occurs when there is a failure in oxygen delivery to tissues, which can happen due to:
    • Reduced oxygen availability (e.g., high altitude, lung diseases reducing gas exchange).
    • Impaired oxygen transport (e.g., anaemia, carbon monoxide poisoning).
    • Impaired perfusion (e.g., shock, heart failure).
    • Cellular utilisation defects (e.g., cyanide poisoning).
    This leads to anaerobic metabolism and lactic acid accumulation, causing acidosis and eventual cellular damage or death.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Hypoxia"][/sc]
  • Hypoxia can develop acutely (e.g., from a pulmonary embolism or acute respiratory distress) or chronically (e.g., in COPD or chronic heart failure). Acute hypoxia typically presents rapidly and can progress within minutes to hours, while chronic hypoxia may develop insidiously over weeks to months.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Hypoxia"][/sc]
  • Ask about dyspnoea - The most common symptom, caused by insufficient oxygen in the bloodstream stimulating respiratory centres.
  • Ask about fatigue - Results from reduced oxygen delivery to tissues, impairing cellular energy production.
  • Ask about confusion - Due to hypoxia affecting brain function, leading to altered mental status or confusion, especially in acute or severe cases.
  • Ask about cyanosis - Bluish discolouration of the skin, typically in the lips or extremities, due to reduced oxygenation of haemoglobin.
  • Ask about chest pain - Often seen in cases of hypoxia related to cardiac or respiratory causes, such as pulmonary embolism.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Hypoxia"][/sc]
  • Check tachypnoea - Increased respiratory rate as the body tries to compensate for low oxygen levels.
  • Check tachycardia - Increased heart rate to improve oxygen delivery to tissues.
  • Check central cyanosis - Bluish discolouration of the central parts of the body (e.g., lips, tongue) indicating severe hypoxia.
  • Check altered mental state - Seen in more severe cases, with confusion or agitation indicating cerebral hypoxia.
  • Check use of accessory muscles - In respiratory distress, patients may use additional muscles (e.g., neck muscles) to breathe, indicating severe respiratory compromise.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Hypoxia"][/sc]
  • Altered consciousness - Suggests severe hypoxia affecting brain function, indicating an urgent need for intervention.
  • Severe cyanosis - Indicates critically low oxygen levels, requiring immediate oxygenation support.
  • Chest pain with hypoxia - May indicate myocardial ischaemia, pulmonary embolism, or another life-threatening condition.
  • Sudden onset of dyspnoea - Can suggest acute life-threatening causes like a pulmonary embolism or pneumothorax.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Hypoxia"][/sc]
  • Silent hypoxia - Common in COVID-19 pneumonia, where patients may have dangerously low oxygen levels but report minimal respiratory discomfort, possibly due to altered central regulation of breathing.
  • Chronic hypoxia - May present with vague symptoms like fatigue, weight loss, and reduced exercise tolerance, which could be mistaken for other chronic conditions.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask hypoxia.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Hypoxia"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Focused bedside assessment and first-line testing to assess severity and immediate risk in suspected hypoxia.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Chest X-ray - To assess for lung pathologies such as pneumonia, pulmonary oedema, or pneumothorax that could cause hypoxia; CT pulmonary.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Hypoxia"][/sc]
  • Pulmonary embolism - Presents with acute hypoxia, tachycardia, and pleuritic chest pain. Differentiated by presence of risk.
  • Pneumonia - Fever, cough, and localised chest signs (crackles) alongside hypoxia. Confirmed with chest X-ray showing.
  • Heart failure - Orthopnoea, peripheral oedema, and pulmonary oedema on imaging, usually with chronic symptoms preceding hypoxia.
  • Anaemia - Hypoxia in the context of pallor, fatigue, and low haemoglobin on blood tests. Hypoxia.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Hypoxia"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Assess severity, physiological stability, and immediate complications.
  • Core care: Deliver condition-specific management for hypoxia, treat underlying drivers, and coordinate appropriate specialist input.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Reassess symptom trajectory, examination findings, and objective markers to confirm response in hypoxia.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Escalate urgently for clinical deterioration, evolving red flags, or failure to improve in suspected hypoxia.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Hypoxia"][/sc]
  • Respiratory failure - Progression of hypoxia to inadequate gas exchange, necessitating ventilatory support.
  • Cardiac arrest - Due to severe hypoxia impairing myocardial oxygenation, potentially leading to arrhythmias or arrest.
  • Organ dysfunction - Prolonged hypoxia can result in multi-organ failure, especially affecting the brain, kidneys, and heart.
