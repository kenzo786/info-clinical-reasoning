Fibroids (Uterine Leiomyomas)
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Fibroids (Uterine Leiomyomas)"][/sc]
  • Fibroids, also known as uterine leiomyomas, are benign smooth muscle tumours of the uterus.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise fibroids features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.

Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Fibroids (Uterine Leiomyomas)"][/sc]
  • Fibroids arise from the smooth muscle layer (myometrium) of the uterus. They are monoclonal tumours, meaning they arise from a single smooth muscle cell that undergoes genetic mutations. The growth of fibroids is influenced by oestrogen and progesterone, which stimulate the proliferation of smooth muscle cells and extracellular matrix deposition.
  • Genetic alterations: Specific mutations in the MED12 gene and HMGA2 gene are commonly associated with fibroid development.
  • Oestrogen and progesterone promote the growth and maintenance of fibroids, explaining why fibroids often shrink after menopause when hormonal levels decline.
  • They may grow slowly or rapidly, depending on factors such as hormonal fluctuations, pregnancy, and genetic predisposition.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Fibroids (Uterine Leiomyomas)"][/sc]
  • Fibroids usually develop during reproductive years and may remain asymptomatic or grow over time. They tend to stabilise or regress after menopause due to reduced hormone levels.
  • In some cases, rapid growth during pregnancy is common, likely due to increased hormone levels.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Fibroids (Uterine Leiomyomas)"][/sc]
  • Ask about menorrhagia (heavy menstrual bleeding) – Common due to distortion of the uterine cavity, leading to an increased surface area of endometrium and disordered vasculature.
  • Ask about pelvic pressure or pain – Due to mass effect from the fibroids, especially if large or multiple. Compression of surrounding structures can lead, with associated clinical features.
  • Ask about urinary frequency or retention – Fibroids pressing on the bladder can cause irritative symptoms like frequency or difficulty emptying the bladder.
  • Ask about constipation – Larger fibroids can compress the rectum, leading to bowel symptoms.
  • Ask about infertility or recurrent miscarriages – Fibroids distorting the uterine cavity can interfere with implantation or fetal growth.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Fibroids (Uterine Leiomyomas)"][/sc]
  • Check enlarged, irregular uterus on bimanual examination – The uterus may feel bulky or irregular in shape due to the fibroids.
  • Check abdominal mass – Large fibroids may be palpable as a mass in the lower abdomen.
  • Check pallor – Anaemia due to chronic menorrhagia can result in pale skin and mucous membranes.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Fibroids (Uterine Leiomyomas)"][/sc]
  • Postmenopausal bleeding – This is a red flag for endometrial carcinoma, which needs to be ruled out as fibroids typically regress after menopause.
  • Rapid growth of fibroids – This could indicate sarcomatous transformation, though rare, it must be considered especially in postmenopausal women.
  • Severe pelvic pain with fever – May indicate degeneration of a fibroid (red degeneration) or secondary infection, both of which require urgent intervention.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Fibroids (Uterine Leiomyomas)"][/sc]
  • Fibroids may rarely present with acute pain due to degeneration or torsion of a pedunculated fibroid.
  • Chronic, unexplained anaemia may be the only clue in cases where menorrhagia is not overtly reported.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask fibroids (uterine leiomyomas).

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Fibroids (Uterine Leiomyomas)"][/sc]
  • Severe anaemia – Due to chronic heavy menstrual bleeding, requiring blood transfusions in extreme cases.
  • Infertility or miscarriage – Especially with submucosal fibroids distorting the uterine cavity.
  • Degeneration – Fibroids may outgrow their blood supply, leading to acute pain and necrosis (red degeneration).
