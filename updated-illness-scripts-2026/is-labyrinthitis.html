Labyrinthitis
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Labyrinthitis"][/sc]
  • Labyrinthitis is an inflammatory condition affecting the labyrinth of the inner ear, typically involving both the cochlea and the vestibular system.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise labyrinthitis features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.

  • Higher-risk or missed groups: Labyrinthitis may be missed when presentation is atypical or obscured by comorbidity.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Labyrinthitis"][/sc]
  • Labyrinthitis results from inflammation of the labyrinth, which contains both the cochlea (responsible for hearing) and the vestibular apparatus (responsible for balance). The inflammation disrupts signal transmission, leading to vertigo and hearing loss. Infections, typically viral (e.g., herpes simplex or respiratory viruses), are the most common cause. Bacterial labyrinthitis is rarer but can occur following otitis media or meningitis.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Labyrinthitis"][/sc]
  • Acute phase: Sudden onset of severe vertigo, nausea, and vomiting, lasting several days.
  • Subacute phase: Gradual improvement in vertigo over weeks, but balance and hearing disturbances may persist.
  • Chronic phase: Residual balance dysfunction or hearing loss may continue for months or, in some cases, become permanent.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Labyrinthitis"][/sc]
  • Ask about vertigo - Sudden onset of spinning sensation, resulting from disruption in the vestibular system.
  • Ask about nausea and vomiting - Occurs due to severe vertigo and the brain’s difficulty in interpreting disordered vestibular signals.
  • Ask about hearing loss - Partial or total, typically in the affected ear, due to inflammation of the cochlea.
  • Ask about tinnitus - Ringing or buzzing sound, explained by damage to the inner ear’s hair cells.
  • Ask about imbalance - Persistent difficulty in maintaining balance, resulting from disrupted vestibular input.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Labyrinthitis"][/sc]
  • Check positive head impulse test - Inability to maintain visual fixation during rapid head movements, indicating vestibular dysfunction.
  • Check spontaneous nystagmus - Rapid, involuntary eye movements observed during physical examination, reflecting imbalance in the vestibular input to the brain.
  • Check hearing impairment - Detected through audiometry or whisper tests, indicating cochlear involvement.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Labyrinthitis"][/sc]
  • Neurological deficits (e.g., facial weakness, dysarthria) - May indicate a central cause, such as stroke, rather than labyrinthitis.
  • Severe headache, neck stiffness - Suggestive of meningitis or a CNS cause.
  • Persistent hearing loss - May indicate permanent damage or a more severe infection.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Labyrinthitis"][/sc]
  • Subtle balance issues without vertigo - In some cases, particularly in older adults, the primary complaint may be unsteadiness rather than overt vertigo.
  • Absence of hearing loss - Viral labyrinthitis can sometimes present without noticeable hearing impairment, making diagnosis more challenging.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask labyrinthitis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Labyrinthitis"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Audiometry - Shows sensorineural hearing loss, indicating cochlear involvement; Head impulse test - Positive test indicates vestibular hypofunction.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Targeted investigations to confirm diagnosis, assess complications, and monitor response in labyrinthitis.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Labyrinthitis"][/sc]
  • Benign paroxysmal positional vertigo (BPPV) - Presents with brief episodes of vertigo triggered by head movements, unlike the continuous vertigo.
  • Vestibular neuritis - Similar vestibular symptoms but without hearing loss, as it only affects the vestibular nerve.
  • Meniere's disease - Distinguish from labyrinthitis using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.
  • Stroke - Central vertigo often presents with other neurological deficits such as weakness or sensory loss.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Labyrinthitis"][/sc]
  • Permanent hearing loss - Occurs in some cases, especially with bacterial labyrinthitis.
  • Chronic imbalance - Some patients may continue to experience balance issues due to persistent vestibular dysfunction.
  • Meningitis - A rare but serious complication of bacterial labyrinthitis if infection spreads to the meninges.
