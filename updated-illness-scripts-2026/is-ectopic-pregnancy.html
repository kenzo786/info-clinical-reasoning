Ectopic Pregnancy
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Ectopic Pregnancy"][/sc]
  • Ectopic pregnancy is a condition in which a fertilised ovum implants outside the uterine cavity, most commonly in the fallopian tube.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise ectopic features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.

Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Ectopic Pregnancy"][/sc]
  • Normally, the fertilised egg travels through the fallopian tube to the uterus. In ectopic pregnancy, an obstruction or impaired tubal function causes implantation within the tube or elsewhere.
  • The most common site is the fallopian tube (95%), followed by the ovary, cervix, or abdominal cavity.
  • The growing embryo stretches the fallopian tube, causing inflammation, pain, and eventually rupture if untreated, leading to haemoperitoneum.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Ectopic Pregnancy"][/sc]
  • Ectopic pregnancy typically becomes symptomatic between 6-10 weeks of gestation. Early symptoms may be subtle but rapidly progress to more severe signs such as pain and haemodynamic instability if rupture occurs.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Ectopic Pregnancy"][/sc]
  • Ask about pelvic pain - The most common symptom, caused by tubal distension or rupture.
  • Ask about amenorrhoea - Missed period is a key clue to pregnancy status.
  • Ask about vaginal bleeding - Light spotting or irregular bleeding due to hormonal dysregulation.
  • Ask about shoulder tip pain - Referred pain from diaphragmatic irritation due to haemoperitoneum in case of rupture.
  • Ask about dizziness or syncope - May occur due to intra-abdominal haemorrhage leading to hypovolaemia.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Ectopic Pregnancy"][/sc]
  • Check adnexal tenderness - Caused by inflammation and distension of the fallopian tube.
  • Check abdominal guarding or rebound tenderness - Signifies peritoneal irritation due to blood leakage.
  • Check hypotension and tachycardia - Occur in cases of rupture, signalling significant blood loss and shock.
  • Check pallor - Due to hypovolaemic shock following tubal rupture.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Ectopic Pregnancy"][/sc]
  • Severe abdominal pain - Signifies possible tubal rupture and requires immediate intervention.
  • Haemodynamic instability (hypotension, tachycardia) - Suggests major haemorrhage and impending shock.
  • Syncope - May indicate significant internal bleeding.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Ectopic Pregnancy"][/sc]
  • Some ectopic pregnancies may present with minimal or no pain and only mild vaginal bleeding, particularly in cases of non-tubal implantation (e.g., cervical or abdominal).
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask ectopic pregnancy.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Ectopic Pregnancy"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Serum β-hCG - Levels are typically lower than expected for gestational age, with slower rise compared to a normal intrauterine pregnancy.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Targeted investigations to confirm diagnosis, assess complications, and monitor response in ectopic pregnancy.

  • Patient with 6 weeks of amenorrhoea, increasing lower abdominal pain, light vaginal bleeding, and dizziness. Her vitals show hypotension and tachycardia, consistent with ectopic pregnancy.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Ectopic Pregnancy"][/sc]
  • Miscarriage - Similar symptoms but often with visible intrauterine pregnancy on ultrasound.
  • Ovarian cyst rupture - Sudden pelvic pain but typically no positive pregnancy test.
  • Pelvic inflammatory disease - Pelvic pain and discharge, but absence of pregnancy and usually gradual onset.
  • Related alternative diagnosis - Distinguish from ectopic pregnancy using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Ectopic Pregnancy"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Assess severity, physiological stability, and immediate complications.
  • Core care: Deliver condition-specific management for ectopic pregnancy, treat underlying drivers, and coordinate appropriate specialist input.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Reassess symptom trajectory, examination findings, and objective markers to confirm response in ectopic pregnancy.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Escalate urgently for clinical deterioration, evolving red flags, or failure to improve in suspected ectopic pregnancy.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Ectopic Pregnancy"][/sc]
  • Tubal rupture - Can lead to life-threatening haemorrhage and hypovolaemic shock.
  • Infertility - Damage to the fallopian tubes increases the risk of future infertility.
  • Recurrence - There is an increased risk of future ectopic pregnancies.
