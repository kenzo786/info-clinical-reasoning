Diabetes
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Diabetes"][/sc]
  • Diabetes mellitus is a group of metabolic disorders characterised by chronic hyperglycaemia resulting from defects in insulin secretion, insulin action, or both.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise diabetes features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Diabetes"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Diabetes"][/sc]
  • Risk factors: Risk factors for diabetes include relevant predisposition, comorbidity, and exposure history.
  • Higher-risk or missed groups: Diabetes may be missed when presentation is atypical or obscured by comorbidity.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Diabetes"][/sc]
  • Type 1 Diabetes: Autoimmune destruction of beta cells in the pancreas leads to absolute insulin deficiency. Triggers may include genetic predisposition and environmental factors (e.g., viral infections).
  • Type 2 Diabetes: Insulin resistance in peripheral tissues (e.g., muscle, liver) causes reduced glucose uptake. Pancreatic beta cells fail to compensate with increased insulin production, leading to hyperglycaemia.
  • Chronic Hyperglycaemia Effects: Glycation of proteins and oxidative stress contribute to microvascular (retinopathy, nephropathy, neuropathy) and macrovascular (ischaemic heart disease, stroke) complications.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Diabetes"][/sc]
  • Type 1 Diabetes: Rapid onset over weeks to months, often in childhood or adolescence.
  • Type 2 Diabetes: Insidious onset, typically over years, often diagnosed in middle-aged or older adults during routine screening or due to complications.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Diabetes"][/sc]
  • Ask about polyuria: Excessive urination due to osmotic diuresis from hyperglycaemia.
  • Ask about polydipsia: Increased thirst triggered by dehydration from osmotic diuresis.
  • Ask about fatigue: Result of reduced glucose uptake by cells due to insulin deficiency or resistance.
  • Ask about unintentional weight loss (Type 1): Catabolism of fat and muscle due to lack of insulin.
  • Ask about blurred vision: Osmotic changes in the lens due to high blood glucose levels.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Diabetes"][/sc]
  • Check acanthosis nigricans: Darkened, velvety skin in folds (common in T2D, linked to insulin resistance).
  • Check fungal infections: Recurrent infections (e.g., candida) due to impaired immunity and glycosuria.
  • Check ketotic breath (Type 1): Fruity-smelling breath from ketone production in diabetic ketoacidosis (DKA).
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Diabetes"][/sc]
  • Diabetic Ketoacidosis (DKA): Vomiting, abdominal pain, rapid breathing. Signifies acute insulin deficiency, leading to high anion gap metabolic acidosis.
  • Hyperosmolar Hyperglycaemic State (HHS): Severe dehydration, altered mental status. Indicates profound hyperglycaemia without ketosis.
  • Severe hypoglycaemia: Confusion, seizures, or unconsciousness. Can result from insulin or medication overdose.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Diabetes"][/sc]
  • Type 2 Diabetes in youth: Increasingly common with obesity; slower progression than Type 1.
  • Latent Autoimmune Diabetes of Adults (LADA): Misdiagnosed as Type 2 due to slower beta cell destruction.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask diabetes.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Diabetes"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: HbA1c: ≥48 mmol/mol (≥6.5%) indicates diabetes. Reflects average glucose over 2-3 months; Fasting plasma glucose: ≥7.0 mmol/L on two occasions.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Random plasma glucose: ≥11.1 mmol/L with symptoms; Oral glucose tolerance test (OGTT): 2-hour glucose ≥11.1 mmol/L.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Diabetes"][/sc]
  • Diabetes insipidus - Presents with polyuria but no hyperglycaemia.
  • Chronic kidney disease - May cause fatigue and weight loss, but with different blood markers.
  • Hypothyroidism - Fatigue and weight gain, not polyuria or hyperglycaemia.
  • Related alternative diagnosis - Distinguish from diabetes using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Diabetes"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Assess severity, physiological stability, and immediate complications.
  • Core care: Deliver condition-specific management for diabetes, treat underlying drivers, and coordinate appropriate specialist input.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Reassess symptom trajectory, examination findings, and objective markers to confirm response in diabetes.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Escalate urgently for clinical deterioration, evolving red flags, or failure to improve in suspected diabetes.
  • Complication Management: Blood pressure and lipid control.
    • Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Diabetes"][/sc]
    • Microvascular: Retinopathy, nephropathy, neuropathy.
    • Macrovascular: Cardiovascular disease, stroke.
    • Infections: Increased susceptibility due to impaired immunity.
