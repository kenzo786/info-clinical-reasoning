Constipation
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Constipation"][/sc]
  • Constipation is defined as infrequent bowel movements (typically less than three per week) or difficulty in passing stools.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise constipation features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Constipation"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Constipation"][/sc]
  • Risk factors: Risk of constipation rises with relevant predisposition, comorbidity, and exposure profile.
  • Higher-risk or missed groups: Constipation may be missed when presentations are atypical or comorbidity obscures key features.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Constipation"][/sc]
  • Constipation occurs due to slowed colonic transit, impaired defecation, or a combination of both. Slowed transit may result from inadequate water absorption, a low-fibre diet, or dysmotility. Impaired defecation could be due to pelvic floor dysfunction or weakened peristalsis. Medications like opioids or conditions such as hypothyroidism may also affect bowel movement through various mechanisms like altering neurotransmitter signalling or fluid regulation in the gut.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Constipation"][/sc]
  • Acute constipation can occur suddenly, often linked to diet changes, medication use, or stress. Chronic constipation develops over weeks to months, and symptoms can persist for longer than three months. Constipation may also alternate with periods of normal bowel function.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Constipation"][/sc]
  • Ask about infrequent bowel movements - Defined as fewer than three bowel movements per week. This occurs due to slowed colonic transit or poor dietary fibre.
  • Ask about hard, lumpy stools - Due to excessive water absorption in the colon, leading to dehydration of stool contents.
  • Ask about straining during defecation - Associated with weakened peristalsis or pelvic floor dysfunction, making bowel movements more difficult.
  • Ask about sensation of incomplete evacuation - May result from impaired rectal sensation or outlet obstruction.
  • Ask about abdominal discomfort - Caused by the accumulation of hard stools or gas in the intestines, leading to bloating and cramps.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Constipation"][/sc]
  • Check abdominal distension - Caused by the accumulation of gas or fecal matter in the intestines.
  • Check palpable mass in the abdomen - May indicate fecal impaction, especially in the left lower quadrant (colon).
  • Check reduced bowel sounds - Suggests reduced peristalsis, a sign of slow colonic transit.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Constipation"][/sc]
  • Unintentional weight loss - Could indicate malignancy or a systemic disease such as hyperthyroidism or coeliac disease.
  • Rectal bleeding - Suggests a more serious underlying condition such as colorectal cancer, diverticulitis, or haemorrhoids.
  • Family history of colorectal cancer - Increases the risk of malignancy, especially in older adults.
  • Severe, persistent abdominal pain - Could indicate bowel obstruction or an inflammatory condition such as diverticulitis.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Constipation"][/sc]
  • Alternating constipation and diarrhoea - May be a sign of irritable bowel syndrome or colorectal cancer, where bowel function becomes irregular.
  • Non-specific systemic symptoms - Fatigue and malaise could be linked to hypothyroidism or other metabolic disorders that affect gut motility.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features or comorbidity can mask the underlying diagnosis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Constipation"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Full blood count (FBC) - May reveal anaemia, which could indicate colorectal cancer or gastrointestinal bleeding; Thyroid function tests - Hypothyroidism.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Colonoscopy - Used to rule out colorectal cancer or structural abnormalities if red flags are present, particularly in patients over 50.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Constipation"][/sc]
  • A 65-year-old female presents with a three-month history of infrequent bowel movements, hard stools, and straining. She reports mild abdominal discomfort but no weight loss or rectal bleeding. Symptoms worsened.
  • Adult with symptom pattern and objective findings suggestive of constipation, requiring focused confirmation and risk stratification.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Constipation"][/sc]
  • Irritable Bowel Syndrome (IBS) - Alternating diarrhoea and constipation, often associated with abdominal pain and bloating. Differs from chronic.
  • Colorectal cancer - Persistent change in bowel habits, unintentional weight loss, rectal bleeding, and anaemia may be.
  • Hypothyroidism - Differentiate from constipation using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.
  • Faecal impaction - Severe constipation leading to a palpable mass and possibly overflow diarrhoea. Abdominal X-ray confirms.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Constipation"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Assess physiological stability and urgent complications at presentation, and initiate early stabilisation where required.
  • Core care: Deliver condition-specific treatment for constipation, address underlying drivers, and involve appropriate specialist support.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Reassess symptom trajectory, objective markers, and treatment response over time to detect complications early.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Worsening symptoms, physiological deterioration, or failure to improve should trigger urgent reassessment for severe or alternative pathology in constipation.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Constipation"][/sc]
  • Faecal impaction: Occurs in severe cases where hard stools accumulate in the colon, leading to obstruction.
  • Haemorrhoids: Straining during defecation can lead to swollen blood vessels in the rectum, causing pain and bleeding.
  • Rectal prolapse: Chronic straining may weaken pelvic muscles, leading to prolapse of rectal tissue through the anus.
  • Colorectal cancer: Chronic constipation has been linked to an increased risk of colorectal cancer, though the exact mechanism is unclear.
