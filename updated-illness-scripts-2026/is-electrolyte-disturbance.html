Electrolyte Disturbance
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Electrolyte Disturbance"][/sc]
  • Electrolyte disturbance refers to an imbalance in the body's essential electrolytes, such as sodium, potassium, calcium, chloride, and magnesium.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise electrolyte features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.

Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Electrolyte Disturbance"][/sc]
  • Electrolyte disturbances occur due to an imbalance in intake, absorption, excretion, or cellular distribution of electrolytes. For example, hypokalaemia may result from increased renal losses (e.g., diuretics), inadequate intake, or intracellular shifting (e.g., insulin). Each electrolyte has specific roles in maintaining cellular membrane potential, neuromuscular excitability, and acid-base balance, and disturbances can lead to systemic effects depending on the electrolyte involved.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Electrolyte Disturbance"][/sc]
  • Electrolyte disturbances can develop acutely, as in the case of fluid shifts or drug effects, or chronically, often seen in long-term conditions like chronic kidney disease or heart failure. The time course can influence symptoms, with rapid changes often producing more severe clinical effects.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Electrolyte Disturbance"][/sc]
  • Ask about muscle weakness - Seen in hypokalaemia or hyperkalaemia due to impaired neuromuscular transmission.
  • Ask about confusion or altered mental status - Common in severe hyponatraemia or hypernatraemia due to cerebral oedema or dehydration.
  • Ask about fatigue - General symptom in most electrolyte disturbances, linked to disrupted cellular energy metabolism.
  • Ask about cardiac arrhythmias - Common in disturbances like hypokalaemia and hyperkalaemia, where changes in membrane potential affect cardiac conduction.
  • Ask about seizures - May occur in severe hyponatraemia or hypocalcaemia due to altered neuronal excitability.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Electrolyte Disturbance"][/sc]
  • Check muscle cramps or spasms - Indicative of hypocalcaemia or hypomagnesaemia due to increased neuromuscular excitability.
  • Check postural hypotension - May be seen in hypovolaemic hyponatraemia, reflecting fluid and electrolyte losses.
  • Check tachycardia - A compensatory response to hypotension or dehydration, often seen in hypernatraemia.
  • Check chvostek’s and Trousseau’s signs - Signs of latent tetany in hypocalcaemia due to increased neuromuscular irritability.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Electrolyte Disturbance"][/sc]
  • Cardiac arrhythmias - Sign of severe potassium or calcium imbalance, potentially leading to sudden cardiac death.
  • Seizures - Reflects critical hyponatraemia or hypocalcaemia and may signal impending brain herniation.
  • Severe muscle weakness - Suggests marked electrolyte imbalance, particularly in potassium disturbances, with the risk of respiratory failure.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Electrolyte Disturbance"][/sc]
  • Subtle cognitive changes - Particularly in elderly patients with chronic hyponatraemia, presenting as mild confusion or reduced concentration.
  • Nocturia or polydipsia - These may be the only presenting features in patients with early stages of sodium disturbances.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask electrolyte disturbance.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Electrolyte Disturbance"][/sc]
  • Cardiac arrhythmias - Especially with severe hypokalaemia or hyperkalaemia, due to disrupted cardiac conduction.
  • Seizures or coma - May result from severe electrolyte disturbances, such as hyponatraemia, leading to cerebral oedema or brain herniation.
  • Rhabdomyolysis - Seen in severe hypokalaemia or hypernatraemia due to muscle breakdown, leading to acute kidney injury.
