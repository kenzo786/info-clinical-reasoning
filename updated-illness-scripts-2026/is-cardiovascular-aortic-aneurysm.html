Aortic Aneurysm [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Aortic Aneurysm"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Aortic Aneurysm"][/sc]
  • Pathological dilatation of the aorta (diameter ≥3 cm, or >1.5× the adjacent normal aorta), most commonly affecting the infrarenal abdominal aorta (AAA); the expanding aneurysm wall risks catastrophic rupture with massive haemorrhage.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the classic triad of ruptured AAA — sudden severe abdominal or back pain, pulsatile expansile abdominal mass, and haemodynamic collapse — and identify which patients require emergency vascular referral versus surveillance; know the NHS AAA screening programme eligibility and size thresholds for intervention.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Aortic Aneurysm"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Aortic Aneurysm"][/sc]
  • AAA affects approximately 1.5–2% of men over 65 in the UK; prevalence rises to ~5–8% lifetime risk in men. Male:female ratio approximately 6:1. Peak incidence 65–75 years; uncommon under 50 years. Approximately 5,000 elective AAA repairs are performed annually in the UK, with around 2,000 emergency operations for ruptured AAA.
  • Strongest modifiable risk factor is smoking (5–10× increased risk relative to never-smokers); other risk factors include hypertension, dyslipidaemia, atherosclerotic disease, and family history (first-degree relative with AAA approximately doubles risk). Women have a lower prevalence but higher rupture risk at smaller absolute diameters (≥5.0 cm vs ≥5.5 cm threshold in men) due to narrower baseline aortic calibre.
  • High-risk groups for rupture: aneurysms ≥5.5 cm in men or ≥5.0 cm in women, rapid expansion (>1 cm/year), ongoing smoking, and uncontrolled hypertension. The NHS AAA Screening Programme invites all men aged 65 for a one-time abdominal aortic ultrasound, reducing AAA-related mortality in screened populations.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Aortic Aneurysm"][/sc]
  • Trigger: Chronic inflammatory infiltration of the aortic wall — particularly the tunica media and adventitia — drives macrophage-mediated release of matrix metalloproteinases (MMPs) that degrade elastin and collagen. This process is most pronounced at the infrarenal aorta where haemodynamic wall stress and relative lack of vasa vasorum reduce the capacity for structural repair.
  • Progression: Progressive loss of elastin leads to dilatation; as diameter increases, wall tension rises proportionally according to the law of Laplace (T = P × r), creating a self-reinforcing cycle of expansion. Intraluminal thrombus accumulates within the sac but does not protect against rupture and may serve as a source of distal thromboembolism. Expansion averages 2–4 mm/year for smaller AAA but accelerates as diameter increases.
  • Consequence: Full-thickness aortic wall rupture, most commonly into the retroperitoneum (partially self-tamponading) or, less commonly, free intraperitoneal haemorrhage. Overall mortality for ruptured AAA approaches 80% including pre-hospital deaths; even with emergency repair, 30-day mortality remains 30–50%. Aortoenteric or aortocaval fistulae are rarer but equally catastrophic consequences of untreated aneurysmal expansion.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Aortic Aneurysm"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Asymptomatic in over 75% of cases — most AAAs are detected incidentally on imaging performed for other reasons, or through the NHS screening programme. Patients may occasionally notice a pulsatile abdominal sensation or mild periumbilical discomfort.
  • Progressive phase: As the aneurysm enlarges over months to years, new-onset back or abdominal pain in a patient with known AAA (symptomatic unruptured AAA) signals rapid expansion or impending rupture. Distal thromboembolism from mural thrombus may produce peripheral ischaemia. Thoracic aneurysms may produce symptoms of compression (dysphagia, hoarseness, stridor).
  • Late phase: Rupture produces sudden, severe abdominal and/or back pain with haemodynamic collapse. Retroperitoneal rupture may produce transient haemodynamic stability before tamponade fails — prognosis deteriorates rapidly, particularly beyond 6 hours of untreated rupture. Free intraperitoneal haemorrhage presents with immediate cardiovascular collapse and carries the highest pre-operative mortality.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Aortic Aneurysm"][/sc]
  • Ask about sudden-onset severe central abdominal, back, or flank pain — the cardinal symptom of rupture; any sudden severe pain in this distribution in an at-risk older patient (male, smoker, known AAA, hypertension) must be treated as ruptured AAA until proved otherwise.
  • Ask about known aortic aneurysm or previous vascular imaging — pre-existing AAA dramatically raises the pre-test probability of rupture and must always be elicited; patients may not volunteer this information unless directly asked.
  • Ask about a pulsatile or throbbing abdominal sensation — often the only symptom of a slowly enlarging unruptured aneurysm; may have been present for months and can be mistaken for a bowel or musculoskeletal complaint.
  • Ask about collapse, presyncope, or a period of relative stability followed by sudden deterioration — the classic retroperitoneal rupture pattern, where temporary tamponade produces a "sentinel" haemodynamic window before re-bleeding; this must never give false reassurance.
  • Ask about symptoms of limb ischaemia (unilateral leg pain, pallor, coldness, numbness) — mural thrombus within the aneurysm sac may embolise distally, causing peripheral arterial occlusion, which can occur prior to or independently of frank rupture.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Aortic Aneurysm"][/sc]
  • Check for a pulsatile expansile abdominal mass in the periumbilical or epigastric region — the cardinal sign of AAA; the mass expands in all directions with each pulse (distinguishing true aneurysm from a transmitted pulse). Sensitivity of clinical examination is limited (50–70%), particularly in obese patients, so a negative examination does not exclude AAA.
  • Check blood pressure and pulse — haemodynamic instability (hypotension + tachycardia) in the context of abdominal or back pain in an at-risk patient constitutes a vascular emergency; delays for further clinical assessment in the haemodynamically unstable patient may increase mortality, and immediate vascular team activation together with CT aortography is typically prioritised over routine workup completion.
  • Check for periumbilical or flank bruising (Grey Turner's sign) — retroperitoneal tracking of blood; a late sign indicating substantial haemorrhage that has dissected to the skin surface, signifying large-volume blood loss.
  • Check peripheral pulses bilaterally in all four limbs — absent or asymmetric femoral or distal pulses suggest iliac involvement or distal embolisation; acute unilateral limb ischaemia with absent pulse in the context of a pulsatile mass is a dual emergency.
  • Check for abdominal tenderness — a tender pulsatile mass is a vascular surgical emergency; pain on deep palpation or voluntary guarding suggests an expanding or leaking aneurysm even in the absence of frank haemodynamic instability.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Aortic Aneurysm"][/sc]
  • Haemodynamic instability with abdominal or back pain in an at-risk patient — ruptured AAA must be excluded immediately; immediate vascular team activation is warranted before investigations are complete, with CT aortography or direct theatre transfer appropriate when the patient cannot be haemodynamically stabilised. Delaying for further workup in this context is a recognised cause of avoidable death.
  • New pain in a patient with known AAA (symptomatic unruptured AAA) — indicates rapid expansion or impending rupture; requires emergency vascular assessment even if the patient is currently haemodynamically stable, as deterioration may occur rapidly.
  • Pulsatile expansile abdominal mass with tenderness — even without overt haemodynamic instability, tenderness over a known or suspected aneurysm signals leaking or rupture. Do not reassure on the basis of preserved blood pressure, particularly where retroperitoneal tamponade may be temporarily masking free haemorrhage.
  • Prolonged or misdiagnosed presentation — prognosis deteriorates rapidly, particularly beyond 6 hours of untreated rupture; classic misdiagnoses include renal colic (flank pain radiating to groin) and musculoskeletal back pain, both of which may delay the correct diagnosis in older at-risk patients.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Aortic Aneurysm"][/sc]
  • Retroperitoneal rupture with transient stability (sentinel presentation): The most clinically dangerous atypical pattern — temporary haemostasis from retroperitoneal tamponade produces a brief window of relative haemodynamic stability. Patients may arrive ambulant and conversant before rapidly decompensating. This "stable" window must not delay vascular activation or CT aortography.
  • Misdiagnosis as renal colic: Severe loin-to-groin pain in an older male may closely mimic ureteric colic; haematuria may occur with ruptured AAA due to ureteric compression, further reinforcing the misdiagnosis. Any older at-risk patient with suspected renal colic must be assessed for haemodynamic stability and a pulsatile abdominal mass before CT KUB is ordered.
  • Thoracic aortic aneurysm (TAA): Presents with chest, interscapular, or jaw pain without a palpable abdominal mass; mediastinal widening on CXR may be the first clue. TAA involving the aortic root can cause aortic regurgitation (collapsing pulse, early diastolic murmur). CT aortography from arch to pelvis is required for full characterisation.
  • Inflammatory AAA: Approximately 5% of AAAs have a periaortic inflammatory component causing adherence to adjacent structures (duodenum, left renal vein, ureter). Presents with back pain, constitutional symptoms (weight loss, fever), and markedly elevated ESR/CRP even in the absence of rupture. May be associated with retroperitoneal fibrosis and ureteric obstruction.
  • Older adults and women: Women have a lower overall prevalence of AAA but present at smaller absolute aneurysm diameters with higher relative rupture risk; rupture thresholds are typically set 0.5 cm lower in women (≥5.0 cm). In older adults, haemodynamic responses to haemorrhage may be blunted by baseline cardiovascular disease or beta-blockade, masking tachycardia and reducing the reliability of vital sign thresholds.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Aortic Aneurysm"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected ruptured AAA (haemodynamically unstable): This is a clinical emergency — immediate vascular team activation is warranted. Bedside point-of-care ultrasound may confirm AAA presence rapidly, but CT aortography is required for operative planning. In the haemodynamically unstable patient who cannot be stabilised, direct transfer to theatre without awaiting imaging may be appropriate. Bloods: FBC, U&E, coagulation, group and crossmatch, arterial blood gas, and lactate.
  • Core for symptomatic or incidentally detected unruptured AAA: Abdominal ultrasound confirms AAA presence and diameter (accurate to ±3 mm; first-line for surveillance). CT aortography defines aneurysm morphology, extent to renal and iliac vessels, and suitability for EVAR vs open repair, and is essential before any operative intervention.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: ECG and troponin (inferior MI may present with epigastric pain and haemodynamic compromise); urinalysis (ureteric colic — but be cautious, haematuria may occur with AAA from ureteric compression); serum lipase/amylase if pancreatitis is considered; ESR and CRP if inflammatory AAA is suspected.
  • Imaging — CT aortography: Gold standard for diagnosis, sizing, morphology, and pre-operative planning. Defines proximal neck anatomy, renal artery involvement, iliac extent, and mural thrombus. Requires IV contrast — do not delay for allergy assessment in the haemodynamically unstable patient. Provides simultaneous assessment of retroperitoneal haematoma and adjacent visceral injury.
  • Imaging — Abdominal ultrasound: First-line for screening and surveillance; highly accurate for AAA diameter measurement and rapid bedside confirmation. Cannot reliably detect retroperitoneal haemorrhage or delineate proximal/distal vessel anatomy for surgical planning. Operator-dependent and limited by bowel gas in emergency settings.
  • Serial reassessment: For AAA below intervention threshold, interval surveillance ultrasound is appropriate — conducted in an outpatient vascular setting according to NHS screening programme thresholds (annual ultrasound for 3.0–4.4 cm; 3-monthly for 4.5–5.4 cm; surgical referral at ≥5.5 cm in men). Any new or worsening symptoms between surveillance intervals requires urgent reassessment.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Aortic Aneurysm"][/sc]
  • A 68-year-old male with a history of smoking and hypertension presents with sudden-onset severe central abdominal and back pain, haemodynamic instability (BP 80/50 mmHg, HR 118 bpm), and a tender pulsatile expansile mass in the periumbilical region on examination.
  • An older male smoker with hypertension and acute abdominal and back pain with a pulsatile abdominal mass and haemodynamic collapse — consistent with ruptured abdominal aortic aneurysm requiring immediate vascular surgical activation and emergency CT aortography or operative intervention.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Aortic Aneurysm"][/sc]
  • Aortic dissection — acute tearing chest or interscapular pain (often maximal at onset); pulse differentials between limbs or blood pressure asymmetry; mediastinal widening on CXR; CT aortography differentiates by demonstrating an intimal flap rather than saccular dilatation. Does not typically produce a pulsatile abdominal mass.
  • Renal colic (ureteric calculus) — colicky loin-to-groin or scrotal pain without haemodynamic instability; frank haematuria on urinalysis; CT KUB distinguishes. Critically, haematuria may also occur with ruptured AAA from ureteric compression, so haemodynamic stability and a pulsatile mass must always be assessed before ureteric colic is accepted as the diagnosis.
  • Acute pancreatitis — epigastric pain radiating to the back, typically associated with nausea, vomiting, and raised serum lipase; no pulsatile abdominal mass; haemodynamic compromise only in severe haemorrhagic pancreatitis (Ranson/Glasgow criteria); CT pancreas differentiates.
  • Acute mesenteric ischaemia — severe central abdominal pain disproportionate to peritoneal findings ("pain out of proportion"); associated with atrial fibrillation, recent MI, or severe atherosclerosis; no pulsatile mass; CT mesenteric angiography distinguishes. Both conditions may coexist in patients with advanced vascular disease.
  • Musculoskeletal back pain — mechanical onset, positional aggravation, no haemodynamic instability, no pulsatile mass; however, this is a dangerous diagnostic trap in older at-risk patients with sudden-onset back pain. Sudden or severe onset in this demographic should prompt vascular consideration before a musculoskeletal label is applied.
  • Inferior myocardial infarction — may present with epigastric pain and haemodynamic compromise; ECG and troponin are essential; no pulsatile abdominal mass. ST changes should not preclude consideration of AAA in an at-risk patient, as haemorrhagic shock may produce secondary cardiac ischaemia.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Aortic Aneurysm"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Recognise ruptured or symptomatic AAA as a vascular emergency; immediate vascular surgical team activation is warranted before investigations are complete. Supportive care often includes large-bore IV access, permissive hypotension targeting a systolic BP of approximately 70–80 mmHg (avoiding aggressive fluid resuscitation which may worsen haemorrhage before operative haemostasis), blood product transfusion with a balanced haemostatic approach, and avoidance of unnecessary delays for further workup in the haemodynamically unstable patient.
  • Operative management — open surgical repair: Direct aortic clamping and prosthetic graft interposition; remains the definitive approach for anatomically unsuitable EVAR candidates and for thoracic aneurysms. Associated with higher 30-day morbidity and longer recovery than EVAR but does not require lifelong radiological surveillance for endoleak.
  • Operative management — endovascular aortic repair (EVAR): Endoluminal stent-graft deployment via femoral access; associated with lower 30-day mortality than open repair in elective cases (EVAR 1 trial, Lancet 2010; DREAM trial) and increasingly used in emergency settings for suitable anatomy (IMPROVE trial). Requires suitable proximal neck anatomy and lifelong post-EVAR surveillance imaging for endoleak detection. Long-term all-cause survival is equivalent to open repair.
  • Elective repair indications: Men with AAA ≥5.5 cm, women ≥5.0 cm, any symptomatic AAA regardless of size, or rapid expansion >1 cm/year. Shared decision-making with the patient includes individualised discussion of operative risk (based on fitness and comorbidities) versus rupture risk at the current aneurysm diameter.
  • Non-operative management (surveillance and risk factor modification): For AAA below intervention threshold, the primary approach involves smoking cessation (the most effective intervention for slowing aneurysm growth), blood pressure optimisation, and statin therapy for cardiovascular risk reduction. Regular surveillance ultrasound according to NHS AAA Screening Programme thresholds. Smoking cessation may reduce expansion rate by approximately 20–40% compared with ongoing smoking.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Post-operative monitoring of renal function, inflammatory markers, and lower limb perfusion. Post-EVAR: lifelong CT or duplex ultrasound surveillance for endoleak; interval and frequency determined by local vascular unit protocol. Surveillance ultrasound thresholds (3.0–4.4 cm annually; 4.5–5.4 cm 3-monthly) should be maintained until intervention threshold is reached or the patient is no longer fit for repair.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: New or worsening back, abdominal, or flank pain in any patient with known AAA should prompt immediate emergency assessment for rupture, even if the patient is currently haemodynamically stable. Patients discharged after incidental AAA detection should be counselled to seek immediate emergency care for any sudden or severe abdominal, back, or flank pain.
  • Exam pharmacology reference (UKMLA/MRCGP): NICE NG156 (Abdominal aortic aneurysm: diagnosis and management) recommends optimisation of cardiovascular risk factors in patients with AAA, including antihypertensive therapy and high-intensity statin therapy, with specific agent selection guided by comorbidities and local prescribing guidelines. No doses or frequencies are cited here — always verify against your local antimicrobial/prescribing formulary before prescribing.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Aortic Aneurysm"][/sc]
  • Rupture: The most catastrophic complication; annual rupture risk increases exponentially with diameter (approximately 2% per year at 5.5 cm, rising to over 25% per year at ≥6.5 cm). Prognosis deteriorates rapidly — particularly beyond 6 hours of untreated rupture — with overall mortality approaching 80% including pre-hospital deaths. Retroperitoneal rupture may allow a brief window of relative haemodynamic stability before tamponade fails.
  • Distal thromboembolism ("trash foot"): Mural thrombus within the aneurysm sac may fragment and embolise distally, causing digital ischaemia, peripheral arterial occlusion, or acute limb ischaemia. Occurs prior to or independently of rupture and may be the first clinical presentation of a previously undiagnosed AAA. Bilateral lower limb involvement should prompt urgent aortic imaging.
  • Endoleak (post-EVAR): Persistent perfusion of the aneurysm sac outside the endograft after endovascular repair; classified Type I (inadequate seal at proximal or distal attachment) through Type V (endotension). Type II endoleak from retrograde lumbar or inferior mesenteric artery flow is most common and often self-limiting; Type I and III are haemodynamically significant and require re-intervention. Lifelong imaging surveillance is required to detect endoleak and sac expansion.
  • Aortoenteric fistula: Rare but life-threatening erosion of the aortic wall or graft into the adjacent gastrointestinal tract, most commonly the duodenum. Classically presents with a "herald bleed" — a small, self-limiting upper GI bleed followed by massive haemorrhage. More common after open repair. This is a surgical emergency with very high mortality if not recognised early; upper GI bleeding in a patient with prior aortic surgery should trigger immediate CT aortography.
  • Post-operative complications (open repair): Include renal impairment from suprarenal cross-clamping, lower limb ischaemia from iliac vessel manipulation, bowel ischaemia from inferior mesenteric artery ligation, and erectile dysfunction from disruption of autonomic nerves overlying the aorta. Risk stratification and informed consent should address these complications as part of the shared decision-making process.
