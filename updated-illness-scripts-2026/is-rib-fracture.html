Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Rib Fracture"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Rib Fracture"][/sc]
  • Risk Factors: Advanced age, osteoporosis, contact sports, motor vehicle accidents, chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD), malignancy with bone metastases, and repetitive stress (e.g., severe coughing) increase the likelihood of rib fractures. Patients on long-term corticosteroids are also at higher risk due to bone demineralisation.
  • Prevention: Preventative measures include fall prevention strategies in the elderly, adequate calcium and vitamin D intake to support bone health, use of protective gear in high-risk sports, seatbelt use to reduce injury severity in motor vehicle accidents, and smoking cessation to reduce osteoporosis risk. Early identification and management of osteoporosis can significantly reduce the risk of fragility fractures.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ai-illness-script-section-links" Q="Rib Fracture" select="103" url-2="" css-2="" url-3="" css-3="" edit="886"][/sc] [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Rib Fracture"][/sc]
  • Rib fractures occur when the bone is subjected to a force greater than its strength. This often results from blunt trauma to the chest, such as a fall, motor vehicle accident, or direct blow during sports. The force can cause a single or multiple ribs to crack or break, leading to pain, instability, and compromised respiratory mechanics.
  • Pathological rib fractures occur when the ribs are weakened by underlying conditions such as osteoporosis or malignancy, requiring minimal trauma to fracture.
  • The damage to surrounding structures, including muscles, blood vessels, and the pleura, can lead to complications like pneumothorax or haemothorax.
Time Course [sc name="ai-illness-script-section-links" Q="Rib Fracture" select="104" url-2="" css-2="" url-3="" css-3="" edit="886"][/sc] [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Rib Fracture"][/sc]
  • Acute phase (0-7 days): Severe pain, particularly with movement and breathing, is prominent immediately after the fracture. Respiratory symptoms like dyspnoea can develop if there is associated lung or pleural injury.
  • Subacute phase (7-21 days): Pain gradually improves as healing progresses, but respiratory issues may persist if complications such as pneumothorax develop.
  • Healing phase (3-6 weeks): Full recovery typically occurs within 6 weeks in uncomplicated cases. Pain subsides, but patients may still experience discomfort with deep breathing or exertion.
Symptoms [sc name="ai-illness-script-section-links" Q="Rib Fracture" select="105" url-2="" css-2="" url-3="" css-3="" edit="886"][/sc] [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Rib Fracture"][/sc]
  • Ask about chest pain: The most common symptom, typically sharp and exacerbated by deep breathing, coughing, or movement. It results from irritation of the periosteum, with associated clinical features.
  • Ask about difficulty breathing (dyspnoea): Caused by pain-induced restricted chest wall movement and potential injury to the underlying lung.
  • Ask about localised tenderness: Focal pain on palpation over the fractured rib, due to direct trauma to the rib structure and surrounding tissue.
  • Ask about haemoptysis: May occur if there is lung injury or pleural involvement, such as in pneumothorax or lung contusion.
Signs [sc name="ai-illness-script-section-links" Q="Rib Fracture" select="106" url-2="" css-2="" url-3="" css-3="" edit="886"][/sc] [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Rib Fracture"][/sc]
  • Check chest wall tenderness: Localised pain on palpation of the affected rib(s), indicative of a fracture.
  • Check crepitus: A crunching or grating sensation felt on palpation over the fracture site, caused by bone fragments rubbing against each other.
  • Check paradoxical chest movement: In cases of flail chest (multiple rib fractures), a segment of the chest may move inward with inspiration instead of expanding.
  • Check decreased breath sounds: Suggestive of associated pneumothorax, haemothorax, or lung contusion, particularly if one side of the chest is involved.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ai-illness-script-section-links" Q="Rib Fracture" select="107" url-2="" css-2="" url-3="" css-3="" edit="886"][/sc] [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Rib Fracture"][/sc]
  • Severe dyspnoea: May indicate pneumothorax, haemothorax, or lung contusion, where breathing is significantly compromised due to lung collapse or blood accumulation.
  • Haemoptysis: Suggests underlying lung or vascular injury, which can be life-threatening.
  • Signs of shock (hypotension, tachycardia): May indicate internal bleeding from haemothorax or vascular injury.

  • Minimal or no pain: May occur in patients with neuropathy or altered pain perception (e.g., elderly or diabetics).
  • Non-traumatic fractures: In patients with underlying osteoporosis or malignancy, rib fractures may occur with minimal trauma or even spontaneously.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask rib fracture.

Complications [sc name="ai-illness-script-section-links" Q="Rib Fracture" select="112" url-2="" css-2="" url-3="" css-3="" edit="886"][/sc] [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Rib Fracture"][/sc]
  • Pneumothorax: Occurs when a fractured rib punctures the lung, leading to air accumulation in the pleural space.
  • Pneumonia: Caused by shallow breathing due to pain, which leads to poor lung expansion and secretion retention.
  • Haemothorax: Results from rib fragments causing injury to intercostal vessels or the lung, leading to blood accumulation in the pleural cavity.
