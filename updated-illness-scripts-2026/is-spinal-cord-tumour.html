Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Spinal Cord Tumour"][/sc]
  • The pathophysiology of spinal cord tumours depends on their location. Intramedullary tumours (e.g., ependymomas, astrocytomas) arise from glial cells, leading to direct cord compression and destruction of neural tissue. Intradural-extramedullary tumours (e.g., meningiomas, schwannomas) compress the cord from outside, causing progressive neurological impairment. Extradural tumours are often metastatic, spreading from primary cancers such as breast, prostate, or lung. Tumour growth results in spinal cord compression, disrupting motor, sensory, and autonomic pathways, causing pain, weakness, and sensory disturbances.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Spinal Cord Tumour"][/sc]
  • The time course varies depending on the tumour type and location. Benign tumours (e.g., meningiomas) may grow slowly over years, causing gradual symptoms. Malignant tumours, including metastatic lesions, can progress rapidly over weeks to months, leading to severe neurological deficits. The time course can be divided into phases of mild discomfort, followed by progressive neurological decline, and eventually severe motor and sensory impairment if untreated.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Spinal Cord Tumour"][/sc]
  • Ask about back Pain - Often the first symptom, caused by tumour invasion or compression of surrounding tissues, including vertebral structures and nerve roots.
  • Ask about weakness - Motor weakness due to compression of descending motor pathways, particularly in corticospinal tracts.
  • Ask about sensory Loss - Numbness, tingling, or paraesthesia due to disruption of ascending sensory pathways (e.g., dorsal columns).
  • Ask about bladder/Bowel Dysfunction - Autonomic pathway disruption, particularly in cases of conus medullaris or cauda equina involvement.
  • Ask about lhermitte’s Sign - Electrical sensation down the spine with neck flexion, indicative of spinal cord compression.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Spinal Cord Tumour"][/sc]
  • Check motor Weakness - Loss of muscle strength, often in a segmental pattern corresponding to the level of cord compression.
  • Check sensory Level - A defined area where sensory loss begins, corresponding to the level of spinal cord involvement.
  • Check hyperreflexia - Increased reflexes due to upper motor neuron involvement in the corticospinal tract.
  • Check spasticity - Increased muscle tone resulting from disruption of descending motor pathways.
  • Check ataxia - Impaired coordination, particularly with intramedullary lesions affecting proprioceptive pathways.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Spinal Cord Tumour"][/sc]
  • Rapidly Progressive Weakness - Indicates severe spinal cord compression requiring urgent intervention to prevent irreversible damage.
  • Bladder/Bowel Incontinence - Suggests involvement of autonomic pathways, often seen in cauda equina syndrome, requiring urgent decompression.
  • Bilateral Symptoms - Neurological deficits on both sides suggest central spinal cord involvement, often seen with larger or more aggressive tumours.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Spinal Cord Tumour"][/sc]
  • Isolated Pain Without Neurological Deficits - Some patients may present with isolated back or radicular pain for extended periods before neurological deficits emerge.
  • Non-Specific Systemic Symptoms - Malaise or unintentional weight loss can occur in cases of malignant tumours or metastases, without overt neurological symptoms initially.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask spinal cord tumour.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Spinal Cord Tumour"][/sc]
  • Degenerative Disc Disease - Often presents with back pain and radiculopathy but lacks the progressive neurological deficits seen.
  • Multiple Sclerosis - Causes demyelination of the spinal cord, but typically has a relapsing-remitting course, with lesions.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Spinal Cord Tumour"][/sc]
  • Permanent Neurological Deficits - Delayed diagnosis and treatment can lead to irreversible damage, including paraplegia or quadriplegia.
  • Spinal Instability - In cases of extradural tumours involving vertebrae, surgical intervention can lead to instability, requiring spinal fusion.
  • Recurrence - Some tumours, particularly malignant or incompletely resected ones, have a high risk of recurrence.
