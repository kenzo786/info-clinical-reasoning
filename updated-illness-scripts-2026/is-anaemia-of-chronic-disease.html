Anaemia of Chronic Disease (ACD)
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Anaemia of Chronic Disease (ACD)"][/sc]
  • Anaemia of chronic disease is inflammation-driven impaired erythropoiesis and iron utilisation causing usually normocytic or mildly microcytic anaemia.
  • UKMLA essentials: chronic inflammatory comorbidity with anaemia, occult blood loss concern, confirm pattern with ferritin and transferrin.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Anaemia of Chronic Disease (ACD)"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Anaemia of Chronic Disease (ACD)"][/sc]
  • Risk factors: Risk of anaemia of chronic disease (acd) rises with relevant predisposition, comorbidity, and exposure profile.
  • Higher-risk or missed groups: Anaemia of Chronic Disease (ACD) may be missed when presentations are atypical or comorbidity obscures key features.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Anaemia of Chronic Disease (ACD)"][/sc]
  • 1. Chronic inflammation triggers an increase in cytokines, particularly interleukin-6 (IL-6).
  • 2. IL-6 stimulates the liver to produce hepcidin, a hormone that inhibits iron absorption from the gut and traps iron within macrophages.
  • 3. This results in reduced iron availability for erythropoiesis, despite normal or increased total body iron stores.
  • 4. Erythropoiesis is further impaired by reduced erythropoietin production and response, leading to fewer red blood cells.
  • 5. Red blood cell lifespan is shortened due to increased phagocytosis and oxidative stress.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Anaemia of Chronic Disease (ACD)"][/sc]
  • Initial phase: As the underlying chronic condition progresses, mild anaemia may develop insidiously over weeks to months.
  • Chronic phase: The anaemia often stabilises at a mild to moderate level, persisting as long as the underlying inflammatory condition remains active.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Anaemia of Chronic Disease (ACD)"][/sc]
  • Ask about fatigue - Reduced oxygen delivery to tissues due to anaemia leads to fatigue. Chronic inflammation also contributes to this symptom via cytokine-mediated mechanisms.
  • Ask about shortness of breath on exertion - Decreased haemoglobin levels reduce oxygen-carrying capacity, causing dyspnoea during physical activity.
  • Ask about pallor - Reduced haemoglobin concentration leads to decreased oxygenation of the skin and mucous membranes, resulting in visible paleness.
  • Ask about often asymptomatic - ACD is frequently mild and may not cause noticeable symptoms, especially in older adults or in the presence of slow-onset anaemia.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Anaemia of Chronic Disease (ACD)"][/sc]
  • Check pallor - Pale skin and mucous membranes reflect reduced haemoglobin levels and decreased blood flow.
  • Check tachycardia - The heart compensates for reduced oxygen delivery by increasing cardiac output.
  • Check mild splenomegaly - Chronic inflammation and increased red cell destruction may cause slight enlargement of the spleen.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Anaemia of Chronic Disease (ACD)"][/sc]
  • Severe anaemia (haemoglobin <80 g/L) - May indicate a superimposed condition like iron deficiency anaemia or acute blood loss, requiring urgent investigation.
  • Sudden worsening of symptoms - Could suggest acute infection, malignancy progression, or bone marrow failure.
  • Unintentional weight loss - May signify an underlying malignancy, particularly if coupled with ACD.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Anaemia of Chronic Disease (ACD)"][/sc]
  • Mild cognitive impairment - Particularly in older adults, chronic anaemia can present as subtle mental changes or reduced concentration, due to diminished cerebral oxygenation.
  • Depression or lethargy - Inflammatory cytokines may exacerbate mood disorders or generalised lethargy.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features or comorbidity can mask the underlying diagnosis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Anaemia of Chronic Disease (ACD)"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Full blood count and indices with clinical review of inflammatory, malignant, renal, or infectious comorbidity driving marrow suppression.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Ferritin, transferrin saturation, CRP/ESR, renal profile, and cause-specific investigations to distinguish inflammatory anaemia from iron deficiency or mixed states.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Anaemia of Chronic Disease (ACD)"][/sc]
  • A 65-year-old man with rheumatoid arthritis reports fatigue and mild exertional breathlessness, with normocytic anaemia, raised CRP, and relatively preserved ferritin.
  • Adult with chronic inflammatory disease and anaemia pattern suggesting inflammation-mediated iron restriction and reduced erythropoiesis.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Anaemia of Chronic Disease (ACD)"][/sc]
  • Iron deficiency anaemia - Lower ferritin with clear iron depletion and possible blood-loss source favours iron deficiency.
  • Chronic kidney disease anaemia - Reduced erythropoietin with renal impairment and normocytic anaemia supports renal aetiology.
  • Myelodysplastic syndrome - Cytopenias, dysplastic blood-film features, and marrow abnormalities suggest clonal haematological disease.
  • Haemolytic anaemia - Reticulocytosis with haemolysis markers and jaundice indicates increased red-cell destruction rather than inflammatory sequestration.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Anaemia of Chronic Disease (ACD)"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Exclude severe symptomatic anaemia and active bleeding while assessing whether inflammation, renal disease, or malignancy is driving presentation.
  • Core care: Optimise treatment of the underlying chronic disorder and correct coexisting nutritional or iron deficiency where present.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Serial haemoglobin, inflammatory markers, iron indices, and comorbidity control should guide ongoing treatment adjustments.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Progressive anaemia, systemic deterioration, unexplained cytopenias, or poor response to targeted management should trigger specialist review.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Anaemia of Chronic Disease (ACD)"][/sc]
  • Progressive anaemia: May occur if the underlying condition worsens or if superimposed iron deficiency develops.
  • Cardiac complications: Chronic anaemia increases the risk of heart failure and ischaemic heart disease, especially in older adults.
  • Impaired quality of life: Fatigue and reduced physical capacity can lead to reduced daily functioning and social withdrawal.
