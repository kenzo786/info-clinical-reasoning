Seizure
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Seizure"][/sc]
  • A seizure is a transient, uncontrolled electrical disturbance in the brain that causes changes in behaviour, movements, sensations, or levels of consciousness.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise seizure features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Seizure"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Seizure"][/sc]
  • Risk factors: Risk factors for seizure include relevant predisposition, comorbidity, and exposure history.
  • Higher-risk or missed groups: Seizure may be missed when presentation is atypical or obscured by comorbidity.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Seizure"][/sc]
  • Seizures occur when there is excessive, synchronous neuronal activity in the brain, disrupting normal electrical signalling. This can result from imbalances between excitatory and inhibitory neurotransmitters (e.g., glutamate and GABA), structural abnormalities (e.g., stroke, tumours), or genetic predispositions affecting ion channel functioning.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Seizure"][/sc]
  • Seizures may last from a few seconds to a few minutes. Postictal confusion may follow, lasting minutes to hours. Chronic seizure disorders, such as epilepsy, may persist lifelong, requiring long-term management.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Seizure"][/sc]
  • Ask about loss of consciousness - Sudden loss of awareness due to abnormal cortical activity, common in generalised seizures.
  • Ask about involuntary jerking movements - These occur due to abnormal motor cortex activation, common in tonic-clonic seizures.
  • Ask about aura - Sensory disturbances (e.g., visual or auditory changes) prior to the seizure, often seen in focal seizures, caused by initial abnormal electrical activity.
  • Ask about confusion post-seizure (postictal state) - Due to the brain recovering from widespread neuronal activity, leading to temporary cognitive dysfunction.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Seizure"][/sc]
  • Check tonic-clonic movements - Generalised stiffening and rhythmic jerking, indicating widespread neuronal involvement.
  • Check tongue biting - Results from involuntary contractions of the jaw muscles during a seizure.
  • Check incontinence - Loss of bladder or bowel control due to involuntary muscle relaxation during a seizure.
  • Check postictal drowsiness or confusion - Seen after the seizure due to temporary disruption of normal brain activity.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Seizure"][/sc]
  • Prolonged seizure (>5 minutes) - May indicate status epilepticus, which is a medical emergency and can lead to brain damage.
  • Seizures in a febrile patient - Suggests possible CNS infection, such as meningitis or encephalitis.
  • Focal neurological deficits post-seizure - May indicate a structural brain lesion (e.g., tumour or stroke).
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Seizure"][/sc]
  • Absence seizures - Brief episodes of staring and unresponsiveness, often mistaken for daydreaming, due to altered electrical activity in the thalamocortical circuits.
  • Non-convulsive seizures - These present without visible motor symptoms, manifesting instead with confusion or altered consciousness, which can be missed if subtle.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Seizure"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Electroencephalogram (EEG) - Abnormal electrical activity patterns, such as spikes or sharp waves, help localise the seizure focus.
  • Targeted and monitoring: CT or MRI brain - Structural abnormalities like tumours, infarcts, or malformations can be identified as causes of seizures; Blood tests.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Seizure"][/sc]
  • Syncope - Sudden loss of consciousness due to transient cerebral hypoperfusion, often lacking the postictal confusion.
  • Panic attacks - Episodes of intense fear and hyperventilation, without true loss of consciousness or postictal confusion.
  • Transient ischaemic attack (TIA) - Brief neurological deficits, usually without loss of consciousness or postictal state, often in elderly.
  • Related alternative diagnosis - Distinguish from seizure using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Seizure"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Assess severity, physiological stability, and immediate complications.
  • Core care: Deliver condition-specific management for seizure, treat underlying drivers, and coordinate appropriate specialist input.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Reassess symptom trajectory, examination findings, and objective markers to confirm response in seizure.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Escalate urgently for clinical deterioration, evolving red flags, or failure to improve in suspected seizure.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Seizure"][/sc]
  • Status epilepticus - Prolonged seizure activity (>5 minutes), leading to potential brain damage or death if untreated.
  • Sudden unexpected death in epilepsy (SUDEP) - Rare, but serious complication of poorly controlled epilepsy, linked to respiratory or cardiac dysfunction during or after seizures.
  • Injuries - Falls or trauma during seizures can lead to fractures, head injuries, or burns.
