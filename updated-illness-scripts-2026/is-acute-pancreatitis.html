Acute Pancreatitis
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Acute Pancreatitis"][/sc]
  • Acute pancreatitis is acute inflammation of the pancreas causing severe upper abdominal pain with biochemical evidence of pancreatic injury.
  • UKMLA essentials: severe epigastric pain, severe hypotension, serum amylase and lipase.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Acute Pancreatitis"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Acute Pancreatitis"][/sc]
  • Acute Pancreatitis is encountered across acute and specialist care, with burden shaped by age, comorbidity, and exposure context.
  • Risk increases in patients with relevant predisposing disease, treatment exposures, or delayed access to care.
  • Missed or late recognition is more likely when presentations are atypical or overlap with common alternative diagnoses.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Acute Pancreatitis"][/sc]
  • Initial insult, commonly due to gallstones or alcohol, triggers acinar cell injury.
  • This results in the activation of pancreatic enzymes (trypsinogen to trypsin) within the pancreas, leading to autodigestion and inflammation.
  • Inflammation recruits neutrophils, macrophages, and cytokines (IL-1, IL-6, TNF-Î±), exacerbating local injury and increasing vascular permeability.
  • Severe cases progress to necrosis and systemic inflammatory response, which can lead to multi-organ dysfunction.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Acute Pancreatitis"][/sc]
  • Early Phase (First 24-48 hours): Acute inflammation, abdominal pain, systemic inflammatory response.
  • Late Phase (>1 week): Potential for complications, including necrosis, infection, and pseudocyst formation if severe.
  • Recovery Phase: Resolution of inflammation and gradual improvement in symptoms, lasting days to weeks.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Acute Pancreatitis"][/sc]
  • Ask about Severe Epigastric Pain - Often radiating to the back; due to inflammation and oedema of pancreatic tissue.
  • Ask about Nausea and Vomiting - Stimulation of the autonomic nervous system by inflammatory cytokines and increased intra-abdominal pressure.
  • Ask about Fever - Systemic inflammatory response, especially in severe cases.
  • Ask about Jaundice - Can occur if gallstones are present, obstructing the bile duct.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Acute Pancreatitis"][/sc]
  • Check Epigastric Tenderness - Due to localised inflammation in the upper abdomen.
  • Check Guarding or Rigidity - May indicate peritonitis in severe cases with pancreatic necrosis.
  • Check Hypotension - Systemic inflammatory response leading to vasodilation and fluid loss.
  • Check Grey-Turner Sign - Ecchymosis of the flanks, associated with retroperitoneal haemorrhage.
  • Check Cullen's Sign - Periumbilical ecchymosis, indicative of severe haemorrhagic pancreatitis.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Acute Pancreatitis"][/sc]
  • Severe Hypotension - Indicates shock and potential multi-organ dysfunction.
  • Persistent Fever or Sepsis - Suggestive of infected pancreatic necrosis, a life-threatening complication.
  • Altered Mental Status - May signal sepsis or encephalopathy from severe systemic inflammation.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Acute Pancreatitis"][/sc]
  • Painless Jaundice - Seen in patients with obstructive pancreatitis secondary to gallstones, may be mistaken for other hepatobiliary diseases.
  • Predominant Respiratory Symptoms - Due to acute respiratory distress syndrome (ARDS) in severe cases, leading to dyspnoea without notable abdominal pain.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Acute Pancreatitis"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Serum amylase and lipase to support diagnosis; bedside observations and urine output to assess early physiological compromise.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Ultrasound to assess for gallstones and biliary obstruction; contrast CT when diagnosis is uncertain or complications are suspected; serial CRP, FBC, renal profile, and observations for severity tracking.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Acute Pancreatitis"][/sc]
  • A 45-year-old male presents with acute onset of severe, radiating epigastric pain, nausea, and vomiting, with elevated serum lipase levels and history of chronic alcohol use.
  • Adult with acute severe epigastric pain radiating to the back, vomiting, and raised pancreatic enzymes, concerning for acute pancreatitis.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Acute Pancreatitis"][/sc]
  • Cholecystitis - Right upper quadrant pain with Murphy's sign and biliary inflammatory pattern is more typical.
  • Perforated peptic ulcer - Sudden generalised peritonitic pain with free intraperitoneal air supports perforation.
  • Mesenteric ischaemia - Severe pain out of proportion to examination with vascular risk factors and raised lactate suggests ischaemia.
  • Inferior myocardial infarction - Epigastric pain with dynamic ECG or troponin changes indicates cardiac rather than pancreatic pathology.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Acute Pancreatitis"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Start early supportive care with intravenous fluids, analgesia, antiemetics, and organ-support assessment.
  • Core care: Treat the underlying cause, including biliary source management and alcohol-related harm support; reserve antibiotics for suspected infected necrosis or another confirmed infection.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Reassess pain, observations, urine output, and blood markers to detect evolving organ dysfunction or local complications.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Worsening sepsis, hypotension, hypoxia, oliguria, or persistent organ dysfunction should prompt urgent senior and critical care review.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Acute Pancreatitis"][/sc]
  • Necrotising Pancreatitis - Leads to extensive tissue damage, associated with high mortality.
  • Pseudocyst formation - Can develop after severe inflammation and may lead to infection, compression, or rupture.
  • Multi-Organ Dysfunction Syndrome (MODS) - In severe cases with systemic inflammatory response, leading to ARDS, renal failure, and sepsis.
