Achilles Tendon Rupture
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Achilles Tendon Rupture"][/sc]
  • Achilles tendon rupture is a partial or complete tear of the Achilles tendon causing sudden loss of plantar-flexion continuity.
  • UKMLA essentials: sudden calf snap pain, positive Thompson test, urgent orthopaedic pathway referral.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Achilles Tendon Rupture"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Achilles Tendon Rupture"][/sc]
  • Achilles tendon rupture is a frequent acute tendon injury in physically active adults, especially during sudden acceleration or jumping activities.
  • Risk is increased by prior Achilles symptoms, intermittent high-load sport, and tendon weakening factors such as recent fluoroquinolone or steroid exposure.
  • Delayed diagnosis is common when patients remain able to walk, which can worsen long-term functional outcome.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Achilles Tendon Rupture"][/sc]
  • Degenerative tendon change or repeated microtrauma reduces tensile strength within the Achilles tendon.
  • A sudden high-load contraction or forced dorsiflexion then causes fibre failure and tendon discontinuity.
  • Loss of continuity impairs plantar-flexion power and push-off function, with haematoma and local swelling at the tear site.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Achilles Tendon Rupture"][/sc]
  • Acute phase: Abrupt onset with snap sensation, pain, and immediate weakness during activity.
  • Early recovery phase: Swelling and bruising evolve over days, with persistent difficulty pushing off or single-leg heel raising.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Achilles Tendon Rupture"][/sc]
  • Ask about sudden posterior ankle or calf pain - Patients often describe being kicked or hearing a pop.
  • Ask about immediate loss of push-off strength - Inability to run, jump, or climb normally is typical.
  • Ask about swelling and bruising - Local tissue bleeding causes progressive posterior ankle discoloration.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Achilles Tendon Rupture"][/sc]
  • Check Thompson test - Absent or reduced plantar flexion on calf squeeze supports rupture.
  • Check for palpable tendon gap - Discontinuity along the tendon can often be felt above the calcaneus.
  • Check single-leg heel raise ability - Inability to perform indicates major plantar-flexion deficit.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Achilles Tendon Rupture"][/sc]
  • Inability to weight-bear with severe pain - May indicate complete rupture or associated fracture.
  • Neurovascular symptoms - Numbness, pallor, or reduced distal perfusion suggests associated limb-threatening injury.
  • Marked swelling with systemic upset - Consider compartment syndrome or infected soft tissue pathology after trauma.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Achilles Tendon Rupture"][/sc]
  • Partial rupture pattern: Pain and weakness may be present without complete functional loss, mimicking severe tendinopathy.
  • Delayed presentation: Some patients continue walking despite rupture, presenting later with persistent weakness and gait asymmetry.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Achilles Tendon Rupture"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Diagnosis is primarily clinical using injury mechanism, palpation, Thompson testing, and functional plantar-flexion assessment.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Ultrasound confirms fibre discontinuity and gap morphology when examination is uncertain; MRI is reserved for equivocal cases or complex associated injury planning.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Achilles Tendon Rupture"][/sc]
  • A 40-year-old man presents with sudden posterior ankle pain and a popping sensation during sport, followed by inability to push off and difficulty walking.
  • Adult with acute snap-like calf injury, plantar-flexion weakness, and positive rupture examination signs, concerning for Achilles tendon rupture.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Achilles Tendon Rupture"][/sc]
  • Severe Achilles tendinopathy - Painful tendon loading occurs but complete plantar-flexion failure is less typical.
  • Medial gastrocnemius tear - Calf pain and bruising predominate with intact Achilles continuity and Thompson response.
  • Ankle sprain - Pain localises to lateral or medial ligaments rather than posterior tendon disruption.
  • Calcaneal avulsion injury - Bony tenderness and fracture findings on imaging distinguish osseous injury.
  • Posterior tibial tendon injury - Medial ankle dysfunction and arch instability suggest a different tendon pathology.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Achilles Tendon Rupture"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Immobilise in plantar flexion, provide analgesia, and arrange urgent orthopaedic assessment.
  • Core care: Functional non-operative rehabilitation or operative repair is chosen based on rupture pattern, patient factors, and shared decision-making.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Supervised rehabilitation tracks tendon healing, calf strength recovery, and return-to-activity progression.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Increasing pain, neurovascular change, wound problems, or functional deterioration should trigger urgent reassessment.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Achilles Tendon Rupture"][/sc]
  • Re-rupture: Recurrence risk rises with inadequate protection or premature return to high-load activity.
  • Persistent calf weakness: Incomplete strength recovery can impair gait and sports performance long-term.
  • Treatment-related complications: Immobilisation, surgery, or reduced mobility can lead to wound issues or venous thromboembolic events.
