Medication-Induced Hip Pain (e.g. Statins)
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Medication-Induced Hip Pain (e.g. Statins)"][/sc]
  • Medication-induced hip pain refers to musculoskeletal discomfort localised to the hip region, attributable to adverse drug effects, commonly associated with lipid-lowering agents such as statins.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise medication features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.

  • Risk factors: Risk factors for medication-induced hip pain (e.g. statins) include relevant predisposition, comorbidity, and exposure history.
  • Higher-risk or missed groups: Medication-Induced Hip Pain (e.g. Statins) may be missed when presentation is atypical or obscured by comorbidity.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Medication-Induced Hip Pain (e.g. Statins)"][/sc]
  • Statins inhibit HMG-CoA reductase, reducing cholesterol synthesis but also impairing isoprenoid production, crucial for muscle cell function.
  • This disruption leads to reduced mitochondrial function and increased oxidative stress, contributing to muscle cell damage.
  • Inflammatory pathways are activated, resulting in localised muscle pain and systemic markers like elevated creatine kinase (CK).
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Medication-Induced Hip Pain (e.g. Statins)"][/sc]
  • Acute: Symptoms typically begin within weeks of initiating statin therapy.
  • Chronic: Persistent myalgia can occur with continued use, especially at higher doses.
  • Resolution: Symptoms usually resolve within weeks of discontinuation or dose reduction.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Medication-Induced Hip Pain (e.g. Statins)"][/sc]
  • Ask about myalgia - Generalised muscle aches or soreness, explained by muscle fibre damage and local inflammation.
  • Ask about hip pain - Discomfort localised to the hip due to statin-induced myopathy affecting proximal muscles.
  • Ask about weakness - Difficulty in physical activities, particularly climbing stairs, due to mitochondrial dysfunction.
  • Ask about fatigue - Generalised tiredness from systemic inflammation and muscle dysfunction.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Medication-Induced Hip Pain (e.g. Statins)"][/sc]
  • Check tenderness - Palpable tenderness over affected muscles due to localised inflammation.
  • Check reduced strength - Weakness in proximal muscle groups on clinical testing.
  • Check elevated CK levels - A biochemical sign of muscle damage.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Medication-Induced Hip Pain (e.g. Statins)"][/sc]
  • Severe pain and dark urine - Suggests rhabdomyolysis, indicating extensive muscle breakdown and risk of acute kidney injury (AKI).
  • Progressive weakness - May signal myopathy requiring urgent assessment to prevent complications.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Medication-Induced Hip Pain (e.g. Statins)"][/sc]
  • Isolated fatigue without pain - May delay diagnosis as it mimics other chronic conditions.
  • Intermittent symptoms - Symptoms that fluctuate may obscure a link to medication use.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask medication-induced hip pain (e.g. statins).
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Medication-Induced Hip Pain (e.g. Statins)"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Serum CK - Elevated levels (>5x ULN) confirm muscle damage.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Renal function tests - Evaluate for AKI in suspected rhabdomyolysis; Medication review - Assess for concomitant drugs (e.g., fibrates) that increase.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Medication-Induced Hip Pain (e.g. Statins)"][/sc]
  • Polymyalgia rheumatica - Bilateral shoulder and hip pain with morning stiffness; raised inflammatory markers.
  • Hip osteoarthritis - Pain localised to the joint with activity and crepitus on examination.
  • Idiopathic inflammatory myopathies - Symmetrical weakness with elevated CK and abnormal EMG findings.
  • Related alternative diagnosis - Distinguish from medication-induced hip pain (e.g. statins) using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Medication-Induced Hip Pain (e.g. Statins)"][/sc]
  • Rhabdomyolysis - Risk of AKI from myoglobinuria, requiring immediate intervention.
  • Chronic pain and weakness - Can persist if not recognised and managed early.
