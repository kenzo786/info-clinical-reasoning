Hyperaldosteronism
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Hyperaldosteronism"][/sc]
  • Hyperaldosteronism is a condition characterised by excessive production of aldosterone, a hormone produced by the adrenal glands.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise hyperaldosteronism features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.

  • Risk factors: Risk factors for hyperaldosteronism include relevant predisposition, comorbidity, and exposure history.
  • Higher-risk or missed groups: Hyperaldosteronism may be missed when presentation is atypical or obscured by comorbidity.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Hyperaldosteronism"][/sc]
  • Hyperaldosteronism results from increased aldosterone secretion, which acts on the distal tubules of the kidney to promote sodium retention and potassium excretion. This leads to increased extracellular fluid volume, contributing to hypertension. Hypokalaemia occurs due to excessive potassium loss, leading to muscle weakness, fatigue, and metabolic alkalosis. Primary hyperaldosteronism (e.g., due to aldosterone-producing adenoma or bilateral adrenal hyperplasia) is characterised by low renin levels, while secondary hyperaldosteronism (e.g., due to renal artery stenosis) results from elevated renin levels.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Hyperaldosteronism"][/sc]
  • The time course of hyperaldosteronism varies depending on its cause. Primary hyperaldosteronism often presents insidiously with gradually worsening hypertension and hypokalaemia over months to years. Secondary hyperaldosteronism may have a more acute onset if due to conditions like renal artery stenosis or heart failure.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Hyperaldosteronism"][/sc]
  • Ask about hypertension - Hypertension is the most common symptom, occurring due to sodium and water retention, increasing blood volume.
  • Ask about fatigue - Hypokalaemia causes muscle weakness and fatigue, as potassium is crucial for neuromuscular function.
  • Ask about muscle cramps - Potassium depletion disrupts muscle contraction, leading to cramps.
  • Ask about polyuria and Polydipsia - Hypokalaemia affects renal tubule function, impairing water reabsorption and causing increased urine output and thirst.
  • Ask about headaches - The persistent high blood pressure may lead to headaches.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Hyperaldosteronism"][/sc]
  • Check hypertension - Elevated blood pressure is common, often resistant to standard antihypertensive therapies.
  • Check hypokalaemia - Low serum potassium levels are a hallmark, particularly in primary hyperaldosteronism.
  • Check metabolic Alkalosis - Excessive potassium loss leads to compensatory hydrogen ion excretion, causing metabolic alkalosis.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Hyperaldosteronism"][/sc]
  • Severe Hypertension - Hypertension resistant to multiple drugs or sudden onset in younger individuals may indicate hyperaldosteronism.
  • Hypokalaemic Paralysis - Severe hypokalaemia can cause life-threatening muscle paralysis and arrhythmias.
  • Renal Impairment - Chronic hypokalaemia can lead to nephropathy, presenting with declining renal function.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Hyperaldosteronism"][/sc]
  • Normokalaemia - Some patients with primary hyperaldosteronism present without hypokalaemia, especially in milder cases.
  • Non-specific Fatigue - In some cases, mild symptoms like fatigue may be the only presenting features, leading to delayed diagnosis.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask hyperaldosteronism.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Hyperaldosteronism"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Plasma Aldosterone-to-Renin Ratio (ARR) - The initial screening test. Elevated aldosterone with suppressed renin suggests primary hyperaldosteronism; Serum Electrolytes - Hypokalaemia.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Adrenal CT Scan - Used to identify adrenal masses or hyperplasia in primary hyperaldosteronism; Saline Infusion Test or Fludrocortisone Suppression Test.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Hyperaldosteronism"][/sc]
  • Essential Hypertension - Most common cause of high blood pressure; distinguished by normal aldosterone and renin levels.
  • Renal Artery Stenosis - Causes secondary hyperaldosteronism with elevated renin levels, unlike primary hyperaldosteronism.
  • Cushing's Syndrome - Features include hypertension, but associated with cortisol excess rather than aldosterone.
  • Pheochromocytoma - Paroxysmal hypertension with episodic symptoms like palpitations and headaches, due to catecholamine excess.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Hyperaldosteronism"][/sc]
  • Cardiovascular Disease - Chronic uncontrolled hypertension increases the risk of stroke, myocardial infarction, and heart failure.
  • Renal Impairment - Long-standing hypokalaemia can lead to chronic kidney disease.
  • Arrhythmias - Severe hypokalaemia may cause life-threatening cardiac arrhythmias.
