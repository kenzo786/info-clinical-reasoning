Sinusitis
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Sinusitis"][/sc]
  • Sinusitis, also known as rhinosinusitis, is inflammation of the mucosal lining of the paranasal sinuses.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise sinusitis features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.

Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Sinusitis"][/sc]
  • Sinusitis begins with inflammation of the nasal mucosa (often viral or allergic), which leads to obstruction of the sinus ostia. This impairs mucus clearance, creating an ideal environment for bacterial overgrowth. Inflammation also triggers vasodilation, increasing vascular permeability and fluid accumulation, further worsening sinus congestion.
  • Acute sinusitis typically lasts less than four weeks and is often viral. Chronic sinusitis (>12 weeks) is associated with persistent inflammation, sometimes involving bacterial biofilms or fungal infections.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Sinusitis"][/sc]
  • Acute sinusitis: Typically lasts less than 4 weeks, with rapid onset of symptoms following a viral upper respiratory tract infection (URTI).
  • Subacute sinusitis: Symptoms persist between 4-12 weeks.
  • Chronic sinusitis: Symptoms last for more than 12 weeks, often with less severe intensity but persistent discomfort.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Sinusitis"][/sc]
  • Ask about facial pain/pressure - This is the most common symptom, caused by increased pressure within obstructed sinuses. It often localises to the affected sinus, such.
  • Ask about nasal congestion - Inflammation causes swelling of the nasal mucosa, obstructing airflow and drainage of sinuses.
  • Ask about purulent nasal discharge - Mucus thickens due to infection, turning yellow or green, indicating an immune response with neutrophil infiltration.
  • Ask about cough - Mucus drainage into the throat, especially when lying down, irritates the upper airway, triggering a cough reflex.
  • Ask about headache - Increased sinus pressure can refer pain to the forehead, temples, or teeth.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Sinusitis"][/sc]
  • Check tenderness over the sinuses - Direct palpation over the affected sinuses (especially maxillary and frontal) may elicit pain, reflecting underlying inflammation.
  • Check nasal mucosal swelling - Examination with an otoscope may show erythematous, swollen nasal turbinates, blocking airflow.
  • Check purulent nasal secretions - Thick, discoloured mucus can often be visualised at the nasal meatus.
  • Check postnasal drip - Secretions flowing down the posterior pharynx may be visible upon oral examination.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Sinusitis"][/sc]
  • Periorbital swelling or erythema - This may indicate orbital cellulitis, a serious complication due to the spread of infection to the orbit.
  • Severe headache - May suggest the spread of infection to the central nervous system (CNS), such as meningitis or intracranial abscess.
  • Visual disturbances - Diplopia or vision loss could indicate orbital or cavernous sinus involvement.
  • High fever and confusion - Systemic signs of severe infection or potential sepsis.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Sinusitis"][/sc]
  • Chronic sinusitis without acute symptoms - Patients may only experience mild nasal congestion or anosmia, lacking the typical pain or purulent discharge seen in acute cases.
  • Sinusitis in immunocompromised patients - These patients may have more subtle presentations with fewer systemic signs despite serious infection.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask sinusitis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Sinusitis"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Clinical diagnosis - Sinusitis is typically diagnosed based on history and physical examination. Imaging is reserved for complications or chronic cases.
  • Targeted and monitoring: CT scan of sinuses - Indicated in chronic or complicated cases to assess the extent of sinus involvement or detect complications.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Sinusitis"][/sc]
  • Orbital cellulitis - Infection spreads to the orbit, potentially leading to vision loss.
  • Meningitis - Direct extension into the cranial cavity, causing life-threatening infection.
  • Abscess formation - Either intracranial or within the sinuses themselves, requiring surgical drainage.
  • Chronic sinusitis - Prolonged inflammation can lead to irreversible damage to the sinus lining, requiring surgical management.
