Temporal Arteritis (Giant Cell Arteritis - GCA)
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Temporal Arteritis (Giant Cell Arteritis - GCA)"][/sc]
  • Temporal arteritis, also known as Giant Cell Arteritis (GCA), is a systemic vasculitis primarily affecting medium and large arteries, particularly the temporal arteries.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise temporal features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.

Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Temporal Arteritis (Giant Cell Arteritis - GCA)"][/sc]
  • GCA involves chronic granulomatous inflammation in the walls of medium and large arteries, particularly the extracranial branches of the carotid artery. T-cell and macrophage activation leads to the formation of multinucleated giant cells and inflammatory cytokine release. This results in intimal hyperplasia and luminal narrowing, reducing blood flow and increasing the risk of ischaemia in affected areas, particularly the eyes, resulting in visual loss.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Temporal Arteritis (Giant Cell Arteritis - GCA)"][/sc]
  • Onset: The onset is often subacute, with symptoms evolving over weeks to months, although it can occasionally present acutely.
  • Phases: Initial symptoms may be non-specific (e.g., fatigue, fever), followed by more specific signs like headache, jaw claudication, and vision changes. If untreated, it can lead to serious complications such as permanent vision loss, typically within days to weeks of symptom onset.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Temporal Arteritis (Giant Cell Arteritis - GCA)"][/sc]
  • Ask about headache - Localised to the temporal region, often severe and throbbing. This occurs due to inflammation of the temporal arteries.
  • Ask about jaw claudication - Pain or fatigue in the jaw muscles during chewing, resulting from ischaemia of the maxillary artery branches.
  • Ask about vision loss - Partial or complete vision loss can occur due to ischaemia of the optic nerve (anterior ischaemic optic neuropathy) resulting from artery.
  • Ask about scalp tenderness - Sensitivity when combing hair or touching the scalp, caused by inflamed temporal arteries.
  • Ask about constitutional symptoms - Fatigue, low-grade fever, weight loss, and night sweats are common, reflecting systemic inflammation.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Temporal Arteritis (Giant Cell Arteritis - GCA)"][/sc]
  • Check temporal artery abnormalities - The temporal arteries may be tender, thickened, or have reduced pulsation due to inflammation and fibrosis.
  • Check visual field defects - Partial or complete loss of vision is a result of optic nerve ischaemia.
  • Check fever - Low-grade fever is common and reflects the systemic inflammatory response.
  • Check scalp tenderness - Visible scalp tenderness on palpation, especially in the temporal region, indicates localised artery inflammation.
  • Check reduced shoulder/hip movement - If associated with PMR, restricted mobility due to pain and stiffness is common.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Temporal Arteritis (Giant Cell Arteritis - GCA)"][/sc]
  • Sudden vision loss - May indicate impending permanent blindness due to occlusion of the central retinal artery or optic nerve ischaemia.
  • Diplopia - This may signify cranial nerve involvement and could precede vision loss.
  • Stroke symptoms - GCA can involve the vertebrobasilar system or carotid arteries, leading to transient ischaemic attacks or strokes.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Temporal Arteritis (Giant Cell Arteritis - GCA)"][/sc]
  • Isolated PMR - Some patients present with only PMR symptoms (proximal muscle pain and stiffness) without overt GCA signs, leading to a delayed diagnosis.
  • Fever of unknown origin - Occurs without headache or jaw claudication and can delay diagnosis if the vasculitic origin is not suspected.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask temporal arteritis (giant cell arteritis - gca).
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Temporal Arteritis (Giant Cell Arteritis - GCA)"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Focused bedside assessment and first-line testing to assess severity and immediate risk in suspected temporal arteritis (giant cell arteritis - gca).
  • Targeted and monitoring: Temporal artery biopsy - Gold standard diagnostic test showing giant cells and granulomatous inflammation. A negative biopsy does not exclude GCA.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Temporal Arteritis (Giant Cell Arteritis - GCA)"][/sc]
  • Tension headache - Lacks the systemic features (e.g., fatigue, jaw claudication) and visual symptoms of GCA.
  • Primary open-angle glaucoma - Gradual vision loss with optic nerve damage, but no inflammatory markers or systemic symptoms.
  • Polymyalgia rheumatica (PMR) - Can overlap with GCA but without headache or vision loss.
  • Related alternative diagnosis - Distinguish from temporal arteritis (giant cell arteritis - gca) using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Temporal Arteritis (Giant Cell Arteritis - GCA)"][/sc]
  • Permanent vision loss - Occurs in up to 20% of untreated patients due to optic nerve ischaemia or central retinal artery occlusion.
  • Aortic aneurysm - GCA can involve the aorta, leading to aneurysms that may rupture if untreated.
  • Stroke - Cerebral ischaemia due to vertebrobasilar or carotid artery involvement.
