Cholecystitis
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Cholecystitis"][/sc]
  • Cholecystitis is inflammation of the gallbladder, most commonly due to obstruction of the cystic duct by gallstones (calculous cholecystitis) but can also occur in the absence of stones (acalculous cholecystitis).
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise cholecystitis features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.

Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Cholecystitis"][/sc]
  • Cholecystitis typically begins with an obstruction of the cystic duct, often by a gallstone, leading to stasis of bile.
  • Bile stasis promotes bacterial overgrowth, mainly by Gram-negative bacteria like Escherichia coli and Klebsiella, initiating inflammation of the gallbladder mucosa.
  • Inflammation progresses to involve the entire gallbladder wall, causing oedema, distension, and potentially ischaemia, which can lead to gangrene or perforation if untreated.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Cholecystitis"][/sc]
  • Initial Phase: Obstruction of the cystic duct causes acute symptoms (within hours to a day), such as pain and tenderness.
  • Progression: Over 1-3 days, inflammation may increase, with fever and worsening pain, leading to potential complications (e.g., abscess or gangrene).
  • Resolution/Complication Phase: With treatment, inflammation typically resolves within a few days; without treatment, severe complications like perforation or sepsis can occur.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Cholecystitis"][/sc]
  • Ask about right Upper Quadrant (RUQ) Pain: Commonest presenting symptom, resulting from distension and inflammation of the gallbladder, often radiating to the right shoulder.
  • Ask about nausea and Vomiting: Often accompany RUQ pain due to irritation of the gastrointestinal tract by inflammation.
  • Ask about fever and Chills: Fever occurs as a systemic response to infection and inflammation, indicating more severe inflammation.
  • Ask about anorexia: Loss of appetite is common due to systemic inflammatory response and discomfort.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Cholecystitis"][/sc]
  • Check murphy’s Sign: Tenderness and sudden inspiratory arrest on palpation of the RUQ, indicative of gallbladder inflammation and contact with the inflamed peritoneum.
  • Check fever: Typically low-grade, resulting from the systemic inflammatory response; high fevers may suggest complications like abscess or gangrene.
  • Check tachycardia: Common in response to pain, fever, or possible systemic inflammation.
  • Check jaundice: Occasionally present if bile duct obstruction or liver involvement occurs, due to increased bilirubin levels.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Cholecystitis"][/sc]
  • Severe, Persistent RUQ Pain: May indicate impending complications such as gangrene or perforation.
  • High Fever (>39°C): Suggests potential abscess formation or systemic infection.
  • Hypotension or Septic Shock: Could indicate biliary sepsis, often a result of bacterial spread, requiring urgent treatment.
  • Peritonitis Signs (e.g., Rebound Tenderness): Indicative of gallbladder perforation or rupture, an emergency requiring surgical intervention.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Cholecystitis"][/sc]
  • Elderly Patients: May present with vague symptoms like malaise or confusion due to reduced inflammatory response, often leading to diagnostic delays.
  • Diabetics: More prone to severe infections (emphysematous cholecystitis), often with subtler symptoms.
  • Pain in Atypical Locations: Left-sided or epigastric pain, especially in individuals with anatomic variants or obesity.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features or comorbidity can mask the underlying diagnosis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Cholecystitis"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Ultrasound: First-line imaging; findings include gallbladder wall thickening (>3 mm), pericholecystic fluid, and presence of gallstones, indicating inflammation and possible obstruction.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Blood Tests: Elevated white cell count, C-reactive protein (CRP), and liver function tests (LFTs) may show mild transaminitis or bilirubin elevation.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Cholecystitis"][/sc]
  • A 50-year-old woman presents with a 24-hour history of severe right upper quadrant pain radiating to the shoulder, nausea, fever, and tenderness on deep palpation of the RUQ. Murphy's sign.
  • Adult with symptom pattern and objective findings suggestive of cholecystitis, requiring focused confirmation and risk stratification.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Cholecystitis"][/sc]
  • Peptic Ulcer Disease - Often epigastric pain, worse with meals; lacks RUQ tenderness and Murphy’s sign.
  • Pancreatitis - Epigastric pain radiating to the back; elevated amylase/lipase distinguishes it.
  • Acute Hepatitis - Diffuse abdominal pain with jaundice and elevated LFTs; less localised RUQ tenderness.
  • Biliary Colic - Pain similar to cholecystitis but without fever or leucocytosis; typically resolves after a few.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Cholecystitis"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Assess physiological stability and urgent complications at presentation, and initiate early stabilisation where required.
  • Core care: Deliver condition-specific treatment for cholecystitis, address underlying drivers, and involve appropriate specialist support.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Reassess symptom trajectory, objective markers, and treatment response over time to detect complications early.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Worsening symptoms, physiological deterioration, or failure to improve should trigger urgent reassessment for severe or alternative pathology in cholecystitis.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Cholecystitis"][/sc]
  • Gangrene and perforation: Prolonged gallbladder ischaemia can cause necrosis, perforation, and secondary peritonitis.
  • Empyema: Abscess formation within the gallbladder from untreated infection, leading to risk of sepsis.
  • Biliary Sepsis: Systemic infection due to bacterial translocation; potentially fatal if untreated.
