Wrist Tendinopathy
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Wrist Tendinopathy"][/sc]
  • Wrist tendinopathy refers to a spectrum of tendon disorders in the wrist, characterised by pain, swelling, and functional impairment.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise wrist features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.

Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Wrist Tendinopathy"][/sc]
  • Repetitive strain or acute overload causes microtears in the tendon fibres.
  • Failure of adequate repair leads to degeneration and disorganisation of collagen fibres (tendinosis).
  • Neurovascular changes and increased sensory nerve endings contribute to chronic pain.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Wrist Tendinopathy"][/sc]
  • Acute phase: Onset of pain following injury or repetitive use.
  • Subacute phase: Persistent symptoms with activity limitations.
  • Chronic phase: Longstanding pain and functional deficits due to poor healing or recurrent injury.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Wrist Tendinopathy"][/sc]
  • Ask about pain - Localised pain over the affected tendon, worsened by movement or palpation, due to microscopic tendon damage and nerve sensitisation.
  • Ask about swelling - Swelling over the wrist due to extracellular matrix disruption and increased vascular permeability.
  • Ask about weakness - Reduced grip strength from pain inhibition and impaired tendon function.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Wrist Tendinopathy"][/sc]
  • Check tenderness - Localised tenderness over the affected tendon due to inflammation or degenerative changes.
  • Check crepitus - Palpable or audible crepitus with movement, caused by tendon thickening or adhesions.
  • Check reduced range of motion - Limitation in active or passive wrist movement, reflecting pain or structural changes.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Wrist Tendinopathy"][/sc]
  • Severe pain at rest - May indicate tendon rupture or infection (septic tenosynovitis).
  • Erythema and warmth - Suggests an inflammatory or infectious aetiology.
  • Systemic symptoms (fever, malaise) - Raises suspicion for infection or inflammatory arthritis.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Wrist Tendinopathy"][/sc]
  • Chronic painless dysfunction, often seen in older adults with longstanding tendinosis without acute inflammation.
  • Diffuse wrist pain without focal tenderness, mimicking other pathologies like carpal tunnel syndrome.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask wrist tendinopathy.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Wrist Tendinopathy"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Ultrasound - Identifies tendon thickening, hypoechoic areas, or neovascularisation, correlating with tendinopathy.
  • Targeted and monitoring: MRI - Reveals tendon degeneration and adjacent soft tissue changes; useful in refractory cases; Plain radiographs - Typically normal but may.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Wrist Tendinopathy"][/sc]
  • De Quervain’s tenosynovitis - Affects the first dorsal compartment; differs with radial-sided pain and positive Finkelstein test.
  • Carpal tunnel syndrome - Features paresthesia and weakness in the median nerve distribution, not associated with tendon tenderness.
  • Wrist osteoarthritis - Pain and stiffness with radiographic evidence of joint degeneration.
  • Related alternative diagnosis - Distinguish from wrist tendinopathy using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Wrist Tendinopathy"][/sc]
  • Chronic pain - Due to persistent degeneration and nerve sensitisation.
  • Tendon rupture - Rare but serious, resulting from untreated or severe tendinopathy.
  • Functional impairment - Long-term disability from reduced tendon elasticity and strength.
