Carpal Tunnel Syndrome
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Carpal Tunnel Syndrome"][/sc]
  • Carpal Tunnel Syndrome (CTS) is a compressive neuropathy caused by entrapment of the median nerve as it passes through the carpal tunnel at the wrist.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise carpal features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.

Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Carpal Tunnel Syndrome"][/sc]
  • Compression of the median nerve within the carpal tunnel, a narrow passage bounded by carpal bones and the transverse carpal ligament.
  • Increased pressure due to inflammation, oedema, repetitive motion, or anatomical variations.
  • Nerve ischemia and impaired axonal transport lead to sensory and motor dysfunction, primarily affecting the first three digits.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Carpal Tunnel Syndrome"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Intermittent symptoms, often at night or after specific activities.
  • Progressive phase: Persistent symptoms with increasing functional impairment and weakness.
  • Chronic phase: Potential irreversible nerve damage and muscle atrophy if untreated.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Carpal Tunnel Syndrome"][/sc]
  • Ask about numbness and tingling - Typically affects the thumb, index, and middle fingers due to median nerve distribution. Caused by sensory nerve compression.
  • Ask about night-time symptoms - Worsening at night due to wrist positioning and fluid redistribution increasing tunnel pressure.
  • Ask about pain - Localised to the wrist or radiating proximally or distally, related to nerve inflammation.
  • Ask about weakness - Difficulty gripping or pinching due to motor fibre involvement and muscle denervation.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Carpal Tunnel Syndrome"][/sc]
  • Check tinel's sign - Tingling or discomfort when tapping over the median nerve at the wrist. Indicates nerve irritation.
  • Check phalen's manoeuvre - Symptoms reproduced with wrist flexion, reflecting increased pressure in the carpal tunnel.
  • Check thenar muscle atrophy - Wasting of the thenar eminence due to prolonged median nerve compression.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Carpal Tunnel Syndrome"][/sc]
  • Sudden severe weakness - May indicate acute nerve injury or ischaemia.
  • Persistent numbness or atrophy - Suggests chronic and potentially irreversible nerve damage.
  • Pain unrelated to activity - May indicate alternative diagnoses such as cervical radiculopathy or peripheral neuropathy.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Carpal Tunnel Syndrome"][/sc]
  • Symptoms outside the median nerve distribution - Can occur due to coexistent conditions like double crush syndrome.
  • Pain without numbness - May occur in early stages or atypical anatomical presentations.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features or comorbidity can mask the underlying diagnosis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Carpal Tunnel Syndrome"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Nerve conduction studies - Prolonged distal latency and reduced conduction velocity. Reflects impaired nerve transmission.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Electromyography (EMG) - Evidence of denervation in thenar muscles in advanced cases; Ultrasound - Median nerve enlargement at the wrist, indicating.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Carpal Tunnel Syndrome"][/sc]
  • A 45-year-old woman presents with a 6-month history of nocturnal paraesthesia and intermittent pain in the right hand, worsening with prolonged computer use. Examination reveals positive Phalen's manoeuvre and Tinel's.
  • Adult with symptom pattern and objective findings suggestive of carpal tunnel syndrome, requiring focused confirmation and risk stratification.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Carpal Tunnel Syndrome"][/sc]
  • Cervical radiculopathy - Radiating pain and paraesthesia originating from the neck, often with neck pain and dermatomal.
  • Diabetic neuropathy - Diffuse rather than focal symptoms, often bilateral and associated with a history of diabetes.
  • Rheumatoid arthritis - Joint pain, swelling, and stiffness in addition to nerve compression symptoms.
  • Alternative serious diagnosis - Distinguishing history, examination, and targeted investigations do not support carpal tunnel syndrome.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Carpal Tunnel Syndrome"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Assess physiological stability and urgent complications at presentation, and initiate early stabilisation where required.
  • Core care: Deliver condition-specific treatment for carpal tunnel syndrome, address underlying drivers, and involve appropriate specialist support.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Reassess symptom trajectory, objective markers, and treatment response over time to detect complications early.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Worsening symptoms, physiological deterioration, or failure to improve should trigger urgent reassessment for severe or alternative pathology in carpal tunnel syndrome.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Carpal Tunnel Syndrome"][/sc]
  • Permanent nerve damage - Leading to chronic pain and functional impairment.
  • Muscle atrophy - Particularly of the thenar muscles, resulting in diminished hand function.
  • Post-surgical complications - Including scar tenderness and incomplete symptom resolution.
