Musculoskeletal Chest Pain
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Musculoskeletal Chest Pain"][/sc]
  • Musculoskeletal chest pain refers to pain arising from the muscles, bones, joints, and soft tissues of the chest wall.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise musculoskeletal features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Musculoskeletal Chest Pain"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Musculoskeletal Chest Pain"][/sc]
  • Risk Factors: Physical overuse, trauma, poor posture, anxiety-related muscle tension, and inflammatory conditions such as rheumatoid arthritis increase the likelihood of musculoskeletal chest pain.
  • Prevention: Maintaining good posture, avoiding overexertion, engaging in strength training, and managing stress can help reduce the risk. Early treatment of osteoporosis may prevent rib fractures.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Musculoskeletal Chest Pain"][/sc]
  • Musculoskeletal chest pain results from mechanical stress, inflammation, or trauma affecting the chest wall structures.
  • Muscle strain: Overuse or injury leads to microtears and inflammation, causing pain.
  • Costochondritis: Inflammation of costal cartilage causes localised pain, exacerbated by movement.
  • Rib fractures: Direct trauma leads to periosteal irritation and sharp, localised pain.
  • Slipping rib syndrome: Hypermobile ribs irritate intercostal nerves, producing sharp pain.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Musculoskeletal Chest Pain"][/sc]
  • Acute: Immediate onset post-trauma or exertion, lasting days to weeks.
  • Subacute: Persistent symptoms for weeks but improving over time.
  • Chronic: Lasts over 12 weeks, often due to incomplete healing or recurrent strain.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Musculoskeletal Chest Pain"][/sc]
  • Ask about localised chest pain: Well-defined, reproducible pain at the affected site.
  • Ask about pain worsened by movement: Indicates muscle or joint involvement.
  • Ask about pain on deep breathing: Suggests rib or intercostal muscle irritation.
  • Ask about absence of systemic symptoms: No fever, shortness of breath, or cardiac signs.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Musculoskeletal Chest Pain"][/sc]
  • Check tenderness on palpation: Pain reproduced by pressing the affected area.
  • Check swelling or bruising: Present in trauma-related cases.
  • Check restricted movement: Pain-induced limitation of upper body motion.
  • Check normal heart and lung exams: Helps differentiate from cardiac or pulmonary causes.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Musculoskeletal Chest Pain"][/sc]
  • Persistent, unexplained pain: May suggest malignancy or infection.
  • Systemic symptoms (fever, weight loss): Raises suspicion of infection or malignancy.
  • Neurological deficits: Numbness or weakness suggests nerve involvement.
  • Significant trauma: Increases the likelihood of fractures or internal injuries.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Musculoskeletal Chest Pain"][/sc]
  • Diffuse chest discomfort: Can be mistaken for cardiac pain.
  • Radiation to the arms or back: May mimic angina.
  • Intermittent sharp pains: Can resemble pleuritic pain or neuralgia.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask musculoskeletal chest pain.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Musculoskeletal Chest Pain"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Clinical Examination: Key to diagnosis; palpation reproduces pain; Chest X-ray: Useful for ruling out rib fractures.
  • Targeted and monitoring: ECG: To exclude cardiac causes if symptoms are atypical; CRP/ESR: May be raised in costochondritis or inflammatory conditions.

MSK Chest Pain Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Musculoskeletal Chest Pain"][/sc]
  • Acute Coronary Syndrome - Pain not typically reproducible on palpation, often associated with exertion.
  • Pulmonary Embolism - Sudden onset pleuritic pain with dyspnoea.
  • Pleuritis - Pain worse with deep breaths, but no tenderness on palpation.
  • Related alternative diagnosis - Distinguish from musculoskeletal chest pain using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.

  • Immediate priorities: Assess severity, physiological stability, and immediate complications.
  • Core care: Deliver condition-specific management for musculoskeletal chest pain, treat underlying drivers, and coordinate appropriate specialist input.

  • Escalation and safety-netting: Escalate urgently for clinical deterioration, evolving red flags, or failure to improve in suspected musculoskeletal chest pain.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Musculoskeletal Chest Pain"][/sc]
  • Chronic pain: Due to inadequate healing or persistent inflammation.
  • Functional impairment: Reduced mobility if pain is severe.
