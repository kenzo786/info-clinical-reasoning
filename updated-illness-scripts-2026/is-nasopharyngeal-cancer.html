Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Nasopharyngeal Cancer"][/sc]
  • Nasopharyngeal cancer (NPC) is a malignant tumour that arises from the epithelial lining of the nasopharynx, the uppermost part of the throat behind the nose.

Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Nasopharyngeal Cancer"][/sc]
  • NPC typically originates in the epithelial cells of the nasopharynx. Its development is linked to a combination of genetic susceptibility, environmental factors, and Epstein-Barr Virus (EBV) infection. EBV infection leads to the transformation of infected nasopharyngeal epithelial cells into malignant cells. Genetic predisposition and consumption of preserved foods (high in nitrosamines) also play a role in tumour development.
  • The tumour invades local structures such as the nasal cavity, oropharynx, and skull base, and can metastasise to cervical lymph nodes and distant organs.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Nasopharyngeal Cancer"][/sc]
  • Early stage: NPC often presents asymptomatically or with mild, non-specific symptoms such as nasal obstruction or a painless neck mass (due to lymphadenopathy).
  • Advanced stage: Tumour growth leads to symptoms related to local invasion (e.g., nasal bleeding, hearing loss, cranial nerve palsies) and distant metastases (e.g., bone pain, respiratory symptoms).
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Nasopharyngeal Cancer"][/sc]
  • Ask about painless neck mass - Often the initial presentation due to lymphatic spread; occurs in approximately 75% of cases. The nasopharynx has rich lymphatic drainage.
  • Ask about nasal obstruction - Caused by tumour expansion into the nasal cavity.
  • Ask about nasal bleeding (epistaxis) - Tumour erosion of blood vessels leads to spontaneous bleeding from the nose.
  • Ask about tinnitus or hearing loss - Eustachian tube blockage from tumour invasion may cause conductive hearing loss and tinnitus, especially in unilateral cases.
  • Ask about headaches - Skull base involvement and intracranial invasion can result in headaches due to increased pressure or nerve irritation.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Nasopharyngeal Cancer"][/sc]
  • Check cervical lymphadenopathy - Enlarged, firm, non-tender lymph nodes, often unilateral, due to early regional metastasis.
  • Check nasal mass or discharge - Visible mass on nasoendoscopy or mucopurulent discharge from tumour involvement.
  • Check hearing loss (conductive) - Secondary to Eustachian tube dysfunction caused by tumour invasion, leading to fluid build-up in the middle ear.
  • Check facial numbness - Involvement of the trigeminal nerve (CN V) from skull base invasion.
  • Check ocular signs - Diplopia or ptosis due to cranial nerve III, IV, or VI involvement.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Nasopharyngeal Cancer"][/sc]
  • Cranial nerve palsies - Indicates advanced local invasion into the skull base and nerves; may signal inoperability or the need for aggressive therapy.
  • Persistent unilateral hearing loss - Suggests Eustachian tube blockage and possible tumour extension into the ear canal.
  • Epistaxis with a neck mass - Strong indicator of nasopharyngeal malignancy and should prompt urgent investigation.
  • Bone pain - May signify distant metastases, particularly to the bones, and indicates advanced disease.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Nasopharyngeal Cancer"][/sc]
  • Asymptomatic - Some patients, particularly in early stages, may only present with cervical lymphadenopathy, which can delay diagnosis.
  • Unilateral ear symptoms without other nasopharyngeal symptoms - Isolated ear pain or hearing loss without nasal or throat symptoms can lead to misdiagnosis as an ear infection or otitis media.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask nasopharyngeal cancer illness script.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Nasopharyngeal Cancer"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Nasoendoscopy - Direct visualisation of the nasopharynx to identify any tumour or suspicious lesions; Biopsy - Histopathological confirmation of malignancy; typically.
  • Targeted and monitoring: EBV serology - EBV DNA or antibody titres (e.g., EBV viral capsid antigen) may support the diagnosis as EBV is associated.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Nasopharyngeal Cancer"][/sc]
  • Metastasis - NPC commonly metastasises to cervical lymph nodes, bones, lungs, and liver. Metastasis is present in up to 10% of newly diagnosed cases.
  • Cranial nerve damage - Tumour invasion into the skull base can cause permanent nerve damage, leading to facial paralysis or diplopia.
  • Radiation-induced damage - Xerostomia, hearing loss, and damage to nearby tissues are common side effects of radiotherapy.
  • Recurrence - Recurrence rates are significant, particularly in locally advanced disease, and require ongoing surveillance.
