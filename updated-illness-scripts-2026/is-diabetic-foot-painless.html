Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Diabetic Foot (Painless)"][/sc]
  • Chronic hyperglycaemia causes glycosylation of proteins and lipids, leading to endothelial dysfunction and microvascular damage.
  • Peripheral neuropathy develops due to axonal degeneration and demyelination, impairing sensory, motor, and autonomic nerves.
  • Sensory loss increases the risk of unnoticed trauma. Autonomic dysfunction leads to dry skin and fissures, creating entry points for infections.
  • Peripheral arterial disease reduces blood supply, impairing wound healing and promoting ischaemia.
  • Chronic infections further damage tissue, exacerbating ulceration and gangrene risk.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Diabetic Foot (Painless)"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Sensory loss and minor unnoticed injuries due to neuropathy.
  • Progression: Development of ulcers, often with superimposed infections. Autonomic dysfunction may cause skin changes (e.g., dryness).
  • Advanced phase: Chronic non-healing ulcers with complications such as osteomyelitis or gangrene, leading to a risk of amputation.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Diabetic Foot (Painless)"][/sc]
  • Ask about ulceration: Painless ulcers due to sensory neuropathy. Patients often notice them incidentally or when complications occur.
  • Ask about skin changes: Dry, cracked skin from autonomic dysfunction increases susceptibility to infection.
  • Ask about swelling: May occur due to infection or inflammation, related to localised tissue damage.
  • Ask about fever or malaise: Indicates systemic infection (e.g., cellulitis or sepsis).
  • Ask about reduced sensation: Patients may report numbness or tingling before complete sensory loss sets, with associated clinical features.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Diabetic Foot (Painless)"][/sc]
  • Check painless ulcers: Often on weight-bearing areas, such as the plantar aspect of the foot.
  • Check decreased sensation: Assessed with monofilament testing or tuning fork.
  • Check skin dryness and fissures: Due to autonomic neuropathy.
  • Check peripheral pulses: May be diminished or absent due to PAD.
  • Check infection signs: Redness, swelling, purulent discharge, or malodour.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Diabetic Foot (Painless)"][/sc]
  • Osteomyelitis: Suggests deep infection, often associated with severe ulceration and systemic signs like fever.
  • Gangrene: Indicates critical ischaemia and tissue necrosis, requiring urgent vascular intervention.
  • Sepsis: Systemic infection with hypotension, tachycardia, and altered mental status.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Diabetic Foot (Painless)"][/sc]
  • Minimal swelling or redness: Seen in immunocompromised patients with poor inflammatory response.
  • Painless necrosis: Without preceding ulcer, due to advanced PAD and neuropathy.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask diabetic foot (painless).
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Diabetic Foot (Painless)"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Blood glucose and HbA1c: Evaluate glycaemic control. Elevated levels suggest poor diabetes management; Monofilament test: Assess peripheral neuropathy. Reduced sensation indicates.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Ankle-brachial index (ABI): Assesses PAD. ABI < 0.9 suggests significant arterial disease; Wound culture and imaging: Detects infection and underlying osteomyelitis.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Diabetic Foot (Painless)"][/sc]
  • Venous ulcer - Painful, often medial malleolus, associated with varicose veins.
  • Arterial ulcer - Painful ulcers on distal extremities with significant PAD but without neuropathy.
  • Pressure ulcer - Occurs in immobile patients at pressure points; often painful without neuropathy.
  • Related alternative diagnosis - Distinguish from diabetic foot (painless) using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Diabetic Foot (Painless)"][/sc]
  • Osteomyelitis: Occurs in 20–60% of severe foot infections. Chronic infection of bone can lead to amputation.
  • Amputation: Risk increases with infection, PAD, and poor glycaemic control.
  • Sepsis: Life-threatening systemic infection from untreated wounds.
