Slipped Capital Femoral Epiphysis (SCFE)
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Slipped Capital Femoral Epiphysis (SCFE)"][/sc]
  • Slipped capital femoral epiphysis (SCFE) is a condition in which the proximal femoral epiphysis (growth plate) slips posteriorly and inferiorly relative to the femoral neck.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise slipped features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.

Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Slipped Capital Femoral Epiphysis (SCFE)"][/sc]
  • The primary mechanism involves weakness in the proximal femoral growth plate, which is less stable during periods of rapid growth or hormonal changes, making it susceptible to mechanical stress and shear forces.
  • Endocrine factors, such as hypothyroidism, obesity, or growth hormone abnormalities, may also contribute to growth plate instability.
  • The epiphysis "slips" off the femoral neck due to these forces, resulting in malalignment and impaired blood supply to the femoral head, risking avascular necrosis.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Slipped Capital Femoral Epiphysis (SCFE)"][/sc]
  • Acute: Sudden onset of hip or knee pain with an inability to bear weight, typically following minor trauma.
  • Chronic: More gradual onset of pain over weeks or months, often presenting with a limp and intermittent discomfort.
  • Acute-on-chronic: Symptoms gradually worsen until a specific incident causes a significant increase in pain and functional impairment.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Slipped Capital Femoral Epiphysis (SCFE)"][/sc]
  • Ask about hip pain - The most common presenting symptom, often due to inflammation and abnormal pressure on the joint surfaces.
  • Ask about knee pain - Referred pain to the knee is frequent, as the obturator nerve can transmit hip joint pain to the knee.
  • Ask about limping or difficulty walking - Affected children may adopt an antalgic gait due to pain and instability of the femoral epiphysis.
  • Ask about stiffness and reduced range of motion - Results from synovial irritation and mechanical restriction due to the slipped epiphysis.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Slipped Capital Femoral Epiphysis (SCFE)"][/sc]
  • Check restricted internal rotation of the hip - Due to mechanical displacement and inflammation limiting joint mobility.
  • Check obligate external rotation - The leg often externally rotates when flexed, as this position minimises strain on the femoral epiphysis.
  • Check positive Trendelenburg sign - Indicative of weakness in hip abductors or joint instability caused by the slipped epiphysis.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Slipped Capital Femoral Epiphysis (SCFE)"][/sc]
  • Inability to bear weight - Signifies possible severe slippage or avascular necrosis, requiring urgent orthopaedic intervention.
  • Sudden worsening of pain - May indicate an acute slip in a chronic SCFE, increasing the risk of complications like avascular necrosis.
  • Leg length discrepancy - Suggestive of progressive slippage, leading to deformity and functional impairment.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Slipped Capital Femoral Epiphysis (SCFE)"][/sc]
  • Painless limp - Some children may present with minimal or no pain but have significant mechanical symptoms.
  • Bilateral hip involvement - Although SCFE is typically unilateral, bilateral cases may be seen, particularly in children with underlying endocrine disorders.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask slipped capital femoral epiphysis (scfe).
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Slipped Capital Femoral Epiphysis (SCFE)"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: X-ray (AP and frog-leg lateral views) - Diagnostic imaging showing displacement of the femoral head relative to the femoral neck; characteristic.
  • Targeted and monitoring: MRI (if X-ray inconclusive) - May show early changes such as oedema in the growth plate, useful in atypical or early.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Slipped Capital Femoral Epiphysis (SCFE)"][/sc]
  • Hip fracture - Often has a history of acute trauma with more abrupt onset of pain.
  • Legg-Calvé-Perthes disease - Typically presents in younger children with gradual onset of hip pain and limp.
  • Juvenile idiopathic arthritis - May present with hip pain but is typically associated with systemic symptoms and inflammation.
  • Transient synovitis - Presents acutely with hip pain but usually resolves within 1-2 weeks.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Slipped Capital Femoral Epiphysis (SCFE)"][/sc]
  • Avascular necrosis of the femoral head - Occurs if blood supply is compromised, leading to long-term disability if untreated.
  • Chondrolysis - Loss of articular cartilage can lead to chronic pain and stiffness, sometimes requiring further surgical intervention.
  • Leg length discrepancy - Progressive slippage may result in leg length differences, impacting gait and function.
