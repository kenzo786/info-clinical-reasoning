Simple Constipation
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Simple Constipation"][/sc]
  • Simple constipation is a common functional gastrointestinal disorder characterised by infrequent, difficult, or painful defaecation.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise simple features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.

  • Risk factors: Risk factors for simple constipation include relevant predisposition, comorbidity, and exposure history.
  • Higher-risk or missed groups: Simple Constipation may be missed when presentation is atypical or obscured by comorbidity.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Simple Constipation"][/sc]
  • Dietary and Lifestyle Factors: Low dietary fibre, dehydration, and sedentary lifestyle reduce stool bulk and slow colonic transit time.
  • Neuromuscular Function: Alterations in colonic motility, potentially due to delayed colonic transit or inadequate propulsive contractions, can limit stool movement.
  • Pelvic Floor Dysfunction: Dyssynergic defaecation, where the pelvic muscles fail to relax during bowel movements, can lead to incomplete evacuation and worsened symptoms.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Simple Constipation"][/sc]
  • Acute Constipation: Short-term, often due to temporary dietary changes, stress, or medications (e.g., opioids).
  • Chronic Constipation: Symptoms persisting over three months, with episodic or continuous patterns depending on lifestyle and management consistency.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Simple Constipation"][/sc]
  • Ask about infrequent Bowel Movements - Defined as fewer than three per week; related to slowed colonic transit and insufficient dietary fibre or fluids.
  • Ask about straining - Due to hard, dry stool that is difficult to pass; results from low fluid intake and prolonged stool transit time.
  • Ask about sensation of Incomplete Evacuation - Caused by retained stool and dyssynergic defaecation; may lead to discomfort and continued straining.
  • Ask about abdominal Discomfort/Bloating - Accumulation of stool and gas in the colon can stretch the bowel wall, causing bloating and cramping sensations.
  • Ask about hard or Lumpy Stools - Results from prolonged transit time, where more water is absorbed from the stool in the colon.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Simple Constipation"][/sc]
  • Check abdominal Tenderness - Mild tenderness, particularly in the lower abdomen, may indicate retained stool or gas build-up.
  • Check palpable Faecal Mass - Felt on palpation of the lower abdomen, indicating faecal loading in the rectosigmoid colon.
  • Check rectal Fullness on DRE (Digital Rectal Examination) - May confirm the presence of hard stool in the rectum, supporting a diagnosis of constipation.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Simple Constipation"][/sc]
  • Weight Loss - Unintentional weight loss may suggest malignancy or metabolic disorder affecting bowel function.
  • Rectal Bleeding - Bright red blood or melena can indicate colorectal cancer, haemorrhoids, or anal fissures.
  • Severe Pain - Constant, severe pain may signal bowel obstruction or ischaemia, requiring immediate investigation.
  • Iron Deficiency Anaemia - Anaemia may indicate chronic GI blood loss, often from a malignancy in older patients.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Simple Constipation"][/sc]
  • Chronic Fatigue - Due to discomfort and possible malabsorption, some patients may present with fatigue without obvious GI symptoms.
  • Urinary Symptoms - Faecal impaction can cause bladder pressure, leading to urinary frequency or retention.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask simple constipation.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Simple Constipation"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Blood Tests: Full blood count (FBC), ferritin, and thyroid function tests (TFTs) to exclude anaemia, thyroid dysfunction, or metabolic disorders.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Abdominal X-ray: May reveal faecal loading in the colon, supporting constipation diagnosis; Colonoscopy (if red flags): Performed if there is unexplained.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Simple Constipation"][/sc]
  • Irritable Bowel Syndrome (IBS) - Constipation subtype presents with abdominal pain relieved by defaecation; no alarm features.
  • Colorectal Cancer - Often has red flags (e.g., weight loss, anaemia); requires colonoscopy if suspected.
  • Hypothyroidism - Causes constipation with fatigue and weight gain; diagnosed via TFTs.
  • Obstructive Lesions - Bowel obstruction may present with severe pain, vomiting, and no gas or stool passage.

  • Escalation and safety-netting: Escalate urgently for clinical deterioration, evolving red flags, or failure to improve in suspected simple constipation.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Simple Constipation"][/sc]
  • Faecal Impaction: Common in chronic cases, can cause rectal pain and incontinence.
  • Anal Fissures or Haemorrhoids: Caused by straining and hard stools, leading to bleeding and pain during defaecation.
  • Diverticulosis: Chronic pressure in the colon can result in the formation of diverticula, potentially leading to inflammation.
