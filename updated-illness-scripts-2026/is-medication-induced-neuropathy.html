Medication-Induced Neuropathy
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Medication-Induced Neuropathy"][/sc]
  • Medication-induced neuropathy refers to peripheral nerve damage caused by the use of certain drugs.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise medication features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Medication-Induced Neuropathy"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Medication-Induced Neuropathy"][/sc]
  • Risk factors: Risk factors for medication-induced neuropathy include relevant predisposition, comorbidity, and exposure history.
  • Higher-risk or missed groups: Medication-Induced Neuropathy may be missed when presentation is atypical or obscured by comorbidity.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Medication-Induced Neuropathy"][/sc]
  • Medications can cause neuropathy through several mechanisms:
    • Axonal degeneration: Most drugs, particularly chemotherapeutics, impair microtubule formation, disrupting axonal transport, leading to axonal injury.
    • Myelin damage: Some agents, like certain antibiotics, cause direct myelin sheath damage, leading to demyelination and nerve dysfunction.
    • Vascular toxicity: Drugs like platinum-based chemotherapies induce endothelial damage, reducing blood flow and causing ischaemic neuropathy.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Medication-Induced Neuropathy"][/sc]
  • Neuropathy onset is typically gradual:
    • Acute phase: Symptoms can emerge within days to weeks of starting the offending medication (e.g., vincristine-induced neuropathy).
    • Subacute phase: Symptoms often build progressively, worsening over weeks to months during ongoing treatment (e.g., platinum-based chemotherapies).
    • Chronic phase: Neuropathy can persist or worsen after discontinuation of the drug, with recovery taking months to years.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Medication-Induced Neuropathy"][/sc]
  • Ask about distal paraesthesia: Patients typically report numbness and tingling, beginning in the feet and hands due to the length-dependent nature of axonal damage.
  • Ask about burning pain: Burning or shooting pain may result from damage to small sensory fibres, disrupting pain perception.
  • Ask about muscle weakness: Particularly in cases involving motor neuropathy, as seen with drugs like vincristine, where motor axons are affected.
  • Ask about loss of balance: Due to sensory loss in the feet, patients may struggle with proprioception, especially in the dark.
  • Ask about autonomic dysfunction: Less common but may include constipation, orthostatic hypotension, or urinary retention, especially with medications affecting autonomic nerves.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Medication-Induced Neuropathy"][/sc]
  • Check reduced reflexes: Diminished or absent ankle reflexes are common due to peripheral nerve dysfunction, primarily affecting large myelinated fibres.
  • Check distal muscle atrophy: Seen in more severe or chronic cases, particularly with motor involvement, indicating long-standing denervation.
  • Check altered sensation: Patients may have decreased vibration or pinprick sensation distally, reflecting sensory fibre involvement.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Medication-Induced Neuropathy"][/sc]
  • Rapid progression of weakness: This suggests more severe axonal damage or Guillain-Barré-like syndromes caused by drugs such as checkpoint inhibitors.
  • Severe autonomic dysfunction: Could indicate involvement of autonomic nerves, requiring immediate attention, as seen in cases with antiretroviral therapy.
  • Respiratory muscle weakness: Rare but serious, especially with vincristine, where involvement of phrenic nerves may lead to respiratory failure.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Medication-Induced Neuropathy"][/sc]
  • Mononeuropathy multiplex: Some medications may cause isolated nerve involvement, resulting in atypical focal neuropathies rather than a length-dependent polyneuropathy.
  • Pain without sensory loss: Certain drugs, particularly antiretrovirals, may cause painful neuropathy without objective sensory findings.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask medication-induced neuropathy.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Medication-Induced Neuropathy"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Nerve conduction studies (NCS): Show reduced amplitude of sensory and motor responses in axonal neuropathies, with or without prolonged latencies depending.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Electromyography (EMG): Used to assess muscle denervation and reinnervation, revealing chronic denervation in motor neuropathies; Serum B12 and folate: Rule out.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Medication-Induced Neuropathy"][/sc]
  • Diabetic neuropathy - Distinguish from medication-induced neuropathy using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.
  • Chronic inflammatory demyelinating polyneuropathy (CIDP) - More likely to have progressive motor weakness and responds to immunotherapy, unlike most drug-induced.
  • Alcohol-related neuropathy - History of chronic alcohol use, with similar distal sensory symptoms, but usually slower onset.
  • Related alternative diagnosis - Distinguish from medication-induced neuropathy using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Medication-Induced Neuropathy"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Assess severity, physiological stability, and immediate complications.
  • Core care: Deliver condition-specific management for medication-induced neuropathy, treat underlying drivers, and coordinate appropriate specialist input.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Reassess symptom trajectory, examination findings, and objective markers to confirm response in medication-induced neuropathy.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Escalate urgently for clinical deterioration, evolving red flags, or failure to improve in suspected medication-induced neuropathy.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Medication-Induced Neuropathy"][/sc]
  • Persistent neuropathy: Even after stopping the offending drug, symptoms may persist for months or years, particularly in the case of chemotherapy-induced neuropathy.
  • Chronic pain: Neuropathic pain may become chronic and require long-term management.
  • Disability: Severe motor neuropathy can lead to long-term disability, particularly with ambulation and fine motor skills.
