Spondylosis
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Spondylosis"][/sc]
  • Spondylosis refers to degenerative changes in the spine, including osteoarthritis, disc degeneration, and bone spur formation.

Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Spondylosis"][/sc]
  • The intervertebral discs degenerate due to ageing, resulting in reduced disc height and increased stress on facet joints.
  • This stress leads to articular cartilage degeneration, subchondral sclerosis, and osteophyte formation, which can impinge on spinal nerves or the spinal cord.
  • Inflammatory processes contribute to pain, while chronic wear may lead to spinal stenosis, radiculopathy, or myelopathy.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Spondylosis"][/sc]
  • Early Phase: Often asymptomatic or with mild stiffness; changes may be incidental findings on imaging.
  • Progressive Phase: Development of chronic pain, stiffness, or radicular symptoms over months to years.
  • Advanced Phase: Complications like spinal stenosis or myelopathy, which can cause significant disability and neurological deficits.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Spondylosis"][/sc]
  • Ask about chronic back or neck pain - Caused by disc degeneration and inflammation in facet joints; often worsens with activity.
  • Ask about stiffness - Due to loss of disc flexibility and formation of osteophytes; typically worse in the morning or after inactivity.
  • Ask about radicular pain - Pain radiating to limbs due to nerve root compression, most common in cervical and lumbar spondylosis.
  • Ask about paresthesia or numbness - From nerve compression, leading to sensory deficits in the affected dermatome.
  • Ask about weakness - Can occur in severe cases of nerve root or spinal cord compression, particularly in myelopathy.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Spondylosis"][/sc]
  • Check reduced range of motion - Due to stiffness from osteophytes and loss of disc flexibility.
  • Check tenderness over affected area - Common in cervical and lumbar regions; indicates local inflammation.
  • Check positive Spurling’s test (cervical spondylosis) - Elicits radicular pain upon neck extension and rotation, suggesting nerve root compression.
  • Check reflex changes (hyperreflexia in myelopathy) - Indicative of spinal cord compression.
  • Check gait abnormalities - In severe cervical myelopathy, due to motor pathway compression in the spinal cord.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Spondylosis"][/sc]
  • Progressive neurological deficit - Worsening weakness, numbness, or bowel/bladder dysfunction; indicates spinal cord or cauda equina compression.
  • Severe, unremitting pain at night - May suggest malignancy, infection, or severe nerve root compression.
  • Incontinence - Can indicate cauda equina syndrome or severe spinal cord compression requiring urgent intervention.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Spondylosis"][/sc]
  • Asymptomatic findings - Many individuals show radiographic evidence of spondylosis without symptoms; often incidental findings on imaging.
  • Atypical radiculopathy without pain - In some cases, only sensory deficits are noted, which may lead to delayed recognition.
  • Isolated weakness - Rare, but may be the only initial presentation in advanced myelopathy.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask spondylosis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Spondylosis"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: X-ray of the spine - Shows osteophytes, reduced disc height, and other degenerative changes typical in spondylosis; MRI - Assesses disc.
  • Targeted and monitoring: CT scan - Useful for detailed assessment of bony changes if MRI is contraindicated; shows facet joint degeneration and stenosis; Nerve.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Spondylosis"][/sc]
  • Disc herniation - Often acute with focal radiculopathy; typically lacks widespread degenerative changes.
  • Spinal stenosis - May coexist with spondylosis but often presents with neurogenic claudication rather than isolated radicular.
  • Myofascial pain syndrome - Pain typically localised with no neurological deficits; lacks imaging evidence of spinal degeneration.
  • Rheumatoid arthritis - Inflammatory pattern with systemic signs and more symmetrical joint involvement; often involves cervical spine.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Spondylosis"][/sc]
  • Spinal stenosis - Often occurs in advanced spondylosis, leading to neurogenic claudication or myelopathy.
  • Radiculopathy - Chronic compression of nerve roots, potentially causing persistent sensory deficits.
  • Myelopathy - Rare but serious complication of cervical spondylosis; may lead to irreversible neurological deficits if untreated.
  • Prognosis - Typically slowly progressive; outcomes vary based on severity and presence of neurological involvement. Conservative management often effective in milder cases.
