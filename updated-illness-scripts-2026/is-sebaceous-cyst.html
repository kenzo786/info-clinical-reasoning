Sebaceous Cyst
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Sebaceous Cyst"][/sc]
  • A sebaceous cyst is a non-cancerous, slow-growing cyst filled with sebum, a thick, greasy substance.

Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Sebaceous Cyst"][/sc]
  • Sebaceous cysts arise from the blockage of a sebaceous gland duct, often due to trauma, damage, or underlying skin conditions. This leads to an accumulation of sebum within the gland. As the sebum collects, a sac forms beneath the skin, surrounded by a fibrous capsule. Over time, the cyst may grow, and in some cases, bacteria can enter the cyst, leading to infection.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Sebaceous Cyst"][/sc]
  • Slow-growing: Sebaceous cysts typically develop over months or even years.
  • Asymptomatic phase: Most sebaceous cysts are asymptomatic initially and may remain unnoticed unless they become infected or inflamed.
  • Complicated phase: Infected cysts can become acutely painful, swollen, and red, with rapid changes occurring over days to weeks.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Sebaceous Cyst"][/sc]
  • Ask about asymptomatic lump - Most cysts are painless and discovered incidentally. The accumulation of sebum causes the swelling without causing discomfort initially.
  • Ask about discomfort or pain - Pain may arise if the cyst is in a pressure-prone area or becomes inflamed, typically due to local irritation.
  • Ask about redness and swelling - These occur when the cyst becomes infected, leading to inflammatory changes in the surrounding tissues.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Sebaceous Cyst"][/sc]
  • Check visible subcutaneous lump - Typically smooth, round, and movable under the skin; the cyst is often visible to the naked eye and palpable as.
  • Check central punctum - A small dark point on the surface of the cyst, representing the blocked duct opening.
  • Check fluctuance and tenderness - Indicative of infection; the cyst becomes soft, filled with pus, and painful on palpation.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Sebaceous Cyst"][/sc]
  • Rapid growth or large size - May suggest malignancy (rare), such as a sebaceous carcinoma.
  • Recurrent infections - Suggests a complicated or improperly drained cyst, increasing the risk of abscess formation.
  • Ulceration or discharge - May indicate chronic infection, rupture, or fistula formation, which can complicate the course.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Sebaceous Cyst"][/sc]
  • Deep-seated cysts - May be mistaken for a lipoma or tumour due to their firm and less visible nature.
  • Multiple cysts - May occur in association with conditions such as Gardner’s syndrome, which can predispose to numerous skin cysts.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask sebaceous cyst.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Sebaceous Cyst"][/sc]
  • Lipoma - A soft, mobile, non-tender fatty lump; lacks central punctum and does not become inflamed.
  • Epidermoid cyst - Similar in appearance but forms from hair follicle components, not sebaceous glands.
  • Abscess - Typically more painful with signs of infection (fluctuance, heat, redness) and may have systemic.
  • Related alternative diagnosis - Distinguish from sebaceous cyst using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Sebaceous Cyst"][/sc]
  • Infection - The most common complication, leading to abscess formation, pain, and swelling.
  • Recurrence - Can occur if the cyst wall is not fully excised.
  • Rupture - An inflamed cyst can rupture, releasing its contents and causing localised inflammation or infection.
