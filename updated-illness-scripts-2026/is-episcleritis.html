Episcleritis
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Episcleritis"][/sc]
  • Episcleritis is a benign, self-limiting inflammation of the episclera, the thin vascular layer between the conjunctiva and the sclera.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise episcleritis features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Episcleritis"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Episcleritis"][/sc]
  • Risk factors: Risk factors for episcleritis include relevant predisposition, comorbidity, and exposure history.
  • Higher-risk or missed groups: Episcleritis may be missed when presentation is atypical or obscured by comorbidity.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Episcleritis"][/sc]
  • Inflammation in episcleritis is mediated by immune activation, leading to vasodilation and infiltration of inflammatory cells in the episcleral tissue.
  • Often idiopathic but can be associated with systemic autoimmune conditions like rheumatoid arthritis or infections.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Episcleritis"][/sc]
  • Acute onset with episodes typically lasting 1–2 weeks.
  • May recur, especially in cases with underlying systemic conditions.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Episcleritis"][/sc]
  • Ask about redness in one or both eyes - Due to superficial episcleral vessel dilatation.
  • Ask about mild discomfort - Often described as a gritty sensation rather than pain; inflammation causes irritation of surrounding tissues.
  • Ask about tearing - Reflex tearing occurs as a response to ocular surface irritation.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Episcleritis"][/sc]
  • Check diffuse or sectoral redness - Episcleral vessels are dilated and mobile when manipulated with a cotton swab.
  • Check no discharge - Distinguishing it from conjunctivitis.
  • Check normal vision - No impact on visual acuity differentiates it from scleritis or uveitis.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Episcleritis"][/sc]
  • Severe pain - May indicate scleritis or other deeper ocular inflammation.
  • Photophobia or vision loss - Suggests possible uveitis or keratitis.
  • Recurrent episodes - Raises concern for underlying systemic diseases such as rheumatoid arthritis or systemic vasculitis.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Episcleritis"][/sc]
  • Bilateral episcleritis - Though uncommon, bilateral presentation might be seen in systemic diseases.
  • Chronic episcleritis - Prolonged course over weeks may occur, particularly with autoimmune associations.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask episcleritis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Episcleritis"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Slit-lamp examination - Identifies inflammation confined to the episcleral layer.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Phenylephrine test - Blanching of episcleral vessels with phenylephrine confirms episcleral vessel involvement; Blood tests - ESR, CRP, and autoimmune panel.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Episcleritis"][/sc]
  • Scleritis - More severe pain, associated with systemic disease, deeper inflammation.
  • Conjunctivitis - Discharge and diffuse conjunctival involvement distinguish it.
  • Keratitis - Associated with corneal involvement, pain, and photophobia.
  • Related alternative diagnosis - Distinguish from episcleritis using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Episcleritis"][/sc]
  • Generally, episcleritis resolves without complications.
  • Recurrent episodes may lead to further investigations for systemic disease.
