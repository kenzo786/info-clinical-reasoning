COPD (Severe)
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="COPD (Severe)"][/sc]
  • Chronic Obstructive Pulmonary Disease (COPD) is a progressive, inflammatory lung disease characterised by irreversible airflow limitation.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise copd features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="COPD (Severe)"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="COPD (Severe)"][/sc]
  • Risk factors: Risk of copd (severe) rises with relevant predisposition, comorbidity, and exposure profile.
  • Higher-risk or missed groups: COPD (Severe) may be missed when presentations are atypical or comorbidity obscures key features.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="COPD (Severe)"][/sc]
  • Chronic exposure to irritants leads to inflammation of the airways and alveoli.
  • In emphysema, protease-antiprotease imbalance results in alveolar destruction, reducing gas exchange surface area and causing hyperinflation.
  • Chronic bronchitis is marked by goblet cell hyperplasia and excessive mucus production, obstructing airways and impairing mucociliary clearance.
  • Both conditions result in airflow limitation, air trapping, and a mismatch between ventilation and perfusion, causing hypoxaemia and hypercapnia.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="COPD (Severe)"][/sc]
  • Initial phase: Often asymptomatic or mild dyspnoea.
  • Progression: Worsening dyspnoea, chronic cough, increased sputum production over years.
  • Late phase: Frequent exacerbations, severe airflow obstruction, respiratory failure.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="COPD (Severe)"][/sc]
  • Ask about dyspnoea (breathlessness) - Most common presenting symptom. Occurs due to airflow limitation and air trapping, leading to increased effort of breathing.
  • Ask about chronic cough - Caused by airway irritation and mucus hypersecretion, especially in chronic bronchitis.
  • Ask about sputum production - Increased goblet cells and impaired clearance cause excess mucus, often worse in the morning.
  • Ask about wheezing - Caused by turbulent airflow through narrowed airways.
  • Ask about fatigue - Due to reduced oxygenation and increased effort in breathing.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="COPD (Severe)"][/sc]
  • Check barrel chest - Hyperinflation causes the chest to take on a rounded shape.
  • Check use of accessory muscles - Patients rely on neck and shoulder muscles to assist with breathing due to diaphragm inefficiency.
  • Check pursed-lip breathing - A compensatory mechanism to maintain airway pressure and improve oxygenation.
  • Check cyanosis - Bluish skin or lips due to hypoxaemia, especially in advanced disease.
  • Check decreased breath sounds - Due to hyperinflation and reduced airflow.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="COPD (Severe)"][/sc]
  • Increased dyspnoea - May indicate an acute exacerbation or impending respiratory failure.
  • Altered mental state - Suggestive of hypercapnic respiratory failure.
  • Peripheral oedema - May signify cor pulmonale (right-sided heart failure).
  • Haemoptysis - Could indicate lung cancer or infection.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="COPD (Severe)"][/sc]
  • Isolated fatigue - Can be an early or under-recognised sign in milder disease.
  • No cough or sputum production - Emphysema-dominant COPD may lack these symptoms but still present with dyspnoea.
  • Progressive dyspnoea without significant smoking history - Consider alpha-1 antitrypsin deficiency.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features or comorbidity can mask the underlying diagnosis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="COPD (Severe)"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Spirometry - FEV1/FVC ratio < 0.7 confirms obstructive lung disease. Severity is based on % predicted FEV1; Chest X-ray - Hyperinflated.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Arterial blood gas (ABG) - Hypoxaemia (low PaO2), hypercapnia (elevated PaCO2) in advanced disease or exacerbations; Full blood count (FBC) .
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="COPD (Severe)"][/sc]
  • A 68-year-old male with a 40-pack-year smoking history presents with progressively worsening dyspnoea, productive cough, and frequent exacerbations over the last 5 years. He reports increasing fatigue and occasional cyanosis.
  • Adult with symptom pattern and objective findings suggestive of copd (severe), requiring focused confirmation and risk stratification.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="COPD (Severe)"][/sc]
  • Asthma - Differentiate from copd (severe) using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.
  • Heart failure - May present with dyspnoea and cough, but with additional features like bilateral pitting oedema.
  • Bronchiectasis - Chronic cough with copious sputum production, often associated with recurrent infections; may show bronchial.
  • Interstitial lung disease (ILD) - Progressive dyspnoea, often with dry cough; spirometry shows restrictive pattern (reduced FEV1 and FVC).
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="COPD (Severe)"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Assess physiological stability and urgent complications at presentation, and initiate early stabilisation where required.
  • Core care: Deliver condition-specific treatment for copd (severe), address underlying drivers, and involve appropriate specialist support.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Reassess symptom trajectory, objective markers, and treatment response over time to detect complications early.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Worsening symptoms, physiological deterioration, or failure to improve should trigger urgent reassessment for severe or alternative pathology in copd (severe).
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="COPD (Severe)"][/sc]
  • Acute exacerbations - Frequent in advanced disease, often triggered by infections.
  • Cor pulmonale - Right heart failure due to chronic pulmonary hypertension.
  • Pneumothorax - More common in severe emphysema due to ruptured bullae.
  • Respiratory failure - Hypercapnic or hypoxaemic respiratory failure in advanced stages.
