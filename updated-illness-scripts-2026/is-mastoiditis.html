Mastoiditis
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Mastoiditis"][/sc]
  • Mastoiditis is a bacterial infection of the mastoid air cells, located within the temporal bone behind the ear.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise mastoiditis features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.

Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Mastoiditis"][/sc]
  • Acute otitis media leads to infection in the middle ear, causing the eustachian tube to become blocked. This allows bacteria to proliferate, leading to pressure build-up. If the infection is not treated, it can spread to the mastoid air cells, leading to inflammation, bone destruction, and pus accumulation in the mastoid process. This can result in the formation of abscesses and, if severe, extension to adjacent structures such as the brain.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Mastoiditis"][/sc]
  • Mastoiditis usually develops days to weeks after the onset of acute otitis media. Early stages involve localised mastoid inflammation, with progressive stages marked by abscess formation, and, if left untreated, further extension to the adjacent skull or intracranial spaces within weeks.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Mastoiditis"][/sc]
  • Ask about otalgia - Ear pain, usually worsening after an episode of acute otitis media, occurs due to inflammation and pressure in the mastoid air cells.
  • Ask about fever - Reflects the body’s systemic inflammatory response to the bacterial infection.
  • Ask about hearing loss - Conductive hearing loss occurs due to fluid and infection affecting the middle ear and mastoid bone.
  • Ask about otorrhoea - Discharge from the ear, often purulent, occurs due to the rupture of the tympanic membrane as pressure builds in the middle ear.
  • Ask about malaise - General unwellness due to the systemic nature of the infection.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Mastoiditis"][/sc]
  • Check postauricular erythema and swelling - Redness and swelling behind the ear are key signs of mastoid involvement and abscess formation.
  • Check protrusion of the auricle - The ear may appear pushed forward due to the swelling of the underlying mastoid process.
  • Check tenderness over the mastoid - Palpation reveals pain due to inflammation of the mastoid bone.
  • Check fluctuant swelling - Indicative of a subperiosteal abscess, where pus collects under the periosteum of the mastoid.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Mastoiditis"][/sc]
  • Neck stiffness - May indicate meningitis, a severe intracranial complication of mastoiditis.
  • Severe headache - Can be a sign of intracranial extension, such as an epidural or brain abscess.
  • Cranial nerve palsies - Facial nerve involvement (e.g., facial droop) suggests the infection has spread to the skull base or intracranial structures.
  • Altered mental status - May indicate encephalitis or other severe intracranial infections.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Mastoiditis"][/sc]
  • Painless swelling behind the ear - Some cases may present without otalgia, particularly in immunocompromised patients.
  • Isolated fever - In early or subtle cases, fever may be the only symptom, especially in young children.
  • Chronic suppurative otitis media - In some cases, mastoiditis may develop slowly over weeks or months without acute symptoms, leading to chronic infection.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask mastoiditis.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Mastoiditis"][/sc]
  • Acute Otitis Media - Less severe with no postauricular swelling or mastoid tenderness.
  • Chronic Suppurative Otitis Media - Typically presents with persistent ear discharge and hearing loss but without acute signs like.
  • Cholesteatoma - Can cause hearing loss and discharge, but the absence of fever and acute inflammation.
  • Lymphadenopathy - Enlarged lymph nodes can mimic mastoid swelling, but tenderness and erythema are usually absent.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Mastoiditis"][/sc]
  • Meningitis - Infection can spread to the meninges, leading to life-threatening bacterial meningitis.
  • Brain abscess - Extension of the infection intracranially can result in abscess formation, requiring urgent surgical intervention.
  • Facial nerve palsy - Direct involvement of the facial nerve can result in facial weakness or paralysis.
  • Hearing loss - Permanent conductive or sensorineural hearing loss may occur due to chronic infection or surgical intervention.
