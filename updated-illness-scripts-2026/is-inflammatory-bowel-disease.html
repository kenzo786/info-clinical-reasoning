Inflammatory Bowel Disease (IBD)
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Inflammatory Bowel Disease (IBD)"][/sc]
  • Inflammatory Bowel Disease (IBD) is a group of chronic, relapsing-remitting inflammatory conditions of the gastrointestinal (GI) tract, primarily comprising Crohn’s disease (CD) and ulcerative colitis (UC).
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise inflammatory features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.

Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Inflammatory Bowel Disease (IBD)"][/sc]
  • Genetic predisposition: Mutations in genes related to immune function (e.g., NOD2, IL23R) lead to an abnormal immune response.
  • Environmental triggers: Smoking, diet, and microbial dysbiosis contribute to immune system dysregulation.
  • Immune dysregulation: An overactive immune response results in the production of pro-inflammatory cytokines (e.g., TNF-alpha, IL-12), leading to tissue injury.
  • Barrier dysfunction: Damage to the intestinal epithelium compromises its barrier function, allowing bacterial translocation, which perpetuates inflammation.
  • Tissue damage: Chronic inflammation results in fibrosis, strictures, and fistula formation in Crohn’s disease, while ulcerative colitis leads to mucosal ulceration and bleeding.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Inflammatory Bowel Disease (IBD)"][/sc]
  • Initial phase: Symptoms may develop insidiously over weeks to months, with intermittent periods of remission.
  • Relapsing-remitting course: Both Crohn’s disease and ulcerative colitis follow an unpredictable course with flares of active inflammation interspersed with symptom-free intervals.
  • Chronic phase: In long-standing disease, complications such as strictures, fistulas, and an increased risk of colorectal cancer may arise.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Inflammatory Bowel Disease (IBD)"][/sc]
  • Ask about diarrhoea: Chronic, often bloody in ulcerative colitis, due to mucosal inflammation and ulceration. In Crohn’s disease, it may be non-bloody due to ileal involvement.
  • Ask about abdominal pain: Crampy and localised to the right lower quadrant in Crohn’s disease or left lower quadrant in ulcerative colitis, caused by intestinal inflammation.
  • Ask about weight loss: Due to malabsorption in Crohn’s disease or anorexia during active flares.
  • Ask about fatigue: Chronic inflammation and anaemia contribute to systemic fatigue.
  • Ask about urgency and tenesmus: Common in ulcerative colitis due to rectal involvement.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Inflammatory Bowel Disease (IBD)"][/sc]
  • Check abdominal tenderness: Localised to affected areas, such as the right lower quadrant in Crohn’s disease or diffuse tenderness in ulcerative colitis.
  • Check fever: Indicates active inflammation or infection, especially during flares.
  • Check perianal disease (Crohn’s disease): Fistulas, abscesses, and skin tags are common in Crohn’s disease due to transmural inflammation.
  • Check pallor: Suggests anaemia from chronic blood loss or nutritional deficiencies.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Inflammatory Bowel Disease (IBD)"][/sc]
  • Severe abdominal pain: May indicate toxic megacolon or perforation, which are life-threatening complications.
  • Severe bleeding: Suggests significant mucosal damage and may require emergency intervention.
  • Unexplained weight loss: Significant weight loss may indicate malnutrition, cancer, or severe Crohn’s disease.
  • Persistent fever: Indicates uncontrolled inflammation, abscess, or sepsis.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Inflammatory Bowel Disease (IBD)"][/sc]
  • Extraintestinal manifestations: Arthritis, uveitis, and erythema nodosum may present before bowel symptoms, often linked to systemic inflammation.
  • Subclinical disease: Some patients may only present with mild anaemia or elevated inflammatory markers without overt GI symptoms.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask inflammatory bowel disease (ibd).
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Inflammatory Bowel Disease (IBD)"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Focused bedside assessment and first-line testing to assess severity and immediate risk in suspected inflammatory bowel disease (ibd).
  • Targeted and monitoring: Faecal calprotectin: Elevated due to neutrophil migration into the intestinal mucosa, a sensitive marker of intestinal inflammation; Colonoscopy with biopsy: Gold.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Inflammatory Bowel Disease (IBD)"][/sc]
  • Irritable bowel syndrome (IBS) - Unlike IBD, IBS does not involve inflammation and is characterised by alternating bowel habits.
  • Coeliac disease - Gluten sensitivity causing malabsorption, with diarrhoea and weight loss but without the chronic inflammation.
  • Infectious colitis - Acute onset of bloody diarrhoea, often with a history of travel or contaminated food.
  • Related alternative diagnosis - Distinguish from inflammatory bowel disease (ibd) using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Inflammatory Bowel Disease (IBD)"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Assess severity, physiological stability, and immediate complications.
  • Core care: Deliver condition-specific management for inflammatory bowel disease (ibd), treat underlying drivers, and coordinate appropriate specialist input.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Reassess symptom trajectory, examination findings, and objective markers to confirm response in inflammatory bowel disease (ibd).
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Escalate urgently for clinical deterioration, evolving red flags, or failure to improve in suspected inflammatory bowel disease (ibd).
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Inflammatory Bowel Disease (IBD)"][/sc]
  • Toxic megacolon: Acute colonic distension leading to perforation if untreated.
  • Colorectal cancer: Long-standing inflammation increases the risk of dysplasia, particularly in ulcerative colitis.
  • Fistulas and strictures: Common in Crohn’s disease due to transmural inflammation and fibrosis.
  • Malnutrition: Due to malabsorption, especially in Crohn’s disease.
