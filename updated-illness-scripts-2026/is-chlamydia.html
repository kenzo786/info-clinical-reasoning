Chlamydia
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Chlamydia"][/sc]
  • Chlamydia is a common bacterial sexually transmitted infection (STI) caused by Chlamydia trachomatis.
  • UKMLA essentials: urethral or cervical symptoms, PID or testicular pain concern, confirm with site-specific NAAT.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Chlamydia"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Chlamydia"][/sc]
  • Risk factors: New or multiple sexual partners, inconsistent barrier use, prior STI, and recent partner diagnosed with chlamydia increase risk.
  • Higher-risk or missed groups: Asymptomatic infection is common, so opportunistic and partner-notification testing is needed to avoid missed diagnosis.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Chlamydia"][/sc]
  • Pathogen: Chlamydia trachomatis is an obligate intracellular bacterium.
  • Cellular Infection: The bacterium enters epithelial cells in the urogenital tract, rectum, oropharynx, or conjunctiva, where it exists in an infectious form (elementary body) and a replicative form (reticulate body).
  • Immune Response: The body’s immune response causes inflammation and tissue damage, particularly in the cervix, urethra, and upper reproductive tract, which can result in scarring and complications if untreated.
  • Complications: Persistent infection can lead to chronic inflammation and fibrosis, potentially causing PID in women or epididymitis in men, with risks of infertility in severe cases.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Chlamydia"][/sc]
  • Incubation Period: Typically 1-3 weeks from exposure to symptom onset if symptomatic.
  • Acute Phase: Symptoms, if present, can last several weeks and may involve dysuria, discharge, or pain. Many cases remain asymptomatic during this phase.
  • Chronic Phase: In untreated cases, chronic inflammation may develop, increasing the risk of PID or epididymitis, typically months to years after initial infection.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Chlamydia"][/sc]
  • Consider asymptomatic infection - Many people have no symptoms, so diagnosis often depends on screening and partner notification.
  • Ask about dysuria - Burning sensation during urination due to urethral inflammation.
  • Ask about vaginal/penile discharge - Mucopurulent discharge from the urethra or cervix due to local infection and inflammatory exudate.
  • Ask about lower abdominal pain - Often mild but can reflect pelvic inflammation in women.
  • Ask about intermenstrual/postcoital bleeding - Cervicitis can cause fragile cervical tissue, leading to bleeding.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Chlamydia"][/sc]
  • Check cervical motion tenderness - Tenderness on cervical movement, suggesting PID or cervicitis.
  • Check mucopurulent cervical or urethral discharge - Thick, yellowish discharge on examination due to epithelial inflammation.
  • Check erythema of the cervix or urethra - Local redness and inflammation due to immune response to infection.
  • Check pelvic or abdominal tenderness - Palpable tenderness in the lower abdomen with PID.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Chlamydia"][/sc]
  • Severe abdominal pain - May indicate advanced PID or tubo-ovarian abscess formation, requiring urgent treatment.
  • Fever - Suggestive of systemic infection or severe PID; requires urgent management to prevent complications.
  • Pelvic mass - Could suggest a tubo-ovarian abscess, a serious complication of PID that may need surgical intervention.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Chlamydia"][/sc]
  • Conjunctivitis - Occurs due to autoinoculation or sexual contact; presents with redness, discharge, and irritation.
  • Rectal infection - Commonly asymptomatic but may present with proctitis, especially in men who have sex with men.
  • Pharyngitis - Rarely symptomatic; may follow orogenital contact and can present with sore throat.
  • How it gets missed: minimally symptomatic extragenital infection and incomplete site-specific sampling can delay diagnosis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Chlamydia"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Site-specific NAAT sampling (first-catch urine or vulvovaginal/cervical, urethral, rectal, or pharyngeal swabs guided by sexual history) is first line.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Co-testing for gonorrhoea and blood-borne viruses by risk profile; repeat NAAT testing when reinfection risk, persistent symptoms, or pregnancy follow-up indicates.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Chlamydia"][/sc]
  • A 22-year-old woman presents with mild lower abdominal pain, dysuria, and intermenstrual bleeding for 2 weeks. Examination reveals cervical motion tenderness and mucopurulent discharge. NAAT confirms chlamydia infection.
  • Young adult with recent sexual exposure, genitourinary symptoms, and positive NAAT, consistent with uncomplicated chlamydial infection.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Chlamydia"][/sc]
  • Gonorrhoea - Similar urethritis or cervicitis symptoms but Gram stain/culture and NAAT profile supports gonococcal infection.
  • Urinary tract infection - Dysuria with urinary frequency and supportive urine dip/culture but no STI exposure pattern suggests UTI.
  • Pelvic inflammatory disease - More severe pelvic pain, fever, and upper genital tract tenderness indicate ascending infection.
  • Trichomoniasis - Vaginal discharge pattern, microscopy, and NAAT testing differentiate trichomoniasis from chlamydia.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Chlamydia"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Assess for pelvic inflammatory disease, epididymo-orchitis, or pregnancy-related complications requiring urgent escalation.
  • Core care: Provide guideline-concordant antimicrobial treatment, partner notification, and abstinence advice until treatment completion.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Reassess symptoms and reinfection risk, and ensure partner management and sexual-health screening are completed.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Worsening abdominal pain, fever, testicular pain, or persistent symptoms after treatment should trigger urgent reassessment.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Chlamydia"][/sc]
  • Pelvic inflammatory disease (PID) - Untreated ascending infection can cause chronic pelvic pain, tubal damage, and infertility risk.
  • Ectopic Pregnancy - Increased risk due to tubal scarring from PID.
  • Epididymitis - In men, chlamydia can cause epididymitis, potentially leading to infertility.
  • reactive arthritis - A reactive arthritis affecting the joints, eyes, and urethra, although rare.
