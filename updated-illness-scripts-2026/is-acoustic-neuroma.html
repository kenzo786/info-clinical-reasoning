Acoustic Neuroma (Vestibular Schwannoma)
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Acoustic Neuroma (Vestibular Schwannoma)"][/sc]
  • Acoustic neuroma (vestibular schwannoma) is a benign schwann-cell tumour of the vestibular nerve in the cerebellopontine angle.
  • UKMLA essentials: unilateral hearing loss pattern, brainstem compression red flags, confirm with contrast MRI.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Acoustic Neuroma (Vestibular Schwannoma)"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Acoustic Neuroma (Vestibular Schwannoma)"][/sc]
  • Vestibular schwannoma is an uncommon intracranial tumour seen mainly in adult neuro-otology and neurosurgical practice.
  • Most cases are sporadic, while bilateral tumours raise concern for neurofibromatosis type 2.
  • Diagnostic delay can occur because early symptoms overlap with common unilateral hearing and tinnitus complaints.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Acoustic Neuroma (Vestibular Schwannoma)"][/sc]
  • The tumour arises from Schwann cells of the vestibular component of cranial nerve VIII.
  • Slow expansion compresses adjacent cochlear, trigeminal, and facial pathways, causing progressive neuro-otological symptoms.
  • Larger lesions can compress the cerebellum or brainstem and impair cerebrospinal fluid flow.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Acoustic Neuroma (Vestibular Schwannoma)"][/sc]
  • Early course: Subtle unilateral hearing change or tinnitus may evolve gradually over a prolonged period.
  • Progressive course: Balance disturbance and cranial nerve features become more evident as lesion size increases.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Acoustic Neuroma (Vestibular Schwannoma)"][/sc]
  • Ask about unilateral progressive hearing loss - Asymmetric sensorineural loss is the most common presenting symptom.
  • Ask about unilateral tinnitus - Persistent one-sided tinnitus often accompanies hearing change.
  • Ask about imbalance or unsteadiness - Chronic disequilibrium is more typical than brief spinning vertigo attacks.
  • Ask about facial numbness or altered sensation - Larger tumours may involve adjacent trigeminal pathways.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Acoustic Neuroma (Vestibular Schwannoma)"][/sc]
  • Check asymmetrical hearing findings - Bedside and audiometric asymmetry supports retrocochlear pathology.
  • Check cranial nerve function - Corneal reflex reduction or facial sensory change suggests local compressive effects.
  • Check gait and cerebellar signs - Ataxia or broad-based gait suggests posterior fossa involvement in larger lesions.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Acoustic Neuroma (Vestibular Schwannoma)"][/sc]
  • Severe headache with vomiting or drowsiness - Suggests raised intracranial pressure or hydrocephalus requiring urgent assessment.
  • Rapid neurological progression - Worsening ataxia, facial weakness, or bulbar symptoms indicate possible brainstem compression.
  • Bilateral vestibulocochlear symptoms - Should prompt urgent evaluation for syndromic pathology including neurofibromatosis type 2.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Acoustic Neuroma (Vestibular Schwannoma)"][/sc]
  • Sudden unilateral hearing loss: Some lesions are first detected during assessment for acute sensorineural hearing loss.
  • Predominant imbalance without clear hearing complaint: Vestibular-dominant presentations may delay diagnosis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Acoustic Neuroma (Vestibular Schwannoma)"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Audiometric assessment for asymmetric sensorineural loss with focused cranial nerve and balance examination.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Contrast-enhanced MRI of the internal auditory meatus and cerebellopontine angle confirms diagnosis; serial MRI and hearing surveillance guide observation or post-treatment follow-up.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Acoustic Neuroma (Vestibular Schwannoma)"][/sc]
  • A 52-year-old woman presents with progressive right-sided hearing loss, unilateral tinnitus, and worsening imbalance over the past year.
  • Adult with chronic unilateral auditory and vestibular symptoms plus cranial nerve asymmetry, concerning for vestibular schwannoma.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Acoustic Neuroma (Vestibular Schwannoma)"][/sc]
  • Meniere disease - Episodic vertigo with fluctuating hearing symptoms is more typical than steady progressive asymmetry.
  • Sudden idiopathic sensorineural hearing loss - Acute onset hearing drop without mass lesion features on imaging suggests non-tumour pathology.
  • Multiple sclerosis - Multifocal neurological signs with demyelinating MRI lesions suggest central inflammatory disease.
  • Chronic otitis media or conductive pathology - Middle-ear abnormalities produce conductive rather than retrocochlear sensorineural patterns.
  • Cerebellopontine angle meningioma - Extra-axial lesion pattern and imaging characteristics distinguish it from schwannoma.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Acoustic Neuroma (Vestibular Schwannoma)"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Identify raised intracranial pressure or evolving brainstem compromise and arrange urgent specialist neuro-otology or neurosurgical review when present.
  • Core care: Management options include active MRI surveillance, stereotactic radiosurgery, or microsurgical resection based on tumour behaviour, symptoms, and patient goals.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Serial imaging, hearing assessment, and cranial nerve evaluation track progression and treatment effects.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: New severe headache, vomiting, worsening imbalance, or facial and bulbar deficits should trigger urgent reassessment.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Acoustic Neuroma (Vestibular Schwannoma)"][/sc]
  • Permanent hearing impairment: Progressive cochlear nerve involvement can lead to persistent unilateral hearing loss.
  • Cranial nerve dysfunction: Facial weakness, numbness, or corneal reflex impairment may occur from tumour growth or treatment effects.
  • Hydrocephalus or brainstem compression: Large lesions can obstruct cerebrospinal fluid pathways and cause life-threatening neurological decline.
