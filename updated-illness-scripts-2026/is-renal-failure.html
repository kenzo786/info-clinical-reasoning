Renal Failure
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Renal Failure"][/sc]
  • Renal failure, also known as kidney failure, is a condition where the kidneys are unable to filter waste products and maintain fluid and electrolyte balance adequately.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise renal features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Renal Failure"][/sc]

  • Risk factors: Risk factors for renal failure include relevant predisposition, comorbidity, and exposure history.
  • Higher-risk or missed groups: Renal Failure may be missed when presentation is atypical or obscured by comorbidity.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Renal Failure"][/sc]
  • Renal failure results from impaired nephron function. In AKI, this is often due to acute tubular necrosis (ATN) from ischaemia or toxins, while CKD is driven by progressive nephron loss, often from diabetes or hypertension. In both cases, the decline in glomerular filtration rate (GFR) leads to uremia, fluid overload, hyperkalaemia, acidosis, and impaired calcium-phosphate homeostasis.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Renal Failure"][/sc]
  • AKI: Onset within hours to days, potentially reversible if underlying cause is treated promptly.
  • CKD: Progressive decline over months to years. Patients remain asymptomatic in early stages but develop symptoms as GFR declines.
  • ESRD: Final stage of CKD where dialysis or transplant is required for survival.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Renal Failure"][/sc]
  • Ask about fatigue - Accumulation of uremic toxins disrupts energy metabolism, leading to tiredness and weakness.
  • Ask about oedema - Fluid overload occurs due to impaired renal excretion, causing swelling in the legs and periorbital region.
  • Ask about shortness of breath - Fluid retention can lead to pulmonary oedema, causing dyspnoea.
  • Ask about nausea and vomiting - Uremic toxins irritate the gastrointestinal tract, causing these symptoms.
  • Ask about pruritus - Accumulation of uremic toxins and phosphate crystals in the skin can cause itching.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Renal Failure"][/sc]
  • Check hypertension - Due to volume overload and dysregulation of the renin-angiotensin-aldosterone system (RAAS).
  • Check peripheral oedema - Fluid accumulation in dependent areas due to impaired kidney function.
  • Check pallor - Anaemia resulting from decreased erythropoietin production by the failing kidneys.
  • Check confusion or altered mental status - Uremic encephalopathy due to toxin accumulation in severe renal failure.
  • Check pericardial rub - May indicate uraemic pericarditis in severe, untreated renal failure.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Renal Failure"][/sc]
  • Oliguria or anuria - Significant reduction or absence of urine output indicates severe AKI or advanced CKD and impending dialysis need.
  • Hyperkalaemia - Life-threatening arrhythmias due to potassium accumulation; needs urgent treatment.
  • Severe acidosis - Uncompensated metabolic acidosis can cause Kussmaul breathing and cardiac dysfunction.
  • Uremic pericarditis - Inflammation of the pericardium caused by uremic toxins, requiring immediate dialysis.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Renal Failure"][/sc]
  • Asymptomatic CKD - Early CKD may be entirely asymptomatic, with diagnosis made only on routine blood tests showing raised creatinine or reduced eGFR.
  • Atypical oedema distribution - Fluid overload may present with ascites or pleural effusions without peripheral oedema.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask renal failure.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Renal Failure"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Serum creatinine - Elevated in renal failure due to reduced GFR. In the UK, normal levels are 60-120 µmol/L. A rise.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Targeted investigations to confirm diagnosis, assess complications, and monitor response in renal failure.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Renal Failure"][/sc]
  • Prerenal AKI - Distinguish from renal failure using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.
  • Postrenal AKI - Differs by a history of urinary obstruction, seen on ultrasound as hydronephrosis.
  • Glomerulonephritis - Presents with proteinuria, haematuria, and hypertension, often associated with autoimmune or infectious triggers.
  • Related alternative diagnosis - Distinguish from renal failure using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Renal Failure"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Assess severity, physiological stability, and immediate complications.
  • Core care: Deliver condition-specific management for renal failure, treat underlying drivers, and coordinate appropriate specialist input.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Reassess symptom trajectory, examination findings, and objective markers to confirm response in renal failure.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Escalate urgently for clinical deterioration, evolving red flags, or failure to improve in suspected renal failure.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Renal Failure"][/sc]
  • Cardiovascular disease - Most common cause of death in CKD patients due to hypertension, left ventricular hypertrophy, and hyperlipidaemia.
  • Hyperkalaemia - Can cause fatal arrhythmias if untreated.
  • Bone disease - Renal osteodystrophy due to disrupted calcium and phosphate balance leads to fractures and skeletal deformities.
