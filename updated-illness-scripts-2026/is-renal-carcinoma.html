Renal Carcinoma
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Renal Carcinoma"][/sc]
  • Renal carcinoma, also known as renal cell carcinoma (RCC), is the most common type of kidney cancer, originating from the renal tubular epithelial cells.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise renal features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.

Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Renal Carcinoma"][/sc]
  • Renal carcinoma arises due to genetic mutations, such as loss of the von Hippel-Lindau (VHL) tumour suppressor gene in clear cell RCC. These mutations lead to abnormal cellular signalling, promoting angiogenesis and uncontrolled cellular proliferation. Over time, this results in tumour growth within the kidney, which can eventually invade surrounding tissues or metastasise to distant sites, such as the lungs, bones, and liver.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Renal Carcinoma"][/sc]
  • Renal carcinoma often progresses slowly, with many cases being detected incidentally on imaging. Early stages may be asymptomatic, but as the tumour grows, symptoms such as haematuria or flank pain may develop. Metastatic disease, if untreated, typically worsens over months to years. Early detection significantly improves prognosis.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Renal Carcinoma"][/sc]
  • Ask about haematuria - Present in 40-60% of cases, haematuria results from tumour invasion of renal vasculature or the renal pelvis, leading to blood in, with associated clinical features.
  • Ask about flank pain - Due to the mass effect of the tumour stretching the renal capsule or compressing surrounding tissues.
  • Ask about painless abdominal mass - Palpable in some patients when the tumour grows large enough, often presenting in the late stages.
  • Ask about weight loss - Unexplained weight loss may occur due to systemic effects of cancer, including increased metabolic demands or paraneoplastic syndromes.
  • Ask about fatigue - A non-specific symptom related to the cancer’s systemic effects and anaemia, which can develop from tumour-related blood loss or inflammation.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Renal Carcinoma"][/sc]
  • Check palpable mass - Often discovered in thin individuals or advanced disease when the tumour is large enough.
  • Check hypertension - Can occur due to compression of renal vasculature, leading to activation of the renin-angiotensin system.
  • Check varicocele - A left-sided varicocele can develop due to obstruction of the left renal vein by the tumour.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Renal Carcinoma"][/sc]
  • Gross haematuria - May indicate advanced disease with invasion into the renal pelvis or urinary tract.
  • Sudden onset of varicocele - Suggests vascular obstruction by the tumour.
  • Unexplained weight loss - Concerning for systemic disease or metastasis.
  • Bone pain or pathological fractures - May indicate bone metastases.
  • Dyspnoea or haemoptysis - Suggestive of pulmonary metastases.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Renal Carcinoma"][/sc]
  • Paraneoplastic syndromes - These include hypercalcaemia (due to ectopic production of parathyroid hormone-related peptide) or erythrocytosis (due to overproduction of erythropoietin by the tumour). These syndromes can present without obvious local tumour symptoms.
  • Incidental finding - Many renal carcinomas are discovered incidentally during imaging for unrelated reasons, as RCC can remain asymptomatic for long periods.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask renal carcinoma.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Renal Carcinoma"][/sc]
  • Metastasis - RCC commonly metastasises to the lungs, bones, and liver, leading to symptoms such as dyspnoea, bone pain, or liver dysfunction. Metastasis occurs in about 25% of cases at diagnosis.
  • Paraneoplastic syndromes - These include hypercalcaemia and erythrocytosis, leading to metabolic imbalances and cardiovascular complications.
  • Local invasion - Tumour invasion into the renal vein or inferior vena cava can lead to thrombus formation, with a risk of embolism.
