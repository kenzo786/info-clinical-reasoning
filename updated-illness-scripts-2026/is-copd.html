COPD
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="COPD"][/sc]
  • Chronic Obstructive Pulmonary Disease (COPD) is a progressive lung disease characterised by persistent airflow limitation due to small airway disease (chronic bronchitis) and alveolar destruction (emphysema).
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise copd features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="COPD"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="COPD"][/sc]
  • Globally, COPD affects more than 200 million people and is the third leading cause of death worldwide.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="COPD"][/sc]
  • Chronic exposure to noxious particles (e.g., cigarette smoke) leads to inflammation in the lungs.
  • This causes narrowing of the small airways (chronic bronchitis) and destruction of alveolar walls (emphysema).
  • The airflow obstruction results from both airway narrowing and loss of elastic recoil, making it difficult to exhale fully.
  • There is also impaired gas exchange, leading to chronic hypoxia and hypercapnia over time.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="COPD"][/sc]
  • COPD is a chronic condition that develops gradually over years, often following prolonged exposure to irritants such as cigarette smoke.
  • Early stages may be asymptomatic, progressing to chronic cough and breathlessness. Exacerbations (acute worsening) may occur due to infections or other triggers.
  • Late stages are marked by persistent breathlessness, frequent exacerbations, and respiratory failure.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="COPD"][/sc]
  • Ask about chronic cough - Often the first symptom, caused by inflammation of the airways leading to mucus hypersecretion.
  • Ask about sputum production - Excess mucus production due to chronic inflammation and airway irritation.
  • Ask about dyspnoea (breathlessness) - The most prominent symptom, initially during exertion and eventually at rest, caused by airflow obstruction and reduced lung compliance.
  • Ask about wheezing - Due to narrowed airways and obstruction.
  • Ask about fatigue - Resulting from the increased work of breathing and reduced oxygen delivery to tissues.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="COPD"][/sc]
  • Check prolonged expiratory phase - Due to airflow obstruction and difficulty in exhaling fully.
  • Check wheezing - Audible on auscultation, due to narrowed airways.
  • Check barrel chest - Due to hyperinflation of the lungs, increasing the anteroposterior diameter of the chest.
  • Check pursed-lip breathing - A compensatory mechanism to increase airway pressure and prevent airway collapse during expiration.
  • Check use of accessory muscles - Indicates increased work of breathing as lung function declines.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="COPD"][/sc]
  • Cyanosis - Indicates hypoxaemia and suggests advanced disease or acute exacerbation.
  • Peripheral oedema - Suggests cor pulmonale, a complication of COPD where chronic hypoxia leads to right-sided heart failure.
  • Altered mental state - Can signify hypercapnia, especially in severe exacerbations.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="COPD"][/sc]
  • Minimal cough or sputum - Some patients may not present with a chronic productive cough but primarily with dyspnoea.
  • Isolated weight loss - Severe emphysema can lead to cachexia without significant respiratory symptoms.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features or comorbidity can mask the underlying diagnosis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="COPD"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Spirometry - The hallmark diagnostic test. FEV1/FVC ratio <70% confirms airflow limitation. FEV1 is often reduced, indicating severity.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Targeted investigations to confirm diagnosis, assess complications, and monitor response in copd.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="COPD"][/sc]
  • A 65-year-old male smoker with a 40-pack-year history presents with chronic productive cough, worsening dyspnoea, and occasional wheezing. He reports frequent exacerbations requiring antibiotics.
  • Adult with symptom pattern and objective findings suggestive of copd, requiring focused confirmation and risk stratification.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="COPD"][/sc]
  • Asthma - Differentiate from copd using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.
  • Bronchiectasis - Chronic productive cough with purulent sputum and recurrent infections. Often associated with a history.
  • Congestive Heart Failure (CHF) - Dyspnoea and oedema, but CXR may show pulmonary congestion, and echocardiogram reveals impaired cardiac.
  • Alternative serious diagnosis - Distinguishing history, examination, and targeted investigations do not support copd.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="COPD"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Assess physiological stability and urgent complications at presentation, and initiate early stabilisation where required.
  • Core care: Deliver condition-specific treatment for copd, address underlying drivers, and involve appropriate specialist support.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Reassess symptom trajectory, objective markers, and treatment response over time to detect complications early.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Worsening symptoms, physiological deterioration, or failure to improve should trigger urgent reassessment for severe or alternative pathology in copd.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="COPD"][/sc]
  • Cor pulmonale - Right heart failure due to chronic pulmonary hypertension from hypoxia.
  • Pneumothorax - Emphysematous bullae can rupture, leading to lung collapse.
  • Respiratory failure - Due to progressive hypoxia and hypercapnia, particularly in end-stage disease.
