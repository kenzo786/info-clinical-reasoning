Bulimia Nervosa
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Bulimia Nervosa"][/sc]
  • Bulimia nervosa is an eating disorder characterised by recurrent episodes of binge eating, followed by inappropriate compensatory behaviours to prevent weight gain, such as self-induced vomiting, misuse of laxatives, fasting.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise bulimia features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.

Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Bulimia Nervosa"][/sc]
  • Bulimia nervosa arises from a complex interaction of genetic, neurobiological, psychological, and sociocultural factors. Dysregulation of neurotransmitters, particularly serotonin and dopamine, contributes to impaired appetite control and mood regulation. Binge episodes are triggered by a combination of emotional distress, hunger, or restriction, leading to an overwhelming urge to consume large quantities of food. Compensatory behaviours aim to alleviate guilt and prevent weight gain. The recurrent cycle of bingeing and purging can cause profound metabolic and gastrointestinal disturbances, including electrolyte imbalances, gastrointestinal damage, and cardiovascular stress.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Bulimia Nervosa"][/sc]
  • Bulimia nervosa often begins in late adolescence or early adulthood, with a chronic course if untreated. Symptoms typically fluctuate, with periods of remission followed by relapse. Without intervention, the disorder can persist for years or even decades, causing long-term psychological and physical health problems.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Bulimia Nervosa"][/sc]
  • Ask about binge eating – Eating a large amount of food in a short time, typically high in calories, accompanied by a lack of control. This.
  • Ask about self-induced vomiting – Common compensatory behaviour aimed at avoiding weight gain, causing damage to the oesophagus and dental enamel due to exposure to gastric.
  • Ask about excessive concern with body weight/shape – Individuals often have a distorted body image, leading to psychological distress, driven by societal pressures and internalised ideals.
  • Ask about fatigue – Resulting from inadequate nutrition and electrolyte imbalances from repeated purging behaviours.
  • Ask about irregular menstrual periods – Due to the body’s response to stress, malnutrition, and hormone dysregulation.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Bulimia Nervosa"][/sc]
  • Check russell's sign – Calluses on the knuckles from repeated self-induced vomiting (from scraping teeth).
  • Check parotid gland enlargement – Chronic vomiting stimulates excessive salivary production, leading to swollen parotid glands.
  • Check dental erosion – Repeated exposure to stomach acid from vomiting causes erosion of the enamel, most prominent on the back of the teeth.
  • Check abdominal bloating – Caused by frequent binge eating and purging behaviours, leading to gastrointestinal dysregulation.
  • Check hypotension and bradycardia – Due to malnutrition and electrolyte imbalances.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Bulimia Nervosa"][/sc]
  • Electrolyte imbalances – Hypokalaemia can lead to cardiac arrhythmias and sudden death, due to frequent vomiting or laxative abuse.
  • Severe dehydration – Caused by purging and insufficient fluid intake, leading to acute kidney injury if untreated.
  • Oesophageal rupture (Boerhaave syndrome) – A rare but life-threatening complication of repeated vomiting causing a full-thickness tear in the oesophagus, requiring emergency treatment.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Bulimia Nervosa"][/sc]
  • No visible weight loss – Unlike anorexia nervosa, individuals with bulimia may maintain a normal or above-normal body weight, making the disorder harder to identify.
  • Minimal or no compensatory behaviours – Some individuals may binge without purging, leading to a delayed or overlooked diagnosis.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features or comorbidity can mask the underlying diagnosis.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Bulimia Nervosa"][/sc]
  • Electrolyte imbalances – Can lead to serious arrhythmias and cardiac complications, particularly hypokalaemia.
  • Gastrointestinal damage – Chronic vomiting causes oesophagitis, gastric ulcers, and even oesophageal rupture.
  • Dental problems – Severe dental erosion, caries, and tooth loss due to repeated vomiting.
  • Mood disorders – Depression and anxiety are common comorbidities, often exacerbating the severity of bulimia.
