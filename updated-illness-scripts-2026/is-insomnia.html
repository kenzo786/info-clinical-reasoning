Insomnia
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Insomnia"][/sc]
  • Insomnia is a sleep disorder characterised by difficulty falling asleep, staying asleep, or waking up too early, with poor sleep quality that results in daytime impairment.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise insomnia features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.

  • Higher-risk or missed groups: Insomnia may be missed when presentation is atypical or obscured by comorbidity.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Insomnia"][/sc]
  • The pathophysiology of insomnia involves hyperarousal, both at night and during the daytime. This hyperarousal may be due to increased levels of stress hormones (e.g., cortisol) or neurotransmitter imbalances (e.g., increased orexin, decreased GABA). Factors such as anxiety, depression, chronic pain, and circadian rhythm disturbances can also exacerbate insomnia.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Insomnia"][/sc]
  • Acute insomnia: Lasts days to weeks, often triggered by stress or illness. It is typically transient and resolves once the triggering factor is addressed.
  • Chronic insomnia: Persists for more than three months, often without a clear precipitating factor. It can become self-perpetuating as individuals develop maladaptive sleep habits, such as irregular sleep schedules and anxiety about sleep.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Insomnia"][/sc]
  • Ask about difficulty falling asleep – Often due to hyperarousal and increased activity of stress hormones, making it hard for the brain to transition into sleep.
  • Ask about frequent awakenings during the night – Fragmented sleep can result from an inability to maintain deep sleep, often associated with anxiety or medical conditions.
  • Ask about early morning awakenings – Often linked with depression, individuals wake up earlier than intended and cannot fall back asleep.
  • Ask about daytime sleepiness or fatigue – A direct consequence of insufficient or poor-quality sleep, leading to impaired cognitive and physical functioning during the day.
  • Ask about irritability or mood changes – Sleep deprivation can alter brain function, increasing sensitivity to stress and reducing the brain’s ability to regulate mood.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Insomnia"][/sc]
  • Check fatigue – Lack of sleep leads to physical exhaustion and low energy levels throughout the day.
  • Check poor concentration – Sleep deprivation impairs cognitive function, affecting attention, memory, and decision-making abilities.
  • Check increased sympathetic tone – Insomnia is often associated with elevated heart rate, higher blood pressure, and increased levels of stress hormones (e.g., cortisol).
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Insomnia"][/sc]
  • Unexplained weight loss – May indicate an underlying serious condition, such as malignancy, hyperthyroidism, or a severe mental health disorder.
  • Severe depression or suicidal ideation – Chronic insomnia is closely linked to mood disorders. Worsening depression or thoughts of self-harm require immediate mental health evaluation.
  • Significant decline in cognitive function – Severe cognitive impairment may suggest neurodegenerative diseases or metabolic imbalances (e.g., hyperthyroidism, hypoglycaemia).
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Insomnia"][/sc]
  • Short sleep duration with no daytime impairment – Some individuals report short sleep (e.g., 4-5 hours) but do not experience daytime impairment, making the condition harder to diagnose.
  • Primary complaint of daytime fatigue without recognised sleep disturbance – This may be more common in older adults, who attribute their daytime fatigue to aging or other conditions rather than poor sleep.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask insomnia.

  • Immediate priorities: Assess severity, physiological stability, and immediate complications.
  • Core care: Deliver condition-specific management for insomnia, treat underlying drivers, and coordinate appropriate specialist input.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Reassess symptom trajectory, examination findings, and objective markers to confirm response in insomnia.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Escalate urgently for clinical deterioration, evolving red flags, or failure to improve in suspected insomnia.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Insomnia"][/sc]
  • Impaired cognitive function – Chronic sleep deprivation can lead to poor concentration, memory impairment, and reduced decision-making ability.
  • Increased risk of cardiovascular disease – Insomnia, especially if associated with increased sympathetic activity, may increase the risk of hypertension, heart disease, and stroke.
  • Mood disorders – Insomnia is closely linked with depression and anxiety, and untreated insomnia can worsen these conditions over time.
