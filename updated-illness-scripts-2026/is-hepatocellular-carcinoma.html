Hepatocellular Carcinoma
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Hepatocellular Carcinoma"][/sc]
  • Hepatocellular carcinoma (HCC) is the most common primary malignancy of the liver, arising from hepatocytes, the main liver cells.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise hepatocellular features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.

  • Risk factors: Risk factors for hepatocellular carcinoma include relevant predisposition, comorbidity, and exposure history.
  • Higher-risk or missed groups: Hepatocellular Carcinoma may be missed when presentation is atypical or obscured by comorbidity.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Hepatocellular Carcinoma"][/sc]
  • Hepatocellular carcinoma often develops from chronic liver inflammation and injury, leading to cirrhosis and genetic mutations in hepatocytes.
  • In chronic liver disease, ongoing injury from toxins (e.g., hepatitis B/C viruses, alcohol) leads to fibrosis and compensatory hepatocyte regeneration.
  • In cirrhotic livers, hepatocytes undergo clonal expansion and may accumulate genetic mutations, which can lead to malignant transformation and tumour formation.
  • Vascular endothelial growth factor (VEGF) and other angiogenic factors stimulate tumour growth and neovascularisation, promoting tumour spread within the liver and potentially leading to metastasis.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Hepatocellular Carcinoma"][/sc]
  • Chronic Phase: Slow progression, often over years or decades in the presence of cirrhosis.
  • Early Tumour Development: Often asymptomatic or mildly symptomatic; early-stage HCC may remain undetected.
  • Advanced Disease: Rapid progression of symptoms with weight loss, abdominal pain, jaundice, and liver failure in advanced stages.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Hepatocellular Carcinoma"][/sc]
  • Ask about abdominal pain - Often in the right upper quadrant, due to liver enlargement and tumour mass effect.
  • Ask about weight loss - Due to cancer cachexia and decreased appetite, common in malignancies.
  • Ask about fatigue - Secondary to liver dysfunction, systemic inflammation, and tumour burden.
  • Ask about nausea and vomiting - Due to liver enlargement, tumour burden, and related gastrointestinal involvement.
  • Ask about jaundice - Occurs in advanced cases due to bile duct obstruction or extensive liver damage; presents as yellowing of skin and sclera.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Hepatocellular Carcinoma"][/sc]
  • Check hepatomegaly - Enlarged liver palpable below the costal margin, due to tumour mass.
  • Check ascites - Accumulation of fluid in the abdomen, often indicating portal hypertension due to liver cirrhosis.
  • Check cachexia - Severe weight loss and muscle wasting from cancer-related metabolic changes.
  • Check splenomegaly - Enlarged spleen due to portal hypertension.
  • Check pallor - Due to chronic blood loss, anaemia, or nutritional deficiencies secondary to liver dysfunction.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Hepatocellular Carcinoma"][/sc]
  • Rapid onset jaundice - May indicate acute liver failure, severe bile duct obstruction, or extensive tumour infiltration.
  • Severe right upper quadrant pain - May suggest tumour rupture or rapid growth, requiring urgent assessment.
  • Haematemesis or melaena - Indicates potential bleeding varices due to portal hypertension; requires emergency intervention.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Hepatocellular Carcinoma"][/sc]
  • Minimal symptoms with abnormal liver function tests - Some cases present only with deranged LFTs in surveillance.
  • Extrahepatic metastasis - Rare, but can present with symptoms related to the lungs, bones, or adrenal glands.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask hepatocellular carcinoma.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Hepatocellular Carcinoma"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Alpha-fetoprotein (AFP) - Elevated in 60-70% of HCC cases; levels >400 ng/mL are highly suggestive; Liver ultrasound - First-line imaging; identifies.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Targeted investigations to confirm diagnosis, assess complications, and monitor response in hepatocellular carcinoma.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Hepatocellular Carcinoma"][/sc]
  • Metastatic liver disease - Often multiple lesions; primary cancer elsewhere.
  • Intrahepatic cholangiocarcinoma - More common in the bile ducts; lacks elevated AFP.
  • Focal nodular hyperplasia - Benign lesion; lacks invasive features on imaging.
  • Related alternative diagnosis - Distinguish from hepatocellular carcinoma using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Hepatocellular Carcinoma"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Assess severity, physiological stability, and immediate complications.
  • Core care: Deliver condition-specific management for hepatocellular carcinoma, treat underlying drivers, and coordinate appropriate specialist input.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Reassess symptom trajectory, examination findings, and objective markers to confirm response in hepatocellular carcinoma.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Escalate urgently for clinical deterioration, evolving red flags, or failure to improve in suspected hepatocellular carcinoma.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Hepatocellular Carcinoma"][/sc]
  • Liver failure - Due to extensive liver damage; can lead to hepatic encephalopathy and death.
  • Portal hypertension - Cirrhosis and tumour effects cause increased portal vein pressure, leading to varices and ascites.
  • Metastasis - Spread to lungs, bones, or adrenal glands in advanced stages, worsening prognosis.
