Pneumothorax [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Pneumothorax"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Pneumothorax"][/sc]
  • A pneumothorax is the abnormal accumulation of air in the pleural space — caused by disruption of the visceral or parietal pleura — resulting in partial or complete lung collapse, with clinical severity ranging from mild dyspnoea in small primary spontaneous pneumothorax to life-threatening haemodynamic collapse in tension pneumothorax.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Distinguish primary spontaneous pneumothorax (PSP) from secondary spontaneous pneumothorax (SSP) and apply BTS pleural guidelines to size-based management decisions; recognise tension pneumothorax as a clinical emergency requiring immediate needle decompression without waiting for imaging; identify indications for aspiration, intercostal drainage, and surgical referral; and understand recurrence risk and safety-netting requirements.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Pneumothorax"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Pneumothorax"][/sc]
  • Primary spontaneous pneumothorax (PSP) has an incidence of approximately 24 per 100,000 per year in men and 9.8 per 100,000 per year in women in the UK, with a male-to-female ratio of approximately 6:1; PSP predominantly affects young adults aged 15–35, particularly tall, slim individuals. Secondary spontaneous pneumothorax (SSP) has an incidence of approximately 6 per 100,000 per year and predominantly affects older patients with established lung disease. The cumulative recurrence risk for PSP after a first episode is approximately 30% within 5 years; recurrence risk is substantially higher after a second ipsilateral episode.
  • Key risk factors for PSP include male sex, tall and slim body habitus, current smoking (risk increased up to 22-fold in heavy smokers compared with non-smokers), family history of pneumothorax, and connective tissue disorders including Marfan syndrome and homocystinuria. SSP complicates established lung disease — most commonly COPD, asthma, cystic fibrosis, interstitial lung disease, and primary or metastatic lung malignancy; Pneumocystis jirovecii pneumonia (PJP) in HIV-positive patients is a cause of SSP that should be considered in the appropriate context. Iatrogenic pneumothorax occurs after central venous catheterisation (particularly subclavian approaches), pleural procedures, bronchoscopy, or barotrauma from mechanical ventilation.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Pneumothorax"][/sc]
  • In primary spontaneous pneumothorax, rupture of apical subpleural blebs or bullae — thin-walled air-filled spaces arising from weakness of the visceral pleura, most commonly at the lung apex where pleural pressure is most negative — creates a communication between the alveolar space and the pleural cavity; in secondary pneumothorax, diseased or necrotic lung parenchyma (emphysematous bullae, cavitating lesions, or infective necrosis) ruptures; in traumatic pneumothorax, chest wall injury or penetrating trauma directly violates the pleural boundary.
  • Once air enters the pleural space, the normal negative intrapleural pressure is abolished; the lung collapses inwards towards the hilum due to its inherent elastic recoil; ventilation-perfusion mismatch causes hypoxia proportional to the degree of collapse; in patients with pre-existing lung disease (SSP), even a small pneumothorax reduces the functional reserve markedly, causing severe dyspnoea disproportionate to the apparent size of collapse; in tension physiology, a one-way valve mechanism allows air to enter the pleural space on inspiration but prevents its exit, causing progressive accumulation with each breath cycle.
  • Tension pneumothorax — if untreated — causes ipsilateral lung complete collapse, mediastinal shift away from the affected side, compression of the contralateral lung, and kinking of the superior and inferior vena cava, severely impairing venous return to the right heart; the result is obstructive shock with rapidly falling cardiac output, cardiovascular collapse, and death; in simple untreated pneumothorax, persistent air leak from a non-sealing defect may prevent re-expansion and lead to trapped lung, empyema, or bronchopleural fistula.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Pneumothorax"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Sudden onset unilateral pleuritic chest pain and dyspnoea at the moment of pleural disruption; in PSP the pain is often sharp and well-localised, and dyspnoea may be mild or moderate even with a large collapse, as the underlying lung is healthy; in SSP symptoms are typically more severe from the outset due to pre-existing reduced respiratory reserve; symptoms may plateau if the air leak self-seals.
  • Progressive phase: Increasing dyspnoea and chest tightness as pleural air accumulates; in SSP even a small pneumothorax may cause rapid respiratory failure; patients with PSP may delay presentation by hours to days if symptoms are mild; any change in character of symptoms — sudden worsening, new haemodynamic symptoms, or distress disproportionate to the apparent size of pneumothorax — should raise suspicion of developing tension physiology or haemopneumothorax.
  • Late phase: Cardiovascular collapse and cardiac arrest develop particularly beyond several hours of untreated tension pneumothorax as mediastinal shift impairs venous return; even in simple pneumothorax, failure of spontaneous re-expansion over several days is associated with persistent air leak, bronchopleural fistula, and risk of trapped lung; haemopneumothorax may cause significant haemorrhage requiring urgent surgical intervention.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Pneumothorax"][/sc]
  • Ask about sudden-onset unilateral pleuritic chest pain — the cardinal symptom; typically sharp, well-localised to the affected hemithorax, and worse on inspiration and coughing; onset is characteristically abrupt, often at rest or during mild exertion in PSP rather than during strenuous activity; ask whether the onset was truly sudden (discriminates pneumothorax from gradual-onset pleurisy or musculoskeletal pain).
  • Ask about dyspnoea — characterise severity; in PSP with a healthy lung, breathlessness may be mild even with a large pneumothorax; in SSP, profound dyspnoea out of proportion to apparent collapse size is expected and reflects the pre-existing reduced respiratory reserve; rapidly worsening breathlessness with haemodynamic symptoms (dizziness, pre-syncope) in any pneumothorax raises suspicion of tension physiology and requires immediate assessment.
  • Ask about previous episodes of similar pain — a history of prior ipsilateral pneumothorax is directly relevant to management (two ipsilateral episodes is a standard indication for VATS surgical referral); a family history of pneumothorax may indicate hereditary connective tissue disease; ask whether the patient has been diagnosed with Marfan syndrome, cystic fibrosis, or another predisposing condition.
  • Ask about underlying lung disease and recent procedures — COPD, asthma, or cystic fibrosis identifies SSP with greater severity implications; recent central venous cannulation, pleural aspiration, bronchoscopy, or mechanical ventilation identifies iatrogenic cause; recent trauma (even minor) to the chest should be established; HIV status and immune function are relevant if PJP-associated SSP is a possibility.
  • Ask about the symptom progression since onset — stable or improving pain after the initial episode suggests a self-sealing defect and supports conservative management; worsening pain, increasing breathlessness, or new palpitations suggest ongoing air leak, tension physiology, or haemopneumothorax and mandates urgent assessment; ask about any activities that exacerbate the pain, as involvement of inspiration and coughing discriminates pleural from musculoskeletal or cardiac origins.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Pneumothorax"][/sc]
  • Check for absent or markedly reduced breath sounds on the affected side — the single most clinically important bedside sign; air in the pleural space acts as an insulator preventing transmission of breath sounds; confirm with vocal resonance and vocal fremitus, which are also reduced ipsilaterally; in a large tension pneumothorax with tracheal shift, absent breath sounds combined with haemodynamic instability confirms the clinical diagnosis without requiring imaging.
  • Check percussion note — hyperresonance (drum-like resonance) over the affected hemithorax reflects the air-filled pleural space; this is the characteristic percussion finding in pneumothorax, in contrast to the stony dullness of a pleural effusion; hyperresonance ipsilaterally with dullness or normal resonance contralaterally discriminates pneumothorax from bilateral hyperinflation in COPD.
  • Check tracheal position — the trachea should be central; deviation away from the affected side indicates significant mediastinal shift from tension pneumothorax or a large haemopneumothorax; tracheal deviation is a late and critical sign — its absence does not exclude tension pneumothorax, as haemodynamic instability often precedes visible tracheal shift; a combination of absent breath sounds, hypotension, and rising JVP constitutes the clinical triad of tension pneumothorax.
  • Check heart rate, blood pressure, SpO₂, and respiratory rate — in simple PSP, vital signs are typically normal or show mild tachycardia; in SSP, hypoxia (SpO₂ <92%) may be present even with a small pneumothorax; in tension pneumothorax, progressive tachycardia, hypotension, and rising JVP indicate obstructive shock from impaired venous return — this constellation, not tracheal deviation, is usually the first haemodynamic sign; subcutaneous emphysema (crepitus on palpating the chest wall or neck) indicates significant air leak, most commonly in traumatic or post-procedural pneumothorax.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Pneumothorax"][/sc]
  • Tension pneumothorax — absent unilateral breath sounds with haemodynamic instability (tachycardia, hypotension, rising JVP, cyanosis) in any patient with chest trauma, a known pneumothorax, or mechanical ventilation; this is a clinical diagnosis — do not delay needle decompression (second intercostal space, midclavicular line) while waiting for confirmatory CXR; cardiovascular collapse and cardiac arrest develop particularly beyond several hours of untreated tension physiology — though in the most severe cases collapse may follow within minutes — making this a clinical diagnosis that must not await imaging confirmation.
  • Secondary spontaneous pneumothorax in severe underlying lung disease — any SSP in a patient with COPD (particularly FEV1 <1L), severe asthma, cystic fibrosis, or significant interstitial lung disease represents a high-risk presentation regardless of apparent pneumothorax size; even a small SSP dramatically reduces already-compromised respiratory reserve, causing severe dyspnoea and rapid deterioration; SSP in any patient with significant lung disease warrants admission and active management, not conservative observation.
  • Haemopneumothorax — combination of pneumothorax and haemothorax, most commonly in trauma; dullness to percussion rather than hyperresonance in the lower zones alongside absent breath sounds; significant haemorrhage from intercostal or pulmonary vessels may cause haemodynamic instability; requires intercostal drainage and surgical assessment as haemothorax may clot and cause retained haemothorax if drained inadequately.
  • Bilateral pneumothorax or pneumomediastinum — bilateral simultaneous pneumothorax is rare but immediately life-threatening as there is no compensatory reserve; pneumomediastinum (air in the mediastinum causing Hamman's sign — a mediastinal crunch on auscultation) may indicate tracheal or oesophageal injury and requires urgent imaging and surgical assessment.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Pneumothorax"][/sc]
  • Secondary spontaneous pneumothorax in COPD: Emphysematous bullae and hyperinflation make clinical detection and CXR interpretation unreliable — large bullae may be mistaken for pneumothorax and vice versa; the trachea is often already deviated in severe COPD, reducing the reliability of this sign; CT chest is often required to differentiate a large bulla from a pneumothorax before safe drain insertion; clinical deterioration in a known COPD patient should prompt active exclusion of SSP even if CXR appears unchanged.
  • Pregnancy: Pneumothorax in pregnancy is rare but carries high maternal and fetal risk; physiological dyspnoea and chest discomfort are common in pregnancy, potentially masking or delaying recognition; maternal hypoxia rapidly compromises placental oxygen delivery to the fetus; active management with intercostal drain insertion (rather than conservative observation) is appropriate for all but the smallest primary pneumothorax in pregnancy; safe positioning for CXR with abdominal shielding is important.
  • Older adults with SSP: Dyspnoea is commonly attributed to a COPD exacerbation or acute heart failure rather than pneumothorax; clinical signs may be subtle if the patient is not examined systematically; SSP in older adults with multiple comorbidities carries substantially higher mortality than PSP in young patients; a low threshold for CXR is warranted in any older adult with unexplained acute worsening of dyspnoea.
  • Iatrogenic pneumothorax post-procedure: Classically presents within 30 minutes to several hours of subclavian central venous cannulation, thoracentesis, or bronchoscopy; may be asymptomatic initially and detected only on the routine post-procedure CXR; in mechanically ventilated patients, barotrauma-induced pneumothorax may present as acute haemodynamic deterioration or rising ventilator peak pressures without obvious external signs; vigilance for pneumothorax is essential in any intubated patient with sudden deterioration.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Pneumothorax"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected pneumothorax: Clinical assessment takes priority — in suspected tension pneumothorax, immediate needle decompression must not be delayed for imaging; for haemodynamically stable patients, erect posteroanterior (PA) CXR is the first-line confirmatory investigation; SpO₂ and oxygen therapy should be established immediately; ABG in SSP or any patient with significant dyspnoea or hypoxia to guide oxygen delivery and establish baseline; ECG to exclude acute coronary syndrome as a differential in older patients.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Troponin and ECG if ACS is suspected (pleuritic pain in an older patient with risk factors); D-dimer and CTPA if PE is the primary differential; CRP and white cell count if pleuritis from infection is suspected; echocardiogram if pericarditis is a possibility (friction rub, diffuse ST changes); pregnancy test in women of reproductive age before CT.
  • Imaging — chest X-ray: The standard confirmatory investigation; the hallmark is a white visceral pleural line visible parallel to the chest wall with absent lung markings peripheral to it; size is estimated by measuring the interpleural distance at the level of the hilum — ≥2 cm indicates a large pneumothorax requiring active management per BTS guidelines; an erect PA film is preferred; a supine AP film (common in trauma) may miss significant pneumothorax as air collects anteriorly rather than at the apex; a normal expiratory CXR does not reliably exclude small pneumothorax.
  • Imaging — CT chest: The gold standard for size assessment when CXR is equivocal — particularly in bullous COPD where bullae may mimic or coexist with pneumothorax; CT identifies underlying blebs and bullae to inform surgical decision-making; CT is the most sensitive modality for small or occult pneumothorax; bedside thoracic ultrasound (loss of lung sliding, absent comet-tail artefacts, barcode sign on M-mode) is increasingly used in emergency and critical care settings as a rapid bedside alternative with high sensitivity in trained hands, though CXR remains the BTS-recommended first-line in the UK.
  • Serial reassessment: Repeat CXR at 2–4 hours is appropriate when a conservative or aspiration management approach is taken, to confirm non-progression before discharge; in-patients managed with intercostal drainage require daily clinical assessment and repeat CXR to confirm re-expansion and air leak resolution; persistent bubbling through an underwater seal drain beyond 5 days suggests a persistent bronchopleural fistula and warrants thoracic surgical referral; deteriorating clinical status should prompt immediate reassessment before the next scheduled imaging.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Pneumothorax"][/sc]
  • A 22-year-old tall, slim male smoker presents with sudden-onset right-sided pleuritic chest pain and mild dyspnoea at rest with no preceding illness, trauma, or procedure; examination reveals absent breath sounds and hyperresonance in the right upper zone, normal tracheal position, SpO₂ 97%, and HR 92/min; CXR confirms a right-sided pneumothorax with a 3 cm interpleural distance at the hilum.
  • Young tall thin male smoker with sudden-onset unilateral pleuritic pain at rest, absent breath sounds, and hyperresonance — consistent with large (≥2 cm) primary spontaneous pneumothorax requiring active management per BTS guidelines.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Pneumothorax"][/sc]
  • Pulmonary embolism — acute dyspnoea and pleuritic chest pain without the percussion asymmetry or absent breath sounds of pneumothorax; normal or reduced percussion note; risk factors for VTE; D-dimer and CTPA differentiate; PE is an important dangerous mimic that must be actively excluded in any acute pleuritic presentation without clear pneumothorax on imaging.
  • Acute pericarditis — central or left-sided pleuritic chest pain with a pericardial friction rub; saddle-shaped ST elevation on ECG; breath sounds and percussion symmetrical; may coexist with pleuritis (pleuropericarditis); troponin may be mildly elevated if myopericarditis.
  • Pleurisy — unilateral pleuritic pain from pleural inflammation (viral, bacterial, autoimmune); breath sounds preserved and symmetrical; a pleural friction rub may be audible; CXR may show underlying effusion or consolidation but no pleural line; inflammatory markers raised.
  • Rib fracture — localised tenderness on direct palpation of the rib or on lateral chest compression; breath sounds preserved; CXR may confirm fracture; rib fractures from trauma may coexist with traumatic pneumothorax and should not be accepted as the sole diagnosis without CXR.
  • Musculoskeletal chest wall pain (costochondritis, intercostal muscle strain) — chest wall tenderness reproducible on palpation; pain typically related to movement and position rather than purely inspiratory; breath sounds and percussion symmetrical; normal CXR; no haemodynamic instability.
  • Aortic dissection — sudden severe "tearing" or "ripping" chest pain radiating to the back; mediastinal widening on CXR; unequal upper limb blood pressures or pulse deficits; no breath sound or percussion asymmetry; CT aortography is diagnostic; this is a life-threatening mimic of acute chest pain.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Pneumothorax"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Recognise tension pneumothorax as an immediate clinical emergency — do not delay decompression for imaging confirmation. Supportive care often includes high-flow oxygen (which significantly accelerates spontaneous pleural air reabsorption by creating a nitrogen gradient — estimated to quadruple the rate of spontaneous resolution compared with air breathing), analgesia for pleuritic pain, SpO₂ monitoring, and continuous vital sign assessment in line with BTS Pleural Disease Guidelines and local acute pathways.
  • Conservative management (small PSP): In haemodynamically stable patients with a small PSP (<2 cm interpleural distance at the hilum) and minimal symptoms, BTS guidelines support a conservative approach — observation with supplemental oxygen, safety-netting, and a repeat CXR at 2–4 hours before considering discharge; the patient must be able to return promptly if symptoms worsen and should avoid flying or diving until confirmed resolution; this approach reflects the high rate of spontaneous air reabsorption in PSP.
  • Aspiration and intercostal drainage: Needle aspiration (via small-bore catheter) is appropriate as a first-line active intervention for symptomatic PSP ≥2 cm in an otherwise healthy patient; a single aspiration attempt should be made and, if successful with symptom improvement, discharge with follow-up is appropriate; intercostal drain (small-bore, ≤14 Fr, as recommended by BTS for most cases) is indicated for SSP regardless of size, failed aspiration, large PSP with haemodynamic compromise, haemopneumothorax, bilateral pneumothorax, and ventilated patients; drain management uses an underwater seal; suction is added if re-expansion fails; drain removal follows resolution of air leak and radiological confirmation of lung re-expansion.
  • Surgical management: Video-assisted thoracoscopic surgery (VATS) with bullectomy and pleurodesis is the definitive treatment for recurrent pneumothorax — indicated for a second ipsilateral PSP, first ipsilateral SSP in selected patients, persistent air leak beyond 5 days, or bilateral pneumothorax; VATS is associated with lower recurrence rates (approximately 3%) compared with chemical pleurodesis alone; shared decision-making should include discussion of recurrence risk, the implications for flying and diving, and the risks of the procedure.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Outpatient follow-up with repeat CXR at 2–4 weeks to confirm resolution; all patients should be advised to avoid air travel until CXR confirms full resolution (British Thoracic Society does not specify an exact interval but full re-expansion is required); patients should be counselled to avoid scuba diving permanently unless VATS has been performed bilaterally; smoking cessation reduces recurrence risk substantially and should be advised in all patients.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Worsening dyspnoea, haemodynamic instability, progressive haemothorax, or failure of re-expansion on drain suction should prompt immediate thoracic surgical review; persistent air leak (continuous bubbling through underwater seal beyond 5 days) warrants VATS assessment; patients discharged conservatively must receive clear verbal and written instructions to return immediately if breathlessness worsens, if chest pain changes character, or if they feel faint — these features suggest tension development or inadequate re-expansion.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Pneumothorax"][/sc]
  • Tension pneumothorax: Develops particularly beyond several hours of untreated pneumothorax with a one-way valve mechanism — air accumulates progressively with each breath cycle, causing complete ipsilateral lung collapse, mediastinal shift, vena cava kinking, and obstructive shock; mortality without immediate needle decompression approaches 100%; haemodynamic instability (tachycardia, hypotension, rising JVP) typically precedes tracheal deviation and should prompt immediate clinical action without imaging.
  • Recurrent pneumothorax: Affects approximately 30% of PSP patients within 5 years; recurrence risk rises substantially after a second episode; each recurrence is an indication to reconsider surgical management (VATS pleurodesis/bullectomy), which reduces recurrence to approximately 3%; SSP carries a higher recurrence risk than PSP and should prompt earlier surgical referral; patients should be counselled on recurrence risk and advised to avoid scuba diving (pressure changes expand pneumothorax) without prior bilateral VATS.
  • Persistent bronchopleural fistula and trapped lung: Failure of the air leak to seal — indicated by ongoing bubbling through the drain underwater seal beyond 5 days — suggests a bronchopleural fistula requiring thoracic surgical assessment; if the visceral pleura fails to oppose the chest wall, inflammatory fibrin deposits may prevent re-expansion (trapped lung) and cause chronic pleural thickening, restrictive respiratory defect, and risk of empyema; this complication is more common in SSP with bullous disease and in immunocompromised patients.
  • Haemopneumothorax (rare but exam-relevant): Simultaneous accumulation of air and blood in the pleural space, most commonly from intercostal vessel or pulmonary parenchymal injury in trauma; presents with both hyperresonance (air) superiorly and dullness (blood) inferiorly on percussion; if blood is not adequately drained it may clot and form a retained haemothorax — a nidus for empyema requiring thoracoscopic evacuation; this distinction from simple pneumothorax is clinically critical as management requires an appropriately sized drain and surgical oversight.
