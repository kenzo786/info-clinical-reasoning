Contact Lens Problems
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Contact Lens Problems"][/sc]
  • Contact lens problems encompass a range of issues associated with the use of contact lenses, including discomfort, dryness, infections, mechanical trauma, and inflammatory conditions.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise contact features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Contact Lens Problems"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Contact Lens Problems"][/sc]
  • Risk factors: Risk of contact lens problems rises with relevant predisposition, comorbidity, and exposure profile.
  • Higher-risk or missed groups: Contact Lens Problems may be missed when presentations are atypical or comorbidity obscures key features.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Contact Lens Problems"][/sc]
  • Mechanical irritation or improper fit can lead to epithelial disruption, enabling bacterial adhesion and infection.
  • Hypoxia results from reduced oxygen permeability of lenses, causing corneal swelling (oedema) and compromising epithelial integrity.
  • Prolonged wear and poor hygiene promote microbial growth, particularly of Pseudomonas aeruginosa, a common pathogen in contact lens-related infections.
  • Inflammatory reactions such as giant papillary conjunctivitis (GPC) are triggered by protein deposits on lenses.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Contact Lens Problems"][/sc]
  • Acute: Sudden onset of pain, redness, and reduced vision typically seen in microbial keratitis.
  • Subacute: Gradual discomfort and redness over days to weeks, often in dry eye or GPC.
  • Chronic: Persistent irritation, mild redness, or dryness occurring over weeks to months due to long-term wear or environmental factors.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Contact Lens Problems"][/sc]
  • Ask about eye pain - Indicates epithelial disruption or microbial infection due to nerve fibre stimulation.
  • Ask about redness - Results from vasodilation due to inflammation or hypoxia.
  • Ask about blurred vision - Caused by corneal oedema or infectious infiltrates obstructing the visual axis.
  • Ask about photophobia - Inflammatory mediators irritate the ciliary body and iris sphincter.
  • Ask about dryness - Often from reduced tear production or increased evaporation with lens wear.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Contact Lens Problems"][/sc]
  • Check corneal staining - Detected with fluorescein, indicates epithelial disruption.
  • Check conjunctival injection - Suggests inflammation due to irritation or infection.
  • Check corneal infiltrates - White or grey opacities from inflammatory cells in microbial keratitis.
  • Check tear film debris - Reflects poor lens hygiene or protein deposition.
  • Check eyelid swelling - Can occur with allergic or severe inflammatory reactions.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Contact Lens Problems"][/sc]
  • Severe pain - May indicate microbial keratitis or corneal ulcer.
  • Significant vision loss - Suggests central corneal involvement or endophthalmitis.
  • Hypopyon - Presence of white inflammatory cells in the anterior chamber, a marker of severe infection.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Contact Lens Problems"][/sc]
  • Chronic redness and discomfort with no significant corneal findings may indicate hypersensitivity reactions or GPC.
  • Minimal symptoms with severe corneal damage in diabetics or immunocompromised patients due to reduced pain perception or immune response.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features or comorbidity can mask the underlying diagnosis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Contact Lens Problems"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Fluorescein staining - Highlights epithelial defects and corneal integrity.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Targeted investigations to confirm diagnosis, assess complications, and monitor response in contact lens problems.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Contact Lens Problems"][/sc]
  • A 25-year-old contact lens wearer presents with 3 days of worsening eye pain, redness, and blurred vision. Examination reveals corneal infiltrates and conjunctival injection.
  • Adult with symptom pattern and objective findings suggestive of contact lens problems, requiring focused confirmation and risk stratification.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Contact Lens Problems"][/sc]
  • Dry eye syndrome - Typically lacks infiltrates or significant injection.
  • Allergic conjunctivitis - Presents with itching and papillae, rather than pain and infiltrates.
  • Corneal abrasion - Sudden onset after trauma, fluorescein staining without infiltrates.
  • Viral keratitis - Dendritic lesions on fluorescein staining, often recurrent.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Contact Lens Problems"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Assess physiological stability and urgent complications at presentation, and initiate early stabilisation where required.
  • Core care: Deliver condition-specific treatment for contact lens problems, address underlying drivers, and involve appropriate specialist support.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Reassess symptom trajectory, objective markers, and treatment response over time to detect complications early.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Worsening symptoms, physiological deterioration, or failure to improve should trigger urgent reassessment for severe or alternative pathology in contact lens problems.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Contact Lens Problems"][/sc]
  • Corneal scarring - Can cause permanent vision loss.
  • Endophthalmitis - Rare but serious intraocular infection with poor prognosis.
  • Chronic dry eye - May develop due to long-term epithelial damage.
