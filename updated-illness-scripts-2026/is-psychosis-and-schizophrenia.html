Psychosis and Schizophrenia
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Psychosis and Schizophrenia"][/sc]
  • Psychosis is a symptom involving the loss of contact with reality, characterised by hallucinations, delusions, and thought disorder.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise psychosis features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.

Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Psychosis and Schizophrenia"][/sc]
  • Dopamine Dysregulation: Increased dopaminergic activity in mesolimbic pathways is associated with positive symptoms (hallucinations, delusions), while decreased dopaminergic activity in mesocortical pathways is associated with negative symptoms (blunted affect, anhedonia).
  • Glutamate Hypofunction: Reduced NMDA receptor function may contribute to cognitive and negative symptoms.
  • Neurodevelopmental Abnormalities: Altered brain structure and function, including enlarged ventricles and decreased grey matter, are common.
  • Genetic Predisposition: Certain genes, such as those regulating dopamine pathways, increase susceptibility.
  • Environmental Factors: Early trauma, infection, and substance abuse (e.g., cannabis) are associated risk factors that may trigger or exacerbate the condition.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Psychosis and Schizophrenia"][/sc]
  • Prodromal Phase: Gradual onset with subtle changes in thoughts, mood, and behaviour. Symptoms like social withdrawal, diminished interest in activities, and mild cognitive changes.
  • Active (Acute) Phase: Florid psychotic symptoms, including delusions, hallucinations, and thought disorder.
  • Residual Phase: Symptoms subside partially but negative symptoms (e.g., low motivation, affective blunting) often persist.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Psychosis and Schizophrenia"][/sc]
  • Ask about hallucinations: Sensory perceptions without an external stimulus, commonly auditory (voices); related to excessive dopamine in mesolimbic pathways.
  • Ask about delusions: Fixed, false beliefs resistant to logic, often paranoid or persecutory; associated with dopamine dysregulation.
  • Ask about thought Disorder: Disorganised thinking, leading to incoherent speech and difficulty maintaining coherent ideas.
  • Ask about social Withdrawal: Decreased interest in social interactions, often early in the course due to cognitive and negative symptoms.
  • Ask about affective Flattening: Reduced emotional expression and motivation due to reduced dopamine in mesocortical pathways.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Psychosis and Schizophrenia"][/sc]
  • Check blunted Affect: Reduced emotional response due to disrupted dopaminergic and limbic interactions.
  • Check poverty of Speech: Limited spontaneous speech, associated with cognitive impairment and negative symptoms.
  • Check formal Thought Disorder: Disorganised thinking, apparent in disordered speech patterns (e.g., derailment, tangentiality).
  • Check lack of Insight: Poor recognition of illness due to cognitive dysfunction and anosognosia.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Psychosis and Schizophrenia"][/sc]
  • Suicidal Ideation: Increased risk due to low mood, cognitive distortion; signifies high risk of self-harm.
  • Command Hallucinations: Hallucinations instructing self-harm or harm to others; can indicate high-risk of violent behaviour.
  • Severe Agitation: Extreme psychomotor disturbance, often requiring immediate sedation to prevent harm.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Psychosis and Schizophrenia"][/sc]
  • Late-Onset Schizophrenia: Presents after age 45, often with more prominent paranoid delusions.
  • Primary Negative Symptoms: Predominance of affective flattening and social withdrawal, with few or absent positive symptoms.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask psychosis and schizophrenia.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Psychosis and Schizophrenia"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Clinical Assessment: Diagnosis is primarily clinical, requiring the presence of positive, negative, or cognitive symptoms.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Psychometric Scales: Tools like the PANSS (Positive and Negative Syndrome Scale) can quantify symptom severity; Neuroimaging (CT or MRI): May show.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Psychosis and Schizophrenia"][/sc]
  • Bipolar Disorder (with psychotic features) - Distinguished by episodic mood changes and psychosis during mood episodes.
  • Schizoaffective Disorder - Psychotic symptoms accompanied by prominent mood disturbances.
  • Substance-Induced Psychosis - Presence of recent substance use; symptoms often resolve following substance clearance.
  • Delusional Disorder - Presence of non-bizarre delusions without other significant psychotic symptoms.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Psychosis and Schizophrenia"][/sc]
  • Suicidal Risk: Up to 10% lifetime risk of suicide due to mood symptoms and social factors.
  • Cardiovascular Disease: Elevated risk due to lifestyle factors and antipsychotic side effects.
  • Social Isolation and Disability: Functional impairment often leads to significant social and occupational challenges.
