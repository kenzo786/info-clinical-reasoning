Aplastic Anaemia
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Aplastic Anaemia"][/sc]
  • Aplastic anaemia is a rare, potentially life-threatening haematological disorder characterised by bone marrow failure, resulting in pancytopenia (reduction in red cells, white cells, and platelets).
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise aplastic features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Aplastic Anaemia"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Aplastic Anaemia"][/sc]
  • Risk factors: Risk of aplastic anaemia rises with relevant predisposition, comorbidity, and exposure profile.
  • Higher-risk or missed groups: Aplastic Anaemia may be missed when presentations are atypical or comorbidity obscures key features.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Aplastic Anaemia"][/sc]
  • Initial injury to haematopoietic stem cells from environmental or genetic factors.
  • Immunological attack: T-cells may target these stem cells, releasing cytokines that inhibit marrow production.
  • Reduction in all blood cell lines: A decrease in haematopoiesis leads to anaemia, neutropenia, and thrombocytopenia.
  • Pancytopenia arises due to bone marrow cellularity reduction, often replaced by fatty tissue.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Aplastic Anaemia"][/sc]
  • Acute phase: Rapid onset of symptoms due to severe cell line deficiencies.
  • Chronic phase: Symptoms may progress over months to years if untreated.
  • Remission phase: Possible with successful treatment, but relapse is common.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Aplastic Anaemia"][/sc]
  • Ask about fatigue and weakness - Due to anaemia and reduced oxygen-carrying capacity of the blood.
  • Ask about frequent infections - Neutropenia leads to impaired immune function, making infections common.
  • Ask about easy bruising and bleeding - Thrombocytopenia results in insufficient clot formation, causing spontaneous bleeding and easy bruising.
  • Ask about pallor - A lack of red blood cells leads to visible pallor.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Aplastic Anaemia"][/sc]
  • Check pallor - Indicates anaemia due to reduced haemoglobin.
  • Check purpura and petechiae - Skin manifestations of low platelets, often seen on limbs and mucous membranes.
  • Check infection-related signs - Such as fever and inflamed sites, due to neutropenia.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Aplastic Anaemia"][/sc]
  • Recurrent infections - Signify neutropenia and potential for sepsis.
  • Uncontrolled bleeding - Indicates severe thrombocytopenia and risk of haemorrhage.
  • Severe fatigue or syncope - Reflects severe anaemia and decreased tissue oxygenation.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Aplastic Anaemia"][/sc]
  • Gradual onset with mild symptoms: May be seen in mild or chronic cases, where pancytopenia progresses slowly.
  • Isolated cytopenia initially: Some cases may present with just anaemia or thrombocytopenia before progressing to pancytopenia.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features or comorbidity can mask the underlying diagnosis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Aplastic Anaemia"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Full blood count - Shows pancytopenia with low haemoglobin, WBC, and platelets.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Reticulocyte count - Low reticulocytes indicating decreased production from the bone marrow; Bone marrow biopsy - Hypocellular marrow with fatty replacement.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Aplastic Anaemia"][/sc]
  • A 30-year-old woman presents with a 3-month history of progressive fatigue, frequent infections, and easy bruising. Examination reveals pallor and petechiae. Blood tests show pancytopenia, and a bone marrow biopsy.
  • Adult with symptom pattern and objective findings suggestive of aplastic anaemia, requiring focused confirmation and risk stratification.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Aplastic Anaemia"][/sc]
  • Myelodysplastic syndrome - Presents with pancytopenia but typically shows dysplasia in bone marrow cells.
  • Acute leukaemia - Rapid onset of pancytopenia with blasts in peripheral blood or bone marrow.
  • Paroxysmal nocturnal haemoglobinuria (PNH) - Haemolysis with pancytopenia, diagnosed by flow cytometry for CD55/CD59 deficiency.
  • Alternative serious diagnosis - Distinguishing history, examination, and targeted investigations do not support aplastic anaemia.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Aplastic Anaemia"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Assess physiological stability and urgent complications at presentation, and initiate early stabilisation where required.
  • Core care: Deliver condition-specific treatment for aplastic anaemia, address underlying drivers, and involve appropriate specialist support.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Reassess symptom trajectory, objective markers, and treatment response over time to detect complications early.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Worsening symptoms, physiological deterioration, or failure to improve should trigger urgent reassessment for severe or alternative pathology in aplastic anaemia.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Aplastic Anaemia"][/sc]
  • Infections - Common due to neutropenia, potentially leading to sepsis.
  • Haemorrhage - Severe bleeding risks due to thrombocytopenia.
  • Progression to myelodysplasia or acute leukaemia - Rare but possible complication with poor prognosis.
