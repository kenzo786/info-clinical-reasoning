Liver Cirrhosis
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Liver Cirrhosis"][/sc]
  • Liver cirrhosis is a chronic liver disease characterised by diffuse fibrosis, abnormal liver architecture, and regenerative nodules.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise liver features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.

  • Risk factors: Risk factors for liver cirrhosis include relevant predisposition, comorbidity, and exposure history.
  • Higher-risk or missed groups: Liver Cirrhosis may be missed when presentation is atypical or obscured by comorbidity.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Liver Cirrhosis"][/sc]
  • Chronic liver injury leads to activation of hepatic stellate cells, causing excessive collagen deposition and fibrosis. Over time, this fibrosis disrupts normal liver architecture, forming nodules. Blood flow through the liver becomes restricted, causing portal hypertension. With disease progression, liver function deteriorates, leading to impaired synthesis of proteins (e.g., albumin, clotting factors), impaired detoxification, and impaired bile production. Portal hypertension also leads to complications such as varices and ascites.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Liver Cirrhosis"][/sc]
  • Early/Compensated phase: Patients are often asymptomatic or have mild symptoms. This phase can last for years, particularly with risk factor modification.
  • Decompensated phase: Occurs when complications such as ascites, hepatic encephalopathy, or variceal bleeding develop. This phase is associated with a worse prognosis and often requires hospitalisation.
  • End-stage liver disease: Represents irreversible liver failure requiring liver transplantation for survival. Mortality is high without transplantation.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Liver Cirrhosis"][/sc]
  • Ask about fatigue - Results from impaired metabolic function of the liver and is often one of the earliest symptoms.
  • Ask about jaundice - Yellowing of the skin and sclera due to the liver's inability to conjugate bilirubin. Occurs in advanced stages.
  • Ask about abdominal distension - Caused by ascites due to portal hypertension and hypoalbuminaemia.
  • Ask about confusion or altered consciousness (hepatic encephalopathy) - Results from the liver’s inability to clear toxins like ammonia from the blood.
  • Ask about pruritus - Itching due to the accumulation of bile acids in the skin.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Liver Cirrhosis"][/sc]
  • Check spider naevi - Dilated superficial capillaries caused by increased oestrogen levels, common in liver disease.
  • Check palmar erythema - Reddening of the palms due to altered hormone metabolism.
  • Check hepatomegaly - Enlarged liver due to fibrosis, typically seen in earlier stages.
  • Check ascites - Fluid accumulation in the abdomen due to portal hypertension and hypoalbuminaemia.
  • Check asterixis - Flapping tremor seen in hepatic encephalopathy due to impaired ammonia metabolism.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Liver Cirrhosis"][/sc]
  • Haematemesis - Vomiting of blood, which may indicate variceal bleeding, a life-threatening complication of portal hypertension.
  • Rapidly worsening confusion or coma - Suggestive of hepatic encephalopathy, indicating liver failure.
  • Severe abdominal pain with fever - May indicate spontaneous bacterial peritonitis, a complication of ascites and liver failure.
  • Sudden onset of jaundice - Could indicate acute liver failure superimposed on cirrhosis (acute-on-chronic liver failure).
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Liver Cirrhosis"][/sc]
  • Asymptomatic - Cirrhosis can be entirely asymptomatic, especially in the early compensated stage, and may be discovered incidentally on imaging or abnormal liver function tests.
  • Subtle cognitive changes - Early hepatic encephalopathy may present with mild cognitive impairment, which is easily missed.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Liver Cirrhosis"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Liver function tests (LFTs) - Raised ALT/AST, with AST:ALT ratio > 1 in alcoholic liver disease. Reduced albumin and prolonged prothrombin.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Fibroscan - Non-invasive measure of liver stiffness, used to assess fibrosis and cirrhosis; Endoscopy - To assess for varices in patients.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Liver Cirrhosis"][/sc]
  • A 55-year-old male with a history of chronic alcohol use presents with progressive fatigue, jaundice, and abdominal distension over the past 6 months. On examination, he has hepatomegaly, ascites, with supportive clinical and investigation findings.
  • Patient with progressive fatigue, jaundice, and abdominal distension over the past 6 months. On examination, he has hepatomegaly, ascites, and, consistent with liver cirrhosis.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Liver Cirrhosis"][/sc]
  • Non-alcoholic fatty liver disease (NAFLD) - Similar presentation with hepatomegaly and abnormal LFTs, but often without a history of alcohol.
  • Chronic hepatitis B/C - Distinguish from liver cirrhosis using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.
  • Heart failure - Can cause hepatomegaly and ascites (cardiac cirrhosis), but often with additional features of heart.
  • Related alternative diagnosis - Distinguish from liver cirrhosis using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Liver Cirrhosis"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Assess severity, physiological stability, and immediate complications.
  • Core care: Deliver condition-specific management for liver cirrhosis, treat underlying drivers, and coordinate appropriate specialist input.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Reassess symptom trajectory, examination findings, and objective markers to confirm response in liver cirrhosis.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Escalate urgently for clinical deterioration, evolving red flags, or failure to improve in suspected liver cirrhosis.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Liver Cirrhosis"][/sc]
  • Variceal bleeding - Life-threatening complication of portal hypertension.
  • Hepatorenal syndrome - Renal failure due to advanced cirrhosis.
  • Liver cancer - Cirrhosis is a major risk factor for hepatocellular carcinoma.
