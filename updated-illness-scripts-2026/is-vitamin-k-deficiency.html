Vitamin K Deficiency
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Vitamin K Deficiency"][/sc]
  • Vitamin K deficiency is a nutritional deficiency in which there is insufficient vitamin K to support normal blood clotting and bone health.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise vitamin features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.

  • Higher-risk or missed groups: Vitamin K Deficiency may be missed when presentation is atypical or obscured by comorbidity.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Vitamin K Deficiency"][/sc]
  • Vitamin K is crucial for the post-translational carboxylation of glutamate residues on clotting factors. Without adequate vitamin K, clotting factors remain inactive, leading to impaired clot formation and increased bleeding risk.
  • The liver requires vitamin K to synthesize these clotting factors. Vitamin K deficiency commonly arises due to poor dietary intake, fat malabsorption (e.g., due to liver disease, bile duct obstruction, or coeliac disease), or use of vitamin K antagonists (e.g., warfarin).
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Vitamin K Deficiency"][/sc]
  • Acute deficiency can develop within days to weeks of inadequate intake, particularly if other risk factors like antibiotic use are present. Chronic deficiency may present subtly with minor bleeding issues that worsen over time if untreated.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Vitamin K Deficiency"][/sc]
  • Ask about easy bruising - Bruising occurs due to impaired coagulation from reduced levels of clotting factors.
  • Ask about bleeding gums - A common bleeding site due to fragile capillaries and reduced clotting capacity.
  • Ask about epistaxis (nosebleeds) - Common due to fragile vessels in the nasal mucosa and reduced coagulation capacity.
  • Ask about menorrhagia in women - Heavy menstrual bleeding can be exacerbated by coagulopathy.
  • Ask about prolonged bleeding from cuts or after procedures - Reflects impaired clot formation in response to injury.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Vitamin K Deficiency"][/sc]
  • Check ecchymoses (large bruises) - Bruises on skin indicate bleeding into subcutaneous tissue due to poor clotting.
  • Check haemarthrosis (joint bleeding) - Rare but may occur with severe deficiency, especially in patients with haemophilia.
  • Check purpura and petechiae - Small, non-blanching spots indicative of microvascular bleeding, common in severe deficiencies.
  • Check oozing at surgical or wound sites - Due to poor clot formation, seen in moderate to severe deficiency cases.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Vitamin K Deficiency"][/sc]
  • Intracranial haemorrhage in neonates - Potentially fatal; signifies severe deficiency and requires urgent intervention.
  • Severe gastrointestinal bleeding - Indicates significant deficiency, particularly in malabsorption disorders, and may lead to hypovolaemia or shock if untreated.
  • Profuse bleeding during or post-surgery - Suggests unrecognised severe deficiency with high-risk implications for patient safety.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Vitamin K Deficiency"][/sc]
  • Asymptomatic with prolonged PT only - May be identified incidentally, often in patients with mild, subclinical deficiency.
  • Haematuria without other bleeding signs - Less common, may be observed in mild deficiency states or with concurrent urological conditions.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask vitamin k deficiency.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Vitamin K Deficiency"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Prothrombin time (PT) - Prolonged in vitamin K deficiency due to reduced synthesis of vitamin K-dependent clotting factors; Activated partial thromboplastin.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Vitamin K serum levels - Low levels confirm deficiency, particularly useful if malabsorption is suspected; Clotting factor assay - Reduced activity.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Vitamin K Deficiency"][/sc]
  • Haemophilia - X-linked bleeding disorder; often family history present, normal vitamin K levels.
  • Platelet dysfunction (e.g., ITP) - Often presents with petechiae, normal PT and aPTT.
  • Liver disease - Leads to prolonged PT and aPTT but may present with additional signs of liver.
  • Related alternative diagnosis - Distinguish from vitamin k deficiency using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Vitamin K Deficiency"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Assess severity, physiological stability, and immediate complications.
  • Core care: Deliver condition-specific management for vitamin k deficiency, treat underlying drivers, and coordinate appropriate specialist input.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Reassess symptom trajectory, examination findings, and objective markers to confirm response in vitamin k deficiency.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Escalate urgently for clinical deterioration, evolving red flags, or failure to improve in suspected vitamin k deficiency.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Vitamin K Deficiency"][/sc]
  • Severe haemorrhage - Common in untreated cases, potentially life-threatening (e.g., intracranial bleeding).
  • Hypovolaemic shock - Secondary to severe bleeding, particularly in gastrointestinal cases.
  • Neurological sequelae - In neonates with intracranial bleeding, long-term developmental complications may result.
