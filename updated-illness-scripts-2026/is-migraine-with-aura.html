Migraine with Aura
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Migraine with Aura"][/sc]
  • Migraine with aura is a primary headache disorder characterised by recurrent episodes of moderate-to-severe headache, typically unilateral, accompanied by transient neurological symptoms called an "aura".
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise migraine features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.

Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Migraine with Aura"][/sc]
  • Cortical spreading depression: A wave of neuronal depolarisation followed by suppression of brain activity propagates across the cerebral cortex, particularly the occipital lobe, leading to the aura symptoms (especially visual disturbances).
  • Activation of the trigeminovascular system: This leads to the release of vasoactive peptides such as CGRP (calcitonin gene-related peptide), which causes vasodilation of cerebral blood vessels, contributing to the headache.
  • Neurogenic inflammation: Release of inflammatory mediators around the meninges leads to further sensitisation of pain pathways, exacerbating headache severity.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Migraine with Aura"][/sc]
  • Prodrome: Occurs hours to days before the headache, includes mood changes, neck stiffness, and food cravings.
  • Aura: Develops over 5–20 minutes, lasting up to 60 minutes. Visual disturbances are the most common, but sensory and speech disturbances may also occur.
  • Headache: Lasts 4–72 hours, often unilateral, pulsating, moderate-to-severe intensity, and aggravated by physical activity.
  • Postdrome: May last 24–48 hours after the headache, with fatigue, difficulty concentrating, and generalised malaise.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Migraine with Aura"][/sc]
  • Ask about visual disturbances (aura): Including zigzag lines, scotomas, and flashes of light. Caused by cortical spreading depression, which predominantly affects the occipital lobe.
  • Ask about unilateral, throbbing headache: Caused by activation of trigeminal pain pathways and vasodilation of intracranial vessels.
  • Ask about nausea and vomiting: Likely due to activation of the brainstem and the autonomic nervous system.
  • Ask about photophobia and phonophobia: Heightened sensitivity to light and sound, linked to the sensitisation of central pain pathways.
  • Ask about speech disturbances: Including dysphasia, due to cortical involvement in the temporal lobe during aura.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Migraine with Aura"][/sc]
  • Check no specific physical signs: Migraine with aura is typically a clinical diagnosis, and no characteristic findings are observable during the headache-free period.
  • Check visual field deficits: May be detected during the aura phase in cases with visual symptoms, due to occipital cortex involvement.
  • Check mild neurological deficits during aura: Such as weakness or altered sensation, which resolve within 60 minutes.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Migraine with Aura"][/sc]
  • Sudden onset "thunderclap" headache: Could indicate subarachnoid haemorrhage (SAH) or other serious pathology rather than migraine.
  • Headache with fever and neck stiffness: May suggest meningitis, a medical emergency.
  • New neurological deficits persisting beyond the aura phase: Raises concern for stroke or transient ischaemic attack (TIA).
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Migraine with Aura"][/sc]
  • Acephalgic migraine: Aura occurs without the subsequent headache, making diagnosis challenging. Caused by the same cortical spreading depression but without activating pain pathways.
  • Hemiplegic migraine: A rare subtype where the aura includes transient hemiparesis, mimicking a stroke.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask migraine with aura.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Migraine with Aura"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Clinical diagnosis: Based on history of recurrent headaches with aura. No specific diagnostic test, but neuroimaging (MRI brain) may be used.
  • Targeted and monitoring: MRI or CT brain: Normal in typical migraine but may be done to exclude structural causes, especially if red flags are.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Migraine with Aura"][/sc]
  • Tension headache - Bilateral, pressing/tightening headache without aura, often related to stress. No nausea or photophobia.
  • Cluster headache - Severe unilateral headache with autonomic features (e.g., lacrimation, rhinorrhoea), but no aura.
  • Transient ischaemic attack (TIA) - Distinguish from migraine with aura using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.
  • Seizure - Distinguish from migraine with aura using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Migraine with Aura"][/sc]
  • Medication overuse headache: Frequent use of acute headache medication may lead to chronic daily headaches.
  • Migraine with brainstem aura: Involves brainstem symptoms such as vertigo, dysarthria, and ataxia, indicating more widespread cortical involvement.
  • Stroke: Migraine with aura is associated with a slightly increased risk of stroke, particularly in women using oestrogen-containing contraceptives.
