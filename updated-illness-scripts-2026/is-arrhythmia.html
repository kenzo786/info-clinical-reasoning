Arrhythmia
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Arrhythmia"][/sc]
  • Arrhythmia refers to an abnormal heart rhythm resulting from improper electrical signalling within the heart.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise arrhythmia features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Arrhythmia"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Arrhythmia"][/sc]
  • Risk factors: Risk of arrhythmia rises with relevant predisposition, comorbidity, and exposure profile.
  • Higher-risk or missed groups: Arrhythmia may be missed when presentations are atypical or comorbidity obscures key features.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Arrhythmia"][/sc]
  • Arrhythmias occur due to disturbances in the heart's electrical conduction system, which includes the sinoatrial (SA) node, atrioventricular (AV) node, and conduction pathways. These disturbances may result from structural heart disease, electrolyte imbalances, ischaemia, or autonomic influences.
  • In atrial fibrillation, rapid and disorganised electrical signals originate from the atria, leading to ineffective atrial contraction and an irregularly irregular ventricular response. Tachycardia may arise from re-entry circuits (e.g., atrioventricular nodal re-entrant tachycardia, AVNRT), enhanced automaticity, or triggered activity.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Arrhythmia"][/sc]
  • Arrhythmias may present acutely, such as with paroxysmal AF, or have a chronic, persistent course requiring long-term management. Paroxysmal episodes can last from seconds to days, while persistent arrhythmias persist longer and require intervention to restore sinus rhythm. Long-term complications like heart failure or thromboembolic events (e.g., stroke) may develop in chronic cases, particularly AF.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Arrhythmia"][/sc]
  • Ask about palpitations - Common symptom due to rapid or irregular beating of the heart. This occurs as the heart struggles to maintain a normal rhythm.
  • Ask about fatigue - Results from decreased cardiac output, particularly in AF where the irregular rhythm reduces the efficiency of atrial contraction.
  • Ask about dizziness or Light-headedness - Occurs due to transient cerebral hypoperfusion, especially in cases of bradycardia or sustained tachyarrhythmias.
  • Ask about shortness of Breath - Can occur due to the reduced cardiac output, leading to pulmonary congestion or inadequate systemic oxygen delivery.
  • Ask about chest Pain - May occur in individuals with underlying coronary artery disease (CAD) due to increased oxygen demand from a rapid heart rate or in this clinical context.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Arrhythmia"][/sc]
  • Check irregular Pulse - Typically seen in AF, characterised by an irregularly irregular rhythm due to disorganised atrial activity.
  • Check tachycardia - May be present in supraventricular tachycardia (SVT) or ventricular tachycardia (VT) where the heart rate exceeds 100 bpm.
  • Check hypotension - Due to reduced cardiac output, particularly in arrhythmias that impair effective ventricular filling, such as AF with rapid ventricular response (RVR).
  • Check jugular Venous Distension (JVD) - May be seen in cases of heart failure due to long-standing arrhythmias, indicating increased central venous pressure.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Arrhythmia"][/sc]
  • Syncope - Suggests severe arrhythmias like ventricular tachycardia (VT) or complete heart block, which may lead to transient or complete loss of consciousness due to inadequate cerebral perfusion.
  • Chest Pain with Arrhythmia - May indicate underlying ischaemia or myocardial infarction, requiring urgent intervention.
  • Heart Failure Symptoms - Such as peripheral oedema or worsening shortness of breath, indicating poor ventricular function secondary to chronic arrhythmia.
  • Severe Bradycardia or Tachycardia (>150 bpm) - Can lead to haemodynamic instability and requires immediate management.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Arrhythmia"][/sc]
  • Silent Atrial Fibrillation - Some patients may have AF without typical symptoms like palpitations, instead presenting with complications like stroke or heart failure.
  • Atypical Chest Discomfort - Rather than classic angina, patients with arrhythmia may report vague chest discomfort, which can be easily overlooked.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features or comorbidity can mask the underlying diagnosis.

Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Arrhythmia"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Assess physiological stability and urgent complications at presentation, and initiate early stabilisation where required.
  • Core care: Deliver condition-specific treatment for arrhythmia, address underlying drivers, and involve appropriate specialist support.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Reassess symptom trajectory, objective markers, and treatment response over time to detect complications early.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Worsening symptoms, physiological deterioration, or failure to improve should trigger urgent reassessment for severe or alternative pathology in arrhythmia.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Arrhythmia"][/sc]
  • Stroke - Due to thromboembolism from stasis of blood in the fibrillating atria, particularly in AF.
  • Heart Failure - Arrhythmias, especially when sustained, can lead to left ventricular dysfunction and exacerbation of heart failure symptoms.
  • Sudden Cardiac Death - A risk in severe arrhythmias such as VT or VF, which can lead to cardiac arrest without immediate intervention.
