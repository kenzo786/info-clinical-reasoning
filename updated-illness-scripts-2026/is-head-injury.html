Head Injury
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Head Injury"][/sc]
  • A head injury refers to any trauma to the scalp, skull, or brain, ranging from minor impacts to severe brain injury.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise head features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Head Injury"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Head Injury"][/sc]
  • Risk factors: Risk factors for head injury include relevant predisposition, comorbidity, and exposure history.
  • Higher-risk or missed groups: Head Injury may be missed when presentation is atypical or obscured by comorbidity.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Head Injury"][/sc]
  • Head injuries cause damage through direct trauma, leading to brain contusions, intracranial haemorrhage, and diffuse axonal injury (DAI).
  • Primary injury occurs at the time of trauma with mechanical disruption of brain tissues.
  • Secondary injury develops hours to days after the trauma, often due to cerebral oedema, hypoxia, or raised intracranial pressure (ICP), resulting in further neuronal injury.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Head Injury"][/sc]
  • Immediate: Altered consciousness, amnesia, and initial neurological deficits.
  • Acute (hours to days): Development of cerebral oedema, haemorrhage, and raised ICP.
  • Subacute (days to weeks): Recovery phase for mild injuries, though persistent symptoms may occur.
  • Chronic: Possible long-term effects include post-concussion syndrome, cognitive deficits, and epilepsy.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Head Injury"][/sc]
  • Ask about headache - Common due to direct trauma or raised ICP.
  • Ask about dizziness - Often a result of vestibular disruption or post-traumatic amnesia.
  • Ask about confusion - Indicates cortical or brainstem injury, affecting consciousness.
  • Ask about nausea and vomiting - Caused by increased ICP or vestibular involvement.
  • Ask about memory loss - Frequently due to temporal lobe injury or DAI.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Head Injury"][/sc]
  • Check altered Glasgow Coma Scale (GCS) - Reflects the level of consciousness; lower scores indicate severe injury.
  • Check bruising around eyes ("raccoon eyes") or behind ears ("Battle’s sign") - Indicates possible skull fracture.
  • Check pupillary asymmetry - May signify raised ICP or brain herniation.
  • Check cSF rhinorrhoea or otorrhoea - Leaking cerebrospinal fluid suggests a skull base fracture.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Head Injury"][/sc]
  • Severe headache and vomiting - May indicate raised ICP or haemorrhage.
  • Progressive drowsiness or confusion - Suggests worsening brain injury or raised ICP.
  • Seizures - Sign of cortical damage, may indicate intracranial haemorrhage.
  • Pupillary changes - Could signify impending herniation.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Head Injury"][/sc]
  • Subtle memory impairment in elderly - Mild head injury may present as subtle cognitive changes.
  • Delayed haemorrhage in anticoagulated patients - Bleeding risk post-minor injury due to anticoagulation therapy.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask head injury.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Head Injury"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: CT Head - Detects skull fractures, haemorrhage, and brain oedema.
  • Targeted and monitoring: MRI Brain - Useful for assessing diffuse axonal injury and delayed haemorrhages; Blood tests - Full blood count and clotting studies.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Head Injury"][/sc]
  • Stroke - Sudden neurological deficits, usually focal; less likely to have trauma history.
  • Seizure - Possible confusion post-event, but no external signs of trauma.
  • Migraine - Recurrent headaches with no trauma, typically without neurological deficit or vomiting.
  • Related alternative diagnosis - Distinguish from head injury using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Head Injury"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Assess severity, physiological stability, and immediate complications.
  • Core care: Deliver condition-specific management for head injury, treat underlying drivers, and coordinate appropriate specialist input.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Reassess symptom trajectory, examination findings, and objective markers to confirm response in head injury.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Escalate urgently for clinical deterioration, evolving red flags, or failure to improve in suspected head injury.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Head Injury"][/sc]
  • Post-concussion syndrome - Common, with symptoms lasting weeks to months.
  • Seizures - Occur in around 5-10% of moderate to severe TBI cases due to cortical scarring.
  • Chronic traumatic encephalopathy (CTE) - Neurodegenerative condition from repeated head injuries, associated with cognitive decline.
