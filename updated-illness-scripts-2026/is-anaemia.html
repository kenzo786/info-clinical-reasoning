Anaemia
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Anaemia"][/sc]
  • Anaemia is reduced haemoglobin concentration causing diminished oxygen-carrying capacity and resulting systemic tissue hypoxia symptoms.
  • UKMLA essentials: fatigue with exertional dyspnoea, syncope or chest pain concern, confirm with FBC and indices.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Anaemia"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Anaemia"][/sc]
  • Risk factors: Risk of anaemia rises with relevant predisposition, comorbidity, and exposure profile.
  • Higher-risk or missed groups: Anaemia may be missed when presentations are atypical or comorbidity obscures key features.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Anaemia"][/sc]
  • Blood loss: Acute or chronic bleeding can lead to iron deficiency anaemia due to the loss of iron-containing RBCs.
  • Decreased RBC production: This can occur due to iron, vitamin B12, or folate deficiency, or due to chronic diseases that impair erythropoiesis (e.g., chronic kidney disease).
  • Increased RBC destruction: Conditions like haemolytic anaemias lead to premature destruction of RBCs, causing anaemia.
  • Impaired haemoglobin synthesis: Conditions such as thalassaemia result in defective haemoglobin production, leading to anaemia.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Anaemia"][/sc]
  • Acute anaemia: May develop quickly in cases of acute blood loss, presenting within hours to days.
  • Chronic anaemia: Develops slowly over weeks to months in conditions like chronic disease or nutritional deficiencies, allowing time for physiological adaptation, leading to fewer symptoms.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Anaemia"][/sc]
  • Ask about fatigue: Reduced oxygen delivery to tissues results in decreased energy production and generalised tiredness.
  • Ask about dyspnoea on exertion: Reduced haemoglobin levels limit oxygen transport, causing shortness of breath during physical activity.
  • Ask about dizziness: Decreased oxygenation of the brain can cause light-headedness or dizziness.
  • Ask about pallor: Less haemoglobin in the blood gives the skin a pale appearance, especially noticeable in areas like the conjunctivae.
  • Ask about asymptomatic: Anaemia can be asymptomatic, particularly if it develops gradually and the body has time to compensate.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Anaemia"][/sc]
  • Check pallor: Visible paleness of the skin and mucous membranes due to decreased oxyhaemoglobin.
  • Check tachycardia: A compensatory increase in heart rate occurs to maintain oxygen delivery to tissues.
  • Check glossitis: In conditions like iron or vitamin B12 deficiency, the tongue may become swollen, smooth, and red.
  • Check spoon nails (koilonychia): Typically associated with iron deficiency anaemia, leading to thin, brittle nails with a concave shape.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Anaemia"][/sc]
  • Severe fatigue with chest pain: May indicate heart failure or myocardial ischaemia due to inadequate oxygen delivery.
  • Dark or bloody stools (melaena): Can indicate gastrointestinal bleeding as a cause of iron deficiency anaemia.
  • Jaundice: Suggests haemolytic anaemia, where RBCs are destroyed at a faster rate, causing bilirubin build-up.
  • Weight loss and night sweats: Could be indicative of malignancy or chronic infection, causing anaemia of chronic disease.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Anaemia"][/sc]
  • Mild anaemia in elderly patients: Often presents with vague symptoms like weakness or cognitive impairment, which may be mistaken for ageing.
  • Anaemia in pregnancy: May present with normal haemoglobin levels but reduced in relation to the increased plasma volume (physiological anaemia of pregnancy).
  • How it gets missed: atypical features or comorbidity can mask the underlying diagnosis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Anaemia"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Full blood count with indices and blood film to classify anaemia pattern, plus haemodynamic assessment for severity and instability.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Iron studies, B12 and folate, reticulocyte count, haemolysis profile, and renal/inflammatory tests guided by clinical context and suspected cause.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Anaemia"][/sc]
  • A 65-year-old woman presents with 3 months of fatigue, exertional breathlessness, and pallor, with haemoglobin 95 g/L on full blood count.
  • Adult with symptomatic low haemoglobin and reduced exercise tolerance, requiring classification of anaemia type and cause.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Anaemia"][/sc]
  • Iron deficiency anaemia - Microcytosis with low ferritin and an identifiable blood-loss or malabsorption source suggests iron deficiency.
  • Anaemia of chronic disease - Normocytic or mildly microcytic anaemia with inflammatory comorbidity and relatively preserved ferritin supports inflammatory sequestration.
  • Vitamin B12 deficiency - Macrocytosis with neurological symptoms and low B12 indicates cobalamin deficiency.
  • Haemolytic anaemia - Jaundice, raised LDH, low haptoglobin, and reticulocytosis indicate increased red-cell destruction.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Anaemia"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Identify haemodynamic instability or end-organ ischaemia and stabilise while investigating urgent causes of significant anaemia.
  • Core care: Treat the underlying aetiology with cause-specific replacement, bleeding control, chronic disease optimisation, or specialist therapy.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Repeat haemoglobin and relevant biomarkers to confirm marrow response and ensure correction of the underlying cause.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Worsening dyspnoea, chest pain, syncope, active bleeding, or failure of haemoglobin recovery should prompt urgent reassessment.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Anaemia"][/sc]
  • Heart failure: Chronic anaemia leads to compensatory mechanisms such as tachycardia, which may strain the heart and lead to failure.
  • Impaired cognitive function: Particularly in the elderly, chronic anaemia can contribute to cognitive decline and dementia.
  • Infections: Severe anaemia can weaken the immune system, increasing susceptibility to infections.
