Haemoptysis
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Haemoptysis"][/sc]
  • Haemoptysis is the expectoration of blood originating from the lower respiratory tract, often from pulmonary vessels or bronchial circulation.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise haemoptysis features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Haemoptysis"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Haemoptysis"][/sc]
  • UK GP population: Haemoptysis is a rare presentation, with most cases attributed to infections, but it can occasionally indicate serious underlying disease.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Haemoptysis"][/sc]
  • Bronchial circulation rupture: Increased pressure in bronchial arteries (e.g., due to chronic lung inflammation or malignancy) can cause vessel rupture and bleeding.
  • Infectious erosion: Pathogens, particularly mycobacterium in tuberculosis, erode lung tissue and vasculature, leading to bleeding into airways.
  • Vascular compromise: Conditions like pulmonary embolism can cause haemorrhage into alveoli due to sudden vascular occlusion, resulting in haemoptysis.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Haemoptysis"][/sc]
  • Acute onset: Commonly seen in infections or pulmonary embolism; sudden with rapid worsening.
  • Subacute/chronic: Seen in cases like lung cancer or bronchiectasis, where haemoptysis develops slowly and may progress gradually.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Haemoptysis"][/sc]
  • Ask about blood in sputum: Ranging from streaks to larger amounts; due to bleeding into respiratory passages.
  • Ask about cough: Often accompanies haemoptysis, as coughing reflex is triggered by blood or irritants in airways.
  • Ask about dyspnoea: Related to airway obstruction from blood or underlying lung disease.
  • Ask about pleuritic chest pain: Especially in cases of pulmonary embolism or infection where pleura are irritated.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Haemoptysis"][/sc]
  • Check tachypnoea: Common due to hypoxia or pain; rapid breathing compensates for compromised oxygen exchange.
  • Check crackles or wheezing: Due to underlying lung disease (e.g., bronchiectasis, infection) causing turbulent airflow.
  • Check cyanosis: Occurs in severe cases due to impaired gas exchange from blood in alveoli or significant underlying lung pathology.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Haemoptysis"][/sc]
  • Massive haemoptysis: Life-threatening; risk of asphyxiation due to airway obstruction or hypovolaemic shock.
  • Weight loss and night sweats: Suggest malignancy or tuberculosis; significant for identifying systemic disease.
  • Persistent or worsening haemoptysis: Concerning for malignancy or untreated infection.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Haemoptysis"][/sc]
  • Scant haemoptysis with non-specific symptoms: May mask underlying cancer or TB, presenting more subtly in elderly or immunocompromised.
  • Haemoptysis without cough: Can be seen in some cases of lung cancer or pulmonary embolism, especially with minimal airway irritation.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask haemoptysis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Haemoptysis"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Chest X-ray: Initial assessment for masses, infections, or structural abnormalities; CT Pulmonary Angiography: Gold standard for detecting pulmonary emboli and assessing.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Sputum culture and acid-fast bacilli testing: Identifies bacterial causes or TB; essential for cases with chronic or recurrent haemoptysis; Bronchoscopy: Used.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Haemoptysis"][/sc]
  • Bronchiectasis - Presents with chronic cough and purulent sputum; differs by having a history of recurrent.
  • Pulmonary embolism - Acute onset, often with pleuritic pain and dyspnoea; blood in sputum tends to be.
  • TB - Distinguish from haemoptysis using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.
  • Lung cancer - Persistent haemoptysis, weight loss, and localised chest pain; may be occult in early stages.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Haemoptysis"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Assess severity, physiological stability, and immediate complications.
  • Core care: Deliver condition-specific management for haemoptysis, treat underlying drivers, and coordinate appropriate specialist input.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Reassess symptom trajectory, examination findings, and objective markers to confirm response in haemoptysis.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Escalate urgently for clinical deterioration, evolving red flags, or failure to improve in suspected haemoptysis.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Haemoptysis"][/sc]
  • Asphyxiation: Massive haemoptysis obstructing airways can result in life-threatening hypoxia.
  • Respiratory failure: Chronic haemoptysis may lead to worsening pulmonary function and oxygenation deficits.
  • Shock: Severe blood loss can lead to hypovolaemic shock, particularly in massive haemoptysis cases.
