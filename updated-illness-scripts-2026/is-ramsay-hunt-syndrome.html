Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Ramsay Hunt Syndrome"][/sc]
  • Ramsay Hunt syndrome is caused by the reactivation of dormant varicella-zoster virus in the geniculate ganglion, which affects the facial nerve. VZV can remain latent in the sensory ganglia after the initial chickenpox infection. Reactivation may occur due to stress, immunosuppression, or ageing, leading to inflammation and damage of the facial nerve and other cranial nerves (V, IX, X) in some cases. The virus may also cause localised vasculitis and nerve oedema.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Ramsay Hunt Syndrome"][/sc]
  • The disease typically begins with non-specific symptoms such as malaise and ear pain. Within 1-3 days, facial paralysis develops, accompanied by a vesicular rash in or around the ear. Symptoms peak in about a week, and without treatment, recovery can take weeks to months, with some cases of permanent nerve damage.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Ramsay Hunt Syndrome"][/sc]
  • Ask about facial paralysis - Due to VZV-induced inflammation of the facial nerve, resulting in unilateral facial drooping.
  • Ask about otalgia (ear pain) - Caused by irritation of the sensory branches of the facial and trigeminal nerves.
  • Ask about vesicular rash - Seen in the external auditory canal or palate, due to viral replication in sensory ganglia.
  • Ask about tinnitus - VZV affects the auditory nerve, leading to abnormal auditory perceptions.
  • Ask about hearing loss - Conductive or sensorineural, related to involvement of the cochlear nerve or middle ear structures.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Ramsay Hunt Syndrome"][/sc]
  • Check unilateral facial weakness - Lower motor neuron facial palsy affecting both upper and lower face, confirming involvement of the facial nerve.
  • Check vesicular rash - Characteristic herpetiform vesicles in the ear canal, tympanic membrane, or oral mucosa.
  • Check loss of taste on the anterior two-thirds of the tongue - Reflecting facial nerve involvement in gustatory sensation.
  • Check hyperacusis - Increased sensitivity to sound due to paralysis of the stapedius muscle, controlled by the facial nerve.
  • Check decreased lacrimation - Reflects parasympathetic dysfunction of the facial nerve affecting tear production.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Ramsay Hunt Syndrome"][/sc]
  • Bilateral facial weakness - May suggest a more systemic neurological disorder such as Guillain-Barré syndrome or sarcoidosis.
  • Hearing loss with vertigo - Indicates vestibulocochlear nerve involvement, which can lead to permanent auditory or balance deficits.
  • Ophthalmoplegia - Involvement of cranial nerve III, IV, or VI suggests spread of the infection and risk of intracranial complications.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Ramsay Hunt Syndrome"][/sc]
  • Absence of rash (zoster sine herpete) - Some patients may have facial palsy and ear pain without visible vesicles, making diagnosis more challenging.
  • Facial pain without paralysis - Rare cases where only sensory involvement occurs, without motor symptoms.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask ramsay hunt syndrome.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Ramsay Hunt Syndrome"][/sc]
  • Permanent facial weakness - Occurs in up to 30% of untreated cases due to nerve degeneration.
  • Postherpetic neuralgia - Persistent pain after the rash heals, more common in older adults.
  • Sensorineural hearing loss - Can be permanent if the cochlear nerve is involved.
