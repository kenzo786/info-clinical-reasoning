Cytomegalovirus (CMV) Infection
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Cytomegalovirus (CMV) Infection"][/sc]
  • Cytomegalovirus (CMV) is a common viral infection caused by a member of the Herpesviridae family.

Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Cytomegalovirus (CMV) Infection"][/sc]
  • CMV is transmitted through body fluids. Following primary infection, CMV remains latent in monocytes and macrophages.
  • Reactivation can occur during immunosuppression, such as in organ transplant recipients or individuals with HIV/AIDS.
  • CMV disrupts cellular immunity, leading to a range of immune-mediated and direct cytopathic effects in organs like the lungs, liver, and retina.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Cytomegalovirus (CMV) Infection"][/sc]
  • Incubation: 3-12 weeks for primary infection, often asymptomatic in immunocompetent individuals.
  • Acute phase: Symptomatic infections typically resolve over 2-6 weeks.
  • Chronic phase: Virus can remain latent indefinitely, with possible reactivation under immunosuppression.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Cytomegalovirus (CMV) Infection"][/sc]
  • Ask about fever - Common in symptomatic CMV; caused by immune response to viral replication.
  • Ask about fatigue - Often reported, likely due to immune activation and cytokine release.
  • Ask about myalgia - Muscle aches are common, related to inflammatory cytokine activity.
  • Ask about pharyngitis - Mild sore throat may occur, resembling mononucleosis.
  • Ask about lymphadenopathy - Enlarged lymph nodes due to immune response.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Cytomegalovirus (CMV) Infection"][/sc]
  • Check fever - Indicates systemic infection.
  • Check hepatosplenomegaly - Common in congenital CMV or immunocompromised patients, due to viral spread to liver and spleen.
  • Check chorioretinitis - Seen in CMV retinitis, indicates severe eye involvement.
  • Check pneumonitis signs - Dyspnoea, crackles on auscultation; due to lung infection.
  • Check oral or genital ulcers - May present in severe cases, related to localised viral cytopathic effects.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Cytomegalovirus (CMV) Infection"][/sc]
  • Visual loss - Indicates CMV retinitis, risk of blindness without treatment.
  • Severe respiratory symptoms - May suggest CMV pneumonitis in immunocompromised patients.
  • Hepatitis - Severe liver inflammation can indicate systemic disease.
  • Severe CNS symptoms - Headache, confusion may suggest encephalitis.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Cytomegalovirus (CMV) Infection"][/sc]
  • Isolated gastrointestinal symptoms - CMV colitis may present with diarrhoea, abdominal pain, common in HIV patients.
  • Asymptomatic - Often found incidentally in immunocompetent individuals during blood tests.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask cytomegalovirus (cmv) infection.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Cytomegalovirus (CMV) Infection"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Serology - CMV IgM and IgG antibodies; IgM suggests recent infection, IgG indicates past exposure; Polymerase chain reaction (PCR) - Detects.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Histology - “Owl’s eye” inclusion bodies in affected tissues confirm CMV infection; Ophthalmologic exam - Indicated for CMV retinitis, shows retinal.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Cytomegalovirus (CMV) Infection"][/sc]
  • Epstein-Barr Virus (EBV) Infection - Presents similarly but often with more pharyngitis and heterophile antibody positivity.
  • Toxoplasmosis - In HIV patients, may cause chorioretinitis but with cerebral lesions seen on imaging.
  • HIV Retinopathy - Non-infectious, slower progression, lacks “owl’s eye” inclusions on histology.
  • Bacterial Pneumonia - Consider if respiratory symptoms predominate but lacks specific CMV serology and PCR findings.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Cytomegalovirus (CMV) Infection"][/sc]
  • Blindness - Due to untreated CMV retinitis.
  • Organ rejection in transplants - CMV can trigger immune activation, leading to graft rejection.
  • Encephalitis - Severe CNS involvement with high morbidity and potential mortality.
  • Pneumonitis - Life-threatening, especially in transplant patients, due to viral infiltration in the lungs.
