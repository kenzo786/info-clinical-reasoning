Subconjunctival Haemorrhage
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Subconjunctival Haemorrhage"][/sc]
  • Subconjunctival haemorrhage is a benign condition characterised by the rupture of small blood vessels beneath the conjunctiva, leading to a sharply demarcated red patch in the sclera.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise subconjunctival features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.

Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Subconjunctival Haemorrhage"][/sc]
  • Rupture of small conjunctival vessels occurs due to sudden increases in venous pressure (e.g., coughing, sneezing, straining).
  • Blood accumulates in the potential space beneath the conjunctiva, which does not absorb blood rapidly, leading to the visible red patch.
  • Common underlying causes include hypertension, trauma, or coagulopathy, which predispose vessels to rupture.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Subconjunctival Haemorrhage"][/sc]
  • Acute onset with blood accumulation visible immediately or within hours.
  • Resolution occurs spontaneously over 1-2 weeks as blood is reabsorbed, with a colour change from red to yellow as it resolves.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Subconjunctival Haemorrhage"][/sc]
  • Ask about asymptomatic - Most patients experience no discomfort; the condition is often noted incidentally.
  • Ask about mild eye irritation - Rarely, there may be a sensation of grittiness or foreign body sensation, caused by local inflammation.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Subconjunctival Haemorrhage"][/sc]
  • Check localised red patch - Sharply demarcated red area on the sclera, explained by extravasation of blood under the conjunctiva.
  • Check no discharge or swelling - Lack of associated conjunctival swelling or purulent discharge distinguishes it from infective causes.
  • Check normal vision - Vision remains unaffected as the haemorrhage does not involve deeper ocular structures.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Subconjunctival Haemorrhage"][/sc]
  • Recurrent haemorrhages - May indicate underlying bleeding disorders or anticoagulant overdose.
  • Associated trauma - Raises concern for globe rupture or orbital fractures.
  • Systemic symptoms - Such as bruising or petechiae, which may suggest coagulopathies or haematological malignancies.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Subconjunctival Haemorrhage"][/sc]
  • Diffuse haemorrhage - Rare, covering the entire sclera and potentially masking underlying trauma or severe systemic causes.
  • Involvement of other areas - Concomitant haemorrhages in other mucosal or skin regions suggest systemic bleeding disorders.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask subconjunctival haemorrhage.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Subconjunctival Haemorrhage"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Blood pressure measurement - To detect hypertension as a potential underlying cause.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Coagulation studies - Including INR and platelet count, to evaluate for coagulopathies; Ocular examination - Using slit-lamp to exclude deeper ocular.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Subconjunctival Haemorrhage"][/sc]
  • Conjunctivitis - Differentiated by discharge, pruritus, and generalised conjunctival redness.
  • Traumatic hyphema - Involves blood in the anterior chamber, typically following trauma, and may affect vision.
  • Scleritis - Associated with pain and a deep bluish-red hue due to inflammation of the sclera.
  • Related alternative diagnosis - Distinguish from subconjunctival haemorrhage using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Subconjunctival Haemorrhage"][/sc]
  • Recurrence - May indicate poorly controlled hypertension or an unrecognised bleeding disorder.
  • Masked injury - In traumatic cases, more serious ocular damage may go undetected.
