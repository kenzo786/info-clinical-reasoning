Aortic Stenosis
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Aortic Stenosis"][/sc]
  • Aortic stenosis (AS) is the narrowing of the aortic valve opening, resulting in restricted blood flow from the left ventricle to the aorta during systole.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise aortic features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Aortic Stenosis"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Aortic Stenosis"][/sc]
  • Risk factors: Risk of aortic stenosis rises with relevant predisposition, comorbidity, and exposure profile.
  • Higher-risk or missed groups: Aortic Stenosis may be missed when presentations are atypical or comorbidity obscures key features.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Aortic Stenosis"][/sc]
  • 1. Progressive calcification or fibrosis causes narrowing of the aortic valve opening.
  • 2. The left ventricle compensates by hypertrophying to maintain cardiac output.
  • 3. Over time, increased left ventricular wall stress leads to diastolic dysfunction and myocardial ischaemia.
  • 4. This results in symptoms of heart failure, angina, and syncope.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Aortic Stenosis"][/sc]
  • Early: Asymptomatic phase lasting years to decades.
  • Progressive: Gradual development of symptoms such as exertional dyspnoea, fatigue, and syncope.
  • Late: Advanced heart failure symptoms, with a high risk of sudden cardiac death if untreated.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Aortic Stenosis"][/sc]
  • Ask about exertional dyspnoea - Reduced cardiac output and increased left ventricular pressure lead to pulmonary congestion during exertion.
  • Ask about chest pain (angina) - Myocardial ischaemia occurs due to increased oxygen demand from hypertrophied myocardium and reduced coronary perfusion.
  • Ask about syncope - Decreased cerebral perfusion during exertion due to fixed cardiac output, resulting in transient loss of consciousness.
  • Ask about fatigue - Due to reduced cardiac output, leading to decreased oxygen delivery to tissues.
  • Ask about palpitations - Often due to arrhythmias secondary to left ventricular hypertrophy or atrial enlargement.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Aortic Stenosis"][/sc]
  • Check ejection systolic murmur - A crescendo-decrescendo murmur heard best at the right upper sternal border, caused by turbulent flow through the narrowed valve.
  • Check reduced or delayed carotid upstroke ("pulsus parvus et tardus") - Caused by reduced blood flow through the aortic valve.
  • Check narrow pulse pressure - The difference between systolic and diastolic pressures is diminished due to reduced stroke volume.
  • Check heaving apex beat - Reflects left ventricular hypertrophy as the heart works harder to pump blood through the stenotic valve.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Aortic Stenosis"][/sc]
  • Syncope - Signifies severe stenosis, often occurring during exertion. Indicates increased risk of sudden cardiac death due to fixed cardiac output.
  • Heart failure symptoms (e.g., orthopnoea, paroxysmal nocturnal dyspnoea) - Suggests advanced disease with significant left ventricular dysfunction.
  • Sudden death - Can occur due to arrhythmias or left ventricular failure in severe aortic stenosis.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Aortic Stenosis"][/sc]
  • Asymptomatic presentation - Especially in older adults, where mild stenosis may go unnoticed. The body compensates until a critical threshold is reached.
  • Atypical chest pain - Instead of typical angina, patients may present with vague or non-specific chest discomfort.
  • Stroke - Rarely, embolisation from calcified aortic valve plaques may lead to a cerebrovascular event.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features or comorbidity can mask the underlying diagnosis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Aortic Stenosis"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Echocardiogram - Shows valve anatomy, degree of stenosis, and left ventricular hypertrophy. Peak gradient >40 mmHg or valve area <1 cm².
  • Targeted and monitoring: Chest X-ray - May show aortic valve calcification and evidence of left ventricular hypertrophy or pulmonary congestion; Cardiac catheterisation - Measures.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Aortic Stenosis"][/sc]
  • Hypertrophic cardiomyopathy - Differentiate from aortic stenosis using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.
  • Aortic regurgitation - Differentiate from aortic stenosis using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.
  • Mitral stenosis - Characterised by a mid-diastolic murmur and symptoms of pulmonary hypertension, with no delayed carotid.
  • Alternative serious diagnosis - Distinguishing history, examination, and targeted investigations do not support aortic stenosis.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Aortic Stenosis"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Assess physiological stability and urgent complications at presentation, and initiate early stabilisation where required.
  • Core care: Deliver condition-specific treatment for aortic stenosis, address underlying drivers, and involve appropriate specialist support.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Reassess symptom trajectory, objective markers, and treatment response over time to detect complications early.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Worsening symptoms, physiological deterioration, or failure to improve should trigger urgent reassessment for severe or alternative pathology in aortic stenosis.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Aortic Stenosis"][/sc]
  • Heart failure - Common, due to chronic pressure overload and left ventricular dysfunction.
  • Arrhythmias - Both atrial fibrillation and ventricular arrhythmias can occur due to left atrial enlargement and ventricular strain.
  • Sudden cardiac death - Particularly in patients with severe stenosis or unrecognised symptoms.
