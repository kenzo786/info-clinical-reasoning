Reactive Lymphadenopathy
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Reactive Lymphadenopathy"][/sc]
  • Reactive lymphadenopathy is a benign, reversible enlargement of lymph nodes due to infection, inflammation, or immune response.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise reactive features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.

  • Higher-risk or missed groups: Reactive Lymphadenopathy may be missed when presentation is atypical or obscured by comorbidity.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Reactive Lymphadenopathy"][/sc]
  • Antigen Exposure: The lymphatic system encounters antigens from infections or inflammation, triggering an immune response.
  • Lymphocyte Proliferation: Antigenic stimulation causes lymphocyte and macrophage proliferation within lymph nodes.
  • Node Enlargement: This cell proliferation and the accompanying inflammatory response cause the node to enlarge.
  • Resolution: Upon clearance of the antigen or infection, lymphocyte activity decreases, and the node gradually returns to normal size.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Reactive Lymphadenopathy"][/sc]
  • Acute Phase: Nodes enlarge rapidly in response to infection or inflammation, lasting days to a few weeks.
  • Resolution Phase: After infection resolves, lymphadenopathy typically subsides over weeks to months.
  • Chronic Phase: Rarely, low-level enlargement may persist if low-grade antigenic stimulation continues (e.g., in chronic infections).
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Reactive Lymphadenopathy"][/sc]
  • Ask about painless lymph node swelling - Most common symptom, caused by immune cell proliferation within the node. Nodes are often movable and soft.
  • Ask about fever - Frequently associated with underlying infections triggering lymphadenopathy.
  • Ask about malaise and fatigue - Generalised symptoms due to systemic immune response.
  • Ask about localised pain (rare) - If the node enlarges rapidly or if inflammation affects surrounding tissue, mild discomfort can occur.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Reactive Lymphadenopathy"][/sc]
  • Check enlarged, palpable lymph nodes - Typically soft, mobile, and non-tender nodes are noted on palpation.
  • Check systemic signs of infection - Fever, pharyngitis, or rash may be present if related to infections such as EBV or streptococcal pharyngitis.
  • Check absence of overlying skin changes - Reactive lymph nodes do not usually cause skin changes, distinguishing them from infected or malignant nodes.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Reactive Lymphadenopathy"][/sc]
  • Persistent, unexplained lymphadenopathy >6 weeks - Raises concern for malignancy or chronic infections like tuberculosis.
  • Firm, fixed nodes - Suggests malignancy, as cancerous nodes are typically non-mobile due to local tissue invasion.
  • Systemic symptoms (night sweats, weight loss) - Indicative of lymphoma or other systemic conditions, warranting further investigation.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Reactive Lymphadenopathy"][/sc]
  • Isolated abdominal lymphadenopathy - Less common and may mimic other intra-abdominal masses, requiring imaging to distinguish.
  • Generalised lymphadenopathy - Seen in systemic infections (e.g., HIV, EBV) but may be mistaken for haematological malignancies.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask reactive lymphadenopathy.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Reactive Lymphadenopathy"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Full blood count (FBC) - May show leukocytosis or atypical lymphocytes if viral infection is present; Serology - Specific for underlying.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Ultrasound - Used for characterisation; reactive nodes are usually oval and retain a fatty hilum, distinguishing them from malignant nodes; Biopsy.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Reactive Lymphadenopathy"][/sc]
  • Malignant lymphadenopathy - Nodes are usually firm, fixed, and associated with systemic symptoms like weight loss.
  • Infectious lymphadenitis - Typically involves painful, warm, tender nodes with overlying erythema.
  • Autoimmune lymphadenopathy (e.g., SLE) - Associated with systemic autoimmune symptoms (e.g., rash, joint pain).
  • Related alternative diagnosis - Distinguish from reactive lymphadenopathy using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Reactive Lymphadenopathy"][/sc]
  • Chronic lymphadenopathy - Rare, may persist after repeated infections due to fibrotic changes.
  • Missed malignancy - If reactive lymphadenopathy is misdiagnosed, especially in cases of atypical presentations.
