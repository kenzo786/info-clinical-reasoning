Severe Upper Gastrointestinal Bleeding (UGIB)
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Severe Upper Gastrointestinal Bleeding (UGIB)"][/sc]
  • Severe upper gastrointestinal bleeding (UGIB) refers to significant bleeding originating from a source proximal to the ligament of Treitz, including the oesophagus, stomach, and duodenum.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise upper features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.

Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Severe Upper Gastrointestinal Bleeding (UGIB)"][/sc]
  • UGIB can arise from multiple sources, including peptic ulcers, oesophageal varices, gastritis, and malignancies. Peptic ulcers result from an imbalance between gastric acid secretion and mucosal protection, often exacerbated by NSAID use or H. pylori infection. Varices are engorged veins due to portal hypertension, commonly seen in cirrhotic patients, which can rupture under pressure.
  • The bleeding leads to haemodynamic compromise, with hypovolaemia triggering compensatory mechanisms such as tachycardia, peripheral vasoconstriction, and a drop in urine output. If untreated, severe blood loss can cause shock and multi-organ failure.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Severe Upper Gastrointestinal Bleeding (UGIB)"][/sc]
  • Acute Phase: Rapid onset of haematemesis or melaena, often within hours to days. If untreated, rapid deterioration with hypotension, tachycardia, and shock may follow.
  • Stabilisation Phase: After initial resuscitation and management, bleeding may cease, but close monitoring is required to detect re-bleeding.
  • Recovery Phase: In stable patients, underlying causes (e.g., ulcers, varices) are treated. Full recovery may take days to weeks, with ongoing risk of recurrence.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Severe Upper Gastrointestinal Bleeding (UGIB)"][/sc]
  • Ask about haematemesis: Vomiting of bright red blood or "coffee-ground" material due to blood exposure to gastric acid. This suggests bleeding from the stomach or oesophagus.
  • Ask about melaena: Black, tarry stools caused by the digestion of blood, indicating bleeding has occurred proximal to the colon.
  • Ask about syncope or Dizziness: Caused by hypovolaemia, resulting from significant blood loss and reduced perfusion to the brain.
  • Ask about epigastric Pain: Common in peptic ulcer disease, due to irritation of the stomach lining or duodenum.
  • Ask about fatigue and Weakness: Resulting from anaemia due to chronic or severe blood loss.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Severe Upper Gastrointestinal Bleeding (UGIB)"][/sc]
  • Check hypotension: Low blood pressure due to hypovolaemia from blood loss.
  • Check tachycardia: A compensatory increase in heart rate in response to reduced circulating volume.
  • Check pallor: Pale skin due to reduced haemoglobin and perfusion, often a sign of significant blood loss.
  • Check cool, Clammy Skin: Due to vasoconstriction as part of the body’s compensatory response to shock.
  • Check reduced Urine Output: A sign of hypovolaemia and impaired renal perfusion.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Severe Upper Gastrointestinal Bleeding (UGIB)"][/sc]
  • Haemodynamic Instability: Signifies ongoing bleeding or shock, and may require urgent transfusion or surgery.
  • Altered Mental Status: Suggests severe hypovolaemia leading to poor cerebral perfusion or multi-organ failure.
  • Recurrent Bleeding After Stabilisation: May indicate a high-risk lesion such as a large peptic ulcer or oesophageal varices.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Severe Upper Gastrointestinal Bleeding (UGIB)"][/sc]
  • Occult GI Bleeding: Some patients, especially the elderly, may present with iron-deficiency anaemia or positive faecal occult blood testing without overt haematemesis or melaena.
  • Chronic Fatigue or Dyspnoea: Chronic UGIB may present subtly as fatigue or shortness of breath due to progressive anaemia.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask severe upper gastrointestinal bleeding (ugib).
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Severe Upper Gastrointestinal Bleeding (UGIB)"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Full Blood Count (FBC): Reduced haemoglobin and haematocrit levels indicating blood loss; Endoscopy: Gold standard for diagnosing the source of bleeding.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Targeted investigations to confirm diagnosis, assess complications, and monitor response in severe upper gastrointestinal bleeding (ugib).

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Severe Upper Gastrointestinal Bleeding (UGIB)"][/sc]
  • Oesophageal Varices - Common in patients with cirrhosis; presents with massive, painless haematemesis.
  • Mallory-Weiss Tear - Vomiting followed by sudden haematemesis, usually less severe than peptic ulcer bleeding.
  • Gastric Cancer - Chronic blood loss with weight loss and early satiety, usually not associated with haemodynamic.
  • Related alternative diagnosis - Distinguish from severe upper gastrointestinal bleeding (ugib) using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Severe Upper Gastrointestinal Bleeding (UGIB)"][/sc]
  • Haemodynamic collapse: Ongoing blood loss can lead to shock, end-organ hypoperfusion, and emergency critical care needs.
  • Rebleeding: Early rebleeding after initial control is associated with higher morbidity and mortality risk.
  • Aspiration and cardiorespiratory complications: Massive haematemesis can precipitate aspiration, hypoxia, and secondary pneumonia.
