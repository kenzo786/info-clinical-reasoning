Measles
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Measles"][/sc]
  • Measles is an acute, highly contagious viral illness caused by the measles virus, a paramyxovirus.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise measles features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Measles"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Measles"][/sc]
  • UK GP population: Measles cases are uncommon in the UK due to high vaccination coverage, but outbreaks still occur, especially among unvaccinated groups.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Measles"][/sc]
  • The measles virus infects the respiratory epithelium and initially multiplies locally.
  • The virus then spreads to lymphatic tissue, where it further replicates and disseminates through the bloodstream, leading to a widespread viral invasion.
  • This viral spread triggers a robust immune response, leading to the release of cytokines that cause systemic symptoms (e.g., fever, malaise) and the characteristic rash.
  • Immunosuppression occurs, making patients more susceptible to secondary infections.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Measles"][/sc]
  • Incubation period: 10–14 days from exposure to symptom onset.
  • Prodromal phase: 3–4 days, characterised by fever, cough, coryza, and conjunctivitis.
  • Rash phase: Occurs approximately 4 days after prodrome onset, lasting 5–6 days, typically starting on the face and spreading downwards.
  • Recovery phase: After the rash fades, patients usually experience resolution of symptoms over 1–2 weeks, though immunosuppression can persist for several weeks.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Measles"][/sc]
  • Ask about fever: Due to systemic inflammatory response, often one of the earliest signs.
  • Ask about cough: Caused by respiratory tract involvement and irritation.
  • Ask about coryza (nasal congestion and discharge): Reflects viral infection and inflammation of nasal mucosa.
  • Ask about conjunctivitis: Viral spread to conjunctiva, causing redness and discomfort in the eyes.
  • Ask about rash: Appears 3-4 days after initial symptoms, beginning at the face and spreading downwards; it is due to the immune response to the virus.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Measles"][/sc]
  • Check koplik spots: Small white lesions on the buccal mucosa, pathognomonic for measles and caused by viral infection of oral epithelium.
  • Check maculopapular rash: Begins on the face and spreads downwards, due to immune response in capillaries.
  • Check conjunctival injection: Redness and inflammation of the conjunctiva due to direct viral invasion.
  • Check generalised lymphadenopathy: Immune response involving lymphoid tissue, often palpable in cervical and occipital regions.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Measles"][/sc]
  • High fever (>40°C): May indicate severe infection and increased risk for complications.
  • Altered mental status: Could indicate encephalitis, a serious complication of measles.
  • Difficulty breathing or chest pain: May signify secondary bacterial pneumonia, a common complication.
  • Severe abdominal pain: Can be associated with acute appendicitis or other gastrointestinal complications.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Measles"][/sc]
  • Atypical rash distribution: In immunocompromised patients, the rash may be less pronounced or absent.
  • Mild or absent prodromal symptoms: Often seen in partially vaccinated individuals or those with some immunity.
  • Prolonged course of illness: More common in patients with immunodeficiency, reflecting impaired immune response.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask measles.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Measles"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Serology: IgM antibodies detectable within 3 days of rash onset; confirms acute infection.
  • Targeted and monitoring: RT-PCR: Detects measles RNA in blood, urine, or respiratory specimens, particularly useful in early infection; Full blood count (FBC): Often reveals.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Measles"][/sc]
  • Rubella - Distinguish from measles using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.
  • Scarlet fever - Typically associated with sore throat and a fine, sandpaper-like rash, not descending rash distribution.
  • Roseola - Rash appears after a sudden high fever, typically in infants, and is usually confined.
  • Kawasaki disease - Has persistent high fever and erythema of palms and soles, often without a cough.

  • Immediate priorities: Assess severity, physiological stability, and immediate complications.
  • Core care: Deliver condition-specific management for measles, treat underlying drivers, and coordinate appropriate specialist input.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Reassess symptom trajectory, examination findings, and objective markers to confirm response in measles.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Escalate urgently for clinical deterioration, evolving red flags, or failure to improve in suspected measles.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Measles"][/sc]
  • Encephalitis: Rare but serious complication leading to neurological sequelae in some cases.
  • Pneumonia: Most common cause of death in measles, particularly among malnourished and immunocompromised individuals.
  • Otitis media: Middle ear infection that can result in hearing loss if not treated.
  • Subacute sclerosing panencephalitis (SSPE): Rare, late-onset complication with progressive neurological deterioration, typically occurring years after infection.
