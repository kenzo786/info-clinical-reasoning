Lymphoedema
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Lymphoedema"][/sc]
  • Lymphoedema is a chronic condition characterised by abnormal accumulation of protein-rich interstitial fluid, usually in the limbs, due to impaired lymphatic drainage.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise lymphoedema features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.

Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Lymphoedema"][/sc]
  • Lymphoedema develops due to a failure of the lymphatic system to drain interstitial fluid, leading to fluid accumulation in the tissues.
  • Step 1: Impaired lymphatic drainage leads to accumulation of protein-rich fluid in the interstitial space.
  • Step 2: Chronic stasis of this fluid triggers inflammation and activation of fibroblasts, resulting in tissue fibrosis and scarring.
  • Step 3: Long-term lymphatic stasis can lead to adipose tissue deposition and further structural changes in the skin and subcutaneous tissues, including hyperkeratosis and papillomatosis.
  • Step 4: The protein-rich fluid creates a fertile environment for bacterial growth, increasing susceptibility to infections such as cellulitis.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Lymphoedema"][/sc]
  • Early stage: Intermittent swelling, often resolving with rest and elevation. Skin and tissues remain soft.
  • Intermediate stage: Persistent swelling, tissue fibrosis begins, pitting oedema may reduce.
  • Late stage: Severe fibrosis and skin changes, such as hyperkeratosis and papillomatosis. Swelling becomes non-pitting and irreversible.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Lymphoedema"][/sc]
  • Ask about swelling of the affected limb(s) - Typically starts intermittently but progresses to persistent swelling. Pathophysiology: Impaired lymphatic drainage leads to fluid accumulation.
  • Ask about heaviness or discomfort - The limb feels heavy or tight due to excess fluid accumulation and tissue fibrosis.
  • Ask about reduced mobility or flexibility - Swelling and fibrosis may limit joint movement, particularly in severe or chronic cases.
  • Ask about skin tightness - Stretching of the skin due to oedema leads to a sensation of tightness.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Lymphoedema"][/sc]
  • Check non-pitting oedema - Swelling that does not indent when pressure is applied, due to chronic tissue fibrosis.
  • Check skin thickening (hyperkeratosis) - Prolonged lymphoedema leads to fibrosis and structural skin changes.
  • Check stemmer’s sign - Inability to pinch the skin on the dorsal surface of the toes or fingers, indicating lymphoedema.
  • Check recurrent cellulitis - Frequent skin infections due to stagnant protein-rich fluid, which promotes bacterial growth.
  • Check papillomatosis - Wart-like skin projections due to chronic inflammation and lymphatic stasis.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Lymphoedema"][/sc]
  • Sudden onset of swelling - May indicate a deep vein thrombosis (DVT) or malignancy. Requires urgent investigation.
  • Recurrent cellulitis - Can lead to severe systemic infection or chronic lymphatic damage.
  • Rapid worsening of oedema - Could signify tumour recurrence in cancer patients or a secondary obstruction.
  • Pain - Lymphoedema is typically painless; new or worsening pain suggests infection or malignancy.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Lymphoedema"][/sc]
  • Swelling in unusual locations - Lymphoedema typically affects the limbs but may present in the face, genitalia, or abdomen, particularly in congenital cases.
  • Asymmetrical swelling - In primary lymphoedema, one limb may be affected years before the other, leading to diagnostic delays.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask lymphoedema.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Lymphoedema"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Doppler ultrasound - To exclude venous thrombosis and assess venous insufficiency. Findings: Absence of DVT or venous obstruction.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Lymphoscintigraphy - Nuclear medicine imaging that evaluates lymphatic drainage. Findings: Delayed or absent lymphatic uptake and transport; Bioimpedance spectroscopy - Measures.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Lymphoedema"][/sc]
  • Chronic venous insufficiency - Swelling is typically pitting, often improves with elevation, and associated with varicose veins. Lymphoedema.
  • Deep vein thrombosis (DVT) - Usually causes acute swelling with pain and erythema. Doppler ultrasound can confirm the presence.
  • Lipodema - Distinguish from lymphoedema using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Lymphoedema"][/sc]
  • Recurrent cellulitis - Inflammation and infection of the skin due to stagnant protein-rich lymph fluid, leading to potential sepsis.
  • Lymphangiosarcoma - Rare but aggressive cancer associated with long-standing lymphoedema. Presents as purplish skin lesions and has a poor prognosis.
  • Fibrosis and immobility - Advanced lymphoedema can cause tissue fibrosis, resulting in significant functional impairment.
