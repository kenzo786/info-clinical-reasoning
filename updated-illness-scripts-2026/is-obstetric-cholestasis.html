Obstetric Cholestasis
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Obstetric Cholestasis"][/sc]
  • Obstetric cholestasis, also known as intrahepatic cholestasis of pregnancy (ICP), is a liver condition that occurs during pregnancy, typically after 24 weeks' gestation.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise obstetric features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Obstetric Cholestasis"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Obstetric Cholestasis"][/sc]
  • In UK hospitals, ICP is a common cause of admission in the third trimester for pruritus and abnormal liver function tests (LFTs).
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Obstetric Cholestasis"][/sc]
  • The condition arises due to genetic and hormonal factors that disrupt bile flow in the liver during pregnancy. Elevated oestrogen levels in pregnancy increase the liver’s susceptibility to cholestasis, particularly in women with specific genetic mutations (e.g., ABCB4, ABCB11 genes).
  • As bile acids accumulate in the liver and bloodstream, they stimulate nerve endings, leading to pruritus. Elevated bile acids also cross the placenta, posing a risk to the fetus by affecting fetal heart function and increasing the risk of preterm birth and stillbirth.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Obstetric Cholestasis"][/sc]
  • Obstetric cholestasis typically develops in the second or third trimester, usually after 24 weeks of gestation, and often resolves within a few days to weeks postpartum as bile acid levels normalise.
  • If untreated, bile acid levels continue to rise, particularly in the later weeks of pregnancy, leading to increased risks of fetal complications, including preterm birth and fetal distress.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Obstetric Cholestasis"][/sc]
  • Ask about pruritus (itching) - Typically the earliest and most prominent symptom, often involving the palms and soles and worse at night. It results from, with associated clinical features.
  • Ask about dark urine - Caused by increased bile pigments excreted in the urine due to impaired bile flow.
  • Ask about pale stools - Result from decreased bile reaching the intestines, reducing stercobilin production, which gives stool its brown colour.
  • Ask about fatigue - Common due to systemic effects of cholestasis and disruption in metabolism.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Obstetric Cholestasis"][/sc]
  • Check excoriations - Scratches due to intense itching, particularly on the arms and legs.
  • Check jaundice (rare) - Occurs in severe cases with significant elevation of bile acids and bilirubin.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Obstetric Cholestasis"][/sc]
  • Severe pruritus - Significant itching, particularly if worsens suddenly, can indicate rapidly rising bile acids, increasing fetal risk.
  • Abdominal pain or discomfort - May signify liver dysfunction or associated conditions and warrants further assessment.
  • Preterm labour - Common in ICP and associated with elevated bile acids; monitoring is required for early intervention.
  • Jaundice - A rare finding that may indicate more severe hepatic involvement and higher maternal risk.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Obstetric Cholestasis"][/sc]
  • Mild pruritus without clear cholestasis markers - Some patients may have itching with initially normal bile acid levels, requiring repeat testing as symptoms progress.
  • Early-onset pruritus - Though rare, itching before 24 weeks’ gestation can occur and should prompt consideration of other causes of liver dysfunction.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask obstetric cholestasis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Obstetric Cholestasis"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Serum bile acids - Elevated (>10 µmol/L), with levels >40 µmol/L associated with increased fetal risk. High bile acids confirm diagnosis.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Targeted investigations to confirm diagnosis, assess complications, and monitor response in obstetric cholestasis.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Obstetric Cholestasis"][/sc]
  • Acute fatty liver of pregnancy - Rapid onset with systemic symptoms like vomiting, malaise, and jaundice; bile acids not typically.
  • HELLP syndrome - Presents with epigastric pain, hypertension, and abnormal LFTs but without isolated pruritus or bile.
  • PUPPP (pruritic urticarial papules and plaques of pregnancy) - Causes pruritus with characteristic rash, no bile acid elevation.
  • Related alternative diagnosis - Distinguish from obstetric cholestasis using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Obstetric Cholestasis"][/sc]
  • Preterm labour - Occurs in a significant proportion of cases with high bile acid levels, increasing neonatal risk.
  • Stillbirth - Associated with bile acid levels >100 µmol/L, particularly beyond 37 weeks’ gestation.
  • Postpartum haemorrhage - Secondary to coagulopathy due to vitamin K deficiency, a recognised risk in cholestasis.
