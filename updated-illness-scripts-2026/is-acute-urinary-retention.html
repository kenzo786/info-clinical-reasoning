Acute Urinary Retention (AUR)
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Acute Urinary Retention (AUR)"][/sc]
  • Acute urinary retention (AUR) is a sudden inability to voluntarily void urine, often leading to painful bladder distension and a rapid onset of symptoms.
  • UKMLA essentials: painful inability to void, AKI or sepsis features, urgent bladder decompression.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Acute Urinary Retention (AUR)"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Acute Urinary Retention (AUR)"][/sc]
  • Acute Urinary Retention (AUR) is encountered across acute and specialist care, with burden shaped by age, comorbidity, and exposure context.
  • Risk increases in patients with relevant predisposing disease, treatment exposures, or delayed access to care.
  • Missed or late recognition is more likely when presentations are atypical or overlap with common alternative diagnoses.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Acute Urinary Retention (AUR)"][/sc]
  • AUR results from either mechanical or functional obstruction of the urinary outflow tract. In men, the most common cause is bladder outlet obstruction due to BPH, where enlarged prostate tissue compresses the urethra.
  • Increased smooth muscle tone in the prostate and bladder neck (mediated by alpha-adrenergic receptors) exacerbates this compression, causing a sudden inability to void.
  • Other causes include neurogenic bladder dysfunction (impaired detrusor muscle contraction), medications (anticholinergics, opioids), or urethral strictures.
  • Bladder overdistension can lead to ischemia of the bladder wall, further reducing detrusor function.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Acute Urinary Retention (AUR)"][/sc]
  • Acute phase: Sudden onset of an inability to void with severe suprapubic pain and discomfort. Patients may present within hours or days of symptom onset.
  • Post-treatment phase: Immediate relief of symptoms typically follows bladder decompression, though some patients may experience ongoing voiding dysfunction.
  • Long-term phase: Recurrence is common if the underlying cause (e.g., BPH) is not treated, and chronic urinary retention may develop in some cases.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Acute Urinary Retention (AUR)"][/sc]
  • Ask about Inability to urinate - The hallmark symptom, caused by bladder outlet obstruction or detrusor dysfunction.
  • Ask about Severe suprapubic pain - Pain arises from bladder overdistension due to retained urine.
  • Ask about Urgency with no ability to void - The patient feels an overwhelming need to urinate, but cannot pass urine due to obstruction.
  • Ask about Distended bladder - Due to the accumulation of urine without voiding, causing abdominal discomfort and a palpable bladder.
  • Ask about Nausea or vomiting - A secondary effect of severe pain or bladder overdistension.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Acute Urinary Retention (AUR)"][/sc]
  • Check Palpable distended bladder - A firm, tender mass in the suprapubic region, reflecting urinary retention.
  • Check Suprapubic tenderness - Tenderness due to bladder overdistension or ischemia of the bladder wall.
  • Check Hypertension/tachycardia - A potential response to pain and discomfort.
  • Check Post-void residual urine - Measured by bladder scan or catheterisation, confirming retention.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Acute Urinary Retention (AUR)"][/sc]
  • Acute kidney injury (AKI) - Indicative of bilateral urinary tract obstruction or prolonged retention, leading to renal failure.
  • Haematuria - Could suggest a bladder stone, malignancy, or severe bladder wall damage due to overdistension.
  • Sepsis or fever - May indicate urinary tract infection (UTI) or pyelonephritis secondary to retention.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Acute Urinary Retention (AUR)"][/sc]
  • Painless urinary retention: May occur in patients with chronic retention or neurogenic bladder, leading to gradual bladder distension without acute pain.
  • Absence of a palpable bladder: Occurs in obese patients or those with a high bladder position, masking the typical finding of bladder distension.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Acute Urinary Retention (AUR)"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Bladder scan to confirm significant retained urine and support urgent decompression; bedside observations and abdominal examination for pain, distension, and instability.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Urine dipstick and culture when infection is suspected; renal profile to assess post-renal AKI; renal tract ultrasound when obstruction level, hydronephrosis, stones, or structural pathology is a concern.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Acute Urinary Retention (AUR)"][/sc]
  • A 70-year-old male presents with a 24-hour history of severe suprapubic pain, inability to pass urine, and a palpable suprapubic mass. He has a history of benign prostatic hyperplasia.
  • Older adult with painful inability to pass urine, suprapubic distension, and large post-void residual, consistent with acute urinary retention.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Acute Urinary Retention (AUR)"][/sc]
  • Benign prostatic hyperplasia (BPH) - Common cause of AUR, with gradual onset and associated lower urinary tract symptoms (LUTS).
  • Bladder stones - Presents with haematuria and pain, typically episodic and associated with movement.
  • Urethral stricture - Leads to obstructed flow, often associated with a history of trauma, infection, or instrumentation.
  • Neurogenic bladder - Associated with neurological conditions (e.g., spinal cord injury), presenting with painless retention.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Acute Urinary Retention (AUR)"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Immediate bladder decompression: Insert a urethral or suprapubic catheter to relieve the obstruction and prevent further bladder damage.
  • Core care: Treat underlying cause: For BPH-related AUR, alpha-blockers (e.g., tamsulosin) may facilitate voiding. Definitive treatment may involve prostate surgery (e.g., TURP).
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Address complications: Manage any concurrent infection with antibiotics and monitor renal function for signs of AKI.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Escalate urgently for failed catheterisation, worsening AKI, sepsis, haematuria with clots, or persistent pain after decompression.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Acute Urinary Retention (AUR)"][/sc]
  • Acute kidney injury (AKI): Due to prolonged obstruction leading to increased pressure in the urinary tract and subsequent renal impairment.
  • Bladder rupture: Rare but can occur with excessive overdistension, leading to peritonitis and a surgical emergency.
  • Chronic urinary retention: Recurrent episodes of AUR may cause permanent bladder dysfunction if untreated.
