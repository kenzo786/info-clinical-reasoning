Meningitis
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Meningitis"][/sc]
  • Meningitis is the inflammation of the meninges, the protective membranes covering the brain and spinal cord, typically caused by bacterial, viral, or fungal infections.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise meningitis features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Meningitis"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Meningitis"][/sc]
  • Risk factors: Risk factors for meningitis include relevant predisposition, comorbidity, and exposure history.
  • Higher-risk or missed groups: Meningitis may be missed when presentation is atypical or obscured by comorbidity.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Meningitis"][/sc]
  • Meningitis occurs when infectious organisms breach the blood-brain barrier and invade the cerebrospinal fluid (CSF). This triggers an inflammatory response in the meninges, leading to increased intracranial pressure (ICP), cerebral oedema, and impaired CSF flow. In bacterial meningitis, the release of endotoxins exacerbates inflammation, causing vasculitis and potentially leading to cerebral infarction.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Meningitis"][/sc]
  • Acute phase: Symptoms such as headache, fever, and neck stiffness develop rapidly over 24-48 hours.
  • Subacute phase: In viral meningitis, symptoms may progress more slowly over several days.
  • Chronic phase: Rare in typical meningitis cases, but fungal or tuberculous meningitis can have a prolonged course over weeks to months.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Meningitis"][/sc]
  • Ask about fever - Caused by systemic inflammation and cytokine release due to infection.
  • Ask about headache - Due to increased intracranial pressure from meningeal inflammation.
  • Ask about neck stiffness (nuchal rigidity) - Results from irritation of the meninges, particularly the spinal meninges.
  • Ask about photophobia - Sensitivity to light is common and may be due to irritation of the optic nerve by meningeal inflammation.
  • Ask about altered mental status - Ranges from confusion to coma, often due to increased intracranial pressure or direct inflammatory damage to brain tissue.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Meningitis"][/sc]
  • Check kernig's sign - Resistance to passive knee extension when the hip is flexed, caused by meningeal irritation.
  • Check brudzinski's sign - Involuntary lifting of the legs when the neck is flexed, due to meningeal irritation.
  • Check focal neurological deficits - May indicate localised brain involvement or complications such as abscesses or infarction.
  • Check non-blanching rash - Seen in meningococcal meningitis, this is caused by disseminated intravascular coagulation (DIC) and sepsis.
  • Check raised intracranial pressure (papilloedema) - Increased pressure from cerebral oedema may cause optic disc swelling, visible on fundoscopy.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Meningitis"][/sc]
  • Severe headache and rapid progression of symptoms - Indicates raised intracranial pressure and a potential for rapid deterioration.
  • Seizures - May suggest cerebral involvement or severe brain inflammation, warranting urgent intervention.
  • Non-blanching purpuric rash - Suggestive of meningococcal septicaemia, indicating a medical emergency.
  • Altered consciousness or coma - Signifies advanced disease and a risk of brain herniation or death.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Meningitis"][/sc]
  • Immunocompromised patients - May present with subtle symptoms like mild fever and confusion, without the classic triad of fever, headache, and neck stiffness.
  • Neonates and infants - Present with non-specific symptoms such as poor feeding, irritability, or lethargy, often without neck stiffness or photophobia.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask meningitis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Meningitis"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Lumbar puncture (LP) - CSF analysis typically shows high white cell count, raised protein, and low glucose in bacterial meningitis. Viral.
  • Targeted and monitoring: CT head - Performed prior to LP if raised ICP is suspected to avoid brain herniation. Findings may include cerebral oedema.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Meningitis"][/sc]
  • Encephalitis - Typically presents with more pronounced neurological deficits such as seizures, behavioural changes, and motor.
  • Subarachnoid haemorrhage (SAH) - Acute onset "thunderclap" headache with possible focal neurological signs. CT head may show blood.
  • Brain abscess - May present with focal neurological deficits, fever, and headache. CT or MRI will show.
  • Related alternative diagnosis - Distinguish from meningitis using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.

  • Immediate priorities: Assess severity, physiological stability, and immediate complications.
  • Core care: Deliver condition-specific management for meningitis, treat underlying drivers, and coordinate appropriate specialist input.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Reassess symptom trajectory, examination findings, and objective markers to confirm response in meningitis.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Escalate urgently for clinical deterioration, evolving red flags, or failure to improve in suspected meningitis.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Meningitis"][/sc]
  • Sepsis - Seen in meningococcal meningitis, this can lead to multi-organ failure and is associated with a high mortality rate.
  • Hydrocephalus - Increased CSF production or decreased absorption can lead to chronic hydrocephalus, requiring shunt placement.
  • Deafness - Inflammation of the inner ear can result in permanent hearing loss, especially in bacterial cases.
  • Seizures and long-term neurological deficits - Ongoing cerebral irritation or infarction can cause epilepsy, motor, or cognitive deficits.
