Metastatic Disease
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Metastatic Disease"][/sc]
  • Metastatic disease refers to the spread of malignant tumour cells from a primary cancer site to distant organs or tissues.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise metastatic features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Metastatic Disease"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Metastatic Disease"][/sc]
  • Globally, metastatic disease affects millions of patients, particularly in advanced-stage cancers.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Metastatic Disease"][/sc]
  • Metastasis occurs in several stages:
  • Local Invasion - Cancer cells invade surrounding tissues, breaking through the basement membrane.
  • Intravasation - Tumour cells enter the bloodstream or lymphatic system.
  • Circulation - Circulating tumour cells (CTCs) survive in the blood or lymph, evading immune detection.
  • Extravasation - Tumour cells exit the vessels and invade distant tissues.
  • Colonisation - Cells proliferate and establish secondary tumours in the new tissue.
  • Angiogenesis - The metastatic tumour forms its own blood supply, promoting further growth.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Metastatic Disease"][/sc]
  • Early Stage - Often asymptomatic as cancer cells begin to metastasise.
  • Mid Stage - Gradual onset of symptoms depending on metastatic location (e.g., bone pain, neurological deficits).
  • Late Stage - Significant tumour burden with organ dysfunction, worsening systemic symptoms, and high mortality risk.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Metastatic Disease"][/sc]
  • Ask about bone Pain - Common in bone metastases (e.g., from breast or prostate cancer), due to tumour invasion causing localised destruction and inflammation.
  • Ask about fatigue - A frequent systemic symptom caused by cancer-related anaemia, inflammatory cytokines, and metabolic demands of the tumour.
  • Ask about weight Loss - Cachexia results from metabolic alterations induced by tumour growth, systemic inflammation, and loss of muscle mass.
  • Ask about shortness of Breath - May result from lung metastases, pleural effusion, or tumour burden affecting respiratory function.
  • Ask about neurological Deficits - Symptoms like headaches, seizures, or motor deficits occur in brain metastases due to increased intracranial pressure or focal damage.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Metastatic Disease"][/sc]
  • Check palpable Masses - Metastases may present as detectable masses (e.g., lymph nodes, liver enlargement) due to tumour growth.
  • Check pathological Fractures - Bone metastases weaken bones, increasing the risk of fractures with minimal trauma.
  • Check neurological Signs - Focal deficits, such as hemiparesis or cranial nerve palsies, suggest brain or spinal metastasis.
  • Check hepatomegaly - Liver metastases can cause an enlarged, palpable liver due to tumour infiltration.
  • Check pleural Effusion - Lung metastases may lead to fluid accumulation in the pleural space, detectable on auscultation or percussion.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Metastatic Disease"][/sc]
  • Severe, Intractable Pain - Suggests extensive bone involvement or nerve compression, often indicating advanced disease.
  • Unexplained Weight Loss - May be a sign of widespread metastatic burden or cachexia, often associated with poor prognosis.
  • Neurological Deficits - Sudden-onset neurological symptoms (e.g., weakness, seizures) can indicate brain metastases or spinal cord compression, requiring urgent intervention.
  • Jaundice - Suggests liver metastases or biliary obstruction due to tumour involvement, associated with significant morbidity.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Metastatic Disease"][/sc]
  • Silent Liver Metastases - May remain asymptomatic until significant liver involvement, presenting late with jaundice or hepatic failure.
  • Spinal Cord Compression - Presents with subtle back pain before progressing to neurological deficits such as leg weakness or bladder dysfunction.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask metastatic disease.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Metastatic Disease"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: CT Scan - Commonly used for imaging metastases in various organs. Findings include masses, lytic lesions (bone), or effusions (lungs/pleura); PET.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Bone Scan - Detects areas of increased bone turnover, common in bone metastases from cancers such as prostate and breast; Liver.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Metastatic Disease"][/sc]
  • Primary Bone Tumour - Typically presents with localised pain but often lacks systemic symptoms seen in metastatic disease.
  • Multiple Myeloma - Bone pain, anaemia, and renal dysfunction, but characterised by "punched-out" bone lesions on imaging.
  • Osteoporosis with Compression Fractures - May cause pain but is associated with normal systemic markers, unlike the elevated tumour.
  • Related alternative diagnosis - Distinguish from metastatic disease using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Metastatic Disease"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Assess severity, physiological stability, and immediate complications.
  • Core care: Deliver condition-specific management for metastatic disease, treat underlying drivers, and coordinate appropriate specialist input.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Reassess symptom trajectory, examination findings, and objective markers to confirm response in metastatic disease.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Escalate urgently for clinical deterioration, evolving red flags, or failure to improve in suspected metastatic disease.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Metastatic Disease"][/sc]
  • Spinal Cord Compression - Can result from vertebral metastases, causing paralysis if untreated.
  • Pathological Fractures - Increased fracture risk in bone metastases, particularly in weight-bearing bones like the femur.
  • Organ Failure - Metastases to organs like the liver or lungs can lead to organ dysfunction or failure.
  • Cachexia - Severe weight loss and muscle wasting due to systemic effects of the tumour, contributing to poor prognosis.
