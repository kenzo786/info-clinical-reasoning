Wilson's Disease
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Wilson's Disease"][/sc]
  • Wilson's disease is a rare autosomal recessive disorder of copper metabolism that leads to the toxic accumulation of copper in various organs, most notably the liver and brain.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise wilson features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Wilson's Disease"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Wilson's Disease"][/sc]
  • It is often underdiagnosed due to the wide range of presenting symptoms, and many cases are not recognised until adolescence or early adulthood.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Wilson's Disease"][/sc]
  • Wilson’s disease is caused by mutations in the ATP7B gene located on chromosome 13, which is responsible for encoding a copper-transporting P-type ATPase. This enzyme is involved in the excretion of copper into bile and its incorporation into ceruloplasmin.
  • Without proper functioning of ATP7B, copper accumulates in hepatocytes. Initially, copper is stored in the liver, leading to hepatocellular damage and cirrhosis.
  • As copper storage exceeds hepatic capacity, it spills over into the bloodstream and deposits in other organs, such as the brain (primarily the basal ganglia), kidneys, and corneas, causing systemic symptoms.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Wilson's Disease"][/sc]
  • The onset of Wilson's disease usually occurs between the ages of 5 and 35, although cases can present as early as age 3 or in late adulthood.
  • The hepatic phase may begin in childhood with asymptomatic liver dysfunction, progressing to cirrhosis or acute liver failure if untreated.
  • Neurological symptoms often appear in late adolescence or adulthood, typically years after hepatic involvement, and can be insidious or rapidly progressive.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Wilson's Disease"][/sc]
  • Ask about fatigue - Due to hepatic dysfunction and chronic inflammation affecting metabolic processes.
  • Ask about jaundice - Resulting from liver damage causing an impaired ability to process bilirubin.
  • Ask about tremor - Often seen in neurological involvement due to copper deposition in the basal ganglia.
  • Ask about personality changes - Psychiatric symptoms can manifest as irritability, depression, or anxiety, linked to brain involvement, particularly in the basal ganglia and frontal lobe.
  • Ask about abdominal pain - May occur due to hepatomegaly, cirrhosis, or gallstones.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Wilson's Disease"][/sc]
  • Check kayser-Fleischer rings - Brownish-green rings around the cornea caused by copper deposition in Descemet’s membrane.
  • Check hepatomegaly - Enlargement of the liver due to copper accumulation and liver dysfunction.
  • Check ataxia - A result of damage to the cerebellum or basal ganglia, leading to uncoordinated movements.
  • Check slurred speech (dysarthria) - Occurs due to neurological damage affecting motor control of speech.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Wilson's Disease"][/sc]
  • Acute liver failure - Can be life-threatening; signifies advanced hepatic involvement.
  • Severe neuropsychiatric symptoms - Sudden onset or rapid progression of neurological signs can indicate brain damage from copper accumulation.
  • Haemolytic anaemia - A possible consequence of copper-induced red blood cell destruction.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Wilson's Disease"][/sc]
  • Asymptomatic liver disease - Some patients may present with subclinical liver disease identified only through abnormal liver function tests.
  • Isolated psychiatric symptoms - These may mimic primary psychiatric disorders such as depression or schizophrenia without evident hepatic or neurological signs.
  • Renal tubular acidosis - An uncommon presentation with features of metabolic acidosis and electrolyte imbalances.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask wilson's disease.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Wilson's Disease"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Focused bedside assessment and first-line testing to assess severity and immediate risk in suspected wilson's disease.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Liver biopsy with copper quantification - Elevated hepatic copper content (>250 µg/g dry weight); Genetic testing - Identification of ATP7B gene.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Wilson's Disease"][/sc]
  • Autoimmune hepatitis - Often presents with jaundice and elevated liver enzymes but lacks Kayser-Fleischer rings and has.
  • Hepatitis B/C - Viral hepatitis can mimic the hepatic manifestations but is confirmed with specific viral serologies.
  • Huntington's disease - Distinguish from wilson's disease using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Wilson's Disease"][/sc]
  • Cirrhosis - Results from chronic liver damage, which may progress to hepatocellular carcinoma.
  • Neurological deterioration - Progressive brain damage can lead to permanent movement disorders or cognitive decline.
  • Acute liver failure - Life-threatening if not managed promptly, often requiring liver transplantation.
