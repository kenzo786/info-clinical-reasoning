Hypoglycaemia
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Hypoglycaemia"][/sc]
  • Hypoglycaemia is defined as a blood glucose level below 4.0 mmol/L, particularly in individuals with diabetes.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise hypoglycaemia features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.

  • Higher-risk or missed groups: Hypoglycaemia may be missed when presentation is atypical or obscured by comorbidity.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Hypoglycaemia"][/sc]
  • The primary mechanism in hypoglycaemia is an excessive reduction in blood glucose levels. Glucose is essential for brain function, and the brain depends on a continuous glucose supply. When blood glucose drops, counter-regulatory hormones (glucagon, adrenaline, cortisol, and growth hormone) are released to raise glucose levels. In patients with diabetes, these regulatory responses may be blunted, particularly with long-term insulin therapy or sulfonylureas, making hypoglycaemia more likely. Hypoglycaemia can also result from other conditions like insulinoma, adrenal insufficiency, or severe liver disease.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Hypoglycaemia"][/sc]
  • Hypoglycaemia can occur acutely within minutes to hours depending on its cause. It may present during periods of fasting, physical exertion, or following insulin/medication administration. Mild hypoglycaemia may resolve with oral glucose intake within 15 minutes, but severe cases can lead to prolonged confusion, coma, or seizures if untreated.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Hypoglycaemia"][/sc]
  • Ask about shakiness - Caused by adrenergic stimulation as the body attempts to counteract the low blood glucose.
  • Ask about sweating - Excessive sweating is triggered by sympathetic nervous system activation in response to low glucose levels.
  • Ask about hunger - The body increases hunger signals due to glucose depletion in an attempt to replenish energy stores.
  • Ask about fatigue/Weakness - Occurs as the body’s cells, especially in muscles and the brain, experience energy deprivation due to low glucose.
  • Ask about confusion - Reflects neuroglycopenia, where insufficient glucose supply to the brain impairs cognitive function.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Hypoglycaemia"][/sc]
  • Check tachycardia - Due to the adrenergic response aimed at mobilising glucose stores and maintaining perfusion.
  • Check diaphoresis - Profuse sweating is a sign of the sympathetic nervous system’s attempt to raise blood glucose levels.
  • Check altered mental status - Ranging from confusion to coma, this reflects neuroglycopenia, where the brain lacks sufficient glucose to function normally.
  • Check seizures - In severe hypoglycaemia, the brain's energy deprivation can trigger seizures.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Hypoglycaemia"][/sc]
  • Loss of consciousness - Indicates severe neuroglycopenia and the risk of permanent brain damage if not corrected urgently.
  • Seizures - Sign of severe hypoglycaemia that requires immediate medical intervention to prevent irreversible damage.
  • Prolonged confusion or coma - Suggests severe and prolonged hypoglycaemia with a potential risk of permanent neurological damage.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Hypoglycaemia"][/sc]
  • In elderly patients, hypoglycaemia may present primarily with confusion, without the typical adrenergic symptoms such as sweating or shaking. This can lead to missed diagnosis, as cognitive decline may be misattributed to dementia.
  • Patients on beta-blockers may not exhibit typical signs like tachycardia or palpitations due to inhibition of the adrenergic response.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Hypoglycaemia"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Capillary blood glucose - Typically <4.0 mmol/L. Confirms hypoglycaemia and helps in acute management.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Targeted investigations to confirm diagnosis, assess complications, and monitor response in hypoglycaemia.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Hypoglycaemia"][/sc]
  • Insulinoma - Presents with recurrent fasting hypoglycaemia, elevated serum insulin, and C-peptide levels.
  • Adrenal insufficiency - Associated with fatigue, hypotension, and weight loss, along with low cortisol levels.
  • Alcohol-induced hypoglycaemia - Occurs after alcohol consumption, particularly in fasting or malnourished patients, with no hyperinsulinaemia.
  • Related alternative diagnosis - Distinguish from hypoglycaemia using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Hypoglycaemia"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Assess severity, physiological stability, and immediate complications.
  • Core care: Deliver condition-specific management for hypoglycaemia, treat underlying drivers, and coordinate appropriate specialist input.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Reassess symptom trajectory, examination findings, and objective markers to confirm response in hypoglycaemia.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Escalate urgently for clinical deterioration, evolving red flags, or failure to improve in suspected hypoglycaemia.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Hypoglycaemia"][/sc]
  • Permanent neurological damage - Due to prolonged neuroglycopenia, can result in cognitive impairment or coma.
  • Cardiac arrhythmias - Severe hypoglycaemia can cause arrhythmias due to autonomic dysfunction.
  • Recurrent hypoglycaemia - Frequent episodes can lead to hypoglycaemia unawareness, increasing the risk of severe episodes.
