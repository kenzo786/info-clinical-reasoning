Functional Abdominal Pain Syndrome (FAPS)
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Functional Abdominal Pain Syndrome (FAPS)"][/sc]
  • Functional Abdominal Pain Syndrome (FAPS) is a chronic pain disorder characterised by persistent or recurrent abdominal pain with no identifiable organic or structural cause.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise functional features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.

Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Functional Abdominal Pain Syndrome (FAPS)"][/sc]
  • FAPS involves complex, multifactorial pathophysiology linked to altered gut-brain axis signalling. Key mechanisms include:
  • Visceral hypersensitivity: Heightened pain perception in the gastrointestinal tract due to altered central pain processing.
  • Central sensitisation: Long-standing pain leads to increased sensitivity of the central nervous system, resulting in amplified pain signals from normally non-painful stimuli.
  • Psychosocial factors: Anxiety, depression, and stress are highly correlated with FAPS, likely influencing pain perception through neuroendocrine and immune changes.
  • Impaired autonomic regulation: Dysregulation of the autonomic nervous system (ANS) affects gut motility and pain processing, contributing to symptom persistence.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Functional Abdominal Pain Syndrome (FAPS)"][/sc]
  • Initial Phase: Symptoms may start with mild, intermittent abdominal pain, often with no clear trigger.
  • Chronic Phase: Pain becomes more consistent, typically lasting over six months, without association to bowel movements or food intake.
  • Persistent/Recurrent Phase: Patients may experience periods of increased pain intensity, often correlated with stress or life events, leading to long-term quality of life impairment.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Functional Abdominal Pain Syndrome (FAPS)"][/sc]
  • Ask about abdominal pain: Persistent or recurrent, poorly localised pain often described as dull or cramp-like; results from altered visceral sensitivity and central sensitisation.
  • Ask about bloating: Sensation of fullness or pressure in the abdomen, possibly due to dysregulated autonomic control affecting gut motility.
  • Ask about nausea: Often co-occurs with pain, possibly linked to autonomic nervous system involvement and altered central pain processing.
  • Ask about sleep disturbances: Due to chronic pain; pain may disrupt sleep quality, worsening fatigue and overall well-being.
  • Ask about anxiety and depression: Commonly associated, likely both as a cause and result of FAPS, contributing to heightened pain perception through stress pathways.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Functional Abdominal Pain Syndrome (FAPS)"][/sc]
  • Check normal abdominal examination: Despite significant pain, no signs of tenderness, guarding, or organomegaly, supporting a functional rather than organic cause.
  • Check non-specific tenderness: Mild, diffuse tenderness on palpation without localisation, likely due to visceral hypersensitivity rather than inflammation.
  • Check psychological signs: Signs of anxiety or stress may be present; patients may appear distressed or tense, reflecting the psychophysiological link.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Functional Abdominal Pain Syndrome (FAPS)"][/sc]
  • Unintentional weight loss: May suggest malignancy, chronic infection, or inflammatory conditions.
  • Blood in stool: Indicative of potential gastrointestinal bleeding, ulceration, or malignancy.
  • Night sweats or fever: Could indicate infection or inflammatory pathology such as inflammatory bowel disease.
  • Onset after age 50: Higher suspicion for organic causes in older patients, including colorectal cancer.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Functional Abdominal Pain Syndrome (FAPS)"][/sc]
  • Presentation with primarily nausea or dizziness, without significant pain, can occasionally occur due to autonomic dysregulation.
  • Some may experience episodic, sharp pain rather than a dull ache, leading to initial misdiagnosis as other conditions like IBS.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask functional abdominal pain syndrome (faps).
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Functional Abdominal Pain Syndrome (FAPS)"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Focused bedside assessment and first-line testing to assess severity and immediate risk in suspected functional abdominal pain syndrome (faps).
  • Targeted and monitoring: Endoscopy/colonoscopy: Reserved for patients with red flag signs; normal findings support a functional diagnosis; Psychological evaluation: Screening for anxiety and depression.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Functional Abdominal Pain Syndrome (FAPS)"][/sc]
  • Irritable Bowel Syndrome (IBS) - Distinguish from functional abdominal pain syndrome (faps) using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.
  • Inflammatory Bowel Disease (IBD) - Distinguished by inflammation markers, weight loss, and abnormal faecal calprotectin levels.
  • Peptic Ulcer Disease - Pain often localised to the epigastrium, associated with eating or relieved by antacids.
  • Chronic Pancreatitis - More likely with history of alcohol use, upper abdominal pain radiating to the back.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Functional Abdominal Pain Syndrome (FAPS)"][/sc]
  • Psychosocial impairment: Long-term FAPS can significantly impact quality of life, employment, and social relationships.
  • Increased healthcare utilisation: High rates of healthcare visits and unnecessary testing due to chronic pain.
  • Prognosis: Symptoms are chronic but can be managed with psychological and pharmacological treatment, though complete resolution is rare.
