Non-epileptic Seizure (NES)
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Non-epileptic Seizure (NES)"][/sc]
  • Non-epileptic seizures (NES) are episodes that resemble epileptic seizures but are not associated with abnormal electrical discharges in the brain.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise epileptic features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Non-epileptic Seizure (NES)"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Non-epileptic Seizure (NES)"][/sc]
  • More common in women than men (approximately 3:1 ratio), with onset typically in young adulthood.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Non-epileptic Seizure (NES)"][/sc]
  • NES is thought to result from a complex interplay of psychological stressors and abnormal brain functioning. Emotional stress, particularly stemming from trauma (e.g., abuse, neglect), can manifest as physical symptoms due to altered brain pathways. In NES, areas of the brain related to emotional regulation, such as the limbic system, interact abnormally with motor control areas, leading to seizure-like movements.
  • No abnormal electrical activity occurs during NES, unlike true epileptic seizures, and the condition is often associated with dissociation, conversion disorder, or somatisation disorder.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Non-epileptic Seizure (NES)"][/sc]
  • Onset: Typically during stressful or emotionally challenging situations; often in late adolescence or early adulthood.
  • Course: NES episodes can occur intermittently over years, often increasing in frequency if psychological distress remains unresolved.
  • Duration: Individual episodes last longer than epileptic seizures, often up to several minutes.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Non-epileptic Seizure (NES)"][/sc]
  • Ask about seizure-like episodes - Sudden, involuntary movements that may resemble epileptic seizures but lack the typical postictal phase. Pathophysiology relates to dissociative or conversion disorders.
  • Ask about eye closure during episodes - Unlike in true epileptic seizures where the eyes are typically open, patients with NES often have their eyes closed.
  • Ask about absence of tongue biting or incontinence - These features, more common in epileptic seizures, are usually absent in NES.
  • Ask about emotional triggers - Episodes often occur during or after periods of psychological stress or trauma, reflecting the underlying emotional distress.
  • Ask about dissociation - Some patients report feeling detached from their surroundings or body, often as a defence mechanism to cope with trauma.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Non-epileptic Seizure (NES)"][/sc]
  • Check asynchronous limb movements - Movements during NES are often out of sync, unlike the coordinated rhythmic movements of epileptic seizures, reflecting the non-neurological basis.
  • Check preserved awareness - During NES, patients may retain partial awareness, differentiating it from epileptic seizures where consciousness is often impaired.
  • Check lack of postictal confusion - Patients recover quickly without the confusion or lethargy typical of the postictal state seen in epileptic seizures.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Non-epileptic Seizure (NES)"][/sc]
  • Severe trauma history - Suggests a strong psychological component, possibly requiring mental health intervention to prevent worsening of NES.
  • Sudden onset in adulthood - May suggest an alternative diagnosis, such as a structural brain lesion or tumour, if not previously identified.
  • Progressively worsening episodes - Could indicate co-existing epilepsy or a different neurological condition, necessitating further investigation.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Non-epileptic Seizure (NES)"][/sc]
  • Intermittent NES without psychological distress - May occur in patients without clear trauma or psychological stress, leading to misdiagnosis. Pathophysiology could involve subconscious factors or unrecognised trauma.
  • Co-occurring epileptic and non-epileptic seizures - Some patients may have both types of seizures, making diagnosis more difficult. Misinterpretation of symptoms may lead to overmedication or undertreatment.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask non-epileptic seizure (nes).
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Non-epileptic Seizure (NES)"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Video EEG monitoring - NES can be differentiated from epileptic seizures by the absence of abnormal electrical activity during episodes. EEG.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Psychiatric evaluation - Assessment for underlying trauma, dissociation, or psychiatric disorders such as anxiety or depression is essential for diagnosis; Neuroimaging.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Non-epileptic Seizure (NES)"][/sc]
  • Misdiagnosis as epilepsy - Leads to unnecessary use of anti-seizure medications with potential side effects and social implications.
  • Chronic disability - Recurrent episodes can lead to significant social and occupational impairment if left untreated.
  • Co-existing psychiatric disorders - Anxiety, depression, and PTSD may worsen if not concurrently managed.
