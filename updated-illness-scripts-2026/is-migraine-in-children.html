Migraine in Children
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Migraine in Children"][/sc]
  • A migraine is a primary headache disorder characterised by recurrent episodes of moderate to severe headaches, often accompanied by nausea, vomiting, photophobia, or phonophobia.

Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Migraine in Children"][/sc]
  • 1. Migraine likely involves the trigeminovascular system: activation leads to the release of neuropeptides (e.g., calcitonin gene-related peptide).
  • 2. Cortical spreading depression causes neuronal depolarisation, leading to the aura and subsequent headache phase.
  • 3. Dysfunctional sensory processing in the brainstem heightens sensitivity to stimuli, resulting in associated symptoms like photophobia and phonophobia.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Migraine in Children"][/sc]
  • Prodrome: May include mood changes, food cravings, or fatigue hours to days before onset.
  • Aura: Develops over 5-60 minutes in 15-30% of cases, with visual, sensory, or speech disturbances.
  • Headache phase: Lasts 1-48 hours in children, often bilateral and moderate to severe in intensity.
  • Postdrome: Fatigue and difficulty concentrating may persist for hours to days.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Migraine in Children"][/sc]
  • Ask about headache - Recurrent, moderate to severe pain, typically bilateral in children. Pathophysiology: Trigeminal nerve activation and inflammation.
  • Ask about nausea and vomiting - Commonly reported, likely due to brainstem involvement affecting gastrointestinal motility.
  • Ask about photophobia and phonophobia - Sensitivity to light and sound, related to hyperactivation of sensory pathways.
  • Ask about aura - Visual disturbances, sensory changes, or speech difficulty in some cases, due to cortical spreading depression.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Migraine in Children"][/sc]
  • Check pallor - Often observed during attacks, potentially due to autonomic dysfunction.
  • Check fatigue - Frequently noted in postdrome phase, reflecting central nervous system recovery.
  • Check irritability - Common in younger children, representing pain-related behavioural changes.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Migraine in Children"][/sc]
  • Headache worsening with coughing or straining - May indicate raised intracranial pressure.
  • Seizures - Suggests an alternative or secondary cause such as a structural lesion.
  • Focal neurological signs - Unusual for migraine; consider stroke or tumour.
  • Sudden, severe onset ("thunderclap headache") - May signify subarachnoid haemorrhage.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Migraine in Children"][/sc]
  • Abdominal migraine - Episodic abdominal pain without headache, possibly linked to the same pathophysiological mechanisms as typical migraine.
  • Hemiplegic migraine - Reversible hemiparesis associated with migraine aura.
  • Cyclic vomiting syndrome - Periodic severe vomiting episodes with migraine-like features in some children.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask migraine in children.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Migraine in Children"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Clinical diagnosis - Based on ICHD-3 criteria; no specific tests required in uncomplicated cases.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Neuroimaging - Indicated if red flag features present, typically MRI to exclude structural abnormalities; EEG - May be considered if seizures.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Migraine in Children"][/sc]
  • Tension-type headache - Typically mild, bilateral, and not associated with nausea or aura.
  • Cluster headache - Rare in children, characterised by severe unilateral orbital pain with autonomic symptoms.
  • Sinusitis - Facial pain and nasal congestion distinguishable from migraine.
  • Idiopathic intracranial hypertension - Associated with papilloedema and vision changes.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Migraine in Children"][/sc]
  • Chronic migraine - Defined as headaches on ≥15 days/month for >3 months, likely due to central sensitisation.
  • Medication overuse headache - Secondary to frequent analgesic use, involving rebound headache mechanisms.
  • Impact on quality of life - School absences, reduced participation in activities.
