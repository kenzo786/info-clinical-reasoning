Atopic Dermatitis
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Atopic Dermatitis"][/sc]
  • Atopic dermatitis (AD), commonly known as eczema, is a chronic, relapsing, inflammatory skin disorder characterised by intense pruritus and eczematous lesions.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise atopic features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Atopic Dermatitis"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Atopic Dermatitis"][/sc]
  • Risk factors: Risk of atopic dermatitis rises with relevant predisposition, comorbidity, and exposure profile.
  • Higher-risk or missed groups: Atopic Dermatitis may be missed when presentations are atypical or comorbidity obscures key features.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Atopic Dermatitis"][/sc]
  • Skin barrier dysfunction due to mutations in the filaggrin gene leads to increased transepidermal water loss, predisposing the skin to dryness and allergens.
  • Immune dysregulation, with an increased Th2 response and production of IgE, leads to hypersensitivity to environmental allergens, contributing to inflammation.
  • Microbial colonisation, especially Staphylococcus aureus, exacerbates skin inflammation due to increased protease production and skin barrier breakdown.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Atopic Dermatitis"][/sc]
  • Infantile Phase (0-2 years): Often presents with facial and extensor lesions, usually with oozing and crusting.
  • Childhood Phase (2-12 years): Pruritic lesions typically in flexural areas (elbows, knees, neck), with lichenification due to scratching.
  • Adulthood Phase (>12 years): Chronic relapsing-remitting pattern with lesions in flexural and periocular areas, and increased skin thickening (lichenification).
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Atopic Dermatitis"][/sc]
  • Ask about pruritus (itching) - Driven by Th2-mediated inflammation and histamine release, pruritus is often severe and worsens at night, impacting sleep and quality of life.
  • Ask about dry skin (xerosis) - Due to impaired skin barrier and filaggrin deficiency, causing reduced moisture retention.
  • Ask about erythematous patches - Inflammatory response from Th2-mediated cytokine release leads to redness.
  • Ask about crusting or weeping - Indicates secondary infection or exudate from intense scratching.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Atopic Dermatitis"][/sc]
  • Check excoriations - Visible signs of scratching, often leading to linear abrasions or erosions.
  • Check lichenification - Thickened skin with accentuated lines, typically from chronic scratching in flexural areas.
  • Check erythematous and scaly patches - Inflammatory plaques, common on flexural surfaces in children and adults.
  • Check hyperpigmentation - Darkening of skin in affected areas from post-inflammatory changes.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Atopic Dermatitis"][/sc]
  • Extensive crusting and erythema - May indicate eczema herpeticum, a severe herpes simplex infection, especially with systemic symptoms.
  • Pustules and malodorous exudate - Suggests bacterial superinfection, commonly by Staphylococcus aureus, requiring systemic antibiotics.
  • Failure to respond to standard treatment - May indicate an atypical presentation or underlying immunodeficiency.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Atopic Dermatitis"][/sc]
  • Hand eczema without flexural involvement - Common in adults, often mistaken for contact dermatitis.
  • Periorbital involvement with minimal erythema - May present with pigmentation changes around the eyes, often mistaken for allergic shiners.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features or comorbidity can mask the underlying diagnosis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Atopic Dermatitis"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Clinical diagnosis - Based on history and physical examination. Laboratory tests are not routinely required but may support diagnosis.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Total IgE levels - Often elevated in AD but not diagnostic; may support diagnosis in atypical cases; Skin swab for bacterial.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Atopic Dermatitis"][/sc]
  • A 5-year-old child with a history of atopic disorders presenting with recurrent pruritic, erythematous patches on the flexural surfaces, associated with dry skin and excoriations.
  • Adult with symptom pattern and objective findings suggestive of atopic dermatitis, requiring focused confirmation and risk stratification.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Atopic Dermatitis"][/sc]
  • Contact dermatitis - Often localised and related to exposure to an allergen or irritant, with a well-defined.
  • Psoriasis - Typically presents with well-demarcated, silvery plaques on extensor surfaces rather than flexures.
  • Scabies - Characterised by intense itching, especially at night, with burrows in interdigital spaces.
  • Alternative serious diagnosis - Distinguishing history, examination, and targeted investigations do not support atopic dermatitis.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Atopic Dermatitis"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Assess physiological stability and urgent complications at presentation, and initiate early stabilisation where required.
  • Core care: Deliver condition-specific treatment for atopic dermatitis, address underlying drivers, and involve appropriate specialist support.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Reassess symptom trajectory, objective markers, and treatment response over time to detect complications early.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Worsening symptoms, physiological deterioration, or failure to improve should trigger urgent reassessment for severe or alternative pathology in atopic dermatitis.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Atopic Dermatitis"][/sc]
  • Secondary bacterial infection - Commonly caused by Staphylococcus aureus due to skin barrier disruption.
  • Eczema herpeticum - Severe viral infection with herpes simplex; rare but potentially life-threatening.
  • Psychological impact - Increased risk of anxiety and depression due to chronic symptoms and visible lesions.
