Burning Mouth Syndrome (BMS)
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Burning Mouth Syndrome (BMS)"][/sc]
  • Burning Mouth Syndrome (BMS) is a chronic condition characterised by a persistent burning or tingling sensation in the oral mucosa, typically without visible clinical signs or lesions.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise burning features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.

Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Burning Mouth Syndrome (BMS)"][/sc]
  • Neurogenic dysfunction: Damage or dysfunction in peripheral or central nervous pathways involved in pain perception is hypothesised, especially dysfunction in small-diameter sensory fibres in the oral mucosa.
  • Altered taste perception: Loss of taste (hypogeusia) or dysgeusia may occur due to abnormal interactions between the gustatory and trigeminal nerves.
  • Hormonal factors: Oestrogen deficiency, particularly in postmenopausal women, is thought to contribute to mucosal sensitivity and neuropathic changes.
  • Psychogenic factors: Anxiety, depression, and chronic stress are commonly associated with BMS and may exacerbate the pain perception mechanisms in affected individuals.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Burning Mouth Syndrome (BMS)"][/sc]
  • Onset: Gradual onset, often following dental procedures, illnesses, or hormonal changes.
  • Course: Chronic, with symptoms persisting for months to years. Symptoms may fluctuate in intensity, often worsening throughout the day.
  • Duration: Persistent, often lasting over 6 months to several years if left untreated.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Burning Mouth Syndrome (BMS)"][/sc]
  • Ask about burning sensation in the mouth - Persistent burning pain in the tongue, lips, or other oral areas. Pathophysiologically, this is due to dysfunction in in this clinical context.
  • Ask about xerostomia (dry mouth) - A sensation of dryness in the mouth, often related to altered salivary gland function or neuropathy affecting oral mucosal moisture.
  • Ask about dysgeusia (altered taste) - Taste disturbances, such as metallic or bitter tastes, due to abnormal interactions between gustatory and pain pathways.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Burning Mouth Syndrome (BMS)"][/sc]
  • Check absence of clinical lesions - Despite significant symptoms, the oral mucosa appears normal on physical examination, which helps distinguish primary BMS from other conditions.
  • Check normal salivary flow rates - Despite complaints of dry mouth, objective measurements of salivary flow are typically within normal limits.
  • Check no localised sensory loss - Unlike other neuropathic conditions, patients typically do not have overt sensory deficits in the oral region on neurological examination.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Burning Mouth Syndrome (BMS)"][/sc]
  • Visible oral lesions - Could suggest conditions such as oral cancer, lichen planus, or candidiasis rather than BMS, requiring biopsy or further investigation.
  • Severe weight loss or dysphagia - Indicates the possibility of malignancy or systemic conditions affecting oral health, such as Sjögren’s syndrome or amyloidosis.
  • Unilateral symptoms - Suggests a potential focal pathology such as trigeminal neuralgia or localised infection/tumour, which needs further imaging.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Burning Mouth Syndrome (BMS)"][/sc]
  • Isolated taste disturbances without pain - In some cases, patients may present primarily with dysgeusia without significant burning sensations, which can lead to delayed diagnosis.
  • Intermittent burning pain - Some patients may report symptoms that come and go, rather than the classic persistent pain, which can be confused with other oral mucosal conditions.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features or comorbidity can mask the underlying diagnosis.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Burning Mouth Syndrome (BMS)"][/sc]
  • Oral candidiasis - Differentiate from burning mouth syndrome (bms) using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.
  • Oral lichen planus - Painful oral condition with characteristic lacy, white, reticular patches on the buccal mucosa, absent.
  • Sjögren’s syndrome - Causes dry mouth but is associated with dry eyes, systemic symptoms, and reduced salivary.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Burning Mouth Syndrome (BMS)"][/sc]
  • Chronic pain and psychological impact - Long-standing burning pain can lead to anxiety, depression, and reduced quality of life.
  • Malnutrition - Persistent oral discomfort may lead to reduced oral intake, causing weight loss or nutritional deficiencies.
