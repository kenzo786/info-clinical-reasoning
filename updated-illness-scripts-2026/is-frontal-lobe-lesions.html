Frontal Lobe Lesions
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Frontal Lobe Lesions"][/sc]
  • Frontal lobe lesions refer to abnormalities, including tumours, traumatic injuries, strokes, or neurodegenerative diseases, that impair the function of the frontal lobe.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise frontal features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.

Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Frontal Lobe Lesions"][/sc]
  • Frontal lobe lesions disrupt neural circuits involved in executive functioning, emotional regulation, and motor control. Damage to the prefrontal cortex may affect decision-making, social behaviour, and emotional responses due to impaired connections with the limbic system. Lesions in the motor cortex result in contralateral motor weakness. Injury to Broca's area, located in the dominant hemisphere, can cause expressive aphasia. Lesions in different regions of the frontal lobe lead to distinct but overlapping deficits due to interconnected cortical and subcortical networks.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Frontal Lobe Lesions"][/sc]
  • Acute onset: Common in cases of trauma or stroke, with symptoms emerging within hours to days.
  • Subacute onset: Tumours or slow-growing lesions often present symptoms over weeks to months as mass effect increases.
  • Chronic progression: Neurodegenerative diseases impacting the frontal lobe, like frontotemporal dementia, progress gradually over years, leading to increasing cognitive and behavioural impairments.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Frontal Lobe Lesions"][/sc]
  • Ask about personality changes - Common in lesions affecting the prefrontal cortex, leading to impulsivity, disinhibition, and lack of empathy, as these areas modulate social behaviours.
  • Ask about executive dysfunction - Difficulty with planning, problem-solving, and multitasking due to impaired frontal lobe processing of complex tasks.
  • Ask about apathy or lack of motivation - A result of disrupted mesolimbic pathways involving reward and motivation centres.
  • Ask about memory impairment - Lesions in the prefrontal cortex can impair working memory, although less commonly than temporal lobe lesions.
  • Ask about expressive aphasia - Lesions in Broca’s area lead to difficulty forming words and constructing sentences.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Frontal Lobe Lesions"][/sc]
  • Check contralateral weakness - Lesions in the primary motor cortex affect motor output, causing weakness on the opposite side of the body.
  • Check positive grasp reflex - A frontal release sign often appearing when higher-order frontal inhibition is lost.
  • Check reduced insight - Patients may lack awareness of their behavioural and cognitive deficits due to impaired self-monitoring.
  • Check disinhibited behaviour - Loss of social restraint and impulsivity due to disrupted prefrontal control of socially appropriate behaviour.
  • Check expressive aphasia - Difficulty in verbal expression without impairing comprehension, common in left frontal lesions involving Broca’s area.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Frontal Lobe Lesions"][/sc]
  • Rapidly progressive symptoms - May indicate a fast-growing tumour, haemorrhage, or abscess.
  • Severe headache - Suggests increased intracranial pressure, commonly seen in high-grade tumours or large lesions.
  • Seizures - Sudden onset or new seizures in an adult may be linked to space-occupying lesions or haemorrhage.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Frontal Lobe Lesions"][/sc]
  • Subtle executive dysfunction without motor signs - Particularly common in early stages of frontal tumours or dementia, often overlooked as normal age-related cognitive changes.
  • Isolated mood or personality changes - Occasionally, frontal lesions may mimic psychiatric conditions such as depression or mania, especially in younger patients.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask frontal lobe lesions.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Frontal Lobe Lesions"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Focused bedside assessment and first-line testing to assess severity and immediate risk in suspected frontal lobe lesions.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Neuropsychological testing - Assesses specific deficits in executive function, memory, and language, valuable for subtle frontal lobe involvement; EEG - May.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Frontal Lobe Lesions"][/sc]
  • Frontotemporal dementia - Progressive cognitive and behavioural changes but without focal neurological signs.
  • Schizophrenia - Typically younger onset with auditory hallucinations, unlike neurological findings in frontal lesions.
  • Stroke - Sudden onset rather than gradual progression; often with focal weakness and language deficits.
  • Related alternative diagnosis - Distinguish from frontal lobe lesions using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.

  • Escalation and safety-netting: Escalate urgently for clinical deterioration, evolving red flags, or failure to improve in suspected frontal lobe lesions.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Frontal Lobe Lesions"][/sc]
  • Seizures - Common with cortical irritation from tumours or haemorrhage, often managed with anticonvulsants.
  • Intracranial hypertension - Caused by growing lesions, leading to symptoms such as headaches, nausea, and potential herniation risks if untreated.
  • Permanent cognitive impairment - Depending on the lesion’s size, location, and treatment efficacy, residual deficits may persist.
