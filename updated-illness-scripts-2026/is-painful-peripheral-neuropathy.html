Painful Peripheral Neuropathy
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Painful Peripheral Neuropathy"][/sc]
  • Painful peripheral neuropathy refers to a disorder of the peripheral nerves characterised by pain and sensory disturbances, often presenting as burning, stabbing, or tingling sensations.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise painful features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.

  • Higher-risk or missed groups: Painful Peripheral Neuropathy may be missed when presentation is atypical or obscured by comorbidity.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Painful Peripheral Neuropathy"][/sc]
  • Peripheral neuropathy results from damage to peripheral nerves due to metabolic, toxic, infectious, or immune-mediated causes.
  • Chronic hyperglycaemia (in diabetes) leads to microvascular damage, oxidative stress, and accumulation of advanced glycation end-products, resulting in nerve ischaemia and axonal degeneration.
  • Toxic exposures (e.g., alcohol, chemotherapy) cause direct axonal injury, disrupting ion channel function and nerve signal conduction.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Painful Peripheral Neuropathy"][/sc]
  • Acute: Sudden onset neuropathy seen in infectious or toxic aetiologies.
  • Subacute: Progresses over weeks to months, common in autoimmune or medication-induced causes.
  • Chronic: Develops over months to years, typical in diabetic and idiopathic cases.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Painful Peripheral Neuropathy"][/sc]
  • Ask about burning pain: Due to hyperexcitable sensory nerve fibres and loss of inhibitory signals.
  • Ask about tingling or paraesthesia: Caused by abnormal nerve signalling from partial axonal injury.
  • Ask about allodynia: Pain from non-painful stimuli, linked to central sensitisation from peripheral nerve damage.
  • Ask about weakness: Seen in motor nerve involvement, often secondary to axonal degeneration.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Painful Peripheral Neuropathy"][/sc]
  • Check reduced sensation: Loss of pain, temperature, or vibration sense due to sensory fibre damage.
  • Check decreased reflexes: Most prominent in the distal limbs, linked to large-fibre neuropathy.
  • Check atrophy: Muscle wasting in severe cases, resulting from motor nerve involvement.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Painful Peripheral Neuropathy"][/sc]
  • Rapid progression: Suggests Guillain-Barré syndrome or malignancy.
  • Severe asymmetry: Raises suspicion for vasculitis or entrapment neuropathies.
  • Autonomic involvement: Signifies possible amyloidosis or diabetes.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Painful Peripheral Neuropathy"][/sc]
  • Isolated cranial neuropathies: Seen in infections like herpes zoster.
  • Mononeuritis multiplex: Discrete sensory and motor nerve deficits, often due to vasculitis.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask painful peripheral neuropathy.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Painful Peripheral Neuropathy"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: HbA1c: Elevated in diabetic neuropathy; Nerve conduction studies: Show reduced conduction velocities in demyelinating conditions.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Serum B12: Low in neuropathy from B12 deficiency; Electromyography (EMG): Identifies axonal versus demyelinating injury patterns.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Painful Peripheral Neuropathy"][/sc]
  • Diabetic neuropathy - Symmetrical distal polyneuropathy with burning pain.
  • Alcoholic neuropathy - Similar presentation but linked to alcohol history.
  • B12 deficiency neuropathy - Accompanied by macrocytic anaemia and glossitis.
  • Guillain-Barré syndrome - Acute, ascending weakness and sensory loss.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Painful Peripheral Neuropathy"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Assess severity, physiological stability, and immediate complications.
  • Core care: Deliver condition-specific management for painful peripheral neuropathy, treat underlying drivers, and coordinate appropriate specialist input.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Reassess symptom trajectory, examination findings, and objective markers to confirm response in painful peripheral neuropathy.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Escalate urgently for clinical deterioration, evolving red flags, or failure to improve in suspected painful peripheral neuropathy.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Painful Peripheral Neuropathy"][/sc]
  • Ulcers: Result from sensory loss and unnoticed injuries.
  • Falls: Caused by balance impairment due to sensory deficits.
  • Chronic pain syndromes: Can significantly impair quality of life.
