Malignant Hypertension
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Malignant Hypertension"][/sc]
  • Malignant hypertension, also known as hypertensive emergency, is a severe form of hypertension characterised by extremely high blood pressure (typically ≥180/120 mmHg) accompanied by evidence of acute target organ damage.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise malignant features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.

  • Higher-risk or missed groups: Malignant Hypertension may be missed when presentation is atypical or obscured by comorbidity.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Malignant Hypertension"][/sc]
  • Malignant hypertension results from a sudden, severe increase in systemic vascular resistance (SVR) due to the failure of autoregulation in the small blood vessels (arterioles). This leads to endothelial injury, increased vascular permeability, and fibrinoid necrosis in various organs, causing ischemia and damage to vital organs like the brain, kidneys, and heart.
  • Renal ischaemia due to narrowing of the renal arteries activates the renin-angiotensin-aldosterone system (RAAS), which further increases blood pressure in a vicious cycle.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Malignant Hypertension"][/sc]
  • Malignant hypertension can develop rapidly over hours to days, leading to acute organ damage.
  • If untreated, the condition can progress to life-threatening complications such as stroke, heart failure, or renal failure within hours or days.
  • With appropriate treatment, blood pressure can be gradually lowered over 24-48 hours to avoid precipitating organ hypoperfusion.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Malignant Hypertension"][/sc]
  • Ask about severe headache - Due to raised intracranial pressure secondary to hypertensive encephalopathy.
  • Ask about blurred vision - From hypertensive retinopathy, papilloedema, and retinal haemorrhages.
  • Ask about nausea and vomiting - Resulting from increased intracranial pressure.
  • Ask about chest pain - May be indicative of myocardial ischaemia or aortic dissection.
  • Ask about shortness of breath - Due to heart failure and pulmonary oedema from left ventricular dysfunction.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Malignant Hypertension"][/sc]
  • Check severely elevated blood pressure - Typically ≥180/120 mmHg, this is the key diagnostic criterion.
  • Check papilloedema - Optic disc swelling due to raised intracranial pressure and retinal damage.
  • Check retinal haemorrhages and exudates - Indicative of severe hypertensive retinopathy.
  • Check signs of heart failure (e.g., bilateral crackles) - From acute left ventricular dysfunction.
  • Check neurological deficits - Focal deficits such as hemiparesis, dysarthria, or visual field defects can occur due to stroke or hypertensive encephalopathy.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Malignant Hypertension"][/sc]
  • Chest pain - May indicate myocardial infarction or aortic dissection, both life-threatening complications.
  • Altered mental status - Suggests hypertensive encephalopathy or stroke, both of which require immediate intervention.
  • Oliguria or anuria - Indicative of acute kidney injury, which may progress to renal failure.
  • Severe headache with visual changes - Could signify papilloedema, retinal haemorrhage, or impending stroke.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Malignant Hypertension"][/sc]
  • Silent malignant hypertension - Patients may present with only mild symptoms or be asymptomatic despite severe organ damage, which can be uncovered during routine evaluations.
  • Non-specific symptoms - Fatigue, mild headache, or dizziness without clear organ damage may delay diagnosis.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask malignant hypertension.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Malignant Hypertension"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Focused bedside assessment and first-line testing to assess severity and immediate risk in suspected malignant hypertension.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Urinalysis - May show proteinuria or haematuria, reflecting kidney damage; ECG - May show signs of left ventricular hypertrophy or ischaemia.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Malignant Hypertension"][/sc]
  • Phaeochromocytoma - May cause paroxysmal hypertension with episodic symptoms (headache, palpitations), unlike sustained malignant hypertension.
  • Acute kidney injury - While it may cause hypertension, the absence of other target organ damage (e.g., retinopathy).
  • Pre-eclampsia - Seen in pregnant women, with hypertension, proteinuria, and oedema, but not usually the severe.
  • Related alternative diagnosis - Distinguish from malignant hypertension using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Malignant Hypertension"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Assess severity, physiological stability, and immediate complications.
  • Core care: Deliver condition-specific management for malignant hypertension, treat underlying drivers, and coordinate appropriate specialist input.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Reassess symptom trajectory, examination findings, and objective markers to confirm response in malignant hypertension.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Escalate urgently for clinical deterioration, evolving red flags, or failure to improve in suspected malignant hypertension.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Malignant Hypertension"][/sc]
  • Stroke - Resulting from hypertensive encephalopathy or intracerebral haemorrhage.
  • Acute kidney injury - Due to renal vascular damage and ischaemia.
  • Heart failure - From left ventricular dysfunction and increased afterload.
  • Retinopathy - Can lead to permanent visual loss.
