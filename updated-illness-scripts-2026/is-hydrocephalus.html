Hydrocephalus
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Hydrocephalus"][/sc]
  • Hydrocephalus is a neurological condition caused by an accumulation of cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) within the ventricles of the brain, leading to increased intracranial pressure.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise hydrocephalus features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.

  • Higher-risk or missed groups: Hydrocephalus may be missed when presentation is atypical or obscured by comorbidity.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Hydrocephalus"][/sc]
  • CSF is normally produced by the choroid plexus, circulates through the ventricles, and is absorbed by arachnoid granulations. In hydrocephalus, blockage (non-communicating), impaired absorption (communicating), or overproduction disrupts this balance, leading to excess CSF, increased ventricular size, and raised intracranial pressure. Chronic hydrocephalus can cause brain atrophy and ventriculomegaly, impacting neurological function.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Hydrocephalus"][/sc]
  • Acute: Rapid onset symptoms, often due to trauma, haemorrhage, or acute obstruction.
  • Subacute/Chronic: Progressive symptoms developing over weeks to months, commonly seen in congenital or idiopathic cases.
  • Normal Pressure Hydrocephalus (NPH): Gradual progression, typically in elderly patients, leading to chronic cognitive and motor impairment.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Hydrocephalus"][/sc]
  • Ask about headache - Caused by increased intracranial pressure stretching pain-sensitive structures in the meninges.
  • Ask about nausea and Vomiting - Resulting from raised intracranial pressure affecting the brainstem and area postrema.
  • Ask about gait Disturbance - Common in NPH, with ventricular enlargement impacting white matter tracts responsible for motor control.
  • Ask about urinary Incontinence - Often seen in NPH, due to disrupted neural control over the bladder as the ventricles enlarge.
  • Ask about cognitive Impairment - Progressive memory and concentration difficulties, common in NPH due to fronto-subcortical dysfunction.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Hydrocephalus"][/sc]
  • Check increased Head Circumference (infants) - Due to skull expansion from elevated CSF pressure.
  • Check papilloedema - Swelling of the optic disc from raised intracranial pressure, seen in acute cases.
  • Check setting-Sun Sign (infants) - Downward gaze caused by pressure on cranial nerve pathways.
  • Check positive Romberg's Test - Unsteadiness due to impaired cerebellar or vestibular function in advanced cases.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Hydrocephalus"][/sc]
  • Severe Headache with Rapid Progression - Suggests acute obstruction or haemorrhage requiring urgent intervention.
  • Loss of Consciousness - Indicates critical intracranial pressure elevation, risking herniation and brainstem compression.
  • Severe Visual Changes or Papilloedema - May indicate optic nerve damage due to persistent intracranial pressure.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Hydrocephalus"][/sc]
  • Isolated Gait Disturbance - Common in elderly patients, may be mistaken for normal ageing or Parkinson’s disease.
  • Subtle Cognitive Decline - Can be gradual, resembling dementia, particularly in NPH.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask hydrocephalus.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Hydrocephalus"][/sc]
  • Parkinson’s Disease - Tremor and rigidity more pronounced, slow progression, lacks ventricular enlargement on imaging.
  • Alzheimer’s Disease - Predominantly cognitive decline without gait disturbance, absence of ventricular dilatation.
  • Brain Tumour - Symptoms may localise based on tumour location, with focal neurological deficits and distinct imaging.
  • Related alternative diagnosis - Distinguish from hydrocephalus using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.

  • Monitoring and follow-up: Reassess symptom trajectory, examination findings, and objective markers to confirm response in hydrocephalus.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Escalate urgently for clinical deterioration, evolving red flags, or failure to improve in suspected hydrocephalus.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Hydrocephalus"][/sc]
  • Shunt Infection: Occurs in 10-15% of cases, leading to meningitis or abscess formation.
  • Shunt Malfunction: Can cause rapid symptom recurrence and requires urgent surgical correction.
  • Brain Herniation: Life-threatening condition caused by critically high intracranial pressure.
  • Prognosis: Generally favourable in treated cases, but untreated hydrocephalus can result in progressive neurological decline and death.
