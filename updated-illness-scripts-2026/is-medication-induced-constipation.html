Medication-induced Constipation
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Medication-induced Constipation"][/sc]
  • Medication-induced constipation is a form of functional constipation caused directly by the side effects of certain medications.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise medication features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.

  • Higher-risk or missed groups: Medication-induced Constipation may be missed when presentation is atypical or obscured by comorbidity.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Medication-induced Constipation"][/sc]
  • Medications such as opioids, anticholinergics, and calcium channel blockers decrease gut motility by inhibiting peristaltic contractions and reducing intestinal secretions. Opioids activate μ-receptors in the gut, reducing water secretion and slowing transit. Anticholinergics inhibit parasympathetic nervous activity, further slowing motility, while calcium channel blockers relax smooth muscle, leading to reduced colonic contractions. These effects collectively result in hard, infrequent stools.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Medication-induced Constipation"][/sc]
  • Acute onset: Common with opioids and anticholinergics, constipation can develop within days to weeks after initiation.
  • Subacute to chronic onset: In cases with long-term medications like calcium channel blockers, constipation may develop gradually over weeks to months as gut motility adapts to decreased peristalsis.
  • Chronic course: Constipation may persist throughout the duration of the offending medication’s use and can become refractory in patients with prolonged use.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Medication-induced Constipation"][/sc]
  • Ask about infrequent bowel movements: Reduced peristalsis leads to prolonged stool transit, causing infrequent passage.
  • Ask about hard, dry stools: Decreased water and electrolyte secretion contributes to harder stools.
  • Ask about abdominal bloating: Slow transit leads to stool accumulation and increased gas production, causing distension.
  • Ask about abdominal pain: Resulting from distension and strain due to hard stools.
  • Ask about straining with defecation: Hard stools require more force to pass, often leading to strain.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Medication-induced Constipation"][/sc]
  • Check palpable abdominal mass: May indicate faecal accumulation in the colon, particularly the rectum.
  • Check decreased bowel sounds: Common with decreased motility, particularly in opioid-induced cases.
  • Check abdominal tenderness: Discomfort upon palpation, especially in the lower abdomen, due to stool retention.
  • Check anal fissures or haemorrhoids: May develop secondary to straining and hard stool passage.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Medication-induced Constipation"][/sc]
  • Rectal bleeding: Significant bleeding may indicate a rectal tear or severe haemorrhoids and warrants further evaluation for malignancy.
  • Unintentional weight loss: Unexpected weight loss could indicate a secondary malignancy or systemic illness rather than medication-related effects.
  • Severe abdominal pain with nausea/vomiting: May suggest bowel obstruction, requiring immediate intervention.
  • New-onset constipation in older adults: Raises concern for colorectal malignancy, needing prompt investigation.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Medication-induced Constipation"][/sc]
  • Mild or asymptomatic constipation: Some patients, especially on anticholinergics, may have reduced motility without significant symptoms.
  • Paradoxical diarrhoea: Overflow diarrhoea can occur when liquid stool bypasses impacted stools, causing loose stools or incontinence.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask medication-induced constipation.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Medication-induced Constipation"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Basic metabolic panel: Evaluates electrolyte imbalances, commonly found with dehydration due to fluid absorption in the colon; Thyroid function tests: Helps.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Imaging (Abdominal X-ray or CT): Used in cases of suspected faecal impaction or obstruction, showing stool accumulation or dilated bowel loops.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Medication-induced Constipation"][/sc]
  • Primary constipation - Often idiopathic, lacks a clear causative medication and may present with a more chronic.
  • Hypothyroidism - Usually associated with additional symptoms such as fatigue, weight gain, and bradycardia.
  • Obstructive colorectal cancer - Presents with red-flag features such as weight loss, rectal bleeding, and significant pain, especially.
  • Related alternative diagnosis - Distinguish from medication-induced constipation using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Medication-induced Constipation"][/sc]
  • Faecal impaction: Occurs when hard stool accumulates in the rectum, often causing pain, nausea, and overflow diarrhoea.
  • Haemorrhoids and anal fissures: Secondary to straining, they may lead to painful defecation and bleeding.
  • Colonic perforation (rare): A severe complication of impaction, more common in elderly or frail patients.
