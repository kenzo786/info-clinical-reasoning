Metabolic Neuropathy
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Metabolic Neuropathy"][/sc]
  • Metabolic neuropathy refers to peripheral nerve damage caused by underlying metabolic disorders, such as diabetes mellitus, renal failure, and hypothyroidism.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise metabolic features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.

Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Metabolic Neuropathy"][/sc]
  • Metabolic neuropathy arises due to chronic exposure of peripheral nerves to metabolic derangements. In diabetes, chronic hyperglycaemia leads to oxidative stress, glycation of nerve proteins, and microvascular damage. In renal failure, the accumulation of uraemic toxins impairs nerve function. In hypothyroidism, axonal swelling occurs due to deposition of mucopolysaccharides, leading to demyelination and nerve dysfunction.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Metabolic Neuropathy"][/sc]
  • Early Phase: In conditions like diabetes, symptoms may be absent or mild, with only small fibre neuropathy (burning, tingling) initially present.
  • Progressive Phase: Symptoms worsen over months to years, with a transition from sensory to motor involvement in severe cases.
  • Late Phase: Motor weakness and autonomic dysfunction (e.g., postural hypotension) may appear in advanced cases, particularly in poorly controlled diabetes or longstanding hypothyroidism.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Metabolic Neuropathy"][/sc]
  • Ask about distal sensory loss (stocking-glove distribution) - Due to length-dependent axonal degeneration, sensory fibres in the extremities are commonly affected first.
  • Ask about paresthesia (tingling, burning) - Small-fibre involvement leads to abnormal sensations due to nerve fibre hyperexcitability.
  • Ask about muscle weakness - Prolonged damage leads to motor nerve involvement, particularly in severe or uncontrolled metabolic states.
  • Ask about autonomic symptoms (e.g., postural hypotension, gastroparesis) - Autonomic fibres are affected in more advanced disease, disrupting sympathetic and parasympathetic function.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Metabolic Neuropathy"][/sc]
  • Check reduced vibration and proprioception - Large-fibre involvement leads to decreased sensation in the lower limbs, assessed via tuning fork tests.
  • Check absent ankle reflexes - Loss of large afferent fibres and motor involvement leads to diminished or absent deep tendon reflexes.
  • Check muscle wasting - Chronic neuropathy results in denervation and atrophy, especially in the distal muscles.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Metabolic Neuropathy"][/sc]
  • Rapid onset of weakness - May indicate Guillain-Barré syndrome or other acute neuropathies, which require urgent intervention.
  • Severe autonomic dysfunction - Potentially life-threatening and indicates advanced disease, such as cardiovascular autonomic neuropathy.
  • Severe, unremitting pain - Could suggest an underlying compressive or neoplastic process rather than purely metabolic causes.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Metabolic Neuropathy"][/sc]
  • Focal mononeuropathy - While metabolic neuropathies are typically symmetric, focal mononeuropathies such as carpal tunnel syndrome may occur in diabetes.
  • Proximal muscle weakness - Diabetic amyotrophy may present with thigh pain and proximal muscle weakness, differing from the usual distal pattern of involvement.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask metabolic neuropathy.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Metabolic Neuropathy"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Focused bedside assessment and first-line testing to assess severity and immediate risk in suspected metabolic neuropathy.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Renal function tests (e.g., urea, creatinine) - Elevated levels indicate uraemia as a potential cause; Thyroid function tests (TFTs) - Low.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Metabolic Neuropathy"][/sc]
  • Guillain-Barré syndrome - Rapid onset of ascending weakness and areflexia, distinguished by acute presentation and CSF findings.

Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Metabolic Neuropathy"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Assess severity, physiological stability, and immediate complications.
  • Core care: Deliver condition-specific management for metabolic neuropathy, treat underlying drivers, and coordinate appropriate specialist input.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Reassess symptom trajectory, examination findings, and objective markers to confirm response in metabolic neuropathy.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Escalate urgently for clinical deterioration, evolving red flags, or failure to improve in suspected metabolic neuropathy.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Metabolic Neuropathy"][/sc]
  • Foot ulcers and amputations - Reduced sensation increases the risk of injury, leading to chronic ulcers and, in severe cases, amputations.
  • Autonomic neuropathy - Can lead to severe complications like gastroparesis, orthostatic hypotension, and arrhythmias.
  • Permanent motor dysfunction - Longstanding disease can cause irreversible motor deficits and atrophy, particularly in the lower limbs.
