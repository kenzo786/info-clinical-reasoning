Baker's Cyst
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Baker's Cyst"][/sc]
  • A Baker's cyst, also known as a popliteal cyst, is a fluid-filled swelling in the popliteal fossa (the space behind the knee) caused by the distension of the gastrocnemius-semimembranosus bursa.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise baker features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.

  • Higher-risk or missed groups: Baker's Cyst may be missed when presentations are atypical or comorbidity obscures key features.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Baker's Cyst"][/sc]
  • Underlying knee joint pathology increases synovial fluid production.
  • Excess synovial fluid causes increased intra-articular pressure.
  • Fluid dissects into the gastrocnemius-semimembranosus bursa through a communication between the bursa and knee joint capsule.
  • The bursa distends, forming a palpable cyst in the popliteal region.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Baker's Cyst"][/sc]
  • Acute Phase: Rapid onset in association with trauma or knee effusion.
  • Chronic Phase: Gradual development over weeks to months, often asymptomatic unless complicated by rupture or compression of surrounding structures.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Baker's Cyst"][/sc]
  • Ask about posterior knee swelling - Most common, caused by distension of the bursa.
  • Ask about knee pain - Often associated with underlying pathology such as osteoarthritis.
  • Ask about stiffness or restricted range of motion - Due to mechanical obstruction or joint inflammation.
  • Consider asymptomatic - Many cases are incidental findings during imaging for other reasons.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Baker's Cyst"][/sc]
  • Check palpable mass in the popliteal fossa - Soft, fluctuant swelling on physical examination.
  • Check tenderness - Over the cyst, indicating inflammation or rupture.
  • Check reduced knee flexion - Mechanical limitation due to the size of the cyst.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Baker's Cyst"][/sc]
  • Sudden severe calf pain - May indicate cyst rupture and fluid tracking down the leg, mimicking deep vein thrombosis (DVT).
  • Redness or warmth - Could suggest infection or inflammatory arthritis.
  • Severe leg swelling - Compression of venous structures, potentially leading to venous insufficiency.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Baker's Cyst"][/sc]
  • Isolated calf swelling without popliteal mass - Due to fluid tracking after rupture.
  • Pain radiating down the leg - Mimics nerve impingement syndromes.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features or comorbidity can mask the underlying diagnosis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Baker's Cyst"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Ultrasound - Identifies a cystic lesion in the popliteal fossa and distinguishes it from solid masses or DVT.
  • Targeted and monitoring: MRI - Provides detailed imaging of the knee joint and identifies associated intra-articular pathology; Doppler ultrasound - Excludes DVT in cases.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Baker's Cyst"][/sc]
  • A 55-year-old woman with a history of knee osteoarthritis presents with gradual onset of posterior knee swelling and mild discomfort, worsened by prolonged standing.
  • Adult with symptom pattern and objective findings suggestive of baker's cyst, requiring focused confirmation and risk stratification.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Baker's Cyst"][/sc]
  • Deep Vein Thrombosis - Unilateral calf swelling, erythema, and warmth.
  • Popliteal artery aneurysm - Pulsatile mass, confirmed by Doppler ultrasound.
  • Soft tissue tumour - Solid mass with no fluctuation or transillumination.
  • Alternative serious diagnosis - Distinguishing history, examination, and targeted investigations do not support baker's cyst.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Baker's Cyst"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Assess physiological stability and urgent complications at presentation, and initiate early stabilisation where required.
  • Core care: Deliver condition-specific treatment for baker's cyst, address underlying drivers, and involve appropriate specialist support.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Reassess symptom trajectory, objective markers, and treatment response over time to detect complications early.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Worsening symptoms, physiological deterioration, or failure to improve should trigger urgent reassessment for severe or alternative pathology in baker's cyst.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Baker's Cyst"][/sc]
  • Cyst rupture - Leads to severe calf pain and swelling.
  • Neurovascular compression - Results in leg paraesthesia or venous insufficiency.
