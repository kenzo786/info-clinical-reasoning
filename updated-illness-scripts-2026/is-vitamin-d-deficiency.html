Vitamin D Deficiency
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Vitamin D Deficiency"][/sc]
  • Vitamin D deficiency is a condition where there is insufficient vitamin D in the body, leading to inadequate calcium absorption, impaired bone metabolism, and various other systemic effects.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise vitamin features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.

Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Vitamin D Deficiency"][/sc]
  • Vitamin D, primarily synthesised in the skin via sunlight (UVB), is converted to its active form, calcitriol, in the liver and kidneys. When deficient, there is reduced calcium absorption in the gut, leading to hypocalcaemia. This triggers an increase in parathyroid hormone (PTH), causing bone resorption to maintain calcium levels, resulting in bone demineralisation, muscle weakness, and impaired bone remodelling.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Vitamin D Deficiency"][/sc]
  • Early stage: Asymptomatic or mild non-specific symptoms like fatigue and muscle weakness over months to years.
  • Progressive phase: Development of bone pain, increased muscle weakness, and hypocalcaemic symptoms over several months.
  • Late stage: Persistent deficiency can result in osteomalacia, rickets in children, or more severe complications like fractures over years.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Vitamin D Deficiency"][/sc]
  • Ask about fatigue - Commonly reported due to impaired calcium metabolism affecting cellular energy processes.
  • Ask about muscle weakness - Due to reduced calcium influx into muscle cells, leading to diminished muscle contraction strength.
  • Ask about bone pain - Especially in weight-bearing areas, as bone demineralisation occurs and leads to softer bones.
  • Ask about hypocalcaemic symptoms (e.g., tetany, paraesthesia) - Low calcium levels cause neuromuscular irritability, manifesting in muscle spasms and tingling.
  • Ask about depression - Low vitamin D levels have been associated with mood disturbances due to its role in brain function.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Vitamin D Deficiency"][/sc]
  • Check myopathy - Proximal muscle weakness due to calcium dysregulation affecting muscle fibre function.
  • Check bone tenderness - Softened bones, especially in the ribs or long bones, can be painful on palpation.
  • Check gait disturbances - Difficulty walking or waddling gait due to muscle weakness and bone pain.
  • Check delayed tooth eruption (in children) - Impaired bone and tooth development caused by inadequate mineralisation.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Vitamin D Deficiency"][/sc]
  • Severe hypocalcaemia - Can present with seizures, laryngospasm, or cardiac arrhythmias, indicating dangerously low calcium levels due to vitamin D deficiency.
  • Fractures - Indicates advanced osteomalacia or osteoporosis due to prolonged deficiency.
  • Rickets (in children) - Severe bowing of the legs or growth retardation, signifying significant impairment of bone formation.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Vitamin D Deficiency"][/sc]
  • Asymptomatic deficiency - Many individuals may have no symptoms and only be diagnosed through routine blood tests showing low vitamin D levels.
  • Neurological symptoms - Some individuals may present with subtle cognitive disturbances or paraesthesia due to hypocalcaemia without classical bone or muscle symptoms.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask vitamin d deficiency.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Vitamin D Deficiency"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Serum 25-hydroxyvitamin D (25(OH)D) levels - Levels < 25 nmol/L indicate deficiency. 25(OH)D is the best marker of overall vitamin D.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Parathyroid hormone (PTH) - Elevated in response to hypocalcaemia as the body tries to maintain calcium levels through bone resorption; X-rays.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Vitamin D Deficiency"][/sc]
  • Hypoparathyroidism - Differentiated by low PTH levels, whereas vitamin D deficiency presents with elevated PTH.
  • Osteoporosis - Typically presents without muscle weakness or hypocalcaemia, primarily leading to fractures without biochemical abnormalities.
  • Chronic kidney disease - May present similarly with hypocalcaemia, but associated with elevated creatinine and phosphate levels due.
  • Related alternative diagnosis - Distinguish from vitamin d deficiency using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Vitamin D Deficiency"][/sc]
  • Fractures - Due to weakened bones and increased risk of osteoporosis and osteomalacia.
  • Cardiovascular disease - There is some evidence linking vitamin D deficiency to increased cardiovascular risk, possibly through effects on blood pressure regulation and inflammation.
  • Immune dysfunction - Increased susceptibility to infections due to vitamin D’s role in immune modulation.
