Sarcoidosis
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Sarcoidosis"][/sc]
  • Sarcoidosis is a multi-system granulomatous disorder of unknown aetiology, characterised by the formation of non-caseating granulomas in affected tissues.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise sarcoidosis features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.

Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Sarcoidosis"][/sc]
  • The precise cause of sarcoidosis is unknown, but it is believed to be an exaggerated immune response to an unknown antigen. The pathophysiology involves the formation of non-caseating granulomas, which are clusters of macrophages surrounded by lymphocytes. These granulomas can cause organ damage by disrupting normal tissue architecture and function. In the lungs, granuloma formation can lead to fibrosis over time, impairing respiratory function.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Sarcoidosis"][/sc]
  • Sarcoidosis typically follows one of two patterns: acute or chronic. The acute form may resolve spontaneously within 1-2 years, while chronic sarcoidosis progresses slowly, potentially leading to long-term organ damage. Acute presentations, such as Löfgren's syndrome, often have a good prognosis, while chronic sarcoidosis, especially with pulmonary involvement, may lead to irreversible fibrosis.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Sarcoidosis"][/sc]
  • Ask about fatigue - Common in over 70% of patients. It may result from chronic inflammation and immune activation.
  • Ask about dyspnoea (shortness of breath) - Caused by lung involvement due to granulomas disrupting normal lung architecture and leading to decreased gas exchange.
  • Ask about cough - A dry cough is common due to pulmonary involvement and irritation from granulomas.
  • Ask about chest pain - Can be pleuritic or due to mediastinal lymphadenopathy compressing surrounding structures.
  • Ask about skin lesions - Such as erythema nodosum, particularly in acute sarcoidosis like Löfgren’s syndrome. The pathophysiology involves immune complex deposition in the subcutaneous fat.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Sarcoidosis"][/sc]
  • Check bilateral hilar lymphadenopathy - Often seen on chest X-ray, due to granulomas in the mediastinal lymph nodes.
  • Check skin nodules (erythema nodosum) - Tender red nodules, often on the shins, caused by an inflammatory reaction in the subcutaneous fat.
  • Check crackles on lung auscultation - Reflects the presence of pulmonary involvement with granulomatous inflammation or fibrosis.
  • Check uveitis - Can present as eye redness, photophobia, and decreased vision due to granulomatous inflammation in the uveal tract.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Sarcoidosis"][/sc]
  • Severe dyspnoea and hypoxaemia - May indicate extensive pulmonary fibrosis or acute respiratory failure.
  • Heart block or arrhythmias - Suggest cardiac involvement, which occurs in about 5% of patients, but may lead to sudden cardiac death if untreated.
  • Neurological symptoms - Including weakness, facial nerve palsy, or seizures, indicating neurosarcoidosis, which can be a serious and potentially life-threatening complication.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Sarcoidosis"][/sc]
  • Isolated extrapulmonary involvement - Sarcoidosis can present without pulmonary symptoms, involving organs such as the skin, eyes, or heart. Lack of respiratory symptoms may delay diagnosis.
  • Chronic fatigue without other symptoms - Fatigue as a solitary symptom may be mistaken for other chronic conditions like depression or fibromyalgia.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask sarcoidosis.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Sarcoidosis"][/sc]
  • Tuberculosis - Differentiated by caseating granulomas on biopsy and a history of exposure or positive Mantoux.
  • Lymphoma - May present with similar lymphadenopathy, but systemic symptoms (fever, weight loss) are more prominent.
  • Hypersensitivity pneumonitis - Presents with similar lung symptoms but is linked to specific environmental exposures and lacks.
  • Related alternative diagnosis - Distinguish from sarcoidosis using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Sarcoidosis"][/sc]
  • Pulmonary fibrosis - Occurs in chronic cases, leading to irreversible lung damage and respiratory failure.
  • Cardiac sarcoidosis - Can cause arrhythmias, heart block, or heart failure.
  • Neurosarcoidosis - May lead to cranial nerve palsies, seizures, or even death if untreated.
  • Blindness - Due to untreated uveitis leading to optic nerve damage.
