Biliary Colic
Definition [sc name="ai-illness-script-section-links" Q="Biliary Colic" select="101" url-2="" css-2="" url-3="" css-3="" edit="886"][/sc] [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Biliary Colic"][/sc]
  • Biliary colic refers to episodic right upper abdominal pain caused by obstruction of the cystic duct or common bile duct by gallstones (cholelithiasis).
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise biliary features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Biliary Colic"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Biliary Colic"][/sc]
  • Risk Factors: Female sex, increasing age, obesity, rapid weight loss, pregnancy, a high-fat diet, and metabolic conditions such as diabetes and hyperlipidaemia increase the likelihood of gallstone formation. Genetic predisposition, liver disease, and use of oestrogen-containing medications (e.g., oral contraceptives, hormone replacement therapy) are also contributing factors.
  • Prevention: Maintaining a healthy weight, adopting a balanced diet low in saturated fats and high in fibre, regular physical activity, and avoiding rapid weight loss can help reduce the risk of gallstone formation. Managing underlying metabolic conditions such as diabetes and hyperlipidaemia can also decrease susceptibility.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ai-illness-script-section-links" Q="Biliary Colic" select="103" url-2="" css-2="" url-3="" css-3="" edit="886"][/sc] [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Biliary Colic"][/sc]
  • Gallstones form due to an imbalance in the composition of bile, particularly an increase in cholesterol or bilirubin concentration or decreased bile salts. These stones can migrate into the cystic duct or common bile duct. When a stone obstructs the cystic duct, the gallbladder contracts, causing increased pressure and pain. This usually occurs after meals, especially fatty foods, as the gallbladder is stimulated to release bile. The pain subsides when the stone moves back into the gallbladder or passes through the duct.
Time Course [sc name="ai-illness-script-section-links" Q="Biliary Colic" select="104" url-2="" css-2="" url-3="" css-3="" edit="886"][/sc] [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Biliary Colic"][/sc]
  • Acute phase: Biliary colic pain begins suddenly, typically after eating a fatty meal. The pain peaks within minutes and can last for 1-6 hours.
  • Intermittent phase: Between episodes, patients are generally asymptomatic. Frequency of episodes varies, with some experiencing only one or two episodes in a lifetime, while others may have recurrent episodes.

Red Flag Features [sc name="ai-illness-script-section-links" Q="Biliary Colic" select="107" url-2="" css-2="" url-3="" css-3="" edit="886"][/sc] [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Biliary Colic"][/sc]
  • Fever and rigors - May suggest the development of acute cholecystitis or cholangitis (infection of the bile ducts), which requires urgent medical attention.
  • Jaundice - Indicates potential obstruction of the common bile duct (choledocholithiasis), leading to complications such as ascending cholangitis or pancreatitis.
  • Persistent pain >6 hours - Suggests progression to acute cholecystitis, where inflammation of the gallbladder requires different management.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ai-illness-script-section-links" Q="Biliary Colic" select="108" url-2="" css-2="" url-3="" css-3="" edit="886"][/sc] [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Biliary Colic"][/sc]
  • Epigastric pain - Rather than RUQ pain, some patients may present with central epigastric pain, which can mimic peptic ulcer disease.
  • Silent gallstones - Many patients have gallstones without any symptoms, detected incidentally during imaging for other reasons.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features or comorbidity can mask the underlying diagnosis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ai-illness-script-section-links" Q="Biliary Colic" select="109" url-2="" css-2="" url-3="" css-3="" edit="886"][/sc] [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Biliary Colic"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Focused bedside assessment and first-line investigations to define immediate risk in suspected biliary colic.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Liver function tests (LFTs) - Typically normal in biliary colic, but elevated bilirubin and liver enzymes suggest common bile duct obstruction.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ai-illness-script-section-links" Q="Biliary Colic" select="111" url-2="" css-2="" url-3="" css-3="" edit="886"][/sc] [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Biliary Colic"][/sc]
  • Acute cholecystitis - Unlike biliary colic, this presents with prolonged pain, fever, and a positive Murphy's sign.
  • Peptic ulcer disease - Presents with epigastric pain, often relieved by eating or antacids, without relation to fatty.
  • Pancreatitis - Characterised by severe epigastric pain radiating to the back, associated with elevated serum amylase.
  • Alternative serious diagnosis - Distinguishing history, examination, and targeted investigations do not support biliary colic.

  • Immediate priorities: Assess physiological stability and urgent complications at presentation, and initiate early stabilisation where required.
  • Core care: Deliver condition-specific treatment for biliary colic, address underlying drivers, and involve appropriate specialist support.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Reassess symptom trajectory, objective markers, and treatment response over time to detect complications early.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Worsening symptoms, physiological deterioration, or failure to improve should trigger urgent reassessment for severe or alternative pathology in biliary colic.
Complications [sc name="ai-illness-script-section-links" Q="Biliary Colic" select="115" url-2="" css-2="" url-3="" css-3="" edit="886"][/sc] [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Biliary Colic"][/sc]
  • Acute cholecystitis - Occurs when a stone remains impacted, leading to inflammation and infection of the gallbladder.
  • Choledocholithiasis - Stone migration to the common bile duct, causing jaundice and potentially leading to cholangitis or pancreatitis.
  • Pancreatitis - Gallstones blocking the pancreatic duct can cause inflammation of the pancreas.
