Reactive Arthritis
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Reactive Arthritis"][/sc]
  • Reactive arthritis (ReA) is a seronegative spondyloarthritis that develops following an infection, most commonly affecting the genitourinary or gastrointestinal tract.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise reactive features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.

  • Higher-risk or missed groups: Reactive Arthritis may be missed when presentation is atypical or obscured by comorbidity.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Reactive Arthritis"][/sc]
  • Triggering infection leads to immune activation. Common infections include Chlamydia trachomatis, Salmonella, Shigella, Yersinia, and Campylobacter.
  • Genetic predisposition: HLA-B27 positive individuals are at increased risk, suggesting a link to antigen presentation and aberrant immune responses.
  • Molecular mimicry: Pathogen antigens may resemble self-antigens, triggering an autoimmune response.
  • Synovial inflammation occurs as immune cells infiltrate joints, leading to pain, swelling, and stiffness.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Reactive Arthritis"][/sc]
  • Acute phase: Develops 1–4 weeks after the inciting infection.
  • Subacute phase: Symptoms may persist for weeks to months if untreated.
  • Chronic phase: In a minority of cases, arthritis becomes persistent, leading to ongoing symptoms or progression to spondyloarthritis.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Reactive Arthritis"][/sc]
  • Ask about joint pain and swelling - Typically affects large weight-bearing joints (e.g., knees, ankles). Pathophysiology: Synovial inflammation due to immune complex deposition and immune cell.
  • Ask about urethritis - Dysuria or discharge may occur. Pathophysiology: Immune response to prior genitourinary infection.
  • Ask about ocular symptoms - Conjunctivitis or uveitis causing redness, pain, and photophobia. Pathophysiology: Inflammatory response in ocular tissues.
  • Ask about back pain - Suggestive of sacroiliitis in some cases. Pathophysiology: Enthesitis and inflammation of sacroiliac joints.
  • Ask about fatigue and malaise - Systemic inflammatory response contributing to constitutional symptoms.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Reactive Arthritis"][/sc]
  • Check joint swelling and tenderness - Observable in affected joints, indicating synovitis.
  • Check conjunctival injection - Redness of the conjunctiva, typical of conjunctivitis.
  • Check plantar fasciitis or Achilles tendonitis - Enthesitis presenting as localised tenderness.
  • Check nail changes - Hyperkeratosis or pitting, sometimes seen in associated conditions like psoriasis.
  • Check skin lesions - Keratoderma blennorrhagicum or circinate balanitis, often related to the underlying infection.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Reactive Arthritis"][/sc]
  • Severe back pain with neurological signs - May indicate sacroiliitis or spinal involvement requiring urgent intervention.
  • Vision loss or severe eye pain - Suggestive of uveitis; untreated cases can lead to vision-threatening complications.
  • Unexplained weight loss or fever - Could indicate an underlying systemic infection or another autoimmune condition.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Reactive Arthritis"][/sc]
  • Isolated sacroiliitis without peripheral arthritis - More common in chronic cases.
  • Predominantly ocular or skin involvement without joint symptoms - May delay diagnosis.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask reactive arthritis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Reactive Arthritis"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Focused bedside assessment and first-line testing to assess severity and immediate risk in suspected reactive arthritis.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Synovial fluid analysis - Excludes septic arthritis; findings include elevated white cells without organisms; Imaging - X-rays or MRI to detect.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Reactive Arthritis"][/sc]
  • Septic arthritis - Typically monoarticular with fever and positive synovial fluid culture.
  • Gout - Acute onset, monosodium urate crystals on joint aspiration.
  • Psoriatic arthritis - Joint and skin involvement, nail changes more common.
  • Ankylosing spondylitis - Chronic back pain and axial stiffness without prior infection.

  • Immediate priorities: Assess severity, physiological stability, and immediate complications.
  • Core care: Deliver condition-specific management for reactive arthritis, treat underlying drivers, and coordinate appropriate specialist input.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Reassess symptom trajectory, examination findings, and objective markers to confirm response in reactive arthritis.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Escalate urgently for clinical deterioration, evolving red flags, or failure to improve in suspected reactive arthritis.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Reactive Arthritis"][/sc]
  • Chronic arthritis - Persistent joint symptoms in a minority of cases.
  • Spondyloarthritis - Progression to axial involvement.
  • Vision loss - Due to untreated uveitis.
