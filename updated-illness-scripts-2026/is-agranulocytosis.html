Agranulocytosis
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Agranulocytosis"][/sc]
  • Agranulocytosis is a severe form of neutropenia, characterised by an absolute neutrophil count (ANC) of less than 0.5 x 10^9/L, leading to an increased risk of severe infections.
  • UKMLA essentials: fever with sore throat, hypotension or confusion in sepsis, urgent FBC with neutrophil count.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Agranulocytosis"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Agranulocytosis"][/sc]
  • Agranulocytosis illness script is encountered across acute and specialist care, with burden shaped by age, comorbidity, and exposure context.
  • Risk increases in patients with relevant predisposing disease, treatment exposures, or delayed access to care.
  • Missed or late recognition is more likely when presentations are atypical or overlap with common alternative diagnoses.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Agranulocytosis"][/sc]
  • Agranulocytosis occurs due to a significant reduction in neutrophils, primarily caused by DMARDs through two mechanisms:
    • Direct bone marrow suppression - Drugs like methotrexate inhibit folate metabolism, impairing DNA synthesis and suppressing myeloid progenitor cells in the bone marrow.
    • Immune-mediated destruction - Some DMARDs may trigger an autoimmune response, leading to the destruction of neutrophils in peripheral blood via antibody-mediated processes.
    The lack of neutrophils leaves patients vulnerable to infections, as neutrophils are critical for controlling bacterial and fungal infections.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Agranulocytosis"][/sc]
  • Agranulocytosis can develop acutely or insidiously:
    • Early phase: Asymptomatic in many cases, especially in mild neutropenia.
    • Acute phase: Once the ANC falls below 0.5 x 10^9/L, patients may develop severe infections, typically within weeks of DMARD initiation or dose escalation.
    • Recovery: Neutrophil counts usually return to normal within 1-3 weeks after discontinuing the offending drug and initiating treatment (e.g., granulocyte-colony stimulating factor [G-CSF]).
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Agranulocytosis"][/sc]
  • Ask about Fever - Most common presenting symptom due to the body's response to infection in the absence of neutrophils, which normally help control infections.
  • Ask about Fatigue - A general symptom associated with systemic infection or bone marrow suppression.
  • Ask about Mouth ulcers - Neutropenia impairs mucosal immunity, leading to mucositis and ulcer formation.
  • Ask about Sore throat - Neutrophil deficiency can lead to oropharyngeal infections, manifesting as a sore throat.
  • Consider asymptomatic neutropenia - Early or monitoring-detected neutropenia may be present before overt infective symptoms emerge.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Agranulocytosis"][/sc]
  • Check Fever - Common in the presence of infection due to impaired neutrophil response.
  • Check Oral ulcers - Inflammatory lesions in the mouth due to mucosal breakdown and secondary infection.
  • Check Pallor - Suggestive of anaemia or generalised bone marrow suppression.
  • Check Tachycardia - Reflective of systemic infection or sepsis.
  • Check No signs of infection - In cases of severe neutropenia, the body may fail to mount a typical inflammatory response, making infections harder to detect.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Agranulocytosis"][/sc]
  • Severe fever - May indicate neutropenic sepsis, which can rapidly progress to shock in agranulocytosis.
  • Hypotension - A sign of septic shock and impending cardiovascular collapse, requiring urgent intervention.
  • Confusion or altered consciousness - Indicates possible central nervous system involvement in sepsis, a late and life-threatening complication.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Agranulocytosis"][/sc]
  • Absence of fever - Some patients with agranulocytosis may not develop fever due to the lack of neutrophils required to mount an inflammatory response.
  • Non-specific symptoms - General malaise, weakness, and fatigue may occur without localising signs of infection, leading to diagnostic delays.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Agranulocytosis"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Full blood count (FBC) - Will show neutropenia, with an ANC <0.5 x 10^9/L. Pancytopenia may suggest more widespread bone marrow suppression.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Bone marrow biopsy - May be performed if the cause of agranulocytosis is unclear, showing hypocellularity and myeloid hypoplasia.; Blood cultures - To identify bacterial or fungal infections, as patients are at high risk of sepsis.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Agranulocytosis"][/sc]
  • A 62-year-old woman on methotrexate for rheumatoid arthritis presents with a 3-day history of fever, mouth ulcers, and fatigue. Blood tests reveal severe neutropenia (ANC 0.3 x 10^9/L).
  • Immunosuppressed adult with fever, mucosal ulceration, and profound neutropenia, concerning for agranulocytosis with high sepsis risk.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Agranulocytosis"][/sc]
  • Viral infections (e.g., Epstein-Barr virus, HIV) - Differentiated by lymphocytosis on FBC rather than neutropenia.
  • Aplastic anaemia - Bone marrow suppression leads to pancytopenia rather than isolated neutropenia.
  • Leukaemia - Characterised by blasts on blood smear and bone marrow infiltration on biopsy.
  • Autoimmune neutropenia - Chronic isolated neutropenia with autoimmune features and less acute sepsis presentation supports this alternative.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Agranulocytosis"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Stop likely causative myelotoxic drugs and treat any fever as neutropenic sepsis until proven otherwise.
  • Core care: Start urgent broad-spectrum antimicrobial cover with haematology input, and consider growth-factor support when clinically indicated.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Serial FBC, cultures, observations, and organ-function tests are required until neutrophil recovery and infection control are established.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Haemodynamic instability, persistent fever, organ dysfunction, or worsening neutropenia should trigger urgent senior and critical care escalation.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Agranulocytosis"][/sc]
  • Sepsis - Life-threatening infection due to lack of neutrophils, leading to multi-organ failure and death in severe cases.
  • Prolonged hospitalisation - Often required due to the need for isolation and close monitoring for infections.
  • Mortality - Can occur if agranulocytosis is complicated by uncontrolled infection or sepsis.
