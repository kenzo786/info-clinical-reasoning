Autonomic Neuropathy
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Autonomic Neuropathy"][/sc]
  • Autonomic neuropathy refers to damage to the autonomic nervous system (ANS), which controls involuntary functions like heart rate, blood pressure, digestion, and temperature regulation.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise autonomic features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.

Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Autonomic Neuropathy"][/sc]
  • The autonomic nervous system has two components: the sympathetic and parasympathetic systems. Autonomic neuropathy occurs when these systems are damaged, usually due to chronic hyperglycaemia in diabetes, inflammatory processes, or toxins. This leads to impaired neural transmission and dysregulation of involuntary functions. In diabetes, prolonged hyperglycaemia causes microvascular damage, affecting the small nerve fibres that supply autonomic organs. Other causes include autoimmune disorders, amyloidosis, and chronic alcoholism, which can lead to direct nerve damage.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Autonomic Neuropathy"][/sc]
  • Early Phase: Subclinical autonomic dysfunction may occur without overt symptoms.
  • Progressive Phase: Symptoms gradually appear and worsen as the neuropathy progresses. This may take months to years, depending on the underlying cause.
  • Chronic Phase: Long-standing autonomic neuropathy leads to severe, irreversible organ dysfunction (e.g., gastroparesis, orthostatic hypotension), significantly impacting quality of life.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Autonomic Neuropathy"][/sc]
  • Ask about orthostatic hypotension - Dizziness or light-headedness when standing due to impaired sympathetic regulation of blood pressure.
  • Ask about gastroparesis - Nausea, vomiting, and bloating caused by delayed gastric emptying from vagus nerve damage.
  • Ask about urinary retention - Difficulty initiating urination and poor bladder emptying due to autonomic dysfunction of the bladder.
  • Ask about constipation or diarrhoea - Gastrointestinal dysmotility, especially in diabetes, results from enteric nervous system impairment.
  • Ask about anhidrosis - Lack of sweating due to disruption of sympathetic nerve control of sweat glands.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Autonomic Neuropathy"][/sc]
  • Check postural hypotension - A drop in blood pressure upon standing due to impaired baroreceptor reflexes.
  • Check loss of heart rate variability - A blunted response in heart rate during deep breathing, indicative of parasympathetic dysfunction.
  • Check absent sweating - A localised or generalised lack of sweating, reflecting sympathetic denervation of sweat glands.
  • Check bladder distension - Enlarged bladder due to impaired bladder emptying and urinary retention.
  • Check skin changes - Dry or cracked skin due to impaired sweating and autonomic control of skin hydration.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Autonomic Neuropathy"][/sc]
  • Severe orthostatic hypotension - May lead to syncope or falls, indicating widespread autonomic failure.
  • Cardiac autonomic dysfunction - Unexplained resting tachycardia or bradycardia can lead to sudden cardiac death.
  • Intractable gastroparesis - Severe nausea, vomiting, and malnutrition, indicating progressive autonomic dysfunction.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Autonomic Neuropathy"][/sc]
  • Asymptomatic presentation - In early stages, patients may have no overt symptoms despite significant autonomic dysfunction, often detected during routine diabetic follow-up.
  • Fluctuating symptoms - Some patients may have intermittent symptoms such as occasional dizziness or gastrointestinal discomfort, leading to delayed diagnosis.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features or comorbidity can mask the underlying diagnosis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Autonomic Neuropathy"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Autonomic function tests - Tilt table test, Valsalva manoeuvre, and deep breathing tests assess the autonomic reflexes. Pathologically, these reveal diminished.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Gastric emptying study - Used to confirm gastroparesis by showing delayed gastric emptying; Urodynamic testing - Assesses bladder function and identifies.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Autonomic Neuropathy"][/sc]
  • Parkinson's disease - Can present with autonomic dysfunction, but would be distinguished by motor symptoms like tremor.
  • Amyloidosis - Presents with autonomic dysfunction but may include additional features such as peripheral neuropathy, heart.
  • Multiple system atrophy - Features early autonomic failure but is associated with rapid progression and Parkinsonism.
  • Chronic alcoholism - May cause autonomic dysfunction but usually with clear history of alcohol abuse and peripheral.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Autonomic Neuropathy"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Assess physiological stability and urgent complications at presentation, and initiate early stabilisation where required.
  • Core care: Deliver condition-specific treatment for autonomic neuropathy, address underlying drivers, and involve appropriate specialist support.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Reassess symptom trajectory, objective markers, and treatment response over time to detect complications early.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Worsening symptoms, physiological deterioration, or failure to improve should trigger urgent reassessment for severe or alternative pathology in autonomic neuropathy.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Autonomic Neuropathy"][/sc]
  • Falls and fractures - Due to severe orthostatic hypotension leading to syncope.
  • Cardiovascular death - Sudden death may result from cardiac autonomic dysfunction, including arrhythmias.
  • Malnutrition - Due to severe gastroparesis leading to poor oral intake.
