Cholesteatoma
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Cholesteatoma"][/sc]
  • Cholesteatoma is a non-cancerous growth of keratinizing squamous epithelium in the middle ear or mastoid.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise cholesteatoma features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.

Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Cholesteatoma"][/sc]
  • Cholesteatoma forms due to the accumulation of squamous epithelium in the middle ear space. The primary mechanism involves eustachian tube dysfunction leading to negative middle ear pressure, retraction of the tympanic membrane, and the trapping of keratin debris. Over time, the trapped debris forms a cyst, which can become infected and erode surrounding structures such as the ossicles, facial nerve, or even the skull base.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Cholesteatoma"][/sc]
  • Cholesteatoma typically develops slowly over months to years. In its early stages, it may be asymptomatic or present with mild hearing loss. Over time, as the cyst grows, more significant symptoms develop, such as chronic ear drainage, more profound hearing loss, and complications like facial nerve palsy or intracranial infections.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Cholesteatoma"][/sc]
  • Ask about hearing loss - Typically conductive hearing loss occurs as the growing cholesteatoma destroys ossicles or obstructs the middle ear. This is due to impaired.
  • Ask about chronic ear discharge (otorrhoea) - Persistent, often foul-smelling discharge is due to secondary bacterial infection within the cholesteatoma.
  • Ask about ear fullness - Patients may experience a feeling of pressure or fullness due to middle ear inflammation or obstruction by the cholesteatoma.
  • Ask about tinnitus - Ringing or buzzing in the ear can result from damage to the middle or inner ear structures.
  • Ask about pain - Typically mild or absent unless there is secondary infection or bone erosion. When present, it may indicate an advanced or complicated case.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Cholesteatoma"][/sc]
  • Check retracted tympanic membrane - A retracted or perforated tympanic membrane is often visible on otoscopy. This results from negative middle ear pressure, which pulls.
  • Check granulation tissue or a pearly white mass - Visible in the ear canal or behind a perforated tympanic membrane, this is the cholesteatoma itself.
  • Check conductive hearing loss - Detected on examination with a tuning fork test (Weber and Rinne). It occurs when sound transmission through the middle ear.
  • Check foul-smelling otorrhoea - Thick, malodorous discharge is often seen in cases with infection or advanced disease.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Cholesteatoma"][/sc]
  • Facial nerve palsy - Indicates possible erosion of the facial nerve canal by the cholesteatoma, which can lead to paralysis if untreated.
  • Dizziness or vertigo - Suggests involvement of the inner ear or labyrinth, possibly indicating progression towards labyrinthine fistula or erosion of the semicircular canals.
  • Headache, fever, neurological symptoms - These could indicate intracranial extension, such as meningitis or brain abscess, which are life-threatening complications.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Cholesteatoma"][/sc]
  • Asymptomatic cases, particularly in early stages or in elderly patients, can present only with mild hearing loss and may lack the classic discharge or pain, leading to a delayed diagnosis.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features or comorbidity can mask the underlying diagnosis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Cholesteatoma"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Otoscopy - Visualises retraction pockets, perforation, or the presence of cholesteatoma. Essential for diagnosis, revealing the characteristic pearly mass.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Audiometry - Typically shows conductive hearing loss, though sensorineural hearing loss may occur in advanced cases with inner ear involvement; CT.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Cholesteatoma"][/sc]
  • A 45-year-old male presents with a 6-month history of progressive hearing loss in the left ear, associated with intermittent foul-smelling discharge and a sensation of fullness in the ear. Otoscopy.
  • Adult with symptom pattern and objective findings suggestive of cholesteatoma, requiring focused confirmation and risk stratification.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Cholesteatoma"][/sc]
  • Chronic Otitis Media - Differentiate from cholesteatoma using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.
  • Acute Otitis Media - More acute onset of pain and discharge, usually associated with systemic symptoms like fever.
  • Otosclerosis - Gradual conductive hearing loss without discharge or tympanic membrane changes. Typically seen in younger.
  • Alternative serious diagnosis - Distinguishing history, examination, and targeted investigations do not support cholesteatoma.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Cholesteatoma"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Assess physiological stability and urgent complications at presentation, and initiate early stabilisation where required.
  • Core care: Deliver condition-specific treatment for cholesteatoma, address underlying drivers, and involve appropriate specialist support.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Reassess symptom trajectory, objective markers, and treatment response over time to detect complications early.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Worsening symptoms, physiological deterioration, or failure to improve should trigger urgent reassessment for severe or alternative pathology in cholesteatoma.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Cholesteatoma"][/sc]
  • Hearing loss - Conductive or mixed hearing loss due to ossicular destruction or damage to the cochlea.
  • Labyrinthine fistula - Erosion into the inner ear leading to dizziness or vertigo.
  • Meningitis or brain abscess - Intracranial extension of infection can lead to these life-threatening complications.
