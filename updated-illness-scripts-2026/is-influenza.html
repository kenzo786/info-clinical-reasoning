Influenza illness script
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Influenza"][/sc]
  • Influenza is an acute viral respiratory illness caused by influenza viruses (mainly types A and B).
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise influenza features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Influenza"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Influenza"][/sc]
  • Globally, seasonal influenza epidemics cause 3-5 million severe cases and 290,000-650,000 deaths annually, according to the World Health Organization (WHO).
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Influenza"][/sc]
  • Influenza virus is transmitted via respiratory droplets. After entry, the virus binds to and invades the epithelial cells of the upper and lower respiratory tract through haemagglutinin proteins.
  • Once inside the host cells, viral replication occurs, leading to direct cellular injury and triggering the release of pro-inflammatory cytokines. This immune response is responsible for many of the systemic symptoms like fever and malaise.
  • The virus's destruction of the respiratory epithelium predisposes patients to secondary bacterial infections, which can lead to pneumonia and other complications.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Influenza"][/sc]
  • Incubation Period: 1-4 days after exposure.
  • Acute Phase: Symptoms typically last for 3-7 days, with fever and systemic symptoms peaking within the first 2-3 days.
  • Recovery Phase: Full recovery generally occurs within 1-2 weeks, though cough and fatigue may persist for several weeks.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Influenza"][/sc]
  • Ask about fever (often >38°C) - Due to cytokine release during the immune response to viral infection.
  • Ask about chills - Associated with the body's effort to raise core temperature during fever.
  • Ask about myalgia - Caused by the systemic inflammatory response and cytokine release.
  • Ask about headache - Linked to inflammatory mediators and fever.
  • Ask about cough (usually dry) - Results from irritation and inflammation of the respiratory epithelium.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Influenza"][/sc]
  • Check fever (>38°C) - Elevated body temperature is a common sign due to the body's immune response.
  • Check tachypnoea - Increased respiratory rate as a result of hypoxia or respiratory distress from lung involvement.
  • Check tachycardia - Elevated heart rate, often due to fever and dehydration.
  • Check rhonchi or wheezing - Indicates lower respiratory tract involvement or secondary bronchitis.
  • Check pharyngeal erythema - Inflammation of the throat due to viral replication and immune response.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Influenza"][/sc]
  • Shortness of breath - May indicate viral pneumonia or secondary bacterial pneumonia.
  • Haemoptysis - Could signify severe lower respiratory tract infection or secondary complications.
  • Confusion - May reflect sepsis, hypoxia, or neurological involvement such as encephalitis.
  • Severe chest pain - Could indicate cardiovascular complications such as myocarditis or acute coronary syndromes.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Influenza"][/sc]
  • Elderly patients: May present with atypical features such as confusion or fatigue rather than fever and respiratory symptoms, due to altered immune response and comorbidities.
  • Immunocompromised patients: May have milder or absent symptoms despite significant viral replication due to blunted immune response.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask influenza illness script.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Influenza"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Focused bedside assessment and first-line testing to assess severity and immediate risk in suspected influenza illness script.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Full blood count (FBC): May show leukopenia or lymphopenia, indicating viral infection; Chest X-ray: Performed if pneumonia is suspected, showing patchy.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Influenza"][/sc]
  • Common cold (rhinovirus) - Typically milder, with predominant nasal congestion and rhinorrhoea rather than high fever and systemic.
  • Pneumonia - May present with similar respiratory symptoms but often includes pleuritic chest pain, productive cough.
  • COVID-19 - May present with fever, cough, and fatigue, but often includes anosmia (loss of smell).
  • Bronchitis - Similar cough and fatigue but often without the systemic features of influenza like high.

  • Immediate priorities: Assess severity, physiological stability, and immediate complications.
  • Core care: Deliver condition-specific management for influenza illness script, treat underlying drivers, and coordinate appropriate specialist input.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Reassess symptom trajectory, examination findings, and objective markers to confirm response in influenza illness script.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Escalate urgently for clinical deterioration, evolving red flags, or failure to improve in suspected influenza illness script.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Influenza"][/sc]
  • Pneumonia: Can be viral or secondary bacterial. Common pathogens include Streptococcus pneumoniae and Staphylococcus aureus.
  • Acute respiratory distress syndrome (ARDS): Severe lung inflammation leading to respiratory failure.
  • Myocarditis: Inflammation of the heart muscle, potentially leading to heart failure or arrhythmias.
  • Encephalitis: Inflammation of the brain, presenting with confusion, seizures, or altered consciousness.
