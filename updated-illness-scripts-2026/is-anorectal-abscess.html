Anorectal Abscess
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Anorectal Abscess"][/sc]
  • An anorectal abscess is a collection of pus in the anorectal region, typically resulting from an infection in the anal glands, crypts, or surrounding tissue.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise anorectal features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Anorectal Abscess"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Anorectal Abscess"][/sc]
  • Risk factors: Risk of anorectal abscess rises with relevant predisposition, comorbidity, and exposure profile.
  • Higher-risk or missed groups: Anorectal Abscess may be missed when presentations are atypical or comorbidity obscures key features.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Anorectal Abscess"][/sc]
  • The pathophysiology of anorectal abscess begins with obstruction of the anal crypt glands. This blockage leads to bacterial proliferation, most commonly involving Staphylococcus aureus, Escherichia coli, and anaerobic organisms. The infection progresses as pus accumulates, causing tissue destruction and formation of an abscess cavity. If untreated, the infection can spread to the surrounding perianal spaces or result in the formation of a fistula.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Anorectal Abscess"][/sc]
  • Acute phase: Symptoms typically begin over a few days to a week, with increasing pain and swelling in the anorectal region.
  • Chronic phase: Without treatment, some abscesses may persist or lead to complications such as fistula formation, which can result in chronic, recurrent infection.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Anorectal Abscess"][/sc]
  • Ask about perianal pain - Localised, throbbing pain that worsens with sitting or defecation, due to pressure and inflammation from the abscess.
  • Ask about swelling - A palpable lump in the perianal or rectal area as a result of the abscess formation.
  • Ask about fever - Systemic signs of infection, often due to bacteraemia or the body’s response to localised infection.
  • Ask about pus drainage - In cases where the abscess spontaneously ruptures, patients may notice purulent discharge from the anal region.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Anorectal Abscess"][/sc]
  • Check perianal swelling - Visible or palpable swelling in the perianal or ischiorectal area due to pus accumulation.
  • Check erythema - Redness overlying the abscess, indicating inflammation and infection of the skin.
  • Check tenderness - Marked tenderness on palpation of the affected area due to the pressure from the abscess.
  • Check fluctuance - A soft, compressible area on palpation, characteristic of a fluid-filled abscess cavity.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Anorectal Abscess"][/sc]
  • Severe systemic symptoms (fever, chills) - May indicate sepsis, a life-threatening complication of the infection spreading systemically.
  • Spreading erythema or induration - Suggests cellulitis or progression of the infection into deeper tissues.
  • Necrotising fasciitis - Rapidly progressing pain, necrosis, and skin changes could indicate this severe, life-threatening infection.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Anorectal Abscess"][/sc]
  • Deep-seated abscesses (e.g., supralevator abscesses) may present with vague lower abdominal pain or discomfort rather than typical perianal symptoms.
  • Immunocompromised patients (e.g., diabetics, HIV) may present with less pronounced symptoms due to a blunted immune response.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features or comorbidity can mask the underlying diagnosis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Anorectal Abscess"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Clinical examination - Often sufficient for diagnosis. Inspection and digital rectal exam may reveal tenderness, swelling, and fluctuance; Ultrasound - Performed.
  • Targeted and monitoring: MRI or CT scan - Used for complex, recurrent, or deep abscesses to assess the extent and rule out fistula formation.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Anorectal Abscess"][/sc]
  • A 40-year-old man presents with a 3-day history of severe perianal pain, swelling, and fever. Examination reveals erythema, swelling, and tenderness in the perianal region with fluctuance.
  • Adult with symptom pattern and objective findings suggestive of anorectal abscess, requiring focused confirmation and risk stratification.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Anorectal Abscess"][/sc]
  • Pilonidal abscess - Occurs in the sacrococcygeal area, unlike anorectal abscesses, which are perianal.
  • Hidradenitis suppurativa - Differentiate using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.
  • Fistula-in-ano - Can present similarly if infected, but fistulas have an external opening and may have.
  • Perirectal cellulitis - Diffuse infection of the perianal tissue without a defined abscess cavity.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Anorectal Abscess"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Assess physiological stability and immediate high-risk complications at presentation, with prompt stabilisation where required.
  • Core care: Provide condition-specific management for anorectal abscess, treat underlying drivers, and coordinate multidisciplinary care.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Reassess symptoms, objective markers, and functional impact over time to confirm response and detect complications early.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Urgent reassessment is required for worsening symptoms, physiological deterioration, or failure to improve with initial management of anorectal abscess.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Anorectal Abscess"][/sc]
  • Anorectal fistula - Occurs in about 30-50% of cases, developing as a communication between the infected crypt gland and the skin surface.
  • Sepsis - A rare but serious complication, especially in immunocompromised patients or if the abscess is left untreated.
  • Recurrent abscess - Inadequate drainage or untreated fistula can lead to recurrence.
