Huntington's Disease
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Huntington's Disease"][/sc]
  • Huntington's disease (HD) is an autosomal dominant neurodegenerative disorder characterised by progressive motor, cognitive, and psychiatric symptoms.

Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Huntington's Disease"][/sc]
  • Huntington's disease is caused by an expanded CAG trinucleotide repeat in the HTT gene, resulting in a mutant huntingtin protein with a toxic gain of function.
  • The huntingtin protein accumulation leads to neuronal dysfunction and cell death, particularly affecting the striatum and cortex, areas crucial for movement control and cognition.
  • Degeneration of medium spiny neurons in the striatum disrupts basal ganglia circuits, impairing motor function and leading to chorea and other movement disorders.
  • Cognitive and psychiatric symptoms arise from progressive cortical atrophy and neurotransmitter imbalances, including dopamine, glutamate, and GABA dysfunction.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Huntington's Disease"][/sc]
  • Huntington's disease progresses through pre-symptomatic, early, middle, and late stages over 10-20 years.
  • Pre-symptomatic: Motor and cognitive changes are minimal or absent, though genetic testing may reveal the mutation.
  • Early stage: Subtle motor symptoms (mild chorea), mood changes, and cognitive decline begin to manifest.
  • Middle stage: Motor symptoms (chorea, dystonia) worsen, and cognitive decline and behavioural disturbances become more pronounced.
  • Late stage: Severe motor impairment (rigidity, bradykinesia), profound cognitive impairment, and high dependency on care. Death typically results from complications such as aspiration pneumonia.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Huntington's Disease"][/sc]
  • Ask about chorea - Involuntary, jerky movements primarily in the face, limbs, and trunk, caused by basal ganglia dysfunction due to striatal neuron loss.
  • Ask about behavioural changes - Includes irritability, impulsivity, and mood swings, due to changes in cortical and subcortical structures.
  • Ask about cognitive decline - Progressively impaired memory, attention, and executive functions, reflecting cortical and subcortical atrophy.
  • Ask about dysphagia - Difficulty swallowing due to motor dysfunction, increasing risk of aspiration.
  • Ask about depression and anxiety - Common early signs, often related to altered brain chemistry and functional changes.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Huntington's Disease"][/sc]
  • Check motor incoordination - Unsteady gait and poor balance due to basal ganglia and cerebellar involvement.
  • Check dysarthria - Slurred speech stemming from motor dysfunction affecting speech muscles.
  • Check oculomotor dysfunction - Abnormal eye movements, especially difficulty with saccades, due to striatal and cortical degeneration.
  • Check bradykinesia - Slowness of movement, often in late-stage disease, due to advanced striatal degeneration.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Huntington's Disease"][/sc]
  • Rapid cognitive or motor decline - May indicate disease acceleration, increased risk of dependency, and complications.
  • Severe psychiatric symptoms - Psychosis, aggression, or suicidal ideation, potentially leading to self-harm or harm to others.
  • Dysphagia with frequent aspiration - Increases the risk of aspiration pneumonia, a common cause of death in advanced HD.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Huntington's Disease"][/sc]
  • Juvenile onset HD - May present with rigidity, seizures, and parkinsonism rather than chorea, due to a more extensive CAG repeat expansion.
  • Lack of family history - In rare cases, HD may occur sporadically or due to a de novo mutation, potentially delaying diagnosis.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask huntington's disease.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Huntington's Disease"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Genetic testing - Confirms diagnosis with CAG repeat expansion in HTT gene (>39 repeats typically diagnostic).
  • Targeted and monitoring: Neuroimaging (MRI or CT) - Shows atrophy of the caudate nucleus and cortical structures in advanced cases; Neuropsychological testing - Assesses.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Huntington's Disease"][/sc]
  • Parkinson’s disease - Tremor and bradykinesia without chorea; usually responds to dopamine replacement therapy.
  • Wilson's disease - Distinguish from huntington's disease using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.
  • Sydenham's chorea - Primarily occurs in younger patients following streptococcal infection, with transient symptoms.
  • Related alternative diagnosis - Distinguish from huntington's disease using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Huntington's Disease"][/sc]
  • Aspiration pneumonia - Common in late-stage HD due to dysphagia, contributing to mortality.
  • Suicidality - Increased risk due to psychiatric symptoms and awareness of disease progression.
  • Falls and fractures - Due to chorea and impaired balance, leading to significant morbidity.
