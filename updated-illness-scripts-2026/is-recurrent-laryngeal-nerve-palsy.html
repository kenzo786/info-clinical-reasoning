Recurrent Laryngeal Nerve (RLN) Palsy
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Recurrent Laryngeal Nerve (RLN) Palsy"][/sc]
  • Recurrent Laryngeal Nerve (RLN) palsy refers to dysfunction of the recurrent branch of the vagus nerve (cranial nerve X), which innervates the intrinsic muscles of the larynx (except the cricothyroid).
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise recurrent features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.

Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Recurrent Laryngeal Nerve (RLN) Palsy"][/sc]
  • The recurrent laryngeal nerve arises from the vagus nerve and loops around the aortic arch (left side) or subclavian artery (right side), ascending to innervate the larynx. Damage to this nerve disrupts motor signals to the laryngeal muscles, resulting in vocal cord paralysis. Causes include trauma during surgery (thyroid, parathyroid, or oesophageal), malignancy, or prolonged intubation. Compression by a tumour, aneurysm, or lymph node enlargement can also affect the nerve, particularly on the left side due to its longer course.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Recurrent Laryngeal Nerve (RLN) Palsy"][/sc]
  • Acute: Symptoms such as hoarseness or breathing difficulties may appear immediately after surgical trauma or nerve compression.
  • Subacute: Gradual onset of symptoms over weeks, often due to neoplastic or inflammatory processes.
  • Chronic: Long-standing RLN palsy can lead to permanent vocal cord dysfunction and possible airway compromise.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Recurrent Laryngeal Nerve (RLN) Palsy"][/sc]
  • Ask about hoarseness - Caused by unilateral vocal cord paralysis, preventing proper adduction during phonation.
  • Ask about breathiness - Results from incomplete closure of the glottis, allowing air to escape during speech.
  • Ask about dyspnoea - In bilateral RLN palsy, both vocal cords may be paralysed in a partially closed position, leading to airway obstruction.
  • Ask about dysphagia - Dysfunction of the laryngeal muscles can impair the swallowing mechanism, leading to aspiration risk.
  • Ask about cough - A weak or ineffective cough may occur due to reduced glottic closure during forced expiration.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Recurrent Laryngeal Nerve (RLN) Palsy"][/sc]
  • Check unilateral vocal cord paralysis - Observed during laryngoscopy, with one vocal cord remaining immobile during breathing and phonation.
  • Check bilateral vocal cord paralysis - Both vocal cords remain immobile, often in a midline or near-midline position, resulting in stridor and breathing difficulties.
  • Check stridor - High-pitched sound during inspiration, more common in bilateral palsy due to partial airway obstruction.
  • Check voice changes - Weak, breathy, or hoarse voice, indicating incomplete vocal cord closure.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Recurrent Laryngeal Nerve (RLN) Palsy"][/sc]
  • Severe dyspnoea/stridor - Indicates potential bilateral vocal cord paralysis, requiring urgent airway management.
  • Progressive dysphagia or weight loss - May suggest malignancy compressing the RLN, particularly in the context of neoplastic causes.
  • Pain associated with voice changes - Uncommon in RLN palsy; suggests malignancy or invasive pathology such as an apical lung tumour (Pancoast tumour).
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Recurrent Laryngeal Nerve (RLN) Palsy"][/sc]
  • Intermittent hoarseness - May occur in RLN palsy secondary to recurrent compression, as in cases of positional or vascular anomalies.
  • Isolated cough without voice changes - Rare, but can happen in early stages or in cases where the nerve's motor function is more affected than sensory input.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask recurrent laryngeal nerve (rln) palsy.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Recurrent Laryngeal Nerve (RLN) Palsy"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Flexible laryngoscopy - Direct visualisation of vocal cord paralysis or paresis. One or both cords may appear immobile.
  • Targeted and monitoring: CT/MRI of the neck and thorax - Used to identify causes such as tumours or aortic arch abnormalities compressing the nerve.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Recurrent Laryngeal Nerve (RLN) Palsy"][/sc]

  • Airway obstruction - In bilateral palsy, airway obstruction can result in stridor and respiratory distress, requiring urgent intervention.
