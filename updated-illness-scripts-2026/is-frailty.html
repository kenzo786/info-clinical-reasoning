Frailty
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Frailty"][/sc]
  • Frailty is a clinical syndrome characterised by a reduced physiological reserve and diminished resistance to stressors, increasing vulnerability to adverse outcomes like falls, hospitalisation, and mortality.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise frailty features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Frailty"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Frailty"][/sc]
  • Risk factors: Risk factors for frailty include relevant predisposition, comorbidity, and exposure history.
  • Higher-risk or missed groups: Frailty may be missed when presentation is atypical or obscured by comorbidity.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Frailty"][/sc]
  • Frailty involves complex, multi-system pathophysiology affecting muscles, the cardiovascular system, immunity, and metabolism. Chronic inflammation (elevated IL-6, TNF-α) leads to sarcopenia and physical decline. Mitochondrial dysfunction impairs cellular energy production, while hormonal changes (decreased oestrogen/testosterone) contribute to muscle wasting. These systemic alterations diminish overall resilience to stressors.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Frailty"][/sc]
  • Frailty typically progresses gradually over years, though acute events like infection or injury can accelerate deterioration. Phases range from prefrailty (mild weakness) to moderate frailty (reduced independence) and severe frailty (high dependence and frequent adverse events).
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Frailty"][/sc]
  • Ask about fatigue - Persistent tiredness is common due to reduced metabolic efficiency and inflammatory responses impairing muscle function.
  • Ask about muscle weakness - Caused by sarcopenia and reduced muscle mass, limiting physical abilities and daily functioning.
  • Ask about weight loss - Often seen due to decreased appetite and muscle catabolism, affecting overall body mass.
  • Ask about mobility issues - Limited physical activity and gait changes from musculoskeletal and neurological impairments.
  • Ask about low mood or cognitive decline - Depression and cognitive impairment are common due to neuroinflammatory changes and social isolation.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Frailty"][/sc]
  • Check slowness in gait - Reduced walking speed reflects decreased muscle power and energy levels.
  • Check unintentional weight loss - Indicates nutritional deficits and muscle wasting, contributing to vulnerability.
  • Check reduced grip strength - A key indicator of sarcopenia and frailty-related physical weakness.
  • Check postural instability - A result of weakened muscles and balance issues, increasing fall risk.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Frailty"][/sc]
  • Frequent falls - Signifies severe frailty and high fall risk, often leading to fractures and hospitalisation.
  • Severe weight loss - Indicates potential for malnutrition, immune dysfunction, and increased mortality risk.
  • Confusion or delirium - May point to cognitive decline or infection, particularly sepsis, in frail patients.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Frailty"][/sc]
  • Some individuals may present with isolated symptoms like minor cognitive changes, apathy, or social withdrawal before physical signs appear, due to subtle systemic inflammatory effects on the brain.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask frailty.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Frailty"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Clinical Frailty Scale (CFS) - Grades frailty from 1 (very fit) to 9 (terminally ill); scores ≥5 indicate frailty; Grip strength.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Timed Up and Go (TUG) Test - Assesses mobility; times >12 seconds suggest frailty; Blood tests - Inflammatory markers (CRP, IL-6).

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Frailty"][/sc]
  • Sarcopenia - Distinguished by muscle wasting without broader systemic frailty signs.
  • Parkinson’s disease - Presents with tremor, rigidity, and bradykinesia, not typical in frailty.
  • Hypothyroidism - Fatigue and weight gain; lacks frailty’s hallmark inflammatory profile.
  • Related alternative diagnosis - Distinguish from frailty using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Frailty"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Assess severity, physiological stability, and immediate complications.
  • Core care: Deliver condition-specific management for frailty, treat underlying drivers, and coordinate appropriate specialist input.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Reassess symptom trajectory, examination findings, and objective markers to confirm response in frailty.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Escalate urgently for clinical deterioration, evolving red flags, or failure to improve in suspected frailty.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Frailty"][/sc]
  • Falls and fractures - Common and often lead to hospitalisation; related to muscle weakness and balance issues.
  • Hospitalisation and dependency - Increased risk due to recurrent illness and injury; often leads to further functional decline.
  • Infection risk - Due to immune dysfunction, leading to increased morbidity and mortality.
