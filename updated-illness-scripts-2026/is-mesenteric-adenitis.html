Mesenteric Adenitis
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Mesenteric Adenitis"][/sc]
  • Mesenteric adenitis is an inflammatory condition affecting the lymph nodes in the mesentery, most commonly in the right lower quadrant of the abdomen.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise mesenteric features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Mesenteric Adenitis"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Mesenteric Adenitis"][/sc]
  • UK GP Population: Mesenteric adenitis is relatively common in children presenting with abdominal pain but is less frequently diagnosed in primary care as symptoms often resolve without intervention.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Mesenteric Adenitis"][/sc]
  • Mesenteric adenitis involves inflammation of mesenteric lymph nodes, usually secondary to a viral infection. Pathogens like adenovirus initiate a lymphoproliferative response as the immune system targets the infecting virus. This results in enlarged, tender lymph nodes that can cause abdominal pain and other systemic symptoms.
  • The nodes’ location in the mesentery near the ileum can lead to localised pain that may resemble appendicitis. The inflammatory process typically resolves as the immune system clears the infection.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Mesenteric Adenitis"][/sc]
  • Acute Phase: Symptoms usually begin suddenly, with abdominal pain and sometimes fever. This phase can last 3-10 days.
  • Resolution Phase: Symptoms often gradually resolve as the infection is cleared, typically within two weeks.
  • Prolonged Phase: Rare; persistent symptoms may indicate complications or alternative diagnoses.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Mesenteric Adenitis"][/sc]
  • Ask about right Lower Quadrant Abdominal Pain - Often the primary symptom; occurs due to inflammation of the lymph nodes near the ileum.
  • Ask about fever - Low-grade fever may occur due to the underlying viral infection triggering an immune response.
  • Ask about nausea and Vomiting - Likely due to the abdominal pain and associated gastrointestinal inflammation.
  • Ask about general Malaise - Fatigue and weakness may accompany systemic inflammation from the infection.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Mesenteric Adenitis"][/sc]
  • Check tenderness in Right Lower Quadrant - Reflects inflammation and swelling in mesenteric lymph nodes, leading to localised tenderness.
  • Check mildly Elevated Temperature - Low-grade fever from systemic response to the infection.
  • Check guarding - Involuntary tightening of abdominal muscles over the affected area due to pain.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Mesenteric Adenitis"][/sc]
  • High Fever (>39°C) - May indicate bacterial superinfection or other serious intra-abdominal pathology.
  • Severe or Persistent Pain - Could signify complications or alternative diagnoses like appendicitis or intussusception.
  • Rebound Tenderness - Suggests peritoneal irritation and raises suspicion for appendicitis or other surgical conditions.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Mesenteric Adenitis"][/sc]
  • Mesenteric adenitis can occasionally present with generalised abdominal pain or symptoms mimicking viral gastroenteritis, leading to a delayed diagnosis or misdiagnosis, especially if fever and leukocytosis are mild.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask mesenteric adenitis.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Mesenteric Adenitis"][/sc]
  • Appendicitis - Differentiated by more severe pain, rebound tenderness, and often a higher fever and white.
  • Gastroenteritis - Tends to cause diffuse abdominal pain, with prominent diarrhoea and vomiting.
  • Intussusception - Presents with colicky abdominal pain, “red currant jelly” stools, and a palpable abdominal mass.
  • Pelvic Inflammatory Disease - In adolescent females; presents with pelvic pain, vaginal discharge, and may include fever.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Mesenteric Adenitis"][/sc]
  • Persistent Abdominal Pain - Rarely, pain may persist, requiring further investigation to exclude other causes.
  • Superimposed Bacterial Infection - Occurs if bacterial pathogens infect inflamed lymph nodes, leading to abscess formation.
