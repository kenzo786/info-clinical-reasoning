Ovarian Cyst
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Ovarian Cyst"][/sc]

Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Ovarian Cyst"][/sc]
  • Ovarian cysts form when fluid accumulates within a sac in the ovary. Functional cysts develop due to normal hormonal variations in the menstrual cycle:
    • Follicular cysts form when a follicle does not release its egg and continues to grow.
    • Corpus luteum cysts occur when the corpus luteum fails to degenerate after ovulation, accumulating fluid.
  • Pathological cysts, such as dermoid cysts or endometriomas, arise from abnormal cellular growth or other underlying conditions, including endometriosis or infection.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Ovarian Cyst"][/sc]
  • Functional cysts typically resolve within 1-3 menstrual cycles without intervention.
  • Pathological cysts may persist or grow and can require surgical intervention.
  • Cysts associated with malignancy, while rare, can progress over months to years and often present later in the disease process.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Ovarian Cyst"][/sc]
  • Ask about asymptomatic - Many ovarian cysts, especially functional ones, do not cause symptoms and are often found incidentally during imaging.
  • Ask about pelvic pain - May be dull or sharp and localised to the side of the cyst due to stretching or rupture. Rupture of, with associated clinical features.
  • Ask about abdominal bloating or distension - Large cysts can press on surrounding organs, leading to discomfort or a feeling of fullness.
  • Ask about changes in menstrual cycle - Hormone-secreting cysts may cause irregular periods or heavier bleeding.
  • Ask about pain during intercourse (dyspareunia) - Occurs when a cyst causes irritation or pressure on the reproductive organs.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Ovarian Cyst"][/sc]
  • Check pelvic mass on palpation - An ovarian cyst may be palpable if large, but smaller cysts are typically not detectable on physical examination.
  • Check tenderness on abdominal or pelvic examination - Tenderness may suggest cyst rupture or torsion.
  • Check signs of peritonitis - If a cyst ruptures or torsion occurs, the patient may present with guarding, rebound tenderness, and other signs of acute.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Ovarian Cyst"][/sc]
  • Severe acute abdominal pain - May indicate ovarian torsion or cyst rupture, both of which are surgical emergencies.
  • Unexplained weight loss and early satiety - These symptoms raise suspicion for malignancy, particularly ovarian cancer.
  • Postmenopausal ovarian cysts - Postmenopausal women with ovarian cysts have a higher risk of malignancy, requiring urgent evaluation.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Ovarian Cyst"][/sc]
  • Chronic pelvic pain without mass - Cysts may present as a source of chronic pelvic discomfort without any acute symptoms or palpable mass, often related to endometriomas.
  • Urinary or gastrointestinal complaints - Atypical presentations may involve symptoms such as constipation or urinary retention due to large cysts exerting pressure on surrounding structures.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask ovarian cyst.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Ovarian Cyst"][/sc]
  • Ectopic pregnancy - Presents with pelvic pain and amenorrhoea; positive pregnancy test differentiates it.
  • Endometriosis - Characterised by chronic pelvic pain and dysmenorrhoea; endometriomas can be mistaken for ovarian cysts.
  • Pelvic inflammatory disease (PID) - Distinguish from ovarian cyst using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.
  • Ovarian torsion - Distinguish from ovarian cyst using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Ovarian Cyst"][/sc]
  • Ovarian torsion - The twisting of the ovary or cyst causes acute ischaemia. Requires urgent surgery to prevent loss of the ovary.
  • Cyst rupture - Can cause internal bleeding and peritonitis, presenting as an acute abdomen.
  • Malignant transformation - Rare, but some cysts can develop into ovarian cancer, particularly in postmenopausal women.
