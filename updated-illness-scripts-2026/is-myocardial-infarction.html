Myocardial Infarction (MI)
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Myocardial Infarction (MI)"][/sc]
  • Myocardial infarction (MI), commonly referred to as a heart attack, is the irreversible necrosis of cardiac muscle resulting from prolonged ischaemia.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise myocardial features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Myocardial Infarction (MI)"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Myocardial Infarction (MI)"][/sc]
  • Risk Factors: Major risk factors include hypertension, hyperlipidaemia, diabetes mellitus, smoking, obesity, sedentary lifestyle, and family history of premature coronary artery disease (CAD). Additional risk factors include chronic kidney disease, excessive alcohol consumption, and psychosocial stress. Emerging risk factors such as inflammatory conditions (e.g., rheumatoid arthritis) and air pollution have also been implicated.
  • Prevention: Primary prevention strategies focus on lifestyle modifications such as smoking cessation, regular physical activity, maintaining a healthy diet (e.g., Mediterranean diet), and weight management. Pharmacological interventions like statins for hyperlipidaemia, antihypertensives for hypertension, and glycaemic control in diabetics are key in reducing MI risk. Secondary prevention includes the use of aspirin, beta-blockers, ACE inhibitors, and statins in patients with established coronary artery disease to prevent recurrent events.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Myocardial Infarction (MI)"][/sc]
  • Step 1: Atherosclerosis leads to the formation of plaques in coronary arteries.
  • Step 2: Plaque rupture exposes the underlying subendothelial tissue, promoting platelet aggregation and activation of the clotting cascade.
  • Step 3: A thrombus forms, occluding the artery, and reducing blood flow to the myocardium.
  • Step 4: Prolonged ischaemia results in myocyte injury and irreversible necrosis, typically starting in the subendocardium and extending transmurally if untreated.
  • Step 5: Necrotic tissue releases cardiac biomarkers (e.g., troponins), which are detectable in the blood.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Myocardial Infarction (MI)"][/sc]
  • Acute Phase: Ischaemia and infarction occur within minutes to hours. If untreated, necrosis progresses transmurally.
  • Subacute Phase: Over days to weeks, the infarcted myocardium begins healing through scar tissue formation, but this can lead to complications like left ventricular (LV) remodelling.
  • Chronic Phase: Weeks to months after MI, patients may develop heart failure or arrhythmias as long-term sequelae of infarcted myocardial tissue.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Myocardial Infarction (MI)"][/sc]
  • Ask about chest Pain - Typically retrosternal, crushing or pressure-like pain that radiates to the left arm, neck, or jaw. It results from myocardial ischaemia triggering.
  • Ask about shortness of Breath - Due to impaired left ventricular function leading to pulmonary congestion and decreased oxygen delivery.
  • Ask about nausea and Vomiting - Often a result of vagal stimulation during an acute coronary event.
  • Ask about diaphoresis - Sympathetic nervous system activation in response to the stress of the MI.
  • Ask about fatigue or Weakness - Caused by reduced cardiac output, leading to decreased tissue perfusion.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Myocardial Infarction (MI)"][/sc]
  • Check tachycardia - Sympathetic activation and compensatory increase in heart rate to maintain cardiac output.
  • Check hypotension - May indicate cardiogenic shock or severe left ventricular dysfunction.
  • Check s3 or S4 Heart Sounds - Suggestive of left ventricular dysfunction or stiffening due to ischaemia or infarction.
  • Check crackles on Lung Auscultation - Due to pulmonary oedema from left-sided heart failure.
  • Check cool, Clammy Skin - Due to sympathetic vasoconstriction as a compensatory response to low cardiac output.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Myocardial Infarction (MI)"][/sc]
  • Sudden Severe Chest Pain - May indicate massive MI or aortic dissection. Requires immediate intervention.
  • Syncope - Suggestive of severe ischaemia affecting electrical conduction (e.g., arrhythmia) or cardiac output.
  • Persistent Hypotension - May indicate cardiogenic shock, a life-threatening complication requiring urgent intervention.
  • New Onset Heart Failure - Signifies significant myocardial damage and poor cardiac function.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Myocardial Infarction (MI)"][/sc]
  • Silent MI - Especially in older adults and diabetics, MI may present without classic chest pain but rather with fatigue, weakness, or even confusion due to autonomic dysfunction.
  • Epigastric Pain - Some patients, particularly women, may experience indigestion-like discomfort rather than typical chest pain, due to referred pain patterns.
  • Dyspnoea without Chest Pain - In some cases, especially in the elderly, the primary symptom may be shortness of breath due to heart failure without the classic pain.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask myocardial infarction (mi).
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Myocardial Infarction (MI)"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: ECG - ST-segment elevation indicates an acute transmural infarction, while ST depression or T wave inversion suggests ischaemia or non-ST elevation.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Coronary Angiography - Identifies the location and extent of coronary artery occlusion. It’s typically performed in acute settings to guide revascularisation.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Myocardial Infarction (MI)"][/sc]
  • Heart Failure - Occurs due to impaired ventricular function and remodelling post-infarction. May be chronic or acute (e.g., cardiogenic shock).
  • Arrhythmias - Ventricular fibrillation or tachycardia is common post-MI and may result from ischaemia-induced conduction abnormalities.
  • Pericarditis - Inflammation of the pericardium may occur as part of Dressler’s syndrome, an immune-mediated response weeks after MI.
  • Left Ventricular Aneurysm - Scar tissue from a large infarction can form an aneurysm, increasing the risk of thromboembolism or heart failure.
