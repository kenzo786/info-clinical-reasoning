Mallory-Weiss Tear
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Mallory-Weiss Tear"][/sc]
  • A Mallory-Weiss tear is a longitudinal mucosal laceration typically located at the gastro-oesophageal junction.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise mallory features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.

Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Mallory-Weiss Tear"][/sc]
  • Repeated vomiting, retching, or physical strain increases intra-abdominal pressure, which is transmitted to the gastro-oesophageal junction.
  • The mucosa at this junction undergoes sudden distension and shearing forces, leading to a tear.
  • This laceration may involve submucosal vessels, resulting in bleeding. Blood loss can vary from minimal to severe, depending on the extent and depth of the tear.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Mallory-Weiss Tear"][/sc]
  • Acute onset: The condition presents abruptly after an episode of vomiting, retching, or other strenuous activity.
  • Resolution: With appropriate management, minor tears often heal spontaneously within a few days. Severe cases with significant bleeding may require urgent intervention.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Mallory-Weiss Tear"][/sc]
  • Ask about haematemesis - The most common symptom, presenting as fresh blood or “coffee-ground” emesis due to bleeding at the tear site.
  • Ask about epigastric pain - Typically occurs due to the tear in the gastro-oesophageal junction.
  • Ask about retching or vomiting preceding the haematemesis - Often reported as the triggering event leading to the tear.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Mallory-Weiss Tear"][/sc]
  • Check haematemesis on inspection - Visible blood in vomit or “coffee-ground” appearance, indicating an upper GI bleed.
  • Check tachycardia - Reflects blood loss and compensatory response to maintain cardiac output.
  • Check hypotension in severe cases - Indicates significant blood loss, potentially signalling shock.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Mallory-Weiss Tear"][/sc]
  • Hypotension or syncope - Suggests significant blood loss and risk of haemorrhagic shock.
  • Melena or haemodynamic instability - Indicates a potential ongoing or more severe bleed.
  • Failure to respond to conservative treatment - May signal a more severe tear or need for endoscopic intervention.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Mallory-Weiss Tear"][/sc]
  • Minimal or no haematemesis - Occurs if bleeding is minor or the tear is limited to the mucosal layer without vascular involvement.
  • Isolated syncope or weakness - Can occur in older patients with severe bleeding but without overt haematemesis.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask mallory-weiss tear.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Mallory-Weiss Tear"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Upper GI endoscopy - Identifies the location and severity of the tear; a visible mucosal laceration with or without active bleeding.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Full blood count (FBC) - May show anaemia in cases of significant blood loss; Blood urea nitrogen (BUN) - Elevated levels.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Mallory-Weiss Tear"][/sc]
  • Peptic ulcer disease - Typically presents with melena and epigastric pain; bleeding is often slower than in Mallory-Weiss.
  • Oesophageal varices - Common in patients with liver cirrhosis and manifests with massive haematemesis without preceding vomiting.
  • Gastric erosions - Distinguish from mallory-weiss tear using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Mallory-Weiss Tear"][/sc]
  • Recurrent bleeding - Occurs in approximately 5-10% of cases; typically within the first 48 hours of initial presentation.
  • Anaemia - Due to blood loss; may develop if the bleeding is recurrent or significant.
  • Haemorrhagic shock - Seen in severe cases with substantial blood loss; requires urgent intervention for stabilisation.
