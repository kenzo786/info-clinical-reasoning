Ocular Tumour
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Ocular Tumour"][/sc]
  • An ocular tumour is an abnormal growth of tissue within or around the eye.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise ocular features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Ocular Tumour"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Ocular Tumour"][/sc]
  • Metastatic ocular tumours are more frequent overall, especially in adults with known systemic cancers such as breast or lung cancer.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Ocular Tumour"][/sc]
  • Ocular tumours arise due to genetic mutations leading to uncontrolled cellular proliferation. For example, mutations in the GNAQ or GNA11 genes are commonly seen in uveal melanoma.
  • Benign tumours like naevi result from localised hyperplasia of pigment cells without invasive potential.
  • Malignant tumours may invade local tissues (e.g., retina, optic nerve) or metastasise systemically via haematogenous routes.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Ocular Tumour"][/sc]
  • Benign tumours may remain stable or grow slowly over decades.
  • Malignant tumours typically exhibit a faster progression, with symptoms developing over weeks to months.
  • Metastatic tumours may present acutely, especially in patients with advanced systemic cancer.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Ocular Tumour"][/sc]
  • Ask about blurred vision - Caused by tumour growth affecting retinal structures or compressing the optic nerve.
  • Ask about floaters - Result from vitreous involvement or retinal detachment secondary to tumour expansion.
  • Ask about visual field defects - Occur due to localised tumour growth impinging on specific visual pathways.
  • Ask about asymptomatic presentation - Common in small, benign tumours detected incidentally during routine eye exams.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Ocular Tumour"][/sc]
  • Check pigmented lesion on the retina or iris - Often indicative of uveal melanoma or naevi.
  • Check proptosis - Seen in large tumours or orbital involvement.
  • Check decreased visual acuity - Reflects macular or optic nerve involvement.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Ocular Tumour"][/sc]
  • Rapid visual loss - Suggests aggressive malignancy or retinal detachment.
  • Ocular pain - Indicates possible tumour invasion into surrounding structures.
  • New onset of floaters and photopsia - May signal retinal involvement or detachment.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Ocular Tumour"][/sc]
  • Small naevi or early melanoma presenting with subtle visual field defects detected only on perimetry testing.
  • Non-pigmented tumours mimicking inflammatory or infectious processes.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask ocular tumour.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Ocular Tumour"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Ophthalmoscopy - Reveals pigmented or vascularised lesions; Ultrasound B-scan - Helps assess lesion size, shape, and internal reflectivity.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Fundus photography - Provides baseline imaging for monitoring; Biopsy - Confirms diagnosis if malignancy is suspected.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Ocular Tumour"][/sc]
  • Choroidal haemangioma - Usually more vascular on imaging, non-pigmented.
  • Retinal detachment - Typically lacks mass effect and may present with a "curtain falling" sensation.
  • Metastatic tumours - History of systemic cancer, often multiple lesions.
  • Related alternative diagnosis - Distinguish from ocular tumour using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.

  • Escalation and safety-netting: Escalate urgently for clinical deterioration, evolving red flags, or failure to improve in suspected ocular tumour.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Ocular Tumour"][/sc]
  • Vision loss - Due to retinal damage or optic nerve involvement.
  • Metastasis - Common with malignant tumours, especially uveal melanoma.
  • Secondary glaucoma - From tumour growth obstructing aqueous humour outflow.
