Lipoma
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Lipoma"][/sc]
  • A lipoma is a benign tumour composed of mature adipocytes (fat cells) that typically develops in the subcutaneous tissue.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise lipoma features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Lipoma"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Lipoma"][/sc]
  • Risk factors: Risk factors for lipoma include relevant predisposition, comorbidity, and exposure history.
  • Higher-risk or missed groups: Lipoma may be missed when presentation is atypical or obscured by comorbidity.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Lipoma"][/sc]
  • Lipomas arise from the uncontrolled proliferation of mature fat cells (adipocytes) in the subcutaneous or deep tissues. While the exact cause is unclear, genetic factors and trauma are considered potential contributors. Most lipomas are encapsulated, separating them from the surrounding tissues.
  • Gene mutations, such as those involving chromosome 12q, are thought to be linked to the development of lipomas in some cases.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Lipoma"][/sc]
  • Lipomas are typically slow-growing and may take several months or years to become noticeable.
  • Most lipomas remain stable once they reach a certain size, but some may continue to grow slowly over time.
  • Rarely, lipomas may enlarge rapidly, raising concern for malignancy (liposarcoma).
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Lipoma"][/sc]
  • Ask about asymptomatic - Most lipomas do not cause symptoms and are discovered incidentally during physical examination.
  • Ask about painless lump - The most common symptom is the presence of a soft, painless mass, typically located in the subcutaneous tissues.
  • Ask about discomfort or tenderness - Occasionally, lipomas may cause discomfort, especially if they compress nearby nerves or are located in areas of repetitive trauma.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Lipoma"][/sc]
  • Check soft, mobile mass - Lipomas are typically soft and easily movable under the skin.
  • Check well-demarcated edges - The margins of a lipoma are usually well-defined, which helps distinguish it from other masses.
  • Check non-tender - Lipomas are generally non-tender to palpation unless they are compressing nearby structures.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Lipoma"][/sc]
  • Rapid growth - A rapidly enlarging mass may raise suspicion for malignancy, such as a liposarcoma.
  • Pain or tenderness - While lipomas are usually painless, new-onset pain may suggest a complication or malignancy.
  • Fixed, immobile mass - A lipoma that becomes fixed and immobile could indicate invasion into surrounding tissues, which may suggest malignancy.
  • Ulceration or skin changes - These features may indicate malignancy and warrant further investigation.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Lipoma"][/sc]
  • Deep-seated lipomas - These lipomas occur beneath the muscle layers and can be mistaken for other soft-tissue tumours. They may cause more significant discomfort or even functional impairment.
  • Multiple lipomas - In conditions like familial multiple lipomatosis, patients may present with numerous lipomas, which can be confused with other conditions like neurofibromatosis.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask lipoma.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Lipoma"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Ultrasound - Typically shows a homogenous, hyperechoic mass, confirming the diagnosis of a lipoma.
  • Targeted and monitoring: MRI - Used in cases where deeper tissue involvement is suspected or to distinguish from liposarcoma. Lipomas appear as well-circumscribed, homogenous.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Lipoma"][/sc]
  • Liposarcoma - Unlike lipomas, liposarcomas are malignant and may present with rapid growth, pain, and invasion.
  • Sebaceous cyst - Distinguish from lipoma using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.
  • Fibroma - A benign fibrous tumour that may feel firmer and less mobile than a lipoma.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Lipoma"][/sc]
  • Recurrence - Rare after complete excision, but lipomas may recur if not fully removed.
  • Cosmetic concerns - Large lipomas or those located in visible areas may cause cosmetic distress for patients.
  • Malignant transformation - Extremely rare, but a lipoma that undergoes rapid growth or becomes painful should be investigated for possible liposarcoma.
