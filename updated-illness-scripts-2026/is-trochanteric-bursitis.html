Trochanteric Bursitis
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Trochanteric Bursitis"][/sc]
  • Trochanteric bursitis is the inflammation of the trochanteric bursa, a fluid-filled sac located near the greater trochanter of the femur.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise trochanteric features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.

Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Trochanteric Bursitis"][/sc]
  • Microtrauma or overuse leads to inflammation of the bursa.
  • Compression from surrounding muscles, particularly the iliotibial band, exacerbates the inflammatory response.
  • Degenerative changes in surrounding tendons (e.g., gluteus medius and minimus tendons) may contribute to symptom development.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Trochanteric Bursitis"][/sc]
  • Acute phase: Pain develops over days to weeks, often after increased activity or trauma.
  • Subacute phase: Symptoms persist for weeks to months if untreated.
  • Chronic phase: Long-standing pain with associated muscle weakness or gait changes.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Trochanteric Bursitis"][/sc]
  • Ask about lateral hip pain - Commonest symptom, due to inflammation of the bursa compressing adjacent nerve endings.
  • Ask about pain worsened by lying on the affected side - Pressure on the inflamed bursa exacerbates discomfort.
  • Ask about pain exacerbated by prolonged walking, climbing stairs, or rising from a seated position - Movements that involve the iliotibial band or gluteal muscles increase.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Trochanteric Bursitis"][/sc]
  • Check tenderness over the greater trochanter - Localised inflammation causes sensitivity to palpation.
  • Check reduced range of motion in the hip - Pain limits active movement, particularly abduction and extension.

Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Trochanteric Bursitis"][/sc]
  • Fever or systemic symptoms - May indicate septic bursitis, a serious infection requiring immediate intervention.
  • Severe, unrelenting pain - Could suggest malignancy or a fracture.
  • Neurological symptoms - Numbness or weakness might indicate nerve impingement or other neurological pathology.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Trochanteric Bursitis"][/sc]
  • Referred pain to the knee or buttock, mimicking sciatica or lumbar radiculopathy.
  • Minimal or no tenderness at the greater trochanter in chronic cases with predominant gait issues.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask trochanteric bursitis.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Trochanteric Bursitis"][/sc]
  • Hip osteoarthritis - Pain more generalised to the groin and exacerbated by weight-bearing.
  • Lumbar radiculopathy - Pain radiates below the knee with neurological findings.
  • Gluteus medius tendinopathy - Localised tenderness but less swelling compared to bursitis.
  • Related alternative diagnosis - Distinguish from trochanteric bursitis using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Trochanteric Bursitis"][/sc]
  • Chronic pain: Can lead to functional limitations and reduced quality of life.
  • Muscle atrophy: Secondary to disuse or prolonged pain-related immobility.
