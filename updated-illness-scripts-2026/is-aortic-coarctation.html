Aortic Coarctation
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Aortic Coarctation"][/sc]
  • Aortic coarctation is a congenital narrowing of the aorta, most commonly just distal to the origin of the left subclavian artery at the site of the ductus arteriosus.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise aortic features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Aortic Coarctation"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Aortic Coarctation"][/sc]
  • It is more common in males than females (2:1 ratio).
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Aortic Coarctation"][/sc]
  • The narrowing in the aorta increases the resistance to blood flow, leading to elevated pressures proximal to the constriction (head, neck, and upper extremities) and reduced perfusion distal to it (abdomen and lower extremities).
  • Left ventricular hypertrophy develops as a compensatory mechanism due to increased afterload.
  • Collateral circulation develops over time via intercostal arteries, which helps bypass the constriction and supply blood to the lower body.
  • If untreated, long-term consequences include hypertension, heart failure, and risk of rupture or dissection of the aorta.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Aortic Coarctation"][/sc]
  • Neonatal Period: Severe coarctation may present in the first few weeks of life with heart failure and shock as the ductus arteriosus closes.
  • Childhood: Mild cases may be asymptomatic, and symptoms often develop gradually as the body compensates with collateral circulation.
  • Adulthood: Undiagnosed cases can present later with hypertension, exertional leg fatigue, or heart failure.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Aortic Coarctation"][/sc]
  • Ask about leg Claudication or Fatigue - Reduced blood flow to the lower body leads to leg pain or fatigue during exercise, particularly in older children.
  • Ask about headaches - Caused by upper body hypertension due to increased pressure proximal to the coarctation.
  • Ask about failure to Thrive - Seen in infants with severe coarctation as poor perfusion limits oxygen and nutrient delivery to vital organs.
  • Ask about exertional Dyspnoea - Increased workload on the heart due to higher afterload can cause heart failure symptoms, including shortness of breath on exertion.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Aortic Coarctation"][/sc]
  • Check blood Pressure Discrepancy - Hypertension in the arms with lower blood pressure in the legs due to the obstruction in the aorta.
  • Check weak Femoral Pulses - Reduced perfusion to the lower extremities leads to weak or absent femoral pulses.
  • Check systolic Murmur - A harsh systolic murmur heard over the left infrascapular region due to turbulent blood flow across the coarctation.
  • Check left Ventricular Heave - Due to left ventricular hypertrophy, the apex beat may be displaced and more forceful.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Aortic Coarctation"][/sc]
  • Shock in Newborns - Critical coarctation can present with shock as the ductus arteriosus closes, leading to sudden heart failure and multi-organ dysfunction.
  • Rib Notching on X-ray - This suggests long-standing coarctation with collateral circulation through intercostal arteries, often associated with severe disease.
  • Severe Hypertension - May indicate long-standing untreated coarctation and is a risk factor for aortic rupture or dissection.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Aortic Coarctation"][/sc]
  • Asymptomatic Hypertension - Some individuals present later in life with isolated high blood pressure, with no other symptoms until the coarctation is found incidentally during investigation.
  • Sudden Cardiac Death - Rare, but untreated severe coarctation can lead to hypertensive crises, aortic rupture, or heart failure, resulting in sudden death.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features or comorbidity can mask the underlying diagnosis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Aortic Coarctation"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Blood Pressure Measurement - Detects hypertension in the arms and a significant blood pressure gradient between the arms and legs; Chest.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Targeted investigations to confirm diagnosis, assess complications, and monitor response in aortic coarctation.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Aortic Coarctation"][/sc]
  • A 10-year-old boy presents with headaches, leg pain during exercise, and a history of hypertension. Physical examination reveals a blood pressure discrepancy between the arms and legs, and weak femoral.
  • Adult with symptom pattern and objective findings suggestive of aortic coarctation, requiring focused confirmation and risk stratification.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Aortic Coarctation"][/sc]
  • Hypertrophic Cardiomyopathy - Presents with exertional dyspnoea and left ventricular hypertrophy but lacks the blood pressure gradient.
  • Renal Artery Stenosis - Can cause secondary hypertension, but typically presents without the pulse or murmur findings associated.
  • Takayasu Arteritis - A vasculitis affecting large arteries, leading to limb claudication and blood pressure discrepancies, but.
  • Alternative serious diagnosis - Distinguishing history, examination, and targeted investigations do not support aortic coarctation.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Aortic Coarctation"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Assess physiological stability and urgent complications at presentation, and initiate early stabilisation where required.
  • Core care: Deliver condition-specific treatment for aortic coarctation, address underlying drivers, and involve appropriate specialist support.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Reassess symptom trajectory, objective markers, and treatment response over time to detect complications early.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Worsening symptoms, physiological deterioration, or failure to improve should trigger urgent reassessment for severe or alternative pathology in aortic coarctation.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Aortic Coarctation"][/sc]
  • Aortic Aneurysm or Dissection - A long-standing coarctation may weaken the aortic wall, increasing the risk of dissection or rupture.
  • Heart Failure - Due to chronic left ventricular hypertrophy and increased afterload, untreated cases may develop heart failure.
  • Stroke - Severe hypertension can lead to an increased risk of intracranial haemorrhage or stroke, especially in older individuals.
