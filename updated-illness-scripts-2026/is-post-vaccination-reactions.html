Post-Vaccination Reactions
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Post-Vaccination Reactions"][/sc]
  • Post-vaccination reactions refer to the spectrum of immune and non-immune responses occurring after the administration of a vaccine.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise post features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.

Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Post-Vaccination Reactions"][/sc]
  • Vaccines stimulate the immune system to produce a protective response without causing the disease. This involves activation of innate immune cells and subsequent adaptive immune responses.
  • Common reactions, such as fever or soreness, are mediated by inflammatory cytokines like IL-6 and TNF-α.
  • Rare adverse events, such as myocarditis, may involve immune cross-reactivity or dysregulated immune responses.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Post-Vaccination Reactions"][/sc]
  • Immediate phase: Reactions such as syncope or anaphylaxis occur within minutes to hours of vaccination.
  • Early phase: Common symptoms like fever, fatigue, and soreness typically appear within 24–48 hours and resolve within 2–3 days.
  • Delayed phase: Rare events like myocarditis or Guillain-Barré syndrome may develop days to weeks post-vaccination.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Post-Vaccination Reactions"][/sc]
  • Ask about injection site pain - Due to localised inflammation and cytokine release at the site of injection.
  • Ask about fever - A systemic response to cytokine release as part of the immune activation process.
  • Ask about fatigue - Associated with systemic inflammatory cytokines such as IL-6.
  • Ask about headache - May be related to cytokine-mediated inflammation or dehydration post-vaccination.
  • Ask about muscle aches - Reflect systemic inflammatory response and local immune activation.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Post-Vaccination Reactions"][/sc]
  • Check localised redness and swelling - Indicates inflammation due to immune activation.
  • Check mild lymphadenopathy - Reflects immune response and lymphatic involvement.
  • Check elevated temperature - Suggests systemic inflammatory cytokine release.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Post-Vaccination Reactions"][/sc]
  • Difficulty breathing - May indicate anaphylaxis, a medical emergency due to histamine release.
  • Chest pain - Could suggest myocarditis or pericarditis; inflammation of the heart tissues.
  • Severe weakness or paralysis - May signify Guillain-Barré syndrome, a rare post-vaccination autoimmune condition.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Post-Vaccination Reactions"][/sc]
  • Asymptomatic course - Common, particularly with mild local or systemic reactions.
  • Delayed hypersensitivity reactions - Skin rashes or other immune-mediated effects days after vaccination.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask post-vaccination reactions.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Post-Vaccination Reactions"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Full blood count (FBC) - May show elevated white cell count if inflammation is present.
  • Targeted and monitoring: C-reactive protein (CRP) - Elevated in systemic inflammatory responses; Troponin levels - Raised in myocarditis cases.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Post-Vaccination Reactions"][/sc]
  • Viral infection - Differs by prolonged symptoms and additional findings like sore throat.
  • Allergic reaction - Presents immediately with urticaria and wheezing.
  • Autoimmune conditions - Rarely, mimics vaccination reactions but typically has a more chronic course.
  • Related alternative diagnosis - Distinguish from post-vaccination reactions using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Post-Vaccination Reactions"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Assess severity, physiological stability, and immediate complications.
  • Core care: Deliver condition-specific management for post-vaccination reactions, treat underlying drivers, and coordinate appropriate specialist input.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Reassess symptom trajectory, examination findings, and objective markers to confirm response in post-vaccination reactions.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Escalate urgently for clinical deterioration, evolving red flags, or failure to improve in suspected post-vaccination reactions.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Post-Vaccination Reactions"][/sc]
  • Anaphylaxis - Occurs in 1 in a million doses; caused by severe IgE-mediated reaction.
  • Myocarditis - Occurs in approximately 1 in 50,000 young males, associated with immune cross-reactivity.
