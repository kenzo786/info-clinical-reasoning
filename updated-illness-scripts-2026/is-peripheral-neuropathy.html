Peripheral Neuropathy
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Peripheral Neuropathy"][/sc]
  • Peripheral neuropathy is a disorder that affects the peripheral nerves, resulting in sensory, motor, or autonomic dysfunction.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise peripheral features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.

Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Peripheral Neuropathy"][/sc]
  • The underlying mechanism involves damage to the peripheral nerves, either through direct axonal injury or demyelination. This can be caused by metabolic dysfunction (e.g., in diabetes), toxins (e.g., alcohol or chemotherapy), immune-mediated attacks (e.g., Guillain-Barré syndrome), or inherited disorders (e.g., Charcot-Marie-Tooth disease). Axonal damage impairs the transmission of electrical signals, while demyelination slows conduction.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Peripheral Neuropathy"][/sc]
  • Peripheral neuropathy can develop acutely (over days to weeks) in conditions such as Guillain-Barré syndrome, subacutely (weeks to months) in autoimmune or toxin-induced causes, or chronically (months to years) in conditions like diabetes or hereditary neuropathies. Chronic cases may present insidiously, making early detection difficult.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Peripheral Neuropathy"][/sc]
  • Ask about distal paraesthesia (tingling or numbness) – Most commonly presents in a “glove and stocking” distribution. This results from the longer peripheral nerves being more.
  • Ask about burning pain or dysesthesia – Caused by abnormal activation of pain pathways due to nerve damage, leading to spontaneous firing or altered signal processing.
  • Ask about muscle weakness – Occurs when motor nerves are affected, often leading to difficulty with tasks like walking or gripping objects. This is due, with associated clinical features.
  • Ask about loss of proprioception – Patients may lose awareness of joint position, particularly in the feet, contributing to imbalance and falls. This occurs due, with associated clinical features.
  • Ask about autonomic symptoms – If autonomic nerves are involved, patients may experience symptoms like dizziness (due to orthostatic hypotension), gastroparesis, or urinary retention. These occur.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Peripheral Neuropathy"][/sc]
  • Check reduced or absent reflexes (hyporeflexia) – A hallmark of peripheral nerve damage, as reflex arcs require intact sensory and motor pathways.
  • Check muscle atrophy – Often seen in chronic neuropathies, due to denervation of muscle fibres following prolonged motor nerve damage.
  • Check sensory loss in a glove-and-stocking distribution – Reflects the selective vulnerability of distal nerve fibres to injury, with earlier involvement of long nerves.
  • Check gait disturbances – Patients may develop an unsteady gait, often described as "foot slapping" due to sensory loss or muscle weakness in the lower.
  • Check foot ulcers – Due to sensory loss, patients may not perceive minor injuries, predisposing them to ulceration and infection, especially in diabetes.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Peripheral Neuropathy"][/sc]
  • Rapid progression of symptoms – May indicate acute inflammatory neuropathies like Guillain-Barré syndrome, requiring urgent intervention due to the risk of respiratory failure or autonomic instability.
  • Asymmetrical or focal neuropathy – Suggests a more localised cause, such as nerve compression, vasculitis, or malignancy, and warrants imaging or further investigation.
  • Severe autonomic involvement – Profound autonomic dysfunction, such as persistent orthostatic hypotension, may signal small fibre neuropathy or paraneoplastic syndromes.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Peripheral Neuropathy"][/sc]
  • Isolated motor neuropathy – Instead of the classic mixed sensory and motor symptoms, some patients may present only with muscle weakness, mimicking conditions like myopathy or motor neurone disease.
  • Proximal muscle involvement – While distal muscles are usually affected first, certain aetiologies like diabetic amyotrophy present with proximal weakness and pain, particularly in the thighs and hips.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask peripheral neuropathy.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Peripheral Neuropathy"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Nerve conduction studies (NCS) – Measure the speed and strength of nerve signals. Slowed conduction indicates demyelination, while reduced amplitude suggests.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Blood tests – Fasting glucose, HbA1c for diabetes, vitamin B12 for deficiency, and ESR/CRP to assess for inflammatory or autoimmune causes.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Peripheral Neuropathy"][/sc]
  • Ulcers and infections – Sensory loss predisposes to unrecognised injuries, particularly in diabetic neuropathy, which can progress to chronic ulcers or osteomyelitis.
  • Falls and fractures – Due to gait disturbances, proprioceptive loss, and muscle weakness, increasing the risk of injury.
  • Autonomic complications – Dysautonomia can lead to life-threatening issues like arrhythmias, gastrointestinal motility disorders, or hypotension.
