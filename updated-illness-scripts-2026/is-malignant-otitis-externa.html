Malignant Otitis Externa
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Malignant Otitis Externa"][/sc]
  • Malignant otitis externa (MOE) is a severe, life-threatening infection of the external auditory canal, which extends to the temporal bone (osteomyelitis) and surrounding tissues.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise malignant features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.

Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Malignant Otitis Externa"][/sc]
  • Malignant otitis externa begins as an infection of the external auditory canal, often secondary to minor trauma, which allows bacteria like *Pseudomonas aeruginosa* to invade. The infection spreads to adjacent soft tissues, cartilage, and eventually the temporal bone (osteomyelitis). As the condition progresses, it can involve the cranial nerves, particularly the facial nerve (VII), leading to palsy. Chronic infection results in necrosis of surrounding tissues and may spread to the meninges, leading to meningitis or brain abscesses if untreated.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Malignant Otitis Externa"][/sc]
  • Acute phase (weeks 1-4): Initial symptoms of severe otalgia and otorrhoea. The infection begins locally and may progress to involve deeper structures.
  • Subacute phase (weeks 4-12): Persistent infection with cranial nerve involvement (often CN VII). Erosion of the temporal bone may begin.
  • Chronic phase (after 12 weeks): Progression to osteomyelitis, further cranial nerve palsies, and potential intracranial complications such as abscess formation or meningitis.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Malignant Otitis Externa"][/sc]
  • Ask about severe, unrelenting otalgia - Often the most prominent symptom, the pain is out of proportion to findings on otoscopy, due to infection extending into.
  • Ask about otorrhoea - Persistent, purulent discharge from the ear due to the infected tissue and drainage from the external auditory canal.
  • Ask about hearing loss - Conductive hearing loss occurs as the infection affects the external auditory canal, and can worsen if bone involvement progresses.
  • Ask about tinnitus - May occur due to inflammation and irritation of the external auditory canal and deeper structures.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Malignant Otitis Externa"][/sc]
  • Check granulation tissue at the bony-cartilaginous junction of the ear canal - A hallmark of the condition, signifying deeper tissue involvement.
  • Check facial nerve palsy (CN VII) - Indicates more advanced disease with cranial nerve involvement. This is due to inflammation and infection spreading to, with associated clinical features.
  • Check swollen, erythematous external auditory canal - Reflects the localised infection and inflammation of the external ear canal.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Malignant Otitis Externa"][/sc]
  • Cranial nerve palsies - Suggests that the infection has spread beyond the external ear and into the skull base, which may lead to severe neurological deficits.
  • Severe headaches or altered mental status - Could indicate intracranial complications such as meningitis or brain abscess.
  • Persistent fever despite antibiotics - Suggests ongoing osteomyelitis or deeper tissue involvement, requiring more aggressive treatment.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Malignant Otitis Externa"][/sc]
  • Diabetics with minimal symptoms - Immunocompromised individuals, particularly those with poorly controlled diabetes, may have a blunted inflammatory response and present with only mild ear pain or discharge initially.
  • Absence of ear pain in elderly or immunocompromised patients - These populations may have atypical presentations with primarily neurological symptoms like facial weakness or difficulty swallowing due to cranial nerve involvement.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask malignant otitis externa.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Malignant Otitis Externa"][/sc]
  • Chronic suppurative otitis media - Typically involves a perforated tympanic membrane and middle ear involvement, whereas MOE does not.
  • Cholesteatoma - Distinguish from malignant otitis externa using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.
  • Temporal bone tumour - Can mimic MOE with facial nerve involvement, but imaging typically reveals a mass rather.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Malignant Otitis Externa"][/sc]
  • Osteomyelitis of the temporal bone - The infection can spread to the bone, requiring long-term antibiotic therapy and leading to significant morbidity.
  • Cranial nerve palsy - Facial nerve palsy is the most common, but other cranial nerves may also be affected, leading to dysphagia, hearing loss, and balance disturbances.
  • Intracranial extension - Meningitis, brain abscess, and venous sinus thrombosis are severe complications that may arise if the infection progresses unchecked.
