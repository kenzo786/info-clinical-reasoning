Non-ulcer Dyspepsia
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Non-ulcer Dyspepsia"][/sc]

Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Non-ulcer Dyspepsia"][/sc]
  • Gastric Motility Dysfunction - Delayed gastric emptying can cause a sensation of bloating and fullness, common in non-ulcer dyspepsia.
  • Visceral Hypersensitivity - Heightened sensitivity of gastric and duodenal receptors contributes to pain and discomfort, even with normal amounts of gastric distension.
  • Psychological Factors - Anxiety and depression are commonly associated, potentially exacerbating symptoms through the brain-gut axis, impacting motility and sensitivity.
  • Gastrointestinal Hormones - Dysregulation of hormones like ghrelin and cholecystokinin may affect satiety and discomfort post-meal.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Non-ulcer Dyspepsia"][/sc]
  • Symptoms often develop gradually and persist over months to years, with fluctuating intensity.
  • Periods of remission and exacerbation are common, potentially triggered by dietary factors or stress.
  • Most patients have chronic or relapsing symptoms over the course of years, with many managing symptoms rather than achieving a cure.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Non-ulcer Dyspepsia"][/sc]
  • Ask about epigastric Pain - Commonest symptom, often a burning or gnawing sensation, due to visceral hypersensitivity in the upper GI tract.
  • Ask about bloating - Caused by delayed gastric emptying and hypersensitivity to gastric distension.
  • Ask about postprandial Fullness - Resulting from slow gastric emptying, leading to prolonged sensation of fullness after meals.
  • Ask about nausea - Thought to stem from autonomic dysfunction and abnormal gastric motility.
  • Ask about belching - May arise from increased intra-abdominal pressure or as a response to alleviate the sensation of fullness.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Non-ulcer Dyspepsia"][/sc]
  • Check epigastric Tenderness - Mild tenderness in the upper abdomen may be elicited, reflecting visceral hypersensitivity.
  • Check absence of Red Flags - Normal physical exam findings without significant weight loss, anaemia, or lymphadenopathy, helping to rule out organic disease.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Non-ulcer Dyspepsia"][/sc]
  • Unintentional Weight Loss - May indicate underlying malignancy or systemic disease, which requires urgent investigation.
  • Anaemia - A sign that can suggest bleeding or malignancy; should prompt further evaluation.
  • Dysphagia - Progressive difficulty swallowing may point towards an obstructive or neoplastic process in the oesophagus.
  • Family History of GI Cancer - Increases the likelihood of malignancy, making endoscopic evaluation necessary.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Non-ulcer Dyspepsia"][/sc]
  • Lower Abdominal Pain - Can be mistaken for irritable bowel syndrome (IBS) if pain localises outside of epigastrium.
  • Isolated Nausea - In the absence of other dyspeptic symptoms, nausea may lead to investigation for functional GI disorders other than dyspepsia.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask non-ulcer dyspepsia.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Non-ulcer Dyspepsia"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Upper GI Endoscopy - Often normal; performed to rule out ulcers, malignancy, or structural abnormalities.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Helicobacter pylori Testing - Negative in most cases; performed to exclude H. pylori-related dyspepsia; Abdominal Ultrasound - Normal; can rule out.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Non-ulcer Dyspepsia"][/sc]
  • Gastroesophageal Reflux Disease (GORD) - Unlike non-ulcer dyspepsia, GORD often presents with heartburn and acid regurgitation.
  • Peptic Ulcer Disease - Distinguish from non-ulcer dyspepsia using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.
  • Gallbladder Disease - Pain may radiate to the back and is often exacerbated by fatty foods.
  • Irritable Bowel Syndrome (IBS) - Associated with altered bowel habits, which are not a feature of non-ulcer dyspepsia.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Non-ulcer Dyspepsia"][/sc]
  • Quality of Life Impairment - Persistent symptoms can significantly affect daily functioning, social, and work life.
  • Psychological Complications - Chronic symptoms may lead to anxiety, depression, or increased health care utilisation due to ongoing distress.
  • Prognosis - Generally benign with no increased mortality, though symptoms may persist or relapse long-term.
