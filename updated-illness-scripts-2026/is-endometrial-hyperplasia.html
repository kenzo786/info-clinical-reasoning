Endometrial Hyperplasia
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Endometrial Hyperplasia"][/sc]
  • Endometrial hyperplasia is a condition characterised by the thickening of the endometrium (the lining of the uterus) due to excessive proliferation of endometrial cells.

Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Endometrial Hyperplasia"][/sc]
  • Endometrial hyperplasia occurs due to chronic stimulation of the endometrium by oestrogen without the balancing effect of progesterone, which normally induces shedding of the endometrial lining. This prolonged oestrogen exposure can lead to uncontrolled proliferation of endometrial cells, resulting in glandular crowding and thickening of the endometrium. Simple hyperplasia features increased gland-to-stroma ratio, whereas complex hyperplasia shows more glandular complexity. Atypical hyperplasia involves cellular atypia, increasing the risk of malignant transformation.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Endometrial Hyperplasia"][/sc]
  • Endometrial hyperplasia typically develops gradually over months to years, particularly in the presence of risk factors such as unopposed oestrogen exposure. Symptoms like abnormal uterine bleeding may present early, but progression to atypia or cancer can take several years, particularly in untreated cases.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Endometrial Hyperplasia"][/sc]
  • Ask about abnormal Uterine Bleeding (AUB) - Most common symptom, presents as heavy menstrual bleeding, irregular cycles, or postmenopausal bleeding. This occurs due to the irregular.
  • Ask about menorrhagia - Prolonged and excessive menstrual bleeding due to increased endometrial surface area.
  • Ask about intermenstrual Bleeding - Bleeding between periods, related to unstable endometrial lining and irregular shedding.
  • Ask about postmenopausal Bleeding - Often a key symptom in older women, potentially signifying malignancy in cases of atypia or complex hyperplasia.
  • Ask about asymptomatic - Some patients may remain asymptomatic, with the condition identified during evaluation for other issues.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Endometrial Hyperplasia"][/sc]
  • Check pelvic Examination - Usually normal but may reveal an enlarged uterus in cases of concurrent pathology such as uterine fibroids.
  • Check abnormal Uterine Size - In rare cases, the uterus may appear bulky due to concurrent uterine pathology.
  • Check endometrial Thickening on Ultrasound - Transvaginal ultrasound often shows increased endometrial thickness, particularly >4 mm in postmenopausal women, indicating hyperplasia.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Endometrial Hyperplasia"][/sc]
  • Postmenopausal Bleeding - May suggest malignancy, as endometrial hyperplasia can progress to cancer, especially in women with atypia.
  • Rapidly Progressive Symptoms - Worsening bleeding or pelvic pain may indicate progression to endometrial carcinoma.
  • Severe Anaemia - Due to heavy blood loss from prolonged or excessive bleeding.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Endometrial Hyperplasia"][/sc]
  • Minimal Symptoms - In some cases, particularly in perimenopausal women, mild or infrequent bleeding may mask the underlying severity of the hyperplasia.
  • Silent Hyperplasia - Some women, particularly those on hormone therapy, may have hyperplasia without significant symptoms, leading to delayed diagnosis.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask endometrial hyperplasia.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Endometrial Hyperplasia"][/sc]
  • Endometrial Carcinoma - A major complication, especially in cases with atypia, with a risk of progression in up to 30% of cases of complex atypical hyperplasia.
  • Severe Anaemia - Resulting from prolonged heavy bleeding, requiring iron supplementation or blood transfusions.
  • Infertility - Long-standing untreated hyperplasia can affect fertility, particularly in cases with hormonal imbalances like PCOS.
