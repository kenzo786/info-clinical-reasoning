Ankle Sprain
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Ankle Sprain"][/sc]
  • An ankle sprain is an acute injury involving partial or complete tearing of the ligaments of the ankle joint, most commonly the lateral ligament complex.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise ankle features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.

  • Higher-risk or missed groups: Ankle Sprain may be missed when presentations are atypical or comorbidity obscures key features.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Ankle Sprain"][/sc]
  • An ankle sprain occurs when an excessive force stretches or tears the ligaments stabilising the ankle joint.
  • The most commonly affected structure is the anterior talofibular ligament (ATFL), part of the lateral ligament complex, due to inversion injuries.
  • This results in localised inflammation, vascular leakage causing oedema, and nociceptor activation leading to pain.
  • Severe sprains may involve damage to adjacent ligaments or even cartilage and tendons.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Ankle Sprain"][/sc]
  • Acute phase (0-3 days): Pain, swelling, and inability to bear weight.
  • Subacute phase (4-14 days): Resolution of swelling and pain, gradual restoration of movement.
  • Chronic phase (>14 days): Lingering instability or pain if improperly managed.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Ankle Sprain"][/sc]
  • Ask about pain: Usually localised over the affected ligament (e.g., lateral malleolus). Caused by ligamentous injury and associated inflammatory mediators.
  • Ask about swelling: Due to vascular leakage in the injured area.
  • Ask about bruising: From subcutaneous haemorrhage following ligament damage.
  • Ask about instability: A sensation of the ankle 'giving way' from compromised ligament integrity.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Ankle Sprain"][/sc]
  • Check tenderness: Localised pain on palpation, typically over the ATFL or CFL.
  • Check swelling: Soft-tissue oedema seen around the ankle.
  • Check limited range of motion: Pain-induced restriction of dorsiflexion and plantarflexion.
  • Check positive anterior drawer test: Suggests instability due to ATFL injury.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Ankle Sprain"][/sc]
  • Severe pain and inability to bear weight: May indicate a fracture or high-grade ligament tear.
  • Persistent swelling or pain >2 weeks: Suggests cartilage damage or undiagnosed syndesmotic injury.
  • Numbness or paraesthesia: Could signal neurovascular compromise.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Ankle Sprain"][/sc]
  • No significant swelling: Seen in partial ligament tears.
  • Medial pain: May indicate a deltoid ligament injury or associated fracture.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features or comorbidity can mask the underlying diagnosis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Ankle Sprain"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: X-ray (Ottawa ankle rules): To exclude fractures; normal findings expected if sprain.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Ultrasound: Demonstrates ligament discontinuity or thickening; MRI: Reserved for severe or chronic cases, showing soft-tissue detail.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Ankle Sprain"][/sc]
  • A 25-year-old footballer presents with acute lateral ankle pain and swelling after an inversion injury during play. Unable to bear weight initially, now ambulatory with discomfort.
  • Adult with symptom pattern and objective findings suggestive of ankle sprain, requiring focused confirmation and risk stratification.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Ankle Sprain"][/sc]
  • Ankle fracture - More severe pain, deformity, and positive X-ray findings.
  • Syndesmotic injury - Tenderness over syndesmosis, positive squeeze test.
  • Achilles tendon rupture - Sudden calf pain, positive Thompson’s test.
  • Alternative serious diagnosis - Distinguishing history, examination, and targeted investigations do not support ankle sprain.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Ankle Sprain"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Assess physiological stability and immediate high-risk complications at presentation, with prompt stabilisation where required.
  • Core care: Provide condition-specific management for ankle sprain, treat underlying drivers, and coordinate multidisciplinary care.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Reassess symptoms, objective markers, and functional impact over time to confirm response and detect complications early.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Urgent reassessment is required for worsening symptoms, physiological deterioration, or failure to improve with initial management of ankle sprain.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Ankle Sprain"][/sc]
  • Chronic instability: Due to incomplete ligament healing.
  • Osteoarthritis: From repetitive trauma or untreated injuries.
  • Persistent pain: Often linked to undiagnosed cartilage damage.
