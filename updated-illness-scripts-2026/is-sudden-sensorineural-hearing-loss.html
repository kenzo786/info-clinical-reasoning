Sudden Sensorineural Hearing Loss (SSNHL)
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Sudden Sensorineural Hearing Loss (SSNHL)"][/sc]
  • Sudden Sensorineural Hearing Loss (SSNHL) is defined as a rapid-onset hearing loss, typically occurring over a period of fewer than 72 hours.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise sudden features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.

  • There is no significant gender predilection.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Sudden Sensorineural Hearing Loss (SSNHL)"][/sc]
  • The exact pathophysiology of SSNHL is unknown, but several mechanisms have been proposed:
    • Viral infections: Viral inflammation may damage the cochlea or auditory nerve, causing hearing loss.
    • Vascular occlusion: Disruption of blood supply to the cochlea due to microvascular events can result in ischaemic damage.
    • Autoimmune response: Antibodies attacking the inner ear structures can result in acute hearing loss.
    • Trauma or perilymph fistula: Sudden changes in pressure can rupture the inner ear membranes.
    • Idiopathic: In most cases (up to 90%), no identifiable cause is found.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Sudden Sensorineural Hearing Loss (SSNHL)"][/sc]
  • Onset: SSNHL typically occurs suddenly, with patients often describing hearing loss noticed upon waking or over a few hours to days (less than 72 hours).
  • Progression: The degree of hearing loss may worsen within a short period, sometimes stabilising after the initial loss.
  • Recovery: Spontaneous recovery occurs in 32–65% of cases, usually within the first two weeks. Prompt treatment improves the likelihood of recovery.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Sudden Sensorineural Hearing Loss (SSNHL)"][/sc]
  • Ask about sudden unilateral hearing loss - The hallmark symptom of SSNHL, typically experienced in one ear, with a rapid onset over a few hours, with associated clinical features.
  • Ask about aural fullness - A sensation of pressure or blockage in the affected ear, often accompanying hearing loss. This may be due to fluid accumulation.
  • Ask about tinnitus - Ringing or buzzing in the ear is common and occurs due to abnormal cochlear nerve activity or damage.
  • Ask about vertigo - Occurs in some patients, caused by a disruption in the vestibular system, which shares structures with the cochlea.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Sudden Sensorineural Hearing Loss (SSNHL)"][/sc]
  • Check positive Rinne test (air > bone conduction) - Confirms sensorineural hearing loss; the sound is heard louder through air conduction because the auditory nerve.
  • Check weber test lateralises to the unaffected ear - In SSNHL, the sound localises to the better hearing ear, indicating a sensorineural loss in, with associated clinical features.
  • Check reduced speech discrimination - The patient may struggle to distinguish words during speech audiometry testing due to auditory nerve dysfunction.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Sudden Sensorineural Hearing Loss (SSNHL)"][/sc]
  • Sudden hearing loss with facial weakness or numbness - May indicate a stroke or acoustic neuroma, requiring urgent evaluation.
  • Severe vertigo and nausea - Could signify Ménière’s disease or vestibular neuritis, warranting further investigation.
  • Bilateral hearing loss - Atypical for SSNHL and may indicate systemic conditions like autoimmune disease or infection (e.g. syphilis).
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Sudden Sensorineural Hearing Loss (SSNHL)"][/sc]
  • Gradual hearing loss over weeks - More characteristic of chronic conditions, such as acoustic neuroma or chronic otitis media, rather than SSNHL.
  • Bilateral involvement - Rare in SSNHL; suggests other systemic diseases like autoimmune inner ear disease or meningitis.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask sudden sensorineural hearing loss (ssnhl).
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Sudden Sensorineural Hearing Loss (SSNHL)"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Audiometry - Pure-tone audiometry reveals a drop of ≥30 dB across three contiguous frequencies in the affected ear, confirming SSNHL.
  • Targeted and monitoring: MRI of the internal auditory meatus - Used to exclude retrocochlear pathologies such as acoustic neuroma or other central causes; Blood.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Sudden Sensorineural Hearing Loss (SSNHL)"][/sc]
  • Acoustic neuroma - Typically causes progressive hearing loss, often associated with imbalance and sometimes facial nerve involvement.
  • Ménière’s disease - Episodic vertigo, fluctuating hearing loss, and tinnitus, usually in association with aural fullness.
  • Labyrinthitis - Associated with acute vertigo, nausea, and often following a viral infection.
  • Otosclerosis - Gradual conductive hearing loss, often with a family history, but presents with normal sensorineural.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Sudden Sensorineural Hearing Loss (SSNHL)"][/sc]
  • Permanent hearing loss - Occurs in about 15–30% of patients despite treatment, potentially due to irreversible cochlear damage.
  • Persistent tinnitus - May continue after hearing recovery, leading to significant distress.
  • Psychosocial impact - SSNHL can result in anxiety, depression, and social isolation due to communication difficulties.
