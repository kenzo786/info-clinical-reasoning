Bradycardia
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Bradycardia"][/sc]
  • Bradycardia is defined as a resting heart rate of fewer than 60 beats per minute (bpm) in adults.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise bradycardia features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Bradycardia"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Bradycardia"][/sc]
  • Risk factors: Risk of bradycardia rises with relevant predisposition, comorbidity, and exposure profile.
  • Higher-risk or missed groups: Bradycardia may be missed when presentations are atypical or comorbidity obscures key features.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Bradycardia"][/sc]
  • Bradycardia results from a disturbance in the heart's electrical conduction system. It may involve sinoatrial node dysfunction (sinus bradycardia), atrioventricular (AV) block, or suppression of normal pacemaker function due to extrinsic factors such as hypothyroidism or medications (e.g., beta-blockers).
  • Sinoatrial Node Dysfunction: The SA node fails to generate impulses at a normal rate.
  • AV Block: Impulse transmission through the AV node is delayed or interrupted.
  • Extrinsic Factors: Drugs or metabolic changes depress pacemaker activity or slow conduction.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Bradycardia"][/sc]
  • Acute Onset: Bradycardia can develop suddenly due to acute causes such as myocardial infarction, overdose of medications (e.g., beta-blockers), or electrolyte disturbances (e.g., hyperkalaemia).
  • Chronic Course: In conditions such as hypothyroidism or chronic sinoatrial node dysfunction, bradycardia may develop slowly over weeks to months.
  • Intermittent Bradycardia: Seen in conditions like sick sinus syndrome, where episodes of bradycardia alternate with tachycardia.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Bradycardia"][/sc]
  • Ask about fatigue - Due to reduced cardiac output, leading to diminished perfusion of tissues.
  • Ask about dizziness - Caused by decreased cerebral perfusion as a result of low heart rate.
  • Ask about syncope - Occurs when bradycardia results in significant hypotension, leading to transient cerebral hypoperfusion.
  • Ask about shortness of Breath - Reduced cardiac output causes poor oxygen delivery to tissues, creating a sensation of breathlessness.
  • Ask about chest Pain - If bradycardia leads to coronary hypoperfusion, myocardial ischaemia can occur, causing chest pain.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Bradycardia"][/sc]
  • Check slow Pulse - Directly indicative of bradycardia (heart rate < 60 bpm).
  • Check hypotension - Due to reduced stroke volume and cardiac output from a slower heart rate.
  • Check cool Extremities - Peripheral vasoconstriction occurs as a compensatory mechanism for low cardiac output.
  • Check altered Mental Status - Severe bradycardia can impair cerebral perfusion, leading to confusion or agitation.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Bradycardia"][/sc]
  • Syncope with Injury - May indicate severe, prolonged bradycardia causing significant cerebral hypoperfusion and potential arrhythmias.
  • Chest Pain - Can signify myocardial ischaemia secondary to reduced coronary perfusion.
  • Severe Hypotension - Indicates dangerously low cardiac output, possibly leading to shock or multi-organ failure.
  • Altered Mental Status - Suggests cerebral hypoperfusion from inadequate cardiac output, necessitating urgent intervention.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Bradycardia"][/sc]
  • Asymptomatic Bradycardia - Often seen in fit athletes or individuals with high vagal tone; there is no clinical consequence despite a heart rate of < 60 bpm.
  • Bradycardia in Hypothyroidism - The patient may primarily present with other hypothyroid symptoms (e.g., fatigue, weight gain) rather than directly with bradycardia.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features or comorbidity can mask the underlying diagnosis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Bradycardia"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: ECG - Confirms bradycardia and identifies the type (sinus bradycardia, AV block). Findings include a heart rate < 60 bpm, prolonged.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Electrolytes - Elevated potassium (hyperkalaemia) or calcium levels can affect the electrical activity of the heart, leading to bradycardia; Holter Monitor.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Bradycardia"][/sc]
  • A 68-year-old male presents with recurrent episodes of dizziness and near-syncope over the last few weeks. ECG shows a heart rate of 45 bpm with intermittent AV block. Thyroid function.
  • Adult with symptom pattern and objective findings suggestive of bradycardia, requiring focused confirmation and risk stratification.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Bradycardia"][/sc]
  • Atrial Fibrillation with Slow Ventricular Response - Irregularly irregular rhythm; no consistent P waves on ECG.
  • Sick Sinus Syndrome - Alternating bradycardia and tachycardia; episodes of bradycardia occur intermittently.
  • Hypothyroidism - Bradycardia accompanied by classic hypothyroid symptoms (e.g., cold intolerance, weight gain); confirmed by elevated.
  • Drug-Induced Bradycardia - Differentiate from bradycardia using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Bradycardia"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Assess physiological stability and urgent complications at presentation, and initiate early stabilisation where required.
  • Core care: Deliver condition-specific treatment for bradycardia, address underlying drivers, and involve appropriate specialist support.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Reassess symptom trajectory, objective markers, and treatment response over time to detect complications early.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Worsening symptoms, physiological deterioration, or failure to improve should trigger urgent reassessment for severe or alternative pathology in bradycardia.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Bradycardia"][/sc]
  • Syncope and Falls - Due to cerebral hypoperfusion; common in elderly patients.
  • Heart Failure - Longstanding bradycardia can lead to reduced cardiac output and heart failure symptoms.
  • Sudden Cardiac Death - Particularly in high-degree AV block or severe bradycardia, if left untreated.
