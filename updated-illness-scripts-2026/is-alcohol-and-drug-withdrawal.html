Alcohol and Drug Withdrawal
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Alcohol and Drug Withdrawal"][/sc]
  • Alcohol and drug withdrawal is a clinical syndrome after abrupt reduction or cessation of dependent substance use causing autonomic and neuropsychiatric disturbance.
  • UKMLA essentials: withdrawal after cessation, seizures or delirium tremens signs, structured withdrawal severity assessment.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Alcohol and Drug Withdrawal"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Alcohol and Drug Withdrawal"][/sc]
  • UK GP population: Alcohol withdrawal is seen in approximately 1 in 10 adults who are dependent drinkers. Around 600,000 people are dependent on alcohol in the UK, but only a portion experience severe withdrawal.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Alcohol and Drug Withdrawal"][/sc]
  • Alcohol and many drugs (e.g., opioids, benzodiazepines) depress the central nervous system (CNS) by enhancing inhibitory neurotransmission (GABA) or inhibiting excitatory pathways (e.g., glutamate).
  • With chronic use, the brain adapts to the presence of these substances by reducing sensitivity to inhibitory neurotransmitters and increasing excitatory signalling to maintain homeostasis.
  • When alcohol or drugs are abruptly reduced or stopped, the lack of CNS depressant effect leads to unopposed excitatory neurotransmission, causing a hyperexcitable state, resulting in withdrawal symptoms.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Alcohol and Drug Withdrawal"][/sc]
  • Alcohol withdrawal: Symptoms usually begin 6-24 hours after cessation, peak at 24-48 hours, and can last for 5-7 days. Delirium tremens (severe form) typically occurs after 48-72 hours.
  • Opioid withdrawal: Symptoms typically begin 6-12 hours after the last dose (short-acting opioids) or 24-48 hours (long-acting opioids) and can last for 7-10 days.
  • Benzodiazepine withdrawal: Symptoms can begin 1-4 days after cessation, especially with long-acting agents, and may persist for several weeks.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Alcohol and Drug Withdrawal"][/sc]
  • Ask about anxiety: Common due to unopposed excitatory signalling (increased norepinephrine and glutamate).
  • Ask about tremors: Result from CNS hyperactivity and increased sympathetic nervous system output.
  • Ask about nausea and vomiting: Caused by hyperactivity of the autonomic nervous system affecting the gastrointestinal tract.
  • Ask about sweating: Due to sympathetic overactivity.
  • Ask about insomnia: Stemming from CNS hyperarousal.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Alcohol and Drug Withdrawal"][/sc]
  • Check tachycardia: Sympathetic nervous system activation due to withdrawal.
  • Check hypertension: Caused by increased sympathetic tone and catecholamine release.
  • Check hyperreflexia: Due to disinhibited CNS and loss of suppressive signals.
  • Check agitation: Reflects CNS hyperactivity.
  • Check seizures (alcohol withdrawal): Generalised tonic-clonic seizures can occur due to severe hyperexcitation in the brain.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Alcohol and Drug Withdrawal"][/sc]
  • Seizures: Indicate severe alcohol withdrawal and risk of developing delirium tremens.
  • Delirium tremens: A medical emergency, marked by confusion, hallucinations, autonomic instability, and can result in death if untreated.
  • Hallucinations: Can signal impending delirium tremens or other serious CNS complications.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Alcohol and Drug Withdrawal"][/sc]
  • Prolonged withdrawal: Symptoms can persist for weeks (protracted withdrawal), especially with benzodiazepines. This is due to slow receptor adaptation back to normal.
  • Missed diagnosis in older patients: Subtle presentation such as fatigue, confusion, or falls rather than tremors or anxiety can lead to missed diagnosis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Alcohol and Drug Withdrawal"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Clinical diagnosis from recent substance reduction history, observations, capillary glucose, ECG, and blood tests including U&Es, magnesium, phosphate, FBC, and LFTs.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Serial CIWA-Ar or COWS scoring to monitor trajectory; toxicology when history is unclear; targeted imaging or infection work-up when alternative causes of delirium are suspected.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Alcohol and Drug Withdrawal"][/sc]
  • A 45-year-old man develops tremor, sweating, anxiety, and agitation 12 hours after stopping heavy daily alcohol use, with tachycardia and hypertension on assessment.
  • Dependent substance user with acute autonomic and neuropsychiatric symptoms after cessation, concerning for clinically significant withdrawal.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Alcohol and Drug Withdrawal"][/sc]
  • Delirium from infection - Fever, focal infective source, or inflammatory markers suggest sepsis-related delirium rather than primary withdrawal.
  • Panic disorder - Episodic anxiety lacks a clear cessation trigger and persistent autonomic progression typical of withdrawal.
  • Thyrotoxicosis - Tremor and tachycardia with thyroid-function abnormalities and no substance-withdrawal timeline favour endocrine disease.
  • Hypoglycaemia - Neuroglycopenic symptoms with low glucose and rapid response to correction indicate metabolic rather than withdrawal pathology.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Alcohol and Drug Withdrawal"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Identify severe withdrawal, protect airway and circulation, correct glucose and electrolyte disturbance, and initiate supervised withdrawal management.
  • Core care: Use protocol-led pharmacological and supportive treatment for the relevant substance, with nutritional support and thiamine where alcohol dependence is present.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Perform serial withdrawal scoring, repeated observations, and laboratory review to detect progression, delirium tremens, or evolving complications.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Escalate urgently for seizures, hallucinosis, severe autonomic instability, persistent confusion, or concern for alternative acute pathology.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Alcohol and Drug Withdrawal"][/sc]
  • Seizures: Occur in 5-10% of patients with alcohol withdrawal; can be life-threatening.
  • Delirium tremens: Occurs in 5% of patients; mortality rates can reach up to 20% if untreated.
  • Wernicke’s encephalopathy: Results from thiamine deficiency, presenting as confusion, ataxia, and ophthalmoplegia.
