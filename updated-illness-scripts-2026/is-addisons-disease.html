Addison's Disease
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Addison's Disease"][/sc]
  • Addison's disease is primary adrenal insufficiency caused by adrenal cortex failure, resulting in cortisol and aldosterone deficiency.
  • UKMLA essentials: chronic fatigue with hyperpigmentation, hypotension or crisis features, confirm with cortisol and ACTH testing.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Addison's Disease"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Addison's Disease"][/sc]
  • Addison's disease is uncommon but clinically important because delayed diagnosis can present as life-threatening adrenal crisis.
  • Autoimmune adrenalitis is the commonest UK cause, with infection, infiltrative disease, and adrenal haemorrhage important alternative causes.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Addison's Disease"][/sc]
  • Addison's disease is primarily caused by autoimmune destruction of the adrenal cortex, resulting in decreased production of glucocorticoids (cortisol) and mineralocorticoids (aldosterone).
  • The loss of cortisol leads to impaired glucose metabolism, stress response, and inflammation regulation.
  • The absence of aldosterone causes dysregulation of sodium, potassium, and water balance, leading to hyponatraemia, hyperkalaemia, and hypotension.
  • The adrenal medulla is typically unaffected, so adrenaline and noradrenaline production remains intact.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Addison's Disease"][/sc]
  • Addison's disease often has an insidious onset, with symptoms gradually worsening over months to years.
  • Early symptoms such as fatigue and hyperpigmentation may be subtle, but if untreated, the condition can progress to an Addisonian crisis, which is a life-threatening emergency.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Addison's Disease"][/sc]
  • Ask about fatigue - Common and progressive, resulting from decreased cortisol levels and impaired energy metabolism.
  • Ask about weight loss - A result of anorexia and dehydration due to electrolyte imbalances and cortisol deficiency.
  • Ask about hyperpigmentation - Increased ACTH stimulates melanocytes, leading to darker skin, especially in sun-exposed areas.
  • Ask about salt craving - Due to aldosterone deficiency causing sodium loss through urine.
  • Ask about dizziness and hypotension - Secondary to aldosterone deficiency, leading to reduced blood volume and vascular tone.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Addison's Disease"][/sc]
  • Check postural hypotension - Reflects hypovolaemia due to aldosterone deficiency, leading to a drop in blood pressure upon standing.
  • Check hyperpigmentation - Particularly noticeable in skin folds, scars, and mucous membranes, linked to elevated ACTH levels.
  • Check dehydration - Due to fluid loss from sodium wasting and aldosterone deficiency.
  • Check weight loss - Visible due to chronic dehydration, anorexia, and muscle wasting from cortisol deficiency.
  • Check vitiligo - Sometimes present due to the autoimmune nature of the disease.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Addison's Disease"][/sc]
  • Addisonian Crisis - Acute onset of severe hypotension, shock, and confusion, often triggered by infection, surgery, or trauma. It signifies critical cortisol deficiency and can lead to death if untreated.
  • Severe hyponatraemia - May cause confusion, seizures, and coma due to cerebral oedema.
  • Hyperkalaemia - Significant potassium elevation can result in cardiac arrhythmias, which may be life-threatening.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Addison's Disease"][/sc]
  • Isolated fatigue and weight loss - Without obvious hyperpigmentation, Addison's disease can be mistaken for functional or psychiatric illness.
  • Intermittent gastrointestinal symptoms - Recurrent nausea, vomiting, and abdominal pain may be misattributed to primary gastrointestinal pathology.
  • Intercurrent illness unmasking deficiency - Mild chronic symptoms may only become apparent when infection or stress precipitates adrenal crisis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Addison's Disease"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Early morning serum cortisol with paired ACTH; U&Es and glucose to assess hyponatraemia, hyperkalaemia, and hypoglycaemia.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Short Synacthen test to confirm adrenal insufficiency; adrenal antibodies for autoimmune disease; pituitary or adrenal imaging when secondary or infiltrative causes are suspected; serial electrolytes and blood pressure monitoring after treatment changes.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Addison's Disease"][/sc]
  • A 45-year-old woman presents with 6 months of worsening fatigue, weight loss, hyperpigmentation, and postural dizziness; blood tests show hyponatraemia, hyperkalaemia, and low morning cortisol.
  • Adult with chronic constitutional symptoms, hyperpigmentation, postural hypotension, and biochemical adrenal insufficiency, concerning for Addison's disease.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Addison's Disease"][/sc]
  • Secondary adrenal insufficiency - Low ACTH with relative aldosterone preservation usually causes less prominent hyperkalaemia.
  • Chronic fatigue syndrome - Persistent fatigue occurs without hyperpigmentation, postural hypotension, or adrenal biochemical abnormalities.
  • Hyperthyroidism - Weight loss and fatigue occur with tremor, tachycardia, and thyroid-function abnormalities rather than mineralocorticoid deficiency.
  • Primary hypothyroidism - Fatigue with weight gain and raised TSH is more typical than sodium and potassium disturbance.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Addison's Disease"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Assess for adrenal crisis in hypotension, vomiting, confusion, or collapse and start emergency steroid-fluid management when unstable.
  • Core care: Long-term glucocorticoid and mineralocorticoid replacement with endocrine follow-up, patient education, and sick-day rule planning.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Review symptoms, postural blood pressure, electrolytes, and replacement adequacy regularly to reduce crisis risk.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Persistent vomiting, intercurrent sepsis, inability to retain oral steroids, or recurrent collapse should prompt urgent emergency reassessment.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Addison's Disease"][/sc]
  • Addisonian Crisis - Most serious complication, with risk of hypovolaemic shock and death without treatment.
  • Osteoporosis - Long-term steroid therapy increases the risk of osteoporosis due to effects on bone metabolism.
  • Infections - Patients are at increased risk of infections due to immunosuppressive effects of corticosteroid therapy.
