Teething
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Teething"][/sc]
  • Teething is the process of primary (deciduous) teeth erupting through the gums in infants and young children, typically beginning around 6 months of age and continuing until approximately 2-3 years.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise teething features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Teething"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Teething"][/sc]
  • Teething affects all children globally, as it is a universal physiological process. In the UK, nearly all infants experience teething during their first two to three years of life.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Teething"][/sc]
  • Teething occurs as the deciduous teeth move through the alveolar bone and erupt through the gingiva. This process causes inflammation of the surrounding gum tissue, which can lead to swelling, tenderness, and discomfort.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Teething"][/sc]
  • Teething generally begins at 6 months and lasts until the complete eruption of primary teeth, usually by 2-3 years. Symptoms can occur intermittently as each tooth erupts.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Teething"][/sc]
  • Ask about irritability - Caused by inflammation and discomfort as teeth erupt through the gums.
  • Ask about increased drooling - Stimulation of salivary glands during teething leads to excessive saliva production.
  • Ask about chewing on objects - Infants often bite objects to counter the pressure and soothe gum discomfort.
  • Ask about mild gum swelling - Localised inflammation due to tooth eruption.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Teething"][/sc]
  • Check red and swollen gums - Resulting from localised inflammation as teeth emerge.
  • Check eruption cysts - Fluid-filled swellings over emerging teeth due to tissue disruption.
  • Check teeth visibility - Emerging tooth tips may be visible or palpable.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Teething"][/sc]
  • High fever (>38°C) - May indicate a concurrent infection, not directly due to teething.
  • Persistent diarrhoea - Suggests other gastrointestinal conditions; teething rarely causes prolonged symptoms.
  • Severe irritability - May point to other underlying issues such as otitis media.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Teething"][/sc]
  • Absence of symptoms - Some infants experience no noticeable teething symptoms, which is a normal variation.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask teething.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Teething"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: No specific diagnostic tests are required for teething. Diagnosis is clinical, based on age, symptoms, and examination findings.
  • Targeted and monitoring: No specific diagnostic tests are required for teething. Diagnosis is clinical, based on age, symptoms, and examination findings.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Teething"][/sc]
  • A 7-month-old infant presenting with irritability, excessive drooling, and mild gum swelling. Symptoms have been intermittent over the past week.
  • Patient with A 7-month-old infant presenting with irritability, excessive drooling, and mild gum swelling. Symptoms have been intermittent over the past week, consistent with teething.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Teething"][/sc]
  • Otitis media - Characterised by ear tugging, fever, and otoscopic findings of middle ear inflammation.
  • Gastroenteritis - Accompanied by diarrhoea and vomiting, which are uncommon with teething.
  • Viral infection - Generalised symptoms like fever and rash.
  • Related alternative diagnosis - Distinguish from teething using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Teething"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Assess severity, physiological stability, and immediate complications.
  • Core care: Deliver condition-specific management for teething, treat underlying drivers, and coordinate appropriate specialist input.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Reassess symptom trajectory, examination findings, and objective markers to confirm response in teething.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Escalate urgently for clinical deterioration, evolving red flags, or failure to improve in suspected teething.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Teething"][/sc]
  • Eruption cysts - Benign and usually self-resolving.
  • Potential for misdiagnosis - Severe symptoms might lead to overlooking other conditions like infections.
