Cervical Spondylosis
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Cervical Spondylosis"][/sc]
  • Cervical spondylosis is a degenerative condition of the cervical spine characterised by progressive wear and tear of the intervertebral discs, vertebral bodies, and associated structures, including facet joints.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise cervical features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.

Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Cervical Spondylosis"][/sc]
  • Degeneration of intervertebral discs leads to loss of disc height and increased load on facet joints.
  • Osteophyte formation occurs as a compensatory response to stabilise the spine but can encroach on neural foramina or the spinal canal.
  • Ligamentum flavum hypertrophy and calcification further contribute to spinal canal narrowing.
  • Chronic irritation of nerve roots or compression of the spinal cord results in radiculopathy or myelopathy, respectively.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Cervical Spondylosis"][/sc]
  • Initial asymptomatic phase: Progressive degenerative changes without noticeable symptoms.
  • Early symptomatic phase: Intermittent neck pain and stiffness, typically worsening with activity.
  • Advanced phase: Persistent pain, neurological symptoms (e.g., radiculopathy, myelopathy), and functional impairment.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Cervical Spondylosis"][/sc]
  • Ask about neck pain - Often localised to the cervical region, worsens with movement; caused by disc degeneration and facet joint inflammation.
  • Ask about stiffness - Difficulty turning the head, especially after prolonged inactivity; due to joint degeneration and muscle spasm.
  • Ask about radicular pain - Shooting pain radiating to the arm, often unilateral; due to nerve root compression.
  • Ask about paresthesia - Tingling or numbness in the upper extremities; caused by neural irritation.
  • Ask about weakness - Reduced grip strength or difficulty with fine motor tasks; indicates nerve root or spinal cord involvement.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Cervical Spondylosis"][/sc]
  • Check reduced cervical range of motion - Limited flexion, extension, or rotation; caused by joint degeneration and muscle stiffness.
  • Check spurling's test positive - Reproduction of radicular pain with neck extension and lateral flexion; suggests nerve root compression.
  • Check hyperreflexia - Exaggerated reflexes in the upper and lower limbs; indicative of cervical myelopathy.
  • Check gait disturbance - Broad-based, unsteady walking; associated with spinal cord compression.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Cervical Spondylosis"][/sc]
  • Severe and persistent pain - May indicate malignancy or infection.
  • Rapidly progressive neurological deficits - Suggests significant cord compression or stroke.
  • Weight loss or systemic symptoms - May indicate malignancy or systemic disease.
  • Bladder or bowel dysfunction - Suggests advanced myelopathy or cauda equina syndrome.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Cervical Spondylosis"][/sc]
  • Chronic headache - May mimic tension-type headache due to referred pain from the cervical spine.
  • Non-specific dizziness - Cervical spondylosis may rarely affect proprioception via the dorsal columns.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features or comorbidity can mask the underlying diagnosis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Cervical Spondylosis"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: X-ray - Shows loss of disc height, osteophytes, and joint degeneration.
  • Targeted and monitoring: MRI - Identifies disc protrusion, nerve root compression, or spinal cord compression; CT myelography - Provides detailed imaging of spinal canal.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Cervical Spondylosis"][/sc]
  • A 55-year-old male with a 6-month history of neck pain and stiffness, now presenting with right arm paraesthesia and weakness. Symptoms worsen with neck movement and improve with rest.
  • Adult with symptom pattern and objective findings suggestive of cervical spondylosis, requiring focused confirmation and risk stratification.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Cervical Spondylosis"][/sc]
  • Herniated cervical disc - Acute onset, radicular pain without bony changes on imaging.
  • Rheumatoid arthritis - Polyarticular involvement, systemic symptoms, and synovial joint erosion.
  • Multiple sclerosis - Episodic neurological deficits, demyelination on MRI.
  • Alternative serious diagnosis - Distinguishing history, examination, and targeted investigations do not support cervical spondylosis.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Cervical Spondylosis"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Assess physiological stability and urgent complications at presentation, and initiate early stabilisation where required.
  • Core care: Deliver condition-specific treatment for cervical spondylosis, address underlying drivers, and involve appropriate specialist support.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Reassess symptom trajectory, objective markers, and treatment response over time to detect complications early.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Worsening symptoms, physiological deterioration, or failure to improve should trigger urgent reassessment for severe or alternative pathology in cervical spondylosis.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Cervical Spondylosis"][/sc]
  • Chronic pain - Due to ongoing degeneration and nerve irritation.
  • Cervical myelopathy - Progressive spinal cord damage leading to disability.
  • Radiculopathy - Persistent nerve root compression causing sensory or motor deficits.
