Urticaria
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Urticaria"][/sc]
  • Urticaria, also known as hives, is a common skin condition characterised by transient, raised, pruritic wheals (often pink or red) that vary in size and shape.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise urticaria features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.

  • Risk factors: Risk factors for urticaria include relevant predisposition, comorbidity, and exposure history.
  • Higher-risk or missed groups: Urticaria may be missed when presentation is atypical or obscured by comorbidity.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Urticaria"][/sc]
  • Urticaria is mediated by the release of histamine and other pro-inflammatory mediators from activated mast cells and basophils in the skin.
  • Histamine release leads to vasodilation and increased vascular permeability, causing plasma leakage and dermal oedema, which forms the wheals.
  • In chronic urticaria, autoimmune mechanisms may contribute, involving autoantibodies against IgE or the IgE receptor (FcεRI) on mast cells, leading to their activation.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Urticaria"][/sc]
  • Acute Phase: Wheals appear suddenly and resolve within 24 hours, though new lesions may continue to appear for days to weeks.
  • Chronic Phase: If symptoms persist for more than 6 weeks, the condition is considered chronic. Symptoms may last for months to years, with spontaneous resolution or fluctuation.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Urticaria"][/sc]
  • Ask about pruritus (itching) - Itching is caused by histamine and other mediators released by mast cells, stimulating sensory nerves in the skin.
  • Ask about burning or stinging sensation - Commonly experienced in areas of extensive wheals, due to irritation of nerve endings by inflammatory mediators.
  • Ask about swelling (angioedema) - May occur with urticaria, especially in deeper tissues, from histamine-induced fluid accumulation in subcutaneous layers.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Urticaria"][/sc]
  • Check wheals - Raised, erythematous, and oedematous plaques with surrounding redness, due to dermal oedema from histamine release.
  • Check dermatographism - Skin changes in response to pressure, often seen as red, raised lines when skin is stroked, indicating heightened skin sensitivity.
  • Check angioedema - Swelling around eyes, lips, or other areas, representing deeper dermal involvement.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Urticaria"][/sc]
  • Difficulty breathing or swallowing - Could indicate laryngeal oedema or anaphylaxis, requiring urgent medical attention.
  • Severe abdominal pain - May suggest visceral angioedema, particularly in cases with associated systemic involvement.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Urticaria"][/sc]
  • Urticarial vasculitis - Presents as painful, long-lasting wheals that may leave residual hyperpigmentation; caused by immune complex deposition in vessel walls.
  • Cold urticaria - Triggered by cold exposure, causing wheals upon rewarming; due to altered mast cell response to temperature change.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask urticaria.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Urticaria"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Complete blood count (CBC) - To check for anaemia or eosinophilia, especially if other allergic conditions are suspected.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Thyroid function tests - Important in chronic urticaria due to possible autoimmune thyroid disease association; Allergy testing - Considered in acute.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Urticaria"][/sc]
  • Contact dermatitis - Localised reaction at the site of contact, usually with a delayed onset, unlike urticaria’s.
  • Viral exanthem - Distinguish from urticaria using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.
  • Urticarial vasculitis - Painful, long-lasting lesions with residual pigmentation, unlike typical transient urticaria wheals.
  • Related alternative diagnosis - Distinguish from urticaria using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.

  • Escalation and safety-netting: Escalate urgently for clinical deterioration, evolving red flags, or failure to improve in suspected urticaria.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Urticaria"][/sc]
  • Anaphylaxis - Rare but severe complication, especially if urticaria is associated with angioedema or systemic symptoms.
  • Impact on quality of life - Chronic itching and sleep disturbance can lead to anxiety, depression, and reduced social interactions.
