Respiratory Failure
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Respiratory Failure"][/sc]
  • Respiratory failure is defined as the inability of the respiratory system to maintain adequate gas exchange, characterised by low oxygen levels (hypoxia) and/or high carbon dioxide levels (hypercapnia).
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise respiratory features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Respiratory Failure"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Respiratory Failure"][/sc]

  • Higher-risk or missed groups: Respiratory Failure may be missed when presentation is atypical or obscured by comorbidity.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Respiratory Failure"][/sc]
  • In respiratory failure, gas exchange in the lungs is impaired. In type 1 failure, the alveoli cannot oxygenate blood due to ventilation-perfusion (V/Q) mismatch, often seen in pneumonia or pulmonary oedema. Type 2 failure involves impaired CO2 elimination due to ventilatory pump failure, commonly due to COPD, neuromuscular disorders, or chest wall abnormalities. Chronic respiratory failure involves compensatory mechanisms like bicarbonate retention to buffer excess CO2.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Respiratory Failure"][/sc]
  • Acute respiratory failure: Rapid onset, minutes to hours, common in severe asthma or trauma.
  • Chronic respiratory failure: Develops over days to weeks, often in COPD or interstitial lung disease, allowing time for metabolic compensation.
  • Acute-on-chronic respiratory failure: Acute exacerbation of chronic respiratory disease, with rapid deterioration in a previously stable patient, as in a COPD exacerbation.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Respiratory Failure"][/sc]
  • Ask about dyspnoea (shortness of breath) - Due to hypoxia or hypercapnia, resulting from poor gas exchange.
  • Ask about cough - A reflex triggered by airway irritation, common in obstructive lung disease.
  • Ask about confusion or lethargy - Due to hypercapnia, which causes cerebral vasodilation and impaired brain function.
  • Ask about cyanosis - Bluish discolouration of the skin and mucous membranes due to desaturated haemoglobin, indicating significant hypoxia.
  • Ask about chest pain - May occur in conditions like pulmonary embolism or pneumothorax leading to respiratory failure.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Respiratory Failure"][/sc]
  • Check tachypnoea - Increased respiratory rate as a compensatory response to hypoxia or hypercapnia.
  • Check use of accessory muscles - Indicates respiratory distress as the patient attempts to enhance ventilation.
  • Check hypoxia (SpO2 < 92%) - Low oxygen saturation detectable via pulse oximetry, often seen in type 1 respiratory failure.
  • Check hypercapnia (PaCO2 > 6.0 kPa) - Indicates type 2 respiratory failure, causing a range of symptoms from headache to altered consciousness.
  • Check altered mental state - Due to the effects of hypercapnia or severe hypoxia on cerebral function.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Respiratory Failure"][/sc]
  • Rapid deterioration in respiratory status - Could signify acute worsening of underlying disease, such as pneumothorax or pulmonary embolism.
  • Confusion or coma - Indicates severe hypercapnia or hypoxia, which may lead to respiratory arrest if untreated.
  • Cyanosis - Indicates severe hypoxia and the potential for tissue hypoxia and organ failure.
  • Paradoxical abdominal movement - Suggests diaphragmatic weakness, seen in neuromuscular causes of respiratory failure.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Respiratory Failure"][/sc]
  • Mild dyspnoea with minimal hypoxia - Chronic compensatory mechanisms in patients with COPD may delay recognition of decompensation.
  • Gradual cognitive decline - In chronic hypercapnia, subtle neurological deficits may precede more obvious symptoms.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask respiratory failure.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Respiratory Failure"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Arterial blood gas (ABG) - Key test showing hypoxaemia (PaO2 6.0 kPa), and acidosis (pH < 7.35); Chest.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Pulmonary function tests - In chronic cases, useful to assess baseline lung function and identify obstructive or restrictive patterns; Blood tests.

  • Patient with increasing shortness of breath, confusion, and a cough over the past 24 hours. His SpO2 is 85% on room air, consistent with respiratory failure.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Respiratory Failure"][/sc]
  • Pulmonary embolism - Sudden onset of dyspnoea and pleuritic chest pain, usually with a normal chest X-ray.
  • Congestive heart failure - May present similarly, but with signs of fluid overload (e.g., peripheral oedema) and cardiomegaly.
  • Pneumonia - Fever, productive cough, and focal infiltrates on chest X-ray, often accompanied by hypoxaemia.
  • Acute asthma exacerbation - Wheezing and tight chest, often in younger patients with hyperinflated lungs on X-ray.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Respiratory Failure"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Assess severity, physiological stability, and immediate complications.
  • Core care: Deliver condition-specific management for respiratory failure, treat underlying drivers, and coordinate appropriate specialist input.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Reassess symptom trajectory, examination findings, and objective markers to confirm response in respiratory failure.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Escalate urgently for clinical deterioration, evolving red flags, or failure to improve in suspected respiratory failure.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Respiratory Failure"][/sc]
  • Cardiac arrest - Due to severe hypoxia or acidosis.
  • Respiratory arrest - If untreated, especially in acute respiratory failure with inadequate ventilation.
  • Multi-organ failure - Hypoxia can lead to widespread tissue damage, affecting organs such as the kidneys and liver.
  • Prolonged mechanical ventilation - Leads to complications like ventilator-associated pneumonia or barotrauma.
