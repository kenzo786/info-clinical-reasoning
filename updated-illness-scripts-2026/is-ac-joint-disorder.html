AC Joint Disorder
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="AC Joint Disorder"][/sc]
  • AC joint disorder describes painful pathology of the acromioclavicular joint, including traumatic sprain or dislocation and degenerative arthropathy.
  • UKMLA essentials: superior shoulder pain focus, deformity or neurovascular red flags, confirm with targeted shoulder imaging.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="AC Joint Disorder"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="AC Joint Disorder"][/sc]
  • AC joint disorders are common causes of superior shoulder pain in both trauma and overuse settings.
  • Traumatic AC injury is frequent in contact sport and falls onto the shoulder, while degenerative change is more common with age and repetitive overhead load.
  • Missed diagnosis can lead to persistent pain, shoulder dysfunction, and delayed return to work or sport.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="AC Joint Disorder"][/sc]
  • Direct trauma or repetitive loading injures the AC capsule and supporting coracoclavicular ligaments.
  • Inflammation and microinstability generate superior shoulder pain, particularly with cross-body and overhead movement.
  • Chronic degeneration can produce osteophyte formation, local joint narrowing, and persistent activity-related discomfort.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="AC Joint Disorder"][/sc]
  • Acute course: Pain begins after trauma or sudden overload and is aggravated by shoulder movement.
  • Persistent course: Symptoms can continue with overhead or cross-body activity if instability or degeneration remains untreated.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="AC Joint Disorder"][/sc]
  • Ask about pain at the top of the shoulder - Localised superior pain is typical of AC joint involvement.
  • Ask about pain with cross-body or overhead use - These movements increase AC compression and provoke symptoms.
  • Ask about weakness or reduced function - Pain-related inhibition can limit lifting, throwing, and pressing activities.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="AC Joint Disorder"][/sc]
  • Check focal AC tenderness - Point tenderness over the AC joint supports local pathology.
  • Check for superior clavicle prominence - Visible step or deformity suggests AC separation.
  • Check cross-body adduction provocation - Reproduction of local superior pain is characteristic of AC joint disease.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="AC Joint Disorder"][/sc]
  • Major deformity after high-energy trauma - Suggests high-grade AC disruption with possible associated shoulder-girdle injury.
  • Neurovascular symptoms in the limb - Numbness, weakness, or vascular compromise needs urgent reassessment.
  • Hot swollen joint with systemic upset - Raises concern for septic arthritis or other acute inflammatory pathology.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="AC Joint Disorder"][/sc]
  • Referred periscapular discomfort: Pain may radiate and mimic cervical or rotator cuff pathology.
  • Predominant weakness with modest pain: Chronic instability can present with functional loss out of proportion to resting pain.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="AC Joint Disorder"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Localised AC examination with comparative shoulder range, cross-body adduction provocation, and neurovascular assessment after trauma.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Shoulder radiographs for alignment and degenerative change; ultrasound or MRI when soft-tissue injury is unclear or symptoms persist despite rehabilitation.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="AC Joint Disorder"][/sc]
  • A 35-year-old man presents with 1 week of superior shoulder pain after a contact-sport fall, worsened by overhead and cross-body movement.
  • Adult with focal superior shoulder pain, movement-provoked AC tenderness, and post-traumatic functional limitation, concerning for AC joint disorder.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="AC Joint Disorder"][/sc]
  • Rotator cuff tendinopathy or tear - Lateral deltoid pain and painful arc pattern are more typical than point AC tenderness.
  • Glenohumeral osteoarthritis - Deep joint pain and global movement restriction suggest glenohumeral pathology.
  • Subacromial bursitis - Painful overhead activity occurs but tenderness localises below the AC joint.
  • Distal clavicle fracture - Bony tenderness and trauma pattern with fracture signs on radiographs distinguish osseous injury.
  • Cervical radiculopathy - Neck-related pain with dermatomal symptoms suggests cervical rather than AC pathology.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="AC Joint Disorder"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Control pain, protect the shoulder after acute injury, and exclude high-grade separation or associated neurovascular compromise.
  • Core care: Most low-grade injuries and degenerative presentations improve with activity modification, analgesia strategy, and structured physiotherapy.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Review pain pattern, shoulder function, and return-to-activity progress to guide rehabilitation pacing.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Persistent disability, progressive deformity, or failure of conservative treatment should prompt orthopaedic reassessment for intervention options.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="AC Joint Disorder"][/sc]
  • Chronic pain and instability: Untreated ligament injury can produce persistent superior shoulder dysfunction.
  • Post-traumatic AC arthropathy: Joint degeneration may develop after recurrent or severe AC injury.
  • Persistent functional limitation: Ongoing pain can reduce occupational and sporting performance.
