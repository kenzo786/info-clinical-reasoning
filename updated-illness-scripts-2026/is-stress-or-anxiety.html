Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Stress or Anxiety"][/sc]
  • Stress activates the hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal (HPA) axis, leading to the release of cortisol and adrenaline, which prepare the body for a "fight or flight" response. In chronic stress, prolonged activation of the HPA axis can lead to dysregulation of cortisol production, resulting in fatigue, immune suppression, and emotional instability.
  • Anxiety is driven by dysregulation in the brain's limbic system, particularly in areas such as the amygdala and prefrontal cortex. Neurotransmitters like serotonin, GABA, and norepinephrine are often imbalanced, leading to heightened arousal and worry. Overactivity of the sympathetic nervous system can contribute to physical symptoms like tachycardia and sweating.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Stress or Anxiety"][/sc]
  • Acute stress or anxiety: Sudden onset following an identifiable stressor, resolving once the stressor is removed or managed.
  • Chronic stress or generalised anxiety disorder: Lasts for months or years, with a slow build-up of symptoms, often waxing and waning in severity.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Stress or Anxiety"][/sc]
  • Ask about excessive worry - Persistent, disproportionate worry about various aspects of life. Related to the overactivity of the amygdala and prefrontal cortex.
  • Ask about fatigue - A common symptom due to prolonged HPA axis activation leading to exhaustion of the body's energy reserves.
  • Ask about difficulty concentrating - Impaired focus due to heightened arousal and cognitive interference from anxious thoughts.
  • Ask about restlessness - A feeling of being "on edge", linked to overactivation of the sympathetic nervous system.
  • Ask about insomnia - Difficulty falling or staying asleep due to an overactive stress response and excessive rumination.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Stress or Anxiety"][/sc]
  • Check tachycardia - Increased heart rate, often associated with sympathetic overdrive.
  • Check trembling or shaking - Physical manifestations of heightened autonomic arousal.
  • Check hyperventilation - Rapid breathing, often in response to perceived threats, leading to dizziness or tingling.
  • Check cold, clammy hands - A physiological response to sympathetic nervous system activation, as blood flow is redirected away from the skin.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Stress or Anxiety"][/sc]
  • Chest pain - May suggest a cardiovascular cause, such as acute coronary syndrome, rather than anxiety alone.
  • Suicidal ideation - Indicates severe anxiety or co-existing depression, requiring urgent intervention to prevent self-harm.
  • Unexplained weight loss - Could point to an underlying medical condition, such as hyperthyroidism or malignancy, rather than anxiety alone.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Stress or Anxiety"][/sc]
  • Somatisation - Presenting with multiple vague physical symptoms, such as gastrointestinal discomfort or headaches, without clear psychological distress.
  • Anger or irritability - In some individuals, anxiety may manifest as irritability or outbursts rather than traditional worry or fear.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask stress or anxiety.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Stress or Anxiety"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: General Health Questionnaire (GHQ-12) - A 12-item screening tool to assess psychological distress and likelihood of anxiety or depression.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Blood tests - Full blood count (FBC), thyroid function tests, and electrolyte panels to exclude organic causes like anaemia or hyperthyroidism.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Stress or Anxiety"][/sc]
  • Cardiovascular disease - Chronic anxiety has been linked to an increased risk of hypertension and coronary artery disease.
  • Substance abuse - Patients may resort to alcohol or drugs to manage symptoms of anxiety.
  • Depression - Untreated anxiety can progress to depression, compounding the impact on daily life.
