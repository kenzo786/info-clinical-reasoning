Macrocytic Anaemia
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Macrocytic Anaemia"][/sc]
  • Macrocytic anaemia is a type of anaemia characterised by an elevated mean corpuscular volume (MCV) of red blood cells, typically greater than 100 fL.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise macrocytic features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.

Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Macrocytic Anaemia"][/sc]
  • Macrocytic anaemia results from defective DNA synthesis, which slows down nuclear maturation in the bone marrow. Cells remain large and fail to divide normally (megaloblastic anaemia). The primary cause is often a deficiency in vitamin B12 or folate, which are essential for DNA production. Non-megaloblastic macrocytic anaemia may result from alcohol abuse, liver disease, or hypothyroidism.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Macrocytic Anaemia"][/sc]
  • Macrocytic anaemia typically develops over weeks to months as stores of vitamin B12 or folate deplete. Symptoms may be subtle at first but can progressively worsen. In chronic alcoholism or liver disease, macrocytosis may be present for years without overt symptoms of anaemia.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Macrocytic Anaemia"][/sc]
  • Ask about fatigue - This results from reduced oxygen delivery to tissues due to low haemoglobin levels.
  • Ask about shortness of breath - Reduced oxygen-carrying capacity leads to dyspnoea on exertion.
  • Ask about pallor - A reflection of reduced haemoglobin and red blood cell mass, leading to decreased skin and mucosal perfusion.
  • Ask about glossitis - Deficiencies in B12 or folate lead to inflammation of the tongue.
  • Ask about neurological symptoms (in B12 deficiency) - B12 deficiency affects the myelination of nerves, causing peripheral neuropathy, paraesthesia, or ataxia.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Macrocytic Anaemia"][/sc]
  • Check pallor - Decreased red blood cells and haemoglobin manifest as pale skin and mucous membranes.
  • Check glossitis - Smooth, red, swollen tongue due to mucosal atrophy from vitamin B12 or folate deficiency.
  • Check peripheral neuropathy (B12 deficiency) - Loss of sensation, reflexes, or proprioception due to demyelination of peripheral nerves.
  • Check ataxia - Loss of coordination, typically seen in advanced B12 deficiency due to spinal cord involvement (subacute combined degeneration).
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Macrocytic Anaemia"][/sc]
  • Severe neurological deficits (e.g. paralysis) - Indicates potential irreversible nerve damage from prolonged B12 deficiency.
  • Marked jaundice - Suggests haemolysis or severe liver disease, possibly leading to worsening macrocytosis.
  • Heart failure - Severe anaemia can precipitate high-output heart failure due to compensatory mechanisms being overwhelmed.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Macrocytic Anaemia"][/sc]
  • Some patients, particularly elderly individuals, may present primarily with cognitive decline or psychiatric symptoms rather than overt signs of anaemia or neurological deficits. B12 deficiency can mimic dementia or depression in these cases.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask macrocytic anaemia.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Macrocytic Anaemia"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Focused bedside assessment and first-line testing to assess severity and immediate risk in suspected macrocytic anaemia.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Serum B12 and folate levels - Reduced in B12 or folate deficiency, respectively; Liver function tests (LFTs) - Abnormal in alcohol-induced.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Macrocytic Anaemia"][/sc]
  • Iron-deficiency anaemia - Usually presents with microcytosis (low MCV), whereas macrocytic anaemia shows elevated MCV.
  • Thalassaemia - Microcytic anaemia with target cells on blood film, unlike the macrocytosis seen in macrocytic.
  • Anaemia of chronic disease - Typically normocytic or microcytic anaemia rather than macrocytic.
  • Liver disease - Can also cause macrocytosis but may present with elevated liver enzymes and a history.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Macrocytic Anaemia"][/sc]
  • Neurological damage - Prolonged B12 deficiency can lead to irreversible peripheral neuropathy and subacute combined degeneration of the spinal cord.
  • Heart failure - Severe anaemia can cause high-output cardiac failure, especially in the elderly.
  • Gastrointestinal malignancy - Pernicious anaemia is associated with an increased risk of gastric cancer due to chronic atrophic gastritis.
