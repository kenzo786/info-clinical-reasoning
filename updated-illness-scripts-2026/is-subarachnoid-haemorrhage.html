Subarachnoid Haemorrhage (SAH)
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Subarachnoid Haemorrhage (SAH)"][/sc]
  • Subarachnoid haemorrhage (SAH) refers to bleeding into the subarachnoid space, the area between the arachnoid membrane and the pia mater surrounding the brain.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise subarachnoid features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.

Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Subarachnoid Haemorrhage (SAH)"][/sc]
  • Subarachnoid haemorrhage occurs when a blood vessel within the brain, typically a cerebral aneurysm, ruptures. The rupture causes blood to rapidly leak into the subarachnoid space, leading to a sharp rise in intracranial pressure.
  • The blood itself can irritate the meninges, causing severe headaches and neck stiffness. Blood breakdown products can lead to vasospasm of nearby arteries, which is a significant cause of delayed cerebral ischaemia.
  • Increased intracranial pressure reduces cerebral perfusion, potentially leading to global or focal brain ischaemia. The haemorrhage also triggers an inflammatory response, exacerbating brain injury.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Subarachnoid Haemorrhage (SAH)"][/sc]
  • Acute phase: Sudden onset of severe symptoms, typically within seconds to minutes after rupture, often presenting with a "thunderclap headache." Neurological deterioration is rapid in cases of large haemorrhages.
  • Early complications: Vasospasm occurs in about 30% of patients between days 4-14, leading to delayed cerebral ischaemia.
  • Chronic phase: If patients survive the acute phase, they may face long-term neurological deficits and require rehabilitation for cognitive and physical impairments.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Subarachnoid Haemorrhage (SAH)"][/sc]
  • Ask about sudden, severe headache ("thunderclap headache") - Caused by the abrupt increase in intracranial pressure and meningeal irritation.
  • Ask about nausea and vomiting - Resulting from raised intracranial pressure and irritation of the vomiting centre in the medulla.
  • Ask about photophobia - Meningeal irritation from the blood in the subarachnoid space can lead to sensitivity to light.
  • Ask about neck stiffness - A sign of meningeal irritation due to blood in the subarachnoid space.
  • Ask about seizures - Occur in about 10-20% of cases, likely due to cortical irritation.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Subarachnoid Haemorrhage (SAH)"][/sc]
  • Check altered level of consciousness - From increased intracranial pressure and brain injury.
  • Check neck stiffness - From meningeal irritation due to blood in the subarachnoid space.
  • Check focal neurological deficits - Depending on the location and size of the haemorrhage, patients may present with hemiparesis, aphasia, or cranial nerve palsies.
  • Check papilloedema - A sign of raised intracranial pressure, seen on fundoscopic examination.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Subarachnoid Haemorrhage (SAH)"][/sc]
  • Thunderclap headache - A hallmark of SAH, indicating rapid intracranial bleeding and increased pressure.
  • Rapid deterioration in consciousness - Suggests significant bleeding or impending brain herniation.
  • Severe neck stiffness - Indicates meningeal irritation from the haemorrhage, particularly in larger bleeds.
  • Focal neurological signs - May indicate a more localised bleed or associated vasospasm causing ischaemia.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Subarachnoid Haemorrhage (SAH)"][/sc]
  • Mild headache - Some patients may present with a less severe headache, leading to a missed or delayed diagnosis.
  • Confusion without headache - In elderly patients, SAH may present primarily with confusion or altered mental status, without the classical headache.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Subarachnoid Haemorrhage (SAH)"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: CT Head - First-line test; reveals blood in the subarachnoid space in 95% of cases within the first 24 hours.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Lumbar puncture - If CT is negative but suspicion remains high; CSF analysis will show xanthochromia (yellowish discolouration) due to breakdown.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Subarachnoid Haemorrhage (SAH)"][/sc]
  • Migraine - Typically has a slower onset and associated with auras; lacks neck stiffness or altered.
  • Meningitis - May present with headache and neck stiffness, but fever and a more gradual onset.
  • Intracerebral haemorrhage - May present similarly, but the bleeding is typically confined to brain parenchyma and does.
  • Related alternative diagnosis - Distinguish from subarachnoid haemorrhage (sah) using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Subarachnoid Haemorrhage (SAH)"][/sc]
  • Re-bleeding - Occurs in 20% of untreated cases, with a high risk of mortality.
  • Vasospasm - Causes delayed cerebral ischaemia, leading to stroke-like symptoms.
  • Hydrocephalus - Due to blockage of cerebrospinal fluid pathways by blood clots, leading to raised intracranial pressure.
