Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Salivary Gland Tumour"][/sc]
  • Salivary gland tumours arise from the epithelial or myoepithelial cells of the glandular tissue. Benign tumours, such as pleomorphic adenoma, result from slow, localised cellular proliferation, whereas malignant tumours (e.g. adenoid cystic carcinoma) involve genetic mutations, leading to uncontrolled cellular growth and invasion into surrounding tissues.
  • Malignant tumours can spread via the lymphatic system, leading to regional lymph node involvement, or metastasise via haematogenous routes, particularly affecting the lungs.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Salivary Gland Tumour"][/sc]
  • Benign tumours: Often slow-growing over months to years and can remain asymptomatic for a long time.
  • Malignant tumours: May exhibit more rapid growth, with potential for invasion into adjacent tissues and distant metastasis over weeks to months.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Salivary Gland Tumour"][/sc]
  • Ask about painless swelling: The most common symptom, particularly in benign tumours. This occurs due to the slow enlargement of the mass within the gland, with associated clinical features.
  • Ask about pain: More common in malignant tumours, where invasion of nearby nerves can cause discomfort or frank pain.
  • Ask about facial nerve weakness: Suggestive of malignant involvement of the facial nerve, particularly in parotid gland tumours.
  • Ask about difficulty swallowing or chewing: Tumours involving the submandibular or sublingual glands can compress adjacent structures like the jaw or tongue.
  • Ask about trismus: Difficulty opening the mouth, often due to tumour invasion into the masseter or pterygoid muscles.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Salivary Gland Tumour"][/sc]
  • Check palpable mass: A firm, non-tender, and sometimes mobile mass is typically felt, especially in the parotid region.
  • Check facial asymmetry: May occur with larger tumours or those affecting the facial nerve, resulting in muscle weakness.
  • Check facial nerve palsy: Indicates malignancy, especially if there is loss of facial movement, drooping of the mouth, or inability to close the eye.
  • Check lymphadenopathy: Suggests metastatic spread or inflammation if lymph nodes are enlarged.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Salivary Gland Tumour"][/sc]
  • Rapid growth: Suggests malignant transformation or a high-grade malignancy.
  • Facial nerve paralysis: Strong indicator of malignancy, particularly in parotid tumours, signifying local invasion.
  • Lymphadenopathy: May indicate metastatic disease, particularly in malignant salivary tumours.
  • Ulceration or fixation of the tumour: Suggests malignancy with local tissue invasion.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Salivary Gland Tumour"][/sc]
  • Small, mobile painless mass: Often seen in benign tumours like pleomorphic adenomas. These can be missed or mistaken for lymph nodes.
  • Non-specific neck discomfort: Sometimes the only presenting complaint in early-stage malignant tumours, particularly in submandibular gland involvement.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Salivary Gland Tumour"][/sc]
  • Lymphoma - Presents with lymphadenopathy, and systemic symptoms like fever or weight loss, unlike salivary gland.
  • Sialadenitis - Typically presents with pain and signs of infection such as fever, with swelling fluctuating.
  • Benign cyst - These are usually fluctuant, well-demarcated, and asymptomatic masses without facial nerve involvement.
  • Related alternative diagnosis - Distinguish from salivary gland tumour using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Salivary Gland Tumour"][/sc]
  • Facial nerve damage: This can occur during surgery, especially in parotidectomy, leading to facial weakness or paralysis.
  • Recurrence: Benign tumours such as pleomorphic adenoma have a 5-10% recurrence rate after surgery.
  • Metastasis: Malignant tumours, particularly adenoid cystic carcinoma, can metastasise to the lungs, leading to poor prognosis.
