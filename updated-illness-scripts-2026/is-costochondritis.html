Costochondritis
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Costochondritis"][/sc]
  • Costochondritis is an inflammation of the costal cartilages that connect the ribs to the sternum (breastbone).
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise costochondritis features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Costochondritis"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Costochondritis"][/sc]
  • Costochondritis Risk Factors: Physical overuse (e.g., repetitive lifting, strenuous exercise), direct trauma to the chest, poor posture, upper respiratory infections (persistent coughing), inflammatory disorders (e.g., rheumatoid arthritis, ankylosing spondylitis), and psychological stress, which can contribute to muscle tension and exacerbate symptoms.
  • Prevention: Maintaining good posture, avoiding excessive upper body strain, using proper lifting techniques, engaging in regular stretching and strength exercises, and managing stress effectively can help prevent costochondritis. Prompt treatment of respiratory infections to reduce chronic coughing may also lower the risk.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Costochondritis"][/sc]
  • Costochondritis results from inflammation of the cartilage at the costosternal, costochondral, or costovertebral joints. This inflammation is often caused by repetitive physical strain (e.g., heavy lifting, coughing), local trauma, or infection. The precise mechanism is unclear, but it may involve local microtrauma leading to inflammatory changes in the cartilage and surrounding tissues. This inflammation triggers pain by sensitising local nerve endings.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Costochondritis"][/sc]
  • Acute phase: Initial onset is characterised by sharp, localised chest pain. This phase may last from hours to days, depending on the severity of inflammation.
  • Chronic phase: Pain may become recurrent or chronic, lasting for weeks to months, especially with ongoing physical strain or inadequate rest.
  • Resolution: Most cases resolve within a few weeks to months, especially with appropriate management (e.g., NSAIDs, rest).
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Costochondritis"][/sc]
  • Ask about sharp chest pain - Localised to the costosternal or costochondral junctions. This pain worsens with movement, coughing, or deep breathing due to inflammation irritating.
  • Ask about pressure-like discomfort - Often described as a dull ache following episodes of sharp pain, which may persist for hours due to ongoing inflammatory processes.
  • Ask about unilateral chest pain - Typically affects one side of the chest, especially on the left side, leading to concerns about cardiac pathology.
  • Ask about pain on palpation - Local tenderness is a key diagnostic feature; palpation of the costal cartilage reproduces the pain.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Costochondritis"][/sc]
  • Check localised tenderness - Palpation over the affected costosternal joints triggers the same pain the patient describes, supporting the diagnosis of costochondritis.
  • Check absence of swelling - Unlike Tietze syndrome, which presents with swelling, costochondritis shows no visible or palpable swelling despite the pain.
  • Check normal cardiac and respiratory examination - No abnormalities are typically found in heart or lung sounds, helping to rule out serious causes of chest.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Costochondritis"][/sc]
  • Persistent pain without improvement - May suggest an underlying pathology, such as a tumour, requiring further investigation.
  • Pain associated with fever or night sweats - Could indicate an infectious cause like septic arthritis or osteomyelitis.
  • Pain with weight loss or neurological deficits - Could be a sign of malignancy, metastasis, or nerve compression and needs urgent assessment.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Costochondritis"][/sc]
  • Bilateral chest pain - Uncommon in costochondritis and may mislead clinicians to suspect other systemic conditions like autoimmune disease.
  • Referred pain - Patients may describe shoulder or back pain due to irritation of intercostal nerves, which can confuse the diagnosis with musculoskeletal or spinal disorders.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask costochondritis.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Costochondritis"][/sc]
  • Myocardial infarction - Typically associated with crushing, substernal chest pain, often radiating to the arm or jaw.
  • Tietze syndrome - Presents similarly but with visible or palpable swelling over the affected costal cartilage.
  • Pleurisy - Characterised by sharp pain with breathing, often associated with respiratory infection, and may present.
  • Rib fracture - Distinguish from costochondritis using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Costochondritis"][/sc]
  • Chronic pain - Occurs in a minority of cases and may require long-term management with analgesics and physiotherapy.
  • Misdiagnosis of cardiac event - This is a common complication due to overlapping symptoms, leading to unnecessary investigations or hospital admissions.
