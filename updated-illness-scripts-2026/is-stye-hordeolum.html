Stye (Hordeolum)
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Stye (Hordeolum)"][/sc]
  • A stye (hordeolum) is a localised, acute bacterial infection or inflammation of the sebaceous glands of the eyelid, typically caused by Staphylococcus aureus.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise stye features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Stye (Hordeolum)"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Stye (Hordeolum)"][/sc]
  • Risk factors: Risk factors for stye (hordeolum) include relevant predisposition, comorbidity, and exposure history.
  • Higher-risk or missed groups: Stye (Hordeolum) may be missed when presentation is atypical or obscured by comorbidity.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Stye (Hordeolum)"][/sc]
  • Blockage of the sebaceous glands creates a favourable environment for bacterial overgrowth, typically by Staphylococcus aureus.
  • Inflammation ensues, resulting in redness, swelling, and pain.
  • If untreated, infection may spread to surrounding tissue, leading to preseptal cellulitis or abscess formation.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Stye (Hordeolum)"][/sc]
  • Acute onset: Develops rapidly over 1–2 days.
  • Resolution: Typically resolves spontaneously within 7–10 days with conservative management. Larger lesions may persist longer or require intervention.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Stye (Hordeolum)"][/sc]
  • Ask about localised eyelid pain: Due to inflammation and pressure from the swelling.
  • Ask about swelling: Caused by accumulation of inflammatory exudates in the glandular tissue.
  • Ask about redness: Secondary to increased blood flow from inflammation.
  • Ask about foreign body sensation: Irritation from the swelling impinging on adjacent structures.
  • Ask about watery eyes: Reflex lacrimation in response to irritation.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Stye (Hordeolum)"][/sc]
  • Check focal swelling: Localised, tender lump on the eyelid margin (external stye) or beneath the lid surface (internal stye).
  • Check erythema: Localised redness due to vascular dilation and inflammation.
  • Check fluctuance: Suggests pus collection, particularly in larger lesions.
  • Check crusting: May occur if the lesion drains spontaneously.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Stye (Hordeolum)"][/sc]
  • Periorbital swelling: May indicate spread to surrounding tissues, raising concern for preseptal or orbital cellulitis.
  • Fever: Suggests systemic involvement or severe infection.
  • Visual disturbance: Indicates possible compression or inflammation of ocular structures, requiring urgent evaluation.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Stye (Hordeolum)"][/sc]
  • Chronic or recurrent styes: May indicate underlying conditions like blepharitis or rosacea.
  • Painless swelling: Suggestive of a chalazion rather than a stye.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask stye (hordeolum).
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Stye (Hordeolum)"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Clinical examination: Diagnosis is typically clinical, based on characteristic symptoms and signs.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Microbial culture: Reserved for recurrent or non-resolving cases, particularly to identify resistant organisms.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Stye (Hordeolum)"][/sc]
  • Chalazion - Chronic, painless swelling without acute inflammation.
  • Preseptal cellulitis - Diffuse swelling and erythema without a focal lesion; systemic symptoms may be present.
  • Orbital cellulitis - Painful proptosis, ophthalmoplegia, and systemic symptoms.
  • Related alternative diagnosis - Distinguish from stye (hordeolum) using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Stye (Hordeolum)"][/sc]
  • Preseptal cellulitis: Spread of infection to surrounding eyelid tissues, requiring systemic antibiotics.
  • Chronic chalazion: Resulting from unresolved blockage.
