Thrombocytopenia
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Thrombocytopenia"][/sc]
  • Thrombocytopenia is defined as a platelet count of less than 150 x 10 9 /L in peripheral blood.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise thrombocytopenia features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.

  • Higher-risk or missed groups: Thrombocytopenia may be missed when presentation is atypical or obscured by comorbidity.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Thrombocytopenia"][/sc]
  • Thrombocytopenia occurs due to one or more of the following mechanisms:
  • Decreased platelet production – Resulting from bone marrow suppression (e.g., leukaemia, aplastic anaemia, chemotherapy, infections like HIV).
  • Increased platelet destruction – As seen in immune-mediated conditions (e.g., ITP, systemic lupus erythematosus) or non-immune causes like disseminated intravascular coagulation (DIC).
  • Sequestration – Occurs when platelets are trapped in the spleen, often seen in patients with splenomegaly, as in liver cirrhosis.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Thrombocytopenia"][/sc]
  • Acute: May occur within hours to days in cases of acute infections, drug-induced thrombocytopenia, or DIC.
  • Subacute/Chronic: Conditions like ITP or bone marrow failure syndromes often develop more gradually over weeks to months, while chronic liver disease can cause long-term thrombocytopenia due to splenic sequestration.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Thrombocytopenia"][/sc]
  • Ask about asymptomatic – Many patients remain asymptomatic, especially if platelet counts are above 50 x 10 9 /L, as coagulation remains relatively preserved.
  • Ask about easy bruising (Ecchymoses) – Caused by capillary fragility due to insufficient platelet-mediated haemostasis.
  • Ask about prolonged bleeding from minor cuts – Impaired clot formation leads to increased bleeding duration from even superficial wounds.
  • Ask about menorrhagia – Common in women due to the inability to maintain adequate platelet plug formation during menstruation.
  • Ask about spontaneous mucosal bleeding – Particularly nasal or oral bleeding; this indicates a severe reduction in platelet count, as mucosal membranes are prone to minor.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Thrombocytopenia"][/sc]
  • Check purpura – Non-blanching red or purple lesions caused by extravasation of blood due to impaired platelet function.
  • Check petechiae – Pinpoint haemorrhages in the skin and mucous membranes, a common finding in severe thrombocytopenia (<30 x 10 9 /L).
  • Check haematoma – Large areas of bruising may develop after minor trauma due to insufficient haemostasis.
  • Check splenomegaly – Seen in conditions such as liver cirrhosis, where platelet sequestration in an enlarged spleen leads to thrombocytopenia.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Thrombocytopenia"][/sc]
  • Spontaneous intracranial haemorrhage – A life-threatening complication in severe thrombocytopenia (<10 x 109/L) due to the inability to maintain cerebral microvascular integrity.
  • Gastrointestinal bleeding – Suggests a critical reduction in platelet count and increased risk for haemorrhage, potentially requiring urgent transfusion and endoscopic intervention.
  • Haematuria – May indicate severe thrombocytopenia or underlying systemic disease (e.g., DIC, vasculitis).
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Thrombocytopenia"][/sc]
  • Painless, severe purpura without any bruising – May occur in elderly patients where thrombocytopenia is due to bone marrow suppression rather than immune-mediated destruction.
  • Thrombocytopenia without bleeding – Found incidentally during routine blood tests, particularly in patients with chronic liver disease or splenomegaly.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask thrombocytopenia.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Thrombocytopenia"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Full Blood Count (FBC) – Confirms low platelet count (<150 x 10 9 /L) and may reveal concurrent anaemia or leukopenia.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Targeted investigations to confirm diagnosis, assess complications, and monitor response in thrombocytopenia.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Thrombocytopenia"][/sc]
  • Severe bleeding – Includes gastrointestinal and intracranial haemorrhage, which can be fatal, particularly when platelet counts drop below 10 x 109/L.
  • Chronic fatigue – Seen in patients with long-term conditions like ITP, particularly when associated with anaemia or secondary infections.
  • Secondary infections – Result from immunosuppression, particularly in patients treated with corticosteroids or immunosuppressive agents.
