Fracture or Dislocation
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Fracture or Dislocation"][/sc]
  • Fracture: A structural break in the continuity of bone caused by trauma, pathological processes, or repetitive stress.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise fracture features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Fracture or Dislocation"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Fracture or Dislocation"][/sc]
  • Global trends: Both conditions are frequent, with trauma being a leading cause of morbidity worldwide.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Fracture or Dislocation"][/sc]
  • Fractures occur when bone undergoes mechanical stress exceeding its tensile or compressive strength, often compounded by reduced bone mineral density (e.g., osteoporosis).
  • Dislocations result from direct or indirect forces disrupting ligamentous integrity, causing joint capsule failure or ligament tears.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Fracture or Dislocation"][/sc]
  • Acute: Immediate pain, swelling, and functional loss.
  • Subacute: Healing with potential immobilisation or surgical intervention (days to weeks).
  • Chronic: Potential for complications like non-union (fractures) or recurrent instability (dislocations).
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Fracture or Dislocation"][/sc]
  • Ask about pain: Localised or diffuse due to periosteal irritation (fractures) or joint capsule stretch (dislocations).
  • Ask about swelling: Results from inflammatory response and haemorrhage at the injury site.
  • Ask about functional impairment: Limited motion or inability to bear weight reflects structural damage.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Fracture or Dislocation"][/sc]
  • Check deformity: Visible misalignment in fractures or joint displacement in dislocations.
  • Check tenderness: Localised pain on palpation, indicating the site of injury.
  • Check reduced range of motion: Limited by pain or mechanical block from the injury.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Fracture or Dislocation"][/sc]
  • Neurovascular compromise: Tingling, numbness, or absent pulses may indicate compartment syndrome or vascular injury.
  • Open fractures: Risk of infection and poor healing necessitate urgent care.
  • Hip dislocations: Prompt reduction is critical to prevent avascular necrosis.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Fracture or Dislocation"][/sc]
  • Missed scaphoid fracture: Wrist pain without deformity can be mistaken for a sprain.
  • Congenital dislocations: May present as developmental delays rather than acute symptoms.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Fracture or Dislocation"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: X-ray: Gold standard for fractures; shows cortical disruption or joint incongruity.
  • Targeted and monitoring: CT scan: Useful for complex fractures or subtle dislocations; MRI: Evaluates soft tissue or occult fractures.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Fracture or Dislocation"][/sc]
  • A 35-year-old male presents with acute shoulder pain and deformity following a fall, unable to abduct the arm.
  • Patient with acute shoulder pain and deformity following a fall, unable to abduct the arm, consistent with fracture or dislocation.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Fracture or Dislocation"][/sc]
  • Soft tissue injury - No visible deformity on X-ray, localised pain, but normal joint congruence.
  • Ligament sprain - Swelling and instability without X-ray evidence of dislocation or fracture.
  • Osteomyelitis - Fever and bone pain, but lacks trauma history.
  • Related alternative diagnosis - Distinguish from fracture or dislocation using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Fracture or Dislocation"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Assess severity, physiological stability, and immediate complications.
  • Core care: Deliver condition-specific management for fracture or dislocation, treat underlying drivers, and coordinate appropriate specialist input.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Reassess symptom trajectory, examination findings, and objective markers to confirm response in fracture or dislocation.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Escalate urgently for clinical deterioration, evolving red flags, or failure to improve in suspected fracture or dislocation.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Fracture or Dislocation"][/sc]
  • Malunion: Poor alignment post-healing, leading to functional impairment.
  • Avascular necrosis: Common in femoral neck fractures or dislocations, due to disrupted blood supply.
  • Chronic pain: May result from nerve injury or joint instability.
