Nasal Polyps
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Nasal Polyps"][/sc]
  • Nasal polyps are benign, soft, painless, and non-cancerous growths that develop on the lining of the nasal passages or sinuses due to chronic inflammation.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise nasal features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Nasal Polyps"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Nasal Polyps"][/sc]
  • Risk factors: Risk factors for nasal polyps include relevant predisposition, comorbidity, and exposure history.
  • Higher-risk or missed groups: Nasal Polyps may be missed when presentation is atypical or obscured by comorbidity.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Nasal Polyps"][/sc]
  • Chronic inflammation, often due to conditions like allergies, infections, or asthma, leads to an overproduction of cytokines and other inflammatory mediators. This causes oedema and hyperplasia of the nasal mucosa. Over time, this results in the formation of soft, gelatinous polyps, most commonly in the middle meatus of the nasal cavity.
  • In some patients, particularly those with aspirin-exacerbated respiratory disease (AERD), nasal polyps are associated with systemic eosinophilic inflammation.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Nasal Polyps"][/sc]
  • Gradual onset over months to years. Chronic sinus inflammation can lead to the slow formation of polyps, with symptoms progressively worsening if left untreated. They may recur despite treatment, particularly in patients with chronic inflammatory conditions.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Nasal Polyps"][/sc]
  • Ask about nasal obstruction - Caused by physical blockage from the polyp tissue obstructing airflow through the nasal passages.
  • Ask about rhinorrhoea (runny nose) - Occurs due to chronic inflammation and excessive mucus production in response to the polyp-induced irritation.
  • Ask about hyposmia (reduced sense of smell) - Polyps physically block the olfactory cleft, preventing airflow to the olfactory nerve endings.
  • Ask about facial pain/pressure - Secondary to sinus congestion and inflammation, especially if the sinuses become infected.
  • Ask about snoring or sleep apnoea - Due to obstruction of the upper airway, leading to increased airway resistance during sleep.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Nasal Polyps"][/sc]
  • Check visible nasal polyps on anterior rhinoscopy - Soft, smooth, pale grey or yellowish masses seen in the nasal cavity.
  • Check nasal congestion or reduced nasal airflow - Evident during examination as the nasal passages are physically narrowed.
  • Check postnasal drip - Mucus accumulation in the throat, caused by increased mucus production due to inflammation.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Nasal Polyps"][/sc]
  • Unilateral nasal obstruction or polyps - May indicate a neoplasm, such as inverted papilloma or nasopharyngeal carcinoma.
  • Recurrent epistaxis - Could suggest malignancy or underlying vascular abnormality.
  • Severe headache or visual changes - Potential signs of sinus involvement extending into the orbit or brain, indicating invasive fungal disease or malignancy.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Nasal Polyps"][/sc]
  • Absence of typical symptoms - Some patients, especially in early stages, may have minimal symptoms despite large polyps.
  • Children - Nasal polyps in children are rare and could be a sign of cystic fibrosis; a sweat test should be considered in these cases.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask nasal polyps.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Nasal Polyps"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Nasal endoscopy - Direct visualisation of polyps in the nasal passages, providing a clear assessment of size, location, and extent; CT.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Allergy testing - Skin prick or serum IgE testing to identify underlying allergic triggers; Blood eosinophil count - Elevated levels may.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Nasal Polyps"][/sc]
  • Chronic rhinosinusitis without polyps - Presents with nasal congestion and rhinorrhoea but lacks visible polyps.
  • Inverted papilloma - Typically unilateral, often associated with epistaxis, and can be more locally destructive than benign.
  • Nasopharyngeal carcinoma - Unilateral obstruction, recurrent epistaxis, and potential facial or cranial nerve involvement.
  • Fungal sinusitis - Distinguish from nasal polyps using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.

  • Immediate priorities: Assess severity, physiological stability, and immediate complications.
  • Core care: Deliver condition-specific management for nasal polyps, treat underlying drivers, and coordinate appropriate specialist input.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Reassess symptom trajectory, examination findings, and objective markers to confirm response in nasal polyps.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Escalate urgently for clinical deterioration, evolving red flags, or failure to improve in suspected nasal polyps.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Nasal Polyps"][/sc]
  • Recurrent infections - Chronic nasal obstruction and inflammation predispose to bacterial sinus infections.
  • Sleep apnoea - Due to upper airway obstruction, polyps can contribute to obstructive sleep apnoea in some patients.
  • Asthma exacerbations - Particularly in patients with AERD, nasal polyps can be part of a triad that includes asthma and sensitivity to aspirin.
