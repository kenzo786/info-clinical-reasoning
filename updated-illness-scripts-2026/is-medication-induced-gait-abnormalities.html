Medication-Induced Gait Abnormalities
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Medication-Induced Gait Abnormalities"][/sc]
  • Medication-induced gait abnormalities refer to alterations in walking patterns or postural stability caused by pharmacological agents.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise medication features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.

Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Medication-Induced Gait Abnormalities"][/sc]
  • Drug-induced gait changes primarily affect the central nervous system (CNS) through effects on neurotransmitters like dopamine, GABA, and serotonin. CNS-acting drugs (e.g., antipsychotics, benzodiazepines) can cause extrapyramidal symptoms (EPS) or sedation, leading to motor instability and poor coordination.
  • Anticholinergic drugs impair proprioceptive feedback and balance, contributing to gait instability.
  • Some antihypertensives (e.g., beta-blockers) affect blood flow and vascular tone, potentially causing postural hypotension and dizziness, which increases the risk of falls and gait disturbances.

  • Acute: Gait disturbances may appear within hours to days after initiating or increasing the Use local protocol-directed treatment.g., sedatives, antipsychotics).
  • Subacute: Symptoms may develop over several days to weeks, especially in drugs that gradually accumulate or reach steady-state levels (e.g., lithium, some antidepressants).
  • Chronic: In some cases, persistent gait abnormalities can occur with prolonged exposure to drugs causing long-term neurological changes (e.g., tardive dyskinesia from chronic antipsychotic use).
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Medication-Induced Gait Abnormalities"][/sc]
  • Ask about unsteadiness - Patients often report feeling off-balance due to sedation or postural instability, especially with CNS depressants like benzodiazepines.
  • Ask about shuffling Gait - A characteristic gait pattern, particularly in patients on antipsychotics due to extrapyramidal side effects (EPS) impacting motor control.
  • Ask about slowed Movements (Bradykinesia) - Common with dopamine antagonists (antipsychotics), bradykinesia results from blocked dopamine pathways affecting voluntary motor function.
  • Ask about stiffness or Rigidity - Often seen with antipsychotics, rigidity arises from neurotransmitter dysregulation affecting motor neuron pathways.
  • Ask about dizziness - Seen in antihypertensives causing orthostatic hypotension; reduced cerebral blood flow causes dizziness and increases fall risk.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Medication-Induced Gait Abnormalities"][/sc]
  • Check ataxic Gait - Impaired coordination may be observed, especially with sedative medications affecting the cerebellum and proprioceptive feedback.
  • Check postural Instability - Frequently seen in benzodiazepine or antipsychotic use, due to CNS depressant effects that impair balance mechanisms.
  • Check tremor - Medications such as lithium or SSRIs can induce tremors through their effects on neurotransmitter regulation.
  • Check hypotension on Standing - Often observed with antihypertensives (especially alpha-blockers) and causes dizziness, increasing fall risk during ambulation.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Medication-Induced Gait Abnormalities"][/sc]
  • Severe Extrapyramidal Symptoms (EPS) - This includes acute dystonia, which can indicate a serious drug reaction and requires immediate attention to prevent airway compromise.
  • Persistent Orthostatic Hypotension - Severe or unresponsive hypotension may indicate the need to adjust or discontinue medication to avoid falls and injury.
  • Altered Mental Status or Confusion - Significant cognitive impairment may signify overdose or toxic effects, particularly in elderly patients with polypharmacy.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Medication-Induced Gait Abnormalities"][/sc]
  • Intermittent Gait Disturbance - Periodic gait instability, particularly with drugs with variable serum levels (e.g., lithium).
  • Masked Bradykinesia - Patients on antipsychotics may develop subtle bradykinesia mistaken for age-related slowness.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask medication-induced gait abnormalities.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Medication-Induced Gait Abnormalities"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Serum Drug Levels - Used to measure drugs like lithium, where serum level monitoring can reveal toxicity contributing to gait issues.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Postural Blood Pressure Measurements - Drop in BP upon standing can confirm orthostatic hypotension induced by antihypertensives; Neurological Examination - Assesses.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Medication-Induced Gait Abnormalities"][/sc]
  • Parkinson’s Disease - Progressive neurodegenerative disorder with similar bradykinesia but usually lacks a clear medication link.
  • Stroke - Distinguish from medication-induced gait abnormalities using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.
  • Normal Pressure Hydrocephalus (NPH) - Gait apraxia, urinary incontinence, and cognitive dysfunction are typical but can mimic medication effects.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Medication-Induced Gait Abnormalities"][/sc]
  • Falls and Fractures - Increased fall risk due to gait instability, particularly in older adults; fractures are a common outcome.
  • Extrapyramidal Symptoms - Chronic use of certain drugs (e.g., antipsychotics) can lead to persistent movement disorders.
  • Deconditioning - Patients may become less active due to fear of falling, worsening overall mobility.
