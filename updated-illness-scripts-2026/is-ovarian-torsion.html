Ovarian Torsion
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Ovarian Torsion"][/sc]
  • Ovarian torsion is the partial or complete rotation of the ovary around its supporting ligaments, leading to vascular compromise.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise ovarian features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.

Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Ovarian Torsion"][/sc]
  • Ovarian torsion often involves the twisting of both the ovary and fallopian tube around the infundibulopelvic and utero-ovarian ligaments. This twist occludes venous outflow, causing congestion, oedema, and ultimately, arterial occlusion. Without prompt intervention, this leads to tissue necrosis and ovarian loss.
  • Risk factors include ovarian enlargement (e.g., cysts, tumours), ovulation induction, pregnancy, and reproductive age. These factors increase the ovary's mobility and likelihood of torsion.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Ovarian Torsion"][/sc]
  • Acute phase: Sudden onset of severe pain, lasting hours, that can progress rapidly to constant severe pain. Vascular compromise can lead to necrosis within hours to days.
  • Subacute phase: Occasionally, the pain fluctuates as the ovary intermittently untwists, leading to spontaneous resolution in rare cases. However, risk of permanent damage remains high.
  • Chronic phase: Chronic pelvic pain may develop if untreated, though cases rarely progress this far due to the severe nature of symptoms in acute cases.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Ovarian Torsion"][/sc]
  • Ask about severe unilateral pelvic pain - Often the most common presenting symptom; results from vascular occlusion and ischaemia of the ovarian tissue.
  • Ask about nausea and vomiting - Common in response to the severe pain and autonomic activation.
  • Ask about radiating pain to the back or thigh - Occurs due to involvement of the pelvic nerve plexus, which may lead to referred pain.
  • Ask about acute abdominal pain - Generalised abdominal pain may occur, often prompting initial suspicion of an intra-abdominal cause.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Ovarian Torsion"][/sc]
  • Check tenderness in the lower abdomen - Localised tenderness, especially over the affected ovary, due to the ischaemic pain.
  • Check guarding or rebound tenderness - Indicates peritoneal irritation from the ischaemic ovarian tissue.
  • Check palpable adnexal mass - Occasionally found on pelvic examination if the ovary is enlarged due to a cyst or tumour.
  • Check tachycardia - Occurs due to pain-induced sympathetic stimulation.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Ovarian Torsion"][/sc]
  • Sudden severe unilateral pain - Signifies acute torsion, indicating potential ischaemia and risk of necrosis.
  • Vomiting and nausea - Suggests severe pain that may accompany an emergency condition.
  • Fever - May indicate ovarian necrosis with potential for infection if torsion persists untreated.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Ovarian Torsion"][/sc]
  • Intermittent or fluctuating pain - Torsion can be intermittent if the ovary untwists, leading to confusing or atypical pain presentation.
  • Mild or no initial pain - Rarely, mild pain or discomfort may be present initially, especially in early or partial torsion cases.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask ovarian torsion.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Ovarian Torsion"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Transvaginal ultrasound (TVUS) - Preferred imaging modality; may show an enlarged ovary, peripheralised follicles, and free pelvic fluid, indicating torsion.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Doppler ultrasound - May demonstrate absent or reduced ovarian blood flow, although normal flow does not rule out torsion as arterial.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Ovarian Torsion"][/sc]
  • Acute appendicitis - Typically presents with right lower quadrant pain and tenderness, though does not usually cause.
  • Ectopic pregnancy - Presents with unilateral pelvic pain and can cause nausea; ruled out by a negative.
  • Ruptured ovarian cyst - Sudden pain onset similar to torsion, though pain may be less intense and Doppler.
  • Pelvic inflammatory disease - Pain is often bilateral with cervical motion tenderness and associated fever; Doppler flow is.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Ovarian Torsion"][/sc]
  • Ovarian necrosis - Occurs if blood flow is not restored promptly, leading to loss of ovarian function and potential fertility issues.
  • Infection and peritonitis - Necrotic ovarian tissue may become infected, leading to peritonitis if untreated.
  • Chronic pelvic pain - Long-term pain may develop after torsion, particularly if there has been significant tissue damage.
