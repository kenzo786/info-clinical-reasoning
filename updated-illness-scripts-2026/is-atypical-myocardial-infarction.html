Atypical Myocardial Infarction
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Atypical Myocardial Infarction"][/sc]
  • Atypical Myocardial Infarction (MI) is a form of myocardial infarction that presents with symptoms not typical for classic angina or myocardial infarction, such as chest pain.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise atypical features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Atypical Myocardial Infarction"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Atypical Myocardial Infarction"][/sc]
  • Risk factors: Risk of atypical myocardial infarction rises with relevant predisposition, comorbidity, and exposure profile.
  • Higher-risk or missed groups: Atypical Myocardial Infarction may be missed when presentations are atypical or comorbidity obscures key features.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Atypical Myocardial Infarction"][/sc]
  • MI occurs due to a sudden occlusion of a coronary artery, often due to atherosclerotic plaque rupture, leading to reduced blood flow and ischemia in the myocardial tissue.
  • Atypical presentation is associated with variations in autonomic neuropathy and altered pain perception, as well as differences in coronary artery disease (CAD) distribution.
  • For example, diabetic patients have increased risk of “silent ischemia” due to autonomic neuropathy, which diminishes pain response.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Atypical Myocardial Infarction"][/sc]
  • Acute Phase: Symptoms develop suddenly over minutes to hours, though presentation may be subtle and evolve slowly in some cases.
  • Subacute Phase: Some atypical presentations may progress over several hours or days with mild symptoms, such as fatigue or nausea.
  • Chronic Phase: Following acute management, some patients continue with post-MI sequelae, such as heart failure or arrhythmias, requiring ongoing care.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Atypical Myocardial Infarction"][/sc]
  • Ask about fatigue - Common due to systemic response to myocardial ischemia; results from reduced cardiac output affecting systemic perfusion.
  • Ask about dyspnoea - Reflects left ventricular dysfunction and congestion in pulmonary circulation.
  • Ask about nausea and Vomiting - Due to vagal stimulation; common in inferior wall MIs due to proximity of vagal nerve.
  • Ask about epigastric or Abdominal Pain - Results from referred pain; often misinterpreted as a gastrointestinal issue.
  • Ask about weakness - Generalised weakness occurs due to decreased cardiac output and perfusion to skeletal muscles.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Atypical Myocardial Infarction"][/sc]
  • Check tachycardia - Sympathetic activation in response to myocardial ischemia and reduced cardiac output.
  • Check hypotension - May indicate cardiogenic shock in severe cases, especially with extensive myocardial damage.
  • Check diaphoresis - Due to sympathetic activation, often accompanying ischemic events.
  • Check pallor - Systemic hypoperfusion can lead to a pale appearance.
  • Check signs of Heart Failure - S3 heart sound, pulmonary crackles; due to decreased left ventricular function.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Atypical Myocardial Infarction"][/sc]
  • Severe Dyspnoea - Indicates potential left heart failure or pulmonary oedema.
  • Hypotension with Cold Extremities - Suggestive of cardiogenic shock, a medical emergency with high mortality.
  • Altered Mental Status - Often seen in severe hypoperfusion, possibly due to reduced cerebral perfusion.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Atypical Myocardial Infarction"][/sc]
  • Silent MI - Seen in diabetics and older adults; may lack classic chest pain due to neuropathy or altered pain threshold.
  • Epigastric Pain - Commonly misdiagnosed as peptic ulcer disease or gastritis, especially in inferior wall MI.
  • Isolated Dyspnoea - More common in females and older adults; misinterpreted as a respiratory issue.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features or comorbidity can mask the underlying diagnosis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Atypical Myocardial Infarction"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: ECG - ST-segment elevation or depression, T-wave inversion. Changes depend on infarct location and may be subtle or non-specific in atypical.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Cardiac Enzymes - Elevated troponin levels confirm myocardial injury; gradual rise may occur, particularly in subacute presentations; Coronary Angiography - Identifies.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Atypical Myocardial Infarction"][/sc]
  • A 72-year-old female with a history of diabetes presents with two days of fatigue and nausea, and recent-onset dyspnoea. No chest pain reported. ECG reveals T-wave inversions in the inferior.
  • Adult with symptom pattern and objective findings suggestive of atypical myocardial infarction, requiring focused confirmation and risk stratification.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Atypical Myocardial Infarction"][/sc]
  • Gastroesophageal Reflux Disease (GERD) - Presents with epigastric pain, but lacks elevated cardiac biomarkers.
  • Pulmonary Embolism - Dyspnoea and tachycardia are common but typically involves chest discomfort or pleuritic pain.
  • Heart Failure Exacerbation - Dyspnoea and fatigue, but without acute elevation of cardiac biomarkers and distinct ECG changes.
  • Alternative serious diagnosis - Distinguishing history, examination, and targeted investigations do not support atypical myocardial infarction.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Atypical Myocardial Infarction"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Assess physiological stability and urgent complications at presentation, and initiate early stabilisation where required.
  • Core care: Deliver condition-specific treatment for atypical myocardial infarction, address underlying drivers, and involve appropriate specialist support.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Reassess symptom trajectory, objective markers, and treatment response over time to detect complications early.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Worsening symptoms, physiological deterioration, or failure to improve should trigger urgent reassessment for severe or alternative pathology in atypical myocardial infarction.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Atypical Myocardial Infarction"][/sc]
  • Heart Failure - Common in extensive MIs; due to permanent myocardial damage and loss of function.
  • Arrhythmias - Atrial fibrillation or ventricular tachyarrhythmias; occur due to ischemic damage disrupting electrical conduction pathways.
  • Recurrent MI - Higher risk in patients with underlying comorbidities such as diabetes.
