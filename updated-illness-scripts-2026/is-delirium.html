Delirium
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Delirium"][/sc]
  • Delirium is an acute, fluctuating disturbance in attention, awareness, and cognition, commonly occurring in hospitalised patients.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise delirium features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Delirium"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Delirium"][/sc]
  • Risk factors: Risk factors for delirium include relevant predisposition, comorbidity, and exposure history.
  • Higher-risk or missed groups: Delirium may be missed when presentation is atypical or obscured by comorbidity.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Delirium"][/sc]
  • Delirium arises from disrupted neurotransmitter systems, notably acetylcholine and dopamine imbalances. Reduced acetylcholine, often due to systemic inflammation or hypoxia, affects cortical function, while increased dopamine activity contributes to hallucinations and agitation.
  • Inflammatory cytokines (e.g., IL-6, TNF-α) released during infections or surgery can alter the blood-brain barrier, exacerbating neural disruptions.
  • Impaired cerebral perfusion, metabolic disturbances, or acute changes in the internal environment (e.g., electrolyte imbalance) further disrupt neuronal communication.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Delirium"][/sc]
  • Onset: Develops rapidly over hours to days, often triggered by acute stressors or medical conditions.
  • Course: Typically fluctuates throughout the day, with symptoms worsening at night (sundowning).
  • Resolution: Can resolve within days with appropriate treatment, but may persist for weeks or even months, especially in older adults.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Delirium"][/sc]
  • Ask about confusion - Fluctuating levels of attention and disorientation due to disrupted cortical function.
  • Ask about inattention - Difficulty focusing on simple tasks as a result of impaired acetylcholine pathways affecting attention regulation.
  • Ask about hallucinations - Primarily visual, due to excess dopamine activity in the limbic system.
  • Ask about agitation or Restlessness - Driven by imbalances in dopamine and reduced cortical control.
  • Ask about sleep-Wake Disturbances - Often manifested as worsening of symptoms at night (sundowning), influenced by circadian rhythm dysregulation.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Delirium"][/sc]
  • Check disorientation - Patients often struggle with knowing time, place, or even personal details, linked to disrupted cortical function.
  • Check reduced Awareness - Decreased awareness of surroundings due to impaired neurotransmitter activity.
  • Check altered Psychomotor Activity - Hyperactivity or hypoactivity; hyperactivity is linked with dopamine excess, hypoactivity with sedative medications or severe metabolic disturbances.
  • Check incoherent Speech - Irregular speech patterns result from cognitive disruption, often related to fluctuating arousal levels.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Delirium"][/sc]
  • Rapid Onset - Sudden onset of symptoms suggests an acute trigger, such as infection or metabolic derangement.
  • Severe Agitation or Violence - Extreme behavioural disturbance may indicate underlying hypoxia or drug withdrawal.
  • Hallucinations with Paranoia - Risk of self-harm or harm to others; commonly associated with severe metabolic or toxic causes.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Delirium"][/sc]
  • Hypoactive Delirium - Presents with drowsiness, lethargy, and reduced interaction, often mistaken for depression, especially in older adults.
  • Subsyndromal Delirium - Milder symptoms without full diagnostic criteria, which may evolve into full delirium if untreated.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask delirium.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Delirium"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Full Blood Count (FBC) - May reveal infection (elevated WBC) or anaemia, both common triggers of delirium; Urea and Electrolytes (U&Es).
  • Targeted and monitoring: Liver Function Tests (LFTs) and Ammonia - Raised ammonia in liver failure can lead to hepatic encephalopathy; CT/MRI of the Brain.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Delirium"][/sc]
  • Dementia - Gradual onset and progressive, without the acute fluctuations typical of delirium.
  • Acute Psychosis - Typically lacks inattention and fluctuating course seen in delirium.
  • Depression - Hypoactive delirium may mimic depression, but delirium presents with fluctuating attention.
  • Related alternative diagnosis - Distinguish from delirium using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Delirium"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Assess severity, physiological stability, and immediate complications.
  • Core care: Deliver condition-specific management for delirium, treat underlying drivers, and coordinate appropriate specialist input.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Reassess symptom trajectory, examination findings, and objective markers to confirm response in delirium.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Escalate urgently for clinical deterioration, evolving red flags, or failure to improve in suspected delirium.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Delirium"][/sc]
  • Increased Mortality - Delirium is associated with a higher risk of death, especially if untreated or due to severe illness.
  • Functional Decline - Older adults often experience prolonged functional impairment post-delirium, potentially leading to institutionalisation.
  • Increased Risk of Dementia - Acute delirium episodes are linked to accelerated cognitive decline in vulnerable populations.
