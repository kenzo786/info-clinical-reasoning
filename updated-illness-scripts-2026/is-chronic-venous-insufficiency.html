Chronic Venous Insufficiency (CVI)
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Chronic Venous Insufficiency (CVI)"][/sc]
  • Chronic venous insufficiency (CVI) is a condition where the veins in the lower extremities fail to efficiently return blood to the heart due to valvular dysfunction.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise venous features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.

Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Chronic Venous Insufficiency (CVI)"][/sc]
  • In CVI, dysfunction of the venous valves causes blood to pool in the lower extremities. This leads to venous hypertension, capillary leakage, and inflammation. Chronic venous pressure damages the vein walls and surrounding tissues, resulting in oedema, skin changes, and eventually ulcers if untreated.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Chronic Venous Insufficiency (CVI)"][/sc]
  • Initial Phase: Symptoms may be mild, with occasional swelling and heaviness in the legs, usually worse after prolonged standing.
  • Intermediate Phase: Symptoms worsen with more consistent leg swelling, skin discolouration, and varicose veins.
  • Late Phase: Severe complications like venous ulcers, which may become chronic if not treated.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Chronic Venous Insufficiency (CVI)"][/sc]
  • Ask about leg swelling (oedema) - Caused by increased venous pressure leading to fluid leakage into the interstitial spaces.
  • Ask about leg heaviness or aching - Due to venous congestion and poor blood return.
  • Ask about itching or tingling - Skin changes and inflammation stimulate local nerves.
  • Ask about skin discolouration (haemosiderin deposits) - Chronic venous pressure causes red blood cell breakdown, leading to iron deposits in the skin.
  • Ask about venous ulcers - Severe form of skin damage due to persistent inflammation and tissue breakdown from prolonged venous stasis.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Chronic Venous Insufficiency (CVI)"][/sc]
  • Check pitting oedema - Venous hypertension forces fluid into the interstitial tissue, which leaves an indentation when pressed.
  • Check varicose veins - Dilated, tortuous veins due to prolonged venous stasis.
  • Check lipodermatosclerosis - Skin hardening and fibrosis caused by chronic inflammation and tissue remodelling in response to persistent venous hypertension.
  • Check venous ulcers - Often located near the medial malleolus, they result from chronic inflammation and skin breakdown.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Chronic Venous Insufficiency (CVI)"][/sc]
  • Sudden increase in pain or swelling - May indicate deep vein thrombosis (DVT), which could complicate into pulmonary embolism.
  • Non-healing venous ulcers - Suggestive of poor management and increased risk of infection or secondary complications like cellulitis.
  • Severe skin changes with necrosis - Possible venous gangrene, a serious complication requiring urgent intervention.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Chronic Venous Insufficiency (CVI)"][/sc]
  • Asymptomatic varicose veins - Some patients may present with significant venous reflux without noticeable symptoms.
  • Venous claudication - Pain upon exertion, mimicking arterial disease, due to venous outflow obstruction.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features or comorbidity can mask the underlying diagnosis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Chronic Venous Insufficiency (CVI)"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Doppler ultrasound - Shows reflux and venous insufficiency by visualising blood flow in the veins.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Ankle-brachial index (ABI) - Used to rule out arterial disease if there are ulcers, as ABI should be >0.9 in CVI.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Chronic Venous Insufficiency (CVI)"][/sc]
  • A 58-year-old woman presents with progressive leg swelling, skin discolouration, and occasional venous ulcers on her lower legs over the past year. She reports significant aching, worse with prolonged standing.
  • Adult with symptom pattern and objective findings suggestive of chronic venous insufficiency (cvi), requiring focused confirmation and risk stratification.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Chronic Venous Insufficiency (CVI)"][/sc]
  • Deep vein thrombosis (DVT) - Acute onset of swelling, pain, and redness, often unilateral, with a risk of pulmonary.
  • Lymphoedema - Often painless swelling, typically non-pitting in later stages. Doppler ultrasound and lack of venous.
  • Peripheral arterial disease (PAD) - Claudication and ulceration but associated with reduced ankle-brachial index (ABI), distinguishing it from CVI.
  • Alternative serious diagnosis - Distinguishing history, examination, and targeted investigations do not support chronic venous insufficiency (cvi).
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Chronic Venous Insufficiency (CVI)"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Assess physiological stability and urgent complications at presentation, and initiate early stabilisation where required.
  • Core care: Deliver condition-specific treatment for chronic venous insufficiency (cvi), address underlying drivers, and involve appropriate specialist support.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Reassess symptom trajectory, objective markers, and treatment response over time to detect complications early.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Worsening symptoms, physiological deterioration, or failure to improve should trigger urgent reassessment for severe or alternative pathology in chronic venous insufficiency (cvi).
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Chronic Venous Insufficiency (CVI)"][/sc]
  • Venous ulcers - Non-healing ulcers due to persistent venous stasis and inflammation.
  • Cellulitis - Secondary infection due to skin breakdown.
  • Venous gangrene - Rare, severe complication where tissue necrosis occurs due to chronic venous insufficiency and thrombosis.
