Drug Withdrawal
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Drug Withdrawal"][/sc]
  • Drug withdrawal refers to the physiological and psychological symptoms that occur when a person stops or reduces the intake of a substance they have developed dependence, with clinically relevant features.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise drug features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Drug Withdrawal"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Drug Withdrawal"][/sc]
  • UK GP Population: Drug withdrawal is a common reason for consultation, especially for alcohol, nicotine, and prescription medication withdrawal.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Drug Withdrawal"][/sc]
  • When substances like opioids, benzodiazepines, or alcohol are used chronically, the body adapts by downregulating or upregulating neurotransmitter systems to maintain homeostasis. For example, opioid use suppresses endogenous opioid production, while alcohol suppresses GABA activity and increases excitatory glutamate signalling.
  • Upon cessation or reduction of the drug, the neurochemical systems remain dysregulated temporarily, leading to withdrawal symptoms. These symptoms reflect the opposite effect of the drug’s action (e.g., anxiety during benzodiazepine withdrawal due to GABA downregulation).
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Drug Withdrawal"][/sc]
  • Acute phase: Symptoms typically begin within hours to a few days of cessation, depending on the substance’s half-life. For example, alcohol withdrawal symptoms may start within 6-12 hours, while opioid withdrawal can start within 12-24 hours.
  • Peak phase: Symptoms generally peak within 24-72 hours after cessation, although this can vary by substance (e.g., benzodiazepines may peak after several days due to their longer half-lives).
  • Resolution phase: Symptoms gradually resolve over a few days to weeks. In the case of opioids and alcohol, withdrawal symptoms typically subside within 5-7 days. However, post-acute withdrawal syndrome (PAWS) can persist for months in some cases.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Drug Withdrawal"][/sc]
  • Ask about anxiety - Common in opioid, alcohol, and benzodiazepine withdrawal due to overactivity of excitatory neurotransmitters (e.g., glutamate) when the sedative effect of the drug.
  • Ask about tremors - Frequently seen in alcohol and benzodiazepine withdrawal due to overactivation of the central nervous system after suppression by GABAergic drugs.
  • Ask about nausea/Vomiting - Occurs in opioid and alcohol withdrawal due to autonomic dysregulation and increased activity of the gastrointestinal system.
  • Ask about restlessness/Insomnia - Seen in opioid, benzodiazepine, and stimulant withdrawal due to imbalance in neurotransmitters like norepinephrine and dopamine.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Drug Withdrawal"][/sc]
  • Check tachycardia - Results from autonomic hyperactivity as the parasympathetic system is no longer suppressed by substances like alcohol or opioids.
  • Check hypertension - Common in alcohol and opioid withdrawal due to sympathetic nervous system overactivation.
  • Check diaphoresis - Increased sweating occurs due to autonomic instability, commonly seen in alcohol and opioid withdrawal.
  • Check piloerection (“goosebumps”) - Specific to opioid withdrawal due to norepinephrine excess.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Drug Withdrawal"][/sc]
  • Seizures - Seen in severe alcohol or benzodiazepine withdrawal due to hyperexcitability of the CNS, indicating a risk for delirium tremens or life-threatening autonomic instability.
  • Delirium Tremens (DTs) - A medical emergency seen in alcohol withdrawal, characterised by confusion, hallucinations, and autonomic instability, typically occurring 48-72 hours after last alcohol intake.
  • Severe dehydration - In cases of prolonged vomiting, diarrhoea, and diaphoresis, this can lead to electrolyte imbalances and acute kidney injury.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Drug Withdrawal"][/sc]
  • Prolonged Withdrawal - Symptoms like anxiety, insomnia, and cravings can persist for months in post-acute withdrawal syndrome (PAWS), especially with opioids and benzodiazepines.
  • Psychiatric Symptoms - Depression or psychosis may occur in withdrawal from stimulants or alcohol, which may overshadow the typical somatic symptoms.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask drug withdrawal.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Drug Withdrawal"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Full Blood Count (FBC) - May reveal leucocytosis due to stress response; Liver Function Tests (LFTs) - Important in alcohol withdrawal.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Targeted investigations to confirm diagnosis, assess complications, and monitor response in drug withdrawal.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Drug Withdrawal"][/sc]
  • A 45-year-old male with a history of chronic alcohol use presents with tremors, nausea, sweating, and anxiety 12 hours after his last drink. His symptoms have worsened, and his pulse.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Drug Withdrawal"][/sc]
  • Alcoholic Hepatitis - Presents with jaundice, abdominal pain, and elevated liver enzymes, without autonomic symptoms like tremors.
  • Anxiety Disorder - May mimic withdrawal symptoms but lacks a history of substance use and typically doesn’t.
  • Hyperthyroidism - Can present with tremors, anxiety, and tachycardia but lacks the history of substance use.
  • Related alternative diagnosis - Distinguish from drug withdrawal using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Drug Withdrawal"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Assess severity, physiological stability, and immediate complications.
  • Core care: Deliver condition-specific management for drug withdrawal, treat underlying drivers, and coordinate appropriate specialist input.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Reassess symptom trajectory, examination findings, and objective markers to confirm response in drug withdrawal.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Escalate urgently for clinical deterioration, evolving red flags, or failure to improve in suspected drug withdrawal.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Drug Withdrawal"][/sc]
  • Seizures - Particularly in alcohol and benzodiazepine withdrawal, due to CNS hyperexcitability.
  • Delirium Tremens - Life-threatening autonomic instability and altered mental status, seen in alcohol withdrawal.
  • Relapse - High risk due to the psychological cravings, particularly in opioids and alcohol withdrawal.
