March Fracture
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="March Fracture"][/sc]
  • March fracture is a type of stress fracture involving the metatarsal bones, most commonly the second or third metatarsal.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise march features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.

  • Higher-risk or missed groups: March Fracture may be missed when presentation is atypical or obscured by comorbidity.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="March Fracture"][/sc]
  • Repetitive mechanical loading overwhelms the ability of bone remodelling to repair microdamage.
  • Increased osteoclastic activity weakens the bone before osteoblastic activity can lay down new bone, creating a microfracture.
  • In the metatarsals, these forces are exacerbated by their role in weight-bearing and propulsion during walking and running.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="March Fracture"][/sc]
  • Initial phase: Subtle onset of pain during activity.
  • Progressive phase: Pain becomes more intense and may persist at rest.
  • Chronic phase: If untreated, may result in complete fracture or deformity.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="March Fracture"][/sc]
  • Ask about gradual onset of foot pain - Typically localised to the forefoot; due to repetitive microtrauma leading to bone stress.
  • Ask about worsening pain with activity - Pain intensifies with weight-bearing activities as mechanical stress exacerbates microdamage.
  • Ask about relief of pain with rest - Pain often subsides with cessation of activity, reflecting the non-traumatic nature of the injury.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="March Fracture"][/sc]
  • Check localised tenderness - Palpable pain over the affected metatarsal, reflecting inflammation and periosteal irritation.
  • Check swelling - Mild soft tissue swelling over the area due to localised inflammatory response.
  • Check antalgic gait - Altered walking pattern to offload weight from the painful region.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="March Fracture"][/sc]
  • Persistent pain despite rest - Suggests progression to a complete fracture or delayed healing.
  • Severe swelling and erythema - May indicate infection or complex regional pain syndrome (CRPS).
  • Numbness or paraesthesia - Could signal nerve entrapment or compromise.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="March Fracture"][/sc]
  • No significant pain - Some patients may only report discomfort or notice swelling without classic pain.
  • Non-localised pain - Pain diffusely in the midfoot, leading to delayed diagnosis.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask march fracture.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="March Fracture"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: X-ray - Often normal initially; later may show periosteal reaction or fracture line.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Bone scan - Increased uptake in the affected area indicates bone remodelling; MRI - Highly sensitive for early stress fractures, showing.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="March Fracture"][/sc]
  • Morton's neuroma - Presents with radiating pain and paraesthesia, typically between the third and fourth toes.
  • Metatarsalgia - Generalised forefoot pain, often associated with biomechanical abnormalities.
  • Plantar fasciitis - Pain localised to the heel and plantar fascia, not the metatarsals.
  • Related alternative diagnosis - Distinguish from march fracture using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="March Fracture"][/sc]
  • Complete fracture - May occur if stress fracture is untreated or ignored.
  • Chronic pain - Prolonged recovery due to delayed diagnosis or inadequate treatment.
