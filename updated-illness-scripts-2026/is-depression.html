Depression
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Depression"][/sc]
  • Depression is a common mental health disorder characterised by persistent low mood, loss of interest or pleasure (anhedonia), and a range of emotional, cognitive, and physical symptoms.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise depression features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Depression"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Depression"][/sc]
  • Risk factors: Risk factors for depression include relevant predisposition, comorbidity, and exposure history.
  • Higher-risk or missed groups: Depression may be missed when presentation is atypical or obscured by comorbidity.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Depression"][/sc]
  • Depression is believed to result from a complex interplay between biological, psychological, and environmental factors. The primary biological hypothesis is the dysregulation of neurotransmitters (particularly serotonin, norepinephrine, and dopamine) in key brain areas such as the prefrontal cortex and limbic system. Chronic stress can lead to hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal (HPA) axis hyperactivity, resulting in elevated cortisol levels and hippocampal atrophy. Inflammatory processes and neuroplasticity disturbances are also implicated in the pathogenesis of depression.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Depression"][/sc]
  • Depression typically progresses through several phases. Initial symptoms may develop insidiously over weeks to months. The acute phase, lasting 6-12 weeks, is characterised by severe symptoms. Untreated, depression may become chronic, lasting for years. Periods of remission may occur, but relapse is common, particularly if underlying causes are not addressed.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Depression"][/sc]
  • Ask about low mood - Persistent sadness or feeling "empty", resulting from dysregulation of serotonin in mood-regulating areas of the brain.
  • Ask about anhedonia - Loss of interest or pleasure in previously enjoyable activities, associated with dopamine dysregulation in reward pathways.
  • Ask about fatigue - Constant tiredness due to changes in sleep patterns, energy metabolism, and neuroendocrine dysfunction.
  • Ask about sleep disturbances - Insomnia or hypersomnia, linked to circadian rhythm disruption and serotonin deficits.
  • Ask about appetite changes - Weight loss or gain, possibly due to altered hypothalamic function affecting hunger and satiety signals.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Depression"][/sc]
  • Check flat affect - Reduced emotional expression, often linked to serotonin dysregulation in the limbic system.
  • Check psychomotor retardation or agitation - Slowed movements or restlessness due to altered basal ganglia function.
  • Check poor eye contact - Indicative of social withdrawal and cognitive disengagement, common in depressed patients.
  • Check slouched posture - A sign of low energy and motivation, potentially linked to physical fatigue and emotional withdrawal.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Depression"][/sc]
  • Suicidal ideation - Requires immediate intervention due to the risk of suicide, which can be driven by extreme hopelessness.
  • Psychotic features - Hallucinations or delusions, often signify severe depression with psychosis, requiring urgent care.
  • Severe self-neglect - A sign of profound depression, with the risk of physical health deterioration and increased morbidity.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Depression"][/sc]
  • Atypical depression - May include mood reactivity, hypersomnia, and weight gain rather than the typical presentation of low mood and insomnia. This variant is thought to be linked to different neurotransmitter imbalances, particularly serotonin and dopamine.
  • Masked depression - Somatic complaints (e.g., pain, gastrointestinal issues) may dominate rather than emotional symptoms, often due to the patient’s inability to recognise or express mood-related issues.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask depression.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Depression"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: PHQ-9 questionnaire - A 9-item scale used to quantify the severity of depressive symptoms, with higher scores indicating more severe depression.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Blood tests (e.g., TSH, FBC) - Used to rule out underlying causes such as hypothyroidism or anaemia, which may mimic depressive.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Depression"][/sc]
  • Hypothyroidism - Characterised by fatigue, weight gain, and low mood, but differs from depression in that.
  • Generalised anxiety disorder (GAD) - Presents with excessive worry and restlessness, but mood reactivity is often more intact than.
  • Dysthymia (Persistent depressive disorder) - Similar to depression but with less severe symptoms lasting for at least 2 years.
  • Related alternative diagnosis - Distinguish from depression using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Depression"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Assess severity, physiological stability, and immediate complications.
  • Core care: Deliver condition-specific management for depression, treat underlying drivers, and coordinate appropriate specialist input.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Reassess symptom trajectory, examination findings, and objective markers to confirm response in depression.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Escalate urgently for clinical deterioration, evolving red flags, or failure to improve in suspected depression.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Depression"][/sc]
  • Suicide - The most severe complication, with an elevated risk in untreated depression.
  • Substance abuse - Depression increases the risk of alcohol and drug abuse as a form of self-medication.
  • Chronic physical illness - Depression has been linked to increased morbidity from chronic conditions such as cardiovascular disease, partly due to lifestyle factors and chronic inflammation.
