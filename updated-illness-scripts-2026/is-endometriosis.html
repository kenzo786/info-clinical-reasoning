Endometriosis
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Endometriosis"][/sc]
  • Endometriosis is a chronic gynaecological condition characterised by the presence of endometrial-like tissue outside the uterus, leading to inflammatory responses, fibrosis, and adhesion formation.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise endometriosis features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.

  • Risk factors: Risk factors for endometriosis include relevant predisposition, comorbidity, and exposure history.
  • Higher-risk or missed groups: Endometriosis may be missed when presentation is atypical or obscured by comorbidity.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Endometriosis"][/sc]
  • Retrograde menstruation: The most widely accepted theory suggests that menstrual blood flows backwards through the fallopian tubes, implanting endometrial cells onto pelvic structures.
  • Metaplasia theory: Proposes that peritoneal cells can transform into endometrial-like cells under certain conditions.
  • Immune dysfunction: Ineffective immune clearance of ectopic endometrial cells may contribute to their persistence and growth outside the uterus.
  • Inflammatory response: Ectopic endometrial cells elicit a localised inflammatory response, causing scarring, fibrosis, and adhesions which lead to symptoms.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Endometriosis"][/sc]
  • Onset: Symptoms often begin in adolescence or early adulthood and may worsen over time.
  • Progression: Endometriosis is typically progressive, with increasing lesion size and associated symptoms; however, it may remain static in some cases.
  • Chronicity: Symptoms often persist unless treated, and may return after discontinuing treatment, particularly after hormonal therapy cessation.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Endometriosis"][/sc]
  • Ask about chronic pelvic pain: Commonest symptom, often cyclical and linked to menstrual bleeding due to inflammatory and fibrotic changes.
  • Ask about dysmenorrhoea: Severe menstrual pain due to inflammation and bleeding of ectopic endometrial tissue during menstruation.
  • Ask about dyspareunia: Pain during intercourse, particularly with deep penetration, resulting from lesions and adhesions in the pelvic cavity.
  • Ask about infertility: Approximately 30-50% of women with endometriosis experience infertility, possibly due to adhesions, tubal obstruction, or altered ovarian function.
  • Ask about dyschezia: Pain during defaecation due to rectosigmoid endometriosis, especially around menstruation.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Endometriosis"][/sc]
  • Check pelvic tenderness: Tenderness on bimanual examination due to inflammation and adhesions in the pelvis.
  • Check fixed or retroverted uterus: Adhesions may fix the uterus in a retroverted position.
  • Check adnexal masses: Tender masses on palpation may indicate endometriomas (ovarian cysts filled with old blood).
  • Check tender nodularity in the posterior fornix: Palpable tender nodules suggest deep infiltrating endometriosis, often found in the uterosacral ligaments.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Endometriosis"][/sc]
  • Severe, acute pelvic pain: May indicate ruptured endometrioma or other complications; urgent evaluation needed to exclude acute abdomen.
  • Haematuria or rectal bleeding: In cases of bladder or bowel involvement; significant as it may indicate deep infiltrative endometriosis affecting these organs.
  • Progressive worsening of pain: Could signify increasing lesion load or fibrosis, warranting further investigation and possible treatment adjustment.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Endometriosis"][/sc]
  • Asymptomatic or minimal symptoms: Occurs in some women, often only discovered during investigations for infertility or unrelated pelvic issues.
  • Extragenital symptoms: Rarely, endometriosis can affect distant sites (e.g., thoracic cavity), causing symptoms such as chest pain or haemoptysis.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask endometriosis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Endometriosis"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Pelvic ultrasound: May show endometriomas, though small lesions are often not visible.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Diagnostic laparoscopy: Gold standard for diagnosis, allowing direct visualisation and biopsy of lesions; MRI pelvis: Useful for deep infiltrating endometriosis, particularly.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Endometriosis"][/sc]
  • Pelvic inflammatory disease - Pain may be similar but usually associated with fever, abnormal discharge, and elevated inflammatory.
  • Irritable bowel syndrome - Overlapping symptoms of pelvic pain and bloating, but no dysmenorrhoea or infertility.
  • Adenomyosis - Similar presentation with dysmenorrhoea, but often presents with a diffusely enlarged, tender uterus.
  • Related alternative diagnosis - Distinguish from endometriosis using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Endometriosis"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Assess severity, physiological stability, and immediate complications.
  • Core care: Deliver condition-specific management for endometriosis, treat underlying drivers, and coordinate appropriate specialist input.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Reassess symptom trajectory, examination findings, and objective markers to confirm response in endometriosis.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Escalate urgently for clinical deterioration, evolving red flags, or failure to improve in suspected endometriosis.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Endometriosis"][/sc]
  • Infertility: Common complication due to tubal adhesions and ovarian dysfunction.
  • Chronic pain syndrome: Due to persistent inflammation and nerve sensitisation.
  • Adhesion formation: Leads to chronic pelvic pain and organ dysfunction.
