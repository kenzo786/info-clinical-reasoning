Dehydration
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Dehydration"][/sc]
  • Dehydration is a condition that occurs when the body loses more fluids than it takes in, leading to a deficit that disrupts normal physiological functions.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise dehydration features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Dehydration"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Dehydration"][/sc]
  • Dehydration is a common condition seen across all age groups, but it is particularly prevalent among the elderly, infants, and patients with chronic illnesses. In the UK, dehydration is a significant cause of hospital admissions, especially among the elderly, accounting for an estimated 1 in 10 hospital admissions for patients aged over 65. Globally, it is a leading cause of mortality in children under 5 years, largely due to diarrhoeal illnesses.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Dehydration"][/sc]
  • Dehydration occurs when fluid losses exceed fluid intake, leading to a reduction in total body water. Water is lost from intracellular and extracellular compartments, causing cellular shrinkage and impaired metabolic processes. Key mechanisms include loss of water through the gastrointestinal tract (vomiting, diarrhoea), skin (sweating, burns), respiratory system (increased respiration), or the kidneys (diuretics, polyuria). As water is lost, electrolytes, particularly sodium, are also lost or concentrated, leading to imbalances such as hypernatremia (if more water is lost than sodium) or hyponatremia (if more sodium is lost relative to water).
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Dehydration"][/sc]
  • The onset of dehydration can be acute or gradual, depending on the cause. In conditions such as acute gastroenteritis or heat stroke, dehydration can develop within hours. Chronic causes, such as inadequate fluid intake in the elderly, can lead to more insidious, gradual dehydration over days to weeks. If untreated, mild dehydration can progress to severe dehydration, resulting in shock, organ failure, and death.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Dehydration"][/sc]
  • Ask about thirst - A common early symptom as the body attempts to stimulate fluid intake to restore fluid balance.
  • Ask about dry mouth - Reduced salivary secretion due to water conservation by the body.
  • Ask about fatigue and weakness - Reduced blood volume and decreased oxygen delivery to tissues lead to lethargy.
  • Ask about dizziness or light-headedness - Hypovolemia results in reduced cerebral perfusion, especially when standing.
  • Ask about decreased urine output - The kidneys conserve water by concentrating urine to prevent further fluid loss.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Dehydration"][/sc]
  • Check tachycardia - The heart compensates for reduced blood volume by increasing heart rate to maintain cardiac output.
  • Check hypotension - As dehydration progresses, decreased blood volume leads to lower blood pressure.
  • Check poor skin turgor - Reduced interstitial fluid volume causes the skin to lose its elasticity.
  • Check sunken eyes - Loss of fluid in the soft tissues around the eyes is often seen in severe dehydration.
  • Check dry mucous membranes - Indicative of reduced water content in the body’s tissues.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Dehydration"][/sc]
  • Altered mental state or confusion - Signifies severe dehydration and possibly impending hypovolemic shock, which can lead to multi-organ failure if not rapidly corrected.
  • Anuria - Lack of urine output indicates kidney dysfunction or failure due to insufficient blood flow.
  • Severe hypotension - A sign of shock, indicating critical hypovolemia requiring urgent intervention.
  • Tachypnoea - Rapid breathing may occur in response to metabolic acidosis, a complication of dehydration.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Dehydration"][/sc]
  • In the elderly - Dehydration may present with non-specific symptoms such as confusion, lethargy, or falls, making it easily missed.
  • In infants - Symptoms like irritability, reduced feeding, or a sunken fontanelle may be seen, but classical signs like thirst are absent.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Dehydration"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Focused bedside assessment and first-line testing to assess severity and immediate risk in suspected dehydration.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Urinalysis - Concentrated urine with a high specific gravity (>1.030) indicates dehydration, as the kidneys conserve water; Full blood count (FBC).

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Dehydration"][/sc]
  • Gastroenteritis - Distinguish from dehydration using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.
  • Heat stroke - Differs in that the primary issue is heat exposure and hyperthermia, with dehydration as.
  • Diabetic ketoacidosis (DKA) - Dehydration presents alongside hyperglycaemia, ketonuria, and acidosis.
  • Adrenal insufficiency - Presents with hypotension and electrolyte imbalance, but typically has a background of adrenal pathology.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Dehydration"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Assess severity, physiological stability, and immediate complications.
  • Core care: Deliver condition-specific management for dehydration, treat underlying drivers, and coordinate appropriate specialist input.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Reassess symptom trajectory, examination findings, and objective markers to confirm response in dehydration.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Escalate urgently for clinical deterioration, evolving red flags, or failure to improve in suspected dehydration.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Dehydration"][/sc]
  • Hypovolemic shock - Caused by severe fluid loss, leading to organ hypoperfusion and failure.
  • Acute kidney injury (AKI) - Prolonged dehydration reduces renal perfusion, causing kidney damage.
  • Electrolyte disturbances - Severe imbalances, particularly of sodium and potassium, can lead to arrhythmias and seizures.
