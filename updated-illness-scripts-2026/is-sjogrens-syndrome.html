Sjogren's Syndrome
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Sjogren's Syndrome"][/sc]
  • Sjögren's syndrome is a chronic autoimmune disease characterised by lymphocytic infiltration and destruction of exocrine glands, primarily affecting salivary and lacrimal glands.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise sjogren features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Sjogren's Syndrome"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Sjogren's Syndrome"][/sc]
  • Most common in women aged 40–60, with a female-to-male ratio of 9:1.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Sjogren's Syndrome"][/sc]
  • Autoimmune targeting of exocrine glands occurs, primarily lacrimal and salivary glands, due to abnormal lymphocyte activation and infiltration.
  • Both genetic predisposition (e.g., HLA-DR and HLA-DQ alleles) and environmental triggers (e.g., viral infections) contribute to onset.
  • Antibodies (anti-Ro/SSA and anti-La/SSB) are produced, further damaging glandular function and contributing to systemic inflammation.
  • Chronic inflammation reduces glandular secretions, leading to dryness of mucous membranes, especially in the eyes and mouth.
  • Other organs may also be affected (e.g., kidneys, liver, lungs, and nerves), due to widespread autoimmune activity.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Sjogren's Syndrome"][/sc]
  • Early stage: Gradual onset of dry eyes and mouth, often unrecognised for years due to mild symptoms.
  • Progressive stage: Increasing severity of dryness and onset of extraglandular manifestations (e.g., arthralgia, fatigue, cutaneous vasculitis).
  • Chronic stage: Persistent dryness symptoms, with risk of systemic involvement (e.g., interstitial lung disease, renal tubular acidosis, or peripheral neuropathy).
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Sjogren's Syndrome"][/sc]
  • Ask about dry eyes (keratoconjunctivitis sicca) - Caused by lymphocytic infiltration of lacrimal glands, leading to reduced tear production.
  • Ask about dry mouth (xerostomia) - Results from lymphocytic infiltration of salivary glands, impairing saliva production.
  • Ask about fatigue - Likely due to systemic inflammation and autoimmune activity.
  • Ask about joint pain and stiffness (arthralgia) - Non-erosive arthritis may be present, commonly in small joints, due to immune complex deposition.
  • Ask about dry skin - Reduced skin hydration from loss of glandular secretions.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Sjogren's Syndrome"][/sc]
  • Check dry, erythematous eyes - Conjunctival dryness and inflammation visible on slit-lamp examination.
  • Check salivary gland swelling - Particularly of the parotid glands, due to chronic inflammation and lymphocytic infiltration.
  • Check dental caries - Common due to lack of saliva, leading to poor oral pH and increased bacterial growth.
  • Check angular cheilitis - Cracks at the corners of the mouth due to dryness and secondary infection.
  • Check purpuric skin rash - Due to cutaneous vasculitis from small-vessel involvement.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Sjogren's Syndrome"][/sc]
  • Painful, persistent salivary gland swelling - May indicate lymphoma, a known complication in Sjögren's.
  • Progressive dyspnoea or cough - Could signal interstitial lung disease, a rare extraglandular manifestation.
  • Peripheral neuropathy - Indicates potential nerve involvement, leading to sensory or motor dysfunction.
  • Renal tubular acidosis - Suggests renal involvement, possibly causing acid-base and electrolyte imbalances.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Sjogren's Syndrome"][/sc]
  • Asymptomatic dry mouth or eyes - Some patients may not report dryness until severe; often detected incidentally.
  • Isolated extraglandular symptoms - Rare presentations may include renal or lung disease without significant dryness.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask sjogren's syndrome.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Sjogren's Syndrome"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Schirmer’s test - Measures tear production; positive if <5 mm wetting in 5 minutes; Salivary gland biopsy - Reveals lymphocytic infiltration.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Anti-Ro/SSA and Anti-La/SSB antibodies - Positive in ~70% of cases; specific for Sjögren's and indicate autoimmunity; Salivary flow rate - Measures.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Sjogren's Syndrome"][/sc]
  • Rheumatoid arthritis - Typically involves joint swelling and erosion; less prominent dry symptoms.
  • Systemic lupus erythematosus (SLE) - Often with systemic signs (e.g., butterfly rash, renal involvement) and less severe sicca symptoms.
  • Sarcoidosis - Presents with systemic granulomas; parotid gland enlargement may mimic Sjögren’s.
  • Medication-induced dryness - Certain drugs (e.g., antihistamines) may cause similar dryness symptoms without autoimmune findings.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Sjogren's Syndrome"][/sc]
  • Lymphoma - Most severe complication, affecting 5% of Sjögren’s patients; due to chronic lymphocytic activation.
  • Dental caries - Common due to xerostomia and low salivary pH.
  • Renal disease (e.g., interstitial nephritis) - Due to renal tubular involvement, causing electrolyte disturbances.
  • Interstitial lung disease - Rare but severe, leading to restrictive lung disease and respiratory compromise.
