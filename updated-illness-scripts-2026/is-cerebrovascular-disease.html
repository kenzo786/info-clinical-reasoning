Cerebrovascular Disease
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Cerebrovascular Disease"][/sc]
  • Cerebrovascular disease includes disorders of cerebral blood vessels causing transient or permanent neurological deficit through ischaemia or intracranial haemorrhage.
  • UKMLA essentials: sudden focal neurological deficit, reduced consciousness or severe headache concern, urgent stroke-pathway neuroimaging.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Cerebrovascular Disease"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Cerebrovascular Disease"][/sc]
  • Risk factors: Risk of cerebrovascular disease rises with relevant predisposition, comorbidity, and exposure profile.
  • Higher-risk or missed groups: Cerebrovascular Disease may be missed when presentations are atypical or comorbidity obscures key features.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Cerebrovascular Disease"][/sc]
  • Ischaemic cerebrovascular disease is caused by atherosclerosis or embolism, leading to narrowing or occlusion of cerebral arteries, causing ischaemia and infarction. Haemorrhagic cerebrovascular disease involves rupture of weakened vessels, leading to bleeding within the brain. Both mechanisms result in neuronal death due to oxygen and nutrient deprivation or direct damage from blood products.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Cerebrovascular Disease"][/sc]
  • Acute phase: Stroke or TIA symptoms occur suddenly, reaching their peak within minutes to hours.
  • Subacute phase: Neurological recovery may occur over weeks to months, depending on the extent of the damage.
  • Chronic phase: Permanent deficits may persist, and in some cases, vascular dementia or recurrent strokes can develop over time.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Cerebrovascular Disease"][/sc]
  • Ask about sudden weakness or numbness - Often unilateral (hemiparesis), caused by ischaemia affecting motor or sensory areas in the brain.
  • Ask about speech disturbances - Aphasia or dysarthria occurs when language or motor speech areas are involved (e.g., Broca’s area).
  • Ask about visual disturbances - Can include vision loss in one eye (amaurosis fugax) or hemianopia due to involvement of the optic pathways.
  • Ask about dizziness and loss of coordination - Caused by cerebellar or brainstem involvement.
  • Ask about headache - Often associated with haemorrhagic stroke due to the sudden increase in intracranial pressure from bleeding.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Cerebrovascular Disease"][/sc]
  • Check hemiparesis - Weakness on one side of the body, indicating damage to the motor cortex or descending motor pathways.
  • Check facial droop - Due to involvement of the facial nerve, often seen in ischaemic strokes.
  • Check slurred speech - Indicative of involvement of motor speech areas or cerebellar dysfunction.
  • Check altered consciousness - Can be seen in large strokes, especially haemorrhagic strokes, due to increased intracranial pressure.
  • Check ataxia - Loss of coordination, commonly seen with cerebellar infarcts.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Cerebrovascular Disease"][/sc]
  • Sudden onset of severe headache - Suggestive of subarachnoid haemorrhage, a medical emergency.
  • Sudden loss of consciousness - Could indicate a large haemorrhagic stroke or massive ischaemic stroke with herniation.
  • Seizures - May indicate a haemorrhagic stroke or cortical involvement in an ischaemic stroke.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Cerebrovascular Disease"][/sc]
  • Silent strokes - Asymptomatic strokes, often detected incidentally on imaging, occur when small infarcts do not produce overt neurological symptoms.
  • Confusion or memory loss - May be misdiagnosed as dementia, especially in older patients, reflecting vascular dementia or multiple small strokes.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features or comorbidity can mask the underlying diagnosis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Cerebrovascular Disease"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Focused bedside assessment and first-line investigations to define immediate risk in suspected cerebrovascular disease.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Carotid Doppler - Assesses carotid artery stenosis, which can lead to ischaemic strokes; ECG - To detect atrial fibrillation, a common.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Cerebrovascular Disease"][/sc]
  • A 72-year-old male with a history of hypertension presents with sudden onset of right-sided weakness and difficulty speaking. CT brain confirms an ischaemic stroke in the left middle cerebral artery.
  • Adult with symptom pattern and objective findings suggestive of cerebrovascular disease, requiring focused confirmation and risk stratification.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Cerebrovascular Disease"][/sc]
  • Migraine with aura - Differentiated by gradual onset of neurological symptoms, often with a history of recurrent headaches.
  • Seizure with post-ictal paralysis (Todd's paralysis) - Transient weakness following a seizure, typically with a history of epilepsy.
  • Hypoglycaemia - Differentiate from cerebrovascular disease using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.
  • Alternative serious diagnosis - Distinguishing history, examination, and targeted investigations do not support cerebrovascular disease.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Cerebrovascular Disease"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Assess physiological stability and urgent complications at presentation, and initiate early stabilisation where required.
  • Core care: Deliver condition-specific treatment for cerebrovascular disease, address underlying drivers, and involve appropriate specialist support.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Reassess symptom trajectory, objective markers, and treatment response over time to detect complications early.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Worsening symptoms, physiological deterioration, or failure to improve should trigger urgent reassessment for severe or alternative pathology in cerebrovascular disease.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Cerebrovascular Disease"][/sc]
  • Disability - Permanent neurological deficits (e.g., hemiparesis, aphasia) are common following large strokes.
  • Vascular dementia - Multiple strokes can lead to progressive cognitive decline.
  • Post-stroke seizures - More common in haemorrhagic strokes or large cortical strokes.
