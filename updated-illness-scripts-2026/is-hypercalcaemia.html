Hypercalcaemia
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Hypercalcaemia"][/sc]
  • Hypercalcaemia is defined as a serum calcium concentration above the normal range, typically >2.6 mmol/L in adults, with moderate to severe cases above 3.0 mmol/L.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise hypercalcaemia features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Hypercalcaemia"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Hypercalcaemia"][/sc]
  • Globally, hypercalcaemia occurs more frequently in older populations and those with chronic kidney disease or cancer.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Hypercalcaemia"][/sc]
  • Excess calcium in blood can occur via:
    • **Increased bone resorption** – commonly due to primary hyperparathyroidism or malignancy.
    • **Increased gastrointestinal absorption** – often due to vitamin D toxicity or granulomatous diseases.
    • **Reduced renal excretion** – as seen in chronic kidney disease or with certain medications like thiazide diuretics.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Hypercalcaemia"][/sc]
  • **Acute**: Rapid onset seen with malignant hypercalcaemia or overdoses of calcium/vitamin D.
  • **Chronic**: Gradual onset commonly linked to primary hyperparathyroidism or chronic kidney disease, with insidious symptoms.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Hypercalcaemia"][/sc]
  • Ask about fatigue – Often due to the cellular effects of elevated calcium impairing energy metabolism.
  • Ask about polyuria and polydipsia – Calcium’s effects on renal tubular function cause nephrogenic diabetes insipidus, leading to excessive urination and thirst.
  • Ask about nausea and vomiting – High calcium disrupts gastric smooth muscle function, slowing gastric motility and causing GI symptoms.
  • Ask about constipation – Hypercalcaemia reduces intestinal smooth muscle tone, impairing bowel movements.
  • Ask about muscle weakness – Caused by neuromuscular dysfunction due to altered ion gradients.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Hypercalcaemia"][/sc]
  • Check dehydration – Reduced renal concentrating ability leads to fluid loss.
  • Check hypertension – Increased peripheral resistance and renal impairment contribute to high blood pressure.
  • Check confusion or lethargy – Seen in moderate to severe hypercalcaemia due to its effects on neuronal excitability.
  • Check reduced deep tendon reflexes – High calcium impairs neuromuscular transmission.
  • Check arrhythmias – Severe cases may lead to QT interval shortening due to ion channel alterations.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Hypercalcaemia"][/sc]
  • Severe dehydration and renal impairment – Suggests hypercalcaemic crisis; untreated, it may progress to renal failure.
  • Confusion or coma – Indicative of severe hypercalcaemia (>3.5 mmol/L), requiring immediate intervention.
  • Arrhythmias or cardiac arrest – Particularly shortened QT interval, signalling potential cardiovascular collapse.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Hypercalcaemia"][/sc]
  • **Mild hypercalcaemia** – Often asymptomatic or with subtle cognitive changes, which may go unnoticed.
  • **Elderly patients** – May present with confusion or falls, attributed to other comorbidities, delaying hypercalcaemia diagnosis.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask hypercalcaemia.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Hypercalcaemia"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Serum Calcium (Total and Ionised) – Elevated levels confirm diagnosis; corrected for albumin if needed; PTH levels – High or inappropriately.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Vitamin D – Helps assess over-supplementation or other metabolic causes; Renal function tests – To evaluate hypercalcaemia’s impact on renal function.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Hypercalcaemia"][/sc]
  • Primary hyperparathyroidism – Elevated PTH levels distinguish it - distinguish by focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.
  • Malignancy-related hypercalcaemia – Rapid onset with elevated calcium - distinguish by focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.
  • Thyrotoxicosis – Often mild hypercalcaemia with accompanying hyperthyroid - distinguish by focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.
  • Vitamin D toxicity – Elevated calcium and 25-hydroxyvitamin - distinguish by focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Hypercalcaemia"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Assess severity, physiological stability, and immediate complications.
  • Core care: Deliver condition-specific management for hypercalcaemia, treat underlying drivers, and coordinate appropriate specialist input.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Reassess symptom trajectory, examination findings, and objective markers to confirm response in hypercalcaemia.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Escalate urgently for clinical deterioration, evolving red flags, or failure to improve in suspected hypercalcaemia.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Hypercalcaemia"][/sc]
  • Renal stones and nephrocalcinosis – Due to calcium crystallisation in the urinary tract.
  • Osteoporosis – High bone resorption rates in chronic hypercalcaemia lead to bone density loss.
  • Cardiovascular disease – Long-term hypercalcaemia may increase the risk of hypertension and arrhythmias.
