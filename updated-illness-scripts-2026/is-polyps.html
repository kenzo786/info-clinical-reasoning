Polyps
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Polyps"][/sc]
  • Polyps are abnormal growths of tissue that project from a mucous membrane.

  • Higher-risk or missed groups: Polyps may be missed when presentation is atypical or obscured by comorbidity.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Polyps"][/sc]
  • Polyps develop due to abnormal proliferation of the epithelial lining of mucous membranes. The exact mechanisms depend on the type of polyp:
  • Adenomatous polyps: Result from mutations in tumour suppressor genes like APC, leading to uncontrolled cell growth. Dysplasia can progress to carcinoma over time (adenoma-carcinoma sequence).
  • Hyperplastic polyps: These arise from benign overgrowth of normal epithelial cells without significant malignant potential.
  • Inflammatory polyps: Typically seen in conditions like inflammatory bowel disease (IBD), they result from chronic mucosal injury and regeneration.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Polyps"][/sc]
  • Polyps typically grow slowly over many years. Adenomatous polyps can take 10-15 years to progress to cancer if left untreated.
  • Some polyps may remain stable and asymptomatic for long periods, while others may increase in size or number, especially in hereditary syndromes like familial adenomatous polyposis (FAP).
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Polyps"][/sc]
  • Ask about asymptomatic: Most polyps, particularly in the colon, are asymptomatic and discovered incidentally during screening colonoscopies.
  • Ask about rectal bleeding: Bleeding occurs when polyps erode the mucosal lining, causing intermittent blood in stools.
  • Ask about change in bowel habits: Larger polyps, especially in the colon, can cause constipation or diarrhoea by partially obstructing the bowel lumen.
  • Ask about abdominal pain: Occasional with larger polyps, likely due to partial obstruction or inflammatory changes.
  • Ask about postnasal drip and nasal congestion: Common with nasal polyps, caused by the obstruction of normal sinus drainage.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Polyps"][/sc]
  • Check rectal mass: On digital rectal examination, large rectal polyps can occasionally be palpated.
  • Check visible polyps: In cases of nasal polyps, a direct nasal examination may reveal soft, pale masses.
  • Check abdominal tenderness: Rare but may occur if there is partial bowel obstruction or inflammation.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Polyps"][/sc]
  • Unexplained weight loss: This may indicate malignancy in a polyp, especially if accompanied by anaemia or bleeding.
  • Iron-deficiency anaemia: Indicates chronic blood loss from a polyp, suggesting potential malignancy or significant size.
  • Persistent abdominal pain with bowel obstruction: Large polyps can cause partial bowel obstruction, which requires prompt intervention.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Polyps"][/sc]
  • Small polyps: These may present with very subtle or no symptoms and are often missed unless screening tests like colonoscopy are performed.
  • Nasal polyps with no sinus symptoms: Some nasal polyps may not cause obstruction or sinus issues, only being discovered during routine ENT evaluations.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Polyps"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Colonoscopy: Direct visualisation of colonic polyps; polyps appear as raised mucosal lesions. Biopsy confirms histology; Flexible sigmoidoscopy: Useful for visualising polyps.
  • Targeted and monitoring: CT colonography: A non-invasive imaging alternative to colonoscopy that can detect large polyps; Nasal endoscopy: Identifies nasal polyps directly, often used.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Polyps"][/sc]
  • Diverticulosis - Unlike polyps, diverticula are outpouchings of the colonic wall. They typically present with left-sided.
  • Colorectal cancer - Distinguish from polyps using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.
  • Inflammatory bowel disease (IBD) - Inflammatory polyps may be present, but IBD is characterised by chronic diarrhoea, abdominal pain.
  • Related alternative diagnosis - Distinguish from polyps using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Polyps"][/sc]
  • Colorectal cancer: Malignant transformation of adenomatous polyps is the most significant complication, particularly with larger polyps (>1 cm) or those with high-grade dysplasia.
  • Intestinal obstruction: Large polyps can obstruct the bowel, leading to significant morbidity.
  • Post-polypectomy bleeding: A common complication following endoscopic removal of larger polyps, typically managed with endoscopic interventions.
