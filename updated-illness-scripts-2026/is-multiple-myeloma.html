Multiple Myeloma
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Multiple Myeloma"][/sc]
  • Multiple myeloma is a malignant neoplasm of plasma cells in the bone marrow, characterised by the uncontrolled production of abnormal immunoglobulin (monoclonal protein or paraprotein).
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise multiple features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Multiple Myeloma"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Multiple Myeloma"][/sc]
  • More common in older adults, with a median age of diagnosis of around 70 years.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Multiple Myeloma"][/sc]
  • In multiple myeloma, a clonal proliferation of plasma cells occurs within the bone marrow, leading to the production of monoclonal immunoglobulins (usually IgG or IgA). The malignant plasma cells secrete substances (RANKL, cytokines) that promote osteoclast activity, causing bone resorption and destruction.
  • The abnormal immunoglobulins accumulate, leading to hyperviscosity and renal damage, primarily due to light-chain deposition in the kidneys (myeloma kidney).
  • Marrow infiltration causes suppression of normal haematopoiesis, leading to anaemia, neutropenia, and thrombocytopenia.
  • Immune suppression arises from the inhibition of normal antibody production, increasing susceptibility to infections.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Multiple Myeloma"][/sc]
  • Smouldering/asymptomatic phase: Early in the disease course, myeloma can be asymptomatic, discovered incidentally on blood tests showing high protein or abnormal immunoglobulins.
  • Symptomatic phase: Progressive disease manifests with bone pain, fatigue, recurrent infections, and end-organ damage (kidneys, bones).
  • Advanced stage: Complications such as fractures, hypercalcaemia, renal failure, and severe immunosuppression become prominent.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Multiple Myeloma"][/sc]
  • Ask about bone pain – Commonly affects the back and ribs due to osteolytic lesions from increased osteoclast activity.
  • Ask about fatigue – Often due to anaemia caused by bone marrow infiltration and suppression of erythropoiesis.
  • Ask about recurrent infections – Results from immunosuppression, as normal antibody production is reduced by the proliferation of abnormal plasma cells.
  • Ask about weight loss – A nonspecific symptom linked to the chronic disease state and cachexia.
  • Ask about renal impairment – Due to light chain deposition and hypercalcaemia, leading to myeloma kidney and worsening kidney function.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Multiple Myeloma"][/sc]
  • Check pallor – Due to anaemia from marrow infiltration and reduced red cell production.
  • Check bone tenderness – Palpation of affected bones, particularly in the back or ribs, can elicit pain due to osteolytic lesions.
  • Check fractures – Pathological fractures, especially in the spine or ribs, due to weakened bones from lytic lesions.
  • Check hypercalcaemia symptoms – Nausea, constipation, and confusion due to increased calcium levels from bone resorption.
  • Check renal failure signs – Oedema and hypertension may develop in severe cases of renal impairment.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Multiple Myeloma"][/sc]
  • Hypercalcaemia – This can indicate extensive bone destruction and poses a risk of cardiac arrhythmias, confusion, and coma.
  • Renal failure – Progression to myeloma kidney may lead to chronic kidney disease or acute kidney injury, necessitating dialysis.
  • Spinal cord compression – A medical emergency due to vertebral fractures or plasmacytomas causing compression of the spinal cord, leading to paralysis.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Multiple Myeloma"][/sc]
  • Asymptomatic hypercalcaemia – Patients may present with mild, nonspecific symptoms such as fatigue or nausea, where myeloma is diagnosed incidentally during investigations for hypercalcaemia.
  • Recurrent or unusual infections – Sometimes the first presenting feature, especially in older adults, without the typical bone pain or renal symptoms.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask multiple myeloma.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Multiple Myeloma"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Full blood count – Anaemia is common due to bone marrow suppression; Serum calcium – Elevated due to increased osteoclast activity.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Targeted investigations to confirm diagnosis, assess complications, and monitor response in multiple myeloma.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Multiple Myeloma"][/sc]
  • Metastatic bone disease – Presents similarly with bone - distinguish by focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.
  • Chronic lymphocytic leukaemia (CLL) – Can cause lymphadenopathy - distinguish by focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.
  • Waldenström's macroglobulinaemia – Similar to multiple myeloma but - distinguish by focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.
  • Related alternative diagnosis - Distinguish from multiple myeloma using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.

  • Immediate priorities: Assess severity, physiological stability, and immediate complications.
  • Core care: Deliver condition-specific management for multiple myeloma, treat underlying drivers, and coordinate appropriate specialist input.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Reassess symptom trajectory, examination findings, and objective markers to confirm response in multiple myeloma.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Escalate urgently for clinical deterioration, evolving red flags, or failure to improve in suspected multiple myeloma.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Multiple Myeloma"][/sc]
  • Fractures – Due to bone destruction from osteolytic lesions.
  • Renal failure – Light chain nephropathy and hypercalcaemia can lead to kidney damage.
  • Infections – Immunosuppression from the disease itself and treatment increases infection risk.
