Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Traveller’s Diarrhoea"][/sc]
  • Traveller’s diarrhoea primarily results from the ingestion of contaminated food or water containing pathogenic microorganisms, most commonly enterotoxigenic *E. coli* (ETEC). These bacteria produce toxins that stimulate intestinal cells to secrete electrolytes and water into the gut, leading to diarrhoea. Other organisms such as *Campylobacter*, *Salmonella*, and viruses like norovirus can also cause this condition. The immune system attempts to clear the infection, resulting in systemic symptoms like fever and malaise.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Traveller’s Diarrhoea"][/sc]
  • Incubation period: Typically 1–3 days after ingestion of contaminated food or water.
  • Acute phase: Symptoms develop rapidly and usually resolve within 3–7 days, although some cases can last up to 10 days.
  • Chronic phase: Rarely, complications such as post-infectious irritable bowel syndrome (IBS) can result in prolonged symptoms lasting weeks to months.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Traveller’s Diarrhoea"][/sc]
  • Ask about diarrhoea – Frequent passage of loose stools occurs due to increased intestinal secretion of fluids and electrolytes in response to bacterial toxins.
  • Ask about abdominal cramping – Spasms of the intestinal muscles caused by inflammation and irritation of the gut lining.
  • Ask about nausea and vomiting – Likely due to toxins or irritation of the gastrointestinal tract, causing activation of the vomiting centre in the brain.
  • Ask about fever – A systemic response to infection, caused by immune activation against pathogens.
  • Ask about fatigue and malaise – Generalised symptoms resulting from the immune response and dehydration.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Traveller’s Diarrhoea"][/sc]
  • Check dehydration – Signs include dry mucous membranes, decreased skin turgor, and reduced urine output, resulting from fluid losses due to diarrhoea.
  • Check tachycardia – Increased heart rate as a compensatory response to fluid loss and dehydration.
  • Check hypotension – Low blood pressure in severe cases of dehydration, resulting from decreased circulating blood volume.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Traveller’s Diarrhoea"][/sc]
  • Blood in stools – May indicate invasive bacterial infection (e.g., *Shigella*, *Campylobacter*) or amoebic dysentery, requiring urgent treatment.
  • Persistent diarrhoea (>10 days) – Could suggest parasitic infections (e.g., *Giardia lamblia*) or post-infectious irritable bowel syndrome.
  • High fever (>38.5°C) – Suggests a more severe or systemic infection that may require hospitalisation and intravenous treatment.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Traveller’s Diarrhoea"][/sc]
  • Some cases may present with only mild diarrhoea or without abdominal pain, leading to underreporting or delayed diagnosis.
  • Chronic symptoms such as fatigue and bloating, especially after return from travel, may be the only signs of persistent infection or post-infectious IBS.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask traveller’s diarrhoea.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Traveller’s Diarrhoea"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Stool culture – Identifies bacterial pathogens. Positive findings indicate infection with organisms such as *E. coli*, *Salmonella*, or *Shigella*; Ova, cysts.
  • Targeted and monitoring: FBC (Full Blood Count) – May show raised white cell count, indicating an immune response to infection; Urea and electrolytes (U&E).

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Traveller’s Diarrhoea"][/sc]
  • Dehydration – Most common complication, especially in vulnerable populations like children and the elderly.
  • Post-infectious IBS – Chronic abdominal pain and altered bowel habits following resolution of the acute infection.
  • Reactive arthritis – Inflammation of the joints following infection with certain pathogens, including *Campylobacter* and *Salmonella*.
