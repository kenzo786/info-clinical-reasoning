Oesophagitis
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Oesophagitis"][/sc]
  • Oesophagitis is an inflammation of the oesophageal mucosa, often resulting from acid reflux, infections, or medication-induced irritation.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise oesophagitis features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.

  • Higher-risk or missed groups: Oesophagitis may be missed when presentation is atypical or obscured by comorbidity.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Oesophagitis"][/sc]
  • Gastro-oesophageal reflux disease (GERD)-related: Chronic acid reflux damages the oesophageal lining, leading to inflammation. Repeated exposure to gastric acid can cause erosive oesophagitis.
  • Infectious oesophagitis: Commonly seen in immunocompromised patients, infections such as Candida, herpes simplex virus, and cytomegalovirus infect and inflame the oesophagus.
  • Medication-induced oesophagitis: Certain medications (e.g., NSAIDs, bisphosphonates) can irritate the oesophageal lining when they linger in the oesophagus.
  • Eosinophilic oesophagitis: An allergic inflammatory response driven by eosinophils, commonly seen in atopic individuals.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Oesophagitis"][/sc]
  • Acute: Sudden onset of symptoms due to irritants or infection, often resolving within days to weeks with treatment.
  • Chronic: GERD-related oesophagitis often follows a relapsing-remitting course with long-term symptoms unless managed with lifestyle modifications or medication.
  • Progression: Untreated chronic oesophagitis may lead to complications like Barrett’s oesophagus, strictures, or even oesophageal cancer.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Oesophagitis"][/sc]
  • Ask about heartburn (pyrosis): Burning sensation behind the sternum due to acid reflux irritating the oesophageal lining.
  • Ask about regurgitation: Feeling of acid or food coming back up, due to lower oesophageal sphincter incompetence.
  • Ask about dysphagia: Difficulty swallowing; may be caused by oesophageal narrowing from inflammation or fibrosis.
  • Ask about odynophagia: Pain on swallowing, often due to severe inflammation or ulceration.
  • Ask about chest pain: Non-cardiac chest pain that may mimic angina, secondary to oesophageal spasm or inflammation.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Oesophagitis"][/sc]
  • Check epigastric tenderness: Localised pain on palpation, often associated with GERD.
  • Check oral candidiasis: Seen in immunocompromised patients with Candida oesophagitis.
  • Check weight loss: In advanced cases, particularly if dysphagia is severe.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Oesophagitis"][/sc]
  • Unintentional weight loss: May indicate malignancy or severe stricture formation.
  • Persistent vomiting: Can suggest complications like strictures or underlying malignancy.
  • Haematemesis or melaena: Indicates possible ulceration or oesophageal bleeding, requiring urgent evaluation.
  • Severe dysphagia: May suggest advanced disease, malignancy, or stricture.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Oesophagitis"][/sc]
  • Chronic cough: Acid reflux may irritate the respiratory tract, leading to a chronic cough.
  • Hoarseness: Acid exposure can irritate the vocal cords.
  • Asthma-like symptoms: Reflux may cause bronchospasm, especially in patients with underlying respiratory conditions.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask oesophagitis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Oesophagitis"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Upper GI endoscopy: Visualisation of the oesophagus to assess inflammation or ulceration. Erythema and erosions are typical in GERD-related oesophagitis; Biopsy.
  • Targeted and monitoring: 24-hour pH monitoring: Evaluates acid exposure in the oesophagus, particularly in GERD; Barium swallow: Useful in detecting strictures or motility disorders.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Oesophagitis"][/sc]
  • Gastro-oesophageal reflux disease (GERD) - Main cause of oesophagitis; often presents similarly but may lack severe mucosal damage.
  • Oesophageal cancer - Presents with progressive dysphagia, weight loss, and occasionally haematemesis.
  • Peptic ulcer disease - Epigastric pain and discomfort without significant oesophageal involvement.
  • Achalasia - Progressive dysphagia without heartburn, diagnosed with barium swallow or manometry.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Oesophagitis"][/sc]
  • Barrett’s oesophagus: Metaplastic change in the oesophageal lining, a precursor to adenocarcinoma, due to chronic acid exposure.
  • Oesophageal stricture: Narrowing of the oesophagus from fibrosis, leading to dysphagia.
  • Oesophageal ulceration and bleeding: Advanced erosive oesophagitis can cause ulcers and haemorrhage.
