Salivary Gland Disorder
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Salivary Gland Disorder"][/sc]
  • Salivary gland disorders encompass a group of conditions affecting the salivary glands, including inflammation (sialadenitis), infection, ductal obstruction (sialolithiasis), neoplasms, and autoimmune conditions such as Sjögren’s syndrome.

Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Salivary Gland Disorder"][/sc]
  • Sialolithiasis: Stones form within the salivary ducts, often due to salivary stasis or increased calcium content, leading to ductal obstruction and gland swelling.
  • Sialadenitis: Bacterial (often Staphylococcus aureus) or viral infection (e.g., mumps) causes inflammation, which may result in glandular swelling, pain, and purulent discharge.
  • Sjögren’s syndrome: Autoimmune destruction of salivary gland tissue results in reduced salivary flow (xerostomia) and eventual glandular atrophy.
  • Neoplasms: Benign (e.g., pleomorphic adenoma) or malignant tumours (e.g., mucoepidermoid carcinoma) cause localised swelling and, in malignancies, rapid growth with possible nerve involvement.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Salivary Gland Disorder"][/sc]
  • Sialadenitis: Typically acute in bacterial infection, with a sudden onset of pain and swelling. Viral sialadenitis (mumps) presents subacutely over several days.
  • Sialolithiasis: Intermittent swelling, particularly with meals, which worsens over weeks to months if untreated.
  • Sjögren’s syndrome: Chronic, insidious progression of xerostomia and dry eyes over months to years.
  • Neoplasms: Benign neoplasms grow slowly over months to years; malignant neoplasms may grow more rapidly over weeks to months.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Salivary Gland Disorder"][/sc]
  • Ask about swelling of the salivary gland - Due to ductal obstruction or inflammation, often intermittent in sialolithiasis (especially after meals) and persistent in neoplasms, with associated clinical features.
  • Ask about pain - Common in sialadenitis due to inflammation and in sialolithiasis when the duct is blocked. Pain may be absent in chronic conditions like.
  • Ask about xerostomia (dry mouth) - A hallmark of Sjögren’s syndrome, caused by reduced salivary secretion. This can also be seen in advanced sialadenitis due, with associated clinical features.
  • Ask about pus discharge - Seen in bacterial sialadenitis when infection results in suppuration within the salivary ducts.
  • Ask about dysphagia or difficulty opening the mouth - May occur in more severe cases due to significant swelling, particularly in infections and neoplasms.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Salivary Gland Disorder"][/sc]
  • Check visible swelling - Most common over the parotid or submandibular glands, often unilateral in sialolithiasis or bacterial infection, bilateral in mumps or Sjögren’s syndrome.
  • Check palpable mass - May suggest a neoplasm or sialolithiasis; the mass in neoplasms tends to be firm, while stones may feel hard.
  • Check tenderness - Present in acute sialadenitis or obstructive sialolithiasis but often absent in neoplasms and chronic autoimmune conditions.
  • Check purulent discharge from the duct - Seen when the salivary duct is compressed, especially in acute bacterial sialadenitis.
  • Check dry mucosa - Reflects significant salivary dysfunction, common in Sjögren’s syndrome and advanced sialadenitis.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Salivary Gland Disorder"][/sc]
  • Facial nerve palsy - Involvement of the facial nerve, particularly with malignant parotid tumours, suggests invasion of surrounding structures.
  • Rapidly growing mass - Raises suspicion for malignancy, as benign tumours typically grow slowly.
  • Systemic symptoms (fever, malaise) - In acute bacterial sialadenitis, these may indicate the spread of infection or abscess formation.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Salivary Gland Disorder"][/sc]
  • Chronic painless swelling - Could suggest a chronic autoimmune process like Sjögren’s syndrome or a slowly growing benign tumour, rather than acute infection.
  • Unilateral dry eye and dry mouth - Involvement of only one side may be seen in Sjögren’s syndrome but could also suggest a localised obstruction or tumour.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask salivary gland disorder.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Salivary Gland Disorder"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Ultrasound - First-line imaging for salivary gland swelling. Stones appear as echogenic foci with acoustic shadowing, while neoplasms and abscesses may.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Targeted investigations to confirm diagnosis, assess complications, and monitor response in salivary gland disorder.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Salivary Gland Disorder"][/sc]
  • Lymphadenopathy - Lymph node swelling can mimic salivary gland enlargement but is usually associated with infection.
  • Temporomandibular joint disorder - Can cause facial pain and swelling but is associated with joint tenderness and limited.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Salivary Gland Disorder"][/sc]
  • Abscess formation - Particularly in untreated bacterial sialadenitis, leading to systemic infection and potential spread to adjacent tissues.
  • Chronic gland dysfunction - In Sjögren’s syndrome or recurrent sialolithiasis, chronic xerostomia can cause dental caries and oral infections.
  • Malignancy - Neoplasms, especially in the parotid gland, can progress to invasive cancer with facial nerve involvement if not treated.
