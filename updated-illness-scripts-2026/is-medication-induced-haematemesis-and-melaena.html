Medication-induced Haematemesis & Melaena
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Medication-induced Haematemesis & Melaena"][/sc]
  • Medication-induced haematemesis and melaena refer to upper gastrointestinal (GI) bleeding caused by drugs that damage the gastric mucosa, leading to blood in vomit (haematemesis) and/or black, tarry stools (melaena).
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise medication features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.

Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Medication-induced Haematemesis & Melaena"][/sc]
  • Medications like NSAIDs and corticosteroids inhibit prostaglandin synthesis, weakening the protective mucosal lining in the stomach and duodenum. This inhibition leads to acid-induced mucosal damage, erosion, and, in severe cases, ulceration. Anticoagulants increase bleeding risk by inhibiting clotting mechanisms, exacerbating blood loss if ulcers or erosions are present.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Medication-induced Haematemesis & Melaena"][/sc]
  • Medication-induced GI bleeding can develop acutely within hours to days after initiating or increasing doses of high-risk drugs. Chronic use, especially of NSAIDs, may lead to gradual erosion and ulceration over weeks to months, culminating in a bleeding episode.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Medication-induced Haematemesis & Melaena"][/sc]
  • Ask about haematemesis - Vomiting blood, either fresh (bright red) or digested (coffee-ground appearance), due to acute bleeding from mucosal erosion or ulceration.
  • Ask about melaena - Passage of black, tarry stools from digested blood, typically from a bleeding source in the upper GI tract.
  • Ask about epigastric pain - Pain due to mucosal erosion or ulceration; NSAIDs inhibit protective prostaglandins, increasing susceptibility.
  • Ask about nausea and vomiting - Resulting from irritation of the gastric mucosa.
  • Ask about fatigue and dizziness - Often related to anaemia from chronic blood loss, particularly if bleeding is slow or unrecognised.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Medication-induced Haematemesis & Melaena"][/sc]
  • Check pallor - Due to anaemia from ongoing blood loss.
  • Check hypotension - Sign of significant blood loss; may progress to shock in severe cases.
  • Check tachycardia - Compensatory response to hypovolaemia from blood loss.
  • Check epigastric tenderness - May indicate mucosal erosion or ulceration.
  • Check black, tarry stools - Indicates bleeding that has been digested; typical of melaena.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Medication-induced Haematemesis & Melaena"][/sc]
  • Haemodynamic instability (hypotension, tachycardia) - Indicates significant blood loss; requires immediate intervention to prevent shock.
  • Persistent haematemesis - Suggests ongoing bleeding that could lead to haemorrhagic shock.
  • Acute abdominal pain with rigidity - Could signify perforation, a life-threatening complication.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Medication-induced Haematemesis & Melaena"][/sc]
  • Asymptomatic anaemia - Some patients may present only with anaemia due to slow, chronic blood loss, especially in elderly patients.
  • Non-specific abdominal discomfort - Low-grade discomfort rather than pain can mask the condition.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask medication-induced haematemesis & melaena.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Medication-induced Haematemesis & Melaena"][/sc]
  • Peptic ulcer disease (PUD) - Distinguish from medication-induced haematemesis & melaena using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.
  • Oesophageal varices - Often associated with liver disease; presents with significant haematemesis but usually without epigastric pain.
  • Gastric malignancy - May cause occult bleeding or haematemesis; associated with weight loss and early satiety.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Medication-induced Haematemesis & Melaena"][/sc]
  • Haemorrhagic shock - Due to rapid blood loss; fatal if untreated.
  • Perforation - Risk increases with prolonged NSAID use; requires surgical intervention.
  • Iron-deficiency anaemia - Chronic bleeding can lead to ongoing anaemia, often requiring iron supplementation.
