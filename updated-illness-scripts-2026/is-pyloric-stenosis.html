Pyloric Stenosis
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Pyloric Stenosis"][/sc]
  • Pyloric stenosis is a condition characterised by hypertrophy and hyperplasia of the pyloric muscle, resulting in obstruction of the gastric outlet.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise pyloric features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Pyloric Stenosis"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Pyloric Stenosis"][/sc]
  • Risk factors: Risk factors for pyloric stenosis include relevant predisposition, comorbidity, and exposure history.
  • Higher-risk or missed groups: Pyloric Stenosis may be missed when presentation is atypical or obscured by comorbidity.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Pyloric Stenosis"][/sc]
  • Hypertrophy and hyperplasia of the pyloric muscle lead to narrowing of the pyloric canal.
  • Impaired gastric emptying results in increased intragastric pressure, leading to projectile vomiting.
  • Chronic vomiting causes hypochloraemic, hypokalaemic metabolic alkalosis due to loss of hydrogen and chloride ions in vomitus.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Pyloric Stenosis"][/sc]
  • Onset: Gradual, typically within 2–8 weeks of life.
  • Progression: Symptoms worsen progressively without treatment, leading to severe dehydration and electrolyte imbalance.
  • Resolution: Symptoms resolve rapidly following surgical intervention (pyloromyotomy).
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Pyloric Stenosis"][/sc]
  • Ask about projectile non-bilious vomiting - Caused by obstruction at the gastric outlet; vomitus lacks bile as the obstruction is proximal to the duodenum.
  • Ask about persistent hunger - Due to inadequate gastric emptying, infants remain unsatisfied despite frequent feeding attempts.
  • Ask about weight loss or poor weight gain - Reflects chronic caloric deficit from vomiting.
  • Ask about dehydration - Caused by fluid loss from recurrent vomiting.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Pyloric Stenosis"][/sc]
  • Check palpable “olive” mass in the epigastrium - Represents hypertrophied pyloric muscle.
  • Check visible peristaltic waves - Indicative of increased gastric effort to overcome the obstruction.
  • Check signs of dehydration - Dry mucous membranes, sunken fontanelles, and decreased skin turgor due to fluid loss.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Pyloric Stenosis"][/sc]
  • Severe dehydration - May indicate impending hypovolaemic shock.
  • Hypochloraemic metabolic alkalosis - A serious electrolyte disturbance that requires urgent correction.
  • Failure to thrive - Suggests delayed diagnosis or other underlying pathology.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Pyloric Stenosis"][/sc]
  • Bilious vomiting - Suggests an alternate or concurrent condition such as malrotation.
  • Late presentation - Beyond the typical age range, may be confused with other causes of vomiting.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask pyloric stenosis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Pyloric Stenosis"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Abdominal ultrasound - Thickened pyloric muscle (>3 mm) and elongated pyloric canal (>15 mm) confirm diagnosis.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Blood gas analysis - Shows metabolic alkalosis (elevated bicarbonate, decreased chloride); Electrolyte panel - Hypokalaemia and hypochloraemia due to vomiting.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Pyloric Stenosis"][/sc]
  • Gastro-oesophageal reflux disease - Vomiting without palpable mass; normal growth.
  • Intestinal malrotation - Bilious vomiting; requires surgical exploration.
  • Milk protein allergy - Vomiting with diarrhoea and blood in stools.
  • Related alternative diagnosis - Distinguish from pyloric stenosis using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Pyloric Stenosis"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Assess severity, physiological stability, and immediate complications.
  • Core care: Deliver condition-specific management for pyloric stenosis, treat underlying drivers, and coordinate appropriate specialist input.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Reassess symptom trajectory, examination findings, and objective markers to confirm response in pyloric stenosis.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Escalate urgently for clinical deterioration, evolving red flags, or failure to improve in suspected pyloric stenosis.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Pyloric Stenosis"][/sc]
  • Dehydration and electrolyte imbalance - Requires urgent correction to prevent shock.
  • Postoperative vomiting - Common but typically resolves spontaneously.
  • Failure to thrive - Occurs if diagnosis and treatment are delayed.
