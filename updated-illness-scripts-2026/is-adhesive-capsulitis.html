Adhesive Capsulitis
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Adhesive Capsulitis"][/sc]
  • Adhesive capsulitis, commonly referred to as "frozen shoulder," is a condition characterised by pain and progressive loss of both active and passive shoulder movements, caused by chronic inflammation.
  • UKMLA essentials: progressive shoulder stiffness, constant resting pain or systemic features, restricted passive external rotation.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Adhesive Capsulitis"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Adhesive Capsulitis"][/sc]
  • Adhesive Capsulitis is encountered across acute and specialist care, with burden shaped by age, comorbidity, and exposure context.
  • Risk increases in patients with relevant predisposing disease, treatment exposures, or delayed access to care.
  • Missed or late recognition is more likely when presentations are atypical or overlap with common alternative diagnoses.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Adhesive Capsulitis"][/sc]
  • Chronic inflammation of the glenohumeral joint capsule leads to synovial hypertrophy.
  • Progressive fibrosis and thickening of the joint capsule result in decreased joint volume and loss of elasticity.
  • Neovascularisation and neural sensitisation contribute to pain and reduced shoulder mobility.
  • In primary adhesive capsulitis, the cause is often idiopathic, while secondary forms arise from trauma, surgery, or systemic conditions like diabetes.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Adhesive Capsulitis"][/sc]
  • Freezing phase: Gradual onset of pain and stiffness, typically lasting 2-9 months.
  • Frozen phase: Significant stiffness with reduced pain, lasting 4-12 months.
  • Thawing phase: Gradual resolution of stiffness and improvement in range of motion, over 12-24 months.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Adhesive Capsulitis"][/sc]
  • Ask about Shoulder pain - Often insidious in onset, worse at night and with activity; due to synovial inflammation and neural sensitisation.
  • Ask about Loss of range of motion - Both active and passive movements are restricted, particularly external rotation; attributed to joint capsule fibrosis.
  • Ask about Shoulder stiffness - Common in the frozen phase; a result of capsular contracture and fibrosis.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Adhesive Capsulitis"][/sc]
  • Check Restricted external rotation - Often the earliest and most specific sign; caused by capsular fibrosis.
  • Check Painful arc - Pain exacerbated in mid-range abduction; due to joint capsule inflammation.
  • Check Generalised shoulder tenderness - Diffuse tenderness on palpation; linked to underlying synovial inflammation.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Adhesive Capsulitis"][/sc]
  • Severe, constant pain unrelated to movement - May indicate septic arthritis or malignancy.
  • Systemic symptoms (e.g., fever, weight loss) - Suggests infection, malignancy, or systemic inflammatory conditions.
  • Neurological deficits (e.g., numbness, weakness) - Could indicate brachial plexus involvement or cervical radiculopathy.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Adhesive Capsulitis"][/sc]
  • Presentation with predominant stiffness but minimal pain, especially in diabetic patients.
  • Unilateral symptoms that mimic other musculoskeletal disorders, such as rotator cuff tears.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Adhesive Capsulitis"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Plain radiographs: Usually normal but can rule out osteoarthritis or fractures.
  • Targeted and monitoring: MRI: May show thickening of the joint capsule and synovitis; confirms adhesive capsulitis.; Ultrasound: Useful for identifying associated rotator cuff pathology or effusion.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Adhesive Capsulitis"][/sc]
  • A 55-year-old woman presents with a 6-month history of worsening left shoulder pain, predominantly at night, and progressive stiffness. No history of trauma. Examination reveals restricted active and passive range of motion, particularly external rotation.
  • Middle-aged adult with insidious painful shoulder stiffness and marked restriction of active and passive external rotation, suggesting adhesive capsulitis.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Adhesive Capsulitis"][/sc]
  • Rotator cuff tear - Pain typically exacerbated by specific movements; MRI can confirm the diagnosis.
  • Osteoarthritis - Radiographs show joint space narrowing and osteophytes.
  • Cervical radiculopathy - Associated neck pain and neurological symptoms; confirmed by MRI.
  • Septic arthritis - Acutely hot painful shoulder with fever and inflammatory markers suggests urgent infective pathology.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Adhesive Capsulitis"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Exclude red-flag infection or malignancy, explain diagnosis, and begin pain-relieving measures that allow shoulder movement.
  • Core care: Structured physiotherapy and home stretching are first-line, with analgesic optimisation and injection options considered for persistent pain-limited rehabilitation.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Track pain, sleep disturbance, and active/passive range of motion to stage disease and guide escalation.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Persistent severe functional restriction despite supervised conservative treatment should prompt specialist review for hydrodilatation or operative options.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Adhesive Capsulitis"][/sc]
  • Chronic pain: Due to persistent inflammation and neural sensitisation.
  • Permanent loss of range of motion: Rare but can occur if untreated.
  • Deconditioning: Prolonged immobility may lead to muscle wasting and weakness.
