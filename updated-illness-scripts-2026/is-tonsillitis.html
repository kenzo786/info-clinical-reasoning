Tonsillitis Illness Script
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Tonsillitis"][/sc]
  • Tonsillitis is an inflammation of the palatine tonsils, typically caused by viral or bacterial infections.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise tonsillitis features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.

Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Tonsillitis"][/sc]
  • In tonsillitis, the tonsils are infected and inflamed, often due to pathogens like viruses (adenovirus, rhinovirus) or bacteria (e.g., GAS). The immune response to infection leads to recruitment of inflammatory cells to the tonsils, resulting in swelling, erythema, and sometimes exudate formation. The inflammatory mediators also cause the characteristic pain and fever associated with the condition.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Tonsillitis"][/sc]
  • Acute tonsillitis typically lasts 5-7 days, with peak symptom severity on days 2-3. Viral cases resolve more quickly, while bacterial tonsillitis may linger without treatment. Complications, such as peritonsillar abscess (quinsy), can develop if untreated.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Tonsillitis"][/sc]
  • Ask about sore throat – Caused by tonsillar inflammation and irritation of local nerves.
  • Ask about dysphagia (painful swallowing) – Due to tonsillar and surrounding tissue swelling, making swallowing difficult.
  • Ask about fever – A systemic inflammatory response triggered by infection.
  • Ask about ear pain (referred otalgia) – Pain referred to the ears via the glossopharyngeal nerve.
  • Ask about headache – Caused by systemic inflammation and dehydration.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Tonsillitis"][/sc]
  • Check tonsillar erythema – Due to local inflammation.
  • Check tonsillar exudates – Often seen in bacterial infections like GAS, reflecting pus formation.
  • Check cervical lymphadenopathy – Enlargement of lymph nodes as they drain the infection in the tonsils.
  • Check halitosis – Resulting from bacterial colonisation and pus in the oropharynx.
  • Check fever – Indicative of systemic infection.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Tonsillitis"][/sc]
  • Difficulty breathing – Suggests airway compromise due to severe swelling or a peritonsillar abscess.
  • Severe neck stiffness – Could indicate extension of infection into the deep neck spaces (e.g., retropharyngeal abscess).
  • Trismus – Inability to open the mouth may signal a peritonsillar abscess.
  • Unilateral tonsillar swelling – Concerning for peritonsillar abscess formation.
  • Drooling – Can suggest airway compromise or significant pharyngeal swelling.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Tonsillitis"][/sc]
  • Asymptomatic presentation – Some viral causes may result in mild or no symptoms, especially in immunocompromised individuals.
  • Chronic tonsillitis – Persistent mild sore throat and enlarged tonsils without acute symptoms.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask tonsillitis illness script.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Tonsillitis"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Throat swab – A throat culture or rapid antigen detection test (RADT) for GAS; a positive result confirms bacterial aetiology; FBC.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Monospot test – If infectious mononucleosis is suspected, this can detect Epstein-Barr virus infection; C-reactive protein (CRP) – Elevated in bacterial.

  • Infectious mononucleosis – Similar presentation but often associated - distinguish by focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.
  • Peritonsillar abscess (quinsy) – Presents with trismus, unilateral - distinguish by focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.
  • Epiglottitis – Acute onset, drooling, and muffled voice - distinguish by focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Tonsillitis"][/sc]
  • Peritonsillar abscess – Localised infection that can cause airway compromise.
  • Rheumatic fever – A systemic immune response to untreated GAS infection, affecting the heart, joints, and skin.
  • Post-streptococcal glomerulonephritis – Immune complex deposition in the kidneys following GAS infection.
