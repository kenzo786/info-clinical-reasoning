Metabolic Disorders
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Metabolic Disorders"][/sc]
  • Metabolic disorders encompass a group of conditions that disrupt normal metabolic processes, leading to deficiencies or excesses in energy, nutrients, or substrates essential for cell function.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise metabolic features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Metabolic Disorders"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Metabolic Disorders"][/sc]
  • Risk factors: Risk factors for metabolic disorders include relevant predisposition, comorbidity, and exposure history.
  • Higher-risk or missed groups: Metabolic Disorders may be missed when presentation is atypical or obscured by comorbidity.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Metabolic Disorders"][/sc]
  • Metabolic disorders arise from enzyme deficiencies, hormonal imbalances, or cellular dysfunctions in metabolic pathways. This may lead to the accumulation of toxic substrates, deficiency of crucial end-products, or disrupted cellular energy production.
  • Genetic metabolic disorders are often due to inherited enzyme defects (e.g., phenylketonuria) that impair substrate breakdown or biosynthesis.
  • Acquired metabolic disorders may result from lifestyle factors (e.g., Type 2 diabetes from insulin resistance) or other systemic diseases impacting metabolism (e.g., thyroid dysfunction affecting basal metabolic rate).
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Metabolic Disorders"][/sc]
  • Acute presentations (e.g., metabolic acidosis in untreated diabetes) may occur over hours to days, particularly if triggered by infection, trauma, or other acute stressors.
  • Chronic metabolic disorders often have a gradual onset with progressive symptoms, such as fatigue, polyuria, or weight changes, and may present asymptomatically initially (e.g., hypercholesterolemia).
  • Congenital metabolic disorders may present in infancy or early childhood, often with symptoms like developmental delay, feeding issues, or recurrent illness episodes.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Metabolic Disorders"][/sc]
  • Ask about fatigue - Common across multiple metabolic disorders due to inefficient cellular energy production, especially in hypothyroidism or diabetes.
  • Ask about weight changes - Weight loss may occur in uncontrolled diabetes due to catabolism; weight gain is typical in hypothyroidism.
  • Ask about polyuria and Polydipsia - In diabetes, hyperglycaemia leads to osmotic diuresis, causing increased thirst and urination.
  • Ask about muscle weakness - Seen in disorders such as hypothyroidism due to reduced cellular metabolism.
  • Ask about nausea and vomiting - Acidosis (e.g., in diabetes) and toxic metabolite accumulation (e.g., urea cycle defects) often stimulate nausea and emesis.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Metabolic Disorders"][/sc]
  • Check abdominal pain - Common in metabolic acidosis as well as inborn errors of metabolism involving the liver.
  • Check altered mental state - May indicate severe metabolic disturbance, including hypoglycaemia, hypercalcaemia, or hyperammonaemia.
  • Check dry skin and hair - Particularly seen in hypothyroidism due to reduced peripheral blood flow and slowed skin turnover.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Metabolic Disorders"][/sc]
  • Altered consciousness - Signifies severe metabolic imbalance or acidosis, as seen in diabetic ketoacidosis, requiring urgent intervention.
  • Severe abdominal pain - Could indicate lactic acidosis or hepatic involvement in metabolic disorders, signalling potential organ dysfunction.
  • Rapid weight loss - Suggestive of uncontrolled diabetes or hyperthyroidism, with risk of dehydration and other complications.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Metabolic Disorders"][/sc]
  • Minimal weight change in diabetes - Patients with high body mass index (BMI) may not present with noticeable weight loss despite hyperglycaemia.
  • Absence of polyuria in dehydration - In advanced kidney disease or in elderly patients with diabetes, polyuria may be masked.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask metabolic disorders.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Metabolic Disorders"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Blood glucose - Elevated in diabetes; fasting glucose >7 mmol/L or HbA1c >48 mmol/mol is diagnostic.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Thyroid function tests (TFTs) - Low free T4 and elevated TSH suggest hypothyroidism; high free T4 and suppressed TSH indicate hyperthyroidism.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Metabolic Disorders"][/sc]
  • A 45-year-old male presents with fatigue, polyuria, and unintentional weight loss over three months, with a history of hypertension. Blood tests reveal elevated HbA1c, consistent with new-onset diabetes mellitus.
  • Patient with fatigue, polyuria, and unintentional weight loss over three months, with a history of hypertension. Blood tests reveal elevated HbA1c, consistent with new-onset diabetes mellitus, consistent with metabolic disorders.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Metabolic Disorders"][/sc]
  • Chronic Kidney Disease - Fatigue and weight changes also seen, but with evidence of proteinuria and high serum.
  • Hypothyroidism - Overlaps with fatigue and weight issues, but polyuria is absent; TFTs are diagnostic.
  • Primary Hyperparathyroidism - Similar symptoms but accompanied by hypercalcaemia; parathyroid hormone levels are diagnostic.
  • Related alternative diagnosis - Distinguish from metabolic disorders using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Metabolic Disorders"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Assess severity, physiological stability, and immediate complications.
  • Core care: Deliver condition-specific management for metabolic disorders, treat underlying drivers, and coordinate appropriate specialist input.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Reassess symptom trajectory, examination findings, and objective markers to confirm response in metabolic disorders.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Escalate urgently for clinical deterioration, evolving red flags, or failure to improve in suspected metabolic disorders.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Metabolic Disorders"][/sc]
  • Cardiovascular disease - Common in diabetes and lipid disorders, due to atherosclerosis and endothelial dysfunction.
  • Chronic kidney disease - Progresses over years with uncontrolled diabetes, leading to proteinuria and eventual renal failure.
  • Neuropathy - In diabetes, chronic hyperglycaemia damages peripheral nerves, resulting in pain and sensory loss.
