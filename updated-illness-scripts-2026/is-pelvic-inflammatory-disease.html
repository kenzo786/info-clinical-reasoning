Pelvic Inflammatory Disease
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Pelvic Inflammatory Disease"][/sc]
  • Pelvic Inflammatory Disease (PID) is an infection of the female upper genital tract, including the uterus, fallopian tubes, and ovaries.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise pelvic features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.

  • Higher-risk or missed groups: Pelvic Inflammatory Disease may be missed when presentation is atypical or obscured by comorbidity.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Pelvic Inflammatory Disease"][/sc]
  • PID typically begins with a bacterial infection of the cervix (cervicitis) due to an STI or other genital infection. If untreated, bacteria ascend through the endometrium to the fallopian tubes and ovaries. This leads to an inflammatory response, which can cause scarring and adhesions, contributing to tubal obstruction, chronic pain, and infertility. In severe cases, tubo-ovarian abscesses may develop.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Pelvic Inflammatory Disease"][/sc]
  • PID can present acutely (over days to weeks) with severe symptoms, or subacutely with milder symptoms that persist for months, making diagnosis challenging. Chronic PID can develop if the acute phase is inadequately treated, leading to long-term complications.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Pelvic Inflammatory Disease"][/sc]
  • Ask about lower abdominal pain - Typically bilateral, due to inflammation of the pelvic organs.
  • Ask about abnormal vaginal discharge - Often mucopurulent due to cervicitis and endometritis.
  • Ask about dyspareunia - Pain during intercourse, reflecting inflammation of the upper genital tract.
  • Ask about fever - May occur due to systemic inflammation, though often mild or absent.
  • Ask about irregular menstruation - Due to disruption of the endometrium.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Pelvic Inflammatory Disease"][/sc]
  • Check lower abdominal tenderness - Commonly bilateral, due to pelvic inflammation.
  • Check cervical motion tenderness - Intense pain upon movement of the cervix during examination, indicating spread of infection to upper genital organs.
  • Check adnexal tenderness - Pain in the region of the fallopian tubes or ovaries, indicating inflammation or abscess formation.
  • Check fever - Present in some cases, especially with more severe infections.
  • Check mucopurulent cervical discharge - Visible on pelvic exam, often due to cervicitis from STIs.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Pelvic Inflammatory Disease"][/sc]
  • Severe lower abdominal pain - May indicate a tubo-ovarian abscess or peritonitis.
  • High fever with rigors - Suggests a severe or systemic infection, potentially signalling a complicated PID or abscess.
  • Right upper quadrant pain - Fitz-Hugh-Curtis syndrome, where inflammation extends to the liver capsule.
  • Sepsis signs - Tachycardia, hypotension, and confusion indicate systemic infection requiring urgent management.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Pelvic Inflammatory Disease"][/sc]
  • Asymptomatic PID - Common, especially with Chlamydia, leading to delayed diagnosis and increased risk of complications.
  • Right-sided abdominal pain - May mimic appendicitis if unilateral, especially with gonococcal infections.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask pelvic inflammatory disease.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Pelvic Inflammatory Disease"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Pelvic examination - Cervical motion tenderness and adnexal tenderness support the diagnosis; NAAT (Nucleic Acid Amplification Tests) for STIs - Identifies.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Ultrasound - Can show pelvic inflammatory changes or abscesses; CRP and WBC counts - Elevated in PID, indicating an inflammatory response.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Pelvic Inflammatory Disease"][/sc]
  • Appendicitis - Right-sided abdominal pain and fever but lacks cervical motion tenderness.
  • Ectopic pregnancy - Severe lower abdominal pain with a missed period, positive pregnancy test.
  • Endometriosis - Chronic pain associated with menstruation, lacks signs of infection.
  • Related alternative diagnosis - Distinguish from pelvic inflammatory disease using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Pelvic Inflammatory Disease"][/sc]
  • Infertility - Due to scarring and obstruction of the fallopian tubes, occurring in ~10-20% of cases.
  • Chronic pelvic pain - Persistent inflammation leads to adhesions and chronic discomfort.
  • Ectopic pregnancy - Tubal damage increases the risk of ectopic implantation, a potentially life-threatening complication.
