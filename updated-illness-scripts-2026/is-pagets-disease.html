Paget's Disease
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Paget's Disease"][/sc]
  • Paget's disease of bone is a chronic, localised skeletal disorder characterised by excessive and disorganised bone remodelling.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise paget features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.

Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Paget's Disease"][/sc]
  • The disease begins with overactive osteoclasts, leading to excessive bone resorption (lytic phase). In response, osteoblasts overcompensate by producing large amounts of new bone in a disorganised manner (sclerotic phase). This results in structurally abnormal and weakened bones that are prone to deformities, fractures, and arthritis.
  • Osteoclast dysfunction may be linked to viral infections (e.g., paramyxovirus) or genetic mutations (e.g., SQSTM1 gene).
  • The abnormal bone structure increases vascularity, leading to warmth over affected areas.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Paget's Disease"][/sc]
  • Paget's disease is typically chronic, progressing slowly over decades. Many patients remain asymptomatic for years. The disease may be discovered incidentally or due to complications such as fractures, deformities, or secondary arthritis.
  • Phases of the disease include the initial lytic phase, followed by the mixed lytic and sclerotic phase, and finally a sclerotic phase where the bone is dense but abnormal.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Paget's Disease"][/sc]
  • Ask about bone pain - Most common symptom, often deep and aching. This is caused by microfractures or abnormal bone remodelling.
  • Ask about joint pain - Results from secondary osteoarthritis due to joint deformities caused by abnormal bone growth.
  • Ask about warmth over affected bones - Due to increased vascularity of the abnormal bone.
  • Ask about hearing loss - Involvement of the skull, particularly the temporal bone, can lead to conductive or sensorineural hearing loss.
  • Ask about headache - Skull involvement may lead to thickening, causing pressure on surrounding structures.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Paget's Disease"][/sc]
  • Check bony deformities - Including bowing of long bones or an enlarged skull due to abnormal bone growth.
  • Check fractures - Pathological fractures are common due to weakened, disorganised bone structure.
  • Check hearing loss - Seen in patients with skull involvement, particularly affecting the temporal bone.
  • Check increased warmth over affected areas - A sign of increased bone vascularity in the affected regions.
  • Check reduced range of motion in joints - Due to secondary osteoarthritis.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Paget's Disease"][/sc]
  • Sudden increase in bone pain - May signify a pathological fracture or progression to osteosarcoma (rare but serious complication).
  • Neurological symptoms - Such as weakness, paraesthesia, or incontinence, which can occur due to compression of the spinal cord or cranial nerves in advanced disease.
  • Sudden deafness - Indicative of temporal bone involvement with compression of the auditory structures.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Paget's Disease"][/sc]
  • Isolated skull involvement - Can lead to purely neurological or hearing symptoms without the classical signs of deformity or pain.
  • Silent presentation - Incidentally discovered on imaging or blood tests without any symptoms, especially in older adults.
  • Asymptomatic vertebral involvement - Can cause vertebral compression fractures with minimal trauma.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask paget's disease.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Paget's Disease"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Serum alkaline phosphatase (ALP) - Elevated in most patients due to increased osteoblastic activity. Normal calcium and phosphate levels help differentiate.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Bone scintigraphy - Helps identify areas of high metabolic activity in the bone, particularly useful in asymptomatic patients or unclear cases.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Paget's Disease"][/sc]
  • Osteomalacia - Causes bone pain, but X-rays show Looser's zones and lab results reveal low calcium.
  • Osteosarcoma - Distinguish from paget's disease using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.
  • Primary hyperparathyroidism - Elevated calcium levels and subperiosteal bone resorption, unlike the normal calcium in Paget's disease.
  • Metastatic bone disease - Typically presents with multiple lytic lesions, while Paget's disease shows both lytic and sclerotic.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Paget's Disease"][/sc]
  • Fractures - Occur frequently due to weakened bone structure.
  • Osteoarthritis - Common in joints adjacent to affected bones due to deformities and abnormal stress.
  • Osteosarcoma - A rare but serious complication, occurring in less than 1% of patients.
  • Neurological complications - Including spinal stenosis and cranial nerve compression.
