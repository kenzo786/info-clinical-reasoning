Corneal Abrasion
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Corneal Abrasion"][/sc]
  • Corneal abrasion refers to a superficial injury to the corneal epithelium, often resulting from trauma or foreign bodies.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise corneal features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.

Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Corneal Abrasion"][/sc]
  • Disruption of the corneal epithelium exposes the underlying nerve endings, causing significant pain and photophobia.
  • The cornea’s avascularity impairs immediate healing but relies on tear film and limbal stem cells for repair.
  • Inflammatory responses contribute to redness and tearing.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Corneal Abrasion"][/sc]
  • Acute onset immediately after trauma.
  • Symptoms generally improve within 24-48 hours in uncomplicated cases.
  • Complete epithelial healing occurs in 3-5 days, though prolonged healing may indicate complications.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Corneal Abrasion"][/sc]
  • Ask about ocular pain - Due to exposed corneal nerve endings.
  • Ask about photophobia - Triggered by light-induced stimulation of injured nerve endings.
  • Ask about tearing - Reflex lacrimation caused by irritation and inflammation.
  • Ask about foreign body sensation - Due to uneven corneal surface.
  • Ask about blurred vision - Interruption of corneal smoothness affecting refraction.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Corneal Abrasion"][/sc]
  • Check conjunctival injection - Inflammation causes dilation of superficial blood vessels.
  • Check corneal epithelial defect visible under fluorescein - Revealed with blue light examination.
  • Check eyelid swelling - Reflexive inflammation and irritation.
  • Check reduced visual acuity - Secondary to irregular corneal surface and tearing.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Corneal Abrasion"][/sc]
  • Severe pain unrelieved by topical anaesthetics - May indicate deeper corneal involvement.
  • Purulent discharge - Suggests secondary bacterial infection or microbial keratitis.
  • Persistent epithelial defect beyond 72 hours - Raises concern for underlying conditions (e.g., herpetic keratitis).
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Corneal Abrasion"][/sc]
  • Painless visual blurring in diabetic patients due to reduced corneal sensitivity.
  • Chronic symptoms in recurrent erosion syndrome, where healing is delayed.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features or comorbidity can mask the underlying diagnosis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Corneal Abrasion"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Fluorescein staining with cobalt blue light - Reveals epithelial defects.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Slit-lamp examination - Allows detailed visualisation of the cornea and anterior segment; Seidel test - Excludes corneal perforation by identifying aqueous.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Corneal Abrasion"][/sc]
  • A 25-year-old male presents with acute onset left eye pain, photophobia, and tearing following foreign body trauma while gardening, with no systemic symptoms.
  • Adult with symptom pattern and objective findings suggestive of corneal abrasion, requiring focused confirmation and risk stratification.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Corneal Abrasion"][/sc]
  • Foreign body - May mimic corneal abrasion but with visible material in the eye.
  • Herpetic keratitis - Associated with dendritic staining on fluorescein.
  • Bacterial keratitis - Presents with purulent discharge and corneal opacity.
  • Alternative serious diagnosis - Distinguishing history, examination, and targeted investigations do not support corneal abrasion.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Corneal Abrasion"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Assess physiological stability and urgent complications at presentation, and initiate early stabilisation where required.
  • Core care: Deliver condition-specific treatment for corneal abrasion, address underlying drivers, and involve appropriate specialist support.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Reassess symptom trajectory, objective markers, and treatment response over time to detect complications early.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Worsening symptoms, physiological deterioration, or failure to improve should trigger urgent reassessment for severe or alternative pathology in corneal abrasion.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Corneal Abrasion"][/sc]
  • Infectious keratitis - Risk if secondary bacterial invasion occurs.
  • Recurrent erosion syndrome - Poor healing may lead to chronic symptoms.
  • Scarring - Rare but possible in severe cases, leading to permanent visual impairment.
