Medication-Induced Hypertension
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Medication-Induced Hypertension"][/sc]
  • Medication-induced hypertension refers to elevated blood pressure (BP) caused by the use of specific medications.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise medication features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.

  • Risk factors: Risk factors for medication-induced hypertension include relevant predisposition, comorbidity, and exposure history.
  • Higher-risk or missed groups: Medication-Induced Hypertension may be missed when presentation is atypical or obscured by comorbidity.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Medication-Induced Hypertension"][/sc]
  • Medications can cause hypertension through various mechanisms:
  • Increased sodium and water retention (e.g., NSAIDs, corticosteroids).
  • Sympathetic nervous system activation (e.g., sympathomimetic drugs, certain antidepressants).
  • Vasoconstriction or vascular remodelling (e.g., calcineurin inhibitors like cyclosporine).
  • Hormonal effects, such as increased aldosterone secretion or estrogen/progesterone-related BP elevation (e.g., oral contraceptives).
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Medication-Induced Hypertension"][/sc]
  • Acute Phase: BP elevation can occur within hours to days after initiating the offending medication.
  • Chronic Phase: Prolonged use of the medication leads to persistent hypertension over weeks to months.
  • Resolution: BP usually returns to baseline within days to weeks after discontinuing the offending drug, although chronic hypertension may persist in some cases.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Medication-Induced Hypertension"][/sc]
  • Ask about asymptomatic - Medication-induced hypertension is often discovered incidentally during routine BP checks, as elevated BP often causes no symptoms in the early stages.
  • Ask about headaches - This symptom arises from increased intracranial pressure due to elevated systemic BP.
  • Ask about dizziness or lightheadedness - Caused by changes in vascular tone or cerebral perfusion in response to high BP.
  • Ask about palpitations - May result from sympathetic nervous system stimulation caused by certain medications (e.g., sympathomimetics or antidepressants).
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Medication-Induced Hypertension"][/sc]
  • Check elevated BP - Defined as systolic BP >140 mmHg and/or diastolic BP >90 mmHg, directly measurable using a sphygmomanometer.
  • Check peripheral oedema - May occur in response to fluid retention caused by drugs such as NSAIDs or corticosteroids.
  • Check weight gain - Linked to fluid retention seen with corticosteroids or certain hormonal therapies.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Medication-Induced Hypertension"][/sc]
  • Severe headache or visual disturbances - Suggestive of hypertensive urgency or emergency with end-organ damage (e.g., hypertensive encephalopathy).
  • Chest pain or dyspnoea - Could indicate acute coronary syndrome or heart failure as a result of untreated severe hypertension.
  • Acute confusion or altered mental status - May signify hypertensive encephalopathy or stroke, both of which are emergencies.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Medication-Induced Hypertension"][/sc]
  • Fatigue or general malaise - These nonspecific symptoms may be the only indication of medication-induced hypertension, particularly in older adults.
  • Worsening of pre-existing hypertension - Patients with controlled hypertension may experience a gradual increase in BP that may be misattributed to poor compliance or disease progression.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask medication-induced hypertension.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Medication-Induced Hypertension"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: 24-hour ambulatory BP monitoring (ABPM) - Helps confirm sustained hypertension, ruling out "white coat" hypertension. Medication-induced hypertension typically shows sustained BP.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Targeted investigations to confirm diagnosis, assess complications, and monitor response in medication-induced hypertension.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Medication-Induced Hypertension"][/sc]
  • Essential hypertension - Unlike medication-induced hypertension, this develops more gradually and is not linked to recent medication.
  • Renal artery stenosis - Distinguish from medication-induced hypertension using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.
  • Phaeochromocytoma - Episodic hypertension, palpitations, and sweating are more characteristic of this catecholamine-secreting tumour.
  • Related alternative diagnosis - Distinguish from medication-induced hypertension using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Medication-Induced Hypertension"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Assess severity, physiological stability, and immediate complications.
  • Core care: Deliver condition-specific management for medication-induced hypertension, treat underlying drivers, and coordinate appropriate specialist input.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Reassess symptom trajectory, examination findings, and objective markers to confirm response in medication-induced hypertension.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Escalate urgently for clinical deterioration, evolving red flags, or failure to improve in suspected medication-induced hypertension.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Medication-Induced Hypertension"][/sc]
  • Chronic kidney disease (CKD) - Persistent hypertension may lead to CKD, particularly if medications impair renal perfusion (e.g., NSAIDs).
  • Hypertensive emergency - Severe cases can lead to organ damage, including acute coronary syndromes, stroke, or heart failure.
  • Left ventricular hypertrophy (LVH) - Prolonged elevated BP increases myocardial workload, causing LVH, which can progress to heart failure.
