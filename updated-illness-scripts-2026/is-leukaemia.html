Leukaemia
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Leukaemia"][/sc]
  • Leukaemia is a malignant disorder of the blood and bone marrow characterised by an abnormal proliferation of immature white blood cells (blasts).
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise leukaemia features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Leukaemia"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Leukaemia"][/sc]
  • ALL is most common in children, while AML and chronic forms (CML and CLL) are more common in older adults.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Leukaemia"][/sc]
  • Leukaemia arises from genetic mutations in haematopoietic stem cells, causing a block in normal differentiation and uncontrolled proliferation of immature cells (blasts). These blasts replace normal bone marrow elements, leading to cytopenias (anaemia, neutropenia, and thrombocytopenia).
  • In acute leukaemias, the rapid accumulation of blasts results in symptoms related to bone marrow failure and infiltration of tissues. Chronic leukaemias progress more slowly, with an initial phase of excessive production of mature but dysfunctional cells.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Leukaemia"][/sc]
  • Acute leukaemia presents rapidly over days to weeks with symptoms related to bone marrow failure and organ infiltration.
  • Chronic leukaemia progresses slowly, often over years, with an initial asymptomatic phase. Symptoms emerge as the disease accelerates or transforms.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Leukaemia"][/sc]
  • Ask about fatigue - Caused by anaemia due to reduced red blood cell production as the bone marrow is replaced by leukaemic blasts.
  • Ask about fever - Associated with infection due to neutropenia or the release of inflammatory cytokines from the malignant cells.
  • Ask about easy bruising or bleeding - Due to thrombocytopenia caused by insufficient platelet production.
  • Ask about bone pain - Caused by the expansion of the bone marrow as leukaemic cells proliferate.
  • Ask about unexplained weight loss - Common in malignancies due to increased metabolic activity and cytokine release.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Leukaemia"][/sc]
  • Check pallor - Indicative of anaemia due to reduced erythropoiesis.
  • Check hepatosplenomegaly - Caused by infiltration of leukaemic cells into the liver and spleen, particularly in acute leukaemias.
  • Check petechiae - Small pinpoint haemorrhages on the skin due to thrombocytopenia.
  • Check lymphadenopathy - More common in ALL or CLL, due to the accumulation of leukaemic cells in lymphoid tissues.
  • Check gingival hyperplasia - A rare sign, seen in AML due to infiltration of the gums by leukaemic blasts.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Leukaemia"][/sc]
  • Severe or sudden-onset bleeding - May indicate disseminated intravascular coagulation (DIC), a life-threatening complication in acute leukaemia.
  • Neurological symptoms - Suggests central nervous system (CNS) involvement, particularly in ALL, where leukaemic cells can infiltrate the meninges.
  • Unexplained weight loss with fever and night sweats - B symptoms, particularly suggestive of aggressive forms of leukaemia or transformation of chronic leukaemia into an acute form.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Leukaemia"][/sc]
  • Chronic leukaemia (CML, CLL) may present with nonspecific symptoms such as fatigue or be entirely asymptomatic, discovered incidentally on routine blood tests.
  • Acute leukaemia in the elderly - May present with vague constitutional symptoms like fatigue, making it easy to miss.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask leukaemia.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Leukaemia"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Full Blood Count (FBC) - Typically shows anaemia, thrombocytopenia, and either leucocytosis or leucopenia, depending on the stage of leukaemia. Blast.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Bone marrow biopsy - Demonstrates hypercellularity with a predominance of blasts (>20% in acute leukaemia); Cytogenetic analysis - Identifies chromosomal abnormalities.

  • An 8-year-old male presents with a 3-week history of fatigue, bruising, and recurrent infections. Full blood count reveals anaemia, thrombocytopenia, and leucocytosis with circulating blasts, suggesting acute lymphoblastic leukaemia (ALL).

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Leukaemia"][/sc]
  • Aplastic anaemia - Typically presents with pancytopenia but without the presence of blast cells on a blood.
  • Lymphoma - Distinguish from leukaemia using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.
  • Myelodysplastic syndromes - Present with cytopenias and dysplastic blood cells but do not typically progress as rapidly.
  • Related alternative diagnosis - Distinguish from leukaemia using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Leukaemia"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Assess severity, physiological stability, and immediate complications.
  • Core care: Deliver condition-specific management for leukaemia, treat underlying drivers, and coordinate appropriate specialist input.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Reassess symptom trajectory, examination findings, and objective markers to confirm response in leukaemia.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Escalate urgently for clinical deterioration, evolving red flags, or failure to improve in suspected leukaemia.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Leukaemia"][/sc]
  • Infection - A leading cause of morbidity and mortality in leukaemia due to neutropenia and immunosuppression.
  • Tumour lysis syndrome - Results from rapid destruction of malignant cells during treatment, leading to electrolyte disturbances and renal failure.
  • CNS infiltration - More common in ALL, causing headaches, seizures, or focal neurological deficits.
