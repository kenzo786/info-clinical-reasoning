Lymphadenopathy
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Lymphadenopathy"][/sc]
  • Lymphadenopathy refers to the abnormal enlargement of lymph nodes, which can occur due to infection, inflammation, malignancy, or other immune-related processes.

Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Lymphadenopathy"][/sc]
  • Enlargement of lymph nodes occurs due to immune cell proliferation, infiltration by malignant cells, or increased cellular activity in response to infection or inflammation. During an immune response, lymphocytes and macrophages proliferate within the lymph nodes, leading to swelling. In malignancies like lymphoma or metastases, cancerous cells infiltrate and replace normal lymphatic tissue.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Lymphadenopathy"][/sc]
  • Acute: Rapid onset, typically associated with infections (e.g., bacterial or viral). Lymph nodes are tender and may resolve after the infection subsides.
  • Subacute: Gradual enlargement over weeks, often seen in chronic infections or inflammatory conditions.
  • Chronic: Lymphadenopathy persisting for months, often linked to malignancies or autoimmune diseases.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Lymphadenopathy"][/sc]
  • Ask about painless swelling of lymph nodes - Common in malignancies (e.g., lymphoma) due to tumour cell infiltration causing gradual, non-inflammatory enlargement.
  • Ask about tender lymph nodes - Typically associated with infections as a result of local inflammation and immune cell proliferation.
  • Ask about fever - Occurs in infections or systemic inflammatory conditions due to the release of pyrogens from immune cells.
  • Ask about night sweats - Seen in lymphoma, related to systemic immune responses to malignant cells.
  • Ask about weight loss - Common in malignancies or chronic infections due to increased metabolic demands.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Lymphadenopathy"][/sc]
  • Check enlarged lymph nodes (>1 cm) - The most common sign, reflecting underlying immune or malignant processes.
  • Check firm, rubbery texture - Suggestive of malignancy, such as lymphoma, due to the infiltration of abnormal cells into the lymph node.
  • Check tenderness to palpation - Indicates inflammatory causes, such as infection, where the nodes are acutely inflamed.
  • Check redness or warmth over lymph node - Suggests acute bacterial infection, often in cases of lymphadenitis.
  • Check splenomegaly - Seen in systemic infections (e.g., EBV) or haematologic malignancies due to widespread immune activity or infiltration of abnormal cells.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Lymphadenopathy"][/sc]
  • Non-tender, hard lymph nodes - Can signify metastatic cancer, where tumour cells invade lymph nodes and cause nodal fibrosis without inflammation.
  • Supraclavicular lymphadenopathy - Often associated with malignancy, particularly abdominal or thoracic cancers, as lymphatic drainage from these areas leads to nodal involvement.
  • Unexplained weight loss, night sweats, and fever ("B symptoms") - These are systemic symptoms of lymphoma, indicating an aggressive or advanced disease stage.
  • Lymphadenopathy lasting more than 4-6 weeks - Persistent lymph node enlargement raises suspicion of malignancy or chronic infection.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Lymphadenopathy"][/sc]
  • Generalised lymphadenopathy without localised symptoms - Can be missed as a sign of systemic diseases like HIV or haematologic malignancies, which may present insidiously.
  • Lymphadenopathy in children - Reactive lymphadenopathy is common and often overlooked, but underlying infections or rare malignancies (e.g., leukaemia) should be considered.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Lymphadenopathy"][/sc]

  • Hodgkin lymphoma - Usually presents with painless, firm lymphadenopathy and "B symptoms". Diagnosis is confirmed via lymph.
  • Metastatic cancer - Distinguish from lymphadenopathy using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.
  • HIV infection - Generalised lymphadenopathy in multiple regions, along with systemic symptoms. Diagnosis is confirmed via serology.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Lymphadenopathy"][/sc]
  • Compression of adjacent structures - Enlarged lymph nodes can compress airways, blood vessels, or nerves, leading to symptoms such as dyspnoea or neurological deficits.
  • Sepsis from untreated infections - Bacterial lymphadenitis may progress to systemic infection if not adequately managed.
  • Transformation to aggressive lymphoma - In cases of indolent lymphomas, there is a risk of transformation to more aggressive subtypes, which worsens prognosis.
