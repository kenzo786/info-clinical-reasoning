Conjunctivitis
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Conjunctivitis"][/sc]
  • Conjunctivitis, commonly known as "pink eye," refers to inflammation of the conjunctiva, the thin, transparent membrane covering the sclera and inner surface of the eyelids.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise conjunctivitis features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Conjunctivitis"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Conjunctivitis"][/sc]
  • It is less commonly a reason for hospital admission unless complications occur.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Conjunctivitis"][/sc]
  • Viral: Often caused by adenoviruses, leading to epithelial cell destruction and an inflammatory response.
  • Bacterial: Streptococcus pneumoniae, Staphylococcus aureus, or Haemophilus species invade the conjunctiva, resulting in purulent discharge and erythema.
  • Allergic: Hypersensitivity reactions result in mast cell degranulation and histamine release, causing itching and swelling.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Conjunctivitis"][/sc]
  • Viral conjunctivitis typically resolves within 7–10 days without treatment.
  • Bacterial conjunctivitis improves within 3–5 days with appropriate antibiotics but may persist up to 2 weeks without treatment.
  • Allergic conjunctivitis can be episodic or chronic, depending on allergen exposure.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Conjunctivitis"][/sc]
  • Ask about redness - Due to vasodilation of conjunctival vessels from inflammation.
  • Ask about tearing - Stimulation of the lacrimal gland in response to irritation or infection.
  • Ask about itching - Common in allergic conjunctivitis due to histamine release.
  • Ask about discharge - Watery in viral, purulent in bacterial, and stringy in allergic conjunctivitis.
  • Ask about foreign body sensation - Resulting from inflammation of the conjunctiva.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Conjunctivitis"][/sc]
  • Check conjunctival injection - Reflects hyperaemia due to inflammation.
  • Check oedema (chemosis) - Results from increased vascular permeability.
  • Check discharge - Indicates the type of conjunctivitis (e.g., purulent in bacterial).
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Conjunctivitis"][/sc]
  • Severe pain - May indicate keratitis or scleritis.
  • Photophobia - Suggests deeper eye involvement such as iritis.
  • Visual loss - Could signify corneal involvement or more severe complications.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Conjunctivitis"][/sc]
  • Unilateral onset - Can be seen in herpetic or toxic conjunctivitis.
  • Chronic conjunctivitis - May suggest Chlamydia trachomatis or ocular rosacea.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features or comorbidity can mask the underlying diagnosis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Conjunctivitis"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Slit lamp examination - To assess corneal involvement.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Conjunctival swab - For bacterial culture and sensitivity in refractory cases; Allergy testing - To confirm allergic conjunctivitis.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Conjunctivitis"][/sc]
  • A 25-year-old male presents with 3 days of bilateral eye redness, watery discharge, and mild itching, with no significant pain or visual changes.
  • Adult with symptom pattern and objective findings suggestive of conjunctivitis, requiring focused confirmation and risk stratification.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Conjunctivitis"][/sc]
  • Blepharitis - Involves eyelid margin inflammation, often with crusting.
  • Keratitis - Features pain, photophobia, and visual impairment.
  • Uveitis - Associated with photophobia and perilimbal injection.
  • Alternative serious diagnosis - Distinguishing history, examination, and targeted investigations do not support conjunctivitis.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Conjunctivitis"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Assess physiological stability and urgent complications at presentation, and initiate early stabilisation where required.
  • Core care: Deliver condition-specific treatment for conjunctivitis, address underlying drivers, and involve appropriate specialist support.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Reassess symptom trajectory, objective markers, and treatment response over time to detect complications early.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Worsening symptoms, physiological deterioration, or failure to improve should trigger urgent reassessment for severe or alternative pathology in conjunctivitis.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Conjunctivitis"][/sc]
  • Keratitis - Can lead to corneal scarring if untreated.
  • Orbital cellulitis - Rare but severe, requiring systemic antibiotics.
  • Chronic conjunctivitis - May cause conjunctival scarring.
