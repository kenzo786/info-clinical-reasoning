Chronic Pancreatitis
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Chronic Pancreatitis"][/sc]
  • Chronic pancreatitis (CP) is a progressive inflammatory disease of the pancreas that leads to irreversible damage, resulting in fibrosis and loss of both exocrine and endocrine function.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise pancreatitis features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Chronic Pancreatitis"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Chronic Pancreatitis"][/sc]
  • CP is more common in men than women and typically presents between the ages of 30-50 years.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Chronic Pancreatitis"][/sc]
  • Chronic pancreatitis develops due to recurrent inflammation that leads to pancreatic fibrosis. This is driven by mechanisms such as oxidative stress, ductal obstruction, and necrosis-fibrosis sequences. Over time, the destruction of acinar cells reduces enzyme production, resulting in exocrine insufficiency and malabsorption. Damage to the islet cells may also occur, leading to diabetes mellitus.
  • Key factors include alcohol consumption (a major cause), genetic predispositions (e.g., mutations in CFTR or PRSS1), and autoimmune conditions. The progressive fibrosis and atrophy of the pancreas result in irreversible damage and loss of function.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Chronic Pancreatitis"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Recurrent episodes of abdominal pain with normal pancreatic function.
  • Mid phase: Persistent pain with gradual onset of exocrine insufficiency, manifesting as steatorrhoea and weight loss.
  • Late phase: Chronic pain or pain-free stage with severe exocrine and endocrine insufficiency (diabetes).
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Chronic Pancreatitis"][/sc]
  • Ask about abdominal pain – Recurrent or chronic epigastric pain radiating to the back, often worsened by eating. This is due to inflammation, fibrosis, and pancreatic.
  • Ask about steatorrhoea – Fatty, foul-smelling stools due to pancreatic exocrine insufficiency and malabsorption of fat.
  • Ask about weight loss – Caused by malabsorption and dietary restriction to avoid pain episodes.
  • Ask about diabetes mellitus – Loss of endocrine function as the islets of Langerhans are progressively destroyed.
  • Ask about nausea and vomiting – May occur due to severe pain or mechanical obstruction of the duodenum by pseudocysts.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Chronic Pancreatitis"][/sc]
  • Check epigastric tenderness – Common during painful flare-ups due to local inflammation and fibrosis.
  • Check malnutrition – Wasting and signs of fat-soluble vitamin deficiencies (e.g., dry skin, night blindness) from malabsorption.
  • Check pseudocyst formation – Palpable abdominal mass or fullness due to fluid-filled collections.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Chronic Pancreatitis"][/sc]
  • Unexplained weight loss – May indicate malignancy or severe malabsorption.
  • New-onset diabetes – A sudden diagnosis of diabetes in someone with chronic pancreatitis raises suspicion of pancreatic cancer.
  • Jaundice – Suggests biliary obstruction, possibly due to a pseudocyst or pancreatic cancer.
  • Persistent vomiting – May indicate gastric outlet obstruction from a large pseudocyst or mass effect.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Chronic Pancreatitis"][/sc]
  • Painless chronic pancreatitis – Some patients, particularly those with advanced disease, may present without significant pain but with exocrine insufficiency (steatorrhoea, weight loss).
  • Isolated diabetes – In rare cases, diabetes may be the presenting feature, especially in older adults.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features or comorbidity can mask the underlying diagnosis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Chronic Pancreatitis"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Faecal elastase – Low levels (<200 µg/g) indicate exocrine pancreatic insufficiency; CT scan or MRI – May reveal pancreatic calcifications, ductal.
  • Targeted and monitoring: MRCP (Magnetic Resonance Cholangiopancreatography) – Provides detailed images of pancreatic and bile ducts to assess for strictures, stones, or ductal changes.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Chronic Pancreatitis"][/sc]
  • Pancreatic cancer – Rapid weight loss, jaundice, and - distinguish by focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.
  • Gallstone disease – Often causes episodic pain, usually - distinguish by focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.
  • Peptic ulcer disease – Pain may be similar - distinguish by focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.
  • Alternative serious diagnosis - Distinguishing history, examination, and targeted investigations do not support chronic pancreatitis.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Chronic Pancreatitis"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Assess physiological stability and urgent complications at presentation, and initiate early stabilisation where required.
  • Core care: Deliver condition-specific treatment for chronic pancreatitis, address underlying drivers, and involve appropriate specialist support.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Reassess symptom trajectory, objective markers, and treatment response over time to detect complications early.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Worsening symptoms, physiological deterioration, or failure to improve should trigger urgent reassessment for severe or alternative pathology in chronic pancreatitis.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Chronic Pancreatitis"][/sc]
  • Pseudocyst formation – Occurs in up to 25% of cases due to fluid accumulation in areas of necrotic pancreatic tissue.
  • Pancreatic cancer – CP is a risk factor for pancreatic adenocarcinoma, particularly in those with hereditary pancreatitis.
  • Diabetes mellitus – Occurs in approximately 50% of patients due to destruction of islet cells.
  • Malnutrition – Due to prolonged malabsorption and decreased caloric intake.
