Rotator Cuff Disorder
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Rotator Cuff Disorder"][/sc]
  • Rotator cuff disorder refers to a spectrum of conditions involving damage or dysfunction of the rotator cuff tendons, most commonly due to degeneration, impingement, or acute trauma.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise rotator features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.

Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Rotator Cuff Disorder"][/sc]
  • Degeneration of collagen within the rotator cuff tendons due to age-related wear and tear or overuse.
  • Subacromial impingement occurs when the tendons are compressed between the acromion and humeral head during arm movement.
  • Acute trauma can lead to tears, especially in younger individuals during high-impact activities.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Rotator Cuff Disorder"][/sc]
  • Acute phase: Sudden onset of pain and dysfunction, often after trauma.
  • Chronic phase: Progressive worsening of symptoms over months to years due to degenerative changes or repetitive strain.
  • Recovery phase: Symptoms may stabilise or improve with treatment, including physiotherapy or surgical repair.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Rotator Cuff Disorder"][/sc]
  • Ask about shoulder pain - Typically worse with overhead activities or at night. Pain arises from inflammation, tendon impingement, or partial tears.
  • Ask about weakness - Difficulty lifting or rotating the arm, due to tendon dysfunction or tears.
  • Ask about stiffness - Can result from compensatory reduced shoulder movement over time.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Rotator Cuff Disorder"][/sc]
  • Check reduced range of motion - Particularly during abduction or rotation; may indicate impingement or a tear.
  • Check tenderness - Localised tenderness over the greater tuberosity or subacromial area suggests inflammation or tendon damage.
  • Check positive impingement tests - Neer’s and Hawkins-Kennedy tests provoke pain, supporting a diagnosis of impingement syndrome.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Rotator Cuff Disorder"][/sc]
  • Severe, sudden weakness - May indicate a complete rotator cuff tear requiring urgent assessment.
  • Severe pain with swelling - Suggests potential fracture or infection.
  • Constitutional symptoms - Fever, weight loss, or night sweats could point to systemic conditions like malignancy or infection.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Rotator Cuff Disorder"][/sc]
  • Isolated stiffness - May occur in early degenerative changes, mimicking adhesive capsulitis.
  • Nocturnal pain only - Can suggest early subacromial impingement without overt mechanical symptoms.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask rotator cuff disorder.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Rotator Cuff Disorder"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Plain radiography - May show calcific deposits or acromial spurs in chronic cases.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Ultrasound - Useful for visualising tendon tears and impingement dynamically; MRI - Gold standard for detailed imaging of rotator cuff tears.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Rotator Cuff Disorder"][/sc]
  • Adhesive capsulitis - Stiffness predominates over weakness; lacks mechanical symptoms.
  • Glenohumeral osteoarthritis - Chronic pain with crepitus, typically seen on imaging.
  • Cervical radiculopathy - Pain radiates down the arm, associated with neurological deficits.
  • Related alternative diagnosis - Distinguish from rotator cuff disorder using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Rotator Cuff Disorder"][/sc]
  • Chronic pain - May result from untreated impingement or progressive degeneration.
  • Frozen shoulder - Secondary to prolonged disuse and inflammation.
  • Complete tendon rupture - Can lead to permanent loss of function if untreated.
