Vitreous Haemorrhage
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Vitreous Haemorrhage"][/sc]
  • Vitreous haemorrhage refers to bleeding into the vitreous humour of the eye, the clear gel that fills the space between the lens and retina.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise vitreous features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Vitreous Haemorrhage"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Vitreous Haemorrhage"][/sc]
  • UK General Practice: Relatively rare, often presenting in patients with diabetes or trauma history.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Vitreous Haemorrhage"][/sc]
  • Disruption of retinal blood vessels leads to leakage of blood into the vitreous cavity.
  • Common causes include proliferative vascular diseases (e.g., diabetic retinopathy), trauma, or retinal tears.
  • The blood obstructs light reaching the retina, impairing vision.
  • Chronic haemorrhage may lead to fibrosis, tractional retinal detachment, or iron toxicity to the retina.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Vitreous Haemorrhage"][/sc]
  • Acute: Sudden onset of visual symptoms, often related to trauma or a spontaneous event like a retinal tear.
  • Subacute: Progressive clearing of haemorrhage over weeks to months.
  • Chronic: Persistent visual impairment due to complications like fibrosis or tractional retinal detachment.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Vitreous Haemorrhage"][/sc]
  • Ask about sudden painless loss of vision - Occurs due to obstruction of light by blood within the vitreous.
  • Ask about floaters - Represent clumps of blood cells within the vitreous casting shadows on the retina.
  • Ask about blurry vision - Results from light scattering by haemorrhagic material.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Vitreous Haemorrhage"][/sc]
  • Check decreased visual acuity - Related to severity and density of haemorrhage.
  • Check reduced red reflex - Due to the obstruction of the reflective pathway by blood.
  • Check presence of retinal or vitreous haemorrhage on fundoscopic examination - Confirms diagnosis and suggests an underlying cause.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Vitreous Haemorrhage"][/sc]
  • Associated flashes and floaters - May indicate retinal tear or detachment.
  • Sudden, severe visual loss - Suggests extensive haemorrhage or another sight-threatening condition.
  • Signs of trauma - Important for detecting underlying globe rupture or severe ocular injury.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Vitreous Haemorrhage"][/sc]
  • Gradual vision loss - May occur in chronic or recurrent cases, often misattributed to cataracts.
  • Minimal symptoms - In mild haemorrhage, especially in non-dominant eyes, patients may not notice significant impairment.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask vitreous haemorrhage.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Vitreous Haemorrhage"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Slit-lamp examination with dilated fundus exam - Reveals vitreous haemorrhage and possible underlying pathology.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Ultrasound (B-scan) - Used when fundus is obscured, detecting retinal detachment or tumours; Optical Coherence Tomography (OCT) - Assesses vitreoretinal interface.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Vitreous Haemorrhage"][/sc]
  • Retinal detachment - Associated with flashes and a "curtain" effect over vision.
  • Posterior vitreous detachment - Typically less severe, with floaters but preserved visual acuity.
  • Central retinal vein occlusion - Characterised by sudden vision loss and retinal haemorrhages on examination.
  • Related alternative diagnosis - Distinguish from vitreous haemorrhage using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Vitreous Haemorrhage"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Assess severity, physiological stability, and immediate complications.
  • Core care: Deliver condition-specific management for vitreous haemorrhage, treat underlying drivers, and coordinate appropriate specialist input.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Reassess symptom trajectory, examination findings, and objective markers to confirm response in vitreous haemorrhage.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Escalate urgently for clinical deterioration, evolving red flags, or failure to improve in suspected vitreous haemorrhage.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Vitreous Haemorrhage"][/sc]
  • Retinal detachment - A potentially sight-threatening complication requiring urgent intervention.
  • Persistent vitreous haemorrhage - May cause fibrosis and tractional detachment.
  • Vision loss - Permanent in some cases due to retinal damage or untreated detachment.
