Trigeminal Neuralgia
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Trigeminal Neuralgia"][/sc]
  • Trigeminal neuralgia (TN) is a chronic pain disorder characterised by recurrent episodes of severe, unilateral facial pain that follows the distribution of the trigeminal nerve (cranial nerve V).

Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Trigeminal Neuralgia"][/sc]
  • Compression of the trigeminal nerve root: The most common cause is compression by an aberrant blood vessel (usually the superior cerebellar artery), leading to demyelination of the nerve. This abnormal signalling results in hyperexcitability, leading to the intense pain characteristic of TN.
  • Secondary trigeminal neuralgia: Less commonly, TN can be secondary to conditions such as multiple sclerosis (due to demyelination within the brainstem), tumours, or vascular malformations that affect the trigeminal nerve.
  • Nerve sensitisation: In the long term, repeated episodes of pain can lead to central sensitisation, where the brain becomes hypersensitive to pain stimuli.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Trigeminal Neuralgia"][/sc]
  • Acute phase: Sudden onset of paroxysmal pain lasting seconds to minutes, often triggered by light touch or movement. May occur multiple times per day.
  • Chronic phase: Without treatment, episodes may increase in frequency and severity over time, leading to significant impairment in quality of life. Pain-free intervals may shorten, and the condition may become more persistent.

Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Trigeminal Neuralgia"][/sc]
  • Bilateral symptoms: Trigeminal neuralgia is typically unilateral. Bilateral symptoms may suggest multiple sclerosis or other systemic neurological conditions.
  • Ophthalmic branch involvement (V1): Isolated pain in the V1 branch (forehead and eye region) may indicate an alternative diagnosis, such as cluster headache or a central lesion like a brainstem tumour.
  • Neurological deficits: Weakness, sensory loss, or ataxia should raise suspicion of secondary causes such as tumours, multiple sclerosis, or vascular malformations.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Trigeminal Neuralgia"][/sc]
  • Atypical facial pain: Some patients may experience constant, dull, burning pain rather than the characteristic sharp, stabbing pain. This may be due to chronic sensitisation of the trigeminal nerve.
  • Pain without clear triggers: In some cases, patients report pain that is not linked to typical triggers, possibly reflecting a central sensitisation process.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask trigeminal neuralgia.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Trigeminal Neuralgia"][/sc]
  • Depression and anxiety: Chronic pain and the fear of triggering an attack can lead to significant psychological distress.
  • Medication side effects: Long-term use of carbamazepine can cause sedation, dizziness, and potential bone marrow suppression (requiring periodic monitoring).
  • Central sensitisation: Recurrent episodes of pain may lead to chronic sensitisation of pain pathways, making the condition harder to treat over time.
