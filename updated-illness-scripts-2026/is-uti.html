Urinary Tract Infection (UTI)
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Urinary Tract Infection (UTI)"][/sc]
  • A urinary tract infection (UTI) is an infection that affects part of the urinary system, which includes the kidneys, ureters, bladder, and urethra.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise urinary features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.

  • Higher-risk or missed groups: Urinary Tract Infection (UTI) may be missed when presentation is atypical or obscured by comorbidity.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Urinary Tract Infection (UTI)"][/sc]
  • UTIs typically occur when pathogens, usually bacteria from the gastrointestinal tract (like *E. coli*), ascend the urethra into the bladder. In some cases, they may ascend further to the kidneys, causing pyelonephritis. Disruption of normal urinary flow, poor immune response, or mechanical issues (such as catheter use) can predispose individuals to infection.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Urinary Tract Infection (UTI)"][/sc]
  • Acute phase: Symptoms of UTI, such as dysuria, frequency, and urgency, usually develop within a few days.
  • Resolution: With appropriate treatment, symptoms typically resolve within 48-72 hours.
  • Chronic or recurrent phase: Some patients, particularly those with underlying conditions, may experience recurrent UTIs over months or years.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Urinary Tract Infection (UTI)"][/sc]
  • Ask about dysuria (painful urination) - Caused by irritation of the urethral and bladder lining due to inflammation.
  • Ask about increased urinary frequency - Inflammation of the bladder leads to heightened sensation of needing to void.
  • Ask about urgency - A sudden, intense need to urinate due to bladder irritation.
  • Ask about suprapubic pain - Occurs due to bladder inflammation and distension.
  • Ask about haematuria - Blood in the urine, often due to irritation of the bladder lining.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Urinary Tract Infection (UTI)"][/sc]
  • Check suprapubic tenderness - Bladder distension and inflammation can cause tenderness in the lower abdomen.
  • Check fever - While uncommon in uncomplicated UTIs, it can occur, particularly in cases of pyelonephritis (upper urinary tract infection).
  • Check costovertebral angle tenderness - Indicates pyelonephritis, due to inflammation and infection of the kidney.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Urinary Tract Infection (UTI)"][/sc]
  • Fever, rigors - May indicate upper urinary tract involvement (pyelonephritis) or sepsis.
  • Flank pain - Suggests pyelonephritis or renal involvement.
  • Hypotension, tachycardia - Signs of urosepsis, a life-threatening complication where infection spreads to the bloodstream.
  • Confusion or altered mental status - Common in elderly patients, indicating systemic involvement, often associated with urosepsis.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Urinary Tract Infection (UTI)"][/sc]
  • Elderly patients: May present with confusion, lethargy, or falls instead of classic urinary symptoms.
  • Pregnancy: Often asymptomatic, but if symptomatic, the presentation may be more severe due to anatomical and physiological changes.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask urinary tract infection (uti).
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Urinary Tract Infection (UTI)"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Urinalysis: Presence of nitrites, leucocytes, and blood suggests infection. Nitrites indicate bacterial conversion of nitrates, typically by *E. coli*; Urine culture.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Full blood count (FBC): May show raised white cell count in cases of systemic infection or pyelonephritis; Blood cultures: Should be.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Urinary Tract Infection (UTI)"][/sc]
  • Vaginitis - Often presents with vaginal discharge and irritation, but without urinary frequency or urgency.
  • Urethritis - Similar symptoms but commonly associated with sexually transmitted infections. Discharge may be present.
  • Interstitial cystitis - Presents with chronic pelvic pain and urinary frequency but lacks infectious findings in urine.
  • Pelvic inflammatory disease (PID) - Characterised by pelvic pain, fever, and vaginal discharge, often following a sexually transmitted infection.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Urinary Tract Infection (UTI)"][/sc]
  • Pyelonephritis: Infection ascends to the kidneys, causing fever, flank pain, and systemic symptoms.
  • Urosepsis: Sepsis originating from the urinary tract can lead to multi-organ failure and is life-threatening.
  • Renal abscess: Rare but serious complication involving localised infection within the kidney tissue.
