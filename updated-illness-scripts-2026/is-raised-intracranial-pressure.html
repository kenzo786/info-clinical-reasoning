Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Raised Intracranial Pressure (ICP)"][/sc]
  • Increased pressure inside the skull occurs due to one or more of the following factors:
    • Brain tissue expansion – Can result from tumours, haemorrhage, or cerebral oedema.
    • Increased CSF – Conditions like hydrocephalus can increase CSF volume.
    • Increased blood volume – Venous outflow obstruction or intracerebral haemorrhage can elevate ICP.
  • As ICP rises, the brain is compressed, reducing cerebral perfusion. This can lead to ischaemia and, if unchecked, brain herniation.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Raised Intracranial Pressure (ICP)"][/sc]
  • Acute – Raised ICP can occur rapidly, often within minutes to hours, in cases of trauma or haemorrhage.
  • Subacute – It may develop over days to weeks in conditions like tumours or subdural haematomas.
  • Chronic – Slow onset, over months, as seen in idiopathic intracranial hypertension (IIH) or chronic hydrocephalus.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Raised Intracranial Pressure (ICP)"][/sc]
  • Ask about headache – A diffuse headache that worsens on waking, with coughing or Valsalva manoeuvres. Due to increased pressure on pain-sensitive structures in the brain.
  • Ask about nausea and vomiting – Often present with headaches, due to pressure on the medulla and the vomiting centre.
  • Ask about visual disturbances – Blurred vision or transient visual obscurations, often related to papilloedema compressing the optic nerve.
  • Ask about altered consciousness – Confusion or drowsiness, due to impaired cerebral perfusion and brainstem dysfunction.
  • Ask about seizures – Due to abnormal cortical activity caused by compression of brain tissue.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Raised Intracranial Pressure (ICP)"][/sc]
  • Check papilloedema – Swelling of the optic disc due to increased ICP obstructing venous return from the retina.
  • Check cushing’s triad – Hypertension, bradycardia, and irregular respiration, a late sign of impending brain herniation due to brainstem compression.
  • Check sixth nerve palsy – Due to stretching of the abducens nerve by increased ICP, causing horizontal diplopia.
  • Check focal neurological deficits – Dependent on the location of the lesion, such as hemiparesis with motor cortex compression.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Raised Intracranial Pressure (ICP)"][/sc]
  • Sudden onset severe headache – May indicate an acute haemorrhage, such as a subarachnoid haemorrhage, which can raise ICP rapidly.
  • Rapidly progressing neurological symptoms – Such as decreased consciousness, suggests a life-threatening mass effect or herniation.
  • Cushing’s triad – Indicates imminent brain herniation, which is a medical emergency.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Raised Intracranial Pressure (ICP)"][/sc]
  • Chronic headache with intermittent symptoms – Seen in idiopathic intracranial hypertension (IIH), where ICP is elevated without clear acute triggers, and symptoms fluctuate over time.
  • Minimal headache but profound visual loss – May be seen in cases where the optic nerve is more severely affected than other structures.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask raised intracranial pressure (icp).
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Raised Intracranial Pressure (ICP)"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: CT head – Will show mass effect, midline shift, or hydrocephalus. Helps rule out causes like tumours or haemorrhage.
  • Targeted and monitoring: MRI brain – Provides more detailed imaging to identify subtle lesions or oedema; LP (Lumbar Puncture) – Not done if raised.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Raised Intracranial Pressure (ICP)"][/sc]
  • Brain herniation – The most feared complication, often leading to coma or death.
  • Permanent neurological deficits – If raised ICP is not corrected in time, prolonged pressure on the brain can cause irreversible damage.
  • Seizures – Due to cortical irritation from pressure or underlying structural lesions.
