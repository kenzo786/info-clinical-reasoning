Ingrown Toenail (IGTN)
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Ingrown Toenail (IGTN)"][/sc]
  • An ingrown toenail (IGTN) is a common condition where the edge of a toenail grows into the periungual skin, causing pain, inflammation, and sometimes infection.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise ingrown features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.

Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Ingrown Toenail (IGTN)"][/sc]
  • Initial stage: Nail edge exerts pressure on adjacent skin, disrupting its integrity.
  • Inflammatory response: Tissue responds with erythema, oedema, and pain due to irritation.
  • Infection phase: If untreated, bacterial invasion (often Staphylococcus aureus) leads to abscess formation and possible cellulitis.
  • Chronic stage: Prolonged irritation results in granulation tissue formation and hypertrophy of the nail fold.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Ingrown Toenail (IGTN)"][/sc]
  • Acute: Pain and redness develop over days due to trauma or improper nail cutting.
  • Subacute: Infection may supervene, with drainage or swelling noted within 1-2 weeks.
  • Chronic: Persistent symptoms with granulation tissue formation over months, often requiring surgical intervention.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Ingrown Toenail (IGTN)"][/sc]
  • Ask about pain: Localised to the affected nail, worsens with pressure due to inflammation.
  • Ask about redness: Indicates local vasodilation and immune response.
  • Ask about swelling: Oedema arises from inflammatory mediator release.
  • Ask about drainage of pus: Suggests secondary bacterial infection.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Ingrown Toenail (IGTN)"][/sc]
  • Check erythema: Reflects localised inflammation.
  • Check swollen nail fold: Indicates oedema and possible granulation tissue development.
  • Check purulent discharge: Confirms secondary infection.
  • Check granulation tissue: Sign of chronic irritation.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Ingrown Toenail (IGTN)"][/sc]
  • Spreading cellulitis: Suggests severe infection and possible systemic involvement.
  • Severe pain unresponsive to treatment: May indicate deeper infection or underlying conditions like osteomyelitis.
  • Signs of ischaemia: Such as pale or cold toes, may complicate healing in patients with diabetes or vascular disease.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Ingrown Toenail (IGTN)"][/sc]
  • Asymptomatic chronic stage: Granulation tissue forms without significant pain, often missed in elderly or diabetic patients.
  • Bilateral involvement: Uncommon, may indicate systemic causes or improper footwear.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Ingrown Toenail (IGTN)"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Clinical examination: Primary diagnostic method; look for erythema, oedema, and discharge.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Targeted investigations to confirm diagnosis, assess complications, and monitor response in ingrown toenail (igtn).

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Ingrown Toenail (IGTN)"][/sc]
  • Paronychia - Differentiated by its localisation to the nail fold without nail edge involvement.
  • Onychomycosis - Chronic discolouration and thickening of the nail, typically painless.
  • Traumatic toe injury - Often associated with a clear history of trauma and absence of granulation tissue.
  • Related alternative diagnosis - Distinguish from ingrown toenail (igtn) using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Ingrown Toenail (IGTN)"][/sc]
  • Cellulitis: Common if infection spreads to surrounding tissue.
  • Osteomyelitis: Rare but serious, occurs in chronic or untreated cases.
  • Cosmetic issues: Permanent nail deformities after repeated surgery or infection.
