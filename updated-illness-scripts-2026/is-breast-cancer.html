Breast Cancer
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Breast Cancer"][/sc]
  • Breast cancer is a malignant neoplasm originating in the breast tissue, most commonly from the epithelial cells lining the ducts (ductal carcinoma) or lobules (lobular carcinoma).
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise breast features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Breast Cancer"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Breast Cancer"][/sc]
  • GP Population: Early-stage cases often first present to primary care with breast changes or a palpable lump.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Breast Cancer"][/sc]
  • Breast cancer develops from genetic mutations (e.g., BRCA1/BRCA2) and other factors that cause uncontrolled cellular proliferation in breast tissue.
  • Cells may become pre-cancerous, progressing to in-situ carcinoma, then to invasive cancer when malignant cells breach the ductal or lobular basement membrane.
  • Metastasis occurs via lymphatic or haematogenous spread, commonly affecting axillary lymph nodes initially and potentially reaching bones, liver, lungs, and brain.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Breast Cancer"][/sc]
  • Early Stage: Often asymptomatic; detection occurs through screening or incidental findings.
  • Localised Disease: Symptoms (e.g., lumps, nipple discharge) develop as the tumour grows, typically over months to years.
  • Advanced Stage: Signs of metastasis (e.g., bone pain, jaundice) may emerge if the cancer spreads, generally over years.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Breast Cancer"][/sc]
  • Ask about breast Lump - The most common presenting symptom, resulting from tumour growth within breast tissue, which may feel firm and immobile.
  • Ask about nipple Discharge - Often associated with ductal involvement; can be serous, bloody, or purulent.
  • Ask about breast Pain - Less common; may arise from tumour infiltration or inflammation, although most breast cancers are painless.
  • Ask about skin Changes - Dimpling, puckering, or erythema may occur due to lymphatic obstruction or invasion of subcutaneous tissues.
  • Ask about axillary Fullness or Swelling - Represents regional lymph node involvement, a sign of metastasis.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Breast Cancer"][/sc]
  • Check palpable Lump - A discrete, firm, and often irregularly shaped mass within the breast, typically painless.
  • Check skin Retraction or Dimpling - Results from tumour adhesion to ligaments or invasion into skin layers.
  • Check peau d'Orange - Skin texture resembling an orange peel due to lymphatic blockage, commonly seen in inflammatory breast cancer.
  • Check nipple Retraction - Occurs if the tumour pulls on surrounding breast tissue or ducts.
  • Check axillary Lymphadenopathy - Enlarged, often firm nodes palpable in the axilla due to regional metastasis.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Breast Cancer"][/sc]
  • Unexplained Breast Lump in a Woman >30 Years - Persistent lumps in this demographic are suspicious for malignancy and require urgent assessment.
  • Nipple Retraction and Bloody Discharge - Suggests invasive disease involving the ducts and potential metastatic spread.
  • Peau d'Orange or Rapid Breast Swelling - Indicative of inflammatory breast cancer, a particularly aggressive subtype.
  • Bone Pain or Jaundice in Known Breast Cancer Patient - Potential signs of metastasis to the skeleton or liver.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Breast Cancer"][/sc]
  • Asymptomatic - Breast cancer is often detected incidentally through screening in asymptomatic individuals.
  • Pain as the Primary Symptom - Less common presentation; pain alone can lead to underestimation of malignancy.
  • Male Breast Cancer - Presents similarly but may be misattributed to benign conditions like gynaecomastia, leading to delays in diagnosis.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features or comorbidity can mask the underlying diagnosis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Breast Cancer"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Mammography - Identifies mass lesions or microcalcifications. Suspect malignancy with spiculated masses or clusters of microcalcifications; Ultrasound - Useful for differentiating.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Core Needle Biopsy - Confirms diagnosis by histology, allowing assessment of receptor status (ER, PR, HER2); MRI - Used in complex.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Breast Cancer"][/sc]
  • A 55-year-old woman presents with a 3-month history of a painless, firm breast lump, skin dimpling, and recent nipple retraction.
  • Adult with symptom pattern and objective findings suggestive of breast cancer, requiring focused confirmation and risk stratification.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Breast Cancer"][/sc]
  • Fibroadenoma - Common in younger women, usually a mobile, painless lump without skin changes.
  • Breast Cyst - Tends to be fluctuant, may be tender, and is usually smooth, unlike cancer’s irregular.
  • Mastitis/Abscess - More often painful, with erythema and warmth; may have systemic symptoms (fever).
  • Fat Necrosis - Can mimic cancer radiographically but often follows trauma.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Breast Cancer"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Assess physiological stability and urgent complications at presentation, and initiate early stabilisation where required.
  • Core care: Deliver condition-specific treatment for breast cancer, address underlying drivers, and involve appropriate specialist support.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Reassess symptom trajectory, objective markers, and treatment response over time to detect complications early.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Worsening symptoms, physiological deterioration, or failure to improve should trigger urgent reassessment for severe or alternative pathology in breast cancer.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Breast Cancer"][/sc]
  • Metastasis - Commonly to bones, liver, lungs, and brain, leading to significant morbidity.
  • Lymphoedema - Secondary to lymph node dissection or radiotherapy, leading to chronic swelling in the arm.
  • Recurrence - Local or distant recurrence can occur even after treatment; regular follow-up is essential.
  • Psychological Impact - Includes anxiety, depression, and body image issues due to treatment effects.
