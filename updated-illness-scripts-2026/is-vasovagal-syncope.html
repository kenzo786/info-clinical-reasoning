Vasovagal Syncope
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Vasovagal Syncope"][/sc]
  • Vasovagal syncope, also known as neurocardiogenic syncope, is a common cause of fainting triggered by a reflex response that causes a sudden drop in heart rate (bradycardia) and blood pressure.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise vasovagal features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.

Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Vasovagal Syncope"][/sc]
  • The underlying mechanism of vasovagal syncope involves a reflex triggered by emotional or physical stress. This reflex, via the autonomic nervous system, causes excessive parasympathetic (vagal) activity and reduced sympathetic tone.
  • This leads to vasodilation (decreased vascular resistance) and/or bradycardia, reducing cardiac output and blood pressure, causing transient global cerebral hypoperfusion, and resulting in syncope.
  • Typically, this is a self-limiting condition with rapid recovery of consciousness once lying down, restoring cerebral blood flow.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Vasovagal Syncope"][/sc]
  • Prodrome: Typically lasts seconds to minutes, with symptoms such as light-headedness, sweating, or nausea.
  • Syncopal episode: Lasts a few seconds to a minute, marked by loss of consciousness.
  • Postdrome: May include brief confusion or fatigue, but full recovery typically occurs within minutes.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Vasovagal Syncope"][/sc]
  • Ask about light-headedness - A feeling of dizziness or faintness due to decreased cerebral perfusion during the prodromal phase.
  • Ask about nausea - Often occurs as a result of autonomic activation (particularly vagal stimulation).
  • Ask about sweating - Diaphoresis due to autonomic activation as part of the reflex response.
  • Ask about blurring or tunnelling of vision - Visual symptoms result from diminished cerebral blood flow, particularly to the occipital lobe.
  • Ask about loss of consciousness - Occurs when cerebral perfusion falls below a critical threshold, usually lasting for seconds.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Vasovagal Syncope"][/sc]
  • Check pallor - A common finding due to peripheral vasodilation and reduced cardiac output.
  • Check bradycardia - Slow heart rate is caused by excessive parasympathetic stimulation.
  • Check hypotension - Low blood pressure due to vasodilation and reduced vascular resistance.
  • Check rapid recovery upon lying down - Restores cerebral perfusion, leading to prompt recovery of consciousness.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Vasovagal Syncope"][/sc]
  • Syncope during exertion - May suggest a more serious cardiac cause, such as arrhythmia or structural heart disease.
  • Family history of sudden cardiac death - Raises suspicion of genetic conditions like long QT syndrome or hypertrophic cardiomyopathy.
  • Prolonged recovery time - Longer than typical post-syncopal confusion or delay in regaining consciousness could indicate neurological conditions such as seizures.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Vasovagal Syncope"][/sc]
  • No prodromal symptoms - Some patients may faint abruptly without warning, complicating diagnosis.
  • Prolonged syncope - Although rare, prolonged episodes might raise suspicion of other causes, including seizures or hypoglycaemia.
  • Recurrent episodes in elderly patients - More difficult to attribute solely to vasovagal syncope, requiring evaluation for other causes like orthostatic hypotension or cardiac arrhythmias.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask vasovagal syncope.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Vasovagal Syncope"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Orthostatic vital signs - To rule out orthostatic hypotension, a significant drop in blood pressure on standing may suggest other causes.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Vasovagal Syncope"][/sc]
  • Cardiac syncope - Sudden onset without prodrome, often during exertion, requires ECG and echocardiogram to rule out.
  • Seizures - Typically associated with postictal confusion and tonic-clonic movements, differentiate using EEG.
  • Orthostatic hypotension - Syncope triggered by standing with sustained hypotension; differentiate using orthostatic vital signs.
  • Hypoglycaemia - May present with confusion, tremors, and sweating; confirm with blood glucose levels.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Vasovagal Syncope"][/sc]
  • Physical injury from falls - The most common complication, including head trauma or fractures from abrupt syncope episodes.
  • Psychosocial impact - Anxiety about recurrent episodes may lead to avoidance of social or occupational situations.
  • Rare complications - In extreme cases, prolonged asystole or cardiac arrest may occur, but these are exceedingly rare in vasovagal syncope.
