Appendicitis
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Appendicitis"][/sc]
  • Appendicitis is an acute inflammation of the vermiform appendix, commonly caused by luminal obstruction.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise appendicitis features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Appendicitis"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Appendicitis"][/sc]
  • Risk factors: Risk of appendicitis rises with relevant predisposition, comorbidity, and exposure profile.
  • Higher-risk or missed groups: Appendicitis may be missed when presentations are atypical or comorbidity obscures key features.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Appendicitis"][/sc]
  • Obstruction of the Appendix: The initial trigger is often blockage of the appendix lumen by faecaliths, lymphoid hyperplasia, or other debris.
  • Increased Intraluminal Pressure: Obstruction causes mucus and fluid accumulation, leading to rising pressure within the appendix.
  • Ischaemia and Bacterial Overgrowth: Rising pressure compromises blood flow, resulting in ischaemia and promoting bacterial invasion and inflammation.
  • Necrosis and Perforation: Without treatment, ischaemic necrosis can lead to perforation, spilling infectious material into the peritoneum and causing peritonitis.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Appendicitis"][/sc]
  • Initial Phase (0-12 hours): Vague, poorly localised periumbilical pain due to referred visceral pain from appendix inflammation.
  • Classic Progression (12-24 hours): Pain migrates to the right iliac fossa (McBurney’s point), becoming sharper due to parietal peritoneal irritation.
  • Complications Phase (24+ hours if untreated): Perforation risk increases, potentially leading to peritonitis or abscess formation.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Appendicitis"][/sc]
  • Ask about periumbilical Pain: Caused by visceral stretching of the appendix, leading to referred pain near the umbilicus due to shared sensory nerves.
  • Ask about right Lower Quadrant Pain: Pain migrates to the right iliac fossa as inflammation extends to the parietal peritoneum, localising the pain.
  • Ask about anorexia: Mediated by inflammatory cytokines affecting the hypothalamus, leading to reduced appetite.
  • Ask about nausea and Vomiting: Likely due to visceral stimulation and the body’s inflammatory response.
  • Ask about low-grade Fever: Reflects systemic inflammation; high fever suggests potential perforation or abscess formation.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Appendicitis"][/sc]
  • Check tenderness at McBurney’s Point: Localised peritoneal irritation causes pain on palpation in the right iliac fossa.
  • Check rebound Tenderness: Indicates peritoneal irritation; sudden release of pressure increases pain.
  • Check guarding: Reflex contraction of abdominal muscles due to parietal peritoneal irritation.
  • Check rovsing’s Sign: Pain in the right lower quadrant when palpating the left side, suggesting local peritoneal inflammation.
  • Check psoas Sign: Pain with right thigh extension, indicating retrocaecal appendix irritation involving the psoas muscle.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Appendicitis"][/sc]
  • High Fever (>38.5°C): May indicate abscess formation or generalised peritonitis.
  • Generalised Abdominal Pain: Suggests possible perforation with resulting peritonitis.
  • Rigidity: Diffuse, involuntary muscle guarding across the abdomen, indicating peritonitis.
  • Tachycardia and Hypotension: Signs of sepsis, which can develop with perforation and infection spread.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Appendicitis"][/sc]
  • Retrocaecal Appendix: May present with flank or back pain instead of typical right iliac fossa pain.
  • Pelvic Appendix: Can cause suprapubic pain, dysuria, or tenesmus due to proximity to the bladder and rectum.
  • Elderly Patients: Less pronounced pain and signs; higher risk of perforation due to delayed diagnosis.
  • Children: Non-specific symptoms like irritability, poor appetite, and diffuse abdominal pain, which can delay diagnosis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Appendicitis"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Full Blood Count (FBC): Raised white cell count (WCC) and neutrophilia indicate an inflammatory response; C-reactive Protein (CRP): Elevated levels suggest.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Targeted investigations to confirm diagnosis, assess complications, and monitor response in appendicitis.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Appendicitis"][/sc]
  • A 22-year-old male with 18 hours of worsening periumbilical pain, migrating to the right iliac fossa, associated with anorexia and low-grade fever.
  • Adult with symptom pattern and objective findings suggestive of appendicitis, requiring focused confirmation and risk stratification.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Appendicitis"][/sc]
  • Gastroenteritis - Diffuse, non-localising pain with diarrhoea; lacks localised tenderness at McBurney's point.
  • Mesenteric Adenitis - Right lower quadrant pain in children but often with viral prodrome; lacks rebound tenderness.
  • Ovarian Torsion - Acute lower abdominal pain in females; ultrasound confirms with Doppler showing lack of ovarian.
  • Renal Colic - Flank pain radiating to the groin; urinalysis shows haematuria.
  • Diverticulitis - Left lower quadrant pain more common in older adults; confirmed via imaging with inflammation.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Appendicitis"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Assess physiological stability and urgent complications at presentation, and initiate early stabilisation where required.
  • Core care: Deliver condition-specific treatment for appendicitis, address underlying drivers, and involve appropriate specialist support.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Reassess symptom trajectory, objective markers, and treatment response over time to detect complications early.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Worsening symptoms, physiological deterioration, or failure to improve should trigger urgent reassessment for severe or alternative pathology in appendicitis.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Appendicitis"][/sc]
  • Perforation: Occurs in 20-30% of untreated cases; risk rises after 24 hours, leading to peritonitis or abscess formation.
  • Abscess Formation: Localised collection of pus, more common in delayed or conservatively managed cases.
  • Peritonitis: Generalised infection of the peritoneal cavity, can lead to sepsis and multi-organ failure if untreated.
  • Sepsis: Systemic inflammatory response due to perforation, requiring aggressive resuscitation and intravenous antibiotics.
