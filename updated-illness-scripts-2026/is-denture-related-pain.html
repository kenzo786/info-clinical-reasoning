Denture-Related Pain
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Denture-Related Pain"][/sc]
  • Denture-related pain is discomfort or soreness associated with the wearing of removable dentures.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise denture features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.

Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Denture-Related Pain"][/sc]
  • Denture-related pain usually arises from mechanical irritation due to the uneven distribution of pressure or movement of the denture. This can cause mucosal trauma, leading to inflammation, ulcers, or hyperplasia. In cases of ill-fitting dentures, these forces are exacerbated, creating sore spots. Poor oral hygiene can lead to microbial colonisation, particularly fungal infections like denture stomatitis. In rare cases, allergic reactions to materials such as acrylic or metals can also cause pain and mucosal irritation.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Denture-Related Pain"][/sc]
  • Acute Phase: Pain may begin shortly after wearing new dentures, especially within the first few days. It can also occur after prolonged wear or changes in oral anatomy.
  • Chronic Phase: Persistent pain may develop over weeks or months due to continuous irritation, poor denture hygiene, or fungal infections. Symptoms typically worsen without intervention.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Denture-Related Pain"][/sc]
  • Ask about pain or soreness - The primary symptom, usually in areas where the denture exerts pressure or rubs. This is due to mucosal irritation, with associated clinical features.
  • Ask about difficulty chewing - Resulting from pain or the instability of poorly fitting dentures.
  • Ask about swelling of the gums - Inflammation can lead to oedema in the mucosal tissues under the denture, particularly in cases of chronic irritation.
  • Ask about oral ulcers - Localised mucosal ulceration may occur from mechanical trauma due to an ill-fitting denture.
  • Ask about bad taste or odour - Often associated with poor oral hygiene, denture stomatitis, or fungal infections.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Denture-Related Pain"][/sc]
  • Check mucosal erythema - Redness of the mucosal tissues under the denture due to inflammation.
  • Check ulceration - Visible sores or lesions on the gums or palate where the denture irritates the tissue.
  • Check swelling of the soft tissue - Oedema or swelling in the areas where the denture exerts pressure.
  • Check denture stomatitis - Red, swollen mucosa with or without white patches, often caused by fungal overgrowth, especially Candida species.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Denture-Related Pain"][/sc]
  • Non-healing ulcers - Persistent ulceration may indicate oral cancer, requiring urgent biopsy and further investigation.
  • Severe pain unresponsive to adjustment - May suggest deeper infections or an underlying malignancy, particularly in older patients.
  • Unexplained weight loss - Could signal systemic disease, including malignancy, and warrants further investigation.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Denture-Related Pain"][/sc]
  • Allergic reactions - Some patients may present with diffuse pain, burning sensations, or erythema due to an allergic reaction to denture materials.
  • Asymptomatic denture stomatitis - Patients with fungal infections like Candida may not have pain or soreness but could show mucosal erythema on examination.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask denture-related pain.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Denture-Related Pain"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Oral examination - Inspection of the mucosa, fit of the dentures, and presence of erythema, ulceration, or stomatitis.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Targeted investigations to confirm diagnosis, assess complications, and monitor response in denture-related pain.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Denture-Related Pain"][/sc]
  • Denture stomatitis - Typically presents with diffuse erythema under the denture and may have white patches due.
  • Oral lichen planus - Presents with reticular white patches, unlike the ulcers and redness seen in denture-related trauma.
  • Oral cancer - Persistent, non-healing ulcers with indurated borders and weight loss distinguish it from benign denture-related.
  • Related alternative diagnosis - Distinguish from denture-related pain using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Denture-Related Pain"][/sc]
  • Denture stomatitis - Chronic mucosal irritation can lead to infection, most commonly fungal, with Candida species.
  • Oral cancer - Long-standing non-healing ulcers in elderly patients with dentures may rarely signify malignant transformation, particularly squamous cell carcinoma.
  • Gingival hyperplasia - Chronic irritation can result in fibrous overgrowth of gum tissue.
