Myasthenia Gravis
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Myasthenia Gravis"][/sc]
  • Myasthenia gravis (MG) is an autoimmune neuromuscular disorder characterised by fluctuating skeletal muscle weakness and fatigability, particularly affecting muscles that control voluntary movements such as the eyes, face, and limbs.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise myasthenia features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Myasthenia Gravis"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Myasthenia Gravis"][/sc]
  • MG is more common in women under 40 and men over 60.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Myasthenia Gravis"][/sc]
  • 1. Autoantibodies (mainly IgG) target acetylcholine receptors (AChR) or associated proteins at the neuromuscular junction.
  • 2. These antibodies block, alter, or destroy the AChRs, reducing the efficiency of neuromuscular transmission.
  • 3. Reduced AChR function leads to impaired muscle contraction and increased muscle fatigue upon exertion.
  • 4. In some cases, antibodies may target muscle-specific kinase (MuSK), disrupting the organisation of the neuromuscular junction.
  • 5. The thymus gland is implicated in the pathogenesis, often abnormal in MG patients, sometimes with thymomas (tumours).
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Myasthenia Gravis"][/sc]
  • Initial phase: Subtle muscle weakness often begins with ocular muscles (ptosis, diplopia).
  • Progressive phase: Weakness spreads to bulbar, limb, and respiratory muscles over weeks to months.
  • Chronic phase: Fluctuating symptoms with exacerbations and remissions. In severe cases, respiratory failure (myasthenic crisis) may occur.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Myasthenia Gravis"][/sc]
  • Ask about ocular symptoms (ptosis, diplopia) - Commonest early symptom due to weakness of the extraocular muscles. The autoantibodies target receptors, leading to muscle fatigue.
  • Ask about bulbar weakness (dysarthria, dysphagia) - Difficulty with speech and swallowing due to impaired transmission at the neuromuscular junction of the muscles involved in speech.
  • Ask about limb weakness - Fluctuating weakness, typically worse with prolonged activity, reflecting impaired muscle contractility from reduced acetylcholine receptor activity.
  • Ask about respiratory weakness - Shortness of breath or respiratory failure in severe cases (myasthenic crisis), reflecting involvement of the respiratory muscles.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Myasthenia Gravis"][/sc]
  • Check ptosis - Drooping of the upper eyelids due to fatigued levator palpebrae muscles.
  • Check diplopia - Double vision due to weakness of extraocular muscles, leading to misalignment of the eyes.
  • Check dysarthria - Slurred speech caused by weakness of the muscles controlling speech.
  • Check generalised muscle weakness - Symmetrical weakness that worsens with use, particularly of proximal limb muscles.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Myasthenia Gravis"][/sc]
  • Respiratory failure (Myasthenic crisis) - Sudden onset of respiratory weakness leading to potential life-threatening respiratory failure, requiring urgent intervention.
  • Severe dysphagia - Inability to swallow, risking aspiration pneumonia, dehydration, and malnutrition.
  • Bulbar weakness with rapid progression - May indicate impending crisis or a severe form of the disease.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Myasthenia Gravis"][/sc]
  • Purely ocular symptoms persisting for years (ocular MG), which may never generalise to other muscle groups.
  • Asymmetric limb weakness, which may be confused with other neuromuscular disorders like motor neurone disease.
  • Weakness predominantly in the neck extensors or wrist flexors ("dropped head syndrome"), which can be misdiagnosed as a localised muscle problem.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask myasthenia gravis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Myasthenia Gravis"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Serological tests: Positive anti-AChR antibodies in ~85% of generalised MG cases. Anti-MuSK antibodies may be found in seronegative cases; Electromyography (EMG).
  • Targeted and monitoring: Edrophonium test: Temporary improvement of muscle weakness after administration of edrophonium (acetylcholinesterase inhibitor), though now less commonly used; CT or MRI.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Myasthenia Gravis"][/sc]
  • Lambert-Eaton Myasthenic Syndrome (LEMS) - Weakness improves with activity, unlike MG. LEMS often associated with small-cell lung cancer.
  • Botulism - Flaccid paralysis with descending pattern, pupils affected, and history of food contamination in cases.
  • Stroke - Sudden onset, often unilateral weakness, and associated with sensory loss or aphasia.
  • Motor neurone disease (MND) - Presents with muscle atrophy and fasciculations, with no fluctuations or significant ocular involvement.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Myasthenia Gravis"][/sc]
  • Myasthenic crisis: Life-threatening respiratory failure requiring mechanical ventilation. Occurs in up to 20% of MG patients.
  • Aspiration pneumonia: Due to bulbar weakness leading to difficulty swallowing and aspiration of food or liquids.
  • Long-term steroid use complications: Osteoporosis, diabetes, and hypertension.
  • Thymoma: Occurs in 10–15% of patients, potentially malignant and requiring surgical removal.
