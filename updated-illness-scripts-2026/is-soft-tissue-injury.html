Soft Tissue Injury
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Soft Tissue Injury"][/sc]
  • A soft tissue injury (STI) refers to damage to muscles, ligaments, or tendons, typically caused by trauma, overuse, or strain.

Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Soft Tissue Injury"][/sc]
  • Soft tissue injury results from mechanical overload or trauma, leading to microscopic or macroscopic disruption of muscle, ligament, or tendon fibres.
  • Initial trauma triggers an inflammatory response, marked by vasodilation and increased capillary permeability, causing swelling and recruitment of immune cells to clear debris and promote repair.
  • Fibroblast activation occurs in the proliferative phase, producing collagen for tissue repair, which later remodels for functional recovery. However, excessive scarring or improper collagen alignment can result in chronic dysfunction.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Soft Tissue Injury"][/sc]
  • Acute Phase (0–72 hours): Characterised by pain, swelling, and functional limitation due to inflammation.
  • Subacute Phase (3–21 days): Repair and healing begin with reduced swelling, though pain may persist with movement or pressure.
  • Chronic Phase (>21 days): Ongoing recovery with potential for scar tissue formation, which may result in stiffness or chronic pain if not properly managed.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Soft Tissue Injury"][/sc]
  • Ask about pain - Localised to the site of injury, resulting from nociceptor activation due to inflammation and tissue damage.
  • Ask about swelling - Caused by fluid accumulation from increased vascular permeability during the inflammatory response.
  • Ask about bruising - Due to blood leakage from damaged capillaries into the surrounding tissues.
  • Ask about stiffness - Reflects inflammatory effects on surrounding tissues, often exacerbated by immobilisation.
  • Ask about weakness - Related to disruption of muscle or tendon integrity and pain-induced inhibition.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Soft Tissue Injury"][/sc]
  • Check tenderness - Pain elicited on palpation due to inflammation and localised tissue injury.
  • Check swelling - Visible or palpable enlargement of the area, indicating inflammatory fluid accumulation.
  • Check ecchymosis - Discolouration due to subcutaneous bleeding.
  • Check reduced Range of Motion - Indicates pain or mechanical obstruction due to swelling or injury.
  • Check instability (for ligament injuries) - Suggests significant ligament damage and impaired joint integrity.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Soft Tissue Injury"][/sc]
  • Severe, persistent pain - May indicate underlying fracture or compartment syndrome.
  • Loss of sensation - Suggests nerve involvement, such as entrapment or injury.
  • Cold or pale extremity - Can signal compromised vascular supply or acute compartment syndrome.
  • Non-resolving symptoms after 2–3 weeks - Raises concern for chronic issues, e.g., tendinopathy or undiagnosed ligament tear.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Soft Tissue Injury"][/sc]
  • Minimal pain but significant functional loss in chronic tendinopathies, possibly due to fibrotic scarring.
  • Delayed bruising in deep injuries, where bleeding is not initially visible.
  • Chronic stiffness without acute swelling in elderly patients with degenerative changes.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask soft tissue injury.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Soft Tissue Injury"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: X-rays - Used to exclude fractures; typically normal in soft tissue injuries.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Ultrasound - Identifies ligament, tendon, or muscle tears; shows hypoechoic areas in partial tears; MRI - Provides detailed imaging of soft.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Soft Tissue Injury"][/sc]
  • Fracture - Presence of severe pain, inability to bear weight, and positive X-ray findings.
  • Compartment Syndrome - Severe pain disproportionate to the injury, tense swelling, and neurovascular compromise.
  • Tendon Rupture - Palpable defect and inability to perform movements specific to the affected tendon.
  • Related alternative diagnosis - Distinguish from soft tissue injury using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Soft Tissue Injury"][/sc]
  • Chronic Pain - Due to improper healing or nerve sensitisation.
  • Stiffness - Resulting from scar tissue or lack of rehabilitation.
  • Joint Instability - Following severe ligament damage without repair.
