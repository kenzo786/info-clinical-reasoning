Genital Herpes
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Genital Herpes"][/sc]
  • Genital herpes is a sexually transmitted infection (STI) caused by herpes simplex virus (HSV), primarily HSV-2, though HSV-1 can also cause genital involvement.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise genital features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Genital Herpes"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Genital Herpes"][/sc]
  • In the UK, genital herpes is one of the most common STIs, with approximately 20,000 new cases diagnosed annually in genitourinary medicine (GUM) clinics.
  • In hospitalised patients, genital herpes is often observed in immunosuppressed individuals or as a coinfection in other STIs.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Genital Herpes"][/sc]
  • HSV enters the body through mucosal surfaces or abraded skin, infecting epithelial cells and leading to local replication.
  • The virus ascends sensory nerves to establish latency in dorsal root ganglia (commonly sacral ganglia in genital herpes).
  • During reactivation, triggered by factors like stress or immunosuppression, the virus travels back to the skin or mucosa, causing symptomatic or asymptomatic shedding.
  • Host immune responses, particularly T-cell-mediated immunity, help control infection but do not eliminate latent virus.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Genital Herpes"][/sc]
  • Primary infection: Severe symptoms with systemic signs such as fever, malaise, and local pain. Lesions heal in 2–4 weeks without scarring.
  • Latent phase: No symptoms, with viral latency in ganglia.
  • Recurrent episodes: Milder symptoms with localised genital ulcers. Frequency varies among individuals.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Genital Herpes"][/sc]
  • Ask about genital pain - Caused by inflammatory response to viral replication and epithelial damage.
  • Ask about blisters and ulcers - Vesicles progress to pustules and painful ulcers due to epithelial destruction.
  • Ask about dysuria - Pain during urination due to ulceration near the urethral opening.
  • Ask about systemic symptoms (e.g., fever, malaise) - More common in primary infection, caused by systemic cytokine release.
  • Ask about asymptomatic presentation - Common, particularly during latency or in HSV-1 genital infections.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Genital Herpes"][/sc]
  • Check grouped vesicles - Classic presentation of HSV on erythematous bases.
  • Check ulcers - Results from rupture of vesicles; usually tender.
  • Check inguinal lymphadenopathy - Tender, enlarged nodes due to immune activation.
  • Check erythema - Surrounding ulcers and inflamed genital tissues.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Genital Herpes"][/sc]
  • Severe systemic symptoms - May indicate disseminated herpes, particularly in immunocompromised individuals.
  • Neurological symptoms (e.g., meningitis, radiculopathy) - Suggests HSV-related meningitis or sacral nerve involvement.
  • Persistent lesions - May indicate underlying immunosuppression or malignancy.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Genital Herpes"][/sc]
  • Fissures - May mimic other dermatological conditions like eczema or fissuring candidiasis.
  • Urethritis without visible ulcers - Can mimic bacterial STIs like gonorrhoea or chlamydia.
  • Asymptomatic shedding - Particularly common in HSV-1 genital infections.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask genital herpes.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Genital Herpes"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Viral PCR - Detects HSV DNA from swabs of lesions. Sensitive and specific.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Type-specific serology - Identifies HSV-1 or HSV-2 antibodies but not useful for acute diagnosis; Tzanck smear - Shows multinucleated giant cells.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Genital Herpes"][/sc]
  • A 28-year-old woman presents with a 4-day history of painful genital ulcers, dysuria, and fever following a new sexual partner. Examination reveals grouped vesicles on an erythematous base and tender.
  • Patient with a 4-day history of painful genital ulcers, dysuria, and fever following a new sexual partner. Examination reveals grouped vesicles on an erythematous base and tender, consistent with genital herpes.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Genital Herpes"][/sc]
  • Syphilis - Typically presents with a single, painless ulcer (chancre).
  • Chancroid - Painful genital ulcer with purulent base, often accompanied by unilateral inguinal lymphadenopathy.
  • Lichen planus - Chronic vulvar lesions that are often itchy and violaceous rather than ulcerative.
  • Related alternative diagnosis - Distinguish from genital herpes using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Genital Herpes"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Assess severity, physiological stability, and immediate complications.
  • Core care: Deliver condition-specific management for genital herpes, treat underlying drivers, and coordinate appropriate specialist input.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Reassess symptom trajectory, examination findings, and objective markers to confirm response in genital herpes.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Escalate urgently for clinical deterioration, evolving red flags, or failure to improve in suspected genital herpes.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Genital Herpes"][/sc]
  • Neonatal herpes - High morbidity and mortality if transmitted during delivery.
  • Disseminated infection - Rare, occurs in immunocompromised patients, can involve multiple organs.
  • Psychological impact - Stigma and anxiety associated with chronic infection.
