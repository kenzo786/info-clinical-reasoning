Eustachian Tube Dysfunction
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Eustachian Tube Dysfunction"][/sc]
  • Eustachian Tube Dysfunction (ETD) refers to the inability of the Eustachian tube, which connects the middle ear to the nasopharynx, to open properly.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise eustachian features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.

Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Eustachian Tube Dysfunction"][/sc]
  • The Eustachian tube is responsible for equalising pressure in the middle ear and draining secretions. Dysfunction arises when the tube fails to open during activities such as swallowing or yawning.
  • This may be caused by inflammation due to viral upper respiratory tract infections (URTIs), allergic rhinitis, or sinusitis. Anatomical variations, such as a narrower tube or adenoid hypertrophy, can also contribute.
  • When the tube does not open properly, negative pressure builds in the middle ear, leading to tympanic membrane retraction, fluid accumulation, and hearing loss.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Eustachian Tube Dysfunction"][/sc]
  • Acute ETD: Often follows a viral URTI, with symptoms resolving within days to weeks as inflammation subsides.
  • Chronic ETD: Symptoms persist for several weeks to months, often due to allergies or anatomical causes like nasal polyps or chronic sinusitis.
  • Intermittent ETD: Symptoms may be triggered by altitude changes (e.g., air travel) or changes in barometric pressure (e.g., diving).
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Eustachian Tube Dysfunction"][/sc]
  • Ask about ear Fullness - The sensation of ear pressure occurs due to the inability of the Eustachian tube to equalise air pressure in the middle.
  • Ask about hearing Loss - Conductive hearing loss results from fluid accumulation or tympanic membrane retraction, reducing sound transmission.
  • Ask about tinnitus - Ringing or buzzing sounds in the ear arise due to abnormal pressure changes affecting cochlear function.
  • Ask about otalgia (Ear Pain) - Discomfort can occur due to inflammation or pressure changes within the middle ear space.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Eustachian Tube Dysfunction"][/sc]
  • Check retracted Tympanic Membrane - Seen on otoscopic examination, due to negative pressure in the middle ear pulling the tympanic membrane inward.
  • Check middle Ear Effusion - Fluid behind the tympanic membrane, visible on otoscopy, may appear as an air-fluid level or bubbles.
  • Check impaired Tympanic Membrane Mobility - Reduced movement of the tympanic membrane when using pneumatic otoscopy indicates poor ventilation of the middle ear.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Eustachian Tube Dysfunction"][/sc]
  • Persistent Unilateral Symptoms - May indicate a more serious condition such as nasopharyngeal carcinoma, which can obstruct the Eustachian tube.
  • Severe Ear Pain with Fever - Suggests acute otitis media or mastoiditis, requiring prompt evaluation and management.
  • Vertigo or Facial Nerve Weakness - Could indicate inner ear involvement or more severe pathology affecting the surrounding structures.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Eustachian Tube Dysfunction"][/sc]
  • Asymptomatic Presentation - Some patients, particularly children, may have no symptoms despite significant tympanic membrane changes or middle ear effusion.
  • Intermittent Symptoms - Symptoms triggered only during activities that alter atmospheric pressure, such as air travel or diving, may be misinterpreted as normal ear pressure fluctuations.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask eustachian tube dysfunction.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Eustachian Tube Dysfunction"][/sc]
  • Acute Otitis Media - Differs with the presence of acute infection, ear pain, and fever.
  • Meniere's Disease - Causes episodic vertigo, tinnitus, and hearing loss, but usually involves sensorineural hearing loss rather.
  • Cholesteatoma - Presents with persistent ear discharge and conductive hearing loss, with a history of chronic.
  • Related alternative diagnosis - Distinguish from eustachian tube dysfunction using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Eustachian Tube Dysfunction"][/sc]
  • Otitis Media with Effusion (Glue Ear) - Chronic ETD can lead to persistent fluid accumulation in the middle ear, impairing hearing.
  • Tympanic Membrane Retraction - Prolonged negative pressure can cause permanent changes, increasing the risk of cholesteatoma formation.
  • Chronic Otitis Media - Recurring ear infections may develop due to poor middle ear ventilation.
