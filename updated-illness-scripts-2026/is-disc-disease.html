Disc Disease
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Disc Disease"][/sc]
  • Disc disease, or degenerative disc disease (DDD), refers to the progressive degeneration or damage of the intervertebral discs in the spine.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise disc features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Disc Disease"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Disc Disease"][/sc]
  • Risk factors: Risk factors for disc disease include relevant predisposition, comorbidity, and exposure history.
  • Higher-risk or missed groups: Disc Disease may be missed when presentation is atypical or obscured by comorbidity.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Disc Disease"][/sc]
  • With ageing, intervertebral discs lose water content, leading to reduced disc height and elasticity.
  • Disc dehydration and fibrosis result in decreased shock absorption, leading to increased stress on the annulus fibrosus and vertebrae.
  • Mechanical stress can cause annular tears, nerve root compression, or herniation, resulting in pain and, in some cases, radiculopathy.
  • Inflammatory mediators released from degenerating discs can irritate local nerves, exacerbating pain.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Disc Disease"][/sc]
  • Early Phase: Patients may experience intermittent mild pain exacerbated by physical activity.
  • Mid-Phase: Pain episodes become more frequent and intense as disc damage progresses.
  • Advanced Phase: Persistent chronic pain, potential neurological symptoms due to nerve compression, and spinal instability.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Disc Disease"][/sc]
  • Ask about chronic back pain: Constant or intermittent low back pain due to structural disc degeneration, worsened by movement or prolonged sitting.
  • Ask about radicular pain (sciatica): Nerve compression in lumbar DDD leads to radiating pain down the legs.
  • Ask about neck pain: In cervical DDD, neck pain with radiation to the shoulders or arms is common.
  • Ask about stiffness: Reduced flexibility due to loss of disc elasticity and vertebral mobility.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Disc Disease"][/sc]
  • Check reduced range of motion: Limited spine movement, especially during flexion, due to stiffness and pain.
  • Check spinal tenderness: Tenderness over affected vertebrae from mechanical or inflammatory irritation.
  • Check neurological deficits (advanced cases): In severe cases, motor weakness or sensory loss due to nerve root involvement.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Disc Disease"][/sc]
  • Progressive weakness: Indicates possible nerve compression, requiring immediate evaluation to prevent irreversible damage.
  • Bladder or bowel dysfunction: Could signify cauda equina syndrome, a surgical emergency due to severe compression of lower spinal nerves.
  • Severe, unrelenting night pain: Raises concern for malignancy or infection rather than typical degenerative disease.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Disc Disease"][/sc]
  • Thoracic pain: DDD usually affects the lumbar or cervical spine; thoracic symptoms may suggest other pathology.
  • Unilateral radiculopathy without back pain: Some cases may present with radiculopathy as the sole symptom.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask disc disease.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Disc Disease"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: MRI: Reveals disc height loss, dehydration, and possible herniation, providing detailed soft tissue imaging.
  • Targeted and monitoring: X-ray: Shows reduced disc height and possible osteophyte formation but lacks soft tissue detail; CT Scan: Useful for assessing bony changes.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Disc Disease"][/sc]
  • A 55-year-old male with chronic low back pain radiating down the left leg, worsened by prolonged sitting and relieved by rest, presents with limited lumbar mobility and occasional tingling, with supportive clinical and investigation findings.
  • Patient with limited lumbar mobility and occasional tingling in, consistent with disc disease.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Disc Disease"][/sc]
  • Spinal stenosis - Often causes neurogenic claudication, unlike DDD, with symptoms relieved by bending forward.
  • Facet joint arthritis - Localised pain without radicular symptoms and with tenderness over facet joints.
  • Sacroiliac joint dysfunction - Pain localised to the sacroiliac region rather than lumbar spine.
  • Related alternative diagnosis - Distinguish from disc disease using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Disc Disease"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Assess severity, physiological stability, and immediate complications.
  • Core care: Deliver condition-specific management for disc disease, treat underlying drivers, and coordinate appropriate specialist input.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Reassess symptom trajectory, examination findings, and objective markers to confirm response in disc disease.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Escalate urgently for clinical deterioration, evolving red flags, or failure to improve in suspected disc disease.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Disc Disease"][/sc]
  • Chronic pain syndrome: Chronic pain with psychological impacts due to persistent symptoms.
  • Neurological complications: Permanent nerve damage if nerve root compression remains untreated.
  • Spinal instability: Advanced degeneration may lead to segmental instability, worsening back pain and potentially requiring stabilisation surgery.
