Acute Coronary Syndrome (ACS)
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Acute Coronary Syndrome (ACS)"][/sc]
  • Acute coronary syndrome is acute myocardial ischaemia caused by coronary plaque disruption with thrombosis, encompassing unstable angina, NSTEMI, and STEMI.
  • UKMLA essentials: persistent ischaemic chest pain, shock or arrhythmia red flags, urgent ECG and troponin assessment.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Acute Coronary Syndrome (ACS)"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Acute Coronary Syndrome (ACS)"][/sc]
  • ACS remains a common cause of emergency admission and unplanned cardiovascular care.
  • Risk increases with atherosclerotic burden and cardiometabolic disease, especially smoking, diabetes, hypertension, and dyslipidaemia.
  • Atypical symptom profiles contribute to under-recognition in older adults, women, and people with chronic comorbidity.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Acute Coronary Syndrome (ACS)"][/sc]
  • Coronary plaque rupture or erosion initiates platelet-rich thrombus formation within the culprit vessel.
  • Reduced coronary flow causes myocardial ischaemia and electrical instability.
  • Prolonged severe ischaemia causes irreversible myocyte injury, differentiating infarction from unstable angina.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Acute Coronary Syndrome (ACS)"][/sc]
  • Initial phase: Symptoms often start abruptly over minutes with ongoing or recurrent chest discomfort.
  • Early evolution: Without rapid treatment, infarct burden and complication risk increase over subsequent hours.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Acute Coronary Syndrome (ACS)"][/sc]
  • Ask about central chest pressure or heaviness - Persistent retrosternal discomfort is the key symptom pattern.
  • Ask about radiation pattern - Pain extending to arm, neck, jaw, or back supports cardiac ischaemia.
  • Ask about autonomic features - Sweating, nausea, and vomiting increase suspicion of acute infarction.
  • Ask about dyspnoea and exertional intolerance - Breathlessness may dominate in heart failure-prone presentations.
  • Ask about atypical symptom clusters - Epigastric discomfort, fatigue, or collapse can represent ACS.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Acute Coronary Syndrome (ACS)"][/sc]
  • Check perfusion and blood pressure - Hypotension, cool peripheries, or delayed capillary refill suggest instability.
  • Check pulse and rhythm - Tachyarrhythmia or bradyarrhythmia may complicate acute ischaemia.
  • Check respiratory status - Crackles or increased work of breathing can indicate acute pulmonary congestion.
  • Check for new murmur or added sounds - New auscultatory findings may indicate mechanical complications.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Acute Coronary Syndrome (ACS)"][/sc]
  • Ongoing severe chest pain with dynamic ECG change - Suggests active coronary occlusion requiring immediate escalation.
  • Haemodynamic collapse - Shock physiology indicates high-risk myocardial compromise.
  • Acute pulmonary oedema - Severe breathlessness with hypoxia indicates acute left ventricular failure.
  • Malignant arrhythmia or syncope - Indicates life-threatening electrical or perfusion instability.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Acute Coronary Syndrome (ACS)"][/sc]
  • Isolated dyspnoea or fatigue: Some patients report minimal pain with predominant cardiorespiratory decline.
  • Epigastric or atypical upper body pain: Non-classic pain localisation can delay recognition without ECG testing.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Acute Coronary Syndrome (ACS)"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Immediate ECG with repeat tracing, high-sensitivity troponin sequence, and bedside assessment of haemodynamic and respiratory compromise.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Echocardiography for wall-motion and complication assessment, followed by coronary angiography to define anatomy and guide reperfusion strategy; continuous rhythm surveillance for arrhythmic events.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Acute Coronary Syndrome (ACS)"][/sc]
  • A 68-year-old man presents with 1 hour of severe central chest tightness radiating to the jaw, with diaphoresis, nausea, and new ECG ischaemic changes.
  • Older adult with acute ischaemic chest pain and autonomic symptoms plus objective myocardial injury pattern, concerning for ACS.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Acute Coronary Syndrome (ACS)"][/sc]
  • Aortic dissection - Tearing chest pain radiating to back with pulse deficit suggests dissection.
  • Pulmonary embolism - Pleuritic pain, tachypnoea, and right-heart strain profile favour embolic disease.
  • Pericarditis - Positional pleuritic pain with diffuse inflammatory ECG features suggests pericardial inflammation.
  • Pneumothorax - Sudden pleuritic pain with unilateral breath-sound reduction points to pneumothorax.
  • Gastro-oesophageal reflux or oesophageal spasm - Meal-related or burning retrosternal pain without dynamic cardiac injury markers suggests oesophageal origin.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Acute Coronary Syndrome (ACS)"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Activate ACS protocol, provide antiplatelet-based emergency care, and secure urgent reperfusion pathway when indicated.
  • Core care: Combine evidence-based antithrombotic therapy with risk-stratified invasive management to restore coronary perfusion.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Repeat ECG and biomarker assessment with close observation for heart failure, arrhythmia, or recurrent ischaemia.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Worsening pain, shock signs, or rhythm instability requires immediate senior cardiology and critical care involvement.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Acute Coronary Syndrome (ACS)"][/sc]
  • Recurrent myocardial ischaemia: Ongoing plaque instability can cause recurrent events despite initial treatment.
  • Heart failure and shock: Large infarcts can produce severe ventricular dysfunction and circulatory collapse.
  • Electrical and mechanical complications: Malignant arrhythmias and structural rupture syndromes can rapidly become fatal.
