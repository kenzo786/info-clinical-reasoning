Orbital Cellulitis
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Orbital Cellulitis"][/sc]
  • Orbital cellulitis is an acute infection of the soft tissues posterior to the orbital septum, typically involving the fat and ocular muscles.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise orbital features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.

Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Orbital Cellulitis"][/sc]
  • Orbital cellulitis usually arises from contiguous spread of infection from the paranasal sinuses (ethmoid sinusitis being the most common source). It can also result from trauma, surgery, or haematogenous spread. Pathogens such as Staphylococcus aureus, Streptococcus species, and, less commonly, anaerobes or fungi, invade the post-septal tissues.
  • This infection leads to inflammatory oedema, causing pressure on orbital structures, impaired ocular motility, and potentially compressive optic neuropathy.
  • If untreated, it may progress to abscess formation, cavernous sinus thrombosis, or intracranial complications.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Orbital Cellulitis"][/sc]
  • Initial Phase: Rapid onset (over hours to days) following a recent sinus infection, trauma, or surgery.
  • Progression: Increasing swelling, pain, and vision impairment over 24-48 hours without treatment.
  • Complications: After 48 hours, risk of abscess, optic nerve compression, and intracranial spread increases significantly.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Orbital Cellulitis"][/sc]
  • Ask about ocular pain - Pain in and around the eye, often exacerbated by eye movement due to inflammation and pressure within the orbit.
  • Ask about proptosis (eye bulging) - Swelling of orbital contents leads to forward displacement of the globe.
  • Ask about diplopia - Double vision arises due to restricted extraocular muscle movement.
  • Ask about reduced visual acuity - Inflammation and oedema can compress the optic nerve, leading to vision impairment.
  • Ask about fever - A systemic sign of infection, indicating the inflammatory response to pathogens.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Orbital Cellulitis"][/sc]
  • Check periorbital oedema - Swelling of the eyelids and surrounding tissues due to localised inflammation.
  • Check proptosis - Bulging of the eye as a result of swelling and accumulation of inflammatory exudates in the orbit.
  • Check restricted ocular motility - Limited eye movement due to inflammation of the extraocular muscles and oedema.
  • Check conjunctival injection/chemosis - Redness and swelling of the conjunctiva due to local inflammation and congestion.
  • Check relative afferent pupillary defect (RAPD) - Indicates optic nerve involvement or significant pressure on the optic nerve.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Orbital Cellulitis"][/sc]
  • Decreased vision - Suggests optic nerve compression, which can lead to permanent vision loss if untreated.
  • Proptosis with severe pain - Indicates increased orbital pressure, with risk of ischaemia or abscess formation.
  • RAPD - Strong indicator of optic nerve compression or damage.
  • Meningism (neck stiffness, photophobia) - May indicate spread to the meninges (meningitis) or cavernous sinus thrombosis.
  • Altered mental status - Can suggest intracranial extension of the infection, which is life-threatening.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Orbital Cellulitis"][/sc]
  • Painless proptosis - Occurs in immunocompromised patients (e.g., diabetics, HIV) where the inflammatory response is blunted.
  • Minimal external signs - In early orbital cellulitis, swelling or redness may be absent, despite underlying orbital involvement.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask orbital cellulitis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Orbital Cellulitis"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: CT Scan (orbits and sinuses) - Shows soft tissue inflammation, abscess formation, and sinusitis. Can also detect extension to the brain.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Blood cultures - Help identify the causative organism, particularly in cases of systemic infection; Full blood count (FBC) - Raised white.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Orbital Cellulitis"][/sc]
  • Preseptal cellulitis - Involves tissues anterior to the orbital septum, lacks proptosis and restricted eye movement.
  • Cavernous sinus thrombosis - More severe, with bilateral proptosis, cranial nerve deficits, and altered mental status.
  • Thyroid eye disease - Chronic course, usually painless proptosis, often with systemic signs of hyperthyroidism.
  • Related alternative diagnosis - Distinguish from orbital cellulitis using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Orbital Cellulitis"][/sc]
  • Optic neuropathy - Compression of the optic nerve can lead to permanent vision loss.
  • Orbital abscess - Requires surgical drainage, occurs in severe or untreated cases.
  • Intracranial spread - Includes meningitis, cavernous sinus thrombosis, and brain abscess, all of which carry a high risk of mortality.
