Sepsis
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Sepsis"][/sc]
  • Sepsis is a life-threatening organ dysfunction caused by a dysregulated host response to infection.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise sepsis features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Sepsis"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Sepsis"][/sc]
  • Risk factors: Risk factors for sepsis include relevant predisposition, comorbidity, and exposure history.
  • Higher-risk or missed groups: Sepsis may be missed when presentation is atypical or obscured by comorbidity.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Sepsis"][/sc]
  • Sepsis begins with an infection, often bacterial, fungal, or viral. The invading pathogen triggers the release of inflammatory mediators, such as cytokines, which promote vasodilation, increased vascular permeability, and leukocyte activation.
  • The immune response becomes dysregulated, leading to widespread endothelial damage, capillary leakage, and microvascular thrombosis. This results in impaired oxygen delivery to tissues and subsequent organ dysfunction.
  • The cardiovascular response is marked by systemic vasodilation, reduced vascular resistance, and hypotension. If untreated, this can lead to septic shock, multi-organ failure, and death.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Sepsis"][/sc]
  • Initial Phase: Early signs include fever, tachycardia, and mild hypotension, often within hours to days of infection.
  • Progressive Phase: Over 12-24 hours, organ dysfunction develops, with altered mental status, renal impairment, or respiratory distress.
  • Late Phase: Septic shock may ensue within 24-48 hours, characterised by persistent hypotension, multi-organ failure, and increased mortality risk.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Sepsis"][/sc]
  • Ask about fever or hypothermia - Fever occurs due to the release of pyrogens from inflammatory cells, while hypothermia may indicate a severe, overwhelming response, with associated clinical features.
  • Ask about tachypnoea (rapid breathing) - Increased respiratory rate is a compensatory mechanism for metabolic acidosis caused by lactic acid build-up from poor tissue perfusion.
  • Ask about confusion or altered mental status - This results from cerebral hypoperfusion and inflammation, reflecting acute organ dysfunction.
  • Ask about fatigue or malaise - Systemic inflammation leads to cytokine release, causing general malaise and weakness.
  • Ask about nausea and vomiting - Gastrointestinal dysfunction from poor perfusion or direct involvement of the GI tract by infection can cause these symptoms.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Sepsis"][/sc]
  • Check tachycardia - Increased heart rate is a compensatory mechanism for maintaining cardiac output in response to hypotension and hypoxia.
  • Check hypotension - Systemic vasodilation from inflammatory mediators leads to decreased systemic vascular resistance and blood pressure.
  • Check oliguria - Reduced urine output is a sign of renal hypoperfusion and early renal dysfunction.
  • Check cold, clammy skin - In later stages, peripheral vasoconstriction occurs to preserve blood flow to vital organs, causing cold extremities.
  • Check jaundice - Liver dysfunction due to hypoperfusion or direct septic injury can lead to hyperbilirubinaemia and jaundice.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Sepsis"][/sc]
  • Hypotension unresponsive to fluids - This indicates septic shock, a critical stage where multi-organ failure is imminent.
  • Altered mental status - Rapid changes in cognition or consciousness suggest acute cerebral hypoperfusion and are predictive of poor outcomes.
  • Oliguria - Signifies severe renal impairment due to prolonged hypoperfusion, a key marker of organ failure.
  • Respiratory distress - Indicates acute respiratory failure, often a precursor to acute respiratory distress syndrome (ARDS).
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Sepsis"][/sc]
  • Afebrile sepsis - Elderly or immunocompromised patients may present without fever due to a blunted immune response, increasing the risk of delayed diagnosis.
  • Hypothermia in the elderly - Instead of fever, older patients may have low body temperature, signifying a poor prognostic factor.
  • Isolated organ dysfunction - Some patients may present with single-organ dysfunction, such as altered mental status or renal failure, without systemic signs of sepsis.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask sepsis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Sepsis"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Full blood count (FBC) - Leukocytosis or leukopenia, reflecting the immune response. In severe cases, neutropenia may occur due to overwhelming.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Serum lactate - Elevated lactate indicates tissue hypoxia and poor perfusion, a key marker of sepsis severity; Blood cultures - Positive.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Sepsis"][/sc]
  • Acute pancreatitis - Presents with severe abdominal pain and elevated lipase, whereas sepsis typically has more generalised.
  • Pneumonia - May present with fever and tachypnoea, but localising chest signs (e.g. crackles) are more.
  • Hypovolaemic shock - Can cause hypotension and tachycardia but lacks the systemic inflammatory response seen in sepsis.
  • Related alternative diagnosis - Distinguish from sepsis using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Sepsis"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Assess severity, physiological stability, and immediate complications.
  • Core care: Deliver condition-specific management for sepsis, treat underlying drivers, and coordinate appropriate specialist input.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Reassess symptom trajectory, examination findings, and objective markers to confirm response in sepsis.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Escalate urgently for clinical deterioration, evolving red flags, or failure to improve in suspected sepsis.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Sepsis"][/sc]
  • Septic shock - Characterised by refractory hypotension, it occurs in approximately 30-50% of sepsis cases and significantly worsens the prognosis.
  • Acute respiratory distress syndrome (ARDS) - Inflammatory damage to the lungs can lead to severe hypoxaemia and is a common cause of death in sepsis.
  • Multi-organ failure - Prolonged hypoperfusion can result in irreversible damage to the kidneys, liver, lungs, and heart.
  • Death - Mortality rates for septic shock range from 30-50%, depending on the speed of recognition and treatment.
