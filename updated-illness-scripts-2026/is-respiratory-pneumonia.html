Pneumonia [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Pneumonia"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Pneumonia"][/sc]
  • Pneumonia is infection of the lung parenchyma causing alveolar consolidation, impaired gas exchange, and a systemic inflammatory response. This script focuses on adult community-acquired pneumonia (CAP); presentations in special groups including older adults, pregnant women, immunosuppressed patients, and children are addressed in section 8.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical and radiological features of community-acquired pneumonia (CAP) in adults, stratify severity using CRB-65 in primary care and CURB-65 in hospital settings, identify atypical presentations in older adults and the immunosuppressed, and know when hospital admission or escalation is warranted.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Pneumonia"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Pneumonia"][/sc]
  • Community-acquired pneumonia (CAP) affects approximately 5–11 per 1,000 adults annually in the UK and is the leading infectious cause of mortality. Incidence rises sharply with age; those aged over 65 account for the majority of hospitalisations. Thirty-day mortality in hospitalised CAP is approximately 5–14%, rising significantly in those requiring critical care.
  • High-risk groups for complications or delayed diagnosis include adults aged over 65, those with COPD, heart failure, diabetes, chronic liver or kidney disease, alcohol dependency, and immunosuppression (including HIV, transplant recipients, and those on immunosuppressive therapy). Nursing home residents and those who aspirate are at particular risk of atypical or polymicrobial infection.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Pneumonia"][/sc]
  • Inhalation or aspiration of pathogenic organisms — bacteria (including pneumococcus, Haemophilus influenzae), atypical organisms (Mycoplasma, Legionella, Chlamydophila), or viruses — overcomes mucociliary and alveolar macrophage defences, initiating infection of the distal airways and alveoli.
  • Local inflammatory response produces alveolar exudate and consolidation, which reduces the gas exchange surface area and causes ventilation-perfusion mismatch, resulting in hypoxaemia; systemic spread of inflammatory mediators drives fever, tachycardia, and raised inflammatory markers.
  • If untreated, progressive hypoxaemia, bacteraemia, and systemic inflammatory response can lead to septic shock, multi-organ dysfunction, and death — outcomes that correlate with the extent of lung involvement and host immune reserve.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Pneumonia"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Prodromal fever, myalgia, and malaise, particularly with atypical or viral pathogens; onset may be insidious over several days or more acute within hours in typical bacterial pneumonia.
  • Progressive phase: Productive cough, pleuritic chest pain, worsening dyspnoea, and consolidation signs develop over 24–72 hours as alveolar exudate accumulates; respiratory rate and oxygen saturation deteriorate as disease advances.
  • Late phase: Complications — parapneumonic effusion, empyema, lung abscess, or septic shock — develop particularly beyond 48–72 hours without adequate treatment or in those with initially severe or undertreated disease.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Pneumonia"][/sc]
  • Ask about cough and sputum character — purulent or rusty-coloured sputum points to bacterial infection (rusty sputum is classically associated with pneumococcal pneumonia); a persistently dry cough with prominent systemic features is more consistent with atypical organisms such as Mycoplasma or Legionella.
  • Ask about pleuritic chest pain — sharp, localised pain that worsens with deep breathing or coughing indicates pleural involvement, which is typical of lobar bacterial pneumonia and helps distinguish it from cardiac or non-respiratory causes of dyspnoea.
  • Ask about dyspnoea and its progression — breathlessness at rest, inability to complete sentences, or rapid deterioration indicates significant impairment of gas exchange and warrants urgent assessment for hypoxia and severity scoring.
  • Ask about fever, rigors, and sweating — high fever with shaking rigors is more typical of bacterial CAP; absence of fever does not exclude serious pneumonia, particularly in older adults, nursing home residents, or the immunosuppressed.
  • Ask about the onset pattern and accompanying systemic symptoms — rapid onset over hours favours bacterial pneumonia; an insidious course over several days with headache, myalgia, gastrointestinal disturbance, or confusion favours atypical organisms (Legionella may also cause hyponatraemia and raised LFTs, which can distract from the respiratory diagnosis).
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Pneumonia"][/sc]
  • Check for consolidation signs — bronchial breathing, dullness to percussion, and increased vocal resonance or tactile vocal fremitus over the affected lobe are the hallmark triad of lobar consolidation; their presence strongly supports the diagnosis and helps localise the affected area.
  • Check respiratory rate and oxygen saturations — tachypnoea (≥30 breaths/min) is a key CRB-65 and CURB-65 severity marker indicating significant respiratory compromise; low oxygen saturations (SpO₂ below 94% is widely used as a clinical threshold, though context matters) signal impaired gas exchange and typically inform escalation decisions.
  • Check for signs of pleural effusion — stony dullness to percussion with diminished or absent breath sounds (distinct from the bronchial breathing of consolidation) suggests a parapneumonic effusion, which may require diagnostic aspiration or drainage.
  • Check temperature and pulse — fever with tachycardia supports a systemic inflammatory response; relative bradycardia in the context of high fever (Faget sign) may raise suspicion of Legionella or typhoid, though this has limited sensitivity; a normal temperature does not exclude severe pneumonia in frail older adults.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Pneumonia"][/sc]
  • Hypoxia — low oxygen saturations (SpO₂ below 94% is a widely used clinical threshold) indicate impaired gas exchange and typically warrant urgent hospital assessment; CRB-65 or CURB-65 scoring alone cannot substitute for oxygen saturation measurement in severity assessment.
  • New confusion or altered mental state — a CRB-65 and CURB-65 marker indicating severe disease or early septic encephalopathy; in older adults it may be the only presenting feature of serious pneumonia, making it a critical diagnostic clue that warrants urgent respiratory and cognitive assessment.
  • Haemodynamic compromise (hypotension or sustained tachycardia) — indicates septic shock complicating pneumonia; typically warrants urgent fluid resuscitation, blood cultures, and escalation to acute medical or critical care teams following local sepsis pathways.
  • Failure to improve, particularly beyond 48–72 hours — persistent or worsening symptoms despite initial management should prompt reassessment for complications (empyema, lung abscess), a resistant or atypical organism, or an alternative diagnosis such as malignancy or pulmonary embolism.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Pneumonia"][/sc]
  • Atypical organisms (Mycoplasma, Legionella, Chlamydophila): Insidious onset with a dry or minimally productive cough, prominent systemic features (headache, myalgia, gastrointestinal symptoms), and sparse consolidation signs on examination; Legionella specifically may cause hyponatraemia, raised LFTs, and confusion, distracting from the respiratory diagnosis — urinary Legionella antigen should be considered in these cases.
  • Older adults: Classic features are commonly absent or blunted; confusion, falls, functional decline, reduced oral intake, or mild dyspnoea may be the only presenting features without significant fever, productive cough, or marked respiratory distress; this delays diagnosis and increases complication risk, making a low threshold for imaging appropriate.
  • Pregnancy: Physiological changes — raised diaphragm, increased oxygen demand, and altered immune response — can make pneumonia more severe and harder to assess clinically; both CRB-65 and CURB-65 may underestimate severity in pregnant women, so a lower threshold for hospital assessment is warranted; ionising radiation should be minimised where clinically possible, with ultrasound for pleural assessment and MRI as an alternative to CT when needed.
  • Immunosuppressed patients: May have minimal symptoms despite extensive radiological disease; opportunistic organisms — including Pneumocystis jirovecii pneumonia (PCP), fungal pathogens, and atypical mycobacteria — should be considered in addition to conventional bacteria; CXR may underestimate disease burden and CT chest is often needed.
  • Children: May present with abdominal pain (particularly with right lower lobe pneumonia), high fever, and rapid deterioration without prominent respiratory complaints; wheeze may complicate the clinical picture and lead to an initial diagnosis of viral wheeze or asthma.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Pneumonia"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected pneumonia: Oxygen saturations (bedside — essential for severity assessment); FBC (raised white cell count supports bacterial infection; normal does not exclude pneumonia, particularly with atypical or viral pathogens); CRP (markedly elevated in bacterial CAP, less so in atypical or viral infection); U&Es (urea is a CURB-65 component; raised creatinine may indicate sepsis-related renal impairment); blood cultures if hospitalised with moderate or severe disease (take before initiating antibiotics where possible); sputum culture if able to produce a sample before antimicrobials — results guide de-escalation; urinary Legionella antigen and urinary pneumococcal antigen in hospitalised patients with moderate or severe disease (NICE NG250).
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: BNP or NT-proBNP and echocardiography if acute cardiac failure is suspected; D-dimer followed by CTPA if pulmonary embolism is the primary concern; sputum AFB microscopy and Mantoux or IGRA if tuberculosis risk factors are present.
  • Imaging — chest X-ray: First-line imaging — lobar or segmental consolidation confirms the diagnosis; multilobar involvement, cavitation, or bilateral interstitial shadowing suggests more severe or atypical disease; a normal CXR does not exclude early pneumonia, particularly with atypical organisms or immunosuppression — clinical suspicion should override a negative film.
  • Imaging — CT chest: Consider when CXR is inconclusive, complications are suspected (empyema, lung abscess, cavitation), or underlying malignancy needs to be excluded; more sensitive than CXR for detecting consolidation extent and structural complications; ultrasound chest is preferred over CT for assessing and guiding drainage of pleural effusions.
  • Serial reassessment: Short-interval repeat clinical assessment, oxygen saturations, and inflammatory markers at 48–72 hours are appropriate in hospitalised or closely monitored patients to confirm clinical improvement; NICE NG250 (2025) does not recommend a routine follow-up CXR for all patients — consider at 6 weeks in those with lung cancer risk factors (including smokers and those aged ≥50 with other risk factors), underlying respiratory disease, persisting or worsening symptoms, or unexplained weight loss.
Clinical Scoring — CRB-65 / CURB-65 [sc name="ask-ai-09a" Q="Pneumonia"][/sc]
  • NICE NG250 (2025) describes two related severity scoring tools for adult CAP: CRB-65 for use in primary care (where blood urea is not immediately available) and CURB-65 for use in hospital. Both are reasoning aids, not rule-in or rule-out tools; local pathways and senior clinical judgement always take precedence.
  • CRB-65 (primary care — total /4): Score one point for each of:
    • Confusion (new onset) — 1 point
    • Respiratory rate ≥30 breaths/min — 1 point
    • Blood pressure (systolic <90 mmHg or diastolic ≤60 mmHg) — 1 point
    • Age ≥65 years — 1 point
    Score 0 is often associated with low risk and may support home management with safety-netting; score 1–2 often warrants hospital assessment; score 3–4 is typically associated with high risk and often warrants urgent hospital admission. CRB-65 is the tool most relevant to MRCGP and primary care settings.
  • CURB-65 (hospital — total /5): Adds blood urea to the four CRB-65 components. Score one point for each of:
    • Confusion (new onset) — 1 point
    • Urea >7 mmol/L — 1 point
    • Respiratory rate ≥30 breaths/min — 1 point
    • Blood pressure (systolic <90 mmHg or diastolic ≤60 mmHg) — 1 point
    • Age ≥65 years — 1 point
    Score 0–1 is often associated with low risk and may support home management; score 2 often warrants hospital assessment; scores 3–5 are typically associated with high risk and often warrant admission, with scores ≥4 sometimes prompting consideration of HDU or ICU involvement.
  • Both tools perform less well in nursing home residents, immunosuppressed patients, and pregnant women — groups where the threshold for hospital assessment should be lower regardless of score, as severity may be significantly underestimated.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Pneumonia"][/sc]
  • A 72-year-old woman presents with 3 days of productive cough with rusty-coloured sputum, right-sided pleuritic chest pain, fever, and progressive dyspnoea, with dullness to percussion, bronchial breathing, and increased vocal resonance at the right base on examination.
  • Older adult with acute febrile respiratory illness, lobar consolidation signs, and a CURB-65 score reflecting moderate severity, concerning for community-acquired pneumonia with possible parapneumonic effusion.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Pneumonia"][/sc]
  • Pulmonary embolism — pleuritic pain and dyspnoea without productive cough or consolidation signs; fever, if present, is usually low-grade; risk factors (immobility, recent surgery, DVT) and D-dimer followed by CTPA are required to exclude.
  • Acute cardiac failure — bilateral basal crackles with dependent oedema, raised JVP, and orthopnoea; BNP and CXR (cardiomegaly, bilateral interstitial shadowing, Kerley B lines) distinguish from unilateral lobar pneumonia.
  • Lung cancer with post-obstructive pneumonia — subacute presentation with weight loss, haemoptysis, and a persistent or recurrent consolidation in the same region; consider a follow-up CXR at 6 weeks in selected higher-risk patients — following NICE NG250 criteria — to exclude an underlying predisposing malignancy.
  • Pulmonary tuberculosis — subacute weight loss, night sweats, haemoptysis, and upper-lobe predominant disease on CXR; consider in those with immunosuppression, recent travel to endemic regions, or known contact.
  • COPD exacerbation — purulent sputum and worsening dyspnoea in a patient with known airways disease, but with predominantly obstructive physiology and wheeze rather than consolidation signs or lobar dullness.
  • COVID-19 pneumonitis — bilateral interstitial pattern rather than lobar consolidation; often with anosmia, myalgia, and a positive rapid antigen or PCR test; may coexist with bacterial superinfection.
  • Lung abscess — cavitating infection with foul-smelling or copious sputum, often following aspiration; CT chest required to characterise and guide management; may initially mimic lobar pneumonia on clinical grounds.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Pneumonia"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Recognise hypoxia and haemodynamic instability early and obtain prompt senior review. Supportive care often includes supplemental oxygen (targeting SpO₂ 94–98% in most patients, or 88–92% in known or suspected COPD), IV fluids if unable to maintain oral hydration, and analgesia for pleuritic chest pain, in line with local acute pathways.
  • Antibiotic management: Antibiotic choice and route (oral vs intravenous) are guided by severity scoring (CRB-65 in primary care, CURB-65 in hospital), clinical assessment, and local antimicrobial guidelines; low-severity CAP is often suitable for home management with oral antibiotics; moderate or high severity typically warrants inpatient care; atypical cover should be considered where atypical organisms are clinically suspected. Defer specific drug choice to local antimicrobial prescribing guidelines.
  • Escalation for severe disease: HDU or ICU involvement may be warranted for septic shock, respiratory failure not responding to supplemental oxygen, or high severity scores with clinical deterioration; early senior medical review and consideration of non-invasive ventilation or invasive ventilation should follow local critical care pathways.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Reassess respiratory rate, oxygen saturations, heart rate, temperature, and CRP at 48–72 hours to confirm improvement; NICE NG250 (2025) does not recommend a routine follow-up CXR for all patients — consider at 6 weeks in those with lung cancer risk factors, underlying respiratory disease, persisting or worsening symptoms, or unexplained weight loss.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Worsening dyspnoea, persistent fever, haemoptysis, new confusion, or clinical deterioration particularly beyond 48–72 hours of treatment should trigger urgent reassessment for complications (empyema, lung abscess, septic shock) or an alternative diagnosis.
  • Exam pharmacology reference (UKMLA/MRCGP): NICE NG250 (2025) describes a risk-stratified antibiotic approach for adult CAP: low-severity CAP (CRB-65 0 / CURB-65 0–1) is typically treated with amoxicillin, with doxycycline or clarithromycin as alternatives where penicillin allergy is documented; moderate-severity CAP (CRB-65 1–2 / CURB-65 2) typically warrants amoxicillin with clarithromycin for atypical cover, or doxycycline alone; high-severity CAP (CRB-65 3–4 / CURB-65 3–5) often warrants IV therapy with co-amoxiclav or benzylpenicillin combined with a macrolide. NICE NG250 recommends a standard 5-day antibiotic course for most adults, extended only if the patient is not clinically stable by day 5. No doses or frequencies are cited here — always verify agent choice, dose, duration, and allergy status against your local antimicrobial formulary before prescribing.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Pneumonia"][/sc]
  • Parapneumonic effusion and empyema: Pleural fluid accumulation is common with bacterial pneumonia; frank empyema (pus in the pleural space) develops particularly beyond 48–72 hours without adequate treatment, and requires image-guided diagnostic aspiration and chest drainage alongside prolonged antibiotic therapy as per local guidelines.
  • Sepsis and septic shock: Bacteraemia complicating pneumonia can cause haemodynamic compromise and multi-organ dysfunction; early recognition and management following local sepsis pathways — including fluid resuscitation and critical care escalation — are essential to reduce mortality.
  • Lung abscess: Cavitating infection within consolidated lung, most often following aspiration or caused by virulent organisms; characterised by persistent fever, cavitation on CXR or CT, and foul-smelling sputum; typically requires prolonged antibiotic therapy with image-guided or surgical drainage in refractory cases.
  • Respiratory failure: Progressive hypoxaemia requiring high-flow oxygen, non-invasive ventilation, or mechanical ventilation; a key complication of severe or multi-lobar disease and a trigger for critical care admission — an important exam-relevant endpoint that underscores the urgency of early severity assessment.
