Fibrocystic Disease
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Fibrocystic Disease"][/sc]
  • Fibrocystic disease, also known as fibrocystic breast changes, is a benign condition characterised by non-cancerous changes in breast tissue.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise fibrocystic features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.

Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Fibrocystic Disease"][/sc]
  • Fibrocystic disease involves hormonally-driven changes in breast tissue, primarily due to fluctuating oestrogen and progesterone levels during the menstrual cycle. Cyclic hormone variation stimulates proliferation of the ductal and stromal components of the breast, leading to formation of cysts, fibrosis, and ductal hyperplasia. Over time, repetitive hormonal stimulation results in benign fibrous tissue and cyst formation, which can contribute to pain, lumpiness, and tenderness.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Fibrocystic Disease"][/sc]
  • Initial Phase: Commonly starts in reproductive years and is linked to menstrual cycles.
  • Progression: Symptoms may intensify with each cycle, often peaking just before menstruation.
  • Stabilisation/Resolution: Tends to resolve or significantly reduce post-menopause when hormone levels decrease.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Fibrocystic Disease"][/sc]
  • Ask about breast Pain (Mastalgia) - Cyclic and often worsens before menstruation due to oestrogen-related ductal stimulation and fluid accumulation within cysts.
  • Ask about breast Lumpiness - Fluctuating, rope-like texture, particularly in the upper outer breast quadrants; related to fibrosis and cystic changes.
  • Ask about breast Tenderness - Localised or generalised pain due to increased fibrous tissue and cyst formation, which stretches the surrounding tissue.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Fibrocystic Disease"][/sc]
  • Check palpable Lumps - Soft to firm and mobile lumps, reflecting cystic and fibrotic changes; often disappear post-menstruation.
  • Check breast Tenderness on Palpation - General or localised tenderness, typically correlating with the areas of fibrocystic change.
  • Check nodularity - Bilateral nodular texture, especially in the upper outer quadrants, due to the localised fibrosis and cyst formation.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Fibrocystic Disease"][/sc]
  • Unilateral Persistent Lump - May indicate a malignancy; requires further assessment if it does not fluctuate with the menstrual cycle.
  • Bloody or Serous Nipple Discharge - Uncommon in fibrocystic disease, potentially indicative of intraductal papilloma or malignancy.
  • Skin Retraction or Dimpling - Suggests malignancy due to tissue infiltration; fibrocystic changes do not usually cause retraction.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Fibrocystic Disease"][/sc]
  • Non-Cyclic Pain - Occasional presentation that might be mistaken for non-hormonal causes; however, it can still be related to fibrocystic changes.
  • Unilateral Symptoms - While fibrocystic changes are typically bilateral, unilateral presentation can occur, which may lead to suspicion of malignancy.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask fibrocystic disease.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Fibrocystic Disease"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Ultrasound - Useful for distinguishing cystic from solid masses, often showing fluid-filled cysts characteristic of fibrocystic disease.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Mammogram - Can reveal dense breast tissue and cystic structures, but dense breast tissue may limit visualisation; Fine-Needle Aspiration - Performed.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Fibrocystic Disease"][/sc]
  • Breast Cancer - Distinguish from fibrocystic disease using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.
  • Fibroadenoma - Distinguish from fibrocystic disease using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.
  • Mastitis - Presents with erythema, warmth, and tenderness, often associated with lactation; fibrocystic disease does not.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Fibrocystic Disease"][/sc]
  • Cyst Rupture - Rare but can cause localised inflammation, which may mimic infection.
  • Psychological Distress - Ongoing pain and fear of cancer may cause anxiety, especially in symptomatic women requiring recurrent imaging.
  • Prognosis - Generally favourable; symptoms typically decrease after menopause, with no increased cancer risk associated with fibrocystic disease alone.
