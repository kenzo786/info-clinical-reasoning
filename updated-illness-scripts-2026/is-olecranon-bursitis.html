Olecranon Bursitis
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Olecranon Bursitis"][/sc]
  • Olecranon bursitis is the inflammation of the olecranon bursa, a small fluid-filled sac located over the olecranon process at the posterior aspect of the elbow.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise olecranon features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.

  • Higher-risk or missed groups: Olecranon Bursitis may be missed when presentation is atypical or obscured by comorbidity.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Olecranon Bursitis"][/sc]
  • The olecranon bursa reduces friction during movement of the elbow. Inflammation occurs when repetitive trauma or acute injury triggers a local inflammatory response. Increased vascular permeability leads to fluid accumulation and swelling. Infective causes, such as septic bursitis, occur due to bacterial invasion, typically Staphylococcus aureus.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Olecranon Bursitis"][/sc]
  • Acute phase: Sudden onset, particularly in cases of infection or trauma.
  • Chronic phase: Gradual swelling, often associated with recurrent trauma or underlying systemic disease.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Olecranon Bursitis"][/sc]
  • Ask about swelling over the posterior elbow: The most common presenting symptom, caused by fluid accumulation in the bursa.
  • Ask about pain or tenderness: Often present in acute or septic cases, due to inflammation and pressure on surrounding tissues.
  • Ask about reduced range of motion: Occurs when significant swelling impedes elbow flexion or extension.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Olecranon Bursitis"][/sc]
  • Check localised swelling: Soft, fluctuant swelling over the olecranon process, typically without erythema in non-infective cases.
  • Check erythema and warmth: Indicative of septic bursitis, due to localised infection and inflammation.
  • Check fluctuance: Suggestive of significant fluid accumulation.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Olecranon Bursitis"][/sc]
  • Severe pain and systemic symptoms (e.g., fever): May indicate septic bursitis, which requires urgent treatment to prevent spread of infection.
  • Rapid progression of swelling and erythema: Suggestive of an abscess or severe infection.
  • Failure to improve with initial management: Could indicate an underlying inflammatory condition or resistant infection.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Olecranon Bursitis"][/sc]
  • Asymptomatic swelling: Common in chronic bursitis without inflammation.
  • Misdiagnosed soft tissue mass: Can mimic neoplastic conditions due to chronic firm swelling.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask olecranon bursitis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Olecranon Bursitis"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Aspiration and fluid analysis: Differentiates septic bursitis (cloudy fluid, elevated WCC) from aseptic causes.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Blood tests: CRP, ESR, and WCC may be elevated in infection or inflammatory conditions; Imaging: Ultrasound confirms fluid collection; X-ray may.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Olecranon Bursitis"][/sc]
  • Septic arthritis - Diffuse joint swelling with fever and more severe pain.
  • Gout - Acute onset with history of hyperuricaemia, often with tophi or erythema.
  • Trauma-related haematoma - Bruising and history of direct injury, often firmer to palpation.
  • Related alternative diagnosis - Distinguish from olecranon bursitis using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Olecranon Bursitis"][/sc]
  • Chronic recurrence: Ongoing inflammation due to repetitive trauma.
  • Abscess formation: Complication of untreated septic bursitis.
  • Spread of infection: Rare progression to cellulitis or osteomyelitis.
