Gilbert's Syndrome
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Gilbert's Syndrome"][/sc]
  • Gilbert’s syndrome is a benign hereditary condition characterised by intermittent, mild jaundice caused by elevated levels of unconjugated bilirubin.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise gilbert features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.

  • Higher-risk or missed groups: Gilbert's Syndrome may be missed when presentation is atypical or obscured by comorbidity.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Gilbert's Syndrome"][/sc]
  • Gilbert’s syndrome is caused by mutations in the UGT1A1 gene, resulting in reduced activity of UDP-glucuronosyltransferase, which conjugates bilirubin in hepatocytes.
  • Decreased conjugation leads to accumulation of unconjugated bilirubin in the blood, especially under stressors like fasting, illness, or dehydration.
  • Because unconjugated bilirubin is less water-soluble, it accumulates in tissues, occasionally causing mild jaundice.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Gilbert's Syndrome"][/sc]
  • Gilbert’s syndrome is lifelong, with symptoms often beginning in late adolescence or early adulthood.
  • Intermittent episodes of jaundice may occur, often triggered by stress, fasting, or illness. Between episodes, bilirubin levels typically remain near the upper normal range.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Gilbert's Syndrome"][/sc]
  • Ask about asymptomatic - Many individuals with Gilbert’s syndrome have no symptoms and may only be diagnosed incidentally.
  • Ask about mild jaundice - Fluctuating yellowing of the skin and eyes, especially during fasting, illness, or stress, due to increased levels of unconjugated bilirubin.
  • Ask about fatigue - Some patients report fatigue, potentially due to the energy demands of increased bilirubin metabolism or psychological factors linked to intermittent jaundice.
  • Ask about abdominal discomfort - Mild, intermittent upper abdominal discomfort may occur in some patients, though this is not well understood pathophysiologically.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Gilbert's Syndrome"][/sc]
  • Check mild jaundice (scleral icterus) - Yellowing of the sclera, typically during episodes of stress or fasting, due to elevated unconjugated bilirubin.
  • Check no hepatosplenomegaly - The absence of liver or spleen enlargement is typical and helps differentiate Gilbert’s syndrome from other jaundice-causing conditions.
  • Check normal physical exam otherwise - Physical examination is generally unremarkable outside of potential mild jaundice.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Gilbert's Syndrome"][/sc]
  • Persistent, worsening jaundice - May indicate underlying liver disease or haemolysis rather than Gilbert’s syndrome, warranting further investigation.
  • Weight loss, dark urine, or pale stools - These symptoms are not typical in Gilbert's syndrome and could suggest obstructive jaundice, hepatitis, or other liver conditions.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Gilbert's Syndrome"][/sc]
  • Severe jaundice - Uncommon in Gilbert’s syndrome, but can be mistaken for liver pathology if bilirubin levels are markedly elevated due to dehydration or other triggers.
  • Psychological symptoms - Some individuals may experience anxiety about jaundice episodes, especially if they are uncertain about the benign nature of Gilbert’s syndrome.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask gilbert's syndrome.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Gilbert's Syndrome"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Focused bedside assessment and first-line testing to assess severity and immediate risk in suspected gilbert's syndrome.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Liver function tests (LFTs) - Normal transaminases and ALP levels, helping to exclude hepatic injury or cholestasis; Genetic testing - UGT1A1.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Gilbert's Syndrome"][/sc]
  • Haemolytic anaemia - Usually shows anaemia, reticulocytosis, and increased LDH, unlike isolated hyperbilirubinaemia in Gilbert’s syndrome.
  • Crigler-Najjar syndrome - Distinguish from gilbert's syndrome using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.
  • Viral hepatitis - Presents with transaminitis and systemic symptoms such as fatigue and malaise, unlike Gilbert’s syndrome’s.
  • Chronic liver disease - Findings such as abnormal liver enzymes, hepatomegaly, or ascites differentiate it from Gilbert’s syndrome.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Gilbert's Syndrome"][/sc]
  • None significant - Gilbert’s syndrome is generally benign with no long-term health complications. Quality of life may be minimally impacted due to occasional jaundice episodes.
