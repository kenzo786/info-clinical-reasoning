Retinal Detachment
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Retinal Detachment"][/sc]
  • Retinal detachment is a condition in which the neurosensory retina separates from the underlying retinal pigment epithelium (RPE).
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise retinal features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Retinal Detachment"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Retinal Detachment"][/sc]
  • UK hospital population: More common in individuals presenting with high myopia, trauma, or a history of eye surgery.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Retinal Detachment"][/sc]
  • Retinal detachment occurs when the neurosensory retina separates from the retinal pigment epithelium (RPE).
  • This separation can be caused by a retinal tear allowing vitreous humour to enter (rhegmatogenous), traction pulling on the retina (tractional), or exudative fluid accumulating beneath the retina (exudative).
  • The lack of blood supply from the RPE leads to retinal ischaemia, photoreceptor dysfunction, and vision loss.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Retinal Detachment"][/sc]
  • Acute phase: Sudden onset of symptoms such as flashes of light, floaters, or a shadow in the visual field.
  • Subacute phase: Progressive enlargement of the visual field defect if untreated.
  • Chronic phase: Permanent vision loss or complications such as proliferative vitreoretinopathy if left untreated.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Retinal Detachment"][/sc]
  • Ask about flashes of light (photopsia): Occurs due to traction on the retina stimulating photoreceptors.
  • Ask about floaters: Result from vitreous debris or bleeding into the vitreous humour, often associated with retinal tears.
  • Ask about shadow or curtain-like visual defect: Indicates the detachment itself, as the retina loses functionality in the detached area.
  • Ask about blurred vision: Caused by ischaemia and photoreceptor dysfunction in the detached region.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Retinal Detachment"][/sc]
  • Check loss of red reflex: Seen in severe cases due to detached retina blocking light transmission.
  • Check elevated retina on ophthalmoscopy: The detached area appears greyish and may have folds or a rippled appearance.
  • Check shafer’s sign: Pigmented cells ("tobacco dust") in the anterior vitreous, indicative of a retinal tear.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Retinal Detachment"][/sc]
  • Sudden onset of vision loss: May signify macular involvement and urgent need for intervention.
  • Rapid progression of visual field loss: Indicates an extensive detachment with a high risk of complications.
  • Associated pain or inflammation: May suggest concurrent uveitis or trauma.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Retinal Detachment"][/sc]
  • Gradual vision loss: May occur in exudative detachment, where the process is slower than rhegmatogenous types.
  • Minimal symptoms: Detachment may occur peripherally, sparing central vision initially.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask retinal detachment.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Retinal Detachment"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Fundoscopy: Reveals elevated, rippled, or greyish retina.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Ultrasound (B-scan): Useful if the view is obscured, showing the detached retina as a mobile echogenic line; OCT (Optical Coherence Tomography).

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Retinal Detachment"][/sc]
  • Posterior vitreous detachment - Similar symptoms but no retinal elevation or Shafer’s sign on fundoscopy.
  • Vitreous haemorrhage - Presents with floaters and vision loss but shows blood in the vitreous on fundoscopy.
  • Central retinal artery occlusion - Distinguish from retinal detachment using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.

Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Retinal Detachment"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Assess severity, physiological stability, and immediate complications.
  • Core care: Deliver condition-specific management for retinal detachment, treat underlying drivers, and coordinate appropriate specialist input.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Reassess symptom trajectory, examination findings, and objective markers to confirm response in retinal detachment.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Escalate urgently for clinical deterioration, evolving red flags, or failure to improve in suspected retinal detachment.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Retinal Detachment"][/sc]
  • Permanent vision loss: If untreated, especially with macular detachment.
  • Proliferative vitreoretinopathy: Scarring that prevents successful retinal reattachment.
  • Recurrence: Detachment can recur post-surgery, requiring additional interventions.
