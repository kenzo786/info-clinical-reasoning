Optic Neuritis
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Optic Neuritis"][/sc]
  • Optic neuritis is an acute or subacute inflammatory demyelination of the optic nerve, often associated with autoimmune or infectious conditions.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise optic features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Optic Neuritis"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Optic Neuritis"][/sc]
  • Risk factors: Risk factors for optic neuritis include relevant predisposition, comorbidity, and exposure history.
  • Higher-risk or missed groups: Optic Neuritis may be missed when presentation is atypical or obscured by comorbidity.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Optic Neuritis"][/sc]
  • Optic neuritis involves inflammatory demyelination of the optic nerve, mediated by immune cells and cytokines. This leads to disrupted conduction of visual signals.
  • Axonal injury may occur, leading to potential permanent visual loss if inflammation persists.
  • Frequently linked to multiple sclerosis or other systemic autoimmune diseases like neuromyelitis optica spectrum disorders (NMOSD).
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Optic Neuritis"][/sc]
  • Acute Phase: Symptoms develop over hours to days, peaking within one week.
  • Recovery Phase: Begins within 2–4 weeks, often with partial or complete resolution of vision loss over 6–12 months.
  • Chronic Phase: Some patients may experience residual deficits or recurrent episodes.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Optic Neuritis"][/sc]
  • Ask about vision Loss: Usually unilateral, painless or painful. Pathophysiology involves conduction block due to demyelination.
  • Ask about pain with Eye Movement: Present in 90% of cases, due to inflammation of the optic nerve sheath.
  • Ask about photopsias: Perception of flashing lights caused by irritation of the optic nerve.
  • Ask about reduced Colour Vision: Commonly affects red hues, attributed to impaired cone cell signalling.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Optic Neuritis"][/sc]
  • Check afferent Pupillary Defect (RAPD): Due to asymmetric optic nerve dysfunction, observed via swinging flashlight test.
  • Check swollen Optic Disc (Papillitis): Seen in 30% of cases, caused by optic nerve inflammation visible on fundoscopy.
  • Check normal Fundus (Retrobulbar Neuritis): Optic disc appears normal despite functional deficits, indicating retrobulbar location of inflammation.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Optic Neuritis"][/sc]
  • Bilateral Vision Loss: May suggest NMOSD or other systemic causes rather than isolated optic neuritis.
  • Severe Persistent Vision Loss: Could indicate alternative diagnoses such as ischaemic optic neuropathy.
  • Progression Beyond Two Weeks: Atypical for optic neuritis, warrants further investigation for infiltrative or neoplastic causes.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Optic Neuritis"][/sc]
  • Bilateral Presentation: May be missed in systemic autoimmune disorders like lupus.
  • Painless Presentation: Less common, potentially indicating other causes such as toxic or nutritional optic neuropathy.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Optic Neuritis"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: OCT: Reveals retinal nerve fibre layer thinning, indicating axonal loss.
  • Targeted and monitoring: MRI Brain and Orbits: Enhancing optic nerve segments confirm inflammation, while white matter lesions may suggest MS; Visual Evoked Potentials: Prolonged.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Optic Neuritis"][/sc]
  • Multiple Sclerosis - Distinguished by history of other neurological symptoms and MRI findings.
  • Neuromyelitis Optica - Often bilateral and associated with longitudinal spinal cord lesions.
  • Ischaemic Optic Neuropathy - Presents in older adults with vascular risk factors, often painless.
  • Related alternative diagnosis - Distinguish from optic neuritis using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Optic Neuritis"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Assess severity, physiological stability, and immediate complications.
  • Core care: Deliver condition-specific management for optic neuritis, treat underlying drivers, and coordinate appropriate specialist input.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Reassess symptom trajectory, examination findings, and objective markers to confirm response in optic neuritis.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Escalate urgently for clinical deterioration, evolving red flags, or failure to improve in suspected optic neuritis.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Optic Neuritis"][/sc]
  • Chronic Visual Deficits: Due to irreversible axonal damage.
  • Recurrent Optic Neuritis: Associated with underlying systemic diseases like MS or NMOSD.
  • Progression to MS: Approximately 50% of patients develop MS within 15 years.
