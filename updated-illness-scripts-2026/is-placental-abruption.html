Placental Abruption
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Placental Abruption"][/sc]
  • Placental abruption is the premature separation of the placenta from the uterine wall before delivery, leading to maternal haemorrhage and potential fetal compromise.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise placental features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Placental Abruption"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Placental Abruption"][/sc]
  • Risk is higher in women with hypertension, smoking, or previous placental abruption.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Placental Abruption"][/sc]
  • Disruption of the maternal blood vessels in the decidua basalis leads to haemorrhage between the placenta and uterine wall.
  • This can cause placental detachment, leading to reduced oxygen supply to the fetus and maternal blood loss.
  • Risk factors include maternal hypertension, trauma, smoking, and thrombophilias.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Placental Abruption"][/sc]
  • Acute onset, often in the third trimester.
  • Symptoms can progress rapidly, leading to maternal haemodynamic instability and fetal distress.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Placental Abruption"][/sc]
  • Ask about vaginal bleeding - Occurs in 80% of cases, due to disruption of the maternal vessels in the placenta.
  • Ask about abdominal pain - Typically sudden and severe, caused by uterine contractions and irritation from the retroplacental clot.
  • Ask about uterine tenderness - Due to irritation and myometrial contractions in response to bleeding.
  • Ask about reduced fetal movements - A sign of fetal hypoxia and distress due to impaired placental function.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Placental Abruption"][/sc]
  • Check maternal tachycardia - Due to hypovolaemia from blood loss.
  • Check hypotension - A late sign of significant haemorrhage.
  • Check uterine hypertonicity - The uterus may feel firm due to contractions triggered by the abruption.
  • Check abnormal fetal heart rate - Fetal bradycardia or late decelerations indicate fetal distress.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Placental Abruption"][/sc]
  • Severe maternal hypotension - Suggests massive haemorrhage and impending shock.
  • Absent fetal heart sounds - Suggests intrauterine fetal demise.
  • Coagulopathy (DIC) - Can occur due to severe abruption triggering widespread clotting activation.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Placental Abruption"][/sc]
  • Silent placental abruption - Minimal or no vaginal bleeding, but signs of fetal distress.
  • Chronic abruption - Recurrent small haemorrhages leading to intrauterine growth restriction.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask placental abruption.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Placental Abruption"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Ultrasound scan - May show a retroplacental clot but has limited sensitivity.
  • Targeted and monitoring: CTG (Cardiotocography) - Shows fetal distress with late decelerations or tachycardia; FBC and coagulation studies - To assess maternal blood loss.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Placental Abruption"][/sc]
  • Placenta praevia - Typically painless bleeding and detected on ultrasound.
  • Uterine rupture - More likely in women with previous C-section scars.
  • Vasa praevia - Bleeding occurs at rupture of membranes, with fetal distress.
  • Related alternative diagnosis - Distinguish from placental abruption using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Placental Abruption"][/sc]
  • Disseminated intravascular coagulation (DIC) - Due to release of thromboplastins from the damaged placenta.
  • Hypovolaemic shock - From excessive maternal blood loss.
  • Fetal hypoxia or stillbirth - Due to compromised placental perfusion.
