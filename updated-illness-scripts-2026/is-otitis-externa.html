Otitis Externa
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Otitis Externa"][/sc]
  • Otitis externa is an inflammation of the external auditory canal, which may involve the auricle and outer ear.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise otitis features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Otitis Externa"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Otitis Externa"][/sc]
  • Globally, it is prevalent in warm, humid climates and among individuals who engage in water activities ("swimmer's ear").
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Otitis Externa"][/sc]
  • The external auditory canal is lined with skin, which contains glands that produce cerumen (earwax). Cerumen protects the canal by creating an acidic, hydrophobic barrier. When this barrier is disrupted—by moisture, trauma, or ear cleaning—the environment becomes more susceptible to infection. Bacteria such as *Pseudomonas aeruginosa* and *Staphylococcus aureus* are common pathogens. Inflammatory responses lead to swelling, pain, and sometimes purulent discharge.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Otitis Externa"][/sc]
  • Acute otitis externa typically develops over a few days to a week. The symptoms may worsen rapidly if untreated. In chronic cases, symptoms last longer than three months and may be more persistent but less intense than acute presentations. Exacerbations may occur intermittently.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Otitis Externa"][/sc]
  • Ask about otalgia (ear pain) - The most common presenting symptom, due to inflammation and pressure on sensory nerves in the external auditory canal.
  • Ask about pruritus (itching) - Caused by irritation of the skin and inflammatory mediators, often preceding the onset of pain.
  • Ask about discharge - Can be serous or purulent, indicating a breach in the integrity of the epithelial barrier due to infection or inflammation.
  • Ask about hearing loss - Conductive hearing loss may occur when the canal is occluded by swelling or debris.
  • Ask about aural fullness - The sensation of ear blockage, caused by swelling of the external ear canal or discharge buildup.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Otitis Externa"][/sc]
  • Check tenderness on tragal pressure - Pain when pressing on the tragus or pulling the pinna is a hallmark of otitis externa, caused by inflammation.
  • Check canal oedema and erythema - Visible swelling and redness due to the inflammatory process.
  • Check otorrhoea - Presence of discharge, typically seen in bacterial infection.
  • Check debris in the ear canal - Dead skin, wax, or purulent material may accumulate within the canal.
  • Check conductive hearing loss - Due to occlusion of the canal, not damage to the middle or inner ear.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Otitis Externa"][/sc]
  • Fever - Suggests a more severe infection, potentially extending beyond the ear canal.
  • Severe pain extending to the face or neck - May indicate the spread of infection to surrounding tissues, including cellulitis or necrotising otitis externa (malignant otitis externa).
  • Cranial nerve palsies - Could signify the involvement of the cranial nerves in necrotising otitis externa, a serious complication often seen in immunocompromised patients.
  • Diabetes or immunocompromised state - Increased risk of necrotising otitis externa, a life-threatening infection of the bone (osteomyelitis) around the ear canal.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Otitis Externa"][/sc]
  • Chronic otitis externa - Presents with mild, persistent symptoms such as itching, discomfort, and flaking skin rather than severe pain or discharge.
  • Fungal otitis externa - Characterised by less pain but more intense pruritus, and the presence of "fluffy" fungal debris in the ear canal.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask otitis externa.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Otitis Externa"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Otoscopy - Reveals canal swelling, erythema, and discharge. This is the primary diagnostic tool for visualising the external ear.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Ear swab and culture - Indicated if the infection is chronic or not responding to treatment. Bacterial culture may identify *Pseudomonas*.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Otitis Externa"][/sc]
  • Acute otitis media - Typically involves the middle ear and is more associated with systemic symptoms like fever.
  • Eustachian tube dysfunction - Distinguish from otitis externa using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Otitis Externa"][/sc]
  • Necrotising otitis externa - A rare but serious complication, where the infection spreads to the skull base, more common in diabetics or immunocompromised patients.
  • Chronic otitis externa - Prolonged inflammation can lead to persistent discomfort and hearing loss.
