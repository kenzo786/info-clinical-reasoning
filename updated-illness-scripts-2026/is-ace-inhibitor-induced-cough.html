ACE-Inhibitor Induced Cough
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="ACE-Inhibitor Induced Cough"][/sc]
  • ACE-inhibitor induced cough is a persistent dry cough triggered by angiotensin-converting enzyme inhibitors through bradykinin-mediated airway sensory irritation.
  • UKMLA essentials: dry cough after ACE inhibitor start, airway swelling red flags, resolve after stopping culprit drug.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="ACE-Inhibitor Induced Cough"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="ACE-Inhibitor Induced Cough"][/sc]
  • ACE-inhibitor induced cough is a common adverse effect in both primary and secondary care after initiation of ACE-inhibitor therapy.
  • Risk is higher in people with prior cough sensitivity, women, and some ethnic groups, so symptoms can be misattributed to respiratory disease.
  • Medication review and early recognition reduce repeated investigations and avoid unnecessary long-term continuation of the causative drug.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="ACE-Inhibitor Induced Cough"][/sc]
  • ACE inhibitors reduce degradation of bradykinin and substance P.
  • Accumulation of these mediators increases airway sensory nerve activation and cough reflex sensitivity.
  • There is usually no intrinsic structural lung disease, so symptoms improve after withdrawal of the causative drug.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="ACE-Inhibitor Induced Cough"][/sc]
  • Onset: Usually begins soon after starting an ACE inhibitor, but delayed presentation can occur after longer exposure.
  • Duration: Cough usually persists while the ACE inhibitor continues and improves after withdrawal over subsequent weeks.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="ACE-Inhibitor Induced Cough"][/sc]
  • Ask about persistent dry cough - Typical presentation is a troublesome non-productive cough after ACE-inhibitor initiation.
  • Ask about throat tickle or urge to cough - Laryngeal irritation is common and may precede frequent cough bouts.
  • Ask about nocturnal or positional worsening - Symptoms often disrupt sleep and become more noticeable when lying flat.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="ACE-Inhibitor Induced Cough"][/sc]
  • Check for absent focal chest signs - Examination is often unremarkable without wheeze, crackles, or focal consolidation.
  • Check for alternative pathology - Abnormal respiratory findings, hypoxia, or systemic illness suggest another diagnosis rather than isolated drug cough.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="ACE-Inhibitor Induced Cough"][/sc]
  • Dyspnoea, stridor, lip or tongue swelling - Suggests ACE-inhibitor related bradykinin-mediated swelling with potential airway compromise.
  • Haemoptysis, weight loss, or persistent systemic symptoms - Raises concern for alternative serious pathology such as malignancy or chronic infection.
  • Cough persisting after drug withdrawal - Ongoing symptoms despite stopping the ACE inhibitor should prompt reassessment for another cause.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="ACE-Inhibitor Induced Cough"][/sc]
  • Predominant throat clearing: Some patients report frequent throat irritation with minimal overt cough.
  • Mixed cough phenotype: Coexisting respiratory disease can produce intermittent sputum and obscure drug causality.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="ACE-Inhibitor Induced Cough"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Focused drug history to confirm ACE-inhibitor exposure and symptom timeline; full respiratory examination and observations to assess for alternative acute pathology.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Chest radiograph or spirometry when symptoms or signs suggest another cause; monitor cough response after ACE-inhibitor withdrawal and substitution to confirm drug causality.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="ACE-Inhibitor Induced Cough"][/sc]
  • A 60-year-old woman presents with 2 weeks of persistent dry cough that began after starting ramipril, worse at night, with normal chest examination and no infective symptoms.
  • Adult with new persistent non-productive cough temporally linked to ACE-inhibitor exposure and no focal respiratory findings, suggesting ACE-inhibitor induced cough.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="ACE-Inhibitor Induced Cough"][/sc]
  • Asthma - Variable wheeze, trigger-related symptoms, and reversible airflow obstruction support airway hyperreactivity.
  • Chronic obstructive pulmonary disease - Smoking history with chronic exertional breathlessness and persistent airflow limitation is more typical.
  • Gastro-oesophageal reflux disease - Heartburn, regurgitation, and post-prandial or nocturnal cough suggest reflux-related cough.
  • Upper airway cough syndrome - Nasal symptoms, postnasal drip, and throat clearing point to rhinitis or sinus disease.
  • Lung malignancy or chronic infection - Weight loss, haemoptysis, abnormal imaging, or persistent systemic features suggest alternative serious pathology.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="ACE-Inhibitor Induced Cough"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Confirm suspected drug causality, exclude red-flag airway compromise, and stop the likely offending ACE inhibitor.
  • Core care: Switch to an alternative renin-angiotensin agent such as an ARB when clinically appropriate and continue condition-directed cardiovascular or renal management.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Review symptom resolution over the following weeks and reassess blood pressure or heart failure control after medication change.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Worsening breathlessness, facial or tongue swelling, stridor, haemoptysis, or persistent cough despite withdrawal should prompt urgent reassessment.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="ACE-Inhibitor Induced Cough"][/sc]
  • Severe upper airway swelling: Bradykinin-mediated swelling can cause life-threatening airway obstruction.
  • Poor treatment adherence: Ongoing cough may lead patients to stop important cardiovascular medication without supervised substitution.
