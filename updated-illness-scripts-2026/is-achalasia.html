Achalasia
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Achalasia"][/sc]
  • Achalasia is a primary oesophageal motility disorder with impaired lower oesophageal sphincter relaxation and absent organised peristalsis, causing functional outflow obstruction.
  • UKMLA essentials: dysphagia to solids and liquids, aspiration or weight-loss red flags, confirm with manometry.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Achalasia"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Achalasia"][/sc]
  • Achalasia is uncommon and usually presents in adulthood in gastroenterology and upper gastrointestinal pathways.
  • Delayed diagnosis is common because symptoms overlap with reflux disease, functional dysphagia, and non-cardiac chest pain syndromes.
  • Rapid progression, marked weight loss, or late-onset dysphagia should prompt exclusion of pseudoachalasia from malignancy.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Achalasia"][/sc]
  • Achalasia involves degeneration of inhibitory neurones in the oesophageal myenteric plexus.
  • Loss of inhibitory signalling impairs lower oesophageal sphincter relaxation and abolishes coordinated oesophageal peristalsis.
  • Progressive stasis of food and fluid causes oesophageal dilatation, regurgitation, and risk of aspiration.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Achalasia"][/sc]
  • Early course: Intermittent dysphagia and retrosternal discomfort may be mild and fluctuate.
  • Progressive course: Symptoms usually worsen over months to years with increasing regurgitation, nutritional impact, and aspiration risk.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Achalasia"][/sc]
  • Ask about dysphagia to solids and liquids - Early involvement of both supports a motility disorder rather than fixed mechanical narrowing.
  • Ask about regurgitation of undigested food - Oesophageal stasis commonly causes nocturnal regurgitation or coughing after lying flat.
  • Ask about retrosternal chest discomfort - Oesophageal distension and spasm can produce non-cardiac chest pain.
  • Ask about weight loss and reduced intake - Progressive swallowing difficulty may lead to undernutrition and fatigue.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Achalasia"][/sc]
  • Check nutritional status - Weight loss, low body mass, or sarcopenic features indicate chronic reduced intake.
  • Check for halitosis - Retained oesophageal contents can cause foul breath.
  • Check chest findings for aspiration - Crackles or focal respiratory signs raise concern for aspiration-related complications.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Achalasia"][/sc]
  • Rapidly progressive dysphagia with marked weight loss - Suggests pseudoachalasia from oesophagogastric malignancy until proven otherwise.
  • Recurrent aspiration symptoms or pneumonia - Indicates unsafe swallowing with respiratory complication risk.
  • Severe chest pain or acute systemic deterioration - Requires urgent reassessment for perforation, obstruction, or an alternative acute diagnosis.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Achalasia"][/sc]
  • Predominant chest pain: Some patients present mainly with non-cardiac chest pain before clear dysphagia develops.
  • Minimal weight change: Early disease may present with significant dysphagia but preserved weight, delaying recognition.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Achalasia"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Focused dysphagia history for solids and liquids, aspiration screen, nutritional assessment, and clinical examination for respiratory consequences.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Upper GI endoscopy to exclude structural obstruction; timed barium swallow for oesophageal emptying pattern; high-resolution manometry to confirm achalasia and subtype; serial symptom and weight review after treatment.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Achalasia"][/sc]
  • A 45-year-old man presents with 12 months of progressive dysphagia to solids and liquids, nocturnal regurgitation, and unintentional weight loss.
  • Adult with chronic progressive oesophageal dysphagia affecting solids and liquids from onset, with regurgitation and nutritional decline, concerning for achalasia.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Achalasia"][/sc]
  • Pseudoachalasia - Later age at onset, rapid symptom progression, and pronounced weight loss suggest malignant infiltration at the gastro-oesophageal junction.
  • Oesophageal stricture - Dysphagia usually starts with solids before liquids and often follows chronic reflux or caustic injury.
  • Gastro-oesophageal reflux disease - Heartburn and acid regurgitation predominate, with dysphagia less typically affecting liquids early.
  • Diffuse oesophageal spasm - Intermittent chest pain and non-progressive dysphagia with preserved sphincter relaxation are more typical.
  • Oropharyngeal dysphagia - Swallow initiation difficulty, nasal regurgitation, or coughing immediately on swallowing localises dysfunction proximally.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Achalasia"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Identify aspiration or dehydration risk, optimise nutrition and hydration, and prioritise urgent assessment if red flags suggest pseudoachalasia or complication.
  • Core care: Definitive lower oesophageal sphincter disruption is achieved with pneumatic dilation, laparoscopic Heller myotomy, or peroral endoscopic myotomy based on patient factors and local expertise.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Reassess dysphagia burden, regurgitation frequency, nutritional trajectory, and reflux symptoms after intervention.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Persistent or recurrent dysphagia, recurrent aspiration, severe chest pain, or systemic decline should prompt repeat evaluation for treatment failure or alternative pathology.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Achalasia"][/sc]
  • Chronic aspiration: Retained oesophageal contents can cause recurrent aspiration events and aspiration pneumonia.
  • Oesophageal dilatation and stasis oesophagitis: Longstanding outflow obstruction can lead to progressive oesophageal enlargement and mucosal injury.
  • Oesophageal malignancy risk: Chronic stasis and inflammation are associated with increased long-term oesophageal cancer risk.
