Pancreatic Cancer
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Pancreatic Cancer"][/sc]
  • Pancreatic cancer is a malignant tumour originating from the exocrine or endocrine components of the pancreas, most commonly from the ductal cells (pancreatic adenocarcinoma).
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise pancreatic features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Pancreatic Cancer"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Pancreatic Cancer"][/sc]
  • Globally, pancreatic cancer ranks as the 12th most common cancer but is the 7th leading cause of cancer death due to its poor prognosis.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Pancreatic Cancer"][/sc]
  • The majority of pancreatic cancers arise from the exocrine cells, specifically the ductal epithelium. Mutations in key genes such as KRAS, p53, and SMAD4 lead to uncontrolled cell growth.
  • As the tumour grows, it invades surrounding tissues, including the bile ducts, causing obstructive jaundice. It can also invade nerves, causing pain, and the blood vessels, leading to vascular complications.
  • Late-stage disease commonly metastasises to the liver, peritoneum, and lungs.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Pancreatic Cancer"][/sc]
  • Pancreatic cancer is insidious, with patients often asymptomatic in the early stages. By the time symptoms develop, the disease is usually advanced.
  • In the early phase, subtle symptoms such as fatigue or anorexia may appear, followed by more specific signs such as jaundice, weight loss, and abdominal pain as the tumour progresses.
  • The median survival after diagnosis is typically less than 6 months for advanced cases, with a 5-year survival rate of about 5-10%.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Pancreatic Cancer"][/sc]
  • Ask about weight loss - Due to cancer-related metabolic demands, malabsorption (from pancreatic exocrine insufficiency), and anorexia.
  • Ask about abdominal pain - Often epigastric and radiating to the back, caused by tumour invasion of the celiac plexus or retroperitoneal structures.
  • Ask about jaundice - Caused by obstruction of the bile duct, leading to accumulation of bilirubin. Common in tumours located in the pancreatic head.
  • Ask about fatigue - A non-specific but common symptom related to the systemic effects of cancer, such as increased cytokine production and nutritional deficiencies.
  • Ask about nausea and vomiting - Often related to gastrointestinal obstruction from tumour growth or bile stasis.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Pancreatic Cancer"][/sc]
  • Check painless jaundice - Classically associated with tumours of the pancreatic head, as bile duct obstruction occurs without inflammation.
  • Check palpable gallbladder (Courvoisier’s sign) - A non-tender, enlarged gallbladder, suggesting obstructive jaundice from a malignant cause rather than gallstones.
  • Check ascites - Indicative of peritoneal metastases or portal vein involvement, leading to increased intra-abdominal pressure and fluid accumulation.
  • Check cachexia - Generalised wasting seen in advanced cancer due to systemic effects of the tumour and chronic malabsorption.
  • Check thrombophlebitis (Trousseau's sign) - A migratory thrombophlebitis reflecting hypercoagulability often associated with pancreatic cancer.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Pancreatic Cancer"][/sc]
  • Painless jaundice - Suggests obstructive jaundice, likely from a tumour at the head of the pancreas. Indicates significant bile duct involvement.
  • Significant weight loss - Rapid and unexplained weight loss is highly suspicious for advanced pancreatic cancer due to malnutrition and tumour burden.
  • New onset diabetes in an older adult - Pancreatic cancer can cause insulin insufficiency and is often associated with diabetes diagnosis in older individuals.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Pancreatic Cancer"][/sc]
  • Acute pancreatitis - Rarely, pancreatic cancer may present as acute pancreatitis, especially in tumours obstructing the pancreatic duct.
  • Diabetes mellitus - New-onset diabetes in an older adult without obvious risk factors can be an atypical sign of underlying pancreatic malignancy.
  • Back pain without abdominal symptoms - Tumour invasion into the retroperitoneum may cause isolated back pain without typical abdominal findings.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask pancreatic cancer.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Pancreatic Cancer"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Focused bedside assessment and first-line testing to assess severity and immediate risk in suspected pancreatic cancer.
  • Targeted and monitoring: CA 19-9 - A tumour marker that is elevated in 70-80% of patients with pancreatic cancer, though non-specific. Levels may correlate.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Pancreatic Cancer"][/sc]
  • Cholangiocarcinoma - Differentiated by lack of pancreatic mass and more central bile duct involvement on imaging.
  • Chronic pancreatitis - Often presents with a history of recurrent pancreatitis, calcifications on imaging, and less severe.
  • Gallstone disease - Associated with episodic, colicky pain and evidence of gallstones on imaging, without a pancreatic.
  • Hepatocellular carcinoma - May present with jaundice, but liver imaging will reveal a primary liver tumour.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Pancreatic Cancer"][/sc]
  • Liver metastasis - The most common site of distant spread, causing liver dysfunction and worsening jaundice.
  • Obstructive jaundice - May lead to cholangitis or biliary cirrhosis if left untreated.
  • Peritoneal carcinomatosis - Spread to the peritoneum can cause ascites, bowel obstruction, and significant abdominal pain.
  • Diabetes mellitus - Tumour destruction of pancreatic tissue can lead to insulin insufficiency and secondary diabetes.
