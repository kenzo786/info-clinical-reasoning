Medication-Induced Falls
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Medication-Induced Falls"][/sc]
  • Medication-induced falls are falls resulting from the effects of specific medications on balance, cognition, blood pressure, and/or muscle function.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise medication features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.

  • Higher-risk or missed groups: Medication-Induced Falls may be missed when presentation is atypical or obscured by comorbidity.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Medication-Induced Falls"][/sc]
  • Many medications affect the central nervous system (CNS), peripheral nervous system (PNS), or cardiovascular system, increasing fall risk. Sedatives and hypnotics impair alertness and reaction time; antihypertensives can cause orthostatic hypotension; anticholinergics impair cognitive function and balance. The combined impact of these effects is often compounded in polypharmacy.
  • Specific medications: Benzodiazepines, opioids, and tricyclic antidepressants have strong sedative effects; antihypertensives and diuretics can lower blood pressure excessively, causing dizziness and postural instability.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Medication-Induced Falls"][/sc]
  • Acute Phase: Symptoms often appear shortly after a medication is initiated or dosages are increased, with falls occurring within days to weeks.
  • Chronic Phase: Risk persists as long as the medication is continued, particularly if doses are not adjusted for age-related pharmacodynamic changes.
  • Resolution Phase: Fall risk may decrease within days to weeks after discontinuing the medication, though some effects (e.g., CNS impact) may take longer to fully resolve.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Medication-Induced Falls"][/sc]
  • Ask about dizziness - Due to orthostatic hypotension or sedative effects; common with antihypertensives and sedatives.
  • Ask about fatigue - Sedative medications reduce alertness and energy levels, increasing fall risk due to delayed reactions.
  • Ask about confusion - CNS-affecting drugs, particularly anticholinergics, can impair cognition, leading to unsteady gait and disorientation.
  • Ask about weakness - Muscle relaxants or drugs causing electrolyte imbalance can lead to muscle weakness and instability.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Medication-Induced Falls"][/sc]
  • Check postural Hypotension - Drop in blood pressure upon standing, commonly due to antihypertensive or diuretic use.
  • Check ataxia - Unsteady gait, often due to CNS depressants or sedatives affecting coordination.
  • Check slurred Speech - Seen with sedative medications or opioids, indicating CNS depression and fall risk.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Medication-Induced Falls"][/sc]
  • Severe confusion or delirium - Can indicate overdose or interaction of multiple CNS depressants, increasing fall risk and injury.
  • Syncope - Sudden loss of consciousness may signal dangerously low blood pressure or arrhythmia secondary to medication.
  • New or worsening ataxia - Worsening imbalance can indicate drug toxicity, especially in cases of renal impairment where medication clearance is reduced.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Medication-Induced Falls"][/sc]
  • Recurrent minor falls - Patients may present with minor, frequent falls rather than a single severe fall, often due to sedative or hypotensive effects that don’t fully impair but reduce stability.
  • Subtle cognitive impairment - Mild cognitive issues in older adults on polypharmacy may precede obvious symptoms of imbalance or dizziness.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask medication-induced falls.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Medication-Induced Falls"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Blood Pressure Measurement - Both supine and standing; orthostatic hypotension may confirm fall risk related to antihypertensives.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Medication Review - Comprehensive review of all medications, especially those with sedative, hypotensive, or anticholinergic effects; Blood Tests - Renal function.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Medication-Induced Falls"][/sc]
  • Parkinson’s Disease - Falls in Parkinson’s are often due to motor symptoms like tremor and rigidity rather.
  • Orthostatic Hypotension from Non-Medication Causes - Can occur in dehydration or autonomic dysfunction; no direct link to medication use.
  • Inner Ear Disorders (e.g., BPPV) - Distinguish from medication-induced falls using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.

Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Medication-Induced Falls"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Assess severity, physiological stability, and immediate complications.
  • Core care: Deliver condition-specific management for medication-induced falls, treat underlying drivers, and coordinate appropriate specialist input.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Reassess symptom trajectory, examination findings, and objective markers to confirm response in medication-induced falls.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Escalate urgently for clinical deterioration, evolving red flags, or failure to improve in suspected medication-induced falls.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Medication-Induced Falls"][/sc]
  • Fractures - Common complication of falls, especially hip fractures in older adults, due to osteoporosis and decreased protective reflexes from sedation.
  • Head Injuries - Due to increased risk of falls with head impact, particularly if anticoagulant use is present.
  • Functional Decline - Fear of falling can lead to reduced mobility and physical activity, further weakening balance and muscle strength.
