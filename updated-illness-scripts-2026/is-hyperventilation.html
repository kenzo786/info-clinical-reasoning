Hyperventilation
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Hyperventilation"][/sc]
  • Hyperventilation is a condition characterised by abnormally rapid or deep breathing, leading to a decrease in arterial carbon dioxide levels (hypocapnia).
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise hyperventilation features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Hyperventilation"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Hyperventilation"][/sc]

  • Higher-risk or missed groups: Hyperventilation may be missed when presentation is atypical or obscured by comorbidity.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Hyperventilation"][/sc]
  • Hyperventilation leads to excessive elimination of CO2, resulting in a decrease in arterial carbon dioxide (PaCO2). This reduction causes respiratory alkalosis, characterised by an increase in blood pH.
  • The higher pH causes vasoconstriction, particularly in cerebral vessels, reducing blood flow to the brain, which can lead to dizziness, lightheadedness, and other neurological symptoms.
  • Alkalosis also shifts the oxygen-haemoglobin dissociation curve to the left, impairing oxygen delivery to tissues.
  • Other systemic effects include altered calcium and potassium levels, leading to muscle twitching and paraesthesia.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Hyperventilation"][/sc]
  • Acute phase: Onset is usually sudden and often associated with emotional triggers (e.g., stress, anxiety) or metabolic causes (e.g., acidosis).
  • Chronic phase: Hyperventilation syndrome may occur, where episodic hyperventilation recurs over a long period, often without clear triggers.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Hyperventilation"][/sc]
  • Ask about dyspnoea (shortness of breath) - Results from the feeling of air hunger despite rapid breathing; pathophysiologically linked to the body's urge to normalise CO.
  • Ask about dizziness or lightheadedness - Caused by cerebral vasoconstriction due to hypocapnia and reduced blood flow to the brain.
  • Ask about chest pain - Often non-cardiac and related to muscle tension or anxiety during hyperventilation.
  • Ask about paraesthesia (numbness and tingling) - Caused by changes in calcium levels due to alkalosis, affecting nerve function, commonly seen in the fingers, toes, with associated clinical features.
  • Ask about palpitations - Triggered by sympathetic activation during anxiety or stress.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Hyperventilation"][/sc]
  • Check tachypnoea (rapid breathing) - The hallmark of hyperventilation, visible as rapid, shallow breathing.
  • Check carpopedal spasm - A classic sign, caused by hypocalcaemia secondary to respiratory alkalosis, leading to involuntary muscle contractions in the hands and feet.
  • Check perioral paraesthesia - Numbness or tingling around the mouth, due to altered calcium and potassium levels.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Hyperventilation"][/sc]
  • Chest pain with radiation - Could indicate a cardiac cause (e.g., myocardial infarction) rather than hyperventilation alone.
  • Severe dyspnoea unresponsive to breathing techniques - May suggest an underlying pulmonary condition, such as a pulmonary embolism or pneumothorax.
  • Altered mental status or confusion - Suggests severe hypocapnia or cerebral hypoxia and requires urgent investigation.
  • Syncope with exertion - May indicate a more serious cardiovascular condition such as aortic stenosis.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Hyperventilation"][/sc]
  • Asymptomatic hyperventilation - Some patients may have recurrent hyperventilation without significant symptoms, particularly those with chronic anxiety.
  • Chronic fatigue or sleep disturbances - In patients with hyperventilation syndrome, these symptoms may dominate the clinical picture rather than acute respiratory distress.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask hyperventilation.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Hyperventilation"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Arterial Blood Gas (ABG) - Typically shows low PaCO 2 (7.45), confirming respiratory alkalosis; ECG - Done to rule out cardiac.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Chest X-ray - Usually normal, but used to exclude structural lung disease or pneumothorax; Serum electrolytes - Hypocalcaemia and hypokalaemia may.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Hyperventilation"][/sc]
  • Panic disorder - Distinguish from hyperventilation using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.
  • Asthma - Hyperventilation may occur, but asthma will also present with wheezing and reversible airway obstruction.
  • Pulmonary embolism - Acute dyspnoea and hyperventilation, but usually with pleuritic chest pain and signs of right.
  • Metabolic acidosis - Hyperventilation compensates for acidosis, but ABG will show low pH and bicarbonate levels.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Hyperventilation"][/sc]
  • Syncope - Cerebral vasoconstriction from severe hypocapnia can reduce brain perfusion, leading to fainting.
  • Severe hypocalcaemia - Prolonged alkalosis can significantly lower ionised calcium levels, causing muscle spasms and tetany.
  • Chronic hyperventilation syndrome - May result in ongoing symptoms like fatigue, muscle tension, and dyspnoea even after acute episodes resolve.
