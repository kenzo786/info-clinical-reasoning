Whooping Cough (Pertussis)
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Whooping Cough (Pertussis)"][/sc]
  • Whooping cough, also known as pertussis, is a highly contagious bacterial respiratory infection caused by Bordetella pertussis.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise whooping features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.

Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Whooping Cough (Pertussis)"][/sc]
  • Bordetella pertussis attaches to the ciliated epithelial cells of the respiratory tract using adhesins such as filamentous haemagglutinin.
  • The bacterium releases toxins, including pertussis toxin, which impairs immune responses and causes local tissue damage.
  • Tracheal cytotoxin disrupts ciliary function, leading to accumulation of mucus and the characteristic cough.
  • Immune response generates significant inflammation, exacerbating airway obstruction.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Whooping Cough (Pertussis)"][/sc]
  • Catarrhal Phase (1-2 weeks): Non-specific symptoms including rhinorrhoea, mild cough, and low-grade fever.
  • Paroxysmal Phase (2-6 weeks): Intense bouts of coughing followed by inspiratory "whoop"; post-tussive vomiting common.
  • Convalescent Phase (weeks to months): Gradual reduction in cough frequency and severity.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Whooping Cough (Pertussis)"][/sc]
  • Ask about paroxysmal cough: Sudden, intense coughing fits due to airway irritation and mucus accumulation.
  • Ask about inspiratory whoop: High-pitched "whoop" sound caused by rapid air intake after coughing fits.
  • Ask about post-tussive vomiting: Vomiting following coughing episodes due to gag reflex stimulation.
  • Ask about rhinorrhoea: Nasal discharge in the early catarrhal phase due to mucosal irritation.
  • Ask about fatigue: Resulting from prolonged coughing episodes and sleep disruption.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Whooping Cough (Pertussis)"][/sc]
  • Check inspiratory whoop: Pathognomonic sound due to airway narrowing and forceful air intake.
  • Check cyanosis: Peripheral or central cyanosis due to hypoxia during prolonged coughing episodes.
  • Check apnoea (infants): Cessation of breathing triggered by severe coughing or airway inflammation.
  • Check subconjunctival haemorrhage: Small haemorrhages caused by increased intrathoracic pressure during coughing.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Whooping Cough (Pertussis)"][/sc]
  • Apnoea: Suggests impending respiratory failure, especially in neonates.
  • Severe cyanosis: Indicates significant hypoxaemia, requiring urgent intervention.
  • Seizures: Can indicate hypoxic encephalopathy or pertussis-related encephalitis.
  • Persistent vomiting: May lead to dehydration and electrolyte imbalances.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Whooping Cough (Pertussis)"][/sc]
  • Adults and adolescents: Prolonged dry cough without inspiratory whoop.
  • Vaccinated individuals: Milder symptoms or only the catarrhal phase due to partial immunity.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask whooping cough (pertussis).
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Whooping Cough (Pertussis)"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Focused bedside assessment and first-line testing to assess severity and immediate risk in suspected whooping cough (pertussis).
  • Targeted and monitoring: Serology: Useful in late disease, detecting IgG antibodies against pertussis toxin; Full blood count: Leukocytosis with lymphocytosis common.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Whooping Cough (Pertussis)"][/sc]
  • Viral bronchiolitis - Differs by wheezing, no whoop, and RSV aetiology.
  • Croup - Barking cough and inspiratory stridor instead of paroxysmal cough.
  • Atypical pneumonia - Persistent cough without inspiratory whoop.
  • Related alternative diagnosis - Distinguish from whooping cough (pertussis) using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Whooping Cough (Pertussis)"][/sc]
  • Pneumonia: Secondary bacterial infection, a leading cause of pertussis-related death.
  • Hypoxaemia: Due to severe coughing and airway obstruction.
  • Seizures: Resulting from hypoxia or pertussis toxin effects on the CNS.
  • Death: Most common in infants under six months of age.
