Acute Abdomen
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Acute Abdomen"][/sc]
  • Acute abdomen is sudden severe abdominal pain with signs suggesting urgent intra-abdominal pathology that may require immediate surgical or medical intervention.
  • UKMLA essentials: pain pattern and peritonism, shock or bleeding red flags, urgent senior surgical assessment.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Acute Abdomen"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Acute Abdomen"][/sc]
  • Acute abdomen is a common emergency presentation across primary care, emergency medicine, and surgical admission pathways.
  • Aetiology varies by age, sex, comorbidity, and pregnancy status, so broad early differential diagnosis is essential.
  • Missed early high-risk diagnoses such as perforation, ischaemia, ruptured aneurysm, or ectopic pregnancy drive avoidable morbidity and mortality.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Acute Abdomen"][/sc]
  • Acute abdomen arises from inflammatory, obstructive, ischaemic, perforative, or haemorrhagic intra-abdominal processes.
  • Peritoneal irritation generates localised tenderness, guarding, and pain on movement.
  • Progression to sepsis, hypovolaemia, or tissue necrosis causes systemic instability and organ dysfunction.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Acute Abdomen"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Pain onset may be abrupt or evolving, often with nausea, vomiting, or anorexia.
  • Progressive phase: Worsening pain, peritonism, and systemic signs suggest advancing pathology requiring urgent intervention.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Acute Abdomen"][/sc]
  • Ask about pain onset and migration - Pattern and site progression help prioritise likely surgical causes.
  • Ask about vomiting, bowel change, and flatus passage - These features help identify obstruction or ileus physiology.
  • Ask about fever or rigors - Systemic inflammatory symptoms raise concern for infection or perforation.
  • Ask about bleeding symptoms - Haematemesis, melaena, rectal bleeding, or vaginal bleeding may indicate high-risk pathology.
  • Ask about urinary and gynaecological symptoms - Dysuria, urinary retention, pregnancy risk, or pelvic pain may redirect differential diagnosis.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Acute Abdomen"][/sc]
  • Check haemodynamic status - Tachycardia, hypotension, or poor perfusion suggests shock or major inflammatory burden.
  • Check for peritonism - Guarding, rebound tenderness, or percussion tenderness indicates peritoneal irritation.
  • Check abdominal distension and bowel sounds - Distension with altered bowel activity supports obstructive or ileus causes.
  • Check focused pelvic and hernia findings where relevant - Targeted examination can reveal surgically important sources.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Acute Abdomen"][/sc]
  • Sudden severe pain with collapse or syncope - Suggests catastrophic vascular or haemorrhagic pathology.
  • Shock, altered mental state, or persistent hypotension - Indicates life-threatening sepsis, bleeding, or ischaemia.
  • Rigid abdomen with severe tenderness - Strongly suggests peritonitis from perforation or advanced intra-abdominal infection.
  • Ongoing gastrointestinal or vaginal bleeding - Requires urgent source identification and resuscitative management.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Acute Abdomen"][/sc]
  • Blunted pain response: Older, immunosuppressed, or neuropathic patients may have severe disease with limited focal tenderness.
  • Predominant systemic upset: Confusion, tachypnoea, or functional decline may dominate before classic abdominal signs emerge.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Acute Abdomen"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Urgent observations, venous blood tests including inflammatory and metabolic profile, lactate, blood group and save or crossmatch, urinalysis, and pregnancy test where relevant.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Early imaging guided by suspected pathology, usually CT abdomen and pelvis in unstable or unclear cases and ultrasound for biliary, pelvic, or aneurysmal assessment; serial examination and repeat observations to detect deterioration.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Acute Abdomen"][/sc]
  • A 45-year-old woman presents with 12 hours of escalating abdominal pain, vomiting, fever, and focal peritonism on examination.
  • Adult with rapidly progressive abdominal pain plus systemic instability and peritoneal signs, concerning for high-risk acute intra-abdominal pathology.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Acute Abdomen"][/sc]
  • Appendicitis - Migratory right iliac fossa pain with localised peritonism is a classic pattern.
  • Perforated peptic ulcer - Abrupt epigastric pain with generalised peritonism and free intraperitoneal air supports perforation.
  • Acute bowel obstruction - Colicky pain, distension, vomiting, and obstipation suggest obstructive pathology.
  • Acute pancreatitis - Severe epigastric pain radiating to the back with pancreatic biochemical elevation is typical.
  • Mesenteric ischaemia - Severe pain out of proportion to early findings with systemic deterioration suggests vascular emergency.
  • Ruptured ectopic pregnancy - Pelvic pain with haemodynamic compromise and positive pregnancy context indicates gynaecological emergency.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Acute Abdomen"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Resuscitate with airway, breathing, and circulation support, provide analgesia, establish intravenous access, and involve senior surgical and emergency teams early.
  • Core care: Definitive management is cause-specific and may include urgent operative, endoscopic, interventional radiology, or targeted medical treatment.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Frequent reassessment of pain, observations, fluid balance, lactate trend, and examination findings is required to detect progression.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Any haemodynamic decline, worsening peritonism, bleeding, or organ dysfunction should trigger immediate escalation to higher acuity care and urgent source control planning.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Acute Abdomen"][/sc]
  • Sepsis and septic shock: Uncontrolled intra-abdominal infection can rapidly progress to circulatory failure.
  • Peritonitis and multiorgan dysfunction: Advanced inflammatory insult may lead to respiratory, renal, and cardiovascular compromise.
  • Adhesive and functional sequelae: Surgical or inflammatory abdominal pathology can lead to future bowel dysfunction and recurrent admissions.
