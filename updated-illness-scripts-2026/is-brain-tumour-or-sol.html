Brain Tumour or Space-Occupying Lesion (SOL)
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Brain Tumour or Space-Occupying Lesion (SOL)"][/sc]
  • A brain tumour or space-occupying lesion (SOL) refers to an abnormal growth within the cranial cavity, which may be benign or malignant.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise brain features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.

Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Brain Tumour or Space-Occupying Lesion (SOL)"][/sc]
  • Brain tumours can originate from various types of cells within the brain (e.g., astrocytes, oligodendrocytes), or from metastatic cancer spread. These abnormal growths disrupt normal brain function through direct invasion, compression of surrounding structures, and increasing intracranial pressure (ICP). Tumours can block CSF pathways, leading to hydrocephalus. Over time, increased ICP and herniation syndromes can develop, which are life-threatening.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Brain Tumour or Space-Occupying Lesion (SOL)"][/sc]
  • Brain tumours may present over weeks to months with slowly progressive symptoms, but malignant tumours may cause more rapid deterioration. The time course is influenced by tumour type, location, and growth rate. Sudden neurological decline can occur with haemorrhage into a tumour or acute hydrocephalus.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Brain Tumour or Space-Occupying Lesion (SOL)"][/sc]
  • Ask about headache - Often a dull, persistent headache, worse in the morning or with changes in posture, due to increased intracranial pressure.
  • Ask about nausea and vomiting - Commonly related to increased ICP affecting the vomiting centre in the medulla.
  • Ask about seizures - Tumours, particularly in the cortical regions, can trigger abnormal electrical discharges, leading to focal or generalised seizures.
  • Ask about focal neurological deficits - Symptoms such as weakness, sensory loss, or visual disturbances depend on the tumour's location in the brain.
  • Ask about cognitive or personality changes - Frontal lobe tumours may cause changes in behaviour, mood, or cognition due to disruption of higher cognitive functions.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Brain Tumour or Space-Occupying Lesion (SOL)"][/sc]
  • Check papilloedema - Swelling of the optic disc seen on fundoscopy, indicating raised ICP.
  • Check focal neurological signs - Depending on tumour location, there may be hemiparesis, aphasia, visual field defects, or cranial nerve palsies.
  • Check altered consciousness - Advanced cases with raised ICP or brainstem involvement can lead to a reduced level of consciousness.
  • Check gait ataxia - Tumours in the cerebellum can lead to uncoordinated movements and balance issues.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Brain Tumour or Space-Occupying Lesion (SOL)"][/sc]
  • Sudden onset of severe headache - May indicate tumour haemorrhage or hydrocephalus.
  • Rapidly worsening neurological deficits - Suggests aggressive tumour growth or mass effect leading to herniation.
  • Seizures in an adult without prior history - Often indicates a new lesion in the brain, such as a tumour.
  • Progressive visual loss - Suggests involvement of the optic pathways or raised ICP.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Brain Tumour or Space-Occupying Lesion (SOL)"][/sc]
  • Subtle cognitive changes - Slow-growing tumours may initially present with vague symptoms like memory loss or personality changes, which may be misdiagnosed as depression or dementia.
  • Chronic headache - Some patients with benign tumours present with a longstanding, non-specific headache without focal neurological signs.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features or comorbidity can mask the underlying diagnosis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Brain Tumour or Space-Occupying Lesion (SOL)"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: MRI of the brain - Preferred imaging modality, showing detailed tumour characteristics such as size, location, and relation to brain structures.
  • Targeted and monitoring: CSF analysis (lumbar puncture) - In cases of suspected malignancy or infection. Elevated protein and pleocytosis may suggest neoplastic or infectious.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Brain Tumour or Space-Occupying Lesion (SOL)"][/sc]
  • Stroke - Typically has a more sudden onset compared to the gradual progression of brain tumours.
  • Subdural haematoma - May present with similar symptoms, especially in the elderly, but often has a history.
  • Brain abscess - More common in patients with a history of infection or immunosuppression, often accompanied by.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Brain Tumour or Space-Occupying Lesion (SOL)"][/sc]
  • Seizures - Occur in up to 30% of brain tumour patients, particularly those with cortical involvement.
  • Hydrocephalus - Obstruction of CSF flow can lead to ventricular enlargement and increased ICP.
  • Brain herniation - Life-threatening complication caused by raised ICP leading to compression of vital brain structures.
  • Neurological deficits - Permanent focal deficits may remain depending on the tumour location and degree of resection.
