Atrial Fibrillation (AF)
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Atrial Fibrillation (AF)"][/sc]
  • Atrial fibrillation (AF) is a common cardiac arrhythmia characterised by an irregular and often rapid heart rhythm, originating from chaotic electrical activity in the atria.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise atrial features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Atrial Fibrillation (AF)"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Atrial Fibrillation (AF)"][/sc]
  • AF is more common with advancing age, particularly in those over 65 years, and is more prevalent in men than in women.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Atrial Fibrillation (AF)"][/sc]
  • Atrial fibrillation occurs when there is disorganised electrical activity in the atria, leading to ineffective atrial contractions.
  • Normally, the sinoatrial (SA) node initiates organised electrical impulses. In AF, abnormal ectopic foci, often originating from the pulmonary veins, cause rapid and irregular atrial depolarisations.
  • The atrioventricular (AV) node filters these irregular impulses but allows an irregular ventricular response, leading to an irregular pulse and potential haemodynamic instability.
  • Over time, AF can cause structural remodelling of the atria, including fibrosis, which further perpetuates the arrhythmia.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Atrial Fibrillation (AF)"][/sc]
  • Paroxysmal AF: Episodes last less than 7 days (usually <48 hours) and resolve spontaneously.
  • Persistent AF: Lasts more than 7 days, requiring medical intervention to restore normal rhythm.
  • Permanent AF: Continuous AF where attempts to restore normal rhythm have failed or not been attempted.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Atrial Fibrillation (AF)"][/sc]
  • Ask about palpitations - Sensation of a rapid, irregular heartbeat due to chaotic atrial and ventricular contractions.
  • Ask about fatigue - Inefficient cardiac output leads to reduced oxygen delivery to tissues.
  • Ask about dyspnoea - Increased atrial pressure and subsequent pulmonary congestion can cause breathlessness.
  • Ask about chest discomfort - Reduced coronary perfusion during AF can result in chest pain, especially in those with underlying coronary artery disease.
  • Ask about dizziness or syncope - Irregular ventricular response may cause reduced cerebral perfusion, leading to light-headedness or fainting.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Atrial Fibrillation (AF)"][/sc]
  • Check irregularly irregular pulse - The hallmark of AF, caused by chaotic electrical impulses resulting in an irregular ventricular response.
  • Check tachycardia - A rapid heart rate is common, particularly in uncontrolled AF, due to excessive ventricular responses to atrial impulses.
  • Check hypotension - Reduced cardiac output may cause low blood pressure, especially in those with pre-existing heart disease.
  • Check signs of heart failure - Peripheral oedema, raised jugular venous pressure (JVP), and crackles on lung auscultation may indicate AF-related heart failure.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Atrial Fibrillation (AF)"][/sc]
  • Syncope - May suggest haemodynamic instability due to rapid ventricular rates or associated underlying structural heart disease.
  • Severe chest pain - Could indicate underlying myocardial ischaemia due to coronary artery disease or AF-related rapid heart rates.
  • Acute heart failure - Signs of worsening heart failure (e.g., marked dyspnoea, pulmonary oedema) may be precipitated by AF.
  • Neurological deficit (stroke) - AF significantly increases the risk of embolic stroke due to clot formation in the atria.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Atrial Fibrillation (AF)"][/sc]
  • Silent AF - Some patients may present without overt symptoms but are diagnosed during routine screening or after a complication like stroke.
  • Vague symptoms - Fatigue or subtle cognitive decline may be the only presenting feature in elderly patients.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features or comorbidity can mask the underlying diagnosis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Atrial Fibrillation (AF)"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: ECG - Diagnostic test showing absent P waves and an irregularly irregular rhythm; 24-hour Holter monitor - Useful for capturing intermittent.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Echocardiogram - To assess atrial size, valvular disease, and cardiac function; atrial enlargement may be seen in chronic AF; Blood tests.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Atrial Fibrillation (AF)"][/sc]
  • Atrial flutter - Similar symptoms but ECG shows "sawtooth" flutter waves rather than chaotic atrial activity.
  • Supraventricular tachycardia (SVT) - Regular rapid heart rate, unlike the irregular rate seen in AF.
  • Ventricular tachycardia - Broad complex tachycardia on ECG, typically seen in patients with structural heart disease.
  • Sinus tachycardia - Regular tachycardia with identifiable P waves, often caused by anxiety or dehydration.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Atrial Fibrillation (AF)"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Assess physiological stability and urgent complications at presentation, and initiate early stabilisation where required.
  • Core care: Deliver condition-specific treatment for atrial fibrillation (af), address underlying drivers, and involve appropriate specialist support.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Reassess symptom trajectory, objective markers, and treatment response over time to detect complications early.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Worsening symptoms, physiological deterioration, or failure to improve should trigger urgent reassessment for severe or alternative pathology in atrial fibrillation (af).
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Atrial Fibrillation (AF)"][/sc]
  • Stroke - AF increases the risk of thromboembolic events, particularly stroke, due to blood stasis in the left atrium.
  • Heart failure - Persistent AF can lead to reduced cardiac output and heart failure, particularly in patients with underlying structural heart disease.
  • Tachycardia-induced cardiomyopathy - Sustained rapid heart rates can weaken the heart muscle over time.
