Septal Haematoma
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Septal Haematoma"][/sc]
  • A septal haematoma is a collection of blood within the nasal septum, typically between the cartilage or bone and the perichondrium/periosteum.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise septal features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Septal Haematoma"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Septal Haematoma"][/sc]
  • Septal haematoma is relatively uncommon. It occurs more frequently in the context of nasal trauma, such as from sports injuries, accidents, or physical altercations.
  • It is more common in children and young adults due to the softer cartilage and less developed vascular network compared to adults.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Septal Haematoma"][/sc]
  • Septal haematomas form when trauma disrupts blood vessels within the septum. Blood accumulates between the septal cartilage and the perichondrium or periosteum, separating the cartilage from its blood supply.
  • The avascular cartilage of the septum relies on diffusion from the perichondrium for nutrients and oxygen. When separated, the cartilage undergoes necrosis if not revascularised promptly.
  • Untreated haematomas may become infected, leading to abscess formation, and eventually cause structural collapse of the septum, resulting in a "saddle nose" deformity.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Septal Haematoma"][/sc]
  • Immediate: Following trauma, a septal haematoma may form within minutes to hours, resulting in nasal obstruction and pain.
  • 24-48 hours: If untreated, the haematoma can enlarge, worsen the obstruction, and increase the risk of infection.
  • 48-72 hours: At this stage, necrosis of the septal cartilage may begin, potentially leading to long-term complications such as nasal deformity.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Septal Haematoma"][/sc]
  • Ask about nasal obstruction: The haematoma causes swelling, which can block the nasal passage. This occurs as blood accumulates, compressing the airway.
  • Ask about nasal pain: Pain occurs due to the pressure of the haematoma on surrounding tissues, particularly when the septal mucosa is stretched.
  • Ask about nasal congestion: Patients may feel a fullness or blocked sensation due to the blood-filled septum preventing normal airflow.
  • Ask about difficulty breathing through the nose: The obstruction from the haematoma reduces airflow, leading to difficulty breathing.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Septal Haematoma"][/sc]
  • Check visible swelling of the septum: Inspection often reveals bilateral or unilateral swelling of the septum, typically bluish or reddish in colour. This indicates blood.
  • Check fluctuant mass on palpation: On examination, a soft, fluctuant swelling can be palpated along the septum, confirming the presence of a fluid collection (blood).
  • Check nasal asymmetry: As the haematoma expands, it may cause visible distortion of the external nose or asymmetry of the nostrils.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Septal Haematoma"][/sc]
  • Fever: Suggests infection, possibly progressing to a septal abscess, which could lead to more serious complications if untreated, such as cavernous sinus thrombosis.
  • Purulent nasal discharge: Indicates secondary bacterial infection. If accompanied by fever, it may signify an abscess.
  • Severe pain with erythema: May suggest the development of an abscess or impending cartilage necrosis.
  • Nasal deformity (saddle nose): A late-stage sign indicating cartilage necrosis and collapse due to untreated or infected septal haematoma.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Septal Haematoma"][/sc]
  • In some cases, particularly in children, a septal haematoma may present with minimal symptoms, such as mild nasal obstruction or discomfort, making it easy to overlook without careful examination.
  • Atypical presentations may also include delayed symptoms, where nasal obstruction becomes noticeable only after the haematoma enlarges or infection sets, with associated clinical features.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask septal haematoma.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Septal Haematoma"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Anterior rhinoscopy: This is the primary diagnostic tool. It reveals a bulging, swollen septum. Fluctuance on palpation further supports the diagnosis.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Nasal endoscopy: May be used for more detailed visualisation, particularly if the haematoma is difficult to view on anterior rhinoscopy; CT.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Septal Haematoma"][/sc]
  • Nasal fracture - May also present with nasal pain and obstruction. However, fractures typically involve visible deformity.
  • Septal abscess - Can be a progression from an untreated haematoma. Signs of infection, such as fever.
  • Nasal polyps - Distinguish from septal haematoma using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Septal Haematoma"][/sc]
  • Septal abscess: An untreated haematoma can become infected, forming an abscess. This can lead to cartilage destruction and necrosis.
  • Saddle nose deformity: Necrosis of the septal cartilage leads to collapse of the nasal structure, resulting in a characteristic depression of the nasal bridge.
  • Cavernous sinus thrombosis: Although rare, untreated infections can spread, leading to serious complications such as cavernous sinus thrombosis, which can be life-threatening.
