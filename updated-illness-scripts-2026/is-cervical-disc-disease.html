Cervical Disc Disease
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Cervical Disc Disease"][/sc]
  • Cervical disc disease refers to degenerative changes affecting the intervertebral discs in the cervical spine, which can lead to disc herniation, spinal canal narrowing (stenosis), and nerve root compression.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise cervical features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.

  • Higher-risk or missed groups: Cervical Disc Disease may be missed when presentations are atypical or comorbidity obscures key features.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Cervical Disc Disease"][/sc]
  • Degenerative changes start with disc desiccation, where the nucleus pulposus loses water content, leading to reduced disc height and increased pressure on the annulus fibrosus. Microtears in the annulus can lead to herniation of disc material.
  • As the disc degenerates, osteophyte formation (bone spurs) occurs around the vertebral bodies. These bony changes can narrow the spinal canal (stenosis) or compress adjacent nerve roots (radiculopathy).
  • Chronic compression of the spinal cord may lead to cervical myelopathy, causing motor and sensory deficits.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Cervical Disc Disease"][/sc]
  • The condition typically progresses over years with an insidious onset. Initial symptoms may be mild and intermittent neck pain, which worsens over time as nerve or spinal cord involvement increases.
  • Acute exacerbations can occur with disc herniation or trauma, leading to sudden onset radiculopathy or myelopathy.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Cervical Disc Disease"][/sc]
  • Ask about neck pain - Caused by inflammation or mechanical pressure on cervical discs and surrounding structures due to degeneration.
  • Ask about radicular pain - Sharp, shooting pain radiating down the arm (cervical radiculopathy) due to nerve root compression.
  • Ask about numbness/paraesthesia - Resulting from nerve root irritation or compression, leading to sensory disturbance in the distribution of the affected nerve root.
  • Ask about weakness - Muscle weakness in the arm or hand, often due to chronic nerve compression affecting motor function.
  • Ask about gait instability - In cases of cervical myelopathy, spinal cord compression can cause lower extremity weakness and difficulty walking.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Cervical Disc Disease"][/sc]
  • Check reduced cervical range of motion - Stiffness due to degeneration and pain from disc involvement.
  • Check positive Spurling's test - Neck extension and lateral bending may reproduce radicular symptoms, suggesting nerve root compression.
  • Check weakness in upper limb muscles - Correlates with nerve root compression (e.g., C5-C6 disc herniation may cause deltoid weakness).
  • Check hyperreflexia - Increased reflexes in the legs due to upper motor neuron involvement in cervical myelopathy.
  • Check hoffman’s sign - A positive test indicates spinal cord involvement in myelopathy.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Cervical Disc Disease"][/sc]
  • Progressive limb weakness - May indicate severe nerve compression or cord damage (cervical myelopathy), requiring urgent intervention to prevent permanent disability.
  • Bowel/bladder dysfunction - Can suggest severe spinal cord compression, an emergency (cauda equina syndrome in lumbar spine but similarly critical in cervical myelopathy).
  • Unrelenting pain, especially at night - Could indicate malignancy, infection, or fracture, necessitating further urgent investigation.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Cervical Disc Disease"][/sc]
  • Asymptomatic cases - Some individuals may have cervical disc degeneration without any symptoms, discovered incidentally on imaging.
  • Isolated shoulder pain - May be mistaken for a shoulder pathology but is caused by referred pain from cervical nerve root compression.
  • Chronic headache - Cervicogenic headaches can be caused by degenerative changes in the upper cervical spine.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features or comorbidity can mask the underlying diagnosis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Cervical Disc Disease"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Plain radiographs (X-ray) - Can show disc space narrowing, osteophyte formation, and vertebral alignment abnormalities; MRI - Gold standard for visualising.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Targeted investigations to confirm diagnosis, assess complications, and monitor response in cervical disc disease.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Cervical Disc Disease"][/sc]
  • A 52-year-old man presents with 3 months of neck pain radiating to the left arm, associated with paraesthesia in the thumb and index finger, and worsening arm weakness. Symptoms worsen.
  • Adult with symptom pattern and objective findings suggestive of cervical disc disease, requiring focused confirmation and risk stratification.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Cervical Disc Disease"][/sc]
  • Shoulder impingement syndrome - Differentiate from cervical disc disease using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.
  • Carpal tunnel syndrome - Compression of the median nerve causes hand symptoms but not arm or neck pain.
  • Multiple sclerosis - May present with similar neurological findings but is usually associated with other systemic signs.
  • Alternative serious diagnosis - Distinguishing history, examination, and targeted investigations do not support cervical disc disease.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Cervical Disc Disease"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Assess physiological stability and urgent complications at presentation, and initiate early stabilisation where required.
  • Core care: Deliver condition-specific treatment for cervical disc disease, address underlying drivers, and involve appropriate specialist support.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Reassess symptom trajectory, objective markers, and treatment response over time to detect complications early.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Worsening symptoms, physiological deterioration, or failure to improve should trigger urgent reassessment for severe or alternative pathology in cervical disc disease.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Cervical Disc Disease"][/sc]
  • Chronic pain - Can develop due to ongoing nerve compression or central sensitisation.
  • Permanent neurological deficits - Occur in cases of untreated or severe myelopathy, leading to long-term disability.
