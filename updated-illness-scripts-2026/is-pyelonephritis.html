Pyelonephritis
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Pyelonephritis"][/sc]
  • Pyelonephritis is an acute, bacterial infection of the renal pelvis and kidney, commonly resulting from ascending urinary tract infection (UTI) that has spread from the bladder to the upper urinary.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise pyelonephritis features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.

Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Pyelonephritis"][/sc]
  • Most cases of pyelonephritis are caused by ascending infection, where uropathogenic bacteria (e.g., *E. coli*) enter the bladder and migrate to the kidneys. Factors like vesicoureteral reflux, obstruction, or immune suppression can facilitate this progression.
  • Bacterial adherence to urothelial cells triggers local inflammation and cytokine release, causing tissue oedema and inflammatory cell infiltration, leading to kidney damage if untreated.
  • Severe cases may cause renal abscesses or bacteraemia due to systemic spread.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Pyelonephritis"][/sc]
  • Acute phase: Symptoms typically develop rapidly over hours to a few days, with fever, flank pain, and urinary symptoms.
  • Resolution phase: With appropriate treatment, symptoms generally resolve within 7–14 days.
  • Complicated cases: In cases of delayed treatment, infection may progress, leading to abscess formation or systemic complications requiring longer treatment courses.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Pyelonephritis"][/sc]
  • Ask about fever - Systemic inflammatory response due to bacterial infection, often accompanied by chills.
  • Ask about flank pain - Localised inflammation of renal parenchyma causes referred pain to the flank.
  • Ask about dysuria and frequency - Indicates concurrent lower UTI, as infection often ascends from the bladder.
  • Ask about nausea and vomiting - Systemic response to severe infection or pyrexia.
  • Ask about malaise and fatigue - General signs of systemic infection and inflammatory response.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Pyelonephritis"][/sc]
  • Check costovertebral angle tenderness (CVA tenderness) - Localised tenderness due to inflammation of the kidney capsule.
  • Check fever >38°C - Indicator of systemic infection, often with rapid onset in pyelonephritis.
  • Check tachycardia - Common in response to fever and infection-induced dehydration.
  • Check hypotension (in severe cases) - May occur if the infection is severe and associated with sepsis.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Pyelonephritis"][/sc]
  • Hypotension - Suggestive of sepsis or septic shock; indicates urgent need for fluid resuscitation and antibiotics.
  • Persistent high fever despite antibiotics - May indicate abscess formation or resistant organisms.
  • Severe flank pain unresponsive to treatment - May signify renal abscess or obstruction.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Pyelonephritis"][/sc]
  • Elderly patients - May present with confusion, weakness, or functional decline rather than classic symptoms.
  • Immunocompromised patients - More likely to have milder symptoms or afebrile presentations despite severe infection.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask pyelonephritis.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Pyelonephritis"][/sc]
  • Acute cystitis - Lacks systemic symptoms (fever, flank pain) but presents with dysuria and frequency.
  • Renal colic - Severe flank pain without fever; haematuria often present without infection.
  • Appendicitis - Fever and right lower quadrant pain but without dysuria; location of pain differs.
  • Pelvic inflammatory disease - May have fever and abdominal pain but is associated with vaginal discharge rather than.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Pyelonephritis"][/sc]
  • Renal abscess - May develop if infection localises and progresses despite treatment.
  • Sepsis and septic shock - Resulting from bacteria spreading into the bloodstream; potentially life-threatening.
  • Chronic pyelonephritis - Recurrent infections may lead to renal scarring, hypertension, and chronic kidney disease.
