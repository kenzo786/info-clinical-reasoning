Fibromyalgia
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Fibromyalgia"][/sc]
  • Fibromyalgia is a chronic disorder characterised by widespread musculoskeletal pain, often accompanied by fatigue, sleep disturbances, cognitive issues (referred to as "fibro fog"), and a heightened sensitivity to pressure.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise fibromyalgia features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.

Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Fibromyalgia"][/sc]
  • The primary mechanism underlying fibromyalgia is central sensitisation. Abnormalities in neurotransmitters, particularly an imbalance between excitatory (e.g., glutamate) and inhibitory (e.g., serotonin, noradrenaline) signals, lead to heightened pain perception. There is also evidence of abnormal hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal (HPA) axis function, with blunted cortisol responses to stress, contributing to fatigue and cognitive symptoms.
  • Peripheral pain signals from muscles and soft tissues may also be exaggerated, although no consistent structural abnormalities are identified in these tissues.
  • Genetic predisposition and environmental triggers (such as physical trauma, infection, or emotional stress) may contribute to the development and persistence of the condition.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Fibromyalgia"][/sc]
  • Fibromyalgia is a chronic condition, often developing insidiously over months to years. Symptoms tend to wax and wane but rarely resolve completely. Episodes of symptom exacerbation may be triggered by physical or psychological stressors.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Fibromyalgia"][/sc]
  • Ask about widespread pain - Generalised musculoskeletal pain, typically described as aching or burning, often worse in areas of pressure. This is linked to central sensitisation.
  • Ask about fatigue - Persistent tiredness not relieved by rest. This may be due to disrupted sleep architecture (reduced restorative sleep) and abnormal HPA axis function.
  • Ask about sleep disturbances - Non-refreshing sleep, frequent awakenings, and difficulty falling asleep, often associated with pain and altered neurotransmitter regulation.
  • Ask about cognitive difficulties ("fibro fog") - Memory issues, difficulty concentrating, and slowed thought processes are common. These are thought to be related to neurotransmitter imbalances.
  • Ask about headaches - Often tension-type or migraine headaches, possibly related to increased pain sensitivity and abnormal muscle tension.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Fibromyalgia"][/sc]
  • Check allodynia - Pain from stimuli that are not typically painful, such as light pressure. This reflects the central sensitisation process, where non-painful stimuli are.
  • Check tender points - Discrete areas of tenderness, often located at specific anatomical sites (e.g., neck, shoulders, elbows, hips, and knees). These are indicative, with associated clinical features.
  • Check absence of synovitis or objective joint swelling - Despite complaints of joint pain, fibromyalgia does not cause inflammatory joint changes, differentiating it from conditions.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Fibromyalgia"][/sc]
  • Weight loss - May suggest malignancy or systemic disease rather than fibromyalgia.
  • Fever - Unexplained fevers are uncommon in fibromyalgia and may indicate infection or an autoimmune condition.
  • Focal neurological deficits - Fibromyalgia does not cause neurological deficits (e.g., weakness, numbness), which may point to multiple sclerosis, stroke, or other neurological conditions.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Fibromyalgia"][/sc]
  • Isolated cognitive dysfunction ("fibro fog") - In some cases, patients may primarily present with cognitive issues, leading to possible misdiagnosis as dementia or psychiatric conditions.
  • Localized pain - While fibromyalgia usually causes widespread pain, some individuals may present with pain in a limited number of body regions, potentially leading to confusion with regional pain syndromes.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask fibromyalgia.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Fibromyalgia"][/sc]
  • Chronic disability - Persistent pain and fatigue can lead to significant impairment in daily functioning and employment.
  • Psychiatric comorbidities - Depression and anxiety are common due to the chronic nature of fibromyalgia and the associated social and functional limitations.
