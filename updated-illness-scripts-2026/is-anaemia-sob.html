Anaemia (Shortness of Breath - SOB)
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Anaemia (Shortness of Breath - SOB)"][/sc]
  • Anaemia causing shortness of breath reflects reduced oxygen-carrying capacity leading to exertional dyspnoea and reduced functional reserve.
  • UKMLA essentials: exertional breathlessness with pallor, heart failure or hypoxia concern, confirm anaemia on FBC.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Anaemia (Shortness of Breath - SOB)"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Anaemia (Shortness of Breath - SOB)"][/sc]
  • Risk factors: Risk of anaemia (shortness of breath - sob) rises with relevant predisposition, comorbidity, and exposure profile.
  • Higher-risk or missed groups: Anaemia (Shortness of Breath - SOB) may be missed when presentations are atypical or comorbidity obscures key features.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Anaemia (Shortness of Breath - SOB)"][/sc]
  • Reduced haemoglobin production: Causes include iron deficiency, vitamin B12/folate deficiency, and chronic diseases impairing erythropoiesis.
  • Increased haemoglobin loss: Due to bleeding (e.g., gastrointestinal, menstrual, trauma).
  • Increased haemolysis: Conditions such as haemolytic anaemias result in accelerated red blood cell destruction.
  • Impaired oxygen transport: Reduced haemoglobin leads to hypoxia, triggering compensatory mechanisms like increased cardiac output and respiratory rate.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Anaemia (Shortness of Breath - SOB)"][/sc]
  • Acute: Rapid onset, often due to bleeding or haemolysis, leading to symptoms like sudden fatigue and dyspnoea.
  • Chronic: Gradual onset over weeks to months, commonly seen in nutritional deficiencies or chronic diseases, with slower compensatory adjustments by the body.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Anaemia (Shortness of Breath - SOB)"][/sc]
  • Ask about fatigue: Caused by tissue hypoxia reducing energy production.
  • Ask about shortness of breath (SOB): Exertional dyspnoea occurs due to insufficient oxygen delivery during physical activity.
  • Ask about palpitations: Compensatory tachycardia increases cardiac output to maintain oxygen delivery.
  • Ask about pallor: Reduced haemoglobin levels result in pale skin and mucous membranes.
  • Ask about dizziness: Hypoperfusion to the brain due to anaemia-induced hypoxia.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Anaemia (Shortness of Breath - SOB)"][/sc]
  • Check pallor: Observed in skin and conjunctiva due to decreased haemoglobin concentration.
  • Check increased heart rate: A compensatory mechanism to improve oxygen delivery to tissues.
  • Check systolic flow murmur: High-output state from decreased blood viscosity in severe anaemia.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Anaemia (Shortness of Breath - SOB)"][/sc]
  • Haematemesis or melena: Suggests significant gastrointestinal bleeding.
  • Unexplained weight loss: May indicate malignancy-related anaemia.
  • Neurological symptoms (e.g., paraesthesia, ataxia): Seen in vitamin B12 deficiency anaemia.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Anaemia (Shortness of Breath - SOB)"][/sc]
  • Asymptomatic: Common in mild anaemia, especially in chronic cases where compensatory mechanisms develop gradually.
  • Heart failure exacerbation: In patients with comorbidities, anaemia may worsen pre-existing conditions.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features or comorbidity can mask the underlying diagnosis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Anaemia (Shortness of Breath - SOB)"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Full blood count with indices, observations, oxygen saturation, and cardiorespiratory examination to assess severity and alternative breathlessness causes.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Iron studies, B12/folate, reticulocyte count, renal profile, and chest or cardiac investigations when dyspnoea appears disproportionate to anaemia severity.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Anaemia (Shortness of Breath - SOB)"][/sc]
  • A 65-year-old woman has 3 months of exertional breathlessness, fatigue, and pallor, with microcytic hypochromic anaemia on blood tests.
  • Adult with chronic dyspnoea and biochemical anaemia, requiring concurrent evaluation of anaemia subtype and cardiorespiratory contributors.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Anaemia (Shortness of Breath - SOB)"][/sc]
  • Heart failure - Orthopnoea, oedema, raised JVP, and cardiac imaging abnormalities suggest primary cardiac pump dysfunction.
  • Chronic obstructive pulmonary disease - Chronic cough, wheeze, smoking history, and obstructive spirometry support COPD.
  • Pulmonary embolism - Pleuritic pain, tachycardia, and acute onset dyspnoea with thromboembolic risk profile favour PE.
  • Deconditioning or anxiety-related breathlessness - Breathlessness without physiological markers of anaemia severity or cardiopulmonary disease supports functional causes.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Anaemia (Shortness of Breath - SOB)"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Assess for hypoxia, haemodynamic compromise, and concurrent acute cardiopulmonary pathology in patients presenting with anaemia-related breathlessness.
  • Core care: Treat the anaemia cause while optimising coexisting cardiorespiratory disease that may amplify dyspnoea burden.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Reassess haemoglobin, exertional symptoms, and functional capacity to confirm improvement and refine management.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Escalate urgently for resting breathlessness, chest pain, syncope, hypoxia, or clinical deterioration despite initial treatment.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Anaemia (Shortness of Breath - SOB)"][/sc]
  • Heart failure: Chronic anaemia increases cardiac workload.
  • Impaired quality of life: Persistent fatigue and reduced functional capacity.
