Cervical Lymphadenopathy
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Cervical Lymphadenopathy"][/sc]
  • Cervical lymphadenopathy refers to the enlargement of lymph nodes in the cervical region (neck).
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise cervical features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Cervical Lymphadenopathy"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Cervical Lymphadenopathy"][/sc]
  • In UK general practice, cervical lymphadenopathy is common, with most cases due to benign infections.
  • In hospital settings, malignancies and systemic diseases are more prevalent causes.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Cervical Lymphadenopathy"][/sc]
  • Infections: Pathogens trigger lymph node immune responses, leading to swelling from inflammatory cell infiltration.
  • Malignancy: Lymph nodes enlarge due to infiltration by malignant cells or reactive hyperplasia.
  • Systemic diseases (e.g., autoimmune conditions): Cytokine-driven lymphoid hyperplasia results in generalised or localised lymph node enlargement.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Cervical Lymphadenopathy"][/sc]
  • Acute: Typically infectious, resolves within days to weeks (e.g., viral URTI).
  • Subacute: Develops over weeks to months; may indicate chronic infections (e.g., tuberculosis) or malignancies.
  • Chronic: Persistent for months, often suggestive of malignancy or systemic diseases.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Cervical Lymphadenopathy"][/sc]
  • Ask about painful lymph nodes - Often due to infections causing inflammation and tenderness.
  • Ask about fever - Indicative of systemic infections or inflammatory response.
  • Ask about weight loss - Seen in malignancy or chronic infections due to increased metabolic demand.
  • Ask about night sweats - Associated with tuberculosis, lymphoma, or systemic infections.
  • Ask about local symptoms - Such as sore throat in infectious mononucleosis.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Cervical Lymphadenopathy"][/sc]
  • Check enlarged lymph nodes - Palpable nodes larger than 1 cm, often firm or rubbery.
  • Check erythema overlying nodes - Suggests infection or inflammation.
  • Check fixed, hard nodes - Often indicative of malignancy.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Cervical Lymphadenopathy"][/sc]
  • Unexplained weight loss - Suggests malignancy or systemic disease.
  • Night sweats - Strongly indicative of lymphoma or tuberculosis.
  • Firm, fixed nodes - Malignancy suspicion.
  • Supraclavicular lymphadenopathy - High likelihood of malignancy.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Cervical Lymphadenopathy"][/sc]
  • Isolated painless enlargement - May be overlooked, especially in slow-growing malignancies.
  • Systemic symptoms without significant local findings - Seen in conditions like sarcoidosis or early lymphoma.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features or comorbidity can mask the underlying diagnosis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Cervical Lymphadenopathy"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Focused bedside assessment and first-line investigations to define immediate risk in suspected cervical lymphadenopathy.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Ultrasound - Identifies node characteristics (e.g., malignant nodes are hypoechoic with loss of hilum); Biopsy - Definitive diagnosis for suspected malignancies.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Cervical Lymphadenopathy"][/sc]
  • A 35-year-old male presents with a 6-week history of painless neck swelling, intermittent fever, and night sweats. Examination reveals firm, non-tender lymph nodes in the posterior triangle.
  • Adult with symptom pattern and objective findings suggestive of cervical lymphadenopathy, requiring focused confirmation and risk stratification.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Cervical Lymphadenopathy"][/sc]
  • Infectious mononucleosis - Typically painful lymph nodes, fever, sore throat.
  • Tuberculosis - Chronic course, systemic symptoms, matted nodes.
  • Lymphoma - Firm, rubbery nodes, systemic symptoms, often painless.
  • Metastatic malignancy - Fixed nodes, possible primary cancer symptoms.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Cervical Lymphadenopathy"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Assess physiological stability and urgent complications at presentation, and initiate early stabilisation where required.
  • Core care: Deliver condition-specific treatment for cervical lymphadenopathy, address underlying drivers, and involve appropriate specialist support.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Reassess symptom trajectory, objective markers, and treatment response over time to detect complications early.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Worsening symptoms, physiological deterioration, or failure to improve should trigger urgent reassessment for severe or alternative pathology in cervical lymphadenopathy.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Cervical Lymphadenopathy"][/sc]
  • Suppuration - Seen in bacterial infections, may require drainage.
  • Systemic spread of malignancy - Progression to distant metastases.
  • Secondary infections - Chronic lymphadenopathy may predispose to superimposed infections.
