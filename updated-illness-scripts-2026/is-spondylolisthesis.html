Spondylolisthesis
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Spondylolisthesis"][/sc]
  • Spondylolisthesis is a condition where one vertebra slips anteriorly over the one below it, most commonly at the lumbosacral junction (L4/L5 or L5/S1 levels).
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise spondylolisthesis features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Spondylolisthesis"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Spondylolisthesis"][/sc]
  • Gender: Females are more commonly affected by degenerative spondylolisthesis, especially after menopause.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Spondylolisthesis"][/sc]
  • Spondylolisthesis occurs due to structural weakness or instability in the vertebrae, allowing one vertebra to slip forward. This instability can be caused by congenital defects in the pars interarticularis (isthmic type), degenerative changes affecting spinal support structures, trauma, or pathological conditions like osteoporosis.
  • Degenerative spondylolisthesis involves progressive wear on intervertebral discs and facet joints, leading to weakened spinal stability. As the supportive tissues deteriorate, forward slippage may occur, especially under mechanical stress.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Spondylolisthesis"][/sc]
  • Acute onset in traumatic cases or progressive development over years in degenerative cases. Symptoms may start as mild and worsen with increased vertebral slippage and nerve compression.
  • In adolescents, progression often halts after growth plate closure, while degenerative cases in adults can gradually worsen over time.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Spondylolisthesis"][/sc]
  • Ask about lower back pain - Most common symptom, typically worse with standing and movement. Caused by instability and mechanical strain on lumbar structures.
  • Ask about radicular pain - Pain radiating down the leg due to nerve root compression, commonly at the L5 or S1 roots.
  • Ask about leg weakness or numbness - From nerve root impingement, especially in more advanced cases.
  • Ask about difficulty with movement - Increased spinal instability may limit physical activity and mobility.
  • Ask about asymptomatic - Mild cases may present without symptoms and are often found incidentally on imaging.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Spondylolisthesis"][/sc]
  • Check palpable "step-off" - A prominent step or dip felt over the affected vertebrae, indicative of slippage.
  • Check reduced lumbar range of motion - Limited flexion or extension due to pain and instability.
  • Check neurological deficits - Weakness or sensory changes, particularly in the distribution of affected nerve roots.
  • Check altered gait - In severe cases, instability and pain may lead to an antalgic gait.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Spondylolisthesis"][/sc]
  • Severe, progressive neurological deficits - May indicate nerve root or cauda equina compression.
  • Incontinence or saddle anaesthesia - Suggests cauda equina syndrome, a surgical emergency.
  • Unexplained weight loss or night pain - Raises suspicion for malignancy or infection.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Spondylolisthesis"][/sc]
  • Isolated thigh pain - Without back pain, especially in younger patients, can be misdiagnosed as hip pathology.
  • Bilateral symptoms without trauma - May be initially mistaken for inflammatory conditions.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask spondylolisthesis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Spondylolisthesis"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: X-rays - Lumbar spine lateral view shows vertebral slippage and is the primary diagnostic tool.
  • Targeted and monitoring: MRI - Assesses nerve root compression, spinal stenosis, and soft tissue structures; CT scan - Provides detailed imaging for surgical planning.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Spondylolisthesis"][/sc]
  • Spinal stenosis - Similar radicular symptoms but without vertebral slippage on imaging.
  • Herniated disc - Often more acute onset, with radicular pain localised to a specific nerve root.
  • Degenerative disc disease - Causes back pain without slippage, identified on MRI or X-ray.
  • Malignancy - Consider in cases with night pain, weight loss, or neurological deficits beyond typical spondylolisthesis.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Spondylolisthesis"][/sc]
  • Cauda equina syndrome - A rare but serious complication from severe nerve compression, leading to incontinence and paralysis.
  • Chronic pain and disability - In untreated or advanced cases, prolonged instability may lead to permanent nerve damage and functional impairment.
