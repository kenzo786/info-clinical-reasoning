Breast Abscess
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Breast Abscess"][/sc]
  • A breast abscess is a localised collection of pus within the breast tissue, typically resulting from bacterial infection.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise breast features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Breast Abscess"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Breast Abscess"][/sc]
  • Risk factors: Risk of breast abscess rises with relevant predisposition, comorbidity, and exposure profile.
  • Higher-risk or missed groups: Breast Abscess may be missed when presentations are atypical or comorbidity obscures key features.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Breast Abscess"][/sc]
  • Breast abscesses often start with ductal obstruction, leading to milk stasis and subsequent bacterial infection, usually by Staphylococcus aureus. Infection triggers an inflammatory response, causing localised tissue necrosis and pus formation. In non-lactating women, abscesses can arise from periductal mastitis, often linked to smoking or nipple piercings.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Breast Abscess"][/sc]
  • Initial phase: Begins with ductal blockage and mastitis, progressing to abscess formation over several days if untreated.
  • Acute phase: Rapid development of localised pain, swelling, and erythema within days to a week.
  • Chronic phase: If untreated, may persist, with possible fibrosis and scarring. Some abscesses may resolve with antibiotic therapy alone, while others may require drainage.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Breast Abscess"][/sc]
  • Ask about localised breast pain - Caused by inflammation and pressure from pus accumulation within the tissue.
  • Ask about swelling - Results from localised oedema and pus collection due to the immune response and infection.
  • Ask about warmth - Increased blood flow to the infected area leads to localised warmth.
  • Ask about fever and malaise - Systemic symptoms result from the body’s inflammatory response to infection.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Breast Abscess"][/sc]
  • Check erythema - Redness over the affected area due to increased blood flow and inflammation.
  • Check fluctuant mass - A soft, compressible mass indicates the presence of pus.
  • Check fever - A systemic response to infection, indicating the presence of an active infection.
  • Check axillary lymphadenopathy - Lymph node enlargement occurs as part of the immune response to infection.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Breast Abscess"][/sc]
  • Skin necrosis - Suggests severe infection with potential for tissue damage.
  • Rapidly expanding erythema - May indicate cellulitis or severe abscess formation requiring urgent intervention.
  • Persistent fever post-treatment - Suggests inadequate treatment or underlying resistant organisms.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Breast Abscess"][/sc]
  • Non-lactational breast abscess in smokers - Presents with chronic symptoms and may mimic periductal mastitis.
  • Painless abscess - Occasionally, the abscess may not cause significant pain, particularly in immunocompromised individuals.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features or comorbidity can mask the underlying diagnosis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Breast Abscess"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Ultrasound - Identifies fluid collection and guides drainage; shows a hypoechoic area with irregular margins; Aspiration with culture - Confirms diagnosis.
  • Targeted and monitoring: FBC - Elevated WBCs indicate infection; CRP - Often elevated, showing an inflammatory response.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Breast Abscess"][/sc]
  • A 30-year-old lactating woman with a 3-day history of localised, painful swelling in the breast, fever, and erythema. Examination reveals a fluctuant, erythematous mass, with a diagnosis confirmed by ultrasound-guided.
  • Adult with symptom pattern and objective findings suggestive of breast abscess, requiring focused confirmation and risk stratification.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Breast Abscess"][/sc]
  • Mastitis - Often presents similarly but lacks fluctuant mass; responds to antibiotics alone without drainage.
  • Galactocele - Milk-filled cyst without signs of infection; typically not erythematous or febrile.
  • Inflammatory breast cancer - Chronic erythema and oedema without fever; peau d'orange appearance on skin.
  • Alternative serious diagnosis - Distinguishing history, examination, and targeted investigations do not support breast abscess.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Breast Abscess"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Assess physiological stability and urgent complications at presentation, and initiate early stabilisation where required.
  • Core care: Deliver condition-specific treatment for breast abscess, address underlying drivers, and involve appropriate specialist support.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Reassess symptom trajectory, objective markers, and treatment response over time to detect complications early.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Worsening symptoms, physiological deterioration, or failure to improve should trigger urgent reassessment for severe or alternative pathology in breast abscess.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Breast Abscess"][/sc]
  • Recurrence - Common if underlying ductal issues are unresolved.
  • Sepsis - Rare but serious; spread of infection into the bloodstream may occur.
  • Scarring - Can lead to cosmetic deformity or fibrosis within the breast tissue.
