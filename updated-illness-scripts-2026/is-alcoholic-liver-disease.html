Alcoholic Liver Disease (ALD)
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Alcoholic Liver Disease (ALD)"][/sc]
  • Alcoholic liver disease is progressive hepatic injury from harmful alcohol exposure, ranging from steatosis to hepatitis, fibrosis, cirrhosis, and decompensation.
  • UKMLA essentials: harmful alcohol exposure, jaundice or ascites decompensation features, stage disease with LFTs and ultrasound.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Alcoholic Liver Disease (ALD)"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Alcoholic Liver Disease (ALD)"][/sc]
  • Risk factors: Risk of alcoholic liver disease (ald) rises with relevant predisposition, comorbidity, and exposure profile.
  • Higher-risk or missed groups: Alcoholic Liver Disease (ALD) may be missed when presentations are atypical or comorbidity obscures key features.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Alcoholic Liver Disease (ALD)"][/sc]
  • Chronic alcohol consumption leads to hepatocyte injury through oxidative stress, direct toxicity of acetaldehyde (alcohol metabolite), and disruption of lipid metabolism, resulting in fatty liver (steatosis).
  • In alcoholic hepatitis, continued injury triggers an inflammatory response with immune cell infiltration and cytokine release, causing necrosis and fibrosis.
  • Over time, fibrosis advances to cirrhosis, characterised by nodular regeneration and severe scarring, impairing liver function, blood flow, and ultimately causing portal hypertension.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Alcoholic Liver Disease (ALD)"][/sc]
  • Steatosis: May develop within weeks of heavy drinking but is usually reversible if alcohol intake is reduced.
  • Alcoholic Hepatitis: Typically manifests after years of heavy alcohol use and can be life-threatening if severe.
  • Cirrhosis: Develops over years to decades of alcohol abuse, representing end-stage liver disease with poor prognosis if drinking continues.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Alcoholic Liver Disease (ALD)"][/sc]
  • Ask about fatigue - Common due to reduced liver function, metabolic disturbances, and systemic inflammation.
  • Ask about abdominal Pain - Typically in the right upper quadrant; inflammation and hepatomegaly exert pressure on the liver capsule.
  • Ask about nausea and Anorexia - Results from impaired metabolism and liver dysfunction affecting appetite.
  • Ask about jaundice - Caused by hyperbilirubinaemia due to impaired bile excretion and liver cell injury.
  • Ask about ascites - Fluid accumulation in the peritoneal cavity from portal hypertension and hypoalbuminaemia in cirrhosis.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Alcoholic Liver Disease (ALD)"][/sc]
  • Check hepatomegaly - Common early finding due to fatty infiltration or inflammation.
  • Check spider Naevi - Hyper-oestrogenism secondary to liver dysfunction causing vascular changes.
  • Check palmar Erythema - Increased oestrogen leads to capillary dilation.
  • Check jaundice - Yellowing of skin/sclera due to elevated bilirubin levels from impaired liver function.
  • Check ascites - Visible abdominal distension from fluid accumulation due to portal hypertension and hypoalbuminaemia.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Alcoholic Liver Disease (ALD)"][/sc]
  • Hepatic Encephalopathy - Confusion, tremors, and coma from ammonia toxicity; indicates severe liver failure.
  • Gastrointestinal Bleeding - Haematemesis or melaena due to variceal rupture in portal hypertension; life-threatening complication.
  • Severe Jaundice - Indicates significant liver dysfunction and potential acute decompensation.
  • Rapid Ascites Accumulation - Could indicate portal hypertension or spontaneous bacterial peritonitis, a severe infection risk.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Alcoholic Liver Disease (ALD)"][/sc]
  • Silent or Mild Presentation - Especially in early stages, patients may lack obvious symptoms until advanced liver damage has occurred.
  • Neuropsychiatric Symptoms - Depression, memory impairment due to subclinical hepatic encephalopathy without marked liver failure signs.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features or comorbidity can mask the underlying diagnosis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Alcoholic Liver Disease (ALD)"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: LFTs, albumin, clotting profile, bilirubin, FBC, U&Es, and clinical examination for jaundice, ascites, encephalopathy, and other stigmata of chronic liver disease.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Liver ultrasound for steatosis, cirrhosis, portal-hypertension signs, and ascites; transient elastography for fibrosis staging; endoscopy when variceal bleeding risk is suspected.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Alcoholic Liver Disease (ALD)"][/sc]
  • A 55-year-old man with long-term heavy alcohol intake presents with jaundice, abdominal distension, and fatigue, with abnormal synthetic liver function on blood tests.
  • Adult with chronic harmful alcohol exposure and clinical plus biochemical evidence of hepatic dysfunction, concerning for alcoholic liver disease.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Alcoholic Liver Disease (ALD)"][/sc]
  • Metabolic dysfunction-associated steatotic liver disease - Similar liver-enzyme abnormalities occur with metabolic risk factors and without harmful alcohol intake.
  • Viral hepatitis - Positive viral serology and exposure risk profile support infective hepatic inflammation rather than alcohol-driven disease.
  • Haemochromatosis - Iron overload markers and family history with multisystem features favour hereditary iron-storage disease.
  • Autoimmune hepatitis - Autoantibody pattern and immunoglobulin elevation in an autoimmune phenotype suggest autoimmune liver inflammation.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Alcoholic Liver Disease (ALD)"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Assess for decompensation, alcohol withdrawal risk, sepsis, and gastrointestinal bleeding, with early haemodynamic stabilisation where needed.
  • Core care: Support alcohol abstinence, provide nutritional rehabilitation including vitamin support, and treat specific complications of cirrhosis or alcoholic hepatitis.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Serial liver function, synthetic markers, renal function, and complication surveillance should guide ongoing hepatology and addiction follow-up.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Worsening jaundice, ascites, encephalopathy, haematemesis, sepsis, or renal deterioration should trigger urgent specialist reassessment.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Alcoholic Liver Disease (ALD)"][/sc]
  • Portal Hypertension: Common in cirrhosis, leading to ascites, splenomegaly, and varices due to increased resistance in the portal venous system.
  • Hepatic Encephalopathy: Cognitive decline and coma in severe liver failure from ammonia and other toxin accumulation.
  • Hepatocellular Carcinoma (HCC): Risk increases in cirrhotic patients; regular surveillance recommended in advanced cases.
  • Renal Failure: Known as hepatorenal syndrome; poor prognosis as liver function declines further.
