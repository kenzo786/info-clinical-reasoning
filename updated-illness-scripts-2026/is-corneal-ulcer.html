Corneal Ulcer
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Corneal Ulcer"][/sc]
  • A corneal ulcer is a full-thickness epithelial defect of the cornea accompanied by underlying stromal inflammation.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise corneal features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Corneal Ulcer"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Corneal Ulcer"][/sc]
  • Risk factors: Risk of corneal ulcer rises with relevant predisposition, comorbidity, and exposure profile.
  • Higher-risk or missed groups: Corneal Ulcer may be missed when presentations are atypical or comorbidity obscures key features.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Corneal Ulcer"][/sc]
  • Initial injury to the corneal epithelium (e.g., trauma, contact lens use) allows microorganisms to penetrate.
  • Infective agents trigger a local immune response, causing inflammation of the corneal stroma.
  • Enzymatic degradation by neutrophils and microbial toxins leads to stromal thinning and necrosis.
  • Corneal oedema, hypopyon formation, and neovascularisation can develop as part of the inflammatory response.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Corneal Ulcer"][/sc]
  • Acute onset: Symptoms typically develop within 24-48 hours of epithelial damage.
  • Progressive phase: Corneal thinning and ulceration may worsen over days without intervention.
  • Resolution phase: With treatment, symptoms improve over weeks, though scarring and visual deficits may persist.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Corneal Ulcer"][/sc]
  • Ask about severe eye pain - Due to exposure of corneal nerves and inflammatory mediators.
  • Ask about redness - Caused by hyperaemia of conjunctival and episcleral vessels.
  • Ask about photophobia - Increased sensitivity to light due to inflammation affecting corneal transparency.
  • Ask about blurred vision - Corneal oedema and ulceration disrupt light refraction.
  • Ask about foreign body sensation - Resulting from epithelial damage.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Corneal Ulcer"][/sc]
  • Check corneal opacity - Stromal infiltration and necrosis lead to a visible white or grey opacity.
  • Check hypopyon - Layering of white cells in the anterior chamber due to severe inflammation.
  • Check conjunctival injection - Engorgement of conjunctival blood vessels near the limbus.
  • Check corneal ulceration on slit-lamp examination - Visible epithelial defect with fluorescein staining.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Corneal Ulcer"][/sc]
  • Hypopyon - Indicates severe infection and risk of corneal perforation.
  • Rapidly worsening symptoms - Suggest aggressive microbial pathogens (e.g., Pseudomonas aeruginosa).
  • Corneal perforation - Can lead to endophthalmitis or permanent blindness.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Corneal Ulcer"][/sc]
  • Minimal pain in neurotrophic keratitis - Reduced corneal sensation masks symptoms.
  • Slow progression in fungal infections - May delay recognition and treatment.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features or comorbidity can mask the underlying diagnosis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Corneal Ulcer"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Slit-lamp examination with fluorescein staining - Reveals epithelial defects and stromal involvement.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Corneal scraping for microbiological culture - Identifies causative organisms and guides antimicrobial therapy; Anterior chamber tap - Performed if endophthalmitis is.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Corneal Ulcer"][/sc]
  • A 35-year-old contact lens wearer presents with acute onset of severe left eye pain, redness, blurred vision, and photophobia for 24 hours.
  • Adult with symptom pattern and objective findings suggestive of corneal ulcer, requiring focused confirmation and risk stratification.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Corneal Ulcer"][/sc]
  • Conjunctivitis - Differentiated by lack of significant pain and absence of corneal involvement.
  • Herpes simplex keratitis - Dendritic ulcer pattern seen on fluorescein staining.
  • Acute anterior uveitis - Associated with photophobia and hypopyon but lacks epithelial defects.
  • Alternative serious diagnosis - Distinguishing history, examination, and targeted investigations do not support corneal ulcer.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Corneal Ulcer"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Assess physiological stability and urgent complications at presentation, and initiate early stabilisation where required.
  • Core care: Deliver condition-specific treatment for corneal ulcer, address underlying drivers, and involve appropriate specialist support.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Reassess symptom trajectory, objective markers, and treatment response over time to detect complications early.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Worsening symptoms, physiological deterioration, or failure to improve should trigger urgent reassessment for severe or alternative pathology in corneal ulcer.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Corneal Ulcer"][/sc]
  • Corneal scarring - Permanent visual impairment.
  • Perforation - Leads to endophthalmitis or need for emergency surgery.
  • Glaucoma - Due to increased intraocular pressure from chronic inflammation.
