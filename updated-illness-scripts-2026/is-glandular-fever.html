Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Glandular fever"][/sc]

Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Glandular fever"][/sc]
  • The Epstein-Barr virus primarily infects B lymphocytes in the oropharynx, leading to their proliferation and an intense immune response. Cytotoxic T cells are activated to control the infection, leading to widespread lymphoid tissue involvement, including lymphadenopathy, splenomegaly, and sometimes hepatitis.
  • Viral replication in the B lymphocytes leads to the production of atypical lymphocytes, which can be detected in blood tests.
  • The immune response and viral shedding in the throat result in the characteristic sore throat, while systemic viral effects cause fever and fatigue.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Glandular fever"][/sc]
  • Incubation period: 4-6 weeks.
  • Acute phase: 1-2 weeks, characterised by severe sore throat, fever, and lymphadenopathy.
  • Convalescent phase: 2-6 weeks, where symptoms gradually resolve, but fatigue may persist for months in some individuals.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Glandular fever"][/sc]
  • Ask about sore throat - Due to viral replication and immune response in the oropharyngeal epithelium.
  • Ask about fever - Caused by systemic immune activation and cytokine release in response to viral infection.
  • Ask about fatigue - A result of both the systemic immune response and direct viral effects on the body.
  • Ask about headache - Related to the systemic inflammatory response and cytokine release.
  • Ask about myalgia - Due to cytokine-mediated inflammation and muscle involvement during the acute infection phase.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Glandular fever"][/sc]
  • Check cervical lymphadenopathy - Enlarged lymph nodes due to immune activation and viral replication in lymphoid tissue.
  • Check tonsillar enlargement and exudate - Reflects viral infection in the tonsils and local immune response.
  • Check splenomegaly - Occurs due to viral proliferation in the spleen and increased immune cell activity.
  • Check hepatomegaly - Viral infection can cause liver inflammation, resulting in an enlarged liver.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Glandular fever"][/sc]
  • Severe abdominal pain - May indicate splenic rupture, a rare but serious complication.
  • Severe jaundice - Suggests significant liver involvement or secondary hepatitis.
  • Breathing difficulties or stridor - May result from tonsillar hypertrophy causing airway obstruction.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Glandular fever"][/sc]
  • Asymptomatic infection is common, especially in children under the age of 10, where the infection may not produce the typical signs and symptoms.
  • Older adults may present with more nonspecific symptoms, such as prolonged fatigue and mild liver enzyme abnormalities without classic lymphadenopathy or sore throat.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask glandular fever illness script.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Glandular fever"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Monospot test - Detects heterophile antibodies; positive in 85% of cases after the first week of symptoms; EBV serology - IgM.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Full blood count - Raised white cell count with lymphocytosis and >10% atypical lymphocytes; Liver function tests - Mildly elevated ALT.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Glandular fever"][/sc]
  • Streptococcal pharyngitis - Similar sore throat and lymphadenopathy, but rapid onset and no splenomegaly or atypical lymphocytes.
  • Cytomegalovirus (CMV) infection - Presents with similar symptoms but is more likely in immunocompromised individuals and usually negative.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Glandular fever"][/sc]
  • Splenic rupture - Rare but life-threatening complication; occurs in approximately 0.1-0.5% of cases due to splenomegaly.
  • Autoimmune haemolytic anaemia - Occurs in up to 3% of cases, mediated by cold agglutinins (IgM).
  • Thrombocytopenia - Due to immune-mediated destruction of platelets, may lead to increased bleeding risk.
  • Chronic fatigue - Prolonged fatigue occurs in 10-20% of individuals, lasting for months post-infection.
