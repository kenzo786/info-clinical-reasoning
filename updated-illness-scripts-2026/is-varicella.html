Varicella (Chickenpox)
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Varicella (Chickenpox)"][/sc]
  • Varicella, commonly known as chickenpox, is an acute, highly contagious viral infection caused by the varicella-zoster virus (VZV).
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise varicella features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Varicella (Chickenpox)"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Varicella (Chickenpox)"][/sc]
  • Globally, varicella is prevalent where VZV vaccination is not routine, affecting about 60 million people annually.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Varicella (Chickenpox)"][/sc]
  • Varicella is caused by primary infection with the varicella-zoster virus (VZV), a DNA virus of the herpesvirus family.
  • After entering the respiratory tract, VZV replicates locally and spreads to lymph nodes and bloodstream (primary viraemia).
  • Through secondary viraemia, VZV disseminates to the skin, infecting epithelial cells, which results in characteristic vesicular lesions.
  • After primary infection, the virus establishes latency in dorsal root ganglia, where it may reactivate later as herpes zoster (shingles).
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Varicella (Chickenpox)"][/sc]
  • Incubation Period: 10–21 days post-exposure.
  • Prodromal Phase: 1–2 days of fever, malaise, and general discomfort before the rash appears, particularly in older children and adults.
  • Acute Rash Phase: Vesicular rash develops and progresses over 4–7 days, with lesions appearing in successive waves.
  • Resolution Phase: Lesions crust over and heal within 1–2 weeks, with potential for post-infectious scarring.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Varicella (Chickenpox)"][/sc]
  • Ask about fever - Initial fever (up to 39°C) is due to systemic immune response triggered by viral replication and secondary viraemia.
  • Ask about malaise and Fatigue - Common due to generalised immune response and inflammatory cytokine release.
  • Ask about pruritic Rash - Begins as red macules that evolve into vesicles; itching is due to immune-mediated skin inflammation.
  • Ask about headache - Often mild, resulting from systemic inflammatory response.
  • Ask about anorexia - Reduced appetite may result from systemic immune activation and fever.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Varicella (Chickenpox)"][/sc]
  • Check vesicular Rash - Highly characteristic; lesions appear in clusters, progressing from macules to vesicles, then crusting. Lesions are seen in various stages due, with associated clinical features.
  • Check generalised Lymphadenopathy - Often present in children; lymph node enlargement results from immune response in lymphoid tissues.
  • Check fever - Observable particularly in early stages and often peaks as the rash develops, associated with immune response to VZV infection.
  • Check scarring - In some cases, post-infectious scars develop, especially if lesions were excoriated or infected.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Varicella (Chickenpox)"][/sc]
  • Respiratory Distress - Can signify varicella pneumonia, more common in adults and immunocompromised individuals.
  • Severe Abdominal Pain - May indicate hepatic involvement or acute varicella hepatitis, especially in immunocompromised patients.
  • Neurological Symptoms (e.g., Ataxia, Confusion) - Possible signs of VZV encephalitis or cerebellitis, requiring urgent intervention.
  • Persistent High Fever (>5 Days) - May suggest secondary bacterial infection or complications like pneumonia.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Varicella (Chickenpox)"][/sc]
  • Asymptomatic or Mild Disease - May occur, especially in vaccinated individuals who develop modified varicella with milder symptoms and fewer lesions.
  • Haemorrhagic Varicella - Rare but severe; associated with bleeding in vesicles and may lead to disseminated intravascular coagulation.
  • Adult Presentation - Typically more severe than in children, with higher rates of complications such as pneumonia and hepatitis.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask varicella (chickenpox).
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Varicella (Chickenpox)"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Polymerase Chain Reaction (PCR) of Vesicle Fluid or Swab - Highly specific; detects VZV DNA and confirms diagnosis.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Targeted investigations to confirm diagnosis, assess complications, and monitor response in varicella (chickenpox).

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Varicella (Chickenpox)"][/sc]
  • Hand, Foot, and Mouth Disease - Presents with vesicles primarily on palms, soles, and inside the mouth; usually no generalised.
  • Impetigo - Superficial bacterial infection; lesions are often honey-coloured and crusted without systemic symptoms.
  • Herpes Simplex Virus (HSV) Infection - Typically localised to the oral or genital area, not a generalised rash.
  • Related alternative diagnosis - Distinguish from varicella (chickenpox) using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Varicella (Chickenpox)"][/sc]
  • Secondary Bacterial Infection - Common in children, especially with excoriated lesions; may lead to cellulitis or invasive infection.
  • Varicella Pneumonia - More common in adults, particularly smokers and immunocompromised; can be severe with high mortality.
  • Encephalitis and Cerebellitis - Rare but serious complications, potentially leading to seizures, ataxia, and long-term neurological deficits.
  • Reactivation as Herpes Zoster - Virus remains latent in dorsal root ganglia; reactivation typically occurs later in life.
