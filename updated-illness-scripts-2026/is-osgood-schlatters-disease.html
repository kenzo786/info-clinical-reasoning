Osgood-Schlatter's Disease
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Osgood-Schlatter's Disease"][/sc]
  • Osgood-Schlatter's disease is an overuse injury involving inflammation of the patellar tendon insertion on the tibial tuberosity.

Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Osgood-Schlatter's Disease"][/sc]
  • In Osgood-Schlatter's disease, repetitive strain from activities involving jumping, running, or squatting pulls the patellar tendon against the tibial tuberosity.
  • This traction causes microfractures and inflammation at the growth plate, leading to pain and swelling.
  • During growth spurts, increased growth plate vulnerability, combined with intensified physical activity, can exacerbate these microinjuries.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Osgood-Schlatter's Disease"][/sc]
  • Acute Phase: Often lasts weeks to months, with pain exacerbated by physical activity.
  • Subacute Phase: Pain may decrease but often recurs with intensive activity.
  • Resolution Phase: Usually self-limiting within 12-24 months as the growth plate matures.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Osgood-Schlatter's Disease"][/sc]
  • Ask about anterior knee pain - Caused by patellar tendon tension on the tibial tuberosity, worsening with activity.
  • Ask about localized swelling - Due to inflammation at the insertion site.
  • Ask about stiffness after activity - Secondary to inflammation and muscle tightness around the knee.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Osgood-Schlatter's Disease"][/sc]
  • Check tenderness over tibial tuberosity - Indicative of localized inflammation.
  • Check swelling or prominence of the tibial tuberosity - Reflects chronic inflammation and potential bone overgrowth.
  • Check pain with resisted knee extension - Indicates stress on the patellar tendon insertion.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Osgood-Schlatter's Disease"][/sc]
  • Severe, unrelenting pain or night pain - May suggest alternative diagnoses such as tumours.
  • Fever or systemic symptoms - Could indicate infection rather than typical inflammatory pathology.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Osgood-Schlatter's Disease"][/sc]
  • Symptoms in younger children or atypical locations - Rare but may suggest variations in physical activity or individual anatomical differences.
  • Persistent symptoms into adulthood - May indicate residual bone prominence or long-term tendonopathy.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask osgood-schlatter's disease.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Osgood-Schlatter's Disease"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: X-ray - May show tibial tuberosity fragmentation or enlargement.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Ultrasound - Used to assess tendon thickening and bursitis at the insertion site; MRI - Rarely used, but can show more.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Osgood-Schlatter's Disease"][/sc]
  • Patellar tendonitis - Differs in tenderness location, usually just below the patella.
  • Sinding-Larsen-Johansson syndrome - Pain is localised at the inferior patellar pole, often in younger children.
  • Tumours (e.g., osteosarcoma) - Typically involve unrelenting pain, systemic signs, or night pain.
  • Related alternative diagnosis - Distinguish from osgood-schlatter's disease using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Osgood-Schlatter's Disease"][/sc]
  • Tibial tuberosity prominence - May persist into adulthood as a residual bump.
  • Patellar tendonitis - Chronic inflammation can lead to patellar tendon thickening and potential tendonitis.
  • Restricted knee movement - Rare, from muscle tightness or tendon pathology.
