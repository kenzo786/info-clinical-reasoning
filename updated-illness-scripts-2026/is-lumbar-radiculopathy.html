Lumbar Radiculopathy
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Lumbar Radiculopathy"][/sc]
  • Lumbar radiculopathy refers to the compression or irritation of one or more spinal nerve roots in the lumbar spine, often resulting in pain, weakness, or sensory changes in the lower.

Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Lumbar Radiculopathy"][/sc]
  • The underlying cause is typically nerve root compression due to herniated discs, spinal stenosis, or degenerative changes. This compression causes inflammation and disrupts normal neural signalling, leading to pain along the nerve distribution, often aggravated by mechanical stress on the spine.
  • Intervertebral disc herniation: When the nucleus pulposus protrudes through the annulus fibrosis, it may compress nerve roots.
  • Spinal stenosis: Narrowing of the spinal canal or neural foramina can cause compression, especially with age-related degenerative changes.
  • Osteophytes (bone spurs) can also impinge nerve roots, leading to radicular symptoms.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Lumbar Radiculopathy"][/sc]
  • Acute phase: Symptoms often present suddenly due to acute disc herniation or injury, with severe pain lasting days to weeks.
  • Subacute phase: Symptoms may persist but often improve within 4-6 weeks as inflammation decreases.
  • Chronic phase: In cases where compression persists, symptoms may continue for months, requiring further intervention or management.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Lumbar Radiculopathy"][/sc]
  • Ask about low back pain - Often the initial symptom, arising from local inflammation and tissue irritation.
  • Ask about radiating leg pain - The hallmark of lumbar radiculopathy; caused by nerve root irritation or compression, following the dermatomal pattern.
  • Ask about paresthesia - Tingling or numbness in the leg or foot due to disrupted nerve function.
  • Ask about muscle weakness - Particularly in the muscles innervated by the affected nerve root, due to impaired motor signalling.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Lumbar Radiculopathy"][/sc]
  • Check positive straight leg raise (SLR) test - Elicits pain in cases of nerve root compression, commonly affecting L5 or S1.
  • Check sensory deficits - Loss of sensation or abnormal sensations in the dermatome associated with the compressed nerve root.
  • Check reduced reflexes - Especially at the knee (L4) or ankle (S1), reflecting neural pathway disruption.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Lumbar Radiculopathy"][/sc]
  • Severe or progressive weakness - May indicate significant nerve compression or cauda equina syndrome, requiring urgent evaluation.
  • Loss of bowel or bladder control - Suggestive of cauda equina syndrome, a neurosurgical emergency due to compression of multiple lumbar and sacral nerve roots.
  • Unexplained weight loss, fever, or history of malignancy - May raise concern for malignancy or infection as a source of radiculopathy.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Lumbar Radiculopathy"][/sc]
  • Isolated hip or knee pain - May be perceived instead of true radicular pain, leading to misdiagnosis as a joint issue.
  • Intermittent symptoms - Symptoms may appear only with specific activities or postures, complicating the diagnosis.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask lumbar radiculopathy.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Lumbar Radiculopathy"][/sc]
  • Peripheral neuropathy - Usually bilateral and progressive, not dermatomal in distribution.
  • Spinal stenosis - Presents with claudication and typically bilateral symptoms, worsened with walking and relieved by sitting.
  • Hip pathology (e.g., osteoarthritis) - Pain typically localised to the hip joint and does not follow a dermatomal pattern.
  • Related alternative diagnosis - Distinguish from lumbar radiculopathy using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Lumbar Radiculopathy"][/sc]
  • Chronic pain - Long-term radiculopathy can lead to persistent pain and disability, affecting quality of life.
  • Muscle atrophy - Prolonged nerve compression can lead to irreversible weakness and muscle wasting.
  • Cauda equina syndrome - Although rare, severe compression may lead to this emergency with potential permanent deficits if untreated.
