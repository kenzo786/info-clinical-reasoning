Myelodysplastic Syndrome (MDS)
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Myelodysplastic Syndrome (MDS)"][/sc]
  • Myelodysplastic syndrome (MDS) refers to a group of clonal haematopoietic disorders characterised by ineffective haematopoiesis, leading to peripheral blood cytopenias and an increased risk of progression to acute myeloid leukaemia.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise myelodysplastic features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.

Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Myelodysplastic Syndrome (MDS)"][/sc]
  • MDS is caused by acquired mutations in haematopoietic stem cells, leading to ineffective haematopoiesis. These mutations result in abnormal differentiation and maturation of blood cells in the bone marrow, producing dysplastic cells. Dysplastic cells either die prematurely (intramedullary apoptosis) or survive but are dysfunctional, causing cytopenias. The risk of progression to AML arises from the accumulation of additional mutations that drive uncontrolled proliferation.
  • The abnormal clones replace normal haematopoietic cells, which accounts for the cytopenias observed in peripheral blood.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Myelodysplastic Syndrome (MDS)"][/sc]
  • MDS usually has an indolent, slowly progressive course, though some patients may remain stable for years. Over time, the disease may evolve to acute myeloid leukaemia (AML) in around 20-30% of cases. The disease is typically diagnosed in the later stages due to non-specific symptoms.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Myelodysplastic Syndrome (MDS)"][/sc]
  • Ask about fatigue - Resulting from anaemia due to ineffective erythropoiesis and low haemoglobin levels.
  • Ask about recurrent infections - Due to neutropenia, which impairs the immune system's ability to fight infections.
  • Ask about easy bruising or bleeding - Due to thrombocytopenia, leading to impaired clot formation.
  • Ask about asymptomatic - Many patients, particularly in early stages, may present asymptomatically and are diagnosed incidentally on routine blood tests.
  • Ask about shortness of breath - Related to anaemia and reduced oxygen-carrying capacity of the blood.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Myelodysplastic Syndrome (MDS)"][/sc]
  • Check pallor - Due to anaemia and reduced red blood cell count.
  • Check petechiae or purpura - These are signs of thrombocytopenia and manifest as small red or purple spots on the skin.
  • Check fever or signs of infection - Suggestive of neutropenia, particularly when infections are recurrent or severe.
  • Check splenomegaly - In some cases, extramedullary haematopoiesis can cause splenic enlargement, although this is less common in MDS than other myeloproliferative disorders.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Myelodysplastic Syndrome (MDS)"][/sc]
  • Severe neutropenia - Increases the risk of life-threatening infections.
  • Rapidly worsening cytopenias - May indicate transformation to acute myeloid leukaemia (AML).
  • Unexplained weight loss or night sweats - Can signify disease progression or transformation to AML.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Myelodysplastic Syndrome (MDS)"][/sc]
  • Asymptomatic with normal blood counts - Some patients may present with only mild blood abnormalities, detected incidentally on routine screening.
  • Unexplained infections without significant cytopenias - Occasionally, infections may occur even in patients without marked neutropenia due to qualitative defects in neutrophils.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask myelodysplastic syndrome (mds).
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Myelodysplastic Syndrome (MDS)"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Full Blood Count (FBC) - Shows cytopenias, particularly anaemia (normocytic or macrocytic), neutropenia, and thrombocytopenia; Peripheral Blood Smear - Demonstrates dysplastic.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Bone Marrow Biopsy - Reveals hypercellularity with dysplastic changes in myeloid, erythroid, and megakaryocytic lineages; Cytogenetic Analysis - Detects chromosomal abnormalities.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Myelodysplastic Syndrome (MDS)"][/sc]
  • Aplastic Anaemia - Pancytopenia with hypocellular bone marrow, but without the dysplastic changes seen in MDS.
  • Acute Myeloid Leukaemia (AML) - Distinguish from myelodysplastic syndrome (mds) using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.
  • Megaloblastic Anaemia - Can cause macrocytic anaemia, but is due to vitamin B12/folate deficiency rather than bone.
  • Related alternative diagnosis - Distinguish from myelodysplastic syndrome (mds) using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Myelodysplastic Syndrome (MDS)"][/sc]
  • Progressive marrow failure: Worsening cytopenias increase risk of fatigue, bleeding, and infection-related complications.
  • Transfusion burden and iron overload: Repeated transfusions can cause organ iron deposition and added treatment complexity.
  • Transformation risk: Some disease subtypes can evolve into acute myeloid leukaemia over time.
