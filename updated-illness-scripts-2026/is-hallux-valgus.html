Hallux Valgus
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Hallux Valgus"][/sc]
  • Hallux valgus is a deformity of the first metatarsophalangeal (MTP) joint where the great toe deviates laterally, often accompanied by medial prominence of the first metatarsal head, forming a bunion.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise hallux features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.

Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Hallux Valgus"][/sc]
  • Hallux valgus develops due to biomechanical imbalance at the MTP joint, leading to lateral deviation of the great toe.
  • The medial collateral ligament and capsule stretch, while lateral structures tighten.
  • Contributing factors include genetic predisposition, footwear (narrow or high-heeled shoes), and underlying conditions such as rheumatoid arthritis.
  • Progression exacerbates joint instability and can lead to secondary osteoarthritis.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Hallux Valgus"][/sc]
  • Insidious onset, typically worsening over years due to cumulative mechanical stress and joint instability.
  • Early stages may be asymptomatic with mild cosmetic deformity.
  • In advanced stages, pain, inflammation, and functional limitations predominate.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Hallux Valgus"][/sc]
  • Ask about pain around the MTP joint - Caused by inflammation or pressure from footwear exacerbating deformity.
  • Ask about difficulty finding comfortable footwear - Due to medial prominence of the bunion and lateral deviation of the toe.
  • Ask about decreased range of motion - Stiffness secondary to chronic joint changes or secondary osteoarthritis.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Hallux Valgus"][/sc]
  • Check medial bunion prominence - Formed by the exposed medial metatarsal head due to lateral deviation of the great toe.
  • Check hallux lateral deviation - Angulation greater than 15° indicates significant deformity.
  • Check callus formation - Results from friction or pressure at points of abnormal weight-bearing.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Hallux Valgus"][/sc]
  • Ulceration or skin breakdown - May indicate ischaemia or neuropathy, requiring urgent evaluation for diabetic foot complications.
  • Severe unrelenting pain - Suggestive of joint infection, gout, or advanced arthritis.
  • Rapid progression of deformity - Could signal underlying systemic conditions such as rheumatoid arthritis or trauma.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Hallux Valgus"][/sc]
  • Pain without visible deformity - Early or subclinical stage, potentially from inflammation or footwear irritation.
  • Bilateral symmetrical deformities - Often linked to systemic conditions like rheumatoid arthritis.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask hallux valgus.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Hallux Valgus"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: X-ray (weight-bearing) - Shows lateral angulation of the great toe (hallux valgus angle >15°) and medial prominence.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Foot examination - Confirms clinical deformity and assesses for calluses or skin breakdown; Blood tests (if systemic symptoms) - Rule out.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Hallux Valgus"][/sc]
  • Gout - Acute monoarthritis, often affecting the first MTP joint, characterised by erythema, swelling, and tenderness.
  • Rheumatoid arthritis - Often presents with bilateral deformities and systemic symptoms like morning stiffness.
  • Bunionette (tailor’s bunion) - Similar deformity on the fifth MTP joint.
  • Related alternative diagnosis - Distinguish from hallux valgus using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Hallux Valgus"][/sc]
  • Osteoarthritis - Chronic mechanical stress leads to joint degeneration.
  • Metatarsalgia - Pain under the lesser metatarsal heads due to altered weight distribution.
  • Recurrent deformity - Can occur after surgery, particularly if risk factors persist.
