Cataract
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Cataract"][/sc]
  • Cataract is an opacification of the lens of the eye, leading to a progressive decline in visual acuity.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise cataract features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Cataract"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Cataract"][/sc]
  • Risk factors: Risk of cataract rises with relevant predisposition, comorbidity, and exposure profile.
  • Higher-risk or missed groups: Cataract may be missed when presentations are atypical or comorbidity obscures key features.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Cataract"][/sc]
  • Age-related cataracts develop due to oxidative damage, protein aggregation, and lens fibre degeneration, leading to loss of transparency.
  • Traumatic cataracts occur due to disruption of lens capsule integrity or metabolic damage following ocular trauma.
  • Congenital cataracts often arise from genetic mutations, metabolic disorders (e.g., galactosaemia), or intrauterine infections (e.g., rubella).
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Cataract"][/sc]
  • Cataracts generally progress slowly over years, although traumatic or metabolic cataracts may progress more rapidly over weeks to months.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Cataract"][/sc]
  • Ask about progressive vision loss - Caused by light scattering and reduced transparency of the lens, leading to blurry or dim vision.
  • Ask about glare - Worsened by bright lights due to light scattering in the opacified lens.
  • Ask about halos around lights - Resulting from diffraction of light by lens opacities.
  • Ask about double vision (monocular) - Occurs due to irregular refraction in the opacified lens.
  • Ask about change in colour perception - Often described as a yellowing of vision due to lens protein changes.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Cataract"][/sc]
  • Check reduced visual acuity - Assessed with a Snellen chart, reflecting the severity of lens opacity.
  • Check lens opacification - Visible on slit-lamp examination, with distinct patterns (e.g., nuclear sclerosis, cortical spokes).
  • Check absent red reflex - Observed during ophthalmoscopy, indicating significant opacification.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Cataract"][/sc]
  • Rapidly progressive vision loss - May indicate posterior subcapsular cataracts or other conditions like retinal detachment.
  • Severe pain and redness - Suggests secondary complications such as phacomorphic glaucoma.
  • Associated neurological symptoms - Raises suspicion for central nervous system pathology.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Cataract"][/sc]
  • Subtle vision changes - Can be missed in early cataracts, especially in patients with good baseline visual acuity.
  • Unilateral cataracts - May present without symptoms if the contralateral eye compensates.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Cataract"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Slit-lamp examination - Demonstrates lens opacities and helps classify the type of cataract.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Visual acuity testing - Identifies the degree of visual impairment; Ophthalmoscopy - Absence of the red reflex confirms significant lens opacification.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Cataract"][/sc]
  • A 72-year-old man presents with a 2-year history of progressive, painless visual decline in both eyes, difficulty driving at night due to glare, and yellowing of vision.
  • Adult with symptom pattern and objective findings suggestive of cataract, requiring focused confirmation and risk stratification.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Cataract"][/sc]
  • Age-related macular degeneration - Central vision loss with drusen on fundoscopy.
  • Glaucoma - Peripheral vision loss with elevated intraocular pressure.
  • Diabetic retinopathy - Retinal microaneurysms and haemorrhages on fundoscopy.
  • Alternative serious diagnosis - Distinguishing history, examination, and targeted investigations do not support cataract.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Cataract"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Assess physiological stability and urgent complications at presentation, and initiate early stabilisation where required.
  • Core care: Deliver condition-specific treatment for cataract, address underlying drivers, and involve appropriate specialist support.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Reassess symptom trajectory, objective markers, and treatment response over time to detect complications early.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Worsening symptoms, physiological deterioration, or failure to improve should trigger urgent reassessment for severe or alternative pathology in cataract.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Cataract"][/sc]
  • Phacomorphic glaucoma - Caused by lens swelling and angle closure.
  • Blindness - Occurs if cataracts remain untreated in advanced cases.
