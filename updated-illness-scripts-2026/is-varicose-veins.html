Varicose Veins
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Varicose Veins"][/sc]
  • Varicose veins are dilated, tortuous, and elongated superficial veins, most commonly affecting the lower limbs.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise varicose features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.

  • Higher-risk or missed groups: Varicose Veins may be missed when presentation is atypical or obscured by comorbidity.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Varicose Veins"][/sc]
  • Primary trigger: Incompetent venous valves in superficial veins, often due to genetic predisposition, pregnancy, or prolonged standing.
  • Venous reflux leads to increased venous pressure (venous hypertension).
  • Chronic venous stasis causes vein wall remodelling, dilation, and valve dysfunction.
  • Increased permeability results in oedema, inflammation, and eventual skin changes (e.g., lipodermatosclerosis).
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Varicose Veins"][/sc]
  • Initial: Asymptomatic or mild discomfort after prolonged standing or activity.
  • Progressive: Development of visible, tortuous veins and symptoms such as heaviness, aching, or itching.
  • Advanced: Skin changes (e.g., hyperpigmentation), venous ulcers, or thrombophlebitis.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Varicose Veins"][/sc]
  • Ask about visible enlarged veins - Aesthetic concern or awareness of prominent veins; due to chronic venous dilation.
  • Ask about leg heaviness or aching - Worsens with prolonged standing or sitting; caused by venous hypertension and pooling.
  • Ask about itching or burning sensation - Local inflammation from venous stasis dermatitis.
  • Ask about oedema - Especially around the ankles; due to increased capillary permeability and fluid retention.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Varicose Veins"][/sc]
  • Check visible varicosities - Tortuous, dilated superficial veins, especially along the medial aspect of the leg.
  • Check ankle oedema - Pitting oedema that improves with limb elevation, indicating venous stasis.
  • Check skin changes - Hyperpigmentation, eczema, or lipodermatosclerosis due to chronic venous insufficiency.
  • Check venous ulcers - Often over the medial malleolus; due to poor wound healing from chronic venous hypertension.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Varicose Veins"][/sc]
  • Rapidly worsening pain - Suggests thrombophlebitis or deep vein thrombosis (DVT).
  • Non-healing venous ulcers - Indicates chronic venous insufficiency or superimposed infection.
  • Sudden severe oedema - May indicate DVT or pelvic vein obstruction.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Varicose Veins"][/sc]
  • Isolated skin pigmentation without visible varicosities - Early chronic venous insufficiency.
  • Recurrent cellulitis - Could mask underlying venous insufficiency.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask varicose veins.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Varicose Veins"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Doppler ultrasound - Confirms venous reflux and identifies valve incompetence.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Photoplethysmography - Measures venous filling time to assess insufficiency; Venography (rarely used) - For complex or recurrent cases, visualising the venous.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Varicose Veins"][/sc]
  • Deep vein thrombosis (DVT) - Usually presents with acute onset pain and significant oedema.
  • Chronic venous insufficiency - More advanced skin changes without prominent varicosities.
  • Peripheral artery disease - May present with claudication rather than oedema or varicosities.
  • Related alternative diagnosis - Distinguish from varicose veins using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Varicose Veins"][/sc]
  • Venous ulcers: Chronic, difficult-to-heal ulcers, especially over the medial malleolus.
  • Superficial thrombophlebitis: Painful inflammation of superficial veins.
  • Deep vein thrombosis (DVT): Rare but serious, due to prolonged venous stasis.
