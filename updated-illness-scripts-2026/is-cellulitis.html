Cellulitis
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Cellulitis"][/sc]
  • Cellulitis is an acute bacterial infection of the skin and subcutaneous tissues, characterised by localised erythema, warmth, pain, and swelling.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise cellulitis features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Cellulitis"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Cellulitis"][/sc]
  • Risk factors: Risk of cellulitis rises with relevant predisposition, comorbidity, and exposure profile.
  • Higher-risk or missed groups: Cellulitis may be missed when presentations are atypical or comorbidity obscures key features.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Cellulitis"][/sc]
  • Cellulitis occurs when bacteria, commonly Staphylococcus aureus or beta-haemolytic streptococci, breach the skin barrier. Once inside, the bacteria multiply, causing an inflammatory response. Proinflammatory cytokines are released, resulting in increased vascular permeability, which leads to local oedema, erythema, and warmth. Neutrophils migrate to the infection site to clear bacteria, leading to tissue swelling and pain. The infection may spread through lymphatic vessels, potentially leading to systemic symptoms and complications such as abscesses or sepsis.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Cellulitis"][/sc]
  • Acute phase: Symptoms of cellulitis often develop rapidly over hours to days, with local signs (pain, erythema, swelling) occurring first.
  • Progression: Without treatment, the infection may worsen over 24–48 hours, spreading to a larger area and potentially leading to systemic involvement such as fever and chills.
  • Resolution: With appropriate treatment, symptoms begin to improve within 48–72 hours. Full resolution of inflammation can take up to 2 weeks.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Cellulitis"][/sc]
  • Ask about pain - Due to tissue inflammation and oedema from the inflammatory response triggered by bacterial infection.
  • Ask about erythema - Caused by increased blood flow to the affected area as part of the inflammatory process.
  • Ask about swelling - Results from increased vascular permeability leading to fluid leakage into the tissues.
  • Ask about warmth - A result of increased blood flow and local inflammation.
  • Ask about fever - A systemic sign of the body’s immune response to infection.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Cellulitis"][/sc]
  • Check erythema - Local redness is due to increased blood flow from the inflammatory response.
  • Check oedema - Swelling due to fluid accumulation in the interstitial tissue.
  • Check warmth - The affected area is palpably warm, reflecting increased blood flow and inflammation.
  • Check induration - The skin feels firm due to oedema and inflammatory cell infiltration.
  • Check lymphangitis - Red streaks extending from the affected area, indicating infection spreading along the lymphatic vessels.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Cellulitis"][/sc]
  • Rapidly spreading erythema - May indicate a more aggressive infection like necrotising fasciitis.
  • Systemic symptoms (fever, tachycardia, hypotension) - Signs of sepsis, which requires urgent medical attention.
  • Blisters or necrosis - Suggests a deeper or more severe infection that could lead to tissue death.
  • Crepitus - May indicate gas-forming organisms, signalling necrotising fasciitis.
  • Severe pain disproportionate to examination findings - A key feature of necrotising fasciitis.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Cellulitis"][/sc]
  • Indolent onset - In immunocompromised patients, cellulitis may develop more slowly with milder symptoms due to blunted immune responses.
  • Painless cellulitis - Some diabetic patients may present with less pain due to neuropathy, masking the severity of the infection.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features or comorbidity can mask the underlying diagnosis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Cellulitis"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Full blood count (FBC) - Raised white cell count (leukocytosis) is often seen, indicating an immune response to infection; C-reactive protein.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Blood cultures - Should be taken if sepsis is suspected; they may identify the causative organism; Wound culture - Not routinely.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Cellulitis"][/sc]
  • A 45-year-old man presents with a 2-day history of a painful, erythematous, swollen area on his lower leg following a minor cut. He also reports fever and chills. On examination.
  • Adult with symptom pattern and objective findings suggestive of cellulitis, requiring focused confirmation and risk stratification.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Cellulitis"][/sc]
  • DVT (Deep Vein Thrombosis) - Similar swelling and erythema in the lower limb, but typically without fever or systemic.
  • Necrotising fasciitis - Rapid progression with severe pain, systemic toxicity, and skin necrosis. Requires immediate surgical intervention.
  • Venous eczema - Chronic condition with skin changes (pigmentation, scaling), usually without acute pain or fever.
  • Contact dermatitis - Differentiate from cellulitis using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Cellulitis"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Assess physiological stability and urgent complications at presentation, and initiate early stabilisation where required.
  • Core care: Deliver condition-specific treatment for cellulitis, address underlying drivers, and involve appropriate specialist support.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Reassess symptom trajectory, objective markers, and treatment response over time to detect complications early.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Worsening symptoms, physiological deterioration, or failure to improve should trigger urgent reassessment for severe or alternative pathology in cellulitis.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Cellulitis"][/sc]
  • Abscess formation - Localised pus accumulation may require drainage if antibiotics alone are insufficient.
  • Sepsis - If the infection spreads systemically, it can lead to sepsis, a life-threatening condition requiring urgent care.
  • Recurrent cellulitis - Patients with venous insufficiency or lymphoedema are at risk of recurrent infections.
  • Chronic lymphoedema - Recurrent cellulitis can damage the lymphatic system, leading to long-term swelling and increased infection risk.
