Heart Failure
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Heart Failure"][/sc]
  • Heart failure (HF) is a clinical syndrome characterised by the heart's inability to pump enough blood to meet the metabolic needs of the body.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise heart features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.

Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Heart Failure"][/sc]
  • Heart failure occurs when cardiac output is insufficient due to either systolic dysfunction (impaired contraction) or diastolic dysfunction (impaired relaxation).
  • Systolic Heart Failure (HFrEF): Impaired contractility of the left ventricle leads to reduced ejection fraction (LVEF ≤40%). Causes include ischaemic heart disease, dilated cardiomyopathy, and myocardial infarction.
  • Diastolic Heart Failure (HFpEF): The left ventricle becomes stiff and unable to relax during diastole, leading to elevated filling pressures despite a normal ejection fraction (LVEF ≥50%). Hypertension and left ventricular hypertrophy are common causes.
  • Neurohormonal Activation: The renin-angiotensin-aldosterone system (RAAS) and sympathetic nervous system (SNS) are activated, leading to fluid retention, vasoconstriction, and increased afterload, further worsening cardiac function.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Heart Failure"][/sc]
  • Acute: Sudden onset of symptoms, often precipitated by a myocardial infarction, arrhythmias, or acute valvular disease.
  • Chronic: Progressively worsening symptoms over months or years, commonly seen in patients with longstanding hypertension or coronary artery disease.
  • Decompensation: Acute exacerbation of chronic heart failure, usually triggered by infections, dietary noncompliance (salt intake), or medication non-adherence.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Heart Failure"][/sc]
  • Ask about dyspnoea (breathlessness) - The most common symptom, resulting from pulmonary congestion and increased left atrial pressure. It is exacerbated by exertion and relieved by.
  • Ask about orthopnoea - Breathlessness when lying flat, caused by redistribution of fluid from the lower extremities to the lungs, increasing pulmonary venous pressure.
  • Ask about paroxysmal Nocturnal Dyspnoea (PND) - Sudden episodes of severe breathlessness at night, due to fluid reabsorption into the circulation when lying down, leading, with associated clinical features.
  • Ask about fatigue - A result of reduced cardiac output, leading to inadequate perfusion of muscles and tissues.
  • Ask about ankle swelling - Peripheral oedema occurs due to increased venous pressure from fluid retention.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Heart Failure"][/sc]
  • Check raised jugular venous pressure (JVP) - Indicates elevated right-sided heart pressures and fluid overload.
  • Check bilateral pitting oedema - Peripheral oedema results from sodium and water retention due to RAAS activation.
  • Check crackles on lung auscultation - Represents pulmonary oedema from fluid transudation into the alveoli.
  • Check displaced apex beat - Lateral displacement of the apex beat suggests left ventricular hypertrophy or dilation.
  • Check third heart sound (S3) - A low-pitched sound heard early in diastole, indicating increased ventricular filling pressures.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Heart Failure"][/sc]
  • Sudden worsening of dyspnoea or orthopnoea - May indicate acute pulmonary oedema, requiring urgent treatment.
  • Syncope or pre-syncope - Suggests severe arrhythmia or advanced heart failure, which could lead to sudden cardiac death.
  • New onset chest pain - May signify a myocardial infarction or acute coronary syndrome, necessitating emergency management.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Heart Failure"][/sc]
  • Chronic fatigue without dyspnoea - Especially in the elderly, heart failure may present with non-specific symptoms like fatigue, without classic signs of fluid overload.
  • Gastrointestinal symptoms (nausea, anorexia) - May occur due to congestion of the gastrointestinal tract and liver in right-sided heart failure.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask heart failure.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Heart Failure"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: BNP (B-type natriuretic peptide) or NT-proBNP: Elevated levels indicate increased ventricular wall stress. A BNP >100 pg/mL or NT-proBNP >300 pg/mL.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Chest X-ray: May show pulmonary congestion, cardiomegaly, or pleural effusion, indicating fluid overload; Electrocardiogram (ECG): Often shows signs of left ventricular.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Heart Failure"][/sc]
  • Chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD) - Typically presents with chronic cough and wheeze rather than fluid overload. Pulmonary function tests.
  • Pulmonary embolism (PE) - Distinguish from heart failure using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.
  • Renal failure - Can cause oedema and breathlessness but without the typical cardiac features like elevated JVP.
  • Related alternative diagnosis - Distinguish from heart failure using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Heart Failure"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Assess severity, physiological stability, and immediate complications.
  • Core care: Deliver condition-specific management for heart failure, treat underlying drivers, and coordinate appropriate specialist input.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Reassess symptom trajectory, examination findings, and objective markers to confirm response in heart failure.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Escalate urgently for clinical deterioration, evolving red flags, or failure to improve in suspected heart failure.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Heart Failure"][/sc]
  • Arrhythmias: Particularly atrial fibrillation, due to atrial enlargement and electrical instability. Increases risk of stroke and worsens heart failure prognosis.
  • Renal dysfunction: Common in advanced heart failure, due to reduced renal perfusion and use of diuretics and ACE inhibitors.
  • Sudden cardiac death: Often due to ventricular arrhythmias, which are more common in severe HFrEF.
