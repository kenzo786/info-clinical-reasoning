Gravitational Oedema
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Gravitational Oedema"][/sc]
  • Gravitational oedema is a form of peripheral oedema resulting from the pooling of fluid in the lower extremities due to prolonged standing or sitting.

Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Gravitational Oedema"][/sc]
  • Gravitational oedema occurs due to venous pooling in the lower extremities, leading to increased capillary hydrostatic pressure. This results in fluid moving from the capillaries into the interstitial space. Prolonged standing or sitting reduces the efficacy of venous return, and if the lymphatic drainage is insufficient to compensate, oedema forms. Over time, this leads to a build-up of fluid, especially in dependent areas such as the ankles and feet.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Gravitational Oedema"][/sc]
  • Acute: Develops after prolonged periods of immobility, such as sitting or standing for hours.
  • Chronic: In patients with chronic venous insufficiency or obesity, oedema may become persistent or recurrent over months to years, worsening towards the end of the day.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Gravitational Oedema"][/sc]
  • Ask about swelling of the lower extremities - Most common symptom, caused by fluid accumulation due to increased venous pressure and gravity.
  • Ask about heaviness or discomfort in legs - Often reported due to the pressure exerted by the oedematous fluid on tissues.
  • Ask about worsening oedema after prolonged standing - Symptoms typically worsen as gravity increases venous pooling and hydrostatic pressure.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Gravitational Oedema"][/sc]
  • Check pitting oedema - On applying pressure to the swollen area, a depression (pit) remains, indicating excess interstitial fluid. Common in dependent areas such as.
  • Check bilateral swelling - Symmetric oedema is characteristic of gravitational oedema, particularly affecting both legs.
  • Check improvement with leg elevation - Swelling typically decreases when the legs are elevated, which helps venous return and decreases hydrostatic pressure.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Gravitational Oedema"][/sc]
  • Unilateral oedema - May indicate deep vein thrombosis (DVT) rather than benign gravitational oedema, due to impaired venous return in one leg.
  • Sudden onset oedema - Acute onset swelling could suggest acute heart failure, DVT, or lymphatic obstruction.
  • Associated pain, redness, or warmth - These findings may indicate infection (cellulitis), thrombophlebitis, or DVT, requiring urgent investigation.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Gravitational Oedema"][/sc]
  • Minimal visible oedema but significant heaviness - Some patients may not have obvious swelling but experience discomfort or heaviness, particularly in occupations requiring prolonged standing.
  • Intermittent oedema - Symptoms may be intermittent, especially in the early stages, and may be overlooked if patients only present when oedema is more pronounced.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask gravitational oedema.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Gravitational Oedema"][/sc]
  • Chronic venous insufficiency - Long-term gravitational oedema can worsen venous insufficiency, leading to skin changes and ulcerations.
  • Venous ulcers - Persistent oedema can lead to skin breakdown and ulcer formation, particularly over the medial malleolus.
  • Infection - Stagnant fluid in the tissues can increase the risk of cellulitis.
