Medication-Induced Jaundice
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Medication-Induced Jaundice"][/sc]
  • Medication-induced jaundice is a form of liver injury where medications lead to an accumulation of bilirubin in the body, resulting in yellowing of the skin and eyes.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise medication features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Medication-Induced Jaundice"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Medication-Induced Jaundice"][/sc]
  • Risk factors: Risk factors for medication-induced jaundice include relevant predisposition, comorbidity, and exposure history.
  • Higher-risk or missed groups: Medication-Induced Jaundice may be missed when presentation is atypical or obscured by comorbidity.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Medication-Induced Jaundice"][/sc]
  • Drug-induced hepatocellular injury: Certain medications can directly damage liver cells, leading to impaired bilirubin conjugation and excretion, thus increasing unconjugated bilirubin in the blood.
  • Cholestatic effects: Some drugs cause cholestasis by impairing bile secretion or causing bile duct obstruction. This leads to conjugated bilirubin buildup.
  • Immunoallergic reactions: Drugs can trigger immune-mediated liver damage, where immune cells attack liver tissue, resulting in inflammation and jaundice.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Medication-Induced Jaundice"][/sc]
  • Acute phase: Typically occurs within days to weeks of drug exposure, particularly with high-dose or prolonged use.
  • Subacute phase: Some medications may cause jaundice after weeks to months of use, especially with cumulative hepatotoxicity.
  • Resolution phase: On drug discontinuation, symptoms may improve within days, though full resolution may take weeks to months.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Medication-Induced Jaundice"][/sc]
  • Ask about yellowing of skin and eyes (jaundice) - Due to bilirubin accumulation from impaired liver processing.
  • Ask about fatigue - Common due to systemic inflammation and liver dysfunction reducing energy metabolism.
  • Ask about nausea and vomiting - Often caused by liver toxicity and impaired bile excretion leading to gastrointestinal discomfort.
  • Ask about dark urine - Caused by high levels of conjugated bilirubin being excreted through the kidneys.
  • Ask about itching (pruritus) - Resulting from bile acids accumulating in the skin, often in cholestatic forms of jaundice.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Medication-Induced Jaundice"][/sc]
  • Check jaundice (yellow skin and sclera) - Visible yellowing due to bilirubin deposition in skin and mucous membranes.
  • Check hepatomegaly - Enlarged liver, possibly palpable, indicating inflammation or congestion due to liver injury.
  • Check dark urine - Visible change in urine colour from high bilirubin excretion.
  • Check clay-coloured stools - Lack of bile reaching the intestines in cases of severe cholestasis.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Medication-Induced Jaundice"][/sc]
  • Severe abdominal pain - May suggest acute liver failure or biliary obstruction; needs urgent assessment.
  • Rapidly worsening jaundice - Can indicate progressing liver failure and requires immediate intervention.
  • Confusion or altered mental status - Could signify hepatic encephalopathy from severe liver dysfunction.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Medication-Induced Jaundice"][/sc]
  • Asymptomatic jaundice: Some patients may not exhibit symptoms until advanced liver injury has occurred, especially with slow-developing hepatotoxicity.
  • Non-specific symptoms: Mild fatigue or malaise can occur without overt jaundice, particularly in early stages.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask medication-induced jaundice.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Medication-Induced Jaundice"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Liver function tests (LFTs) - Elevated ALT and AST in hepatocellular injury, elevated ALP and GGT in cholestasis; bilirubin levels may.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Serum bilirubin - Elevated total and direct bilirubin, indicating impaired liver processing or excretion; Imaging (Ultrasound, MRI) - To rule out.

  • A 45-year-old woman presents with 2 weeks of yellowing of the eyes, dark urine, and fatigue after starting a new medication (e.g., amoxicillin-clavulanate). Liver function tests reveal elevated ALT, AST.
  • Patient with 2 weeks of yellowing of the eyes, dark urine, and fatigue after starting a new medication (e.g., amoxicillin-clavulanate). Liver function tests reveal elevated ALT, AST, consistent with medication-induced jaundice.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Medication-Induced Jaundice"][/sc]
  • Viral hepatitis - Presents with fever and systemic symptoms; confirmed by hepatitis serology.
  • Gallstones with biliary obstruction - Often causes severe pain, confirmed by imaging (e.g., ultrasound).
  • Autoimmune hepatitis - Chronic liver inflammation with autoantibodies and requires immunosuppressive treatment.
  • Related alternative diagnosis - Distinguish from medication-induced jaundice using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Medication-Induced Jaundice"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Assess severity, physiological stability, and immediate complications.
  • Core care: Deliver condition-specific management for medication-induced jaundice, treat underlying drivers, and coordinate appropriate specialist input.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Reassess symptom trajectory, examination findings, and objective markers to confirm response in medication-induced jaundice.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Escalate urgently for clinical deterioration, evolving red flags, or failure to improve in suspected medication-induced jaundice.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Medication-Induced Jaundice"][/sc]
  • Acute liver failure - Due to severe hepatocellular injury; requires urgent treatment and possible liver transplantation.
  • Chronic liver disease - Can develop if hepatotoxicity is not resolved, leading to cirrhosis.
