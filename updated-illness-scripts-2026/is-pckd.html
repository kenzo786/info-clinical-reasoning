Polycystic Kidney Disease (PCKD)
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Polycystic Kidney Disease (PCKD)"][/sc]
  • Polycystic Kidney Disease (PCKD) is a genetic disorder characterised by the development of numerous fluid-filled cysts in the kidneys.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise polycystic features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.

Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Polycystic Kidney Disease (PCKD)"][/sc]
  • PCKD is caused by mutations in genes responsible for the production of proteins involved in kidney tubule function. In ADPKD, mutations in the PKD1 or PKD2 genes lead to the formation of numerous cysts. These cysts gradually expand, distorting renal architecture and impairing kidney function. The cysts also trigger a local inflammatory response, resulting in interstitial fibrosis. Over time, this fibrosis contributes to chronic kidney disease (CKD) and eventual ESRD.
  • In ARPKD, mutations in the PKHD1 gene result in cystic dilation of the collecting ducts, with more severe and earlier-onset renal failure.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Polycystic Kidney Disease (PCKD)"][/sc]
  • Early Stage (Childhood to Early Adulthood): In ADPKD, patients are typically asymptomatic or have mild symptoms, with preserved kidney function.
  • Middle Stage (20-50 years): Cysts enlarge progressively, and patients may develop hypertension, haematuria, and flank pain. Renal function begins to decline.
  • Late Stage (Over 50 years): Many patients progress to CKD and ESRD, requiring dialysis or kidney transplantation.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Polycystic Kidney Disease (PCKD)"][/sc]
  • Ask about asymptomatic (early stages): PCKD often presents asymptomatically in its early stages, especially ADPKD. This is due to the slow growth of cysts with no.
  • Ask about flank Pain: Pain results from the expansion of renal cysts, causing capsular stretching or cyst rupture.
  • Ask about hypertension: Due to the cysts compressing blood vessels, leading to renal ischaemia and increased renin production.
  • Ask about haematuria: Cyst rupture can lead to bleeding into the urinary system, resulting in visible or microscopic blood in the urine.
  • Ask about recurrent UTIs: The cysts can become infected, leading to recurrent urinary tract infections.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Polycystic Kidney Disease (PCKD)"][/sc]
  • Check hypertension: Elevated blood pressure due to activation of the renin-angiotensin system as a result of cyst-induced renal ischaemia.
  • Check palpable Enlarged Kidneys: On physical examination, the kidneys may be palpable due to cystic enlargement.
  • Check abdominal Tenderness: Localised tenderness may occur in the flanks due to expanding cysts or cyst rupture.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Polycystic Kidney Disease (PCKD)"][/sc]
  • Severe Flank Pain with Fever: This may indicate cyst infection or rupture, a serious complication requiring urgent treatment.
  • Gross Haematuria: Significant haematuria could signal a ruptured cyst or bleeding from an aneurysm, requiring prompt investigation to prevent further complications.
  • Sudden Onset Severe Headache: In patients with PCKD, this could be due to a ruptured intracranial aneurysm, which is a life-threatening complication.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Polycystic Kidney Disease (PCKD)"][/sc]
  • Minimal Renal Symptoms with Neurological Complications: In some patients, the first symptom may be a cerebral aneurysm rupture, rather than kidney-related issues.
  • Isolated Hypertension: Some patients may present only with hypertension, without overt kidney-related symptoms, which can delay the diagnosis of PCKD.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask polycystic kidney disease (pckd).
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Polycystic Kidney Disease (PCKD)"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Focused bedside assessment and first-line testing to assess severity and immediate risk in suspected polycystic kidney disease (pckd).
  • Targeted and monitoring: Genetic Testing: Confirms mutations in PKD1 or PKD2 genes for ADPKD and PKHD1 for ARPKD. This is particularly useful in cases.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Polycystic Kidney Disease (PCKD)"][/sc]
  • Medullary Sponge Kidney - This condition also involves renal cysts but presents with recurrent kidney stones and urinary.
  • Renal Cell Carcinoma (RCC) - Unlike PCKD, RCC often presents with a single mass and may cause systemic symptoms.
  • Hydronephrosis - Caused by obstruction of urinary flow, leading to kidney enlargement, but without the multiple.
  • Related alternative diagnosis - Distinguish from polycystic kidney disease (pckd) using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.

  • Immediate priorities: Assess severity, physiological stability, and immediate complications.
  • Core care: Deliver condition-specific management for polycystic kidney disease (pckd), treat underlying drivers, and coordinate appropriate specialist input.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Reassess symptom trajectory, examination findings, and objective markers to confirm response in polycystic kidney disease (pckd).
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Escalate urgently for clinical deterioration, evolving red flags, or failure to improve in suspected polycystic kidney disease (pckd).
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Polycystic Kidney Disease (PCKD)"][/sc]
  • Progressive chronic kidney disease: Enlarging cyst burden can lead to declining renal function and eventual kidney failure.
  • Cardiovascular and hypertensive complications: Poorly controlled blood pressure increases risk of cardiac and cerebrovascular events.
  • Cyst-related complications: Pain, infection, or bleeding episodes can cause recurrent admissions and morbidity.
