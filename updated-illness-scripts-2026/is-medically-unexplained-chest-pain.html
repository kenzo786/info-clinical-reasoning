Medically Unexplained Chest Pain
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Medically Unexplained Chest Pain"][/sc]
  • Medically unexplained chest pain (MUCP) refers to persistent or recurrent chest pain for which no clear organic cause can be identified after appropriate medical evaluation.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise medically features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Medically Unexplained Chest Pain"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Medically Unexplained Chest Pain"][/sc]
  • Risk Factors: Psychological conditions such as anxiety and panic disorder, heightened pain sensitivity, history of functional somatic syndromes (e.g., irritable bowel syndrome, fibromyalgia), autonomic dysregulation, and prior negative healthcare experiences increase the risk of Medically Unexplained Chest Pain.
  • Prevention: Early recognition and management of anxiety and psychological distress, patient education on the benign nature of symptoms, cognitive-behavioural therapy (CBT) for at-risk individuals, and promoting healthy coping mechanisms for stress may reduce the risk of developing chronic unexplained chest pain.
MUCP Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Medically Unexplained Chest Pain"][/sc]
  • Increased visceral sensitivity: Hypersensitivity of oesophageal or cardiac nociceptors leading to amplified pain perception.
  • Altered central pain processing: Functional MRI studies suggest increased activation of pain-processing areas.
  • Autonomic dysfunction: Imbalance in sympathetic and parasympathetic nervous systems contributing to chest discomfort.
  • Psychological factors: Anxiety, panic disorder, and somatisation contribute to symptom generation.
MUCP Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Medically Unexplained Chest Pain"][/sc]
  • Acute episodes lasting minutes to hours, often recurrent.
  • May be persistent over months or years in some patients.
  • Symptoms may fluctuate in severity depending on stress and psychological state.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Medically Unexplained Chest Pain"][/sc]
  • Ask about chest pain – Often described as sharp, stabbing, or burning. Lacks a clear cardiac pattern.
  • Ask about dyspnoea – Likely due to associated anxiety and hyperventilation.
  • Ask about palpitations – Secondary to heightened autonomic arousal.
  • Ask about globus sensation – Feeling of a lump in the throat, commonly linked to oesophageal hypersensitivity.
  • Ask about reflux symptoms – May mimic cardiac pain due to shared neural pathways.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Medically Unexplained Chest Pain"][/sc]
  • Check normal cardiovascular examination – No signs of cardiac pathology.
  • Check hyperventilation signs – Rapid breathing, dizziness, and tingling in fingers.
  • Check psychological distress – Anxious affect, sweating, and restlessness.
MUCP Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Medically Unexplained Chest Pain"][/sc]
  • Exertional chest pain – Suggests underlying ischaemic heart disease.
  • Syncope – Could indicate arrhythmias or structural heart disease.
  • Neurological symptoms – Consider stroke or aortic dissection.
  • Progressive weight loss – Raises suspicion of malignancy.
MUCP Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Medically Unexplained Chest Pain"][/sc]
  • Epigastric pain mimicking angina.
  • Chronic pain with no acute exacerbations.
  • Pain triggered by emotional stress without exertion.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask medically unexplained chest pain.
MUCP Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Medically Unexplained Chest Pain"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: ECG – Normal in medically unexplained chest pain but done to rule out ischaemia; Troponins – Normal unless coincidental pathology.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Oesophageal pH testing – May detect reflux; Psychological assessment – Useful for identifying underlying anxiety or panic disorder.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Medically Unexplained Chest Pain"][/sc]
  • Chronic pain syndrome.
  • Repeated unnecessary medical investigations.
  • Reduced quality of life due to persistent symptoms.
