Hirschsprung’s Disease (Children)
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Hirschsprung’s Disease (Children)"][/sc]
  • Hirschsprung’s disease is a congenital disorder characterised by the absence of ganglion cells in the distal bowel, leading to a functional obstruction.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise hirschsprung features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.

  • Higher-risk or missed groups: Hirschsprung’s Disease (Children) may be missed when presentation is atypical or obscured by comorbidity.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Hirschsprung’s Disease (Children)"][/sc]
  • Developmental Defect: Hirschsprung’s disease arises due to a failure of neural crest cells to migrate to the distal bowel during embryonic development, resulting in aganglionosis.
  • Absence of Ganglion Cells: The aganglionic segment cannot perform peristalsis, leading to functional bowel obstruction in the affected area.
  • Consequences: Accumulation of faecal material and dilatation of the proximal bowel (megacolon) due to an inability to move stool through the affected segment.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Hirschsprung’s Disease (Children)"][/sc]
  • Neonatal Period: Symptoms typically appear within 48 hours of birth, with delayed meconium passage and abdominal distention.
  • Infancy: If undiagnosed, persistent constipation, vomiting, and feeding difficulties may develop.
  • Childhood: Recurrent episodes of constipation and potential enterocolitis in severe cases.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Hirschsprung’s Disease (Children)"][/sc]
  • Ask about delayed passage of meconium (>48 hours after birth) - A classic sign due to failure of peristalsis in the aganglionic bowel segment.
  • Ask about chronic constipation - Caused by obstruction in the affected bowel area, leading to difficulty passing stool.
  • Ask about abdominal distention - Accumulation of faecal material and gas proximal to the obstruction.
  • Ask about vomiting - May occur due to the severity of obstruction and abdominal pressure.
  • Ask about poor feeding and failure to thrive - Reduced nutrient intake and malabsorption in chronic cases.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Hirschsprung’s Disease (Children)"][/sc]
  • Check abdominal distention - Swollen abdomen due to trapped faeces and gas in the bowel.
  • Check palpable mass of faeces in the abdomen - Stool accumulation proximal to the aganglionic segment.
  • Check explosive expulsion of stool on rectal examination - Occurs when digital stimulation relieves a temporary obstruction.
  • Check poor growth - Due to malnutrition from chronic obstruction and inadequate nutrient absorption.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Hirschsprung’s Disease (Children)"][/sc]
  • Enterocolitis - Severe abdominal pain, diarrhoea, fever; can indicate Hirschsprung-associated enterocolitis (HAEC), a life-threatening condition due to bacterial overgrowth and bowel inflammation.
  • Sepsis - Possible in severe cases of HAEC, requiring prompt medical intervention to avoid systemic complications.
  • Failure to thrive - Signifies chronic malabsorption and possible nutritional deficiencies.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Hirschsprung’s Disease (Children)"][/sc]
  • Late-onset Hirschsprung’s disease - Can present with chronic constipation in older children without neonatal symptoms; pathophysiology involves less extensive aganglionosis.
  • Intermittent diarrhoea - Due to proximal overflow of faecal material, mimicking other gastrointestinal disorders.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask hirschsprung’s disease (children).
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Hirschsprung’s Disease (Children)"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Contrast enema: Shows a transition zone between the dilated proximal bowel and the narrow distal segment.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Targeted investigations to confirm diagnosis, assess complications, and monitor response in hirschsprung’s disease (children).

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Hirschsprung’s Disease (Children)"][/sc]
  • Meconium ileus - Often associated with cystic fibrosis, characterised by thickened meconium causing obstruction.
  • Intestinal atresia - Congenital malformation where segments of the intestine are missing; no transition zone on imaging.
  • Chronic constipation due to functional causes - No aganglionosis or transition zone; responds to conservative measures.
  • Related alternative diagnosis - Distinguish from hirschsprung’s disease (children) using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Hirschsprung’s Disease (Children)"][/sc]
  • Hirschsprung-associated enterocolitis (HAEC): Occurs in 30-50% of cases; can lead to bowel perforation and sepsis.
  • Strictures: Fibrotic narrowing of the bowel segment post-surgery; may require further intervention.
  • Incontinence and constipation: Persistent bowel dysfunction post-surgery in some children.
