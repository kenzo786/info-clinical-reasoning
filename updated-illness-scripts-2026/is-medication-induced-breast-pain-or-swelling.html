Medication-Induced Breast Pain or Swelling
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Medication-Induced Breast Pain or Swelling"][/sc]
  • Medication-induced breast pain or swelling is discomfort or enlargement of breast tissue resulting from the side effects of certain medications.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise medication features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.

  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Medication-Induced Breast Pain or Swelling"][/sc]
  • Risk factors: Risk factors for medication-induced breast pain or swelling include relevant predisposition, comorbidity, and exposure history.
  • Higher-risk or missed groups: Medication-Induced Breast Pain or Swelling may be missed when presentation is atypical or obscured by comorbidity.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Medication-Induced Breast Pain or Swelling"][/sc]
  • Certain medications can increase levels of prolactin or mimic oestrogenic effects, leading to increased fluid retention and cellular proliferation in breast tissue.
  • Antipsychotics and antidepressants may increase prolactin secretion by blocking dopamine, resulting in breast enlargement and tenderness.
  • Oestrogenic medications may increase ductal and stromal tissue proliferation, causing discomfort or visible swelling.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Medication-Induced Breast Pain or Swelling"][/sc]
  • Onset: Symptoms typically begin within weeks to months of starting the medication.
  • Progression: Symptoms may progress gradually and stabilise with continued use; alternatively, they may worsen if dosage is increased.
  • Resolution: Symptoms often improve within weeks after discontinuing the offending medication.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Medication-Induced Breast Pain or Swelling"][/sc]
  • Ask about breast tenderness or pain - Due to increased fluid retention and proliferation in breast tissue, leading to pain or a dull ache.
  • Ask about swelling or enlargement - Caused by ductal and stromal growth, often noted in those on hormonal therapies.
  • Ask about nipple discharge - Often related to medications that increase prolactin (e.g., antipsychotics), leading to galactorrhoea in some cases.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Medication-Induced Breast Pain or Swelling"][/sc]
  • Check palpable breast enlargement - Tender or firm breast tissue may be observed during examination.
  • Check discharge upon nipple expression - Milky discharge may be noted, particularly in those with elevated prolactin levels.
  • Check symmetric or asymmetric swelling - Enlargement may be unilateral or bilateral, depending on individual response and medication effect.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Medication-Induced Breast Pain or Swelling"][/sc]
  • New-onset, unilateral breast mass - May indicate malignancy rather than medication-induced changes and warrants further evaluation.
  • Bloody nipple discharge - Suggestive of intraductal papilloma or malignancy, requiring urgent investigation.
  • Skin changes (dimpling, erythema) - Potential signs of inflammatory breast cancer or infection, not typical of medication-induced effects.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Medication-Induced Breast Pain or Swelling"][/sc]
  • Some patients may present with asymmetry or minimal pain, which may be mistaken for normal variation rather than a medication side effect.
  • Patients with minimal prolactin elevation may still experience swelling or tenderness without galactorrhoea.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask medication-induced breast pain or swelling.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Medication-Induced Breast Pain or Swelling"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Prolactin levels: Elevated in cases where prolactin-elevating medications are involved; aids in distinguishing from other causes.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Targeted investigations to confirm diagnosis, assess complications, and monitor response in medication-induced breast pain or swelling.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Medication-Induced Breast Pain or Swelling"][/sc]
  • Mastitis - Typically presents with erythema and warmth, often accompanied by systemic symptoms such as fever.
  • Fibrocystic breast changes - Cyclic breast pain that is typically associated with the menstrual cycle, and not linked.
  • Breast cancer - Usually unilateral, presenting as a firm, fixed mass with possible skin changes or nipple.
  • Related alternative diagnosis - Distinguish from medication-induced breast pain or swelling using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Medication-Induced Breast Pain or Swelling"][/sc]
  • Persistent pain and swelling: Can impact quality of life, especially if symptoms persist despite medication changes.
  • Emotional and psychological impact: Breast changes can be distressing, potentially exacerbating underlying mental health conditions.
