Ocular Sarcoidosis
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Ocular Sarcoidosis"][/sc]
  • Ocular sarcoidosis is a chronic inflammatory condition characterised by the formation of non-caseating granulomas in ocular tissues, often associated with systemic sarcoidosis.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise ocular features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.

  • Higher-risk or missed groups: Ocular Sarcoidosis may be missed when presentation is atypical or obscured by comorbidity.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Ocular Sarcoidosis"][/sc]
  • Ocular sarcoidosis results from an exaggerated immune response leading to the formation of granulomas. Antigen-presenting cells present unknown antigens to T-helper cells, activating them and causing cytokine release (e.g., TNF-α, IL-2). This promotes macrophage aggregation and granuloma formation, disrupting normal tissue function in the eye.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Ocular Sarcoidosis"][/sc]
  • Acute Phase: Sudden onset of symptoms such as red eye, pain, and photophobia, often in anterior uveitis.
  • Chronic Phase: Insidious development of complications like glaucoma, cataracts, or retinal damage, leading to progressive visual impairment.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Ocular Sarcoidosis"][/sc]
  • Ask about blurred Vision: Due to inflammation affecting the cornea, lens, or retina.
  • Ask about eye Pain: Associated with anterior uveitis or scleritis from granulomatous inflammation.
  • Ask about photophobia: Caused by irritation of the iris and ciliary body.
  • Ask about dry Eyes: Resulting from lacrimal gland involvement and reduced tear production.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Ocular Sarcoidosis"][/sc]
  • Check conjunctival Nodules: Reflect granuloma formation in the conjunctiva.
  • Check mutton-Fat Keratic Precipitates: Large inflammatory deposits on the corneal endothelium, indicating granulomatous uveitis.
  • Check posterior Synechiae: Adhesions between the iris and lens due to prolonged inflammation.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Ocular Sarcoidosis"][/sc]
  • Severe Vision Loss: May signify retinal detachment or optic neuropathy.
  • Intense Eye Pain with Nausea: Could indicate secondary glaucoma.
  • Systemic Symptoms with Ocular Signs: Suggestive of multi-organ involvement requiring urgent systemic evaluation.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Ocular Sarcoidosis"][/sc]
  • Asymptomatic Presentation: Detected incidentally during examination for systemic sarcoidosis.
  • Isolated Posterior Uveitis: Without systemic symptoms, which may delay diagnosis.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask ocular sarcoidosis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Ocular Sarcoidosis"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Slit-Lamp Examination: Identifies keratic precipitates, iris nodules, or posterior synechiae; Chest X-ray: May show hilar lymphadenopathy, supporting systemic sarcoidosis.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Serum ACE Levels: Elevated in sarcoidosis but nonspecific; Ocular Imaging: OCT to assess retinal or choroidal involvement.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Ocular Sarcoidosis"][/sc]
  • Infectious Uveitis - Differentiated by positive infectious serologies or PCR testing.
  • Ankylosing Spondylitis-associated Uveitis - Associated with HLA-B27 positivity and back pain.
  • Vogt-Koyanagi-Harada Syndrome - Accompanied by auditory and neurological symptoms.
  • Related alternative diagnosis - Distinguish from ocular sarcoidosis using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.

  • Escalation and safety-netting: Escalate urgently for clinical deterioration, evolving red flags, or failure to improve in suspected ocular sarcoidosis.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Ocular Sarcoidosis"][/sc]
  • Glaucoma: Due to chronic inflammation or steroid use.
  • Cataracts: Often secondary to prolonged corticosteroid therapy.
  • Retinal Detachment: From severe granulomatous involvement.
