Autoimmune Hepatitis
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Autoimmune Hepatitis"][/sc]
  • Autoimmune hepatitis (AIH) is a chronic inflammatory liver disease characterised by immune-mediated attack on liver cells, resulting in hepatocellular injury and inflammation.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise autoimmune features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Autoimmune Hepatitis"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Autoimmune Hepatitis"][/sc]
  • Risk factors: Risk of autoimmune hepatitis rises with relevant predisposition, comorbidity, and exposure profile.
  • Higher-risk or missed groups: Autoimmune Hepatitis may be missed when presentations are atypical or comorbidity obscures key features.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Autoimmune Hepatitis"][/sc]
  • AIH is thought to result from a combination of genetic predisposition and environmental triggers (e.g., viral infections or medications) that lead to loss of tolerance to liver antigens. This triggers an autoimmune response, characterised by T-lymphocyte activation and production of autoantibodies, which target hepatocytes and cause progressive liver inflammation and fibrosis.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Autoimmune Hepatitis"][/sc]
  • Initial Phase: AIH may present abruptly or insidiously over months to years.
  • Chronic Phase: Persistent liver inflammation can lead to fibrosis and cirrhosis over years if untreated.
  • Flare-Ups: Recurrent exacerbations are common and may be triggered by infections, medications, or stressors.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Autoimmune Hepatitis"][/sc]
  • Ask about fatigue - Common and often severe; related to ongoing immune activation and hepatic inflammation.
  • Ask about jaundice - Due to hepatocellular injury leading to impaired bilirubin processing.
  • Ask about abdominal Pain - Usually in the right upper quadrant due to hepatic inflammation.
  • Ask about arthralgia - Caused by systemic immune activation, common in autoimmune diseases.
  • Ask about fever - May occur during flare-ups due to immune-mediated hepatic inflammation.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Autoimmune Hepatitis"][/sc]
  • Check hepatomegaly - Due to liver inflammation and swelling.
  • Check splenomegaly - Associated with portal hypertension in advanced disease stages.
  • Check spider Naevi - Resulting from hyperestrogenism due to impaired liver function.
  • Check palmar Erythema - Also due to hyperestrogenism associated with chronic liver disease.
  • Check ascites - Indicative of advanced liver disease with portal hypertension and hypoalbuminaemia.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Autoimmune Hepatitis"][/sc]
  • Hepatic Encephalopathy - Suggests advanced liver dysfunction and risk of acute liver failure.
  • Severe Jaundice - May indicate significant hepatocellular injury or impending liver failure.
  • Coagulopathy - Elevated INR can signal severe synthetic liver dysfunction.
  • Ascites - Suggestive of portal hypertension and decompensated liver disease.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Autoimmune Hepatitis"][/sc]
  • Acute Severe Hepatitis - Rapidly progressive liver failure with coagulopathy and jaundice, less common but critical to identify.
  • Extrahepatic Manifestations - Systemic symptoms like arthritis or thyroiditis, which may overshadow liver-related symptoms.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features or comorbidity can mask the underlying diagnosis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Autoimmune Hepatitis"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Liver Function Tests (LFTs): Elevated ALT and AST, often in a hepatocellular pattern; Autoantibodies: Positive ANA, SMA, or LKM-1 antibodies, indicating.
  • Targeted and monitoring: IgG Levels: Raised IgG is a typical marker of AIH; Liver Biopsy: Shows interface hepatitis with lymphoplasmacytic infiltration; confirms diagnosis.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Autoimmune Hepatitis"][/sc]
  • A 35-year-old female with fatigue, jaundice, and elevated liver enzymes. Autoantibodies are positive for ANA and SMA, and liver biopsy reveals interface hepatitis.
  • Adult with symptom pattern and objective findings suggestive of autoimmune hepatitis, requiring focused confirmation and risk stratification.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Autoimmune Hepatitis"][/sc]
  • Viral Hepatitis - Differentiated by positive viral serologies (HBV, HCV) and absence of autoantibodies.
  • Primary Biliary Cholangitis - Marked by antimitochondrial antibodies (AMA) and cholestatic LFT pattern.
  • Drug-Induced Liver Injury - Recent medication use; histology shows different injury patterns.
  • Non-Alcoholic Steatohepatitis (NASH) - Linked with obesity/metabolic syndrome, generally lacks autoantibodies.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Autoimmune Hepatitis"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Assess physiological stability and urgent complications at presentation, and initiate early stabilisation where required.
  • Core care: Deliver condition-specific treatment for autoimmune hepatitis, address underlying drivers, and involve appropriate specialist support.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Reassess symptom trajectory, objective markers, and treatment response over time to detect complications early.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Worsening symptoms, physiological deterioration, or failure to improve should trigger urgent reassessment for severe or alternative pathology in autoimmune hepatitis.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Autoimmune Hepatitis"][/sc]
  • Cirrhosis: Develops in 30–40% of untreated patients, resulting from chronic liver inflammation and scarring.
  • Liver Failure: Potential outcome in advanced disease, with risks of hepatic encephalopathy and coagulopathy.
  • Hepatocellular Carcinoma (HCC): Increased risk in patients with cirrhosis due to chronic hepatocellular injury.
