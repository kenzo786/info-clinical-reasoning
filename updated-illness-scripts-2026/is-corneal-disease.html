Corneal Disease
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Corneal Disease"][/sc]
  • Corneal disease encompasses a range of conditions that affect the cornea, the transparent, dome-shaped surface that covers the front of the eye.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise corneal features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Corneal Disease"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Corneal Disease"][/sc]
  • Worldwide, corneal opacities are a leading cause of blindness, affecting approximately 4–5 million people.
  • Corneal dystrophies and degenerative conditions are less common but contribute significantly to referrals for corneal transplants in developed nations.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Corneal Disease"][/sc]
  • The cornea is avascular and relies on the tear film and aqueous humour for nutrients and immune defence.
  • Infections disrupt the epithelial barrier, allowing pathogens to penetrate and trigger an inflammatory response, often mediated by cytokines and neutrophils.
  • Degenerative or dystrophic changes impair stromal organisation, leading to loss of transparency and biomechanical strength.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Corneal Disease"][/sc]
  • Acute phase: Corneal infections such as bacterial keratitis may develop over 24–48 hours with rapid progression.
  • Chronic phase: Degenerative conditions like keratoconus evolve over years, causing gradual visual decline.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Corneal Disease"][/sc]
  • Ask about blurred vision: Caused by disruption of corneal transparency or surface irregularities.
  • Ask about pain: Results from corneal nerve irritation, common in infections or trauma.
  • Ask about photophobia: Secondary to corneal inflammation affecting light-sensitive nerve endings.
  • Ask about redness: Reflects underlying inflammation and vasodilation of conjunctival vessels.
  • Ask about tearing: Increased tear production due to irritation of the ocular surface.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Corneal Disease"][/sc]
  • Check corneal opacity: Indicative of stromal scarring or active infection.
  • Check hypopyon: Presence of inflammatory cells in the anterior chamber, seen in severe infections.
  • Check epithelial defect: Fluorescein staining reveals breaches in the corneal epithelium.
  • Check corneal thinning: Seen in degenerative conditions such as keratoconus.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Corneal Disease"][/sc]
  • Severe pain and rapid vision loss: May indicate acute infectious keratitis or perforation.
  • Hypopyon: Suggestive of a severe infection requiring urgent intervention.
  • Non-healing epithelial defect: Could signify neurotrophic keratopathy or underlying systemic disease such as diabetes.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Corneal Disease"][/sc]
  • Peripheral ulcerative keratitis: Often associated with systemic autoimmune diseases like rheumatoid arthritis.
  • Recurrent erosion syndrome: Presents as transient episodes of pain and blurring due to unstable corneal epithelium.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features or comorbidity can mask the underlying diagnosis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Corneal Disease"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Slit-lamp examination: Reveals corneal defects, opacities, and inflammation; Fluorescein staining: Highlights epithelial defects under blue light.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Corneal cultures: Identifies pathogens in infectious cases; Pachymetry: Measures corneal thickness, useful in keratoconus.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Corneal Disease"][/sc]
  • Conjunctivitis - Lacks corneal involvement and significant pain.
  • Uveitis - Typically associated with photophobia and anterior chamber inflammation but no corneal opacity.
  • Dry eye disease - Symptoms are chronic and milder, with no fluorescein staining abnormalities.
  • Alternative serious diagnosis - Distinguishing history, examination, and targeted investigations do not support corneal disease.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Corneal Disease"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Assess physiological stability and urgent complications at presentation, and initiate early stabilisation where required.
  • Core care: Deliver condition-specific treatment for corneal disease, address underlying drivers, and involve appropriate specialist support.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Reassess symptom trajectory, objective markers, and treatment response over time to detect complications early.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Worsening symptoms, physiological deterioration, or failure to improve should trigger urgent reassessment for severe or alternative pathology in corneal disease.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Corneal Disease"][/sc]
  • Corneal scarring: Leads to permanent vision loss and may require transplantation.
  • Corneal perforation: Results from untreated infectious keratitis or severe thinning, potentially causing endophthalmitis.
