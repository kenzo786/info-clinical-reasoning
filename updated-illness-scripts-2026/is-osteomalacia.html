Osteomalacia
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Osteomalacia"][/sc]
  • Osteomalacia is a metabolic bone disorder characterised by impaired bone mineralisation, leading to soft and weakened bones.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise osteomalacia features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Osteomalacia"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Osteomalacia"][/sc]
  • Risk factors: Risk factors for osteomalacia include relevant predisposition, comorbidity, and exposure history.
  • Higher-risk or missed groups: Osteomalacia may be missed when presentation is atypical or obscured by comorbidity.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Osteomalacia"][/sc]
  • Osteomalacia develops due to insufficient calcium and phosphate, necessary for bone mineralisation.
  • Step-by-Step: Low vitamin D → decreased intestinal absorption of calcium and phosphate → hypocalcaemia stimulates parathyroid hormone (PTH) → PTH increases bone resorption to normalise calcium but reduces serum phosphate → bone becomes undermineralised, soft, and prone to fractures.
  • Primary Mechanisms: Vitamin D deficiency, phosphate depletion, and impaired vitamin D metabolism in renal or hepatic disease.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Osteomalacia"][/sc]
  • Insidious Onset: Symptoms progress slowly over months to years.
  • Chronic Course: Without intervention, osteomalacia remains progressive, causing worsening symptoms over time.
  • Potential Reversibility: Early intervention with vitamin D, calcium, and phosphate supplementation can stabilise and reverse bone changes.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Osteomalacia"][/sc]
  • Ask about bone Pain: Common in weight-bearing bones due to weakened bone matrix, particularly in hips, lower back, and legs.
  • Ask about muscle Weakness: Due to hypocalcaemia affecting muscle function and causing proximal muscle weakness, particularly in the thighs and shoulders.
  • Ask about fatigue: Systemic symptoms from chronic pain and muscle weakness.
  • Ask about fractures: Low trauma fractures occur due to bone fragility, especially in the vertebrae, pelvis, and long bones.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Osteomalacia"][/sc]
  • Check waddling Gait: Proximal muscle weakness causes an altered gait pattern.
  • Check bony Tenderness: Localised tenderness over ribs, pelvis, and spine due to undermineralised bones.
  • Check kyphosis and Skeletal Deformities: Long-term cases can present with spinal deformities due to vertebral weakness.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Osteomalacia"][/sc]
  • Pathological Fractures: Indicative of severe undermineralisation and bone fragility, which can lead to immobility.
  • Severe Hypocalcaemia: Can present with tetany or seizures, requiring urgent correction.
  • Rickets (in children): Osteomalacia in children can result in severe deformities and growth retardation.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Osteomalacia"][/sc]
  • Asymptomatic: Some patients may be asymptomatic and are diagnosed incidentally on imaging or routine blood work.
  • Diffuse Pain or Fatigue: Non-specific presentations may mimic fibromyalgia or chronic fatigue syndrome.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask osteomalacia.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Osteomalacia"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Serum Calcium and Phosphate: Low in osteomalacia, reflecting impaired mineralisation; 25-hydroxyvitamin D: Levels are typically below 25 nmol/L in deficiency states.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Alkaline Phosphatase (ALP): Elevated due to increased bone turnover; X-rays: Show Looser zones or pseudofractures, characteristic of osteomalacia; Bone Density Scan.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Osteomalacia"][/sc]
  • Osteoporosis - Unlike osteomalacia, osteoporosis involves reduced bone density without impaired mineralisation; no elevated ALP is.
  • Fibromyalgia - Diffuse pain and fatigue without biochemical abnormalities or X-ray findings typical of osteomalacia.
  • Rheumatoid Arthritis - May present with pain and fatigue but also joint-specific inflammation and deformities.
  • Related alternative diagnosis - Distinguish from osteomalacia using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.

  • Escalation and safety-netting: Escalate urgently for clinical deterioration, evolving red flags, or failure to improve in suspected osteomalacia.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Osteomalacia"][/sc]
  • Pathological Fractures: Frequent due to weakened bone structure, impacting mobility and quality of life.
  • Severe Deformities: In untreated cases, especially in children, can lead to permanent skeletal deformities.
  • Chronic Pain and Immobility: Result from ongoing bone weakness and fractures, potentially leading to a dependency on assistive devices.
