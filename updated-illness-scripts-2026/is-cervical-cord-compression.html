Cervical Cord Compression
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Cervical Cord Compression"][/sc]
  • Cervical cord compression refers to the mechanical pressure exerted on the cervical segment of the spinal cord, often caused by degenerative changes, trauma, tumour, infection, or congenital abnormalities.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise cervical features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.

Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Cervical Cord Compression"][/sc]
  • Compression of the cervical spinal cord disrupts the transmission of neural signals.
  • Direct mechanical pressure causes neuronal and axonal injury.
  • Ischaemia occurs due to compromised blood flow in the anterior spinal artery.
  • Chronic compression leads to demyelination, gliosis, and eventual neuronal loss.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Cervical Cord Compression"][/sc]
  • Acute phase: Sudden onset, usually following trauma or disc herniation.
  • Subacute phase: Progressive neurological decline over weeks to months.
  • Chronic phase: Long-term deterioration with potential for stabilisation following treatment.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Cervical Cord Compression"][/sc]
  • Ask about neck pain - Localised or radiating pain due to inflammation or irritation of surrounding structures.
  • Ask about paraesthesia - Tingling or numbness, often in the upper limbs, due to sensory pathway disruption.
  • Ask about motor weakness - Weakness or clumsiness in the hands or arms caused by corticospinal tract involvement.
  • Ask about gait instability - Unsteady walking due to impaired proprioception and motor control.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Cervical Cord Compression"][/sc]
  • Check hyperreflexia - Increased deep tendon reflexes due to loss of upper motor neuron inhibition.
  • Check positive Babinski sign - Upgoing plantar response, indicative of corticospinal tract damage.
  • Check spasticity - Muscle stiffness or increased tone from disinhibition of motor pathways.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Cervical Cord Compression"][/sc]
  • Sphincter dysfunction - Urinary or faecal incontinence due to sacral cord compression, signifying severe cord involvement.
  • Rapid neurological decline - Sudden worsening of motor or sensory function, often indicative of acute trauma or compression.
  • Bilateral symptoms - May indicate central cord involvement and higher severity.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Cervical Cord Compression"][/sc]
  • Chronic, mild paraesthesia - May be dismissed as peripheral neuropathy.
  • Unilateral symptoms - Sometimes misdiagnosed as radiculopathy without considering central causes.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Cervical Cord Compression"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: MRI of the cervical spine - Identifies compression, cord signal changes, and degenerative pathology.
  • Targeted and monitoring: X-ray - May show degenerative changes such as spondylosis or fractures; Electromyography (EMG) - Assesses nerve function and rules out peripheral.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Cervical Cord Compression"][/sc]
  • Multiple sclerosis - Differentiated by disseminated lesions on MRI and a relapsing-remitting course.
  • Peripheral neuropathy - Distal symmetrical symptoms and normal MRI of the cervical spine.
  • Motor neurone disease - Lack of sensory symptoms and progressive motor deficits without structural compression.
  • Alternative serious diagnosis - Distinguishing history, examination, and targeted investigations do not support cervical cord compression.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Cervical Cord Compression"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Assess physiological stability and urgent complications at presentation, and initiate early stabilisation where required.
  • Core care: Deliver condition-specific treatment for cervical cord compression, address underlying drivers, and involve appropriate specialist support.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Reassess symptom trajectory, objective markers, and treatment response over time to detect complications early.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Worsening symptoms, physiological deterioration, or failure to improve should trigger urgent reassessment for severe or alternative pathology in cervical cord compression.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Cervical Cord Compression"][/sc]
  • Permanent neurological deficits - From prolonged compression or delayed treatment.
  • Paralysis - In severe untreated cases, loss of motor function below the compression level.
  • Chronic pain - Due to irreversible nerve damage or post-surgical outcomes.
