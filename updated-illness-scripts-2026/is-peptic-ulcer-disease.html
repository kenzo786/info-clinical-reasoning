Peptic Ulcer Disease (PUD)
PUD Definition [sc name="ai-illness-script-section-links" Q="Peptic Ulcer Disease" select="101" url-2="" css-2="" url-3="" css-3="" edit="886"][/sc] [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Peptic Ulcer Disease (PUD)"][/sc]
  • Peptic Ulcer Disease (PUD) refers to mucosal erosions extending through the muscularis mucosae, occurring most commonly in the stomach (gastric ulcers) and duodenum (duodenal ulcers).
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise peptic features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.

  • Prevention: Reducing NSAID use or co-prescribing proton pump inhibitors (PPIs) for high-risk patients can lower ulcer risk. Eradicating H. pylori in infected individuals reduces recurrence. Smoking cessation and moderating alcohol intake contribute to mucosal protection. Stress management and avoiding excessive caffeine consumption may also help prevent ulcer formation.
PUD Pathophysiology [sc name="ai-illness-script-section-links" Q="Peptic Ulcer Disease" select="103" url-2="" css-2="" url-3="" css-3="" edit="886"][/sc] [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Peptic Ulcer Disease (PUD)"][/sc]
  • The development of PUD is a result of the imbalance between mucosal defence and damaging agents. Key factors include:
  • H. pylori infection: This bacterium colonises the gastric mucosa, causing inflammation and weakening the protective mucus layer, allowing gastric acid to damage the tissue.
  • NSAIDs: These medications inhibit cyclooxygenase (COX) enzymes, decreasing the production of prostaglandins, which are essential for maintaining the gastric mucosal barrier.
  • Gastric acid hypersecretion: Conditions like Zollinger-Ellison syndrome cause excessive acid production, overwhelming the mucosal defences.
  • Other factors: Smoking, alcohol use, and stress have been linked to increased risk by impairing mucosal defences.
PUD Time Course [sc name="ai-illness-script-section-links" Q="Peptic Ulcer Disease" select="104" url-2="" css-2="" url-3="" css-3="" edit="886"][/sc] [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Peptic Ulcer Disease (PUD)"][/sc]
  • Initial phase: Symptoms may develop gradually over weeks to months, with periods of symptomatic relief between episodes of pain.
  • Chronic phase: Long-term, intermittent symptoms may continue if underlying factors (e.g., H. pylori or NSAID use) are not addressed. Complications like bleeding or perforation can occur at any stage.
  • Acute exacerbation: Sudden complications such as bleeding, perforation, or obstruction can occur, especially in untreated or poorly managed cases.
PUD Symptoms [sc name="ai-illness-script-section-links" Q="Peptic Ulcer Disease" select="105" url-2="" css-2="" url-3="" css-3="" edit="886"][/sc] [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Peptic Ulcer Disease (PUD)"][/sc]
  • Ask about epigastric pain: This is the most common symptom, often described as a burning or gnawing pain, which may improve with food (duodenal ulcer), with associated clinical features.
  • Ask about bloating and fullness: Due to impaired gastric emptying and irritation of the stomach lining.
  • Ask about nausea and vomiting: These symptoms occur due to irritation and inflammation of the gastric mucosa, especially in gastric ulcers.
  • Ask about haematemesis or melaena: Occurs in cases with ulcer bleeding. The blood arises from erosion into gastric vessels.
  • Ask about asymptomatic: Up to 20% of patients, particularly the elderly or those taking NSAIDs, may have ulcers without noticeable symptoms until complications arise.
PUD Signs [sc name="ai-illness-script-section-links" Q="Peptic Ulcer Disease" select="106" url-2="" css-2="" url-3="" css-3="" edit="886"][/sc] [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Peptic Ulcer Disease (PUD)"][/sc]
  • Check epigastric tenderness: Localised pain on palpation due to inflammation or ulceration of the stomach or duodenal wall.
  • Check pallor: May indicate anaemia from chronic blood loss in ulcer bleeding.
  • Check hypotension or tachycardia: Suggestive of significant bleeding or perforation.
  • Check rebound tenderness/guarding: These are signs of peritonitis, often indicating perforation of the ulcer.
PUD Red Flag Features [sc name="ai-illness-script-section-links" Q="Peptic Ulcer Disease" select="107" url-2="" css-2="" url-3="" css-3="" edit="886"][/sc] [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Peptic Ulcer Disease (PUD)"][/sc]
  • Haematemesis (vomiting blood): Suggests active ulcer bleeding, potentially leading to haemorrhagic shock if untreated.
  • Melaena (black, tarry stools): Indicates digested blood from an upper GI bleed.
  • Sudden severe abdominal pain with rigidity: May indicate perforation, causing peritonitis and requiring urgent surgical intervention.
  • Unexplained weight loss: May indicate malignancy or chronic disease-related malnutrition.
PUD Atypical Presentations [sc name="ai-illness-script-section-links" Q="Peptic Ulcer Disease" select="108" url-2="" css-2="" url-3="" css-3="" edit="886"][/sc] [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Peptic Ulcer Disease (PUD)"][/sc]
  • Silent PUD: Common in older adults or patients on NSAIDs, who may not experience typical pain but present with complications like bleeding or perforation.
  • Chest pain: Occasionally, PUD can mimic cardiac pain, leading to misdiagnosis as angina or myocardial infarction.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask peptic ulcer disease (pud).
PUD Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ai-illness-script-section-links" Q="Peptic Ulcer Disease" select="109" url-2="" css-2="" url-3="" css-3="" edit="886"][/sc] [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Peptic Ulcer Disease (PUD)"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Upper GI endoscopy: The gold standard for diagnosing PUD, allowing direct visualisation of ulcers and biopsies for H. pylori or malignancy.
  • Targeted and monitoring: FBC (Full Blood Count): May show anaemia due to chronic blood loss from ulceration; Faecal occult blood test: May be positive.

PUD Complications [sc name="ai-illness-script-section-links" Q="Peptic Ulcer Disease" select="115" url-2="" css-2="" url-3="" css-3="" edit="886"][/sc] [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Peptic Ulcer Disease (PUD)"][/sc]
  • Bleeding: Occurs in 15-20% of ulcers. Chronic blood loss may lead to anaemia, while acute bleeding can cause haemorrhagic shock.
  • Perforation: Occurs in about 5% of ulcers, leading to peritonitis. Surgical intervention is often required.
  • Gastric outlet obstruction: Scarring from chronic ulcers may lead to obstruction, causing persistent vomiting and weight loss.
  • Malignancy: Gastric ulcers, but not duodenal ulcers, carry a small risk of malignant transformation, particularly if non-healing despite treatment.
