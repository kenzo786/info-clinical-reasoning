Interstitial Cystitis
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Interstitial Cystitis"][/sc]
  • Interstitial cystitis (IC), also known as bladder pain syndrome (BPS), is a chronic condition characterised by bladder pain or discomfort, urinary urgency, and frequency, in the absence of infection, with clinically relevant features.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise interstitial features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.

  • Higher-risk or missed groups: Interstitial Cystitis may be missed when presentation is atypical or obscured by comorbidity.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Interstitial Cystitis"][/sc]
  • Disruption of the glycosaminoglycan (GAG) layer of the bladder leads to increased permeability to urine solutes.
  • Local inflammation and mast cell activation contribute to chronic pain and hypersensitivity.
  • Neurogenic upregulation results in heightened sensory input from bladder afferents, leading to pain and urgency.
  • Potential autoimmune or chronic infection triggers have been proposed but not consistently confirmed.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Interstitial Cystitis"][/sc]
  • Initial phase: Gradual onset of urinary symptoms and pain, often over months to years.
  • Chronic phase: Persistent or fluctuating symptoms lasting years, often without complete resolution.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Interstitial Cystitis"][/sc]
  • Ask about bladder pain or discomfort - Localised pain, worsened with bladder filling; due to increased sensitivity from bladder afferents.
  • Ask about urinary urgency - Sudden strong urge to urinate, linked to neural upregulation and detrusor hypersensitivity.
  • Ask about increased urinary frequency - Often voiding more than 10 times daily; a compensatory response to avoid bladder discomfort.
  • Ask about nocturia - Frequently waking at night to urinate; linked to disrupted bladder filling sensation.
  • Ask about dyspareunia - Pain during intercourse, possibly due to pelvic floor dysfunction or referred pain.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Interstitial Cystitis"][/sc]
  • Check pelvic tenderness - Detected on bimanual examination, indicating localised inflammation or pelvic floor dysfunction.
  • Check absence of other pathological signs - Important in ruling out infections or structural abnormalities.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Interstitial Cystitis"][/sc]
  • Haematuria - May indicate malignancy or urolithiasis; requires further evaluation.
  • Significant weight loss - Suggests possible systemic illness, such as cancer.
  • Severe, sudden onset pain - Raises concern for acute pathology, e.g., urolithiasis or infection.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Interstitial Cystitis"][/sc]
  • Mild or intermittent symptoms - Can delay diagnosis; may still show bladder wall inflammation on cystoscopy.
  • Predominantly pelvic pain - Without overt urinary symptoms, leading to misdiagnosis as chronic pelvic pain syndrome.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask interstitial cystitis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Interstitial Cystitis"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Urinalysis - Excludes infection (negative nitrites/leukocytes) and haematuria; Urine culture - Rules out bacterial infection.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Cystoscopy - Visualisation of bladder wall; findings may include Hunner’s lesions or glomerulations; Potassium sensitivity test - Rarely used; identifies bladder.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Interstitial Cystitis"][/sc]
  • Recurrent urinary tract infections (UTIs) - Differentiated by positive cultures and response to antibiotics.
  • Overactive bladder - Features urgency and frequency but lacks pain as a hallmark symptom.
  • Endometriosis - May mimic IC but has cyclical symptoms linked to menstruation.
  • Bladder cancer - Distinguished by persistent haematuria and possible mass on imaging.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Interstitial Cystitis"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Assess severity, physiological stability, and immediate complications.
  • Core care: Deliver condition-specific management for interstitial cystitis, treat underlying drivers, and coordinate appropriate specialist input.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Reassess symptom trajectory, examination findings, and objective markers to confirm response in interstitial cystitis.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Escalate urgently for clinical deterioration, evolving red flags, or failure to improve in suspected interstitial cystitis.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Interstitial Cystitis"][/sc]
  • Chronic pelvic pain and functional impairment: Persistent symptoms can markedly reduce daily activity, sleep quality, and mental wellbeing.
  • Psychological distress: Ongoing pain and urinary urgency are associated with anxiety, low mood, and reduced quality of life.
  • Sexual dysfunction: Dyspareunia and pain flares can impair intimacy and relationships over time.
