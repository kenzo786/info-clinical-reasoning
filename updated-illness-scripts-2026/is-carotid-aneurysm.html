Carotid Aneurysm
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Carotid Aneurysm"][/sc]
  • A carotid aneurysm is a rare but potentially life-threatening condition characterised by abnormal dilation or ballooning of the carotid artery wall, typically in the cervical portion.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise carotid features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.

Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Carotid Aneurysm"][/sc]
  • The pathophysiology of a carotid aneurysm involves degeneration of the arterial wall's media layer, often due to atherosclerosis, connective tissue disorders, or trauma. This results in weakening and eventual dilation of the vessel wall. As the aneurysm grows, the vessel wall becomes prone to rupture, thrombosis, or embolism, with the latter potentially causing ischaemic stroke.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Carotid Aneurysm"][/sc]
  • The time course of carotid aneurysms can vary. They may be asymptomatic for years or present with acute symptoms if complications arise. Slowly expanding aneurysms may lead to chronic neurological deficits or cranial nerve involvement, while acute rupture or thromboembolic events can lead to rapid, life-threatening neurological deterioration.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Carotid Aneurysm"][/sc]
  • Consider asymptomatic - Many carotid aneurysms remain asymptomatic until complications arise. This is due to their slow growth and lack of impact on surrounding structures.
  • Ask about transient ischaemic attacks (TIAs) - Caused by embolisation of small thrombi from the aneurysm into the cerebral circulation, leading to temporary neurological deficits.
  • Ask about headache or neck pain - Due to the mass effect of the aneurysm on surrounding tissues, particularly the cervical nerves.
  • Ask about cranial nerve palsies - If the aneurysm compresses the adjacent cranial nerves (e.g., vagus or hypoglossal nerve), leading to hoarseness or tongue deviation.
  • Ask about stroke-like symptoms - Sudden onset of hemiparesis, aphasia, or vision loss may occur if the aneurysm ruptures or causes thromboembolism, leading to cerebral infarction.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Carotid Aneurysm"][/sc]
  • Check pulsatile neck mass - A palpable, pulsating mass in the neck may be observed, particularly in cases of large aneurysms.
  • Check carotid bruit - A turbulent sound heard on auscultation due to disturbed blood flow within the aneurysm or associated stenosis.
  • Check cranial nerve deficits - Weakness or palsy of cranial nerves IX, X, XI, or XII, leading to symptoms such as hoarseness or dysphagia.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Carotid Aneurysm"][/sc]
  • Sudden-onset stroke symptoms - May indicate rupture or thromboembolism from the aneurysm, requiring urgent evaluation to prevent permanent neurological damage.
  • Rapidly enlarging neck mass - Suggests impending rupture, which can lead to catastrophic haemorrhage.
  • Severe headache or neurological deficits - These may signify aneurysm rupture or impending rupture, particularly if accompanied by signs of raised intracranial pressure.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Carotid Aneurysm"][/sc]
  • Isolated cranial nerve palsies - May be mistaken for other causes such as tumours or neuropathy, as the aneurysm compresses adjacent cranial nerves without overt vascular symptoms.
  • Asymptomatic with incidental finding - Discovered during imaging for unrelated issues, with no prior symptoms or signs of vascular compromise.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features or comorbidity can mask the underlying diagnosis.

  • Immediate priorities: Assess physiological stability and urgent complications at presentation, and initiate early stabilisation where required.
  • Core care: Deliver condition-specific treatment for carotid aneurysm, address underlying drivers, and involve appropriate specialist support.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Reassess symptom trajectory, objective markers, and treatment response over time to detect complications early.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Worsening symptoms, physiological deterioration, or failure to improve should trigger urgent reassessment for severe or alternative pathology in carotid aneurysm.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Carotid Aneurysm"][/sc]
  • Stroke - The most serious complication, often resulting from embolism or thrombus formation within the aneurysm.
  • Rupture - Aneurysm rupture can cause catastrophic haemorrhage, particularly if it occurs in a critical region, such as near the base of the skull.
  • Cranial nerve compression - Can lead to permanent neurological deficits if the aneurysm compresses adjacent nerves over time.
