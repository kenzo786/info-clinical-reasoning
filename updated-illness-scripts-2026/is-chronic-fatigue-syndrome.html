Chronic Fatigue Syndrome (CFS)
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Chronic Fatigue Syndrome (CFS)"][/sc]
  • Chronic Fatigue Syndrome (CFS), also known as Myalgic Encephalomyelitis (ME), is a complex and debilitating condition characterised by persistent, unexplained fatigue lasting for at least six months.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise fatigue features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.

  • CFS is more common in females than males, with a typical onset between the ages of 20 and 40.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Chronic Fatigue Syndrome (CFS)"][/sc]
  • The pathophysiology of CFS is poorly understood and likely multifactorial. Potential mechanisms include:
  • Immune dysfunction: Abnormal immune responses, such as chronic low-grade inflammation and altered cytokine profiles, are commonly reported.
  • Neuroendocrine dysregulation: Dysfunctions in the hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal (HPA) axis, leading to altered stress hormone levels.
  • Mitochondrial dysfunction: Impaired energy metabolism at the cellular level, potentially explaining the hallmark fatigue.
  • Autonomic nervous system imbalance: Some studies suggest autonomic dysfunction, particularly involving abnormal heart rate and blood pressure responses.
  • Central sensitisation: Amplified pain and fatigue perception due to abnormal sensory processing in the central nervous system.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Chronic Fatigue Syndrome (CFS)"][/sc]
  • Acute phase: Symptoms often follow an acute viral or stressful event and persist beyond normal recovery time.
  • Chronic phase: Fatigue and other symptoms last for months or years. Fluctuations in symptom severity are common, with relapses often triggered by overexertion or stress.
  • Remission/relapse: Some patients experience periods of improvement, but complete recovery is rare. Relapses are common, and the condition may worsen over time.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Chronic Fatigue Syndrome (CFS)"][/sc]
  • Ask about fatigue - Persistent, overwhelming fatigue is the hallmark symptom. It is thought to result from mitochondrial dysfunction and impaired energy metabolism.
  • Ask about post-exertional malaise - Worsening of symptoms following physical or mental activity, likely due to abnormal energy production and autonomic dysregulation.
  • Ask about unrefreshing sleep - Despite adequate sleep, patients feel unrefreshed, possibly due to abnormal sleep architecture and HPA axis dysfunction.
  • Ask about cognitive difficulties ("brain fog") - Memory impairment and concentration problems, likely due to central nervous system involvement and neuroinflammation.
  • Ask about muscle and joint pain - Widespread pain without clear inflammatory markers, potentially due to central sensitisation and altered pain perception.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Chronic Fatigue Syndrome (CFS)"][/sc]
  • Check orthostatic hypotension - Common, possibly due to autonomic dysfunction and impaired vascular regulation.
  • Check tachycardia - Increased heart rate, especially upon standing (postural orthostatic tachycardia syndrome), which can be linked to autonomic nervous system imbalance.
  • Check pallor - In some patients, pallor may be observed, potentially related to poor peripheral circulation.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Chronic Fatigue Syndrome (CFS)"][/sc]
  • Unintentional weight loss - May suggest malignancy or other serious conditions.
  • Neurological signs - Focal neurological deficits (e.g., limb weakness, numbness) could indicate multiple sclerosis or other neurological disorders.
  • Severe night sweats - May indicate infection or malignancy, and is not typically associated with CFS.
  • Lymphadenopathy - Generalised or significant lymph node enlargement could suggest lymphoma or chronic infection.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Chronic Fatigue Syndrome (CFS)"][/sc]
  • Some patients may present with isolated cognitive impairment ("brain fog") or unrefreshing sleep as dominant symptoms, leading to under-recognition of the condition.
  • Older patients may attribute fatigue to ageing, delaying diagnosis.
  • Some patients may have minimal post-exertional malaise but still experience significant fatigue, making differentiation from depression challenging.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features or comorbidity can mask the underlying diagnosis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Chronic Fatigue Syndrome (CFS)"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Full blood count (FBC) - Typically normal. Used to exclude anaemia or infection; Thyroid function tests (TFTs) - Should be normal.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Liver function tests (LFTs) - Normal. Rule out hepatic causes of fatigue; Vitamin B12 and folate levels - To rule out.

Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Chronic Fatigue Syndrome (CFS)"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Assess physiological stability and urgent complications at presentation, and initiate early stabilisation where required.
  • Core care: Deliver condition-specific treatment for chronic fatigue syndrome (cfs), address underlying drivers, and involve appropriate specialist support.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Reassess symptom trajectory, objective markers, and treatment response over time to detect complications early.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Worsening symptoms, physiological deterioration, or failure to improve should trigger urgent reassessment for severe or alternative pathology in chronic fatigue syndrome (cfs).
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Chronic Fatigue Syndrome (CFS)"][/sc]
  • Depression and anxiety: Common due to the chronic and disabling nature of the illness.
  • Deconditioning: Physical inactivity can lead to muscle weakness and cardiovascular deconditioning.
  • Social isolation: The severe fatigue may result in reduced ability to engage in social and occupational activities.
