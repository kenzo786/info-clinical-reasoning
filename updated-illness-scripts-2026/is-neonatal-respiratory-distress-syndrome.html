Neonatal Respiratory Distress Syndrome (RDS)
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Neonatal Respiratory Distress Syndrome (RDS)"][/sc]
  • Neonatal Respiratory Distress Syndrome (RDS) is a condition characterised by insufficient surfactant production in the lungs of preterm infants, leading to alveolar collapse, impaired gas exchange, and respiratory distress.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise neonatal features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Neonatal Respiratory Distress Syndrome (RDS)"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Neonatal Respiratory Distress Syndrome (RDS)"][/sc]
  • Risk factors: Risk factors for neonatal respiratory distress syndrome (rds) include relevant predisposition, comorbidity, and exposure history.
  • Higher-risk or missed groups: Neonatal Respiratory Distress Syndrome (RDS) may be missed when presentation is atypical or obscured by comorbidity.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Neonatal Respiratory Distress Syndrome (RDS)"][/sc]
  • Preterm birth leads to underdeveloped type II pneumocytes in the lungs.
  • Insufficient surfactant production reduces surface tension within alveoli, causing collapse during expiration (atelectasis).
  • Atelectasis leads to impaired gas exchange, hypoxia, and hypercapnia.
  • Secondary consequences include respiratory acidosis and pulmonary vasoconstriction, worsening oxygenation.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Neonatal Respiratory Distress Syndrome (RDS)"][/sc]
  • Onset: Symptoms usually begin within minutes to hours after birth.
  • Progression: Worsens over 24-48 hours if untreated, with increasing respiratory failure.
  • Resolution: Improves gradually over several days with appropriate treatment as surfactant production increases endogenously.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Neonatal Respiratory Distress Syndrome (RDS)"][/sc]
  • Ask about tachypnoea - Increased respiratory rate due to hypoxaemia and hypercapnia.
  • Ask about grunting - A compensatory mechanism to maintain positive end-expiratory pressure and prevent alveolar collapse.
  • Ask about subcostal and intercostal retractions - Use of accessory muscles due to increased work of breathing.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Neonatal Respiratory Distress Syndrome (RDS)"][/sc]
  • Check cyanosis - Bluish discolouration due to hypoxaemia.
  • Check nasal flaring - A compensatory effort to reduce airway resistance and improve oxygenation.
  • Check diminished breath sounds - Reflects reduced air entry due to alveolar collapse.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Neonatal Respiratory Distress Syndrome (RDS)"][/sc]
  • Severe hypoxaemia unresponsive to oxygen therapy - Indicates potential complications such as pneumothorax or persistent pulmonary hypertension of the newborn (PPHN).
  • Bradycardia - Suggestive of critical hypoxia and impending respiratory failure.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Neonatal Respiratory Distress Syndrome (RDS)"][/sc]
  • Late onset after birth in infants with mild surfactant deficiency or infection.
  • Minimal respiratory distress despite severe hypoxaemia, seen in cases of mixed aetiologies like congenital diaphragmatic hernia.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask neonatal respiratory distress syndrome (rds).
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Neonatal Respiratory Distress Syndrome (RDS)"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Chest X-ray - Ground-glass opacities and air bronchograms indicative of alveolar collapse.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Arterial blood gas - Hypoxaemia, hypercapnia, and respiratory acidosis; Pulse oximetry - Persistent oxygen desaturation despite oxygen therapy.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Neonatal Respiratory Distress Syndrome (RDS)"][/sc]
  • Transient tachypnoea of the newborn - Self-limiting condition with milder respiratory distress and normal chest X-ray.
  • Pneumonia - Presents with fever and sepsis markers, along with lung infiltrates on imaging.
  • Persistent pulmonary hypertension of the newborn (PPHN) - Associated with severe hypoxaemia and preductal-postductal oxygen saturation differences.
  • Related alternative diagnosis - Distinguish from neonatal respiratory distress syndrome (rds) using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Neonatal Respiratory Distress Syndrome (RDS)"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Assess severity, physiological stability, and immediate complications.
  • Core care: Deliver condition-specific management for neonatal respiratory distress syndrome (rds), treat underlying drivers, and coordinate appropriate specialist input.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Reassess symptom trajectory, examination findings, and objective markers to confirm response in neonatal respiratory distress syndrome (rds).
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Escalate urgently for clinical deterioration, evolving red flags, or failure to improve in suspected neonatal respiratory distress syndrome (rds).
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Neonatal Respiratory Distress Syndrome (RDS)"][/sc]
  • Bronchopulmonary dysplasia (BPD) - Chronic lung disease resulting from prolonged mechanical ventilation and oxygen therapy.
  • Intraventricular haemorrhage - Due to fragile germinal matrix vessels in preterm infants.
  • Retinopathy of prematurity - Caused by oxygen therapy in preterm infants.
