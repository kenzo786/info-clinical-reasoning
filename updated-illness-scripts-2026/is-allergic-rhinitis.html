Allergic Rhinitis
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Allergic Rhinitis"][/sc]
  • Allergic rhinitis is IgE-mediated inflammation of the nasal mucosa triggered by allergen exposure, causing recurrent upper-airway symptoms.
  • UKMLA essentials: seasonal sneeze-rhinorrhoea symptoms, unilateral blockage or epistaxis concern, diagnose clinically with allergen correlation.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Allergic Rhinitis"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Allergic Rhinitis"][/sc]
  • Risk factors: Risk of allergic rhinitis rises with relevant predisposition, comorbidity, and exposure profile.
  • Higher-risk or missed groups: Allergic Rhinitis may be missed when presentations are atypical or comorbidity obscures key features.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Allergic Rhinitis"][/sc]
  • Exposure to allergen: Initial sensitisation occurs when allergens (e.g., pollen, dust mites) are inhaled.
  • IgE production: Allergen-specific IgE is produced by plasma cells and binds to receptors on mast cells in the nasal mucosa.
  • Subsequent exposure: Re-exposure leads to allergen cross-linking on mast cells, causing degranulation.
  • Release of mediators: Histamine, leukotrienes, and prostaglandins cause vasodilation, increased permeability, and stimulation of sensory nerves.
  • Late-phase reaction: Recruitment of eosinophils and other inflammatory cells prolongs the response, leading to persistent symptoms.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Allergic Rhinitis"][/sc]
  • Acute phase: Onset occurs minutes after allergen exposure, dominated by sneezing and itching.
  • Late phase: Begins 4–6 hours post-exposure, with sustained nasal congestion and eosinophilic infiltration.
  • Chronic phase: Persistent symptoms if exposure is ongoing or untreated.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Allergic Rhinitis"][/sc]
  • Ask about nasal congestion: Caused by vasodilation and increased vascular permeability leading to mucosal oedema.
  • Ask about sneezing: Triggered by histamine acting on sensory nerves.
  • Ask about rhinorrhoea: Clear nasal discharge due to increased glandular secretion.
  • Ask about nasal itching: Histamine-induced stimulation of nerve endings.
  • Ask about postnasal drip: Excess mucus drainage into the throat, especially in perennial rhinitis.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Allergic Rhinitis"][/sc]
  • Check allergic salute: Repeated upward rubbing of the nose leading to a transverse nasal crease.
  • Check pale, oedematous nasal mucosa: Caused by chronic inflammation and vasodilation.
  • Check conjunctival injection: Hyperaemia of conjunctiva due to associated allergic conjunctivitis.
  • Check dark circles under eyes (allergic shiners): Due to venous stasis from nasal congestion.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Allergic Rhinitis"][/sc]
  • Unilateral symptoms: May indicate a nasal polyp, foreign body, or tumour.
  • Persistent facial pain: Suggests sinusitis or malignancy.
  • Bloody nasal discharge: Raises suspicion of malignancy or granulomatosis with polyangiitis.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Allergic Rhinitis"][/sc]
  • Chronic cough: May be misdiagnosed as asthma or GERD due to postnasal drip.
  • Fatigue: Resulting from disrupted sleep due to nasal congestion.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features or comorbidity can mask the underlying diagnosis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Allergic Rhinitis"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Clinical diagnosis from symptom pattern, trigger history, and nasal examination for pale swollen mucosa, turbinate oedema, and associated conjunctival signs.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Skin-prick or specific IgE testing when diagnosis is uncertain or immunotherapy is considered; assess asthma comorbidity and response to intranasal treatment over time.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Allergic Rhinitis"][/sc]
  • A 25-year-old man reports recurrent sneezing, clear rhinorrhoea, itchy eyes, and nasal blockage each spring, worse outdoors and improved indoors.
  • Adult with allergen-linked intermittent nasal and ocular symptoms without infective features, consistent with allergic rhinitis.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Allergic Rhinitis"][/sc]
  • Non-allergic rhinitis - Persistent nasal symptoms without clear allergen trigger or IgE sensitisation support a non-allergic mechanism.
  • Chronic rhinosinusitis - Facial pressure, hyposmia, and persistent mucopurulent discharge suggest sinus disease rather than isolated allergic rhinitis.
  • Nasal polyps - Chronic obstruction with reduced smell and polypoid lesions on examination indicates polyp disease.
  • Medication-induced rhinitis - Symptom onset linked to topical decongestant overuse or triggering medicines suggests drug-related rhinitis.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Allergic Rhinitis"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Confirm diagnosis, assess sleep and functional impact, and identify red-flag unilateral symptoms needing ENT review.
  • Core care: Allergen avoidance advice, regular intranasal corticosteroid therapy, and antihistamine strategies form the main management pathway.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Review symptom control, adherence, inhaler comorbidity plans, and adverse effects, stepping treatment according to seasonal or persistent burden.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Persistent unilateral obstruction, recurrent epistaxis, marked anosmia, or poor control despite optimised therapy should prompt specialist reassessment.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Allergic Rhinitis"][/sc]
  • Sinusitis: Due to obstruction of sinus drainage.
  • Eustachian tube dysfunction: Leading to otitis media.
  • Impaired quality of life: Poor sleep and daytime fatigue.
