Medication-Induced Muscle Pain and Weakness
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Medication-Induced Muscle Pain and Weakness"][/sc]
  • Medication-induced muscle pain and weakness refers to myalgia, myopathy, or myositis caused by certain medications, most commonly statins, corticosteroids, or antimalarials.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise medication features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.

  • Higher-risk or missed groups: Medication-Induced Muscle Pain and Weakness may be missed when presentation is atypical or obscured by comorbidity.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Medication-Induced Muscle Pain and Weakness"][/sc]
  • Drugs like statins inhibit HMG-CoA reductase, impairing cholesterol synthesis and disrupting mitochondrial function in muscle cells, leading to energy depletion and muscle damage.
  • Corticosteroids induce muscle atrophy via proteolysis and reduced protein synthesis, weakening the musculature.
  • Antimalarials interfere with lysosomal function, causing accumulation of toxic substances within muscle fibres.
  • Severe muscle breakdown (rhabdomyolysis) leads to the release of myoglobin, which can precipitate acute kidney injury (AKI).
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Medication-Induced Muscle Pain and Weakness"][/sc]
  • Onset: Symptoms typically develop within weeks of starting the medication, but delayed onset can occur, particularly with chronic use.
  • Progression: Mild myalgia may remain stable or worsen with continued drug exposure. Severe cases, like rhabdomyolysis, can progress rapidly.
  • Resolution: Symptoms often resolve within days to weeks after discontinuing the causative medication.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Medication-Induced Muscle Pain and Weakness"][/sc]
  • Ask about muscle Pain (Myalgia): Diffuse aching often localised to larger muscle groups, due to inflammation or mitochondrial dysfunction.
  • Ask about muscle Weakness: Reduced strength, particularly in proximal muscles, from impaired energy production and muscle fibre damage.
  • Ask about fatigue: Generalised tiredness due to systemic inflammation and disrupted muscle metabolism.
  • Ask about dark Urine: A hallmark of rhabdomyolysis, caused by myoglobinuria.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Medication-Induced Muscle Pain and Weakness"][/sc]
  • Check muscle Tenderness: Localised pain on palpation due to inflammatory changes.
  • Check decreased Muscle Strength: Notable in proximal limbs during examination, indicative of myopathy.
  • Check oedema: Swelling in severe cases, secondary to inflammation or rhabdomyolysis.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Medication-Induced Muscle Pain and Weakness"][/sc]
  • Severe Weakness: Suggests myositis or early rhabdomyolysis requiring immediate evaluation.
  • Dark Urine: Indicates myoglobinuria, a risk factor for acute kidney injury.
  • Elevated Creatine Kinase (CK): Levels >10 times the upper limit of normal suggest significant muscle damage.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Medication-Induced Muscle Pain and Weakness"][/sc]
  • Focal Muscle Symptoms: Rarely, pain and weakness localise to a single muscle group.
  • Delayed Onset: Symptoms appearing months to years after starting the medication.
  • Asymptomatic CK Elevation: Occurs in some patients with subclinical myopathy.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask medication-induced muscle pain and weakness.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Medication-Induced Muscle Pain and Weakness"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Creatine Kinase (CK): Elevated in most cases, reflecting muscle breakdown.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Urine Myoglobin: Positive in rhabdomyolysis, explaining dark urine; Liver Function Tests (LFTs): Mildly raised transaminases may accompany myopathy due to mitochondrial.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Medication-Induced Muscle Pain and Weakness"][/sc]
  • Polymyalgia Rheumatica - Typically involves morning stiffness and elevated inflammatory markers.
  • Hypothyroid Myopathy - Associated with fatigue, weight gain, and delayed deep tendon reflexes.
  • Inflammatory Myopathies (e.g., Dermatomyositis) - Often presents with skin rash and markedly elevated CK.
  • Related alternative diagnosis - Distinguish from medication-induced muscle pain and weakness using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Medication-Induced Muscle Pain and Weakness"][/sc]
  • Rhabdomyolysis: Can lead to AKI due to myoglobin-induced tubular damage.
  • Chronic Myopathy: Persistent weakness in severe or untreated cases.
