Peripheral Arterial Disease (PAD)
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Peripheral Arterial Disease (PAD)"][/sc]
  • Peripheral Arterial Disease (PAD) is a chronic condition characterised by atherosclerosis of the peripheral arteries, leading to reduced blood flow to the limbs, most commonly the legs.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise peripheral features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.

Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Peripheral Arterial Disease (PAD)"][/sc]
  • Atherosclerosis, the underlying cause of PAD, begins with endothelial injury due to factors like smoking, hypertension, or hyperlipidaemia. This leads to lipid deposition, inflammation, and plaque formation in the arterial walls.
  • As the plaques grow, they narrow the lumen, reducing blood flow. In the limbs, this leads to ischaemia, particularly during exertion when oxygen demand increases.
  • Over time, complete occlusion or plaque rupture can cause acute limb ischaemia, while chronic progressive narrowing leads to intermittent claudication or critical limb ischaemia.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Peripheral Arterial Disease (PAD)"][/sc]
  • Early stage: Asymptomatic or mild claudication; progression may be slow.
  • Moderate stage: Claudication becomes more frequent with increased exertion.
  • Late stage: Critical limb ischaemia, characterised by rest pain, ulcers, and risk of gangrene.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Peripheral Arterial Disease (PAD)"][/sc]
  • Ask about intermittent Claudication - Cramping pain in the calves, thighs, or buttocks during exertion due to reduced blood flow. As exertion increases, oxygen demand exceeds.
  • Ask about rest Pain - In severe PAD, pain at rest due to critically reduced blood flow, especially at night when legs are elevated, reducing arterial.
  • Ask about ulceration and Non-healing Wounds - Poor perfusion results in tissue hypoxia, impairing healing and increasing the risk of skin breakdown and ulcers.
  • Ask about asymptomatic - Many patients, especially in early stages, have no symptoms due to adequate compensatory blood flow at rest.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Peripheral Arterial Disease (PAD)"][/sc]
  • Check diminished Peripheral Pulses - Reduced or absent pulses (e.g., dorsalis pedis, posterior tibial) due to obstructed arterial flow.
  • Check cool, Pale Skin - Ischaemia causes reduced oxygen and nutrient delivery, leading to pallor and cold extremities.
  • Check dependent Rubor - When the leg is lowered, it becomes red due to dilation of superficial vessels in response to chronic ischaemia.
  • Check muscle Atrophy - Chronic lack of blood supply leads to muscle wasting over time.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Peripheral Arterial Disease (PAD)"][/sc]
  • Rest Pain - Indicates critical limb ischaemia, a serious form of PAD requiring urgent intervention to prevent limb loss.
  • Ulceration or Gangrene - Sign of tissue necrosis and very poor blood supply. May lead to amputation if not treated rapidly.
  • Sudden Onset Pain - Suggests acute limb ischaemia due to embolic or thrombotic occlusion. Requires emergency treatment to prevent permanent damage.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Peripheral Arterial Disease (PAD)"][/sc]
  • Non-specific Leg Fatigue - Some patients may report vague fatigue without the classic claudication pain, leading to delayed diagnosis.
  • Atypical Location of Pain - Pain may occur in the thighs or buttocks rather than calves, especially with aortoiliac disease.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask peripheral arterial disease (pad).

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Peripheral Arterial Disease (PAD)"][/sc]
  • Critical Limb Ischaemia - Chronic ischaemia leads to tissue necrosis, ulcers, and gangrene, necessitating amputation in up to 20% of cases.
  • Acute Limb Ischaemia - Sudden blockage can cause irreversible muscle and nerve damage within hours, requiring urgent intervention.
  • Cardiovascular Events - PAD is a strong predictor of myocardial infarction and stroke, with a 3-5 times increased risk of cardiovascular mortality.
