Vertebrobasilar Insufficiency
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Vertebrobasilar Insufficiency"][/sc]
  • Vertebrobasilar insufficiency (VBI), also known as posterior circulation ischemia, refers to the reduced blood flow to the vertebral and basilar arteries, which supply the brainstem, cerebellum, occipital lobes, and thalamus.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise vertebrobasilar features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.

Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Vertebrobasilar Insufficiency"][/sc]
  • The vertebrobasilar arteries supply critical regions of the brain, including the brainstem and cerebellum. VBI occurs due to a reduction in blood flow from atherosclerosis, emboli, or dissection. Atherosclerosis leads to stenosis, reducing perfusion to these areas. Emboli or thrombi can occlude the arteries, leading to transient ischaemic attacks (TIAs) or stroke, while arterial dissection may result from trauma or underlying vessel fragility.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Vertebrobasilar Insufficiency"][/sc]
  • VBI can present acutely, such as during a TIA, or progress over time in chronic cases of atherosclerosis. Symptoms typically last minutes to hours but may recur over weeks or months in episodic attacks. Acute stroke may result in more permanent deficits.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Vertebrobasilar Insufficiency"][/sc]
  • Ask about dizziness/Vertigo - Common due to impaired perfusion of the brainstem and cerebellum, affecting balance and spatial orientation.
  • Ask about diplopia (Double Vision) - Results from ischaemia of the cranial nerves controlling eye movements.
  • Ask about dysarthria (Slurred Speech) - Ischaemia affecting the brainstem can impair speech articulation.
  • Ask about ataxia - Lack of coordination occurs due to cerebellar hypoperfusion.
  • Ask about syncope - Transient global ischaemia may lead to fainting due to insufficient blood flow to the brainstem.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Vertebrobasilar Insufficiency"][/sc]
  • Check nystagmus - Abnormal eye movements due to cerebellar or brainstem involvement.
  • Check ataxia - Unsteady gait or difficulty with coordination, indicating cerebellar ischaemia.
  • Check dysmetria - Impaired ability to control the distance, speed, and power of a movement, often observed in cerebellar dysfunction.
  • Check cranial Nerve Palsies - Involvement of cranial nerves III, IV, and VI may result in eye movement abnormalities.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Vertebrobasilar Insufficiency"][/sc]
  • Sudden onset of symptoms - May suggest a TIA or stroke, requiring urgent evaluation and management.
  • Loss of consciousness - Could indicate extensive brainstem involvement or severe ischaemia.
  • Persistent focal neurological deficits - Likely represents progression to a stroke and requires immediate intervention to prevent further damage.
  • Severe headache - Could indicate dissection or haemorrhagic transformation of an ischaemic event.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Vertebrobasilar Insufficiency"][/sc]
  • Isolated vertigo - While vertigo is a common symptom, it can be easily misattributed to benign causes like labyrinthitis if not accompanied by other neurological signs.
  • Transient visual disturbances - Patients may present with only fleeting visual changes without other overt symptoms, particularly in elderly patients.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask vertebrobasilar insufficiency.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Vertebrobasilar Insufficiency"][/sc]
  • Benign Paroxysmal Positional Vertigo (BPPV) - Unlike VBI, BPPV is triggered by head movements and lacks neurological deficits such as.
  • Labyrinthitis - Typically presents with isolated vertigo and hearing loss, whereas VBI involves multiple neurological symptoms.
  • Meniere's Disease - Features episodic vertigo, hearing loss, and tinnitus; however, there is no ataxia or cranial.
  • Stroke - Stroke causes more prolonged neurological deficits compared to the transient nature of VBI episodes.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Vertebrobasilar Insufficiency"][/sc]
  • Stroke - The most significant complication, leading to permanent neurological deficits. The risk of posterior circulation stroke is significant if VBI is untreated.
  • Dissection - Vertebral artery dissection can occur, particularly after trauma, and may lead to stroke.
  • Recurrent TIAs - Without management, patients may experience repeated episodes, increasing their risk of permanent damage.
