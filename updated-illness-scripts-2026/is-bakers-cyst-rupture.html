Baker's Cyst Rupture
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Baker's Cyst Rupture"][/sc]
  • A Baker's cyst rupture occurs when a popliteal cyst, located in the posterior aspect of the knee joint, bursts, releasing synovial fluid into the surrounding tissues.

Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Baker's Cyst Rupture"][/sc]
  • Baker’s cysts form due to the accumulation of excess synovial fluid in the knee joint, often secondary to underlying joint inflammation (e.g., osteoarthritis or rheumatoid arthritis). When the cyst ruptures, the synovial fluid escapes into the calf, causing an inflammatory response. This leads to sudden pain, swelling, and occasionally discolouration as the fluid tracks down the leg, mimicking deep vein thrombosis (DVT).
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Baker's Cyst Rupture"][/sc]
  • Formation of Baker’s cysts is typically gradual, in response to chronic knee joint pathology. The rupture, however, presents acutely with a sudden onset of symptoms.
  • After rupture, symptoms can persist for days to weeks, with gradual resolution as the fluid is reabsorbed. Some patients may experience recurrent episodes if the underlying joint pathology is not treated.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Baker's Cyst Rupture"][/sc]
  • Ask about sudden calf pain - Caused by the escape of synovial fluid into the calf, irritating surrounding tissues.
  • Ask about calf swelling - Due to fluid accumulation in the calf following cyst rupture.
  • Ask about bruising or discolouration - Occurs as the synovial fluid tracks down the leg, causing inflammation and bruising, particularly around the ankle (crescent sign).
  • Ask about stiffness or reduced range of motion - Due to swelling and discomfort around the knee and calf.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Baker's Cyst Rupture"][/sc]
  • Check tenderness in the calf - Palpable pain in the calf, related to inflammation from leaked synovial fluid.
  • Check calf swelling - Observable swelling due to fluid accumulation in the subcutaneous tissues of the lower leg.
  • Check crescent sign - A characteristic bruising pattern over the medial malleolus of the ankle, which results from synovial fluid tracking downwards.
  • Check warmth in the calf - Reflects inflammation following fluid leakage.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Baker's Cyst Rupture"][/sc]
  • Severe or persistent calf pain and swelling - Could indicate deep vein thrombosis (DVT), which is a significant complication if misdiagnosed.
  • Increased warmth and erythema - Suggests possible superimposed infection or severe inflammation.
  • Shortness of breath - A rare but serious complication is pulmonary embolism due to DVT from the affected leg.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Baker's Cyst Rupture"][/sc]
  • Some patients may present with minimal symptoms, or chronic intermittent swelling of the calf, without the classic sudden onset, leading to delayed diagnosis.
  • In elderly or less active individuals, calf pain may be mild or confused with symptoms of other chronic conditions such as venous insufficiency.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features or comorbidity can mask the underlying diagnosis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Baker's Cyst Rupture"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Ultrasound of the leg - Typically shows fluid collection in the calf, confirming the rupture, and helps exclude DVT.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Doppler ultrasound - Performed to rule out DVT, particularly if the clinical presentation is unclear; MRI - Useful in complex cases.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Baker's Cyst Rupture"][/sc]
  • Recurrent rupture - Occurs if the underlying joint disease is not adequately managed.
  • Deep vein thrombosis (DVT) - A misdiagnosis of DVT can occur due to similar presentations, and untreated DVT can lead to pulmonary embolism.
  • Superimposed infection - Rare but possible, particularly in immunocompromised patients.
