Medication-Induced Diarrhoea
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Medication-Induced Diarrhoea"][/sc]
  • Medication-induced diarrhoea refers to the onset of diarrhoea due to the side effects of medications.

Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Medication-Induced Diarrhoea"][/sc]
  • Different medications cause diarrhoea through various mechanisms:
    • Osmotic effects: Certain drugs, such as magnesium-containing antacids, draw water into the intestine, causing loose stools.
    • Alteration of gut flora: Antibiotics disrupt the normal gut microbiota, reducing colonisation resistance and promoting overgrowth of pathogens, such as Clostridium difficile, leading to diarrhoea.
    • Increased motility: Prokinetic drugs like metoclopramide stimulate bowel movements, resulting in faster transit and decreased water absorption.
    • Mucosal injury: Nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs) may cause direct irritation or inflammation of the gastrointestinal mucosa, resulting in diarrhoea.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Medication-Induced Diarrhoea"][/sc]
  • Medication-induced diarrhoea can present acutely, within hours to days of starting a medication, or subacutely, developing weeks after exposure. The condition may resolve after discontinuation of the offending drug, but in some cases, diarrhoea may persist, particularly if complications like infections or mucosal damage occur.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Medication-Induced Diarrhoea"][/sc]
  • Ask about increased stool frequency and liquidity – The hallmark of diarrhoea, caused by reduced water absorption in the colon or increased intestinal secretion. The severity.
  • Ask about abdominal cramping – Commonly due to increased gut motility or irritation of the gastrointestinal mucosa.
  • Ask about urgency – This often accompanies frequent stools and results from increased peristaltic activity.
  • Ask about fever – More likely if diarrhoea is related to an infection such as Clostridium difficile overgrowth post-antibiotic use.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Medication-Induced Diarrhoea"][/sc]
  • Check dehydration – Seen in patients with severe or prolonged diarrhoea, leading to signs like dry mucous membranes and reduced skin turgor.
  • Check abdominal tenderness – Particularly over the lower abdomen, indicating irritation or inflammation of the bowel mucosa.
  • Check fever – May be observed if an infectious process, such as C. difficile , is present.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Medication-Induced Diarrhoea"][/sc]
  • Bloody diarrhoea – May indicate severe colitis, gastrointestinal bleeding, or an infection like C. difficile that requires urgent intervention.
  • Severe dehydration – Characterised by hypotension, tachycardia, and oliguria, it signals significant fluid loss and may lead to shock.
  • Weight loss – Persistent weight loss may signify chronic malabsorption or severe colitis due to continued drug use.
  • High fever (≥38.5°C) – Suggests a serious underlying infection or systemic inflammatory response, potentially related to sepsis.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Medication-Induced Diarrhoea"][/sc]
  • Some patients, particularly the elderly, may present with confusion or lethargy due to dehydration rather than overt diarrhoea, leading to a delayed diagnosis.
  • Occult diarrhoea, where the patient experiences faecal incontinence rather than obvious diarrhoea, can occur, especially in frail individuals.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask medication-induced diarrhoea.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Medication-Induced Diarrhoea"][/sc]
  • Severe dehydration – Due to excessive fluid loss, particularly in the elderly or those with comorbidities.
  • Pseudomembranous colitis – A severe form of colitis, typically associated with C. difficile infection, leading to significant morbidity.
  • Electrolyte imbalances – Prolonged diarrhoea can cause hypokalaemia and metabolic acidosis, requiring correction.
