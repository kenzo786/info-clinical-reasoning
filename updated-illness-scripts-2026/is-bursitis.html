Bursitis
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Bursitis"][/sc]
  • Bursitis refers to inflammation of a bursa, a small fluid-filled sac that reduces friction between tissues such as bones, tendons, and muscles.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise bursitis features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Bursitis"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Bursitis"][/sc]
  • Worldwide, bursitis is prevalent among individuals involved in repetitive activities or occupations requiring prolonged pressure on joints.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Bursitis"][/sc]
  • Repetitive microtrauma or direct pressure leads to irritation of the synovial lining of the bursa, resulting in increased production of synovial fluid and swelling.
  • Inflammatory mediators, such as prostaglandins and cytokines, exacerbate pain and tissue damage.
  • Infective bursitis occurs when pathogens, often Staphylococcus aureus, invade the bursa, triggering an acute inflammatory response.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Bursitis"][/sc]
  • Acute phase: Rapid onset of pain, swelling, and restricted joint movement within days.
  • Chronic phase: Symptoms persist over weeks to months, often due to ongoing mechanical stress or incomplete resolution of acute inflammation.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Bursitis"][/sc]
  • Ask about localised Pain - Due to the irritation of sensory nerve endings within the inflamed bursa.
  • Ask about swelling - Caused by excess synovial fluid production as part of the inflammatory process.
  • Ask about reduced Range of Motion - Results from pain and mechanical obstruction due to swelling.
  • Ask about erythema and Warmth (in septic bursitis) - Reflects increased local vascular permeability and immune response.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Bursitis"][/sc]
  • Check tenderness Over the Bursa - Indicative of local inflammation.
  • Check visible Swelling - Suggests fluid accumulation within the bursa.
  • Check restricted Movement - Joint mobility is impaired due to pain or mechanical effects of swelling.
  • Check fluctuance (in septic bursitis) - Suggests an abscess within the bursa.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Bursitis"][/sc]
  • Fever - May indicate septic bursitis, requiring urgent intervention.
  • Severe Pain Unresponsive to Treatment - Suggests complications such as abscess formation or necrosis.
  • Rapidly Spreading Redness - Suggestive of cellulitis or deep tissue infection.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Bursitis"][/sc]
  • Painless swelling, especially in chronic cases, due to reduced inflammatory response over time.
  • Systemic symptoms (e.g., fatigue, malaise) in cases of autoimmune-associated bursitis.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features or comorbidity can mask the underlying diagnosis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Bursitis"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Ultrasound - Reveals bursal fluid collection and thickened synovium.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Targeted investigations to confirm diagnosis, assess complications, and monitor response in bursitis.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Bursitis"][/sc]
  • A 45-year-old male presents with a 3-day history of painful swelling over the right elbow, worsened by movement. Examination reveals local tenderness, warmth, and restricted motion.
  • Adult with symptom pattern and objective findings suggestive of bursitis, requiring focused confirmation and risk stratification.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Bursitis"][/sc]
  • Gout - Presence of tophi or history of hyperuricaemia.
  • Cellulitis - Diffuse redness without discrete swelling.
  • Septic Arthritis - Typically involves joint space, with more severe systemic features.
  • Alternative serious diagnosis - Distinguishing history, examination, and targeted investigations do not support bursitis.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Bursitis"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Assess physiological stability and urgent complications at presentation, and initiate early stabilisation where required.
  • Core care: Deliver condition-specific treatment for bursitis, address underlying drivers, and involve appropriate specialist support.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Reassess symptom trajectory, objective markers, and treatment response over time to detect complications early.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Worsening symptoms, physiological deterioration, or failure to improve should trigger urgent reassessment for severe or alternative pathology in bursitis.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Bursitis"][/sc]
  • Chronic Bursitis - Persistent symptoms due to fibrosis or recurrent irritation.
  • Sepsis - Rare but severe if infection spreads systemically.
