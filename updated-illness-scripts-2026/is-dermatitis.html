Dermatitis
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Dermatitis"][/sc]
  • Dermatitis, commonly known as eczema, is an inflammatory skin condition characterised by erythema (redness), pruritus (itchiness), and in some cases, vesiculation or scaling.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise dermatitis features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.

Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Dermatitis"][/sc]
  • Dermatitis results from a dysfunctional skin barrier and immune dysregulation. In atopic dermatitis, genetic mutations (such as in the FLG gene encoding filaggrin) compromise skin barrier integrity, increasing susceptibility to allergens and irritants. The immune system, particularly T-helper cells, becomes hyperactive, leading to chronic inflammation and hypersensitivity.
  • In contact dermatitis, exposure to allergens (allergic contact dermatitis) or irritants (irritant contact dermatitis) triggers an immune response, leading to localised inflammation in response to direct skin contact with the offending substance.
  • In seborrhoeic dermatitis, overgrowth of Malassezia yeast on the skin, combined with sebum production, contributes to inflammation, particularly in sebaceous gland-rich areas.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Dermatitis"][/sc]
  • Acute phase: Characterised by intense itching, redness, and possibly vesiculation or oozing lesions, lasting days to weeks depending on triggers and interventions.
  • Subacute phase: Following initial inflammation, symptoms may evolve to less intense erythema and scaling, often persisting for weeks.
  • Chronic phase: In chronic cases, affected skin becomes lichenified (thickened) with dry, scaly patches due to repeated scratching or prolonged inflammation.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Dermatitis"][/sc]
  • Ask about pruritus (Itching) - The most common and distressing symptom, resulting from immune-mediated release of histamines and cytokines, which activate sensory nerves in the skin.
  • Ask about erythema (Redness) - Due to local vasodilation from inflammatory cytokines in affected skin areas.
  • Ask about burning or stinging sensation - Often associated with irritant dermatitis; can be caused by skin barrier dysfunction and exposure to external irritants.
  • Ask about dry skin (Xerosis) - Common in chronic dermatitis due to impaired skin barrier and reduced moisture retention.
  • Ask about oozing or weeping - Seen in acute phases due to vesiculation and breakdown of the epidermis.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Dermatitis"][/sc]
  • Check erythematous patches - Visible inflammation due to increased blood flow and immune response at affected sites.
  • Check vesicles or blisters - In acute dermatitis, inflammation can cause fluid accumulation within the epidermis.
  • Check scaling or crusting - Occurs as the skin heals and as a response to chronic scratching or inflammation.
  • Check excoriations - Scratch marks or abrasions from itching, often leading to secondary infections.
  • Check lichenification - Thickened skin with exaggerated skin lines in chronic dermatitis due to prolonged scratching or rubbing.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Dermatitis"][/sc]
  • Fever or malaise - Suggests a possible secondary infection, such as cellulitis, which may require systemic antibiotics.
  • Pustules or intense pain - Indicative of bacterial superinfection (e.g., Staphylococcus aureus) or viral infection (e.g., eczema herpeticum from HSV).
  • Widespread erythema with desquamation - May signify erythroderma, a severe condition needing urgent dermatological assessment.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Dermatitis"][/sc]
  • Discoid or nummular pattern - Coin-shaped lesions may appear in some individuals, especially in older adults, and can be mistaken for fungal infections.
  • Papular dermatitis - May present as small, raised papules instead of typical patches, more common in children.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask dermatitis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Dermatitis"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Skin Prick Test - Assesses sensitivity to specific allergens in suspected allergic dermatitis.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Patch Testing - Identifies allergens in suspected contact dermatitis; positive results show localised redness and swelling at contact points; Skin biopsy.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Dermatitis"][/sc]
  • Psoriasis - Distinguish from dermatitis using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.
  • Fungal infection (tinea) - Commonly presents with a ring-shaped pattern and central clearing; confirmed by fungal scraping.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Dermatitis"][/sc]
  • Skin infections: Increased risk of bacterial (e.g., Staphylococcus) or viral (e.g., herpes simplex) infections due to skin barrier defects.
  • Chronic lichenification: Persistent thickening of skin due to chronic scratching, often reducing with effective control of dermatitis.
  • Psychosocial impact: Pruritus and visible lesions may affect mental health, leading to anxiety or social withdrawal.
