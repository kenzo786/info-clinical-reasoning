Diabetic Neuropathy
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Diabetic Neuropathy"][/sc]
  • Diabetic neuropathy is a progressive nerve disorder caused by prolonged hyperglycaemia, leading to nerve damage throughout the body.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise diabetic features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.

Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Diabetic Neuropathy"][/sc]
  • Hyperglycaemia leads to the accumulation of advanced glycation end-products (AGEs) and sorbitol within nerve tissues, causing oxidative stress and microvascular damage. This results in axonal degeneration, demyelination, and impaired nerve conduction. The small nerve fibres are often affected first, leading to sensory loss, followed by damage to large fibres, contributing to motor and proprioceptive deficits.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Diabetic Neuropathy"][/sc]
  • Diabetic neuropathy typically develops gradually over months to years. In early stages, it may be asymptomatic or mild, progressing to more severe symptoms such as pain and motor deficits. Without intervention, nerve damage worsens, leading to chronic complications like foot ulcers and autonomic dysfunction.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Diabetic Neuropathy"][/sc]
  • Ask about distal numbness – Sensory loss, typically starting in the feet and hands, follows a "glove and stocking" distribution due to the early involvement, with associated clinical features.
  • Ask about tingling or burning sensations – These are common initial symptoms of small fibre neuropathy, caused by hyperexcitability of damaged nerves.
  • Ask about sharp, shooting pain – Occurs due to damage to sensory fibres, often worse at night.
  • Ask about muscle weakness – Motor nerve involvement may cause weakness, especially in the lower limbs.
  • Ask about loss of coordination – Damage to proprioceptive fibres results in difficulty with balance and gait.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Diabetic Neuropathy"][/sc]
  • Check reduced or absent ankle reflexes – Early sign of large-fibre neuropathy due to demyelination and loss of nerve conduction.
  • Check decreased vibration sense – Indicates involvement of large sensory fibres, detected through tuning fork tests.
  • Check impaired light touch and pain sensation – Resulting from small fibre degeneration, tested with a monofilament or pinprick.
  • Check muscle atrophy – Chronic motor neuropathy can lead to wasting of intrinsic foot muscles.
  • Check foot ulcers – Result from a combination of sensory loss, motor dysfunction, and poor circulation.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Diabetic Neuropathy"][/sc]
  • Sudden onset of severe pain – Could indicate an alternative diagnosis like vasculitis or lumbar disc herniation.
  • Foot ulcers with infection – Signifies a complication of diabetic neuropathy, leading to risk of osteomyelitis and amputation.
  • Autonomic dysfunction – Including gastroparesis, erectile dysfunction, or orthostatic hypotension, may indicate severe or widespread neuropathy.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Diabetic Neuropathy"][/sc]
  • Proximal muscle weakness – Seen in diabetic amyotrophy, where motor neuropathy affects larger, more proximal muscles rather than the typical distal involvement.
  • Asymmetric sensory loss – May be mistaken for a mononeuropathy or entrapment syndrome like carpal tunnel syndrome.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask diabetic neuropathy.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Diabetic Neuropathy"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Monofilament test – Assesses light touch sensation, with failure indicating significant sensory neuropathy; Electromyography (EMG) and nerve conduction studies (NCS) –.
  • Targeted and monitoring: HbA1c – Used to assess glycaemic control, which correlates with the severity of neuropathy. UK normal value: 48 mmol/mol or below.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Diabetic Neuropathy"][/sc]
  • Alcoholic neuropathy – Similar sensory and motor deficits - distinguish by focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.
  • Chronic inflammatory demyelinating polyneuropathy (CIDP) – More rapid - distinguish by focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.
  • Vitamin B12 deficiency – Presents with sensory neuropathy - distinguish by focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.
  • Related alternative diagnosis - Distinguish from diabetic neuropathy using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Diabetic Neuropathy"][/sc]
  • Foot ulcers – Affect 15% of diabetic patients with neuropathy, often leading to infections and, in severe cases, amputations.
  • Autonomic dysfunction – Can cause arrhythmias, postural hypotension, and gastrointestinal motility issues, leading to significant morbidity.
  • Charcot joint – Progressive joint destruction due to lack of protective sensation, particularly in the feet.
