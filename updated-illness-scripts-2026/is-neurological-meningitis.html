Meningitis [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Meningitis"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Meningitis"][/sc]
  • Meningitis is inflammation of the meninges — the membranes surrounding the brain and spinal cord — most commonly caused by bacterial or viral infection, producing a characteristic syndrome of severe headache, neck stiffness, fever, and photophobia.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise classic and atypical meningitis presentations, identify non-blanching purpura as a meningococcal emergency, know which features contraindicate immediate lumbar puncture, understand that antibiotics must not be delayed pending investigation, and distinguish bacterial from viral meningitis using clinical and CSF features.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Meningitis"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Meningitis"][/sc]
  • Bacterial meningitis affects approximately 1 per 100,000 people annually in the UK, with viral meningitis (predominantly enteroviral) considerably more common at approximately 10–15 per 100,000. Case fatality for bacterial meningitis is approximately 10% overall, rising to 20–30% for pneumococcal disease and approaching 50% for fulminant meningococcal septicaemia with shock. Peak incidence is in infants under 1 year and young adults aged 15–24 (particularly university students in shared accommodation). Meningococcal disease has declined significantly since the introduction of MenB and MenACWY vaccination programmes in the UK.
  • High-risk groups include infants and young children (immature blood-brain barrier and immune response), young adults in communal living, asplenic patients (all encapsulated organisms), those with complement deficiencies (particularly meningococcal), immunosuppressed patients including those on biological agents, adults over 60 (Listeria monocytogenes risk), patients with skull base fractures or CSF fistulae, and cochlear implant recipients. Unvaccinated individuals and travellers from meningitis belt regions are at increased risk for meningococcal serogroup A disease.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Meningitis"][/sc]
  • Bacteria (most commonly Neisseria meningitidis, Streptococcus pneumoniae, or Listeria monocytogenes) colonise the nasopharynx and breach the blood-brain barrier via haematogenous spread or direct extension; viral pathogens (predominantly enteroviruses, followed by HSV-2) reach the meninges via viraemia or, in the case of HSV, via neuronal spread.
  • Microbial invasion triggers an intense meningeal inflammatory response — cytokine release, leukocyte infiltration of the subarachnoid space, and increased BBB permeability — causing cerebral oedema, raised intracranial pressure, and impaired CSF flow; meningeal irritation produces the classic triad; bacterial toxins (particularly meningococcal endotoxin) can simultaneously trigger systemic sepsis and DIC.
  • If untreated, progressive cerebral oedema causes transtentorial herniation, brainstem compression, and death; meningococcal septicaemia with DIC leads to haemorrhagic skin necrosis, bilateral adrenal haemorrhage (Waterhouse-Friderichsen syndrome), and septic shock; survivors face sensorineural hearing loss, cognitive impairment, and limb loss from skin necrosis.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Meningitis"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Non-specific prodrome of fever, severe headache, myalgia, and malaise; symptoms resemble influenza or viral URTI, and the diagnosis may not be immediately apparent; viral meningitis may plateau at this stage, whereas bacterial disease characteristically deteriorates rapidly.
  • Progressive phase: The meningitic triad of severe headache, neck stiffness, and photophobia becomes established over hours to 1–2 days; nausea, vomiting, and phonophobia are common; a non-blanching petechial or purpuric rash signals meningococcal septicaemia and may spread rapidly from sparse petechiae to confluent purpura within hours.
  • Late phase: Deteriorating consciousness, focal neurological deficits, and seizures develop particularly beyond 12–24 hours of untreated bacterial disease; haemodynamic collapse from septic shock may accompany fulminant meningococcal disease; cerebral herniation is the terminal event of unchecked raised intracranial pressure.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Meningitis"][/sc]
  • Ask about headache character and severity — severe, global, and rapidly progressive headache is the cardinal symptom; patients may describe it as "the worst headache of my life"; its presence with fever and neck stiffness constitutes the classical meningitic triad, though this triad is present together in only approximately 40–45% of confirmed cases, making each component individually important.
  • Ask about neck stiffness and photophobia — inability or reluctance to flex the neck, sensitivity to light, and sensitivity to sound (phonophobia) reflect meningeal irritation; these features help distinguish meningitis from other causes of severe headache, though their absence, particularly in older adults and infants, should not lower suspicion in a febrile patient with severe headache.
  • Ask about rash — a non-blanching petechial or purpuric rash is pathognomonic of meningococcal septicaemia in the clinical context; ask directly whether any spots or rash have been noticed, and check skin carefully — early lesions may be sparse, few millimetres, and easily missed on unexposed skin; a blanching erythematous rash may also accompany viral or early bacterial meningitis but is less specific.
  • Ask about altered consciousness and seizures — confusion, drowsiness, or seizures indicate either direct CNS involvement or raised intracranial pressure; their presence represents a significant escalation in severity and should prompt immediate consideration of contraindications to lumbar puncture.
  • Ask about the pace of deterioration and preceding illness — bacterial meningitis characteristically deteriorates over hours, with patients or witnesses often able to describe rapid worsening; viral meningitis tends to evolve more gradually over 1–2 days; preceding URTI, travel to endemic regions, close contact with a confirmed case, and vaccination history all modify clinical probability significantly.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Meningitis"][/sc]
  • Check for neck stiffness (meningismus) — resistance to passive neck flexion in a supine patient; Kernig's sign (inability to extend the knee with the hip flexed to 90°, causing pain) and Brudzinski's sign (involuntary hip and knee flexion when the neck is passively flexed) are classical meningeal irritation signs; each has moderate sensitivity (~50%) but reasonable specificity when clearly positive — their absence does not exclude bacterial meningitis.
  • Check the skin systematically for non-blanching purpura — use the "glass test" (pressing a clear glass against the skin); petechiae or purpura that remain visible under pressure indicate systemic meningococcal disease with cutaneous bleeding from DIC; rash may begin as one or two petechiae on the trunk or lower limbs and spread rapidly — a spreading non-blanching rash is a haemodynamic emergency and should trigger immediate action.
  • Check conscious level using the GCS — a GCS of 14 or below indicates significant neurological compromise; a falling GCS is an urgent red flag for cerebral oedema, raised ICP, or imminent herniation; Cushing's triad (hypertension with bradycardia and irregular breathing) signals severely raised ICP and requires immediate escalation.
  • Check temperature, heart rate, and blood pressure — high fever with tachycardia reflects systemic inflammatory response; hypotension indicates septic shock, particularly in meningococcal disease; examine for papilloedema on fundoscopy if available — its presence is a contraindication to immediate lumbar puncture, though absence of papilloedema does not guarantee it is safe to proceed without CT.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Meningitis"][/sc]
  • Non-blanching purpuric rash — indicates meningococcal septicaemia with DIC; this is one of the most rapidly fatal presentations in medicine and warrants immediate parenteral antibiotics without waiting for investigation or lumbar puncture; a single petechial lesion in a febrile patient demands urgent assessment.
  • Deteriorating or impaired conscious level (GCS <14) — indicates raised intracranial pressure or direct CNS compromise; lumbar puncture is contraindicated without prior CT imaging; treatment must not be delayed pending neuroimaging — antibiotics should be given immediately with CT arranged concurrently.
  • Focal neurological deficits or new seizures — indicate cerebral oedema, infarction, or abscess formation complicating meningitis; lumbar puncture is contraindicated; urgent CT followed by neurosurgical or neurology involvement is warranted.
  • Haemodynamic instability (hypotension or shock) — fulminant meningococcal septicaemia may cause septic shock within hours of rash onset; warrants immediate fluid resuscitation per local sepsis pathways and critical care escalation alongside antibiotic therapy — delay of even 1 hour worsens outcome significantly.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Meningitis"][/sc]
  • Neonates and infants under 2 years: The classical meningitic triad is typically absent; presentation is with irritability, poor feeding, high-pitched cry, vomiting, seizures, and a bulging fontanelle; neck stiffness may be absent or difficult to assess; fever is not always present in neonates; any febrile neonate with a bulging fontanelle or unexplained irritability warrants immediate assessment for meningitis.
  • Older adults and the immunosuppressed: Classical features are frequently absent or attenuated; presentation may be with confusion, falls, or non-specific functional decline without prominent headache or neck stiffness; Listeria monocytogenes is disproportionately more common in adults over 60 and in immunosuppressed patients; a high index of suspicion is required when fever and new confusion coexist in these groups.
  • Pregnancy: Listeria monocytogenes infection is disproportionately more common in pregnancy due to gestational immunosuppression and poses particular risk of preterm labour, chorioamnionitis, and neonatal meningitis; classic meningeal features may be less prominent; empirical antibiotic therapy in pregnant women should include specific cover for Listeria (an additional antibiotic class beyond standard empirical cephalosporin therapy is typically required); CT head radiation and LP positioning require consideration but must not delay urgent investigation or treatment where clinically indicated.
  • Partially treated meningitis: Prior antibiotic therapy — even a single pre-hospital dose — can suppress CSF pleocytosis, normalise culture results, and significantly alter CSF Gram stain findings; lumbar puncture should still be performed as CSF PCR (meningococcal, pneumococcal, and enteroviral) remains positive even after antibiotic exposure and is more reliable than culture in this context.
  • Tuberculous meningitis: Follows a subacute course evolving over days to weeks rather than hours; prominent in HIV-positive individuals, those on immunosuppressive therapy, and patients from or with recent travel to high-prevalence regions; cranial nerve palsies (especially of cranial nerves III, IV, VI) may be a presenting feature; CSF shows lymphocytosis with very high protein and very low glucose — the classic triad of "lymphocytes, high protein, low glucose" — distinguishing it from acute bacterial meningitis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Meningitis"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected meningitis: FBC (raised white cell count with neutrophilia in bacterial disease), CRP and ESR (markedly elevated in bacterial meningitis; may be mildly elevated or normal in viral), blood cultures (take before antibiotics where possible without causing delay — a single set provides aetiology in approximately 50% of bacterial cases), whole blood EDTA tube for meningococcal and pneumococcal PCR (remains positive even after antibiotic exposure and is a key diagnostic tool), blood glucose (for comparison with CSF glucose ratio), coagulation screen (to screen for DIC in meningococcal disease), and electrolytes (hyponatraemia from SIADH complicates bacterial meningitis).
  • Lumbar puncture and CSF analysis: The definitive diagnostic test; should be performed without prior CT if there are no contraindications (GCS ≥13, no focal neurology, no papilloedema, no recent seizures within 1 hour, no immunosuppression, no coagulopathy); bacterial CSF typically shows neutrophilic pleocytosis, elevated protein (>1 g/L), and low glucose (CSF:blood glucose ratio <0.5); viral CSF shows lymphocytic pleocytosis with normal or mildly elevated protein and normal glucose; PCR of CSF for meningococcus, pneumococcus, enterovirus, and HSV should be requested in all cases; Gram stain and culture guide definitive therapy; a traumatic tap can complicate interpretation.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: CT head to exclude subarachnoid haemorrhage or space-occupying lesion if the headache is thunderclap; throat and nasopharyngeal swabs for viral culture if viral meningitis is suspected; LP opening pressure (elevated in bacterial meningitis and idiopathic intracranial hypertension); MRI brain if TB meningitis or HSV encephalitis is suspected.
  • Imaging — CT head: Required before LP in patients with GCS <13, focal neurological signs, papilloedema, recent seizure, or immunosuppression; does not directly diagnose meningitis but excludes structural lesions and brain herniation risk before CSF sampling; normal CT does not guarantee safe LP in all clinical contexts — clinical assessment and senior review should guide the decision; antibiotic therapy must not be withheld while awaiting CT.
  • Imaging — MRI brain: Preferred over CT for suspected TB meningitis, HSV encephalitis, posterior fossa involvement, and where CT is inconclusive; demonstrates meningeal enhancement, temporal lobe signal change (HSV encephalitis), cerebral abscess, and venous sinus thrombosis with greater sensitivity than CT; MRI is preferred over CT in pregnant patients to avoid ionising radiation where clinically feasible; availability and scan time limit its use in acute emergencies.
  • Serial reassessment: Hourly neurological observations (GCS, pupils, limb power, vital signs) are essential in all confirmed or suspected bacterial meningitis in the inpatient setting; repeat LP is occasionally used to confirm response to treatment in neonates or if initial CSF results are uninterpretable; formal audiological assessment at 6–8 weeks post-recovery is recommended for all patients with bacterial meningitis to detect sensorineural hearing loss.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Meningitis"][/sc]
  • An 18-year-old male university student presents with 12 hours of rapidly worsening severe headache, photophobia, and fever, with neck stiffness and bilateral non-blanching petechiae on his legs and trunk, and a GCS of 14 on examination.
  • Young adult in communal living with acute febrile meningitic syndrome, non-blanching rash, and impaired conscious level, consistent with meningococcal meningitis with early septicaemia — a medical emergency requiring immediate antibiotic therapy.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Meningitis"][/sc]
  • Viral meningitis (vs bacterial) — more gradual onset over 1–2 days, systemic toxicity less severe, rash absent, patient often alert; CSF shows lymphocytic pleocytosis with normal glucose and mildly elevated protein; distinguishing bacterial from viral is the central clinical challenge as the presentations may overlap early.
  • Subarachnoid haemorrhage — thunderclap headache reaching maximum intensity within seconds; neck stiffness may develop but fever is absent or late; CT head within 6 hours has >99% sensitivity; xanthochromia on LP (12 hours to 2 weeks from ictus) is diagnostic if CT is negative.
  • HSV encephalitis — fever with altered consciousness and seizures but prominent temporal lobe involvement; personality change, behavioural disturbance, or focal deficits are more prominent than meningism; MRI shows temporal lobe signal change; CSF PCR for HSV-1 is the diagnostic test — this is a critical diagnosis not to miss as it is treatable.
  • Brain abscess — subacute headache with fever and focal neurological deficits; ring-enhancing lesion on CT or MRI; LP is contraindicated due to raised ICP risk; meningism may be present if the abscess ruptures into the CSF.
  • Migraine (severe) — severe headache with photophobia and phonophobia but without fever, meningism, or systemic illness; typically recurrent with a personal or family history; normal neurological examination; responds to migraine-specific management.
  • Idiopathic intracranial hypertension — headache, papilloedema, and raised CSF opening pressure but with normal CSF composition and absence of infection; predominantly affects obese women of reproductive age; no fever or meningism.
  • Septic shock from other sources — fever and haemodynamic compromise without the meningitic triad; non-blanching petechiae can occur in other causes of DIC including Gram-negative sepsis and haematological malignancy — clinical context and CSF analysis differentiate.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Meningitis"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Recognise the combination of fever, severe headache, neck stiffness, and particularly non-blanching rash or impaired consciousness as a potential meningococcal emergency. Supportive care often includes IV access, oxygen if hypoxic, fluid resuscitation for haemodynamic compromise, and seizure management per local pathways. Antibiotics must not be delayed for lumbar puncture if red flag features are present — blood cultures and PCR should be taken immediately before antibiotic administration where possible without causing delay.
  • Antibiotic therapy: Empirical broad-spectrum antibiotic therapy should be started immediately once the clinical diagnosis of bacterial meningitis is suspected, particularly if lumbar puncture is contraindicated or delayed; the regimen is guided by local protocol, likely causative organism (determined by age, risk factors, and clinical context), and allergy status; therapy is subsequently narrowed based on CSF culture and sensitivity results.
  • Adjunctive corticosteroid therapy: A corticosteroid is recommended as an adjunct in adult bacterial meningitis (particularly pneumococcal disease) in UK guidelines; it reduces the severity of the meningeal inflammatory response, lowers the risk of sensorineural hearing loss, and reduces overall neurological sequelae; it is most beneficial when given with or immediately before the first antibiotic dose — benefit is substantially reduced if given after antibiotics are already underway.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Hourly neurological observations (GCS, pupillary responses, vital signs) in the acute phase; monitor for SIADH (fluid restriction may be needed); serum electrolytes and coagulation screen; brain CT if GCS falls or focal signs emerge; formal audiological assessment at 6–8 weeks post-discharge is recommended for all survivors of bacterial meningitis to detect sensorineural hearing loss.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Deteriorating GCS, new focal neurology, spreading purpura, or haemodynamic compromise should trigger immediate critical care escalation; close household contacts of confirmed meningococcal disease require antibiotic chemoprophylaxis and vaccination arranged via local public health (UKHSA) as a matter of urgency — this is a commonly examined UKMLA and MRCGP point.
  • Exam pharmacology reference (UKMLA/MRCGP): NICE CG102 and the British Infection Association describe a third-generation cephalosporin (ceftriaxone) as standard first-line empirical therapy for suspected bacterial meningitis in adults and children over 3 months; benzylpenicillin may be given by a GP pre-hospital if meningococcal disease is strongly suspected and parenteral access is available; amoxicillin or ampicillin should be added to cover Listeria monocytogenes in neonates, adults over 60, and immunocompromised patients; dexamethasone is recommended as adjunctive therapy ideally given with or before the first antibiotic dose; chemoprophylaxis for close contacts of meningococcal disease is typically with ciprofloxacin or rifampicin per UKHSA guidance. No doses or frequencies are cited here — always verify agent choice, duration, and allergy status against your local antimicrobial and prescribing guidelines before prescribing.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Meningitis"][/sc]
  • Sensorineural hearing loss: The most common neurological sequela of bacterial meningitis, occurring in approximately 10% of survivors; caused by cochlear inflammation from CSF-borne bacteria and inflammatory mediators; risk is highest with pneumococcal meningitis; adjunctive dexamethasone reduces but does not eliminate this risk; audiological assessment at 6–8 weeks post-recovery is recommended for all survivors, with cochlear implant assessment for those with severe loss.
  • Cerebral oedema and herniation: The most immediately life-threatening complication of raised intracranial pressure; develops particularly beyond 12–24 hours of untreated or inadequately treated bacterial disease; manifests as falling GCS, Cushing's triad (hypertension with bradycardia and irregular respiration), and fixed dilated pupils; requires urgent ICP management and neurosurgical involvement.
  • Waterhouse-Friderichsen syndrome: Bilateral adrenal haemorrhage and acute adrenal crisis complicating fulminant meningococcal septicaemia and DIC; characterised by haemorrhagic purpura, cardiovascular collapse, and refractory shock; a critical exam-relevant complication of meningococcal disease that explains why some patients deteriorate despite antibiotics — adrenal support may be required.
  • Long-term neurological sequelae and limb loss: Approximately 25% of bacterial meningitis survivors experience long-term complications including cognitive impairment, epilepsy, hydrocephalus, and learning difficulties; patients with meningococcal septicaemia and extensive skin necrosis may require digit or limb amputation from DIC-related ischaemia — a devastating complication that underscores the imperative of early recognition and treatment.
