Age-Related Purpura
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Age-Related Purpura"][/sc]
  • Age-related purpura, also known as senile purpura, is a benign condition commonly seen in older adults, characterised by ecchymotic patches, often on the forearms and hands.
  • UKMLA essentials: painless forearm ecchymoses, widespread or mucosal bruising concern, normal FBC and coagulation screen.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Age-Related Purpura"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Age-Related Purpura"][/sc]
  • Age-Related Purpura is encountered across acute and specialist care, with burden shaped by age, comorbidity, and exposure context.
  • Risk increases in patients with relevant predisposing disease, treatment exposures, or delayed access to care.
  • Missed or late recognition is more likely when presentations are atypical or overlap with common alternative diagnoses.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Age-Related Purpura"][/sc]
  • Age-related purpura results from structural changes in the skin, particularly a decrease in collagen and elastin fibres within the dermis, which weakens blood vessel support.
  • Reduced dermal thickness and increased capillary fragility lead to blood extravasation into the skin with minor trauma.
  • Repeated minor trauma accumulates in sun-exposed areas, especially the forearms and hands, as UV exposure exacerbates dermal thinning and vascular fragility.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Age-Related Purpura"][/sc]
  • Onset: Develops gradually with ageing and sun exposure, often appearing around ages 60-70.
  • Progression: Recurrent lesions may appear with increased frequency due to progressive skin thinning and cumulative sun exposure.
  • Resolution: Individual ecchymoses typically resolve within 1-3 weeks but may recur frequently.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Age-Related Purpura"][/sc]
  • Ask about Ecchymoses (bruising) without known trauma - Appears spontaneously due to capillary fragility; common on sun-exposed areas.
  • Ask about Bruising exacerbated by minor trauma - Results from weakened dermal structures unable to support blood vessels adequately.
  • Ask about Non-painful lesions - Lesions are typically painless, indicating the absence of active inflammation or infection.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Age-Related Purpura"][/sc]
  • Check Purple or dark red ecchymotic patches - Result from blood extravasation; typically irregular in shape and found on exposed areas like forearms.
  • Check Thin, atrophic skin - Thinning of the dermis is visibly noticeable, often with increased transparency.
  • Check Lack of surrounding inflammation - Indicative of non-inflammatory vascular fragility rather than vasculitis or an infection.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Age-Related Purpura"][/sc]
  • Bruising in non-exposed areas or extensive bruising - May indicate coagulopathy or platelet dysfunction rather than age-related purpura alone.
  • Associated systemic symptoms (e.g., fatigue, unexplained weight loss) - Could indicate underlying haematologic conditions such as leukaemia or vasculitis.
  • Persistent or very large ecchymoses - Might suggest conditions like amyloidosis, which also weakens vascular integrity.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Age-Related Purpura"][/sc]
  • Bruising on lower legs - Though typically on forearms, in individuals with immobility, bruising may appear on lower legs due to minor trauma during limited movement.
  • Persistent lesions - Bruises lasting beyond typical healing time may be mistaken for haematomas or skin malignancies.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Age-Related Purpura"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Full blood count (FBC): Expected normal platelet count and coagulation parameters; abnormal findings would prompt further investigation.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Coagulation screen: Typically normal, helping exclude coagulopathy.; Skin biopsy (rarely needed): If performed, may show thinning of the dermis and reduced vascular support structures.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Age-Related Purpura"][/sc]
  • A 72-year-old female presents with recurrent, painless bruising on the forearms and hands, exacerbated by minor trauma. The bruising has been gradually worsening over the past few years, with a history of significant sun exposure.
  • Older adult with recurrent painless bruising on sun-exposed forearms and fragile atrophic skin, consistent with age-related purpura.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Age-Related Purpura"][/sc]
  • Coagulopathy (e.g., haemophilia, anticoagulant use) - Differs in that bruising often affects non-exposed areas and may accompany abnormal coagulation results.
  • Vasculitis (e.g., Henoch-Schonlein purpura) - Palpable purpura with systemic features and possible renal involvement favours vasculitis.
  • Thrombocytopenia - Tends to cause petechiae in addition to bruising; FBC would reveal a low platelet count.
  • Non-accidental injury or safeguarding concern - Bruise pattern, distribution, and inconsistent history should trigger careful safeguarding assessment.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Age-Related Purpura"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Confirm benign pattern and exclude bleeding disorders when bruising distribution or systemic features are atypical.
  • Core care: Focus on skin protection, trauma minimisation, and sun-protection strategies, with medication review for anticoagulants or antiplatelets where appropriate.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Provide education on expected recurrence, lesion resolution time, and when to seek reassessment for new concerning features.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Rapidly progressive bruising, mucosal bleeding, systemic symptoms, or bruising in unusual sites should prompt urgent reassessment.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Age-Related Purpura"][/sc]
  • Psychosocial impact: Visible bruising may lead to cosmetic concerns or self-consciousness, particularly in social settings.
  • Misdiagnosis: Persistent lesions may be mistaken for abuse or underlying blood disorders, leading to unnecessary testing.
  • Prognosis: Generally good, with no associated increase in morbidity or mortality, but lesions may persist or worsen without preventive measures.
