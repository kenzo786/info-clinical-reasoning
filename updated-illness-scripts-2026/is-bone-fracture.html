Bone Fracture
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Bone Fracture"][/sc]
  • A bone fracture is a partial or complete break in the continuity of bone tissue, typically caused by direct trauma, stress, or underlying bone disease.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise bone features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Bone Fracture"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Bone Fracture"][/sc]
  • Risk factors: Risk of bone fracture rises with relevant predisposition, comorbidity, and exposure profile.
  • Higher-risk or missed groups: Bone Fracture may be missed when presentations are atypical or comorbidity obscures key features.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Bone Fracture"][/sc]
  • Fractures occur when an external force exceeds the tensile strength of bone. The pathophysiological process involves:
    1. Direct trauma or stress: Mechanical load disrupts bone continuity, often at the point of highest stress.
    2. Inflammatory response: Damage initiates local inflammation, with release of cytokines, vasodilation, and cellular infiltration.
    3. Bone healing: The healing process progresses through stages of haematoma formation, fibrocartilaginous callus formation, bony callus formation, and finally remodelling, restoring bone strength over time.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Bone Fracture"][/sc]
  • Acute Phase: Immediate pain, swelling, and loss of function following injury.
  • Subacute Phase: Inflammation and haematoma formation within days; soft callus begins forming within weeks.
  • Recovery Phase: Hard callus forms in 4-12 weeks, followed by remodelling over several months, depending on the fracture site and patient factors.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Bone Fracture"][/sc]
  • Ask about pain at the fracture site - Due to periosteal nerve irritation and local inflammation.
  • Ask about swelling - Results from local inflammatory response and fluid accumulation at the injury site.
  • Ask about bruising - Occurs as blood vessels are damaged, leading to extravasation of blood in surrounding tissue.
  • Ask about reduced mobility or inability to bear weight - Pain and structural compromise hinder normal function.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Bone Fracture"][/sc]
  • Check tenderness on palpation - Localised tenderness due to bone and periosteal damage.
  • Check deformity - Visible bone misalignment or angulation due to fracture displacement.
  • Check crepitus - Palpable grating sensation from bone fragments rubbing against each other.
  • Check swelling and ecchymosis - Indicative of bleeding and inflammation surrounding the fracture.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Bone Fracture"][/sc]
  • Neurovascular compromise (e.g., numbness, decreased pulses) - Can indicate nerve or vessel injury, risk of compartment syndrome.
  • Severe pain unrelieved by immobilisation - May indicate compartment syndrome or associated soft tissue damage.
  • Open fracture (bone exposed through the skin) - High risk of infection and osteomyelitis, requires urgent surgical intervention.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Bone Fracture"][/sc]
  • Occult fractures - Fractures with minimal initial symptoms, often in elderly or osteoporotic patients, may present later with chronic pain or mobility issues.
  • Stress fractures - Gradual onset pain, often in athletes, with no acute traumatic event.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features or comorbidity can mask the underlying diagnosis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Bone Fracture"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: X-ray: Initial test to visualise fracture line, alignment, and bone continuity.
  • Targeted and monitoring: CT scan: Provides detailed view of complex fractures, especially intra-articular fractures; MRI: Useful for soft tissue injuries and occult fractures not.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Bone Fracture"][/sc]
  • 65-year-old female presents with acute pain and swelling in the hip following a fall, inability to bear weight, and marked tenderness over the affected area, consistent with hip fracture.
  • Adult with symptom pattern and objective findings suggestive of bone fracture, requiring focused confirmation and risk stratification.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Bone Fracture"][/sc]
  • Sprain/Strain - Soft tissue injury with no bony abnormality, characterised by tenderness without deformity or crepitus.
  • Dislocation - Joint displacement with abnormal alignment and limited range of motion, often without crepitus.
  • Osteomyelitis - Bone infection presenting with gradual onset pain, fever, and systemic symptoms.
  • Alternative serious diagnosis - Distinguishing history, examination, and targeted investigations do not support bone fracture.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Bone Fracture"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Assess physiological stability and urgent complications at presentation, and initiate early stabilisation where required.
  • Core care: Deliver condition-specific treatment for bone fracture, address underlying drivers, and involve appropriate specialist support.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Reassess symptom trajectory, objective markers, and treatment response over time to detect complications early.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Worsening symptoms, physiological deterioration, or failure to improve should trigger urgent reassessment for severe or alternative pathology in bone fracture.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Bone Fracture"][/sc]
  • Non-union or delayed healing: More common in osteoporotic or non-compliant patients, due to inadequate vascularity or stability.
  • Compartment syndrome: Elevated intracompartmental pressure causing pain, risk of muscle and nerve damage.
  • Infection (especially in open fractures): Potentially leading to osteomyelitis, requiring prolonged antibiotics or surgery.
