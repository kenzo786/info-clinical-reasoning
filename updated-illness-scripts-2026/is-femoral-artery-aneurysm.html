Femoral Artery Aneurysm
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Femoral Artery Aneurysm"][/sc]
  • A femoral artery aneurysm is a focal dilation of the femoral artery, commonly the common femoral artery, with a diameter exceeding 1.5 times its normal size.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise femoral features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.

  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Femoral Artery Aneurysm"][/sc]
  • More prevalent in older men, particularly those over the age of 60, with a male-to-female ratio of approximately 10:1.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Femoral Artery Aneurysm"][/sc]
  • The pathogenesis involves weakening of the arterial wall due to atherosclerosis, which causes degradation of the structural proteins, elastin, and collagen, within the vessel wall.
  • Progressive vessel wall weakening leads to dilation under the force of blood flow. Chronic inflammation and cellular dysfunction further contribute to the degradation and thinning of the vessel wall.
  • Risk factors include advanced age, hypertension, smoking, and hyperlipidaemia, all of which exacerbate arterial wall degradation.
  • Femoral artery aneurysms carry a high risk of thromboembolism, as the turbulent blood flow within the aneurysmal sac increases the risk of thrombus formation.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Femoral Artery Aneurysm"][/sc]
  • Femoral artery aneurysms tend to develop slowly over years, often remaining asymptomatic initially.
  • Once symptomatic, rapid progression may occur due to thrombus formation or rupture, particularly in larger aneurysms.
  • Without intervention, there is a significant risk of limb-threatening ischaemia or rupture.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Femoral Artery Aneurysm"][/sc]
  • Ask about asymptomatic - Many cases are asymptomatic and discovered incidentally on imaging. The aneurysm may be silent until it causes complications like thromboembolism or rupture.
  • Ask about pain in the groin or thigh - A dull or aching pain in the groin or thigh, caused by pressure of the aneurysm, with associated clinical features.
  • Ask about limb ischaemia - Sudden onset of pain, pallor, or paraesthesia in the leg, which may be due to thromboembolism from the aneurysm, leading, with associated clinical features.
  • Ask about swelling in the groin - A palpable mass in the groin may be noticed by the patient or on examination, due to the enlarged.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Femoral Artery Aneurysm"][/sc]
  • Check pulsatile mass in the groin - A visible or palpable pulsating mass in the groin due to the dilated aneurysmal femoral artery.
  • Check decreased or absent distal pulses - Due to distal embolisation or thrombosis affecting blood flow to the lower extremity.
  • Check skin changes (pallor, cyanosis) - May occur with acute limb ischaemia as blood flow to the limb is compromised by thromboembolism.
  • Check bruit over the aneurysm - A turbulent blood flow sound on auscultation over the femoral artery may be noted due to the aneurysm.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Femoral Artery Aneurysm"][/sc]
  • Severe limb pain or loss of sensation - Suggests acute limb ischaemia, a limb-threatening emergency due to thromboembolism.
  • Rapidly expanding mass or new pain - May indicate impending rupture, which is a surgical emergency due to the risk of haemorrhage.
  • Skin ulceration or necrosis - Indicates advanced ischaemia with possible tissue loss, requiring urgent intervention.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Femoral Artery Aneurysm"][/sc]
  • Isolated leg pain without a palpable mass - May be mistaken for musculoskeletal pain; however, underlying aneurysm with thromboembolism should be considered if pulses are diminished.
  • Painless limb ischaemia - Rarely, embolisation may occur without pain, particularly in individuals with neuropathy, leading to delayed presentation.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask femoral artery aneurysm.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Femoral Artery Aneurysm"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Ultrasound Doppler - First-line imaging to assess aneurysm size and blood flow characteristics, identifying thrombosis or turbulent flow in the aneurysmal.
  • Targeted and monitoring: CT Angiography - Provides detailed visualisation of the aneurysm, surrounding structures, and extent of any thrombus. Useful in planning surgical intervention.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Femoral Artery Aneurysm"][/sc]
  • Inguinal hernia - Presents with a groin mass, but typically reducible and non-pulsatile, often with a cough.
  • Lymphadenopathy - Usually firm, non-pulsatile, and may be tender; often associated with systemic signs of infection.
  • Pseudoaneurysm - Often secondary to trauma or iatrogenic causes; presents as a pulsatile groin mass but.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Femoral Artery Aneurysm"][/sc]
  • Thromboembolism - Common and potentially limb-threatening; may lead to acute limb ischaemia.
  • Rupture - Rare but life-threatening complication; can cause significant haemorrhage, leading to hypotension and shock.
  • Chronic limb ischaemia - Due to recurrent thromboembolism, causing long-term limb disability or tissue loss.
