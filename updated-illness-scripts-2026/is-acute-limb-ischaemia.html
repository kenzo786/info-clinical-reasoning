Acute Limb Ischaemia
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Acute Limb Ischaemia"][/sc]
  • Acute limb ischaemia is sudden critical reduction in arterial perfusion to a limb, threatening tissue viability over hours.
  • UKMLA essentials: sudden six-P symptom pattern, motor or sensory loss red flags, immediate vascular referral and anticoagulation.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Acute Limb Ischaemia"][/sc]
  • Ask about sudden severe limb pain - Abrupt pain onset is a key early warning of arterial occlusion.
  • Ask about paraesthesia - Distal numbness or tingling suggests evolving neural ischaemia.
  • Ask about weakness or loss of movement - Declining motor function indicates threatened limb viability.
  • Ask about limb coldness - Subjective cold sensation reflects reduced arterial inflow.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Acute Limb Ischaemia"][/sc]
  • Check distal pulses - Absent or markedly reduced pulses distal to an occlusion support acute arterial compromise.
  • Check limb colour and temperature - Pallor and coolness indicate impaired perfusion.
  • Check capillary refill and Doppler signal - Delayed refill or absent arterial Doppler signal indicates severe ischaemia.
  • Check motor and sensory function - Deficits indicate advanced limb threat and urgent revascularisation need.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Acute Limb Ischaemia"][/sc]
  • Progressive sensory loss or paralysis - Suggests imminent irreversible ischaemic nerve and muscle injury.
  • Fixed mottling with absent Doppler flow - Indicates critically poor tissue perfusion and high amputation risk.
  • Systemic deterioration with severe limb pain - Raises concern for advanced ischaemia with metabolic complications.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Acute Limb Ischaemia"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Acute Limb Ischaemia"][/sc]
  • Acute limb ischaemia is an important vascular emergency seen in older adults with established arterial or cardioembolic disease.
  • Risk is increased by atrial fibrillation, peripheral arterial disease, prior vascular intervention, and prothrombotic states.
  • Delayed presentation or misdiagnosis markedly increases risk of limb loss and systemic complications.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Acute Limb Ischaemia"][/sc]
  • ALI arises from abrupt arterial occlusion, usually embolic occlusion or in-situ thrombosis of diseased vessels.
  • Reduced oxygen delivery causes rapid neuromuscular dysfunction, endothelial injury, and progressive tissue infarction.
  • Reperfusion after prolonged ischaemia can trigger systemic inflammatory and metabolic complications.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Acute Limb Ischaemia"][/sc]
  • Hyperacute phase: Sudden symptom onset over minutes to hours with pain and perfusion change.
  • Progressive phase: Sensory and motor deficits can evolve rapidly without prompt revascularisation.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Acute Limb Ischaemia"][/sc]
  • Reduced pain perception: Neuropathy or delayed presentation may blunt pain despite severe ischaemia.
  • Intermittent symptoms with collateral flow: Fluctuating findings can obscure severity and delay escalation.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Acute Limb Ischaemia"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Urgent limb viability assessment with Doppler pulse evaluation, neurological examination, and immediate blood tests including lactate and renal profile.
  • Targeted and monitoring: CT angiography defines occlusion site and revascularisation strategy; serial limb assessment tracks deterioration or reperfusion injury after intervention.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Acute Limb Ischaemia"][/sc]
  • A 72-year-old woman with atrial fibrillation presents with 3 hours of sudden severe left leg pain, coldness, paraesthesia, and absent distal pulses.
  • Older adult with abrupt limb pain and objective perfusion loss plus evolving neuro-motor deficits, concerning for acute limb ischaemia.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Acute Limb Ischaemia"][/sc]
  • Deep vein thrombosis - Limb swelling and warmth with preserved arterial pulses suggests venous rather than arterial occlusion.
  • Acute compartment syndrome - Severe pain with tense compartments and pain on passive stretch occurs despite maintained major arterial flow early on.
  • Chronic limb-threatening ischaemia - Gradual rest pain and tissue loss history suggests chronic progression rather than abrupt event.
  • Acute peripheral neuropathy - Sensory symptoms without objective arterial perfusion deficit suggest neurological pathology.
  • Aortic dissection with limb malperfusion - Chest or back pain with pulse asymmetry indicates proximal vascular catastrophe.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Acute Limb Ischaemia"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Start urgent anticoagulation, provide analgesia, keep limb dependent and warm, and obtain immediate vascular specialist input.
  • Core care: Revascularisation is delivered by thrombectomy, thrombolysis, bypass, or endovascular strategy according to limb viability and occlusion pattern.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Reassess perfusion, neurological status, and biochemical markers for reperfusion injury or compartment syndrome.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Worsening pain, increasing neurological deficit, metabolic derangement, or failed reperfusion needs urgent senior re-evaluation and rescue planning.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Acute Limb Ischaemia"][/sc]
  • Irreversible limb loss: Prolonged ischaemia can progress to non-viable tissue requiring amputation.
  • Reperfusion injury syndrome: Muscle necrosis can trigger hyperkalaemia, acidosis, rhabdomyolysis, and acute kidney injury.
  • Systemic decompensation and sepsis risk: Necrotic tissue and metabolic stress can cause multi-organ deterioration.
