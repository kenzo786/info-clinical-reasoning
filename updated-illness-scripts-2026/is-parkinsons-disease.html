Parkinson's Disease
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Parkinson's Disease"][/sc]
  • Parkinson's disease is a progressive neurodegenerative disorder characterised by the degeneration of dopaminergic neurons in the substantia nigra pars compacta, leading to a classic triad of motor symptoms: bradykinesia, rigidity.

Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Parkinson's Disease"][/sc]
  • Parkinson's disease is caused by the degeneration of dopaminergic neurons in the substantia nigra, which leads to a depletion of dopamine in the basal ganglia. This disrupts the motor circuit and leads to impaired movement regulation.
  • Dopamine deficiency affects direct (excitatory) and indirect (inhibitory) pathways of motor control, causing bradykinesia and rigidity.
  • The classic tremor of Parkinson’s disease is linked to the dysfunction of other neurotransmitters, such as acetylcholine, contributing to resting tremors.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Parkinson's Disease"][/sc]
  • Parkinson’s disease progresses slowly over several years, starting with mild motor symptoms that worsen with time.
  • In the early phase, symptoms are often unilateral and subtle but gradually become bilateral.
  • Later stages include more prominent motor impairments, cognitive decline, and increasing disability, often leading to a dependence on caregivers.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Parkinson's Disease"][/sc]
  • Ask about bradykinesia - Slowness of movement is the cardinal feature of Parkinson's and results from impaired motor control due to dopamine deficiency in the basal.
  • Ask about tremor (Resting) - A characteristic 4-6 Hz tremor occurs at rest, caused by imbalances between dopaminergic and cholinergic systems.
  • Ask about muscle Rigidity - Increased muscle tone or stiffness results from hyperactivity in the extrapyramidal system, typically described as "cogwheel" rigidity.
  • Ask about postural Instability - Later in the disease, impaired postural reflexes lead to difficulties in balance and frequent falls.
  • Ask about non-Motor Symptoms - These include constipation, anosmia, depression, and REM sleep behaviour disorder, often preceding motor symptoms.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Parkinson's Disease"][/sc]
  • Check resting Tremor - Visible tremor in the hands or limbs when at rest, often described as "pill-rolling."
  • Check cogwheel Rigidity - Resistance to passive movement in joints, felt as intermittent "catches."
  • Check bradykinesia - Slowness of movement is demonstrated by slow finger taps or reduced arm swing during walking.
  • Check gait Disturbance - Short, shuffling steps with a stooped posture are often observed, referred to as "festinating gait."
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Parkinson's Disease"][/sc]
  • Rapid Progression of Symptoms - Suggests atypical parkinsonism or secondary causes such as multiple system atrophy (MSA).
  • Early Cognitive Decline - May indicate dementia with Lewy bodies or other neurodegenerative conditions.
  • Prominent Autonomic Dysfunction - Severe hypotension or urinary incontinence may suggest multiple system atrophy (MSA).
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Parkinson's Disease"][/sc]
  • Early Falls - Atypical if occurring early, suggests a more aggressive form of parkinsonism or progressive supranuclear palsy (PSP).
  • Asymmetry of Symptoms - While typical in early disease, prolonged or marked asymmetry may indicate stroke or other neurological conditions.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask parkinson's disease.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Parkinson's Disease"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Clinical Diagnosis - Based on clinical criteria including bradykinesia and at least one other cardinal feature (rigidity, tremor, or postural instability).
  • Targeted and monitoring: DaTSCAN (SPECT) - Can be used to confirm dopamine transporter deficiency in the basal ganglia, supporting diagnosis in uncertain cases; MRI.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Parkinson's Disease"][/sc]
  • Essential Tremor - Differentiated by the lack of bradykinesia or rigidity and the presence of tremor during.
  • Multiple System Atrophy (MSA) - Features early autonomic dysfunction, more rapid progression, and poor response to levodopa.
  • Progressive Supranuclear Palsy (PSP) - Characterised by early falls, vertical gaze palsy, and more pronounced gait disturbance.
  • Related alternative diagnosis - Distinguish from parkinson's disease using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Parkinson's Disease"][/sc]
  • Motor Complications - Long-term levodopa use can result in motor fluctuations and dyskinesias.
  • Cognitive Decline - Many patients develop dementia in later stages of the disease.
  • Falls and Fractures - Due to postural instability and gait disturbances, falls are common and can lead to significant injury.
