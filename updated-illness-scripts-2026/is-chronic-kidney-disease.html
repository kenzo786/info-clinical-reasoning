Chronic Kidney Disease (CKD)
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Chronic Kidney Disease (CKD)"][/sc]
  • Chronic Kidney Disease (CKD) is a long-term condition characterised by progressive, irreversible damage to the kidneys, leading to a decline in kidney function.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise kidney features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Chronic Kidney Disease (CKD)"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Chronic Kidney Disease (CKD)"][/sc]
  • Risk factors: Risk of chronic kidney disease (ckd) rises with relevant predisposition, comorbidity, and exposure profile.
  • Higher-risk or missed groups: Chronic Kidney Disease (CKD) may be missed when presentations are atypical or comorbidity obscures key features.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Chronic Kidney Disease (CKD)"][/sc]
  • CKD involves progressive nephron loss due to various underlying causes (e.g., diabetes, hypertension, glomerulonephritis). As nephrons are lost, the remaining nephrons undergo hyperfiltration to maintain overall kidney function. Over time, this hyperfiltration leads to further nephron injury, promoting a cycle of renal damage. Inflammation, fibrosis, and scarring contribute to structural damage, which results in decreased glomerular filtration rate (GFR) and eventual kidney failure.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Chronic Kidney Disease (CKD)"][/sc]
  • CKD progresses slowly over years, often in stages. In the early stages (1-2), the patient may be asymptomatic with minor reductions in GFR. In stages 3-4, symptoms such as fatigue, oedema, and anaemia develop due to further decline in kidney function. End-stage renal disease (stage 5) occurs when GFR drops below 15 mL/min/1.73 m², necessitating dialysis or kidney transplantation.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Chronic Kidney Disease (CKD)"][/sc]
  • Ask about fatigue - Due to anaemia (reduced erythropoietin production by the kidneys), electrolyte imbalances, and uraemia.
  • Ask about oedema - Caused by reduced excretion of sodium and water, leading to fluid retention.
  • Ask about dyspnoea - Related to fluid overload, pulmonary oedema, and anaemia.
  • Ask about nocturia - Impaired kidney function reduces the ability to concentrate urine, leading to increased urination at night.
  • Ask about pruritus - Uraemia causes the accumulation of waste products, which can deposit in the skin, leading to itching.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Chronic Kidney Disease (CKD)"][/sc]
  • Check hypertension - Due to fluid overload and activation of the renin-angiotensin-aldosterone system (RAAS).
  • Check pallor - Resulting from anaemia secondary to decreased erythropoietin production.
  • Check pitting oedema - Occurs due to fluid retention, most commonly seen in the legs and ankles.
  • Check pericardial rub - A sign of uraemic pericarditis, which occurs in advanced CKD due to the accumulation of uraemic toxins.
  • Check cachexia - Wasting syndrome observed in advanced CKD due to poor appetite, malnutrition, and uraemia.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Chronic Kidney Disease (CKD)"][/sc]
  • Oliguria or anuria - Indicates rapid decline in kidney function or obstruction, a potential emergency requiring immediate intervention.
  • Severe hyperkalaemia - Can cause life-threatening arrhythmias; usually indicates advanced CKD or acute kidney injury (AKI) superimposed on CKD.
  • Severe dyspnoea - Suggestive of pulmonary oedema or heart failure due to volume overload, requiring urgent management.
  • Pericarditis - A sign of uraemic pericarditis, which may lead to tamponade if untreated.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Chronic Kidney Disease (CKD)"][/sc]
  • Isolated hypertension - In some patients, CKD may initially present with poorly controlled hypertension without other significant symptoms.
  • Asymptomatic proteinuria - CKD may be identified incidentally through routine blood or urine tests in asymptomatic patients.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features or comorbidity can mask the underlying diagnosis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Chronic Kidney Disease (CKD)"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Serum creatinine and eGFR - Elevated creatinine and reduced estimated GFR (eGFR) indicate decreased kidney function; Urine dipstick and ACR (albumin:creatinine.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Ultrasound - May show small, shrunken kidneys, suggesting chronicity, or structural abnormalities such as cysts; Serum electrolytes - Hyperkalaemia and metabolic.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Chronic Kidney Disease (CKD)"][/sc]
  • A 65-year-old male presents with progressive fatigue, ankle swelling, and nocturia over the last 6 months. He has a history of hypertension and poorly controlled type 2 diabetes. Blood tests.
  • Adult with symptom pattern and objective findings suggestive of chronic kidney disease (ckd), requiring focused confirmation and risk stratification.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Chronic Kidney Disease (CKD)"][/sc]
  • Acute Kidney Injury (AKI) - Sudden decline in kidney function, often reversible, compared to the gradual and irreversible decline.
  • Nephrotic Syndrome - Differentiate from chronic kidney disease (ckd) using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.
  • Polycystic Kidney Disease - Differentiate from chronic kidney disease (ckd) using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.
  • Alternative serious diagnosis - Distinguishing history, examination, and targeted investigations do not support chronic kidney disease (ckd).
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Chronic Kidney Disease (CKD)"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Assess physiological stability and urgent complications at presentation, and initiate early stabilisation where required.
  • Core care: Deliver condition-specific treatment for chronic kidney disease (ckd), address underlying drivers, and involve appropriate specialist support.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Reassess symptom trajectory, objective markers, and treatment response over time to detect complications early.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Worsening symptoms, physiological deterioration, or failure to improve should trigger urgent reassessment for severe or alternative pathology in chronic kidney disease (ckd).
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Chronic Kidney Disease (CKD)"][/sc]
  • Cardiovascular disease - Leading cause of death in CKD patients due to hypertension, hyperlipidaemia, and vascular calcification.
  • Bone disease - CKD-mineral and bone disorder (CKD-MBD) occurs due to impaired phosphate excretion and altered calcium metabolism, leading to bone fragility.
  • Hyperkalaemia - Can cause life-threatening cardiac arrhythmias, especially in advanced CKD.
  • Metabolic acidosis - Due to the kidneys' inability to excrete acid, leading to bone resorption and muscle wasting.
