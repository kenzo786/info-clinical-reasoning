Cartilage Injury
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Cartilage Injury"][/sc]
  • Cartilage injury refers to damage to the articular cartilage, a specialised connective tissue that lines joint surfaces and facilitates smooth movement while absorbing mechanical stress.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise cartilage features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.

  • Risk factors: Risk of cartilage injury rises with relevant predisposition, comorbidity, and exposure profile.
  • Higher-risk or missed groups: Cartilage Injury may be missed when presentations are atypical or comorbidity obscures key features.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Cartilage Injury"][/sc]
  • Cartilage lacks a direct blood supply, relying on diffusion from synovial fluid, which limits its healing capacity.
  • Trauma or repetitive microtrauma damages the matrix and chondrocytes, leading to reduced biomechanical properties.
  • Severe injuries exposing subchondral bone provoke an inflammatory response, potentially accelerating degenerative changes.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Cartilage Injury"][/sc]
  • Acute phase: Pain, swelling, and joint instability within hours to days.
  • Subacute phase: Persistent joint pain and mechanical symptoms over weeks.
  • Chronic phase: Untreated injuries may lead to degenerative changes and osteoarthritis over months to years.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Cartilage Injury"][/sc]
  • Ask about joint Pain - Commonly localised pain due to exposure of subchondral bone or inflammatory mediators in synovial fluid.
  • Ask about swelling - Often caused by synovial fluid effusion in response to injury or associated trauma.
  • Ask about mechanical Symptoms - Catching, locking, or giving way due to loose cartilage fragments or altered joint congruity.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Cartilage Injury"][/sc]
  • Check joint Effusion - Visible or palpable swelling due to increased synovial fluid production.
  • Check reduced Range of Motion - Limited movement secondary to pain, swelling, or mechanical blockages.
  • Check crepitus - Palpable or audible grinding due to irregular cartilage surfaces.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Cartilage Injury"][/sc]
  • Severe Persistent Pain - May indicate progression to osteoarthritis or associated bone injury.
  • Joint Instability - Suggestive of associated ligament or meniscal injury.
  • Recurrent Effusions - May signify ongoing inflammation or chronic cartilage degeneration.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Cartilage Injury"][/sc]
  • Asymptomatic - Particularly in low-grade lesions; incidental findings during imaging or arthroscopy.
  • Referred Pain - Misattributed to other joints or structures due to altered biomechanics.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features or comorbidity can mask the underlying diagnosis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Cartilage Injury"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Plain Radiographs - May show joint space narrowing or osteochondral fractures in chronic cases.
  • Targeted and monitoring: MRI - Gold standard; demonstrates cartilage defects, oedema, and subchondral bone changes; Arthroscopy - Direct visualisation and assessment of cartilage integrity.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Cartilage Injury"][/sc]
  • A 25-year-old male presents with 3 weeks of knee pain, swelling, and locking following a football injury. No prior joint issues.
  • Adult with symptom pattern and objective findings suggestive of cartilage injury, requiring focused confirmation and risk stratification.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Cartilage Injury"][/sc]
  • Meniscal Injury - Mechanical symptoms more pronounced, MRI shows meniscal tear.
  • Ligament Tear - Instability prominent, positive Lachman or pivot shift test.
  • Osteoarthritis - Gradual onset, radiographs show joint space narrowing and osteophytes.
  • Alternative serious diagnosis - Distinguishing history, examination, and targeted investigations do not support cartilage injury.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Cartilage Injury"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Assess physiological stability and urgent complications at presentation, and initiate early stabilisation where required.
  • Core care: Deliver condition-specific treatment for cartilage injury, address underlying drivers, and involve appropriate specialist support.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Reassess symptom trajectory, objective markers, and treatment response over time to detect complications early.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Worsening symptoms, physiological deterioration, or failure to improve should trigger urgent reassessment for severe or alternative pathology in cartilage injury.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Cartilage Injury"][/sc]
  • Osteoarthritis - Progressive degeneration due to untreated or inadequately treated cartilage injuries.
  • Chronic Pain - Persistent pain impacting quality of life.
