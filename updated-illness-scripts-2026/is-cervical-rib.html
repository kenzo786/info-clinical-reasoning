Cervical Rib
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Cervical Rib"][/sc]

Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Cervical Rib"][/sc]
  • A cervical rib forms due to abnormal segmentation during embryonic development. It may compress adjacent structures like the brachial plexus, subclavian artery, or subclavian vein, leading to symptoms. Compression of the brachial plexus can result in neurogenic thoracic outlet syndrome, while vascular compression may lead to arterial or venous thoracic outlet syndrome. Chronic compression can cause ischaemia, pain, and neurological dysfunction in the upper limb.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Cervical Rib"][/sc]
  • The condition is typically present from birth but may remain asymptomatic for years or even a lifetime. Symptomatic cases tend to develop gradually over months to years, particularly if repetitive upper limb movement, trauma, or postural changes exacerbate the compression of neurovascular structures.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Cervical Rib"][/sc]
  • Ask about neck and shoulder pain – Often due to brachial plexus compression, leading to pain radiating from the neck into the shoulder or upper limb.
  • Ask about numbness or tingling in the arm – Neurogenic thoracic outlet syndrome can lead to sensory deficits, including paraesthesia, particularly in the ulnar nerve distribution.
  • Ask about weakness of the hand – Compression of motor nerves within the brachial plexus can result in weakness, most commonly affecting hand grip strength.
  • Ask about ischaemic pain in the arm – Subclavian artery compression may reduce blood flow to the arm, leading to ischaemic symptoms such as pain or in this clinical context.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Cervical Rib"][/sc]
  • Check positive Adson’s test – Loss of the radial pulse when the patient turns their head toward the symptomatic side and inhales deeply, indicating vascular.
  • Check weak or absent radial pulse – This may be present in severe cases of arterial thoracic outlet syndrome due to subclavian artery compression.
  • Check muscle wasting of the hand – Chronic neurogenic thoracic outlet syndrome may result in atrophy of intrinsic hand muscles due to long-term nerve compression.
  • Check swelling or discolouration of the arm – Venous thoracic outlet syndrome may result in venous congestion, leading to swelling and cyanosis of the affected.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Cervical Rib"][/sc]
  • Severe arm ischaemia – Sudden or severe arm pain with pallor and coldness suggests significant subclavian artery compression or embolism, a potential surgical emergency.
  • Venous thrombosis – Swelling, discolouration, and pain in the arm may indicate subclavian vein thrombosis (Paget-Schroetter syndrome), requiring urgent treatment to prevent further complications.
  • Progressive neurological deficits – Worsening sensory or motor loss may indicate irreversible nerve damage due to chronic compression, requiring prompt intervention to prevent permanent dysfunction.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Cervical Rib"][/sc]
  • Isolated hand symptoms – In some cases, patients may present with isolated hand weakness or numbness without neck or shoulder pain, potentially leading to misdiagnosis as carpal tunnel syndrome or ulnar neuropathy.
  • Unilateral symptoms only – Even if a cervical rib is present bilaterally, patients may only develop symptoms on one side, complicating diagnosis.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features or comorbidity can mask the underlying diagnosis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Cervical Rib"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Focused bedside assessment and first-line investigations to define immediate risk in suspected cervical rib.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Nerve conduction studies – Useful in neurogenic thoracic outlet syndrome to evaluate brachial plexus function and locate sites of nerve compression.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Cervical Rib"][/sc]
  • Chronic nerve damage – Long-term compression can result in irreversible brachial plexus damage, leading to permanent sensory or motor deficits.
  • Arterial embolism – In severe cases of arterial compression, emboli can form and migrate distally, leading to acute limb ischaemia.
  • Venous thrombosis – Can result in pulmonary embolism if untreated, significantly increasing morbidity and mortality.
