Medically Unexplained Symptoms (MUS)
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Medically Unexplained Symptoms (MUS)"][/sc]
  • Medically Unexplained Symptoms (MUS) refers to physical symptoms for which no adequate medical explanation can be found after thorough investigation.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise medically features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.

Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Medically Unexplained Symptoms (MUS)"][/sc]
  • The exact pathophysiology of MUS is not well understood, but it is believed to involve an interaction between biological, psychological, and social factors. Central sensitisation, heightened awareness of bodily sensations, and dysregulation of the hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal (HPA) axis are often implicated. Psychological stressors may lead to altered perception and processing of normal physiological sensations, which can be misinterpreted as pathological. Chronic stress, anxiety, and depression often exacerbate symptom perception through autonomic dysfunction and chronic inflammatory responses.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Medically Unexplained Symptoms (MUS)"][/sc]
  • MUS often begins with acute symptoms following a physical or emotional stressor. Symptoms may become chronic over months or years, persisting despite negative test results. The condition can fluctuate, with periods of exacerbation and partial remission. Early intervention may improve outcomes, while prolonged symptoms increase the risk of long-term disability and reduced quality of life.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Medically Unexplained Symptoms (MUS)"][/sc]
  • Ask about fatigue - The most common presenting symptom. Pathophysiology may involve dysregulation of the HPA axis, leading to persistent feelings of exhaustion.
  • Ask about musculoskeletal pain - Often reported as diffuse or localised pain without a clear anatomical cause. Central sensitisation or altered pain perception may explain this.
  • Ask about gastrointestinal symptoms (e.g., irritable bowel syndrome, bloating) - Commonly linked to visceral hypersensitivity and altered gut-brain axis signalling.
  • Ask about headaches - Frequently tension-type or migrainous in nature. Psychological stressors may exacerbate muscle tension and vascular changes, leading to pain.
  • Ask about dizziness - Often described as a feeling of imbalance or light-headedness, potentially related to autonomic dysregulation or hyperventilation during anxiety episodes.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Medically Unexplained Symptoms (MUS)"][/sc]
  • Check no objective findings on physical examination - A hallmark of MUS, as the symptoms do not correlate with measurable abnormalities in clinical or laboratory.
  • Check non-specific tenderness - Some patients may exhibit tenderness in various locations, often without a clear musculoskeletal cause, potentially linked to central sensitisation.
  • Check normal neurological exam - Despite complaints of neurological symptoms (e.g., headaches, dizziness), examination typically yields no abnormal findings.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Medically Unexplained Symptoms (MUS)"][/sc]
  • Weight loss - Unintentional weight loss raises concerns about malignancy or severe systemic disease.
  • Haemoptysis - Coughing up blood requires urgent investigation for pulmonary causes, including infection or neoplasia.
  • Neurological deficits - New, focal neurological signs suggest an organic central nervous system pathology, such as stroke or multiple sclerosis, and warrant immediate referral.
  • Persistent fever - May indicate infection, malignancy, or autoimmune disease rather than MUS.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Medically Unexplained Symptoms (MUS)"][/sc]
  • Isolated gastrointestinal or cardiovascular symptoms - In some cases, patients present with symptoms resembling specific conditions like irritable bowel syndrome or non-cardiac chest pain, which may delay recognition of MUS.
  • Intermittent symptoms with long asymptomatic periods - These presentations can be misleading, as the absence of persistent symptoms may reduce clinical suspicion of MUS.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask medically unexplained symptoms (mus).
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Medically Unexplained Symptoms (MUS)"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Blood tests - Typically normal, including full blood count, liver function tests, thyroid function tests, and inflammatory markers.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Imaging - Normal findings on imaging such as X-rays, CT scans, or MRIs help rule out organic causes but do not.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Medically Unexplained Symptoms (MUS)"][/sc]
  • Chronic Fatigue Syndrome (CFS) - Overlaps with MUS but distinguished by the hallmark post-exertional malaise.
  • Fibromyalgia - Similar to MUS but typically associated with specific tender points and sleep disturbances.
  • Somatic Symptom Disorder - MUS may be considered a subset of this condition, which emphasises distress and preoccupation.
  • Depression with somatisation - Often presents with multiple unexplained physical symptoms, but prominent mood disturbances are also present.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Medically Unexplained Symptoms (MUS)"][/sc]
  • Reduced quality of life - Persistent symptoms can lead to functional impairment and disability.
  • Depression and anxiety - Commonly co-exist and may worsen over time without proper psychological intervention.
  • Healthcare overuse - Repeated investigations and specialist consultations can contribute to increased healthcare costs and patient anxiety.
