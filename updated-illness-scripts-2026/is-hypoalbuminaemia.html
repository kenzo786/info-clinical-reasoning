Hypoalbuminaemia
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Hypoalbuminaemia"][/sc]
  • Hypoalbuminaemia is defined as a serum albumin level below 35 g/L in adults.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise hypoalbuminaemia features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.

  • Higher-risk or missed groups: Hypoalbuminaemia may be missed when presentation is atypical or obscured by comorbidity.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Hypoalbuminaemia"][/sc]
  • Hypoalbuminaemia results from several mechanisms:
  • Decreased synthesis: Chronic liver diseases like cirrhosis impair albumin production.
  • Increased loss: Conditions like nephrotic syndrome cause significant urinary loss of albumin.
  • Increased catabolism: Chronic inflammation increases albumin breakdown.
  • Redistribution: Albumin shifts from the vascular space to the extravascular space during sepsis or in protein-losing enteropathy.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Hypoalbuminaemia"][/sc]
  • Acute hypoalbuminaemia: Can develop rapidly in the setting of acute illness such as sepsis, trauma, or burns.
  • Chronic hypoalbuminaemia: Typically seen in patients with chronic liver disease, nephrotic syndrome, or malnutrition, developing over weeks to months.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Hypoalbuminaemia"][/sc]
  • Ask about fatigue - Due to reduced oncotic pressure leading to fluid shift and tissue oedema, which impairs normal cellular function.
  • Ask about peripheral oedema - Common symptom due to reduced plasma oncotic pressure, causing fluid to leak into interstitial spaces.
  • Ask about dyspnoea - Can result from pleural effusions, which develop due to hypoalbuminaemia-induced fluid accumulation.
  • Ask about abdominal distension - Caused by ascites, which occurs due to a shift in fluid into the peritoneal cavity, particularly in liver disease.
  • Ask about often asymptomatic - Many cases of mild hypoalbuminaemia are found incidentally on blood tests, especially in chronic conditions.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Hypoalbuminaemia"][/sc]
  • Check peripheral oedema - Seen in the ankles or sacrum, caused by fluid leakage due to decreased oncotic pressure.
  • Check ascites - Fluid accumulation in the abdomen, often associated with liver disease or nephrotic syndrome.
  • Check pleural effusion - Fluid in the pleural cavity, leading to decreased breath sounds and dullness to percussion.
  • Check muscle wasting - Results from prolonged protein deficiency and catabolism.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Hypoalbuminaemia"][/sc]
  • Severe peripheral oedema - May indicate advanced hypoalbuminaemia, suggesting significant fluid imbalance and the risk of skin breakdown or infection.
  • Rapid onset ascites - Can be a sign of worsening liver failure or severe nephrotic syndrome, requiring urgent investigation.
  • Pulmonary oedema - Can lead to respiratory distress and signifies severe hypoalbuminaemia or a rapid worsening of the underlying disease, such as nephrotic syndrome or heart failure.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Hypoalbuminaemia"][/sc]
  • Isolated ascites - Some patients may present with isolated abdominal distension due to ascites without peripheral oedema, particularly in liver disease.
  • Generalised weakness - In patients with chronic conditions like malignancy, hypoalbuminaemia can present with non-specific symptoms like weakness or lethargy.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask hypoalbuminaemia.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Hypoalbuminaemia"][/sc]
  • Nephrotic syndrome - Typically presents with proteinuria, oedema, and hypoalbuminaemia. Unlike liver disease, urinalysis reveals significant protein.
  • Liver cirrhosis - Distinguish from hypoalbuminaemia using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.
  • Heart failure - Causes oedema and dyspnoea, but hypoalbuminaemia is less common. Echocardiography and raised BNP levels.
  • Protein-losing enteropathy - Causes hypoalbuminaemia due to gastrointestinal loss of protein, often with diarrhoea and weight loss.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Hypoalbuminaemia"][/sc]
  • Infections: Patients with hypoalbuminaemia are at increased risk of infections due to impaired immune response and skin breakdown from oedema.
  • Thromboembolism: Particularly in nephrotic syndrome, due to hypercoagulability associated with protein loss.
  • Severe fluid overload: Persistent hypoalbuminaemia can lead to significant ascites, pleural effusion, or even pulmonary oedema, complicating management.
