Cervical Ectropion
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Cervical Ectropion"][/sc]
  • Cervical ectropion, also known as cervical eversion, occurs when the columnar epithelium that lines the endocervical canal extends outward to the ectocervix.

Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Cervical Ectropion"][/sc]
  • The endocervix is lined by columnar epithelium, while the ectocervix is covered by stratified squamous epithelium. In cervical ectropion, the columnar epithelium extends outward onto the ectocervix, which is often caused by increased levels of oestrogen. This can lead to increased cervical mucus production and a more fragile surface that is prone to irritation or minor trauma, leading to symptoms such as post-coital bleeding or increased vaginal discharge.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Cervical Ectropion"][/sc]
  • Cervical ectropion typically develops gradually with hormonal changes and may persist for months or years, particularly in women who are pregnant or taking oral contraceptives. It may resolve spontaneously or after hormonal changes, such as cessation of contraceptive use or post-menopause.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Cervical Ectropion"][/sc]
  • Consider asymptomatic (commonest) - Most women with cervical ectropion do not experience any symptoms, and it is usually found incidentally during a routine cervical smear.
  • Ask about increased vaginal discharge - Excessive mucus production due to exposure of the glandular cells, which produce more mucus than squamous epithelium.
  • Ask about post-coital bleeding - The exposed columnar epithelium is more fragile and prone to trauma, leading to bleeding after intercourse.
  • Ask about intermenstrual spotting - Similar to post-coital bleeding, the fragile columnar cells may bleed spontaneously between menstrual periods.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Cervical Ectropion"][/sc]
  • Check visible ectropion on speculum examination - The cervix appears redder around the os due to the presence of glandular columnar epithelium, which is thinner.
  • Check cervical tenderness - Occasionally, there may be mild tenderness on palpation if the ectropion is inflamed or irritated.
  • Check increased cervical mucus - A speculum examination may reveal excess mucus production from the ectropion.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Cervical Ectropion"][/sc]
  • Persistent post-coital bleeding - While this is often benign, it could signify more serious pathology, such as cervical cancer, and requires further investigation with colposcopy.
  • Abnormal cervical appearance - Any irregular, raised, or ulcerated areas should raise suspicion for malignancy.
  • Unexplained pelvic pain - If present with bleeding, this may suggest a more sinister condition, such as cervical infection or malignancy, requiring urgent attention.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Cervical Ectropion"][/sc]
  • Severe pelvic pain - Cervical ectropion usually doesn’t cause significant pain. If severe pain is present, consider an alternate diagnosis like pelvic inflammatory disease or endometriosis.
  • Recurrent infections - Women with cervical ectropion may report recurrent infections, though this is not a typical feature. Increased exposure of glandular cells could make the cervix more susceptible to infections.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features or comorbidity can mask the underlying diagnosis.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Cervical Ectropion"][/sc]
  • Recurrent bleeding - Persistent post-coital bleeding may affect quality of life and sexual health, necessitating further evaluation.
  • Infection risk - The exposed glandular epithelium may have a higher susceptibility to infection, though this is not well-established.
  • Anxiety or concern - Due to the bleeding or discharge, many women may become anxious about more serious conditions like cancer, requiring reassurance and appropriate investigation.
