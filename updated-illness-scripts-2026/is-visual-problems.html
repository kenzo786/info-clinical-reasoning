Visual Problems
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Visual Problems"][/sc]
  • Visual problems encompass a range of abnormalities in vision, including blurring, double vision, peripheral vision loss, and other visual disturbances.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise visual features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.

  • Higher-risk or missed groups: Visual Problems may be missed when presentation is atypical or obscured by comorbidity.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Visual Problems"][/sc]
  • Visual problems can result from abnormalities in light focusing (e.g., refractive errors), ocular structures (e.g., cataract), retinal or optic nerve dysfunction (e.g., diabetic retinopathy, glaucoma), or cortical processing (e.g., stroke-related visual field deficits).
  • Key pathways include disruption in ocular transparency, blood flow to retinal and neural tissues, and nerve signal transmission.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Visual Problems"][/sc]
  • Acute onset: Often seen in vascular or traumatic causes (e.g., retinal detachment, optic neuritis).
  • Subacute: Common in progressive disorders (e.g., diabetic retinopathy, age-related macular degeneration).
  • Chronic and gradual: Frequently due to refractive errors, cataracts, or chronic glaucoma.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Visual Problems"][/sc]
  • Ask about blurry vision - Often due to refractive errors or cataracts. Caused by incorrect light focusing on the retina or lens opacity.
  • Ask about loss of visual field - Can be peripheral or central. Common in glaucoma (peripheral loss) or macular degeneration (central loss), due to optic nerve.
  • Ask about photopsia (flashes of light) - Frequently occurs with retinal detachment due to traction on the retina.
  • Ask about diplopia (double vision) - Often arises from cranial nerve palsies or ocular misalignment, affecting coordinated eye movement.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Visual Problems"][/sc]
  • Check reduced visual acuity - Often seen in refractive errors or retinal disease due to impaired light focus or retinal function.
  • Check visual field defects - May present as scotomas (blind spots) in specific patterns, indicating damage to retinal or visual pathways (e.g., bitemporal hemianopia, with associated clinical features.
  • Check abnormal pupillary reflexes - Afferent pupillary defect can signify optic neuropathy or severe retinal disease.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Visual Problems"][/sc]
  • Sudden vision loss - Indicates possible retinal detachment, optic neuritis, or vascular occlusion, which can lead to permanent damage without prompt intervention.
  • Severe eye pain - May indicate acute angle-closure glaucoma or optic neuritis, both of which require urgent treatment to prevent vision loss.
  • Flashes and floaters with vision loss - Suggests retinal detachment, requiring urgent ophthalmological evaluation.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Visual Problems"][/sc]
  • Asymptomatic until advanced (e.g., glaucoma) - Vision changes may go unnoticed due to gradual progression and lack of acute symptoms.
  • Subtle visual field changes - May present with vague symptoms such as “difficulty reading” without overt vision loss, especially in early macular degeneration.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask visual problems.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Visual Problems"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Visual acuity testing - Assesses clarity of vision; reduced acuity suggests refractive errors or retinal conditions.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Fundoscopy - Reveals retinal changes like haemorrhages or macular degeneration signs; Visual field testing - Identifies specific field defects, indicating possible.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Visual Problems"][/sc]
  • Glaucoma - Typically peripheral vision loss, painless and progressive, unlike acute angle closure which is painful.
  • Macular degeneration - Central vision loss, different from peripheral loss seen in glaucoma.
  • Optic neuritis - Often painful, acute vision loss, especially in young adults; associated with MS.
  • Related alternative diagnosis - Distinguish from visual problems using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Visual Problems"][/sc]
  • Permanent vision loss - Seen in advanced glaucoma or untreated retinal detachment.
  • Secondary complications - May include reduced quality of life, increased fall risk, and dependency.
