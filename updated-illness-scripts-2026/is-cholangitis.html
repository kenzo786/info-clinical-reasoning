Cholangitis
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Cholangitis"][/sc]
  • Cholangitis is an infection and inflammation of the biliary tree, typically due to bacterial pathogens entering the biliary system secondary to obstruction (e.g., gallstones, strictures, or malignancy).
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise cholangitis features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.

  • Higher-risk or missed groups: Cholangitis may be missed when presentations are atypical or comorbidity obscures key features.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Cholangitis"][/sc]
  • Biliary obstruction leads to increased biliary pressure, stasis, and impaired bile flow.
  • Stasis and impaired flow allow bacterial colonisation, most commonly from the duodenum. Gram-negative bacteria (e.g., E. coli, Klebsiella) are typical causative agents.
  • Bacterial colonisation triggers localised inflammation and systemic infection.
  • This can lead to systemic inflammatory response syndrome (SIRS) and, if untreated, severe sepsis, organ dysfunction, and shock.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Cholangitis"][/sc]
  • Acute phase: Rapid onset of fever, jaundice, and abdominal pain over hours to a few days.
  • Subacute phase: If untreated, progression to sepsis and multi-organ failure within days to a week.
  • Recovery phase: Following treatment, gradual resolution of symptoms within days to weeks, depending on severity and response to intervention.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Cholangitis"][/sc]
  • Ask about fever - Common due to systemic response to infection and inflammation; often high-grade.
  • Ask about jaundice - Due to bile duct obstruction and hepatocyte dysfunction leading to elevated bilirubin.
  • Ask about right upper quadrant abdominal pain - Due to distension of biliary ducts and local inflammation.
  • Ask about nausea and vomiting - Secondary to pain and systemic inflammatory response.
  • Ask about malaise - A result of systemic infection and cytokine release.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Cholangitis"][/sc]
  • Check fever - High temperature often over 38°C, a systemic sign of infection.
  • Check jaundice - Yellowing of skin and sclerae, visible at bilirubin levels over 2.5 mg/dL.
  • Check tenderness in right upper quadrant - Localised due to inflammation of the biliary tract.
  • Check tachycardia - Often seen due to systemic infection and fever.
  • Check hypotension (in severe cases) - Due to sepsis, indicating potential progression to septic shock.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Cholangitis"][/sc]
  • Altered mental status - Sign of advanced sepsis with possible encephalopathy due to systemic infection and bilirubin elevation.
  • Hypotension - Suggests sepsis with potential progression to septic shock.
  • Refractory fever - May indicate an abscess or persistent biliary obstruction needing urgent intervention.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Cholangitis"][/sc]
  • Atypical presentation in elderly patients - May present with subtle symptoms, such as confusion or general malaise, without the classic triad.
  • Immunocompromised patients - Less likely to mount a fever but may have disproportionate hypotension and jaundice.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features or comorbidity can mask the underlying diagnosis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Cholangitis"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Blood cultures - Often show growth of gram-negative organisms, aiding in pathogen identification; Complete blood count (CBC) - Leukocytosis common due.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Targeted investigations to confirm diagnosis, assess complications, and monitor response in cholangitis.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Cholangitis"][/sc]
  • Cholecystitis - Similar pain but often without jaundice or systemic features unless complicated.
  • Hepatitis - Elevated liver enzymes with jaundice, but usually lacks biliary dilation and right upper quadrant.
  • Biliary pancreatitis - Jaundice and right upper quadrant pain are possible, but elevated lipase and amylase suggest.
  • Alternative serious diagnosis - Distinguishing history, examination, and targeted investigations do not support cholangitis.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Cholangitis"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Assess physiological stability and urgent complications at presentation, and initiate early stabilisation where required.
  • Core care: Deliver condition-specific treatment for cholangitis, address underlying drivers, and involve appropriate specialist support.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Reassess symptom trajectory, objective markers, and treatment response over time to detect complications early.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Worsening symptoms, physiological deterioration, or failure to improve should trigger urgent reassessment for severe or alternative pathology in cholangitis.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Cholangitis"][/sc]
  • Sepsis - A common complication due to systemic infection, often requires intensive care if severe.
  • Septic shock - High mortality risk if unresponsive to initial therapy; requires vasopressors and ICU admission.
  • Liver abscess - May occur if infection persists or is inadequately treated; needs further drainage and antibiotics.
