Cerebellar Disorder
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Cerebellar Disorder"][/sc]
  • Cerebellar disorders refer to a group of conditions that affect the cerebellum, the part of the brain responsible for coordinating voluntary movements, balance, posture, and motor learning.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise cerebellar features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.

Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Cerebellar Disorder"][/sc]
  • The cerebellum integrates sensory and motor information to ensure smooth, coordinated movements. Disruption to its function, whether through ischaemia (stroke), degeneration (genetic ataxias), toxins (alcohol), or demyelination (multiple sclerosis), leads to impaired motor coordination. This dysfunction manifests as ataxia, dysmetria (inaccurate targeting of movements), and dysdiadochokinesia (difficulty with rapid alternating movements).
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Cerebellar Disorder"][/sc]
  • Cerebellar disorders can present acutely, such as in cerebellar stroke, or progressively, as in genetic ataxias or tumours. The time course varies depending on the cause:
    • Acute onset: Symptoms develop over minutes to hours, typical in stroke or acute intoxications.
    • Subacute onset: Develops over days to weeks, often seen in paraneoplastic cerebellar degeneration.
    • Chronic/progressive: Gradual worsening of symptoms over months to years, seen in neurodegenerative disorders like spinocerebellar ataxias.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Cerebellar Disorder"][/sc]
  • Ask about ataxia - Gait unsteadiness, difficulty with coordination, and clumsy movements due to impaired integration of motor signals by the cerebellum.
  • Ask about dysmetria - Inaccurate movements, such as overshooting or undershooting a target, caused by the loss of fine motor control.
  • Ask about dysarthria - Slurred speech due to poor coordination of the muscles used in speech, reflecting cerebellar involvement in fine motor control.
  • Ask about nystagmus - Involuntary, jerky eye movements due to the cerebellum’s role in controlling eye movements.
  • Ask about vertigo - Sensation of spinning or imbalance due to involvement of the cerebellum’s vestibular connections.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Cerebellar Disorder"][/sc]
  • Check gait ataxia - Unsteady, wide-based gait due to impaired motor coordination.
  • Check dysdiadochokinesia - Inability to perform rapid alternating movements, reflecting cerebellar dysfunction in motor coordination.
  • Check hypotonia - Decreased muscle tone, often observed in cerebellar disorders, due to altered motor feedback to muscles.
  • Check positive Romberg sign - Difficulty maintaining balance with eyes closed, indicating proprioceptive or vestibular dysfunction.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Cerebellar Disorder"][/sc]
  • Acute onset of severe headache and vomiting - May suggest cerebellar haemorrhage or stroke, requiring urgent neuroimaging.
  • Sudden onset of ataxia - Suggestive of cerebellar stroke or acute intoxication (e.g., alcohol, toxins), a medical emergency.
  • Severe vertigo with nystagmus - Could indicate a cerebellar or brainstem lesion, such as a tumour or stroke.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Cerebellar Disorder"][/sc]
  • Subtle gait imbalance in elderly - May be misattributed to age-related degeneration, delaying diagnosis of underlying cerebellar pathology.
  • Isolated dysarthria - Can present without ataxia in some cerebellar strokes, leading to initial misdiagnosis as aphasia or stroke in other brain regions.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features or comorbidity can mask the underlying diagnosis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Cerebellar Disorder"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Focused bedside assessment and first-line investigations to define immediate risk in suspected cerebellar disorder.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Genetic testing - For hereditary ataxias like spinocerebellar ataxia, identifying mutations in genes such as ATXN1, ATXN2; Electrolyte and metabolic panels.

  • Adult with symptom pattern and objective findings suggestive of cerebellar disorder, requiring focused confirmation and risk stratification.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Cerebellar Disorder"][/sc]
  • Parkinson's disease - Differs in the presence of bradykinesia, rigidity, and resting tremor rather than ataxia.
  • Multiple sclerosis - Can cause cerebellar symptoms but usually presents with additional signs such as optic neuritis.
  • Peripheral neuropathy - Leads to imbalance but typically shows distal sensory loss and absent reflexes, unlike cerebellar.
  • Vertigo (vestibular disorders) - Causes imbalance and nystagmus but does not cause ataxia or dysmetria.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Cerebellar Disorder"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Assess physiological stability and urgent complications at presentation, and initiate early stabilisation where required.
  • Core care: Deliver condition-specific treatment for cerebellar disorder, address underlying drivers, and involve appropriate specialist support.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Reassess symptom trajectory, objective markers, and treatment response over time to detect complications early.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Worsening symptoms, physiological deterioration, or failure to improve should trigger urgent reassessment for severe or alternative pathology in cerebellar disorder.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Cerebellar Disorder"][/sc]
  • Falls - Common due to gait ataxia and poor balance, leading to fractures or head injuries.
  • Speech difficulties - Dysarthria may significantly impact communication and quality of life.
  • Progressive disability - Chronic cerebellar disorders like hereditary ataxias lead to gradual loss of independence in activities of daily living.
