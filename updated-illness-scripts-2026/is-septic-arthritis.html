Septic Arthritis
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Septic Arthritis"][/sc]
  • Septic arthritis is a joint infection caused by bacteria, fungi, or viruses, most commonly bacterial.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise septic features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Septic Arthritis"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Septic Arthritis"][/sc]
  • Risk factors: Risk factors for septic arthritis include relevant predisposition, comorbidity, and exposure history.
  • Higher-risk or missed groups: Septic Arthritis may be missed when presentation is atypical or obscured by comorbidity.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Septic Arthritis"][/sc]
  • Septic arthritis typically arises from haematogenous spread, where pathogens enter the joint from the bloodstream. Direct inoculation via trauma or joint surgery and local spread from adjacent infections are also possible.
  • Bacterial invasion triggers an inflammatory response, recruiting neutrophils and releasing cytokines, causing synovitis and increased intra-articular pressure.
  • Pro-inflammatory cytokines lead to cartilage breakdown and bone damage, particularly in weight-bearing joints, resulting in rapid joint destruction without prompt treatment.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Septic Arthritis"][/sc]
  • Initial Phase (1-2 days): Acute onset of joint pain, swelling, and fever.
  • Progressive Phase (3-7 days): Worsening pain, increased swelling, restricted joint movement, and possible systemic signs such as chills and fatigue.
  • Chronic Phase (Weeks): In untreated cases, significant joint destruction, decreased mobility, and long-term disability.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Septic Arthritis"][/sc]
  • Ask about joint Pain - Sudden, severe pain in the affected joint due to inflammatory cytokines and swelling increasing intra-articular pressure.
  • Ask about swelling - Marked swelling from synovial fluid accumulation caused by inflammatory exudate in response to infection.
  • Ask about fever - Common systemic response to infection, often over 38°C due to pyrogen release from the infection.
  • Ask about reduced Joint Mobility - Pain and swelling limit range of motion due to joint effusion and synovial inflammation.
  • Ask about systemic Symptoms - Chills, malaise, and generalised fatigue reflect the body’s systemic response to infection.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Septic Arthritis"][/sc]
  • Check joint Warmth - Local inflammation causes vasodilation and increased blood flow, making the joint feel warm to the touch.
  • Check redness - Erythema around the joint due to inflammatory mediators that increase vascular permeability and cause localised redness.
  • Check tenderness - Extreme tenderness upon palpation from heightened sensitivity and pressure due to inflammation.
  • Check effusion - Visible joint swelling, especially in superficial joints, due to fluid accumulation in the joint capsule.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Septic Arthritis"][/sc]
  • Severe Pain and Rapidly Worsening Swelling - Suggests aggressive infection, potentially leading to joint destruction if untreated.
  • Systemic Symptoms of Sepsis (Hypotension, Tachycardia) - Can indicate systemic involvement or bacteraemia and signal high risk for multi-organ failure.
  • High Fever & Rigors - May reflect bacteraemia, requiring urgent intervention to prevent metastatic infections.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Septic Arthritis"][/sc]
  • Painless Joint Swelling - Seen in immunocompromised or elderly patients, potentially delaying diagnosis as the typical pain response is diminished.
  • Low-Grade Fever or Afebrile - Some older or immunosuppressed patients may not mount a febrile response, complicating early diagnosis.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask septic arthritis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Septic Arthritis"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Joint Aspiration and Synovial Fluid Analysis - Shows purulent fluid with elevated white cells (>50,000/mm³), often with neutrophil predominance. Culture identifies.
  • Targeted and monitoring: CRP and ESR - Both are significantly elevated due to systemic inflammation; X-ray and Ultrasound - May show joint effusion; however.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Septic Arthritis"][/sc]
  • Crystal Arthropathy (e.g., Gout) - Often presents with intense joint pain and swelling but has a characteristic negative bacterial.
  • Rheumatoid Arthritis - Distinguish from septic arthritis using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.
  • Reactive Arthritis - Follows infection in other body parts; synovial fluid is sterile, distinguishing it from septic.
  • Osteoarthritis - Distinguish from septic arthritis using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.

  • Immediate priorities: Assess severity, physiological stability, and immediate complications.
  • Core care: Deliver condition-specific management for septic arthritis, treat underlying drivers, and coordinate appropriate specialist input.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Reassess symptom trajectory, examination findings, and objective markers to confirm response in septic arthritis.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Escalate urgently for clinical deterioration, evolving red flags, or failure to improve in suspected septic arthritis.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Septic Arthritis"][/sc]
  • Joint Destruction - Rapid joint erosion due to inflammatory cytokines, leading to chronic disability and requiring potential joint replacement.
  • Sepsis - Infection may spread, leading to systemic involvement and multi-organ dysfunction, especially if diagnosis and treatment are delayed.
  • Osteomyelitis - Infection may spread to nearby bone, requiring extended treatment and increasing risk for long-term disability.
