Viral Gastroenteritis
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Viral Gastroenteritis"][/sc]
  • Viral gastroenteritis is an acute inflammation of the gastrointestinal tract caused by viruses, characterised by diarrhoea, vomiting, abdominal pain, and systemic symptoms like fever.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise viral features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Viral Gastroenteritis"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Viral Gastroenteritis"][/sc]
  • Worldwide, viral gastroenteritis is a leading cause of morbidity, with norovirus and rotavirus being significant contributors to disease burden, especially in low-resource settings.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Viral Gastroenteritis"][/sc]
  • Viral infection damages enterocytes in the small intestine, disrupting absorption of water and electrolytes, leading to diarrhoea.
  • Stimulation of the enteric nervous system and gut inflammation can cause vomiting and abdominal pain.
  • Release of inflammatory mediators triggers systemic symptoms like fever and malaise.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Viral Gastroenteritis"][/sc]
  • Incubation period: 1-2 days post-exposure.
  • Acute phase: Symptoms peak within 2-3 days and resolve within 7 days in most cases.
  • Recovery phase: Complete recovery typically occurs within 1-2 weeks, although fatigue may persist.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Viral Gastroenteritis"][/sc]
  • Ask about diarrhoea - Caused by impaired water and electrolyte absorption due to enterocyte damage.
  • Ask about vomiting - Stimulation of the gut-brain axis and inflammation of the enteric nervous system.
  • Ask about abdominal pain - Likely due to intestinal cramping and local inflammation.
  • Ask about fever - Systemic inflammatory response to viral infection.
  • Ask about malaise and fatigue - General systemic response to infection.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Viral Gastroenteritis"][/sc]
  • Check dehydration signs - Dry mucous membranes, decreased skin turgor due to fluid loss.
  • Check fever - Elevated temperature due to systemic inflammation.
  • Check hyperactive bowel sounds - Due to increased gut motility.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Viral Gastroenteritis"][/sc]
  • Severe dehydration - May cause hypotension, tachycardia, or reduced urine output, indicating potential shock.
  • Persistent high fever - May suggest bacterial superinfection.
  • Bloody diarrhoea - Could indicate a more serious condition such as haemorrhagic colitis.
  • Prolonged symptoms >7 days - May suggest an alternative diagnosis such as inflammatory bowel disease.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Viral Gastroenteritis"][/sc]
  • Asymptomatic cases - Particularly in adults with robust immune responses.
  • Severe presentations in immunocompromised patients - Due to prolonged viral replication.
  • Predominant nausea or fatigue - With minimal diarrhoea, possibly delaying recognition.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask viral gastroenteritis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Viral Gastroenteritis"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Stool PCR - Identifies viral pathogens; often shows presence of norovirus or rotavirus.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Electrolyte panel - May reveal hypokalaemia or acidosis due to fluid loss; Full blood count - Generally normal; leukocytosis may indicate.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Viral Gastroenteritis"][/sc]
  • Bacterial gastroenteritis - Higher likelihood of bloody stools and severe abdominal pain.
  • Parasitic infections - Chronic symptoms or travel history may help differentiate.
  • Food poisoning - Often has a very acute onset and resolves quickly.
  • Inflammatory bowel disease - Chronicity and presence of systemic symptoms such as weight loss.

  • Core care: Deliver condition-specific management for viral gastroenteritis, treat underlying drivers, and coordinate appropriate specialist input.

  • Escalation and safety-netting: Escalate urgently for clinical deterioration, evolving red flags, or failure to improve in suspected viral gastroenteritis.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Viral Gastroenteritis"][/sc]
  • Severe dehydration - Particularly in children and the elderly; may lead to hypovolaemic shock.
  • Electrolyte imbalances - Hypokalaemia or hypernatraemia due to fluid loss.
  • Secondary bacterial infection - May arise due to disrupted gut mucosa.
