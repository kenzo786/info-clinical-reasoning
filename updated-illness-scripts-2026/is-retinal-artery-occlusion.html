Retinal Artery Occlusion
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Retinal Artery Occlusion"][/sc]
  • Retinal artery occlusion (RAO) is a sudden blockage of the central retinal artery or one of its branches, leading to ischaemic damage to the retina and acute, painless monocular vision.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise retinal features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.

  • Higher-risk or missed groups: Retinal Artery Occlusion may be missed when presentation is atypical or obscured by comorbidity.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Retinal Artery Occlusion"][/sc]
  • Retinal artery occlusion results from an embolus (most commonly from carotid or cardiac sources) or thrombosis in the retinal arterial system.
  • The obstruction causes ischaemia, leading to retinal ganglion cell death and subsequent retinal infarction.
  • The retina has a high metabolic demand, and irreversible damage can occur within 90-100 minutes of occlusion.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Retinal Artery Occlusion"][/sc]
  • Acute phase: Sudden onset of painless monocular vision loss, lasting minutes to hours.
  • Subacute phase: Progression to irreversible vision loss if untreated.
  • Chronic phase: Residual visual deficits and retinal atrophy.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Retinal Artery Occlusion"][/sc]
  • Ask about sudden, painless vision loss: Complete or partial monocular loss due to ischaemia affecting the retinal ganglion cells.
  • Ask about amaurosis fugax: Transient vision loss preceding the event, often described as a curtain descending over the vision.
  • Ask about peripheral visual field loss: Seen in branch retinal artery occlusion (BRAO) due to localised ischaemia.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Retinal Artery Occlusion"][/sc]
  • Check cherry red spot: A central retinal finding due to the contrast between a pale ischaemic retina and the intact choroidal circulation at the macula.
  • Check retinal whitening: Opaque retina from oedema caused by ischaemia.
  • Check attenuated retinal vessels: Thinning and narrowing of arteries due to reduced blood flow.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Retinal Artery Occlusion"][/sc]
  • Sudden complete vision loss: May signify central retinal artery occlusion (CRAO), which has a poor prognosis if not urgently managed.
  • Symptoms of stroke/TIA: Weakness, dysphasia, or facial droop indicating a systemic embolic source.
  • Headache and jaw claudication: Suggestive of giant cell arteritis, a critical cause requiring immediate treatment.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Retinal Artery Occlusion"][/sc]
  • Asymptomatic BRAO: Detected incidentally during routine eye examinations.
  • Prolonged vision loss with mild symptoms: Seen in partial or incomplete occlusions.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask retinal artery occlusion.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Retinal Artery Occlusion"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Fundoscopy: Identifies retinal whitening and a cherry-red spot; Optical coherence tomography (OCT): Shows retinal thickening and oedema.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Fluorescein angiography: Delayed or absent filling of retinal arteries; Carotid Doppler and echocardiogram: To assess embolic sources.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Retinal Artery Occlusion"][/sc]
  • Retinal vein occlusion - Presents with more gradual vision loss and retinal haemorrhages.
  • Giant cell arteritis - Associated with headache and systemic symptoms like jaw claudication.
  • Optic neuritis - Painful vision loss with optic disc swelling.
  • Related alternative diagnosis - Distinguish from retinal artery occlusion using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.

  • Escalation and safety-netting: Escalate urgently for clinical deterioration, evolving red flags, or failure to improve in suspected retinal artery occlusion.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Retinal Artery Occlusion"][/sc]
  • Permanent vision loss: Common in CRAO if not treated promptly.
  • Neovascularisation: Can lead to glaucoma or further vision loss.
