Acute Viral Myositis
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Acute Viral Myositis"][/sc]
  • Acute viral myositis is a transient inflammation of the skeletal muscles caused by viral infections, commonly influenza.
  • UKMLA essentials: myalgia, dark urine, creatine kinase ck.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Acute Viral Myositis"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Acute Viral Myositis"][/sc]
  • Acute Viral Myositis is encountered across acute and specialist care, with burden shaped by age, comorbidity, and exposure context.
  • Risk increases in patients with relevant predisposing disease, treatment exposures, or delayed access to care.
  • Missed or late recognition is more likely when presentations are atypical or overlap with common alternative diagnoses.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Acute Viral Myositis"][/sc]
  • Viral entry: Respiratory viruses, especially influenza, may infiltrate muscle tissue or trigger immune responses that cause inflammation.
  • Immune-mediated myositis: Inflammatory cytokines such as IL-6 and TNF-Î± increase, damaging muscle fibres and leading to myositis.
  • Metabolic disruption: Viral infections impair muscle metabolism, leading to energy deficits and muscle cell death (rhabdomyolysis in severe cases).
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Acute Viral Myositis"][/sc]
  • Incubation period: A few days after viral exposure.
  • Acute phase: Myositis onset occurs within 1-3 days of flu-like symptoms and lasts approximately 3-7 days.
  • Recovery: Muscle symptoms resolve within a week in most cases, though weakness can persist slightly longer in severe cases.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Acute Viral Myositis"][/sc]
  • Ask about Myalgia - Sudden onset of severe muscle pain, especially in the calves. Caused by localised muscle inflammation and immune response to viral infection.
  • Ask about Weakness - Reduced muscle strength due to inflammation and local tissue damage affecting muscle function.
  • Ask about Fever - Due to systemic viral infection, causing a pyretic response as the body tries to inhibit viral replication.
  • Ask about Fatigue - Generalised fatigue from systemic inflammation and metabolic disruptions associated with viral illness.
  • Ask about Upper respiratory symptoms - Often precede myositis and include cough, sore throat, and rhinorrhoea, consistent with viral infection.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Acute Viral Myositis"][/sc]
  • Check Muscle tenderness - Localised to the calves or thighs; indicative of underlying muscle inflammation.
  • Check Calf swelling - Swelling from inflammation and oedema may be noted on palpation.
  • Check Gait disturbances - Patients, especially children, may have difficulty walking due to muscle pain and weakness.
  • Check Fever - Often low-grade but may be high, aligning with systemic viral infection signs.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Acute Viral Myositis"][/sc]
  • Dark urine - Suggestive of rhabdomyolysis and myoglobinuria, indicating potential muscle breakdown.
  • Severe weakness - Significant muscle weakness may suggest more extensive muscle involvement or potential for prolonged recovery.
  • Reduced urine output - May signal kidney damage from myoglobin accumulation if rhabdomyolysis is present.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Acute Viral Myositis"][/sc]
  • Asymptomatic - Rare but possible, especially in mild cases where myalgia is minimal and overshadowed by respiratory symptoms.
  • Delayed onset - Muscle pain appearing after initial flu-like symptoms have improved, which may be confusing diagnostically.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Acute Viral Myositis"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Creatine kinase to confirm muscle injury; urinalysis and renal profile to screen for rhabdomyolysis-related kidney involvement.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Viral PCR when confirmation changes infection-control or cohorting decisions; repeat CK, renal function, and urine output monitoring until clear clinical improvement.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Acute Viral Myositis"][/sc]
  • A 6-year-old child presents with acute-onset bilateral calf pain and difficulty walking following a flu-like illness. Examination reveals muscle tenderness with mildly elevated CK levels.
  • Child with acute bilateral calf pain after a viral prodrome, raised CK, and no focal neurological deficit, suggesting acute viral myositis.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Acute Viral Myositis"][/sc]
  • Rhabdomyolysis - Often more severe CK elevation and systemic signs (dark urine, kidney dysfunction).
  • Bacterial myositis - Localised inflammation, often unilateral, with erythema and more marked systemic symptoms.
  • Juvenile dermatomyositis - Chronic muscle weakness, rash, and progressive symptoms not linked to acute viral illness.
  • Polymyositis - Symmetric muscle weakness, typically more prolonged and without preceding flu-like symptoms.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Acute Viral Myositis"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Provide hydration, analgesia, and mobility support, while screening early for rhabdomyolysis and renal impairment.
  • Core care: Most cases are self-limiting with supportive management; treat concurrent viral illness and avoid excessive exertion during recovery.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Reassess pain, walking ability, urine colour, CK trend, and renal function until symptoms and biochemical markers improve.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Escalate urgently for dark urine, oliguria, worsening weakness, rising CK, or systemic deterioration suggestive of complications.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Acute Viral Myositis"][/sc]
  • Rhabdomyolysis - Rare but serious; myoglobin release can cause kidney injury.
  • Kidney damage - Acute kidney injury can occur from myoglobin build-up, requiring intensive monitoring and fluid therapy.
  • Prognosis - Generally excellent; most cases resolve fully within a week with supportive care.
