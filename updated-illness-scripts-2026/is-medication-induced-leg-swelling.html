Medication-Induced Leg Swelling
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Medication-Induced Leg Swelling"][/sc]
  • Medication-induced leg swelling is the accumulation of fluid in the interstitial tissue of the lower extremities due to adverse effects of certain medications.

Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Medication-Induced Leg Swelling"][/sc]
  • Medications such as calcium channel blockers, NSAIDs, corticosteroids, and certain diabetes drugs (e.g. thiazolidinediones) can cause leg swelling by altering vascular permeability or sodium and water retention. Calcium channel blockers, for instance, cause vasodilation of arterioles without comparable dilation of venules, leading to increased capillary hydrostatic pressure and fluid leakage into the interstitial tissue. NSAIDs impair renal excretion of sodium, contributing to fluid retention. Corticosteroids upregulate sodium retention through effects on the kidneys.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Medication-Induced Leg Swelling"][/sc]
  • Onset: Medication-induced oedema typically develops within days to weeks of starting the offending drug.
  • Progression: The swelling may gradually increase if the medication is continued.
  • Resolution: Usually resolves within days to weeks of discontinuing the causative drug, though diuretic use may expedite symptom relief.

Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Medication-Induced Leg Swelling"][/sc]
  • Unilateral swelling - This may suggest a more serious underlying cause such as deep vein thrombosis (DVT), rather than medication-induced oedema.
  • Swelling associated with shortness of breath - May indicate heart failure or pulmonary oedema as a complication, requiring urgent investigation.
  • Severe, painful swelling - Can signify cellulitis or compartment syndrome, which necessitate immediate medical intervention.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Medication-Induced Leg Swelling"][/sc]
  • Unilateral leg swelling without obvious drug cause - Rarely, medication-induced oedema may present unilaterally, leading to confusion with DVT. This could occur due to pre-existing venous insufficiency.
  • Swelling without significant weight gain - Some patients may present with leg oedema in the absence of generalised fluid retention, which can make the diagnosis challenging.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask medication-induced leg swelling.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Medication-Induced Leg Swelling"][/sc]
  • Chronic leg oedema - Prolonged use of the offending medication can lead to persistent leg swelling, with potential for skin changes and venous stasis.
  • Increased risk of DVT - Long-term oedema, particularly if unilateral, may predispose to venous thromboembolism.
