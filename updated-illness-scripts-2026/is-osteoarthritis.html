Osteoarthritis
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Osteoarthritis"][/sc]
  • Osteoarthritis (OA) is a degenerative joint disease characterised by progressive loss of articular cartilage, joint pain, stiffness, and reduced function.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise osteoarthritis features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.

Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Osteoarthritis"][/sc]
  • OA involves progressive degradation of joint cartilage due to an imbalance between cartilage breakdown and repair.
  • 1. Cartilage Degradation: Repeated mechanical stress and age-related changes lead to cartilage matrix loss, causing loss of cushioning in the joint.
  • 2. Subchondral Bone Sclerosis: Bone under the cartilage thickens (sclerosis) and develops cysts, attempting to adapt to cartilage loss, but this leads to pain and stiffness.
  • 3. Synovial Inflammation: Low-grade inflammation occurs in the synovial membrane, releasing cytokines that further degrade cartilage.
  • 4. Osteophyte Formation: Bone spurs or osteophytes develop at the joint margins, leading to pain and limited movement.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Osteoarthritis"][/sc]
  • Early Phase: Minor cartilage damage, occasional pain with movement, mild stiffness.
  • Moderate Phase: Progressive cartilage loss, increased pain with activity, reduced joint space on imaging.
  • Advanced Phase: Marked cartilage erosion, frequent pain at rest, joint deformity, and functional disability.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Osteoarthritis"][/sc]
  • Ask about joint Pain - Most common symptom, worsens with activity and relieved by rest; due to mechanical stress and bone changes.
  • Ask about stiffness - Particularly morning stiffness lasting less than 30 minutes, due to joint capsule thickening and effusion.
  • Ask about reduced Range of Motion - From cartilage loss and osteophyte formation restricting joint movement.
  • Ask about crepitus - A grating sound/sensation due to rough joint surfaces rubbing together.
  • Ask about joint Instability - Occurs in advanced disease as joint deformities progress, often associated with weakness.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Osteoarthritis"][/sc]
  • Check tenderness on Palpation - Commonly over joint line due to cartilage loss and bony changes.
  • Check crepitus - Detectable through palpation; indicates roughened joint surfaces.
  • Check joint Effusion - Mild swelling from synovial fluid, secondary to inflammation.
  • Check osteophytes - Palpable bony enlargements around the joint, common in knees, fingers (e.g., Heberden’s and Bouchard’s nodes).
  • Check joint Deformity - Visible in advanced cases, such as varus deformity in knee OA.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Osteoarthritis"][/sc]
  • Severe, Constant Night Pain - Could indicate secondary causes like infection or malignancy.
  • Rapid Joint Swelling and Erythema - Suggests possible infection or crystal arthropathy.
  • Weight Loss and Fatigue - Could signal systemic inflammatory conditions or malignancy.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Osteoarthritis"][/sc]
  • Non-Painful OA - Some individuals, particularly elderly, may have radiographic OA but report little pain, possibly due to reduced pain sensitivity.
  • Upper Limb Predominance - Uncommon but seen in patients with occupations causing repetitive upper limb strain.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask osteoarthritis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Osteoarthritis"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Plain Radiographs - Common findings: joint space narrowing, osteophytes, subchondral sclerosis. Pathophysiology: reflects cartilage loss and bone adaptation.
  • Targeted and monitoring: ESR/CRP - Usually normal in OA; elevated levels may suggest an inflammatory condition; MRI - Not routinely indicated but may show.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Osteoarthritis"][/sc]
  • Rheumatoid Arthritis - Typically involves smaller joints, prolonged morning stiffness, systemic symptoms.
  • Gout - Acute onset with redness and swelling, often affecting the big toe, elevated serum urate.
  • Psoriatic Arthritis - Associated with skin psoriasis, dactylitis, and nail changes.
  • Related alternative diagnosis - Distinguish from osteoarthritis using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Osteoarthritis"][/sc]
  • Functional Disability - Leading to reduced quality of life, commonly from severe knee or hip OA.
  • Joint Instability - Progression of deformity, causing falls and mobility issues.
  • Psychological Impact - Depression and anxiety due to chronic pain and reduced mobility.
