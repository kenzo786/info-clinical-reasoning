Restless Leg Syndrome (RLS)
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Restless Leg Syndrome (RLS)"][/sc]
  • Restless Leg Syndrome (RLS), also known as Willis-Ekbom disease, is a neurological disorder characterised by an uncontrollable urge to move the legs, often accompanied by unpleasant sensations such as tingling.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise restless features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.

  • Higher-risk or missed groups: Restless Leg Syndrome (RLS) may be missed when presentation is atypical or obscured by comorbidity.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Restless Leg Syndrome (RLS)"][/sc]
  • RLS is associated with dysregulation in dopamine pathways in the central nervous system, particularly in the basal ganglia, which modulates movement and sensory processing.
  • Iron deficiency in the brain is thought to impair dopamine metabolism and function, contributing to symptoms.
  • Genetic factors play a role, with familial clustering observed in approximately 50% of cases.
  • Secondary causes include chronic renal failure, pregnancy, and neuropathy, suggesting multifactorial mechanisms.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Restless Leg Syndrome (RLS)"][/sc]
  • Chronic Course: Symptoms often begin insidiously in early or middle adulthood and gradually worsen over time.
  • Relapsing-Remitting Pattern: Episodes of symptoms with intermittent periods of remission, especially in mild cases or with effective treatment.
  • Pregnancy-Associated: Symptoms commonly emerge in the third trimester and resolve postpartum.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Restless Leg Syndrome (RLS)"][/sc]
  • Ask about unpleasant leg sensations (tingling, burning, or crawling) - Arises due to dysregulated sensory processing in the CNS, exacerbated by iron and dopamine dysfunction.
  • Ask about urge to move legs - A compensatory mechanism to relieve discomfort, linked to activation of dopaminergic pathways.
  • Ask about worse at rest or night - Circadian influences on dopamine and iron metabolism contribute to nocturnal symptom exacerbation.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Restless Leg Syndrome (RLS)"][/sc]
  • Check nocturnal leg movements - Observable during polysomnography or by partner reporting, linked to overactivity in spinal reflex pathways.
  • Check secondary signs of iron deficiency - Such as pallor or brittle nails, indicating potential systemic contributors.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Restless Leg Syndrome (RLS)"][/sc]
  • Sudden onset in older adults - May indicate a secondary cause such as neuropathy or iron-deficiency anaemia.
  • Rapid progression - Suggests an underlying neurological or systemic condition requiring further investigation.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Restless Leg Syndrome (RLS)"][/sc]
  • Symptoms in the arms or other body parts - A less common but recognised variant linked to more severe cases or secondary causes.
  • Absence of discomfort - Rarely, patients may report an urge to move without accompanying sensations, complicating diagnosis.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask restless leg syndrome (rls).
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Restless Leg Syndrome (RLS)"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Clinical diagnosis: Based on history meeting diagnostic criteria (URGE: Urge to move, Rest-induced, Gets better with movement, Evening or night worsening).
  • Targeted and monitoring: Ferritin levels: Low levels (<50 µg/L) indicate iron deficiency, a modifiable contributor; Polysomnography: Identifies periodic limb movements during sleep, supporting diagnosis.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Restless Leg Syndrome (RLS)"][/sc]
  • Nocturnal leg cramps - Typically involve painful muscle contractions rather than sensory symptoms.
  • Peripheral neuropathy - Sensory symptoms are more constant and not relieved by movement.
  • Varicose veins - Discomfort is positional and linked to venous stasis rather than circadian variation.
  • Related alternative diagnosis - Distinguish from restless leg syndrome (rls) using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.

  • Immediate priorities: Assess severity, physiological stability, and immediate complications.
  • Core care: Deliver condition-specific management for restless leg syndrome (rls), treat underlying drivers, and coordinate appropriate specialist input.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Reassess symptom trajectory, examination findings, and objective markers to confirm response in restless leg syndrome (rls).
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Escalate urgently for clinical deterioration, evolving red flags, or failure to improve in suspected restless leg syndrome (rls).
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Restless Leg Syndrome (RLS)"][/sc]
  • Sleep disturbances: Leading to insomnia and daytime fatigue.
  • Depression and anxiety: Associated with chronic symptom burden.
  • Augmentation: Worsening symptoms with prolonged dopaminergic treatment.
