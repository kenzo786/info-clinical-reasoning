Silent Myocardial Infarction (SMI)
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Silent Myocardial Infarction (SMI)"][/sc]
  • Silent Myocardial Infarction (SMI) refers to a myocardial infarction (MI) that occurs without the classical symptoms of chest pain or discomfort.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise silent features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Silent Myocardial Infarction (SMI)"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Silent Myocardial Infarction (SMI)"][/sc]
  • SMIs are more common in the elderly, patients with diabetes, and individuals with a history of coronary artery disease (CAD).
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Silent Myocardial Infarction (SMI)"][/sc]
  • SMIs occur due to a similar process as symptomatic MIs, primarily involving the rupture of an atherosclerotic plaque in coronary arteries. This leads to partial or complete occlusion of the vessel, reducing blood flow to the myocardium and causing ischaemia.
  • In silent MIs, the autonomic response to ischaemia may be blunted, particularly in patients with diabetes (due to neuropathy), resulting in an absence of pain perception. Additionally, a higher threshold for symptom recognition in the elderly or those with prior cardiovascular conditions may contribute to the "silent" nature of the event.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Silent Myocardial Infarction (SMI)"][/sc]
  • Silent MIs may present acutely, but more commonly, they are discovered retrospectively during investigations for other cardiac issues. The course typically mirrors that of symptomatic MIs in terms of myocardial damage but without a clear symptom onset.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Silent Myocardial Infarction (SMI)"][/sc]
  • Ask about fatigue - Generalised weakness or fatigue may be the only symptom, resulting from reduced cardiac output due to myocardial damage.
  • Ask about shortness of breath (dyspnoea) - May occur due to impaired ventricular function and increased pulmonary pressure.
  • Ask about mild discomfort - Some patients may report vague or atypical discomfort, such as in the jaw, back, or epigastrium, rather than classic chest pain.
  • Ask about palpitations - Irregular heartbeats can arise from electrical instability caused by myocardial ischaemia.
  • Ask about no symptoms - Many SMIs are entirely asymptomatic, especially in diabetic and elderly populations due to altered pain perception.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Silent Myocardial Infarction (SMI)"][/sc]
  • Check tachycardia - A faster heart rate may reflect the body’s compensatory response to decreased cardiac output or underlying ischaemia.
  • Check hypotension - Reduced cardiac output due to myocardial injury can cause low blood pressure, indicating a significant loss of myocardial function.
  • Check heart failure signs - Such as peripheral oedema, elevated jugular venous pressure (JVP), or pulmonary crackles may be present in patients with impaired ventricular.
  • Check diaphoresis - Sweating may occur, though it is less frequent in silent MIs compared to classic presentations.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Silent Myocardial Infarction (SMI)"][/sc]
  • Unexplained syncope - May indicate a significant ischaemic event leading to arrhythmias.
  • Sudden dyspnoea - Could signify acute heart failure or significant left ventricular dysfunction.
  • Palpitations with dizziness - Suggestive of arrhythmias, particularly ventricular tachyarrhythmias, which are life-threatening.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Silent Myocardial Infarction (SMI)"][/sc]
  • Some patients may only report generalised malaise, indigestion-like symptoms, or mild breathlessness. This is more common in women, older adults, and diabetic patients. These atypical symptoms occur due to differences in autonomic regulation and possibly varying thresholds for symptom perception.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask silent myocardial infarction (smi).

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Silent Myocardial Infarction (SMI)"][/sc]
  • Stable angina - Characterised by predictable chest pain triggered by exertion, typically relieved by rest or nitroglycerin.
  • Heart failure - Can present with fatigue and dyspnoea but typically involves other signs of fluid overload.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Silent Myocardial Infarction (SMI)"][/sc]
  • Heart failure - Common in cases where significant myocardial tissue has been damaged.
  • Arrhythmias - Ventricular tachycardia or fibrillation can occur due to ischaemic damage to the electrical conduction system.
  • Cardiogenic shock - Results from severe impairment of myocardial function, leading to inadequate systemic perfusion.
