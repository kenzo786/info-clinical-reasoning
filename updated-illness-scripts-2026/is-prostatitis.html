Prostatitis
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Prostatitis"][/sc]
  • Prostatitis is an inflammatory condition of the prostate gland, often caused by bacterial infection or chronic inflammation.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise prostatitis features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.

Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Prostatitis"][/sc]
  • Prostatitis often begins with an infection in the urinary tract, which can ascend to the prostate, particularly in cases of acute bacterial prostatitis. Common pathogens include Escherichia coli, Klebsiella, and Proteus species.
  • In chronic bacterial prostatitis, recurrent infections lead to persistent inflammation within the prostate, sometimes due to biofilm formation by bacteria, which resists immune clearance.
  • In chronic pelvic pain syndrome (CPPS), no bacterial cause is identified, and pathophysiology involves inflammatory and neuropathic mechanisms, likely related to pelvic muscle dysfunction and nerve sensitisation.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Prostatitis"][/sc]
  • Acute bacterial prostatitis: Sudden onset of symptoms, typically within days. Symptoms may resolve with treatment over 2-4 weeks.
  • Chronic bacterial prostatitis: Recurrent or persistent symptoms lasting >3 months, with remissions and exacerbations.
  • Chronic pelvic pain syndrome (CPPS): Persistent symptoms for >3 months, often fluctuating in intensity.
  • Asymptomatic inflammatory prostatitis: Detected incidentally without symptoms; no acute time course.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Prostatitis"][/sc]
  • Ask about pelvic or perineal pain - Common in all types; due to inflammation of the prostate and surrounding structures.
  • Ask about dysuria - Pain during urination, resulting from inflammation of the prostate and nearby urethra.
  • Ask about urinary frequency and urgency - Increased sensitivity of the bladder and surrounding nerves, often with urinary irritation.
  • Ask about fever and chills - In acute bacterial prostatitis, systemic inflammation causes these symptoms.
  • Ask about lower back pain - Due to proximity of the prostate to pelvic and sacral nerves.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Prostatitis"][/sc]
  • Check tender prostate on digital rectal examination (DRE) - In acute cases, the prostate may be swollen and tender; in chronic cases, tenderness may be.
  • Check suprapubic tenderness - Reflects inflammation in the pelvic region.
  • Check fever - Common in acute bacterial prostatitis, as systemic infection activates the immune response.
  • Check reduced or interrupted urine flow - Occurs due to inflammation and swelling around the urethra.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Prostatitis"][/sc]
  • Sepsis - Systemic infection can progress to sepsis in acute bacterial prostatitis; manifests as fever, tachycardia, hypotension, and confusion.
  • Urinary retention - Inflammation may obstruct urine flow, leading to acute retention and bladder distension.
  • Abscess formation - A complication in severe cases, indicated by fluctuating fevers and worsening pelvic pain.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Prostatitis"][/sc]
  • Asymptomatic inflammatory prostatitis - Identified incidentally during investigations for other conditions, with no reported symptoms.
  • Isolated lower back pain - In chronic cases, may present without classic urinary symptoms, causing diagnostic challenges.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask prostatitis.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Prostatitis"][/sc]
  • Urinary tract infection (UTI) - Similar urinary symptoms but lacks prostate tenderness on DRE.
  • Benign prostatic hyperplasia (BPH) - Common in older men with urinary symptoms but without pain or systemic symptoms.
  • Prostate cancer - Usually asymptomatic or with obstructive symptoms; often detected with elevated PSA and biopsy.
  • Related alternative diagnosis - Distinguish from prostatitis using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Prostatitis"][/sc]
  • Sepsis - More likely in acute bacterial prostatitis if untreated, with potential systemic impact.
  • Chronic prostatitis - Untreated acute prostatitis can progress to chronic symptoms and recurrent infections.
  • Prostate abscess - Develops in severe cases of bacterial infection, requiring drainage and prolonged antibiotics.
