Patellar Tendon Rupture
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Patellar Tendon Rupture"][/sc]
  • Patellar tendon rupture refers to a complete or partial tear of the patellar tendon, which connects the patella (kneecap) to the tibial tuberosity.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise patellar features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.

Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Patellar Tendon Rupture"][/sc]
  • Mechanical overload of the patellar tendon, typically during activities involving rapid forceful quadriceps contraction (e.g., jumping).
  • Underlying tendon degeneration (tendinopathy) or chronic overuse weakens the structure of the tendon, predisposing it to rupture.
  • Acute trauma or systemic conditions (e.g., diabetes, chronic steroid use) may further compromise tendon integrity.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Patellar Tendon Rupture"][/sc]
  • Acute phase: Immediate pain, swelling, and functional impairment following the injury.
  • Subacute phase: If untreated, persistent inability to bear weight or extend the knee, with progressive atrophy of the quadriceps muscle.
  • Chronic phase: Fibrosis and malalignment of the knee extensor mechanism, leading to long-term disability.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Patellar Tendon Rupture"][/sc]
  • Ask about sudden onset knee pain - Directly correlates with the traumatic event that causes the rupture, due to acute inflammation and damage to tissue.
  • Ask about inability to extend the knee - Reflects loss of continuity in the extensor mechanism, rendering active extension mechanically impossible.
  • Ask about swelling - Localised oedema results from inflammation and haemorrhage into the surrounding tissues.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Patellar Tendon Rupture"][/sc]
  • Check palpable gap below the patella - Indicates a discontinuity in the tendon.
  • Check high-riding patella (patella alta) - Seen on imaging or inspection, as the unanchored patella is pulled proximally by the quadriceps.
  • Check inability to perform straight leg raise - Reflects the disruption of the extensor mechanism.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Patellar Tendon Rupture"][/sc]
  • Severe pain with deformity - May indicate a complete rupture and need for urgent orthopaedic intervention.
  • Inability to bear weight - Suggests extensive functional impairment.
  • Associated neurovascular compromise - Rare but signifies a limb-threatening complication.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Patellar Tendon Rupture"][/sc]
  • Partial ruptures may present with mild pain and residual extension, mimicking tendinopathy.
  • Chronic ruptures might be misinterpreted as osteoarthritis due to prolonged weakness and stiffness.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask patellar tendon rupture.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Patellar Tendon Rupture"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: X-ray - Shows patella alta and rule out fractures.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Targeted investigations to confirm diagnosis, assess complications, and monitor response in patellar tendon rupture.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Patellar Tendon Rupture"][/sc]
  • Quadriceps tendon rupture - Typically results in low-riding patella and difficulty with knee extension.
  • Patellar fracture - Presents with acute pain and inability to extend the knee, but imaging reveals fractures.
  • Knee dislocation - Severe deformity and associated neurovascular compromise are key distinguishing features.
  • Related alternative diagnosis - Distinguish from patellar tendon rupture using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Patellar Tendon Rupture"][/sc]
  • Chronic extensor lag - Due to inadequate repair or delayed treatment.
  • Re-rupture - More common in those with poorly controlled risk factors (e.g., diabetes).
  • Osteoarthritis - Due to long-term biomechanical imbalance and joint stress.
