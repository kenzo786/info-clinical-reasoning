Medication-induced Lymphadenopathy
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Medication-induced Lymphadenopathy"][/sc]
  • Medication-induced lymphadenopathy is an adverse drug reaction characterised by the enlargement of lymph nodes in response to certain medications.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise medication features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.

Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Medication-induced Lymphadenopathy"][/sc]
  • Drug-induced lymphadenopathy typically results from hypersensitivity reactions, particularly type III (immune complex-mediated) or type IV (T-cell mediated) reactions. The drug or its metabolites act as haptens, triggering immune activation and resulting in lymphocyte proliferation, lymph node enlargement, and inflammation.
  • Certain medications may provoke immune responses directly through antigen presentation, causing an immune cascade that leads to lymph node hyperplasia.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Medication-induced Lymphadenopathy"][/sc]
  • Initial phase: Lymphadenopathy typically arises within 2-8 weeks after starting the implicated medication, coinciding with the development of immune responses.
  • Progression: Lymph node enlargement can persist while the medication is continued but generally resolves upon cessation.
  • Resolution: Lymphadenopathy usually resolves within 1-4 weeks after stopping the offending drug, although complete regression may take longer depending on immune modulation and lymph node histological changes.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Medication-induced Lymphadenopathy"][/sc]
  • Ask about painless lymph node swelling - Frequently the sole symptom; reflects immune-mediated proliferation of lymphocytes and associated inflammatory processes.
  • Ask about fever - An immune response to inflammation and cytokine release, often associated with systemic drug reactions.
  • Ask about fatigue - Commonly observed in systemic drug reactions; cytokine-mediated effects on energy levels.
  • Ask about skin rash (if part of drug reaction) - Associated with certain medications (e.g., anticonvulsants); represents hypersensitivity response with T-cell involvement.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Medication-induced Lymphadenopathy"][/sc]
  • Check enlarged lymph nodes - Soft, mobile, non-tender lymph nodes, primarily cervical but can be generalised; reflects lymphocyte and macrophage proliferation within nodes.
  • Check maculopapular rash - Often seen in drug-induced reactions, particularly with antibiotics and anticonvulsants, as part of systemic hypersensitivity.
  • Check splenomegaly (rare) - May occur due to systemic immune response and immune cell accumulation.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Medication-induced Lymphadenopathy"][/sc]
  • Rapidly progressive lymphadenopathy - May indicate malignancy rather than drug reaction; requires immediate evaluation to rule out lymphoproliferative disorders.
  • Severe systemic symptoms (fever, weight loss, night sweats) - B symptoms may suggest an alternative diagnosis like lymphoma; further workup with imaging and biopsy may be necessary.
  • Fixed, hard, non-mobile nodes - Suggestive of malignancy rather than reactive lymphadenopathy; concerning for neoplastic involvement.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Medication-induced Lymphadenopathy"][/sc]
  • Single-site lymphadenopathy - May mimic infections or primary lymph node disease; often under-recognised as drug-related.
  • Asymptomatic lymphadenopathy - Some patients may have isolated lymphadenopathy without systemic symptoms; requires careful history to identify medication-related causes.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask medication-induced lymphadenopathy.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Medication-induced Lymphadenopathy"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Complete blood count (CBC) - May show eosinophilia if drug reaction has eosinophilic involvement, commonly seen in hypersensitivity reactions; Blood chemistry.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Lymph node biopsy - Reserved for unclear cases; histology shows reactive hyperplasia without atypia, ruling out malignancy; Imaging (ultrasound, CT) .

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Medication-induced Lymphadenopathy"][/sc]
  • Infectious lymphadenopathy - Generally associated with tenderness and specific systemic symptoms (e.g., sore throat in bacterial infections).
  • Lymphoma - Presents with ‘B’ symptoms (fever, night sweats, weight loss), and often with firm, non-mobile.
  • Autoimmune lymphadenopathy (e.g., SLE) - More commonly multisystem involvement, may have serological markers indicating autoimmune disease.
  • Related alternative diagnosis - Distinguish from medication-induced lymphadenopathy using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Medication-induced Lymphadenopathy"][/sc]
  • Persistent lymphadenopathy - May continue for an extended period after drug cessation, occasionally requiring further diagnostic workup.
  • Systemic drug reactions (e.g., DRESS syndrome) - Associated with severe hypersensitivity reactions, can involve liver and kidney damage; requires intensive management.
  • Increased risk of secondary infections - Particularly if corticosteroids are used for management, leading to immunosuppression.
