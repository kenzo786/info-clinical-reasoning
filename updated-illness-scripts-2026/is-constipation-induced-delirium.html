Constipation-Induced Delirium
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Constipation-Induced Delirium"][/sc]
  • Constipation-induced delirium refers to an acute confusional state triggered by severe constipation.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise constipation features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Constipation-Induced Delirium"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Constipation-Induced Delirium"][/sc]
  • Worldwide, similar trends are seen, particularly in aged care and post-surgical settings where constipation and cognitive impairment often overlap.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Constipation-Induced Delirium"][/sc]
  • Step 1: Severe constipation leads to faecal impaction, distending the bowel.
  • Step 2: This distention triggers increased bowel wall pressure, which can lead to autonomic dysregulation, causing metabolic imbalances such as electrolyte disturbances (hypokalaemia, hyponatraemia).
  • Step 3: Reduced oral intake due to discomfort from constipation can cause dehydration, exacerbating the electrolyte imbalance.
  • Step 4: These imbalances disrupt cerebral neurotransmission, particularly acetylcholine and dopamine, leading to cognitive dysfunction and delirium.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Constipation-Induced Delirium"][/sc]
  • Early Phase: Constipation presents first, often developing over several days to weeks.
  • Progressive Phase: Cognitive changes such as confusion, disorientation, and reduced attention span appear, typically within hours to days after significant faecal impaction or metabolic disruption.
  • Recovery Phase: With appropriate treatment, delirium usually resolves within days, but full recovery may take longer in those with underlying cognitive impairment.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Constipation-Induced Delirium"][/sc]
  • Ask about constipation: Primary symptom; manifests as infrequent, hard stools and difficulty defecating. Prolonged stool retention leads to faecal impaction, which triggers systemic effects.
  • Ask about confusion: The main feature of delirium, arising from electrolyte imbalances and impaired neurotransmission.
  • Ask about disorientation: Patients may struggle to recognise their surroundings due to altered cerebral function.
  • Ask about agitation: Restlessness or irritability is common in delirium, linked to neurotransmitter disruption, especially acetylcholine deficits.
  • Ask about lethargy: Dehydration and metabolic imbalances can cause weakness and drowsiness.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Constipation-Induced Delirium"][/sc]
  • Check faecal impaction: On abdominal examination, there may be a palpable mass in the lower abdomen or on rectal examination.
  • Check tachycardia: Often present due to dehydration or stress caused by faecal retention.
  • Check altered mental status: Fluctuations in cognitive function are hallmarks of delirium, with periods of lucidity interspersed with confusion.
  • Check dry mucous membranes: Indicative of dehydration, often contributing to the metabolic disturbances causing delirium.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Constipation-Induced Delirium"][/sc]
  • Severe abdominal pain: May indicate bowel obstruction or perforation due to faecal impaction.
  • Sepsis-like symptoms: Fever, hypotension, and altered mental status can suggest a perforation or serious infection.
  • Severe electrolyte imbalances: Can manifest as muscle cramps, arrhythmias, or seizures, signifying life-threatening metabolic disturbances.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Constipation-Induced Delirium"][/sc]
  • Silent confusion: Some patients, especially the elderly, may present primarily with cognitive decline, without overt gastrointestinal complaints.
  • Delirium without obvious constipation: In cognitively impaired individuals, signs of constipation may be missed, and delirium could be attributed to other causes, delaying diagnosis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Constipation-Induced Delirium"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Abdominal X-ray: Shows faecal loading in the colon, particularly in cases of faecal impaction.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Serum electrolytes: Hypokalaemia, hyponatraemia, or hypercalcaemia can be detected, reflecting the metabolic derangements associated with constipation and dehydration; Full blood count.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Constipation-Induced Delirium"][/sc]
  • An 82-year-old female with a history of dementia presents with worsening confusion and abdominal discomfort over three days. She has not opened her bowels for a week and appears disoriented.
  • Adult with symptom pattern and objective findings suggestive of constipation-induced delirium, requiring focused confirmation and risk stratification.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Constipation-Induced Delirium"][/sc]
  • Urinary tract infection (UTI) - Both UTI and constipation-induced delirium can present with confusion, especially in older adults, but.
  • Electrolyte imbalance from other causes - Could be secondary to diuretics or renal failure, without faecal impaction on imaging.
  • Dementia exacerbation - A chronic decline in cognitive function differs from the acute onset and fluctuating nature.
  • Alternative serious diagnosis - Distinguishing history, examination, and targeted investigations do not support constipation-induced delirium.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Constipation-Induced Delirium"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Assess physiological stability and urgent complications at presentation, and initiate early stabilisation where required.
  • Core care: Deliver condition-specific treatment for constipation-induced delirium, address underlying drivers, and involve appropriate specialist support.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Reassess symptom trajectory, objective markers, and treatment response over time to detect complications early.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Worsening symptoms, physiological deterioration, or failure to improve should trigger urgent reassessment for severe or alternative pathology in constipation-induced delirium.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Constipation-Induced Delirium"][/sc]
  • Bowel obstruction: Can result from untreated faecal impaction.
  • Bowel perforation: A rare but life-threatening complication of severe faecal impaction.
  • Severe dehydration: Can lead to kidney injury and significant electrolyte disturbances.
