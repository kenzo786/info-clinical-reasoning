Intra-abdominal Abscess
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Intra-abdominal Abscess"][/sc]
  • An intra-abdominal abscess is a localised collection of pus within the peritoneal cavity, typically caused by infection following gastrointestinal perforation, surgery, or trauma.

Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Intra-abdominal Abscess"][/sc]
  • An intra-abdominal abscess forms when bacteria, introduced into the sterile peritoneal cavity through perforation or surgery, trigger an inflammatory response. Neutrophils and macrophages sequester the bacteria, leading to localised pus formation. Fibrin deposition walls off the abscess, limiting spread but causing pain and systemic symptoms.
  • Common pathogens include gut flora, such as *Escherichia coli*, *Bacteroides fragilis*, and *Enterococcus* species.
  • Abscess formation is a late complication, typically following a delay in resolving the primary cause of infection (e.g., bowel perforation, post-operative leak).
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Intra-abdominal Abscess"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Within hours to days post-perforation or trauma, localised peritonitis develops.
  • Abscess formation: Over days to weeks, the abscess matures as the body attempts to wall off infection.
  • Chronic phase: If untreated, the abscess can persist for weeks to months, leading to further complications like fistula formation or sepsis.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Intra-abdominal Abscess"][/sc]
  • Ask about fever - Commonly presents due to systemic inflammation and cytokine release (IL-1, TNF) in response to infection.
  • Ask about abdominal pain - Results from localised inflammation and stretching of the peritoneum; typically localised over the abscess site.
  • Ask about malaise and fatigue - General systemic response to infection, possibly due to cytokine-mediated effects on energy metabolism.
  • Ask about loss of appetite - Common in severe infections due to systemic inflammatory response syndrome (SIRS).
  • Ask about nausea and vomiting - Often due to irritation of the gastrointestinal tract or the involvement of nearby structures.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Intra-abdominal Abscess"][/sc]
  • Check tachycardia - Due to systemic inflammation and fever, leading to increased metabolic demand.
  • Check abdominal tenderness - Localised over the abscess site, indicative of peritoneal irritation.
  • Check rebound tenderness - Suggests peritonitis, typically seen when the abscess irritates the surrounding peritoneal structures.
  • Check pallor - Associated with systemic illness or chronic infection.
  • Check guarding or rigidity - Suggestive of peritoneal irritation and involuntary contraction of abdominal muscles.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Intra-abdominal Abscess"][/sc]
  • Severe sepsis or septic shock - Indicated by hypotension, altered mental status, and multi-organ failure, suggesting systemic infection has overwhelmed local defences.
  • Worsening abdominal pain with signs of peritonitis - Suggests perforation or rupture of the abscess.
  • Jaundice - Can indicate liver involvement or biliary abscess, leading to systemic complications.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Intra-abdominal Abscess"][/sc]
  • Absence of fever in immunocompromised patients - Due to impaired immune response, these patients may not mount a febrile response despite severe infection.
  • Minimal abdominal pain in elderly patients - Altered pain perception in elderly individuals can mask classic symptoms of abdominal abscess.
  • Vague systemic symptoms in diabetics - Diabetic patients may present with only non-specific signs, such as fatigue or confusion, due to altered immune and pain responses.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask intra-abdominal abscess.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Intra-abdominal Abscess"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Focused bedside assessment and first-line testing to assess severity and immediate risk in suspected intra-abdominal abscess.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Ultrasound - Useful initial imaging modality to localise the abscess, particularly in superficial areas or the pelvis; CT Abdomen/Pelvis with contrast.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Intra-abdominal Abscess"][/sc]
  • Diverticulitis - Typically presents with left lower quadrant pain, fever, and altered bowel habits; CT imaging.
  • Pancreatic abscess - Presents with epigastric pain radiating to the back, usually following pancreatitis; CT will localise.
  • Psoas abscess - Distinguish from intra-abdominal abscess using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Intra-abdominal Abscess"][/sc]
  • Sepsis and septic shock - Resulting from systemic spread of infection; occurs in untreated or ruptured abscesses.
  • Fistula formation - Chronic abscesses may erode into surrounding structures, forming fistulae, which can complicate management.
  • Recurrence - Abscesses may recur, particularly if the underlying cause (e.g., bowel perforation) is not adequately addressed.
