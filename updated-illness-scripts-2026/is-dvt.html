Deep Vein Thrombosis (DVT)
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Deep Vein Thrombosis (DVT)"][/sc]
  • DVT is the formation of a blood clot (thrombus) in a deep vein, typically in the legs.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise deep features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.

Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Deep Vein Thrombosis (DVT)"][/sc]
  • DVT is primarily driven by Virchow's triad: venous stasis, endothelial injury, and hypercoagulability.
  • Venous stasis occurs when blood flow is slowed, often due to immobility, surgery, or prolonged sitting.
  • Endothelial injury triggers the coagulation cascade, leading to clot formation. This may occur due to trauma, surgery, or vascular inflammation.
  • Hypercoagulability can be inherited (e.g., factor V Leiden mutation) or acquired (e.g., cancer, oral contraceptives).
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Deep Vein Thrombosis (DVT)"][/sc]
  • Acute phase: DVT typically develops over hours to days, especially after triggering events like surgery or prolonged immobility.
  • Subacute phase: The clot stabilises over days to weeks; symptoms may improve or worsen depending on size and location of the thrombus.
  • Chronic phase: Untreated DVT can lead to long-term complications such as post-thrombotic syndrome, characterised by chronic leg swelling and pain.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Deep Vein Thrombosis (DVT)"][/sc]
  • Ask about unilateral leg pain: Commonly due to venous obstruction and inflammatory mediators causing localised pain.
  • Ask about swelling in one leg: Caused by impaired venous return leading to fluid accumulation (oedema).
  • Ask about redness or warmth: Inflammatory response in the surrounding tissues due to the thrombus.
  • Ask about asymptomatic: Around 50% of DVT cases may be clinically silent, with symptoms only manifesting after complications such as PE.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Deep Vein Thrombosis (DVT)"][/sc]
  • Check calf tenderness: Indicative of local inflammation due to the presence of a thrombus in the deep veins.
  • Check unilateral leg oedema: Caused by venous outflow obstruction leading to fluid accumulation.
  • Check homan's sign: Pain on dorsiflexion of the foot, though it is not specific or highly sensitive for DVT.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Deep Vein Thrombosis (DVT)"][/sc]
  • Shortness of breath and chest pain: May indicate a pulmonary embolism (PE), a potentially life-threatening complication of DVT.
  • Severe leg pain with cyanosis: Suggests a rare complication called phlegmasia cerulea dolens, which can lead to venous gangrene if untreated.
  • Tachycardia, hypotension: These signs could be associated with a massive PE causing cardiovascular compromise.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Deep Vein Thrombosis (DVT)"][/sc]
  • Bilateral leg swelling: Rare but possible, especially in pelvic vein DVT, which may cause more widespread venous obstruction.
  • Upper extremity DVT: Rare and usually associated with central venous catheters or thoracic outlet syndrome.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask deep vein thrombosis (dvt).
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Deep Vein Thrombosis (DVT)"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: D-dimer: Elevated in DVT due to fibrin degradation products; however, non-specific and can be elevated in other conditions.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Compression ultrasound: The gold standard in diagnosing DVT. Lack of vein compressibility is a key finding; Venography: Rarely used but considered.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Deep Vein Thrombosis (DVT)"][/sc]
  • Pulmonary embolism: Occurs in up to 50% of untreated DVTs, can be life-threatening.
  • Post-thrombotic syndrome: Chronic pain and swelling due to venous insufficiency; affects up to 50% of patients.
  • Phlegmasia cerulea dolens: Severe complication with high morbidity, involving venous gangrene due to extensive clotting and venous obstruction.
