Henoch-Schönlein Purpura (HSP)
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Henoch-Schönlein Purpura (HSP)"][/sc]
  • Henoch-Schönlein Purpura (HSP) is an immune-mediated vasculitis affecting small vessels, predominantly in the skin, gastrointestinal tract, kidneys, and joints.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise henoch features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.

Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Henoch-Schönlein Purpura (HSP)"][/sc]
  • HSP is an IgA-mediated immune complex vasculitis that triggers inflammation in small vessels.
    • 1. Antigens, often following an infection (commonly streptococcal or viral), lead to an abnormal immune response.
    • 2. IgA immune complexes form and deposit in vessel walls, particularly in skin, kidneys, and GI tract.
    • 3. Immune complexes activate the complement system, causing neutrophil and macrophage infiltration, leading to vessel inflammation and tissue damage.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Henoch-Schönlein Purpura (HSP)"][/sc]
  • Acute phase (2-6 weeks): Symptoms develop, often with purpura and joint pain initially, followed by abdominal pain and renal involvement.
  • Resolution phase: Symptoms gradually subside; however, renal symptoms can persist or recur over months, particularly in adults.
  • Chronic phase: In rare cases, chronic kidney disease may develop as a complication of prolonged renal involvement.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Henoch-Schönlein Purpura (HSP)"][/sc]
  • Ask about purpura - Non-blanching purpura, typically on lower limbs and buttocks, due to small vessel vasculitis causing extravasation of red blood cells.
  • Ask about arthralgia/Arthritis - Pain and swelling in knees and ankles, related to immune complex deposition in joint tissues.
  • Ask about abdominal Pain - Colicky pain due to intestinal involvement, often presenting with nausea or vomiting, caused by vascular inflammation in the GI tract.
  • Ask about haematuria/Proteinuria - Renal involvement may lead to blood/protein in urine, a result of glomerular inflammation and IgA deposition.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Henoch-Schönlein Purpura (HSP)"][/sc]
  • Check palpable Purpura - Non-blanching purpuric rash on lower extremities and buttocks.
  • Check joint Swelling - Swelling and tenderness in large joints, especially the knees and ankles.
  • Check abdominal Tenderness - Localised tenderness, particularly in the lower abdomen.
  • Check hypertension - May occur with significant renal involvement, due to impaired kidney function.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Henoch-Schönlein Purpura (HSP)"][/sc]
  • Severe abdominal pain - May indicate intussusception, a serious complication of HSP in children.
  • Renal impairment signs - Persistent hypertension, oliguria, or rising creatinine may signal developing nephropathy.
  • Severe proteinuria or nephrotic syndrome - Indicates significant glomerular involvement and risk for long-term renal damage.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Henoch-Schönlein Purpura (HSP)"][/sc]
  • HSP without purpura is rare but may occur; GI or renal symptoms alone can delay diagnosis.
  • Severe renal involvement without systemic symptoms may be more common in adults, leading to an insidious presentation.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask henoch-schönlein purpura (hsp).
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Henoch-Schönlein Purpura (HSP)"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Urinalysis: Haematuria and proteinuria are common, reflecting renal involvement; Renal function tests: Elevated creatinine or decreased GFR in severe cases indicate.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Full blood count: Platelet count is normal, helping distinguish HSP from thrombocytopenic purpura; IgA levels: May be elevated but are not.

  • Kawasaki Disease - Presents with fever and lymphadenopathy, often without purpura, and usually affects younger children.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Henoch-Schönlein Purpura (HSP)"][/sc]
  • Chronic kidney disease (CKD): Occurs in a minority, particularly adults with prolonged nephropathy.
  • Intussusception: Rare but life-threatening complication due to intestinal swelling and obstruction in children.
  • Hypertension: Can develop secondary to chronic kidney disease.
