Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Tension Type Headache (TTH)"][/sc]
  • Though the exact mechanism is not fully understood, TTH is thought to result from increased sensitivity of pain pathways in the central nervous system, particularly involving the pericranial muscles. Prolonged muscle tension and stress are believed to activate nociceptors, leading to pain perception. Altered serotonin and endogenous opioid systems may also play a role in pain regulation and perception.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Tension Type Headache (TTH)"][/sc]
  • Episodic TTH – Attacks last from 30 minutes to 7 days, occurring infrequently or up to 15 days per month.
  • Chronic TTH – Headache occurs on more than 15 days per month for at least 3 months.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Tension Type Headache (TTH)"][/sc]
  • Ask about bilateral headache – Most commonly described as a dull, non-pulsating pain on both sides of the head. Pathophysiology: Thought to be related to muscle.
  • Ask about pressure or tightening sensation – Patients often describe the pain as feeling like a tight band around the head. Pathophysiology: Linked to sustained contraction.
  • Ask about absence of nausea or vomiting – A key distinguishing feature from migraine. Pathophysiology: Lack of central sensitisation typical of migraines.
  • Ask about photophobia or phonophobia (mild) – Some patients may experience light or sound sensitivity, but these are less pronounced than in migraine. Pathophysiology: Mild hyperexcitability.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Tension Type Headache (TTH)"][/sc]
  • Check tenderness of pericranial muscles – On palpation, patients often report tenderness over the scalp, neck, or shoulders. Pathophysiology: Muscle tension and myofascial trigger points.
  • Check no neurological deficits – Unlike migraines or cluster headaches, there are no focal neurological signs. Pathophysiology: Lack of involvement of central nervous system pathways.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Tension Type Headache (TTH)"][/sc]
  • Sudden onset ("thunderclap" headache) – May indicate subarachnoid haemorrhage or other serious intracranial event. Pathophysiology: Rapid intracranial pressure change.
  • Progressive or worsening headaches – Could suggest a space-occupying lesion or increased intracranial pressure. Pathophysiology: Mass effect in the brain.
  • Focal neurological signs – Suggests a structural lesion or stroke. Pathophysiology: Localised disruption of brain function.
  • Headache triggered by cough, exertion, or Valsalva manoeuvre – Can indicate increased intracranial pressure (ICP) or Chiari malformation. Pathophysiology: Increased ICP or structural abnormalities at the base of the skull.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Tension Type Headache (TTH)"][/sc]
  • Unilateral pain – While TTH is typically bilateral, some cases may present unilaterally, which could lead to confusion with migraine.
  • Shorter duration – Tension headaches lasting less than 30 minutes may be mistaken for other headache types.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask tension type headache (tth).
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Tension Type Headache (TTH)"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Clinical diagnosis – Based on history and physical examination. No specific imaging or tests are required unless red flag features are.
  • Targeted and monitoring: CT/MRI head – Indicated only if red flags are present. Expected findings: Normal imaging, unless a secondary cause (e.g., mass lesion).
