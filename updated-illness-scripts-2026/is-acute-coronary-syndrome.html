Acute Coronary Syndrome (ACS)
ACS Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Acute Coronary Syndrome (ACS)"][/sc]
  • Acute coronary syndrome is acute myocardial ischaemia from coronary plaque disruption and thrombosis, including unstable angina, NSTEMI, and STEMI.
  • UKMLA essentials: central ischaemic chest pain, shock or arrhythmia red flags, immediate ECG and troponin pathway.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Acute Coronary Syndrome (ACS)"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Acute Coronary Syndrome (ACS)"][/sc]
  • ACS is a common emergency presentation in adults with cardiovascular risk factors in both pre-hospital and hospital care.
  • Risk is increased by smoking, diabetes, hypertension, dyslipidaemia, chronic kidney disease, and prior coronary disease.
  • Delayed recognition is more likely in atypical presentations, particularly in older adults, women, and people with diabetes.
ACS Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Acute Coronary Syndrome (ACS)"][/sc]
  • Plaque rupture or erosion exposes thrombogenic material, triggering platelet aggregation and thrombus formation.
  • Partial or complete coronary occlusion causes myocardial oxygen supply-demand mismatch and ischaemia.
  • Persistent severe ischaemia leads to myocardial necrosis, producing NSTEMI or STEMI depending on infarct pattern.
ACS Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Acute Coronary Syndrome (ACS)"][/sc]
  • Onset: Symptoms usually begin abruptly over minutes to hours with persistent or recurrent ischaemic discomfort.
  • Progression: Without timely reperfusion or anti-ischaemic treatment, injury extends and complications such as arrhythmia or heart failure can develop.
ACS Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Acute Coronary Syndrome (ACS)"][/sc]
  • Ask about central chest pressure or tightness - Typical pain is persistent and may radiate to arm, jaw, neck, or back.
  • Ask about breathlessness - Dyspnoea may accompany pain or be the dominant symptom in high-risk groups.
  • Ask about autonomic symptoms - Sweating, nausea, or vomiting can signal significant myocardial ischaemia.
  • Ask about presyncope or syncope - Collapse may indicate malignant arrhythmia or haemodynamic compromise.
  • Ask about atypical pain sites - Epigastric, jaw, or isolated arm discomfort can represent ACS.
ACS Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Acute Coronary Syndrome (ACS)"][/sc]
  • Check haemodynamic status - Tachycardia, hypotension, or cool peripheries suggest impaired cardiac output.
  • Check respiratory findings - Bibasal crackles or hypoxia suggest acute left ventricular failure.
  • Check rhythm abnormalities - Irregular rhythm or bradyarrhythmia may indicate conduction system involvement.
  • Check for new cardiac murmur - New systolic murmur can indicate acute mechanical complication.
ACS Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Acute Coronary Syndrome (ACS)"][/sc]
  • Persistent chest pain despite initial therapy - Suggests ongoing coronary occlusion needing urgent escalation.
  • Shock or refractory hypotension - Indicates high-risk myocardial dysfunction or mechanical complication.
  • Pulmonary oedema or severe hypoxia - Signals acute heart failure from extensive myocardial injury.
  • Sustained ventricular arrhythmia or high-grade block - Represents immediate life-threatening electrical instability.
ACS Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Acute Coronary Syndrome (ACS)"][/sc]
  • Predominant dyspnoea: Some patients present without clear chest pain and instead with unexplained acute breathlessness.
  • Non-specific systemic symptoms: Fatigue, confusion, or epigastric discomfort may be the presenting features in older adults.
ACS Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Acute Coronary Syndrome (ACS)"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Immediate serial ECGs and high-sensitivity troponin pathway with observations, bedside glucose, and focused examination for heart failure or shock.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Early echocardiography when instability or mechanical complication is suspected; coronary angiography for definitive coronary assessment and revascularisation planning; ongoing rhythm and haemodynamic monitoring.
ACS Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Acute Coronary Syndrome (ACS)"][/sc]
  • A 65-year-old man presents with 45 minutes of crushing central chest pain radiating to the left arm, diaphoresis, and dyspnoea, with dynamic ECG ischaemic changes.
  • Older adult with acute persistent ischaemic chest pain and autonomic symptoms plus objective cardiac injury markers, concerning for acute coronary syndrome.
ACS Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Acute Coronary Syndrome (ACS)"][/sc]
  • Aortic dissection - Abrupt tearing chest or back pain with pulse or blood-pressure asymmetry suggests dissection rather than ACS.
  • Pulmonary embolism - Pleuritic pain, disproportionate dyspnoea, and right-heart strain features favour pulmonary embolism.
  • Pericarditis - Pleuritic pain relieved by sitting forward with diffuse ECG changes supports pericarditis.
  • Pneumothorax - Unilateral pleuritic pain with reduced breath sounds and hyper-resonance suggests pneumothorax.
  • Gastro-oesophageal reflux disease - Burning post-prandial pain with acid reflux pattern is more consistent with oesophageal disease.
  • Musculoskeletal chest wall pain - Reproducible local tenderness and movement-related pain suggest chest wall origin.
ACS Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Acute Coronary Syndrome (ACS)"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Start antiplatelet-focused emergency pathway, control pain and oxygenation, and identify STEMI or unstable NSTEMI patterns requiring urgent reperfusion.
  • Core care: Deliver guideline-directed antithrombotic and anti-ischaemic management with coronary revascularisation strategy tailored to ACS subtype and risk profile.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Continuous rhythm and haemodynamic monitoring with repeat ECG and biomarker review to detect infarct evolution or complications.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Refractory pain, arrhythmia, heart failure, or haemodynamic collapse requires immediate senior cardiology and critical care escalation.
ACS Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Acute Coronary Syndrome (ACS)"][/sc]
  • Life-threatening arrhythmias: Ventricular tachyarrhythmia and high-grade conduction block can cause sudden deterioration.
  • Acute heart failure and cardiogenic shock: Extensive myocardial injury can produce severe pump failure and tissue hypoperfusion.
  • Mechanical complications: Papillary muscle, septal, or free-wall rupture can follow transmural infarction.
  • Recurrent ischaemia and long-term ventricular dysfunction: Ongoing coronary instability may lead to reinfarction and chronic heart failure.
