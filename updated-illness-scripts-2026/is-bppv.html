Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Benign Paroxysmal Positional Vertigo (BPPV)"][/sc]

Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Benign Paroxysmal Positional Vertigo (BPPV)"][/sc]
  • Normally, the otoliths are located in the utricle and saccule of the vestibular system, which help detect linear acceleration. In BPPV, these otoliths become dislodged and enter the semicircular canals, most commonly the posterior canal.
  • When the head moves, these free-floating particles cause abnormal stimulation of the hair cells in the semicircular canals, leading to the perception of vertigo.
  • The posterior canal is the most frequently affected due to its anatomical position, making it more prone to trap the dislodged otoliths.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Benign Paroxysmal Positional Vertigo (BPPV)"][/sc]
  • Acute phase: Episodes of vertigo typically last less than 60 seconds, triggered by head movements such as rolling over in bed, looking up, or bending down.
  • Recurrent phase: Symptoms tend to recur intermittently, often with prolonged symptom-free intervals between episodes.
  • Resolution phase: Symptoms may resolve spontaneously over days to weeks, though in some cases, they persist and require treatment.

Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Benign Paroxysmal Positional Vertigo (BPPV)"][/sc]
  • Persistent vertigo lasting hours or days - May indicate a central cause such as a vestibular migraine or stroke.
  • Focal neurological signs (e.g., limb weakness, dysarthria) - Suggests a central lesion, such as a cerebellar stroke, rather than peripheral causes like BPPV.
  • Sudden hearing loss or tinnitus - May signify Meniere's disease or a vestibular schwannoma, both of which require further investigation.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Benign Paroxysmal Positional Vertigo (BPPV)"][/sc]
  • Vertigo without head movement - Some patients report vertigo without clear triggers, leading to a delay in diagnosis.
  • Prolonged episodes - While BPPV episodes typically last seconds, prolonged symptoms may lead to confusion with other vestibular disorders.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features or comorbidity can mask the underlying diagnosis.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Benign Paroxysmal Positional Vertigo (BPPV)"][/sc]
  • Falls - Due to sudden vertigo, increasing the risk of injury, particularly in older adults.
  • Anxiety - Recurrent episodes of vertigo may lead to fear of movement and a decline in quality of life.
