Metastatic Cancer (e.g., Prostate Cancer)
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Metastatic Cancer (e.g., Prostate Cancer)"][/sc]
  • Metastatic cancer refers to cancer that has spread from its primary site to distant organs or tissues.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise metastatic features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.

Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Metastatic Cancer (e.g., Prostate Cancer)"][/sc]
  • Prostate cancer cells acquire mutations enabling them to detach from the primary tumour.
  • Cells invade local tissues and blood/lymphatic vessels via epithelial-to-mesenchymal transition.
  • Once in circulation, cells evade immune surveillance and adhere to endothelial cells at distant sites.
  • Prostate cancer has a tropism for bone, where metastatic cells disrupt normal bone remodelling, causing osteoblastic lesions.
  • Other mechanisms include angiogenesis and the alteration of local tissue environments to support tumour growth.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Metastatic Cancer (e.g., Prostate Cancer)"][/sc]
  • Early stage: Often asymptomatic or mild symptoms confined to local disease.
  • Progression: Symptoms of bone pain, systemic effects, or organ-specific dysfunction emerge as metastases develop.
  • Advanced stage: Debilitating symptoms, significant organ dysfunction, and complications such as pathological fractures or spinal cord compression.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Metastatic Cancer (e.g., Prostate Cancer)"][/sc]
  • Ask about bone pain - Commonly in the back, pelvis, or hips due to osteoblastic activity in bone metastases disrupting normal bone architecture.
  • Ask about fatigue - Often a result of anaemia or systemic inflammation caused by tumour activity.
  • Ask about weight loss - Associated with cancer cachexia, driven by systemic metabolic derangements and tumour burden.
  • Ask about haematuria - May occur if the urinary system is affected by local tumour invasion.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Metastatic Cancer (e.g., Prostate Cancer)"][/sc]
  • Check pallor - Indicative of anaemia due to chronic disease or bone marrow infiltration.
  • Check pathological fractures - Suggestive of weakened bone from metastases disrupting normal bone integrity.
  • Check lymphadenopathy - Enlarged nodes often reflect metastatic spread to pelvic or retroperitoneal lymph nodes.
  • Check spinal cord compression signs - Neurological deficits such as weakness or incontinence due to epidural metastases.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Metastatic Cancer (e.g., Prostate Cancer)"][/sc]
  • Severe bone pain with neurological symptoms - May signify spinal cord compression, requiring urgent intervention to prevent paralysis.
  • Hypercalcaemia - Symptoms such as confusion, polyuria, and constipation indicate significant skeletal involvement and require immediate correction.
  • Significant weight loss - Suggestive of advanced disease or systemic cachexia.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Metastatic Cancer (e.g., Prostate Cancer)"][/sc]
  • Isolated lymphadenopathy - May be misdiagnosed as lymphoma in the absence of bone symptoms.
  • Visceral metastases - Rarely, patients may present with symptoms of liver or lung involvement without bone lesions.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask metastatic cancer (e.g., prostate cancer).
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Metastatic Cancer (e.g., Prostate Cancer)"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Prostate-specific antigen (PSA) - Elevated in metastatic prostate cancer; levels above 100 ng/mL are often suggestive of advanced disease; Bone scintigraphy.
  • Targeted and monitoring: MRI - Essential for identifying spinal cord compression or pelvic disease; CT scan - Useful for detecting visceral metastases.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Metastatic Cancer (e.g., Prostate Cancer)"][/sc]
  • Multiple myeloma - Differentiated by lytic bone lesions, hypercalcaemia, and monoclonal protein on serum electrophoresis.
  • Benign prostatic hyperplasia - Lacks systemic symptoms and metastases; PSA levels are typically modestly elevated.
  • Renal cell carcinoma - Distinguish from metastatic cancer (e.g., prostate cancer) using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Metastatic Cancer (e.g., Prostate Cancer)"][/sc]
  • Pathological fractures - Common due to weakened bones from metastases.
  • Spinal cord compression - Can cause paralysis if untreated.
  • Renal failure - Secondary to obstructive uropathy or hypercalcaemia.
