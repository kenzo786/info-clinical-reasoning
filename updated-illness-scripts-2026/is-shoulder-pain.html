Shoulder Pain
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Shoulder Pain"][/sc]
  • Shoulder pain refers to discomfort or aching in the shoulder joint or surrounding structures, including muscles, tendons, ligaments, and bursae.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise shoulder features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.

Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Shoulder Pain"][/sc]
  • Shoulder pain arises from local inflammation, mechanical stress, degenerative changes, or referred pain from other structures (e.g., the neck).
  • Common mechanisms include rotator cuff tendinopathy (overuse leading to microtrauma and inflammation), impingement syndrome (compression of tendons in the subacromial space), and adhesive capsulitis (fibrotic changes in the joint capsule).
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Shoulder Pain"][/sc]
  • Acute phase: Sudden onset of pain, often following trauma or overuse.
  • Subacute phase: Gradual reduction in acute inflammation but persistent discomfort or stiffness.
  • Chronic phase: Lasting pain and functional limitation, potentially accompanied by compensatory biomechanical changes.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Shoulder Pain"][/sc]
  • Ask about pain with movement - Indicates inflammation or mechanical impingement; commonly due to rotator cuff injuries.
  • Ask about stiffness - Often linked to adhesive capsulitis, caused by fibrosis limiting range of motion.
  • Ask about night pain - Suggests conditions like rotator cuff tears or bursitis due to positional exacerbation.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Shoulder Pain"][/sc]
  • Check reduced range of motion - Indicates structural abnormalities such as adhesive capsulitis or impingement.
  • Check tenderness on palpation - Suggests localised inflammation or trauma, e.g., in rotator cuff tendinopathy.

Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Shoulder Pain"][/sc]
  • Severe pain at rest - May indicate infection or malignancy.
  • Neurological deficits - Suggests nerve root compression or brachial plexopathy.
  • Systemic symptoms (fever, weight loss) - May indicate septic arthritis or metastatic disease.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Shoulder Pain"][/sc]
  • Referred pain from cervical spine disorders or myocardial ischaemia may present as isolated shoulder pain without clear musculoskeletal findings.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask shoulder pain.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Shoulder Pain"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: X-ray - May show osteoarthritis, fractures, or calcific tendinopathy.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Ultrasound - Useful for detecting rotator cuff tears or bursitis; MRI - Gold standard for soft tissue injuries and labral tears.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Shoulder Pain"][/sc]
  • Rotator cuff tendinopathy - Pain localised to lateral shoulder, aggravated by lifting.
  • Adhesive capsulitis - Global stiffness, gradual onset.
  • Glenohumeral arthritis - Crepitus, constant deep pain.
  • Related alternative diagnosis - Distinguish from shoulder pain using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Shoulder Pain"][/sc]

  • Secondary osteoarthritis due to altered biomechanics.
