Prepatellar Bursitis
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Prepatellar Bursitis"][/sc]
  • Prepatellar bursitis, also known as "housemaid's knee," is inflammation of the prepatellar bursa, a small fluid-filled sac located anterior to the patella.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise prepatellar features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.

  • Higher-risk or missed groups: Prepatellar Bursitis may be missed when presentation is atypical or obscured by comorbidity.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Prepatellar Bursitis"][/sc]
  • Chronic mechanical irritation from repetitive kneeling leads to bursal thickening and synovial fluid overproduction.
  • Trauma can cause haemorrhagic fluid accumulation, promoting an inflammatory response.
  • Infectious prepatellar bursitis often results from bacterial inoculation, typically Staphylococcus aureus, through minor skin breaches.
  • Crystals (e.g., urate in gout) can precipitate inflammatory bursitis by direct irritation and immune activation.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Prepatellar Bursitis"][/sc]
  • Acute phase: Rapid onset of pain and swelling, often associated with infection or trauma.
  • Chronic phase: Gradual development of swelling and discomfort, typically with mechanical irritation.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Prepatellar Bursitis"][/sc]
  • Ask about swelling - Prominent anterior knee swelling caused by fluid accumulation in the prepatellar bursa.
  • Ask about pain - Localised pain, exacerbated by kneeling or pressure on the knee due to inflammation and increased tissue tension.
  • Ask about reduced mobility - Difficulty bending the knee due to pain and swelling restricting movement.
  • Ask about warmth/redness (in infectious bursitis) - Localised inflammatory response or systemic infection.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Prepatellar Bursitis"][/sc]
  • Check palpable swelling - Soft, fluctuant swelling over the patella, indicative of fluid accumulation.
  • Check erythema and warmth - Suggestive of inflammation or infection.
  • Check tenderness - Pain upon palpation due to inflammation.
  • Check limited flexion - Pain-induced movement restriction.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Prepatellar Bursitis"][/sc]
  • Severe redness and warmth with fever - Suggestive of septic bursitis, requiring urgent treatment to prevent systemic spread.
  • Purulent aspirate - Indicates infection; a potentially serious condition if untreated.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Prepatellar Bursitis"][/sc]
  • Asymptomatic swelling noted incidentally during routine physical examination, particularly in chronic cases.
  • Non-localised pain misinterpreted as other knee pathologies such as osteoarthritis.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask prepatellar bursitis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Prepatellar Bursitis"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Aspiration and analysis of bursal fluid - Purulent fluid with high WBC count indicates infection; crystals suggest gout.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Ultrasound - Identifies fluid collection and helps differentiate bursitis from other soft tissue pathologies; Blood tests - Elevated inflammatory markers (CRP.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Prepatellar Bursitis"][/sc]
  • Septic arthritis - Typically involves the joint itself; often with systemic symptoms such as fever and severe.
  • Gout - May present similarly but often involves other joints, with monosodium urate crystals in synovial.
  • Cellulitis - Diffuse soft tissue infection, not limited to the bursa, with widespread erythema.
  • Related alternative diagnosis - Distinguish from prepatellar bursitis using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Prepatellar Bursitis"][/sc]
  • Chronic pain and swelling - Persistent symptoms from unresolved inflammation or repeated trauma.
  • Sepsis - In untreated septic bursitis, bacteria may spread systemically, leading to life-threatening conditions.
