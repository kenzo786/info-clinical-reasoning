Benign Prostatic Hypertrophy (BPH)
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Benign Prostatic Hypertrophy (BPH)"][/sc]
  • Benign prostatic hypertrophy (BPH) is a non-malignant enlargement of the prostate gland, commonly associated with ageing.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise benign features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.

  • Higher-risk or missed groups: Benign Prostatic Hypertrophy (BPH) may be missed when presentations are atypical or comorbidity obscures key features.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Benign Prostatic Hypertrophy (BPH)"][/sc]
  • Hyperplasia of prostate glandular and stromal tissue, driven by dihydrotestosterone (DHT), a potent androgen derived from testosterone.
  • Compression of the urethra leads to obstruction, increasing bladder outlet resistance.
  • Compensatory detrusor muscle hypertrophy in the bladder to overcome obstruction, eventually leading to bladder dysfunction.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Benign Prostatic Hypertrophy (BPH)"][/sc]
  • Slow, progressive condition over decades, often asymptomatic initially.
  • Early phase: Mild LUTS due to partial urethral obstruction.
  • Advanced phase: Significant LUTS with potential bladder decompensation and complications like retention or infections.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Benign Prostatic Hypertrophy (BPH)"][/sc]
  • Ask about urinary frequency - Due to reduced bladder capacity and incomplete emptying.
  • Ask about urgency - Resulting from detrusor overactivity caused by obstruction.
  • Ask about nocturia - Increased night-time voiding due to incomplete bladder emptying and reduced nocturnal bladder capacity.
  • Ask about weak urine stream - Caused by increased urethral resistance.
  • Ask about hesitancy - Difficulty initiating urination due to obstruction.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Benign Prostatic Hypertrophy (BPH)"][/sc]
  • Check palpable enlarged prostate - Detected on digital rectal examination (DRE), reflects glandular hyperplasia.
  • Check bladder distension - Observed in severe cases with urinary retention.
  • Check reduced urine flow rate - Objectively measured during uroflowmetry.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Benign Prostatic Hypertrophy (BPH)"][/sc]
  • Haematuria - May indicate bladder cancer or stones.
  • Acute urinary retention - Suggests severe obstruction requiring immediate intervention.
  • Recurrent UTIs - Indicates stagnant urine and potential bladder dysfunction.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Benign Prostatic Hypertrophy (BPH)"][/sc]
  • Asymptomatic - Detected incidentally during evaluation for other conditions.
  • Isolated nocturia - Misattributed to ageing or nocturnal polyuria.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features or comorbidity can mask the underlying diagnosis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Benign Prostatic Hypertrophy (BPH)"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: PSA test - Elevated in BPH but not diagnostic; helps rule out prostate cancer; Urine dipstick - Excludes infection or haematuria.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Uroflowmetry - Shows reduced peak flow rate (<15 mL/s); Post-void residual volume - Elevated in incomplete bladder emptying.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Benign Prostatic Hypertrophy (BPH)"][/sc]
  • A 65-year-old male presents with a 2-year history of urinary frequency, nocturia, and weak stream, gradually worsening, with no systemic symptoms.
  • Adult with symptom pattern and objective findings suggestive of benign prostatic hypertrophy (bph), requiring focused confirmation and risk stratification.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Benign Prostatic Hypertrophy (BPH)"][/sc]
  • Prostate cancer - Distinguished by elevated PSA, firm prostate nodules on DRE, or imaging findings.
  • Bladder stones - Associated with haematuria and pain.
  • Overactive bladder syndrome - Predominantly urgency without obstruction signs.
  • Alternative serious diagnosis - Distinguishing history, examination, and targeted investigations do not support benign prostatic hypertrophy (bph).
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Benign Prostatic Hypertrophy (BPH)"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Assess physiological stability and urgent complications at presentation, and initiate early stabilisation where required.
  • Core care: Deliver condition-specific treatment for benign prostatic hypertrophy (bph), address underlying drivers, and involve appropriate specialist support.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Reassess symptom trajectory, objective markers, and treatment response over time to detect complications early.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Worsening symptoms, physiological deterioration, or failure to improve should trigger urgent reassessment for severe or alternative pathology in benign prostatic hypertrophy (bph).
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Benign Prostatic Hypertrophy (BPH)"][/sc]
  • Acute urinary retention - Painful inability to void; can lead to infection or hydronephrosis.
  • Recurrent UTIs - Due to incomplete bladder emptying.
  • Bladder stones - Result from chronic urinary stasis.
