Medication-Induced Nausea and Vomiting
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Medication-Induced Nausea and Vomiting"][/sc]
  • Medication-induced nausea and vomiting (MINV) refers to the nausea, vomiting, or both that occur as a side effect of taking certain medications.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise medication features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.

Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Medication-Induced Nausea and Vomiting"][/sc]
  • Step 1: Certain medications activate the chemoreceptor trigger zone (CTZ) in the area postrema, a structure that is sensitive to emetic stimuli.
  • Step 2: Direct irritation of the gastrointestinal mucosa by medications (e.g., NSAIDs, antibiotics) can lead to local inflammatory reactions, stimulating vagal afferents and triggering the vomiting centre.
  • Step 3: Other drugs (e.g., opioids) slow gastric emptying, leading to increased gastric distension, which also stimulates nausea pathways via the vagus nerve.
  • Step 4: In some cases, medications (e.g., chemotherapy, antibiotics) disrupt the balance of the inner ear, causing vestibular dysfunction and subsequent nausea and vomiting.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Medication-Induced Nausea and Vomiting"][/sc]
  • Acute phase: Symptoms usually occur within hours to days after starting the medication, especially with agents that act quickly (e.g., opioids, chemotherapy).
  • Chronic phase: Symptoms can persist with long-term medication use, especially with drugs like opioids and some antibiotics, as tolerance or adaptation may not develop.
  • Resolution phase: Symptoms typically resolve within a few days to weeks after discontinuation or dose adjustment of the offending medication.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Medication-Induced Nausea and Vomiting"][/sc]
  • Ask about nausea - A sensation of queasiness often felt in the upper stomach, primarily caused by activation of the CTZ and vagal afferents in response.
  • Ask about vomiting - The physical expulsion of stomach contents, mediated by the vomiting centre in the brainstem after stimulation by the CTZ or vagal afferents.
  • Ask about anorexia - Loss of appetite often accompanies nausea and vomiting due to the gastrointestinal discomfort and vagal stimulation.
  • Ask about dizziness - Certain medications, especially those affecting the vestibular system, can cause dizziness, which is closely linked to nausea.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Medication-Induced Nausea and Vomiting"][/sc]
  • Check dehydration (dry mucous membranes, tachycardia) - Repeated vomiting can lead to significant fluid loss, causing these clinical signs.
  • Check hypotension - Volume depletion from fluid loss due to vomiting may result in low blood pressure.
  • Check electrolyte imbalance (e.g., hyponatraemia, hypokalaemia) - Vomiting leads to the loss of electrolytes, particularly potassium and sodium, contributing to these abnormalities.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Medication-Induced Nausea and Vomiting"][/sc]
  • Severe dehydration (sunken eyes, lethargy) - Signifies significant volume depletion, which can lead to acute kidney injury or shock if untreated.
  • Persistent vomiting (>24 hours) - May suggest severe toxicity or drug overdose, potentially requiring urgent intervention.
  • Haematemesis - Vomiting of blood may indicate gastrointestinal mucosal injury (e.g., erosions, ulcers) from medications like NSAIDs.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Medication-Induced Nausea and Vomiting"][/sc]
  • Mild, intermittent nausea without vomiting - In some cases, especially with drugs like selective serotonin reuptake inhibitors (SSRIs), only mild nausea may be present without vomiting, making diagnosis more difficult.
  • Delayed onset nausea/vomiting - In patients taking certain medications (e.g., chemotherapy), nausea and vomiting may occur days after the drug is administered.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask medication-induced nausea and vomiting.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Medication-Induced Nausea and Vomiting"][/sc]
  • Gastroenteritis - Distinguished by accompanying fever and diarrhoea, which are absent in MINV.
  • Vestibular neuronitis - Distinguish from medication-induced nausea and vomiting using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.
  • Diabetic gastroparesis - Often associated with longstanding diabetes, characterised by delayed gastric emptying similar to some medication.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Medication-Induced Nausea and Vomiting"][/sc]
  • Electrolyte disturbances - Frequent vomiting can cause life-threatening imbalances such as hypokalaemia, which can lead to cardiac arrhythmias.
  • Mallory-Weiss tears - Severe or forceful vomiting can cause tears in the oesophagus, leading to haematemesis.
  • Acute kidney injury - Secondary to dehydration, particularly in elderly or frail patients.
