Mastitis
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Mastitis"][/sc]
  • Mastitis is an inflammation of breast tissue, often accompanied by infection.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise mastitis features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Mastitis"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Mastitis"][/sc]
  • Risk factors: Risk factors for mastitis include relevant predisposition, comorbidity, and exposure history.
  • Higher-risk or missed groups: Mastitis may be missed when presentation is atypical or obscured by comorbidity.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Mastitis"][/sc]
  • Mastitis occurs when milk stasis (blocked milk flow) in the ducts creates an environment conducive to infection. Milk stasis is often due to improper breastfeeding technique or infrequent feeding.
  • Milk stasis leads to local inflammation, and bacteria (often Staphylococcus aureus) from the infant’s mouth or skin can enter through cracked nipples, leading to bacterial infection.
  • This process triggers an immune response in the breast tissue, causing inflammation, pain, and systemic symptoms if infection spreads.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Mastitis"][/sc]
  • Early Phase: Begins with mild breast tenderness and swelling, often within the first few weeks of breastfeeding.
  • Progressive Phase: Without intervention, symptoms escalate to include redness, increased warmth, pain, and fever within 24-48 hours.
  • Late/Complicated Phase: Abscess formation may occur after several days if untreated, potentially requiring surgical intervention.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Mastitis"][/sc]
  • Ask about breast pain - Due to inflammation of the breast tissue as the immune system responds to the infection.
  • Ask about breast erythema - The result of increased blood flow and local inflammatory response.
  • Ask about swelling - Caused by fluid accumulation in response to inflammation.
  • Ask about fever and chills - Systemic response to bacterial infection, with fever due to cytokine release.
  • Ask about fatigue - Often accompanies infection as the body directs energy towards the immune response.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Mastitis"][/sc]
  • Check breast warmth - Caused by increased local blood flow during the inflammatory response.
  • Check localized erythema - Reflects the area of inflammation within the breast tissue.
  • Check tenderness to palpation - Due to inflamed and sensitised breast tissue.
  • Check fever (>38°C) - Indicates systemic infection if present, common in bacterial mastitis.
  • Check firm, indurated area - A result of localised infection and inflammatory response; may progress to abscess.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Mastitis"][/sc]
  • Unilateral swelling with rapid progression - May indicate abscess formation, a potentially serious complication requiring drainage.
  • Persistent fever despite antibiotics - Suggests resistant bacteria or underlying abscess, needing further evaluation.
  • Nipple retraction or skin dimpling - Rare but can be an indication of inflammatory breast cancer in non-lactating women.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Mastitis"][/sc]
  • Non-lactational mastitis - Occurs in non-breastfeeding individuals, often linked with duct ectasia or smoking; presents with similar signs and symptoms but can be recurrent.
  • Subacute mastitis - Presents with less severe symptoms without fever, often confused with blocked ducts, especially in lactating women.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask mastitis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Mastitis"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Clinical examination - Key for diagnosis, based on characteristic signs and symptoms.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Breast ultrasound - Used if an abscess is suspected; shows fluid collection in abscess cases; Milk culture - Performed in recurrent.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Mastitis"][/sc]
  • Blocked milk duct - Localised breast tenderness without fever or erythema; often resolves with breastfeeding.
  • Breast abscess - Presents with a palpable, fluctuant mass and severe pain, typically following untreated mastitis.
  • Inflammatory breast cancer - Rare; associated with skin dimpling, nipple inversion, and rapid onset in non-lactating women.
  • Related alternative diagnosis - Distinguish from mastitis using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Mastitis"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Assess severity, physiological stability, and immediate complications.
  • Core care: Deliver condition-specific management for mastitis, treat underlying drivers, and coordinate appropriate specialist input.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Reassess symptom trajectory, examination findings, and objective markers to confirm response in mastitis.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Escalate urgently for clinical deterioration, evolving red flags, or failure to improve in suspected mastitis.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Mastitis"][/sc]
  • Breast abscess - Occurs in untreated cases; can cause persistent infection and tissue necrosis.
  • Chronic mastitis - Recurrent inflammation with ductal scarring, more common in non-lactational cases.
  • Sepsis - Rare; may develop in cases of untreated, severe infection, particularly in immunocompromised individuals.
