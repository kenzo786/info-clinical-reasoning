Medication-Induced Weight Loss
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Medication-Induced Weight Loss"][/sc]
  • Medication-induced weight loss refers to unintentional or excessive loss of body weight due to the effects of prescribed or over-the-counter drugs.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise medication features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.

Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Medication-Induced Weight Loss"][/sc]
  • Appetite suppression: Medications like stimulants and certain antidepressants (e.g., SSRIs) reduce appetite by altering neurotransmitter levels (e.g., serotonin, dopamine).
  • Increased metabolism: Some drugs (e.g., thyroid hormone replacements) increase metabolic rate, leading to higher energy expenditure and weight loss.
  • Gastrointestinal effects: Drugs like metformin or GLP-1 receptor agonists cause nausea, vomiting, or diarrhoea, reducing caloric intake and nutrient absorption.
  • Malabsorption: Chemotherapy and other agents affecting the gastrointestinal lining impair nutrient absorption, leading to weight loss.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Medication-Induced Weight Loss"][/sc]
  • Acute phase: Rapid onset of weight loss can occur with chemotherapy or high-dose stimulant medications within weeks of initiation.
  • Chronic phase: Gradual weight loss may develop over months with long-term use of appetite-suppressing or metabolism-enhancing drugs like GLP-1 receptor agonists or antidepressants.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Medication-Induced Weight Loss"][/sc]
  • Ask about weight loss - The most prominent symptom, typically a gradual or sometimes rapid reduction in body weight. Caused by appetite suppression, increased metabolism, with associated clinical features.
  • Ask about loss of appetite - Frequently seen with drugs that affect neurotransmitter levels (e.g., SSRIs, stimulants). Reduced hunger signalling in the hypothalamus is a major.
  • Ask about fatigue - Occurs due to reduced caloric intake or nutrient deficiency, especially with drugs that cause gastrointestinal upset (e.g., GLP-1 receptor agonists).
  • Ask about gastrointestinal discomfort - Nausea, vomiting, or diarrhoea associated with drugs like metformin or chemotherapy agents, contributing to decreased food intake.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Medication-Induced Weight Loss"][/sc]
  • Check visible weight loss - Loss of body fat or muscle mass, noticeable over time.
  • Check muscle wasting - In chronic cases or with chemotherapy, there may be muscle atrophy due to nutrient deficiencies and catabolism.
  • Check pallor or frailty - May indicate malnutrition or underlying metabolic imbalance, especially in cases involving nutrient malabsorption.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Medication-Induced Weight Loss"][/sc]
  • Unintentional rapid weight loss - Can signify severe malnutrition, malignancy, or a severe adverse reaction to medication.
  • Severe gastrointestinal symptoms - Persistent nausea, vomiting, or diarrhoea may indicate serious drug toxicity or malabsorption, leading to dehydration and electrolyte imbalance.
  • Muscle wasting - Suggests prolonged catabolism or malnutrition, raising concerns for cachexia, especially in cancer patients.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Medication-Induced Weight Loss"][/sc]
  • Weight loss without noticeable appetite reduction - Can occur with metabolism-boosting drugs like thyroid hormones.
  • Minimal gastrointestinal symptoms - Some patients on GLP-1 receptor agonists may not experience the expected nausea but still lose weight.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask medication-induced weight loss.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Medication-Induced Weight Loss"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Focused bedside assessment and first-line testing to assess severity and immediate risk in suspected medication-induced weight loss.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Thyroid function tests (TFTs) - To rule out hyperthyroidism as a cause of increased metabolism and weight loss; Liver function tests.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Medication-Induced Weight Loss"][/sc]
  • Hyperthyroidism - Differentiated by symptoms such as heat intolerance, tremors, and palpitations, along with elevated thyroid.
  • Malignancy - More likely with constitutional symptoms like night sweats, fever, and persistent fatigue.
  • Chronic infection (e.g., tuberculosis) - Weight loss accompanied by fever, night sweats, and cough.
  • Related alternative diagnosis - Distinguish from medication-induced weight loss using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Medication-Induced Weight Loss"][/sc]
  • Malnutrition - Prolonged weight loss may lead to deficiencies in vital nutrients, affecting overall health.
  • Dehydration - Especially in patients with significant gastrointestinal side effects.
  • Muscle wasting and weakness - Occurs due to prolonged caloric and protein deficiency.
