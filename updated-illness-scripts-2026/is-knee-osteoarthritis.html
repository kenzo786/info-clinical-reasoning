Knee Osteoarthritis
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Knee Osteoarthritis"][/sc]
  • Knee osteoarthritis (OA) is a chronic degenerative joint disease characterised by the progressive breakdown of articular cartilage, subchondral bone remodelling, and secondary inflammation of the synovium.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise knee features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Knee Osteoarthritis"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Knee Osteoarthritis"][/sc]
  • In UK hospitals, it is one of the leading causes of elective joint replacement surgeries.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Knee Osteoarthritis"][/sc]
  • Initial damage to articular cartilage leads to increased water content and collagen disruption.
  • Loss of cartilage results in bone-on-bone contact, causing subchondral bone sclerosis and osteophyte formation.
  • Synovial inflammation contributes to pain and stiffness due to cytokine release.
  • Progressive structural changes culminate in joint deformity and reduced range of motion.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Knee Osteoarthritis"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Intermittent pain and mild stiffness, often activity-related.
  • Progressive phase: Chronic pain, increased stiffness, reduced joint mobility.
  • Advanced phase: Constant pain, significant deformity, and severe functional limitations.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Knee Osteoarthritis"][/sc]
  • Ask about joint pain - Typically worsens with activity and relieved by rest; caused by cartilage loss and bone friction.
  • Ask about morning stiffness - Lasts <30 minutes, due to synovial fluid stasis overnight.
  • Ask about reduced range of motion - Stiffening of the joint from structural changes and inflammation.
  • Ask about swelling - Mild to moderate, due to synovitis or joint effusion.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Knee Osteoarthritis"][/sc]
  • Check crepitus - Audible or palpable grinding during joint movement, due to cartilage wear.
  • Check bony enlargement - From osteophyte formation and joint remodelling.
  • Check tenderness - Localised to the joint margin from inflammation or subchondral bone exposure.
  • Check limited range of motion - Due to structural damage and pain.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Knee Osteoarthritis"][/sc]
  • Rapidly progressive pain - Suggests secondary causes like avascular necrosis or infection.
  • Severe swelling and erythema - May indicate septic arthritis or crystalline arthropathy.
  • Systemic symptoms - Weight loss, fever, or night sweats could suggest malignancy or infection.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Knee Osteoarthritis"][/sc]
  • Isolated effusion without pain - Can occur early in disease with synovial inflammation.
  • Referred hip or lower back pain - Confusing diagnosis, as these areas may compensate for knee dysfunction.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask knee osteoarthritis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Knee Osteoarthritis"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: X-ray - Shows joint space narrowing, osteophytes, subchondral sclerosis, and bone cysts.
  • Targeted and monitoring: MRI - Used for soft tissue and early cartilage changes, but rarely necessary for diagnosis; Joint aspiration - To exclude gout.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Knee Osteoarthritis"][/sc]
  • Rheumatoid arthritis - Bilateral joint involvement, systemic symptoms, prolonged morning stiffness.
  • Gout - Acute onset, severe pain, erythema, and urate crystals in synovial fluid.
  • Meniscal tear - History of trauma, locking or clicking sensation, localised tenderness.
  • Related alternative diagnosis - Distinguish from knee osteoarthritis using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Knee Osteoarthritis"][/sc]
  • Chronic pain: Can lead to depression and decreased quality of life.
  • Joint deformity: Advanced disease may cause varus or valgus deformities.
  • Reduced mobility: Associated with increased fall risk and sedentary lifestyle-related complications.
