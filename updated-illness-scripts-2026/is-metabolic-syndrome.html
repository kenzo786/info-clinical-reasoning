Metabolic Syndrome
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Metabolic Syndrome"][/sc]
  • Metabolic Syndrome is a cluster of interrelated cardiovascular and metabolic risk factors, including central obesity, insulin resistance, dyslipidaemia (elevated triglycerides and reduced HDL), and hypertension.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise metabolic features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.

  • Higher-risk or missed groups: Metabolic Syndrome may be missed when presentation is atypical or obscured by comorbidity.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Metabolic Syndrome"][/sc]
  • Metabolic Syndrome is primarily driven by central obesity and insulin resistance. Adipose tissue, especially visceral fat, secretes pro-inflammatory cytokines like TNF-α and IL-6, leading to chronic low-grade inflammation. Insulin resistance develops as a result, reducing glucose uptake in peripheral tissues, causing hyperglycaemia. The liver responds by increasing lipogenesis, resulting in hypertriglyceridaemia. Insulin resistance also contributes to hypertension by activating the sympathetic nervous system and increasing renal sodium retention.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Metabolic Syndrome"][/sc]
  • Early stage: Insulin resistance and central obesity develop gradually, often asymptomatic.
  • Progression: Over time, dyslipidaemia, hypertension, and impaired glucose tolerance may manifest.
  • Late stage: Uncontrolled progression can lead to type 2 diabetes, cardiovascular events (e.g., myocardial infarction, stroke), and non-alcoholic fatty liver disease (NAFLD).
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Metabolic Syndrome"][/sc]
  • Ask about asymptomatic (common) - Many patients present with no symptoms until complications arise.
  • Ask about fatigue - Results from insulin resistance leading to impaired glucose utilisation in tissues.
  • Ask about polyuria and Polydipsia - Indicate advancing insulin resistance or type 2 diabetes, due to osmotic diuresis from hyperglycaemia.
  • Ask about weight gain (central obesity) - Caused by increased visceral adiposity and dysregulated lipid metabolism.
  • Ask about shortness of breath on exertion - May suggest underlying cardiovascular disease related to metabolic syndrome.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Metabolic Syndrome"][/sc]
  • Check central obesity (waist circumference > 94 cm in men, > 80 cm in women) - Indicates visceral fat accumulation, which is a key driver.
  • Check hypertension (blood pressure > 130/85 mmHg) - Common due to insulin resistance, which increases vascular tone.
  • Check acanthosis nigricans - Hyperpigmented, velvety skin often found in skin folds, associated with insulin resistance.
  • Check hypertriglyceridaemia (> 1.7 mmol/L) - Reflects dyslipidaemia caused by insulin resistance and increased liver lipogenesis.
  • Check reduced HDL cholesterol (< 1.0 mmol/L in men, < 1.3 mmol/L in women) - Another component of dyslipidaemia that increases cardiovascular risk.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Metabolic Syndrome"][/sc]
  • Uncontrolled hypertension (> 180/110 mmHg) - Signifies a high risk of stroke or other cardiovascular events.
  • Chest pain or angina - May indicate underlying ischaemic heart disease, requiring urgent investigation.
  • Sudden vision loss or weakness - Suggests potential cerebrovascular events (stroke) associated with uncontrolled metabolic syndrome.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Metabolic Syndrome"][/sc]
  • Normal weight but metabolically unhealthy ("TOFI" - Thin Outside, Fat Inside) - Some patients may have a normal BMI but exhibit central obesity and metabolic disturbances.
  • Peripheral neuropathy - Can present without frank diabetes, due to long-standing insulin resistance affecting nerve function.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask metabolic syndrome.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Metabolic Syndrome"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Fasting plasma glucose > 5.6 mmol/L - Indicates impaired glucose tolerance or diabetes; Triglycerides > 1.7 mmol/L - High levels are.
  • Targeted and monitoring: HDL cholesterol < 1.0 mmol/L (men), < 1.3 mmol/L (women) - Low levels increase cardiovascular risk; Oral glucose tolerance test (OGTT).

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Metabolic Syndrome"][/sc]
  • Type 2 Diabetes Mellitus - May be differentiated by more prominent hyperglycaemia and a confirmed diagnosis of diabetes.
  • Polycystic Ovary Syndrome (PCOS) - In women, can present similarly but with menstrual irregularities and androgen excess.
  • Hypothyroidism - May cause similar weight gain and lipid abnormalities but usually presents with fatigue, cold.
  • Related alternative diagnosis - Distinguish from metabolic syndrome using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Metabolic Syndrome"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Assess severity, physiological stability, and immediate complications.
  • Core care: Deliver condition-specific management for metabolic syndrome, treat underlying drivers, and coordinate appropriate specialist input.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Reassess symptom trajectory, examination findings, and objective markers to confirm response in metabolic syndrome.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Escalate urgently for clinical deterioration, evolving red flags, or failure to improve in suspected metabolic syndrome.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Metabolic Syndrome"][/sc]
  • Type 2 Diabetes Mellitus - A major complication, affecting 10-20% of patients with metabolic syndrome over time.
  • Cardiovascular disease - Leading cause of morbidity and mortality; patients have a significantly increased risk of myocardial infarction and stroke.
  • Non-alcoholic fatty liver disease (NAFLD) - Common in patients with metabolic syndrome, with the potential to progress to cirrhosis.
