Pulmonary Embolism (PE)
Pulmonary Embolism Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Pulmonary Embolism (PE)"][/sc]
  • Pulmonary Embolism (PE) is the blockage of one or more pulmonary arteries by a thrombus (blood clot) that has usually travelled from a deep vein in the legs or pelvis.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise pulmonary features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Pulmonary Embolism (PE)"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Pulmonary Embolism (PE)"][/sc]
  • Pulmonary Embolism Risk Factors: Recent surgery, prolonged immobility (e.g., long-haul travel, hospitalisation), malignancy, pregnancy, oestrogen use (oral contraceptives, HRT), thrombophilia (e.g., Factor V Leiden mutation), smoking, and obesity increase the risk of venous thromboembolism.
  • Prevention: Early mobilisation, compression stockings, and pharmacological thromboprophylaxis (low molecular weight heparin, DOACs) in high-risk patients reduce PE risk. Lifestyle measures such as weight management, smoking cessation, and regular movement during travel also help prevent PE.
Pulmonary Embolism Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Pulmonary Embolism (PE)"][/sc]
  • PE usually originates from a thrombus in the deep veins of the lower extremities or pelvis. Upon detachment, the thrombus embolises, travelling through the venous system to the right side of the heart and eventually becoming lodged in a pulmonary artery.
  • The obstruction leads to increased pulmonary vascular resistance, [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Right ventricular strain: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="right ventricular strain"][/sc], and impaired gas exchange. The extent of the impact depends on the size of the embolus and the affected pulmonary artery branches.
  • Hypoxaemia results from impaired perfusion in ventilated lung regions ([sc name="jitl-query" Q="Ventilation-perfusion mismatch: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="ventilation-perfusion mismatch"][/sc]), while right heart strain can lead to heart failure if untreated.
Pulmonary Embolism Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Pulmonary Embolism (PE)"][/sc]
  • PE can present acutely, typically over hours to days, with sudden onset symptoms such as shortness of breath and chest pain.
  • In subacute or chronic PE, symptoms may develop more gradually over weeks to months, often in cases of recurrent small emboli leading to progressive pulmonary hypertension.
Pulmonary Embolism Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Pulmonary Embolism (PE)"][/sc]
  • Ask about dyspnoea (shortness of breath) - Occurs due to impaired gas exchange and reduced oxygenation. It is the most common symptom, present in over 80%.
  • Ask about pleuritic chest pain - Caused by inflammation of the pleura due to ischaemia of the lung parenchyma. This pain is sharp and worsens, with associated clinical features.
  • Ask about haemoptysis - May occur if the infarcted lung tissue causes capillary leakage into the alveoli, although it is less common.
  • Ask about syncope - Sudden loss of consciousness may result from reduced cardiac output due to right heart strain or a massive PE that compromises circulation.
  • Ask about calf or thigh pain/swelling - This suggests DVT as the source of the embolism.
Pulmonary Embolism Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Pulmonary Embolism (PE)"][/sc]
  • Check tachypnoea (rapid breathing) - A compensatory mechanism to improve oxygenation due to hypoxaemia.
  • Check tachycardia - Results from sympathetic activation due to hypoxia and right ventricular strain.
  • Check hypoxia (low oxygen saturation) - Reflects the impaired oxygen exchange in the lungs.
  • Check jugular venous distension - Indicates increased pressure in the right side of the heart due to strain or failure.
  • Check low-grade fever - Can occur in response to inflammation from lung tissue ischaemia.
Pulmonary Embolism Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Pulmonary Embolism (PE)"][/sc]
  • Massive PE (hypotension, shock, or cardiac arrest) - Suggests large central emboli obstructing a significant portion of the pulmonary vasculature, causing cardiovascular collapse.
  • Syncope or sudden death - Can occur if PE causes immediate and profound cardiac failure or arrhythmias due to hypoxia.
  • Signs of right heart failure - Peripheral oedema, ascites, or hepatomegaly indicate advanced right ventricular dysfunction, a severe consequence of untreated or recurrent PE.
Pulmonary Embolism Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Pulmonary Embolism (PE)"][/sc]
  • Isolated dyspnoea without chest pain - Can occur in smaller or subacute PEs where pleuritic pain does not develop.
  • Minimal symptoms in elderly or immobile patients - These patients may present with vague complaints of fatigue or weakness rather than classic symptoms of PE.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask pulmonary embolism (pe).
Pulmonary Embolism Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Pulmonary Embolism (PE)"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Focused bedside assessment and first-line testing to assess severity and immediate risk in suspected pulmonary embolism (pe).
  • Pre-test probability: The Wells PE score can support pre-test probability assessment, but it should be interpreted alongside the overall clinical picture; a calculator is available at Wells criteria for pulmonary embolism on MDCalc.
  • Targeted and monitoring: ECG may show sinus tachycardia or right heart strain patterns, although it is non-diagnostic; D-dimer helps only when pre-test probability is low or intermediate; CTPA is the key confirmatory imaging test when PE remains likely after clinical assessment.

Pulmonary Embolism Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Pulmonary Embolism (PE)"][/sc]
  • Pneumonia - Distinguish from pulmonary embolism (pe) using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.
  • Pneumothorax - Sudden chest pain and dyspnoea, but typically accompanied by hyper-resonance and absent breath sounds.
  • Acute Myocardial Infarction (MI) - Chest pain in MI is typically central and crushing, often associated with ST changes.
