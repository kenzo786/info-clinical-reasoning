Dementia
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Dementia"][/sc]
  • Dementia is a clinical syndrome characterised by a progressive decline in cognitive function severe enough to interfere with daily life.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise dementia features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Dementia"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Dementia"][/sc]
  • Risk factors: Risk factors for dementia include relevant predisposition, comorbidity, and exposure history.
  • Higher-risk or missed groups: Dementia may be missed when presentation is atypical or obscured by comorbidity.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Dementia"][/sc]
  • Dementia results from the progressive degeneration of neurons in key brain regions responsible for cognitive processes. In Alzheimer's disease, accumulation of beta-amyloid plaques and tau protein tangles disrupt synaptic communication and lead to neuronal death. In vascular dementia, chronic reduced blood flow leads to ischaemic damage. In Lewy body dementia, misfolded alpha-synuclein proteins form inclusions within neurons, impairing function. Other forms, such as frontotemporal dementia, involve selective degeneration of the frontal and temporal lobes.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Dementia"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Mild memory problems and subtle cognitive impairments (e.g. difficulty with complex tasks or planning).
  • Middle phase: Worsening memory, language deficits, impaired judgement, and functional decline (e.g. getting lost in familiar places).
  • Late phase: Severe cognitive and functional impairment, loss of independence, personality changes, and difficulty with basic self-care.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Dementia"][/sc]
  • Ask about memory loss - Often the first symptom, especially affecting short-term memory due to hippocampal degeneration in Alzheimer’s disease.
  • Ask about disorientation - Loss of spatial and temporal awareness, associated with parietal lobe involvement.
  • Ask about language impairment - Difficulty finding words or following conversations, linked to damage in the temporal lobe.
  • Ask about difficulty with problem-solving and planning - Executive dysfunction arises due to frontal lobe involvement, affecting judgement and decision-making.
  • Ask about personality changes - Irritability, apathy, or socially inappropriate behaviour due to frontal lobe atrophy, common in frontotemporal dementia.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Dementia"][/sc]
  • Check cognitive impairment - Deficits in memory, attention, and language, assessed via tools like the MMSE (Mini-Mental State Examination).
  • Check altered gait - Seen in vascular dementia and Lewy body dementia due to basal ganglia involvement, leading to parkinsonism.
  • Check myoclonus - Brief, involuntary muscle jerks, often noted in late-stage Alzheimer’s disease.
  • Check rigidity - Common in Lewy body dementia, reflecting parkinsonian features.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Dementia"][/sc]
  • Rapid progression - Suggestive of prion disease or an underlying malignancy rather than typical neurodegenerative dementias.
  • Focal neurological deficits - May indicate vascular dementia or a mass lesion rather than Alzheimer’s disease.
  • Severe behavioural disturbances - Early, severe personality changes may indicate frontotemporal dementia.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Dementia"][/sc]
  • Early-onset dementia (before 65) - Presents with greater behavioural or language symptoms and may be misdiagnosed as depression or stress.
  • Misattributed memory loss - Mild symptoms can be dismissed as "normal ageing," delaying diagnosis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Dementia"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Cognitive screening tests - MMSE (score < 24/30 suggests dementia), MoCA (Montreal Cognitive Assessment), or Addenbrooke's Cognitive Examination; Brain imaging (CT/MRI).
  • Targeted and monitoring: Blood tests - Exclude reversible causes like vitamin B12 deficiency, thyroid dysfunction, or infections (e.g., syphilis, HIV); CSF analysis - May.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Dementia"][/sc]
  • Delirium - Acute onset, fluctuating course, and reversible with treatment of the underlying cause (e.g., infection.
  • Depression - Also called pseudodementia; presents with cognitive complaints but lacks true cognitive deficits on formal.
  • Normal pressure hydrocephalus - Distinguish from dementia using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.
  • Parkinson’s disease dementia - Motor symptoms predominate early, with cognitive decline occurring later in the disease course.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Dementia"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Assess severity, physiological stability, and immediate complications.
  • Core care: Deliver condition-specific management for dementia, treat underlying drivers, and coordinate appropriate specialist input.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Reassess symptom trajectory, examination findings, and objective markers to confirm response in dementia.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Escalate urgently for clinical deterioration, evolving red flags, or failure to improve in suspected dementia.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Dementia"][/sc]
  • Functional decline - Inability to perform basic activities of daily living, leading to dependence.
  • Falls - Common in advanced stages due to poor coordination, leading to fractures or head injuries.
  • Infections - Aspiration pneumonia or urinary tract infections are common due to immobility and reduced self-care.
  • Death - Dementia is a terminal condition, with the median survival being 4-8 years after diagnosis.
