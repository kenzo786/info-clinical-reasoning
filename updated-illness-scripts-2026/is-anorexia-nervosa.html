Anorexia Nervosa
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Anorexia Nervosa"][/sc]
  • Anorexia nervosa is an eating disorder marked by persistent energy restriction, intense fear of weight gain, and distorted weight or shape beliefs causing low body weight and medical risk.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise anorexia features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Anorexia Nervosa"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Anorexia Nervosa"][/sc]
  • Risk factors: Adolescence, family history of eating disorders, perfectionism, anxiety traits, autistic traits, trauma or adverse events, and social pressure around weight and shape.
  • Higher-risk or missed groups: People with rapid weight loss at any BMI, males, and older adults may be under-recognised despite comparable medical risk.
  • Prevention and risk reduction: Early recognition of restrictive eating, non-non-judgemental conversations, and early referral to eating-disorder services can reduce deterioration.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Anorexia Nervosa"][/sc]
  • Anorexia nervosa is multifactorial, combining biological vulnerability, psychological drivers, and social context.
  • Sustained energy restriction triggers adaptive endocrine and metabolic changes that produce bradycardia, hypotension, cold intolerance, and menstrual disturbance.
  • Undernutrition affects cardiovascular, endocrine, bone, and cognitive systems, explaining broad physical morbidity.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Anorexia Nervosa"][/sc]
  • Onset: Often gradual with progressive restriction, meal avoidance, and increasing preoccupation with food, weight, and shape.
  • Progression: Without intervention, physiological compromise and cognitive rigidity usually increase over weeks to months.
  • Recovery pattern: Improvement is possible with specialist treatment, but relapse risk means follow-up should be sustained.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Anorexia Nervosa"][/sc]
  • Ask about restrictive eating patterns and avoidance rituals - indicates persistent energy restriction driving weight loss.
  • Ask about fear of weight gain and body-image overvaluation - supports core eating-disorder psychopathology.
  • Ask about fatigue, poor concentration, and cold intolerance - suggests reduced energy availability and metabolic adaptation.
  • Ask about menstrual change or amenorrhoea - may indicate hypothalamic suppression from undernutrition.
  • Ask about social withdrawal, low mood, or anxiety - common comorbidity that worsens risk and function.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Anorexia Nervosa"][/sc]
  • Check weight trajectory and BMI context - quantifies nutritional compromise even when BMI is not low.
  • Check pulse and postural blood pressure - identifies cardiovascular instability from starvation and volume depletion.
  • Check temperature, skin, and lanugo - supports chronic undernutrition on examination.
  • Check muscle bulk and power - reflects catabolic loss and frailty risk.
  • Check peripheral oedema - can occur in severe malnutrition or during refeeding.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Anorexia Nervosa"][/sc]
  • Physiological instability: Marked bradycardia, hypotension, syncope, or chest symptoms need urgent medical assessment.
  • Biochemical risk: Significant electrolyte disturbance, especially phosphate, potassium, or magnesium abnormalities, can precede sudden deterioration.
  • Neurological risk: Confusion, reduced consciousness, seizures, or severe weakness suggests urgent escalation.
  • Psychiatric and safeguarding risk: Suicidal ideation, severe self-neglect, or inability to maintain intake requires urgent specialist escalation.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Anorexia Nervosa"][/sc]
  • Atypical anorexia can present with substantial weight loss and equivalent medical risk despite BMI not being underweight.
  • How it gets missed: symptoms may be attributed to stress, fitness goals, or gastrointestinal complaints unless eating-disorder cognition is directly explored.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Anorexia Nervosa"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: FBC, U and E, glucose, phosphate, magnesium, LFTs, ECG, and orthostatic observations to assess immediate medical stability.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Repeat electrolytes and ECG trends, plus structured psychiatric risk assessment, to guide escalation and safe nutritional rehabilitation.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Anorexia Nervosa"][/sc]
  • A young person with progressive dietary restriction, weight loss, fear of weight gain, and body-image distortion, with bradycardia or postural instability indicating combined psychiatric and medical risk.
  • An eating disorder causing self-reinforcing restriction and multisystem undernutrition that requires integrated medical and mental-health management.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Anorexia Nervosa"][/sc]
  • Bulimia nervosa - recurrent binge-purge behavior is more prominent and persistent low weight is less typical.
  • Depression or anxiety disorders - appetite change can occur but weight or shape overvaluation is not the central driver.
  • Hyperthyroidism - weight loss with adrenergic or thyroid features rather than restrictive eating cognition.
  • Chronic gastrointestinal or inflammatory disease - weight loss is linked to organic symptoms and objective inflammatory or malabsorptive findings.
  • Malignancy - progressive systemic red flags without eating-disorder psychopathology.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Anorexia Nervosa"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Assess physiological stability, identify refeeding risk, and correct fluid or electrolyte abnormalities.
  • Core care: Management usually involves multidisciplinary eating-disorder treatment combining nutritional rehabilitation and psychological therapy, with family-based approaches commonly used in adolescents.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Track weight trajectory, observations, ECG, and biochemical markers alongside mental-state and risk review.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Urgent specialist referral or admission is required for severe instability, rapid deterioration, high psychiatric risk, or inability to maintain oral intake.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Anorexia Nervosa"][/sc]
  • Cardiovascular instability - arrhythmia and circulatory collapse are major life-threatening risks in severe malnutrition.
  • Refeeding syndrome - rapid nutritional restoration without monitoring can trigger critical electrolyte shifts.
  • Bone and endocrine morbidity - reduced bone density, menstrual disturbance, and reduced fertility risk can persist with prolonged illness.
  • Psychiatric and social sequelae - chronic mood symptoms, isolation, and relapse risk are common without sustained follow-up.
