Infantile Colic
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Infantile Colic"][/sc]
  • Infantile colic is defined as excessive crying or fussiness in an otherwise healthy infant, typically starting within the first few weeks of life and resolving by 3–4 months of age.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise infantile features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Infantile Colic"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Infantile Colic"][/sc]
  • Risk factors: Risk factors for infantile colic include relevant predisposition, comorbidity, and exposure history.
  • Higher-risk or missed groups: Infantile Colic may be missed when presentation is atypical or obscured by comorbidity.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Infantile Colic"][/sc]
  • While the exact cause is unknown, proposed mechanisms include gastrointestinal discomfort (e.g., gas, immaturity of gut motility), hypersensitivity to stimuli, or dysbiosis in gut microbiota. Neurodevelopmental immaturity may contribute to difficulties in self-regulation.
  • Excessive crying may result in swallowing air, exacerbating gastrointestinal symptoms.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Infantile Colic"][/sc]
  • Onset: Typically begins at 2–3 weeks of age.
  • Duration: Peaks at 6 weeks of age and usually resolves by 3–4 months of age.
  • Phases: Episodic with crying bouts most common in the evening.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Infantile Colic"][/sc]
  • Ask about excessive crying - Crying episodes exceeding 3 hours per day; thought to result from discomfort or sensory overload.
  • Ask about fussiness - Periods of irritability unrelated to hunger or nappy changes, potentially linked to gut motility issues.
  • Ask about difficulty soothing - Crying often unresponsive to usual soothing techniques, attributed to neurodevelopmental immaturity.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Infantile Colic"][/sc]
  • Check tensed abdomen - Indicates gastrointestinal discomfort due to gas or bloating.
  • Check flexed legs - Often seen during crying episodes, reflecting a pain response in the abdomen.
  • Check normal growth and development - Confirms the absence of an underlying pathological condition.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Infantile Colic"][/sc]
  • Failure to thrive - Suggests an underlying condition such as malabsorption or metabolic disorder.
  • Fever - May indicate infection, requiring further evaluation.
  • Haematemesis or blood in stool - Sign of possible gastrointestinal pathology like intussusception or cow’s milk protein allergy.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Infantile Colic"][/sc]
  • Prolonged duration - Crying persisting beyond 4 months of age, possibly indicating an alternative diagnosis like reflux or neurological conditions.
  • Intermittent severe episodes - May be misdiagnosed as colic but represent conditions such as intussusception.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask infantile colic.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Infantile Colic"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Clinical diagnosis - Based on history and exclusion of red flag features.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Stool analysis - Considered if blood in stool is present, to rule out cow’s milk protein allergy; Growth charts - Used.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Infantile Colic"][/sc]
  • A 6-week-old male infant presents with daily episodes of inconsolable crying lasting 4–5 hours, especially in the evenings, with no fever, vomiting, or signs of failure to thrive.
  • Patient with daily episodes of inconsolable crying lasting 4–5 hours, especially in the evenings, with no fever, vomiting, or signs of failure to thrive, consistent with infantile colic.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Infantile Colic"][/sc]
  • Gastro-oesophageal reflux disease (GORD) - Distinguished by frequent regurgitation and irritability associated with feeding.
  • Cow’s milk protein allergy - Often presents with eczema, bloody stools, and a family history of atopy.
  • Intussusception - Severe intermittent crying with "red currant jelly" stools and a palpable abdominal mass.
  • Related alternative diagnosis - Distinguish from infantile colic using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Infantile Colic"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Assess severity, physiological stability, and immediate complications.
  • Core care: Deliver condition-specific management for infantile colic, treat underlying drivers, and coordinate appropriate specialist input.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Reassess symptom trajectory, examination findings, and objective markers to confirm response in infantile colic.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Escalate urgently for clinical deterioration, evolving red flags, or failure to improve in suspected infantile colic.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Infantile Colic"][/sc]
  • Parental stress and depression - Significant psychological impact on caregivers.
  • Increased risk of shaken baby syndrome - Due to frustration from inconsolable crying.
  • Over-medicalisation - Unnecessary investigations or treatments due to misdiagnosis.
