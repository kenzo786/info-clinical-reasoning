Food Allergy and Intolerance
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Food Allergy and Intolerance"][/sc]
  • Food allergy is an immune-mediated adverse reaction to food proteins that can involve IgE (immunoglobulin E) or non-IgE pathways.

Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Food Allergy and Intolerance"][/sc]
  • IgE-mediated allergy: Exposure to specific food proteins triggers IgE antibodies, leading to histamine and cytokine release from mast cells and basophils, causing symptoms like hives, bronchospasm, and anaphylaxis.
  • Non-IgE-mediated allergy: Delayed hypersensitivity involving T-cell activation against food antigens, common in conditions like eosinophilic oesophagitis, leading to gastrointestinal symptoms.
  • Food intolerance: Often involves enzymatic deficiencies (e.g., lactase for lactose intolerance) or the inability to properly digest certain compounds, leading to bloating, abdominal pain, and diarrhoea.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Food Allergy and Intolerance"][/sc]
  • Acute (IgE-mediated allergy): Onset within minutes to hours after exposure, rapidly progressive in severe cases (e.g., anaphylaxis).
  • Subacute/Chronic (Non-IgE-mediated allergy and intolerance): Symptoms may develop within hours to days, often with persistent or recurrent presentations.
  • Resolution: Food allergies in children may resolve over time (e.g., milk allergy), whereas intolerances are usually lifelong.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Food Allergy and Intolerance"][/sc]
  • Ask about itching, hives, and swelling - Histamine release causes itching and hives; angioedema results from increased capillary permeability.
  • Ask about gastrointestinal distress (nausea, vomiting, diarrhoea) - Common in both allergies (due to immune reaction) and intolerances (enzyme deficiencies or irritants).
  • Ask about respiratory symptoms (wheezing, bronchospasm) - Primarily in IgE-mediated allergies, caused by bronchial constriction due to histamine.
  • Ask about anaphylaxis (severe reactions) - IgE reaction leading to multi-organ involvement; may cause hypotension, airway obstruction.
  • Ask about abdominal pain and bloating - Typically linked to intolerances like lactose intolerance, due to incomplete digestion and gas formation.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Food Allergy and Intolerance"][/sc]
  • Check skin changes (urticaria, angioedema) - Visible signs of IgE-mediated mast cell degranulation.
  • Check respiratory distress (wheezing, cyanosis) - Wheezing due to bronchoconstriction, cyanosis from hypoxia in severe cases.
  • Check hypotension and shock (in anaphylaxis) - Vascular dilation and increased permeability cause fluid shifts, reducing blood pressure.
  • Check abdominal distension and tenderness - Common in intolerances, due to gas and fluid accumulation from incomplete digestion.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Food Allergy and Intolerance"][/sc]
  • Hypotension, stridor, respiratory distress - Indicates possible anaphylaxis, which requires immediate intervention.
  • Severe vomiting and diarrhoea in infants - May suggest milk protein allergy and risk for dehydration, growth impairment.
  • Persistent abdominal pain and blood in stools - Could indicate eosinophilic gastrointestinal disease, needing specialist referral.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Food Allergy and Intolerance"][/sc]
  • Mild chronic cough or eczema - Can be a non-IgE manifestation of food allergies, often missed if symptoms are low-grade.
  • Headaches and mood changes - Occasionally seen in food intolerances due to non-specific responses to food components like caffeine or MSG.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Food Allergy and Intolerance"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Skin prick testing (SPT) - Raised wheal indicates IgE-mediated allergy; negative result suggests non-IgE allergy or intolerance; Specific IgE blood tests.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Targeted investigations to confirm diagnosis, assess complications, and monitor response in food allergy and intolerance.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Food Allergy and Intolerance"][/sc]
  • Coeliac disease - Unlike allergy, an autoimmune response to gluten; associated with malabsorption and villous atrophy.
  • Irritable bowel syndrome (IBS) - Often overlaps with intolerance symptoms but lacks immune-mediated aetiology.
  • Lactose intolerance - Enzyme deficiency causing GI symptoms after dairy ingestion, without immune involvement.
  • Eosinophilic oesophagitis - Mimics GI food allergies but involves eosinophil-driven inflammation, diagnosed with endoscopy and biopsy.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Food Allergy and Intolerance"][/sc]
  • Anaphylaxis - Life-threatening, requires immediate adrenaline; long-term management involves avoidance and carrying adrenaline auto-injectors.
  • Chronic inflammation - In non-IgE allergies, may lead to eosinophilic gastroenteritis or oesophagitis, requiring immunosuppressive treatment.
  • Psychosocial impacts - Anxiety around food and social restrictions are common, particularly in children with severe food allergies.
