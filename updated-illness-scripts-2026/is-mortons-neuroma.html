Morton's Neuroma
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Morton's Neuroma"][/sc]
  • Morton's neuroma is a benign, painful condition characterised by fibrous thickening of the interdigital nerve, most commonly between the third and fourth metatarsal heads.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise morton features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.

Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Morton's Neuroma"][/sc]
  • Repeated trauma or compression leads to perineural fibrosis and thickening of the nerve sheath.
  • This often occurs due to mechanical stress, such as wearing tight shoes or engaging in activities involving repetitive forefoot pressure.
  • Fibrosis irritates the nerve, resulting in neuropathic pain and sensory disturbances.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Morton's Neuroma"][/sc]
  • Initial phase: Often asymptomatic or presenting with mild discomfort.
  • Progressive phase: Gradual onset of burning pain and paraesthesia in the affected interdigital space.
  • Chronic phase: Persistent pain exacerbated by weight-bearing activities and relieved by rest or shoe removal.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Morton's Neuroma"][/sc]
  • Ask about burning pain in the forefoot - Caused by nerve irritation and inflammation in the affected intermetatarsal space.
  • Ask about paraesthesia - Tingling or numbness radiating to the toes due to compression of the interdigital nerve.
  • Ask about sensation of a lump or "pebble in the shoe" - Reflects localised swelling and fibrosis.
  • Ask about symptom relief on removing tight shoes - Indicates a mechanical component to symptom exacerbation.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Morton's Neuroma"][/sc]
  • Check tenderness on palpation of the intermetatarsal space - Indicative of localised inflammation and fibrosis.
  • Check mulder’s click - A palpable or audible click when the metatarsal heads are compressed, caused by the thickened nerve moving between the bones.
  • Check exacerbation of symptoms with compression - Confirms the role of mechanical irritation.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Morton's Neuroma"][/sc]
  • Rapidly progressive pain or swelling - Could indicate a malignancy or infection.
  • Systemic symptoms (fever, weight loss) - May suggest an alternative diagnosis such as a systemic inflammatory condition or cancer.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Morton's Neuroma"][/sc]
  • Isolated paraesthesia without pain, often mistaken for peripheral neuropathy.
  • Symptoms bilaterally, which can mimic systemic neuropathic conditions.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask morton's neuroma.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Morton's Neuroma"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Clinical examination - Mulder’s test and palpation are diagnostic.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Ultrasound - Identifies nerve thickening and surrounding inflammation; MRI - Useful for ruling out other causes like tumours or joint pathology.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Morton's Neuroma"][/sc]
  • Metatarsalgia - Pain without nerve involvement; lacks paraesthesia and Mulder’s sign.
  • Stress fracture - Localised tenderness and radiographic changes on X-ray.
  • Peripheral neuropathy - Bilateral, diffuse symptoms, often associated with systemic conditions like diabetes.
  • Related alternative diagnosis - Distinguish from morton's neuroma using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Morton's Neuroma"][/sc]

  • Neuropathy - Due to surgical complications.
  • Recurrence - Can occur post-surgery in up to 20% of cases.
