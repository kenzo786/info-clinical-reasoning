Paroxysmal Supraventricular Tachycardia (PSVT)
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Paroxysmal Supraventricular Tachycardia (PSVT)"][/sc]
  • Paroxysmal supraventricular tachycardia (PSVT) is a type of arrhythmia characterised by intermittent episodes of rapid heart rate that originate above the ventricles.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise paroxysmal features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.

Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Paroxysmal Supraventricular Tachycardia (PSVT)"][/sc]
  • PSVT is usually caused by re-entry mechanisms within the atrioventricular (AV) node or accessory pathways between the atria and ventricles.
  • In AV nodal re-entry tachycardia (AVNRT), an electrical impulse re-enters the AV node, creating a self-perpetuating loop, leading to rapid atrial and ventricular activation.
  • Accessory pathways (as seen in AVRT) allow impulses to bypass the AV node, leading to faster-than-normal conduction.
  • Triggers such as emotional stress, caffeine, or alcohol can exacerbate PSVT by increasing sympathetic tone, thus favouring re-entry.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Paroxysmal Supraventricular Tachycardia (PSVT)"][/sc]
  • PSVT episodes can last from a few seconds to several hours and can occur sporadically or more frequently. Without treatment, these episodes may recur.
  • Onset is typically sudden, and termination of episodes is also abrupt.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Paroxysmal Supraventricular Tachycardia (PSVT)"][/sc]
  • Ask about palpitations - Sudden awareness of rapid, pounding heartbeats. This occurs due to rapid atrial depolarisation causing increased ventricular contraction frequency.
  • Ask about dizziness - Caused by transient reduced cardiac output during tachycardic episodes, reducing cerebral perfusion.
  • Ask about shortness of breath - Related to poor ventricular filling time during rapid heart rates, leading to decreased stroke volume and oxygen delivery.
  • Ask about chest discomfort - Typically due to increased myocardial oxygen demand during tachycardia or anxiety related to the rapid heart rate.
  • Ask about fatigue - Caused by inefficient cardiac output and the physical strain of maintaining a high heart rate for prolonged periods.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Paroxysmal Supraventricular Tachycardia (PSVT)"][/sc]
  • Check rapid heart rate (150-250 bpm) - Primary sign due to the tachycardia originating from the re-entry circuit in the AV node or atria.
  • Check hypotension - Due to reduced cardiac output as a result of poor ventricular filling time.
  • Check pallor or diaphoresis - These signs of sympathetic nervous system activation can occur during episodes due to reduced perfusion and increased adrenergic drive.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Paroxysmal Supraventricular Tachycardia (PSVT)"][/sc]
  • Syncope - Suggests severe haemodynamic compromise due to prolonged tachycardia. Can be a sign of dangerous arrhythmias or heart failure.
  • Chest pain suggestive of ischaemia - May indicate myocardial ischaemia secondary to prolonged high heart rates.
  • Severe hypotension - Indicates poor cardiac output and can be a precursor to cardiogenic shock if prolonged.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Paroxysmal Supraventricular Tachycardia (PSVT)"][/sc]
  • Some patients may experience asymptomatic PSVT, only detected on routine ECG or during unrelated examinations.
  • Older patients may present with general fatigue or mild dizziness without typical palpitations, due to less sensitivity to rapid heart rates.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask paroxysmal supraventricular tachycardia (psvt).
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Paroxysmal Supraventricular Tachycardia (PSVT)"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: ECG - Narrow complex tachycardia (QRS < 120 ms) is the hallmark finding. P waves may be hidden or occur just.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Holter monitoring - Used for patients with intermittent symptoms. Can capture episodes that may not be present during an in-office ECG.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Paroxysmal Supraventricular Tachycardia (PSVT)"][/sc]
  • Heart failure - Persistent PSVT can lead to impaired ventricular function due to chronically increased heart rates.
  • Thromboembolism - Prolonged episodes of PSVT can increase the risk of clot formation, especially in patients with other risk factors for atrial fibrillation.
  • Cardiogenic shock - Severe, prolonged episodes may compromise cardiac output, leading to hypotension and shock.
