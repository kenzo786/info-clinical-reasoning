Crohn's Disease
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Crohn's Disease"][/sc]
  • Crohn's disease is a chronic, relapsing inflammatory bowel disease (IBD) that can affect any part of the gastrointestinal tract, from mouth to anus.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise crohn features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.

Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Crohn's Disease"][/sc]
  • Crohn’s disease arises from a complex interaction between genetic predisposition, environmental triggers, and an abnormal immune response. A dysregulated immune system in the gut leads to inflammation, predominantly affecting the terminal ileum but potentially involving any part of the GI tract.
  • The transmural nature of inflammation can lead to complications such as strictures (due to fibrosis), fistulas (due to penetrating inflammation), and malabsorption (from mucosal damage).
  • Involvement of mesenteric lymph nodes and granulomas are hallmark features, contributing to the chronicity of inflammation and fibrosis.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Crohn's Disease"][/sc]
  • Crohn’s disease typically has an insidious onset, with symptoms developing gradually over weeks to months.
  • It follows a relapsing-remitting course, with periods of flare-ups and remission. During a flare, inflammation can worsen leading to severe symptoms and complications.
  • Progression of disease can lead to chronic complications such as strictures, fistulas, and intestinal obstruction.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Crohn's Disease"][/sc]
  • Ask about diarrhoea - Chronic, often non-bloody, diarrhoea occurs due to inflammation and malabsorption in the small intestine.
  • Ask about abdominal pain - Typically cramping, located in the right lower quadrant, results from inflammation, strictures, or bowel obstruction.
  • Ask about weight loss - Caused by malabsorption of nutrients, reduced appetite, and increased metabolic demand due to chronic inflammation.
  • Ask about fatigue - A common non-specific symptom linked to chronic inflammation and anaemia.
  • Ask about fever - Reflects active inflammation and may be associated with abscess formation.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Crohn's Disease"][/sc]
  • Check abdominal tenderness - Most commonly in the right lower quadrant, due to ileocaecal involvement.
  • Check pallor - Reflects anaemia, which can be due to chronic inflammation or malabsorption of iron and vitamin B12.
  • Check perianal disease - Skin tags, fistulas, and abscesses may occur due to the transmural inflammation.
  • Check oral ulcers - A less common manifestation reflecting gastrointestinal tract involvement extending to the mouth.
  • Check weight loss - Clinically noticeable in severe or chronic cases due to malabsorption and nutritional deficiencies.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Crohn's Disease"][/sc]
  • Severe abdominal pain and vomiting - May indicate bowel obstruction due to strictures.
  • Unintentional weight loss - Can signify malabsorption and a more aggressive disease course.
  • Perianal fistulas - Reflects advanced disease and may lead to perirectal abscesses.
  • Severe bleeding - Suggests complications such as intestinal perforation or severe inflammation.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Crohn's Disease"][/sc]
  • Isolated perianal disease - In some patients, Crohn's can present solely with perianal disease without significant GI symptoms.
  • Extraintestinal manifestations - Arthritis, uveitis, and erythema nodosum may be the first signs before gastrointestinal symptoms appear.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask crohn's disease.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Crohn's Disease"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Colonoscopy with biopsy - The gold standard for diagnosis, typically revealing skip lesions, cobblestone mucosa, and granulomas; Faecal calprotectin - Elevated.
  • Targeted and monitoring: CRP and ESR - Markers of systemic inflammation that are commonly elevated during flares; Small bowel imaging (MR enterography) - Useful.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Crohn's Disease"][/sc]
  • Ulcerative colitis - Differs as it is limited to the colon, presents with bloody diarrhoea, and lacks.
  • Infectious colitis - Acute onset of diarrhoea and fever, often resolving with treatment, unlike the chronic course.
  • Coeliac disease - Presents with malabsorption but lacks the inflammatory findings on endoscopy typical of Crohn's.
  • Related alternative diagnosis - Distinguish from crohn's disease using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Crohn's Disease"][/sc]
  • Bowel obstruction - Caused by strictures from chronic inflammation.
  • Fistulas and abscesses - Due to transmural inflammation, commonly in the perianal region.
  • Malnutrition - From chronic malabsorption of nutrients, particularly in extensive small bowel involvement.
  • Colorectal cancer - Increased risk due to chronic inflammation, particularly if colonic involvement is present.
