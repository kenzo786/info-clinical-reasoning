Undescended Testis
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Undescended Testis"][/sc]
  • An undescended testis, also known as cryptorchidism, is a condition in which one or both testes fail to descend into the scrotum, remaining in the abdomen, inguinal canal, or upper.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise undescended features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.

Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Undescended Testis"][/sc]
  • Testicular descent is a complex process influenced by hormonal, mechanical, and genetic factors. The descent is divided into two main phases: the transabdominal phase, guided by the gubernaculum, and the inguinoscrotal phase, largely driven by androgens and the genitofemoral nerve.
  • Failure of descent can occur due to disruptions in hormonal pathways (e.g., testosterone deficiency), anatomical abnormalities (e.g., patent processus vaginalis), or genetic factors, leading to retention of the testis in the abdominal or inguinal region.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Undescended Testis"][/sc]
  • Neonatal Period: Undescended testes are identified during newborn examination, with many cases descending spontaneously by 3-6 months.
  • Persistent Cases: Testes that remain undescended beyond 6-12 months are less likely to descend without intervention and may require surgical correction.
  • Long-term: If untreated, cryptorchidism can lead to complications such as infertility and an increased risk of testicular malignancy.

  • Ask about fertility Concerns in Adolescents/Adults - In longstanding cases, undescended testes may be associated with reduced fertility due to prolonged exposure to higher intra-abdominal temperatures.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Undescended Testis"][/sc]
  • Check non-palpable or Ectopic Testis - Absence of the testis in the scrotum during physical examination, which may be located higher in the inguinal canal.
  • Check inguinal Fullness or Mass - In some cases, an undescended testis may be felt as a small mass in the inguinal area, particularly, with associated clinical features.
  • Check retractile Testis - Some testes may move up temporarily but can be manipulated down into the scrotum; this should be differentiated from truly undescended.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Undescended Testis"][/sc]
  • Bilateral Non-palpable Testes - This raises concern for an underlying endocrine disorder, such as congenital adrenal hyperplasia, requiring immediate endocrinological evaluation.
  • Acute Pain or Tenderness - Sudden onset of pain may indicate testicular torsion, which is a urological emergency.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Undescended Testis"][/sc]
  • Retractile Testis - This is often confused with undescended testis as the testis is not consistently present in the scrotum but can be manually positioned; it usually resolves with growth and does not require surgery.
  • Cryptorchidism in Older Children or Adolescents - If diagnosed later in life, it may be associated with hormonal disorders or acquired undescended testis.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask undescended testis.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Undescended Testis"][/sc]
  • Retractile Testis - Testis can be moved into the scrotum; unlike cryptorchidism, it does not require surgery.
  • Inguinal Hernia - May present as an inguinal mass; however, a hernia is usually reducible, and transillumination.
  • Testicular Agenesis - Absence of the testis, confirmed through imaging or surgical exploration; requires no surgical repositioning.
  • Related alternative diagnosis - Distinguish from undescended testis using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Undescended Testis"][/sc]
  • Infertility - Higher temperatures in undescended testis impair spermatogenesis; occurs in untreated bilateral cases more frequently.
  • Testicular Cancer - Increased risk of germ cell tumours in undescended testis, even after orchiopexy.
  • Torsion of Undescended Testis - Increased risk of torsion in undescended testes, potentially leading to loss of testicular tissue.
