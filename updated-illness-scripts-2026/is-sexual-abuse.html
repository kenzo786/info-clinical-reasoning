Sexual Abuse
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Sexual Abuse"][/sc]
  • Sexual abuse is any form of non-consensual sexual activity, often involving coercion, manipulation, or exploitation.

Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Sexual Abuse"][/sc]
  • Sexual abuse is a trauma that affects both the body and mind. The physiological stress response (release of cortisol, adrenaline) can lead to short-term effects like anxiety and agitation, while long-term stress may result in chronic psychological conditions, such as PTSD or depression. Physical trauma from sexual abuse may cause genital injuries, pain, or sexually transmitted infections (STIs). Emotional dissociation and neurobiological changes can develop as protective mechanisms, disrupting normal brain function and emotional regulation.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Sexual Abuse"][/sc]
  • Acute phase - In the immediate aftermath of abuse, victims may experience physical injuries, shock, confusion, and intense emotional responses (fear, shame). Physical symptoms such as pain and genital trauma may be prominent.
  • Subacute phase - Over the weeks to months following the incident, emotional and psychological distress typically become more dominant. Symptoms of PTSD, anxiety, or depression may emerge.
  • Chronic phase - Long-term consequences can include chronic mental health issues (PTSD, substance abuse), physical complications (chronic pelvic pain, STIs), and social difficulties (relationship breakdowns, trust issues).
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Sexual Abuse"][/sc]
  • Ask about psychological distress - Anxiety, depression, and PTSD are common due to the trauma affecting emotional regulation and causing hyperarousal of the nervous system.
  • Ask about flashbacks or intrusive thoughts - Often linked to PTSD, these result from the brain's difficulty in processing and storing the traumatic experience.
  • Ask about somatic symptoms - Non-specific symptoms like headaches, abdominal pain, and pelvic pain are common, reflecting both physical injuries and stress-related body responses.
  • Ask about sexual dysfunction - Fear, anxiety, or pain during sexual activity may arise due to the physical and emotional trauma from abuse.
  • Ask about dissociation - A psychological coping mechanism where the person feels detached from reality, due to overwhelming trauma.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Sexual Abuse"][/sc]
  • Check genital injuries - Bruising, lacerations, or swelling in the genital area, often resulting from physical trauma.
  • Check sexually transmitted infections (STIs) - Presence of STIs may indicate unprotected sexual contact, which could be associated with abuse.
  • Check psychosomatic signs - Visible tension, hypervigilance, or avoidance of physical contact, often seen as a manifestation of PTSD or anxiety.
  • Check self-harm - Physical injuries from self-inflicted harm may indicate ongoing psychological distress related to the trauma.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Sexual Abuse"][/sc]
  • Severe dissociation - Signifies extreme psychological trauma and may predispose the individual to further psychological breakdown.
  • Suicidal ideation - Indicates a high level of emotional distress and risk of self-harm or suicide, requiring immediate intervention.
  • Physical injuries - Signs of genital or anal trauma, along with unexplained bruises or fractures, suggest acute physical abuse and warrant immediate protective action.
  • Substance abuse - May signal self-medication for emotional pain and an attempt to cope with ongoing trauma.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Sexual Abuse"][/sc]
  • Asymptomatic presentation - Many survivors may not show immediate or obvious symptoms, particularly if abuse is non-physical or if coping mechanisms are in place.
  • Unexplained behavioural changes - Especially in children, symptoms such as withdrawal, aggression, or school refusal may be the only indicators of sexual abuse.
  • Vague somatic complaints - Chronic headaches, digestive issues, and fatigue can sometimes mask the underlying psychological trauma of abuse.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask sexual abuse.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Sexual Abuse"][/sc]
  • Chronic PTSD - Long-term psychological trauma can result in significant impairment in social and occupational functioning.
  • Chronic pain syndromes - Persistent somatic pain (e.g., pelvic or abdominal pain) can occur, sometimes linked to the original trauma.
  • Relationship difficulties - Survivors may experience long-term difficulties with trust, intimacy, and forming relationships.
  • Increased risk of substance abuse - Many survivors of sexual abuse turn to alcohol or drugs as a coping mechanism.
