Malignancy-related Weight Loss
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Malignancy-related Weight Loss"][/sc]
  • Malignancy-related weight loss refers to unintentional weight loss associated with underlying cancer.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise malignancy features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.

Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Malignancy-related Weight Loss"][/sc]
  • The pathophysiology of malignancy-related weight loss involves several mechanisms:
  • **Increased Metabolic Demand**: Cancer cells exhibit rapid and unregulated growth, leading to high energy consumption.
  • **Systemic Inflammation**: Tumours induce the release of pro-inflammatory cytokines (e.g., IL-6, TNF-alpha), which contribute to muscle and fat catabolism.
  • **Tumour Necrosis**: Some tumours cause necrosis, leading to a systemic catabolic state and anorexia (loss of appetite).
  • **Reduced Nutritional Intake**: Patients may experience early satiety, dysphagia, or nausea due to tumour location or treatment-related effects, contributing to poor caloric intake.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Malignancy-related Weight Loss"][/sc]
  • Malignancy-related weight loss typically occurs gradually over months, but can be rapid depending on the tumour type and aggressiveness. Initial phases may be subtle, with progressive cachexia (muscle wasting) occurring later. Weight loss may continue despite adequate nutritional intake as the disease progresses.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Malignancy-related Weight Loss"][/sc]
  • Ask about unintentional Weight Loss - Progressive loss of body mass, typically more than 5% of body weight over 6-12 months, is the hallmark symptom, driven.
  • Ask about anorexia - Loss of appetite due to the tumour or its metabolic by-products, contributing to reduced caloric intake.
  • Ask about fatigue - A common symptom related to the body’s inability to meet energy demands due to both poor nutrition and the cancer’s metabolic activity.
  • Ask about night Sweats - Often associated with lymphomas or cancers causing systemic inflammation and fever.
  • Ask about early Satiety - Frequently seen in gastrointestinal cancers, where the tumour compresses surrounding organs or causes obstruction, limiting food intake.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Malignancy-related Weight Loss"][/sc]
  • Check pallor - Due to anaemia, often seen in advanced cancer or as a consequence of bone marrow infiltration.
  • Check cachexia - Visible muscle wasting and loss of fat stores, driven by catabolic processes initiated by the tumour.
  • Check palpable Mass - A mass may be felt on examination, indicating tumour growth, particularly in abdominal or breast cancers.
  • Check lymphadenopathy - Enlarged lymph nodes, often a sign of metastatic or haematological cancers.
  • Check jaundice - Indicates liver involvement, either due to metastatic disease or primary liver/pancreatic cancer.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Malignancy-related Weight Loss"][/sc]
  • Weight loss of >5% in 6 months - Significant unintentional weight loss is concerning for malignancy.
  • Night Sweats and Fever - Associated with haematological malignancies such as lymphoma or leukaemia.
  • Dysphagia or Early Satiety - Suggests oesophageal or gastric cancer.
  • Painless Jaundice - Indicates possible pancreatic cancer with liver metastasis or obstruction.
  • Palpable Abdominal Mass - May indicate advanced cancer, such as colon or ovarian malignancy.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Malignancy-related Weight Loss"][/sc]
  • Minimal Weight Loss - In early-stage cancers, patients may experience anorexia or fatigue before significant weight loss occurs.
  • Chronic Fatigue without Weight Loss - Some patients may primarily complain of fatigue, particularly in haematological malignancies, before weight loss is noticeable.
  • Non-specific Gastrointestinal Symptoms - Mild gastrointestinal symptoms like bloating or indigestion can precede weight loss in gastrointestinal cancers.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask malignancy-related weight loss.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Malignancy-related Weight Loss"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Full Blood Count (FBC) - May show anaemia, leukocytosis, or thrombocytosis, suggesting bone marrow infiltration or chronic disease; Liver Function Tests.
  • Targeted and monitoring: CT Scan - Cross-sectional imaging to identify primary tumour, metastases, or lymphadenopathy. CT of the chest, abdomen, and pelvis is often.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Malignancy-related Weight Loss"][/sc]
  • Cachexia - Progressive muscle wasting that is difficult to reverse, leading to poor prognosis.
  • Malnutrition - Further weakens immune response and contributes to overall deterioration.
  • Organ Dysfunction - Advanced cancer can lead to liver, kidney, or heart failure depending on metastasis location.
