Venous Leg Ulcer
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Venous Leg Ulcer"][/sc]
  • Venous leg ulcers are chronic skin wounds occurring due to venous insufficiency, typically located on the lower leg, above the ankle.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise venous features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.

Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Venous Leg Ulcer"][/sc]
  • Venous hypertension results from valve incompetence, venous obstruction, or calf muscle pump dysfunction.
  • Increased venous pressure causes capillary leakage and inflammation, leading to fibrin deposition and reduced oxygen diffusion.
  • Chronic inflammation damages skin and subcutaneous tissues, impairing wound healing and predisposing to ulcer formation.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Venous Leg Ulcer"][/sc]
  • Acute phase: Painful, shallow wound development over days to weeks.
  • Chronic phase: Persistent ulceration, typically lasting weeks to months, often recurrent.
  • Healing phase: Gradual closure of the wound over months with appropriate treatment.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Venous Leg Ulcer"][/sc]
  • Ask about leg pain - Common, due to inflammation and tissue damage caused by venous hypertension.
  • Ask about itching or burning sensation - Secondary to skin inflammation and chronic irritation.
  • Ask about swelling - Caused by fluid leakage into the interstitial space due to venous stasis.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Venous Leg Ulcer"][/sc]
  • Check shallow ulcer with irregular edges - Indicative of venous aetiology.
  • Check oedema - Persistent fluid accumulation due to poor venous return.
  • Check hyperpigmentation - Resulting from hemosiderin deposition in the skin.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Venous Leg Ulcer"][/sc]
  • Rapidly worsening pain or redness - May indicate cellulitis or deep vein thrombosis.
  • Non-healing despite optimal treatment - Raises suspicion for malignancy or atypical infection.
  • Systemic symptoms (fever, malaise) - Suggest possible systemic infection.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Venous Leg Ulcer"][/sc]
  • Ulcers presenting above the knee - May indicate mixed aetiology, such as arterial involvement.
  • Minimal oedema with significant pain - Consider arterial insufficiency or vasculitis.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask venous leg ulcer.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Venous Leg Ulcer"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Ankle-Brachial Pressure Index (ABPI) - <0.8 suggests arterial involvement, contraindicating compression therapy.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Doppler ultrasound - Demonstrates venous reflux or obstruction; Wound swab or biopsy - To rule out infection or malignancy in non-healing.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Venous Leg Ulcer"][/sc]
  • Arterial ulcer - Painful, punched-out appearance, distal location.
  • Diabetic ulcer - Painless, located on pressure points, associated with neuropathy.
  • Vasculitic ulcer - Painful, irregular margins, systemic inflammatory signs.
  • Related alternative diagnosis - Distinguish from venous leg ulcer using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Venous Leg Ulcer"][/sc]
  • Infection - Common, leading to cellulitis or osteomyelitis if untreated.
  • Chronic pain - Due to persistent inflammation.
  • Malignant transformation - Rare, squamous cell carcinoma can develop in chronic ulcers.
