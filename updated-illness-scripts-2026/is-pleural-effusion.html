Pleural Effusion
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Pleural Effusion"][/sc]
  • Pleural effusion is the accumulation of excess fluid between the layers of the pleura, which are the thin membranes that cover the lungs and line the chest cavity.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise pleural features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.

Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Pleural Effusion"][/sc]
  • Pleural effusion develops when the balance between pleural fluid formation and absorption is disrupted. Causes include:
    • Increased hydrostatic pressure, such as in congestive heart failure, leads to transudative effusion.
    • Increased capillary permeability or inflammation, such as in infection or malignancy, leads to exudative effusion.
    • Decreased oncotic pressure, due to low serum albumin (e.g., in nephrotic syndrome), can also cause transudative effusions.
    • Blockage of lymphatic drainage due to malignancy or trauma can cause fluid accumulation.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Pleural Effusion"][/sc]
  • The time course of pleural effusion varies depending on the underlying cause:
    • Acute onset is seen in conditions like pulmonary embolism or pneumonia.
    • Chronic, slowly progressing effusion is common in malignancies or heart failure.
    • Recurrent effusions are often associated with malignancies or heart failure that is not well controlled.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Pleural Effusion"][/sc]
  • Ask about dyspnoea (shortness of breath) – Caused by the compression of lung tissue due to fluid accumulation, limiting lung expansion.
  • Ask about pleuritic chest pain – This sharp, localised pain worsens with inspiration and is due to irritation of the inflamed pleural surfaces.
  • Ask about cough – Often dry and non-productive, the cough is caused by lung compression or irritation of the pleura.
  • Ask about fatigue – General fatigue can result from underlying conditions such as malignancy or infection that cause the effusion.
  • Ask about pleural effusion may be asymptomatic in small volumes or slowly developing cases.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Pleural Effusion"][/sc]
  • Check dullness to percussion – The presence of fluid dampens the percussion note over the affected area.
  • Check reduced or absent breath sounds – Fluid in the pleural space impairs sound transmission from the lungs.
  • Check decreased tactile fremitus – The accumulation of fluid decreases the transmission of vibrations from the vocal cords to the chest wall.
  • Check pleural rub – Inflammation of the pleura can produce a rubbing sound on auscultation in some cases.
  • Check tracheal deviation (in large effusions) – Large volumes of fluid can push the trachea to the opposite side.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Pleural Effusion"][/sc]
  • Haemoptysis – Suggests underlying malignancy or pulmonary embolism.
  • Weight loss and night sweats – Can indicate tuberculosis or malignancy.
  • Sudden-onset severe dyspnoea – May suggest pulmonary embolism or large pleural effusion causing respiratory compromise.
  • Chest pain radiating to the back or arm – Can suggest thoracic malignancy or a complicated infectious process.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Pleural Effusion"][/sc]
  • Minimal or absent respiratory symptoms – In slow-developing effusions, such as those caused by heart failure, symptoms may be mild or absent.
  • Fever and chills – More common in parapneumonic effusions or empyema, which are bacterial infections of the pleural space.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask pleural effusion.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Pleural Effusion"][/sc]
  • Congestive Heart Failure – Often causes bilateral pleural - distinguish by focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.
  • Pulmonary Embolism – May present with sudden-onset dyspnoea - distinguish by focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.
  • Pneumonia – Can cause parapneumonic effusion or empyema - distinguish by focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.
  • Malignancy – Typically presents with a unilateral effusion - distinguish by focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.
  • Cirrhosis – Causes hepatic hydrothorax, often right-sided. Ascites - distinguish by focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Pleural Effusion"][/sc]
  • Empyema – Infection of the pleural fluid, often requiring prolonged antibiotics and drainage.
  • Fibrosis and trapped lung – Chronic effusion or infection can lead to pleural thickening, restricting lung expansion.
  • Respiratory failure – In large or rapidly accumulating effusions, respiratory compromise can occur, necessitating urgent intervention.
