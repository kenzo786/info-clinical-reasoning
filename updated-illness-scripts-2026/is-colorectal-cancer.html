Colorectal Cancer
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Colorectal Cancer"][/sc]
  • Colorectal cancer is a malignant neoplasm that arises from the lining of the colon or rectum.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise colorectal features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.

Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Colorectal Cancer"][/sc]
  • Colorectal cancer typically begins as benign adenomatous polyps, which accumulate mutations in key oncogenes (e.g., KRAS) and tumour suppressor genes (e.g., APC, TP53). These mutations lead to the progression from normal mucosa to dysplasia and, ultimately, invasive carcinoma.
  • Early-stage cancer is often confined to the mucosa and submucosa, but as the tumour grows, it invades deeper into the bowel wall, potentially breaching the muscularis propria. This can lead to local invasion into adjacent organs, lymphatic spread, and haematogenous metastasis, particularly to the liver and lungs.
  • Microsatellite instability (MSI) and chromosomal instability are two common pathways in colorectal cancer development. MSI is associated with Lynch syndrome, while chromosomal instability is more common in sporadic cases.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Colorectal Cancer"][/sc]
  • Early Phase: The development of colorectal cancer is insidious and often asymptomatic. The transformation from adenomatous polyp to carcinoma can take 10-15 years.
  • Intermediate Phase: Symptoms such as changes in bowel habits, rectal bleeding, and abdominal discomfort may arise as the tumour grows.
  • Late Phase: Advanced disease can present with weight loss, anaemia, bowel obstruction, or metastasis to organs like the liver or lungs.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Colorectal Cancer"][/sc]
  • Ask about rectal bleeding - Caused by mucosal disruption and ulceration of the tumour. Bleeding can be occult or overt.
  • Ask about change in bowel habits - Alternating constipation and diarrhoea occur due to tumour obstruction or irritation of the bowel wall.
  • Ask about abdominal pain - Often caused by tumour growth leading to partial obstruction or local invasion.
  • Ask about unintentional weight loss - Indicative of advanced disease or systemic malignancy.
  • Ask about fatigue - Often a result of chronic blood loss and iron deficiency anaemia.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Colorectal Cancer"][/sc]
  • Check pallor - Due to iron deficiency anaemia from chronic blood loss.
  • Check abdominal mass - A palpable mass may indicate locally advanced or metastatic disease.
  • Check hepatomegaly - Can be a sign of liver metastasis.
  • Check obstructive bowel signs - Including distension and hyperactive bowel sounds, suggestive of a mechanical obstruction.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Colorectal Cancer"][/sc]
  • Unexplained rectal bleeding - Especially in those over 50 years, indicates possible malignancy.
  • Iron deficiency anaemia - In older adults, this raises concern for chronic blood loss due to colorectal cancer.
  • Change in bowel habits lasting more than six weeks - Especially alternating diarrhoea and constipation or new onset constipation, suggests obstructive pathology.
  • Unintentional weight loss - Indicates systemic involvement or metastasis.
  • Abdominal mass or pain - A mass could indicate advanced local disease, while pain may signify bowel obstruction or peritoneal involvement.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Colorectal Cancer"][/sc]
  • Right-sided colorectal cancer - May present only with fatigue and anaemia without overt gastrointestinal symptoms, due to slow occult bleeding.
  • Rectal cancer - Can present with tenesmus or a feeling of incomplete evacuation rather than overt bleeding.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features or comorbidity can mask the underlying diagnosis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Colorectal Cancer"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Faecal immunochemical test (FIT) - Detects blood in stool. Used for screening and diagnostic purposes. Positive results suggest occult bleeding, potentially.
  • Targeted and monitoring: CT colonography - Used in patients who cannot undergo colonoscopy. Can detect masses or polyps, particularly larger tumours; CT scan (chest.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Colorectal Cancer"][/sc]
  • Haemorrhoids - Unlike colorectal cancer, bleeding is often associated with defecation, and the blood is bright.
  • Diverticular disease - Can present with bleeding or changes in bowel habits but lacks the systemic symptoms.
  • Inflammatory bowel disease - Differentiate from colorectal cancer using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.
  • Ischaemic colitis - Differentiate from colorectal cancer using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Colorectal Cancer"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Assess physiological stability and urgent complications at presentation, and initiate early stabilisation where required.
  • Core care: Deliver condition-specific treatment for colorectal cancer, address underlying drivers, and involve appropriate specialist support.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Reassess symptom trajectory, objective markers, and treatment response over time to detect complications early.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Worsening symptoms, physiological deterioration, or failure to improve should trigger urgent reassessment for severe or alternative pathology in colorectal cancer.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Colorectal Cancer"][/sc]
  • Bowel obstruction - Due to tumour growth causing luminal narrowing.
  • Perforation - May occur if the tumour erodes through the bowel wall.
  • Liver metastasis - The most common site of distant spread due to venous drainage of the colon through the portal circulation.
  • Prognosis - Overall 5-year survival for colorectal cancer in the UK is approximately 60%. Early detection significantly improves survival rates.
