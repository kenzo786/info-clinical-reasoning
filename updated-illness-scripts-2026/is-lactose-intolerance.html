Lactose Intolerance
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Lactose Intolerance"][/sc]
  • Lactose intolerance is a digestive disorder caused by the inability to digest lactose, a sugar found in milk and dairy products.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise lactose features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.

  • Risk factors: Risk factors for lactose intolerance include relevant predisposition, comorbidity, and exposure history.
  • Higher-risk or missed groups: Lactose Intolerance may be missed when presentation is atypical or obscured by comorbidity.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Lactose Intolerance"][/sc]
  • In lactose intolerance, the deficiency of the enzyme lactase in the small intestine impairs the breakdown of lactose into glucose and galactose. As a result, undigested lactose passes into the colon, where it is fermented by gut bacteria. This fermentation produces gas (hydrogen, methane, and carbon dioxide) and osmotic effects, drawing water into the colon, which leads to bloating, diarrhoea, and flatulence.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Lactose Intolerance"][/sc]
  • Acute Phase: Symptoms typically begin 30 minutes to 2 hours after consuming lactose-containing foods.
  • Chronic Phase: If lactose consumption continues, symptoms persist with varying intensity depending on the amount of lactose ingested.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Lactose Intolerance"][/sc]
  • Ask about bloating - Caused by gas produced during the fermentation of undigested lactose in the colon.
  • Ask about diarrhoea - Osmotic effect of lactose draws water into the bowel, leading to loose stools.
  • Ask about flatulence - Excess gas from bacterial fermentation of lactose results in increased flatulence.
  • Ask about abdominal pain - Cramping or discomfort due to gas and increased bowel motility.
  • Ask about nausea - Sometimes occurs, especially in severe cases or when large amounts of lactose are consumed.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Lactose Intolerance"][/sc]
  • Check abdominal distension - Visible bloating due to gas accumulation.
  • Check borborygmi (stomach rumbling) - Audible bowel sounds due to gas and fluid movement in the intestines.
  • Check watery stools - Seen during diarrhoeal episodes from the osmotic effect of unabsorbed lactose.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Lactose Intolerance"][/sc]
  • Weight loss - May indicate malabsorption or another underlying gastrointestinal condition.
  • Blood in stools - Suggests an alternative diagnosis, such as inflammatory bowel disease (IBD) or colorectal cancer.
  • Persistent vomiting - May indicate a more serious gastrointestinal obstruction or disorder.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Lactose Intolerance"][/sc]
  • Chronic fatigue - Lactose intolerance may rarely present with nonspecific symptoms such as fatigue due to malabsorption of nutrients.
  • Minimal or no gastrointestinal symptoms - Some individuals may have only mild bloating or intermittent diarrhoea, which can be mistaken for irritable bowel syndrome (IBS).
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask lactose intolerance.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Lactose Intolerance"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Hydrogen breath test - Elevated hydrogen levels after ingesting lactose indicate bacterial fermentation of undigested lactose in the colon. Hydrogen levels.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Lactose tolerance test - Blood glucose levels are measured after ingesting lactose. A lack of significant rise in glucose (<1.1 mmol/L).
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Lactose Intolerance"][/sc]
  • A 30-year-old male of Asian descent presents with abdominal bloating, diarrhoea, and flatulence after consuming dairy products for the past 6 months. Symptoms resolve on avoiding milk products.
  • Patient with abdominal bloating, diarrhoea, and flatulence after consuming dairy products for the past 6 months. Symptoms resolve on avoiding milk products, consistent with lactose intolerance.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Lactose Intolerance"][/sc]
  • Irritable bowel syndrome (IBS) - IBS presents with bloating and diarrhoea but may also include constipation and does not.
  • Inflammatory bowel disease (IBD) - Distinguish from lactose intolerance using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.
  • Coeliac disease - Coeliac disease causes malabsorption and presents with diarrhoea, weight loss, and fatigue. It improves.
  • Small intestinal bacterial overgrowth (SIBO) - SIBO may cause bloating, diarrhoea, and flatulence, but hydrogen breath test shows abnormal patterns.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Lactose Intolerance"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Assess severity, physiological stability, and immediate complications.
  • Core care: Deliver condition-specific management for lactose intolerance, treat underlying drivers, and coordinate appropriate specialist input.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Reassess symptom trajectory, examination findings, and objective markers to confirm response in lactose intolerance.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Escalate urgently for clinical deterioration, evolving red flags, or failure to improve in suspected lactose intolerance.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Lactose Intolerance"][/sc]
  • Nutritional deficiencies - Long-term avoidance of dairy can lead to deficiencies in calcium and vitamin D, increasing the risk of osteoporosis.
  • Secondary malnutrition - In severe cases, chronic diarrhoea may contribute to broader malnutrition if lactose intolerance is misdiagnosed or untreated.
