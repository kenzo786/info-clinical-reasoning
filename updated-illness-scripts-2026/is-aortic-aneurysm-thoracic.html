Aortic Aneurysm (Thoracic)
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Aortic Aneurysm (Thoracic)"][/sc]
  • A thoracic aortic aneurysm (TAA) is a pathological dilation of the thoracic portion of the aorta, typically defined as an aortic diameter ≥ 1.5 times the normal size.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise aortic features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Aortic Aneurysm (Thoracic)"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Aortic Aneurysm (Thoracic)"][/sc]
  • Male predominance, with a ratio of around 2:1.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Aortic Aneurysm (Thoracic)"][/sc]
  • Structural weakening of the aortic wall occurs due to degeneration of the elastic and smooth muscle layers, primarily from atherosclerosis, genetic factors (e.g., Marfan syndrome), or inflammation (e.g., syphilis, vasculitis).
  • This results in progressive dilation, which may eventually rupture due to the wall's inability to sustain the high-pressure arterial blood flow.
  • Risk factors for the degeneration include hypertension, smoking, and genetic conditions affecting connective tissue integrity.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Aortic Aneurysm (Thoracic)"][/sc]
  • Slow, asymptomatic enlargement over months to years.
  • Symptoms may develop with significant dilation or rupture, typically over a few days to weeks.
  • Rupture often occurs suddenly, leading to haemodynamic collapse within hours if untreated.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Aortic Aneurysm (Thoracic)"][/sc]
  • Ask about asymptomatic - The majority of TAAs are detected incidentally during imaging for other reasons.
  • Ask about chest pain - May be described as sharp or tearing, often radiating to the back; due to stretching or impending rupture of the aortic.
  • Ask about hoarseness - Caused by compression of the recurrent laryngeal nerve.
  • Ask about dyspnoea - Compression of the trachea or bronchi can result in difficulty breathing.
  • Ask about cough - Irritation or compression of the tracheobronchial tree.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Aortic Aneurysm (Thoracic)"][/sc]
  • Check hypertension - Often present due to coexisting atherosclerosis or contributing to aneurysm formation.
  • Check distended neck veins - May indicate superior vena cava compression by the aneurysm.
  • Check tracheal deviation - A large aneurysm can shift mediastinal structures.
  • Check bruits - A systolic murmur or bruit may be audible due to turbulent flow through a dilated aorta.
  • Check aortic regurgitation murmur - May result from aortic root dilation causing valve incompetence.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Aortic Aneurysm (Thoracic)"][/sc]
  • Sudden onset of severe chest/back pain - Suggests impending rupture, a medical emergency.
  • Syncope - May indicate rupture with haemodynamic compromise.
  • Hypotension with pulsatile mass - Indicates rupture or dissection with haemorrhage.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Aortic Aneurysm (Thoracic)"][/sc]
  • Isolated hoarseness - May be missed as an early sign of aneurysm compressing the recurrent laryngeal nerve.
  • Recurrent pulmonary infections - Due to tracheal compression, but not typically linked to aneurysm at first glance.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Aortic Aneurysm (Thoracic)"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: CT Angiography - The gold standard for diagnosis. Shows the size and extent of the aneurysm. Dilation ≥5.5 cm is significant.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Targeted investigations to confirm diagnosis, assess complications, and monitor response in aortic aneurysm (thoracic).
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Aortic Aneurysm (Thoracic)"][/sc]
  • A 67-year-old male with a history of hypertension and smoking presents with sudden severe chest pain radiating to the back. On examination, there is a systolic murmur, and imaging reveals.
  • Adult with symptom pattern and objective findings suggestive of aortic aneurysm (thoracic), requiring focused confirmation and risk stratification.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Aortic Aneurysm (Thoracic)"][/sc]
  • Aortic Dissection - Distinguished by a more abrupt onset of pain and a "tearing" sensation.
  • Myocardial Infarction - May present similarly with chest pain, but usually with ECG changes and elevated troponins.
  • Pulmonary Embolism - Typically associated with pleuritic chest pain, tachycardia, and dyspnoea.
  • Mediastinal Mass - Can compress surrounding structures, but lacks the acute nature of an aneurysm.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Aortic Aneurysm (Thoracic)"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Assess physiological stability and urgent complications at presentation, and initiate early stabilisation where required.
  • Core care: Deliver condition-specific treatment for aortic aneurysm (thoracic), address underlying drivers, and involve appropriate specialist support.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Reassess symptom trajectory, objective markers, and treatment response over time to detect complications early.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Worsening symptoms, physiological deterioration, or failure to improve should trigger urgent reassessment for severe or alternative pathology in aortic aneurysm (thoracic).
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Aortic Aneurysm (Thoracic)"][/sc]
  • Rupture - Life-threatening haemorrhage with a mortality rate >80% if untreated.
  • Aortic Dissection - Can complicate a large aneurysm, leading to acute pain, hypotension, and organ ischaemia.
  • Aortic Regurgitation - May occur due to dilation of the aortic root, leading to heart failure symptoms.
