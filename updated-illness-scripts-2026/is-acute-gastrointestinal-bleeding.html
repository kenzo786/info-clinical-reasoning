Acute Gastrointestinal Bleeding
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Acute Gastrointestinal Bleeding"][/sc]
  • Acute gastrointestinal bleeding is brisk blood loss from the gastrointestinal tract presenting with haematemesis, melaena, or haematochezia and potential haemodynamic instability.
  • UKMLA essentials: overt GI blood loss pattern, shock red flags, urgent resuscitation and source control.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Acute Gastrointestinal Bleeding"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Acute Gastrointestinal Bleeding"][/sc]
  • Acute GI bleeding is a frequent emergency admission, particularly in older adults and patients with comorbidity.
  • Risk is increased by peptic ulcer disease, portal hypertension, anticoagulant or antiplatelet exposure, and chronic liver disease.
  • Presentation may be subtle in frail patients, where hypotension and anaemia can precede obvious bleeding history.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Acute Gastrointestinal Bleeding"][/sc]
  • Bleeding follows mucosal injury or vascular disruption from ulceration, variceal hypertension, inflammation, or structural lesions.
  • Intravascular volume loss reduces tissue perfusion and can trigger compensatory tachycardia, then hypotension and shock.
  • Ongoing blood loss causes anaemia, organ hypoperfusion, and rising risk of multisystem failure without source control.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Acute Gastrointestinal Bleeding"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Bleeding may begin abruptly with visible blood loss or dark stool change over hours.
  • Progressive phase: Persistent haemorrhage rapidly causes circulatory compromise, requiring urgent haemostatic intervention.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Acute Gastrointestinal Bleeding"][/sc]
  • Ask about haematemesis - Fresh blood or coffee-ground vomit suggests upper GI source.
  • Ask about melaena - Black tarry stool usually indicates digested upper GI blood.
  • Ask about haematochezia - Bright rectal blood suggests lower source or very brisk upper tract bleeding.
  • Ask about syncope or dizziness - Presyncope indicates significant acute blood loss.
  • Ask about ulcer and liver history - NSAID exposure, alcohol-related liver disease, and prior bleeding episodes alter likely source.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Acute Gastrointestinal Bleeding"][/sc]
  • Check haemodynamic status - Tachycardia, hypotension, and postural drop indicate clinically important blood loss.
  • Check perfusion markers - Cool peripheries, reduced urine output, or altered cognition suggest shock physiology.
  • Check abdominal and rectal findings - Abdominal tenderness, melaena, or fresh rectal blood helps localise source.
  • Check chronic liver disease stigmata - Portal hypertension signs raise concern for variceal bleeding.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Acute Gastrointestinal Bleeding"][/sc]
  • Persistent hypotension or shock despite fluid resuscitation - Suggests ongoing major haemorrhage requiring urgent definitive control.
  • Large-volume haematemesis or brisk haematochezia - Indicates high-risk active bleeding with rapid decompensation risk.
  • Altered mental status with poor perfusion - Signals critical systemic compromise.
  • Evidence of end-organ dysfunction - Rising lactate, oliguria, or worsening acidosis indicates severe ongoing hypoperfusion.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Acute Gastrointestinal Bleeding"][/sc]
  • Occult blood loss pattern: Some patients present with unexplained anaemia and fatigue before overt bleeding is recognised.
  • Minimal abdominal symptoms: Significant bleeding can occur without pain, particularly in older adults and anticoagulated patients.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Acute Gastrointestinal Bleeding"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Urgent blood tests including full blood count, coagulation profile, renal profile, lactate, and group and save or crossmatch, plus serial observations and haemodynamic assessment.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Early endoscopic assessment for source diagnosis and haemostasis; CT angiography when bleeding source remains unclear or ongoing brisk lower tract bleeding is suspected; serial haemoglobin and perfusion monitoring.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Acute Gastrointestinal Bleeding"][/sc]
  • A 65-year-old man presents with 12 hours of haematemesis and melaena, with dizziness, tachycardia, and borderline hypotension after recent NSAID use.
  • Older adult with overt upper GI blood loss and early haemodynamic compromise, concerning for acute gastrointestinal haemorrhage.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Acute Gastrointestinal Bleeding"][/sc]
  • Peptic ulcer bleeding - Epigastric risk profile with haematemesis or melaena suggests ulcer-related upper tract haemorrhage.
  • Oesophageal or gastric variceal bleeding - Background liver disease with abrupt large haematemesis supports variceal source.
  • Diverticular bleeding - Painless brisk haematochezia in older adults is typical of colonic diverticular bleeding.
  • Ischaemic colitis - Lower GI bleeding with abdominal pain and vascular risk factors suggests colonic ischaemia.
  • Colorectal malignancy bleeding - Chronic change in bowel habit with recurrent blood loss and anaemia suggests malignant source.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Acute Gastrointestinal Bleeding"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Resuscitate with airway and circulation support, obtain blood products when needed, and reverse contributory coagulopathy where appropriate.
  • Core care: Deliver cause-directed haemostasis, usually endoscopic first-line, with radiological or surgical control for refractory bleeding.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Reassess haemodynamic status, stool or vomit blood loss pattern, and serial blood results to confirm stability.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Continued bleeding, haemodynamic deterioration, or organ dysfunction requires urgent senior multidisciplinary escalation.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Acute Gastrointestinal Bleeding"][/sc]
  • Hypovolaemic shock: Major uncontrolled blood loss can cause circulatory collapse.
  • Acute organ injury: Sustained hypoperfusion can result in renal, cardiac, or cerebral dysfunction.
  • Rebleeding and mortality risk: Inadequate source control increases recurrence and adverse outcome risk.
