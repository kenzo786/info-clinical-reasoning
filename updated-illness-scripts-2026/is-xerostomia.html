Xerostomia (Dry Mouth)
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Xerostomia (Dry Mouth)"][/sc]

Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Xerostomia (Dry Mouth)"][/sc]
  • Saliva production is regulated by both the parasympathetic and sympathetic nervous systems. Stimulation of the parasympathetic nerves (via acetylcholine) increases the production of watery saliva, while sympathetic stimulation produces thicker mucous-rich saliva.
  • Xerostomia occurs when the salivary glands either produce insufficient saliva or when the salivary composition is altered. Causes include salivary gland destruction, dysfunction, or inhibition through medications, dehydration, autoimmune diseases, or direct injury (e.g., from radiation therapy).
  • Drugs like anticholinergics, antihistamines, and diuretics inhibit parasympathetic stimulation, leading to reduced saliva production. Autoimmune diseases such as Sjögren's syndrome cause lymphocytic infiltration and destruction of the salivary glands.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Xerostomia (Dry Mouth)"][/sc]
  • Xerostomia can develop acutely, such as with medication use or dehydration, or chronically, as in cases of autoimmune conditions like Sjögren’s syndrome or after radiation therapy.
  • Chronic xerostomia can be insidious, often progressing over months to years, particularly in autoimmune diseases, and may be irreversible if gland damage is significant.
  • Acute cases may resolve with discontinuation of the causative agent or adequate hydration, whereas chronic forms usually require ongoing management.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Xerostomia (Dry Mouth)"][/sc]
  • Ask about dry sensation in the mouth - This is the hallmark symptom of xerostomia, resulting from inadequate saliva production due to glandular dysfunction or inhibition.
  • Ask about difficulty speaking or swallowing - The absence of saliva impairs the lubrication of the oral cavity, making it difficult to form words or swallow.
  • Ask about altered taste - Saliva is crucial for taste perception; reduced saliva affects taste receptor function, leading to dysgeusia (distorted taste).
  • Ask about burning or sore tongue - This may be due to the lack of protective saliva and subsequent irritation of the oral mucosa.
  • Ask about frequent thirst - A compensatory response to the body’s sensation of dryness and fluid loss.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Xerostomia (Dry Mouth)"][/sc]
  • Check dry, cracked lips - Due to the lack of moisture in the oral cavity, leading to dehydration of surrounding tissues.
  • Check dry and fissured tongue - The tongue may appear rough, with deep grooves due to the absence of protective saliva.
  • Check oral mucosal dryness - A visibly dry oral cavity upon examination, sometimes with a lack of pooling saliva.
  • Check dental caries - Saliva plays a critical role in protecting teeth from decay, and xerostomia predisposes to dental caries, especially at the gum line.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Xerostomia (Dry Mouth)"][/sc]
  • Unexplained weight loss - Could signify underlying systemic conditions like Sjögren's syndrome or malignancies.
  • Persistent oral ulceration - May indicate autoimmune conditions, oral malignancy, or complications from severe xerostomia.
  • Swollen salivary glands - Suggests underlying infection, obstruction, or autoimmune disease, such as Sjögren’s syndrome.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Xerostomia (Dry Mouth)"][/sc]
  • Absence of dry mouth sensation in elderly patients - Older adults may not report classic symptoms but present with dental caries or oral infections instead, possibly due to reduced sensory perception.
  • Intermittent symptoms - In cases of medication-induced xerostomia, symptoms may fluctuate depending on the timing of the drug.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask xerostomia (dry mouth).
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Xerostomia (Dry Mouth)"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Salivary flow rate test - Measures the quantity of saliva produced over time. A reduced flow rate (<0.1 mL/min unstimulated) indicates.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Sialometry - Quantifies salivary gland output. Abnormal findings suggest impaired salivary function; Schirmer's test - A test primarily for Sjögren’s syndrome.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Xerostomia (Dry Mouth)"][/sc]
  • Sjögren’s syndrome - Characterised by systemic dryness, including eyes and mouth, and often associated with systemic symptoms.
  • Medication-induced xerostomia - Distinguish from xerostomia (dry mouth) using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.
  • Diabetes mellitus - Polydipsia and dry mouth due to osmotic diuresis, often associated with polyuria and weight.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Xerostomia (Dry Mouth)"][/sc]
  • Dental caries - Without saliva’s protective effects, patients are prone to tooth decay, particularly along the gumline.
  • Oral infections (e.g., candidiasis) - Reduced salivary flow allows fungal overgrowth, leading to oral thrush.
  • Dysphagia - Difficulty swallowing due to a lack of lubrication may lead to malnutrition and weight loss.
