Guillain-Barré Syndrome
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Guillain-Barré Syndrome"][/sc]
  • Guillain-Barré syndrome (GBS) is an acute, immune-mediated polyneuropathy primarily affecting the peripheral nervous system, leading to rapid-onset muscle weakness and areflexia.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise guillain features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.

Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Guillain-Barré Syndrome"][/sc]
  • The pathogenesis of GBS involves an autoimmune response, typically triggered by a preceding infection. Molecular mimicry between microbial antigens (e.g., from Campylobacter or viral agents) and peripheral nerve components (such as gangliosides) leads to immune-mediated damage of the myelin sheath and, in some cases, the axon itself. This results in conduction block and progressive muscle weakness. The demyelinating form, known as acute inflammatory demyelinating polyradiculoneuropathy (AIDP), is the most common variant.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Guillain-Barré Syndrome"][/sc]
  • Initial phase (1-3 weeks): Progressive weakness, starting in the legs and ascending. Weakness may peak within 2-4 weeks.
  • Plateau phase (up to 4 weeks): Stabilisation of weakness, with no further progression but significant functional impairment.
  • Recovery phase (weeks to months): Gradual improvement of muscle strength, typically beginning 2-4 weeks after the plateau phase.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Guillain-Barré Syndrome"][/sc]
  • Ask about symmetrical, ascending weakness - Starts in the lower limbs and progresses upwards, explained by peripheral nerve demyelination disrupting motor nerve conduction.
  • Ask about paresthesia - Tingling and numbness in the extremities, due to sensory nerve involvement.
  • Ask about autonomic dysfunction - Fluctuations in blood pressure, arrhythmias, and bladder dysfunction, stemming from autonomic nerve involvement.
  • Ask about facial weakness - Bilateral facial palsy occurs in about 50% of cases due to cranial nerve involvement.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Guillain-Barré Syndrome"][/sc]
  • Check areflexia - Loss of deep tendon reflexes in the affected limbs, due to nerve conduction impairment.
  • Check symmetric muscle weakness - Generally flaccid paralysis in a distal-to-proximal pattern.
  • Check respiratory weakness - Diaphragmatic paralysis may occur, leading to respiratory failure, seen in severe cases.
  • Check cranial nerve involvement - Facial nerve weakness (bilateral) and dysphagia can be seen, particularly affecting the VIIth and IXth nerves.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Guillain-Barré Syndrome"][/sc]
  • Respiratory distress - Indicative of respiratory muscle weakness, may suggest impending respiratory failure.
  • Bulbar dysfunction - Difficulty swallowing and speaking due to cranial nerve involvement can lead to aspiration and requires urgent attention.
  • Autonomic instability - Severe fluctuations in blood pressure or arrhythmias signal autonomic nervous system involvement and may lead to life-threatening complications.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Guillain-Barré Syndrome"][/sc]
  • Miller Fisher syndrome - A variant of GBS that presents with ophthalmoplegia, ataxia, and areflexia, but without significant limb weakness.
  • Pure motor or pure sensory variants - Some patients may present with isolated motor or sensory involvement, potentially delaying diagnosis.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask guillain-barré syndrome.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Guillain-Barré Syndrome"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: CSF analysis - Elevated protein levels with a normal white cell count (albuminocytological dissociation), typically seen 1-2 weeks after symptom onset.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Nerve conduction studies (NCS) - Shows slowed conduction velocities and prolonged F-wave latencies, indicating demyelination; MRI spine - May show thickened.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Guillain-Barré Syndrome"][/sc]
  • Chronic inflammatory demyelinating polyradiculoneuropathy (CIDP) - Similar to GBS but with a slower, chronic progression (over weeks to months).
  • Myasthenia gravis - Fluctuating muscle weakness without sensory involvement, typically worsens with activity.
  • Spinal cord compression - Presents with motor and sensory deficits but often with a sensory level and hyperreflexia.
  • Related alternative diagnosis - Distinguish from guillain-barré syndrome using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Guillain-Barré Syndrome"][/sc]
  • Respiratory failure - Occurs in up to 30% of patients, requiring mechanical ventilation.
  • Autonomic dysfunction - Severe blood pressure fluctuations or arrhythmias may lead to sudden death.
  • Chronic weakness - Approximately 20% of patients have long-term residual weakness.
