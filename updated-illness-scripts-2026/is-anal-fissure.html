Anal Fissure
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Anal Fissure"][/sc]
  • Anal fissure is a linear tear in the anoderm, usually causing severe pain during and after defaecation with bright rectal bleeding.
  • UKMLA essentials: severe post-defecation pain, fever or perianal swelling concern, diagnose on careful anal inspection.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Anal Fissure"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Anal Fissure"][/sc]
  • Anal fissures are common, affecting 1 in 350 adults annually in the UK primary care population.
  • In hospital settings, they are less frequent but still significant, particularly in patients with chronic constipation or inflammatory bowel disease (IBD).
  • Worldwide, they are prevalent across all populations, with no significant gender difference, though they tend to occur more frequently in young to middle-aged adults.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Anal Fissure"][/sc]
  • The pathophysiology of anal fissures involves trauma to the anal mucosa, typically due to passing hard or large stools (common in constipation) or repeated episodes of diarrhoea. The tear exposes the underlying muscle (internal anal sphincter), leading to pain. This can result in a cycle of sphincter spasm, reduced blood flow to the area, and delayed healing. Chronic fissures may develop as a result of repeated trauma and poor perfusion to the wound site.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Anal Fissure"][/sc]
  • Acute phase: Lasts less than 6 weeks and involves sharp pain during defaecation, sometimes with bright red blood.
  • Chronic phase: Persists beyond 6 weeks, often characterised by a less intense but more constant pain, skin tag formation (sentinel pile), and fibrosis.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Anal Fissure"][/sc]
  • Ask about pain during defaecation - A sharp, burning pain felt during bowel movements due to the mucosal tear, worsened by the passage of hard stools.
  • Ask about rectal bleeding - Bright red blood on toilet paper or in the stool, a result of the tear in the highly vascularised anal canal.
  • Ask about chronic pain - With chronic fissures, pain may persist between bowel movements, due to ongoing inflammation and muscle spasm.
  • Ask about constipation - Often a consequence of pain avoidance behaviours leading to delayed defaecation, which exacerbates the condition.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Anal Fissure"][/sc]
  • Check visible tear in the anal canal - Typically in the posterior midline upon visual inspection.
  • Check sentinel skin tag - Chronic fissures often lead to fibrotic tissue at the distal end of the fissure, forming a skin tag.
  • Check increased anal sphincter tone - Palpable during a digital rectal exam, due to spasm of the internal anal sphincter.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Anal Fissure"][/sc]
  • Painless bleeding - Suggests a diagnosis other than anal fissure, such as colorectal cancer or haemorrhoids.
  • Persistent or recurrent symptoms - May indicate underlying pathology, such as Crohn's disease, which often presents with atypical fissures off the midline.
  • Systemic symptoms (fever, weight loss) - Suggests systemic infection or malignancy, which requires urgent evaluation.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Anal Fissure"][/sc]
  • Fissures off the midline - These are uncommon and often associated with conditions like Crohn's disease or syphilis.
  • Multiple fissures - Can occur in conditions like HIV, tuberculosis, or leukaemia, where immune compromise leads to poor wound healing.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features or comorbidity can mask the underlying diagnosis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Anal Fissure"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Clinical diagnosis by gentle inspection of the anal verge identifying a fissure, usually posterior midline, with associated sphincter spasm.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Further anorectal evaluation when fissure is atypical, recurrent, or non-healing to exclude Crohn's disease, malignancy, fistula, or other secondary causes.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Anal Fissure"][/sc]
  • A 35-year-old man reports sharp post-defecation anal pain with small-volume bright rectal bleeding for 3 weeks, and a posterior midline fissure is seen on inspection.
  • Adult with severe pain on defaecation and bright red bleeding from a visible midline anoderm tear, consistent with anal fissure.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Anal Fissure"][/sc]
  • Haemorrhoids - Bleeding with less intense post-defecation pain and haemorrhoidal cushions on examination suggests haemorrhoidal disease.
  • Perianal abscess - Constant throbbing pain, fever, and a tender fluctuant mass indicate abscess rather than fissure.
  • Crohn's perianal disease - Lateral or multiple fissures with diarrhoea, weight loss, or fistulating disease suggests inflammatory bowel disease.
  • Anal carcinoma - Persistent pain, bleeding, mass effect, or non-healing ulcer with systemic features warrants urgent exclusion of malignancy.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Anal Fissure"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Relieve pain and constipation while excluding perianal sepsis or secondary pathology in atypical fissure presentations.
  • Core care: First-line management combines stool-softening strategies, hydration and fibre advice, and topical vasodilator or local therapies as clinically indicated.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Review pain, bleeding, bowel habit, and fissure healing trajectory to confirm response and adherence.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Persistent severe symptoms, recurrent fissures, lateral lesions, fever, or non-healing wounds should prompt colorectal reassessment.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Anal Fissure"][/sc]
  • Chronic fissure formation - Leads to persistent pain and discomfort, and can result in fibrosis or the development of sentinel piles.
  • Infection - Rarely, fissures can become infected, leading to abscess formation or fistula development.
  • Faecal incontinence - A potential complication of surgical treatment, particularly sphincterotomy, due to the weakening of the anal sphincter.
