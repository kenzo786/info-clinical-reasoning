Generalised Anxiety Disorder (GAD)
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Generalised Anxiety Disorder (GAD)"][/sc]
  • Generalised Anxiety Disorder (GAD) is a common psychiatric disorder characterised by excessive, persistent worry about a variety of everyday activities and events, lasting for six months or more.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise generalised features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.

Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Generalised Anxiety Disorder (GAD)"][/sc]
  • GAD is believed to involve dysregulation of neurotransmitters, particularly serotonin, norepinephrine, and gamma-aminobutyric acid (GABA).
  • Functional and structural abnormalities in brain regions associated with fear and emotional regulation (e.g., amygdala, prefrontal cortex) play a role in heightened anxiety responses.
  • Chronic activation of the hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal (HPA) axis leads to stress hormone imbalance, contributing to physiological symptoms like muscle tension and fatigue.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Generalised Anxiety Disorder (GAD)"][/sc]
  • GAD is often a chronic condition, with symptoms developing gradually over months or years.
  • Episodes may fluctuate, with periods of improvement and exacerbation triggered by stressful events.
  • Without treatment, GAD can persist long-term, although symptoms may lessen with age in some individuals.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Generalised Anxiety Disorder (GAD)"][/sc]
  • Ask about excessive worry - Persistent, uncontrollable worry about various aspects of daily life; due to hyperactivity in fear-regulation centres in the brain.
  • Ask about restlessness or feeling “on edge” - Commonly described as feeling tense or unable to relax; related to chronic sympathetic nervous system activation.
  • Ask about muscle tension - Due to chronic stress and heightened HPA axis activity, often presenting as neck, back, or jaw tightness.
  • Ask about fatigue - Often due to sustained mental exertion from chronic worry and poor sleep quality; linked to neurotransmitter dysregulation.
  • Ask about difficulty concentrating - Due to hyperfocus on worries, leading to attention deficits in other areas.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Generalised Anxiety Disorder (GAD)"][/sc]
  • Check restlessness or fidgeting - Visible restlessness, such as pacing or tapping, due to heightened arousal and anxiety.
  • Check muscle stiffness - Palpable tightness in muscles, particularly neck and shoulders, due to chronic muscle tension.
  • Check hypervigilance - Patients may appear alert and hyperaware of surroundings; related to chronic anxiety and alertness.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Generalised Anxiety Disorder (GAD)"][/sc]
  • Suicidal thoughts or behaviours - May indicate severe anxiety or comorbid depression; urgent assessment needed to prevent self-harm.
  • Significant weight loss - Can suggest chronic stress response or indicate other medical causes of anxiety-like symptoms.
  • Substance abuse - May co-occur in attempts to self-medicate, requiring a comprehensive substance use history.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Generalised Anxiety Disorder (GAD)"][/sc]
  • Gastrointestinal symptoms - Such as nausea or irritable bowel symptoms, possibly due to anxiety-induced dysregulation of the enteric nervous system.
  • Generalised physical symptoms - Including chronic pain, especially in older adults, which may obscure the primary psychological symptoms.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask generalised anxiety disorder (gad).
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Generalised Anxiety Disorder (GAD)"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: GAD-7 questionnaire - A validated self-report tool; scores help assess severity of anxiety symptoms.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Thyroid function tests - Hyperthyroidism can mimic anxiety symptoms; TSH and T4 levels are useful in ruling out thyroid disease; Complete.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Generalised Anxiety Disorder (GAD)"][/sc]
  • Major Depressive Disorder - Can present with fatigue and concentration issues, but is more marked by pervasive low.
  • Hyperthyroidism - Overlaps with anxiety symptoms like restlessness and palpitations; differentiated by elevated T3/T4, suppressed TSH.
  • Panic Disorder - Episodic panic attacks rather than chronic worry; sudden onset of intense anxiety episodes.
  • Related alternative diagnosis - Distinguish from generalised anxiety disorder (gad) using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Generalised Anxiety Disorder (GAD)"][/sc]
  • Depression - Common comorbidity; anxiety can worsen depressive symptoms and increase risk of suicidal thoughts.
  • Substance use disorders - Patients may self-medicate, leading to dependency on substances such as alcohol.
  • Cardiovascular issues - Chronic anxiety has been associated with hypertension and increased risk of heart disease.
