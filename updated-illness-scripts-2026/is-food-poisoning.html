Food Poisoning
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Food Poisoning"][/sc]
  • Food poisoning is an acute gastrointestinal illness caused by ingestion of contaminated food or water.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise food features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Food Poisoning"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Food Poisoning"][/sc]
  • Risk factors: Risk factors for food poisoning include relevant predisposition, comorbidity, and exposure history.
  • Higher-risk or missed groups: Food Poisoning may be missed when presentation is atypical or obscured by comorbidity.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Food Poisoning"][/sc]
  • 1. Contaminated food or water introduces pathogens or toxins into the gastrointestinal tract.
  • 2. The pathogen may adhere to the intestinal lining or produce enterotoxins, causing local irritation and inflammation.
  • 3. Toxins or pathogens increase intestinal secretion of electrolytes and fluids, leading to diarrhoea.
  • 4. Some organisms invade intestinal mucosa, causing direct tissue damage and a systemic inflammatory response (fever, malaise).
  • 5. The immune system responds with neutrophilic infiltration, which further exacerbates inflammation and fluid loss.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Food Poisoning"][/sc]
  • Incubation period: The time from ingestion to symptom onset varies from a few hours (e.g., Staphylococcus aureus) to a few days (e.g., Salmonella or Campylobacter).
  • Acute phase: Symptoms such as diarrhoea, vomiting, and abdominal pain typically last 1–3 days.
  • Recovery phase: Most cases resolve within a week without complications, but fatigue and mild gastrointestinal symptoms may persist for several more days.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Food Poisoning"][/sc]
  • Ask about diarrhoea – Occurs due to the increased secretion of electrolytes and water into the intestine, often caused by bacterial enterotoxins or viral infections.
  • Ask about nausea and vomiting – Stimulation of the chemoreceptor trigger zone (CTZ) in the brain by toxins or the presence of pathogens in the gut.
  • Ask about abdominal pain/cramping – Results from intestinal spasms and inflammation of the gut mucosa.
  • Ask about fever – Systemic inflammatory response to certain invasive organisms (e.g., Salmonella, Campylobacter).
  • Ask about malaise – Generalised fatigue and discomfort due to the immune response to infection.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Food Poisoning"][/sc]
  • Check dehydration – Manifested by dry mucous membranes, reduced skin turgor, and tachycardia, due to fluid loss from diarrhoea and vomiting.
  • Check fever – Often present in bacterial or invasive infections due to the systemic inflammatory response.
  • Check abdominal tenderness – Localised or diffuse tenderness, particularly in cases of significant intestinal inflammation.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Food Poisoning"][/sc]
  • Severe dehydration – Indicated by hypotension, dizziness, or altered mental state; can lead to shock or acute kidney injury.
  • Bloody diarrhoea – Suggests invasive bacterial infections (e.g., Shigella, Campylobacter) or haemolytic uraemic syndrome (HUS).
  • High fever (above 38.5°C) – May indicate a more severe infection requiring hospitalisation.
  • Prolonged symptoms (over a week) – Could indicate secondary complications such as post-infectious irritable bowel syndrome or parasitic infections.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Food Poisoning"][/sc]
  • Asymptomatic carriers – Some individuals, particularly with Salmonella or E. coli, may harbour pathogens without symptoms, contributing to disease spread.
  • Neurological symptoms – Botulism may present with neurological signs (e.g., blurred vision, muscle weakness) instead of typical gastrointestinal symptoms.
  • Sepsis – In immunocompromised or elderly individuals, food poisoning may lead to bacteraemia and septic shock without severe gastrointestinal symptoms.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask food poisoning.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Food Poisoning"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Stool culture – Identifies bacterial pathogens (e.g., Salmonella, Campylobacter). This is indicated for bloody diarrhoea, prolonged symptoms, or immunocompromised patients; Stool.
  • Targeted and monitoring: FBC (Full Blood Count) – May show leukocytosis in bacterial infections, and thrombocytopenia in cases of HUS; Urea and Electrolytes –.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Food Poisoning"][/sc]
  • Viral gastroenteritis – More likely to cause watery - distinguish by focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.
  • Inflammatory bowel disease (IBD) – Chronic, relapsing symptoms - distinguish by focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.
  • Irritable bowel syndrome (IBS) – Chronic, functional bowel - distinguish by focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.
  • Peptic ulcer disease – Abdominal pain and nausea - distinguish by focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Food Poisoning"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Assess severity, physiological stability, and immediate complications.
  • Core care: Deliver condition-specific management for food poisoning, treat underlying drivers, and coordinate appropriate specialist input.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Reassess symptom trajectory, examination findings, and objective markers to confirm response in food poisoning.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Escalate urgently for clinical deterioration, evolving red flags, or failure to improve in suspected food poisoning.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Food Poisoning"][/sc]
  • Dehydration – Especially in children and elderly, can lead to acute kidney injury if untreated.
  • Haemolytic uraemic syndrome (HUS) – A complication of Shiga toxin-producing E. coli (STEC) infection, leading to thrombocytopenia, haemolysis, and renal failure.
  • Sepsis – Particularly in the elderly or immunocompromised, can occur due to invasive bacteria.
