Early Pregnancy
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Early Pregnancy"][/sc]
  • Early pregnancy refers to the initial stages of pregnancy, typically defined as the first trimester (up to 12 weeks of gestation).
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise early features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.

  • Higher-risk or missed groups: Early Pregnancy may be missed when presentation is atypical or obscured by comorbidity.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Early Pregnancy"][/sc]
  • Fertilisation occurs when a sperm and ovum meet in the fallopian tube. The zygote undergoes cleavage and travels to the uterus, forming a blastocyst.
  • Implantation occurs around day 5-6 post-fertilisation, involving invasion of the uterine endometrium.
  • Human chorionic gonadotropin (hCG) is secreted by the trophoblast, supporting the corpus luteum and maintaining progesterone production essential for pregnancy.
  • Rapid embryonic development occurs, including organogenesis and placental formation, primarily by week 10-12.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Early Pregnancy"][/sc]
  • Weeks 1-4: Fertilisation, implantation, and biochemical pregnancy.
  • Weeks 5-8: Early embryonic development and organogenesis.
  • Weeks 9-12: Consolidation of placental function and continued fetal growth.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Early Pregnancy"][/sc]
  • Ask about missed period: Absence of menstruation is the hallmark symptom, due to maintained progesterone levels from the corpus luteum.
  • Ask about nausea and vomiting: Often called "morning sickness," this is linked to rising hCG levels.
  • Ask about breast tenderness: Hormonal changes, particularly increased oestrogen and progesterone, cause breast tissue changes.
  • Ask about fatigue: Likely due to metabolic changes and increased progesterone levels.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Early Pregnancy"][/sc]
  • Check positive pregnancy test: Detection of hCG in urine or serum confirms pregnancy.
  • Check softened cervix (Goodell's sign): Increased vascularisation causes softening.
  • Check uterine enlargement: Detectable from approximately 6 weeks gestation during bimanual examination.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Early Pregnancy"][/sc]
  • Severe abdominal pain: May indicate ectopic pregnancy or miscarriage.
  • Heavy vaginal bleeding: Suggestive of threatened or inevitable miscarriage.
  • Dizziness or syncope: Can signal haemodynamic instability from significant bleeding, as in ectopic pregnancy.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Early Pregnancy"][/sc]
  • Asymptomatic early pregnancy: Particularly in women not actively tracking cycles.
  • Spotting: Confused with menstrual bleeding; implantation bleeding may mimic light periods.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask early pregnancy.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Early Pregnancy"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Urine hCG: Detectable approximately 14 days post-ovulation; confirms pregnancy.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Serum beta-hCG: Quantitative levels help in assessing viability or ectopic pregnancy. Levels should double every 48-72 hours in a normal pregnancy.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Early Pregnancy"][/sc]
  • Ectopic pregnancy - Distinguished by severe pain, lack of intrauterine pregnancy on ultrasound, and abnormal hCG rise.
  • Miscarriage - Presents with vaginal bleeding and cramping; ultrasound may show retained products of conception.
  • Hormonal irregularities - Lack of gestational sac and absence of pregnancy symptoms.
  • Related alternative diagnosis - Distinguish from early pregnancy using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Early Pregnancy"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Assess severity, physiological stability, and immediate complications.
  • Core care: Deliver condition-specific management for early pregnancy, treat underlying drivers, and coordinate appropriate specialist input.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Reassess symptom trajectory, examination findings, and objective markers to confirm response in early pregnancy.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Escalate urgently for clinical deterioration, evolving red flags, or failure to improve in suspected early pregnancy.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Early Pregnancy"][/sc]
  • Ectopic pregnancy: Occurs in 1-2% of pregnancies; risk of rupture and haemorrhage.
  • Miscarriage: Affects approximately 10-20% of known pregnancies; often idiopathic or due to chromosomal abnormalities.
  • Hyperemesis gravidarum: Severe nausea and vomiting can lead to dehydration and electrolyte imbalance.
