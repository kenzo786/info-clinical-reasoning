Tuberculosis (TB)
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Tuberculosis (TB)"][/sc]
  • Tuberculosis (TB) is an infectious disease primarily affecting the lungs (pulmonary TB) but can involve other organs (extrapulmonary TB).
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise tuberculosis features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Tuberculosis (TB)"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Tuberculosis (TB)"][/sc]
  • Risk factors: Risk factors for tuberculosis (tb) include relevant predisposition, comorbidity, and exposure history.
  • Higher-risk or missed groups: Tuberculosis (TB) may be missed when presentation is atypical or obscured by comorbidity.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Tuberculosis (TB)"][/sc]
  • Inhalation of *Mycobacterium tuberculosis* leads to alveolar infection.
  • The bacilli are engulfed by alveolar macrophages, where they resist destruction and multiply intracellularly.
  • An immune response forms granulomas to contain the infection. Latent TB occurs when the immune system suppresses the bacteria but doesn’t eradicate it.
  • In active TB, the granulomas break down, releasing bacilli, leading to tissue destruction and symptomatic disease, especially in the lungs.
  • Dissemination via blood or lymphatics can lead to extrapulmonary TB.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Tuberculosis (TB)"][/sc]
  • Initial infection: Often asymptomatic or mild flu-like symptoms. Most individuals enter the latent phase, which may last years or a lifetime.
  • Reactivation TB: Can occur months to years later, particularly in immunosuppressed individuals.
  • Active TB: Develops progressively, with symptoms worsening over weeks to months.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Tuberculosis (TB)"][/sc]
  • Ask about cough (chronic) - Persistent for >3 weeks, usually dry initially, becoming productive. Caused by lung parenchyma involvement and granuloma formation.
  • Ask about weight loss - Due to chronic inflammation and systemic effects of the infection, leading to catabolism.
  • Ask about fever - Intermittent or low-grade fever from the immune response to the infection.
  • Ask about night sweats - Common in TB, likely due to cytokine release and fever regulation disturbance.
  • Ask about fatigue - General malaise caused by the chronic inflammatory state.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Tuberculosis (TB)"][/sc]
  • Check cachexia (wasting) - Generalised wasting due to the prolonged inflammatory process and malnutrition.
  • Check lymphadenopathy - Especially cervical (scrofula), caused by local immune response to TB bacilli in lymph nodes.
  • Check crackles (lung auscultation) - Resulting from lung parenchymal involvement and cavitation.
  • Check clubbing - Can be seen in chronic pulmonary TB due to chronic hypoxia.
  • Check dullness to percussion - Indicative of pleural effusion or lung consolidation.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Tuberculosis (TB)"][/sc]
  • Haemoptysis - Can indicate advanced pulmonary disease with cavitation or erosion into blood vessels, signalling the risk of massive haemorrhage.
  • Pleuritic chest pain and dyspnoea - May indicate pleural TB or pneumothorax, both serious complications.
  • Altered mental state - Suggests CNS involvement (tuberculous meningitis), a life-threatening form.
  • Progressive weight loss - Indicates worsening disease or disseminated TB.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Tuberculosis (TB)"][/sc]
  • Extrapulmonary TB - Involves lymph nodes, bones (Pott’s disease), kidneys, or CNS, often without pulmonary symptoms. Can be missed if clinicians focus solely on respiratory complaints.
  • Latent TB - Asymptomatic; detected through screening in high-risk populations.
  • Miliary TB - Disseminated form of TB that can present with nonspecific systemic symptoms (fever, weight loss) and may lack respiratory symptoms.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Tuberculosis (TB)"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Sputum culture for Mycobacterium tuberculosis - Gold standard but takes weeks. Positive in active pulmonary TB due to bacterial growth; Chest.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Interferon-gamma release assay (IGRA) - Blood test used to detect latent TB by measuring immune response; Tuberculin skin test (Mantoux) .

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Tuberculosis (TB)"][/sc]
  • Lung cancer - Similar weight loss and haemoptysis but often associated with a smoking history; diagnosis via.
  • Pneumonia - Acute presentation with fever and productive cough, typically bacterial, resolved with antibiotics.
  • Chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD) - Productive cough and shortness of breath, history of smoking; no night sweats or weight.
  • Related alternative diagnosis - Distinguish from tuberculosis (tb) using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Tuberculosis (TB)"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Assess severity, physiological stability, and immediate complications.
  • Core care: Deliver condition-specific management for tuberculosis (tb), treat underlying drivers, and coordinate appropriate specialist input.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Reassess symptom trajectory, examination findings, and objective markers to confirm response in tuberculosis (tb).
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Escalate urgently for clinical deterioration, evolving red flags, or failure to improve in suspected tuberculosis (tb).
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Tuberculosis (TB)"][/sc]
  • Respiratory failure - Due to extensive lung destruction and fibrosis.
  • Haemoptysis - From erosion of bronchial arteries in cavitating lesions.
  • Pneumothorax - Due to rupture of a cavity into the pleural space.
  • Disseminated TB - Miliary TB with multi-organ involvement, especially in immunocompromised patients.
