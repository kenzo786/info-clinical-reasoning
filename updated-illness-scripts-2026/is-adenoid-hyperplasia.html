Adenoid Hyperplasia
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Adenoid Hyperplasia"][/sc]
  • Adenoid hyperplasia refers to the abnormal enlargement of the adenoid tissue, which is located in the nasopharynx.
  • UKMLA essentials: chronic nasal obstruction, severe obstructive sleep apnoea, nasal endoscopy.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Adenoid Hyperplasia"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Adenoid Hyperplasia"][/sc]
  • Adenoid Hyperplasia is encountered across acute and specialist care, with burden shaped by age, comorbidity, and exposure context.
  • Risk increases in patients with relevant predisposing disease, treatment exposures, or delayed access to care.
  • Missed or late recognition is more likely when presentations are atypical or overlap with common alternative diagnoses.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Adenoid Hyperplasia"][/sc]
  • The adenoids, like other lymphoid tissues, become hypertrophic in response to repeated exposure to infectious agents, allergens, or irritants. This hypertrophy is part of the immune response but, when excessive, leads to obstruction of the nasopharyngeal airway. Blockage of the nasal passages impairs normal airflow, causing mouth breathing and potentially contributing to chronic otitis media due to Eustachian tube dysfunction.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Adenoid Hyperplasia"][/sc]
  • Adenoid hyperplasia typically develops over months to years as the child encounters recurrent respiratory infections or allergen exposure. The symptoms can be persistent but fluctuate in severity depending on acute infections or exacerbations of nasal congestion. In many cases, adenoid hyperplasia resolves naturally as the adenoid tissue atrophies during adolescence.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Adenoid Hyperplasia"][/sc]
  • Ask about Chronic nasal obstruction - Caused by physical blockage of the nasopharynx, leading to mouth breathing and difficulty breathing through the nose.
  • Ask about Mouth breathing - Due to nasal obstruction, patients often breathe through their mouths, especially during sleep, which can cause dry mouth and snoring.
  • Ask about Snoring - Narrowing of the airway results in noisy breathing, particularly at night. This is often associated with obstructive sleep apnoea (OSA).
  • Ask about Recurrent ear infections - Eustachian tube dysfunction from adenoid enlargement can lead to middle ear effusion and otitis media.
  • Ask about Chronic rhinorrhoea - Increased mucus production as a result of chronic inflammation of the nasopharyngeal mucosa.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Adenoid Hyperplasia"][/sc]
  • Check Enlarged adenoids on examination - Direct visualisation with nasopharyngoscopy or endoscopy may reveal hypertrophic adenoids obstructing the airway.
  • Check Hyponasal speech - Speech may sound "nasal" due to impaired airflow through the nasal passages.
  • Check Facial deformities (adenoid facies) - In chronic cases, prolonged mouth breathing can result in long-term facial changes such as an elongated face, high arched palate, and malocclusion of teeth.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Adenoid Hyperplasia"][/sc]
  • Severe obstructive sleep apnoea (OSA) - This may present as frequent apnoeic episodes, failure to thrive, excessive daytime sleepiness, or behavioural problems. OSA in children can lead to cardiovascular complications if untreated.
  • Failure to thrive - Chronic sleep disturbances and difficulty eating due to mouth breathing can impair growth in children.
  • Chronic ear infections with hearing loss - Persistent middle ear infections can cause conductive hearing loss, affecting speech and cognitive development.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Adenoid Hyperplasia"][/sc]
  • Adult adenoid hyperplasia - Rare but can occur, often associated with chronic infections or allergic rhinitis. It may be missed because adenoid enlargement is unusual in adults.
  • Asymptomatic - In some children, adenoid hyperplasia may cause no significant symptoms, especially if the obstruction is partial and airflow is minimally affected.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Adenoid Hyperplasia"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Nasal endoscopy - Direct visualisation of the adenoids is the gold standard for diagnosing adenoid hyperplasia, revealing the degree of obstruction.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Lateral neck X-ray - May show enlargement of adenoidal tissue causing airway obstruction.; Sleep study (polysomnography) - Performed to assess the severity of obstructive sleep apnoea if present.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Adenoid Hyperplasia"][/sc]
  • A 5-year-old boy presents with chronic mouth breathing, snoring, and recurrent ear infections over the past year. Physical examination reveals hyponasal speech and an elongated facial structure suggestive of chronic mouth breathing.
  • Child with chronic nasal obstruction, mouth breathing, snoring, and recurrent otitis media, consistent with symptomatic adenoid hyperplasia.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Adenoid Hyperplasia"][/sc]
  • Allergic rhinitis - Presents with nasal congestion, sneezing, and rhinorrhoea but typically without chronic mouth breathing or snoring unless complicated by adenoid hypertrophy.
  • Tonsillar hypertrophy - May cause obstructive symptoms similar to adenoid hyperplasia but often accompanied by sore throat and visible tonsillar enlargement.
  • Chronic sinusitis - Nasal congestion, purulent nasal discharge, and facial pain predominate, often without the facial deformities or sleep disturbances seen in adenoid hyperplasia.
  • Choanal atresia or nasal mass - Persistent unilateral obstruction, epistaxis, or atypical endoscopic findings suggest structural pathology.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Adenoid Hyperplasia"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Assess airway and sleep-disordered breathing severity, and identify hearing or growth impact requiring earlier ENT review.
  • Core care: Optimise medical management for contributory rhinitis and consider adenoidectomy when obstruction, recurrent otitis media, or OSA remains clinically significant.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Review sleep quality, nasal breathing, hearing, and school-day functioning to guide timing of intervention.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Worsening OSA symptoms, poor growth, persistent hearing concerns, or recurrent severe infections should prompt urgent specialist reassessment.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Adenoid Hyperplasia"][/sc]
  • Obstructive sleep apnoea (OSA) - Chronic airway obstruction can lead to sleep disturbances and cardiovascular complications if untreated.
  • Facial deformities (adenoid facies) - Chronic mouth breathing may cause long-term structural changes to the face, affecting appearance and dental alignment.
  • Recurrent otitis media - Eustachian tube dysfunction increases the risk of chronic ear infections and subsequent hearing loss.
