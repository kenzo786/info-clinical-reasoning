Dizziness
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Dizziness"][/sc]
  • Dizziness is a non-specific term describing a sensation of disturbed spatial orientation, imbalance, or unsteadiness, often felt as a spinning (vertigo), feeling faint (presyncope), or general disequilibrium.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise dizziness features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.

  • Higher-risk or missed groups: Dizziness may be missed when presentation is atypical or obscured by comorbidity.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Dizziness"][/sc]
  • The sensation of dizziness can result from disturbances in one or more of the following systems:
  • Vestibular system: Peripheral vertigo (e.g., benign paroxysmal positional vertigo or BPPV) results from inner ear dysfunction, leading to abnormal signals about head positioning.
  • Cardiovascular system: Poor cerebral perfusion due to arrhythmias, orthostatic hypotension, or other causes can reduce blood flow, causing presyncope.
  • Neurological system: Central nervous system (CNS) issues such as stroke, migraine, or multiple sclerosis can impair sensory processing and balance.
  • Psychological factors: Anxiety or panic attacks may lead to hyperventilation, reducing cerebral oxygenation and causing lightheadedness or dizziness.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Dizziness"][/sc]
  • Acute (seconds to hours): Typically seen in vestibular causes such as BPPV or vestibular neuritis.
  • Subacute (hours to days): May be seen in vestibular migraine or post-viral labyrinthitis.
  • Chronic (weeks to months): Often indicative of a neurological or psychological component, e.g., anxiety-related dizziness or CNS pathology.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Dizziness"][/sc]
  • Ask about vertigo (spinning sensation): Often due to vestibular system involvement, such as BPPV or Meniere's disease, caused by abnormal signals in the inner ear.
  • Ask about lightheadedness (presyncope): Often from reduced cerebral perfusion, as seen in orthostatic hypotension or cardiac arrhythmias.
  • Ask about imbalance: Common in CNS disorders and ageing, due to impaired proprioception or vestibular input.
  • Ask about feeling of detachment or fogginess: Often associated with anxiety or hyperventilation-induced dizziness.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Dizziness"][/sc]
  • Check nystagmus: Involuntary eye movement, often seen in vestibular causes (e.g., BPPV), due to inner ear dysfunction affecting ocular motor reflexes.
  • Check positive Dix-Hallpike manoeuvre: Induces vertigo in patients with BPPV, due to otoliths shifting in the semicircular canals.
  • Check orthostatic hypotension: A drop in blood pressure upon standing, causing presyncope, common in cardiovascular causes.
  • Check ataxia: Unsteady gait often associated with CNS pathologies, such as cerebellar dysfunction.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Dizziness"][/sc]
  • Sudden severe headache: May indicate intracranial haemorrhage or stroke, requiring urgent evaluation.
  • Acute onset of unilateral weakness: Suggests possible stroke, especially in patients with risk factors.
  • Persistent vertigo with nystagmus: Could indicate a CNS lesion, e.g., a posterior circulation stroke.
  • Diplopia, dysarthria, or ataxia: Signs of brainstem or cerebellar involvement, seen in strokes or tumours.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Dizziness"][/sc]
  • Isolated imbalance without vertigo: May be mistaken for general unsteadiness; seen in cerebellar lesions.
  • Intermittent dizziness with no clear trigger: Often associated with anxiety-related causes or autonomic dysfunction.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask dizziness.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Dizziness"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Full blood count (FBC): Can reveal anaemia, which may cause dizziness; Electrocardiogram (ECG): Detects arrhythmias, common in presyncopal dizziness.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Dix-Hallpike manoeuvre: Diagnoses BPPV by provoking vertigo and nystagmus; Imaging (MRI or CT): Used to rule out CNS causes like stroke.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Dizziness"][/sc]
  • Benign Paroxysmal Positional Vertigo (BPPV) - Vertigo triggered by head movements, diagnosed by Dix-Hallpike.
  • Meniere’s disease - Recurrent vertigo, tinnitus, and hearing loss differentiate it from BPPV.
  • Vestibular neuritis - Acute, severe vertigo without hearing loss, lasting days.
  • Stroke - Presents with vertigo plus neurological deficits like weakness, ataxia, or dysarthria.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Dizziness"][/sc]
  • Falls and injuries: Increased risk due to imbalance, especially in elderly patients.
  • Psychological distress: Anxiety and depression due to persistent or recurrent dizziness.
  • Chronic disability: Long-term dizziness can limit mobility and daily functioning.
