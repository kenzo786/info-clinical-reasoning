Cervical Radiculopathy
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Cervical Radiculopathy"][/sc]
  • Cervical radiculopathy is a neurological condition caused by compression or irritation of a cervical spinal nerve root, leading to sensory, motor, or reflex changes in the corresponding dermatome or myotome.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise cervical features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.

  • Higher-risk or missed groups: Cervical Radiculopathy may be missed when presentations are atypical or comorbidity obscures key features.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Cervical Radiculopathy"][/sc]
  • Degenerative changes or trauma lead to intervertebral disc herniation or osteophyte formation.
  • These changes narrow the intervertebral foramina, compressing nerve roots.
  • Compression results in inflammation, altered nerve conduction, and ischemia.
  • Compression correlates to the level of the affected cervical vertebra, causing dermatomal or myotomal patterns of dysfunction.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Cervical Radiculopathy"][/sc]
  • Acute: Sudden onset, often related to trauma or disc herniation.
  • Subacute: Symptoms develop over weeks, often due to progressive degenerative changes.
  • Chronic: Persistent or relapsing symptoms over months to years, associated with chronic cervical spondylosis.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Cervical Radiculopathy"][/sc]
  • Ask about neck pain - Localised or referred pain due to inflammation and irritation of the affected nerve root.
  • Ask about radiating arm pain - Pain follows a dermatomal distribution, commonly in the shoulder, arm, or hand, due to nerve root compression.
  • Ask about numbness or tingling - Sensory deficits in the affected dermatome caused by altered sensory nerve conduction.
  • Ask about weakness - Muscle weakness in the myotome associated with the compressed nerve root.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Cervical Radiculopathy"][/sc]
  • Check reduced reflexes - Hyporeflexia in reflexes corresponding to the affected nerve root (e.g., biceps reflex for C5).
  • Check myotomal weakness - Weakness in muscles innervated by the affected nerve root (e.g., deltoid for C5).
  • Check sensory loss - Decreased sensation in the dermatome supplied by the compressed nerve root.
  • Check positive Spurling's test - Pain or paraesthesia reproduced by cervical spine extension and lateral rotation, compressing the foramina.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Cervical Radiculopathy"][/sc]
  • Progressive neurological deficit - May indicate spinal cord compression or myelopathy requiring urgent intervention.
  • Severe unremitting pain - Could signal a neoplastic or infectious process such as a tumour or spinal infection.
  • History of trauma - Suggests potential spinal fracture or instability.
  • Systemic symptoms - Fever, weight loss, or night sweats may suggest malignancy or infection.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Cervical Radiculopathy"][/sc]
  • Bilateral symptoms - May suggest central canal stenosis or myelopathy rather than isolated radiculopathy.
  • No radiating pain - Occasionally presents only as neck stiffness or non-specific arm pain, leading to diagnostic delay.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features or comorbidity can mask the underlying diagnosis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Cervical Radiculopathy"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Magnetic Resonance Imaging (MRI) - Identifies nerve root compression, disc herniation, or osteophytes; gold standard for diagnosis.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Electromyography (EMG) and Nerve Conduction Studies - Assess nerve function and confirm radiculopathy, distinguishing it from peripheral neuropathy; Plain X-rays .

  • A 45-year-old man presents with a 3-week history of progressive neck pain radiating to the right arm, associated with paraesthesia in the thumb and weakness in shoulder abduction.
  • Adult with symptom pattern and objective findings suggestive of cervical radiculopathy, requiring focused confirmation and risk stratification.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Cervical Radiculopathy"][/sc]
  • Shoulder impingement syndrome - Localised shoulder pain without neurological deficits.
  • Carpal tunnel syndrome - Median nerve distribution sensory loss and sparing of the neck and proximal arm.
  • Peripheral neuropathy - Diffuse symptoms not localised to a specific dermatome or myotome.
  • Myelopathy - Bilateral symptoms with upper motor neuron signs such as hyperreflexia and spasticity.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Cervical Radiculopathy"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Assess physiological stability and urgent complications at presentation, and initiate early stabilisation where required.
  • Core care: Deliver condition-specific treatment for cervical radiculopathy, address underlying drivers, and involve appropriate specialist support.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Reassess symptom trajectory, objective markers, and treatment response over time to detect complications early.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Worsening symptoms, physiological deterioration, or failure to improve should trigger urgent reassessment for severe or alternative pathology in cervical radiculopathy.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Cervical Radiculopathy"][/sc]
  • Chronic pain - Persistent symptoms leading to reduced quality of life.
  • Permanent neurological deficits - Untreated compression can lead to irreversible sensory or motor loss.
  • Myelopathy - Severe central compression leading to upper motor neuron signs and disability.
