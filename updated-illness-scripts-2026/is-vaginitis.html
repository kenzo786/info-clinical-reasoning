Vaginitis
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Vaginitis"][/sc]
  • Vaginitis is an inflammation of the vaginal mucosa commonly caused by infections such as bacterial vaginosis (BV), vulvovaginal candidiasis (VVC), and trichomoniasis.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise vaginitis features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.

Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Vaginitis"][/sc]
  • Vaginitis occurs when the normal vaginal environment is disrupted, often by a change in pH or microbial flora. In bacterial vaginosis, overgrowth of anaerobic bacteria like *Gardnerella vaginalis* replaces the normal lactobacilli. In vulvovaginal candidiasis, overgrowth of *Candida* species, typically *Candida albicans*, results in infection. In trichomoniasis, the protozoan *Trichomonas vaginalis* infects the vagina. Non-infectious vaginitis can result from allergic reactions, hormonal changes, or irritants.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Vaginitis"][/sc]
  • Acute vaginitis symptoms usually appear within a few days to weeks of exposure to the causative agent. Symptoms of bacterial vaginosis or trichomoniasis can develop within a few days, whereas vulvovaginal candidiasis may manifest more gradually. Chronic or recurrent vaginitis, especially with fungal infections or irritant-induced vaginitis, may persist or recur over months.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Vaginitis"][/sc]
  • Ask about abnormal vaginal discharge - In bacterial vaginosis, discharge is typically thin, grey, and malodorous ("fishy" smell), while in vulvovaginal candidiasis, discharge is thick, with associated clinical features.
  • Ask about vaginal pruritus - Common in vulvovaginal candidiasis due to inflammatory responses to the overgrowth of *Candida*. Itching can also occur in non-infectious vaginitis due.
  • Ask about dyspareunia - Pain during intercourse, seen in all forms of vaginitis, is due to inflammation and irritation of the vaginal mucosa.
  • Ask about burning sensation - Vaginal burning can result from inflammation of the vaginal tissue, commonly seen in candidiasis and irritant-induced vaginitis.
  • Ask about asymptomatic presentation - Up to 50% of cases of bacterial vaginosis and trichomoniasis may present without symptoms, particularly in the early stages, due, with associated clinical features.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Vaginitis"][/sc]
  • Check vaginal discharge - On examination, BV may show thin, grey, homogeneous discharge. Candidiasis is characterised by thick, white, clumpy discharge. Trichomoniasis may present, with associated clinical features.
  • Check vaginal erythema - Inflammation of the vaginal mucosa is common in candidiasis and irritant-induced vaginitis due to immune system activation.
  • Check vulvar excoriation - Scratching due to pruritus can lead to skin excoriation, particularly in vulvovaginal candidiasis or irritant vaginitis.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Vaginitis"][/sc]
  • Pelvic pain - May suggest pelvic inflammatory disease (PID), a complication of untreated bacterial vaginosis or trichomoniasis. This can lead to infertility if not treated.
  • Fever - Not typical in uncomplicated vaginitis but may indicate upper genital tract infection or systemic involvement.
  • Postmenopausal bleeding - Can suggest atrophic vaginitis or, in rare cases, malignancy. Should always prompt further investigation.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Vaginitis"][/sc]
  • Women with vaginitis caused by *Candida glabrata* may present with minimal discharge or pruritus, as this strain is less inflammatory compared to *Candida albicans*.
  • Chronic or recurrent vulvovaginal candidiasis can present with mild, persistent symptoms without the typical thick discharge, making it harder to recognise.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask vaginitis.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Vaginitis"][/sc]
  • Urinary tract infection (UTI) - Typically presents with dysuria and frequency, but without abnormal vaginal discharge.
  • Cervicitis - Inflammation of the cervix due to sexually transmitted infections (e.g., chlamydia or gonorrhoea) may.
  • Atrophic vaginitis - Occurs in postmenopausal women due to oestrogen deficiency, leading to dryness, itching, and dyspareunia.
  • Related alternative diagnosis - Distinguish from vaginitis using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Vaginitis"][/sc]
  • Pelvic inflammatory disease (PID) - Untreated bacterial vaginosis or trichomoniasis can ascend the genital tract, leading to PID, which can cause chronic pelvic pain and infertility.
  • Recurrent vaginitis - Repeated infections, particularly with candidiasis, can occur and may require longer courses of treatment.
  • Adverse pregnancy outcomes - BV and trichomoniasis during pregnancy are associated with preterm birth and low birth weight.
