Keratitis (Bacterial)
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Keratitis (Bacterial)"][/sc]
  • Bacterial keratitis is an infection of the cornea caused by bacterial pathogens, typically associated with corneal epithelial disruption.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise keratitis features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.

  • Risk factors: Risk factors for keratitis (bacterial) include relevant predisposition, comorbidity, and exposure history.
  • Higher-risk or missed groups: Keratitis (Bacterial) may be missed when presentation is atypical or obscured by comorbidity.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Keratitis (Bacterial)"][/sc]
  • Disruption of the corneal epithelium (e.g., from trauma or contact lens use) allows bacterial pathogens to invade the corneal stroma.
  • Bacterial toxins and host inflammatory responses cause keratocyte death, stromal degradation, and tissue necrosis.
  • Common pathogens include *Pseudomonas aeruginosa*, *Staphylococcus aureus*, and *Streptococcus pneumoniae*.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Keratitis (Bacterial)"][/sc]
  • Initial stage: Rapid onset within 24–48 hours of symptoms.
  • Progressive stage: Untreated, may lead to corneal ulceration and stromal necrosis within days.
  • Resolution stage: With appropriate treatment, symptoms improve within 1–2 weeks, but scarring may remain.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Keratitis (Bacterial)"][/sc]
  • Ask about severe ocular pain - Due to nerve stimulation in the corneal stroma by inflammatory mediators.
  • Ask about photophobia - Caused by inflammation affecting the iris and ciliary body.
  • Ask about blurred vision - Results from corneal oedema and irregular surface causing light scattering.
  • Ask about redness - Due to dilation of conjunctival and episcleral vessels.
  • Ask about tearing - Reflex tearing stimulated by corneal irritation.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Keratitis (Bacterial)"][/sc]
  • Check corneal ulcer - Localised defect seen on slit-lamp examination, representing stromal necrosis.
  • Check hypopyon - Layer of inflammatory cells in the anterior chamber, indicating severe inflammation.
  • Check conjunctival injection - Prominent redness due to vascular dilation.
  • Check decreased corneal sensation - Observed in advanced cases, indicating extensive damage.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Keratitis (Bacterial)"][/sc]
  • Progressive visual loss - Suggests impending corneal perforation or endophthalmitis.
  • Severe pain unresponsive to treatment - May indicate deeper ocular involvement.
  • Hypopyon - Signifies severe intraocular inflammation and risk of complications.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Keratitis (Bacterial)"][/sc]
  • Minimal redness or discomfort in immunocompromised patients due to blunted inflammatory response.
  • Chronic indolent course in cases with suboptimal contact lens hygiene or mild trauma.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask keratitis (bacterial).
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Keratitis (Bacterial)"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Corneal scrape for microscopy and culture - Identifies causative bacteria and guides antibiotic therapy.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Fluorescein staining - Highlights epithelial defects visible under blue light; Slit-lamp examination - Confirms corneal ulceration, infiltrates, and anterior chamber inflammation.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Keratitis (Bacterial)"][/sc]
  • Herpetic keratitis - Dendritic ulcer seen on fluorescein staining, minimal discharge.
  • Fungal keratitis - Gradual onset, satellite lesions, associated with plant trauma.
  • Acanthamoeba keratitis - Severe pain out of proportion to findings, ring infiltrate.
  • Related alternative diagnosis - Distinguish from keratitis (bacterial) using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Keratitis (Bacterial)"][/sc]
  • Corneal scarring - Leads to permanent visual impairment.
  • Corneal perforation - May result in secondary endophthalmitis and loss of the eye.
  • Glaucoma - Secondary to intraocular inflammation or steroid use.
