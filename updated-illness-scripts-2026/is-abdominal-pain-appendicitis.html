Appendicitis
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Appendicitis"][/sc]
  • Appendicitis is acute inflammation of the appendix, usually from luminal obstruction causing progressive local infection and peritoneal irritation.
  • UKMLA essentials: migrating periumbilical pain, peritonism or sepsis features, urgent surgical assessment with imaging.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Appendicitis"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Appendicitis"][/sc]
  • Appendicitis is one of the commonest causes of acute surgical abdominal pain, especially in adolescents and young adults.
  • Risk is higher with appendiceal obstruction from faecalith or lymphoid hyperplasia, and delayed presentation in pregnancy or older adults increases perforation risk.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Appendicitis"][/sc]
  • Obstruction of the appendix lumen by faecaliths, lymphoid hyperplasia, or foreign bodies triggers bacterial growth and localised infection.
  • The trapped bacteria cause mucosal injury and inflammation, leading to increased intraluminal pressure. This compresses blood vessels, causing ischaemia and further bacterial proliferation.
  • If untreated, the pressure may lead to rupture, releasing bacteria into the peritoneal cavity, resulting in peritonitis and potentially life-threatening sepsis.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Appendicitis"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Mild, poorly localised central abdominal pain with nausea.
  • Progressive phase: Pain intensifies and localises to the right lower quadrant (McBurney's point) over hours.
  • Late phase: Untreated inflammation may progress to perforation, local abscess, or generalised peritonitis over a short period.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Appendicitis"][/sc]
  • Ask about pain migration - Pain often starts centrally then localises to the right iliac fossa as parietal peritoneal irritation develops.
  • Ask about nausea or vomiting - Gastrointestinal upset commonly follows the onset of abdominal pain in acute appendicitis.
  • Ask about anorexia - Loss of appetite is a frequent accompanying symptom and supports an inflammatory intra-abdominal process.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Appendicitis"][/sc]
  • Check right iliac fossa tenderness - Maximal tenderness near McBurney's point supports localised appendiceal inflammation.
  • Check for peritonism - Rebound tenderness, percussion tenderness, or guarding suggests peritoneal irritation.
  • Check Rovsing's sign - Left iliac fossa palpation reproducing right iliac fossa pain supports peritoneal irritation from appendicitis.
  • Check temperature and pulse - Fever with tachycardia raises concern for complicated appendicitis or evolving sepsis.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Appendicitis"][/sc]
  • High fever and chills - Suggest evolving sepsis or developing intra-abdominal abscess from complicated appendicitis.
  • Severe generalised abdominal pain - Suggests perforation with diffuse peritoneal irritation rather than localised uncomplicated disease.
  • Tachycardia and hypotension - Indicate haemodynamic compromise from intra-abdominal sepsis requiring urgent escalation.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Appendicitis"][/sc]
  • Retrocaecal appendix: Pain may be less focal in the right iliac fossa and present as right flank or back discomfort.
  • Pelvic appendix: Irritation of bladder or rectum can cause urinary frequency, dysuria, diarrhoea, or tenesmus and mimic UTI or gastroenteritis.
  • Pregnancy: Gravid uterine displacement can alter pain location and reduce typical right iliac fossa findings.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Appendicitis"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: FBC and CRP for inflammatory response; urinalysis to exclude urinary causes; pregnancy test where relevant before imaging decisions.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Ultrasound as first-line in children, pregnancy, or gynaecological uncertainty; CT abdomen/pelvis when diagnosis is unclear or complications are suspected; serial abdominal examination and observations when the clinical picture is evolving.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Appendicitis"][/sc]
  • An 18-year-old man presents with 24 hours of abdominal pain migrating from periumbilical to right iliac fossa, with nausea, anorexia, fever, and focal tenderness on examination.
  • Young adult with acute migratory abdominal pain, gastrointestinal upset, and right iliac fossa peritonism, concerning for appendicitis with possible complications.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Appendicitis"][/sc]
  • Gastroenteritis - Diffuse cramping pain with prominent diarrhoea and less focal right iliac fossa peritonism.
  • Mesenteric adenitis - Often follows a recent viral illness in children and may mimic appendicitis with milder peritoneal signs.
  • Right ureteric colic - Colicky flank-to-groin pain with haematuria is more typical than progressive focal peritonism.
  • Ovarian torsion - Sudden unilateral pelvic pain with adnexal tenderness and gynaecological ultrasound findings distinguishes it.
  • Crohn disease flare or terminal ileitis - Subacute diarrhoea, weight loss, and recurrent episodes favour inflammatory bowel disease.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Appendicitis"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Recognise potential perforation or sepsis early, obtain prompt surgical review, and start supportive measures including analgesia, fluids, and antimicrobial cover when complicated appendicitis is suspected or the patient is systemically unwell.
  • Core care: Definitive treatment is usually appendicectomy, with non-operative management reserved for selected uncomplicated cases after senior surgical assessment.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Reassess pain trajectory, abdominal findings, observations, and inflammatory markers to confirm clinical improvement after intervention.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Worsening pain, persistent fever, vomiting, or systemic instability should trigger urgent reassessment for perforation, abscess, or an alternative diagnosis.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Appendicitis"][/sc]
  • Perforation: Delayed or severe inflammation can lead to perforation with local or generalised peritonitis.
  • Abscess formation: Localised collection of pus may develop and can require image-guided or operative drainage.
  • Sepsis: Untreated complicated appendicitis can progress to systemic infection with haemodynamic compromise.
