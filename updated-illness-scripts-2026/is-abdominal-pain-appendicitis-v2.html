Appendicitis
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Appendicitis"][/sc]
  • Appendicitis is acute inflammation of the appendix, usually from luminal obstruction causing progressive local infection and peritoneal irritation.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the classic presentation of migratory periumbilical to right iliac fossa pain, identify features of peritonism or sepsis, and know when to refer urgently for surgical assessment and imaging.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Appendicitis"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Appendicitis"][/sc]
  • Appendicitis is one of the commonest causes of acute surgical abdominal pain. Lifetime risk is approximately 8%, with peak incidence in those aged 10–19 years. It is slightly more common in males (male:female ratio approximately 1.4:1).
  • Risk is higher with appendiceal obstruction from faecalith or lymphoid hyperplasia. Delayed presentation — particularly in pregnancy, elderly patients, and those who are immunosuppressed — significantly increases perforation risk.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Appendicitis"][/sc]
  • Obstruction of the appendix lumen by faecaliths, lymphoid hyperplasia, or foreign bodies triggers bacterial growth and localised infection.
  • The trapped bacteria cause mucosal injury and inflammation, leading to increased intraluminal pressure. This compresses blood vessels, causing ischaemia and further bacterial proliferation.
  • If untreated, the pressure may lead to rupture, releasing bacteria into the peritoneal cavity, resulting in peritonitis and potentially life-threatening sepsis.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Appendicitis"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Mild, poorly localised central abdominal pain with nausea.
  • Progressive phase: Pain intensifies and localises to the right lower quadrant (McBurney's point) over hours.
  • Late phase: Untreated inflammation may progress to perforation, local abscess, or generalised peritonitis. Perforation risk rises with longer symptom duration, particularly beyond 48–72 hours.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Appendicitis"][/sc]
  • Ask about pain migration - Pain often starts centrally then localises to the right iliac fossa as parietal peritoneal irritation develops.
  • Ask about nausea or vomiting - Gastrointestinal upset commonly follows the onset of abdominal pain in acute appendicitis.
  • Ask about anorexia - Loss of appetite is a frequent accompanying symptom and supports an inflammatory intra-abdominal process.
  • Ask about fever - Low-grade fever is typical of early appendicitis; high spiking temperature suggests complicated or perforated disease.
  • Ask about the sequence of symptoms - Vomiting that precedes abdominal pain makes gastroenteritis more likely; in appendicitis, pain typically comes first.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Appendicitis"][/sc]
  • Check right iliac fossa tenderness - Maximal tenderness near McBurney's point supports localised appendiceal inflammation.
  • Check for peritonism - Rebound tenderness, percussion tenderness, or guarding suggests peritoneal irritation. Ask about pain worsening with movement or coughing — a simple but sensitive clue to peritoneal involvement.
  • Check Rovsing's sign - Left iliac fossa palpation reproducing right iliac fossa pain supports peritoneal irritation from appendicitis.
  • Check temperature and pulse - Fever with tachycardia raises concern for complicated appendicitis or evolving sepsis.
  • Check psoas sign - Pain on passive right hip extension suggests a retrocaecal appendix in contact with the iliopsoas muscle.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Appendicitis"][/sc]
  • High fever and chills - Suggest evolving sepsis or developing intra-abdominal abscess from complicated appendicitis.
  • Severe generalised abdominal pain - Suggests perforation with diffuse peritoneal irritation rather than localised uncomplicated disease.
  • Tachycardia and hypotension - Indicate haemodynamic compromise from intra-abdominal sepsis requiring urgent escalation.
  • Prolonged symptom duration - Perforation risk rises with longer symptom duration, especially beyond 48–72 hours; prioritise urgent imaging and surgical review.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Appendicitis"][/sc]
  • Retrocaecal appendix: Pain may be less focal in the right iliac fossa and present as right flank or back discomfort. Psoas sign may be positive.
  • Pelvic appendix: Irritation of bladder or rectum can cause urinary frequency, dysuria, diarrhoea, or tenesmus and mimic UTI or gastroenteritis.
  • Pregnancy: Gravid uterine displacement can alter pain location superiorly and reduce typical right iliac fossa findings. Maintain a high index of suspicion as perforation risk is higher.
  • Older adults: Classic features are often absent or blunted. Presentation may be non-specific (confusion, vague abdominal discomfort) with perforation already present at diagnosis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Appendicitis"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected appendicitis: FBC (raised WCC supports inflammation; normal does not exclude appendicitis), CRP (rises with severity and useful for monitoring), urinalysis (note: sterile pyuria can occur with pelvic appendix), and urine or serum pregnancy test in all women of childbearing age before imaging decisions.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: U&Es, LFTs, and amylase/lipase to help exclude biliary, renal, or pancreatic pathology in undifferentiated abdominal pain.
  • Imaging — ultrasound: First-line in children, pregnant patients, and where gynaecological pathology is suspected. Operator-dependent and may be non-diagnostic if the appendix is not visualised.
  • Imaging — CT abdomen/pelvis: High sensitivity and specificity; use when ultrasound is inconclusive, diagnosis is unclear, or complications are suspected. Consider radiation dose in younger patients.
  • Imaging — MRI: Preferred alternative to CT in pregnancy when ultrasound is non-diagnostic, to avoid ionising radiation.
  • Serial reassessment: Short-interval repeat abdominal examination, observations, and inflammatory markers are appropriate when the clinical picture is evolving or the diagnosis remains uncertain — typically in an urgent care or inpatient setting where safe observation can be maintained.
Clinical Scoring — Alvarado Score [sc name="ask-ai-109a" Q="Appendicitis"][/sc]
  • The Alvarado (MANTRELS) score is a 10-point clinical risk stratification tool, commonly referenced in UKMLA and MRCGP contexts. It is a reasoning aid, not a rule-in or rule-out tool.
  • Score components (total /10):
    • Migration of pain to RIF — 1 point
    • Anorexia — 1 point
    • Nausea or vomiting — 1 point
    • RIF tenderness — 2 points
    • Rebound tenderness — 1 point
    • Elevated temperature (>37.3°C) — 1 point
    • Leucocytosis (>10 × 10⁹/L) — 2 points
    • Neutrophil left shift — 1 point
  • Risk stratification: Score 1–4 is often associated with lower risk and may support discharge with clear safety-netting; Score 5–6 is intermediate and often warrants observation and reassessment; Score 7–10 is high risk and typically prompts urgent surgical review. Local pathways and senior clinical judgement should always take precedence over the score alone.
  • The score performs less well in children, pregnant patients, and older adults — groups where atypical presentations are common and the threshold for imaging should be lower regardless of score.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Appendicitis"][/sc]
  • An 18-year-old man presents with 24 hours of abdominal pain migrating from periumbilical to right iliac fossa, with nausea, anorexia, fever, and focal tenderness on examination.
  • Young adult with acute migratory abdominal pain, gastrointestinal upset, and right iliac fossa peritonism, concerning for appendicitis with possible complications.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Appendicitis"][/sc]
  • Gastroenteritis - Diffuse cramping pain with prominent diarrhoea and less focal right iliac fossa peritonism.
  • Mesenteric adenitis - Often follows a recent viral illness in children and may mimic appendicitis with milder peritoneal signs.
  • Right ureteric colic - Colicky flank-to-groin pain with haematuria is more typical than progressive focal peritonism.
  • Ovarian torsion - Sudden unilateral pelvic pain with adnexal tenderness and gynaecological ultrasound findings distinguishes it.
  • Ectopic pregnancy - Right-sided pelvic pain with amenorrhoea or irregular bleeding; exclude with pregnancy test and urgent ultrasound.
  • Pelvic inflammatory disease (PID) - Bilateral or right-sided lower abdominal pain in sexually active women, often with cervical excitation, vaginal discharge, and fever; absence of pain migration and a more gradual onset help differentiate from appendicitis.
  • Crohn disease flare or terminal ileitis - Subacute diarrhoea, weight loss, and recurrent episodes favour inflammatory bowel disease.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Appendicitis"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Recognise potential perforation or sepsis early and obtain prompt surgical review. Supportive care often includes analgesia, IV fluids, and antimicrobial cover when complicated appendicitis is suspected or the patient is systemically unwell, in line with local acute abdomen and sepsis pathways.
  • Operative management: Appendicectomy (laparoscopic preferred) remains the definitive treatment for most cases and is standard for complicated appendicitis or where non-operative management has failed.
  • Non-operative management: IV then oral antibiotics are an option for selected adults with uncomplicated appendicitis (no perforation, no appendicolith, no peritonitis). The APPAC trial (2015) showed approximately 73% success at 1 year; however, around 27% required appendicectomy within 12 months. Recurrence risk and the implications of delayed surgery should be part of shared decision-making. Antibiotic choice should follow local antimicrobial guidelines. This approach requires careful patient selection and senior surgical input — it is not appropriate for complicated disease.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Reassess pain trajectory, abdominal findings, observations, and inflammatory markers to confirm clinical improvement after intervention.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Worsening pain, persistent fever, vomiting, or systemic instability should trigger urgent reassessment for perforation, abscess, or an alternative diagnosis.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Appendicitis"][/sc]
  • Perforation: Risk rises with longer symptom duration, particularly beyond 48–72 hours. Can lead to localised or generalised peritonitis requiring emergency surgery and carries higher morbidity and mortality.
  • Abscess formation: Localised collection of pus may develop (peri-appendiceal or pelvic). Often managed initially with IV antibiotics and image-guided drainage, with interval appendicectomy considered.
  • Sepsis: Untreated complicated appendicitis can progress to systemic infection with haemodynamic compromise, requiring ICU-level care.
  • Stump appendicitis: Rare recurrence of appendicitis in residual appendiceal stump following incomplete appendicectomy — a diagnosis of exclusion in post-appendicectomy right iliac fossa pain.
