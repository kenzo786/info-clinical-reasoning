Pneumothorax
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Pneumothorax"][/sc]
  • Pneumothorax is the presence of air in the pleural space, which leads to partial or complete collapse of the lung.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise pneumothorax features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Pneumothorax"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Pneumothorax"][/sc]
  • Risk Factors: Tall, thin males aged 10-30, smoking (up to 22× risk increase), family history, and subpleural blebs contribute to primary spontaneous pneumothorax. Secondary causes include COPD, cystic fibrosis, tuberculosis, lung malignancy, and connective tissue disorders. Trauma, mechanical ventilation, and invasive procedures can also induce pneumothorax.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Pneumothorax"][/sc]
  • Pneumothorax occurs when air enters the pleural space, either due to a breach in the visceral pleura (lining the lung) or the parietal pleura (lining the chest wall). The negative pressure that usually keeps the lungs expanded is lost, leading to partial or complete lung collapse. In primary pneumothorax, this is typically due to the rupture of small subpleural blebs, while in secondary pneumothorax, it is often related to underlying lung diseases like COPD, where the structural integrity of the lung is compromised.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Pneumothorax"][/sc]
  • Acute onset: Symptoms typically develop suddenly, often over minutes to hours.
  • Untreated course: If untreated, a pneumothorax can persist for days or even weeks, depending on the size and cause. Small pneumothoraces may resolve on their own, but larger ones can progress to life-threatening complications like tension pneumothorax.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Pneumothorax"][/sc]
  • Ask about sudden onset of pleuritic chest pain: Caused by the stretching of the pleura as the lung collapses.
  • Ask about acute dyspnoea: Due to reduced lung volume and impaired ventilation.
  • Ask about cough: A non-productive, dry cough may occur due to irritation of the pleura.
  • Ask about asymptomatic: Especially in small pneumothoraces, symptoms may be minimal or absent.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Pneumothorax"][/sc]
  • Check reduced breath sounds on the affected side: Due to lack of air movement in the collapsed lung.
  • Check hyper-resonance on percussion: Caused by the presence of air in the pleural space.
  • Check decreased chest expansion: Especially on the side of the pneumothorax.
  • Check tachypnoea: Increased respiratory rate as compensation for hypoxia.
  • Check tracheal deviation (in tension pneumothorax): A late sign indicating increased intrathoracic pressure on one side, shifting the mediastinum.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Pneumothorax"][/sc]
  • Tracheal deviation away from the affected side: Indicates tension pneumothorax, a medical emergency that can cause cardiovascular collapse due to compression of the great vessels.
  • Hypotension: Suggests decreased venous return and possible tension pneumothorax.
  • Distended neck veins: A sign of elevated intrathoracic pressure in tension pneumothorax, impairing venous return to the heart.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Pneumothorax"][/sc]
  • Mild or no symptoms in small pneumothoraces: Particularly in young, healthy individuals, small pneumothoraces may not cause noticeable symptoms.
  • Misinterpreted as musculoskeletal pain: Pleuritic chest pain may be mistaken for muscle strain or costochondritis.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask pneumothorax.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Pneumothorax"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Chest X-ray: Shows a visible rim of air with the absence of lung markings in the area of pneumothorax. The lung.
  • Targeted and monitoring: CT scan: More sensitive for small or complex pneumothoraces, especially in secondary causes or trauma; Arterial blood gases (ABG): May show.

Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Pneumothorax"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Assess severity, physiological stability, and immediate complications.
  • Core care: Deliver condition-specific management for pneumothorax, treat underlying drivers, and coordinate appropriate specialist input.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Reassess symptom trajectory, examination findings, and objective markers to confirm response in pneumothorax.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Escalate urgently for clinical deterioration, evolving red flags, or failure to improve in suspected pneumothorax.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Pneumothorax"][/sc]
  • Tension pneumothorax: Life-threatening complication due to increased intrathoracic pressure, causing cardiovascular collapse. Requires emergency decompression.
  • Recurrent pneumothorax: Up to 30% of patients may experience recurrence, particularly if no preventive treatment like pleurodesis is performed.
  • Respiratory failure: In secondary pneumothorax, patients with pre-existing lung disease may experience significant hypoxia and respiratory compromise.
