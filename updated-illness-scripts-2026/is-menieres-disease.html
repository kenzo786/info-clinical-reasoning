Meniere's Disease
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Meniere's Disease"][/sc]
  • Meniere's disease is an idiopathic disorder of the inner ear characterised by recurrent episodes of vertigo, hearing loss, tinnitus, and a sensation of aural fullness.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise meniere features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Meniere's Disease"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Meniere's Disease"][/sc]
  • It most commonly presents between the ages of 40-60, with a slight female predominance.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Meniere's Disease"][/sc]
  • Meniere's disease is thought to be caused by an imbalance in the production and absorption of endolymph within the inner ear, leading to endolymphatic hydrops. Excess endolymph distends the membranous labyrinth, causing episodic dysfunction of the cochlear and vestibular systems. Vertigo arises from abnormal vestibular signalling, while hearing loss, tinnitus, and aural fullness are related to cochlear involvement. The exact aetiology is unknown, but viral infections, autoimmune processes, and genetic factors may play a role.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Meniere's Disease"][/sc]
  • Meniere's disease typically follows a relapsing-remitting course, with episodic attacks lasting 20 minutes to several hours. Early in the disease, episodes may occur sporadically, often months apart, but with disease progression, they may become more frequent. Hearing loss initially fluctuates but can become permanent over time.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Meniere's Disease"][/sc]
  • Ask about vertigo - Sudden, recurrent episodes of severe spinning vertigo lasting 20 minutes to several hours, caused by abnormal vestibular signalling due to endolymphatic hydrops.
  • Ask about hearing loss - Initially fluctuating, low-frequency sensorineural hearing loss during or between episodes. Progressive as cochlear damage accumulates due to endolymphatic pressure changes.
  • Ask about tinnitus - A constant or fluctuating ringing, buzzing, or roaring sound in the affected ear, often exacerbated during episodes of vertigo due to increased.
  • Ask about aural fullness - A sensation of pressure or fullness in the affected ear, attributed to the buildup of endolymphatic fluid in the inner ear.
  • Ask about nausea and vomiting - Common during vertigo episodes due to vestibular system dysfunction and its connection to the vomiting centre in the brainstem.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Meniere's Disease"][/sc]
  • Check spontaneous nystagmus - A key feature during acute vertigo episodes, resulting from vestibular imbalance.
  • Check positive Romberg test - Indicates vestibular dysfunction, with patients swaying or falling towards the affected side when eyes are closed.
  • Check hearing loss on audiometry - Early in the disease, low-frequency sensorineural hearing loss is noted, progressing to a broader frequency range as the disease.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Meniere's Disease"][/sc]
  • Sudden, persistent hearing loss - May indicate a more serious condition like sudden sensorineural hearing loss (SSNHL) requiring urgent intervention.
  • Severe headache, altered consciousness, or neurological deficit - Suggests a central cause of vertigo such as stroke or brainstem pathology, necessitating urgent imaging and specialist referral.
  • Facial numbness or weakness - Could be a sign of a vestibular schwannoma or other intracranial pathology pressing on the cranial nerves.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Meniere's Disease"][/sc]
  • Patients may present with isolated symptoms such as tinnitus or aural fullness without vertigo. In early stages, hearing loss may be mistaken for age-related changes, and vertigo attacks can be infrequent, leading to underdiagnosis.
  • Some patients may have bilateral symptoms, although unilateral involvement is more typical.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask meniere's disease.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Meniere's Disease"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Audiometry - Typically shows low-frequency sensorineural hearing loss early on, with progression to involve higher frequencies. This is due to damage.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Electrocochleography (ECochG) - May show increased summating potential to action potential ratio (SP/AP), indicating endolymphatic hydrops; MRI with contrast - Performed.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Meniere's Disease"][/sc]
  • Vestibular neuritis - Presents with acute, prolonged vertigo without hearing loss or tinnitus. No fluctuating symptoms like.
  • Benign paroxysmal positional vertigo (BPPV) - Short episodes of vertigo triggered by head movements, with no hearing loss or aural.
  • Acoustic neuroma (vestibular schwannoma) - Progressive unilateral hearing loss with or without vertigo, often accompanied by facial nerve symptoms.
  • Labyrinthitis - Acute onset of vertigo with hearing loss, often following an upper respiratory infection.
  • Migraine-associated vertigo - Associated with migraine headaches, photophobia, and phonophobia, with less prominent hearing loss and tinnitus.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Meniere's Disease"][/sc]
  • Permanent hearing loss - Progressive loss of cochlear function due to repeated endolymphatic hydrops episodes.
  • Falls and accidents - Due to vertigo-induced imbalance, leading to potential injuries.
  • Psychological impact - Depression, anxiety, and social isolation may result from the unpredictability and disabling nature of symptoms.
