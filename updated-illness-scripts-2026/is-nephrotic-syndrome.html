Nephrotic Syndrome
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Nephrotic Syndrome"][/sc]
  • Nephrotic syndrome is a renal disorder characterised by significant proteinuria (>3.5 g/day), hypoalbuminaemia (<30 g/L), oedema, and hyperlipidaemia.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise nephrotic features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.

Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Nephrotic Syndrome"][/sc]
  • Nephrotic syndrome occurs due to damage to the glomerular filtration barrier, which is composed of endothelial cells, the glomerular basement membrane, and podocytes. Injury to any of these structures (e.g., immune complex deposition, toxins, or genetic defects) increases permeability, allowing large proteins like albumin to pass into the urine. This protein loss reduces oncotic pressure, causing fluid to leak into tissues, leading to oedema. Hypoalbuminaemia also stimulates the liver to produce lipoproteins, resulting in hyperlipidaemia.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Nephrotic Syndrome"][/sc]
  • The onset of nephrotic syndrome can be sudden, especially in children with minimal change disease, or more gradual in cases of membranous nephropathy. Symptoms may develop over days to weeks, with worsening oedema and progressive proteinuria. If untreated, complications like infections or thromboembolism can develop within weeks to months.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Nephrotic Syndrome"][/sc]
  • Ask about oedema - Caused by a decrease in plasma oncotic pressure due to hypoalbuminaemia, typically presenting in dependent areas such as the ankles and around.
  • Ask about fatigue - Secondary to generalised oedema and decreased circulating albumin, leading to reduced plasma volume and perfusion to vital organs.
  • Ask about frothy urine - Caused by the presence of large amounts of protein in the urine, which changes the urine’s appearance.
  • Ask about weight gain - Due to fluid retention rather than increased body mass.
  • Ask about infections - Patients are prone to infections, particularly peritonitis, due to urinary loss of immunoglobulins and other proteins important for immunity.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Nephrotic Syndrome"][/sc]
  • Check periorbital oedema - Visible puffiness around the eyes, especially in the morning, caused by fluid shifting to the face during sleep.
  • Check pitting oedema - Pressing on swollen areas leaves an indentation, commonly seen in the lower extremities due to gravity pulling fluid into dependent regions.
  • Check ascites - The accumulation of fluid in the abdominal cavity, resulting from severe hypoalbuminaemia.
  • Check pleural effusion - Fluid build-up in the pleural space, which can lead to breathlessness and decreased lung sounds on auscultation.
  • Check hypertension - May occur due to renal sodium retention as the body tries to compensate for decreased plasma volume.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Nephrotic Syndrome"][/sc]
  • Severe abdominal pain - Can indicate spontaneous bacterial peritonitis, a serious complication caused by immunoglobulin loss and increased susceptibility to infections.
  • Shortness of breath - May signify pleural effusion or pulmonary oedema, both potentially life-threatening complications due to fluid overload or hypoalbuminaemia.
  • Unexplained bleeding or bruising - Indicates possible coagulopathy or thromboembolism due to altered coagulation from loss of anticoagulant proteins in the urine.
  • Sudden swelling of one leg - A potential sign of deep vein thrombosis, as nephrotic syndrome increases the risk of hypercoagulability.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Nephrotic Syndrome"][/sc]
  • Isolated proteinuria without oedema - Some patients, particularly those with minimal change disease, may present with significant proteinuria but minimal or no oedema, making diagnosis less straightforward.
  • Microscopic haematuria - Although rare, haematuria may be seen, particularly in patients with more advanced glomerular disease such as membranous nephropathy.
  • Normotension - In contrast to typical cases, some patients may not develop hypertension despite severe protein loss and fluid shifts, particularly in early stages.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask nephrotic syndrome.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Nephrotic Syndrome"][/sc]
  • Thromboembolism - Increased risk due to urinary loss of antithrombin III and other anticoagulants, particularly in severe hypoalbuminaemia.
  • Infections - Patients are at increased risk of infections such as spontaneous bacterial peritonitis due to immunoglobulin loss.
  • Acute kidney injury (AKI) - Can occur due to hypovolaemia or direct damage to the kidneys, particularly in patients with underlying glomerular diseases.
  • Chronic kidney disease (CKD) - Long-term proteinuria and glomerular damage may lead to progressive CKD and end-stage renal disease.
