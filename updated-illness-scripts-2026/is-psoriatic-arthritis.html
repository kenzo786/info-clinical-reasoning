Psoriatic Arthritis
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Psoriatic Arthritis"][/sc]
  • Psoriatic arthritis (PsA) is a chronic inflammatory arthritis associated with psoriasis.

Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Psoriatic Arthritis"][/sc]
  • The exact pathophysiology of PsA is not fully understood but involves immune system dysregulation, likely triggered by genetic and environmental factors.
  • Autoimmune responses are directed against joint structures, entheses, and skin, often due to T-cell activation and subsequent cytokine release (IL-17, TNF-α) causing inflammation.
  • Chronic inflammation leads to synovial proliferation (pannus formation), joint erosion, and bone destruction, as well as enthesitis in areas like Achilles tendon insertions.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Psoriatic Arthritis"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Initial presentation of joint pain and stiffness, often in distal interphalangeal (DIP) joints, associated with skin lesions of psoriasis.
  • Progressive phase: Over months to years, additional joint involvement and progressive structural damage occur without treatment.
  • Chronic phase: Permanent joint deformity and functional impairment may develop in untreated cases, along with potential extra-articular manifestations.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Psoriatic Arthritis"][/sc]
  • Ask about joint Pain - Most commonly affects hands and feet, particularly DIP joints; caused by inflammation within the joint capsule and surrounding entheses.
  • Ask about joint Stiffness - Worse in the morning and lasting over 30 minutes, due to prolonged inflammation overnight and reduced joint fluidity.
  • Ask about swelling - Often in fingers or toes (dactylitis), caused by inflammation and oedema in joint and surrounding soft tissues.
  • Ask about psoriatic Skin Lesions - Plaque psoriasis commonly affects the scalp, elbows, and knees; triggered by immune-mediated epidermal hyperproliferation.
  • Ask about fatigue - Due to chronic inflammation and immune activation, leading to systemic effects on energy metabolism.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Psoriatic Arthritis"][/sc]
  • Check dactylitis ("Sausage digits") - Swelling of entire fingers or toes, resulting from inflammation of both the joint and enthesis.
  • Check nail Pitting - Tiny indentations in the nails, linked to psoriatic involvement of the nail matrix.
  • Check enthesitis - Tenderness at tendon insertion sites, such as Achilles or plantar fascia; inflammation of tendon insertion points.
  • Check asymmetrical Arthritis - Predominantly affects a few joints on one side, often in hands and feet, due to the nature of psoriatic inflammation.
  • Check skin Plaques - Raised, red, scaly patches typical of psoriasis, usually on extensor surfaces.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Psoriatic Arthritis"][/sc]
  • Spinal Involvement - Severe back pain or stiffness; may indicate axial PsA, which can cause significant functional limitation and deformity if untreated.
  • Ocular Symptoms - Eye pain or redness can signal uveitis, an inflammatory eye condition associated with PsA and other spondyloarthropathies, posing a risk for vision loss.
  • Severe Nail Changes - Marked nail destruction can signify more aggressive disease and correlates with higher risk of joint damage.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Psoriatic Arthritis"][/sc]
  • Isolated axial involvement without peripheral arthritis, which may resemble ankylosing spondylitis.
  • Arthritis without skin lesions (10-15% of cases), sometimes only with nail changes, making PsA difficult to distinguish from other arthropathies.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask psoriatic arthritis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Psoriatic Arthritis"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Inflammatory Markers (ESR, CRP) - Typically elevated due to systemic inflammation, but not specific to PsA; X-ray - Shows joint erosion.
  • Targeted and monitoring: MRI - Useful for early detection of enthesitis and sacroiliitis; highlights active inflammation and structural changes; Rheumatoid Factor (RF) and Anti-CCP.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Psoriatic Arthritis"][/sc]
  • Rheumatoid Arthritis - Symmetrical joint involvement, typically with positive RF and anti-CCP antibodies.
  • Ankylosing Spondylitis - Mainly axial involvement; strong HLA-B27 association, less peripheral joint disease.
  • Osteoarthritis - Often affects weight-bearing joints with no systemic symptoms or inflammatory markers elevation.
  • Gout - Acute, often monoarticular arthritis, elevated serum uric acid, and crystals in joint fluid.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Psoriatic Arthritis"][/sc]
  • Joint Destruction - Progressive structural damage and deformity if untreated.
  • Cardiovascular Disease - Increased risk of atherosclerosis due to systemic inflammation.
  • Ocular Complications - Uveitis and other eye disorders associated with PsA can lead to vision impairment.
