Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Sickle Cell Disease"][/sc]
  • Gene Mutation: SCD is caused by a single point mutation in the beta-globin gene (HBB), leading to the substitution of valine for glutamic acid at the sixth position of the beta-globin chain.
  • Haemoglobin S Polymerisation: When deoxygenated, HbS polymerises, leading to the deformation of RBCs into a sickle shape, making them rigid and sticky.
  • Vaso-occlusion: Sickled cells can obstruct small blood vessels, leading to tissue ischaemia and pain crises, one of the hallmark features of SCD.
  • Haemolysis: Sickled RBCs are fragile and prone to destruction, resulting in chronic haemolytic anaemia, which contributes to fatigue and jaundice.
  • End-organ Damage: Recurrent vaso-occlusion and haemolysis cause progressive damage to organs such as the spleen, kidneys, lungs, and brain.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Sickle Cell Disease"][/sc]
  • Neonatal Period: Newborns with SCD are typically asymptomatic due to the presence of fetal haemoglobin (HbF), which prevents sickling. Symptoms begin to emerge around 6 months of age as HbF levels decrease.
  • Childhood and Adolescence: Recurrent painful vaso-occlusive crises, frequent infections, and organ complications such as splenic infarction and acute chest syndrome may occur.
  • Adulthood: Progressive organ damage becomes more prominent, leading to complications such as pulmonary hypertension, renal failure, and strokes.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Sickle Cell Disease"][/sc]
  • Ask about vaso-occlusive Pain Crises: Caused by sickled cells obstructing blood flow to tissues, resulting in ischaemia and severe pain, commonly affecting the bones and joints.
  • Ask about fatigue: Due to chronic haemolysis, patients often present with anaemia, which leads to generalised fatigue and weakness.
  • Ask about jaundice: Haemolysis releases bilirubin, leading to yellowing of the skin and sclerae.
  • Ask about frequent Infections: Splenic dysfunction due to repeated infarctions leads to increased susceptibility to encapsulated bacteria.
  • Ask about dyspnoea: Pulmonary complications such as acute chest syndrome and chronic lung damage can result in shortness of breath.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Sickle Cell Disease"][/sc]
  • Check pallor: Reflects anaemia due to haemolysis, often observed in the conjunctiva and skin.
  • Check splenomegaly: Common in children due to splenic sequestration of sickled cells, though the spleen may become fibrotic in adulthood due to repeated infarctions.
  • Check leg Ulcers: Due to chronic vascular occlusion and poor healing, particularly around the ankles.
  • Check hypoxaemia: Reflects acute chest syndrome or chronic lung involvement, often presenting with low oxygen saturation.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Sickle Cell Disease"][/sc]
  • Severe Chest Pain or Dyspnoea: May indicate acute chest syndrome, a life-threatening complication due to pulmonary infarction or infection.
  • Neurological Deficits: Sudden weakness, facial drooping, or slurred speech could signify a cerebrovascular event (stroke).
  • Severe Abdominal Pain: Could be a sign of splenic sequestration crisis or mesenteric ischaemia.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Sickle Cell Disease"][/sc]
  • Asymptomatic in Early Life: Due to protective levels of HbF in infancy, patients may not exhibit symptoms until later in childhood.
  • Stroke in Young Adults: Though more common in children, silent strokes can occur without classic symptoms and lead to cognitive decline.
  • Priapism: Persistent painful erection due to vaso-occlusion in the penile vasculature, sometimes occurring without other SCD symptoms.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask sickle cell disease [sc name="ask-ai-100" q="sickle cell disease"][/sc].

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Sickle Cell Disease"][/sc]

  • Autoimmune Haemolytic Anaemia (AIHA) - AIHA presents with similar haemolysis but lacks the recurrent vaso-occlusive crises seen in SCD.
  • G6PD Deficiency - Haemolysis is typically triggered by oxidative stress (e.g., infection or drugs) and is associated.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Sickle Cell Disease"][/sc]
  • Stroke: Particularly common in children and young adults due to cerebrovascular occlusion.
  • Acute Chest Syndrome: A life-threatening complication often triggered by infection or infarction, leading to respiratory failure.
  • Renal Failure: Chronic kidney damage occurs due to repeated vaso-occlusion and reduced blood flow to the kidneys.
  • Avascular Necrosis: Infarction of bone, particularly in the femoral head, leading to joint destruction and chronic pain.
