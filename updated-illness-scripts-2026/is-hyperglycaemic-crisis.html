Hyperglycaemic Crisis
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Hyperglycaemic Crisis"][/sc]
  • Hyperglycaemic crises are acute complications of diabetes mellitus, characterised by severe hyperglycaemia.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise hyperglycaemic features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Hyperglycaemic Crisis"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Hyperglycaemic Crisis"][/sc]
  • Risk factors: Risk factors for hyperglycaemic crisis include relevant predisposition, comorbidity, and exposure history.
  • Higher-risk or missed groups: Hyperglycaemic Crisis may be missed when presentation is atypical or obscured by comorbidity.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Hyperglycaemic Crisis"][/sc]
  • Diabetic Ketoacidosis (DKA): Insulin deficiency and counter-regulatory hormone excess (glucagon, cortisol, epinephrine) lead to hyperglycaemia, lipolysis, and production of ketone bodies (beta-hydroxybutyrate). Ketonaemia causes metabolic acidosis.
  • Hyperosmolar Hyperglycaemic State (HHS): Insulin deficiency, usually less severe than in DKA, prevents ketosis but results in extreme hyperglycaemia. Hyperosmolality and dehydration develop from osmotic diuresis.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Hyperglycaemic Crisis"][/sc]
  • DKA: Onset is rapid, typically over hours to 1-2 days, triggered by infection, insulin omission, or new-onset diabetes.
  • HHS: Develops more insidiously over several days to weeks, often in response to an acute illness or poor glycaemic control.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Hyperglycaemic Crisis"][/sc]
  • Ask about polyuria - Osmotic diuresis from hyperglycaemia increases urine output, leading to significant fluid loss.
  • Ask about polydipsia - Resulting dehydration triggers excessive thirst.
  • Ask about fatigue - Due to hyperglycaemia, dehydration, and electrolyte imbalance.
  • Ask about abdominal pain (common in DKA) - Related to acidosis and electrolyte disturbances (especially in children and young adults).
  • Ask about altered mental state - Ranges from confusion to coma, more common in HHS due to hyperosmolality.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Hyperglycaemic Crisis"][/sc]
  • Check dehydration - Dry mucous membranes, decreased skin turgor, and hypotension from fluid losses.
  • Check kussmaul breathing (in DKA) - Deep, laboured breathing is a compensatory mechanism for metabolic acidosis.
  • Check fruity breath odour (DKA) - Caused by exhalation of acetone, a byproduct of ketone metabolism.
  • Check altered mental status - Ranging from confusion to stupor or coma, especially in HHS due to severe hyperosmolality.
  • Check tachycardia - Compensation for hypovolaemia and acidosis.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Hyperglycaemic Crisis"][/sc]
  • Severe dehydration and hypotension - Indicates profound fluid loss, may progress to shock.
  • Coma - Suggests severe hyperosmolality or cerebral oedema, especially in HHS.
  • Refractory acidosis - May indicate impending respiratory failure or multi-organ dysfunction.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Hyperglycaemic Crisis"][/sc]
  • DKA without significant hyperglycaemia (euglycaemic DKA) - Occurs in patients using SGLT2 inhibitors or in pregnancy.
  • HHS with mild hyperglycaemia - Seen in some elderly patients with impaired thirst sensation and access to water.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask hyperglycaemic crisis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Hyperglycaemic Crisis"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Blood glucose - Elevated in both DKA (usually >11 mmol/L) and HHS (>30 mmol/L); Serum ketones - High in DKA (beta-hydroxybutyrate.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Arterial blood gases - Metabolic acidosis in DKA (pH <7.3, HCO3 <15 mmol/L); typically normal or only mildly abnormal in HHS.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Hyperglycaemic Crisis"][/sc]
  • Lactic acidosis - Distinguished by history of shock or hypoxia, no significant hyperglycaemia, and elevated lactate levels.
  • Alcoholic ketoacidosis - Typically seen in chronic alcoholics, with a history of alcohol binge, without marked hyperglycaemia.
  • Sepsis - Overlapping symptoms of altered mental status and dehydration, but with underlying infection and no.
  • Related alternative diagnosis - Distinguish from hyperglycaemic crisis using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Hyperglycaemic Crisis"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Assess severity, physiological stability, and immediate complications.
  • Core care: Deliver condition-specific management for hyperglycaemic crisis, treat underlying drivers, and coordinate appropriate specialist input.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Reassess symptom trajectory, examination findings, and objective markers to confirm response in hyperglycaemic crisis.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Escalate urgently for clinical deterioration, evolving red flags, or failure to improve in suspected hyperglycaemic crisis.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Hyperglycaemic Crisis"][/sc]
  • Cerebral oedema - Especially in younger patients with DKA, results from rapid osmotic shifts during treatment.
  • Thromboembolism - Increased risk in HHS due to dehydration and hyperosmolality.
  • Acute kidney injury - Due to severe dehydration and poor perfusion.
