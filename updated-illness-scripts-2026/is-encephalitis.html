Encephalitis
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Encephalitis"][/sc]
  • Encephalitis is an acute inflammation of the brain parenchyma, most commonly caused by viral infections, though it can also result from autoimmune reactions, bacterial infections, or post-infectious processes.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise encephalitis features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.

Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Encephalitis"][/sc]
  • The most common cause of encephalitis, viral infection, leads to direct viral invasion of brain tissue or a post-infectious autoimmune response. In the case of HSV encephalitis, the virus reactivates from latency, typically in the trigeminal ganglia, and spreads to the temporal and frontal lobes, causing necrosis. The inflammatory response results in cerebral oedema, neuronal damage, and disruption of normal brain function, leading to neurological symptoms.
  • In autoimmune or paraneoplastic encephalitis, immune-mediated destruction of neuronal cells or synaptic proteins leads to encephalitis. These processes may involve antibody formation against neuronal tissue, such as anti-NMDA receptor encephalitis.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Encephalitis"][/sc]
  • Prodromal phase: Often starts with flu-like symptoms, including fever, headache, and fatigue. This phase can last from a few days to a week.
  • Acute phase: Rapid onset of neurological symptoms, including confusion, seizures, and focal neurological deficits. The acute phase usually occurs within days to a week.
  • Recovery phase: Recovery can take weeks to months, with potential for persistent neurological deficits, depending on the severity and rapidity of treatment initiation.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Encephalitis"][/sc]
  • Ask about fever - The inflammatory process in response to infection leads to pyrexia as part of the body’s immune response.
  • Ask about headache - Increased intracranial pressure (ICP) from cerebral inflammation and oedema causes a diffuse headache.
  • Ask about altered mental status - Ranges from mild confusion to coma, caused by diffuse cortical dysfunction or brain oedema.
  • Ask about seizures - Neuronal damage and cortical irritation lead to increased excitability and risk of seizures.
  • Ask about photophobia - Inflammation of the meninges (meningoencephalitis) can cause sensitivity to light due to irritation of cranial nerves and brain tissues.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Encephalitis"][/sc]
  • Check fever - A raised temperature is a common systemic sign of infection or inflammation.
  • Check altered consciousness - Changes in alertness or responsiveness reflect brain dysfunction, typically seen in the acute phase.
  • Check neck stiffness - Seen in meningoencephalitis, where meningeal irritation causes resistance to neck flexion.
  • Check focal neurological signs - Hemiparesis, aphasia, or other deficits indicate specific brain region involvement, commonly in HSV encephalitis (temporal lobe signs).
  • Check kernig’s sign/Brudzinski’s sign - These signs reflect meningeal irritation if encephalitis is accompanied by meningitis.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Encephalitis"][/sc]
  • Rapid progression of symptoms - This may indicate severe cerebral oedema or widespread brain inflammation, increasing the risk of herniation or death.
  • Refractory seizures - Persistent seizures that are resistant to initial treatment may signify ongoing brain damage or necrosis.
  • Coma or deep altered mental state - A profound loss of consciousness is a critical marker of widespread brain dysfunction, often seen in severe encephalitis and may indicate impending cerebral herniation.
  • New focal neurological deficits - Sudden-onset focal deficits may indicate necrotic damage to specific areas of the brain, such as the temporal lobes in HSV encephalitis, and may worsen the prognosis.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Encephalitis"][/sc]
  • Subacute encephalitis - Some cases, especially in autoimmune encephalitis, may develop more slowly, with vague cognitive or psychiatric symptoms that may be misdiagnosed as psychiatric illness initially.
  • Non-febrile encephalitis - Rare cases of encephalitis, particularly in immunocompromised individuals, may present without fever due to a blunted inflammatory response.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask encephalitis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Encephalitis"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: CSF analysis (Lumbar puncture) - CSF typically shows lymphocytic pleocytosis, elevated protein, and normal glucose in viral encephalitis. In bacterial encephalitis.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Brain MRI - MRI may show hyperintense lesions in the temporal lobes (especially in HSV encephalitis), reflecting inflammation or necrosis; EEG.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Encephalitis"][/sc]
  • Permanent neurological deficits - Depending on the severity of brain damage, survivors of encephalitis may have lasting impairments, including memory deficits, speech difficulties, and personality changes.
  • Seizures - Some patients may develop chronic epilepsy following an acute episode of encephalitis.
  • Death - Mortality rates for untreated HSV encephalitis are as high as 70%. With appropriate treatment, mortality drops significantly, but can still reach up to 20%.
