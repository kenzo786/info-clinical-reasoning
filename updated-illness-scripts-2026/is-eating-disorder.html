Eating Disorder
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Eating Disorder"][/sc]
  • Eating disorders are a group of psychological conditions characterised by abnormal eating habits that negatively affect a person's physical and mental health.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise eating features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.

  • Hospital admission rates for eating disorders in the UK have risen significantly, with around 20,000 hospitalisations annually.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Eating Disorder"][/sc]
  • Eating disorders develop through a complex interplay of genetic, psychological, and environmental factors.
  • Genetics: Predisposing factors include heritability, with genetic influences on appetite regulation, impulsivity, and mood.
  • Neurobiology: Dysregulation of neurotransmitters (serotonin, dopamine) affecting mood, reward pathways, and appetite.
  • Psychosocial: Perfectionism, low self-esteem, and trauma often contribute to disordered eating. Social pressures regarding thinness, as well as a distorted body image, reinforce unhealthy eating patterns.
  • Hormonal: Starvation affects the hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal axis, leading to decreased leptin, ghrelin, and thyroid hormones, which further dysregulate appetite and metabolism.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Eating Disorder"][/sc]
  • Eating disorders can develop insidiously over months to years. Initial phases often involve subtle changes in eating habits, followed by an increasing preoccupation with weight and food, leading to more restrictive or binge-purge behaviours. Without intervention, chronicity is common, leading to severe physical and mental health deterioration.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Eating Disorder"][/sc]
  • Ask about preoccupation with food, weight, and body image - Persistent thoughts and anxiety about weight gain, linked to societal and personal standards of thinness.
  • Ask about restriction of food intake - Voluntary starvation in anorexia nervosa due to fear of weight gain.
  • Ask about binge eating episodes - Recurrent episodes of overeating in bulimia nervosa and binge eating disorder, caused by poor impulse control or emotional distress.
  • Ask about purging behaviours - Vomiting, laxative misuse, or diuretic abuse, common in bulimia nervosa, serve to relieve guilt after binge eating or to avoid weight.
  • Ask about amenorrhoea or menstrual irregularities - Chronic malnutrition suppresses the hypothalamic-pituitary-gonadal axis.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Eating Disorder"][/sc]
  • Check low body weight (BMI <17.5 kg/m²) - Often seen in anorexia nervosa due to severe caloric restriction.
  • Check bradycardia and hypotension - Due to the body's compensatory slowing of metabolism in response to starvation.
  • Check lanugo (fine body hair) - Body's attempt to insulate itself due to fat loss.
  • Check parotid gland enlargement - Found in bulimia nervosa, due to recurrent vomiting.
  • Check dental erosion - Chronic exposure of teeth to stomach acid in bulimics who purge via vomiting.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Eating Disorder"][/sc]
  • Severe electrolyte disturbances (hypokalaemia) - Can cause arrhythmias, seizures, and sudden cardiac death.
  • Prolonged QT interval - A marker of severe malnutrition and electrolyte imbalance, increasing risk of fatal arrhythmias.
  • Severe bradycardia (<40 bpm) - Reflects profound energy deficit and can lead to heart failure.
  • Refusal to eat or drink - Signals imminent risk of life-threatening complications, including refeeding syndrome.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Eating Disorder"][/sc]
  • Atypical anorexia nervosa - Normal-weight or overweight individuals who still engage in significant food restriction and have distorted body image.
  • Males with eating disorders - May present with muscle dysmorphia and an intense focus on building muscle rather than losing weight.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask eating disorder.

Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Eating Disorder"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Assess severity, physiological stability, and immediate complications.
  • Core care: Deliver condition-specific management for eating disorder, treat underlying drivers, and coordinate appropriate specialist input.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Reassess symptom trajectory, examination findings, and objective markers to confirm response in eating disorder.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Escalate urgently for clinical deterioration, evolving red flags, or failure to improve in suspected eating disorder.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Eating Disorder"][/sc]
  • Osteoporosis - Low oestrogen levels and chronic malnutrition lead to decreased bone density, increasing fracture risk.
  • Cardiac complications - Electrolyte imbalances can cause arrhythmias, while prolonged malnutrition can result in heart failure.
  • Refeeding syndrome - A potentially fatal condition caused by rapid refeeding after prolonged starvation, leading to severe electrolyte shifts (especially hypophosphataemia).
  • Psychological distress - Chronic anxiety, depression, and suicidal ideation are common in untreated or severe eating disorders.
