Brain Abscess
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Brain Abscess"][/sc]
  • A brain abscess is a focal, intracranial infection characterised by a collection of pus within the brain parenchyma.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise brain features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.

  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Brain Abscess"][/sc]
  • Risk factors: Risk of brain abscess rises with relevant predisposition, comorbidity, and exposure profile.
  • Higher-risk or missed groups: Brain Abscess may be missed when presentations are atypical or comorbidity obscures key features.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Brain Abscess"][/sc]
  • Brain abscess formation occurs in stages. First, a local infection (e.g., sinusitis, dental infection) spreads to brain tissue. The infection triggers an inflammatory response, leading to oedema and neutrophil infiltration. This results in the formation of a cerebritis (inflammatory area), which later encapsulates as a pus-filled abscess. If the abscess enlarges, it increases intracranial pressure (ICP) and may cause herniation or rupture, spreading infection into the ventricles or subarachnoid space.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Brain Abscess"][/sc]
  • Brain abscesses typically develop over days to weeks. Initial symptoms are often vague, with headache and fever in the early stages. As the abscess grows, symptoms related to increased ICP (e.g., vomiting, drowsiness) and focal neurological deficits may emerge. Without treatment, rapid deterioration can occur due to herniation, rupture, or sepsis.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Brain Abscess"][/sc]
  • Ask about headache - The most common presenting symptom, caused by increased intracranial pressure (ICP) from the abscess or surrounding oedema.
  • Ask about fever - Reflects the body's systemic inflammatory response to infection, though it may be absent in later stages.
  • Ask about nausea and vomiting - Result from raised ICP affecting the vomiting centre in the medulla oblongata.
  • Ask about focal neurological deficits - Depend on the location of the abscess; e.g., motor weakness if in the motor cortex, or visual disturbances if in in this clinical context.
  • Ask about altered consciousness - As ICP rises, cerebral perfusion decreases, leading to drowsiness, confusion, and in severe cases, coma.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Brain Abscess"][/sc]
  • Check focal neurological signs - Motor weakness, aphasia, or sensory deficits are related to abscess location and surrounding tissue involvement.
  • Check papilloedema - A sign of raised intracranial pressure, seen on fundoscopic examination.
  • Check seizures - May occur due to irritation of cortical tissue by the abscess.
  • Check signs of infection - Fever, tachycardia, and signs of the primary infection source (e.g., ear infection, dental abscess).
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Brain Abscess"][/sc]
  • Rapidly worsening headache - May indicate abscess enlargement, increased ICP, or impending herniation.
  • Sudden alteration in consciousness - Suggests herniation or rupture of the abscess, leading to acute intracranial pressure changes.
  • New-onset seizures - A sign of cortical irritation, indicating worsening of the condition or increased pressure.
  • Signs of meningism - Stiff neck and photophobia may suggest abscess rupture into the ventricles, leading to meningitis.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Brain Abscess"][/sc]
  • Chronic low-grade symptoms - Some patients, especially immunocompromised, may present with a prolonged course of mild headache and confusion without overt infection signs, making the diagnosis difficult.
  • Absence of fever - Particularly in the elderly or those with impaired immune responses, fever may be absent despite significant infection.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features or comorbidity can mask the underlying diagnosis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Brain Abscess"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: MRI with contrast - Preferred imaging modality; reveals a ring-enhancing lesion with surrounding oedema, due to the capsule formation and inflammation.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Blood cultures - Used to identify the causative organism, particularly if haematogenous spread is suspected; Full blood count - May show.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Brain Abscess"][/sc]
  • A 45-year-old male presents with a 10-day history of worsening headache, fever, and vomiting. He reports a recent history of chronic sinusitis. On examination, he has right-sided hemiparesis and papilloedema.
  • Adult with symptom pattern and objective findings suggestive of brain abscess, requiring focused confirmation and risk stratification.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Brain Abscess"][/sc]
  • Subdural empyema - Presents similarly but typically shows crescentic fluid collection on imaging, without the well-formed capsule.
  • Stroke - Differentiate from brain abscess using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.
  • Primary brain tumour - Often causes progressive symptoms, but lacks systemic infection signs, and imaging shows solid, non-enhancing.
  • Alternative serious diagnosis - Distinguishing history, examination, and targeted investigations do not support brain abscess.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Brain Abscess"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Assess physiological stability and urgent complications at presentation, and initiate early stabilisation where required.
  • Core care: Deliver condition-specific treatment for brain abscess, address underlying drivers, and involve appropriate specialist support.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Reassess symptom trajectory, objective markers, and treatment response over time to detect complications early.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Worsening symptoms, physiological deterioration, or failure to improve should trigger urgent reassessment for severe or alternative pathology in brain abscess.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Brain Abscess"][/sc]
  • Intracranial rupture - The abscess may rupture into the ventricles, leading to ventriculitis and acute hydrocephalus.
  • Herniation - Rapid increase in ICP may cause brain herniation, a life-threatening complication.
  • Sepsis - Spread of infection to the bloodstream may result in systemic inflammatory response syndrome (SIRS) and septic shock.
