Ascites
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Ascites"][/sc]
  • Ascites is the abnormal accumulation of fluid in the peritoneal cavity, typically due to portal hypertension.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise ascites features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Ascites"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Ascites"][/sc]
  • UK GP Population: Ascites is rare in primary care, usually seen in patients with known cirrhosis or heart failure.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Ascites"][/sc]
  • Ascites forms due to an imbalance between capillary hydrostatic pressure and oncotic pressure in the peritoneum. The most common cause, liver cirrhosis, leads to portal hypertension, increasing hydrostatic pressure in the splanchnic vessels. This promotes fluid leakage into the peritoneal cavity. Concurrent hypoalbuminaemia decreases plasma oncotic pressure, reducing fluid reabsorption. In response, the kidneys retain sodium and water, worsening fluid accumulation.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Ascites"][/sc]
  • Initial phase: Gradual accumulation of fluid, often asymptomatic.
  • Progressive phase: Increasing abdominal distension, discomfort, and weight gain.
  • Advanced phase: Difficulty breathing (due to diaphragmatic elevation), early satiety, and significant oedema.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Ascites"][/sc]
  • Ask about abdominal distension - Gradual increase in abdominal size due to the accumulation of peritoneal fluid. Caused by increased hydrostatic pressure from portal hypertension.
  • Ask about abdominal discomfort - Stretching of the peritoneum leads to vague discomfort or pressure in the abdomen.
  • Ask about weight gain - Rapid weight increase is common, reflecting fluid retention rather than fat accumulation.
  • Ask about dyspnoea - Difficulty breathing due to diaphragmatic elevation caused by fluid pressure.
  • Ask about early satiety - Compression of the stomach by fluid-filled peritoneum reduces the ability to eat normal portions.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Ascites"][/sc]
  • Check shifting dullness - Due to the movement of free fluid in the peritoneal cavity, detectable on percussion.
  • Check fluid thrill - Palpation of a wave of fluid across the abdomen when percussing one side indicates significant ascites.
  • Check peripheral oedema - Caused by hypoalbuminaemia and sodium retention, leading to fluid accumulation in dependent tissues.
  • Check hepatomegaly - Enlarged liver may be palpable if cirrhosis is present, causing increased portal hypertension.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Ascites"][/sc]
  • Spontaneous bacterial peritonitis (SBP) - Fever, abdominal pain, or encephalopathy could suggest SBP, a life-threatening infection of ascitic fluid. Requires urgent diagnostic paracentesis.
  • Hepatorenal syndrome - Progressive renal failure in cirrhotic patients with ascites, characterised by oliguria and increasing serum creatinine levels.
  • Rapid onset ascites - Suggests malignancy or venous thrombosis (e.g., Budd-Chiari syndrome).
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Ascites"][/sc]
  • Minimal ascites - Small amounts of fluid may be asymptomatic and detectable only via imaging.
  • Pseudoascites - Obesity or gaseous distension may mimic ascites but lacks fluid signs such as shifting dullness.
  • Ascites in non-cirrhotic patients - Can occur due to malignancy, pancreatitis, or tuberculosis, and may be misattributed to other causes without full investigation.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features or comorbidity can mask the underlying diagnosis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Ascites"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Abdominal ultrasound - Confirms the presence of free fluid and may show cirrhosis or malignancy. Typical finding: hypoechoic fluid collection; Diagnostic.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Full blood count (FBC) - Leukocytosis may indicate infection such as SBP. Anaemia is common in chronic liver disease; Liver function.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Ascites"][/sc]
  • A 55-year-old male with a history of chronic alcohol use presents with progressive abdominal distension, dyspnoea, and weight gain over the past two months. Examination reveals shifting dullness, peripheral oedema.
  • Adult with symptom pattern and objective findings suggestive of ascites, requiring focused confirmation and risk stratification.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Ascites"][/sc]
  • Malignancy - Malignant ascites is associated with rapid onset, significant weight loss, and low SAAG. Imaging.
  • Heart failure - Ascites due to right-sided heart failure presents with elevated jugular venous pressure and peripheral.
  • Nephrotic syndrome - Presents with generalised oedema, proteinuria, and hypoalbuminaemia. Ascites is usually part of a wider.
  • Alternative serious diagnosis - Distinguishing history, examination, and targeted investigations do not support ascites.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Ascites"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Assess physiological stability and urgent complications at presentation, and initiate early stabilisation where required.
  • Core care: Deliver condition-specific treatment for ascites, address underlying drivers, and involve appropriate specialist support.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Reassess symptom trajectory, objective markers, and treatment response over time to detect complications early.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Worsening symptoms, physiological deterioration, or failure to improve should trigger urgent reassessment for severe or alternative pathology in ascites.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Ascites"][/sc]
  • Spontaneous bacterial peritonitis (SBP) - Occurs in 10-30% of patients with ascites; mortality is high without treatment.
  • Hepatorenal syndrome - Progresses in cirrhotic patients, with a poor prognosis without liver transplantation.
  • Refractory ascites - Ascites that no longer responds to medical management, requiring frequent paracentesis or consideration for transplantation.
