Lyme Disease
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Lyme Disease"][/sc]
  • Lyme disease is a multisystemic infectious disease caused by the spirochete Borrelia burgdorferi , transmitted to humans through the bite of infected Ixodes ticks.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise lyme features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.

  • Risk factors: Risk factors for lyme disease include relevant predisposition, comorbidity, and exposure history.
  • Higher-risk or missed groups: Lyme Disease may be missed when presentation is atypical or obscured by comorbidity.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Lyme Disease"][/sc]
  • Infection: Borrelia burgdorferi enters the host via tick saliva during feeding.
  • Localisation: Initial replication at the bite site results in localised erythema migrans (EM).
  • Dissemination: Haematogenous spread leads to multisystemic involvement, targeting joints, the heart, and the nervous system.
  • Immune response: Persistent infection stimulates chronic inflammation, driving late-stage symptoms such as arthritis.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Lyme Disease"][/sc]
  • Early Localised (Days to Weeks): Characterised by erythema migrans and flu-like symptoms.
  • Early Disseminated (Weeks to Months): Includes neurological (e.g., facial palsy) and cardiac involvement.
  • Late Disseminated (Months to Years): Chronic arthritis and neurological sequelae dominate.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Lyme Disease"][/sc]
  • Ask about erythema Migrans: A hallmark rash at the tick bite site, due to localised immune response and spirochete proliferation.
  • Ask about fatigue and Malaise: Systemic inflammatory response contributes to these non-specific symptoms.
  • Ask about arthralgia: Inflammatory mediators in affected joints lead to joint pain.
  • Ask about neurological Symptoms: Headache, facial palsy, and meningitis occur due to spirochete invasion of the nervous system.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Lyme Disease"][/sc]
  • Check erythema Migrans: Expanding, ring-like rash with central clearing, pathognomonic for Lyme disease.
  • Check facial Nerve Palsy: Unilateral paralysis due to cranial nerve VII involvement in disseminated disease.
  • Check heart Block: Bradycardia or arrhythmias due to cardiac conduction system involvement.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Lyme Disease"][/sc]
  • Severe Headache or Neck Stiffness: Indicates meningitis or encephalitis, requiring urgent intervention.
  • Palpitations or Syncope: Suggests Lyme carditis, with risk of complete heart block.
  • Severe Arthralgia: May signify late disseminated disease and irreversible joint damage.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Lyme Disease"][/sc]
  • Generalised Rash: Non-EM rashes resembling viral exanthema may be misdiagnosed.
  • Isolated Neurological Symptoms: Such as mild cognitive changes without prior EM, potentially delaying diagnosis.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask lyme disease.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Lyme Disease"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Serology: Two-tier testing (ELISA followed by confirmatory Western blot); detects anti-Borrelia antibodies.
  • Targeted and monitoring: CSF Analysis: Raised lymphocytes and oligoclonal bands in neuroborreliosis; ECG: Evaluates for heart block or arrhythmias in Lyme carditis.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Lyme Disease"][/sc]
  • Cellulitis - Lacks the central clearing of EM; usually painful and warm.
  • Viral Meningitis - Differs in history (no tick exposure) and serology findings.
  • Bell’s Palsy - Absence of systemic or rash features distinguishes it from Lyme-related facial palsy.
  • Related alternative diagnosis - Distinguish from lyme disease using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Lyme Disease"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Assess severity, physiological stability, and immediate complications.
  • Core care: Deliver condition-specific management for lyme disease, treat underlying drivers, and coordinate appropriate specialist input.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Reassess symptom trajectory, examination findings, and objective markers to confirm response in lyme disease.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Escalate urgently for clinical deterioration, evolving red flags, or failure to improve in suspected lyme disease.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Lyme Disease"][/sc]
  • Chronic Lyme Arthritis: Persistent joint inflammation due to ongoing immune activation.
  • Neuroborreliosis: Chronic neurological deficits, including encephalopathy and peripheral neuropathy.
  • Cardiac Arrhythmias: Permanent conduction system damage can result in pacemaker requirement.
