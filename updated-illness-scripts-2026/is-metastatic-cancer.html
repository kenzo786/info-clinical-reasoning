Metastatic Cancer
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Metastatic Cancer"][/sc]
  • Metastatic cancer refers to the spread of malignant cells from the primary tumour site to distant organs or tissues.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise metastatic features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Metastatic Cancer"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Metastatic Cancer"][/sc]
  • Common cancers that metastasise include lung, breast, prostate, colorectal, and melanoma.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Metastatic Cancer"][/sc]
  • The process of metastasis begins with local invasion of the primary tumour into surrounding tissues.
  • Tumour cells enter the bloodstream or lymphatic system (intravasation) and travel to distant organs.
  • Upon reaching a distant site, cells exit the circulation (extravasation) and invade the new tissue environment, where they proliferate and establish secondary tumours.
  • The ability of cancer cells to survive and grow in foreign environments depends on factors such as immune evasion, angiogenesis, and resistance to apoptosis.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Metastatic Cancer"][/sc]
  • Metastasis may develop at any point during the course of cancer, though it often signifies late-stage disease. The time from initial diagnosis to metastasis can range from months to years depending on cancer type.
  • Early phase: asymptomatic or mild symptoms.
  • Mid phase: increasing symptoms related to the size and location of metastatic tumours.
  • Late phase: severe systemic symptoms (e.g., cachexia, severe pain) and organ failure if vital structures are involved.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Metastatic Cancer"][/sc]
  • Ask about fatigue - Common due to systemic effects of cancer, such as anaemia, metabolic disturbances, and inflammatory cytokines.
  • Ask about weight loss - Often related to cancer cachexia, where metabolic alterations lead to muscle wasting and fat loss.
  • Ask about pain - Localised to sites of metastasis (e.g., bone, liver) due to tumour invasion of sensitive structures.
  • Ask about cough or shortness of breath - Seen in lung metastasis due to obstruction or pleural effusion.
  • Ask about neurological symptoms (e.g., headaches, seizures) - Occur with brain metastasis due to increased intracranial pressure or tumour invasion of brain tissue.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Metastatic Cancer"][/sc]
  • Check palpable mass - May be noted in superficial organs such as the liver or soft tissue metastases.
  • Check lymphadenopathy - Enlarged lymph nodes due to metastatic spread, particularly in the cervical, axillary, or inguinal regions.
  • Check jaundice - A sign of liver metastasis, due to bile duct obstruction or liver dysfunction.
  • Check neurological deficits - Focal deficits (e.g., weakness, aphasia) may occur in cases of brain or spinal cord metastasis.
  • Check ascites - Abdominal distension due to peritoneal metastasis or liver involvement causing portal hypertension.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Metastatic Cancer"][/sc]
  • Severe, persistent pain - May indicate bone metastasis or nerve compression.
  • Sudden neurological changes - New onset seizures or focal neurological deficits can signify brain metastasis or spinal cord compression.
  • Severe dyspnoea - Could suggest large pleural effusion or lung metastasis impairing respiratory function.
  • Unexplained bleeding - May be a sign of coagulopathy or liver dysfunction due to extensive liver metastasis.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Metastatic Cancer"][/sc]
  • Some cancers metastasise in ways that are unusual for their type, such as gastrointestinal cancers presenting with brain metastasis. These may be missed unless thorough investigation is pursued.
  • Occult metastasis: Patients may present with symptoms like unexplained weight loss or fatigue without obvious primary tumour symptoms, complicating early detection.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Metastatic Cancer"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Imaging (CT, MRI, PET) - Identifies metastatic deposits. CT and MRI show structural changes, while PET highlights metabolically active areas; Biopsy.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Tumour markers - Blood tests such as PSA (prostate), CEA (colorectal), and CA-125 (ovarian) can indicate metastatic spread; Blood tests .

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Metastatic Cancer"][/sc]
  • Primary bone tumour - Typically presents with localised bone pain, but imaging and biopsy would show primary bone.
  • Chronic infection (e.g., tuberculosis) - Can cause weight loss and systemic symptoms, but imaging shows granulomatous changes rather than.
  • Multiple myeloma - Presents with bone pain and lytic lesions, but associated with raised paraproteins and plasmacytosis.
  • Related alternative diagnosis - Distinguish from metastatic cancer using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Metastatic Cancer"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Assess severity, physiological stability, and immediate complications.
  • Core care: Deliver condition-specific management for metastatic cancer, treat underlying drivers, and coordinate appropriate specialist input.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Reassess symptom trajectory, examination findings, and objective markers to confirm response in metastatic cancer.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Escalate urgently for clinical deterioration, evolving red flags, or failure to improve in suspected metastatic cancer.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Metastatic Cancer"][/sc]
  • Organ failure - Metastasis to vital organs such as the liver or lungs can lead to their failure.
  • Spinal cord compression - A life-threatening complication of vertebral metastasis.
  • Hypercalcaemia - Often seen in bone metastases, leading to confusion, polyuria, and arrhythmias.
  • Prognosis - Generally poor, with median survival varying widely based on tumour type, ranging from months to years depending on the response to treatment.
