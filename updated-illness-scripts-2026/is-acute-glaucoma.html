Acute Glaucoma
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Acute Glaucoma"][/sc]
  • Acute glaucoma usually refers to acute angle-closure glaucoma, where sudden iridocorneal angle obstruction causes rapid intraocular pressure rise and threatens vision.
  • UKMLA essentials: painful red eye onset, fixed pupil vision-loss red flags, urgent pressure-lowering ophthalmology pathway.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Acute Glaucoma"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Acute Glaucoma"][/sc]
  • Acute angle closure is an uncommon but vision-threatening ophthalmic emergency across primary and emergency care.
  • Risk is increased by anatomically narrow angles, increasing age, hypermetropia, and precipitating pupil dilation.
  • Delayed treatment can cause irreversible optic nerve injury and permanent visual impairment within a short timeframe.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Acute Glaucoma"][/sc]
  • Obstruction of aqueous humour drainage at the anterior chamber angle causes abrupt intraocular pressure rise.
  • Raised pressure leads to corneal oedema, iris ischaemia, and optic nerve stress with rapid visual dysfunction.
  • Persistent pressure elevation can cause irreversible retinal ganglion cell and optic nerve damage.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Acute Glaucoma"][/sc]
  • Acute phase: Symptoms evolve rapidly over hours with escalating ocular pain and visual disturbance.
  • Progressive phase: Without urgent decompression, optic nerve injury progresses and vision may be permanently lost.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Acute Glaucoma"][/sc]
  • Ask about severe unilateral eye pain - Sudden deep ocular pain is a classic presenting complaint.
  • Ask about blurred vision and halos - Corneal oedema causes visual haze and halo perception around lights.
  • Ask about headache with nausea or vomiting - Autonomic symptoms often accompany marked intraocular pressure rise.
  • Ask about rapid visual decline - Worsening acuity over hours signals urgent optic nerve threat.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Acute Glaucoma"][/sc]
  • Check conjunctival injection with cloudy cornea - Red painful eye with corneal haze supports acute angle closure.
  • Check pupil shape and reactivity - Mid-dilated sluggish or fixed pupil is characteristic.
  • Check intraocular pressure - Markedly raised pressure confirms emergency severity.
  • Check anterior chamber depth - Shallow chamber supports angle crowding mechanism.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Acute Glaucoma"][/sc]
  • Rapid visual loss with severe pain - Indicates imminent or ongoing optic nerve injury.
  • Fixed mid-dilated pupil with corneal haze - Suggests established angle closure requiring immediate treatment.
  • Persistent vomiting or systemic deterioration - Reflects severe pressure rise and urgent need for specialist intervention.
  • Second-eye symptoms or bilateral angle risk - Requires urgent prophylactic planning to prevent contralateral attack.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Acute Glaucoma"][/sc]
  • Intermittent subacute attacks: Short episodes of blurred vision and headache in dim light may precede a full acute event.
  • Predominant headache phenotype: Ocular symptoms may be overshadowed by severe unilateral headache and nausea.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Acute Glaucoma"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Immediate visual acuity check, pupil assessment, slit-lamp anterior segment examination, and urgent tonometry where available.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Gonioscopy confirms angle closure after initial pressure control; optic nerve and visual-field documentation supports follow-up damage assessment once the acute episode is stabilised.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Acute Glaucoma"][/sc]
  • A 60-year-old woman presents with hours of severe unilateral eye pain, blurred vision with halos, headache, nausea, and a fixed mid-dilated pupil.
  • Adult with acute painful red eye and rapid visual disturbance plus classic angle-closure examination features, concerning for acute glaucoma.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Acute Glaucoma"][/sc]
  • Anterior uveitis - Photophobia with constricted irregular pupil and inflammatory cells suggests uveitis rather than angle closure.
  • Keratitis or corneal ulcer - Marked corneal epithelial defect and infective signs point to corneal pathology.
  • Conjunctivitis - Red eye with discharge and minimal pain or visual compromise is more typical of conjunctivitis.
  • Scleritis - Deep boring eye pain with tender sclera and preserved angle structure suggests scleritis.
  • Migraine with visual aura - Transient visual symptoms without fixed ocular examination abnormalities suggest migraine.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Acute Glaucoma"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Initiate emergency pressure-lowering treatment and urgent ophthalmology involvement to protect vision.
  • Core care: Combine topical and systemic pressure-lowering strategies followed by definitive laser peripheral iridotomy when safe.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Reassess pressure, corneal clarity, and visual function closely through acute stabilisation and recovery.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Persistent pain, uncontrolled pressure, or progressive visual loss requires immediate specialist escalation and treatment revision.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Acute Glaucoma"][/sc]
  • Permanent optic neuropathy: Sustained pressure elevation can cause irreversible visual field and acuity loss.
  • Chronic angle-closure glaucoma: Recurrent or partially treated attacks can lead to long-term pressure dysregulation.
  • Corneal decompensation: Prolonged oedema can damage corneal clarity and visual quality.
