Scleritis
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Scleritis"][/sc]
  • Scleritis is an inflammatory condition affecting the sclera, the white, fibrous outer layer of the eye.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise scleritis features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Scleritis"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Scleritis"][/sc]
  • More commonly seen in patients attending rheumatology or ophthalmology services due to its association with autoimmune conditions.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Scleritis"][/sc]
  • Involves immune-mediated inflammation of the sclera, often triggered by autoimmune diseases like rheumatoid arthritis or granulomatosis with polyangiitis.
  • Leukocyte infiltration leads to scleral oedema, necrosis, and vascular damage. Cytokines like IL-6 and TNF-alpha play key roles.
  • Posterior scleritis involves similar processes but affects structures posterior to the globe, causing distinct complications such as exudative retinal detachment.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Scleritis"][/sc]
  • Acute phase: Rapid onset over days, characterised by severe pain and redness.
  • Chronic phase: Symptoms may wax and wane, often correlating with the activity of underlying systemic disease.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Scleritis"][/sc]
  • Ask about severe eye pain - Often described as deep, boring pain due to inflammation of the richly innervated scleral tissue.
  • Ask about redness - Diffuse or sectoral redness caused by dilation of episcleral and scleral vessels.
  • Ask about photophobia - Sensitivity to light due to irritation of the cornea or uvea.
  • Ask about blurred vision - Caused by associated keratitis, uveitis, or scleral thinning.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Scleritis"][/sc]
  • Check scleral oedema - Visible thickening due to inflammation and infiltration.
  • Check nodules - Localised nodular swellings in nodular scleritis.
  • Check decreased visual acuity - Secondary to complications such as posterior scleritis or uveitis.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Scleritis"][/sc]
  • Severe necrotising pain with visual loss - Indicates necrotising scleritis, which may lead to scleral perforation.
  • Proptosis - Suggestive of posterior scleritis, requiring urgent imaging and intervention.
  • Systemic symptoms - Fever, weight loss, or arthralgia may signify an underlying systemic vasculitis or autoimmune disease.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Scleritis"][/sc]
  • Posterior scleritis with headache and minimal redness - May mimic orbital cellulitis or optic neuritis.
  • Asymptomatic scleral thinning - Detected incidentally, often in longstanding autoimmune disease.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask scleritis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Scleritis"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Slit-lamp examination - Reveals scleral oedema, vascular injection, or nodules.
  • Targeted and monitoring: B-scan ultrasonography - Used for posterior scleritis, showing thickened sclera and fluid in Tenon’s space; Blood tests - ESR, CRP, ANA.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Scleritis"][/sc]
  • A 45-year-old woman with rheumatoid arthritis presents with severe unilateral eye pain, redness, and blurred vision for 3 days, worsening progressively.
  • Patient with severe unilateral eye pain, redness, and blurred vision for 3 days, worsening progressively, consistent with scleritis.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Scleritis"][/sc]
  • Episcleritis - Milder pain, sectoral redness, and no visual loss; resolves spontaneously.
  • Orbital cellulitis - Proptosis, pain with eye movement, and systemic signs like fever.
  • Keratitis - Corneal involvement with ulceration or opacities on slit-lamp exam.
  • Related alternative diagnosis - Distinguish from scleritis using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Scleritis"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Assess severity, physiological stability, and immediate complications.
  • Core care: Deliver condition-specific management for scleritis, treat underlying drivers, and coordinate appropriate specialist input.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Reassess symptom trajectory, examination findings, and objective markers to confirm response in scleritis.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Escalate urgently for clinical deterioration, evolving red flags, or failure to improve in suspected scleritis.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Scleritis"][/sc]
  • Vision loss - Due to uveitis, glaucoma, or scleral perforation.
  • Corneal thinning and perforation - Secondary to necrotising scleritis.
  • Secondary glaucoma - Resulting from chronic inflammation or steroid use.
