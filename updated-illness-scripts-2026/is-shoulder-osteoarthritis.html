Shoulder Osteoarthritis
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Shoulder Osteoarthritis"][/sc]
  • Shoulder osteoarthritis (OA) is a degenerative joint disease characterised by progressive loss of articular cartilage, subchondral bone remodelling, and the formation of osteophytes, primarily affecting the glenohumeral or acromioclavicular joint.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise shoulder features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.

Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Shoulder Osteoarthritis"][/sc]
  • Progressive degeneration of articular cartilage due to mechanical wear and tear and inflammatory processes.
  • Exposure of subchondral bone, leading to sclerosis and cyst formation.
  • Osteophyte formation at joint margins as a compensatory response.
  • Synovial inflammation contributes to pain and stiffness.
  • Decreased joint space visible on imaging reflects cartilage loss.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Shoulder Osteoarthritis"][/sc]
  • Early stage: Mild symptoms, often intermittent pain with activity.
  • Progressive stage: Increasing pain and stiffness, affecting daily activities.
  • Late stage: Chronic pain, marked joint stiffness, and possible deformity.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Shoulder Osteoarthritis"][/sc]
  • Ask about shoulder pain: Often dull and aching, worsens with activity due to joint inflammation and mechanical wear.
  • Ask about stiffness: Particularly in the morning or after inactivity, caused by synovial thickening and loss of cartilage.
  • Ask about reduced range of motion: Gradual decline in both active and passive movement due to joint structure damage and osteophyte formation.
  • Ask about crepitus: A grinding or clicking sensation due to uneven joint surfaces and osteophytes.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Shoulder Osteoarthritis"][/sc]
  • Check tenderness: Localised to the joint line, reflecting underlying inflammation or bone involvement.
  • Check swelling: May occur due to synovial inflammation or effusion.
  • Check deformity: Visible in advanced cases due to osteophyte proliferation and joint remodelling.
  • Check weakness: Often due to pain inhibition and disuse of the affected arm.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Shoulder Osteoarthritis"][/sc]
  • Severe, constant pain unresponsive to treatment: May indicate infection or malignancy.
  • Rapid joint swelling: Suggests inflammatory or infectious arthritis.
  • Neurological symptoms (e.g., paraesthesia, weakness): Possible cervical spine involvement or nerve impingement.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Shoulder Osteoarthritis"][/sc]
  • Isolated stiffness: May mimic adhesive capsulitis, particularly in early disease stages.
  • Referred pain: Felt in the upper arm or neck due to overlapping nerve supply.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask shoulder osteoarthritis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Shoulder Osteoarthritis"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Plain radiographs: Joint space narrowing, osteophytes, subchondral sclerosis, and cysts.
  • Targeted and monitoring: MRI: Detailed view of cartilage damage and synovial thickening; used in unclear cases; Blood tests: Generally normal; used to exclude inflammatory.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Shoulder Osteoarthritis"][/sc]
  • Adhesive capsulitis - More pronounced stiffness than pain; no radiographic changes.
  • Rotator cuff tear - Pain worsened by specific movements; MRI shows tendon damage.
  • Rheumatoid arthritis - Bilateral involvement, systemic symptoms, raised inflammatory markers.
  • Related alternative diagnosis - Distinguish from shoulder osteoarthritis using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Shoulder Osteoarthritis"][/sc]
  • Chronic pain: Can impair quality of life and mental health.
  • Joint deformity: Due to progressive structural damage.
  • Reduced function: Leads to dependence on assistive devices or caregivers.
