Epiglottitis Illness Script
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Epiglottitis"][/sc]
  • Epiglottitis is an acute, life-threatening inflammatory condition characterised by swelling of the epiglottis and surrounding tissues, leading to potential airway obstruction.

Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Epiglottitis"][/sc]
  • Bacterial infection (most commonly Hib) invades the epiglottis and adjacent tissues, leading to inflammatory oedema. The epiglottis becomes swollen, and its ability to protect the airway during swallowing is compromised. Severe oedema can obstruct the airway, leading to respiratory distress and hypoxia. In unvaccinated individuals, Hib remains the most common causative agent, though Streptococcus pneumoniae and Staphylococcus aureus may also be involved.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Epiglottitis"][/sc]
  • Epiglottitis typically develops rapidly over hours to days. It begins with mild upper respiratory symptoms, such as sore throat, followed by high fever, worsening dysphagia, and rapidly progressive respiratory distress. Without prompt intervention, airway obstruction can occur within 24-48 hours of symptom onset.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Epiglottitis"][/sc]
  • Ask about sore throat – Initial symptom due to local inflammation and infection of the epiglottis.
  • Ask about dysphagia – Difficulty swallowing occurs as the epiglottis swells, impeding the swallowing mechanism.
  • Ask about drooling – Patients cannot swallow saliva due to dysphagia and pain, leading to drooling.
  • Ask about high fever – A systemic response to infection, typically seen in bacterial infections like Hib.
  • Ask about stridor – Inspiratory stridor is a hallmark of upper airway obstruction, caused by the swollen epiglottis narrowing the airway.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Epiglottitis"][/sc]
  • Check tripod positioning – A posture adopted to maximise airway patency, where the patient leans forward with hands on knees.
  • Check stridor – A high-pitched sound heard during inspiration due to partial airway obstruction.
  • Check intercostal and suprasternal retractions – Sign of respiratory distress, with visible chest wall retraction during inspiration as the body attempts to overcome airway obstruction.
  • Check cyanosis – A late sign of severe hypoxia caused by significant airway obstruction.
  • Check tachycardia – A compensatory response to hypoxia.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Epiglottitis"][/sc]
  • Sudden onset respiratory distress – Can signify impending airway obstruction, requiring immediate intervention to prevent asphyxia.
  • Drooling with stridor – Strongly suggests airway compromise due to epiglottitis, requiring urgent airway management.
  • Cyanosis – Indicates critical hypoxaemia and impending respiratory failure.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Epiglottitis"][/sc]
  • In adults, epiglottitis may present more insidiously with sore throat and mild dysphagia but without classic signs like stridor or severe respiratory distress. This may lead to delayed diagnosis and progression to airway obstruction.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask epiglottitis illness script.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Epiglottitis"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Lateral neck X-ray – A characteristic "thumbprint sign" (swollen epiglottis) may be visible, indicating airway obstruction.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Flexible fibreoptic laryngoscopy – Gold standard for visualisation of the swollen, cherry-red epiglottis. This should only be performed by an experienced.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Epiglottitis"][/sc]
  • Airway obstruction – The most feared complication, leading to respiratory failure and death if not promptly managed.
  • Sepsis – Bacterial infection can disseminate, particularly in unvaccinated individuals.
  • Pneumonia – Can occur as a secondary infection due to compromised airway and aspiration.
