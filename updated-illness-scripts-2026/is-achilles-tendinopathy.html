Achilles Tendinopathy
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Achilles Tendinopathy"][/sc]
  • Achilles tendinopathy is a chronic overload disorder of the Achilles tendon causing pain, stiffness, and reduced tendon function without complete rupture.
  • UKMLA essentials: load-related Achilles pain, sudden rupture red flags, diagnose clinically with focused tendon examination.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Achilles Tendinopathy"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Achilles Tendinopathy"][/sc]
  • Achilles tendinopathy is common in running and jumping sports and also affects less active adults after abrupt activity increase.
  • Risk is increased by training errors, reduced calf strength, footwear or load changes, and prior tendon symptoms.
  • Delayed recognition can lead to prolonged disability and higher risk of progression to partial or complete tendon rupture.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Achilles Tendinopathy"][/sc]
  • Repeated tendon overload exceeds repair capacity and produces matrix disruption within the Achilles tendon.
  • Collagen disorganisation, neovascular change, and neural sensitisation contribute to persistent pain and stiffness.
  • Ongoing mechanical stress without graded rehabilitation can drive chronic tendinopathic change and structural weakness.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Achilles Tendinopathy"][/sc]
  • Early course: Pain appears with loading, often with morning stiffness that eases after movement.
  • Progressive course: Symptoms become more persistent, activity tolerance falls, and recovery after exercise becomes slower.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Achilles Tendinopathy"][/sc]
  • Ask about load-related Achilles pain - Pain typically worsens during or after running, jumping, or hill work.
  • Ask about morning stiffness - First steps after rest are often painful with gradual easing on mobilising.
  • Ask about tendon thickening or local swelling - Patients may notice focal fullness along the tendon.
  • Ask about reduced performance - Loss of push-off power and reduced training capacity are common.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Achilles Tendinopathy"][/sc]
  • Check focal tendon tenderness - Localised pain on palpation supports Achilles tendinopathy.
  • Check tendon thickening - Nodular or diffuse thickening is consistent with chronic tendon remodelling.
  • Check calf raise function - Pain or weakness on single-leg heel raise indicates functional compromise.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Achilles Tendinopathy"][/sc]
  • Sudden snap with immediate weakness - Suggests acute Achilles rupture rather than tendinopathy.
  • Rapid swelling, warmth, or systemic illness - Raises concern for infection or inflammatory tendon pathology.
  • Inability to weight-bear or plantar flex - Requires urgent assessment for rupture or associated injury.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Achilles Tendinopathy"][/sc]
  • Insertional pain pattern: Symptoms localise to the calcaneal insertion and can mimic retrocalcaneal bursitis.
  • Bilateral tendon pain: Consider systemic inflammatory or metabolic contributors when both tendons are affected.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Achilles Tendinopathy"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Clinical diagnosis from history and tendon examination, including palpation and single-leg loading tests; assess gait and calf function.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Ultrasound for tendon structure and neovascular change when diagnosis is uncertain or to assess progression; MRI when rupture or alternative deep pathology is suspected.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Achilles Tendinopathy"][/sc]
  • A 40-year-old recreational runner presents with 3 months of posterior heel pain and morning stiffness, worsening after speed sessions and hill running.
  • Active adult with chronic load-related Achilles pain, tendon stiffness, and reduced push-off performance, consistent with Achilles tendinopathy.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Achilles Tendinopathy"][/sc]
  • Achilles tendon rupture - Acute onset with snap sensation and marked plantar-flexion weakness distinguishes rupture from tendinopathy.
  • Retrocalcaneal bursitis - Pain is more anterior to the tendon insertion with local bursal tenderness.
  • Plantar fasciitis - Medial plantar heel pain on first steps is more typical than posterior tendon pain.
  • Posterior ankle impingement - Pain is provoked by forced plantar flexion rather than tendon loading progression.
  • Sural neuropathy - Burning lateral ankle pain with sensory symptoms suggests neural rather than tendon pathology.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Achilles Tendinopathy"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Reduce provoking load, identify rupture red flags, and provide pain control that allows early functional rehabilitation.
  • Core care: Structured progressive calf loading and biomechanical optimisation are the mainstays, supported by physiotherapy-guided return-to-activity planning.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Track pain during loading, tendon function, and activity tolerance to adjust rehabilitation stages safely.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Sudden deterioration, functional collapse, or failure to improve with supervised rehabilitation should prompt specialist review and imaging for alternative pathology.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Achilles Tendinopathy"][/sc]
  • Partial or complete tendon rupture: Progressive tendon degeneration under continued high load increases rupture risk.
  • Persistent pain and functional limitation: Inadequate rehabilitation can lead to chronic activity restriction.
  • Reduced participation in work or sport: Ongoing symptoms may cause prolonged deconditioning and reduced quality of life.
