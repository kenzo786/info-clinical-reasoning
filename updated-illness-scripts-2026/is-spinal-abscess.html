Spinal Abscess
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Spinal Abscess"][/sc]
  • A spinal abscess, also known as an epidural abscess, is a collection of pus within the spinal epidural space, often caused by infection.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise spinal features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.

Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Spinal Abscess"][/sc]
  • Initial Infection: The causative bacteria often enter the bloodstream, commonly via skin infections, intravenous drug use, or during spinal instrumentation (e.g., lumbar punctures or spinal surgeries).
  • Spread to the Epidural Space: The pathogens travel through the bloodstream to the vertebral blood vessels and localise in the epidural space around the spinal cord.
  • Inflammation and Pus Formation: The infection induces an inflammatory response, leading to pus formation. As pus accumulates, it compresses the spinal cord, causing ischemia and nerve damage.
  • Progression to Neurological Damage: Persistent compression and lack of blood flow lead to permanent damage to spinal cord tissues if untreated, potentially resulting in paralysis.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Spinal Abscess"][/sc]
  • Acute Phase (days to weeks): Rapid onset of pain and neurological symptoms, particularly if abscess is large or rapidly expanding.
  • Subacute Phase (weeks): Neurological deficits develop more gradually if the abscess grows slowly.
  • Chronic Phase: Uncommon; some abscesses develop over months, especially in immunocompromised individuals, with slow progression of symptoms.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Spinal Abscess"][/sc]
  • Ask about back Pain - Persistent, severe pain at the level of the abscess, often the first symptom, caused by inflammation and pressure on local tissues.
  • Ask about fever - Common due to systemic infection response, often seen with bacterial infections.
  • Ask about radicular Pain - Pain radiating along nerve roots, indicating nerve compression from the abscess.
  • Ask about neurological Deficits - Weakness, numbness, or bowel/bladder dysfunction develop due to spinal cord compression and nerve ischemia.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Spinal Abscess"][/sc]
  • Check localised Tenderness - Pain on palpation of the vertebrae or muscles near the abscess site, caused by inflammation.
  • Check motor Weakness - Due to nerve root or spinal cord compression, often affecting the lower limbs.
  • Check sensory Deficits - Reduced sensation below the level of the lesion due to nerve involvement.
  • Check fever - Persistent or intermittent fever due to the underlying infection.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Spinal Abscess"][/sc]
  • Rapidly Progressive Weakness - Signifies severe spinal cord compression; potential for irreversible damage if untreated.
  • Loss of Bladder or Bowel Control - Indicates advanced compression affecting autonomic nerves, a marker for urgent intervention.
  • Severe, Unrelenting Pain - Especially when combined with fever, this may indicate expanding infection and abscess growth.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Spinal Abscess"][/sc]
  • Slow progression without fever, more common in immunocompromised patients (e.g., diabetes or HIV), can lead to missed diagnosis until neurological symptoms appear.
  • Isolated back pain without neurological symptoms; delays diagnosis as it may mimic musculoskeletal issues.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Spinal Abscess"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: MRI with Contrast - Gold standard, shows an epidural fluid collection and compression; contrast highlights abscess walls and inflammation; Blood Cultures.
  • Targeted and monitoring: ESR and CRP - Typically elevated, indicating inflammation, although nonspecific; FBC - May show leukocytosis as part of infection response.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Spinal Abscess"][/sc]
  • Discitis - Often more localised pain, typically lacks fever or neurological deficits until late.
  • Vertebral Osteomyelitis - Similar infection but within bone; MRI shows bone involvement without epidural fluid collection.
  • Spinal Tumour - Presents with back pain and neurological deficits but lacks fever; MRI shows a mass.
  • Related alternative diagnosis - Distinguish from spinal abscess using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Spinal Abscess"][/sc]
  • Paralysis - Due to irreversible spinal cord damage from delayed treatment.
  • Sepsis - Systemic spread of the infection, especially if untreated or in immunocompromised patients.
  • Chronic Pain - Persistent pain in cases with delayed treatment or residual nerve damage.
