Laryngitis
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Laryngitis"][/sc]
  • Laryngitis refers to inflammation of the larynx, commonly due to viral infection, overuse of the vocal cords, or irritation from substances such as smoke.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise laryngitis features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.

  • Risk factors: Risk factors for laryngitis include relevant predisposition, comorbidity, and exposure history.
  • Higher-risk or missed groups: Laryngitis may be missed when presentation is atypical or obscured by comorbidity.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Laryngitis"][/sc]
  • Step 1: Infection or irritation causes inflammation of the laryngeal mucosa.
  • Step 2: Inflammatory mediators such as cytokines and prostaglandins increase blood flow to the larynx, leading to oedema.
  • Step 3: Oedema affects vocal fold vibration, resulting in hoarseness or aphonia (loss of voice).
  • Step 4: Persistent irritation may lead to further damage of the laryngeal tissue, particularly with chronic exposure to irritants.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Laryngitis"][/sc]
  • Acute: Lasts less than 3 weeks, typically resolves within 7-14 days. Most common form.
  • Chronic: Persists for more than 3 weeks, often due to prolonged exposure to irritants or chronic vocal strain.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Laryngitis"][/sc]
  • Ask about hoarseness - Due to inflammation and oedema of the vocal cords, affecting their ability to vibrate properly.
  • Ask about loss of voice (aphonia) - Severe oedema or inflammation can completely obstruct the vocal folds, resulting in temporary loss of voice.
  • Ask about sore throat - Inflammation in the laryngeal mucosa causes discomfort and pain.
  • Ask about dry cough - The irritated laryngeal mucosa triggers a reflexive, non-productive cough.
  • Ask about fever - If laryngitis is caused by infection, low-grade fever may occur as part of the systemic inflammatory response.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Laryngitis"][/sc]
  • Check hoarse voice - The most recognisable sign, resulting from vocal cord oedema and inflammation.
  • Check red or swollen larynx - Direct visualisation via laryngoscopy may show inflammation and swelling of the laryngeal mucosa.
  • Check stridor (in severe cases) - Indicates significant laryngeal oedema or obstruction, typically seen in more severe or complicated cases.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Laryngitis"][/sc]
  • Stridor - Suggests significant upper airway obstruction, which may progress to respiratory distress. It may be a sign of epiglottitis, an emergency condition.
  • Difficulty breathing - May indicate impending airway compromise, particularly in cases of bacterial infection or in immunocompromised patients.
  • High fever - Can be a sign of bacterial laryngitis, abscess formation, or epiglottitis, which require urgent intervention.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Laryngitis"][/sc]
  • Chronic laryngitis without hoarseness - In some cases, patients may present with chronic cough or throat clearing, with little change in voice quality, particularly in smokers or individuals with chronic reflux.
  • Asymptomatic presentation - Laryngitis may occasionally present asymptomatically, particularly in cases related to gastro-oesophageal reflux or mild viral infections.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask laryngitis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Laryngitis"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Laryngoscopy - Visualisation of the larynx reveals oedema, erythema, or vocal cord abnormalities. This is used for persistent cases lasting >2-3.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Viral swabs - If a viral aetiology is suspected, nasopharyngeal swabs may confirm common viruses (e.g., rhinovirus, influenza); Throat swabs .

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Laryngitis"][/sc]
  • Pharyngitis - While both conditions may present with sore throat, pharyngitis typically lacks the hoarseness seen.
  • Epiglottitis - A more severe condition that presents with fever, drooling, stridor, and rapid-onset respiratory distress.
  • Vocal cord nodules - Chronic hoarseness without associated cough or throat discomfort, often in individuals with voice overuse.
  • Related alternative diagnosis - Distinguish from laryngitis using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Laryngitis"][/sc]
  • Chronic laryngitis - May develop if acute laryngitis is recurrent or prolonged, particularly with continued exposure to irritants like smoking or reflux.
  • Vocal cord nodules - Prolonged vocal strain and inflammation may lead to the development of nodules, causing persistent hoarseness.
  • Airway obstruction - Severe bacterial infections, such as epiglottitis, may lead to airway compromise and require emergency intervention.
