Polymyalgia Rheumatica (PMR)
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Polymyalgia Rheumatica (PMR)"][/sc]
  • Polymyalgia rheumatica (PMR) is an inflammatory disorder characterised by pain and stiffness, primarily affecting the shoulder and pelvic girdles.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise polymyalgia features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.

Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Polymyalgia Rheumatica (PMR)"][/sc]
  • PMR is believed to result from an immune-mediated inflammatory response, though the precise trigger remains unclear. It primarily affects the synovial tissues and bursae around large proximal joints. Elevated levels of pro-inflammatory cytokines such as interleukin-6 (IL-6) and tumour necrosis factor-alpha (TNF-α) contribute to systemic inflammation, leading to characteristic symptoms. PMR is closely associated with giant cell arteritis (GCA), with shared inflammatory pathways but differing clinical manifestations.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Polymyalgia Rheumatica (PMR)"][/sc]
  • PMR typically develops over weeks to months, with a subacute onset of pain and stiffness. Symptoms may fluctuate, but without treatment, the disease can last for years. Most patients experience symptom resolution within 2-3 years with appropriate corticosteroid therapy.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Polymyalgia Rheumatica (PMR)"][/sc]
  • Ask about bilateral shoulder pain - Commonest symptom; caused by synovitis and bursitis around the shoulder joints.
  • Ask about morning stiffness - Often lasting over 45 minutes, due to systemic inflammation affecting synovial membranes.
  • Ask about pelvic girdle pain - Pain in the hip region due to synovitis in the large proximal joints.
  • Ask about fatigue - Systemic inflammation leads to malaise and a general feeling of tiredness.
  • Ask about weight loss - Chronic inflammation can cause metabolic changes and reduced appetite.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Polymyalgia Rheumatica (PMR)"][/sc]
  • Check reduced range of motion in shoulders - Due to pain and synovitis affecting the joints.
  • Check weakness - Often perceived as weakness due to pain, although muscle strength is usually preserved.
  • Check tenderness over affected muscles - Tenderness is due to inflammation of periarticular structures rather than the muscles themselves.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Polymyalgia Rheumatica (PMR)"][/sc]
  • Headache and scalp tenderness - May indicate giant cell arteritis (GCA), a serious complication that requires urgent treatment to prevent vision loss.
  • Jaw claudication - A symptom of GCA, resulting from inflammation of the arteries supplying the jaw muscles.
  • Visual disturbances - Blurred or double vision may precede permanent vision loss in GCA.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Polymyalgia Rheumatica (PMR)"][/sc]
  • Some patients may present with isolated limb pain without morning stiffness, which can be misinterpreted as mechanical causes such as osteoarthritis. Mild cases of PMR may be mistaken for fibromyalgia, particularly when the inflammatory markers are normal.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask polymyalgia rheumatica (pmr).
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Polymyalgia Rheumatica (PMR)"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Raised erythrocyte sedimentation rate (ESR) - Typically >40 mm/hour, reflects systemic inflammation due to elevated fibrinogen levels.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Elevated C-reactive protein (CRP) - CRP is usually raised, often used to monitor disease activity and response to treatment; Normal creatine.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Polymyalgia Rheumatica (PMR)"][/sc]
  • Rheumatoid arthritis (RA) - RA typically involves small joints of the hands and feet, whereas PMR affects proximal.
  • Fibromyalgia - Fibromyalgia lacks the inflammatory markers found in PMR and often presents with chronic widespread.
  • Myositis - Elevation in creatine kinase (CK) helps differentiate myositis from PMR.
  • Related alternative diagnosis - Distinguish from polymyalgia rheumatica (pmr) using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Polymyalgia Rheumatica (PMR)"][/sc]
  • Giant cell arteritis (GCA) - Occurs in 10-15% of PMR patients and can lead to serious complications, including irreversible vision loss due to occlusive arteritis of the ophthalmic artery.
  • Corticosteroid side effects - Long-term steroid use can lead to osteoporosis, diabetes, hypertension, and increased risk of infections.
