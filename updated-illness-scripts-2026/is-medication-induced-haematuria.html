Medication-induced Haematuria
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Medication-induced Haematuria"][/sc]
  • Medication-induced haematuria is the presence of blood in the urine due to drug effects on the renal or urinary tract.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise medication features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Medication-induced Haematuria"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Medication-induced Haematuria"][/sc]
  • Medication-induced haematuria is relatively rare but may contribute to a small percentage of haematuria cases seen in primary and secondary care.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Medication-induced Haematuria"][/sc]
  • Medications may cause haematuria through several mechanisms:
  • Direct renal toxicity: Some drugs (e.g., certain antibiotics and NSAIDs) can damage renal tubules, leading to blood leakage into the urine.
  • Bladder irritation: Drugs that concentrate in the bladder (e.g., cyclophosphamide) may cause direct mucosal irritation, leading to haemorrhagic cystitis.
  • Coagulopathy: Anticoagulants and antiplatelet agents may impair haemostasis, increasing bleeding risks within the urinary tract.
  • Hypersensitivity reactions: Rarely, drugs can cause immune-mediated inflammation within the kidneys, leading to glomerulonephritis and haematuria.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Medication-induced Haematuria"][/sc]
  • Acute onset: Medication-induced haematuria often develops within days to weeks of starting the offending drug.
  • Chronic exposure: Some cases, particularly with NSAIDs or anticoagulants, may lead to intermittent or chronic haematuria over months if the medication is continued.
  • Resolution: Generally resolves once the medication is stopped, though in cases of direct renal damage, haematuria may persist longer.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Medication-induced Haematuria"][/sc]
  • Ask about visible blood in urine (macroscopic haematuria) - Often a sudden presentation, especially with anticoagulant-associated bleeding.
  • Ask about urinary frequency and urgency - Common with bladder-irritating drugs, due to mucosal irritation.
  • Ask about dysuria - Painful urination can occur if haemorrhagic cystitis is induced by drugs such as cyclophosphamide.
  • Ask about fatigue - May be due to underlying anaemia if blood loss is significant.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Medication-induced Haematuria"][/sc]
  • Check haematuria on urinalysis - Detection of blood in the urine via dipstick or microscopic examination.
  • Check low haemoglobin - If blood loss is chronic or significant, anaemia may develop.
  • Check abnormal coagulation profile - Commonly seen in patients on anticoagulants; prolonged PT or INR may suggest increased bleeding risk.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Medication-induced Haematuria"][/sc]
  • Clots in urine - Large clots may indicate significant bleeding, potentially warranting cessation of anticoagulant therapy and further evaluation.
  • Persistent haematuria despite stopping medication - Suggests possible underlying pathology like malignancy or structural abnormality, needing urgent referral.
  • Signs of systemic bleeding - Bleeding at other sites (e.g., gums, bruising) may indicate severe coagulopathy or overdose of anticoagulants.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Medication-induced Haematuria"][/sc]
  • Microscopic haematuria only - May be asymptomatic and only detected on routine screening, especially in patients on low-dose aspirin or NSAIDs.
  • Flank pain without visible haematuria - Some patients may experience renal inflammation or mild irritation without obvious blood in the urine.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask medication-induced haematuria.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Medication-induced Haematuria"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Urine dipstick and microscopy - Detects blood; absence of infection supports non-infective causes like medication; Full blood count (FBC) - May.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Coagulation profile - Especially in patients on anticoagulants, an elevated INR or prolonged PT may indicate bleeding risk; Renal function tests.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Medication-induced Haematuria"][/sc]
  • Urinary tract infection (UTI) - Differs as it often presents with pyuria and positive urine culture.
  • Bladder or renal malignancy - May cause painless haematuria and is usually more persistent.
  • Renal stones - Often causes colicky flank pain alongside haematuria.
  • Glomerulonephritis - Can cause haematuria with proteinuria, typically with hypertension and renal impairment.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Medication-induced Haematuria"][/sc]
  • Chronic anaemia - Repeated haematuria can lead to iron deficiency anaemia.
  • Acute kidney injury (AKI) - Possible in cases where NSAIDs or nephrotoxic agents cause renal damage.
  • Bladder dysfunction - Chronic haemorrhagic cystitis may impair bladder function.
