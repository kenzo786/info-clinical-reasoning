Rheumatic Fever
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Rheumatic Fever"][/sc]
  • Rheumatic Fever (RF) is an autoimmune, inflammatory disease that occurs as a delayed complication of a group A Streptococcus (GAS) pharyngeal infection.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise rheumatic features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Rheumatic Fever"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Rheumatic Fever"][/sc]
  • Risk factors: Risk factors for rheumatic fever include relevant predisposition, comorbidity, and exposure history.
  • Higher-risk or missed groups: Rheumatic Fever may be missed when presentation is atypical or obscured by comorbidity.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Rheumatic Fever"][/sc]
  • Step 1: Group A Streptococcus (GAS) pharyngeal infection initiates the immune response.
  • Step 2: Cross-reactivity occurs due to molecular mimicry between GAS antigens (e.g., M protein) and host tissues (e.g., heart valves, synovium, basal ganglia).
  • Step 3: Autoimmune inflammatory processes lead to multisystem involvement, causing carditis, arthritis, chorea, and skin manifestations.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Rheumatic Fever"][/sc]
  • Acute Phase: Symptoms develop 2-4 weeks after a GAS throat infection and can last weeks to months without treatment.
  • Chronic Phase: Long-term sequelae, particularly rheumatic heart disease, may persist or worsen over years, especially with recurrent episodes.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Rheumatic Fever"][/sc]
  • Ask about sore Throat: Preceding GAS pharyngitis is often reported but may be absent; caused by streptococcal infection of the pharyngeal mucosa.
  • Ask about fever: A systemic inflammatory response to infection and autoimmune activation.
  • Ask about joint Pain: Migratory arthritis, particularly affecting large joints, due to immune complex deposition in synovium.
  • Ask about fatigue: Generalised inflammatory response and anaemia of chronic disease.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Rheumatic Fever"][/sc]
  • Check heart Murmur: Suggestive of carditis, caused by inflammation or fibrosis of heart valves.
  • Check erythema Marginatum: Rash with a serpiginous border, reflecting vasculitis and immune complex deposition in the skin.
  • Check sydenham's Chorea: Involuntary movements due to autoimmune attack on basal ganglia.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Rheumatic Fever"][/sc]
  • Severe Carditis: Indicates risk of heart failure or progression to chronic rheumatic heart disease.
  • Sydenham's Chorea: Associated with neuropsychiatric complications and prolonged disease course.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Rheumatic Fever"][/sc]
  • Isolated carditis without arthritis or rash, which may mimic congenital or viral causes.
  • Silent carditis, detected only via echocardiography in asymptomatic individuals.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask rheumatic fever.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Rheumatic Fever"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Throat Swab Culture: Identifies GAS; useful in acute phases; ASO and Anti-DNase B Titres: Elevated, reflecting recent streptococcal infection.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Echocardiography: Detects valvular lesions, vegetations, or cardiac dysfunction; CRP/ESR: Raised levels indicate systemic inflammation.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Rheumatic Fever"][/sc]
  • A 10-year-old child presents with a 2-week history of migratory joint pain, fever, and fatigue, following a sore throat. Examination reveals a murmur, rash, and involuntary movements.
  • Patient with a 2-week history of migratory joint pain, fever, and fatigue, following a sore throat. Examination reveals a murmur, rash, and involuntary movements, consistent with rheumatic fever.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Rheumatic Fever"][/sc]
  • Infective Endocarditis - Distinguished by persistent fever and positive blood cultures.
  • Juvenile Idiopathic Arthritis - Chronic joint inflammation without cardiac involvement.
  • Post-Streptococcal Reactive Arthritis - Non-migratory arthritis and no carditis.
  • Related alternative diagnosis - Distinguish from rheumatic fever using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Rheumatic Fever"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Assess severity, physiological stability, and immediate complications.
  • Core care: Deliver condition-specific management for rheumatic fever, treat underlying drivers, and coordinate appropriate specialist input.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Reassess symptom trajectory, examination findings, and objective markers to confirm response in rheumatic fever.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Escalate urgently for clinical deterioration, evolving red flags, or failure to improve in suspected rheumatic fever.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Rheumatic Fever"][/sc]
  • Rheumatic Heart Disease: Chronic valve dysfunction leading to heart failure.
  • Heart Failure: Resulting from severe or repeated carditis episodes.
