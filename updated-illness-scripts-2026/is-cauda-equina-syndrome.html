Cauda Equina Syndrome
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Cauda Equina Syndrome"][/sc]
  • Cauda equina syndrome is acute compression of lumbosacral nerve roots causing bladder, bowel, saddle sensory, and lower-limb neurological dysfunction.
  • UKMLA essentials: saddle anaesthesia or urinary retention, progressive neurological deficit, urgent MRI and spinal surgical assessment.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Cauda Equina Syndrome"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Cauda Equina Syndrome"][/sc]
  • In hospitalised populations, it is most commonly seen among those with herniated lumbar discs or significant spinal trauma.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Cauda Equina Syndrome"][/sc]
  • CES occurs when the cauda equina nerves (L2-S5) are compressed, most often by a herniated disc, tumour, abscess, or trauma.
  • This compression disrupts the transmission of motor and sensory signals, particularly affecting lower extremities, bladder, and bowel control.
  • Ischaemia due to nerve compression can lead to irreversible nerve damage if untreated, resulting in permanent dysfunction.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Cauda Equina Syndrome"][/sc]
  • CES typically presents acutely, but symptoms may develop subacutely over days to weeks.
  • Without intervention, progressive worsening occurs, with potential for permanent disability if treatment is delayed.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Cauda Equina Syndrome"][/sc]
  • Ask about low back pain - Commonly seen as one of the earliest symptoms, arising from nerve root irritation.
  • Ask about bilateral sciatica - Radicular pain radiating down both legs due to compression of lumbar and sacral nerve roots.
  • Ask about urinary retention - One of the hallmark symptoms due to loss of bladder innervation; often an initial presenting complaint.
  • Ask about saddle anaesthesia - Numbness in the perineal region (saddle area) is highly specific for CES, indicating nerve root compression at S2-S4.
  • Ask about sexual dysfunction - Commonly reported, stemming from sacral nerve impairment.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Cauda Equina Syndrome"][/sc]
  • Check decreased anal sphincter tone - Indicates sacral nerve damage, assessed by digital rectal examination.
  • Check reduced or absent lower limb reflexes - Due to impaired nerve function in the lower extremities.
  • Check weakness in lower extremities - Particularly in dorsiflexion or plantar flexion, depending on affected nerve roots.
  • Check sensory loss in saddle region - Reflecting impaired sensory innervation from sacral nerves.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Cauda Equina Syndrome"][/sc]
  • Saddle anaesthesia - Loss of sensation in the perineal area, indicating high risk of permanent sensory loss if untreated.
  • Urinary retention or incontinence - Due to loss of bladder control; untreated, this can lead to chronic incontinence and bladder damage.
  • Bowel incontinence - Indicates significant nerve compression with risk of permanent bowel dysfunction.
  • Progressive motor weakness - Sign of severe nerve root involvement with risk of permanent disability.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Cauda Equina Syndrome"][/sc]
  • Unilateral leg symptoms - Less common; may be mistaken for unilateral radiculopathy.
  • Gradual onset of symptoms - May present with slowly progressing symptoms, making early diagnosis challenging.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features or comorbidity can mask the underlying diagnosis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Cauda Equina Syndrome"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: MRI of the lumbar spine - Gold standard for diagnosing CES, showing nerve compression.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Bladder scan - Assesses post-void residual volume, with high volumes suggestive of urinary retention due to nerve impairment; Electromyography (EMG) .
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Cauda Equina Syndrome"][/sc]
  • A 45-year-old male presents with a three-day history of severe low back pain, bilateral leg pain, urinary retention, and perineal numbness, with onset of lower extremity weakness.
  • Adult with symptom pattern and objective findings suggestive of cauda equina syndrome, requiring focused confirmation and risk stratification.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Cauda Equina Syndrome"][/sc]
  • Acute lumbar disc herniation - Similar symptoms but lacks urinary or bowel dysfunction.
  • Spinal epidural abscess - Presents with fever and increased inflammatory markers.
  • Spinal tumour - Symptoms progress more gradually; imaging shows a mass.
  • Alternative serious diagnosis - Distinguishing history, examination, and targeted investigations do not support cauda equina syndrome.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Cauda Equina Syndrome"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Assess physiological stability and urgent complications at presentation, and initiate early stabilisation where required.
  • Core care: Deliver condition-specific treatment for cauda equina syndrome, address underlying drivers, and involve appropriate specialist support.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Reassess symptom trajectory, objective markers, and treatment response over time to detect complications early.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Worsening symptoms, physiological deterioration, or failure to improve should trigger urgent reassessment for severe or alternative pathology in cauda equina syndrome.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Cauda Equina Syndrome"][/sc]
  • Permanent bladder dysfunction - High risk if decompression is delayed.
  • Chronic neuropathic pain - Due to persistent nerve damage, leading to poor quality of life.
  • Sexual dysfunction - Common and often permanent complication of untreated CES.
