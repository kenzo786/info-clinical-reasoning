Cat Scratch Disease
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Cat Scratch Disease"][/sc]
  • Cat scratch disease (CSD) is an infectious disease caused by the bacterium Bartonella henselae , typically transmitted through scratches, bites, or exposure to saliva from infected cats.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise scratch features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Cat Scratch Disease"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Cat Scratch Disease"][/sc]
  • UK GP Population: Rare, but typically seen in paediatric and adolescent populations.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Cat Scratch Disease"][/sc]
  • Bartonella henselae enters the skin through a scratch or bite, leading to local infection.
  • The bacteria invade local lymph nodes, causing lymphadenitis due to inflammatory and immune responses.
  • In some cases, the bacteria may disseminate, causing systemic symptoms or affecting organs such as the liver, spleen, eyes, and central nervous system.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Cat Scratch Disease"][/sc]
  • Incubation Period: 3-10 days post-exposure.
  • Acute Phase: Local lymphadenopathy develops 1-3 weeks after exposure and can last for several weeks.
  • Resolution: Most cases resolve over 2-4 months without intervention, though some lymphadenopathy may persist.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Cat Scratch Disease"][/sc]
  • Ask about lymphadenopathy - Localised swollen lymph nodes, often tender, usually near the site of the scratch or bite, due to lymph node infection and inflammation.
  • Ask about fever - Low-grade fever commonly accompanies lymphadenopathy, driven by the body's immune response to infection.
  • Ask about malaise - Non-specific sense of unwellness, likely due to systemic immune activation.
  • Ask about headache - Present in some cases, potentially linked to immune-mediated cytokine release.
  • Ask about fatigue - Fatigue may persist beyond the acute phase as the body recovers from the infection.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Cat Scratch Disease"][/sc]
  • Check regional Lymphadenopathy - Often the most prominent sign, with swollen, tender lymph nodes usually near the inoculation site.
  • Check inoculation Lesion - A small, erythematous papule or pustule at the site of the scratch or bite, indicating the initial entry point of infection.
  • Check fever - Often mild, consistent with systemic immune response.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Cat Scratch Disease"][/sc]
  • Hepatosplenic Involvement - Hepatosplenomegaly may indicate systemic spread, warranting further evaluation to rule out liver or spleen abscesses.
  • Neurological Symptoms - Seizures, altered mental status, or focal neurological deficits may signal central nervous system involvement.
  • Ocular Involvement - Visual disturbances or uveitis could indicate Parinaud oculoglandular syndrome, a rare form of CSD affecting the eye and preauricular lymph nodes.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Cat Scratch Disease"][/sc]
  • Endocarditis - Rare, presents with fever, heart murmur, and signs of systemic infection, particularly in immunocompromised patients.
  • Bone or Joint Involvement - May present as osteomyelitis or arthritis, caused by haematogenous spread of Bartonella henselae.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features or comorbidity can mask the underlying diagnosis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Cat Scratch Disease"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Serology - Detection of Bartonella henselae antibodies. A positive IgM indicates recent infection.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Polymerase Chain Reaction (PCR) - PCR of lymph node aspirate or blood to detect B. henselae DNA, particularly in atypical cases.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Cat Scratch Disease"][/sc]
  • An 8-year-old boy presents with low-grade fever, malaise, and tender lymphadenopathy in the axilla following a recent scratch from a household cat, with no systemic symptoms.
  • Adult with symptom pattern and objective findings suggestive of cat scratch disease, requiring focused confirmation and risk stratification.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Cat Scratch Disease"][/sc]
  • Toxoplasmosis - Presents with lymphadenopathy, but exposure often involves raw meat or cat faeces, not scratches.
  • Infectious Mononucleosis - Commonly causes generalised lymphadenopathy, fever, and sore throat, with positive Monospot test.
  • Lymphoma - Persistent, firm lymphadenopathy without fever, often accompanied by weight loss and night sweats.
  • Alternative serious diagnosis - Distinguishing history, examination, and targeted investigations do not support cat scratch disease.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Cat Scratch Disease"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Assess physiological stability and urgent complications at presentation, and initiate early stabilisation where required.
  • Core care: Deliver condition-specific treatment for cat scratch disease, address underlying drivers, and involve appropriate specialist support.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Reassess symptom trajectory, objective markers, and treatment response over time to detect complications early.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Worsening symptoms, physiological deterioration, or failure to improve should trigger urgent reassessment for severe or alternative pathology in cat scratch disease.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Cat Scratch Disease"][/sc]
  • Hepatosplenic Abscess - Rare, due to bacterial spread; managed with antibiotics and, occasionally, drainage.
  • Ocular Complications - Including Parinaud oculoglandular syndrome, potentially leading to uveitis if untreated.
  • Neurological Sequelae - CNS infection can lead to chronic neurological deficits, though rare.
