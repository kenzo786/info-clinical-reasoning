White Coat Hypertension
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="White Coat Hypertension"][/sc]
  • White coat hypertension is a condition where patients exhibit elevated blood pressure (BP) readings in clinical settings but have normal readings outside of the healthcare environment.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise white features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="White Coat Hypertension"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="White Coat Hypertension"][/sc]
  • It is more common in individuals over 50 years of age and in females.
  • Approximately one-third of individuals diagnosed with hypertension based solely on clinic measurements may actually have white coat hypertension.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="White Coat Hypertension"][/sc]
  • The core mechanism is the acute stress response triggered by the clinical environment, leading to a surge in sympathetic nervous system activity.
  • This sympathetic activation results in vasoconstriction and increased heart rate, causing transient elevations in blood pressure.
  • While the homeostatic mechanisms normally maintain BP at baseline levels, repeated exposure to clinical settings may condition patients to exhibit these responses whenever in similar environments, without true underlying hypertension.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="White Coat Hypertension"][/sc]
  • White coat hypertension typically presents as an episodic phenomenon, with BP elevation occurring acutely during healthcare visits and returning to normal once the patient is in a non-clinical environment.
  • In some patients, white coat hypertension can persist for years if not identified, with potential progression to sustained hypertension due to vascular changes over time.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="White Coat Hypertension"][/sc]
  • Ask about asymptomatic in most cases - The majority of individuals with white coat hypertension do not experience symptoms because the BP elevation is transient, with associated clinical features.
  • Ask about occasional headache or lightheadedness - In some cases, the acute rise in BP during clinical visits may cause mild symptoms such as headaches, with associated clinical features.
  • Ask about anxiety-related symptoms - Patients may report palpitations, sweating, or nervousness, which are driven by the stress and anxiety of being in a healthcare setting.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="White Coat Hypertension"][/sc]
  • Check elevated blood pressure in clinic - The most significant finding, usually >140/90 mmHg in office settings, often normal when checked outside.
  • Check normal cardiovascular examination - Apart from elevated BP, the rest of the examination (heart sounds, peripheral pulses) is typically normal.
  • Check signs of anxiety - The patient may exhibit visible signs of nervousness, such as rapid breathing, fidgeting, or sweating during the clinic visit.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="White Coat Hypertension"][/sc]
  • Persistently elevated BP despite ambulatory/home monitoring - This may indicate sustained hypertension rather than white coat hypertension, necessitating further investigation and possible treatment.
  • Signs of end-organ damage - Symptoms such as chest pain, visual disturbances, or signs of heart failure may suggest true hypertension with target organ damage, requiring urgent intervention.
  • Severe headache or neurological deficits - These could indicate hypertensive emergency, though rare in white coat hypertension, and demand immediate attention.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="White Coat Hypertension"][/sc]
  • Masked hypertension - In this condition, the patient exhibits normal BP in the clinic but elevated readings at home, opposite to white coat hypertension.
  • Patients with known anxiety disorders - These individuals may show highly variable BP, not necessarily confined to clinic settings, complicating the diagnosis.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask white coat hypertension.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="White Coat Hypertension"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Focused bedside assessment and first-line testing to assess severity and immediate risk in suspected white coat hypertension.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Home blood pressure monitoring (HBPM) - Patients can record their BP at home. If readings at home are consistently <135/85 mmHg.

Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="White Coat Hypertension"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Assess severity, physiological stability, and immediate complications.
  • Core care: Deliver condition-specific management for white coat hypertension, treat underlying drivers, and coordinate appropriate specialist input.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="White Coat Hypertension"][/sc]
  • Progression to sustained hypertension - Around 25% of individuals with white coat hypertension eventually develop true hypertension, likely due to cumulative vascular stress.
  • Increased cardiovascular risk - While generally low, individuals with white coat hypertension may have a slightly higher risk of cardiovascular events compared to normotensive individuals.
