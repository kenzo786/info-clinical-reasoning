Blepharitis
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Blepharitis"][/sc]
  • Blepharitis is a chronic inflammation of the eyelid margins, typically caused by bacterial colonisation, dysfunction of the meibomian glands, or skin conditions such as seborrhoeic dermatitis or rosacea.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise blepharitis features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Blepharitis"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Blepharitis"][/sc]
  • Risk factors: Risk of blepharitis rises with relevant predisposition, comorbidity, and exposure profile.
  • Higher-risk or missed groups: Blepharitis may be missed when presentations are atypical or comorbidity obscures key features.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Blepharitis"][/sc]
  • Blepharitis arises from inflammation due to the overgrowth of commensal bacteria (e.g., Staphylococcus spp.) on the eyelids, leading to biofilm formation.
  • Dysfunction of the meibomian glands reduces lipid secretion, causing tear film instability and increased evaporation, exacerbating dryness and irritation.
  • Skin conditions such as seborrhoeic dermatitis contribute to flaking and inflammation around the eyelids, further disrupting the ocular surface.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Blepharitis"][/sc]
  • Acute phase: Rapid onset of irritation, redness, and crusting, often in response to bacterial overgrowth or exacerbation of underlying skin conditions.
  • Chronic phase: Persistent or relapsing symptoms lasting weeks to months, with periods of worsening during environmental triggers or stress.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Blepharitis"][/sc]
  • Ask about itchy eyelids: Caused by inflammatory mediators and irritation of the eyelid skin.
  • Ask about burning or gritty sensation: Tear film instability leads to corneal exposure and dryness.
  • Ask about crusting around the eyelashes: Accumulation of debris and bacterial biofilm during periods of inflammation.
  • Ask about photophobia: Inflammation and dryness can sensitise the ocular surface to light.
  • Ask about intermittent blurred vision: Tear film instability disrupts the refractive surface.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Blepharitis"][/sc]
  • Check redness and swelling of the eyelid margins: Indicates inflammation from bacterial toxins or meibomian gland dysfunction.
  • Check flaking or scales on the eyelashes: Represents desquamation due to seborrhoeic dermatitis or biofilm formation.
  • Check plugged or capped meibomian glands: Evidence of posterior blepharitis and lipid secretion dysfunction.
  • Check conjunctival hyperaemia: Result of associated ocular surface inflammation.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Blepharitis"][/sc]
  • Severe eye pain: May indicate complications such as keratitis or corneal ulceration.
  • Loss of vision: Suggestive of advanced complications like corneal scarring or severe dry eye disease.
  • Asymmetrical presentation: Raises suspicion for malignancies like sebaceous carcinoma.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Blepharitis"][/sc]
  • Isolated eyelid oedema: May be mistaken for an allergic reaction.
  • Minimal visible crusting: Often seen in posterior blepharitis, where meibomian gland dysfunction is predominant.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features or comorbidity can mask the underlying diagnosis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Blepharitis"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Slit-lamp examination: Reveals plugged meibomian glands, eyelid margin inflammation, and debris along the eyelashes.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Tear break-up time (TBUT): Reduced TBUT indicates tear film instability due to poor lipid production; Microbial cultures: Occasionally indicated to rule.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Blepharitis"][/sc]
  • A 65-year-old man with a 6-month history of bilateral itchy, burning eyelids, crusting along the lashes, and intermittent blurred vision. Symptoms worsen in the morning and improve with warm compresses.
  • Adult with symptom pattern and objective findings suggestive of blepharitis, requiring focused confirmation and risk stratification.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Blepharitis"][/sc]
  • Dry eye syndrome - Differentiated by absence of significant eyelid inflammation or crusting.
  • Conjunctivitis - Typically has more prominent conjunctival injection and discharge.
  • Demodicosis - Presence of cylindrical dandruff and demodex mites on eyelash evaluation.
  • Alternative serious diagnosis - Distinguishing history, examination, and targeted investigations do not support blepharitis.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Blepharitis"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Assess physiological stability and urgent complications at presentation, and initiate early stabilisation where required.
  • Core care: Deliver condition-specific treatment for blepharitis, address underlying drivers, and involve appropriate specialist support.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Reassess symptom trajectory, objective markers, and treatment response over time to detect complications early.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Worsening symptoms, physiological deterioration, or failure to improve should trigger urgent reassessment for severe or alternative pathology in blepharitis.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Blepharitis"][/sc]
  • Chalazion: Chronic inflammation of meibomian glands.
  • Corneal damage: From persistent dryness or secondary infection.
  • Recurrent conjunctivitis: Due to bacterial spread from the eyelid margin.
