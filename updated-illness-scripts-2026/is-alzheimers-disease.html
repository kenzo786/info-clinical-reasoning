Alzheimer's Disease
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Alzheimer's Disease"][/sc]
  • Alzheimer's disease is a progressive neurodegenerative dementia causing gradual decline in memory, executive function, language, and daily living independence.
  • UKMLA essentials: progressive episodic memory loss, delirium or safeguarding concern, assess cognition with collateral history.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Alzheimer's Disease"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Alzheimer's Disease"][/sc]
  • Risk factors: Risk of alzheimer's disease rises with relevant predisposition, comorbidity, and exposure profile.
  • Higher-risk or missed groups: Alzheimer's Disease may be missed when presentations are atypical or comorbidity obscures key features.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Alzheimer's Disease"][/sc]
  • Alzheimer’s disease is marked by the accumulation of amyloid-beta (Aβ) peptides forming plaques in the brain. These plaques disrupt cell function, particularly in the hippocampus and cortical areas essential for memory and cognition.
  • Tau protein in neurons becomes hyperphosphorylated, leading to neurofibrillary tangles that destabilise microtubules, impeding intracellular transport and resulting in neuronal death.
  • Neuroinflammation is triggered, exacerbating neuronal damage and disrupting synaptic communication. This ongoing cycle leads to progressive brain atrophy, particularly in the temporal and parietal lobes.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Alzheimer's Disease"][/sc]
  • Preclinical: Silent accumulation of Aβ and tau proteins occurs years before symptoms appear.
  • Mild cognitive impairment (MCI): Subtle cognitive difficulties emerge, but daily functioning is not significantly impaired.
  • Mild Alzheimer’s disease: Noticeable memory loss and cognitive decline with difficulty in complex tasks.
  • Moderate to severe stages: Increasing dependency, severe memory impairment, language loss, behavioural changes, and inability to manage daily activities.
  • End stage: Profound cognitive and physical debilitation, leading to total care dependency.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Alzheimer's Disease"][/sc]
  • Ask about memory loss - Often the earliest symptom, especially with recent events, due to hippocampal neuron loss and disruption of memory consolidation pathways.
  • Ask about difficulty with complex tasks - Challenges in planning or organising, as frontal and parietal lobes are affected by plaque and tangle deposition.
  • Ask about language difficulties - Trouble finding words or following conversations, stemming from progressive loss in temporal lobe function.
  • Ask about disorientation - Confusion regarding time, place, and events due to widespread cortical atrophy.
  • Ask about behavioural changes - Apathy, mood swings, or aggression arise as a result of changes in frontal lobe and amygdala function.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Alzheimer's Disease"][/sc]
  • Check memory impairment on cognitive testing - Poor recall on tasks such as word-list learning, reflecting hippocampal degeneration.
  • Check impaired executive function - Shown by difficulty with multi-step tasks, as frontal lobe function declines.
  • Check language deficits - Observed as word-finding difficulty or reduced fluency due to temporal lobe involvement.
  • Check visuospatial deficits - Issues with perception and spatial tasks, linked to parietal lobe atrophy.
  • Check affective symptoms - Anxiety, depression, or apathy often appear, potentially due to limbic system involvement.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Alzheimer's Disease"][/sc]
  • Rapid cognitive decline - May suggest alternative diagnoses like prion disease or dementia with Lewy bodies.
  • Severe behavioural changes early in disease course - Indicates possible frontotemporal dementia (FTD) rather than Alzheimer’s.
  • Motor symptoms - Tremors or rigidity could point to dementia with Lewy bodies or Parkinson's disease dementia.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Alzheimer's Disease"][/sc]
  • Posterior cortical atrophy - Primarily visuospatial impairments, due to early parietal involvement.
  • Early-onset Alzheimer’s - Symptoms may begin before age 65 with faster progression, often linked to genetic mutations.
  • Primary progressive aphasia - Language dysfunction without memory loss, often due to temporal lobe involvement in early disease.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features or comorbidity can mask the underlying diagnosis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Alzheimer's Disease"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Cognitive assessment with collateral history, functional assessment, medication review, and examination for reversible contributors or focal neurology.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Blood tests for reversible cognitive causes; brain imaging to exclude structural pathology and support subtype assessment; serial cognition and function review for trajectory.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Alzheimer's Disease"][/sc]
  • A 75-year-old woman has 18 months of worsening memory, disorientation, and impaired medication and finance management, with corroborative decline from family.
  • Older adult with insidious progressive cognitive and functional decline in memory-dominant pattern, concerning for Alzheimer's disease.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Alzheimer's Disease"][/sc]
  • Vascular dementia - Stepwise decline with focal neurological signs and cerebrovascular burden on imaging supports vascular aetiology.
  • Dementia with Lewy bodies - Fluctuating cognition, visual hallucinations, and parkinsonism suggest Lewy body disease.
  • Frontotemporal dementia - Earlier behavioural disinhibition or language-predominant change with relative memory sparing suggests FTD.
  • Depression-related cognitive impairment - Prominent affective symptoms with variable effort and improvement after mood treatment suggest pseudodementia.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Alzheimer's Disease"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Exclude delirium and acute reversible causes, assess capacity and safety risk, and involve carers early in planning.
  • Core care: Combine cognitive-enhancing therapy where appropriate with non-pharmacological support, comorbidity optimisation, and structured social-care planning.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Regular review of cognition, function, behavioural symptoms, carer strain, and safeguarding issues should guide care escalation.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Rapid decline, new neurological deficits, severe behavioural disturbance, or unsafe living circumstances require urgent specialist reassessment.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Alzheimer's Disease"][/sc]
  • Increased risk of falls - Due to cognitive and motor impairments, potentially leading to fractures or head injury.
  • Infections - Aspiration pneumonia risk increases as swallowing deteriorates in advanced stages.
  • Malnutrition and dehydration - Result from impaired feeding and self-care abilities, leading to general decline.
  • Prognosis - Progressive with a median survival of 8–10 years post-diagnosis; disease-modifying treatments are limited.
