Phaeochromocytoma
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Phaeochromocytoma"][/sc]
  • Phaeochromocytoma is a rare neuroendocrine tumour originating from chromaffin cells of the adrenal medulla.

Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Phaeochromocytoma"][/sc]
  • Phaeochromocytomas arise from chromaffin cells, which are responsible for synthesising and secreting catecholamines. In normal physiology, catecholamine release is tightly regulated by the autonomic nervous system.
  • These tumours produce excess catecholamines autonomously, leading to overstimulation of adrenergic receptors. The β-adrenergic stimulation increases heart rate, contractility, and vasodilation, while α-adrenergic stimulation causes vasoconstriction, both contributing to hypertension and systemic effects.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Phaeochromocytoma"][/sc]
  • Phaeochromocytomas may present acutely with sudden hypertensive crises or more chronically with intermittent episodes of symptoms such as headaches and palpitations. Over time, tumour growth can lead to more consistent symptomatology, although progression rates vary between individuals.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Phaeochromocytoma"][/sc]
  • Ask about hypertension (paroxysmal or sustained) - Due to excessive catecholamine secretion, leading to increased vascular resistance (α-adrenergic) and cardiac output (β-adrenergic).
  • Ask about headaches - Caused by vasoconstriction and hypertensive surges.
  • Ask about palpitations - Catecholamine excess increases heart rate and contractility via β1-adrenergic receptors.
  • Ask about excessive sweating - Due to sympathetic overactivation and increased thermogenesis.
  • Ask about anxiety or panic attacks - Catecholamine surges mimic adrenergic ‘fight or flight’ responses, resulting in acute anxiety episodes.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Phaeochromocytoma"][/sc]
  • Check hypertension - Persistent or episodic, due to increased catecholamine-mediated vasoconstriction.
  • Check tachycardia - Results from catecholamine excess acting on β1-adrenergic receptors in the heart.
  • Check pallor or flushing - Alternating sympathetic vasoconstriction and secondary vasodilation from β-adrenergic effects.
  • Check weight loss - Increased basal metabolic rate from prolonged adrenergic stimulation.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Phaeochromocytoma"][/sc]
  • Hypertensive crisis - Sudden, severe hypertension can result in end-organ damage, such as myocardial infarction, stroke, or acute renal failure.
  • Cardiac arrhythmias - Prolonged catecholamine excess can lead to life-threatening arrhythmias, including ventricular tachycardia and fibrillation.
  • Severe anxiety or agitation - Episodes may escalate to confusion or delirium in extreme cases of catecholamine crisis.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Phaeochromocytoma"][/sc]
  • Asymptomatic hypertension - Some patients may present with sustained hypertension without typical adrenergic symptoms, which can delay diagnosis.
  • Episodic gastrointestinal complaints - Nausea, vomiting, or abdominal pain may be the primary symptoms due to catecholamine effects on gut motility.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask phaeochromocytoma.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Phaeochromocytoma"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Plasma free metanephrines - Elevated due to increased catecholamine metabolism (sensitivity ~97%); 24-hour urinary catecholamines and metanephrines - Higher levels confirm.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Targeted investigations to confirm diagnosis, assess complications, and monitor response in phaeochromocytoma.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Phaeochromocytoma"][/sc]
  • Essential hypertension - Typically sustained hypertension without episodic symptoms or associated catecholamine effects.
  • Hyperthyroidism - Can cause palpitations and anxiety but usually includes other signs like weight loss, tremor.
  • Panic disorder - May mimic episodic catecholamine surges but lacks sustained hypertension and objective catecholamine elevation.
  • Related alternative diagnosis - Distinguish from phaeochromocytoma using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Phaeochromocytoma"][/sc]
  • Cardiac complications - Prolonged catecholamine excess can lead to cardiomyopathy, arrhythmias, or heart failure.
  • Stroke - Hypertensive crisis may result in haemorrhagic or ischaemic stroke.
  • Chronic hypertension - Even after tumour removal, long-term vascular changes may sustain hypertension.
