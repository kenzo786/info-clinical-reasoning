Medication-Induced Syncope
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Medication-Induced Syncope"][/sc]
  • Medication-induced syncope refers to a transient loss of consciousness due to a temporary reduction in cerebral perfusion, triggered by the pharmacological effects of certain medications.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise medication features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.

Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Medication-Induced Syncope"][/sc]
  • Certain medications can cause vasodilation, slow heart rate (bradycardia), or induce dehydration (through diuresis), reducing venous return and cardiac output. When cerebral perfusion drops below a critical threshold, syncope occurs. Common pathways include:
  • Antihypertensives: Cause vasodilation or inhibit the autonomic response, leading to hypotension.
  • Diuretics: Lead to dehydration, electrolyte imbalances, and reduced intravascular volume, contributing to syncope.
  • Antiarrhythmics: Can induce bradycardia or arrhythmias that impair cardiac output.
  • Psychotropics: May cause orthostatic hypotension due to alpha-adrenergic blockade.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Medication-Induced Syncope"][/sc]
  • Acute onset: Syncope can occur within minutes to hours after medication administration, especially when initiating or increasing the Use local protocol-directed treatment.
  • Chronic course: Some patients experience recurrent episodes, especially with long-term use of medications that affect blood pressure or heart rate.
  • Recovery: Episodes are typically brief (seconds to a few minutes), with full recovery once cerebral perfusion is restored. However, the underlying risk persists as long as the medication is in use.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Medication-Induced Syncope"][/sc]
  • Ask about transient loss of consciousness - Due to reduced cerebral perfusion; patients experience sudden collapse, often with no warning.
  • Ask about dizziness or lightheadedness - Caused by hypotension or bradycardia before syncope occurs.
  • Ask about palpitations - Some patients may report feeling their heart racing (if arrhythmia is involved) before syncope.
  • Ask about fatigue - Medication-induced hypotension may cause a feeling of tiredness.
  • Ask about orthostatic symptoms - Dizziness or syncope upon standing due to impaired autonomic response to posture changes.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Medication-Induced Syncope"][/sc]
  • Check hypotension - Commonly observed, particularly in patients on antihypertensives or diuretics, explaining reduced cerebral perfusion.
  • Check bradycardia - Can occur in patients on beta-blockers or calcium channel blockers, reducing cardiac output.
  • Check electrolyte imbalances - Hyponatraemia or hypokalaemia due to diuretics can predispose to arrhythmias and syncope.
  • Check pallor - A common feature during syncope, secondary to reduced peripheral perfusion.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Medication-Induced Syncope"][/sc]
  • Severe bradycardia (<40 bpm) - Indicates potential for life-threatening arrhythmias or severe cardiac output reduction, possibly leading to cardiac arrest.
  • Prolonged unconsciousness (>5 minutes) - Suggests a more serious underlying condition such as prolonged arrhythmia, which may lead to cardiac arrest.
  • Chest pain - May indicate myocardial ischaemia or infarction, necessitating urgent evaluation.
  • Seizure-like activity - Could suggest convulsive syncope due to prolonged cerebral hypoxia.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Medication-Induced Syncope"][/sc]
  • Asymptomatic hypotension - Some patients may not notice any symptoms before collapsing, especially the elderly, who may not report dizziness.
  • Confusion rather than syncope - Particularly in elderly patients, medication-induced syncope can present with altered mental status without complete loss of consciousness.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask medication-induced syncope.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Medication-Induced Syncope"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Electrocardiogram (ECG): May show bradycardia, prolonged QT interval, or arrhythmias associated with medication-induced syncope (e.g. beta-blockers, antiarrhythmics); Blood pressure monitoring: Orthostatic.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Electrolyte panel: Diuretics may cause hypokalaemia or hyponatraemia, leading to arrhythmias and syncope; Holter monitoring: For patients with suspected intermittent arrhythmias.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Medication-Induced Syncope"][/sc]
  • Vasovagal syncope - Typically associated with a clear trigger such as emotional stress or prolonged standing; prodromal.
  • Cardiac arrhythmias - May present with palpitations or occur suddenly without provocation, often without the postural changes.
  • Orthostatic hypotension - Distinguish from medication-induced syncope using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Medication-Induced Syncope"][/sc]
  • Falls and fractures: Syncope, particularly in elderly patients, can lead to traumatic injuries such as hip fractures.
  • Cardiac arrest: In rare cases, severe bradycardia or arrhythmias may progress to cardiac arrest if untreated.
  • Recurrent syncope: Without addressing the underlying medication cause, patients may experience repeated episodes, increasing the risk of injury.
