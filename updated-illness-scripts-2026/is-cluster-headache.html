Cluster Headache
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Cluster Headache"][/sc]
  • Cluster headache is a primary headache disorder characterised by recurrent, unilateral attacks of severe orbital, supraorbital, or temporal pain, often accompanied by ipsilateral autonomic symptoms (e.g., lacrimation, nasal congestion).
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise cluster features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.

Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Cluster Headache"][/sc]
  • Cluster headaches are believed to originate from the hypothalamus, which plays a role in circadian rhythms. Dysfunctional hypothalamic activation triggers the trigeminal-autonomic reflex, leading to pain via activation of the trigeminal nerve and associated autonomic symptoms through the parasympathetic system.
  • Neurotransmitter changes, particularly involving serotonin and calcitonin gene-related peptide (CGRP), also play a role in the pathogenesis of cluster headaches.
  • Circadian periodicity is thought to arise from abnormal hypothalamic control, leading to consistent daily attacks during active bouts.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Cluster Headache"][/sc]
  • Cluster headaches typically present in episodic forms (about 85% of cases). Patients experience daily attacks for 4-12 weeks (the "cluster period") followed by months or even years of remission.
  • Each headache attack lasts from 15 minutes to 3 hours, and attacks can occur up to 8 times per day, often at similar times each day.
  • Chronic cluster headaches occur in 10-15% of patients, where remission periods are either absent or last less than 1 month in a year.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Cluster Headache"][/sc]
  • Ask about severe unilateral orbital or temporal pain - This hallmark symptom is caused by activation of the trigeminal nerve, leading to intense pain in the in this clinical context.
  • Ask about lacrimation - Caused by parasympathetic hyperactivity, leading to overproduction of tears from the lacrimal gland.
  • Ask about nasal congestion or rhinorrhoea - Nasal symptoms result from parasympathetic activation affecting the nasal mucosa.
  • Ask about ptosis - Caused by dysfunction of the sympathetic nerves controlling the upper eyelid muscles.
  • Ask about restlessness or agitation - Patients often describe a sense of unease or discomfort during attacks, attributed to the severity of pain.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Cluster Headache"][/sc]
  • Check ipsilateral lacrimation - Visible tearing in the affected eye, due to parasympathetic overactivity.
  • Check conjunctival injection - Reddening of the affected eye caused by vasodilation of the conjunctival vessels.
  • Check horner's syndrome - Characterised by ptosis, miosis, and anhidrosis, reflecting sympathetic disruption.
  • Check facial sweating - Seen on the affected side, related to parasympathetic overactivity.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Cluster Headache"][/sc]
  • New onset headache in patients over 50 - Raises concern for secondary causes such as giant cell arteritis or brain tumours.
  • Fever or signs of systemic infection - May suggest meningitis or intracranial abscess.
  • Altered mental status - May indicate a neurological emergency such as stroke or encephalitis.
  • Prolonged visual changes - Could indicate optic nerve compression or vascular occlusion.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Cluster Headache"][/sc]
  • Bilateral cluster headache - Although cluster headaches are typically unilateral, some patients experience bilateral pain, which can be mistaken for other headache syndromes.
  • Absence of autonomic features - A minority of patients may present without typical parasympathetic symptoms, making the diagnosis more challenging.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features or comorbidity can mask the underlying diagnosis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Cluster Headache"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Clinical Diagnosis - Cluster headache is primarily diagnosed based on history and clinical criteria. No specific biomarker or test is available.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Cluster Headache"][/sc]
  • Migraine - Differentiate from cluster headache using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.
  • Trigeminal neuralgia - Presents with sharp, electric shock-like pain but lacks the autonomic features seen in cluster.
  • Paroxysmal hemicrania - Similar to cluster headache but shorter-lasting attacks (2-30 minutes) and responds completely to indomethacin.
  • Alternative serious diagnosis - Distinguishing history, examination, and targeted investigations do not support cluster headache.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Cluster Headache"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Assess physiological stability and urgent complications at presentation, and initiate early stabilisation where required.
  • Core care: Deliver condition-specific treatment for cluster headache, address underlying drivers, and involve appropriate specialist support.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Reassess symptom trajectory, objective markers, and treatment response over time to detect complications early.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Worsening symptoms, physiological deterioration, or failure to improve should trigger urgent reassessment for severe or alternative pathology in cluster headache.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Cluster Headache"][/sc]
  • Medication overuse headache - Frequent use of abortive medications can lead to chronic daily headaches.
  • Psychological impact - The severe and disabling nature of cluster headaches may lead to depression and anxiety in affected individuals.
  • Intractable pain - Chronic cases may become resistant to treatment, leading to a significant reduction in quality of life.
