Type 1 Diabetes Mellitus
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Type 1 Diabetes Mellitus"][/sc]
  • Type 1 Diabetes Mellitus (T1DM) is a chronic autoimmune condition characterised by the destruction of insulin-producing beta cells in the pancreas.
  • UKMLA essentials: polyuria with weight loss, ketosis or acidosis features, urgent insulin pathway and DKA assessment.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Type 1 Diabetes Mellitus"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Type 1 Diabetes Mellitus"][/sc]
  • Risk factors: Risk factors for type 1 diabetes mellitus include relevant predisposition, comorbidity, and exposure history.
  • Higher-risk or missed groups: Type 1 Diabetes Mellitus may be missed when presentation is atypical or obscured by comorbidity.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Type 1 Diabetes Mellitus"][/sc]
  • Autoimmune destruction of pancreatic beta cells, typically mediated by T-cells, leads to an absolute deficiency of insulin.
  • Without insulin, glucose cannot enter cells, leading to hyperglycaemia.
  • The liver continues to produce glucose (gluconeogenesis), worsening hyperglycaemia.
  • The kidneys filter excess glucose, leading to glycosuria and osmotic diuresis (polyuria and dehydration).
  • Lack of insulin also leads to unregulated lipolysis and ketogenesis, resulting in diabetic ketoacidosis (DKA) in severe cases.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Type 1 Diabetes Mellitus"][/sc]
  • Initial Phase: Gradual autoimmune destruction, with subclinical hyperglycaemia for months to years.
  • Onset: Acute presentation once beta cell mass falls below 20%, often with symptoms of hyperglycaemia.
  • Chronic Phase: Lifelong insulin dependency after diagnosis.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Type 1 Diabetes Mellitus"][/sc]
  • Ask about polyuria - Excessive urination due to osmotic diuresis caused by glycosuria.
  • Ask about polydipsia - Excessive thirst due to dehydration from osmotic diuresis.
  • Ask about weight loss - Due to catabolism of fat and muscle in the absence of insulin.
  • Ask about fatigue - Results from the inability of cells to use glucose for energy without insulin.
  • Ask about blurred vision - Caused by glucose-induced osmotic changes in the lens of the eye.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Type 1 Diabetes Mellitus"][/sc]
  • Check dehydration - Dry mucous membranes, poor skin turgor due to osmotic diuresis.
  • Check ketonuria - Presence of ketones in the urine due to fat metabolism.
  • Check fruity breath odour - Caused by exhaled acetone in ketoacidosis.
  • Check altered mental status - Can occur in severe hyperglycaemia or ketoacidosis.
  • Check tachycardia and hypotension - Signs of dehydration and possible impending shock in ketoacidosis.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Type 1 Diabetes Mellitus"][/sc]
  • Kussmaul breathing - Deep, laboured breathing due to metabolic acidosis, a hallmark of diabetic ketoacidosis (DKA).
  • Altered consciousness - Suggests severe DKA or hypoglycaemia; can lead to coma.
  • Severe dehydration - May indicate impending shock in DKA, requiring immediate fluid resuscitation.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Type 1 Diabetes Mellitus"][/sc]
  • Late onset Type 1 diabetes (LADA) - Presents in adulthood with slow progression, often initially misdiagnosed as Type 2 diabetes.
  • Silent diabetes - Rarely, T1DM may be diagnosed incidentally without the typical symptomatic presentation, especially in screening tests.
  • How it gets missed: slower-onset autoimmune diabetes in adults can be mistaken for type 2 diabetes without antibody or C-peptide assessment.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Type 1 Diabetes Mellitus"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Capillary or plasma glucose with blood/urine ketones, blood gas, and bedside observations to assess severity and ketoacidosis risk.
  • Targeted and monitoring: HbA1c, diabetes autoantibodies, and C-peptide where subtype is uncertain; serial ketones, electrolytes, and acid-base markers when DKA is suspected.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Type 1 Diabetes Mellitus"][/sc]
  • A 14-year-old boy presents with 3 weeks of polyuria, polydipsia, weight loss, and fatigue, with hyperglycaemia and ketonuria on testing.
  • Young person with classic osmotic symptoms, weight loss, and ketonaemia, consistent with new-onset type 1 diabetes mellitus.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Type 1 Diabetes Mellitus"][/sc]
  • Type 2 diabetes - Often associated with insulin resistance phenotype, preserved C-peptide, and absent autoimmune markers.
  • MODY - Familial early-onset diabetes with non-autoimmune pattern and specific genetic subtype features.
  • Pancreatogenic diabetes - Pancreatic disease history with exocrine features suggests secondary diabetes rather than autoimmune beta-cell failure.
  • Stress hyperglycaemia - Transient hyperglycaemia during acute illness without persistent autoimmune diabetes markers.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Type 1 Diabetes Mellitus"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Assess for diabetic ketoacidosis and stabilise airway, breathing, circulation, hydration, and metabolic disturbance when present.
  • Core care: Start insulin replacement and structured diabetes education, with individualised nutrition, glucose monitoring, and psychosocial support.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Ongoing review of glucose trends, HbA1c, hypoglycaemia risk, injection technique, and screening for microvascular complications is essential.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Vomiting, abdominal pain, ketone rise, persistent hyperglycaemia, recurrent hypoglycaemia, or reduced consciousness should prompt urgent reassessment.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Type 1 Diabetes Mellitus"][/sc]
  • Diabetic ketoacidosis (DKA) - Acute, life-threatening complication due to severe insulin deficiency.
  • Hypoglycaemia - Common side effect of insulin therapy, especially if insulin dosing is not well matched to carbohydrate intake.
  • Microvascular complications - Retinopathy, nephropathy, and neuropathy due to chronic hyperglycaemia.
  • Macrovascular complications - Increased risk of cardiovascular disease.
