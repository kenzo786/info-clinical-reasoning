Medication-Induced Dizziness
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Medication-Induced Dizziness"][/sc]
  • Medication-induced dizziness refers to the sensation of light-headedness, vertigo, or disequilibrium caused by the side effects of certain medications.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise medication features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.

Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Medication-Induced Dizziness"][/sc]
  • Several pathways can lead to medication-induced dizziness depending on the class of drug involved:
    • **Antihypertensives**: Can cause hypotension, reducing cerebral perfusion, leading to dizziness or faintness.
    • **CNS depressants (e.g., benzodiazepines, antipsychotics)**: Can impair the brain's balance centres, resulting in vertigo or imbalance.
    • **Ototoxic drugs (e.g., aminoglycosides, loop diuretics)**: Can directly damage the vestibular apparatus, causing vertigo.
    • **Anticholinergic drugs**: Can interfere with normal autonomic regulation, leading to orthostatic hypotension and dizziness.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Medication-Induced Dizziness"][/sc]
  • The onset of medication-induced dizziness typically correlates with:
    • **Immediate onset**: With medications that directly affect blood pressure or CNS function (e.g., nitrates, sedatives).
    • **Gradual onset**: With medications that accumulate in the system (e.g., ototoxic drugs like aminoglycosides).
    • **Resolution**: Symptoms often resolve upon cessation of the offending drug or dose adjustment, although permanent damage (e.g., ototoxicity) may occur in rare cases.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Medication-Induced Dizziness"][/sc]
  • Ask about dizziness - A non-specific term describing light-headedness, vertigo, or disequilibrium. Pathophysiology depends on the drug’s effect on blood pressure, vestibular function, or CNS balance.
  • Ask about vertigo - A sensation of spinning, usually caused by drugs that affect the vestibular apparatus, such as ototoxic medications.
  • Ask about light-headedness - Often due to hypotension caused by antihypertensive or vasodilatory medications, leading to reduced cerebral perfusion.
  • Ask about nausea - Common in vertigo-inducing drugs due to vestibular disturbance, which affects the autonomic system.
  • Ask about imbalance or disequilibrium - Common with CNS depressants or vestibulotoxic drugs, leading to impaired coordination and balance.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Medication-Induced Dizziness"][/sc]
  • Check postural hypotension - A significant drop in blood pressure upon standing, often seen with antihypertensive drugs, leading to dizziness.
  • Check nystagmus - Involuntary eye movement indicating vestibular dysfunction, commonly seen with ototoxic or vestibulotoxic medications.
  • Check ataxia - Unsteady gait or lack of coordination, often seen with CNS depressants (e.g., sedatives, antiepileptics).
  • Check bradycardia - Slower heart rate caused by drugs affecting autonomic regulation, such as beta-blockers, leading to reduced cardiac output and dizziness.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Medication-Induced Dizziness"][/sc]
  • Severe hypotension (BP < 90/60 mmHg) - Suggests excessive drug effect, such as from overmedication with antihypertensives, and may indicate impending syncope.
  • Persistent vertigo - May suggest permanent damage to the vestibular system, particularly with ototoxic medications (e.g., aminoglycosides).
  • Confusion or altered mental status - Indicates CNS depression or overdose of sedative medications, which can lead to respiratory depression and death.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Medication-Induced Dizziness"][/sc]
  • Subtle dizziness in the elderly - Older patients may underreport dizziness or attribute it to age, leading to missed diagnosis, especially in the context of polypharmacy.
  • Intermittent vertigo - Drugs with fluctuating plasma concentrations (e.g., diuretics) may cause episodic dizziness rather than continuous symptoms.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Medication-Induced Dizziness"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Blood pressure monitoring - Measurement of postural changes in BP can identify hypotension as a cause of dizziness; ECG - Useful.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Targeted investigations to confirm diagnosis, assess complications, and monitor response in medication-induced dizziness.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Medication-Induced Dizziness"][/sc]
  • Benign Paroxysmal Positional Vertigo (BPPV) - Distinguish from medication-induced dizziness using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.
  • Orthostatic Hypotension (Non-medication related) - May result from dehydration or autonomic failure but shares symptoms with medication-induced hypotension.
  • Meniere’s Disease - Associated with hearing loss and tinnitus, which are absent in medication-induced vertigo unless ototoxicity.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Medication-Induced Dizziness"][/sc]
  • Falls and fractures - Especially in elderly patients with dizziness, increasing the risk of injury.
  • Permanent vestibular damage - From ototoxic drugs like aminoglycosides, leading to chronic vertigo.
  • Severe hypotension - Can lead to syncope, and in extreme cases, hypoperfusion-related organ damage.
