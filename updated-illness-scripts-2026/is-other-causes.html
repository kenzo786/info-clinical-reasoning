Other Causes
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Other Causes"][/sc]
  • Other causes refers to a broad category of conditions that are not typically grouped under common diagnostic labels.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise causes features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Other Causes"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Other Causes"][/sc]
  • Risk factors: Risk factors for other causes include relevant predisposition, comorbidity, and exposure history.
  • Higher-risk or missed groups: Other Causes may be missed when presentation is atypical or obscured by comorbidity.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Other Causes"][/sc]
  • The pathophysiology of these "other causes" depends on the individual condition. For example, rare infections may involve atypical pathogens or immune responses, while genetic disorders may arise from mutations that disrupt normal cell function, protein synthesis, or biochemical pathways. Environmental exposures could lead to toxic or allergic reactions, causing multi-system effects.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Other Causes"][/sc]
  • The time course of "Other causes" varies greatly depending on the underlying condition. Rare infections may present acutely with rapid onset, while genetic disorders can manifest gradually over time. Some conditions may present subclinically for years before becoming symptomatic, while others follow a relapsing-remitting course, such as rare autoimmune conditions.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Other Causes"][/sc]
  • Ask about symptom 1: Fatigue - Common in many rare and systemic conditions due to increased metabolic demand, chronic inflammation, or organ dysfunction.
  • Ask about symptom 2: Weight loss - Often indicates underlying chronic disease, malignancy, or severe systemic infection. Catabolic processes or malabsorption may explain this in genetic.
  • Ask about symptom 3: Fever - A hallmark of many infectious or inflammatory conditions, fever reflects the body’s immune response to pathogens or abnormal immune activity.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Other Causes"][/sc]
  • Check pallor - Common in anaemia, which can result from chronic disease, bone marrow failure, or rare haemolytic conditions.
  • Check rashes - Certain infections, autoimmune conditions, or genetic diseases present with characteristic skin findings (e.g., butterfly rash in lupus).
  • Check hepatosplenomegaly - Enlargement of the liver and spleen can occur in certain infections, haematological diseases, or metabolic storage disorders.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Other Causes"][/sc]
  • Unexplained weight loss - May indicate malignancy or severe chronic disease.
  • Night sweats and fever - Could signal occult infection (e.g., tuberculosis) or lymphoma.
  • Neurological deficits - Suggests involvement of the central nervous system, requiring urgent investigation for conditions like paraneoplastic syndromes or certain neurodegenerative disorders.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Other Causes"][/sc]
  • Some conditions present atypically with non-specific symptoms such as mild fatigue or gastrointestinal upset. For example, a rare metabolic disorder might initially present only with irritability and abdominal pain, potentially leading to delayed diagnosis.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask other causes.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Other Causes"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Blood tests - Full blood count, inflammatory markers (CRP, ESR), and specific antibody tests can help identify autoimmune or infectious causes.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Imaging - CT or MRI scans may be needed to assess internal organ involvement, particularly if malignancy, infection, or structural abnormalities.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Other Causes"][/sc]
  • Chronic infection (e.g., tuberculosis) - Distinguished by more pronounced respiratory symptoms and night sweats.
  • Autoimmune diseases (e.g., lupus) - May present with more specific symptoms like joint pain and rashes.
  • Haematological malignancies (e.g., lymphoma) - Often accompanied by lymphadenopathy and more consistent fevers.
  • Related alternative diagnosis - Distinguish from other causes using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Other Causes"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Assess severity, physiological stability, and immediate complications.
  • Core care: Deliver condition-specific management for other causes, treat underlying drivers, and coordinate appropriate specialist input.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Reassess symptom trajectory, examination findings, and objective markers to confirm response in other causes.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Escalate urgently for clinical deterioration, evolving red flags, or failure to improve in suspected other causes.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Other Causes"][/sc]
  • Organ dysfunction - Many rare diseases or infections can lead to multi-organ failure if untreated.
  • Malignancy - Some rare conditions, particularly genetic or immunological, increase the risk of developing cancers.
  • Neurological damage - Progressive conditions or delayed diagnosis can lead to irreversible neurological impairment in cases involving the CNS.
