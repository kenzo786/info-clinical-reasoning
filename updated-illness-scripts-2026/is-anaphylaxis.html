Anaphylaxis
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Anaphylaxis"][/sc]
  • Anaphylaxis is an acute severe systemic hypersensitivity reaction causing rapidly progressive airway, breathing, and/or circulatory compromise.
  • UKMLA essentials: rapid airway-breathing-circulation compromise, hypotension or wheeze concern, treat immediately with intramuscular adrenaline.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Anaphylaxis"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Anaphylaxis"][/sc]
  • Risk factors: Risk of anaphylaxis rises with relevant predisposition, comorbidity, and exposure profile.
  • Higher-risk or missed groups: Anaphylaxis may be missed when presentations are atypical or comorbidity obscures key features.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Anaphylaxis"][/sc]
  • Anaphylaxis results from an IgE-mediated immune response where an allergen binds to IgE antibodies on the surface of mast cells and basophils, leading to the release of histamine and other inflammatory mediators.
  • This causes vasodilation, increased vascular permeability (leading to swelling), bronchoconstriction, and activation of the coagulation system, which can result in shock, airway obstruction, and multi-system involvement.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Anaphylaxis"][/sc]
  • Anaphylaxis typically develops within minutes to hours after exposure to the allergen. Initial symptoms may be mild but can progress rapidly, peaking within 5-30 minutes.
  • In biphasic anaphylaxis, symptoms may recur 1-72 hours after the initial reaction.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Anaphylaxis"][/sc]
  • Ask about skin/mucosal changes (flushing, urticaria, angioedema) - These are the most common symptoms, occurring in up to 90% of cases. They result from histamine-induced vasodilation.
  • Ask about respiratory symptoms (wheezing, dyspnoea, stridor) - Bronchoconstriction and laryngeal oedema due to histamine release can cause airway obstruction and respiratory distress.
  • Ask about hypotension and shock - Histamine and other mediators cause vasodilation and capillary leakage, leading to distributive shock and circulatory collapse in severe cases.
  • Ask about gastrointestinal symptoms (nausea, vomiting, diarrhoea) - Less common, occurring in about 30-45% of cases, and result from smooth muscle contraction and increased fluid secretion.
  • Ask about dizziness or syncope - This results from hypotension and cerebral hypoperfusion.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Anaphylaxis"][/sc]
  • Check tachycardia - A compensatory response to hypotension caused by systemic vasodilation and fluid loss into the interstitial space.
  • Check wheezing - Indicative of bronchoconstriction due to histamine release affecting the lower airways.
  • Check angioedema - Swelling of the lips, eyelids, and face, resulting from increased vascular permeability.
  • Check stridor - A high-pitched breathing sound caused by upper airway obstruction from laryngeal oedema.
  • Check hypotension - A severe drop in blood pressure due to systemic vasodilation and leakage of fluid from the blood vessels.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Anaphylaxis"][/sc]
  • Sudden onset of respiratory distress - May signify severe bronchoconstriction or laryngeal oedema, both of which can quickly lead to complete airway obstruction.
  • Hypotension or shock unresponsive to fluid resuscitation - Indicates profound vasodilation and fluid extravasation requiring immediate administration of adrenaline (epinephrine).
  • Biphasic reaction - A second wave of symptoms occurring after the initial reaction may indicate a more severe or prolonged immune response, warranting extended observation and treatment.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Anaphylaxis"][/sc]
  • Isolated gastrointestinal symptoms - In rare cases, anaphylaxis may present predominantly with nausea, vomiting, or abdominal pain, which can delay diagnosis.
  • Absence of skin symptoms - While uncommon, anaphylaxis may occur without the classic skin findings, especially in cases of drug or insect venom-induced anaphylaxis.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features or comorbidity can mask the underlying diagnosis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Anaphylaxis"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Clinical diagnosis from acute multisystem reaction with continuous airway, breathing, circulation observations and repeated reassessment after treatment.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Timed serum tryptase after stabilisation to support diagnosis; allergy testing and trigger identification after recovery; ongoing observation for biphasic reaction risk.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Anaphylaxis"][/sc]
  • A 25-year-old woman develops urticaria, wheeze, throat tightness, and hypotension within minutes of peanut exposure.
  • Patient with rapid multisystem allergic reaction and cardiovascular or respiratory compromise, consistent with anaphylaxis.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Anaphylaxis"][/sc]
  • Vasovagal syncope - Brief hypotension and pallor without urticaria, bronchospasm, or persistent airway swelling suggest vasovagal collapse.
  • Acute asthma exacerbation - Isolated bronchospasm without skin or circulatory involvement supports asthma rather than anaphylaxis.
  • Panic attack - Hyperventilation and distress without hypotension, urticaria, or objective airway oedema favours panic disorder.
  • Septic shock - Infective source with fever and progressive shock without immediate allergen link suggests sepsis.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Anaphylaxis"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Give intramuscular adrenaline promptly, call for emergency support, and secure airway, breathing, and circulation with high-flow oxygen and fluids as needed.
  • Core care: Remove suspected trigger, provide adjunctive therapy after adrenaline, and continue protocol-based resuscitation with senior support.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Observe for biphasic reactions, document trigger history, and arrange allergy referral with emergency action planning and auto-injector education.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Refractory hypotension, persistent airway compromise, recurrent symptoms, or deterioration despite initial treatment requires immediate critical care escalation.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Anaphylaxis"][/sc]
  • Airway obstruction - Laryngeal oedema can lead to fatal airway compromise if not treated promptly.
  • Anaphylactic shock - Circulatory collapse due to widespread vasodilation may cause multi-organ failure if not rapidly reversed with adrenaline and fluids.
  • Biphasic anaphylaxis - A recurrence of symptoms after initial resolution, requiring prolonged monitoring and treatment.
