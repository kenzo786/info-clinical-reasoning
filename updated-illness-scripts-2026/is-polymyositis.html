Polymyositis
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Polymyositis"][/sc]
  • Polymyositis is a rare idiopathic inflammatory myopathy characterised by symmetrical, proximal muscle weakness due to chronic inflammation of skeletal muscle.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise polymyositis features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.

  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Polymyositis"][/sc]
  • The condition is more common in females and typically presents in individuals aged 30-60 years.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Polymyositis"][/sc]
  • Polymyositis is driven by an autoimmune process where CD8+ T cells and macrophages invade muscle fibres, leading to necrosis. The precise trigger for this immune reaction is unknown, but both genetic and environmental factors likely play a role.
  • Chronic inflammation results in ongoing muscle fibre damage, leading to the characteristic weakness. Cytokines like TNF-α and IL-1 contribute to the inflammatory response, exacerbating muscle damage.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Polymyositis"][/sc]
  • The onset of polymyositis is typically gradual over months. Early symptoms may be subtle, leading to delayed diagnosis. The disease progresses with increasing muscle weakness over time if untreated.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Polymyositis"][/sc]
  • Ask about proximal muscle weakness - The most common symptom, especially affecting the shoulder and hip girdle. This occurs due to inflammatory damage to muscle fibres.
  • Ask about fatigue - Often present due to systemic inflammation and muscle damage.
  • Ask about myalgia - Muscle pain occurs in about 25-50% of patients, a result of inflammation of the muscle tissue.
  • Ask about dysphagia - Difficulty swallowing may occur if the muscles of the upper oesophagus are affected.
  • Ask about respiratory difficulty - May occur if the diaphragm or intercostal muscles are involved.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Polymyositis"][/sc]
  • Check symmetrical muscle weakness - Particularly in the proximal muscles of the arms and legs. Muscle strength is reduced due to inflammatory myositis.
  • Check muscle tenderness - On palpation, tenderness may be noted due to active inflammation of muscle tissue.
  • Check decreased muscle bulk - Over time, chronic inflammation leads to muscle wasting and atrophy.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Polymyositis"][/sc]
  • Respiratory muscle weakness - Indicates diaphragmatic involvement and may lead to respiratory failure.
  • Dysphagia with aspiration risk - Suggests involvement of the pharyngeal muscles, potentially leading to aspiration pneumonia.
  • Rapidly progressive muscle weakness - May signify overlap with other autoimmune conditions or malignancy-associated myositis.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Polymyositis"][/sc]
  • Polymyositis can rarely present with distal muscle weakness or predominantly respiratory symptoms, leading to an initial misdiagnosis of another neuromuscular condition such as motor neuron disease.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask polymyositis.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Polymyositis"][/sc]
  • Respiratory failure - Due to diaphragmatic involvement, leading to ventilatory compromise.
  • Aspiration pneumonia - Due to dysphagia and weakened pharyngeal muscles.
  • Chronic disability - Muscle weakness may persist despite treatment, leading to long-term functional impairment.
  • Malignancy - There is an increased risk of malignancy, particularly ovarian, lung, and colorectal cancers.
