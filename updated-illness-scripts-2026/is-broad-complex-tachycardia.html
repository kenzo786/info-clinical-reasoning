Broad Complex Tachycardia
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Broad Complex Tachycardia"][/sc]
  • Broad complex tachycardia is a tachyarrhythmia with heart rate above 100 beats per minute and QRS duration at least 120 ms on ECG.
  • UKMLA essentials: broad tachycardia on ECG, haemodynamic instability features, treat as ventricular tachycardia until proven otherwise.

Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Broad Complex Tachycardia"][/sc]
  • The broad QRS complex arises due to delayed conduction through the ventricles. This occurs either from a ventricular origin (as in ventricular tachycardia) or aberrant conduction through diseased parts of the conduction system (as in bundle branch block). Triggers include myocardial ischaemia, electrolyte imbalances, or re-entrant circuits in scarred myocardium. If untreated, it can lead to haemodynamic instability or progress to ventricular fibrillation.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Broad Complex Tachycardia"][/sc]
  • BCT can have an acute onset, especially in the context of myocardial infarction or electrolyte disturbances. It may present as intermittent episodes (paroxysmal) or sustained arrhythmias. Without timely treatment, it can evolve rapidly into life-threatening conditions like ventricular fibrillation within minutes to hours.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Broad Complex Tachycardia"][/sc]
  • Ask about palpitations - Most patients feel a rapid and irregular heartbeat due to ventricular tachycardia or aberrant conduction.
  • Ask about syncope - Caused by reduced cerebral perfusion during sustained arrhythmia leading to a brief loss of consciousness.
  • Ask about dyspnoea - The decreased cardiac output reduces oxygen delivery, resulting in shortness of breath.
  • Ask about chest pain - Ischaemia due to increased myocardial oxygen demand or underlying coronary artery disease may manifest as chest pain.
  • Ask about dizziness or lightheadedness - This is due to transient hypotension or poor perfusion of the brain.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Broad Complex Tachycardia"][/sc]
  • Check tachycardia (>100 bpm) - The defining feature of BCT due to rapid ventricular depolarisation.
  • Check hypotension - A result of reduced stroke volume and cardiac output during prolonged arrhythmia.
  • Check jugular venous distension (JVD) - Reflects increased central venous pressure due to impaired cardiac filling during arrhythmia.
  • Check cold, clammy extremities - Indicative of poor peripheral perfusion caused by the low cardiac output state.
  • Check absent or weak peripheral pulses - Occurs when the arrhythmia leads to reduced cardiac output, making pulses difficult to palpate.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Broad Complex Tachycardia"][/sc]
  • Haemodynamic instability (e.g., hypotension, altered mental state) - This suggests poor end-organ perfusion and may require immediate cardioversion to prevent cardiac arrest.
  • Polymorphic broad complex tachycardia (Torsades de Pointes) - This is associated with QT prolongation and can quickly degenerate into ventricular fibrillation.
  • Chest pain with ST-segment changes - Indicates myocardial ischaemia, potentially complicating into a full myocardial infarction.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Broad Complex Tachycardia"][/sc]
  • Patients may present with generalised weakness or fatigue, especially in the elderly. Asymptomatic presentations occur, particularly in patients with implanted devices like pacemakers. Symptoms may be missed in individuals with underlying heart failure who already have reduced exercise tolerance and dyspnoea.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Broad Complex Tachycardia"][/sc]
  • Supraventricular tachycardia with aberrancy - May present similarly but is usually initiated by a supraventricular focus; QRS morphology often.
  • Torsades de Pointes - Characterised by a twisting morphology on ECG and associated with QT prolongation.
  • Atrial fibrillation with rapid ventricular response - Irregularly irregular rhythm with varying QRS duration, typically presenting in patients with pre-existing bundle.
  • Alternative serious diagnosis - Distinguishing history, examination, and targeted investigations do not support broad complex tachycardia.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Broad Complex Tachycardia"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Assess physiological stability and urgent complications at presentation, and initiate early stabilisation where required.
  • Core care: Deliver condition-specific treatment for broad complex tachycardia, address underlying drivers, and involve appropriate specialist support.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Reassess symptom trajectory, objective markers, and treatment response over time to detect complications early.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Worsening symptoms, physiological deterioration, or failure to improve should trigger urgent reassessment for severe or alternative pathology in broad complex tachycardia.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Broad Complex Tachycardia"][/sc]
  • Ventricular fibrillation - Without intervention, BCT can deteriorate into ventricular fibrillation, a life-threatening arrhythmia leading to cardiac arrest.
  • Cardiac arrest - Prolonged untreated BCT leads to poor cardiac output, ultimately resulting in arrest.
  • Heart failure exacerbation - Chronic tachyarrhythmias worsen pre-existing heart failure by reducing ventricular filling time and increasing myocardial oxygen demand.
