Cyclical Breast Pain
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Cyclical Breast Pain"][/sc]
  • Cyclical breast pain (mastalgia) is a recurring discomfort or pain in one or both breasts, often linked to the menstrual cycle.

Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Cyclical Breast Pain"][/sc]
  • Cyclical breast pain is thought to result from hormonal fluctuations, primarily oestrogen and progesterone, affecting breast tissue during the menstrual cycle. These hormonal changes may increase water retention, alter the ductal and stromal tissue structure, and heighten sensitivity, leading to pain or discomfort. High prolactin levels in response to stress and dietary factors may also play a role.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Cyclical Breast Pain"][/sc]
  • The pain typically begins during the luteal phase (about 1-2 weeks before menstruation) and resolves with the onset of menses. The cyclic nature is recurrent with each menstrual cycle.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Cyclical Breast Pain"][/sc]
  • Ask about bilateral breast pain - Most common symptom; pain is usually diffuse, affecting the upper outer quadrants and may radiate to the axillae. This is.
  • Ask about swelling or fullness - Some patients report breast swelling or heaviness, linked to water retention in response to fluctuating oestrogen and progesterone levels.
  • Ask about breast tenderness - The breasts may feel sore to touch, likely due to oedema and ductal congestion as hormonal fluctuations impact tissue sensitivity.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Cyclical Breast Pain"][/sc]
  • Check diffuse breast tenderness - Palpation often reveals tenderness without discrete lumps, aligning with the hormonal sensitivity affecting the whole breast tissue.
  • Check breast fullness or engorgement - Visible fullness or engorgement may be noted, especially in premenstrual phases, due to hormonal-induced water retention in the tissue.
  • Check absence of masses - Typically, no palpable masses are present, distinguishing cyclical mastalgia from other causes like fibroadenomas or malignancies.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Cyclical Breast Pain"][/sc]
  • Unilateral, non-cyclical pain - Suggests a potential underlying pathology, such as infection or malignancy, as cyclical pain is generally bilateral.
  • Discrete lump - A fixed, hard, or irregular lump that does not fluctuate with the menstrual cycle could indicate breast cancer.
  • Skin changes - Dimpling, retraction, or peau d'orange appearance warrants further evaluation for malignancy.
  • Nipple discharge - Particularly if bloody or unilateral, it may indicate an intraductal papilloma or malignancy.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Cyclical Breast Pain"][/sc]
  • Some women may experience pain that does not resolve with menstruation, raising suspicion for a non-cyclical cause or coexisting pathology.
  • Occasional unilateral cyclical pain can mimic conditions like mastitis or early-stage breast cancer, leading to missed diagnosis without a thorough history.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Cyclical Breast Pain"][/sc]
  • Fibrocystic breast changes - Similar cyclical pattern, but typically associated with discrete cysts palpable on examination.
  • Non-cyclical mastalgia - Pain unrelated to the menstrual cycle, more likely due to trauma, infection, or musculoskeletal.
  • Mastitis - Often presents with localised pain, erythema, and systemic symptoms like fever.
  • Breast cancer - Distinguish from cyclical breast pain using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Cyclical Breast Pain"][/sc]
  • Chronic pain - Some patients may develop persistent pain that impacts quality of life, necessitating ongoing pain management or hormonal therapy.
  • Psychological impact - Anxiety related to breast cancer fears can cause emotional distress and impact daily life, particularly if pain recurs or intensifies.
