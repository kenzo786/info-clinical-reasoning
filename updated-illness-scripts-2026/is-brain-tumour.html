Brain Tumour
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Brain Tumour"][/sc]
  • A brain tumour is an abnormal growth of tissue within the brain or central spinal canal.

Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Brain Tumour"][/sc]
  • Brain tumours result from uncontrolled cell proliferation. Genetic mutations disrupt the normal regulatory mechanisms that control cell division. As the tumour enlarges, it exerts pressure on surrounding brain structures, leading to localised neurological deficits. Malignant tumours may invade adjacent tissues and cause widespread destruction. Increased intracranial pressure (ICP) from tumour growth or associated oedema is a key contributor to many symptoms.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Brain Tumour"][/sc]
  • The time course of brain tumours varies widely depending on the type and location. Benign tumours typically grow slowly over months to years, while malignant tumours can progress rapidly over weeks to months. Symptoms may worsen gradually or acutely if there is haemorrhage, oedema, or increased intracranial pressure.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Brain Tumour"][/sc]
  • Ask about headache - Often worse in the morning and may be accompanied by nausea/vomiting due to increased intracranial pressure.
  • Ask about seizures - Occur in about 30% of patients with brain tumours, caused by disruption of normal electrical activity in the brain.
  • Ask about cognitive or personality changes - Tumours in the frontal lobe can cause disinhibition, memory problems, or personality shifts due to interference with executive functions.
  • Ask about nausea and vomiting - Often due to increased intracranial pressure or direct irritation of brain structures.
  • Ask about focal neurological deficits - Symptoms like weakness, visual field loss, or speech difficulties depend on the tumour's location.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Brain Tumour"][/sc]
  • Check papilloedema - Swelling of the optic disc due to raised intracranial pressure.
  • Check hemiparesis - Weakness on one side of the body, often seen with tumours affecting the motor cortex or corticospinal tracts.
  • Check aphasia - Difficulty with speech or language, particularly with tumours in the dominant hemisphere’s temporal or frontal lobes.
  • Check ataxia - Tumours in the cerebellum can disrupt coordination and balance.
  • Check altered consciousness - Due to raised intracranial pressure or large tumours compressing brainstem structures.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Brain Tumour"][/sc]
  • New-onset seizures in an adult - Suggestive of intracranial pathology such as a tumour disrupting normal brain function.
  • Progressive focal neurological deficits - Indicates localised brain tissue damage or compression from tumour growth.
  • Headaches worsening in the morning or with Valsalva manoeuvres - Typical of increased intracranial pressure, which can be a sign of brain tumours.
  • Vomiting without nausea - A sign of raised intracranial pressure affecting the brainstem vomiting centre.
  • Altered consciousness - May signal brainstem involvement or raised intracranial pressure at a critical level.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Brain Tumour"][/sc]
  • Subtle personality changes - Can be easily mistaken for psychiatric conditions, particularly in frontal lobe tumours.
  • Chronic headaches with no neurological signs - Slow-growing tumours can present with non-specific symptoms, sometimes confused with migraines or tension headaches.
  • Intermittent visual disturbances - Tumours in the occipital lobe may cause visual aura-like symptoms, often misinterpreted as migraine.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features or comorbidity can mask the underlying diagnosis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Brain Tumour"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: MRI of the brain - Preferred imaging modality to visualise brain tumours. Expected findings include mass effect, oedema, and contrast enhancement.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Biopsy - Essential for histological diagnosis and grading of the tumour. Determines if the tumour is benign or malignant; EEG (Electroencephalogram).
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Brain Tumour"][/sc]
  • A 55-year-old male presents with a 3-month history of progressively worsening headaches, new-onset seizures, and subtle memory deficits. MRI reveals a left temporal lobe mass consistent with a glioma.
  • Adult with symptom pattern and objective findings suggestive of brain tumour, requiring focused confirmation and risk stratification.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Brain Tumour"][/sc]
  • Stroke - Sudden onset compared to the gradual progression seen in brain tumours. Imaging will show.
  • Brain abscess - More likely to present with fever and systemic infection markers. MRI shows a ring-enhancing.
  • Migraine - Headaches may mimic those of brain tumours, but migraines often have a long history.
  • Multiple sclerosis - Symptoms may resemble those of a tumour, but MRI will show demyelination rather than.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Brain Tumour"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Assess physiological stability and urgent complications at presentation, and initiate early stabilisation where required.
  • Core care: Deliver condition-specific treatment for brain tumour, address underlying drivers, and involve appropriate specialist support.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Reassess symptom trajectory, objective markers, and treatment response over time to detect complications early.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Worsening symptoms, physiological deterioration, or failure to improve should trigger urgent reassessment for severe or alternative pathology in brain tumour.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Brain Tumour"][/sc]
  • Raised intracranial pressure - Can lead to brain herniation and death if untreated. Frequent in large tumours or those causing significant oedema.
  • Neurological deficits - Permanent impairments such as hemiparesis, aphasia, or visual loss may occur depending on tumour location.
  • Seizures - Can become chronic and refractory to treatment, especially in cases of cortical involvement.
  • Recurrence - Malignant tumours, particularly glioblastomas, have a high risk of recurrence even after treatment.
