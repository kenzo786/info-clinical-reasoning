Endometritis
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Endometritis"][/sc]
  • Endometritis is an inflammation of the endometrium, the inner lining of the uterus.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise endometritis features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Endometritis"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Endometritis"][/sc]
  • Risk factors: Risk factors for endometritis include relevant predisposition, comorbidity, and exposure history.
  • Higher-risk or missed groups: Endometritis may be missed when presentation is atypical or obscured by comorbidity.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Endometritis"][/sc]
  • Endometritis typically results from an ascending infection from the lower genital tract. Bacteria, such as *Escherichia coli*, *Group B Streptococcus*, or *Chlamydia trachomatis*, enter the uterine cavity and trigger an inflammatory response. In postpartum cases, retained placental tissue or membranes provide a nidus for bacterial colonisation, exacerbating infection. The inflammation can disrupt the normal architecture of the endometrium, potentially leading to chronic changes or scarring if untreated.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Endometritis"][/sc]
  • Acute endometritis: Onset is typically within 2-10 days post-delivery or procedure, with rapid progression if untreated.
  • Chronic endometritis: This can develop over weeks to months, often with subtle or asymptomatic presentation, particularly in cases related to infertility or pelvic inflammatory disease (PID).
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Endometritis"][/sc]
  • Ask about fever - Due to systemic inflammatory response and the release of pyrogens by the infective organisms.
  • Ask about lower abdominal pain - Caused by inflammation of the uterine lining, leading to discomfort or cramping sensations.
  • Ask about abnormal vaginal discharge - Typically foul-smelling, as bacterial infection causes necrosis of tissue and pus production.
  • Ask about heavy or prolonged menstrual bleeding - This can occur due to endometrial disruption and inflammation in chronic cases.
  • Ask about fatigue or malaise - A generalised response to infection, where systemic inflammation leads to lethargy and malaise.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Endometritis"][/sc]
  • Check fever (≥38°C) - Indicative of systemic infection and inflammation.
  • Check lower abdominal tenderness - Palpation reveals tenderness due to inflammation of the uterus.
  • Check purulent vaginal discharge - Seen on pelvic examination, reflecting infection and pus accumulation.
  • Check cervical motion tenderness - A sign of pelvic inflammatory disease, where infection has spread to adjacent structures.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Endometritis"][/sc]
  • High-grade fever (>39°C) - Suggests severe infection, possibly progressing to sepsis.
  • Signs of sepsis (hypotension, tachycardia, altered mental state) - May indicate systemic involvement requiring urgent management.
  • Persistent or worsening pain despite antibiotics - Could signify abscess formation or spread of infection (e.g., to the peritoneum).
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Endometritis"][/sc]
  • Subclinical chronic endometritis - May present asymptomatically or with mild, non-specific symptoms such as chronic pelvic discomfort or abnormal uterine bleeding.
  • Post-menopausal women - Endometritis in this group may be missed due to the assumption of non-infective causes for postmenopausal bleeding.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Endometritis"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Full blood count (FBC) - Raised white cell count (WCC) is indicative of infection and inflammation; C-reactive protein (CRP) - Elevated.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Endometrial biopsy (in chronic cases) - Histological examination shows plasma cells in the endometrial stroma, indicative of chronic endometritis; Cervical swabs.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Endometritis"][/sc]
  • A 30-year-old woman, 5 days postpartum following caesarean section, presents with fever, lower abdominal pain, and foul-smelling vaginal discharge.
  • Patient with fever, lower abdominal pain, and foul-smelling vaginal discharge, consistent with endometritis.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Endometritis"][/sc]
  • Pelvic Inflammatory Disease (PID) - Distinguish from endometritis using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.
  • Retained products of conception (RPOC) - Can present with ongoing bleeding and infection, often confirmed via ultrasound imaging.
  • Urinary tract infection (UTI) - Presents with dysuria and frequency, and can co-occur postpartum but without the typical uterine.
  • Chorioamnionitis - Occurs during labour with fever and uterine tenderness, but would not present postpartum.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Endometritis"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Assess severity, physiological stability, and immediate complications.
  • Core care: Deliver condition-specific management for endometritis, treat underlying drivers, and coordinate appropriate specialist input.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Reassess symptom trajectory, examination findings, and objective markers to confirm response in endometritis.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Escalate urgently for clinical deterioration, evolving red flags, or failure to improve in suspected endometritis.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Endometritis"][/sc]
  • Sepsis - Occurs when infection spreads systemically, with a high mortality risk if untreated.
  • Pelvic abscess - A localised collection of pus may form, requiring drainage.
  • Infertility - Chronic inflammation and scarring of the endometrium can lead to infertility, particularly in chronic cases.
