Ovarian Cancer
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Ovarian Cancer"][/sc]
  • Ovarian cancer refers to malignant tumours arising from different cells within the ovary.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise ovarian features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Ovarian Cancer"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Ovarian Cancer"][/sc]
  • In the UK, ovarian cancer is the sixth most common cancer in women, with approximately 7,500 new cases diagnosed annually. The lifetime risk of developing ovarian cancer in the UK is about 1 in 52.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Ovarian Cancer"][/sc]
  • Epithelial ovarian cancers likely originate from the ovarian surface epithelium or fallopian tube epithelium. Genetic mutations (e.g., BRCA1, BRCA2) lead to unchecked cell growth and tumour formation.
  • Advanced-stage ovarian cancer often spreads via direct extension, peritoneal seeding, and lymphatic or haematogenous routes, causing widespread intra-abdominal disease.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Ovarian Cancer"][/sc]
  • Ovarian cancer typically develops insidiously and remains asymptomatic for a long period. Early-stage disease may persist for years without noticeable symptoms, while advanced-stage disease can progress over months with worsening symptoms due to peritoneal spread and mass effect.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Ovarian Cancer"][/sc]
  • Ask about abdominal bloating - Caused by ascites or large ovarian masses pressing on surrounding structures.
  • Ask about pelvic or abdominal pain - Results from tumour invasion or pressure on nearby organs.
  • Ask about early satiety - Tumour mass effect on the stomach, reducing appetite and food intake.
  • Ask about urinary frequency or urgency - Caused by compression of the bladder.
  • Ask about unintentional weight loss - Reflects advanced disease and systemic metabolic effects of cancer.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Ovarian Cancer"][/sc]
  • Check abdominal distension - Caused by ascites or a large ovarian mass.
  • Check pelvic mass - Palpable during bimanual examination or detected on imaging.
  • Check ascites - Accumulation of fluid in the peritoneal cavity, often a sign of advanced disease.
  • Check cachexia - Wasting and muscle loss in advanced disease, often with significant weight loss.
  • Check lymphadenopathy - Enlarged lymph nodes due to metastasis, often pelvic or para-aortic.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Ovarian Cancer"][/sc]
  • Unexplained postmenopausal bleeding - Could indicate metastatic disease to the endometrium or other structures.
  • Rapidly progressive ascites - Suggests aggressive tumour spread within the abdomen and pelvis.
  • Severe abdominal pain - Could be due to tumour rupture, bowel obstruction, or peritonitis.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Ovarian Cancer"][/sc]
  • Chronic lower back pain - May be due to tumour impinging on nerves or structures in the pelvis.
  • Deep vein thrombosis (DVT) - Paraneoplastic hypercoagulability may present as a thrombotic event without typical abdominal symptoms.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask ovarian cancer.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Ovarian Cancer"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: CA-125 (Cancer Antigen 125) - Elevated (>35 U/mL) in over 80% of advanced ovarian cancers, though not specific. Useful for monitoring.
  • Targeted and monitoring: CT scan - More detailed evaluation of the extent of disease spread, including lymph nodes, liver, and peritoneum; Histopathology - Diagnosis.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Ovarian Cancer"][/sc]
  • A 63-year-old postmenopausal woman presents with progressive abdominal bloating, early satiety, and pelvic pain over the last 3 months. Physical examination reveals abdominal distension and a palpable pelvic mass. CA-125.
  • Patient with progressive abdominal bloating, early satiety, and pelvic pain over the last 3 months. Physical examination reveals abdominal distension and a palpable pelvic mass. CA-125, consistent with ovarian cancer.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Ovarian Cancer"][/sc]
  • Benign ovarian cysts - Typically asymptomatic or mild discomfort; no systemic symptoms; CA-125 usually normal.
  • Endometriosis - Painful periods and chronic pelvic pain; younger women; no ascites or systemic illness.
  • Irritable bowel syndrome (IBS) - Chronic abdominal pain and bloating but no mass or ascites; often relieved with defecation.
  • Ascites due to liver cirrhosis - Known liver disease; often jaundiced, no pelvic mass or raised CA-125.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Ovarian Cancer"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Assess severity, physiological stability, and immediate complications.
  • Core care: Deliver condition-specific management for ovarian cancer, treat underlying drivers, and coordinate appropriate specialist input.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Reassess symptom trajectory, examination findings, and objective markers to confirm response in ovarian cancer.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Escalate urgently for clinical deterioration, evolving red flags, or failure to improve in suspected ovarian cancer.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Ovarian Cancer"][/sc]
  • Bowel obstruction - Common in advanced stages due to tumour infiltration.
  • Peritoneal carcinomatosis - Spread of cancer throughout the peritoneum, leading to ascites and cachexia.
  • Venous thromboembolism - Ovarian cancer is highly associated with DVT and pulmonary embolism due to hypercoagulable state.
