Short Bowel Syndrome (SBS)
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Short Bowel Syndrome (SBS)"][/sc]
  • Short Bowel Syndrome (SBS) is a malabsorptive condition resulting from extensive surgical resection of the small intestine.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise short features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.

Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Short Bowel Syndrome (SBS)"][/sc]
  • The small intestine is responsible for nutrient absorption. In SBS, a large portion of this organ is removed, which drastically reduces the absorptive surface area.
  • This results in inadequate absorption of macronutrients, electrolytes, and water, leading to malnutrition, dehydration, and diarrhoea.
  • The ileum is especially important for absorbing bile salts and vitamin B12. When the ileum is resected, bile salts are lost, leading to fat malabsorption and steatorrhoea.
  • In severe cases, intestinal adaptation may not fully compensate, necessitating lifelong parenteral nutrition.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Short Bowel Syndrome (SBS)"][/sc]
  • Acute Phase (first 4-6 weeks post-surgery): Patients experience significant diarrhoea, dehydration, and malnutrition due to the immediate loss of intestinal function.
  • Adaptation Phase (6-12 months): The remaining bowel undergoes structural and functional adaptation, increasing absorption efficiency.
  • Maintenance Phase: The patient reaches a stable state of intestinal absorption, but may still require dietary modifications and possibly parenteral nutrition.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Short Bowel Syndrome (SBS)"][/sc]
  • Ask about diarrhoea - The most common symptom due to the loss of absorptive capacity of the bowel, leading to excess water in the stool.
  • Ask about weight loss - Due to malabsorption of nutrients, especially in the acute phase.
  • Ask about dehydration - Caused by excessive water loss in stools, worsened by reduced electrolyte absorption.
  • Ask about steatorrhoea - Fatty stools due to malabsorption of fats, especially if the ileum is resected.
  • Ask about fatigue - A result of malnutrition and electrolyte imbalances, especially low potassium and magnesium.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Short Bowel Syndrome (SBS)"][/sc]
  • Check muscle wasting - Due to chronic malnutrition and weight loss.
  • Check dry mucous membranes - A sign of dehydration, often noted on examination.
  • Check abdominal distension - May be present due to bowel gas or fluid accumulation, particularly in cases of bacterial overgrowth.
  • Check pallor - Due to anaemia, often caused by iron or vitamin B12 deficiency.
  • Check oedema - Secondary to protein malnutrition, which leads to hypoalbuminaemia and subsequent fluid retention.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Short Bowel Syndrome (SBS)"][/sc]
  • Severe dehydration - May indicate the need for hospitalisation and parenteral fluid therapy.
  • Refractory diarrhoea - Suggests inadequate adaptation or underlying infection requiring further investigation.
  • Sudden weight loss - Could signal severe malnutrition or complications like intestinal obstruction.
  • Sepsis or fever - Could indicate bacterial translocation or an intra-abdominal abscess, which is a potential complication of SBS.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Short Bowel Syndrome (SBS)"][/sc]
  • Mild symptoms - In some cases, patients with less extensive resections may present with subtle symptoms like mild diarrhoea or fatigue, which could be mistaken for irritable bowel syndrome (IBS) or other benign conditions.
  • Intermittent symptoms - Periodic flare-ups of diarrhoea and malabsorption, sometimes associated with dietary changes, may delay diagnosis.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask short bowel syndrome (sbs).
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Short Bowel Syndrome (SBS)"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Full Blood Count (FBC) - May show anaemia due to vitamin B12 or iron deficiency; Electrolytes (U&Es) - May reveal low.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Stool fat analysis - Confirmatory for steatorrhoea, indicating fat malabsorption; Serum vitamin levels - Low levels of fat-soluble vitamins (A, D.

  • A 45-year-old male, post-resection of 70% of his small intestine following mesenteric ischemia, presents with chronic diarrhoea, weight loss, and fatigue. He has a history of multiple hospital admissions, with supportive clinical and investigation findings.
  • Patient with chronic diarrhoea, weight loss, and fatigue. He has a history of multiple hospital admissions for, consistent with short bowel syndrome (sbs).
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Short Bowel Syndrome (SBS)"][/sc]
  • Crohn’s disease - Differentiated by inflammatory markers and endoscopic findings of transmural inflammation.
  • Coeliac disease - Diagnosed through serological testing (anti-tTG) and duodenal biopsy showing villous atrophy.
  • Chronic pancreatitis - Associated with fat malabsorption, but differentiated by imaging showing pancreatic calcifications or enzyme levels.
  • Small intestinal bacterial overgrowth (SIBO) - Overlaps with SBS but typically responds to antibiotic therapy and may show positive breath.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Short Bowel Syndrome (SBS)"][/sc]
  • Chronic kidney disease (CKD) - Due to recurrent dehydration and electrolyte imbalances.
  • Liver disease - Parenteral nutrition-associated liver disease is a significant long-term complication.
  • Intestinal failure - In severe cases, the remaining bowel is insufficient to sustain life without parenteral nutrition.
  • Small bowel bacterial overgrowth (SIBO) - Due to altered gut motility and stasis in the remaining bowel, leading to bloating, diarrhoea, and malabsorption.
