Bronchitis
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Bronchitis"][/sc]
  • Bronchitis is inflammation of the bronchi in the lungs, characterised by increased mucus production and swelling of the bronchial walls.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise bronchitis features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Bronchitis"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Bronchitis"][/sc]
  • Risk factors: Risk of bronchitis rises with relevant predisposition, comorbidity, and exposure profile.
  • Higher-risk or missed groups: Bronchitis may be missed when presentations are atypical or comorbidity obscures key features.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Bronchitis"][/sc]
  • Acute bronchitis involves infection-induced inflammation of bronchial epithelial cells, leading to oedema, increased mucus production, and impaired ciliary function.
  • Chronic bronchitis is driven by chronic exposure to irritants, resulting in goblet cell hyperplasia, mucus hypersecretion, and persistent airway inflammation.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Bronchitis"][/sc]
  • Acute bronchitis typically lasts less than three weeks, with resolution of symptoms by this time.
  • Chronic bronchitis is persistent, with symptoms lasting for three months or more in two consecutive years.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Bronchitis"][/sc]
  • Ask about cough - Often productive in chronic bronchitis and dry initially in acute bronchitis. Pathophysiology involves irritation and inflammation of the bronchial mucosa.
  • Ask about sputum production - May be clear or purulent in acute bronchitis; typically mucoid in chronic bronchitis due to mucus hypersecretion.
  • Ask about shortness of breath - More prominent in chronic bronchitis due to airflow obstruction and reduced gas exchange.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Bronchitis"][/sc]
  • Check wheezing - Results from narrowed airways due to inflammation and mucus plugging.
  • Check rhonchi - Caused by turbulent airflow through mucus-filled bronchi.
  • Check reduced air entry - May be noted in chronic cases due to airway obstruction.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Bronchitis"][/sc]
  • Haemoptysis - May indicate malignancy or tuberculosis.
  • Persistent fever - Could suggest pneumonia or other complications.
  • Weight loss - Raises suspicion for underlying malignancy.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Bronchitis"][/sc]
  • Chronic bronchitis presenting with mild dyspnoea without significant cough may be missed.
  • Acute bronchitis in elderly patients may present primarily with confusion or lethargy due to systemic effects of infection.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Bronchitis"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Chest X-ray - Usually normal in bronchitis but performed to rule out pneumonia (consolidation).
  • Targeted and monitoring: Sputum culture - May identify causative pathogens, particularly in chronic bronchitis exacerbations; Spirometry - Reveals airflow limitation in chronic bronchitis.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Bronchitis"][/sc]
  • A 45-year-old male smoker presenting with a 2-week history of productive cough, low-grade fever, and wheezing, resolving spontaneously without intervention.
  • Adult with symptom pattern and objective findings suggestive of bronchitis, requiring focused confirmation and risk stratification.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Bronchitis"][/sc]
  • Pneumonia - Features high fever and focal chest signs.
  • Asthma - Reversible airflow obstruction with history of episodic wheezing.
  • Chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD) - Persistent airflow limitation with significant smoking history.
  • Alternative serious diagnosis - Distinguishing history, examination, and targeted investigations do not support bronchitis.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Bronchitis"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Assess physiological stability and urgent complications at presentation, and initiate early stabilisation where required.
  • Core care: Deliver condition-specific treatment for bronchitis, address underlying drivers, and involve appropriate specialist support.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Reassess symptom trajectory, objective markers, and treatment response over time to detect complications early.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Worsening symptoms, physiological deterioration, or failure to improve should trigger urgent reassessment for severe or alternative pathology in bronchitis.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Bronchitis"][/sc]
  • Acute bronchitis: Progression to pneumonia (rare).
  • Chronic bronchitis: Pulmonary hypertension, cor pulmonale, and recurrent respiratory infections.
