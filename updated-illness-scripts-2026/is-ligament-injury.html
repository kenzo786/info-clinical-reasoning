Ligament Injury
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Ligament Injury"][/sc]
  • A ligament injury involves partial or complete tearing of the dense connective tissue bands that connect bones across a joint, stabilising it and controlling movement.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise ligament features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Ligament Injury"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Ligament Injury"][/sc]
  • Common in hospital orthopaedic and sports medicine departments, particularly anterior cruciate ligament (ACL) tears.
  • Worldwide, ligament injuries are prevalent in athletes, particularly in sports involving high-impact or rapid directional changes.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Ligament Injury"][/sc]
  • Excessive force or abnormal motion stretches the ligament beyond its elastic limit.
  • This leads to fibre disruption, local bleeding, and inflammatory response.
  • Depending on the severity (graded I-III), healing may involve scar formation (reducing flexibility) or incomplete recovery, affecting joint stability.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Ligament Injury"][/sc]
  • Acute phase: Immediate pain, swelling, and instability lasting days to weeks.
  • Subacute phase: Decreased swelling and inflammation; partial functional recovery begins (weeks to months).
  • Chronic phase: Persistent instability or dysfunction if improperly treated (months to years).
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Ligament Injury"][/sc]
  • Ask about pain - Sharp or dull ache due to nerve ending irritation and inflammation.
  • Ask about swelling - Localised oedema caused by inflammation and vascular injury.
  • Ask about instability - Joint feels weak or gives way due to mechanical disruption.
  • Ask about limited range of motion - Protective muscle spasm and swelling restrict joint movement.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Ligament Injury"][/sc]
  • Check tenderness - Pain upon palpation indicates localised injury.
  • Check joint effusion - Swelling within the joint capsule detectable on examination.
  • Check abnormal laxity - Positive stress tests (e.g., Lachman test for ACL) due to ligament disruption.
  • Check ecchymosis - Bruising from subcutaneous bleeding indicates vascular involvement.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Ligament Injury"][/sc]
  • Severe pain and rapid swelling - Suggests major ligament rupture or haemarthrosis.
  • Persistent instability - May indicate failed healing or additional injuries (e.g., meniscal tear).
  • Neurovascular compromise - Numbness or pallor can suggest associated nerve or vascular injury.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Ligament Injury"][/sc]
  • Delayed swelling - Seen in less severe grades; may be mistaken for minor injury.
  • No immediate pain - Some complete ruptures may not initially hurt due to severed pain fibres.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask ligament injury.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Ligament Injury"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Physical examination - Stress tests to assess joint stability (e.g., anterior drawer for ACL).
  • Targeted and monitoring: MRI - Visualises ligament fibres, confirming the extent of injury; Ultrasound - Identifies partial tears and joint effusions in some cases.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Ligament Injury"][/sc]
  • Meniscal tear - Locking and clicking differ from ligament injury’s instability.
  • Fracture - Bony tenderness and X-ray findings distinguish fractures.
  • Dislocation - Gross deformity with obvious joint displacement.
  • Related alternative diagnosis - Distinguish from ligament injury using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.

  • Immediate priorities: Assess severity, physiological stability, and immediate complications.
  • Core care: Deliver condition-specific management for ligament injury, treat underlying drivers, and coordinate appropriate specialist input.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Reassess symptom trajectory, examination findings, and objective markers to confirm response in ligament injury.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Escalate urgently for clinical deterioration, evolving red flags, or failure to improve in suspected ligament injury.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Ligament Injury"][/sc]
  • Chronic instability - Increases the risk of osteoarthritis.
  • Joint stiffness - From excessive scar tissue or inadequate rehabilitation.
