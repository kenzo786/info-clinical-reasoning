Iron Deficiency
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Iron Deficiency"][/sc]
  • Iron deficiency is a condition characterised by inadequate iron levels to meet the body’s requirements for normal erythropoiesis, leading to impaired production of haemoglobin.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise iron features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.

  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Iron Deficiency"][/sc]

  • Higher-risk or missed groups: Iron Deficiency may be missed when presentation is atypical or obscured by comorbidity.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Iron Deficiency"][/sc]
  • Reduced Iron Intake or Absorption: Iron deficiency often begins due to low dietary iron or impaired absorption (e.g., coeliac disease, gastrectomy).
  • Increased Demand: Growth spurts, pregnancy, and lactation increase the body's iron requirement, making deficiency more likely.
  • Increased Loss: Chronic blood loss, such as from heavy menstruation or gastrointestinal bleeding (peptic ulcers, malignancy), depletes iron stores.
  • Impaired Erythropoiesis: As iron becomes depleted, haemoglobin synthesis is reduced, leading to hypochromic, microcytic anaemia and a subsequent reduction in oxygen delivery to tissues.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Iron Deficiency"][/sc]
  • Initial Phase: Asymptomatic period where iron stores are depleted, but haemoglobin remains normal.
  • Intermediate Phase: Mild anaemia develops, with symptoms like fatigue and reduced exercise tolerance.
  • Late Phase: Severe anaemia with marked symptoms such as pallor, breathlessness, and possible cardiovascular strain (tachycardia, heart failure).
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Iron Deficiency"][/sc]
  • Ask about fatigue: Reduced oxygen delivery to tissues causes diminished energy production and tiredness.
  • Ask about dyspnoea on exertion: Inadequate oxygen-carrying capacity makes physical activity more difficult.
  • Ask about dizziness: Cerebral hypoxia can lead to feelings of light-headedness.
  • Ask about palpitations: The heart works harder to compensate for reduced oxygenation, leading to awareness of heartbeat.
  • Ask about cold intolerance: Impaired oxygen delivery affects thermoregulation, making patients more sensitive to cold.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Iron Deficiency"][/sc]
  • Check pallor: Reduced haemoglobin gives a pale appearance to the skin and mucous membranes.
  • Check koilonychia (spoon-shaped nails): Long-standing iron deficiency affects nail growth and structure.
  • Check tachycardia: The heart compensates for anaemia by increasing heart rate to boost oxygen delivery.
  • Check angular cheilitis: Inflammation and cracking at the corners of the mouth, related to reduced epithelial regeneration.
  • Check glossitis: Atrophy and inflammation of the tongue, possibly due to impaired mucosal turnover.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Iron Deficiency"][/sc]
  • Unexplained weight loss: May suggest underlying malignancy, particularly in the gastrointestinal tract.
  • Persistent GI symptoms (e.g., abdominal pain, change in bowel habit): Could indicate peptic ulcer disease, coeliac disease, or malignancy.
  • Post-menopausal bleeding: Associated with gynaecological malignancies or other serious pathology.
  • Signs of heart failure (e.g., oedema, breathlessness): Severe, chronic anaemia can result in high-output cardiac failure.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Iron Deficiency"][/sc]
  • Mild cognitive impairment: Elderly patients may present with subtle mental status changes due to chronic cerebral hypoxia.
  • Restless legs syndrome: An uncommon but recognised symptom in chronic iron deficiency, possibly due to reduced dopamine production in the brain.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask iron deficiency.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Iron Deficiency"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Full Blood Count (FBC): Low haemoglobin, microcytic (MCV <80 fL), hypochromic (MCHC <320 g/L) indices; Serum Ferritin: Low (<30 ng/mL) confirms.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Serum Iron & Transferrin Saturation: Low serum iron with raised total iron-binding capacity (TIBC), leading to low transferrin saturation (<15%); Peripheral.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Iron Deficiency"][/sc]
  • Thalassaemia - Distinguished by normal or raised ferritin and a family history. Blood film shows target.
  • Anaemia of chronic disease - Presents with normocytic or microcytic anaemia, but ferritin is raised or normal and transferrin.
  • Sideroblastic anaemia - Causes microcytic anaemia but is distinguished by the presence of ringed sideroblasts on bone.
  • Coeliac disease - Can cause iron deficiency due to malabsorption; check tissue transglutaminase antibodies.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Iron Deficiency"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Assess severity, physiological stability, and immediate complications.
  • Core care: Deliver condition-specific management for iron deficiency, treat underlying drivers, and coordinate appropriate specialist input.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Reassess symptom trajectory, examination findings, and objective markers to confirm response in iron deficiency.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Escalate urgently for clinical deterioration, evolving red flags, or failure to improve in suspected iron deficiency.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Iron Deficiency"][/sc]
  • Heart Failure: Chronic iron deficiency and severe anaemia can lead to high-output heart failure.
  • Immune Dysfunction: Impaired immune response, leading to increased susceptibility to infections.
  • Impaired Cognitive Development: In children, prolonged iron deficiency can cause developmental delays and behavioural disturbances.
  • Prognosis: Generally good with appropriate treatment; however, untreated severe iron deficiency can lead to significant morbidity.
