Contact Dermatitis
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Contact Dermatitis"][/sc]
  • Contact dermatitis is an inflammatory skin reaction that occurs when the skin comes into contact with specific irritants or allergens.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise contact features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Contact Dermatitis"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Contact Dermatitis"][/sc]
  • Risk factors: Risk of contact dermatitis rises with relevant predisposition, comorbidity, and exposure profile.
  • Higher-risk or missed groups: Contact Dermatitis may be missed when presentations are atypical or comorbidity obscures key features.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Contact Dermatitis"][/sc]
  • Irritant Contact Dermatitis (ICD): ICD occurs due to direct damage to the skin's barrier from repeated exposure to irritants (e.g., soaps, chemicals). This results in cellular injury, inflammation, and release of pro-inflammatory cytokines.
  • Allergic Contact Dermatitis (ACD): ACD involves a Type IV hypersensitivity reaction where allergen exposure sensitises T-cells. Upon re-exposure, these sensitised T-cells release inflammatory mediators, causing localised skin inflammation.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Contact Dermatitis"][/sc]
  • Acute Phase: Begins hours to days post-exposure, with erythema, swelling, and vesicle formation in affected areas.
  • Subacute Phase: Lasts for 1-2 weeks with less erythema and increased scaling or peeling.
  • Chronic Phase: Persistent cases lead to thickening, lichenification, and hyperpigmentation due to prolonged inflammation.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Contact Dermatitis"][/sc]
  • Ask about itching (pruritus): Caused by release of histamine and inflammatory cytokines; typically most intense in ACD.
  • Ask about burning Sensation: More common in ICD due to direct skin damage and nerve irritation.
  • Ask about pain: May occur in severe ICD cases, especially if erosions or fissures develop.
  • Ask about dryness: Results from disruption of the skin barrier in chronic or subacute dermatitis.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Contact Dermatitis"][/sc]
  • Check erythema (redness): Due to vasodilation and increased blood flow from inflammatory mediators.
  • Check swelling (oedema): Localised oedema caused by inflammatory fluid accumulation in the skin.
  • Check vesicles or Bullae: Fluid-filled blisters, especially in ACD, result from intense inflammation and fluid exudation.
  • Check crusting and Scaling: Develop as vesicles rupture and dry out in the subacute to chronic phases.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Contact Dermatitis"][/sc]
  • Extensive Skin Involvement: May indicate secondary infection, especially if oozing or purulent discharge is present, requiring prompt evaluation.
  • Systemic Symptoms: Fever or malaise suggests potential systemic reaction or infection, such as cellulitis.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Contact Dermatitis"][/sc]
  • Non-pruritic presentations: Certain irritant exposures may cause dermatitis without significant itching, especially in elderly patients or those with sensory neuropathy.
  • Delayed onset in ACD: Reactions may take 24-72 hours post-exposure, which can lead to diagnostic delays if the exposure is not recognised.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features or comorbidity can mask the underlying diagnosis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Contact Dermatitis"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Patch Testing: Identifies allergens responsible for ACD by applying common allergens on the skin and observing reactions 48-72 hours later.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Skin Biopsy: In uncertain cases, biopsy may reveal spongiosis and epidermal changes, confirming dermatitis diagnosis.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Contact Dermatitis"][/sc]
  • A 35-year-old woman presents with a 3-day history of itchy, erythematous rash on her hands, worsened by contact with cleaning products.
  • Adult with symptom pattern and objective findings suggestive of contact dermatitis, requiring focused confirmation and risk stratification.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Contact Dermatitis"][/sc]
  • Atopic Dermatitis - Differentiate from contact dermatitis using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.
  • Psoriasis - Characterised by thick, scaly plaques; typically non-pruritic and distributed on extensor surfaces.
  • Tinea (fungal infection) - Presents with an annular, scaly pattern; confirmed by skin scraping and microscopy.
  • Alternative serious diagnosis - Distinguishing history, examination, and targeted investigations do not support contact dermatitis.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Contact Dermatitis"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Assess physiological stability and urgent complications at presentation, and initiate early stabilisation where required.
  • Core care: Deliver condition-specific treatment for contact dermatitis, address underlying drivers, and involve appropriate specialist support.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Reassess symptom trajectory, objective markers, and treatment response over time to detect complications early.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Worsening symptoms, physiological deterioration, or failure to improve should trigger urgent reassessment for severe or alternative pathology in contact dermatitis.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Contact Dermatitis"][/sc]
  • Secondary Infection: Due to skin barrier disruption, leading to bacterial infections, e.g., impetigo, cellulitis.
  • Chronic Dermatitis: Repeated exposure to allergens/irritants may cause persistent lichenification and hyperpigmentation.
  • Impact on Quality of Life: Chronic itching and visible lesions can cause psychosocial stress and occupational limitations.
