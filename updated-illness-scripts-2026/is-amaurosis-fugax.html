Amaurosis Fugax
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Amaurosis Fugax"][/sc]
  • Amaurosis fugax is transient monocular visual loss from temporary retinal ischaemia, usually representing a retinal transient ischaemic attack.
  • UKMLA essentials: transient monocular vision loss, concurrent focal neurology concern, urgent TIA pathway with carotid imaging.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Amaurosis Fugax"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Amaurosis Fugax"][/sc]
  • Risk factors: Risk of amaurosis fugax rises with relevant predisposition, comorbidity, and exposure profile.
  • Higher-risk or missed groups: Amaurosis Fugax may be missed when presentations are atypical or comorbidity obscures key features.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Amaurosis Fugax"][/sc]
  • Amaurosis fugax results from transient retinal ischaemia, often due to emboli originating from carotid artery disease or atrial fibrillation. Less commonly, it may result from vasospasm or systemic hypoperfusion.
  • Emboli cause obstruction of retinal arterioles, reducing blood supply to retinal ganglion cells, leading to temporary visual loss.
  • Resolution occurs as emboli dissolve or perfusion improves, restoring retinal blood flow.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Amaurosis Fugax"][/sc]
  • Acute phase: Sudden onset of monocular vision loss lasting seconds to minutes, rarely up to an hour.
  • Intermittent episodes: May occur sporadically over days to weeks if underlying embolic source persists.
  • Resolution: Visual recovery is typically complete without residual deficits once perfusion is restored.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Amaurosis Fugax"][/sc]
  • Ask about sudden monocular vision loss - Painless and transient, caused by retinal ischaemia from reduced blood flow.
  • Ask about described as a "curtain" or "shade" - This visual disturbance reflects the specific loss of blood flow to retinal ganglion cells.
  • Ask about complete visual recovery - Indicative of transient ischaemic episodes where retinal perfusion is quickly restored.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Amaurosis Fugax"][/sc]
  • Check normal fundoscopic examination - Most episodes resolve before assessment; however, embolic plaques (Hollenhorst plaques) may occasionally be visible.
  • Check carotid bruits - Suggests significant carotid artery stenosis as a source of emboli.
  • Check neurological examination - Typically normal unless a concurrent transient ischaemic attack (TIA) occurs.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Amaurosis Fugax"][/sc]
  • Concurrent weakness or aphasia - Suggests a TIA involving the cerebral hemisphere, requiring urgent intervention to prevent stroke.
  • Severe headache - May indicate giant cell arteritis, especially in patients over 50 with systemic symptoms.
  • Prolonged vision loss - Raises concern for retinal artery occlusion or other serious vascular events.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Amaurosis Fugax"][/sc]
  • Bilateral transient visual loss - May occur with vertebrobasilar ischaemia instead of typical monocular loss.
  • Gradual onset - Rare but may suggest optic neuropathy or other non-vascular causes.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features or comorbidity can mask the underlying diagnosis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Amaurosis Fugax"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: Urgent history and neuro-ophthalmic examination with blood pressure, pulse rhythm, and fundoscopy to assess retinal or embolic ischaemic features.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Carotid imaging for stenosis; ECG and cardiac evaluation for embolic source; ESR/CRP when giant cell arteritis is suspected; urgent stroke-clinic monitoring.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Amaurosis Fugax"][/sc]
  • A 65-year-old man with vascular risk factors reports a sudden curtain-like painless loss of vision in one eye lasting 10 minutes with full recovery.
  • Older adult with transient painless monocular visual loss and vascular risk profile, concerning for retinal ischaemia requiring urgent stroke-risk work-up.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Amaurosis Fugax"][/sc]
  • Retinal artery occlusion - Persistent monocular visual loss with characteristic retinal findings indicates established occlusion rather than transient ischaemia.
  • Giant cell arteritis - New headache, jaw claudication, scalp tenderness, and inflammatory marker rise suggest arteritic visual ischaemia.
  • Optic neuritis - Painful visual loss with colour desaturation and afferent pupillary defect supports inflammatory optic neuropathy.
  • Ocular migraine - Recurrent positive visual aura phenomena without vascular embolic risk profile suggest migraine-associated disturbance.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Amaurosis Fugax"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Treat as a TIA equivalent with same-day stroke risk assessment and immediate evaluation for embolic source.
  • Core care: Initiate secondary vascular prevention and address carotid, cardiac, and modifiable vascular risk factors through specialist pathways.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Ongoing follow-up should track recurrent episodes, neurological symptoms, and adherence to stroke prevention strategies.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Recurrent episodes, persistent deficit, new focal neurology, or headache with systemic features should trigger emergency reassessment.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Amaurosis Fugax"][/sc]
  • Ischaemic stroke - High risk in untreated patients with embolic amaurosis fugax.
  • Permanent vision loss - May occur if the underlying cause is not addressed.
  • Recurrent episodes - Suggest poor control of embolic or cardiovascular risk factors.
