Radiculopathy
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Radiculopathy"][/sc]
  • Radiculopathy is a condition characterised by the compression or irritation of a nerve root as it exits the spinal column, causing pain, numbness, weakness, or altered sensation along the distribution.
  • UKMLA essentials: recognise radiculopathy features, identify urgent deterioration, prioritise focused testing.

  • Higher-risk or missed groups: Radiculopathy may be missed when presentation is atypical or obscured by comorbidity.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Radiculopathy"][/sc]
  • Radiculopathy occurs when a nerve root becomes compressed or irritated, typically by degenerative disc disease, herniated discs, osteophytes, or spinal stenosis. Mechanical compression leads to local inflammation and disruption of nerve conduction, causing sensory or motor deficits along the distribution of the affected nerve. The lumbar and cervical regions are most commonly involved due to their increased mobility and susceptibility to degenerative changes.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Radiculopathy"][/sc]
  • Acute: Sudden onset of symptoms due to trauma, disc herniation, or acute injury. Symptoms can persist for days to weeks.
  • Subacute: Gradual worsening over weeks to months, often seen with degenerative causes like spondylosis.
  • Chronic: Persistent symptoms lasting longer than 12 weeks, often associated with long-term degenerative changes.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Radiculopathy"][/sc]
  • Ask about pain: Radiating pain in the arm or leg, following the dermatome of the affected nerve root. This is caused by nerve irritation or compression.
  • Ask about numbness and paraesthesia: Patients often report tingling or "pins and needles" in the distribution of the affected nerve. This occurs due to altered sensory.
  • Ask about weakness: Depending on the affected nerve root, patients may experience muscle weakness in the corresponding myotome due to impaired motor function.
  • Ask about loss of reflexes: Affected reflexes are diminished due to disruption of the nerve pathway responsible for deep tendon reflexes.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Radiculopathy"][/sc]
  • Check positive straight leg raise (SLR) test: For lumbar radiculopathy, raising the leg passively causes sciatic pain, indicating nerve root irritation.
  • Check weakness in specific muscle groups: Depending on the nerve root involved (e.g., wrist extension weakness in C6 radiculopathy or ankle dorsiflexion weakness in L5.
  • Check sensory loss: Loss of sensation in the dermatome of the affected nerve root, such as in the thumb and index finger in C6 radiculopathy.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Radiculopathy"][/sc]
  • Saddle anaesthesia: Indicates potential cauda equina syndrome, a surgical emergency requiring urgent intervention to prevent permanent damage.
  • Progressive neurological deficit: Suggests worsening compression or damage to the nerve root, possibly due to a large herniated disc or tumour.
  • Unexplained weight loss, fever, or night sweats: Could indicate malignancy or spinal infection, requiring further investigation.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Radiculopathy"][/sc]
  • In some cases, patients may present with non-specific back or neck pain without clear radicular symptoms, leading to delayed diagnosis. In others, they may only experience isolated weakness or sensory changes without significant pain.
  • How it gets missed: atypical features and comorbidity can mask radiculopathy.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Radiculopathy"][/sc]
  • Baseline and bedside: MRI of the spine: This is the gold standard for visualising nerve root compression. Findings may include disc herniation, spinal stenosis.
  • Targeted and monitoring: Targeted investigations to confirm diagnosis, assess complications, and monitor response in radiculopathy.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Radiculopathy"][/sc]
  • Peripheral neuropathy - Typically presents with bilateral symptoms and is often associated with diabetes or other systemic.
  • Spinal stenosis - Similar to radiculopathy but often involves multiple nerve roots and presents with symptoms worsening.
  • Piriformis syndrome - Affects the sciatic nerve but typically presents with buttock pain radiating down the leg.
  • Related alternative diagnosis - Distinguish from radiculopathy using focused history, examination, and targeted investigations.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Radiculopathy"][/sc]
  • Chronic pain: Persistent nerve root compression can lead to ongoing pain and disability.
  • Permanent neurological deficits: If untreated, severe cases may result in muscle weakness, loss of sensation, or loss of reflexes.
  • Cauda equina syndrome: A rare but serious complication involving compression of the lower spinal nerve roots, leading to bowel or bladder dysfunction, saddle anaesthesia, and potentially irreversible neurological damage.
