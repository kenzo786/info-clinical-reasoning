Huntington's disease [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Huntington's disease"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Huntington's disease"][/sc]
  • Huntington's disease is an inherited, progressive neurodegenerative disorder caused by a CAG trinucleotide repeat expansion in the *HTT* gene, leading to neuronal dysfunction and death in specific brain regions.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical features of Huntington's disease, understand its genetic basis, and appreciate the multidisciplinary approach to symptomatic management and genetic counselling.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Huntington's disease"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Huntington's disease"][/sc]
  • Huntington's disease affects approximately 1 in 10,000 to 1 in 20,000 people in the UK [1].
  • It is an autosomal dominant condition, meaning a child has a 50% chance of inheriting the altered gene if one parent is affected [2].
  • Onset typically occurs between 30 and 50 years of age, but can manifest at any age, including juvenile forms [2].
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Huntington's disease"][/sc]
  • The underlying cause is an expanded CAG trinucleotide repeat in the *HTT* gene on chromosome 4, leading to the production of an abnormally long huntingtin protein.
  • This mutant huntingtin protein accumulates and misfolds, causing neuronal toxicity and selective degeneration, particularly in the striatum (caudate and putamen) and cerebral cortex. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="CAG repeat expansion: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="CAG repeat expansion"][/sc]
  • The progressive loss of neurons results in characteristic motor, cognitive, and psychiatric symptoms, ultimately leading to severe functional decline and death.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Huntington's disease"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Subtle changes in mood, cognition, and motor control may precede overt symptoms by many years. Patients may experience irritability, depression, anxiety, and minor involuntary movements.
  • Progressive phase: Motor symptoms such as chorea become more pronounced, accompanied by significant cognitive decline (e.g., impaired executive function, memory problems) and psychiatric disturbances (e.g., psychosis, obsessive-compulsive behaviours). Functional independence gradually diminishes.
  • Late phase: Severe motor impairment, including rigidity, dystonia, and dysphagia, leads to complete dependence. Communication becomes difficult, and cognitive function is severely compromised. Death typically occurs 15-20 years after symptom onset, often due to complications like aspiration pneumonia or falls.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Huntington's disease"][/sc]
  • Ask about involuntary movements (chorea) - these are often the most recognisable motor symptom, but can be subtle initially and worsen with stress.
  • Ask about cognitive changes - difficulty with planning, organisation, and problem-solving (executive dysfunction) is common and can precede motor symptoms.
  • Ask about psychiatric symptoms - depression, anxiety, irritability, and apathy are frequent and can significantly impact quality of life.
  • Ask about weight loss - often unexplained, it can be an early sign and contributes to overall decline.
  • Ask about speech and swallowing difficulties (dysarthria and dysphagia) - these develop as the disease progresses and increase the risk of aspiration.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Huntington's disease"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - generally normal in early stages, but weight loss may be evident.
  • Check for chorea - involuntary, jerky, dance-like movements, often affecting the limbs, trunk, and face. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Chorea: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Chorea"][/sc]
  • Check for dystonia and rigidity - these can develop later in the disease, particularly in juvenile onset, leading to abnormal postures and stiffness.
  • Check eye movements - saccadic abnormalities (slowed or impaired voluntary eye movements) are common.
  • Check gait and balance - often impaired, leading to frequent falls.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Huntington's disease"][/sc]
  • Rapid progression of symptoms: Suggests a more aggressive disease course, particularly in younger individuals, and warrants urgent specialist review.
  • Severe dysphagia: Indicates a high risk of aspiration pneumonia and malnutrition, requiring immediate assessment for feeding support and speech and language therapy.
  • Acute behavioural changes or psychosis: May require urgent psychiatric assessment and management to ensure patient safety and prevent harm to self or others.
  • Recurrent falls: Signifies significant motor impairment and increased risk of injury, necessitating a comprehensive falls assessment and safety planning.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Huntington's disease"][/sc]
  • Juvenile Huntington's disease: Onset before age 20, often presenting with rigidity, dystonia, seizures, and rapid cognitive decline rather than chorea. This is associated with larger CAG repeat expansions. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Anticipation in Huntington's disease: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Anticipation"][/sc]
  • Westphal variant: A rigid-akinetic form of Huntington's disease, particularly seen in juvenile onset, where chorea is absent or minimal.
  • Late-onset Huntington's disease: Onset after age 60, typically with milder symptoms and slower progression, often with less prominent chorea.
  • Psychiatric-onset Huntington's disease: Initial presentation dominated by psychiatric symptoms such as depression, anxiety, obsessive-compulsive disorder, or psychosis, with motor symptoms developing later.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Huntington's disease"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected Huntington's disease: Genetic testing for CAG repeat expansion in the *HTT* gene is the definitive diagnostic test. A repeat length of 40 or more confirms the diagnosis. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="CAG repeat threshold in Huntington's disease: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="CAG repeat threshold"][/sc]
  • Severity/risk tools: The Unified Huntington\'s Disease Rating Scale (UHDRS) is a comprehensive assessment tool used to quantify motor, cognitive, behavioural, and functional impairments. UHDRS on MDCalc
  • Imaging - MRI brain: May show caudate and putaminal atrophy, and ventricular enlargement, particularly in later stages, but is not diagnostic.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Consider other causes of chorea or cognitive decline, such as autoimmune encephalitis, drug-induced movement disorders, or other neurodegenerative conditions.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Huntington's disease"][/sc]
  • A 45-year-old male presents with a 2-year history of progressive involuntary jerky movements, mood swings, and subtle cognitive difficulties, with a paternal history of similar symptoms.
  • Progressive chorea and cognitive decline with a strong family history, concerning for Huntington's disease.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Huntington's disease"][/sc]
  • Tardive dyskinesia - history of long-term dopamine receptor blocking agents, often reversible upon discontinuation.
  • Wilson\'s disease - typically presents at a younger age, with Kayser-Fleischer rings and abnormal copper metabolism.
  • Neuroacanthocytosis - rare genetic disorders with chorea, dystonia, and characteristic acanthocytes on blood smear.
  • Drug-induced chorea - temporal relationship with medication initiation, often resolves with drug withdrawal.
  • Benign hereditary chorea - non-progressive chorea, usually presenting in childhood, without cognitive decline or psychiatric features.
  • Spinocerebellar ataxias (SCAs) - a group of genetic disorders that can cause ataxia, dysarthria, and sometimes chorea, but typically with prominent cerebellar signs.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Huntington's disease"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Recognition of symptoms, referral to a specialist neurology service, and initiation of multidisciplinary supportive care.
  • Definitive management: Currently, there is no disease-modifying treatment. Management focuses on symptomatic control and supportive care.
  • Adjunctive management: May include physiotherapy for motor symptoms, occupational therapy for daily living activities, speech and language therapy for dysphagia and dysarthria, and psychological support for psychiatric symptoms.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Regular follow-up with a multidisciplinary team to monitor symptom progression, adjust treatments, and provide ongoing support.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Patients and families should be educated on potential complications and when to seek urgent medical attention (e.g., severe dysphagia, acute behavioural changes).
  • Exam pharmacology reference (UKMLA/MRCGP): Tetrabenazine may be considered for the management of chorea. Antidepressants (e.g., SSRIs) and antipsychotics may be used for psychiatric symptoms. Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Huntington's disease"][/sc]
  • Aspiration pneumonia: Due to progressive dysphagia, increasing the risk of respiratory infections and a common cause of death in late-stage disease.
  • Falls and injuries: Resulting from motor incoordination, balance impairment, and dystonia, leading to fractures and head trauma.
  • Malnutrition and weight loss: Due to dysphagia, increased energy expenditure from involuntary movements, and cognitive impairment affecting eating habits.
  • Depression and suicide: Psychiatric symptoms are prevalent, and the risk of suicide is significantly elevated, particularly in the early and middle stages of the disease.
  • Social isolation and loss of independence: Progressive functional decline leads to increasing dependence on carers and withdrawal from social activities.
