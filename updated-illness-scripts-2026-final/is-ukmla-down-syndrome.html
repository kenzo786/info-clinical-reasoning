Down syndrome [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Down syndrome"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Down syndrome"][/sc]
  • Down syndrome is a genetic condition caused by the presence of an extra copy of chromosome 21 (trisomy 21), leading to characteristic physical features and developmental delays.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical features, common complications, and management principles of Down syndrome.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Down syndrome"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Down syndrome"][/sc]
  • Down syndrome is the most common chromosomal condition, affecting approximately 1 in 1,000 live births in the UK (ONS data).
  • Maternal age is the primary risk factor, with incidence increasing significantly in women over 35 years.
  • Risk factors also include a family history of Down syndrome or being a carrier of a balanced translocation.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Down syndrome"][/sc]
  • Trigger: The presence of an extra copy of chromosome 21, usually due to non-disjunction during meiosis, leading to trisomy 21.
  • Progression: Overexpression of genes on chromosome 21 disrupts normal developmental pathways, affecting multiple organ systems and cognitive function.
  • Consequence: Characteristic facial features, intellectual disability, congenital heart defects, gastrointestinal anomalies, and increased risk of other health issues.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Down syndrome"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Developmental delays and characteristic physical features are often apparent from birth or early infancy.
  • Progressive phase: Ongoing developmental challenges, increased susceptibility to infections, and potential for cardiac or gastrointestinal complications, particularly beyond the first year of life.
  • Late phase: Increased risk of Alzheimer's disease-like dementia and other age-related health issues, particularly beyond middle age.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Down syndrome"][/sc]
  • Ask about feeding difficulties - may indicate hypotonia or congenital heart disease.
  • Ask about developmental milestones - delays are common across all domains, crucial for early intervention.
  • Ask about recurrent infections - increased susceptibility, particularly respiratory, due to immune dysfunction.
  • Ask about constipation - common gastrointestinal issue, may indicate Hirschsprung's disease.
  • Ask about snoring or restless sleep - suggests obstructive sleep apnoea, common due to anatomical features.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Down syndrome"][/sc]
  • Check for hypotonia - generalised muscle floppiness, impacting motor development.
  • Check for characteristic facial features - upslanting palpebral fissures, epicanthic folds, flat nasal bridge, small ears, protruding tongue.
  • Check for single palmar crease - a common dermatoglyphic finding, though not pathognomonic.
  • Check for cardiac murmurs - indicative of congenital heart defects, e.g., ventricular septal defect.
  • Check vitals - particularly heart rate and oxygen saturation, to assess for cardiac or respiratory compromise.
