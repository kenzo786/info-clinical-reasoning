Anaphylaxis [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Anaphylaxis"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Anaphylaxis"][/sc]
  • Anaphylaxis is a severe, life-threatening, generalised or systemic hypersensitivity reaction, characterised by rapidly developing airway and/or breathing and/or circulatory problems, usually associated with skin and mucosal changes.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical features of anaphylaxis and initiate immediate management.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Anaphylaxis"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Anaphylaxis"][/sc]
  • The incidence of anaphylaxis in the UK is estimated to be between 1.5 and 7.9 cases per 100,000 person-years, with a rising trend, particularly in children.
  • Common triggers include foods (e.g., peanuts, tree nuts, milk, eggs), insect stings (e.g., bees, wasps), and medications (e.g., antibiotics, NSAIDs).
  • Risk factors for severe reactions include pre-existing asthma, cardiovascular disease, and mast cell disorders.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Anaphylaxis"][/sc]
  • Triggering of mast cells and basophils by an allergen (IgE-mediated) or non-IgE mechanisms, leading to the release of potent mediators such as histamine, tryptase, and prostaglandins.
  • Progression involves widespread vasodilation, increased vascular permeability, and smooth muscle contraction, affecting multiple organ systems.
  • Consequence is a rapid onset of systemic symptoms, including cardiovascular collapse, respiratory distress, and cutaneous manifestations, potentially leading to death if untreated.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Anaphylaxis"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Symptoms typically develop rapidly, often within minutes to a few hours of exposure to the trigger.
  • Progressive phase: Symptoms can worsen quickly, with respiratory and cardiovascular compromise becoming prominent, particularly beyond the initial 30 minutes.
  • Late phase: A biphasic reaction may occur in a minority of cases, with a recurrence of symptoms hours after the initial resolution, particularly beyond 4-6 hours.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Anaphylaxis"][/sc]
  • Ask about sudden onset of rash, itching, or hives - indicating cutaneous involvement, often an early sign.
  • Ask about difficulty breathing, wheezing, or stridor - suggesting airway obstruction or bronchospasm.
  • Ask about dizziness, lightheadedness, or collapse - pointing to circulatory compromise and hypotension.
  • Ask about abdominal pain, vomiting, or diarrhoea - indicating gastrointestinal involvement.
  • Ask about a sense of impending doom - a non-specific but often reported symptom of severe systemic reactions.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Anaphylaxis"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - hypotension, tachycardia, tachypnoea, and reduced oxygen saturation are critical indicators of severity.
  • Check for urticaria, angioedema, or flushing - visible signs of mast cell degranulation.
  • Check for wheeze or stridor on auscultation - indicative of bronchospasm or upper airway oedema.
  • Check for altered mental status - may suggest cerebral hypoperfusion due to shock.
  • Check for signs of poor perfusion (e.g., prolonged capillary refill time, cool peripheries) - indicating circulatory compromise.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Anaphylaxis"][/sc]
  • Airway compromise - stridor, hoarseness, or inability to speak, indicating severe upper airway oedema.
  • Breathing difficulties - severe wheeze, cyanosis, or respiratory arrest, signifying profound bronchospasm or respiratory failure.
  • Circulatory collapse - hypotension, syncope, or cardiac arrest, representing life-threatening shock.
  • Persistent vomiting or diarrhoea - can exacerbate hypovolaemia and circulatory instability.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Anaphylaxis"][/sc]
  • Older adults: May present with less prominent skin symptoms and more cardiovascular symptoms (e.g., chest pain, arrhythmias), which can be mistaken for other acute cardiac events.
  • Beta-blocker use: Patients on beta-blockers may have refractory hypotension and bradycardia, and may not respond well to adrenaline, making management challenging.
  • Exercise-induced anaphylaxis: Symptoms occur only after exercise following ingestion of a specific food, often presenting without clear triggers in other contexts.
  • Biphasic anaphylaxis: A recurrence of symptoms hours after initial resolution, which can be missed if patients are discharged too early.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Anaphylaxis"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected Anaphylaxis: Serum tryptase levels, ideally taken within 1-2 hours of symptom onset, can support the diagnosis but normal levels do not exclude anaphylaxis.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Consider ECG to rule out cardiac ischaemia, and chest X-ray if respiratory symptoms are prominent and other causes are suspected.
  • Serial reassessment: Continuous monitoring of vital signs, oxygen saturation, and clinical response to treatment is crucial.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Anaphylaxis"][/sc]
  • A 35-year-old female presents to the emergency department with sudden onset of generalised urticaria, wheezing, and dizziness within 10 minutes of eating a prawn curry at a restaurant.
  • A young adult, acutely unwell, experiencing rapid-onset multi-system involvement following allergen exposure, suggesting a severe hypersensitivity reaction.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Anaphylaxis"][/sc]
  • Asthma exacerbation - typically lacks prominent skin or circulatory features.
  • Vasovagal syncope - usually resolves quickly, often with bradycardia, and no skin or respiratory signs.
  • Panic attack - often associated with hyperventilation and anxiety, but without objective signs of airway compromise or hypotension.
  • Scombroid poisoning - ingestion of spoiled fish causing histamine-like symptoms, but usually without severe respiratory or circulatory compromise.
  • Carcinoid syndrome - episodic flushing, diarrhoea, and bronchospasm, but typically chronic and not acute onset following an identifiable trigger.
  • Angioedema (hereditary or acquired) - localised swelling, but usually without urticaria or systemic features of shock.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Anaphylaxis"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Call for help, lie the patient flat with legs raised (if conscious), administer high-flow oxygen, and give intramuscular adrenaline without delay.
  • Definitive management: Remove the trigger if possible, consider intravenous fluids for hypotension, and administer antihistamines and corticosteroids as adjunctive therapies.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Observe for at least 6-12 hours due to the risk of biphasic reactions, provide an adrenaline auto-injector, and refer to an allergy specialist.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: If no improvement with initial adrenaline, repeat doses every 5 minutes; consider intravenous adrenaline infusion in severe, refractory cases in a critical care setting.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Anaphylaxis"][/sc]
  • Hypoxic brain injury: Due to prolonged respiratory or circulatory compromise, leading to irreversible neurological damage.
  • Cardiac arrest: A direct consequence of severe hypotension and myocardial dysfunction, particularly in patients with pre-existing cardiac conditions.
  • Biphasic reaction: Recurrence of symptoms hours after initial resolution, which can lead to delayed morbidity or mortality if not anticipated and monitored.
  • Acute kidney injury: Resulting from prolonged hypoperfusion and shock, impacting renal function.
  • Psychological impact: Significant anxiety and fear of future reactions, affecting quality of life.
