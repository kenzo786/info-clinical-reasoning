Atopic dermatitis/ eczema [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Atopic dermatitis/ eczema"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Atopic dermatitis/ eczema"][/sc]
  • Atopic dermatitis, also known as atopic eczema, is a chronic, relapsing inflammatory skin condition characterised by intense pruritus, erythema, and xerosis, often with periods of exacerbation and remission.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical features, common triggers, and management principles of atopic eczema, including appropriate use of emollients and topical corticosteroids.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Atopic dermatitis/ eczema"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Atopic dermatitis/ eczema"][/sc]
  • Affects approximately 1 in 5 children and 1 in 10 adults in the UK, making it one of the most common chronic inflammatory skin conditions.
  • Onset is typically in early childhood, with around 60% of cases diagnosed in the first year of life, and 85% by age five.
  • Risk factors include a personal or family history of atopy (asthma, allergic rhinitis, or atopic eczema), genetic predisposition (e.g., filaggrin gene mutations), and environmental factors such as irritants and allergens.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Atopic dermatitis/ eczema"][/sc]
  • Trigger: A complex interplay of genetic predisposition (e.g., mutations in the FLG gene encoding filaggrin), immune dysregulation, and environmental factors leads to a compromised skin barrier.
  • Progression: Impaired skin barrier function [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Skin barrier dysfunction in atopic dermatitis: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="skin barrier dysfunction"][/sc] allows increased penetration of allergens, irritants, and microbes, triggering an exaggerated immune response involving Th2 cells and inflammatory cytokines.
  • Consequence: Chronic inflammation, intense pruritus, and scratching further damage the skin barrier, leading to a vicious cycle of inflammation, itching, and infection, often exacerbated by a deficiency in filaggrin [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Filaggrin deficiency in atopic dermatitis: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="filaggrin deficiency"][/sc].
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Atopic dermatitis/ eczema"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Typically presents in infancy or early childhood with acute erythematous, papulovesicular lesions, often on the face and extensor surfaces. Flares can be frequent and intense, lasting days to weeks.
  • Progressive phase: In older children and adults, lesions commonly shift to flexural areas (antecubital and popliteal fossae), becoming more lichenified and chronic. Periods of remission and exacerbation are characteristic, with flares lasting several weeks to months.
  • Late phase: While many children improve with age, atopic eczema can persist into adulthood, or reappear later in life, often with chronic hand eczema or localised lichenification. The condition is characterised by its relapsing nature, with symptoms potentially recurring throughout life.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Atopic dermatitis/ eczema"][/sc]
  • Ask about pruritus (itching) - this is the cardinal symptom and often precedes visible skin lesions, significantly impacting quality of life and sleep.
  • Ask about dry skin (xerosis) - a common feature due to impaired skin barrier function, contributing to itch and inflammation.
  • Ask about rash distribution and morphology - typically presents as erythematous patches, papules, and plaques, often with excoriation, scaling, and lichenification, varying with age and chronicity.
  • Ask about triggers and aggravating factors - identifying irritants (e.g., soaps, detergents), allergens (e.g., dust mites, pollen, certain foods), stress, or climate changes can help in management.
  • Ask about sleep disturbance - severe pruritus, particularly at night, can lead to significant sleep disruption for both the patient and their family.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Atopic dermatitis/ eczema"][/sc]
  • Check for erythema and excoriation - redness and scratch marks are common, indicating inflammation and pruritus.
  • Check for lichenification - thickening of the skin with exaggerated skin markings, typically seen in chronic atopic eczema due to repeated scratching.
  • Check for xerosis - dry, scaly skin, often widespread, reflecting impaired barrier function.
  • Check for flexural involvement - characteristic distribution in older children and adults, affecting the antecubital and popliteal fossae, wrists, and neck.
  • Check vital signs - usually normal in uncomplicated atopic eczema; fever or tachycardia may suggest secondary infection.
  • Check for Dennie-Morgan folds [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Dennie-Morgan folds: what are they and why do they matter clinically?" text="Dennie-Morgan folds"][/sc] - infraorbital folds that can indicate chronic inflammation and oedema around the eyes.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Atopic dermatitis/ eczema"][/sc]
  • Widespread acute flare with systemic symptoms: Suggests severe infection (e.g., eczema herpeticum, cellulitis) requiring urgent assessment and systemic treatment, particularly if rapidly progressive over hours to days.
  • Failure to respond to optimal topical therapy: May indicate misdiagnosis, severe disease requiring systemic therapy, or underlying complications such as secondary infection or contact dermatitis, warranting specialist review within weeks.
  • Significant impact on quality of life: Severe sleep disturbance, psychological distress, or impairment of daily activities despite treatment necessitates prompt psychological support and consideration of advanced therapies, typically within days to weeks.
  • Sudden onset of painful, crusted, vesicular lesions: Highly suspicious for eczema herpeticum, a dermatological emergency requiring immediate antiviral treatment to prevent widespread dissemination, often developing over hours.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Atopic dermatitis/ eczema"][/sc]
  • Infantile Atopic Eczema: Often presents on the face (cheeks, forehead) and extensor surfaces, with weeping and crusting, rather than the typical flexural involvement seen in older children.
  • Nummular Eczema: Characterised by coin-shaped lesions, often on the trunk and limbs, which can be intensely pruritic and may be mistaken for fungal infections.
  • Follicular Eczema: Presents as papules around hair follicles, particularly in individuals with darker skin tones, and can be a challenging variant to diagnose.
  • Atopic Erythroderma: A rare but severe form where nearly the entire skin surface is inflamed, red, and scaly, often associated with systemic symptoms and requiring hospitalisation.
  • Head and Neck Dermatitis: Predominantly affects the head, neck, and upper chest, often with severe pruritus and lichenification, and can be resistant to conventional treatments [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Head and neck dermatitis in atopic eczema: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Head and Neck Dermatitis"][/sc].
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Atopic dermatitis/ eczema"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected Atopic dermatitis: Diagnosis is primarily clinical, based on characteristic symptoms (pruritus) and signs (eczematous lesions, typical distribution, chronic relapsing course). No specific diagnostic blood tests are routinely required.
  • Severity/risk tools: Patient-Oriented Eczema Measure (POEM) and Children\'s Dermatology Life Quality Index (CDLQI) are validated tools for assessing severity and impact on quality of life. POEM on MDCalc, CDLQI on MDCalc.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Skin swabs for bacterial or viral culture (e.g., Staphylococcus aureus, Herpes simplex virus) if secondary infection is suspected. Patch testing may be considered if allergic contact dermatitis is a differential.
  • Allergy testing: Specific IgE (RAST) or skin prick tests may be considered in severe, refractory cases, particularly in young children with suspected food allergy, but are not routinely recommended for diagnosis of atopic eczema itself. Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Atopic dermatitis/ eczema"][/sc]
  • A 5-year-old boy presents with a 3-year history of intensely itchy, erythematous, and lichenified plaques in the antecubital and popliteal fossae, exacerbated by soaps and dry weather, significantly disrupting his sleep.
  • A child with chronic, relapsing pruritic dermatitis, consistent with atopic eczema, complicated by sleep disturbance and potential environmental triggers.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Atopic dermatitis/ eczema"][/sc]
  • Contact Dermatitis: Often localised to areas of contact with an irritant or allergen, with a clearer demarcation than atopic eczema, and resolves with avoidance of the trigger.
  • Psoriasis: Characterised by well-demarcated erythematous plaques with silvery scales, typically on extensor surfaces, scalp, and nails, and less pruritic than atopic eczema.
  • Seborrhoeic Dermatitis: Affects sebum-rich areas (scalp, face, chest) with greasy, yellowish scales on an erythematous base, and is less pruritic than atopic eczema.
  • Scabies: Presents with intense pruritus, especially at night, and characteristic burrows, papules, and vesicles in specific areas (finger webs, wrists, axillae, genitalia), often affecting multiple family members.
  • Fungal Infections (Tinea Corporis): Typically presents as annular, erythematous, scaly plaques with central clearing, often unilateral, and can be confirmed by microscopy and culture.
  • Ichthyosis Vulgaris: A genetic disorder causing dry, scaly skin, particularly on the shins, but without the significant inflammation and pruritus characteristic of atopic eczema. Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Atopic dermatitis/ eczema"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Recognising and managing acute flares, identifying and avoiding triggers, and initiating appropriate topical therapy to control symptoms and prevent complications.
  • Definitive management: Often includes regular and liberal use of emollients to restore skin barrier function, and topical corticosteroids of appropriate potency for managing inflammation during flares.
  • Adjunctive management: May support with topical calcineurin inhibitors (tacrolimus, pimecrolimus) for sensitive areas or steroid-sparing effects, wet wraps, or bandages for severe flares.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Regular review to assess disease control, adherence to treatment, identify new triggers, and monitor for complications such as infection or growth faltering in children.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Consider referral to dermatology for severe, widespread, or refractory atopic eczema, recurrent infections, or significant impact on quality of life, to explore options such as phototherapy or systemic immunosuppressants (e.g., dupilumab).
  • Exam pharmacology reference (UKMLA/MRCGP): Topical corticosteroids (e.g., hydrocortisone, betamethasone) and topical calcineurin inhibitors (e.g., tacrolimus, pimecrolimus) are commonly tested. Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Atopic dermatitis/ eczema"][/sc]
  • Secondary Bacterial Infection: Often caused by Staphylococcus aureus, presenting as crusting, weeping, pustules, or cellulitis, particularly during acute flares lasting days. This can worsen eczema and delay healing.
  • Eczema Herpeticum: A severe viral infection caused by Herpes simplex virus, characterised by painful, monomorphic, punched-out erosions and vesicles, rapidly spreading over hours to days, and can lead to systemic illness.
  • Growth Faltering: In severe, chronic atopic eczema in children, persistent inflammation, sleep disturbance, and dietary restrictions can contribute to poor growth, evident over months to years.
  • Psychological Impact: Chronic pruritus, visible skin lesions, and sleep deprivation can lead to significant anxiety, depression, social isolation, and reduced quality of life, developing over weeks to months.
  • Contact Dermatitis: Patients with atopic eczema are more susceptible to developing allergic contact dermatitis due to impaired skin barrier, often presenting as a worsening or change in eczema pattern over days to weeks.
  • Ocular Complications: Chronic inflammation and scratching around the eyes can lead to conditions such as atopic keratoconjunctivitis, cataracts, and retinal detachment, typically developing over years.
