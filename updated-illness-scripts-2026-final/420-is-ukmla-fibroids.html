Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Fibroids (Gynaecological tract)"][/sc]
  • Uterine fibroids (leiomyomas) are benign, non-cancerous growths of smooth muscle and fibrous tissue that develop in or around the womb (uterus).
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical presentation of uterine fibroids, understand their classification, and outline the principles of management, including conservative, medical, and surgical options.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Fibroids (Gynaecological tract)"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Fibroids (Gynaecological tract)"][/sc]
  • Fibroids are common, affecting around 1 in 3 women who menstruate at some point in their life, with prevalence increasing with age, most often occurring between 30 and 50 years.
  • They are thought to be more frequent in women of African-Caribbean origin and in overweight or obese women due to increased oestrogen levels.
  • Women who have had children have a lower risk of developing fibroids.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Fibroids (Gynaecological tract)"][/sc]
  • Fibroid development is linked to oestrogen and progesterone, with growth typically occurring during reproductive years when hormone levels are high.
  • These benign tumours arise from the smooth muscle cells of the myometrium, forming distinct masses within the uterine wall or on its surfaces. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Myometrium: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Myometrium"][/sc]
  • The presence and growth of fibroids can lead to distortion of the uterine cavity, increased uterine surface area, and altered uterine contractility, contributing to symptoms like heavy menstrual bleeding and pressure effects. Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Fibroids (Gynaecological tract)"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Fibroids often develop asymptomatically, growing slowly over years during reproductive age, frequently discovered incidentally during routine examinations.
  • Progressive phase: As fibroids grow, symptoms such as heavy menstrual bleeding, pelvic pressure, and pain may gradually worsen, particularly if fibroids are large or submucosal.
  • Late phase: After menopause, fibroids typically shrink due to reduced oestrogen levels, leading to an improvement or resolution of symptoms.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Fibroids (Gynaecological tract)"][/sc]
  • Ask about heavy menstrual bleeding (menorrhagia) - this is a common symptom and can lead to anaemia and impact quality of life.
  • Ask about pelvic pressure or fullness - indicates larger fibroids causing mass effect on surrounding organs.
  • Ask about urinary frequency or constipation - suggests fibroids are compressing the bladder or rectum.
  • Ask about pain or discomfort during sex (dyspareunia) - may indicate fibroids affecting the uterine position or causing pressure.
  • Ask about lower back pain - can be a referred pain from uterine pressure or nerve compression.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Fibroids (Gynaecological tract)"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - to assess for signs of anaemia (e.g., tachycardia, pallor) secondary to heavy menstrual bleeding.
  • Check abdominal palpation - may reveal a palpable, firm, irregular pelvic mass, particularly with larger fibroids.
  • Check bimanual pelvic examination - can identify an enlarged, irregularly shaped uterus, often mobile and non-tender.
  • Check for pallor of conjunctivae or nail beds - suggests chronic blood loss and anaemia.
  • Check for signs of deep vein thrombosis (DVT) - large fibroids can cause venous compression, increasing DVT risk. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Deep vein thrombosis: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Deep vein thrombosis"][/sc]
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Fibroids (Gynaecological tract)"][/sc]
  • Rapid uterine growth: May indicate a rare malignant transformation to leiomyosarcoma, warranting urgent investigation.
  • Postmenopausal bleeding: Fibroids typically shrink after menopause; new or worsening bleeding requires exclusion of endometrial pathology.
  • Severe acute pelvic pain: Could signify fibroid degeneration (e.g., red degeneration during pregnancy) or torsion of a pedunculated fibroid, requiring immediate assessment.
  • Unexplained anaemia: Persistent or severe anaemia despite iron supplementation may indicate significant ongoing blood loss from fibroids or other underlying pathology.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Fibroids (Gynaecological tract)"][/sc]
  • Pregnancy: Fibroids may grow during pregnancy due to hormonal changes, potentially causing pain (red degeneration), preterm labour, or obstructing labour.
  • Large fibroids: Can present with significant bulk symptoms, such as abdominal distension, hydronephrosis due to ureteric compression, or venous compression leading to leg swelling.
  • Submucosal fibroids: Even small submucosal fibroids can cause disproportionately heavy menstrual bleeding and infertility due to their location distorting the uterine cavity. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Submucosal fibroids: what are they and why do they matter clinically?" text="Submucosal fibroids"][/sc]
  • Asymptomatic presentation: Many women have fibroids without any symptoms, often discovered incidentally during imaging for other conditions.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Fibroids (Gynaecological tract)"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected fibroids: Transvaginal ultrasound (TVS) is the primary imaging modality, identifying fibroid number, size, and location.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Pelvic MRI provides detailed anatomical information, especially for large or multiple fibroids, or when ultrasound is inconclusive.
  • Imaging - Hysteroscopy: For suspected submucosal fibroids, hysteroscopy allows direct visualisation of the uterine cavity and guided biopsy if needed.
  • Severity/risk tools: The Uterine Fibroid Symptom and Health-Related Quality of Life (UFS-QOL) questionnaire can assess symptom severity and impact on quality of life, guiding management decisions.
  • Blood tests: Full blood count (FBC) to assess for anaemia due to heavy menstrual bleeding; iron studies if anaemia is present.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Fibroids (Gynaecological tract)"][/sc]
  • A 45-year-old woman presents with progressively heavy and prolonged menstrual bleeding for the past 6 months, associated with pelvic pressure and increased urinary frequency, with bimanual examination revealing an enlarged, irregular uterus.
  • Middle-aged woman with menorrhagia and bulk symptoms, concerning for uterine fibroids.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Fibroids (Gynaecological tract)"][/sc]
  • Adenomyosis - diffuse endometrial tissue within the myometrium, causing heavy and painful periods, but typically a symmetrically enlarged uterus.
  • Endometrial polyps - benign growths of the uterine lining, causing intermenstrual or heavy bleeding, often smaller and softer than fibroids.
  • Endometrial hyperplasia/cancer - abnormal thickening of the uterine lining, particularly in postmenopausal women, requiring biopsy for definitive diagnosis.
  • Ovarian mass - can cause pelvic pressure and pain, but typically distinct from the uterus on examination and imaging.
  • Ectopic pregnancy - acute abdominal pain and vaginal bleeding in a reproductive-aged woman, requiring urgent exclusion.
  • Pelvic inflammatory disease (PID) - pelvic pain, fever, and vaginal discharge, often with cervical motion tenderness.
  • Coagulopathy - systemic bleeding disorder causing heavy menstrual bleeding, without uterine structural abnormalities. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Coagulopathy: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Coagulopathy"][/sc]
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Fibroids (Gynaecological tract)"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Assessment of anaemia and haemodynamic stability in cases of heavy menstrual bleeding; consider iron supplementation.
  • Definitive management: Management options range from conservative (watchful waiting for asymptomatic fibroids) to medical (GnRH analogues, tranexamic acid, mefenamic acid, combined oral contraceptives, levonorgestrel-releasing intrauterine system) and surgical (myomectomy, uterine artery embolisation, hysterectomy).
  • Adjunctive management: Pain relief with NSAIDs for dysmenorrhoea; management of constipation or urinary symptoms as appropriate.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Regular review of symptoms and fibroid size, particularly if conservative management is chosen.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Advise patients to seek medical attention for worsening symptoms, severe pain, or new postmenopausal bleeding.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Fibroids (Gynaecological tract)"][/sc]
  • Anaemia: Chronic heavy menstrual bleeding can lead to iron deficiency anaemia, causing fatigue, pallor, and reduced exercise tolerance.
  • Infertility/Subfertility: Submucosal or large intramural fibroids can distort the uterine cavity, interfering with implantation or sperm transport, particularly during the progressive phase.
  • Pregnancy complications: Increased risk of miscarriage, preterm labour, placental abruption, and obstructed labour, especially with large or lower segment fibroids.
  • Acute pain: Degeneration (e.g., red degeneration) or torsion of pedunculated fibroids can cause sudden, severe abdominal pain, requiring urgent medical attention.
  • Hydronephrosis: Large fibroids, particularly subserosal, can compress the ureters, leading to hydronephrosis and potential renal impairment.
  • Malignant transformation: Extremely rare (0.1-0.5%) transformation to leiomyosarcoma, typically characterised by rapid growth, particularly after menopause.
