Encephalitis [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Encephalitis"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Encephalitis"][/sc]
  • Encephalitis is an acute inflammation of the brain parenchyma, often caused by viral infection, leading to neurological dysfunction.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical features, common causes, and immediate management of acute encephalitis.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Encephalitis"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Encephalitis"][/sc]
  • In the UK, viral encephalitis has an incidence of approximately 5-10 cases per 100,000 population per year, with herpes simplex virus (HSV) being the most common identifiable cause in adults.
  • Risk factors include extremes of age, immunosuppression, and geographical exposure to arboviruses (e.g., during travel).
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Encephalitis"][/sc]
  • Viral entry into the central nervous system (CNS) often occurs via haematogenous spread or neuronal retrograde transport, leading to direct infection of brain cells.
  • The host immune response, including both innate and adaptive mechanisms, is activated, causing inflammation, oedema, and neuronal damage within the brain parenchyma.
  • This widespread inflammation and cellular destruction result in neurological dysfunction, manifesting as altered mental status, seizures, and focal neurological deficits.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Encephalitis"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Symptoms typically develop acutely over hours to a few days, often preceded by a prodromal viral illness.
  • Progressive phase: Neurological symptoms rapidly worsen over several days, particularly beyond 72 hours, with increasing confusion, seizures, and focal deficits.
  • Late phase: If untreated, progression to coma, brain herniation, and death can occur within days to weeks, with significant long-term neurological sequelae in survivors.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Encephalitis"][/sc]
  • Ask about altered mental status - this is a hallmark symptom, ranging from confusion and disorientation to drowsiness and coma, indicating diffuse brain involvement.
  • Ask about new-onset seizures - these are common and suggest cortical irritation, requiring urgent management.
  • Ask about fever and headache - these non-specific symptoms often precede neurological changes and can mimic other infections.
  • Ask about focal neurological deficits - such as weakness, speech disturbance, or sensory changes, which may indicate localised brain inflammation.
  • Ask about behavioural changes - including irritability, agitation, or personality shifts, which can be subtle but important indicators of limbic system involvement.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Encephalitis"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - fever, tachycardia, and hypertension are common, reflecting systemic inflammation and autonomic dysfunction.
  • Check Glasgow Coma Scale (GCS) - a declining GCS indicates worsening neurological function and increased intracranial pressure.
  • Check meningism (neck stiffness, photophobia) - may be present, particularly if there is associated meningitis, but can be absent in pure encephalitis.
  • Check focal neurological signs - such as hemiparesis, aphasia, or cranial nerve palsies, which help localise the lesion.
  • Check signs of increased intracranial pressure - including papilloedema, Cushing's triad (hypertension, bradycardia, irregular respiration), which are ominous findings.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Encephalitis"][/sc]
  • Rapidly deteriorating consciousness - suggests severe brain inflammation and impending herniation, requiring immediate intervention.
  • New-onset focal neurological deficits - indicates active, destructive brain lesions and necessitates urgent neuroimaging and specialist review.
  • Status epilepticus - prolonged or recurrent seizures are a medical emergency, causing further brain injury and requiring aggressive anticonvulsant therapy.
  • Signs of increased intracranial pressure - such as papilloedema or Cushing's triad, signal critical brain swelling and risk of herniation.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Encephalitis"][/sc]
  • Older adults: May present with more subtle cognitive changes or lethargy, rather than overt fever or seizures, making diagnosis challenging.
  • Immunosuppressed patients: Can have atypical pathogens (e.g., CMV, toxoplasmosis) and a blunted inflammatory response, leading to delayed diagnosis and severe disease.
  • Children: Often present with irritability, poor feeding, or non-specific febrile illness, with neurological signs emerging later.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Encephalitis"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected encephalitis: Lumbar puncture for cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) analysis (cell count, protein, glucose, PCR for common viruses like HSV), blood tests (FBC, U&Es, LFTs, CRP, viral serology).
  • Imaging - MRI brain: Essential for identifying characteristic lesions (e.g., temporal lobe involvement in HSV encephalitis), oedema, or ruling out other pathologies.
  • Electroencephalography (EEG): May show generalised slowing or focal abnormalities (e.g., periodic lateralising epileptiform discharges in HSV), supporting the diagnosis.
  • Serial reassessment: Repeat neurological examination and GCS monitoring are crucial to detect deterioration or response to treatment.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Encephalitis"][/sc]
  • A 45-year-old male presents with a 3-day history of fever, headache, and progressive confusion, now experiencing new-onset focal seizures and expressive dysphasia.
  • Acute, febrile, encephalopathic illness with focal neurological deficits and seizures.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Encephalitis"][/sc]
  • Bacterial meningitis - distinguished by more prominent meningism and CSF findings (high neutrophils, low glucose).
  • Cerebral abscess - often presents with focal neurological deficits and signs of infection, but typically slower onset and ring-enhancing lesion on imaging.
  • Stroke - acute onset of focal neurological deficits, but usually without fever or altered mental status, and characteristic imaging findings.
  • Autoimmune encephalitis - subacute onset, often with psychiatric symptoms, movement disorders, and specific autoantibodies.
  • Toxic-metabolic encephalopathy - altered mental status without focal signs or fever, often with identifiable metabolic derangement.
  • Subarachnoid haemorrhage - sudden severe headache, meningism, but usually without fever or progressive encephalopathy.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Encephalitis"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Secure airway, breathing, and circulation (ABC), manage seizures, reduce intracranial pressure if elevated, and initiate empiric antiviral therapy.
  • Definitive management: Targeted antiviral therapy (e.g., [sc name="jitl-medication" Q="Aciclovir use in Encephalitis" text="aciclovir"] for HSV), supportive care in an intensive care setting, and management of complications.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Close neurological monitoring, serial imaging, and long-term rehabilitation for cognitive and physical sequelae.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Urgent referral to neurology and infectious diseases specialists; advise patients/carers on signs of deterioration requiring immediate medical attention.
  • Exam pharmacology reference (UKMLA/MRCGP): Aciclovir is a nucleoside analogue that inhibits viral DNA polymerase, crucial for treating HSV encephalitis. No doses or frequencies are cited here — always verify against your local antimicrobial/prescribing formulary before prescribing.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Encephalitis"][/sc]
  • Cognitive impairment: Memory deficits, executive dysfunction, and attention problems are common due to widespread neuronal damage, particularly affecting the temporal lobes.
  • Epilepsy: Seizures can persist as a long-term sequela, requiring ongoing anticonvulsant therapy, especially after severe or focal encephalitis.
  • Behavioural and psychiatric changes: Mood disorders, personality changes, and psychosis can arise from damage to frontal and limbic regions.
  • Motor deficits: Weakness, ataxia, or movement disorders may result from damage to motor pathways or basal ganglia.
  • Hydrocephalus: Inflammation can obstruct CSF flow, leading to increased intracranial pressure and requiring shunting in some cases.
