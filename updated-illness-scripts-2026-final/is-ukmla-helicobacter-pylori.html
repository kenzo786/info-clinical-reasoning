Helicobacter pylori (Infections) [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Helicobacter pylori (Infections)"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Helicobacter pylori (Infections)"][/sc]
  • _Helicobacter pylori_ (H. pylori) are spiral-shaped, Gram-negative bacteria that colonise the gastric mucosa, leading to chronic inflammation and a range of gastroduodenal diseases.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical manifestations of H. pylori infection, understand its role in peptic ulcer disease and gastric malignancy, and outline appropriate diagnostic and eradication strategies.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Helicobacter pylori (Infections)"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Helicobacter pylori (Infections)"][/sc]
  • Up to 2 in 5 people in the UK carry H. pylori, though most remain asymptomatic [Guts Charity].
  • Prevalence is higher in older age groups and in individuals from lower socioeconomic backgrounds or those born in countries with higher endemic rates.
  • Transmission is primarily person-to-person, often within families, through oral-oral or faecal-oral routes.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Helicobacter pylori (Infections)"][/sc]
  • H. pylori colonises the gastric mucosa, producing urease which hydrolyses urea to ammonia and carbon dioxide, creating a neutral microenvironment that allows bacterial survival in the acidic stomach. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Urease activity in H. pylori: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Urease activity"][/sc]
  • Chronic inflammation is induced by bacterial virulence factors (e.g., CagA, VacA) and host immune response, leading to gastritis, disruption of the mucosal barrier, and altered gastric acid secretion.
  • Persistent inflammation can progress to peptic ulcer disease, gastric atrophy, intestinal metaplasia, and significantly increases the risk of gastric adenocarcinoma and gastric MALT lymphoma.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Helicobacter pylori (Infections)"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Initial infection is often asymptomatic or may cause acute gastritis with transient dyspeptic symptoms, which typically resolve within days to weeks.
  • Progressive phase: Chronic colonisation develops over months to years, leading to persistent low-grade inflammation (chronic gastritis) which can remain asymptomatic or cause recurrent dyspepsia.
  • Late phase: Decades after initial infection, a subset of individuals may develop complications such as peptic ulcers, gastric atrophy, or gastric cancer, particularly beyond 10-20 years of untreated infection.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Helicobacter pylori (Infections)"][/sc]
  • Ask about epigastric pain - often described as burning or gnawing, typically worse on an empty stomach and relieved by food or antacids, suggesting peptic ulceration.
  • Ask about dyspepsia - including bloating, early satiety, and nausea, which are common but non-specific symptoms of gastritis.
  • Ask about unintentional weight loss - a concerning symptom that may indicate gastric malignancy, particularly in older patients.
  • Ask about vomiting - especially if persistent or containing blood (haematemesis), which suggests severe gastritis, peptic ulcer complications, or malignancy.
  • Ask about dysphagia or odynophagia - these red flag symptoms may indicate oesophageal involvement or advanced gastric cancer.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Helicobacter pylori (Infections)"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - to assess for signs of hypovolaemia (tachycardia, hypotension) in cases of gastrointestinal bleeding, or fever in severe infection.
  • Check abdominal examination - typically unremarkable in uncomplicated H. pylori gastritis, but may reveal epigastric tenderness in peptic ulcer disease.
  • Check pallor - may indicate chronic blood loss from peptic ulcers, leading to iron deficiency anaemia.
  • Check lymphadenopathy - particularly supraclavicular (Virchow's node) or umbilical (Sister Mary Joseph nodule), which are signs of metastatic gastric cancer.
  • Check signs of gastric outlet obstruction - such as succussion splash, suggesting severe peptic ulcer scarring or advanced gastric malignancy. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Succussion splash: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Succussion splash"][/sc]
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Helicobacter pylori (Infections)"][/sc]
  • Unexplained weight loss - raises suspicion for gastric malignancy.
  • Persistent vomiting - may indicate gastric outlet obstruction or advanced disease.
  • Dysphagia or odynophagia - suggests oesophageal or gastric cancer.
  • Gastrointestinal bleeding (haematemesis, melaena, or unexplained anaemia) - points to peptic ulcer complications or malignancy.
  • New onset dyspepsia in patients over 55 years - warrants urgent investigation due to increased risk of gastric cancer.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Helicobacter pylori (Infections)"][/sc]
  • Children: Symptoms may be less specific, often presenting as recurrent abdominal pain, nausea, or vomiting, with peptic ulcer disease being less common than in adults.
  • Older adults: May present with vague symptoms or complications such as anaemia from chronic blood loss, rather than classic dyspepsia.
  • Asymptomatic carriers: A significant proportion of infected individuals never develop symptoms or complications, highlighting the variable clinical course.
  • Gastric MALT lymphoma: H. pylori infection is a primary cause, and eradication can lead to regression of the lymphoma in early stages. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Gastric MALT lymphoma and H. pylori: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Gastric MALT lymphoma"][/sc]
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Helicobacter pylori (Infections)"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected H. pylori:
    • Urea breath test: Highly accurate non-invasive test, detecting active infection by measuring labelled CO2 produced by bacterial urease. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Urea breath test for H. pylori: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Urea breath test"][/sc]
    • Stool antigen test: Non-invasive test detecting H. pylori antigens in faeces, also indicating active infection.
    • Serology: Detects H. pylori antibodies, indicating past or present infection; not suitable for confirming eradication.
  • If endoscopy indicated (e.g., alarm features):
    • Rapid urease test (CLO test): Biopsy samples from gastric mucosa are tested for urease activity, providing a rapid diagnosis during endoscopy. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="CLO test for H. pylori: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="CLO test"][/sc]
    • Histology: Biopsy samples are examined for H. pylori organisms and associated inflammation.
    • Culture: Performed if antibiotic resistance is suspected, allowing for susceptibility testing.
  • Severity/risk tools: No specific validated severity/risk score for H. pylori infection itself, but relevant for associated conditions like peptic ulcer bleeding (e.g., Blatchford Score on MDCalc).
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Helicobacter pylori (Infections)"][/sc]
  • A 45-year-old male presents with a 3-month history of intermittent epigastric burning pain, worse before meals and relieved by food, associated with bloating and occasional nausea.
  • Chronic dyspepsia with features suggestive of peptic ulcer disease, concerning for _Helicobacter pylori_ infection.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Helicobacter pylori (Infections)"][/sc]
  • Gastro-oesophageal reflux disease (GORD) - typically presents with heartburn and regurgitation, often worse after meals or lying down, without the classic ulcer pain pattern.
  • Functional dyspepsia - chronic dyspeptic symptoms without an identifiable organic cause on investigation.
  • NSAID-induced gastropathy - history of NSAID use, often with similar symptoms to peptic ulcer disease but without H. pylori infection.
  • Gastric cancer - alarm features (weight loss, dysphagia, persistent vomiting, anaemia) are more prominent, requiring endoscopic evaluation.
  • Biliary colic - episodic right upper quadrant pain, often post-prandial, radiating to the back or shoulder, associated with gallstones.
  • Pancreatitis - severe epigastric pain radiating to the back, often associated with nausea, vomiting, and elevated amylase/lipase.
  • Irritable bowel syndrome (IBS) - abdominal pain associated with altered bowel habits (constipation/diarrhoea), typically relieved by defecation. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Irritable bowel syndrome vs H. pylori: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Irritable bowel syndrome"][/sc]
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Helicobacter pylori (Infections)"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Recognition of alarm features and appropriate escalation for urgent endoscopy if indicated. Supportive care for dyspeptic symptoms may include proton pump inhibitors (PPIs) or antacids.
  • Definitive management: Eradication therapy is typically recommended for all patients with confirmed H. pylori infection and associated gastroduodenal disease. First-line treatment in the UK often includes a 7-day, twice-daily course of triple therapy (a PPI plus two antibiotics, e.g., amoxicillin and clarithromycin, or clarithromycin and metronidazole).
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Confirmation of eradication is recommended 4-6 weeks after completing therapy, using a urea breath test or stool antigen test, especially in patients with peptic ulcer disease or persistent symptoms.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: If first-line eradication fails, referral to a gastroenterologist for second-line therapy (e.g., bismuth-based quadruple therapy) and further investigation is warranted. Patients should be advised on alarm symptoms requiring immediate medical attention.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Helicobacter pylori (Infections)"][/sc]
  • Peptic ulcer disease: H. pylori is the leading cause of gastric and duodenal ulcers, resulting from chronic inflammation and mucosal damage, often presenting years after initial infection.
  • Gastric adenocarcinoma: Long-term H. pylori infection is a major risk factor for gastric cancer, progressing through a cascade of chronic gastritis, atrophy, intestinal metaplasia, and dysplasia, typically decades after initial infection.
  • Gastric MALT lymphoma: A low-grade B-cell lymphoma of the stomach, strongly associated with chronic H. pylori infection, which can regress with successful eradication therapy.
  • Iron deficiency anaemia: Chronic, low-grade blood loss from gastritis or peptic ulcers can lead to iron deficiency anaemia, particularly in the progressive and late phases of infection.
  • Dyspepsia: Persistent or recurrent dyspeptic symptoms, even without ulceration, can significantly impact quality of life throughout the progressive phase of infection.
