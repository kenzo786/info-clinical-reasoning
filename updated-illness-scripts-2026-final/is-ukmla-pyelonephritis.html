Pyelonephritis [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Pyelonephritis"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Pyelonephritis"][/sc]
  • Pyelonephritis is an infection of the renal pelvis and renal parenchyma, typically caused by ascending bacterial infection from the lower urinary tract.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical features of pyelonephritis, differentiate it from lower urinary tract infection, and understand the principles of initial management and referral pathways.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Pyelonephritis"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Pyelonephritis"][/sc]
  • Acute pyelonephritis affects approximately 1 in 830 people in the UK annually, with females being six times more likely to be affected than males.
  • The incidence of pyelonephritis in women aged 18-49 years is estimated at 28/10,000, with 7% of cases requiring hospital admission.
  • Risk factors include female sex, pregnancy, urinary tract obstruction, vesicoureteral reflux, diabetes mellitus, and immunosuppression.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Pyelonephritis"][/sc]
  • Bacterial colonisation of the periurethral area, often by uropathogenic Escherichia coli, leads to ascending infection of the bladder.
  • Bacteria then ascend the ureters to the renal pelvis and parenchyma, often facilitated by vesicoureteral reflux or urinary stasis, causing inflammation and infection.
  • This inflammation can lead to renal oedema, abscess formation, and potentially impaired renal function or sepsis if untreated. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Renal oedema: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Renal oedema"][/sc]
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Pyelonephritis"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Symptoms typically develop rapidly over hours to a day, including fever, chills, and loin pain.
  • Progressive phase: Without appropriate treatment, symptoms may worsen over 24-48 hours, potentially leading to systemic signs of infection such as rigors, tachycardia, and hypotension.
  • Late phase: If untreated, complications such as renal abscess, perinephric abscess, or urosepsis can develop, particularly beyond 48-72 hours.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Pyelonephritis"][/sc]
  • Ask about loin pain - typically unilateral, constant, and dull, radiating to the groin, indicating renal capsule distension.
  • Ask about fever and rigors - common systemic inflammatory responses, suggesting upper urinary tract involvement.
  • Ask about dysuria, frequency, and urgency - lower urinary tract symptoms that often precede or co-exist with pyelonephritis.
  • Ask about nausea and vomiting - can indicate systemic illness and dehydration, and may complicate oral antibiotic administration.
  • Ask about recent urinary tract infection - a history of recurrent UTIs increases the risk of pyelonephritis.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Pyelonephritis"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - fever (>38°C), tachycardia, and hypotension may indicate systemic inflammatory response or sepsis.
  • Check costovertebral angle tenderness (CVAT) - elicited by percussion over the kidney, a classic sign of pyelonephritis. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Costovertebral angle tenderness: how is it elicited and what does it imply?" text="Costovertebral angle tenderness"][/sc]
  • Check abdominal examination - may reveal tenderness, guarding, or rebound, particularly in the affected flank.
  • Check signs of dehydration - dry mucous membranes, reduced skin turgor, and prolonged capillary refill time.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Pyelonephritis"][/sc]
  • Sepsis: Clinical features such as altered mental status, respiratory rate >22/min, or systolic blood pressure <100 mmHg, requiring urgent assessment and management.
  • Urosepsis: Sepsis specifically originating from a urinary tract infection, often associated with obstructive uropathy or immunocompromise.
  • Rapid deterioration: Worsening symptoms or signs within 24 hours despite initial treatment, suggesting inadequate response or developing complications.
  • Pregnancy: Pyelonephritis in pregnancy carries significant risks to both mother and foetus, necessitating prompt hospital admission and intravenous antibiotics.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Pyelonephritis"][/sc]
  • Older adults: May present with non-specific symptoms such as confusion, falls, or general malaise, without classic fever or loin pain.
  • Diabetic patients: Increased risk of severe infection, including emphysematous pyelonephritis, which involves gas formation in the renal parenchyma and requires urgent imaging and management. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Emphysematous pyelonephritis: what is it and why is it important in diabetes?" text="Emphysematous pyelonephritis"][/sc]
  • Immunocompromised patients: Symptoms may be blunted, and infection can be more severe and rapidly progressive, requiring a high index of suspicion.
  • Children: Younger children may present with fever, irritability, poor feeding, or vomiting, without specific urinary symptoms.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Pyelonephritis"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected pyelonephritis:
    • Urine dipstick: positive for nitrites and leucocyte esterase.
    • Midstream urine (MSU) culture and sensitivity: essential for identifying causative organism and guiding antibiotic choice.
    • Full blood count: leucocytosis with neutrophilia is common.
    • C-reactive protein (CRP) and Erythrocyte Sedimentation Rate (ESR): elevated inflammatory markers.
    • Urea and electrolytes (U&Es): to assess renal function and identify dehydration.
  • Severity/risk tools:
    • CURB-65 Score on MDCalc (for sepsis risk stratification, though not specific to pyelonephritis, it can be used for overall severity assessment in suspected urosepsis).
  • Imaging - Ultrasound: Often used to identify urinary tract obstruction, hydronephrosis, or renal abscesses, particularly in patients not responding to treatment or with recurrent infections. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Hydronephrosis: what is it and why is it relevant in pyelonephritis?" text="Hydronephrosis"][/sc]
  • Imaging - CT KUB: Indicated for suspected complications like renal or perinephric abscess, emphysematous pyelonephritis, or persistent symptoms despite adequate antibiotic therapy.
  • Serial reassessment: Regular monitoring of vital signs, symptoms, and inflammatory markers to assess treatment response and identify deterioration.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Pyelonephritis"][/sc]
  • A 35-year-old female presents with acute onset of right-sided loin pain, fever, rigors, and dysuria for 24 hours, with associated nausea and vomiting.
  • Febrile patient with acute unilateral loin pain and systemic inflammatory response, concerning for acute pyelonephritis.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Pyelonephritis"][/sc]
  • Acute appendicitis - typically presents with right iliac fossa pain, but atypical presentations can mimic right-sided pyelonephritis.
  • Renal colic - severe, colicky flank pain often radiating to the groin, but usually without fever or systemic signs of infection.
  • Lower urinary tract infection (cystitis) - dysuria, frequency, and urgency without systemic symptoms or loin pain.
  • Acute cholecystitis - right upper quadrant pain, fever, and nausea, but typically without urinary symptoms or CVAT.
  • Basal pneumonia - pleuritic chest pain that can radiate to the abdomen or flank, with respiratory symptoms.
  • Perinephric abscess - persistent fever and loin pain despite antibiotics, often with a palpable mass or tenderness. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Perinephric abscess: what is it and how does it differ from pyelonephritis?" text="Perinephric abscess"][/sc]
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Pyelonephritis"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Recognition of severe infection or sepsis, escalation to senior medical staff, supportive care including adequate hydration and analgesia. Defer to local antimicrobial stewardship pathways for initial antibiotic choice.
  • Definitive management: Antibiotic therapy guided by local resistance patterns and urine culture results. Oral antibiotics are often suitable for mild-moderate cases, while intravenous antibiotics are typically warranted for severe infection, inability to tolerate oral intake, or pregnancy.
  • Adjunctive management: Adequate analgesia (e.g., paracetamol, NSAIDs if renal function permits), antiemetics for nausea/vomiting, and ensuring adequate fluid intake.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Clinical reassessment within 48-72 hours to ensure improvement. Follow-up urine culture may be considered in specific cases (e.g., pregnant women, recurrent infections).
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Advise patients to seek urgent medical attention if symptoms worsen, do not improve within 48 hours, or if new red flag features develop.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Pyelonephritis"][/sc]
  • Sepsis/Urosepsis: Systemic inflammatory response to infection, potentially leading to organ dysfunction and shock, particularly if treatment is delayed beyond 48 hours.
  • Renal abscess: Localised collection of pus within the kidney, which may develop if infection is severe or inadequately treated, often presenting with persistent fever and flank pain.
  • Perinephric abscess: Collection of pus in the space surrounding the kidney, a serious complication that can arise from untreated or complicated pyelonephritis.
  • Emphysematous pyelonephritis: A severe, necrotising infection of the kidney characterised by gas formation, almost exclusively seen in diabetic or immunocompromised patients, and can develop rapidly within 24-48 hours.
  • Acute kidney injury (AKI): Can result from severe infection, dehydration, or obstructive uropathy, and may develop within days of onset.
