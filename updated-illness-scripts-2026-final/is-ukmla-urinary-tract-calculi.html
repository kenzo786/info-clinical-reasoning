Urinary tract calculi [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Urinary tract calculi"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Urinary tract calculi"][/sc]
  • Urinary tract calculi are solid concretions formed within the urinary system, often composed of minerals and salts, which can cause obstruction and pain.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical presentation, risk factors, and initial management of urinary tract calculi.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Urinary tract calculi"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Urinary tract calculi"][/sc]
  • The annual prevalence rate of kidney stones in England increased from 11 cases per 10,000 in 2003/04 to 16 per 10,000 in 2013/14.
  • There are regional variations in incidence across the UK, with Wales and southern England generally having higher rates.
  • Risk factors include dehydration, dietary factors (e.g., high sodium, animal protein), obesity, certain medical conditions (e.g., hyperparathyroidism, inflammatory bowel disease), and a family history of stones.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Urinary tract calculi"][/sc]
  • Supersaturation of urine with stone-forming minerals (e.g., calcium, oxalate, uric acid) leads to crystal nucleation and aggregation.
  • These crystals adhere to the urothelium, forming a nidus for further stone growth within the kidney or urinary tract.
  • Stone migration or obstruction causes distension of the collecting system, leading to renal colic, inflammation, and potential renal damage or infection.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Urinary tract calculi"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Acute onset of severe, colicky flank pain radiating to the groin, often accompanied by nausea and vomiting, typically lasting hours to a few days.
  • Progressive phase: Pain may fluctuate in intensity as the stone moves, potentially leading to hydronephrosis if obstruction persists, particularly beyond a few days.
  • Late phase: Resolution of acute symptoms if the stone passes spontaneously or is removed; however, recurrent stone formation is common, particularly beyond 5 years without preventative measures.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Urinary tract calculi"][/sc]
  • Ask about severe, colicky flank pain - this is characteristic of renal colic and suggests obstruction.
  • Ask about pain radiating to the groin or genitalia - indicates stone migration down the ureter.
  • Ask about nausea and vomiting - common systemic response to severe pain.
  • Ask about haematuria (visible or non-visible) - suggests mucosal trauma from the stone.
  • Ask about dysuria, frequency, or urgency - may indicate a stone in the lower ureter or bladder, or associated infection.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Urinary tract calculi"][/sc]
  • Check for tenderness in the costovertebral angle - suggests renal capsule distension.
  • Check for patient restlessness and inability to find a comfortable position - typical of severe colicky pain.
  • Check vital signs - tachycardia and hypertension due to pain; fever and hypotension may indicate urosepsis.
  • Check for abdominal distension or guarding - may suggest complications like ileus or peritonitis.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Urinary tract calculi"][/sc]
  • Fever with loin pain: Suggests infected obstructed kidney (urosepsis), a urological emergency requiring urgent decompression.
  • Anuria or acute kidney injury: Indicates bilateral obstruction or obstruction of a solitary kidney, requiring urgent intervention.
  • Severe, unremitting pain: Despite analgesia, may indicate complete obstruction or other complications.
  • Haemodynamic instability: Suggests severe sepsis or other life-threatening complications.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Urinary tract calculi"][/sc]
  • Older adults: May present with less severe or atypical pain, or primarily with symptoms of infection or acute kidney injury, delaying diagnosis.
  • Pregnancy: Symptoms can mimic normal pregnancy discomforts; diagnostic imaging is limited due to radiation concerns, potentially delaying diagnosis and management.
  • Children: Often present with non-specific abdominal pain, nausea, or vomiting; haematuria may be the only clue.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Urinary tract calculi"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected Urinary tract calculi: Urine dipstick (for haematuria, leucocytes, nitrites), urine microscopy and culture (to rule out infection), serum creatinine (assess renal function), full blood count (assess for infection/inflammation).
  • Imaging - Non-contrast CT KUB: The gold standard for diagnosing urinary tract calculi, identifying stone size, location, and presence of hydronephrosis.
  • Imaging - Renal ultrasound: Useful in pregnancy or when radiation exposure is a concern; can identify hydronephrosis and larger renal stones, but less sensitive for ureteric stones.
  • Serial reassessment: Regular monitoring of renal function and pain control, especially for conservatively managed stones.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Urinary tract calculi"][/sc]
  • A 45-year-old male presents with sudden onset, severe right flank pain radiating to his groin, associated with nausea and visible haematuria, developing over the last 4 hours.
  • Acute, colicky, unilateral flank pain with radiation, haematuria, and systemic symptoms in a middle-aged male, highly suggestive of urinary tract obstruction.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Urinary tract calculi"][/sc]
  • Pyelonephritis - typically associated with fever, dysuria, and systemic signs of infection, often without colicky pain.
  • Appendicitis - pain typically starts periumbilically and migrates to the right iliac fossa, often with guarding and rebound tenderness.
  • Aortic aneurysm rupture - sudden, severe abdominal or back pain, often with haemodynamic instability; consider in older patients with risk factors.
  • Biliary colic - right upper quadrant pain, often post-prandial, radiating to the back or shoulder, without haematuria.
  • Musculoskeletal back pain - usually exacerbated by movement, less severe and not colicky, without associated urinary symptoms.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Urinary tract calculi"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Effective analgesia (e.g., NSAIDs, opioids), antiemetics, and assessment for signs of infection or obstruction requiring urgent intervention.
  • Definitive management: May include medical expulsive therapy (e.g., alpha-blockers for distal ureteric stones), extracorporeal shockwave lithotripsy (ESWL), ureteroscopy, or percutaneous nephrolithotomy (PCNL) depending on stone size, location, and patient factors.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Regular assessment of stone passage, renal function, and pain control; metabolic work-up for recurrent stone formers.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Advise patients to return if pain worsens, develops fever, or experiences anuria; urgent urological review for infected obstructed systems or acute kidney injury.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Urinary tract calculi"][/sc]
  • Obstructed uropathy: Stone blockage can lead to hydronephrosis, causing renal damage and potentially acute kidney injury if prolonged or bilateral.
  • Urosepsis: An infected obstructed kidney is a medical emergency, potentially leading to septic shock and multi-organ failure.
  • Chronic kidney disease: Recurrent stone formation and associated interventions can contribute to progressive renal impairment over time.
  • Stone recurrence: High risk of forming new stones, particularly without dietary or pharmacological preventative measures, impacting long-term renal health.
