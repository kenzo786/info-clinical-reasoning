Neutropenia (including neutropenic sepsis) [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Neutropenia (including neutropenic sepsis)"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Neutropenia (including neutropenic sepsis)"][/sc]
  • Neutropenia is an abnormally low count of neutrophils, a type of white blood cell crucial for fighting bacterial and fungal infections. Neutropenic sepsis is a life-threatening complication characterised by fever and other signs of sepsis in a neutropenic patient.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: The candidate must recognise the clinical features of neutropenia and neutropenic sepsis, understand the urgency of management, and initiate appropriate investigations and empirical broad-spectrum antibiotics.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Neutropenia (including neutropenic sepsis)"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Neutropenia (including neutropenic sepsis)"][/sc]
  • Neutropenia is a common side effect of chemotherapy, affecting up to 50% of patients receiving myelosuppressive regimens, with neutropenic sepsis occurring in 10-50% of these cases, according to NICE guidelines.
  • Risk factors include intensive chemotherapy, advanced malignancy, older age, poor performance status, pre-existing infections, and renal or hepatic dysfunction.
  • The incidence of severe neutropenia in the general population is rare, but can be associated with autoimmune conditions, viral infections (e.g., HIV), and certain medications (e.g., antithyroid drugs, some antibiotics).
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Neutropenia (including neutropenic sepsis)"][/sc]
  • Trigger: Bone marrow suppression (e.g., chemotherapy, radiation, aplastic anaemia) or peripheral destruction/sequestration of neutrophils leads to a critical reduction in circulating neutrophils, impairing innate immunity.
  • Progression: Compromised immune surveillance allows commensal bacteria or opportunistic pathogens to proliferate, often from mucosal surfaces (e.g., gut, oral cavity), leading to local infection and systemic inflammatory response.
  • Consequence: Uncontrolled infection and systemic inflammation can rapidly progress to sepsis, septic shock, and multi-organ dysfunction, with high mortality if not promptly recognised and treated. Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Neutropenia (including neutropenic sepsis)"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Neutropenia typically develops within days to a week following myelosuppressive therapy. Fever and initial signs of infection can emerge rapidly, often within hours of neutropenia onset.
  • Progressive phase: If untreated, local infections can quickly disseminate, leading to systemic inflammatory response syndrome (SIRS) and overt sepsis within 24-48 hours, particularly beyond 2 days.
  • Late phase: Without aggressive management, neutropenic sepsis can progress to septic shock, multi-organ failure, and death, particularly beyond 3-5 days, even with antibiotic therapy if source control is not achieved.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Neutropenia (including neutropenic sepsis)"][/sc]
  • Ask about fever - often the sole initial symptom, indicating a potential life-threatening infection in a neutropenic patient.
  • Ask about chills or rigors - suggesting systemic inflammatory response and bacteraemia.
  • Ask about localised pain or discomfort (e.g., sore throat, abdominal pain, dysuria) - to identify potential sources of infection, which may be subtle due to lack of inflammatory cells.
  • Ask about general malaise, fatigue, or weakness - non-specific symptoms that can indicate systemic illness.
  • Ask about new cough or shortness of breath - suggesting respiratory tract infection.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Neutropenia (including neutropenic sepsis)"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - particularly temperature (often >38°C), tachycardia, tachypnoea, and hypotension (indicating sepsis or septic shock).
  • Check for mucositis or oral ulceration - common sites for bacterial entry in chemotherapy-induced neutropenia.
  • Check skin for rashes, cellulitis, or abscesses - potential sites of infection, though inflammation may be blunted.
  • Check lung fields for crackles or reduced breath sounds - suggestive of pneumonia, even without typical inflammatory signs.
  • Check for abdominal tenderness or guarding - indicating intra-abdominal infection, which can be subtle. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Neutropenic enterocolitis: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Neutropenic enterocolitis"][/sc]
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Neutropenia (including neutropenic sepsis)"][/sc]
  • Fever in a neutropenic patient - immediate medical emergency requiring urgent assessment and empirical broad-spectrum antibiotics.
  • Hypotension or signs of shock - indicates severe sepsis, requiring fluid resuscitation and vasopressors.
  • New onset confusion or altered mental status - suggestive of severe sepsis, CNS infection, or metabolic derangement.
  • Rapidly worsening clinical picture - progression of symptoms or signs over hours, indicating aggressive infection.
  • Severe localised pain - despite minimal objective signs, can indicate deep-seated infection or abscess.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Neutropenia (including neutropenic sepsis)"][/sc]
  • Older adults: May present with less pronounced fever or non-specific symptoms like confusion or functional decline, making diagnosis challenging.
  • Immunosuppressed (non-chemotherapy): Patients with autoimmune neutropenia or chronic granulomatous disease may have recurrent infections but less severe systemic responses if neutropenia is chronic and compensated.
  • Profound neutropenia (<0.1 x 10^9/L): May have minimal inflammatory signs (e.g., no pus formation, less erythema) despite severe infection, due to the absence of neutrophils. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Agranulocytosis: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Agranulocytosis"][/sc]
  • Fungal infections: Often present later in the course of prolonged neutropenia, with persistent fever despite broad-spectrum antibiotics, and may have subtle radiological signs.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Neutropenia (including neutropenic sepsis)"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected neutropenic sepsis: Full blood count (to confirm neutropenia, absolute neutrophil count <1.0 x 10^9/L), blood cultures (at least two sets, peripheral and from central lines if present), C-reactive protein (CRP), urea and electrolytes, liver function tests.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Consider lactate (for sepsis severity), coagulation screen (for DIC), urinalysis and urine culture, viral swabs (e.g., HSV, VZV if mucositis), fungal markers (e.g., galactomannan, beta-D-glucan) if prolonged neutropenia.
  • Imaging - Chest X-ray: For respiratory symptoms, to identify pneumonia. Consider CT chest/abdomen/pelvis if no source identified and persistent fever, or specific localised symptoms.
  • Serial reassessment: Repeat blood cultures and inflammatory markers to monitor response to treatment. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Procalcitonin in sepsis: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Procalcitonin"][/sc]
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Neutropenia (including neutropenic sepsis)"][/sc]
  • A 68-year-old male, two weeks post-chemotherapy for lymphoma, presents to the emergency department with a 6-hour history of rigors, subjective fever, and generalised malaise, with no clear focus of infection on initial examination.
  • An elderly, immunocompromised patient with acute febrile illness and no localising signs, at high risk of rapid deterioration due to profound neutropenia.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Neutropenia (including neutropenic sepsis)"][/sc]
  • Bacterial sepsis (non-neutropenic): Similar presentation but with normal neutrophil count, often a clear source of infection.
  • Viral infection: May cause fever and malaise, but typically less rapid deterioration and often associated with lymphocytosis or atypical lymphocytes.
  • Fungal infection: Persistent fever despite broad-spectrum antibiotics in prolonged neutropenia, often with subtle signs.
  • Drug-induced fever: Fever without infection, often resolves on stopping the offending agent, but diagnosis of exclusion in neutropenia.
  • Disease progression/relapse: Underlying malignancy can cause fever, but usually without the acute septic picture.
  • Transfusion reaction: Acute febrile reaction during or shortly after blood product transfusion. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Transfusion reactions: what are they and why do they matter clinically?" text="Transfusion reactions"][/sc]
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Neutropenia (including neutropenic sepsis)"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Urgent assessment, blood cultures, intravenous fluid resuscitation for hypotension, and prompt administration of empirical broad-spectrum intravenous antibiotics within one hour of presentation.
  • Definitive management: Antibiotic regimen should cover common Gram-positive and Gram-negative pathogens, including pseudomonas (e.g., piperacillin/tazobactam). Adjust antibiotics based on culture results and clinical response. Consider adding antifungals if fever persists beyond 4-7 days despite appropriate antibacterial therapy.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Close monitoring of vital signs, fluid balance, and inflammatory markers. Daily review of microbiology results and clinical progress.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Early involvement of critical care for patients with septic shock or multi-organ failure. Advise patients to seek immediate medical attention for any new fever or signs of infection post-discharge.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Neutropenia (including neutropenic sepsis)"][/sc]
  • Septic shock: Uncontrolled systemic inflammatory response leading to persistent hypotension requiring vasopressors, due to overwhelming infection.
  • Multi-organ failure: Progressive dysfunction of multiple organ systems (e.g., acute kidney injury, acute respiratory distress syndrome, disseminated intravascular coagulation) as a consequence of severe sepsis.
  • Invasive fungal infections: Prolonged neutropenia increases risk of candidiasis or aspergillosis, which are difficult to treat and carry high mortality.
  • Clostridioides difficile infection: Often a complication of broad-spectrum antibiotic use, leading to severe colitis and potential toxic megacolon.
  • Death: Despite aggressive management, neutropenic sepsis carries a significant mortality risk, particularly if treatment is delayed or the underlying condition is severe.
