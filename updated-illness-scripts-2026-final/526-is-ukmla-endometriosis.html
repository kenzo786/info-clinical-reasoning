Endometriosis [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Endometriosis"]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Endometriosis"]
  • Endometriosis is a chronic inflammatory condition where endometrial-like tissue grows outside the uterus, most commonly in the pelvic cavity, leading to pain and infertility.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical presentation of endometriosis, initiate appropriate investigations, and understand the principles of medical and surgical management.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Endometriosis"]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Endometriosis"]
  • Endometriosis affects approximately 1 in 10 women of reproductive age in the UK, representing around 1.5 million individuals.
  • It is the second most common gynaecological condition in the UK, often leading to significant delays in diagnosis (average 7.5 years).
  • Risk factors include early menarche, short menstrual cycles, heavy menstrual bleeding, and a family history of endometriosis.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Endometriosis"]
  • Endometrial-like tissue (stroma and glands) is found outside the uterine cavity, most commonly on the ovaries, pelvic peritoneum, and uterosacral ligaments.
  • This ectopic tissue responds to cyclical hormonal changes, leading to local inflammation, bleeding, and fibrosis, which causes pain and adhesion formation. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Retrograde menstruation: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Retrograde menstruation"] is a leading theory for its development.
  • The chronic inflammatory process and adhesion formation can distort pelvic anatomy, impair organ function, and contribute to subfertility and chronic pelvic pain.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Endometriosis"]
  • Early phase: Symptoms often begin around menarche, with dysmenorrhoea that progressively worsens over months to years, often initially dismissed as normal period pain.
  • Progressive phase: Pain becomes more severe and chronic, extending beyond menstruation to include deep dyspareunia, dyschezia, and non-cyclical pelvic pain, particularly beyond 6 months.
  • Late phase: Established disease can lead to significant organ involvement (e.g., bowel, bladder), severe adhesions, chronic debilitating pain, and infertility, persisting for many years if untreated.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Endometriosis"]
  • Ask about dysmenorrhoea - often severe, debilitating, and unresponsive to simple analgesia, impacting daily activities and distinguishing it from primary dysmenorrhoea.
  • Ask about deep dyspareunia - pain during or after sexual intercourse, which can be a significant indicator of deep infiltrating endometriosis.
  • Ask about dyschezia or cyclical bowel symptoms - painful bowel movements, particularly during menstruation, suggesting bowel involvement.
  • Ask about chronic pelvic pain - persistent pelvic pain lasting more than 6 months, which may or may not be cyclical, indicating widespread disease or adhesions.
  • Ask about subfertility - difficulty conceiving, as endometriosis can impair fertility through various mechanisms including anatomical distortion and inflammation.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Endometriosis"]
  • Check vital signs - usually normal, but may reveal tachycardia or hypotension in cases of acute complications like endometrioma rupture.
  • Check abdominal examination - may reveal tenderness, guarding, or palpable masses, particularly if there are large endometriomas or significant adhesions.
  • Check pelvic examination - often reveals reduced organ mobility, tender nodularity in the posterior vaginal fornix, fixed retroverted uterus, or visible endometriotic lesions on the vagina or cervix. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Chocolate cysts: what are they and why do they matter clinically?" text="Chocolate cysts"] (endometriomas) may be palpable as adnexal masses.
  • Check for signs of rectovaginal or bladder involvement - tenderness or nodularity on rectal examination, or haematuria/dysuria if bladder endometriosis is present.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Endometriosis"]
  • Acute severe pelvic pain: Suggests complications such as ovarian endometrioma rupture, ovarian torsion, or acute bowel obstruction, requiring urgent surgical assessment.
  • Progressive neurological symptoms: Indicates potential sciatic nerve or other nerve root involvement, which can lead to motor or sensory deficits if not addressed promptly.
  • Signs of ureteric obstruction: Flank pain, hydronephrosis on imaging, or renal impairment, suggesting ureteric endometriosis and risk of kidney damage if prolonged.
  • Significant rectal bleeding or change in bowel habit: Especially if progressive or associated with weight loss, raising concern for extensive bowel involvement or malignancy, requiring urgent gastroenterological review.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Endometriosis"]
  • Adolescents: Severe, progressive dysmenorrhoea from menarche, often initially misdiagnosed as primary dysmenorrhoea, leading to significant diagnostic delay.
  • Post-menopausal women: Endometriosis can reactivate or present de novo, particularly in those on hormone replacement therapy (HRT), or rarely due to malignant transformation.
  • Extrapelvic endometriosis: Presentation with cyclical symptoms in unusual sites such as the diaphragm (cyclical shoulder pain), lungs (cyclical haemoptysis/pneumothorax), or umbilicus (cyclical bleeding). [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Peritoneal implants: what are they and why do they matter clinically?" text="Peritoneal implants"] can occur in various locations.
  • Asymptomatic: Endometriosis may be an incidental finding during imaging or surgery for other conditions, highlighting the variable nature of the disease.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Endometriosis"]
  • Core for suspected endometriosis: Transvaginal ultrasound is the first-line imaging investigation to identify endometriomas, deep infiltrating endometriosis, and exclude other pelvic pathology.
  • Biomarkers: CA-125 levels may be elevated in endometriosis, particularly with endometriomas, but it is not diagnostic and lacks sufficient sensitivity and specificity for screening.
  • Gold Standard: Laparoscopy with histological confirmation of endometriotic lesions remains the definitive diagnostic method, allowing for direct visualisation and biopsy.
  • Severity/risk tools: While no single severity score is routinely used for diagnosis, the American Society for Reproductive Medicine (ASRM) classification system is used surgically to stage disease severity.
  • Imaging - MRI: Pelvic MRI can be useful for mapping deep infiltrating endometriosis, particularly prior to complex surgical planning, offering superior soft tissue contrast compared to ultrasound.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Endometriosis"]
  • A 32-year-old woman presents with a 5-year history of progressively worsening, severe dysmenorrhoea, deep dyspareunia, and cyclical dyschezia, alongside increasing difficulty conceiving.
  • A young woman with chronic, cyclical pelvic pain and subfertility, concerning for endometriosis.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Endometriosis"]
  • Pelvic Inflammatory Disease (PID) - often associated with acute onset, fever, vaginal discharge, and positive STI screening, though chronic PID can mimic endometriosis.
  • Ectopic Pregnancy - acute onset of unilateral pelvic pain, amenorrhoea, and positive pregnancy test, requiring urgent exclusion.
  • Irritable Bowel Syndrome (IBS) - shares cyclical bowel symptoms (dyschezia, bloating) but typically lacks severe dysmenorrhoea or deep dyspareunia.
  • Adenomyosis - presents with heavy menstrual bleeding and severe dysmenorrhoea, but pain is typically confined to the uterus and does not involve extrapelvic sites. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Adenomyosis: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Adenomyosis"] is often co-morbid.
  • Ovarian Cysts (functional or pathological) - can cause acute or chronic pelvic pain, but symptoms are often unilateral and not necessarily cyclical, and ultrasound typically differentiates.
  • Fibroids - typically cause heavy menstrual bleeding and pressure symptoms, but pain is less common unless degenerating or very large.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Endometriosis"]
  • Immediate priorities: Initial management often includes adequate analgesia (paracetamol, NSAIDs) and hormonal suppression to alleviate symptoms while awaiting definitive diagnosis or specialist review.
  • Definitive management: Hormonal treatments (e.g., combined oral contraceptives, progestogens, GnRH analogues) are typically used to suppress ovarian function and reduce endometriotic lesion activity.
  • Surgical management: Laparoscopic excision or ablation of endometriotic lesions and adhesiolysis may be considered for symptom relief, fertility improvement, or diagnosis, particularly for ovarian endometriomas.
  • Adjunctive management: Referral to pain management services, physiotherapy, and psychological support may be beneficial for chronic pain and associated psychological impact.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Regular review of symptoms, treatment efficacy, and disease progression is essential, often involving a multidisciplinary team approach.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Patients with severe or complex endometriosis, or those with organ involvement, should be referred to a specialist endometriosis centre for comprehensive management.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Endometriosis"]
  • Subfertility/Infertility: Endometriosis can significantly impair fertility through various mechanisms, including anatomical distortion, inflammation, and impaired ovarian function, persisting over many years.
  • Chronic Pelvic Pain: Persistent and often debilitating pelvic pain, even after treatment, due to nerve sensitisation, adhesions, and ongoing inflammation, impacting quality of life for prolonged periods.
  • Ovarian Endometriomas: Formation of "chocolate cysts" on the ovaries, which can rupture, undergo torsion, or contribute to chronic pain and reduced ovarian reserve, with potential for acute presentations.
  • Adhesion Formation: Extensive adhesions can bind pelvic organs together, leading to chronic pain, bowel obstruction, or ureteric obstruction, particularly in advanced disease.
  • Malignant Transformation: Although rare, long-standing endometriosis, particularly ovarian endometriomas, has a slightly increased risk of malignant transformation to clear cell or endometrioid ovarian cancer, typically occurring in later life.
