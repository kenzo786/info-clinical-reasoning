Hypertensive retinopathy [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Hypertensive retinopathy"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Hypertensive retinopathy"][/sc]
  • Hypertensive retinopathy is a spectrum of retinal vascular changes and damage caused by systemic hypertension.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the ocular manifestations of chronic and acute severe hypertension, including their implications for systemic management.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Hypertensive retinopathy"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Hypertensive retinopathy"][/sc]
  • The prevalence of hypertensive retinopathy increases with age and duration of hypertension, affecting a significant proportion of adults with chronic hypertension in the UK.
  • Risk factors include poorly controlled hypertension, diabetes mellitus, hyperlipidaemia, smoking, and renal disease, which collectively exacerbate vascular damage.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Hypertensive retinopathy"][/sc]
  • Trigger: Sustained elevation in systemic blood pressure leads to autoregulatory vasoconstriction of retinal arterioles, reducing blood flow.
  • Progression: Chronic hypertension causes structural changes such as arteriolar wall thickening (arteriosclerosis) and endothelial dysfunction, leading to leakage and ischaemia.
  • Consequence: Severe or acute hypertension can result in breakdown of the blood-retinal barrier, causing haemorrhages, exudates, and optic disc oedema, potentially leading to vision loss.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Hypertensive retinopathy"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Mild arteriolar narrowing and tortuosity may develop insidiously over months to years in chronic, often asymptomatic, hypertension.
  • Progressive phase: Over years, sustained hypertension can lead to more pronounced vascular changes, such as arteriovenous nipping and copper/silver wiring, particularly beyond 5-10 years of uncontrolled disease.
  • Late phase: Acute, severe hypertension can precipitate rapid onset of retinal haemorrhages, cotton wool spots, and optic disc oedema, developing over days to weeks.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Hypertensive retinopathy"][/sc]
  • Ask about blurred vision - this may indicate macular oedema or ischaemia, affecting central acuity.
  • Ask about headaches - often associated with severe hypertension, which can also manifest as acute retinopathy.
  • Ask about visual field defects - suggests retinal ischaemia or nerve fibre layer damage.
  • Ask about floaters or flashes of light - can be due to vitreous haemorrhage or retinal detachment, which may be secondary to severe retinopathy.
  • Ask about diplopia - though less common, can indicate cranial nerve palsies secondary to microvascular ischaemia from hypertension.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Hypertensive retinopathy"][/sc]
  • Check blood pressure - essential for assessing the severity of systemic hypertension and its correlation with retinal changes.
  • Check visual acuity - a reduction may indicate macular involvement or optic nerve damage.
  • Check fundoscopy for arteriolar narrowing - a key early sign of hypertensive vascular changes.
  • Check fundoscopy for cotton wool spots - indicative of nerve fibre layer ischaemia, often seen in severe hypertension.
  • Check fundoscopy for retinal haemorrhages - suggests vascular leakage and damage, particularly flame-shaped haemorrhages.
  • Check fundoscopy for optic disc oedema - a critical sign of malignant hypertension requiring urgent management.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Hypertensive retinopathy"][/sc]
  • Optic disc oedema - specific to malignant hypertension, indicating severe, acute end-organ damage and requiring immediate blood pressure reduction.
  • Macular star formation - a pattern of hard exudates radiating from the fovea, suggesting chronic severe hypertension with significant vascular leakage.
  • Sudden, severe vision loss - may indicate retinal artery or vein occlusion, or vitreous haemorrhage, often exacerbated by underlying hypertension.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Hypertensive retinopathy"][/sc]
  • Younger adults: May present with more acute and severe retinopathy, including optic disc oedema, due to a less developed vascular autoregulatory capacity compared to older individuals.
  • Pregnancy (Pre-eclampsia/Eclampsia): Retinopathy can develop rapidly, often with severe vasospasm and exudative changes, reflecting acute systemic vascular dysfunction.
  • Diabetic patients: Co-existing diabetic retinopathy can mask or exacerbate hypertensive changes, making differentiation challenging and often leading to more severe combined pathology.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Hypertensive retinopathy"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected Hypertensive retinopathy: Fundoscopy is the primary diagnostic tool, allowing direct visualisation of retinal vascular changes, haemorrhages, exudates, and optic disc oedema.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Optical Coherence Tomography (OCT) can assess macular oedema and retinal nerve fibre layer thickness, aiding in differentiating from other retinopathies.
  • Imaging - Fluorescein Angiography: May be used in complex cases to evaluate retinal perfusion, identify areas of ischaemia, and assess vascular leakage.
  • Serial reassessment: Regular blood pressure monitoring and repeat fundoscopy are crucial to assess disease progression and response to systemic hypertension management.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Hypertensive retinopathy"][/sc]
  • A 65-year-old male with a 15-year history of poorly controlled hypertension presents with gradually worsening blurred vision over the past 6 months and recent onset of headaches, with fundoscopy revealing arteriovenous nipping, cotton wool spots, and flame-shaped haemorrhages.
  • A chronic, progressive, and potentially acute hypertensive vasculopathy affecting the retina, leading to ischaemia and exudation, with systemic implications.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Hypertensive retinopathy"][/sc]
  • Diabetic retinopathy - distinguished by microaneurysms, dot-blot haemorrhages, and neovascularisation, often with a history of diabetes.
  • Retinal vein occlusion - typically presents with sudden, painless vision loss and extensive retinal haemorrhages in a specific vascular distribution.
  • Retinal artery occlusion - characterised by sudden, profound vision loss and a pale, oedematous retina with a cherry-red spot.
  • Age-related macular degeneration - involves drusen, pigmentary changes, and neovascularisation primarily affecting the macula, more common in older individuals.
  • Anaemia - can cause retinal haemorrhages and cotton wool spots, but typically without the arteriolar changes characteristic of hypertension.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Hypertensive retinopathy"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: For severe hypertensive retinopathy (e.g., with optic disc oedema), urgent and controlled reduction of blood pressure is paramount to prevent further end-organ damage.
  • Definitive management: Long-term management involves optimising systemic blood pressure control through lifestyle modifications and appropriate antihypertensive medications.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Regular ophthalmological review and systemic blood pressure monitoring are essential to assess response to treatment and detect progression.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Patients with severe or worsening retinopathy require prompt referral to ophthalmology and internal medicine for co-management and investigation of secondary causes of hypertension.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Hypertensive retinopathy"][/sc]
  • Vision loss: Can result from macular oedema, retinal ischaemia, vitreous haemorrhage, or optic neuropathy, significantly impacting quality of life.
  • Retinal artery or vein occlusion: Increased risk due to underlying vascular damage and endothelial dysfunction, leading to acute and severe visual impairment.
  • Optic atrophy: Chronic or severe optic disc oedema can lead to irreversible damage to the optic nerve, resulting in permanent visual field defects or blindness.
  • Exudative retinal detachment: Severe vascular leakage can accumulate subretinally, causing serous detachment and significant visual compromise.
