Parkinsonism (including Parkinson disease) [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Parkinsonism (including Parkinson disease)"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Parkinsonism (including Parkinson disease)"][/sc]
  • Parkinsonism is a clinical syndrome characterised by bradykinesia, rigidity, tremor, and postural instability, often caused by neurodegenerative conditions like Parkinson disease.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: recognise the key clinical features, differential diagnoses, and initial management strategies for Parkinsonism, including Parkinson disease.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Parkinsonism (including Parkinson disease)"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Parkinsonism (including Parkinson disease)"][/sc]
  • Parkinson disease affects approximately 1 in 500 people in the UK, with prevalence increasing significantly with age, particularly over 60 years.
  • Risk factors include advanced age, male sex, certain genetic mutations, and exposure to environmental toxins, though most cases are sporadic.
  • Secondary Parkinsonism, often drug-induced, is also a significant cause, particularly in older adults receiving antipsychotics or antiemetics.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Parkinsonism (including Parkinson disease)"][/sc]
  • Trigger: Degeneration of dopaminergic neurons in the substantia nigra pars compacta, leading to reduced dopamine production in the basal ganglia.
  • Progression: Accumulation of alpha-synuclein protein into Lewy bodies within neurons, spreading through the brain and affecting motor and non-motor functions.
  • Consequence: Imbalance between excitatory cholinergic and inhibitory dopaminergic pathways, resulting in characteristic motor symptoms of bradykinesia, rigidity, and tremor.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Parkinsonism (including Parkinson disease)"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Subtle, often unilateral motor symptoms (e.g., tremor, stiffness) and non-motor symptoms (e.g., anosmia, constipation, sleep disturbance) that may precede motor onset by years.
  • Progressive phase: Gradual worsening and spread of motor symptoms, development of postural instability, and increasing impact of non-motor symptoms, particularly beyond a few years of diagnosis.
  • Late phase: Significant functional impairment, severe gait disturbance, falls, cognitive decline, and autonomic dysfunction, particularly beyond a decade of disease onset.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Parkinsonism (including Parkinson disease)"][/sc]
  • Ask about tremor - often a resting tremor, typically unilateral at onset, which can be a key distinguishing feature.
  • Ask about bradykinesia - slowness of movement, difficulty initiating and continuing movements, which impacts daily activities like dressing or eating.
  • Ask about rigidity - stiffness or inflexibility of limbs or trunk, often described as 'cogwheel' or 'lead-pipe' rigidity, contributing to discomfort.
  • Ask about postural instability - impaired balance and coordination, leading to falls, especially in later stages, which is a significant cause of morbidity.
  • Ask about non-motor symptoms - including anosmia, constipation, sleep disturbances (e.g., REM sleep behaviour disorder), and mood changes, which can precede motor symptoms and significantly impact quality of life.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Parkinsonism (including Parkinson disease)"][/sc]
  • Check for resting tremor - typically 4-6 Hz, often 'pill-rolling' in the hands, which diminishes with voluntary movement.
  • Check for rigidity - assess passive movement of limbs for increased tone, noting cogwheel (ratchety) or lead-pipe (sustained) resistance.
  • Check for bradykinesia - observe for decreased arm swing during gait, reduced facial expression (hypomimia), and difficulty with rapid alternating movements (e.g., finger tapping).
  • Check for postural instability - perform the pull test, observing for impaired balance and tendency to fall backwards, indicating significant axial involvement.
  • Check vital signs - particularly blood pressure for orthostatic hypotension, which is common due to autonomic dysfunction and can contribute to falls.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Parkinsonism (including Parkinson disease)"][/sc]
  • Early severe gait impairment - suggests atypical Parkinsonism rather than idiopathic Parkinson disease, often within the first 3 years.
  • Symmetrical onset - Parkinson disease typically starts unilaterally; symmetrical presentation points towards secondary or atypical Parkinsonism.
  • Poor response to levodopa - a lack of significant improvement with adequate levodopa dosage raises suspicion for atypical Parkinsonism.
  • Early cognitive impairment or dementia - while common in late Parkinson disease, early onset (within 1 year of motor symptoms) suggests other neurodegenerative conditions.
  • Recurrent falls from onset - indicates severe postural instability early in the disease course, atypical for idiopathic Parkinson disease.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Parkinsonism (including Parkinson disease)"][/sc]
  • Drug-induced Parkinsonism: Often symmetrical, rapid onset, and may resolve upon withdrawal of the offending medication (e.g., antipsychotics, antiemetics).
  • Vascular Parkinsonism: Characterised by lower body Parkinsonism, gait disturbance, and often associated with a history of cerebrovascular disease, with imaging showing vascular lesions.
  • Progressive Supranuclear Palsy (PSP): Presents with early and prominent postural instability, vertical gaze palsy, and axial rigidity, often with a poor response to levodopa.
  • Multiple System Atrophy (MSA): Features prominent autonomic dysfunction (e.g., orthostatic hypotension, urinary incontinence) alongside Parkinsonian or cerebellar signs.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Parkinsonism (including Parkinson disease)"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected Parkinsonism: Clinical diagnosis based on history and neurological examination, focusing on bradykinesia plus at least one of tremor or rigidity.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Consider a trial of levodopa to assess response; significant improvement supports Parkinson disease.
  • Imaging - MRI brain: Primarily to exclude other causes of Parkinsonism such as vascular lesions, hydrocephalus, or structural abnormalities; not diagnostic for Parkinson disease itself.
  • Imaging - DaTscan (Dopamine Transporter Scan): May be used to differentiate essential tremor from Parkinsonian syndromes by assessing presynaptic dopaminergic integrity, but not typically required for a clear clinical diagnosis of Parkinson disease.
  • Serial reassessment: Regular clinical review is crucial to monitor disease progression and adjust management, as Parkinsonism is a progressive condition.
