Post traumatic stress disorder [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Post traumatic stress disorder"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Post traumatic stress disorder"][/sc]
  • A mental health condition that can develop after experiencing or witnessing a traumatic event, characterised by intrusive memories, avoidance, negative changes in thinking and mood, and alterations in arousal and reactivity.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical features, diagnostic criteria, and initial management principles of post-traumatic stress disorder.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Post traumatic stress disorder"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Post traumatic stress disorder"][/sc]
  • Approximately 1 in 10 people in the UK are expected to experience PTSD at some point in their lives, with around 4 in 100 people affected at any given time (PTSD UK).
  • Recent data suggests a rising prevalence, with 5.7% of adults in England screening positive for PTSD in the past month in 2023/24, up from 4.4% in 2014 (NHS Digital).
  • Risk factors include the severity and duration of the traumatic event, lack of social support, pre-existing mental health conditions, and a history of childhood adversity.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Post traumatic stress disorder"][/sc]
  • Trigger: Exposure to a traumatic event (e.g., serious accident, combat, assault) leads to an acute stress response, activating the sympathetic nervous system and the hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal (HPA) axis.
  • Progression: In susceptible individuals, this acute response fails to resolve, leading to persistent dysregulation of fear circuitry, including hyperactivity of the amygdala, hypoactivity of the medial prefrontal cortex, and structural changes in the hippocampus. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Amygdala hyperactivity in PTSD: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Amygdala hyperactivity"][/sc]
  • Consequence: These neurobiological alterations result in impaired fear extinction, exaggerated startle responses, intrusive memories, and avoidance behaviours, perpetuating the cycle of distress and functional impairment characteristic of PTSD.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Post traumatic stress disorder"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Acute stress reaction immediately following trauma, with symptoms typically resolving within a few days to weeks. If symptoms persist beyond approximately 4 weeks, it may indicate progression to PTSD.
  • Progressive phase: Symptoms persist for more than approximately 4 weeks, meeting diagnostic criteria for PTSD. This phase can last for months or even years if untreated, with fluctuating symptom severity.
  • Late phase: Chronic PTSD, where symptoms endure for many years, significantly impacting daily functioning and quality of life. In some cases, there can be a delayed onset of full diagnostic criteria, particularly beyond 6 months post-trauma.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Post traumatic stress disorder"][/sc]
  • Ask about intrusive memories (e.g., flashbacks, nightmares) - these are hallmark re-experiencing symptoms that indicate the traumatic event is not being processed adaptively.
  • Ask about avoidance behaviours (e.g., avoiding places, people, or conversations) - this highlights attempts to reduce distress, but perpetuates the condition by preventing engagement with trauma-related cues.
  • Ask about negative alterations in cognitions and mood (e.g., persistent negative beliefs about self/world, anhedonia, emotional numbing) - these reflect the psychological impact of trauma and can significantly impair daily functioning and relationships.
  • Ask about alterations in arousal and reactivity (e.g., hypervigilance, exaggerated startle response, irritability, sleep disturbance) - these indicate a persistent state of heightened threat perception and sympathetic nervous system overactivity.
  • Ask about dissociative symptoms (e.g., depersonalisation, derealisation) - these can occur during traumatic re-experiencing and suggest a more complex presentation, potentially impacting treatment engagement.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Post traumatic stress disorder"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - persistent tachycardia or elevated blood pressure may indicate chronic sympathetic nervous system activation due to hyperarousal.
  • Check for an exaggerated startle response - an involuntary physical reaction to sudden, unexpected stimuli, suggesting heightened threat perception.
  • Check for signs of hypervigilance - observable scanning of the environment, restlessness, or difficulty relaxing, indicating a constant state of alert. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Hypervigilance in PTSD: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Hypervigilance"][/sc]
  • Check for physical manifestations of anxiety - tremor, sweating, or muscle tension, which can be present even in the absence of an immediate threat.
  • Check for social withdrawal or emotional blunting - reduced eye contact, flat affect, or reluctance to engage in social interactions, reflecting avoidance behaviours and emotional numbing.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Post traumatic stress disorder"][/sc]
  • Active suicidal ideation or intent: Indicates an immediate risk to life and requires urgent mental health assessment and safety planning.
  • Severe dissociative symptoms (e.g., depersonalisation, derealisation, dissociative amnesia): May impair reality testing and increase vulnerability, necessitating specialised psychological intervention.
  • Rapid escalation of symptoms or functional impairment: Suggests a decompensation that requires prompt clinical review and potential adjustment of management strategies.
  • New onset or worsening psychosis: While rare, psychotic features can occur in severe PTSD and warrant immediate psychiatric evaluation.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Post traumatic stress disorder"][/sc]
  • Older adults: May present with somatic complaints, increased irritability, or social withdrawal rather than classic re-experiencing symptoms, often due to age-related changes in coping mechanisms or reluctance to report psychological distress.
  • Children: Presentation can vary by age; younger children may exhibit regressive behaviours (e.g., bedwetting, thumb-sucking), play reenactment of the trauma, or new fears, while adolescents may show increased aggression, risk-taking behaviours, or academic decline.
  • Comorbid substance use disorder: Individuals may use substances to self-medicate PTSD symptoms, masking the underlying trauma-related distress and complicating diagnosis and treatment.
  • Dissociative subtype: Characterised by prominent dissociative symptoms (depersonalisation, derealisation) in response to trauma reminders, which can be mistaken for other psychiatric conditions or neurological events.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Post traumatic stress disorder"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected Post traumatic stress disorder: Clinical assessment and structured diagnostic interviews such as the Clinician-Administered PTSD Scale for DSM-5 (CAPS-5) or Structured Clinical Interview for DSM-5 (SCID-5). Screening tools like the Primary Care PTSD Screen for DSM-5 (PC-PTSD-5) on MDCalc or the PTSD Checklist for DSM-5 (PCL-5) are useful for initial identification and symptom monitoring.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Consider investigations to rule out other medical or psychiatric conditions that may mimic PTSD symptoms, such as thyroid dysfunction, neurological disorders, or other anxiety disorders.
  • Imaging - Neuroimaging: Not routinely used for the diagnosis of PTSD in clinical practice, but functional MRI or PET scans may be used in research settings to study neurobiological correlates.
  • Serial reassessment: Regular monitoring of symptom severity and functional impairment using validated scales (e.g., PCL-5) to track treatment response and identify potential relapse.

He actively avoids situations that remind him of the event, experiences significant irritability, and has difficulty sleeping, leading to occupational impairment.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Post traumatic stress disorder"][/sc]
  • Acute Stress Disorder (ASD) - symptoms occur within 1 month of the trauma and resolve within 4 weeks; PTSD symptoms persist beyond 1 month.
  • Generalized Anxiety Disorder (GAD) - characterised by persistent, excessive worry about various life events, not specifically tied to a traumatic event.
  • Major Depressive Disorder (MDD) - shares symptoms like anhedonia and sleep disturbance, but the core features of re-experiencing and avoidance are absent in MDD.
  • Complex PTSD (C-PTSD) - typically results from prolonged, repeated trauma (e.g., childhood abuse), leading to more pervasive difficulties in emotion regulation, self-perception, and relationships, in addition to core PTSD symptoms.
  • Panic Disorder - involves recurrent, unexpected panic attacks, which may or may not be related to a specific traumatic trigger, unlike PTSD where panic-like symptoms are often cued by trauma reminders.
  • Substance Use Disorder - can co-occur with PTSD, but primary diagnosis of SUD involves compulsive drug-seeking and use despite harmful consequences, whereas in PTSD, substance use may be a coping mechanism for trauma symptoms.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Post traumatic stress disorder"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Ensure safety by assessing for suicidal ideation or self-harm risk. Provide psychoeducation about PTSD and normalise reactions to trauma. Offer immediate practical and emotional support.
  • Definitive management: Trauma-focused psychological therapies are recommended as first-line treatment, such as Eye Movement Desensitisation and Reprocessing (EMDR) or trauma-focused Cognitive Behavioural Therapy (CBT), as per NICE guidelines.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Regular clinical reviews to assess symptom severity, functional improvement, and adherence to treatment. Monitor for comorbid conditions such as depression or substance use disorders.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: If symptoms worsen, there is an increase in risk, or treatment is ineffective, consider referral to specialist mental health services. Provide clear information on how to access crisis support.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Post traumatic stress disorder"][/sc]
  • Chronic PTSD: Persistence of symptoms beyond several months, leading to long-term distress and functional impairment, often requiring sustained therapeutic intervention.
  • Comorbid mental health conditions: High rates of co-occurrence with depression, other anxiety disorders (e.g., GAD, panic disorder), and substance use disorders, which can complicate treatment and worsen prognosis.
  • Functional impairment: Significant difficulties in maintaining employment, stable relationships, and engaging in social activities, stemming from avoidance behaviours, emotional numbing, and hyperarousal.
  • Increased risk of self-harm and suicide: A critical concern, particularly in individuals with severe symptoms, comorbid depression, or limited social support, necessitating ongoing risk assessment and safety planning.
  • Physical health problems: Chronic activation of the stress response system can contribute to cardiovascular disease, chronic pain syndromes, gastrointestinal issues, and weakened immune function over time.
