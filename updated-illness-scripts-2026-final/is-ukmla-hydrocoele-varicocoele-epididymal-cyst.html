Hydrocoele/ varicocoele/ epididymal cyst [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Hydrocoele/ varicocoele/ epididymal cyst"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Hydrocoele/ varicocoele/ epididymal cyst"][/sc]
  • **Hydrocoele:** An abnormal collection of serous fluid within the tunica vaginalis of the testis or along the spermatic cord.
  • **UKMLA learning outcome:** Recognise the clinical features, common causes, and initial management of scrotal swellings including hydrocoele, varicocoele, and epididymal cysts.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Hydrocoele/ varicocoele/ epididymal cyst"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Hydrocoele/ varicocoele/ epididymal cyst"][/sc]
  • Hydrocoeles are common in neonates, often resolving spontaneously; in adults, they typically result from inflammation, trauma, or infection.
  • Varicocoeles affect approximately 15-20% of males, with a higher prevalence in infertile men, often presenting during adolescence.
  • Epididymal cysts are common benign lesions, increasing in incidence with age, and are often asymptomatic.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Hydrocoele/ varicocoele/ epididymal cyst"][/sc]
  • **Hydrocoele:** Imbalance between fluid secretion and absorption by the tunica vaginalis, often due to a patent processus vaginalis (congenital) or inflammation/trauma (acquired).
  • **Varicocoele:** Incompetent or absent valves in the spermatic vein lead to retrograde blood flow and dilatation of the pampiniform plexus, causing increased scrotal temperature and potential testicular dysfunction.
  • **Epididymal cyst:** Accumulation of fluid within a diverticulum of the epididymis, thought to arise from obstruction of epididymal tubules or developmental anomalies.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Hydrocoele/ varicocoele/ epididymal cyst"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Acute onset of scrotal swelling or discomfort, particularly beyond a few hours, may indicate an acute cause requiring urgent assessment.
  • Progressive phase: Gradual increase in size of a hydrocoele or epididymal cyst over weeks to months, or worsening discomfort from a varicocoele, often warrants further investigation.
  • Late phase: Long-standing, untreated large hydrocoeles or varicocoeles may lead to chronic discomfort, cosmetic concerns, or fertility issues, particularly beyond several months.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Hydrocoele/ varicocoele/ epididymal cyst"][/sc]
  • Ask about scrotal swelling - to determine onset, progression, and any associated pain or discomfort.
  • Ask about pain or heaviness in the scrotum - varicocoeles often cause a dull ache, particularly after prolonged standing, which can be relieved by lying down.
  • Ask about changes in testicular size or consistency - to identify potential testicular atrophy associated with varicocoeles or underlying pathology.
  • Ask about fertility concerns - varicocoeles are a common cause of male subfertility and may be a presenting symptom.
  • Ask about transillumination - hydrocoeles typically transilluminate, which helps differentiate them from solid masses.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Hydrocoele/ varicocoele/ epididymal cyst"][/sc]
  • Check for scrotal swelling - hydrocoeles are typically smooth, non-tender, and fluctuate in size; epididymal cysts are usually distinct, smooth, and superior to the testis.
  • Check for a "bag of worms" sensation on palpation - characteristic of a varicocoele, particularly when the patient is standing or performing a Valsalva manoeuvre.
  • Check for testicular consistency and size - atrophy may be present in varicocoeles, and the testis should be distinct from the swelling in hydrocoeles and epididymal cysts.
  • Check for transillumination - hydrocoeles will transilluminate, while solid masses or varicocoeles will not.
  • Check vital signs - to assess for systemic signs of infection or inflammation, especially in acute presentations.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Hydrocoele/ varicocoele/ epididymal cyst"][/sc]
  • Sudden onset severe scrotal pain - highly suggestive of testicular torsion, requiring immediate surgical exploration.
  • Non-transilluminating scrotal mass - raises suspicion of a solid testicular tumour, necessitating urgent ultrasound and urological referral.
  • Associated systemic symptoms (e.g., fever, rigors) - may indicate epididymo-orchitis or other infective processes.
  • Fixed, hard, or irregular testicular mass - concerning for malignancy, requiring urgent investigation.
  • Unilateral right-sided varicocoele in an adult - may indicate retroperitoneal pathology (e.g., renal tumour) compressing the right gonadal vein.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Hydrocoele/ varicocoele/ epididymal cyst"][/sc]
  • Older adults: Hydrocoeles may be secondary to underlying pathology such as malignancy or infection, rather than primary.
  • Children: Communicating hydrocoeles may present with fluctuating swelling that is more prominent at the end of the day or with crying.
  • Immunosuppressed patients: May present with atypical or severe infections leading to epididymo-orchitis, which can mimic other scrotal swellings.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Hydrocoele/ varicocoele/ epididymal cyst"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected scrotal swelling: Scrotal ultrasound with Doppler - essential for differentiating between solid and cystic lesions, assessing testicular blood flow, and identifying varicocoeles.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Urinalysis and urine culture - to rule out urinary tract infection in cases of suspected epididymo-orchitis.
  • Imaging - Ultrasound: Provides detailed visualisation of scrotal contents, confirming the diagnosis of hydrocoele (anechoic fluid collection), varicocoele (dilated tortuous veins with retrograde flow), or epididymal cyst (simple cyst in the epididymis).
  • Serial reassessment: For asymptomatic, small hydrocoeles or epididymal cysts, watchful waiting with regular clinical review is often appropriate.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Hydrocoele/ varicocoele/ epididymal cyst"][/sc]
  • A 28-year-old male presents with a several-month history of a soft, non-tender, transilluminating right scrotal swelling that fluctuates in size, without associated pain or systemic symptoms.
  • A young adult male with a chronic, painless, fluctuating scrotal swelling, consistent with a benign fluid collection, without features of acute inflammation or malignancy.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Hydrocoele/ varicocoele/ epididymal cyst"][/sc]
  • Testicular torsion - acute, severe pain, absent cremasteric reflex, high-riding testis.
  • Epididymo-orchitis - acute pain, tenderness, fever, dysuria, often associated with UTI or STI.
  • Inguinal hernia - swelling that extends into the inguinal canal, often reducible, does not transilluminate.
  • Spermatocele - a type of epididymal cyst containing spermatozoa, typically smaller and located at the head of the epididymis.
  • Testicular tumour - firm, painless, non-transilluminating mass within the testis.
  • Haematocoele - collection of blood in the tunica vaginalis, usually following trauma, does not transilluminate.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Hydrocoele/ varicocoele/ epididymal cyst"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Exclude testicular torsion in acute scrotal pain; arrange urgent ultrasound for any suspicious testicular mass.
  • Definitive management: For symptomatic hydrocoeles or varicocoeles, surgical correction (e.g., hydrocelectomy, varicocelectomy) may be considered. Asymptomatic epididymal cysts generally require no treatment.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Regular self-examination and clinical review for asymptomatic scrotal swellings to monitor for changes in size or character.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Advise patients to seek urgent medical attention for sudden worsening pain, rapid increase in swelling, or development of new systemic symptoms.
  • Exam pharmacology reference (UKMLA/MRCGP): Analgesia for symptomatic relief (e.g., paracetamol, ibuprofen). No doses or frequencies are cited here — always verify against your local antimicrobial/prescribing formulary before prescribing.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Hydrocoele/ varicocoele/ epididymal cyst"][/sc]
  • Infection: Hydrocoeles or epididymal cysts can become infected, leading to pain, redness, and fever, which may complicate diagnosis.
  • Testicular atrophy: Chronic varicocoeles can lead to impaired spermatogenesis and testicular atrophy due to increased scrotal temperature and venous stasis, impacting fertility.
  • Infertility: Varicocoeles are a significant cause of male subfertility, and their presence warrants assessment of semen parameters.
  • Recurrence: Surgical correction of hydrocoeles or varicocoeles carries a small risk of recurrence, which may necessitate further intervention.
  • Psychological distress: Any scrotal swelling can cause anxiety and distress for the patient, regardless of its benign nature.
