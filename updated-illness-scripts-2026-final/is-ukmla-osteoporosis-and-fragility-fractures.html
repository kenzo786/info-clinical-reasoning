Osteoporosis and fragility fractures [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Osteoporosis and fragility fractures"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Osteoporosis and fragility fractures"][/sc]
  • Osteoporosis is a health condition that weakens bones, making them fragile and more likely to break. It develops slowly over several years and is often only diagnosed when a fall or sudden impact causes a bone to break (fracture).
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the risk factors, clinical presentation, diagnosis, and management principles of osteoporosis and fragility fractures.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Osteoporosis and fragility fractures"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Osteoporosis and fragility fractures"][/sc]
  • In England and Wales, approximately 180,000 fractures occur annually due to osteoporosis.
  • The prevalence of osteoporosis significantly increases with age, affecting around 2% of individuals at 50 years and nearly 50% by 80 years of age.
  • Key risk factors include female sex, early menopause, family history of osteoporosis, prolonged corticosteroid use, certain medical conditions (e.g., inflammatory, endocrine, malabsorption disorders), eating disorders, low body mass index, sedentary lifestyle, and excessive alcohol consumption or smoking.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Osteoporosis and fragility fractures"][/sc]
  • Trigger: Osteoporosis primarily results from an imbalance between osteoclastic bone resorption and osteoblastic bone formation, often accelerated by ageing and hormonal changes (e.g., oestrogen deficiency post-menopause).
  • Progression: This imbalance leads to a progressive reduction in bone mineral density and microarchitectural deterioration of bone tissue, making bones increasingly porous and brittle.
  • Consequence: The weakened bone structure significantly increases susceptibility to fragility fractures, which occur from low-energy trauma that would not typically cause a fracture in healthy bone. Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Osteoporosis and fragility fractures"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Osteoporosis typically develops insidiously over many years, often starting with osteopenia (reduced bone density) which is usually asymptomatic and not clinically apparent.
  • Progressive phase: As bone loss continues, bone strength diminishes, leading to osteoporosis. This phase remains largely asymptomatic until a fragility fracture occurs, often from minimal trauma.
  • Late phase: The clinical manifestation of osteoporosis is often a fragility fracture. Healing of these fractures can take several weeks to months, with pain and functional limitations potentially persisting beyond several months. Patients are at significantly increased risk of further fractures, particularly in the first year or two following an initial event.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Osteoporosis and fragility fractures"][/sc]
  • Ask about back pain - often indicative of vertebral compression fractures, which can be asymptomatic or cause significant discomfort.
  • Ask about loss of height - a common sign of multiple vertebral compression fractures over time.
  • Ask about changes in posture - such as stooping or developing a 'dowager's hump', suggesting spinal deformity due to vertebral fractures.
  • Ask about sudden, severe pain following a low-energy fall or minor trauma - highly suggestive of a fragility fracture.
  • Ask about difficulty with mobility or weight-bearing - particularly after a fall, indicating a potential fracture in a limb or hip.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Osteoporosis and fragility fractures"][/sc]
  • Check height and compare to previous measurements - a significant loss of height may indicate vertebral compression fractures.
  • Check for kyphosis or stooped posture - suggestive of multiple vertebral fractures and spinal deformity.
  • Check for localised pain, swelling, bruising, or deformity - indicative of an acute fragility fracture.
  • Check ability to bear weight on affected limb - inability to do so strongly suggests a fracture.
  • Check vital signs - typically normal in uncomplicated osteoporosis; however, pain and anxiety associated with acute fractures may cause mild tachycardia or hypertension.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Osteoporosis and fragility fractures"][/sc]
  • Fragility fracture: A fracture occurring from a fall from standing height or less, or from no apparent trauma, is a definitive red flag for underlying osteoporosis.
  • Significant height loss: A documented loss of 4cm or more in height over time strongly suggests vertebral compression fractures.
  • New or worsening kyphosis: Progressive spinal curvature (e.g., 'dowager's hump') indicates potential multiple vertebral fractures.
  • Unexplained chronic back pain: Especially in older adults, this may signify vertebral fractures even without a clear traumatic event.
  • Prolonged systemic corticosteroid use: A major risk factor that significantly increases the likelihood of osteoporosis and fractures.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Osteoporosis and fragility fractures"][/sc]
  • Atypical femoral fractures (AFFs): These are rare but serious stress fractures of the femur, typically occurring in patients on long-term bisphosphonate therapy. They present with prodromal thigh or groin pain, often bilateral.
  • Osteoporosis in younger adults (<50 years): May present with fragility fractures without obvious risk factors, requiring thorough investigation for secondary causes (e.g., genetic disorders, endocrine abnormalities, chronic diseases).
  • Osteoporosis in men: Often under-recognised and under-treated. Men typically present with osteoporosis at an older age than women, and secondary causes are more common.
  • Vertebral fragility fractures without significant trauma: These can be asymptomatic or present as chronic back pain, leading to progressive kyphosis and height loss without an acute event.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Osteoporosis and fragility fractures"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected Osteoporosis:
    • Dual-energy X-ray absorptiometry (DEXA) scan: Measures bone mineral density (BMD) at the hip and spine. T-scores are used for diagnosis (osteoporosis if T-score ≤ -2.5 SD).
    • Fracture Risk Assessment Tool (FRAX): An online algorithm that estimates the 10-year probability of hip and major osteoporotic fractures.
    • Q-Fracture: Another online tool used in the UK to assess 10-year fracture risk.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible:
    • Blood tests: Full blood count, erythrocyte sedimentation rate (ESR), C-reactive protein (CRP), calcium, phosphate, alkaline phosphatase, liver and renal function tests, thyroid function tests, parathyroid hormone, vitamin D, serum protein electrophoresis (to exclude myeloma), testosterone (in men).
  • Imaging - X-ray: Used to identify existing fractures, particularly vertebral compression fractures.
  • Imaging - CT/MRI: May be used for more detailed assessment of complex fractures or to rule out other spinal pathologies.
  • Serial reassessment: Regular monitoring of BMD with DEXA scans, typically every 1-3 years, to assess treatment efficacy and disease progression.

  • A 78-year-old post-menopausal woman presents to the emergency department after a low-energy fall at home, sustaining a wrist fracture. She reports a gradual loss of height over the past few years and occasional dull back pain.
  • An elderly, post-menopausal female with chronic bone demineralisation and recurrent low-trauma fractures.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Osteoporosis and fragility fractures"][/sc]
  • Osteomalacia - impaired mineralisation of new bone matrix, often due to vitamin D deficiency, leading to soft bones rather than brittle.
  • Multiple Myeloma - a plasma cell malignancy that can cause lytic bone lesions and pathological fractures, often associated with systemic symptoms like fatigue and weight loss.
  • Bone metastases - secondary cancerous deposits in bone, leading to localised bone destruction and pathological fractures, typically with a history of primary malignancy.
  • Hyperparathyroidism - excessive parathyroid hormone leading to increased bone turnover and resorption, often with elevated serum calcium.
  • Paget's disease of bone - localised disorder of bone remodelling, resulting in enlarged and deformed bones with increased fracture risk, often asymptomatic or presenting with bone pain/deformity.
  • Glucocorticoid-induced osteoporosis - bone loss directly attributable to prolonged systemic corticosteroid therapy, a common iatrogenic cause.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Osteoporosis and fragility fractures"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities:
    • Prompt assessment and management of acute fragility fractures (e.g., pain control, immobilisation, surgical fixation where appropriate).
    • Initiate secondary fracture prevention strategies as soon as possible after a fragility fracture, as the risk of re-fracture is highest immediately post-event.
  • Definitive management:
    • Lifestyle modifications: Optimise calcium and vitamin D intake (dietary and/or supplementation), regular weight-bearing exercise, smoking cessation, and reduced alcohol consumption.
    • Pharmacological therapy: Selection depends on fracture risk assessment (e.g., FRAX score, DEXA results).
  • Monitoring and follow-up:
    • Regular review of treatment adherence and efficacy.
    • Repeat DEXA scans (typically every 1-3 years) to monitor changes in bone mineral density.
    • Monitor for adverse effects of medication.
  • Escalation and safety-netting:
    • Referral to specialist services (e.g., Fracture Liaison Service, endocrinology) for complex cases, treatment failure, or atypical presentations.
    • Educate patients on fall prevention strategies and signs of new fractures.
  • Exam pharmacology reference (UKMLA/MRCGP):
    • Bisphosphonates (e.g., [sc name="jitl-medication" Q="Alendronate use in Osteoporosis" text="alendronate"], [sc name="jitl-medication" Q="Risedronate use in Osteoporosis" text="risedronate"], [sc name="jitl-medication" Q="Zoledronic acid use in Osteoporosis" text="zoledronic acid"]) - reduce bone resorption.
    • Denosumab ([sc name="jitl-medication" Q="Denosumab use in Osteoporosis" text="denosumab"]) - monoclonal antibody inhibiting osteoclast formation and function.
    • Teriparatide ([sc name="jitl-medication" Q="Teriparatide use in Osteoporosis" text="teriparatide"]) - parathyroid hormone analogue stimulating bone formation (anabolic agent).
    • Hormone Replacement Therapy (HRT) - may be considered in younger post-menopausal women for symptom relief and fracture prevention.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Osteoporosis and fragility fractures"][/sc]
  • Recurrent fragility fractures: A prior fragility fracture is the strongest predictor of future fractures, with the risk being highest in the first 1-2 years. This significantly increases morbidity, mortality, and healthcare burden.
  • Chronic pain: Particularly from vertebral compression fractures, leading to persistent discomfort, reduced quality of life, and increased reliance on analgesia.
  • Loss of mobility and independence: Fractures, especially hip fractures, often result in significant disability, requiring long-term care, and can lead to a loss of ability to perform activities of daily living.
  • Spinal deformity (Kyphosis): Multiple vertebral compression fractures can lead to progressive kyphosis, causing changes in posture, reduced physical function, and potentially impacting respiratory and gastrointestinal systems.
  • Increased mortality: Hip fractures, in particular, are associated with a significant increase in mortality in the year following the fracture, especially in older adults.
  • Psychological impact: Patients often experience anxiety, depression, and a fear of falling, leading to social isolation and further reduction in physical activity.
