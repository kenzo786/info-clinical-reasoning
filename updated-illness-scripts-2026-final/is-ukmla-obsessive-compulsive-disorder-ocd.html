Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Obsessive-compulsive disorder (OCD)"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Obsessive-compulsive disorder (OCD)"][/sc]
  • OCD affects approximately 1.2% of the UK population, with onset often in adolescence or early adulthood.
  • Risk factors include a family history of OCD, stressful life events, and certain personality traits.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Obsessive-compulsive disorder (OCD)"][/sc]
  • Trigger: Dysregulation in cortico-striato-thalamo-cortical (CSTC) circuits, particularly involving serotonin and dopamine pathways.
  • Progression: Repetitive thoughts (obsessions) and behaviours (compulsions) become entrenched as maladaptive coping mechanisms.
  • Consequence: Significant distress and functional impairment due to the intrusive nature of obsessions and time-consuming compulsions.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Obsessive-compulsive disorder (OCD)"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Insidious onset, often in childhood or adolescence, with initial symptoms potentially dismissed or hidden.
  • Progressive phase: Symptoms typically worsen over time, particularly beyond several months, leading to increased distress and functional impairment if untreated.
  • Late phase: Chronic and fluctuating course, with periods of remission and exacerbation, often requiring long-term management.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Obsessive-compulsive disorder (OCD)"][/sc]
  • Ask about intrusive thoughts - why they are unwanted and cause significant anxiety.
  • Ask about repetitive behaviours or mental acts - why they are performed in response to an obsession or according to rigid rules.
  • Ask about time spent on obsessions/compulsions - often more than one hour per day, indicating clinical significance.
  • Ask about impact on daily functioning - how symptoms interfere with work, education, or social activities.
  • Ask about insight - whether the individual recognises their obsessions/compulsions are excessive or unreasonable.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Obsessive-compulsive disorder (OCD)"][/sc]
  • Check for visible compulsions - such as excessive hand washing, checking, or arranging, which may be subtle.
  • Check for signs of anxiety or distress - restlessness, tension, or avoidance behaviours during the consultation.
  • Check skin integrity - particularly hands, for dermatitis or excoriation from excessive washing.
  • Check vitals - typically normal, but may show signs of anxiety (e.g., mild tachycardia).
  • Check for co-morbid conditions - such as depression or other anxiety disorders, which often co-exist with OCD.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Obsessive-compulsive disorder (OCD)"][/sc]
  • Sudden onset of severe symptoms - may indicate an underlying organic cause or acute stressor.
  • Psychotic features - suggests a differential diagnosis such as schizophrenia or schizoaffective disorder.
  • Significant functional impairment - inability to maintain employment, education, or personal hygiene, requiring urgent intervention.
  • Suicidal ideation or intent - a critical red flag requiring immediate risk assessment and safety planning.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Obsessive-compulsive disorder (OCD)"][/sc]
  • Children and adolescents: Symptoms may be less overt, often manifesting as excessive reassurance-seeking or avoidance, and may be misattributed to typical childhood anxieties.
  • Older adults: OCD may be misdiagnosed as dementia or other neurocognitive disorders due to repetitive behaviours or memory concerns.
  • Perinatal period: New onset or exacerbation of OCD, particularly with obsessions related to harm to the baby, which can be highly distressing and require sensitive management.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Obsessive-compulsive disorder (OCD)"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected Obsessive-compulsive disorder (OCD): Clinical interview and mental state examination, using validated screening tools such as the Yale-Brown Obsessive Compulsive Scale (Y-BOCS).
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Routine blood tests (FBC, U&Es, LFTs, TFTs, B12, folate) to rule out organic causes of anxiety or behavioural changes.
  • Imaging - MRI brain: Not routinely indicated for OCD, but may be considered if neurological signs or symptoms are present to exclude structural abnormalities.
  • Validated score: Yale-Brown Obsessive Compulsive Scale (Y-BOCS) on MDCalc for severity assessment.

  • A 28-year-old female presents with a 6-month history of intrusive thoughts about contamination, leading to excessive hand washing and cleaning rituals, causing significant distress and impacting her work.
  • A young adult with chronic, ego-dystonic, anxiety-provoking thoughts and repetitive, time-consuming behaviours, resulting in marked functional impairment.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Obsessive-compulsive disorder (OCD)"][/sc]
  • Generalised Anxiety Disorder - obsessions are ego-dystonic and specific to OCD, whereas GAD worries are typically ego-syntonic and about everyday concerns.
  • Panic Disorder - panic attacks are episodic, while OCD anxiety is persistent and linked to specific obsessions.
  • Body Dysmorphic Disorder - preoccupation with perceived flaws in physical appearance, distinct from the broader range of OCD obsessions.
  • Tourette\'s Syndrome - tics are typically sudden, rapid, recurrent, non-rhythmic motor movements or vocalisations, distinct from complex compulsions.
  • Obsessive-Compulsive Personality Disorder - a pervasive pattern of preoccupation with orderliness, perfectionism, and control, without true obsessions or compulsions.
  • Hypochondriasis (Illness Anxiety Disorder) - preoccupation with having or acquiring a serious illness, without the repetitive behaviours of OCD.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Obsessive-compulsive disorder (OCD)"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Assess risk, particularly suicidal ideation; provide psychoeducation and validate distress.
  • Definitive management: Cognitive Behavioural Therapy (CBT) with Exposure and Response Prevention (ERP) is first-line; pharmacotherapy with Selective Serotonin Reuptake Inhibitors (SSRIs) may be considered.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Regular review of symptom severity, functional improvement, and side effects of medication; consider stepped care approach.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Refer to specialist mental health services for severe or refractory cases; provide crisis contact information.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Obsessive-compulsive disorder (OCD)"][/sc]
  • Major Depressive Disorder: Common comorbidity, often secondary to the chronic distress and functional impairment caused by OCD, worsening prognosis.
  • Anxiety Disorders: Other anxiety disorders, such as social anxiety or panic disorder, frequently co-occur, complicating diagnosis and treatment.
  • Substance Misuse: Individuals may use alcohol or drugs to self-medicate anxiety or distress associated with obsessions and compulsions.
  • Significant Functional Impairment: Chronic and severe OCD can lead to unemployment, academic failure, and social isolation, impacting quality of life.
  • Relationship Difficulties: Symptoms can strain personal relationships due to time-consuming rituals or avoidance behaviours.
