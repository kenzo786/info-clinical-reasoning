Malabsorption [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Malabsorption"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Malabsorption"][/sc]
  • Malabsorption refers to the impaired absorption of nutrients, including fats, carbohydrates, proteins, vitamins, and minerals, from the gastrointestinal tract into the bloodstream.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical features and common causes of malabsorption, and outline the initial diagnostic approach and management principles.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Malabsorption"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Malabsorption"][/sc]
  • Bile acid malabsorption affects up to 1 in 100 people in the UK, often presenting with chronic diarrhoea.
  • Coeliac disease, a common cause of malabsorption, affects approximately 1 in 100 people in the UK, though many remain undiagnosed.
  • Risk factors include inflammatory bowel disease (e.g., Crohn\'s disease), pancreatic insufficiency, gastrointestinal surgery, and certain medications.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Malabsorption"][/sc]
  • Trigger: Damage to the intestinal lining (e.g., coeliac disease, Crohn\'s disease), pancreatic enzyme deficiency (e.g., chronic pancreatitis), or disruption of bile acid enterohepatic circulation (e.g., bile acid malabsorption).
  • Progression: Impaired digestion or absorption of macronutrients and micronutrients due to reduced surface area, enzyme insufficiency, or altered transport mechanisms. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Steatorrhoea: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Steatorrhoea"][/sc]
  • Consequence: Nutritional deficiencies, weight loss, chronic diarrhoea, and systemic complications affecting multiple organ systems.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Malabsorption"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Subtle and non-specific symptoms such as bloating, abdominal discomfort, or mild changes in bowel habits, often developing insidiously over weeks to months.
  • Progressive phase: Worsening diarrhoea, steatorrhoea, significant weight loss, and emerging signs of specific nutrient deficiencies (e.g., anaemia, osteomalacia), typically evolving over several months.
  • Late phase: Severe malnutrition, profound weakness, and established complications such as osteoporosis, neurological deficits, or increased susceptibility to infections, particularly beyond six months of untreated malabsorption.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Malabsorption"][/sc]
  • Ask about chronic diarrhoea - often pale, bulky, foul-smelling, and difficult to flush, indicating fat malabsorption (steatorrhoea).
  • Ask about unintentional weight loss - despite adequate or increased food intake, suggesting caloric malabsorption.
  • Ask about abdominal bloating and pain - common, but non-specific, symptoms that can indicate underlying gastrointestinal pathology or gas production from unabsorbed carbohydrates.
  • Ask about fatigue and weakness - often due to anaemia (iron, B12, folate deficiency) or general malnutrition.
  • Ask about muscle wasting and oedema - signs of severe protein malabsorption.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Malabsorption"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - may reveal hypotension or tachycardia in severe dehydration from diarrhoea; fever in infectious causes.
  • Check for pallor - suggests anaemia (iron, B12, folate deficiency).
  • Check for muscle wasting and reduced subcutaneous fat - indicative of chronic malnutrition.
  • Check for oedema - particularly peripheral, suggesting hypoalbuminaemia from protein malabsorption. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Hypoalbuminaemia: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Hypoalbuminaemia"][/sc]
  • Check for glossitis or angular stomatitis - signs of B vitamin deficiencies.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Malabsorption"][/sc]
  • Unexplained significant weight loss - raises concern for underlying malignancy or severe malabsorptive disorder requiring urgent investigation.
  • New onset chronic diarrhoea in older adults - warrants prompt evaluation to exclude colorectal malignancy or inflammatory bowel disease.
  • Severe abdominal pain with signs of peritonism - suggests acute abdominal pathology, potentially related to complications of malabsorption (e.g., perforation).
  • Evidence of severe dehydration or electrolyte imbalance - requires immediate medical attention and fluid resuscitation.
  • Neurological symptoms (e.g., ataxia, peripheral neuropathy) - may indicate severe vitamin deficiencies (e.g., B12, E) requiring urgent replacement to prevent irreversible damage, particularly if prolonged beyond several months.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Malabsorption"][/sc]
  • Older adults: May present with subtle symptoms, often attributed to ageing, leading to delayed diagnosis. Malnutrition and sarcopenia may be prominent.
  • Children: Failure to thrive, poor growth, abdominal distension, and irritability may be the primary manifestations rather than classic diarrhoea.
  • Post-surgical (e.g., bariatric surgery, ileal resection): Malabsorption can develop years after surgery, often presenting with specific micronutrient deficiencies (e.g., B12, iron, fat-soluble vitamins) rather than overt steatorrhoea. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Short bowel syndrome: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Short bowel syndrome"][/sc]
  • Subclinical coeliac disease: May present with isolated iron deficiency anaemia, osteoporosis, or neurological symptoms without significant gastrointestinal complaints.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Malabsorption"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected malabsorption: Full blood count (anaemia), ferritin (iron stores), B12 and folate, liver function tests (albumin), calcium, phosphate, alkaline phosphatase (bone health), thyroid function tests. Faecal elastase-1 (pancreatic exocrine insufficiency), coeliac serology (anti-TTG IgA, total IgA).
  • Severity/risk tools: Malnutrition Universal Screening Tool (MUST) for nutritional assessment. MUST Score on MDCalc
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Stool microscopy and culture (infection), faecal calprotectin (inflammatory bowel disease), hydrogen breath test (lactose intolerance, SIBO).
  • Imaging - Endoscopy with biopsy: Upper GI endoscopy with duodenal biopsies for coeliac disease; colonoscopy with biopsies for inflammatory bowel disease.
  • Imaging - Small bowel imaging: CT enterography or MRI enterography for Crohn\'s disease or other structural abnormalities.
  • Serial reassessment: Monitoring of nutritional markers (e.g., albumin, vitamin levels) and symptom response to treatment.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Malabsorption"][/sc]
  • A 45-year-old female presents with a six-month history of chronic, foul-smelling diarrhoea, unintentional weight loss, and persistent fatigue.
  • Chronic diarrhoea with weight loss and nutritional deficiencies, concerning for a malabsorptive disorder.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Malabsorption"][/sc]
  • Irritable Bowel Syndrome (IBS) - typically no weight loss or nutritional deficiencies, symptoms often relieved by defecation.
  • Inflammatory Bowel Disease (Crohn\'s/Ulcerative Colitis) - often associated with abdominal pain, rectal bleeding, and systemic inflammation. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Inflammatory Bowel Disease: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Inflammatory Bowel Disease"][/sc]
  • Infectious Gastroenteritis (chronic) - usually a history of travel or exposure, specific pathogens identified on stool tests.
  • Thyrotoxicosis - hypermetabolic state with weight loss, but typically with tachycardia, tremor, and goitre.
  • Colorectal Malignancy - may cause weight loss and change in bowel habit, but often with rectal bleeding and obstructive symptoms.
  • Lactose Intolerance - symptoms specifically triggered by dairy products, often without significant weight loss.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Malabsorption"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Recognition of severe dehydration or electrolyte imbalance and initiation of supportive care. Identification and treatment of the underlying cause.
  • Definitive management: Treatment is directed at the underlying cause (e.g., gluten-free diet for coeliac disease, pancreatic enzyme replacement for chronic pancreatitis, antibiotics for SIBO).
  • Adjunctive management: Nutritional support, including dietary modifications (e.g., low-fat diet for steatorrhoea) and supplementation of deficient vitamins and minerals (e.g., iron, B12, fat-soluble vitamins).
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Regular assessment of nutritional status, symptom control, and monitoring for complications.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Patients with persistent symptoms, worsening nutritional status, or red flag features often warrant specialist gastroenterology review.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Malabsorption"][/sc]
  • Malnutrition: Progressive weight loss, muscle wasting, and impaired immune function due to chronic nutrient deficiencies, particularly if untreated for several months.
  • Anaemia: Iron, B12, or folate deficiency leading to fatigue, weakness, and reduced exercise tolerance.
  • Bone disease: Osteoporosis or osteomalacia due to malabsorption of calcium and vitamin D, increasing fracture risk over time.
  • Neurological complications: Peripheral neuropathy, ataxia, or cognitive impairment due to deficiencies of vitamins (e.g., B12, E), potentially irreversible if prolonged beyond several weeks.
  • Increased infection risk: Impaired immune function due to malnutrition, particularly in the late phase of untreated malabsorption.
  • Specific fat-soluble vitamin deficiencies: Vitamin A (night blindness, xerophthalmia), Vitamin D (osteomalacia, osteoporosis), Vitamin E (neuropathy, ataxia), Vitamin K (coagulopathy, bleeding tendencies).
