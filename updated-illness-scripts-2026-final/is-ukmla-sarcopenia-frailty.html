Sarcopenia/ frailty [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Sarcopenia/ frailty"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Sarcopenia/ frailty"][/sc]
  • Sarcopenia is the progressive and generalised loss of skeletal muscle mass and strength/function, typically associated with ageing, while frailty is a multi-system impairment characterised by increased vulnerability to stressors, often encompassing sarcopenia [1] [2].
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise and assess multi-system diseases and frailty in older adults, understanding their impact on patient vulnerability and management.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Sarcopenia/ frailty"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Sarcopenia/ frailty"][/sc]
  • The prevalence of probable sarcopenia in the UK Biobank population (ages 40-70) is estimated at 5.3%, with the risk increasing with advancing age [3].
  • Frailty affects approximately 1.8 million people aged ≥60 in the UK, with prevalence rising significantly from 6.5% in those aged 60-69 years to 65% in those aged 90 or over [4] [5].
  • Key risk factors include age over 65 years, sedentary lifestyle, malnutrition (increasing risk by three to four times), and the presence of multiple chronic diseases [6] [7].
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Sarcopenia/ frailty"][/sc]
  • Triggered by age-related declines in anabolic hormones such as testosterone, human growth hormone (HGH), and insulin-like growth factor-1 (IGF-1), which are crucial for muscle development and maintenance [1].
  • Progression involves insulin resistance, often seen in "sarcopenic obesity," where metabolic dysfunction impairs insulin's effects on skeletal muscle, alongside age-related neurodegeneration affecting alpha motor neurons and neuromuscular junctions [1].
  • Consequences include reduced muscle strength and size, further exacerbated by chronic low-grade inflammation with elevated C-reactive protein (CRP), tumour necrosis factor-alpha (TNF), and interleukins (IL-6, IL-1), contributing to muscle catabolism [1].
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Sarcopenia/ frailty"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Subtle decline in muscle mass and strength, often unnoticed, potentially beginning in middle age with a sedentary lifestyle or inadequate nutrition.
  • Progressive phase: Noticeable reduction in physical performance, increased fatigue, and difficulty with activities of daily living, leading to a higher risk of falls and hospitalisation.
  • Late phase: Severe muscle wasting and weakness, significant functional impairment, and increased vulnerability to acute stressors, often resulting in loss of independence and increased mortality.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Sarcopenia/ frailty"][/sc]
  • Ask about unintentional weight loss - particularly loss of muscle bulk, which may indicate underlying sarcopenia and frailty.
  • Ask about increasing fatigue or low energy levels - a common manifestation of reduced physiological reserve and muscle weakness.
  • Ask about self-reported muscle weakness - difficulty with tasks requiring strength, such as opening jars or rising from a chair.
  • Ask about slow walking speed - a key indicator of impaired physical performance and increased fall risk.
  • Ask about reduced physical activity - a decline in engagement with usual activities or hobbies due to physical limitations.
  • Ask about recurrent falls - a critical symptom of impaired balance and muscle strength, often indicating advanced frailty.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Sarcopenia/ frailty"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - particularly heart rate and blood pressure, which may show orthostatic changes indicative of autonomic dysfunction in frailty.
  • Check grip strength - using a dynamometer, as reduced grip strength correlates with overall muscle weakness and adverse outcomes [1].
  • Check gait speed - a timed 4-metre walk test, where a speed of <0.8 m/s suggests increased frailty and sarcopenia [8].
  • Check chair stand test - assessing the number of times a patient can stand from a chair without using their arms in 30 seconds, indicating lower extremity strength [1].
  • Check muscle mass - visual inspection for muscle wasting, particularly in the limbs, or objective measures like calf circumference or DEXA scan if available.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Sarcopenia/ frailty"][/sc]
  • Sudden, unexplained weight loss: May indicate underlying malignancy, severe malnutrition, or acute illness contributing to rapid muscle wasting.
  • Recurrent falls with injury: Suggests significant balance impairment and muscle weakness, requiring urgent assessment to prevent further harm.
  • Acute functional decline: A rapid deterioration in the ability to perform activities of daily living, often precipitated by an acute illness or event, indicating decompensation of frailty.
  • Delirium or acute confusion: A common presentation in frail older adults during acute illness, signalling increased vulnerability and poor prognosis.
  • Severe dysphagia: Difficulty swallowing can lead to malnutrition, aspiration pneumonia, and further exacerbate sarcopenia.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Sarcopenia/ frailty"][/sc]
  • Obese individuals: Sarcopenic obesity, where significant muscle loss is masked by excess adipose tissue, leading to delayed diagnosis and management.
  • Younger adults with chronic disease: Sarcopenia can occur in younger individuals with conditions like chronic kidney disease, heart failure, or cancer, not solely an age-related phenomenon.
  • Post-acute illness or hospitalisation: Rapid onset or worsening of sarcopenia and frailty can occur following critical illness, surgery, or prolonged bed rest, even in previously robust individuals.
  • Cognitive impairment: Frailty may present predominantly as cognitive decline or behavioural changes, with physical manifestations being less obvious initially.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Sarcopenia/ frailty"][/sc]
  • Core assessments:
    • SARC-F questionnaire: A rapid screening tool for self-reported signs of sarcopenia (Strength, Assistance with walking, Rising from a chair, Climbing stairs, Falls). A score of ≥4 suggests probable sarcopenia [1].
    • Handgrip strength: Measured with a dynamometer; <27 kg for males and <16 kg for females are suggested cut-off points for weakness [1].
    • Chair stand test: Measures the number of times a patient can stand from a chair without using their arms in 30 seconds; >15 seconds for five rises is a suggested cut-off for poor lower extremity strength [1].
    • Gait speed: Timed 4-metre walk test; <0.8 m/s indicates poor physical performance [8].
  • Imaging for muscle mass:
    • Dual-energy X-ray absorptiometry (DEXA): Considered the reference standard for assessing appendicular skeletal muscle mass (ASM). Suggested cut-offs for ASM/height² are <7.0 kg/m² for males and <5.5 kg/m² for females [1].
    • Bioelectrical impedance analysis (BIA): A more portable and less expensive alternative to DEXA for estimating body composition and muscle mass.
    • Computed tomography (CT) or Magnetic Resonance Imaging (MRI): "Gold standard" for highly accurate muscle mass measurements, particularly cross-sectional area, but less practical for routine clinical use [1].
  • If alternative diagnoses considered:
    • Full blood count, inflammatory markers (CRP, ESR), thyroid function tests, vitamin D levels, renal and liver function tests: To exclude reversible causes of muscle weakness or fatigue.
    • Serum albumin/prealbumin: Indicators of nutritional status.
  • Serial reassessment:
    • Regular monitoring of physical performance, strength, and nutritional status to track progression and response to interventions.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Sarcopenia/ frailty"][/sc]
  • An elderly patient presenting with progressive weakness, unintentional weight loss, and recurrent falls, leading to reduced independence and increased vulnerability to acute illness.
  • A frail, older individual with multi-system physiological decline, characterised by sarcopenia, impaired mobility, and reduced resilience to stressors, often resulting in a cascade of adverse health outcomes.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Sarcopenia/ frailty"][/sc]
  • Cachexia: Characterised by weight loss, muscle wasting, and fatigue, but typically associated with an underlying chronic inflammatory disease (e.g., cancer, severe heart failure), whereas sarcopenia is primarily age-related.
  • Malnutrition: Inadequate intake or absorption of nutrients leading to weight loss and muscle wasting, which can coexist with and exacerbate sarcopenia and frailty.
  • Depression: Can present with fatigue, low energy, and reduced physical activity, mimicking symptoms of frailty, but typically lacks objective signs of muscle weakness or wasting.
  • Hypothyroidism: May cause fatigue, weakness, and muscle aches, but is distinguishable by other systemic symptoms and abnormal thyroid function tests.
  • Polymyalgia Rheumatica: Presents with muscle pain and stiffness, particularly in the shoulders and hips, but typically without significant muscle wasting and with elevated inflammatory markers.
  • Neurological disorders (e.g., Parkinson's disease, motor neurone disease): Cause progressive muscle weakness and functional decline, but are characterised by specific neurological signs and symptoms not typically seen in uncomplicated sarcopenia/frailty.
  • Anaemia: Can cause fatigue, weakness, and reduced exercise tolerance, but is diagnosed by a low haemoglobin level and often responds to treatment of the underlying cause.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Sarcopenia/ frailty"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities:
    • Address acute medical issues: Treat any underlying acute illness or infection that may be precipitating or exacerbating frailty.
    • Optimise nutrition: Ensure adequate protein and calorie intake, potentially with oral nutritional supplements, to support muscle protein synthesis.
    • Review medications: Identify and discontinue inappropriate medications (e.g., sedatives, anticholinergics) that may contribute to falls or cognitive impairment.
  • Definitive management:
    • Resistance exercise training: Progressive resistance exercises are crucial for improving muscle strength, mass, and physical function [1].
    • Aerobic exercise: May support cardiovascular health and overall endurance.
    • Protein supplementation: Consider if dietary intake is insufficient, aiming for 1.0-1.2 g/kg body weight/day [1].
    • Vitamin D supplementation: Address deficiency, as it may contribute to muscle weakness.
    • Multidisciplinary team approach: Involve physiotherapists, occupational therapists, dietitians, and geriatricians for comprehensive assessment and tailored interventions.
  • Monitoring and follow-up:
    • Regular assessment of functional status, grip strength, gait speed, and nutritional parameters.
    • Monitor for falls and implement fall prevention strategies.
    • Assess for social isolation and provide appropriate support.
  • Escalation and safety-netting:
    • Educate patients and carers on signs of deterioration and when to seek medical attention.
    • Consider advanced care planning and goals of care discussions in line with patient preferences.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Sarcopenia/ frailty"][/sc]
  • Increased risk of falls and fractures: Due to muscle weakness, impaired balance, and reduced bone mineral density, leading to significant morbidity and mortality [1].
  • Loss of independence: Progressive decline in physical function makes activities of daily living challenging, often requiring increased care and support.
  • Hospitalisation and prolonged recovery: Frail individuals are more susceptible to acute illnesses and have extended hospital stays with slower, less complete recovery [1].
  • Increased mortality: Sarcopenia and frailty are independently associated with a higher risk of death from various causes [1].
  • Reduced quality of life: Physical limitations, pain, and social isolation contribute to a diminished overall well-being.
  • Malnutrition: Reduced appetite, difficulty preparing food, and dysphagia can lead to further nutritional deficiencies, creating a vicious cycle.
