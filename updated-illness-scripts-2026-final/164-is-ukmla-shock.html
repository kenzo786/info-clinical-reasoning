Shock [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Shock"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Shock"][/sc]
  • Shock is a life-threatening, generalised form of acute circulatory failure associated with inadequate oxygen utilisation by the cells. It is a state in which the circulatory system fails to maintain adequate perfusion of vital organs.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: To be able to recognise and start immediate management of a patient with shock, including performing an ABCDE assessment and escalating care appropriately, while working to identify the underlying cause.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Shock"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Shock"][/sc]
  • In the UK, sepsis is the most common cause of distributive shock, with an estimated 245,000 cases and 48,000 deaths annually.
  • Cardiogenic shock complicates 5-10% of hospitalisations for acute myocardial infarction.
  • Haemorrhage is a leading cause of hypovolaemic shock, particularly in trauma, which is a significant cause of death in people under 40 in the UK.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Shock"][/sc]
  • Trigger: An initial insult (e.g., severe infection, major haemorrhage, myocardial infarction, or pulmonary embolism) initiates one of four primary pathophysiological processes: loss of intravascular volume (hypovolaemic), maldistribution of blood flow (distributive), primary pump failure (cardiogenic), or extracardiac obstruction to blood flow (obstructive).
  • Progression: The initial insult leads to a critical reduction in effective circulating volume and/or cardiac output, causing a mismatch between systemic oxygen delivery (DO2) and oxygen consumption (VO2). The body's compensatory mechanisms (e.g., tachycardia, vasoconstriction, fluid retention) are activated to preserve perfusion to vital organs.
  • Consequence: If the underlying cause is not corrected, compensatory mechanisms fail, leading to global tissue hypoxia, a switch to anaerobic metabolism, lactic acidosis, and the release of inflammatory mediators. This results in a vicious cycle of worsening cellular dysfunction, multiple organ failure, and ultimately, irreversible shock and death. Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Shock"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Within the first few hours, clinical signs are often subtle and reflect compensatory mechanisms. The patient may be anxious with cool peripheries, tachycardia, and a normal or slightly decreased blood pressure.
  • Progressive phase: As shock progresses, compensatory mechanisms begin to fail. Hypotension worsens, tachycardia becomes more pronounced, and signs of organ dysfunction appear, such as altered mental state, oliguria, and worsening metabolic acidosis.
  • Late phase: Beyond several hours without effective treatment, cellular and organ damage becomes irreversible. The patient develops profound hypotension, bradycardia, anuria, and coma, leading to cardiac arrest and death.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Shock"][/sc]
  • Ask about light-headedness, dizziness, or confusion - suggests reduced cerebral perfusion.
  • Ask about palpitations or a feeling of a 'racing heart' - indicates a compensatory tachycardia.
  • Ask about shortness of breath - may indicate cardiogenic shock (pulmonary oedema) or obstructive shock (pulmonary embolism).
  • Ask about recent illness, fever, or rigors - points towards a septic (distributive) cause.
  • Ask about recent trauma, bleeding (e.g., melaena, haematemesis), or fluid loss (e.g., vomiting, diarrhoea) - suggests hypovolaemia.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Shock"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - hypotension (systolic BP <90 mmHg or a significant drop from baseline) and tachycardia are classic signs. The pulse may be weak and thready.
  • Check for cool, clammy, and pale or mottled skin - indicates peripheral vasoconstriction as the body diverts blood to vital organs. In distributive shock, peripheries may initially be warm.
  • Check capillary refill time - a prolonged refill time (>2 seconds) suggests poor peripheral perfusion.
  • Check urine output - oliguria (<0.5 mL/kg/hr) is a sensitive indicator of reduced renal perfusion.
  • Check for altered mental state - confusion, agitation, or reduced consciousness can result from cerebral hypoperfusion.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Shock"][/sc]
  • Hypotension: A systolic blood pressure <90 mmHg or mean arterial pressure <65 mmHg is a critical sign of decompensated shock.
  • Altered mental state: Any new confusion, agitation, or a decrease in the Glasgow Coma Scale (GCS) score indicates inadequate brain perfusion.
  • Anuria or severe oliguria: Indicates acute kidney injury due to hypoperfusion.
  • Lactate >2 mmol/L: A raised serum lactate is a key biomarker of tissue hypoxia and is associated with increased mortality.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Shock"][/sc]
  • Older adults: May not mount a significant tachycardic response due to age-related changes or medications (e.g., beta-blockers). Confusion may be the only presenting sign.
  • Patients on beta-blockers: The expected compensatory tachycardia may be absent, masking the severity of the shock state.
  • Athletes: A high level of physiological reserve may mean they maintain a normal blood pressure until they are in an advanced state of shock.
  • Pregnancy: Physiological changes in pregnancy (e.g., increased blood volume) can mask signs of hypovolaemia until significant blood loss has occurred.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Shock"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected shock: An ABCDE approach is the priority. Initial tests include an ECG, arterial blood gas (for lactate, pH, and oxygenation), and bloods (FBC, U&Es, LFTs, coagulation screen, troponin, and cross-match if bleeding is suspected). Blood cultures should be taken if sepsis is a possibility.
  • Severity/risk tools: The National Early Warning Score (NEWS2) is used in the UK to identify acutely unwell patients. A score of 5 or more is a key trigger for urgent assessment. NEWS2 Score on MDCalc.
  • Imaging - Ultrasound: Focused Assessment with Sonography for Trauma (FAST) scan can identify free fluid in the abdomen in trauma. Focused echocardiography can assess cardiac function and identify causes of obstructive shock (e.g., cardiac tamponade, massive pulmonary embolism).
  • Imaging - Other: Chest X-ray may show signs of heart failure or pneumonia. CT scan may be required to identify the source of bleeding or infection.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Shock"][/sc]
  • Hypovolaemic shock: Consider in trauma, gastrointestinal bleeding, or severe vomiting and diarrhoea.
  • Cardiogenic shock: Suspect in patients with acute coronary syndrome, arrhythmia, or known heart failure.
  • Distributive shock: Sepsis is the most common cause. Also consider anaphylaxis (history of allergen exposure) and neurogenic shock (history of spinal cord injury).
  • Obstructive shock: Consider massive pulmonary embolism (acute dyspnoea, pleuritic chest pain), tension pneumothorax (tracheal deviation, reduced breath sounds), and cardiac tamponade (muffled heart sounds, distended neck veins).
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Shock"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: An ABCDE approach is essential. This includes securing the airway, giving high-flow oxygen, and obtaining intravenous access. Early escalation to senior clinicians and critical care is vital.
  • Definitive management: Treatment is directed at the underlying cause. This may include fluid resuscitation for hypovolaemic and distributive shock, inotropes and vasopressors for cardiogenic and distributive shock, and emergency procedures for obstructive shock (e.g., chest drain for tension pneumothorax).
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Continuous monitoring of vital signs, urine output, and lactate is crucial to guide resuscitation. An arterial line and central venous access are often required.
  • Exam pharmacology reference (UKMLA/MRCGP): Knowledge of vasopressors is important. Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Shock"][/sc]
  • Acute kidney injury: Reduced renal perfusion can lead to kidney damage, which may require renal replacement therapy.
  • Acute respiratory distress syndrome (ARDS): A severe inflammatory reaction in the lungs that can occur in any type of shock, leading to profound hypoxia.
  • Disseminated intravascular coagulation (DIC): A condition of widespread activation of clotting, leading to a consumptive coagulopathy and both thrombosis and bleeding.
  • Multiple organ dysfunction syndrome (MODS): The progressive failure of two or more organ systems. This is the most severe complication of shock and has a high mortality rate.
