Antepartum haemorrhage (Pregnancy and the puerperium) [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Antepartum haemorrhage (Pregnancy and the puerperium)"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Antepartum haemorrhage (Pregnancy and the puerperium)"][/sc]
  • Antepartum haemorrhage (APH) is defined as bleeding from or into the genital tract, occurring from 24+0 weeks of pregnancy and prior to the birth of the baby.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical presentations of antepartum haemorrhage, differentiate between common causes such as placenta praevia and placental abruption, and outline initial management steps including assessment of maternal and fetal well-being.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Antepartum haemorrhage (Pregnancy and the puerperium)"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Antepartum haemorrhage (Pregnancy and the puerperium)"][/sc]
  • APH complicates 2-5% of all pregnancies in the UK, contributing significantly to maternal and perinatal morbidity and mortality.
  • Placenta praevia affects approximately 1 in 200 pregnancies, while placental abruption occurs in about 1 in 100 pregnancies.
  • Risk factors include previous APH, previous Caesarean section, increasing maternal age, multiparity, smoking, cocaine use, and uterine abnormalities.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Antepartum haemorrhage (Pregnancy and the puerperium)"][/sc]
  • Trigger: Disruption of the uteroplacental interface, either due to abnormal placental implantation (placenta praevia) or premature separation of a normally implanted placenta (placental abruption).
  • Progression: Bleeding into the decidua basalis or directly into the maternal circulation, leading to maternal hypovolaemia and fetal compromise due to reduced uteroplacental perfusion.
  • Consequence: Potential for severe maternal haemorrhage, coagulopathy (especially with abruption), fetal hypoxia, preterm birth, and perinatal death. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Placental abruption: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Placental abruption"][/sc]
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Antepartum haemorrhage (Pregnancy and the puerperium)"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Initial bleeding, often painless and intermittent in placenta praevia, or sudden and painful in placental abruption, typically occurring after 24 weeks gestation.
  • Progressive phase: Continued or increasing haemorrhage, leading to signs of maternal shock and fetal distress, particularly within minutes to hours of onset if not managed.
  • Late phase: Development of severe maternal complications such as disseminated intravascular coagulation (DIC) or acute kidney injury, and fetal complications including severe hypoxia, neurological damage, or intrauterine death, particularly beyond several hours of unmanaged severe bleeding.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Antepartum haemorrhage (Pregnancy and the puerperium)"][/sc]
  • Ask about vaginal bleeding - to quantify amount, colour (fresh red vs. dark), and presence of clots, which helps differentiate causes and assess severity.
  • Ask about abdominal pain - severe, continuous pain suggests placental abruption, while painless bleeding is more typical of placenta praevia.
  • Ask about uterine contractions - painful contractions accompanying bleeding may indicate labour or abruption.
  • Ask about fetal movements - reduced or absent fetal movements are a critical indicator of fetal compromise.
  • Ask about associated symptoms like dizziness or syncope - these suggest significant maternal blood loss and hypovolaemia.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Antepartum haemorrhage (Pregnancy and the puerperium)"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs (BP, HR, RR, SpO2) - maternal tachycardia and hypotension indicate hypovolaemic shock, while tachypnoea may suggest metabolic acidosis.
  • Check abdominal palpation - a tense, tender uterus suggests placental abruption, whereas a soft, non-tender uterus is more consistent with placenta praevia. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Uterine tenderness: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Uterine tenderness"][/sc]
  • Check fetal heart rate (FHR) - abnormalities such as bradycardia, tachycardia, or reduced variability indicate fetal distress.
  • Check speculum examination - to identify the source of bleeding (cervical, vaginal, or uterine) and exclude local causes, while avoiding digital vaginal examination if placenta praevia is suspected.
  • Check signs of coagulopathy - petechiae, purpura, or bleeding from venepuncture sites may indicate disseminated intravascular coagulation (DIC), particularly in severe abruption.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Antepartum haemorrhage (Pregnancy and the puerperium)"][/sc]
  • Heavy vaginal bleeding - indicates significant blood loss, potentially leading to maternal hypovolaemic shock and fetal compromise.
  • Persistent abdominal pain - suggests placental abruption, which can rapidly progress to severe maternal and fetal morbidity.
  • Fetal distress - evidenced by abnormal fetal heart rate patterns, signifying acute fetal hypoxia and requiring urgent intervention.
  • Signs of maternal shock - such as hypotension, tachycardia, and altered mental status, indicating critical maternal instability requiring immediate resuscitation.
  • Absence of fetal movements - a grave sign of severe fetal compromise or demise, necessitating urgent assessment.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Antepartum haemorrhage (Pregnancy and the puerperium)"][/sc]
  • Occult abruption: Bleeding may be concealed behind the placenta, leading to severe maternal and fetal compromise without obvious external haemorrhage. This can manifest as disproportionate pain and uterine tenderness.
  • Minor APH with significant fetal compromise: Even small amounts of visible bleeding can be associated with significant fetal distress, particularly if the bleeding is from the fetal circulation (e.g., vasa praevia). [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Vasa praevia: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Vasa praevia"][/sc]
  • Painless bleeding with posterior placenta praevia: While typically painless, a posterior placenta praevia may present with back pain rather than overt abdominal pain, making diagnosis challenging.
  • APH in multiple pregnancies: Increased risk of APH due to larger placental surface area and increased uterine distension, with potential for more complex presentations and management.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Antepartum haemorrhage (Pregnancy and the puerperium)"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected APH: Full blood count (FBC) to assess anaemia, coagulation screen (prothrombin time, activated partial thromboplastin time, fibrinogen) to assess for coagulopathy, and group and save/crossmatch for potential transfusion.
  • Imaging - Ultrasound: Transabdominal and transvaginal ultrasound to locate the placenta and assess for placenta praevia, placental abruption (though often difficult to visualise directly), and vasa praevia. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Placental grading: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Placental grading"][/sc]
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: High vaginal swab for infection, urinalysis for urinary tract infection, and speculum examination to exclude cervical or vaginal lesions.
  • Severity/risk tools: Assessment of maternal haemodynamic stability and estimated blood loss are key. The Shock Index on MDCalc can be used to quantify maternal shock.
  • Serial reassessment: Continuous fetal heart rate monitoring (cardiotocography - CTG) to assess fetal well-being, and serial maternal vital signs and blood tests to monitor for ongoing haemorrhage and coagulopathy.
  • Kleihauer test: To quantify feto-maternal haemorrhage, particularly after placental abruption, guiding the dose of anti-D immunoglobulin for Rh-negative women. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Kleihauer test: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Kleihauer test"][/sc]

  • A 32-year-old primigravida at 34 weeks gestation presents with sudden onset, painless, bright red vaginal bleeding and a soft, non-tender uterus, with normal fetal heart rate.
  • Pregnant woman with third-trimester vaginal bleeding, concerning for placenta praevia.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Antepartum haemorrhage (Pregnancy and the puerperium)"][/sc]
  • Placenta praevia - painless, bright red vaginal bleeding, often recurrent, with a soft uterus and normal fetal heart rate.
  • Placental abruption - sudden onset, painful, dark red vaginal bleeding, often with a tense, tender uterus and fetal distress.
  • Vasa praevia - painless vaginal bleeding, typically occurring at rupture of membranes, with rapid onset of fetal bradycardia.
  • Local cervical/vaginal causes - bleeding from cervical ectropion, polyps, cervicitis, or vaginal trauma, usually diagnosed on speculum examination. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Cervical ectropion: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Cervical ectropion"][/sc]
  • Bloody show - small amount of blood-stained mucus, often preceding labour, not associated with significant haemorrhage or fetal compromise.
  • Uterine rupture - severe abdominal pain, sudden cessation of contractions, maternal shock, and fetal bradycardia, often in women with previous uterine surgery.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Antepartum haemorrhage (Pregnancy and the puerperium)"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Recognition of APH, urgent escalation to senior obstetric and anaesthetic teams, maternal resuscitation (IV fluids, blood products as needed), continuous fetal monitoring, and preparation for potential emergency delivery.
  • Definitive management: Depends on the cause, gestational age, and maternal/fetal condition. For placenta praevia, planned Caesarean section is often warranted. For placental abruption, expedited delivery (vaginal or Caesarean) is typically indicated once maternal stability is achieved.
  • Adjunctive management: Administration of anti-D immunoglobulin for Rh-negative women, corticosteroids for fetal lung maturation if preterm, and tocolysis in selected cases to prolong pregnancy for corticosteroid benefit (if bleeding is not active and maternal/fetal condition allows).
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Close observation of maternal vital signs, blood loss, and fetal well-being. Post-delivery, monitoring for postpartum haemorrhage and complications of coagulopathy is essential.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Any deterioration in maternal or fetal condition warrants immediate re-evaluation and escalation of care. Patients should be advised on signs of recurrent bleeding or fetal compromise.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Antepartum haemorrhage (Pregnancy and the puerperium)"][/sc]
  • Maternal haemorrhage: Significant blood loss can lead to hypovolaemic shock, requiring blood transfusion and potentially resulting in organ dysfunction.
  • Disseminated intravascular coagulation (DIC): Particularly with severe placental abruption, widespread activation of the clotting cascade can lead to both thrombosis and severe bleeding.
  • Preterm birth: APH is a leading cause of iatrogenic and spontaneous preterm delivery, increasing risks of neonatal morbidity and mortality.
  • Fetal hypoxia/asphyxia: Reduced uteroplacental perfusion due to haemorrhage can lead to fetal distress, neurological damage, or intrauterine death, especially if unmanaged for several hours.
  • Postpartum haemorrhage: Women with APH are at increased risk of heavy bleeding after delivery due to uterine atony or retained placental tissue.
  • Maternal psychological impact: The acute and potentially life-threatening nature of APH can lead to significant anxiety, stress, and post-traumatic stress disorder.
