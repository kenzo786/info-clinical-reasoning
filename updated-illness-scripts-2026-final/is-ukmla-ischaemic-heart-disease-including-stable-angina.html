Ischaemic heart disease (including stable angina) [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Ischaemic heart disease (including stable angina)"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Ischaemic heart disease (including stable angina)"][/sc]
  • Ischaemic heart disease, including stable angina, is characterised by chest pain or discomfort resulting from an insufficient blood supply to the myocardium, typically due to coronary artery disease.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical presentation and underlying pathophysiology of ischaemic heart disease, particularly stable angina, and its implications for patient management.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Ischaemic heart disease (including stable angina)"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Ischaemic heart disease (including stable angina)"][/sc]
  • In the UK, approximately 8% of men and 3% of women aged 55-64 years have, or have had, angina, with prevalence increasing with age.
  • People of South Asian origin in the UK face an increased risk of coronary heart disease, while black Afro-Caribbean individuals have a reduced risk compared to the overall UK population.
  • Key risk factors include family history, smoking, diabetes mellitus, metabolic syndrome, hyperlipidaemia, hypertension, obesity, lack of exercise, and cardiac abnormalities such as aortic stenosis.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Ischaemic heart disease (including stable angina)"][/sc]
  • Trigger: Increased myocardial oxygen demand, often due to physical exertion or emotional stress, in the presence of narrowed coronary arteries.
  • Progression: Atherosclerotic plaques in the coronary arteries restrict blood flow, leading to an imbalance between oxygen supply and demand.
  • Consequence: Myocardial ischaemia, manifesting as chest pain or discomfort (angina), which is typically relieved by rest or nitrates.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Ischaemic heart disease (including stable angina)"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Anginal symptoms typically last for a few minutes, usually less than 10, and are promptly relieved by rest or sublingual glyceryl trinitrate (GTN).
  • Progressive phase: Over time, the frequency, severity, or duration of anginal episodes may increase, or they may occur with less exertion, indicating potential worsening of the underlying coronary artery disease.
  • Late phase: Persistent or worsening symptoms, particularly beyond several weeks, despite optimal medical therapy, may suggest unstable angina or other acute coronary syndromes, requiring urgent reassessment.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Ischaemic heart disease (including stable angina)"][/sc]
  • Ask about chest pain/discomfort - typically constricting, heavy, or tight, often radiating to the left arm, jaw, neck, or back.
  • Ask about precipitation by exertion or emotional stress - a key feature distinguishing stable angina.
  • Ask about relief by rest or glyceryl trinitrate (GTN) - usually within minutes, indicating reversible ischaemia.
  • Ask about associated symptoms - such as breathlessness, nausea, sweating, or fatigue, which may accompany anginal episodes.
  • Ask about duration - typically short-lived (less than 10 minutes), differentiating it from prolonged pain of myocardial infarction.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Ischaemic heart disease (including stable angina)"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - blood pressure and heart rate may be normal or elevated during an anginal episode; assess for signs of heart failure (e.g., elevated JVP, peripheral oedema).
  • Check for heart murmurs - particularly aortic stenosis, which can exacerbate myocardial ischaemia.
  • Check peripheral pulses - to assess for generalised atherosclerosis.
  • Check for xanthomata or xanthelasma - signs of hyperlipidaemia.
  • Check for signs of anaemia or thyroid dysfunction - conditions that can worsen angina.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Ischaemic heart disease (including stable angina)"][/sc]
  • New-onset angina or abrupt deterioration: Suggests unstable angina or acute coronary syndrome, requiring urgent assessment.
  • Angina at rest or with minimal exertion: Indicates severe coronary artery disease and increased risk of myocardial infarction.
  • Angina unresponsive to GTN: May signify prolonged ischaemia or myocardial infarction.
  • Associated symptoms of acute coronary syndrome: Such as severe breathlessness, profuse sweating, or syncope, warrant immediate medical attention.
  • Haemodynamic instability: Hypotension, tachycardia, or signs of cardiogenic shock are critical red flags.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Ischaemic heart disease (including stable angina)"][/sc]
  • Older adults: May present with atypical symptoms such as breathlessness, fatigue, or syncope, rather than classic chest pain, making diagnosis challenging.
  • Women: Often experience non-chest pain symptoms like nausea, indigestion, back pain, or extreme fatigue, which can be misattributed.
  • Diabetic patients: May have 'silent ischaemia' due to autonomic neuropathy, presenting with minimal or no chest pain, increasing the risk of delayed diagnosis.
  • Immunosuppressed patients: May have altered pain perception or atypical inflammatory responses, leading to unusual presentations.
  • Patients with psychiatric conditions: Chest pain may be attributed to anxiety or panic attacks, delaying appropriate cardiac evaluation.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Ischaemic heart disease (including stable angina)"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected ischaemic heart disease: 12-lead ECG (may show ischaemic changes, but normal ECG does not rule out diagnosis), FBC (to exclude anaemia), renal function and electrolytes, fasting blood glucose, fasting lipid profile, TFTs.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Consider chest X-ray (for pulmonary causes), D-dimer (for pulmonary embolism), upper GI endoscopy (for gastro-oesophageal reflux).
  • Imaging - CT coronary angiography: Recommended for patients with suspected stable angina and an estimated likelihood of CAD between 10-90% to assess for coronary artery disease.
  • Imaging - Stress echocardiography/MRI/nuclear perfusion scans: Used to assess for inducible ischaemia in patients where CTCA is inconclusive or contraindicated.
  • Validated score: Diamond-Forrester Risk Score on MDCalc (for pre-test probability of CAD).
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Ischaemic heart disease (including stable angina)"][/sc]
  • A 65-year-old male presents with a 3-month history of central chest tightness, occurring predictably on exertion (e.g., walking uphill) and relieved by rest within 5 minutes. He has a history of hypertension and hyperlipidaemia.
  • An elderly male with multiple cardiovascular risk factors presenting with exertional, reproducible, and transient chest discomfort, consistent with stable angina.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Ischaemic heart disease (including stable angina)"][/sc]
  • Acute Coronary Syndrome (ACS) - pain is prolonged, not relieved by rest/GTN, or new-onset/worsening angina.
  • Gastro-oesophageal reflux disease (GORD) - burning pain, often post-prandial or nocturnal, relieved by antacids.
  • Musculoskeletal chest pain - pleuritic, localised tenderness, reproducible with movement/palpation.
  • Pericarditis - sharp, pleuritic pain, relieved by leaning forward, often preceded by viral illness.
  • Aortic dissection - sudden onset, severe, tearing pain radiating to the back.
  • Anxiety/Panic attack - associated with psychological symptoms, hyperventilation, and often atypical chest discomfort.
  • Pulmonary embolism - sudden onset pleuritic chest pain, dyspnoea, often with risk factors for VTE.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Ischaemic heart disease (including stable angina)"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Provide sublingual glyceryl trinitrate (GTN) for symptom relief. Advise rest.
  • Definitive management: Lifestyle modifications (smoking cessation, diet, exercise), management of cardiovascular risk factors (hypertension, hyperlipidaemia, diabetes). Antiplatelet therapy (e.g., aspirin), beta-blockers or calcium-channel blockers as first-line anti-anginal agents.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Regular clinical review to assess symptom control, medication adherence, and risk factor management. Consider exercise tolerance testing or further imaging if symptoms worsen.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Advise patients to seek urgent medical attention if angina symptoms change (e.g., increased frequency, severity, or occurrence at rest), are not relieved by GTN, or are associated with new symptoms.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Ischaemic heart disease (including stable angina)"][/sc]
  • Acute Coronary Syndrome (ACS): Progression of stable angina to unstable angina, myocardial infarction, or sudden cardiac death due to plaque rupture and thrombus formation.
  • Heart Failure: Chronic myocardial ischaemia can lead to impaired left ventricular function and subsequent heart failure.
  • Arrhythmias: Ischaemia can irritate myocardial tissue, leading to various cardiac arrhythmias, including ventricular tachycardia or fibrillation.
  • Cardiogenic Shock: Severe myocardial damage from extensive ischaemia or infarction can result in profound circulatory collapse.
  • Recurrent Angina: Despite treatment, some patients may experience persistent or recurrent anginal symptoms, impacting quality of life.
