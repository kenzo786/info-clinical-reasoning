Aortic valve disease [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Aortic valve disease"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Aortic valve disease"][/sc]
  • Aortic valve disease encompasses conditions affecting the aortic valve, primarily **aortic stenosis (AS)**, a narrowing of the valve, and **aortic regurgitation (AR)**, a leakage of the valve, both impairing efficient blood flow from the left ventricle to the aorta.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical features, investigate, and understand the management principles of aortic stenosis and regurgitation, including their impact on cardiac function and patient prognosis.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Aortic valve disease"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Aortic valve disease"][/sc]
  • Aortic stenosis is the most common valvular heart disease in the UK, with prevalence increasing significantly with age, affecting approximately 2-4% of the population over 65 years and up to 10% of those over 80 years [NICE NG208].
  • Calcific degeneration is the leading cause of AS in developed countries, often associated with risk factors such as hypertension, hyperlipidaemia, diabetes, and chronic kidney disease. Bicuspid aortic valve, a congenital anomaly, is present in 1-2% of the general population and is a significant cause of AS in younger individuals.
  • Aortic regurgitation prevalence is lower than AS, often resulting from aortic root dilation (e.g., hypertension, Marfan syndrome) or primary valve leaflet pathology (e.g., endocarditis, rheumatic heart disease).
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Aortic valve disease"][/sc]
  • In **aortic stenosis**, chronic pressure overload on the left ventricle (LV) due to outflow obstruction leads to compensatory LV hypertrophy, aiming to maintain cardiac output.
  • Persistent hypertrophy eventually results in myocardial fibrosis, diastolic dysfunction, and impaired coronary blood flow, leading to symptoms like angina and syncope. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Left ventricular hypertrophy in AS: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Left ventricular hypertrophy"][/sc]
  • In **aortic regurgitation**, volume overload on the LV due to backflow of blood from the aorta causes LV dilation and eccentric hypertrophy, initially maintaining stroke volume but eventually leading to LV dysfunction and heart failure. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Volume overload in AR: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Volume overload"][/sc]
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Aortic valve disease"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Aortic valve disease often remains asymptomatic for many years, with progression typically slow, particularly in calcific aortic stenosis, where symptoms may not manifest until severe disease is present.
  • Progressive phase: As the disease advances, compensatory mechanisms begin to fail, leading to the gradual onset of symptoms such as exertional dyspnoea, angina, or syncope, which indicate a critical stage of the disease.
  • Late phase: Once symptoms develop, the prognosis significantly worsens without intervention, with rapid deterioration into heart failure, arrhythmias, and increased mortality, often within 2-5 years.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Aortic valve disease"][/sc]
  • Ask about exertional dyspnoea - often the earliest symptom, indicating reduced cardiac reserve and left ventricular dysfunction.
  • Ask about angina - due to increased myocardial oxygen demand from left ventricular hypertrophy and reduced coronary perfusion, even in the absence of coronary artery disease.
  • Ask about syncope or pre-syncope - typically exertional, caused by transient cerebral hypoperfusion due to fixed cardiac output unable to meet increased peripheral demand, or arrhythmias.
  • Ask about palpitations - may indicate arrhythmias, particularly atrial fibrillation, which can significantly worsen haemodynamics in both AS and AR.
  • Ask about fatigue and reduced exercise tolerance - non-specific but common, reflecting overall reduced cardiac output and systemic hypoperfusion.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Aortic valve disease"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - may reveal narrow pulse pressure in severe AS, or wide pulse pressure in severe AR. Tachycardia can exacerbate symptoms.
  • Check character of pulse - slow-rising and small volume (pulsus parvus et tardus) in severe AS; collapsing (water-hammer) pulse in severe AR. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Pulsus parvus et tardus: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Pulsus parvus et tardus"][/sc]
  • Check cardiac auscultation - **Aortic Stenosis:** harsh ejection systolic murmur, crescendo-decrescendo, loudest at the right upper sternal edge, radiating to the carotids, often with a soft or absent S2. **Aortic Regurgitation:** early diastolic murmur, decrescendo, loudest at the left sternal edge (often third intercostal space), best heard with patient leaning forward and in expiration.
  • Check apex beat - displaced and heaving in severe AS (pressure overload hypertrophy); displaced and thrusting in severe AR (volume overload hypertrophy).
  • Check signs of heart failure - peripheral oedema, raised JVP, hepatomegaly, pulmonary crepitations, indicating decompensation.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Aortic valve disease"][/sc]
  • Exertional syncope: Indicates critical aortic stenosis with inadequate cerebral perfusion during increased demand, portending a very poor prognosis without urgent intervention.
  • Angina at rest: Suggests severe myocardial ischaemia due to critical AS or AR, requiring immediate assessment and management.
  • Rapidly worsening dyspnoea: Points to acute left ventricular decompensation or acute pulmonary oedema, often requiring emergency hospital admission.
  • Signs of acute heart failure: Such as new onset peripheral oedema, significantly raised JVP, or acute pulmonary crepitations, indicating cardiac decompensation requiring urgent medical attention.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Aortic valve disease"][/sc]
  • Older adults: May present with non-specific symptoms like fatigue, reduced mobility, or falls, rather than classic angina or syncope, making diagnosis challenging.
  • Low-gradient aortic stenosis: Patients with severe AS but a low transvalvular gradient due to poor LV function may have less prominent murmurs and require dobutamine stress echocardiography for accurate assessment. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Low-gradient AS: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Low-gradient AS"][/sc]
  • Aortic regurgitation with preserved LV function: Can remain asymptomatic for prolonged periods despite severe regurgitation, with symptoms only appearing late in the disease course.
  • Pregnancy: Haemodynamic changes during pregnancy can exacerbate previously mild or moderate aortic valve disease, leading to earlier onset of symptoms or decompensation.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Aortic valve disease"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected aortic valve disease: Transthoracic echocardiography (TTE) is the primary diagnostic tool, assessing valve morphology, severity of stenosis/regurgitation, LV function, and pulmonary pressures.
  • Severity/risk tools: [sc name="jitl-query" Q="EuroSCORE II: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="EuroSCORE II"][/sc] can be used to assess surgical risk for intervention. EuroSCORE II on MDCalc
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: ECG to detect arrhythmias or LV hypertrophy; chest X-ray for cardiomegaly or pulmonary oedema.
  • Imaging - Transoesophageal echocardiography (TOE): Provides more detailed views of valve morphology, particularly useful for surgical planning or when TTE is inconclusive.
  • Imaging - Cardiac MRI: Offers comprehensive assessment of ventricular volumes, function, and myocardial fibrosis, especially in AR or when echocardiography is suboptimal.
  • Imaging - Cardiac CT: Useful for assessing aortic valve calcification in AS, and for pre-procedural planning for transcatheter aortic valve implantation (TAVI).
  • Serial reassessment: Regular echocardiographic follow-up is crucial to monitor disease progression and timing of intervention, particularly in asymptomatic patients.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Aortic valve disease"][/sc]
  • A 72-year-old male presents with a 6-month history of progressive exertional dyspnoea, occasional dizziness, and a harsh ejection systolic murmur radiating to the carotids.
  • Elderly male with progressive exertional symptoms and classic auscultatory findings, concerning for severe aortic stenosis.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Aortic valve disease"][/sc]
  • Mitral regurgitation - typically presents with a pansystolic murmur loudest at the apex, radiating to the axilla, and often associated with left atrial enlargement.
  • Hypertrophic cardiomyopathy - can cause dynamic outflow tract obstruction mimicking AS, but often has a family history, prominent S4, and characteristic ECG changes. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Hypertrophic cardiomyopathy vs AS: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Hypertrophic cardiomyopathy"][/sc]
  • Angina pectoris (due to coronary artery disease) - symptoms of chest pain on exertion, but typically without the characteristic murmurs or signs of valvular disease.
  • Heart failure with preserved ejection fraction (HFpEF) - presents with dyspnoea and signs of fluid overload, but echocardiography shows normal or near-normal LV ejection fraction and evidence of diastolic dysfunction without significant valvular pathology.
  • Anaemia - can cause exertional dyspnoea and fatigue, and may exacerbate underlying cardiac conditions, but a full blood count would reveal low haemoglobin.
  • Pulmonary hypertension - presents with dyspnoea and signs of right heart strain, but echocardiography would show elevated pulmonary artery pressures and normal aortic valve function.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Aortic valve disease"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Recognition of symptomatic severe disease, particularly with red flag features, warrants urgent referral to a specialist cardiology team for assessment and consideration of intervention. Supportive care for heart failure symptoms may be initiated.
  • Definitive management: For symptomatic severe aortic stenosis, intervention (surgical aortic valve replacement [SAVR] or transcatheter aortic valve implantation [TAVI]) is indicated. For severe aortic regurgitation, SAVR is the primary treatment. The choice between SAVR and TAVI depends on patient age, comorbidities, and surgical risk profile.
  • Adjunctive management: Management of cardiovascular risk factors (hypertension, hyperlipidaemia, diabetes) is important. Diuretics may be considered for symptomatic relief of fluid overload in heart failure.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Regular clinical review and echocardiography are essential to monitor disease progression, left ventricular function, and the effectiveness of interventions.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Patients should be advised on symptoms of worsening disease (e.g., increasing dyspnoea, new-onset syncope, angina at rest) and when to seek urgent medical attention.
  • Exam pharmacology reference (UKMLA/MRCGP): Beta-blockers may be considered for rate control in atrial fibrillation in patients with aortic valve disease, but should be used with caution in severe aortic stenosis due to potential for reduced cardiac output. ACE inhibitors/ARBs may be used for hypertension or heart failure with reduced ejection fraction, but careful monitoring is required. Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Aortic valve disease"][/sc]
  • Heart failure: Both AS and AR can lead to progressive left ventricular dysfunction and eventual heart failure due to chronic pressure or volume overload, manifesting as dyspnoea, oedema, and fatigue in the late phase.
  • Arrhythmias: Atrial fibrillation is common, particularly in advanced disease, and can significantly worsen haemodynamics and increase the risk of stroke. Ventricular arrhythmias may also occur.
  • Sudden cardiac death: A significant risk in symptomatic severe aortic stenosis, often due to ventricular arrhythmias or acute myocardial ischaemia, particularly during exertion.
  • Endocarditis: Abnormal aortic valves are susceptible to bacterial endocarditis, which can lead to further valve damage, embolisation, and systemic infection.
  • Aortic dissection (in AR): In cases of aortic regurgitation due to aortic root dilation (e.g., Marfan syndrome), there is an increased risk of aortic dissection, a life-threatening emergency.
