Urticaria [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Urticaria"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Urticaria"][/sc]
  • Urticaria is a common skin condition characterised by the sudden appearance of itchy, raised weals (hives) that can be round, ring-shaped, or confluent, often accompanied by angioedema.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise and differentiate acute and chronic urticaria, identify associated angioedema, and understand the implications of hereditary angioedema.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Urticaria"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Urticaria"][/sc]
  • Urticaria affects up to 20% of people at some point in their lives, with acute forms being more common than chronic.
  • Chronic urticaria, defined as symptoms lasting six weeks or more, affects approximately 0.5-1% of the population in the UK.
  • Hereditary angioedema is a rare genetic condition, affecting between 1 in 20,000 and 1 in 50,000 individuals.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Urticaria"][/sc]
  • Trigger: Urticaria results from the release of histamine and other vasoactive mediators from mast cells and basophils in the skin, leading to vasodilation and increased vascular permeability. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Mast cell degranulation: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Mast cell degranulation"][/sc] is the core process.
  • Progression: This release can be triggered by various factors including allergic reactions (IgE-mediated), non-allergic mechanisms (e.g., direct mast cell activation by drugs, physical stimuli), or autoimmune processes.
  • Consequence: The mediators cause characteristic weals (superficial dermal oedema) and/or angioedema (deeper dermal and subcutaneous oedema). [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Hereditary angioedema mechanism: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Hereditary angioedema"][/sc] is caused by a deficiency or dysfunction of C1-esterase inhibitor, leading to uncontrolled bradykinin production.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Urticaria"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Acute urticaria typically manifests with weals appearing rapidly, often within minutes to hours of exposure to a trigger, and individual lesions usually resolve within 24 hours.
  • Progressive phase: In acute cases, episodes generally resolve completely within 6 weeks. Chronic urticaria, however, persists for more than 6 weeks, with daily or almost daily occurrence of weals and/or angioedema.
  • Late phase: Chronic urticaria can be unpredictable, with symptoms lasting months to years, often fluctuating in severity. Hereditary angioedema attacks can last several days if untreated.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Urticaria"][/sc]
  • Ask about pruritus (itch) - it is the cardinal symptom of urticaria, often severe and disruptive to sleep and daily activities.
  • Ask about weals (hives) - these are transient, erythematous, raised lesions with central pallor, which blanch on pressure and typically resolve within 24 hours without scarring.
  • Ask about angioedema - this presents as deeper, non-pitting swelling, often affecting the face (lips, eyelids), tongue, throat, or extremities, and is usually painful or burning rather than itchy.
  • Ask about triggers - identifying specific triggers (e.g., foods, drugs, physical stimuli, infections) can help differentiate inducible or allergic urticaria from spontaneous forms.
  • Ask about systemic symptoms - fever, malaise, or joint pain may suggest urticarial vasculitis or an underlying systemic condition rather than simple urticaria.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Urticaria"][/sc]
  • Check for weals - observe for characteristic erythematous, oedematous plaques of varying size and shape, which are migratory and evanescent.
  • Check for angioedema - inspect for localised, non-pitting swelling, particularly around the eyes, lips, and tongue, which may be asymmetric.
  • Check for dermographism - gently stroke the skin with a blunt object; a linear weal appearing within minutes suggests inducible urticaria.
  • Check vital signs - assess for signs of anaphylaxis (e.g., hypotension, tachycardia, respiratory distress) if angioedema is severe or rapidly progressing, especially involving the airway.
  • Check for signs of chronicity or systemic involvement - persistent skin changes, bruising, or signs of underlying autoimmune disease may warrant further investigation. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Urticarial vasculitis: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Urticarial vasculitis"][/sc] presents with weals lasting >24 hours, often painful or burning, and may leave residual bruising.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Urticaria"][/sc]
  • Airway compromise: Rapidly progressing angioedema involving the tongue, pharynx, or larynx indicates potential airway obstruction and requires immediate emergency assessment.
  • Systemic symptoms: Fever, arthralgia, significant malaise, or weight loss accompanying urticaria may suggest an underlying systemic inflammatory condition or urticarial vasculitis.
  • Weals lasting >24 hours: Individual weals persisting beyond 24 hours, especially if painful, burning, or leaving residual pigmentation/bruising, are indicative of urticarial vasculitis, requiring biopsy for confirmation.
  • Lack of response to antihistamines: Urticaria unresponsive to standard doses of H1-antihistamines, particularly if severe, warrants specialist referral to exclude alternative diagnoses or consider advanced therapies.
  • New onset angioedema on ACE inhibitor: Angioedema developing in a patient taking an ACE inhibitor requires immediate cessation of the drug due to the risk of severe, potentially life-threatening, bradykinin-mediated angioedema.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Urticaria"][/sc]
  • Paediatric population: In young children, acute urticaria is often triggered by viral infections or food allergies, and presentation may be less typical, with widespread, confluent weals.
  • Older adults: Chronic urticaria in older adults may be associated with underlying systemic diseases, malignancies, or drug reactions, requiring a thorough diagnostic work-up.
  • Angioedema without weals: Isolated angioedema, particularly if recurrent and not associated with weals, should prompt investigation for hereditary angioedema or ACE inhibitor-induced angioedema. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Bradykinin-mediated angioedema: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Bradykinin-mediated angioedema"][/sc] is distinct from histamine-mediated urticaria and requires different management.
  • Physical urticarias: Weals triggered by specific physical stimuli (e.g., cold, pressure, heat, sunlight, vibration) represent inducible urticaria, which can be challenging to diagnose and manage if not specifically sought.
  • Urticaria in pregnancy: Management of urticaria during pregnancy requires careful consideration of drug safety, with non-sedating H1-antihistamines generally considered safe, but specialist advice may be needed for severe cases.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Urticaria"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected urticaria: Diagnosis is primarily clinical, based on history and examination. Routine investigations are generally not required for acute urticaria.
  • If chronic urticaria or atypical features:
    • Blood tests: Full blood count, erythrocyte sedimentation rate (ESR) or C-reactive protein (CRP), liver and renal function tests, thyroid function tests, and autoantibodies (e.g., antinuclear antibodies) may be considered to exclude underlying systemic disease.
    • Specific allergy tests: Skin prick tests or specific IgE blood tests may be indicated if a clear allergic trigger is suspected, particularly in acute urticaria.
    • C1-esterase inhibitor levels: Measurement of C4, C1-esterase inhibitor (C1-INH) quantitative and functional levels is crucial to diagnose hereditary angioedema (HAE) or acquired C1-INH deficiency. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="C1-esterase inhibitor deficiency: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="C1-esterase inhibitor deficiency"][/sc] leads to uncontrolled complement activation and bradykinin generation.
  • Severity/risk tools: The Urticaria Activity Score (UAS7) on MDCalc can be used to objectively assess disease activity and response to treatment in chronic urticaria.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Skin biopsy may be required to differentiate urticaria from urticarial vasculitis or other inflammatory dermatoses.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Urticaria"][/sc]
  • A 35-year-old female presents with a 3-month history of daily, intensely itchy, migratory weals and intermittent lip swelling, significantly impacting her sleep and quality of life.
  • Chronic spontaneous urticaria with angioedema, concerning for autoimmune aetiology or idiopathic origin, requiring stepwise management.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Urticaria"][/sc]
  • Urticarial vasculitis - weals persist for >24 hours, are often painful/burning, and may leave bruising or hyperpigmentation.
  • Maculopapular drug eruption - typically morbilliform rash, often itchy, but lesions are fixed and do not migrate or resolve within 24 hours.
  • Erythema multiforme - target lesions with three concentric zones, often precipitated by infections (e.g., HSV) or drugs, not migratory weals.
  • Contact dermatitis - localised eczematous rash with pruritus, erythema, and vesicles, occurring at the site of contact with an allergen or irritant, not transient weals.
  • Mastocytosis (urticaria pigmentosa) - persistent pigmented macules or papules that urticate when rubbed (Darier\'s sign), due to abnormal mast cell proliferation.
  • Angioedema (isolated) - swelling without associated weals, potentially hereditary (C1-INH deficiency) or drug-induced (ACE inhibitors). [sc name="jitl-query" Q="ACE inhibitor-induced angioedema: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="ACE inhibitor-induced angioedema"][/sc] is a life-threatening cause of airway obstruction.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Urticaria"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Assess airway patency in angioedema; consider adrenaline for anaphylaxis. Remove identified triggers where possible.
  • Definitive management:
    • Acute urticaria: Often includes non-sedating H1-antihistamines (e.g., cetirizine, loratadine) as first-line. A short course of oral corticosteroids may be considered for severe acute exacerbations.
    • Chronic urticaria: Stepwise approach starting with regular non-sedating H1-antihistamines, with dosage increased up to fourfold if symptoms persist.
    • Hereditary angioedema: Acute attacks may require C1-INH concentrate, icatibant, or ecallantide. Long-term prophylaxis may involve C1-INH concentrate or lanadelumab.
  • Adjunctive management: Avoiding exacerbating factors such as NSAIDs, alcohol, and stress may be beneficial.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Regular review to assess symptom control, treatment adherence, and side effects. Specialist referral for chronic urticaria unresponsive to high-dose antihistamines or for suspected hereditary angioedema.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Patients with recurrent angioedema, especially involving the airway, should be educated on emergency action plans and may require access to self-administered emergency medication.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Urticaria"][/sc]
  • Airway obstruction: Severe angioedema of the larynx or pharynx can lead to life-threatening airway compromise, requiring urgent medical intervention.
  • Anaphylaxis: Although rare, urticaria can be a symptom of anaphylaxis, a severe, systemic allergic reaction requiring immediate emergency treatment.
  • Reduced quality of life: Chronic pruritus, visible weals, and recurrent angioedema can significantly impair sleep, psychological well-being, and social functioning.
  • Urticarial vasculitis: Persistent weals (>24 hours) with pain, burning, or bruising may indicate underlying vasculitis, which can be associated with systemic inflammation and organ involvement.
  • Psychological impact: The unpredictable nature and visible symptoms of chronic urticaria can lead to anxiety, depression, and social isolation.
