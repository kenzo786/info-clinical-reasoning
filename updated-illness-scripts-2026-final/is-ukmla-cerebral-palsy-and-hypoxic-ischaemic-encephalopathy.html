Cerebral palsy and hypoxic-ischaemic encephalopathy [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Cerebral palsy and hypoxic-ischaemic encephalopathy"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Cerebral palsy and hypoxic-ischaemic encephalopathy"][/sc]
  • Cerebral palsy (CP) is an umbrella term for a group of permanent movement and posture disorders of the developing fetal or infant brain which limit activity, resulting from non-progressive damage to the developing brain before, during, or shortly after birth. Hypoxic-ischaemic encephalopathy (HIE) is a type of brain injury that occurs when a baby's brain does not receive enough oxygen and/or blood flow around the time of birth, and is a significant cause of CP.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical features of cerebral palsy and hypoxic-ischaemic encephalopathy, understand their aetiology, and outline the principles of their management.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Cerebral palsy and hypoxic-ischaemic encephalopathy"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Cerebral palsy and hypoxic-ischaemic encephalopathy"][/sc]
  • Cerebral palsy affects approximately 1 in 400 children in the UK, with a birth prevalence of around 2 in every 1,000 live births.
  • Hypoxic-ischaemic encephalopathy (HIE) occurs in 1.2–3.0 per 1000 live births in the United Kingdom, and remains a leading cause of neonatal mortality and neurodisability.
  • Risk factors for CP include prematurity, low birth weight, multiple births, infections during pregnancy, and birth complications such as HIE.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Cerebral palsy and hypoxic-ischaemic encephalopathy"][/sc]
  • Perinatal hypoxia-ischaemia, often due to complications during labour and delivery, leads to a reduction in oxygen and blood flow to the fetal or neonatal brain.
  • This initial insult triggers a cascade of cellular events, including energy failure, excitotoxicity, inflammation, and apoptosis, leading to neuronal cell death and brain injury. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Excitotoxicity in HIE: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Excitotoxicity"]
  • The resulting non-progressive brain damage, particularly to motor control centres, manifests as the diverse range of movement and posture disorders characteristic of cerebral palsy.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Cerebral palsy and hypoxic-ischaemic encephalopathy"][/sc]
  • Early phase: The acute phase of HIE immediately following the hypoxic-ischaemic event, typically within the first 6-12 hours, is characterised by primary energy failure and neuronal damage.
  • Progressive phase: Over the next 24-72 hours, secondary energy failure and reperfusion injury can exacerbate brain damage, particularly beyond 6 hours post-insult, leading to evolving neurological signs.
  • Late phase: The long-term consequences of the brain injury, including the development of cerebral palsy, become apparent over months to years, with motor and cognitive deficits becoming more defined as the child develops.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Cerebral palsy and hypoxic-ischaemic encephalopathy"][/sc]
  • Ask about developmental delay - often the first indicator, particularly delays in achieving motor milestones such as sitting, crawling, or walking.
  • Ask about abnormal muscle tone - may present as hypotonia (floppiness) in infancy, evolving to hypertonia (stiffness) or spasticity, which can affect movement and posture.
  • Ask about feeding difficulties - poor suck/swallow reflex in infants with HIE, or ongoing feeding issues in children with CP due to oral motor dysfunction.
  • Ask about seizures - can occur in the acute phase of HIE and may be a feature of CP, indicating ongoing neurological irritability.
  • Ask about persistent primitive reflexes - such as the Moro or asymmetric tonic neck reflex, which normally disappear in early infancy, indicating delayed neurological maturation.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Cerebral palsy and hypoxic-ischaemic encephalopathy"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - in acute HIE, may show signs of cardiorespiratory instability; in CP, vitals are typically normal unless there are comorbidities.
  • Check muscle tone - can range from hypotonia to hypertonia, spasticity (increased resistance to passive movement, velocity-dependent), dystonia (involuntary sustained muscle contractions), or ataxia (impaired coordination). [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Spasticity vs Dystonia in CP: what is the difference and why does it matter clinically?" text="Spasticity"]
  • Check reflexes - hyperreflexia, clonus, and extensor plantar responses are common in spastic CP.
  • Check posture and movement patterns - abnormal postures, asymmetric movements, or a preference for one side of the body can indicate CP.
  • Check for microcephaly - may be present if there has been significant brain injury or abnormal brain development.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Cerebral palsy and hypoxic-ischaemic encephalopathy"][/sc]
  • Acute neurological deterioration: Suggests an evolving or new neurological insult, requiring urgent reassessment and investigation, particularly within the first 72 hours post-HIE.
  • Uncontrolled seizures: Indicates significant neurological irritability and potential for ongoing brain injury, necessitating prompt anticonvulsant management.
  • Failure to meet developmental milestones: Persistent and significant delays in motor, cognitive, or communication skills beyond expected age ranges warrant specialist paediatric assessment.
  • Progressive motor weakness: While CP is non-progressive, new or worsening weakness should prompt investigation for alternative or co-existing neurological conditions.
  • Signs of increased intracranial pressure: Such as bulging fontanelle, persistent vomiting, or altered consciousness, indicating potential complications like hydrocephalus or intracranial haemorrhage.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Cerebral palsy and hypoxic-ischaemic encephalopathy"][/sc]
  • Mild HIE: Infants may initially appear well or have subtle neurological signs, making early diagnosis challenging, but can still develop long-term neurodevelopmental issues.
  • Premature infants: May present with a more fluctuating course, and their immature brains are particularly vulnerable to white matter injury, leading to periventricular leukomalacia, a common cause of spastic diplegia. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Periventricular leukomalacia: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Periventricular leukomalacia"]
  • Older children with CP: May present with secondary complications such as orthopaedic deformities, pain, or communication difficulties, rather than primary motor symptoms.
  • Associated comorbidities: CP often co-exists with intellectual disability, epilepsy, visual or hearing impairment, and speech difficulties, which can mask or alter the primary motor presentation.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Cerebral palsy and hypoxic-ischaemic encephalopathy"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected HIE: Blood gas analysis (umbilical cord or neonatal), lactate levels, full blood count, coagulation screen, and cranial ultrasound to assess for acute brain injury.
  • Severity/risk tools: The Sarnat staging system is used to classify the severity of HIE based on clinical signs, EEG findings, and duration of symptoms, guiding management decisions such as therapeutic hypothermia.   • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Metabolic screening, genetic testing, and cerebrospinal fluid analysis may be considered to rule out other causes of developmental delay or neurological impairment.
  • Imaging - MRI brain: The gold standard for assessing brain injury in HIE and for diagnosing CP, providing detailed information on the location and extent of brain damage.
  • Imaging - EEG: Electroencephalography is crucial in HIE to detect seizure activity and assess background brain activity, which helps in grading severity and guiding prognosis.
  • Serial reassessment: Regular developmental assessments (e.g., Bayley Scales of Infant Development) are essential to monitor progress and identify emerging deficits in children at risk of or diagnosed with CP.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Cerebral palsy and hypoxic-ischaemic encephalopathy"][/sc]
  • Genetic syndromes - often associated with dysmorphic features or specific organ involvement not typically seen in CP.
  • Metabolic disorders - may present with progressive neurological deterioration or specific biochemical abnormalities.
  • Neuromuscular disorders - such as muscular dystrophies or spinal muscular atrophy, typically involve progressive muscle weakness and fasciculations, rather than fixed motor deficits.
  • Acquired brain injury (post-neonatal) - e.g., head trauma, infection, or stroke occurring after the perinatal period, which would alter the timing of onset.
  • Developmental coordination disorder - involves motor clumsiness without the underlying neurological signs of CP, such as abnormal tone or reflexes.
  • Autism spectrum disorder - primarily affects social communication and repetitive behaviours, though motor difficulties can co-exist, they are not the primary defining feature. Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Cerebral palsy and hypoxic-ischaemic encephalopathy"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities (for HIE): Stabilisation of cardiorespiratory function, seizure management, and consideration of therapeutic hypothermia for eligible infants within 6 hours of birth to mitigate brain injury.
  • Definitive management (for CP): A multidisciplinary approach focusing on optimising function and participation, often involving physiotherapy, occupational therapy, speech and language therapy, and educational support.
  • Adjunctive management: May include orthotics, assistive devices, nutritional support, and management of associated conditions such as epilepsy or gastro-oesophageal reflux.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Regular developmental surveillance, neurological assessments, and review by a paediatrician and specialist therapists to adapt interventions as the child grows.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Parents/carers should be advised on signs of worsening neurological status, uncontrolled seizures, or new medical concerns requiring urgent medical attention.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Cerebral palsy and hypoxic-ischaemic encephalopathy"][/sc]
  • Epilepsy: A common comorbidity in CP, resulting from the underlying brain injury, and can significantly impact quality of life and developmental progress, often emerging in the progressive or late phases.
  • Intellectual disability: Varies widely depending on the severity and location of brain damage, affecting learning and adaptive functioning, typically becoming apparent in the late phase as developmental milestones are missed.
  • Orthopaedic deformities: Such as hip dislocation, scoliosis, and contractures, develop over time due to abnormal muscle tone and growth, requiring ongoing orthopaedic management in the progressive and late phases.
  • Communication difficulties: Dysarthria or anarthria due to motor control issues affecting speech muscles, or broader language delays, impacting social interaction and learning, evident in the progressive and late phases.
  • Feeding and nutritional problems: Due to oral motor dysfunction, gastro-oesophageal reflux, or constipation, leading to poor weight gain and aspiration risk, often present from the early phase and persisting into the late phase.
  • Visual and hearing impairments: Can result from damage to sensory pathways or cortical areas, impacting development and requiring early detection and intervention, typically identified in the progressive phase.
