Lymphoma [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Lymphoma"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Lymphoma"][/sc]
  • Lymphoma is a heterogeneous group of cancers originating from lymphocytes, primarily affecting the lymphatic system but capable of involving other organs.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical features, initial investigations, and principles of management for common lymphomas, distinguishing between Hodgkin and non-Hodgkin types.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Lymphoma"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Lymphoma"][/sc]
  • Non-Hodgkin lymphoma (NHL) is more common than Hodgkin lymphoma (HL) in the UK, with around 14,000 new cases of NHL and 2,100 new cases of HL diagnosed annually (Cancer Research UK).
  • Incidence of NHL generally increases with age, with most cases diagnosed in individuals over 60, while HL has a bimodal age distribution, peaking in young adults (20s) and older adults (70s).
  • Risk factors include immunosuppression (e.g., HIV, organ transplant recipients), certain infections (e.g., Epstein-Barr virus, Helicobacter pylori), autoimmune conditions, and exposure to some pesticides or chemicals.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Lymphoma"][/sc]
  • Trigger: Genetic mutations or epigenetic changes within lymphocytes (B-cells, T-cells, or NK-cells) lead to uncontrolled proliferation and impaired apoptosis.
  • Progression: Malignant lymphocytes accumulate, forming tumours primarily in lymph nodes, spleen, and bone marrow, but can disseminate to any organ.
  • Consequence: Disruption of normal immune function, organ infiltration, and systemic symptoms (B symptoms) due to cytokine release.
