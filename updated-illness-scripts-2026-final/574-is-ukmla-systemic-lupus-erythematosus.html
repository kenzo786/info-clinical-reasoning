Systemic lupus erythematosus [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Systemic lupus erythematosus"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Systemic lupus erythematosus"][/sc]
  • Systemic lupus erythematosus (SLE) is a chronic, multisystem autoimmune disease characterized by inflammation and tissue damage in various organs, resulting from the immune system attacking its own healthy tissues.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: The candidate must recognise the multisystemic nature and autoimmune aetiology of Systemic Lupus Erythematosus, and its potential for widespread inflammation and organ damage.

Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Systemic lupus erythematosus"][/sc]
  • Trigger: A combination of genetic predisposition and environmental factors (e.g., UV light, certain infections, drugs) leads to immune dysregulation and the breakdown of self-tolerance.
  • Progression: This dysregulation results in the overproduction of autoantibodies, particularly antinuclear antibodies (ANAs), and the formation of immune complexes. These complexes deposit in various tissues, activating the complement system and recruiting inflammatory cells.
  • Consequence: Chronic inflammation and immune-mediated damage to organs and tissues throughout the body, leading to the diverse clinical manifestations of SLE. Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Systemic lupus erythematosus"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Initial symptoms often emerge between the teen years and 30s, presenting variably as mild, severe, sporadic, or continual. Diagnosis can be delayed, with progression from initial symptoms to established SLE taking several years in some cases.
  • Progressive phase: SLE is characterized by a relapsing-remitting course with unpredictable flares and periods of remission. Disease activity can lead to widespread inflammation and immune-mediated damage affecting various organs, often within the initial 3-5 years of disease onset, particularly beyond the first few months.
  • Late phase: Chronic inflammation and cumulative organ damage can lead to significant morbidity and long-term complications, including kidney failure, cardiovascular disease, and neurological issues. Flares can still occur even after prolonged remission, particularly beyond several years without active disease.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Systemic lupus erythematosus"][/sc]
  • Ask about fatigue - Extreme and persistent fatigue is a hallmark of SLE and can be debilitating, often disproportionate to activity levels, impacting daily function.
  • Ask about joint pain and swelling (arthritis) - Typically symmetrical polyarthritis, often migratory and non-erosive, affecting small joints of the hands and feet. Important to differentiate from other inflammatory arthropathies.
  • Ask about skin rashes - Characteristic malar (butterfly) rash across the nose and cheeks, discoid lesions, or photosensitivity, which can trigger disease flares.
  • Ask about oral or nasal ulcers - Often painless, recurrent, and can be a subtle but important diagnostic clue for mucosal involvement.
  • Ask about Raynaud\'s phenomenon - Episodes of digital vasospasm in response to cold or stress, indicating potential systemic vascular involvement.
  • Ask about pleuritic chest pain - Sharp, localised chest pain exacerbated by deep breathing, suggestive of serositis (inflammation of the pleural lining).
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Systemic lupus erythematosus"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - Low-grade fever may indicate disease activity or infection; hypertension can be a sign of renal involvement (lupus nephritis).
  • Check for malar rash - A characteristic erythematous rash over the cheeks and nasal bridge, often sparing the nasolabial folds, is highly suggestive of SLE.
  • Check for discoid rash - Erythematous, raised patches with adherent scales and follicular plugging, leading to scarring and atrophy, indicative of chronic cutaneous lupus.
  • Check for joint swelling and tenderness - Objective evidence of arthritis, typically symmetrical polyarthritis, supporting the diagnosis of inflammatory arthropathy.
  • Check for peripheral oedema - May suggest renal involvement (nephrotic syndrome) or cardiac dysfunction. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Peripheral oedema in SLE: what causes it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Peripheral oedema"][/sc]
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Systemic lupus erythematosus"][/sc]
  • Unexplained fever: Persistent or recurrent fever without an obvious infectious cause may indicate active systemic inflammation or an impending flare.
  • New onset neurological symptoms: Seizures, psychosis, severe headaches, or focal neurological deficits can signify central nervous system involvement (neuropsychiatric lupus), requiring urgent assessment.
  • Rapidly progressive renal impairment: A sudden decline in kidney function (e.g., rising creatinine, proteinuria, haematuria) suggests severe lupus nephritis, which can lead to end-stage renal disease if not promptly treated.
  • Severe cytopenias: Profound anaemia, leukopenia, or thrombocytopenia can indicate significant haematological involvement and increase the risk of infection or bleeding.
  • Pleuritic chest pain with dyspnoea: Acute onset of chest pain exacerbated by breathing, accompanied by shortness of breath, may signal severe serositis (pleuritis or pericarditis) or pulmonary haemorrhage, requiring immediate investigation.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Systemic lupus erythematosus"][/sc]
  • Older adults: Late-onset SLE (after age 50) often presents with less classic features like malar rash, arthritis, and nephritis. Instead, it may manifest with more constitutional symptoms (e.g., fatigue, fever), pulmonary involvement (e.g., interstitial lung disease), and neurological symptoms, making diagnosis challenging.
  • Pregnancy: Physiological changes during pregnancy can mimic SLE symptoms (e.g., arthralgias, fatigue, rashes, swelling), making it difficult to distinguish between normal pregnancy symptoms and disease flares. Pregnant individuals with SLE are at higher risk for complications like preeclampsia and premature delivery.
  • Immunosuppressed patients: Patients on immunosuppressive therapy for SLE are at increased risk of opportunistic infections, which can present with symptoms that overlap with SLE flares, complicating diagnosis and management.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Systemic lupus erythematosus"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected Systemic Lupus Erythematosus:
    • Antinuclear antibodies (ANA): Positive in almost all patients with SLE (NPV >99%).
    • Anti-double-stranded DNA (anti-dsDNA) antibodies: Highly specific for SLE, often fluctuate with disease activity.
    • Complement levels (C3, C4): Often decreased during active disease due to immune complex consumption.
    • Erythrocyte sedimentation rate (ESR) and C-reactive protein (CRP): Elevated during inflammation, though CRP may be less elevated in SLE than in other inflammatory conditions.
    • Full blood count (FBC): May reveal cytopenias (anaemia, leukopenia, thrombocytopenia).
    • Urinalysis and renal function tests (U&Es): To screen for lupus nephritis (proteinuria, haematuria, elevated creatinine).
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Consider other autoantibody panels (e.g., anti-Ro, anti-La, anti-Sm, anti-RNP) to differentiate from other connective tissue diseases.
  • Imaging - various modalities:
    • Chest X-ray: To assess for pleuritis, pericarditis, or pulmonary involvement.
    • Ultrasound, MRI: Can be used for evaluating joint involvement, serositis, or specific organ damage.
  • Serial reassessment: Regular monitoring of blood and urine tests (e.g., FBC, U&Es, complement levels, anti-dsDNA, urinalysis) is crucial to assess disease activity, treatment response, and detect complications.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Systemic lupus erythematosus"][/sc]
  • Rheumatoid Arthritis - typically presents with more consistent, erosive polyarthritis and specific autoantibodies (anti-CCP, RF), often sparing other organ systems affected by SLE.
  • Sjögren\'s Syndrome - characterized by prominent sicca symptoms (dry eyes, dry mouth) due to exocrine gland dysfunction, though can overlap with SLE and share some autoantibodies.
  • Drug-induced Lupus Erythematosus - a history of exposure to specific medications (e.g., procainamide, hydralazine), with symptoms typically resolving upon drug cessation and often positive anti-histone antibodies.
  • Mixed Connective Tissue Disease - features an overlap of symptoms from SLE, systemic sclerosis, and polymyositis, distinguished by very high titres of anti-U1-RNP antibodies.
  • Fibromyalgia - presents with widespread chronic pain, fatigue, and tenderness, but lacks objective signs of inflammation, organ involvement, or the specific autoantibody profile seen in SLE.
  • Viral Arthritis - acute onset, self-limiting joint pain, often preceded by a viral prodrome, and absence of the characteristic autoantibodies and systemic features of SLE.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Systemic lupus erythematosus"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Address acute, life-threatening manifestations such as severe organ involvement (e.g., lupus nephritis, CNS lupus, severe cytopenias) with high-dose corticosteroids or immunosuppressants.
  • Definitive management:
    • Hydroxychloroquine: Considered foundational therapy for most SLE patients, helping to reduce flares and improve long-term outcomes.
    • Corticosteroids: Used to control disease flares and manage inflammation, typically tapered to the lowest effective dose.
    • Immunosuppressants (e.g., methotrexate, azathioprine, mycophenolate mofetil): Used for moderate to severe disease, particularly with organ involvement, to reduce corticosteroid dependence.
    • Biologics (e.g., belimumab, rituximab): Considered for refractory disease or specific manifestations.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Regular clinical assessment for disease activity and damage, alongside laboratory monitoring (e.g., FBC, U&Es, ESR, CRP, complement levels, anti-dsDNA, urinalysis) to detect flares, treatment response, and adverse effects.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Patients should be educated on recognising symptoms of flares and complications, with clear instructions on when to seek urgent medical attention. Escalation of therapy is guided by disease activity and organ involvement.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Systemic lupus erythematosus"][/sc]
  • Lupus Nephritis: Immune complex deposition in the kidneys leads to inflammation and damage, which can progress to chronic kidney disease and end-stage renal failure, significantly impacting prognosis. This often manifests in the progressive phase of the disease.
  • Cardiovascular Disease: Chronic inflammation and traditional risk factors contribute to accelerated atherosclerosis, increasing the risk of myocardial infarction, stroke, and peripheral vascular disease. This is a significant long-term complication.
  • Neuropsychiatric Lupus: Autoantibody-mediated damage and inflammation in the central nervous system can cause a wide range of manifestations, including seizures, psychosis, cognitive dysfunction, and stroke, often presenting as red flag features.
  • Infections: Both the disease itself and immunosuppressive treatments increase susceptibility to bacterial, viral, and fungal infections, which are a leading cause of morbidity and mortality in SLE patients.
  • Thromboembolic Events: The presence of antiphospholipid antibodies can lead to a prothrombotic state, increasing the risk of deep vein thrombosis, pulmonary embolism, and arterial thrombosis.
