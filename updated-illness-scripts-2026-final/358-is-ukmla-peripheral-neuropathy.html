Peripheral neuropathy (Peripheral nervous system and neuromuscular junction) [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Peripheral neuropathy"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Peripheral neuropathy"][/sc]
  • Peripheral neuropathy is a condition resulting from damage to the peripheral nerves, which transmit information between the brain and spinal cord and the rest of the body.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical features of peripheral neuropathy, differentiate between common causes, and understand the principles of investigation and management.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Peripheral neuropathy"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Peripheral neuropathy"][/sc]
  • In the UK, it is estimated that almost 1 in 10 people aged 55 or over are affected by peripheral neuropathy [1].
  • Diabetes (both type 1 and type 2) is the most common cause of peripheral neuropathy in the UK, with prevalence estimated between 20% to 30% in people with diabetes [1, 2].
  • Risk factors include diabetes, excessive alcohol consumption, vitamin B12 deficiency, hypothyroidism, certain medications, and autoimmune conditions.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Peripheral neuropathy"][/sc]
  • Damage to peripheral nerves can be triggered by metabolic derangements (e.g., hyperglycaemia in diabetes), toxic exposures (e.g., alcohol), nutritional deficiencies (e.g., B12), or immune-mediated processes.
  • This damage can progress to affect either the myelin sheath (demyelinating neuropathy) or the axon itself (axonal neuropathy), leading to impaired nerve conduction. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Axonal vs Demyelinating Neuropathy: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Axonal vs Demyelinating Neuropathy"][/sc]
  • The consequence is a disruption in the transmission of sensory, motor, or autonomic signals, resulting in a range of symptoms depending on the nerve fibres affected.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Peripheral neuropathy"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Symptoms often begin subtly and distally, typically in the feet, with gradual onset of numbness, tingling, or burning sensations over weeks to months.
  • Progressive phase: Symptoms may slowly spread proximally and affect the hands, with increasing severity of sensory loss, pain, and potentially muscle weakness over months to years.
  • Late phase: Established neuropathy can lead to chronic pain, significant sensory deficits, motor weakness, and autonomic dysfunction, with potential for complications such as foot ulcers and cardiovascular issues, particularly beyond several years if the underlying cause is not addressed.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Peripheral neuropathy"][/sc]
  • Ask about numbness and tingling - these are often the earliest and most common sensory symptoms, typically starting in a "glove and stocking" distribution.
  • Ask about burning, stabbing, or shooting pain - neuropathic pain can be a prominent and debilitating feature, distinct from nociceptive pain.
  • Ask about loss of balance or unsteadiness - this may indicate proprioceptive loss, increasing the risk of falls.
  • Ask about muscle weakness or difficulty with fine motor tasks - suggests motor nerve involvement, which can impact daily activities.
  • Ask about symptoms of autonomic dysfunction (e.g., postural dizziness, altered sweating, bowel/bladder changes) - indicates involvement of autonomic nerve fibres.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Peripheral neuropathy"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - particularly blood pressure for orthostatic hypotension, which may indicate autonomic neuropathy.
  • Check sensory modalities (light touch, pinprick, vibration, proprioception) - often reduced in a length-dependent pattern, starting distally.
  • Check muscle strength and tone - distal weakness (e.g., foot drop) is common, and muscle wasting may be present in chronic cases.
  • Check deep tendon reflexes - typically reduced or absent, especially ankle jerks, reflecting damage to the reflex arc.
  • Check for trophic changes (e.g., dry skin, hair loss, nail changes, foot ulcers) - indicative of chronic denervation and impaired autonomic function. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Trophic changes in neuropathy: what are they and why do they matter clinically?" text="Trophic changes"][/sc]
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Peripheral neuropathy"][/sc]
  • Rapidly progressive weakness: Suggests acute or subacute demyelinating conditions like Guillain-Barré syndrome, requiring urgent neurological assessment and potential immunotherapy.
  • Asymmetrical or focal deficits: May indicate mononeuropathy multiplex or a compressive neuropathy, necessitating targeted investigation.
  • Motor predominance with minimal sensory loss: Raises suspicion for motor neuron disease or multifocal motor neuropathy.
  • Autonomic instability: Severe orthostatic hypotension, gastroparesis, or cardiac arrhythmias can be life-threatening and require immediate management.
  • Associated systemic symptoms (e.g., fever, weight loss, rash): Points towards an underlying systemic inflammatory, infectious, or malignant cause that needs prompt diagnosis and treatment.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Peripheral neuropathy"][/sc]
  • Older adults: Polyneuropathy is common, with a significant proportion being idiopathic. Presentation may be subtle, leading to delayed diagnosis or attribution to normal ageing.
  • Diabetic patients: While typically length-dependent, some may develop acute painful neuropathies or mononeuropathies (e.g., third nerve palsy), requiring careful differentiation.
  • Critical illness neuropathy: Patients in intensive care may develop acute weakness and sensory loss, often axonal, complicating ventilator weaning and recovery.
  • Paraproteinaemic neuropathy: Can present with predominantly demyelinating features, mimicking CIDP, and requires investigation for underlying monoclonal gammopathy. Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Peripheral neuropathy"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected peripheral neuropathy:
    • Full blood count, electrolytes, renal and liver function tests.
    • HbA1c to screen for diabetes.
    • Thyroid function tests (TSH, free T4) to exclude hypothyroidism.
    • Vitamin B12 and folate levels to detect nutritional deficiencies.
    • Serum protein electrophoresis with immunofixation to screen for paraproteinaemia.
    • Nerve conduction studies (NCS) and electromyography (EMG) are crucial to confirm neuropathy, classify it as axonal or demyelinating, and determine distribution.   • Severity/risk tools:
    • Overall Neuropathy Limitations Scale (ONLS) can be used to assess the degree of disability. Overall Neuropathy Limitations Scale (ONLS) on MDCalc
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible:
    • Autoimmune screen (ANA, ENA, ANCA) if vasculitis or connective tissue disease is suspected.
    • CSF analysis if inflammatory demyelinating polyneuropathy (e.g., GBS, CIDP) is considered.
    • Genetic testing for inherited neuropathies if family history or early onset.
  • Serial reassessment:
    • Regular clinical review and repeat NCS/EMG may be indicated to monitor progression or response to treatment.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Peripheral neuropathy"][/sc]
  • Radiculopathy - often unilateral, dermatomal sensory loss, and may have back/neck pain; typically spares the hands if lumbar, or feet if cervical.
  • Myelopathy - upper motor neuron signs (spasticity, hyperreflexia), sensory level, and bladder/bowel dysfunction; often affects trunk and proximal limbs.
  • Myopathy - pure motor weakness, typically proximal, with normal sensation and reflexes.
  • Entrapment neuropathy (e.g., carpal tunnel syndrome) - focal symptoms in a specific nerve distribution, often with positive provocative tests.
  • Restless legs syndrome - unpleasant leg sensations relieved by movement, primarily at rest and at night, without objective sensory loss.
  • Fibromyalgia - widespread musculoskeletal pain, fatigue, and tender points, without objective neurological deficits.
  • Functional neurological disorder - inconsistent or non-anatomical neurological signs, often with identifiable psychological triggers. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Functional Neurological Disorder (FND): what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Functional Neurological Disorder"][/sc]
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Peripheral neuropathy"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Recognition of red flag features warranting urgent neurological referral; supportive care including pain management and fall prevention strategies.
  • Definitive management: Primarily involves identifying and treating the underlying cause (e.g., optimising glycaemic control in diabetes, vitamin B12 supplementation, alcohol cessation).
  • Adjunctive management: May support symptomatic relief of neuropathic pain with agents such as amitriptyline, duloxetine, gabapentin, or pregabalin, often initiated in a stepped-care approach.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Regular review to assess symptom control, monitor for progression, and manage complications such as foot ulcers.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Patients should be advised to seek prompt medical attention for new or worsening weakness, rapidly progressive symptoms, or signs of infection in affected areas.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Peripheral neuropathy"][/sc]
  • Chronic pain: Persistent neuropathic pain can significantly impair quality of life, leading to sleep disturbance, depression, and reduced functional capacity.
  • Foot ulcers and infections: Sensory loss, particularly in the feet, increases the risk of unnoticed injuries, which can progress to severe infections, osteomyelitis, and potentially amputation if not managed promptly.
  • Falls and injuries: Proprioceptive loss and muscle weakness contribute to instability and an increased risk of falls, leading to fractures and other trauma.
  • Autonomic dysfunction: Can manifest as orthostatic hypotension (risk of syncope), gastroparesis, bladder dysfunction, and sexual dysfunction, impacting multiple organ systems.
  • Charcot neuroarthropathy: A severe, progressive degenerative arthropathy affecting joints, most commonly in the foot and ankle, due to repetitive trauma in the presence of sensory neuropathy.
  • Severe motor weakness: In advanced cases, significant motor nerve damage can lead to profound weakness, muscle atrophy, and functional disability, requiring mobility aids or assistance.
