Cutaneous warts [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Cutaneous warts"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Cutaneous warts"][/sc]
  • Cutaneous warts are benign epidermal proliferations caused by infection with the human papillomavirus (HPV), commonly affecting the skin and mucous membranes.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the typical clinical presentation of common cutaneous warts and understand their aetiology and management principles.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Cutaneous warts"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Cutaneous warts"][/sc]
  • Cutaneous warts are highly prevalent, particularly in children and adolescents, with an estimated prevalence of 10-20% in school-aged children in the UK.
  • Transmission typically occurs through direct skin-to-skin contact or indirect contact via contaminated surfaces, with minor skin trauma increasing susceptibility.
  • Risk factors include immunosuppression (e.g., organ transplant recipients, HIV), atopic dermatitis, and occupations involving frequent handling of meat or fish (butcher's warts).
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Cutaneous warts"][/sc]
  • HPV infection of basal keratinocytes, often facilitated by micro-abrasions, initiates viral replication within the epidermal cells.
  • Viral proteins (E6 and E7) interfere with host cell cycle regulation, leading to increased keratinocyte proliferation and delayed differentiation.
  • This results in the characteristic epidermal hyperplasia, hyperkeratosis, and papillomatosis, forming the visible wart lesion.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Cutaneous warts"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Incubation period can range from weeks to months (typically 1-6 months) after HPV exposure before a visible lesion appears.
  • Progressive phase: Warts may slowly enlarge or multiply over months to years, particularly beyond 6 months, often influenced by immune response and continued exposure.
  • Late phase: Spontaneous regression is common, especially in children, often occurring within 2 years, but some warts can persist for many years or recur after treatment.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Cutaneous warts"][/sc]
  • Ask about pain or tenderness - particularly if on weight-bearing areas like the soles of the feet (plantar warts), which can impact mobility.
  • Ask about itching - which may indicate irritation or an inflammatory response, though less common than pain.
  • Ask about cosmetic concern - as visible warts, especially on the hands or face, can cause significant psychological distress.
  • Ask about changes in size, shape, or colour - to assess for potential complications or alternative diagnoses, though malignant transformation is exceedingly rare.
  • Ask about previous treatments and their efficacy - to guide further management strategies and understand patient expectations.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Cutaneous warts"][/sc]
  • Check for well-demarcated, exophytic papules or nodules with a rough, verrucous surface - characteristic appearance of common warts.
  • Check for punctate black dots (thrombosed capillaries) upon paring the lesion - a key distinguishing feature from calluses.
  • Check for absence of skin lines crossing the lesion - another feature differentiating warts from calluses, particularly on palms and soles.
  • Check for location and distribution - common on hands, feet, and periungual areas; mosaic warts are coalesced plantar warts.
  • Check vital signs - typically normal, as warts are a localised skin condition without systemic effects.
