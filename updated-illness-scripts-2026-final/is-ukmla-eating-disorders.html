Eating disorders (Mental health) [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Eating disorders"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Eating disorders"][/sc]
  • Eating disorders are serious mental health conditions characterised by persistent disturbances in eating behaviours, thoughts, and emotions, leading to significant physical and psychological impairment. Key types include anorexia nervosa (characterised by restricted energy intake leading to significantly low body weight, intense fear of gaining weight, and distorted body image) and bulimia nervosa (characterised by recurrent episodes of binge eating followed by compensatory behaviours like purging or excessive exercise).
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the diverse presentations of eating disorders, including anorexia nervosa and bulimia nervosa, identify associated physical and psychological complications, and understand the principles of initial assessment and referral for specialist management.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Eating disorders"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Eating disorders"][/sc]
  • It is estimated that at least 1.25 million people in the UK are affected by an eating disorder [1]. Approximately 25% of those affected are male [1].
  • The prevalence of possible eating problems in England in 2023 was 12.3% in children aged 11 to 16 years, rising significantly to 59.4% in young people aged 17 to 19 years [2].
  • Risk factors include a family history of eating disorders, other mental health conditions (e.g., anxiety, depression, OCD), perfectionism, body image dissatisfaction, and participation in activities that emphasise leanness or weight control (e.g., professional sport, fashion, dance) [3].
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Eating disorders"][/sc]
  • Trigger: A complex interplay of genetic predisposition, psychological vulnerabilities (e.g., perfectionism, low self-esteem), and socio-cultural pressures (e.g., idealisation of thinness) can initiate disordered eating behaviours.
  • Progression: Initial restrictive eating or compensatory behaviours can lead to a vicious cycle. In anorexia nervosa, prolonged starvation induces neurobiological changes that reinforce restrictive eating and body image distortion. In bulimia nervosa, the binge-purge cycle becomes a maladaptive coping mechanism, often driven by emotional dysregulation and intense fear of weight gain.
  • Consequence: Chronic disordered eating results in significant physiological and psychological consequences, including malnutrition, electrolyte imbalances, cardiac complications, and exacerbation of mental health comorbidities. A critical concern, particularly in severe anorexia nervosa, is the risk of [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Refeeding syndrome: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="refeeding syndrome"][/sc] upon nutritional rehabilitation.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Eating disorders"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Often insidious, beginning with dieting or body image concerns, gradually escalating to restrictive eating, compensatory behaviours, or bingeing. This phase can span months to a few years, with symptoms often initially concealed.
  • Progressive phase: As disordered eating patterns become entrenched, physical and psychological complications emerge and worsen. This can involve significant weight loss or fluctuations, electrolyte imbalances, and increasing preoccupation with food, weight, and shape. This phase may persist for several years if untreated.
  • Late phase: Chronic eating disorders can lead to severe, life-threatening medical complications (e.g., cardiac arrhythmias, osteoporosis, organ damage) and enduring psychological distress, particularly beyond several years of illness. Recovery is still possible, but the risk of chronicity and associated morbidity increases with duration.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Eating disorders"][/sc]
  • Ask about restrictive eating behaviours - to identify patterns of severe calorie restriction, often associated with anorexia nervosa, or dietary rules preceding binge-purge cycles in bulimia nervosa.
  • Ask about binge eating episodes - characterised by consuming a large amount of food in a short period with a sense of loss of control, a key feature of bulimia nervosa and binge eating disorder.
  • Ask about compensatory behaviours - such as self-induced vomiting, misuse of laxatives/diuretics, excessive exercise, or fasting, which are typically seen in bulimia nervosa following binge eating.
  • Ask about distorted body image or intense fear of gaining weight - a central psychological feature in anorexia nervosa, driving restrictive behaviours despite being underweight.
  • Ask about amenorrhoea - the absence of menstruation, which can be a physiological consequence of significant weight loss and nutritional deficiency in anorexia nervosa.
  • Ask about preoccupation with food, weight, or shape - to understand the extent to which these thoughts dominate the individual\'s mental landscape, impacting daily functioning and decision-making.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Eating disorders"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - bradycardia, hypotension, and hypothermia often indicate severe malnutrition and physiological compromise, particularly in anorexia nervosa.
  • Check Body Mass Index (BMI) - a BMI below 17.5 kg/m² is a diagnostic criterion for anorexia nervosa, while individuals with bulimia nervosa typically maintain a normal weight.
  • Check for Russell\'s sign - calluses or abrasions on the dorsum of the hand, indicative of repeated self-induced vomiting, commonly seen in bulimia nervosa and some forms of anorexia nervosa.
  • Check for lanugo hair - fine, downy hair on the body, a compensatory mechanism for heat loss due to lack of subcutaneous fat in severe anorexia nervosa.
  • Check for signs of hypokalaemia - muscle weakness, fatigue, or cardiac arrhythmias, which can result from recurrent vomiting or laxative misuse in bulimia nervosa and severe anorexia nervosa. Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Eating disorders"][/sc]
  • Severe bradycardia or hypotension - indicative of significant cardiovascular compromise due to malnutrition, requiring urgent medical assessment and intervention to prevent cardiac arrest.
  • Rapid or severe weight loss - a critical indicator of deteriorating physical health, particularly in anorexia nervosa, increasing the risk of refeeding syndrome and other medical complications.
  • Electrolyte disturbances - especially hypokalaemia, which can lead to life-threatening cardiac arrhythmias, often seen with recurrent purging behaviours.
  • Refeeding syndrome risk - individuals with severe anorexia nervosa are at high risk during nutritional rehabilitation, necessitating careful monitoring and management to prevent fluid and electrolyte shifts.
  • Suicidal ideation or self-harm - eating disorders have a high comorbidity with mood disorders and self-harm, requiring immediate psychiatric assessment and safety planning.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Eating disorders"][/sc]
  • Males: Eating disorders in males are often under-recognised due to societal stereotypes, leading to delayed diagnosis and treatment. Presentations may focus more on muscularity and exercise addiction rather than thinness.
  • Older adults: Eating disorders can emerge or re-emerge in later life, often masked by other age-related health issues or social isolation. Weight loss may be attributed to medical conditions, delaying appropriate psychiatric assessment.
  • Athletes: High-performance athletes, particularly in sports emphasising leanness (e.g., gymnastics, running), are at increased risk. Their disordered eating may be rationalised as performance enhancement, making it difficult to identify.
  • Individuals with higher BMI: Eating disorders, particularly atypical anorexia nervosa, can occur in individuals who are not underweight. They may exhibit significant weight loss from a higher baseline, but still fall within a \'normal\' or \'overweight\' BMI range, leading to diagnostic oversight. Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Eating disorders"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected eating disorder: Full blood count, urea and electrolytes (U&Es), liver function tests (LFTs), bone profile (calcium, phosphate, magnesium), glucose, thyroid function tests (TFTs), electrocardiogram (ECG) to assess for cardiac abnormalities, and urinalysis.
  • Severity/risk tools: The [sc name="jitl-query" Q="MARSIPAN guidelines: what are they and why do they matter clinically?" text="MARSIPAN guidelines"] [/sc] (Management of Really Sick Patients with Anorexia Nervosa) provide a framework for assessing medical risk in anorexia nervosa. The MEED (Medical Emergencies in Eating Disorders) guidance has superseded MARSIPAN and offers a risk assessment framework tool using a traffic light system [4].
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Consider further investigations such as coeliac serology, inflammatory markers, or imaging (e.g., abdominal ultrasound) to rule out gastrointestinal or other medical conditions mimicking eating disorder symptoms.
  • Imaging - Bone Densitometry (DEXA scan): Recommended for individuals with anorexia nervosa with prolonged amenorrhoea or significant weight loss to assess for osteoporosis, a common complication.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Eating disorders"][/sc]
  • A young female presenting with a 6-month history of significant weight loss, intense fear of gaining weight, and amenorrhoea, alongside bradycardia and lanugo hair on examination.
  • A young adult with recurrent episodes of binge eating followed by self-induced vomiting, normal BMI, and Russell\'s sign, concerning for bulimia nervosa.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Eating disorders"][/sc]
  • Depression - often co-occurs with eating disorders; however, primary depression may cause appetite changes and weight loss without the intense body image preoccupation or specific disordered eating behaviours.
  • Anxiety disorders - can lead to somatic symptoms affecting appetite or digestion, but typically lack the core psychopathology of body image distortion and fear of weight gain seen in eating disorders.
  • Inflammatory Bowel Disease (IBD) - chronic IBD can cause weight loss, abdominal pain, and nutritional deficiencies, but typically presents with gastrointestinal symptoms (e.g., diarrhoea, rectal bleeding) and inflammatory markers not directly linked to body image concerns.
  • Hyperthyroidism - can cause weight loss, increased appetite, and anxiety, but is distinguished by thyroid function test abnormalities and other signs like goitre or exophthalmos.
  • Coeliac disease - presents with malabsorption, weight loss, and gastrointestinal symptoms, but is diagnosed by specific serological markers and duodenal biopsy, and does not involve the psychological features of eating disorders.
  • Substance use disorders - can lead to weight loss and nutritional compromise, but the primary driver is substance use rather than disordered eating behaviours or body image concerns. Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Eating disorders"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Recognition and urgent medical assessment are paramount, especially in cases of severe malnutrition, electrolyte imbalance, or cardiac compromise. Escalation to specialist eating disorder services or acute medical care is often warranted, guided by MEED (Medical Emergencies in Eating Disorders) criteria. Supportive care often includes rehydration and careful nutritional rehabilitation to prevent refeeding syndrome.
  • Definitive management: Psychological therapies are the cornerstone of treatment. For anorexia nervosa, family-based treatment (FBT) is recommended for children and young people, while cognitive behavioural therapy (CBT) or specialist supportive clinical management (SSCM) may be considered for adults. For bulimia nervosa, CBT-BN is the first-line psychological treatment.
  • Adjunctive management: Nutritional counselling and dietetic support are integral to restoring healthy eating patterns and weight. Management of co-morbid mental health conditions (e.g., depression, anxiety) may also be required.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Regular physical health monitoring, including weight, vital signs, and blood tests (e.g., U&Es), is essential throughout treatment and recovery. Long-term follow-up with specialist services is typically recommended to prevent relapse.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Clear pathways for escalation to higher levels of care (e.g., inpatient treatment) should be in place for individuals whose physical or mental health deteriorates, or who are at high risk of self-harm or suicide. Safety-netting advice for patients and carers should cover warning signs and how to seek urgent help.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Eating disorders"][/sc]
  • Cardiac arrhythmias: Electrolyte imbalances (e.g., hypokalaemia, hypophosphataemia) and structural changes due to malnutrition can lead to life-threatening arrhythmias, particularly in the progressive and late phases of eating disorders.
  • Refeeding syndrome: A potentially fatal shift in fluids and electrolytes that can occur in severely malnourished individuals undergoing nutritional rehabilitation, especially in the early phase of refeeding. This is a critical danger pattern requiring careful monitoring.
  • Osteoporosis: Chronic nutritional deficiency and hormonal dysregulation, particularly in anorexia nervosa, lead to reduced bone mineral density and increased fracture risk, a long-term complication evident in the late phase.
  • Gastrointestinal dysfunction: Delayed gastric emptying, constipation, and oesophageal damage (from recurrent vomiting) are common, contributing to discomfort and perpetuating disordered eating patterns throughout the progressive and late phases.
  • Renal impairment: Dehydration, electrolyte disturbances, and laxative abuse can lead to acute kidney injury or chronic renal dysfunction, a risk that increases in the progressive and late phases.
  • Psychiatric comorbidities: High rates of depression, anxiety, obsessive-compulsive disorder, and substance use disorders persist throughout the illness, significantly impacting prognosis and quality of life.
