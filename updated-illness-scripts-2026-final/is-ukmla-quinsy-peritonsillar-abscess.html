Quinsy/ peritonsillar abscess [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Quinsy/ peritonsillar abscess"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Quinsy/ peritonsillar abscess"][/sc]
  • A peritonsillar abscess, or quinsy, is a collection of pus located between the tonsillar capsule and the superior constrictor muscle.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: recognise the clinical features, diagnosis, and initial management of a peritonsillar abscess.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Quinsy/ peritonsillar abscess"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Quinsy/ peritonsillar abscess"][/sc]
  • Quinsy affects approximately 10-20 per 100,000 people annually in the UK, with a peak incidence in young adults (NICE).
  • Risk factors include recurrent tonsillitis, smoking, and infectious mononucleosis.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Quinsy/ peritonsillar abscess"][/sc]
  • Trigger: Bacterial infection, often streptococcal, of the tonsillar crypts or minor salivary glands in the peritonsillar space.
  • Progression: Inflammation and oedema lead to tissue necrosis and pus formation, creating an abscess.
  • Consequence: The expanding abscess displaces the tonsil medially and anteriorly, causing severe pain and airway compromise.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Quinsy/ peritonsillar abscess"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Symptoms typically develop over 2-5 days, often following a bout of tonsillitis.
  • Progressive phase: Pain and dysphagia worsen, with trismus and muffled voice becoming prominent, particularly beyond 3-5 days.
  • Late phase: Untreated, symptoms can progress to severe airway obstruction or systemic sepsis, particularly beyond 7-10 days.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Quinsy/ peritonsillar abscess"][/sc]
  • Ask about severe unilateral sore throat - often radiating to the ear, indicating localised inflammation.
  • Ask about dysphagia and odynophagia - difficulty and pain on swallowing, suggesting pharyngeal irritation and swelling.
  • Ask about trismus - difficulty opening the mouth, due to spasm of the pterygoid muscles.
