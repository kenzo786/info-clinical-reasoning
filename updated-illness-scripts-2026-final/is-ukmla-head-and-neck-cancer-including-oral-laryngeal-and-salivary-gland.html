Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Head and neck cancer (including oral, laryngeal and salivary gland)"][/sc]
  • Head and neck cancer encompasses a diverse group of malignancies originating from the oral cavity, pharynx, larynx, paranasal sinuses, nasal cavity, and salivary glands.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the common presentations, risk factors, and initial management of head and neck cancers, including the importance of early referral.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Head and neck cancer (including oral, laryngeal and salivary gland)"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Head and neck cancer (including oral, laryngeal and salivary gland)"][/sc]
  • Approximately 12,000 new cases of head and neck cancer are diagnosed annually in the UK, accounting for about 3% of all cancers, with a higher incidence in men.
  • Major risk factors include tobacco smoking (cigarettes, cigars, pipes, smokeless tobacco) and excessive alcohol consumption, which act synergistically to increase risk.
  • Human papillomavirus (HPV) infection, particularly HPV-16, is a growing risk factor for oropharyngeal cancers, especially in younger, non-smoking individuals.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Head and neck cancer (including oral, laryngeal and salivary gland)"][/sc]
  • Chronic exposure to carcinogens (e.g., tobacco, alcohol, HPV) induces genetic mutations in the mucosal lining of the upper aerodigestive tract, leading to dysplastic changes.
  • These dysplastic cells undergo further genetic alterations, promoting uncontrolled proliferation, invasion of local tissues, and evasion of immune surveillance.
  • Progressive tumour growth and local invasion can lead to regional lymph node metastasis and, in advanced stages, distant metastasis, resulting in systemic symptoms and organ dysfunction.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Head and neck cancer (including oral, laryngeal and salivary gland)"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Often asymptomatic or presents with non-specific symptoms such as persistent sore throat, hoarseness, or a small oral lesion, which may be overlooked, particularly beyond a few weeks.
  • Progressive phase: As the tumour grows, symptoms become more pronounced, including dysphagia, odynophagia, persistent neck lump, or otalgia, particularly beyond a few months.
  • Late phase: Advanced disease can manifest with significant weight loss, airway obstruction, severe pain, trismus, or cranial nerve palsies, particularly beyond six months to a year.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Head and neck cancer (including oral, laryngeal and salivary gland)"][/sc]
  • Ask about persistent sore throat - may indicate pharyngeal or laryngeal involvement, especially if unilateral or associated with dysphagia.
  • Ask about hoarseness or voice changes - a key symptom of laryngeal cancer, suggesting vocal cord dysfunction.
  • Ask about dysphagia or odynophagia - difficulty or pain on swallowing, often progressive, pointing to pharyngeal or oesophageal involvement.
  • Ask about persistent neck lump - a common presentation of metastatic lymphadenopathy, often painless and firm.
  • Ask about unexplained weight loss - a non-specific but important systemic symptom of advanced malignancy.
  • Ask about oral lesions that do not heal - particularly white (leukoplakia) or red (erythroplakia) patches, or ulcers, which can be early signs of oral cancer.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Head and neck cancer (including oral, laryngeal and salivary gland)"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - may reveal tachycardia or hypotension in advanced disease with cachexia or dehydration, or fever if secondary infection is present.
  • Check for a palpable, firm, non-tender, fixed neck lump - highly suspicious for metastatic lymphadenopathy.
  • Check the oral cavity for leukoplakia, erythroplakia, or ulcerated lesions - visual inspection is crucial for early detection of oral cancers.
  • Check for trismus - difficulty opening the mouth, which can indicate tumour involvement of the pterygoid muscles or temporomandibular joint.
  • Check for cranial nerve palsies - particularly IX, X, XI, XII, suggesting skull base invasion or extensive nodal disease. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Cranial nerve palsies in head and neck cancer: what do they indicate?" text="Cranial nerve palsies"][/sc]
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Head and neck cancer (including oral, laryngeal and salivary gland)"][/sc]
  • Persistent unexplained neck lump: Especially if firm, fixed, and painless, warrants urgent investigation for metastatic disease.
  • Unexplained hoarseness for >3 weeks: A critical indicator for potential laryngeal cancer, requiring prompt referral to ENT.
  • Persistent oral ulceration or red/white patches for >3 weeks: Suggests oral malignancy and necessitates urgent dental or oral medicine review.
  • Unilateral sore throat or otalgia for >4 weeks: Particularly if associated with dysphagia, can be a referred pain from pharyngeal or laryngeal tumours.
  • Unexplained weight loss and fatigue: General systemic symptoms that, in the context of other head and neck symptoms, raise suspicion for advanced cancer.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Head and neck cancer (including oral, laryngeal and salivary gland)"][/sc]
  • Older adults: May present with vague symptoms such as general malaise or reduced appetite, and may attribute symptoms to aging, delaying presentation.
  • HPV-positive oropharyngeal cancer: Often affects younger, non-smoking individuals and may present with a cystic neck mass (metastatic node) with minimal oropharyngeal symptoms. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="HPV-positive oropharyngeal cancer: how does it differ clinically?" text="HPV-positive oropharyngeal cancer"][/sc]
  • Early laryngeal cancer: Can present solely with subtle voice changes, which may be dismissed as laryngitis, particularly if there is no pain or dysphagia.
  • Salivary gland tumours: May present as a slow-growing, painless mass in the parotid or submandibular region, often without other typical head and neck cancer symptoms.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Head and neck cancer (including oral, laryngeal and salivary gland)"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected head and neck cancer: Flexible nasoendoscopy to visualise the upper aerodigestive tract, followed by biopsy of suspicious lesions.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Full blood count, inflammatory markers (CRP, ESR) to rule out infection or inflammatory conditions.
  • Imaging - CT scan: Of the neck and chest with contrast to assess primary tumour extent, regional lymph node involvement, and distant metastasis.
  • Imaging - MRI scan: Particularly useful for assessing soft tissue involvement, perineural spread, and skull base invasion.
  • Imaging - PET-CT scan: Often used for staging advanced disease, detecting occult primary tumours, or assessing recurrence.
  • Fine Needle Aspiration Cytology (FNAC): For palpable neck lumps to confirm malignancy and determine cell type.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Head and neck cancer (including oral, laryngeal and salivary gland)"][/sc]
  • Laryngitis - typically acute, viral, and resolves spontaneously, unlike persistent hoarseness from cancer.
  • Pharyngitis/Tonsillitis - usually acute, painful, and associated with fever, often responding to antibiotics.
  • Benign vocal cord lesions (e.g., polyps, nodules) - cause hoarseness but typically lack other systemic or progressive symptoms of malignancy.
  • Thyroid nodule - often a solitary lump in the anterior neck, moves with swallowing, and is usually benign, but can be malignant.
  • Branchial cleft cyst - congenital neck lump, usually soft and mobile, often presenting in younger individuals.
  • Tuberculosis lymphadenitis - chronic neck lymphadenopathy, often associated with systemic symptoms like night sweats and weight loss, but typically softer than malignant nodes. Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Head and neck cancer (including oral, laryngeal and salivary gland)"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Secure the airway if there are signs of obstruction; manage pain; arrange urgent specialist referral to ENT/Maxillofacial surgery.
  • Definitive management: Multidisciplinary team (MDT) discussion to determine optimal treatment, which may include surgery, radiotherapy, chemotherapy, or a combination, depending on tumour stage and location.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Regular clinical review, imaging, and endoscopy to detect recurrence or new primary tumours, and to manage treatment-related side effects.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Advise patients to seek immediate medical attention for worsening symptoms, particularly difficulty breathing, severe pain, or rapid weight loss.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Head and neck cancer (including oral, laryngeal and salivary gland)"][/sc]
  • Airway obstruction: Due to large laryngeal or pharyngeal tumours, leading to stridor and respiratory distress, requiring urgent intervention.
  • Dysphagia and malnutrition: Tumour bulk or treatment side effects can severely impair swallowing, leading to significant weight loss and nutritional deficiencies, often progressive over months.
  • Haemorrhage: From tumour erosion into major blood vessels, a potentially life-threatening complication, particularly in advanced disease.
  • Trismus: Due to tumour infiltration or fibrosis from radiotherapy, limiting mouth opening and impacting speech, eating, and oral hygiene.
  • Speech impairment (dysphonia/aphonia): Resulting from laryngeal involvement or treatment, significantly affecting communication and quality of life.
