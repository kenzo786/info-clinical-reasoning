Fibroadenoma [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Fibroadenoma"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Fibroadenoma"][/sc]
  • A fibroadenoma is a common, benign, solid breast tumour composed of both glandular and stromal tissue, typically presenting as a painless, mobile lump.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical features, diagnostic approach, and management principles of benign breast lumps, particularly fibroadenomas, differentiating them from malignant lesions.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Fibroadenoma"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Fibroadenoma"][/sc]
  • Fibroadenomas are the most common benign breast tumours, accounting for approximately 50% of all breast biopsies, with a peak incidence in women aged 15-35 years.
  • Risk factors include younger age, African-Caribbean ethnicity, and potentially oral contraceptive use before age 20. They are less common post-menopause.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Fibroadenoma"][/sc]
  • Trigger: Localised hypersensitivity of breast stromal and epithelial cells to oestrogen, leading to abnormal proliferation.
  • Progression: The proliferating cells form a well-circumscribed, encapsulated mass with both glandular and fibrous components, often growing slowly.
  • Consequence: Formation of a palpable, mobile lump that is typically benign and rarely progresses to malignancy.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Fibroadenoma"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Typically presents as a newly noticed, small, painless lump, often growing slowly over months.
  • Progressive phase: The lump may increase in size, particularly during pregnancy or with hormonal fluctuations, but usually remains mobile and non-tender.
  • Late phase: Growth often stabilises or may even regress, particularly beyond 40 years of age or post-menopause, with malignant transformation being exceedingly rare.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Fibroadenoma"][/sc]
  • Ask about a painless breast lump - this is the most common presentation and helps differentiate from inflammatory or infectious causes.
  • Ask about a mobile lump - a 'breast mouse' sensation is characteristic and suggests a benign, well-circumscribed lesion.
  • Ask about changes in size with menstrual cycle or pregnancy - hormonal sensitivity can cause fluctuations, indicating benign aetiology.
  • Ask about nipple discharge or skin changes - absence of these features helps rule out more concerning pathologies.
  • Ask about family history of breast cancer - while not directly linked to fibroadenomas, it is crucial for overall risk assessment.
