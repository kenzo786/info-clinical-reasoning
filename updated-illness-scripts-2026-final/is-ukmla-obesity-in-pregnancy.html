Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Obesity in pregnancy (Pregnancy and the puerperium)"][/sc]
  • Obesity in pregnancy is defined as a pre-pregnancy or early pregnancy body mass index (BMI) of 30 kg/m² or greater.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the increased risks associated with obesity in pregnancy for both mother and fetus, and understand the principles of antenatal care and management strategies to mitigate these risks.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Obesity in pregnancy (Pregnancy and the puerperium)"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Obesity in pregnancy (Pregnancy and the puerperium)"][/sc]
  • Approximately one in five pregnant women in the UK are obese (BMI ≥30 kg/m²), with a rising prevalence.
  • Risk factors include socioeconomic deprivation, certain ethnic backgrounds, and pre-existing medical conditions such as polycystic ovary syndrome.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Obesity in pregnancy (Pregnancy and the puerperium)"][/sc]
  • Trigger: Excess adipose tissue leads to a chronic low-grade inflammatory state and insulin resistance, exacerbated by the physiological changes of pregnancy.
  • Progression: This metabolic dysregulation contributes to impaired glucose tolerance, endothelial dysfunction, and altered hormonal profiles, impacting placental development and maternal-fetal nutrient exchange.
  • Consequence: Increased risk of gestational diabetes mellitus (GDM), pre-eclampsia, macrosomia, and adverse birth outcomes due to the complex interplay of metabolic, inflammatory, and endocrine factors. Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Obesity in pregnancy (Pregnancy and the puerperium)"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Pre-conception counselling and early antenatal booking are crucial for risk assessment and initial management planning, ideally before 10 weeks gestation.
  • Progressive phase: Increased monitoring for complications such as GDM (typically screened at 24-28 weeks) and pre-eclampsia (often developing in the second or third trimester) is required throughout pregnancy.
  • Late phase: Risks extend into labour and the puerperium, with increased likelihood of prolonged labour, shoulder dystocia, caesarean section, and postpartum haemorrhage, particularly beyond 40 weeks gestation.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Obesity in pregnancy (Pregnancy and the puerperium)"][/sc]
  • Ask about general well-being and energy levels - may be impacted by comorbidities associated with obesity, such as sleep apnoea.
  • Ask about symptoms of gestational diabetes (e.g., polydipsia, polyuria, fatigue) - these may be subtle or absent, necessitating routine screening.
  • Ask about symptoms of pre-eclampsia (e.g., headache, visual disturbances, epigastric pain) - early recognition is vital for timely intervention.
  • Ask about mobility and discomfort - increased weight can exacerbate musculoskeletal issues and reduce physical activity, impacting overall health.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Obesity in pregnancy (Pregnancy and the puerperium)"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs (blood pressure, pulse, respiratory rate) - essential for monitoring for pre-eclampsia and other cardiovascular complications.
  • Check BMI - a key indicator for risk stratification and management planning.
  • Check for oedema - may indicate pre-eclampsia or venous thromboembolism (VTE). [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Venous thromboembolism in pregnancy: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Venous thromboembolism"][/sc]
  • Check fundal height - may be difficult to accurately assess due to increased adipose tissue, potentially masking fetal growth abnormalities.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Obesity in pregnancy (Pregnancy and the puerperium)"][/sc]
  • Severe headache with visual changes: Suggestive of severe pre-eclampsia, requiring urgent assessment and management to prevent eclampsia.
  • Sudden onset leg swelling with pain: Raises suspicion for deep vein thrombosis (DVT), a potentially life-threatening complication of VTE.
  • Reduced fetal movements: Indicates potential fetal compromise, necessitating immediate investigation and intervention.
  • Persistent epigastric pain or right upper quadrant pain: May signify HELLP syndrome, a severe variant of pre-eclampsia, requiring emergency care.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Obesity in pregnancy (Pregnancy and the puerperium)"][/sc]
  • Women with extreme obesity (BMI >40 kg/m²): Present with significantly higher risks and may require more intensive monitoring and specialist input throughout pregnancy.
  • Women with rapid weight gain in pregnancy: May indicate underlying gestational diabetes or fluid retention, even if initial BMI was not in the obese range.
  • Women with co-morbidities (e.g., hypertension, diabetes): The interplay of obesity with pre-existing conditions can lead to more severe and earlier onset complications. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Pre-eclampsia: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Pre-eclampsia"][/sc]
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Obesity in pregnancy (Pregnancy and the puerperium)"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected obesity in pregnancy:
    • Early pregnancy booking bloods: Full blood count, blood group and antibodies, syphilis, hepatitis B, HIV, rubella immunity.
    • Oral glucose tolerance test (OGTT): Typically performed at 24-28 weeks gestation to screen for GDM.
    • Urine dipstick: Regular screening for proteinuria to detect pre-eclampsia.
  • Severity/risk tools:
    • VTE risk assessment: RCOG Green-top Guideline No. 37a provides a comprehensive risk assessment tool for VTE in pregnancy.   • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible:
    • Thyroid function tests: To exclude thyroid dysfunction, which can mimic some symptoms of pregnancy complications.
  • Imaging - Ultrasound:
    • Anomaly scan: Performed at 18-21 weeks, may be technically more challenging in obese women.
    • Fetal growth scans: Serial scans may be indicated if there is concern for macrosomia or fetal growth restriction.
  • Serial reassessment: Regular antenatal appointments with monitoring of blood pressure, urine, and fetal growth.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Obesity in pregnancy (Pregnancy and the puerperium)"][/sc]
  • Gestational hypertension: Shares features with pre-eclampsia (hypertension in pregnancy) but lacks proteinuria or other systemic features.
  • Pre-existing diabetes mellitus: Differentiated from GDM by diagnosis prior to pregnancy, though management principles share similarities.
  • Normal physiological changes of pregnancy: Some symptoms (e.g., fatigue, oedema) can overlap with complications, requiring careful assessment.
  • Polyhydramnios: Can be associated with GDM and macrosomia, but is a consequence rather than a primary differential for obesity in pregnancy itself.
  • Thyroid dysfunction: Can cause weight changes and fatigue, which may be misattributed to obesity or pregnancy. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Thyroid dysfunction in pregnancy: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Thyroid dysfunction"][/sc]
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Obesity in pregnancy (Pregnancy and the puerperium)"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Early identification of obesity, comprehensive risk assessment, and individualised care planning from booking.
  • Definitive management: Lifestyle interventions including dietary advice and regular physical activity are fundamental. Referral to a dietitian and specialist obstetric services is often warranted.
  • Adjunctive management: Pharmacological interventions may be considered for specific complications, such as anti-hypertensives for pre-eclampsia or insulin for GDM not controlled by diet.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Enhanced antenatal surveillance, including more frequent appointments, blood pressure monitoring, urine testing, and fetal growth scans.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Clear pathways for referral to specialist teams (e.g., obstetricians, anaesthetists, endocrinologists) and patient education on red flag symptoms.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Obesity in pregnancy (Pregnancy and the puerperium)"][/sc]
  • Gestational diabetes mellitus (GDM): Increased risk due to insulin resistance, leading to fetal macrosomia and increased risk of type 2 diabetes postpartum.
  • Pre-eclampsia: Higher incidence of hypertensive disorders, potentially progressing to eclampsia or HELLP syndrome, particularly in the second and third trimesters.
  • Venous thromboembolism (VTE): Elevated risk of DVT and pulmonary embolism, especially in the antenatal and postnatal periods, requiring careful risk stratification. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Shoulder dystocia: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Shoulder dystocia"][/sc]
  • Caesarean section: Higher rates due to labour complications, fetal macrosomia, and failure to progress, with associated risks of infection and haemorrhage.
  • Postpartum haemorrhage: Increased risk due to uterine atony, prolonged labour, and larger babies, particularly in the immediate postpartum period.
