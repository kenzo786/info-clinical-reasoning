Postpartum psychosis [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Postpartum psychosis"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Postpartum psychosis"][/sc]
  • A severe, acute mental health illness that develops rapidly after childbirth, characterised by psychotic symptoms such as delusions, hallucinations, and mood disturbances.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the rapid onset and severe nature of postpartum psychosis as a psychiatric emergency requiring urgent intervention.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Postpartum psychosis"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Postpartum psychosis"][/sc]
  • Affects approximately 1 to 2 per 1,000 women following childbirth in the UK, often within the first few weeks postpartum.
  • Risk factors include a personal or family history of bipolar disorder or previous postpartum psychosis, and primiparity.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Postpartum psychosis"][/sc]
  • Trigger: Abrupt hormonal shifts (e.g., rapid decline in oestrogen and progesterone) post-delivery, combined with sleep deprivation and the stress of childbirth, may precipitate the condition.
  • Progression: These physiological changes are thought to interact with genetic predispositions, leading to dysregulation of neurotransmitter systems (e.g., dopamine, serotonin) and altered brain circuitry.
  • Consequence: The resulting neurobiological imbalance manifests as severe mood lability, psychotic symptoms, and cognitive disorganisation, characteristic of a psychotic episode.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Postpartum psychosis"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Typically presents acutely within the first 2-4 weeks postpartum, often within days of delivery, with rapid onset of symptoms.
  • Progressive phase: Symptoms can escalate quickly, leading to severe mood disturbance, psychotic features, and significant functional impairment, particularly beyond the initial few weeks.
  • Late phase: If untreated, symptoms can persist for weeks to months, with potential for severe maternal and infant morbidity, particularly beyond 6-8 weeks postpartum.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Postpartum psychosis"][/sc]
  • Ask about mood lability - rapid and extreme shifts in mood (e.g., euphoria to despair) are characteristic and distinguish it from postnatal depression.
  • Ask about psychotic features - presence of delusions (e.g., paranoid, grandiose, bizarre beliefs about the baby) or hallucinations (e.g., auditory, visual) indicates a severe psychiatric emergency.
  • Ask about disorganised thinking - incoherent speech, tangentiality, or flight of ideas suggest a severe thought disorder.
  • Ask about confusion and disorientation - inability to recognise familiar people or surroundings, or a lack of awareness of time and place, points to acute cognitive impairment.
  • Ask about severe anxiety or agitation - extreme restlessness, panic, or inability to settle, which can be distressing and potentially dangerous.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Postpartum psychosis"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - assess for signs of dehydration, exhaustion, or physical neglect due to severe mental disturbance.
  • Check behaviour - observe for agitation, restlessness, disorganised or bizarre actions, or withdrawal, which are indicative of a psychotic state.
  • Check speech - note for pressured speech, flight of ideas, or incoherence, reflecting thought disorder.
  • Check affect - observe for incongruent affect (e.g., laughing at serious topics) or flat affect, suggesting severe mood dysregulation.
  • Check self-care - assess for neglect of personal hygiene, poor nutritional intake, or lack of sleep, which are common in severe mental illness.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Postpartum psychosis"][/sc]
  • Rapid onset of symptoms - within days to 2-4 weeks postpartum, indicating an acute and severe presentation.
  • Psychotic symptoms - delusions (e.g., paranoid, infanticidal) or hallucinations (e.g., commanding voices) signify a psychiatric emergency with potential for harm.
  • Severe mood disturbance - extreme lability, severe depression, or mania, which can lead to impaired judgment and risk to self or baby.
  • Disorganised behaviour or thought - inability to care for self or baby, or incoherent communication, suggesting profound functional impairment.
  • Suicidal or infanticidal ideation - any thoughts of harming self or baby require immediate psychiatric assessment and intervention.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Postpartum psychosis"][/sc]
  • Subtle onset: In some cases, initial symptoms may be less dramatic, presenting as severe anxiety, insomnia, or irritability, which can be mistaken for severe postnatal depression before progressing to psychosis.
  • "Good mother" facade: Some individuals may attempt to mask their symptoms, particularly delusions or hallucinations, due to fear of judgment or having their baby removed, making early detection challenging.
  • Cultural variations: Presentation may differ across cultures, with some individuals expressing distress through somatic symptoms or culturally specific beliefs rather than overt psychotic features.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Postpartum psychosis"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected postpartum psychosis: Comprehensive psychiatric assessment, including detailed history from the individual and family, mental state examination, and risk assessment (suicide, infanticide, self-neglect).
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Full blood count, electrolytes, thyroid function tests, inflammatory markers, and urine toxicology to rule out organic causes (e.g., infection, metabolic disturbance, substance misuse).
  • Imaging - CT/MRI brain: Consider if there are focal neurological signs, new-onset seizures, or suspicion of an organic brain pathology.
  • Validated score: The Edinburgh Postnatal Depression Scale (EPDS) can screen for mood disorders, but is not diagnostic for psychosis.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Postpartum psychosis"][/sc]
  • A 28-year-old primiparous woman, 10 days postpartum, presents with acute onset of severe insomnia, racing thoughts, grandiose delusions about her baby, and auditory hallucinations commanding her to harm herself.
  • A young, recently delivered woman with a history of bipolar disorder, experiencing acute, severe mood lability and psychotic symptoms, representing a psychiatric emergency.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Postpartum psychosis"][/sc]
  • Postnatal depression with psychotic features - distinguished by less severe and often mood-congruent psychotic symptoms, and a less acute onset.
  • Bipolar disorder (manic or mixed episode) - shares features of mood lability and psychosis, but postpartum psychosis is specifically triggered by childbirth.
  • Schizophrenia (first episode or relapse) - distinguished by a more chronic course and often a lack of clear postpartum trigger, though a relapse can be triggered by stress.
  • Organic psychosis (e.g., infection, metabolic encephalopathy, substance-induced) - distinguished by presence of physical signs, abnormal investigations, or history of substance use.
  • Thyroid dysfunction (e.g., postpartum thyroiditis) - can cause mood and cognitive changes, but typically without overt psychotic features.
  • Severe anxiety disorder - characterised by intense anxiety and panic, but without delusions, hallucinations, or thought disorder.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Postpartum psychosis"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Urgent psychiatric assessment, admission to a specialist mother and baby unit (MBU) where available, and ensuring safety of both mother and baby.
  • Definitive management: Pharmacological treatment with antipsychotics (e.g., [sc name="jitl-medication" Q="Olanzapine use in Postpartum psychosis" text="olanzapine"][/sc], [sc name="jitl-medication" Q="Risperidone use in Postpartum psychosis" text="risperidone"][/sc]) and mood stabilisers (e.g., [sc name="jitl-medication" Q="Lithium use in Postpartum psychosis" text="lithium"][/sc]), often combined with psychological therapies.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Close monitoring of mental state, side effects of medication, and mother-infant bonding. Long-term follow-up by perinatal mental health services is crucial.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: If an MBU is unavailable, inpatient psychiatric admission is necessary. Crisis plans should be in place, and family/partners educated on warning signs of relapse.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Postpartum psychosis"][/sc]
  • Maternal suicide: A significant risk due to severe depression, hopelessness, or psychotic commands, particularly in the acute phase and if untreated.
  • Infanticide: A rare but devastating complication, often driven by delusions or psychotic commands, highlighting the need for urgent safety measures.
  • Impaired mother-infant bonding: Psychotic symptoms and severe mood disturbance can interfere with the mother\'s ability to bond with her baby, leading to long-term developmental impacts for the infant.
  • Relapse: High risk of recurrence in subsequent pregnancies or during periods of stress, particularly for individuals with a history of bipolar disorder.
  • Chronic mental health issues: Without adequate treatment, postpartum psychosis can lead to persistent mood disorders, anxiety, or functional impairment.
