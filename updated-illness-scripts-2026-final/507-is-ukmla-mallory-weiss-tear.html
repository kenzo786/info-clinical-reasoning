Mallory-Weiss tear [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Mallory-Weiss tear"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Mallory-Weiss tear"][/sc]
  • A longitudinal mucosal tear at the gastro-oesophageal junction (GOJ), typically caused by a sudden increase in intra-abdominal pressure, most commonly from forceful vomiting or retching.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical presentation of upper gastrointestinal bleeding secondary to Mallory-Weiss tear and understand its typical self-limiting course and diagnostic approach.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Mallory-Weiss tear"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Mallory-Weiss tear"][/sc]
  • Mallory-Weiss tears account for approximately 5-10% of all cases of upper gastrointestinal bleeding (UGIB) in the UK.
  • Incidence is estimated at 5-6 per 100,000 population annually, with a higher prevalence in individuals with a history of alcohol misuse or bulimia nervosa.
  • Risk factors include conditions that increase intra-abdominal pressure such as forceful vomiting, retching, coughing, heavy lifting, abdominal straining, and hiatal hernia.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Mallory-Weiss tear"][/sc]
  • Trigger: A sudden, severe increase in intra-abdominal pressure, often from forceful vomiting or retching, leads to a rapid rise in intra-gastric pressure against a closed glottis.
  • Progression: This pressure differential causes a longitudinal tear in the gastric or oesophageal mucosa, typically at the gastro-oesophageal junction, due to the shearing forces. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Gastro-oesophageal junction anatomy: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Gastro-oesophageal junction anatomy"][/sc]
  • Consequence: The mucosal tear exposes underlying blood vessels, resulting in bleeding into the gastrointestinal lumen, which manifests as haematemesis.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Mallory-Weiss tear"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Acute onset of haematemesis, often immediately following an episode of forceful vomiting or retching. Bleeding is typically self-limiting within 24-48 hours in the majority of cases.
  • Progressive phase: In a minority of cases, bleeding may persist or recur, particularly beyond 48 hours, necessitating endoscopic intervention.
  • Late phase: Resolution of bleeding is usually complete within a few days. Complications such as anaemia or rarely, perforation, may manifest if bleeding is severe or prolonged.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Mallory-Weiss tear"][/sc]
  • Ask about haematemesis - to confirm upper GI bleeding, noting if it follows an episode of forceful vomiting or retching, which is highly suggestive.
  • Ask about preceding non-bloody vomiting or retching - this sequence is characteristic and helps differentiate from other causes of UGIB.
  • Ask about melaena - indicates passage of altered blood through the GI tract, suggesting ongoing or significant bleeding.
  • Ask about epigastric pain or discomfort - may be present but is typically not severe and can be overshadowed by the vomiting and bleeding.
  • Ask about symptoms of hypovolaemia (e.g., dizziness, syncope) - to assess the severity of blood loss and potential for haemodynamic instability.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Mallory-Weiss tear"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - to assess for haemodynamic instability (tachycardia, hypotension) indicating significant blood loss.
  • Check pallor - suggests anaemia from acute or chronic blood loss.
  • Check abdominal examination - typically unremarkable, but mild epigastric tenderness may be present. Absence of peritonism helps rule out perforation.
  • Check for signs of chronic liver disease (e.g., jaundice, ascites, spider naevi) - as this is a common comorbidity in patients with alcohol misuse, a risk factor for Mallory-Weiss tear.
  • Check for evidence of recent forceful vomiting (e.g., conjunctival haemorrhage) - supports the history of increased intra-abdominal pressure.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Mallory-Weiss tear"][/sc]
  • Haemodynamic instability: Persistent tachycardia, hypotension, or signs of shock indicate significant ongoing blood loss requiring urgent resuscitation and intervention.
  • Persistent or recurrent haematemesis: Bleeding that does not spontaneously resolve within 24-48 hours, or recurs after initial cessation, suggests a larger tear or underlying coagulopathy.
  • Severe abdominal pain with signs of peritonism: While rare, this could indicate oesophageal perforation (Boerhaave syndrome), a life-threatening condition requiring immediate surgical assessment. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Boerhaave syndrome: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Boerhaave syndrome distinction"][/sc]
  • Failure to respond to conservative management: If supportive care and initial medical management do not stabilise the patient, further investigation and endoscopic therapy are warranted.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Mallory-Weiss tear"][/sc]
  • Elderly patients: May present with less pronounced symptoms of hypovolaemia due to blunted physiological responses, or with confusion as the primary symptom of blood loss.
  • Patients on anticoagulants/antiplatelets: Bleeding may be more severe and prolonged, requiring careful management of medication and potential reversal.
  • Pregnant women: Forceful vomiting due to hyperemesis gravidarum can lead to Mallory-Weiss tears. Presentation is similar, but management requires consideration of fetal well-being.
  • Patients with underlying coagulopathy: Conditions such as liver disease or inherited bleeding disorders can exacerbate bleeding from a Mallory-Weiss tear, making it less likely to be self-limiting.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Mallory-Weiss tear"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected Mallory-Weiss tear:
    • Full blood count (FBC): To assess for anaemia and guide transfusion requirements.
    • Urea and electrolytes (U&Es): To assess renal function and detect pre-renal azotaemia due to hypovolaemia.
    • Liver function tests (LFTs): To assess for underlying liver disease, which can impact coagulation.
    • Coagulation screen (PT/INR, APTT): To identify coagulopathy that may exacerbate bleeding.
    • Group and Save/Crossmatch: In anticipation of potential blood transfusion.
    • Oesophago-gastro-duodenoscopy (OGD): The definitive diagnostic procedure, allowing direct visualisation of the tear and therapeutic intervention if needed. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Oesophago-gastro-duodenoscopy (OGD): what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Endoscopy"][/sc]
  • Severity/risk tools:
    • Blatchford Score on MDCalc: Used to assess the need for intervention and predict outcomes in upper GI bleeding prior to endoscopy.
    • Rockall Score on MDCalc: Used to assess the risk of rebleeding and mortality after endoscopy.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible:
    • CT angiography: May be considered in cases of massive ongoing bleeding where endoscopy is inconclusive or technically difficult.
  • Serial reassessment:
    • Regular monitoring of vital signs, FBC, and clinical status to detect ongoing bleeding or haemodynamic deterioration.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Mallory-Weiss tear"][/sc]
  • A middle-aged male with a history of alcohol excess presents with acute onset of bright red haematemesis following an episode of forceful retching, accompanied by mild epigastric discomfort and dizziness.
  • An acutely unwell patient with upper gastrointestinal bleeding, precipitated by increased intra-abdominal pressure, concerning for a Mallory-Weiss tear.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Mallory-Weiss tear"][/sc]
  • Peptic ulcer disease: More common cause of UGIB, often associated with NSAID use or H. pylori infection, but typically without preceding forceful vomiting.
  • Oesophageal varices: Seen in patients with portal hypertension (e.g., chronic liver disease), often presenting with massive, painless haematemesis.
  • Oesophagitis/Gastritis: Inflammation of the oesophagus or stomach lining, can cause UGIB but usually less severe and without a clear precipitating event like forceful vomiting.
  • Boerhaave syndrome: A full-thickness oesophageal rupture, a much more severe condition with excruciating chest/epigastric pain, fever, and signs of sepsis, often following forceful vomiting. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Boerhaave syndrome vs Mallory-Weiss tear: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Boerhaave syndrome distinction"][/sc]
  • Gastric malignancy: Can present with chronic low-grade bleeding or acute UGIB, but usually associated with weight loss, anorexia, and persistent symptoms.
  • Arteriovenous malformation (AVM): Rare cause of UGIB, often intermittent and can be difficult to diagnose endoscopically.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Mallory-Weiss tear"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Initial management focuses on resuscitation with intravenous fluids and blood products as needed, airway protection if altered consciousness, and prompt assessment of bleeding severity. Escalation to a senior clinician and gastroenterology team is often warranted.
  • Definitive management: The majority of Mallory-Weiss tears are self-limiting and resolve spontaneously. Endoscopic therapy (e.g., adrenaline injection, clip application, thermal coagulation) may be considered for actively bleeding tears or those with stigmata of recent haemorrhage.
  • Adjunctive management: Proton pump inhibitors (PPIs) are often prescribed to reduce gastric acid secretion, which may promote healing and reduce the risk of rebleeding, although evidence for their efficacy in Mallory-Weiss tears is less robust than for peptic ulcers.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Close observation for signs of rebleeding or complications is essential. Patients should be advised to avoid triggers such as excessive alcohol intake and medications that irritate the gastric mucosa.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Patients should be educated on red flag symptoms indicating rebleeding or complications and advised to seek immediate medical attention if these occur.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Mallory-Weiss tear"][/sc]
  • Anaemia: Significant or prolonged bleeding can lead to iron deficiency anaemia, manifesting as fatigue, pallor, and dyspnoea.
  • Hypovolaemic shock: Massive acute bleeding can result in haemodynamic instability, requiring urgent fluid resuscitation and blood transfusion.
  • Recurrent bleeding: Although most tears are self-limiting, rebleeding can occur, particularly within the first 24-48 hours, necessitating further endoscopic intervention.
  • Oesophageal perforation: A rare but life-threatening complication, especially if the tear extends through all layers of the oesophageal wall (Boerhaave syndrome), leading to mediastinitis and sepsis.
  • Aspiration pneumonitis: In patients with impaired consciousness or persistent vomiting, aspiration of gastric contents can lead to lung inflammation and infection.
