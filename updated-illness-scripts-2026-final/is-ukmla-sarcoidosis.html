Sarcoidosis [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Sarcoidosis"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Sarcoidosis"][/sc]
  • Sarcoidosis is a multisystem granulomatous disorder of unknown aetiology, characterised by the formation of non-caseating granulomas in affected organs.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: recognise the varied clinical presentations of sarcoidosis, particularly pulmonary and extrapulmonary manifestations, and understand the diagnostic approach.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Sarcoidosis"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Sarcoidosis"][/sc]
  • Incidence in the UK is estimated at 5-10 per 100,000 population, with a higher prevalence in individuals of African or Afro-Caribbean descent (NICE).
  • Typically affects young to middle-aged adults (20-50 years), with a slight female predominance.
  • Risk factors are largely unknown, but genetic predisposition and environmental triggers (e.g., occupational exposures, infections) are thought to play a role.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Sarcoidosis"][/sc]
  • Triggering event (e.g., environmental antigen, genetic predisposition) leads to an exaggerated immune response in genetically susceptible individuals.
  • Accumulation of activated T-lymphocytes and macrophages results in the formation of non-caseating granulomas, primarily in the lungs and lymph nodes.
  • Granuloma formation can disrupt normal organ architecture and function, leading to fibrosis and organ damage, particularly in chronic or progressive disease.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Sarcoidosis"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Acute onset, often presenting with Löfgren's syndrome (erythema nodosum, bilateral hilar lymphadenopathy, arthralgia) or Heerfordt's syndrome (uveitis, parotitis, facial nerve palsy, fever), typically resolving spontaneously within months.
  • Progressive phase: Symptoms persist or worsen beyond a few months, particularly beyond 6-12 months, with increasing organ involvement and potential for fibrosis, requiring systemic treatment.
  • Late phase: Chronic sarcoidosis, persisting for years, often leading to irreversible organ damage (e.g., pulmonary fibrosis, cardiac arrhythmias) and significant morbidity, particularly beyond 2 years.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Sarcoidosis"][/sc]
  • Ask about persistent dry cough - often the most common pulmonary symptom, indicating airway or parenchymal involvement.
  • Ask about dyspnoea - can range from mild exertional to severe, reflecting the extent of lung disease.
  • Ask about fatigue - a debilitating symptom, often disproportionate to other findings, significantly impacting quality of life.
  • Ask about skin lesions (e.g., erythema nodosum, lupus pernio) - visible manifestations that can be key diagnostic clues.
  • Ask about blurred vision or eye pain - suggestive of ocular sarcoidosis (uveitis), which can lead to blindness if untreated.
  • Ask about arthralgia - joint pain, particularly in acute presentations like Löfgren's syndrome.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Sarcoidosis"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - may reveal tachycardia or tachypnoea in severe pulmonary involvement, or fever in acute presentations.
  • Check for lymphadenopathy - palpable enlarged lymph nodes, especially cervical, supraclavicular, or axillary, are common.
  • Check skin for lesions - erythema nodosum (tender red nodules on shins) or lupus pernio (violaceous plaques on face/nose) are highly suggestive.
  • Check eyes for uveitis - slit lamp examination may show inflammatory cells, though often asymptomatic initially.
  • Check for crackles or wheeze on chest auscultation - may indicate parenchymal inflammation or airway involvement, though lungs can be clear.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Sarcoidosis"][/sc]
  • Progressive dyspnoea: Suggests worsening pulmonary involvement or fibrosis, requiring urgent assessment and potential treatment escalation.
  • New neurological symptoms: Indicates neurosarcoidosis, which can be severe and requires prompt diagnosis and management to prevent irreversible damage.
  • Cardiac arrhythmias or syncope: Points to cardiac sarcoidosis, a potentially life-threatening complication due to conduction abnormalities or cardiomyopathy.
  • Hypercalcaemia: Can lead to renal impairment, polyuria, and confusion, requiring immediate management.
  • Visual impairment: Suggests active ocular sarcoidosis (uveitis) that needs urgent ophthalmological review to prevent permanent vision loss.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Sarcoidosis"][/sc]
  • Older adults: May present with more insidious onset, often with extrapulmonary manifestations and less typical acute syndromes, leading to diagnostic delay.
  • Asymptomatic presentation: Often discovered incidentally on chest X-ray (e.g., bilateral hilar lymphadenopathy) performed for other reasons, requiring careful monitoring.
  • Isolated organ involvement: Can present with symptoms restricted to a single organ (e.g., neurosarcoidosis, cardiac sarcoidosis) without typical pulmonary features, making diagnosis challenging.
  • Childhood sarcoidosis: Rare, often presenting with skin, joint, and eye involvement, differing from adult pulmonary predominance.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Sarcoidosis"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected Sarcoidosis: Chest X-ray (bilateral hilar lymphadenopathy, parenchymal infiltrates), pulmonary function tests (restrictive pattern, reduced diffusion capacity), serum angiotensin-converting enzyme (ACE) level (elevated in ~60% but non-specific).
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Full blood count (anaemia, leucopenia), liver function tests (granulomatous hepatitis), renal function tests (hypercalcaemia, renal involvement), ECG (cardiac sarcoidosis).
  • Imaging - CT chest: High-resolution CT (HRCT) provides detailed assessment of pulmonary involvement, including fibrosis and mediastinal lymphadenopathy.
  • Histological confirmation: Biopsy of affected tissue (e.g., lymph node, skin, lung) showing non-caseating granulomas is often required for definitive diagnosis.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Sarcoidosis"][/sc]
  • A 35-year-old female presents with a 3-month history of worsening dry cough, exertional dyspnoea, and significant fatigue, accompanied by tender red nodules on her shins.
  • A young adult female with subacute, progressive constitutional and respiratory symptoms, alongside characteristic dermatological findings, suggesting a multisystem inflammatory granulomatous process.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Sarcoidosis"][/sc]
  • Tuberculosis - caseating granulomas, positive acid-fast bacilli, often specific geographical/exposure history.
  • Lymphoma - B symptoms, often more aggressive lymphadenopathy, different histological features.
  • Hypersensitivity pneumonitis - clear exposure history to inhaled antigens, often acute onset after exposure.
  • Connective tissue diseases (e.g., Sjögren's syndrome) - specific autoantibodies, other systemic features.
  • Fungal infections - specific travel history, positive fungal cultures, different granuloma morphology.
  • Drug-induced lung disease - clear temporal relationship to medication initiation.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Sarcoidosis"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Assess for critical organ involvement (e.g., cardiac, neurological, severe hypercalcaemia) requiring urgent intervention.
  • Definitive management: Watchful waiting for asymptomatic or mild disease (spontaneous remission is common). Systemic corticosteroids (e.g., prednisolone) are the mainstay for progressive or symptomatic disease, or critical organ involvement.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Regular clinical review, pulmonary function tests, chest imaging, and assessment for extrapulmonary manifestations to monitor disease activity and treatment response.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: For refractory disease or steroid intolerance, consider steroid-sparing agents (e.g., methotrexate, azathioprine, anti-TNF agents). Educate patients on symptoms of worsening disease or complications.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Sarcoidosis"][/sc]
  • Pulmonary fibrosis: Occurs in chronic sarcoidosis, leading to irreversible lung damage, severe dyspnoea, and respiratory failure, particularly in late-stage disease.
  • Cardiac sarcoidosis: Granulomatous infiltration of the heart can cause arrhythmias, heart block, cardiomyopathy, and sudden cardiac death, often presenting in the progressive phase.
  • Neurosarcoidosis: Granulomas affecting the central or peripheral nervous system can lead to cranial neuropathies (e.g., facial nerve palsy), seizures, meningitis, or myelopathy, requiring prompt recognition.
  • Hypercalcaemia and nephrolithiasis: Due to increased vitamin D activation by granulomas, leading to renal impairment and kidney stones, can occur at any stage.
  • Ocular complications: Chronic uveitis can lead to glaucoma, cataracts, and permanent vision loss if not adequately treated, particularly in progressive or late disease.
