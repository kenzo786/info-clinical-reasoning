Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="HIV (Infections)"][/sc]
  • Human Immunodeficiency Virus (HIV) is a retrovirus that primarily infects CD4+ T-lymphocytes, leading to progressive immune system impairment and increased susceptibility to opportunistic infections and certain cancers.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical manifestations of HIV infection, including seroconversion illness and AIDS-defining conditions, and understand the principles of diagnosis and antiretroviral therapy.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="HIV (Infections)"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="HIV (Infections)"][/sc]
  • In 2024, there were 3,043 new HIV diagnoses in the UK, with 2,773 in England, representing a 4% decrease from 2023.
  • Globally, HIV-1 is the most common type, responsible for the vast majority of infections worldwide.
  • Risk factors include unprotected sexual intercourse (vaginal, anal, oral), sharing needles or drug injecting equipment, and mother-to-child transmission during pregnancy, birth, or breastfeeding.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="HIV (Infections)"][/sc]
  • Trigger: HIV primarily targets CD4+ T-lymphocytes, using the CD4 receptor and co-receptors (CCR5 or CXCR4) to enter and replicate within these immune cells.
  • Progression: Viral replication leads to the destruction of CD4+ T-cells, progressively depleting the immune system and impairing both cellular and humoral immunity. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="CD4 count and viral load in HIV: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="CD4 count and viral load"][/sc]
  • Consequence: Profound immunosuppression eventually leads to Acquired Immunodeficiency Syndrome (AIDS), characterised by opportunistic infections, neurological complications, and certain malignancies.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="HIV (Infections)"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Acute HIV infection (seroconversion illness) typically occurs 2-6 weeks post-exposure, presenting with flu-like symptoms that usually resolve spontaneously within a few weeks.
  • Progressive phase: Clinical latency, an asymptomatic period, can last for many years (often 8-10 years without treatment), during which the virus continues to replicate and CD4 cell counts gradually decline.
  • Late phase: Advanced HIV infection or AIDS develops when the CD4 count falls below 200 cells/mm³ or AIDS-defining illnesses occur, leading to severe opportunistic infections and cancers.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="HIV (Infections)"][/sc]
  • Ask about fever - often a prominent feature of acute seroconversion illness, indicating early viral dissemination.
  • Ask about sore throat and rash - common non-specific symptoms during seroconversion, which can be easily mistaken for other viral infections.
  • Ask about swollen glands (lymphadenopathy) - persistent generalised lymphadenopathy is a common sign of chronic HIV infection, reflecting ongoing immune activation.
  • Ask about unexplained weight loss or chronic diarrhoea - suggestive of advanced HIV disease or opportunistic infections.
  • Ask about neurological symptoms (e.g., headache, cognitive changes) - may indicate HIV-associated neurocognitive disorder (HAND) or central nervous system opportunistic infections.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="HIV (Infections)"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - fever may be present during acute seroconversion or opportunistic infections; otherwise, often normal in asymptomatic chronic HIV.
  • Check lymph nodes - persistent generalised lymphadenopathy (PGL) is common, particularly in the cervical, axillary, and inguinal regions.
  • Check oral cavity - oral candidiasis (thrush) or hairy leukoplakia are common early indicators of immune compromise. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Oral candidiasis in HIV: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Oral candidiasis"][/sc]
  • Check skin - various dermatological manifestations, including maculopapular rash during seroconversion, seborrhoeic dermatitis, or Kaposi sarcoma lesions in advanced disease.
  • Check fundoscopy - for signs of cytomegalovirus (CMV) retinitis in advanced immunosuppression.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="HIV (Infections)"][/sc]
  • New onset neurological symptoms: May indicate opportunistic CNS infections (e.g., toxoplasmosis, cryptococcal meningitis), primary CNS lymphoma, or progressive multifocal leukoencephalopathy (PML), particularly in late-stage disease.
  • Unexplained fever and weight loss: Suggestive of advanced immunosuppression and potential underlying opportunistic infections or malignancies.
  • Dyspnoea and cough: Especially if progressive, may indicate *Pneumocystis jirovecii* pneumonia (PCP), a common AIDS-defining illness.
  • New or worsening visual disturbance: Could be a sign of CMV retinitis, requiring urgent ophthalmological review to prevent blindness.
  • Severe mucocutaneous candidiasis: Extensive or recurrent oral/oesophageal candidiasis can signify significant immune compromise.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="HIV (Infections)"][/sc]
  • Older adults: Diagnosis may be delayed due to non-specific symptoms attributed to ageing, leading to presentation with advanced disease.
  • Women: May present with recurrent vaginal candidiasis, pelvic inflammatory disease, or cervical dysplasia, which can be overlooked as HIV indicators.
  • Pregnancy: Early diagnosis and treatment are crucial to prevent mother-to-child transmission, often identified through routine antenatal screening.
  • Immunosuppressed (non-HIV related): Co-infection with HIV can complicate management and lead to more aggressive disease progression, requiring careful differentiation. Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="HIV (Infections)"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected HIV:
    • Fourth-generation HIV antigen/antibody immunoassay - detects both HIV p24 antigen (early infection) and antibodies (later infection), with high sensitivity and specificity.
    • HIV viral load (HIV RNA PCR) - used to confirm diagnosis, monitor treatment response, and assess disease progression.
  • Severity/risk tools:
    • CD4 T-lymphocyte count - essential for staging HIV infection, guiding prophylactic treatment for opportunistic infections, and assessing immune status. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="CD4 thresholds for opportunistic infections: what are they and why do they matter clinically?" text="CD4 thresholds"][/sc]
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible:
    • Full blood count, liver and renal function tests, syphilis serology, hepatitis B and C screening - to assess overall health, co-infections, and potential treatment toxicities.
  • Imaging - Chest X-ray: To investigate respiratory symptoms, particularly for *Pneumocystis jirovecii* pneumonia (PCP) or tuberculosis.
  • Serial reassessment: Regular monitoring of CD4 count and viral load is crucial to assess disease progression and response to antiretroviral therapy (ART).
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="HIV (Infections)"][/sc]
  • A 35-year-old male presents with a 3-week history of fever, widespread maculopapular rash, and generalised lymphadenopathy following unprotected sexual intercourse.
  • A young adult with acute flu-like illness and risk factors for exposure, concerning for acute HIV seroconversion.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="HIV (Infections)"][/sc]
  • Infectious mononucleosis - typically presents with pharyngitis, lymphadenopathy, and fatigue, but HIV seroconversion lacks heterophile antibodies.
  • Secondary syphilis - can cause a widespread rash and lymphadenopathy, but serology will differentiate from HIV.
  • Other viral exanthems (e.g., measles, rubella) - distinguished by specific rash characteristics, epidemiological context, and absence of HIV risk factors.
  • Tuberculosis - can present with constitutional symptoms and pulmonary involvement, often an opportunistic infection in advanced HIV. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Tuberculosis in HIV: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Tuberculosis"][/sc]
  • Lymphoma - may cause lymphadenopathy, fever, and weight loss, but typically progresses more rapidly and has distinct histological features.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="HIV (Infections)"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Recognition of acute seroconversion or advanced disease, prompt initiation of antiretroviral therapy (ART), and management of opportunistic infections.
  • Definitive management: Antiretroviral therapy (ART) is the cornerstone of treatment, typically involving a combination of three or more antiretroviral drugs from at least two different classes to suppress viral replication and restore immune function.
  • Adjunctive management: Prophylaxis against opportunistic infections (e.g., co-trimoxazole for PCP) is often warranted based on CD4 count thresholds.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Regular monitoring of HIV viral load, CD4 count, and screening for co-morbidities and treatment side effects is essential.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Patients with new diagnoses or advanced disease should be managed by specialist HIV services; education on adherence and prevention of transmission is vital.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="HIV (Infections)"][/sc]
  • Opportunistic infections: Occur particularly in late-stage disease with low CD4 counts, including *Pneumocystis jirovecii* pneumonia (PCP), toxoplasmosis, cryptococcal meningitis, and cytomegalovirus (CMV) disease, indicating severe immunosuppression.
  • AIDS-defining malignancies: Kaposi sarcoma, non-Hodgkin lymphoma, and invasive cervical cancer are more common in advanced HIV due to immune dysregulation.
  • HIV-associated neurocognitive disorders (HAND): A spectrum of cognitive, motor, and behavioural impairments ranging from mild to severe dementia, resulting from direct HIV effects on the central nervous system.
  • Wasting syndrome: Characterised by involuntary weight loss, chronic diarrhoea, and weakness, often seen in advanced untreated HIV.
  • Renal disease: HIV-associated nephropathy (HIVAN) and other kidney disorders can develop, contributing to morbidity.
