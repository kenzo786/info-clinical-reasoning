Sjogren syndrome [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Sjogren syndrome"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Sjogren syndrome"][/sc]
  • Sjogren syndrome is a chronic, systemic autoimmune disease characterised by lymphocytic infiltration of exocrine glands, primarily the lacrimal and salivary glands, leading to sicca symptoms (dry eyes and mouth).
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical features, diagnostic criteria, and potential systemic manifestations of Sjogren syndrome, differentiating it from other causes of sicca symptoms.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Sjogren syndrome"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Sjogren syndrome"][/sc]
  • Sjogren syndrome affects approximately 0.1-0.4% of the UK population, with a significant female predominance (9:1 ratio) and typical onset in middle age (40-60 years).
  • Risk factors include genetic predisposition (e.g., HLA-DR3, HLA-B8), female sex, and association with other autoimmune conditions such as rheumatoid arthritis or systemic lupus erythematosus.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Sjogren syndrome"][/sc]
  • Trigger: Genetic susceptibility combined with environmental factors (e.g., viral infections) may initiate an autoimmune response targeting exocrine glands.
  • Progression: Activated B and T lymphocytes infiltrate glandular tissue, leading to chronic inflammation and destruction of secretory cells.
  • Consequence: Impaired glandular function results in reduced tear and saliva production, causing sicca symptoms and increasing susceptibility to local infections and systemic complications.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Sjogren syndrome"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Insidious onset of dry eyes and mouth, often developing gradually over months to years, with initial symptoms being mild and intermittent.
  • Progressive phase: Worsening sicca symptoms, particularly beyond several years, leading to significant discomfort, dental issues, and potential development of extraglandular manifestations.
  • Late phase: Chronic disease with established sicca symptoms and increased risk of severe systemic complications, including lymphoma, particularly beyond a decade from symptom onset.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Sjogren syndrome"][/sc]
  • Ask about dry eyes (xerophthalmia) - often described as gritty, sandy, or burning sensation, indicating ocular surface damage.
  • Ask about dry mouth (xerostomia) - difficulty chewing, swallowing, or speaking, suggesting impaired salivary gland function.
  • Ask about fatigue - a common and often debilitating systemic symptom, impacting quality of life significantly.
  • Ask about joint pain (arthralgia) - typically migratory and non-erosive, differentiating it from inflammatory arthropathies.
  • Ask about vaginal dryness - leading to dyspareunia, reflecting involvement of other exocrine glands.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Sjogren syndrome"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - usually normal, but may reveal low-grade fever in acute flares or systemic involvement.
  • Check ocular examination - conjunctival injection, corneal erosions (fluorescein staining), or reduced tear film breakup time.
  • Check oral examination - dry, fissured tongue, dental caries, or absence of salivary pooling in the floor of the mouth.
  • Check parotid gland enlargement - persistent or recurrent swelling, indicating glandular inflammation.
  • Check skin examination - purpura, vasculitic rash, or Raynaud's phenomenon, suggesting extraglandular manifestations.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Sjogren syndrome"][/sc]
  • Persistent parotid swelling - raises concern for lymphoma, a significant complication of Sjogren syndrome.
  • Progressive neurological symptoms - may indicate central nervous system involvement, such as myelopathy or neuropathy.
  • New onset weakness or sensory changes - suggests peripheral neuropathy or myositis, requiring urgent investigation.
  • Unexplained weight loss or fever - could signify systemic vasculitis or lymphoproliferative disorder.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Sjogren syndrome"][/sc]
  • Older adults: Sicca symptoms may be attributed to age-related changes or polypharmacy, delaying diagnosis and management.
  • Men: Less common in males, leading to lower index of suspicion and potential misdiagnosis or delayed recognition.
  • Early systemic features: Some patients may present with extraglandular manifestations (e.g., vasculitis, neuropathy) before prominent sicca symptoms develop, making diagnosis challenging.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Sjogren syndrome"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected Sjogren syndrome:
    • Autoantibodies: Anti-Ro/SSA and Anti-La/SSB are highly specific.
    • Schirmer's test: Measures tear production (less than 5mm in 5 minutes is abnormal).
    • Unstimulated whole salivary flow: Measures saliva production (less than 0.1 mL/min is abnormal).
    • Minor salivary gland biopsy: Demonstrates focal lymphocytic sialadenitis (focus score ≥1).
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible:
    • Full blood count, ESR, CRP: To assess for inflammation or anaemia.
    • Rheumatoid factor, ANA: To screen for other autoimmune conditions.
    • Thyroid function tests: To exclude thyroid disease, which can cause sicca symptoms.
  • Imaging - Sialography/Salivary gland ultrasound: To assess for ductal damage or glandular enlargement.
  • Validated score: EULAR Sjogren's Syndrome Disease Activity Index (ESSDAI) on MDCalc for disease activity assessment.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Sjogren syndrome"][/sc]
  • A 55-year-old woman presents with a 2-year history of progressive dry eyes and mouth, difficulty swallowing, and persistent fatigue, alongside intermittent migratory arthralgia.
  • This is a middle-aged female with chronic, insidious onset of sicca symptoms and systemic features, suggestive of a systemic autoimmune disorder affecting exocrine glands.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Sjogren syndrome"][/sc]
  • Age-related sicca - absence of systemic features and specific autoantibodies.
  • Medication-induced dry mouth/eyes - history of anticholinergic drugs or other medications with drying side effects.
  • Systemic lupus erythematosus (SLE) - presence of other specific SLE criteria (e.g., malar rash, serositis, renal involvement).
  • Rheumatoid arthritis (RA) - erosive arthritis, specific RA autoantibodies (RF, anti-CCP), and less prominent sicca symptoms.
  • Sarcoidosis - granulomatous inflammation in other organs (e.g., lungs, skin), often with elevated ACE levels.
  • Amyloidosis - macroglossia, periorbital purpura, and evidence of amyloid deposition in tissues.
