Osteomalacia [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Osteomalacia"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Osteomalacia"][/sc]
  • Osteomalacia is a metabolic bone disease in adults characterised by inadequate mineralisation of newly formed osteoid, leading to soft, weak bones that are prone to bending and fracture.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical features, risk factors, and biochemical abnormalities associated with osteomalacia, and outline initial diagnostic steps and management principles.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Osteomalacia"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Osteomalacia"][/sc]
  • Vitamin D deficiency, the primary cause of osteomalacia, is a significant public health concern in the UK, particularly among certain population groups [1].
  • Prevalence is higher in individuals with limited sun exposure, darker skin pigmentation, malabsorption syndromes, and chronic kidney disease [2].
  • While exact UK prevalence data for osteomalacia itself is limited, studies indicate a notable incidence of biochemical osteomalacia in adults undergoing vitamin D testing [3].
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Osteomalacia"][/sc]
  • Inadequate vitamin D levels lead to reduced intestinal absorption of calcium and phosphate, triggering secondary hyperparathyroidism [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Secondary hyperparathyroidism: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="secondary hyperparathyroidism"][/sc].
  • Elevated parathyroid hormone (PTH) attempts to normalise serum calcium by increasing renal phosphate excretion and bone resorption, but persistently low calcium and phosphate impair bone mineralisation.
  • The accumulation of unmineralised osteoid, a hallmark of osteomalacia, results in weakened bone structure and increased susceptibility to microfractures and deformities.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Osteomalacia"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Often asymptomatic or presents with vague, non-specific symptoms such as generalised fatigue and mild muscle weakness, developing insidiously over months.
  • Progressive phase: Worsening bone pain, particularly in the axial skeleton and proximal limbs, and increasing muscle weakness, leading to a waddling gait and difficulty with mobility, typically evolving over several months to a year.
  • Late phase: Development of skeletal deformities, pathological fractures (e.g., Looser zones), and severe functional impairment, which can manifest after prolonged deficiency spanning years.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Osteomalacia"][/sc]
  • Ask about bone pain - often diffuse, aching, and symmetrical, worse with weight-bearing, distinguishing it from localised pain.
  • Ask about muscle weakness - typically proximal (thighs, pelvic girdle), leading to difficulty rising from a chair or climbing stairs, indicating myopathy.
  • Ask about waddling gait - a consequence of proximal muscle weakness and bone pain, which can be a key discriminator from other musculoskeletal conditions.
  • Ask about paraesthesia - tingling or numbness, particularly in severe cases due to hypocalcaemia, suggesting neurological involvement.
  • Ask about history of malabsorption or dietary restrictions - crucial for identifying underlying causes of vitamin D deficiency.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Osteomalacia"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - usually normal, but may reveal tachycardia if severe pain is present.
  • Check muscle strength - proximal muscle weakness (e.g., inability to stand from squat without support) is a key finding.
  • Check gait - a waddling gait may be observed due to pelvic girdle weakness [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Waddling gait: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="waddling gait"][/sc].
  • Check bone tenderness - diffuse tenderness on palpation, particularly over the tibia, sternum, and pelvis, is characteristic.
  • Check for skeletal deformities - bowing of long bones, kyphoscoliosis, or pelvic distortion in advanced disease.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Osteomalacia"][/sc]
  • Pathological fractures: Suggests severe bone demineralisation and warrants urgent investigation to prevent further morbidity.
  • Severe proximal myopathy: Rapidly progressive muscle weakness impacting mobility and increasing fall risk, requiring prompt intervention.
  • Hypocalcaemic tetany: Manifestations such as perioral numbness, carpopedal spasm, or seizures indicate critically low calcium levels requiring immediate correction.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Osteomalacia"][/sc]
  • Older adults: May present primarily with falls or non-specific fatigue, often misdiagnosed as age-related sarcopenia or osteoporosis.
  • Patients with chronic kidney disease: Renal osteodystrophy [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Renal osteodystrophy: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="renal osteodystrophy"][/sc] can coexist, complicating diagnosis and management, with symptoms often masked by other uraemic features.
  • Post-gastrectomy patients: Increased risk due to malabsorption of vitamin D and calcium, presenting with bone pain and weakness years after surgery.
  • Patients on certain medications: Anticonvulsants (e.g., phenytoin) or antiretrovirals can interfere with vitamin D metabolism, leading to drug-induced osteomalacia.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Osteomalacia"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected Osteomalacia:
    • Serum 25-hydroxyvitamin D (25(OH)D): Low levels (<25 nmol/L) are diagnostic of vitamin D deficiency.
    • Serum calcium: Often low or low-normal.
    • Serum phosphate: Typically low.
    • Serum alkaline phosphatase (ALP): Usually elevated due to increased osteoblastic activity.
    • Parathyroid hormone (PTH): Often elevated (secondary hyperparathyroidism).
  • Imaging - X-ray: May reveal characteristic findings such as reduced bone density, cortical thinning, and pseudofractures (Looser zones), particularly in the pelvis, femoral necks, and ribs [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Looser zones and pseudofractures: what are they and why do they matter clinically?" text="Looser zones and pseudofractures"][/sc].
  • Imaging - Bone biopsy: Gold standard, showing increased unmineralised osteoid seams, though rarely performed clinically.
  • Severity/risk tools: Consider FRAX Fracture Risk Assessment Tool on MDCalc for fracture risk assessment, especially to distinguish from osteoporosis.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Osteomalacia"][/sc]
  • A middle-aged individual presenting with chronic, diffuse bone pain, progressive proximal muscle weakness, and a waddling gait, alongside biochemical evidence of vitamin D deficiency, hypocalcaemia, hypophosphataemia, and elevated alkaline phosphatase.
  • Chronic musculoskeletal pain and weakness with biochemical evidence of impaired bone mineralisation, concerning for osteomalacia.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Osteomalacia"][/sc]
  • Osteoporosis - characterised by reduced bone mass and microarchitectural deterioration, but with normally mineralised bone; typically presents with fragility fractures without bone pain or muscle weakness unless a fracture has occurred [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Osteoporosis vs. Osteomalacia: what are the key differences and why do they matter clinically?" text="osteoporosis"][/sc].
  • Fibromyalgia - presents with widespread musculoskeletal pain and fatigue, but typically lacks objective signs of muscle weakness, biochemical abnormalities, or radiological changes of osteomalacia.
  • Polymyalgia Rheumatica - presents with pain and stiffness in the shoulders and hip girdles, particularly in older adults, but is associated with elevated inflammatory markers and responds to corticosteroids, unlike osteomalacia.
  • Metastatic bone disease - can cause severe bone pain and pathological fractures, but usually in the context of a known primary malignancy and often with focal lesions on imaging, rather than diffuse demineralisation.
  • Hypophosphataemic rickets/osteomalacia - a group of disorders characterised by renal phosphate wasting, leading to hypophosphataemia and impaired mineralisation, but often with normal vitamin D and calcium levels.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Osteomalacia"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Recognition of severe symptoms (e.g., pathological fracture, severe hypocalcaemia) and appropriate escalation for supportive care and rapid correction of electrolyte imbalances.
  • Definitive management: Repletion of vitamin D and calcium. This often includes high-dose oral vitamin D supplementation (e.g., colecalciferol) followed by maintenance, alongside adequate dietary calcium intake.
  • Adjunctive management: Addressing underlying causes such as malabsorption syndromes (e.g., coeliac disease, chronic pancreatitis) or renal disease is crucial for long-term success.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Regular monitoring of biochemical markers (25(OH)D, calcium, phosphate, ALP, PTH) to ensure treatment efficacy and guide dose adjustments, typically every 3-6 months until normalisation.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Patients should be advised on symptoms of worsening bone pain, muscle weakness, or signs of hypocalcaemia, and when to seek urgent medical review.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Osteomalacia"][/sc]
  • Pathological fractures: Occur due to severely weakened bones, particularly in the ribs, vertebrae, and long bones, leading to acute pain and significant morbidity if not addressed in the progressive or late phases.
  • Skeletal deformities: Persistent bone softening can result in bowing of long bones (e.g., tibia, femur), kyphoscoliosis, or pelvic distortion, especially after prolonged deficiency spanning years.
  • Severe hypocalcaemia: Critically low calcium levels can lead to neurological complications such as tetany, paraesthesia, and seizures, typically in the late phase if underlying vitamin D deficiency is uncorrected.
  • Chronic pain and disability: Unresolved bone pain and muscle weakness can lead to significant functional impairment, reduced quality of life, and increased dependence, persisting into the late phase.
