Allergic disorder (including food allergy) [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Allergic disorder (including food allergy)"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Allergic disorder (including food allergy)"][/sc]
  • An allergic disorder, such as a food allergy, is an adverse immune-mediated response to specific substances (allergens) that the body mistakenly identifies as harmful [2, 6].
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical presentation and potential severity of allergic disorders, including food allergy [9].
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Allergic disorder (including food allergy)"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Allergic disorder (including food allergy)"][/sc]
  • Approximately 6% of the UK adult population, equating to around 2.4 million individuals, are estimated to have a clinically confirmed food allergy [7].
  • The incidence of probable food allergy in England doubled between 2008 and 2018, rising from 75.8 to 159.5 individuals per 100,000 person-years [8, 10].
  • Common food allergens include peanuts, tree nuts, milk, egg, fish, shellfish, wheat, and soya, with early childhood being a period of particularly high prevalence [3, 14].
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Allergic disorder (including food allergy)"][/sc]
  • Trigger: Initial exposure to an allergen leads to sensitisation, where the immune system produces allergen-specific IgE antibodies that bind to mast cells and basophils [17, 19].
  • Progression: Subsequent exposure to the same allergen cross-links the IgE antibodies on sensitised mast cells, triggering the release of inflammatory mediators such as histamine, leukotrienes, and prostaglandins [18, 20].
  • Consequence: These mediators cause a cascade of effects, including vasodilation, increased vascular permeability, smooth muscle contraction, and tissue oedema, leading to the diverse clinical manifestations of an allergic reaction, ranging from mild symptoms to life-threatening anaphylaxis [18, 20].
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Allergic disorder (including food allergy)"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Symptoms typically manifest rapidly, often within minutes to two hours of allergen exposure, reflecting immediate immune mediator release [21, 26].
  • Progressive phase: In some cases, symptoms may evolve or worsen over several hours, particularly beyond the initial two-hour window, potentially indicating a biphasic reaction or delayed onset of more severe manifestations [21, 23].
  • Late phase: While most reactions resolve within hours, some individuals may experience a recurrence of symptoms several hours later, or persistent symptoms that take days to subside, necessitating ongoing monitoring [23].
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Allergic disorder (including food allergy)"][/sc]
  • Ask about itchy skin or a raised rash (hives) - these are common cutaneous manifestations, indicating histamine release and can range from mild to widespread [27, 30].
  • Ask about swelling of the lips, face, and eyes (angioedema) - this suggests deeper tissue involvement and can compromise airways if severe [28, 32].
  • Ask about difficulty breathing, wheezing, or nasal congestion - these respiratory symptoms point to airway inflammation and constriction, which can be life-threatening [27, 28].
  • Ask about abdominal pain, nausea, vomiting, or diarrhoea - gastrointestinal symptoms are frequent, reflecting immune reactions within the digestive tract [29, 31].
  • Ask about feeling dizzy or lightheaded - these may indicate cardiovascular compromise, particularly in severe reactions like anaphylaxis [27, 34].
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Allergic disorder (including food allergy)"][/sc]
  • Check for urticaria (hives) or angioedema - these visible skin and subcutaneous swellings are direct evidence of allergic inflammation [36, 37].
  • Check respiratory effort, including wheezing or stridor - these indicate airway obstruction, ranging from lower airway bronchospasm to upper airway laryngeal oedema [35, 37].
  • Check cardiovascular status, including pulse rate and blood pressure - a rapid, weak pulse and hypotension are critical signs of anaphylactic shock [35, 40].
  • Check for pallor or cyanosis - these suggest poor perfusion or hypoxaemia, respectively, and are signs of severe systemic compromise [35].
  • Check vital signs (heart rate, respiratory rate, blood pressure, oxygen saturation) - these provide an objective measure of systemic involvement and guide immediate management [35, 40].
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Allergic disorder (including food allergy)"][/sc]
  • Airway compromise: Swelling of the tongue or throat, hoarse voice, or stridor indicate impending airway obstruction, requiring urgent intervention [47].
  • Breathing difficulties: Persistent wheezing, severe shortness of breath, or rapid, shallow breathing suggest significant respiratory distress and potential anaphylaxis [47].
  • Circulatory collapse: Dizziness, faintness, pallor, cold clammy skin, or a rapid, weak pulse are signs of hypotension and shock, demanding immediate resuscitation [47].
  • Sudden onset and rapid progression: Symptoms developing within minutes of exposure and quickly worsening are highly indicative of a severe allergic reaction [41, 47].
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Allergic disorder (including food allergy)"][/sc]
  • Older adults: Food allergies can manifest differently in older individuals, often mimicking other age-related conditions like digestive problems, and symptoms may be more severe due to age-related immune changes [48, 50].
  • Pregnancy: Allergic reactions during pregnancy can have profound effects on maternal and foetal health, with potential risks including miscarriage or premature birth, necessitating careful management [52, 53].
  • Exercise-induced anaphylaxis: Symptoms may only occur when allergen exposure is followed by physical exertion, making diagnosis challenging as the link to food alone is not obvious [41].
  • Oral Allergy Syndrome (OAS): Localised itching or tingling in the mouth and throat after eating raw fruits or vegetables, often in individuals with pollen allergies, where symptoms are generally mild and short-lived [29, 33].
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Allergic disorder (including food allergy)"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected allergic disorder: A detailed clinical history is paramount, focusing on symptom onset, specific foods, quantity consumed, and reproducibility of reactions [55]. Skin prick tests (SPT) and specific IgE blood tests (e.g., ImmunoCAP) help identify sensitisation to common allergens [55, 56].
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Consider tests for food intolerance (e.g., hydrogen breath test for lactose intolerance) or other conditions that mimic allergic reactions, such as mastocytosis or carcinoid syndrome [43].
  • Imaging - Endoscopy: In cases of suspected eosinophilic oesophagitis (EoE), endoscopy with biopsies is required to confirm eosinophilic infiltration [38].
  • Oral food challenge: Under medical supervision, a graded oral food challenge remains the gold standard for confirming a food allergy, particularly when history and initial tests are inconclusive [55].
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Allergic disorder (including food allergy)"][/sc]
  • A 28-year-old female presents to the emergency department with acute onset generalised urticaria, facial swelling, and wheezing, developing within 30 minutes of consuming a prawn curry at a restaurant. She reports a known allergy to shellfish since childhood.
  • A young adult, with a history of atopy, presenting with rapid-onset multi-system allergic manifestations following dietary exposure to a known allergen, highly suggestive of anaphylaxis.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Allergic disorder (including food allergy)"][/sc]
  • Food intolerance - symptoms are typically gastrointestinal, dose-dependent, and do not involve an immune response [44].
  • Food poisoning - often presents with gastrointestinal symptoms, but usually affects multiple individuals consuming the same contaminated food, and lacks allergic features [41].
  • Anaphylaxis mimics (e.g., vasovagal syncope, panic attack) - these conditions lack the characteristic skin, respiratory, and gastrointestinal signs of anaphylaxis [41].
  • Scombroid poisoning - caused by bacterial spoilage of fish, leading to histamine-like symptoms (flushing, headache, palpitations), but is not immune-mediated [41].
  • Angioedema (hereditary or acquired) - presents with swelling, but typically without urticaria or pruritus, and may have a family history [41].
  • Mastocytosis - a rare condition characterised by excessive mast cell accumulation, leading to symptoms that can mimic severe allergic reactions [41].
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Allergic disorder (including food allergy)"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Ensure airway patency, support breathing with oxygen, and maintain circulation with intravenous fluids if hypotensive. Administer intramuscular adrenaline promptly for suspected anaphylaxis [47, 60].
  • Definitive management: Remove the allergen if possible. Administer antihistamines (e.g., oral cetirizine) for cutaneous symptoms and corticosteroids (e.g., oral prednisolone) for persistent or severe reactions [60].
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Observe patients for several hours post-reaction due to the risk of biphasic anaphylaxis. Provide an adrenaline auto-injector (AAI) and an emergency action plan for future reactions [60].
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Educate patients on allergen avoidance, how to recognise symptoms, and when to use their AAI. Refer to an allergy specialist for further assessment and management [60].

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Allergic disorder (including food allergy)"][/sc]
  • Anaphylaxis: A severe, life-threatening systemic allergic reaction involving multiple organ systems, characterised by rapid onset and potentially fatal airway obstruction, breathing difficulties, and circulatory collapse [47, 61].
  • Biphasic reaction: A recurrence of allergic symptoms after initial resolution, typically within 1-72 hours, due to continued mediator release or delayed immune responses, necessitating prolonged observation [23, 62].
  • Chronic allergic conditions: Persistent exposure or poorly managed allergies can lead to chronic conditions such as atopic dermatitis, asthma, or eosinophilic gastrointestinal disorders, impacting long-term quality of life [30, 38].
  • Psychological impact: The fear of accidental exposure and severe reactions can lead to significant anxiety, depression, and reduced quality of life for individuals and their families [63].
