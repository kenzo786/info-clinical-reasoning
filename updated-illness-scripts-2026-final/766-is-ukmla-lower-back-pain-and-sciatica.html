Lower back pain and sciatica [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Lower back pain and sciatica"] [/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Lower back pain and sciatica"] [/sc]
  • Lower back pain (LBP) is a common musculoskeletal symptom, often non-specific, while sciatica refers to radicular pain radiating down the leg due to sciatic nerve compression or irritation.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical features of lower back pain and sciatica, differentiate between mechanical and radicular causes, identify red flag symptoms, and outline initial management strategies.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Lower back pain and sciatica"] [/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Lower back pain and sciatica"] [/sc]
  • Lower back pain affects approximately 80% of adults at some point in their lives, with a significant proportion experiencing recurrent episodes; it is a leading cause of disability globally and in the UK.
  • Sciatica, a specific type of radicular pain, has an annual incidence of around 5 per 1,000 adults in the UK, with peak incidence between 30 and 50 years of age.
  • Risk factors include occupational lifting, prolonged sitting, obesity, smoking, and psychological distress such as anxiety and depression.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Lower back pain and sciatica"] [/sc]
  • Trigger: Mechanical stress or injury to spinal structures, such as intervertebral disc herniation, facet joint arthropathy, or muscle strain, often initiating an inflammatory response.
  • Progression: Inflammation and direct compression of a lumbar nerve root (e.g., by a prolapsed disc) leads to radicular pain, paraesthesia, and potentially motor weakness along the nerve's dermatomal and myotomal distribution.
  • Consequence: Persistent nociceptive input and neuropathic changes can lead to central sensitisation, chronic pain states, and significant functional impairment.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Lower back pain and sciatica"] [/sc]
  • Early phase: Acute lower back pain and sciatica typically resolve within 6-12 weeks, with most improvement occurring in the first few weeks.
  • Progressive phase: Symptoms persisting beyond 6-12 weeks are considered subacute or chronic, often indicating ongoing nerve irritation or central sensitisation.
  • Late phase: A small proportion of individuals develop persistent, disabling chronic lower back pain and sciatica, particularly beyond 12 weeks, requiring multidisciplinary management.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Lower back pain and sciatica"] [/sc]
  • Ask about back pain characteristics - to differentiate mechanical (worse with movement, better with rest) from inflammatory (worse with rest, better with movement) or radicular pain.
  • Ask about leg pain distribution - to determine if it follows a dermatomal pattern, suggesting nerve root involvement (sciatica).
  • Ask about paraesthesia or numbness - to assess for sensory nerve compromise, often accompanying radicular pain.
  • Ask about motor weakness - to identify potential motor nerve root compression, which may indicate more severe pathology.
  • Ask about bladder or bowel dysfunction - to screen for cauda equina syndrome, a neurological emergency.
  • Ask about fever, unexplained weight loss, or night sweats - to screen for systemic illness, infection, or malignancy.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Lower back pain and sciatica"] [/sc]
  • Check vital signs - to identify systemic signs of infection, inflammation, or malignancy (e.g., fever, tachycardia).
  • Check neurological examination (motor, sensory, reflexes) - to localise the affected nerve root and assess severity of neurological deficit.
  • Check straight leg raise test - a positive test (pain radiating below the knee at <60 degrees) is highly suggestive of lumbosacral nerve root irritation.
  • Check gait and posture - to observe antalgic gait, spinal deformity, or muscle spasm indicative of pain and functional limitation.
  • Check for saddle anaesthesia or reduced anal tone - to identify signs of cauda equina syndrome, requiring urgent referral.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Lower back pain and sciatica"] [/sc]
  • Cauda Equina Syndrome - new onset bladder or bowel dysfunction, saddle anaesthesia, bilateral sciatica, or progressive motor weakness, indicating urgent surgical assessment.
  • Spinal Infection (e.g., discitis, osteomyelitis) - fever, recent infection, intravenous drug use, immunosuppression, or persistent pain unresponsive to conservative management, suggesting need for imaging and blood tests.
  • Spinal Malignancy - history of cancer, unexplained weight loss, night pain, or pain unresponsive to rest, warranting investigation for metastatic disease.
  • Vertebral Fracture - history of trauma, osteoporosis, or prolonged corticosteroid use, particularly in older adults, requiring imaging to exclude fracture.
  • Severe Progressive Neurological Deficit - rapidly worsening motor weakness or sensory loss, indicating significant nerve compression and potential for irreversible damage.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Lower back pain and sciatica"] [/sc]
  • Older adults: May present with less pronounced pain but more significant neurological deficits due to spinal stenosis or osteoporotic fractures, which can be masked by other comorbidities.
  • Pregnancy: Physiological changes can exacerbate LBP and sciatica; distinguishing from obstetric causes or cauda equina syndrome requires careful assessment, often limiting imaging options.
  • Immunosuppressed patients: Higher risk of spinal infection (e.g., discitis, epidural abscess) presenting with atypical pain patterns, fever, and rapid progression, necessitating a lower threshold for investigation.
  • Psychological overlay: Chronic pain can be amplified by anxiety, depression, or somatisation, leading to disproportionate symptoms and functional impairment, requiring a biopsychosocial approach.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Lower back pain and sciatica"] [/sc]
  • Core for suspected mechanical LBP/sciatica: Clinical assessment is paramount; imaging is generally not recommended in the first 6 weeks unless red flags are present.
  • If red flags are present: Full blood count, ESR, C-reactive protein to screen for infection or inflammation.
  • Imaging - MRI Lumbar Spine: Indicated for red flag symptoms, progressive neurological deficit, or persistent radicular pain unresponsive to conservative management after 6-12 weeks, to visualise nerve root compression, disc pathology, or other structural causes.
  • Imaging - X-ray Lumbar Spine: Limited utility for LBP/sciatica but may be considered if fracture or spondyloarthropathy is suspected.
  • Serial reassessment: Regular clinical review to monitor symptom progression, identify new red flags, and assess response to conservative management.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Lower back pain and sciatica"] [/sc]
  • A 45-year-old male presents with a 3-week history of severe lower back pain radiating down his left leg to the foot, exacerbated by sitting and coughing, associated with numbness in the lateral calf. On examination, he has reduced sensation in the L5 dermatome and weakness on left foot dorsiflexion.
  • Middle-aged male with subacute, severe, radicular lower back pain and neurological deficit, concerning for lumbar disc herniation with L5 nerve root compression.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Lower back pain and sciatica"] [/sc]
  • Lumbar disc herniation - radicular pain in a dermatomal distribution, often exacerbated by Valsalva manoeuvres.
  • Spinal stenosis - neurogenic claudication, pain relieved by leaning forward, often bilateral leg symptoms.
  • Piriformis syndrome - gluteal pain radiating down the leg, often exacerbated by hip internal rotation, without true dermatomal pattern.
  • Spondylolisthesis - back pain, sometimes radicular, associated with vertebral slippage, often worse with extension.
  • Facet joint arthropathy - localised back pain, worse with extension and rotation, rarely with radicular features.
  • Sacroiliac joint dysfunction - unilateral gluteal pain, sometimes radiating to thigh, positive provocative SIJ tests.
  • Non-spinal causes (e.g., hip pathology, peripheral neuropathy) - pain not originating from the spine, often with different aggravating/relieving factors.
