Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Osteoarthritis"][/sc]
  • Osteoarthritis (OA) is a long-term disorder of synovial joints characterised by damage and subsequent repair processes leading to structural changes within the joint, including cartilage loss, subchondral bone remodelling, and osteophyte formation.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical features, risk factors, and diagnostic approach to osteoarthritis, and outline the principles of its non-pharmacological and pharmacological management.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Osteoarthritis"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Osteoarthritis"][/sc]
  • Osteoarthritis is a highly prevalent condition in the UK; in 2017, the prevalence was estimated at 10.7% of the population, with an incidence of 6.8 per 1000 person-years.
  • The prevalence of knee OA in England rose from approximately 3.02 million cases in 1990 to over 4.5 million cases by 2021, reflecting an ageing population and increasing obesity rates.
  • Risk factors include increasing age, obesity, previous joint injury, genetic predisposition, and certain occupations involving repetitive joint stress.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Osteoarthritis"][/sc]
  • Initial trigger often involves mechanical stress or injury to articular cartilage, leading to chondrocyte activation and release of pro-inflammatory mediators and catabolic enzymes.
  • Progressive degradation of the extracellular matrix, particularly collagen and proteoglycans, results in cartilage thinning, fibrillation, and eventual loss, alongside subchondral bone sclerosis and osteophyte formation. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Osteophytes: what are they and why do they matter clinically?" text="Osteophytes"][/sc]
  • The consequence is joint pain, stiffness, reduced range of motion, and functional impairment due to altered joint mechanics and inflammation of surrounding soft tissues.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Osteoarthritis"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Often insidious onset, with intermittent, activity-related joint pain and stiffness, typically worse after periods of inactivity and easing with movement.
  • Progressive phase: Pain becomes more persistent, affecting daily activities, with increasing stiffness, reduced joint mobility, and potential for night pain.
  • Late phase: Chronic, severe pain, significant functional limitation, joint deformity, and potential for secondary complications such as muscle weakness and instability, often developing over years to decades.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Osteoarthritis"][/sc]
  • Ask about joint pain - typically insidious in onset, activity-related, and relieved by rest, differentiating it from inflammatory arthropathies.
  • Ask about morning stiffness - usually lasting less than 30 minutes, distinguishing it from inflammatory conditions where stiffness is often prolonged.
  • Ask about functional limitation - difficulty with activities such as walking, climbing stairs, or dressing, indicating disease progression and impact on quality of life.
  • Ask about crepitus - a grating or crackling sensation during joint movement, which can be a sign of cartilage degradation.
  • Ask about joint swelling - often intermittent and bony, rather than soft tissue swelling seen in inflammatory arthritis.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Osteoarthritis"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - typically normal in uncomplicated osteoarthritis, helping to rule out systemic inflammatory processes or infection.
  • Check joint tenderness - localised tenderness on palpation, particularly along the joint line.
  • Check bony enlargement - palpable osteophytes, such as Heberden nodes (DIP joints) and Bouchard nodes (PIP joints), indicating structural changes. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Heberden vs Bouchard nodes: distinguishing features and clinical significance?" text="Heberden vs Bouchard nodes"][/sc]
  • Check reduced range of motion - often with pain at the extremes of movement, reflecting joint damage and stiffness.
  • Check crepitus - a palpable or audible grating sensation during passive or active joint movement.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Osteoarthritis"][/sc]
  • Acute, severe joint pain and swelling: May indicate septic arthritis, gout, pseudogout, or fracture, requiring urgent investigation to prevent joint destruction or systemic illness.
  • Systemic symptoms (fever, weight loss, night sweats): Suggests an underlying inflammatory arthropathy, malignancy, or infection, necessitating a broader diagnostic work-up.
  • Neurological symptoms (radiculopathy, weakness): Particularly in spinal osteoarthritis, may indicate nerve root compression or myelopathy, requiring neurological assessment.
  • Rapidly progressive joint destruction: Could signify a rapidly destructive arthropathy or neuropathic arthropathy, which requires prompt diagnosis and management.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Osteoarthritis"][/sc]
  • Younger adults: OA in younger individuals may be secondary to trauma, congenital abnormalities, or metabolic disorders, warranting investigation for underlying causes.
  • Monarticular presentation: While OA often affects multiple joints, a single joint presentation, especially if acute, should prompt consideration of other diagnoses like septic arthritis or crystal arthropathy.
  • Inflammatory features: Some patients with OA may experience intermittent inflammatory flares, making differentiation from inflammatory arthritis challenging; careful history and examination are crucial. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Inflammatory vs mechanical joint pain: key discriminators?" text="Inflammatory vs mechanical joint pain"][/sc]
  • Asymptomatic radiological findings: Significant radiological evidence of OA can be present in individuals with minimal or no symptoms, highlighting the importance of correlating imaging with clinical presentation.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Osteoarthritis"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected osteoarthritis: Clinical diagnosis based on history and examination is often sufficient; imaging is not routinely required for diagnosis but can confirm findings.
  • Severity/risk tools: Western Ontario and McMaster Universities Osteoarthritis Index (WOMAC) on MDCalc (WOMAC Osteoarthritis Index on MDCalc) can assess pain, stiffness, and physical function.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Blood tests such as ESR, CRP, rheumatoid factor, and anti-CCP antibodies may be considered to exclude inflammatory arthropathies.
  • Imaging - Plain radiography: X-rays typically show joint space narrowing, osteophytes, subchondral sclerosis, and subchondral cysts, which support the diagnosis. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Radiological features of osteoarthritis: what to look for?" text="Radiological features of OA"][/sc]
  • Imaging - MRI: May be used in complex cases or when considering surgical intervention to assess cartilage, menisci, ligaments, and bone marrow oedema.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Osteoarthritis"][/sc]
  • Rheumatoid Arthritis - typically symmetrical polyarthritis, prolonged morning stiffness (>30 mins), systemic symptoms, and positive inflammatory markers/autoantibodies.
  • Gout/Pseudogout - acute, severe monoarticular pain, often with erythema and swelling, associated with crystal deposition.
  • Psoriatic Arthritis - associated with psoriasis, dactylitis, enthesitis, and often asymmetrical joint involvement.
  • Septic Arthritis - acute onset of severe pain, swelling, warmth, and restricted movement in a single joint, often with fever and elevated inflammatory markers.
  • Tendinopathy/Bursitis - localised pain and tenderness, often exacerbated by specific movements, without widespread joint involvement or radiological changes of OA.
  • Fibromyalgia - widespread chronic pain, fatigue, and tenderness at specific points, without objective joint pathology. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Fibromyalgia vs osteoarthritis: differentiating features?" text="Fibromyalgia"][/sc]
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Osteoarthritis"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Provide information and education about the condition, encourage self-management strategies, and offer pain relief.
  • Definitive management: Core treatments include exercise (aerobic, strengthening, and local muscle strengthening), weight loss (if overweight or obese), and optimising pain management.
  • Adjunctive management: May include walking aids, appropriate footwear, heat/cold packs, and transcutaneous electrical nerve stimulation (TENS).
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Regular review to assess pain, function, and treatment effectiveness, adjusting management as needed.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Consider referral to physiotherapy, occupational therapy, or secondary care (orthopaedics/rheumatology) if symptoms are severe, unresponsive to primary care management, or if surgical options are being considered.
  • Exam pharmacology reference (UKMLA/MRCGP): Analgesia often follows a stepped approach, starting with paracetamol, then topical or oral NSAIDs, and potentially weak opioids if other options are insufficient. Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Osteoarthritis"][/sc]
  • Chronic pain: Persistent pain can lead to reduced quality of life, sleep disturbances, and psychological distress, often developing over years.
  • Functional disability: Progressive joint damage and pain can severely limit mobility and ability to perform daily activities, impacting independence in the late phase.
  • Joint deformity and instability: Advanced OA can result in significant structural changes, leading to joint misalignment and increased risk of falls or further injury.
  • Muscle weakness and atrophy: Pain and disuse can lead to weakening of muscles surrounding the affected joint, further contributing to instability and functional decline.
  • Side effects from long-term medication: Chronic use of analgesics, particularly NSAIDs, carries risks of gastrointestinal, cardiovascular, and renal complications, especially in the progressive and late phases.
