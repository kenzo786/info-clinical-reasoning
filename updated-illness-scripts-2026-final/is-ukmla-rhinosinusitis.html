Rhinosinusitis [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Rhinosinusitis"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Rhinosinusitis"][/sc]
  • Rhinosinusitis is an inflammatory condition affecting the nasal cavity and paranasal sinuses, characterised by two or more symptoms, one of which should be nasal blockage/discharge (anterior/posterior), facial pain/pressure, or reduction/loss of smell.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical features of acute rhinosinusitis, differentiate between viral and bacterial causes, and outline appropriate management strategies, including when to consider antimicrobial prescribing and referral for complications.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Rhinosinusitis"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Rhinosinusitis"][/sc]
  • Acute rhinosinusitis is a common condition, affecting approximately 10-15% of the UK population annually, with most cases being viral in origin [1].
  • Bacterial acute rhinosinusitis accounts for a smaller proportion (0.5-2%) of cases, often following a viral infection, and is more prevalent in individuals with predisposing factors such as allergic rhinitis, asthma, or anatomical variations [1].
  • Risk factors include viral upper respiratory tract infections, allergic rhinitis, nasal polyps, anatomical abnormalities (e.g., deviated septum), smoking, and immunocompromise.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Rhinosinusitis"][/sc]
  • Initial trigger is typically a viral upper respiratory tract infection, leading to inflammation and oedema of the sinonasal mucosa, impairing mucociliary clearance and obstructing the sinus ostia.
  • This obstruction creates a hypoxic environment within the sinuses, promoting bacterial overgrowth, which can lead to secondary bacterial infection, particularly if symptoms persist or worsen. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Double sickening sign in rhinosinusitis: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Double sickening sign"][/sc]
  • Consequence is a cycle of inflammation, impaired drainage, and potential infection, resulting in characteristic symptoms of facial pain/pressure, nasal discharge, and congestion.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Rhinosinusitis"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Symptoms typically develop within 1-3 days of a viral upper respiratory tract infection, characterised by nasal congestion, rhinorrhoea, and mild facial discomfort.
  • Progressive phase: Viral rhinosinusitis usually resolves spontaneously within 7-10 days. If symptoms persist beyond 10 days without improvement, or worsen significantly after initial improvement (double sickening), bacterial superinfection should be considered.
  • Late phase: Unresolved acute bacterial rhinosinusitis, particularly beyond 4 weeks, may progress to chronic rhinosinusitis, or in rare cases, lead to local or intracranial complications.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Rhinosinusitis"][/sc]
  • Ask about nasal blockage or congestion - indicates inflammation and oedema of the nasal mucosa, contributing to discomfort and impaired breathing.
  • Ask about nasal discharge (anterior or posterior) - purulent or discoloured discharge, especially unilateral, may suggest bacterial infection, distinguishing it from clear viral discharge.
  • Ask about facial pain or pressure - localised over the sinuses (frontal, maxillary, ethmoid) and often worse on bending forward, helps pinpoint affected areas.
  • Ask about reduction or loss of sense of smell (hyposmia/anosmia) - a common symptom due to mucosal swelling and inflammation affecting olfactory receptors.
  • Ask about duration and progression of symptoms - symptoms lasting over 10 days without improvement, or worsening after initial improvement (double sickening), strongly suggest bacterial aetiology.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Rhinosinusitis"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - fever (temperature >38°C) may indicate bacterial infection or systemic illness, warranting further assessment.
  • Check nasal endoscopy/anterior rhinoscopy - mucosal oedema, erythema, and purulent discharge in the middle meatus are indicative of rhinosinusitis.
  • Check facial tenderness on palpation - localised tenderness over the frontal or maxillary sinuses can correlate with the site of inflammation.
  • Check for signs of orbital or intracranial complications - periorbital oedema, proptosis, diplopia, or altered mental status are red flags requiring urgent referral. Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Rhinosinusitis"][/sc]
  • Severe localised unilateral pain: Particularly over the teeth and jaw, suggesting a possible dental origin or severe bacterial infection.
  • Periorbital oedema or cellulitis: Indicates spread of infection beyond the sinuses into the orbit, a serious complication.
  • Double vision (diplopia) or reduced visual acuity: Suggests orbital apex syndrome or optic nerve involvement, requiring immediate ophthalmological review.
  • Neurological signs (e.g., altered mental status, focal neurological deficits, severe frontal headache): May indicate intracranial spread of infection, such as meningitis or brain abscess.
  • Symptoms persisting beyond 3 weeks or rapidly worsening: Raises concern for bacterial infection, complications, or an alternative diagnosis.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Rhinosinusitis"][/sc]
  • Children: Often present with non-specific upper respiratory tract symptoms like cough and discoloured nasal discharge, making diagnosis challenging. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Rhinosinusitis in children: how does it differ from adults and why does it matter clinically?" text="Rhinosinusitis in children"][/sc]
  • Older adults: May have less pronounced symptoms or atypical pain patterns, and are at higher risk for complications due to comorbidities and immunosuppression.
  • Immunocompromised individuals: Can develop severe or rapidly progressive infections with unusual pathogens, requiring a high index of suspicion and aggressive management.
  • Pregnant women: Management options are limited due to concerns about fetal safety, necessitating careful consideration of treatment choices.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Rhinosinusitis"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected acute rhinosinusitis: Clinical diagnosis based on history and examination is usually sufficient. No routine laboratory tests or imaging are recommended for uncomplicated cases.
  • Severity/risk tools: The Sinusitis Symptom Score (SNOT-22) can be used for assessing symptom severity and treatment response in chronic cases, though not typically for acute diagnosis.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Consider investigations for dental infection (dental X-ray), migraine (neurological assessment), or allergic rhinitis (allergy testing).
  • Imaging - CT scan of paranasal sinuses: Reserved for cases with suspected complications (e.g., orbital or intracranial spread), persistent symptoms despite appropriate treatment, or prior to surgical intervention. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Indications for CT scan in rhinosinusitis: what are they and why do they matter clinically?" text="CT scan indications"][/sc]
  • Microbiological culture: Nasal swabs are generally not useful. Endoscopically guided middle meatal swabs may be considered in severe, persistent, or recurrent cases, or in immunocompromised patients, to guide targeted antibiotic therapy.

  • A 35-year-old male presents with a 12-day history of worsening facial pain, purulent nasal discharge, and fever, following an initial viral upper respiratory tract infection.
  • An adult with prolonged and worsening sinonasal symptoms, concerning for bacterial superinfection of acute rhinosinusitis.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Rhinosinusitis"][/sc]
  • Viral upper respiratory tract infection (common cold) - typically resolves within 7-10 days, without worsening symptoms after initial improvement.
  • Allergic rhinitis - usually presents with clear nasal discharge, sneezing, and itching, often with seasonal or allergen exposure triggers.
  • Dental infection - localised facial pain, especially over the maxillary sinus, may originate from a dental abscess or caries.
  • Migraine or other headache disorders - can cause facial pain, but typically lack nasal symptoms and are associated with photophobia, phonophobia, or aura.
  • Trigeminal neuralgia - characterised by sudden, severe, unilateral facial pain in the distribution of the trigeminal nerve, without associated nasal symptoms.
  • Nasal foreign body (especially in children) - often presents with unilateral, foul-smelling nasal discharge.
  • Nasal polyps - can cause chronic nasal obstruction and hyposmia, but acute pain and fever are less common unless infected. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Nasal polyps vs rhinosinusitis: what are the key differences and why do they matter clinically?" text="Nasal polyps"][/sc]
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Rhinosinusitis"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Recognition of severe infection or complications (e.g., orbital cellulitis, intracranial spread) warrants urgent referral to secondary care. Supportive care includes adequate hydration and rest.
  • Definitive management: For viral rhinosinusitis, symptomatic relief with analgesics (paracetamol or ibuprofen) and nasal saline irrigation is often sufficient. For acute bacterial rhinosinusitis, a watchful waiting approach is often appropriate, with antibiotics considered if symptoms do not improve after 7-10 days or worsen significantly.
  • Adjunctive management: Intranasal corticosteroids may be considered for adults and children over 12 years with acute rhinosinusitis, particularly if symptoms are persistent or severe, to reduce mucosal inflammation.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Advise patients to seek medical help if symptoms worsen rapidly or significantly, do not improve after 3 weeks, or if new red flag symptoms develop.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Provide clear instructions on when to seek urgent medical attention, emphasising signs of worsening infection or complications.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Rhinosinusitis"][/sc]
  • Orbital complications: Direct spread of infection from the ethmoid sinuses can lead to periorbital cellulitis, orbital cellulitis, subperiosteal abscess, or orbital abscess, manifesting as periorbital oedema, proptosis, or visual changes.
  • Intracranial complications: Rare but serious, including meningitis, epidural abscess, subdural empyema, or brain abscess, typically resulting from direct extension or thrombophlebitis of emissary veins, presenting with severe headache, focal neurological deficits, or altered consciousness.
  • Osteomyelitis: Infection of the frontal bone (Pott\'s puffy tumour) can occur, particularly in adolescents, presenting as a tender, doughy swelling over the forehead.
  • Chronic rhinosinusitis: Persistence of symptoms beyond 12 weeks, often due to ongoing inflammation, impaired mucociliary clearance, or fungal involvement, leading to significant morbidity.
