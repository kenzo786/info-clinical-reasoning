Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Candidiasis (Infections)"][/sc]
  • Candidiasis is a fungal infection caused by *Candida* species, most commonly *Candida albicans*, which can manifest as superficial mucocutaneous infections (e.g., oropharyngeal thrush, vulvovaginal candidiasis) or severe invasive disease (e.g., candidaemia) [1].
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the diverse clinical presentations of candidiasis, identify key risk factors for both superficial and invasive forms, and understand the principles of diagnosis and management, including appropriate antifungal therapy and when to escalate care.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Candidiasis (Infections)"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Candidiasis (Infections)"][/sc]
  • *Candida* species are commensal organisms in up to 60% of healthy individuals, primarily in the gastrointestinal tract [1].
  • Candidaemia, the most common presentation of invasive candidiasis, had an overall rate of 3.5 cases per 100,000 population in England in 2020 [1].
  • Vulvovaginal candidiasis affects approximately 75% of women at least once in their lifetime, with 5-8% experiencing recurrent episodes [2].
  • Oropharyngeal candidiasis is common in neonates (5%) and highly prevalent in individuals with advanced HIV/AIDS (around 90%) [1].
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Candidiasis (Infections)"][/sc]
  • *Candida* species, typically *C. albicans*, transition from commensal flora to pathogenic forms (hyphae and pseudohyphae) when host defences are compromised or local environmental factors favour overgrowth [1].
  • The progression involves adherence to host tissues, secretion of hydrolytic enzymes (e.g., aspartyl proteinases, phospholipases) that damage host cells, and evasion of immune responses, leading to localised infection [1].
  • Invasive candidiasis occurs when *Candida* breaches mucosal barriers, often facilitated by factors like central venous catheters or gastrointestinal surgery, leading to dissemination via the bloodstream (candidaemia) and potential deep organ involvement [1]. Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Candidiasis (Infections)"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Superficial mucocutaneous candidiasis (e.g., oral thrush, vulvovaginal candidiasis) typically develops over days to weeks following predisposing factors such as antibiotic use or immunosuppression.
  • Progressive phase: If predisposing factors persist or host immunity is severely compromised, localised infections may become more extensive or lead to invasive disease, particularly beyond the initial week of symptoms.
  • Late phase: Invasive candidiasis, such as candidaemia, can rapidly progress to disseminated infection and organ damage within days if not promptly diagnosed and treated, often leading to prolonged hospitalisation and significant morbidity.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Candidiasis (Infections)"][/sc]
  • Ask about white patches in the mouth - suggestive of oropharyngeal candidiasis (thrush), which are typically easily scraped off, differentiating them from other oral lesions [1].
  • Ask about difficulty or pain on swallowing (odynophagia/dysphagia) - a key symptom of oesophageal candidiasis, particularly in immunocompromised individuals [1].
  • Ask about vaginal itching, soreness, and a thick, white, curd-like discharge - characteristic of vulvovaginal candidiasis [1].
  • Ask about redness, itching, and satellite lesions in skin folds (e.g., intertrigo) - indicative of cutaneous candidiasis [1].
  • Ask about fever, chills, and signs of sepsis unresponsive to broad-spectrum antibiotics - concerning for invasive candidiasis or candidaemia, especially in high-risk patients [1].
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Candidiasis (Infections)"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - fever, tachycardia, or hypotension may indicate systemic infection or sepsis, particularly in invasive candidiasis.
  • Check oral cavity for white, creamy patches on the tongue, buccal mucosa, or palate - these are pseudomembranous plaques of oral thrush, which can be scraped off to reveal erythematous, sometimes bleeding, mucosa [1].
  • Check skin folds (axillae, groin, inframammary) for erythematous, macerated areas with satellite papules or pustules - typical presentation of candidal intertrigo [1].
  • Check perineum and vulva for erythema, oedema, excoriation, and a thick, white, adherent discharge - signs of vulvovaginal candidiasis [1].
  • Check for signs of endophthalmitis (e.g., visual changes, eye pain) or chorioretinitis on fundoscopy - may indicate disseminated candidiasis [1]. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Candida endophthalmitis: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Candida endophthalmitis"][/sc]
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Candidiasis (Infections)"][/sc]
  • Systemic signs of infection: Fever, chills, tachycardia, or hypotension in a patient with suspected candidiasis, particularly if immunocompromised, suggests invasive disease (e.g., candidaemia) and warrants urgent investigation and management.
  • Oesophageal symptoms: Dysphagia or odynophagia, especially in an immunocompromised patient, indicates oesophageal candidiasis, which requires systemic antifungal therapy and can be a harbinger of more widespread disease [1].
  • Visual changes or eye pain: These symptoms may signal candidal endophthalmitis or chorioretinitis, a serious complication of disseminated candidiasis that can lead to permanent vision loss if not treated promptly [1].
  • Lack of response to appropriate superficial antifungal therapy: Persistent or worsening symptoms despite adequate treatment for mucocutaneous candidiasis may indicate an underlying immune deficiency, misdiagnosis, or antifungal resistance, requiring further investigation.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Candidiasis (Infections)"][/sc]
  • Immunocompromised patients (e.g., HIV, chemotherapy, transplant recipients): May present with extensive or recurrent mucocutaneous candidiasis, or with invasive disease without typical signs of inflammation, making diagnosis challenging [1]. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Risk factors for invasive candidiasis in immunocompromised patients: what are they and why do they matter clinically?" text="Risk factors for invasive candidiasis"][/sc]
  • Neonates and very low birth weight infants: Can develop invasive candidiasis with non-specific symptoms (e.g., apnoea, bradycardia, feeding intolerance), leading to high mortality and morbidity [1].
  • Chronic mucocutaneous candidiasis: A rare primary immunodeficiency characterised by persistent, recurrent candidal infections of the skin, nails, and mucous membranes, often associated with endocrine disorders [1].
  • Candidal peritonitis: Can occur as a complication of peritoneal dialysis or gastrointestinal surgery, presenting with abdominal pain and fever, but blood cultures may be negative [1].
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Candidiasis (Infections)"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected mucocutaneous candidiasis: Clinical diagnosis is often sufficient. For confirmation or in recurrent/refractory cases, microscopy (KOH wet mount) and culture of swabs (oral, vaginal, skin) can identify *Candida* species and assess antifungal susceptibility [1].
  • Core for suspected invasive candidiasis/candidaemia: Blood cultures are essential, though often negative in disseminated disease. Beta-D-glucan (BDG) and T2 Candida Panel are non-culture-based diagnostic tests that can aid early diagnosis [1]. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Beta-D-glucan test: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Beta-D-glucan test"][/sc]
  • Severity/risk tools: For critically ill patients, scores like the Candida Score can help assess the risk of invasive candidiasis. Candida Score on MDCalc
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Consider bacterial cultures, viral swabs, or biopsies depending on the clinical presentation to rule out other infections or conditions.
  • Imaging - CT scan: For suspected deep-seated candidiasis (e.g., hepatosplenic candidiasis, renal candidiasis), CT imaging can identify organ involvement and abscess formation [1].
  • Serial reassessment: Repeat blood cultures for candidaemia to confirm clearance of infection, and monitor clinical response to therapy.

  • An immunocompromised patient with mucocutaneous lesions and systemic symptoms, concerning for disseminated fungal infection.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Candidiasis (Infections)"][/sc]
  • Bacterial infection: Can mimic candidiasis, especially in skin folds (e.g., erythrasma) or systemically (e.g., bacterial sepsis); cultures differentiate.
  • Viral infections: Oral hairy leukoplakia (EBV) or herpes simplex virus can cause oral lesions; viral swabs distinguish.
  • Leukoplakia: White patches in the mouth that cannot be scraped off, unlike candidiasis, and are often precancerous [1].
  • Geographic tongue: Benign inflammatory condition of the tongue, can be mistaken for oral candidiasis but lacks fungal elements.
  • Pityriasis versicolor: Superficial fungal infection of the skin caused by *Malassezia* species, presenting with hypopigmented or hyperpigmented patches, differentiated by microscopy.
  • Other fungal infections: Less common systemic mycoses can present similarly to invasive candidiasis, requiring specific fungal cultures and serology for diagnosis. Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Candidiasis (Infections)"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Recognition of severe or invasive disease, escalation to specialist teams (e.g., infectious disease, microbiology), and initiation of appropriate empiric antifungal therapy while awaiting culture results. Supportive care for sepsis may be required.
  • Definitive management: For mucocutaneous candidiasis, topical antifungals (e.g., miconazole, nystatin) are often sufficient. Systemic antifungals (e.g., fluconazole) are typically used for extensive, recurrent, or oesophageal candidiasis. Invasive candidiasis often warrants echinocandins (e.g., caspofungin, micafungin) as first-line therapy, with step-down to fluconazole once stable and *C. albicans* is confirmed [1].
  • Adjunctive management: Removal of predisposing factors (e.g., discontinuing unnecessary antibiotics, removing central venous catheters), optimising glycaemic control in diabetes, and addressing underlying immunosuppression may support treatment success.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Clinical response should be monitored closely. For candidaemia, repeat blood cultures are essential to confirm clearance. Follow-up with ophthalmology for invasive candidiasis is often warranted to screen for endophthalmitis.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Patients should be advised on symptoms of worsening infection or systemic spread and when to seek urgent medical attention. Non-resolving or recurrent infections warrant specialist review.
  • Exam pharmacology reference (UKMLA/MRCGP): Fluconazole is a commonly tested antifungal. Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Candidiasis (Infections)"][/sc]
  • Disseminated candidiasis: Occurs when *Candida* spreads from the bloodstream to multiple organs (e.g., liver, spleen, kidneys, brain, eyes), leading to severe organ dysfunction and high mortality, particularly beyond the initial days of candidaemia [1].
  • Candidal endophthalmitis: A serious ocular complication of candidaemia, where *Candida* infects the eye, potentially causing permanent vision loss if not promptly treated with systemic antifungals and sometimes vitrectomy [1].
  • Septic shock and multi-organ failure: Invasive candidiasis can rapidly progress to sepsis, leading to circulatory collapse, acute kidney injury, respiratory failure, and death, especially in critically ill or immunocompromised patients.
  • Chronic mucocutaneous candidiasis: Long-term, recurrent infections of skin, nails, and mucous membranes, often indicative of an underlying immune defect, leading to significant morbidity and impact on quality of life.
  • Antifungal resistance: Repeated or inappropriate antifungal use can lead to the development of drug-resistant *Candida* strains, making treatment more challenging and increasing the risk of treatment failure and recurrent infections.
