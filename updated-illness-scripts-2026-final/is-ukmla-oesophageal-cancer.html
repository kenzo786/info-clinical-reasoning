Oesophageal cancer (Gastrointestinal tract) [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Oesophageal cancer"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Oesophageal cancer"][/sc]
  • Oesophageal cancer is a malignancy arising from the epithelial cells lining the oesophagus, the muscular tube connecting the pharynx to the stomach. It is broadly classified into squamous cell carcinoma (SCC) and adenocarcinoma (AC).
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the common presentations and risk factors for oesophageal cancer, differentiate between types, and understand the principles of urgent referral and initial investigation.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Oesophageal cancer"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Oesophageal cancer"][/sc]
  • Oesophageal cancer accounts for approximately 2% of all new cancer cases in the UK, with around 9,400 new diagnoses annually (2017-2019 data) [1]. It is more common in males than females.
  • Incidence rates in the UK are higher than many other European countries, with approximately 14.2 cases per 100,000 people [2]. The most affected age group is typically 75-79 years [3].
  • Risk factors for oesophageal squamous cell carcinoma (SCC) include smoking, excessive alcohol consumption, and dietary factors. Risk factors for adenocarcinoma (AC) include gastro-oesophageal reflux disease (GORD), Barrett\'s oesophagus, and obesity [4].
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Oesophageal cancer"][/sc]
  • Trigger: Chronic irritation or inflammation of the oesophageal lining, often due to gastro-oesophageal reflux (leading to Barrett\'s oesophagus for AC) or exposure to carcinogens like tobacco and alcohol (for SCC). [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Barrett\'s oesophagus: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Barrett\'s oesophagus"][/sc]
  • Progression: Metaplastic changes (e.g., Barrett\'s oesophagus) can progress to dysplasia and then to invasive adenocarcinoma. Similarly, chronic irritation can lead to squamous dysplasia and SCC. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Oesophageal adenocarcinoma vs squamous cell carcinoma: what are the key differences?" text="Adenocarcinoma vs SCC"][/sc]
  • Consequence: Uncontrolled cellular proliferation results in tumour formation, leading to luminal narrowing, dysphagia, weight loss, and potential local invasion or distant metastasis.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Oesophageal cancer"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Often asymptomatic or presents with subtle, non-specific symptoms such as mild indigestion or heartburn, which may be attributed to other common gastrointestinal conditions.
  • Progressive phase: As the tumour grows, dysphagia typically develops, initially for solids and then progressing to liquids. This is often accompanied by significant unintentional weight loss and persistent dyspepsia.
  • Late phase: Advanced disease may manifest with odynophagia, hoarseness (due to recurrent laryngeal nerve involvement), persistent cough, melaena, or symptoms related to metastatic spread, such as bone pain or jaundice.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Oesophageal cancer"][/sc]
  • Ask about dysphagia - progressive difficulty swallowing, initially for solids and later for liquids, is the cardinal symptom and indicates luminal narrowing.
  • Ask about unintentional weight loss - often significant and rapid, reflecting impaired nutritional intake due to dysphagia and the catabolic effects of malignancy.
  • Ask about persistent dyspepsia or heartburn - new onset or worsening symptoms, particularly if unresponsive to usual treatments, may indicate underlying oesophageal pathology.
  • Ask about odynophagia - painful swallowing, which can suggest ulceration or inflammation associated with the tumour.
  • Ask about persistent vomiting or regurgitation - may occur due to obstruction of the oesophagus, particularly after eating.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Oesophageal cancer"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - may reveal tachycardia or hypotension in cases of significant dehydration or anaemia from chronic blood loss.
  • Check for signs of cachexia - generalised wasting, muscle loss, and pallor, indicative of advanced malignancy and poor nutritional status. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Cancer cachexia: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Cachexia"][/sc]
  • Check for lymphadenopathy - palpable supraclavicular (Virchow\'s node) or cervical lymph nodes can indicate metastatic spread.
  • Check for hepatomegaly - an enlarged liver may suggest hepatic metastases.
  • Check for clubbing - rarely seen, but can be a paraneoplastic phenomenon in some advanced cancers.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Oesophageal cancer"][/sc]
  • Dysphagia: New onset or progressive difficulty swallowing, particularly in individuals over 55 years, warrants urgent investigation for oesophageal cancer [5].
  • Unexplained weight loss: Significant and unintentional weight loss, especially when combined with dysphagia or persistent upper gastrointestinal symptoms, is a key indicator for urgent referral.
  • Upper abdominal mass: A palpable mass in the epigastrium or upper abdomen may suggest advanced local disease or metastatic spread.
  • Iron deficiency anaemia: Unexplained iron deficiency anaemia, particularly in older adults, can be a sign of chronic gastrointestinal blood loss from an oesophageal tumour.
  • Persistent vomiting: New onset or persistent vomiting, especially if associated with dysphagia, should prompt urgent evaluation.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Oesophageal cancer"][/sc]
  • Younger patients: Oesophageal cancer is less common in younger individuals, but when it occurs, it may be associated with more aggressive disease or specific genetic predispositions, leading to delayed diagnosis due to lower index of suspicion.
  • Non-specific symptoms: Some patients may present with only vague symptoms such as chronic cough, hoarseness, or chest pain, which can be misdiagnosed as respiratory or cardiac conditions.
  • Barrett\'s oesophagus surveillance: Patients undergoing surveillance for Barrett\'s oesophagus may be diagnosed at an earlier, asymptomatic stage through endoscopy, highlighting the importance of regular follow-up.
  • Silent metastasis: Rarely, patients may present with symptoms related to metastatic disease (e.g., bone pain, jaundice) without significant oesophageal symptoms, making the primary diagnosis challenging. Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Oesophageal cancer"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected oesophageal cancer:
    • Upper gastrointestinal endoscopy with biopsy: Essential for direct visualisation of the oesophageal mucosa and histological confirmation of malignancy. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Upper GI endoscopy with biopsy: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Endoscopy with biopsy"][/sc]
    • CT scan of chest, abdomen, and pelvis: For staging, assessing local invasion, and detecting metastatic disease.
  • Severity/risk tools: No widely used severity/risk scores for oesophageal cancer are typically used in primary care or for initial diagnosis.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible:
    • Barium swallow: Can delineate oesophageal strictures or masses, particularly if endoscopy is difficult or contraindicated.
    • Oesophageal manometry: To assess oesophageal motility disorders, which can mimic dysphagia caused by malignancy.
  • Imaging - PET-CT: May be used for more accurate staging, particularly to detect distant metastases not visible on conventional CT.
  • Serial reassessment: Regular follow-up endoscopy for patients with Barrett\'s oesophagus is crucial for early detection of dysplasia or adenocarcinoma.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Oesophageal cancer"][/sc]
  • Achalasia - dysphagia to both solids and liquids from onset, often associated with regurgitation of undigested food, but typically without significant weight loss in early stages. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Achalasia: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Achalasia"][/sc]
  • Oesophageal stricture (benign) - typically a history of caustic ingestion or chronic reflux, with dysphagia primarily for solids, but usually less rapid progression and weight loss than malignancy.
  • Oesophageal spasm - intermittent dysphagia and chest pain, often triggered by hot or cold foods, without progressive weight loss.
  • Pharyngeal pouch - dysphagia associated with regurgitation of undigested food, halitosis, and gurgling sounds in the neck.
  • Gastro-oesophageal reflux disease (GORD) - common cause of heartburn and regurgitation, but persistent dysphagia and weight loss are red flags requiring further investigation.
  • Globus sensation - feeling of a lump in the throat, often intermittent and relieved by swallowing, not associated with true dysphagia or weight loss.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Oesophageal cancer"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Recognition of red flag symptoms and urgent referral to a specialist for endoscopy and staging, as per NICE NG12 guidelines for suspected cancer. Supportive care may include nutritional support and pain management.
  • Definitive management: Treatment approaches are highly individualised and depend on the stage, type, and location of the cancer, as well as patient fitness. Options often include surgical resection (oesophagectomy), chemotherapy, radiotherapy, or a combination (chemoradiotherapy).
  • Adjunctive management: Nutritional support is crucial, often involving dietary advice, oral nutritional supplements, or enteral feeding (e.g., nasogastric tube, gastrostomy) to manage weight loss and dysphagia.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Post-treatment surveillance involves regular clinical review, endoscopy, and imaging to monitor for recurrence and manage long-term complications.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Patients should be advised to seek urgent medical attention for worsening dysphagia, significant bleeding, or new symptoms suggestive of complications or progression.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Oesophageal cancer"][/sc]
  • Oesophageal obstruction: As the tumour grows, it can cause significant luminal narrowing, leading to complete inability to swallow (aphagia), severe malnutrition, and aspiration risk.
  • Haemorrhage: Tumour ulceration can lead to chronic blood loss, resulting in iron deficiency anaemia, or acute, life-threatening upper gastrointestinal bleeding.
  • Fistula formation: Advanced tumours can erode into adjacent structures, such as the trachea or bronchi, leading to tracheo-oesophageal fistula, causing recurrent aspiration pneumonia and severe respiratory compromise.
  • Metastatic spread: Oesophageal cancer commonly metastasises to regional lymph nodes, liver, lungs, and bone, leading to symptoms related to the site of metastasis and significantly impacting prognosis.
  • Cachexia: Profound weight loss, muscle wasting, and general debility due to the systemic effects of cancer and impaired nutritional intake, contributing to poor quality of life and treatment tolerance.
