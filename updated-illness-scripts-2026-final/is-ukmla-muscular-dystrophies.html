Muscular dystrophies [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Muscular dystrophies"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Muscular dystrophies"][/sc]
  • Muscular dystrophies are a group of inherited genetic disorders characterized by progressive muscle weakness and degeneration, leading to disability.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical features, inheritance patterns, and management principles of common muscular dystrophies.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Muscular dystrophies"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Muscular dystrophies"][/sc]
  • Duchenne muscular dystrophy (DMD) is the most common and severe form, affecting approximately 1 in 3,500 to 5,000 live male births in the UK [1].
  • Becker muscular dystrophy (BMD) is less common and milder, with an incidence of about 1 in 18,000 to 30,000 live male births [2].
  • Risk factors are primarily genetic, with X-linked recessive inheritance for DMD and BMD, and autosomal dominant or recessive patterns for other forms.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Muscular dystrophies"][/sc]
  • Genetic mutations lead to defective or absent proteins (e.g., dystrophin in DMD/BMD) essential for muscle fibre integrity and function.
  • This defect triggers a cascade of muscle fibre damage, inflammation, and cycles of degeneration and regeneration, which eventually exhaust the muscle\'s repair capacity.
  • Progressive replacement of muscle tissue with fibrous and adipose tissue results in irreversible muscle weakness, atrophy, and functional impairment.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Muscular dystrophies"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Symptoms typically emerge in early childhood (2-5 years for DMD), often presenting as developmental delay in motor milestones.
  • Progressive phase: Muscle weakness gradually worsens, affecting proximal muscles first, leading to loss of ambulation, particularly beyond 10-12 years in DMD.
  • Late phase: Respiratory and cardiac complications become prominent, often beyond 15-20 years in DMD, requiring significant supportive care.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Muscular dystrophies"][/sc]
  • Ask about difficulty with motor skills - suggests proximal muscle weakness affecting activities like running, jumping, and climbing stairs.
  • Ask about frequent falls - indicates impaired balance and muscle weakness, often an early sign.
  • Ask about waddling gait - characteristic of pelvic girdle weakness, leading to a compensatory gait pattern.
  • Ask about difficulty rising from the floor (Gowers\' sign) - a classic indicator of proximal lower limb weakness.
  • Ask about muscle cramps or pain - can occur due to muscle fatigue and damage, though less prominent than weakness.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Muscular dystrophies"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - assess for respiratory compromise (e.g., tachypnoea, reduced oxygen saturation) or cardiac involvement (e.g., tachycardia, arrhythmias).
  • Check muscle strength - proximal weakness (e.g., hip flexors, shoulder abductors) is typically more affected than distal.
  • Check calf pseudohypertrophy - enlargement of calf muscles due to fibrous and fatty tissue replacement, despite underlying weakness.
  • Check deep tendon reflexes - often diminished or absent, particularly in later stages.
  • Check for scoliosis - common in advanced stages due to truncal muscle weakness.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Muscular dystrophies"][/sc]
  • Rapid progression of weakness - suggests a more aggressive form or an alternative diagnosis requiring urgent investigation.
  • Early onset respiratory difficulty - indicates significant diaphragm or intercostal muscle involvement, necessitating ventilatory support.
  • Cardiac arrhythmias or cardiomyopathy - requires prompt cardiological assessment and management to prevent life-threatening complications.
  • Significant dysphagia - risk of aspiration pneumonia and nutritional deficiencies, requiring speech and language therapy input.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Muscular dystrophies"][/sc]
  • Female carriers of DMD: May exhibit mild symptoms such as muscle weakness or cardiomyopathy due to skewed X-inactivation, which can be overlooked.
  • Limb-girdle muscular dystrophies (LGMD): Highly heterogeneous group with variable age of onset and progression, often mimicking other neuromuscular conditions.
  • Facioscapulohumeral muscular dystrophy (FSHD): Characterised by asymmetric weakness affecting facial, shoulder girdle, and upper arm muscles, often with later onset and slower progression.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Muscular dystrophies"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected muscular dystrophy: Serum creatine kinase (CK) levels are typically significantly elevated (often >10x normal) in active muscle degeneration.
  • Core for suspected muscular dystrophy: Genetic testing (e.g., multiplex ligation-dependent probe amplification (MLPA) for DMD/BMD) is definitive for confirming the diagnosis and identifying the specific mutation.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Electromyography (EMG) and nerve conduction studies (NCS) can help differentiate myopathic from neuropathic conditions.
  • Imaging - MRI: Muscle MRI can show patterns of muscle involvement and fatty infiltration, aiding in diagnosis and differentiating types of dystrophy.
  • Serial reassessment: Regular pulmonary function tests (PFTs) and echocardiograms are crucial for monitoring respiratory and cardiac involvement.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Muscular dystrophies"][/sc]
  • A 6-year-old boy presents with a 1-year history of progressive difficulty climbing stairs, frequent falls, and a waddling gait, with examination revealing calf pseudohypertrophy and proximal muscle weakness.
  • A young boy with chronic, progressive, proximal muscle weakness and associated gait disturbance, suggestive of an inherited myopathy.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Muscular dystrophies"][/sc]
  • Spinal muscular atrophy (SMA) - primary motor neuron degeneration leading to weakness, but typically with fasciculations and absent reflexes early.
  • Congenital myopathies - present at birth or early infancy with hypotonia and weakness, often non-progressive or slowly progressive.
  • Metabolic myopathies - episodic weakness or cramps, often triggered by exercise or fasting, with normal muscle strength between episodes.
  • Inflammatory myopathies (e.g., juvenile dermatomyositis) - typically acute or subacute onset, often with skin rash and systemic symptoms.
  • Neuropathies (e.g., Charcot-Marie-Tooth disease) - primarily affect distal muscles, leading to foot drop and sensory disturbances.
  • Myasthenia gravis - fluctuating weakness, worse with exertion, often affecting ocular and bulbar muscles, with fatigability.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Muscular dystrophies"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Address acute respiratory distress or cardiac arrhythmias if present.
  • Definitive management: Glucocorticoids (e.g., prednisolone) can slow disease progression and preserve muscle function in DMD. Gene-targeted therapies (e.g., exon skipping for DMD) are emerging.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Regular multidisciplinary team (MDT) reviews, including neurology, cardiology, respiratory, physiotherapy, occupational therapy, and dietetics.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Educate families on signs of respiratory compromise or cardiac issues, and provide clear pathways for urgent medical review.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Muscular dystrophies"][/sc]
  • Respiratory failure: Progressive weakness of respiratory muscles leads to hypoventilation, particularly nocturnal, and increased susceptibility to chest infections, often occurring in the late phase.
  • Cardiomyopathy: Weakness of the heart muscle can lead to heart failure and arrhythmias, a significant cause of morbidity and mortality, often becoming clinically apparent in the progressive to late phases.
  • Scoliosis: Weakness of truncal muscles results in spinal curvature, which can further impair respiratory function and cause discomfort, typically progressing in the progressive phase.
  • Contractures: Shortening of muscles and tendons limits joint movement, leading to deformities and reduced mobility, developing progressively from the early phase.
  • Bone fragility and fractures: Due to reduced mobility, corticosteroid use, and underlying disease, increasing the risk of fractures, especially in the progressive and late phases.
