Nephrotic syndrome [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Nephrotic syndrome"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Nephrotic syndrome definition"][/sc]
  • Nephrotic syndrome is a clinical syndrome characterised by massive proteinuria (>3.5g/24h or urine protein:creatinine ratio >300mg/mmol), hypoalbuminaemia (<30g/L), and peripheral oedema.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical features of nephrotic syndrome, differentiate between common causes, and outline initial management principles including assessment for complications.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Nephrotic syndrome epidemiology"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Nephrotic syndrome"][/sc]
  • The incidence of primary nephrotic syndrome in adults is approximately 3 cases per 100,000 per year in the UK [3].
  • Minimal change disease (MCD) is the most common cause of nephrotic syndrome in children (accounting for 80-90% of cases) and 10-15% of cases in adults [1].
  • Membranous nephropathy is the most common cause of primary nephrotic syndrome in non-diabetic White adults over the age of 40, with an estimated incidence rate of 6.55 per million population per year [4].
  • Risk factors include certain autoimmune diseases (e.g., lupus), infections (e.g., HIV, hepatitis B/C), certain medications (e.g., NSAIDs), and some malignancies.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Nephrotic syndrome pathophysiology"][/sc]
  • Glomerular damage (e.g., podocyte injury in minimal change disease, immune complex deposition in membranous nephropathy) leads to increased permeability of the glomerular filtration barrier.
  • This increased permeability results in significant proteinuria, exceeding the renal tubule\'s reabsorptive capacity, leading to massive loss of albumin and other plasma proteins. This results in hypoalbuminaemia, which reduces plasma oncotic pressure.
  • Reduced plasma oncotic pressure causes fluid to shift from the intravascular space into the interstitial space, leading to generalised oedema. The subsequent reduction in effective circulating volume can activate the renin-angiotensin-aldosterone system, further contributing to fluid retention. Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Nephrotic syndrome time course"][/sc]
  • Early phase: The onset of symptoms such as periorbital oedema and weight gain may be insidious over days to weeks, or more acute, particularly in children. Proteinuria develops early, often before overt oedema is noticed.
  • Progressive phase: Without intervention, oedema typically progresses to involve the lower limbs, ascites, and pleural effusions over weeks to months. Complications such as infection and thrombosis may develop during this phase.
  • Late phase: The course is variable depending on the underlying cause and response to treatment. Some forms, like minimal change disease, often respond well to steroids with remission within weeks, while others, such as FSGS, may progress to chronic kidney disease and end-stage renal disease over months to years.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Nephrotic syndrome symptoms"][/sc]
  • Ask about oedema - typically starting periorbitally, especially in the morning, and progressing to dependent areas like ankles and feet, indicating fluid retention due to hypoalbuminaemia.
  • Ask about weight gain - often a rapid increase due to fluid accumulation, which can be a key indicator of the severity of oedema.
  • Ask about frothy urine - caused by high levels of protein in the urine, which reduces surface tension and creates bubbles. This is a direct sign of proteinuria.
  • Ask about fatigue and malaise - non-specific symptoms that can be related to the underlying systemic illness, anaemia, or the metabolic consequences of protein loss.
  • Ask about abdominal pain or distension - may indicate ascites or peritonitis, a serious complication, particularly in children.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Nephrotic syndrome signs"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - blood pressure may be normal, low (due to hypovolaemia from fluid shifts), or elevated (due to fluid overload or underlying renal disease). Tachycardia may indicate hypovolaemia.
  • Check for pitting oedema - typically present in dependent areas (ankles, sacrum) and periorbitally, indicating significant interstitial fluid accumulation.
  • Check for ascites and pleural effusions - detected by abdominal distension, shifting dullness, and reduced breath sounds, respectively, indicating severe fluid overload.
  • Check for xanthomas or xanthelasma - may be present due to hyperlipidaemia, a common consequence of nephrotic syndrome. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Hyperlipidaemia in nephrotic syndrome: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Hyperlipidaemia"][/sc]
  • Check for signs of malnutrition or muscle wasting - can occur in chronic cases due to persistent protein loss and catabolism.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Nephrotic syndrome red flags"][/sc]
  • Acute kidney injury: Rapid decline in renal function indicated by rising creatinine, suggesting severe glomerular damage or complications like acute tubular necrosis.
  • Severe hypovolaemia/shock: Signs of circulatory collapse (tachycardia, hypotension, reduced urine output) due to massive fluid shifts, requiring urgent fluid resuscitation.
  • Thromboembolism: Sudden onset of chest pain, dyspnoea, or limb swelling, indicating a hypercoagulable state and potential pulmonary embolism or deep vein thrombosis.
  • Severe infection: Fever, chills, localised pain, or peritonism, as patients are immunocompromised due to immunoglobulin loss and steroid use, increasing risk of sepsis.
  • Hypertensive crisis: Markedly elevated blood pressure with end-organ damage, requiring immediate antihypertensive treatment to prevent stroke or further renal damage.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Nephrotic syndrome atypical presentations"][/sc]
  • Older adults: May present with less prominent oedema but more severe complications such as thromboembolism or infection, and may have underlying malignancy as a secondary cause.
  • Minimal change disease in adults: While typically steroid-responsive in children, adults with MCD may have a more protracted course, require higher steroid doses, or be less responsive, necessitating biopsy to confirm diagnosis.
  • Membranous nephropathy: Often presents in adults, particularly those over 40, and is frequently associated with anti-PLA2R antibodies. It has a higher risk of progression to end-stage renal disease and thromboembolic events compared to MCD. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Anti-PLA2R antibody: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Anti-PLA2R antibody"][/sc]
  • Focal segmental glomerulosclerosis (FSGS): Can be primary or secondary and may be steroid-resistant, often progressing to chronic kidney disease. It can recur post-transplant.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Nephrotic syndrome diagnostic tests"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected nephrotic syndrome:
    • Urine dipstick: Gross proteinuria (3+ or 4+).
    • 24-hour urine collection for protein or urine protein:creatinine ratio (UPCR): To quantify proteinuria (>3.5g/24h or >300mg/mmol).
    • Serum albumin: Hypoalbuminaemia (<30g/L).
    • Serum creatinine and eGFR: To assess renal function.
    • Lipid profile: Often shows hypercholesterolaemia and hypertriglyceridaemia.
    • Full blood count: May show anaemia of chronic disease or elevated haemoglobin due to haemoconcentration.
  • Severity/risk tools:
    • CHA2DS2-VASc Score on MDCalc: While not specific for nephrotic syndrome, it can be used to assess general thrombotic risk in patients with additional risk factors.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible or to determine aetiology:
    • Renal biopsy: Essential for definitive diagnosis of the underlying glomerular pathology (e.g., MCD, FSGS, membranous nephropathy) and guiding treatment, especially in adults or steroid-resistant cases.
    • Immunological screen: ANA, anti-dsDNA, C3, C4, ANCA, anti-GBM antibodies if systemic lupus erythematosus or other autoimmune glomerulonephritis is suspected.
    • Serology for infections: HIV, Hepatitis B and C, if clinically indicated.
    • Anti-PLA2R antibody: For diagnosis and monitoring of primary membranous nephropathy. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Renal biopsy in nephrotic syndrome: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Renal biopsy"][/sc]
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Nephrotic syndrome problem representation"][/sc]
  • A 45-year-old male presents with a 3-week history of progressive bilateral ankle swelling, frothy urine, and recent weight gain, found to have significant proteinuria and hypoalbuminaemia on initial investigations.
  • A middle-aged man with insidious onset oedema and heavy proteinuria, concerning for nephrotic syndrome, requiring further investigation to determine underlying aetiology and guide management.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Nephrotic syndrome differential diagnosis"][/sc]
  • Cardiac failure - typically presents with elevated jugular venous pressure, cardiomegaly, and signs of pulmonary congestion, unlike the primary renal pathology of nephrotic syndrome.
  • Liver cirrhosis - characterised by stigmata of chronic liver disease, portal hypertension, and ascites, with oedema often secondary to hypoalbuminaemia but distinct from primary glomerular disease.
  • Protein-losing enteropathy - involves protein loss via the gastrointestinal tract, often with diarrhoea and malabsorption, and typically normal renal function.
  • Severe malnutrition - can cause hypoalbuminaemia and oedema, but usually without significant proteinuria, and with a clear history of inadequate dietary intake.
  • Deep vein thrombosis (DVT) - typically causes unilateral leg swelling, pain, and tenderness, without generalised oedema or significant proteinuria. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Deep vein thrombosis: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Deep vein thrombosis"][/sc]
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Nephrotic syndrome treatment"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Recognition of severe oedema, hypovolaemia, or signs of complications (e.g., infection, thrombosis) often warrants urgent hospital admission for supportive care and further investigation.
  • Definitive management: Treatment is guided by the underlying cause identified through renal biopsy. For minimal change disease, corticosteroids (e.g., prednisolone) are typically the first-line treatment. Immunosuppressants (e.g., ciclosporin, tacrolimus, cyclophosphamide, rituximab) may be considered for steroid-resistant or frequently relapsing cases, or for specific pathologies like membranous nephropathy.
  • Adjunctive management:
    • Diuretics: Loop diuretics (e.g., furosemide) may be used to manage oedema, often in combination with albumin infusions in severe cases.
    • ACE inhibitors/ARBs: May be considered to reduce proteinuria and control blood pressure, with careful monitoring of renal function and potassium.
    • Anticoagulation: Prophylactic anticoagulation may be considered in patients with very low albumin levels (<20g/L) or additional risk factors for thrombosis.
    • Dietary modifications: A low-salt diet is often recommended to help manage oedema.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Regular monitoring of urine protein, serum albumin, renal function, and blood pressure is essential to assess treatment response and detect complications.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Patients should be advised to seek urgent medical attention for worsening oedema, fever, severe pain, or signs of thrombosis.
  • Exam pharmacology reference (UKMLA/MRCGP):
    • [sc name="jitl-medication" Q="Prednisolone use in Nephrotic syndrome" text="Prednisolone"][/sc]: A corticosteroid, often first-line for minimal change disease.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Nephrotic syndrome complications"][/sc]
  • Thromboembolism: Increased risk of deep vein thrombosis and pulmonary embolism due to loss of anticoagulant proteins (e.g., antithrombin III) in the urine, particularly with severe hypoalbuminaemia. This risk persists throughout the active disease phase.
  • Infection: Increased susceptibility to bacterial infections (e.g., spontaneous bacterial peritonitis, cellulitis) due to loss of immunoglobulins and complement factors, and often exacerbated by immunosuppressive therapy. Infections can develop rapidly.
  • Acute kidney injury: Can result from severe hypovolaemia, interstitial nephritis (e.g., due to NSAIDs), or progression of the underlying glomerular disease. This may occur at any stage of the disease.
  • Hyperlipidaemia: Elevated cholesterol and triglyceride levels due to increased hepatic synthesis and reduced catabolism, contributing to long-term cardiovascular risk. This is a chronic complication.
  • Malnutrition: Chronic protein loss can lead to protein-energy malnutrition, particularly in prolonged or frequently relapsing cases.
  • End-stage renal disease: A long-term complication of progressive glomerular diseases like FSGS and some forms of membranous nephropathy, leading to the need for renal replacement therapy over months to years.
