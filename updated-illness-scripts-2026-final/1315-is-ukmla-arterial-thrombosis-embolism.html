Arterial thrombosis/embolism [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Arterial thrombosis/embolism"]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Arterial thrombosis/embolism"]
  • Arterial thrombosis/embolism refers to the sudden occlusion of an artery by a thrombus (clot formed in situ) or an embolus (clot or other material travelling from elsewhere), leading to acute ischaemia of the supplied tissue.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical features of acute limb ischaemia, differentiate embolic from thrombotic causes, and understand the immediate management principles to preserve limb viability.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Arterial thrombosis/embolism"]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Arterial thrombosis/embolism"]
  • Acute limb ischaemia (ALI) has an estimated incidence of approximately 1 per 6000 population per year in the UK [1].
  • The incidence of ALI caused by embolic disease is decreasing, while presentations on a background of peripheral arterial disease (thrombotic) are becoming more common, particularly in an ageing population [1].
  • Risk factors for arterial thrombosis/embolism include atrial fibrillation, peripheral arterial disease, recent myocardial infarction, valvular heart disease, hypercoagulable states, and arterial aneurysms.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Arterial thrombosis/embolism"]
  • Trigger: An embolus, often originating from the heart (e.g., in atrial fibrillation), or a thrombus forming in situ, typically on an atherosclerotic plaque, suddenly obstructs arterial blood flow.
  • Progression: The sudden cessation of blood supply leads to tissue hypoxia and ischaemia distal to the occlusion, initiating an inflammatory response and cellular damage. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Ischaemia-reperfusion injury: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Ischaemia-reperfusion injury"]
  • Consequence: Prolonged ischaemia results in irreversible tissue damage, potentially leading to necrosis, limb loss, or organ dysfunction (e.g., in mesenteric ischaemia).
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Arterial thrombosis/embolism"]
  • Early phase: Symptoms of acute limb ischaemia typically develop rapidly, often within minutes to hours, following the sudden arterial occlusion.
  • Progressive phase: Without timely revascularisation, ischaemic damage progresses over several hours, with increasing pain, sensory loss, and motor weakness, particularly beyond 6 hours.
  • Late phase: Irreversible tissue damage and necrosis can occur within 6-12 hours, leading to limb loss or systemic complications if not addressed promptly.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Arterial thrombosis/embolism"]
  • Ask about sudden onset pain - often severe and distal to the occlusion, indicating acute lack of blood flow.
  • Ask about paraesthesia - numbness or tingling, reflecting nerve ischaemia and a more advanced stage of limb threat.
  • Ask about paralysis - weakness or inability to move the affected limb, signifying severe nerve and muscle ischaemia, often a poor prognostic sign.
  • Ask about claudication history - a sudden worsening of pre-existing claudication may suggest acute-on-chronic thrombosis.
  • Ask about abdominal pain - in the context of mesenteric ischaemia, this is typically severe, out of proportion to examination findings, and post-prandial.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Arterial thrombosis/embolism"]
  • Check for the "Six Ps" of acute limb ischaemia: Pain, Pallor, Pulselessness, Perishingly cold, Paraesthesia, Paralysis – these indicate the severity of ischaemia. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Six Ps of acute limb ischaemia: what are they and what do they indicate?" text="Six Ps"]
  • Check pulses - absence of pulses distal to the occlusion is a key diagnostic sign.
  • Check capillary refill time - prolonged capillary refill indicates poor perfusion.
  • Check for motor and sensory deficits - these are critical for assessing limb viability and guiding management decisions.
  • Check vital signs - hypotension may indicate a systemic cause or severe reperfusion injury.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Arterial thrombosis/embolism"]
  • Paralysis or profound sensory loss: Indicates severe and potentially irreversible ischaemia, requiring immediate revascularisation to prevent limb loss.
  • Fixed mottling or muscle rigidity: Suggests established tissue necrosis, often precluding successful revascularisation and indicating a need for primary amputation.
  • Sudden, severe abdominal pain with minimal examination findings: Highly suspicious for acute mesenteric ischaemia, a life-threatening condition requiring urgent diagnosis and intervention.
  • Rapid progression of symptoms: A quick deterioration in pain, sensation, or motor function over a few hours highlights the urgency of intervention.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Arterial thrombosis/embolism"]
  • Acute-on-chronic limb ischaemia: Patients with pre-existing peripheral arterial disease may have attenuated "Six Ps" due to collateral circulation, making diagnosis challenging. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Acute-on-chronic limb ischaemia: how does it differ from acute limb ischaemia?" text="Acute-on-chronic ischaemia"]
  • Mesenteric ischaemia: Presents as severe abdominal pain, often disproportionate to physical findings, and can be easily missed or misdiagnosed as other abdominal pathologies.
  • Blue toe syndrome: Microemboli from proximal atherosclerotic disease can cause painful, discoloured toes without major limb ischaemia, indicating a source of emboli that needs addressing.
  • Upper limb ischaemia: Less common than lower limb, often due to emboli from the heart or subclavian artery disease, presenting with similar ischaemic symptoms in the arm/hand.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Arterial thrombosis/embolism"]
  • Core for suspected acute limb ischaemia:
    • Arterial Doppler ultrasound: To confirm absent flow and locate the level of occlusion.
    • CT angiography (CTA) or MR angiography (MRA): To precisely delineate the arterial anatomy, extent of occlusion, and identify embolic source or underlying atherosclerotic disease.
    • Electrocardiogram (ECG): To identify atrial fibrillation or recent myocardial infarction as a potential embolic source.
    • Blood tests: Full blood count, urea and electrolytes, coagulation screen, C-reactive protein, lactate (elevated in severe ischaemia).
  • Severity/risk tools:
    • Rutherford Classification of Acute Limb Ischaemia: Used to classify severity and guide management. Rutherford Classification on MDCalc
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible:
    • Venous duplex ultrasound: To rule out deep vein thrombosis.
  • Serial reassessment:
    • Regular clinical assessment of the limb (pain, sensation, motor function, pulses) is crucial to monitor progression and response to treatment.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Arterial thrombosis/embolism"]
  • A 70-year-old male with a history of atrial fibrillation presents with sudden onset severe pain, pallor, and pulselessness in his right leg, developing over the last 4 hours.
  • Elderly male with a cardiac history presenting with acute, painful, pale, pulseless, and perishingly cold limb, concerning for acute limb ischaemia secondary to arterial embolism.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Arterial thrombosis/embolism"]
  • Acute Deep Vein Thrombosis (DVT) - typically presents with swelling, warmth, and tenderness, but pulses are usually preserved.
  • Cellulitis - usually presents with erythema, warmth, and tenderness, often with a clear portal of entry, and pulses are preserved.
  • Peripheral Neuropathy - chronic condition with sensory and motor deficits, but typically not acute onset pain and absent pulses.
  • Compartment Syndrome - severe pain, often disproportionate to injury, with tense compartments and pain on passive stretch, but pulses may be present initially. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Compartment syndrome: what is it and how does it present?" text="Compartment syndrome"]
  • Spinal Cord Ischaemia - sudden onset neurological deficits, but usually bilateral and without peripheral vascular signs.
  • Vasculitis - can cause ischaemia, but often with systemic symptoms and other organ involvement, and typically not as acutely severe as arterial occlusion.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Arterial thrombosis/embolism"]
  • Immediate priorities: Recognition of acute limb ischaemia, urgent referral to a vascular surgical team, and initiation of intravenous unfractionated heparin to prevent clot propagation.
  • Definitive management: Often involves revascularisation via surgical embolectomy/thrombectomy or endovascular thrombolysis, depending on the severity, duration of ischaemia, and patient factors. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Embolectomy vs thrombolysis in acute limb ischaemia: when is each indicated?" text="Embolectomy vs thrombolysis"]
  • Adjunctive management: Pain control, fluid management, and close monitoring for reperfusion injury.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Regular assessment of limb viability, pulse checks, and monitoring for complications such as compartment syndrome or renal failure.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Any deterioration in limb status (increasing pain, sensory/motor loss) warrants immediate re-evaluation and discussion with the vascular team.
  • Exam pharmacology reference (UKMLA/MRCGP):
    • Anticoagulation: [sc name="jitl-medication" Q="Unfractionated heparin use in arterial thrombosis/embolism" text="Unfractionated heparin"] is often used acutely. Long-term anticoagulation with [sc name="jitl-medication" Q="Warfarin use in arterial thrombosis/embolism" text="warfarin"] or [sc name="jitl-medication" Q="DOACs use in arterial thrombosis/embolism" text="direct oral anticoagulants (DOACs)"] may be indicated for embolic sources like atrial fibrillation.
    • Antiplatelet agents: [sc name="jitl-medication" Q="Aspirin use in arterial thrombosis/embolism" text="Aspirin"] or [sc name="jitl-medication" Q="Clopidogrel use in arterial thrombosis/embolism" text="clopidogrel"] may be used in thrombotic disease.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Arterial thrombosis/embolism"]
  • Limb loss: Irreversible ischaemia, particularly if revascularisation is delayed beyond 6-12 hours, can necessitate amputation.
  • Reperfusion injury: Upon restoration of blood flow, the sudden influx of oxygen and inflammatory mediators can cause further tissue damage, oedema, and systemic effects, often occurring within the first 24-48 hours post-revascularisation.
  • Compartment syndrome: Severe limb oedema following reperfusion can increase intracompartmental pressure, compromising blood flow and requiring urgent fasciotomy to prevent further muscle and nerve damage, typically developing within hours of revascularisation.
  • Acute kidney injury: Release of myoglobin from damaged muscle cells (rhabdomyolysis) can lead to acute tubular necrosis, a risk that increases with the duration and severity of ischaemia.
  • Systemic inflammatory response syndrome (SIRS): Widespread inflammation due to reperfusion injury or absorption of toxins from necrotic tissue can lead to multi-organ dysfunction, often manifesting within days.
  • Death: High mortality rates are associated with severe acute limb ischaemia and mesenteric ischaemia, particularly if diagnosis and intervention are delayed.
