Bladder cancer [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Bladder cancer"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Bladder cancer"][/sc]
  • Bladder cancer encompasses malignant neoplasms arising from the urothelium lining the bladder, with transitional cell carcinoma being the most common histological type.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the common presentations of bladder cancer, including painless haematuria, and understand the principles of urgent referral and initial diagnostic pathways.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Bladder cancer"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Bladder cancer"][/sc]
  • Approximately 10,000 new bladder cancers are diagnosed each year in the UK, making it the seventh most common cancer overall.
  • Incidence is higher in men than women, and risk increases significantly with age, particularly over 60 years.
  • Smoking is the most significant modifiable risk factor, accounting for approximately half of all cases. Other risk factors include occupational exposure to certain chemicals (e.g., aromatic amines), chronic bladder inflammation, and previous pelvic radiotherapy.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Bladder cancer"][/sc]
  • Exposure to carcinogens, primarily from tobacco smoke, leads to DNA damage and genetic mutations within the urothelial cells lining the bladder.
  • These genetic alterations promote uncontrolled proliferation of abnormal urothelial cells, forming a tumour that can be non-muscle invasive or progress to invade the muscular layer of the bladder wall. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Urothelial carcinoma: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Urothelial carcinoma"][/sc]
  • Continued tumour growth and invasion can lead to local complications such as haematuria and obstruction, and ultimately distant metastasis if not effectively treated.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Bladder cancer"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Often presents with intermittent, painless haematuria, which may resolve spontaneously, leading to delayed presentation. Non-muscle invasive bladder cancer (NMIBC) can recur within months to years.
  • Progressive phase: NMIBC may progress to muscle-invasive bladder cancer (MIBC) over months to years, particularly if high-grade or recurrent. This progression significantly worsens prognosis and requires more aggressive treatment.
  • Late phase: Untreated or aggressive MIBC can lead to local invasion of surrounding organs and distant metastasis, typically to lymph nodes, lungs, liver, and bone, often within months of muscle invasion.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Bladder cancer"][/sc]
  • Ask about painless visible haematuria - this is the cardinal symptom and should always prompt urgent investigation for bladder cancer, even if intermittent.
  • Ask about irritative voiding symptoms (e.g., frequency, urgency, dysuria) - these can mimic urinary tract infection but may indicate carcinoma in situ or advanced disease, especially in the absence of infection.
  • Ask about suprapubic or flank pain - persistent pain may suggest local invasion or hydronephrosis due to ureteric obstruction.
  • Ask about constitutional symptoms (e.g., weight loss, fatigue) - these are non-specific but can indicate advanced or metastatic disease.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Bladder cancer"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - may reveal pallor and tachycardia in cases of significant chronic haematuria leading to anaemia, or signs of systemic illness in advanced metastatic disease.
  • Check for suprapubic tenderness or a palpable mass - may indicate a large bladder tumour or local invasion in advanced stages.
  • Check for lower extremity oedema - can suggest iliac vessel compression by advanced pelvic disease.
  • Check for flank tenderness - may indicate hydronephrosis secondary to ureteric obstruction by tumour. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Painless haematuria: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Painless haematuria"][/sc]
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Bladder cancer"][/sc]
  • Painless visible haematuria: Requires urgent referral (within 2 weeks) for investigation, particularly in individuals aged 45 and over, due to its strong association with bladder cancer.
  • Persistent or recurrent unexplained visible haematuria: Even if initial episodes resolve, recurrence mandates prompt re-evaluation as it signifies ongoing risk.
  • Unexplained non-visible haematuria with dysuria or raised white cell count: In individuals aged 60 and over, this combination warrants investigation for bladder cancer, especially if other causes are excluded.
  • Persistent lower urinary tract symptoms (LUTS): New onset or worsening LUTS, particularly in older patients and in the absence of infection, should raise suspicion for bladder cancer and prompt further assessment.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Bladder cancer"][/sc]
  • Women: Bladder cancer symptoms, particularly irritative voiding symptoms, are often misdiagnosed as recurrent urinary tract infections (UTIs), leading to delayed diagnosis.
  • Older adults: May present with non-specific symptoms such as fatigue or general malaise, or have co-morbidities that mask typical urinary symptoms, making diagnosis challenging.
  • Carcinoma in situ (CIS): This aggressive form of non-invasive bladder cancer may present solely with irritative voiding symptoms (frequency, urgency, dysuria) without visible haematuria, making it difficult to distinguish from benign conditions. Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Bladder cancer"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected bladder cancer:
    • Urine dipstick and microscopy: To confirm haematuria and exclude infection.
    • Urine cytology: Can detect malignant cells, particularly in high-grade tumours, but has variable sensitivity.
    • Cystoscopy with biopsy: The gold standard for diagnosis, allowing direct visualisation of the bladder mucosa and histological confirmation of tumour.
  • Severity/risk tools:
    • EORTC Bladder Cancer Recurrence and Progression Calculator: Used to estimate the risk of recurrence and progression in non-muscle invasive bladder cancer. EORTC Bladder Cancer Recurrence and Progression Calculator on MDCalc
  • Imaging - Ultrasound: Initial imaging modality to assess for hydronephrosis and large bladder masses, often performed in primary care.
  • Imaging - CT Urography: Recommended for staging and to assess the upper urinary tract for synchronous tumours, particularly in patients with visible haematuria.
  • Imaging - MRI: Increasingly used for local staging of muscle-invasive bladder cancer, providing better soft tissue contrast than CT.
  • Serial reassessment: Regular cystoscopic surveillance is crucial for non-muscle invasive bladder cancer due to high recurrence rates.

  • A 68-year-old male presents with recurrent, painless visible haematuria over the past three months, associated with increased urinary frequency but no dysuria or fever.
  • Elderly male with recurrent painless haematuria and irritative voiding symptoms, concerning for urothelial carcinoma of the bladder.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Bladder cancer"][/sc]
  • Urinary Tract Infection (UTI): Often presents with dysuria, frequency, and haematuria, but typically associated with positive urine culture and responds to antibiotics.
  • Benign Prostatic Hyperplasia (BPH): In men, can cause lower urinary tract symptoms (LUTS) and sometimes haematuria, but usually without the same red flag features as bladder cancer.
  • Urolithiasis (Kidney Stones): May cause haematuria and flank pain, but pain is typically colicky and severe, and stones are visible on imaging.
  • Renal Cell Carcinoma: Can present with haematuria, but often accompanied by flank pain and a palpable mass, and originates from the kidney rather than the bladder.
  • Overactive Bladder (OAB): Characterised by urgency and frequency, but typically without haematuria or other signs suggestive of malignancy.
  • Radiation Cystitis: Occurs in patients with a history of pelvic radiotherapy, causing irritative voiding symptoms and haematuria due to bladder inflammation. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Causes of haematuria: what are the common and serious causes?" text="Haematuria causes"][/sc]
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Bladder cancer"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Recognition of red flag symptoms and urgent referral to urology for investigation is paramount. Supportive care may include analgesia for pain and management of anaemia secondary to haematuria.
  • Definitive management:
    • Transurethral resection of bladder tumour (TURBT): Primary treatment for non-muscle invasive bladder cancer (NMIBC), both diagnostic and therapeutic.
    • Intravesical therapy: Often includes Bacillus Calmette-Guérin (BCG) or chemotherapy (e.g., Mitomycin C) instilled directly into the bladder to reduce recurrence risk in NMIBC, particularly high-risk disease.
    • Radical cystectomy: Surgical removal of the bladder, typically for muscle-invasive bladder cancer (MIBC), often with urinary diversion.
    • Radiotherapy and chemotherapy: May be used in combination for MIBC, either as neoadjuvant, adjuvant, or definitive treatment, depending on tumour stage and patient fitness.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Regular cystoscopic surveillance is essential for NMIBC due to high recurrence rates. Imaging (CT/MRI) and urine cytology are used for ongoing monitoring, especially for MIBC.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Patients should be advised to report any recurrence of haematuria or worsening urinary symptoms promptly. Clear pathways for re-referral and escalation of care should be in place.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Bladder cancer"][/sc]
  • Ureteric obstruction and hydronephrosis: Local tumour growth can obstruct the ureters, leading to back pressure on the kidneys, potentially causing renal impairment if not relieved. This can occur in the progressive or late phases.
  • Haemorrhage: Tumour ulceration can lead to significant, recurrent haematuria, potentially causing severe anaemia and requiring transfusions. This can be a feature throughout the disease course.
  • Local invasion: Advanced bladder cancer can invade adjacent organs such as the prostate, seminal vesicles, uterus, or vaginal wall, leading to pain, fistula formation, and impaired organ function. This is characteristic of the late phase.
  • Metastatic disease: Spread to distant sites, commonly lymph nodes, lungs, liver, and bone, leads to systemic symptoms (e.g., bone pain, pathological fractures, dyspnoea, weight loss, fatigue) and significantly impacts prognosis. This occurs in the late phase.
  • Recurrence: Non-muscle invasive bladder cancer has a high rate of recurrence, necessitating long-term surveillance and repeated interventions, impacting quality of life and increasing the risk of progression. This is a persistent risk throughout the post-treatment course.
