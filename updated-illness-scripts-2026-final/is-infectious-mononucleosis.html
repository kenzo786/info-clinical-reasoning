Infectious mononucleosis [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Infectious mononucleosis"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Infectious mononucleosis"][/sc]
  • Infectious mononucleosis, also known as glandular fever, is a clinical syndrome most commonly caused by primary infection with the Epstein-Barr virus (EBV), a member of the human herpes virus family.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the classic presentation of infectious mononucleosis and identify key complications such as splenic rupture and airway obstruction.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Infectious mononucleosis"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Infectious mononucleosis"][/sc]
  • In the UK, infectious mononucleosis is most common in people aged 15 to 24 years old. The annual incidence in people aged 15 to 19 years was 2.7%, and among those aged 20 to 24 years was 1.3%.[1]
  • EBV seroprevalence in the UK appears to have increased, with a sharp rise in seropositivity occurring earlier in life.[2]
  • Transmission primarily occurs through saliva (e.g., kissing, sharing utensils), with individuals being infectious for weeks before symptom onset and potentially for several months after recovery.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Infectious mononucleosis"][/sc]
  • Primary infection with Epstein-Barr virus (EBV) typically occurs via oral secretions, leading to replication in oropharyngeal epithelial cells and subsequent infection of B lymphocytes.
  • Infected B cells proliferate and disseminate throughout the lymphoid system, triggering a robust cytotoxic T-lymphocyte response that is responsible for many of the clinical manifestations. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Epstein-Barr virus: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Epstein-Barr virus"][/sc]
  • The immune response, while controlling viral replication, can lead to widespread lymphadenopathy, splenomegaly, and pharyngitis, with latent infection persisting in B cells for life.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Infectious mononucleosis"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Following an incubation period of 4-6 weeks, initial symptoms such as malaise, fatigue, and headache typically develop gradually over several days.
  • Progressive phase: Within a week of symptom onset, the classic triad of fever, pharyngitis, and lymphadenopathy usually becomes prominent, often accompanied by significant fatigue that can be debilitating.
  • Late phase: Acute symptoms generally resolve within 2-4 weeks, though fatigue can persist for several months, particularly beyond 4 weeks, with full recovery sometimes taking up to 6 months.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Infectious mononucleosis"][/sc]
  • Ask about extreme fatigue - _this is often the most prominent and prolonged symptom, impacting daily activities_.
  • Ask about sore throat - _often severe, exudative, and persistent, which can be mistaken for bacterial pharyngitis_.
  • Ask about fever - _typically high-grade and can be sustained, contributing to overall malaise_.
  • Ask about headache - _a common non-specific symptom, but can be severe_.
  • Ask about rash - _may occur, especially if amoxicillin or ampicillin has been administered, which is a key discriminator_.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Infectious mononucleosis"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - _fever is common, and tachycardia may be present_.
  • Check lymphadenopathy - _generalized, tender lymph node enlargement, particularly in the posterior cervical chain, axillae, and groin_. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Lymphadenopathy: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Lymphadenopathy"][/sc]
  • Check pharyngeal examination - _erythema, exudates, and palatal petechiae are frequently observed_.
  • Check abdominal examination - _splenomegaly is common (50-60% of cases) and hepatomegaly may also be present_.
  • Check for rash - _maculopapular rash may be present, especially if antibiotics like amoxicillin have been given_.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Infectious mononucleosis"][/sc]
  • Splenic rupture: Acute onset of severe left upper quadrant abdominal pain, often radiating to the left shoulder (Kehr\'s sign), indicating a potentially life-threatening haemorrhage, particularly during the second and third weeks of illness.
  • Airway obstruction: Severe tonsillar hypertrophy and pharyngeal oedema leading to stridor, dysphagia, or respiratory distress, requiring urgent assessment and management.
  • Neurological complications: Seizures, focal neurological deficits, or altered mental status, suggesting conditions such as encephalitis, meningitis, or Guillain-Barré syndrome.
  • Severe dehydration: Due to painful swallowing and reduced oral intake, particularly in prolonged illness.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Infectious mononucleosis"][/sc]
  • Young children: Often asymptomatic or present with mild, non-specific symptoms that may be mistaken for other common viral infections, making diagnosis challenging.
  • Older adults: May present with less prominent lymphadenopathy and pharyngitis, but more pronounced hepatomegaly, jaundice, and prolonged fatigue, potentially mimicking other systemic illnesses.
  • Immunocompromised individuals: Can experience more severe and prolonged illness, with a higher risk of complications and reactivation, due to impaired immune control of EBV. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Immunocompromised: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Immunocompromised"][/sc]
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Infectious mononucleosis"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected infectious mononucleosis:
    • Full blood count (FBC): May show lymphocytosis with atypical lymphocytes.
    • Heterophile antibody test (Monospot test): Often positive in adolescents and adults, though may be negative in young children or early in the illness. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Heterophile antibody test: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Heterophile antibody test"][/sc]
    • EBV-specific serology: Detects antibodies to viral capsid antigen (VCA), early antigen (EA), and nuclear antigen (EBNA) to confirm acute or past infection, particularly if Monospot is negative or in atypical cases.
    • Liver function tests (LFTs): Mild transaminitis is common.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible:
    • Throat swab for bacterial culture (e.g., Group A Streptococcus) if streptococcal pharyngitis is suspected.
    • CMV serology if CMV mononucleosis is considered, especially with negative EBV tests.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Infectious mononucleosis"][/sc]
  • A 19-year-old male presents with a two-week history of severe fatigue, sore throat, and palpable lumps in his neck, accompanied by a maculopapular rash after taking amoxicillin.
  • Adolescent with prolonged constitutional symptoms, prominent lymphadenopathy, and drug-induced rash, concerning for infectious mononucleosis.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Infectious mononucleosis"][/sc]
  • Cytomegalovirus (CMV) infection - _can cause a mononucleosis-like syndrome but typically with less severe pharyngitis and lymphadenopathy, and a negative heterophile antibody test_.
  • Streptococcal pharyngitis - _often presents with acute onset, prominent tonsillar exudates, and absence of significant fatigue or generalized lymphadenopathy_.
  • Acute HIV infection - _may present with fever, rash, lymphadenopathy, and pharyngitis, but usually lacks atypical lymphocytes and heterophile antibodies_. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Acute HIV infection: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Acute HIV infection"][/sc]
  • Toxoplasmosis - _can cause lymphadenopathy and fatigue, but usually without severe pharyngitis or atypical lymphocytes_.
  • Lymphoma - _persistent, non-tender lymphadenopathy, often without acute viral symptoms, should raise suspicion_.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Infectious mononucleosis"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Recognition of severe complications such as airway compromise or splenic rupture, requiring urgent medical assessment and escalation to secondary care. Supportive care often includes rest and adequate hydration.
  • Definitive management: Treatment is primarily symptomatic, focusing on alleviating discomfort. This typically involves analgesics (e.g., paracetamol, ibuprofen) for fever and pain.
  • Adjunctive management: Corticosteroids may be considered in severe cases with significant airway obstruction or severe thrombocytopenia, but their routine use is not recommended.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Patients should be advised to avoid strenuous physical activity and contact sports for at least 3-4 weeks (or until splenomegaly resolves) to prevent splenic rupture.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Patients should be educated on red flag symptoms (e.g., severe abdominal pain, difficulty breathing) and advised to seek immediate medical attention if these develop.
  • Exam pharmacology reference (UKMLA/MRCGP):
    • Avoidance of amoxicillin/ampicillin: These antibiotics often induce a widespread maculopapular rash in patients with infectious mononucleosis, which can be mistaken for an allergic reaction.
    • [sc name="jitl-medication" Q="Aciclovir use in Infectious mononucleosis" text="Aciclovir"][/sc]: Antiviral agents like aciclovir are not routinely recommended as they do not significantly alter the course of uncomplicated infectious mononucleosis.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Infectious mononucleosis"][/sc]
  • Splenic rupture: A rare but life-threatening complication, most commonly occurring in the second or third week of illness, due to capsular distension and fragility of the enlarged spleen.
  • Airway obstruction: Can result from massive tonsillar and pharyngeal lymphoid hyperplasia, particularly during the acute phase of the illness, leading to respiratory compromise.
  • Neurological complications: Include encephalitis, meningitis, Guillain-Barré syndrome, and cranial nerve palsies, typically manifesting during the acute or subacute phase.
  • Haematological complications: Such as autoimmune haemolytic anaemia or severe thrombocytopenia, which can occur during the acute illness and require close monitoring.
  • Chronic fatigue: Persistent and debilitating fatigue can extend for several months beyond the resolution of acute symptoms, significantly impacting recovery.
