Hypoglycaemia [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Hypoglycaemia"]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Hypoglycaemia"]
  • Hypoglycaemia is defined as a plasma glucose concentration below 3.0 mmol/L, or a level sufficient to cause symptoms and/or signs of impaired brain function.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical features of hypoglycaemia, identify common causes including iatrogenic, and outline immediate management principles.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Hypoglycaemia"]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Hypoglycaemia"]
  • Hypoglycaemia is a common complication of diabetes treatment, particularly with insulin or sulphonylureas, affecting up to 50% of insulin-treated patients annually.
  • Severe hypoglycaemia (requiring assistance from another person) occurs in approximately 10% of insulin-treated patients with type 1 diabetes per year.
  • Non-diabetic causes are rare, with insulinoma having an estimated incidence of 1-4 cases per million per year in the UK.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Hypoglycaemia"]
  • Trigger: An imbalance between glucose supply and demand, often due to excessive insulin action (iatrogenic, insulinoma) or impaired glucose counter-regulation (e.g., Addison disease).
  • Progression: As plasma glucose falls, counter-regulatory hormones (glucagon, adrenaline, cortisol, growth hormone) are released to increase glucose production, but if these mechanisms fail or are overwhelmed, neuroglycopenic symptoms develop.
  • Consequence: Prolonged or severe neuroglycopenia can lead to neuronal dysfunction, seizures, coma, and irreversible brain damage, particularly in vulnerable populations.

  • [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Insulinoma: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Insulinoma"]
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Hypoglycaemia"]
  • Early phase: Adrenergic symptoms (tremor, palpitations, anxiety, sweating) typically manifest rapidly as glucose levels begin to fall, often within minutes to an hour of onset.
  • Progressive phase: Neuroglycopenic symptoms (confusion, difficulty concentrating, slurred speech, weakness) emerge as glucose levels continue to decline, usually within 1-2 hours if untreated.
  • Late phase: Severe neuroglycopenia can lead to seizures, coma, and potentially irreversible neurological damage if not corrected promptly, particularly beyond 2-4 hours of profound hypoglycaemia.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Hypoglycaemia"]
  • Ask about tremor, palpitations, and anxiety - these adrenergic symptoms are often the earliest warning signs, prompting self-treatment.
  • Ask about sweating and hunger - these are also common autonomic symptoms that can precede neuroglycopenia.
  • Ask about confusion, difficulty concentrating, and unusual behaviour - these neuroglycopenic symptoms indicate cerebral glucose deprivation and require urgent intervention.
  • Ask about headache, visual disturbances (e.g., blurred vision, diplopia), and weakness - these can also be neuroglycopenic manifestations.
  • Ask about the sequence of symptoms - adrenergic symptoms typically precede neuroglycopenic symptoms, but this sequence may be blunted in recurrent or severe hypoglycaemia (hypoglycaemia unawareness).
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Hypoglycaemia"]
  • Check vital signs - tachycardia, hypertension (early), or hypotension (late/severe) may be present; tachypnoea can occur due to metabolic acidosis.
  • Check neurological status - altered mental state ranging from mild confusion to coma, focal neurological deficits (e.g., hemiparesis), or seizures may be observed.
  • Check for diaphoresis - profuse sweating is a classic adrenergic sign.
  • Check for tremor - fine or coarse tremor can be a prominent adrenergic manifestation.
  • Check for signs of underlying cause - e.g., signs of Addison disease (hyperpigmentation, postural hypotension) or features suggestive of insulinoma.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Hypoglycaemia"]
  • Persistent altered mental state: Indicates ongoing neuroglycopenia or secondary neurological injury, requiring immediate and sustained glucose correction.
  • Seizures: A sign of severe cerebral glucose deprivation, necessitating urgent intravenous glucose and investigation for underlying cause.
  • Unconsciousness: Represents profound neuroglycopenia and carries a high risk of aspiration and neurological sequelae if not rapidly reversed.
  • Recurrent hypoglycaemia without clear cause: Suggests an underlying pathology such as insulinoma or adrenal insufficiency, warranting specialist endocrine investigation.
  • Hypoglycaemia unawareness: Absence of typical adrenergic warning symptoms, increasing the risk of severe neuroglycopenia and delaying intervention, particularly beyond 1-2 hours of onset.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Hypoglycaemia"]
  • Older adults: May present with non-specific symptoms such as falls, confusion, or cognitive decline, often without typical adrenergic warning signs, making diagnosis challenging.
  • Patients on beta-blockers: Adrenergic symptoms (e.g., tremor, palpitations) may be masked, increasing the risk of hypoglycaemia unawareness and severe neuroglycopenia.
  • Critical illness: Hypoglycaemia can be difficult to recognise in sedated or ventilated patients, often presenting as unexplained neurological deterioration or cardiac arrhythmias.
  • Renal impairment: Reduced insulin clearance can prolong the action of insulin or sulphonylureas, increasing the risk and duration of hypoglycaemia.
  • Alcohol intoxication: Can impair hepatic gluconeogenesis, exacerbating hypoglycaemia and mimicking its neurological symptoms, leading to delayed diagnosis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Hypoglycaemia"]
  • Core for suspected hypoglycaemia:
    • Capillary blood glucose (point-of-care testing) for immediate confirmation.
    • Laboratory plasma glucose for definitive diagnosis and to correlate with symptoms (Whipple triad).
    • Paired samples (glucose, insulin, C-peptide, proinsulin, sulphonylurea screen) during a hypoglycaemic episode to investigate endogenous hyperinsulinism (e.g., insulinoma).
  • Severity/risk tools: No widely validated severity/risk scores are routinely used for acute hypoglycaemia in clinical practice.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible:
    • Liver function tests and coagulation screen to assess for severe hepatic dysfunction.
    • Adrenal function tests (e.g., short synacthen test) if Addison disease is suspected.
    • Thyroid function tests to exclude hypothyroidism.
  • Imaging - CT/MRI abdomen: If insulinoma is strongly suspected based on biochemical testing, imaging may be used to localise the tumour.
  • Serial reassessment: Regular blood glucose monitoring is crucial to ensure sustained correction and prevent recurrence, especially after initial treatment.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Hypoglycaemia"]
  • A 68-year-old male with type 2 diabetes on gliclazide presents with acute confusion, slurred speech, and profuse sweating, found to have a capillary blood glucose of 2.1 mmol/L.
  • An elderly diabetic patient with altered mental status and autonomic symptoms, concerning for iatrogenic hypoglycaemia.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Hypoglycaemia"]
  • Stroke/TIA - focal neurological deficits may mimic hypoglycaemia, but glucose levels are typically normal.
  • Seizure disorder - can cause altered consciousness and post-ictal confusion, but usually without the preceding autonomic symptoms of hypoglycaemia.
  • Sepsis/Infection - can cause altered mental state and physiological derangement, but often with fever and other signs of infection.
  • Alcohol intoxication - can cause confusion, slurred speech, and impaired consciousness, but breath alcohol levels would be elevated.
  • Hyperglycaemic hyperosmolar state (HHS) - presents with profound dehydration and altered mental state, but with very high glucose levels.
  • Diabetic ketoacidosis (DKA) - presents with hyperglycaemia, acidosis, and ketosis, often with abdominal pain and vomiting.
  • [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Addison disease: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Addison disease"] - can cause hypoglycaemia due to cortisol deficiency, often with other features like hypotension and hyperpigmentation.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Hypoglycaemia"]
  • Immediate priorities: Recognition of hypoglycaemia, rapid administration of glucose (oral if conscious, intravenous if unconscious or unable to swallow), and escalation to senior medical staff.
  • Definitive management: Correction of glucose deficit with oral rapidly absorbed carbohydrates (e.g., glucose tablets, sugary drinks) or intravenous 10% or 20% glucose solution, followed by complex carbohydrates to prevent recurrence.
  • Adjunctive management: Glucagon injection (intramuscular or subcutaneous) may be considered in unconscious patients where intravenous access is difficult, particularly in the community setting.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Frequent blood glucose monitoring until stable, identification and addressing of the underlying cause, and patient education on prevention and management of future episodes.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Patients with severe or recurrent hypoglycaemia, or those with suspected underlying pathology, often warrant specialist endocrine review and adjustment of their treatment regimen.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Hypoglycaemia"]
  • Neurological damage: Prolonged or severe neuroglycopenia, particularly beyond 2-4 hours, can lead to irreversible cognitive impairment, seizures, and persistent vegetative state due to neuronal cell death.
  • Cardiovascular events: Hypoglycaemia can trigger arrhythmias, myocardial ischaemia, and sudden cardiac death, especially in patients with pre-existing cardiovascular disease, often occurring within hours of the episode.
  • Falls and injuries: Impaired consciousness and coordination during hypoglycaemia increase the risk of falls, fractures, and other trauma, which can occur rapidly with onset of symptoms.
  • Recurrent hypoglycaemia: Frequent episodes can lead to hypoglycaemia unawareness, making future episodes harder to detect and increasing the risk of severe complications.
  • Increased mortality: Severe hypoglycaemia is independently associated with increased short-term and long-term mortality, with risks persisting for days to weeks after an episode.
