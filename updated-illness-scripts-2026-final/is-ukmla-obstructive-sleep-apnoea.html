Obstructive sleep apnoea (Lungs, pleura and airways) [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Obstructive sleep apnoea"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Obstructive sleep apnoea"][/sc]
  • Obstructive sleep apnoea (OSA) is a condition characterised by recurrent episodes of upper airway collapse during sleep, leading to partial (hypopnoea) or complete (apnoea) cessation of breathing.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical features of obstructive sleep apnoea, understand its diagnostic criteria, and outline initial management strategies including lifestyle modifications and continuous positive airway pressure (CPAP).
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Obstructive sleep apnoea"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Obstructive sleep apnoea"][/sc]
  • Prevalence in the UK is estimated to be around 4% in middle-aged men and 2% in middle-aged women, though it is often underdiagnosed.
  • Risk factors include obesity (BMI >30 kg/m²), male sex, increasing age, craniofacial abnormalities, and alcohol or sedative use.
  • The Apnoea-Hypopnoea Index (AHI) is a key diagnostic measure, with >5 events/hour indicating OSA.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Obstructive sleep apnoea"][/sc]
  • Trigger: During sleep, reduced muscle tone in the upper airway, particularly the pharynx, leads to narrowing or collapse, often exacerbated by anatomical factors like enlarged tonsils or retrognathia.
  • Progression: Airway obstruction causes a reduction in airflow, leading to apnoeas (cessation of breathing) or hypopnoeas (partial reduction in airflow), resulting in intermittent hypoxia and hypercapnia.
  • Consequence: The body responds to hypoxia and hypercapnia with brief arousals from sleep, which restore airway patency but fragment sleep architecture, leading to daytime somnolence and systemic sequelae. Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Obstructive sleep apnoea"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Symptoms typically develop insidiously over months to years, often presenting with snoring and daytime fatigue, which may initially be dismissed as normal variations in sleep.
  • Progressive phase: As the condition worsens, episodes of apnoea become more frequent and prolonged, leading to significant sleep fragmentation, marked daytime somnolence, and an increasing impact on quality of life, particularly beyond several years of untreated disease.
  • Late phase: Chronic, untreated OSA can lead to long-term complications such as hypertension, cardiovascular disease, and metabolic dysfunction, manifesting after many years of persistent sleep disturbance.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Obstructive sleep apnoea"][/sc]
  • Ask about loud, habitual snoring - often reported by a bed partner, indicating turbulent airflow through a narrowed airway.
  • Ask about witnessed apnoeas - a bed partner observing pauses in breathing during sleep, directly indicating airway obstruction.
  • Ask about excessive daytime sleepiness - a cardinal symptom, reflecting fragmented sleep and poor sleep quality, impacting daily function and increasing accident risk.
  • Ask about morning headaches - due to nocturnal hypercapnia and cerebral vasodilation.
  • Ask about unrefreshing sleep - despite adequate time in bed, the individual does not feel rested, suggesting poor sleep architecture.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Obstructive sleep apnoea"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - may reveal hypertension, particularly systemic and pulmonary, reflecting chronic sympathetic activation.
  • Check body mass index (BMI) - obesity (BMI >30 kg/m²) is a significant risk factor and often a key physical finding.
  • Check for signs of upper airway crowding - such as an enlarged tongue, tonsillar hypertrophy, or a high arched palate, which predispose to airway collapse.
  • Check neck circumference - a large neck circumference (>17 inches in men, >16 inches in women) correlates with increased risk of OSA.
  • Check for signs of retrognathia or micrognathia - anatomical features that can reduce posterior airway space.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Obstructive sleep apnoea"][/sc]
  • Severe daytime somnolence: Impairing daily activities or leading to dangerous situations (e.g., falling asleep while driving), indicating significant sleep deprivation and requiring urgent assessment.
  • Witnessed prolonged apnoeas: Episodes of breathing cessation lasting >10 seconds, especially if associated with cyanosis, suggesting severe airway obstruction and potential for significant hypoxaemia.
  • Symptoms of right heart failure: Peripheral oedema, raised JVP, or hepatomegaly, which may indicate chronic pulmonary hypertension secondary to severe, long-standing OSA.
  • Driving regulations (DVLA) implications: Patients with excessive sleepiness that affects driving must inform the DVLA, as it poses a significant public safety risk and requires prompt management.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Obstructive sleep apnoea"][/sc]
  • Women: May present with less prominent snoring and more subtle symptoms like fatigue, insomnia, or depression, leading to delayed diagnosis.
  • Older adults: Symptoms may be attributed to normal ageing, and daytime sleepiness can be less pronounced, while cognitive impairment may be more prominent.
  • Children: Often presents with behavioural problems, hyperactivity, poor school performance, or enuresis, rather than classic daytime sleepiness.
  • Non-obese individuals: OSA can occur in individuals with normal BMI due to craniofacial abnormalities or other anatomical factors, challenging the typical association with obesity.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Obstructive sleep apnoea"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected obstructive sleep apnoea: Sleep study (polysomnography or home sleep apnoea test) to measure AHI, oxygen desaturations, and sleep architecture.
  • Severity/risk tools: STOP-BANG questionnaire is a validated screening tool for OSA risk. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="STOP-BANG questionnaire: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="STOP-BANG questionnaire"][/sc] STOP-BANG Score for Obstructive Sleep Apnea on MDCalc
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Thyroid function tests to exclude hypothyroidism, which can contribute to fatigue and weight gain.
  • Imaging - Nasopharyngoscopy: May be performed by ENT specialists to identify anatomical sites of obstruction in the upper airway.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Obstructive sleep apnoea"][/sc]
  • A 55-year-old obese male presents with a 2-year history of loud snoring, witnessed apnoeas by his wife, and excessive daytime sleepiness, impacting his work and increasing his risk of road traffic accidents.
  • A middle-aged, obese male with chronic nocturnal airway obstruction and fragmented sleep, concerning for obstructive sleep apnoea with significant functional impairment.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Obstructive sleep apnoea"][/sc]
  • Central Sleep Apnoea: Lack of respiratory effort due to central nervous system dysfunction, rather than airway obstruction.
  • Narcolepsy: Characterised by irresistible urges to sleep, often with cataplexy, but without nocturnal airway obstruction.
  • Chronic Fatigue Syndrome: Persistent, unexplained fatigue not relieved by rest, without the specific nocturnal breathing disturbances of OSA.
  • Hypothyroidism: Can cause fatigue, weight gain, and somnolence, but a sleep study would differentiate it from OSA.
  • Insomnia: Difficulty initiating or maintaining sleep, but typically without witnessed apnoeas or loud snoring.
  • Depression: Can cause fatigue, hypersomnia, and poor concentration, but a sleep study would distinguish it from OSA.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Obstructive sleep apnoea"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Recognition of severe daytime sleepiness and its implications for driving (DVLA regulations) and occupational safety. Referral to a sleep specialist for diagnostic sleep study.
  • Definitive management: Continuous Positive Airway Pressure (CPAP) is often the first-line treatment, providing a pneumatic splint to keep the airway open during sleep. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="CPAP mechanism: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="CPAP mechanism"][/sc]
  • Adjunctive management: Lifestyle modifications including weight loss, avoidance of alcohol and sedatives before sleep, and positional therapy (avoiding supine sleep). Oral appliances may be considered for mild-moderate OSA.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Regular review to assess CPAP adherence, symptom control, and screen for long-term cardiovascular and metabolic complications.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Surgical options (e.g., uvulopalatopharyngoplasty, maxillomandibular advancement) may be considered in selected cases where CPAP is not tolerated or effective.
  • Exam pharmacology reference (UKMLA/MRCGP): No specific drug treatments are typically used for the underlying cause of OSA. Modafinil or solriamfetol may be considered for residual daytime sleepiness in some patients already on CPAP. Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Obstructive sleep apnoea"][/sc]
  • Cardiovascular disease: Chronic intermittent hypoxia and sympathetic activation contribute to hypertension, coronary artery disease, arrhythmias (e.g., atrial fibrillation), and heart failure, manifesting over many years of untreated disease.
  • Metabolic dysfunction: Increased risk of insulin resistance, type 2 diabetes, and non-alcoholic fatty liver disease, linked to chronic sleep disruption and inflammation.
  • Neurocognitive impairment: Chronic sleep fragmentation leads to impaired concentration, memory deficits, and reduced executive function, which can be progressive over time.
  • Increased accident risk: Excessive daytime sleepiness significantly increases the risk of road traffic accidents and occupational injuries, particularly in the progressive phase of the condition.
  • Reduced quality of life: Impact on mood, relationships, and overall well-being due to chronic fatigue and sleep disturbance.
