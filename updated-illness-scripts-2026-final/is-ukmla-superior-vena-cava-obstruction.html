Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Superior vena cava obstruction"][/sc]
  • Superior vena cava obstruction (SVCO) is a clinical syndrome resulting from partial or complete blockage of blood flow through the superior vena cava (SVC).
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical features of SVCO, identify common underlying causes, and understand the principles of initial management.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Superior vena cava obstruction"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Superior vena cava obstruction"][/sc]
  • Malignancy is the most common cause of SVCO, accounting for approximately 85% of cases, with lung cancer (small-cell and non-small cell) being the predominant aetiology [patient.info].
  • SVCO affects up to 10% of small-cell lung cancer patients and 2-4% of all lung cancer patients [patient.info].
  • Benign causes, such as thrombosis associated with intravascular devices (e.g., central venous catheters, pacemakers), are increasing in incidence [patient.info].
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Superior vena cava obstruction"][/sc]
  • Obstruction of the thin-walled, low-pressure superior vena cava (SVC) typically occurs due to external compression by mediastinal masses (e.g., tumours, enlarged lymph nodes) or intrinsic thrombosis within the vessel lumen [patient.info].
  • Reduced venous return from the head, neck, upper extremities, and upper thorax leads to increased hydrostatic pressure in these regions, causing oedema and [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Collateral circulation in SVC obstruction: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="collateral vessel formation"][/sc].
  • Severe or rapid obstruction can compromise cerebral or laryngeal venous drainage, leading to life-threatening cerebral or laryngeal oedema.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Superior vena cava obstruction"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Symptoms may develop gradually over days to weeks, often starting with facial or neck swelling, particularly noticeable in the mornings.
  • Progressive phase: As obstruction worsens, symptoms like dyspnoea, cough, and arm swelling become more pronounced, and collateral venous circulation may become visible.
  • Late phase: In severe, rapidly progressing cases, life-threatening complications such as cerebral oedema or laryngeal oedema can develop, particularly beyond 24-48 hours of significant obstruction.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Superior vena cava obstruction"][/sc]
  • Ask about facial swelling - often the earliest symptom, worse in the morning or when bending over, indicating impaired venous drainage from the head.
  • Ask about dyspnoea - a common and often distressing symptom, resulting from venous congestion in the upper airways and lungs.
  • Ask about cough - may be persistent and non-productive, related to tracheal compression or venous congestion.
  • Ask about arm swelling - typically unilateral or bilateral, indicating impaired venous return from the upper limbs.
  • Ask about headache or dizziness - can suggest increased intracranial pressure due to impaired cerebral venous drainage.
  • Ask about chest pain - may occur due to underlying malignancy or mediastinal involvement.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Superior vena cava obstruction"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - may reveal tachypnoea or tachycardia, particularly with significant respiratory compromise.
  • Check facial and neck oedema - often accompanied by plethora (reddish-blue discoloration) and dilated superficial veins.
  • Check arm oedema - typically pitting, affecting one or both upper limbs.
  • Check dilated collateral veins - visible over the chest wall, neck, and shoulders, indicating alternative venous drainage pathways.
  • Check [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Pemberton sign: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Pemberton sign"][/sc] - facial plethora, cyanosis, and distension of neck veins when arms are raised, indicating SVC obstruction.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Superior vena cava obstruction"][/sc]
  • Stridor or hoarseness - suggests laryngeal oedema or vocal cord paralysis due to recurrent laryngeal nerve compression, indicating impending airway compromise.
  • Altered mental status or seizures - indicative of cerebral oedema and increased intracranial pressure, requiring urgent intervention.
  • Rapidly worsening dyspnoea - suggests acute progression of airway compromise or significant pulmonary venous congestion.
  • Syncope or severe dizziness - may indicate significant reduction in cerebral perfusion.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Superior vena cava obstruction"][/sc]
  • Children: SVCO is rare and often associated with T-cell acute lymphoblastic leukaemia or non-Hodgkin\'s lymphoma, presenting with similar but sometimes more rapid onset of symptoms [patient.info].
  • Benign causes: Patients with [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Intravascular devices and SVC obstruction: what is the link and why does it matter clinically?" text="intravascular devices"][/sc] may develop SVCO due to thrombosis, often with a more insidious onset and potentially less dramatic symptoms initially.
  • Partial obstruction: Mild or intermittent symptoms, such as morning facial puffiness that resolves during the day, may indicate partial obstruction and can be easily overlooked.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Superior vena cava obstruction"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected SVCO:
    • Chest X-ray (CXR): May show mediastinal widening, pleural effusion, or a mass, but is often non-specific [patient.info].
    • [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Contrast-enhanced CT in SVC obstruction: what does it show and why is it the imaging modality of choice?" text="Contrast-enhanced CT scan of the chest"][/sc]: The imaging modality of choice, providing detailed anatomical information about the obstruction, its cause, and collateral circulation [patient.info].
  • Severity/risk tools: No widely validated severity or risk score is typically used for SVCO in routine clinical practice.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible:
    • MRI chest: An alternative to CT if iodine contrast is contraindicated [patient.info].
    • Doppler ultrasound: Can assess SVC flow and collateral circulation, particularly useful for thrombosis [patient.info].
  • Tissue diagnosis: Essential to determine the underlying cause, typically obtained via bronchoscopy, CT-guided biopsy, or mediastinoscopy, once the patient is stable [patient.info].

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Superior vena cava obstruction"][/sc]
  • Congestive heart failure - typically presents with bilateral lower limb oedema and orthopnoea, rather than predominant upper body swelling.
  • Thyroid eye disease - can cause periorbital oedema and proptosis, but usually without widespread facial or arm swelling or [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Plethora: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="plethora"][/sc].
  • Angioedema - acute onset, often transient, and may involve other areas like lips and tongue, without evidence of venous congestion.
  • Cellulitis of the face or arm - usually unilateral, with localised erythema, warmth, and tenderness, often with a clear portal of entry.
  • Deep vein thrombosis (DVT) of the upper extremity - typically unilateral arm swelling, but without facial or neck oedema unless very proximal.
  • Mediastinal tumour without SVC compression - may cause cough or dyspnoea, but without the classic signs of venous obstruction.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Superior vena cava obstruction"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Recognition of life-threatening features (e.g., stridor, altered mental status) and prompt escalation to senior medical staff. Supportive care often includes head elevation and oxygen therapy.
  • Definitive management: Treatment is guided by the underlying cause. For malignant SVCO, options often include endovascular stenting for rapid symptom relief, radiotherapy, or chemotherapy for chemosensitive tumours (e.g., small-cell lung cancer) [NICE IPG79, patient.info]. For benign causes, stenting or surgical bypass may be considered [patient.info].
  • Adjunctive management: Corticosteroids and diuretics may be considered in emergency situations (e.g., cerebral or laryngeal oedema), although evidence for their efficacy is limited [patient.info]. Anticoagulation may be indicated if central vein thrombosis is present [patient.info].
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Close monitoring for symptom resolution and potential complications is essential.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Patients should be advised on symptoms requiring immediate medical attention, particularly worsening dyspnoea, stridor, or neurological changes.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Superior vena cava obstruction"][/sc]
  • Cerebral oedema: Impaired venous drainage from the brain can lead to increased intracranial pressure, manifesting as headache, altered mental status, seizures, or coma, particularly beyond 24-48 hours of significant obstruction.
  • Laryngeal oedema: Swelling of the larynx can cause stridor and acute airway obstruction, a life-threatening emergency.
  • Respiratory failure: Severe venous congestion in the lungs and airway compression can lead to significant dyspnoea and respiratory compromise.
  • Thromboembolism: Stasis of blood in the SVC and collateral veins can predispose to thrombosis and subsequent pulmonary embolism.
  • Superior vena cava syndrome recurrence: Re-obstruction can occur after initial treatment, requiring further intervention.
