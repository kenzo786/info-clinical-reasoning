Cutaneous fungal infection
Definition
  • A common dermatological condition caused by pathogenic fungi, primarily dermatophytes, yeasts, or moulds, affecting the skin, hair, or nails.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical features, common presentations, and initial management of cutaneous fungal infections.
Epidemiology
  • Cutaneous fungal infections are highly prevalent globally, with dermatophyte infections (tinea) affecting up to 20-25% of the population at any given time, particularly in warmer climates.
  • Risk factors include communal living, occlusive footwear, hyperhidrosis, diabetes mellitus, immunosuppression, and peripheral vascular disease.
  • NICE CKS reports tinea pedis as the most common dermatophyte infection, affecting 15% of the UK population annually.
Pathophysiology
  • Trigger: Fungal spores or hyphae gain entry to the stratum corneum, often facilitated by minor skin trauma, moisture, or occlusive environments.
  • Progression: Fungi proliferate, utilising keratin as a nutrient source, leading to an inflammatory response in the epidermis and dermis.
  • Consequence: Clinical manifestations such as erythema, scaling, pruritus, and vesicle formation result from fungal metabolic byproducts and host immune reactions.
Time Course
  • Early phase: Initial symptoms, such as mild pruritus and subtle erythema, may develop insidiously over days to weeks following exposure.
  • Progressive phase: Without intervention, lesions typically expand centrifugally, with increasing inflammation, scaling, and potential for secondary bacterial infection, particularly beyond 2-4 weeks.
  • Late phase: Chronic infections, especially of the nails (onychomycosis) or scalp (tinea capitis), can persist for months to years, leading to significant cosmetic and functional impairment.
Symptoms
  • Ask about pruritus - often intense, especially in warm, moist areas, suggesting an inflammatory response to fungal antigens.
  • Ask about scaling or flaking skin - indicative of increased epidermal turnover and desquamation due to fungal activity.
  • Ask about erythema - a common sign of inflammation, which may be annular or irregular depending on the fungal type and location.
  • Ask about blistering or pustules - can occur in acute inflammatory reactions, particularly in tinea pedis, and may indicate secondary bacterial infection.
  • Ask about changes in nail appearance - discolouration, thickening, or crumbling suggests onychomycosis.
Signs
  • Check for well-demarcated erythematous plaques with raised, scaly borders - characteristic of dermatophyte infections (e.g., tinea corporis).
  • Check for interdigital maceration and fissuring - commonly seen in tinea pedis, indicating chronic moisture and fungal growth.
  • Check for white or yellow discolouration, subungual hyperkeratosis, and onycholysis - typical signs of onychomycosis.
  • Check for satellite lesions - small papules or pustules surrounding a larger erythematous patch, often seen in candidiasis.
  • Check vital signs - typically normal, but fever may suggest extensive secondary bacterial infection or systemic involvement in immunocompromised patients.
Red Flag Features
  • Extensive or rapidly spreading lesions - may indicate severe infection, immunosuppression, or an alternative diagnosis requiring urgent evaluation.
  • Systemic symptoms (fever, malaise) - suggests potential for systemic fungal infection, particularly in immunocompromised individuals, or secondary bacterial sepsis.
  • Failure to respond to appropriate topical therapy after 4-6 weeks - warrants re-evaluation for incorrect diagnosis, resistant organism, or underlying predisposing factors.
  • Deep or painful nodules/abscesses - could indicate a more invasive fungal infection (e.g., sporotrichosis) or a bacterial cellulitis.
Atypical Presentations
  • Immunocompromised individuals: May present with widespread, atypical, or severe infections, often lacking classic inflammatory signs due to impaired immune response.
  • Infants and young children: Tinea capitis is more common, presenting as scaling patches with hair loss, which can be mistaken for seborrhoeic dermatitis.
  • Older adults: Often have chronic, less inflammatory presentations, particularly onychomycosis, which can be exacerbated by comorbidities like diabetes and peripheral vascular disease.
  • Tinea incognito: Fungal infections modified by topical corticosteroids, leading to reduced inflammation, altered morphology, and delayed diagnosis.
Diagnostic Tests
  • Core for suspected cutaneous fungal infection: Skin scrapings, nail clippings, or hair samples for microscopy (potassium hydroxide preparation) and fungal culture.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Bacterial swabs for culture and sensitivity if secondary bacterial infection is suspected.
  • Imaging - Wood's lamp examination: Useful for identifying certain fungal infections (e.g., tinea capitis caused by Microsporum species) that fluoresce.
  • Serial reassessment: Clinical review after initial treatment to assess response and consider further investigations if symptoms persist.
Problem Representation
  • A 35-year-old male presents with a 3-week history of an intensely itchy, expanding red rash with a raised, scaly border on his left groin, exacerbated by sweating.
  • A pruritic, erythematous, annular, and centrifugally spreading dermatosis in an otherwise healthy adult, suggestive of a superficial fungal infection.
Differential Diagnosis
  • Eczema (e.g., contact dermatitis) - often poorly demarcated, with less prominent central clearing and different distribution.
  • Psoriasis - typically silvery scales on erythematous plaques, often on extensor surfaces, with Auspitz sign.
  • Bacterial infection (e.g., impetigo, cellulitis) - rapid onset, pain, purulent discharge, systemic signs.
  • Granuloma annulare - annular lesions without scaling, often on hands and feet.
  • Seborrhoeic dermatitis - greasy scales on erythematous patches, typically on scalp, face, and chest.
  • Pityriasis rosea - herald patch followed by smaller oval lesions in a 'Christmas tree' distribution.
Treatment
  • Immediate priorities: Confirm diagnosis with microscopy/culture if possible, advise on hygiene measures (e.g., keeping affected areas dry, loose clothing).
  • Definitive management: Topical antifungals (e.g., clotrimazole, terbinafine) for localised infections; oral antifungals (e.g., terbinafine, itraconazole) for extensive, chronic, or nail/hair involvement.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Review clinical response after 2-4 weeks of topical therapy or 4-6 weeks of oral therapy; continue treatment for recommended duration to prevent recurrence.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Refer to dermatology if diagnosis is uncertain, infection is severe/widespread, or fails to respond to initial appropriate treatment.

Complications
  • Secondary bacterial infection: Common due to skin barrier disruption and pruritus leading to excoriation, complicating management and increasing morbidity.
  • Post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation/hypopigmentation: Can occur after resolution of inflammation, particularly in individuals with darker skin types, affecting cosmetic appearance.
  • Recurrence: High risk, especially in predisposing conditions or inadequate treatment, leading to chronic discomfort and potential for spread.
  • Spread to other body sites: Autoinoculation can lead to new lesions (e.g., tinea manuum from tinea pedis), requiring broader treatment.
  • Onychomycosis: Chronic fungal infection of the nails, which is difficult to treat and can cause significant nail dystrophy and pain.
