Abnormal blood film [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Abnormal blood film"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Abnormal blood film"][/sc]
  • An abnormal blood film refers to microscopic examination of a peripheral blood smear revealing morphological changes in red blood cells, white blood cells, or platelets, or the presence of abnormal cells, which can indicate various underlying haematological or systemic conditions.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise and interpret abnormal findings on a peripheral blood film in the context of clinical presentation to guide further investigation and management.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Abnormal blood film"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Abnormal blood film"][/sc]
  • The prevalence of abnormal blood films directly reflects the incidence and prevalence of underlying haematological and systemic conditions in the UK population.
  • Risk factors for an abnormal blood film are diverse and include genetic predispositions (e.g., thalassaemia, G6PD deficiency), exposure to certain medications (e.g., methotrexate, some anticonvulsants), chronic diseases (e.g., renal failure, liver disease, autoimmune conditions), infections, and malignancies (e.g., leukaemia, myelodysplastic syndromes).
  • Abnormal blood films are frequently requested following abnormal full blood count (FBC) results, which are common in primary and secondary care settings across the UK.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Abnormal blood film"][/sc]
  • Trigger: Underlying disease processes (e.g., genetic mutations, infections, autoimmune conditions, nutritional deficiencies, toxic exposures, or malignancies) initiate cellular dysfunction or damage within the haematopoietic system or peripheral circulation.
  • Progression: These initial triggers lead to altered production, maturation, survival, or destruction of blood cells, resulting in changes to their size, shape, colour, or internal structure. For example, defective haemoglobin synthesis can lead to microcytic, hypochromic red cells. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Poikilocytosis: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Poikilocytosis"][/sc]
  • Consequence: The morphological abnormalities observed on the blood film reflect these pathological changes, providing diagnostic clues to the specific underlying condition and its impact on haematological function, potentially leading to anaemia, coagulopathy, or immunodeficiency.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Abnormal blood film"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Initial subtle changes in blood cell morphology or counts may be present for days to weeks, often before overt clinical symptoms develop.
  • Progressive phase: As the underlying condition advances, more distinct and diverse abnormalities become evident on the blood film over weeks to months, correlating with the progression of clinical signs and symptoms.
  • Late phase: Established disease states can manifest with significant and often severe haematological changes on the blood film, persisting for months to years, and may be associated with chronic complications.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Abnormal blood film"][/sc]
  • Ask about fatigue and weakness - these are common non-specific symptoms of anaemia, which can be caused by various red blood cell abnormalities seen on a blood film.
  • Ask about shortness of breath or dyspnoea - this can indicate significant anaemia, where the reduced oxygen-carrying capacity of the blood leads to respiratory compensation.
  • Ask about unusual paleness (pallor) - a key clinical sign of anaemia, reflecting reduced haemoglobin levels or red blood cell count.
  • Ask about easy bruising or unexplained bleeding (e.g., epistaxis, menorrhagia) - these suggest platelet disorders (thrombocytopenia or thrombopathy) or coagulation abnormalities, which may be identified or further investigated via blood film.
  • Ask about recurrent or prolonged infections - this can point towards abnormalities in white blood cell number or function, such as neutropenia or leukaemia.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Abnormal blood film"][/sc]
  • Check for pallor (conjunctival, palmar crease) - this indicates anaemia, suggesting reduced haemoglobin or red blood cell count.
  • Check for petechiae, purpura, or ecchymoses - these signs of bleeding suggest thrombocytopenia or platelet dysfunction. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Petechiae: what are they and what do they indicate?" text="Petechiae"][/sc]
  • Check for lymphadenopathy, splenomegaly, or hepatomegaly - these may indicate underlying haematological malignancies or systemic inflammatory conditions.
  • Check for jaundice - this can be a sign of haemolysis, where red blood cells are being prematurely destroyed.
  • Check vital signs (heart rate, respiratory rate, blood pressure) - tachycardia and tachypnoea may compensate for reduced oxygen delivery in anaemia, while hypotension can indicate significant blood loss.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Abnormal blood film"][/sc]
  • Circulating blast cells: Indicates acute leukaemia, requiring immediate haematological assessment and intervention.
  • Features of microangiopathic haemolytic anaemia (MAHA): Suggests conditions like thrombotic thrombocytopenic purpura (TTP) or haemolytic uraemic syndrome (HUS), necessitating urgent management due to high mortality if untreated.
  • Profound cytopenias (e.g., severe anaemia, thrombocytopenia, neutropenia) with atypical morphology: May indicate bone marrow failure, myelodysplastic syndromes, or acute leukaemia, requiring urgent investigation.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Abnormal blood film"][/sc]
  • Older adults: Atypical presentations are common due to physiological changes of ageing, polypharmacy, and co-morbidities, often masking or altering classic haematological symptoms. For example, chronic lymphocytic leukaemia (CLL) is more prevalent in this demographic.
  • Pregnancy: Physiological changes in pregnancy can lead to variations in blood parameters. Immature white cell forms (myelocytes, metamyelocytes) may be present in healthy pregnant women and are not necessarily pathological. Mild thrombocytopenia is also common.
  • Immunosuppressed patients: These individuals are prone to various infections and malignancies that can significantly alter blood cell morphology and counts. Atypical cells, such as virally infected cells (e.g., CMV-infected endothelial cells), may be observed, necessitating careful interpretation.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Abnormal blood film"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected Abnormal blood film: Peripheral blood film (PBS) examination and Full Blood Count (FBC) are the primary diagnostic tools to identify and characterise cellular abnormalities.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Bone marrow examination (biopsy and aspirate) may be required to investigate underlying haematological disorders like leukaemia, myelodysplastic syndromes, or aplastic anaemia.
  • Imaging - various modalities: Imaging (e.g., ultrasound, CT, PET) may be used to assess for lymphadenopathy, splenomegaly, hepatomegaly, or other organ involvement in systemic conditions or haematological malignancies.
  • Serial reassessment: Repeat blood films and FBCs are crucial for monitoring disease progression, response to treatment, and detecting new abnormalities over time.
  • Validated scores: Consider using the Mentzer Index on MDCalc to differentiate between iron deficiency anaemia and beta thalassaemia trait, or the 4Ts Score for Heparin-Induced Thrombocytopenia on MDCalc for suspected HIT.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Abnormal blood film"][/sc]
  • A 72-year-old female presents to her GP with a 3-month history of increasing fatigue, exertional dyspnoea, and noticeable pallor. Her full blood count reveals a microcytic, hypochromic anaemia with a haemoglobin of 8.5 g/dL.
  • A chronic, progressive anaemia characterised by microcytic and hypochromic red cell morphology, indicating impaired haemoglobin synthesis.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Abnormal blood film"][/sc]
  • Iron deficiency anaemia: Microcytic, hypochromic red cells with reduced ferritin and increased total iron-binding capacity.
  • Thalassaemia: Microcytic, hypochromic red cells, often with normal iron studies; confirmed by haemoglobin electrophoresis.
  • Anaemia of chronic disease: Typically normocytic, normochromic anaemia, but can be microcytic, with elevated inflammatory markers and normal or elevated ferritin.
  • Megaloblastic anaemia (B12/folate deficiency): Macrocytic red cells with hypersegmented neutrophils; confirmed by B12 and folate levels.
  • Acute Leukaemia: Presence of circulating blast cells, often with pancytopenia or markedly elevated white cell count; requires bone marrow biopsy for diagnosis.
  • Thrombocytopenia (e.g., ITP, TTP): Isolated low platelet count (ITP) or low platelets with microangiopathic haemolytic anaemia (TTP); clinical context and specific tests differentiate.
  • Myelodysplastic Syndromes (MDS): Dysplastic changes in one or more cell lines, often with refractory cytopenias; diagnosed by bone marrow examination.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Abnormal blood film"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Address any life-threatening complications arising from the abnormal blood film findings, such as severe anaemia requiring transfusion, active bleeding due to thrombocytopenia, or signs of acute leukaemia requiring urgent haematological referral.
  • Definitive management: Treatment is directed at the underlying cause identified by the abnormal blood film. This may involve iron supplementation for iron deficiency anaemia, specific therapies for autoimmune conditions, chemotherapy for malignancies, or cessation of causative medications.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Regular full blood counts and repeat blood films are essential to monitor the response to treatment, assess disease progression, and detect new or evolving abnormalities. Clinical follow-up should be tailored to the specific underlying condition.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Patients with rapidly worsening cytopenias, new onset of red flag features, or failure to respond to initial management require urgent re-evaluation and escalation to specialist haematological services. Patients should be counselled on symptoms requiring immediate medical attention.
  • Exam pharmacology reference (UKMLA/MRCGP): Drug knowledge is commonly tested in the context of specific haematological conditions (e.g., iron supplements for iron deficiency, immunosuppressants for autoimmune haemolysis, chemotherapy agents for leukaemia). No doses or frequencies are cited here — always verify against your local antimicrobial/prescribing formulary before prescribing.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Abnormal blood film"][/sc]
  • Severe anaemia: Prolonged or severe anaemia (resulting from various red cell abnormalities) can lead to high-output cardiac failure, myocardial ischaemia, and multi-organ dysfunction due to inadequate oxygen delivery, significantly impacting prognosis.
  • Life-threatening haemorrhage: Profound thrombocytopenia or severe coagulation factor deficiencies (which may be indicated by platelet abnormalities on film) can result in spontaneous and life-threatening bleeding, particularly intracranial haemorrhage, requiring urgent intervention.
  • Overwhelming infection: Severe neutropenia or neutrophil dysfunction (evident on the blood film) significantly compromises the immune system, leading to recurrent and severe bacterial or fungal infections, which are a major cause of morbidity and mortality in conditions like leukaemia.
  • Thrombotic events: Paradoxically, some conditions causing abnormal blood films (e.g., microangiopathic haemolytic anaemia like TTP, or myeloproliferative neoplasms) can lead to thrombotic complications due to endothelial damage or abnormal platelet activation, with significant prognostic implications.
  • Organ infiltration and failure: In haematological malignancies (e.g., leukaemia, lymphoma), abnormal cells can infiltrate organs such as the liver, spleen, kidneys, or central nervous system, leading to organomegaly and dysfunction, which is a key prognostic factor.
