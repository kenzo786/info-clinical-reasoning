Status epilepticus (Brain and spinal cord) [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Status epilepticus"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Status epilepticus"][/sc]
  • Status epilepticus is defined as a seizure lasting 5 minutes or longer, or recurrent seizures without recovery of consciousness between episodes.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical features of status epilepticus, initiate immediate management, and understand the principles of escalation of care.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Status epilepticus"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Status epilepticus"][/sc]
  • The incidence of status epilepticus in the UK is estimated to be between 9.9 and 41 per 100,000 people per year, with a bimodal distribution peaking in children and older adults [3, 5].
  • Risk factors include a history of epilepsy, cerebrovascular disease, central nervous system infections, metabolic disturbances, and non-adherence to antiseizure medication [1].
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Status epilepticus"][/sc]
  • Trigger: An imbalance between excitatory and inhibitory neurotransmission, often due to an acute brain insult (e.g., stroke, infection, trauma) or withdrawal from antiseizure medications, leads to sustained neuronal depolarisation.
  • Progression: Failure of normal seizure-terminating mechanisms results in continuous or rapidly recurring seizures, leading to increased metabolic demand and potential neuronal injury [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Excitotoxicity in status epilepticus: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Excitotoxicity"][/sc].
  • Consequence: Prolonged seizure activity can cause irreversible neuronal damage, systemic complications (e.g., hypoxia, acidosis), and increased morbidity and mortality [2].
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Status epilepticus"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Seizure activity typically lasts for 5 minutes or more, or there are multiple seizures without full recovery of consciousness. This phase requires immediate intervention with first-line antiseizure medications.
  • Progressive phase: If seizures continue beyond 5-10 minutes despite initial treatment, the condition progresses, often requiring second-line agents. Neurological damage can begin to occur, particularly beyond 30 minutes.
  • Late phase: Refractory status epilepticus, defined as seizures persisting after two adequate doses of antiseizure medications, often necessitates advanced therapies and intensive care management, with increasing risk of long-term neurological sequelae and mortality, particularly beyond 60 minutes.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Status epilepticus"][/sc]
  • Ask about seizure duration - crucial for diagnosis and guiding immediate management, as seizures lasting >5 minutes define status epilepticus.
  • Ask about recovery of consciousness between seizures - absence of recovery indicates ongoing status epilepticus, necessitating urgent intervention.
  • Ask about previous epilepsy diagnosis or seizure history - informs the likelihood of recurrence and guides the emergency management plan.
  • Ask about recent medication changes or non-adherence - a common precipitant for status epilepticus in patients with known epilepsy.
  • Ask about headache or focal weakness post-seizure (Todd's paresis) - may indicate a focal onset seizure or underlying structural lesion [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Todd's paresis: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Todd's paresis"][/sc].
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Status epilepticus"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - tachycardia, hypertension, hyperthermia, and hypoxia are common systemic responses to prolonged seizure activity and require immediate correction.
  • Check for continuous generalised tonic-clonic movements - overt convulsive activity is the most recognisable sign, but can evolve into more subtle forms.
  • Check for subtle motor signs - twitching of eyelids, nystagmus, or minor limb movements may indicate non-convulsive status epilepticus, especially in sedated patients.
  • Check pupillary responses and eye movements - may reveal focal neurological deficits or indicate depth of encephalopathy.
  • Check for signs of injury - tongue biting, incontinence, or trauma from falls can occur during convulsive seizures.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Status epilepticus"][/sc]
  • Seizure duration >5 minutes: Indicates established status epilepticus requiring urgent pharmacological intervention.
  • Recurrent seizures without recovery of consciousness: Signifies ongoing status epilepticus, necessitating prompt treatment escalation.
  • Non-convulsive status epilepticus: Subtle or absent motor manifestations can delay diagnosis, leading to prolonged brain injury if not recognised and treated.
  • New onset status epilepticus: Suggests an acute underlying cause (e.g., infection, stroke, metabolic derangement) requiring thorough investigation.
  • Refractory status epilepticus: Failure to respond to first- and second-line antiseizure medications, indicating a need for advanced therapies and intensive care management.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Status epilepticus"][/sc]
  • Older adults: May present with subtle or non-convulsive status epilepticus, often due to cerebrovascular disease or metabolic derangements, leading to delayed diagnosis and treatment.
  • Children: Febrile seizures are a common cause of status epilepticus in children; prolonged febrile seizures can lead to hippocampal sclerosis.
  • Non-convulsive status epilepticus (NCSE): Characterised by altered mental status, confusion, or subtle motor signs without overt convulsions, making diagnosis challenging and requiring EEG confirmation [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Non-convulsive status epilepticus: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Non-convulsive status epilepticus"][/sc].
  • Super-refractory status epilepticus (SRSE): Status epilepticus that continues or recurs 24 hours or more after the initiation of anaesthetic treatment, including cases where status epilepticus recurs on weaning or reduction of anaesthesia [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Super-refractory status epilepticus: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Super-refractory status epilepticus"][/sc].
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Status epilepticus"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected status epilepticus:
    • Blood glucose: To identify and correct hypoglycaemia as a treatable cause.
    • Full blood count, electrolytes, renal and liver function tests: To assess for metabolic derangements and systemic complications.
    • Arterial blood gas: To assess for acidosis and hypoxia.
    • Electrocardiogram (ECG): To rule out cardiac causes of collapse or arrhythmias.
  • Severity/risk tools:
    • Status Epilepticus Severity Score (STESS) on MDCalc: A prognostic tool to estimate mortality risk in status epilepticus.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible:
    • Toxicology screen: To detect illicit drug use or poisoning.
    • Lumbar puncture: If meningitis or encephalitis is suspected.
  • Imaging - CT head: Urgent CT head is often performed to identify acute structural lesions (e.g., haemorrhage, stroke, tumour) that may be precipitating status epilepticus.
  • Imaging - MRI brain: May be considered for more detailed evaluation of underlying structural abnormalities or inflammatory conditions, particularly if CT is normal or non-diagnostic.
  • Serial reassessment: Continuous EEG monitoring is crucial for diagnosing non-convulsive status epilepticus and monitoring treatment response, especially in critically ill patients [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Continuous EEG monitoring in status epilepticus: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Continuous EEG monitoring"][/sc].
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Status epilepticus"][/sc]
  • A 65-year-old male with a history of epilepsy presents to the emergency department with continuous generalised tonic-clonic seizure activity lasting for 10 minutes, unresponsive to initial buccal midazolam.
  • An elderly male with known epilepsy experiencing prolonged, unremitting convulsive activity, concerning for established status epilepticus requiring urgent escalation of antiseizure therapy.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Status epilepticus"][/sc]
  • Psychogenic non-epileptic seizures (PNES) - typically lack typical epileptic EEG changes and may respond to suggestion rather than antiseizure medication.
  • Syncope - usually brief loss of consciousness with rapid recovery, often triggered by specific events, and not associated with prolonged tonic-clonic movements.
  • Transient ischaemic attack (TIA) - focal neurological deficits are typically transient and not associated with convulsive movements, though some forms of TIA can mimic seizures.
  • Migraine with aura - neurological symptoms are usually sensory or visual, gradually evolving, and not associated with loss of consciousness or convulsive activity.
  • Metabolic encephalopathy - altered mental status and myoclonus may occur, but organised convulsive activity is less common and often resolves with correction of the underlying metabolic disturbance [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Metabolic encephalopathy vs status epilepticus: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Metabolic encephalopathy"][/sc].
  • Movement disorders (e.g., tremor, dystonia) - movements are typically patterned, suppressible, and occur with preserved consciousness, differentiating them from epileptic seizures.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Status epilepticus"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Recognition of status epilepticus, ensuring airway patency, adequate oxygenation, establishing intravenous access, and monitoring vital signs. Escalation to senior medical staff and critical care teams is often warranted.
  • Definitive management:
    • First-line therapy (0-5 minutes): Benzodiazepines (e.g., buccal midazolam, rectal diazepam in the community; intravenous lorazepam if IV access available) are typically administered.
    • Second-line therapy (5-30 minutes): If benzodiazepines are ineffective, intravenous levetiracetam, phenytoin, or sodium valproate are considered. Levetiracetam may be preferred due to quicker administration and fewer adverse effects [1].
    • Third-line therapy (>30 minutes): If second-line agents fail, options include phenobarbital or general anaesthesia (e.g., propofol, thiopental) under expert guidance [1].
  • Adjunctive management: Identification and treatment of underlying causes (e.g., hypoglycaemia, infection, electrolyte imbalances) are crucial.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Continuous clinical and EEG monitoring is often required, especially in refractory cases, to assess treatment response and detect non-convulsive status epilepticus.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Patients with ongoing status epilepticus or those at high risk of recurrence should have an individualised emergency management plan developed.
  • Exam pharmacology reference (UKMLA/MRCGP): Knowledge of the sequential use of antiseizure medications in status epilepticus is commonly tested.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Status epilepticus"][/sc]
  • Neuronal injury and brain damage: Prolonged seizure activity, particularly beyond 30 minutes, can lead to excitotoxicity and irreversible neuronal loss, impacting long-term neurological function.
  • Systemic complications: Hypoxia, acidosis, hyperthermia, rhabdomyolysis, and cardiovascular instability can arise from sustained muscle activity and metabolic derangements during prolonged seizures.
  • Refractory status epilepticus: Failure of seizures to respond to multiple antiseizure medications, often requiring intensive care admission and anaesthetic agents, increasing the risk of morbidity and mortality.
  • Increased mortality: The risk of death significantly increases with longer duration of status epilepticus and the development of refractory forms, particularly beyond 60 minutes [2].
  • Long-term neurological sequelae: Patients who survive status epilepticus may experience new or worsened cognitive impairment, motor deficits, or psychiatric comorbidities.
