Bacterial vaginosis [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Bacterial vaginosis"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Bacterial vaginosis"][/sc]
  • Bacterial vaginosis (BV) is a common vaginal condition caused by an overgrowth of certain bacteria, leading to an imbalance in the natural vaginal flora.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical presentation, risk factors, and initial management of bacterial vaginosis.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Bacterial vaginosis"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Bacterial vaginosis"][/sc]
  • BV is the most common cause of vaginal discharge in women of reproductive age, affecting approximately 1 in 3 women in the UK at some point in their lives (NICE CKS).
  • Risk factors include having new or multiple sexual partners, douching, and use of intrauterine devices (IUDs).
  • Prevalence rates vary, with studies reporting around 12% in pregnant women and higher rates in certain ethnic groups, such as Black women (NHS inform).
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Bacterial vaginosis"][/sc]
  • Trigger: A reduction in the protective, hydrogen peroxide-producing Lactobacillus species, often due to factors like douching or new sexual partners, disrupts the normal vaginal microbiome.
  • Progression: This reduction allows for an overgrowth of anaerobic bacteria, particularly Gardnerella vaginalis and other BV-associated bacteria, leading to a shift in vaginal pH from acidic to alkaline.
  • Consequence: The metabolic activity of these anaerobic bacteria produces volatile amines, which are responsible for the characteristic 'fishy' odour and increased vaginal discharge.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Bacterial vaginosis"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Onset of symptoms, which can be subtle or entirely asymptomatic, often developing over several days.
  • Progressive phase: If untreated, symptoms may persist and worsen, particularly beyond a few days, though spontaneous resolution can occur in approximately 30% of cases.
  • Late phase: High rates of recurrence are common, with up to 80% of women experiencing a return of BV within 6-12 months even after successful initial treatment.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Bacterial vaginosis"][/sc]
  • Ask about unusual vaginal discharge - often thin, white or grey, which is a key diagnostic feature.
  • Ask about a 'fishy' vaginal odour - typically more noticeable after sexual intercourse or during menstruation, indicating the presence of volatile amines.
  • Ask about vaginal itching or irritation - though less common than discharge or odour, it can indicate associated inflammation.
  • Ask about burning during urination (dysuria) - this can be present if there is associated vulval irritation, and helps differentiate from urinary tract infection.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Bacterial vaginosis"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - typically normal; fever or tachycardia are unusual and may suggest complications like pelvic inflammatory disease.
  • Check vaginal discharge - often thin, homogeneous, and grey-white, coating the vaginal walls, without significant inflammation of the vaginal mucosa.
  • Check vaginal pH - elevated vaginal pH (>4.5) is a key diagnostic criterion, reflecting the shift in microbial balance.
  • Check for a 'fishy' odour (whiff test) - becomes more prominent when a drop of 10% potassium hydroxide (KOH) is added to a sample of discharge, due to the release of volatile amines.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Bacterial vaginosis"][/sc]
  • Fever or systemic illness: Suggests a more widespread infection, such as pelvic inflammatory disease (PID), rather than isolated BV.
  • Severe lower abdominal or pelvic pain: May indicate PID, ectopic pregnancy, or other acute gynaecological conditions requiring urgent assessment.
  • Abnormal vaginal bleeding: Not a typical symptom of BV; warrants investigation for other gynaecological pathology.
  • Symptoms in pregnancy: BV in pregnancy increases the risk of adverse outcomes, including preterm labour and miscarriage, necessitating prompt diagnosis and treatment.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Bacterial vaginosis"][/sc]
  • Pregnancy: While symptoms may be similar, BV in pregnancy carries significant risks, including increased likelihood of preterm labour, premature rupture of membranes, and low birth weight, necessitating prompt treatment.
  • Postmenopausal women: Hormonal changes leading to vaginal atrophy and altered vaginal pH can predispose to BV. Symptoms might be less pronounced or confused with other menopausal changes, and standard diagnostic criteria may be less sensitive.
  • Asymptomatic presentation: A significant proportion of women with BV remain asymptomatic, highlighting the importance of screening in high-risk groups or during pregnancy.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Bacterial vaginosis"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected Bacterial vaginosis: Clinical diagnosis based on Amsel criteria (at least three of: thin, white discharge; vaginal pH >4.5; positive whiff test; clue cells on microscopy).
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Vaginal swabs for microscopy, culture, and sensitivity to rule out other infections like candidiasis or trichomoniasis.
  • Microscopy: Presence of clue cells (vaginal epithelial cells covered in bacteria) is highly indicative. Gram stain can be used for Nugent scoring, which is a laboratory-based gold standard but less common in routine clinical practice.
  • Validated score: Amsel Criteria for Bacterial Vaginosis on MDCalc.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Bacterial vaginosis"][/sc]
  • A 28-year-old female presents with a 3-day history of increased, thin, greyish-white vaginal discharge and a noticeable 'fishy' odour, particularly after intercourse. On examination, vaginal pH is 5.0 and a positive whiff test is elicited.
  • A young woman with subacute onset of malodorous vaginal discharge, suggestive of a vaginal flora imbalance.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Bacterial vaginosis"][/sc]
  • Vulvovaginal candidiasis: Characterised by thick, white, 'cottage cheese' discharge, often with intense itching and soreness, but typically a normal vaginal pH and no fishy odour.
  • Trichomoniasis: Presents with frothy, greenish-yellow discharge, a strong foul odour, and often vaginal inflammation; it is a sexually transmitted infection caused by a parasite.
  • Atrophic vaginitis: Occurs in postmenopausal women due to oestrogen deficiency, leading to vaginal dryness, itching, and sometimes discharge, but typically without the characteristic fishy odour of BV.
  • Cervicitis: Inflammation of the cervix, which can cause vaginal discharge and sometimes bleeding, but the discharge may be purulent and often associated with specific sexually transmitted infections.
  • Physiological discharge: Normal vaginal discharge is usually clear or whitish, odourless, and does not cause irritation or itching, varying in consistency throughout the menstrual cycle.
  • Retained foreign body: Can cause a foul-smelling discharge, often with a history of recent tampon or other object use.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Bacterial vaginosis"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Confirm diagnosis and exclude other infections, especially sexually transmitted infections, which may co-exist.
  • Definitive management: Antibiotic treatment to restore normal vaginal flora. Oral or topical options are available.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Advise on recurrence prevention strategies, such as avoiding douching. Follow-up is generally not required if symptoms resolve, but consider if symptoms persist or recur.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Advise patients to seek medical attention if symptoms worsen, new symptoms develop (e.g., fever, severe pain), or if pregnant.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Bacterial vaginosis"][/sc]
  • Increased susceptibility to STIs: BV disrupts the vaginal mucosal barrier and immune response, increasing the risk of acquiring sexually transmitted infections, including HIV, chlamydia, and gonorrhoea.
  • Pelvic Inflammatory Disease (PID): The altered vaginal flora can ascend to the upper genital tract, leading to PID, which can cause chronic pelvic pain, infertility, and ectopic pregnancy.
  • Adverse pregnancy outcomes: In pregnant women, BV is associated with an increased risk of preterm labour, premature rupture of membranes, low birth weight, and late miscarriage, particularly if untreated.
  • Post-surgical infections: BV increases the risk of developing infections following gynaecological procedures such as hysterectomy, abortion, or insertion of an intrauterine device.
