Labyrinthitis vestibular neuritis [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Labyrinthitis vestibular neuritis"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Labyrinthitis vestibular neuritis"][/sc]
  • Labyrinthitis and vestibular neuritis are inflammatory conditions affecting the inner ear or the vestibular nerve, respectively, typically triggered by a viral infection. Labyrinthitis involves the labyrinth, leading to vertigo and hearing loss, while vestibular neuritis affects only the vestibular nerve, causing vertigo without hearing impairment.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical features distinguishing labyrinthitis from vestibular neuritis, and understand the initial assessment of acute vertigo to differentiate peripheral from central causes.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Labyrinthitis vestibular neuritis"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Labyrinthitis vestibular neuritis"][/sc]
  • The annual incidence of vestibular neuritis is estimated to be around 3.5 to 15.5 cases per 100,000 population, with viral labyrinthitis being a common form.
  • These conditions most frequently affect adults between 30 and 60 years of age.
  • Risk factors primarily include recent or concurrent viral infections, such as the common cold, influenza, herpes simplex virus (cold sore virus), or varicella-zoster virus (shingles).
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Labyrinthitis vestibular neuritis"][/sc]
  • Viral infection, often post-respiratory or gastrointestinal, triggers inflammation of the vestibular nerve (vestibular neuritis) or the entire labyrinth, including the cochlea (labyrinthitis).
  • This inflammation disrupts the normal transmission of balance signals from the affected inner ear to the brain, leading to a sensory mismatch with signals from the unaffected ear and other sensory inputs.
  • The brain's inability to reconcile these conflicting signals results in the characteristic symptoms of acute vertigo, nausea, vomiting, and imbalance. Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Labyrinthitis vestibular neuritis"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Symptoms typically begin suddenly, often upon waking, and are most intense during the first few days, characterised by severe vertigo, nausea, and vomiting.
  • Progressive phase: Over the subsequent 1-2 weeks, acute symptoms gradually subside as the brain begins to compensate for the unilateral vestibular deficit.
  • Late phase: While most individuals regain full balance within 2 to 6 weeks, some may experience residual unsteadiness or motion sensitivity for up to 2-3 months, or even longer in a small number of cases.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Labyrinthitis vestibular neuritis symptoms"][/sc]
  • Ask about vertigo - a sudden, severe sensation of spinning or movement, often worse with head movement, indicating acute vestibular dysfunction.
  • Ask about nausea and vomiting - common autonomic responses to severe vertigo, which can lead to dehydration.
  • Ask about hearing loss - present in labyrinthitis but absent in vestibular neuritis, crucial for differentiating the two conditions.
  • Ask about tinnitus - ringing or buzzing in the ears, also indicative of labyrinthine involvement and absent in pure vestibular neuritis.
  • Ask about recent viral illness - a preceding cold, flu, or other viral infection is a common trigger for both conditions.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Labyrinthitis vestibular neuritis signs"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - typically normal, but dehydration from vomiting may cause postural hypotension or tachycardia.
  • Check nystagmus - spontaneous, unidirectional, horizontal-rotatory nystagmus that is suppressed by visual fixation suggests a peripheral vestibular cause. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="HINTS exam: how to differentiate central from peripheral vertigo?" text="HINTS exam for vertigo"][/sc]
  • Check head impulse test - an abnormal (corrective saccade) response on the affected side indicates a peripheral vestibular lesion.
  • Check gait and balance - often unsteady, with a tendency to fall towards the affected side, particularly during the acute phase.
  • Check otoscopy - usually normal, as the inflammation is typically viral and affects the inner ear, not the middle or outer ear.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Labyrinthitis vestibular neuritis red flags"][/sc]
  • New, severe headache - may indicate a central nervous system pathology such as stroke or haemorrhage.
  • Focal neurological deficits - e.g., weakness, numbness, dysarthria, or diplopia, suggesting a central cause of vertigo.
  • Sudden hearing loss in one ear without vertigo - while labyrinthitis can cause hearing loss, isolated sudden hearing loss warrants urgent ENT assessment to exclude other causes.
  • Vertical or purely torsional nystagmus - highly suggestive of a central vestibular lesion, requiring immediate neurological evaluation.
  • Inability to stand or walk unaided - disproportionate to the degree of vertigo, raising concern for central pathology.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Labyrinthitis vestibular neuritis atypical presentations"][/sc]
  • Older adults: May present with less pronounced vertigo but more prominent unsteadiness and gait disturbance, increasing fall risk.
  • Immunocompromised patients: Atypical pathogens or prolonged/severe course may occur, requiring broader investigation.
  • Recurrent episodes: While typically a single episode, recurrent vertigo should prompt consideration of other vestibular disorders like Meniere's disease or vestibular migraine.
  • Persistent postural-perceptual dizziness (PPPD): Some patients may develop chronic dizziness and unsteadiness following an acute episode, even after the initial inflammation resolves.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Labyrinthitis vestibular neuritis diagnostic tests"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected labyrinthitis/vestibular neuritis: Clinical diagnosis based on history and examination (including HINTS exam) is usually sufficient; routine investigations are not typically required.
  • Severity/risk tools: HINTS for Stroke in Acute Vestibular Syndrome on MDCalc can help differentiate central from peripheral causes of acute vestibular syndrome, particularly in the emergency setting.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Consider audiometry if hearing loss is a prominent or new symptom, to assess the degree and type of hearing impairment.
  • Imaging - MRI brain: Indicated if red flag features are present, or if symptoms are atypical or not resolving as expected, to exclude central pathology such as stroke, tumour, or demyelination.
  • Serial reassessment: Regular clinical review to monitor symptom resolution and identify any evolving red flag features or atypical progression.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Labyrinthitis vestibular neuritis problem representation"][/sc]
  • A 45-year-old female presents with sudden onset, severe, persistent vertigo, nausea, and vomiting for 2 days, preceded by an upper respiratory tract infection, with no focal neurological deficits or new headache.
  • An adult with acute, prolonged, isolated vertigo and associated autonomic symptoms, following a viral prodrome, concerning for a peripheral vestibular disorder.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Labyrinthitis vestibular neuritis differential diagnosis"][/sc]
  • Benign Paroxysmal Positional Vertigo (BPPV) - typically brief episodes of vertigo triggered by specific head movements, unlike the persistent vertigo of labyrinthitis/vestibular neuritis.
  • Meniere's Disease - characterised by recurrent episodes of vertigo, fluctuating hearing loss, tinnitus, and aural fullness, often lasting hours.
  • Vestibular Migraine - episodic vertigo associated with migrainous features (headache, photophobia, phonophobia), often without auditory symptoms.
  • Stroke (posterior circulation) - central vertigo often accompanied by other neurological deficits (e.g., dysarthria, ataxia, diplopia) and abnormal HINTS exam findings. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Central vs peripheral vertigo: key differentiating features?" text="Central vs peripheral vertigo"][/sc]
  • Acoustic Neuroma (Vestibular Schwannoma) - slowly progressive unilateral hearing loss, tinnitus, and imbalance, with vertigo being less common and typically mild.
  • Multiple Sclerosis - can cause episodic or persistent vertigo, often with other neurological symptoms and signs disseminated in space and time.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Labyrinthitis vestibular neuritis treatment"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Recognition of acute vertigo and exclusion of central causes (e.g., stroke) using clinical assessment (e.g., HINTS exam) and consideration of imaging if red flags are present. Supportive care often includes antiemetics for nausea and vomiting.
  • Definitive management: As most cases are viral, antibiotics are generally not indicated unless a bacterial infection is suspected. Symptomatic relief may be provided with short-term vestibular suppressants (e.g., antihistamines or prochlorperazine) for up to 3 days to avoid delaying central compensation.
  • Adjunctive management: Vestibular rehabilitation therapy (VRT) is crucial for patients with persistent balance problems beyond the acute phase, involving specific exercises to promote central compensation and adaptation.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Monitor for resolution of symptoms and improvement in balance. Follow-up is indicated if symptoms worsen, new neurological signs develop, or if there is no improvement within a few weeks.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Patients should be advised to seek urgent medical attention for new or worsening neurological symptoms, severe headache, or changes in hearing.
  • Exam pharmacology reference (UKMLA/MRCGP): Antihistamines (e.g., cyclizine, cinnarizine) and antiemetics (e.g., prochlorperazine) are often considered for symptomatic relief of acute vertigo and nausea. Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Labyrinthitis vestibular neuritis complications"][/sc]
  • Persistent postural-perceptual dizziness (PPPD): Chronic dizziness and unsteadiness that can develop after an acute vestibular event, even after the initial inflammation has resolved, often requiring specific rehabilitation strategies.
  • Anxiety and depression: The debilitating nature of vertigo and imbalance can significantly impact mental health, leading to secondary psychological distress.
  • Falls and injuries: Impaired balance, particularly in older adults, increases the risk of falls and associated injuries during the acute and subacute phases.
  • Delayed recovery/incomplete compensation: In some cases, central compensation may be incomplete, leading to prolonged symptoms and functional impairment, especially if vestibular suppressants are used for too long.
