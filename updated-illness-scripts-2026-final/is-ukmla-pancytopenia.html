Pancytopenia [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Pancytopenia"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Pancytopenia"][/sc]
  • Pancytopenia is a haematological disorder characterised by a reduction in all three major blood cell lines: red blood cells (anaemia), white blood cells (leukopenia, often neutropenia), and platelets (thrombocytopenia).
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical presentation and initial investigative approach to a patient with suspected pancytopenia, understanding its diverse aetiologies.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Pancytopenia"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Pancytopenia"][/sc]
  • The incidence of pancytopenia varies significantly with its underlying cause, with aplastic anaemia affecting approximately 2-5 per million people annually in the UK.
  • Myelodysplastic syndromes, a common cause in older adults, have an incidence of around 4 per 100,000 population per year, rising with age.
  • Risk factors often include exposure to certain drugs (e.g., chemotherapy, some antibiotics), toxins (e.g., benzene), viral infections (e.g., HIV, EBV), and autoimmune conditions.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Pancytopenia"][/sc]
  • Trigger: Bone marrow failure (e.g., aplastic anaemia, myelodysplastic syndromes, infiltration by malignancy) or peripheral destruction/sequestration of blood cells (e.g., hypersplenism, autoimmune conditions).
  • Progression: Reduced production or increased destruction of haematopoietic stem cells or their progeny leads to a quantitative decrease in red cells, white cells, and platelets.
  • Consequence: The combined deficiencies result in symptoms of anaemia (fatigue, dyspnoea), leukopenia (recurrent infections), and thrombocytopenia (bleeding, bruising). [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Haematopoiesis: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Haematopoiesis"][/sc]
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Pancytopenia"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Symptoms may be insidious, developing over weeks to months, often presenting with non-specific fatigue, recurrent minor infections, or easy bruising.
  • Progressive phase: As cell counts decline, symptoms become more pronounced, with increasing severity of anaemia, more frequent or severe infections, and spontaneous bleeding, particularly beyond 4-6 weeks.
  • Late phase: Severe pancytopenia can lead to life-threatening complications such as severe sepsis, major haemorrhage, or progression to acute leukaemia, particularly beyond 3-6 months if untreated.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Pancytopenia"][/sc]
  • Ask about fatigue and exertional dyspnoea - indicative of anaemia, which can be severe and impact daily activities.
  • Ask about recurrent or unusual infections (e.g., oral thrush, cellulitis) - suggests neutropenia, compromising immune defence.
  • Ask about easy bruising, petechiae, epistaxis, or menorrhagia - points to thrombocytopenia, increasing bleeding risk.
  • Ask about weight loss, night sweats, or fever - may indicate an underlying malignancy or chronic infection.
  • Ask about exposure to drugs (e.g., chemotherapy, chloramphenicol), toxins (e.g., benzene), or recent viral illnesses - crucial for identifying potential aetiological factors.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Pancytopenia"][/sc]
  • Check pallor of conjunctivae and mucous membranes - suggests anaemia, reflecting reduced haemoglobin.
  • Check petechiae, purpura, or ecchymoses - indicates thrombocytopenia and increased capillary fragility.
  • Check signs of infection (e.g., fever, oral candidiasis, skin lesions) - points to neutropenia and impaired immune response.
  • Check hepatosplenomegaly or lymphadenopathy - may suggest underlying haematological malignancy, liver disease, or infection. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Splenomegaly: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Splenomegaly"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs: Tachycardia and tachypnoea may reflect severe anaemia; fever indicates infection.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Pancytopenia"][/sc]
  • Severe symptomatic anaemia - requiring urgent transfusion to prevent cardiac decompensation.
  • Febrile neutropenia - a medical emergency requiring prompt broad-spectrum antibiotics to prevent overwhelming sepsis.
  • Significant bleeding - such as gastrointestinal haemorrhage or intracranial bleeding, necessitating urgent platelet transfusion and investigation.
  • Rapidly progressive symptoms - suggesting an aggressive underlying process like acute leukaemia or severe aplastic anaemia, particularly over days to a few weeks.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Pancytopenia"][/sc]
  • Older adults: Pancytopenia may be subtle, presenting with non-specific decline, fatigue, or recurrent falls, often due to myelodysplastic syndromes or chronic conditions.
  • Pregnancy: Physiological dilutional anaemia and thrombocytopenia can mask or complicate the diagnosis of true pancytopenia, requiring careful interpretation of blood counts.
  • Immunosuppressed patients: Pancytopenia can be a side effect of immunosuppressive medications or a manifestation of opportunistic infections, making diagnosis challenging.
  • Incidental finding: Occasionally discovered on routine blood tests in asymptomatic individuals, necessitating thorough investigation to rule out serious underlying pathology.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Pancytopenia"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected Pancytopenia: Full blood count (FBC) with differential, reticulocyte count, peripheral blood film (essential for morphological assessment), liver and renal function tests, LDH, B12, folate, thyroid function tests, viral serology (HIV, hepatitis, EBV, CMV).
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Autoimmune screen (ANA, ENA, anti-dsDNA), direct antiglobulin test (DAT), paroxysmal nocturnal haemoglobinuria (PNH) screen.
  • Bone marrow aspiration and trephine biopsy: Crucial for definitive diagnosis, assessing cellularity, morphology, and detecting abnormal cells or infiltration. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Bone marrow biopsy: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Bone marrow biopsy"][/sc]
  • Imaging - Ultrasound/CT abdomen: To assess for splenomegaly or lymphadenopathy, which may indicate hypersplenism or malignancy.
  • Serial reassessment: Regular FBCs to monitor trends and response to treatment.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Pancytopenia"][/sc]
  • A 68-year-old male presents with a 3-month history of increasing fatigue, recurrent oral candidiasis, and easy bruising, with recent blood tests showing haemoglobin 8.5 g/dL, white cell count 2.1 x 10^9/L (neutrophils 0.8 x 10^9/L), and platelets 65 x 10^9/L.
  • An elderly male with chronic, progressive, multi-lineage cytopenias, presenting with constitutional symptoms, recurrent infections, and bleeding diathesis, requiring urgent haematological evaluation.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Pancytopenia"][/sc]
  • Aplastic Anaemia - hypocellular bone marrow with no abnormal cells.
  • Myelodysplastic Syndromes (MDS) - dysplastic features in bone marrow, often with cytogenetic abnormalities.
  • Acute Leukaemia - presence of >20% blasts in bone marrow.
  • Hypersplenism - enlarged spleen with increased destruction of blood cells.
  • Severe Megaloblastic Anaemia - macro-ovalocytes and hypersegmented neutrophils on blood film, often due to B12/folate deficiency.
  • Systemic Lupus Erythematosus (SLE) - autoimmune features, positive ANA, and other systemic manifestations.
  • Viral Infections (e.g., HIV, EBV) - serological evidence of infection, often with reactive lymphocytosis.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Pancytopenia"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Address life-threatening complications such as severe anaemia (red cell transfusions), febrile neutropenia (empirical broad-spectrum antibiotics), and significant bleeding (platelet transfusions, antifibrinolytics).
  • Definitive management: Highly dependent on the underlying cause. This may include immunosuppressive therapy (e.g., ciclosporin, anti-thymocyte globulin for aplastic anaemia), chemotherapy (for leukaemia or MDS), or splenectomy (for hypersplenism).
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Regular full blood counts, monitoring for infections, bleeding, and disease progression. Nutritional support and infection prophylaxis may be required.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Patients should be advised to seek urgent medical attention for fever, new bleeding, or worsening symptoms. Referral to a haematologist is essential for diagnosis and management.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Pancytopenia"][/sc]
  • Severe infection: Due to neutropenia, leading to sepsis and potentially septic shock, particularly if prolonged beyond weeks.
  • Haemorrhage: Due to thrombocytopenia, ranging from petechiae to life-threatening internal bleeding, especially if platelet counts are critically low.
  • Anaemic heart failure: Severe, chronic anaemia can lead to high-output cardiac failure, particularly in vulnerable patients.
  • Transformation to acute leukaemia: A significant risk in certain underlying conditions like myelodysplastic syndromes, which can occur over months to years.
  • Iron overload: From repeated blood transfusions, requiring chelation therapy to prevent organ damage.
