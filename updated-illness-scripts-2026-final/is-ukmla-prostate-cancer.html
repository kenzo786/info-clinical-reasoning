Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Prostate cancer"][/sc]
  • Prostate cancer is a malignant neoplasm arising from the glandular epithelial cells of the prostate, primarily affecting older men.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the common presentations of prostate cancer, understand the diagnostic pathway including PSA testing and biopsy, and be aware of the principles of management options such as active surveillance, radical prostatectomy, and radiotherapy.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Prostate cancer"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Prostate cancer"][/sc]
  • Prostate cancer is the most common cancer in men in the UK, with over 52,000 new cases diagnosed each year [1].
  • The lifetime risk of prostate cancer for men in the UK is approximately 1 in 8 [1].
  • Risk factors include increasing age (most cases diagnosed in men over 50), ethnic origin (higher incidence in Black men), and a family history of prostate cancer.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Prostate cancer"][/sc]
  • Trigger: Genetic and environmental factors lead to uncontrolled proliferation of prostatic epithelial cells, often in the peripheral zone of the gland.
  • Progression: Malignant cells may invade the prostatic capsule, seminal vesicles, and regional lymph nodes. The Gleason grading system, and subsequently the ISUP grade groups, classify the architectural patterns of prostate cancer, correlating with aggressiveness and prognosis. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Gleason grading: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Gleason grading"][/sc]
  • Consequence: Advanced disease can lead to distant metastases, commonly to bone, causing pain, pathological fractures, and spinal cord compression.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Prostate cancer"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Often asymptomatic, detected incidentally through elevated PSA or DRE findings. Progression can be very slow, spanning many years, particularly for low-grade disease.
  • Progressive phase: Localised disease may progress to locally advanced, causing lower urinary tract symptoms (LUTS) such as frequency, nocturia, hesitancy, and weak stream, or haematuria. This progression can occur over months to years.
  • Late phase: Metastatic disease, often to bone, can manifest with bone pain, pathological fractures, or spinal cord compression. This stage typically develops over months to a few years from the onset of symptoms, particularly beyond initial diagnosis.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Prostate cancer"][/sc]
  • Ask about lower urinary tract symptoms (LUTS) - these can be indicative of prostatic enlargement, either benign or malignant, and include frequency, urgency, nocturia, hesitancy, weak stream, and incomplete emptying.
  • Ask about haematuria or haemospermia - these symptoms, while often benign, can be a sign of prostatic malignancy or other genitourinary pathology.
  • Ask about bone pain - new or worsening bone pain, especially in the back, hips, or ribs, may suggest metastatic disease.
  • Ask about unexplained weight loss or fatigue - these non-specific systemic symptoms can indicate advanced malignancy.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Prostate cancer"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - usually normal in early disease; may show signs of systemic illness (e.g., fever in infection, tachycardia in anaemia) in advanced or complicated cases.
  • Check digital rectal examination (DRE) - findings such as a hard, nodular, or asymmetrical prostate are suspicious for malignancy. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Digital rectal examination in prostate cancer: what are the key findings and their significance?" text="Digital rectal examination"][/sc]
  • Check for lymphadenopathy - palpable inguinal or supraclavicular lymph nodes may indicate metastatic spread.
  • Check for bone tenderness or neurological deficits - these can suggest bone metastases or spinal cord compression.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Prostate cancer"][/sc]
  • New onset bone pain - suggests possible bone metastases, requiring urgent investigation.
  • Neurological deficits (e.g., leg weakness, sensory loss) - indicates potential spinal cord compression due to metastases, a medical emergency.
  • Obstructive uropathy (e.g., anuria, acute kidney injury) - can result from local tumour extension or nodal compression, necessitating prompt intervention.
  • Unexplained significant weight loss - a general red flag for advanced malignancy.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Prostate cancer"][/sc]
  • Younger men: Prostate cancer is less common but often more aggressive in younger men, presenting with more advanced disease or rapid progression.
  • Metastatic disease at presentation: Some men present with symptoms solely related to metastases (e.g., bone pain, pathological fracture) without significant urinary symptoms.
  • PSA-negative prostate cancer: Rarely, aggressive prostate cancers (e.g., neuroendocrine variants) may not produce significant PSA, leading to delayed diagnosis. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="PSA limitations: what are the false positive and false negative causes?" text="PSA limitations"][/sc]
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Prostate cancer"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected prostate cancer:
    • Prostate-specific antigen (PSA) blood test: Used for screening and diagnosis, but has limitations due to false positives (e.g., BPH, prostatitis) and false negatives. NICE NG131 recommends discussing risks and benefits of PSA testing [2].
    • Multiparametric MRI (mpMRI) of the prostate: Recommended as a first-line investigation for suspected clinically localised prostate cancer, reported using a 5-point Likert scale [2].
    • Prostate biopsy: Offered if mpMRI Likert score is 3 or more. Transperineal biopsy is increasingly preferred over TRUS biopsy due to lower sepsis risk [2]. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="TRUS biopsy: what are the indications, procedure, and complications?" text="TRUS biopsy"][/sc]
  • Severity/risk tools:
    • Gleason Score and ISUP Grade Group: Histopathological grading of biopsy samples to assess cancer aggressiveness. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="ISUP grading: how does it relate to Gleason score and what is its prognostic significance?" text="ISUP Grade Group"][/sc]
    • PSA Doubling Time on MDCalc: Can be used to assess disease progression in active surveillance.
  • Imaging - Staging:
    • CT scan of abdomen/pelvis: To assess for nodal involvement or visceral metastases in higher-risk disease.
    • Bone scan (Technetium-99m): To detect bone metastases in men with high-risk localised prostate cancer or symptoms suggestive of metastases [2].
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Prostate cancer"][/sc]
  • A 68-year-old man presents with increasing nocturia, hesitancy, and recent onset lower back pain, with a DRE revealing a hard, nodular prostate and an elevated PSA.
  • An older man with progressive lower urinary tract symptoms and bone pain, concerning for metastatic prostate cancer.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Prostate cancer"][/sc]
  • Benign Prostatic Hyperplasia (BPH) - typically causes LUTS but without the suspicious DRE findings or rapid PSA rise suggestive of malignancy.
  • Prostatitis - often presents with LUTS, perineal pain, and sometimes fever, but PSA elevation is usually acute and inflammatory markers may be raised.
  • Urinary Tract Infection (UTI) - presents with dysuria, frequency, urgency, and sometimes haematuria, usually responsive to antibiotics.
  • Bladder cancer - can cause haematuria and LUTS, but typically without prostatic abnormalities on DRE.
  • Metastatic disease from other primary - bone pain could be from other primary cancers (e.g., lung, renal) but usually with different primary symptoms.
  • Spinal degenerative disease - back pain without other systemic symptoms or prostatic findings. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Spinal cord compression: what are the red flag symptoms and signs?" text="Spinal cord compression"][/sc]
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Prostate cancer"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Recognition of red flag features (e.g., spinal cord compression) and urgent referral to specialist services. Supportive care for symptoms such as pain.
  • Definitive management: Treatment options for localised prostate cancer include active surveillance, radical prostatectomy, or radical radiotherapy, with the choice depending on disease risk, patient preference, and comorbidities [2]. For locally advanced or metastatic disease, androgen deprivation therapy (ADT) is often the cornerstone, sometimes combined with chemotherapy (e.g., docetaxel) or newer hormonal agents [2].
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Regular PSA monitoring, clinical review, and imaging as appropriate, depending on the chosen management strategy.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Patients on active surveillance require clear criteria for escalation to active treatment if disease progression is suspected. Safety-netting advice should be provided for worsening symptoms or new concerns.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Prostate cancer"][/sc]
  • Bone metastases: Can lead to severe pain, pathological fractures, and spinal cord compression, significantly impacting quality of life and prognosis, typically occurring in the late phase of the disease.
  • Urinary obstruction: Local tumour growth can cause bladder outlet obstruction, leading to acute urinary retention, recurrent UTIs, and hydronephrosis, often developing in the progressive phase.
  • Treatment-related side effects: Radical prostatectomy can cause urinary incontinence and erectile dysfunction. Radiotherapy can lead to bowel and bladder irritation. These can manifest early post-treatment and persist long-term.
  • Hormone-refractory prostate cancer: Progression of disease despite androgen deprivation therapy, indicating a more aggressive phenotype and requiring further systemic treatments, typically occurring months to years after initial ADT.
