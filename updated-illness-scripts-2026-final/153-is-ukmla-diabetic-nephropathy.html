Diabetic nephropathy [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Diabetic nephropathy"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Diabetic nephropathy"][/sc]
  • Diabetic nephropathy is a progressive kidney disease caused by long-standing diabetes mellitus, characterised by albuminuria and a decline in glomerular filtration rate (GFR).
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical features, progression, and management principles of diabetic nephropathy, including the significance of microalbuminuria and renoprotective strategies.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Diabetic nephropathy"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Diabetic nephropathy"][/sc]
  • Diabetic nephropathy affects approximately 20-40% of individuals with diabetes in the UK, making it the leading cause of end-stage renal disease (ESRD) [1].
  • The prevalence of microalbuminuria (albumin excretion rate 30-300 mg/24h) is around 25% in type 1 diabetes and 30% in type 2 diabetes, with progression to macroalbuminuria (albumin excretion rate >300 mg/24h) occurring in a significant proportion without intervention [2].
  • Key risk factors include duration of diabetes, poor glycaemic control (HbA1c), hypertension, dyslipidaemia, obesity, and genetic predisposition [3].
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Diabetic nephropathy"][/sc]
  • Sustained hyperglycaemia leads to glomerular hyperfiltration, increased intraglomerular pressure, and activation of various pathways including advanced glycation end-products (AGEs) and protein kinase C (PKC).
  • These changes result in mesangial expansion, thickening of the glomerular basement membrane, and podocyte injury, culminating in glomerulosclerosis and the formation of Kimmelstiel-Wilson nodules [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Kimmelstiel-Wilson nodules: what are they and why do they matter clinically?" text="Kimmelstiel-Wilson nodules"][/sc].
  • Progressive damage to the renal filtration barrier leads to increased albuminuria, declining GFR, and ultimately, chronic kidney disease (CKD) and end-stage renal disease (ESRD).
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Diabetic nephropathy"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Characterised by glomerular hyperfiltration and microalbuminuria, often asymptomatic, developing typically within 5-10 years of diabetes onset.
  • Progressive phase: Microalbuminuria progresses to macroalbuminuria, with a gradual decline in GFR, usually over several years. This phase is particularly beyond 10-15 years of diabetes duration.
  • Late phase: Overt proteinuria, significant reduction in GFR, and development of symptoms of uraemia, leading to ESRD typically beyond 15-20 years of diabetes duration.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Diabetic nephropathy"][/sc]
  • Ask about oedema - particularly in the ankles, feet, or around the eyes, indicating fluid retention due to impaired kidney function.
  • Ask about fatigue and weakness - often non-specific, but can signify anaemia (due to reduced erythropoietin production) or accumulation of uraemic toxins.
  • Ask about nausea, loss of appetite, or metallic taste - symptoms of uraemia, suggesting advanced kidney dysfunction.
  • Ask about changes in urination - such as nocturia or foamy urine (due to proteinuria), which may be early indicators of renal involvement.
  • Ask about shortness of breath - can be a symptom of fluid overload (pulmonary oedema) or anaemia.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Diabetic nephropathy"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - particularly blood pressure, as hypertension is almost universally present and contributes to progression.
  • Check for peripheral oedema - pitting oedema in the lower limbs or sacrum, indicating fluid retention.
  • Check for signs of retinopathy - such as cotton wool spots, haemorrhages, or neovascularisation, as diabetic retinopathy often co-exists with nephropathy [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Diabetic retinopathy: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="diabetic retinopathy"][/sc].
  • Check for pallor - suggesting anaemia, a common complication of advanced CKD.
  • Check for skin changes - such as pruritus or uraemic frost (rare), indicating severe uraemia.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Diabetic nephropathy"][/sc]
  • Rapid decline in GFR - suggests an acute kidney injury or rapidly progressive glomerulonephritis, requiring urgent investigation beyond typical diabetic nephropathy progression.
  • Sudden onset of macroalbuminuria - particularly in the absence of long-standing diabetes or with atypical features, may indicate a non-diabetic renal disease.
  • Unexplained severe hypertension - especially if resistant to multiple agents, may point to renovascular disease or other secondary causes.
  • Symptoms of uraemia - such as persistent nausea, vomiting, confusion, or pericardial rub, indicating severe renal failure requiring immediate intervention.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Diabetic nephropathy"][/sc]
  • Normoalbuminuric diabetic kidney disease: Some individuals with diabetes experience a decline in GFR without preceding microalbuminuria, particularly older adults or those with type 2 diabetes. This highlights that GFR decline can occur independently of albuminuria progression.
  • Rapid progression in younger patients: While typically a slow progressive disease, a rapid decline in renal function in younger individuals with diabetes may suggest an superimposed non-diabetic kidney disease or a more aggressive form of diabetic nephropathy.
  • Absence of retinopathy: Diabetic retinopathy and nephropathy often co-exist. The absence of retinopathy in a patient with significant albuminuria should prompt consideration of a non-diabetic cause for kidney disease [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Non-diabetic renal disease in diabetes: what are the key considerations?" text="non-diabetic renal disease"][/sc].
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Diabetic nephropathy"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected diabetic nephropathy:
    • Urine albumin-to-creatinine ratio (ACR) - annual screening for microalbuminuria in all diabetic patients.
    • Estimated glomerular filtration rate (eGFR) - calculated from serum creatinine, also for annual monitoring.
    • Blood pressure measurement - to identify and manage hypertension.
    • HbA1c - to assess glycaemic control.
  • Severity/risk tools:
    • CKD-EPI equation for eGFR.
    • CKD Risk Equation (2013) on MDCalc - for assessing risk of CKD progression.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible:
    • Renal ultrasound - to assess kidney size, rule out obstruction, and identify other structural abnormalities.
    • Renal biopsy - considered in atypical presentations (e.g., rapid GFR decline, absence of retinopathy, active urine sediment) to differentiate from non-diabetic kidney disease [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Renal biopsy in diabetic nephropathy: when is it indicated?" text="renal biopsy"][/sc].
  • Serial reassessment:
    • Regular monitoring of ACR, eGFR, and blood pressure is crucial to track disease progression and assess treatment effectiveness.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Diabetic nephropathy"][/sc]
  • A 55-year-old male with a 15-year history of type 2 diabetes and poorly controlled hypertension presents with increasing bilateral ankle oedema and fatigue, with recent blood tests showing elevated creatinine and macroalbuminuria.
  • A middle-aged male with long-standing, suboptimally controlled type 2 diabetes and hypertension, presenting with insidious onset of fluid retention and symptoms of uraemia, concerning for progressive diabetic nephropathy.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Diabetic nephropathy"][/sc]
  • Hypertensive nephrosclerosis - often co-exists with diabetes, but can be the primary cause of CKD, typically with less proteinuria than diabetic nephropathy.
  • Chronic glomerulonephritis - can present with proteinuria and declining GFR, but often has an active urine sediment (e.g., red cell casts) and may lack typical diabetic microvascular complications.
  • Acute kidney injury (AKI) - a rapid decline in renal function, often reversible, triggered by factors like dehydration, nephrotoxic drugs, or sepsis, which needs to be differentiated from chronic progression.
  • Drug-induced nephropathy - certain medications (e.g., NSAIDs, some antibiotics) can cause kidney damage, mimicking or exacerbating diabetic nephropathy.
  • Renovascular disease - can cause hypertension and kidney dysfunction, particularly if there is a sudden worsening of blood pressure control or asymmetric kidney size.
  • Amyloidosis - can cause significant proteinuria and renal impairment, but often has systemic features beyond diabetes [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Amyloidosis and kidney disease: what are the key features?" text="amyloidosis"][/sc].
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Diabetic nephropathy"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Recognition of declining renal function and prompt referral to specialist renal services for comprehensive management and to exclude reversible causes or non-diabetic kidney disease.
  • Definitive management:
    • Optimisation of glycaemic control (HbA1c targets individualised, typically 48-58 mmol/mol or 6.5-7.5%) to slow progression.
    • Strict blood pressure control (target <130/80 mmHg, or lower if tolerated) using ACE inhibitors or Angiotensin Receptor Blockers (ARBs) as first-line agents for renoprotection.
    • Consideration of SGLT2 inhibitors (e.g., dapagliflozin, empagliflozin) in patients with type 2 diabetes and CKD for cardiorenal protection, even in the absence of diabetes.
    • Consideration of finerenone, a non-steroidal mineralocorticoid receptor antagonist, for patients with type 2 diabetes and CKD (stage 3 or 4 with albuminuria) to reduce the risk of CKD progression and cardiovascular events [4].
  • Adjunctive management:
    • Dietary protein restriction (0.8 g/kg/day) in advanced CKD.
    • Management of dyslipidaemia with statins.
    • Smoking cessation and weight management.
  • Monitoring and follow-up:
    • Regular monitoring of eGFR, ACR, blood pressure, and electrolytes.
    • Multidisciplinary team approach involving diabetologists, nephrologists, dietitians, and specialist nurses.
  • Escalation and safety-netting:
    • Education on symptoms of worsening kidney function and when to seek urgent medical attention.
    • Preparation for renal replacement therapy (dialysis or transplantation) in advanced ESRD.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Diabetic nephropathy"][/sc]
  • End-stage renal disease (ESRD): The ultimate consequence of progressive diabetic nephropathy, requiring renal replacement therapy (dialysis or transplantation) typically beyond 15-20 years of diabetes duration, significantly impacting quality of life and survival.
  • Cardiovascular disease: Diabetic nephropathy is an independent risk factor for cardiovascular events (e.g., myocardial infarction, stroke, heart failure), with increased albuminuria correlating with higher cardiovascular mortality. This risk is particularly elevated in the progressive and late phases.
  • Anaemia: Common in advanced CKD due to reduced erythropoietin production by the kidneys, contributing to fatigue and reduced exercise tolerance.
  • Bone and mineral disorders: Renal osteodystrophy and disturbances in calcium, phosphate, and vitamin D metabolism develop as kidney function declines.
  • Diabetic retinopathy and neuropathy: Often co-exist with nephropathy, reflecting widespread microvascular damage, and can worsen in parallel with renal decline.
  • Increased susceptibility to infections: Impaired immune function in CKD increases the risk of infections, particularly urinary tract infections and pneumonia.
