Chronic pancreatitis [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Chronic pancreatitis"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Chronic pancreatitis"][/sc]
  • Chronic pancreatitis is a progressive inflammatory disease of the pancreas characterised by irreversible morphological changes, typically leading to permanent impairment of exocrine and endocrine function.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical features of chronic pancreatitis, identify common aetiologies, and outline the principles of its long-term management and complication screening.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Chronic pancreatitis"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Chronic pancreatitis"][/sc]
  • The incidence of chronic pancreatitis in the UK is estimated to be 5-12 per 100,000 population per year, with a prevalence of 25-50 per 100,000 (NICE, 2018).
  • Alcohol misuse is the most common cause in adults, accounting for 40-70% of cases, particularly in men aged 30-50 years.
  • Other risk factors include genetic mutations (e.g., PRSS1, CFTR), autoimmune diseases, severe acute pancreatitis, ductal obstruction, and idiopathic causes.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Chronic pancreatitis"][/sc]
  • Triggering factors, most commonly chronic alcohol exposure, lead to recurrent episodes of pancreatic inflammation and premature activation of digestive enzymes within the pancreas.
  • Persistent inflammation and oxidative stress cause progressive fibrosis, acinar cell loss, and calcification of the pancreatic parenchyma, impairing both exocrine and endocrine function. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Pancreatic calcification: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Pancreatic calcification"][/sc]
  • The irreversible damage results in maldigestion due to exocrine insufficiency (e.g., steatorrhoea) and impaired glucose regulation due to endocrine insufficiency (pancreatogenic diabetes).
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Chronic pancreatitis"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Characterised by recurrent episodes of acute pancreatitis, often triggered by alcohol, which gradually lead to subclinical pancreatic damage over months to years.
  • Progressive phase: As pancreatic damage accumulates, patients develop chronic abdominal pain, maldigestion symptoms (e.g., steatorrhoea), and weight loss, typically over several years.
  • Late phase: Marked by significant exocrine and endocrine insufficiency, leading to established pancreatogenic diabetes and persistent malabsorption, particularly beyond 5-10 years from symptom onset.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Chronic pancreatitis"][/sc]
  • Ask about recurrent or persistent epigastric pain - often radiating to the back, exacerbated by food, and partially relieved by leaning forward, indicating pancreatic inflammation.
  • Ask about steatorrhoea - pale, bulky, foul-smelling stools that are difficult to flush, suggesting pancreatic exocrine insufficiency and fat malabsorption.
  • Ask about unintentional weight loss and signs of malabsorption - due to inadequate digestion and absorption of nutrients, particularly fats.
  • Ask about new-onset diabetes mellitus - indicating significant endocrine dysfunction of the pancreas.
  • Ask about history of alcohol consumption - a crucial risk factor that can guide diagnostic suspicion.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Chronic pancreatitis"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - may be normal in stable chronic pancreatitis, but fever and tachycardia could indicate an acute exacerbation or complication.
  • Check for epigastric tenderness - may be present on deep palpation, reflecting ongoing pancreatic inflammation.
  • Check for signs of malnutrition - muscle wasting, reduced subcutaneous fat, and pallor can indicate chronic malabsorption.
  • Check for jaundice - suggests biliary obstruction, potentially due to pancreatic head inflammation or stricture.
  • Check for a palpable abdominal mass - rarely, a pseudocyst or inflammatory mass may be felt.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Chronic pancreatitis"][/sc]
  • Sudden worsening of pain: May indicate an acute exacerbation, pseudocyst formation, or pancreatic cancer.
  • New-onset jaundice: Suggests biliary obstruction, potentially due to pancreatic head mass or stricture, requiring urgent investigation.
  • Rapid, unexplained weight loss: Raises concern for pancreatic malignancy, particularly in the context of chronic pancreatitis.
  • Persistent vomiting: Can indicate gastric outlet obstruction due to inflammation or pseudocyst compression.
  • Signs of sepsis: Fever, tachycardia, and hypotension may point to infected pseudocyst or cholangitis, requiring immediate medical attention.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Chronic pancreatitis"][/sc]
  • Older adults: May present with less prominent pain but more pronounced malabsorption or new-onset diabetes, making diagnosis challenging.
  • Autoimmune pancreatitis: Can mimic pancreatic cancer with diffuse pancreatic enlargement and obstructive jaundice, requiring specific serological and histological diagnosis.
  • Genetic forms: Patients with hereditary pancreatitis may present at a younger age with recurrent acute pancreatitis episodes, often with a family history.
  • Pain-free pancreatitis: A subset of patients may have significant pancreatic damage and insufficiency without the typical severe abdominal pain, presenting primarily with malabsorption or diabetes.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Chronic pancreatitis"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected chronic pancreatitis:
    • Full blood count, urea and electrolytes, liver function tests (to assess for biliary obstruction), C-reactive protein.
    • Amylase and lipase may be normal or only mildly elevated, as significant acinar cell destruction limits enzyme production.
    • Faecal elastase-1: A non-invasive test for pancreatic exocrine insufficiency; levels <200 mcg/g suggest insufficiency. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Pancreatic exocrine insufficiency: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Pancreatic exocrine insufficiency"][/sc]
  • Severity/risk tools:
    • Modified Glasgow Prognostic Score on MDCalc for acute exacerbations of chronic pancreatitis. Modified Glasgow Prognostic Score on MDCalc
  • Imaging - CT abdomen: Often the initial imaging modality, showing pancreatic calcification, ductal dilation, atrophy, or pseudocysts.
  • Imaging - MRI/MRCP: Provides detailed visualisation of the pancreatic parenchyma and ductal system, superior for detecting early changes and ductal abnormalities.
  • Imaging - Endoscopic ultrasound (EUS): Highly sensitive for detecting subtle changes of chronic pancreatitis, such as lobularity, stranding, and calcifications, even when other imaging is normal.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Chronic pancreatitis"][/sc]
  • A 45-year-old male with a significant alcohol history presents with chronic, waxing and waning epigastric pain radiating to the back, associated with steatorrhoea and recent diagnosis of diabetes.
  • Middle-aged male with chronic abdominal pain and malabsorption, concerning for chronic pancreatitis.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Chronic pancreatitis"][/sc]
  • Pancreatic cancer: Can present with similar pain, weight loss, and jaundice, but typically has a more rapid progression and may lack a history of recurrent acute episodes.
  • Acute pancreatitis: Characterised by sudden onset severe pain and elevated amylase/lipase, but typically resolves without permanent damage unless recurrent.
  • Irritable bowel syndrome (IBS): Presents with chronic abdominal pain and altered bowel habits, but typically lacks features of malabsorption or pancreatic insufficiency.
  • Peptic ulcer disease: Epigastric pain, but often relieved by antacids and associated with dyspeptic symptoms rather than steatorrhoea.
  • Biliary colic/cholecystitis: Right upper quadrant pain, often post-prandial, but typically lacks features of chronic pancreatic dysfunction.
  • Coeliac disease: Malabsorption and steatorrhoea, but usually without significant abdominal pain and associated with specific serological markers and duodenal biopsy findings.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Chronic pancreatitis"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Recognition of acute exacerbations, adequate pain control, and supportive care including intravenous fluids and nutritional support.
  • Definitive management: Addressing the underlying cause (e.g., alcohol cessation), pain management (often multimodal), and managing exocrine and endocrine insufficiency.
  • Adjunctive management: Endoscopic or surgical interventions may be considered for ductal obstruction, pseudocysts, or intractable pain.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Regular monitoring for complications such as pancreatic cancer, pseudocysts, and worsening diabetes.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Patients should be advised to seek urgent medical attention for sudden worsening of pain, jaundice, or signs of infection.
  • Exam pharmacology reference (UKMLA/MRCGP): Pancreatic enzyme replacement therapy (PERT) is often warranted for symptomatic pancreatic exocrine insufficiency. Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Chronic pancreatitis"][/sc]
  • Pancreatic pseudocyst: Fluid collections that can develop within or adjacent to the pancreas, often causing pain, gastric outlet obstruction, or infection, typically occurring in the progressive phase.
  • Biliary obstruction: Due to inflammation or stricture of the common bile duct as it passes through the pancreatic head, leading to jaundice and cholangitis, particularly in the late phase.
  • Pancreatogenic diabetes: Impaired insulin production due to extensive islet cell destruction, leading to type 3c diabetes mellitus, usually manifesting in the late phase.
  • Pancreatic exocrine insufficiency: Malabsorption of fats and fat-soluble vitamins due to insufficient digestive enzyme production, resulting in steatorrhoea and weight loss, prominent in the progressive and late phases.
  • Pancreatic cancer: Chronic pancreatitis is a significant risk factor for pancreatic adenocarcinoma, with an increased risk that persists throughout the disease course, particularly beyond 10-20 years.
  • Splenic vein thrombosis: Inflammation can lead to thrombosis of the splenic vein, potentially causing gastric varices and gastrointestinal bleeding.
