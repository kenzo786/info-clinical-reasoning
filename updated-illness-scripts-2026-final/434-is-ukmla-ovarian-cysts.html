Ovarian cysts [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Ovarian cysts"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Ovarian cysts"][/sc]
  • Ovarian cysts are fluid-filled sacs that develop on or within an ovary. They are very common, mostly benign, and can be classified as functional (related to the menstrual cycle, e.g., follicular, corpus luteum) or pathological (unrelated to the menstrual cycle, e.g., dermoid, endometrioma, cystadenoma).
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the features of simple and complex ovarian cysts, assess for complications like torsion or rupture, and apply the principles of risk stratification (e.g., RMI) to guide initial management and referral in primary care.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Ovarian cysts"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Ovarian cysts"][/sc]
  • Approximately 1 in 10 women in the UK will require surgery for an ovarian cyst in their lifetime [1].
  • Ovarian cysts are very common, with many women developing them at some point; most are functional and resolve spontaneously [2].
  • Risk factors for pathological cysts include endometriosis (for endometriomas), genetic predisposition (for dermoid cysts), and certain hormonal therapies [3].
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Ovarian cysts"][/sc]
  • Ovarian cysts typically arise from either normal physiological processes (functional cysts) or abnormal cellular proliferation and tissue development (pathological cysts).
  • Functional cysts involve continued fluid accumulation in a follicle or corpus luteum, while pathological cysts, such as [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Dermoid cyst: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="dermoid cysts"], [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Rokitansky protuberance: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Rokitansky protuberance"], endometriomas, and cystadenomas, involve the growth of ectopic or abnormal tissue within the ovary.
  • While most cysts are benign and resolve spontaneously, larger or pathological cysts can lead to symptoms through mass effect, hormonal activity, or complications like rupture, haemorrhage, or torsion.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Ovarian cysts"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Functional cysts typically develop over days to weeks, coinciding with the menstrual cycle, and often resolve spontaneously within 1-3 months. Pathological cysts can develop over months to years, with symptoms often insidious in onset.
  • Progressive phase: As cysts grow, symptoms may become more pronounced, including persistent pelvic pain, pressure, or changes in bowel/bladder habits. Complications like torsion or rupture can present acutely, requiring immediate medical attention.
  • Late phase: Long-standing or large cysts may lead to chronic pelvic pain, infertility, or malignant transformation in rare cases. Post-menopausal cysts require careful monitoring due to a higher risk of malignancy.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Ovarian cysts"][/sc]
  • Often asymptomatic, especially small functional cysts.
  • Pelvic pain or discomfort: often dull, aching, or unilateral; can be acute and severe if rupture or torsion occurs.
  • Abdominal bloating or distension.
  • Changes in bowel habits (constipation) or urinary frequency due to pressure effects.
  • Dyspareunia (painful intercourse).
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Ovarian cysts"][/sc]
  • Often normal on examination, especially for small cysts.
  • Abdominal tenderness or guarding, particularly if rupture or torsion has occurred.
  • Palpable pelvic mass on bimanual examination (size, mobility, consistency are important).
  • Ascites (fluid in the abdomen) in cases of large or malignant cysts.
  • Signs of hormonal activity (e.g., hirsutism in androgen-producing tumours).
  • [sc name="jitl-query" Q="What are the key features of ovarian torsion on examination?" text="Signs of ovarian torsion"] include severe, sudden-onset unilateral pelvic pain, often with nausea and vomiting, and a tender adnexal mass.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Ovarian cysts"][/sc]
  • Sudden onset, severe, unilateral pelvic pain, often with nausea and vomiting, suggesting ovarian torsion or rupture.
  • Ascites, unexplained weight loss, or new onset of irregular bleeding, which may indicate malignancy.
  • Rapid increase in cyst size or development of solid components on imaging, particularly in post-menopausal women.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Ovarian cysts"][/sc]
  • Ovarian cysts can be asymptomatic and discovered incidentally during imaging for other conditions.
  • Presentation with acute abdomen due to rupture or haemorrhage, mimicking appendicitis or diverticulitis.
  • Hormonally active cysts may present with endocrine symptoms (e.g., precocious puberty, virilisation).
  • [sc name="jitl-query" Q="What are the atypical presentations of ovarian cysts in post-menopausal women?" text="Atypical presentations in post-menopausal women"] may include vague abdominal symptoms or an incidentally found mass, which warrants careful investigation due to increased malignancy risk.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Ovarian cysts"][/sc]
  • Initial assessment: Pelvic ultrasound is the primary imaging modality to characterise ovarian cysts (size, morphology, presence of solid components, septations, vascularity).
  • Tumour markers: CA125 is used, particularly in post-menopausal women, to assess malignancy risk. Other markers (e.g., AFP, hCG, LDH) may be relevant for specific germ cell tumours.
  • Risk stratification: The Risk of Malignancy Index (RMI) combines ultrasound findings, menopausal status, and CA125 levels to estimate the risk of ovarian cancer. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="How is the Risk of Malignancy Index (RMI) calculated and interpreted?" text="MDCalc link for RMI"]
  • Further imaging (MRI, CT) may be required for complex cases or suspected malignancy.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Ovarian cysts"][/sc]
  • A patient presents with pelvic pain and abdominal distension. On examination, a palpable adnexal mass is noted. Imaging reveals a complex ovarian cyst.
  • This presentation raises concern for benign ovarian pathology versus malignancy, requiring further investigation to rule out ovarian cancer.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Ovarian cysts (Gynaological tract)"][/sc]
  • Physiological cysts: Follicular cysts, corpus luteum cysts (most common, often resolve spontaneously).
  • Benign pathological cysts: Endometriomas, dermoid cysts (teratomas), serous/mucinous cystadenomas.
  • Malignant ovarian tumours: Epithelial ovarian cancer, germ cell tumours, sex cord-stromal tumours.
  • Non-ovarian gynaecological conditions: Ectopic pregnancy, pelvic inflammatory disease, fibroids, hydrosalpinx.
  • Non-gynaecological conditions: Appendicitis, diverticulitis, irritable bowel syndrome, urinary tract infection.
  • [sc name="jitl-query" Q="What are the key differential diagnoses for a pelvic mass in a reproductive-aged woman?" text="Key differential diagnoses for a pelvic mass"] vary significantly with age and menopausal status.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Ovarian cysts"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Manage acute pain with analgesia. Assess for signs of ovarian torsion or rupture, which require urgent surgical intervention.
  • Definitive management:
    • Expectant management: Most functional cysts resolve spontaneously. Small, asymptomatic, simple cysts in pre-menopausal women can be monitored with repeat ultrasound in 6-12 weeks.
    • Surgical intervention: Indicated for persistent, symptomatic, or rapidly growing cysts; cysts with suspicious features (e.g., solid components, large size); or complications like torsion or rupture. May involve cystectomy or oophorectomy.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Regular ultrasound surveillance for cysts managed conservatively. Post-operative follow-up as per surgical guidelines.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Advise patients to seek urgent medical attention for sudden worsening pain, fever, or signs of hypovolaemia. Refer to gynaecology for complex cysts or suspected malignancy.
  • Exam pharmacology reference (UKMLA/MRCGP): [sc name="jitl-medication" Q="Combined oral contraceptive pill in ovarian cysts: what is the evidence for its use?" text="Combined oral contraceptive pills"] are sometimes used to prevent recurrence of functional cysts, though evidence for their role in resolving existing cysts is limited.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Ovarian cysts"][/sc]
  • Ovarian torsion: Acute twisting of the ovary and fallopian tube, compromising blood supply, leading to severe pain and requiring emergency surgery.
  • Rupture and haemorrhage: Cysts can rupture, causing acute abdominal pain and potentially internal bleeding, which may require surgical intervention if severe.
  • Malignant transformation: While most ovarian cysts are benign, some, particularly in post-menopausal women or those with suspicious features, can be malignant.
  • Infertility: Certain types of cysts, such as endometriomas, can contribute to infertility.
