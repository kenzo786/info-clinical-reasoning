Psychotic disorder [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Psychotic disorder"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Psychotic disorder"][/sc]
  • Psychotic disorder encompasses a range of severe mental health conditions characterised by a significant loss of contact with reality, leading to impaired thought processes, perceptions, and behaviour.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: The candidate must recognise the key features of psychotic disorders, including positive and negative symptoms, and understand the urgency of early intervention and risk assessment.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Psychotic disorder"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Psychotic disorder"][/sc]
  • The lifetime prevalence of psychotic disorders, including schizophrenia, is estimated to be around 1% in the UK population, with incidence peaking in early adulthood (NICE).
  • Risk factors often include a family history of psychosis, cannabis use, childhood trauma, urban living, and migration, which may interact with genetic predispositions.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Psychotic disorder"][/sc]
  • A complex interplay of genetic vulnerability and environmental stressors can trigger neurodevelopmental abnormalities and dysregulation of neurotransmitter systems, particularly dopamine.
  • This dysregulation leads to altered neural circuitry in areas responsible for perception, cognition, and emotion, resulting in the emergence of positive and negative psychotic symptoms.
  • The consequence is a progressive decline in functional capacity and social engagement, often associated with structural brain changes over time if untreated.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Psychotic disorder"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Often characterised by a prodromal period of non-specific symptoms such as social withdrawal, decline in functioning, and unusual beliefs, particularly beyond several weeks to months.
  • Progressive phase: Marked by the emergence of overt psychotic symptoms like hallucinations and delusions, which can become persistent and severely disruptive to daily life, particularly beyond a few months.
  • Late phase: May involve chronic or relapsing episodes, with potential for residual negative symptoms and functional impairment, particularly beyond several years without effective management.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Psychotic disorder"][/sc]
  • Ask about hallucinations - to identify sensory experiences without external stimuli, such as auditory voices, which are common in psychosis.
  • Ask about delusions - to uncover fixed, false beliefs that are not amenable to change in light of conflicting evidence, indicating a break from reality.
  • Ask about disorganised thought and speech - to assess for illogical thinking, tangentiality, or incoherent communication, which are core features of formal thought disorder.
  • Ask about negative symptoms - to identify a reduction or absence of normal functions, such as avolition (lack of motivation) or alogia (poverty of speech), which impact daily functioning.
  • Ask about mood changes - to determine if there are co-occurring symptoms of depression or mania, as psychotic features can occur in affective disorders.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Psychotic disorder"][/sc]
  • Check mental state examination - to systematically assess appearance, behaviour, speech, mood, thought form and content, perception, and insight.
  • Check vital signs - to identify any physiological instability or side effects of medication, including tachycardia, hypertension, or fever.
  • Check neurological examination - to rule out organic causes of psychosis, such as neurological disorders or substance-induced states.
  • Check for signs of self-neglect - to observe for poor hygiene, unkempt appearance, or malnutrition, which can indicate severe functional impairment.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Psychotic disorder"][/sc]
  • Acute onset psychosis - specific implication: suggests an organic cause or severe stressor, requiring urgent medical evaluation.
  • First-episode psychosis with suicidal ideation - specific implication: indicates high risk of self-harm, necessitating immediate psychiatric assessment and safety planning.
  • Psychosis with fever and neurological signs - specific implication: raises concern for encephalitis, meningitis, or neuroleptic malignant syndrome, requiring emergency medical attention.
  • Psychosis with significant functional decline - specific implication: suggests severe illness impact, requiring comprehensive assessment and intensive support.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Psychotic disorder"][/sc]
  • Older adults: Psychosis may present with more prominent cognitive decline and visual hallucinations, often secondary to delirium, dementia, or medical comorbidities, which matters for accurate diagnosis and management.
  • Adolescents: Psychotic symptoms can be subtle and easily mistaken for normal adolescent behaviour or other mental health conditions, delaying diagnosis and early intervention.
  • Postpartum psychosis: A severe and rapid onset of psychotic symptoms within weeks of childbirth, requiring urgent psychiatric intervention due to high risk to mother and infant.
  • Substance-induced psychosis: Psychotic features directly attributable to substance use or withdrawal, which matters for differentiating from primary psychotic disorders and guiding treatment.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Psychotic disorder"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected Psychotic disorder: Comprehensive mental state examination, detailed history including collateral information, physical examination, and routine blood tests (FBC, U&Es, LFTs, TFTs, glucose, CRP).
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Urine toxicology screen, syphilis serology, HIV test, autoimmune screen (e.g., ANA, anti-NMDA receptor antibodies).
  • Imaging - MRI brain: Considered if there are atypical features, focal neurological signs, or suspicion of organic brain pathology.
  • Serial reassessment: Ongoing monitoring of symptoms, functioning, and side effects of medication is crucial for treatment optimisation and relapse prevention.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Psychotic disorder"][/sc]
  • A 24-year-old male presents with a 3-month history of hearing voices telling him to harm himself, increasing social isolation, and neglecting personal hygiene, alongside a recent decline in academic performance.
  • A young adult with subacute onset of auditory hallucinations, paranoid delusions, and functional deterioration, raising concern for a primary psychotic disorder.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Psychotic disorder"][/sc]
  • Schizophrenia - distinguishing feature: persistent symptoms for at least 6 months, including at least one month of active-phase symptoms (delusions, hallucinations, disorganised speech).
  • Bipolar disorder with psychotic features - distinguishing feature: psychotic symptoms occur exclusively during manic or depressive episodes.
  • Depressive disorder with psychotic features - distinguishing feature: psychotic symptoms occur exclusively during a major depressive episode.
  • Substance-induced psychotic disorder - distinguishing feature: evidence of substance use or withdrawal directly causing the psychotic symptoms.
  • Schizoaffective disorder - distinguishing feature: a continuous period of illness during which there is a major mood episode concurrent with Criterion A of schizophrenia, and delusions or hallucinations for at least 2 weeks in the absence of a major mood episode.
  • Organic psychosis (e.g., encephalitis, brain tumour) - distinguishing feature: presence of neurological signs, abnormal physical examination findings, or atypical presentation.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Psychotic disorder"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Ensure safety of the patient and others, assess for acute behavioural disturbance and de-escalation, consider urgent psychiatric review and admission if risk is high.
  • Definitive management: Initiate antipsychotic medication, offer psychological interventions such as cognitive behavioural therapy (CBT) for psychosis, and provide social support and occupational therapy.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Regular monitoring for medication side effects, symptom response, physical health, and relapse prevention strategies, including adherence support.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Advise on signs of relapse, provide crisis contact numbers, and ensure clear pathways for re-referral or emergency care.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Psychotic disorder"][/sc]
  • Relapse: mechanism and why it matters diagnostically/prognostically: Recurrence of acute psychotic symptoms, often due to medication non-adherence or stressors, leading to further functional decline and increased morbidity.
  • Suicide: mechanism and why it matters diagnostically/prognostically: Increased risk, particularly in early stages or during depressive episodes, significantly impacting prognosis and requiring vigilant risk assessment.
  • Substance misuse: mechanism and why it matters diagnostically/prognostically: Often used as self-medication, exacerbating psychotic symptoms and interfering with treatment, worsening long-term outcomes.
  • Physical health comorbidities: mechanism and why it matters diagnostically/prognostically: Higher rates of cardiovascular disease, diabetes, and obesity due to lifestyle factors and medication side effects, reducing life expectancy.
  • Social and occupational impairment: mechanism and why it matters diagnostically/prognostically: Difficulty maintaining relationships, employment, or education, leading to isolation and reduced quality of life.
