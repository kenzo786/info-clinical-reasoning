Primary aldosteronism [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Primary aldosteronism"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Primary aldosteronism"][/sc]
  • Primary aldosteronism is a group of disorders where aldosterone production is inappropriately high for sodium status, leading to hypertension, hypokalaemia, and suppressed plasma renin activity.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical features, investigations, and management principles of primary aldosteronism, distinguishing it from other causes of secondary hypertension.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Primary aldosteronism"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Primary aldosteronism"][/sc]
  • Primary aldosteronism is estimated to affect 5-10% of patients with hypertension in the UK, and up to 20% of those with resistant hypertension.
  • Risk factors include family history of early-onset hypertension or stroke, spontaneous hypokalaemia, and adrenal incidentaloma.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Primary aldosteronism"][/sc]
  • Trigger: Autonomous or excessive aldosterone secretion from the adrenal glands, often due to an adenoma (Conn's syndrome) or bilateral adrenal hyperplasia.
  • Progression: High aldosterone levels lead to increased sodium reabsorption and potassium excretion in the renal collecting ducts, causing sodium retention and hypokalaemia.
  • Consequence: Sustained sodium retention expands extracellular fluid volume, leading to hypertension, while hypokalaemia can cause muscle weakness and cardiac arrhythmias. Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Primary aldosteronism"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Often asymptomatic, with hypertension developing insidiously over months to years, sometimes discovered incidentally during routine checks.
  • Progressive phase: Hypertension may become more severe or resistant to conventional treatment, and hypokalaemia may develop, particularly beyond several months, leading to symptoms like muscle weakness.
  • Late phase: Prolonged hypertension and hypokalaemia can lead to target organ damage, including cardiovascular events, renal impairment, and metabolic complications, typically beyond several years if untreated.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Primary aldosteronism"][/sc]
  • Ask about muscle weakness or cramps - due to hypokalaemia, which can range from mild to severe and impact daily activities.
  • Ask about polyuria and polydipsia - hypokalaemia can impair renal concentrating ability, leading to nephrogenic diabetes insipidus.
  • Ask about headaches and palpitations - common non-specific symptoms of hypertension, which may be severe or resistant to treatment.
  • Ask about fatigue and lethargy - general symptoms that can be associated with chronic hypokalaemia and hypertension.
  • Ask about nocturia - often a consequence of impaired renal concentrating ability and increased fluid excretion at night.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Primary aldosteronism"][/sc]
  • Check blood pressure - typically elevated, often severe or resistant hypertension (above 140/90 mmHg despite three antihypertensive agents).
  • Check heart rate and rhythm - may reveal arrhythmias such as atrial fibrillation due to hypokalaemia.
  • Check for signs of hypokalaemia - such as diminished deep tendon reflexes or muscle tenderness, though often subtle.
  • Check for peripheral oedema - usually absent despite sodium retention, as aldosterone escape mechanisms prevent overt fluid overload.
  • Vitals: Blood pressure is consistently elevated, often with no orthostatic drop. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Resistant hypertension: definition and causes?" text="Resistant hypertension"] is a key indicator.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Primary aldosteronism"][/sc]
  • Spontaneous hypokalaemia: Suggests significant aldosterone excess and warrants urgent investigation.
  • Severe or resistant hypertension: Blood pressure poorly controlled despite multiple medications, indicating a secondary cause.
  • Early-onset hypertension: Hypertension developing before the age of 40, especially if severe, increases suspicion for secondary causes like primary aldosteronism.
  • Adrenal incidentaloma with hypertension: An adrenal mass discovered incidentally in a hypertensive patient should prompt screening for primary aldosteronism.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Primary aldosteronism"][/sc]
  • Normokalaemic primary aldosteronism: Patients may present with hypertension but normal potassium levels, making diagnosis more challenging as hypokalaemia is a classic but not universal feature.
  • Older adults: May present with less pronounced hypokalaemia or more severe cardiovascular complications due to longer disease duration and comorbidities.
  • Pregnancy: Hypertension in pregnancy, particularly if severe or associated with hypokalaemia, should prompt consideration of primary aldosteronism, requiring careful management due to fetal implications.
  • Children/adolescents: Rare, but can present with severe hypertension and growth retardation, often due to genetic forms of aldosteronism.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Primary aldosteronism"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected Primary Aldosteronism: Initial screening involves measuring plasma aldosterone concentration (PAC) and plasma renin activity (PRA) to calculate the aldosterone-to-renin ratio (ARR). A high ARR with elevated PAC is suggestive.
  • Confirmatory tests: Oral sodium loading test, saline infusion test, or fludrocortisone suppression test are used to confirm autonomous aldosterone secretion.
  • Imaging - CT abdomen: Used to identify adrenal adenomas or bilateral adrenal hyperplasia after biochemical confirmation.
  • Adrenal venous sampling (AVS): The gold standard for distinguishing unilateral aldosterone production (amenable to surgery) from bilateral hyperplasia, especially if imaging is equivocal or bilateral lesions are present. Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Primary aldosteronism"][/sc]
  • A 55-year-old male presents with resistant hypertension, muscle weakness, and fatigue for the past year, despite being on three antihypertensive medications.
  • A middle-aged man with chronic, resistant hypertension and hypokalaemia, suggesting an endocrine cause of secondary hypertension.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Primary aldosteronism"][/sc]
  • Essential hypertension - absence of clear secondary causes, typically normokalaemic.
  • Renovascular hypertension - often presents with abdominal bruits, flash pulmonary oedema, or acute kidney injury with ACE inhibitors.
  • Phaeochromocytoma - paroxysmal hypertension, headaches, palpitations, and sweating.
  • Cushing's syndrome - central obesity, striae, proximal muscle weakness, and glucose intolerance.
  • Licorice ingestion - can mimic primary aldosteronism due to mineralocorticoid effects, but renin is suppressed.
  • Diuretic abuse - hypokalaemia and metabolic alkalosis, but often with dehydration and low blood pressure.
  • Congenital adrenal hyperplasia (late-onset) - may present with hypertension and hypokalaemia, often with virilisation in females. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Congenital adrenal hyperplasia: what are the different types and their clinical features?" text="Congenital adrenal hyperplasia"] can be a subtle cause.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Primary aldosteronism"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Correct hypokalaemia with potassium supplementation. Initiate mineralocorticoid receptor antagonists (MRAs) to control blood pressure and potassium.
  • Definitive management: For unilateral aldosterone-producing adenoma, laparoscopic adrenalectomy is curative. For bilateral adrenal hyperplasia, lifelong MRA therapy is the mainstay.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Regular monitoring of blood pressure, serum potassium, and renal function is essential. Adjust MRA dosage as needed.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Patients should be advised on symptoms of severe hypokalaemia (e.g., profound muscle weakness, arrhythmias) and resistant hypertension, and when to seek urgent medical attention.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Primary aldosteronism"][/sc]
  • Cardiovascular events: Increased risk of stroke, myocardial infarction, and atrial fibrillation due to prolonged hypertension and hypokalaemia.
  • Renal impairment: Chronic hypertension and hypokalaemia can lead to nephrosclerosis and progressive decline in renal function.
  • Metabolic syndrome: Higher prevalence of glucose intolerance and dyslipidaemia, contributing to overall cardiovascular risk.
  • Left ventricular hypertrophy: Sustained hypertension leads to cardiac remodelling, increasing the risk of heart failure.
