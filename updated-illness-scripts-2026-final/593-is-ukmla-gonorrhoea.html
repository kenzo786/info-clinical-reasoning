Gonorrhoea [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Gonorrhoea"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Gonorrhoea"][/sc]
  • Gonorrhoea is a sexually transmitted infection (STI) caused by the bacterium Neisseria gonorrhoeae, primarily affecting mucous membranes of the urethra, cervix, rectum, pharynx, and conjunctiva.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical features of gonorrhoea, understand its transmission and diagnostic approach, and outline appropriate management and public health implications.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Gonorrhoea"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Gonorrhoea"][/sc]
  • Gonorrhoea remains a significant public health concern in the UK; in 2022, there were 82,592 diagnoses of gonorrhoea reported in England, an increase of 50% compared to 2021, representing the highest number since records began in 1918 (UK Health Security Agency).
  • The highest rates of diagnosis are typically observed in young adults (15-24 years old), men who have sex with men (MSM), and individuals living in urban areas.
  • Risk factors include unprotected sexual intercourse, multiple sexual partners, a history of other STIs, and concurrent bacterial vaginosis in women.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Gonorrhoea"][/sc]
  • Trigger: Infection occurs through direct contact with infected mucous membranes during sexual activity, allowing Neisseria gonorrhoeae to adhere to epithelial cells via pili and outer membrane proteins.
  • Progression: The bacteria invade host cells, replicate, and induce an inflammatory response, leading to symptoms such as urethral discharge and dysuria; [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Neisseria gonorrhoeae: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Neisseria gonorrhoeae"][/sc] possesses mechanisms to evade the immune system, contributing to persistent infection.
  • Consequence: Untreated infection can ascend to cause pelvic inflammatory disease (PID) in women, epididymitis in men, or disseminate systemically, leading to complications like disseminated gonococcal infection (DGI).
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Gonorrhoea"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Symptoms typically develop within 2-14 days after exposure, though many infections, particularly in women, can be asymptomatic for weeks or months.
  • Progressive phase: If untreated, local infection can persist and potentially spread to adjacent structures within weeks, leading to complications such as epididymitis or pelvic inflammatory disease.
  • Late phase: Disseminated gonococcal infection (DGI) can occur weeks to months after initial infection, particularly in asymptomatic individuals, manifesting as migratory polyarthralgia, tenosynovitis, and dermatitis.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Gonorrhoea"][/sc]
  • Ask about urethral discharge - often purulent or mucopurulent, indicating urethritis in men.
  • Ask about dysuria - a common symptom in both men and women, suggesting urethral inflammation.
  • Ask about vaginal discharge - may be increased or altered in consistency, often associated with cervicitis.
  • Ask about rectal discomfort or discharge - indicative of proctitis, especially in individuals engaging in receptive anal intercourse.
  • Ask about pharyngeal symptoms - usually asymptomatic, but can present with sore throat, important as a reservoir for transmission.
  • Ask about lower abdominal pain - may suggest pelvic inflammatory disease in women, requiring urgent assessment.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Gonorrhoea"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - typically normal in uncomplicated gonorrhoea, but fever may indicate disseminated infection or PID.
  • Check for urethral discharge - visible upon examination, particularly after milking the urethra in men.
  • Check for cervical inflammation - speculum examination may reveal mucopurulent cervicitis, easily friable cervix.
  • Check for rectal discharge or inflammation - proctoscopy may show erythema or exudate.
  • Check for joint swelling or tenderness - suggestive of septic arthritis or tenosynovitis in disseminated gonococcal infection. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Disseminated gonococcal infection: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="disseminated gonococcal infection"][/sc]
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Gonorrhoea"][/sc]
  • Fever and systemic symptoms: Suggests disseminated gonococcal infection or pelvic inflammatory disease, requiring urgent hospital assessment and parenteral antibiotics.
  • Severe lower abdominal pain with guarding/rebound: Indicates acute pelvic inflammatory disease, potentially leading to tubo-ovarian abscess or peritonitis.
  • Joint swelling with severe pain and restricted movement: Points to septic arthritis, a serious complication of DGI, necessitating joint aspiration and urgent management.
  • Neurological symptoms (e.g., meningitis) or endocarditis: Rare but life-threatening manifestations of DGI, demanding immediate specialist intervention.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Gonorrhoea"][/sc]
  • Asymptomatic infection: Common, especially in women (up to 80% of cervical infections) and pharyngeal/rectal infections, leading to delayed diagnosis and increased transmission risk.
  • Women: Symptoms can be non-specific or mild, often mistaken for other genitourinary infections, delaying diagnosis and increasing risk of PID.
  • Men who have sex with men (MSM): Higher prevalence of pharyngeal and rectal infections, which are frequently asymptomatic but can be a source of transmission.
  • Disseminated Gonococcal Infection (DGI): Presents with migratory polyarthralgia, tenosynovitis, and characteristic skin lesions (papules, pustules, haemorrhagic lesions), often without overt genitourinary symptoms. Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Gonorrhoea"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected gonorrhoea: Nucleic acid amplification tests (NAATs) on first-pass urine (men), vulvovaginal swabs (women), or site-specific swabs (rectal, pharyngeal, conjunctival) are the gold standard due to high sensitivity and specificity.
  • Microscopy: Gram stain of urethral discharge in symptomatic men may show Gram-negative intracellular diplococci, providing a rapid presumptive diagnosis.
  • Culture: Essential for antimicrobial susceptibility testing, particularly given increasing antimicrobial resistance, and for medico-legal cases.
  • Severity/risk tools: For suspected pelvic inflammatory disease, consider a clinical assessment using criteria from BASHH guidelines; for disseminated gonococcal infection, clinical suspicion is key.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Co-testing for other STIs (Chlamydia, HIV, Syphilis) is routinely recommended due to high rates of co-infection.
  • Serial reassessment: Test of cure is recommended for pharyngeal and rectal infections, and in cases of treatment failure or suspected resistance.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Gonorrhoea"][/sc]
  • A 22-year-old male presents with a 3-day history of purulent urethral discharge and dysuria after unprotected sexual intercourse with a new partner, with no systemic symptoms.
  • Acute urethritis in a young, sexually active male, concerning for a sexually transmitted infection, most likely gonorrhoea.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Gonorrhoea"][/sc]
  • Chlamydia trachomatis infection - often causes milder or asymptomatic urethritis/cervicitis, discharge typically less purulent than gonorrhoea.
  • Non-gonococcal urethritis (NGU) - caused by various organisms (e.g., Mycoplasma genitalium, Ureaplasma urealyticum, adenovirus), often diagnosed after excluding gonorrhoea and chlamydia.
  • Trichomoniasis - can cause frothy, malodorous vaginal discharge and dysuria in women, and asymptomatic urethritis in men.
  • Bacterial vaginosis - characterised by a 'fishy' smelling vaginal discharge, but typically without dysuria or urethral symptoms.
  • Cystitis/UTI - primarily dysuria and urinary frequency, usually without significant urethral or vaginal discharge.
  • Herpes simplex virus (HSV) infection - presents with painful genital ulcers, vesicles, or pustules, often with associated lymphadenopathy.
  • Pelvic Inflammatory Disease (PID) - can be caused by gonorrhoea or chlamydia, presenting with lower abdominal pain, deep dyspareunia, and cervical motion tenderness. Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Gonorrhoea"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Prompt diagnosis and initiation of appropriate antimicrobial therapy to prevent complications and onward transmission. Partner notification and treatment are crucial.
  • Definitive management: Uncomplicated anogenital and pharyngeal gonorrhoea is typically managed with a single dose of intramuscular ceftriaxone, often combined with oral azithromycin (though BASHH guidelines are moving away from routine dual therapy due to resistance concerns).
  • Adjunctive management: Symptomatic relief with analgesia. Abstinence from sexual activity until treatment is completed and symptoms resolve, and partners are treated.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Test of cure is recommended for pharyngeal and rectal infections, and in all cases of disseminated infection, treatment failure, or suspected resistance.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Patients with disseminated gonococcal infection or pelvic inflammatory disease require hospital admission and often intravenous antibiotics. Advise patients to return if symptoms worsen or new symptoms develop.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Gonorrhoea"][/sc]
  • Pelvic Inflammatory Disease (PID): In women, untreated ascending infection can lead to PID within weeks, causing chronic pelvic pain, infertility, and ectopic pregnancy.
  • Epididymitis/Orchitis: In men, ascending infection can cause inflammation of the epididymis and/or testes, leading to pain, swelling, and potentially infertility.
  • Disseminated Gonococcal Infection (DGI): Occurs when the bacteria enter the bloodstream, typically weeks to months after initial infection, leading to migratory polyarthralgia, tenosynovitis, dermatitis, and rarely septic arthritis, endocarditis, or meningitis.
  • Infertility: Both PID in women and epididymitis in men can result in long-term reproductive complications.
  • Increased HIV transmission: Gonorrhoea can increase the risk of acquiring and transmitting HIV due to genital inflammation and mucosal disruption.
  • Neonatal conjunctivitis (Ophthalmia neonatorum): Transmitted from an infected mother to her newborn during vaginal delivery, potentially causing blindness if untreated.
