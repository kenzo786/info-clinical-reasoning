Kawasaki disease [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Kawasaki disease"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Kawasaki disease"][/sc]
  • Kawasaki disease is an acute, self-limiting systemic vasculitis primarily affecting medium-sized arteries, predominantly in children under 5 years of age.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical features of Kawasaki disease, understand its potential for cardiac complications, and initiate appropriate management to prevent long-term sequelae.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Kawasaki disease"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Kawasaki disease"][/sc]
  • The incidence of Kawasaki disease in the UK and Ireland has been reported to be around 8.9 per 100,000 children under 5 years annually, with a rising trend.
  • It is the leading cause of acquired heart disease in children in developed countries.
  • More common in children of East Asian descent, suggesting a genetic predisposition.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Kawasaki disease"][/sc]
  • Trigger: An unknown environmental trigger, likely an infection, initiates an immune response in genetically predisposed individuals.
  • Progression: This leads to widespread systemic inflammation, particularly affecting the medium-sized arteries, including the coronary arteries. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Vasculitis: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Vasculitis"][/sc]
  • Consequence: Uncontrolled inflammation can result in damage to the arterial walls, leading to aneurysm formation, especially in the coronary arteries, which can have long-term cardiac implications.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Kawasaki disease"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Characterised by an acute, persistent fever lasting at least 5 days, often accompanied by other acute symptoms such as rash, conjunctivitis, and oral changes.
  • Progressive phase: Typically occurs within the first 10 days of illness, where inflammation may lead to coronary artery abnormalities if untreated.
  • Late phase: Beyond 4 weeks, the acute symptoms resolve, but the risk of coronary artery complications persists, necessitating long-term follow-up for affected individuals.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Kawasaki disease"][/sc]
  • Ask about persistent fever - typically high-grade and unresponsive to antipyretics, lasting for 5 days or more, which is a cardinal diagnostic criterion.
  • Ask about rash - a polymorphous, non-vesicular rash that can appear anywhere on the body, often presenting early in the illness.
  • Ask about conjunctivitis - bilateral, non-exudative conjunctival injection, often noted without discharge.
  • Ask about changes in extremities - redness and swelling of the hands and feet, followed by desquamation (peeling) of the skin on the fingertips and toes in the convalescent phase.
  • Ask about oral changes - red, cracked lips and a "strawberry tongue" (red, bumpy tongue), indicating mucositis.
  • Ask about irritability - often a prominent feature in affected children, reflecting systemic illness.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Kawasaki disease"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - persistent fever (>38.5°C) is a key indicator; tachycardia may also be present due to systemic inflammation or cardiac involvement.
  • Check cervical lymphadenopathy - typically a single, non-suppurative lymph node greater than 1.5 cm in diameter, often unilateral.
  • Check for peripheral oedema and erythema - swelling and redness of the hands and feet, which can be painful to touch.
  • Check for peeling skin - particularly on the fingertips and toes, which is a characteristic finding in the subacute phase. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Desquamation: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Desquamation"][/sc]
  • Check for signs of cardiac involvement - murmurs, gallop rhythm, or signs of heart failure, indicating potential coronary artery damage.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Kawasaki disease"][/sc]
  • Persistent fever beyond 5 days: Increases the likelihood of Kawasaki disease and the risk of coronary artery aneurysms if untreated.
  • Incomplete presentation in infants: Younger children (under 6 months) may present with fewer classic symptoms, making diagnosis challenging but increasing the risk of delayed treatment and cardiac complications.
  • Signs of cardiac dysfunction: New heart murmur, gallop rhythm, or signs of heart failure (e.g., tachypnoea, poor perfusion) indicate significant cardiac involvement requiring urgent intervention.
  • Recurrent fever after initial treatment: May suggest treatment failure or resistance to initial therapy, necessitating further evaluation and alternative management strategies.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Kawasaki disease"][/sc]
  • Infants under 6 months: May present with prolonged fever and irritability but fewer of the classic mucocutaneous features, leading to delayed diagnosis and a higher risk of coronary artery aneurysms.
  • Older children/adolescents: Can present with atypical symptoms, sometimes mimicking other febrile illnesses, and may have a more severe course with increased cardiac involvement.
  • Incomplete Kawasaki disease: Patients may not meet the full diagnostic criteria (fewer than 4 principal features) but still have coronary artery involvement, requiring a high index of suspicion. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Incomplete Kawasaki disease: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Incomplete Kawasaki disease"][/sc]
  • Kawasaki disease shock syndrome: A rare but severe variant characterised by hypotension or signs of shock, myocardial dysfunction, and significant systemic inflammation, requiring intensive care.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Kawasaki disease"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected Kawasaki disease:
    • Full blood count (FBC): Often shows leukocytosis with neutrophilia, anaemia, and thrombocytosis (in the subacute phase).
    • Inflammatory markers: Elevated C-reactive protein (CRP) and erythrocyte sedimentation rate (ESR) are consistently found.
    • Liver function tests (LFTs): May show elevated transaminases.
    • Urine dipstick: Sterile pyuria can be present.
    • Echocardiogram: Essential for assessing coronary artery involvement (aneurysms, dilatation) and myocardial function. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Coronary artery aneurysm: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Coronary artery aneurysm"][/sc]
  • Severity/risk tools:
    • Kawasaki Disease Coronary Artery Z-score on MDCalc: Used to standardise coronary artery diameter measurements for age and body surface area.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible:
    • Blood cultures: To rule out bacterial sepsis.
    • Viral swabs: To exclude common viral infections.
    • Lumbar puncture: If meningitis is suspected.
  • Serial reassessment:
    • Repeat echocardiograms: Typically performed at diagnosis, 1-2 weeks, and 4-6 weeks after onset to monitor for coronary artery changes.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Kawasaki disease"][/sc]
  • A previously well 3-year-old child presents with 6 days of high-grade fever, polymorphous rash, bilateral conjunctivitis, and swollen, red hands.
  • Young child with prolonged fever and mucocutaneous inflammation, concerning for systemic vasculitis.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Kawasaki disease"][/sc]
  • Measles - typically presents with Koplik spots and a maculopapular rash that starts on the face and spreads downwards, often with cough, coryza, and conjunctivitis, but usually without the extremity changes or prolonged fever of Kawasaki disease.
  • Scarlet fever - caused by Group A Streptococcus, presents with a fine, sandpaper-like rash, "strawberry tongue," and pharyngitis, but usually responds to antibiotics and lacks the persistent fever and cardiac risks of Kawasaki disease.
  • Adenovirus infection - can cause fever, conjunctivitis, and pharyngitis, but typically has a milder course and does not involve the systemic vasculitis or coronary artery complications seen in Kawasaki disease.
  • Systemic juvenile idiopathic arthritis (sJIA) - presents with daily spiking fevers, evanescent rash, and arthritis, but typically has a longer duration of symptoms and different inflammatory markers compared to the acute presentation of Kawasaki disease.
  • Drug hypersensitivity reaction - can cause fever and rash, but usually has a clear temporal association with drug exposure and lacks the specific constellation of mucocutaneous findings and cardiac risk of Kawasaki disease.
  • Toxic shock syndrome - presents with fever, rash, hypotension, and multi-organ involvement, but is typically associated with bacterial toxins and has a more acute, severe onset with prominent shock features. Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Kawasaki disease"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Prompt recognition and referral to paediatric specialists for urgent assessment and initiation of treatment to minimise the risk of coronary artery complications. Supportive care often includes fluid management and close monitoring of vital signs.
  • Definitive management: Intravenous immunoglobulin (IVIG) is typically administered as a single high dose (2g/kg) within the first 10 days of illness to reduce inflammation and prevent coronary artery aneurysms.
  • Adjunctive management: High-dose aspirin is often initiated concurrently with IVIG to reduce inflammation and platelet aggregation, transitioning to low-dose aspirin once the child is afebrile for 48-72 hours. Corticosteroids may be considered in cases of IVIG resistance or severe inflammation.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Regular echocardiograms are crucial to monitor for coronary artery changes. Long-term follow-up with a paediatric cardiologist is often warranted, especially if coronary artery abnormalities develop.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Parents and carers should be advised on signs of worsening condition or recurrence of fever, and when to seek urgent medical attention.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Kawasaki disease"][/sc]
  • Coronary artery aneurysms: The most serious complication, occurring in up to 25% of untreated children, resulting from persistent inflammation and weakening of the coronary artery walls, leading to potential ischaemia, thrombosis, or rupture.
  • Myocardial infarction: Can occur due to thrombosis within coronary artery aneurysms, particularly in the late phase, leading to significant cardiac morbidity and mortality.
  • Myocarditis: Inflammation of the heart muscle, which can lead to impaired cardiac function and heart failure during the acute phase of the illness.
  • Valvular heart disease: Less common, but inflammation can affect heart valves, leading to regurgitation or stenosis.
  • Arrhythmias: May arise from myocardial inflammation or ischaemia, potentially causing palpitations or syncope.
