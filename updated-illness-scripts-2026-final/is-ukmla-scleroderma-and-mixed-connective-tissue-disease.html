Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Scleroderma and mixed connective tissue disease"][/sc]
  • Scleroderma, or systemic sclerosis (SSc), is a rare, chronic autoimmune connective tissue disease characterised by widespread vascular dysfunction, immune activation, and progressive fibrosis of the skin and internal organs. Mixed connective tissue disease (MCTD) is an overlap syndrome with features of SSc, systemic lupus erythematosus, and polymyositis.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the key clinical features of scleroderma and mixed connective tissue disease, differentiate between limited and diffuse cutaneous forms, and identify common organ-specific complications to guide initial investigation and referral.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Scleroderma and mixed connective tissue disease"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Scleroderma and mixed connective tissue disease"][/sc]
  • Systemic sclerosis affects approximately 1 in 5,000 people in the UK, with an incidence of 1-2 per 100,000 per year. It is more common in women, with a female-to-male ratio of 3-4:1, typically presenting between 30 and 50 years of age.
  • Limited cutaneous SSc (lcSSc) accounts for about 80% of cases, while diffuse cutaneous SSc (dcSSc) is less common but associated with more severe internal organ involvement.
  • Risk factors include genetic predisposition (e.g., certain HLA alleles), environmental triggers (e.g., silica dust, organic solvents), and exposure to certain drugs.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Scleroderma and mixed connective tissue disease"][/sc]
  • Initial trigger involves endothelial cell injury and activation, leading to vascular damage and dysfunction, particularly in small blood vessels. This results in impaired blood flow and tissue ischaemia.
  • Progression involves immune system activation, with T and B lymphocytes contributing to inflammation and the production of autoantibodies (e.g., anti-centromere, anti-Scl-70). This immune dysregulation stimulates fibroblasts.
  • Consequence is excessive collagen and extracellular matrix deposition by activated fibroblasts, leading to progressive fibrosis and hardening of the skin and internal organs, such as the lungs, heart, kidneys, and gastrointestinal tract. Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Scleroderma and mixed connective tissue disease"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Often insidious, characterised by Raynaud phenomenon, which may precede other symptoms by years. Skin changes typically begin in the fingers and hands, progressing proximally.
  • Progressive phase: Over months to a few years, skin thickening and internal organ involvement (e.g., gastrointestinal, pulmonary, renal) become more apparent. The rate and extent of progression vary significantly between limited and diffuse forms.
  • Late phase: Established organ damage, such as pulmonary hypertension, interstitial lung disease, or renal crisis, can lead to significant morbidity and mortality, particularly beyond 5-10 years from diagnosis.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Scleroderma and mixed connective tissue disease"][/sc]
  • Ask about Raynaud phenomenon - this is often the earliest symptom, presenting as episodic digital ischaemia, and can precede other manifestations by years, indicating underlying vascular dysfunction.
  • Ask about skin changes - specifically tightening, thickening, and swelling of the skin, which can restrict movement and indicate the extent of fibrosis.
  • Ask about joint pain and stiffness - common in early disease, often symmetrical, and can be confused with other rheumatological conditions.
  • Ask about dysphagia or heartburn - these suggest oesophageal dysmotility or reflux, common gastrointestinal manifestations due to smooth muscle fibrosis.
  • Ask about shortness of breath or cough - these are crucial for identifying potential interstitial lung disease or pulmonary hypertension, which are major causes of morbidity and mortality.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Scleroderma and mixed connective tissue disease"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - particularly blood pressure for renal crisis, and oxygen saturation for pulmonary involvement.
  • Check skin for thickening and tethering - especially on the fingers (sclerodactyly), hands, and face, noting the extent and distribution to differentiate limited from diffuse forms.
  • Check for digital ulcers or pitting scars - these are signs of severe Raynaud phenomenon and microvascular damage.
  • Check for telangiectasias - small, dilated blood vessels on the skin, often seen in limited cutaneous SSc (CREST syndrome). [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Telangiectasias in scleroderma: what are they and why do they matter clinically?" text="Telangiectasias"][/sc]
  • Check for crepitations on lung auscultation - may indicate interstitial lung disease.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Scleroderma and mixed connective tissue disease"][/sc]
  • Rapidly progressive skin thickening: Suggests diffuse cutaneous SSc, which is associated with a higher risk of early and severe internal organ involvement within the first 3-5 years.
  • New onset or worsening shortness of breath: May indicate developing interstitial lung disease or pulmonary hypertension, requiring urgent respiratory and cardiac assessment.
  • Acute onset hypertension with renal impairment: Highly suggestive of scleroderma renal crisis, a life-threatening complication requiring immediate management.
  • Severe digital ischaemia or gangrene: Indicates critical vascular compromise and risk of limb loss, necessitating prompt intervention.
  • Unexplained weight loss and malabsorption: Can signify severe gastrointestinal involvement, requiring nutritional support and investigation.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Scleroderma and mixed connective tissue disease"][/sc]
  • Scleroderma sine scleroderma: Patients present with internal organ involvement (e.g., pulmonary fibrosis, renal crisis) but without the characteristic skin changes, making diagnosis challenging.
  • Early diffuse cutaneous SSc: Can present with significant oedema and inflammatory features, mimicking other inflammatory arthropathies before skin thickening becomes prominent.
  • Mixed Connective Tissue Disease (MCTD): Features overlap with SLE, polymyositis, and SSc, often with high titres of anti-U1-RNP antibodies. The presentation can be variable and evolve over time. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Mixed connective tissue disease: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Mixed Connective Tissue Disease"][/sc]
  • Childhood-onset scleroderma: While rare, presentation in children can be more aggressive with rapid progression of skin and organ involvement, requiring specialised paediatric rheumatology input.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Scleroderma and mixed connective tissue disease"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected scleroderma:
    • Autoantibody screen: Antinuclear antibodies (ANA) are positive in >90% of SSc patients. Specific antibodies include anti-centromere (associated with lcSSc/CREST) and anti-Scl-70 (topoisomerase I, associated with dcSSc). Anti-U1-RNP is characteristic of MCTD. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Anti-centromere antibody: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Anti-centromere antibody"][/sc]
    • Full blood count, renal function, liver function tests, inflammatory markers (ESR, CRP).
    • Urinalysis for proteinuria and haematuria.
  • Severity/risk tools:
    • Modified Rodnan Skin Score (mRSS) for assessing skin thickening.
    • EULAR Scleroderma Mortality Risk Score on MDCalc for prognostic assessment.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Further investigations based on clinical suspicion, e.g., muscle enzymes for myositis, complement levels for SLE.
  • Imaging - Chest X-ray and High-Resolution CT (HRCT) chest: To assess for interstitial lung disease.
  • Imaging - Echocardiogram: To screen for pulmonary hypertension and cardiac involvement.
  • Serial reassessment: Regular monitoring of organ function (e.g., lung function tests, echocardiograms, renal function) is crucial due to the progressive nature of the disease.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Scleroderma and mixed connective tissue disease"][/sc]
  • Systemic Lupus Erythematosus (SLE) - distinguished by different autoantibody profiles (e.g., anti-dsDNA, anti-Sm), prominent inflammatory arthritis, and renal involvement typically presenting as glomerulonephritis rather than renal crisis.
  • Polymyositis/Dermatomyositis - distinguished by prominent muscle weakness and elevated muscle enzymes, often with characteristic skin rashes (e.g., Gottron\'s papules, heliotrope rash) not seen in SSc.
  • Rheumatoid Arthritis - distinguished by symmetrical inflammatory polyarthritis, often with small joint involvement, and specific autoantibodies (rheumatoid factor, anti-CCP) without significant skin fibrosis or Raynaud phenomenon.
  • Eosinophilic Fasciitis - distinguished by rapid onset of skin thickening, often after strenuous exercise, affecting the fascia rather than primarily the dermis, and typically lacks Raynaud phenomenon or internal organ involvement.
  • Drug-induced scleroderma-like syndromes - distinguished by a clear temporal relationship to certain medications (e.g., bleomycin, pentazocine) and resolution upon drug withdrawal.
  • Primary Raynaud\'s Phenomenon - distinguished by the absence of underlying connective tissue disease symptoms, signs, or autoantibodies. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Primary Raynaud\'s phenomenon: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Primary Raynaud\'s Phenomenon"][/sc]
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Scleroderma and mixed connective tissue disease"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Recognition and prompt referral to a rheumatologist. Supportive care for symptoms such as Raynaud phenomenon (e.g., keeping warm, avoiding triggers) and gastroesophageal reflux (e.g., PPIs).
  • Definitive management: Often involves immunosuppressive agents (e.g., methotrexate, mycophenolate mofetil, cyclophosphamide) for progressive skin or lung disease, and targeted therapies for specific organ complications (e.g., endothelin receptor antagonists, phosphodiesterase-5 inhibitors for pulmonary hypertension).
  • Adjunctive management: Regular physiotherapy and occupational therapy to maintain joint mobility and function. Psychological support for chronic disease management.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Regular clinical review, including assessment of skin involvement, lung function tests, echocardiograms, and renal function, to monitor disease progression and detect complications early.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Patients should be educated on red flag symptoms (e.g., acute shortness of breath, sudden severe headache, new onset hypertension) and advised to seek urgent medical attention.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Scleroderma and mixed connective tissue disease"][/sc]
  • Pulmonary Hypertension: Progressive narrowing of pulmonary arteries leads to increased pressure, right heart strain, and ultimately right heart failure, significantly impacting prognosis, particularly beyond 5 years.
  • Interstitial Lung Disease (ILD): Fibrosis of the lung parenchyma causes progressive breathlessness and reduced lung function, often developing within the first few years of disease onset.
  • Scleroderma Renal Crisis: A medical emergency characterised by acute onset severe hypertension and rapidly progressive renal failure, typically occurring early in the disease course (within 3-5 years) in patients with diffuse cutaneous SSc.
  • Gastrointestinal Dysmotility: Fibrosis and atrophy of smooth muscle in the oesophagus, stomach, and intestines can lead to dysphagia, reflux, malabsorption, and pseudo-obstruction, causing significant nutritional deficits.
  • Digital Ulcers and Amputation: Severe Raynaud phenomenon and microvascular damage can lead to chronic, painful digital ulcers, which are prone to infection and may necessitate amputation if not managed aggressively.
