Myasthenia gravis [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Myasthenia gravis"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Myasthenia gravis"][/sc]
  • Myasthenia gravis (MG) is a chronic autoimmune disorder affecting the neuromuscular junction, characterised by fluctuating muscle weakness and fatigue. It results from autoantibodies (most commonly anti-acetylcholine receptor, AChR, or anti-muscle-specific kinase, MuSK) that impair neuromuscular transmission.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical features of myasthenia gravis, differentiate it from other causes of muscle weakness, and understand the principles of its acute and long-term management, including myasthenic crisis.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Myasthenia gravis"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Myasthenia gravis"][/sc]
  • The prevalence of myasthenia gravis in the UK is estimated to be around 15 per 100,000 population [1]. Incidence rates are approximately 1.1 per 100,000 population per year [2].
  • MG is more common in women than men, with symptoms typically starting in women under 40 years and men over 60 years [3].
  • Risk factors include other autoimmune conditions (e.g., thyroid disease, rheumatoid arthritis, lupus) and thymic abnormalities such as thymoma or thymic hyperplasia [1].
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Myasthenia gravis"][/sc]
  • Trigger: Autoantibodies, primarily against the acetylcholine receptor (AChR) or muscle-specific kinase (MuSK) at the neuromuscular junction, are produced, often initiated by thymic dysfunction [1].
  • Progression: These antibodies lead to the destruction or functional blockade of AChRs, reducing the number of available receptors for acetylcholine. This impairs signal transmission from nerve to muscle, causing muscle weakness that worsens with sustained activity [1]. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Neuromuscular junction: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Neuromuscular junction"][/sc]
  • Consequence: The fluctuating weakness affects voluntary muscles, particularly those of the eyes, face, throat, and limbs, leading to symptoms such as ptosis, diplopia, dysphagia, and fatigable limb weakness [1].
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Myasthenia gravis"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Symptoms often begin insidiously, typically with ocular manifestations like ptosis and diplopia, which may fluctuate daily or even hourly. This phase can last for weeks to months [1].
  • Progressive phase: Weakness often spreads to other muscle groups, including facial, bulbar (swallowing, speech), and limb muscles, particularly within the first 2-3 years of disease onset. Approximately 50% of patients with ocular MG develop generalised MG within 2 years [1].
  • Late phase: The disease course is variable, but with treatment, many patients achieve stable remission or minimal symptoms. However, some may experience chronic fluctuating weakness or severe exacerbations, such as myasthenic crisis, particularly during periods of stress, infection, or medication changes [1].
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Myasthenia gravis"][/sc]
  • Ask about fluctuating muscle weakness - this is the hallmark symptom, worsening with activity and improving with rest, which is crucial for distinguishing MG from other neurological conditions.
  • Ask about ocular symptoms (e.g., diplopia, ptosis) - these are often the initial presenting symptoms and can be isolated to the eyes (ocular myasthenia).
  • Ask about bulbar symptoms (e.g., dysphagia, dysarthria, hoarseness) - these indicate involvement of throat and facial muscles, raising concern for airway compromise.
  • Ask about limb weakness - typically proximal and asymmetrical, worsening with repetitive movements (e.g., combing hair, climbing stairs).
  • Ask about respiratory difficulty - this is a critical symptom, as it can indicate impending myasthenic crisis and respiratory failure.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Myasthenia gravis"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - particularly respiratory rate and effort, as tachypnoea or shallow breathing can indicate respiratory muscle weakness and impending crisis.
  • Check for fatigable ptosis - sustained upward gaze or repetitive blinking can exacerbate eyelid drooping. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Fatigable ptosis: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Fatigable ptosis"][/sc]
  • Check for ophthalmoplegia - impaired eye movements that worsen with sustained gaze, often asymmetrical and non-conforming to single nerve palsies.
  • Check bulbar muscle strength - assess speech (dysarthria), swallowing (dysphagia), and facial weakness (e.g., "myasthenic sneer").
  • Check limb muscle strength - assess for proximal weakness that fatigues with repetitive movements (e.g., repeated arm abduction or knee extension).
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Myasthenia gravis"][/sc]
  • Respiratory distress: Rapidly progressive shortness of breath, shallow breathing, or inability to clear secretions, indicating respiratory muscle weakness and impending myasthenic crisis.
  • Severe dysphagia: Difficulty swallowing leading to choking, aspiration, or inability to manage oral secretions, posing a significant risk of aspiration pneumonia.
  • Bulbar weakness: Acute worsening of speech or facial weakness, which can rapidly progress to compromise airway protection and ventilation.
  • Rapidly progressive generalised weakness: Worsening weakness affecting multiple muscle groups over hours to days, suggesting an escalating disease severity that requires urgent intervention.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Myasthenia gravis"][/sc]
  • Ocular Myasthenia: Weakness confined to the extraocular muscles, causing ptosis and diplopia without generalised weakness. Approximately 50% of these patients may progress to generalised MG within 2 years [1].
  • Myasthenia in pregnancy: MG can fluctuate during pregnancy, with potential exacerbations in the first trimester or postpartum period. Neonatal myasthenia can occur in infants born to mothers with MG due to transplacental passage of antibodies [1].
  • Myasthenia in older adults: Often presents with more severe bulbar and respiratory symptoms, and may be associated with thymoma. Diagnosis can be delayed due to attribution of symptoms to age-related changes [1]. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Thymoma: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Thymoma"][/sc]
  • MuSK-positive Myasthenia Gravis: Patients with anti-MuSK antibodies often present with prominent bulbar and neck extensor weakness, and may respond differently to treatments compared to AChR-positive MG [1].
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Myasthenia gravis"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected Myasthenia gravis:
    • Acetylcholine receptor (AChR) antibody testing: Highly sensitive and specific for generalised MG (85% positive), less so for ocular MG. Cell-based assays offer better sensitivity [1].
    • Muscle-specific kinase (MuSK) antibody testing: Performed if AChR antibodies are negative, positive in about 10% of generalised MG cases [1].
    • Thyroid function tests: To rule out associated autoimmune thyroid disease [1].
  • Severity/risk tools: Myasthenia Gravis Activities of Daily Living (MG-ADL) score and Myasthenia Gravis Composite score are used for objective assessment of disease severity and treatment response. MG-ADL on MDCalc, MG Composite Score on MDCalc.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible:
    • Neurophysiology (repetitive nerve stimulation, single-fibre electromyography): Can confirm neuromuscular transmission defect, especially in seronegative cases [1].
    • Brain and orbital MRI: To exclude structural intracranial or orbital disease if ocular symptoms are prominent and antibody tests are negative [1].
  • Imaging - Chest CT/MRI: To rule out thymoma or thymic hyperplasia, recommended for all patients with suspected MG [1].
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Myasthenia gravis"][/sc]
  • A 45-year-old woman presents with a 3-month history of fluctuating double vision and eyelid drooping, worsening towards the end of the day, alongside increasing difficulty climbing stairs.
  • A middle-aged woman with fatigable ocular and proximal limb weakness, concerning for an autoimmune neuromuscular junction disorder.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Myasthenia gravis"][/sc]
  • Lambert-Eaton Myasthenic Syndrome (LEMS) - often associated with small cell lung cancer, presents with proximal muscle weakness that improves with activity, and absent or diminished reflexes. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Lambert-Eaton Myasthenic Syndrome: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Lambert-Eaton Myasthenic Syndrome"][/sc]
  • Botulism - acute onset of descending paralysis, often with prominent bulbar symptoms and autonomic dysfunction, typically linked to toxin exposure.
  • Stroke - sudden onset of focal neurological deficits, usually unilateral, without the fatigability characteristic of MG.
  • Guillain-Barré Syndrome (GBS) - acute ascending flaccid paralysis with areflexia, often preceded by an infection.
  • Motor neurone disease (MND) - progressive muscle weakness, wasting, and fasciculations, typically without sensory involvement or fluctuating fatigue.
  • Thyroid eye disease - proptosis, lid lag, and restrictive ophthalmopathy, but usually without generalised fatigable weakness.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Myasthenia gravis"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Assess airway, breathing, and circulation, particularly in patients with bulbar or respiratory weakness. Escalate to senior medical staff and consider intensive care unit admission for myasthenic crisis.
  • Definitive management:
    • Symptomatic treatment: Pyridostigmine (acetylcholinesterase inhibitor) is often used to improve muscle strength [1].
    • Immunosuppression: Corticosteroids (e.g., prednisolone) are typically initiated for moderate to severe symptoms. Steroid-sparing agents (e.g., azathioprine, mycophenolate mofetil) are often introduced for long-term management [1].
    • Thymectomy: Considered for patients with generalised AChR-positive MG, especially those under 65 years, and for all patients with thymoma [1].
  • Acute exacerbations/Myasthenic crisis: Often warrants plasma exchange (PLEX) or intravenous immunoglobulin (IVIG) for rapid improvement in severe weakness or respiratory compromise [1].
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Regular clinical assessment using MG-ADL or MG Composite scores, monitoring for treatment side effects, and adjusting immunosuppression as needed.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Patients should be educated on recognising worsening symptoms, particularly respiratory or bulbar weakness, and advised on when to seek urgent medical attention.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Myasthenia gravis"][/sc]
  • Myasthenic crisis: Severe worsening of muscle weakness, particularly affecting respiratory and bulbar muscles, leading to respiratory failure requiring mechanical ventilation. This is a life-threatening emergency. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Myasthenic crisis triggers: what are they and why do they matter clinically?" text="Myasthenic crisis triggers"][/sc]
  • Cholinergic crisis: Caused by excessive acetylcholine due to overmedication with acetylcholinesterase inhibitors (e.g., pyridostigmine), leading to increased muscle weakness, fasciculations, and autonomic symptoms.
  • Aspiration pneumonia: Due to severe dysphagia and impaired airway protection, increasing the risk of lung infection.
  • Treatment-related side effects: Long-term corticosteroid use can lead to osteoporosis, diabetes, hypertension, and increased infection risk. Immunosuppressants also carry risks of infection and other adverse effects.
  • Thymoma-related complications: Thymomas can be malignant and may require surgical resection, with potential complications related to surgery.
