Delirium [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Delirium"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Delirium"][/sc]
  • Delirium is an acute neuropsychiatric syndrome characterised by a disturbance in attention, awareness, and cognition, developing over a short period (hours to days) and fluctuating in severity.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise, assess, and initiate management for patients presenting with acute confusion or delirium, differentiating it from other cognitive impairments like dementia.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Delirium"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Delirium"][/sc]
  • Delirium complicates approximately 10% of all hospital admissions in the UK, with higher rates in older adults and intensive care settings [1] [2].
  • Point prevalence in the general population is estimated at 7.2%, but can be as high as 15% in older adults using screening tools like the 4AT [3] [4].
  • Key risk factors include advanced age, pre-existing cognitive impairment (e.g., dementia), multiple comorbidities, polypharmacy, and sensory impairment.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Delirium"][/sc]
  • Delirium is triggered by an acute insult (e.g., infection, metabolic disturbance, medication) leading to widespread cerebral dysfunction.
  • Progression involves complex neurobiological changes, including neuroinflammation, neurotransmitter imbalances (e.g., acetylcholine deficiency, dopamine excess), and impaired neuronal network connectivity.
  • The consequence is a state of acute brain failure, manifesting as disturbed attention, awareness, and cognitive function, which is often reversible with prompt identification and treatment of the underlying cause.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Delirium"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Symptoms typically develop acutely over hours to a few days, often with a clear precipitating event.
  • Progressive phase: The course is fluctuating, with symptoms waxing and waning throughout the day, particularly beyond the initial 24-48 hours.
  • Late phase: Resolution usually occurs within days to weeks once the underlying cause is addressed, though some patients, especially those with pre-existing cognitive impairment, may experience persistent symptoms or cognitive decline.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Delirium"][/sc]
  • Ask about acute changes in attention – patients may struggle to focus, be easily distracted, or have difficulty following conversations, indicating core diagnostic features.
  • Ask about fluctuating levels of consciousness or awareness – periods of lucidity interspersed with confusion are highly characteristic and differentiate from dementia.
  • Ask about perceptual disturbances – visual or auditory hallucinations (e.g., seeing insects, hearing voices) often suggest a hyperactive subtype.
  • Ask about altered sleep-wake cycle – patients may be drowsy during the day and agitated at night, disrupting normal circadian rhythms.
  • Ask about emotional lability – rapid shifts between anxiety, fear, irritability, or apathy are common and can be distressing for patients and carers.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Delirium"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs – fever, tachycardia, hypotension, or hypoxia may indicate an underlying infection, dehydration, or cardiorespiratory compromise.
  • Check for psychomotor changes – patients may exhibit hyperactive (agitation, restlessness, aggression) or hypoactive (lethargy, reduced movement, withdrawn) states.
  • Check cognitive function – formal assessment (e.g., 4AT, CAM) reveals impaired attention, disorientation, memory deficits, and disorganised thinking.
  • Check neurological signs – focal neurological deficits are typically absent in pure delirium, but their presence may suggest an alternative or co-existing pathology.
  • Check for signs of underlying causes – e.g., signs of infection (localised tenderness, rash), dehydration (dry mucous membranes), or medication side effects.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Delirium"][/sc]
  • Acute onset and fluctuating course – a sudden change in mental status over hours to days, with symptoms varying throughout the day, strongly indicates delirium rather than chronic cognitive decline.
  • Inattention – inability to focus, sustain, or shift attention is the cardinal feature of delirium and a critical diagnostic criterion.
  • New focal neurological signs – while not typical of delirium itself, their presence necessitates urgent investigation for acute neurological events such as stroke or intracranial pathology.
  • Severe agitation or aggression – may indicate a hyperactive delirium requiring urgent de-escalation and management to prevent harm to the patient or others.
  • Rapid deterioration in physical health – a sudden decline in physiological parameters alongside confusion suggests a severe underlying medical emergency.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Delirium"][/sc]
  • Hypoactive delirium: Often missed, particularly in older adults, presenting as quiet withdrawal, lethargy, reduced movement, and apathy rather than agitation. This can be mistaken for depression or fatigue.
  • Delirium superimposed on dementia: Patients with pre-existing dementia have a lower cognitive reserve and are highly susceptible to delirium from minor insults. The acute change from their baseline is key.
  • Post-operative delirium: Common after surgery, especially in older patients, presenting with confusion, agitation, or somnolence in the recovery period.
  • Terminal delirium: Occurs in the last days or weeks of life, often resistant to treatment, and requires careful symptom management and communication with families.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Delirium"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected Delirium: A comprehensive "delirium screen" including full blood count (FBC), urea and electrolytes (U&Es), liver function tests (LFTs), C-reactive protein (CRP), thyroid function tests (TFTs), calcium, glucose, B12/folate, urine dipstick and culture, chest X-ray, and blood cultures.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Consider further investigations such as an electrocardiogram (ECG) for cardiac causes, arterial blood gas (ABG) for respiratory failure or metabolic acidosis, or toxicology screen if substance intoxication/withdrawal is suspected.
  • Imaging - CT head: Not routinely required for every confused patient, but indicated if there are new focal neurological signs, head injury, sudden onset severe headache, or suspicion of intracranial pathology.
  • Serial reassessment: Regular use of a validated screening tool such as the 4AT (4 A\'s Test) or Confusion Assessment Method (CAM) to monitor for the presence and severity of delirium.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Delirium"][/sc]
  • An 82-year-old woman, usually independent, presents with a 2-day history of acute confusion, fluctuating between periods of agitation and drowsiness, following a recent urinary tract infection.
  • An elderly patient with acute-on-chronic cognitive impairment, exhibiting waxing and waning attention and altered psychomotor activity, likely secondary to an underlying physiological stressor.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Delirium"][/sc]
  • Dementia – insidious onset, chronic progressive course, attention often preserved until late stages.
  • Depression – typically preserved attention, often associated with low mood and anhedonia, less fluctuation in cognitive state.
  • Psychosis – primary thought disorder, often with preserved consciousness and attention, though content may be disorganised.
  • Non-convulsive status epilepticus – can present with fluctuating consciousness and confusion, requiring EEG for diagnosis.
  • Acute stroke – often presents with focal neurological deficits, though can cause global confusion, especially in posterior circulation strokes.
  • Subdural haematoma – can cause fluctuating consciousness and cognitive impairment, particularly in older adults after minor head trauma.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Delirium"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Identify and treat the underlying cause (e.g., antibiotics for infection, rehydration for dehydration, review and cease culprit medications). Ensure patient safety, manage agitation non-pharmacologically where possible, and provide a calm, reorienting environment.
  • Definitive management: Address the specific physiological derangement causing the delirium. This may involve optimising oxygenation, correcting electrolyte imbalances, managing pain, or treating organ failure.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Regular reassessment of cognitive status and vital signs. Post-delirium cognitive assessment and support for patients and carers are crucial, as there is a risk of long-term cognitive impairment.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: If delirium persists or worsens despite initial management, or if new red flags emerge, escalate care to senior medical staff. Provide clear safety-netting advice to carers regarding signs of deterioration.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Delirium"][/sc]
  • Increased mortality: Delirium is an independent predictor of increased short-term and long-term mortality, often due to the severity of the underlying illness and associated complications.
  • Prolonged hospital stay: Patients with delirium typically have longer hospital admissions, increasing healthcare costs and exposure to nosocomial complications.
  • Functional decline: Many patients experience a decline in their activities of daily living (ADLs) and instrumental ADLs (IADLs) post-delirium, often requiring increased care support.
  • Long-term cognitive impairment: Delirium can lead to new-onset or accelerated cognitive decline, even in those without pre-existing dementia, impacting quality of life.
  • Increased risk of institutionalisation: Due to functional and cognitive decline, patients are at a higher risk of requiring long-term care placement after an episode of delirium.
