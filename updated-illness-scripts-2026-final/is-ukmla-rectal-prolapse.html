Rectal prolapse [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Rectal prolapse"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Rectal prolapse"][/sc]
  • Rectal prolapse is the protrusion of the rectal wall through the anus, which can be partial (mucosal) or full-thickness.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical presentation and initial management of rectal prolapse, distinguishing it from haemorrhoids.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Rectal prolapse"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Rectal prolapse"][/sc]
  • More common in older women (over 50 years) with a history of multiple vaginal deliveries or chronic constipation, with a female-to-male ratio of approximately 6:1.
  • Incidence is estimated to be around 2.5 per 100,000 population per year in the UK, with a peak incidence in the seventh decade of life.
  • Risk factors include chronic straining, previous pelvic surgery, neurological conditions (e.g., spinal cord injury, multiple sclerosis), and anatomical defects of the pelvic floor.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Rectal prolapse"][/sc]
  • Trigger: Weakening of the pelvic floor muscles and ligaments, often exacerbated by chronic straining during defecation or childbirth, leads to inadequate support for the rectum.
  • Progression: The loss of anatomical support allows the rectum to telescope into itself and protrude through the anal canal, particularly during increased intra-abdominal pressure.
  • Consequence: This results in symptoms such as a visible mass, faecal incontinence, constipation, and discomfort, potentially leading to mucosal ulceration or strangulation.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Rectal prolapse"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Intermittent protrusion of rectal tissue, often reducible spontaneously or manually, particularly after defecation.
  • Progressive phase: Prolapse becomes more frequent, larger, and requires manual reduction, or may remain prolapsed for longer durations, particularly beyond a few weeks.
  • Late phase: Persistent, irreducible prolapse with associated complications such as ulceration, bleeding, or strangulation, particularly beyond several months without intervention.
