Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Venous insufficiency including varicose veins"][/sc]
  • Venous insufficiency is a condition where venous blood flow is impaired, leading to venous hypertension, often manifesting as varicose veins, skin changes, and potentially ulceration.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical features of chronic venous insufficiency and varicose veins, understand their pathophysiology, and outline initial management strategies including when to refer for specialist assessment.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Venous insufficiency including varicose veins"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Venous insufficiency including varicose veins"][/sc]
  • Visible varicose veins in the lower limbs are estimated to affect at least a third of the UK population [1].
  • Chronic venous insufficiency (CVI) affects approximately 10-20% of adults in the UK, with prevalence increasing with age [2].
  • Risk factors include increasing age, female sex, pregnancy, family history, obesity, prolonged standing, and previous deep vein thrombosis [1].
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Venous insufficiency including varicose veins"][/sc]
  • Trigger: Incompetence of venous valves, often due to congenital weakness or damage from thrombosis, leads to retrograde blood flow and increased pressure in the superficial venous system.
  • Progression: Sustained venous hypertension causes remodelling of the vein wall, leading to dilation and tortuosity (varicose veins), and impairs microcirculation in the skin.
  • Consequence: Extravasation of red blood cells and fluid into the interstitial space results in haemosiderin deposition (pigmentation), inflammation, lipodermatosclerosis, and ultimately venous ulceration. Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Venous insufficiency including varicose veins"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Symptoms such as aching, heaviness, and itching may develop gradually over months to years, often exacerbated by prolonged standing.
  • Progressive phase: Visible varicose veins and early skin changes like pigmentation or eczema may appear over several years, indicating worsening venous hypertension.
  • Late phase: If untreated, chronic venous insufficiency can lead to severe skin changes, lipodermatosclerosis, and the development of venous leg ulcers, which can persist for months or years.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Venous insufficiency including varicose veins"][/sc]
  • Ask about aching or heaviness in the legs - typically worse at the end of the day or after prolonged standing, relieved by elevation.
  • Ask about itching or discomfort - often associated with venous eczema, indicating skin inflammation due to venous hypertension.
  • Ask about swelling in the ankles or lower legs - usually pitting oedema, worse during the day and improving overnight, suggesting fluid extravasation.
  • Ask about night cramps or restless legs - common non-specific symptoms that can be associated with venous insufficiency.
  • Ask about skin changes - such as pigmentation (haemosiderin deposition), hardening of the skin (lipodermatosclerosis), or previous ulceration.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Venous insufficiency including varicose veins"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - usually normal, but important to exclude systemic causes of oedema or infection if ulcerated.
  • Check for visible dilated, tortuous superficial veins (varicose veins) - most commonly in the lower limbs, indicating venous reflux.
  • Check for skin changes in the 'gaiter area' (above the ankle) - including haemosiderin pigmentation (brown discolouration), venous eczema (red, scaly, itchy skin), lipodermatosclerosis (inverted champagne bottle appearance), or atrophie blanche. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Atrophie blanche: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Atrophie blanche"][/sc]
  • Check for oedema - typically pitting oedema of the ankle and lower leg, often unilateral or asymmetrical.
  • Check for active or healed venous leg ulcers - usually shallow, irregular, and located above the medial malleolus.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Venous insufficiency including varicose veins"][/sc]
  • Sudden onset severe leg pain and swelling: May indicate acute deep vein thrombosis (DVT), requiring urgent investigation and anticoagulation.
  • Rapidly expanding haematoma or active bleeding from a varicose vein: Requires immediate compression and urgent surgical review to prevent significant blood loss.
  • Signs of infection around a venous ulcer (increasing pain, redness, warmth, purulent discharge, fever): Suggests cellulitis or infected ulcer, necessitating antibiotic treatment.
  • Unilateral leg swelling with pain and discolouration, particularly if associated with systemic symptoms: Could indicate a more serious underlying pathology such as malignancy or systemic inflammatory condition.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Venous insufficiency including varicose veins"][/sc]
  • Older adults: May present with less prominent symptoms due to reduced activity levels, but more severe skin changes and ulceration.
  • Obesity: Increased intra-abdominal pressure can exacerbate venous hypertension, and adipose tissue can obscure visible varicose veins, making diagnosis challenging.
  • Pregnancy: Hormonal changes and increased uterine pressure can lead to new onset or worsening of varicose veins, often resolving postpartum but may persist.
  • Post-thrombotic syndrome: Previous DVT can lead to valve damage and chronic venous insufficiency, presenting with symptoms similar to primary varicose veins but with a different aetiology.   • Severity/risk tools: CEAP (Clinical, Etiological, Anatomical, Pathophysiological) classification system is used to grade the severity of chronic venous disease. Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Venous insufficiency including varicose veins"][/sc]
  • Peripheral arterial disease: Typically presents with claudication, cold limbs, and arterial ulcers (punched-out, painful), contrasting with venous symptoms.
  • Deep vein thrombosis (DVT): Acute onset of unilateral leg pain, swelling, and warmth, requiring urgent investigation and treatment.
  • Cellulitis: Acute bacterial infection of the skin, characterised by rapidly spreading erythema, warmth, pain, and often fever.
  • Lymphoedema: Non-pitting oedema, often affecting the foot and toes, typically chronic and progressive, without prominent varicose veins.
  • Cardiac oedema (e.g., heart failure): Bilateral, symmetrical pitting oedema, often associated with dyspnoea and other signs of cardiac dysfunction.
  • Renal oedema (e.g., nephrotic syndrome): Generalised oedema, often periorbital, associated with proteinuria and other signs of renal impairment.
  • Baker\'s cyst rupture: Acute calf pain and swelling, mimicking DVT, but often with a history of knee pathology. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Baker\'s cyst rupture: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Baker\'s cyst rupture"][/sc]
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Venous insufficiency including varicose veins"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Recognition of complications like active bleeding or acute DVT, and appropriate escalation to emergency services or vascular specialists.
  • Conservative management: Lifestyle advice including weight loss, regular exercise, leg elevation, and compression hosiery for symptom relief [1].
  • Definitive management: Referral to a vascular service for assessment and interventional treatment for symptomatic varicose veins with truncal reflux. Options include endothermal ablation (e.g., EVLA), ultrasound-guided foam sclerotherapy, or surgery [1].
  • Adjunctive management: Management of venous eczema with topical corticosteroids and emollients; wound care for venous ulcers.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Regular review to assess symptom control, progression of disease, and healing of ulcers.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Advise patients to seek medical attention for sudden worsening of pain, swelling, new bleeding, or signs of infection.
  • Exam pharmacology reference (UKMLA/MRCGP): Anticoagulants (e.g., low molecular weight heparin, direct oral anticoagulants) may be considered for superficial vein thrombosis extending towards the deep venous system. Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Venous insufficiency including varicose veins"][/sc]
  • Venous leg ulceration: Chronic, non-healing wounds, typically in the gaiter area, resulting from severe, long-standing venous hypertension and microcirculatory dysfunction.
  • Venous eczema: Inflammatory skin condition due to chronic venous hypertension, leading to itching, redness, and scaling, which can predispose to infection.
  • Lipodermatosclerosis: Progressive hardening and fibrosis of the skin and subcutaneous tissue in the lower leg, often leading to an 'inverted champagne bottle' appearance, indicating severe chronic venous insufficiency.
  • Superficial thrombophlebitis: Inflammation and thrombosis of a superficial varicose vein, presenting as a painful, red, palpable cord, which can extend to the deep venous system.
  • Haemorrhage from ruptured varicose veins: Can occur from minor trauma to thin-walled superficial veins, potentially leading to significant blood loss.
  • Deep vein thrombosis (DVT): Although varicose veins are a risk factor for DVT, DVT itself can also cause or exacerbate venous insufficiency (post-thrombotic syndrome).
