Syphilis [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Syphilis"]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Syphilis"]
  • Syphilis is a systemic sexually transmitted infection (STI) caused by the bacterium Treponema pallidum, characterized by distinct clinical stages if left untreated.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: To recognize the varied clinical presentations of syphilis across its stages, understand its transmission, and identify appropriate diagnostic and management pathways to prevent long-term complications and onward transmission.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Syphilis"]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Syphilis"]
  • In England, there were 9,535 diagnoses of early-stage syphilis in 2024, a 2% rise from 2023, indicating a concerning upward trend [6] [11].
  • Men who have sex with men (MSM) continue to be disproportionately affected, though heterosexual transmission is also increasing [1] [8].
  • Congenital syphilis remains a significant public health concern, with 24 cases identified in England between 2015 and 2020, often linked to inadequate maternal screening and treatment [3].
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Syphilis"]
  • Infection is initiated when Treponema pallidum penetrates mucous membranes or abraded skin, typically during sexual contact, leading to local replication at the inoculation site [7] [8].
  • The spirochetes disseminate haematogenously and lymphatically throughout the body, leading to systemic infection and the varied manifestations of secondary syphilis. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Treponema pallidum: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Treponema pallidum"] [9] [10].
  • Persistent infection, particularly if untreated, can lead to chronic inflammation and tissue destruction in various organ systems, characteristic of tertiary syphilis [4].
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Syphilis"]
  • Early phase: Primary syphilis typically manifests 10-90 days (average 21 days) post-exposure with a painless chancre at the site of inoculation, which heals spontaneously within 3-6 weeks [12] [13] [14]. Secondary syphilis, characterized by systemic symptoms and a mucocutaneous rash, often appears 2-12 weeks after the chancre emerges or as it heals [15].
  • Progressive phase: Following secondary syphilis, individuals enter a latent stage, which can be early latent (within 1 year of infection) or late latent (more than 1 year). This phase is asymptomatic but serologically reactive, and can last for years [12].
  • Late phase: Approximately 10-30 years after initial infection, about 15-30% of untreated individuals may progress to tertiary syphilis, involving severe complications such as gummatous, cardiovascular, or neurosyphilis [12] [16].
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Syphilis"]
  • Ask about painless genital or oral sores - to identify a primary chancre, which is often overlooked due to its lack of discomfort [17].
  • Ask about a widespread rash, particularly on palms and soles - a hallmark of secondary syphilis, crucial for early diagnosis and preventing progression [18] [19].
  • Ask about flu-like symptoms (fever, sore throat, malaise, lymphadenopathy) - these non-specific symptoms often accompany the rash in secondary syphilis and can be easily misattributed [20].
  • Ask about neurological symptoms (headache, visual changes, hearing loss, altered mental status) - to screen for neurosyphilis, which can occur at any stage but is a serious complication [16].
  • Ask about a history of unprotected sexual contact - essential for risk assessment and guiding diagnostic testing [8].
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Syphilis"]
  • Check for a painless, indurated ulcer (chancre) - typically found at the site of inoculation (genitals, anus, mouth), characteristic of primary syphilis [17].
  • Check for a maculopapular rash, including palms and soles - a classic sign of secondary syphilis, which may be faint and non-itchy [18] [19].
  • Check for condylomata lata - moist, wart-like lesions in intertriginous areas (e.g., perineum, groin), indicative of secondary syphilis. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Condylomata lata: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Condylomata lata"] [21] [22].
  • Check for generalized lymphadenopathy - often present in secondary syphilis, reflecting systemic immune activation [20].
  • Check vital signs - particularly for fever, which can be present in secondary syphilis, indicating systemic illness.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Syphilis"]
  • Neurological symptoms: New onset headache, visual disturbances, hearing loss, or altered mental status may indicate neurosyphilis, requiring urgent investigation and treatment to prevent irreversible damage [16].
  • Ocular symptoms: Any sudden vision changes, pain, or redness in the eye can signify ocular syphilis, which can lead to permanent blindness if not promptly managed [16].
  • Pregnancy: Syphilis in pregnancy poses a significant risk of congenital syphilis, leading to severe fetal and neonatal complications including stillbirth, prematurity, and long-term developmental issues [23] [24].
  • Immunosuppression: Individuals with HIV or other immunosuppressive conditions may have accelerated disease progression or atypical presentations, necessitating a heightened index of suspicion and prompt intervention [25].
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Syphilis"]
  • Immunocompromised individuals (e.g., HIV): May present with more aggressive disease, rapid progression to neurosyphilis, or unusual dermatological manifestations, making diagnosis challenging [25].
  • Older adults: Symptoms may be subtle or misattributed to other age-related conditions, leading to delayed diagnosis and treatment [26].
  • Ocular syphilis: Can present with isolated visual changes (e.g., uveitis, optic neuritis) without other classic signs of syphilis, requiring specific ophthalmological examination [16].
  • Otosyphilis: Manifests as sudden sensorineural hearing loss, tinnitus, or vertigo, often without other systemic symptoms, necessitating audiological assessment [16].
  • Congenital syphilis: Can range from asymptomatic at birth to severe multi-system involvement (e.g., hepatosplenomegaly, rash, bone abnormalities) in neonates, depending on the timing of maternal infection and treatment. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Congenital syphilis: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Congenital syphilis"] [3] [23].
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Syphilis"]
  • Core for suspected syphilis: Serological testing involves a two-step approach: initial screening with a treponemal test (e.g., EIA, CLIA, TPPA, FTA-ABS) followed by a non-treponemal test (e.g., VDRL, RPR) for confirmation and monitoring disease activity. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="VDRL/TPPA/FTA-ABS: what are they and why do they matter clinically?" text="VDRL/TPPA/FTA-ABS"] [27] [28] [29].
  • Direct detection: Dark-field microscopy of chancre exudate can directly visualize Treponema pallidum, particularly useful in primary syphilis before seroconversion [27].
  • Neurosyphilis investigation: Lumbar puncture for cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) analysis (cell count, protein, VDRL) is indicated in patients with neurological symptoms or treatment failure [27] [30].
  • Serial reassessment: Quantitative non-treponemal tests (VDRL/RPR) are used to monitor treatment response, with a four-fold decrease in titre indicating successful therapy [27].
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Syphilis"]
  • A 35-year-old male presents with a painless genital ulcer and a widespread non-itchy rash on his trunk, palms, and soles, developed over the past two months, following unprotected sexual contact.
  • Young adult with mucocutaneous lesions and systemic symptoms, concerning for a sexually transmitted infection, specifically secondary syphilis.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Syphilis"]
  • Genital herpes: Painful, vesicular lesions that ulcerate, unlike the typically painless chancre of primary syphilis.
  • Chancroid: Painful genital ulcers with associated suppurative lymphadenopathy, distinct from syphilis.
  • Lymphogranuloma venereum (LGV): Transient, often unnoticed genital lesion followed by painful inguinal lymphadenopathy.
  • Pityriasis rosea: A self-limiting rash that can mimic secondary syphilis but typically spares palms and soles.
  • Drug eruption: Can cause widespread rashes, but usually has a clear temporal association with medication exposure.
  • HIV seroconversion illness: Presents with flu-like symptoms and a rash, but serological tests for syphilis would be negative.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Syphilis"]
  • Immediate priorities: Prompt diagnosis and initiation of appropriate antibiotic therapy are crucial to prevent disease progression and onward transmission. Public health notification and contact tracing are also essential.
  • Definitive management: Penicillin G is the treatment of choice for all stages of syphilis. The dosage and duration vary depending on the stage of infection (e.g., single dose for early syphilis, longer courses for late latent or neurosyphilis) [31].
  • Adjunctive management: Management of Jarisch-Herxheimer reaction (a self-limiting febrile reaction after initial treatment) with antipyretics may be considered.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Regular serological testing (quantitative non-treponemal tests) is essential to monitor treatment response and detect re-infection. Clinical follow-up is also important to assess for resolution of symptoms and signs.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Patients with neurosyphilis, ocular syphilis, or syphilis in pregnancy require specialist management. Education on safe sexual practices and partner notification is vital.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Syphilis"]
  • Neurosyphilis: Can occur at any stage but is more common in late syphilis, leading to meningitis, stroke, cranial nerve palsies (e.g., Argyll Robertson pupil), or general paresis, significantly impacting neurological function. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Argyll Robertson pupil: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Argyll Robertson pupil"] [16].
  • Cardiovascular syphilis: Typically manifests 10-30 years after initial infection, primarily affecting the aorta (e.g., aortitis, aneurysm formation), leading to serious cardiac complications [16].
  • Gummatous syphilis: Characterized by granulomatous lesions (gummas) that can affect any organ, leading to tissue destruction and dysfunction, often seen in late tertiary syphilis. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Gummatous syphilis: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Gummatous syphilis"] [16].
  • Congenital syphilis: If untreated during pregnancy, can result in miscarriage, stillbirth, prematurity, or severe multi-system disease in the neonate, with long-term developmental consequences [3] [23].
  • Ocular and Otosyphilis: Can cause permanent vision loss or hearing impairment if not promptly diagnosed and treated, highlighting the importance of early recognition of these specific manifestations [16].
