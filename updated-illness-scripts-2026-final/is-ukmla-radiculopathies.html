Radiculopathies [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Radiculopathies"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Radiculopathies"][/sc]
  • Radiculopathies refer to a set of conditions resulting from compression or irritation of a nerve root as it exits the spinal column, leading to neurological symptoms in the dermatome or myotome supplied by that nerve.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical features and common causes of radiculopathies, distinguishing them from other peripheral neuropathies.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Radiculopathies"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Radiculopathies"][/sc]
  • Cervical radiculopathy has an estimated annual incidence of 83 per 100,000 population in the UK, with lumbar radiculopathy being more common, affecting up to 5% of the population at some point.
  • Risk factors include age-related degenerative changes, occupational activities involving heavy lifting or repetitive movements, and a history of spinal trauma.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Radiculopathies"][/sc]
  • Compression or irritation of a spinal nerve root, often due to disc herniation, osteophytes, or spinal stenosis, acts as the initial trigger.
  • This leads to inflammation, demyelination, and axonal injury of the nerve root, causing impaired nerve conduction and pain.
  • Consequence is a constellation of sensory (pain, numbness, paraesthesia) and motor (weakness, reflex changes) deficits in the distribution of the affected nerve root.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Radiculopathies"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Acute onset of sharp, shooting pain radiating into a limb, often exacerbated by movement or specific postures, typically lasting days to a few weeks.
  • Progressive phase: Persistence of pain, development of sensory deficits (numbness, tingling), and potentially motor weakness, particularly beyond 4-6 weeks if compression is sustained.
  • Late phase: Chronic pain, persistent neurological deficits, and potential muscle atrophy if not adequately managed, particularly beyond 3 months.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Radiculopathies"][/sc]
  • Ask about radiating pain - to determine the dermatomal distribution and potential nerve root involvement.
  • Ask about numbness or tingling - to assess for sensory deficits in the affected dermatome.
  • Ask about muscle weakness - to identify motor deficits and their corresponding myotomal distribution.
  • Ask about aggravating and relieving factors - to understand the mechanical nature of the pain and potential triggers.
  • Ask about bladder or bowel dysfunction - to screen for cauda equina syndrome, a red flag.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Radiculopathies"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - to rule out systemic illness or other acute conditions.
  • Check motor power - to identify myotomal weakness (e.g., deltoid weakness in C5 radiculopathy).
  • Check sensory examination - to map dermatomal sensory loss or altered sensation.
  • Check deep tendon reflexes - to detect diminished or absent reflexes corresponding to the affected nerve root.
  • Check straight leg raise test - a positive test often indicates lumbar nerve root irritation.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Radiculopathies"][/sc]
  • Cauda Equina Syndrome - acute onset of bilateral leg weakness, saddle anaesthesia, and bladder/bowel dysfunction, indicating urgent surgical review.
  • Progressive Neurological Deficit - rapidly worsening motor weakness or sensory loss, suggesting increasing nerve compression.
  • Constitutional Symptoms - unexplained weight loss, fever, night sweats, or history of malignancy, raising suspicion for neoplastic or infectious causes.
  • Trauma - significant recent trauma to the spine, potentially indicating fracture or severe ligamentous injury.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Radiculopathies"][/sc]
  • Older adults: May present with less pronounced pain but more prominent weakness or gait disturbance due to underlying degenerative changes and reduced pain perception.
  • Diabetic neuropathy: Can mimic radiculopathy, requiring careful differentiation based on distribution and associated symptoms.
  • Brachial plexopathy: Can present with similar upper limb symptoms but typically involves multiple nerve roots and a more diffuse pattern of weakness and sensory loss.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Radiculopathies"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected Radiculopathies: Clinical assessment is paramount; imaging is not always immediately required unless red flags are present.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Blood tests such as FBC, ESR, CRP to rule out inflammatory or infectious causes.
  • Imaging - MRI: The gold standard for visualising nerve root compression, disc herniation, or spinal stenosis.
  • Imaging - X-ray: May show degenerative changes but is less useful for soft tissue compression.
  • Serial reassessment: Monitor for resolution or progression of symptoms, especially in conservative management.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Radiculopathies"][/sc]
  • A 45-year-old male presents with a 3-week history of sharp, shooting pain radiating from his lower back down the posterior aspect of his left leg to his foot, exacerbated by sitting and coughing, associated with numbness in his left great toe.
  • A middle-aged man with acute-on-chronic low back pain and unilateral leg pain, associated with dermatomal sensory loss and myotomal weakness, suggestive of nerve root compression.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Radiculopathies"][/sc]
  • Sciatica - often used interchangeably with lumbar radiculopathy, but can also refer to non-radicular causes of leg pain.
  • Peripheral neuropathy - typically presents with a stocking-glove distribution of sensory loss, rather than dermatomal.
  • Spinal stenosis - causes neurogenic claudication, often bilateral, relieved by leaning forward.
  • Piriformis syndrome - buttock pain radiating down the leg, without true nerve root compression.
  • Facet joint arthropathy - localised back pain, often worse with extension, without radicular symptoms.
  • Referred pain from hip pathology - pain typically in the groin or buttock, not following a dermatomal pattern.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Radiculopathies"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Pain control with analgesia, patient education on activity modification, and reassurance regarding prognosis.
  • Definitive management: Conservative management with physiotherapy, exercise, and analgesia is often effective. Surgical intervention (e.g., discectomy) may be considered for persistent symptoms or progressive neurological deficits.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Regular review of symptoms, neurological status, and functional improvement.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Advise patients to seek urgent medical attention for red flag symptoms (e.g., cauda equina, progressive weakness).

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Radiculopathies"][/sc]
  • Chronic pain: Persistent nerve irritation can lead to chronic neuropathic pain, significantly impacting quality of life.
  • Persistent neurological deficit: Prolonged compression can result in irreversible nerve damage, leading to permanent weakness, numbness, or reflex loss.
  • Muscle atrophy: Chronic denervation of muscles can lead to wasting and functional impairment.
  • Cauda Equina Syndrome: A rare but severe complication of lumbar radiculopathy, requiring emergency surgical decompression to prevent permanent neurological damage.
