Hyperkalaemia/ hypokalaemia [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Hyperkalaemia/ hypokalaemia"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Hyperkalaemia/ hypokalaemia"][/sc]
  • Hyperkalaemia is an electrolyte imbalance characterised by a serum potassium concentration exceeding 5.0-5.5 mmol/L. Hypokalaemia is defined as a serum potassium concentration below 3.5 mmol/L. Both conditions can have significant cardiac and neuromuscular consequences.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical features, causes, and initial management of hyperkalaemia and hypokalaemia.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Hyperkalaemia/ hypokalaemia"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Hyperkalaemia/ hypokalaemia"][/sc]
  • In a large UK primary care study, the overall incidence rate of a hyperkalaemic event was 2.9 per 100 person-years, with men more likely to be affected than women [1].
  • The prevalence of hypokalaemia is estimated to be highest among hospitalised individuals, affecting 20–40% of this population [2].
  • Key risk factors for hyperkalaemia include chronic kidney disease, diabetes mellitus, adrenal insufficiency, and the use of medications such as ACE inhibitors and potassium-sparing diuretics [3]. Hypokalaemia is often associated with increased potassium excretion (e.g., gastrointestinal losses, diuretic use) or intracellular shifts, rather than solely dietary deficiency.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Hyperkalaemia/ hypokalaemia"][/sc]
  • Disruptions to potassium homeostasis, whether due to impaired renal excretion, excessive losses, or transcellular shifts, lead to abnormal serum potassium concentrations.
  • These altered potassium levels directly impact the of excitable cells, particularly in cardiac and neuromuscular tissues.
  • Consequently, severe hyperkalaemia can cause cellular inexcitability and life-threatening arrhythmias, whilst profound hypokalaemia results in cellular hyperpolarisation, leading to muscle weakness and cardiac dysrhythmias.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Hyperkalaemia/ hypokalaemia"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Both hyperkalaemia and hypokalaemia can be asymptomatic or present with non-specific symptoms such as mild muscle weakness or fatigue, particularly in gradual onset cases.
  • Progressive phase: As electrolyte derangement worsens, symptoms become more pronounced, including increasing muscle weakness, paraesthesia, and gastrointestinal disturbances. Cardiac manifestations, such as ECG changes, typically emerge in this phase, particularly beyond hours to a few days.
  • Late phase: Severe, uncorrected imbalances can lead to profound neuromuscular dysfunction, flaccid paralysis, and life-threatening cardiac arrhythmias, which can develop rapidly, particularly beyond a few days.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Hyperkalaemia/ hypokalaemia"][/sc]
  • Ask about muscle weakness or fatigue - to assess the severity and progression of electrolyte disturbance, as profound weakness can indicate impending paralysis.
  • Ask about palpitations or chest discomfort - to identify potential cardiac involvement, which is a critical and life-threatening complication of both hyperkalaemia and hypokalaemia.
  • Ask about nausea, vomiting, or abdominal pain - these non-specific gastrointestinal symptoms can accompany significant electrolyte imbalances and may indicate underlying causes or severity.
  • Ask about paraesthesia or numbness - often reported in hyperkalaemia, reflecting altered nerve excitability, and important for differentiating from other neurological causes.
  • Ask about muscle cramps or spasms - more characteristic of hypokalaemia, indicating neuromuscular irritability due to low potassium.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Hyperkalaemia/ hypokalaemia"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - bradycardia and hypotension can be present in severe hyperkalaemia, while hypokalaemia may cause arrhythmias and sometimes hypotension. Respiratory rate may be affected by severe muscle weakness.
  • Check neurological examination (muscle strength and tone, deep tendon reflexes) - flaccid paralysis or reduced reflexes suggest severe electrolyte disturbance, particularly hypokalaemia.
  • Check cardiovascular examination (heart sounds, peripheral pulses) - to identify arrhythmias or signs of cardiac compromise.
  • Check abdominal examination (bowel sounds, distension) - paralytic ileus can occur in severe hypokalaemia.
  • Check - peaked T waves, prolonged PR interval, and widened QRS in hyperkalaemia; U waves, flattened T waves, and prolonged QT interval in hypokalaemia, indicating cardiac electrical instability.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Hyperkalaemia/ hypokalaemia"][/sc]
  • Life-threatening ECG changes: Indicates immediate risk of cardiac arrest and requires urgent stabilisation and treatment.
  • Severe muscle weakness or flaccid paralysis: Suggests profound electrolyte disturbance with potential for respiratory muscle involvement and respiratory failure.
  • Significant cardiac symptoms: Such as new-onset chest pain, severe palpitations, or syncope, warrant urgent cardiac assessment due to the risk of arrhythmias.
  • Rapidly worsening symptoms: A quick deterioration in clinical status necessitates immediate medical attention and intervention.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Hyperkalaemia/ hypokalaemia"][/sc]
  • Older adults: May present with slowly progressive and non-specific symptoms of hyperkalaemia, often due to underlying chronic kidney disease and polypharmacy (e.g., RAS inhibitors). This matters as the insidious onset can delay recognition of a potentially life-threatening condition.
  • Pregnancy: Hypokalaemia can be exacerbated by conditions like hyperemesis gravidarum, or it may be the initial presentation of an underlying renal tubulopathy. This is critical due to the increased risk of severe complications, including arrhythmias and paralysis, for both the mother and the foetus.
  • Immunosuppressed patients (e.g., transplant recipients): Hyperkalaemia is a common complication, particularly in kidney transplant recipients, often linked to immunosuppressive medications like tacrolimus. This matters because these patients are already vulnerable, and hyperkalaemia significantly increases their risk of fatal arrhythmias.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Hyperkalaemia/ hypokalaemia"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected Hyperkalaemia/Hypokalaemia: Serum electrolytes (U&Es) to measure potassium, sodium, creatinine, and urea. A 12-lead ECG is crucial to assess for cardiac manifestations, particularly if serum potassium is ≥6.0 mmol/L for hyperkalaemia or <3.0 mmol/L for hypokalaemia. Consider an arterial blood gas (ABG) to evaluate acid-base status.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: For hypokalaemia, assess urinary potassium excretion (e.g., 24-hour urine collection) to differentiate between renal and extra-renal losses. Further investigations may be guided by clinical suspicion of underlying causes, such as renal artery stenosis or endocrine disorders.
  • Serial reassessment: Regular monitoring of serum potassium levels and repeat ECGs are essential, especially during acute management and treatment, to track response and identify worsening cardiac toxicity.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Hyperkalaemia/ hypokalaemia"][/sc]
  • A 72-year-old male with a history of chronic kidney disease and heart failure presents to the emergency department with a 2-day history of increasing generalised weakness, lethargy, and palpitations. His medications include ramipril and spironolactone. Initial blood tests reveal a serum potassium of 6.8 mmol/L and ECG shows peaked T waves.
  • An elderly male with multiple comorbidities and polypharmacy presenting with acute, severe electrolyte derangement affecting cardiac and neuromuscular function.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Hyperkalaemia/ hypokalaemia"][/sc]
  • Pseudohyperkalaemia - elevated potassium level in a blood sample due to haemolysis during venepuncture or prolonged tourniquet use, not true physiological hyperkalaemia.
  • Acute Kidney Injury (AKI) - often presents with oliguria, elevated creatinine and urea, and is a common cause of true hyperkalaemia due to impaired renal excretion.
  • Diabetic Ketoacidosis (DKA) - can cause hyperkalaemia due to transcellular shift of potassium out of cells in the presence of acidosis, despite total body potassium depletion.
  • Adrenal Insufficiency (Addison's disease) - characterised by hyponatraemia, hyperkalaemia, and metabolic acidosis due to mineralocorticoid deficiency.
  • Diuretic use (e.g., loop or thiazide diuretics) - a common cause of hypokalaemia due to increased renal potassium excretion.
  • Gastrointestinal losses (e.g., severe vomiting or diarrhoea) - can lead to significant hypokalaemia due to direct loss of potassium from the body.
  • Renal Tubular Acidosis (RTA) - specific types of RTA can cause hypokalaemia (e.g., Type 1 and 2) or hyperkalaemia (e.g., Type 4) depending on the tubular defect.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Hyperkalaemia/ hypokalaemia"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities (Hyperkalaemia): Stabilisation of cardiac membranes with intravenous calcium gluconate (if ECG changes are present). Shifting potassium intracellularly using insulin/glucose infusion and salbutamol nebulisation.
  • Immediate priorities (Hypokalaemia): Oral potassium replacement for mild to moderate cases. Intravenous potassium replacement for severe hypokalaemia or those with ECG changes, ensuring careful monitoring to avoid overcorrection.
  • Definitive management: Addressing the underlying cause of the electrolyte imbalance. This may involve adjusting medications (e.g., discontinuing ACE inhibitors or potassium-sparing diuretics in hyperkalaemia, or loop diuretics in hypokalaemia), managing renal dysfunction, or treating gastrointestinal losses. Potassium binders (e.g., sodium zirconium cyclosilicate, patiromer) may be considered for chronic hyperkalaemia.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Serial monitoring of serum potassium and ECGs is essential during acute management. Long-term follow-up involves regular electrolyte checks and management of underlying conditions.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Patients with severe or rapidly worsening electrolyte imbalances, or those with significant ECG changes, require urgent specialist review and admission for intensive monitoring and management. Educate patients on symptoms of recurrence and when to seek medical attention.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Hyperkalaemia/ hypokalaemia"][/sc]
  • Life-threatening cardiac arrhythmias: Both severe hyperkalaemia and hypokalaemia can cause profound disturbances in cardiac electrical activity, leading to ventricular fibrillation, asystole, or other fatal arrhythmias, often rapidly in the late phase.
  • Respiratory failure: Severe muscle weakness or flaccid paralysis, particularly affecting the diaphragm and intercostal muscles, can result from profound electrolyte imbalances, leading to respiratory compromise and failure. This typically occurs in the progressive to late phases.
  • Cardiac arrest: The ultimate and most severe complication of uncorrected, critical hyperkalaemia or hypokalaemia, directly stemming from the life-threatening arrhythmias.
  • Rhabdomyolysis: In severe hypokalaemia, muscle cell damage can occur, leading to the breakdown of muscle tissue and release of myoglobin into the bloodstream, which can cause acute kidney injury.
