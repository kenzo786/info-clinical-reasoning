Chronic glaucoma (Eye) [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Chronic glaucoma"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Chronic glaucoma"][/sc]
  • Chronic glaucoma, most commonly primary open-angle glaucoma (POAG), is a progressive optic neuropathy characterised by insidious peripheral visual field loss and optic disc cupping, often associated with elevated intraocular pressure (IOP) but can occur at normal IOP.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical features, risk factors, and diagnostic approach to chronic glaucoma, and understand the principles of its long-term management to prevent irreversible vision loss.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Chronic glaucoma"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Chronic glaucoma"][/sc]
  • Approximately 1.1 million people (3% of adults over 40) in the UK currently have glaucoma, with projections to reach 1.6 million by 2060 (BMJ Group, 2026).
  • Prevalence increases significantly with age, affecting around 10% of those over 75 years.
  • Risk factors include increasing age, black African or Caribbean ethnicity, family history of glaucoma, myopia, and ocular hypertension.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Chronic glaucoma"][/sc]
  • Elevated intraocular pressure (IOP), often due to impaired aqueous humour outflow through the trabecular meshwork, is a primary trigger, though normal-tension glaucoma exists.
  • Sustained or fluctuating IOP, coupled with other vascular and mechanical factors, leads to progressive damage and remodelling of the optic nerve head, particularly the retinal ganglion cell axons.
  • This results in characteristic optic disc cupping and irreversible loss of peripheral vision, which can advance to central vision loss and blindness if untreated. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Optic disc cupping: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="optic disc cupping"][/sc]
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Chronic glaucoma"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Often asymptomatic, with subtle or undetectable peripheral visual field defects. IOP may be mildly elevated or within normal limits.
  • Progressive phase: Gradual, often unnoticed, worsening of visual field defects, typically sparing central vision until late stages. Optic disc changes become more pronounced.
  • Late phase: Significant and irreversible loss of central vision, leading to severe visual impairment or blindness, particularly if diagnosis and treatment are delayed.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Chronic glaucoma"][/sc]
  • Ask about peripheral vision changes - patients rarely notice early peripheral field loss, often adapting unconsciously until significant damage has occurred.
  • Ask about difficulty with night vision or adapting to dark environments - may indicate early functional impairment.
  • Ask about history of eye pain or discomfort - typically absent in chronic open-angle glaucoma, its presence might suggest other ocular conditions or acute angle closure.
  • Ask about family history of glaucoma - a strong genetic predisposition is a key risk factor, prompting earlier screening.
  • Ask about current and past use of steroid medications (oral, inhaled, topical) - can induce or exacerbate glaucoma.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Chronic glaucoma"][/sc]
  • Check visual acuity - usually normal until advanced disease, indicating central vision preservation.
  • Check intraocular pressure (IOP) - often elevated (>21 mmHg) but can be normal in normal-tension glaucoma. Goldmann applanation tonometry is the gold standard.
  • Check optic disc appearance - characteristic cupping and pallor, often asymmetric between eyes. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Cup-to-disc ratio: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="cup-to-disc ratio"][/sc]
  • Check visual fields - perimetry reveals characteristic patterns of peripheral field loss (e.g., arcuate scotomas, nasal step).
  • Check vital signs - generally normal, but systemic conditions like hypertension or diabetes can be associated risk factors.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Chronic glaucoma"][/sc]
  • Sudden, severe eye pain with blurred vision: Suggests acute angle-closure glaucoma, a medical emergency requiring immediate intervention to prevent rapid, irreversible vision loss.
  • Rapidly progressive visual field loss: Indicates aggressive disease progression despite treatment, warranting urgent re-evaluation of management strategy.
  • New onset diplopia or proptosis: May suggest a secondary cause of glaucoma, such as an orbital mass or inflammatory process, requiring further investigation.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Chronic glaucoma"][/sc]
  • Normal-tension glaucoma: Optic nerve damage and visual field loss occur despite consistently normal IOP, suggesting other factors like vascular dysregulation or structural weakness play a greater role.
  • Paediatric glaucoma: Presents with different signs (e.g., buphthalmos, photophobia, epiphora) and requires prompt diagnosis and management to preserve vision in developing eyes.
  • Secondary glaucoma: Develops as a consequence of other eye conditions (e.g., uveitis, trauma, pseudoexfoliation syndrome) or systemic diseases, and its management often involves addressing the underlying cause. Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Chronic glaucoma"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected chronic glaucoma:
    • Goldmann applanation tonometry for accurate IOP measurement.
    • Slit lamp biomicroscopy for optic nerve head assessment (cupping, rim thinning, haemorrhages).
    • Standard automated perimetry (SAP) for visual field assessment.
    • Gonioscopy for assessment of the anterior chamber angle.
    • Central corneal thickness (CCT) measurement, as thinner corneas can lead to falsely low IOP readings.
  • Severity/risk tools: The Ocular Hypertension Treatment Study (OHTS) Calculator can estimate the 5-year risk of developing primary open-angle glaucoma in patients with ocular hypertension. OHTS Calculator on MDCalc
  • Imaging - OCT: Optical coherence tomography (OCT) of the optic nerve head and retinal nerve fibre layer provides objective, quantitative assessment of structural damage and progression. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Optical coherence tomography (OCT) in glaucoma: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="optical coherence tomography (OCT)"][/sc]
  • Serial reassessment: Regular follow-up with repeat IOP measurements, visual field tests, and optic nerve head is essential to monitor disease progression and treatment effectiveness.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Chronic glaucoma"][/sc]
  • A 68-year-old female presents for routine optometry review, reporting no visual symptoms, but with a history of ocular hypertension and a family history of glaucoma, found to have elevated intraocular pressure and asymmetric optic disc cupping on examination.
  • An elderly patient with chronic, asymptomatic, progressive peripheral vision loss and characteristic optic disc changes, concerning for primary open-angle glaucoma.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Chronic glaucoma"][/sc]
  • Ocular hypertension: Elevated IOP without optic nerve damage or visual field loss; represents a risk factor for glaucoma rather than glaucoma itself.
  • Normal-tension glaucoma: Optic nerve damage and visual field loss with consistently normal IOP; requires careful exclusion of other causes of optic neuropathy.
  • Secondary glaucoma: Glaucoma caused by an identifiable underlying condition (e.g., uveitis, trauma, steroid use, pseudoexfoliation syndrome); management involves treating the primary cause.
  • Optic neuropathy (non-glaucomatous): Optic disc pallor and visual field defects due to other causes (e.g., ischaemic optic neuropathy, optic neuritis, compressive lesions); often presents with different patterns of visual loss and optic disc changes. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Ischaemic optic neuropathy: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="ischaemic optic neuropathy"][/sc]
  • Retinal vascular occlusions: Can cause sudden, severe vision loss and visual field defects, but typically with retinal haemorrhages or oedema, distinct from glaucomatous changes.
  • Retinitis pigmentosa: A group of inherited retinal dystrophies causing progressive peripheral vision loss and night blindness, but with characteristic retinal changes (e.g., bone spicule pigmentation) and often a family history.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Chronic glaucoma"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Referral to ophthalmology for comprehensive assessment and initiation of IOP-lowering therapy to prevent further optic nerve damage.
  • Definitive management: Primarily involves reducing intraocular pressure (IOP) through topical medications (e.g., prostaglandin analogues, beta-blockers), laser therapy (e.g., selective laser trabeculoplasty), or surgery (e.g., trabeculectomy, aqueous shunt implantation).
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Lifelong regular monitoring of IOP, visual fields, and optic nerve head is essential to detect progression and adjust treatment as needed.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Patients should be advised to seek urgent ophthalmological review for sudden vision changes, severe eye pain, or worsening symptoms.
  • Exam pharmacology reference (UKMLA/MRCGP):
    • Prostaglandin analogues (e.g., [sc name="jitl-medication" Q="Latanoprost use in Chronic glaucoma" text="latanoprost"][/sc]) often reduce IOP by increasing uveoscleral outflow.
    • Beta-blockers (e.g., [sc name="jitl-medication" Q="Timolol use in Chronic glaucoma" text="timolol"][/sc]) typically reduce IOP by decreasing aqueous humour production.
    • Carbonic anhydrase inhibitors (e.g., [sc name="jitl-medication" Q="Dorzolamide use in Chronic glaucoma" text="dorzolamide"][/sc]) may reduce IOP by inhibiting aqueous humour secretion.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Chronic glaucoma"][/sc]
  • Irreversible vision loss and blindness: The primary and most devastating complication, resulting from progressive optic nerve damage if the condition is undiagnosed or inadequately treated over time.
  • Reduced quality of life: Visual impairment significantly impacts daily activities, independence, and overall well-being, particularly in the late phases of the disease.
  • Falls and injuries: Peripheral visual field loss increases the risk of falls and accidents, especially in older adults, due to impaired spatial awareness.
  • Psychological impact: Living with a chronic, progressive condition that can lead to blindness often results in anxiety, depression, and social isolation.
