Urinary incontinence [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Urinary incontinence"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Urinary incontinence"][/sc]
  • Urinary incontinence is the involuntary leakage of urine, a common and often distressing condition that significantly impacts quality of life.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the different types of urinary incontinence, their common causes, and initial management strategies.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Urinary incontinence"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Urinary incontinence"][/sc]
  • Affects approximately 1 in 3 women in the UK, with prevalence increasing with age, as per NICE guidelines.
  • Risk factors include parity, obesity, chronic cough, neurological conditions, and certain medications.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Urinary incontinence"][/sc]
  • Trigger: Weakness of pelvic floor muscles or dysfunction of the urethral sphincter, often exacerbated by increased intra-abdominal pressure.
  • Progression: Inadequate bladder support or inappropriate detrusor muscle activity leads to involuntary urine loss.
  • Consequence: Significant impact on social, psychological, and physical well-being, potentially leading to skin irritation and recurrent urinary tract infections.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Urinary incontinence"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Often presents insidiously with occasional leakage, particularly during coughing or sneezing, which may be dismissed as a normal part of ageing or childbirth.
  • Progressive phase: Symptoms become more frequent and severe, impacting daily activities and social engagement, particularly beyond a few months without intervention.
  • Late phase: Chronic and persistent leakage, potentially leading to severe skin breakdown, recurrent infections, and significant psychological distress, particularly beyond several years of untreated symptoms.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Urinary incontinence"][/sc]
  • Ask about involuntary urine leakage - to determine the type (stress, urge, mixed) and severity of incontinence.
  • Ask about frequency and urgency of urination - suggestive of overactive bladder and urge incontinence.
  • Ask about nocturia - to assess the impact on sleep and potential underlying causes.
  • Ask about precipitating factors (e.g., cough, sneeze, exercise, sudden urge) - crucial for differentiating stress from urge incontinence.
  • Ask about impact on daily activities and quality of life - to understand the patient's perspective and guide management goals.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Urinary incontinence"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - to rule out systemic illness or infection.
  • Check abdominal examination - for bladder distension or masses.
  • Check pelvic examination (women) - to assess for pelvic organ prolapse, vaginal atrophy, and perform a cough test to elicit stress incontinence.
  • Check digital rectal examination (men) - to assess prostate size and tone, and anal sphincter tone.
  • Check neurological examination - to identify any underlying neurological conditions affecting bladder control.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Urinary incontinence"][/sc]
  • Haematuria: May indicate urinary tract infection, stones, or malignancy, requiring urgent investigation.
  • New onset neurological symptoms: Suggests underlying neurological pathology affecting bladder control, such as cauda equina syndrome or multiple sclerosis.
  • Painful bladder or voiding: Could indicate infection, interstitial cystitis, or other inflammatory conditions.
  • Rapid onset of symptoms: Particularly if associated with systemic symptoms, may suggest acute infection or other serious pathology.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Urinary incontinence"][/sc]
  • Older adults: Often present with mixed incontinence or functional incontinence due to cognitive impairment or mobility issues, making diagnosis challenging.
  • Pregnancy: Stress incontinence is common due to hormonal changes and increased intra-abdominal pressure, typically resolving postpartum but can persist.
  • Immunosuppressed: May have atypical or severe presentations of urinary tract infections contributing to incontinence, requiring a lower threshold for investigation.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Urinary incontinence"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected urinary incontinence: Urinalysis and urine culture to exclude urinary tract infection.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Renal function tests (urea, creatinine, eGFR) to assess kidney health.
  • Imaging - Ultrasound: Renal and bladder ultrasound to assess for hydronephrosis, bladder stones, or post-void residual volume.
  • Serial reassessment: Bladder diary for 3-7 days to record fluid intake, voiding frequency, and episodes of leakage, aiding in diagnosis and management planning.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Urinary incontinence"][/sc]
  • A 68-year-old female presents with a 6-month history of involuntary urine leakage when she coughs or sneezes, alongside a frequent and sudden urge to urinate.
  • An elderly female with chronic, bothersome, mixed urinary incontinence, impacting her social activities and quality of life.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Urinary incontinence"][/sc]
  • Stress urinary incontinence - leakage with increased intra-abdominal pressure.
  • Urge urinary incontinence (overactive bladder) - sudden, strong urge to void with involuntary leakage.
  • Mixed urinary incontinence - features of both stress and urge incontinence.
  • Overflow incontinence - bladder distension due to outlet obstruction or detrusor underactivity.
  • Functional incontinence - inability to reach the toilet in time due to physical or cognitive impairment.
  • Urinary tract infection - acute onset of symptoms, dysuria, frequency, urgency.
  • Medication-induced incontinence - side effect of diuretics, alpha-blockers, sedatives.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Urinary incontinence"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Address reversible causes such as urinary tract infection, constipation, or medication side effects.
  • Definitive management: Lifestyle modifications (weight loss, fluid management), supervised pelvic floor muscle training, bladder training, and consideration of pharmacological agents or surgical options depending on the type and severity.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Regular review of symptoms, bladder diary, and adherence to treatment, with escalation to specialist services if conservative measures fail.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Advise patients to seek medical attention for red flag symptoms or worsening incontinence.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Urinary incontinence"][/sc]
  • Skin irritation and breakdown: Due to prolonged exposure to urine, leading to discomfort and increased risk of infection.
  • Recurrent urinary tract infections: Stasis of urine and compromised skin integrity can predispose to ascending infections.
  • Social isolation and psychological distress: Embarrassment and fear of leakage can lead to avoidance of social activities and impact mental health.
  • Falls: Particularly in older adults rushing to the toilet due to urgency, increasing the risk of injury.
