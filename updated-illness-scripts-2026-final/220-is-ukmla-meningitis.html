Meningitis (Brain and spinal cord) [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Meningitis"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Meningitis"][/sc]
  • Meningitis is an inflammation of the protective membranes (meninges) surrounding the brain and spinal cord, typically caused by infection.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical features of meningitis, differentiate between bacterial and viral causes, and initiate appropriate urgent investigations and empirical management.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Meningitis"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Meningitis"][/sc]
  • The annual incidence of acute bacterial meningitis in developed countries is estimated at 2–5 per 100,000 population, with viral meningitis accounting for over half of all cases [1].
  • Bacterial meningitis is most common in infants, with a second peak in teenagers and young adults, while viral meningitis is often caused by enteroviruses [1].
  • Risk factors include young age, immunocompromise, asplenia, neonatal complications, and overcrowded living environments (e.g., university students) [1].
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Meningitis"][/sc]
  • Infectious agents (bacteria, viruses, fungi) typically colonise the nasopharynx and invade the bloodstream, crossing the blood-brain barrier to infect the meninges.
  • The inflammatory response in the subarachnoid space leads to increased intracranial pressure, cerebral oedema, and potential vasculitis, impairing cerebral blood flow.
  • Consequences include neuronal damage, hydrocephalus, and systemic complications such as sepsis and disseminated intravascular coagulation.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Meningitis"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Symptoms are often vague and non-specific, developing over hours to a day, mimicking common viral illnesses.
  • Progressive phase: Over 12-24 hours, more specific signs such as neck stiffness, photophobia, and altered mental state emerge, particularly beyond 24 hours in bacterial meningitis.
  • Late phase: Rapid deterioration can occur within days, leading to seizures, focal neurological deficits, and coma if not promptly treated.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Meningitis"][/sc]
  • Ask about headache - often severe and persistent, indicating meningeal irritation.
  • Ask about fever - a common systemic response to infection, though may be absent in immunocompromised or elderly patients.
  • Ask about nausea and vomiting - frequently accompanies headache and can indicate raised intracranial pressure.
  • Ask about altered mental state - ranging from lethargy and irritability to confusion or reduced consciousness, suggesting cerebral involvement.
  • Ask about photophobia - sensitivity to light, a classic symptom of meningeal inflammation.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Meningitis"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - fever, tachycardia, and hypotension may indicate sepsis, while bradycardia and hypertension can suggest Cushing\'s reflex due to raised intracranial pressure.
  • Check neck stiffness - resistance to passive neck flexion, a key sign of meningeal irritation.
  • Check [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Non-blanching rash in meningitis: significance and appearance?" text="non-blanching rash"] - petechial or purpuric rash, a critical sign of meningococcal septicaemia, which can coexist with meningitis.
  • Check [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Kernig\'s sign: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Kernig\'s sign"] - pain and resistance on passive knee extension with hips fully flexed, indicating meningeal irritation.
  • Check [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Brudzinski\'s sign: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Brudzinski\'s sign"] - involuntary flexion of the hips and knees on passive neck flexion, also indicative of meningeal irritation.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Meningitis"][/sc]
  • Non-blanching rash - indicates meningococcal septicaemia, a life-threatening condition requiring immediate treatment.
  • Rapidly deteriorating conscious level - suggests increasing intracranial pressure or severe cerebral involvement, necessitating urgent intervention.
  • Focal neurological deficits or seizures - may indicate cerebral abscess, encephalitis, or severe localised inflammation, requiring neuroimaging and specific management.
  • Signs of shock - hypotension, tachycardia, and poor perfusion suggest severe sepsis, demanding aggressive resuscitation.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Meningitis"][/sc]
  • Neonates and infants: May present with non-specific symptoms like poor feeding, irritability, lethargy, or a [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Bulging fontanelle in infants with meningitis: significance?" text="bulging fontanelle"], often without fever or neck stiffness.
  • Older adults and immunocompromised: Symptoms can be subtle or atypical, with less pronounced fever, headache, or meningism, leading to delayed diagnosis.
  • Tuberculous meningitis: Often has an insidious onset with a more prolonged prodrome of non-specific symptoms over weeks, making early diagnosis challenging.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Meningitis"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected Meningitis: Lumbar puncture (LP) for cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) analysis (cell count, protein, glucose, Gram stain, culture, PCR). This is crucial for differentiating bacterial from viral meningitis.
  • [sc name="jitl-query" Q="CSF analysis interpretation in meningitis: bacterial vs viral vs tuberculous?" text="CSF analysis interpretation"]: This is crucial for differentiating bacterial from viral meningitis.
  • CSF findings interpretation:
    • Bacterial: High white cell count (predominantly neutrophils), high protein, low glucose.
    • Viral: Moderately raised white cell count (predominantly lymphocytes), normal or slightly raised protein, normal glucose.
    • Tuberculous: High white cell count (predominantly lymphocytes), very high protein, very low glucose.
  • Severity/risk tools: Consider Glasgow Coma Scale (GCS) on MDCalc for assessing conscious level.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Blood cultures, full blood count, C-reactive protein, urea and electrolytes, blood glucose.
  • Imaging - CT head: Indicated before LP if there are signs of raised intracranial pressure (e.g., GCS <10, focal neurological deficit, papilloedema, new-onset seizures) to rule out space-occupying lesions.
  • Serial reassessment: Close monitoring of vital signs, conscious level, and neurological status is essential to detect deterioration or response to treatment.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Meningitis"][/sc]
  • A young adult presenting with acute onset fever, severe headache, neck stiffness, and photophobia, with rapid deterioration in conscious level.
  • Acute febrile illness with meningism and altered mental status, concerning for central nervous system infection.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Meningitis"][/sc]
  • Encephalitis - inflammation of brain parenchyma, often with altered mental status, seizures, or focal neurological deficits, but less prominent meningism.
  • Subarachnoid haemorrhage - sudden onset severe headache ("thunderclap"), often with meningism, but typically without fever unless complicated.
  • Sepsis (without meningitis) - systemic inflammatory response to infection, with fever, tachycardia, and hypotension, but without direct meningeal irritation.
  • Migraine with aura - severe headache, photophobia, and phonophobia, but usually with a history of similar episodes and absence of fever or neck stiffness.
  • Intracranial abscess - focal neurological deficits and signs of raised intracranial pressure, often with a more subacute presentation than acute meningitis.
  • Viral prodrome - common viral illnesses can cause fever, headache, and myalgia, but typically lack true meningism or significant altered mental state.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Meningitis"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Prompt recognition, urgent escalation to senior medical staff, and initiation of empirical antibiotics and supportive care (e.g., airway management, fluid resuscitation) without delay.
  • Definitive management: Targeted antimicrobial therapy based on CSF Gram stain and culture results; often involves high-dose intravenous antibiotics (e.g., ceftriaxone, cefotaxime) for bacterial meningitis.
  • Adjunctive management: Dexamethasone may be considered in bacterial meningitis (especially in adults and children over 3 months) to reduce neurological sequelae.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Close neurological monitoring, repeat lumbar puncture if clinical deterioration or inadequate response to treatment, and audiological assessment post-recovery.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Advise patients and carers on signs of deterioration and when to seek urgent medical review; ensure appropriate public health notification for meningococcal disease.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Meningitis"][/sc]
  • Hearing loss: Occurs in up to 33% of bacterial meningitis survivors, often due to inflammation affecting the cochlea or auditory nerve, impacting long-term quality of life.
  • Seizures: Can occur during the acute phase due to cerebral irritation or oedema, or as a long-term sequela due to cortical damage.
  • Cognitive impairment: Ranging from subtle learning difficulties to severe intellectual disability, particularly after bacterial meningitis, affecting academic and functional outcomes.
  • Hydrocephalus: May develop due to obstruction of CSF flow by inflammatory exudates, leading to increased intracranial pressure and requiring CSF diversion.
  • Focal neurological deficits: Such as paresis, cranial nerve palsies, or visual impairment, resulting from localised brain damage or vasculitis.
  • Death: Despite advances in treatment, bacterial meningitis carries a significant mortality rate (up to 30% in some forms), particularly if diagnosis and treatment are delayed.
