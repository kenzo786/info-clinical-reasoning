Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Cow milk protein allergy (Child health)"][/sc]
  • Cow milk protein allergy (CMPA) is an immune-mediated adverse reaction to proteins in cow\'s milk, distinct from lactose intolerance, and is one of the most common food allergies in childhood [1].
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the varied presentations of cow milk protein allergy, differentiate between IgE-mediated and non-IgE-mediated forms, and understand the principles of diagnosis and initial management in primary care.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Cow milk protein allergy (Child health)"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Cow milk protein allergy (Child health)"][/sc]
  • CMPA affects approximately 2-3% of infants in the UK, with most cases developing in the first year of life [1].
  • Risk factors include a family history of atopy (e.g., asthma, eczema, hay fever) in a first-degree relative, although many affected infants have no such history [1].
  • The prevalence of IgE-mediated CMPA is estimated to be around 0.5-1%, while non-IgE-mediated CMPA is more common, affecting approximately 2% of infants [1].
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Cow milk protein allergy (Child health)"][/sc]
  • Trigger: Exposure to cow\'s milk proteins (primarily casein and whey) through ingestion, leading to an immune response in sensitised individuals.
  • Progression: In IgE-mediated reactions, mast cells and basophils release histamine and other mediators upon re-exposure, causing rapid onset symptoms. In non-IgE-mediated reactions, T-cell activation leads to a delayed inflammatory response in the gastrointestinal tract or skin [1]. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="IgE-mediated vs non-IgE-mediated allergy: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="IgE vs non-IgE mediated allergy distinction"][/sc]
  • Consequence: The immune response results in a range of clinical manifestations affecting the skin, gastrointestinal tract, and respiratory system, varying in severity and timing depending on the immune mechanism involved [1].
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Cow milk protein allergy (Child health)"][/sc]
  • Early phase: IgE-mediated reactions typically manifest rapidly, within minutes to 2 hours of cow\'s milk ingestion, with symptoms such as urticaria, angioedema, vomiting, or anaphylaxis [1].
  • Progressive phase: Non-IgE-mediated reactions have a delayed onset, usually appearing from 2 hours up to several days after ingestion, often presenting with gastrointestinal symptoms (e.g., reflux, diarrhoea, constipation), atopic eczema, or faltering growth [1].
  • Late phase: Symptoms of non-IgE-mediated CMPA can persist for weeks or months if cow\'s milk protein is not eliminated from the diet, leading to chronic issues such as severe eczema or persistent gastrointestinal distress [1].
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Cow milk protein allergy (Child health)"][/sc]
  • Ask about acute urticaria or angioedema - these suggest an IgE-mediated reaction requiring prompt management.
  • Ask about vomiting or diarrhoea - common in both IgE and non-IgE mediated reactions, but delayed onset points towards non-IgE.
  • Ask about chronic reflux or constipation - persistent gastrointestinal symptoms are often indicative of non-IgE-mediated CMPA.
  • Ask about atopic eczema - worsening or persistent eczema, particularly if severe, can be a manifestation of non-IgE-mediated CMPA.
  • Ask about respiratory symptoms (e.g., wheezing, cough) - can occur in IgE-mediated reactions, often alongside skin or GI symptoms.
  • Ask about faltering growth - a significant concern in non-IgE-mediated CMPA, especially when associated with chronic GI symptoms.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Cow milk protein allergy (Child health)"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - assess for signs of anaphylaxis (e.g., hypotension, tachycardia, tachypnoea) in acute IgE-mediated reactions.
  • Check skin for urticaria, angioedema, or exacerbation of atopic eczema - visual cues for both IgE and non-IgE mediated allergy.
  • Check abdomen for distension, tenderness, or abnormal bowel sounds - can indicate gastrointestinal inflammation in non-IgE-mediated CMPA.
  • Check growth parameters (weight, height, head circumference) - faltering growth is a key indicator of chronic non-IgE-mediated CMPA. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Faltering growth in CMPA: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Faltering growth"][/sc]
  • Check for pallor or signs of anaemia - may be present in severe, chronic gastrointestinal CMPA due to malabsorption or blood loss.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Cow milk protein allergy (Child health)"][/sc]
  • Anaphylaxis: A severe, potentially life-threatening systemic allergic reaction that can occur rapidly after exposure, leading to airway compromise, circulatory collapse, and death if not promptly treated.
  • Severe acute reactions: Widespread urticaria, angioedema affecting the face or throat, significant respiratory distress, or signs of shock occurring within minutes to 2 hours of exposure.
  • Persistent faltering growth: Inadequate weight gain or weight loss despite appropriate dietary intervention, suggesting ongoing malabsorption or severe underlying disease.
  • Blood in stool: Especially if significant or persistent, may indicate allergic proctocolitis or enteropathy, requiring specialist evaluation.
  • Severe atopic eczema unresponsive to treatment: Extensive or rapidly worsening eczema that does not improve with standard topical therapy, potentially indicating underlying food allergy.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Cow milk protein allergy (Child health)"][/sc]
  • Exclusively breastfed infants: Symptoms can occur due to transfer of cow\'s milk proteins from the maternal diet into breast milk, often presenting as non-IgE-mediated symptoms like eczema or GI issues.
  • Older children: While most common in infancy, CMPA can persist or present later, sometimes with more subtle or varied symptoms, making diagnosis challenging.
  • Mixed IgE and non-IgE reactions: Some individuals may experience both immediate and delayed symptoms, complicating the clinical picture and diagnostic approach. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Mixed IgE and non-IgE reactions: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Mixed IgE/non-IgE reactions"][/sc]
  • Oral allergy syndrome: Rarely, older children with pollen allergy may experience oral symptoms (itching, tingling) upon consuming cow\'s milk due to cross-reactivity, though this is less typical for true CMPA.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Cow milk protein allergy (Child health)"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected CMPA: An allergy-focused clinical history is paramount, followed by a diagnostic elimination diet (2-4 weeks) and reintroduction challenge. This is the gold standard for diagnosis, particularly for non-IgE-mediated CMPA [1]. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Diagnostic elimination diet and reintroduction challenge: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Elimination diet and challenge"][/sc]
  • IgE-mediated allergy tests: Skin prick tests (SPT) or specific IgE blood tests (e.g., RAST, ImmunoCAP) to cow\'s milk proteins can support the diagnosis of IgE-mediated CMPA, but a positive result alone does not confirm clinical allergy [1].
  • Severity/risk tools: No specific validated severity/risk score for CMPA is routinely used in primary care.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Consider investigations for other conditions presenting with similar symptoms, such as coeliac disease, cystic fibrosis, or inflammatory bowel disease, especially if symptoms are severe or atypical.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Cow milk protein allergy (Child health)"][/sc]
  • A 6-month-old infant presents with chronic vomiting, persistent atopic eczema, and faltering growth since 2 months of age, which worsens after consuming standard infant formula.
  • Infant with delayed, multi-systemic symptoms concerning for non-IgE-mediated cow milk protein allergy.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Cow milk protein allergy (Child health)"][/sc]
  • Lactose intolerance: Presents with gastrointestinal symptoms (bloating, diarrhoea) but is due to lactase deficiency, not an immune reaction to milk proteins.
  • Gastro-oesophageal reflux disease (GORD): Common in infants, but symptoms typically improve with standard anti-reflux measures and are not associated with skin changes or faltering growth unless complicated by CMPA.
  • Infectious gastroenteritis: Acute onset of vomiting and diarrhoea, usually self-limiting, and often associated with fever or other systemic signs of infection.
  • Coeliac disease: Presents with chronic gastrointestinal symptoms and faltering growth, but is an immune reaction to gluten, not cow\'s milk protein.
  • Cystic fibrosis: Can cause malabsorption and faltering growth, but typically involves respiratory symptoms and positive sweat test.
  • Eosinophilic oesophagitis/gastroenteritis: Chronic inflammatory conditions of the GI tract, often triggered by food allergens, but typically diagnosed via endoscopy and biopsies. Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Cow milk protein allergy (Child health)"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: For suspected IgE-mediated reactions with severe symptoms (e.g., anaphylaxis), immediate administration of adrenaline and supportive care is crucial.
  • Definitive management: Complete elimination of cow\'s milk protein from the infant\'s diet. For breastfed infants, the mother should eliminate all cow\'s milk products from her diet. For formula-fed infants, prescribe an extensively hydrolysed formula (eHF) or, in severe cases, an amino acid formula (AAF) [1].
  • Adjunctive management: Management of associated symptoms such as eczema (topical corticosteroids) or reflux (thickened feeds, alginates) as per standard guidelines, ensuring they do not contain cow\'s milk protein.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Regular review of symptoms, growth, and nutritional status. Dietary reintroduction of cow\'s milk should be considered under medical supervision, typically after 6-12 months of elimination, as most children outgrow CMPA [1].
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Refer to a paediatric dietitian or allergy specialist for complex cases, severe reactions, or if diagnosis remains uncertain. Educate parents on recognising symptoms of allergic reactions and when to seek urgent medical attention.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Cow milk protein allergy (Child health)"][/sc]
  • Anaphylaxis: A severe, potentially life-threatening systemic allergic reaction that can occur rapidly after exposure, leading to airway compromise, circulatory collapse, and death if not promptly treated.
  • Faltering growth and malnutrition: Chronic malabsorption, reduced oral intake due to discomfort, or inappropriate dietary restrictions can lead to inadequate nutrient intake and impaired growth.
  • Nutritional deficiencies: Strict elimination diets without adequate substitution can result in deficiencies of calcium, vitamin D, and other essential nutrients, impacting bone health and overall development.
  • Psychosocial impact: The burden of managing a restricted diet can lead to parental anxiety, stress, and impact family quality of life.
  • Persistent symptoms and reduced quality of life: If CMPA is not diagnosed or managed effectively, children may experience ongoing discomfort, pain, and chronic symptoms affecting their daily life and well-being.
