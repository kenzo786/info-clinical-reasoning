Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Drug overdose (Drugs and drug effects)"][/sc]
  • Drug overdose refers to the ingestion or exposure to a substance in quantities greater than recommended or usually taken, leading to toxic effects on the body.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical features of common drug overdoses (paracetamol, opioids, TCAs, benzodiazepines, salicylates), initiate appropriate supportive care, and understand the indications for specific antidotes and enhanced elimination strategies.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Drug overdose (Drugs and drug effects)"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Drug overdose (Drugs and drug effects)"][/sc]
  • In the UK, paracetamol is the medication most commonly involved in deliberate overdose cases, with around 160,000 hospital admissions annually related to poisoning or overdose [1].
  • Opioid-related drug deaths average 60 per week in Great Britain, with increasing trends in mortality related to prescription opioid misuse since 2013 [2].
  • Tricyclic antidepressant (TCA) overdose accounts for a significant proportion of poisoning deaths in the UK, with 272 deaths related to TCA overdose reported in 2005 [3].
  • Risk factors for drug overdose include mental health conditions, substance use disorders, chronic pain, access to medications, and a history of self-harm or previous overdose.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Drug overdose (Drugs and drug effects)"][/sc]
  • Trigger: Ingestion of a toxic amount of a substance leads to systemic absorption and distribution to target organs, overwhelming normal metabolic or excretory pathways.
  • Progression: For paracetamol, a toxic metabolite (NAPQI) depletes hepatic glutathione, leading to hepatocellular damage. Opioids act on mu-opioid receptors, causing central nervous system and respiratory depression. TCAs block alpha-adrenergic receptors and have anticholinergic effects, leading to cardiovascular and neurological dysfunction. Benzodiazepines enhance GABAergic neurotransmission, causing CNS depression. Salicylates cause acid-base disturbances, electrolyte abnormalities, and CNS effects.
  • Consequence: Organ dysfunction, such as liver failure (paracetamol), respiratory arrest (opioids, benzodiazepines), cardiac arrhythmias (TCAs), or severe metabolic derangements (salicylates), can lead to multi-organ failure and death.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Drug overdose (Drugs and drug effects)"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Within minutes to a few hours post-ingestion, immediate effects such as nausea, vomiting, drowsiness, or altered mental status may manifest, depending on the substance. For opioids, respiratory depression can occur rapidly.
  • Progressive phase: Over several hours to 24-48 hours, more severe toxicities can develop. Paracetamol overdose may initially be asymptomatic, with liver damage becoming evident after 24 hours. TCA toxicity can progress to cardiac arrhythmias and seizures within hours. Benzodiazepine effects typically peak within hours but can be prolonged with long-acting agents or co-ingestions.
  • Late phase: Beyond 24-48 hours, irreversible organ damage or delayed complications may become apparent. Liver failure from paracetamol overdose can be fully established, and prolonged respiratory depression from opioids or benzodiazepines may lead to aspiration or hypoxic brain injury. Salicylate toxicity can lead to severe metabolic acidosis and renal insufficiency if not managed promptly.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Drug overdose (Drugs and drug effects)"][/sc]
  • Ask about nausea and vomiting - these are common early symptoms across many overdoses, particularly paracetamol, but can be non-specific.
  • Ask about drowsiness or altered mental status - this is a key indicator of central nervous system depression, prominent in opioid, benzodiazepine, and TCA overdoses, and can rapidly progress to coma.
  • Ask about abdominal pain - often a feature of paracetamol overdose as liver damage progresses, and can also be present in salicylate toxicity.
  • Ask about blurred vision or dry mouth - these anticholinergic symptoms are characteristic of TCA overdose.
  • Ask about tinnitus or hearing loss - these are classic symptoms of salicylate toxicity.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Drug overdose (Drugs and drug effects)"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - respiratory rate, heart rate, blood pressure, and temperature are crucial for assessing the severity of overdose; respiratory depression is a hallmark of opioid and benzodiazepine toxicity, while tachycardia and hypotension are common in TCA overdose.
  • Check pupillary response - miosis (pinpoint pupils) is characteristic of opioid overdose, whereas mydriasis (dilated pupils) can be seen in TCA overdose.
  • Check skin for pallor or jaundice - pallor is an early sign in paracetamol overdose, while jaundice indicates developing liver failure.
  • Check neurological status - assess Glasgow Coma Scale (GCS) to quantify level of consciousness, which is often depressed in opioid, benzodiazepine, and TCA overdoses.
  • Check for QRS widening on ECG - a critical sign in TCA overdose indicating cardiotoxicity [sc name="jitl-query" Q="QRS widening: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="QRS widening"][/sc].
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Drug overdose (Drugs and drug effects)"][/sc]
  • Respiratory depression: indicates impending respiratory arrest, particularly with opioid or benzodiazepine overdose, requiring immediate airway management and naloxone administration for opioids.
  • QRS prolongation: suggests severe cardiotoxicity in TCA overdose, necessitating urgent sodium bicarbonate therapy.
  • Altered mental status (GCS < 9): signifies significant central nervous system depression, increasing the risk of aspiration and requiring airway protection.
  • Hypotension refractory to fluids: may indicate severe vasodilatation or myocardial depression, common in TCA overdose, requiring vasopressor support.
  • Seizures: a sign of severe neurotoxicity, which can occur in TCA and salicylate overdoses, requiring anticonvulsant treatment.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Drug overdose (Drugs and drug effects)"][/sc]
  • Co-ingestion: The presence of multiple substances can mask typical toxidromes or exacerbate toxicity, such as benzodiazepines potentiating opioid-induced respiratory depression.
  • Chronic overdose: Salicylate toxicity can present indolently with more chronic exposure, leading to a delayed diagnosis and insidious onset of symptoms like confusion or metabolic acidosis.
  • Paediatric population: Children may present with non-specific symptoms, and even small ingestions can lead to significant toxicity due to their smaller body mass.
  • Elderly patients: May have altered pharmacokinetics and co-morbidities, leading to exaggerated responses to drugs and increased susceptibility to adverse effects, often presenting with confusion or falls. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Altered pharmacokinetics in elderly: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Altered pharmacokinetics in elderly"][/sc]
  • Staggered overdose: Ingestion of a substance over a prolonged period (e.g., paracetamol) can make timing of exposure difficult to ascertain, complicating nomogram use and requiring immediate antidote administration.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Drug overdose (Drugs and drug effects)"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected drug overdose:
    • Blood gas analysis (ABG or VBG): to assess acid-base status and oxygenation, particularly for metabolic acidosis in salicylate overdose or respiratory acidosis in opioid/benzodiazepine overdose. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Anion gap: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Anion gap"][/sc]
    • Electrolytes, urea, creatinine: to assess renal function and electrolyte imbalances, crucial in salicylate toxicity.
    • Liver function tests (LFTs): essential for paracetamol overdose to monitor for hepatotoxicity.
    • Paracetamol level: to guide acetylcysteine administration based on the nomogram.
    • ECG: to identify cardiac conduction abnormalities, especially QRS widening in TCA overdose.
  • Severity/risk tools:
    • Rumack-Matthew nomogram: for paracetamol overdose, to determine the need for acetylcysteine based on plasma paracetamol levels and time since ingestion.   • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible:
    • Toxicology screen (urine/blood): may identify other co-ingestions but has limited role in acute management.
  • Serial reassessment:
    • Repeat paracetamol levels, LFTs, and ABGs as indicated by clinical course and specific overdose guidelines.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Drug overdose (Drugs and drug effects)"][/sc]
  • Hypoglycaemia: can mimic altered mental status and confusion, but typically lacks specific toxidrome features like pinpoint pupils or QRS widening.
  • Stroke/intracranial haemorrhage: may cause acute neurological deficits, but usually without a clear history of drug ingestion or the systemic effects seen in overdose.
  • Sepsis/meningitis: can present with altered mental status and physiological derangements, but often accompanied by fever and signs of infection rather than specific overdose symptoms.
  • Diabetic ketoacidosis (DKA): shares metabolic acidosis with salicylate toxicity, but is distinguished by hyperglycaemia and ketosis.
  • Non-accidental injury/trauma: may lead to altered consciousness, but typically has external signs of injury and a different history.
  • Electrolyte disturbances: severe hyponatraemia or hypernatraemia can cause neurological symptoms, but are usually identified on blood tests and lack specific drug-related features. Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Drug overdose (Drugs and drug effects)"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Recognition of overdose, rapid assessment using ABCDE approach, escalation to senior medical staff, and provision of supportive care including airway management, oxygenation, and intravenous fluids. Defer to local poisoning pathways and National Poisons Information Service (NPIS) for specific advice.
  • Definitive management:
    • Paracetamol: Intravenous acetylcysteine (NAC) is the antidote, administered based on the Rumack-Matthew nomogram or immediately in staggered/unknown timing overdoses.
    • Opioid: Naloxone is the antidote, titrated intravenously to reverse respiratory depression while avoiding full reversal of consciousness and acute withdrawal.
    • TCA: Sodium bicarbonate is often indicated for QRS widening or arrhythmias, aiming to achieve a serum pH of 7.45-7.55. Vasopressors may be considered for refractory hypotension.
    • Benzodiazepine: Supportive care is the mainstay. Flumazenil may be considered in specific circumstances but carries a risk of precipitating seizures, especially in mixed overdoses or chronic users.
    • Salicylate: Management typically involves activated charcoal if early, urinary alkalinisation to enhance elimination, and haemodialysis for severe toxicity or refractory metabolic derangements.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Continuous cardiac monitoring for TCA overdose, serial LFTs for paracetamol, and close observation for respiratory status in opioid and benzodiazepine overdoses. Follow-up for mental health assessment and harm reduction strategies is crucial.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Any deterioration in clinical status, particularly respiratory or neurological, warrants immediate re-evaluation and escalation of care. Patients with intentional overdose require mental health assessment prior to discharge.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Drug overdose (Drugs and drug effects)"][/sc]
  • Acute liver failure: A critical complication of paracetamol overdose, particularly if acetylcysteine is delayed beyond the early phase, leading to coagulopathy, encephalopathy, and potentially death.
  • Respiratory arrest: A life-threatening consequence of severe opioid or benzodiazepine overdose, often occurring rapidly in the early phase, necessitating immediate ventilatory support.
  • Cardiac arrhythmias: A significant danger in TCA overdose, manifesting in the progressive phase as QRS widening and potentially leading to ventricular fibrillation or asystole.
  • Seizures: Can occur in the progressive phase of TCA and salicylate overdoses, indicating severe neurotoxicity and requiring prompt anticonvulsant management.
  • Metabolic acidosis: A hallmark of severe salicylate toxicity, which can be refractory to initial treatment and lead to multi-organ dysfunction in the late phase.
  • Aspiration pneumonia: A common complication across various overdoses, especially with altered mental status or vomiting, increasing morbidity and mortality.
