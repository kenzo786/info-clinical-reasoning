Perianal abscesses and fistulae [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Perianal abscesses and fistulae"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Perianal abscesses and fistulae"][/sc]
  • A perianal abscess is a collection of pus in the soft tissues surrounding the anus, typically resulting from an infection of the anal glands [1, 2].
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise and manage perianal abscesses and fistulae [3].
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Perianal abscesses and fistulae"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Perianal abscesses and fistulae"][/sc]
  • The annual incidence of perianal abscess is estimated between 14,000 and 20,000 people in the UK, leading to approximately 12,500 operations annually [4].
  • The prevalence of anal fistula is estimated to be 1-2 per 10,000 patients in the UK, though population-based studies are limited [5].
  • Risk factors include inflammatory bowel disease (particularly Crohn's disease), diabetes mellitus, pregnancy, smoking, and immunosuppression [6, 7].
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Perianal abscesses and fistulae"][/sc]
  • Trigger: Perianal abscesses typically originate from an infection of the anal glands, located at the dentate line, which become obstructed and inflamed [1, 8].
  • Progression: The infection leads to the formation of a pus-filled cavity (abscess). If this abscess ruptures spontaneously or is surgically drained, it may develop into an anal fistula, an abnormal epithelialised tract connecting the anal canal or rectum to the perianal skin [9, 10].
  • Consequence: Once a fistula forms, it can allow bacteria and faecal matter to enter the tract, leading to persistent infection, recurrent abscesses, and chronic drainage [11].
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Perianal abscesses and fistulae"][/sc]
  • Early phase: The initial infection of an anal gland can rapidly progress to abscess formation, typically within days to one or two weeks [12].
  • Progressive phase: If an abscess is not promptly treated or if it ruptures spontaneously, a fistula may form, often within weeks to a few months following abscess drainage [13, 14].
  • Late phase: Established fistulae can persist for months or years, leading to chronic drainage, recurrent infections, and potential complications, particularly beyond several weeks without definitive management [15].
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Perianal abscesses and fistulae"][/sc]
  • Ask about perianal pain - often described as throbbing, constant, and exacerbated by sitting, movement, or defecation, indicating inflammation and pressure from an abscess [16, 17].
  • Ask about perianal swelling or a palpable lump - a visible or palpable mass suggests an underlying collection of pus, characteristic of an abscess [18].
  • Ask about discharge from the perianal area - pus or serosanguinous fluid, often foul-smelling, is indicative of a draining abscess or an established fistula [19].
  • Ask about systemic symptoms such as fever and chills - these suggest a more widespread inflammatory response or sepsis, particularly with an undrained abscess [16].
  • Ask about discomfort during defecation or constipation - pain can lead to reluctance to pass stools, potentially worsening the condition or indicating a deeper abscess [16].
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Perianal abscesses and fistulae"][/sc]
  • Check perianal inspection - erythema, swelling, or a visible fluctuant mass near the anal orifice indicates an abscess [20]. An external opening with purulent discharge suggests a fistula [21].
  • Check digital rectal examination (DRE) - may reveal tenderness, induration, or a palpable mass within the anal canal or perirectal space, aiding in the localisation of deeper abscesses or the tract of a fistula [22].
  • Check vital signs - fever, tachycardia, or hypotension may indicate systemic infection or sepsis, particularly in the context of a large or undrained abscess [16].
  • Check for tenderness on palpation - localised pain upon touching the perianal area is a key indicator of inflammation and infection [20].
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Perianal abscesses and fistulae"][/sc]
  • Systemic sepsis: Manifested by high fever, chills, tachycardia, or hypotension, indicating a spreading infection that requires immediate intervention [16, 23].
  • Rapidly worsening local symptoms: Increasing pain, swelling, or erythema, particularly if accompanied by skin discolouration or crepitus, may suggest necrotising fasciitis, a surgical emergency [24].
  • Urinary retention: Severe perianal pain and inflammation can lead to reflex urinary retention, necessitating prompt management [25].
  • Extensive cellulitis: Spreading redness and warmth beyond the immediate perianal area, especially in immunocompromised patients, indicates a more severe infection [26].
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Perianal abscesses and fistulae"][/sc]
  • In pregnancy: Symptoms may be subtle or attributed to other pregnancy-related discomforts, potentially delaying diagnosis and increasing the risk of complications [27].
  • In immunosuppressed patients (e.g., diabetes, Crohn's disease): The inflammatory response may be blunted, leading to less pronounced local signs and symptoms, but potentially more extensive or aggressive disease, including complex fistulae in Crohn's [6, 28].
  • Deep abscesses (e.g., ischiorectal, supralevator): May present with less obvious external signs, with pain being more diffuse or referred, making diagnosis challenging and increasing the risk of systemic sepsis if undrained [29].
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Perianal abscesses and fistulae"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected perianal abscess/fistula: Clinical examination, including careful inspection and digital rectal examination, is often sufficient for initial diagnosis [30, 31]. Blood tests (e.g., full blood count, C-reactive protein) may indicate systemic inflammation or infection severity.
  • Imaging - MRI: Magnetic Resonance Imaging (MRI) is the gold standard for assessing anal fistulae, providing detailed anatomical information about the fistula tract, its relationship to sphincter muscles, and identifying any associated abscesses or extensions [32, 33, 34].
  • Imaging - Endoscopic Ultrasound (EUS): Can be used to visualise the fistula tract and internal opening, particularly useful in complex cases or when MRI is contraindicated [35].
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Consider colonoscopy to exclude inflammatory bowel disease, especially in recurrent or complex cases, or if other gastrointestinal symptoms are present.
  • Serial reassessment: Important to monitor for progression of abscess to fistula or recurrence after treatment.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Perianal abscesses and fistulae"][/sc]
  • A 45-year-old male presents with a 3-day history of severe, throbbing perianal pain, worse on sitting and defecation, associated with a tender, erythematous swelling near the anus and a low-grade fever.
  • Middle-aged male with acute, severe perianal pain and inflammatory signs, suggestive of localised infection.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Perianal abscesses and fistulae"][/sc]
  • Anal Fissure - typically presents with sharp, tearing pain during defecation and bright red rectal bleeding, without significant perianal swelling or systemic signs of infection [36].
  • Thrombosed External Haemorrhoid - characterised by acute onset of severe, constant pain and a firm, tender, bluish lump at the anal verge, but usually without purulent discharge or fever [36].
  • Pilonidal Abscess - located in the natal cleft, typically presenting with pain, swelling, and discharge in the sacrococcygeal region, distinct from the immediate perianal area [36].
  • Crohn's Disease (Perianal Manifestation) - can cause complex fistulae, skin tags, and abscesses, but often accompanied by other gastrointestinal symptoms such as abdominal pain, diarrhoea, and weight loss [6].
  • Hidradenitis Suppurativa - a chronic inflammatory skin condition causing recurrent abscesses and sinus tracts, but typically involves other apocrine gland-bearing areas like the axillae and groin, and lesions are often multiple and widespread [37].
  • Anal Cancer - a less common but important consideration for chronic, non-healing lesions or those with atypical features; may present with bleeding, pain, or a mass, but often without acute inflammatory signs unless secondarily infected [36].
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Perianal abscesses and fistulae"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Incision and drainage of perianal abscesses to relieve pain and prevent spread of infection [38]. Adequate analgesia is crucial.
  • Definitive management:
    • For abscesses: Surgical incision and drainage is the primary treatment [38].
    • For fistulae: Surgical intervention is usually required, as fistulae rarely heal spontaneously. Options include fistulotomy, seton placement, advancement flaps, or LIFT procedure, depending on the complexity and location of the fistula [39, 40].
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Regular wound care, hygiene advice, and monitoring for signs of recurrence or fistula formation after abscess drainage [41]. Follow-up is essential after fistula surgery to assess healing and identify complications.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Patients should be advised to seek urgent medical attention for worsening pain, increasing swelling, spreading redness, high fever, or systemic unwellness, as these may indicate persistent infection, sepsis, or necrotising fasciitis.
  • Exam pharmacology reference (UKMLA/MRCGP): Antibiotics are generally not indicated for uncomplicated perianal abscesses after adequate drainage [42]. However, they are used in cases of systemic infection (sepsis), significant surrounding cellulitis, in immunocompromised patients, or those with specific comorbidities like diabetes [43, 44]. Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Perianal abscesses and fistulae"][/sc]
  • Fistula-in-ano formation: Approximately 50% of perianal abscesses will develop into a chronic anal fistula, an abnormal tract connecting the anal canal to the perianal skin, leading to persistent drainage and recurrent infections [45, 46].
  • Recurrent abscesses: Often occur if an underlying fistula is not identified and treated, as the fistula provides a pathway for ongoing infection [47].
  • Sepsis: Untreated or rapidly progressing perianal abscesses can lead to systemic infection, which can be life-threatening if not promptly managed [23].
  • Necrotising fasciitis: A rare but severe complication, particularly in immunocompromised individuals, characterised by rapidly spreading infection and tissue destruction, requiring urgent surgical debridement [24].
  • Faecal incontinence: A potential long-term complication, especially after extensive or repeated surgical interventions for complex fistulae, due to damage to the anal sphincter muscles [48].
