Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Hiatus hernia (Gastrointestinal tract)"][/sc]
  • A hiatus hernia occurs when a portion of the stomach protrudes through the oesophageal hiatus of the diaphragm into the thoracic cavity.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the types and clinical presentations of hiatus hernia, distinguishing between sliding and para-oesophageal variants and their associated risks.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Hiatus hernia (Gastrointestinal tract)"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Hiatus hernia (Gastrointestinal tract)"][/sc]
  • Prevalence is estimated to be around one-third of people over 50 in the UK, with 55-60% of over 50s having a hiatus hernia, though only 9% are symptomatic [1, 2].
  • Risk factors include advanced age, obesity, pregnancy, ascites, and conditions causing oesophageal shortening [2].
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Hiatus hernia (Gastrointestinal tract)"][/sc]
  • Trigger: Weakening of the diaphragmatic musculature around the oesophageal hiatus, often exacerbated by increased intra-abdominal pressure (e.g., obesity, pregnancy) or oesophageal shortening [2].
  • Progression: The stomach, or a portion of it, herniates through the enlarged hiatus into the chest. Sliding hernias involve the gastro-oesophageal junction moving upwards, while para-oesophageal hernias involve the fundus rolling up alongside the oesophagus [2].
  • Consequence: Disruption of the anti-reflux barrier, leading to gastro-oesophageal reflux disease (GORD) in sliding hernias, and potential for obstruction, volvulus, or ischaemia in para-oesophageal hernias [2]. Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Hiatus hernia (Gastrointestinal tract)"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Often asymptomatic, or presents with mild, intermittent symptoms of GORD, such as heartburn or regurgitation, particularly after large meals or when lying down.
  • Progressive phase: Symptoms may become more frequent and severe, potentially leading to oesophagitis, dysphagia, or chronic cough, particularly beyond several months without lifestyle modification or treatment.
  • Late phase: In some cases, particularly with para-oesophageal hernias, complications such as gastric volvulus, strangulation, or significant bleeding may develop, often presenting acutely over hours to days.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Hiatus hernia (Gastrointestinal tract)"][/sc]
  • Ask about heartburn - a painful burning sensation in the chest, often worse after eating or lying down, indicating GORD due to reflux.
  • Ask about acid regurgitation - bringing up bitter-tasting fluid or food into the mouth, a direct consequence of reflux.
  • Ask about dysphagia - difficulty or pain when swallowing, which may suggest oesophageal inflammation, stricture, or, rarely, obstruction from a large hernia.
  • Ask about bloating and early satiety - feeling full quickly or having abdominal distension, which can occur with gastric displacement or delayed gastric emptying.
  • Ask about chest pain - non-cardiac chest pain can be a symptom of oesophageal spasm or irritation from reflux, or, in para-oesophageal hernias, a sign of gastric distension or ischaemia.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Hiatus hernia (Gastrointestinal tract)"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - usually normal in uncomplicated hiatus hernia; however, tachycardia or hypotension may indicate complications such as acute bleeding or strangulation.
  • Check abdominal examination - typically unremarkable; however, epigastric tenderness or distension may be present in cases of gastric obstruction or volvulus.
  • Check signs of anaemia - pallor, conjunctival pallor, or fatigue may suggest chronic blood loss from oesophagitis or gastric erosions within the hernia.
  • Check respiratory examination - usually normal; however, crackles or wheeze may be present if recurrent aspiration from severe reflux has occurred. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Aspiration pneumonia in hiatus hernia: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Aspiration pneumonia"][/sc]
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Hiatus hernia (Gastrointestinal tract)"][/sc]
  • Unexplained weight loss: May indicate underlying malignancy (e.g., oesophageal adenocarcinoma) or severe, chronic dysphagia.
  • Progressive dysphagia: Suggests oesophageal stricture, malignancy, or significant mechanical obstruction, particularly if solid food dysphagia progresses to liquids over weeks to months.
  • Haematemesis or melaena: Indicates upper gastrointestinal bleeding, potentially from severe oesophagitis, gastric erosions within the hernia, or a gastric volvulus.
  • Persistent vomiting: Can signify gastric outlet obstruction, often associated with a large para-oesophageal hernia or gastric volvulus, especially if it occurs acutely over hours.
  • Acute severe chest or upper abdominal pain with dysphagia/vomiting: Highly concerning for gastric volvulus or strangulation of a para-oesophageal hernia, requiring urgent surgical assessment.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Hiatus hernia (Gastrointestinal tract)"][/sc]
  • Older adults: May present with non-specific symptoms such as chronic cough, recurrent aspiration pneumonia, or iron-deficiency anaemia due to chronic blood loss, rather than classic heartburn.
  • Children/Infants: Can present with recurrent vomiting, failure to thrive, irritability, or respiratory symptoms like wheezing and recurrent chest infections due to reflux and aspiration.
  • Large para-oesophageal hernias: May present with post-prandial fullness, dyspnoea, or cardiac symptoms (e.g., palpitations, angina-like pain) due to mass effect on the heart and lungs. Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Hiatus hernia (Gastrointestinal tract)"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected GORD/Hiatus Hernia: Upper gastrointestinal endoscopy is the primary investigation to visualise the oesophagus and stomach, assess for hiatus hernia, oesophagitis, Barrett's oesophagus, or malignancy. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Upper GI endoscopy in hiatus hernia: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Upper GI endoscopy"][/sc]
  • Imaging - Barium swallow: Can demonstrate the presence and type of hiatus hernia (sliding vs. para-oesophageal) and assess for reflux or oesophageal motility disorders.
  • Imaging - Chest X-ray: May incidentally show a retrocardiac air-fluid level, diagnostic of a para-oesophageal hiatus hernia, particularly in acute presentations.
  • Severity/risk tools: Consider using the Glasgow Blatchford Score on MDCalc or Rockall Score on MDCalc for risk stratification in patients presenting with upper GI bleeding.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Hiatus hernia (Gastrointestinal tract)"][/sc]
  • Gastro-oesophageal reflux disease (GORD) without hiatus hernia - similar symptoms but absence of anatomical herniation on endoscopy.
  • Oesophagitis (non-reflux) - inflammation of the oesophagus due to other causes (e.g., eosinophilic, infective), often with dysphagia and odynophagia but distinct endoscopic findings.
  • Peptic ulcer disease - epigastric pain, often relieved by food or antacids, but typically without prominent regurgitation; diagnosed by endoscopy.
  • Oesophageal malignancy - progressive dysphagia, weight loss, and anaemia, particularly in older patients; requires urgent endoscopic evaluation. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Oesophageal adenocarcinoma: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Oesophageal adenocarcinoma"][/sc]
  • Achalasia - dysphagia to both solids and liquids, regurgitation of undigested food, and chest pain, but manometry shows absent peristalsis and impaired lower oesophageal sphincter relaxation.
  • Cardiac chest pain - angina or myocardial infarction can mimic oesophageal pain; distinguished by ECG, cardiac biomarkers, and response to nitrates.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Hiatus hernia (Gastrointestinal tract)"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Recognition of complications (e.g., gastric volvulus, acute bleeding) warrants urgent surgical assessment and supportive care.
  • Definitive management: Lifestyle modifications (e.g., weight loss, smaller meals, elevating head of bed) are often recommended. Pharmacological management typically involves proton pump inhibitors (PPIs) for symptom control in GORD.
  • Adjunctive management: Antacids or H2-receptor antagonists may support symptom relief, particularly for breakthrough symptoms.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Regular review of symptoms and medication effectiveness. Endoscopy may be repeated for persistent or worsening symptoms, or for surveillance in Barrett's oesophagus.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Patients with persistent severe symptoms despite optimal medical therapy, or those with complications of para-oesophageal hernia, may be considered for surgical intervention (e.g., laparoscopic fundoplication).

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Hiatus hernia (Gastrointestinal tract)"][/sc]
  • Gastro-oesophageal reflux disease (GORD): The most common complication of sliding hiatus hernias, leading to chronic heartburn, regurgitation, and potential oesophagitis, developing over weeks to months.
  • Oesophagitis and stricture: Chronic acid exposure can cause inflammation (oesophagitis) and subsequent scarring, leading to oesophageal strictures and progressive dysphagia over months to years.
  • Barrett's oesophagus: Metaplastic change of the oesophageal lining due to chronic reflux, increasing the risk of oesophageal adenocarcinoma over many years.
  • Oesophageal adenocarcinoma: A serious long-term complication, particularly in patients with Barrett's oesophagus, developing insidiously over years.
  • Gastric volvulus: A rare but acute and life-threatening complication of para-oesophageal hernias, where the stomach twists on its axis, presenting with sudden severe pain, retching, and inability to vomit, typically over hours. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Para-oesophageal hernia complications: what are they and why do they matter clinically?" text="Para-oesophageal hernia complications"][/sc]
  • Haemorrhage: Chronic blood loss from oesophagitis or gastric erosions within the hernia can lead to iron-deficiency anaemia, while acute severe bleeding can present as haematemesis or melaena over hours.
