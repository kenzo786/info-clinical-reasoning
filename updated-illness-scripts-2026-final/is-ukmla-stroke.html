Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Stroke (Brain and spinal cord)"][/sc]
  • A stroke is a clinical syndrome of rapidly developing focal or global neurological impairment, with symptoms lasting more than 24 hours or leading to death, with no apparent cause other than that of vascular origin. It is primarily caused by ischaemia (85%) or haemorrhage (15%).
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the signs and symptoms of acute stroke and transient ischaemic attack (TIA), differentiate between ischaemic and haemorrhagic stroke, and understand the principles of immediate management and secondary prevention.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Stroke (Brain and spinal cord)"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Stroke"][/sc]
  • Stroke is a major cause of morbidity and mortality in the UK, with over 100,000 strokes occurring each year, leading to approximately 34,000 deaths. It is a leading cause of disability.
  • The incidence of stroke is projected to rise, with an estimated increase from 117,600 in 2015 to 186,900 in 2035 in the UK.
  • Key risk factors include hypertension (the single biggest risk factor), atrial fibrillation (accounting for at least 20% of ischaemic strokes), diabetes, hyperlipidaemia, smoking, obesity, and a sedentary lifestyle.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Stroke (Brain and spinal cord)"][/sc]
  • Trigger: Ischaemic stroke typically results from thrombotic or embolic occlusion of a cerebral artery, often due to atherosclerosis or cardiac emboli (e.g., from atrial fibrillation). Haemorrhagic stroke occurs due to rupture of a blood vessel, commonly from uncontrolled hypertension or cerebral aneurysms.
  • Progression: In ischaemic stroke, the cessation of blood flow leads to neuronal energy failure and cell death in the core infarct zone. Surrounding this is the [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Ischaemic penumbra: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="ischaemic penumbra"], a region of hypoperfused but still viable tissue that can be salvaged if reperfusion is achieved promptly.
  • Consequence: Irreversible neuronal damage in the infarct core leads to focal neurological deficits corresponding to the affected brain region. Haemorrhagic stroke causes direct tissue damage, mass effect, and secondary ischaemia due to increased intracranial pressure.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Stroke (Brain and spinal cord)"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Acute onset of neurological symptoms, typically evolving over minutes to hours. This critical window is when interventions like thrombolysis (within 4.5 hours) and thrombectomy (up to 6-24 hours in selected cases) are most effective.
  • Progressive phase: Symptoms may worsen over the first 24-72 hours due to oedema, haemorrhagic transformation, or further ischaemia. Careful monitoring and supportive care are crucial during this period.
  • Late phase: Beyond the acute phase, recovery and rehabilitation efforts focus on mitigating long-term disability. Complications such as spasticity, dysphagia, and cognitive impairment may manifest weeks to months after the initial event.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Stroke (Brain and spinal cord)"][/sc]
  • Ask about facial weakness - often unilateral, causing asymmetry, and can be a key component of the FAST test.
  • Ask about arm weakness - typically unilateral, presenting as an inability to raise one arm or maintain its position, another component of the FAST test.
  • Ask about speech disturbance - may manifest as dysarthria (slurred speech) or aphasia (difficulty understanding or producing language), crucial for identifying neurological compromise.
  • Ask about sudden onset of symptoms - the abruptness of neurological deficit is highly characteristic of stroke and differentiates it from more gradual onset conditions.
  • Ask about visual disturbance - sudden loss of vision in one eye (amaurosis fugax) or a visual field defect (e.g., homonymous hemianopia) can indicate posterior circulation involvement.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Stroke (Brain and spinal cord)"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - hypertension is common and can indicate aetiology (haemorrhagic stroke) or be a risk factor; fever may suggest infection or central thermoregulation dysfunction; hypoxia can worsen cerebral ischaemia.
  • Check facial asymmetry - observe for drooping of one side of the face, particularly when smiling or attempting to show teeth, indicating facial nerve involvement.
  • Check motor weakness - assess for unilateral arm drift, leg weakness, or hemiparesis, which localises the lesion to the contralateral motor cortex or descending tracts.
  • Check speech - listen for dysarthria (slurred, indistinct speech) or aphasia (difficulty with language comprehension or expression), which points to dominant hemisphere involvement.
  • Check eye movements - gaze deviation, nystagmus, or visual field defects can indicate specific brainstem or cortical lesions.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Stroke (Brain and spinal cord)"][/sc]
  • Sudden onset of focal neurological deficit - demands immediate assessment and imaging to determine stroke type and eligibility for acute reperfusion therapies within the critical time window.
  • Decreased level of consciousness - may indicate a large stroke, brainstem involvement, or increased intracranial pressure, requiring urgent neurosurgical review.
  • Signs of meningism (e.g., neck stiffness, photophobia) - can suggest subarachnoid haemorrhage, a type of haemorrhagic stroke, necessitating prompt investigation.
  • Rapidly worsening symptoms within hours - indicates ongoing neurological damage and necessitates urgent re-evaluation for potential interventions or complications.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Stroke (Brain and spinal cord)"][/sc]
  • Posterior circulation stroke: May present with isolated vertigo, ataxia, diplopia, or unilateral limb weakness without typical FAST symptoms, making diagnosis challenging.
  • Wake-up stroke: Symptoms are noticed upon waking, making the exact time of onset unknown. This impacts eligibility for time-dependent treatments like thrombolysis, requiring advanced imaging to estimate stroke age.
  • Transient Ischaemic Attack (TIA): Transient neurological dysfunction caused by focal brain, spinal cord, or retinal ischaemia, without acute infarction. Symptoms typically resolve within minutes, but it is a strong predictor of future stroke.
  • Stroke mimics: Conditions such as seizures, migraine with aura, hypoglycaemia, or functional neurological disorder can present with stroke-like symptoms, requiring careful differentiation.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Stroke (Brain and spinal cord)"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected stroke: Immediate non-contrast CT head to differentiate ischaemic from haemorrhagic stroke. This is crucial as management differs significantly.
  • Severity/risk tools: The [sc name="jitl-query" Q="NIHSS: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="National Institutes of Health Stroke Scale (NIHSS)"] is used to quantify neurological deficit and monitor changes. The ABCD2 score on MDCalc is used to assess stroke risk after TIA.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Blood glucose (to rule out hypoglycaemia), full blood count, electrolytes, renal function, and ECG (to detect atrial fibrillation).
  • Imaging - CT Angiography (CTA): Performed to identify large vessel occlusion in ischaemic stroke, particularly if thrombectomy is being considered.
  • Imaging - CT Perfusion (CTP) or MRI: May be used in selected cases, especially for wake-up strokes or those presenting in an extended time window, to identify the [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Ischaemic penumbra: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="ischaemic penumbra"] and guide reperfusion therapy decisions.
  • Serial reassessment: Regular neurological observations and repeat imaging if clinical deterioration occurs.

  • An elderly male with vascular risk factors presenting with acute, focal neurological deficits, concerning for an evolving cerebrovascular event.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Stroke (Brain and spinal cord)"][/sc]
  • Seizure with Todd\'s paresis - typically involves post-ictal weakness that resolves over hours, often preceded by seizure activity, unlike the abrupt onset of stroke.
  • Migraine with aura - neurological symptoms usually evolve gradually over minutes and are often followed by a headache, which is less common in acute stroke.
  • Hypoglycaemia - can cause focal neurological deficits that rapidly resolve with glucose administration, a key differentiator from stroke.
  • Bell\'s palsy - causes unilateral facial weakness but typically spares the forehead, unlike central facial weakness seen in stroke.
  • Intracranial tumour - symptoms usually have a more gradual, progressive onset over weeks to months, although acute presentations can occur due to haemorrhage or oedema.
  • Functional neurological disorder - neurological signs are often inconsistent, non-anatomical, and may be incongruent with organic disease patterns.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Stroke (Brain and spinal cord)"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Urgent transfer to a specialist stroke unit. Initial assessment includes ABCDE, rapid neurological assessment (e.g., FAST, NIHSS), and immediate non-contrast CT head to differentiate ischaemic from haemorrhagic stroke.
  • Definitive management (ischaemic stroke): Intravenous thrombolysis with [sc name="jitl-medication" Q="Alteplase use in ischaemic stroke" text="alteplase"] may be considered for eligible patients presenting within 4.5 hours of symptom onset. Mechanical thrombectomy may be offered for large vessel occlusion within 6 hours, and up to 24 hours in selected cases based on imaging.
  • Definitive management (haemorrhagic stroke): Management focuses on blood pressure control, reversal of anticoagulation if applicable, and neurosurgical consultation for potential intervention (e.g., aneurysm clipping/coiling, haematoma evacuation).
  • Adjunctive management: Supportive care includes maintaining normoglycaemia, normothermia, and adequate hydration. Early mobilisation and dysphagia screening are crucial.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Close neurological monitoring in a stroke unit. Secondary prevention strategies, including antiplatelets (e.g., aspirin, clopidogrel), anticoagulation (for AF), statins, and blood pressure control, are initiated.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Patients and families are counselled on recognising signs of neurological deterioration or recurrent stroke, and advised on when to seek urgent medical attention.
  • Exam pharmacology reference (UKMLA/MRCGP): [sc name="jitl-medication" Q="Alteplase use in ischaemic stroke" text="Alteplase"] (recombinant tissue plasminogen activator) is a fibrinolytic agent used in acute ischaemic stroke to dissolve clots. [sc name="jitl-medication" Q="Aspirin use in stroke prevention" text="Aspirin"] and [sc name="jitl-medication" Q="Clopidogrel use in stroke prevention" text="Clopidogrel"] are antiplatelet agents used for secondary prevention. Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Stroke (Brain and spinal cord)"][/sc]
  • Haemorrhagic transformation: Ischaemic strokes can convert to haemorrhagic strokes, particularly after reperfusion therapies, leading to worsening neurological deficits and increased mortality.
  • Cerebral oedema and raised intracranial pressure: Can develop within the first 24-72 hours, especially with large infarcts, potentially causing brain herniation and death.
  • Aspiration pneumonia: Common in patients with dysphagia, particularly in the early and progressive phases, increasing morbidity and mortality.
  • Deep vein thrombosis (DVT) and pulmonary embolism (PE): Immobility post-stroke increases the risk of venous thromboembolism, which can be fatal.
  • Post-stroke epilepsy: Seizures can occur acutely or in the late phase, impacting long-term quality of life and requiring anticonvulsant management.
  • Depression and anxiety: Common psychological complications that can significantly hinder rehabilitation and recovery, often manifesting weeks to months after the event.
