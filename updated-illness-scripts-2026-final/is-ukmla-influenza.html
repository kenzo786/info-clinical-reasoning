Influenza (Infections) [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Influenza (Infections)"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Influenza (Infections)"][/sc]
  • Influenza, commonly known as the flu, is an acute viral respiratory infection caused by influenza viruses, primarily types A and B, which circulate globally and cause seasonal epidemics.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical presentation of influenza, identify individuals at high risk of complications, and understand the principles of antiviral treatment and vaccination strategies.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Influenza (Infections)"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Influenza (Infections)"][/sc]
  • In a mean season, around 1.5% of the UK population are estimated to present to GPs with diverse respiratory syndromes attributable to influenza A or B [1].
  • Influenza activity in the UK typically peaks during the winter months, with varying intensity each year depending on circulating strains and population immunity [2].
  • Risk factors for severe influenza include age (very young children and older adults >65 years), chronic respiratory, cardiac, renal, hepatic, neurological diseases, diabetes mellitus, immunosuppression, and pregnancy [3].
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Influenza (Infections)"][/sc]
  • Influenza viruses (orthomyxoviruses) primarily target and replicate in the respiratory epithelial cells, leading to cell damage and inflammation.
  • Antigenic drift involves minor genetic mutations in the haemagglutinin (HA) and neuraminidase (NA) surface proteins, leading to new strains that can evade existing immunity and cause seasonal epidemics [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Antigenic drift: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Antigenic drift"][/sc].
  • Antigenic shift involves major, abrupt changes in HA or NA proteins, typically through reassortment of human and animal influenza viruses, which can lead to pandemics due to a completely novel strain to which the population has no immunity [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Antigenic shift: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Antigenic shift"][/sc].
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Influenza (Infections)"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Symptoms typically appear abruptly 1-4 days (average 2 days) after exposure, including fever, cough, sore throat, muscle aches, and headache.
  • Progressive phase: Symptoms usually worsen over 2-3 days, with the acute illness lasting for 5-7 days. Cough and malaise may persist for 2 weeks or longer.
  • Late phase: Resolution of acute symptoms, though fatigue and cough can linger. Secondary bacterial infections, such as pneumonia, typically develop beyond the first week of illness.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Influenza (Infections)"][/sc]
  • Ask about sudden onset of fever - often high, indicating an acute systemic inflammatory response.
  • Ask about dry cough - a prominent and often persistent symptom, distinguishing it from the typically wetter cough of a common cold.
  • Ask about muscle aches (myalgia) and joint pain (arthralgia) - widespread body pain is characteristic of influenza and can be severe.
  • Ask about headache - often frontal and intense, contributing to overall malaise.
  • Ask about sore throat and nasal congestion - common upper respiratory symptoms, though usually less prominent than systemic symptoms.
  • Ask about fatigue and malaise - profound tiredness and a general feeling of unwellness that can be debilitating.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Influenza (Infections)"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - fever (often >38°C), tachycardia, and tachypnoea may be present, indicating systemic illness.
  • Check for pharyngeal erythema - redness of the throat, often without significant exudates.
  • Check for cervical lymphadenopathy - mild, tender enlargement of cervical lymph nodes may be present.
  • Check for signs of respiratory distress - increased work of breathing, use of accessory muscles, or cyanosis, suggesting severe illness or complications like pneumonia [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Respiratory distress in influenza: what are the signs and why do they matter clinically?" text="Respiratory distress"][/sc].
  • Check for crackles or wheezes on chest auscultation - may indicate lower respiratory tract involvement or secondary pneumonia.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Influenza (Infections)"][/sc]
  • Shortness of breath or difficulty breathing - suggests severe respiratory compromise, potentially due to primary viral pneumonia or secondary bacterial pneumonia, requiring urgent medical attention.
  • Chest pain or pressure - may indicate cardiac complications (e.g., myocarditis) or severe pulmonary involvement.
  • Sudden dizziness or confusion - points to neurological complications (e.g., encephalitis) or severe systemic illness with organ dysfunction.
  • Severe or persistent vomiting - can lead to dehydration and electrolyte imbalance, particularly in children and older adults.
  • Worsening of chronic medical conditions - acute exacerbation of underlying conditions (e.g., asthma, COPD, heart failure) due to influenza infection.
  • Return of fever and worsening cough after initial improvement - highly suggestive of a secondary bacterial infection, most commonly pneumonia, typically occurring 5-10 days into the illness.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Influenza (Infections)"][/sc]
  • Older adults: May present with less prominent fever and respiratory symptoms, instead exhibiting confusion, falls, or general weakness, making diagnosis challenging.
  • Young children: Can present with gastrointestinal symptoms (vomiting, diarrhoea) more frequently than adults, and may develop febrile seizures.
  • Immunosuppressed individuals: May have prolonged viral shedding, atypical or severe presentations, and a higher risk of complications, including primary viral pneumonia.
  • Pregnant women: Are at increased risk of severe illness and complications, including pneumonia and adverse pregnancy outcomes, due to physiological changes in pregnancy [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Influenza in pregnancy: why is it high-risk and how does it present?" text="Influenza in pregnancy"][/sc].
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Influenza (Infections)"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected influenza: Clinical diagnosis is often sufficient during periods of high community influenza activity. Rapid influenza diagnostic tests (RIDTs) or polymerase chain reaction (PCR) tests on respiratory samples (nasopharyngeal swab) can confirm diagnosis, particularly in hospitalised patients or those at high risk of complications.
  • Severity/risk tools: CURB-65 score for pneumonia severity can be used to assess the need for hospital admission in patients with suspected influenza and lower respiratory tract infection. CURB-65 Score on MDCalc
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Consider bacterial cultures (sputum, blood) if secondary bacterial infection is suspected, especially pneumonia.
  • Imaging - Chest X-ray: Indicated if pneumonia is suspected (e.g., new or worsening cough, dyspnoea, focal chest signs) to identify infiltrates.
  • Serial reassessment: Close monitoring of vital signs and clinical status is crucial, especially in high-risk individuals, to detect deterioration or development of complications.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Influenza (Infections)"][/sc]
  • A 45-year-old male presents with sudden onset of high fever, severe myalgia, dry cough, and headache for 2 days, during a period of high community influenza activity.
  • Acute febrile respiratory illness with prominent systemic symptoms, concerning for influenza.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Influenza (Infections)"][/sc]
  • Common cold - typically milder symptoms, less prominent fever and systemic upset, more nasal congestion and sneezing.
  • Other viral respiratory infections (e.g., RSV, adenovirus, rhinovirus) - can mimic influenza, often requiring viral testing for definitive differentiation.
  • Bacterial pneumonia - may present with similar respiratory symptoms but often with purulent sputum, focal chest signs, and higher inflammatory markers.
  • COVID-19 - shares many symptoms with influenza, requiring specific testing for differentiation, especially given overlapping seasonal prevalence [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Differentiating influenza from COVID-19: what are the key clinical distinctions?" text="COVID-19"][/sc].
  • Acute bronchitis - persistent cough, often productive, but usually without the severe systemic symptoms of influenza.
  • Meningitis/Encephalitis - consider if severe headache, neck stiffness, photophobia, or altered mental status are prominent, especially in atypical presentations.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Influenza (Infections)"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Supportive care including adequate hydration, rest, and symptomatic relief with paracetamol or ibuprofen. Early recognition of high-risk individuals and potential complications is crucial.
  • Definitive management: Antiviral medications (oseltamivir, zanamivir) may be considered for treatment in individuals with confirmed or suspected influenza who are in an at-risk group, or who present with severe or progressive illness. Treatment is most effective when initiated within 48 hours of symptom onset [3].
  • Adjunctive management: Management of complications such as secondary bacterial pneumonia with appropriate antibiotics.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Close monitoring for signs of deterioration or development of complications, particularly in high-risk groups. Safety-netting advice should be provided.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Advise patients and carers on symptoms that warrant urgent medical review (e.g., worsening shortness of breath, chest pain, confusion, return of fever after initial improvement).
  • Exam pharmacology reference (UKMLA/MRCGP): Oseltamivir and zanamivir are neuraminidase inhibitors used for treatment and prophylaxis of influenza. Oseltamivir is administered orally, while zanamivir is inhaled. Amantadine is not recommended due to resistance and side effects [3]. Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Influenza (Infections)"][/sc]
  • Primary viral pneumonia: Direct viral damage to lung tissue, leading to severe respiratory failure, often rapidly progressive and occurring within the first few days of illness.
  • Secondary bacterial pneumonia: Bacterial superinfection, most commonly by *Streptococcus pneumoniae*, *Staphylococcus aureus*, or *Haemophilus influenzae*, typically presenting with worsening respiratory symptoms and fever after initial improvement, usually 5-10 days into the illness.
  • Exacerbation of underlying chronic diseases: Influenza can trigger acute exacerbations of conditions like asthma, COPD, and congestive heart failure, leading to significant morbidity.
  • Myocarditis/Pericarditis: Inflammation of the heart muscle or pericardium, which can lead to arrhythmias, heart failure, or sudden cardiac death, though rare.
  • Encephalitis/Meningitis: Rare but severe neurological complications, presenting with altered mental status, seizures, or focal neurological deficits.
  • Rhabdomyolysis: Breakdown of muscle tissue, leading to muscle pain, weakness, and potentially acute kidney injury, particularly in severe cases.
