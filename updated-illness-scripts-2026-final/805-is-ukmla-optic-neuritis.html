Optic neuritis [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Optic neuritis"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Optic neuritis"][/sc]
  • Optic neuritis is an inflammatory demyelinating condition affecting the optic nerve, leading to acute or subacute vision loss, often accompanied by pain on eye movement.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical features of optic neuritis, differentiate it from other causes of acute vision loss, and understand its association with demyelinating diseases like multiple sclerosis.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Optic neuritis"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Optic neuritis"][/sc]
  • The incidence of optic neuritis in the UK is approximately 3.7 per 100,000 person-years, with a prevalence of 115 per 100,000 population.
  • It typically affects adults aged 20-40 years, with a mean age of onset around 36 years. Females are more commonly affected than males (odds ratio 1.31).
  • Approximately 50% of individuals experiencing an isolated episode of optic neuritis will develop multiple sclerosis within 15 years, particularly those with specific genetic predispositions (e.g., HLA-DRB1*1501 genotype).
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Optic neuritis"][/sc]
  • Optic neuritis is triggered by an autoimmune inflammatory response, where the body\'s immune system mistakenly attacks the myelin sheath surrounding the optic nerve.
  • This inflammation leads to demyelination and axonal damage within the optic nerve, impairing its ability to transmit visual signals from the eye to the brain.
  • The consequence is acute or subacute vision loss, often temporary, but with potential for residual visual deficits and a significant association with future development of multiple sclerosis. Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Optic neuritis"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Symptoms typically develop over hours to a few days, with vision loss reaching its nadir within 1-2 weeks. Pain on eye movement often precedes or accompanies vision loss.
  • Progressive phase: Vision usually begins to improve spontaneously within 2-4 weeks of onset, continuing over several months.
  • Late phase: Most patients experience significant visual recovery, though some may have persistent deficits such as reduced visual acuity, colour desaturation, or contrast sensitivity. Recurrence is possible, particularly in those with underlying demyelinating disease.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Optic neuritis"][/sc]
  • Ask about pain on eye movement - often precedes or accompanies vision loss, a key discriminator for demyelinating optic neuritis.
  • Ask about acute or subacute monocular vision loss - typically develops over hours to a few days, ranging from blurred vision to complete loss.
  • Ask about colour desaturation - particularly red desaturation, where colours appear washed out or less vibrant in the affected eye, a sensitive indicator of optic nerve dysfunction.
  • Ask about blind spots or visual field defects - patients may report areas of missing vision, commonly a central scotoma.
  • Ask about heat-related worsening of vision (Uhthoff\'s phenomenon) - a transient worsening of symptoms with increased body temperature, suggestive of underlying demyelinating disease.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Optic neuritis"][/sc]
  • Check visual acuity - typically reduced in the affected eye, ranging from mild to severe.
  • Check colour vision - often impaired, especially red-green discrimination, which can be tested with Ishihara plates or a red cap desaturation test.
  • Check pupillary reflexes - a relative afferent pupillary defect (RAPD) in the affected eye is a hallmark sign, indicating reduced afferent input from the optic nerve. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Relative Afferent Pupillary Defect (RAPD): what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Relative Afferent Pupillary Defect (RAPD)"][/sc]
  • Check fundoscopy - may be normal (retrobulbar optic neuritis) or show optic disc oedema (papillitis).
  • Check vital signs - typically normal; abnormal vital signs may suggest a systemic inflammatory or infectious cause.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Optic neuritis"][/sc]
  • Severe bilateral vision loss - suggests alternative diagnoses such as neuromyelitis optica spectrum disorder (NMOSD), toxic optic neuropathy, or compressive lesions.
  • Absence of pain - while not universal, pain on eye movement is characteristic of demyelinating optic neuritis; its absence should prompt consideration of other causes.
  • Progressive worsening beyond 2 weeks or lack of improvement after 4 weeks - typical optic neuritis improves spontaneously within this timeframe; persistent deterioration suggests an alternative or more severe underlying pathology.
  • Significant optic disc oedema with macular star - may indicate neuroretinitis, which has a different aetiology and prognosis.
  • Systemic symptoms - fever, profound headache, or other focal neurological deficits (e.g., motor weakness, sensory loss) should raise suspicion for systemic inflammatory diseases, infections, or vasculitis.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Optic neuritis"][/sc]
  • Paediatric optic neuritis: Can present with bilateral involvement and more prominent optic disc oedema, making differentiation from papilloedema challenging.
  • Painless optic neuritis: While pain on eye movement is characteristic, a subset of patients, particularly those with MS, may experience painless vision loss, which can delay diagnosis.
  • Recurrent optic neuritis: Multiple episodes in the same or contralateral eye are common, especially in the context of MS or other autoimmune conditions like NMOSD or MOGAD.
  • Optic neuritis with normal MRI: A normal brain MRI does not exclude optic neuritis, especially in isolated episodes, but may prompt further investigation for other causes of optic neuropathy.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Optic neuritis"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected optic neuritis:
    • Full ophthalmic examination: including visual acuity, colour vision, visual fields, pupillary reflexes (for RAPD), and fundoscopy.
    • Brain and orbital MRI with contrast: essential to assess for demyelinating lesions in the brain (suggestive of MS) and inflammation of the optic nerve. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="MRI in optic neuritis: what does it show and why is it important?" text="MRI in optic neuritis"][/sc]
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible:
    • Blood tests: FBC, ESR, CRP, ANA, ENA, ANCA, Aquaporin-4 (AQP4) antibody, Myelin Oligodendrocyte Glycoprotein (MOG) antibody – to rule out systemic inflammatory conditions, NMOSD, or MOGAD.
    • Lumbar puncture: to analyse CSF for oligoclonal bands, which can support a diagnosis of MS.
  • Imaging - Optical Coherence Tomography (OCT): Measures retinal nerve fibre layer thickness, which can show axonal loss after an episode of optic neuritis.
  • Serial reassessment: Regular follow-up to monitor visual recovery and assess for new neurological symptoms, particularly in patients at high risk of developing MS.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Optic neuritis"][/sc]
  • A 30-year-old female presents with acute onset, painful monocular vision loss, colour desaturation, and a relative afferent pupillary defect.
  • Acute, painful, monocular vision loss with RAPD, concerning for inflammatory demyelination of the optic nerve.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Optic neuritis"][/sc]
  • Ischaemic optic neuropathy - typically painless, sudden vision loss, often in older patients with vascular risk factors.
  • Compressive optic neuropathy - gradual, progressive vision loss, often painless, with signs of optic disc pallor on fundoscopy.
  • Infectious optic neuropathy - may be associated with systemic signs of infection, often bilateral, and can be painful.
  • Toxic/nutritional optic neuropathy - bilateral, progressive vision loss, associated with exposure to toxins or nutritional deficiencies.
  • Neuromyelitis Optica Spectrum Disorder (NMOSD) - often more severe vision loss, frequently bilateral, and associated with AQP4 antibodies. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Neuromyelitis Optica Spectrum Disorder (NMOSD): what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="NMOSD"][/sc]
  • Myelin Oligodendrocyte Glycoprotein Antibody-Associated Disease (MOGAD) - can mimic MS, but often presents with more severe optic neuritis, sometimes bilateral, and may have better visual recovery.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Optic neuritis"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Recognition of acute vision loss and prompt referral to ophthalmology or neurology for specialist assessment and diagnosis.
  • Definitive management: High-dose intravenous corticosteroids (e.g., methylprednisolone) may be considered to accelerate visual recovery, particularly in severe cases, though long-term visual outcome may not be significantly altered.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Close monitoring for visual recovery and development of new neurological symptoms. Patients with optic neuritis, especially those with brain MRI lesions, often warrant referral to a neurologist for assessment of MS risk and potential disease-modifying therapies.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Optic neuritis"][/sc]
  • Permanent vision loss: Despite recovery, some patients may experience residual visual deficits, including reduced acuity, colour desaturation, or contrast sensitivity, particularly after severe or recurrent episodes.
  • Optic atrophy: Chronic inflammation and axonal damage can lead to thinning of the retinal nerve fibre layer and optic disc pallor, indicative of irreversible nerve damage.
  • Recurrence: Optic neuritis can recur in the same or contralateral eye, increasing the risk of cumulative visual impairment.
  • Development of Multiple Sclerosis (MS): Approximately 50% of patients with an isolated episode of optic neuritis will develop MS within 15 years, especially if brain MRI shows demyelinating lesions.
  • Depression and anxiety: The impact of vision loss and the uncertainty of an MS diagnosis can significantly affect mental health.
