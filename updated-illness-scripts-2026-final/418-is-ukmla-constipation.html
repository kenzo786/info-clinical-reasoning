Constipation [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Constipation"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Constipation"][/sc]
  • Constipation is a symptom-based disorder characterized by unsatisfactory defecation due to infrequent stools, difficult stool passage, or a sensation of incomplete evacuation [1].
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise and assess constipation, differentiating between primary (functional) and secondary causes, and formulate an initial management plan.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Constipation"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Constipation"][/sc]
  • Chronic constipation affects approximately 1 in 7 adults in the UK [2].
  • The incidence is 2–3 times higher in women than in men, and it is more common in older adults, with over 50% of those in nursing homes affected [3].
  • Risk factors include insufficient physical activity, low dietary fibre intake, inadequate fluid intake, certain medications (e.g., opioids, anticholinergics), and underlying medical conditions such as hypothyroidism or neurological disorders [4].
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Constipation"][/sc]
  • Reduced colonic motility or impaired anorectal function can lead to slower transit of faecal matter through the colon, allowing for increased water reabsorption and hardening of stools.
  • Disruption of the defecation reflex, often due to habitual suppression or pelvic floor dysfunction, can result in stool retention and a cycle of worsening constipation [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Defecation reflex: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="defecation reflex"][/sc].
  • The consequence is difficult, infrequent, and often painful passage of stools, potentially leading to complications such as faecal impaction or haemorrhoids.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Constipation"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Acute constipation typically develops over days to weeks, often triggered by dietary changes, medication, or acute illness.
  • Progressive phase: If underlying causes are not addressed, or if there is habitual stool withholding, acute constipation can progress to chronic constipation, defined by symptoms lasting for at least three months [5].
  • Late phase: Prolonged chronic constipation, particularly beyond several months, can lead to complications such as faecal impaction, overflow diarrhoea, or significant impact on quality of life.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Constipation"][/sc]
  • Ask about stool frequency and consistency - Infrequent bowel movements (fewer than three per week) and hard stools (Bristol Stool Chart types 1-2) are key diagnostic indicators [6].
  • Ask about straining during defecation - Suggests difficult stool passage, a common feature of functional constipation.
  • Ask about sensation of incomplete evacuation - Indicates a potential issue with anorectal function or stool retention.
  • Ask about abdominal pain or bloating - Often associated with constipation, particularly in irritable bowel syndrome with constipation (IBS-C).
  • Ask about need for manual manoeuvres to facilitate defecation - A red flag for pelvic floor dysfunction or outlet obstruction.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Constipation"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - Usually normal in uncomplicated constipation, but fever or tachycardia may indicate complications like perforation or severe infection.
  • Check abdominal examination - May reveal distension, tenderness, or palpable faecal masses, particularly in the left iliac fossa [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Faecal impaction: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="faecal impaction"][/sc].
  • Check digital rectal examination (DRE) - Assesses anal tone, presence of faeces in the rectum, and can identify anorectal abnormalities or pelvic floor dysfunction.
  • Check Bristol Stool Chart - Visual assessment of stool consistency provides an objective measure for diagnosis and monitoring [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Bristol Stool Chart: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Bristol Stool Chart"][/sc].
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Constipation"][/sc]
  • New onset constipation in older adults (>50 years): May indicate underlying colorectal malignancy and warrants urgent investigation.
  • Rectal bleeding or unexplained anaemia: Suggests gastrointestinal pathology, including malignancy or inflammatory bowel disease.
  • Unexplained weight loss: A concerning symptom that may point towards malignancy or other systemic illness.
  • Abdominal mass: Could indicate a tumour, faecal impaction, or other serious intra-abdominal pathology.
  • Failure to respond to conventional treatment: Persistent symptoms despite appropriate laxative use may require further investigation to identify secondary causes or complex functional disorders.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Constipation"][/sc]
  • Older adults: May present with confusion, falls, or urinary incontinence due to faecal impaction, rather than classic constipation symptoms.
  • Children: Often presents as stool withholding, soiling (encopresis), or abdominal pain, with a fear of defecation [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Encopresis: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="encopresis"][/sc].
  • Patients on opioids: Opioid-induced constipation (OIC) is common and often severe, requiring specific management strategies beyond conventional laxatives.
  • Neurological conditions: Patients with Parkinson\'s disease, multiple sclerosis, or spinal cord injury frequently experience constipation due to impaired gut motility and neurological control.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Constipation"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected constipation: History and physical examination, including digital rectal examination, are usually sufficient for diagnosis of functional constipation based on Rome IV criteria [7].
  • Severity/risk tools: The Rome IV Diagnostic Criteria for Functional Constipation can be used to formally diagnose functional constipation Rome IV Diagnostic Criteria for Functional Constipation on MDCalc [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Rome IV criteria for functional constipation: what are they and why do they matter clinically?" text="Rome IV criteria"][/sc].
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Full blood count, thyroid function tests, calcium, and glucose to exclude secondary causes.
  • Imaging - Colonoscopy: Indicated for new onset constipation in patients over 50 years, or those with red flag symptoms, to exclude colorectal malignancy or inflammatory bowel disease.
  • Imaging - Abdominal X-ray: May be considered in severe cases to assess for faecal loading or impaction.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Constipation"][/sc]
  • A 65-year-old female presents with a 3-month history of infrequent bowel movements (once a week), significant straining, and a sensation of incomplete evacuation, without red flag symptoms.
  • Chronic functional constipation in an older adult, concerning for impact on quality of life and potential for faecal impaction.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Constipation"][/sc]
  • Irritable Bowel Syndrome (IBS-C): Characterized by abdominal pain associated with changes in bowel habits, which is not typically a prominent feature of functional constipation alone [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Irritable Bowel Syndrome with Constipation (IBS-C): what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="IBS-C"][/sc].
  • Colorectal malignancy: Often presents with new onset constipation, rectal bleeding, weight loss, or anaemia, which are red flag symptoms not typically seen in functional constipation.
  • Hypothyroidism: May cause generalized slowing of bodily functions, including gut motility, leading to constipation. Thyroid function tests can differentiate.
  • Medication-induced constipation: A thorough medication history can identify drugs such as opioids, anticholinergics, or iron supplements as the cause.
  • Neurological disorders: Conditions like Parkinson\'s disease or multiple sclerosis can impair neurological control of bowel function, leading to constipation.
  • Pelvic floor dysfunction: Impaired coordination of pelvic floor muscles during defecation can cause outlet obstruction symptoms, which may be distinguished by specific anorectal physiology tests.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Constipation"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Recognition of red flag symptoms warranting urgent investigation, and supportive care including adequate fluid intake and dietary fibre.
  • Definitive management: Lifestyle modifications (increased fluid and fibre intake, regular exercise) are often the first-line approach. If these are insufficient, laxatives are typically introduced, starting with bulk-forming agents, then osmotic laxatives, and finally stimulant laxatives if needed [8].
  • Adjunctive management: For refractory cases, newer agents such as prucalopride or linaclotide may be considered, particularly for chronic idiopathic constipation [9].
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Regular review of symptoms, stool consistency (using Bristol Stool Chart), and treatment response is important, with adjustment of laxative regimen as necessary.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Patients with persistent or worsening symptoms, or development of red flags, often warrant further investigation or specialist referral.
  • Exam pharmacology reference (UKMLA/MRCGP): Common laxative classes include bulk-forming (e.g., ispaghula), osmotic (e.g., macrogol, lactulose), and stimulant (e.g., senna, bisacodyl) agents. Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Constipation"][/sc]
  • Faecal impaction: Prolonged stool retention can lead to a hard mass of faeces in the rectum or colon, causing overflow diarrhoea, abdominal pain, and potentially bowel obstruction.
  • Haemorrhoids and anal fissures: Chronic straining during defecation increases pressure, leading to the development or exacerbation of haemorrhoids and anal fissures.
  • Rectal prolapse: Persistent straining can weaken pelvic floor muscles and ligaments, increasing the risk of rectal prolapse, particularly in older adults.
  • Reduced quality of life: Chronic constipation, particularly beyond several months, can significantly impact a patient\'s physical and psychological well-being, leading to anxiety, depression, and social isolation.
