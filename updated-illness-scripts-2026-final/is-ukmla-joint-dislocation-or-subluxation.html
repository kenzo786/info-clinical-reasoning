Joint dislocation or subluxation [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Joint dislocation or subluxation"]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Joint dislocation or subluxation"]
  • A joint dislocation involves the complete separation of articulating bony surfaces within a joint, while a subluxation is a partial or incomplete separation, where the joint surfaces remain in partial contact.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise and differentiate between joint dislocation and subluxation, understand the immediate assessment priorities including neurovascular status, and appreciate the principles of initial management and potential complications.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Joint dislocation or subluxation"]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Joint dislocation or subluxation"]
  • Shoulder dislocations are the most common large joint dislocation, with an incidence rate in the UK estimated at 21.9 per 100,000 population for first-time dislocations, and up to 90% recurrence risk in those under 20 years old [1] [2] [3].
  • Hip dislocations are less common but often associated with high-energy trauma, with posterior dislocations accounting for 80-90% of cases [4].
  • Patellar dislocations are common, particularly in young, active individuals, often occurring during sports activities [5].
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Joint dislocation or subluxation"]
  • Trigger: Significant external force or trauma exceeding the joint capsule and ligamentous integrity, leading to displacement of articular surfaces.
  • Progression: The initial displacement can cause damage to surrounding soft tissues, including ligaments, tendons, and joint capsule, potentially leading to instability and recurrent events.
  • Consequence: Persistent instability, chronic pain, early osteoarthritis, and potential neurovascular compromise due to compression or stretch injuries, such as [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Axillary nerve injury in shoulder dislocation: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="axillary nerve injury"] in shoulder dislocations or [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Sciatic nerve injury in hip dislocation: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="sciatic nerve injury"] in hip dislocations.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Joint dislocation or subluxation"]
  • Early phase: Immediate pain, deformity, and loss of function following the traumatic event. Prompt reduction within hours is crucial to minimise complications.
  • Progressive phase: If not reduced promptly, muscle spasm and oedema can make reduction more difficult. Continued neurovascular compromise can lead to irreversible damage, particularly beyond 6-8 hours.
  • Late phase: After reduction, recovery typically involves a period of immobilisation followed by rehabilitation over several weeks to months. Chronic instability or recurrent dislocations may develop over months to years.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Joint dislocation or subluxation"]
  • Ask about severe pain - often described as excruciating and sudden in onset, indicating significant tissue disruption.
  • Ask about visible deformity or asymmetry - a key indicator of joint displacement, such as a flattened deltoid in anterior shoulder dislocation.
  • Ask about inability to move the affected joint - due to pain, mechanical block, or muscle spasm, suggesting loss of normal joint articulation.
  • Ask about associated numbness or tingling - particularly in the distal limb, raising concern for nerve impingement or injury.
  • Ask about mechanism of injury - high-energy trauma (e.g., falls, sports injuries) often precedes dislocation, providing clues to potential associated injuries.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Joint dislocation or subluxation"]
  • Check vital signs - assess for signs of shock, especially in high-energy trauma, though often normal in isolated dislocations.
  • Check for obvious joint deformity and swelling - a prominent humeral head anteriorly in shoulder dislocation or a flexed, adducted, internally rotated hip in posterior hip dislocation.
  • Check neurovascular status distal to the injury - assess sensation, motor function, and pulses to detect nerve or arterial compromise, e.g., [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Axillary nerve assessment: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="axillary nerve function"] in shoulder dislocations.
  • Check range of motion - severely restricted and painful, often with a springy block to movement.
  • Check for associated skin compromise - open dislocations require urgent attention due to infection risk.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Joint dislocation or subluxation"]
  • Open dislocation - immediate risk of infection and requires urgent surgical debridement.
  • Neurovascular compromise - indicates potential limb-threatening injury requiring urgent reduction and reassessment, particularly if present on initial examination or developing rapidly (within minutes to hours).
  • Irreducible dislocation - suggests interposition of soft tissue or bone, necessitating surgical intervention.
  • Associated fracture - may complicate reduction and require specific orthopaedic management.
  • Dislocation of hip or elbow in children - hip dislocations in children are rare and often associated with significant trauma, while a "pulled elbow" (radial head subluxation) is common but requires specific reduction techniques.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Joint dislocation or subluxation"]
  • Elderly patients: May present with less obvious deformity or pain due to reduced muscle tone or pre-existing degenerative changes, increasing the risk of delayed diagnosis.
  • Recurrent dislocations: Patients with chronic instability may experience dislocations with minimal trauma, or even spontaneously, due to underlying ligamentous laxity or bony defects.
  • Posterior shoulder dislocation: Often missed on initial assessment, especially if associated with seizures or electrocution, as the arm may be internally rotated and adducted, mimicking other conditions.
  • Pulled elbow (radial head subluxation) in children: Typically presents with sudden refusal to use the arm after a traction injury, without obvious deformity or swelling, and is easily reduced with specific manoeuvres.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Joint dislocation or subluxation"]
  • Core for suspected dislocation: Plain film radiographs (X-rays) of the affected joint in at least two planes are essential to confirm dislocation, identify associated fractures, and guide reduction.
  • Severity/risk tools: The [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Western Ontario Shoulder Instability Index (WOSI): what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Western Ontario Shoulder Instability Index (WOSI)"] can be used to assess functional impairment and quality of life in shoulder instability, though not a severity score for acute dislocation.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Consider ultrasound for soft tissue injuries or MRI for detailed assessment of ligamentous and capsular damage, especially in recurrent dislocations or if neurovascular injury is suspected.
  • Imaging - CT scan: May be required for complex dislocations, particularly hip dislocations, to identify subtle fractures, assess reduction quality, and detect intra-articular fragments.
  • Serial reassessment: Repeat neurovascular examination post-reduction is critical to ensure no iatrogenic injury and to monitor for delayed complications.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Joint dislocation or subluxation"]
  • A young male presents after a fall onto an outstretched hand, experiencing immediate severe shoulder pain, visible deformity, and inability to move the arm.
  • Acute musculoskeletal trauma with joint instability, concerning for dislocation with potential neurovascular compromise.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Joint dislocation or subluxation"]
  • Fracture - often presents with similar pain and deformity but typically lacks the characteristic "empty" joint feeling or specific mechanism of dislocation.
  • Sprain/Strain - involves ligamentous or muscular injury without joint displacement, allowing for some range of motion, albeit painful.
  • Contusion - localised soft tissue injury with pain and swelling but no joint instability or deformity.
  • Rotator cuff tear - primarily affects shoulder function and strength, often without gross deformity, though acute tears can mimic dislocation symptoms.
  • Septic arthritis - presents with acute joint pain, swelling, and restricted movement, but typically accompanied by systemic signs of infection and no history of trauma.
  • Transient synovitis (in children) - a common cause of hip pain and limping in children, but without trauma or joint deformity.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Joint dislocation or subluxation"]
  • Immediate priorities: Prompt recognition, neurovascular assessment, analgesia, and urgent reduction of the dislocation to minimise complications. Escalation to orthopaedic services is often warranted.
  • Definitive management: Closed reduction under appropriate analgesia and/or sedation is the primary treatment. Surgical intervention may be required for irreducible dislocations, open dislocations, or those with significant associated fractures.
  • Adjunctive management: Post-reduction immobilisation (e.g., sling for shoulder, brace for patella) for a short period, followed by a structured physiotherapy programme to restore range of motion, strength, and stability.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Regular neurovascular checks post-reduction. Follow-up with orthopaedics or physiotherapy to monitor recovery and address any ongoing instability or complications.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Patients should be advised on red flag symptoms (e.g., worsening pain, new neurological deficits) and when to seek urgent medical review.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Joint dislocation or subluxation"]
  • Recurrent dislocation: Particularly common in young, active individuals after a primary shoulder dislocation, often due to persistent capsuloligamentous laxity, developing over months to years.
  • Neurovascular injury: Can occur at the time of injury or during reduction, leading to transient or permanent nerve deficits (e.g., axillary nerve in shoulder, sciatic nerve in hip) or arterial compromise, with immediate to progressive onset (minutes to hours).
  • Post-traumatic osteoarthritis: Long-term consequence of articular cartilage damage at the time of dislocation or due to chronic instability, developing years after the initial injury.
  • Avascular necrosis: A significant risk, especially in hip dislocations (e.g., femoral head), if blood supply is disrupted, with symptoms often presenting months after the event.
  • Rotator cuff tears: Can be associated with shoulder dislocations, particularly in older patients, contributing to ongoing pain and weakness if not identified and managed.
