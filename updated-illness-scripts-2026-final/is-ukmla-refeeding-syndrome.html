Refeeding syndrome [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Refeeding syndrome"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Refeeding syndrome"][/sc]
  • Refeeding syndrome is a potentially fatal shift in fluids and electrolytes that can occur in malnourished patients receiving artificial refeeding (enteral or parenteral).
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the risk factors, clinical features, and management principles of refeeding syndrome to prevent and treat this critical condition.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Refeeding syndrome"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Refeeding syndrome"][/sc]
  • The incidence of refeeding syndrome varies widely, with estimates ranging from 10% to 50% in at-risk hospitalised patients, particularly those with anorexia nervosa or chronic alcoholism.
  • Risk factors include prolonged periods of inadequate nutritional intake (e.g., >5 days), significant weight loss (>10-15% in 3-6 months), low pre-feeding levels of phosphate, potassium, or magnesium, and a history of chronic conditions such as cancer, inflammatory bowel disease, or dysphagia.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Refeeding syndrome"][/sc]
  • Trigger: Rapid introduction of carbohydrates after a period of starvation shifts metabolism from fat to carbohydrate utilisation, leading to increased insulin secretion.
  • Progression: Insulin drives glucose, phosphate, potassium, and magnesium into cells, causing severe extracellular depletion of these electrolytes and thiamine.
  • Consequence: Profound electrolyte imbalances and vitamin deficiencies result in cardiac, respiratory, neurological, and haematological complications, which can be life-threatening.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Refeeding syndrome"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Symptoms typically emerge within the first 24-72 hours of refeeding, often manifesting as cardiac arrhythmias or fluid retention.
  • Progressive phase: Electrolyte abnormalities, particularly hypophosphataemia, may worsen over the first 3-5 days, leading to more severe organ dysfunction.
  • Late phase: Complications such as respiratory failure or neurological impairment can develop, particularly beyond 5-7 days if not recognised and managed promptly.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Refeeding syndrome"][/sc]
  • Ask about weakness or fatigue - may indicate hypophosphataemia affecting muscle function.
  • Ask about palpitations or chest discomfort - suggestive of cardiac arrhythmias due to electrolyte disturbances.
  • Ask about shortness of breath - can signify fluid overload or respiratory muscle weakness.
  • Ask about confusion or altered mental status - points towards neurological complications from electrolyte imbalances or thiamine deficiency.
  • Ask about oedema or swelling - indicates fluid retention, a common feature of refeeding.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Refeeding syndrome"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - look for tachycardia, hypotension, or tachypnoea, which can indicate cardiac or respiratory compromise.
  • Check for peripheral oedema - suggests fluid retention, particularly in the lower limbs or sacrum.
  • Check neurological status - assess for confusion, seizures, or reduced consciousness, indicating cerebral involvement.
  • Check for muscle weakness or tremors - may be a sign of severe hypophosphataemia or hypomagnesaemia.
  • Check for signs of heart failure - such as raised jugular venous pressure or crackles on lung auscultation.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Refeeding syndrome"][/sc]
  • Acute cardiac arrhythmia - immediate risk of cardiac arrest.
  • Rapidly worsening respiratory distress - indicates impending respiratory failure.
  • New-onset seizures or profound confusion - suggests severe neurological compromise.
  • Severe hypophosphataemia (<0.3 mmol/L) - associated with significant morbidity and mortality.
  • Significant fluid overload with pulmonary oedema - requires urgent intervention to prevent respiratory collapse.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Refeeding syndrome"][/sc]
  • Older adults: May present with more subtle signs of cardiac dysfunction or confusion, often attributed to other comorbidities, delaying diagnosis.
  • Patients with chronic kidney disease: Electrolyte imbalances may be exacerbated or masked by pre-existing renal impairment, requiring careful monitoring.
  • Patients with severe sepsis: The inflammatory response can complicate electrolyte management and fluid balance, making refeeding syndrome harder to distinguish.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Refeeding syndrome"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected Refeeding syndrome: Full blood count, urea and electrolytes (including phosphate, magnesium, calcium), liver function tests, glucose, and thiamine levels.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Consider cardiac enzymes if chest pain, or arterial blood gases if respiratory distress.
  • Imaging - Chest X-ray: To assess for pulmonary oedema or effusions in cases of respiratory compromise.
  • Serial reassessment: Daily monitoring of electrolytes (phosphate, potassium, magnesium) for at least the first week of refeeding, or until stable.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Refeeding syndrome"][/sc]
  • A 68-year-old male with a history of oesophageal cancer and recent significant weight loss presents with acute confusion, palpitations, and peripheral oedema 48 hours after commencing enteral feeding.
  • Elderly, malnourished, re-fed patient with acute neurological, cardiac, and fluid balance derangements.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Refeeding syndrome"][/sc]
  • Sepsis - often presents with systemic inflammatory response, but electrolyte derangements may be less specific.
  • Cardiac failure - can cause oedema and dyspnoea, but typically without the acute electrolyte shifts seen in refeeding.
  • Electrolyte disturbance from other causes - e.g., renal impairment, diuretic use, but usually without the context of recent refeeding.
  • Wernicke's encephalopathy - shares neurological symptoms, but specifically due to thiamine deficiency, which is a component of refeeding syndrome.
  • Drug toxicity - certain medications can cause similar symptoms, requiring careful medication history.
  • Hypoglycaemia - can cause confusion and weakness, but typically resolves with glucose administration.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Refeeding syndrome"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Correct electrolyte imbalances (especially hypophosphataemia, hypokalaemia, hypomagnesaemia), cautiously manage fluid balance, and administer thiamine.
  • Definitive management: Gradually reintroduce nutrition, starting with a low caloric intake (e.g., 5-10 kcal/kg/day) and slowly increasing over several days, while closely monitoring electrolytes.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Daily clinical assessment, fluid balance charts, and electrolyte monitoring until stable.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Refer to a dietitian for nutritional support, and consider intensive care unit admission for severe cases with organ dysfunction.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Refeeding syndrome"][/sc]
  • Cardiac arrhythmias: Due to severe hypokalaemia and hypomagnesaemia, leading to potentially fatal dysrhythmias, particularly in the early phase of refeeding.
  • Respiratory failure: Caused by diaphragmatic muscle weakness from hypophosphataemia and fluid overload, often manifesting in the progressive phase.
  • Neurological dysfunction: Including confusion, seizures, and Wernicke's encephalopathy, resulting from electrolyte imbalances and thiamine deficiency, typically developing within the first week.
  • Fluid overload and heart failure: Rapid refeeding can lead to sodium and water retention, exacerbating pre-existing cardiac conditions or causing new-onset heart failure.
  • Rhabdomyolysis: Severe hypophosphataemia can cause muscle breakdown, leading to acute kidney injury.
