Hypothyroidism (Endocrine and metabolic) [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Hypothyroidism"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Hypothyroidism"][/sc]
  • Hypothyroidism is a common condition resulting from insufficient production of thyroid hormones (thyroxine [T4] and tri-iodothyronine [T3]) by the thyroid gland, leading to a generalised slowing of metabolic processes [1].
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical features of hypothyroidism, interpret thyroid function tests, and outline the principles of management with levothyroxine.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Hypothyroidism"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Hypothyroidism"][/sc]
  • The prevalence of treated hypothyroidism in the UK increased from 2.3% (1.4 million) to 3.5% (2.2 million) of the total population between 2005 and 2014 [2].
  • It is more common in women, with an estimated prevalence of 6.4% in females compared to 3.4% in males [3].
  • Risk factors include female sex, increasing age, family history of thyroid disease, autoimmune conditions (e.g., Type 1 diabetes, pernicious anaemia), previous thyroid surgery or radioiodine therapy, and certain medications (e.g., amiodarone, lithium) [1].
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Hypothyroidism"][/sc]
  • **Trigger:** The most common cause of primary hypothyroidism in the UK is autoimmune thyroiditis (Hashimoto's thyroiditis), leading to gradual destruction of thyroid follicular cells. Other causes include post-radioiodine therapy, post-thyroidectomy, or iodine deficiency. Secondary hypothyroidism, a less common cause, results from pituitary or hypothalamic dysfunction leading to insufficient TSH production [1].
  • **Progression:** Reduced thyroid hormone synthesis and secretion lead to decreased circulating T4 and T3 levels. In primary hypothyroidism, this triggers a compensatory increase in TSH from the pituitary gland, while in secondary hypothyroidism, TSH levels are inappropriately normal or low [1]. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Hashimoto thyroiditis: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Hashimoto thyroiditis"][/sc]
  • **Consequence:** The systemic deficiency of thyroid hormones causes a generalised slowing of metabolic processes, affecting multiple organ systems and leading to a wide range of clinical manifestations. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Myxoedema: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Myxoedema"][/sc]
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Hypothyroidism"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Symptoms are often subtle and non-specific, developing insidiously over months to years. Patients may experience mild fatigue, weight gain, and cold intolerance, which can be easily overlooked or attributed to other causes.
  • Progressive phase: As thyroid hormone deficiency worsens, symptoms become more pronounced and diverse, including constipation, dry skin, hair loss, bradycardia, and cognitive slowing. This phase can last for several months or even years without diagnosis.
  • Late phase: If untreated, severe hypothyroidism can progress to life-threatening complications such as myxoedema coma, particularly beyond several years of significant hormone deficiency or during acute illness.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Hypothyroidism"][/sc]
  • Ask about fatigue and lethargy - often an early and prominent symptom, indicating reduced metabolic energy.
  • Ask about cold intolerance - due to decreased thermogenesis, suggesting a systemic metabolic slowdown.
  • Ask about weight gain - despite reduced appetite, reflecting fluid retention and decreased metabolic rate.
  • Ask about constipation - a common gastrointestinal symptom due to slowed gut motility.
  • Ask about dry skin and hair loss - indicative of reduced cellular turnover and epidermal function.
  • Ask about changes in mood or cognition - such as depression, poor concentration, or memory impairment, reflecting central nervous system effects.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Hypothyroidism"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - often reveals bradycardia and hypothermia, reflecting systemic metabolic suppression.
  • Check for non-pitting oedema (myxoedema) - particularly in the face, hands, and lower limbs, due to accumulation of glycosaminoglycans in interstitial tissues. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Myxoedema: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Myxoedema"][/sc]
  • Check skin and hair - for dryness, coarseness, and brittle hair, or loss of the outer third of the eyebrows (madarosis).
  • Check reflexes - delayed relaxation phase of deep tendon reflexes (e.g., ankle jerk) is a classic sign.
  • Check thyroid gland - may be impalpable, normal, or diffusely enlarged (goitre), depending on the aetiology.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Hypothyroidism"][/sc]
  • Altered mental status: Suggests progression to myxoedema coma, requiring immediate medical attention.
  • Severe hypothermia: Core body temperature below 35°C, a critical sign of myxoedema coma and severe metabolic decompensation.
  • Bradycardia with hypotension: Indicates significant cardiovascular compromise and impending circulatory collapse, particularly in the context of severe hypothyroidism.
  • Respiratory depression: Reduced respiratory drive and hypoventilation, which can lead to hypercapnia and respiratory failure, often seen in advanced stages.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Hypothyroidism"][/sc]
  • Older adults: Symptoms may be subtle and non-specific, often mistaken for normal ageing or other comorbidities, such as cognitive impairment or depression.
  • Children and young people: May present with abnormal growth, delayed puberty, or unexplained changes in behaviour or school performance. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Congenital hypothyroidism: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Congenital hypothyroidism"][/sc]
  • Pregnancy: Undiagnosed or inadequately treated hypothyroidism can lead to adverse maternal and fetal outcomes, including pre-eclampsia, miscarriage, and impaired neurodevelopment.
  • Myxoedema coma: A rare but life-threatening presentation of severe, decompensated hypothyroidism, often triggered by infection, cold exposure, or drugs.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Hypothyroidism"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected hypothyroidism:
    • Thyroid-stimulating hormone (TSH): Elevated in primary hypothyroidism, normal or low in secondary hypothyroidism [1].
    • Free Thyroxine (FT4): Low in both primary and secondary hypothyroidism.
    • Thyroid peroxidase antibodies (TPOAbs): Present in most cases of autoimmune (Hashimoto's) thyroiditis, supporting the diagnosis of primary hypothyroidism [1]. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Thyroid peroxidase antibodies: what are they and why do they matter clinically?" text="TPOAbs"][/sc]
  • Severity/risk tools: No single validated severity or risk score is routinely used for hypothyroidism on MDCalc.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible:
    • Full blood count: May show anaemia.
    • Lipid profile: Often shows hypercholesterolaemia.
    • Electrolytes: May reveal hyponatraemia in severe cases.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Hypothyroidism"][/sc]
  • A 45-year-old female presents with a 6-month history of increasing fatigue, weight gain, cold intolerance, and constipation, with examination revealing bradycardia and dry skin.
  • Middle-aged female with insidious onset of systemic metabolic slowing, concerning for primary hypothyroidism.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Hypothyroidism"][/sc]
  • **Depression** - shares symptoms like fatigue, low mood, and cognitive impairment, but typically lacks the specific physical signs of hypothyroidism (e.g., bradycardia, myxoedema).
  • **Chronic fatigue syndrome** - presents with profound fatigue, but thyroid function tests are normal.
  • **Anaemia** - causes fatigue and pallor, but is distinguished by abnormal full blood count and normal thyroid function.
  • **Obesity** - associated with weight gain, but usually without the specific metabolic slowing and other systemic symptoms of hypothyroidism.
  • **Perimenopause/Menopause** - can cause fatigue, weight changes, and mood disturbances in women, but thyroid function tests are normal. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Menopause vs hypothyroidism: how to differentiate?" text="Menopause"][/sc]
  • **Drug-induced hypothyroidism** - certain medications (e.g., amiodarone, lithium) can cause hypothyroidism, which is distinguished by drug history.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Hypothyroidism"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Recognition and diagnosis are key. In severe cases (myxoedema coma), immediate supportive care, including airway management, fluid resuscitation, and intravenous thyroid hormone replacement, is often warranted.
  • Definitive management: Levothyroxine replacement therapy is the first-line treatment for primary hypothyroidism, aiming to restore euthyroidism and alleviate symptoms [1].
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Regular monitoring of TSH and FT4 levels is essential to ensure appropriate dosing and maintain thyroid function within the target range. Adjustments to levothyroxine dosage are often required [1].
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Patients should be advised on symptoms of over- or under-treatment and when to seek medical advice. Escalation to specialist care is considered for complex cases, such as secondary hypothyroidism, pregnancy, or myxoedema coma.
  • Exam pharmacology reference (UKMLA/MRCGP): [sc name="jitl-medication" Q="Levothyroxine use in Hypothyroidism" text="Levothyroxine"][/sc] is a synthetic T4 preparation, converted to T3 in the body. It has a long half-life, allowing for once-daily dosing. No doses or frequencies are cited here — always verify against your local antimicrobial/prescribing formulary before prescribing.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Hypothyroidism"][/sc]
  • Myxoedema coma: A rare but life-threatening decompensated state of severe hypothyroidism, characterised by altered mental status, hypothermia, bradycardia, and hypotension, typically occurring after several years of untreated or undertreated hypothyroidism, often triggered by acute illness.
  • Cardiovascular complications: Untreated hypothyroidism can lead to hypercholesterolaemia, atherosclerosis, and an increased risk of coronary artery disease and heart failure over the long term.
  • Infertility and obstetric complications: Hypothyroidism can cause menstrual irregularities and anovulation, leading to infertility. In pregnancy, it increases the risk of miscarriage, pre-eclampsia, and adverse fetal neurodevelopmental outcomes.
  • Cognitive impairment: Chronic untreated hypothyroidism can result in significant cognitive slowing, memory impairment, and depression, which may be partially reversible with treatment.
