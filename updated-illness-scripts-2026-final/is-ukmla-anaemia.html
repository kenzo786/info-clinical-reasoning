Anaemia [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Anaemia"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Anaemia"][/sc]
  • A condition characterised by a reduction in the number of red blood cells or the haemoglobin concentration within them, leading to reduced oxygen-carrying capacity of the blood.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the common causes, clinical features, and initial management of anaemia, including iron deficiency anaemia and anaemia of chronic disease.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Anaemia"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Anaemia"][/sc]
  • Anaemia affects approximately one-quarter of the global population, with iron deficiency anaemia being the most common type, particularly prevalent in women of reproductive age and young children.
  • In the UK, iron deficiency anaemia is common, with an estimated prevalence of 2-5% in adult men and postmenopausal women, and up to 15% in premenopausal women (NICE).
  • Risk factors include chronic blood loss (e.g., heavy menstrual bleeding, gastrointestinal bleeding), inadequate dietary intake of iron or vitamins (B12, folate), malabsorption syndromes (e.g., coeliac disease), chronic inflammatory conditions, and renal disease.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Anaemia"][/sc]
  • Reduced red blood cell production or increased red blood cell destruction/loss triggers a compensatory response, including increased erythropoietin release from the kidneys.
  • Progression involves the bone marrow attempting to increase erythropoiesis, but if the underlying cause persists (e.g., chronic blood loss, nutrient deficiency), this compensatory mechanism becomes overwhelmed.
  • Consequence is a reduction in the oxygen-carrying capacity of the blood, leading to tissue hypoxia and the clinical manifestations of anaemia, such as fatigue and pallor.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Anaemia"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Insidious onset over weeks to months, particularly beyond 2-4 weeks, as iron stores are gradually depleted or chronic blood loss accumulates, often with minimal or non-specific symptoms.
  • Progressive phase: Symptoms become more noticeable over several months, particularly beyond 3-6 months, as haemoglobin levels fall further, leading to more pronounced fatigue, dyspnoea, and pallor.
  • Late phase: Severe anaemia, particularly beyond 6-12 months, can lead to significant functional impairment and may precipitate or exacerbate cardiovascular symptoms, indicating a prolonged and unaddressed underlying cause.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Anaemia"][/sc]
  • Ask about fatigue and lethargy - these are often the earliest and most common symptoms, indicating reduced oxygen delivery to tissues.
  • Ask about shortness of breath (dyspnoea) on exertion - reflects the body's attempt to compensate for reduced oxygen-carrying capacity.
  • Ask about palpitations - the heart works harder to circulate oxygenated blood, leading to increased heart rate.
  • Ask about headache and dizziness - due to reduced oxygen supply to the brain.
  • Ask about cold intolerance - impaired thermoregulation due to reduced metabolic rate and blood flow.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Anaemia"][/sc]
  • Check pallor of mucous membranes (conjunctivae, tongue) and skin - a key indicator of reduced haemoglobin concentration.
  • Check vital signs - tachycardia and tachypnoea may be present as compensatory mechanisms; hypotension in severe cases.
  • Check koilonychia (spoon-shaped nails) - a specific but late sign of chronic iron deficiency.
  • Check glossitis (smooth, sore tongue) or angular stomatitis - can indicate iron, B12, or folate deficiency.
  • Check for signs of underlying cause, e.g., hepatosplenomegaly (haemolytic anaemia), lymphadenopathy (malignancy).
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Anaemia"][/sc]
  • Melaena or haematemesis: Suggests acute or chronic upper gastrointestinal bleeding, requiring urgent investigation.
  • Unexplained weight loss: Raises suspicion for underlying malignancy, particularly gastrointestinal.
  • New onset anaemia in men or postmenopausal women: Requires investigation for gastrointestinal blood loss until proven otherwise.
  • Severe symptomatic anaemia: Indicating significant physiological compromise and requiring prompt intervention, potentially including blood transfusion.
  • Neurological symptoms (e.g., paraesthesia, ataxia): Highly suggestive of vitamin B12 deficiency, which can lead to irreversible damage if untreated.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Anaemia"][/sc]
  • Older adults: May present with non-specific symptoms like confusion, falls, or worsening of pre-existing cardiac conditions, rather than classic fatigue, making diagnosis challenging.
  • Pregnancy: Physiological anaemia is common due to increased plasma volume, but true iron deficiency anaemia can be masked and requires careful monitoring to prevent adverse maternal and foetal outcomes.
  • Immunosuppressed patients: Anaemia may be multifactorial (e.g., chronic disease, medication side effects, infection), and symptoms can be overshadowed by their primary condition, delaying diagnosis.
