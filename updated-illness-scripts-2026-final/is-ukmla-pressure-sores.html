Pressure sores [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Pressure sores"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Pressure sores"][/sc]
  • Pressure sores (also known as pressure ulcers, bedsores, or decubitus ulcers) are localised injuries to the skin and/or underlying tissue, usually over a bony prominence, resulting from sustained pressure or pressure in combination with shear, which compromises blood flow and leads to tissue damage [1, 2, 3].
  • UKMLA learning outcome: The candidate must recognise the risk factors for pressure sore development, classify their severity, and understand the principles of prevention and initial management.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Pressure sores"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Pressure sores"][/sc]
  • In the UK, over 700,000 people are affected by pressure ulcers each year, with approximately 180,000 newly acquired cases annually [4]. Prevalence in older patients can be as high as 5-9%, with over 70% occurring in those over 70 years of age [5].
  • Key risk factors include limited mobility (e.g., bedridden, wheelchair-bound, spinal cord injury, stroke), incontinence leading to skin maceration, malnutrition, advanced age, and certain medical conditions such as diabetes or peripheral vascular disease [6, 7, 8].
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Pressure sores"][/sc]
  • Sustained external pressure and/or shear forces, typically over bony prominences, compress capillaries and impede blood flow to the skin and underlying tissues, leading to localised ischaemia and hypoxia [1, 9].
  • Prolonged ischaemia results in cellular damage and death (necrosis) due to a lack of oxygen and nutrients. This process is exacerbated by factors such as friction, moisture (maceration), and inflammation, which further compromise tissue integrity [3, 10].
  • The consequence is progressive tissue breakdown, manifesting as a pressure ulcer. The severity can range from superficial skin discolouration to deep tissue injury involving muscle, bone, and supporting structures [2, 11].
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Pressure sores"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Initial tissue damage can occur rapidly, within hours of sustained pressure. Stage 1 pressure ulcers, characterised by non-blanchable erythema, may resolve within a few days if pressure is immediately relieved [12].
  • Progressive phase: Without intervention, tissue damage progresses. Stage 2 pressure ulcers (partial-thickness skin loss) can develop within days to a week, and may take several weeks to heal. Deeper ulcers (Stage 3 and 4) can develop over weeks, particularly beyond one week of sustained pressure, and require prolonged healing [13, 14].
  • Late phase: Established deep pressure ulcers (Stage 3 and 4) can take months to heal, particularly beyond several weeks, and are prone to chronic infection and other severe complications, significantly impacting patient prognosis and quality of life [15].
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Pressure sores"][/sc]
  • Ask about pain or discomfort in specific areas – this is a crucial early indicator in patients with intact sensation, prompting investigation of underlying tissue damage [1].
  • Ask about changes in skin colour, temperature, or texture (e.g., hardness, sponginess) – these can be subtle early signs of tissue compromise, particularly non-blanchable erythema in lighter skin tones or persistent discolouration in darker skin tones [16, 17].
  • Ask about any new or worsening skin lesions, such as blisters, open wounds, or areas of broken skin – indicating progression beyond early stages and increased risk of infection [16].
  • Ask about altered sensation or numbness in vulnerable areas – patients with neurological impairment may not perceive pain, making visual inspection and other cues critical for early detection [18].
  • Ask about recent changes in mobility, activity levels, or ability to reposition – reduced movement significantly increases the risk of sustained pressure and subsequent tissue damage [6].
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Pressure sores"][/sc]
  • Check skin integrity, particularly over bony prominences (sacrum, heels, hips) – look for non-blanchable erythema (redness that does not fade when pressed) in lighter skin tones, or persistent discolouration (purple/blue) in darker skin tones, which implies underlying tissue damage [16, 19].
  • Check for blisters, skin tears, or open wounds – these indicate partial or full-thickness skin loss, signifying progression of the pressure ulcer [16].
  • Check for presence of slough (yellow, tan, grey, green, or brown dead tissue) or eschar (dark, hard, leathery dead tissue) – these are indicators of deeper tissue damage and necrosis [19].
  • Check for warmth, swelling, or purulent discharge around the wound – these findings may suggest local infection or cellulitis, requiring further assessment [16].
  • Check vital signs (temperature, pulse, blood pressure) – while often normal in early stages, fever, tachycardia, or hypotension may indicate systemic infection (sepsis) secondary to a worsening pressure ulcer [16, 20, 21].
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Pressure sores"][/sc]
  • Signs of systemic infection (sepsis): Fever, chills, tachycardia, hypotension, or altered mental status in a patient with a pressure ulcer implies potential systemic spread of infection and requires urgent medical review [16, 22].
  • Rapid deterioration of wound: Sudden increase in size, depth, pain, or purulent discharge, particularly beyond the early phase, suggests worsening tissue necrosis or infection [16].
  • Exposed bone, tendon, or muscle: Indicates a Stage 4 pressure ulcer, signifying extensive tissue destruction and a high risk of osteomyelitis or other deep-seated infections [19].
  • Persistent Autonomic Dysreflexia: In patients with spinal cord injury, this can be triggered by pressure ulcers and is a medical emergency requiring immediate intervention [23].
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Pressure sores"][/sc]
  • Older adults: Pressure ulcers are more prevalent and can progress more rapidly due to age-related skin changes (thinner skin, reduced elasticity), co-morbidities, malnutrition, and polypharmacy, making early detection and aggressive prevention crucial [24, 25].
  • Immunosuppressed patients: Individuals on immunosuppressive therapy or with compromised immune systems (e.g., HIV, chemotherapy) may exhibit delayed wound healing, increased susceptibility to infection, and less pronounced inflammatory responses, masking the severity of the ulcer [26].
  • Pregnant individuals: Pressure ulcers can develop during pregnancy, labour, and postpartum, particularly over the sacrum and heels, due to prolonged immobility, altered body mechanics, and hormonal changes affecting skin integrity. Awareness and preventative measures are essential in this population [27, 28].
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Pressure sores"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected Pressure sores: Clinical assessment and staging of the ulcer based on visual inspection and palpation is paramount [1, 19]. A complete blood count (CBC) may reveal an elevated white blood cell count, indicating inflammation or infection [29].
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Consider other skin conditions that may mimic pressure ulcers, such as moisture-associated skin damage, diabetic foot ulcers, or vascular ulcers. Biopsy may be considered in atypical presentations or non-healing wounds to rule out malignancy [24].
  • Imaging - MRI: Magnetic Resonance Imaging (MRI) is the gold standard for diagnosing osteomyelitis, a serious complication of deep pressure ulcers, especially when bone involvement is suspected [30].
  • Validated risk assessment scores: The Braden Scale for Predicting Pressure Sore Risk on MDCalc and the Norton Scale are commonly used tools to assess a patient's risk of developing pressure ulcers [31, 32, 33].
  • Serial reassessment: Regular photographic documentation and reassessment of the ulcer are crucial to monitor healing progress and guide treatment decisions [29].
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Pressure sores"][/sc]
  • A 78-year-old bedridden female, with a history of stroke and urinary incontinence, presents with a 2-week history of a non-healing wound on her sacrum, initially noted as a reddened area that has since broken down.
  • An elderly, immobile patient with impaired sensation and continence, presenting with a chronic, non-healing skin lesion over a bony prominence, indicative of tissue necrosis.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Pressure sores"][/sc]
  • Incontinence-associated dermatitis (IAD) - Diffuse redness or skin breakdown in areas exposed to moisture (e.g., perineum, buttocks), often with irregular borders, rather than over bony prominences [34].
  • Arterial ulcers - Typically found on the lower extremities (toes, feet, ankles), often painful, with a 'punched-out' appearance, pale wound bed, and associated with signs of peripheral arterial disease (e.g., absent pulses, cool skin) [35].
  • Venous leg ulcers - Usually located around the gaiter area of the lower leg, often shallow with irregular borders, associated with oedema, varicose veins, and haemosiderin staining [35].
  • Diabetic foot ulcers - Occur in diabetic patients, often on the plantar surface of the foot or over pressure points, associated with neuropathy and peripheral arterial disease, and may be painless [36].
  • Cellulitis - Presents as spreading erythema, warmth, and tenderness, often without a clear break in the skin initially, and can be differentiated by its rapid progression and systemic signs of infection [37].
  • Skin tears - Traumatic wounds caused by friction or shear, often with a flap of skin, not necessarily related to sustained pressure over a bony prominence [34].
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Pressure sores"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Pressure relief is paramount; this involves frequent repositioning (e.g., every 2 hours for bedridden patients, every 15-30 minutes for wheelchair users), use of pressure-relieving mattresses and cushions, and ensuring proper body alignment [38, 39]. Initial wound cleansing with saline and appropriate dressing application to maintain a moist wound environment is also crucial [40].
  • Definitive management: This often includes debridement of necrotic tissue (surgical, mechanical, enzymatic, or autolytic) to promote healing. Selection of appropriate dressings depends on the wound stage and characteristics (e.g., hydrocolloids for shallow wounds, alginates for exudative wounds) [41, 42]. Optimising nutrition and hydration is essential for wound healing [38].
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Regular assessment of the pressure ulcer, including size, depth, exudate, and surrounding skin, is necessary. Photographic documentation can aid in tracking progress. Reassessment of risk factors and ongoing preventative measures are vital [29, 43].
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Refer to specialist wound care services for complex or non-healing ulcers, or those with signs of deep tissue infection. Surgical intervention (e.g., flap reconstruction) may be considered for severe, chronic ulcers [44]. Patients and carers should be educated on warning signs of deterioration and when to seek urgent medical advice [45].
  • Exam pharmacology reference (UKMLA/MRCGP): Systemic antibiotics may be considered for pressure ulcers with signs of spreading infection (e.g., cellulitis, osteomyelitis). Topical antimicrobials may be used for localised infection. Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Pressure sores"][/sc]
  • Infection: Pressure ulcers are highly susceptible to bacterial infection, which can range from localised cellulitis to severe systemic infections like sepsis, particularly in deeper ulcers (Stage 3 and 4) [1, 46]. This risk increases significantly beyond the early phase of ulcer development.
  • Osteomyelitis: A serious complication of deep pressure ulcers (Stage 4), where infection spreads to the underlying bone. This can lead to chronic infection, bone necrosis, and may necessitate surgical intervention or prolonged antibiotic therapy [30, 47].
  • Pain: While early-stage pressure ulcers may be painful, chronic pain can develop, significantly impacting quality of life and requiring comprehensive pain management strategies [1].
  • Recurrence: Patients who have had one pressure ulcer are at high risk of developing another, highlighting the importance of ongoing preventative measures and risk factor management [48].
  • Amputation: In severe, non-healing pressure ulcers, particularly those complicated by osteomyelitis or extensive tissue necrosis, amputation of the affected limb may be required as a last resort [49].
