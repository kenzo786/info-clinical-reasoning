Hepatocellular carcinoma (Liver, pancreas, biliary system) [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Hepatocellular carcinoma"]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Hepatocellular carcinoma"]
  • Hepatocellular carcinoma (HCC) is the most common primary malignancy of the liver, typically arising in the context of chronic liver disease and cirrhosis.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the risk factors, clinical presentation, and diagnostic approach to hepatocellular carcinoma, particularly in patients with underlying chronic liver disease.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Hepatocellular carcinoma"]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Hepatocellular carcinoma"]
  • The incidence of primary liver cancer, including HCC, has been rising in the UK. It accounts for 2% of all new cancer cases in the UK, with around 2,200 new cases in females and 4,400 in males annually (2017-2019 data) [1].
  • HCC incidence and mortality rates in the UK have trebled between 1997 and 2016 [2].
  • Major risk factors include chronic hepatitis B virus (HBV) and hepatitis C virus (HCV) infection, non-alcoholic fatty liver disease (NAFLD)/metabolic dysfunction-associated steatotic liver disease (MASLD), alcohol-related liver disease, and cirrhosis of any aetiology [3].
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Hepatocellular carcinoma"]
  • Chronic liver injury from various aetiologies (e.g., viral hepatitis, alcohol, MASLD) leads to sustained inflammation, hepatocyte damage, and regenerative hyperplasia.
  • Persistent cycles of injury and regeneration, coupled with genetic mutations and epigenetic alterations, promote the accumulation of pre-neoplastic changes within hepatocytes, often in the context of advanced fibrosis or established [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Cirrhosis: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="cirrhosis"].
  • This culminates in uncontrolled proliferation of malignant hepatocytes, forming a tumour through a multi-step process known as [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Hepatocarcinogenesis: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="hepatocarcinogenesis"].
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Hepatocellular carcinoma"]
  • Early phase: HCC often develops insidiously in the context of chronic liver disease, with early stages typically asymptomatic and detected through surveillance.
  • Progressive phase: As the tumour grows, symptoms may emerge due to mass effect, liver dysfunction, or metastasis. This phase can vary from weeks to months depending on tumour aggressiveness and underlying liver function.
  • Late phase: Advanced HCC is characterised by significant tumour burden, decompensated liver function, and widespread metastasis, leading to rapid clinical deterioration over weeks to a few months without effective treatment.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Hepatocellular carcinoma"]
  • Ask about unexplained weight loss - a common non-specific symptom of malignancy, indicating catabolism.
  • Ask about new or worsening abdominal pain - may indicate tumour growth, capsular stretch, or complications like rupture.
  • Ask about early satiety or abdominal fullness - suggests hepatomegaly or ascites due to mass effect or liver decompensation.
  • Ask about jaundice or dark urine - points to biliary obstruction or significant liver dysfunction, often a sign of advanced disease.
  • Ask about fatigue and malaise - general symptoms of chronic illness and malignancy, reflecting systemic impact.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Hepatocellular carcinoma"]
  • Check vital signs - may reveal tachycardia, hypotension (e.g., with variceal bleed), or fever (e.g., with tumour necrosis or infection).
  • Check for hepatomegaly or a palpable liver mass - suggests tumour burden; a hard, irregular liver edge is concerning.
  • Check for signs of chronic liver disease (e.g., spider naevi, palmar erythema, ascites, splenomegaly) - HCC almost always arises in the context of underlying liver disease.
  • Check for jaundice - indicates significant liver dysfunction or biliary obstruction.
  • Check for [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Hepatic encephalopathy: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="hepatic encephalopathy"] - a sign of advanced liver failure, which can be precipitated or worsened by HCC.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Hepatocellular carcinoma"]
  • Sudden worsening of liver function: Suggests rapid tumour progression or acute decompensation of underlying cirrhosis.
  • New onset or worsening ascites: May indicate portal vein thrombosis, peritoneal carcinomatosis, or further liver decompensation.
  • Unexplained fever and abdominal pain: Could signal tumour necrosis, infection, or rupture.
  • Haemodynamic instability: Potentially due to tumour rupture with haemoperitoneum, a life-threatening complication.
  • Rapidly increasing alpha-fetoprotein (AFP): A significant rise in AFP, especially in a cirrhotic patient, is highly suspicious for HCC.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Hepatocellular carcinoma"]
  • Early stage HCC: Often asymptomatic, detected incidentally on imaging or through surveillance in high-risk groups, highlighting the importance of screening.
  • Fibrolamellar HCC: A rare variant typically affecting younger patients without underlying liver disease, presenting with a large abdominal mass and normal AFP levels.
  • HCC in non-cirrhotic liver: Less common, but can occur, particularly in patients with chronic HBV infection or specific genetic conditions; diagnosis may be delayed due to lower index of suspicion.
  • Paraneoplastic syndromes: Rare presentations including erythrocytosis (due to erythropoietin production), hypercalcaemia (PTHrP), or hypoglycaemia (insulin-like growth factor), which can obscure the primary diagnosis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Hepatocellular carcinoma"]
  • Core for suspected HCC:
    • Liver function tests (LFTs), full blood count (FBC), coagulation screen, renal function.
    • Alpha-fetoprotein (AFP) - a tumour marker, elevated in many but not all HCC cases; useful for surveillance and diagnosis, but not definitive alone.
  • Imaging - Ultrasound: Initial screening tool for HCC in high-risk patients (cirrhosis).
  • Imaging - Multiphasic CT or MRI abdomen: Diagnostic imaging modality of choice. Characteristic findings include [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Arterial enhancement and washout: what is it and why does it matter clinically in HCC?" text="arterial enhancement in the arterial phase and washout in the portal venous or delayed phases"].
  • Severity/risk tools: Barcelona Clinic Liver Cancer (BCLC) staging system - integrates tumour characteristics, liver function, and performance status to guide treatment and prognosis. BCLC Staging on MDCalc
  • Biopsy: Not always required if imaging is typical for HCC in a cirrhotic liver, but may be necessary for atypical presentations or equivocal imaging.

  • A 68-year-old male with a history of hepatitis C-related cirrhosis presents with new-onset right upper quadrant pain, unexplained weight loss, and a rapidly rising alpha-fetoprotein level.
  • Elderly male with chronic liver disease and acute decompensation, concerning for hepatocellular carcinoma.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Hepatocellular carcinoma"]
  • Benign liver lesions (e.g., haemangioma, focal nodular hyperplasia): Lack typical arterial enhancement and washout pattern on dynamic imaging.
  • Liver metastases: Usually multiple lesions, often with a known primary malignancy; imaging characteristics differ from HCC.
  • Cholangiocarcinoma: Originates from bile duct epithelium, typically presents with biliary obstruction and different imaging features; tumour markers like CA19-9 may be elevated.
  • Liver abscess: Presents with fever, pain, and leucocytosis; imaging shows a fluid-filled lesion, often with gas.
  • Hepatitis (acute or chronic exacerbation): Elevated transaminases, but typically without a focal liver lesion or rapidly rising AFP.
  • Cirrhotic nodule (regenerative or dysplastic): Can be difficult to distinguish from early HCC on imaging; requires careful follow-up and sometimes biopsy. Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Hepatocellular carcinoma"]
  • Immediate priorities: Recognition of potential HCC, prompt referral to a specialist hepatobiliary multidisciplinary team (MDT) for assessment and staging. Management of complications such as variceal bleeding or liver decompensation.
  • Definitive management: Treatment options are highly dependent on tumour stage (e.g., BCLC staging), liver function, and patient performance status. These may include surgical resection, liver transplantation, locoregional therapies (e.g., radiofrequency ablation, transarterial chemoembolisation), or systemic therapies (e.g., tyrosine kinase inhibitors, immunotherapy).
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Regular surveillance post-treatment is crucial to detect recurrence or new lesions. This often involves imaging (ultrasound, CT, MRI) and AFP monitoring.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Patients should be advised on symptoms of worsening liver disease or potential HCC complications and when to seek urgent medical attention.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Hepatocellular carcinoma"]
  • Tumour rupture: A life-threatening emergency presenting with sudden, severe abdominal pain and haemodynamic instability, typically occurring in the progressive or late phase.
  • Portal vein thrombosis: Can lead to acute worsening of portal hypertension, ascites, and variceal bleeding, often occurring in the progressive phase.
  • Biliary obstruction: If the tumour compresses or invades bile ducts, leading to jaundice and cholangitis, typically in the progressive or late phase.
  • Liver decompensation: Worsening of underlying cirrhosis (e.g., ascites, encephalopathy, variceal bleeding) due to tumour burden or treatment side effects, often seen in the progressive to late phase.
  • Metastasis: Spread to distant organs (e.g., lungs, bone, adrenal glands) leading to new symptoms and significantly impacting prognosis, characteristic of the late phase.
