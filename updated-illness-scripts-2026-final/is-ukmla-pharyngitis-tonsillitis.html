Pharyngitis/ tonsillitis [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Pharyngitis/ tonsillitis"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Pharyngitis/ tonsillitis"][/sc]
  • Pharyngitis is inflammation of the pharynx, while tonsillitis is inflammation of the tonsils, often occurring concurrently and presenting as a sore throat.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: recognise the common causes, clinical features, and appropriate management of pharyngitis and tonsillitis, distinguishing between viral and bacterial aetiologies.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Pharyngitis/ tonsillitis"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Pharyngitis/ tonsillitis"][/sc]
  • Acute pharyngitis is one of the most common reasons for primary care consultations in the UK, with viral causes accounting for 85-95% of cases in adults and 70% in children.
  • Group A Streptococcus (GAS) is responsible for 5-15% of adult cases and 20-30% of paediatric cases, peaking in late winter and early spring.
  • Risk factors include close contact with infected individuals, crowded living conditions, and age (children and adolescents are more susceptible to bacterial tonsillitis).
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Pharyngitis/ tonsillitis"][/sc]
  • Trigger: Viral (e.g., rhinovirus, adenovirus, influenza) or bacterial (e.g., Group A Streptococcus) pathogens infect the pharyngeal and tonsillar mucosa.
  • Progression: Pathogen replication and host immune response lead to local inflammation, oedema, and exudate formation in the throat.
  • Consequence: Activation of nociceptors results in pain (sore throat), dysphagia, and systemic symptoms such as fever and malaise.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Pharyngitis/ tonsillitis"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Symptoms typically develop rapidly over hours to a day, often starting with a sudden sore throat, dysphagia, and fever.
  • Progressive phase: Viral infections usually resolve spontaneously within 3-7 days; bacterial infections may persist or worsen without antibiotics, particularly beyond 48-72 hours.
  • Late phase: Uncomplicated cases resolve completely. Persistent or worsening symptoms beyond a week, or new symptoms like unilateral swelling, may indicate complications.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Pharyngitis/ tonsillitis"][/sc]
  • Ask about sore throat - to establish severity, onset, and impact on swallowing.
  • Ask about fever/chills - to assess systemic inflammatory response and differentiate from local irritation.
  • Ask about dysphagia/odynophagia - to gauge the extent of inflammation and potential for airway compromise.
  • Ask about cough/rhinorrhoea/conjunctivitis - to identify associated viral upper respiratory tract infection symptoms, suggesting a viral aetiology.
  • Ask about headache/malaise - to evaluate systemic symptoms and overall illness burden.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Pharyngitis/ tonsillitis"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - to identify fever, tachycardia, or tachypnoea which may indicate systemic infection or sepsis.
  • Check pharyngeal erythema and tonsillar exudates - to assess local inflammation and presence of pus, often seen in bacterial infections.
  • Check cervical lymphadenopathy - to detect tender, enlarged anterior cervical nodes, common in bacterial tonsillitis.
  • Check palatal petechiae - to look for small red spots on the soft palate, a sign suggestive of Group A Streptococcal infection.
  • Check for rash - to identify scarlatiniform rash, indicative of scarlet fever, a complication of GAS.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Pharyngitis/ tonsillitis"][/sc]
  • Stridor/severe dysphagia: implies potential airway obstruction, requiring urgent assessment.
  • Unilateral tonsillar swelling/deviation of uvula: suggests peritonsillar abscess (quinsy), a serious complication.
  • Neck stiffness/trismus: may indicate deep neck space infection or meningitis.
  • Non-blanching rash: raises concern for meningococcal disease or other severe systemic infection.
  • Immunocompromise: increases risk of severe or atypical infections, requiring lower threshold for investigation.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Pharyngitis/ tonsillitis"][/sc]
  • Young children: may present with non-specific symptoms like irritability, poor feeding, or abdominal pain rather than a clear sore throat.
  • Older adults: symptoms may be attenuated or atypical, with less prominent fever or pharyngeal signs, increasing diagnostic challenge.
  • Immunocompromised: infections can be more severe, caused by unusual pathogens, and may lack typical inflammatory signs, delaying diagnosis.
  • Mononucleosis (glandular fever): presents with severe pharyngitis, profound fatigue, lymphadenopathy, and splenomegaly, often mimicking bacterial tonsillitis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Pharyngitis/ tonsillitis"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected pharyngitis/tonsillitis: Clinical assessment using a validated scoring system like the Centor or FeverPAIN score to estimate the probability of Group A Streptococcal infection.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Throat swab for bacterial culture or rapid antigen detection test (RADT) if Centor/FeverPAIN score suggests intermediate to high probability of GAS, particularly in areas with high rates of acute rheumatic fever.
  • Imaging - Lateral neck X-ray: considered if there is suspicion of epiglottitis or retropharyngeal abscess, especially with stridor or severe neck pain.
  • Serial reassessment: important for patients with worsening symptoms or new red flags, to monitor for complications like peritonsillar abscess.
  • Validated score: Centor Score on MDCalc or FeverPAIN Score on MDCalc.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Pharyngitis/ tonsillitis"][/sc]
  • A 28-year-old male presents with a 3-day history of sudden onset severe sore throat, difficulty swallowing, and fever, accompanied by tender anterior cervical lymphadenopathy and tonsillar exudates on examination.
  • An acutely unwell young adult with an inflammatory pharyngitis, characterised by rapid onset, systemic symptoms, and local signs of infection, raising concern for bacterial aetiology.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Pharyngitis/ tonsillitis"][/sc]
  • Viral pharyngitis - often associated with cough, rhinorrhoea, and conjunctivitis.
  • Infectious mononucleosis - prolonged fatigue, generalised lymphadenopathy, and splenomegaly.
  • Peritonsillar abscess (quinsy) - severe unilateral throat pain, trismus, and uvular deviation.
  • Epiglottitis - rapid onset severe dysphagia, drooling, stridor, and muffled voice.
  • Retropharyngeal abscess - severe neck pain, torticollis, and systemic toxicity.
  • Gastro-oesophageal reflux disease (GORD) - chronic throat irritation, hoarseness, and heartburn, typically without fever.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Pharyngitis/ tonsillitis"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Assess for airway compromise and red flag features. Provide symptomatic relief with analgesia (paracetamol, ibuprofen).
  • Definitive management: Antibiotics (e.g., phenoxymethylpenicillin) are considered for bacterial pharyngitis/tonsillitis based on clinical scoring (Centor/FeverPAIN) to reduce symptom duration and prevent complications.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Advise on symptom progression and when to seek further medical attention. Reassess if symptoms worsen or do not improve as expected.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Instruct patients to return immediately for worsening pain, difficulty breathing, drooling, or inability to swallow fluids.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Pharyngitis/ tonsillitis"][/sc]
  • Peritonsillar abscess (quinsy): results from spread of infection into the peritonsillar space, causing severe unilateral pain, trismus, and uvular deviation.
  • Rheumatic fever: a rare but serious immune-mediated complication of untreated Group A Streptococcal infection, affecting heart, joints, brain, and skin.
  • Post-streptococcal glomerulonephritis: another rare immune-mediated complication of GAS, leading to acute kidney injury, typically presenting 1-2 weeks after infection.
  • Deep neck space infection: spread of infection beyond the tonsillar capsule into fascial planes of the neck, potentially leading to airway compromise or sepsis.
  • Obstructive sleep apnoea: chronic tonsillar hypertrophy, particularly in children, can lead to airway obstruction during sleep.
