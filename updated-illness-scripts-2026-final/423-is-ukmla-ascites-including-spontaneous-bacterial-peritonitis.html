Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Ascites (including spontaneous bacterial peritonitis)"][/sc]
  • Ascites is the pathological accumulation of fluid within the peritoneal cavity, often a manifestation of underlying liver disease, particularly cirrhosis. Spontaneous bacterial peritonitis (SBP) is an infection of this ascitic fluid without an obvious intra-abdominal source.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical features, common causes, and initial management of ascites and spontaneous bacterial peritonitis.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Ascites (including spontaneous bacterial peritonitis)"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Ascites (including spontaneous bacterial peritonitis)"][/sc]
  • Ascites is a common complication of cirrhosis, affecting approximately 50% of patients within 10 years of diagnosis [1]. The incidence of SBP in patients with cirrhotic ascites is about 10-30% per year [2].
  • Risk factors for ascites primarily include chronic liver diseases such as alcoholic liver disease, non-alcoholic fatty liver disease (NAFLD), and viral hepatitis. For SBP, risk factors include low ascitic fluid protein concentration (<15 g/L), prior episodes of SBP, and gastrointestinal haemorrhage [3].
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Ascites (including spontaneous bacterial peritonitis)"][/sc]
  • Trigger: Portal hypertension, primarily due to cirrhosis, leads to splanchnic vasodilation and reduced effective circulating blood volume, activating the renin-angiotensin-aldosterone system (RAAS) and sympathetic nervous system.
  • Progression: This neurohumoral activation results in renal vasoconstriction and increased sodium and water retention, exacerbating fluid accumulation in the peritoneal cavity. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Portal hypertension: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Portal hypertension"][/sc]
  • Consequence: The presence of protein-poor ascitic fluid, coupled with impaired immune function in advanced liver disease, increases susceptibility to bacterial translocation from the gut, leading to spontaneous bacterial peritonitis (SBP).
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Ascites (including spontaneous bacterial peritonitis)"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Gradual accumulation of fluid, often asymptomatic until significant volume is present. May be insidious, particularly beyond several weeks.
  • Progressive phase: Increasing abdominal distension, discomfort, and potential for complications like SBP. This phase can evolve over days to weeks.
  • Late phase: Refractory ascites, recurrent SBP, and other decompensating events of liver disease, indicating advanced stage and poor prognosis.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Ascites (including spontaneous bacterial peritonitis)"][/sc]
  • Ask about increasing abdominal girth or distension - often the first noticeable symptom, indicating fluid accumulation.
  • Ask about abdominal discomfort or pain - can range from mild pressure to severe pain, especially if SBP is developing.
  • Ask about shortness of breath - large volume ascites can restrict diaphragmatic movement, leading to dyspnoea.
  • Ask about ankle swelling - often co-occurs with ascites due to systemic fluid retention.
  • Ask about fever or chills - suggestive of infection, particularly SBP in the context of ascites.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Ascites (including spontaneous bacterial peritonitis)"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - fever, tachycardia, and hypotension may indicate SBP or other complications.
  • Check for abdominal distension and shifting dullness - classic signs of significant ascitic fluid accumulation.
  • Check for fluid thrill - another physical sign indicating a large volume of ascites.
  • Check for peripheral oedema - often present due to systemic fluid overload.
  • Check for signs of chronic liver disease (e.g., jaundice, spider naevi, palmar erythema) - indicates the underlying cause of ascites. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Signs of chronic liver disease: what are they and what do they indicate?" text="Signs of chronic liver disease"][/sc]
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Ascites (including spontaneous bacterial peritonitis)"][/sc]
  • Fever and abdominal pain: Highly suggestive of spontaneous bacterial peritonitis (SBP), requiring urgent diagnostic paracentesis and empirical antibiotics.
  • Acute kidney injury: Can indicate hepatorenal syndrome, a severe complication of advanced liver disease and ascites, necessitating prompt management.
  • Gastrointestinal haemorrhage: Often precipitates hepatic decompensation and increases the risk of SBP.
  • Rapidly increasing ascites: May suggest underlying malignancy or acute liver injury, warranting further investigation.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Ascites (including spontaneous bacterial peritonitis)"][/sc]
  • Older adults: May present with less pronounced symptoms or non-specific complaints, making diagnosis challenging. Co-morbidities can complicate management.
  • Immunosuppressed patients: Increased risk of SBP and other infections, potentially with blunted inflammatory responses, leading to delayed diagnosis.
  • Cardiac ascites: Ascites due to right-sided heart failure may present with elevated jugular venous pressure and peripheral oedema, distinguishing it from liver-related ascites. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Cardiac ascites vs. liver ascites: how to differentiate?" text="Cardiac ascites"][/sc]
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Ascites (including spontaneous bacterial peritonitis)"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected ascites: Liver function tests (LFTs), renal function tests (U&Es), full blood count (FBC), coagulation screen.
  • Core for suspected SBP: Diagnostic paracentesis with ascitic fluid analysis (cell count and differential, protein, albumin, culture). A polymorphonuclear (PMN) count ≥ 250 cells/mm³ is diagnostic of SBP.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Consider cardiac biomarkers (e.g., BNP) for cardiac ascites, or imaging for malignancy.
  • Imaging - Ultrasound abdomen: Essential for confirming ascites, assessing liver morphology, and guiding paracentesis.
  • Serial reassessment: Regular monitoring of fluid balance, weight, and renal function.
  • The MELD Score on MDCalc can be used to assess prognosis in liver disease.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Ascites (including spontaneous bacterial peritonitis)"][/sc]
  • A 58-year-old male with a history of alcoholic cirrhosis presents with 3 weeks of increasing abdominal distension, mild diffuse abdominal pain, and a low-grade fever for 2 days. On examination, he has shifting dullness, peripheral oedema, and mild tenderness in the lower abdomen.
  • A middle-aged male with chronic liver disease presenting with subacute abdominal distension, acute abdominal pain, and systemic inflammatory response, suggesting decompensated cirrhosis with superimposed infection.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Ascites (including spontaneous bacterial peritonitis)"][/sc]
  • Cardiac ascites (e.g., right heart failure) - elevated JVP, signs of heart failure, higher ascitic fluid protein.
  • Malignant ascites (e.g., peritoneal carcinomatosis) - often associated with weight loss, abdominal masses, cytology positive for malignant cells.
  • Pancreatic ascites (e.g., pancreatitis, pancreatic duct disruption) - very high ascitic fluid amylase.
  • Tuberculous peritonitis - chronic course, fever, weight loss, high ascitic fluid lymphocyte count, positive AFB stain/culture.
  • Budd-Chiari syndrome - acute onset, hepatomegaly, abdominal pain, characteristic imaging findings of hepatic vein obstruction.
  • Nephrotic syndrome - severe proteinuria, generalised oedema, normal liver function.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Ascites (including spontaneous bacterial peritonitis)"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Assess for complications like SBP or hepatorenal syndrome. Perform diagnostic paracentesis if SBP is suspected.
  • Definitive management: Address the underlying cause (e.g., alcohol cessation for alcoholic liver disease). Manage ascites with sodium restriction and diuretics (e.g., spironolactone, furosemide). Large volume paracentesis may be required for symptomatic relief.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Regular monitoring of weight, abdominal girth, renal function, and electrolytes. Consider prophylactic antibiotics for high-risk SBP patients.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Advise patients on symptoms of worsening ascites or signs of infection. Refer for liver transplant assessment if appropriate.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Ascites (including spontaneous bacterial peritonitis)"][/sc]
  • Spontaneous bacterial peritonitis (SBP): Infection of ascitic fluid, often occurring in advanced liver disease, leading to increased morbidity and mortality.
  • Hepatorenal syndrome (HRS): A severe complication characterised by renal failure in patients with advanced liver disease and ascites, typically triggered by an event like SBP or gastrointestinal haemorrhage.
  • Hepatic encephalopathy: Worsening liver function can lead to accumulation of toxins, affecting brain function, manifesting as confusion, altered consciousness, and asterixis.
  • Oesophageal variceal haemorrhage: Increased portal hypertension can lead to rupture of oesophageal varices, causing life-threatening gastrointestinal bleeding.
  • Umbilical hernia: Increased intra-abdominal pressure from large volume ascites can lead to or exacerbate umbilical hernias, with risk of incarceration or rupture.
