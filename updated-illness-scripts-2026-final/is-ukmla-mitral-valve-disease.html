Mitral valve disease (Heart and vasculature) [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Mitral valve disease"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Mitral valve disease"][/sc]
  • Mitral valve disease encompasses conditions affecting the mitral valve, leading to either impaired opening (stenosis) or incomplete closure (regurgitation), thereby disrupting normal blood flow between the left atrium and left ventricle.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical features, differentiate between types, and understand the principles of management for mitral valve disease, including its common causes and complications.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Mitral valve disease"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Mitral valve disease"][/sc]
  • Rheumatic heart disease remains a significant cause of mitral stenosis globally, though its incidence has declined in the UK due to improved public health and antibiotic use [1].
  • Mitral regurgitation is the most common form of heart valve disease, with prevalence increasing with age, affecting approximately 2-3% of the general population and up to 10% in those over 75 years [1].
  • Risk factors include advanced age, history of rheumatic fever, infective endocarditis, ischaemic heart disease, and connective tissue disorders.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Mitral valve disease"][/sc]
  • Mitral stenosis typically results from chronic inflammation and fibrosis of the valve leaflets and chordae tendineae, often post-rheumatic fever, leading to a narrowed orifice that impedes left ventricular filling.
  • Mitral regurgitation involves the failure of the mitral valve to close completely during systole, causing blood to flow backward into the left atrium, which can be due to leaflet abnormalities, annular dilatation, or papillary muscle dysfunction. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Papillary muscle rupture: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Papillary muscle rupture"][/sc]
  • Both conditions can lead to increased pressures in the left atrium and pulmonary circulation, eventually causing pulmonary hypertension, right ventricular failure, and atrial fibrillation.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Mitral valve disease"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Often asymptomatic, particularly in mild to moderate disease, with compensatory mechanisms maintaining cardiac output.
  • Progressive phase: Gradual onset of symptoms such as dyspnoea on exertion, fatigue, and palpitations as the valve dysfunction worsens and compensatory mechanisms become overwhelmed, typically over months to years.
  • Late phase: Development of severe symptoms, heart failure, pulmonary hypertension, and atrial fibrillation, with a significantly reduced quality of life and increased risk of complications, particularly beyond several years of untreated severe disease.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Mitral valve disease"][/sc]
  • Ask about dyspnoea - often the earliest symptom, initially on exertion, progressing to orthopnoea and paroxysmal nocturnal dyspnoea, indicating pulmonary congestion.
  • Ask about fatigue - due to reduced cardiac output and impaired tissue perfusion.
  • Ask about palpitations - common with atrial fibrillation, a frequent complication, especially in mitral stenosis.
  • Ask about chest pain - atypical, but can occur due to right ventricular strain in severe pulmonary hypertension.
  • Ask about haemoptysis - rare, but can occur in severe mitral stenosis due to ruptured bronchial veins from high pulmonary pressures.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Mitral valve disease"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - may reveal tachycardia (especially with AF), tachypnoea, and elevated jugular venous pressure (JVP) in advanced disease.
  • Check auscultation for murmurs - Mitral stenosis presents with a mid-diastolic rumble, opening snap, and loud S1; mitral regurgitation with a pansystolic murmur radiating to the axilla. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Austin Flint murmur: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Austin Flint murmur"][/sc]
  • Check signs of pulmonary hypertension - loud P2, right ventricular heave, and peripheral oedema.
  • Check peripheral oedema - indicates right heart failure.
  • Check displaced apex beat - typically inferolaterally displaced in chronic mitral regurgitation due to left ventricular dilatation.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Mitral valve disease"][/sc]
  • Acute pulmonary oedema - sudden onset severe dyspnoea, orthopnoea, and pink frothy sputum, requiring immediate medical attention.
  • New onset or rapidly worsening atrial fibrillation with rapid ventricular response - can lead to acute decompensation and haemodynamic instability.
  • Signs of systemic embolisation - sudden neurological deficit (stroke), acute limb ischaemia, or abdominal pain, indicating thrombus formation and dislodgement, particularly in mitral stenosis with AF.
  • Severe haemodynamic instability - hypotension, shock, or syncope, suggesting critical valve dysfunction or acute complications.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Mitral valve disease"][/sc]
  • Older adults: May present with non-specific symptoms like fatigue or reduced exercise tolerance, often attributed to age, delaying diagnosis.
  • Pregnancy: Physiological volume overload can unmask or worsen previously asymptomatic mitral valve disease, leading to acute heart failure. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Mitral valve disease in pregnancy: what are the considerations?" text="Mitral valve disease in pregnancy"][/sc]
  • Acute mitral regurgitation: Can present as cardiogenic shock with sudden onset dyspnoea and hypotension, often due to papillary muscle rupture post-myocardial infarction.
  • Asymptomatic severe disease: Patients may have significant valve dysfunction without symptoms, identified incidentally on imaging, highlighting the importance of screening in at-risk groups.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Mitral valve disease"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected mitral valve disease: Transthoracic echocardiography (TTE) is the primary diagnostic tool, assessing valve morphology, function, chamber sizes, and pulmonary pressures.
  • Severity/risk tools: NYHA Functional Classification for symptom severity. EuroSCORE II on MDCalc for surgical risk assessment.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: ECG to detect atrial fibrillation or ventricular hypertrophy; chest X-ray for cardiomegaly or pulmonary congestion.
  • Imaging - Transoesophageal echocardiography (TOE): Provides more detailed views of the mitral valve, particularly useful for assessing leaflet pathology, chordal rupture, and guiding interventions. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Transoesophageal echocardiography: indications and findings in mitral valve disease" text="Transoesophageal echocardiography"][/sc]
  • Imaging - Cardiac MRI: Offers comprehensive assessment of ventricular function, myocardial fibrosis, and quantification of regurgitant volume, especially when echocardiography is inconclusive.
  • Serial reassessment: Regular echocardiography is crucial for monitoring disease progression and timing of intervention, as per NICE guidelines [1].
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Mitral valve disease"][/sc]
  • A 68-year-old female with a history of rheumatic fever presents with progressive exertional dyspnoea, palpitations, and a mid-diastolic murmur on auscultation.
  • Elderly female with chronic progressive dyspnoea and a diastolic murmur, concerning for mitral stenosis with associated atrial fibrillation.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Mitral valve disease"][/sc]
  • Aortic stenosis - Ejection systolic murmur, often radiating to the carotids, with symptoms of angina, syncope, and dyspnoea.
  • Heart failure with preserved ejection fraction (HFpEF) - Presents with dyspnoea and signs of fluid overload but normal left ventricular systolic function.
  • Pulmonary hypertension (primary) - Dyspnoea, fatigue, and signs of right heart failure without significant left-sided heart disease.
  • Tricuspid regurgitation - Pansystolic murmur loudest at the left sternal edge, increasing with inspiration, often associated with right heart failure.
  • Constrictive pericarditis - Presents with signs of right heart failure, elevated JVP with a prominent y descent, and often a pericardial knock. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Constrictive pericarditis: how does it mimic mitral valve disease?" text="Constrictive pericarditis"][/sc]
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Mitral valve disease"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Recognition of acute decompensation (e.g., acute pulmonary oedema), escalation to senior medical staff, and supportive care including oxygen, diuretics, and rate control for atrial fibrillation.
  • Definitive management: May involve medical management to control symptoms and complications (e.g., beta-blockers for mitral stenosis, ACE inhibitors for mitral regurgitation), or surgical/percutaneous intervention (e.g., balloon valvuloplasty, valve repair or replacement) based on symptom severity, valve dysfunction, and ventricular function [1].
  • Adjunctive management: Anticoagulation for atrial fibrillation or history of thromboembolism is often warranted to prevent systemic embolisation [1].
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Regular clinical review and echocardiographic assessment are crucial to monitor disease progression and assess the timing of intervention [1].
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Patients should be advised on symptoms of worsening heart failure or new neurological deficits, and when to seek urgent medical review.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Mitral valve disease"][/sc]
  • Atrial fibrillation: Common, particularly in mitral stenosis, due to left atrial enlargement and pressure overload, increasing the risk of systemic embolisation.
  • Pulmonary hypertension: Develops due to chronic elevation of left atrial and pulmonary venous pressures, leading to right ventricular strain and eventual failure.
  • Systemic embolisation: Thrombi forming in the dilated left atrium (especially with AF) can dislodge and cause strokes or peripheral arterial occlusions.
  • Heart failure: Both mitral stenosis and regurgitation can lead to left-sided heart failure, and subsequently right-sided heart failure, as the heart struggles to maintain adequate cardiac output.
  • Infective endocarditis: Patients with mitral valve disease are at increased risk, especially with prosthetic valves or prior episodes, requiring vigilance for new murmurs or fever.
