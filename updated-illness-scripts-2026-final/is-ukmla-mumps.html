Mumps (Infections) [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Mumps"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Mumps"][/sc]
  • Mumps is an acute viral infection caused by a paramyxovirus, primarily affecting the parotid glands, leading to painful swelling. It is spread by direct contact with saliva.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical presentation of mumps, its common complications, and the importance of vaccination and public health notification.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Mumps"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Mumps"][/sc]
  • Mumps is a notifiable disease in England and Wales since October 1988, with recent confirmed cases in England at 39 in Q1 2025 and 20 in Q3 2025.
  • MMR vaccination coverage for the first dose in England was 88.9% in 2023-24, which is below the WHO target of 95%.
  • Unvaccinated individuals and those in close contact with infected persons are at highest risk, particularly young adults aged 13-25 who may not have received two doses of MMR vaccine.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Mumps"][/sc]
  • Infection with mumps [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Paramyxovirus: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="paramyxovirus"][/sc] typically occurs via respiratory droplets, with initial replication in the upper respiratory tract.
  • The virus spreads via [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Viremia in mumps: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="viremia"][/sc] to glandular and neural tissues, with a predilection for the parotid glands.
  • This leads to an inflammatory response and swelling of affected tissues, which can include the parotid glands, testes, ovaries, pancreas, and central nervous system.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Mumps"][/sc]
  • Early phase: The incubation period typically ranges from 14 to 18 days, but can extend up to 25 days. Prodromal symptoms such as malaise, headache, low-grade fever, myalgia, and anorexia may precede parotitis by 1-2 days.
  • Progressive phase: Parotitis usually develops within 24 hours of prodromal symptoms, often presenting unilaterally before becoming bilateral within a few days. Swelling typically peaks within 1-3 days and subsides over 7-10 days.
  • Late phase: Complications such as orchitis, oophoritis, meningitis, or pancreatitis may develop concurrently with or after parotitis, particularly beyond 5-7 days of symptom onset.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Mumps"][/sc]
  • Ask about painful swelling of the parotid glands - often unilateral initially, becoming bilateral, which is characteristic of mumps.
  • Ask about fever, headache, and malaise - these non-specific prodromal symptoms can precede parotitis and indicate a viral illness.
  • Ask about earache or pain on chewing/swallowing - suggests parotid gland involvement and inflammation.
  • Ask about testicular pain or swelling in post-pubertal males - indicates potential [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Mumps orchitis: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="orchitis" ][/sc], a significant complication.
  • Ask about abdominal pain or tenderness - may suggest pancreatitis or oophoritis, particularly in females.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Mumps"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - typically a low-grade fever, but higher temperatures may indicate complications like meningitis.
  • Check for parotid gland swelling - usually tender, firm, and obliterates the angle of the jaw, often bilateral.
  • Check for [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Stensen's duct oedema: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Stensen's duct oedema"] [/sc] - visible or palpable oedema at the opening of the parotid duct inside the cheek, indicating inflammation.
  • Check for testicular swelling and tenderness - unilateral or bilateral orchitis, a common complication in post-pubertal males.
  • Check for signs of meningism (neck stiffness, photophobia) - suggests aseptic meningitis, which can occur in mumps.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Mumps"][/sc]
  • Severe headache and neck stiffness: May indicate mumps meningitis or meningoencephalitis, requiring urgent assessment.
  • Sudden onset severe abdominal pain: Could suggest mumps pancreatitis, which can be a serious complication.
  • High fever and altered mental status: Raises concern for encephalitis, a rare but severe neurological complication.
  • Unilateral testicular pain and swelling in a post-pubertal male with mumps: Suggests orchitis, which carries a risk of infertility.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Mumps"][/sc]
  • Asymptomatic infection: Up to 20% of mumps infections are asymptomatic, making disease control challenging.
  • Mumps without parotitis: Mumps can present with complications like orchitis, meningitis, or pancreatitis in the absence of parotid swelling, making diagnosis difficult.
  • Immunocompromised individuals: May have atypical or prolonged courses, and potentially more severe complications.
  • Vaccinated individuals: Can still contract mumps, but typically experience milder symptoms and fewer complications, often presenting with less pronounced parotitis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Mumps"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected mumps: Diagnosis is often clinical, but confirmation is via laboratory testing of a saliva sample for mumps IgM antibodies or PCR.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Consider full blood count (FBC) and C-reactive protein (CRP) to rule out bacterial parotitis, or amylase/lipase for suspected pancreatitis.
  • Imaging - Ultrasound: May be used to differentiate mumps parotitis from other causes of salivary gland swelling or to assess for orchitis.
  • Severity/risk tools: No widely used severity score for mumps, but clinical assessment of complications is crucial.
  • Lumbar puncture: Indicated if meningitis or encephalitis is suspected, to analyse cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) for viral aetiology.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Mumps"][/sc]
  • A 22-year-old unvaccinated male presents with a 3-day history of fever, headache, and painful, bilateral parotid swelling, followed by unilateral testicular pain.
  • A young adult with recent exposure to mumps, presenting with parotitis and new-onset orchitis, concerning for mumps infection with complications.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Mumps"][/sc]
  • Bacterial parotitis: Often unilateral, purulent discharge from Stensen's duct, more severe systemic symptoms, and may occur in dehydrated or elderly patients.
  • Cervical lymphadenitis: Swelling is typically in the neck, not obliterating the angle of the jaw, and may be associated with upper respiratory tract infections.
  • Sialolithiasis (salivary gland stones): Presents with intermittent swelling and pain, often exacerbated by eating, and may be palpable.
  • Other viral parotitis (e.g., influenza, parainfluenza, adenovirus): Clinically similar to mumps, but less likely to cause orchitis or other systemic complications.
  • HIV-associated salivary gland disease: Chronic, non-tender parotid swelling, typically in individuals with known HIV infection.
  • Testicular torsion: Acute, severe testicular pain and swelling, often with absent cremasteric reflex, requiring urgent surgical intervention. Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Mumps"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Recognition of mumps as a notifiable disease, isolation to prevent spread, and supportive care for symptoms.
  • Definitive management: Mumps is a viral illness, so management is primarily supportive, focusing on symptom relief.
  • Adjunctive management: Analgesia (e.g., paracetamol, ibuprofen) for pain and fever, and adequate hydration. Cold compresses may support parotid swelling.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Monitor for complications such as orchitis, meningitis, or pancreatitis. Advise on rest and isolation until swelling subsides (typically 5 days after onset of parotitis).
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Advise patients to seek urgent medical attention for severe headache, neck stiffness, persistent vomiting, severe abdominal pain, or altered consciousness.
  • Exam pharmacology reference (UKMLA/MRCGP): No specific antiviral treatments are routinely used for mumps. Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Mumps"][/sc]
  • Orchitis: Inflammation of the testes, occurring in 20-30% of post-pubertal males, typically 5-7 days after parotitis onset. Can lead to testicular atrophy and, rarely, infertility. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Infertility risk from mumps orchitis: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Infertility risk"] is a key concern.
  • Oophoritis: Inflammation of the ovaries, occurring in about 5% of post-pubertal females, usually presenting with lower abdominal pain.
  • Aseptic meningitis: Occurs in up to 10% of cases, often mild and self-limiting, presenting with headache, fever, and neck stiffness, typically developing within a week of parotitis.
  • Pancreatitis: Occurs in about 4% of cases, presenting with epigastric pain, nausea, and vomiting, usually within the first week of illness.
  • Sensorineural hearing loss: A rare but permanent complication, usually unilateral.
  • Encephalitis: A very rare but severe neurological complication, presenting with altered consciousness, seizures, and focal neurological deficits.
