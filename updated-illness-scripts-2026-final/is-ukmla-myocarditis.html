Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Myocarditis"][/sc]
  • Myocarditis is an inflammatory condition of the myocardium, often triggered by infection, immune-mediated processes, or drug exposure, leading to myocardial injury and dysfunction.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the varied presentations of myocarditis, understand its common aetiologies, and identify key diagnostic features and red flags necessitating urgent cardiac assessment.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Myocarditis"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Myocarditis"][/sc]
  • Myocarditis accounts for approximately 0.04% of all NHS admissions in the UK, with around 12,927 admissions recorded annually.
  • It predominantly affects young adults aged 19-35 years, though severe cases have been reported in neonates, often linked to viral infections.
  • Viral infections, particularly Coxsackie B virus, are the most common cause, with recent data also highlighting COVID-19 as a significant trigger.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Myocarditis"][/sc]
  • Trigger: An initial insult, often a viral infection (e.g., Coxsackie B, adenovirus, parvovirus B19), leads to direct myocardial cell damage and triggers an innate immune response.
  • Progression: The immune response, involving T-lymphocytes and macrophages, can become dysregulated, leading to ongoing inflammation and autoimmune destruction of myocardial cells, even after the initial trigger is cleared. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Myocardial inflammation: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Myocardial inflammation"][/sc]
  • Consequence: Persistent inflammation and myocyte necrosis result in impaired cardiac function, ranging from mild dysfunction to severe heart failure, arrhythmias, and sudden cardiac death.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Myocarditis"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Symptoms typically develop acutely over days to a few weeks following a viral prodrome, often presenting with chest pain, dyspnoea, or palpitations.
  • Progressive phase: If inflammation persists, cardiac function may progressively worsen over weeks to months, potentially leading to dilated cardiomyopathy or chronic heart failure.
  • Late phase: Long-term sequelae can include chronic heart failure, recurrent arrhythmias, or sudden cardiac death, particularly beyond six months in severe cases.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Myocarditis"][/sc]
  • Ask about chest pain - often pleuritic or pericarditic in nature, mimicking ischaemia but typically not relieved by nitrates, indicating myocardial inflammation.
  • Ask about dyspnoea - can range from exertional to dyspnoea at rest, reflecting impaired cardiac output and potential heart failure.
  • Ask about palpitations - may indicate underlying arrhythmias secondary to myocardial irritation or scarring.
  • Ask about fatigue - a common, non-specific symptom, but can be profound and persistent, suggesting systemic inflammatory burden and reduced cardiac reserve.
  • Ask about recent viral illness - a preceding flu-like illness, gastroenteritis, or upper respiratory tract infection is a crucial discriminator for infectious aetiology.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Myocarditis"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - tachycardia is common, hypotension may indicate cardiogenic shock, and fever can be present in acute infective cases.
  • Check for signs of heart failure - elevated jugular venous pressure, peripheral oedema, and crackles on lung auscultation suggest significant cardiac dysfunction.
  • Check for muffled heart sounds or a pericardial rub - may indicate associated pericarditis or pericardial effusion.
  • Check for gallop rhythm (S3) - indicative of ventricular dysfunction and increased filling pressures. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Gallop rhythm (S3): what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Gallop rhythm (S3)"][/sc]
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Myocarditis"][/sc]
  • New onset heart failure: Rapidly deteriorating cardiac function, particularly within days to weeks of symptom onset, suggests acute severe myocardial inflammation.
  • Sustained ventricular arrhythmias: Ventricular tachycardia or fibrillation indicates significant myocardial irritability and a high risk of sudden cardiac death.
  • Cardiogenic shock: Hypotension, end-organ hypoperfusion, and severe cardiac dysfunction require immediate intensive care and advanced circulatory support.
  • High-grade AV block: New onset complete heart block or other significant conduction abnormalities suggest inflammation involving the cardiac conduction system.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Myocarditis"][/sc]
  • Children and neonates: May present with non-specific symptoms like irritability, poor feeding, tachypnoea, or lethargy, often progressing rapidly to cardiogenic shock.
  • Older adults: Symptoms can be subtle, often mistaken for ischaemic heart disease or age-related cardiac decline, leading to delayed diagnosis.
  • Immunosuppressed patients: May present with fulminant myocarditis due to opportunistic infections or atypical immune responses, with rapid deterioration.
  • Post-vaccination: Rare cases of myocarditis and pericarditis have been reported following certain vaccinations, typically presenting with acute chest pain and dyspnoea.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Myocarditis"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected myocarditis: Electrocardiogram (ECG) may show diffuse ST-T wave changes, arrhythmias, or conduction blocks; cardiac biomarkers (troponin I/T) are typically elevated, indicating myocardial injury.
  • Core for suspected myocarditis: Echocardiography often reveals ventricular dysfunction, regional wall motion abnormalities, or pericardial effusion, helping to assess cardiac mechanics.
  • Imaging - Cardiac MRI: The gold standard for non-invasive diagnosis, using T1 and T2 mapping and late gadolinium enhancement to identify myocardial oedema, hyperaemia, and necrosis (Lake Louise criteria). [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Lake Louise criteria: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Lake Louise criteria"][/sc]
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Coronary angiography should be considered to rule out ischaemic heart disease, especially in patients with chest pain and risk factors, where coronaries are typically normal in myocarditis.
  • Severity/risk tools: While no single score is universally adopted, clinical assessment of left ventricular ejection fraction and presence of arrhythmias guides risk stratification.
  • Serial reassessment: Repeat ECGs, troponins, and echocardiograms are crucial to monitor disease progression and response to supportive therapy.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Myocarditis"][/sc]
  • A young adult presenting with acute chest pain, dyspnoea, and elevated troponin following a recent viral illness, with normal coronary arteries on angiography.
  • Acute cardiac inflammation with myocardial injury, concerning for myocarditis.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Myocarditis"][/sc]
  • Acute coronary syndrome (ACS) - typically presents with ischaemic chest pain, often with risk factors, and coronary angiography reveals obstructive disease, unlike myocarditis.
  • Pericarditis - chest pain is often pleuritic and relieved by leaning forward, and ECG changes are typically diffuse ST elevation without reciprocal depression, though it can co-exist with myocarditis.
  • Stress-induced cardiomyopathy (Takotsubo) - often triggered by emotional or physical stress, with transient left ventricular apical ballooning and normal coronaries, but typically without significant inflammation.
  • Pulmonary embolism (PE) - presents with acute dyspnoea and pleuritic chest pain, but ECG may show right heart strain, and D-dimer is usually elevated, with imaging confirming pulmonary arterial thrombi.
  • Sepsis with myocardial depression - systemic inflammatory response can cause global cardiac dysfunction, but the primary pathology is infection elsewhere, not direct myocardial inflammation.
  • Drug-induced cardiotoxicity - history of exposure to cardiotoxic agents (e.g., chemotherapy) is key, and presentation can mimic myocarditis, necessitating careful drug history. Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Myocarditis"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Recognition of acute cardiac compromise, prompt escalation to cardiology, and initiation of supportive care including oxygen, fluid management, and treatment of arrhythmias or heart failure.
  • Definitive management: Primarily supportive, focusing on managing heart failure symptoms with diuretics and ACE inhibitors/ARBs, and beta-blockers once stable. Immunosuppressive therapy may be considered in specific autoimmune or giant cell myocarditis cases.
  • Adjunctive management: Consideration of antiviral therapy in specific viral aetiologies (e.g., influenza), though evidence for routine use is limited.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Regular clinical assessment, serial echocardiography, and cardiac MRI are essential to monitor ventricular function recovery and detect chronic complications.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Patients with severe ventricular dysfunction, refractory arrhythmias, or cardiogenic shock may require advanced therapies such as mechanical circulatory support or cardiac transplantation.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Myocarditis"][/sc]
  • Dilated cardiomyopathy: Chronic inflammation and myocardial damage can lead to ventricular remodelling and progressive heart failure, often developing over weeks to months.
  • Arrhythmias: Myocardial scarring and electrical instability can cause both supraventricular and ventricular arrhythmias, including life-threatening ventricular tachycardia/fibrillation, particularly in the acute and subacute phases.
  • Sudden cardiac death: A critical and often early complication, resulting from severe arrhythmias or acute pump failure, especially in the initial days to weeks of presentation.
  • Thromboembolism: Ventricular dysfunction and stasis of blood can increase the risk of intracardiac thrombus formation, leading to systemic embolisation (e.g., stroke), particularly in the progressive phase.
  • Pericarditis: Inflammation can extend to the pericardium, causing pericarditis or pericardial effusion, which may present concurrently or develop during the early phase.
