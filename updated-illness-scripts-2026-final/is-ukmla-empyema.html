Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Empyema (Lungs, pleura and airways)"][/sc]
  • Empyema is the accumulation of pus within the pleural space, typically as a complication of bacterial pneumonia.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical features and diagnostic criteria for empyema, including the interpretation of pleural fluid analysis and imaging findings.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Empyema (Lungs, pleura and airways)"][/sc]

  • The incidence of empyema admissions in England has steadily increased, from approximately 4,916 in 2008 to 7,011 in 2017, with a male predominance (67% vs 33%) [1].
  • Risk factors include bacterial pneumonia, recent thoracic surgery, trauma, oesophageal perforation, and immunocompromise (e.g., diabetes mellitus) [2].
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Empyema (Lungs, pleura and airways)"][/sc]
  • Initial trigger: Bacterial pneumonia leads to inflammation of the pleura, resulting in a parapneumonic effusion (exudative phase) [3].
  • Progression: Bacterial invasion of the pleural space causes fibrin deposition, loculation, and pus formation (fibrinopurulent phase) [3]. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Empyema three-stage model: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Three-stage model of empyema"][/sc]
  • Consequence: Untreated, the pleural space becomes organised with thick fibrous peels, trapping the lung and impairing respiratory function (organised phase) [3].
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Empyema (Lungs, pleura and airways)"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Within days of pneumonia onset, a sterile exudative pleural effusion may develop, often resolving with antibiotic treatment of the underlying pneumonia.
  • Progressive phase: Over several days to weeks, bacterial invasion and inflammation lead to a fibrinopurulent effusion, with increasing viscosity and loculation.
  • Late phase: If untreated, within weeks to months, the effusion becomes organised, forming a thick fibrous peel that can restrict lung expansion.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Empyema (Lungs, pleura and airways)"][/sc]
  • Ask about persistent fever - often indicates ongoing infection despite antibiotic therapy for pneumonia.
  • Ask about pleuritic chest pain - suggests pleural inflammation, which can be severe and localised.
  • Ask about shortness of breath - due to lung compression by the accumulating fluid and impaired respiratory mechanics.
  • Ask about productive cough - may indicate underlying pneumonia, but persistent cough with purulent sputum can be a feature.
  • Ask about malaise and weight loss - non-specific symptoms of chronic infection or inflammation, particularly in the organised phase.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Empyema (Lungs, pleura and airways)"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - persistent fever, tachycardia, and tachypnoea are common, indicating systemic inflammatory response.
  • Check reduced chest expansion - on the affected side due to fluid accumulation.
  • Check dullness to percussion - over the area of pleural effusion.
  • Check diminished or absent breath sounds - over the effusion, indicating fluid separating the lung from the chest wall.
  • Check vocal fremitus and resonance - typically reduced over an effusion. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Vocal fremitus and resonance in pleural effusion: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Vocal fremitus and resonance"][/sc]
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Empyema (Lungs, pleura and airways)"][/sc]
  • Sepsis: Rapid deterioration with signs of systemic infection (e.g., hypotension, altered mental status) warrants immediate escalation.
  • Rapidly increasing effusion: Suggests ongoing inflammation or inadequate drainage, requiring urgent reassessment.
  • Failure to improve after 48-72 hours of antibiotics: Indicates resistant infection, loculated effusion, or alternative diagnosis, necessitating further investigation.
  • Persistent fever and pleuritic pain: Despite initial management, may signify inadequate source control or progression of the empyema.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Empyema (Lungs, pleura and airways)"][/sc]
  • Older adults: May present with non-specific symptoms like confusion, falls, or general malaise, with less prominent respiratory signs.
  • Immunocompromised patients: Can have delayed or blunted inflammatory responses, leading to insidious onset and rapid progression.
  • Children: Often present with fever, cough, and respiratory distress, but may have less localised pain and can progress rapidly.
  • Post-surgical patients: Empyema can develop after thoracic surgery, sometimes masked by post-operative pain and inflammation.
  • Non-specific symptoms in older adults: Older patients may present with subtle or atypical symptoms, making diagnosis challenging. Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Empyema (Lungs, pleura and airways)"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected empyema:
    • Chest X-ray: May show pleural effusion, but often underestimates its size and complexity.
    • Thoracic ultrasound: Highly sensitive for detecting pleural fluid, guiding thoracentesis, and identifying loculations.
    • CT chest with contrast: Provides detailed anatomical information, identifies loculations, pleural thickening, and underlying lung pathology.
    • Pleural fluid analysis (thoracentesis): Essential for diagnosis. Grossly purulent fluid confirms empyema. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Pleural fluid analysis in empyema: what are the key parameters and why do they matter clinically?" text="Pleural fluid analysis"][/sc]

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Empyema (Lungs, pleura and airways)"][/sc]
  • Parapneumonic effusion: Differentiated by the absence of frank pus, negative Gram stain/culture, and less severe pleural fluid biochemistry (e.g., higher pH, higher glucose, lower LDH). [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Parapneumonic effusion vs empyema: what are the key differences and why do they matter clinically?" text="Parapneumonic effusion"][/sc]
  • Lung abscess: Characterised by a cavitating lesion within the lung parenchyma, whereas empyema is confined to the pleural space.
  • Malignant pleural effusion: Often insidious onset, with cytology positive for malignant cells and typically less inflammatory pleural fluid.
  • Tuberculous pleurisy: Granulomatous inflammation on pleural biopsy, positive acid-fast bacilli (AFB) stain or culture, and often a lymphocytic exudate.
  • Subphrenic abscess: Located below the diaphragm, can cause reactive pleural effusion but the primary pathology is intra-abdominal.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Empyema (Lungs, pleura and airways)"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Recognition, escalation to a senior clinician, and initiation of appropriate broad-spectrum antibiotics.
  • Definitive management: Drainage of the infected pleural fluid, typically via chest drain insertion. Intrapleural fibrinolytics (e.g., tissue plasminogen activator and deoxyribonuclease) may be considered for multiloculated effusions to improve drainage, as supported by the MIST2 trial.
  • Adjunctive management: Optimisation of pain control and respiratory support.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Regular clinical assessment, imaging (e.g., chest X-ray, ultrasound), and repeat pleural fluid analysis as indicated.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Surgical intervention (e.g., VATS, decortication) may be warranted for persistent or complicated empyema not responding to medical management.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Empyema (Lungs, pleura and airways)"][/sc]
  • Sepsis: Systemic inflammatory response to infection, potentially leading to organ dysfunction and septic shock, particularly if drainage is delayed.
  • Bronchopleural fistula: An abnormal communication between the bronchial tree and the pleural space, leading to persistent air leak and potential for recurrent infection.
  • Trapped lung: Formation of a thick fibrous peel around the lung, preventing its re-expansion and leading to chronic restrictive lung disease, often developing weeks to months after initial infection.
  • Osteomyelitis of ribs/vertebrae: Direct extension of infection from the pleural space to adjacent bone structures, a rare but serious complication.
  • Empyema necessitans: Extension of the empyema through the chest wall into subcutaneous tissues, presenting as a palpable mass, typically in chronic, untreated cases.
