Multiple myeloma [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Multiple myeloma"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Multiple myeloma"][/sc]
  • Multiple myeloma is a progressive malignant disease of abnormal plasma cells in the bone marrow, characterised by the production of monoclonal immunoglobulins (paraproteins).
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical features and diagnostic criteria for multiple myeloma, including the significance of CRAB criteria and paraproteins.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Multiple myeloma"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Multiple myeloma"][/sc]
  • Myeloma accounts for 2% of all new cancer cases in the UK, with approximately 2,600 new cases in females and 3,600 in males annually (2017-2019).
  • Incidence rates are highest in people aged 85 to 89 in the UK, with around 9% of all new myeloma cases diagnosed in this age group (2017-2019).
  • Incidence rates have increased by almost two-fifths (38%) in the UK since the early 1990s and by more than a tenth (12%) in the last decade (2017-2019).
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Multiple myeloma"][/sc]
  • Trigger: Uncontrolled proliferation of a single clone of plasma cells in the bone marrow, often preceded by monoclonal gammopathy of undetermined significance (MGUS).
  • Progression: Accumulation of malignant plasma cells leads to bone marrow infiltration, production of monoclonal immunoglobulins ([sc name="jitl-query" Q="Paraprotein: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="paraprotein"][/sc]), and suppression of normal haematopoiesis.
  • Consequence: Systemic effects due to plasma cell overgrowth and paraprotein activity, manifesting as the [sc name="jitl-query" Q="CRAB criteria: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="CRAB criteria"] (hypercalcaemia, renal failure, anaemia, bone lesions) and other complications.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Multiple myeloma"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Often asymptomatic, with progression from MGUS over years. Initial symptoms are typically vague, such as fatigue or non-specific bone pain.
  • Progressive phase: As plasma cell burden increases, symptoms become more pronounced, including persistent bone pain, pathological fractures, recurrent infections, and signs of renal impairment.
  • Late phase: Advanced disease with significant organ damage, severe bone destruction, intractable pain, and profound cytopenias, leading to increased morbidity and mortality.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Multiple myeloma"][/sc]
  • Ask about bone pain - often persistent, worse with movement, and can indicate pathological fractures or vertebral collapse.
  • Ask about fatigue - a common and often debilitating symptom due to anaemia, renal impairment, or systemic inflammation.
  • Ask about recurrent infections - due to immunosuppression from abnormal plasma cell function and reduced normal immunoglobulin production.
  • Ask about unexplained weight loss - a non-specific but important indicator of underlying malignancy.
  • Ask about neurological symptoms - such as weakness or numbness, which may suggest spinal cord compression or peripheral neuropathy.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Multiple myeloma"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - may reveal tachycardia or hypotension in severe anaemia or dehydration; fever in infection.
  • Check pallor - indicates anaemia, a common feature of myeloma due to bone marrow infiltration.
  • Check bone tenderness or deformity - suggests lytic lesions or pathological fractures, particularly in the spine or ribs.
  • Check neurological examination - to detect signs of spinal cord compression (e.g., motor weakness, sensory loss, bladder/bowel dysfunction) or peripheral neuropathy.
  • Check fundoscopy - for signs of hyperviscosity syndrome, such as dilated tortuous retinal veins or retinal haemorrhages, and [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Rouleaux formation: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="rouleaux formation"] on peripheral blood film.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Multiple myeloma"][/sc]
  • Unexplained persistent bone pain: particularly in the elderly, warrants urgent investigation for underlying malignancy, including myeloma.
  • Pathological fracture: a fracture occurring with minimal trauma, highly suggestive of bone weakening due to lytic lesions from myeloma.
  • Hypercalcaemia: often presents with confusion, polyuria, constipation, and can be a life-threatening metabolic emergency in myeloma.
  • Acute kidney injury: new onset or worsening renal function without clear cause, especially in the presence of other myeloma symptoms, requires immediate assessment.
  • Spinal cord compression: new or worsening back pain with neurological deficits (weakness, sensory changes, bladder/bowel dysfunction) is an oncological emergency requiring urgent imaging and management.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Multiple myeloma"][/sc]
  • Elderly patients: May present with non-specific symptoms like fatigue or general malaise, often attributed to ageing, delaying diagnosis.
  • Non-secretory myeloma: Absence of detectable paraprotein in serum or urine, making diagnosis challenging and requiring bone marrow biopsy for confirmation.
  • Plasma cell leukaemia: A rare and aggressive variant where plasma cells circulate in the peripheral blood, presenting with rapid disease progression and poor prognosis.
  • Solitary plasmacytoma: Localised collection of plasma cells, typically in bone or soft tissue, which can progress to multiple myeloma over time.
  • Light chain amyloidosis: Deposition of monoclonal light chains in tissues, leading to organ dysfunction (e.g., cardiac, renal) that can overshadow typical myeloma symptoms. Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Multiple myeloma"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected Multiple Myeloma:
    • Full blood count (anaemia, thrombocytopenia).
    • Renal function (elevated creatinine, hypercalcaemia).
    • Serum protein electrophoresis and immunofixation (detects paraprotein).
    • Serum free light chain assay (detects Bence-Jones protein and assesses light chain ratio).
    • Bone marrow aspirate and trephine biopsy (confirms plasma cell infiltration >10%).
  • Severity/risk tools: International Staging System (ISS) and Revised International Staging System (R-ISS) for prognosis. R-ISS for Multiple Myeloma on MDCalc
  • Imaging - Whole-body MRI: Recommended as first-line imaging to detect bone lesions and extramedullary plasmacytomas.
  • Imaging - Whole-body low-dose CT: Alternative first-line imaging if MRI is unsuitable.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Consider further investigations based on differential diagnoses, e.g., inflammatory markers for rheumatological conditions.
  • Serial reassessment: Regular monitoring of paraprotein levels, FBC, U&Es, and calcium to assess disease activity and treatment response. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Bence-Jones protein: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Bence-Jones protein"][/sc]

  • An elderly patient presenting with persistent bone pain, fatigue, and recurrent infections, with laboratory findings of anaemia, renal impairment, and a monoclonal paraprotein.
  • Elderly patient with CRAB criteria and evidence of monoclonal gammopathy, concerning for multiple myeloma.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Multiple myeloma"][/sc]
  • Monoclonal Gammopathy of Undetermined Significance (MGUS) - absence of CRAB criteria and <10% plasma cells in bone marrow.
  • Metastatic bone disease - typically presents with lytic or blastic lesions on imaging, but without paraproteinemia or diffuse plasma cell infiltration.
  • Osteoporosis - causes bone pain and fractures, but lacks paraprotein, anaemia, or renal dysfunction associated with myeloma.
  • Anaemia of chronic disease - can cause fatigue, but usually without significant bone pain, hypercalcaemia, or paraprotein.
  • Renal failure of unknown origin - may present with fatigue and electrolyte disturbances, but without the characteristic haematological or bone manifestations of myeloma.
  • Lymphoma - can cause bone pain and systemic symptoms, but typically involves lymphadenopathy and different haematological markers. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Waldenstrom's Macroglobulinemia: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Waldenstrom's Macroglobulinemia"][/sc]
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Multiple myeloma"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Recognition of CRAB criteria and other complications, prompt referral to haematology, supportive care for hypercalcaemia, renal impairment, and pain management.
  • Definitive management: Often includes chemotherapy (e.g., bortezomib, lenalidomide, thalidomide, cyclophosphamide, dexamethasone) and may involve autologous stem cell transplantation for eligible patients.
  • Adjunctive management: Bisphosphonates to manage bone disease, erythropoietin-stimulating agents for anaemia, and prophylactic antibiotics for recurrent infections.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Regular assessment of disease response (paraprotein levels), renal function, calcium, and FBC.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Patients should be educated on red flag symptoms and when to seek urgent medical attention, particularly for worsening bone pain, neurological changes, or signs of infection.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Multiple myeloma"][/sc]
  • Bone disease: Lytic lesions, pathological fractures, and spinal cord compression due to osteoclast activation and plasma cell infiltration, leading to significant pain and morbidity throughout the disease course.
  • Renal failure: Often caused by light chain cast nephropathy, hypercalcaemia, or amyloidosis, typically developing in the progressive phase and requiring careful monitoring.
  • Anaemia: Multifactorial, including bone marrow infiltration, renal impairment, and chronic inflammation, contributing to fatigue and reduced quality of life, particularly in the progressive and late phases.
  • Hypercalcaemia: Resulting from increased bone resorption, can lead to confusion, polyuria, and cardiac arrhythmias, often seen in the progressive phase and requiring urgent management.
  • Infections: Due to immunosuppression from disease and treatment, increasing susceptibility to bacterial and viral infections, a significant cause of morbidity and mortality throughout the disease.
  • Hyperviscosity syndrome: Less common, but can occur with very high paraprotein levels, leading to neurological symptoms, visual disturbances, and bleeding, typically in the late phase.
