Polycystic kidney disease [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Polycystic kidney disease"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Polycystic kidney disease"][/sc]
  • Polycystic kidney disease (PKD) is a group of inherited disorders characterised by the development of numerous fluid-filled cysts in the kidneys, leading to progressive renal impairment and potential extrarenal manifestations.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical features of autosomal dominant polycystic kidney disease (ADPKD) and autosomal recessive polycystic kidney disease (ARPKD), understand their genetic basis, and outline key principles of management and monitoring.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Polycystic kidney disease"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Polycystic kidney disease"][/sc]
  • Autosomal dominant polycystic kidney disease (ADPKD) is the most common inherited kidney disorder, affecting approximately 1 in 1,000 to 2,500 people in the UK, accounting for 30,000 to 70,000 individuals [1].
  • ADPKD is caused by mutations in the *PKD1* gene (78% of cases) or *PKD2* gene (15% of cases), with *PKD1* mutations typically leading to more severe disease [1].
  • Autosomal recessive polycystic kidney disease (ARPKD) is rarer, affecting about 1 in 20,000 babies in the UK, and is caused by mutations in the *PKHD1* gene [2].
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Polycystic kidney disease"][/sc]
  • Genetic mutations (e.g., *PKD1*, *PKD2*, *PKHD1*) disrupt normal renal tubular cell function, leading to abnormal proliferation and fluid secretion within the renal tubules.
  • This abnormal cellular activity results in the formation and progressive enlargement of numerous fluid-filled cysts, which gradually replace normal kidney parenchyma.
  • The expanding cysts compress and destroy surrounding healthy kidney tissue, leading to impaired glomerular filtration, chronic inflammation, fibrosis, and ultimately, end-stage renal disease. Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Polycystic kidney disease"][/sc]
  • Early phase: In ADPKD, cysts are present from birth but often remain asymptomatic until adulthood (typically 30-40 years of age), with slow cyst growth and preserved renal function. In ARPKD, symptoms usually manifest in utero or shortly after birth, with rapid progression of renal and hepatic disease.
  • Progressive phase: For ADPKD, cyst enlargement accelerates, leading to symptoms such as abdominal pain, hypertension, and recurrent urinary tract infections. Renal function gradually declines, often requiring renal replacement therapy by the sixth decade of life. For ARPKD, progressive renal failure and hepatic fibrosis develop in childhood, with significant morbidity and mortality.
  • Late phase: End-stage renal disease (ESRD) necessitates dialysis or kidney transplantation. Extrarenal manifestations, such as berry aneurysms and hepatic cysts in ADPKD, may become clinically significant, particularly beyond the initial presentation of renal symptoms.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Polycystic kidney disease"][/sc]
  • Ask about abdominal or flank pain - often due to enlarging cysts, haemorrhage into a cyst, or urinary tract infection, which can be a presenting symptom.
  • Ask about haematuria - may be macroscopic (visible blood in urine) or microscopic, often indicating cyst rupture or kidney stone passage.
  • Ask about symptoms of urinary tract infection (UTI) - such as dysuria, frequency, or fever, as cysts can become infected and require prompt treatment.
  • Ask about headaches or visual disturbances - particularly in ADPKD, as hypertension is common and intracranial aneurysms (berry aneurysms) are a recognised extrarenal complication.
  • Ask about fatigue, nausea, or poor appetite - these can be non-specific symptoms of declining renal function.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Polycystic kidney disease"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - particularly blood pressure, as hypertension is a very common and early manifestation of PKD, contributing to disease progression.
  • Check for palpable, enlarged kidneys - often bilateral and ballotable on abdominal examination, indicating significant cyst burden.
  • Check for abdominal distension or tenderness - may suggest large cysts, cyst haemorrhage, or infection.
  • Check for hepatomegaly or splenomegaly - especially in ARPKD due to associated hepatic fibrosis, or in ADPKD due to hepatic cysts.
  • Check for signs of chronic kidney disease - such as pallor (anaemia), oedema, or uraemic frost (rare), indicating advanced renal impairment. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Palpable kidneys in PKD: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Palpable kidneys"][/sc]
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Polycystic kidney disease"][/sc]
  • Sudden severe flank pain with fever: Suggests acute cyst infection or haemorrhage, requiring urgent imaging and antibiotic therapy.
  • Sudden onset severe headache, stiff neck, or neurological deficit: Raises concern for ruptured intracranial aneurysm (subarachnoid haemorrhage), particularly in ADPKD, necessitating immediate neurosurgical assessment.
  • Rapid decline in renal function: May indicate acute kidney injury superimposed on chronic disease, or accelerated cyst growth, requiring urgent nephrology review.
  • Signs of cardiac failure or pulmonary oedema: Can be a complication of severe hypertension or fluid overload in advanced kidney disease.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Polycystic kidney disease"][/sc]
  • Children (ADPKD): While typically adult-onset, a small proportion of children with ADPKD may present with hypertension, proteinuria, or early cyst development, necessitating early diagnosis and management.
  • Older adults (ADPKD): May present with complications of long-standing disease, such as advanced renal failure, recurrent cyst infections, or complications of intracranial aneurysms, where the diagnosis may have been missed earlier.
  • Pregnancy (ADPKD): Can be complicated by worsening hypertension, pre-eclampsia, or increased risk of cyst rupture, requiring careful monitoring and multidisciplinary care.
  • ARPKD in older children/adults: While primarily a childhood disease, some individuals with milder ARPKD phenotypes may survive into adulthood, presenting with progressive hepatic fibrosis, portal hypertension, and cholangitis in addition to renal impairment. Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Polycystic kidney disease"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected Polycystic kidney disease:
    • Renal ultrasound: Initial diagnostic tool to visualise renal cysts, assess kidney size, and evaluate for hydronephrosis. Diagnostic criteria for ADPKD are based on age and number of cysts.
    • Genetic testing: Confirms diagnosis, especially in atypical presentations or for family planning, identifying mutations in *PKD1*, *PKD2*, or *PKHD1*.
    • Urinalysis: To detect haematuria, proteinuria, or signs of infection.
    • Blood tests: Urea, creatinine, eGFR to assess renal function; full blood count for anaemia; electrolytes.
  • Severity/risk tools:
    • Total Kidney Volume (TKV) measurement (e.g., by MRI or CT): A prognostic marker for disease progression in ADPKD.
    • Mayo ADPKD Prognostic Model on MDCalc: Predicts risk of progression to ESRD in ADPKD.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible:
    • CT abdomen/pelvis: Provides more detailed imaging of renal cysts and extrarenal manifestations, differentiating PKD from other cystic kidney diseases.
  • Imaging - Brain:
    • MRI brain with MRA: Screening for intracranial aneurysms (berry aneurysms) in high-risk ADPKD patients (e.g., family history of aneurysm or rupture, prior rupture, high-risk occupations). Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Polycystic kidney disease"][/sc]
  • A 45-year-old male with a family history of kidney disease presents with chronic flank pain, hypertension, and recurrent haematuria, with imaging revealing significantly enlarged kidneys containing multiple cysts.
  • Adult-onset progressive cystic renal disease with extrarenal manifestations, concerning for autosomal dominant polycystic kidney disease.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Polycystic kidney disease"][/sc]
  • Simple renal cysts - typically solitary or few, not progressive, and do not cause renal dysfunction.
  • Acquired cystic kidney disease - develops in patients with long-standing chronic kidney disease, not inherited.
  • Medullary cystic kidney disease - smaller kidneys, cysts primarily in the medulla, and often associated with salt wasting.
  • Tuberous Sclerosis Complex - associated with renal angiomyolipomas and cysts, but also characteristic skin lesions and neurological features.
  • Von Hippel-Lindau disease - renal cysts and clear cell renal cell carcinoma, but also haemangioblastomas of the retina and central nervous system.
  • Multicystic dysplastic kidney - typically unilateral, non-functional, and often diagnosed prenatally. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Differentiating PKD from simple renal cysts: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Simple renal cysts"][/sc]
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Polycystic kidney disease"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Management of acute complications such as cyst infection (antibiotics), cyst haemorrhage (analgesia, rest, rarely intervention), or acute pain (analgesia). Prompt control of hypertension is crucial to slow disease progression.
  • Definitive management: No cure exists. Management focuses on slowing disease progression and managing complications. Tolvaptan may be considered in rapidly progressing ADPKD to slow the rate of decline in renal function and cyst growth.
  • Adjunctive management: Strict blood pressure control (target <130/80 mmHg, often with ACE inhibitors or ARBs), adequate hydration (to suppress vasopressin), pain management, and dietary modifications (low salt, protein restriction in advanced CKD).
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Regular monitoring of renal function (eGFR), blood pressure, and imaging (ultrasound, MRI) to assess cyst growth and detect complications. Screening for intracranial aneurysms may be indicated in high-risk individuals.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Referral to nephrology for diagnosis confirmation, disease monitoring, and consideration of specific therapies like tolvaptan. Preparation for renal replacement therapy (dialysis or transplantation) as ESRD approaches.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Polycystic kidney disease"][/sc]
  • Chronic kidney disease (CKD) and End-Stage Renal Disease (ESRD): Progressive cyst growth leads to irreversible renal damage, culminating in ESRD requiring dialysis or transplantation, typically in adulthood for ADPKD and childhood for ARPKD.
  • Hypertension: Very common, often an early manifestation, contributing to cardiovascular morbidity and accelerating renal decline if not adequately controlled.
  • Cyst infection or haemorrhage: Acute complications causing severe pain, fever, and potential sepsis, requiring prompt diagnosis and management, particularly beyond the initial presentation of renal symptoms.
  • Intracranial aneurysms (berry aneurysms): A significant extrarenal complication of ADPKD, with a risk of rupture leading to subarachnoid haemorrhage, which can be life-threatening. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Berry aneurysms in ADPKD: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Berry aneurysms"][/sc]
  • Hepatic cysts: Common in ADPKD, usually asymptomatic but can cause pain or mass effect. In ARPKD, congenital hepatic fibrosis and portal hypertension are significant complications.
  • Mitral valve prolapse: A less common but recognised extrarenal manifestation in ADPKD, usually asymptomatic but can rarely lead to valvular dysfunction.
