Neonatal surgical emergencies (Child health) [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Neonatal surgical emergencies"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Neonatal surgical emergencies"][/sc]
  • Neonatal surgical emergencies encompass acute conditions in newborns requiring urgent surgical intervention, primarily including pyloric stenosis and intussusception, which can lead to significant morbidity and mortality if not promptly managed.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical presentations of common neonatal surgical emergencies, such as pyloric stenosis and intussusception, and understand the principles of their initial assessment and management.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Neonatal surgical emergencies"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Neonatal surgical emergencies"][/sc]
  • Pyloric stenosis affects approximately 1 to 3 per 1,000 live births in the UK, with a male-to-female ratio of 4:1 and a peak incidence between 2 and 8 weeks of age [1].
  • Intussusception is the most common cause of intestinal obstruction in infants aged 3 months to 3 years, with an incidence of 1 to 4 per 1,000 live births in the UK, peaking between 5 and 9 months of age [2].
  • Risk factors for pyloric stenosis include male sex, prematurity, first-born child, and a family history of the condition.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Neonatal surgical emergencies"][/sc]
  • Pyloric Stenosis: Hypertrophy and hyperplasia of the circular muscle of the pylorus leads to progressive narrowing of the gastric outlet, obstructing the passage of gastric contents into the duodenum.
  • Intussusception: A segment of the intestine (intussusceptum) invaginates or "telescopes" into an adjacent distal segment (intussuscipiens), leading to bowel obstruction, venous congestion, and potentially ischaemia and necrosis.
  • Consequence: Both conditions result in vomiting, dehydration, and electrolyte disturbances; pyloric stenosis specifically causes [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Hypochloraemic metabolic alkalosis: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="hypochloraemic metabolic alkalosis"] due to loss of gastric acid.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Neonatal surgical emergencies"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Pyloric stenosis typically presents with mild, non-projectile vomiting around 2-3 weeks of age, gradually progressing over days to weeks. Intussusception often has an acute onset of intermittent, severe abdominal pain.
  • Progressive phase: In pyloric stenosis, vomiting becomes progressively more forceful and projectile, leading to dehydration and electrolyte imbalance. In intussusception, pain episodes become more frequent, and vomiting, lethargy, and passage of redcurrant jelly stools may develop within hours.
  • Late phase: If untreated, pyloric stenosis results in severe dehydration, malnutrition, and metabolic derangements. Untreated intussusception can lead to bowel ischaemia, perforation, peritonitis, and sepsis, particularly beyond 12-24 hours.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Neonatal surgical emergencies"][/sc]
  • Ask about projectile vomiting - characteristic of pyloric stenosis, often non-bilious and occurring shortly after feeding, distinguishing it from simple reflux.
  • Ask about intermittent severe abdominal pain - typically seen in intussusception, where infants draw their legs up to their abdomen during episodes, indicating colicky pain.
  • Ask about redcurrant jelly stools - a classic, though late, sign of intussusception, resulting from blood and mucus in the stool due to bowel ischaemia.
  • Ask about lethargy and poor feeding - common in both conditions due to dehydration and discomfort, indicating systemic compromise.
  • Ask about weight loss or failure to thrive - particularly in pyloric stenosis, due to persistent vomiting and inability to retain nutrients.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Neonatal surgical emergencies"][/sc]
  • Check for an olive-shaped mass in the right upper quadrant - a palpable hypertrophied pylorus, pathognomonic for pyloric stenosis.
  • Check for a sausage-shaped mass in the right upper quadrant - often palpable in intussusception, representing the telescoped bowel.
  • Check vital signs - tachycardia, poor capillary refill, and reduced urine output indicate dehydration, common in both conditions.
  • Check for abdominal distension and tenderness - suggestive of bowel obstruction or peritonitis, particularly in advanced intussusception.
  • Check for visible gastric peristalsis - waves moving from left to right across the upper abdomen, seen in pyloric stenosis as the stomach attempts to overcome the obstruction.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Neonatal surgical emergencies"][/sc]
  • Bilious vomiting: Indicates bowel obstruction distal to the ampulla of Vater, a surgical emergency requiring immediate investigation, particularly concerning for malrotation with volvulus.
  • Severe dehydration: Manifested by sunken fontanelle, reduced skin turgor, prolonged capillary refill time, and oliguria, necessitating urgent fluid resuscitation.
  • Abdominal distension with tenderness: Suggests peritonitis or bowel perforation, often a late sign of complicated intussusception or other bowel pathology.
  • Haemodynamic instability: Tachycardia, hypotension, and poor perfusion indicate shock, requiring immediate resuscitation and surgical consultation.
  • Absent bowel sounds: May indicate paralytic ileus or peritonitis, often associated with advanced bowel obstruction or perforation.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Neonatal surgical emergencies"][/sc]
  • Pyloric stenosis in older infants: While typically presenting in early infancy, pyloric stenosis can rarely occur later, potentially leading to delayed diagnosis and more severe dehydration.
  • Intussusception in neonates: Less common than in older infants, and may present with non-specific symptoms like lethargy or poor feeding, making diagnosis challenging.
  • Intussusception with lead point: In a small percentage of cases, particularly in older children, intussusception may be caused by a pathological lead point such as a Meckel\\'s diverticulum or polyp, and may present with chronic or recurrent abdominal pain.
  • Pyloric stenosis in females: Less common than in males, but when it occurs, it may present later and with more severe symptoms, potentially due to a higher threshold for diagnosis.
  • Intussusception without classic triad: Not all infants with intussusception will present with the classic triad of abdominal pain, vomiting, and redcurrant jelly stools; some may only have lethargy or altered consciousness, particularly in younger infants.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Neonatal surgical emergencies"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected pyloric stenosis: Abdominal ultrasound is the diagnostic modality of choice, demonstrating pyloric muscle thickness >3mm and pyloric channel length >15mm [1].
  • Core for suspected intussusception: Abdominal ultrasound is the primary diagnostic tool, revealing a "target sign" or "pseudokidney sign" [2].
  • Laboratory investigations: In pyloric stenosis, blood gas analysis typically shows [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Hypochloraemic alkalosis: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="hypochloraemic metabolic alkalosis"]() with hypokalaemia. Full blood count, electrolytes, and C-reactive protein are important in both conditions to assess for dehydration, electrolyte imbalance, and infection.
  • Severity/risk tools: No specific validated severity/risk scores are routinely used for initial diagnosis in these conditions, but clinical assessment of dehydration and electrolyte status guides management.
  • Imaging - Plain abdominal X-ray: May show signs of obstruction (dilated bowel loops, air-fluid levels) or perforation (free air under diaphragm), particularly useful if intussusception is suspected to have complicated.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Neonatal surgical emergencies"][/sc]
  • A 6-week-old male infant presents with a 2-week history of progressively worsening non-bilious projectile vomiting after feeds, associated with weight loss and lethargy, and an olive-shaped mass palpable in the right upper quadrant.
  • A 9-month-old infant presents with sudden onset intermittent severe abdominal pain, drawing up of legs, and passage of redcurrant jelly stools, with a palpable sausage-shaped mass in the right upper quadrant.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Neonatal surgical emergencies"][/sc]
  • Gastro-oesophageal reflux disease (GORD): Distinguished from pyloric stenosis by non-projectile vomiting, absence of an olive-shaped mass, and typically good weight gain.
  • Malrotation with volvulus: Presents with bilious vomiting, which is a critical differentiator from pyloric stenosis, and can rapidly lead to bowel ischaemia.
  • Gastroenteritis: Often associated with diarrhoea in addition to vomiting, and typically lacks the specific abdominal masses or projectile nature of vomiting seen in surgical emergencies.
  • Milk protein allergy: May cause vomiting and poor weight gain, but usually without the forceful projectile nature or palpable masses of pyloric stenosis, and may have other allergic manifestations.
  • Appendicitis (in older infants/children): While rare in neonates, it can present with abdominal pain and vomiting, but the pain is typically localised to the right iliac fossa and continuous, unlike the intermittent pain of intussusception.
  • Incarcerated hernia: Can cause bowel obstruction and vomiting, but a reducible or irreducible lump is typically visible or palpable in the inguinal region.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Neonatal surgical emergencies"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Recognition of dehydration and electrolyte imbalance, particularly in pyloric stenosis, warrants aggressive intravenous fluid resuscitation and correction of metabolic alkalosis prior to surgical intervention. In intussusception, stabilisation and fluid resuscitation are crucial before reduction attempts.
  • Definitive management (Pyloric Stenosis): Surgical pyloromyotomy (Ramstedt\\'s operation) is the definitive treatment, involving incision of the hypertrophied pyloric muscle to relieve the obstruction.
  • Definitive management (Intussusception): Hydrostatic or pneumatic (air) enema reduction is often the first-line treatment, guided by ultrasound or fluoroscopy. If unsuccessful or contraindicated, surgical reduction (with or without resection of necrotic bowel) is required.
  • Adjunctive management: Nasogastric tube insertion for gastric decompression in both conditions, and broad-spectrum antibiotics if bowel ischaemia or perforation is suspected in intussusception.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Post-operatively, infants require careful monitoring of feeding tolerance, hydration status, and electrolyte balance. Follow-up typically involves outpatient appointments to ensure adequate weight gain and resolution of symptoms.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Any signs of worsening abdominal pain, persistent vomiting, lethargy, or fever should prompt immediate re-evaluation and escalation to a paediatric surgical team.
  • Exam pharmacology reference (UKMLA/MRCGP): No doses or frequencies are cited here — always verify against your local antimicrobial/prescribing formulary before prescribing.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Neonatal surgical emergencies"][/sc]
  • Severe dehydration and electrolyte imbalance: Untreated pyloric stenosis can lead to profound dehydration, hypokalaemia, and life-threatening hypochloraemic metabolic alkalosis, impacting cardiac and neurological function.
  • Bowel ischaemia and perforation: In intussusception, prolonged telescoping can compromise blood supply to the bowel, leading to necrosis, perforation, peritonitis, and sepsis, which can be rapidly fatal if not addressed.
  • Recurrence: Intussusception has a recurrence rate of approximately 5%, particularly in the immediate post-reduction period, necessitating vigilance for re-presentation.
  • Post-operative complications: Surgical interventions carry risks such as wound infection, bleeding, anastomotic leak (if resection performed), or incomplete pyloromyotomy, requiring careful post-operative monitoring.
