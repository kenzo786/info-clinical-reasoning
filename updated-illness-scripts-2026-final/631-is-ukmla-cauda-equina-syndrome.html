Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Cauda equina syndrome"][/sc]
  • Cauda equina syndrome (CES) is a rare but serious condition resulting from severe compression of the nerve roots of the cauda equina in the lumbar spinal canal. This compression can lead to significant neurological deficits, including motor and sensory dysfunction in the lower limbs, and bladder, bowel, and sexual dysfunction.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the red flag symptoms and signs of cauda equina syndrome, understand the urgency of investigation and management, and identify appropriate referral pathways to prevent permanent neurological damage.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Cauda equina syndrome"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Cauda equina syndrome"][/sc]
  • CES is a rare condition, with an estimated incidence of 1 to 3 in 100,000 population per year.
  • Approximately 8000 patients are assessed for suspected CES every year in England.
  • It occurs most frequently in individuals aged 30–49 years, and more commonly in women, with an incidence of 7.2 per 100,000 women aged 30–39 years per year.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Cauda equina syndrome"][/sc]
  • Trigger: The most common cause of CES is a large lumbar disc prolapse, typically at L4/L5 or L5/S1, leading to direct compression of the nerve roots within the spinal canal. Other causes include spinal tumours, trauma, infection, haematoma, or epidural abscess.
  • Progression: Sustained compression of the cauda equina nerve roots results in ischaemia and inflammation, leading to impaired nerve conduction and axonal damage. This progressive neurological compromise affects motor, sensory, and autonomic functions.
  • Consequence: If compression is not relieved promptly, irreversible nerve damage can occur, resulting in permanent neurological deficits such as lower limb paralysis, sensory loss (including saddle anaesthesia), and severe bladder, bowel, and sexual dysfunction. Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Cauda equina syndrome"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Symptoms often develop acutely or subacutely, typically within hours to a few days, following a precipitating event such as a disc herniation. Initial symptoms may include severe low back pain and unilateral or bilateral sciatica.
  • Progressive phase: As nerve compression worsens, new or progressive neurological symptoms emerge, such as saddle anaesthesia, bilateral leg weakness, and changes in bladder or bowel function. This progression can be rapid, often within hours to 24-48 hours, necessitating urgent assessment.
  • Late phase: If surgical decompression is delayed beyond 24-48 hours from the onset of severe symptoms, the risk of permanent neurological deficits significantly increases, including irreversible loss of bladder, bowel, and sexual function, and persistent motor weakness.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Cauda equina syndrome"][/sc]
  • Ask about difficulty initiating micturition or impaired sensation of urinary flow - early bladder dysfunction is a critical red flag, indicating compression of sacral nerve roots.
  • Ask about altered perianal, perineal or genital sensation (saddle anaesthesia) - this sensory loss in the S2-S5 dermatomes is highly indicative of cauda equina compression. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Saddle anaesthesia: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Saddle anaesthesia"][/sc]
  • Ask about severe or progressive neurological deficit of both legs, such as major motor weakness with knee extension, ankle eversion or foot dorsiflexion - bilateral motor weakness suggests significant nerve root involvement and can progress rapidly.
  • Ask about loss of sensation of rectal fullness or inability to control bowel movements - bowel dysfunction is a late but serious sign of sacral nerve root compression.
  • Ask about sexual dysfunction, such as inability to achieve erection or loss of vaginal sensation - this reflects autonomic nerve involvement and is a key symptom of CES.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Cauda equina syndrome"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - while not specific to CES, vital signs can indicate systemic illness, infection, or other acute conditions that may mimic or complicate CES.
  • Check perianal sensation - objective testing of sensation in the S2-S5 dermatomes (saddle area) can confirm subjective reports of altered sensation.
  • Check lower limb motor power - assess for bilateral motor weakness, particularly in knee extension, ankle eversion, and foot dorsiflexion, which can indicate significant nerve root compression.
  • Check anal tone - reduced or absent anal tone is a significant sign of sacral nerve root dysfunction, though its diagnostic value is debated.
  • Check for absent or diminished ankle reflexes - loss of ankle jerk reflex (S1) can indicate nerve root compression affecting the reflex arc.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Cauda equina syndrome"][/sc]
  • New onset urinary retention or incontinence - indicates significant compromise of bladder function due to sacral nerve root compression, requiring immediate investigation.
  • Saddle anaesthesia (altered perianal, perineal or genital sensation) - a hallmark symptom of CES, suggesting sensory nerve involvement in the S2-S5 dermatomes.
  • Bilateral leg weakness (severe or progressive) - rapidly worsening motor deficits in both lower limbs point to extensive nerve root compression and a high risk of permanent disability.
  • Loss of sensation of rectal fullness or faecal incontinence - signifies severe compromise of bowel function, often a later sign of CES.
  • Sexual dysfunction (new onset) - inability to achieve erection or loss of vaginal sensation indicates autonomic nerve involvement and is a critical red flag.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Cauda equina syndrome"][/sc]
  • Incomplete CES: Patients may present with some, but not all, classic symptoms, making diagnosis challenging. For example, unilateral sciatica progressing to bilateral, or subtle bladder dysfunction without complete retention. Early recognition of these subtle signs is crucial to prevent progression to complete CES.
  • Slowly progressive CES: In some cases, symptoms may develop gradually over weeks or months, rather than acutely. This can lead to delayed diagnosis as the insidious onset may be mistaken for other conditions. Vigilance for evolving neurological symptoms is important.
  • Post-traumatic CES: Following spinal trauma, CES can be masked by other injuries or pain, making assessment difficult. A high index of suspicion is required in patients with significant lumbar spine trauma and new neurological symptoms.
  • CES in pregnancy: Pregnant patients may experience CES due to disc herniation, often exacerbated by hormonal changes and increased lumbar lordosis. Diagnosis can be challenging due to physiological changes mimicking some CES symptoms. MRI is generally safe in pregnancy and should not be delayed if CES is suspected. Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Cauda equina syndrome"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected Cauda equina syndrome: Urgent Magnetic Resonance Imaging (MRI) of the lumbar spine is the gold standard for diagnosing CES. It can identify the cause and extent of nerve root compression. MRI should be performed as soon as possible, ideally within 4 hours of request, especially if symptoms are acute or rapidly progressive.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Consider blood tests (e.g., inflammatory markers, infection screens) if an inflammatory, infective, or neoplastic cause is suspected. Plain radiographs are generally not helpful for diagnosing CES itself but may identify bony abnormalities.
  • Imaging - CT scan: If MRI is contraindicated (e.g., due to metallic implants), a CT myelogram or a non-contrast CT scan may be used as an alternative, though MRI is preferred for its superior soft tissue resolution.
  • Serial reassessment: Regular neurological examination, including assessment of motor power, sensation, and bladder/bowel function, is crucial to monitor for progression of symptoms while awaiting imaging or definitive treatment. Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Cauda equina syndrome"][/sc]
  • Conus Medullaris Syndrome - presents with more symmetrical and abrupt onset of symptoms, often with prominent back pain and hyperreflexia, contrasting with the typically asymmetrical and progressive flaccid paralysis of CES.
  • Severe Lumbar Radiculopathy (bilateral sciatica) - while sharing leg pain, it typically lacks the bladder/bowel dysfunction and saddle anaesthesia characteristic of CES. However, bilateral sciatica can precede CES.
  • Spinal Cord Compression (thoracic/cervical) - presents with upper motor neuron signs (spasticity, hyperreflexia, Babinski sign) and sensory levels, which are absent in CES.
  • Peripheral Neuropathy - typically causes distal, symmetrical sensory and motor deficits without the acute onset of bladder/bowel dysfunction or saddle anaesthesia seen in CES.
  • Transverse Myelitis - an inflammatory condition causing acute motor, sensory, and autonomic dysfunction below a specific spinal cord level, but usually presents with a clear sensory level and upper motor neuron signs.
  • Spinal Tumour (non-compressive) - can cause back pain and neurological symptoms, but often with a more insidious onset and without the acute, severe nerve root compression features of CES. Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Cauda equina syndrome"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Recognition of red flag symptoms, urgent referral to a facility with emergency MRI provision, and immediate neurosurgical or spinal surgical review. Supportive care may include pain management and bladder catheterisation if urinary retention is present.
  • Definitive management: Urgent surgical decompression of the cauda equina nerve roots is the definitive treatment, typically performed within 24-48 hours of symptom onset to maximise the chance of neurological recovery and prevent permanent deficits.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Post-operatively, patients require close monitoring of neurological function, bladder and bowel management, and intensive rehabilitation. Long-term follow-up is essential to assess recovery and manage any residual deficits.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Patients with suspected CES require clear safety-netting advice regarding symptom progression and when to seek immediate medical attention. Local pathways for urgent assessment and transfer should be followed.
  • Exam pharmacology reference (UKMLA/MRCGP): While specific drug knowledge is not a primary focus for CES management, understanding the role of analgesia (e.g., paracetamol, NSAIDs, weak opioids) for pain control and laxatives for bowel management may be relevant. Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Cauda equina syndrome"][/sc]
  • Permanent bladder dysfunction: If nerve compression is prolonged, irreversible damage to the sacral nerves can lead to chronic urinary retention, incontinence, or a neurogenic bladder, requiring long-term catheterisation or other management.
  • Permanent bowel dysfunction: Similar to bladder dysfunction, persistent nerve damage can result in chronic faecal incontinence or constipation, significantly impacting quality of life.
  • Persistent lower limb weakness and sensory loss: Irreversible motor and sensory deficits in the legs can lead to chronic pain, gait disturbances, and reduced mobility, even after surgical decompression.
  • Chronic pain: Neuropathic pain can persist despite successful decompression, often requiring multidisciplinary pain management strategies.
  • Sexual dysfunction: Long-term loss of sexual function, including erectile dysfunction in men and loss of vaginal sensation in women, can be a devastating complication of delayed treatment.
