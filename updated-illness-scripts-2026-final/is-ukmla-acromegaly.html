Acromegaly [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Acromegaly"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Acromegaly"][/sc]
  • Acromegaly is a rare, chronic endocrine disorder characterised by excessive secretion of growth hormone (GH) and insulin-like growth factor 1 (IGF-1) in adulthood, typically due to a pituitary adenoma, leading to disproportionate skeletal, tissue, and organ growth [1].
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical features, diagnostic pathway, and complications of acromegaly to facilitate early diagnosis and management.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Acromegaly"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Acromegaly"][/sc]
  • Acromegaly is a rare condition with an estimated prevalence in the UK of approximately 40 to 83 cases per million population [2, 3].
  • The annual incidence in the UK is around 4-6 new cases per million people [2].
  • Risk factors are primarily genetic syndromes that predispose individuals to growth hormone-secreting pituitary adenomas, though most cases are sporadic [4].
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Acromegaly"][/sc]
  • Trigger: A pituitary adenoma, typically benign, secretes excessive growth hormone (GH) [1].
  • Progression: Chronic GH excess stimulates the liver to produce high levels of insulin-like growth factor 1 (IGF-1), which mediates most of the growth-promoting effects [1].
  • Consequence: Sustained elevation of GH and IGF-1 leads to overgrowth of bone and soft tissues, resulting in the characteristic clinical features and systemic complications of acromegaly [1].
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Acromegaly"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Insidious onset of subtle changes, often developing over several years, making early diagnosis challenging.
  • Progressive phase: Gradual coarsening of facial features, acral enlargement, and systemic manifestations become more apparent, particularly beyond several years of symptom onset.
  • Late phase: Untreated acromegaly leads to significant comorbidities, including cardiovascular disease, diabetes, and arthropathy, particularly beyond decades of disease progression.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Acromegaly"][/sc]
  • Ask about swollen hands and feet - as patients may notice changes in ring or shoe size [1].
  • Ask about headaches - which can be due to the pituitary tumour itself [1].
  • Ask about excessive sweating - a common symptom due to increased metabolic rate [1].
  • Ask about joint pain - particularly in large joints, due to cartilage overgrowth and degeneration [1].
  • Ask about changes in facial features - such as a prominent brow, enlarged nose, or thick lips, often noticed by family members or from old photographs [1].
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Acromegaly"][/sc]
  • Check for coarsening of facial features - including frontal bossing, prognathism, and macroglossia, which are classic indicators [1].
  • Check for acral enlargement - specifically spade-like hands and enlarged feet, which may be evident on examination [1].
  • Check for skin changes - such as oily skin, skin tags, and hyperhidrosis [1].
  • Check vital signs - particularly blood pressure, as hypertension is a common comorbidity [1].
  • Check visual fields - for bitemporal hemianopia, indicating optic chiasm compression by a macroadenoma [1].
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Acromegaly"][/sc]
  • Sudden onset severe headache - may indicate pituitary apoplexy, requiring urgent neurosurgical assessment.
  • Rapid visual deterioration - suggests acute compression of the optic chiasm, necessitating immediate intervention.
  • New onset neurological deficits - such as cranial nerve palsies, indicating tumour expansion and potential neurological compromise.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Acromegaly"][/sc]
  • Older adults: Symptoms may be attributed to normal ageing, leading to delayed diagnosis and more advanced complications.
  • Patients with small adenomas: May present with subtle symptoms, making clinical recognition challenging without overt physical changes.
  • Ectopic GH production: Very rare, but can present without a pituitary mass, requiring investigation for non-pituitary sources of GH or GHRH.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Acromegaly"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected Acromegaly: Serum Insulin-like Growth Factor 1 (IGF-1) level, which is the best initial screening test due to its stable reflection of average GH levels [1].
  • Definitive diagnostic test: Oral Glucose Tolerance Test (OGTT) with serial GH measurements. Failure of GH to suppress below 1 µg/L (or local lab reference) confirms the diagnosis [1].
  • Imaging - MRI: MRI scan of the pituitary gland with contrast is the gold standard to localise the adenoma, determine its size, and assess for compression of surrounding structures [1].
  • Visual field testing: Formal visual field assessment (e.g., Humphrey visual field analyser) is crucial for macroadenomas to detect bitemporal hemianopia [1].
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Acromegaly"][/sc]
  • A 45-year-old man presents with a 5-year history of gradually enlarging hands and feet, increasing shoe size, persistent headaches, and excessive sweating, with recent development of snoring and joint pains.
  • A middle-aged man with a chronic, progressive endocrine disorder characterised by disproportionate growth, systemic comorbidities, and a pituitary mass effect.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Acromegaly"][/sc]
  • Hypothyroidism - may cause facial puffiness and fatigue, but typically without true tissue overgrowth.
  • Gigantism - occurs due to GH excess before epiphyseal plate fusion, leading to proportional tall stature.
  • Familial coarse features - genetic predisposition to prominent facial features without endocrine dysfunction.
  • Pachydermoperiostosis - rare genetic disorder causing skin thickening and bone changes, but distinct from endocrine pathology.
  • Diabetic neuropathy - can cause numbness and tingling, but typically in a stocking-glove distribution and associated with diabetes.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Acromegaly"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Manage acute complications such as pituitary apoplexy or severe visual impairment.
  • Definitive management: Transsphenoidal surgery is often the first-line treatment to remove the pituitary adenoma and normalise GH/IGF-1 levels [1].
  • Medical therapy: Somatostatin analogues (e.g., [sc name="jitl-medication" Q="Octreotide use in Acromegaly" text="octreotide"][/sc], [sc name="jitl-medication" Q="Lanreotide use in Acromegaly" text="lanreotide"][/sc]) or dopamine agonists (e.g., [sc name="jitl-medication" Q="Cabergoline use in Acromegaly" text="cabergoline"][/sc]) may be used if surgery is unsuccessful or contraindicated [1].   • Radiotherapy: May be considered as an adjunct to surgery or medical therapy, particularly for residual tumour or persistent GH/IGF-1 elevation [1].
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Regular biochemical assessment of GH and IGF-1, pituitary imaging, and screening for comorbidities (e.g., diabetes, hypertension, cardiovascular disease) [1].
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Patients should be advised to seek urgent medical attention for sudden severe headaches, visual changes, or new neurological symptoms.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Acromegaly"][/sc]
  • Cardiovascular disease: Hypertension, cardiomyopathy, and heart failure are major causes of morbidity and mortality due to chronic GH/IGF-1 excess [1].
  • Type 2 Diabetes Mellitus: Insulin resistance induced by GH excess often leads to impaired glucose tolerance and overt diabetes [1].
  • Arthropathy: Joint pain and degenerative arthritis are common due to cartilage and bone overgrowth [1].
  • Sleep apnoea: Macroglossia and soft tissue hypertrophy in the upper airway contribute to obstructive sleep apnoea [1].
  • Bowel polyps: Increased risk of colonic polyps and potentially colorectal cancer, necessitating regular screening [1].
  • Visual impairment: Due to compression of the optic chiasm by an expanding pituitary macroadenoma [1].
