Urinary retention and obstructive uropathy [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Urinary retention and obstructive uropathy"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Urinary retention and obstructive uropathy"][/sc]
  • Urinary retention and obstructive uropathy refers to the impaired outflow of urine from the bladder due to a physical obstruction within the urinary tract, leading to urine accumulation and potential kidney damage.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the signs and symptoms of acute and chronic urinary retention, identify common obstructive causes, and outline immediate management strategies including catheterisation.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Urinary retention and obstructive uropathy"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Urinary retention and obstructive uropathy"][/sc]
  • Acute urinary retention (AUR) is a common urological emergency, with an incidence of approximately 3 per 1000 patients per year in the UK emergency department setting [1].
  • Benign prostatic hyperplasia (BPH) is the most common cause of AUR in men, with the risk increasing significantly with age; approximately 1 in 10 men aged over 70 will experience AUR [2].
  • Chronic urinary retention (CUR) is less common but can lead to silent kidney damage. Risk factors include older age, male sex, neurological conditions, and certain medications.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Urinary retention and obstructive uropathy"][/sc]
  • Obstruction to urine outflow (e.g., BPH, urethral stricture, bladder neck dyssynergia) leads to increased intravesical pressure and bladder wall hypertrophy.
  • Prolonged obstruction causes detrusor muscle decompensation, leading to ineffective bladder contractions and incomplete emptying, eventually resulting in urinary retention. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Detrusor muscle decompensation: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Detrusor muscle decompensation"][/sc]
  • Chronic back pressure on the kidneys can lead to hydronephrosis and progressive renal parenchymal damage, culminating in obstructive nephropathy and acute kidney injury.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Urinary retention and obstructive uropathy"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Acute urinary retention typically presents suddenly with severe lower abdominal pain and inability to void, often developing over hours.
  • Progressive phase: If the obstruction is not relieved, symptoms may progress to overflow incontinence, recurrent urinary tract infections, and gradual onset of renal impairment over days to weeks.
  • Late phase: Chronic, unrecognised obstructive uropathy can lead to irreversible renal damage and chronic kidney disease, particularly beyond several weeks of sustained obstruction.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Urinary retention and obstructive uropathy"][/sc]
  • Ask about acute onset of severe lower abdominal pain and inability to pass urine - highly suggestive of acute urinary retention, requiring urgent intervention.
  • Ask about a sensation of incomplete bladder emptying or frequent small voids - indicative of chronic retention with overflow.
  • Ask about a history of lower urinary tract symptoms (LUTS) such as hesitancy, poor stream, or nocturia - these often precede obstructive retention, particularly in men with BPH.
  • Ask about changes in bowel habits, particularly constipation - severe constipation can cause extrinsic compression of the urethra, leading to retention.
  • Ask about recent medication changes - certain drugs (e.g., anticholinergics, opioids) can precipitate urinary retention.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Urinary retention and obstructive uropathy"][/sc]
  • Check for a palpable, distended bladder on abdominal examination - a key sign of significant urinary retention.
  • Check vital signs - fever or tachycardia may indicate associated infection (urosepsis).
  • Check for costovertebral angle tenderness - may suggest hydronephrosis or pyelonephritis.
  • Check for signs of neurological deficits (e.g., perianal sensation, anal tone) - to rule out neurogenic causes of bladder dysfunction. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Neurogenic bladder: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Neurogenic bladder"][/sc]
  • Check for prostatic enlargement on digital rectal examination (DRE) in men - a common cause of bladder outlet obstruction.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Urinary retention and obstructive uropathy"][/sc]
  • Acute kidney injury: Elevated serum creatinine or urea suggests renal impairment secondary to obstructive uropathy, requiring urgent decompression.
  • Sepsis: Fever, tachycardia, hypotension, or other signs of systemic infection indicate urosepsis, a life-threatening complication.
  • Anuria: Complete absence of urine output, particularly in the context of bilateral obstruction, signifies severe renal compromise.
  • Neurological deficits: New or worsening neurological symptoms (e.g., saddle anaesthesia, lower limb weakness) may point to cauda equina syndrome, which can cause urinary retention.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Urinary retention and obstructive uropathy"][/sc]
  • Painless chronic retention: Older adults may present with overflow incontinence or a large, non-tender palpable bladder without acute pain, often due to gradual detrusor decompensation.
  • Women: Obstructive uropathy in women is less common but can be caused by pelvic organ prolapse, gynaecological masses, or urethral strictures, often presenting with non-specific LUTS.
  • Post-operative retention: Following surgery, particularly pelvic or spinal procedures, patients may develop urinary retention due to anaesthetic effects, pain, or nerve injury. Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Urinary retention and obstructive uropathy"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected urinary retention:
    • Urinalysis and urine culture: To detect infection.
    • Serum electrolytes and creatinine: To assess renal function and detect acute kidney injury.
    • Bladder scan: To measure post-void residual (PVR) volume. A PVR >150ml is often considered significant, and >300ml is highly suggestive of retention. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Post-void residual volume: how is it measured and what do the values indicate?" text="Post-void residual volume"][/sc]
  • Imaging - Renal tract ultrasound: To assess for hydronephrosis, bladder wall thickening, and identify potential causes of obstruction (e.g., prostatic enlargement, bladder stones).
  • Severity/risk tools: Consider using the International Prostate Symptom Score (IPSS) on MDCalc for men with LUTS to quantify symptom severity and impact on quality of life.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Consider further imaging such as CT KUB or cystoscopy to identify complex anatomical obstructions.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Urinary retention and obstructive uropathy"][/sc]
  • A 78-year-old man presents with sudden onset of severe suprapubic pain and inability to void for 12 hours, with a history of increasing urinary hesitancy and nocturia.
  • Elderly male with acute, painful inability to void and a background of progressive obstructive LUTS, concerning for acute urinary retention secondary to benign prostatic obstruction.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Urinary retention and obstructive uropathy"][/sc]
  • Neurogenic bladder dysfunction: Lack of sensation or motor control leading to retention, often associated with neurological conditions (e.g., spinal cord injury, multiple sclerosis) - distinguished by neurological examination findings.
  • Medication-induced retention: Anticholinergics, opioids, and tricyclic antidepressants can impair bladder contractility - distinguished by medication history.
  • Bladder pain syndrome/Interstitial cystitis: Chronic pelvic pain with urinary symptoms but typically without significant outflow obstruction or elevated PVR.
  • Psychogenic retention: Rare, functional inability to void without organic cause - diagnosis of exclusion.
  • Impacted faeces: Severe constipation can cause extrinsic compression of the urethra, particularly in older adults - distinguished by rectal examination. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Impacted faeces causing urinary retention: mechanism and management?" text="Impacted faeces"][/sc]
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Urinary retention and obstructive uropathy"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities:
    • Urgent bladder decompression via urethral or suprapubic catheterisation to relieve pain and prevent renal damage [1].
    • Assessment for signs of infection or acute kidney injury.
  • Definitive management:
    • Management of the underlying cause of obstruction (e.g., alpha-blockers for BPH, surgical intervention for strictures or stones).
    • Trial without catheter (TWOC) may be considered after a period of catheterisation, often with alpha-blocker therapy in men with BPH [2].
  • Monitoring and follow-up:
    • Monitor urine output, renal function, and electrolytes, particularly for post-obstructive diuresis.
    • Regular review of symptoms and bladder function.
  • Exam pharmacology reference (UKMLA/MRCGP):
    • Alpha-adrenoceptor antagonists (e.g., [sc name="jitl-medication" Q="Tamsulosin use in BPH and urinary retention" text="tamsulosin"][/sc], [sc name="jitl-medication" Q="Alfuzosin use in BPH and urinary retention" text="alfuzosin"][/sc]) may support relaxation of prostatic smooth muscle, improving urine flow and increasing the success rate of TWOC in men with BPH [2].
    • No doses or frequencies are cited here — always verify against your local antimicrobial/prescribing formulary before prescribing.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Urinary retention and obstructive uropathy"][/sc]
  • Acute kidney injury: Prolonged obstruction leads to increased intrarenal pressure, reducing glomerular filtration and causing acute kidney injury if not promptly relieved.
  • Urinary tract infection/Urosepsis: Stasis of urine in the bladder and upper tracts increases the risk of bacterial proliferation, leading to UTIs and potentially life-threatening urosepsis.
  • Post-obstructive diuresis: Following relief of chronic urinary retention, a rapid and excessive urine output can occur, leading to dehydration and electrolyte imbalances, particularly in the initial 24-48 hours. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Post-obstructive diuresis: definition, causes, and management?" text="Post-obstructive diuresis"][/sc]
  • Bladder dysfunction: Chronic overdistension can lead to irreversible detrusor muscle damage, resulting in persistent bladder atony and a need for long-term catheterisation or intermittent self-catheterisation.
  • Bladder stones: Urinary stasis and infection can promote the formation of bladder calculi.
