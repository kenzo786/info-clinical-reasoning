Hospital Acquired Infections (Infections) [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Hospital Acquired Infections"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Hospital Acquired Infections"][/sc]
  • Hospital acquired infections (HAIs), also known as healthcare-associated infections (HCAIs), are infections acquired by patients while receiving care in a healthcare setting, which were neither present nor incubating at the time of admission.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the common types of hospital acquired infections, their risk factors, and the principles of prevention and control in various healthcare settings.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Hospital Acquired Infections"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Hospital Acquired Infections"][/sc]
  • In England, an estimated 6.4% of patients in acute hospitals had at least one healthcare-associated infection in 2011, with surgical site infections and urinary tract infections being the most common.
  • _Clostridioides difficile_ infection (CDI) and Methicillin-resistant _Staphylococcus aureus_ (MRSA) bacteraemia are significant HAIs, with mandatory surveillance data collected in the UK.
  • Risk factors include prolonged hospital stays, invasive medical devices (e.g., catheters, central lines), surgical procedures, immunosuppression, extremes of age, and broad-spectrum antibiotic use.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Hospital Acquired Infections"][/sc]
  • Trigger: Compromised host defences (e.g., surgical wounds, indwelling devices, immunosuppression) allow opportunistic pathogens, often from the patient\'s own flora or the healthcare environment, to colonise and invade normally sterile sites.
  • Progression: Pathogens multiply and establish infection, often facilitated by [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Biofilm formation: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="biofilm formation"][/sc] on medical devices or antibiotic resistance mechanisms, leading to local and systemic inflammatory responses.
  • Consequence: Uncontrolled infection can lead to sepsis, organ dysfunction, prolonged hospitalisation, increased morbidity, and mortality.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Hospital Acquired Infections"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Symptoms typically develop beyond 48 hours of hospital admission or within 30 days of a surgical procedure, often manifesting as localised infection at a surgical site or around an invasive device.
  • Progressive phase: If untreated or resistant, localised infections may progress to systemic involvement, leading to bacteraemia or sepsis within days to weeks.
  • Late phase: Persistent or recurrent infections, particularly with multidrug-resistant organisms, can lead to chronic complications or prolonged recovery, extending over weeks to months.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Hospital Acquired Infections"][/sc]
  • Ask about fever or chills - suggests systemic inflammatory response, potentially bacteraemia.
  • Ask about localised pain, redness, swelling, or purulent discharge - indicates a potential surgical site infection or device-related infection.
  • Ask about new or worsening cough with sputum production - concerning for hospital-acquired pneumonia.
  • Ask about dysuria, increased urinary frequency, or suprapubic pain - points towards a catheter-associated urinary tract infection.
  • Ask about new onset diarrhoea, especially watery stools - highly suggestive of _Clostridioides difficile_ infection (CDI).
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Hospital Acquired Infections"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - fever, tachycardia, tachypnoea, or hypotension may indicate sepsis.
  • Check surgical wounds or device insertion sites - erythema, warmth, tenderness, or purulent exudate are signs of local infection.
  • Check respiratory examination - crackles, rhonchi, or dullness to percussion may suggest pneumonia.
  • Check abdominal examination - tenderness, distension, or guarding could indicate intra-abdominal infection or severe colitis from CDI.
  • Check skin for new rashes or lesions - may indicate systemic spread or specific infections like [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Cellulitis: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="cellulitis"][/sc].
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Hospital Acquired Infections"][/sc]
  • Sepsis: Rapid deterioration in physiological parameters (e.g., new onset confusion, persistent hypotension, oliguria) indicating organ dysfunction due to infection, requiring immediate escalation.
  • Toxic megacolon: Severe abdominal distension, tenderness, and systemic toxicity in a patient with CDI, indicating a life-threatening complication requiring urgent surgical review.
  • Necrotising fasciitis: Rapidly spreading skin and soft tissue infection with severe pain, crepitus, and disproportionate systemic illness, demanding emergency surgical debridement.
  • Septic shock: Sepsis with persistent hypotension requiring vasopressors to maintain mean arterial pressure ≥65 mmHg and having a serum lactate level >2 mmol/L despite adequate volume resuscitation, signifying critical illness.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Hospital Acquired Infections"][/sc]
  • Older adults: May present with non-specific symptoms like confusion, falls, or functional decline without overt fever or localised signs, making diagnosis challenging.
  • Immunosuppressed patients: (e.g., chemotherapy, transplant recipients) may have blunted inflammatory responses, leading to subtle signs of infection and rapid progression to severe disease.
  • Patients on broad-spectrum antibiotics: May develop CDI without a clear history of recent antibiotic use, or present with atypical gastrointestinal symptoms.
  • Patients with indwelling devices: (e.g., central venous catheters) may develop device-related bloodstream infections with only fever and no local signs at the insertion site.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Hospital Acquired Infections"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected infection: Full blood count (leukocytosis, neutrophilia), C-reactive protein (elevated), blood cultures (for bacteraemia), urine dipstick and culture (for UTI), wound swabs (for SSI).
  • Specific for CDI: Stool sample for _C. difficile_ toxin A/B and glutamate dehydrogenase (GDH) antigen.
  • Severity/risk tools: Consider using CURB-65 score on MDCalc for pneumonia severity, or APACHE II score on MDCalc for general critical illness severity.
  • Imaging - Chest X-ray: For suspected hospital-acquired pneumonia (infiltrates, consolidation).
  • Imaging - CT abdomen: For severe CDI with suspected complications like [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Toxic megacolon: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="toxic megacolon"][/sc] or perforation.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Hospital Acquired Infections"][/sc]
  • A 72-year-old male, post-abdominal surgery, develops fever, increasing wound pain, and purulent discharge on day 5 post-op, with elevated inflammatory markers.
  • An elderly, hospitalised patient with recent antibiotic exposure, presenting with new-onset watery diarrhoea and abdominal pain, concerning for _Clostridioides difficile_ infection.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Hospital Acquired Infections"][/sc]
  • Community-acquired infection - distinguishing feature is onset outside of healthcare setting or within 48 hours of admission.
  • Drug-induced fever - absence of other infection signs, resolution upon drug withdrawal.
  • Post-operative inflammatory response - typically resolves within 48-72 hours, without progressive signs of infection.
  • Other causes of diarrhoea (e.g., viral gastroenteritis, inflammatory bowel disease) - absence of _C. difficile_ toxin, different clinical course.
  • [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Colonisation vs Infection: what is the difference and why does it matter clinically?" text="Colonisation with multidrug-resistant organisms (e.g., MRSA, VRE)"][/sc] - presence of organism without clinical signs of infection.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Hospital Acquired Infections"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Prompt recognition and escalation to senior medical staff, supportive care (fluid resuscitation, oxygen), and early initiation of empiric broad-spectrum antibiotics guided by local antimicrobial guidelines.
  • Definitive management: Targeted antimicrobial therapy based on culture and sensitivity results, source control (e.g., surgical debridement of infected tissue, removal of infected devices), and adherence to infection prevention and control measures.
  • Infection prevention and control: Strict adherence to hand hygiene, appropriate use of personal protective equipment, environmental cleaning, and isolation precautions for patients with multidrug-resistant organisms.
  • Exam pharmacology reference (UKMLA/MRCGP): For CDI, oral [sc name="jitl-medication" Q="Fidaxomicin use in Clostridioides difficile infection" text="fidaxomicin"][/sc] or [sc name="jitl-medication" Q="Vancomycin use in Clostridioides difficile infection" text="vancomycin"][/sc] is often considered. For MRSA, treatment may involve [sc name="jitl-medication" Q="Vancomycin use in MRSA infection" text="vancomycin"][/sc], [sc name="jitl-medication" Q="Teicoplanin use in MRSA infection" text="teicoplanin"][/sc], or [sc name="jitl-medication" Q="Linezolid use in MRSA infection" text="linezolid"][/sc]. For VRE, options may include [sc name="jitl-medication" Q="Linezolid use in VRE infection" text="linezolid"][/sc] or [sc name="jitl-medication" Q="Daptomycin use in VRE infection" text="daptomycin"][/sc]. Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Hospital Acquired Infections"][/sc]
  • Sepsis and septic shock: Uncontrolled systemic inflammatory response to infection, leading to widespread organ dysfunction and high mortality, often developing within days of infection onset.
  • [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Carbapenemase-producing organisms: what are they and why do they matter clinically?" text="Carbapenemase-producing organism (CPO) infection"][/sc]: Infection with bacteria resistant to carbapenem antibiotics, posing a significant treatment challenge and risk of outbreaks.
  • Recurrent _Clostridioides difficile_ infection (CDI): Relapse of CDI symptoms after initial treatment, common within weeks to months, often requiring further courses of antibiotics or faecal microbiota transplantation.
  • Organ dysfunction/failure: Direct damage to organs (e.g., acute kidney injury, acute respiratory distress syndrome) due to severe infection or sepsis, potentially leading to long-term disability.
  • Increased length of hospital stay and healthcare costs: HAIs significantly prolong recovery and increase the financial burden on healthcare systems.
