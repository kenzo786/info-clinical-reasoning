Termination of pregnancy [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Termination of pregnancy"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Termination of pregnancy"][/sc]
  • A medical procedure to end a pregnancy, resulting in the removal of the foetus and placenta from the uterus.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the indications, methods, and potential complications associated with termination of pregnancy.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Termination of pregnancy"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Termination of pregnancy"][/sc]
  • In the UK, approximately 200,000 terminations of pregnancy are performed annually, with rates varying by age group.
  • Risk factors often include socioeconomic deprivation, lack of access to contraception, and relationship instability.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Termination of pregnancy"][/sc]
  • Trigger: Unwanted pregnancy or medical indications necessitating the termination of pregnancy.
  • Progression: Medical methods induce uterine contractions and cervical dilation, or surgical methods involve instrumental evacuation of uterine contents.
  • Consequence: Expulsion or removal of the foetus and placental tissue, leading to the cessation of pregnancy.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Termination of pregnancy"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Decision-making and initial consultation, typically within the first trimester.
  • Progressive phase: Administration of medical agents or surgical procedure, often completed within hours to a few days.
  • Late phase: Recovery and follow-up, particularly beyond 2-4 weeks post-procedure.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Termination of pregnancy"][/sc]
  • Ask about vaginal bleeding - to assess for complications like haemorrhage or retained products of conception.
  • Ask about abdominal pain - to differentiate between expected cramping and signs of infection or uterine injury.
  • Ask about fever or chills - to identify potential infection.
  • Ask about persistent pregnancy symptoms - to evaluate for incomplete termination.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Termination of pregnancy"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - to identify signs of infection (fever, tachycardia) or haemorrhage (hypotension).
  • Check abdominal tenderness - to assess for uterine perforation or infection.
  • Check vaginal discharge - to note colour, odour, and amount, indicating infection or retained products.
  • Check cervical os patency - to evaluate for retained products of conception.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Termination of pregnancy"][/sc]
  • Heavy vaginal bleeding - indicative of haemorrhage requiring immediate intervention.
  • Severe abdominal pain - may suggest uterine perforation or ectopic pregnancy.
  • Fever with offensive vaginal discharge - highly suggestive of endometritis or pelvic infection.
  • Persistent positive pregnancy test beyond expected timeframe - indicates incomplete termination or ongoing pregnancy.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Termination of pregnancy"][/sc]
  • Older adults: May present with less typical pain patterns or delayed recognition of symptoms due to co-morbidities.
  • Immunosuppressed: Increased risk of severe infection with attenuated inflammatory response, making diagnosis challenging.
  • Psychological distress: Can manifest as somatic symptoms, potentially masking physical complications.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Termination of pregnancy"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected complications: Full blood count (FBC) to assess for anaemia or infection, C-reactive protein (CRP) for inflammation.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Urine dipstick for urinary tract infection, vaginal swabs for sexually transmitted infections.
  • Imaging - Transvaginal ultrasound: To assess for retained products of conception, uterine perforation, or ectopic pregnancy.
  • Serial reassessment: Quantitative beta-hCG levels to confirm resolution of pregnancy.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Termination of pregnancy"][/sc]
  • A 28-year-old female presents two weeks post-medical termination of pregnancy with persistent heavy vaginal bleeding, lower abdominal pain, and a low-grade fever.
  • Young female, post-procedure, persistent symptoms, systemic inflammatory response.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Termination of pregnancy"][/sc]
  • Incomplete termination - persistent pregnancy symptoms and retained products of conception.
  • Endometritis - fever, offensive vaginal discharge, uterine tenderness.
  • Haemorrhage - excessive vaginal bleeding, signs of hypovolaemia.
  • Uterine perforation - severe, sudden abdominal pain, peritonism.
  • Ectopic pregnancy (undiagnosed) - persistent positive pregnancy test, abdominal pain, often unilateral.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Termination of pregnancy"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Assess haemodynamic stability, manage pain, consider anti-emetics if indicated.
  • Definitive management: May involve expectant management, medical treatment (e.g., uterotonics), or surgical evacuation of retained products of conception.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Clinical review, serial beta-hCG, contraception counselling.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Advise on signs of worsening infection or haemorrhage, provide contact details for urgent review.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Termination of pregnancy"][/sc]
  • Haemorrhage: Due to uterine atony or retained products, leading to significant blood loss and potential hypovolaemic shock.
  • Infection (Endometritis): Ascending infection from vaginal flora, causing fever, pain, and potentially sepsis if untreated.
  • Incomplete termination: Retention of foetal or placental tissue, resulting in persistent bleeding and pain, requiring further intervention.
  • Uterine perforation: Rare but serious complication during surgical procedures, potentially leading to intra-abdominal injury and peritonitis.
  • Psychological distress: Can include anxiety, depression, or post-traumatic stress, impacting long-term well-being.
