Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Autism spectrum disorder (ASD)"][/sc]
  • Autism spectrum disorder (ASD) is a lifelong neurodevelopmental condition characterised by persistent difficulties in social communication and social interaction, alongside restricted, repetitive patterns of behaviour, interests, or activities.
  • **UKMLA learning outcome:** Recognise the key features and diagnostic criteria for autism spectrum disorder, understanding its neurodevelopmental basis and varied presentations across the lifespan presentation.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Autism spectrum disorder (ASD)"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Autism spectrum disorder (ASD)"][/sc]
  • In the UK, the estimated prevalence of autism spectrum disorder in adults is approximately 1.1% [1]. It is estimated that around 700,000 people in the UK have a diagnosis of autism, with about 1 in 100 children receiving a diagnosis [2].
  • Risk factors include a significant genetic predisposition, with studies indicating that genetic factors are largely responsible for both autism spectrum disorder and milder autistic traits [3]. Other associated factors include advanced parental age and certain prenatal, perinatal, and neonatal environmental factors [4].
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Autism spectrum disorder (ASD)"][/sc]
  • **Trigger:** Autism spectrum disorder is primarily influenced by a complex interplay of genetic predispositions, epigenetic modifications, and environmental factors, leading to atypical neurodevelopment from early life [12, 14].
  • **Progression:** This atypical development manifests as alterations in brain structure and function, including differences in neural connectivity, synaptic pruning, and disrupted signaling pathways, particularly affecting areas involved in social cognition and communication [1, 3, 4, 7]. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Neural connectivity in ASD: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Neural connectivity in ASD"][/sc]
  • **Consequence:** These underlying neurobiological differences result in the characteristic behavioural and cognitive profiles of ASD, including persistent challenges in social interaction and communication, and the presence of restricted, repetitive patterns of behaviours, interests, or activities [1, 8].
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Autism spectrum disorder (ASD)"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Manifestations of autism spectrum disorder typically emerge in early childhood, often becoming noticeable before 3 years of age. Initial signs may include reduced eye contact, lack of response to one\\'s name, or limited social babbling, particularly beyond 12-18 months of age [1, 2, 3].
  • Progressive phase: The presentation of ASD can evolve throughout childhood and adolescence, with symptom severity and expression changing over time. Diagnostic recognition may occur at various stages, from early childhood into adulthood, as individuals navigate increasing social and developmental demands [4, 5].
  • Late phase: Autism spectrum disorder is a lifelong neurodevelopmental condition. While adaptive strategies and support can significantly improve functioning, core challenges in social communication and restricted behaviours generally persist throughout adulthood, particularly beyond early intervention periods [1, 6].
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Autism spectrum disorder (ASD)"][/sc]
  • Ask about difficulties with social communication and interaction – this is central to diagnosis, encompassing challenges in reciprocal conversation, sharing interests, and understanding non-verbal cues [1, 2].
  • Ask about restricted, repetitive patterns of behaviour, interests, or activities – these can include adherence to routines, highly circumscribed interests, or repetitive motor movements, which are key diagnostic features [1, 3].
  • Ask about sensory sensitivities – individuals with ASD may experience hyper- or hypo-sensitivity to sensory input (e.g., sounds, lights, textures), which can significantly impact daily functioning and behaviour [4].
  • Ask about anxiety or distress in social situations – this is a common co-occurring feature, often stemming from difficulties navigating social complexities and understanding social expectations [5].
  • Ask about challenges in understanding others\\' perspectives or emotions (theory of mind) – this impacts empathy and social reasoning, contributing to difficulties in social relationships [1].
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Autism spectrum disorder (ASD)"][/sc]
  • Check non-verbal communication – limited or inconsistent eye contact, atypical facial expressions, or reduced use of gestures may imply difficulties in social engagement and understanding social cues [1, 2]. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Atypical non-verbal communication in ASD: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Atypical non-verbal communication"][/sc]
  • Check for repetitive behaviours – observations of hand-flapping, rocking, spinning, or an intense, circumscribed focus on specific objects or routines suggest restricted, repetitive patterns of behaviour, a core diagnostic feature [3, 4].
  • Check sensory responses – unusual reactions to sounds, lights, textures, or smells (e.g., extreme distress or fascination) indicate sensory processing differences that can impact daily functioning [5].
  • Check social interaction patterns – an apparent disinterest in reciprocal social play, difficulty initiating or maintaining conversations, or challenges in understanding social cues implies significant difficulties in social relatedness [1, 6].
  • Check vital signs – typically within normal limits for age, but may be elevated (e.g., increased heart rate, respiratory rate) if the individual is experiencing significant anxiety, distress, or sensory overload in the clinical setting.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Autism spectrum disorder (ASD)"][/sc]
  • Developmental Regression - loss of previously acquired language, social, or motor skills, which warrants urgent investigation as it can indicate a more complex neurodevelopmental trajectory [1, 2].
  • Absence of Joint Attention - lack of pointing to share interest, showing objects, or following another\\'s gaze by 12-18 months, indicating fundamental difficulties in social reciprocity [3, 4].
  • Limited or Absent Eye Contact - persistent avoidance of eye contact or inconsistent eye gaze, particularly in social interactions, which is a key indicator of atypical social engagement [5, 6].
  • Significant Speech Delay - no single words by 16 months or no two-word spontaneous phrases by 24 months, suggesting a need for early communication intervention [7, 8].
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Autism spectrum disorder (ASD)"][/sc]
  • Women: Autistic women often present with less overt social communication difficulties and restricted, repetitive behaviours due to camouflaging or \\\'masking\\\' their autistic traits. This can lead to under-recognition or misdiagnosis, as their presentation may not align with traditionally male-centric diagnostic criteria [1, 2, 3].
  • Older adults: Diagnosis of ASD in older adults can be challenging, as symptoms may be masked by co-occurring psychiatric conditions or attributed to age-related changes. Existing diagnostic tools may not be sensitive to subtle presentations in this population, leading to late diagnosis or misdiagnosis [4, 5]. Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Autism spectrum disorder (ASD)"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected Autism Spectrum Disorder: Diagnosis is primarily clinical, based on detailed developmental history, observation of behaviour, and structured assessments conducted by a multidisciplinary team of autism specialists. Key tools include the Autism Diagnostic Observation Schedule – Generic (ADOS-G) and the Autism Diagnostic Interview – Revised (ADI-R) [1, 2].
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Consider comprehensive assessments for co-occurring conditions such as intellectual disability, specific language disorders, attention-deficit/hyperactivity disorder (ADHD), or other psychiatric conditions, as these can present with overlapping features [3, 4].
  • Imaging - Neuroimaging: Routine neuroimaging (e.g., MRI, CT) is not typically used for the diagnosis of ASD. It may be considered in specific cases to rule out other neurological conditions if indicated by clinical presentation (e.g., seizures, focal neurological deficits).
  • Serial reassessment: Ongoing developmental monitoring and serial reassessments are crucial, particularly in younger children, to observe the persistence and evolution of symptoms and to confirm the diagnosis over time [5].

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Autism spectrum disorder (ASD)"][/sc]
  • Social (Pragmatic) Communication Disorder (SCD) - lacks the restricted, repetitive patterns of behaviour, interests, or activities seen in ASD [1, 2].
  • Attention-Deficit/Hyperactivity Disorder (ADHD) - primary difficulties are with inattention and hyperactivity/impulsivity, rather than fundamental social communication deficits and restricted interests [3, 4].
  • Intellectual Developmental Disorder (IDD) - characterised by deficits in intellectual and adaptive functioning across multiple domains, which may co-occur with ASD but is not its defining feature [5].
  • Anxiety Disorders (e.g., Social Anxiety Disorder) - social difficulties are driven by fear of negative evaluation, whereas in ASD they stem from intrinsic challenges in social understanding and reciprocity [6].
  • Obsessive-Compulsive Disorder (OCD) - repetitive behaviours are typically ego-dystonic and driven by obsessions or compulsions, contrasting with the often ego-syntonic and preferred patterns in ASD [7].
  • Language Disorders - primary difficulties are with language acquisition and use, without the pervasive social communication impairments or restricted behaviours of ASD [8].
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Autism spectrum disorder (ASD)"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Focus on creating a supportive and predictable environment to minimise distress and sensory overload. This may involve adjusting sensory input, providing clear communication, and offering opportunities for self-regulation [1].
  • Definitive management: Management is multidisciplinary and individualised, often involving a combination of behavioural, educational, and psychological interventions. These can include applied behaviour analysis (ABA), speech and language therapy, occupational therapy, and social skills training, aimed at developing communication, social interaction, and adaptive living skills [2, 3].
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Regular monitoring of developmental progress, mental health, and co-occurring conditions is essential. Follow-up should involve ongoing assessment of needs and adjustment of support strategies as the individual develops and their environment changes [4].
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Escalation may be required if there is a significant increase in challenging behaviours, severe mental health concerns, or a crisis situation. Safety-netting involves providing clear guidance on accessing crisis support services and mental health professionals [5].
  • Exam pharmacology reference (UKMLA/MRCGP): Pharmacological interventions are generally considered for managing co-occurring conditions such as anxiety, depression, ADHD, or severe challenging behaviours, rather than for core ASD symptoms. Examples may include selective serotonin reuptake inhibitors (SSRIs) for anxiety or irritability, or atypical antipsychotics for severe aggression or self-injurious behaviour. Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Autism spectrum disorder (ASD)"][/sc]
  • Mental Health Conditions: High prevalence of co-occurring anxiety disorders, depression, and obsessive-compulsive disorder (OCD), which can significantly impact quality of life and may mask underlying ASD traits, making diagnosis and management more complex [1, 2].
  • Attention-Deficit/Hyperactivity Disorder (ADHD): ADHD is a very common comorbidity, affecting a significant proportion of individuals with ASD. The overlapping symptoms can complicate diagnosis and require integrated management approaches [3, 4].
  • Epilepsy: Individuals with ASD have a higher risk of developing epilepsy compared to the general population, with seizures potentially emerging at any age and requiring careful neurological assessment and management [5].
  • Sleep Disorders: Chronic sleep disturbances, including insomnia and irregular sleep-wake cycles, are frequently observed in ASD, contributing to behavioural challenges, daytime fatigue, and impacting overall well-being and cognitive function [6].
  • Gastrointestinal Issues: A higher incidence of gastrointestinal problems, such as chronic constipation, abdominal pain, and reflux, is reported in individuals with ASD, which can contribute to distress and behavioural difficulties if not addressed [7].
