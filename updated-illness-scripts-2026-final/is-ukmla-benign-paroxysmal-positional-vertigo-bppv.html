Benign paroxysmal positional vertigo (BPPV) [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Benign paroxysmal positional vertigo (BPPV)"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Benign paroxysmal positional vertigo (BPPV)"][/sc]
  • Benign paroxysmal positional vertigo (BPPV) is a peripheral vestibular disorder characterised by sudden, brief episodes of vertigo triggered by specific head movements, resulting from the displacement of otoconia into the semicircular canals of the inner ear [1] [2].
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical features of BPPV and understand the principles of diagnosis and non-pharmacological management, including canalith repositioning manoeuvres.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Benign paroxysmal positional vertigo (BPPV)"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Benign paroxysmal positional vertigo (BPPV)"][/sc]
  • BPPV is the most common cause of vertigo, with a lifetime prevalence estimated at 2.4% in the general population, increasing with age [3] [4].
  • The annual incidence is reported to be between 10.7 and 64.0 cases per 100,000 adults, with a higher prevalence in females [3] [4].
  • Risk factors include increasing age, female sex, head trauma, and previous vestibular neuronitis [2].
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Benign paroxysmal positional vertigo (BPPV)"][/sc]
  • Tiny calcium carbonate crystals, known as otoconia, detach from the utricle within the inner ear, often due to age-related degeneration or head trauma [1] [2].
  • These displaced otoconia migrate into one of the semicircular canals, most commonly the posterior canal (canalithiasis), making the canal abnormally sensitive to gravity [1] [2].
  • Movement of the head causes the otoconia to shift within the canal, leading to abnormal fluid displacement and stimulation of the hair cells, which sends conflicting signals to the brain, resulting in vertigo [1] [2]. Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Benign paroxysmal positional vertigo (BPPV)"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Episodes of vertigo are typically brief, lasting less than one minute (often 20-30 seconds), and are triggered by specific head movements [5].
  • Progressive phase: Symptoms may spontaneously resolve within weeks to months as otoconia dissolve or move to an asymptomatic location, but recurrences are common [5].
  • Late phase: In some individuals, symptoms may persist for years or recur frequently, particularly beyond several months, necessitating repeated interventions [5].
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Benign paroxysmal positional vertigo (BPPV)"][/sc]
  • Ask about sudden, brief spinning sensation (vertigo) - this is the hallmark symptom, often described as the room spinning, and helps differentiate from lightheadedness [5].
  • Ask about specific head movements that trigger vertigo - common triggers include getting out of bed, rolling over in bed, or looking upwards, indicating positional dependency [5].
  • Ask about associated nausea or vomiting - nausea is common, but vomiting is rare, providing insight into symptom severity [5].
  • Ask about symptom-free intervals between episodes - patients typically feel well between attacks, which helps distinguish BPPV from other continuous vestibular disorders [5].
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Benign paroxysmal positional vertigo (BPPV)"][/sc]
  • Check for nystagmus during positional testing (e.g., Dix-Hallpike manoeuvre) - a characteristic rotatory nystagmus with a latency of a few seconds, lasting less than 60 seconds, is diagnostic of posterior canal BPPV [2]. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Dix-Hallpike test: how is it performed and what does a positive result indicate?" text="Dix-Hallpike test"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - typically normal, as BPPV is a peripheral vestibular disorder and does not cause systemic instability.
  • Check for other neurological deficits - absence of focal neurological signs helps rule out central causes of vertigo [2].
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Benign paroxysmal positional vertigo (BPPV)"][/sc]
  • Persistent vertigo: Vertigo that is continuous and not episodic, or lasts significantly longer than one minute, may suggest a central cause or other vestibular pathology [5].
  • Associated neurological symptoms: Presence of headache, double vision, weakness, numbness, or difficulty speaking/swallowing indicates potential brainstem or cerebellar involvement [2].
  • New hearing loss or tinnitus: Concurrent onset of hearing changes with vertigo may point towards conditions like Meniere\'s disease or acoustic neuroma [5].
  • Atypical nystagmus: Purely vertical or horizontal nystagmus, or nystagmus that changes direction, suggests a central lesion rather than BPPV [2].
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Benign paroxysmal positional vertigo (BPPV)"][/sc]
  • Older adults: May present with less pronounced vertigo but more significant unsteadiness and increased risk of falls, impacting functional independence [6].
  • Horizontal canal BPPV: Characterised by horizontal nystagmus during supine head roll test, with vertigo triggered by rolling the head to either side, requiring different repositioning manoeuvres [2]. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Horizontal canal BPPV: how does it differ from posterior canal BPPV?" text="Horizontal canal BPPV"][/sc]
  • Anterior canal BPPV: Less common, typically presents with downbeating nystagmus during Dix-Hallpike, and may require specific manoeuvres like the deep head-hanging manoeuvre [2].
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Benign paroxysmal positional vertigo (BPPV)"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected BPPV: Dix-Hallpike manoeuvre is the primary diagnostic test for posterior and anterior canal BPPV, eliciting characteristic vertigo and nystagmus [2].
  • Core for suspected BPPV: Supine head roll test (Pagnini-McClure manoeuvre) is used to diagnose horizontal canal BPPV, observing geotropic or apogeotropic nystagmus [2]. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Supine head roll test: how is it performed and what does a positive result indicate?" text="Supine head roll test"][/sc]
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Audiometry may be considered if hearing loss is a prominent symptom, to rule out other inner ear pathologies [2].
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Brain MRI may be indicated if red flag symptoms suggest a central neurological cause, such as stroke or tumour [2].

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Benign paroxysmal positional vertigo (BPPV)"][/sc]
  • Vestibular migraine - often associated with headache, photophobia, or phonophobia, and vertigo episodes may last longer and be less strictly positional than BPPV [5].
  • Vestibular neuronitis/labyrinthitis - typically presents with acute, continuous vertigo lasting days, often preceded by a viral illness, and not specifically triggered by head position [5].
  • Meniere\'s disease - characterised by recurrent episodes of vertigo, fluctuating sensorineural hearing loss, tinnitus, and aural fullness, with vertigo lasting hours [5].
  • Orthostatic hypotension - causes lightheadedness or dizziness upon standing, but typically not a spinning sensation, and resolves quickly upon sitting or lying down [2].
  • Posterior circulation stroke/TIA - may present with vertigo but usually accompanied by other focal neurological deficits (e.g., diplopia, ataxia, dysarthria), and vertigo is often continuous or prolonged [2]. Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Benign paroxysmal positional vertigo (BPPV)"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Recognition of BPPV and differentiation from central causes of vertigo is crucial; supportive care may include antiemetics for severe nausea.
  • Definitive management: Canalith repositioning manoeuvres, such as the Epley manoeuvre for posterior canal BPPV, are highly effective in relocating displaced otoconia back to the utricle [2]. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Canalith repositioning manoeuvres: what are the principles and common types?" text="Canalith repositioning manoeuvres"][/sc]
  • Definitive management: The Semont manoeuvre or Brandt-Daroff exercises may be considered as alternatives or for home management [5].
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Patients should be advised on potential recurrence and instructed on self-administered manoeuvres or when to seek further medical advice if symptoms persist or worsen.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Referral to an ENT specialist or neurologist is warranted if diagnosis is uncertain, if atypical features are present, or if symptoms are refractory to repositioning manoeuvres [5].
  • Exam pharmacology reference (UKMLA/MRCGP): No specific drug knowledge is commonly tested for BPPV as management is primarily non-pharmacological. No doses or frequencies are cited here — always verify against your local antimicrobial/prescribing formulary before prescribing.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Benign paroxysmal positional vertigo (BPPV)"][/sc]
  • Recurrence: BPPV has a high recurrence rate, with approximately 50% of patients experiencing another episode within 5 years, which can significantly impact quality of life [5].
  • Falls and injury: Particularly in older adults, recurrent vertigo and unsteadiness can lead to an increased risk of falls and associated injuries [6].
  • Anxiety and depression: The unpredictable nature of vertigo episodes can cause significant anxiety, fear of movement, and social isolation, potentially leading to depression [5].
  • Functional impairment: Persistent or recurrent symptoms can interfere with daily activities, driving, and work, leading to reduced functional capacity [5].
