Bursitis [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Bursitis"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Bursitis"][/sc]
  • Inflammation of a bursa, a fluid-filled sac that cushions bones, tendons, and muscles near joints, leading to pain and restricted movement.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the common presentations, risk factors, and initial management of bursitis, distinguishing it from other musculoskeletal conditions.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Bursitis"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Bursitis"][/sc]
  • Common in the UK, particularly affecting individuals involved in repetitive activities or those with underlying inflammatory conditions; prevalence estimates vary by specific bursa affected.
  • Risk factors include repetitive motion, prolonged pressure on joints, trauma, infection, and systemic inflammatory diseases such as rheumatoid arthritis or gout.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Bursitis"][/sc]
  • Repetitive friction, pressure, or acute trauma to a bursa triggers an inflammatory response within the synovial lining.
  • This leads to increased fluid production, thickening of the bursal wall, and accumulation of inflammatory mediators, causing the bursa to swell and become painful.
  • Consequence is localised pain, tenderness, and restricted range of motion in the adjacent joint, potentially leading to chronic pain or functional impairment if untreated.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Bursitis"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Acute onset of localised pain and tenderness, often developing over hours to a few days following an inciting event or repetitive activity.
  • Progressive phase: Symptoms may worsen over several days to weeks, particularly beyond 2-3 weeks, with increasing pain, swelling, and limitation of movement if the causative factors persist or inflammation is not managed.
  • Late phase: Chronic bursitis can develop if inflammation is prolonged, leading to persistent pain, bursal thickening, and potential calcification, particularly beyond 6-8 weeks.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Bursitis"][/sc]
  • Ask about localised pain - often sharp or aching, exacerbated by movement or pressure over the affected bursa, indicating inflammation.
  • Ask about swelling or a palpable lump - suggests fluid accumulation within the bursa, particularly in superficial bursae.
  • Ask about restricted movement - pain often limits the range of motion of the adjacent joint, differentiating it from tendonitis where movement might be painful but less restricted.
  • Ask about history of repetitive activity or trauma - provides clues to the aetiology of the bursitis.
  • Ask about fever or redness - may indicate septic bursitis, a medical emergency.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Bursitis"][/sc]
  • Check localised tenderness - elicited by palpation directly over the affected bursa, a key diagnostic feature.
  • Check swelling and erythema - visible signs of inflammation, particularly prominent in superficial bursae like olecranon or prepatellar.
  • Check pain on resisted movement - may be present if the bursa is compressed during muscle contraction, but typically less pronounced than in tendonitis.
  • Check vital signs - fever, tachycardia, or hypotension may suggest septic bursitis, requiring urgent investigation.
  • Check range of motion - often limited by pain, both actively and passively, in the adjacent joint.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Bursitis"][/sc]
  • Systemic signs of infection - fever, chills, malaise, or spreading cellulitis around the bursa indicate potential septic bursitis, requiring urgent aspiration and antibiotics.
  • Rapidly worsening pain and swelling - suggests an acute inflammatory process, possibly infectious or crystalline, needing prompt assessment.
  • Significant functional impairment - inability to use the limb or bear weight, which may point to a more severe underlying pathology or complication.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Bursitis"][/sc]
  • Older adults: May present with less pronounced inflammatory signs and more insidious onset of pain, often attributed to degenerative changes, delaying diagnosis.
  • Immunosuppressed patients: Higher risk of septic bursitis, which may present with subtle signs of infection, requiring a lower threshold for aspiration and culture.
  • Deep bursitis: Pain may be less localised and swelling not visible, making diagnosis challenging and often requiring imaging for confirmation.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Bursitis"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected bursitis: Clinical assessment is usually sufficient for diagnosis, especially for superficial bursitis.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Consider blood tests such as full blood count, C-reactive protein, and erythrocyte sedimentation rate to assess for systemic inflammation or infection. Uric acid levels may be checked if gout is suspected.
  • Imaging - Ultrasound: Useful for confirming bursal distension, assessing fluid characteristics, and guiding aspiration, particularly for deep bursae or when diagnosis is uncertain.
  • Imaging - X-ray: Primarily to rule out bony pathology or calcification within the bursa, not for diagnosing bursitis itself.
  • Bursal aspiration: Indicated if septic bursitis is suspected (fever, erythema, rapidly worsening pain) to obtain fluid for cell count, Gram stain, culture, and crystal analysis.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Bursitis"][/sc]
  • A 55-year-old male presenting with a 3-week history of localised pain and swelling over his right elbow, exacerbated by leaning on the arm, with no systemic symptoms.
  • A middle-aged man with acute, localised, tender swelling over a superficial joint, worsened by pressure and movement, suggesting an inflammatory process within a bursa.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Bursitis"][/sc]
  • Cellulitis - more diffuse redness and warmth, less localised tenderness, often with a portal of entry for infection.
  • Tendonitis - pain typically worse with specific resisted movements, tenderness along the tendon, less prominent swelling directly over a bursa.
  • Arthritis (e.g., gout, rheumatoid arthritis) - involves the joint capsule, pain on joint movement in all directions, effusions within the joint, systemic features.
  • Soft tissue tumour - usually a slower growing, non-tender mass, though can mimic bursitis if inflamed.
  • Fracture - acute trauma, severe pain, bony tenderness, often with deformity or crepitus.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Bursitis"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Rest, ice, compression, and elevation (RICE) for acute inflammatory bursitis. Analgesia with paracetamol or NSAIDs.
  • Definitive management: Address underlying causes such as repetitive activities. Aspiration of bursal fluid for symptomatic relief and diagnostic purposes (especially if septic). Corticosteroid injection into the bursa may be considered for persistent inflammatory bursitis.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Review symptoms and signs for improvement. Monitor for recurrence or development of complications.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Advise patients to seek urgent medical attention for worsening pain, fever, spreading redness, or inability to move the affected limb, as these may indicate septic bursitis or other serious complications.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Bursitis"][/sc]
  • Chronic pain: Persistent inflammation and irritation can lead to ongoing discomfort and functional limitation, particularly if causative factors are not removed.
  • Recurrent bursitis: Without addressing the underlying cause, bursitis can frequently recur, leading to repeated episodes of pain and swelling.
  • Septic bursitis: Bacterial infection of the bursa, often from skin breaks, leading to severe pain, redness, warmth, and systemic signs of infection, requiring urgent treatment to prevent spread.
  • Bursal calcification: Long-standing inflammation can lead to calcium deposits within the bursa, which may cause chronic pain and stiffness.
