Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Tachyarrhythmias (including atrial fibrillation, atrial flutter, supraventricular tachycardia, torsades de pointes, ventricular tachycardia, ventricular fibrillation)"][/sc]
  • A group of cardiac rhythm disturbances characterised by a heart rate greater than 100 beats per minute, originating from either the atria or ventricles, leading to impaired cardiac function.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the common presentations and initial management of various tachyarrhythmias, including atrial fibrillation, atrial flutter, supraventricular tachycardia, torsades de pointes, ventricular tachycardia, and ventricular fibrillation.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Tachyarrhythmias (including atrial fibrillation, atrial flutter, supraventricular tachycardia, torsades de pointes, ventricular tachycardia, ventricular fibrillation)"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Tachyarrhythmias (including atrial fibrillation, atrial flutter, supraventricular tachycardia, torsades de pointes, ventricular tachycardia, ventricular fibrillation)"][/sc]
  • Atrial fibrillation is the most common sustained arrhythmia, affecting approximately 1.4 million people in the UK, with prevalence increasing significantly with age (NICE).
  • Risk factors for tachyarrhythmias often include underlying structural heart disease, hypertension, diabetes, obesity, excessive alcohol consumption, and hyperthyroidism.
  • Supraventricular tachycardia (SVT) has an estimated incidence of 35 per 100,000 person-years, often presenting in younger individuals without structural heart disease (CKS).
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Tachyarrhythmias (including atrial fibrillation, atrial flutter, supraventricular tachycardia, torsades de pointes, ventricular tachycardia, ventricular fibrillation)"][/sc]
  • Abnormal electrical impulse generation or conduction within the heart, often triggered by re-entrant circuits, enhanced automaticity, or triggered activity.
  • This leads to rapid and disorganised atrial or ventricular activation, impairing the heart's ability to fill and eject blood efficiently.
  • Consequence is reduced cardiac output, potentially causing symptoms such as palpitations, dizziness, syncope, and in severe cases, cardiogenic shock or sudden cardiac death.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Tachyarrhythmias (including atrial fibrillation, atrial flutter, supraventricular tachycardia, torsades de pointes, ventricular tachycardia, ventricular fibrillation)"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Acute onset of palpitations, dizziness, or chest discomfort, often within minutes to hours of the arrhythmia beginning.
  • Progressive phase: If sustained, symptoms may worsen with increasing fatigue, dyspnoea, and potential for decompensation, particularly beyond 24-48 hours for conditions like atrial fibrillation.
  • Late phase: Chronic or recurrent tachyarrhythmias can lead to structural cardiac remodelling, heart failure, and increased risk of thromboembolic events, particularly beyond several weeks or months.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Tachyarrhythmias (including atrial fibrillation, atrial flutter, supraventricular tachycardia, torsades de pointes, ventricular tachycardia, ventricular fibrillation)"][/sc]
  • Ask about palpitations - to understand the patient's perception of heart rate and rhythm, which can indicate the type of arrhythmia.
  • Ask about dizziness or light-headedness - to assess for reduced cerebral perfusion due to decreased cardiac output.
  • Ask about chest pain or discomfort - to evaluate for myocardial ischaemia, especially in the context of rapid heart rates.
  • Ask about shortness of breath (dyspnoea) - to identify signs of pulmonary congestion or heart failure due to impaired cardiac function.
  • Ask about syncope or pre-syncope - to determine if there has been transient loss of consciousness or near-fainting episodes, indicating severe haemodynamic compromise.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Tachyarrhythmias (including atrial fibrillation, atrial flutter, supraventricular tachycardia, torsades de pointes, ventricular tachycardia, ventricular fibrillation)"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - a heart rate >100 bpm is diagnostic, and hypotension, tachypnoea, or reduced oxygen saturation indicate haemodynamic instability.
  • Check pulse - to assess rate, rhythm (regularly irregular, irregularly irregular), and volume, which can help differentiate arrhythmias.
  • Check jugular venous pressure (JVP) - to look for cannon A waves (e.g., in ventricular tachycardia) or elevated JVP indicating right heart failure.
  • Check heart sounds - to identify murmurs, gallop rhythms, or variable intensity of S1 (e.g., in complete heart block with ventricular escape rhythm).
  • Check peripheral perfusion - cool peripheries, prolonged capillary refill time, or reduced urine output suggest hypoperfusion and shock.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Tachyarrhythmias (including atrial fibrillation, atrial flutter, supraventricular tachycardia, torsades de pointes, ventricular tachycardia, ventricular fibrillation)"][/sc]
  • Haemodynamic instability - hypotension, signs of shock, or acute heart failure indicate an urgent need for cardioversion.
  • Syncope or near-syncope - suggests significant cerebral hypoperfusion and a high risk of adverse events.
  • Ongoing chest pain suggestive of ischaemia - rapid heart rates can precipitate or worsen myocardial ischaemia, requiring immediate intervention.
  • Ventricular fibrillation or pulseless ventricular tachycardia - these are cardiac arrest rhythms requiring immediate defibrillation.
  • Broad complex tachycardia with haemodynamic compromise - highly suspicious for ventricular tachycardia, which can rapidly deteriorate.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Tachyarrhythmias (including atrial fibrillation, atrial flutter, supraventricular tachycardia, torsades de pointes, ventricular tachycardia, ventricular fibrillation)"][/sc]
  • Older adults: May present with non-specific symptoms like fatigue, confusion, or falls rather than classic palpitations, making diagnosis challenging.
  • Patients with pre-existing heart failure: Tachyarrhythmias can cause acute decompensation with worsening dyspnoea and oedema, even with relatively modest heart rate increases.
  • Post-operative patients: Tachyarrhythmias, particularly atrial fibrillation, are common after cardiac or major non-cardiac surgery, often triggered by inflammation or sympathetic activation.
  • Patients on certain medications: Drugs like QT-prolonging agents can predispose to torsades de pointes, which may present as recurrent syncope.
