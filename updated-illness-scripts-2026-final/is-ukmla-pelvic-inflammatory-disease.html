Pelvic inflammatory disease (Gynaecological tract) [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Pelvic inflammatory disease (Gynaecological tract)"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Pelvic inflammatory disease (Gynaecological tract)"][/sc]
  • Pelvic inflammatory disease (PID) is an inflammatory continuum of the upper female genital tract, including any combination of endometritis, salpingitis, oophoritis, and tubo-ovarian abscess, most commonly caused by ascending infection from the cervix.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the features of pelvic inflammatory disease in a sexually active young woman, arrange appropriate diagnostic tests, and initiate empirical antibiotic treatment according to national guidelines while considering the possibility of ectopic pregnancy.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Pelvic inflammatory disease (Gynaecological tract)"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Pelvic inflammatory disease (Gynaecological tract)"][/sc]
  • The highest rates of PID in the UK are in young, sexually active women, particularly those aged 15-24.
  • Chlamydia trachomatis and Neisseria gonorrhoeae are the most common causative organisms, accounting for up to 35% of cases.
  • Risk factors include multiple sexual partners, previous STIs, intrauterine device (IUD) insertion within the last 3-6 weeks, and previous PID.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Pelvic inflammatory disease (Gynaecological tract)"][/sc]
  • Trigger: Infection ascends from the lower genital tract (cervix and vagina) into the normally sterile upper genital tract, often due to a breakdown of the cervical mucus barrier.
  • Progression: The infection spreads through the endometrium (endometritis) to the fallopian tubes (salpingitis), ovaries (oophoritis), and surrounding pelvic structures, causing inflammation and oedema.
  • Consequence: Tubal inflammation and damage can lead to scarring, adhesions, and obstruction, increasing the risk of infertility, ectopic pregnancy, and chronic pelvic pain. In severe cases, a tubo-ovarian abscess may form.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Pelvic inflammatory disease (Gynaecological tract)"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Within days of infection, patients may experience subtle or non-specific symptoms like lower abdominal ache, deep dyspareunia, or abnormal vaginal discharge.
  • Progressive phase: Particularly beyond 7-10 days, symptoms typically worsen with more pronounced bilateral lower abdominal pain, fever, and constitutional symptoms as the infection spreads through the upper genital tract.
  • Late phase: Beyond several weeks or with recurrent episodes, complications such as chronic pelvic pain, infertility, or ectopic pregnancy may manifest due to tubal scarring and adhesions.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Pelvic inflammatory disease (Gynaecological tract)"][/sc]
  • Ask about bilateral lower abdominal pain - this is the cardinal symptom, though its absence does not exclude PID.
  • Ask about abnormal vaginal discharge (purulent or abnormal colour/odour) - suggests underlying cervicitis or endometritis.
  • Ask about deep dyspareunia or dyspareunia - indicates inflammation and irritation of pelvic structures.
  • Ask about intermenstrual or post-coital bleeding - suggests endometrial inflammation and friability.
  • Ask about systemic symptoms like fever, malaise, and nausea - suggests a more severe, systemic inflammatory response.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Pelvic inflammatory disease (Gynaecological tract)"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs for fever and tachycardia - indicates a systemic inflammatory response and potential sepsis.
  • Check for lower abdominal tenderness on palpation, which may be bilateral - localises the inflammatory process to the pelvis.
  • Check for cervical motion tenderness ([sc name="jitl-query" Q="Chandelier sign: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Chandelier sign"][/sc]) on bimanual examination - a classic sign of pelvic peritonitis.
  • Check for adnexal tenderness on bimanual examination - suggests salpingitis or oophoritis.
  • Check for purulent discharge from the cervical os on speculum examination - direct evidence of endocervical infection.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Pelvic inflammatory disease (Gynaecological tract)"][/sc]
  • Severe systemic illness (high fever, tachycardia, hypotension) - may indicate sepsis or a tubo-ovarian abscess requiring urgent inpatient management.
  • Palpable adnexal mass - raises suspicion of a tubo-ovarian abscess, which carries a risk of rupture and peritonitis.
  • Right upper quadrant pain - suggests perihepatitis ([sc name="jitl-query" Q="Fitz-Hugh-Curtis syndrome: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Fitz-Hugh-Curtis syndrome"][/sc]), a complication of PID requiring specific consideration.
  • Suspicion of ectopic pregnancy (e.g., amenorrhoea, unilateral pain, haemodynamic instability) - a life-threatening emergency that must be excluded in all women of childbearing age with pelvic pain.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Pelvic inflammatory disease (Gynaecological tract)"][/sc]
  • Post-instrumentation: Women who have recently had an IUD inserted or other uterine instrumentation may present with PID, sometimes with less prominent sexual history risk factors.
  • Pregnancy: PID in pregnancy is rare but serious, often presenting with more subtle signs; a high index of suspicion is required as it increases the risk of maternal and foetal complications.
  • Immunosuppressed patients: May have a blunted inflammatory response, presenting with less pain or fever, potentially delaying diagnosis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Pelvic inflammatory disease (Gynaecological tract)"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected PID: A pregnancy test is mandatory to exclude ectopic pregnancy. High vaginal and endocervical swabs (NAAT) for Chlamydia trachomatis and Neisseria gonorrhoeae. Inflammatory markers (CRP, ESR) are often elevated but non-specific.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Urinalysis and culture to exclude urinary tract infection.
  • Imaging - Pelvic ultrasound: May be useful to exclude other pathologies or identify features of PID such as thickened, fluid-filled fallopian tubes or a tubo-ovarian abscess.
  • Serial reassessment: Clinical review within 72 hours is crucial to assess response to empirical treatment and confirm the diagnosis.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Pelvic inflammatory disease (Gynaecological tract)"][/sc]
  • A 22-year-old woman presents with a 5-day history of bilateral lower abdominal pain, abnormal vaginal discharge, and fever, found to have cervical motion and adnexal tenderness on examination.
  • This is a presentation of a young, sexually active woman with symptoms and signs of an ascending pelvic infection, concerning for pelvic inflammatory disease, with the key immediate differential being ectopic pregnancy.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Pelvic inflammatory disease (Gynaecological tract)"][/sc]
  • Ectopic pregnancy - distinguished by a positive pregnancy test and specific ultrasound findings.
  • Appendicitis - pain is typically migratory, starting centrally and localising to the right iliac fossa, often with more prominent gastrointestinal symptoms.
  • Urinary tract infection / Pyelonephritis - distinguished by urinary symptoms (dysuria, frequency) and a positive urine dipstick/culture.
  • Ovarian cyst accident (torsion or haemorrhage) - pain is often sudden, severe, and unilateral.
  • Endometriosis - pain is typically cyclical and chronic, though an acute flare can mimic PID.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Pelvic inflammatory disease (Gynaecological tract)"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Recognition of the clinical syndrome, exclusion of surgical emergencies like ectopic pregnancy, and prompt initiation of broad-spectrum empirical antibiotics as per national guidelines (e.g., BASHH).
  • Definitive management: A course of antibiotics covering the likely pathogens (Chlamydia, Gonorrhoea, and anaerobes) is the mainstay of treatment. The choice of regimen depends on local guidelines and severity.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Patients should be reviewed at 72 hours to ensure clinical improvement. Contact tracing and sexual health screening for partners are essential.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Advise patients to seek urgent review if symptoms worsen, they develop severe pain, or are unable to tolerate oral antibiotics. Low threshold for admission in severe cases, pregnancy, or if a tubo-ovarian abscess is suspected.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Pelvic inflammatory disease (Gynaecological tract)"][/sc]
  • Tubo-ovarian abscess: A collection of pus in the adnexa, which can lead to sepsis and may require surgical drainage if it ruptures or fails to respond to antibiotics.
  • Infertility: Occurs in up to 10-15% of women after one episode of PID due to tubal scarring and obstruction, impairing ovum transport.
  • Ectopic pregnancy: The risk is increased up to 10-fold due to damage to the fallopian tube epithelium, which impedes the transit of a fertilised egg to the uterus.
  • Chronic pelvic pain: Persistent pain lasting more than 6 months can result from pelvic adhesions and chronic inflammation.
  • Perihepatitis ([sc name="jitl-query" Q="Fitz-Hugh-Curtis syndrome: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Fitz-Hugh-Curtis syndrome"][/sc]): Inflammation of the liver capsule and peritoneum, causing right upper quadrant pain due to the spread of infection within the peritoneal cavity.
