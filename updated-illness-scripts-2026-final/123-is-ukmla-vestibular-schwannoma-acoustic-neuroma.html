Vestibular schwannoma (acoustic neuroma) [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Vestibular schwannoma (acoustic neuroma)"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Vestibular schwannoma (acoustic neuroma)"][/sc]
  • A vestibular schwannoma (acoustic neuroma) is a benign tumour arising from the Schwann cells of the vestibulocochlear nerve (cranial nerve VIII), which connects the inner ear to the brain [1].
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise vestibular schwannoma as a non-cancerous tumour of the vestibulocochlear nerve, understanding its impact on hearing and balance.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Vestibular schwannoma (acoustic neuroma)"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Vestibular schwannoma (acoustic neuroma)"][/sc]
  • The estimated incidence of vestibular schwannoma in the UK ranges from 14 to 20 cases per million per year [3], with approximately 1200 new diagnoses annually [5].
  • The risk of developing vestibular schwannoma increases with age, with most diagnoses occurring between 30 and 60 years [4].
  • The only confirmed genetic risk factor is Neurofibromatosis type 2 (NF2), an autosomal dominant condition [1]. Other potential risk factors include noise exposure and ionising radiation [12].
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Vestibular schwannoma (acoustic neuroma)"][/sc]
  • Vestibular schwannomas typically arise from a loss of function of the merlin protein, encoded by the *NF2* gene on chromosome 22, leading to uncontrolled proliferation of Schwann cells on the vestibular portion of the eighth cranial nerve [4, 14].
  • The tumour grows slowly, gradually expanding within the internal auditory canal and cerebellopontine angle, causing progressive compression of the vestibulocochlear nerve [1, 5].
  • Consequent nerve compression results in sensorineural hearing loss, tinnitus, and vestibular dysfunction. Larger tumours may impinge on the brainstem and other cranial nerves, leading to hydrocephalus or facial nerve palsy [6].
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Vestibular schwannoma (acoustic neuroma)"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Vestibular schwannomas are typically slow-growing tumours, often asymptomatic for years. Initial symptoms, such as unilateral hearing loss or tinnitus, develop insidiously and may be subtle [1, 10].
  • Progressive phase: As the tumour enlarges, it progressively compresses the vestibulocochlear nerve and may extend into the cerebellopontine angle. This leads to worsening hearing loss, balance disturbance, and potentially facial numbness or weakness, particularly beyond several months to a year [1, 10].
  • Late phase: In rare cases, very large tumours can compress the brainstem, leading to hydrocephalus, ataxia, and other neurological deficits, which can become life-threatening [1, 6].
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Vestibular schwannoma (acoustic neuroma)"][/sc]
  • Ask about unilateral hearing loss - often insidious and progressive, indicating nerve compression.
  • Ask about tinnitus - ringing or buzzing in the affected ear, a common early symptom.
  • Ask about imbalance or unsteadiness - reflecting vestibular nerve involvement.
  • Ask about dizziness - distinct from vertigo, suggesting vestibular dysfunction.
  • Ask about facial numbness or weakness - indicating potential trigeminal or facial nerve compression by a larger tumour.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Vestibular schwannoma (acoustic neuroma)"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - typically normal, but may reveal signs of increased intracranial pressure in very late stages.
  • Check audiometry - often reveals unilateral sensorineural hearing loss, particularly high-frequency [1, 13].
  • Check cranial nerve examination - specifically for facial asymmetry (CN VII palsy), diminished corneal reflex (CN V), and nystagmus or ataxia (CN VIII, cerebellum) [1, 14].
  • Check Romberg's test and gait assessment - may demonstrate unsteadiness or imbalance, indicating vestibular dysfunction.
  • Check for [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Nystagmus: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="nystagmus"] - an involuntary eye movement that can indicate vestibular pathology.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Vestibular schwannoma (acoustic neuroma)"][/sc]
  • Sudden or rapidly progressive unilateral hearing loss - warrants urgent investigation to rule out a rapidly growing tumour or other acute pathology [1].
  • New onset or worsening facial numbness/weakness/paralysis - suggests significant compression of the trigeminal (CN V) or facial (CN VII) nerves, indicating a larger or strategically located tumour [1, 14].
  • Persistent headaches with nausea/vomiting or visual changes - may indicate signs of increased intracranial pressure due to hydrocephalus caused by brainstem compression [1, 6].
  • Significant ataxia or gait disturbance - points to brainstem or cerebellar involvement, requiring prompt neurosurgical assessment [1].
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Vestibular schwannoma (acoustic neuroma)"][/sc]
  • Sudden sensorineural hearing loss: While typically gradual, a sudden onset of hearing loss can be an atypical presentation, requiring prompt investigation [17].
  • Isolated facial pain or numbness: This may occur due to trigeminal nerve compression, even in the absence of significant auditory or vestibular symptoms [17].
  • Older adults: In this population, symptoms may be attributed to age-related changes, leading to delayed diagnosis. Management often prioritises watchful waiting due to slower tumour growth and surgical risks [22].
  • Isolated lingual nerve paresthesia: A very rare atypical presentation where patients experience numbness or tingling of the tongue [18].
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Vestibular schwannoma (acoustic neuroma)"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected vestibular schwannoma: Audiometry (pure-tone audiogram and speech discrimination) to assess for unilateral sensorineural hearing loss [13].
  • Imaging - MRI with gadolinium contrast: This is the gold standard for diagnosis, capable of detecting tumours as small as 1-2 mm [1, 23, 24].
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Consider further neurological workup based on differential diagnoses.
  • Serial reassessment: For conservatively managed tumours, serial MRI scans are used to monitor tumour growth and assess for changes in symptoms [25].
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Vestibular schwannoma (acoustic neuroma)"][/sc]
  • A 55-year-old female presents with a 6-month history of progressive right-sided hearing loss, associated with constant high-pitched tinnitus and occasional unsteadiness, without vertigo or focal neurological deficits.
  • A middle-aged female with chronic, progressive, unilateral sensorineural hearing loss, ipsilateral tinnitus, and mild disequilibrium.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Vestibular schwannoma (acoustic neuroma)"][/sc]
  • Meniere's disease - characterised by episodic vertigo, fluctuating hearing loss, and aural fullness, unlike the progressive, often unilateral hearing loss of VS [26].
  • Labyrinthitis/Vestibular neuritis - acute onset of severe vertigo, nausea, and vomiting, often preceded by a viral illness, typically without hearing loss in vestibular neuritis [27].
  • Sudden sensorineural hearing loss (idiopathic) - acute onset of hearing loss, often treated with steroids, and requires MRI to rule out VS [17].
  • Trigeminal neuralgia - presents with paroxysmal, severe facial pain, but typically without auditory or vestibular symptoms unless a tumour is also compressing the trigeminal nerve [17].
  • Meningioma - another benign intracranial tumour that can occur in the cerebellopontine angle, mimicking VS, but arises from the meninges rather than the nerve sheath [28].
  • Brainstem glioma - can cause cranial nerve deficits and ataxia, but typically presents with more widespread neurological signs and symptoms, and different imaging characteristics.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Vestibular schwannoma (acoustic neuroma)"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Assess for red flag features (e.g., signs of raised intracranial pressure, rapid neurological deterioration) requiring urgent neurosurgical review.
  • Definitive management:
    • Observation (Wait and Scan): Appropriate for small, asymptomatic, or slowly growing tumours, especially in older patients or those with significant comorbidities. Involves regular MRI surveillance [22, 29].
    • Microsurgical resection: Surgical removal of the tumour, aiming for complete excision while preserving facial nerve function and hearing where possible [30].
    • Stereotactic radiosurgery (SRS)/Fractionated radiotherapy: Uses highly focused radiation to control tumour growth, often preferred for smaller tumours, residual tumours after surgery, or in patients unsuitable for surgery [31, 32].
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Regular clinical assessment and MRI scans are crucial to monitor tumour growth and symptom progression, particularly for those managed conservatively or after treatment [29, 33].
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Patients should be advised to seek urgent medical attention for new or worsening neurological symptoms, severe headaches, or changes in vision/consciousness.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Vestibular schwannoma (acoustic neuroma)"][/sc]
  • Permanent hearing loss: Can result from direct tumour compression of the cochlear nerve or as a complication of surgical intervention, significantly impacting quality of life [1, 34].
  • Facial nerve palsy/paresis: Occurs due to compression or damage to the facial nerve (CN VII) by the growing tumour or during surgery, leading to facial weakness or paralysis and potential eye complications [1, 35].
  • Balance dysfunction: Persistent unsteadiness, dizziness, or vertigo can result from irreversible damage to the vestibular nerve, impacting mobility and increasing fall risk [1, 36].
  • Hydrocephalus: In cases of very large tumours, brainstem compression can obstruct cerebrospinal fluid flow, leading to a build-up of fluid in the brain, causing increased intracranial pressure and requiring urgent intervention [1, 6].
  • Trigeminal nerve dysfunction: Compression of the trigeminal nerve (CN V) can lead to facial numbness or pain, affecting sensation and potentially mastication [17, 37].
  • Cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) leak and meningitis: These are potential post-surgical complications, requiring prompt recognition and management to prevent serious infection [38].
