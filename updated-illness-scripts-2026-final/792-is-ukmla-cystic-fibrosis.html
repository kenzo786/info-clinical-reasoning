Cystic fibrosis [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Cystic fibrosis"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Cystic fibrosis"][/sc]
  • Cystic fibrosis (CF) is a life-limiting, autosomal recessive genetic disorder affecting exocrine glands, leading to the production of thick, sticky mucus that obstructs ducts and passages, primarily in the lungs, pancreas, and gastrointestinal tract.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical features, diagnostic approach, and principles of management for cystic fibrosis, including its multisystemic impact and genetic basis.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Cystic fibrosis"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Cystic fibrosis"][/sc]
  • Cystic fibrosis is the most common life-limiting inherited disease in the UK, affecting approximately 1 in 2,500 live births [1].
  • Around 11,000 people in the UK are living with cystic fibrosis [2].
  • The carrier frequency for the CFTR gene mutation is about 1 in 25 of the Caucasian population [3].
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Cystic fibrosis"][/sc]
  • Trigger: Mutations in the Cystic Fibrosis Transmembrane Conductance Regulator (CFTR) gene, most commonly F508del, lead to a dysfunctional or absent CFTR protein, which is a chloride channel. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="CFTR gene and protein function: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="CFTR gene and protein function"][/sc]
  • Progression: Impaired chloride transport across epithelial cell membranes results in reduced water secretion and increased sodium absorption, leading to dehydrated, thick, and sticky secretions in various organs.
  • Consequence: This abnormal mucus obstructs ducts in the pancreas (pancreatic insufficiency), bile ducts (liver disease), vas deferens (male infertility), and most critically, the airways, leading to chronic infection, inflammation, and progressive bronchiectasis.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Cystic fibrosis"][/sc]
  • Early phase (infancy/childhood): Symptoms often manifest in infancy or early childhood, including meconium ileus in neonates, faltering growth, recurrent respiratory infections, and steatorrhoea. Newborn screening typically identifies CF early, allowing for prompt intervention.
  • Progressive phase (childhood/adolescence): Chronic pulmonary infection, particularly with *Pseudomonas aeruginosa* and *Staphylococcus aureus*, leads to progressive lung damage, bronchiectasis, and declining lung function. Pancreatic insufficiency often necessitates enzyme replacement therapy.
  • Late phase (adulthood): Advanced lung disease, including respiratory failure, becomes the dominant feature. Other complications such as cystic fibrosis-related diabetes, liver disease, and osteoporosis become more prevalent, significantly impacting quality of life and prognosis.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Cystic fibrosis"][/sc]
  • Ask about chronic cough – often productive of thick, purulent sputum, indicating chronic airway inflammation and infection.
  • Ask about recurrent respiratory infections – such as pneumonia or bronchitis, suggesting impaired mucociliary clearance and bacterial colonisation.
  • Ask about steatorrhoea – pale, bulky, foul-smelling stools, indicative of pancreatic exocrine insufficiency and malabsorption.
  • Ask about faltering growth or poor weight gain despite adequate caloric intake – a sign of malabsorption and increased metabolic demands due to chronic illness.
  • Ask about abdominal pain or distension – may suggest distal intestinal obstruction syndrome (DIOS) or constipation.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Cystic fibrosis"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs – may reveal tachypnoea, tachycardia, or hypoxaemia in acute exacerbations or advanced lung disease.
  • Check chest examination – crackles, wheezes, or reduced air entry, consistent with bronchiectasis and airway obstruction.
  • Check digital clubbing – a common sign of chronic hypoxaemia and lung disease.
  • Check nutritional status – low body mass index (BMI) or signs of vitamin deficiencies, reflecting malabsorption and increased energy expenditure.
  • Check abdominal examination – palpable mass in the right lower quadrant may indicate DIOS. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Distal Intestinal Obstruction Syndrome (DIOS): what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="DIOS"][/sc]
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Cystic fibrosis"][/sc]
  • Acute respiratory distress – rapidly worsening dyspnoea, increased work of breathing, or significant desaturation, indicating severe pulmonary exacerbation or respiratory failure, typically requiring immediate intervention.
  • Haemoptysis – coughing up blood, which can range from streaks to massive haemorrhage, often due to bronchiectasis or aspergilloma, warranting urgent assessment.
  • Severe abdominal pain with bilious vomiting – suggesting acute intestinal obstruction (e.g., DIOS) requiring urgent surgical review.
  • Sudden decline in lung function – a rapid drop in FEV1, indicating a severe exacerbation or new complication, requiring prompt investigation.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Cystic fibrosis"][/sc]
  • Adults with milder mutations: May present later in life with atypical symptoms such as recurrent pancreatitis, male infertility (obstructive azoospermia), or isolated bronchiectasis without significant pancreatic involvement.
  • CFTR-related metabolic syndrome (CRMS) / CF Screen Positive, Inconclusive Diagnosis (CFSPID): Infants identified by newborn screening with some, but not all, diagnostic criteria for CF, requiring careful monitoring and follow-up. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="CFTR-related metabolic syndrome (CRMS): what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="CRMS"][/sc]
  • Older adults: With improved survival, older adults may present with complications such as osteoporosis, arthritis, or advanced liver disease, alongside chronic respiratory issues.
  • Infants with prolonged jaundice: Can be an early sign of CF-related liver disease or biliary obstruction.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Cystic fibrosis"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected Cystic Fibrosis:
    • Sweat chloride test: Elevated chloride levels (>60 mmol/L) are diagnostic. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Sweat chloride test interpretation: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Sweat chloride test"][/sc]
    • CFTR gene mutation analysis: Identifies specific mutations (e.g., F508del).
    • Newborn screening: Immunoreactive trypsinogen (IRT) followed by DNA testing or sweat test.
  • Severity/risk tools:
    • Cystic Fibrosis Pulmonary Exacerbation Score on MDCalc – aids in assessing severity of pulmonary exacerbations.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible:
    • Sputum culture: To identify common CF pathogens (e.g., *Pseudomonas aeruginosa*, *Staphylococcus aureus*).
    • Faecal elastase-1: To assess pancreatic exocrine function.
  • Imaging - Chest X-ray: May show hyperinflation, peribronchial thickening, or bronchiectasis.
  • Imaging - High-resolution CT (HRCT) chest: Provides detailed assessment of bronchiectasis and other lung pathology.
  • Serial reassessment: Regular lung function tests (spirometry, FEV1), nutritional assessments, and monitoring for complications.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Cystic fibrosis"][/sc]
  • A 25-year-old male with a known history of cystic fibrosis presents with a 2-week history of increased productive cough, dyspnoea, and reduced exercise tolerance, alongside decreased appetite and weight loss.
  • Chronic multisystem genetic disorder with progressive pulmonary disease and pancreatic insufficiency, concerning for a pulmonary exacerbation of cystic fibrosis.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Cystic fibrosis"][/sc]
  • Primary Ciliary Dyskinesia (PCD) – shares recurrent respiratory infections and bronchiectasis, but typically lacks pancreatic insufficiency and sweat chloride abnormality.
  • Asthma – presents with wheeze and cough, but usually responds well to bronchodilators and corticosteroids, and lacks the multisystem involvement of CF.
  • Bronchiectasis (non-CF) – chronic cough and sputum production, but without the underlying genetic defect or pancreatic involvement of CF. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Non-CF bronchiectasis vs Cystic Fibrosis: what are the key distinguishing features and why do they matter clinically?" text="Non-CF bronchiectasis"][/sc]
  • Immunodeficiency (e.g., Common Variable Immunodeficiency) – recurrent infections, but with different underlying immunological defects and typically normal sweat test.
  • Alpha-1 Antitrypsin Deficiency – can cause early-onset emphysema and liver disease, but typically presents later and lacks the characteristic thick secretions of CF.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Cystic fibrosis"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Recognition of acute exacerbation, assessment of respiratory status, and escalation to specialist CF team for prompt initiation of appropriate antibiotics and airway clearance.
  • Definitive management: Often includes a multidisciplinary approach focusing on airway clearance techniques, antibiotics for infection control, pancreatic enzyme replacement therapy, and nutritional support. CFTR modulators are a key component for eligible patients. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="CFTR modulators mechanism of action: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="CFTR modulators"][/sc]
  • Adjunctive management: May support management of complications such as cystic fibrosis-related diabetes, liver disease, and osteoporosis.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Regular clinical reviews, lung function tests, sputum microbiology, and nutritional assessments are typically warranted to monitor disease progression and treatment effectiveness.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Patients and families should be educated on recognising signs of exacerbation and when to seek urgent medical attention.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Cystic fibrosis"][/sc]
  • Respiratory failure: Progressive lung damage from chronic infection and inflammation ultimately leads to irreversible loss of lung function, necessitating oxygen therapy or lung transplantation, typically in the late phase.
  • Cystic fibrosis-related diabetes (CFRD): Pancreatic damage impairs insulin production, leading to glucose intolerance and overt diabetes, requiring insulin therapy and careful monitoring, often developing in the progressive to late phases.
  • Liver disease: Obstruction of bile ducts can lead to focal biliary cirrhosis, portal hypertension, and liver failure, which may require liver transplantation, typically manifesting in progressive or late phases.
  • Distal intestinal obstruction syndrome (DIOS): Accumulation of thick faecal material in the ileocaecum can cause partial or complete bowel obstruction, requiring medical or surgical intervention, and can occur at any phase.
  • Infertility: Affects nearly all males due to congenital bilateral absence of the vas deferens, and can affect females due to thick cervical mucus and impaired nutritional status, typically becoming apparent in adolescence or adulthood.
  • Osteoporosis: Multifactorial, due to chronic inflammation, malabsorption of fat-soluble vitamins, and corticosteroid use, increasing fracture risk, often seen in progressive to late phases.
