Testicular cancer [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Testicular cancer"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Testicular cancer"][/sc]
  • Testicular cancer is a malignant tumour arising from the germ cells of the testis, primarily affecting young and middle-aged men.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the common presentations, risk factors, and initial management of testicular cancer, including the importance of early diagnosis.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Testicular cancer"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Testicular cancer"][/sc]
  • Testicular cancer is the most common cancer in men aged 15-49 years in the UK, with around 2,400 new cases diagnosed each year (Cancer Research UK).
  • Risk factors include cryptorchidism (undescended testis), a family history of testicular cancer, previous testicular cancer in the contralateral testis, and certain genetic conditions like Klinefelter syndrome.
  • Incidence rates have been steadily increasing over the past few decades, though mortality rates have significantly declined due to advances in treatment.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Testicular cancer"][/sc]
  • Trigger: Malignant transformation of germ cells, often originating from carcinoma in situ (CIS) which is thought to develop from primordial germ cells that fail to differentiate normally during foetal development.
  • Progression: Uncontrolled proliferation of these abnormal germ cells, leading to the formation of a primary tumour within the testis, which can then invade local structures and metastasise via lymphatic and haematogenous routes.
  • Consequence: Local testicular destruction, systemic spread to retroperitoneal lymph nodes, lungs, liver, and brain, resulting in organ dysfunction and potentially life-threatening complications if untreated.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Testicular cancer"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Often presents as a painless testicular lump, which may be noticed incidentally by the patient or during routine examination, typically developing over weeks to a few months.
  • Progressive phase: As the tumour grows, local symptoms such as a dragging sensation, heaviness, or mild discomfort may develop, and systemic symptoms like back pain or dyspnoea may indicate metastatic spread, particularly beyond 3-6 months.
  • Late phase: Advanced metastatic disease can lead to significant constitutional symptoms, widespread organ involvement, and severe complications, often becoming apparent if diagnosis and treatment are delayed beyond 6-12 months.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Testicular cancer"][/sc]
  • Ask about a painless lump or swelling in one testicle - this is the most common presenting symptom and often the first indication of disease.
  • Ask about a feeling of heaviness or a dull ache in the scrotum or lower abdomen - these non-specific symptoms may accompany tumour growth.
  • Ask about any sudden collection of fluid in the scrotum (hydrocele) - this can sometimes be associated with testicular tumours.
  • Ask about back pain, cough, dyspnoea, or swelling in the legs - these suggest potential metastatic spread to retroperitoneal lymph nodes, lungs, or vena cava compression.
  • Ask about breast tenderness or gynaecomastia - rare symptoms that may indicate hormone production by certain tumour types.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Testicular cancer"][/sc]
  • Check for a firm, non-tender, irregular lump within the testis - this is the classic finding on palpation and suggests a solid mass rather than an epididymal cyst or hydrocele.
  • Check for testicular swelling or enlargement - the affected testis may appear larger than the contralateral side.
  • Check for signs of hydrocele or epididymitis - these can sometimes coexist or mimic testicular cancer, requiring careful differentiation.
  • Check for supraclavicular or inguinal lymphadenopathy - palpable lymph nodes may indicate metastatic disease.
  • Check vital signs: typically normal in early disease, but fever, tachycardia, or hypotension may suggest advanced disease or complications.
