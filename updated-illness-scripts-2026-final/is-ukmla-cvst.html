Cerebral venous sinus thrombosis (CVST) [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Cerebral venous sinus thrombosis"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Cerebral venous sinus thrombosis"][/sc]
  • Cerebral venous sinus thrombosis (CVST) involves the formation of a blood clot within the dural venous sinuses or cerebral veins, impeding venous drainage from the brain. This can lead to increased intracranial pressure, cerebral oedema, and potentially haemorrhagic infarction.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the varied presentations of CVST, initiate appropriate urgent neuroimaging, and understand the principles of anticoagulation management, even in the presence of intracranial haemorrhage.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Cerebral venous sinus thrombosis"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Cerebral venous sinus thrombosis"][/sc]
  • CVST is a rare condition, accounting for approximately 0.5-1% of all strokes, with an estimated annual incidence of 1.3-1.5 per 100,000 adults in the UK. It is more common in women, particularly those of childbearing age.
  • Risk factors include prothrombotic states (e.g., Factor V Leiden, protein C/S deficiency, antiphospholipid syndrome), pregnancy and puerperium, oral contraceptive use, infection (e.g., sinusitis, mastoiditis), dehydration, malignancy, and recent head injury or neurosurgery.
  • A notable association has been observed with vaccine-induced thrombotic thrombocytopenia (VITT) following certain COVID-19 vaccines, presenting as CVST with thrombocytopenia and elevated D-dimer.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Cerebral venous sinus thrombosis"][/sc]
  • Thrombus formation within a cerebral vein or dural venous sinus obstructs normal venous outflow, leading to venous congestion and increased cerebral venous pressure.
  • Elevated venous pressure impairs capillary perfusion, disrupts the blood-brain barrier, and reduces cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) absorption, resulting in cerebral oedema and elevated intracranial pressure. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Intracranial pressure: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Intracranial pressure"][/sc]
  • Severe or prolonged venous congestion can lead to parenchymal injury, including cytotoxic and vasogenic oedema, and potentially venous infarction with or without haemorrhagic transformation.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Cerebral venous sinus thrombosis"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Symptoms often develop subacutely over several days to weeks, though acute presentations (within hours) or chronic presentations (over months) can occur. Headache is typically the initial symptom.
  • Progressive phase: As venous obstruction and intracranial pressure increase, neurological deficits, seizures, or altered consciousness may develop and worsen over hours to days.
  • Late phase: If untreated, complications such as severe neurological disability, persistent seizures, or death can occur. With appropriate treatment, symptoms may resolve over weeks to months, but long-term follow-up is often required.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Cerebral venous sinus thrombosis"][/sc]
  • Ask about headache - often the most common symptom, which can be diffuse, progressive, severe, or thunderclap in nature, and may be associated with vomiting or visual changes, indicating raised intracranial pressure.
  • Ask about focal neurological deficits - such as weakness, sensory changes, or speech difficulties, which suggest involvement of specific brain regions due to venous infarction or oedema.
  • Ask about seizures - new-onset seizures, particularly focal, are a common presentation and can be the sole symptom, indicating cortical irritation.
  • Ask about altered mental status - including confusion, drowsiness, or coma, which may signify extensive cerebral involvement or severe intracranial hypertension.
  • Ask about visual disturbances - such as blurred vision, diplopia, or transient visual obscurations, often related to papilloedema and raised intracranial pressure.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Cerebral venous sinus thrombosis"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - may be normal, but fever can indicate an underlying infection, and hypertension may be present due to raised intracranial pressure.
  • Check neurological examination - for focal deficits such as hemiparesis, aphasia, or cranial nerve palsies, which localise the affected brain region.
  • Check fundoscopy - for papilloedema, a key sign of raised intracranial pressure, though it may not be present in early stages.
  • Check for signs of cavernous sinus thrombosis - including chemosis, proptosis, and ophthalmoplegia, which indicate specific sinus involvement. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Cavernous sinus thrombosis: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Cavernous sinus thrombosis"][/sc]
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Cerebral venous sinus thrombosis"][/sc]
  • New-onset severe or thunderclap headache - warrants urgent investigation to exclude serious intracranial pathology, including CVST.
  • Focal neurological deficits - such as new weakness, sensory loss, or speech disturbance, particularly if progressive, necessitate immediate neuroimaging.
  • New-onset seizures - especially if focal, in a patient with headache or other neurological symptoms, should prompt consideration of CVST.
  • Rapidly deteriorating conscious level - indicates severe cerebral compromise and requires emergency medical attention and imaging.
  • Signs of raised intracranial pressure - such as papilloedema, persistent vomiting, or Cushing's triad, demand urgent assessment and management to prevent herniation.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Cerebral venous sinus thrombosis"][/sc]
  • Isolated intracranial hypertension: Presentation solely with headache, visual disturbances, and papilloedema without focal deficits or seizures, mimicking idiopathic intracranial hypertension. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Idiopathic intracranial hypertension: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Isolated intracranial hypertension"][/sc]
  • Psychiatric symptoms: Rarely, CVST can manifest with acute psychiatric symptoms such as psychosis, confusion, or behavioural changes, making diagnosis challenging.
  • Subarachnoid haemorrhage: CVST can cause subarachnoid haemorrhage due to venous rupture, which may initially be misdiagnosed as arterial aneurysm rupture.
  • Paediatric population: In children, CVST often presents with non-specific symptoms like irritability, lethargy, poor feeding, or developmental regression, making diagnosis difficult.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Cerebral venous sinus thrombosis"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected CVST: Urgent neuroimaging is crucial. Magnetic Resonance Venography (MRV) is the preferred modality, demonstrating absent flow or intraluminal thrombus. Computed Tomography Venography (CTV) is an alternative if MRI is contraindicated or unavailable, and can show the "empty delta sign" in the superior sagittal sinus. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Empty delta sign: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Empty delta sign"][/sc]
  • Laboratory investigations: Full blood count (FBC) for thrombocytopenia (e.g., VITT), coagulation screen, D-dimer (elevated in CVST but normal D-dimer does not exclude diagnosis), and thrombophilia screen (e.g., Factor V Leiden, protein C/S deficiency, antiphospholipid antibodies) after the acute phase.
  • Severity/risk tools: While no specific severity score is universally used for CVST, clinical assessment of neurological status and imaging findings guide management.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Lumbar puncture may be considered to exclude meningitis or measure CSF pressure if idiopathic intracranial hypertension is suspected.

  • A 35-year-old woman presents with a progressive headache over five days, new-onset focal seizures, and mild right-sided weakness, with a history of recent oral contraceptive use.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Cerebral venous sinus thrombosis"][/sc]
  • Migraine with aura - typically recurrent, shorter duration, and often without objective neurological deficits.
  • Tension-type headache - usually bilateral, pressing, mild-to-moderate intensity, and not associated with focal neurology or seizures.
  • Subarachnoid haemorrhage (SAH) - often presents with sudden thunderclap headache, but neuroimaging (CT head) will typically show arterial bleeding.
  • Meningitis/Encephalitis - presents with fever, neck stiffness, and altered mental status, but CSF analysis would show inflammatory changes. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Meningitis: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Meningitis"][/sc]
  • Brain tumour - symptoms are often progressive but typically insidious, and neuroimaging would reveal a mass lesion.
  • Ischaemic stroke - usually presents with acute focal neurological deficits corresponding to an arterial territory, and diffusion-weighted MRI would show restricted diffusion.
  • Idiopathic intracranial hypertension - presents with headache and papilloedema, but neuroimaging is typically normal, and CSF pressure is elevated without evidence of thrombosis.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Cerebral venous sinus thrombosis"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Recognition and urgent neuroimaging to confirm diagnosis. Supportive care often includes managing seizures with antiepileptic medication and controlling raised intracranial pressure through optimal hydration and, if necessary, neurosurgical consultation.
  • Definitive management: Anticoagulation is the cornerstone of treatment, typically initiated with low molecular weight heparin (LMWH) or unfractionated heparin (UFH), even in the presence of intracranial haemorrhage, to prevent thrombus propagation and promote recanalisation.
  • Adjunctive management: Addressing underlying causes, such as treating infections with antibiotics or managing prothrombotic states. Endovascular thrombolysis or mechanical thrombectomy may be considered in severe cases with neurological deterioration despite adequate anticoagulation.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Close neurological monitoring is essential. Repeat neuroimaging (MRI/MRV) is often performed at 3-6 months to assess recanalisation. Long-term anticoagulation (e.g., with warfarin or direct oral anticoagulants) is typically continued for 3-12 months, depending on the cause and risk of recurrence.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Patients with progressive neurological deterioration, worsening intracranial pressure, or new haemorrhage require urgent re-evaluation and discussion with neurology/neurosurgery.
  • Exam pharmacology reference (UKMLA/MRCGP): Anticoagulants are commonly tested. Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Cerebral venous sinus thrombosis"][/sc]
  • Venous infarction and haemorrhage: Can lead to permanent neurological deficits, including weakness, sensory loss, or cognitive impairment, due to brain tissue damage from impaired venous drainage.
  • Status epilepticus: Prolonged or recurrent seizures can result in further brain injury and are a significant cause of morbidity.
  • Persistent intracranial hypertension: Can cause chronic headache, visual impairment, and papilloedema, potentially leading to permanent vision loss if not managed effectively.
  • Recurrence of thrombosis: Patients with underlying prothrombotic conditions or unprovoked CVST are at higher risk of future thrombotic events, including further CVST or other venous thromboembolism.
  • Death: Although rare, severe CVST can be fatal, particularly in cases with extensive parenchymal involvement, large haemorrhages, or refractory raised intracranial pressure.
