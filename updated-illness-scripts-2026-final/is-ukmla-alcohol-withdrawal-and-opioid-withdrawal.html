Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Alcohol withdrawal and opioid withdrawal"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Symptoms typically appear within hours of cessation, particularly beyond 6-12 hours for alcohol and 6-12 hours for short-acting opioids.
  • Progressive phase: Symptoms intensify over 24-72 hours, particularly beyond 24 hours for alcohol and 36-72 hours for opioids.
  • Late phase: Complications like seizures or delirium tremens may emerge, particularly beyond 48-72 hours for alcohol withdrawal. Protracted abstinence syndrome for opioids can last weeks to months.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Alcohol withdrawal and opioid withdrawal"][/sc]
  • Ask about tremors - to assess severity of alcohol withdrawal.
  • Ask about anxiety/agitation - common in both, indicates CNS hyperexcitability.
  • Ask about nausea/vomiting - prominent in opioid withdrawal, also present in alcohol withdrawal.
  • Ask about rhinorrhoea/lacrimation - classic opioid withdrawal symptoms.
  • Ask about hallucinations - particularly visual/auditory in alcohol withdrawal, differentiating from other mental health conditions.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Alcohol withdrawal and opioid withdrawal"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - tachycardia, hypertension, fever are common in both, indicating autonomic dysregulation.
  • Check sweating - diaphoresis is a key sign of autonomic hyperactivity.
  • Check pupillary size - dilated pupils in opioid withdrawal, may be normal or slightly dilated in alcohol withdrawal.
  • Check mental state - assess for disorientation, confusion, or delirium.
  • Check motor signs - hyperreflexia, ataxia, or seizures.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Alcohol withdrawal and opioid withdrawal"][/sc]
  • Seizures - indicates severe alcohol withdrawal, risk of status epilepticus.
  • Delirium tremens - severe alcohol withdrawal, high mortality if untreated.
  • Respiratory depression - particularly if opioid overdose is co-occurring or recent.
  • Severe dehydration - due to vomiting, sweating, and poor oral intake.
  • Altered consciousness - suggests severe complications or alternative diagnoses.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Alcohol withdrawal and opioid withdrawal"][/sc]
  • Older adults: May present with more subtle symptoms, increased risk of delirium and falls, and polypharmacy interactions.
  • Pregnancy: Withdrawal can harm foetus; management requires careful consideration of maternal and foetal well-being.
  • Polysubstance use: Symptoms may be masked or complicated by other substances, making diagnosis and management challenging.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Alcohol withdrawal and opioid withdrawal"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected alcohol/opioid withdrawal: Full blood count, electrolytes, liver function tests, glucose, urine toxicology screen.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: ECG, chest X-ray, head CT (if altered mental status or focal neurology).
  • Serial reassessment: Clinical Institute Withdrawal Assessment for Alcohol (CIWA-Ar) or Clinical Opiate Withdrawal Scale (COWS) to guide management. CIWA-Ar on MDCalc, COWS on MDCalc.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Alcohol withdrawal and opioid withdrawal"][/sc]
  • Hypoglycaemia - can mimic withdrawal symptoms, especially tremors and altered mental status.
  • Sepsis - fever, tachycardia, and altered mental status can overlap.
  • Other toxicities/withdrawals (e.g., benzodiazepine withdrawal) - similar CNS hyperexcitability.
  • Thyrotoxicosis - presents with anxiety, tremors, tachycardia.
  • Anxiety disorder/panic attack - can cause significant autonomic symptoms.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Alcohol withdrawal and opioid withdrawal"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Secure airway, breathing, circulation. Assess and manage agitation, seizures, and delirium. Correct fluid and electrolyte imbalances.
  • Definitive management: Benzodiazepines (e.g., chlordiazepoxide, diazepam) for alcohol withdrawal to reduce CNS hyperexcitability. Opioid agonists (e.g., buprenorphine, methadone) for opioid withdrawal to manage symptoms and prevent relapse.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Regular clinical assessment using CIWA-Ar/COWS. Close observation for complications. Referral to addiction services for ongoing support.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Consider intensive care unit admission for severe delirium tremens or refractory seizures. Advise patients on signs of worsening symptoms and when to seek urgent medical attention.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Alcohol withdrawal and opioid withdrawal"][/sc]
  • Withdrawal seizures: Occur typically within 12-48 hours of alcohol cessation, can lead to status epilepticus.
  • Delirium tremens: Severe form of alcohol withdrawal, usually 48-96 hours after last drink, characterised by confusion, hallucinations, and autonomic instability.
  • Aspiration pneumonia: Risk increased due to vomiting and altered consciousness during withdrawal.
  • Cardiac arrhythmias: Autonomic dysregulation can precipitate tachyarrhythmias.
  • Relapse: High risk of returning to substance use, particularly without adequate support and treatment.
