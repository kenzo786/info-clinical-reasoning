Metastatic cancer [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Metastatic cancer"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Metastatic cancer"][/sc]
  • Metastatic cancer refers to the spread of cancer cells from the primary tumour to distant sites in the body, forming secondary tumours. This process, known as metastasis, typically occurs via the bloodstream or lymphatic system.
  • UKMLA Learning Outcome: Multi-system diseases - Recognise and manage patients presenting with metastatic disease, understanding its impact on various organ systems and the principles of palliative care.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Metastatic cancer"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Metastatic cancer"][/sc]
  • In the UK, approximately 50% of all cancer diagnoses are made at an advanced stage, often with evidence of metastatic spread.
  • Age is a significant risk factor, with the incidence of metastatic cancer increasing substantially in individuals over 65 years.
  • Specific primary cancer types, such as lung, colorectal, breast, and prostate cancer, account for a large proportion of metastatic disease burden in the UK.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Metastatic cancer"][/sc]
  • Cancer cells acquire mutations enabling them to detach from the primary tumour, invade surrounding tissues, and enter the circulation (intravasation).
  • These circulating tumour cells (CTCs) travel through the bloodstream or lymphatic system, eventually extravasating into distant organs where they establish secondary tumour growth. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Circulating tumour cells: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Circulating tumour cells"][/sc]
  • The formation of a new tumour at a distant site requires the metastatic cells to adapt to the new microenvironment, proliferate, and induce angiogenesis to ensure nutrient supply.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Metastatic cancer"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Initial development of a primary tumour, often asymptomatic or with localised symptoms.
  • Progressive phase: Local invasion and spread to regional lymph nodes, followed by distant metastasis, which may occur months to years after primary diagnosis.
  • Late phase: Widespread metastatic disease, leading to significant organ dysfunction, cachexia, and a decline in performance status, often requiring palliative care.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Metastatic cancer"][/sc]
  • Ask about unexplained weight loss - often warrants investigation for underlying malignancy, including metastatic disease.
  • Ask about persistent pain - may indicate bone metastases or tumour compression of nerves.
  • Ask about fatigue and malaise - common non-specific symptoms of advanced cancer, impacting quality of life.
  • Ask about new or worsening neurological symptoms (e.g., headaches, seizures, focal weakness) - suggests brain metastases.
  • Ask about shortness of breath or cough - may indicate lung metastases or pleural effusion.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Metastatic cancer"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - assess for fever (infection/paraneoplastic), tachycardia (anaemia/dehydration), hypotension (sepsis/adrenal insufficiency).
  • Check for lymphadenopathy - palpable enlarged lymph nodes may indicate metastatic spread.
  • Check for hepatomegaly or jaundice - suggests liver metastases or biliary obstruction.
  • Check for bone tenderness or pathological fractures - indicative of bone metastases.
  • Check for neurological deficits - focal weakness, sensory changes, or altered mental status may point to brain or spinal cord metastases. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Spinal cord compression: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Spinal cord compression"][/sc]
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Metastatic cancer"][/sc]
  • New onset neurological deficit: May indicate brain metastases or spinal cord compression, requiring urgent imaging and intervention.
  • Pathological fracture: Suggests significant bone involvement, necessitating prompt orthopaedic assessment and pain management.
  • Hypercalcaemia of malignancy: A life-threatening metabolic complication requiring immediate medical attention.
  • Superior vena cava obstruction: Presents with facial swelling, dyspnoea, and dilated neck veins, demanding urgent investigation and treatment.
  • Rapidly worsening performance status: Indicates aggressive disease progression and a need for urgent review of treatment goals and palliative care input.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Metastatic cancer"][/sc]
  • Elderly patients: May present with non-specific symptoms like falls, confusion, or functional decline, masking the underlying malignancy.
  • Patients with unknown primary: Metastatic disease identified without an obvious primary tumour, requiring extensive diagnostic work-up.
  • Paraneoplastic syndromes: Remote effects of cancer not due to direct tumour invasion, such as Lambert-Eaton myasthenic syndrome or Cushing's syndrome.
  • Spontaneous tumour lysis syndrome: Rare but severe metabolic derangement occurring before or shortly after initiation of chemotherapy, particularly in rapidly growing tumours.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Metastatic cancer"][/sc]
  • Core investigations:
    • Full blood count (FBC) - to assess for anaemia of chronic disease or bone marrow involvement.
    • Liver function tests (LFTs) - to evaluate for liver metastases.
    • Renal function tests (U&Es) - to assess kidney function, especially before systemic therapies.
    • Bone profile (calcium, phosphate, alkaline phosphatase) - to screen for bone metastases and hypercalcaemia.
    • Tumour markers (e.g., CEA, CA19-9, PSA) - may support diagnosis and monitor treatment response, but not diagnostic on their own.
  • Imaging:
    • CT scan (chest, abdomen, pelvis) - primary imaging modality for staging and detecting metastases.
    • MRI scan (brain, spine) - for suspected brain or spinal cord metastases.
    • PET-CT scan - useful for detecting occult metastases and assessing metabolic activity of tumours.
    • Bone scan - to identify bone metastases, particularly in prostate and breast cancer.
  • If alternative diagnosis considered:
    • Biopsy of suspicious lesions (e.g., lymph node, liver lesion) - essential for histological confirmation of malignancy and primary tumour identification.
  • Serial reassessment:
    • Regular clinical review and repeat imaging as guided by treatment protocols and patient symptoms.

  • This is a case of advanced, multi-systemic disease, likely requiring palliative care input and complex symptom management.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Metastatic cancer"][/sc]
  • Primary cancer with local invasion - distinguished by absence of distant spread on imaging.
  • Benign conditions mimicking cancer (e.g., infection, inflammation) - often differentiated by biopsy and specific inflammatory markers.
  • Other multi-system diseases (e.g., autoimmune conditions, chronic infections) - distinguished by specific serological markers and clinical presentation.
  • Lymphoma - can present with widespread lymphadenopathy and systemic symptoms, requiring biopsy for differentiation.
  • Multiple myeloma - a plasma cell malignancy causing bone pain and systemic symptoms, diagnosed by bone marrow biopsy and serum/urine electrophoresis.
  • Sarcoidosis - a granulomatous disease that can affect multiple organs, differentiated by biopsy and specific imaging patterns.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Metastatic cancer"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities:
    • Symptom control, particularly pain management, using a stepped approach as per WHO guidelines.
    • Addressing acute complications such as spinal cord compression or hypercalcaemia.
    • Psychological support and clear communication regarding prognosis and treatment options.
  • Definitive management:
    • Systemic therapies: chemotherapy, targeted therapy, immunotherapy, or hormonal therapy, chosen based on primary tumour type, molecular characteristics, and patient fitness.
    • Localised therapies: radiotherapy for symptom control (e.g., bone pain, brain metastases) or surgical debulking in selected cases.
    • Palliative care input: integral to managing symptoms, improving quality of life, and supporting end-of-life care discussions.
  • Monitoring and follow-up:
    • Regular clinical assessment of symptoms, performance status, and treatment side effects.
    • Imaging (e.g., CT scans) to assess treatment response and disease progression.
    • Blood tests to monitor organ function and tumour markers.
  • Escalation and safety-netting:
    • Clear pathways for escalation of care for worsening symptoms or acute complications.
    • Providing patients and carers with information on when and how to seek urgent medical advice.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Metastatic cancer"][/sc]
  • Bone metastases: Can lead to severe pain, pathological fractures, and spinal cord compression, significantly impacting mobility and quality of life.
  • Brain metastases: May cause neurological deficits, seizures, headaches, and cognitive impairment, requiring urgent management to preserve neurological function.
  • Liver metastases: Can result in hepatomegaly, jaundice, ascites, and liver failure, affecting metabolic functions and overall prognosis.
  • Lung metastases: May cause dyspnoea, cough, haemoptysis, and pleural effusions, leading to respiratory compromise.
  • Malignant effusions (pleural, peritoneal, pericardial): Accumulation of fluid in body cavities, causing symptoms like dyspnoea, abdominal distension, or cardiac tamponade.
  • Cachexia: A complex metabolic syndrome characterised by severe weight loss, muscle wasting, and fatigue, contributing to functional decline and reduced survival.
