Respiratory failure [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Respiratory failure"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Respiratory failure"][/sc]
  • Respiratory failure is a life-threatening condition characterised by the inability of the respiratory system to maintain adequate gas exchange, leading to hypoxaemia (low arterial oxygen) with or without hypercapnia (high arterial carbon dioxide).
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise and differentiate between Type 1 and Type 2 respiratory failure based on arterial blood gas analysis, and understand the immediate management principles for each.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Respiratory failure"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Respiratory failure"][/sc]
  • Acute respiratory failure is a common reason for admission to critical care units in the UK, with an estimated incidence of 77-88 cases per 100,000 population per year [1].
  • Chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD) exacerbations are a leading cause of Type 2 respiratory failure, accounting for a significant proportion of hospital admissions and mortality in the UK [2].
  • Risk factors include pre-existing lung disease (e.g., COPD, asthma, interstitial lung disease), cardiac conditions (e.g., heart failure), neuromuscular disorders, obesity, and acute infections like pneumonia.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Respiratory failure"][/sc]
  • Trigger: Respiratory failure often begins with an insult to the respiratory system, such as pneumonia, pulmonary oedema, or an exacerbation of chronic lung disease, impairing gas exchange.
  • Progression: This impairment leads to either a ventilation/perfusion (V/Q) mismatch (Type 1) where oxygenation is primarily affected, or alveolar hypoventilation (Type 2) where both oxygenation and carbon dioxide elimination are compromised.
  • Consequence: Persistent hypoxaemia and/or hypercapnia can lead to cellular dysfunction, organ damage, and if uncorrected, cardiac arrest and death. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="V/Q mismatch: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="V/Q mismatch"][/sc]
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Respiratory failure"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Acute onset, often developing over minutes to hours, characterised by rapid deterioration in gas exchange.
  • Progressive phase: If untreated, respiratory failure can worsen over several hours, leading to increasing hypoxaemia and/or hypercapnia, and signs of respiratory distress.
  • Late phase: Particularly beyond 24-48 hours, sustained respiratory failure can result in multi-organ dysfunction, requiring advanced respiratory support and intensive care.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Respiratory failure"][/sc]
  • Ask about dyspnoea - sudden onset or worsening breathlessness is a cardinal symptom, indicating impaired gas exchange.
  • Ask about cough and sputum production - may indicate an underlying infection or exacerbation of chronic lung disease contributing to respiratory compromise.
  • Ask about altered mental status - confusion, drowsiness, or agitation can be signs of severe hypoxaemia or hypercapnia affecting cerebral function.
  • Ask about chest pain - pleuritic chest pain may suggest conditions like pneumonia or pulmonary embolism, which can precipitate respiratory failure.
  • Ask about orthopnoea or paroxysmal nocturnal dyspnoea - suggestive of cardiac failure leading to pulmonary oedema and Type 1 respiratory failure.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Respiratory failure"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - tachypnoea, tachycardia, hypotension, and oxygen desaturation are common and indicate the severity of respiratory compromise.
  • Check respiratory effort - use of accessory muscles, nasal flaring, and tracheal tug suggest increased work of breathing.
  • Check auscultation - crackles may indicate pulmonary oedema or pneumonia, wheeze suggests bronchoconstriction, and reduced breath sounds can be seen in pneumothorax or pleural effusion.
  • Check cyanosis - a bluish discolouration of skin or mucous membranes, indicating significant hypoxaemia.
  • Check neurological signs - asterixis or papilloedema can be present in severe hypercapnia. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Hypercapnic drive: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Hypercapnic drive"][/sc]
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Respiratory failure"][/sc]
  • Severe dyspnoea at rest - indicates critical respiratory compromise requiring immediate intervention.
  • Altered mental status - confusion, reduced consciousness, or unresponsiveness suggest severe hypoxaemia or hypercapnia with cerebral impairment.
  • Haemodynamic instability - hypotension, tachycardia, or signs of shock indicate impending cardiovascular collapse.
  • Paradoxical abdominal breathing - suggests severe diaphragmatic fatigue and impending respiratory arrest.
  • Rapid progression of symptoms - worsening breathlessness or signs over minutes to a few hours necessitates urgent medical review.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Respiratory failure"][/sc]
  • Older adults: May present with subtle symptoms like confusion or weakness rather than overt dyspnoea, making diagnosis challenging.
  • Immunosuppressed patients: May develop respiratory failure from opportunistic infections with less pronounced inflammatory responses, delaying recognition.
  • Patients with chronic hypercapnia: May tolerate higher PaCO2 levels, and their respiratory drive may be more dependent on hypoxic drive, requiring careful oxygen administration. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Hypoxic drive: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Hypoxic drive"][/sc]
  • Obese patients: Increased work of breathing and reduced lung volumes can predispose to respiratory failure, often with a hypercapnic component.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Respiratory failure"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected respiratory failure: Arterial blood gas (ABG) analysis is crucial for confirming hypoxaemia and hypercapnia, and differentiating between Type 1 and Type 2 respiratory failure.
  • Severity/risk tools: Consider using the CURB-65 score for pneumonia severity to guide management decisions. CURB-65 Score for Pneumonia Severity on MDCalc
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: D-dimer for suspected pulmonary embolism, cardiac biomarkers for acute coronary syndrome or heart failure.
  • Imaging - Chest X-ray: To identify underlying causes such as pneumonia, pulmonary oedema, pneumothorax, or pleural effusion.
  • Imaging - CT Pulmonary Angiogram (CTPA): If pulmonary embolism is suspected as a cause of acute respiratory failure.
  • Serial reassessment: Repeat ABGs and clinical assessment are essential to monitor response to treatment and detect deterioration. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Arterial blood gas interpretation: what are the key steps and clinical implications?" text="ABG interpretation"][/sc]
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Respiratory failure"][/sc]
  • A 68-year-old male with a history of COPD presents with acute worsening dyspnoea, productive cough, and reduced oxygen saturation, requiring supplemental oxygen.
  • Elderly male with chronic lung disease and acute on chronic breathlessness, concerning for Type 2 respiratory failure secondary to COPD exacerbation.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Respiratory failure"][/sc]
  • Acute Exacerbation of COPD - often presents with increased dyspnoea, cough, and sputum, leading to Type 2 respiratory failure.
  • Pneumonia - can cause Type 1 respiratory failure due to V/Q mismatch from consolidation, or Type 2 if severe and leading to ventilatory fatigue.
  • Pulmonary Embolism - typically causes Type 1 respiratory failure due to V/Q mismatch, with sudden onset dyspnoea and pleuritic chest pain.
  • Acute Heart Failure/Pulmonary Oedema - results in Type 1 respiratory failure due to fluid accumulation in the alveoli, impairing oxygen diffusion.
  • Asthma Exacerbation - severe bronchoconstriction can lead to air trapping, V/Q mismatch, and eventually ventilatory fatigue causing Type 2 respiratory failure.
  • Neuromuscular Weakness (e.g., Guillain-Barré Syndrome, Myasthenia Gravis crisis) - leads to Type 2 respiratory failure due to inadequate respiratory muscle strength. Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Respiratory failure"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Recognition of respiratory failure, escalation to senior medical staff, securing the airway if necessary, and initiation of supportive care including controlled oxygen therapy.
  • Definitive management: Addressing the underlying cause (e.g., antibiotics for infection, diuretics for pulmonary oedema, bronchodilators for bronchospasm). Non-invasive ventilation (NIV) is often considered for Type 2 respiratory failure (BiPAP) and sometimes for Type 1 (CPAP).
  • Adjunctive management: Positioning, physiotherapy, and careful fluid management may support respiratory function.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Close monitoring of vital signs, oxygen saturation, and serial ABGs is essential. Regular clinical review to assess response to treatment.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Clear criteria for escalation to higher levels of care (e.g., HDU/ICU) and for intubation and invasive mechanical ventilation should be established.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Respiratory failure"][/sc]
  • Acute Respiratory Distress Syndrome (ARDS): Severe lung inflammation leading to widespread alveolar damage and profound hypoxaemia, often developing within hours to days.
  • Multi-organ failure: Prolonged hypoxaemia and/or hypercapnia can lead to dysfunction of the heart, kidneys, brain, and liver, particularly beyond 24-48 hours.
  • Cardiac arrhythmias: Hypoxaemia, hypercapnia, and acidosis can precipitate various cardiac arrhythmias, increasing the risk of cardiac arrest.
  • Ventilator-associated pneumonia (VAP): A common complication in patients requiring mechanical ventilation, increasing morbidity and mortality.
  • Barotrauma/Volutrauma: Lung injury due to excessive pressure or volume during mechanical ventilation, leading to pneumothorax or pneumomediastinum.
