Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Cellulitis (Skin and soft tissue)"][/sc]
  • Cellulitis is an acute bacterial infection of the dermis and subcutaneous tissue, typically presenting as a rapidly spreading area of erythema, warmth, swelling, and tenderness.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical features of cellulitis, differentiate it from other skin conditions, and initiate appropriate empirical antimicrobial therapy.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Cellulitis (Skin and soft tissue)"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Cellulitis (Skin and soft tissue)"][/sc]
  • Cellulitis is a common condition, with an estimated incidence of 200 per 100,000 person-years in the UK, leading to significant healthcare burden [1].
  • The main bacteria causing cellulitis are _Streptococcus pyogenes_ and _Staphylococcus aureus_, often entering through breaks in the skin barrier [1].
  • Risk factors include skin breaks (e.g., wounds, insect bites, tinea pedis), oedema, obesity, venous insufficiency, lymphoedema, and immunosuppression [1].
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Cellulitis (Skin and soft tissue)"][/sc]
  • Bacterial entry through a breach in the skin barrier (e.g., minor trauma, fungal infection) allows pathogens, most commonly Group A Streptococcus or _Staphylococcus aureus_, to colonise the dermis and subcutaneous tissue.
  • Local bacterial proliferation and release of toxins trigger an inflammatory response, leading to vasodilation, increased capillary permeability, and infiltration of immune cells, resulting in the characteristic signs of inflammation.
  • Uncontrolled infection can lead to local tissue destruction, abscess formation, and systemic spread via lymphatic or haematogenous routes, potentially causing sepsis or necrotising fasciitis. Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Cellulitis (Skin and soft tissue)"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Rapid onset and progression of localised erythema, warmth, swelling, and tenderness, typically developing over hours to 1-2 days.
  • Progressive phase: Without effective treatment, the infection can spread rapidly, with worsening local signs and potential development of systemic symptoms (fever, malaise) within 24-48 hours.
  • Late phase: Resolution of symptoms usually begins within 2-3 days of appropriate antibiotic therapy, though skin changes like redness and swelling may take several weeks to fully subside, particularly beyond 7 days.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Cellulitis (Skin and soft tissue)"][/sc]
  • Ask about rapidly spreading redness - indicates an active inflammatory process, crucial for distinguishing from static lesions.
  • Ask about pain and tenderness - often disproportionate to the visible signs, suggesting deeper tissue involvement.
  • Ask about warmth and swelling - cardinal signs of inflammation, helping to delineate the affected area.
  • Ask about recent skin breaks or trauma - identifies potential entry points for bacteria.
  • Ask about systemic symptoms (fever, chills, malaise) - suggests systemic inflammatory response, indicating more severe infection.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Cellulitis (Skin and soft tissue)"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - fever, tachycardia, or hypotension may indicate systemic infection or sepsis.
  • Check for diffuse, poorly demarcated erythema - characteristic of cellulitis, distinguishing it from the sharply demarcated border of erysipelas.
  • Check for warmth and oedema - confirms local inflammation and tissue involvement.
  • Check for regional lymphadenopathy - indicates lymphatic spread of infection.
  • Check for blistering or necrosis - suggests severe infection or potential necrotising fasciitis. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Eron classification of cellulitis: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Eron classification"]
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Cellulitis (Skin and soft tissue)"][/sc]
  • Systemic toxicity: Signs of sepsis (e.g., high fever, hypotension, altered mental status) indicate severe infection requiring urgent hospital admission and intravenous antibiotics.
  • Rapid progression: Worsening erythema, pain, or swelling despite 24-48 hours of oral antibiotics, or very rapid spread over hours, suggests inadequate treatment or aggressive pathogen.
  • Crepitus or bullae: Suggests gas-forming organisms or deeper tissue necrosis, raising suspicion for necrotising fasciitis.
  • Periorbital involvement: Cellulitis around the eye can rapidly progress to orbital cellulitis, a medical emergency with risk of vision loss or intracranial spread.
  • Pain out of proportion: Severe pain disproportionate to clinical signs, especially with tense swelling, is a hallmark of necrotising fasciitis.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Cellulitis (Skin and soft tissue)"][/sc]
  • Immunocompromised patients: May present with less pronounced inflammatory signs despite severe infection, requiring a high index of suspicion.
  • Diabetic foot cellulitis: Often polymicrobial, with potential for underlying osteomyelitis, and may present with subtle signs due to neuropathy.
  • Recurrent cellulitis: Frequently associated with chronic oedema or lymphoedema, where skin changes can be subtle and diagnosis challenging.
  • Facial cellulitis: Can be associated with dental infections or sinusitis, and carries a higher risk of complications due to proximity to vital structures.
  • Cellulitis in intravenous drug users: Often occurs at injection sites, may be associated with unusual pathogens, and can rapidly progress to abscess or necrotising infection. Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Cellulitis (Skin and soft tissue)"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected cellulitis: Clinical diagnosis is primary. Mark the area of erythema with a pen to monitor spread or regression.
  • Severity/risk tools: The Eron classification system helps guide management decisions, stratifying severity from Class I (mild) to Class IV (sepsis). Eron Classification of Cellulitis on MDCalc
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Consider D-dimer for suspected DVT, or imaging for abscess.
  • Imaging - Ultrasound: Can help differentiate cellulitis from abscess or DVT, and identify fluid collections.
  • Imaging - MRI: Useful for suspected deeper infections like osteomyelitis or necrotising fasciitis, particularly in complex cases.
  • Serial reassessment: Regular clinical review is essential to monitor response to treatment and identify worsening or complications. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Distinguishing cellulitis from DVT: what are the key clinical features?" text="Distinguishing from DVT"]

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Cellulitis (Skin and soft tissue)"][/sc]
  • Erysipelas - Sharply demarcated, raised border, often affecting the face or lower limbs, typically caused by Group A Streptococcus.
  • Deep Vein Thrombosis (DVT) - Unilateral leg swelling, pain, and warmth, but typically without significant erythema or fever, and often associated with risk factors for thrombosis. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Distinguishing cellulitis from erysipelas: what are the key clinical features?" text="Distinguishing from erysipelas"]
  • Contact Dermatitis - Pruritic, erythematous rash, often with vesicles or bullae, typically with a clear exposure history and less systemic upset.
  • Lipodermatosclerosis - Chronic inflammatory condition of the lower legs, often associated with venous insufficiency, presenting with induration, hyperpigmentation, and an 'inverted champagne bottle' appearance, not acute infection.
  • Gout - Acute onset of severe joint pain, swelling, and erythema, typically monoarticular, often affecting the great toe, and associated with hyperuricaemia.
  • Abscess - Localised collection of pus, often fluctuant, with surrounding erythema and tenderness, but distinct from diffuse cellulitis.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Cellulitis (Skin and soft tissue)"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Recognition of severity (e.g., Eron classification), assessment for systemic involvement, and consideration of hospital admission for severe cases or those with red flag features. Supportive care often includes analgesia and elevation of the affected limb.
  • Definitive management: Empirical antibiotic therapy targeting common pathogens (Group A Streptococcus, _Staphylococcus aureus_). Oral flucloxacillin is typically first-line for uncomplicated cellulitis in adults.
  • Adjunctive management: Management of underlying predisposing factors such as oedema, venous insufficiency, or tinea pedis is important to prevent recurrence.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Reassessment within 24-48 hours to evaluate response to antibiotics. The area of erythema should be marked to monitor spread or regression.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Patients should be advised to seek medical attention if symptoms worsen rapidly, new red flag features develop, or if there is no improvement within 2-3 days.
  • Exam pharmacology reference (UKMLA/MRCGP): Flucloxacillin is a narrow-spectrum penicillinase-resistant penicillin, often used for staphylococcal and streptococcal skin infections. Clarithromycin or doxycycline may be considered for penicillin allergy. Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Cellulitis (Skin and soft tissue)"][/sc]
  • Abscess formation: Localised collection of pus requiring drainage, occurring if the infection is not adequately treated or if there is a virulent pathogen.
  • Sepsis: Systemic inflammatory response to infection, potentially leading to organ dysfunction and shock, particularly if cellulitis is extensive or in immunocompromised individuals.
  • Necrotising fasciitis: A rare but life-threatening rapidly progressive bacterial infection of the fascia and subcutaneous tissue, requiring urgent surgical debridement.
  • Recurrent cellulitis: Common in patients with predisposing factors like lymphoedema or venous insufficiency, leading to chronic skin changes and increased morbidity.
  • Post-streptococcal glomerulonephritis: A rare complication following streptococcal cellulitis, particularly in children, presenting with haematuria and proteinuria weeks after the skin infection.
