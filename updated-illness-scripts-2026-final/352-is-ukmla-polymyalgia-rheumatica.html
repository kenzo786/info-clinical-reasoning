Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Polymyalgia rheumatica"][/sc]
  • Polymyalgia rheumatica (PMR) is an inflammatory condition causing pain, stiffness, and inflammation in the muscles around the shoulders, neck, and hips.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical features of polymyalgia rheumatica, differentiate it from other musculoskeletal conditions, and understand the principles of its initial management, including the need to assess for giant cell arteritis.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Polymyalgia rheumatica"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Polymyalgia rheumatica"][/sc]
  • PMR is an age-related condition, rarely affecting individuals under 50 years, with most diagnoses occurring in those over 65 years. It is more common in women than men. [1]
  • It is one of the most common inflammatory rheumatic diseases in the elderly, with an estimated incidence of 1 in 1,000 people over 50 years in the UK. [2]
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Polymyalgia rheumatica"][/sc]
  • The exact cause of PMR is unknown, but it is thought to involve a combination of genetic predisposition and environmental triggers, potentially viral infections, leading to an autoimmune response.
  • This triggers systemic inflammation, primarily affecting the synovial membranes of large joints (shoulders and hips) and periarticular structures, resulting in pain and stiffness. Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Polymyalgia rheumatica"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Symptoms often develop rapidly over days or weeks, characterised by bilateral shoulder and/or pelvic girdle pain and stiffness, particularly beyond 45 minutes in the morning.
  • Progressive phase: Without treatment, symptoms persist and may worsen, leading to significant functional limitation and reduced quality of life.
  • Late phase: With appropriate corticosteroid treatment, symptoms typically resolve quickly, often within days, but a prolonged course of treatment (1-2 years) is usually required to prevent relapse.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Polymyalgia rheumatica"][/sc]
  • Ask about bilateral shoulder and/or pelvic girdle pain - this symmetrical distribution is characteristic and helps differentiate from localised musculoskeletal issues.
  • Ask about morning stiffness lasting more than 45 minutes - this prolonged stiffness is a hallmark of inflammatory arthritis and a key diagnostic criterion.
  • Ask about difficulty with activities of daily living, such as dressing or getting out of a chair - functional impairment is a direct consequence of pain and stiffness.
  • Ask about systemic symptoms like fatigue, malaise, low-grade fever, loss of appetite, or weight loss - these non-specific symptoms indicate a systemic inflammatory process.
  • Ask about new headache, jaw claudication, or visual disturbances - these are red flag symptoms for giant cell arteritis, which can co-exist with PMR.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Polymyalgia rheumatica"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - often normal, but low-grade fever may be present, indicating systemic inflammation.
  • Check for tenderness and pain on passive movement of the shoulders and hips - this indicates inflammation of the joint capsules and surrounding structures.
  • Check for normal muscle strength - distinguishing PMR from myositis, where muscle weakness is prominent. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Differentiating Polymyalgia rheumatica from myositis: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Muscle strength in PMR"][/sc]
  • Check for peripheral synovitis (e.g., in wrists or knees) - can occur in a minority of PMR patients, sometimes overlapping with rheumatoid arthritis.
  • Check for signs of giant cell arteritis (GCA), such as temporal artery tenderness or thickening, absent temporal artery pulse, or visual field defects - crucial for immediate management.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Polymyalgia rheumatica"][/sc]
  • New onset headache: Suggests cranial arteritis, a serious complication requiring urgent assessment and treatment to prevent irreversible vision loss.
  • Jaw claudication: Pain in the jaw muscles with chewing, indicating ischaemia due to temporal arteritis.
  • Visual disturbances: Sudden, transient, or permanent vision loss (amaurosis fugax, diplopia) are ophthalmic emergencies in the context of GCA.
  • Scalp tenderness: Often localised over the temporal arteries, a sign of inflammation.
  • Constitutional symptoms disproportionate to PMR: Significant unexplained weight loss or high fever may suggest an alternative diagnosis or malignancy.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Polymyalgia rheumatica"][/sc]
  • Younger patients (<50 years): PMR is rare in this age group; consider alternative diagnoses such as seronegative rheumatoid arthritis or other inflammatory arthropathies.
  • Normal inflammatory markers: A small proportion of patients with classic clinical features of PMR may have normal ESR and CRP; diagnosis relies more heavily on clinical response to steroids. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Polymyalgia rheumatica with normal inflammatory markers: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Seronegative PMR"][/sc]
  • Predominant peripheral arthritis: While typically affecting proximal joints, some patients may present with peripheral synovitis, which can mimic rheumatoid arthritis.
  • Isolated limb pain: Unilateral or localised pain, rather than the classic bilateral and symmetrical presentation, should prompt consideration of other diagnoses.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Polymyalgia rheumatica"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected Polymyalgia rheumatica:
    • Full blood count (FBC): May show normochromic normocytic anaemia.
    • Erythrocyte Sedimentation Rate (ESR) and C-reactive protein (CRP): Typically elevated (>40 mm/hr for ESR), indicating systemic inflammation.
    • Liver function tests (LFTs) and renal function tests (U&Es): To assess baseline organ function before steroid therapy.
    • Thyroid function tests (TFTs): To exclude hypothyroidism, which can mimic PMR symptoms.
    • Creatine kinase (CK): Usually normal, helping to exclude myositis.
  • Severity/risk tools: The PMR Activity Score (PMR-AS) can be used to monitor disease activity, though not routinely used for diagnosis.   • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible:
    • Rheumatoid factor (RF) and anti-citrullinated protein antibodies (ACPA): To exclude rheumatoid arthritis.
    • Imaging (ultrasound or MRI) of shoulders/hips: May show subacromial/subdeltoid bursitis, biceps tenosynovitis, or glenohumeral synovitis.
  • Imaging - Ultrasound: Ultrasound of the shoulders and hips can reveal characteristic inflammatory changes, such as bilateral subacromial/subdeltoid bursitis, biceps tenosynovitis, and glenohumeral synovitis.
  • Imaging - Temporal artery biopsy: Indicated if there is suspicion of giant cell arteritis (GCA) to confirm diagnosis.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Polymyalgia rheumatica"][/sc]
  • Rheumatoid Arthritis: Typically involves smaller joints symmetrically, though can present with proximal symptoms; often associated with positive RF/ACPA and erosive changes on imaging.
  • Osteoarthritis: Characterised by mechanical pain, often worse with activity and relieved by rest; morning stiffness is usually shorter (<30 minutes) and inflammatory markers are normal.
  • Hypothyroidism: Can cause generalised aches, stiffness, and fatigue; distinguished by abnormal thyroid function tests.
  • Myositis (e.g., Polymyositis, Dermatomyositis): Presents with muscle weakness, often proximal, and elevated creatine kinase levels. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Myositis vs Polymyalgia rheumatica: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Myositis"][/sc]
  • Fibromyalgia: Characterised by widespread chronic pain and tenderness at specific points, but without objective signs of inflammation or elevated inflammatory markers.
  • Malignancy (Paraneoplastic syndromes): Can cause constitutional symptoms and musculoskeletal pain; requires careful exclusion, especially in atypical presentations of PMR.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Polymyalgia rheumatica"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Prompt recognition and initiation of corticosteroid therapy, especially if giant cell arteritis is suspected, to prevent irreversible complications.
  • Definitive management: Oral corticosteroids, typically prednisolone, are the cornerstone of treatment, with a rapid and dramatic response often observed.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Regular clinical review and monitoring of inflammatory markers (ESR, CRP) to assess response to treatment and guide steroid tapering.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Patients should be educated on symptoms of GCA and advised to seek immediate medical attention if they develop.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Polymyalgia rheumatica"][/sc]
  • Giant Cell Arteritis (GCA): Occurs in up to 1 in 5 PMR patients, particularly in the early phase, and can lead to permanent vision loss or stroke if not promptly treated.
  • Corticosteroid-related adverse effects: Long-term steroid use, often required for 1-2 years, can lead to osteoporosis, diabetes, hypertension, and increased infection risk.
  • Relapse: Symptoms may recur during steroid tapering or after discontinuation, necessitating re-initiation or increase in corticosteroid dose.
  • Functional impairment: Persistent pain and stiffness, especially if untreated or inadequately managed, can significantly impact mobility and quality of life.
