Subarachnoid haemorrhage [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Subarachnoid haemorrhage"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Subarachnoid haemorrhage"][/sc]
  • Subarachnoid haemorrhage (SAH) is the extravasation of blood into the subarachnoid space between the pial and arachnoid membranes, often resulting from a ruptured intracranial aneurysm.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the acute presentation of subarachnoid haemorrhage and understand its life-threatening nature.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Subarachnoid haemorrhage"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Subarachnoid haemorrhage"][/sc]
  • In the UK, subarachnoid haemorrhage affects approximately 8,500 people each year, representing a significant cause of stroke-related mortality and morbidity.
  • Globally, the age-standardised incidence of SAH is around 8–9 per 100,000 persons, with higher rates observed in individuals with hypertension, smoking, and a family history of aneurysm rupture.
  • Key risk factors include uncontrolled hypertension, smoking, excessive alcohol consumption, and certain genetic conditions such as polycystic kidney disease and Ehlers-Danlos syndrome.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Subarachnoid haemorrhage"][/sc]
  • Intracranial aneurysms, often developing at arterial bifurcations within the circle of Willis, are prone to wall weakening due to hemodynamic stress and lack of elastic lamina.
  • Structural weakening of the aneurysm wall, exacerbated by turbulent blood flow and hypertension, predisposes to expansion and eventual rupture, leading to blood extravasation into the subarachnoid space.
  • The sudden increase in intracranial pressure upon rupture causes immediate symptoms, while subsequent vasospasm, delayed cerebral ischaemia, and hydrocephalus contribute to secondary brain injury and significant morbidity.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Subarachnoid haemorrhage"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Symptoms, particularly a sudden severe headache, typically manifest immediately upon aneurysm rupture, often peaking in intensity within minutes.
  • Progressive phase: Neurological deterioration, including altered consciousness, focal deficits, and signs of meningism, may develop over hours to days due to rising intracranial pressure and early complications.
  • Late phase: Complications such as vasospasm, hydrocephalus, and delayed cerebral ischaemia can emerge days to weeks after the initial event, significantly impacting long-term prognosis and recovery.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Subarachnoid haemorrhage"][/sc]
  • Ask about thunderclap headache - an extremely painful headache that starts suddenly and does not go away, often described as the "worst headache of my life".
  • Ask about neck pain or stiffness - due to meningeal irritation from blood in the subarachnoid space.
  • Ask about feeling and being sick - common non-specific symptoms associated with increased intracranial pressure.
  • Ask about photophobia - sensitivity to light, another sign of meningeal irritation.
  • Ask about brief loss of consciousness or confusion - indicating significant neurological impact.
  • Ask about focal neurological deficits - such as drooping on one side of the face, slurred speech, or weakness on one side of the body, which may suggest a larger bleed or associated ischaemia.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Subarachnoid haemorrhage"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - particularly blood pressure, which may be elevated due to sympathetic surge, and heart rate for arrhythmias.
  • Check for signs of meningism - neck stiffness, Kernig\'s sign, Brudzinski\'s sign, indicating meningeal irritation.
  • Check neurological status - Glasgow Coma Scale (GCS) for level of consciousness, pupillary responses, and focal neurological deficits.
  • Check fundoscopy - for papilloedema or subhyaloid haemorrhages, which are highly suggestive of SAH.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Subarachnoid haemorrhage"][/sc]
  • Thunderclap headache - sudden onset, severe headache reaching maximum intensity within minutes, necessitating urgent investigation.
  • New onset seizure - particularly in the context of acute severe headache, suggests significant cerebral irritation or injury.
  • Altered level of consciousness - any drowsiness, confusion, or reduced GCS score warrants immediate neuroimaging.
  • Focal neurological deficit - new weakness, sensory loss, or cranial nerve palsy indicates localised brain injury.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Subarachnoid haemorrhage"][/sc]
  • Older adults: May present with less severe headache, confusion, or subtle neurological changes, making diagnosis challenging.
  • Patients with pre-existing headache disorders: SAH can be misdiagnosed as a severe migraine; any new or significantly different headache should be investigated.
  • Minor haemorrhage ("warning leak"): A less severe headache that resolves spontaneously, often preceding a major SAH by days or weeks, can be easily missed.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Subarachnoid haemorrhage"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected Subarachnoid haemorrhage: Non-contrast CT head scan, ideally performed within 6 hours of symptom onset, is the first-line investigation.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Lumbar puncture to detect xanthochromia (elevated bilirubin) if CT is negative or performed after 6 hours, or if clinical suspicion remains high.
  • Imaging - CT Angiography: Performed without delay after SAH diagnosis to identify the source of bleeding, typically an intracranial aneurysm, and guide definitive treatment.
  • Imaging - Digital Subtraction Angiography (DSA) or Magnetic Resonance Angiography (MRA): May be used if CTA is inconclusive or to further characterise aneurysms.
  • Validated score: The Ottawa SAH Rule on MDCalc can help identify patients with acute headache who require neuroimaging.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Subarachnoid haemorrhage"][/sc]
  • A 55-year-old female presents to the emergency department with a sudden-onset, excruciating headache, described as the "worst of her life," accompanied by neck stiffness and vomiting, developing over the last hour.
  • An acutely unwell middle-aged female with an abrupt, severe headache and signs of meningeal irritation, raising concern for an acute intracranial bleed.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Subarachnoid haemorrhage"][/sc]
  • Migraine - typically has a prodrome, gradual onset, and associated aura, unlike the abrupt thunderclap headache of SAH.
  • Tension headache - usually dull, bilateral, and pressure-like, lacking the sudden severity and associated neurological symptoms of SAH.
  • Meningitis/Encephalitis - presents with fever, headache, neck stiffness, and altered mental status, but SAH often lacks fever initially and has a more acute onset of severe headache.
  • Cerebral venous sinus thrombosis - can cause headache and neurological deficits, but often has a more subacute onset and may be associated with risk factors for thrombosis.
  • Intracerebral haemorrhage - bleeding within the brain parenchyma, often presenting with focal neurological deficits and headache, but typically without the diffuse meningeal irritation of SAH.
  • Dissection of cervical or intracranial artery - can cause headache, neck pain, and focal neurological symptoms, but often has a different character of pain and may be associated with trauma.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Subarachnoid haemorrhage"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Stabilisation of airway, breathing, and circulation; urgent pain management; blood pressure control to prevent rebleeding; and early neurosurgical or neurointerventional consultation.
  • Definitive management: Surgical clipping or endovascular coiling of the ruptured aneurysm to prevent rebleeding, performed as soon as clinically appropriate.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Close neurological monitoring for signs of vasospasm, hydrocephalus, and delayed cerebral ischaemia; regular follow-up imaging to assess for aneurysm recurrence or new aneurysms.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Educate patients and families on warning signs of complications; ensure clear pathways for rapid re-assessment if neurological deterioration occurs.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Subarachnoid haemorrhage"][/sc]
  • Rebleeding: The most immediate and devastating complication, occurring most frequently within the first 24 hours, significantly increasing mortality and morbidity.
  • Vasospasm and Delayed Cerebral Ischaemia (DCI): Narrowing of cerebral blood vessels, typically occurring 3-14 days post-SAH, leading to reduced blood flow and potential irreversible brain injury.
  • Hydrocephalus: Accumulation of cerebrospinal fluid due to impaired reabsorption or obstruction, leading to increased intracranial pressure, which can be acute or chronic.
  • Seizures: Can occur acutely due to cortical irritation from blood or as a long-term complication, impacting neurological recovery.
  • Hyponatraemia: Common electrolyte disturbance, often due to Syndrome of Inappropriate Antidiuretic Hormone (SIADH) or Cerebral Salt Wasting (CSW), requiring careful fluid and electrolyte management.
  • Cardiac complications: Neurogenic stunned myocardium, arrhythmias, and myocardial ischaemia can occur due to sympathetic surge following SAH, impacting overall prognosis.
