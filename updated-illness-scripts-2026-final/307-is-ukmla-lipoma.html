Lipoma [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Lipoma"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Lipoma"][/sc]
  • Lipomas are common, benign, slow-growing tumours composed of mature adipose tissue, typically encapsulated and located in the subcutaneous layer.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical features of a lipoma, differentiate it from other soft tissue lumps, and identify features suggestive of malignancy requiring urgent referral.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Lipoma"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Lipoma"][/sc]
  • Lipomas are the most common benign soft tissue tumour, with an estimated incidence of approximately 1 in 1,000 people in the general population.
  • They typically present in adults aged 40-60 years, with a slight male predominance, and can be solitary or multiple (e.g., in familial multiple lipomatosis).
  • Risk factors include genetics (e.g., familial multiple lipomatosis, Gardner syndrome), obesity, and trauma, though many occur sporadically.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Lipoma"][/sc]
  • Trigger: The exact trigger for sporadic lipoma formation is often unknown, but genetic predisposition, particularly chromosomal rearrangements (e.g., 12q13-15), and local trauma are implicated in some cases, leading to abnormal adipocyte proliferation.
  • Progression: Adipocytes within the lipoma proliferate slowly, accumulating fat and forming a well-circumscribed, encapsulated mass that expands within the subcutaneous tissue or deeper fascial planes.
  • Consequence: The slow, benign growth typically results in a soft, mobile, non-tender lump that rarely causes symptoms unless it grows large enough to compress nerves or other structures. Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Lipoma"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Lipomas typically present as small, asymptomatic subcutaneous nodules that grow very slowly over months to years, often unnoticed until they reach a palpable size.
  • Progressive phase: Growth continues gradually, with the lesion usually remaining soft, mobile, and non-tender. Significant or rapid growth, particularly over a few weeks or months, warrants further investigation.
  • Late phase: In rare instances, lipomas can grow to a large size, potentially causing cosmetic concerns, discomfort due to mass effect, or rarely, pain if nerve compression occurs.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Lipoma"][/sc]
  • Ask about lump presence - Lipomas are typically asymptomatic, so the primary symptom is the presence of a soft tissue lump, often noticed incidentally.
  • Ask about pain or tenderness - Lipomas are usually non-tender; pain or tenderness may suggest inflammation, rapid growth, or an alternative diagnosis such as angiolipoma or malignancy.
  • Ask about change in size or character - Slow, gradual growth is typical; rapid increase in size, change in consistency (e.g., becoming harder), or fixation to deeper structures are red flags for malignancy.
  • Ask about duration - Lipomas are slow-growing and often present for months or years; a newly appearing or rapidly growing lump requires careful assessment.
  • Ask about associated systemic symptoms - Absence of fever, weight loss, or malaise helps distinguish benign lipomas from infectious or malignant processes.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Lipoma"][/sc]
  • Check for a soft, rubbery, mobile, non-tender subcutaneous mass - This classic presentation is highly suggestive of a benign lipoma, indicating it is composed of fat and not fixed to underlying tissues.
  • Check for overlying skin changes - The skin over a lipoma is typically normal; discolouration, ulceration, or tethering may indicate inflammation, infection, or malignancy.
  • Check size and depth - Most lipomas are <5 cm and superficial; larger lesions (>5 cm) or those that feel deep-seated and fixed to fascia or muscle warrant suspicion for liposarcoma. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Distinguishing lipoma from liposarcoma: what are the key clinical features?" text="Lipoma vs. Liposarcoma"] [/sc]
  • Check for multiple lesions - The presence of multiple lipomas may suggest familial multiple lipomatosis or other syndromes like Madelung disease (symmetric lipomatosis).
  • Check vital signs - Normal vital signs are expected; fever or signs of systemic illness would point away from an uncomplicated lipoma.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Lipoma"][/sc]
  • Rapid growth: A sudden or significant increase in the size of the lump over weeks to a few months raises concern for malignancy, particularly liposarcoma.
  • Deep-seated or fixed lesion: Lumps that are not freely mobile, feel fixed to underlying fascia or muscle, or are located deep to the fascia are more likely to be malignant.
  • Size >5 cm: While not definitive, lipomas larger than 5 cm, especially if deep-seated, have a higher probability of being a liposarcoma and require further investigation.
  • Hard or firm consistency: A lump that feels hard or firm, rather than soft and rubbery, is atypical for a benign lipoma and should prompt urgent referral.
  • Pain or tenderness: Although some benign lesions can be painful, new or worsening pain associated with a soft tissue lump, particularly without clear inflammatory signs, is a red flag.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Lipoma"][/sc]
  • Madelung disease (Multiple Symmetric Lipomatosis): Characterised by large, unencapsulated fat masses symmetrically distributed around the neck, shoulders, and proximal limbs, predominantly affecting middle-aged men with a history of alcohol abuse. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Madelung disease: what are its key features and clinical significance?" text="Madelung disease"] [/sc]
  • Angiolipoma: A variant of lipoma containing a vascular component, often presenting as a painful subcutaneous nodule, typically on the trunk or extremities, and can be multiple.
  • Spinal lipoma: Rare, often congenital, lipomas within the spinal canal can cause neurological symptoms due to cord compression, requiring neurosurgical assessment.
  • Intramuscular lipoma: Located within muscle tissue, these can be less mobile and may be mistaken for other soft tissue tumours; imaging is crucial for diagnosis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Lipoma"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected lipoma: Clinical examination is usually sufficient for diagnosis of typical, superficial lipomas.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible or red flags are present:
    • Imaging - Ultrasound: Often the first-line investigation for soft tissue lumps, it can confirm the fatty nature of a lesion, assess its depth, vascularity, and delineate its margins, helping to differentiate benign lipomas from other masses.
    • Imaging - MRI: Indicated for larger, deep-seated, or complex lesions, or when ultrasound is inconclusive, to provide detailed anatomical information and better characterise the tissue, aiding in the differentiation from liposarcoma.
    • Biopsy: Fine needle aspiration (FNA) or core needle biopsy may be considered for suspicious lesions (e.g., rapidly growing, deep, fixed, >5cm, hard) to obtain histological confirmation and rule out malignancy. Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Lipoma"][/sc]
  • Liposarcoma - typically harder, fixed to deeper structures, often >5cm, and may show rapid growth or pain.
  • Epidermoid cyst - often has a punctum, may be fluctuant, and can become inflamed or infected, discharging keratinous material.
  • Sebaceous cyst - similar to epidermoid cyst, but arises from sebaceous glands.
  • Neurofibroma - may be associated with café-au-lait spots (Neurofibromatosis Type 1), often feels 'rubbery' or 'button-hole' sign, and can be tender.
  • Dermatofibroma - typically smaller, firm, often pigmented, and may show a 'dimple sign' when compressed.
  • Abscess - usually acutely painful, warm, erythematous, and fluctuant, often with systemic signs of infection.
  • Hernia - reducible, often increases in size with coughing or straining, and may be associated with a palpable defect in the abdominal wall. Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Lipoma"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Reassurance is often the primary management for asymptomatic, clinically benign lipomas. Education regarding red flag features for urgent review is crucial.
  • Definitive management: Surgical excision is the primary treatment for symptomatic lipomas (e.g., pain, cosmetic concern, functional impairment) or those where the diagnosis is uncertain and malignancy cannot be excluded clinically or radiologically.
  • Adjunctive management: For small, asymptomatic lipomas, a 'watch and wait' approach is often appropriate, with regular self-monitoring for changes.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Patients should be advised to seek medical attention if the lump changes in size, becomes painful, or develops other concerning features.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Any soft tissue lump with red flag features (rapid growth, deep-seated, fixed, >5cm, hard, painful) warrants urgent referral to a specialist (e.g., plastic surgery, orthopaedics, sarcoma service) for further investigation and management.
  • Exam pharmacology reference (UKMLA/MRCGP): No specific pharmacological treatments are typically indicated for benign lipomas. No doses or frequencies are cited here — always verify against your local antimicrobial/prescribing formulary before prescribing.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Lipoma"][/sc]
  • Cosmetic disfigurement: Larger or prominently located lipomas can cause significant cosmetic concerns, impacting quality of life, particularly if they continue to grow over time.
  • Pain or discomfort: While typically asymptomatic, lipomas can cause pain if they compress nerves, grow rapidly, or become inflamed, especially in later stages of growth.
  • Functional impairment: Depending on their size and location, lipomas can restrict movement or interfere with daily activities, particularly if they are large and located near joints or pressure points.
  • Malignant transformation (rare): Although extremely rare, a lipoma can, in theory, undergo malignant transformation into a liposarcoma, or a liposarcoma may be misdiagnosed as a benign lipoma initially, especially if red flag features are overlooked.
  • Recurrence post-excision: Incomplete surgical excision, though uncommon for simple lipomas, can lead to recurrence of the lesion, necessitating further intervention.
