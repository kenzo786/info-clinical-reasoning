Gastric cancer [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Gastric cancer"]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Gastric cancer"]
  • Gastric cancer, predominantly adenocarcinoma, arises from the stomach lining, often progressing from chronic inflammation to metaplasia, dysplasia, and invasive carcinoma.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the risk factors, common presentations, and key diagnostic and management principles for gastric cancer, including urgent referral criteria.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Gastric cancer"]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Gastric cancer"]
  • Gastric cancer is the 16th most common cause of cancer death in the UK, accounting for 2% of all cancer deaths (2021-2023 data) [1].
  • Approximately 6,600 new cases of stomach cancer are diagnosed annually in the UK (2017-2019 average) [2].
  • Key risk factors include chronic Helicobacter pylori infection, older age (over 50, with over half of diagnoses in those 75+), male sex, smoking, high salt intake, processed meats, obesity, and a family history of gastric cancer [3] [4].
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Gastric cancer"]
  • Trigger: Chronic inflammation of the gastric mucosa, most commonly due to Helicobacter pylori infection, initiates a cascade of cellular changes.
  • Progression: Persistent inflammation leads to atrophic gastritis, intestinal metaplasia, and dysplasia, often following the Correa cascade. The [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Lauren classification of gastric cancer: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Lauren classification"] divides gastric adenocarcinomas into intestinal (gland-forming, better prognosis) and diffuse (poorly cohesive, aggressive) types, reflecting different pathogenic pathways.
  • Consequence: Uncontrolled cellular proliferation and invasion through the gastric wall, with potential for local spread, lymphatic dissemination (e.g., [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Virchow node: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Virchow node"]), peritoneal metastasis (e.g., [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Krukenberg tumour: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Krukenberg tumour"]), and distant haematogenous spread (e.g., [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Sister Mary Joseph nodule: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Sister Mary Joseph nodule"] in umbilical region).
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Gastric cancer"]
  • Early phase: Often asymptomatic or presents with non-specific dyspeptic symptoms, such as indigestion or mild abdominal discomfort, which may be intermittent over weeks to months.
  • Progressive phase: As the tumour grows, symptoms become more persistent and severe, including early satiety, dysphagia, persistent vomiting, and unexplained weight loss, typically evolving over several months.
  • Late phase: Advanced disease manifests with features of obstruction, cachexia, gastrointestinal bleeding (anaemia), and signs of metastatic spread (e.g., ascites, jaundice, palpable lymphadenopathy), often developing over months to a year or more.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Gastric cancer"]
  • Ask about persistent dyspepsia - often non-specific, but new onset or worsening dyspepsia, particularly in those over 55, warrants investigation.
  • Ask about dysphagia or odynophagia - suggests involvement of the gastro-oesophageal junction or significant tumour bulk.
  • Ask about early satiety or post-prandial fullness - indicates reduced gastric capacity or outflow obstruction.
  • Ask about unexplained weight loss and anorexia - common in advanced disease due to metabolic changes and reduced intake.
  • Ask about persistent vomiting - may indicate gastric outlet obstruction.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Gastric cancer"]
  • Check vital signs - may reveal tachycardia or hypotension in cases of significant gastrointestinal bleeding, or fever if complicated by infection.
  • Check for anaemia (pallor) - often due to chronic blood loss from the tumour.
  • Check for palpable abdominal mass or hepatomegaly - suggests advanced local disease or liver metastases.
  • Check for supraclavicular lymphadenopathy (e.g., [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Virchow node: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Virchow node"]) - indicates lymphatic spread, particularly to the left supraclavicular fossa.
  • Check for periumbilical nodule (e.g., [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Sister Mary Joseph nodule: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Sister Mary Joseph nodule"]) or ovarian masses ([sc name="jitl-query" Q="Krukenberg tumour: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Krukenberg tumour"]) - signs of peritoneal dissemination.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Gastric cancer"]
  • Unexplained upper gastrointestinal bleeding: Indicates a potentially serious underlying pathology requiring urgent endoscopic evaluation.
  • Progressive dysphagia: Suggests luminal obstruction, often due to an oesophageal or gastro-oesophageal junctional tumour.
  • Unexplained weight loss with upper GI symptoms: Highly concerning for malignancy, particularly in older adults.
  • Persistent vomiting: May signal gastric outlet obstruction from an advanced tumour.
  • New onset dyspepsia in patients aged 55 or over: Triggers urgent referral for endoscopy as per NICE NG12 guidelines for suspected upper GI cancer.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Gastric cancer"]
  • Younger patients: May present with diffuse-type gastric cancer, which is often more aggressive and can be missed due to non-specific symptoms and lower index of suspicion.
  • Elderly patients: Non-specific symptoms like fatigue, anorexia, or general malaise may be attributed to age or comorbidities, delaying diagnosis.
  • Metastatic disease at presentation: Initial symptoms may relate to distant spread (e.g., jaundice from liver metastases, ascites from peritoneal carcinomatosis, bone pain from bone metastases) rather than primary gastric symptoms.
  • Paraneoplastic syndromes: Rarely, gastric cancer can present with syndromes such as acanthosis nigricans, Leser-Trélat sign, or Trousseau syndrome, which may precede gastrointestinal symptoms.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Gastric cancer"]
  • Core for suspected gastric cancer: Upper gastrointestinal endoscopy with biopsy is the gold standard for diagnosis and histological confirmation.
  • Severity/risk tools: While no specific severity score for gastric cancer diagnosis is widely used, performance status (e.g., ECOG or Karnofsky) is crucial for treatment planning.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Consider tests for peptic ulcer disease (e.g., H. pylori testing), gastro-oesophageal reflux disease, or functional dyspepsia.
  • Imaging - CT scan: Contrast-enhanced CT of the chest, abdomen, and pelvis is essential for staging, assessing local invasion, nodal involvement, and distant metastases.
  • Imaging - PET-CT: F-18 FDG PET-CT may be offered for oesophageal and gastro-oesophageal junctional tumours suitable for radical treatment, and considered for gastric cancer if metastatic disease is suspected to guide management [5].
  • Imaging - Staging laparoscopy: Recommended for all people with potentially curable gastric cancer to detect occult peritoneal metastases [5].
  • Serial reassessment: Regular follow-up imaging and clinical review are vital post-treatment to monitor for recurrence.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Gastric cancer"]
  • A 68-year-old male with a history of H. pylori infection presents with 3 months of progressive dysphagia, early satiety, and unintentional weight loss, alongside new onset epigastric pain and a palpable supraclavicular lymph node.
  • Elderly male with chronic inflammatory trigger and progressive obstructive symptoms, concerning for advanced gastric malignancy with lymphatic dissemination.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Gastric cancer"]
  • Peptic Ulcer Disease - typically responds to acid suppression; gastric cancer symptoms are often progressive and unresponsive.
  • Gastro-oesophageal Reflux Disease (GORD) - usually presents with heartburn and regurgitation, without significant weight loss or dysphagia unless complicated.
  • Oesophageal Cancer - shares many symptoms (dysphagia, weight loss) but primary site is oesophagus; endoscopy differentiates.
  • Pancreatic Cancer - can cause epigastric pain, weight loss, and jaundice, but often with back pain and different imaging findings.
  • Gastric Lymphoma - can mimic adenocarcinoma clinically and radiologically; biopsy with immunohistochemistry is diagnostic.
  • Achalasia - presents with dysphagia to solids and liquids, but typically a longer history and characteristic manometric findings.
  • Functional Dyspepsia - diagnosis of exclusion, lacks alarm features and structural abnormalities on investigation.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Gastric cancer"]
  • Immediate priorities: Recognition of alarm features, urgent referral to a specialist multidisciplinary team (MDT) for suspected upper GI cancer (NICE NG12), and supportive care including nutritional assessment and management.
  • Definitive management: For resectable disease, often includes neoadjuvant chemotherapy (or chemoradiotherapy) followed by radical gastrectomy with D2 lymphadenectomy. Adjuvant therapy may be considered post-surgery [5].
  • Adjunctive management: Endoscopic interventions (e.g., stenting for obstruction), pain management, and nutritional support (e.g., enteral feeding) may support symptom control and quality of life.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Regular surveillance with imaging and endoscopy is typically warranted post-treatment to detect recurrence.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Patients should be advised to seek urgent medical attention for worsening symptoms, new bleeding, or signs of obstruction.
  • Exam pharmacology reference (UKMLA/MRCGP): Chemotherapeutic agents often include platinum-based drugs (e.g., [sc name="jitl-medication" Q="Cisplatin use in Gastric cancer" text="cisplatin"]) and fluoropyrimidines (e.g., [sc name="jitl-medication" Q="Capecitabine use in Gastric cancer" text="capecitabine"] or [sc name="jitl-medication" Q="5-Fluorouracil use in Gastric cancer" text="5-fluorouracil"]). Trastuzumab may be considered for HER2-positive metastatic gastric cancer [5]. Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Gastric cancer"]
  • Gastrointestinal bleeding: Chronic low-grade bleeding can lead to iron deficiency anaemia, while acute severe haemorrhage can be life-threatening, often occurring in the progressive or late phases.
  • Gastric outlet obstruction: Tumour growth can block the pylorus, causing persistent vomiting, early satiety, and significant weight loss, typically in the progressive to late phases.
  • Perforation: Tumour invasion through the gastric wall can lead to peritonitis, a surgical emergency, more common in advanced disease.
  • Metastasis: Spread to distant organs (e.g., liver, lungs, bones, peritoneum) leads to organ dysfunction and systemic symptoms, characteristic of the late phase.
  • Cachexia: Profound weight loss, muscle wasting, and fatigue due to the systemic effects of advanced cancer, often seen in the late phase.
