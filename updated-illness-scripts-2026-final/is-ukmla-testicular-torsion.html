Testicular Torsion [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Testicular Torsion"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Testicular Torsion"][/sc]
  • Testicular torsion is a urological emergency caused by the twisting of the spermatic cord, leading to venous occlusion, arterial compromise, and subsequent testicular ischaemia.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical features of testicular torsion, understand its time-critical nature, and initiate immediate referral for surgical exploration to preserve testicular viability.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Testicular Torsion"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Testicular Torsion"][/sc]
  • The annual incidence in the USA is approximately 1 per 4,000 males younger than 25 years of age [1]. In England, there were 3,304 episodes of testicular torsion in 2013/14, with 2,501 occurring in children [2].
  • Testicular torsion can occur at any age, including prenatally, but has a bimodal distribution with peaks in infancy and adolescence (12-18 years, with a peak incidence between 13-16 years) [1] [3].
  • Risk factors include the 'bell-clapper deformity' (lack of normal testicular fixation allowing free rotation), cryptorchidism, pubertal changes, horizontal testicular lie, strenuous exercise, and a family history of torsion [3].
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Testicular Torsion"][/sc]
  • The primary trigger is the twisting of the spermatic cord, often due to an anatomical predisposition such as the [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Bell-clapper deformity: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="bell-clapper deformity" ][/sc], which allows the testis to rotate freely within the tunica vaginalis [3].
  • This twisting leads to progressive occlusion of the testicular blood vessels, initially affecting venous outflow, causing congestion and oedema, followed by arterial inflow compromise [4].
  • Sustained ischaemia results in irreversible damage to the testicular germ cells, potentially leading to testicular necrosis and atrophy if not promptly detorsed [4].
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Testicular Torsion"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Sudden onset of severe, unilateral scrotal pain, often accompanied by nausea and vomiting, typically within minutes to a few hours of onset.
  • Progressive phase: Within 4-6 hours, ischaemia progresses, leading to increasing pain, scrotal swelling, and tenderness. Testicular viability significantly decreases beyond this window.
  • Late phase: Beyond 6-8 hours, irreversible testicular damage and necrosis become highly probable, increasing the likelihood of orchiectomy and long-term complications such as atrophy and subfertility.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Testicular Torsion"][/sc]
  • Ask about sudden onset of severe, unilateral testicular pain - this is the cardinal symptom and distinguishes it from the more gradual onset of epididymo-orchitis [3].
  • Ask about associated nausea and vomiting - these systemic symptoms are common and increase the suspicion of torsion [3].
  • Ask about lower abdominal pain - referred pain from the scrotum can present as abdominal pain, necessitating testicular examination in all males with acute abdominal pain [3].
  • Ask about previous, transient episodes of similar pain - a history of intermittent torsion and detorsion suggests a predisposing anatomical anomaly [3].
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Testicular Torsion"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - typically normal in early torsion, but may show tachycardia or signs of systemic upset with severe pain or delayed presentation.
  • Check for a high-riding, swollen, and exquisitely tender testis - the affected testis is often retracted upwards due to spermatic cord shortening and is very painful to palpate [3].
  • Check for absence of the [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Cremasteric reflex: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="cremasteric reflex" ][/sc] - this reflex is typically absent on the affected side in testicular torsion, offering high sensitivity for diagnosis [3].
  • Check for a horizontal lie of the testis - the 'bell-clapper' position can result in the testis lying horizontally rather than vertically [3].
  • Check for scrotal reddening and oedema - these signs develop as ischaemia and inflammation progress [3].
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Testicular Torsion"][/sc]
  • Sudden onset severe unilateral scrotal pain: This is the most critical red flag, indicating a time-sensitive urological emergency requiring immediate assessment and intervention.
  • Absent cremasteric reflex: A highly sensitive clinical sign for testicular torsion, its absence necessitates urgent surgical referral.
  • High-riding or horizontal lie of the testis: Suggests rotation of the spermatic cord and is a strong indicator of torsion.
  • Pain duration exceeding 4-6 hours: Indicates a higher risk of irreversible testicular ischaemia and necrosis, necessitating even more urgent intervention.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Testicular Torsion"][/sc]
  • Neonates: May present with a firm, hard, non-transilluminable scrotal mass at birth, often without pain, due to [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Extravaginal torsion: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="extravaginal torsion" ][/sc] occurring in utero or perinatally [3].
  • Intermittent torsion: A history of recurrent, self-limiting episodes of severe scrotal pain suggests spontaneous torsion and detorsion, indicating an underlying anatomical predisposition [3].
  • Older adults: While less common, testicular torsion can occur in older men, and symptoms may be less acute or misdiagnosed as epididymitis, leading to delayed presentation and poorer outcomes.
  • Mild or gradual pain: Occasionally, symptoms may be milder or less acute, which can delay diagnosis and treatment, highlighting the importance of a high index of suspicion [3].
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Testicular Torsion"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected testicular torsion: Clinical assessment remains paramount. A history and physical examination highly suggestive of torsion should prompt immediate surgical exploration without delay for imaging [2].
  • Imaging - Doppler ultrasound: May be used to evaluate acute scrotum, demonstrating reduced or absent blood flow to the affected testis and potentially a 'whirlpool sign' of the spermatic cord [5]. However, imaging should not delay surgical intervention if clinical suspicion is high, as false negatives can occur, especially in early or intermittent torsion [2] [5].
  • Severity/risk tools: The Testicular Torsion Score (TTS) or TWIST score can aid in risk stratification, but should not replace clinical judgment or delay surgical referral for high-risk patients. For example, the TWIST score on MDCalc incorporates factors like testicular swelling, hardness, absent cremasteric reflex, nausea/vomiting, and high-riding testis to stratify risk [sc name="jitl-query" Q="TWIST score for testicular torsion: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="TWIST score" ][/sc].
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Urinalysis and urine culture may be considered to rule out urinary tract infection, particularly if epididymo-orchitis is suspected.
  • Serial reassessment: For equivocal cases where torsion is not definitively ruled out and immediate surgery is not pursued, close observation and repeated clinical examinations are crucial.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Testicular Torsion"][/sc]
  • A previously well 15-year-old male presents with sudden onset, severe left scrotal pain, associated with nausea and vomiting, and an absent left cremasteric reflex on examination.
  • An adolescent male with acute, severe, unilateral scrotal pain and absent cremasteric reflex, highly concerning for testicular torsion.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Testicular Torsion"][/sc]
  • Epididymo-orchitis - typically presents with more gradual onset of pain, often associated with dysuria or fever, and a preserved [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Prehn's sign: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="cremasteric reflex" ][/sc]; lifting the testis may relieve pain (Prehn's sign) [3].
  • Torsion of testicular appendage - more common in pre-pubertal boys (7-12 years), with localised tenderness to the upper pole of the testis and sometimes a 'blue dot sign' [3].
  • Incarcerated inguinal hernia - may present with scrotal swelling and pain, but careful examination of the inguinal canal usually reveals the hernia [3].
  • Testicular trauma - a clear history of injury precedes pain, though trauma can sometimes precipitate torsion.
  • Hydrocele - usually painless scrotal swelling that transilluminates [3].
  • Testicular tumour - typically presents as a painless scrotal mass, with pain being a rare feature [3].
  • Mumps orchitis - associated with parotid gland swelling and usually occurs post-pubertally in unvaccinated individuals [3].
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Testicular Torsion"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Recognition of the time-critical nature, immediate referral to the emergency urology or surgical team, withholding food, and providing adequate analgesia [3].
  • Definitive management: Urgent surgical exploration is the definitive treatment. This involves detorsion of the spermatic cord and bilateral orchidopexy (fixation of both testes to the scrotal wall) to prevent recurrence [3].
  • Adjunctive management: Manual detorsion may be attempted in the emergency department by external rotation of the testis, but this is a temporary measure and does not negate the need for surgical exploration and fixation [3].
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Post-operative monitoring for complications such as testicular atrophy and subfertility is important, with appropriate counselling for patients [3].
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Any suspicion of testicular torsion warrants immediate emergency referral; delays significantly impact testicular viability and future fertility [3].
  • Exam pharmacology reference (UKMLA/MRCGP): No specific drug knowledge is commonly tested for the acute management of testicular torsion, as it is primarily a surgical emergency. Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Testicular Torsion"][/sc]
  • Testicular infarction and necrosis: Occurs due to prolonged ischaemia (typically beyond 6-8 hours), leading to irreversible damage and often necessitating orchiectomy (testicular removal) [3].
  • Testicular atrophy: Even after successful detorsion, up to half of patients may develop testicular atrophy, impacting future endocrine and reproductive function [3].
  • Subfertility: Unilateral testicular torsion can seriously impair spermatogenesis, with subfertility found in a significant proportion of patients, even with timely intervention [3].
  • Recurrence: Without bilateral orchidopexy, there is a high risk of torsion recurring in either the affected or contralateral testis [3].
  • Infection: Necrotic testicular tissue can become a nidus for infection, potentially leading to epididymo-orchitis or abscess formation [3].
