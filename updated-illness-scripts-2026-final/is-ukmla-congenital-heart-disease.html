Congenital heart disease [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Congenital heart disease"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Congenital heart disease"][/sc]
  • Congenital heart disease (CHD) refers to structural abnormalities of the heart or great vessels that are present at birth. These defects arise from errors during cardiac development in utero.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the common presentations and initial management of congenital heart disease in neonates, infants, and children.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Congenital heart disease"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Congenital heart disease"][/sc]
  • Congenital heart disease affects approximately 8-10 per 1,000 live births in the UK, making it the most common type of birth defect [1].
  • Risk factors include maternal diabetes, rubella infection during pregnancy, certain genetic syndromes (e.g., Down syndrome), and a family history of CHD.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Congenital heart disease"][/sc]
  • Trigger: Abnormal cardiac development during the first trimester of pregnancy, often due to a combination of genetic and environmental factors.
  • Progression: Structural defects lead to altered blood flow dynamics, causing increased pressure or volume load on specific heart chambers or vessels.
  • Consequence: Over time, this can result in heart failure, pulmonary hypertension, cyanosis, or other systemic complications, depending on the specific defect.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Congenital heart disease"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Symptoms may be present at birth or develop within the first few days or weeks of life, particularly with severe defects like hypoplastic left heart syndrome or transposition of the great arteries.
  • Progressive phase: Less severe defects, such as a small ventricular septal defect, may remain asymptomatic for months or years, with symptoms gradually emerging as the child grows and cardiac demands increase.
  • Late phase: Uncorrected or palliated defects can lead to chronic complications like pulmonary hypertension or Eisenmenger syndrome, particularly beyond the first decade of life.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Congenital heart disease"][/sc]
  • Ask about poor feeding or fatigue during feeds - indicates increased work of breathing or cardiac output demands.
  • Ask about breathlessness or rapid breathing (tachypnoea) - suggests pulmonary congestion or increased pulmonary blood flow.
  • Ask about cyanosis (bluish discolouration of skin/lips) - signifies inadequate oxygenation due to right-to-left shunting or reduced pulmonary blood flow.
  • Ask about recurrent respiratory tract infections - children with increased pulmonary blood flow are more susceptible.
  • Ask about poor weight gain or failure to thrive - a common sign of chronic heart failure in infants.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Congenital heart disease"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - tachypnoea, tachycardia, hypotension, or oxygen desaturation are critical indicators of cardiac compromise.
  • Check for central or peripheral cyanosis - central cyanosis (lips, tongue) suggests significant desaturation, while peripheral (extremities) may be due to poor perfusion.
  • Check for heart murmurs - characteristic murmurs can indicate specific structural defects, though their absence does not rule out CHD.
  • Check for hepatomegaly or peripheral oedema - signs of right-sided heart failure.
  • Check for clubbing of digits - a chronic sign of hypoxaemia, often seen in older children with uncorrected cyanotic heart disease.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Congenital heart disease"][/sc]
  • Severe cyanosis at birth - may indicate duct-dependent lesions requiring urgent prostaglandin infusion.
  • Cardiogenic shock - presents with poor perfusion, hypotension, and metabolic acidosis, demanding immediate resuscitation.
  • Persistent tachypnoea and poor feeding in an infant - highly suggestive of heart failure, requiring prompt investigation.
  • New murmur with fever and malaise - raises concern for infective endocarditis, especially in children with known CHD.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Congenital heart disease"][/sc]
  • Older children with unrepaired defects: May present with exercise intolerance, arrhythmias, or features of pulmonary hypertension, rather than typical infant symptoms.
  • Coarctation of the aorta in neonates: Can present with sudden collapse and shock as the ductus arteriosus closes, leading to critical systemic hypoperfusion.
  • Small ventricular septal defects: Often asymptomatic and may be discovered incidentally during a routine examination, with the murmur being the only sign.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Congenital heart disease"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected Congenital Heart Disease:
    • Pulse oximetry: Screening for critical CHD in neonates.
    • Electrocardiogram (ECG): May show chamber enlargement, hypertrophy, or arrhythmias.
    • Chest X-ray (CXR): Can reveal cardiomegaly, pulmonary vascular markings, or specific cardiac contours (e.g., "boot-shaped" heart in Tetralogy of Fallot).
    • Echocardiography: The definitive diagnostic tool, providing detailed structural and functional assessment of the heart.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible:
    • Full blood count (FBC): To assess for anaemia or polycythaemia.
    • Blood gas analysis: To evaluate for metabolic acidosis in shock.
  • Imaging - Cardiac MRI/CT: Used for complex anatomy or when echocardiography is inconclusive, providing detailed anatomical information.
  • Serial reassessment: Regular clinical review and echocardiography are crucial for monitoring disease progression and assessing the timing of intervention.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Congenital heart disease"][/sc]
  • A 3-month-old male infant presents with a 2-week history of poor feeding, increased work of breathing, and failure to gain weight, with a loud pansystolic murmur heard on examination.
  • A cyanotic infant with a structural cardiac defect leading to altered haemodynamics, resulting in symptoms of heart failure and impaired growth.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Congenital heart disease"][/sc]
  • Respiratory distress syndrome - typically presents at birth with respiratory symptoms, but without a primary cardiac murmur.
  • Sepsis - can cause poor feeding and lethargy, but usually associated with fever and other systemic signs of infection.
  • Metabolic disorders - may cause poor feeding and lethargy, but often have specific biochemical abnormalities and lack cardiac signs.
  • Persistent pulmonary hypertension of the newborn (PPHN) - presents with cyanosis and respiratory distress, but echocardiography shows structurally normal heart with elevated pulmonary pressures.
  • Transient tachypnoea of the newborn (TTN) - self-limiting respiratory distress in term infants, usually resolving within 24-48 hours.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Congenital heart disease"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities:
    • Stabilisation of airway, breathing, and circulation.
    • Oxygen therapy for hypoxaemia.
    • Prostaglandin E1 infusion for duct-dependent lesions to maintain ductal patency.
    • Diuretics (e.g., furosemide) for pulmonary oedema in heart failure.
  • Definitive management:
    • Surgical repair or palliation of the cardiac defect (e.g., closure of VSD, arterial switch operation for TGA).
    • Transcatheter interventions (e.g., balloon valvuloplasty, device closure of ASD/VSD).
  • Monitoring and follow-up:
    • Regular cardiology follow-up with clinical assessment and echocardiography.
    • Monitoring for complications such as arrhythmias, pulmonary hypertension, and infective endocarditis.
  • Escalation and safety-netting:
    • Educate parents on signs of worsening heart failure or cyanosis, and when to seek urgent medical attention.
    • Referral to specialist paediatric cardiology services for ongoing management.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Congenital heart disease"][/sc]
  • Heart failure: Due to chronic volume or pressure overload, leading to symptoms like tachypnoea, poor feeding, and hepatomegaly.
  • Pulmonary hypertension: Can develop from increased pulmonary blood flow or obstruction, potentially leading to irreversible vascular changes.
  • Cyanotic spells: Acute episodes of profound cyanosis and hypoxaemia, particularly in conditions like Tetralogy of Fallot, requiring urgent intervention.
  • Infective endocarditis: Increased risk in patients with structural heart defects, requiring prophylactic measures in some cases.
  • Growth and developmental delay: Chronic illness and increased metabolic demands can impair physical growth and neurodevelopment.
