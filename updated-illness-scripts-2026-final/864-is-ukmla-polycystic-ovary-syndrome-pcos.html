Polycystic ovary syndrome (PCOS) [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Polycystic ovary syndrome (PCOS)"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Polycystic ovary syndrome (PCOS)"][/sc]
  • Polycystic ovary syndrome (PCOS) is a common, complex endocrine disorder affecting women of reproductive age, characterised by a combination of oligo-anovulation, clinical or biochemical hyperandrogenism, and polycystic ovaries on ultrasound, after exclusion of other aetiologies.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical features, diagnostic criteria, and long-term health implications of PCOS, and outline initial management strategies.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Polycystic ovary syndrome (PCOS)"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Polycystic ovary syndrome (PCOS)"][/sc]
  • PCOS affects approximately 5-10% of women of reproductive age in the UK, making it one of the most common endocrine disorders in this demographic. [1]
  • Risk factors include a family history of PCOS, type 2 diabetes, or premature baldness, and obesity, which can exacerbate symptoms and increase metabolic risks.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Polycystic ovary syndrome (PCOS)"][/sc]
  • Trigger: The exact aetiology is multifactorial, involving genetic predisposition and environmental factors, leading to a primary defect in insulin signalling and/or ovarian steroidogenesis.
  • Progression: Insulin resistance leads to compensatory hyperinsulinaemia, which stimulates ovarian androgen production and reduces hepatic sex hormone-binding globulin (SHBG) synthesis, increasing free androgen levels. This, coupled with abnormal gonadotrophin secretion (elevated LH:FSH ratio), disrupts follicular development, leading to anovulation and the characteristic polycystic ovarian morphology. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="LH:FSH ratio in PCOS: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="LH:FSH ratio"][/sc]
  • Consequence: Chronic anovulation results in irregular periods and infertility, while hyperandrogenism manifests as hirsutism, acne, and androgenic alopecia. Long-term, PCOS increases the risk of metabolic syndrome, type 2 diabetes, cardiovascular disease, and endometrial hyperplasia/carcinoma.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Polycystic ovary syndrome (PCOS)"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Symptoms often emerge around puberty or in early adulthood, typically presenting with menstrual irregularities (oligomenorrhoea or amenorrhoea) and signs of hyperandrogenism such as acne and hirsutism.
  • Progressive phase: Over months to years, metabolic complications like insulin resistance and weight gain may become more prominent, exacerbating hormonal imbalances and increasing the risk of type 2 diabetes and cardiovascular issues.
  • Late phase: Long-term complications, particularly beyond 30-40 years of age, can include increased risk of endometrial cancer due to unopposed oestrogen, and continued challenges with fertility and metabolic health.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Polycystic ovary syndrome (PCOS)"][/sc]
  • Ask about menstrual irregularities - oligomenorrhoea (infrequent periods) or amenorrhoea (absence of periods) are key indicators of anovulation.
  • Ask about hirsutism - excess coarse hair growth in a male pattern (e.g., upper lip, chin, chest, abdomen) suggests hyperandrogenism.
  • Ask about acne - persistent, often severe, acne unresponsive to conventional treatments can be a sign of elevated androgen levels.
  • Ask about subfertility or infertility - difficulty conceiving is common due to irregular or absent ovulation.
  • Ask about weight gain or difficulty losing weight - often associated with insulin resistance, which is a common feature of PCOS.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Polycystic ovary syndrome (PCOS)"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - blood pressure may be elevated due to increased cardiovascular risk associated with metabolic syndrome.
  • Check for hirsutism - objectively assess hair growth using a modified Ferriman-Gallwey score, indicating clinical hyperandrogenism.
  • Check for acne - particularly on the face, chest, and back, noting severity and distribution.
  • Check for acanthosis nigricans - darkened, thickened skin, especially in skin folds (neck, axillae, groin), which is a cutaneous marker of insulin resistance. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Acanthosis nigricans: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Acanthosis nigricans"][/sc]
  • Check for male-pattern hair loss - thinning of hair on the scalp, particularly at the temples and crown, indicative of androgenic alopecia.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Polycystic ovary syndrome (PCOS)"][/sc]
  • Rapid onset of symptoms: A sudden development of hirsutism or menstrual irregularities, particularly if severe, may suggest an androgen-secreting tumour rather than typical PCOS.
  • Virilisation: Features such as clitoromegaly, deepening voice, or significant muscle mass increase are uncommon in PCOS and warrant urgent investigation for alternative androgen excess causes.
  • Unexplained weight loss: While weight gain is common in PCOS, unexplained weight loss alongside other symptoms should prompt consideration of other endocrine disorders or malignancy.
  • Severe, unremitting abdominal pain: Could indicate ovarian torsion or other acute gynaecological pathology, requiring immediate assessment.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Polycystic ovary syndrome (PCOS)"][/sc]
  • Adolescents: Diagnosis can be challenging as menstrual irregularity is common in early post-menarcheal years. Diagnosis requires both hyperandrogenism and irregular menstrual cycles, and should not be made within 2 years of menarche. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="PCOS diagnosis in adolescents: what are the specific criteria and why do they matter clinically?" text="PCOS in adolescents"][/sc]
  • Post-menopausal women: Menstrual irregularities are no longer a diagnostic criterion. Diagnosis relies on evidence of hyperandrogenism and a history consistent with PCOS prior to menopause.
  • Lean PCOS: Women with PCOS can have a normal BMI, but still exhibit insulin resistance and other hormonal features, highlighting that obesity is not a prerequisite for diagnosis.
  • PCOS without polycystic ovaries on USS: Approximately 20-30% of women meeting other Rotterdam criteria may not have polycystic ovarian morphology on ultrasound, emphasizing the importance of clinical and biochemical criteria.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Polycystic ovary syndrome (PCOS)"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected PCOS: Diagnosis is based on the Rotterdam criteria (presence of at least two of: oligo-anovulation, clinical/biochemical hyperandrogenism, polycystic ovaries on ultrasound), after exclusion of other causes. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Rotterdam criteria for PCOS: what are they and why do they matter clinically?" text="Rotterdam criteria"][/sc]
  • Biochemical assessment:
    • Serum total and free testosterone: to assess for hyperandrogenism.
    • Sex hormone-binding globulin (SHBG): to calculate free androgen index (FAI).
    • Luteinising hormone (LH) and Follicle-stimulating hormone (FSH): an elevated LH:FSH ratio (>2:1 or 3:1) is often seen but is not diagnostic.
    • Thyroid function tests, prolactin, 17-hydroxyprogesterone: to exclude other endocrine disorders.
  • Imaging - Transvaginal ultrasound (TVS): To assess ovarian morphology (≥12 follicles 2-9mm in diameter in each ovary, and/or ovarian volume >10ml).
  • Severity/risk tools: Consider using the PCOS Risk Assessment on MDCalc to evaluate metabolic and cardiovascular risk factors.
  • Serial reassessment: Regular monitoring of metabolic parameters (e.g., HbA1c, lipid profile, blood pressure) is crucial due to increased long-term risks.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Polycystic ovary syndrome (PCOS)"][/sc]
  • Hypothalamic amenorrhoea - distinguished by low BMI, excessive exercise, or stress, with low gonadotrophin levels.
  • Thyroid dysfunction (hypothyroidism) - differentiated by abnormal TSH and free T4 levels, and other systemic symptoms.
  • Hyperprolactinaemia - excluded by elevated prolactin levels, often with galactorrhoea.
  • Non-classical congenital adrenal hyperplasia (NCCAH) - distinguished by elevated 17-hydroxyprogesterone levels, particularly after ACTH stimulation. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Non-classical congenital adrenal hyperplasia: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="NCCAH"][/sc]
  • Androgen-secreting tumours (ovarian or adrenal) - suggested by rapid onset of severe hyperandrogenism or virilisation, with markedly elevated testosterone levels.
  • Cushing\'s syndrome - differentiated by clinical features of cortisol excess (e.g., central obesity, striae, proximal myopathy) and abnormal cortisol rhythm.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Polycystic ovary syndrome (PCOS)"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Recognition of the syndrome and assessment of individual patient concerns (e.g., fertility, cosmetic, metabolic). Lifestyle modifications, including diet and exercise, are foundational for all women with PCOS, particularly those with overweight or obesity.
  • Definitive management: Management is symptomatic and aims to address specific concerns. For menstrual irregularities and hyperandrogenism, combined oral contraceptives (COCs) are often considered. For infertility, ovulation induction agents like clomiphene citrate or letrozole may be used.
  • Adjunctive management: Metformin may be considered for women with PCOS and a BMI >25 kg/m² who have not responded to lifestyle changes, particularly if they have impaired glucose tolerance or are at high risk of type 2 diabetes.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Regular monitoring for metabolic complications (e.g., glucose tolerance, lipid profile, blood pressure) and psychological well-being is essential. Annual review of symptoms and risk factors is typically warranted.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Referral to specialist endocrinology, gynaecology, or fertility services may be required for complex cases, persistent symptoms, or those failing to conceive. Patients should be advised on symptoms requiring urgent medical attention.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Polycystic ovary syndrome (PCOS)"][/sc]
  • Infertility: Chronic anovulation is a primary cause of subfertility or infertility, impacting reproductive planning.
  • Type 2 Diabetes Mellitus: Insulin resistance, a core feature of PCOS, significantly increases the risk of developing type 2 diabetes over months to years.
  • Cardiovascular Disease: Increased prevalence of metabolic syndrome (hypertension, dyslipidaemia, obesity) contributes to a higher long-term risk of cardiovascular events, particularly beyond 30-40 years of age.
  • Endometrial Hyperplasia and Carcinoma: Unopposed oestrogen stimulation due to chronic anovulation increases the risk of endometrial thickening and, in the late phase, endometrial cancer.
  • Obstructive Sleep Apnoea: More common in women with PCOS, particularly those with obesity, contributing to fatigue and cardiovascular risk.
  • Psychological Impact: Symptoms like hirsutism, acne, weight issues, and infertility can lead to significant anxiety, depression, and reduced quality of life.
