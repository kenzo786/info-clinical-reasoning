Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Acute glaucoma (Eye)"][/sc]
  • Acute angle-closure glaucoma (AACG) is an ophthalmological emergency characterised by a rapid and significant increase in intraocular pressure (IOP) due to sudden obstruction of aqueous humour outflow.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the signs and symptoms of acute angle-closure glaucoma, understand its urgent nature, and initiate appropriate immediate management and referral.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Acute glaucoma (Eye)"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Acute glaucoma (Eye)"][/sc]
  • Primary angle-closure glaucoma (PACG), which includes AACG, affects approximately 0.4% of white European adults over 40 in the UK, accounting for about 130,000 individuals [6].
  • The incidence of acute angle-closure crises in European populations is typically reported between 2–5 per 100,000 per year [18] [19].
  • Risk factors include increasing age, female sex (approximately 3:1 female to male ratio), Asian ethnicity (three-fold higher incidence in Chinese patients compared to Caucasians), shallow anterior chamber depth, and a family history of angle-closure disease [12] [9].
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Acute glaucoma (Eye)"][/sc]
  • Trigger: Pupil block, often due to apposition of the iris to the lens, impedes the flow of aqueous humour from the posterior to the anterior chamber, leading to a build-up of pressure behind the iris.
  • Progression: The increased pressure in the posterior chamber causes the peripheral iris to bow forward, obstructing the [sc name="jitl-query" Q="trabecular meshwork: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="trabecular meshwork"][/sc] and blocking the [sc name="jitl-query" Q="aqueous outflow pathway: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="aqueous outflow pathway"] [/sc] at the iridocorneal angle.
  • Consequence: This sudden and complete closure of the angle results in a rapid and severe elevation of intraocular pressure, leading to ischaemic damage to the optic nerve and retina if not promptly relieved.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Acute glaucoma (Eye)"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Symptoms typically have a sudden onset, developing over minutes to hours, characterised by severe eye pain, blurred vision, and headache.
  • Progressive phase: Without intervention, the intraocular pressure remains extremely high, leading to progressive optic nerve damage and irreversible vision loss within hours to days.
  • Late phase: Prolonged or recurrent attacks can result in chronic angle-closure glaucoma, with persistent visual field defects and potential for complete blindness, particularly beyond 24-48 hours of unrelieved high IOP.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Acute glaucoma (Eye)"][/sc]
  • Ask about sudden, severe unilateral eye pain - this is a hallmark symptom and indicates significant IOP elevation.
  • Ask about blurred vision or sudden vision loss - often described as \'misty\' vision, due to corneal oedema from high IOP.
  • Ask about seeing halos or rainbows around lights - a classic symptom caused by light diffraction through corneal oedema.
  • Ask about headache, nausea, and vomiting - these systemic symptoms are common due to vagal stimulation from severe ocular pain and high IOP.
  • Ask about a history of previous transient episodes of similar symptoms - suggestive of intermittent angle closure, which can precede an acute attack.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Acute glaucoma (Eye)"][/sc]
  • Check visual acuity - typically markedly reduced in the affected eye.
  • Check intraocular pressure (IOP) - usually very high, often >40 mmHg, which is critical for diagnosis.
  • Check pupil reaction - the pupil is typically fixed, mid-dilated (4-6 mm), and unreactive to light due to ischaemia of the iris sphincter.
  • Check conjunctival injection - diffuse redness of the conjunctiva, often with ciliary flush (perilimbal injection).
  • Check corneal clarity - often hazy or \'steamy\' due to epithelial oedema, which can obscure anterior chamber details.
  • Check vital signs - may reveal bradycardia or hypotension due to vagal response, or tachycardia/hypertension from pain.
  • Check for [sc name="jitl-query" Q="ciliary flush: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="ciliary flush"][/sc] - a perilimbal injection indicating inflammation of the ciliary body or iris.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Acute glaucoma (Eye)"][/sc]
  • Sudden, severe unilateral eye pain with blurred vision: Indicates an acute ocular emergency requiring immediate ophthalmological assessment to prevent irreversible vision loss.
  • Fixed, mid-dilated pupil: A classic sign of acute angle-closure, signifying iris ischaemia and high intraocular pressure.
  • Halos around lights and corneal oedema: Suggestive of significantly elevated IOP causing corneal decompensation.
  • Nausea and vomiting with ocular symptoms: Systemic symptoms accompanying severe eye pain should raise suspicion for AACG, distinguishing it from less urgent ocular conditions.
  • Rapidly worsening vision over hours: Implies ongoing damage to the optic nerve due to sustained high IOP, necessitating urgent intervention.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Acute glaucoma (Eye)"][/sc]
  • Subacute angle-closure: Patients may experience recurrent, transient episodes of mild pain, blurred vision, or halos, which resolve spontaneously. These can precede a full acute attack and are often missed.
  • Chronic angle-closure: Gradual closure of the angle may occur without acute symptoms, leading to progressive optic nerve damage and visual field loss, similar to open-angle glaucoma.
  • Drug-induced angle-closure: Certain medications, such as topiramate or some antidepressants, can induce acute angle-closure through mechanisms other than pupil block (e.g., ciliary body oedema), presenting with bilateral involvement and often a shallower anterior chamber.
  • Post-operative angle-closure: Can occur after intraocular surgery, particularly cataract surgery, due to various mechanisms including aqueous misdirection or pupillary block.
  • Plateau iris syndrome: An anatomical variant where the ciliary body is anteriorly rotated, pushing the peripheral iris forward and predisposing to angle closure, even after a patent iridotomy [sc name="jitl-query" Q="plateau iris syndrome: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="plateau iris syndrome"][/sc].
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Acute glaucoma (Eye)"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected acute angle-closure:
    • Tonometry: Measurement of intraocular pressure (IOP) is crucial, typically revealing significantly elevated levels (>21 mmHg, often >40 mmHg).
    • Slit-lamp examination: Assesses corneal oedema, anterior chamber depth (often shallow), pupil size and reactivity, and presence of cells/flare.
    • Gonioscopy: The definitive diagnostic tool to directly visualise the iridocorneal angle and confirm angle closure [sc name="jitl-query" Q="gonioscopy: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="gonioscopy"][/sc].
  • Severity/risk tools:
    • No specific severity/risk score is routinely used for acute angle-closure glaucoma in the acute setting; clinical assessment of IOP and optic nerve status guides management.
  • Imaging - Ocular Coherence Tomography (OCT):
    • Anterior segment OCT (AS-OCT): Provides detailed cross-sectional images of the anterior chamber angle, aiding in the assessment of angle configuration and extent of closure, particularly useful for documentation and follow-up [750] [sc name="jitl-query" Q="anterior segment OCT: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="AS-OCT"][/sc].
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible:
    • Consider B-scan ultrasonography if posterior segment pathology is suspected or if corneal oedema precludes adequate visualisation.

  • Elderly female with acute painful red eye, fixed mid-dilated pupil, and high IOP, concerning for acute angle-closure glaucoma.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Acute glaucoma (Eye)"][/sc]
  • Acute iritis/uveitis: Typically presents with pain, photophobia, and ciliary injection, but usually has a small, reactive pupil and normal or low IOP.
  • Corneal abrasion/foreign body: Characterised by sudden pain, photophobia, and foreign body sensation, but usually with a normal pupil and IOP.
  • Conjunctivitis: Presents with redness, discharge, and irritation, but typically no severe pain, normal vision, and normal IOP.
  • Migraine with aura: Can cause headache and visual disturbances (halos, zigzags), but usually without eye pain, redness, or elevated IOP.
  • Temporal arteritis: May cause headache and vision loss, particularly in older patients, but typically without acute eye pain or elevated IOP, and often associated with jaw claudication and scalp tenderness.
  • Orbital cellulitis: Presents with painful eye movements, proptosis, and eyelid swelling, but usually with normal IOP and no fixed, mid-dilated pupil.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Acute glaucoma (Eye)"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Recognition of acute angle-closure glaucoma as an ophthalmological emergency, immediate referral to ophthalmology, and initiation of measures to lower intraocular pressure.
  • Definitive management: Often involves laser peripheral iridotomy (LPI) to create an alternative pathway for aqueous flow, or clear lens extraction in some cases, as guided by ophthalmology.
  • Adjunctive management: Systemic and topical medications may be used to rapidly reduce IOP, including topical beta-blockers, alpha-agonists, carbonic anhydrase inhibitors, and systemic acetazolamide.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Close monitoring of IOP and visual function is essential, with regular ophthalmological follow-up to assess for recurrence or progression to chronic glaucoma.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Patients should be advised of the symptoms of recurrence and instructed to seek immediate medical attention if they occur.
  • Exam pharmacology reference (UKMLA/MRCGP):
    • [sc name="jitl-medication" Q="Pilocarpine use in Acute Angle-Closure Glaucoma" text="Pilocarpine"] [/sc]: A topical miotic that may be used to constrict the pupil, pulling the iris away from the angle, but should be used with caution as it can worsen pupil block in some mechanisms.
    • [sc name="jitl-medication" Q="Acetazolamide use in Acute Angle-Closure Glaucoma" text="Acetazolamide"] [/sc]: A systemic carbonic anhydrase inhibitor that reduces aqueous humour production, rapidly lowering IOP.
    • [sc name="jitl-medication" Q="Timolol use in Acute Angle-Closure Glaucoma" text="Timolol"] [/sc]: A topical beta-blocker that reduces aqueous humour production.
    • [sc name="jitl-medication" Q="Brimonidine use in Acute Angle-Closure Glaucoma" text="Brimonidine"] [/sc]: A topical alpha-agonist that reduces aqueous humour production and increases uveoscleral outflow.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Acute glaucoma (Eye)"][/sc]
  • Irreversible vision loss: Sustained high intraocular pressure rapidly damages the optic nerve, leading to permanent visual field defects and potentially blindness within hours if not treated promptly.
  • Corneal decompensation: Prolonged corneal oedema due to high IOP can lead to bullous keratopathy and permanent corneal scarring, affecting visual clarity.
  • Posterior synechiae: Inflammation and iris ischaemia can cause adhesions between the iris and the lens, leading to pupil distortion and further obstruction of aqueous flow.
  • Cataract formation: Acute attacks can accelerate the development of cataracts, further impairing vision.
  • Recurrent attacks: Without definitive treatment, there is a high risk of recurrent acute angle-closure episodes in the affected eye and often in the fellow eye.
