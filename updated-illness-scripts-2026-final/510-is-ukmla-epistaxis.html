Epistaxis [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Epistaxis"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Epistaxis"][/sc]
  • Epistaxis, commonly known as a nosebleed, is the loss of blood from the tissue lining the inside of the nose. It is typically classified as anterior or posterior based on the bleeding site.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the common causes and risk factors for epistaxis, differentiate between anterior and posterior bleeds, and outline the immediate first-aid management and indications for further medical intervention.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Epistaxis"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Epistaxis"][/sc]
  • Epistaxis is a common condition, with an estimated lifetime incidence of up to 60% in the general population, though only 6% seek medical attention [BMJ Best Practice].
  • It exhibits a bimodal age distribution, occurring more frequently in children (2-10 years) and older adults (50-80 years) [BMJ Best Practice].
  • Risk factors include dry weather, nasal mucosal inflammation, minor trauma (e.g., nose picking), nasal foreign bodies, and certain medications such as anticoagulants and antiplatelet agents [BMJ Best Practice].
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Epistaxis"][/sc]
  • Trigger: Disruption of the fragile nasal mucosa, often due to trauma, inflammation, or dryness, exposes underlying blood vessels.
  • Progression: Bleeding typically originates from the Kiesselbach plexus (Little\'s area) in the anterior nasal septum for anterior bleeds, or from deeper vessels like the sphenopalatine artery for more severe posterior bleeds [BMJ Best Practice]. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Kiesselbach plexus: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Kiesselbach plexus"][/sc]
  • Consequence: Uncontrolled bleeding can lead to significant blood loss, aspiration, and in severe cases, hypovolaemic shock, particularly with posterior epistaxis.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Epistaxis"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Most epistaxis episodes are self-limiting and resolve spontaneously or with simple first-aid measures within 10-20 minutes.
  • Progressive phase: If bleeding persists beyond 20 minutes despite adequate first aid, it suggests a more significant bleed, potentially requiring medical intervention such as cautery or nasal packing.
  • Late phase: Recurrent epistaxis, particularly beyond 24-48 hours after initial control, may indicate underlying systemic causes, inadequate initial management, or persistent local factors requiring further investigation.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Epistaxis"][/sc]
  • Ask about unilateral or bilateral bleeding - helps differentiate between anterior (often unilateral) and posterior (can be bilateral or present as haematemesis/melaena) sources.
  • Ask about duration and frequency of bleeding - prolonged or recurrent episodes may suggest underlying coagulopathy or persistent local factors.
  • Ask about recent nasal trauma or nose picking - common precipitating factors for anterior epistaxis.
  • Ask about medications, especially anticoagulants or antiplatelet agents - these significantly increase bleeding risk and severity.
  • Ask about symptoms of anaemia (e.g., dizziness, fatigue) - indicates significant blood loss, particularly with recurrent or severe bleeds.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Epistaxis"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs (heart rate, blood pressure, respiratory rate) - to assess for hypovolaemia, especially in severe or prolonged bleeding.
  • Check for active bleeding from the anterior nares - confirms anterior epistaxis, often visible on examination.
  • Check for blood in the oropharynx or haematemesis - suggests posterior epistaxis, where blood drains posteriorly. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Posterior epistaxis signs: what are they and why do they matter clinically?" text="Posterior epistaxis signs"][/sc]
  • Check for nasal mucosal dryness, crusting, or septal deviation - common local factors contributing to epistaxis.
  • Check for signs of underlying systemic disease (e.g., petechiae, purpura, telangiectasias) - may indicate coagulopathy or hereditary haemorrhagic telangiectasia.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Epistaxis"][/sc]
  • Persistent bleeding: Bleeding that continues for more than 20 minutes despite correct first-aid measures, indicating a potentially severe or posterior bleed.
  • Haemodynamic instability: Signs of shock (e.g., tachycardia, hypotension, pallor, altered mental status), suggesting significant blood loss requiring urgent resuscitation.
  • Airway compromise: Difficulty breathing, stridor, or aspiration of blood, which necessitates immediate airway management.
  • Recurrent severe epistaxis: Repeated episodes of heavy bleeding, especially without an obvious local cause, warranting investigation for underlying systemic conditions.
  • Bleeding from other sites: Concomitant bleeding from gums, bruising, or prolonged bleeding after minor trauma, raising suspicion for a coagulopathy.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Epistaxis"][/sc]
  • Children: Often presents as recurrent, self-limiting anterior bleeds due to digital trauma or nasal dryness. Posterior bleeds are rare but can be more challenging to manage.
  • Older adults: More prone to severe and posterior epistaxis due to fragile vessels, atherosclerosis, and increased use of anticoagulants/antiplatelets. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Posterior epistaxis: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Posterior epistaxis"][/sc]
  • Patients on anticoagulants/antiplatelets: Bleeding may be more prolonged, profuse, and difficult to control, even from minor trauma.
  • Hereditary haemorrhagic telangiectasia (HHT): Presents with recurrent, often severe epistaxis from childhood, associated with mucocutaneous telangiectasias and visceral arteriovenous malformations.
  • Nasal tumours: Unilateral, persistent, or recurrent epistaxis, sometimes associated with nasal obstruction, pain, or facial swelling, should raise suspicion for malignancy.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Epistaxis"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected epistaxis: Clinical diagnosis based on history and direct visualisation of the bleeding site (anterior rhinoscopy).
  • Severity/risk tools: No widely validated severity score for epistaxis, but assessment of haemodynamic stability and estimated blood loss guides management. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Epistaxis severity assessment: what factors are considered?" text="Epistaxis severity assessment"][/sc]
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible or bleeding is severe/recurrent:
    • Full blood count (FBC) - to assess for anaemia and platelet count.
    • Coagulation screen (INR, APTT) - to evaluate for coagulopathy, especially in patients on anticoagulants or with a bleeding diathesis.
    • Group and save - for potential blood transfusion in severe cases.
  • Imaging - Nasal endoscopy: May be performed by ENT specialists to identify the bleeding source, particularly in posterior or recurrent epistaxis, or to rule out underlying pathology.
  • Serial reassessment: Ongoing monitoring of vital signs and bleeding status is crucial, especially after initial management, to detect re-bleeding or signs of deterioration.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Epistaxis"][/sc]
  • A 68-year-old male on warfarin presents with sudden onset, profuse right-sided epistaxis for 30 minutes, associated with dizziness and a blood pressure of 90/60 mmHg.
  • An elderly male with a history of anticoagulation and haemodynamic instability, concerning for severe posterior epistaxis with significant blood loss.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Epistaxis"][/sc]
  • Haemoptysis: Coughing up blood from the respiratory tract, distinguished by frothy sputum and respiratory symptoms, whereas epistaxis typically presents as blood from the nose or draining posteriorly.
  • Haematemesis: Vomiting blood from the gastrointestinal tract, often dark and mixed with food particles, but can be confused with swallowed blood from severe posterior epistaxis.
  • Nasal foreign body: More common in children, presenting with unilateral foul-smelling discharge and epistaxis, often visible on examination.
  • Nasal polyps: Can cause nasal obstruction and occasional epistaxis, but typically associated with chronic rhinosinusitis symptoms.
  • Rhinosinusitis: Nasal inflammation and discharge, sometimes with blood-tinged mucus, but usually not frank epistaxis unless severe.
  • Coagulopathy (e.g., haemophilia, thrombocytopenia): Presents with epistaxis alongside other bleeding manifestations (e.g., bruising, prolonged bleeding), often with abnormal coagulation tests. Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Epistaxis"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities:
    • Ensure airway patency and assess breathing and circulation (ABCDE approach).
    • Position the patient sitting upright, leaning forward, and pinching the soft part of the nose continuously for 10-15 minutes.
    • Spit out any blood into a bowl to prevent swallowing and subsequent nausea/vomiting.
    • Consider applying ice packs to the bridge of the nose or neck, though evidence for efficacy is limited.
  • Definitive management:
    • If first aid fails, identify the bleeding point via anterior rhinoscopy.
    • Chemical cautery (e.g., silver nitrate) for visible anterior bleeding points.
    • Nasal packing (e.g., Merocel, Rapid Rhino) for persistent anterior bleeds or suspected posterior bleeds.
    • Referral to ENT for endoscopic cautery, posterior packing, or surgical ligation of vessels for severe, refractory posterior epistaxis.
  • Adjunctive management:
    • Reversal of anticoagulation if clinically appropriate and discussed with haematology/senior medical team.
    • Analgesia for discomfort from packing.
    • Prophylactic antibiotics may be considered for patients with nasal packing to reduce the risk of toxic shock syndrome, though not routinely recommended.
  • Monitoring and follow-up:
    • Monitor vital signs and observe for re-bleeding after intervention.
    • Discharge advice should include avoiding nose picking, strenuous activity, hot drinks, and straining for several days.
    • Follow-up with ENT may be arranged for recurrent or complex cases.
  • Escalation and safety-netting:
    • Patients with persistent bleeding, haemodynamic instability, or airway compromise require immediate escalation to senior medical staff and ENT.
    • Advise patients to return if bleeding recurs, becomes heavier, or if they develop new symptoms such as dizziness or shortness of breath.
  • Exam pharmacology reference (UKMLA/MRCGP): Tranexamic acid may be considered in some cases to reduce bleeding. Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Epistaxis"][/sc]
  • Significant blood loss and anaemia: Prolonged or severe epistaxis can lead to hypovolaemia and iron-deficiency anaemia, particularly if recurrent or in vulnerable patients.
  • Airway obstruction/aspiration: Swallowing large amounts of blood, especially with posterior bleeds, can cause nausea, vomiting, and potentially aspiration into the lungs.
  • Infection: Nasal packing can predispose to local infections (e.g., sinusitis) or, rarely, toxic shock syndrome, particularly if left in situ for extended periods.
  • Nasal septal perforation: A potential complication of aggressive cautery or trauma to the nasal septum, leading to whistling, crusting, and recurrent epistaxis.
  • Recurrence: Despite initial successful management, epistaxis can recur, necessitating further interventions and investigation into underlying causes.
  • Psychological distress: Frequent or severe nosebleeds can cause anxiety and impact quality of life.
