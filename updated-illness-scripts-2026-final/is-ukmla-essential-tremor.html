Essential tremor [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Essential tremor"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Essential tremor"][/sc]
  • Essential tremor (ET) is characterised by a **symmetrical tremor** of the **voluntary muscles** in the absence of any other neurological signs or symptoms.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical features, epidemiology, and differential diagnoses of essential tremor.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Essential tremor"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Essential tremor"][/sc]
  • Essential tremor is one of the most common movement disorders, affecting an estimated 1,000,000 people in the UK, making it 8-10 times more prevalent than Parkinson's disease.
  • Risk factors include increasing age and a strong genetic predisposition, with 50-70% of cases being inherited in an autosomal dominant pattern.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Essential tremor"][/sc]
  • The underlying cause of Essential Tremor (ET) is thought to involve neurodegeneration within the **cerebello-thalamo-cortical circuit**, leading to aberrant neural oscillations.
  • This circuit dysfunction results in abnormal communication between the cerebellum, thalamus, and motor cortex, contributing to the generation and maintenance of rhythmic tremor.
  • The consequence is a progressive, involuntary, rhythmic movement disorder, primarily affecting the upper limbs, which can worsen with activity and significantly impact quality of life.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Essential tremor"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Symptoms may be subtle and intermittent, often noticed during specific tasks. Progression is typically slow, particularly in cases with earlier onset.
  • Progressive phase: Tremor becomes more consistent and noticeable, impacting daily activities. The severity can gradually increase over months to years, with additional body parts potentially becoming affected.
  • Late phase: Tremor can become severe and debilitating, significantly affecting quality of life and functional independence, particularly beyond several years of onset.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Essential tremor"][/sc]
  • Ask about fine, bilateral, and symmetrical tremor - *This is the hallmark of ET, affecting hands, head, voice, legs, or trunk. Helps distinguish from other tremors.*
  • Ask about worsening with movement - *Action tremor is characteristic; it often worsens during voluntary tasks like writing or eating.*
  • Ask about improvement with alcohol - *A classic, though non-specific, feature that can aid in diagnosis.*
  • Ask about absence during sleep - *Tremor typically resolves during sleep, differentiating it from other movement disorders.*
  • Ask about impact on daily activities - *Assesses the functional severity and guides management decisions.*
  • Ask about family history of tremor - *ET often has an autosomal dominant inheritance pattern.*
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Essential tremor"][/sc]
  • Check for a bilateral and symmetrical action tremor - *This is the most common presentation, often seen when holding arms outstretched or during fine motor tasks.*
  • Check for head tremor (yes-yes or no-no pattern) or voice tremor - *These can occur in some patients and are characteristic of ET.*
  • Check for a resting tremor - *While less common, a resting tremor can be present in some ET patients, which can sometimes be confused with Parkinson's disease.*
  • Check vital signs - *Typically normal in ET, but can help rule out secondary causes of tremor such as hyperthyroidism or anxiety.*
  • Check for cerebellar signs - *[sc name="jitl-query" Q="Cerebellar signs: what are they and why do they matter clinically?" text="Cerebellar dysfunction"] is implicated in ET pathophysiology, though overt cerebellar signs are usually absent.*
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Essential tremor"][/sc]
  • Unilateral tremor or asymmetry: Suggests an alternative diagnosis such as Parkinson's disease, which typically presents with asymmetrical tremor.
  • Sudden onset or rapid progression: Essential tremor usually has a gradual onset and slow progression; sudden changes may indicate a secondary cause (e.g., drug-induced, metabolic, or acute neurological event).
  • Presence of other neurological signs: Features like rigidity, bradykinesia, significant ataxia, or weakness are not typical of isolated essential tremor and warrant investigation for other neurological conditions.
  • Lack of improvement with alcohol: While not universal, a history of tremor improving with alcohol can be a diagnostic clue for ET; its absence or worsening may suggest other causes.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Essential tremor"][/sc]
  • Older adults: While ET prevalence increases with age, older onset may be associated with more rapid progression and can sometimes be misdiagnosed as Parkinson's disease due to the potential for a resting component to the tremor.
  • Pregnancy: Hormonal fluctuations during pregnancy can influence tremor severity, with some women reporting changes. Management requires careful consideration of medication safety for both mother and foetus.
  • Early-onset ET: Patients with an earlier age of onset (e.g., before 40) often experience a slower rate of progression, though the tremor can still become debilitating over many years.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Essential tremor"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected Essential Tremor: Essential tremor is primarily a clinical diagnosis based on history and neurological examination. No specific diagnostic test exists.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible:
    • Blood tests: Thyroid function tests (to exclude hyperthyroidism), serum ceruloplasmin (to exclude Wilson's disease), and toxicology screens (to exclude drug-induced tremor).
  • Imaging - MRI head: Not routinely required for ET diagnosis, but may be performed to exclude other neurological causes of tremor (e.g., structural lesions, cerebellar pathology) if suggested by atypical features.
  • Imaging - Dopamine transporter (DaT) scan: Can be used in cases of diagnostic uncertainty to differentiate ET (typically negative) from Parkinson's disease (typically positive), but it is an expensive test and not routinely performed.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Essential tremor"][/sc]
  • A 68-year-old male presents with a 5-year history of gradually worsening bilateral hand tremor, particularly noticeable when holding objects or writing, which improves slightly with alcohol and is absent during sleep.
  • Progressive, symmetrical, action-predominant tremor in an older adult, responsive to alcohol, without other neurological deficits.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Essential tremor"][/sc]
  • Parkinson's disease - *Typically presents with asymmetrical resting tremor, bradykinesia, rigidity, and postural instability.*
  • Drug-induced tremor - *Often linked to medications like valproate, lithium, or beta-agonists; usually resolves with drug cessation.*
  • Hyperthyroidism - *Associated with a fine, rapid tremor, along with other symptoms like weight loss, palpitations, and anxiety.*
  • Wilson's disease - *A rare genetic disorder causing liver disease and neurological symptoms, including tremor, dysarthria, and dystonia, often with Kayser-Fleischer rings.*
  • Cerebellar tremor - *Characterised by an intention tremor that worsens as the limb approaches a target, often associated with ataxia and dysmetria.*
  • Enhanced physiological tremor - *Exaggerated normal tremor, often triggered by anxiety, fatigue, or certain drugs (e.g., caffeine), and usually resolves when the trigger is removed.*
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Essential tremor"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities:
    • Patient education and reassurance regarding the benign nature of the condition, though it can be progressive.
    • Consider occupational therapy for adaptive strategies and devices to mitigate functional impact.
    • Speech and language therapy may be beneficial for vocal tremors.
    • Encourage stress reduction techniques, as stress can exacerbate tremor.
  • Definitive management:
    • Medical management often involves beta-blockers, such as [sc name="jitl-medication" Q="Propranolol use in Essential Tremor" text="propranolol"].
    • If beta-blockers are ineffective or contraindicated, certain anti-epileptic medications, such as [sc name="jitl-medication" Q="Primidone use in Essential Tremor" text="primidone"], may be considered.
    • Surgical options, including deep brain stimulation (DBS) or MRI-guided focused ultrasound thalamotomy, are typically reserved for severe, medically refractory cases.
  • Monitoring and follow-up:
    • Regular or patient-initiated follow-ups are often appropriate to assess tremor progression and impact on quality of life, adjusting management as needed.
  • Escalation and safety-netting:
    • Escalation to specialist movement disorder services is indicated for diagnostic uncertainty, refractory symptoms, or consideration of surgical interventions.
    • Safety-netting advice should include seeking medical attention for sudden worsening of tremor, development of new neurological symptoms, or adverse effects from medication.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Essential tremor"][/sc]
  • Reduced quality of life: The persistent and often progressive nature of tremor can significantly impair daily activities such as eating, drinking, writing, and dressing, leading to functional disability and reduced independence.
  • Psychological impact: Living with a visible tremor often leads to increased rates of anxiety, depression, social embarrassment, and reduced self-esteem, impacting mental well-being and social participation.
  • Cognitive impairment: Emerging evidence suggests an association between Essential Tremor, particularly with older onset, and an increased risk of mild cognitive impairment, affecting memory and executive functions.
  • Increased mortality risk: Some studies indicate a slightly increased mortality risk in patients with severe Essential Tremor, though the exact mechanisms are still under investigation.
  • Social isolation: Due to the visible nature of the tremor and associated psychological distress, individuals may withdraw from social activities, leading to isolation and further impacting quality of life.
