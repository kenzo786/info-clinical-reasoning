Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Hernias (Gastrointestinal tract)"][/sc]
  • A hernia occurs when an internal part of the body, such as fatty tissue or a section of bowel, protrudes through a weakness in the muscle or surrounding tissue wall, most commonly in the abdomen or groin [1].
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the different types of hernias (inguinal, femoral, umbilical, incisional), differentiate between reducible, irreducible, and strangulated presentations, and understand the clinical implications for management.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Hernias (Gastrointestinal tract)"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Hernias (Gastrointestinal tract)"][/sc]
  • Inguinal hernias are the most common type, predominantly affecting men, with an estimated lifetime risk of 27-43% in males and 3-6% in females [2]. In 2020/21, there were 29,255 primary inguinal hernia repairs in NHS hospitals in England [3].
  • Femoral hernias are less common, accounting for about 5% of all abdominal hernias, and are more prevalent in women, particularly middle-aged and elderly parous women [3]. In 2020/21, there were 1,098 primary femoral hernia repairs in NHS hospitals in England [3].
  • Risk factors include male sex, increasing age, obesity, chronic cough, constipation, heavy lifting, and prematurity in infants [2].
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Hernias (Gastrointestinal tract)"][/sc]
  • Trigger: A congenital defect (e.g., patent processus vaginalis for indirect inguinal hernias) or acquired weakness in the abdominal wall fascia, often exacerbated by increased intra-abdominal pressure (e.g., chronic cough, heavy lifting, straining) [2].
  • Progression: Abdominal contents (peritoneum, omentum, or bowel) are forced through the weakened area, forming a hernial sac. This can initially be reducible, but over time, fibrous adhesions may form, making it irreducible [2]. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Reducible vs Irreducible Hernia: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Reducible vs Irreducible Hernia"][/sc]
  • Consequence: The protruding contents can become incarcerated (irreducible), obstructed (bowel lumen blocked), or strangulated (compromised blood supply), leading to ischaemia, necrosis, and potentially perforation and peritonitis [2].
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Hernias (Gastrointestinal tract)"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Often asymptomatic or presents as a soft, reducible lump that may appear with straining or standing and disappear when lying down. Discomfort may be mild and intermittent.
  • Progressive phase: The hernia may enlarge, become more difficult to reduce, and cause increasing discomfort or pain, particularly with physical activity.
  • Late phase: If complications arise, such as incarceration, obstruction, or strangulation, symptoms can rapidly progress over hours to days, leading to severe pain, systemic signs of infection, and bowel compromise.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Hernias (Gastrointestinal tract)"][/sc]
  • Ask about a lump or swelling in the groin or abdomen - this is the most common presenting symptom and its reducibility is a key diagnostic feature.
  • Ask about pain or discomfort - often described as a dull ache, heaviness, or burning sensation, particularly exacerbated by coughing, straining, or lifting.
  • Ask about a dragging sensation in the scrotum (for inguinal hernias) - indicates the presence of an indirect inguinal hernia extending into the scrotum.
  • Ask about sudden, severe pain, nausea, vomiting, or inability to pass flatus/stool - these are red flag symptoms suggestive of incarceration, obstruction, or strangulation.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Hernias (Gastrointestinal tract)"][/sc]
  • Check for a visible or palpable bulge in the groin or abdominal wall - assess its location relative to anatomical landmarks (e.g., pubic tubercle) and its reducibility. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Inguinal vs Femoral Hernia: anatomical differentiation and clinical significance?" text="Inguinal vs Femoral Hernia"][/sc]
  • Check for a cough impulse - an expansile impulse on coughing suggests a communication with the abdominal cavity.
  • Check for tenderness, erythema, or warmth over the lump - these signs indicate inflammation or compromised blood supply, suggesting strangulation.
  • Check vital signs (temperature, heart rate, blood pressure) - fever, tachycardia, or hypotension may indicate systemic inflammatory response syndrome (SIRS) due to strangulation or peritonitis.
  • Check for signs of bowel obstruction (abdominal distension, absent bowel sounds) - suggests incarcerated bowel within the hernial sac.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Hernias (Gastrointestinal tract)"][/sc]
  • Irreducible, tender, or firm lump: Indicates incarceration or strangulation, requiring urgent surgical assessment to prevent tissue necrosis.
  • Sudden, severe pain: Suggests acute onset of strangulation or obstruction, necessitating immediate medical attention.
  • Nausea, vomiting, abdominal distension, or absolute constipation: Symptoms of bowel obstruction, which can be a life-threatening complication of a hernia.
  • Systemic signs of infection (fever, tachycardia, hypotension): May indicate peritonitis secondary to bowel perforation from strangulation, a surgical emergency.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Hernias (Gastrointestinal tract)"][/sc]
  • Women: Femoral hernias are more common in women and have a higher risk of strangulation, often presenting acutely with pain rather than a noticeable lump [3].
  • Obese individuals: Hernias may be difficult to palpate or visualise due to adipose tissue, leading to delayed diagnosis and increased risk of complications [3].
  • Elderly: May present with vague abdominal discomfort or altered bowel habits, and signs of strangulation may be masked or atypical, leading to delayed presentation and higher mortality [3].
  • Occult hernias: Small hernias, particularly inguinal, may present with groin pain without an obvious bulge, requiring imaging for diagnosis [4]. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Occult Hernia: definition, diagnosis, and clinical relevance?" text="Occult Hernia"][/sc]
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Hernias (Gastrointestinal tract)"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected hernia: Clinical examination is paramount, assessing for a reducible or irreducible lump, cough impulse, and tenderness.
  • Severity/risk tools: The European Hernia Society classification provides a framework for classifying hernias, though not a severity score.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible or for occult hernias: Ultrasound is often the first-line imaging modality to confirm diagnosis and differentiate from other groin lumps [2].
  • Imaging - CT scan: Indicated in cases of suspected strangulation, obstruction, or when ultrasound is inconclusive, providing detailed anatomical information and assessing for bowel compromise [2]. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="CT scan in Hernia: indications and findings?" text="CT scan in Hernia"][/sc]
  • Serial reassessment: For reducible, asymptomatic hernias, watchful waiting with regular clinical review may be considered, particularly for men with inguinal hernias [2].
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Hernias (Gastrointestinal tract)"][/sc]
  • A 65-year-old male presents with a several-month history of a reducible groin swelling that becomes prominent on standing and is associated with a dull ache, now experiencing sudden onset of severe pain and irreducibility.
  • Elderly male with chronic groin swelling, acutely painful and irreducible, concerning for strangulated inguinal hernia.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Hernias (Gastrointestinal tract)"][/sc]
  • Lymphadenopathy - often multiple, firm, and mobile, without a cough impulse.
  • Lipoma - soft, non-reducible, and typically lacks a cough impulse.
  • Femoral artery aneurysm - pulsatile mass, often with a bruit, located over the femoral artery.
  • Saphena varix - soft, compressible swelling that disappears on lying down and has a thrill on coughing, located at the saphenofemoral junction. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Saphena Varix: clinical features and differentiation from hernia?" text="Saphena Varix"][/sc]
  • Hydrocele (in males) - transilluminates, and it is possible to get above the swelling on examination.
  • Undescended testis (in children) - absence of testis in the scrotum with a palpable mass in the inguinal canal.
  • Ectopic pregnancy (in women of childbearing age with abdominal pain) - consider in acute abdominal pain, especially if associated with vaginal bleeding and positive pregnancy test.
  • Pelvic inflammatory disease (PID) (in women with lower abdominal pain) - typically associated with vaginal discharge, fever, and cervical motion tenderness.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Hernias (Gastrointestinal tract)"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Recognition of red flag features, urgent surgical referral for suspected incarceration, obstruction, or strangulation. Supportive care often includes analgesia and fluid resuscitation.
  • Definitive management: Surgical repair (herniorrhaphy or hernioplasty) is the primary treatment for most symptomatic hernias. Approaches include open surgery or laparoscopic (keyhole) repair, with mesh often used to reinforce the abdominal wall [2].
  • Adjunctive management: For asymptomatic, reducible inguinal hernias in men, watchful waiting may be an option, but surgical repair is generally recommended to prevent complications [2].
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Post-operative care involves monitoring for complications such as pain, infection, haematoma, seroma, and hernia recurrence.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Patients should be advised to seek immediate medical attention if they develop sudden severe pain, irreducibility of the lump, vomiting, or signs of systemic illness.
  • Exam pharmacology reference (UKMLA/MRCGP): No specific drug knowledge is commonly tested for the direct management of hernias, as surgical intervention is primary. However, pain management (e.g., [sc name="jitl-medication" Q="Paracetamol use in post-operative pain" text="paracetamol"], [sc name="jitl-medication" Q="NSAIDs use in post-operative pain" text="NSAIDs"]) and antiemetics may be relevant in the peri-operative period. Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Hernias (Gastrointestinal tract)"][/sc]
  • Incarceration: The hernia becomes irreducible, meaning the contents cannot be returned to the abdominal cavity. This can occur gradually over time or acutely.
  • Obstruction: The lumen of the bowel within the hernial sac becomes blocked, leading to symptoms of bowel obstruction (nausea, vomiting, abdominal distension, absolute constipation). This typically develops over hours to days.
  • Strangulation: The blood supply to the incarcerated contents is compromised, leading to ischaemia, necrosis, and potentially perforation. This is a surgical emergency with rapid onset, often within hours, and is associated with severe pain and systemic signs. Femoral hernias have a significantly higher risk of strangulation compared to inguinal hernias [3].
  • Perforation: Necrosis of the bowel wall due to strangulation can lead to perforation, resulting in peritonitis and sepsis, a life-threatening condition that can develop rapidly after strangulation.
  • Recurrence: Despite surgical repair, hernias can recur, particularly if there is persistent weakness in the abdominal wall or if tension-free repair is not achieved.
  • Chronic pain: Post-herniorrhaphy pain can be a persistent complication, affecting quality of life.
