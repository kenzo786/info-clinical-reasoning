Transient ischaemic attacks (TIA) [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Transient ischaemic attacks (TIA)"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Transient ischaemic attacks (TIA)"][/sc]
  • A transient ischaemic attack (TIA) is a transient episode of neurological dysfunction caused by focal brain, spinal cord, or retinal ischaemia, without acute infarction.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: The candidate must recognise the clinical features and immediate management of a TIA to prevent subsequent stroke.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Transient ischaemic attacks (TIA)"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Transient ischaemic attacks (TIA)"][/sc]
  • Approximately 46,000 TIAs are diagnosed in England and Wales each year, with incidence increasing with age, particularly over 60 years [1].
  • Risk factors mirror those for ischaemic stroke, including hypertension, hyperlipidaemia, diabetes mellitus, atrial fibrillation, smoking, and obesity [2].
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Transient ischaemic attacks (TIA)"][/sc]
  • Atherosclerotic plaque rupture or embolisation from the carotid arteries or heart (e.g., in atrial fibrillation) often triggers a temporary occlusion of a cerebral artery.
  • This leads to a transient reduction in cerebral blood flow, causing focal neurological deficits due to ischaemia.
  • Unlike stroke, the ischaemia is insufficient in duration or severity to cause permanent tissue damage or infarction, with symptoms typically resolving within minutes to hours.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Transient ischaemic attacks (TIA)"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Symptoms are sudden in onset and typically resolve completely within minutes, and by definition, within 24 hours.
  • Progressive phase: There is a significant risk of recurrent TIA or subsequent ischaemic stroke, particularly within the first 48 hours and beyond 7 days if not managed appropriately.
  • Late phase: Patients remain at increased long-term risk of stroke, myocardial infarction, and vascular dementia, necessitating ongoing risk factor modification.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Transient ischaemic attacks (TIA)"][/sc]
  • Ask about sudden unilateral weakness or numbness - this indicates focal neurological dysfunction.
  • Ask about sudden speech disturbance (dysphasia or dysarthria) - suggests involvement of dominant hemisphere language areas.
  • Ask about sudden visual loss in one eye (amaurosis fugax) - often described as a "curtain coming down" and is highly suggestive of ipsilateral carotid artery disease.
  • Ask about sudden balance problems or dizziness with other neurological symptoms - may indicate vertebrobasilar ischaemia.
  • Ask about the duration of symptoms - by definition, TIA symptoms resolve completely within 24 hours.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Transient ischaemic attacks (TIA)"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs (blood pressure, heart rate, oxygen saturation) - hypertension is a major risk factor, and atrial fibrillation can be a source of emboli.
  • Check for carotid bruits - may indicate carotid artery stenosis, a common cause of TIA.
  • Check for any residual neurological deficits - by definition, there should be no objective signs of infarction on examination after a TIA.
  • Check for signs of peripheral vascular disease - often co-exists with cerebrovascular disease.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Transient ischaemic attacks (TIA)"][/sc]
  • Persistent neurological deficit: Suggests an evolving or completed stroke rather than a TIA.
  • Symptoms lasting >24 hours: By definition, this is a stroke, even if symptoms subsequently resolve.
  • ABCD2 score ≥4: Indicates a high risk of stroke within 2 days, requiring urgent specialist assessment.
  • New onset atrial fibrillation: High embolic risk, requiring prompt anticoagulation.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Transient ischaemic attacks (TIA)"][/sc]
  • Isolated dizziness or vertigo: Rarely a TIA unless accompanied by other focal neurological symptoms, as it is a common non-specific symptom.
  • Isolated confusion or altered consciousness: Not typical for TIA; consider other causes like metabolic derangement or seizure.
  • Younger patients: Consider less common causes such as arterial dissection, vasculitis, or prothrombotic states.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Transient ischaemic attacks (TIA)"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected TIA: Full blood count, electrolytes, glucose, lipid profile, ECG (to detect atrial fibrillation).
  • Imaging - Brain: Urgent diffusion-weighted MRI brain is preferred to exclude acute infarction, or non-contrast CT brain if MRI is unavailable or contraindicated.
  • Imaging - Carotid arteries: Carotid ultrasound Doppler to assess for carotid artery stenosis, particularly if symptoms are hemispheric.
  • Serial reassessment: Clinical review within 24 hours for high-risk TIA (ABCD2 score ≥4) or within 7 days for lower-risk TIA.

Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Transient ischaemic attacks (TIA)"][/sc]
  • A 68-year-old male with a history of hypertension and smoking presents with sudden onset of right-sided arm weakness and expressive dysphasia, which completely resolved within 30 minutes.
  • Acute, transient, focal neurological deficit in an elderly patient with vascular risk factors, highly suggestive of cerebrovascular ischaemia.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Transient ischaemic attacks (TIA)"][/sc]
  • Ischaemic Stroke - neurological deficits persist beyond 24 hours or show evidence of infarction on imaging.
  • Migraine with aura - symptoms typically evolve over minutes and include headache, often with positive neurological phenomena (e.g., visual scintillations).
  • Seizure (Todd\'s paralysis) - post-ictal weakness following a seizure, usually with a history of seizure activity.
  • Hypoglycaemia - can cause focal neurological deficits, but usually accompanied by other systemic symptoms of hypoglycaemia and resolves with glucose administration.
  • Syncope - transient loss of consciousness due to global cerebral hypoperfusion, without focal neurological signs.
  • Vestibular disorders - cause dizziness or vertigo, but typically without other focal neurological symptoms.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Transient ischaemic attacks (TIA)"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Urgent specialist assessment (within 24 hours for high-risk TIA, 7 days for low-risk TIA), dual antiplatelet therapy (aspirin and clopidogrel) initiated promptly.
  • Definitive management: Optimise cardiovascular risk factors (blood pressure control, statin therapy, diabetes management), consider carotid endarterectomy or stenting for significant carotid stenosis.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Regular blood pressure monitoring, lipid profile checks, and review of antiplatelet/anticoagulant therapy.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Advise patient to seek immediate medical attention for any recurrence or worsening of neurological symptoms.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Transient ischaemic attacks (TIA)"][/sc]
  • Ischaemic Stroke: The most significant complication, with the highest risk in the first 48 hours after a TIA, due to ongoing cerebral ischaemia or recurrent embolisation.
  • Recurrent TIA: Indicates ongoing vascular instability and increased risk of subsequent stroke.
  • Vascular Dementia: Long-term cognitive impairment can result from recurrent TIAs or subclinical cerebrovascular disease.
  • Myocardial Infarction: Patients with TIA share common risk factors with coronary artery disease, increasing their risk of cardiac events.
