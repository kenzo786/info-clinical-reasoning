Diverticular disease and diverticulitis [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Diverticular disease and diverticulitis"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Diverticular disease and diverticulitis"][/sc]
  • Diverticular disease is characterised by the presence of diverticula (small pouches) in the wall of the colon, most commonly in the sigmoid colon. Diverticulitis occurs when these diverticula become inflamed or infected [1].
  • UKMLA learning outcome: To recognise the clinical features, diagnosis, and initial management principles of diverticular disease and acute diverticulitis, including identification of complications.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Diverticular disease and diverticulitis"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Diverticular disease and diverticulitis"][/sc]
  • The prevalence of diverticulosis increases with age, affecting approximately 5% of people aged under 45 years and up to 65% by 85 years of age [2].
  • Around 10-25% of individuals with diverticulosis will develop diverticulitis during their lifetime [2].
  • Risk factors include increasing age, a low-fibre diet, obesity, smoking, and use of non-steroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs) [1].
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Diverticular disease and diverticulitis"][/sc]
  • Diverticula form due to increased intraluminal pressure in the colon, often associated with a low-fibre diet, leading to herniation of the mucosa and submucosa through weaknesses in the muscularis layer, typically where blood vessels penetrate [3].
  • Diverticulitis arises when a diverticulum becomes obstructed, often by faecal material, leading to increased pressure, ischaemia, and micro- or macro-perforation. This results in inflammation and potential infection of the diverticular sac and surrounding tissues [4].
  • The inflammatory process can range from localised inflammation (phlegmon) to abscess formation, peritonitis, or fistula formation, depending on the severity of perforation and host response [5]. Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Diverticular disease and diverticulitis"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Uncomplicated diverticulitis often resolves within a week with conservative management [6].
  • Progressive phase: Symptoms may persist or worsen beyond 48-72 hours, indicating potential complications or treatment failure, often necessitating hospital admission and further investigation [1].
  • Late phase: Elective surgical intervention for recurrent or complicated diverticulitis is typically delayed for at least 6 weeks after an acute episode to allow inflammation to subside [7].
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Diverticular disease and diverticulitis"][/sc]
  • Ask about abdominal pain - typically intermittent, colicky, and localised to the left lower quadrant in diverticular disease, but constant and severe in diverticulitis [1].
  • Ask about change in bowel habit - constipation or diarrhoea may be present in diverticular disease, while acute diverticulitis can present with sudden changes, including significant rectal bleeding or passage of mucus [1].
  • Ask about fever - usually absent in diverticular disease, but a key indicator of inflammation and infection in diverticulitis [1].
  • Ask about nausea and vomiting - may occur with diverticulitis, particularly if there is intestinal obstruction [8].
  • Ask about urinary symptoms - dysuria or frequency may suggest inflammation near the bladder or fistula formation [9].
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Diverticular disease and diverticulitis"][/sc]
  • Check abdominal tenderness - localised to the left lower quadrant in diverticular disease and diverticulitis, but may be right-sided in a minority or those of Asian origin [1].
  • Check palpable abdominal mass - may indicate a phlegmon or abscess in complicated diverticulitis [1].
  • Check vital signs - fever, tachycardia, and hypotension suggest systemic inflammation or sepsis in diverticulitis [1].
  • Check for peritonism - guarding and rigidity indicate peritoneal irritation, suggesting perforation and peritonitis [1]. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Peritonism: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Peritonism"][/sc]
  • Check rectal examination - peri-rectal fullness may indicate a pelvic abscess [1].
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Diverticular disease and diverticulitis"][/sc]
  • Uncontrolled abdominal pain: Suggests complicated diverticulitis or inadequate response to initial management [1].
  • Signs of sepsis: Altered mental state, raised respiratory rate, low systolic blood pressure, raised heart rate, low tympanic temperature, or reduced urine output indicate systemic infection and require urgent assessment [1].
  • Peritonism: Guarding and rigidity on abdominal examination are signs of peritoneal irritation, highly suggestive of bowel perforation and peritonitis [1].
  • Abdominal mass: A palpable mass may indicate an abscess or phlegmon formation [1].
  • Significant rectal bleeding: While occasional bleeding can occur, significant or persistent rectal bleeding warrants investigation to rule out other serious conditions [1].
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Diverticular disease and diverticulitis"][/sc]
  • Older adults: May present with atypical symptoms such as fatigue, confusion, or acute kidney injury, with less pronounced abdominal pain or fever, leading to delayed diagnosis [10].
  • Immunosuppressed patients: Have an increased risk of severe diverticulitis and complications, often presenting with subtle signs of inflammation despite significant underlying pathology [11].
  • Right-sided diverticulitis: More common in Asian populations, it can mimic acute appendicitis due to right lower quadrant pain, requiring careful differentiation [12]. Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Diverticular disease and diverticulitis"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected diverticulitis: Full blood count (FBC) to assess for leucocytosis, C-reactive protein (CRP) to indicate inflammation, and urea and electrolytes (U&E) to assess renal function and hydration status [1].
  • Severity/risk tools: The Hinchey classification is used to stage complicated acute diverticulitis based on CT findings, guiding management decisions [13]. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Hinchey classification: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Hinchey classification"][/sc]
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Urine dipstick and culture to rule out urinary tract infection, pregnancy test in women of childbearing age, and liver function tests to exclude hepatobiliary pathology [1].
  • Imaging - CT abdomen and pelvis: Contrast-enhanced CT is the gold standard for diagnosing acute diverticulitis and its complications, providing detailed information on inflammation, abscess formation, and perforation [1].
  • Serial reassessment: Clinical re-evaluation and repeat inflammatory markers are crucial to monitor response to treatment and detect worsening disease or complications [1].

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Diverticular disease and diverticulitis"][/sc]
  • Irritable Bowel Syndrome (IBS) - often presents with chronic, intermittent abdominal pain and altered bowel habit without inflammatory markers or structural changes [1].
  • Colorectal cancer - may present with similar symptoms such as altered bowel habit, abdominal pain, and rectal bleeding, necessitating colonoscopy to differentiate [1].
  • Ischaemic colitis - typically presents with sudden onset abdominal pain and bloody diarrhoea, often in patients with cardiovascular risk factors [14].
  • Inflammatory Bowel Disease (IBD) - chronic abdominal pain, diarrhoea, and inflammatory markers, but distinguished by endoscopic and histological findings [15].
  • Acute appendicitis (if right-sided pain) - presents with right lower quadrant pain, nausea, vomiting, and fever, requiring careful differentiation from right-sided diverticulitis [12].
  • Ectopic pregnancy (in females) - acute abdominal pain, often unilateral, with vaginal bleeding and positive pregnancy test [16].
  • Pelvic Inflammatory Disease (PID) (in females) - lower abdominal pain, vaginal discharge, and fever, typically in sexually active women [17].
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Diverticular disease and diverticulitis"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Assessment for complications (e.g., perforation, abscess, sepsis), pain control (e.g., paracetamol), and supportive care with intravenous fluids if dehydrated or unable to tolerate oral intake [1].
  • Definitive management: For uncomplicated diverticulitis, conservative management with oral antibiotics (if indicated, e.g., in immunocompromised patients or those with significant comorbidities) and a liquid diet, gradually progressing to a low-fibre diet, is often warranted [1]. Surgical intervention may be considered for complicated or recurrent cases [1].
  • Adjunctive management: Avoiding NSAIDs and opioid analgesia where possible, as they may increase the risk of diverticular perforation [1]. Bulk-forming laxatives may be considered for persistent constipation or diarrhoea [1].
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Clinical reassessment to ensure resolution of symptoms and inflammatory markers. Follow-up colonoscopy may be recommended after an episode of complicated diverticulitis to exclude malignancy [1].
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Patients should be advised on red flag symptoms requiring urgent medical review, such as worsening pain, persistent fever, or signs of peritonitis [1].

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Diverticular disease and diverticulitis"][/sc]
  • Perforation: Can lead to localised abscess formation or generalised peritonitis, a life-threatening condition requiring urgent surgical intervention [18].
  • Abscess formation: A collection of pus adjacent to the inflamed diverticulum, often requiring percutaneous drainage or surgical management if large or unresponsive to antibiotics [1].
  • Fistula formation: Chronic inflammation can lead to the development of abnormal connections between the colon and adjacent organs, such as the bladder (colovesical fistula) or vagina (colovaginal fistula) [19].
  • Bowel obstruction: Recurrent episodes of diverticulitis can cause fibrosis and stricture formation in the colon, leading to partial or complete bowel obstruction [20].
  • Bleeding: Diverticular bleeding can occur, often presenting as painless rectal bleeding, which can be significant and require intervention [21].
  • Recurrent diverticulitis: A significant proportion of patients experience further episodes of diverticulitis, highlighting the chronic nature of the disease [22].
