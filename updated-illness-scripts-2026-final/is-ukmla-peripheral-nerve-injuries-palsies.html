Peripheral nerve injuries/ palsies [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Peripheral nerve injuries/ palsies"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Peripheral nerve injuries/ palsies"][/sc]
  • Peripheral nerve injuries (PNIs) encompass a diverse group of disorders resulting from trauma, compression, ischemia, or iatrogenic causes, affecting the nerves that link the brain and spinal cord to other parts of the body [2, 3, 5].
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the common causes, clinical features, and initial management of peripheral nerve injuries and palsies.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Peripheral nerve injuries/ palsies"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Peripheral nerve injuries/ palsies"][/sc]
  • In the UK, it is estimated that almost 1 in 10 people aged 55 or over are affected by peripheral neuropathy [12].
  • The national incidence of peripheral nerve injury is approximately 11.2 events per 100,000 population per year, with males being at least twice as likely to be affected [8, 11].
  • Risk factors include trauma, nerve compression, systemic diseases such as diabetes and autoimmune conditions, and iatrogenic causes [1, 5, 13].
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Peripheral nerve injuries/ palsies"][/sc]
  • Trigger: Peripheral nerve injuries are often initiated by mechanical factors such as compression, stretch, or transection, or by systemic conditions like diabetes mellitus causing metabolic derangement and microvascular damage [1, 5, 11].
  • Progression: This initial insult leads to a cascade of events including neural tissue oedema, inflammation, and disruption of axonal transport. Depending on the severity, this can result in demyelination (neuropraxia), axonal degeneration with intact connective tissue (axonotmesis), or complete nerve transection (neurotmesis) [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Seddon's classification of nerve injury: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Seddon's classification"][/sc] [15].
  • Consequence: The compromised nerve integrity impairs its ability to conduct electrical impulses, leading to sensory disturbances (e.g., numbness, paraesthesia), motor weakness or paralysis, and neuropathic pain, ultimately affecting functional capacity [2, 10].
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Peripheral nerve injuries/ palsies"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Immediately following injury, symptoms may include acute pain, inflammation, and initial sensory or motor deficits. Mild nerve injuries, such as those from compression, may show significant improvement within weeks [9].
  • Progressive phase: For more severe injuries, symptoms can progress over time to muscle weakness, muscle wasting, and persistent sensory disturbances. Nerve regeneration is a slow process, typically occurring at a rate of approximately 1mm per day, meaning maximal recovery may take many months or even several years [4, 7].
  • Late phase: If regeneration is incomplete or absent, chronic pain, permanent sensory deficits, and irreversible motor dysfunction can ensue. Delayed or inadequate treatment can lead to long-term disability and complications [2, 13].
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Peripheral nerve injuries/ palsies"][/sc]
  • Ask about numbness or tingling (paraesthesia) - often an early indicator of sensory nerve involvement, helping to localise the affected nerve distribution [7, 12].
  • Ask about pain characteristics (e.g., sharp, throbbing, burning) - neuropathic pain suggests nerve damage and can help differentiate from musculoskeletal pain [7, 8].
  • Ask about muscle weakness or difficulty with specific movements - indicates motor nerve involvement and can help identify the affected muscle groups and their innervating nerves [7, 12].
  • Ask about loss of balance or clumsiness - suggests proprioceptive deficits due to large fibre sensory neuropathy, particularly noticeable in the dark or on uneven surfaces [9, 10].
  • Ask about extreme sensitivity to touch (allodynia) - a hallmark of neuropathic pain, indicating altered pain processing within the damaged nerve pathways [8].
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Peripheral nerve injuries/ palsies"][/sc]
  • Check muscle strength and tone - focal weakness or flaccidity implies motor nerve damage, helping to localise the lesion.
  • Check sensory modalities (light touch, pinprick, vibration, proprioception) - deficits map to specific dermatomes or nerve distributions, indicating sensory nerve involvement.
  • Check deep tendon reflexes - diminished or absent reflexes suggest a lesion within the reflex arc, either afferent (sensory) or efferent (motor) pathways.
  • Check for Tinel's sign - tapping over a suspected site of nerve compression or injury may elicit tingling or paraesthesia in the nerve's distribution, suggesting nerve irritation or regenerating axons [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Tinel's sign: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Tinel's sign"][/sc].
  • Check vital signs - particularly orthostatic blood pressure, which may reveal autonomic dysfunction in certain peripheral neuropathies.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Peripheral nerve injuries/ palsies"][/sc]
  • Rapidly progressive weakness: Suggests an acute and potentially severe neurological process, such as Guillain-Barré syndrome or acute compressive neuropathy, requiring urgent investigation and intervention [11].
  • Bowel or bladder dysfunction: Particularly new-onset urinary retention or faecal incontinence, is a critical red flag for cauda equina syndrome, indicating compression of sacral nerve roots and necessitating immediate neurosurgical assessment [12, 13, 14].
  • Saddle anaesthesia: Numbness or altered sensation in the perineal region (buttocks, genitals, inner thighs) is another key indicator of cauda equina syndrome, requiring emergency evaluation [12, 13].
  • Sudden loss of movement or sensation: Acute onset of complete paralysis or anaesthesia in a limb or specific nerve distribution may indicate severe nerve transection or acute compression, warranting urgent assessment to preserve nerve function [11].
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Peripheral nerve injuries/ palsies"][/sc]
  • Older adults: Peripheral neuropathy is more common in older adults and can be associated with increased falls, functional decline, and cognitive impairment. Symptoms may be subtle or attributed to normal ageing, leading to delayed diagnosis [1, 2].
  • Pregnancy: Physiological changes during pregnancy can predispose to certain peripheral neuropathies, most commonly carpal tunnel syndrome, presenting with nocturnal pain and sensory disturbances in the median nerve distribution. Other nerve compressions can also occur [7, 8, 9].
  • Immunosuppressed patients: In immunocompromised individuals, peripheral nerve injuries may present atypically or with a more aggressive course due to opportunistic infections or inflammatory processes. The presentation can sometimes mimic other neurological conditions, making diagnosis challenging [10, 11].
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Peripheral nerve injuries/ palsies"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected peripheral nerve injury:
    • Nerve Conduction Studies (NCS) and Electromyography (EMG): These electrodiagnostic tests are crucial for confirming the presence, type (demyelinating vs. axonal), severity, and localisation of nerve damage [1, 2, 3].
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible:
    • Blood tests: To identify underlying systemic causes such as diabetes (HbA1c), autoimmune conditions (autoantibodies), vitamin deficiencies (B12, folate), or renal/hepatic dysfunction [4].
  • Imaging - MRI/MR Neurography: Magnetic Resonance Imaging (MRI) and specifically MR Neurography can visualise the nerve directly, identify structural abnormalities like compression, tumours, or inflammation, and assess nerve continuity after trauma [5, 6, 7].
  • Imaging - Ultrasound: High-resolution ultrasound can be used to assess nerve morphology, identify compression sites, and guide injections or biopsies [8].
  • Validated scores:
    • Neuropathic Pain Scale (NPS) on MDCalc: To quantify the severity and characteristics of neuropathic pain.
    • Overall Neuropathy Limitations Scale (ONLS) on MDCalc: To assess the overall disability related to the patient's neuropathy.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Peripheral nerve injuries/ palsies"][/sc]
  • A 45-year-old male presents with a 3-month history of progressive numbness and tingling in the right thumb, index, and middle fingers, worse at night, and reports frequently dropping objects. Examination reveals weakness in thumb opposition and abduction, reduced sensation in the median nerve distribution, and a positive Tinel's sign at the right wrist.
  • A middle-aged male with subacute, progressive right median mononeuropathy, manifesting as sensory and motor deficits, suggestive of a compressive neuropathy.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Peripheral nerve injuries/ palsies"][/sc]
  • Cervical Radiculopathy: Symptoms often include neck pain, may extend above the elbow, and can involve multiple nerve roots (dermatomes/myotomes), unlike the more focal distribution of a single peripheral nerve injury [6, 7].
  • Brachial Plexopathy: Presents with a broader pattern of motor and sensory deficits across the arm and hand, reflecting involvement of the brachial plexus rather than a single peripheral nerve [11].
  • Central Nervous System (CNS) Lesions (e.g., stroke, spinal cord injury): Often accompanied by upper motor neuron signs (e.g., spasticity, hyperreflexia), cranial nerve involvement, or other central neurological symptoms not typically seen in isolated peripheral nerve injuries [5].
  • Myopathy: Characterised by muscle weakness without significant sensory loss or reflex abnormalities, indicating a primary muscle disorder rather than nerve damage.
  • Systemic Polyneuropathy (e.g., diabetic neuropathy): Typically presents with symmetrical, length-dependent sensory and motor deficits, often affecting the longest nerves first (e.g., 'glove and stocking' distribution), contrasting with the focal nature of many peripheral nerve injuries [12].
  • Entrapment Neuropathies (e.g., Carpal Tunnel Syndrome): While a type of peripheral nerve injury, it is crucial to differentiate from other causes of nerve compression or systemic neuropathies based on specific anatomical distribution and provocative tests [6].
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Peripheral nerve injuries/ palsies"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities:
    • Pain management: Initial analgesia to control acute pain.
    • Protection of the injured nerve: Immobilisation or splinting to prevent further damage, especially in traumatic injuries.
    • Address underlying cause: For example, blood glucose control in diabetic neuropathy or surgical decompression for acute nerve entrapment.
  • Definitive management:
    • Conservative management: Physical therapy, occupational therapy, and activity modification to improve function and reduce symptoms.
    • Surgical intervention: For nerve transections, severe compressions, or tumours, surgical repair (e.g., nerve grafting, direct repair) or decompression may be necessary to restore nerve function [1, 2, 3].
  • Monitoring and follow-up:
    • Regular neurological assessments to monitor recovery or progression of deficits.
    • Electrophysiological studies (NCS/EMG) to track nerve regeneration and functional improvement.
  • Escalation and safety-netting:
    • Advise patients to seek urgent medical attention for rapidly worsening weakness, new bowel/bladder dysfunction, or signs of infection.
    • Provide clear instructions on when to contact healthcare professionals if symptoms change or new concerns arise.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Peripheral nerve injuries/ palsies"][/sc]
  • Chronic Neuropathic Pain: Persistent pain that can develop after nerve injury, resulting from maladaptive changes in the nervous system (e.g., sensitisation of pain pathways). This can be debilitating and significantly impact quality of life, often requiring long-term management [1, 7, 8].
  • Muscle Atrophy and Weakness: If motor nerves are severely damaged or regeneration is incomplete, the denervated muscles will waste away, leading to permanent weakness and functional impairment. This is a direct consequence of the nerve's inability to transmit signals to the muscle [11, 12, 13].
  • Functional Disability and Loss of Independence: Sensory and motor deficits can severely limit a patient's ability to perform daily activities, leading to reduced mobility, difficulty with fine motor tasks, and increased risk of falls, particularly if recovery is not achieved within months to years [1, 2].
  • Contractures and Joint Stiffness: Prolonged immobility, muscle imbalance, or spasticity resulting from nerve injury can lead to shortening of muscles and tendons, causing fixed deformities and reduced range of motion in affected joints. This typically develops in the progressive to late phases if not prevented by rehabilitation.
  • Pressure Sores and Ulcers: Loss of protective sensation means patients may not perceive pressure or injury to affected areas, leading to skin breakdown, particularly over bony prominences. This risk increases over time if sensory deficits are profound and unaddressed.
