Colorectal cancer (Gastrointestinal tract) [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Colorectal cancer"]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Colorectal cancer"]
  • Colorectal cancer (CRC) is a malignancy arising from the epithelial lining of the large bowel (colon or rectum), often developing from adenomatous polyps.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the common presentations and risk factors for colorectal cancer, initiate appropriate investigations including faecal immunochemical testing (FIT), and understand referral pathways for suspected malignancy.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Colorectal cancer"]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Colorectal cancer"]
  • Colorectal cancer is the fourth most common cancer in the UK, with around 40,000 new cases diagnosed annually, and is the second most common cause of cancer death [1, 2]. Incidence increases with age, with most cases diagnosed in those over 60, but rates are rising in younger adults [3].
  • Key risk factors include increasing age, a diet high in red and processed meat, obesity, alcohol consumption, smoking, inflammatory bowel disease (Crohn\'s disease or ulcerative colitis), and a family history of CRC or adenomatous polyps [1].
  • Genetic predispositions, such as Lynch syndrome (formerly HNPCC) and familial adenomatous polyposis (FAP), account for a small but significant proportion of cases [4].
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Colorectal cancer"]
  • Trigger: Most colorectal cancers develop from benign adenomatous polyps through a multi-step process involving genetic mutations (e.g., APC, KRAS, TP53) and epigenetic changes, leading to uncontrolled cell growth.
  • Progression: As polyps grow, they can undergo malignant transformation, invading the bowel wall and potentially spreading to regional lymph nodes via lymphatic drainage. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="TNM staging: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="TNM staging"]
  • Consequence: Further progression involves distant metastasis, commonly to the liver and lungs, through haematogenous spread, leading to systemic disease and significant morbidity and mortality.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Colorectal cancer"]
  • Early phase: The development of adenomatous polyps is often asymptomatic and can take many years. Early-stage cancer within the bowel wall may also be asymptomatic or present with subtle, non-specific symptoms like mild changes in bowel habit.
  • Progressive phase: As the tumour grows and invades deeper into the bowel wall or regional lymph nodes, symptoms become more apparent, including persistent changes in bowel habit, rectal bleeding, abdominal pain, and unexplained weight loss. This phase can evolve over months to a few years.
  • Late phase: With distant metastasis, symptoms reflect the involvement of other organs (e.g., jaundice with liver metastases, dyspnoea with lung metastases). Complications such as bowel obstruction or perforation can develop acutely, requiring urgent intervention.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Colorectal cancer"]
  • Ask about change in bowel habit - particularly persistent (more than 3 weeks) diarrhoea or constipation, or increased frequency of defecation, as this can indicate luminal narrowing or irritation by a tumour.
  • Ask about rectal bleeding - fresh blood mixed with stool or on the toilet paper, which may be a sign of a distal colorectal lesion, though often attributed to benign causes.
  • Ask about abdominal pain - new onset, persistent, or worsening abdominal pain, especially if associated with a change in bowel habit, suggesting tumour growth or partial obstruction.
  • Ask about unexplained weight loss - a significant and unintentional reduction in body weight, which is a common systemic symptom of advanced malignancy.
  • Ask about symptoms of anaemia - fatigue, pallor, and shortness of breath, which can result from chronic blood loss from the tumour, particularly in right-sided colon cancers.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Colorectal cancer"]
  • Check vital signs - for signs of anaemia (tachycardia, tachypnoea) or systemic inflammation (fever), which may indicate advanced disease or complications.
  • Check abdominal examination - for palpable abdominal masses, distension, or tenderness, which can suggest a large tumour, obstruction, or peritoneal involvement.
  • Check digital rectal examination (DRE) - for palpable rectal masses, blood, or abnormal strictures, crucial for detecting rectal cancers.
  • Check for signs of cachexia - muscle wasting and general debility, indicating advanced disease and nutritional compromise.
  • Check for hepatomegaly or ascites - suggesting liver metastases or peritoneal carcinomatosis, indicative of advanced metastatic disease. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Metastasis: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Metastasis"]
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Colorectal cancer"]
  • Unexplained iron-deficiency anaemia: Suggests chronic gastrointestinal blood loss, particularly from right-sided colon cancers, warranting urgent investigation.
  • Persistent change in bowel habit: New onset, persistent (more than 3 weeks) change in bowel habit, especially to looser stools or increased frequency, in individuals aged 60 or over, indicates a need for urgent referral.
  • Rectal bleeding with change in bowel habit: Any age with persistent rectal bleeding associated with a change in bowel habit requires urgent assessment for colorectal malignancy.
  • Palpable abdominal or rectal mass: A new, unexplained mass in the abdomen or rectum is highly suspicious for advanced colorectal cancer and necessitates urgent referral.
  • FIT result of at least 10 micrograms of haemoglobin per gram of faeces: A positive faecal immunochemical test (FIT) result indicates the presence of blood in stool, triggering a suspected cancer pathway referral.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Colorectal cancer"]
  • Younger adults: May present with non-specific symptoms, leading to delayed diagnosis. A higher index of suspicion is needed for persistent abdominal pain, rectal bleeding, or unexplained anaemia, even in the absence of typical age-related risk factors.
  • Right-sided colon cancer: Often presents with iron-deficiency anaemia due to chronic occult blood loss, rather than overt changes in bowel habit or rectal bleeding, as stools are more liquid and masses may not obstruct until very late.
  • Lynch syndrome (HNPCC): Individuals with this inherited condition may develop CRC at a younger age and often have synchronous or metachronous tumours. Screening protocols differ significantly. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Lynch syndrome: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Lynch syndrome"]
  • Elderly patients: Co-morbidities may mask symptoms, or symptoms may be attributed to other age-related conditions, leading to advanced disease at presentation.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Colorectal cancer"]
  • Core for suspected colorectal cancer: Faecal immunochemical test (FIT) is a quantitative test for occult blood in faeces, used in primary care to triage patients for urgent referral. A full blood count (FBC) assesses for iron-deficiency anaemia. Colonoscopy is the gold standard for diagnosis, allowing direct visualisation, biopsy, and polyp removal. Histopathological examination of biopsy samples confirms malignancy and determines tumour type and grade.
  • Severity/risk tools: The Dukes/TNM staging systems are essential for determining the extent of disease and guiding treatment. The Glasgow Prognostic Score (GPS) on MDCalc is a simple inflammatory score used to predict prognosis.
  • Imaging - CT scan: CT scan of the chest, abdomen, and pelvis is used for staging to detect local invasion and distant metastases.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Inflammatory markers (CRP, ESR) may be elevated in inflammatory bowel disease or infection. Coeliac serology may be considered if malabsorption or chronic diarrhoea is a prominent feature.
  • Specialist tests: Testing for [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Microsatellite instability: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="microsatellite instability (MSI)"] is important in assessing for Lynch syndrome.

  • Elderly male with chronic anaemia, altered bowel habit, and weight loss, concerning for colorectal malignancy.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Colorectal cancer"]
  • Diverticular disease - often presents with abdominal pain and altered bowel habit, but typically lacks significant weight loss or iron-deficiency anaemia unless complicated by bleeding.
  • Inflammatory bowel disease (Crohn\'s disease/Ulcerative colitis) - chronic diarrhoea, abdominal pain, and rectal bleeding are common, but usually with inflammatory markers and specific endoscopic findings.
  • Irritable bowel syndrome (IBS) - characterised by chronic abdominal pain and altered bowel habit, but typically without rectal bleeding, weight loss, or anaemia, and symptoms often fluctuate.
  • Haemorrhoids - common cause of rectal bleeding, usually bright red and on wiping, not typically mixed with stool or associated with systemic symptoms like weight loss or anaemia.
  • Benign colorectal polyps - may cause occult bleeding, but are asymptomatic until they become large or undergo malignant transformation.
  • Ischaemic colitis - acute onset abdominal pain and bloody diarrhoea, often in older patients with vascular risk factors, typically self-limiting.
  • Infectious colitis - acute onset diarrhoea, abdominal pain, and sometimes fever, often with a clear infective trigger and self-limiting course.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Colorectal cancer"]
  • Immediate priorities: Recognition of red flag symptoms and urgent referral via suspected cancer pathways. Supportive care may include managing anaemia with blood transfusions or iron supplementation, and addressing symptoms like pain or nausea.
  • Definitive management: Surgical resection of the tumour is the primary treatment for localised colorectal cancer, aiming for complete removal with clear margins. The extent of surgery depends on tumour location and stage.
  • Adjunctive management: Adjuvant chemotherapy (e.g., FOLFOX, CAPOX) is often considered after surgery for stage III and some high-risk stage II cancers to reduce recurrence risk. Neoadjuvant chemoradiotherapy may be used for rectal cancers to downstage the tumour before surgery.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Regular surveillance with colonoscopy, CT imaging, and CEA (carcinoembryonic antigen) monitoring is crucial post-treatment to detect recurrence or new primary lesions.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Patients should be educated on symptoms of recurrence and advised to seek prompt medical attention for any new or worsening concerns. Multidisciplinary team (MDT) discussions guide complex cases.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Colorectal cancer"]
  • Bowel obstruction: Occurs when the tumour physically blocks the lumen of the bowel, leading to abdominal pain, distension, vomiting, and absolute constipation, requiring urgent surgical or endoscopic intervention.
  • Bowel perforation: A rare but life-threatening complication where the tumour erodes through the bowel wall, leading to peritonitis and sepsis, necessitating emergency surgery.
  • Haemorrhage: Chronic occult bleeding can lead to iron-deficiency anaemia, while acute severe bleeding can cause hypovolaemic shock, requiring blood transfusion and endoscopic or surgical control.
  • Metastatic disease: Spread to distant organs, most commonly the liver and lungs, leading to organ dysfunction, pain, and systemic symptoms, significantly impacting prognosis and requiring palliative or systemic treatment.
  • Recurrence: Despite curative treatment, local or distant recurrence can occur, necessitating ongoing surveillance and prompt re-evaluation for further treatment options.
