Hyperosmolar hyperglycaemic state (HHS) [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Hyperosmolar hyperglycaemic state (HHS)"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Hyperosmolar hyperglycaemic state (HHS)"][/sc]
  • Hyperosmolar hyperglycaemic state (HHS) is a serious, life-threatening metabolic emergency characterised by severe hyperglycaemia, hyperosmolality, and dehydration, typically without significant ketoacidosis.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical features and biochemical criteria for HHS, differentiate it from diabetic ketoacidosis, and understand the principles of its initial management.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Hyperosmolar hyperglycaemic state (HHS)"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Hyperosmolar hyperglycaemic state (HHS)"][/sc]
  • HHS is less common than diabetic ketoacidosis (DKA), but is associated with a higher mortality rate, ranging from 5% to 20% [1]. It accounts for less than 1% of all diabetes-related hospital admissions [1].
  • Typically affects older individuals, often over 45 years of age, with pre-existing type 2 diabetes mellitus (T2DM), though it can be the presenting feature of T2DM [2].
  • Common precipitants include infections (e.g., chest or urinary tract infections), myocardial infarction, stroke, or other acute illnesses [2].
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Hyperosmolar hyperglycaemic state (HHS)"][/sc]
  • HHS is triggered by a relative insulin deficiency or increased insulin resistance, often precipitated by illness or stress, leading to profound hyperglycaemia.
  • The severe hyperglycaemia results in significant osmotic diuresis, causing substantial fluid and electrolyte depletion, leading to severe dehydration and increased serum osmolality.
  • Unlike DKA, sufficient insulin is typically present to suppress significant lipolysis and ketogenesis, but not enough to control blood glucose, resulting in hyperosmolality and dehydration as the primary metabolic derangements.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Hyperosmolar hyperglycaemic state (HHS)"][/sc]
  • Early phase: HHS typically develops insidiously over several days to weeks, often triggered by an acute illness or inadequate fluid intake.
  • Progressive phase: As hyperglycaemia and osmotic diuresis persist, dehydration and hyperosmolality progressively worsen, leading to increasing lethargy and neurological symptoms, particularly beyond 24-48 hours.
  • Late phase: Without intervention, severe dehydration and electrolyte imbalances can lead to profound neurological impairment, coma, and multi-organ dysfunction, often developing over 3-5 days or more.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Hyperosmolar hyperglycaemic state (HHS)"][/sc]
  • Ask about polyuria and polydipsia - these reflect the osmotic diuresis and developing dehydration.
  • Ask about generalised weakness and lethargy - indicating severe dehydration and metabolic derangement.
  • Ask about altered mental status (e.g., confusion, drowsiness, decreased consciousness) - a critical indicator of severe hyperosmolality affecting cerebral function.
  • Ask about nausea and vomiting - often present due to severe illness, but less prominent than in DKA.
  • Ask about recent illness or reduced fluid intake - identifying potential precipitants for HHS development.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Hyperosmolar hyperglycaemic state (HHS)"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - tachycardia and hypotension indicate significant hypovolaemia; tachypnoea may be present but Kussmaul breathing is absent.
  • Check for signs of severe dehydration - dry mucous membranes, reduced skin turgor, and sunken eyes reflect profound fluid deficit.
  • Check neurological status - altered mental status (confusion, lethargy, drowsiness, or coma) is a key indicator of hyperosmolality affecting the brain.
  • Check general appearance - patients often appear acutely unwell and exhausted.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Hyperosmolar hyperglycaemic state (HHS)"][/sc]
  • Profound altered mental status: Indicates severe hyperosmolality and cerebral dehydration, requiring urgent neurological assessment and aggressive fluid resuscitation.
  • Signs of hypovolaemic shock: Including significant hypotension, tachycardia, and reduced urine output, signifying critical fluid depletion and impending circulatory collapse.
  • Rapidly worsening neurological deficits: Such as focal weakness, seizures, or deepening coma, which may suggest cerebral oedema or other severe complications.
  • Extreme hyperglycaemia (>30 mmol/L) with high calculated serum osmolality (>320 mOsm/kg): These biochemical markers indicate severe metabolic derangement requiring immediate intervention.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Hyperosmolar hyperglycaemic state (HHS)"][/sc]
  • Older adults: May present with more subtle symptoms of dehydration and altered mental status, often attributed to other age-related conditions, delaying diagnosis.
  • Children and adolescents: While traditionally rare, HHS is increasingly recognised in younger individuals with type 2 diabetes, often presenting with severe dehydration and neurological symptoms.
  • Patients on certain medications: Glucocorticoids, diuretics, and some antipsychotics can precipitate HHS or worsen hyperglycaemia, leading to atypical presentations.
  • Patients with underlying renal impairment: May have exaggerated fluid and electrolyte imbalances due to impaired renal compensatory mechanisms.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Hyperosmolar hyperglycaemic state (HHS)"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected HHS:
    • Blood glucose: Typically >30 mmol/L.
    • Serum electrolytes (Na, K, Cl, bicarbonate), urea, and creatinine: To assess dehydration, renal function, and electrolyte imbalances.
    • Calculated serum osmolality: Usually ≥320 mOsm/kg. Formula: (2 x Na+) + glucose + urea.
    • Arterial or venous blood gas: To assess pH and bicarbonate, confirming absence of significant acidosis (pH ≥7.3, bicarbonate ≥15.0 mmol/L).
    • Blood ketones: Typically ≤3.0 mmol/L, distinguishing HHS from DKA.
    • Full blood count: May show haemoconcentration due to dehydration.
  • Severity/risk tools: Consider using Sodium Correction for Hyperglycemia on MDCalc to accurately interpret sodium levels in the presence of severe hyperglycaemia.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Further investigations such as cultures (blood, urine, sputum) to identify underlying infection, ECG for cardiac events, or imaging (e.g., chest X-ray) may be warranted.
  • Serial reassessment: Regular monitoring of blood glucose, electrolytes, osmolality, and clinical status is crucial to guide fluid and insulin therapy and prevent complications.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Hyperosmolar hyperglycaemic state (HHS)"][/sc]
  • An elderly patient with a history of type 2 diabetes presents with a several-day history of increasing thirst, polyuria, profound weakness, and altered mental status, found to have extreme hyperglycaemia and hyperosmolality without significant ketosis.
  • An older adult with poorly controlled type 2 diabetes and an acute illness, presenting with severe dehydration and neurological dysfunction, concerning for hyperosmolar hyperglycaemic state.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Hyperosmolar hyperglycaemic state (HHS)"][/sc]
  • Diabetic Ketoacidosis (DKA) - distinguished by significant ketonaemia and metabolic acidosis (pH <7.3, bicarbonate <15 mmol/L).
  • Severe Dehydration (non-diabetic) - absence of marked hyperglycaemia and hyperosmolality.
  • Stroke or Transient Ischaemic Attack (TIA) - HHS can present with focal neurological deficits, mimicking cerebrovascular events.
  • Sepsis with encephalopathy - sepsis can precipitate HHS, but encephalopathy due to sepsis alone would typically not have the extreme hyperglycaemia and hyperosmolality of HHS.
  • Alcoholic ketoacidosis - characterised by ketosis and acidosis, often with a history of heavy alcohol intake, but typically without the extreme hyperglycaemia of HHS.
  • Uremic encephalopathy - occurs in severe renal failure, presenting with altered mental status, but distinguished by biochemical markers of uraemia rather than extreme hyperglycaemia and hyperosmolality.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Hyperosmolar hyperglycaemic state (HHS)"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Recognition and escalation to a senior clinician. Initial management often includes careful fluid resuscitation with intravenous 0.9% sodium chloride to restore circulating volume and correct dehydration.
  • Definitive management: Fluid replacement is paramount, aiming to replace approximately 50% of the estimated fluid deficit over the first 12 hours, with the remainder over the subsequent 12-24 hours. Insulin therapy is typically initiated after initial fluid resuscitation and when osmolality is falling, to gradually reduce blood glucose.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Frequent monitoring of blood glucose, serum electrolytes (especially sodium and potassium), and calculated serum osmolality is essential. The rate of fall in osmolality should not exceed 3.0-8.0 mOsm/kg/hr, and blood glucose reduction should not exceed 5.0 mmol/L/hr, to prevent complications such as cerebral oedema.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Patients with HHS often require management in a high-dependency or intensive care setting due to the complexity and severity of the condition. Underlying precipitating factors, such as infection, require identification and appropriate treatment.
  • Exam pharmacology reference (UKMLA/MRCGP): Insulin is a key medication in the management of HHS, typically administered intravenously. Prophylactic low molecular weight heparin is often considered due to the increased risk of venous thromboembolism. Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Hyperosmolar hyperglycaemic state (HHS)"][/sc]
  • Cerebral oedema/Osmotic demyelination syndrome (CPM): Can occur if serum osmolality is corrected too rapidly, leading to fluid shifts into brain cells. This is a severe neurological complication that can manifest during the progressive or late phases of treatment.
  • Venous thromboembolism (VTE): The hyperviscosity and dehydration characteristic of HHS significantly increase the risk of deep vein thrombosis (DVT) and pulmonary embolism (PE), particularly during the progressive and late phases.
  • Acute kidney injury (AKI): Severe dehydration and hypoperfusion to the kidneys can lead to AKI, which may develop during the early and progressive phases if fluid resuscitation is inadequate.
  • Cardiac complications: Including myocardial infarction, arrhythmias, or heart failure, especially in older patients with pre-existing cardiovascular disease, exacerbated by severe dehydration and electrolyte imbalances throughout the course of HHS.
  • Hypoglycaemia: A potential complication of overly aggressive insulin therapy, particularly during the treatment phase, requiring careful monitoring.
