Jaundice in pregnancy (Pregnancy and the puerperium) [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Jaundice in pregnancy"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Jaundice in pregnancy"][/sc]
  • Jaundice in pregnancy is the yellow discolouration of skin and sclera due to bilirubin accumulation, often indicating underlying liver dysfunction or haemolysis, with specific pregnancy-related causes such as Intrahepatic Cholestasis of Pregnancy (ICP), Acute Fatty Liver of Pregnancy (AFLP), and HELLP syndrome.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the causes of jaundice in pregnancy, differentiate between benign and serious conditions, and understand the immediate management principles to safeguard maternal and fetal well-being.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Jaundice in pregnancy"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Jaundice in pregnancy"][/sc]
  • Intrahepatic Cholestasis of Pregnancy (ICP) affects approximately 0.7% of pregnancies in the UK, with higher incidence in certain ethnic groups (e.g., South Asian women) [1].
  • Acute Fatty Liver of Pregnancy (AFLP) is a rare but severe condition, occurring in about 1 in 10,000 to 1 in 15,000 pregnancies [2].
  • HELLP syndrome, often considered a severe variant of pre-eclampsia, complicates 0.5-0.9% of all pregnancies and 10-20% of severe pre-eclampsia cases [3].
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Jaundice in pregnancy"][/sc]
  • Trigger: Hormonal changes during pregnancy, particularly high oestrogen and progesterone levels, can impair bile flow and liver function in susceptible individuals, leading to conditions like ICP.
  • Progression: In ICP, impaired hepatobiliary transport of bile acids results in their accumulation in the maternal circulation, causing pruritus and potentially crossing the placenta to affect the fetus [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Bile acid threshold in ICP: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="bile acid accumulation"][/sc]. AFLP involves microvesicular fatty infiltration of hepatocytes, leading to acute liver failure, while HELLP syndrome is characterised by microangiopathic haemolytic anaemia, elevated liver enzymes, and low platelets.
  • Consequence: Unmanaged jaundice in pregnancy can lead to significant maternal morbidity (e.g., liver failure, coagulopathy) and adverse fetal outcomes (e.g., preterm birth, stillbirth, fetal distress).
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Jaundice in pregnancy"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Jaundice can present at any gestational age, but ICP typically manifests in the late second or third trimester, often progressing over days to weeks.
  • Progressive phase: Conditions like AFLP and HELLP syndrome usually develop acutely in the third trimester or early postpartum period, with rapid deterioration over hours to days if not recognised and managed promptly.
  • Late phase: Resolution of jaundice and associated symptoms for ICP typically occurs within days to weeks postpartum. For AFLP and HELLP, recovery can be prolonged, requiring intensive care and close monitoring for several weeks after delivery.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Jaundice in pregnancy"][/sc]
  • Ask about pruritus - often severe, generalised, worse at night, and typically without a rash, highly suggestive of ICP.
  • Ask about nausea and vomiting - common in AFLP and HELLP syndrome, often severe and persistent, distinguishing it from typical pregnancy sickness.
  • Ask about right upper quadrant or epigastric pain - a key symptom in HELLP syndrome and AFLP, indicating liver involvement and potential capsular distension.
  • Ask about fatigue and malaise - non-specific but often pronounced in severe liver conditions like AFLP and HELLP, reflecting systemic illness.
  • Ask about dark urine and pale stools - indicative of cholestasis and impaired bilirubin excretion, suggesting obstructive or hepatocellular jaundice.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Jaundice in pregnancy"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - hypertension and proteinuria are characteristic of pre-eclampsia and HELLP syndrome; fever may indicate infection.
  • Check scleral icterus and skin yellowing - direct visual evidence of jaundice, indicating hyperbilirubinaemia.
  • Check abdominal tenderness - particularly right upper quadrant tenderness, which can be a sign of liver involvement in AFLP or HELLP syndrome.
  • Check oedema - generalised oedema, especially facial and hand swelling, is common in pre-eclampsia and can be present in HELLP syndrome.
  • Check neurological status - altered mental status, confusion, or seizures may indicate hepatic encephalopathy in severe AFLP or eclampsia in HELLP syndrome [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Hepatic encephalopathy in pregnancy: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="hepatic encephalopathy"][/sc].
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Jaundice in pregnancy"][/sc]
  • Severe pruritus with elevated bile acids: Indicates ICP, which carries risks of fetal distress and stillbirth, particularly with very high bile acid levels.
  • Rapid onset of jaundice with systemic symptoms: Suggests acute liver pathology like AFLP or severe HELLP syndrome, requiring urgent medical intervention due to high maternal and fetal mortality.
  • Hypertension and proteinuria with epigastric pain: Highly suspicious for pre-eclampsia with evolving HELLP syndrome, necessitating immediate assessment and management to prevent maternal complications like eclampsia or liver rupture.
  • Altered mental status or seizures: Points towards severe hepatic encephalopathy in AFLP or eclampsia in HELLP syndrome, both obstetric emergencies requiring urgent stabilisation and delivery.
  • Signs of coagulopathy (e.g., easy bruising, bleeding): May indicate severe liver dysfunction in AFLP or severe thrombocytopenia in HELLP syndrome, increasing risk of haemorrhage.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Jaundice in pregnancy"][/sc]
  • Early pregnancy: Jaundice in the first or early second trimester is less commonly due to ICP, AFLP, or HELLP, prompting consideration of other causes such as viral hepatitis or pre-existing liver disease.
  • Postpartum onset: While most conditions manifest antenatally, HELLP syndrome and AFLP can present in the immediate postpartum period, often within 48-72 hours after delivery, requiring continued vigilance.
  • Absence of pruritus in ICP: Rarely, women with ICP may present with elevated bile acids and liver dysfunction without significant itching, making diagnosis challenging and requiring a high index of suspicion [sc name="jitl-query" Q="ICP without pruritus: how is it diagnosed and why does it matter clinically?" text="pruritus-negative ICP"][/sc].
  • Mild pre-eclampsia with HELLP: HELLP syndrome can occur in the absence of severe hypertension or proteinuria, sometimes referred to as "partial" or "atypical" HELLP, making diagnosis difficult if not considered.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Jaundice in pregnancy"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected jaundice in pregnancy:
    • Liver function tests (LFTs): Elevated bilirubin (conjugated and unconjugated), ALT, AST, ALP, GGT.
    • Serum bile acids: Crucial for diagnosing ICP; levels >10 µmol/L are diagnostic, with >40 µmol/L indicating severe ICP [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Serum bile acid levels in ICP: what are the thresholds and their clinical significance?" text="serum bile acids"][/sc].
    • Full blood count (FBC): Thrombocytopenia (HELLP), anaemia (haemolysis in HELLP).
    • Coagulation screen: PT, APTT, fibrinogen (deranged in severe AFLP or HELLP).
    • Urea and electrolytes (U&Es): Assess renal function, often affected in severe pre-eclampsia/HELLP.
    • Urinalysis: Proteinuria (pre-eclampsia/HELLP).
  • Severity/risk tools:
    • For pre-eclampsia: Pre-eclampsia Severity Index on MDCalc (consider if pre-eclampsia is the primary diagnosis or co-exists).
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible:
    • Viral hepatitis screen (Hepatitis A, B, C, E).
    • Autoimmune screen (ANA, ASMA, LKM-1).
    • Ceruloplasmin (Wilson disease).
    • Alpha-1 antitrypsin levels.
  • Imaging - Ultrasound:
    • Abdominal ultrasound to assess liver parenchyma, bile ducts, and rule out gallstones or other structural abnormalities.
  • Serial reassessment:
    • Regular monitoring of LFTs, bile acids, FBC, and coagulation parameters is essential to track disease progression and response to treatment.

  • A pregnant woman with late-trimester pruritus and jaundice, concerning for cholestatic liver disease, requiring urgent differentiation between ICP, AFLP, and HELLP syndrome.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Jaundice in pregnancy"][/sc]
  • Viral Hepatitis (A, B, C, E) - distinguished by specific serological markers and often prodromal flu-like symptoms before jaundice onset.
  • Pre-eclampsia/HELLP Syndrome - typically presents with hypertension, proteinuria, and specific haematological/liver enzyme derangements, though jaundice can be a late feature.
  • Acute Fatty Liver of Pregnancy (AFLP) - characterised by rapid onset of severe liver dysfunction, often with coagulopathy and encephalopathy, requiring urgent delivery.
  • Gallstone disease/Choledocholithiasis - suggested by colicky right upper quadrant pain, fever, and dilated bile ducts on ultrasound, though can co-exist with pregnancy-related liver conditions.
  • Drug-induced liver injury (DILI) - history of new medication use, with varying patterns of liver enzyme elevation, often resolving after drug withdrawal.
  • Pre-existing liver disease (e.g., autoimmune hepatitis, primary biliary cholangitis) - often has a prior history of liver dysfunction or specific autoantibodies, though pregnancy can exacerbate these conditions [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Autoimmune hepatitis in pregnancy: how does it present and what are the management considerations?" text="pre-existing liver disease"][/sc].
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Jaundice in pregnancy"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Recognition of severe liver disease (AFLP, HELLP) warrants urgent multidisciplinary team involvement, including obstetricians, hepatologists, and intensivists, for stabilisation and consideration of timely delivery.
  • Definitive management:
    • For ICP: Ursodeoxycholic acid (UDCA) is often used to improve bile flow, reduce pruritus, and lower bile acid levels, potentially improving fetal outcomes.
    • For AFLP and HELLP syndrome: Prompt delivery of the fetus and placenta is the definitive treatment, often leading to rapid maternal recovery.
  • Adjunctive management:
    • Symptomatic relief for pruritus in ICP (e.g., emollients, antihistamines).
    • Supportive care for AFLP/HELLP, including fluid and electrolyte management, correction of coagulopathy, and management of complications like hypoglycaemia or renal failure.
  • Monitoring and follow-up:
    • Close maternal and fetal monitoring (e.g., fetal growth scans, CTG) is crucial, especially in ICP and pre-eclampsia/HELLP.
    • Postpartum follow-up of LFTs and bile acids to ensure resolution and rule out underlying chronic liver disease.
  • Escalation and safety-netting:
    • Any signs of worsening maternal condition (e.g., increasing jaundice, encephalopathy, coagulopathy) or fetal compromise warrant immediate escalation to a higher level of care.
    • Patients should be counselled on red flag symptoms and when to seek urgent medical attention.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Jaundice in pregnancy"][/sc]
  • Fetal distress and stillbirth (ICP): Elevated maternal bile acids can cross the placenta, leading to meconium staining, fetal arrhythmia, and in severe cases, unexplained stillbirth, particularly with bile acid levels >100 µmol/L.
  • Preterm birth (ICP, AFLP, HELLP): All three conditions are associated with an increased risk of spontaneous or iatrogenic preterm delivery, contributing to neonatal morbidity.
  • Maternal liver failure and coagulopathy (AFLP, HELLP): Severe cases can lead to acute liver failure, disseminated intravascular coagulation (DIC), renal failure, and pancreatitis, posing significant threat to maternal life.
  • Postpartum haemorrhage (AFLP, HELLP): Coagulopathy and uterine atony, particularly in AFLP and severe HELLP, increase the risk of excessive bleeding after delivery.
  • Recurrence in subsequent pregnancies (ICP): Women with a history of ICP have a high recurrence rate (60-90%) in future pregnancies, requiring early screening and management.
