Multiple sclerosis (MS) [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Multiple sclerosis (MS)"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Multiple sclerosis (MS)"][/sc]
  • Multiple sclerosis (MS) is a chronic, inflammatory, demyelinating disease of the central nervous system, leading to a wide range of neurological symptoms.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the varied clinical presentations of multiple sclerosis, understand its progressive nature, and identify key diagnostic and management principles.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Multiple sclerosis (MS)"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Multiple sclerosis (MS)"][/sc]
  • MS affects around 130,000 people in the UK, with a prevalence of approximately 1 in 500, and is more common in women than men (ratio 2-3:1).
  • Onset typically occurs between 20 and 40 years of age, making it the most common cause of non-traumatic neurological disability in young adults.
  • Risk factors include genetic predisposition (e.g., HLA-DRB1*1501 allele), environmental factors such as low vitamin D levels, Epstein-Barr virus infection, and smoking.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Multiple sclerosis (MS)"][/sc]
  • Trigger: An unknown environmental trigger in genetically susceptible individuals leads to an autoimmune response targeting myelin in the central nervous system.
  • Progression: Activated T-cells and B-cells cross the blood-brain barrier, initiating inflammation, demyelination, and axonal damage, forming characteristic plaques or lesions.
  • Consequence: Demyelination impairs nerve impulse conduction, leading to neurological deficits, while axonal loss contributes to irreversible disability.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Multiple sclerosis (MS)"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Characterised by relapses (new or worsening neurological symptoms lasting particularly beyond 24 hours) followed by periods of partial or complete remission.
  • Progressive phase: Over time, many individuals transition to a secondary progressive course, where disability gradually accumulates independent of relapses, particularly beyond 10-15 years from onset.
  • Late phase: Significant neurological impairment, often requiring mobility aids, with increased risk of complications such as infections, particularly beyond 20 years.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Multiple sclerosis (MS)"][/sc]
  • Ask about visual disturbances (e.g., optic neuritis, diplopia) - these are often early and highly suggestive of demyelination.
  • Ask about sensory symptoms (e.g., numbness, tingling, Lhermitte's sign) - common and can be widespread, indicating spinal cord involvement.
  • Ask about motor weakness or spasticity - impacts mobility and daily activities, crucial for assessing functional impairment.
  • Ask about fatigue - a pervasive and often debilitating symptom, significantly affecting quality of life.
  • Ask about bladder dysfunction (e.g., urgency, incontinence) - common in later stages, indicating autonomic involvement.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Multiple sclerosis (MS)"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - generally normal in MS, but fever may indicate infection, which can trigger a relapse.
  • Check eye movements for nystagmus or internuclear ophthalmoplegia - highly indicative of brainstem lesions.
  • Check muscle tone and power for spasticity, weakness, or ataxia - reflects pyramidal tract or cerebellar involvement.
  • Check sensation for altered light touch, pinprick, or proprioception - helps localise spinal cord or brainstem lesions.
  • Check reflexes for briskness or extensor plantar responses - suggests upper motor neuron pathology.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Multiple sclerosis (MS)"][/sc]
  • Rapidly progressive weakness: Suggests an acute inflammatory demyelinating syndrome or other rapidly evolving neurological condition.
  • Fever or systemic illness: May indicate an infection mimicking or triggering an MS relapse, requiring urgent investigation.
  • Isolated peripheral neuropathy: MS is a CNS disease; peripheral symptoms alone warrant investigation for other causes.
  • Early severe cognitive decline: While cognitive impairment occurs in MS, severe decline early on may suggest other neurodegenerative conditions.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Multiple sclerosis (MS)"][/sc]
  • Paediatric MS: Often presents with more frequent relapses and multifocal symptoms, but with better recovery from individual relapses compared to adults.
  • Late-onset MS (LOMS): Onset after age 50, often presents with a primary progressive course and spinal cord symptoms, making diagnosis challenging.
  • Benign MS: Characterised by mild disease and minimal disability accumulation over many years, often diagnosed retrospectively.
  • Fulminant MS (Marburg variant): A rare, aggressive form with rapid progression and severe disability or death within a short period.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Multiple sclerosis (MS)"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected MS: Clinical history and neurological examination, followed by brain and spinal cord MRI with contrast.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Blood tests (e.g., B12, ANA, ESR, CRP, aquaporin-4 antibodies for neuromyelitis optica spectrum disorder).
  • Imaging - MRI: Demonstrates dissemination in space (lesions in at least two CNS areas) and dissemination in time (new lesions or gadolinium-enhancing lesions).
  • Lumbar puncture: Cerebrospinal fluid analysis for oligoclonal bands (OCBs) and elevated IgG index, supporting intrathecal immunoglobulin synthesis.
  • Evoked potentials: Visual, brainstem auditory, or somatosensory evoked potentials can detect subclinical lesions.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Multiple sclerosis (MS)"][/sc]
  • A 32-year-old woman presents with recurrent episodes of unilateral visual loss, numbness in her left arm, and unsteady gait over the past two years, with each episode lasting several weeks and partially resolving.
  • A young adult female with recurrent, multifocal neurological deficits, separated by time and space, suggestive of a chronic demyelinating process.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Multiple sclerosis (MS)"][/sc]
  • Neuromyelitis Optica Spectrum Disorder (NMOSD) - presence of aquaporin-4 or MOG antibodies, often more severe optic neuritis and myelitis.
  • Systemic Lupus Erythematosus (SLE) - systemic symptoms, positive autoantibodies, and CNS involvement can mimic MS.
  • Sarcoidosis - can cause granulomatous inflammation in the CNS, mimicking MS lesions on MRI.
  • Vitamin B12 deficiency - can cause myelopathy and neuropathy, which may be confused with MS symptoms.
  • Acute Disseminated Encephalomyelitis (ADEM) - typically monophasic, often post-infectious, and more diffuse lesions on MRI.
  • Migraine with aura - visual and sensory symptoms can overlap, but typically shorter duration and lack objective neurological signs.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Multiple sclerosis (MS)"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Manage acute relapses with high-dose corticosteroids (e.g., methylprednisolone) to shorten duration and accelerate recovery.
  • Definitive management: Disease-modifying therapies (DMTs) to reduce relapse frequency, slow disability progression, and decrease new lesion formation.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Regular clinical reviews, MRI surveillance, and symptom management (e.g., physiotherapy, occupational therapy, pain management).
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Educate patients on relapse recognition, provide contact information for urgent advice, and discuss treatment escalation if disease activity persists.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Multiple sclerosis (MS)"][/sc]
  • Mobility impairment: Due to weakness, spasticity, and ataxia, leading to falls and reduced independence, particularly in the progressive and late phases.
  • Bladder and bowel dysfunction: Common neurological symptoms that can lead to recurrent urinary tract infections and significant impact on quality of life.
  • Cognitive impairment: Affects memory, attention, and processing speed, impacting employment and daily functioning.
  • Depression and anxiety: High prevalence due to chronic illness, disability, and neurological changes, requiring psychological support.
  • Pain: Neuropathic pain, musculoskeletal pain, and spasticity-related pain are common and can be debilitating.
