Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Bronchopulmonary dysplasia (chronic lung disease of prematurity)"][/sc]
  • Bronchopulmonary dysplasia (BPD) is a chronic lung disease affecting premature infants, characterised by abnormal lung development and persistent respiratory symptoms.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical features, risk factors, and management principles of bronchopulmonary dysplasia in neonates and infants.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Bronchopulmonary dysplasia (chronic lung disease of prematurity)"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Bronchopulmonary dysplasia (chronic lung disease of prematurity)"][/sc]
  • BPD affects approximately 10,000 infants annually in the UK, predominantly those born before 28 weeks gestation or with very low birth weight.
  • Risk factors include extreme prematurity, low birth weight, prolonged mechanical ventilation, oxygen toxicity, infection, and genetic predisposition.
  • The incidence of BPD has remained relatively stable despite advances in neonatal care, with a shift towards milder forms due to improved survival of extremely premature infants.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Bronchopulmonary dysplasia (chronic lung disease of prematurity)"][/sc]
  • Trigger: Premature birth and exposure to injurious factors such as mechanical ventilation and high oxygen concentrations disrupt normal lung development, particularly alveolarisation and vasculogenesis.
  • Progression: This leads to inflammation, impaired lung growth, and remodelling of the airways and pulmonary vasculature, resulting in fewer and larger alveoli and dysmorphic capillaries.
  • Consequence: The outcome is reduced gas exchange efficiency, increased airway resistance, and pulmonary hypertension, manifesting as chronic respiratory insufficiency. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Alveolarisation: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Alveolarisation"][/sc]
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Bronchopulmonary dysplasia (chronic lung disease of prematurity)"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Symptoms typically emerge in the first few weeks of life, often in infants requiring respiratory support, with persistent oxygen requirements beyond 28 days of postnatal age.
  • Progressive phase: Respiratory symptoms may persist and fluctuate for several months, particularly beyond 36 weeks post-menstrual age, often requiring ongoing oxygen and ventilatory support.
  • Late phase: While many infants show improvement over the first two years, some experience long-term respiratory morbidity, including recurrent wheezing, respiratory infections, and exercise intolerance, persisting into childhood and adolescence.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Bronchopulmonary dysplasia (chronic lung disease of prematurity)"][/sc]
  • Ask about persistent tachypnoea - indicates increased work of breathing and respiratory distress.
  • Ask about recurrent wheezing or coughing - suggests airway hyperreactivity and inflammation common in BPD.
  • Ask about feeding difficulties or poor weight gain - respiratory effort can interfere with feeding, leading to failure to thrive.
  • Ask about episodes of cyanosis or desaturation - points to inadequate oxygenation, especially during activity or sleep.
  • Ask about increased oxygen requirements - a hallmark of BPD, indicating impaired gas exchange.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Bronchopulmonary dysplasia (chronic lung disease of prematurity)"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - persistent tachypnoea, tachycardia, and oxygen saturations below target range (e.g., <92-95%) are common.
  • Check for signs of increased work of breathing - nasal flaring, subcostal/intercostal retractions, and grunting.
  • Check chest auscultation - often reveals crackles, wheezes, or diminished breath sounds, reflecting airway obstruction and parenchymal changes.
  • Check for signs of pulmonary hypertension - loud P2, right ventricular heave, hepatomegaly (in severe cases).
  • Check for growth parameters - often show poor weight gain and length, indicative of chronic illness and increased metabolic demand. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Pulmonary hypertension in neonates: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Pulmonary hypertension"][/sc]
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Bronchopulmonary dysplasia (chronic lung disease of prematurity)"][/sc]
  • Acute respiratory decompensation - sudden worsening of breathing, increased oxygen needs, or apnoea, suggesting an acute exacerbation or infection.
  • Signs of right heart failure - peripheral oedema, hepatomegaly, or increasing oxygen requirement, indicating worsening pulmonary hypertension.
  • Severe feeding intolerance with respiratory distress - may lead to aspiration or further respiratory compromise, requiring urgent intervention.
  • Persistent hypoxaemia despite maximal support - suggests severe lung disease or an underlying cardiac shunt.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Bronchopulmonary dysplasia (chronic lung disease of prematurity)"][/sc]
  • Older infants/toddlers: May present with recurrent severe respiratory infections, chronic cough, or exercise intolerance rather than typical neonatal respiratory distress, as the initial acute phase may have been managed.
  • Mild BPD: Infants with milder forms may only have subtle signs of increased work of breathing or require minimal supplemental oxygen, making diagnosis challenging without a history of prematurity.
  • Co-existing cardiac anomalies: Infants with congenital heart disease may have their BPD symptoms masked or exacerbated by cardiac issues, requiring careful differentiation.
  • Neurologically impaired infants: Poor cough reflex and swallowing dysfunction can predispose to recurrent aspiration and worsen respiratory status, complicating the BPD picture. Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Bronchopulmonary dysplasia (chronic lung disease of prematurity)"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected Bronchopulmonary dysplasia: Clinical diagnosis based on oxygen requirement for at least 28 days in a premature infant, often classified by severity at 36 weeks post-menstrual age.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Consider a sweat test for cystic fibrosis, echocardiogram to rule out congenital heart disease, or immunology screen for primary immunodeficiency.
  • Imaging - Chest X-ray: May show diffuse haziness, hyperinflation, atelectasis, and interstitial changes; not diagnostic but supportive.
  • Serial reassessment: Regular monitoring of oxygen saturation, growth, and respiratory status is crucial for assessing disease progression and response to therapy.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Bronchopulmonary dysplasia (chronic lung disease of prematurity)"][/sc]
  • A 6-month-old male infant, born at 26 weeks gestation, presents with persistent tachypnoea, recurrent wheezing, and ongoing oxygen requirement since birth, despite optimal medical management.
  • A premature infant with chronic respiratory insufficiency, characterised by persistent oxygen dependence and recurrent exacerbations, secondary to arrested lung development.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Bronchopulmonary dysplasia (chronic lung disease of prematurity)"][/sc]
  • Cystic fibrosis - pancreatic insufficiency, meconium ileus, positive sweat test.
  • Congenital heart disease - cardiac murmurs, specific echocardiographic findings, different oxygen saturation patterns.
  • Primary ciliary dyskinesia - chronic wet cough, situs inversus, abnormal ciliary function.
  • Recurrent viral bronchiolitis - episodic wheezing, often triggered by infections, typically resolves.
  • Tracheomalacia/bronchomalacia - inspiratory stridor, barking cough, dynamic airway collapse on bronchoscopy.
  • Gastro-oesophageal reflux disease with aspiration - feeding difficulties, recurrent pneumonia, evidence of aspiration on swallow study.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Bronchopulmonary dysplasia (chronic lung disease of prematurity)"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Optimise respiratory support (oxygen, non-invasive ventilation), manage fluid balance, and ensure adequate nutrition to support lung growth.
  • Definitive management: Long-term oxygen therapy, diuretics to manage pulmonary oedema, bronchodilators for airway reactivity, and corticosteroids (inhaled or systemic for exacerbations).
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Regular respiratory assessments, growth monitoring, developmental surveillance, and immunisations (especially RSV prophylaxis).
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Educate parents on signs of respiratory distress, provide emergency contact information, and establish a clear plan for acute exacerbations.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Bronchopulmonary dysplasia (chronic lung disease of prematurity)"][/sc]
  • Pulmonary hypertension: Due to vascular remodelling and hypoxaemia, leading to increased afterload on the right ventricle and potential right heart failure.
  • Recurrent respiratory infections: Impaired mucociliary clearance and chronic inflammation increase susceptibility to viral (e.g., RSV) and bacterial infections, causing exacerbations.
  • Growth failure: Increased metabolic demands from chronic respiratory effort and feeding difficulties can impede normal growth and development.
  • Neurodevelopmental impairment: Often co-occurs with prematurity and BPD, including cognitive delays, cerebral palsy, and sensory deficits, due to perinatal brain injury and chronic hypoxaemia.
  • Airway malacia: Weakness of the tracheal or bronchial walls can lead to dynamic airway collapse, causing stridor, wheezing, and recurrent atelectasis.
