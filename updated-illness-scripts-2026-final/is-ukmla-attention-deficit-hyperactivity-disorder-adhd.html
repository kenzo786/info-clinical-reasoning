Attention deficit hyperactivity disorder (ADHD) [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Attention deficit hyperactivity disorder (ADHD)"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Attention deficit hyperactivity disorder (ADHD)"][/sc]
  • Attention deficit hyperactivity disorder (ADHD) is a neurodevelopmental condition characterised by persistent patterns of inattention and/or hyperactivity-impulsivity that interfere with functioning or development.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the core symptoms of ADHD and understand its impact on daily life and academic/occupational performance.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Attention deficit hyperactivity disorder (ADHD)"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Attention deficit hyperactivity disorder (ADHD)"][/sc]
  • In the UK, ADHD is estimated to affect 3-5% of school-aged children and adolescents, with prevalence rates in adults around 2-5% (NICE, 2018).
  • Risk factors include genetic predisposition (heritability estimated at 70-80%), premature birth, low birth weight, and maternal smoking or alcohol use during pregnancy.
  • ADHD is more commonly diagnosed in males in childhood, but prevalence rates are more similar in adulthood, with females often presenting with predominantly inattentive symptoms.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Attention deficit hyperactivity disorder (ADHD)"][/sc]
  • Dysregulation of neurotransmitter systems, particularly dopamine and noradrenaline, in prefrontal cortical-striatal-thalamic-cortical circuits, is implicated in ADHD.
  • This leads to impaired executive functions, including working memory, inhibitory control, and sustained attention, affecting the ability to regulate behaviour and attention.
  • The consequence is a persistent pattern of inattention and/or hyperactivity-impulsivity that significantly impacts academic, occupational, and social functioning.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Attention deficit hyperactivity disorder (ADHD)"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Symptoms typically emerge in early childhood, often before the age of 7, and must be present in multiple settings for diagnosis.
  • Progressive phase: Symptoms may persist into adolescence and adulthood, though hyperactivity can become less prominent, manifesting as restlessness or inner fidgeting, particularly beyond early adulthood.
  • Late phase: ADHD is a lifelong condition for many, with symptoms fluctuating in severity but often requiring ongoing management to mitigate functional impairment.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Attention deficit hyperactivity disorder (ADHD)"][/sc]
  • Ask about inattention - difficulty sustaining attention in tasks or play activities, often leading to careless mistakes or incomplete work.
  • Ask about hyperactivity - excessive motor activity, fidgeting, squirming, or an inability to remain seated when expected.
  • Ask about impulsivity - difficulty waiting for one's turn, interrupting others, or making hasty decisions without considering consequences.
  • Ask about emotional dysregulation - frequent mood swings, irritability, or difficulty managing frustration, which can impact social relationships.
  • Ask about executive dysfunction - problems with planning, organisation, time management, and task initiation, which are crucial for academic and occupational success.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Attention deficit hyperactivity disorder (ADHD)"][/sc]
  • Check for restlessness or fidgeting - observed during consultation, indicating underlying hyperactivity.
  • Check for difficulty maintaining eye contact or shifting attention - suggesting inattentiveness.
  • Check for disorganised appearance or difficulty following instructions - reflecting executive function challenges.
  • Check vital signs - typically normal, but important to rule out other conditions or medication side effects.
  • Check for [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Co-occurring conditions with ADHD: what are they and why do they matter clinically?" text="co-occurring conditions"] such as anxiety, depression, or learning difficulties - these are common and can complicate presentation.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Attention deficit hyperactivity disorder (ADHD)"][/sc]
  • Acute onset of symptoms - suggests an alternative neurological or psychiatric cause rather than a neurodevelopmental condition.
  • Symptoms only present in one setting - indicates situational factors rather than a pervasive neurodevelopmental disorder.
  • Significant decline in cognitive function - may point towards a progressive neurological disorder or substance misuse.
  • Psychotic symptoms - requires urgent assessment for primary psychotic disorders.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Attention deficit hyperactivity disorder (ADHD)"][/sc]
  • Adults: Hyperactivity may manifest as inner restlessness, difficulty relaxing, or excessive talking, rather than overt motor activity. Inattention can lead to chronic disorganisation and procrastination.
  • Females: Often present with predominantly inattentive symptoms, which can be misattributed to anxiety or depression, leading to delayed diagnosis.
  • Older adults: Diagnosis is rare, but symptoms may be masked by age-related cognitive decline or co-morbid conditions, making recognition challenging.
  • Individuals with high intelligence: May compensate for ADHD symptoms, leading to later diagnosis or presentation only when academic/occupational demands increase significantly.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Attention deficit hyperactivity disorder (ADHD)"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected ADHD: Comprehensive clinical interview with the individual and collateral information from parents, teachers, or partners. Use of validated rating scales (e.g., Conners' Rating Scales, ADHD Rating Scale-IV) to assess symptom severity and impairment.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Consider blood tests (e.g., thyroid function, full blood count) to rule out medical conditions mimicking ADHD symptoms.
  • Neuropsychological assessment: May be used to assess specific cognitive functions, but is not typically required for diagnosis.
  • Screening for co-occurring conditions: Essential to assess for anxiety, depression, learning disabilities, and autism spectrum disorder, which frequently co-exist with ADHD.
