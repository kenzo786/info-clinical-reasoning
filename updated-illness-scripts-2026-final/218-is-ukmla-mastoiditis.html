Mastoiditis [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Mastoiditis"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Mastoiditis"][/sc]
  • Mastoiditis is an acute inflammation of the mastoid air cells within the temporal bone, typically arising as a complication of acute otitis media.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical features of mastoiditis, understand its potential complications, and initiate appropriate urgent management, including referral to ENT specialists.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Mastoiditis"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Mastoiditis"][/sc]
  • In the UK, the annual incidence of mastoiditis ranges between 1.2 and 4.2 per 100,000 individuals, with a higher prevalence in children [1].
  • Risk factors include severe or recurrent acute otitis media, particularly in young children, and inadequate or delayed treatment of middle ear infections [2].
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Mastoiditis"][/sc]
  • Bacterial infection, most commonly *Streptococcus pneumoniae*, *Haemophilus influenzae*, or *Streptococcus pyogenes*, spreads from the middle ear to the mastoid air cells, often following acute otitis media.
  • Inflammation and oedema of the mastoid mucosa lead to obstruction of the aditus ad antrum, impairing drainage and causing pus accumulation within the mastoid air cells [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Mastoid air cell anatomy: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="mastoid air cell anatomy"][/sc].
  • Increased pressure and osteitis can result in bone erosion, forming subperiosteal abscesses or spreading to adjacent structures, leading to intracranial or extracranial complications [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Complications of mastoiditis: what are they and why do they matter clinically?" text="complications of mastoiditis"][/sc].
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Mastoiditis"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Symptoms typically develop days to weeks after the onset of acute otitis media, often presenting with persistent ear pain, fever, and general malaise despite initial antibiotic treatment for the ear infection.
  • Progressive phase: As the infection advances, localised tenderness and swelling over the mastoid process become evident, often displacing the pinna anteriorly and inferiorly. Systemic symptoms may worsen, indicating potential spread.
  • Late phase: Without effective intervention, the infection can erode the mastoid bone, leading to abscess formation (subperiosteal or Bezold's abscess) and potentially life-threatening intracranial complications, particularly beyond 48-72 hours of worsening symptoms.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Mastoiditis"][/sc]
  • Ask about persistent, throbbing ear pain - often disproportionate to acute otitis media and worsening at night, indicating inflammation and pressure within the mastoid.
  • Ask about recent or recurrent ear infections - mastoiditis is almost always a complication of inadequately treated or severe acute otitis media.
  • Ask about headache - particularly if localised to the temporal or occipital region, suggesting potential intracranial involvement or spread.
  • Ask about discharge from the ear - especially if profuse, pulsatile, or persistent despite treatment, indicating tympanic membrane perforation and ongoing infection.
  • Ask about fever and general malaise - systemic inflammatory response is common, indicating significant infection.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Mastoiditis"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - fever, tachycardia, and tachypnoea may indicate systemic sepsis or severe infection.
  • Check for post-auricular tenderness, erythema, and swelling - classic signs of mastoid inflammation, often causing the pinna to be displaced outwards and downwards [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Displaced pinna in mastoiditis: what does it indicate and why does it matter clinically?" text="displaced pinna"][/sc].
  • Check for loss of the post-auricular sulcus - swelling obliterates the normal crease behind the ear, a key diagnostic sign.
  • Check otoscopy findings - signs of acute otitis media (bulging, red tympanic membrane) or perforation with purulent discharge are often present.
  • Check for facial nerve palsy - a rare but serious complication indicating spread of infection to the facial nerve canal.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Mastoiditis"][/sc]
  • Meningism: Neck stiffness, photophobia, or altered mental status suggests meningitis, a life-threatening intracranial complication requiring immediate neurosurgical review.
  • Focal neurological deficits: Weakness, numbness, or seizures indicate potential brain abscess or venous sinus thrombosis, necessitating urgent imaging and specialist intervention.
  • Significant proptosis or orbital swelling: Suggests spread of infection to the orbit, requiring urgent ophthalmological and ENT assessment.
  • Persistent or worsening symptoms despite IV antibiotics for >48 hours: Indicates treatment failure or developing complications, warranting re-evaluation and consideration of surgical intervention.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Mastoiditis"][/sc]
  • Immunocompromised patients: May present with less pronounced inflammatory signs and symptoms, making diagnosis challenging; a high index of suspicion is required [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Mastoiditis in immunocompromised patients: how does it differ and why does it matter clinically?" text="immunocompromised mastoiditis"][/sc].
  • Neonates and infants: Symptoms can be non-specific, such as irritability, feeding difficulties, or unexplained fever, without obvious post-auricular swelling due to an underdeveloped mastoid process.
  • Chronic mastoiditis: May present with recurrent ear discharge, hearing loss, and cholesteatoma, often without acute inflammatory signs, requiring different diagnostic and management approaches.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Mastoiditis"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected mastoiditis:
    • Full blood count (FBC) - often shows leucocytosis with neutrophilia, indicating bacterial infection.
    • C-reactive protein (CRP) and Erythrocyte Sedimentation Rate (ESR) - typically elevated, reflecting systemic inflammation.
    • Ear swab for microbiology - culture and sensitivity of any ear discharge guides targeted antibiotic therapy.
  • Imaging - CT Mastoids: High-resolution computed tomography (CT) of the temporal bones is the gold standard for confirming diagnosis, assessing the extent of mastoid air cell opacification, bone erosion, and identifying abscess formation or intracranial spread [sc name="jitl-query" Q="CT Mastoids in mastoiditis: what does it show and why does it matter clinically?" text="CT Mastoids"][/sc].
  • Imaging - MRI: Magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) with contrast is superior for detecting intracranial complications such as meningitis, brain abscess, or venous sinus thrombosis.
  • Severity/risk tools: Consider using a paediatric early warning score (PEWS) for ongoing assessment of clinical deterioration in children.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Mastoiditis"][/sc]
  • A 5-year-old child presents with a 3-day history of worsening right ear pain, fever, and a tender, erythematous swelling behind the ear, displacing the pinna, following an incompletely treated acute otitis media.
  • Child with recent acute otitis media, now presenting with post-auricular inflammation and systemic signs, concerning for mastoiditis.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Mastoiditis"][/sc]
  • Acute otitis externa: Pain is typically exacerbated by tragal pressure or movement of the pinna; mastoid tenderness and swelling are absent.
  • Perichondritis: Inflammation of the ear cartilage, often following trauma or piercing, but typically spares the mastoid process.
  • Lymphadenitis: Swollen, tender post-auricular lymph nodes may mimic mastoiditis, but the nodes are usually discrete and mobile, and there is no associated pinna displacement or bony tenderness.
  • Cellulitis: Localised skin infection that can affect the post-auricular area, but usually lacks the deep bony tenderness and pinna displacement characteristic of mastoiditis.
  • Furuncle/abscess of the external auditory canal: Localised painful swelling within the ear canal, without mastoid involvement.
  • Ramsay Hunt Syndrome (Herpes Zoster Oticus): Characterised by facial nerve palsy, ear pain, and vesicular rash in the external auditory canal or pinna, but typically without signs of deep infection [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Ramsay Hunt Syndrome: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Ramsay Hunt Syndrome"][/sc].
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Mastoiditis"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Prompt recognition, urgent referral to ENT, and initiation of intravenous antibiotics are crucial to prevent complications. Supportive care includes analgesia and antipyretics.
  • Definitive management: Intravenous antibiotics are the mainstay of treatment, often a broad-spectrum agent such as ceftriaxone or co-amoxiclav, tailored to local resistance patterns and culture results.
  • Surgical intervention: If there is no clinical improvement after 24-48 hours of IV antibiotics, or if complications (e.g., subperiosteal abscess, bone erosion) are present, surgical drainage (e.g., cortical mastoidectomy, myringotomy with grommet insertion) may be warranted.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Close inpatient monitoring for clinical response and signs of complications is essential. Follow-up with ENT is required to ensure complete resolution and assess for any residual hearing loss.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Patients and carers should be advised on red flag symptoms (e.g., worsening headache, neurological changes) that necessitate immediate medical review.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Mastoiditis"][/sc]
  • Subperiosteal abscess: Accumulation of pus between the mastoid bone and its periosteum, typically presenting as a fluctuant swelling behind the ear, often requiring surgical drainage.
  • Intracranial complications: Rare but serious, including meningitis, extradural abscess, subdural empyema, brain abscess, and lateral sinus thrombosis, particularly if infection is prolonged or untreated beyond 72 hours.
  • Facial nerve palsy: Inflammation or compression of the facial nerve within its canal, leading to weakness or paralysis of facial muscles on the affected side.
  • Hearing loss: Can be conductive (due to middle ear effusion or ossicular damage) or sensorineural (due to spread of inflammation to the inner ear), potentially permanent if not managed promptly.
  • Osteomyelitis: Chronic infection of the mastoid bone itself, leading to persistent pain, discharge, and requiring prolonged antibiotic therapy or surgical debridement.
