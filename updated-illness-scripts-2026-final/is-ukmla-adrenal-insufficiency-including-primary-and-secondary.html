Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Adrenal insufficiency (including primary and secondary)"][/sc]
  • Adrenal insufficiency is a clinical syndrome resulting from inadequate production of glucocorticoids, and often mineralocorticoids, by the adrenal glands.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical features, investigations, and initial management of primary and secondary adrenal insufficiency, including adrenal crisis.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Adrenal insufficiency (including primary and secondary)"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Adrenal insufficiency (including primary and secondary)"][/sc]
  • Primary adrenal insufficiency (Addison's disease) has an estimated prevalence of 93-140 per million in the UK, with an incidence of 4.7-6.2 per million per year [1].
  • Secondary adrenal insufficiency is more common, often due to exogenous glucocorticoid use or pituitary/hypothalamic disease.
  • Risk factors for primary adrenal insufficiency include autoimmune conditions (e.g., type 1 diabetes, pernicious anaemia, thyroid disease), while risk factors for secondary adrenal insufficiency include long-term corticosteroid therapy, pituitary tumours, and cranial irradiation.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Adrenal insufficiency (including primary and secondary)"][/sc]
  • Trigger: Damage to the adrenal cortex (primary) or impaired ACTH secretion from the pituitary/hypothalamus (secondary) leads to reduced hormone synthesis.
  • Progression: Decreased cortisol (and often aldosterone in primary AI) results in impaired stress response, electrolyte imbalances, and metabolic disturbances.
  • Consequence: Uncorrected hormone deficiencies can lead to adrenal crisis, a life-threatening emergency characterised by hypotension, shock, and severe metabolic derangements.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Adrenal insufficiency (including primary and secondary)"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Symptoms often develop insidiously over weeks to months, including fatigue, weakness, and gastrointestinal upset.
  • Progressive phase: As hormone deficiency worsens, symptoms become more pronounced, with weight loss, postural dizziness, and skin hyperpigmentation (in primary AI) becoming evident, particularly beyond a few months.
  • Late phase: Without diagnosis and treatment, patients are at risk of developing an acute adrenal crisis, often precipitated by stress or illness, which can manifest rapidly over hours to days.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Adrenal insufficiency (including primary and secondary)"][/sc]
  • Ask about chronic fatigue and malaise - often an early and non-specific symptom, indicating systemic metabolic disturbance.
  • Ask about muscle weakness and joint pain - can be debilitating and contribute to reduced quality of life.
  • Ask about nausea, vomiting, abdominal pain, and diarrhoea - common gastrointestinal manifestations due to cortisol deficiency.
  • Ask about weight loss and anorexia - often progressive and concerning, reflecting chronic illness.
  • Ask about postural dizziness or syncope - suggests orthostatic hypotension due to mineralocorticoid deficiency (primary AI) or hypovolaemia.
  • Ask about increased skin pigmentation (especially in sun-exposed areas, scars, palmar creases) - a key diagnostic clue for primary adrenal insufficiency due to elevated ACTH.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Adrenal insufficiency (including primary and secondary)"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - hypotension, particularly orthostatic, and tachycardia are common, especially in adrenal crisis.
  • Check for hyperpigmentation of skin and mucous membranes (e.g., buccal mucosa, gums) - a classic sign of primary adrenal insufficiency.
  • Check for signs of dehydration - reduced skin turgor, dry mucous membranes, sunken eyes, indicating fluid loss.
  • Check for muscle wasting and generalised weakness - reflects chronic catabolic state.
  • Check for loss of axillary and pubic hair in women - can indicate adrenal androgen deficiency.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Adrenal insufficiency (including primary and secondary)"][/sc]
  • Acute deterioration with hypotension and shock - suggests adrenal crisis, requiring immediate intervention.
  • Unexplained severe abdominal pain with vomiting - can mimic an acute abdomen but may be a presentation of adrenal crisis.
  • Profound hyponatraemia and hyperkalaemia - electrolyte derangements highly suggestive of primary adrenal insufficiency.
  • Hypoglycaemia, especially in children or during stress - indicates severe glucocorticoid deficiency.
  • Rapidly progressive weakness and confusion - may signify worsening adrenal function or impending crisis.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Adrenal insufficiency (including primary and secondary)"][/sc]
  • Older adults: Symptoms may be non-specific and attributed to ageing or other comorbidities, leading to delayed diagnosis.
  • Pregnancy: Adrenal insufficiency can be difficult to diagnose due to physiological changes mimicking symptoms, and adrenal crisis poses risks to both mother and foetus.
  • Immunosuppressed: Patients on chronic glucocorticoids for immunosuppression are at risk of secondary adrenal insufficiency if steroids are abruptly withdrawn, or during periods of stress.
  • Paediatric population: May present with poor feeding, vomiting, lethargy, and failure to thrive; adrenal crisis can be rapidly fatal.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Adrenal insufficiency (including primary and secondary)"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected adrenal insufficiency: Short synacthen test (ACTH stimulation test) is the gold standard. Measure serum cortisol at baseline, 30, and 60 minutes after 250 micrograms of synthetic ACTH (tetracosactide) administration.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Full blood count, urea and electrolytes, liver function tests, thyroid function tests, random glucose.
  • Imaging - Adrenal glands: CT abdomen may be used to investigate the cause of primary adrenal insufficiency (e.g., adrenal haemorrhage, tuberculosis, metastases).
  • Imaging - Pituitary: MRI pituitary may be indicated for suspected secondary adrenal insufficiency to identify pituitary or hypothalamic pathology.
  • Serial reassessment: Electrolytes and glucose should be monitored, especially during initiation of replacement therapy or in adrenal crisis.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Adrenal insufficiency (including primary and secondary)"][/sc]
  • A 45-year-old female presents with a 3-month history of progressive fatigue, weight loss, postural dizziness, and increased skin pigmentation, alongside recurrent episodes of nausea and vomiting.
  • A middle-aged woman with chronic insidious fatigue, gastrointestinal upset, and orthostatic symptoms, exhibiting hyperpigmentation, suggesting a chronic endocrine deficiency.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Adrenal insufficiency (including primary and secondary)"][/sc]
  • Chronic fatigue syndrome - lacks specific endocrine signs and biochemical abnormalities.
  • Depression - often presents with fatigue and weight changes, but typically without electrolyte disturbances or hyperpigmentation.
  • Anorexia nervosa - shares weight loss and fatigue, but typically involves distorted body image and intentional food restriction.
  • Hypothyroidism - causes fatigue and weight gain, but usually presents with bradycardia and cold intolerance, and distinct thyroid function test abnormalities.
  • Phaeochromocytoma - can cause episodic hypertension, palpitations, and sweating, but usually not chronic fatigue and hypotension.
  • Gastroenteritis - acute onset of nausea, vomiting, and diarrhoea, but typically self-limiting and without chronic systemic features.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Adrenal insufficiency (including primary and secondary)"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: In suspected adrenal crisis, administer intravenous hydrocortisone, intravenous fluids (0.9% sodium chloride or 5% glucose), and correct electrolyte imbalances.
  • Definitive management: Long-term hormone replacement therapy with glucocorticoids (e.g., hydrocortisone) and, for primary adrenal insufficiency, mineralocorticoids (e.g., fludrocortisone).
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Regular clinical review, blood pressure monitoring, and electrolyte checks. Adjust hormone doses based on clinical response and biochemical parameters.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Educate patients on "sick day rules" for increasing glucocorticoid dose during illness or stress, and provide an emergency hydrocortisone injection kit.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Adrenal insufficiency (including primary and secondary)"][/sc]
  • Adrenal crisis: A life-threatening emergency due to severe deficiency of adrenal hormones, often precipitated by stress, infection, or trauma, leading to shock and multi-organ failure.
  • Electrolyte imbalances: Chronic hyponatraemia and hyperkalaemia (in primary AI) can lead to cardiac arrhythmias and neurological dysfunction.
  • Osteoporosis: Long-term glucocorticoid replacement, even at physiological doses, can contribute to reduced bone mineral density.
  • Psychological impact: Chronic illness, fatigue, and the need for lifelong medication can lead to anxiety, depression, and reduced quality of life.
  • Increased susceptibility to infections: Impaired cortisol response can compromise immune function, increasing risk of severe infections.
