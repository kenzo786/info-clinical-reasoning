Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Occupational lung disease (Lungs, pleura and airways)"][/sc]
  • Occupational lung diseases encompass a range of respiratory conditions caused or exacerbated by workplace exposures to dusts, chemicals, or other irritants.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical features and occupational exposures associated with common occupational lung diseases, including asbestosis, silicosis, coal workers pneumoconiosis, and occupational asthma, and understand the principles of their investigation and management.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Occupational lung disease (Lungs, pleura and airways)"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Occupational lung disease (Lungs, pleura and airways)"][/sc]
  • In the UK, over 5,000 deaths annually are attributed to asbestos-related diseases, with mesothelioma diagnoses exceeding 2,700 cases per year (NHS, 2025).
  • Silicosis remains a concern, with approximately 14 cases reported annually between 2018-2023, and an estimated 600,000 workers exposed to silica each year (Thorax, 2024; British Safety Council, 2024).
  • Occupational asthma accounts for approximately 25% of all acute work-related respiratory diseases, frequently affecting craft-related occupations and machinists (NIH, 2005).
  • Risk factors include prolonged exposure to specific airborne agents (e.g., asbestos, silica, coal dust, diisocyanates, flour, latex), inadequate personal protective equipment, and certain genetic predispositions.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Occupational lung disease (Lungs, pleura and airways)"][/sc]
  • Trigger: Inhalation of harmful airborne particles (e.g., asbestos fibres, crystalline silica, coal dust) or sensitising agents (e.g., isocyanates, flour, latex) initiates an inflammatory or fibrotic response in the respiratory tract.
  • Progression: Chronic exposure leads to persistent inflammation, immune cell recruitment, and fibroblast activation, resulting in tissue damage, remodelling, and fibrosis. In occupational asthma, repeated exposure to a sensitiser triggers IgE-mediated or non-IgE-mediated hypersensitivity reactions.
  • Consequence: Progressive lung damage impairs gas exchange, reduces lung compliance, and can lead to chronic respiratory symptoms, irreversible lung function decline, and increased susceptibility to other respiratory infections or malignancies. Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Occupational lung disease (Lungs, pleura and airways)"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Initial exposure may be asymptomatic or cause acute, transient symptoms such as cough or irritation. Occupational asthma can manifest within weeks to months of sensitisation.
  • Progressive phase: For fibrotic diseases like asbestosis and silicosis, symptoms typically develop insidiously over decades (often 10-40 years) following initial exposure, as cumulative damage leads to significant lung pathology. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Asbestos exposure latency: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Asbestos exposure latency"][/sc]
  • Late phase: Advanced disease is characterised by chronic, debilitating respiratory symptoms, progressive lung function decline, and potential complications such as respiratory failure, cor pulmonale, or malignancy (e.g., lung cancer, mesothelioma).
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Occupational lung disease (Lungs, pleura and airways)"][/sc]
  • Ask about dyspnoea - often progressive, initially on exertion, indicating restrictive lung disease (e.g., asbestosis, silicosis) or airflow obstruction (e.g., occupational asthma).
  • Ask about chronic cough - may be dry in fibrotic conditions or productive in coal workers pneumoconiosis or occupational asthma.
  • Ask about chest tightness or wheeze - particularly relevant in occupational asthma, often worse during workdays and improving on weekends or holidays.
  • Ask about pleuritic chest pain - can indicate pleural involvement, such as pleural plaques or mesothelioma in asbestos-related disease.
  • Ask about constitutional symptoms (e.g., fatigue, weight loss) - may suggest advanced disease or malignancy.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Occupational lung disease (Lungs, pleura and airways)"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - tachypnoea, tachycardia, and hypoxaemia may be present in advanced disease.
  • Check respiratory examination - fine end-inspiratory crackles (bibasal in asbestosis, diffuse in silicosis), reduced breath sounds, or wheeze (occupational asthma).
  • Check for finger clubbing - can be present in asbestosis and other fibrotic lung diseases.
  • Check for signs of cor pulmonale - peripheral oedema, elevated JVP, right ventricular heave in severe, long-standing disease.
  • Check for pleural effusions or masses - may indicate mesothelioma or other asbestos-related pleural disease.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Occupational lung disease (Lungs, pleura and airways)"][/sc]
  • Rapidly progressive dyspnoea: Suggests acute exacerbation, severe progression of fibrosis, or superimposed infection.
  • Haemoptysis: Raises concern for malignancy (e.g., lung cancer, mesothelioma) or severe parenchymal damage.
  • Unexplained weight loss and constitutional symptoms: May indicate underlying malignancy or advanced systemic disease.
  • New onset or worsening pleuritic chest pain with pleural effusion: Requires urgent investigation for mesothelioma or other serious pleural pathology.
  • Significant decline in lung function over a short period: Warrants immediate review to identify reversible causes or rapid disease progression.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Occupational lung disease (Lungs, pleura and airways)"][/sc]
  • Latent presentation: Many occupational lung diseases, particularly those related to asbestos and silica, have very long latency periods (decades), meaning the link to past occupational exposure may be overlooked.
  • Co-morbidities masking symptoms: Patients with pre-existing COPD or asthma may have their occupational lung disease symptoms attributed solely to their existing conditions, delaying diagnosis.
  • Non-specific symptoms: Early stages can present with vague symptoms like fatigue or mild cough, making diagnosis challenging without a detailed occupational history.
  • Pleural-only disease: Asbestos exposure can lead to isolated pleural plaques or benign asbestos pleural effusion without significant parenchymal lung disease, which may be asymptomatic or cause atypical chest pain.
  • Occupational asthma with non-work triggers: While typically worse at work, some individuals with occupational asthma may develop persistent symptoms that are no longer solely linked to workplace exposure.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Occupational lung disease (Lungs, pleura and airways)"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected occupational lung disease:
    • Detailed occupational history: Crucial for identifying relevant exposures, including duration, intensity, and latency.
    • Spirometry with bronchodilator reversibility: To assess for obstructive or restrictive patterns and reversibility.
    • Chest X-ray: May show pleural plaques (asbestosis), nodular opacities (silicosis, CWP), or interstitial changes.
    • High-resolution computed tomography (HRCT) of the thorax: More sensitive for detecting early fibrotic changes, pleural disease, and differentiating types of occupational lung disease.
  • Severity/risk tools:
    • MDCalc link for relevant severity score (if applicable).
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible:
    • Alpha-1 antitrypsin levels: If emphysema is present, especially in non-smokers.
    • Autoimmune screen: If connective tissue disease-associated interstitial lung disease is suspected.
  • Imaging - CT Thorax:
    • HRCT is essential for characterising parenchymal and pleural abnormalities, guiding diagnosis, and monitoring disease progression.
  • Serial reassessment:
    • Regular lung function tests and imaging are often required to monitor disease progression and treatment response.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Occupational lung disease (Lungs, pleura and airways)"][/sc]
  • Idiopathic Pulmonary Fibrosis (IPF) - absence of identifiable occupational exposure and characteristic HRCT patterns (e.g., usual interstitial pneumonia).
  • Chronic Obstructive Pulmonary Disease (COPD) - primarily linked to smoking, with predominantly obstructive spirometry and emphysematous changes on imaging.
  • Sarcoidosis - multi-system granulomatous disease, often with hilar lymphadenopathy and specific histological findings.
  • Hypersensitivity Pneumonitis - immune-mediated lung disease due to inhaled organic antigens, often with a more acute or subacute presentation and specific HRCT features.
  • Connective Tissue Disease-associated Interstitial Lung Disease (CTD-ILD) - presence of systemic autoimmune features and positive autoantibodies.
  • Lung Cancer - can co-exist with occupational lung disease, but typically presents with focal mass lesions or lymphadenopathy.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Occupational lung disease (Lungs, pleura and airways)"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities:
    • Remove from exposure: Essential to prevent further disease progression.
    • Supportive care: Oxygen therapy for hypoxaemia, bronchodilators for airflow obstruction.
    • Referral to occupational health and respiratory specialist: For definitive diagnosis, management, and compensation claims (e.g., Industrial Injuries Disablement Benefit).
  • Definitive management:
    • No specific cure for most fibrotic occupational lung diseases; management focuses on slowing progression and symptom control.
    • Antifibrotic agents (e.g., pirfenidone, nintedanib) may be considered for progressive fibrotic conditions like asbestosis, similar to IPF.
    • Occupational asthma: Avoidance of sensitiser is paramount; inhaled corticosteroids and bronchodilators as per asthma guidelines.
  • Adjunctive management:
    • Pulmonary rehabilitation: To improve exercise tolerance and quality of life.
    • Vaccinations: Annual influenza and pneumococcal vaccination to prevent respiratory infections.
    • Smoking cessation: Crucial to reduce risk of lung cancer and accelerate disease progression.
  • Monitoring and follow-up:
    • Regular respiratory function tests, imaging, and clinical review to monitor disease stability or progression.
    • Screening for lung cancer, especially in asbestos-exposed individuals.
  • Escalation and safety-netting:
    • Patients should be advised to seek urgent medical attention for worsening dyspnoea, haemoptysis, or signs of infection.
    • Discussion of end-of-life care and palliative support as disease progresses.
  • Exam pharmacology reference (UKMLA/MRCGP):

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Occupational lung disease (Lungs, pleura and airways)"][/sc]
  • Respiratory failure: Progressive fibrosis and impaired gas exchange can lead to chronic hypoxaemia and hypercapnia, requiring long-term oxygen therapy.
  • Cor pulmonale: Chronic lung disease increases pulmonary vascular resistance, leading to right ventricular hypertrophy and eventual right heart failure.
  • Lung cancer: Increased risk, particularly with asbestos exposure (synergistic effect with smoking) and silicosis.
  • Mesothelioma: A highly aggressive malignancy of the pleura or peritoneum, almost exclusively linked to asbestos exposure, often with a very long latency period (20-50 years).
  • Increased susceptibility to infections: Damaged lung tissue and impaired mucociliary clearance increase the risk of recurrent respiratory infections, including pneumonia.
  • Psychological impact: Chronic illness, dyspnoea, and the knowledge of a work-related cause can lead to anxiety, depression, and reduced quality of life.
