Toxoplasmosis

(Infections)

1. UKMLA Learning Outcome

Understand the epidemiology, clinical manifestations, diagnosis, and management of Toxoplasmosis, particularly in immunocompromised individuals and pregnant women, and its congenital implications.

2. Epidemiology
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Toxoplasmosis"] Approximately 350 cases of toxoplasmosis are diagnosed in England and Wales each year. Seroprevalence in the UK is estimated to be between 23-33%, with regional variations.
  • Caused by the obligate intracellular parasite Toxoplasma gondii.
  • Transmission occurs through ingestion of undercooked meat containing tissue cysts, contaminated food or water with oocysts from cat faeces, or vertically from mother to fetus.
3. Pathophysiology
  • After ingestion, bradyzoites (from cysts) or sporozoites (from oocysts) transform into rapidly multiplying tachyzoites.
  • Tachyzoites disseminate hematogenously and lymphatically to various organs, including the brain, eyes, and muscles.
  • In immunocompetent individuals, the immune system controls tachyzoite replication, leading to the formation of dormant tissue cysts (bradyzoites), primarily in the brain and muscle.
  • [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Toxoplasma gondii life cycle"]
  • Immunosuppression (e.g., HIV, organ transplant, chemotherapy) can lead to reactivation of these cysts, causing severe disease.
4. Clinical Features

Immunocompetent individuals:

  • Often asymptomatic.
  • When symptomatic, typically presents as a self-limiting, flu-like illness with lymphadenopathy (especially cervical), fever, malaise, and myalgia.
  • Symptoms usually resolve within weeks to months.

Immunosuppressed individuals (e.g., HIV/AIDS, transplant recipients):

  • Cerebral toxoplasmosis: Most common manifestation, presenting with focal neurological deficits, seizures, headache, altered mental status, and fever. Characteristic ring-enhancing lesions on MRI.
  • Pneumonitis, chorioretinitis, and disseminated disease can also occur.

Congenital toxoplasmosis:

  • Severity depends on the timing of maternal infection during pregnancy.
  • Classic triad (hydrocephalus, chorioretinitis, intracranial calcifications) is rare but severe.
  • May present with chorioretinitis (leading to visual impairment), hydrocephalus, microcephaly, seizures, developmental delay, or hearing loss.
  • Many congenitally infected infants are asymptomatic at birth but develop symptoms later in life, particularly chorioretinitis.
5. Differential Diagnosis
  • Immunocompetent: Infectious mononucleosis (Epstein-Barr virus), cytomegalovirus infection, other viral infections.
  • Immunosuppressed (cerebral toxoplasmosis): CNS lymphoma, other opportunistic infections (e.g., progressive multifocal leukoencephalopathy, cryptococcosis), stroke.
  • [sc name="jitl-query" Q="ring-enhancing lesion differential diagnosis"]
  • Congenital: Other congenital infections (e.g., rubella, CMV, herpes simplex), genetic disorders.
6. Investigations
  • Serology: Detection of anti-Toxoplasma gondii IgG and IgM antibodies. IgG indicates past exposure, IgM suggests recent infection. Avidity testing can help differentiate acute from chronic infection.
  • PCR: Detection of T. gondii DNA in blood, CSF, amniotic fluid, or tissue biopsies, especially useful in immunocompromised patients and for congenital diagnosis.
  • Neuroimaging (CT/MRI brain): For cerebral toxoplasmosis, typically shows multiple ring-enhancing lesions with surrounding oedema.
  • Ophthalmological examination: For chorioretinitis.
  • [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Toxoplasmosis serology interpretation"]
7. Management

Immunocompetent individuals:

  • Usually self-limiting and does not require specific treatment.
  • Symptomatic treatment for fever and pain.

Immunosuppressed individuals (e.g., cerebral toxoplasmosis):

  • First-line: Pyrimethamine + sulfadiazine + folinic acid.
  • Alternative regimens: Pyrimethamine + clindamycin + folinic acid; trimethoprim-sulfamethoxazole (co-trimoxazole).
  • Treatment duration typically 4-6 weeks, followed by secondary prophylaxis to prevent relapse.
  • Corticosteroids may be used for significant cerebral oedema.

Pregnant women:

  • Treatment depends on gestational age and whether fetal infection is confirmed.
  • Spiramycin for maternal infection to reduce vertical transmission risk.
  • If fetal infection confirmed, pyrimethamine + sulfadiazine + folinic acid.

Congenital toxoplasmosis:

  • Pyrimethamine + sulfadiazine + folinic acid for 12 months.
  • Regular ophthalmological and developmental follow-up.
8. Complications
  • Immunocompromised: Relapse of cerebral toxoplasmosis, pneumonitis, disseminated disease.
  • Congenital: Chorioretinitis (leading to blindness), hydrocephalus, microcephaly, seizures, developmental delay, hearing loss.
  • [sc name="jitl-query" Q="congenital toxoplasmosis complications"]
9. Prognosis
  • Immunocompetent: Excellent, usually full recovery.
  • Immunosuppressed: Good with appropriate treatment, but lifelong secondary prophylaxis may be needed. Neurological sequelae can occur.
  • Congenital: Variable, depending on severity at birth and promptness of treatment. Long-term neurological and ophthalmological complications are common.
  • [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Toxoplasmosis prognosis factors"]
10. Prevention
  • Avoid eating undercooked meat; cook meat thoroughly.
  • Wash hands thoroughly after handling raw meat, gardening, or contact with soil.
  • Wash fruits and vegetables before eating.
  • Avoid contact with cat faeces (e.g., wear gloves when changing cat litter, pregnant women should avoid changing litter boxes).
  • Pregnant women should be advised on food hygiene and avoiding potential sources of infection.
11. Key Learning Points
  • Toxoplasmosis is often asymptomatic in immunocompetent individuals but can cause severe disease in immunocompromised patients and congenitally infected infants.
  • Cerebral toxoplasmosis with ring-enhancing lesions is a key manifestation in immunosuppressed patients.
  • Congenital toxoplasmosis can lead to severe neurological and ophthalmological sequelae.
