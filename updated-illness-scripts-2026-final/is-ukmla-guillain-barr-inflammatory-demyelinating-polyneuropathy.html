Guillain- Barré/ inflammatory demyelinating polyneuropathy [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Guillain- Barré/ inflammatory demyelinating polyneuropathy"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Guillain- Barré/ inflammatory demyelinating polyneuropathy"][/sc]
  • Guillain-Barré Syndrome (GBS) is an acute, autoimmune-mediated polyradiculoneuropathy affecting the peripheral nervous system, leading to rapidly progressive muscle weakness and paralysis.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical features, diagnostic approach, and initial management of Guillain-Barré syndrome, including its variants and potential complications.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Guillain- Barré/ inflammatory demyelinating polyneuropathy"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Guillain- Barré/ inflammatory demyelinating polyneuropathy"][/sc]
  • Incidence in the UK is approximately 1-2 cases per 100,000 population annually, affecting all age groups but with a slight increase in older adults (NICE).
  • Often follows an infection, with 60-70% of cases preceded by a respiratory or gastrointestinal illness, most commonly Campylobacter jejuni, cytomegalovirus, or Epstein-Barr virus.
  • Risk factors include recent infection, certain vaccinations (rarely), and surgery.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Guillain- Barré/ inflammatory demyelinopathy"][/sc]
  • An antecedent infection (e.g., Campylobacter jejuni) triggers an immune response, leading to molecular mimicry where antibodies mistakenly attack gangliosides on peripheral nerve myelin or axons.
  • This autoimmune attack results in demyelination (most common, Acute Inflammatory Demyelinating Polyneuropathy - AIDP) or axonal damage (Acute Motor Axonal Neuropathy - AMAN), impairing nerve conduction.
  • The consequence is progressive, ascending muscle weakness and sensory disturbances, potentially leading to respiratory failure due to diaphragmatic paralysis.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Guillain- Barré/ inflammatory demyelinating polyneuropathy"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Symptoms typically develop over days to 4 weeks, with weakness often starting in the legs and ascending symmetrically.
  • Progressive phase: Weakness usually peaks within 2-4 weeks of onset, particularly beyond 2 weeks, before a plateau is reached.
  • Late phase: Recovery can take weeks to months, or even years, with some patients experiencing residual weakness or fatigue.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Guillain- Barré/ inflammatory demyelinating polyneuropathy"][/sc]
  • Ask about ascending weakness - crucial for identifying the characteristic pattern of GBS progression.
  • Ask about paraesthesia or numbness - often precedes or accompanies weakness, indicating sensory nerve involvement.
  • Ask about back pain or radicular pain - common early symptom, suggesting nerve root inflammation.
  • Ask about difficulty breathing or swallowing - indicates potential involvement of respiratory or bulbar muscles, a critical red flag.
  • Ask about recent infection (e.g., gastrointestinal upset, flu-like illness) - helps establish a potential trigger for the autoimmune response.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Guillain- Barré/ inflammatory demyelinating polyneuropathy"][/sc]
  • Check muscle power (MRC scale) - to quantify and monitor progressive, symmetrical weakness, often more pronounced proximally in later stages.
  • Check deep tendon reflexes - typically diminished or absent, a hallmark sign of peripheral nerve involvement.
  • Check sensory examination (light touch, pinprick, proprioception) - to identify glove-and-stocking distribution or other patterns of sensory loss.
  • Check vital signs (respiratory rate, oxygen saturation, blood pressure, heart rate) - essential for detecting autonomic dysfunction and impending respiratory compromise.
  • Check cranial nerves - for facial weakness, ophthalmoplegia (Miller Fisher variant), or dysphagia, indicating bulbar involvement.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Guillain- Barré/ inflammatory demyelinating polyneuropathy"][/sc]
  • Rapidly progressive weakness - suggests an aggressive course requiring urgent intervention.
  • Respiratory compromise - indicated by dyspnoea, reduced vital capacity, or declining oxygen saturation, necessitating ventilatory support.
  • Bulbar weakness - difficulty swallowing or speaking, increasing risk of aspiration and airway compromise.
  • Autonomic dysfunction - significant fluctuations in blood pressure, heart rate, or arrhythmias, indicating severe systemic involvement.
  • Inability to walk independently - a critical marker for severe functional impairment and need for hospital admission.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Guillain- Barré/ inflammatory demyelinating polyneuropathy"][/sc]
  • Miller Fisher Syndrome: Characterised by ophthalmoplegia, ataxia, and areflexia, often with anti-GQ1b antibodies, differing from typical ascending paralysis.
  • Pharyngeal-cervical-brachial variant: Weakness predominantly affecting the pharyngeal, neck, and upper limb muscles, which can lead to early respiratory compromise.
  • Pure sensory GBS: Rare variant presenting primarily with sensory symptoms without significant motor weakness, making diagnosis challenging.
  • Acute Motor Axonal Neuropathy (AMAN): Predominantly motor involvement with axonal damage rather than demyelination, often associated with Campylobacter jejuni infection.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Guillain- Barré/ inflammatory demyelinating polyneuropathy"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected Guillain-Barré: Lumbar puncture for cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) analysis typically shows albuminocytological dissociation (elevated protein with normal cell count) after the first week.
  • Core for suspected Guillain-Barré: Nerve conduction studies and electromyography (NCS/EMG) demonstrate demyelinating or axonal neuropathy, confirming peripheral nerve involvement.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Blood tests including full blood count, electrolytes, liver and renal function, thyroid function, and inflammatory markers to exclude other causes of weakness.
  • Imaging - MRI spine: May be considered to rule out spinal cord compression or inflammation, particularly if atypical features or severe back pain are present.
  • Serial reassessment: Regular monitoring of vital capacity and negative inspiratory force is crucial to detect impending respiratory failure.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Guillain- Barré/ inflammatory demyelinating polyneuropathy"][/sc]
  • A 45-year-old male presents with a 5-day history of progressive, symmetrical ascending weakness in his lower limbs, accompanied by tingling sensations in his feet, following a recent diarrhoeal illness.
  • A subacute, ascending, flaccid paralysis with paraesthesia, preceded by an infectious prodrome, suggesting an immune-mediated peripheral neuropathy.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Guillain- Barré/ inflammatory demyelinating polyneuropathy"][/sc]
  • Transverse Myelitis - sensory level, bladder/bowel dysfunction, upper motor neuron signs.
  • Botulism - descending paralysis, prominent bulbar symptoms, normal sensation.
  • Tick Paralysis - ascending paralysis, absent reflexes, history of tick exposure.
  • Critical Illness Polyneuropathy - occurs in critically ill patients, often with sepsis or multi-organ failure.
  • Myasthenia Gravis - fluctuating weakness, fatigability, often ocular or bulbar onset.
  • Acute Poliomyelitis - asymmetrical weakness, fever, meningeal signs.
  • Hypokalaemia - muscle weakness, but reflexes usually preserved unless severe.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Guillain- Barré/ inflammatory demyelinating polyneuropathy"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Airway management and respiratory support (e.g., intubation and mechanical ventilation if vital capacity is compromised), cardiac monitoring for autonomic instability.
  • Definitive management: Immunomodulatory therapy with intravenous immunoglobulin (IVIg) or plasma exchange (PLEX) to reduce the severity and duration of illness.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Close neurological monitoring, respiratory function tests, physiotherapy, and occupational therapy for rehabilitation.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Advise patients and carers on signs of worsening weakness, respiratory distress, or autonomic symptoms, and to seek immediate medical attention.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Guillain- Barré/ inflammatory demyelinating polyneuropathy"][/sc]
  • Respiratory failure: Due to diaphragmatic and intercostal muscle paralysis, a life-threatening complication requiring ventilatory support.
  • Autonomic dysfunction: Manifests as cardiac arrhythmias, labile blood pressure, or paralytic ileus, impacting systemic stability.
  • Deep vein thrombosis (DVT) and pulmonary embolism (PE): Risk increased due to immobility, necessitating prophylactic measures.
  • Pressure sores: Prolonged immobility can lead to skin breakdown, requiring meticulous nursing care.
  • Residual weakness and fatigue: Many patients experience long-term neurological deficits, affecting quality of life and functional independence.
  • Pain: Neuropathic pain or musculoskeletal pain is common, requiring effective pain management strategies.
