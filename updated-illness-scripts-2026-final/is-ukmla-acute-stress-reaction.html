Acute stress reaction [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Acute stress reaction"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Acute stress reaction"][/sc]
  • Acute stress reaction is a transient psychological disorder that develops in response to an exceptional physical or psychological stressor, typically resolving within hours or days.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the presentation of acute stress reaction and differentiate it from other mental health conditions.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Acute stress reaction"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Acute stress reaction"][/sc]
  • Prevalence is difficult to ascertain due to its transient nature, but it is commonly observed following traumatic events such as accidents, natural disasters, or violent assaults.
  • Risk factors include pre-existing mental health conditions, a history of trauma, and lack of social support following a stressful event.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Acute stress reaction"][/sc]
  • Exposure to an acute, overwhelming stressor triggers a rapid activation of the sympathetic nervous system and the hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal (HPA) axis.
  • This leads to a surge in stress hormones and neurotransmitters, resulting in immediate psychological and physiological responses such as hyperarousal and emotional numbing.
  • The acute physiological and psychological changes are typically self-limiting, resolving as the individual's coping mechanisms engage and the immediate threat subsides.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Acute stress reaction"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Symptoms typically emerge immediately or within minutes of the stressor.
  • Progressive phase: Symptoms usually peak within hours and begin to subside within a few days, often within 72 hours.
  • Late phase: Resolution is expected within one month of the stressor; persistence beyond this period suggests an alternative diagnosis such as post-traumatic stress disorder.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Acute stress reaction"][/sc]
  • Ask about emotional numbing - indicates a dissociative response to the trauma.
  • Ask about detachment from surroundings - suggests depersonalisation or derealisation.
  • Ask about anxiety and panic - common immediate reactions, including palpitations and shortness of breath.
  • Ask about sleep disturbance - difficulty falling or staying asleep due to intrusive thoughts or hyperarousal.
  • Ask about intrusive memories or flashbacks - involuntary re-experiencing of the traumatic event.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Acute stress reaction"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - may show tachycardia or tachypnoea due to sympathetic activation, but often normal.
  • Check for hypervigilance - an exaggerated startle response or heightened awareness of surroundings.
  • Check for restlessness or agitation - visible signs of anxiety or distress.
  • Check for emotional lability - rapid shifts in mood or affect during assessment.
  • Check for impaired concentration - difficulty focusing or following instructions.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Acute stress reaction"][/sc]
  • Persistent symptoms beyond one month - suggests progression to Post-Traumatic Stress Disorder (PTSD) or other anxiety disorders.
  • Severe functional impairment - inability to perform daily activities, work, or maintain relationships.
  • Psychotic symptoms - hallucinations, delusions, or severe disorganisation, indicating a more severe mental health crisis.
  • Active suicidal ideation or intent - requires immediate mental health assessment and intervention.
  • Substance misuse or dependence - may be used as a maladaptive coping mechanism, complicating management.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Acute stress reaction"][/sc]
  • Older adults: May present with more somatic symptoms or cognitive complaints, with less overt emotional distress, potentially masking the underlying reaction.
  • Children: Often manifest through behavioural changes such as regression, irritability, or play reenacting the traumatic event, rather than verbalising distress.
  • Individuals with pre-existing mental health conditions: Symptoms may exacerbate existing conditions or present as a relapse, making differentiation challenging.
  • Individuals with intellectual disabilities: Communication barriers may lead to atypical expressions of distress, requiring careful observation of behavioural cues.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Acute stress reaction"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected Acute stress reaction: Clinical assessment based on DSM-5 or ICD-11 criteria, focusing on the temporal relationship between the stressor and symptom onset.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Consider routine blood tests (e.g., full blood count, thyroid function tests) to exclude organic causes of anxiety or agitation.
  • Validated screening tools: Acute Stress Disorder Scale on MDCalc can aid in symptom quantification and monitoring, though diagnosis remains clinical.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Acute stress reaction"][/sc]
  • A 35-year-old female presents to the emergency department immediately after witnessing a serious road traffic accident, reporting intense fear, emotional numbness, and a sense of unreality.
  • A young adult experiencing acute, transient, dissociative and hyperarousal symptoms following an identifiable traumatic event.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Acute stress reaction"][/sc]
  • Post-Traumatic Stress Disorder (PTSD) - symptoms persist beyond one month.
  • Panic Disorder - recurrent unexpected panic attacks without a specific traumatic trigger.
  • Generalised Anxiety Disorder - chronic, pervasive worry not linked to a single acute stressor.
  • Depressive Episode - prominent low mood, anhedonia, and vegetative symptoms.
  • Adjustment Disorder - emotional or behavioural symptoms in response to an identifiable stressor, but typically less severe and not meeting criteria for acute stress reaction.
  • Substance-induced anxiety disorder - anxiety symptoms directly attributable to substance use or withdrawal.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Acute stress reaction"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Provide psychological first aid, ensure safety, and offer practical support.
  • Definitive management: Psychoeducation, active monitoring, and watchful waiting. Consider trauma-focused cognitive behavioural therapy (CBT) or eye movement desensitisation and reprocessing (EMDR) if symptoms persist or worsen.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Regular review of symptoms and functional impairment, particularly within the first month.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Refer to specialist mental health services if symptoms are severe, prolonged, or if there is a risk of self-harm or harm to others.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Acute stress reaction"][/sc]
  • Progression to PTSD: If symptoms persist beyond one month, the condition may evolve into Post-Traumatic Stress Disorder, requiring more intensive and prolonged treatment.
  • Chronic anxiety or depression: Untreated or poorly managed acute stress can lead to the development of other long-term mental health conditions.
  • Functional impairment: Significant disruption to daily life, work, and social relationships due to persistent distress and avoidance behaviours.
  • Substance misuse: Individuals may turn to alcohol or drugs as a coping mechanism, leading to dependence and further health problems.
  • Suicidal ideation: Severe and prolonged distress can increase the risk of suicidal thoughts and behaviours, necessitating urgent mental health intervention.
