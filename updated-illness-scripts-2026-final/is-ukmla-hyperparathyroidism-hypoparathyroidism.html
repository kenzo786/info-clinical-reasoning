Hyperparathyroidism/ hypoparathyroidism [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Hyperparathyroidism/ hypoparathyroidism"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Hyperparathyroidism/ hypoparathyroidism"][/sc]
  • Hyperparathyroidism is a condition characterised by excessive parathyroid hormone (PTH) secretion, leading to hypercalcaemia, while hypoparathyroidism involves insufficient PTH, resulting in hypocalcaemia.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical features, investigations, and initial management of hyperparathyroidism and hypoparathyroidism, including their acute presentations.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Hyperparathyroidism/ hypoparathyroidism"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Hyperparathyroidism/ hypoparathyroidism"][/sc]
  • Primary hyperparathyroidism affects approximately 1 in 1000 people in the UK, with a higher incidence in post-menopausal women.
  • Hypoparathyroidism is rarer, with an estimated prevalence of 2-3 per 100,000 in the UK, often iatrogenic following thyroid or parathyroid surgery.
  • Risk factors for primary hyperparathyroidism include female sex, older age, and certain genetic syndromes (e.g., MEN1, MEN2A).
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Hyperparathyroidism/ hypoparathyroidism"][/sc]
  • Trigger: In hyperparathyroidism, an adenoma or hyperplasia of the parathyroid glands leads to autonomous overproduction of PTH, or in hypoparathyroidism, damage or removal of parathyroid glands results in insufficient PTH.
  • Progression: Excess PTH in hyperparathyroidism causes increased bone resorption, renal calcium reabsorption, and vitamin D activation, elevating serum calcium; conversely, PTH deficiency in hypoparathyroidism impairs these processes, leading to hypocalcaemia and hyperphosphataemia.
  • Consequence: Sustained hypercalcaemia can cause renal stones, bone demineralisation, and neurological dysfunction, while chronic hypocalcaemia can lead to neuromuscular irritability, cardiac arrhythmias, and basal ganglia calcification.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Hyperparathyroidism/ hypoparathyroidism"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Acute symptomatic hypercalcaemia or hypocalcaemia can develop over hours to days, particularly in crisis situations or post-surgical complications.
  • Progressive phase: Chronic hyperparathyroidism or hypoparathyroidism often evolves insidiously over months to years, with symptoms becoming more apparent as calcium imbalances persist.
  • Late phase: Untreated, severe or prolonged calcium dysregulation can lead to irreversible organ damage, particularly beyond several months, affecting renal, skeletal, and neurological systems.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Hyperparathyroidism/ hypoparathyroidism"][/sc]
  • Ask about fatigue and weakness - these are non-specific but common in both conditions, reflecting metabolic disturbance.
  • Ask about bone pain or fractures (hyperparathyroidism) - indicates bone demineralisation due to chronic PTH excess.
  • Ask about muscle cramps, spasms, or paraesthesia (hypoparathyroidism) - suggests neuromuscular irritability from hypocalcaemia.
  • Ask about polyuria and polydipsia (hyperparathyroidism) - due to hypercalcaemia-induced nephrogenic diabetes insipidus.
  • Ask about mood changes, anxiety, or depression - common in both, impacting quality of life and potentially indicating neurological effects.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Hyperparathyroidism/ hypoparathyroidism"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - hypercalcaemia can cause bradycardia and hypertension; hypocalcaemia can prolong the QT interval.
  • Check for Chvostek's sign or Trousseau's sign (hypoparathyroidism) - indicative of latent tetany due to hypocalcaemia.
  • Check for band keratopathy (hyperparathyroidism) - calcium deposits in the cornea, a rare but specific sign of chronic hypercalcaemia.
  • Check for reduced muscle tone and proximal muscle weakness (hyperparathyroidism) - can be a feature of chronic hypercalcaemia.
  • Check for dry skin, brittle nails, and coarse hair (hypoparathyroidism) - non-specific signs that may accompany chronic hypocalcaemia.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Hyperparathyroidism/ hypoparathyroidism"][/sc]
  • Hypercalcaemic crisis - severe hypercalcaemia with altered mental status, acute kidney injury, or cardiac arrhythmias, requiring urgent intervention.
  • Tetany or seizures - acute, severe hypocalcaemia causing neuromuscular hyperexcitability, indicating immediate need for calcium replacement.
  • Pathological fractures - unexpected bone fractures in hyperparathyroidism, suggesting significant bone demineralisation and increased fragility.
  • Stridor or airway compromise - rare but critical complication of severe hypocalcaemia leading to laryngospasm.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Hyperparathyroidism/ hypoparathyroidism"][/sc]
  • Older adults: May present with more subtle or non-specific symptoms like cognitive impairment or generalised weakness, often attributed to ageing, delaying diagnosis.
  • Pregnancy: Hyperparathyroidism can lead to foetal and neonatal complications (e.g., hypocalcaemia in the neonate), while hypoparathyroidism requires careful calcium management to prevent maternal and foetal harm.
  • Asymptomatic hyperparathyroidism: Increasingly common, discovered incidentally on routine blood tests, posing a management dilemma regarding surveillance versus intervention.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Hyperparathyroidism/ hypoparathyroidism"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected hyperparathyroidism: Serum calcium (corrected), PTH, phosphate, alkaline phosphatase, and vitamin D levels.
  • Core for suspected hypoparathyroidism: Serum calcium (corrected), PTH, phosphate, magnesium, and vitamin D levels.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Renal function tests, liver function tests, serum electrophoresis, and thyroid function tests.
  • Imaging - parathyroid ultrasound: To localise parathyroid adenomas in primary hyperparathyroidism.
  • Imaging - Sestamibi scan: A nuclear medicine scan used to identify overactive parathyroid glands, particularly useful if ultrasound is inconclusive.
  • Serial reassessment: Regular monitoring of calcium and PTH levels is crucial for managing chronic conditions and assessing treatment efficacy.
