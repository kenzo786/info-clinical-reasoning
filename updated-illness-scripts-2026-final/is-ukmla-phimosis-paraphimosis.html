Phimosis/ paraphimosis [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Phimosis/ paraphimosis"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Phimosis/ paraphimosis"][/sc]
  • Phimosis is a condition where the foreskin cannot be fully retracted over the glans penis, while paraphimosis is a urological emergency where a retracted foreskin becomes trapped behind the glans, leading to swelling and potential ischaemia.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: The candidate must recognise the presentation, differentiate between phimosis and paraphimosis, and understand initial management principles.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Phimosis/ paraphimosis"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Phimosis/ paraphimosis"][/sc]
  • Phimosis is common in uncircumcised male infants, often resolving spontaneously by age 5-7 years; pathological phimosis is less common and may be associated with inflammation or scarring.
  • Paraphimosis is less common but represents a significant proportion of male genitourinary emergencies, with incidence varying by age group, often linked to iatrogenic causes or vigorous sexual activity.
  • Risk factors for pathological phimosis include recurrent balanitis, poor hygiene, and certain dermatological conditions; for paraphimosis, risk factors include forceful foreskin retraction, catheterisation, and unrecognised phimosis.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Phimosis/ paraphimosis"][/sc]
  • Trigger: In phimosis, the foreskin opening is too narrow to permit full retraction, often physiological in childhood or acquired due to scarring from inflammation or trauma.
  • Progression: In paraphimosis, a retracted, tight foreskin constricts the glans, leading to venous and lymphatic congestion, causing oedema and further constriction.
  • Consequence: Persistent constriction can impair arterial blood flow, potentially leading to glans ischaemia and necrosis if not promptly reduced.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Phimosis/ paraphimosis"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Physiological phimosis is present from birth and typically resolves over several years; acquired phimosis may develop gradually over months to years.
  • Progressive phase: Paraphimosis develops acutely, with rapid onset of pain and swelling, progressing over hours.
  • Late phase: If paraphimosis is unreduced, ischaemia and necrosis of the glans may occur, particularly beyond 6-12 hours, leading to irreversible damage.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Phimosis/ paraphimosis"][/sc]
  • Ask about difficulty retracting foreskin - indicates phimosis, which may be physiological or pathological.
  • Ask about pain during erection or urination - suggests pathological phimosis or balanitis.
  • Ask about sudden onset of severe pain and swelling of the glans - highly indicative of paraphimosis.
  • Ask about inability to return foreskin to normal position - confirms paraphimosis.
  • Ask about previous episodes of foreskin retraction and difficulty returning it - may suggest underlying phimosis or risk of recurrence.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Phimosis/ paraphimosis"][/sc]
  • Check for foreskin retractability - inability to retract past the coronal sulcus indicates phimosis.
  • Check for a constricted, oedematous ring of foreskin behind the glans - a key sign of paraphimosis.
  • Check for glans oedema and discolouration (e.g., dusky or cyanotic) - suggests venous congestion and potential ischaemia in paraphimosis.
  • Check vital signs - typically normal in phimosis; paraphimosis may cause tachycardia due to pain.
  • Check for signs of infection (e.g., erythema, purulent discharge) - may indicate associated balanitis or posthitis.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Phimosis/ paraphimosis"][/sc]
  • Inability to reduce paraphimosis: Requires urgent urological intervention to prevent glans ischaemia.
  • Glans discolouration (dusky/cyanotic): Suggests compromised arterial blood flow, indicating a high risk of necrosis.
  • Severe pain unresponsive to analgesia: May indicate significant tissue compromise or impending complications.
  • Signs of systemic infection (fever, malaise): Suggests complicated balanitis or cellulitis requiring broader management.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Phimosis/ paraphimosis"][/sc]
  • Older adults: Phimosis may develop due to loss of skin elasticity or recurrent infections, potentially presenting with urinary obstruction or recurrent UTIs.
  • Diabetic patients: More prone to recurrent balanitis and subsequent scarring, leading to acquired phimosis, which may be more severe.
  • Children with physiological phimosis: Parents may present with concerns about foreskin retraction, which is often normal and requires reassurance rather than intervention.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Phimosis/ paraphimosis"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected phimosis/paraphimosis: Clinical examination is usually sufficient for diagnosis.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Urinalysis to rule out urinary tract infection if symptoms suggest.
  • Imaging - Ultrasound: Rarely indicated, but may be used in complex cases to assess for complications or underlying anatomical abnormalities.
  • Serial reassessment: For paraphimosis, regular assessment of glans viability and oedema is crucial after reduction attempts.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Phimosis/ paraphimosis"][/sc]
  • A 45-year-old uncircumcised male presents to the emergency department with sudden onset of severe penile pain and swelling for 3 hours after sexual activity, with the foreskin retracted behind the glans and unable to be returned.
  • An acutely painful, swollen, and irreducible foreskin in an uncircumcised adult male, suggesting an emergent urological condition.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Phimosis/ paraphimosis"][/sc]
  • Balanitis - inflammation of the glans, often with erythema and discharge, but foreskin is usually reducible.
  • Penile cellulitis - diffuse inflammation of the penile shaft, typically without a constricting band.
  • Angioedema - rapid swelling, but usually resolves spontaneously and lacks the constricting ring of paraphimosis.
  • Penile fracture - history of trauma, acute pain, and often a 'snapping' sound, with penile deviation.
  • Priapism - persistent erection, not related to foreskin retraction or constriction.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Phimosis/ paraphimosis"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: For paraphimosis, manual reduction is the primary goal, often requiring analgesia and local anaesthesia; osmotic agents (e.g., sugar, mannitol) or compression may aid reduction.
  • Definitive management: For phimosis, conservative management with topical corticosteroids may be attempted; surgical options include dorsal slit or circumcision if conservative measures fail or for recurrent pathological phimosis. For paraphimosis, after successful reduction, treatment of underlying phimosis (if present) may be considered.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Post-reduction, monitor for recurrence, glans viability, and complications. Follow-up with primary care or urology for definitive management of phimosis.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Failure of manual reduction of paraphimosis requires urgent urological consultation for surgical intervention (e.g., dorsal slit).

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Phimosis/ paraphimosis"][/sc]
  • Glans ischaemia/necrosis: In paraphimosis, prolonged constriction can compromise blood supply, leading to tissue death, particularly if unreduced beyond several hours.
  • Infection (balanitis/posthitis): Both phimosis and paraphimosis can predispose to infection due to poor hygiene or tissue compromise, leading to pain, swelling, and discharge.
  • Urinary retention: Severe phimosis can obstruct urine flow, leading to difficulty voiding or acute urinary retention.
  • Recurrence: Paraphimosis may recur if underlying phimosis is not addressed, or if the foreskin is not properly repositioned after retraction.
