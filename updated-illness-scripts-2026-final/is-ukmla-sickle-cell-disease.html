Sickle cell disease (Blood and lymph) [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Sickle cell disease"]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Sickle cell disease"]
  • Sickle cell disease (SCD) is a group of inherited haemoglobinopathies characterised by the presence of abnormal haemoglobin S (HbS). Homozygous HbS (HbSS), also known as sickle cell anaemia, is the most common and severe form, resulting from inheriting a beta-globin gene mutation from both parents.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical features, common complications (e.g., vaso-occlusive crisis, acute chest syndrome, stroke, splenic sequestration, aplastic crisis), and principles of management for sickle cell disease, including the role of hydroxyurea.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Sickle cell disease"]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Sickle cell disease"]
  • Sickle cell disease is the most common genetic disorder in the UK, affecting an estimated 12,500–15,000 people, predominantly those of African, Caribbean, and Asian descent [1].
  • Approximately 1 in 2,100 babies screened in England in 2018-2019 were positive for significant sickle cell conditions [2].
  • The prevalence of sickle cell trait (heterozygous HbS) is significantly higher in certain populations, with up to 1 in 10 people of African or Caribbean origin in the UK being carriers [1].
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Sickle cell disease"]
  • Trigger: A single amino acid substitution (glutamic acid to valine) in the beta-globin chain leads to the production of abnormal haemoglobin S (HbS). Under deoxygenated conditions, HbS polymerises, forming rigid fibres within red blood cells [3].
  • Progression: This polymerisation distorts red blood cells into a characteristic sickle shape, making them rigid and less deformable. These sickled cells have a reduced lifespan and are prone to premature haemolysis, leading to chronic anaemia [3]. Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Sickle cell disease"]
  • Early phase: Symptoms typically begin after 6 months of age, once fetal haemoglobin (HbF) levels decline. Dactylitis (hand-foot syndrome) is often the first manifestation in infants and toddlers, presenting as painful swelling of the extremities.
  • Progressive phase: Throughout childhood and adulthood, patients experience recurrent vaso-occlusive crises, acute chest syndrome, and an increased susceptibility to infections. The frequency and severity of these events can vary widely.
  • Late phase: Chronic complications such as organ damage (e.g., renal failure, pulmonary hypertension, avascular necrosis) and neurological events (e.g., stroke) become more prevalent, particularly beyond the second decade of life, leading to significant morbidity and reduced life expectancy.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Sickle cell disease"]
  • Ask about pain - as it is the hallmark symptom of vaso-occlusive crises, which can be dull, throbbing, or stabbing and occur anywhere in the body, most commonly in the limbs, back, and abdomen [4].
  • Ask about fatigue and weakness - as these are common manifestations of chronic anaemia, a persistent feature of sickle cell disease due to ongoing haemolysis [4].
  • Ask about swelling of hands and feet (dactylitis) - particularly in young children, as this is often the earliest symptom of vaso-occlusion in the small bones of the extremities [5].
  • Ask about fever, cough, and shortness of breath - as these symptoms, especially when combined with chest pain, may indicate acute chest syndrome, a life-threatening pulmonary complication [6].
  • Ask about jaundice or pale skin - as these can be signs of increased red blood cell destruction (haemolysis) and anaemia, respectively [4].
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Sickle cell disease"]
  • Check vital signs - particularly temperature (fever may indicate infection or acute chest syndrome), respiratory rate (tachypnoea in acute chest syndrome), and oxygen saturation (hypoxaemia is a critical sign of pulmonary involvement) [6].
  • Check for pallor or jaundice - indicating anaemia or haemolysis, respectively, which are chronic features of the disease [4].
  • Check for localised tenderness or swelling - especially in the extremities (dactylitis) or abdomen (splenomegaly, hepatic crisis), which can signify vaso-occlusive events [5]. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Dactylitis in sickle cell disease: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Dactylitis"]
  • Check for signs of acute chest syndrome - such as crackles or reduced breath sounds on auscultation, and evidence of respiratory distress [6].
  • Check neurological status - for any focal neurological deficits, altered consciousness, or seizures, which may indicate a stroke or transient ischaemic attack [7].
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Sickle cell disease"]
  • Acute Chest Syndrome: New pulmonary infiltrate on chest X-ray accompanied by fever, cough, chest pain, or hypoxaemia, indicating a medical emergency requiring urgent intervention to prevent respiratory failure [6].
  • Severe Pain Crisis: Uncontrolled pain despite adequate analgesia, or pain associated with neurological changes, severe abdominal tenderness, or signs of infection, necessitating hospital admission and escalation of care [4].
  • Neurological Deficits: Sudden onset of weakness, speech disturbance, visual changes, or altered mental status, suggesting a stroke or transient ischaemic attack, which requires immediate neurological assessment and management [7].
  • Splenic Sequestration Crisis: Rapidly enlarging spleen, sudden drop in haemoglobin (>2g/dL), and signs of hypovolaemic shock, particularly in young children, indicating acute blood pooling in the spleen and requiring urgent fluid resuscitation and potentially transfusion [8].
  • Aplastic Crisis: Acute, severe anaemia with a very low reticulocyte count, often triggered by parvovirus B19 infection, leading to profound fatigue, pallor, and cardiovascular compromise, necessitating urgent blood transfusion [9].
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Sickle cell disease"]
  • Older Adults: May present with more chronic complications such as renal failure, pulmonary hypertension, or avascular necrosis, with less frequent but potentially more severe acute painful crises.
  • Pregnancy: Increased risk of vaso-occlusive crises, acute chest syndrome, pre-eclampsia, and foetal complications (e.g., intrauterine growth restriction, preterm birth), requiring multidisciplinary management.
  • Silent Cerebral Infarcts: Neurological damage without overt clinical symptoms, often detected incidentally on MRI, leading to cognitive impairment and academic difficulties, particularly in children. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Silent cerebral infarcts in sickle cell disease: what are they and why do they matter clinically?" text="Silent cerebral infarcts"]
  • Priapism: Persistent, painful erection in males due to vaso-occlusion in the penile vasculature, requiring urgent medical attention to prevent erectile dysfunction.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Sickle cell disease"]
  • Core for suspected sickle cell disease:
    • Full blood count (FBC): typically shows normocytic, normochromic anaemia with a high reticulocyte count (unless aplastic crisis) [10].
    • Blood film: presence of sickle cells, target cells, and features of hyposplenism (e.g., Howell-Jolly bodies) [10].
    • Haemoglobin electrophoresis or high-performance liquid chromatography (HPLC): confirms the presence and quantity of HbS and other haemoglobin variants [10].
  • Severity/risk tools: Transcranial Doppler (TCD) ultrasonography: screens children for increased stroke risk by measuring cerebral blood flow velocities.   • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Consider inflammatory markers (CRP, ESR), blood cultures (for infection), and other specific investigations based on clinical presentation.
  • Imaging - Chest X-ray: Essential for diagnosing acute chest syndrome, showing new pulmonary infiltrates [6].
  • Imaging - MRI brain: Used to detect silent cerebral infarcts or overt stroke [7].
  • Serial reassessment: Regular monitoring of FBC, reticulocyte count, and clinical status during acute crises to assess response to treatment and detect complications.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Sickle cell disease"]
  • A young adult with known homozygous sickle cell disease presents with sudden onset severe generalised pain, fever, and shortness of breath, following a recent viral illness.
  • A young adult with chronic haemolytic anaemia and recurrent vaso-occlusive crises, now presenting with acute respiratory distress and new pulmonary infiltrates, concerning for acute chest syndrome.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Sickle cell disease"]
  • Other causes of acute pain - musculoskeletal pain, appendicitis, cholecystitis, or renal colic, which can mimic vaso-occlusive crisis but lack the underlying sickling pathophysiology.
  • Other causes of anaemia - iron deficiency anaemia, vitamin B12/folate deficiency, or other haemolytic anaemias, distinguished by different red cell indices and haemoglobinopathy screening.
  • Other causes of acute chest pain and dyspnoea - pneumonia, asthma exacerbation, pulmonary embolism, or cardiac events, differentiated by imaging, cardiac markers, and specific risk factors. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Acute chest syndrome vs pneumonia in sickle cell disease: how to differentiate?" text="Acute chest syndrome vs pneumonia"]
  • Osteomyelitis or septic arthritis - can present with bone or joint pain and fever, similar to vaso-occlusive crisis, but typically have localised signs of infection and positive cultures.
  • Malaria - in endemic areas, can cause fever, anaemia, and splenomegaly, overlapping with sickle cell crisis symptoms, requiring malaria parasite testing.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Sickle cell disease"]
  • Immediate priorities: Prompt recognition and assessment of acute crises, aggressive pain management (often requiring opioids), hydration, and oxygen therapy if hypoxic. Escalation to specialist care is crucial for severe presentations [11].
  • Definitive management: Hydroxycarbamide (hydroxyurea) is a disease-modifying therapy that increases foetal haemoglobin (HbF) production, reducing sickling and the frequency of vaso-occlusive crises and acute chest syndrome [12].
  • Adjunctive management: Regular blood transfusions (simple or exchange) may be used for severe anaemia, stroke prevention, or acute complications. Prophylactic antibiotics (e.g., penicillin) and vaccinations are essential to prevent infections [11].
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Lifelong specialist follow-up is required, including regular blood tests, organ function monitoring, and screening for complications (e.g., TCD for stroke risk).
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Patients and carers should be educated on recognising red flag symptoms and when to seek urgent medical attention. Clear care plans should be in place [11].

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Sickle cell disease"]
  • Vaso-occlusive Crisis (VOC): Recurrent episodes of severe pain due to microvascular occlusion, leading to tissue ischaemia and infarction, which can affect any organ system and significantly impact quality of life.
  • Acute Chest Syndrome (ACS): A life-threatening pulmonary complication characterised by new lung infiltrates, fever, and respiratory symptoms, often triggered by infection or fat embolism, leading to hypoxaemia and respiratory failure [6].
  • Stroke: Occurs due to cerebral vaso-occlusion or haemorrhage, leading to focal neurological deficits. Both overt and silent cerebral infarcts are common, causing significant neurocognitive impairment, particularly beyond early childhood [7].
  • Splenic Sequestration Crisis: Acute pooling of blood in the spleen, leading to rapid splenomegaly, severe anaemia, and hypovolaemic shock, predominantly in young children, requiring urgent intervention [8].
  • Aplastic Crisis: Transient cessation of red blood cell production in the bone marrow, typically triggered by parvovirus B19 infection, resulting in profound anaemia and requiring urgent blood transfusion [9].
  • Chronic Organ Damage: Long-term complications include chronic kidney disease, pulmonary hypertension, avascular necrosis of bones, leg ulcers, and retinopathy, all stemming from recurrent ischaemia and inflammation.
