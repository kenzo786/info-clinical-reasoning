Breast cancer [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Breast cancer"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Breast cancer"][/sc]
  • A malignant proliferation of epithelial cells lining the ducts or lobules of the breast. The most common types are invasive ductal carcinoma and invasive lobular carcinoma, with ductal carcinoma in situ (DCIS) being a non-invasive precursor.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the features of breast cancer, initiate triple assessment for suspected cases, and understand the roles of surgery, radiotherapy, and systemic therapies based on tumour biology (ER, PR, HER2 status).
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Breast cancer"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Breast cancer"][/sc]
  • Breast cancer is the most common cancer in the UK, with around 55,000 new cases diagnosed each year. Lifetime risk for women is approximately 1 in 7.
  • Incidence increases with age, with most cases occurring in women over 50. Key risk factors include female sex, increasing age, family history (especially BRCA1/2 mutations), early menarche, late menopause, nulliparity, and significant alcohol intake.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Breast cancer"][/sc]
  • Trigger: Genetic mutations (e.g., BRCA1/2) or somatic mutations in breast epithelial cells, often influenced by hormonal exposure (oestrogen), lead to disordered cell growth.
  • Progression: Proliferation of abnormal cells forms a tumour. This may be non-invasive (in situ) or become invasive, breaching the basement membrane and gaining access to lymphatics and blood vessels. Tumour growth is often driven by oestrogen (ER-positive), progesterone (PR-positive), or human epidermal growth factor receptor 2 ([sc name="jitl-query" Q="HER2: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="HER2"][/sc]).
  • Consequence: Local invasion can cause skin or chest wall changes. Metastatic spread via lymphatics (most commonly to axillary nodes) or blood (to bones, lungs, liver, brain) leads to systemic disease and significant morbidity.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Breast cancer"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Often asymptomatic and detected via screening mammography. A new, painless lump may be noticed, typically over weeks to months.
  • Progressive phase: The lump may enlarge. Skin changes such as dimpling (peau d\'orange), nipple retraction, or a persistent rash (Paget\'s disease of the nipple) can develop over months. Axillary lymphadenopathy may become palpable.
  • Late phase: Locally advanced disease can present with skin ulceration, fixation to the chest wall, or significant pain. Symptoms of metastatic disease (e.g., bone pain, breathlessness, jaundice) may appear, particularly beyond 1-2 years after initial presentation if untreated.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Breast cancer"][/sc]
  • Ask about a new breast lump - this is the most common presenting symptom; inquire about its texture (hard, irregular) and whether it is fixed or mobile.
  • Ask about skin changes - dimpling, puckering, or an orange-peel appearance (peau d\'orange) suggests underlying lymphatic obstruction.
  • Ask about nipple changes - recent inversion, discharge (especially if bloody or unilateral), or a scaly, eczematous rash can indicate underlying malignancy.
  • Ask about axillary swelling - a lump in the armpit may represent nodal involvement.
  • Ask about bone pain, persistent cough, or unexplained weight loss - these are non-specific but may indicate metastatic disease in advanced cases.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Breast cancer"][/sc]
  • Check for a palpable breast mass - typically hard, irregular, and tethered to surrounding tissue or skin.
  • Check for skin changes - look for asymmetry, erythema, oedema (peau d\'orange), or ulceration.
  • Check the nipples - for inversion, discharge, or Paget\'s disease of the nipple.
  • Check the axillary and supraclavicular fossae - for palpable lymphadenopathy, which suggests nodal metastasis.
  • Check vital signs and general status - usually normal in early disease, but cachexia or specific findings (e.g., hepatomegaly) may be present in advanced, metastatic cancer.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Breast cancer"][/sc]
  • New lump in a person over 30 - warrants urgent referral for assessment, particularly if persistent.
  • Skin changes of peau d\'orange - indicates dermal lymphatic invasion and is a sign of inflammatory breast cancer or locally advanced disease.
  • Palpable axillary node - suggests lymphatic spread and upstages the disease.
  • Bone pain or fracture - may indicate bony metastases, a common site for breast cancer spread.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Breast cancer"][/sc]
  • Younger women (<40): Often present with more aggressive, higher-grade tumours (e.g., triple-negative) and may have a stronger genetic predisposition ([sc name="jitl-query" Q="BRCA1 and BRCA2 genes: what are they and why do they matter clinically?" text="BRCA1/2 mutations"][/sc]).
  • Pregnancy: Diagnosis can be delayed as physiological breast changes (firmness, nodularity) mask lumps. Mammography is often avoided, and ultrasound is the preferred initial imaging.
  • Men: Breast cancer is rare in men (~370 cases/year in UK) but presents similarly with a painless lump, often subareolar. Awareness is lower, leading to later presentation.
  • Inflammatory breast cancer: Presents as a rapidly progressing, tender, and erythematous breast, often without a discrete lump, mimicking mastitis. This is an aggressive form of the disease.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Breast cancer"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected breast cancer ([sc name="jitl-query" Q="Triple assessment in breast cancer: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Triple Assessment"][/sc]):
    • Clinical examination
    • Imaging: Mammography and/or ultrasound
    • Pathology: Core biopsy or fine-needle aspiration cytology (FNAC)
  • Severity/risk tools: The Nottingham Prognostic Index (NPI) is used post-operatively to guide adjuvant therapy decisions. The Nottingham Prognostic Index on MDCalc can be used to estimate prognosis.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: A skin biopsy may be needed for suspected Paget\'s disease. If mastitis is suspected but does not respond to antibiotics, cancer must be excluded.
  • Imaging - Staging: For locally advanced or metastatic disease, CT of the chest, abdomen, and pelvis, and a whole-body bone scan or PET-CT are used to detect distant metastases.
  • Serial reassessment: Patients undergo regular follow-up including annual mammography to monitor for recurrence.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Breast cancer"][/sc]
  • A 62-year-old woman presents with a 3-week history of a painless, hard lump in the upper outer quadrant of her right breast. On examination, there is a 2cm, irregular, firm mass with some associated skin tethering.
  • This is a post-menopausal woman with a new, suspicious breast lump, concerning for breast cancer. The key next step is urgent referral for triple assessment.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Breast cancer"][/sc]
  • Fibroadenoma - A common benign tumour, typically in younger women; usually smooth, mobile (\'breast mouse\'), and well-circumscribed, unlike the irregular feel of a carcinoma.
  • Breast cyst - A fluid-filled sac, common in perimenopausal women; often smooth and mobile, and can be confirmed and aspirated under ultrasound.
  • Fat necrosis - Can present as a firm, irregular lump, often with skin tethering, mimicking cancer. A history of trauma to the breast is a key distinguishing feature, though not always present.
  • Mastitis/Abscess - Presents with pain, redness, and warmth, often with systemic symptoms like fever. Cancer is a differential if it does not resolve with antibiotics.
  • Lipoma - A benign tumour of fat cells; typically soft, mobile, and painless.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Breast cancer"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Urgent referral to a breast clinic for triple assessment. Psychological support is crucial from the point of suspicion.
  • Definitive management: A combination of surgery (breast-conserving or mastectomy with axillary node assessment), radiotherapy, and systemic therapy. The approach is guided by tumour size, grade, nodal status, and receptor status ([sc name="jitl-query" Q="ER, PR, and HER2 status in breast cancer: what are they and why do they matter clinically?" text="ER/PR/HER2 status"][/sc]).
  • Adjunctive management: Neoadjuvant chemotherapy may be used to downsize large tumours before surgery. Adjuvant (post-operative) therapy is common and includes chemotherapy, endocrine therapy, and/or targeted therapy (e.g., for HER2+ disease).
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Regular clinical review and annual mammography are standard. Patients on endocrine therapy are monitored for side effects.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Patients should be advised to report any new symptoms such as bone pain, persistent cough, or signs of lymphoedema promptly.
  • Exam pharmacology reference (UKMLA/MRCGP): Endocrine therapies like [sc name="jitl-medication" Q="Tamoxifen use in Breast cancer" text="tamoxifen"][/sc] (a SERM) and aromatase inhibitors such as [sc name="jitl-medication" Q="Letrozole use in Breast cancer" text="letrozole"][/sc] are central to ER-positive disease. Targeted therapy with [sc name="jitl-medication" Q="Trastuzumab use in Breast cancer" text="trastuzumab"][/sc] (Herceptin) is used for HER2-positive cancers. Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Breast cancer"][/sc]
  • Metastatic disease: Spread to bone, liver, lung, and brain is the primary cause of mortality. This can occur years after initial treatment and presents with symptoms specific to the affected organ.
  • Lymphoedema: Chronic swelling of the arm on the side of surgery, caused by disruption of axillary lymphatic drainage from surgery or radiotherapy. It can develop months or years later.
  • Psychological distress: Anxiety, depression, and body image issues are common throughout the patient journey.
  • Treatment-related side effects: Early menopause from chemotherapy, osteoporosis from aromatase inhibitors, and cardiotoxicity from some chemotherapy agents and trastuzumab are significant long-term issues.
