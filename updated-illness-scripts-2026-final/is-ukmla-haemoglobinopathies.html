Haemoglobinopathies [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Haemoglobinopathies"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Haemoglobinopathies"][/sc]
  • A group of inherited blood disorders characterised by abnormal haemoglobin production or structure, leading to various clinical manifestations such as anaemia.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical features, diagnostic approaches, and management principles of common haemoglobinopathies, including sickle cell disease and thalassaemia.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Haemoglobinopathies"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Haemoglobinopathies"][/sc]
  • In the UK, screening programmes identify thousands of carriers annually, with approximately 1 in 2,400 babies born with a serious haemoglobinopathy (NHS data).
  • Risk factors include family history and ethnic origin, particularly individuals of African, Caribbean, Mediterranean, and Asian descent.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Haemoglobinopathies"][/sc]
  • Genetic mutations affect the globin chains of haemoglobin, leading to structural abnormalities (e.g., sickle cell disease) or reduced synthesis (e.g., thalassaemia).
  • Abnormal haemoglobin molecules polymerise or precipitate, causing red blood cell fragility, premature destruction, and ineffective erythropoiesis.
  • This progression results in chronic anaemia, tissue hypoxia, and compensatory erythroid hyperplasia, contributing to systemic complications.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Haemoglobinopathies"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Symptoms often manifest in infancy or early childhood, particularly beyond 6 months of age as foetal haemoglobin levels decline.
  • Progressive phase: Chronic anaemia and recurrent crises (e.g., vaso-occlusive, haemolytic) typically characterise childhood and adolescence, requiring ongoing management.
  • Late phase: Long-term complications such as organ damage, pulmonary hypertension, and iron overload often develop in adulthood, particularly beyond decades of disease.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Haemoglobinopathies"][/sc]
  • Ask about fatigue and pallor - these are common indicators of chronic anaemia, which is a hallmark of many haemoglobinopathies.
  • Ask about recurrent pain episodes - suggestive of vaso-occlusive crises in sickle cell disease, indicating microvascular obstruction.
  • Ask about jaundice or dark urine - may indicate haemolysis, a feature of some haemoglobinopathies.
  • Ask about growth delay or failure to thrive - chronic illness and anaemia can significantly impact development in affected children.
  • Ask about family history of anaemia or blood disorders - genetic inheritance is key to diagnosis.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Haemoglobinopathies"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - tachycardia and tachypnoea may indicate significant anaemia or hypovolaemia during a crisis.
  • Check pallor of conjunctivae and mucous membranes - a direct sign of anaemia.
  • Check jaundice - visible yellowing of skin or sclera, indicating increased bilirubin from haemolysis.
  • Check splenomegaly or hepatomegaly - can be due to extramedullary haematopoiesis or sequestration crises.
  • Check bone deformities or frontal bossing - chronic erythroid hyperplasia can lead to skeletal changes, particularly in thalassaemia.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Haemoglobinopathies"][/sc]
  • Acute chest syndrome: Severe chest pain, fever, cough, and hypoxia, indicating a life-threatening pulmonary complication in sickle cell disease.
  • Splenic sequestration crisis: Sudden, painful enlargement of the spleen with rapid drop in haemoglobin, requiring urgent transfusion.
  • Aplastic crisis: Profound anaemia due to temporary cessation of red blood cell production, often triggered by parvovirus B19 infection.
  • Severe pain crisis unresponsive to analgesia: Suggests a severe vaso-occlusive event requiring hospital admission and aggressive management.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Haemoglobinopathies"][/sc]
  • Older adults: May present with insidious onset of symptoms, or complications such as chronic leg ulcers or progressive organ damage, rather than acute crises.
  • Pregnancy: Increased risk of crises, severe anaemia, and complications for both mother and foetus, requiring close monitoring and specialised care.
  • Immunosuppressed patients: May have atypical or more severe infections, which can trigger or exacerbate crises.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Haemoglobinopathies"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected Haemoglobinopathies: Full blood count (FBC) with red cell indices (MCV, MCH), reticulocyte count, and blood film examination.
  • Core for suspected Haemoglobinopathies: Haemoglobin electrophoresis or high-performance liquid chromatography (HPLC) to identify abnormal haemoglobin variants.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Iron studies (ferritin, serum iron, TIBC) to rule out iron deficiency anaemia, which can mimic some features.
  • Imaging - Ultrasound: Abdominal ultrasound to assess for splenomegaly, gallstones (common in haemolytic anaemias), or liver iron overload.
  • Serial reassessment: Regular FBC and clinical review to monitor disease progression and response to treatment.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Haemoglobinopathies"][/sc]
  • A 25-year-old female presents with a 3-day history of severe, generalised bone pain, fatigue, and jaundice, with a known family history of sickle cell disease.
  • Young adult, chronic inherited haematological disorder, acute painful crisis, haemolytic features, genetic predisposition.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Haemoglobinopathies"][/sc]
  • Iron deficiency anaemia - microcytic anaemia but normal haemoglobin electrophoresis.
  • Anaemia of chronic disease - often normocytic or microcytic, with elevated inflammatory markers.
  • G6PD deficiency - episodic haemolysis triggered by certain drugs or foods.
  • Hereditary spherocytosis - chronic haemolytic anaemia with spherocytes on blood film.
  • Leukaemia or lymphoma - can cause anaemia and splenomegaly but with different bone marrow findings.
  • Autoimmune haemolytic anaemia - positive direct antiglobulin test (DAT).
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Haemoglobinopathies"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Analgesia for pain crises, hydration, and oxygen supplementation if hypoxic.
  • Definitive management: Regular blood transfusions for severe anaemia (e.g., in thalassaemia major), chelation therapy for iron overload, and hydroxycarbamide for sickle cell disease to reduce crisis frequency.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Regular clinical reviews, FBCs, iron studies, and screening for complications such as organ damage.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Advise patients to seek urgent medical attention for fever, severe pain, sudden worsening of pallor, or respiratory distress.
  • Exam pharmacology reference (UKMLA/MRCGP): The use of [sc name="jitl-medication" Q="Hydroxycarbamide use in Sickle Cell Disease" text="hydroxycarbamide"][/sc] to increase foetal haemoglobin production and reduce sickling. Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Haemoglobinopathies"][/sc]
  • Chronic anaemia: Leads to fatigue, pallor, and reduced exercise tolerance, impacting quality of life over many years.
  • Iron overload: From repeated transfusions, causing damage to organs such as the heart, liver, and endocrine glands, particularly in the progressive and late phases.
  • Organ damage: Including stroke, renal failure, pulmonary hypertension, and avascular necrosis, often developing progressively over decades.
  • Infections: Increased susceptibility, especially to encapsulated organisms due to functional asplenia, a risk throughout the disease course.
  • Gallstones: Due to chronic haemolysis, leading to cholecystitis or biliary obstruction, often seen in adulthood.
