Infectious colitis (including Clostridium difficile) [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Infectious colitis (including Clostridium difficile)"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Infectious colitis (including Clostridium difficile)"][/sc]
  • Infectious colitis is an inflammation of the colon caused by pathogenic microorganisms, leading to symptoms such as diarrhoea, abdominal pain, and fever.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical presentation, common causative organisms (including Clostridium difficile), and initial management of infectious colitis.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Infectious colitis (including Clostridium difficile)"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Infectious colitis (including Clostridium difficile)"][/sc]
  • In the UK, acute infectious intestinal disease affects approximately 1 in 5 people annually, with a significant proportion attributable to bacterial causes like Campylobacter and Salmonella.
  • Clostridium difficile infection (CDI) incidence in England has shown fluctuations, with approximately 15,000-20,000 cases reported annually, predominantly affecting older adults and those with recent antibiotic exposure.
  • Risk factors include recent travel, consumption of contaminated food or water, close contact with infected individuals, antibiotic use (especially broad-spectrum), immunosuppression, and extremes of age.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Infectious colitis (including Clostridium difficile)"][/sc]
  • Ingestion of pathogenic microorganisms or their toxins (e.g., C. difficile toxins A and B) leads to colonisation and direct mucosal damage or immune-mediated inflammation.
  • This results in disruption of the intestinal epithelial barrier, leading to increased permeability, fluid secretion, and impaired absorption, manifesting as diarrhoea.
  • The inflammatory response, often exacerbated by bacterial virulence factors, can cause ulceration, pseudomembrane formation (in CDI), and systemic symptoms such as fever and malaise.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Infectious colitis (including Clostridium difficile)"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Symptoms typically develop within hours to a few days post-exposure, characterised by acute onset of diarrhoea and abdominal discomfort.
  • Progressive phase: Without appropriate intervention, symptoms may worsen over several days, particularly beyond 3-5 days, potentially leading to dehydration, electrolyte imbalance, and increased abdominal pain.
  • Late phase: Resolution usually occurs within a week to 10 days with supportive care, though some infections, particularly severe C. difficile, can persist for weeks or lead to complications if not adequately treated.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Infectious colitis (including Clostridium difficile)"][/sc]
  • Ask about diarrhoea characteristics - to assess severity, frequency, consistency, and presence of blood or mucus, which can indicate inflammatory or invasive pathogens.
  • Ask about abdominal pain - to determine location, character (cramping vs. constant), and severity, helping to differentiate from other abdominal pathologies.
  • Ask about fever and chills - to evaluate for systemic inflammatory response and potential bacteraemia.
  • Ask about recent antibiotic use - crucial for suspecting Clostridium difficile infection, a common cause of antibiotic-associated colitis.
  • Ask about recent travel history or food consumption - to identify potential sources of infection and guide microbiological investigations.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Infectious colitis (including Clostridium difficile)"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - to assess for signs of dehydration (tachycardia, hypotension), fever, and systemic toxicity.
  • Check abdominal tenderness - to localise inflammation and assess for signs of peritonism, suggesting severe colitis or perforation.
  • Check for signs of dehydration - such as reduced skin turgor, dry mucous membranes, and sunken eyes, indicating significant fluid loss.
  • Check for abdominal distension - may indicate ileus or toxic megacolon, particularly in severe C. difficile infection.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Infectious colitis (including Clostridium difficile)"][/sc]
  • Severe dehydration - requiring intravenous fluid resuscitation.
  • Signs of sepsis - indicating systemic infection and potential organ dysfunction.
  • Significant abdominal pain with guarding or rebound tenderness - suggestive of peritonitis or bowel perforation.
  • Bloody diarrhoea with fever - may indicate invasive bacterial infection or severe inflammatory colitis.
  • Toxic megacolon - a life-threatening complication, particularly of severe C. difficile infection.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Infectious colitis (including Clostridium difficile)"][/sc]
  • Older adults: May present with non-specific symptoms like confusion, lethargy, or falls, with diarrhoea being less prominent, making diagnosis challenging.
  • Immunosuppressed patients: Can have attenuated inflammatory responses, leading to less severe symptoms despite significant underlying infection, or present with unusual pathogens.
  • Post-antibiotic diarrhoea without overt colitis: Patients may experience diarrhoea after antibiotic use without the full picture of colitis, but still be at risk for C. difficile infection.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Infectious colitis (including Clostridium difficile)"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected infectious colitis: Stool culture for bacterial pathogens (e.g., Salmonella, Campylobacter, Shigella, E. coli O157), and PCR for Clostridium difficile toxin.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Full blood count (leukocytosis, anaemia), C-reactive protein (CRP), urea and electrolytes (U&Es) to assess hydration and renal function.
  • Imaging - Abdominal X-ray: To assess for toxic megacolon or perforation in severe cases.
  • Imaging - CT abdomen and pelvis: May be considered in severe or complicated cases to assess for bowel wall thickening, perforation, or abscess formation.
  • Serial reassessment: Regular clinical review and repeat vital signs, particularly in patients with red flag features or those not responding to initial management.

  • A 68-year-old female, recently discharged from hospital after a course of co-amoxiclav for pneumonia, presents with a 3-day history of watery diarrhoea, abdominal cramps, and a low-grade fever.
