Crystal arthropathy [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Crystal arthropathy"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Crystal arthropathy"][/sc]
  • Crystal arthropathy is a group of inflammatory joint conditions caused by the deposition of crystals within joints or periarticular tissues, leading to acute or chronic arthritis.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: The candidate must recognise the clinical features, investigations, and management of common crystal arthropathies, particularly gout and pseudogout.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Crystal arthropathy"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Crystal arthropathy"][/sc]
  • Gout is the most common inflammatory arthritis in men over 40, with a prevalence of 1-2% in the UK, increasing with age [1]. Risk factors include hyperuricaemia, obesity, hypertension, chronic kidney disease, diuretic use, and high purine diet.
  • Pseudogout (calcium pyrophosphate deposition disease) prevalence increases with age, affecting 10-15% of individuals over 65 and up to 50% over 85 [2]. Risk factors include advanced age, osteoarthritis, hyperparathyroidism, haemochromatosis, and hypomagnesaemia.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Crystal arthropathy"][/sc]
  • Trigger: Supersaturation of synovial fluid with urate (gout) or calcium pyrophosphate (pseudogout) leads to crystal formation, often triggered by minor trauma, surgery, or rapid changes in serum crystal levels.
  • Progression: Crystals are phagocytosed by synovial macrophages and neutrophils, leading to the release of pro-inflammatory cytokines (e.g., IL-1β), initiating an acute inflammatory response.
  • Consequence: Intense inflammation causes severe pain, swelling, erythema, and warmth in the affected joint, which can lead to joint damage and chronic arthropathy if left untreated. Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Crystal arthropathy"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Acute attacks typically have a rapid onset, reaching peak intensity within 6-12 hours, often presenting overnight.
  • Progressive phase: Untreated acute attacks usually resolve spontaneously within 7-14 days, but recurrent attacks can become more frequent and severe, particularly beyond 12 months.
  • Late phase: Chronic crystal arthropathy can develop with persistent inflammation, joint damage, and tophi formation (gout) or chronic pain and functional impairment (pseudogout), particularly beyond several years of recurrent attacks.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Crystal arthropathy"][/sc]
  • Ask about sudden onset of severe joint pain - often described as excruciating, indicating an acute inflammatory process.
  • Ask about joint swelling, redness, and warmth - cardinal signs of inflammation, helping to differentiate from mechanical pain.
  • Ask about previous episodes of similar joint pain - suggestive of recurrent crystal attacks.
  • Ask about associated fever or chills - may indicate systemic inflammation or septic arthritis.
  • Ask about diet, alcohol intake, and medication history (e.g., diuretics) - relevant for identifying risk factors for gout.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Crystal arthropathy"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - fever may be present in acute attacks, indicating systemic response.
  • Check affected joint for erythema, swelling, warmth, and exquisite tenderness - characteristic of acute crystal arthritis.
  • Check for tophi (gout) - subcutaneous nodules, often around joints, ears, or olecranon, indicating chronic urate deposition.
  • Check range of motion - typically severely limited due to pain and swelling. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Joint examination findings in crystal arthropathy: what do they indicate?" text="Joint examination findings"][/sc]
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Crystal arthropathy"][/sc]
  • Systemic sepsis: High fever, rigors, rapidly worsening systemic illness - necessitates urgent exclusion of septic arthritis.
  • Polyarticular involvement: Multiple joints affected, especially if symmetrical - may suggest other inflammatory arthropathies or systemic conditions.
  • Failure to respond to conventional treatment: Persistent symptoms despite appropriate management - prompts reconsideration of diagnosis or complications.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Crystal arthropathy"][/sc]
  • Older adults: Gout and pseudogout can present as polyarticular arthritis, mimicking rheumatoid arthritis or osteoarthritis, making diagnosis challenging.
  • Immunosuppressed patients: Inflammatory response may be blunted, leading to less pronounced symptoms and signs, delaying diagnosis of acute attacks.
  • Chronic tophaceous gout: Can present with chronic joint pain and swelling without acute flares, mimicking osteoarthritis or rheumatoid arthritis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Crystal arthropathy"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected crystal arthropathy: Synovial fluid aspiration and microscopy for crystal identification (urate crystals in gout, CPP crystals in pseudogout) is the gold standard.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Full blood count (leukocytosis), ESR/CRP (elevated in inflammation), renal function (hyperuricaemia), serum urate (often elevated in gout, but can be normal during acute attack).
  • Imaging - X-ray: May show soft tissue swelling, erosions (gout), or chondrocalcinosis (pseudogout).
  • Serial reassessment: Clinical review and repeat inflammatory markers if initial diagnosis is unclear or response to treatment is poor.

  • A 60-year-old male presents with sudden onset, excruciating pain, swelling, and redness in his right great toe, which started overnight after a celebratory meal with alcohol.
  • An older male with acute, monoarticular inflammatory arthritis, suggestive of crystal deposition.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Crystal arthropathy"][/sc]
  • Septic arthritis - rapid onset, severe pain, systemic features, but often with risk factors like recent surgery or immunosuppression.
  • Rheumatoid arthritis - typically polyarticular, symmetrical, chronic, with morning stiffness, but can have acute flares.
  • Psoriatic arthritis - associated with psoriasis, dactylitis, enthesitis, but can mimic gout in some presentations.
  • Osteoarthritis - chronic, mechanical pain, less inflammatory signs, but can have acute inflammatory episodes.
  • Reactive arthritis - often follows an infection, asymmetrical oligoarthritis, but typically involves lower limbs.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Crystal arthropathy"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Pain relief and reduction of inflammation. Consider NSAIDs (e.g., ibuprofen, naproxen), colchicine, or oral corticosteroids depending on patient comorbidities and severity.
  • Definitive management: For gout, initiate urate-lowering therapy (ULT) with allopurinol or febuxostat once acute attack has settled, to prevent recurrent flares and complications. For pseudogout, manage symptoms as there is no specific ULT.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Monitor serum urate levels regularly in gout patients on ULT to ensure target levels are achieved. Clinical review to assess response to treatment and address lifestyle modifications.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Advise patients to seek medical attention if symptoms worsen, new symptoms develop, or if there are signs of infection.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Crystal arthropathy"][/sc]
  • Chronic arthropathy: Persistent inflammation and crystal deposition can lead to irreversible joint damage, deformity, and functional impairment, particularly beyond several years of recurrent attacks.
  • Tophi (gout): Urate crystal deposits can form visible and palpable nodules, leading to joint destruction, nerve compression, and skin ulceration.
  • Renal complications (gout): Chronic hyperuricaemia can lead to urate nephropathy and kidney stone formation.
  • Infection: Acute crystal arthritis can be difficult to distinguish from septic arthritis, and co-existence is possible, leading to diagnostic delay and increased morbidity.
