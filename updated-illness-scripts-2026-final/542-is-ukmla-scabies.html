Scabies (Skin and soft tissue) [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Scabies"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Scabies"][/sc]
  • Scabies is an intensely itchy skin infestation caused by the human parasite Sarcoptes scabiei.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical presentation of scabies, identify risk factors for transmission, and outline appropriate management strategies for the patient and contacts.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Scabies"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Scabies"][/sc]
  • Scabies is a common parasitic infestation globally, affecting people of all ages and socioeconomic groups.
  • In the UK, scabies diagnoses in sexual health services increased by 44% between 2023 and 2024, with 41% of diagnoses in 2024 occurring in individuals aged 20-24.
  • Transmission occurs primarily through close, prolonged skin-to-skin contact, making household contacts, sexually active individuals, and those in institutional settings (e.g., care homes, nurseries) at higher risk.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Scabies"][/sc]
  • The female Sarcoptes scabiei mite burrows into the stratum corneum of the epidermis, laying 2-3 eggs per day for 4-6 weeks.
  • Mite faeces and eggs trigger a delayed-type hypersensitivity reaction, leading to intense pruritus, typically 2-6 weeks after initial infestation in immunocompetent individuals, or within days in re-infestation. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Scabies hypersensitivity reaction: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Delayed-type hypersensitivity reaction"][/sc]
  • Persistent scratching can lead to excoriations, secondary bacterial infections (e.g., impetigo, cellulitis), and in severe cases, crusted (Norwegian) scabies, particularly in immunosuppressed individuals.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Scabies"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Following initial infestation, an asymptomatic incubation period of 2-6 weeks typically occurs before the onset of pruritus and rash. In re-infestation, symptoms may appear within days.
  • Progressive phase: Without treatment, symptoms persist and often worsen, with nocturnal pruritus becoming particularly severe. The rash may spread to cover most of the body, sparing the head and neck in adults.
  • Late phase: If untreated, chronic scabies can lead to significant skin damage from scratching, including lichenification and secondary bacterial infections. Post-treatment pruritus can persist for several weeks, particularly beyond 2-4 weeks, due to residual mite antigens.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Scabies"][/sc]
  • Ask about intense pruritus - typically worse at night and after a hot bath or shower, indicating a hypersensitivity reaction to mite products.
  • Ask about rash distribution - often starting in finger webs, wrists, elbows, axillae, belt line, genitalia, and buttocks, providing clues to mite burrowing sites.
  • Ask about history of contact - recent close skin-to-skin contact with an itchy individual, which is crucial for epidemiological diagnosis and contact tracing.
  • Ask about duration of symptoms - onset 2-6 weeks post-exposure (or days in re-infestation), helping to differentiate from other acute dermatoses.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Scabies"][/sc]
  • Check for burrows - fine, wavy, thread-like lines (2-10 mm long) with a small papule or vesicle at one end, pathognomonic for scabies, often found in finger webs, wrists, and genitalia. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Scabies burrows: what are they and why do they matter clinically?" text="Scabies burrows"][/sc]
  • Check for erythematous papules and vesicles - often excoriated, particularly in areas of mite predilection, indicating inflammatory response.
  • Check for secondary skin changes - excoriations, crusting, lichenification, or impetiginisation, suggesting chronicity or secondary bacterial infection.
  • Check vital signs - typically normal; fever or systemic signs may indicate secondary bacterial infection or a severe variant like crusted scabies.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Scabies"][/sc]
  • Crusted (Norwegian) Scabies - widespread, hyperkeratotic, crusted lesions, particularly in immunosuppressed individuals, indicating a highly contagious and severe form with a high mite burden.
  • Extensive secondary bacterial infection - rapidly spreading erythema, warmth, pain, or purulent discharge, suggesting cellulitis or abscess formation requiring urgent antibiotic treatment.
  • Systemic symptoms - fever, malaise, or lymphadenopathy in conjunction with skin lesions, raising concern for severe secondary infection or an alternative diagnosis.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Scabies"][/sc]
  • Infants and young children: Rash may involve the head, neck, palms, and soles, and can present as vesicles, pustules, or nodules, differing from adult distribution.
  • Older adults: Pruritus may be less intense, and the rash may be subtle or atypical, leading to delayed diagnosis and potential for widespread outbreaks in care settings.
  • Immunosuppressed individuals: May develop crusted (Norwegian) scabies with minimal pruritus but extensive hyperkeratotic lesions, posing a significant infection risk. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Crusted (Norwegian) scabies: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Crusted (Norwegian) scabies"][/sc]
  • Clean scabies: Individuals who maintain excellent hygiene may have very few mites and atypical lesions, making diagnosis challenging.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Scabies"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected scabies: Clinical diagnosis based on characteristic symptoms (pruritus, nocturnal worsening, contact history) and signs (burrows, typical rash distribution).
  • Confirmatory tests: Skin scrapings or dermoscopy to identify mites, eggs, or faecal pellets, particularly when clinical diagnosis is uncertain. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Dermoscopy in scabies: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Dermoscopy for scabies"][/sc]
  • Severity/risk tools: No widely validated severity or risk score is routinely used for classic scabies; assessment is clinical.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Consider skin biopsy for histopathology if other dermatological conditions are suspected.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Scabies"][/sc]
  • A young adult presenting with intensely itchy, erythematous papules and burrows in the finger webs, worse at night, following recent close contact with an affected individual.
  • Generalized pruritic rash with characteristic burrows, excoriations, and a history of close contact, concerning for parasitic skin infestation.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Scabies"][/sc]
  • Eczema (dermatitis) - often chronic, with different distribution and absence of burrows; can co-exist with scabies.
  • Insect bites (e.g., bed bugs, fleas) - typically larger, more localised lesions, often with a central punctum; lack burrows.
  • Psoriasis - well-demarcated erythematous plaques with silvery scales, different distribution; can be exacerbated by scratching.
  • Lichen planus - purplish, polygonal, pruritic papules, often on flexor surfaces; Wickham 's striae may be present.
  • Urticaria - transient, migratory wheals; typically resolves within 24 hours.
  • Prurigo nodularis - intensely itchy, firm nodules, often secondary to chronic scratching.
  • Dermatitis herpetiformis - intensely pruritic vesicles and papules, symmetrically distributed, associated with coeliac disease.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Scabies"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Confirm diagnosis, identify and treat all close contacts simultaneously, and provide symptomatic relief for pruritus.
  • Definitive management: Topical permethrin 5% cream is often the first-line treatment, applied to the entire body (excluding the head and neck in adults) and washed off after 8-12 hours. A second application is typically recommended 7 days later.
  • Adjunctive management: Oral antihistamines may support symptomatic relief of pruritus. Emollients can help soothe irritated skin.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Review for persistent symptoms after 2-4 weeks. Persistent itching is common post-treatment and does not necessarily indicate treatment failure or re-infestation.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Advise patients to seek medical attention if new lesions appear after 4 weeks, if signs of secondary bacterial infection develop, or if symptoms worsen despite treatment.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Scabies"][/sc]
  • Secondary bacterial infection: Persistent scratching can lead to skin breakdown, allowing entry of bacteria (e.g., Staphylococcus aureus, Streptococcus pyogenes), resulting in impetigo, cellulitis, or even sepsis, particularly if not recognised and treated promptly.
  • Post-scabetic pruritus: Itching may persist for several weeks (particularly beyond 2-4 weeks) after successful eradication of mites due to residual mite antigens and ongoing hypersensitivity, which can be diagnostically challenging.
  • Crusted (Norwegian) scabies: A severe, highly contagious form occurring in immunosuppressed individuals, characterised by hyperkeratotic crusts containing millions of mites, posing a significant public health risk and requiring aggressive treatment.
  • Psychological impact: The intense pruritus, visible rash, and social stigma associated with scabies can lead to sleep disturbance, anxiety, depression, and social isolation.
