Juvenile idiopathic arthritis [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Juvenile idiopathic arthritis"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Juvenile idiopathic arthritis"][/sc]
  • Juvenile idiopathic arthritis (JIA) is a heterogeneous group of chronic arthritides of unknown aetiology, affecting children under 16 years of age for at least six weeks.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the diverse presentations of JIA, understand its classification, and identify key features indicating potential complications such as uveitis.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Juvenile idiopathic arthritis"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Juvenile idiopathic arthritis"][/sc]
  • Approximately 1,000 children are diagnosed with JIA in the UK annually, with an estimated prevalence of 1 in 1,000 children [NICE TA373].
  • JIA is more common in females, with oligoarticular JIA typically presenting in those under 6 years old, and polyarticular JIA having peaks in toddler to preschool age and pre-adolescence [patient.info].
  • Risk factors include genetic predisposition (e.g., HLA B27 in enthesitis-related JIA) and certain autoimmune conditions.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Juvenile idiopathic arthritis"][/sc]
  • Trigger: An interplay of genetic susceptibility and environmental factors leads to immune dysregulation, initiating chronic inflammation within the joints.
  • Progression: Activated immune cells, including T-cells and macrophages, release pro-inflammatory cytokines (e.g., TNF-alpha, IL-6), leading to synovial hypertrophy and cartilage degradation. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Cytokines in JIA: what are they and why do they matter clinically?" text="Cytokines"][/sc]
  • Consequence: Persistent inflammation results in joint damage, pain, stiffness, and potential long-term disability, including growth disturbances and osteoporosis.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Juvenile idiopathic arthritis"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Symptoms typically manifest insidiously over weeks to months, often with morning stiffness and joint swelling, which may be subtle and initially painless, particularly in younger children.
  • Progressive phase: Without effective management, inflammation can persist for months to years, leading to chronic pain, joint damage, and functional impairment.
  • Late phase: Long-standing disease, particularly beyond several years, can result in irreversible joint destruction, growth abnormalities, and extra-articular complications such as uveitis.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Juvenile idiopathic arthritis"][/sc]
  • Ask about joint pain and swelling - often worse in the morning or after inactivity, indicating inflammatory arthritis.
  • Ask about morning stiffness - a hallmark symptom of inflammatory arthritis, typically lasting more than 30 minutes.
  • Ask about limping or reluctance to use a limb - especially in younger children, this may be the only presenting symptom of joint involvement.
  • Ask about fatigue and irritability - common systemic symptoms, particularly in systemic JIA, reflecting chronic inflammation.
  • Ask about eye symptoms (redness, pain, photophobia) - crucial for identifying uveitis, a serious extra-articular complication.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Juvenile idiopathic arthritis"][/sc]
  • Check for joint swelling and warmth - objective signs of inflammation, often without significant erythema.
  • Check for restricted range of motion - due to pain, swelling, or capsular thickening, indicating active arthritis.
  • Check for tenderness on palpation of affected joints - a sign of active inflammation.
  • Check for vital signs - fever (especially quotidian pattern in systemic JIA), tachycardia, and other signs of systemic illness. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Quotidian fever: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Quotidian fever"][/sc]
  • Check for skin rash - evanescent, salmon-pink rash in systemic JIA, or psoriatic lesions in juvenile psoriatic arthritis.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Juvenile idiopathic arthritis"][/sc]
  • Persistent unexplained fever: Suggests systemic JIA or other serious conditions, requiring urgent investigation.
  • Acute visual changes or red eye: Indicates potential uveitis, which can lead to irreversible vision loss if not promptly treated.
  • Sudden onset severe pain and inability to bear weight: May suggest septic arthritis or osteomyelitis, requiring immediate assessment and management.
  • New neurological symptoms: Could indicate central nervous system involvement, a rare but serious complication.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Juvenile idiopathic arthritis"][/sc]
  • Young children: May present with unexplained limping, irritability, or regression of motor skills rather than overt joint pain.
  • Oligoarticular JIA: Often presents with minimal pain despite significant joint swelling, particularly in the knee or ankle. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Oligoarticular JIA: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Oligoarticular JIA"][/sc]
  • Systemic JIA: Initial presentation may be dominated by systemic features like high fevers, rash, and lymphadenopathy, with arthritis developing later.
  • Uveitis without active arthritis: In some cases, asymptomatic uveitis can precede or occur independently of joint symptoms, especially in ANA-positive oligoarticular JIA.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Juvenile idiopathic arthritis"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected JIA: Full blood count (FBC) to check for anaemia and thrombocytosis, ESR/CRP for inflammatory markers, ANA, RF, and HLA-B27 for classification and risk stratification.
  • Severity/risk tools: [sc name="jitl-query" Q="JIA activity scores: what are they and why do they matter clinically?" text="JIA activity scores"][/sc] Pediatric Rheumatology Activity Score (PRAS) on MDCalc
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Consider infection screens (e.g., blood cultures, joint aspirate), imaging for malignancy (e.g., X-ray, MRI), or other autoimmune markers.
  • Imaging - X-ray: Often normal in early JIA but useful to exclude other pathologies like trauma, osteomyelitis, or malignancy.
  • Imaging - Ultrasound: Can detect joint effusions, synovial hypertrophy, and early erosions not visible on plain X-rays.
  • Imaging - MRI: Provides detailed assessment of joint inflammation, cartilage, and bone changes, particularly useful for assessing disease activity and damage.
  • Serial reassessment: Regular ophthalmological review for uveitis screening, especially in high-risk groups (oligoarticular JIA, ANA positive).
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Juvenile idiopathic arthritis"][/sc]
  • A pre-school aged child presents with a several-month history of morning stiffness, limping, and painless knee swelling, with no history of trauma or infection.
  • Chronic inflammatory arthropathy in a child, concerning for juvenile idiopathic arthritis.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Juvenile idiopathic arthritis"][/sc]
  • Septic arthritis - acute onset, severe pain, systemic toxicity, often monoarticular.
  • Osteomyelitis - localised bone pain, fever, often insidious onset, may mimic joint pain.
  • Reactive arthritis - follows an infection, typically asymmetrical oligoarthritis.
  • Lyme disease - history of tick bite, migratory arthralgia, erythema migrans.
  • Leukaemia - bone pain, pallor, bruising, lymphadenopathy, hepatosplenomegaly. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Leukaemia vs JIA: how to differentiate clinically?" text="Leukaemia"][/sc]
  • Connective tissue diseases (e.g., SLE) - multi-system involvement, specific autoantibodies.
  • Mechanical pain/trauma - clear history of injury, pain often activity-related, no inflammatory markers.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Juvenile idiopathic arthritis"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Recognition of inflammatory arthritis, referral to paediatric rheumatology, and initiation of supportive care including pain management.
  • Definitive management: Often includes non-steroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs), disease-modifying anti-rheumatic drugs (DMARDs) such as methotrexate, and biological therapies (e.g., TNF-alpha inhibitors) for persistent disease.
  • Adjunctive management: Physiotherapy and occupational therapy are crucial for maintaining joint function, strength, and preventing deformities.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Regular clinical reviews, laboratory monitoring for disease activity and drug toxicity, and ophthalmological screening for uveitis.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Advise parents on red flag symptoms (e.g., worsening pain, fever, visual changes) and when to seek urgent medical attention.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Juvenile idiopathic arthritis"][/sc]
  • Chronic pain: Persistent inflammation can lead to ongoing pain, significantly impacting quality of life and daily activities.
  • Joint damage and deformity: Uncontrolled inflammation, particularly over months to years, can cause irreversible cartilage and bone erosion, leading to functional limitations.
  • Uveitis: Inflammation of the eye, especially common in oligoarticular JIA and ANA-positive patients, can lead to cataracts, glaucoma, and permanent vision loss if not detected and treated early. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Uveitis screening in JIA: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Uveitis screening in JIA"][/sc]
  • Growth disturbance: Chronic inflammation and corticosteroid use can impair normal growth and development.
  • Osteoporosis: Reduced bone mineral density due to chronic inflammation, immobility, and corticosteroid use increases fracture risk.
  • Psychosocial impact: Chronic illness can lead to anxiety, depression, and difficulties with school attendance and social integration.
