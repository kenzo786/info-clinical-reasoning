Tuberculosis (Infections) [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Tuberculosis (Infections)"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Tuberculosis (Infections)"][/sc]
  • Tuberculosis (TB) is a granulomatous disease caused by the Mycobacterium tuberculosis complex, an infection that primarily affects the lungs but can involve any organ. It can be serious if not treated.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical presentations of both pulmonary and extrapulmonary tuberculosis, understand its transmission and key risk factors, and identify appropriate initial investigations and management principles.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Tuberculosis (Infections)"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Tuberculosis"][/sc]
  • In 2024, there was a 13.6% increase in the number of TB notifications in England, from 4,831 in 2023 to 5,490 (GOV.UK).
  • The rate of TB in 2023 was 15.7 per 100,000 in the 10% of the population living in the most deprived areas, compared with only 3.3 per 100,000 in less deprived areas (GOV.UK).
  • Transmission occurs through droplet inhalation, commonly spread by coughing and sneezing from individuals with active pulmonary TB.
  • Risk factors include close contact with an infected person, being born in or having lived in areas with high TB prevalence, weakened immune systems (e.g., HIV, immunosuppression, diabetes, malignancy), age (under 5 years), living in overcrowded or unhealthy conditions, and substance misuse (smoking, alcohol, drugs).
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Tuberculosis (Infections)"][/sc]
  • Trigger: Inhalation of airborne droplets containing Mycobacterium tuberculosis, typically from an individual with active pulmonary TB. The bacilli reach the alveoli and are phagocytosed by alveolar macrophages.
  • Progression: In most immunocompetent individuals, the immune system contains the infection, forming granulomas (tubercles) with central caseous necrosis, often in the mid-zones of the lungs. This can lead to a Ghon focus (primary lesion) and associated hilar lymphadenopathy, forming a Ghon complex. The infection may become latent, with viable bacilli persisting within the granulomas without causing active disease. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Ghon focus: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Ghon focus"][/sc]
  • Consequence: Latent TB can reactivate (post-primary or secondary TB) years or decades later, particularly when host immunity wanes. This often occurs in the lung apices (upper lobes) due to higher oxygen tension, leading to progressive lung destruction, cavitation, and potential dissemination via the bloodstream or lymphatic system to other organs (extrapulmonary TB).
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Tuberculosis (Infections)"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Initial infection with Mycobacterium tuberculosis, often asymptomatic. Within weeks, the immune system typically contains the infection, leading to latent tuberculosis (LTBI). A small proportion of individuals, particularly young children or immunocompromised, may develop primary progressive TB within weeks to a few months.
  • Progressive phase: Latent TB can persist for years or decades. Reactivation (post-primary TB) can occur when host immunity is compromised, leading to active disease. This progression can be insidious, with symptoms developing gradually over weeks to months.
  • Late phase: If untreated, active TB progresses, causing significant organ damage (e.g., lung cavitation, extrapulmonary dissemination). This can lead to chronic illness, severe complications, and death, particularly beyond several months of active, untreated disease.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Tuberculosis (Infections)"][/sc]
  • Ask about persistent cough (lasting >3 weeks) - may indicate active pulmonary disease and potential for transmission.
  • Ask about unexplained weight loss and anorexia - common constitutional symptoms suggesting chronic infection.
  • Ask about fever and night sweats - classic systemic symptoms of active TB, often insidious in onset.
  • Ask about haemoptysis or blood-stained sputum - a concerning symptom of pulmonary TB, indicating lung parenchymal damage.
  • Ask about breathlessness or chest pain - may suggest significant pulmonary involvement, pleural effusion, or pericardial disease.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Tuberculosis (Infections)"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - fever, tachycardia, and tachypnoea may indicate systemic inflammatory response; hypoxaemia in severe pulmonary disease.
  • Check lymphadenopathy - palpable, often non-tender, matted lymph nodes (e.g., cervical, supraclavicular) can indicate extrapulmonary TB, particularly scrofula. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Scrofula: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Scrofula"][/sc]
  • Check respiratory examination - dullness to percussion, reduced breath sounds (pleural effusion), crackles (pulmonary consolidation), or amphoric breath sounds (cavitation) may be present.
  • Check skin for erythema nodosum or lupus vulgaris - cutaneous manifestations of TB, indicating extrapulmonary spread.
  • Check neurological signs - meningism (stiff neck, photophobia) or focal neurological deficits may indicate TB meningitis or tuberculoma.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Tuberculosis (Infections)"][/sc]
  • Haemoptysis: Suggests active pulmonary TB with potential for significant lung damage and requires urgent investigation.
  • Meningism (stiff neck, photophobia) with headache: Indicates potential central nervous system involvement (TB meningitis), a medical emergency requiring immediate management.
  • Unexplained weight loss and persistent fever: May signify advanced or disseminated disease, particularly in immunocompromised individuals.
  • New onset neurological deficit or altered mental status: Raises concern for intracranial tuberculoma or TB meningitis.
  • Progressive breathlessness or significant chest pain: Could indicate extensive pulmonary involvement, pleural effusion, or pericardial disease, warranting urgent respiratory or emergency assessment.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Tuberculosis (Infections)"][/sc]
  • Older adults: May present with non-specific symptoms such as fatigue, anorexia, and weight loss, often without classic respiratory symptoms. Reactivation TB is more common.
  • Immunocompromised individuals (e.g., HIV, organ transplant recipients, on immunosuppressants): Can have disseminated or extrapulmonary TB, often with atypical radiological findings and rapid progression. Symptoms may be subtle or masked by other conditions.
  • Children: Primary TB is more common, often presenting with non-specific symptoms like fever, failure to thrive, and lymphadenopathy. Pulmonary symptoms may be absent, and diagnosis can be challenging.
  • Extrapulmonary TB: Presentation varies widely depending on the affected organ (e.g., sterile pyuria in renal TB, back pain in Pott\'s disease, headache/meningism in TB meningitis). Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Tuberculosis (Infections)"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected Tuberculosis:
    • Sputum microscopy for acid-fast bacilli (AFB) and culture for Mycobacterium tuberculosis (gold standard for pulmonary TB).
    • Nucleic acid amplification tests (NAATs) for rapid detection of M. tuberculosis and drug resistance.
    • Chest X-ray: May show consolidation, cavitation (especially upper lobes), Ghon complex, or miliary patterns. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Upper lobe cavitation in TB: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Upper lobe cavitation"][/sc]
    • Mantoux tuberculin skin test (TST) or Interferon-Gamma Release Assay (IGRA): Used to detect latent TB infection. IGRA is generally preferred due to higher specificity and less influence from BCG vaccination. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="IGRA vs Mantoux test: what is the distinction and why does it matter clinically?" text="IGRA vs Mantoux"][/sc]
  • Severity/risk tools: No specific MDCalc score is routinely used for severity assessment in general TB, but clinical assessment of disease extent and patient factors guides management.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Consider CT scan of chest/abdomen/pelvis, bronchoscopy with bronchoalveolar lavage (BAL), or biopsy of affected tissue/lymph nodes.
  • Serial reassessment: Clinical and microbiological monitoring during treatment to assess response and detect drug resistance.

  • A 35-year-old male, recently immigrated from a high-prevalence country, presents with a 6-week history of productive cough, night sweats, and unintentional weight loss, concerning for active pulmonary tuberculosis.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Tuberculosis (Infections)"][/sc]
  • Community-acquired pneumonia (bacterial or viral) - acute onset, often with more prominent systemic inflammatory response.
  • Lung malignancy - insidious onset, weight loss, cough, haemoptysis, but typically older age group and different risk factors.
  • Sarcoidosis - granulomatous disease with similar radiological features (hilar lymphadenopathy, pulmonary infiltrates), but often with extrapulmonary manifestations like erythema nodosum or uveitis. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Sarcoidosis vs Tuberculosis: what is the distinction and why does it matter clinically?" text="Sarcoidosis vs Tuberculosis"][/sc]
  • Bronchiectasis - chronic cough with sputum production, but usually a history of recurrent respiratory infections and characteristic CT findings.
  • Fungal infections (e.g., aspergilloma) - can cause cavitating lung lesions and haemoptysis, particularly in immunocompromised individuals.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Tuberculosis (Infections)"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Prompt recognition and isolation of suspected active pulmonary TB cases to prevent transmission. Notification to public health authorities. Initiation of empiric treatment after obtaining appropriate samples.
  • Definitive management: Standard treatment for active TB involves a multi-drug regimen (RIPE therapy) for 6 months. The initial phase typically includes rifampicin, isoniazid, pyrazinamide, and ethambutol for 2 months, followed by a continuation phase of rifampicin and isoniazid for 4 months. Treatment duration may be longer for extrapulmonary or drug-resistant TB.
  • Adjunctive management: Pyridoxine (vitamin B6) is co-administered with isoniazid to prevent peripheral neuropathy. Directly Observed Therapy (DOT) may be used to ensure adherence, particularly in vulnerable populations.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Regular clinical review, monitoring of liver function tests (due to hepatotoxic drugs), renal function, and visual acuity (for ethambutol). Sputum smear and culture conversion are monitored to assess treatment response.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Patients should be advised on symptoms of drug toxicity (e.g., jaundice, rash, visual changes) and worsening disease. Non-adherence or treatment failure requires specialist review and investigation for drug resistance.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Tuberculosis (Infections)"][/sc]
  • Respiratory failure: Can result from extensive pulmonary destruction, pleural effusions, or pneumothorax, particularly in advanced or cavitating disease.
  • Haemoptysis: Significant or life-threatening bleeding can occur from cavitating lesions or Rasmussen\'s aneurysm.
  • Drug-resistant TB: Emergence of multi-drug resistant (MDR-TB) or extensively drug-resistant (XDR-TB) strains due to inadequate treatment or non-adherence, leading to prolonged and more toxic regimens.
  • Disseminated (Miliary) TB: Widespread haematogenous spread of bacilli to multiple organs, presenting with non-specific symptoms and high mortality if untreated. This is particularly relevant in immunocompromised individuals.
  • TB meningitis: A severe form of extrapulmonary TB affecting the central nervous system, leading to hydrocephalus, neurological deficits, and high mortality/morbidity despite treatment. Timing is critical for prognosis.
  • Adverse drug reactions: Hepatotoxicity (rifampicin, isoniazid, pyrazinamide), peripheral neuropathy (isoniazid), optic neuritis (ethambutol), and gastrointestinal upset are common, requiring careful monitoring and management.
