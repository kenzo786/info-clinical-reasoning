Polyhydramnios/Oligohydramnios (Pregnancy and the puerperium) [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Polyhydramnios/Oligohydramnios (Pregnancy and the puerperium)"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Polyhydramnios/Oligohydramnios (Pregnancy and the puerperium)"][/sc]
  • Polyhydramnios is an excessive volume of amniotic fluid (Amniotic Fluid Index [AFI] >24cm), often idiopathic but can be associated with gestational diabetes or fetal anomalies. Oligohydramnios is a reduced volume of amniotic fluid (AFI <5cm), often linked to IUGR, renal agenesis, or PPROM.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise and differentiate between polyhydramnios and oligohydramnios on ultrasound, identify potential underlying causes (maternal and fetal), and understand the implications for ongoing antenatal management and delivery planning.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Polyhydramnios/Oligohydramnios (Pregnancy and the puerperium)"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Polyhydramnios/Oligohydramnios (Pregnancy and the puerperium)"][/sc]
  • Polyhydramnios affects approximately 1-2% of pregnancies in the UK. Common risk factors include maternal diabetes (gestational or pre-existing), multiple pregnancies, and fetal anomalies affecting swallowing (e.g., oesophageal atresia).
  • Oligohydramnios is found in approximately 4-5% of pregnancies at term. It is strongly associated with fetal growth restriction, post-term gestation, preterm prelabour rupture of membranes (PPROM), and fetal renal anomalies.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Polyhydramnios/Oligohydramnios (Pregnancy and the puerperium)"][/sc]
  • Trigger: An imbalance in the steady state of amniotic fluid, which is regulated by a combination of fetal urination (production) and fetal swallowing (removal).
  • Progression: Polyhydramnios results from either increased production (e.g., fetal polyuria secondary to maternal hyperglycaemia) or decreased removal (e.g., impaired fetal swallowing from a gastrointestinal obstruction). Oligohydramnios results from decreased production (e.g., fetal renal impairment) or increased loss (e.g., ruptured membranes).
  • Consequence: Severe, prolonged oligohydramnios in utero can lead to pulmonary hypoplasia and limb contractures, a pattern known as [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Potter sequence: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Potter sequence"][/sc]. Polyhydramnios can cause uterine overdistension, increasing the risk of preterm labour and postpartum haemorrhage.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Polyhydramnios/Oligohydramnios (Pregnancy and the puerperium)"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Often asymptomatic and identified incidentally on routine second or third-trimester ultrasound scans.
  • Progressive phase: With polyhydramnios, the patient may develop gradual onset of breathlessness, abdominal discomfort, and uterine contractions. With oligohydramnios, the primary symptom may be reduced fetal movements.
  • Late phase: In the third trimester, polyhydramnios increases the risk of preterm labour, premature rupture of membranes, and unstable fetal lie. Oligohydramnios increases the risk of cord compression and fetal distress during labour.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Polyhydramnios/Oligohydramnios (Pregnancy and the puerperium)"][/sc]
  • Ask about breathlessness or orthopnoea - suggests significant uterine overdistension from polyhydramnios, compromising maternal respiratory function.
  • Ask about abdominal discomfort or tightness - may indicate rapid fluid accumulation in polyhydramnios or uterine irritability.
  • Ask about reduced fetal movements - a critical symptom in both conditions, but particularly concerning in oligohydramnios as it may signal fetal compromise.
  • Ask about any history of fluid leakage from the vagina - this is a key symptom of [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Preterm Prelabour Rupture of Membranes (PPROM): what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="PPROM"][/sc], a major cause of oligohydramnios.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Polyhydramnios/Oligohydramnios (Pregnancy and the puerperium)"][/sc]
  • Check maternal vital signs - while often normal, tachycardia or tachypnoea can occur with severe polyhydramnios.
  • Check symphysial fundal height (SFH) - a key sign; SFH will be larger than expected for gestational age in polyhydramnios and smaller in oligohydramnios.
  • Check the abdomen on palpation - in polyhydramnios, the uterus may feel tense and it can be difficult to palpate fetal parts or auscultate the fetal heart. In oligohydramnios, fetal parts may be easily felt.
  • Check for a [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Fluid thrill: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="fluid thrill"][/sc] - a clinical sign that may be elicited in severe polyhydramnios.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Polyhydramnios/Oligohydramnios (Pregnancy and the puerperium)"][/sc]
  • Maternal respiratory distress - indicates severe polyhydramnios requiring urgent intervention to relieve pressure.
  • Absent or significantly reduced fetal movements - a sign of potential fetal compromise in either condition, warranting immediate fetal wellbeing assessment.
  • Signs of preterm labour (e.g., regular painful contractions) - a common complication of polyhydramnios due to uterine stretch.
  • Suspicion of ruptured membranes - requires confirmation and management to reduce the risk of infection and manage oligohydramnios.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Polyhydramnios/Oligohydramnios (Pregnancy and the puerperium)"][/sc]
  • Multiple pregnancy: One twin may have polyhydramnios while the other has oligohydramnios, a hallmark of [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Twin-to-Twin Transfusion Syndrome (TTTS): what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Twin-to-Twin Transfusion Syndrome (TTTS)"][/sc].
  • Maternal diabetes: Polyhydramnios can develop rapidly in cases of poor glycaemic control, presenting with acute abdominal pain and breathlessness.
  • Post-term pregnancy (>41 weeks): Oligohydramnios can develop physiologically due to placental ageing and reduced renal perfusion, increasing risks during induction of labour.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Polyhydramnios/Oligohydramnios (Pregnancy and the puerperium)"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected Polyhydramnios/Oligohydramnios: An oral glucose tolerance test (OGTT) is essential to screen for gestational diabetes in cases of polyhydramnios. A Kleihauer test may be considered if feto-maternal haemorrhage is suspected.
  • Imaging - Ultrasound: This is the definitive diagnostic tool. It is used to measure the Amniotic Fluid Index (AFI) or single deepest vertical pool, assess fetal growth and anatomy (looking for causes like renal agenesis or duodenal atresia), and evaluate fetal wellbeing with Doppler studies.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: In cases of suspected PPROM causing oligohydramnios, a speculum examination to look for pooling of amniotic fluid is required. Amnisure or Actim PROM tests may also be used.
  • Serial reassessment: Regular ultrasound scans are used to monitor the trend of amniotic fluid volume and fetal growth, which informs the timing of delivery.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Polyhydramnios/Oligohydramnios (Pregnancy and the puerperium)"][/sc]
  • A 32-year-old woman, G2P1 at 34 weeks gestation, presents with increasing breathlessness and a fundal height of 38cm. An ultrasound confirms polyhydramnios (AFI 28cm) and a normally grown fetus.
  • A pregnant patient in the third trimester with a symphysial fundal height that is static or small for dates, associated with reduced fetal movements, is concerning for oligohydramnios, likely secondary to fetal growth restriction or placental insufficiency.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Polyhydramnios/Oligohydramnios (Pregnancy and the puerperium)"][/sc]
  • Macrosomia - A large-for-gestational-age fetus can cause a large SFH, mimicking polyhydramnios, but ultrasound will show a normal AFI.
  • Multiple pregnancy - Can present with a large SFH, but ultrasound will clearly identify multiple fetuses.
  • Intrauterine Growth Restriction (IUGR) - A common cause of a small SFH and is a primary differential for, and cause of, oligohydramnios.
  • Wrong dates - An incorrect estimated date of delivery can lead to a discrepancy between the expected and actual uterine size.
  • Maternal obesity - Can make clinical assessment of fundal height and fetal palpation difficult, potentially masking or mimicking fluid abnormalities.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Polyhydramnios/Oligohydramnios (Pregnancy and the puerperium)"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Focus on assessing maternal and fetal wellbeing. This includes a cardiotocograph (CTG) if there are concerns about fetal movements and urgent ultrasound assessment.
  • Definitive management: Management is directed at the underlying cause if one is identified. For severe, symptomatic polyhydramnios, an amnioreduction may be considered. For oligohydramnios near term, induction of labour is often warranted.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Involves serial ultrasound scans to monitor fluid volume and fetal growth, and regular fetal wellbeing surveillance (e.g., CTG, fetal movement counting).
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Patients should be advised to report any reduced fetal movements, signs of labour, or rupture of membranes immediately. All cases require obstetric-led care.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Polyhydramnios/Oligohydramnios (Pregnancy and the puerperium)"][/sc]
  • Preterm labour: A significant risk with polyhydramnios due to uterine over-distension and myometrial stretch.
  • Cord prolapse: More likely to occur at rupture of membranes if the presenting part is not engaged, a situation common with polyhydramnios.
  • Fetal malpresentation (e.g., breech, transverse): The excess fluid in polyhydramnios allows the fetus to move freely, leading to an unstable lie.
  • Pulmonary hypoplasia: A devastating consequence of severe, early-onset oligohydramnios, as amniotic fluid is essential for lung development.
  • Postpartum haemorrhage (PPH): The overstretched uterus in polyhydramnios may fail to contract effectively after delivery, leading to atonic PPH.
