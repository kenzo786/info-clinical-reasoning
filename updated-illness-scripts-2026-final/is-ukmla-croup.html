Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Croup (Child health)"][/sc]
  • Croup, also known as laryngotracheobronchitis, is a common acute respiratory infection in young children, characterised by inflammation of the larynx, trachea, and bronchi, typically caused by viral infection.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical features of croup, assess its severity, and understand the principles of initial management, including when to escalate care.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Croup (Child health)"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Croup (Child health)"][/sc]
  • Croup is most common in children aged 6 months to 3 years, with a peak incidence in the second year of life. It is less common in infants under 3 months and rare after 6 years.
  • It accounts for approximately 15% of all respiratory tract infections in children and is more prevalent in autumn and winter months.
  • Males are slightly more affected than females.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Croup (Child health)"][/sc]
  • Viral infection, most commonly parainfluenza virus types 1, 2, and 3, leads to inflammation and oedema of the subglottic region of the larynx and trachea.
  • This inflammation narrows the airway, particularly at the cricoid cartilage, leading to increased airway resistance and turbulent airflow.
  • The narrowed airway and turbulent airflow manifest clinically as a characteristic barking cough, inspiratory stridor, and hoarseness. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Steeple sign: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Steeple sign"][/sc]
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Croup (Child health)"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Symptoms often begin with a prodrome of upper respiratory tract infection (e.g., runny nose, mild cough, low-grade fever) lasting 12-48 hours.
  • Progressive phase: The characteristic barking cough, hoarseness, and inspiratory stridor typically develop suddenly, often worsening at night, and usually resolve within 48 hours, though a mild cough may persist for up to a week.
  • Late phase: Resolution of acute symptoms generally occurs within 3-7 days, with stridor and respiratory distress subsiding first, followed by improvement in cough.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Croup (Child health)"][/sc]
  • Ask about barking cough - often described as seal-like, it is the hallmark symptom and indicates subglottic oedema.
  • Ask about inspiratory stridor - a high-pitched sound on inspiration, signifying significant upper airway obstruction.
  • Ask about hoarse voice - due to inflammation affecting the vocal cords.
  • Ask about preceding cold-like symptoms - a viral prodrome is common and helps differentiate from other causes of stridor.
  • Ask about worsening at night - symptoms are typically more severe at night, possibly due to diurnal variation in cortisol levels or increased vagal tone.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Croup (Child health)"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - assess for fever, tachypnoea, tachycardia, and oxygen saturation to gauge severity and identify respiratory distress.
  • Check for inspiratory stridor at rest - indicates moderate to severe airway obstruction.
  • Check for chest wall recession (e.g., subcostal, intercostal, suprasternal) - a sign of increased work of breathing.
  • Check for hoarseness - confirms laryngeal involvement.
  • Check for level of consciousness and hydration status - indicators of overall clinical well-being and potential for deterioration. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Westley Croup Score: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Westley Croup Score"][/sc]
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Croup (Child health)"][/sc]
  • Increasing respiratory distress: evidenced by severe tachypnoea, significant chest wall recession, or grunting, indicating worsening airway obstruction.
  • Cyanosis or pallor: suggestive of hypoxaemia and impending respiratory failure.
  • Altered consciousness: lethargy, drowsiness, or agitation can indicate hypoxaemia or hypercapnia.
  • Drooling or inability to swallow secretions: raises suspicion of epiglottitis or bacterial tracheitis, which are more severe conditions.
  • Lack of improvement or worsening after initial treatment: necessitates urgent re-evaluation and consideration of alternative diagnoses or escalation of care.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Croup (Child health)"][/sc]
  • Infants under 6 months: may present with less typical barking cough and more subtle signs of respiratory distress, making diagnosis challenging.
  • Recurrent croup: suggests underlying airway abnormalities or gastro-oesophageal reflux, requiring further investigation.
  • Spasmodic croup: sudden onset of barking cough and stridor, often at night, without a preceding viral prodrome or fever, thought to be allergic in origin.
  • Bacterial tracheitis: a severe bacterial infection often superimposed on viral croup, presenting with high fever, toxicity, and poor response to standard croup treatment. Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Croup (Child health)"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected Croup: Clinical diagnosis based on characteristic symptoms (barking cough, stridor, hoarseness) and signs (respiratory distress). No routine laboratory or imaging tests are usually required.
  • Severity/risk tools: Westley Croup Score is used to assess severity and guide management. Westley Croup Score on MDCalc
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Consider a lateral neck X-ray to rule out epiglottitis (thumb sign) or foreign body, or an AP neck X-ray for the "steeple sign" in severe or atypical cases. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Epiglottitis vs Croup: what are the key differentiating features?" text="Epiglottitis vs Croup"][/sc]
  • Imaging - Plain radiograph (neck): A frontal neck X-ray may show subglottic narrowing, known as the "steeple sign," but this is not routinely recommended for diagnosis.
  • Serial reassessment: Regular clinical assessment, particularly of respiratory effort and oxygen saturation, is crucial to monitor progression and response to treatment.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Croup (Child health)"][/sc]
  • A 2-year-old child presents with a 2-day history of cold-like symptoms, followed by sudden onset of a barking cough, inspiratory stridor, and hoarse voice, worse at night, with mild subcostal recession.
  • Viral upper airway inflammation with subglottic oedema, concerning for croup.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Croup (Child health)"][/sc]
  • Epiglottitis: Rapid onset, high fever, toxic appearance, drooling, dysphagia, muffled voice, absence of barking cough.
  • Bacterial tracheitis: High fever, toxic appearance, purulent secretions, poor response to croup treatment, often follows viral prodrome.
  • Foreign body aspiration: Sudden onset of choking, coughing, or stridor, often without preceding viral symptoms; may have unilateral wheeze.
  • Peritonsillar abscess/Retropharyngeal abscess: Severe sore throat, dysphagia, muffled voice, trismus, neck stiffness, usually older children.
  • Anaphylaxis/Angioedema: Rapid onset of airway swelling, urticaria, angioedema, often with exposure to allergen.
  • Laryngomalacia: Chronic inspiratory stridor, typically worse when feeding or crying, present from birth or early infancy. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Laryngomalacia: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Laryngomalacia"][/sc]
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Croup (Child health)"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Minimise distress, maintain a calm environment, assess severity using Westley Croup Score, and ensure airway patency. Consider early involvement of senior staff for moderate to severe cases.
  • Definitive management: Oral dexamethasone (0.15 mg/kg single dose) is often recommended for all severities of croup to reduce airway oedema.
  • Adjunctive management: Nebulised adrenaline (epinephrine) may support temporary relief of severe airway obstruction, particularly in moderate to severe croup, with close monitoring for rebound symptoms.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Children with moderate to severe croup require observation for several hours post-treatment to ensure stability and rule out rebound. Safety-netting advice for parents is crucial.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Advise parents on red flag symptoms requiring immediate medical attention (e.g., increasing respiratory distress, cyanosis, altered consciousness).

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Croup (Child health)"][/sc]
  • Respiratory failure: Progressive airway obstruction can lead to hypoxaemia and hypercapnia, necessitating ventilatory support.
  • Dehydration: Due to increased work of breathing and reduced oral intake, particularly in younger children.
  • Secondary bacterial infection: Such as bacterial tracheitis or pneumonia, which can complicate the viral illness and lead to a more severe course.
  • Recurrent croup: Some children experience repeated episodes, which may indicate underlying airway issues or predispositions.
  • Anxiety and parental distress: The sudden onset and alarming symptoms of croup can cause significant anxiety for both the child and parents, impacting management and adherence.
