Corneal ulcer/ abrasion (Eye) [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Corneal ulcer/ abrasion (Eye)"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Corneal ulcer/ abrasion (Eye)"][/sc]
  • A corneal abrasion is a superficial defect of the corneal epithelium, whereas a corneal ulcer is a deeper open sore involving the underlying stroma, often due to infection.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: To be able to assess a patient with a painful red eye, correctly use fluorescein to distinguish between a simple abrasion and a sight-threatening corneal ulcer, and know when to refer urgently to ophthalmology.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Corneal ulcer/ abrasion (Eye)"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Corneal ulcer/ abrasion (Eye)"][/sc]
  • Corneal abrasions are very common, accounting for up to 10% of ocular emergencies in the UK. The incidence of microbial keratitis (corneal ulcer) is lower, estimated at 2-4 per 10,000 per year, but is significantly higher in contact lens wearers.
  • Major risk factors for abrasion include trauma (including foreign bodies) and contact lens wear. For ulcers, the most significant risk factor is contact lens wear (especially overnight use), followed by ocular surface disease, trauma, and previous ocular surgery.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Corneal ulcer/ abrasion (Eye)"][/sc]
  • Trigger: A mechanical force (e.g., fingernail, foreign body, contact lens) disrupts the integrity of the protective corneal epithelium, creating an epithelial defect.
  • Progression: In an abrasion, the defect is confined to the epithelium, which typically heals by cell migration and mitosis. In an ulcer, pathogens (e.g., Pseudomonas aeruginosa in contact lens wearers, or HSV) invade the underlying stroma, triggering an intense inflammatory response with stromal necrosis and suppuration.
  • Consequence: Simple abrasions usually heal without scarring. Ulceration, however, involves stromal tissue destruction, which heals by fibrosis, often leading to a permanent corneal opacity, neovascularisation, and potential vision loss. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Corneal scarring: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Corneal scarring"][/sc] may result.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Corneal ulcer/ abrasion (Eye)"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Acute onset of severe pain, foreign body sensation, and watering immediately following an injury (abrasion) or over several hours (ulcer).
  • Progressive phase: Over 12-24 hours, pain may worsen, and photophobia and blurred vision develop. In ulcers, a visible white spot (infiltrate) may appear on the cornea, and discharge can become purulent.
  • Late phase: Simple abrasions typically heal within 24-72 hours. Untreated ulcers, particularly beyond 48 hours, can rapidly enlarge, leading to corneal perforation, endophthalmitis, and permanent sight loss.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Corneal ulcer/ abrasion (Eye)"][/sc]
  • Ask about severe eye pain and foreign body sensation - these are the cardinal symptoms due to the rich sensory innervation of the cornea.
  • Ask about photophobia (light sensitivity) - indicates significant intraocular inflammation (anterior uveitis) often associated with ulcers.
  • Ask about blurred vision - suggests the abrasion or ulcer involves the central visual axis or there is significant corneal oedema or inflammation.
  • Ask about eye discharge - a watery discharge is typical of an abrasion, while a purulent (pus-like) discharge is highly suggestive of a bacterial ulcer.
  • Ask about contact lens wear - this is the single most important risk factor for microbial keratitis and should dramatically increase suspicion of an ulcer.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Corneal ulcer/ abrasion (Eye)"][/sc]
  • Check visual acuity - a reduction in acuity is a red flag and indicates a more severe process affecting the visual axis.
  • Check for conjunctival injection - typically a diffuse redness is present. A localised, intense perilimbal injection (ciliary flush) suggests a more severe inflammatory process like an ulcer.
  • Check the cornea with a pen torch and then fluorescein stain - an abrasion appears as a well-defined epithelial defect that stains bright green, with no underlying opacity. An ulcer typically shows an epithelial defect overlying a stromal infiltrate ([sc name="jitl-query" Q="Corneal infiltrate: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="corneal infiltrate"][/sc]), which appears as an opaque, whitish lesion.
  • Check for a hypopyon - the presence of a layer of white cells in the anterior chamber is a sign of severe intraocular inflammation and is a hallmark of an advanced infectious ulcer.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Corneal ulcer/ abrasion (Eye)"][/sc]
  • White spot on the cornea - highly suggestive of a stromal infiltrate, indicating a corneal ulcer requiring urgent specialist management.
  • Reduced visual acuity - implies the visual axis is compromised or there is significant inflammation, warranting urgent review.
  • Contact lens wearer with a painful red eye - must be treated as a microbial ulcer until proven otherwise; requires immediate referral.
  • Hypopyon - indicates severe intraocular inflammation and likely advanced infection, a true ophthalmic emergency.
  • Failure of an abrasion to heal within 72 hours - suggests a misdiagnosis, a persistent foreign body, or a secondary infection developing.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Corneal ulcer/ abrasion (Eye)"][/sc]
  • Contact lens wearers: May present with a more indolent course initially, but are at high risk of aggressive Pseudomonas or [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Acanthamoeba keratitis: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Acanthamoeba keratitis"][/sc], the latter causing pain out of proportion to clinical signs.
  • Herpes Simplex Virus (HSV) keratitis: Can present as a dendritic (branching) ulcer on fluorescein staining, often with reduced corneal sensation. It is a common cause of unilateral infectious corneal blindness.
  • Immunosuppressed or diabetic patients: May have a reduced inflammatory response, masking the severity of infection, and are more susceptible to fungal or atypical microbial keratitis.
  • Patients with neurotrophic corneas: (e.g., post-herpes zoster, stroke, diabetes) Have reduced corneal sensation and may present with significant ulceration but minimal pain.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Corneal ulcer/ abrasion (Eye)"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected corneal epithelial defect: Instillation of fluorescein 2% dye and examination with a cobalt blue light source is the key diagnostic step to reveal epithelial defects.
  • If an ulcer is suspected: Corneal scraping for microscopy, culture, and sensitivities (MC&S) is performed in secondary care to identify the causative organism and guide antimicrobial therapy. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Corneal scraping: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Corneal scraping"][/sc] is a specialist procedure.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Measurement of intraocular pressure may be performed if acute angle-closure glaucoma is a possibility (though may be deferred if globe rupture is suspected).
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Corneal ulcer/ abrasion (Eye)"][/sc]
  • Acute angle-closure glaucoma - presents with a painful red eye and blurred vision, but pain is typically deep and boring, associated with headache and nausea, and the cornea is hazy with a fixed, mid-dilated pupil.
  • Anterior uveitis/iritis - causes photophobia and a painful red eye, but the pupil may be small and irregular, and there is no epithelial defect on fluorescein staining.
  • Scleritis - causes severe, deep, boring pain that may wake the patient at night, with a characteristic violaceous hue of the sclera and potential globe tenderness.
  • Conjunctivitis (bacterial or viral) - causes a red, gritty eye, but pain and photophobia are typically mild, and vision is usually unaffected. There is no corneal staining.
  • Subtarsal foreign body - can cause linear, vertical scratches on the cornea (abrasions). Eversion of the upper eyelid is required to identify and remove the foreign body.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Corneal ulcer/ abrasion (Eye)"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: For any suspected corneal ulcer, the immediate priority is urgent (same-day) referral to an ophthalmologist. For simple abrasions, initial management focuses on pain relief and preventing secondary infection.
  • Definitive management: Simple, non-contact lens related abrasions are often managed with prophylactic topical antibiotics. Corneal ulcers require intensive, fortified topical antibiotics (often hourly) guided by corneal scrape results, managed by an ophthalmologist.
  • Adjunctive management: Cycloplegic drops (e.g., cyclopentolate) may be used to relieve the pain from ciliary spasm associated with both large abrasions and ulcers. Oral analgesia is also appropriate. Eye patching is generally not recommended for abrasions as it may delay healing and is contraindicated in contact lens wearers.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Patients with simple abrasions should be advised to return if pain worsens, vision deteriorates, or the eye is not comfortable within 48-72 hours. Any patient with a feature suggestive of an ulcer must be referred immediately.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Corneal ulcer/ abrasion (Eye)"][/sc]
  • Corneal scarring: The most common complication of a corneal ulcer, resulting from stromal healing by fibrosis. If the scar is on the visual axis, it can cause permanent vision impairment.
  • Corneal perforation: A full-thickness breach of the cornea due to progressive tissue necrosis from an aggressive, untreated ulcer. This is a globe-threatening emergency.
  • Endophthalmitis: Infection spreading from the cornea into the internal structures of the eye, which can lead to complete loss of vision and the eye itself.
  • Recurrent corneal erosion syndrome: A complication of traumatic abrasions where the epithelium fails to adhere properly to the underlying basement membrane, leading to spontaneous, recurrent, painful epithelial defects.
