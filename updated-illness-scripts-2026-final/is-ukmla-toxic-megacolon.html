Toxic megacolon [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Toxic megacolon"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Toxic megacolon"][/sc]
  • A life-threatening complication characterised by acute, non-obstructive colonic dilatation with systemic toxicity.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the signs and symptoms of toxic megacolon, understand its underlying causes, and initiate appropriate emergency management.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Toxic megacolon"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Toxic megacolon"][/sc]
  • Toxic megacolon is a rare but severe complication, most commonly associated with inflammatory bowel disease (IBD), particularly ulcerative colitis, affecting approximately 1-5% of patients with severe colitis.
  • Other causes include infectious colitis (e.g., Clostridioides difficile infection), ischaemic colitis, and obstructive conditions, with varying incidence rates across different patient populations in the UK.
  • Risk factors include severe active colitis, electrolyte imbalances (hypokalaemia), opioid use, anticholinergic medications, and recent colonoscopy with air insufflation.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Toxic megacolon"][/sc]
  • Trigger: Severe inflammation of the colon, often due to ulcerative colitis or C. difficile infection, leads to deep mucosal damage and release of inflammatory mediators.
  • Progression: Inflammatory mediators and bacterial toxins cause nitric oxide release, inhibiting smooth muscle tone and leading to colonic paralysis and rapid dilatation.
  • Consequence: Progressive colonic distension impairs blood flow, increasing the risk of ischaemia, perforation, and systemic sepsis.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Toxic megacolon"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Rapid onset of symptoms, typically over hours to a few days, following an exacerbation of underlying colitis.
  • Progressive phase: Worsening abdominal distension and systemic toxicity, particularly beyond 24-48 hours, indicating ongoing colonic dilatation and inflammation.
  • Late phase: Development of complications such as perforation or sepsis, particularly beyond 72 hours if not managed aggressively.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Toxic megacolon"][/sc]
  • Ask about abdominal pain - often severe and diffuse, indicating widespread colonic inflammation and distension.
  • Ask about abdominal distension - a key feature, reflecting acute colonic dilatation.
  • Ask about bloody diarrhoea - common in underlying colitis, but may decrease or cease with colonic paralysis.
  • Ask about fever and chills - signs of systemic inflammation and potential sepsis.
  • Ask about nausea and vomiting - non-specific but can indicate severe gastrointestinal dysfunction.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Toxic megacolon"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - tachycardia, hypotension, and fever are common indicators of systemic toxicity.
  • Check abdominal examination - marked abdominal distension, tenderness, and reduced or absent bowel sounds are characteristic.
  • Check signs of peritonism - rebound tenderness or guarding suggest impending or actual perforation.
  • Check mental status - altered consciousness may indicate severe sepsis or metabolic derangement.
  • Check hydration status - signs of dehydration due to fluid shifts and reduced oral intake.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Toxic megacolon"][/sc]
  • Rapidly worsening abdominal distension: Suggests acute colonic dilatation and impending perforation.
  • Signs of peritonism: Indicates colonic perforation or severe transmural inflammation.
  • Haemodynamic instability: Tachycardia, hypotension, and signs of shock point to severe sepsis.
  • Altered mental status: A critical sign of severe systemic toxicity and organ dysfunction.
  • Persistent fever despite antibiotics: May indicate ongoing infection or severe inflammatory process.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Toxic megacolon"][/sc]
  • Older adults: May present with less pronounced abdominal pain or fever, making diagnosis challenging; often have higher mortality.
  • Immunosuppressed patients: Inflammatory response may be blunted, leading to delayed recognition of systemic toxicity and less obvious signs of peritonism.
  • Patients on high-dose corticosteroids: Steroids can mask signs of inflammation and peritonitis, delaying diagnosis and increasing risk of perforation.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Toxic megacolon"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected Toxic megacolon: Full blood count (leukocytosis, anaemia), electrolytes (hypokalaemia), C-reactive protein (elevated), liver function tests, renal function tests, blood cultures.
  • Imaging - Abdominal X-ray: Crucial for diagnosis, showing colonic dilatation (transverse colon diameter >6 cm), loss of haustral markings, and potentially intramural gas or free air under the diaphragm.
  • Imaging - CT abdomen and pelvis: Provides more detailed assessment of colonic wall thickening, pericolic fluid, and can identify perforation or abscesses.
  • Stool studies: To rule out infectious causes, particularly Clostridioides difficile toxin.
  • Serial reassessment: Regular clinical examination, vital signs, and serial abdominal X-rays are essential to monitor progression and response to treatment.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Toxic megacolon"][/sc]
  • A 45-year-old male with a history of ulcerative colitis presents with acute onset severe abdominal pain, distension, bloody diarrhoea, fever, and tachycardia, with an abdominal X-ray showing significant colonic dilatation.
  • A systemically unwell, febrile, and tachycardic patient with acute, severe abdominal distension and evidence of colonic dilatation on imaging, in the context of underlying inflammatory bowel disease.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Toxic megacolon"][/sc]
  • Severe ulcerative colitis exacerbation without megacolon - absence of significant colonic dilatation and systemic toxicity.
  • Clostridioides difficile colitis - often presents with severe diarrhoea and systemic symptoms, but megacolon is a specific complication.
  • Ischaemic colitis - can cause abdominal pain and bloody diarrhoea, but typically without the profound colonic dilatation.
  • Large bowel obstruction - mechanical obstruction is usually evident on imaging, unlike the functional dilatation of toxic megacolon.
  • Perforated viscus - presents with acute abdomen and peritonism, but initial imaging may differentiate from primary megacolon.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Toxic megacolon"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Resuscitation with intravenous fluids, broad-spectrum antibiotics, bowel rest (nil by mouth), nasogastric tube decompression, and correction of electrolyte imbalances.
  • Definitive management: High-dose intravenous corticosteroids for IBD-related cases; intravenous metronidazole and vancomycin for C. difficile-related cases.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Close monitoring in a high-dependency or intensive care setting, including serial abdominal examinations, vital signs, and daily abdominal X-rays.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Early surgical consultation is crucial, as subtotal colectomy with ileostomy is often required if medical management fails or perforation occurs.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Toxic megacolon"][/sc]
  • Colonic perforation: Occurs due to severe distension and ischaemia, leading to peritonitis and high mortality; often necessitates emergency surgery.
  • Sepsis and septic shock: Systemic inflammatory response to bacterial translocation from the damaged colon, leading to multi-organ dysfunction.
  • Massive gastrointestinal haemorrhage: Severe mucosal damage can result in significant bleeding, requiring transfusions and potentially surgical intervention.
  • Electrolyte imbalances: Profound fluid and electrolyte shifts can lead to cardiac arrhythmias and renal dysfunction.
  • Death: Despite aggressive management, toxic megacolon carries a significant mortality rate, particularly if complicated by perforation or delayed diagnosis.
