Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Schizophrenia"][/sc]
  • Schizophrenia is a severe, long-term mental health condition characterised by a range of psychological symptoms, including disruptions in thought processes, perceptions, emotional responsiveness, and social interactions [1] [2].
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the key features of psychosis and schizophrenia, understand the principles of initial assessment and management, and appreciate the long-term impact on individuals and their carers.

Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Schizophrenia"][/sc]
  • The **dopamine hypothesis** of schizophrenia suggests that an imbalance or overactivity of dopamine signalling, particularly in the mesolimbic pathway, contributes to the positive symptoms of the condition [5] [6].
  • More recent theories suggest a dysregulation of dopamine in both mesolimbic (excess) and prefrontal (deficit) brain regions, contributing to both positive and negative symptoms [5].

Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Schizophrenia"][/sc]
  • Prodromal phase: This phase precedes the first episode of psychosis and can last for weeks, months, or even years. It is characterized by subtle changes in thoughts, perceptions, and behaviour, such as social withdrawal, decline in functioning, and unusual beliefs [7].
  • Active phase: This phase is marked by the presence of prominent psychotic symptoms, including hallucinations, delusions, and disorganised speech and behaviour. This is often when the individual experiences their first episode of psychosis [7].
  • Residual phase: Following the active phase, symptoms may lessen in intensity but some residual symptoms, particularly negative symptoms (e.g., flat affect, alogia, avolition), may persist. Functioning may remain impaired [7].
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Schizophrenia"][/sc]
  • **Positive symptoms:** These are psychotic symptoms that represent an excess or distortion of normal functions. They include:
    • **Hallucinations:** Perceptions in the absence of an external stimulus (e.g., auditory, visual, tactile) [8].
    • **Delusions:** Fixed, false beliefs that are not amenable to change in light of conflicting evidence (e.g., persecutory, grandiose) [8].
    • **Disorganised thinking and speech:** Inferred from disorganised speech patterns (e.g., tangentiality, word salad) [8].
  • **Negative symptoms:** These involve a reduction or loss of normal functions. They include:
    • **Flat affect:** Reduced emotional expression.
    • **Alogia:** Reduced speech output.
    • **Avolition:** Decreased motivation for purposeful activities.
    • **Anhedonia:** Decreased ability to experience pleasure.
    • **Asociality:** Reduced social interaction.
  • [sc name="jitl-query" Q="positive vs negative symptoms schizophrenia" answer="Positive symptoms are additions to normal experience (e.g., hallucinations, delusions), while negative symptoms are reductions or absences of normal functions (e.g., flat affect, avolition)."][/sc]
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Schizophrenia"][/sc]
  • **Appearance and Behaviour:** May appear dishevelled, internally preoccupied, with reduced eye contact or an intense stare. Movements can be stiff, awkward, or include catatonic features [9].
  • **Speech:** Disorganised speech patterns (e.g., tangentiality, circumstantiality, word salad) and alogia (poverty of speech) are common [8, 9].
  • **Affect:** Often presents as flat, blunted, or inappropriate to the situation [9].
  • **Thought Process:** Evidence of thought disorder, including delusions (e.g., persecutory, grandiose, reference) and thought broadcasting/insertion/withdrawal [8, 9].
  • **Perception:** Hallucinations, most commonly auditory (e.g., hearing voices), but can also be visual, tactile, olfactory, or gustatory [8].

Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Schizophrenia"][/sc]
  • **Acute onset of psychotic symptoms:** A sudden and rapid development of hallucinations, delusions, or severe thought disorder warrants urgent assessment [10].
  • **Significant functional decline:** A rapid deterioration in daily functioning, self-care, or social interactions, particularly if associated with new psychotic symptoms [10].
  • **Risk to self or others:** Any indication of suicidal ideation, self-harm, or aggressive behaviour, especially if driven by psychotic experiences, requires immediate intervention [10].
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Schizophrenia"][/sc]
  • **First-episode psychosis (FEP):** While not strictly 'atypical', FEP can present with a wide range of symptoms and may not immediately fit the classic schizophrenia picture, requiring careful assessment to differentiate from other psychotic disorders [11].
  • **Catatonia:** Can occur in schizophrenia, characterised by marked psychomotor disturbances, including stupor, catalepsy, waxy flexibility, mutism, and negativism. It can also be seen in other psychiatric or medical conditions [12].
  • **Co-morbid substance use:** Schizophrenia frequently co-occurs with substance use disorders, which can complicate presentation, diagnosis, and management, often mimicking or exacerbating psychotic symptoms [13].
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Schizophrenia"][/sc]
  • Diagnosis is primarily clinical, based on a comprehensive psychiatric assessment, including a detailed history and mental state examination, to identify characteristic symptoms and their duration [14].
  • Physical examination and investigations (e.g., blood tests, neuroimaging like MRI/CT) are crucial to rule out other medical or neurological conditions that can mimic psychotic symptoms [15].
  • Severity of symptoms can be assessed using structured rating scales such as the Positive and Negative Syndrome Scale (PANSS).

Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Schizophrenia"][/sc]
  • A young adult presenting with a history of progressive functional decline, social withdrawal, and the emergence of positive symptoms (e.g., persistent auditory hallucinations, persecutory delusions, disorganised thought and speech) over several months.
  • Negative symptoms (e.g., flat affect, avolition) may also be prominent, significantly impacting daily life and requiring comprehensive psychiatric assessment to confirm diagnosis and initiate multidisciplinary management.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Schizophrenia"][/sc]
  • **Schizoaffective Disorder:** Characterised by a combination of schizophrenia symptoms and mood disorder symptoms (e.g., depression or mania), where psychotic symptoms are present for at least two weeks in the absence of a major mood episode [16].
  • **Bipolar Disorder with Psychotic Features:** Psychotic symptoms occur exclusively during episodes of mania or severe depression. The key differentiator from schizoaffective disorder is the temporal relationship between mood and psychotic symptoms [16].
  • **Major Depressive Disorder with Psychotic Features:** Psychotic symptoms are typically mood-congruent and occur only during severe depressive episodes [16].
  • **Substance-Induced Psychotic Disorder:** Psychotic symptoms are directly attributable to the physiological effects of a substance (e.g., cannabis, stimulants) or withdrawal from a substance. A thorough history and toxicology screen are essential [16].
  • **Medical Conditions:** Various medical conditions (e.g., neurological disorders, autoimmune diseases, endocrine disorders, infections) can present with psychotic symptoms. A comprehensive medical workup is necessary to rule these out [16].

Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Schizophrenia"][/sc]
  • **Pharmacological management:** Antipsychotic medications are the cornerstone of treatment. First-line agents are typically second-generation antipsychotics.
  • **Treatment-resistant schizophrenia:** For individuals whose symptoms do not respond adequately to at least two different antipsychotics, **clozapine** is the recommended treatment of choice [17].
  • **Clozapine monitoring:** Due to potential serious side effects, particularly agranulocytosis, clozapine requires mandatory regular blood monitoring (e.g., full blood count, absolute neutrophil count) as per national guidelines (e.g., NICE) [18].
  • **Psychological interventions:** Cognitive behavioural therapy (CBT) for psychosis, family interventions, and arts therapies are important adjuncts to pharmacological treatment [17].
  • **Social and vocational support:** Support for employment, education, and housing is crucial for recovery and improving quality of life [17].

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Schizophrenia"][/sc]
  • **Increased risk of suicide:** Individuals with schizophrenia have a significantly higher risk of suicide, particularly during acute psychotic episodes or periods of remission [19].
  • **Co-morbid mental health conditions:** High rates of co-occurring conditions such as depression, anxiety disorders (including OCD), and substance use disorders are common, complicating treatment and prognosis [20].
  • **Physical health problems:** Higher rates of cardiovascular disease, diabetes, and metabolic syndrome, often due to lifestyle factors, side effects of antipsychotic medications, and reduced access to healthcare [21].
  • **Social and functional impairment:** Significant difficulties in maintaining employment, education, and social relationships, leading to social isolation and reduced quality of life [22].
