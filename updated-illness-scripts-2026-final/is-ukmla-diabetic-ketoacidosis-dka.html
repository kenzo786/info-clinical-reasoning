Diabetic ketoacidosis (DKA) [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Diabetic ketoacidosis (DKA)"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Diabetic ketoacidosis (DKA)"][/sc]
  • Diabetic ketoacidosis (DKA) is a life-threatening acute metabolic complication of diabetes mellitus, characterised by a triad of hyperglycaemia, ketosis, and metabolic acidosis, resulting from severe insulin deficiency.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical features and biochemical derangements of DKA, initiate appropriate emergency management, and identify potential complications including cerebral oedema.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Diabetic ketoacidosis (DKA)"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Diabetic ketoacidosis (DKA)"][/sc]
  • Incidence rates of DKA admissions in the UK for type 1 diabetes were 1607 per 100,000 people with diabetes during the first wave of the COVID-19 pandemic [1].
  • The prevalence of DKA in a 2-year study in Leicestershire, England, was calculated as 13.7 per 1000 people [2].
  • A UK nationwide study identified 85 confirmed DKA cases among 4073 hospitalised individuals [3].
  • DKA is the leading cause of mortality in individuals with type 1 diabetes under 50 years of age, though inpatient mortality rates are generally very low, typically less than 1% in the UK [4].
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Diabetic ketoacidosis (DKA)"][/sc]
  • Severe insulin deficiency, often triggered by infection or missed insulin doses, leads to uncontrolled glucose production and impaired glucose utilisation by cells.
  • The body compensates by breaking down fatty acids for energy, producing acidic ketone bodies (beta-hydroxybutyrate, acetoacetate, and acetone), which accumulate and cause a high [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Anion gap: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="anion gap"] metabolic acidosis.
  • The resulting hyperglycaemia causes osmotic diuresis, leading to significant fluid and electrolyte losses, further exacerbating dehydration and metabolic derangement.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Diabetic ketoacidosis (DKA)"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Symptoms typically develop rapidly over 12-24 hours, including polyuria, polydipsia, and fatigue, as hyperglycaemia and dehydration progress.
  • Progressive phase: Within 24-48 hours, ketosis and acidosis become prominent, leading to nausea, vomiting, abdominal pain, and Kussmaul respirations.
  • Late phase: If untreated, severe dehydration, electrolyte imbalances, and profound acidosis can lead to altered mental status, coma, and potentially death, particularly beyond 48 hours.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Diabetic ketoacidosis (DKA)"][/sc]
  • Ask about polyuria and polydipsia - these indicate significant osmotic diuresis and dehydration.
  • Ask about nausea, vomiting, and abdominal pain - these are common gastrointestinal manifestations of ketosis and acidosis.
  • Ask about a history of diabetes, recent illness, or missed insulin doses - these help identify potential triggers.
  • Ask about altered mental status or confusion - this suggests severe metabolic derangement and impending cerebral oedema, especially in children.
  • Ask about a "pear drop" or fruity smell on breath - this is a classic sign of acetone, a ketone body.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Diabetic ketoacidosis (DKA)"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - tachycardia and hypotension indicate hypovolaemia; Kussmaul respirations (deep, rapid breathing) suggest metabolic acidosis.
  • Check hydration status (skin turgor, mucous membranes, capillary refill time) - to assess the degree of dehydration.
  • Check mental status (GCS) - to evaluate for cerebral dysfunction or impending coma.
  • Check for signs of infection (fever, localised tenderness) - as infection is a common precipitant.
  • Check blood glucose and ketone levels - to confirm hyperglycaemia and ketosis, which are central to the diagnosis.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Diabetic ketoacidosis (DKA)"][/sc]
  • Altered mental status: Indicates severe metabolic derangement and requires urgent neurological assessment, particularly in children due to the risk of cerebral oedema.
  • Persistent vomiting: Suggests severe ketosis and acidosis, increasing the risk of dehydration and electrolyte imbalance, and hindering oral rehydration.
  • Severe abdominal pain: While common in DKA, it can also mask an underlying acute abdominal pathology that may have precipitated the DKA.
  • Hypotension or shock: Signifies profound dehydration and circulatory collapse, necessitating immediate and aggressive fluid resuscitation.
  • Rapidly worsening symptoms: A quick deterioration over hours indicates a severe and rapidly progressing DKA, requiring immediate escalation of care.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Diabetic ketoacidosis (DKA)"][/sc]
  • Euglycaemic DKA: Patients may present with normal or near-normal blood glucose levels, often due to SGLT2 inhibitor use, pregnancy, or prolonged fasting, making diagnosis challenging as hyperglycaemia is absent.
  • DKA in Type 2 Diabetes: While more common in Type 1, DKA can occur in Type 2 diabetes during severe physiological stress (e.g., infection, myocardial infarction), requiring careful differentiation from HHS.
  • Paediatric DKA: Children, especially those newly diagnosed, are at higher risk of DKA and its complications, particularly cerebral oedema, often presenting with subtle neurological changes.
  • DKA in pregnancy: Can occur at lower glucose levels due to physiological changes, posing risks to both mother and foetus, and requiring careful management to avoid complications.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Diabetic ketoacidosis (DKA)"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected DKA: Capillary blood glucose (>11 mmol/L), capillary ketones (>3.0 mmol/L), and venous blood gas (pH <7.3, bicarbonate <15 mmol/L, anion gap >10-12 mmol/L).
  • Severity/risk tools: The [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Cerebral oedema risk in DKA: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Paediatric DKA Risk Score"] can help assess the risk of cerebral oedema in children.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Consider full blood count (for infection), urea and electrolytes (renal function, electrolyte imbalances), C-reactive protein (inflammation), and cultures (blood, urine) to identify underlying triggers.
  • Serial reassessment: Regular monitoring of blood glucose, ketones, pH, bicarbonate, and electrolytes (especially potassium) is crucial to guide management and assess response to treatment.

  • A young individual with type 1 diabetes presents with a 24-hour history of polyuria, polydipsia, vomiting, and abdominal pain, accompanied by Kussmaul respirations and a fruity breath odour.
  • A young diabetic with acute onset gastrointestinal symptoms and altered breathing, concerning for metabolic acidosis secondary to severe insulin deficiency.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Diabetic ketoacidosis (DKA)"][/sc]
  • Hyperosmolar Hyperglycaemic State (HHS): Characterised by severe hyperglycaemia and dehydration without significant ketosis or acidosis, typically in older patients with Type 2 diabetes.
  • Alcoholic Ketoacidosis: Occurs in chronic alcohol users with poor nutritional intake, leading to ketosis and acidosis but usually with normal or low blood glucose.
  • Starvation Ketoacidosis: Develops during prolonged fasting, resulting in ketosis but typically with normal blood glucose and mild acidosis.
  • Lactic Acidosis: Caused by increased lactate production (e.g., sepsis, shock) or decreased lactate clearance, leading to a high anion gap metabolic acidosis without significant ketosis.
  • Salicylate Toxicity: Can cause a mixed acid-base disturbance, including a high anion gap metabolic acidosis, often with neurological symptoms.
  • Acute Abdomen: Conditions like appendicitis or pancreatitis can mimic DKA symptoms (abdominal pain, vomiting) but lack the characteristic biochemical triad.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Diabetic ketoacidosis (DKA)"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Prompt recognition, urgent escalation to senior medical staff, and initiation of supportive care including ABCDE assessment and fluid resuscitation according to local guidelines (e.g., JBDS).
  • Definitive management: Involves intravenous fluid replacement (0.9% sodium chloride), fixed-rate intravenous insulin infusion, and careful potassium replacement to correct dehydration, hyperglycaemia, and acidosis.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Meticulous hourly monitoring of blood glucose, ketones, pH, bicarbonate, and electrolytes is essential, alongside strict fluid balance charting.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Close observation for complications such as cerebral oedema, particularly in children, and prompt adjustment of treatment based on biochemical parameters.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Diabetic ketoacidosis (DKA)"][/sc]
  • Cerebral oedema: A rare but devastating complication, particularly in children, often occurring during treatment due to rapid fluid shifts or overly aggressive glucose lowering, leading to neurological deterioration.
  • Hypoglycaemia: Can occur if insulin is continued without adequate glucose infusion once blood glucose levels fall, necessitating careful monitoring and adjustment of dextrose-containing fluids.
  • Hypokalaemia: Insulin therapy drives potassium into cells, and ongoing renal losses can lead to severe hypokalaemia, increasing the risk of cardiac arrhythmias if not adequately replaced.
  • Acute kidney injury: Pre-renal AKI due to severe dehydration is common, and careful fluid management is crucial to restore renal perfusion and function.
  • Aspiration pneumonitis: Patients with altered mental status and vomiting are at increased risk of aspirating gastric contents, leading to lung injury.
  • Thromboembolism: DKA is a pro-thrombotic state due to dehydration and inflammation, increasing the risk of deep vein thrombosis and pulmonary embolism, particularly in severe cases.
