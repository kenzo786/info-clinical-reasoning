Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Pericardial disease (including pericarditis, pericardial effusion, cardiac tamponade)"][/sc]
  • Pericardial disease encompasses a spectrum of conditions affecting the pericardium, the double-layered sac surrounding the heart, including inflammation (pericarditis), fluid accumulation (pericardial effusion), and critical compression of the heart (cardiac tamponade).
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the various presentations and clinical significance of pericardial disease, including pericarditis, pericardial effusion, and cardiac tamponade.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Pericardial disease (including pericarditis, pericardial effusion, cardiac tamponade)"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Pericardial disease (including pericarditis, pericardial effusion, cardiac tamponade)"][/sc]
  • The yearly incidence of acute pericarditis in the general population is approximately 0.03%, with 15% to 30% of affected individuals experiencing recurrent episodes [1].
  • It occurs more frequently in young males and represents the most common form of inflammatory heart disease [1].
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Pericardial disease (including pericarditis, pericardial effusion, cardiac tamponade)"][/sc]
  • Trigger: Inflammation of the pericardium, often initiated by viral infections (e.g., coxsackieviruses, echoviruses) or autoimmune processes, leading to increased vascular permeability and fluid exudation into the pericardial space [1].
  • Progression: Accumulation of fluid (pericardial effusion) within the pericardial sac. The rate of accumulation is more critical than the volume; rapid accumulation can lead to increased intrapericardial pressure [1].
  • Consequence: If intrapericardial pressure rises sufficiently, it can restrict cardiac diastolic filling, impairing ventricular function and leading to reduced cardiac output and obstructive shock (cardiac tamponade) [1].
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Pericardial disease (including pericarditis, pericardial effusion, cardiac tamponade)"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Acute pericarditis typically lasts less than 4 to 6 weeks [1].
  • Progressive phase: Incessant pericarditis persists beyond 4 to 6 weeks but resolves within 3 months. Pericardial effusion can develop gradually over weeks, particularly beyond 6 weeks, before causing constrictive physiology [1].
  • Late phase: Chronic pericarditis extends beyond 3 months. Constrictive pericarditis may manifest months or even years after the initial insult [1].
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Pericardial disease (including pericarditis, pericardial effusion, cardiac tamponade)"][/sc]
  • Ask about chest pain - typically sharp, severe, retrosternal, pleuritic (worsens with deep inspiration), and positional (improves with sitting up and leaning forward) [1].
  • Ask about pain radiation - pain may radiate to the trapezius ridges if the phrenic nerve is inflamed [1].
  • Ask about dyspnoea - may indicate significant pericardial effusion or cardiac tamponade, or co-existing myocarditis [1].
  • Ask about flu-like prodrome - fever or upper respiratory symptoms often precede viral pericarditis [1].
  • Ask about systemic manifestations - polyarthritis, skin rashes, or kidney issues may suggest autoimmune or inflammatory etiologies [1].
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Pericardial disease (including pericarditis, pericardial effusion, cardiac tamponade)"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - tachycardia, hypotension, and narrow pulse pressure may indicate cardiac tamponade. Fever may be present in acute pericarditis [1].
  • Check for pericardial rub - a high-pitched, scratching sound heard best at the left sternal border with the patient leaning forward and holding breath in expiration; pathognomonic for pericarditis [1].
  • Check for muffled heart sounds - suggests pericardial effusion [1].
  • Check for elevated jugular venous pressure (JVP) - particularly with Kussmaul's sign (paradoxical rise in JVP with inspiration), suggesting constrictive pericarditis or cardiac tamponade [1].
  • Check for pulsus paradoxus - an inspiratory fall in systolic blood pressure >10 mmHg, highly suggestive of cardiac tamponade [1].
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Pericardial disease (including pericarditis, pericardial effusion, cardiac tamponade)"][/sc]
  • Cardiac Tamponade: Rapid accumulation of pericardial fluid leading to haemodynamic compromise, requiring urgent pericardiocentesis [1].
  • Large Pericardial Effusion: Significant fluid accumulation that may progress to tamponade, even if currently asymptomatic [1].
  • Myocarditis: Concomitant myocardial inflammation, indicated by elevated troponin, which increases the risk of heart failure and arrhythmias [1].
  • Recurrent Pericarditis: Multiple episodes of pericarditis, which can lead to chronic pain and potentially constrictive pericarditis [1].
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Pericardial disease (including pericarditis, pericardial effusion, cardiac tamponade)"][/sc]
  • Older adults: May present with less typical chest pain, or with dyspnoea and fatigue as predominant symptoms, making diagnosis challenging and often delaying appropriate management [1].
  • Immunosuppressed patients: Higher risk of infectious pericarditis (e.g., bacterial, fungal, tuberculous) and may have a more severe or rapidly progressive course, necessitating a broader diagnostic work-up [1].
  • Post-cardiac injury syndrome (Dressler's syndrome): Occurs days to weeks after myocardial infarction or cardiac surgery, presenting with fever, pleuritic pain, and pericardial effusion due to an autoimmune response [1].
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Pericardial disease (including pericarditis, pericardial effusion, cardiac tamponade)"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected pericarditis: Electrocardiogram (ECG) showing widespread ST elevation and PR depression; elevated inflammatory markers (C-reactive protein, erythrocyte sedimentation rate); troponin may be mildly elevated if there is associated myocarditis [1].
  • Core for suspected pericardial effusion/tamponade: Echocardiography to assess for pericardial fluid, cardiac chamber collapse, and respiratory variation in transvalvular flow [1].
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Consider D-dimer for pulmonary embolism, chest X-ray for pneumonia or pneumothorax, and cardiac enzymes for myocardial infarction [1].
  • Imaging - CT/MRI: Cardiac CT or MRI may be used to further characterise pericardial thickening, calcification, or masses, particularly in suspected constrictive pericarditis [1].
  • Serial reassessment: Regular clinical review and repeat echocardiography to monitor effusion size and haemodynamic status, especially in patients with moderate to large effusions [1].
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Pericardial disease (including pericarditis, pericardial effusion, cardiac tamponade)"][/sc]
  • A 35-year-old male presents with acute onset, severe, retrosternal chest pain that is worse on inspiration and lying flat, and relieved by sitting forward. He reports a recent viral illness. On examination, a triphasic pericardial friction rub is audible.
  • A young male with acute, positional, pleuritic chest pain following a viral prodrome, accompanied by a pericardial friction rub, suggesting inflammatory pericardial disease.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Pericardial disease (including pericarditis, pericardial effusion, cardiac tamponade)"][/sc]
  • Myocardial Infarction (MI) - typically presents with exertional chest pain, often radiating to the left arm or jaw, and is not relieved by sitting forward. ECG shows localised ST elevation, not widespread [1].
  • Pulmonary Embolism (PE) - sudden onset dyspnoea and pleuritic chest pain, often with risk factors for venous thromboembolism. ECG may show S1Q3T3 pattern or right heart strain [1].
  • Aortic Dissection - sudden onset, severe, tearing chest pain radiating to the back, often associated with blood pressure differential between arms [1].
  • Pneumonia - pleuritic chest pain with cough, sputum production, and fever. Chest X-ray shows focal infiltrates [1].
  • Gastro-oesophageal Reflux Disease (GORD) - burning retrosternal pain, often post-prandial or nocturnal, relieved by antacids, without positional changes [1].
  • Costochondritis - localised chest wall tenderness, reproducible on palpation, without systemic symptoms or ECG changes [1].
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Pericardial disease (including pericarditis, pericardial effusion, cardiac tamponade)"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Assess for signs of cardiac tamponade (e.g., hypotension, pulsus paradoxus, muffled heart sounds) and initiate urgent pericardiocentesis if present [1].
  • Definitive management: For acute pericarditis, management often includes non-steroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs) such as ibuprofen, and colchicine to reduce inflammation and prevent recurrence [1]. For large or symptomatic pericardial effusions, pericardiocentesis may be required [1].
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Regular clinical review and echocardiography to monitor for resolution of inflammation and effusion, and to detect complications such as constrictive pericarditis [1].
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Advise patients to seek immediate medical attention for worsening chest pain, dyspnoea, syncope, or signs of shock. Consider referral to cardiology for recurrent pericarditis or complex effusions [1].

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Pericardial disease (including pericarditis, pericardial effusion, cardiac tamponade)"][/sc]
  • Cardiac Tamponade: Occurs when rapid or significant pericardial fluid accumulation compresses the heart, impeding diastolic filling and leading to reduced cardiac output and shock. This is a life-threatening emergency requiring immediate drainage [1].
  • Constrictive Pericarditis: Results from chronic inflammation and fibrosis of the pericardium, leading to a rigid, non-compliant pericardial sac that restricts ventricular filling. This can develop insidiously over months to years and causes symptoms of right-sided heart failure [1].
  • Recurrent Pericarditis: Characterised by repeated episodes of pericarditis after an initial acute attack. This can be debilitating and significantly impact quality of life, often requiring long-term anti-inflammatory therapy [1].
  • Chronic Pericardial Effusion: Persistent fluid accumulation in the pericardial sac, which may be asymptomatic but carries a risk of progression to cardiac tamponade or constrictive pericarditis over time [1].
