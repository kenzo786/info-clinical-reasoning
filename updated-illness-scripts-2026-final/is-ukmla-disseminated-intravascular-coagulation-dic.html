Disseminated intravascular coagulation (DIC) [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Disseminated intravascular coagulation (DIC)"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Disseminated intravascular coagulation (DIC)"][/sc]
  • A complex, acquired disorder characterised by systemic activation of coagulation, leading to widespread fibrin formation in small and medium-sized vessels, which can result in microvascular thrombosis, organ dysfunction, and consumption of platelets and coagulation factors, ultimately causing severe bleeding.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical features, diagnostic criteria, and management principles of disseminated intravascular coagulation (DIC) in various clinical settings.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Disseminated intravascular coagulation (DIC)"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Disseminated intravascular coagulation (DIC)"][/sc]
  • DIC is not a primary disease but a complication of various underlying conditions, with sepsis and malignancy being the most common causes, accounting for a significant proportion of cases in the UK.
  • The incidence of DIC is highly variable, reflecting the prevalence of its underlying causes; for instance, it is estimated to occur in 1% of hospitalised patients, with higher rates in critical care settings.
  • Risk factors include severe infection (sepsis), trauma, obstetric complications (e.g., pre-eclampsia, amniotic fluid embolism), malignancy (especially acute promyelocytic leukaemia), and severe liver disease.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Disseminated intravascular coagulation (DIC)"][/sc]
  • Triggering event (e.g., sepsis, trauma) leads to widespread endothelial damage and exposure of tissue factor, initiating uncontrolled activation of the coagulation cascade.
  • Excessive thrombin generation overwhelms natural anticoagulant mechanisms, resulting in systemic microvascular thrombosis and consumption of platelets and clotting factors.
  • Depletion of coagulation factors and platelets, coupled with secondary fibrinolysis, leads to a paradoxical state of both thrombosis and severe haemorrhage, contributing to multi-organ dysfunction.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Disseminated intravascular coagulation (DIC)"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Rapid onset, often within hours of the precipitating event, characterised by initial hypercoagulability and microthrombi formation.
  • Progressive phase: Within days, consumption of clotting factors and platelets leads to a shift towards a hypocoagulable state, with increasing risk of bleeding, particularly beyond 2-3 days.
  • Late phase: If the underlying cause is not addressed, persistent activation of coagulation and fibrinolysis can lead to severe haemorrhage, organ failure, and a high mortality rate, particularly beyond 5-7 days.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Disseminated intravascular coagulation (DIC)"][/sc]
  • Ask about bleeding - such as petechiae, purpura, ecchymoses, epistaxis, gingival bleeding, or gastrointestinal bleeding, as these indicate consumption coagulopathy.
  • Ask about signs of thrombosis - including chest pain (myocardial ischaemia), shortness of breath (pulmonary embolism), or neurological deficits (stroke), which highlight microvascular occlusion.
  • Ask about general malaise and fatigue - often present due to the underlying severe illness and systemic inflammation.
  • Ask about oliguria or anuria - suggesting renal impairment secondary to microthrombi in the renal vasculature.
  • Ask about abdominal pain - which may indicate visceral ischaemia or haemorrhage.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Disseminated intravascular coagulation (DIC)"][/sc]
  • Check for petechiae, purpura, and ecchymoses - indicative of platelet consumption and microvascular bleeding.
  • Check for signs of organ dysfunction - such as jaundice (hepatic dysfunction), altered mental status (cerebral ischaemia/haemorrhage), or acute kidney injury (renal ischaemia).
  • Check for signs of thrombosis - including livedo reticularis, digital ischaemia, or superficial thrombophlebitis.
  • Check vital signs - typically revealing tachycardia, hypotension, and tachypnoea, reflecting systemic inflammatory response and hypovolaemia from bleeding.
  • Check for oozing from venepuncture sites or surgical wounds - a classic sign of impaired haemostasis.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Disseminated intravascular coagulation (DIC)"][/sc]
  • Widespread spontaneous bleeding: Suggests severe consumption coagulopathy and imminent risk of major haemorrhage.
  • Rapidly deteriorating organ function: Indicates progressive microvascular thrombosis and ischaemic damage.
  • New onset purpura fulminans: A severe and rapidly progressive thrombotic disorder often associated with overwhelming sepsis and DIC.
  • Unexplained shock in the context of infection or trauma: May signify profound systemic compromise and early DIC.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Disseminated intravascular coagulation (DIC)"][/sc]
  • Localised DIC: Can occur in conditions like aortic aneurysm or giant haemangioma, where coagulation is activated locally, leading to local thrombosis and systemic consumption.
  • Chronic DIC: Often associated with solid tumours or large aneurysms, presenting primarily with thrombotic manifestations rather than bleeding, due to compensatory mechanisms.
  • Early compensated DIC: Patients may have abnormal laboratory parameters (e.g., elevated D-dimer) without overt clinical signs of bleeding or thrombosis, making diagnosis challenging.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Disseminated intravascular coagulation (DIC)"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected Disseminated intravascular coagulation (DIC): Full blood count (thrombocytopenia), coagulation screen (prolonged PT/APTT, reduced fibrinogen), D-dimer (markedly elevated).
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Liver function tests, renal function tests, blood cultures (for sepsis), arterial blood gas (acidosis, hypoxia).
  • Imaging - Chest X-ray/CT scan: To identify underlying causes like pneumonia, malignancy, or pulmonary embolism.
  • Serial reassessment: Repeat coagulation studies and D-dimer every 6-12 hours to monitor progression and response to treatment. Consider ISTH DIC Score on MDCalc for diagnostic support.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Disseminated intravascular coagulation (DIC)"][/sc]
  • A 68-year-old male presents with acute onset shortness of breath, petechial rash, and oozing from a recent venepuncture site, following a severe bout of pneumonia.
  • An acutely unwell, elderly male with a severe infection, presenting with both microvascular bleeding and thrombotic manifestations, indicating a systemic derangement of haemostasis.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Disseminated intravascular coagulation (DIC)"][/sc]
  • Severe liver disease - often presents with coagulopathy, but typically has normal or only mildly elevated D-dimer and specific liver enzyme derangements.
  • Thrombotic thrombocytopenic purpura (TTP) - characterised by microangiopathic haemolytic anaemia, thrombocytopenia, renal failure, neurological symptoms, and fever, but without significant activation of the coagulation cascade.
  • Heparin-induced thrombocytopenia (HIT) - presents with thrombocytopenia and paradoxical thrombosis, but requires prior heparin exposure and specific antibody testing.
  • Sepsis-induced coagulopathy (SIC) - a milder form of coagulopathy seen in sepsis, which can progress to overt DIC if not managed.
  • Primary fibrinolysis - rare, presents with bleeding but typically has normal platelet count and markedly reduced fibrinogen without significant D-dimer elevation.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Disseminated intravascular coagulation (DIC)"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Address the underlying cause (e.g., antibiotics for sepsis, surgical intervention for trauma), maintain haemodynamic stability, and provide organ support.
  • Definitive management: Transfusion of blood products (platelets, fresh frozen plasma, cryoprecipitate) guided by laboratory parameters and clinical bleeding.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Close monitoring of clinical status, vital signs, urine output, and serial coagulation profiles.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Early involvement of critical care and haematology specialists. Patients and families should be counselled on the severity and potential complications.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Disseminated intravascular coagulation (DIC)"][/sc]
  • Severe haemorrhage: Due to consumption of clotting factors and platelets, leading to life-threatening bleeding from multiple sites, particularly in the progressive and late phases.
  • Multi-organ failure: Resulting from widespread microvascular thrombosis causing ischaemia and infarction in organs such as the kidneys, lungs, liver, and brain.
  • Thrombotic complications: Including deep vein thrombosis, pulmonary embolism, and arterial thrombosis, which can occur paradoxically alongside bleeding, especially in chronic or early phases.
  • Acute respiratory distress syndrome (ARDS): Often a consequence of the underlying sepsis or trauma, exacerbated by pulmonary microthrombi and inflammation.
  • Limb ischaemia/gangrene: Due to severe microvascular thrombosis, particularly in the digits, which can necessitate amputation.
