Endometrial cancer [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Endometrial cancer"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Endometrial cancer"][/sc]
  • Endometrial cancer is a malignancy arising from the epithelial lining (endometrium) of the uterus, most commonly affecting post-menopausal individuals [1] [2].
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the typical presentation of endometrial cancer, identify key risk factors, and understand the initial diagnostic steps and referral pathways [3].
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Endometrial cancer epidemiology"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Endometrial cancer"][/sc]
  • Uterine cancer, predominantly endometrial cancer, accounts for 3% of all new cancer cases in the UK, with approximately 9,800 new cases annually (2017-2019) [4].
  • Incidence rates are highest in females aged 75 to 79 in the UK, with over 1 in 10 (12%) of all new cases diagnosed in this age group (2017-2019) [4].
  • Rates have increased by around three-fifths (59%) in the UK since the early 1990s and by a tenth (10%) in the last decade (2017-2019) [4].
  • Key risk factors include obesity, type 2 diabetes mellitus, hypothyroidism, hypertension, early menarche, late menopause, nulliparity, polycystic ovarian syndrome (PCOS), Lynch syndrome, and tamoxifen use [2] [5].
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Endometrial cancer pathophysiology"][/sc]
  • Trigger: Endometrial cancer typically develops due to prolonged exposure to unopposed oestrogen, leading to endometrial hyperplasia [2].
  • Progression: This unopposed oestrogen can be endogenous (e.g., PCOS, obesity, anovulatory cycles) or exogenous (e.g., oestrogen-only hormone replacement therapy, tamoxifen) [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Unopposed oestrogen: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="unopposed oestrogen"][/sc] [2].
  • Consequence: Persistent endometrial hyperplasia, particularly atypical hyperplasia, can progress to adenocarcinoma, the most common histological type of endometrial cancer [2].
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Endometrial cancer time course"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Often asymptomatic or presents with subtle abnormal uterine bleeding, such as irregular periods in younger individuals or post-menopausal bleeding, which may be intermittent [2].
  • Progressive phase: As the tumour grows, bleeding may become more frequent or heavier, accompanied by increased vaginal discharge, and potentially pyometra [2].
  • Late phase: In advanced disease, symptoms such as pelvic pain, oedema, weight loss, and fatigue may develop, particularly beyond the initial presentation, indicating local spread or metastasis [2].
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Endometrial cancer symptoms"][/sc]
  • Ask about post-menopausal bleeding (PMB) - this is the most common presenting symptom and warrants urgent investigation [2] [6].
  • Ask about abnormal uterine bleeding in pre-menopausal women (e.g., heavy menstrual bleeding, intermenstrual bleeding, irregular bleeding) - these can be subtle indicators, especially in the presence of risk factors [2].
  • Ask about increased vaginal discharge - this can be a non-specific symptom but may indicate endometrial pathology [2].
  • Ask about pelvic pain or pressure - suggests more advanced disease or uterine enlargement [2].
  • Ask about unexplained weight loss or fatigue - these are constitutional symptoms that may indicate advanced malignancy [2].
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Endometrial cancer signs"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - to assess for signs of anaemia (e.g., tachycardia, pallor) due to chronic blood loss, or systemic illness [2].
  • Check for uterine bleeding or increased vaginal discharge on speculum examination - direct visualisation can confirm active bleeding or abnormal discharge [2].
  • Check for an enlarged or immobile uterus on bimanual palpation - suggests local tumour growth or spread [2].
  • Check for a palpable pelvic mass on abdominal examination - indicates advanced disease with significant tumour burden [2].
  • Check for signs of oedema, particularly lower limb - may indicate lymphatic obstruction or advanced disease [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Lymphatic obstruction in gynaecological cancer: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="oedema"][/sc] [2].
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Endometrial cancer red flags"][/sc]
  • Post-menopausal bleeding (PMB): Any vaginal bleeding occurring more than 12 months after the last menstrual period requires urgent investigation to exclude endometrial cancer [6].
  • Persistent abnormal uterine bleeding in pre-menopausal women with risk factors: Irregular, heavy, or intermenstrual bleeding, particularly in the presence of obesity, PCOS, or tamoxifen use, warrants further evaluation [2].
  • Pelvic pain or mass with abnormal bleeding: The co-occurrence of these symptoms suggests advanced disease and necessitates prompt referral [2].
  • Unexplained weight loss or new-onset oedema: These systemic symptoms, especially in older individuals, may indicate metastatic disease and require urgent assessment, particularly if present for several weeks [2].
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Endometrial cancer atypical presentations"][/sc]
  • Younger individuals: May present with abnormal uterine bleeding (e.g., heavy or irregular periods) rather than PMB, especially with underlying conditions like PCOS or Lynch syndrome [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Lynch syndrome and endometrial cancer risk: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Lynch syndrome"][/sc] [2].
  • Obese individuals: Higher risk due to increased peripheral oestrogen conversion, potentially masking symptoms or delaying diagnosis [2].
  • Type 2 Endometrial Cancer: Less common, often serous or clear cell histology, typically more aggressive and not directly linked to oestrogen exposure, presenting at a later stage with non-specific symptoms [2].
  • Asymptomatic presentation: Rarely, endometrial cancer may be an incidental finding during investigations for other conditions [2].
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Endometrial cancer diagnostic tests"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected endometrial cancer: Transvaginal ultrasound (TVUS) to assess endometrial thickness (ET). An ET ≥ 4mm in post-menopausal women or persistent bleeding with ET ≤ 4mm warrants further investigation [2] [6].
  • Confirmatory test: Endometrial biopsy (e.g., Pipelle or Vabra aspirator) is crucial for histological diagnosis, often performed in an outpatient setting [2].
  • Visualisation: Hysteroscopy allows direct visualisation of the endometrial cavity and targeted biopsies [2].
  • Staging imaging: CT scan of the chest, abdomen, and pelvis for preoperative staging and to assess for distant metastasis. MRI pelvis can further delineate local tumour extent and lymph node involvement [sc name="jitl-query" Q="FIGO staging for endometrial cancer: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="FIGO staging"][/sc] [2].
  • Severity/risk tools: The [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Endometrial cancer risk stratification: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="ESGO-ESMO-ESTRO consensus guidelines for endometrial cancer"] [7] provide risk stratification, but no single MDCalc score is routinely used for severity/risk assessment in primary care.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Endometrial cancer problem representation"][/sc]
  • A 68-year-old post-menopausal woman presents with intermittent vaginal bleeding for three months, associated with increased vaginal discharge and a history of obesity and type 2 diabetes.
  • Post-menopausal bleeding with risk factors, concerning for endometrial malignancy.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Endometrial cancer differential diagnosis"][/sc]
  • Endometrial polyp: Benign growth of endometrial tissue, often causing abnormal uterine bleeding but typically distinguishable by hysteroscopy and biopsy [2].
  • Uterine fibroids (leiomyoma): Benign smooth muscle tumours of the uterus, can cause heavy menstrual bleeding or pelvic pressure, but usually identified on ultrasound by their characteristic appearance [2].
  • Endometrial hyperplasia: Precancerous condition caused by unopposed oestrogen, diagnosed by biopsy, and may progress to cancer if untreated [2].
  • Atrophic vaginitis/endometritis: Thinning and inflammation of vaginal/endometrial lining due to oestrogen deficiency, common in post-menopausal women, usually responsive to local oestrogen therapy [2].
  • Cervical cancer: Malignancy of the cervix, can cause abnormal vaginal bleeding, but distinguished by cervical screening and colposcopy/biopsy [2].
  • Ectopic pregnancy: (In reproductive-aged women) Implantation of a fertilised ovum outside the uterus, presenting with abdominal pain and abnormal bleeding, diagnosed by hCG and ultrasound [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Ectopic pregnancy diagnosis: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Ectopic pregnancy"][/sc].
  • Pelvic Inflammatory Disease (PID): (In reproductive-aged women) Infection of the upper female reproductive tract, causing pelvic pain, fever, and vaginal discharge, but typically without significant abnormal bleeding unless severe [2].
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Endometrial cancer treatment"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Recognition of suspected endometrial cancer and urgent referral to gynaecological oncology services for specialist assessment and management. Supportive care may be considered for symptoms such as anaemia [2] [6].
  • Definitive management: Surgical intervention, typically total abdominal hysterectomy and bilateral salpingo-oophorectomy, is the mainstay of treatment for early-stage disease. Lymphadenectomy may also be performed for staging [2].
  • Adjunctive management: Adjuvant radiotherapy (external beam or brachytherapy) may be considered for high-risk early-stage disease or stage II-IV. Chemotherapy (e.g., doxorubicin, paclitaxel, carboplatin/cisplatin) may be used for advanced or recurrent disease, though response rates can be poor [2].
  • Hormonal therapies: Progestogens or tamoxifen may be considered for recurrent or advanced disease, particularly in oestrogen-receptor positive tumours [2].
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Regular clinical review and imaging are typically warranted post-treatment to monitor for recurrence [2].
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Patients should be advised on symptoms of recurrence and when to seek urgent medical attention. Local pathways for rapid re-assessment should be in place [2].
  • Exam pharmacology reference (UKMLA/MRCGP): Knowledge of tamoxifen\'s role as an anti-oestrogen in breast cancer treatment and its association with increased endometrial cancer risk is commonly tested. Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Endometrial cancer complications"][/sc]
  • Anaemia: Chronic or acute blood loss from the tumour can lead to anaemia, presenting with fatigue, weakness, and palpitations, particularly if bleeding is prolonged [2].
  • Metastasis: Spread of cancer to distant organs (e.g., lungs, liver, bone, bladder, rectum) can lead to organ-specific symptoms such as cough, dyspnoea, jaundice, or bone pain, often occurring in the late phase of the disease [2].
  • Lymphoedema: Surgical removal of lymph nodes can disrupt lymphatic drainage, leading to swelling in the lower limbs, which may develop weeks to months post-operatively [2].
  • Vaginal stenosis/atrophy: Radiotherapy can cause scarring and thinning of vaginal tissues, leading to vaginal stenosis or atrophy, impacting sexual function and quality of life [2].
  • Bowel or bladder fistula: Advanced disease or radiotherapy can lead to abnormal connections between the bowel or bladder and the vagina, causing leakage and infection, typically a late-stage complication [2].
