Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Upper respiratory tract infection (Ear, nose and throat)"][/sc]
  • An upper respiratory tract infection (URTI) is a common, acute, self-limiting infection affecting the nose, sinuses, pharynx, and larynx, typically caused by viruses.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the common, self-limiting nature of URTIs, identify red flag features necessitating further assessment, and apply principles of antimicrobial stewardship.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Upper respiratory tract infection (Ear, nose and throat)"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Upper respiratory tract infection (Ear, nose and throat)"][/sc]
  • URTIs are the most common cause of acute cough in the UK, with most individuals experiencing one to two episodes annually [1] [2].
  • Rhinoviruses are the most frequent viral pathogens, accounting for a significant proportion of common colds [3] [4].
  • Incidence is highest in young children, particularly those under two years of age [5].
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Upper respiratory tract infection (Ear, nose and throat)"][/sc]
  • Viral particles (e.g., rhinovirus) are inhaled or transferred via direct contact to the nasal or pharyngeal mucosa, initiating infection of epithelial cells.
  • Local inflammatory response leads to oedema, increased mucus production, and irritation of nerve endings, resulting in characteristic symptoms. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Inflammation in URTI: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Inflammation"][/sc]
  • Self-limiting course as the immune system clears the viral infection, typically without significant tissue damage or long-term sequelae.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Upper respiratory tract infection (Ear, nose and throat)"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Symptoms typically develop within 1-3 days of exposure, often starting with a sore throat, followed by nasal symptoms.
  • Progressive phase: Peak symptom severity usually occurs around 2-3 days, with cough and nasal congestion often becoming more prominent.
  • Late phase: Symptoms gradually resolve over 7-10 days, though a cough may persist for up to 3-4 weeks [1].
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Upper respiratory tract infection (Ear, nose and throat)"][/sc]
  • Ask about nasal congestion/rhinorrhoea - differentiates from lower respiratory tract infections.
  • Ask about sore throat - often an early symptom, can indicate pharyngitis or tonsillitis.
  • Ask about cough - can be dry or productive, and may persist longer than other symptoms.
  • Ask about malaise/fatigue - general systemic symptom, usually mild.
  • Ask about fever - typically low-grade, but can be higher, especially in children.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Upper respiratory tract infection (Ear, nose and throat)"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - usually normal or mildly elevated temperature; significant abnormalities may suggest a more serious condition.
  • Check pharynx/tonsils - erythema, exudates, or swelling may indicate pharyngitis/tonsillitis. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Tonsillitis: what are the key features and clinical relevance?" text="Tonsillitis"][/sc]
  • Check nasal mucosa - often erythematous and oedematous with clear or purulent discharge.
  • Check cervical lymph nodes - may be mildly enlarged and tender, reflecting regional inflammation.
  • Check chest auscultation - typically clear; adventitious sounds may suggest lower respiratory tract involvement.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Upper respiratory tract infection (Ear, nose and throat)"][/sc]
  • Worsening or rapidly deteriorating symptoms: Suggests potential progression to a more severe infection or complication, particularly beyond 7-10 days.
  • Significant breathlessness or chest pain: Raises concern for lower respiratory tract infection (e.g., pneumonia) or other serious cardiorespiratory pathology.
  • Systemic unwellness (e.g., confusion, severe lethargy, reduced consciousness): Indicates potential sepsis or severe systemic infection.
  • New focal neurological signs: May suggest intracranial complications, though rare.
  • Immunocompromise or significant comorbidities: Increased risk of severe disease and complications, requiring a lower threshold for investigation and intervention.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Upper respiratory tract infection (Ear, nose and throat)"][/sc]
  • Older adults: May present with more subtle symptoms, such as general malaise or confusion, with less prominent respiratory signs. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Atypical URTI in older adults: how does it present and why is it important?" text="Older adults"][/sc]
  • Immunocompromised individuals: Symptoms may be prolonged or more severe, with a higher risk of bacterial superinfection or unusual pathogens.
  • Infants and young children: May present with non-specific symptoms like irritability, poor feeding, or vomiting, making diagnosis challenging.
  • Asthma/COPD exacerbation: URTI can trigger acute exacerbations, presenting with increased wheeze, breathlessness, and cough.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Upper respiratory tract infection (Ear, nose and throat)"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected URTI: Clinical assessment based on history and examination is usually sufficient; no routine laboratory tests are typically required [6].
  • Severity/risk tools: For suspected bacterial tonsillitis, consider using the Centor or FeverPAIN score on MDCalc to guide antibiotic prescribing decisions. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Centor/FeverPAIN score: what is it and how is it used in URTI?" text="Centor/FeverPAIN score"][/sc]
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Consider tests for specific pathogens (e.g., influenza, COVID-19) if clinically indicated and impacts management, or for surveillance purposes [6].
  • Serial reassessment: Important if symptoms are not improving or worsening, to re-evaluate for complications or alternative diagnoses.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Upper respiratory tract infection (Ear, nose and throat)"][/sc]
  • Influenza: Often presents with more abrupt onset, higher fever, and more pronounced systemic symptoms (e.g., myalgia, severe fatigue).
  • Acute bacterial sinusitis: Persistent or worsening symptoms beyond 10 days, often with facial pain/pressure and purulent nasal discharge.
  • Acute bacterial tonsillitis/pharyngitis: More severe sore throat, often with exudates, fever, and tender cervical lymphadenopathy, without prominent cough or nasal symptoms.
  • Allergic rhinitis: Recurrent symptoms, often with clear rhinorrhoea, sneezing, and itchy eyes/nose, triggered by allergens rather than infection.
  • Pneumonia: Lower respiratory tract symptoms such as significant breathlessness, productive cough, chest pain, and abnormal chest signs. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Pneumonia vs URTI: what are the distinguishing features?" text="Pneumonia"][/sc]
  • COVID-19: Can present similarly to URTI, but may have specific symptoms like anosmia/ageusia, and often requires specific testing for differentiation.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Upper respiratory tract infection (Ear, nose and throat)"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Recognition of self-limiting nature, identification of red flags, and appropriate safety-netting advice.
  • Definitive management: Primarily supportive care, including rest, adequate fluid intake, and symptomatic relief (e.g., paracetamol, ibuprofen for pain/fever).
  • Antibiotic stewardship: Antibiotics are generally not indicated for viral URTIs; their use should be reserved for bacterial complications or high-risk individuals, guided by clinical assessment and local guidelines [1].
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Advise patients on expected symptom duration and when to seek further medical attention if symptoms worsen or do not improve.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Provide clear instructions on symptoms that warrant re-evaluation (e.g., increasing breathlessness, severe chest pain, systemic unwellness).
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Upper respiratory tract infection (Ear, nose and throat)"][/sc]
  • Acute otitis media: Often develops within 3-7 days of URTI onset, presenting with ear pain, particularly in children.
  • Acute bacterial sinusitis: Persistent or worsening nasal discharge and facial pain beyond 10 days, indicating bacterial superinfection.
  • Acute bronchitis: Inflammation of the bronchi, often presenting with a persistent cough, which may follow a viral URTI.
  • Pneumonia: A more serious lower respiratory tract infection, characterised by new or worsening cough, breathlessness, and systemic signs, typically developing after the initial URTI phase.
  • Exacerbation of underlying chronic conditions: URTIs can trigger exacerbations of asthma, COPD, or other chronic respiratory diseases.
