Pituitary tumours [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Pituitary tumours"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Pituitary tumours"][/sc]
  • A pituitary tumour is an abnormal growth of cells in the pituitary gland, a small gland at the base of the brain that produces hormones regulating many bodily functions.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical presentation and initial management of pituitary tumours, including their hormonal and mass effects.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Pituitary tumours"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Pituitary tumours"][/sc]
  • Pituitary adenomas are common, with an estimated prevalence of 1 in 1000 in the general population, often discovered incidentally on imaging for other conditions [1].
  • The incidence of clinically significant pituitary tumours in the UK is approximately 4 per 100,000 people per year, with a slight female predominance for prolactinomas and a male predominance for somatotrophinomas [2].
  • Risk factors include certain genetic syndromes such as Multiple Endocrine Neoplasia type 1 (MEN1) and Carney complex, though most pituitary tumours are sporadic.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Pituitary tumours"][/sc]
  • Trigger: Abnormal proliferation of one or more cell types within the anterior pituitary gland, often monoclonal in origin, leading to tumour formation.
  • Progression: Tumour growth can lead to local mass effects (e.g., compression of the optic chiasm) and/or hormonal dysfunction due to overproduction of specific hormones (e.g., prolactin, growth hormone, ACTH) or hypopituitarism from compression of normal pituitary tissue.
  • Consequence: Clinical manifestations arise from either mass effects (e.g., visual field defects, headaches) or hormonal imbalances (e.g., galactorrhoea, acromegaly, Cushing\\'s disease), impacting multiple organ systems.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Pituitary tumours"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Often insidious onset, with symptoms developing gradually over months to years, particularly beyond a few months, as tumours slowly enlarge or hormone levels subtly rise.
  • Progressive phase: Symptoms may become more pronounced and diverse, particularly beyond a few years, as mass effects become significant or hormonal imbalances lead to systemic complications.
  • Late phase: Severe visual impairment, neurological deficits, or life-threatening endocrine crises can occur if untreated, particularly beyond several years of progression.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Pituitary tumours"][/sc]
  • Ask about headaches - often frontal or retro-orbital, can be severe and persistent, indicating mass effect.
  • Ask about visual disturbances - particularly bitemporal hemianopia, suggesting optic chiasm compression.
  • Ask about changes in menstrual cycle or libido - amenorrhoea, galactorrhoea, erectile dysfunction, indicating prolactinoma or hypogonadism.
  • Ask about changes in appearance or growth - coarsening of facial features, increased hand/foot size, excessive sweating, suggesting acromegaly.
  • Ask about unexplained weight gain, easy bruising, muscle weakness - features of Cushing\\'s disease.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Pituitary tumours"][/sc]
  • Check visual fields - formal perimetry or confrontation testing to detect bitemporal hemianopia.
  • Check vital signs - blood pressure may be elevated in Cushing\\'s disease or acromegaly; pulse may be irregular.
  • Check for galactorrhoea - spontaneous milk discharge from nipples, indicative of hyperprolactinaemia.
  • Check for signs of acromegaly - prognathism, large hands/feet, skin tags, oily skin.
  • Check for signs of Cushing\\'s - central obesity, moon facies, buffalo hump, striae, proximal myopathy.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Pituitary tumours"][/sc]
  • Sudden severe headache with visual loss: Suggests pituitary apoplexy, a medical emergency requiring urgent neurosurgical review.
  • Rapidly progressive visual field defects: Indicates significant optic chiasm compression, necessitating prompt imaging and endocrine assessment.
  • New onset diplopia or cranial nerve palsies: May signify lateral extension of the tumour into the cavernous sinus, requiring urgent investigation.
  • Symptoms of acute adrenal insufficiency: (e.g., hypotension, confusion, severe weakness) in a patient with known pituitary disease or recent surgery, indicating a life-threatening endocrine emergency.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Pituitary tumours"][/sc]
  • Older adults: Symptoms may be subtle and attributed to ageing, leading to delayed diagnosis. Visual field defects might be missed due to co-existing cataracts or glaucoma.
  • Pregnancy: Pre-existing prolactinomas can enlarge due to oestrogen stimulation, potentially causing visual symptoms. Management requires careful consideration of fetal safety.
  • Incidentaloma: Pituitary tumours are often discovered incidentally on MRI scans performed for unrelated reasons, with many remaining asymptomatic and non-functional.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Pituitary tumours"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected pituitary tumour: Full endocrine work-up including prolactin, IGF-1 (for acromegaly), 24-hour urinary free cortisol (for Cushing\\'s), thyroid function tests, and pituitary hormones (LH, FSH, TSH, ACTH, cortisol).
  • Imaging - MRI brain with pituitary protocol: Essential for visualising the tumour, assessing its size, location, and relationship to surrounding structures like the optic chiasm and cavernous sinuses.
  • Formal visual field assessment: Performed by ophthalmology to quantify any visual impairment and monitor progression.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Consider other causes of headaches or visual disturbance, such as intracranial masses or neurological conditions, which may require broader neurological investigation.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Pituitary tumours"][/sc]
  • A 45-year-old female presents with a 6-month history of worsening headaches, blurred vision, and irregular menstrual periods, alongside new onset galactorrhoea.
  • A middle-aged female with chronic progressive mass effect symptoms and signs of hyperprolactinaemia, requiring urgent endocrine and neuro-ophthalmological assessment.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Pituitary tumours"][/sc]
  • Craniopharyngioma - typically presents in childhood or older adults, often calcified on imaging, can cause hypopituitarism and visual defects.
  • Meningioma - usually arises from meninges, can compress pituitary and optic chiasm, often has a dural tail on MRI.
  • Hypothalamic tumours - can cause similar endocrine and visual symptoms, but often with additional neurological features.
  • Empty sella syndrome - enlarged sella turcica filled with CSF, pituitary gland flattened, usually asymptomatic but can rarely cause hypopituitarism.
  • Lymphocytic hypophysitis - autoimmune inflammation of the pituitary, often in peripartum women, can mimic tumour with mass effect and hypopituitarism.
  • Physiological galactorrhoea - due to medication, stress, or nipple stimulation, distinguished by normal prolactin levels and absence of mass effects.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Pituitary tumours"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Assess for pituitary apoplexy or severe visual impairment. Correct any acute hormonal deficiencies (e.g., hydrocortisone for adrenal insufficiency).
  • Definitive management: Depends on tumour type. Prolactinomas are primarily managed medically with dopamine agonists (e.g., cabergoline, bromocriptine). Other functional tumours (e.g., growth hormone-secreting, ACTH-secreting) and non-functional macroadenomas often require transsphenoidal surgery. Radiotherapy may be considered for residual or recurrent disease.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Regular endocrine assessment, visual field testing, and MRI imaging to monitor tumour size and hormonal status. Lifelong hormone replacement may be necessary for hypopituitarism.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Advise patients to seek urgent medical attention for sudden worsening of headaches, visual loss, or symptoms of adrenal crisis.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Pituitary tumours"][/sc]
  • Visual impairment: Due to compression of the optic chiasm, leading to bitemporal hemianopia or even blindness if untreated, impacting quality of life and daily activities.
  • Hypopituitarism: Compression or destruction of normal pituitary tissue can lead to deficiencies of one or more pituitary hormones (e.g., TSH, ACTH, LH/FSH), requiring lifelong hormone replacement.
  • Pituitary apoplexy: Acute haemorrhage or infarction within the tumour, causing sudden severe headache, visual loss, and hypopituitarism, a neurosurgical emergency.
  • Cranial nerve palsies: Lateral extension of the tumour into the cavernous sinus can affect cranial nerves III, IV, V1, V2, and VI, leading to diplopia or facial numbness.
  • Diabetes insipidus: Damage to the posterior pituitary or hypothalamus can impair ADH secretion, leading to polyuria and polydipsia, requiring desmopressin replacement.
