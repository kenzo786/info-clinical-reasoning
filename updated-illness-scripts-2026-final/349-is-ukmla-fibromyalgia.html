Fibromyalgia [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Fibromyalgia"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Fibromyalgia"][/sc]
  • Fibromyalgia is a chronic widespread pain syndrome, characterised by pain lasting more than 3 months, often accompanied by fatigue, unrefreshing sleep, and cognitive dysfunction ("fibro-fog"). Diagnosis is typically based on the 2010 American College of Rheumatology (ACR) criteria, which focus on widespread pain index and symptom severity scale, and is understood to involve central sensitisation.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the key features of fibromyalgia, including chronic widespread pain, fatigue, and associated symptoms, to differentiate it from other musculoskeletal and systemic conditions.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Fibromyalgia"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Fibromyalgia"][/sc]
  • Fibromyalgia affects approximately 1.5 to 2 million people in the UK [6]. Estimates suggest nearly 1 in 20 people may be affected to some degree [3].
  • The prevalence of fibromyalgia in the UK general population is estimated at 5.4% [5].
  • Fibromyalgia is more common in women than men, with a female-to-male ratio of approximately 5:1 to 7:1 [4]. It typically develops between the ages of 25 and 55, but can occur at any age [3].
  • Risk factors include a history of physical or emotional trauma, infections (e.g., Epstein-Barr virus, Lyme disease), and genetic predisposition [3] [7].
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Fibromyalgia"][/sc]
  • Fibromyalgia often appears to be triggered by significant physical or emotional trauma, infections (e.g., Epstein-Barr virus, Lyme disease), or chronic stress. In many cases, symptoms may also develop gradually over time without a single identifiable precipitating event [3] [7].
  • The progression of fibromyalgia involves alterations in the central nervous system\'s processing of pain messages, leading to a state of [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Central sensitisation: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="central sensitisation"][/sc]. This results in a heightened state where nerves overreact to stimuli, lowering the pain threshold [1] [8].
  • The consequence of this altered pain processing is chronic widespread pain, often accompanied by profound fatigue, unrefreshing sleep, and cognitive dysfunction, sometimes referred to as [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Fibro-fog: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="fibro-fog"] [/sc] [1] [9].
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Fibromyalgia"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Symptoms often begin subtly, with localised pain that gradually becomes more widespread over weeks to months. Onset can be triggered by physical trauma, infection, or significant psychological stress, or may develop insidiously without a clear precipitating event [3].
  • Progressive phase: The condition is characterised by a fluctuating course, with periods of increased symptom severity (flare-ups) and relative remission. Pain, fatigue, and cognitive symptoms can intensify, significantly impacting daily activities and quality of life, particularly beyond the initial few months [3] [9].
  • Late phase: Fibromyalgia is a chronic, long-term condition. While symptoms can be managed, they are unlikely to disappear completely. The long-term impact involves persistent widespread pain, chronic fatigue, and potential for ongoing functional impairment and psychological distress [3] [9].
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Fibromyalgia"][/sc]
  • Ask about widespread pain - this is the cardinal symptom, often described as a constant dull ache that has lasted for at least three months. Its presence is essential for diagnosis.
  • Ask about fatigue and unrefreshing sleep - this helps distinguish fibromyalgia from simple musculoskeletal pain and points towards a central sensitisation syndrome.
  • Ask about cognitive symptoms ("fibro-fog") - inquiring about difficulties with concentration, memory, and mental clarity can further support a fibromyalgia diagnosis and helps to assess the full impact of the condition on the patient\'s life.
  • Ask about associated symptoms like headaches, irritable bowel syndrome (IBS), and increased sensitivity to pain - these are common comorbidities and their presence strengthens the diagnosis of fibromyalgia.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Fibromyalgia"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - typically normal, indicating the absence of acute systemic inflammation or infection, which helps in differentiating from other conditions.
  • Check for widespread tenderness to palpation - this is a key diagnostic feature, particularly in areas that are not anatomically related, implying [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Widespread tenderness to palpation in fibromyalgia: what does it imply clinically?" text="central sensitisation"] [10].
  • Check for muscle stiffness - often reported, especially in the morning, but without objective signs of inflammation or joint pathology.
  • Check for normal neurological examination - helps to exclude neurological conditions that might mimic fibromyalgia symptoms.
  • Check for absence of objective inflammatory signs - no joint swelling, erythema, or warmth, which helps to differentiate from inflammatory arthropathies.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Fibromyalgia"][/sc]
  • Objective joint swelling or inflammation: Suggests an inflammatory arthropathy rather than fibromyalgia, which is characterised by the absence of objective inflammatory signs.
  • Progressive neurological deficits: Such as focal weakness, sensory loss, or reflex changes, which may indicate an underlying neurological condition requiring urgent investigation, rather than the diffuse, non-focal symptoms of fibromyalgia.
  • Unexplained weight loss or persistent fever: These systemic symptoms are not typical of fibromyalgia and warrant investigation for underlying malignancy, infection, or other systemic diseases.
  • New onset of widespread pain in older adults without clear trigger: May necessitate a broader differential diagnosis to exclude conditions like polymyalgia rheumatica, malignancy, or other systemic illnesses that can present with diffuse pain.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Fibromyalgia"][/sc]
  • Men: While less common, men can develop fibromyalgia with similar pain and symptom profiles as women. However, diagnosis may be delayed due to lower suspicion and potential underreporting of symptoms, leading to significant impact on mental well-being and daily life [11].
  • Children and Adolescents: [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Juvenile fibromyalgia: how does it present and why does it matter clinically?" text="Juvenile fibromyalgia"] is most common in adolescent girls, typically presenting between ages 11 and 15. Symptoms mirror adult fibromyalgia, including widespread pain, fatigue, sleep disturbances, and mood issues, but can significantly impact school attendance and social development [12] [13].
  • Localized pain: Some individuals may initially present with more localized pain before it becomes widespread, making diagnosis challenging in the early stages and potentially mimicking other regional pain syndromes.
  • Atypical chest pain: Fibromyalgia can sometimes present with atypical chest pain, which may be initially misdiagnosed as cardiac in origin, highlighting the importance of considering fibromyalgia in the differential diagnosis of unexplained chest pain [14].
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Fibromyalgia"][/sc]
  • Fibromyalgia is a clinical diagnosis based on symptoms and physical examination findings, as there are no specific laboratory or imaging tests to confirm the condition [15].
  • The American College of Rheumatology (ACR) 2010 criteria are commonly used for diagnosis, which involve:
    • A [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Widespread Pain Index (WPI): how is it calculated and why does it matter clinically?" text="Widespread Pain Index (WPI)"] ≥7 and a Symptom Severity Scale (SSS) score ≥5, or a WPI of 3-6 and an SSS score ≥9 [16].
    • Symptoms present at a similar level for at least 3 months [16].
    • No other disorder that would otherwise explain the pain [16].
  • Routine blood tests (e.g., FBC, ESR, CRP, TFTs, LFTs, U&Es) are typically normal and are primarily used to exclude other conditions that may mimic fibromyalgia, such as hypothyroidism, inflammatory arthropathies, or systemic lupus erythematosus [15].
  • Imaging studies (e.g., X-rays, MRI) are generally not indicated for diagnosing fibromyalgia but may be used to rule out structural abnormalities or other causes of pain [15].

Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Fibromyalgia"][/sc]
  • A middle-aged female presenting with chronic (>3 months) widespread musculoskeletal pain, often accompanied by significant fatigue, unrefreshing sleep, and cognitive difficulties, in the absence of objective inflammatory signs or other clear alternative diagnoses.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Fibromyalgia"][/sc]
  • Hypothyroidism: Can cause fatigue, muscle aches, and cognitive impairment, mimicking fibromyalgia. However, thyroid function tests will be abnormal in hypothyroidism, whereas they are typically normal in fibromyalgia [17].
  • Inflammatory Arthropathies (e.g., Rheumatoid Arthritis, Systemic Lupus Erythematosus): These conditions present with pain, stiffness, and fatigue, but are distinguished by objective signs of inflammation (e.g., joint swelling, warmth, elevated ESR/CRP) and specific autoantibody profiles, which are absent in fibromyalgia [17].
  • Polymyalgia Rheumatica (PMR): Characterised by proximal muscle pain and stiffness, particularly in the shoulders and hips, often with elevated inflammatory markers. PMR typically affects older adults and responds well to corticosteroids, unlike fibromyalgia [18].
  • Chronic Fatigue Syndrome (CFS/ME): Shares significant symptomatic overlap with fibromyalgia, including profound fatigue, unrefreshing sleep, and cognitive dysfunction. The distinction often lies in the predominance of pain in fibromyalgia versus fatigue in CFS/ME, though co-occurrence is common [19].
  • Small Fibre Neuropathy (SFN): Can present with widespread pain, burning sensations, and paresthesias, which may be confused with fibromyalgia. Diagnosis of SFN involves specific neurological testing and skin biopsies to assess nerve fibre density [20].
  • Myofascial Pain Syndrome: Characterised by localised or regional pain with trigger points, unlike the widespread pain of fibromyalgia. Trigger points in myofascial pain syndrome are typically taut bands of muscle that refer pain in a predictable pattern, whereas fibromyalgia tender points are areas of generalised tenderness [21].
  • Vitamin D Deficiency: Can cause diffuse musculoskeletal pain and fatigue. Diagnosis is made by measuring serum vitamin D levels, and symptoms typically resolve with supplementation [22].
  • Depression and Anxiety Disorders: Often co-exist with fibromyalgia and can contribute to pain perception, fatigue, and sleep disturbances. While these conditions can mimic some fibromyalgia symptoms, the widespread pain component is central to fibromyalgia diagnosis [23].
  • [sc name="jitl-query" Q="How to differentiate fibromyalgia from other chronic widespread pain conditions?" text="Differentiating fibromyalgia from other chronic widespread pain conditions"] requires careful clinical assessment, exclusion of other diagnoses through appropriate investigations, and application of diagnostic criteria.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Fibromyalgia"][/sc]
  • A [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Multidisciplinary approach to fibromyalgia management: what does it involve and why is it important?" text="multidisciplinary approach"] is recommended, focusing on symptom management and improving quality of life, as there is no cure for fibromyalgia [24] [25].
  • Non-pharmacological interventions:
    • Exercise: Supervised group exercise programmes are recommended, tailored to individual capabilities, to improve physical function and reduce pain [26] [27].
    • Psychological therapies: Cognitive Behavioural Therapy (CBT) and Acceptance and Commitment Therapy (ACT) can help manage pain, fatigue, and associated psychological distress [27].
    • Acupuncture: May be considered for some individuals, though evidence for long-term effectiveness is limited [26].
    • Education and self-management: Understanding the condition and developing coping strategies are crucial for long-term management [24].
  • Pharmacological interventions:
    • Medications can help reduce symptoms but do not cure fibromyalgia. NICE guideline NG193 (2021) recommends against offering gabapentinoids (e.g., [sc name="jitl-medication" Q="Gabapentin: mechanism of action and clinical use in chronic pain?" text="gabapentin"], [sc name="jitl-medication" Q="Pregabalin: mechanism of action and clinical use in chronic pain?" text="pregabalin"]), opioids, or benzodiazepines for chronic primary pain, including fibromyalgia [28].
    • Antidepressants, such as tricyclic antidepressants (e.g., [sc name="jitl-medication" Q="Amitriptyline: mechanism of action and clinical use in chronic pain?" text="amitriptyline"]) or Selective Serotonin-Norepinephrine Reuptake Inhibitors (SNRIs) like [sc name="jitl-medication" Q="Duloxetine: mechanism of action and clinical use in chronic pain?" text="duloxetine"], may be considered if there is a co-morbid mood disorder or if other treatments have been ineffective, but should not be initiated solely for chronic primary pain [28] [29].
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-113" Q="Fibromyalgia"][/sc]
  • Impact on quality of life: The chronic pain, fatigue, and cognitive dysfunction associated with fibromyalgia can significantly impair daily activities, work productivity, and social interactions, leading to reduced overall quality of life [30].
  • Mental health comorbidities: Fibromyalgia is frequently associated with depression, anxiety disorders, and post-traumatic stress disorder (PTSD), which can exacerbate symptoms and complicate management [31].
  • Increased healthcare utilisation: Patients with fibromyalgia often experience higher rates of hospitalisation and healthcare visits due to persistent symptoms and associated comorbidities [32].
  • Sleep disturbances: Chronic unrefreshing sleep is a hallmark of fibromyalgia, contributing to fatigue and pain amplification, and can lead to further sleep disorders such as restless legs syndrome or sleep apnea [33].
  • Associated pain conditions: Fibromyalgia commonly co-occurs with other chronic pain conditions, including irritable bowel syndrome (IBS), migraines, and temporomandibular joint disorders, further increasing symptom burden [34].
  • [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Long-term prognosis of fibromyalgia: what are the key factors influencing outcome?" text="Long-term prognosis"] is variable, with many individuals experiencing persistent symptoms despite treatment. However, a significant proportion can achieve symptom improvement and better functional outcomes with comprehensive, multidisciplinary management [35].
