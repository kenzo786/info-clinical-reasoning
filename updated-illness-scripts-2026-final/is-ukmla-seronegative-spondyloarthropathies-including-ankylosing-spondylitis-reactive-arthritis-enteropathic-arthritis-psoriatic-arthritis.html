Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Seronegative spondyloarthropathies (including ankylosing spondylitis, reactive arthritis, enteropathic arthritis, psoriatic arthritis)"][/sc]
  • Seronegative spondyloarthropathies are a group of chronic inflammatory rheumatic diseases primarily affecting the axial skeleton and peripheral joints, characterized by the absence of rheumatoid factor and other autoantibodies.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical features, diagnostic criteria, and management principles of seronegative spondyloarthropathies, including ankylosing spondylitis, reactive arthritis, enteropathic arthritis, and psoriatic arthritis.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Seronegative spondyloarthropathies (including ankylosing spondylitis, reactive arthritis, enteropathic arthritis, psoriatic arthritis)"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Seronegative spondyloarthropathies (including ankylosing spondylitis, reactive arthritis, enteropathic arthritis, psoriatic arthritis)"][/sc]
  • In the UK, the prevalence of ankylosing spondylitis, a key seronegative spondyloarthropathy, is estimated to be around 0.15% in primary care populations, with overall prevalence ranging from 0.05% to 0.23% [5, 11].
  • Psoriatic arthritis affects approximately 1 in 3 individuals with psoriasis, equating to about 400,000 people in the UK [13].
  • A significant risk factor across seronegative spondyloarthropathies is the presence of the HLA-B27 gene variant, which is found in up to 90% of patients with ankylosing spondylitis and a substantial proportion of those with reactive arthritis and enteropathic arthritis [4, 7].
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Seronegative spondyloarthropathies (including ankylosing spondylitis, reactive arthritis, enteropathic arthritis, psoriatic arthritis)"][/sc]
  • The pathogenesis involves a complex interplay of genetic predisposition, notably the HLA-B27 allele, and environmental triggers such as gastrointestinal or genitourinary infections, leading to an aberrant immune response [4, 12].
  • This immune dysregulation results in chronic inflammation primarily affecting the entheses and axial skeleton, driven by both autoinflammatory and autoimmune mechanisms. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Enthesitis: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Enthesitis"][/sc]
  • Persistent inflammation leads to structural damage, including erosions and new bone formation (e.g., syndesmophytes), culminating in pain, stiffness, and progressive functional impairment.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Seronegative spondyloarthropathies (including ankylosing spondylitis, reactive arthritis, enteropathic arthritis, psoriatic arthritis)"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Symptoms often begin insidiously, typically with inflammatory back pain and stiffness, particularly beyond 3 months, often worse in the morning and improving with activity. Peripheral joint involvement or enthesitis may also be initial manifestations.
  • Progressive phase: Disease activity can fluctuate with periods of flares and remission. Without effective management, chronic inflammation leads to structural changes, such as syndesmophyte formation and progressive joint damage, particularly beyond several years.
  • Late phase: Advanced disease may result in significant functional impairment, spinal rigidity (e.g., 'bamboo spine' in ankylosing spondylitis), severe peripheral joint destruction, and increased risk of extra-articular manifestations, particularly beyond a decade of disease onset.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Seronegative spondyloarthropathies (including ankylosing spondylitis, reactive arthritis, enteropathic arthritis, psoriatic arthritis)"][/sc]
  • Ask about inflammatory back pain and stiffness – typically worse in the morning, improving with exercise, and often present for more than 3 months, which helps differentiate from mechanical back pain.
  • Ask about peripheral joint pain, swelling, and stiffness – often asymmetrical and affecting large joints, which can distinguish from rheumatoid arthritis.
  • Ask about enthesitis (pain at tendon or ligament insertions, e.g., Achilles tendon, plantar fascia) – a hallmark feature that points towards spondyloarthropathy.
  • Ask about dactylitis (diffuse swelling of an entire digit, giving a 'sausage digit' appearance) – highly suggestive of psoriatic arthritis or reactive arthritis.
  • Ask about skin rashes (psoriasis), nail changes (pitting, onycholysis), and eye inflammation (uveitis) – these extra-articular manifestations are crucial for diagnosis and classification.
  • Ask about a history of recent gastrointestinal or genitourinary infection – particularly relevant for reactive arthritis.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Seronegative spondyloarthropathies (including ankylosing spondylitis, reactive arthritis, enteropathic arthritis, psoriatic arthritis)"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs – may reveal low-grade fever or tachycardia during acute flares, indicating systemic inflammation.
  • Check for swollen and tender peripheral joints – often asymmetrical, particularly in the hands and feet, suggesting active synovitis.
  • Check for enthesitis – palpate common sites like the Achilles tendon insertion, plantar fascia, or costochondral junctions for tenderness, indicating inflammation at tendon/ligament attachments. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Enthesitis: clinical examination and significance?" text="Enthesitis"] [/sc]
  • Check for dactylitis – observe for diffuse swelling of an entire digit, giving a characteristic 'sausage digit' appearance, which is a strong indicator of psoriatic arthritis or reactive arthritis.
  • Check spinal mobility – assess for reduced range of motion in the cervical, thoracic, and lumbar spine, particularly with the modified Schober's test for ankylosing spondylitis.
  • Check skin and nails – inspect for psoriatic plaques, nail pitting, or onycholysis, which are key extra-articular manifestations.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Seronegative spondyloarthropathies (including ankylosing spondylitis, reactive arthritis, enteropathic arthritis, psoriatic arthritis)"][/sc]
  • Acute uveitis/iritis - sudden onset of painful, red eye with photophobia and blurred vision, requiring urgent ophthalmological assessment to prevent vision loss.
  • New or worsening neurological symptoms (e.g., radiculopathy, cauda equina syndrome) - suggests spinal cord compression or severe nerve impingement, necessitating urgent neurosurgical or rheumatological review.
  • Systemic symptoms such as unexplained weight loss, persistent fever, or severe fatigue - may indicate aggressive disease activity, infection, or other systemic complications.
  • Rapidly progressive spinal stiffness or deformity - indicates accelerated disease progression and potential for severe functional impairment.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Seronegative spondyloarthropathies (including ankylosing spondylitis, reactive arthritis, enteropathic arthritis, psoriatic arthritis)"][/sc]
  • Older adults: Late-onset ankylosing spondylitis may present with more peripheral joint involvement and less severe axial symptoms, making diagnosis challenging as it can be mistaken for osteoarthritis.
  • Pregnancy: Psoriatic arthritis symptoms can fluctuate during pregnancy, with some women experiencing improvement and others worsening, which can impact treatment decisions and fetal well-being.
  • Immunosuppressed individuals: In patients who are immunosuppressed, such as those with HIV, reactive arthritis may present with more severe and persistent symptoms, and the underlying infection may be atypical or difficult to identify.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Seronegative spondyloarthropathies (including ankylosing spondylitis, reactive arthritis, enteropathic arthritis, psoriatic arthritis)"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected seronegative spondyloarthropathy:
    • Full blood count (FBC) – may show anaemia of chronic disease.
    • Erythrocyte sedimentation rate (ESR) and C-reactive protein (CRP) – often elevated, indicating systemic inflammation, but can be normal.
    • HLA-B27 genetic testing – positive in a high proportion of patients, particularly with ankylosing spondylitis, but not diagnostic on its own.
    • Rheumatoid factor (RF) and anti-cyclic citrullinated peptide (anti-CCP) antibodies – typically negative, helping to differentiate from rheumatoid arthritis.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible:
    • Urine dipstick and culture – to rule out genitourinary infection in suspected reactive arthritis.
    • Stool culture – to rule out gastrointestinal infection in suspected reactive arthritis.
    • Antinuclear antibodies (ANA) – to screen for other autoimmune conditions if indicated.
  • Imaging - Sacroiliac joints:
    • X-rays – may show sacroiliitis (joint erosion, sclerosis, fusion) in later stages of axial spondyloarthropathy.
    • Magnetic Resonance Imaging (MRI) – more sensitive for detecting early inflammatory changes (e.g., bone marrow oedema) in the sacroiliac joints and spine, even before radiographic changes are evident.
  • Serial reassessment:
    • Disease activity scores such as ASDAS-ESR on MDCalc for ankylosing spondylitis, or ASAS Criteria for Axial Spondyloarthritis on MDCalc and ASAS Criteria for Peripheral Spondyloarthritis on MDCalc for classification and monitoring disease activity and treatment response.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Seronegative spondyloarthropathies (including ankylosing spondylitis, reactive arthritis, enteropathic arthritis, psoriatic arthritis)"][/sc]
  • Mechanical back pain – lacks inflammatory features (e.g., morning stiffness improving with exercise), often has a clear precipitating event.
  • Rheumatoid arthritis – typically seropositive (RF/anti-CCP positive), symmetrical polyarthritis, and spares the axial skeleton (except cervical spine).
  • Osteoarthritis – pain worsens with activity and improves with rest, lacks systemic inflammatory markers, and imaging shows degenerative changes rather than inflammatory erosions.
  • Gout/Pseudogout – acute monoarticular arthritis with rapid onset, often involving specific joints (e.g., big toe in gout), and identifiable crystals in synovial fluid.
  • Septic arthritis – acute, severe monoarticular pain with systemic signs of infection (fever, chills), and positive joint fluid culture.
  • Fibromyalgia – widespread musculoskeletal pain, fatigue, and tender points, but without objective signs of inflammation or structural joint damage.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Seronegative spondyloarthropathies (including ankylosing spondylitis, reactive arthritis, enteropathic arthritis, psoriatic arthritis)"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Pain management with non-steroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs) is often the first-line pharmacological intervention, alongside physical therapy to maintain mobility and reduce stiffness [3, 7].
  • Definitive management: For persistent symptoms or progressive disease, conventional synthetic disease-modifying anti-rheumatic drugs (csDMARDs) such as methotrexate or sulfasalazine may be considered. Biological DMARDs (bDMARDs), particularly TNF-alpha inhibitors, are indicated for patients with active disease unresponsive to conventional therapies [7, 8].
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Regular assessment of disease activity using validated scores (e.g., ASDAS-ESR) and monitoring for extra-articular manifestations are crucial. Routine imaging may be performed to track disease progression.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Patients with rapidly progressive disease, severe functional impairment, or significant extra-articular involvement (e.g., acute uveitis) require prompt referral to a rheumatologist for specialist management and consideration of advanced therapies.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Seronegative spondyloarthropathies (including ankylosing spondylitis, reactive arthritis, enteropathic arthritis, psoriatic arthritis)"][/sc]
  • Acute anterior uveitis: Inflammation of the anterior segment of the eye, a common extra-articular manifestation that can lead to pain, photophobia, and potentially permanent vision loss if not promptly treated.
  • Spinal fractures and osteoporosis: Chronic inflammation and new bone formation can lead to increased bone fragility and stiffness, predisposing individuals to vertebral fractures, even with minor trauma, and can result in neurological compromise.
  • Cardiovascular disease: Systemic inflammation associated with seronegative spondyloarthropathies increases the risk of cardiovascular events, including ischaemic heart disease and stroke, impacting long-term prognosis.
  • Inflammatory bowel disease (IBD): A significant proportion of patients, particularly those with enteropathic arthritis, may develop or have co-existing Crohn's disease or ulcerative colitis, adding to systemic burden and requiring integrated management.
  • Cauda equina syndrome: A rare but severe neurological complication of advanced axial spondyloarthropathy, resulting from compression of the cauda equina nerves, leading to sensory, motor, and bladder/bowel dysfunction, necessitating urgent intervention.
