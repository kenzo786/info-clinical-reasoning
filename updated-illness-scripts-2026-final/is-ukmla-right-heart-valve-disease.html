Right heart valve disease (Heart and vasculature) [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Right heart valve disease"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Right heart valve disease"][/sc]
  • Right heart valve disease encompasses conditions affecting the tricuspid and pulmonary valves, leading to impaired blood flow between the right atrium, right ventricle, and pulmonary artery.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical manifestations and potential complications of right heart valve disease, including tricuspid regurgitation, pulmonary stenosis, and pulmonary regurgitation.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Right heart valve disease"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Right heart valve disease"][/sc]
  • Tricuspid regurgitation is a common finding, with mild forms detected in up to 75% of adults on routine echocardiography, though clinically significant regurgitation is less frequent [1].
  • Pulmonary stenosis is predominantly congenital, with an estimated incidence of 0.6–0.8 per 1000 live births, accounting for 7–10% of all congenital heart defects [2].
  • Pulmonary regurgitation is often mild and asymptomatic, frequently observed after repair of congenital heart defects such as tetralogy of Fallot, or as a consequence of pulmonary hypertension [3].
  • Risk factors for acquired right heart valve disease include intravenous drug use (leading to infective endocarditis), carcinoid syndrome, and conditions causing pulmonary hypertension.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Right heart valve disease"][/sc]
  • Initial triggers vary by valve and aetiology, including congenital malformations (e.g., pulmonary stenosis), infectious processes (e.g., tricuspid valve endocarditis in intravenous drug users), or systemic conditions (e.g., [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Carcinoid heart disease: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="carcinoid heart disease"]()[4]).
  • Progression involves the development of valvular lesions such as thickening, retraction, or incomplete coaptation of leaflets, leading to either stenosis (restricted forward flow) or regurgitation (backward flow) and increased pressure or volume load on the right ventricle.
  • The sustained pressure or volume overload on the right ventricle can lead to right ventricular hypertrophy and dilation, eventually culminating in right ventricular dysfunction and overt right heart failure with systemic venous congestion.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Right heart valve disease"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Often asymptomatic, particularly in mild forms of valve disease, with compensatory mechanisms maintaining cardiac output.
  • Progressive phase: Gradual onset of symptoms as right ventricular compensation fails, typically over months to years, leading to increasing exercise intolerance and signs of systemic congestion.
  • Late phase: Characterised by severe right ventricular dysfunction and overt right heart failure, with symptoms and signs becoming more pronounced and persistent, often requiring urgent intervention.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Right heart valve disease"][/sc]
  • Ask about exertional dyspnoea - often the earliest symptom, reflecting reduced cardiac reserve and impaired pulmonary blood flow.
  • Ask about fatigue and weakness - due to reduced cardiac output and systemic hypoperfusion.
  • Ask about peripheral oedema (e.g., ankle swelling) - indicates increased systemic venous pressure and fluid retention, a hallmark of right heart failure.
  • Ask about abdominal distension or discomfort - caused by hepatomegaly and ascites from passive hepatic congestion.
  • Ask about pulsatile sensation in the neck - may indicate severe tricuspid regurgitation with prominent V waves in the jugular venous pulse.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Right heart valve disease"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - may reveal tachycardia, tachypnoea, and hypotension in advanced disease, or normal vitals in early stages.
  • Check jugular venous pressure (JVP) - elevated JVP with prominent V waves suggests severe tricuspid regurgitation, while a sustained elevation with a rapid Y descent may indicate right heart failure.
  • Check for peripheral oedema - pitting oedema in the ankles and sacrum is a common sign of systemic venous congestion.
  • Check for hepatomegaly and hepatojugular reflux - indicative of right ventricular dysfunction and increased right atrial pressure.
  • Check for [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Kussmaul\'s sign: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Kussmaul\'s sign"]() - a paradoxical rise in JVP with inspiration, suggesting impaired right ventricular filling or severe right heart failure.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Right heart valve disease"][/sc]
  • Acute decompensation: Rapid worsening of dyspnoea, oedema, or signs of systemic congestion, indicating acute right heart failure and requiring urgent medical assessment.
  • Syncope or pre-syncope: Suggests severely reduced cardiac output, particularly with exertion, and may indicate critical pulmonary stenosis or severe tricuspid regurgitation.
  • New onset or worsening chest pain: May indicate myocardial ischaemia due to increased right ventricular workload or associated coronary artery disease.
  • Haemoptysis: Can be a sign of severe pulmonary hypertension, which can be a complication of right heart valve disease.
  • Signs of systemic emboli: Such as neurological deficits or limb ischaemia, especially in the context of infective endocarditis or atrial fibrillation with right-sided thrombi.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Right heart valve disease"][/sc]
  • Functional tricuspid regurgitation: Often presents as worsening right heart failure symptoms without primary tricuspid valve pathology, instead caused by right ventricular dilation secondary to left heart disease or pulmonary hypertension [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Functional tricuspid regurgitation: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="functional tricuspid regurgitation"]().
  • Carcinoid heart disease: Patients with carcinoid syndrome may develop right-sided valve lesions (typically tricuspid regurgitation and pulmonary stenosis) leading to right heart failure, often with cutaneous flushing and diarrhoea.
  • Intravenous drug users: May present with acute right-sided infective endocarditis, often affecting the tricuspid valve, leading to rapid onset of fever, systemic emboli, and signs of severe tricuspid regurgitation.
  • Pregnancy: Pre-existing right heart valve disease can decompensate during pregnancy due to increased circulatory volume and cardiac output, leading to worsening symptoms and potential complications for both mother and foetus.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Right heart valve disease"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected right heart valve disease:
    • Echocardiography (transthoracic and transoesophageal) - essential for assessing valve morphology, function, severity of stenosis/regurgitation, and right ventricular size/function.
    • Electrocardiogram (ECG) - may show right atrial enlargement, right ventricular hypertrophy, or arrhythmias (e.g., atrial fibrillation).
    • Chest X-ray - may show cardiomegaly, pulmonary artery dilation, or signs of pulmonary congestion.
    • Blood tests - including B-type natriuretic peptide (BNP) or N-terminal proBNP (NT-proBNP) to assess heart failure severity, and liver function tests in cases of hepatic congestion.
  • Severity/risk tools:
    • Tricuspid Regurgitation Impact on Outcomes (TRIO) score - TRIO score on Mayo Clinic - estimates 1- and 5-year mortality in patients with moderate to severe tricuspid regurgitation.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible:
    • Cardiac MRI - provides detailed anatomical and functional assessment, particularly useful for quantifying right ventricular volumes and ejection fraction, and assessing pulmonary artery flow.
    • [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Right heart catheterisation: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Right heart catheterisation"]() - invasive measurement of intracardiac pressures and cardiac output, crucial for confirming pulmonary hypertension and assessing its severity.
  • Imaging - CT: Cardiac CT - useful for assessing valve calcification, annular dimensions, and guiding transcatheter interventions.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Right heart valve disease"][/sc]
  • A middle-aged male with a history of intravenous drug use presents with progressive exertional dyspnoea, peripheral oedema, and a pulsatile neck vein over several months, accompanied by a pansystolic murmur loudest at the left sternal edge.
  • A chronic, progressive right-sided cardiac condition with signs of systemic venous congestion, concerning for right heart valve pathology and subsequent right heart failure.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Right heart valve disease"][/sc]
  • Left heart failure - typically presents with pulmonary oedema and orthopnoea, whereas right heart failure primarily causes systemic venous congestion.
  • Constrictive pericarditis - shares features of systemic venous congestion but often has a pericardial knock and characteristic imaging findings (e.g., pericardial thickening).
  • Pulmonary hypertension (primary) - can lead to right heart failure and similar symptoms, but the primary pathology is in the pulmonary vasculature rather than the valves.
  • Liver cirrhosis - can cause ascites and peripheral oedema, but typically without significant cardiac murmurs or elevated JVP, and with distinct liver function abnormalities.
  • Nephrotic syndrome - causes generalised oedema due to proteinuria and hypoalbuminaemia, but usually without cardiac signs or symptoms.
  • [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Restrictive cardiomyopathy: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Restrictive cardiomyopathy"]() - can mimic right heart failure with preserved ejection fraction, but echocardiography reveals characteristic restrictive filling patterns.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Right heart valve disease"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Recognition of acute decompensation, escalation to specialist cardiology services, and supportive care to manage symptoms of right heart failure (e.g., oxygen, diuretics).
  • Definitive management: Often involves surgical intervention (valve repair or replacement) for severe symptomatic valve disease, or transcatheter procedures for suitable candidates. Management of underlying conditions (e.g., carcinoid syndrome, pulmonary hypertension) is crucial.
  • Adjunctive management: May include diuretics to manage fluid overload, and potentially vasodilators or inotropes in severe right heart failure, guided by specialist assessment.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Regular clinical review and echocardiography are typically warranted to assess disease progression, treatment efficacy, and detect complications.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Patients should be advised on symptoms of worsening heart failure or red flag features that necessitate urgent medical attention.
  • Exam pharmacology reference (UKMLA/MRCGP):

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Right heart valve disease"][/sc]
  • Progressive right heart failure: Unmanaged or severe right heart valve disease can lead to chronic and progressive right ventricular dysfunction, resulting in severe systemic venous congestion, hepatic dysfunction, and renal impairment.
  • Pulmonary hypertension: Particularly in cases of severe pulmonary stenosis or long-standing tricuspid regurgitation, increased right ventricular pressures can lead to secondary pulmonary hypertension, further exacerbating right heart strain.
  • Arrhythmias: Right atrial dilation, often seen in tricuspid valve disease, can predispose to atrial fibrillation and other supraventricular arrhythmias, increasing the risk of systemic emboli.
  • Infective endocarditis: Damaged or prosthetic right heart valves are susceptible to bacterial colonisation, leading to infective endocarditis, which can cause further valve destruction and septic emboli.
  • Sudden cardiac death: In severe cases, particularly with critical pulmonary stenosis or advanced right heart failure, there is an increased risk of sudden cardiac death due to arrhythmias or acute haemodynamic collapse.
