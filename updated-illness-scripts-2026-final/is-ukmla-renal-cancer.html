Renal cancer [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Renal cancer"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Renal cancer"][/sc]
  • Renal cancer, also known as kidney cancer, refers to malignant tumours arising from the kidney parenchyma, most commonly renal cell carcinoma (RCC).
  • UKMLA Learning Outcome: Understand the epidemiology, risk factors, clinical presentation, diagnosis, staging, and management of renal cell carcinoma.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Renal cancer"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Renal cancer"][/sc]
  • Renal cancer accounts for approximately 3% of all adult cancers in the UK, with around 13,000 new cases diagnosed annually [1].
  • Incidence rates are higher in men than women, and typically increase with age, peaking in the 60s and 70s [1].
  • Key risk factors include smoking, obesity, hypertension, and certain genetic syndromes such as von Hippel-Lindau disease [2].
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Renal cancer"][/sc]
  • The trigger for renal cancer often involves genetic mutations in renal tubular epithelial cells, leading to uncontrolled cell proliferation and tumour formation.
  • Progression typically involves local invasion into the renal capsule, perinephric fat, and renal vein, with potential for distant metastasis via haematogenous or lymphatic spread.
  • The consequence of advanced disease can include paraneoplastic syndromes, organ dysfunction due to metastatic deposits, and systemic symptoms such as weight loss and fatigue.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Renal cancer"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Often asymptomatic, with incidental discovery on imaging performed for other reasons.
  • Progressive phase: Local tumour growth may lead to classic triad symptoms (haematuria, flank pain, palpable mass), though this is uncommon.
  • Late phase: Metastatic disease can cause symptoms related to secondary tumour sites (e.g., bone pain, cough, neurological deficits) and systemic symptoms.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Renal cancer"][/sc]
  • Ask about haematuria - often painless and macroscopic, indicating potential urinary tract pathology.
  • Ask about flank pain - typically dull and persistent, suggesting local tumour expansion or invasion.
  • Ask about a palpable abdominal mass - may indicate a large tumour, particularly in thinner individuals.
  • Ask about unexplained weight loss and fatigue - common systemic symptoms of malignancy.
  • Ask about fever of unknown origin - can be a paraneoplastic manifestation.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Renal cancer"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - hypertension may be present due to renin production, or fever in paraneoplastic syndromes.
  • Check for a palpable abdominal mass - often firm, irregular, and non-tender in the renal angle.
  • Check for supraclavicular lymphadenopathy - suggests metastatic spread.
  • Check for oedema of the lower limbs - may indicate inferior vena cava involvement.
  • Check for varicocele (left-sided) - can be caused by obstruction of the left testicular vein by a renal tumour.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Renal cancer"][/sc]
  • Painless macroscopic haematuria: warrants urgent investigation for urinary tract malignancy.
  • Persistent flank pain with a palpable mass: highly suspicious for a renal tumour.
  • Unexplained weight loss, fatigue, and night sweats: systemic symptoms suggestive of advanced malignancy.
  • New onset varicocele in an adult (especially left-sided): may indicate obstruction of the renal vein by a tumour.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Renal cancer"][/sc]
  • Elderly patients: may present with non-specific symptoms such as general malaise, anaemia, or falls, masking the underlying malignancy.
  • Paraneoplastic syndromes: can present with symptoms like polycythaemia, hypercalcaemia, hypertension, or Stauffer's syndrome (hepatic dysfunction), without typical renal symptoms.
  • Incidental finding: increasingly, renal cancers are discovered incidentally on imaging for unrelated conditions, as they are often asymptomatic in early stages.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Renal cancer"][/sc]
  • Core:
    • Urinalysis: to confirm haematuria.
    • Full blood count: may show anaemia (chronic disease or haemorrhage) or polycythaemia (paraneoplastic).
    • Renal function tests (urea, creatinine, eGFR): to assess kidney function.
    • Liver function tests: to assess for metastatic disease or paraneoplastic Stauffer's syndrome.
    • Calcium: to check for hypercalcaemia (paraneoplastic).
  • Imaging:
    • Ultrasound scan of kidneys, ureters, and bladder (KUB): initial imaging to identify renal masses.
    • CT abdomen and pelvis with contrast: definitive imaging for characterisation, staging, and assessment of local invasion and nodal involvement.
    • CT chest: to assess for lung metastases.
    • MRI abdomen: an alternative to CT, particularly if contrast is contraindicated or for better soft tissue characterisation.
    • Bone scan: if bone pain or elevated alkaline phosphatase suggests bone metastases.
  • If alternative diagnosis considered:
    • Cystoscopy: if bladder pathology is suspected as the cause of haematuria.
  • Serial reassessment:
    • Biopsy of renal mass: typically performed if imaging is equivocal or for small renal masses where active surveillance is considered.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Renal cancer"][/sc]
  • A middle-aged individual presenting with painless macroscopic haematuria, flank pain, and unexplained weight loss, often warrants investigation for renal cancer.
  • This is a progressive, potentially metastatic malignancy of the kidney, requiring prompt diagnosis and multidisciplinary management.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Renal cancer"][/sc]
  • Benign renal cyst - typically simple, thin-walled, and anechoic on ultrasound, lacking solid components or septations.
  • Angiomyolipoma - a benign renal tumour containing fat, muscle, and blood vessels, often diagnosed on CT by its fat content.
  • Pyelonephritis - presents with fever, dysuria, and loin pain, often with bacteriuria, unlike the typically painless haematuria of renal cancer.
  • Urolithiasis - causes acute, colicky flank pain radiating to the groin, often with microscopic or macroscopic haematuria, but usually without a palpable mass.
  • Bladder cancer - also causes haematuria, but typically without flank pain or a renal mass, and often associated with irritative voiding symptoms.
  • Renal abscess - presents with fever, chills, and localised flank pain, often with a history of urinary tract infection, and imaging shows a fluid collection.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Renal cancer"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities:
    • Stabilisation of any acute symptoms, such as severe pain or significant haemorrhage.
    • Expeditious diagnostic work-up and staging to guide management decisions.
  • Definitive management:
    • Surgical resection (radical or partial nephrectomy) is the mainstay for localised disease.
    • Active surveillance may be considered for small, low-risk renal masses, particularly in elderly or frail patients.
    • Systemic therapies (e.g., targeted therapies, immunotherapy) are used for advanced or metastatic disease.
    • Radiotherapy may be used for palliation of symptoms from metastatic sites.
  • Monitoring and follow-up:
    • Regular imaging (CT or MRI) and clinical review to detect recurrence or metastatic progression.
    • Monitoring of renal function and blood pressure, especially after nephrectomy.
  • Escalation and safety-netting:
    • Patients should be counselled on symptoms of recurrence or progression and advised when to seek urgent medical attention.
    • Multidisciplinary team (MDT) discussion is crucial for complex cases and treatment planning.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Renal cancer"][/sc]
  • Haemorrhage: Tumour rupture or invasion into blood vessels can lead to significant bleeding, potentially requiring urgent intervention.
  • Metastasis: Spread to distant organs (e.g., lungs, bone, liver, brain) is a common complication, leading to organ-specific symptoms and reduced survival.
  • Renal vein or inferior vena cava thrombosis: Tumour extension into these vessels can cause venous obstruction, leading to lower limb oedema or pulmonary embolism.
  • Paraneoplastic syndromes: Secretion of hormones or cytokines by the tumour can cause systemic effects such as polycythaemia, hypercalcaemia, or hypertension.
  • Renal failure: Can occur due to extensive tumour burden, bilateral disease, or complications of nephrectomy, particularly in patients with pre-existing renal impairment.
