Bell palsy [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Bell palsy"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Bell palsy"][/sc]
  • Bell palsy is an acute, unilateral, idiopathic facial nerve paralysis, typically presenting as a lower motor neuron lesion affecting one side of the face.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical features of Bell palsy, distinguish it from other causes of facial weakness, and initiate appropriate acute management including eye protection and corticosteroids.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Bell palsy"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Bell palsy"][/sc]
  • The annual incidence of Bell palsy in the UK is approximately 20-30 per 100,000 people, making it the most common cause of acute unilateral facial paralysis [1] [2].
  • It affects males and females equally, with a peak incidence between 20 and 40 years of age, though it can occur at any age [1].
  • Risk factors include pregnancy, diabetes mellitus, obesity, hypertension, and recent upper respiratory tract infection [1].
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Bell palsy"][/sc]
  • The precise trigger is often unknown, but viral reactivation (commonly Herpes Simplex Virus type 1) is thought to cause inflammation and oedema of the facial nerve (cranial nerve VII) within the bony facial canal [3].
  • This inflammation leads to compression and ischaemia of the nerve, impairing its ability to transmit signals to the facial muscles.
  • The consequence is acute, unilateral weakness or paralysis of the facial muscles, affecting all branches of the facial nerve on the affected side, characteristic of a [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Lower motor neuron lesion: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="lower motor neuron lesion"][/sc].
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Bell palsy"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Symptoms typically develop rapidly over hours to 3 days, reaching maximal weakness within 72 hours of onset.
  • Progressive phase: Weakness may continue to worsen slightly for up to 72 hours, after which recovery usually begins.
  • Late phase: Significant recovery often occurs within 2-3 weeks, with most patients achieving full or near-full recovery within 3-6 months, particularly if treated early.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Bell palsy"][/sc]
  • Ask about sudden onset of unilateral facial weakness - this is the cardinal symptom and helps differentiate from chronic or progressive conditions.
  • Ask about difficulty closing the eye on the affected side - important for assessing corneal exposure risk.
  • Ask about difficulty with facial expressions (e.g., smiling, frowning, raising eyebrow) - indicates involvement of all facial nerve branches.
  • Ask about altered taste sensation (dysgeusia) on the anterior two-thirds of the tongue - suggests involvement of the chorda tympani nerve.
  • Ask about pain around the ear or mastoid process preceding or accompanying weakness - may suggest inflammatory aetiology.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Bell palsy"][/sc]
  • Check for complete unilateral facial weakness affecting the forehead, eyebrow, eye closure, nasolabial fold, and mouth - this pattern is characteristic of a [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Lower motor neuron facial palsy: what are its key features?" text="lower motor neuron facial palsy" ][/sc].
  • Check for inability to close the eye fully (lagophthalmos) and reduced blinking - crucial for assessing ocular protection needs.
  • Check for drooping of the corner of the mouth and inability to show teeth evenly - indicates lower facial nerve involvement.
  • Check for loss of forehead furrows and inability to raise the eyebrow on the affected side - helps distinguish from upper motor neuron lesions which typically spare the forehead.
  • Check vital signs - typically normal, helping to exclude systemic causes or severe infections.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Bell palsy"][/sc]
  • Slowly progressive weakness: Suggests an alternative diagnosis such as a tumour or inflammatory condition, warranting urgent neurological review.
  • Facial weakness not confined to one side: Bilateral facial weakness points towards conditions like Guillain-Barré syndrome or Lyme disease.
  • Associated neurological symptoms: Weakness in other limbs, sensory changes, or diplopia may indicate a central nervous system lesion.
  • Recurrent facial palsy: While possible in Bell palsy, recurrence should prompt investigation for underlying causes such as sarcoidosis or tumour.
  • Absence of recovery after 3-6 months: May indicate a more severe nerve injury or an alternative diagnosis, requiring further investigation.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Bell palsy"][/sc]
  • Children: Bell palsy can occur in children, but other causes of facial palsy (e.g., trauma, infection) are relatively more common and should be considered.
  • Pregnancy: Pregnant individuals have an increased risk of Bell palsy, particularly in the third trimester and puerperium, which may necessitate careful management of corticosteroids.
  • Incomplete palsy: Some patients may present with only partial facial weakness, which generally carries a better prognosis for full recovery.
  • Pain preceding weakness: While common, severe or persistent pain, particularly if associated with vesicles, may suggest Ramsay Hunt syndrome (herpes zoster oticus), a [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Ramsay Hunt syndrome: how does it differ from Bell palsy?" text="key differential"][/sc].
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Bell palsy"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected Bell palsy: Diagnosis is primarily clinical, based on the characteristic acute onset of unilateral lower motor neuron facial weakness and exclusion of other causes.
  • Severity/risk tools: The [sc name="jitl-query" Q="House-Brackmann grading system: how is it used in facial palsy?" text="House-Brackmann Facial Nerve Grading System"][/sc] is used to objectively grade the severity of facial nerve dysfunction, from Grade I (normal) to Grade VI (total paralysis). Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Bell palsy"][/sc]
  • A 35-year-old female presents with sudden onset, progressive unilateral facial weakness over 48 hours, affecting her ability to close her right eye and smile, without other neurological symptoms.
  • Acute, isolated, unilateral lower motor neuron facial palsy, concerning for Bell palsy.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Bell palsy"][/sc]
  • Stroke/TIA - typically presents with [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Upper motor neuron facial palsy: how does it differ from lower motor neuron?" text="upper motor neuron facial palsy"] [/sc] (forehead sparing) and often other neurological deficits.
  • Ramsay Hunt Syndrome (Herpes Zoster Oticus) - associated with severe ear pain, vesicular rash in the ear canal, and often more profound facial palsy and poorer prognosis.
  • Lyme Disease - may cause facial palsy (often bilateral) in endemic areas, usually with a history of tick bite and other systemic symptoms.
  • Tumour (e.g., acoustic neuroma, parotid gland tumour) - typically causes slowly progressive facial weakness, often with other cranial nerve deficits.
  • Otitis Media/Mastoiditis - facial palsy may occur as a complication of severe ear infection, usually with clear signs of infection.
  • Sarcoidosis - can cause facial nerve palsy (often bilateral) as part of a systemic inflammatory process.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Bell palsy"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Recognition of acute onset unilateral facial weakness, exclusion of red flags, and prompt initiation of eye protection.
  • Definitive management: Oral corticosteroids (e.g., prednisolone) are often considered within 72 hours of symptom onset to improve the rate of full recovery.
  • Adjunctive management: Eye care is crucial to prevent corneal damage due to lagophthalmos and reduced blinking; this often includes lubricating eye drops during the day and eye ointment with taping the eye closed at night.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Regular review to assess recovery, ensure adequate eye protection, and identify any emerging red flags.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Patients should be advised to seek urgent medical attention if symptoms worsen, new neurological symptoms develop, or eye irritation/pain occurs.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Bell palsy"][/sc]
  • Corneal damage: Due to incomplete eye closure (lagophthalmos) and reduced tear production, the cornea is vulnerable to drying, abrasion, and ulceration, particularly in the early phase.
  • Synkinesis: Abnormal involuntary co-contraction of facial muscles (e.g., eye closure when smiling) can occur during recovery, resulting from aberrant nerve regeneration in the late phase.
  • Persistent facial weakness/paralysis: While most recover, a minority of patients may experience incomplete recovery or permanent facial weakness, impacting quality of life and facial symmetry.
  • Crocodile tears (gustatory lacrimation): Involuntary tearing while eating, a rare complication resulting from misdirected nerve regeneration, typically manifesting in the late phase of recovery.
