    Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Postpartum depression"][/sc]
    • 'Baby blues' - transient mood disturbance, typically resolving within 10-14 days postpartum, less severe and shorter duration.
    • Postpartum psychosis - severe psychiatric emergency with psychotic features (hallucinations, delusions), requiring immediate intervention.
    • Thyroid dysfunction (e.g., postpartum thyroiditis) - can mimic depressive symptoms, distinguished by thyroid function tests.
    • Anaemia - fatigue and low energy, but typically without the core mood and anhedonia symptoms of depression.
    • Adjustment disorder - depressive symptoms in response to a significant life stressor, but typically less pervasive and severe than major depression.
    • Anxiety disorder - prominent anxiety symptoms, but without the primary mood disturbance and anhedonia characteristic of depression.
    Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Postpartum depression"][/sc]
    • Immediate priorities: Assess for risk of self-harm or harm to the baby; ensure safety and provide immediate support.
    • Definitive management: Psychological therapies (e.g., cognitive behavioural therapy, interpersonal therapy) are often first-line. Antidepressant medication (e.g., SSRIs) may be considered, particularly for moderate to severe depression, weighing risks and benefits during breastfeeding.
    • Monitoring and follow-up: Regular review of symptoms, treatment adherence, and maternal-infant interaction.
    • Escalation and safety-netting: Refer to specialist perinatal mental health services for severe or complex cases, or if there are concerns about safety.

    Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Postpartum depression"][/sc]
    • Chronic depression: Untreated or inadequately treated postpartum depression can persist for years, leading to long-term morbidity and reduced quality of life.
    • Impaired maternal-infant bonding: Depressive symptoms can interfere with a mother's ability to bond with her infant, potentially impacting infant development and attachment.
    • Child developmental issues: Children of mothers with untreated postpartum depression may experience cognitive, emotional, and behavioural problems.
    • Relationship difficulties: The stress of postpartum depression can strain marital or partner relationships, leading to conflict and reduced support.
    • Recurrence: Women with a history of postpartum depression have a significantly increased risk of experiencing future depressive episodes.
