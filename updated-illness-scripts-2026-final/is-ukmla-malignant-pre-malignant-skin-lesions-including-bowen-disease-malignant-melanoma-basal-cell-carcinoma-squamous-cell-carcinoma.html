Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Malignant/ pre-malignant skin lesions (including Bowen disease, malignant melanoma, basal cell carcinoma, squamous cell carcinoma)"][/sc]
  • UKMLA learning outcome: The candidate must recognise and manage malignant and pre-malignant skin lesions, including Bowen disease, malignant melanoma, basal cell carcinoma, and squamous cell carcinoma.
  • A group of dermatological conditions characterised by uncontrolled cell growth, ranging from pre-cancerous changes to aggressive malignancies, often influenced by UV exposure and genetic factors.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Malignant/ pre-malignant skin lesions (including Bowen disease, malignant melanoma, basal cell carcinoma, squamous cell carcinoma)"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Malignant/ pre-malignant skin lesions (including Bowen disease, malignant melanoma, basal cell carcinoma, squamous cell carcinoma)"][/sc]
  • Basal cell carcinoma (BCC) is the most common skin cancer in the UK, accounting for approximately 80% of all non-melanoma skin cancers, with incidence rising, particularly in older adults [1].
  • Squamous cell carcinoma (SCC) is the second most common, representing about 15-20% of non-melanoma skin cancers, with a higher metastatic potential than BCC [2].
  • Malignant melanoma, though less common, is responsible for the majority of skin cancer deaths, with over 16,000 new cases diagnosed annually in the UK [3]. Risk factors include excessive UV exposure, fair skin, multiple moles, and a family history of melanoma.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Malignant/ pre-malignant skin lesions (including Bowen disease, malignant melanoma, basal cell carcinoma, squamous cell carcinoma)"][/sc]
  • Trigger: Chronic or intense intermittent exposure to ultraviolet (UV) radiation causes DNA damage in keratinocytes and melanocytes, leading to mutations in tumour suppressor genes (e.g., p53) and oncogenes (e.g., BRAF, PTCH1).
  • Progression: Accumulation of these genetic alterations disrupts normal cell cycle control, promoting uncontrolled cellular proliferation and evasion of apoptosis, leading to the formation of pre-malignant lesions (e.g., actinic keratoses, Bowen disease) or in situ carcinomas.
  • Consequence: Further mutations and microenvironmental changes facilitate invasion through the basement membrane into the dermis, enabling local tissue destruction and potential for distant metastasis via lymphatic or haematogenous spread.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Malignant/ pre-malignant skin lesions (including Bowen disease, malignant melanoma, basal cell carcinoma, squamous cell carcinoma)"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Pre-malignant lesions like actinic keratoses or Bowen disease may develop slowly over months to years, often presenting as persistent, non-healing lesions.
  • Progressive phase: Basal cell carcinomas typically grow slowly over months to years, while squamous cell carcinomas can progress more rapidly, particularly beyond a few months, showing increased size or ulceration.
  • Late phase: Malignant melanoma can grow rapidly, with vertical growth phase developing over weeks to months, and metastatic disease potentially manifesting within months to a few years if not detected early.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Malignant/ pre-malignant skin lesions (including Bowen disease, malignant melanoma, basal cell carcinoma, squamous cell carcinoma)"][/sc]
  • Ask about changes in a mole or skin lesion - particularly size, shape, colour, or new symptoms like itching or bleeding, as these are key indicators for melanoma (ABCDE criteria).
  • Ask about persistent, non-healing sores or scabs - this often warrants investigation for basal cell carcinoma or squamous cell carcinoma, especially in sun-exposed areas.
  • Ask about pain or tenderness associated with a lesion - while many skin cancers are asymptomatic, pain can indicate deeper invasion or ulceration.
  • Ask about rapid growth or development of a new lesion - sudden changes are concerning for aggressive malignancies like melanoma or rapidly growing SCC.
  • Ask about a lesion that bleeds easily with minor trauma - this is a common feature of ulcerated BCCs and SCCs.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Malignant/ pre-malignant skin lesions (including Bowen disease, malignant melanoma, basal cell carcinoma, squamous cell carcinoma)"][/sc]
  • Check for asymmetry, border irregularity, colour variation, diameter >6mm, and evolving nature (ABCDE) of pigmented lesions - these features strongly suggest malignant melanoma.
  • Check for pearly papules with rolled borders and telangiectasia - characteristic of basal cell carcinoma, often found on the face or neck.
  • Check for scaly, erythematous plaques or nodules, sometimes ulcerated - typical presentation of squamous cell carcinoma, particularly in sun-damaged skin.
  • Check for a solitary, well-demarcated, erythematous, scaly patch, often resembling eczema or psoriasis - indicative of Bowen disease (SCC in situ).
  • Check vital signs - typically normal in early localised disease, but fever or weight loss may suggest advanced metastatic disease.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Malignant/ pre-malignant skin lesions (including Bowen disease, malignant melanoma, basal cell carcinoma, squamous cell carcinoma)"][/sc]
  • Rapid growth or change in a lesion - particularly over weeks to months, suggests aggressive malignancy like melanoma or high-grade SCC.
  • Persistent ulceration or bleeding - especially if non-healing despite appropriate wound care, raises suspicion for BCC or SCC.
  • Satellite lesions or regional lymphadenopathy - indicates local spread or metastatic disease, requiring urgent assessment.
  • Neurological symptoms (e.g., numbness, weakness) associated with a lesion - may suggest perineural invasion, a feature of aggressive SCC.
  • Lesion in a high-risk anatomical site - such as the head and neck, lips, ears, or genitalia, which are associated with higher rates of recurrence and metastasis for SCC.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Malignant/ pre-malignant skin lesions (including Bowen disease, malignant melanoma, basal cell carcinoma, squamous cell carcinoma)"][/sc]
  • Amelanotic melanoma: Presents as a pink, red, or skin-coloured lesion, lacking typical pigmentation, making diagnosis challenging and often delayed.
  • Nodular melanoma: Can grow rapidly as a firm, dark nodule, often without the radial growth phase, leading to earlier vertical growth and poorer prognosis.
  • Immunosuppressed patients: (e.g., transplant recipients) have a significantly increased risk of aggressive SCCs, which may present atypically and metastasise more readily due to impaired immune surveillance.
  • Older adults: May present with large, neglected lesions due to reduced self-examination or delayed presentation, leading to more advanced disease at diagnosis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Malignant/ pre-malignant skin lesions (including Bowen disease, malignant melanoma, basal cell carcinoma, squamous cell carcinoma)"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected malignant/pre-malignant skin lesions: Excisional biopsy with adequate margins is the gold standard for diagnosis and often curative for primary lesions. Incisional or punch biopsy may be used for larger lesions or those in cosmetically sensitive areas.
  • Dermoscopy: A non-invasive technique using a dermatoscope to examine skin lesions, enhancing the visualisation of morphological features not visible to the naked eye, significantly improving diagnostic accuracy for melanoma and other skin cancers.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Skin scrapings for fungal culture if tinea corporis is suspected, or patch testing if contact dermatitis is a differential.
  • Imaging - Ultrasound: High-resolution ultrasound may be used to assess regional lymph nodes for metastatic spread, particularly in higher-risk melanomas or SCCs.
  • Imaging - CT/MRI/PET: Reserved for staging advanced melanoma or high-risk SCCs with suspected distant metastasis.
  • Serial reassessment: For atypical but benign-appearing lesions, photographic documentation and follow-up at 3-6 months can help monitor for changes.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Malignant/ pre-malignant skin lesions (including Bowen disease, malignant melanoma, basal cell carcinoma, squamous cell carcinoma)"][/sc]
  • Seborrhoeic keratosis - benign, 'stuck-on' appearance, often waxy or crumbly, and lacks the features of malignancy.
  • Actinic keratosis - pre-malignant, presents as a rough, scaly patch on sun-exposed skin, but is typically flat rather than nodular.
  • Pyogenic granuloma - benign vascular tumour that grows rapidly and bleeds easily, but is usually a brighter red and more friable than skin cancers.
  • Keratoacanthoma - a rapidly growing, dome-shaped tumour with a central keratin plug, which can be difficult to distinguish from SCC and is often treated as such.
  • Benign melanocytic naevus (mole) - typically stable, symmetrical, and uniform in colour, but atypical naevi can mimic melanoma.
  • Dermatofibroma - a firm, benign dermal nodule that may dimple when squeezed, but lacks the surface changes of most skin cancers.
  • Nummular eczema - presents as coin-shaped, erythematous, scaly plaques, but is usually pruritic and responds to topical steroids.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Malignant/ pre-malignant skin lesions (including Bowen disease, malignant melanoma, basal cell carcinoma, squamous cell carcinoma)"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Urgent referral to a dermatologist or skin cancer specialist for any lesion suspicious of melanoma or high-risk SCC. For suspected BCC, a routine referral is usually appropriate.
  • Definitive management: Surgical excision with appropriate margins is the mainstay of treatment for most skin cancers. Mohs micrographic surgery may be considered for lesions in cosmetically sensitive areas or with ill-defined borders. Other options for superficial lesions include cryotherapy, topical chemotherapy (e.g., 5-fluorouracil, imiquimod), and photodynamic therapy.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Regular skin self-examination and follow-up with a dermatologist are crucial, as patients with one skin cancer are at increased risk of developing others.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Advise patients on sun protection measures (sunscreen, protective clothing) to reduce the risk of future skin cancers. Any new or changing lesions should be promptly reviewed. For advanced or metastatic disease, management may involve radiotherapy, chemotherapy, or targeted therapies (e.g., BRAF inhibitors for melanoma).
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Malignant/ pre-malignant skin lesions (including Bowen disease, malignant melanoma, basal cell carcinoma, squamous cell carcinoma)"][/sc]
  • Local recurrence: Incomplete excision or aggressive tumour biology can lead to recurrence at the primary site, necessitating further surgery and potentially wider margins.
  • Metastasis: Particularly with melanoma and SCC, cancer cells can spread to regional lymph nodes or distant organs, leading to significant morbidity and mortality.
  • Disfigurement and functional impairment: Extensive surgical excisions, especially on the face, can result in cosmetic disfigurement and functional deficits (e.g., eyelid retraction, nasal obstruction).
  • Psychological impact: Diagnosis and treatment of skin cancer can cause significant anxiety, depression, and body image concerns for patients.
  • Secondary primary skin cancers: Patients with a history of one skin cancer are at increased risk of developing new primary lesions, highlighting the need for ongoing surveillance.
