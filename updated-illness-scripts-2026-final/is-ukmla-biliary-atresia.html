Biliary atresia [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Biliary atresia"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Biliary atresia"][/sc]
  • Biliary atresia is a rare, congenital condition affecting the biliary tree, characterised by progressive inflammation and fibrosis of the extrahepatic ducts, leading to cholestasis, liver damage, and ultimately liver failure.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical features of biliary atresia, understand the importance of early diagnosis and referral, and outline the principles of its management, including the Kasai portoenterostomy and liver transplantation.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Biliary atresia"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Biliary atresia"][/sc]
  • Affects approximately 1 in 17,000 live births annually in the UK, making it the most common cause of liver transplantation in children worldwide [1].
  • The aetiology is largely unknown, but is believed to be multifactorial, involving genetic predispositions, viral infections (e.g., rotavirus, reovirus type 3), immune-mediated processes, and defects in morphogenesis [2].
  • Risk factors are not clearly defined, but some associations include prematurity and certain genetic syndromes, though most cases are sporadic [2].
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Biliary atresia"][/sc]
  • Trigger: Progressive inflammatory and fibrosing process affecting the extrahepatic bile ducts, typically commencing in utero or shortly after birth.
  • Progression: This inflammation leads to obliteration of the bile ducts, impeding bile flow from the liver to the duodenum.
  • Consequence: Unrelieved cholestasis results in progressive liver damage, cirrhosis, and ultimately liver failure if not surgically corrected. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Cholestasis: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Cholestasis"][/sc]
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Biliary atresia"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Typically presents within the first few weeks of life with persistent neonatal jaundice, pale stools, and dark urine.
  • Progressive phase: If untreated, liver damage progresses rapidly, leading to hepatosplenomegaly, ascites, and failure to thrive, often becoming evident by 2-3 months of age.
  • Late phase: Without intervention, end-stage liver disease and its complications develop, necessitating liver transplantation, usually by 1-2 years of age.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Biliary atresia"][/sc]
  • Ask about persistent jaundice - particularly beyond 14 days in term infants or 21 days in preterm infants, as this is the cardinal symptom of cholestasis.
  • Ask about pale, chalky, or acholic stools - indicating a lack of bile pigments reaching the intestine, a key distinguishing feature from physiological jaundice.
  • Ask about dark urine - due to the excretion of conjugated bilirubin through the kidneys.
  • Ask about poor weight gain or failure to thrive - reflecting malabsorption of fats and fat-soluble vitamins due to impaired bile flow.
  • Ask about easy bruising or bleeding - suggesting vitamin K deficiency due to malabsorption.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Biliary atresia"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - typically normal in early stages, but may show signs of systemic illness or decompensation in advanced disease.
  • Check for jaundice - visible yellowing of the skin and sclera, often more pronounced than physiological jaundice.
  • Check for hepatomegaly - an enlarged, firm liver, often an early sign of liver fibrosis.
  • Check for splenomegaly - may develop later due to portal hypertension. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Portal hypertension: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Portal hypertension"][/sc]
  • Check for ascites - accumulation of fluid in the peritoneal cavity, indicating advanced liver disease.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Biliary atresia"][/sc]
  • Persistent jaundice: Jaundice lasting beyond 14 days in term infants or 21 days in preterm infants, necessitating urgent investigation for cholestasis.
  • Pale or acholic stools: A critical sign indicating complete or near-complete obstruction of bile flow, requiring immediate referral.
  • Dark urine: Suggests conjugated hyperbilirubinaemia, which is always pathological in neonates.
  • Hepatosplenomegaly: Indicates significant liver involvement and potential portal hypertension.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Biliary atresia"][/sc]
  • Biliary Atresia Splenic Malformation (BASM) Syndrome: A subset of patients presenting with biliary atresia alongside other congenital anomalies, particularly polysplenia, situs inversus, and cardiac defects. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Biliary Atresia Splenic Malformation Syndrome: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="BASM Syndrome"][/sc]
  • Late presentation: Infants diagnosed beyond 60 days of life often have poorer outcomes from Kasai portoenterostomy due to more advanced liver fibrosis.
  • Perinatal form: A less common variant where bile duct obliteration occurs in utero, potentially leading to earlier and more severe presentation.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Biliary atresia"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected biliary atresia:
    • Conjugated (direct) and unconjugated (indirect) bilirubin levels: Conjugated hyperbilirubinaemia (conjugated bilirubin >20% of total or >25 µmol/L) is the hallmark.
    • Liver function tests (LFTs): Elevated gamma-glutamyl transferase (GGT) is highly suggestive, along with elevated AST/ALT.
    • Abdominal ultrasound: May show an absent or abnormal gallbladder, a "triangular cord sign" (fibrotic remnant of the bile duct), and hepatic artery changes.
    • Hepatobiliary scintigraphy (HIDA scan): Non-excretion of tracer into the intestine after phenobarbital pre-treatment is highly indicative of biliary atresia.
    • Percutaneous liver biopsy: Histological features include bile duct proliferation with bile plugs, portal tract oedema, and fibrosis.
    • Operative cholangiography: The gold standard for diagnosis, directly visualising the biliary tree during surgery.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible:
    • Infectious screen (e.g., TORCH infections, sepsis workup).
    • Metabolic screen (e.g., alpha-1 antitrypsin deficiency, galactosaemia, tyrosinaemia).
    • Genetic testing for specific cholestatic disorders.
  • Severity/risk tools: No specific MDCalc score is routinely used for biliary atresia severity.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Biliary atresia"][/sc]
  • A 6-week-old infant presents with persistent jaundice, pale stools, dark urine, and hepatomegaly.
  • Neonatal cholestatic jaundice with acholic stools and hepatomegaly, concerning for biliary atresia.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Biliary atresia"][/sc]
  • Physiological jaundice - typically resolves by 14 days in term infants and does not cause acholic stools or dark urine.
  • Neonatal hepatitis - often presents with cholestasis but usually has patent bile ducts; can be viral, metabolic, or idiopathic. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Neonatal hepatitis: what is it and how does it differ from biliary atresia?" text="Neonatal hepatitis"][/sc]
  • Alpha-1 antitrypsin deficiency - a genetic metabolic disorder causing cholestasis and liver damage.
  • Progressive familial intrahepatic cholestasis (PFIC) - a group of genetic disorders affecting bile acid transport.
  • Alagille syndrome - a genetic disorder affecting multiple organ systems, including the liver (paucity of intrahepatic bile ducts).
  • Cystic fibrosis - can cause cholestasis due to inspissated bile in small bile ducts.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Biliary atresia"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Prompt referral to a specialist paediatric liver centre for urgent diagnosis and management. Supportive care includes adequate nutrition and vitamin supplementation (A, D, E, K).
  • Definitive management: The Kasai portoenterostomy (hepatoportoenterostomy) is the primary surgical intervention, aiming to re-establish bile flow by connecting a loop of intestine directly to the liver. This procedure is most effective when performed before 45-60 days of life.
  • Adjunctive management: Post-Kasai, ursodeoxycholic acid may be used to promote bile flow, and antibiotics are often prescribed prophylactically to prevent ascending cholangitis.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Lifelong follow-up with a paediatric hepatologist is essential to monitor for complications, liver function, and growth.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Liver transplantation is required if the Kasai procedure fails to establish adequate bile flow, or if progressive liver disease and its complications develop despite initial surgical success.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Biliary atresia"][/sc]
  • Ascending cholangitis: A common post-Kasai complication, characterised by fever, jaundice, and acholic stools, requiring prompt antibiotic treatment to prevent further liver damage.
  • Portal hypertension: Develops due to progressive liver fibrosis, leading to complications such as oesophageal varices, ascites, and splenomegaly.
  • Cirrhosis and liver failure: Inevitable if bile flow is not adequately established or maintained, leading to the need for liver transplantation.
  • Growth retardation and malnutrition: Resulting from chronic cholestasis and malabsorption, requiring aggressive nutritional support.
  • Hepatocellular carcinoma: A long-term risk in patients with chronic liver disease, even after successful Kasai or transplantation.
