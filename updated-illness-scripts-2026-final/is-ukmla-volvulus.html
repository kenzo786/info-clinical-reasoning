kb-accordion kb-parent"> Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Volvulus"][/sc]
  • A segment of the bowel, often the sigmoid colon or caecum, twists on its mesenteric axis, leading to mechanical obstruction.
  • Continued twisting and distension can compromise the mesenteric blood supply, causing venous congestion, arterial ischaemia, and eventually infarction of the bowel wall.
  • Bowel ischaemia and necrosis can lead to perforation, peritonitis, sepsis, and multi-organ failure if not promptly managed.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Volvulus"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Symptoms of acute bowel obstruction, such as colicky abdominal pain, distension, and absolute constipation, may develop rapidly over hours.
  • Progressive phase: As ischaemia develops, pain becomes more constant and severe, and signs of peritonism may emerge, particularly beyond 24-48 hours.
  • Late phase: Bowel necrosis and perforation can lead to rapid clinical deterioration, sepsis, and shock, often within 72 hours of symptom onset.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Volvulus"][/sc]
  • Ask about sudden onset, severe, colicky abdominal pain - this is characteristic of bowel obstruction and often precedes other symptoms.
  • Ask about abdominal distension - a prominent feature, especially in sigmoid volvulus, indicating trapped gas and fluid.
  • Ask about absolute constipation and inability to pass flatus - a key indicator of complete bowel obstruction.
  • Ask about nausea and vomiting - often bilious, reflecting proximal obstruction and retrograde peristalsis.
  • Ask about recent changes in bowel habits or chronic constipation - important risk factors, particularly for sigmoid volvulus.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Volvulus"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - tachycardia, hypotension, and fever may indicate dehydration, sepsis, or peritonitis.
  • Check for marked abdominal distension - often asymmetrical and tympanitic, particularly in sigmoid volvulus (e.g., 'coffee bean' sign on imaging).
  • Check for tenderness and guarding on palpation - suggests peritonitis, indicating bowel ischaemia or perforation.
  • Check bowel sounds - initially hyperactive, then diminished or absent with progressive obstruction or ischaemia.
  • Check for signs of dehydration - dry mucous membranes, reduced skin turgor, sunken eyes.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Volvulus"][/sc]
  • Peritonism - indicates bowel ischaemia, necrosis, or perforation, requiring immediate surgical intervention.
  • Haemodynamic instability - suggests sepsis or severe dehydration, necessitating urgent resuscitation and surgical review.
  • Rectal bleeding - may indicate mucosal ischaemia or necrosis, particularly in the context of obstruction.
  • Rapidly worsening abdominal pain - suggests progression from simple obstruction to complicated volvulus with ischaemia.
  • Failure to pass flatus or stool for >24 hours with distension - highly suggestive of complete bowel obstruction.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Volvulus"][/sc]
  • Older adults: May present with less acute pain, more insidious onset of constipation and distension, or altered mental status, making diagnosis challenging.
  • Pregnant women: Symptoms can be masked by physiological changes of pregnancy; caecal volvulus is more common, often presenting in the third trimester.
  • Recurrent volvulus: Patients may have a history of intermittent, self-resolving episodes of abdominal pain and distension, which can delay definitive diagnosis.
  • Children (midgut volvulus): Typically presents in neonates with bilious vomiting, abdominal distension, and signs of shock, a surgical emergency.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Volvulus"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected volvulus: Abdominal X-ray (AXR) may show a dilated, air-filled loop of bowel (e.g., 'coffee bean' sign for sigmoid, 'kidney bean' sign for caecal) and absence of gas in the distal colon.
  • Imaging - CT abdomen and pelvis with intravenous contrast: The gold standard for diagnosis, demonstrating the twisted mesentery ('whirl sign'), point of obstruction, and assessing for ischaemia or perforation.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Blood tests including full blood count (leukocytosis), electrolytes (dehydration), C-reactive protein (inflammation), and lactate (ischaemia) are essential.
  • Serial reassessment: Regular clinical examination and repeat lactate measurements are crucial to monitor for progression of ischaemia and guide management.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Volvulus"][/sc]
  • A 78-year-old man with a history of chronic constipation presents with sudden onset, severe, colicky abdominal pain, marked distension, and absolute constipation for 24 hours. On examination, he is tachycardic with a tympanitic, tender abdomen.
  • An older adult with acute, severe, obstructive abdominal pain and distension, suggesting a closed-loop obstruction with potential for ischaemia.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Volvulus"][/sc]
  • Large bowel obstruction (e.g., colorectal cancer) - typically more gradual onset, often with a history of altered bowel habits and weight loss.
  • Small bowel obstruction (e.g., adhesions) - usually presents with more prominent vomiting and less abdominal distension, and often a history of previous abdominal surgery.
  • Acute diverticulitis - localised pain, fever, and inflammatory markers, but typically without the severe distension and absolute constipation of volvulus.
  • Pseudo-obstruction (Ogilvie's syndrome) - massive colonic dilatation without mechanical obstruction, often in critically ill or post-surgical patients.
  • Acute appendicitis (for caecal volvulus) - localised right iliac fossa pain, but typically without the marked distension and obstructive symptoms.
  • Mesenteric ischaemia - severe abdominal pain out of proportion to physical findings, often with risk factors for atherosclerosis, but without mechanical obstruction.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Volvulus"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Resuscitation with intravenous fluids, analgesia, and broad-spectrum antibiotics if signs of sepsis or peritonitis are present. Urgent surgical consultation is paramount.
  • Definitive management: For sigmoid volvulus, endoscopic decompression (detorsion) with a flexible sigmoidoscope may be attempted. If successful, elective sigmoid colectomy is often recommended to prevent recurrence. If endoscopic decompression fails, or if there are signs of ischaemia/perforation, emergency laparotomy with resection of the affected bowel and primary anastomosis or formation of a stoma is required. For caecal volvulus, emergency laparotomy with right hemicolectomy or caecopexy is typically performed.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Close monitoring of vital signs, urine output, and abdominal examination for signs of deterioration. Post-operatively, monitor for complications such as anastomotic leak, infection, and recurrence.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Any signs of peritonism, haemodynamic instability, or worsening abdominal pain warrant immediate re-evaluation and escalation to a senior surgical team.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Volvulus"][/sc]
  • Bowel ischaemia and necrosis: Due to compromised blood supply from twisting, leading to irreversible damage to the bowel wall and requiring urgent resection.
  • Bowel perforation: A consequence of necrosis, leading to faecal peritonitis, severe sepsis, and high mortality.
  • Peritonitis: Inflammation of the peritoneum, either sterile (from ischaemia) or septic (from perforation), causing severe abdominal pain and systemic inflammatory response.
  • Sepsis and septic shock: Systemic inflammatory response to infection, particularly following perforation, leading to multi-organ dysfunction and high mortality.
  • Recurrence: Especially common after endoscopic decompression of sigmoid volvulus if definitive surgical correction is not performed, highlighting the need for elective surgery.
  • Short bowel syndrome: A long-term complication if extensive bowel resection is required, leading to malabsorption and nutritional deficiencies.
