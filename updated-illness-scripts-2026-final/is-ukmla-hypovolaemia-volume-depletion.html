Hypovolaemia/ volume depletion [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Hypovolaemia/ volume depletion"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Hypovolaemia/ volume depletion"][/sc]
  • Hypovolaemia, or volume depletion, refers to a reduction in the volume of extracellular fluid, primarily affecting the intravascular compartment, often due to fluid loss from the body.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Understand the causes, clinical features, and initial management of hypovolaemia and dehydration.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Hypovolaemia/ volume depletion"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Hypovolaemia/ volume depletion"][/sc]
  • In the UK, acute gastroenteritis is a common cause, particularly in children and the elderly, leading to significant fluid loss.
  • Hospitalised patients, especially those undergoing surgery or with chronic conditions, are at a higher risk of developing iatrogenic hypovolaemia.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Hypovolaemia/ volume depletion"][/sc]
  • Fluid loss (e.g., vomiting, diarrhoea, haemorrhage) triggers a decrease in circulating blood volume, leading to reduced venous return and cardiac output.
  • The body attempts to compensate through sympathetic activation, increasing heart rate and peripheral vasoconstriction, and activation of the renin-angiotensin-aldosterone system to conserve sodium and water.
  • Persistent hypovolaemia can lead to inadequate tissue perfusion, cellular hypoxia, and ultimately organ dysfunction, potentially progressing to hypovolaemic shock.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Hypovolaemia/ volume depletion"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Initial compensatory mechanisms are activated, with subtle clinical signs such as mild tachycardia or postural hypotension.
  • Progressive phase: As fluid loss continues, compensatory mechanisms become overwhelmed, leading to more overt signs like reduced urine output, increased thirst, and cool peripheries.
  • Late phase: Severe hypovolaemia results in significant organ hypoperfusion, manifesting as altered mental status, profound hypotension, and metabolic acidosis, indicating impending shock.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Hypovolaemia/ volume depletion"][/sc]
  • Ask about thirst - indicates the body's attempt to increase fluid intake.
  • Ask about dizziness or light-headedness - suggests orthostatic hypotension due to reduced cerebral perfusion.
  • Ask about reduced urine output - a key indicator of renal hypoperfusion and fluid conservation.
  • Ask about fatigue or weakness - reflects generalised tissue hypoperfusion and metabolic derangements.
  • Ask about nausea or vomiting - can be both a cause and a symptom of fluid imbalance.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Hypovolaemia/ volume depletion"][/sc]
  • Check heart rate - tachycardia is an early compensatory sign.
  • Check blood pressure - hypotension, particularly postural, is a significant indicator of volume depletion.
  • Check capillary refill time - prolonged capillary refill suggests poor peripheral perfusion.
  • Check skin turgor and mucous membranes - reduced turgor and dry membranes indicate interstitial fluid loss.
  • Check urine output - oliguria or anuria is a critical sign of renal hypoperfusion.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Hypovolaemia/ volume depletion"][/sc]
  • Altered mental status - indicates severe cerebral hypoperfusion and requires urgent intervention.
  • Profound hypotension (systolic BP <90 mmHg) - suggests decompensated shock and imminent organ failure.
  • Anuria - signifies severe renal hypoperfusion and potential acute kidney injury.
  • Severe tachycardia (>120 bpm) - a sign of significant compensatory effort that may be unsustainable.
  • Evidence of active haemorrhage - demands immediate identification and control of the bleeding source.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Hypovolaemia/ volume depletion"][/sc]
  • Elderly patients: May present with confusion or falls rather than classic thirst, and their compensatory mechanisms can be blunted.
  • Patients on beta-blockers: May not exhibit the expected tachycardia despite significant volume depletion.
  • Patients with chronic kidney disease: May have altered fluid and electrolyte regulation, making assessment of volume status more challenging.
  • Athletes: Can develop exercise-induced hypovolaemia, often masked by high baseline fitness levels.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Hypovolaemia/ volume depletion"][/sc]
  • Core:
    • Full blood count (FBC) - may show elevated haemoglobin and haematocrit due to haemoconcentration (unless haemorrhage is the cause).
    • Urea and electrolytes (U&Es) - elevated urea and creatinine, with a disproportionately high urea:creatinine ratio, suggest pre-renal acute kidney injury.
    • Arterial blood gas (ABG) - may reveal metabolic acidosis.
    • Urinalysis - typically shows high urine specific gravity and low urinary sodium.
  • Imaging:
    • Point-of-care ultrasound (POCUS) - can assess inferior vena cava (IVC) collapsibility as an indicator of volume status.
  • If alternative diagnosis considered:
    • Echocardiography - to assess cardiac function and rule out cardiogenic shock.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Hypovolaemia/ volume depletion"][/sc]
  • A previously well 68-year-old male presents with three days of profuse watery diarrhoea, now complaining of severe thirst, dizziness on standing, and has not passed urine for 12 hours.
  • An elderly male with acute onset, significant fluid loss, manifesting with systemic signs of reduced perfusion and renal compromise.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Hypovolaemia/ volume depletion"][/sc]
  • Sepsis - often presents with hypotension and tachycardia, but typically accompanied by fever and signs of infection.
  • Cardiogenic shock - characterised by signs of fluid overload (e.g., pulmonary oedema, raised JVP) despite hypotension.
  • Anaphylaxis - acute onset, often with rash, bronchospasm, and angioedema.
  • Adrenal insufficiency - can cause hypovolaemia, hypotension, and electrolyte disturbances (hyponatraemia, hyperkalaemia).
  • Diabetic ketoacidosis (DKA) / Hyperosmolar Hyperglycaemic State (HHS) - severe dehydration with hyperglycaemia and metabolic acidosis (DKA).
  • Neurogenic shock - typically associated with spinal cord injury, presenting with hypotension and bradycardia.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Hypovolaemia/ volume depletion"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities:
    • Secure airway, support breathing, and optimise circulation (ABCDE approach).
    • Establish intravenous access and commence fluid resuscitation with crystalloids (e.g., 0.9% sodium chloride) in boluses, guided by clinical response.
    • Identify and address the underlying cause of fluid loss (e.g., antiemetics for vomiting, stopping diuretics).
  • Definitive management:
    • Continue fluid replacement, adjusting rate and volume based on ongoing losses and clinical parameters (e.g., urine output, blood pressure, heart rate).
    • Consider blood product transfusion if significant haemorrhage is the cause.
    • Address electrolyte imbalances that may accompany fluid depletion.
  • Monitoring and follow-up:
    • Frequent monitoring of vital signs, urine output, and fluid balance charts.
    • Regular assessment of U&Es and other relevant blood tests to guide fluid and electrolyte management.
  • Escalation and safety-netting:
    • Escalate to senior medical staff if there is inadequate response to initial fluid resuscitation or signs of worsening organ dysfunction.
    • Advise patients on symptoms of worsening dehydration and when to seek further medical attention.
  • Pharmacology reference:
    • Intravenous fluids (e.g., 0.9% sodium chloride, Hartmann's solution) are used for volume expansion. No doses or frequencies are cited here — always verify against your local antimicrobial/prescribing formulary before prescribing.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Hypovolaemia/ volume depletion"][/sc]
  • Acute kidney injury (AKI): Reduced renal perfusion can lead to tubular damage and impaired kidney function, which often warrants prompt fluid restoration.
  • Hypovolaemic shock: Severe, uncorrected volume depletion can result in circulatory collapse, leading to multi-organ failure and death.
  • Electrolyte imbalances: Fluid losses often involve electrolytes, leading to hyponatraemia, hypernatraemia, or hypokalaemia, which may support specific fluid choices.
  • Thromboembolic events: Haemoconcentration due to dehydration can increase blood viscosity and the risk of deep vein thrombosis or pulmonary embolism.
  • Cardiac arrhythmias: Electrolyte disturbances and myocardial ischaemia secondary to hypovolaemia can precipitate arrhythmias.
