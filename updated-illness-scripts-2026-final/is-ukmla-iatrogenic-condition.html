Iatrogenic condition [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Iatrogenic condition"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Iatrogenic condition"][/sc]
  • An iatrogenic condition is any adverse outcome or complication that results directly from medical intervention, diagnostic procedures, or treatment. This can include diseases, symptoms, or other harmful effects caused by healthcare professionals or medical care.
  • UKMLA Learning Outcome: Recognise and manage common iatrogenic conditions, understanding their causes, prevention, and appropriate responses.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Iatrogenic condition"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Iatrogenic condition"][/sc]
  • In England, 6.1% of births are preterm, with 52.8% of these being iatrogenic, particularly increasing after 32 weeks gestation [1].
  • Approximately 5% of the 8.5 million patients admitted to hospitals in England and Wales annually experience preventable adverse events [2].
  • Risk factors for iatrogenic conditions often include diagnostic overshadowing, inadequate physical health assessment, poor risk management, and failure to respond to clinical deterioration.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Iatrogenic condition"][/sc]
  • Trigger: Iatrogenic conditions are typically triggered by medical interventions, such as diagnostic procedures, surgical operations, pharmacological treatments, or even misdiagnosis. These interventions, while intended to be therapeutic, can inadvertently initiate a cascade of adverse effects.
  • Progression: The progression often involves an initial insult (e.g., drug side effect, surgical complication, healthcare-associated infection) that, if not recognised or managed effectively, can lead to further deterioration, organ damage, or systemic compromise. This can be exacerbated by patient vulnerability or comorbidities.
  • Consequence: The consequences range from mild, transient symptoms to severe, life-threatening complications, prolonged hospital stays, increased morbidity and mortality, and significant psychological distress for the patient. Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Iatrogenic condition"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Acute onset of symptoms or complications, often within hours to days of the medical intervention. This might include immediate adverse drug reactions, post-procedural pain or infection, or acute organ dysfunction.
  • Progressive phase: If the iatrogenic harm is not recognised or managed, it may worsen over days to weeks. This could involve escalating infection, organ failure progression, or the development of secondary complications such as deep vein thrombosis or pulmonary embolism.
  • Late phase: Chronic or long-term consequences can manifest weeks, months, or even years after the initial event. Examples include chronic pain syndromes, permanent organ damage, disability, psychological distress, or the need for further corrective surgeries.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Iatrogenic condition"][/sc]
  • Ask about new or worsening pain - to identify post-procedural complications, nerve damage, or inflammatory responses.
  • Ask about nausea, vomiting, or changes in bowel habits - to assess for adverse drug reactions, gastrointestinal injury, or infection.
  • Ask about fatigue or malaise - to indicate systemic infection, inflammation, or metabolic disturbances secondary to treatment.
  • Ask about dizziness or lightheadedness - to evaluate for orthostatic hypotension, electrolyte imbalances, or neurological side effects of medication.
  • Ask about skin rashes or itching - to identify allergic reactions to drugs or contact dermatitis from medical devices.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Iatrogenic condition"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs (temperature, pulse, blood pressure, respiratory rate) - to identify systemic inflammatory response syndrome (SIRS), sepsis, haemorrhage, or adverse drug effects.
  • Check surgical sites or injection sites for localised signs of infection (erythema, warmth, swelling, purulent discharge) - to detect wound infection or cellulitis.
  • Check neurological status (level of consciousness, focal deficits) - to assess for drug-induced encephalopathy, stroke, or nerve damage.
  • Check skin for rashes, petechiae, or ecchymoses - to identify allergic reactions, drug-induced coagulopathy, or vasculitis.
  • Check for new oedema or fluid overload - to evaluate for renal dysfunction, cardiac compromise, or medication side effects.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Iatrogenic condition"][/sc]
  • Sudden neurological deficit: Indicates potential stroke, nerve damage, or severe drug toxicity requiring urgent assessment.
  • Haemodynamic instability: Suggests significant haemorrhage, sepsis, anaphylaxis, or cardiac compromise demanding immediate resuscitation.
  • Acute kidney injury: May result from nephrotoxic drugs, hypovolaemia, or obstruction, necessitating prompt investigation and management to prevent irreversible damage.
  • Signs of sepsis: Fever, tachycardia, tachypnoea, and altered mental status in a patient post-intervention require urgent sepsis six pathway activation.
  • Anaphylaxis: Rapid onset of rash, angioedema, bronchospasm, or hypotension post-drug administration is a medical emergency.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Iatrogenic condition"][/sc]
  • Older adults: Often present with non-specific symptoms such as confusion, falls, or functional decline, rather than classic signs of organ-specific pathology, due to altered physiological reserves and polypharmacy.
  • Children: May exhibit subtle signs or behavioural changes that are difficult to interpret, as they may not be able to articulate their symptoms clearly. Adverse drug reactions can also manifest differently due to developmental differences.
  • Patients with multiple comorbidities: The presentation of an iatrogenic condition can be masked or complicated by existing illnesses, making diagnosis challenging and delaying appropriate management.
  • Immunocompromised patients: May develop severe or unusual infections following medical procedures or immunosuppressive therapies, with attenuated inflammatory responses.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Iatrogenic condition"][/sc]
  • Core investigations:
    • Full blood count (FBC): To assess for anaemia (e.g., from haemorrhage), infection (leukocytosis), or drug-induced cytopenias.
    • Urea and electrolytes (U&Es): To check renal function (e.g., nephrotoxic drugs, acute kidney injury) and electrolyte imbalances.
    • Liver function tests (LFTs): To evaluate for drug-induced liver injury.
    • C-reactive protein (CRP) and erythrocyte sedimentation rate (ESR): Inflammatory markers to detect infection or systemic inflammation.
    • Blood cultures: If infection is suspected, particularly in febrile patients post-procedure.
  • Imaging:
    • Chest X-ray: To assess for pulmonary complications (e.g., aspiration, pulmonary oedema, pneumothorax).
    • Ultrasound, CT, or MRI: Depending on the suspected organ system involved (e.g., abdominal pain post-surgery, neurological deficits).
  • If alternative diagnoses are considered: Further specific tests may be warranted based on the clinical picture.
  • Serial reassessment: Regular monitoring of vital signs, clinical status, and relevant blood tests to track progression or response to intervention.
  • Relevant Scores: Consider using scores like HAS-BLED Score on MDCalc for assessing bleeding risk if anticoagulation is involved.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Iatrogenic condition"][/sc]
  • A patient presenting with new onset symptoms or complications following a recent medical intervention, such as surgery, medication administration, or diagnostic procedure.
  • An acute or subacute adverse event, potentially life-threatening, occurring in a hospitalised or recently treated patient, often characterised by systemic inflammation, organ dysfunction, or localised complications.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Iatrogenic condition"][/sc]
  • Progression of underlying disease: Symptoms are consistent with the natural history or expected complications of the patient\'s primary illness, rather than a new event related to intervention.
  • New, unrelated pathology: The development of a completely separate medical condition that coincidentally arises during or after medical care, with symptoms not directly linked to the intervention.
  • Expected adverse drug reaction: Recognised and documented side effects of a medication, which are anticipated and not necessarily indicative of an error or unexpected harm.
  • Nosocomial infection: An infection acquired in a healthcare setting, which, while often iatrogenic, has specific microbiological and epidemiological features distinguishing it from other forms of iatrogenic harm.
  • Psychosomatic illness or health anxiety: Symptoms that are genuinely experienced by the patient but lack an identifiable organic cause, potentially exacerbated by the stress of medical treatment or fear of complications.
  • Missed diagnosis or delayed diagnosis: The patient\'s symptoms are due to an existing condition that was not correctly identified or treated in a timely manner, rather than a new problem caused by intervention.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Iatrogenic condition"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities:
    • Stop the offending agent or intervention immediately if identified and safe to do so.
    • Ensure patient safety and stabilise vital signs (e.g., ABCDE approach for acute deterioration).
    • Provide supportive care (e.g., oxygen, intravenous fluids, analgesia).
    • Alert senior medical staff and relevant specialists.
  • Definitive management:
    • Specific treatment will depend on the nature of the iatrogenic condition (e.g., antidote for drug toxicity, surgical repair for perforation, antibiotics for infection).
    • Consider reversal agents if appropriate (e.g., for anticoagulants).
    • Manage complications as they arise.
  • Monitoring and follow-up:
    • Close observation of clinical status, vital signs, and relevant laboratory parameters.
    • Regular reassessment of the patient\'s condition and response to treatment.
    • Long-term follow-up may be required for chronic iatrogenic conditions or those with lasting sequelae.
  • Escalation and safety-netting:
    • Establish clear criteria for escalating care (e.g., to critical care).
    • Provide patients and their families with clear information on warning signs and when to seek further medical attention.
    • Ensure robust communication within the healthcare team.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Iatrogenic condition"][/sc]
  • Organ damage: Direct injury to organs (e.g., kidney injury from nephrotoxic drugs, liver damage from hepatotoxic agents, nerve damage from surgical trauma) leading to acute or chronic dysfunction.
  • Infection: Healthcare-associated infections (e.g., surgical site infections, catheter-related bloodstream infections, pneumonia) due to breaches in aseptic technique or compromised host defences, leading to prolonged illness and increased mortality.
  • Haemorrhage: Unintended bleeding during or after procedures, or due to anticoagulant therapy, which can lead to anaemia, hypovolaemic shock, and necessitate transfusions or further surgical intervention.
  • Thromboembolism: Formation of blood clots (e.g., deep vein thrombosis, pulmonary embolism) often linked to immobility post-surgery, vascular injury, or procoagulant effects of certain medications, posing a risk of life-threatening events.
  • Psychological distress: Patients may experience anxiety, depression, or post-traumatic stress disorder following iatrogenic harm, impacting their quality of life and recovery.
  • Chronic pain syndromes: Persistent pain resulting from nerve damage, surgical complications, or prolonged inflammation, significantly affecting daily function and requiring long-term management.
