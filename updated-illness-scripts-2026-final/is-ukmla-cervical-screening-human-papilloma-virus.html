Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Cervical screening (human papilloma virus)"][/sc]
  • Cervical screening is a public health programme designed to prevent cervical cancer by detecting and treating pre-cancerous changes in the cervix, primarily caused by persistent high-risk human papillomavirus (HPV) infection.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the role of cervical screening in preventing cervical cancer and the significance of HPV in its aetiology.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Cervical screening (human papilloma virus)"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Cervical screening (human papilloma virus)"][/sc]
  • In the UK, all women and people with a cervix aged 25 to 64 are invited for cervical screening, with screening intervals of 3 years for those aged 25-49 and 5 years for those aged 50-64 [2].
  • HPV infection is extremely common, with most sexually active individuals acquiring HPV at some point in their lives; however, persistent infection with high-risk HPV types (e.g., HPV16, HPV18) is responsible for over 99% of cervical cancers [1].
  • Risk factors for persistent high-risk HPV infection and subsequent cervical abnormalities include early age of first sexual intercourse, multiple sexual partners, immunosuppression (e.g., HIV infection, organ transplant recipients), and smoking.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Cervical screening (human papilloma virus)"][/sc]
  • Persistent infection with high-risk HPV types, typically acquired through sexual contact, leads to viral integration into host basal epithelial cells of the cervix, particularly at the squamocolumnar junction.
  • Viral oncoproteins E6 and E7 interfere with host cell cycle regulation (e.g., inactivating tumour suppressor genes p53 and Rb), promoting uncontrolled cell proliferation and genomic instability, leading to the development of cervical intraepithelial neoplasia (CIN).
  • Progression from low-grade CIN to high-grade CIN and eventually invasive cervical cancer occurs over several years, driven by cumulative genetic alterations and the evasion of immune surveillance.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Cervical screening (human papilloma virus)"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Initial HPV infection is often asymptomatic and transient, with most infections clearing spontaneously within 1-2 years.
  • Progressive phase: Persistent infection with high-risk HPV can lead to the development of low-grade cervical intraepithelial neoplasia (CIN1) over months to a few years, particularly beyond 1 year of persistent infection.
  • Late phase: Progression from high-grade CIN (CIN2/CIN3) to invasive cervical cancer typically takes 10-20 years, particularly beyond 5 years of high-grade changes, highlighting the long window for screening intervention.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Cervical screening (human papilloma virus)"][/sc]
  • Ask about abnormal vaginal bleeding - post-coital bleeding, intermenstrual bleeding, or post-menopausal bleeding can indicate cervical pathology, including advanced pre-cancerous changes or cancer.
  • Ask about unusual vaginal discharge - persistent, watery, bloody, or foul-smelling discharge may suggest infection, inflammation, or malignancy.
  • Ask about pelvic pain or discomfort - while often non-specific, persistent or worsening pelvic pain can be a symptom of more advanced cervical cancer.
  • Ask about pain during sexual intercourse (dyspareunia) - can be associated with cervical inflammation or lesions.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Cervical screening (human papilloma virus)"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - typically normal in early stages; abnormal findings (e.g., anaemia, weight loss) may suggest advanced disease or systemic involvement.
  • Check speculum examination - visual inspection of the cervix may reveal abnormal appearances such as erythema, ulceration, friability, or visible lesions, though early changes are often macroscopic.
  • Check bimanual palpation - may detect cervical tenderness, uterine enlargement, or adnexal masses, which could indicate local spread of disease.
  • Check lymphadenopathy - palpable inguinal or supraclavicular lymph nodes can indicate metastatic spread of cervical cancer.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Cervical screening (human papilloma virus)"][/sc]
  • Persistent abnormal vaginal bleeding: particularly post-coital, intermenstrual, or post-menopausal bleeding, as this is the most common symptom of cervical cancer and warrants urgent investigation.
  • Visible cervical lesion on speculum examination: any suspicious mass, ulceration, or friable area on the cervix requires immediate referral for colposcopy and biopsy.
  • Unexplained weight loss or fatigue: systemic symptoms can indicate advanced malignancy and metastatic disease.
  • Unilateral leg swelling with pelvic pain: may suggest deep vein thrombosis secondary to pelvic mass compression or direct tumour invasion.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Cervical screening (human papilloma virus)"][/sc]
  • Older adults: May present with vague symptoms or be less likely to attend screening, leading to diagnosis at a more advanced stage. Post-menopausal bleeding should always be investigated.
  • Immunosuppressed individuals (e.g., HIV, transplant recipients): Higher risk of persistent HPV infection and more rapid progression to high-grade lesions and cancer, often requiring more frequent screening.
  • Pregnancy: Cervical changes can be physiological, but abnormal bleeding or suspicious lesions require careful assessment, often with colposcopy, to differentiate from pathology.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Cervical screening (human papilloma virus)"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected cervical pathology:
    • Cervical screening (HPV primary screening with cytology triage): Detects high-risk HPV and identifies abnormal cervical cells, guiding referral for colposcopy.
    • Colposcopy with directed biopsies: Visual examination of the cervix with magnification, allowing targeted biopsies of abnormal areas for histological diagnosis.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible:
    • Full blood count: To assess for anaemia in cases of significant bleeding.
    • Inflammatory markers (CRP, ESR): May be elevated in infection or advanced malignancy.
  • Imaging - Pelvic MRI: For staging confirmed cervical cancer, assessing local tumour extent, parametrial invasion, and lymph node involvement.
  • Serial reassessment: For low-grade abnormalities (e.g., CIN1) or borderline changes, active surveillance with repeat HPV testing and/or cytology may be employed to monitor for regression or progression.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Cervical screening (human papilloma virus)"][/sc]
  • A 32-year-old female presents with recurrent post-coital bleeding and an abnormal cervical screening result showing high-risk HPV and low-grade dyskaryosis.
  • A young adult female with persistent abnormal vaginal bleeding and a history of high-risk HPV infection, indicating potential cervical intraepithelial neoplasia requiring further evaluation.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Cervical screening (human papilloma virus)"][/sc]
  • Cervical ectropion - common benign condition where columnar epithelium extends onto the ectocervix, often causing post-coital bleeding, but no dyskaryosis on cytology.
  • Cervicitis - inflammation of the cervix, often due to infection (e.g., Chlamydia, Gonorrhoea), causing discharge and bleeding, but typically resolves with treatment and no persistent HPV.
  • Vaginal atrophy - thinning and drying of vaginal tissues, particularly post-menopause, leading to bleeding and discomfort, but distinct from cervical pathology.
  • Endometrial pathology (e.g., polyps, hyperplasia, cancer) - can cause abnormal uterine bleeding, but typically originates from the uterine lining rather than the cervix.
  • Vaginal cancer - rare, but can present with similar symptoms to cervical cancer, requiring careful examination and biopsy to differentiate.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Cervical screening (human papilloma virus)"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Referral for colposcopy following abnormal screening results or suspicious symptoms to establish a definitive diagnosis and assess the grade of cervical intraepithelial neoplasia (CIN).
  • Definitive management:
    • Conservative management: For low-grade CIN (CIN1), active surveillance with repeat HPV testing and/or cytology is often appropriate, as many lesions regress spontaneously.
    • Excisional treatment: For high-grade CIN (CIN2/CIN3), loop electrosurgical excision procedure (LEEP) or cone biopsy is typically performed to remove the abnormal tissue and prevent progression to cancer.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Regular post-treatment surveillance with HPV testing and cytology is crucial to detect recurrence or new lesions.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Patients should be advised to seek medical attention for any persistent or worsening symptoms, particularly abnormal bleeding, despite treatment.
  • Exam pharmacology reference (UKMLA/MRCGP):
    • HPV vaccination: Primary prevention against high-risk HPV types, significantly reducing the incidence of cervical cancer and pre-cancerous lesions.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Cervical screening (human papilloma virus)"][/sc]
  • Progression to invasive cervical cancer: Untreated or undetected high-grade CIN can progress to invasive cancer, leading to significant morbidity and mortality if not managed promptly.
  • Recurrence of CIN: Even after excisional treatment, there is a risk of recurrent CIN, necessitating ongoing surveillance.
  • Psychological distress: An abnormal screening result or diagnosis of CIN can cause anxiety and distress, impacting quality of life.
  • Complications of treatment: Procedures like LEEP or cone biopsy can lead to complications such as bleeding, infection, cervical stenosis, or preterm birth in future pregnancies.
