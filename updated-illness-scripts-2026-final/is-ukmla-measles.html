Measles [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Measles"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Measles"][/sc]
  • Measles is a highly contagious viral infection caused by a morbillivirus of the paramyxovirus family, characterised by a prodrome of fever, cough, coryza, conjunctivitis, and pathognomonic Koplik spots, followed by a maculopapular rash.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical presentation of measles, understand its highly infectious nature, and initiate appropriate public health measures including notification and vaccination advice.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Measles"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Measles"][/sc]
  • Measles is highly transmissible, with a basic reproduction number (R0) of 12-18 in unvaccinated populations, making it one of the most contagious infectious diseases.
  • In England, there were 959 laboratory-confirmed measles cases in 2025, and 2,911 cases in 2024, representing the highest annual numbers in decades, predominantly affecting children aged 10 years and under.
  • Risk factors include unvaccinated status, travel to endemic areas, and close contact with infected individuals, particularly in crowded settings.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Measles"][/sc]
  • Infection begins with inhalation of airborne droplets containing the measles virus, which replicates in the respiratory tract epithelium and regional lymph nodes.
  • Viral dissemination occurs via the bloodstream (primary viraemia) to the reticuloendothelial system, followed by secondary viraemia leading to widespread infection of epithelial cells, including skin and mucous membranes.
  • The characteristic rash and symptoms result from immune complex deposition and T-cell mediated immune response against infected endothelial cells, alongside transient immunosuppression that increases susceptibility to secondary infections. Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Measles"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Following an incubation period of approximately 10-12 days, the prodromal phase begins, lasting 2-4 days, characterised by fever, malaise, and the classic triad of cough, coryza, and conjunctivitis.
  • Progressive phase: Koplik spots typically appear 1-2 days before the rash and persist for 1-2 days after its onset. The maculopapular rash emerges 3-5 days after symptom onset, starting on the face and behind the ears, then spreading centrifugally to the trunk and extremities over 2-3 days.
  • Late phase: The rash fades in the same order it appeared, resolving over 5-6 days, often with fine desquamation. Clinical recovery usually occurs within 7-10 days of rash onset, though cough may persist for up to 2 weeks.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Measles"][/sc]
  • Ask about fever - often high-grade (≥39°C) and preceding the rash, indicating systemic viral infection.
  • Ask about cough, coryza (runny nose), and conjunctivitis (red, watery eyes) - the classic prodromal triad, highly suggestive of measles before rash onset.
  • Ask about malaise and irritability - non-specific symptoms reflecting systemic illness, particularly important in young children.
  • Ask about photophobia - a common symptom due to conjunctivitis, which can be severe.
  • Ask about rash progression - a maculopapular rash appearing on the face and spreading downwards, differentiating it from other exanthems.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Measles"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - particularly temperature (often high), and respiratory rate (may be elevated with respiratory complications).
  • Check oral mucosa for Koplik spots - pathognomonic bluish-white spots on an erythematous base, typically opposite the molars, appearing 1-2 days before the rash. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Koplik spots: what are they and why do they matter clinically?" text="Koplik spots"][/sc]
  • Check skin for maculopapular rash - erythematous, non-itchy, blanching rash that becomes confluent, starting on the face/behind ears and spreading centrifugally.
  • Check conjunctivae - for injection and lacrimation, consistent with conjunctivitis.
  • Check lymph nodes - cervical lymphadenopathy may be present.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Measles"][/sc]
  • Respiratory distress - indicates severe pneumonia, a common and potentially fatal complication, particularly in young children or immunocompromised individuals.
  • Altered consciousness or seizures - suggests encephalitis, a rare but severe neurological complication that can lead to permanent neurological damage or death.
  • Haemorrhagic rash or bleeding - may indicate severe thrombocytopenia or disseminated intravascular coagulation (DIC), associated with severe disease.
  • Persistent high fever beyond rash onset - can signal secondary bacterial infections, such as otitis media or pneumonia, or severe measles.
  • Dehydration - due to poor oral intake, vomiting, or diarrhoea, requiring urgent fluid management.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Measles"][/sc]
  • Vaccinated individuals (modified measles): May present with milder symptoms, a less extensive rash, or an atypical rash distribution, making diagnosis challenging but still infectious.
  • Infants (maternal antibody protection): Very young infants may have partial protection from maternal antibodies, leading to attenuated disease, but are still vulnerable to severe infection if exposed.
  • Immunocompromised individuals: Can develop severe, progressive measles without a typical rash, or with giant cell pneumonia, and are at high risk of complications and mortality. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Measles in immunocompromised patients: how does it differ and why is it severe?" text="Measles in immunocompromised patients"][/sc]
  • Adults: Often experience more severe symptoms and a higher rate of complications compared to children, including pneumonia and hepatitis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Measles"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected measles: Oral fluid or serum samples for measles-specific IgM antibodies and PCR for measles RNA. Notification to public health is crucial. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Measles IgM and PCR testing: what do they indicate and when are they used?" text="Measles IgM and PCR testing"][/sc]
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Consider viral swabs for other respiratory viruses (e.g., influenza, adenovirus) or serology for other exanthematous diseases (e.g., rubella, parvovirus B19).
  • Imaging - Chest X-ray: Indicated if pneumonia is suspected, revealing infiltrates or consolidation.
  • Severity/risk tools: No validated severity/risk score is routinely used for measles, but clinical assessment of hydration, respiratory status, and neurological signs is paramount.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Measles"][/sc]
  • A 4-year-old unvaccinated child presents with a 3-day history of high fever, cough, coryza, conjunctivitis, and recent onset of a maculopapular rash spreading from the face, with visible Koplik spots.
  • An unvaccinated child with a febrile prodrome, characteristic rash, and pathognomonic oral lesions, concerning for measles.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Measles"][/sc]
  • Rubella: Milder prodrome, less prominent rash, and often associated with post-auricular lymphadenopathy; rash fades more quickly.
  • Parvovirus B19 (Erythema Infectiosum): Characteristic ‘slapped cheek’ rash, often with a lacy pattern on the trunk and limbs, without Koplik spots.
  • Roseola Infantum (HHV-6): High fever for 3-5 days followed by a rash as the fever breaks, typically in infants, without prodromal respiratory symptoms.
  • Kawasaki Disease: Persistent fever, polymorphous rash, conjunctivitis, oral changes, and cervical lymphadenopathy, but typically without cough or coryza, and with specific diagnostic criteria.
  • Drug Eruption: Can cause a maculopapular rash, but usually lacks the prodromal symptoms and Koplik spots of measles, and is linked to medication exposure.
  • Scarlet Fever: Fine, sandpaper-like rash, often with circumoral pallor and strawberry tongue, caused by Group A Streptococcus, and typically responds to antibiotics.
  • Enteroviral Exanthems: Diverse presentations, often with fever and rash, but usually milder and without the specific prodrome or Koplik spots of measles.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Measles"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Isolation of suspected cases to prevent further spread, notification to public health authorities, and supportive care for symptoms.
  • Definitive management: Primarily supportive, focusing on hydration, fever reduction (e.g., paracetamol), and managing complications. Vitamin A supplementation is recommended for children in areas with high measles mortality or known vitamin A deficiency.
  • Adjunctive management: Antibiotics may be considered for secondary bacterial infections such as otitis media or pneumonia.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Close monitoring for complications, particularly respiratory distress or neurological changes. Follow-up to ensure full recovery and address any persistent issues.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Advise parents/carers on red flag symptoms (e.g., worsening respiratory effort, altered consciousness) that warrant immediate medical review.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Measles"][/sc]
  • Pneumonia: A common and serious complication, occurring in 1-6% of cases, often due to secondary bacterial infection or measles giant cell pneumonia, and is a leading cause of measles-related mortality, particularly in young children.
  • Otitis Media: Occurs in 7-9% of cases, typically a bacterial superinfection, presenting during the late phase of the illness.
  • Diarrhoea: Affects approximately 8% of cases, contributing to dehydration, especially in young children.
  • Encephalitis: A rare but severe neurological complication (0.1% of cases), typically occurring 5-10 days after rash onset, leading to seizures, altered consciousness, and potential long-term neurological sequelae.
  • Subacute Sclerosing Panencephalitis (SSPE): A very rare, progressive, and fatal neurodegenerative disease that can develop years after measles infection, particularly if infection occurred in early childhood. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Subacute Sclerosing Panencephalitis (SSPE): what is it and why is it a late complication of measles?" text="Subacute Sclerosing Panencephalitis (SSPE)"][/sc]
  • Blindness: Can result from severe conjunctivitis, keratitis, or vitamin A deficiency exacerbated by measles.
