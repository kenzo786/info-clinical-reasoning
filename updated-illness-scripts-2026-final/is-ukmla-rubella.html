Rubella [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Rubella"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Rubella"][/sc]
  • Rubella, also known as German measles, is a mild, self-limiting viral infection caused by the rubella virus (a togavirus), primarily affecting the skin and lymph nodes.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical features of rubella, understand its transmission, and appreciate the significant risks of congenital rubella syndrome in pregnancy, including the importance of vaccination and public health measures.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Rubella"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Rubella"][/sc]
  • In the UK, rubella is now rare, with only 31 rubella infections in pregnancy identified through routine surveillance (0.23 per 100,000 pregnancies) in a recent period, largely in women born abroad.
  • Before the introduction of routine vaccination, rubella was common in the UK, with over 80% of infections occurring in children aged 5–9 years.
  • Risk factors include lack of vaccination (MMR), travel to endemic areas, and close contact with an infected individual.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Rubella"][/sc]
  • Trigger: Infection with the rubella virus, typically via respiratory droplets from an infected individual, leading to initial replication in the nasopharynx and regional lymph nodes.
  • Progression: Following initial replication, viraemia disseminates the virus throughout the body, resulting in systemic symptoms such as rash, lymphadenopathy, and low-grade fever.
  • Consequence: In most cases, the infection is mild and self-limiting; however, maternal infection during early pregnancy can lead to transplacental transmission and severe foetal damage, known as [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Congenital Rubella Syndrome: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Congenital Rubella Syndrome"].
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Rubella"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Incubation period typically 14–21 days (range 12–23 days) post-exposure, often asymptomatic, followed by a mild prodrome of low-grade fever, malaise, and lymphadenopathy.
  • Progressive phase: Rash onset, usually 1–5 days after prodrome, starting on the face and spreading centrifugally to the trunk and limbs, lasting approximately 3 days.
  • Late phase: Resolution of rash and systemic symptoms within 7–10 days; however, arthralgia or arthritis may persist for weeks in adult women, and complications such as congenital rubella syndrome manifest during foetal development if maternal infection occurs in early pregnancy.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Rubella"][/sc]
  • Ask about rash - typically a fine, pink or red maculopapular rash that starts on the face and spreads downwards, distinguishing it from measles (darker, confluent rash).
  • Ask about low-grade fever - often mild and transient, indicating a less severe systemic response compared to other exanthematous illnesses.
  • Ask about lymphadenopathy - particularly post-auricular, posterior cervical, and suboccipital nodes, which are characteristic and often precede the rash.
  • Ask about arthralgia or arthritis - common in adult women, affecting small joints of hands, wrists, and knees, which can be a significant source of discomfort.
  • Ask about conjunctivitis - mild, non-purulent inflammation of the conjunctiva, suggesting viral aetiology.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Rubella"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - typically a low-grade fever (e.g., 37.5-38.5°C) and stable cardiovascular parameters, reflecting the generally mild nature of the illness.
  • Check maculopapular rash - fine, discrete, pink-to-red lesions, often blanching on pressure, appearing first on the face and neck, then spreading to the trunk and extremities within 24 hours.
  • Check lymphadenopathy - palpable, tender enlargement of post-auricular, posterior cervical, and suboccipital lymph nodes, a key diagnostic clue.
  • Check Forschheimer spots - petechial lesions on the soft palate, which are a less common but specific enanthem for rubella, indicating [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Forschheimer spots: what are they and why do they matter clinically?" text="viral exanthem"].
  • Check for signs of arthritis - joint swelling, tenderness, or reduced range of motion, particularly in adult women.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Rubella"][/sc]
  • Pregnancy in non-immune women: Maternal rubella infection, especially in the first trimester, carries a high risk of congenital rubella syndrome, necessitating urgent specialist assessment.
  • Haemorrhagic manifestations: Rare complications such as thrombocytopenic purpura or encephalitis, indicating severe systemic involvement and requiring immediate medical attention.
  • Neurological symptoms: Signs of encephalitis (e.g., altered mental status, seizures, focal neurological deficits) are rare but life-threatening complications that require urgent investigation and management.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Rubella"][/sc]
  • Asymptomatic infection: Up to 50% of rubella infections, particularly in children, may be subclinical, making diagnosis challenging and highlighting the importance of serological testing in contact tracing.
  • Adults: May experience more pronounced prodromal symptoms, higher fever, and more frequent arthralgia/arthritis, which can be debilitating and mimic other rheumatological conditions.
  • Immunocompromised individuals: May have prolonged viral shedding and atypical or severe presentations, including rubella encephalitis, due to impaired immune response.
  • Congenital Rubella Syndrome (CRS): Foetal infection results in a constellation of birth defects, including [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Congenital Rubella Syndrome features: what are they and why do they matter clinically?" text="deafness, cataracts, cardiac defects (e.g., patent ductus arteriosus, pulmonary artery stenosis), microcephaly, and developmental delay"], which are lifelong and severe.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Rubella"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected Rubella: Serological testing for rubella-specific IgM antibodies (indicating acute infection) and IgG antibodies (indicating past infection or immunity).
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Viral PCR from throat swab, nasopharyngeal aspirate, or urine can confirm rubella infection, especially in atypical presentations or for public health surveillance.
  • Severity/risk tools: No validated severity or risk scores are routinely used for uncomplicated rubella; however, in pregnancy, the risk of congenital rubella syndrome is assessed based on gestational age at maternal infection.
  • Serial reassessment: Repeat serology may be indicated in equivocal cases or to confirm seroconversion in non-immune pregnant women exposed to rubella, guiding decisions on [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Rubella seroconversion in pregnancy: what does it mean and why does it matter clinically?" text="management of pregnancy"].
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Rubella"][/sc]
  • A 28-year-old unvaccinated pregnant woman presents with a 3-day history of a fine, pink rash starting on her face and spreading to her trunk, accompanied by mild fever and tender posterior cervical lymph nodes, following contact with a child with a similar illness.
  • An unvaccinated pregnant woman with a maculopapular rash and lymphadenopathy, concerning for acute rubella infection with potential for congenital rubella syndrome.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Rubella"][/sc]
  • Measles - typically presents with a darker, confluent rash, higher fever, cough, coryza, conjunctivitis, and Koplik spots, distinguishing it from the milder rubella.
  • Scarlet fever - characterised by a fine, sandpaper-like rash, often with circumoral pallor and a "strawberry tongue," in the context of streptococcal pharyngitis.
  • Roseola infantum (HHV-6) - common in infants, presenting with high fever followed by a rash as the fever subsides, unlike rubella where rash and fever often coincide.
  • Parvovirus B19 (Erythema infectiosum) - presents with a characteristic "slapped cheek" rash and a lacy, reticular rash on the trunk and limbs, often associated with arthralgia in adults.
  • Drug eruption - can cause a widespread maculopapular rash, but typically lacks the characteristic lymphadenopathy and prodromal symptoms of rubella, and is linked to medication exposure.
  • Other viral exanthems (e.g., enteroviruses, adenovirus) - can cause non-specific rashes, but usually lack the specific constellation of rubella features like prominent post-auricular lymphadenopathy, making [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Distinguishing viral exanthems: what are the key features and why do they matter clinically?" text="differentiation crucial"].
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Rubella"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Recognition of suspected rubella, particularly in pregnant women, warrants prompt isolation to prevent further spread and immediate notification to public health authorities.
  • Definitive management: There is no specific antiviral treatment for rubella; management is primarily supportive, focusing on symptom relief with rest, adequate hydration, and antipyretics/analgesics (e.g., paracetamol or ibuprofen).
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Individuals with rubella should be advised to avoid contact with pregnant women and unvaccinated individuals for at least 7 days after rash onset. Pregnant women exposed to rubella require urgent serological testing and specialist obstetric and infectious disease consultation.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Advise patients to seek medical attention if symptoms worsen, new neurological symptoms develop, or if there are concerns about potential complications.
  • Exam pharmacology reference (UKMLA/MRCGP): The MMR vaccine (containing live attenuated measles, mumps, and rubella viruses) is the primary preventative measure against rubella. Post-exposure prophylaxis with immunoglobulin may be considered for non-immune pregnant women exposed to rubella, though its efficacy in preventing CRS is limited. Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Rubella"][/sc]
  • Congenital Rubella Syndrome (CRS): Occurs when a non-immune pregnant woman contracts rubella, especially in the first trimester, leading to severe and permanent birth defects in the foetus, including deafness, cataracts, cardiac malformations, and developmental delay.
  • Arthritis/Arthralgia: More common in adult women, presenting as transient joint pain or inflammation, typically resolving within weeks but can be debilitating.
  • Thrombocytopenic purpura: A rare complication characterised by a low platelet count, leading to petechiae, purpura, and increased bleeding risk, usually self-limiting.
  • Encephalitis: A very rare but serious neurological complication, presenting with altered consciousness, seizures, or focal neurological deficits, with potential for long-term neurological sequelae.
  • Orchitis: Rare in post-pubertal males, causing testicular pain and swelling.
