Haemangioma [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Haemangioma"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Haemangioma"][/sc]
  • A common benign vascular tumour characterised by rapid proliferation during infancy, followed by slow involution.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the typical presentation and natural history of infantile haemangiomas, distinguishing them from other vascular anomalies.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Haemangioma"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Haemangioma"][/sc]
  • Infantile haemangiomas are the most common benign tumours of infancy, affecting approximately 4-5% of infants in the UK.
  • More common in premature infants, females, and those with low birth weight, with a higher incidence in Caucasian populations.
  • Often appear within the first few weeks of life, with rapid growth typically occurring in the first 5 months.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Haemangioma"][/sc]
  • Trigger: Abnormal proliferation of endothelial cells, often linked to placental cell embolisation or hypoxia-induced angiogenesis during fetal development.
  • Progression: Rapid endothelial cell proliferation and mast cell infiltration during the proliferative phase, leading to tumour growth.
  • Consequence: Subsequent involution phase characterised by apoptosis of endothelial cells and replacement with fibrofatty tissue, resulting in regression.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Haemangioma"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Appears within the first few weeks of life, with rapid growth (proliferative phase) typically peaking between 3-5 months of age.
  • Progressive phase: Growth continues for approximately 6-9 months, with the majority of growth completed by 5 months, particularly beyond 6 months.
  • Late phase: Slow involution begins around 12 months, continuing for several years, with 50% resolution by age 5 and 70% by age 7, particularly beyond 1 year.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Haemangioma"][/sc]
  • Ask about initial appearance - to confirm onset after birth, differentiating from congenital vascular malformations.
  • Ask about growth pattern - to establish rapid proliferation followed by stabilisation or regression.
  • Ask about pain or tenderness - to assess for ulceration or secondary infection, which can be a complication.
  • Ask about functional impairment - to determine if the haemangioma is obstructing vision, breathing, or feeding.
  • Ask about bleeding - to identify ulcerated lesions that may bleed easily.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Haemangioma"][/sc]
  • Check for a raised, bright red (strawberry) lesion - characteristic appearance of superficial infantile haemangiomas.
  • Check for a bluish hue or swelling beneath the skin - indicative of deeper or mixed haemangiomas.
  • Check for ulceration or crusting - suggests a complication requiring specific management.
  • Check for location - periocular, perioral, lumbosacral, or airway locations may indicate higher risk for complications.
  • Check vital signs - to rule out systemic involvement or complications, especially in large or multiple lesions.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Haemangioma"][/sc]
  • Periocular haemangioma - risk of amblyopia due to visual axis obstruction or astigmatism.
  • Perioral haemangioma - potential for feeding difficulties and ulceration.
  • Segmental haemangioma - associated with PHACE syndrome (Posterior fossa malformations, Haemangiomas, Arterial anomalies, Cardiac defects, Eye anomalies).
  • Large facial haemangioma - may indicate underlying structural anomalies or airway involvement.
  • Lumbosacral haemangioma - association with underlying spinal dysraphism.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Haemangioma"][/sc]
  • Congenital haemangioma (RICH/NICH): Present at birth, fully formed, and do not proliferate. RICH (Rapidly Involuting Congenital Haemangioma) involutes quickly, while NICH (Non-Involuting Congenital Haemangioma) persists. This matters as their natural history and management differ significantly from infantile haemangiomas.
  • Ulcerated haemangioma: Presents with pain, bleeding, and potential for infection, requiring wound care and pain management. This matters as it is a common complication causing significant morbidity.
  • Deep haemangioma: May present as a subcutaneous mass with minimal skin changes, making diagnosis more challenging. This matters as the lack of superficial signs can delay diagnosis and intervention for high-risk lesions.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Haemangioma"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected Haemangioma: Clinical diagnosis based on history and characteristic appearance is usually sufficient.
  • Imaging - Ultrasound: May be used to confirm diagnosis, assess depth, and differentiate from other vascular lesions, especially for deep or atypical presentations.
  • Imaging - MRI: Indicated for large or complicated haemangiomas, particularly those involving critical structures (e.g., airway, brain, spine) or suspected PHACE syndrome.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Biopsy may be considered in rare cases of diagnostic uncertainty, though typically avoided due to bleeding risk.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Haemangioma"][/sc]
  • A 3-month-old female infant presents with a rapidly growing, raised, bright red lesion on her left cheek that appeared a few weeks after birth.
  • A rapidly proliferating, benign vascular tumour of infancy, typically presenting in the first few weeks of life and undergoing spontaneous involution.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Haemangioma"][/sc]
  • Vascular malformation - present at birth, grow proportionally with the child, do not involute.
  • Pyogenic granuloma - rapidly growing, friable lesion, often follows trauma, typically solitary.
  • Neuroblastoma - can present as cutaneous nodules, but usually with systemic symptoms and rapid progression.
  • Langerhans cell histiocytosis - can cause skin lesions, but often associated with other organ involvement.
  • Blue rubber bleb naevus syndrome - multiple venous malformations, often painful and compressible.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Haemangioma"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Address complications such as ulceration (wound care, pain relief) or functional impairment (e.g., visual obstruction).
  • Definitive management: Oral propranolol is the first-line treatment for problematic or high-risk infantile haemangiomas. Topical timolol may be used for small, superficial lesions.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Regular clinical review to assess growth, involution, and treatment response.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Refer to a specialist (dermatology, plastic surgery, ophthalmology) for high-risk lesions, complications, or diagnostic uncertainty.
  • Exam pharmacology reference (UKMLA/MRCGP): [sc name="jitl-medication" Q="Propranolol use in Haemangioma" text="Propranolol"][/sc] for systemic treatment of problematic infantile haemangiomas. Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Haemangioma"][/sc]
  • Ulceration: Occurs in 10-15% of haemangiomas, particularly in high-friction areas, leading to pain, bleeding, and infection risk.
  • Functional impairment: Haemangiomas near eyes, airway, or mouth can obstruct vision (amblyopia), breathing, or feeding, impacting development.
  • Disfigurement: Large or prominent haemangiomas, especially on the face, can cause significant cosmetic concerns and psychological distress, even after involution.
  • PHACE syndrome: Association of large segmental facial haemangiomas with posterior fossa malformations, arterial anomalies, cardiac defects, and eye anomalies, requiring comprehensive evaluation.
  • Cardiac failure: Very large or multiple haemangiomas can act as arteriovenous shunts, leading to high-output cardiac failure in rare cases.
