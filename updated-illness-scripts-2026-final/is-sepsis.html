Sepsis (Infections) [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Sepsis"]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Sepsis"]
  • Sepsis is defined as a life-threatening organ dysfunction caused by a dysregulated host response to infection [1].
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the signs and symptoms of sepsis, evaluate risk using appropriate tools (e.g., NEWS2, qSOFA), and understand the principles of early management.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Sepsis"]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Sepsis"]
  • Sepsis affects approximately 250,000 people in the UK annually, with around 48,000 deaths [Source: UK Sepsis Trust].
  • Risk factors include age (extremes of age, particularly over 75), impaired immune function (e.g., due to illness or medical treatment), recent surgery or invasive procedures, indwelling catheters, and multimorbidities [1].
  • People from ethnic minority backgrounds, those with communication challenges, or individuals experiencing homelessness or living in deprived areas may also face increased risk or delayed identification [1].
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Sepsis"]
  • An infection triggers a systemic inflammatory response, often involving the release of pro-inflammatory mediators.
  • This dysregulated host response leads to widespread endothelial dysfunction, microvascular thrombosis, and increased vascular permeability, resulting in organ hypoperfusion and cellular hypoxia.
  • Ultimately, this can progress to multi-organ dysfunction syndrome (MODS) and septic shock, characterised by persistent hypotension requiring vasopressors and elevated lactate levels despite adequate fluid resuscitation [Source: Sepsis-3 Consensus]. Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Sepsis"]
  • Early phase: Initial infection may present with non-specific symptoms, but rapid progression to organ dysfunction can occur within hours.
  • Progressive phase: If untreated, organ dysfunction can worsen, leading to critical illness and potential septic shock, particularly beyond the first 6-12 hours of onset.
  • Late phase: Prolonged or severe sepsis can result in persistent critical illness, long-term physical and cognitive sequelae, and increased mortality risk over days to weeks.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Sepsis"]
  • Ask about feeling very unwell or "just not right" - this non-specific symptom can be a crucial early indicator, especially if out of character for the patient [1].
  • Ask about new or worsening confusion/disorientation - indicates cerebral hypoperfusion or direct central nervous system involvement.
  • Ask about shortness of breath or rapid breathing - suggests respiratory compensation for metabolic acidosis or direct lung injury.
  • Ask about reduced urine output - a key sign of renal hypoperfusion and acute kidney injury.
  • Ask about cold or clammy skin, or new rash - indicates peripheral vasoconstriction and poor perfusion, or disseminated intravascular coagulation.
  • Ask about severe muscle pain or inability to stand - can signify widespread tissue injury and metabolic derangement.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Sepsis"]
  • Check vital signs (NEWS2 parameters) - a score of 5 or more, or a single parameter score of 3, should prompt urgent review for suspected sepsis [1].
  • Check capillary refill time (CRT) - prolonged CRT (>2 seconds) suggests poor peripheral perfusion.
  • Check skin for mottling, cyanosis, or non-blanching rash - these are critical indicators of severe hypoperfusion or coagulopathy [1].
  • Check mental status - new or altered mentation (e.g., GCS <15) is a component of qSOFA and indicates cerebral dysfunction [Source: Sepsis-3 Consensus]. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="qSOFA criteria: what are they and why do they matter clinically?" text="qSOFA criteria"]
  • Check for signs of infection source - localised pain, swelling, redness, or purulent discharge can help identify the primary site of infection.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Sepsis"]
  • New altered mental state: Indicates significant cerebral dysfunction requiring immediate assessment.
  • Systolic blood pressure ≤90 mmHg (or >40 mmHg drop from normal): Suggests circulatory collapse and impending shock.
  • Heart rate >130 bpm: A compensatory mechanism for hypovolaemia or reduced cardiac output, indicating severe physiological stress.
  • Respiratory rate ≥25 bpm: Signifies respiratory distress or compensation for metabolic acidosis.
  • Non-blanching rash or mottled/ashen/cyanotic skin: Critical signs of poor perfusion and potential disseminated intravascular coagulation, demanding urgent intervention.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Sepsis"]
  • Older adults: May present with subtle symptoms like confusion, falls, or general malaise without fever or significant tachycardia, making early recognition challenging.
  • Immunosuppressed patients: (e.g., chemotherapy, chronic steroids) may have blunted inflammatory responses, leading to less pronounced fever or leucocytosis despite severe infection.
  • Patients on beta-blockers: May not exhibit the expected tachycardia in response to hypovolaemia or shock, masking a critical sign.
  • Patients with chronic conditions: (e.g., chronic kidney disease, heart failure) may have baseline abnormalities in vital signs or organ function, complicating the assessment of acute deterioration.
  • Children: While this guideline focuses on adults, it is important to note that children often present with different signs, such as lethargy, poor feeding, or non-specific irritability.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Sepsis"]
  • Core for suspected sepsis: Full blood count (leucocytosis/leucopenia, thrombocytopenia), C-reactive protein (CRP), lactate (indicator of tissue hypoperfusion), renal function (urea, creatinine), liver function tests, coagulation screen (INR, APTT), blood cultures (before antibiotics if possible), urine dipstick and culture, chest X-ray.
  • Severity/risk tools: Sequential Organ Failure Assessment (SOFA) score is used to quantify organ dysfunction and predict mortality in critically ill patients [Source: Sepsis-3 Consensus].   • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Further imaging (e.g., CT scan of abdomen/pelvis, ultrasound) or specific microbiological tests may be warranted based on clinical suspicion.
  • Serial reassessment: Repeat lactate, CRP, and NEWS2 scores to monitor response to treatment and identify ongoing organ dysfunction.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Sepsis"]
  • A 78-year-old male presents with a 24-hour history of increasing confusion, shortness of breath, and reduced urine output, following a recent urinary tract infection, with a NEWS2 score of 7.
  • An elderly male with acute-on-chronic confusion and multi-organ dysfunction, likely secondary to a dysregulated host response to infection, concerning for sepsis.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Sepsis"]
  • Anaphylaxis: Rapid onset of systemic symptoms, often with urticaria/angioedema and bronchospasm, typically triggered by an allergen, distinguishing it from infection-driven sepsis.
  • Cardiogenic shock: Characterised by signs of heart failure (e.g., raised JVP, pulmonary oedema) and low cardiac output, often without clear evidence of infection.
  • Hypovolaemic shock: Due to significant fluid loss (e.g., haemorrhage, severe dehydration), typically without an infectious focus and responding to fluid resuscitation alone.
  • Adrenal crisis: Presents with hypotension, hyponatraemia, hyperkalaemia, and often gastrointestinal symptoms, but usually without a primary infectious trigger.
  • Pulmonary embolism: Acute onset of dyspnoea, pleuritic chest pain, and hypoxaemia, often with risk factors for VTE, distinguishing it from sepsis-induced respiratory compromise.
  • Diabetic ketoacidosis (DKA) / Hyperosmolar Hyperglycaemic State (HHS): Characterised by severe hyperglycaemia, metabolic acidosis (DKA), and dehydration, often with a precipitating factor other than infection.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Sepsis"]
  • Immediate priorities: Early recognition and escalation to a senior clinician, securing intravenous access, taking blood cultures, administering broad-spectrum intravenous antibiotics, initiating intravenous fluid resuscitation (e.g., 500ml crystalloid stat, repeated if necessary, up to 1000ml within the first hour), and oxygen therapy to maintain target saturations (NICE NG51 Sepsis 6 principles) [1].
  • Definitive management: Prompt identification and control of the source of infection (e.g., surgical drainage of abscess, removal of infected lines), guided by microbiological results and clinical response.
  • Adjunctive management: Consideration of vasopressors (e.g., noradrenaline) if hypotension persists despite adequate fluid resuscitation, aiming to maintain mean arterial pressure (MAP) >65 mmHg.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Continuous monitoring of vital signs, urine output, and lactate levels; regular reassessment of NEWS2 score and clinical status; involvement of critical care outreach or intensive care unit (ICU) as appropriate.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Clear communication with patients and families about the diagnosis and management plan; providing safety-netting advice for ongoing symptoms or deterioration upon discharge.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Sepsis"]
  • Acute Respiratory Distress Syndrome (ARDS): Occurs due to widespread inflammatory lung injury, leading to severe hypoxaemia and requiring ventilatory support, often developing within days of sepsis onset.
  • Acute Kidney Injury (AKI): Resulting from renal hypoperfusion and direct inflammatory damage, can range from mild impairment to complete renal failure requiring dialysis, typically manifesting in the early to progressive phases.
  • Disseminated Intravascular Coagulation (DIC): A complex coagulopathy leading to both thrombosis and haemorrhage, driven by systemic inflammation, and can develop rapidly in severe sepsis.
  • Septic Cardiomyopathy: Myocardial dysfunction leading to reduced cardiac output, which can exacerbate circulatory shock and contribute to multi-organ failure, often evident in the progressive phase.
  • Long-term Post-Sepsis Syndrome: Persistent physical (e.g., muscle weakness, fatigue), psychological (e.g., PTSD, anxiety, depression), and cognitive (e.g., memory impairment) sequelae that can affect patients for months to years after recovery from acute sepsis.
