Gallstones and biliary colic [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Gallstones and biliary colic"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Gallstones and biliary colic"][/sc]
  • Gallstones are hardened deposits of bile that form in the gallbladder, while biliary colic is the acute, spasmodic pain caused by temporary obstruction of the cystic duct or bile duct by a gallstone.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical presentation of gallstones and biliary colic, differentiate it from other acute abdominal conditions, and understand initial diagnostic steps.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Gallstones and biliary colic"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Gallstones and biliary colic"][/sc]
  • The prevalence of gallstones in adults in Europe and the UK is approximately 10–15% [1] [2].
  • Risk factors for gallstone formation include being female, age 40 or over, obesity, rapid weight loss, certain medical conditions (e.g., Crohn\'s disease, diabetes), and a family history of gallstones [3] [4].
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Gallstones and biliary colic"][/sc]
  • Gallstones form when bile constituents, primarily cholesterol or bilirubin, become supersaturated and precipitate, leading to crystal formation and subsequent stone growth.
  • Biliary colic occurs when a gallstone temporarily obstructs the cystic duct or common bile duct, causing the gallbladder to contract forcefully against the obstruction, leading to visceral pain. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Cystic duct obstruction: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Cystic duct obstruction"][/sc]
  • Prolonged or complete obstruction can lead to inflammation (cholecystitis), infection (cholangitis), or pancreatitis, depending on the location of the obstruction.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Gallstones and biliary colic"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Biliary colic typically presents as sudden onset, severe, constant right upper quadrant or epigastric pain, often radiating to the back or right shoulder, lasting from 30 minutes to several hours. It is commonly triggered by fatty meals.
  • Progressive phase: If the obstruction is prolonged, the pain may become more constant and severe, potentially indicating progression to acute cholecystitis, particularly beyond 4-6 hours.
  • Late phase: Unresolved obstruction or inflammation can lead to complications such as cholangitis or pancreatitis, which may manifest with systemic signs of infection or more diffuse abdominal pain.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Gallstones and biliary colic"][/sc]
  • Ask about right upper quadrant or epigastric pain - often severe, constant, and radiating to the back or right shoulder, which is characteristic of biliary colic.
  • Ask about post-prandial pain - typically occurring after fatty meals, as this stimulates gallbladder contraction.
  • Ask about nausea and vomiting - common accompanying symptoms due to visceral pain and autonomic stimulation.
  • Ask about duration of pain - biliary colic usually lasts 30 minutes to several hours, resolving spontaneously. Pain lasting longer than 6 hours may suggest acute cholecystitis.
  • Ask about previous episodes - recurrent episodes of similar pain are highly suggestive of gallstone disease.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Gallstones and biliary colic"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - usually normal in uncomplicated biliary colic, but fever, tachycardia, or hypotension may indicate complications like cholecystitis or cholangitis.
  • Check for right upper quadrant tenderness - often present during an attack of biliary colic.
  • Check for Murphy\'s sign - inspiratory arrest on palpation of the right upper quadrant, highly suggestive of acute cholecystitis, but typically absent in uncomplicated biliary colic. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Murphy\'s sign: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Murphy\'s sign"][/sc]
  • Check for scleral icterus or jaundice - suggests obstruction of the common bile duct (choledocholithiasis) or cholangitis.
  • Check for abdominal distension or guarding - may indicate peritonitis in severe cases of cholecystitis or perforation.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Gallstones and biliary colic"][/sc]
  • Persistent pain (>6 hours): Suggests progression from biliary colic to acute cholecystitis, indicating sustained obstruction and inflammation of the gallbladder.
  • Fever and chills: May indicate acute cholecystitis or ascending cholangitis, particularly if associated with jaundice and right upper quadrant pain (Charcot\'s triad).
  • Jaundice: Implies obstruction of the common bile duct (choledocholithiasis) or severe liver dysfunction, requiring urgent investigation.
  • Peritonism or guarding: Suggests localised or generalised peritonitis, potentially due to gallbladder perforation or severe inflammation.
  • Hypotension and tachycardia: Indicate systemic inflammatory response syndrome (SIRS) or sepsis, particularly concerning in the context of cholangitis (Reynold\'s pentad).
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Gallstones and biliary colic"][/sc]
  • Older adults: May present with vague symptoms such as anorexia, malaise, or confusion, with less pronounced pain or fever, leading to delayed diagnosis.
  • Pregnant women: Biliary colic is more common during pregnancy, and symptoms can be more severe. Diagnosis can be challenging due to physiological changes and concerns about fetal safety with imaging.
  • Diabetic patients: May have a higher incidence of gallstones and can present with less severe pain due to neuropathy, increasing the risk of complications and delayed presentation.
  • Acalculous cholecystitis: Inflammation of the gallbladder without gallstones, often seen in critically ill patients, presenting with similar symptoms but lacking evidence of stones.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Gallstones and biliary colic"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected gallstone disease:
    • Liver function tests (LFTs): Elevated bilirubin, alkaline phosphatase, and gamma-glutamyl transferase (GGT) may indicate biliary obstruction. Amylase and lipase may be elevated if pancreatitis is present.
    • Full blood count (FBC): Leukocytosis may suggest inflammation or infection (e.g., cholecystitis, cholangitis).
    • Abdominal ultrasound: First-line imaging modality to visualise gallstones, gallbladder wall thickening, pericholecystic fluid, and bile duct dilatation.
  • Severity/risk tools:
    • Tokyo Guidelines for Acute Cholecystitis 2018 on MDCalc: Used for diagnosis and severity grading of acute cholecystitis.
    • Tokyo Guidelines for Acute Cholangitis 2018 on MDCalc: Used for diagnosis and severity grading of acute cholangitis.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible:
    • CT abdomen: May be used to rule out other causes of abdominal pain or to identify complications not clearly seen on ultrasound.
  • Imaging - MRCP: Magnetic Resonance Cholangiopancreatography (MRCP) is considered if common bile duct stones are suspected but not confirmed by ultrasound, especially if LFTs are abnormal or the bile duct is dilated. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="MRCP: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="MRCP"][/sc]
  • Imaging - ERCP: Endoscopic Retrograde Cholangiopancreatography (ERCP) is therapeutic and diagnostic, used for direct visualisation and removal of common bile duct stones.
  • Serial reassessment: Clinical and biochemical reassessment is crucial to monitor for progression of symptoms or development of complications.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Gallstones and biliary colic"][/sc]
  • A 45-year-old obese female presents with sudden onset, severe right upper quadrant pain radiating to her back, occurring after a fatty meal, associated with nausea but no fever.
  • Middle-aged, obese female with post-prandial right upper quadrant pain and nausea, concerning for symptomatic cholelithiasis (biliary colic).
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Gallstones and biliary colic"][/sc]
  • Acute Cholecystitis - persistent right upper quadrant pain (>6 hours), fever, and positive Murphy\'s sign.
  • Acute Pancreatitis - severe epigastric pain radiating to the back, often associated with elevated amylase and lipase.
  • Peptic Ulcer Disease - epigastric pain, often relieved by antacids, and may be associated with H. pylori infection.
  • Gastro-oesophageal Reflux Disease (GORD) - burning retrosternal pain, regurgitation, and often worse with lying down.
  • Hepatitis - right upper quadrant discomfort, jaundice, fatigue, and elevated liver transaminases.
  • Ectopic Pregnancy (in females of childbearing age) - sudden, severe unilateral lower abdominal pain, vaginal bleeding, and positive pregnancy test.
  • Pelvic Inflammatory Disease (PID) (in females) - lower abdominal pain, vaginal discharge, fever, and cervical motion tenderness. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Pelvic Inflammatory Disease: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Pelvic Inflammatory Disease"][/sc]
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Gallstones and biliary colic"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Recognition of biliary colic and differentiation from complications. Supportive care often includes analgesia and antiemetics. Escalation to surgical team for assessment is typically warranted.
  • Definitive management: For symptomatic gallstones, laparoscopic cholecystectomy is the definitive treatment. This is often offered as an elective procedure, but early cholecystectomy may be considered for acute cholecystitis.
  • Adjunctive management: Dietary modification (avoiding fatty foods) may help reduce the frequency of biliary colic episodes while awaiting definitive treatment.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Post-cholecystectomy, patients are typically advised that dietary restrictions are no longer necessary. Follow-up is usually arranged with the surgical team.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Patients should be advised to seek urgent medical attention if symptoms worsen, persist, or new symptoms such as fever, jaundice, or severe abdominal pain develop.
  • Exam pharmacology reference (UKMLA/MRCGP):
    • Non-steroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs) such as diclofenac or ibuprofen may be used for pain relief in biliary colic. [sc name="jitl-medication" Q="NSAIDs use in biliary colic" text="NSAIDs"][/sc]
    • Opioids such as pethidine or morphine may be considered for severe pain unresponsive to NSAIDs. [sc name="jitl-medication" Q="Opioids use in biliary colic" text="Opioids"][/sc]

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Gallstones and biliary colic"][/sc]
  • Acute Cholecystitis: Persistent obstruction of the cystic duct by a gallstone leads to inflammation and infection of the gallbladder, typically presenting with pain lasting longer than 6 hours, fever, and a positive Murphy\'s sign.
  • Choledocholithiasis: Gallstones migrating into the common bile duct can cause obstruction, leading to jaundice, dark urine, pale stools, and potentially ascending cholangitis.
  • Acute Cholangitis: A severe bacterial infection of the bile ducts, often secondary to choledocholithiasis, characterised by Charcot\'s triad (fever, jaundice, right upper quadrant pain). [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Charcot triad: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Charcot triad"][/sc]
  • Gallstone Pancreatitis: A gallstone obstructing the ampulla of Vater can cause inflammation of the pancreas, presenting with severe epigastric pain radiating to the back, nausea, and vomiting.
  • Gallbladder Perforation: A rare but life-threatening complication of acute cholecystitis, where the inflamed gallbladder wall ruptures, leading to peritonitis.
  • Reynold\'s Pentad: A severe form of ascending cholangitis characterised by Charcot\'s triad plus altered mental status and hypotension, indicating septic shock and requiring urgent intervention. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Reynold\'s Pentad: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Reynold\'s Pentad"][/sc]
