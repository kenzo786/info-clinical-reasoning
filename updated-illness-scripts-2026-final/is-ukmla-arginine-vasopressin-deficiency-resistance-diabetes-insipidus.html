Arginine vasopressin deficiency/ resistance (diabetes insipidus) [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Arginine vasopressin deficiency/ resistance (diabetes insipidus)"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Arginine vasopressin deficiency/ resistance (diabetes insipidus)"][/sc]
  • A condition characterised by the inability to concentrate urine due to insufficient production of arginine vasopressin (AVP) by the hypothalamus (cranial DI) or impaired renal response to AVP (nephrogenic DI), leading to excessive urination and thirst.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical presentation, interpret diagnostic tests, and outline initial management strategies for arginine vasopressin deficiency/resistance (diabetes insipidus).
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Arginine vasopressin deficiency/ resistance (diabetes insipidus)"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Arginine vasopressin deficiency/ resistance (diabetes insipidus)"][/sc]
  • Cranial diabetes insipidus is rare, with an estimated prevalence of 1 in 25,000 in the UK, often secondary to pituitary or hypothalamic damage from trauma, surgery, or tumours.
  • Nephrogenic diabetes insipidus is even rarer, with congenital forms affecting approximately 1 in 250,000 individuals, while acquired forms are more common, frequently linked to medications like lithium or chronic kidney disease.
  • Risk factors include a history of neurosurgery, head trauma, genetic predisposition (for congenital forms), and certain drug exposures.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Arginine vasopressin deficiency/ resistance (diabetes insipidus)"][/sc]
  • Trigger: Damage to the hypothalamus or posterior pituitary gland (cranial DI) impairs AVP synthesis or release, or renal tubule cells become unresponsive to AVP (nephrogenic DI), often due to genetic mutations or drug effects.
  • Progression: Reduced AVP action leads to decreased water reabsorption in the renal collecting ducts, resulting in the excretion of large volumes of dilute urine, despite rising plasma osmolality.
  • Consequence: Persistent free water loss causes hypernatraemia and increased plasma osmolality, stimulating intense thirst and compensatory polydipsia, which can lead to severe dehydration if fluid intake is inadequate.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Arginine vasopressin deficiency/ resistance (diabetes insipidus)"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Symptoms of polyuria and polydipsia typically develop acutely over hours to days following an insult to the pituitary or hypothalamus, or gradually over weeks with drug-induced nephrogenic forms.
  • Progressive phase: If untreated, fluid imbalance can worsen over days, particularly beyond 48-72 hours, leading to significant dehydration and electrolyte disturbances, especially hypernatraemia.
  • Late phase: Chronic, unmanaged diabetes insipidus can lead to persistent dehydration, electrolyte abnormalities, and potential long-term renal complications, particularly beyond several weeks or months.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Arginine vasopressin deficiency/ resistance (diabetes insipidus)"][/sc]
  • Ask about polyuria - often defined as passing more than 3 litres of urine in 24 hours, which is a cardinal symptom indicating impaired urinary concentration.
  • Ask about polydipsia - intense thirst, often for cold water, which is a compensatory mechanism to maintain hydration in response to excessive fluid loss.
  • Ask about nocturia - frequent urination at night, which can significantly disrupt sleep and indicate a severe degree of polyuria.
  • Ask about fatigue and weakness - non-specific symptoms that may arise from dehydration, electrolyte imbalances, or sleep disturbance due to nocturia.
  • Ask about recent head injury or neurosurgery - these are important historical clues for potential cranial diabetes insipidus.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Arginine vasopressin deficiency/ resistance (diabetes insipidus)"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - particularly blood pressure and heart rate, as orthostatic hypotension and tachycardia may indicate significant dehydration.
  • Check skin turgor and mucous membranes - reduced skin turgor and dry mucous membranes are classic signs of dehydration.
  • Check neurological status - altered consciousness, confusion, or seizures can occur in severe hypernatraemia, indicating a critical electrolyte disturbance.
  • Check for signs of underlying pituitary pathology - such as visual field defects or other endocrine deficiencies, which may point to the cause of cranial DI.
  • Check urine output and specific gravity - high urine output with a low specific gravity (typically <1.005) is characteristic of diabetes insipidus.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Arginine vasopressin deficiency/ resistance (diabetes insipidus)"][/sc]
  • Rapid onset of severe polyuria and polydipsia - particularly after neurosurgery or head trauma, suggesting acute cranial diabetes insipidus.
  • Altered mental status or seizures - indicating severe hypernatraemia and neurological compromise requiring urgent intervention.
  • Signs of hypovolaemic shock - such as profound hypotension and tachycardia, signifying critical dehydration and circulatory collapse.
  • Unexplained weight loss or growth failure in children - can be a subtle presentation of chronic diabetes insipidus, leading to significant morbidity.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Arginine vasopressin deficiency/ resistance (diabetes insipidus)"][/sc]
  • Older adults: May present with less pronounced thirst sensation, increasing the risk of severe dehydration and hypernatraemia, as compensatory mechanisms are blunted.
  • Infants/young children: Often present with non-specific symptoms like irritability, poor feeding, fever, and failure to thrive, as they cannot articulate thirst or polyuria.
  • Patients with impaired access to water: Individuals who are unconscious, intubated, or have physical limitations may not be able to drink enough to compensate, leading to rapid and severe dehydration.
  • Partial diabetes insipidus: Milder forms may present with less dramatic polyuria and polydipsia, making diagnosis more challenging and requiring specific dynamic testing.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Arginine vasopressin deficiency/ resistance (diabetes insipidus)"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected arginine vasopressin deficiency/resistance: Serum electrolytes (sodium, potassium, urea, creatinine), plasma osmolality, urine osmolality, and urine specific gravity. A water deprivation test is the gold standard for diagnosis and differentiation between cranial and nephrogenic forms.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Fasting blood glucose to exclude diabetes mellitus, serum calcium and potassium to identify other causes of polyuria, and thyroid function tests.
  • Imaging - MRI brain: Indicated for suspected cranial diabetes insipidus to identify pituitary or hypothalamic lesions, such as tumours or inflammatory processes.
  • Serial reassessment: Close monitoring of fluid balance, weight, serum sodium, and osmolality is crucial during diagnostic testing and initiation of treatment.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Arginine vasopressin deficiency/ resistance (diabetes insipidus)"][/sc]
  • A 45-year-old male presents with a 3-day history of passing large volumes of dilute urine, intense thirst, and waking multiple times at night to drink water, following recent neurosurgery for a pituitary adenoma.
  • A post-operative, middle-aged man with acute polyuria and polydipsia, suggesting impaired water reabsorption.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Arginine vasopressin deficiency/ resistance (diabetes insipidus)"][/sc]
  • Primary polydipsia - distinguished by low plasma osmolality and inappropriately dilute urine, often with a history of psychiatric illness.
  • Diabetes mellitus - excluded by normal blood glucose levels, though both cause polyuria and polydipsia.
  • Diuretic use - history of medication use and electrolyte abnormalities consistent with diuretic action.
  • Hypercalcaemia - can cause nephrogenic diabetes insipidus, identified by elevated serum calcium.
  • Hypokalaemia - can also cause nephrogenic diabetes insipidus, identified by low serum potassium.
  • Chronic kidney disease - often presents with impaired urine concentration, but typically with elevated creatinine and other signs of renal dysfunction.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Arginine vasopressin deficiency/ resistance (diabetes insipidus)"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Ensure adequate fluid replacement to prevent or correct dehydration and hypernatraemia. Intravenous 0.9% sodium chloride or 5% dextrose may be required, with careful monitoring of serum sodium.
  • Definitive management: For cranial diabetes insipidus, desmopressin (a synthetic AVP analogue) is the mainstay of treatment, administered orally, intranasally, or intravenously. For nephrogenic diabetes insipidus, management involves addressing the underlying cause (e.g., discontinuing lithium), ensuring adequate hydration, and sometimes using thiazide diuretics or NSAIDs.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Regular monitoring of fluid balance, urine output, serum electrolytes, and osmolality is essential to titrate desmopressin dosage and prevent complications.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Patients should be advised to seek urgent medical attention if they develop severe thirst, confusion, or reduced consciousness, as these may indicate worsening dehydration or hypernatraemia.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Arginine vasopressin deficiency/ resistance (diabetes insipidus)"][/sc]
  • Dehydration: Occurs rapidly if fluid intake does not match excessive urine output, leading to hypovolaemia and potential organ hypoperfusion.
  • Hypernatraemia: A critical electrolyte imbalance resulting from free water loss, which can cause neurological dysfunction including confusion, seizures, and coma.
  • Electrolyte disturbances: Beyond hypernatraemia, rapid fluid shifts can lead to other imbalances, impacting cardiac and neurological function.
  • Renal damage: Chronic polyuria and compensatory polydipsia can lead to hydronephrosis and long-term renal impairment due to the sheer volume of fluid passing through the kidneys.
  • Psychological impact: The constant thirst and frequent urination can significantly impair quality of life, leading to anxiety, sleep disturbance, and social limitations.
