Cardiac arrest [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Cardiac arrest"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Cardiac arrest"][/sc]
  • Cardiac arrest is the sudden cessation of cardiac mechanical activity, confirmed by the absence of signs of circulation. It is a medical emergency requiring immediate intervention.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the signs of cardiac arrest, initiate basic life support (BLS) and advanced life support (ALS) protocols, and identify reversible causes in accordance with current Resuscitation Council UK guidelines.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Cardiac arrest"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Cardiac arrest"][/sc]
  • In the UK, approximately 30,000 out-of-hospital cardiac arrests (OHCAs) are attended by ambulance services each year [1].
  • Survival to hospital discharge for OHCA in the UK remains low, at around 8-10% [1].
  • Coronary heart disease (CHD) is the leading cause of sudden cardiac death (SCD), accounting for 80% of cases, particularly in older patients. Non-ischaemic cardiomyopathies contribute to 10-15% of SCD cases [2].
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Cardiac arrest"][/sc]
  • Cardiac arrest is typically triggered by an acute cardiac event, such as myocardial ischaemia or a life-threatening arrhythmia, leading to an abrupt loss of effective cardiac output.
  • This progresses to global cerebral and systemic hypoperfusion, resulting in rapid loss of consciousness and cessation of vital organ function. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Global cerebral hypoperfusion: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Global cerebral hypoperfusion"][/sc]
  • The ultimate consequence is irreversible cellular damage and death if spontaneous circulation is not restored promptly, with neurological injury being a major determinant of long-term outcome.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Cardiac arrest"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Within minutes of cardiac arrest, irreversible brain damage begins due to lack of oxygen and glucose. Early recognition and initiation of chest compressions and defibrillation are critical within the first few minutes.
  • Progressive phase: As time progresses beyond 5-10 minutes without effective resuscitation, the likelihood of successful resuscitation diminishes significantly, and the risk of severe anoxic brain injury increases.
  • Late phase: Survival with good neurological outcome is rare if resuscitation efforts are prolonged beyond 20-30 minutes, particularly in the absence of reversible causes or hypothermia.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Cardiac arrest"][/sc]
  • Ask about sudden collapse - indicates an abrupt loss of consciousness, a hallmark of cardiac arrest.
  • Ask about absence of breathing or abnormal breathing (e.g., agonal gasps) - distinguishes cardiac arrest from other causes of unconsciousness.
  • Ask about chest pain or discomfort prior to collapse - may suggest an underlying acute coronary syndrome as a precipitating factor.
  • Ask about palpitations or dizziness before collapse - could indicate a preceding arrhythmia.
  • Ask about seizure-like activity - can occur due to cerebral hypoperfusion during cardiac arrest and should not be confused with epilepsy.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Cardiac arrest"][/sc]
  • Check for unresponsiveness - the patient does not respond to verbal or painful stimuli.
  • Check for absence of normal breathing - no breathing or only infrequent, noisy gasps (agonal breathing).
  • Check for absence of pulse (carotid or femoral) - confirms circulatory arrest.
  • Check for dilated pupils - a non-specific sign of severe cerebral anoxia.
  • Check vital signs (if measurable) - complete absence of blood pressure and heart rate. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Agonal breathing: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Agonal breathing"][/sc]
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Cardiac arrest"][/sc]
  • Unresponsiveness with absent breathing/pulse: Immediate indication for emergency medical services activation and initiation of cardiopulmonary resuscitation (CPR).
  • Witnessed collapse with immediate onset of symptoms: Suggests a primary cardiac event and may indicate a higher chance of successful resuscitation with early defibrillation.
  • Known pre-existing cardiac disease: Increases the index of suspicion for a cardiac cause and guides the search for reversible factors.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Cardiac arrest"][/sc]
  • Paediatric cardiac arrest: Often secondary to respiratory failure or shock rather than primary cardiac causes, requiring a different emphasis in initial management.
  • Cardiac arrest in pregnancy: Requires specific considerations including left lateral tilt, early perimortem Caesarean section if resuscitation is unsuccessful after 4 minutes, and addressing pregnancy-specific reversible causes. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Perimortem Caesarean section: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Perimortem Caesarean section"][/sc]
  • Hypothermic cardiac arrest: Patients may appear clinically dead but can be successfully resuscitated even after prolonged periods; "not dead until warm and dead."
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Cardiac arrest"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected cardiac arrest: Clinical assessment (unresponsiveness, absence of breathing/pulse) is paramount.
  • During resuscitation: Electrocardiogram (ECG) rhythm analysis (shockable vs. non-shockable), waveform capnography (confirms tracheal intubation, indicates return of spontaneous circulation (ROSC)), arterial blood gas (ABG) analysis (guides metabolic management). [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Waveform capnography: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Waveform capnography"][/sc]
  • Post-ROSC investigations: 12-lead ECG (identify myocardial ischaemia/infarction), echocardiography (assess cardiac function, identify structural abnormalities), blood tests (electrolytes, renal function, cardiac biomarkers, toxicology screen).
  • Imaging - Chest X-ray: To identify pulmonary causes (e.g., pneumothorax) or guide central line placement.
  • Imaging - Coronary angiography: Indicated for patients with suspected cardiac cause of arrest, especially if ST-elevation myocardial infarction (STEMI) is present or highly suspected.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Cardiac arrest"][/sc]
  • A 68-year-old male with a history of ischaemic heart disease suddenly collapsed at home, found unresponsive with agonal breathing by his wife.
  • Elderly male with cardiac history, presenting with sudden collapse and absent vital signs, concerning for cardiac arrest secondary to an acute coronary event.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Cardiac arrest"][/sc]
  • Syncope: Transient loss of consciousness with spontaneous recovery, typically with a preceding prodrome; cardiac arrest involves sustained loss of circulation.
  • Seizure: Often associated with tonic-clonic movements and post-ictal confusion; cardiac arrest can cause seizure-like activity due to cerebral hypoperfusion but lacks a true epileptic discharge.
  • Stroke: Focal neurological deficits are characteristic; cardiac arrest presents with global neurological dysfunction.
  • Severe hypoxaemia/hypercapnia: Can lead to unconsciousness and respiratory arrest but typically maintains some cardiac activity initially.
  • Drug overdose: May cause respiratory depression and unconsciousness, but cardiac arrest is usually a secondary event. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Prodrome in syncope: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Prodrome in syncope"][/sc]
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Cardiac arrest"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Early recognition, activation of emergency medical services, high-quality chest compressions, early defibrillation for shockable rhythms (VF/pVT), and identification/treatment of reversible causes (4Hs and 4Ts).
  • Definitive management: Following the Resuscitation Council UK 2021 Advanced Life Support (ALS) algorithm, which includes continuous chest compressions, airway management, ventilation, vascular access, and drug administration (adrenaline, amiodarone).
  • Post-resuscitation care: Optimisation of oxygenation and ventilation, blood pressure management, targeted temperature management (TTM) for comatose patients post-ROSC, and urgent investigation/treatment of the underlying cause.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Continuous cardiac monitoring, neurological assessment, and critical care support to prevent re-arrest and manage organ dysfunction.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Clear communication with family, consideration of advanced care planning, and appropriate referral to specialist services (e.g., cardiology, neurology).

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Cardiac arrest"][/sc]
  • Post-cardiac arrest syndrome: A complex constellation of pathophysiological processes occurring after ROSC, including brain injury, myocardial dysfunction, systemic ischaemia/reperfusion response, and persistent precipitating pathology.
  • Anoxic brain injury: Occurs due to global cerebral hypoperfusion during cardiac arrest, leading to a spectrum of neurological deficits ranging from mild cognitive impairment to persistent vegetative state or brain death. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Targeted temperature management: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Targeted temperature management"][/sc]
  • Myocardial dysfunction: The heart may be "stunned" after cardiac arrest, leading to reduced contractility and arrhythmias, which can contribute to haemodynamic instability.
  • Multi-organ failure: Systemic ischaemia-reperfusion injury can affect kidneys, liver, lungs, and gut, leading to acute kidney injury, acute respiratory distress syndrome, and other organ dysfunctions.
  • Recurrent cardiac arrest: Patients remain at high risk of re-arrest, particularly in the early hours and days following ROSC, necessitating continuous monitoring and aggressive management of underlying causes.
