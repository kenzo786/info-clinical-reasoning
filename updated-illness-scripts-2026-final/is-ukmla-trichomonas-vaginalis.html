Trichomonas vaginalis [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Trichomonas vaginalis"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Trichomonas vaginalis"][/sc]
  • Definition: Trichomonas vaginalis (TV) is a flagellated protozoan parasite that causes trichomoniasis, a common sexually transmitted infection (STI) affecting the urogenital tract.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical presentation of trichomoniasis, understand its transmission, and identify appropriate diagnostic and management strategies in line with BASHH guidelines.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Trichomonas vaginalis"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Trichomonas vaginalis"][/sc]
  • Trichomonas vaginalis infection is uncommon in the British population. A study of national STI data (2009–2011) in England found rates were highest in London and the West Midlands. The prevalence of self-reported history of T. vaginalis (past 5 years) was 0.1% in women and 0.0% in men aged 16-44 years.
  • Risk factors include older age, non-white ethnicity (particularly black Caribbean and black ‘other’ ethnic groups), and current gonorrhoea or chlamydia infection in women. Transmission is almost exclusively through sexual intercourse.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Trichomonas vaginalis"][/sc]
  • Trigger: Sexual transmission of the flagellated protozoan parasite, Trichomonas vaginalis, leading to inoculation of the urogenital tract.
  • Progression: The parasite adheres to epithelial cells, causing inflammation and irritation of the vaginal and urethral mucosa. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Trichomonas vaginalis: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Trichomonas vaginalis"]
  • Consequence: This leads to symptoms such as vaginitis, urethritis, and cervicitis, with potential for complications if left untreated.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Trichomonas vaginalis"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Many individuals, particularly men and some women, remain asymptomatic for weeks to months following infection. When symptoms develop, they typically appear within 5-28 days post-exposure.
  • Progressive phase: If untreated, symptoms can persist and may fluctuate in severity. The infection can lead to ongoing inflammation and discomfort, potentially increasing the risk of other STIs.
  • Late phase: Chronic infection, particularly beyond several months, can lead to persistent symptoms and may contribute to adverse pregnancy outcomes in women, and potentially prostate inflammation in men.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Trichomonas vaginalis"][/sc]
  • Ask about vaginal discharge - often offensive odour, varying in consistency from thin and scanty to profuse and thick.
  • Ask about vulval itching - a common symptom of irritation.
  • Ask about dysuria - discomfort during urination, indicating urethral involvement.
  • Ask about lower abdominal discomfort - occasionally a presenting complaint.
  • Ask about vulval ulceration - rarely seen, but can be a presenting complaint.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Trichomonas vaginalis"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - to assess for systemic infection or complications, though often normal in uncomplicated cases.
  • Check vaginal discharge - classically frothy, yellow-green, and malodorous, present in 10-30% of women.
  • Check vulvitis and vaginitis - signs of inflammation and irritation of the vulva and vagina.
  • Check cervix - for the characteristic [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Strawberry cervix: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="strawberry cervix"] appearance (punctate haemorrhages), seen in approximately 2% of cases on naked eye examination.
  • Check urethral discharge in males - typically small to moderate amounts, present in 20-60% of symptomatic men.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Trichomonas vaginalis"][/sc]
  • Persistent symptoms despite treatment - may indicate treatment failure, re-infection, or antimicrobial resistance, warranting further investigation.
  • Lower abdominal pain with systemic symptoms - could suggest pelvic inflammatory disease (PID) or other serious co-infection, requiring urgent assessment.
  • Pregnancy with new or worsening symptoms - Trichomonas vaginalis infection in pregnancy is associated with adverse outcomes such as preterm delivery and low birth weight, necessitating careful management.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Trichomonas vaginalis"][/sc]
  • Asymptomatic infection: A significant proportion of individuals, particularly 10-50% of women and 15-50% of men, may be asymptomatic, serving as reservoirs for transmission. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Asymptomatic Trichomonas vaginalis: why does it matter clinically?" text="Asymptomatic infection"]
  • Men: While often asymptomatic, men can present with urethral discharge, dysuria, urethral irritation, or urinary frequency. Rarely, copious purulent urethral discharge or prostatitis may occur.
  • Pregnancy: Routine screening for T. vaginalis in pregnant women is not currently recommended by BASHH, but infection can lead to adverse pregnancy outcomes such as preterm delivery and low birth weight.
  • HIV-positive individuals: There is an epidemiological association between HIV and trichomoniasis, with evidence that T. vaginalis infection may enhance HIV transmission and vice versa.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Trichomonas vaginalis"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected Trichomonas vaginalis:
    • Nucleic Acid Amplification Tests (NAATs): Offer the highest sensitivity and are considered the \'gold standard\' for detection. They can be performed on vaginal or endocervical swabs and urine samples in women. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="NAATs for Trichomonas vaginalis: what are they and why are they important?" text="NAATs"]
    • Microscopy (wet mount): A rapid, point-of-care test for symptomatic women, identifying motile trichomonads. However, sensitivity is lower (40-60%) than NAATs, and trichomonads quickly lose motility.
    • Point-of-care tests (e.g., OSOM® Trichomonas Rapid Test): Provide results within 30 minutes with higher sensitivity (80-94%) than microscopy, suitable as an alternative to culture or molecular testing.
  • Further Investigations: Screening for coexistent sexually transmitted infections (STIs), including HIV, should be undertaken in anyone diagnosed with TV.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Trichomonas vaginalis"][/sc]
  • A 28-year-old female presents with a 3-day history of frothy, yellow-green vaginal discharge, vulval itching, and dysuria, following unprotected sexual intercourse with a new partner.
  • Young female with acute onset of urogenital symptoms and recent sexual exposure, concerning for sexually transmitted infection, specifically Trichomonas vaginalis.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Trichomonas vaginalis"][/sc]
  • Bacterial Vaginosis (BV) - typically presents with a thin, greyish-white discharge with a \'fishy\' odour, without significant vulval inflammation or frothy discharge.
  • Vulvovaginal Candidiasis (Thrush) - characterised by thick, white, \'cottage cheese\' like discharge, intense vulval itching, and erythema, but usually without frothy discharge or dysuria.
  • Gonorrhoea - often asymptomatic, but can cause purulent discharge, dysuria, and pelvic pain; diagnosis requires specific microbiological testing.
  • Chlamydia - frequently asymptomatic, but can cause mucopurulent cervicitis or urethritis; diagnosis requires specific microbiological testing.
  • Pelvic Inflammatory Disease (PID) - presents with lower abdominal pain, deep dyspareunia, and cervical motion tenderness, often associated with fever and systemic symptoms, which are less common in uncomplicated trichomoniasis.
  • Ectopic Pregnancy - presents with abdominal pain and vaginal bleeding, often with a positive pregnancy test, and can be life-threatening; requires urgent exclusion in sexually active women with abdominal pain.
  • Cervicitis (non-STI) - inflammation of the cervix that can cause vaginal discharge and bleeding, but typically lacks the characteristic frothy discharge and protozoan presence of trichomoniasis.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Trichomonas vaginalis"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Recognition of the infection and appropriate referral to a genito-urinary medicine (GUM) clinic or specialist sexual health service for management.
  • Definitive management: Systemic antibiotic therapy with nitroimidazoles is typically required. Metronidazole 400–500 mg twice daily for 7 days is the recommended regimen. Single-dose regimens are not recommended due to higher rates of treatment failure.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Tests of cure are generally recommended only if symptoms persist or recur after treatment. The optimal timing for NAAT test of cure is four weeks after the start of treatment.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: In cases of treatment failure, re-infection, or suspected resistance, further investigation and higher-dose or alternative nitroimidazole regimens may be considered, often in consultation with specialist services.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Trichomonas vaginalis"][/sc]
  • Adverse pregnancy outcomes: Untreated Trichomonas vaginalis infection, particularly if persistent, is associated with an increased risk of preterm delivery and low birth weight.
  • Increased HIV transmission and acquisition: Trichomoniasis can increase both the risk of acquiring HIV and transmitting it to sexual partners, especially in the progressive and late phases of infection.
  • Pelvic Inflammatory Disease (PID): While not a direct cause, untreated trichomoniasis can contribute to the development or exacerbation of PID, particularly if other co-infections are present.
  • Postpartum sepsis: TV infection at delivery may predispose to maternal postpartum sepsis.
