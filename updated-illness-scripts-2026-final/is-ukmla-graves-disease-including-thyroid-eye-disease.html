Graves disease (including thyroid eye disease) [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Graves disease (including thyroid eye disease)"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Graves disease (including thyroid eye disease)"][/sc]
  • Graves disease is an autoimmune condition characterised by the production of thyroid-stimulating immunoglobulins (TSI) that bind to and activate the TSH receptor on thyroid follicular cells, leading to excessive thyroid hormone synthesis and secretion (hyperthyroidism). It is the most common cause of hyperthyroidism.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical features, diagnostic approach, and management principles of Graves disease, including its ocular manifestations (thyroid eye disease) and potential complications.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Graves disease (including thyroid eye disease)"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Graves disease (including thyroid eye disease)"][/sc]
  • Graves disease affects approximately 1 in 200 people in the UK, with a higher prevalence in women (5-10 times more common than in men), typically presenting between 30 and 50 years of age.
  • Risk factors include a family history of autoimmune thyroid disease, other autoimmune conditions (e.g., type 1 diabetes, pernicious anaemia), smoking (particularly for thyroid eye disease), and stress.
  • The incidence of hyperthyroidism in the UK is estimated at 20-30 cases per 100,000 population per year, with Graves disease accounting for 60-80% of these cases (NICE).
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Graves disease (including thyroid eye disease)"][/sc]
  • Trigger: Genetic predisposition and environmental factors (e.g., stress, smoking, infection) lead to a breakdown in immune tolerance, resulting in the production of autoantibodies, primarily thyroid-stimulating immunoglobulins (TSI).
  • Progression: TSI antibodies bind to and activate the TSH receptors on thyroid follicular cells, mimicking the action of TSH. This persistently stimulates the thyroid gland, leading to hyperplasia, increased vascularity, and autonomous overproduction of thyroid hormones (T3 and T4).
  • Consequence: Elevated thyroid hormone levels accelerate metabolic processes throughout the body, causing a wide range of hyperthyroid symptoms. In thyroid eye disease, autoantibodies also target TSH receptors and insulin-like growth factor 1 receptors on orbital fibroblasts, leading to inflammation, oedema, and fat/muscle expansion behind the eyes.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Graves disease (including thyroid eye disease)"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Symptoms of hyperthyroidism, such as anxiety, palpitations, and weight loss, typically develop insidiously over weeks to months. Thyroid eye disease may present concurrently or up to 6 months before or after the onset of hyperthyroidism.
  • Progressive phase: If untreated, symptoms often worsen, and complications like cardiac arrhythmias or thyroid storm may develop, particularly beyond 6-12 months. Thyroid eye disease can also progress, leading to significant proptosis, diplopia, and vision impairment.
  • Late phase: With appropriate treatment, hyperthyroidism can be controlled, but Graves disease may recur. Thyroid eye disease can enter a quiescent phase, but residual visual impairment or cosmetic changes may persist, particularly beyond 18-24 months from onset.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Graves disease (including thyroid eye disease)"][/sc]
  • Ask about palpitations and tachycardia - indicating increased cardiac output and adrenergic stimulation.
  • Ask about unintentional weight loss despite increased appetite - due to accelerated metabolism.
  • Ask about anxiety, irritability, and tremor - reflecting central nervous system overstimulation.
  • Ask about heat intolerance and increased sweating - a consequence of elevated basal metabolic rate.
  • Ask about changes in vision, gritty sensation, or double vision - suggestive of thyroid eye disease.
  • Ask about fatigue and muscle weakness - despite hypermetabolic state, chronic overstimulation can lead to muscle catabolism.
