Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Zoonotic infections including Lyme disease"][/sc]
  • Lyme disease is a multisystem inflammatory condition caused by the spirochete Borrelia burgdorferi, transmitted to humans through the bite of infected Ixodes ticks [1].
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical presentations of Lyme disease, including erythema migrans and later disseminated manifestations, and understand the principles of its diagnosis and antibiotic management.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Zoonotic infections including Lyme disease"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Zoonotic infections including Lyme disease"][/sc]
  • Infected Ixodes ticks are found throughout the UK and Ireland, with particularly high-risk areas including the South of England and Scottish Highlands [2].
  • The incidence of Lyme disease in England and Wales is estimated to be around 2-3 cases per 100,000 population per year, though this is likely an underestimate due to under-reporting [3].
  • Risk factors include engaging in outdoor activities in grassy and wooded areas where ticks are prevalent, such as hiking, camping, and gardening [2].
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Zoonotic infections including Lyme disease"][/sc]
  • Trigger: Transmission of Borrelia burgdorferi spirochetes into the human bloodstream occurs during prolonged attachment (typically >24-48 hours) of an infected Ixodes tick [2].
  • Progression: The bacteria initially multiply locally in the skin, causing erythema migrans, and then disseminate haematogenously or lymphatically to other organs, including the joints, heart, and nervous system [1].
  • Consequence: An inflammatory response to the spirochetes and their components leads to the diverse clinical manifestations of Lyme disease, including neurological, cardiac, and rheumatological complications [1]. Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Zoonotic infections including Lyme disease"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Erythema migrans typically appears 1 to 4 weeks after a tick bite, but can range from 3 days to 3 months. Early disseminated symptoms, such as flu-like illness, facial palsy, or mild meningitis, can occur weeks to months after infection [1].
  • Progressive phase: Untreated, the infection can progress to more severe manifestations, including Lyme arthritis, carditis (e.g., heart block), or neuroborreliosis (e.g., meningitis, radiculopathy) within weeks to several months [1].
  • Late phase: Chronic manifestations, such as persistent arthritis, neurological symptoms, or acrodermatitis chronica atrophicans, can develop months to years after the initial infection if left untreated [1].
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Zoonotic infections including Lyme disease"][/sc]
  • Ask about erythema migrans - a characteristic expanding red rash, often with central clearing, that is usually not itchy, hot, or painful and appears at the site of a tick bite [2]. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Erythema migrans: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Erythema migrans"][/sc]
  • Ask about flu-like symptoms - fever, sweats, malaise, fatigue, and headache, which can accompany early Lyme disease and may be non-specific [2].
  • Ask about joint pain - migratory arthralgia or inflammatory arthritis affecting one or more large joints, which can fluctuate in severity [2].
  • Ask about neurological symptoms - facial palsy, neck stiffness, severe headache, or paraesthesia, indicating possible neuroborreliosis [2].
  • Ask about cardiac symptoms - palpitations, chest pain, or shortness of breath, suggesting Lyme carditis [2].
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Zoonotic infections including Lyme disease"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - fever may be present in early disseminated disease, but vital signs can be normal. Bradycardia or heart block may indicate Lyme carditis [2].
  • Check skin for erythema migrans - a target-like lesion, often warm but not usually tender or pruritic, expanding over days to weeks [2].
  • Check neurological examination - unilateral or bilateral facial nerve palsy, other cranial neuropathies, or signs of meningitis (e.g., neck stiffness) [2].
  • Check joint examination - effusions, warmth, or tenderness in large joints, particularly the knee, consistent with Lyme arthritis [2].
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Zoonotic infections including Lyme disease"][/sc]
  • New-onset heart block: Suggests Lyme carditis, which can lead to severe arrhythmias and requires urgent cardiac assessment and management [2].
  • Acute neurological deficit: Such as facial palsy, meningitis, or radiculopathy, indicating neuroborreliosis requiring prompt investigation and treatment to prevent long-term sequelae [2].
  • Rapidly expanding erythema migrans with systemic symptoms: While erythema migrans alone is not a red flag, its rapid progression accompanied by severe systemic symptoms may indicate a more aggressive course or early dissemination [2].
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Zoonotic infections including Lyme disease"][/sc]
  • Children: May present with less typical rashes or non-specific symptoms, making diagnosis challenging. Facial palsy is a common early neurological manifestation in children [2].
  • Older adults: Symptoms can be non-specific and mimic other age-related conditions, such as arthritis or neurological disorders, leading to delayed diagnosis [2].
  • Pregnant women: Diagnosis and treatment are similar to non-pregnant individuals, but careful antibiotic selection is crucial to avoid fetal harm. Vertical transmission is rare but possible [2]. Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Zoonotic infections including Lyme disease"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected Lyme disease (without erythema migrans): Enzyme-linked immunosorbent assay (ELISA) for IgM and IgG antibodies. If positive or equivocal, confirm with an immunoblot test [2]. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Lyme disease serology: what are the indications and interpretation of ELISA and immunoblot tests?" text="Lyme disease serology"][/sc]
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Consider tests for other conditions presenting with similar symptoms, such as viral infections, other arthropathies, or neurological disorders [2].
  • Imaging - MRI: May be used for suspected neuroborreliosis to assess for inflammation or white matter changes in the brain or spinal cord [2].
  • Lumbar puncture: For suspected Lyme meningitis or meningoencephalitis, cerebrospinal fluid analysis may show pleocytosis and elevated protein [2].

  • A 45-year-old male presents with a 3-week history of an expanding, non-itchy red rash on his leg, accompanied by migratory joint pains, following a recent camping trip in the Scottish Highlands.
  • Middle-aged male with subacute, migratory arthralgia and a characteristic skin lesion after tick exposure, concerning for disseminated Lyme disease.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Zoonotic infections including Lyme disease"][/sc]
  • Cellulitis - typically painful, hot, and rapidly spreading, often with a clear portal of entry, unlike erythema migrans [2].
  • Erysipelas - a superficial skin infection with well-demarcated borders, usually more painful and febrile than erythema migrans [2].
  • Rheumatoid arthritis - chronic symmetrical polyarthritis, often with morning stiffness and systemic features, distinct from the migratory oligoarthritis of Lyme [2].
  • Reactive arthritis - can follow gastrointestinal or genitourinary infections, often involves asymmetric oligoarthritis, enthesitis, and dactylitis [2].
  • Viral meningitis - typically presents with headache, fever, and neck stiffness, but usually lacks the characteristic rash or other systemic features of Lyme disease [2]. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Viral meningitis vs Lyme meningitis: how to differentiate?" text="Viral meningitis"][/sc]
  • Bell's palsy - idiopathic facial nerve paralysis, a diagnosis of exclusion after ruling out secondary causes like Lyme disease [2].
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Zoonotic infections including Lyme disease"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Recognition of erythema migrans or suspected disseminated disease, prompt initiation of appropriate antibiotic therapy, and referral to specialists for complex presentations (e.g., cardiology for carditis, neurology for neuroborreliosis) [2].
  • Definitive management: Antibiotic therapy is the cornerstone of treatment. The choice and duration of antibiotics depend on the stage and manifestations of the disease [2].
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Clinical review to assess symptom resolution and identify any persistent or new symptoms. Serological testing is not recommended for monitoring treatment response [2].
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Advise patients to seek medical attention if symptoms worsen, new symptoms develop, or if they experience a Jarisch-Herxheimer reaction [2].

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Zoonotic infections including Lyme disease"][/sc]
  • Lyme arthritis: Persistent or recurrent inflammatory arthritis, particularly affecting large joints, can develop weeks to months after infection if untreated, leading to joint damage [1].
  • Neuroborreliosis: Early disseminated forms include facial nerve palsy, meningitis, or radiculopathy. Late forms can involve chronic encephalopathy or polyneuropathy, developing months to years after infection [1].
  • Lyme carditis: Conduction abnormalities, such as atrioventricular block, or myocarditis/pericarditis can occur in the early disseminated phase, potentially leading to syncope or heart failure [1].
  • Post-treatment Lyme disease syndrome (PTLDS): Persistent non-specific symptoms like fatigue, widespread pain, and cognitive difficulties can occur after antibiotic treatment, lasting months to years [2].
