Erythema nodosum [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Erythema nodosum"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Erythema nodosum"][/sc]
  • Erythema nodosum is an acute, inflammatory panniculitis characterised by tender, red nodules, typically on the shins, representing inflammation of subcutaneous fat.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise erythema nodosum as a reactive process, often associated with underlying systemic conditions, and understand its typical clinical presentation and diagnostic approach.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Erythema nodosum"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Erythema nodosum"][/sc]
  • Erythema nodosum is more common in women, with a female-to-male ratio of approximately 3-6:1, particularly affecting those in their 20s and 30s.
  • The incidence in the UK is estimated to be around 1-5 per 100,000 population per year, with a significant proportion being idiopathic.
  • Key risk factors include recent streptococcal infection, sarcoidosis, inflammatory bowel disease (Crohn's disease, ulcerative colitis), certain medications (e.g., oral contraceptives, sulphonamides), and pregnancy.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Erythema nodosum"][/sc]
  • Trigger: An immune-mediated hypersensitivity reaction, often to an antigen from an infection (e.g., Group A Streptococcus) or systemic disease, leads to the formation of immune complexes.
  • Progression: These immune complexes deposit in the venules of the subcutaneous fat, triggering an inflammatory response characterised by septal panniculitis without vasculitis.
  • Consequence: The inflammation results in the characteristic tender, erythematous nodules due to oedema and infiltration of inflammatory cells within the fat septa.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Erythema nodosum"][/sc]
  • Early phase: The onset is typically acute, with tender, erythematous nodules appearing over several days, often accompanied by prodromal symptoms such as fever, malaise, and arthralgia.
  • Progressive phase: Lesions evolve over 1-3 weeks, changing colour from red to purple, then brown or yellow, resembling a bruise, particularly beyond 2 weeks. New lesions may continue to appear during this period.
  • Late phase: Resolution usually occurs spontaneously within 3-6 weeks without ulceration or scarring, though post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation may persist for months.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Erythema nodosum"][/sc]
  • Ask about tender, painful lumps on the shins - this is the hallmark symptom and helps differentiate from other skin lesions.
  • Ask about recent upper respiratory tract infection or sore throat - a common preceding event, suggesting a streptococcal trigger.
  • Ask about joint pain (arthralgia), particularly in the ankles and knees - frequently accompanies erythema nodosum and indicates systemic inflammation.
  • Ask about fever, malaise, and fatigue - non-specific systemic symptoms that often precede or coincide with the skin lesions.
  • Ask about gastrointestinal symptoms (e.g., abdominal pain, diarrhoea) - may suggest underlying inflammatory bowel disease.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Erythema nodosum"][/sc]
  • Check for multiple, bilateral, tender, erythematous nodules, typically 1-5 cm in diameter, predominantly on the anterior aspects of the lower legs - this distribution is characteristic.
  • Check for evolving colour changes of the lesions (red to purple to brownish-yellow) - indicative of resolving inflammation, resembling a bruise.
  • Check for signs of systemic inflammation: fever, tachycardia, or lymphadenopathy - may point to an underlying cause.
  • Check for joint swelling or tenderness - often present, particularly in the ankles, reflecting associated arthralgia.
  • Check vital signs: temperature, heart rate, blood pressure, respiratory rate - to assess for systemic illness or severity.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Erythema nodosum"][/sc]
  • Persistent or recurrent lesions: Suggests an ongoing or poorly controlled underlying systemic condition, warranting further investigation.
  • Ulceration or scarring of lesions: Atypical for erythema nodosum and may indicate a different form of panniculitis or vasculitis, requiring biopsy.
  • Severe systemic symptoms (e.g., high fever, significant weight loss): Raises concern for serious underlying conditions such as malignancy, severe infection, or active systemic autoimmune disease.
  • Respiratory symptoms (e.g., cough, dyspnoea): May point towards sarcoidosis or tuberculosis as an underlying cause.
  • Abnormal liver function tests or jaundice: Could indicate a drug-induced reaction or systemic disease affecting the liver.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Erythema nodosum"][/sc]
  • Children: Often presents with a more prominent febrile illness and is strongly associated with streptococcal infections.
  • Older adults: May have less pronounced tenderness and can be associated with underlying malignancy or drug reactions, making diagnosis more challenging.
  • Pregnancy: Erythema nodosum can occur during pregnancy, often in the first trimester, and typically resolves spontaneously postpartum, but requires careful exclusion of other causes.
  • Immunosuppressed patients: The clinical presentation may be subtle or atypical, and the underlying cause is more likely to be an opportunistic infection or drug reaction.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Erythema nodosum"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected Erythema Nodosum: Full blood count (FBC) to check for anaemia or leucocytosis, erythrocyte sedimentation rate (ESR) and C-reactive protein (CRP) for inflammation, urea and electrolytes (U&Es) and liver function tests (LFTs) to assess organ function.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Antistreptolysin O (ASO) titre or throat swab for streptococcal infection, angiotensin-converting enzyme (ACE) levels and chest X-ray for sarcoidosis, stool culture for gastrointestinal infections, tuberculin skin test or interferon-gamma release assay (IGRA) for tuberculosis.
  • Imaging - Chest X-ray: To screen for hilar lymphadenopathy suggestive of sarcoidosis or tuberculosis.
  • Biopsy: Skin biopsy is usually not required for typical presentations but may be considered for atypical lesions or if other forms of panniculitis are suspected.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Erythema nodosum"][/sc]
  • A 28-year-old female presents with a 2-week history of bilateral, tender, erythematous nodules on her shins, preceded by a sore throat and accompanied by arthralgia and low-grade fever.
  • An acute, self-limiting, inflammatory panniculitis, likely reactive, presenting with characteristic cutaneous lesions and systemic inflammatory features in a young adult.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Erythema nodosum"][/sc]
  • Erythema induratum (Bazin's disease) - typically on calves, often ulcerates, associated with tuberculosis.
  • Nodular vasculitis - often unilateral, may ulcerate, associated with systemic vasculitis.
  • Superficial thrombophlebitis - linear, cord-like lesions, often associated with varicose veins or hypercoagulable states.
  • Cellulitis - poorly demarcated erythema, warmth, rapidly spreading, usually unilateral.
  • Insect bites - often pruritic, central punctum, less diffuse inflammation.
  • Trauma (e.g., contusions) - history of injury, often unilateral, resolves with typical bruising pattern.
  • Panniculitis (other types) - may have different histological features or clinical associations (e.g., pancreatic panniculitis).
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Erythema nodosum"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Reassurance about the benign and self-limiting nature of the condition, and identification and treatment of any underlying cause.
  • Definitive management: Rest, elevation of affected limbs, and compression bandages to reduce oedema and pain. Non-steroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs) such as ibuprofen or naproxen can be used for symptomatic relief.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Monitor for resolution of lesions and symptoms. Follow-up is crucial to ensure the underlying cause is adequately managed or has resolved.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Advise patients to seek medical attention if symptoms worsen, new lesions appear, or if there are signs of systemic illness. Referral to dermatology or rheumatology may be needed for persistent or atypical cases, or if a serious underlying condition is suspected.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Erythema nodosum"][/sc]
  • Post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation: Residual brownish discolouration of the skin at the site of resolved lesions, which can persist for several months but is benign.
  • Recurrence: Erythema nodosum can recur, particularly if the underlying trigger is not identified or adequately managed, leading to prolonged discomfort and diagnostic uncertainty.
  • Chronic pain: In some cases, persistent discomfort or tenderness may linger even after the visible lesions have resolved, impacting quality of life.
  • Psychological distress: The visible nature of the lesions and the associated pain can cause significant anxiety and distress for patients.
