Ehlers-Danlos syndrome [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Ehlers-Danlos syndrome"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Ehlers-Danlos syndrome"][/sc]
  • Ehlers-Danlos syndrome (EDS) is a heterogeneous group of inherited connective tissue disorders primarily affecting collagen synthesis and structure, leading to tissue fragility across multiple organ systems.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical features and genetic basis of Ehlers-Danlos syndrome, understanding its multisystemic impact and the importance of multidisciplinary management.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Ehlers-Danlos syndrome"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Ehlers-Danlos syndrome"][/sc]
  • The prevalence of all types of EDS combined is estimated to be around 1 in 5,000 people in the UK, with hypermobile EDS (hEDS) being the most common subtype [1].
  • Risk factors are primarily genetic, with various inheritance patterns depending on the specific EDS subtype, including autosomal dominant and autosomal recessive forms [2].
  • Specific gene mutations, such as those in *COL5A1*, *COL5A2*, and *COL3A1*, are associated with classical and vascular EDS, respectively [3].
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Ehlers-Danlos syndrome"][/sc]
  • Genetic mutations affecting collagen synthesis or processing (e.g., *COL5A1*, *COL3A1*) lead to structurally abnormal or deficient collagen.
  • This defective collagen impairs the integrity and strength of connective tissues throughout the body, including skin, joints, blood vessels, and internal organs.
  • The consequence is a spectrum of clinical manifestations, such as joint hypermobility, skin hyperextensibility, tissue fragility, and chronic pain, varying in severity and organ involvement depending on the specific EDS subtype.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Ehlers-Danlos syndrome"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Symptoms often manifest in childhood or adolescence, with joint hypermobility and frequent dislocations being common initial presentations, particularly beyond early childhood.
  • Progressive phase: As individuals age, chronic pain, fatigue, and autonomic dysfunction may become more prominent, particularly beyond early adulthood, impacting daily activities and quality of life.
  • Late phase: Severe complications, such as vascular rupture or organ prolapse, can emerge, particularly beyond middle age, necessitating careful monitoring and management.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Ehlers-Danlos syndrome"][/sc]
  • Ask about joint hypermobility - it is a cardinal feature, leading to frequent dislocations, subluxations, and chronic joint pain.
  • Ask about skin hyperextensibility and fragility - this indicates defective collagen, resulting in easy bruising, poor wound healing, and atrophic scarring.
  • Ask about chronic widespread pain and fatigue - these are common and significantly impact quality of life, often due to musculoskeletal instability and autonomic dysfunction.
  • Ask about autonomic symptoms (e.g., postural orthostatic tachycardia syndrome, gastrointestinal dysmotility) - these are frequently co-occurring and contribute to the multisystemic nature of EDS.
  • Ask about family history of similar symptoms or sudden unexplained deaths - this is crucial for identifying genetic predisposition and specific EDS subtypes, especially vascular EDS.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Ehlers-Danlos syndrome"][/sc]
  • Check for generalised joint hypermobility using the Beighton score - a score of 5/9 or more suggests hypermobility, indicating laxity of ligaments and tendons.
  • Check skin texture and elasticity - typically soft, velvety, and hyperextensible, returning to normal slowly after stretching, suggesting collagen deficiency.
  • Check for atrophic scars, particularly over pressure points or areas of trauma - these "cigarette paper" scars are characteristic of impaired wound healing.
  • Check vital signs - orthostatic hypotension or tachycardia may indicate autonomic dysfunction, a common comorbidity.
  • Check for easy bruising or haematomas - indicative of capillary fragility and impaired connective tissue support around blood vessels.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Ehlers-Danlos syndrome"][/sc]
  • Sudden severe abdominal or chest pain: specific implication is potential vascular rupture or organ perforation, particularly in vascular EDS, requiring immediate emergency assessment.
  • Rapidly expanding haematoma: specific implication is arterial or visceral bleeding, especially in individuals with vascular EDS, necessitating urgent investigation and intervention.
  • New onset neurological deficits: specific implication is cervical instability or intracranial vascular complications, which can be life-threatening and require specialist neurological review.
  • Progressive cardiac symptoms (e.g., dyspnoea, palpitations): specific implication is valvular heart disease or aortic root dilatation, common in some EDS subtypes, requiring cardiological assessment.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Ehlers-Danlos syndrome"][/sc]
  • Older adults: Joint hypermobility may decrease with age due to degenerative changes, making diagnosis challenging; chronic pain and fatigue may be attributed to other age-related conditions.
  • Pregnancy: Increased risk of premature rupture of membranes, uterine prolapse, and peripartum haemorrhage due to tissue fragility, requiring careful obstetric management.
  • Immunosuppressed: No direct impact on EDS presentation, but co-existing conditions or treatments may complicate management of chronic pain and fatigue.
  • Children: May present primarily with developmental delay, hypotonia, or frequent injuries, which can be misdiagnosed as non-accidental trauma or other genetic conditions.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Ehlers-Danlos syndrome"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected Ehlers-Danlos syndrome: Clinical assessment based on the revised Brighton criteria for hEDS or specific diagnostic criteria for other subtypes, followed by genetic testing for confirmation.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Consider inflammatory markers (ESR, CRP) to rule out inflammatory arthropathies, or thyroid function tests to exclude endocrine causes of fatigue.
  • Imaging - Echocardiography: Recommended for all EDS patients to screen for aortic root dilatation and valvular abnormalities, particularly in classical and vascular types.
  • Imaging - MRI of spine: May be indicated if neurological symptoms suggest cervical instability or tethered cord syndrome.
  • Genetic testing: Confirmatory for most EDS subtypes, identifying specific gene mutations (e.g., *COL5A1*, *COL3A1*).
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Ehlers-Danlos syndrome"][/sc]
  • A 28-year-old female presents with a lifelong history of recurrent joint dislocations, easily bruised skin, and chronic widespread musculoskeletal pain, exacerbated by fatigue.
  • A young adult female with chronic, multisystemic connective tissue fragility, manifesting as recurrent joint instability, cutaneous signs, and persistent pain, suggestive of an underlying genetic disorder.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Ehlers-Danlos syndrome"][/sc]
  • Marfan syndrome - distinguished by tall stature, arachnodactyly, and specific ocular (lens dislocation) and cardiovascular (aortic dissection) features, often without significant skin hyperextensibility.
  • Loeys-Dietz syndrome - shares vascular fragility and aortic dilatation but typically presents with craniofacial features (e.g., hypertelorism, bifid uvula) not seen in EDS.
  • Osteogenesis imperfecta - primarily characterised by bone fragility and recurrent fractures, with blue sclerae, often without the marked joint hypermobility or skin changes of EDS.
  • Rheumatoid arthritis - distinguished by inflammatory joint pain, morning stiffness, and symmetrical small joint involvement, with positive autoantibodies, unlike the mechanical pain and hypermobility of EDS.
  • Fibromyalgia - presents with widespread chronic pain and fatigue but lacks the objective signs of connective tissue fragility (e.g., joint hypermobility, skin changes) seen in EDS.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Ehlers-Danlos syndrome"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Manage acute dislocations and pain exacerbations; assess for and address any red flag features, particularly vascular complications.
  • Definitive management: Multidisciplinary approach focusing on symptom management, including physiotherapy for joint stability, pain management strategies, and occupational therapy for adaptive techniques.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Regular cardiology surveillance for aortic dilatation, genetic counselling, and ongoing specialist review for chronic pain and fatigue.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Educate patients on red flag symptoms requiring urgent medical attention (e.g., sudden severe pain, signs of bleeding); provide emergency contact information.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Ehlers-Danlos syndrome"][/sc]
  • Chronic pain: Mechanism is joint instability, tissue fragility, and neuropathic components, leading to significant disability and reduced quality of life.
  • Joint dislocations and subluxations: Mechanism is ligamentous laxity, causing recurrent joint damage, functional impairment, and increased pain.
  • Vascular rupture: Mechanism is fragile arterial walls, particularly in vascular EDS, leading to life-threatening haemorrhage and organ ischaemia.
  • Organ prolapse: Mechanism is weakened connective tissue support for internal organs (e.g., uterus, bladder, rectum), resulting in functional impairment and discomfort.
  • Gastrointestinal dysmotility: Mechanism is impaired connective tissue in the gut wall and autonomic dysfunction, leading to symptoms like gastroparesis, reflux, and constipation.
