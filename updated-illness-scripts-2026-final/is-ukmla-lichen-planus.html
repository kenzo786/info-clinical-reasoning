Lichen planus (Skin and soft tissue) [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Lichen planus"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Lichen planus"][/sc]
  • Lichen planus is a chronic inflammatory condition affecting the skin, hair, nails, and mucous membranes, characterised by distinctive pruritic, purple, polygonal, planar papules and plaques.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the characteristic clinical features of lichen planus, differentiate it from other inflammatory dermatoses, and understand its immune-mediated pathophysiology.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Lichen planus"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Lichen planus"][/sc]
  • Affects approximately 1 in 5,000 people in the UK, with most cases occurring in individuals over 45 years of age, though it can manifest at any age [1].
  • Risk factors include certain medications (e.g., antimalarials, beta-blockers, NSAIDs, thiazide diuretics), contact with photographic developing chemicals, and an association with hepatitis C virus infection [1].
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Lichen planus"][/sc]
  • Trigger: The exact trigger is unknown, but it is thought to involve an immune-mediated response where T-lymphocytes attack basal keratinocytes in the skin and mucous membranes.
  • Progression: This cellular attack leads to inflammation and damage, resulting in the characteristic clinical lesions. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Lichenoid reaction: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Lichenoid reactions"] are a key histological feature.
  • Consequence: The inflammatory process causes hyperkeratosis, acanthosis, and degeneration of basal cells, leading to the formation of papules, plaques, and mucosal changes.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Lichen planus"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Onset can be acute, with the rash developing rapidly over a few days, or more gradually over several weeks [1].
  • Progressive phase: Cutaneous lichen planus typically lasts between 6 to 18 months, with new lesions potentially appearing as older ones fade [1]. Oral lichen planus can persist for several years [1].
  • Late phase: Resolution often leaves post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation, which can take months to years to completely fade [1]. Recurrence is possible in approximately 1 in 5 individuals with cutaneous involvement [1].
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Lichen planus"][/sc]
  • Ask about pruritus - often severe and can disturb sleep, leading to rubbing rather than scratching [1].
  • Ask about skin lesions - typically presenting as clusters of shiny, raised, purple-red blotches (papules and plaques), often on the inner wrists, lower legs, and lower back [1].
  • Ask about oral symptoms - white patches on the gums, tongue, or buccal mucosa, which may be painless or cause burning and stinging, especially with hot or spicy foods [1].
  • Ask about genital involvement - painful red sore areas or white streaks on the vulva or glans penis, which can cause dyspareunia in women [1].
  • Ask about nail changes - minor ridging or, rarely, severe damage leading to nail dystrophy [1].
  • Ask about hair loss - bald patches on the scalp, indicative of lichen planopilaris [1].
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Lichen planus"][/sc]
  • Check for the 6 Ps: Planar (flat-topped), Purple, Polygonal, Pruritic, Papules, and Plaques - these are the classic cutaneous manifestations.
  • Check for [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Wickham striae: what are they and why do they matter clinically?" text="Wickham striae"] - fine white lacy streaks often visible on the surface of skin lesions or within oral white patches, pathognomonic for lichen planus [1].
  • Check oral mucosa - for white lacy patterns (reticular lichen planus) or, less commonly, erosive lesions or desquamative gingivitis [1].
  • Check nails - for longitudinal ridging, thinning, or pterygium formation.
  • Check for Koebner phenomenon - new lesions appearing along lines of trauma, indicating disease activity.
  • Check vital signs - usually normal, as lichen planus is primarily a dermatological condition without systemic inflammatory response in most cases.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Lichen planus"][/sc]
  • Erosive oral or genital lichen planus - persistent, painful ulceration may indicate a higher risk of malignant transformation (squamous cell carcinoma) [1].
  • Extensive skin involvement - severe, widespread cutaneous lichen planus may warrant systemic therapy and closer monitoring for complications.
  • Rapidly progressive hair loss - suggests lichen planopilaris, which can lead to permanent scarring alopecia if not managed promptly.
  • Oesophageal involvement - dysphagia or odynophagia may indicate oesophageal lichen planus, requiring specialist gastroenterological assessment.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Lichen planus"][/sc]
  • Hypertrophic Lichen Planus: Presents as thick, verrucous plaques, often on the shins, which can be intensely pruritic and difficult to treat.
  • Atrophic Lichen Planus: Characterised by depressed, whitish-blue lesions, often seen on the trunk or limbs, representing a rare variant.
  • Follicular Lichen Planus (Lichen Planopilaris): Affects hair follicles, leading to perifollicular erythema, scaling, and progressive scarring alopecia, particularly on the scalp. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Lichen planopilaris: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Lichen planopilaris"] requires early diagnosis to prevent irreversible hair loss.
  • Bullous Lichen Planus: A rare form where vesicles and bullae develop on pre-existing lichen planus lesions or normal skin, often requiring differentiation from other bullous dermatoses.
  • Lichen Planus Pigmentosus: Presents as diffuse or reticulated grey-brown macules, typically in sun-exposed areas, more common in individuals with darker skin types.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Lichen planus"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected Lichen planus: Clinical diagnosis is often sufficient based on characteristic morphology and distribution.
  • If diagnosis is uncertain or for atypical presentations: Skin biopsy with histopathological examination is the gold standard, revealing characteristic features such as a band-like lymphocytic infiltrate at the dermo-epidermal junction, basal cell degeneration, and hypergranulosis. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Skin biopsy in dermatology: when is it indicated and what does it show?" text="Skin biopsy"] helps confirm the diagnosis and exclude mimics.
  • Severity/risk tools: No widely validated severity or risk scores are routinely used in clinical practice for lichen planus.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Consider investigations for conditions in the differential diagnosis (e.g., fungal scrapings for tinea, serology for syphilis).
  • Hepatitis C screening: Consider testing for hepatitis C virus, particularly in patients with widespread or persistent lichen planus, given the known association.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Lichen planus"][/sc]
  • A 55-year-old female presents with a 3-month history of intensely itchy, purple, flat-topped papules on her wrists and ankles, accompanied by white lacy patches in her mouth.
  • Middle-aged adult with chronic pruritic, polygonal, violaceous papules and oral mucosal changes, concerning for lichen planus.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Lichen planus"][/sc]
  • Eczema: Often presents with erythema, scaling, and excoriation, but typically lacks the purple, polygonal morphology and Wickham striae of lichen planus.
  • Psoriasis: Characterised by well-demarcated erythematous plaques with silvery scales, usually on extensor surfaces, and typically not pruritic in the same way as lichen planus.
  • Lichenoid drug eruption: Clinically and histologically similar to lichen planus, but with a clear temporal association with drug intake. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Lichenoid drug eruption: how does it differ from lichen planus?" text="Lichenoid drug eruptions"] resolve upon withdrawal of the offending agent.
  • Pityriasis rosea: Presents with a herald patch followed by smaller, oval, erythematous, scaly lesions in a \'Christmas tree\' distribution, typically self-limiting and less pruritic.
  • Graft-versus-host disease (GVHD): Can mimic lichen planus, especially in its chronic form, and occurs in patients post-allogeneic stem cell transplant.
  • Oral candidiasis: White patches in the mouth that can be scraped off, unlike the adherent white lacy patterns of oral lichen planus.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Lichen planus"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Symptomatic relief of pruritus and pain, especially in erosive forms. Avoidance of triggers (e.g., certain medications, irritants).
  • Definitive management: Topical corticosteroids are first-line for localised cutaneous lichen planus. Potent or very potent preparations are often required [1].
  • Adjunctive management: Oral antihistamines may support symptomatic relief of pruritus, particularly sedating antihistamines at night [1]. Emollients can help moisturise the skin and reduce itching [1].
  • Systemic therapies: For severe, widespread, or recalcitrant cases, systemic corticosteroids may be considered for short courses [1]. Other options include phototherapy (PUVA) or immunosuppressants, often managed by specialists [1].
  • Oral lichen planus management: Topical corticosteroids in gels, pastes, or mouthwashes are often used for oral lesions [1].
  • Exam pharmacology reference (UKMLA/MRCGP): Systemic corticosteroids (e.g., prednisolone) are often used for severe cases. Topical corticosteroids (e.g., clobetasol propionate) are the mainstay for localised disease. Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Lichen planus"][/sc]
  • Post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation: Common after resolution of cutaneous lesions, particularly in individuals with darker skin types, and can persist for months to years [1].
  • Scarring alopecia: In lichen planopilaris, inflammation around hair follicles can lead to permanent hair loss and scarring of the scalp.
  • Nail dystrophy: Severe nail involvement can result in permanent nail damage, including anonychia (absence of nails) or pterygium formation.
  • Malignant transformation: Chronic erosive oral or genital lichen planus carries a small but increased risk of developing squamous cell carcinoma, necessitating regular monitoring [1].
  • Psychological impact: Chronic pruritus, visible lesions, and involvement of sensitive areas can significantly impact quality of life, leading to distress and anxiety.
