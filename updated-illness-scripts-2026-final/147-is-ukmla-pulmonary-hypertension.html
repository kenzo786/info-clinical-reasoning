Pulmonary hypertension [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Pulmonary hypertension"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Pulmonary hypertension"][/sc]
  • Pulmonary hypertension (PH) is a haemodynamic condition characterised by elevated mean pulmonary arterial pressure (mPAP) >20 mmHg at rest, as assessed by right heart catheterisation.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical features of pulmonary hypertension, understand its classification, and identify the role of right heart catheterisation in diagnosis.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Pulmonary hypertension"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Pulmonary hypertension"][/sc]
  • Pulmonary hypertension is a rare condition in the UK, affecting approximately 55 people per million, with around 4,000 individuals currently affected [3].
  • The annual incidence of pulmonary arterial hypertension (PAH) in the UK was estimated at 7.1 cases per million population in 2002, with a prevalence of 52 cases per million [2].
  • Risk factors include underlying conditions such as left-sided heart disease, chronic lung diseases (e.g., COPD), hypoxia, and recurrent pulmonary emboli [4].
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Pulmonary hypertension"][/sc]
  • Initial triggers, such as genetic predisposition, connective tissue disease, or chronic hypoxia, lead to endothelial dysfunction and remodelling of the pulmonary vasculature.
  • This progression involves proliferation of smooth muscle cells, fibrosis, and vasoconstriction, resulting in narrowing and stiffening of the pulmonary arteries, increasing pulmonary vascular resistance [4].
  • The consequence is increased workload on the right ventricle, leading to right ventricular hypertrophy and eventual right heart failure [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Right heart failure: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="right heart failure"][/sc].
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Pulmonary hypertension"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Often asymptomatic or with non-specific symptoms such as mild dyspnoea on exertion, which may be present for months to years and can be easily overlooked.
  • Progressive phase: As pulmonary vascular resistance increases, symptoms like increasing dyspnoea, fatigue, and chest pain become more prominent, typically over several months.
  • Late phase: Characterised by overt right heart failure, with symptoms such as peripheral oedema, syncope, and severe exercise limitation, often developing over weeks to months, particularly beyond the point of significant right ventricular dysfunction.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Pulmonary hypertension"][/sc]
  • Ask about progressive shortness of breath - often initially on exertion, indicating reduced cardiac reserve and increasing pulmonary vascular resistance.
  • Ask about fatigue and weakness - reflecting reduced cardiac output and impaired oxygen delivery to tissues.
  • Ask about chest pain (angina) - may be due to right ventricular ischaemia from increased workload or compression of coronary arteries.
  • Ask about dizziness or syncope - particularly on exertion, suggesting inadequate cerebral perfusion due to fixed cardiac output in the face of increased demand.
  • Ask about peripheral oedema or abdominal swelling - indicating fluid retention secondary to right heart failure.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Pulmonary hypertension"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - may reveal tachycardia, tachypnoea, and low blood pressure in advanced disease, reflecting cardiac compromise.
  • Check for elevated jugular venous pressure (JVP) - a sign of increased right atrial pressure and right heart failure.
  • Check for a loud P2 heart sound - indicative of increased pulmonary artery pressure [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Loud P2 heart sound: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="loud P2 heart sound"][/sc].
  • Check for peripheral oedema (e.g., ankle swelling) and hepatomegaly - signs of systemic venous congestion due to right heart failure.
  • Check for a right ventricular heave or parasternal impulse - suggesting right ventricular hypertrophy.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Pulmonary hypertension"][/sc]
  • New onset or worsening syncope: Suggests severe right ventricular dysfunction and critically low cardiac output, requiring urgent assessment.
  • Rapidly progressive dyspnoea: Indicates acute decompensation or rapid progression of the underlying pulmonary vascular disease, necessitating immediate intervention.
  • Signs of acute right heart failure: Such as rapidly worsening peripheral oedema, ascites, or hypotension, signifying imminent cardiovascular collapse.
  • Haemoptysis: May indicate pulmonary infarction or rupture of pulmonary vessels, requiring urgent investigation.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Pulmonary hypertension"][/sc]
  • Older adults: May present with non-specific symptoms like general weakness or reduced mobility, often attributed to ageing, delaying diagnosis.
  • Patients with connective tissue disease: PH may develop insidiously and be masked by other systemic symptoms, requiring vigilance for early detection [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Connective tissue disease and pulmonary hypertension: what is the link and why does it matter clinically?" text="connective tissue disease-associated PH"][/sc].
  • Pregnancy: PH can be exacerbated by the physiological changes of pregnancy, leading to rapid deterioration and high maternal mortality, often presenting as severe dyspnoea.
  • Children: Presentation can be subtle, with poor feeding, failure to thrive, or exercise intolerance, often misdiagnosed as asthma or recurrent respiratory infections.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Pulmonary hypertension"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected pulmonary hypertension:
    • Echocardiography: Initial screening tool to estimate pulmonary artery pressures and assess right ventricular function.
    • Right heart catheterisation: Gold standard for definitive diagnosis, measuring mPAP, pulmonary capillary wedge pressure (PCWP), and pulmonary vascular resistance (PVR) [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Right heart catheterisation: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="right heart catheterisation"][/sc].
  • Severity/risk tools:
    • WHO functional class: Assesses symptom severity and exercise capacity.
    • Pulmonary Hypertension Risk Score on MDCalc: Integrates clinical, functional, and haemodynamic parameters to predict prognosis.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible:
    • Chest X-ray and CT scan: To evaluate for underlying lung disease or thromboembolic disease.
    • Ventilation-perfusion (V/Q) scan: To rule out chronic thromboembolic pulmonary hypertension (CTEPH).
  • Imaging - MRI: Cardiac MRI can provide detailed assessment of right ventricular structure and function.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Pulmonary hypertension"][/sc]
  • A 55-year-old female with a history of systemic sclerosis presents with progressively worsening dyspnoea on exertion, fatigue, and recent onset ankle swelling over the past six months.
  • Chronic dyspnoea and fatigue with signs of right heart strain in a patient with connective tissue disease, concerning for pulmonary hypertension.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Pulmonary hypertension"][/sc]
  • Left heart failure - distinguished by elevated PCWP on right heart catheterisation and echocardiographic evidence of left ventricular dysfunction.
  • Chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD) - distinguished by significant airflow obstruction on spirometry and often less severe PH.
  • Interstitial lung disease - distinguished by characteristic imaging findings and restrictive lung physiology.
  • Chronic thromboembolic pulmonary hypertension (CTEPH) - distinguished by perfusion defects on V/Q scan and evidence of organised thrombi on pulmonary angiography [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Chronic thromboembolic pulmonary hypertension: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="CTEPH"][/sc].
  • Anaemia - distinguished by low haemoglobin levels and resolution of symptoms with iron supplementation or transfusion.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Pulmonary hypertension"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Recognition and escalation to specialist centres, supportive care including oxygen therapy and diuretics for fluid overload, and management of any acute precipitants.
  • Definitive management: Often includes targeted pulmonary vasodilator therapies (e.g., prostacyclin analogues, endothelin receptor antagonists, phosphodiesterase-5 inhibitors) based on WHO group classification and risk assessment.
  • Adjunctive management: May support with anticoagulation in selected cases, and pulmonary rehabilitation to improve exercise capacity and quality of life.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Regular assessment of symptoms, functional class, echocardiography, and repeat right heart catheterisation to monitor disease progression and treatment response.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Patients should be advised on symptoms of worsening PH or right heart failure, and when to seek urgent medical attention.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Pulmonary hypertension"][/sc]
  • Right heart failure: The most common and serious complication, developing progressively over months to years as the right ventricle struggles against increased afterload, leading to systemic congestion and reduced cardiac output.
  • Arrhythmias: Both atrial and ventricular arrhythmias can occur due to right atrial and ventricular dilation and fibrosis, particularly in the later stages of the disease.
  • Syncope and sudden cardiac death: Resulting from severe right ventricular dysfunction and inability to augment cardiac output during exertion, often occurring in the progressive or late phases.
  • Pulmonary haemorrhage: Can occur due to rupture of fragile pulmonary vessels, especially in severe cases, presenting as haemoptysis.
  • Thromboembolism: Increased risk of in-situ thrombosis within the pulmonary arteries, particularly in patients with PAH, contributing to disease progression.
