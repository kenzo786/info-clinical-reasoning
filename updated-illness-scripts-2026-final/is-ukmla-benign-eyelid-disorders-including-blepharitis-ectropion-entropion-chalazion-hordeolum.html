kb-accordion kb-parent"> Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Benign eyelid disorders (including blepharitis, ectropion/ entropion, chalazion, hordeolum)"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Acute onset of symptoms such as irritation, redness, or a lump, often resolving within days to a week with conservative measures.
  • Progressive phase: Symptoms persist or recur, indicating chronic inflammation or structural changes, particularly beyond a few weeks, requiring ongoing management.
  • Late phase: Established chronic disease with potential for complications like corneal involvement or persistent eyelid malposition, particularly beyond several months without effective intervention.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Benign eyelid disorders (including blepharitis, ectropion/ entropion, chalazion, hordeolum)"][/sc]
  • Ask about gritty sensation - often indicates ocular surface irritation from tear film instability or foreign body sensation.
  • Ask about redness or swelling of the eyelids - suggests inflammation (blepharitis, hordeolum) or allergic reaction.
  • Ask about discharge or crusting on eyelashes - characteristic of blepharitis, particularly in the mornings.
  • Ask about a lump or swelling on the eyelid - points towards chalazion (painless) or hordeolum (painful).
  • Ask about excessive tearing or dry eyes - can be paradoxical in ectropion (tearing) or entropion (irritation leading to reflex tearing, or dry eye from poor lid apposition).
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Benign eyelid disorders (including blepharitis, ectropion/ entropion, chalazion, hordeolum)"][/sc]
  • Check visual acuity and extraocular movements - to rule out more serious orbital pathology.
  • Check eyelid margins for redness, scaling, or telangiectasias - indicative of blepharitis.
  • Check for inward (entropion) or outward (ectropion) turning of the eyelid margin - directly visualises the malposition.
  • Check for a palpable, non-tender nodule within the eyelid - suggests a chalazion.
  • Check vital signs - to assess for systemic infection or inflammation, though usually normal in benign eyelid disorders.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Benign eyelid disorders (including blepharitis, ectropion/ entropion, chalazion, hordeolum)"][/sc]
  • Sudden, painful vision loss - suggests corneal involvement or orbital cellulitis.
  • Proptosis or diplopia - indicates orbital pathology beyond the eyelid.
  • Fixed, firm, or irregular eyelid lesion - raises suspicion for malignancy, especially if associated with loss of eyelashes.
  • Systemic signs of infection (fever, malaise) - may indicate preseptal or orbital cellulitis.
  • Eyelid swelling extending beyond the orbital rim - concerning for orbital cellulitis.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Benign eyelid disorders (including blepharitis, ectropion/ entropion, chalazion, hordeolum)"][/sc]
  • Older adults: May present with more severe or chronic forms of blepharitis due to age-related meibomian gland dysfunction, or recurrent chalazia.
  • Children: Hordeola are common, but recurrent or persistent chalazia may warrant investigation for underlying refractive error or systemic conditions.
  • Immunosuppressed: Increased risk of severe or atypical infections, requiring a lower threshold for aggressive management and systemic antibiotics.
  • Contact lens wearers: Higher incidence of blepharitis and meibomian gland dysfunction, often exacerbated by poor lens hygiene.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Benign eyelid disorders (including blepharitis, ectropion/ entropion, chalazion, hordeolum)"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected benign eyelid disorder: Clinical examination with good illumination and magnification (e.g., slit lamp if available) is usually sufficient for diagnosis.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Swab for bacterial culture if severe or recurrent infection, or if not responding to standard treatment.
  • Imaging - CT/MRI orbit: Consider if red flag features suggest orbital involvement (e.g., proptosis, diplopia, vision loss) to rule out orbital cellulitis or tumour.
  • Biopsy: For suspicious or recurrent lesions that do not resolve, to rule out malignancy.

  • A 68-year-old female presents with a 3-month history of bilateral gritty eyes, red eyelid margins, and crusting on her eyelashes, worse in the mornings.
  • An elderly female with chronic bilateral eyelid inflammation, presenting with ocular surface irritation and morning crusting, consistent with chronic blepharitis.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Benign eyelid disorders (including blepharitis, ectropion/ entropion, chalazion, hordeolum)"][/sc]
  • Conjunctivitis - diffuse redness, often with discharge, but typically spares eyelid margins.
  • Allergic eye disease - intense itching, bilateral, often seasonal, with chemosis.
  • Dry eye syndrome - chronic gritty sensation, often worse with reading or screen use, but without prominent eyelid inflammation.
  • Preseptal cellulitis - diffuse, tender eyelid swelling, often with fever, but globe is not involved.
  • Orbital cellulitis - painful proptosis, ophthalmoplegia, vision loss, systemic illness.
  • Eyelid malignancy (e.g., basal cell carcinoma) - persistent, irregular lesion, often with loss of eyelashes, not resolving with conservative treatment.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Benign eyelid disorders (including blepharitis, ectropion/ entropion, chalazion, hordeolum)"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Reassurance, education on eyelid hygiene (warm compresses, lid massage, gentle cleansing), and artificial tears for symptomatic relief.
  • Definitive management: For blepharitis, long-term eyelid hygiene. For hordeolum/chalazion, warm compresses; incision and drainage for persistent chalazion. For ectropion/entropion, surgical correction is often required.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Review response to conservative measures; refer to ophthalmology for persistent or recurrent lesions, or for surgical correction of malpositions.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Advise patients to seek urgent medical attention for worsening pain, vision changes, or signs of spreading infection.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Benign eyelid disorders (including blepharitis, ectropion/ entropion, chalazion, hordeolum)"][/sc]
  • Corneal ulceration: Due to chronic irritation from entropion or severe blepharitis, leading to pain and potential vision loss.
  • Conjunctivitis: Secondary to chronic inflammation or bacterial overgrowth from blepharitis, causing redness and discharge.
  • Recurrent chalazion/hordeolum: Indicative of underlying chronic meibomian gland dysfunction or incomplete resolution, requiring ongoing management.
  • Eyelid scarring or distortion: From chronic inflammation or repeated surgical interventions, affecting cosmesis and function.
  • Vision impairment: If severe corneal involvement or chronic tear film instability affects visual acuity.
