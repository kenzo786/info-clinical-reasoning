Benign prostatic hyperplasia [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Benign prostatic hyperplasia"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Benign prostatic hyperplasia"][/sc]
  • Benign prostatic hyperplasia (BPH) is a non-malignant, age-related enlargement of the prostate gland, leading to lower urinary tract symptoms (LUTS) due to bladder outlet obstruction.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical features, common complications, and initial management principles of benign prostatic hyperplasia, including when to refer for specialist assessment.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Benign prostatic hyperplasia"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Benign prostatic hyperplasia"][/sc]
  • The prevalence of histological BPH increases with age, affecting approximately 50% of men aged 51-60 years and up to 90% of men over 80 years.
  • Clinical BPH, characterised by bothersome LUTS, affects about one-third of men over 50 years in the UK.
  • Risk factors include increasing age, genetic predisposition (family history), and hormonal factors (androgens).
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Benign prostatic hyperplasia"][/sc]
  • Trigger: Androgen-driven stromal and epithelial cell proliferation within the prostate\'s transition zone, often influenced by chronic inflammation and growth factors.
  • Progression: The enlarging prostate compresses the urethra, leading to increased urethral resistance and bladder outlet obstruction (BOO).
  • Consequence: The bladder detrusor muscle hypertrophies and becomes less compliant to overcome BOO, eventually leading to detrusor instability, incomplete emptying, and potential renal impairment. Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Benign prostatic hyperplasia"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Gradual onset of mild storage LUTS (e.g., increased frequency, nocturia) and voiding LUTS (e.g., hesitancy, weak stream) over months to years, often initially intermittent.
  • Progressive phase: Symptoms typically worsen over several years, becoming more persistent and bothersome, with increasing impact on quality of life and potential for recurrent urinary tract infections.
  • Late phase: If untreated, symptoms may progress to severe BOO, leading to complications such as acute urinary retention, chronic kidney disease, or bladder calculi, particularly beyond several years of significant obstruction.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Benign prostatic hyperplasia"][/sc]
  • Ask about storage LUTS (frequency, urgency, nocturia) - these reflect bladder irritability and reduced functional capacity due to BOO.
  • Ask about voiding LUTS (hesitancy, weak stream, straining, intermittency, incomplete emptying, post-micturition dribble) - these directly indicate bladder outlet obstruction.
  • Ask about the International Prostate Symptom Score (IPSS) - a validated questionnaire to quantify symptom severity and impact on quality of life, useful for monitoring.
  • Ask about haematuria - may occur due to prostatic bleeding or associated bladder pathology, requiring further investigation.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Benign prostatic hyperplasia"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - usually normal in uncomplicated BPH; fever or tachycardia may suggest infection (e.g., prostatitis, pyelonephritis) or sepsis in acute retention.
  • Check abdominal examination - palpate for a distended bladder, indicating urinary retention; suprapubic tenderness may suggest cystitis.
  • Check digital rectal examination (DRE) - assesses prostate size, consistency, and presence of nodules; in BPH, the prostate is typically enlarged, smooth, and firm. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Digital rectal examination in BPH: what does it reveal and why is it important?" text="Digital rectal examination"][/sc]
  • Check neurological examination (lower limbs, perineum) - to exclude neurogenic bladder as a cause of LUTS.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Benign prostatic hyperplasia"][/sc]
  • New onset LUTS under 40 years: Suggests alternative diagnoses such as prostatitis, urethral stricture, or neurological conditions, as BPH is rare in this age group.
  • Haematuria (unexplained): Particularly macroscopic haematuria, requires urgent investigation to exclude malignancy (bladder or prostate cancer).
  • Renal impairment: Elevated creatinine or urea may indicate obstructive uropathy and potential kidney damage, necessitating prompt urological assessment.
  • Recurrent urinary tract infections: Suggests incomplete bladder emptying, which can predispose to infection and potentially lead to urosepsis.
  • Palpable bladder or acute urinary retention: Indicates significant bladder outlet obstruction requiring immediate intervention to relieve pressure and prevent renal damage.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Benign prostatic hyperplasia"][/sc]
  • Younger men (under 50): BPH is less common; consider other causes of LUTS such as prostatitis, urethral stricture, or neurogenic bladder.
  • Predominant storage symptoms with small prostate: May indicate overactive bladder syndrome rather than significant BOO from BPH.
  • Silent BPH: Some men may develop significant BOO and even renal impairment without experiencing bothersome LUTS, often detected incidentally.
  • Acute urinary retention as first presentation: Can occur without prior significant LUTS, often precipitated by factors like cold medications or immobility. Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Benign prostatic hyperplasia"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected BPH: Urine dipstick (to exclude infection/haematuria), serum creatinine (to assess renal function), prostate-specific antigen (PSA) if clinically indicated after discussion of risks/benefits.
  • Severity/risk tools: International Prostate Symptom Score (IPSS) questionnaire to quantify symptom severity and impact on quality of life. International Prostate Symptom Score (IPSS) on MDCalc
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Urine culture (for infection), urine cytology (for bladder malignancy if haematuria present), neurological assessment.
  • Imaging - Ultrasound: Renal and bladder ultrasound to assess for hydronephrosis, bladder wall thickening, bladder calculi, and post-void residual volume. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Post-void residual volume: what does it indicate and why is it measured in BPH?" text="Post-void residual volume"][/sc]
  • Serial reassessment: Regular review of symptoms (e.g., IPSS), physical examination, and renal function in men managed conservatively.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Benign prostatic hyperplasia"][/sc]
  • Prostate cancer - may present with similar LUTS, but often associated with elevated PSA and/or prostatic nodules on DRE.
  • Prostatitis (acute/chronic) - typically presents with pain (perineal, suprapubic), fever (acute), and dysuria, often in younger men.
  • Urethral stricture - characterised by a progressively worsening, very weak stream and often a history of urethral trauma or instrumentation.
  • Overactive bladder syndrome - predominantly storage LUTS (urgency, frequency, nocturia) without significant BOO, often responsive to anticholinergics.
  • Bladder calculi - can cause intermittent BOO, pain, and haematuria, often associated with recurrent UTIs.
  • Neurogenic bladder - LUTS due to neurological conditions (e.g., Parkinson\'s, stroke, spinal cord injury) affecting bladder control. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Neurogenic bladder: what are its causes and how does it differ from BPH?" text="Neurogenic bladder"][/sc]
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Benign prostatic hyperplasia"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: For acute urinary retention, immediate catheterisation is often warranted to relieve obstruction and prevent renal damage.
  • Definitive management: Initial management often includes lifestyle advice (e.g., fluid modification, timed voiding) and watchful waiting for mild symptoms. Pharmacological options include alpha-blockers (e.g., tamsulosin) to relax prostatic smooth muscle, and 5-alpha reductase inhibitors (e.g., finasteride) to reduce prostate size. Surgical intervention (e.g., TURP) may be considered for severe symptoms or complications.
  • Adjunctive management: Management of associated complications such as urinary tract infections.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Regular assessment of symptom severity (IPSS), flow rates, and post-void residual volume to monitor treatment efficacy and disease progression.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Patients should be advised to seek urgent medical attention for worsening symptoms, inability to pass urine, fever, or significant haematuria.
  • Exam pharmacology reference (UKMLA/MRCGP): Alpha-blockers (e.g., [sc name="jitl-medication" Q="Tamsulosin use in Benign prostatic hyperplasia" text="tamsulosin"][/sc]) work by relaxing smooth muscle in the prostate and bladder neck, reducing urethral resistance. 5-alpha reductase inhibitors (e.g., [sc name="jitl-medication" Q="Finasteride use in Benign prostatic hyperplasia" text="finasteride"][/sc]) reduce prostate volume by inhibiting the conversion of testosterone to dihydrotestosterone. Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Benign prostatic hyperplasia"][/sc]
  • Acute urinary retention: The inability to pass urine, often presenting suddenly, which can lead to significant pain and requires urgent catheterisation to prevent bladder and renal damage. This typically occurs in the progressive or late phases of BPH.
  • Recurrent urinary tract infections: Incomplete bladder emptying due to BOO creates a residual urine pool, fostering bacterial growth and increasing the risk of cystitis or pyelonephritis, particularly as the condition progresses over several years.
  • Bladder calculi: Stasis of urine and chronic infection can lead to the formation of bladder stones, which may exacerbate BOO, cause pain, or lead to haematuria.
  • Hydronephrosis and renal impairment: Chronic, unremitting BOO can cause back pressure on the kidneys, leading to dilatation of the renal collecting system (hydronephrosis) and progressive decline in renal function, often seen in the late phase.
  • Bladder diverticula: Chronic high intravesical pressure can cause herniation of the bladder mucosa through the detrusor muscle, forming diverticula which can further impair emptying and predispose to infection.
