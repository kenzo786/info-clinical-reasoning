Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Necrotising enterocolitis"][/sc]
  • Necrotising enterocolitis (NEC) is a severe inflammatory disease of the immature intestine, primarily affecting premature neonates, leading to ischaemic necrosis and potentially perforation.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical features and radiological signs of necrotising enterocolitis in a premature neonate, understand the principles of its initial management, and identify indications for surgical consultation.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Necrotising enterocolitis"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Necrotising enterocolitis"][/sc]
  • NEC affects approximately 10% of prematurely born neonates, with an incidence of severe NEC around 0.4% in UK neonatal units.
  • Mortality rates for NEC can be as high as 50%, with a significant proportion of deaths occurring after surgical intervention.
  • Risk factors include prematurity (especially very low birth weight), formula feeding, intestinal ischaemia, and bacterial colonisation.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Necrotising enterocolitis"][/sc]
  • Trigger: The immature gastrointestinal tract of premature infants, often exacerbated by factors like formula feeding and intestinal ischaemia, creates an environment susceptible to bacterial overgrowth and dysbiosis.
  • Progression: An exaggerated inflammatory response to intestinal microbiota, coupled with impaired mucosal barrier function, leads to localised ischaemia and necrosis of the intestinal wall.
  • Consequence: This inflammatory cascade can result in transmural necrosis, leading to intestinal perforation, peritonitis, and systemic sepsis, with significant morbidity and mortality. Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Necrotising enterocolitis"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Initial subtle signs, such as feeding intolerance, abdominal distension, and lethargy, may develop over hours to a few days following the introduction of enteral feeds.
  • Progressive phase: As the condition advances, abdominal tenderness, bilious aspirates, bloody stools, and systemic signs of sepsis typically emerge within 24-48 hours of symptom onset.
  • Late phase: Severe disease can rapidly progress to intestinal perforation, peritonitis, and shock, often within hours of the progressive phase, necessitating urgent surgical intervention.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Necrotising enterocolitis"][/sc]
  • Ask about feeding intolerance - often the earliest sign, manifesting as increased gastric residuals or vomiting, indicating impaired gut motility.
  • Ask about abdominal distension - a common and often progressive symptom due to gas accumulation and intestinal oedema.
  • Ask about bloody stools - suggests mucosal injury and haemorrhage within the intestinal tract.
  • Ask about lethargy or irritability - non-specific signs of systemic illness or pain in neonates.
  • Ask about apnoea or bradycardia - systemic manifestations of sepsis or physiological stress in a vulnerable infant.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Necrotising enterocolitis"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - tachycardia, tachypnoea, temperature instability (hypothermia or fever), and hypotension indicate systemic compromise and sepsis.
  • Check abdominal tenderness and guarding - suggest peritoneal irritation and inflammation, progressing to peritonitis in severe cases.
  • Check abdominal wall erythema or discolouration - a late and ominous sign of transmural necrosis and impending perforation.
  • Check absent or diminished bowel sounds - indicative of ileus secondary to intestinal inflammation.
  • Check for a palpable abdominal mass - may suggest a localised area of necrotic bowel or abscess formation. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Bell staging criteria for necrotising enterocolitis: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Bell staging"][/sc]
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Necrotising enterocolitis"][/sc]
  • Abdominal wall erythema: Suggests advanced transmural necrosis and a high risk of perforation, requiring immediate surgical review.
  • Pneumoperitoneum on AXR: Definitive sign of intestinal perforation, necessitating emergency surgical intervention.
  • Persistent metabolic acidosis: Indicates severe systemic illness and inadequate tissue perfusion, often seen in advanced stages.
  • Refractory hypotension: Suggests septic shock, requiring aggressive resuscitation and critical care support.
  • Rapid clinical deterioration: A sudden worsening of symptoms and signs over hours, indicating rapid progression of the disease.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Necrotising enterocolitis"][/sc]
  • Term infants: While predominantly a disease of prematurity, NEC can rarely occur in full-term infants, often associated with congenital heart disease, perinatal asphyxia, or other underlying conditions, making diagnosis challenging.
  • Focal intestinal perforation: Some infants may present with isolated intestinal perforation without the classic diffuse signs of NEC, which can delay diagnosis if NEC is not considered.
  • Slowly progressive NEC: A less acute onset with more indolent symptoms can occur, making early recognition difficult and potentially leading to delayed intervention.
  • Post-surgical NEC: NEC can develop following abdominal surgery in neonates, where post-operative ileus may mask typical symptoms.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Necrotising enterocolitis"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected Necrotising enterocolitis:
    • Full blood count: May show neutropenia, thrombocytopenia, or anaemia.
    • C-reactive protein (CRP): Elevated in inflammation and infection.
    • Blood gas: Metabolic acidosis is common in severe disease.
    • Blood culture: To identify causative organisms in sepsis.
    • Abdominal X-ray (AXR): Essential for diagnosis.   • Imaging - Ultrasound: Abdominal ultrasound may show bowel wall thickening, absent peristalsis, abnormal perfusion, and ascites, and can detect pneumatosis intestinalis and portal venous gas earlier than AXR.
  • Serial reassessment: Serial AXRs (every 6-8 hours) and clinical examinations are crucial to monitor disease progression and detect complications like perforation.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Necrotising enterocolitis"][/sc]
  • Sepsis: Can present with similar systemic signs (lethargy, temperature instability, apnoea) but typically lacks the prominent abdominal signs and specific radiological findings of NEC.
  • Spontaneous intestinal perforation: Presents with acute pneumoperitoneum but often without the diffuse bowel inflammation and pneumatosis intestinalis seen in NEC.
  • Intestinal obstruction (e.g., meconium ileus, atresia): Causes abdominal distension and feeding intolerance but usually presents earlier, with different radiological features (e.g., transition zones, lack of pneumatosis).
  • Hirschsprung disease: Typically presents with delayed passage of meconium and chronic constipation, though acute presentations can mimic obstruction.   • Transient tachypnoea of the newborn (TTN): Primarily a respiratory condition, but can cause feeding intolerance and mild abdominal distension, though without the severe abdominal signs of NEC.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Necrotising enterocolitis"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Recognition of suspected NEC, prompt cessation of enteral feeds, initiation of broad-spectrum intravenous antibiotics, gastric decompression via nasogastric tube, and fluid resuscitation to maintain perfusion.
  • Definitive management: Medical management involves supportive care, close monitoring, and antibiotic therapy. Surgical intervention (laparotomy with resection of necrotic bowel or placement of drains) is considered for intestinal perforation or progressive disease unresponsive to medical management.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Serial clinical examinations, abdominal imaging, and laboratory tests are essential to monitor disease progression and response to treatment. Long-term follow-up may be required for potential complications such as strictures or short bowel syndrome.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Early consultation with a neonatologist and paediatric surgeon is crucial. Parents should be counselled on signs of worsening condition and when to seek urgent medical review.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Necrotising enterocolitis"][/sc]
  • Intestinal perforation: Occurs when transmural necrosis leads to a breach in the bowel wall, resulting in pneumoperitoneum and peritonitis, necessitating emergency surgery.
  • Intestinal stricture: A common long-term complication, often developing weeks to months after recovery from acute NEC, due to fibrotic healing of damaged bowel segments, potentially causing obstruction.
  • Short bowel syndrome: May result from extensive surgical resection of necrotic bowel, leading to malabsorption and dependence on parenteral nutrition.
  • Sepsis and multi-organ failure: Systemic inflammatory response can lead to widespread organ dysfunction and is a major cause of mortality in severe NEC.
  • Neurodevelopmental impairment: Survivors of severe NEC, particularly those requiring surgery, are at increased risk of long-term neurodevelopmental problems.
