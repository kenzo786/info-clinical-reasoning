Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Infectious diarrhoea (including infective gastroenteritis)"][/sc]
  • Infectious diarrhoea, often referred to as infective gastroenteritis, is an inflammation of the stomach and intestines caused by pathogenic microorganisms (viruses, bacteria, or parasites), leading to symptoms such as watery diarrhoea, vomiting, abdominal cramps, and fever.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Candidates must recognise the clinical presentation of common infectious diarrhoeal illnesses, differentiate them from non-infectious causes, and understand the principles of initial assessment and management.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Infectious diarrhoea (including infective gastroenteritis)"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Infectious diarrhoea (including infective gastroenteritis)"][/sc]
  • Acute infectious diarrhoea is a common condition in the UK, with an estimated 17 million cases and 1 million GP consultations annually [1]. Viruses, particularly norovirus, are the most frequent cause, accounting for a significant proportion of community cases [2, 3].
  • Risk factors include close contact with infected individuals, consumption of contaminated food or water, and outbreaks in institutional settings such as care homes and hospitals [4]. Young children and older adults are particularly susceptible to severe illness [5].
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Infectious diarrhoea (including infective gastroenteritis)"][/sc]
  • Ingestion of pathogenic microorganisms (viruses, bacteria, or parasites) through contaminated food, water, or direct contact triggers an immune response in the gastrointestinal tract.
  • These pathogens colonise the intestinal mucosa, causing inflammation and disrupting normal enterocyte function, leading to either increased fluid secretion (e.g., via bacterial [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Enterotoxins: what are they and why do they matter clinically?" text="enterotoxins"]) or impaired fluid absorption.
  • The resulting imbalance in fluid and electrolyte transport within the bowel lumen leads to increased stool water content and frequency, manifesting as diarrhoea and associated symptoms like abdominal pain and vomiting.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Infectious diarrhoea (including infective gastroenteritis)"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Following an incubation period of typically 12-72 hours, symptoms such as nausea, vomiting, and watery diarrhoea rapidly develop, often reaching their peak intensity within the first 24-48 hours.
  • Progressive phase: The acute diarrhoeal illness usually continues for several days, with symptoms persisting for up to 7-10 days. During this phase, dehydration and electrolyte imbalances are significant concerns, particularly in vulnerable populations.
  • Late phase: While most cases are self-limiting and resolve within a week, symptoms may persist particularly beyond 7-10 days, indicating a prolonged infection, post-infectious sequelae, or the development of complications such as dehydration or haemolytic uraemic syndrome.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Infectious diarrhoea (including infective gastroenteritis)"][/sc]
  • Ask about diarrhoea characteristics (frequency, consistency, presence of blood/mucus) - to differentiate between inflammatory and non-inflammatory causes and assess severity.
  • Ask about vomiting (frequency, duration, content) - to assess fluid loss and potential for dehydration, and to distinguish from conditions primarily causing vomiting.
  • Ask about abdominal pain/cramps - to localise the infection and assess severity, as severe pain may indicate complications or alternative diagnoses.
  • Ask about fever/chills - to indicate systemic infection and inflammatory response, suggesting a bacterial or viral aetiology.
  • Ask about recent travel, food/water intake, or contact with sick individuals - to identify potential sources of infection and guide microbiological investigations.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Infectious diarrhoea (including infective gastroenteritis)"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs (temperature, heart rate, blood pressure, respiratory rate) - to identify fever, tachycardia, hypotension, and tachypnoea, which may indicate systemic infection, dehydration, or sepsis.
  • Check hydration status (skin turgor, mucous membranes, capillary refill time, sunken eyes) - to assess the degree of [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Dehydration: what are the clinical signs and why do they matter?" text="dehydration"], a critical complication.
  • Check abdominal examination (tenderness, distension, bowel sounds) - to identify localised or generalised inflammation, peritonitis, or paralytic ileus, and to differentiate from other causes of abdominal pain.
  • Check for signs of systemic toxicity (lethargy, altered mental status) - to identify severe infection or metabolic derangement, particularly in vulnerable patients.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Infectious diarrhoea (including infective gastroenteritis)"][/sc]
  • Bloody or black, tarry stools - indicates gastrointestinal bleeding, potentially from inflammatory or invasive pathogens, warranting urgent investigation.
  • Signs of severe dehydration (e.g., reduced consciousness, anuria, profound weakness) - implies significant fluid and electrolyte loss requiring immediate intravenous fluid resuscitation.
  • High fever (>38.5°C) with systemic toxicity - suggests a more severe bacterial infection or potential sepsis, necessitating prompt medical assessment and possible antimicrobial therapy.
  • Severe, localised abdominal pain or rebound tenderness - may indicate complications such as appendicitis, diverticulitis, or peritonitis, requiring surgical evaluation.
  • Persistent diarrhoea beyond 7-10 days - raises suspicion for chronic infections, inflammatory bowel disease, or other non-infectious causes, requiring further diagnostic work-up.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Infectious diarrhoea (including infective gastroenteritis)"][/sc]
  • Older adults: May present with subtle or fewer classic symptoms (e.g., less fever, abdominal pain, or bloody diarrhoea), making diagnosis challenging. They are at higher risk of rapid dehydration, electrolyte disturbances, and cognitive impairment, leading to faster deterioration.
  • Pregnancy: While often mild, severe infectious diarrhoea can lead to significant maternal dehydration and electrolyte imbalance, potentially triggering preterm labour or affecting fetal well-being. Specific pathogens like *Listeria monocytogenes* pose a greater risk for serious maternal and fetal complications.
  • Immunocompromised individuals: Patients with compromised immune systems (e.g., HIV/AIDS, organ transplant recipients, those on immunosuppressants) are susceptible to more severe, prolonged, or recurrent infections, including opportunistic pathogens. Their immune response may be blunted, leading to less pronounced symptoms despite significant illness.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Infectious diarrhoea (including infective gastroenteritis)"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected Infectious diarrhoea: Clinical assessment for dehydration and electrolyte imbalance is paramount. Stool studies, including bacterial culture, viral PCR panels (e.g., for norovirus, rotavirus), and ova and parasite examination, are indicated for severe illness, bloody diarrhoea, fever, immunocompromised patients, or in cases with public health implications [6].
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Blood tests such as full blood count (FBC), urea and electrolytes (U&Es), and C-reactive protein (CRP) may be considered to assess for systemic inflammation, electrolyte derangements, or anaemia, particularly if red flag features are present.
  • Imaging - Abdominal ultrasound/CT: Generally not required for uncomplicated infectious diarrhoea. However, imaging may be considered if there are red flag features suggesting complications (e.g., appendicitis, diverticulitis, toxic megacolon) or an alternative intra-abdominal pathology.
  • Serial reassessment: Regular monitoring of hydration status, vital signs, and symptom resolution is crucial, especially in vulnerable populations, to guide ongoing management and identify deterioration.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Infectious diarrhoea (including infective gastroenteritis)"][/sc]
  • Inflammatory Bowel Disease (IBD) - chronic, relapsing course with bloody diarrhoea, abdominal pain, weight loss, and often extra-intestinal manifestations; distinguished by endoscopic and histological findings.
  • Irritable Bowel Syndrome (IBS) - chronic abdominal pain and altered bowel habits without structural abnormalities; distinguished by absence of red flag features (e.g., fever, bloody stools, weight loss) and often associated with stress.
  • Drug-induced diarrhoea - temporal association with new medication use (e.g., antibiotics, laxatives, metformin); *Clostridioides difficile* infection is a key consideration post-antibiotics.
  • Food poisoning (non-infectious) - rapid onset of symptoms after ingestion of contaminated food, often self-limiting, and typically without fever or systemic signs of infection.
  • Malabsorption syndromes (e.g., Coeliac disease, Pancreatic insufficiency) - chronic diarrhoea with steatorrhoea, weight loss, and nutritional deficiencies; distinguished by specific diagnostic tests (e.g., coeliac serology, faecal elastase).
  • Ischaemic colitis - acute onset of abdominal pain, often in older patients with cardiovascular risk factors, followed by bloody diarrhoea; distinguished by colonoscopic findings of ischaemic changes.
  • Diverticulitis - localised abdominal pain (typically left lower quadrant), fever, and inflammatory markers; distinguished by CT imaging showing diverticular inflammation.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Infectious diarrhoea (including infective gastroenteritis)"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Focus on fluid and electrolyte replacement to prevent and treat dehydration. Oral rehydration solutions (ORS) are the cornerstone of management for mild to moderate dehydration. Intravenous fluids may be necessary for severe dehydration or persistent vomiting [7].
  • Definitive management: Most cases of infectious diarrhoea are self-limiting and resolve without specific antimicrobial treatment. Antibiotics are generally not recommended for viral gastroenteritis and should be used judiciously in bacterial infections to avoid promoting antimicrobial resistance and potential adverse effects (e.g., *Clostridioides difficile* infection) [8]. Consider antibiotics in severe cases, immunocompromised patients, or specific bacterial infections (e.g., suspected shigellosis, cholera, or severe campylobacter infection) [9].
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Advise patients to continue oral fluid intake, reintroduce normal diet as tolerated, and monitor for signs of worsening dehydration or red flag features. Follow-up is typically not required for uncomplicated cases, but patients should be advised when to seek further medical attention.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Patients with red flag features, severe dehydration, persistent high fever, bloody stools, or worsening symptoms should be referred for urgent medical review or hospital admission. Provide clear safety-netting advice regarding signs of deterioration.
  • Exam pharmacology reference (UKMLA/MRCGP): Knowledge of appropriate antimicrobial choices for specific bacterial pathogens (e.g., [sc name="jitl-medication" Q="Azithromycin use in Campylobacter enteritis" text="azithromycin"] for severe *Campylobacter* enteritis, [sc name="jitl-medication" Q="Ciprofloxacin use in Shigellosis" text="ciprofloxacin"] for severe shigellosis, [sc name="jitl-medication" Q="Metronidazole use in Giardiasis" text="metronidazole"] for giardiasis) is expected. Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Infectious diarrhoea (including infective gastroenteritis)"][/sc]
  • Dehydration and electrolyte imbalance: The most common and immediate complication, particularly in infants, older adults, and immunocompromised individuals, due to significant fluid and electrolyte loss through vomiting and diarrhoea. This can lead to acute kidney injury, cardiac arrhythmias, and, in severe cases, hypovolaemic shock.
  • Post-infectious Irritable Bowel Syndrome (PI-IBS): A chronic functional gastrointestinal disorder that can develop after an episode of acute gastroenteritis, characterised by persistent abdominal pain and altered bowel habits (diarrhoea, constipation, or mixed) even after the infection has cleared.
  • Haemolytic Uraemic Syndrome (HUS): A severe complication, primarily associated with Shiga toxin-producing *E. coli* (STEC) infections (e.g., E. coli O157), leading to haemolytic anaemia, thrombocytopenia, and acute kidney injury. It typically develops several days after the onset of bloody diarrhoea.
  • Reactive arthritis: An inflammatory arthritis that can occur following certain enteric infections (e.g., *Salmonella*, *Shigella*, *Campylobacter*, *Yersinia*), typically affecting large joints and presenting weeks after the initial infection.
  • Malnutrition and weight loss: Prolonged or recurrent infectious diarrhoea can lead to malabsorption and inadequate nutrient intake, resulting in malnutrition and significant weight loss, especially in children and those in resource-limited settings.
