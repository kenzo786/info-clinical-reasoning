Childhood cancer (including retinoblastoma) [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Childhood cancer (including retinoblastoma)"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Childhood cancer (including retinoblastoma)"][/sc]
  • Childhood cancer encompasses a diverse group of malignancies arising in individuals under 15 years of age, often with distinct biological and clinical characteristics compared to adult cancers.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the common presentations and initial management principles of childhood cancers, including retinoblastoma.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Childhood cancer (including retinoblastoma)"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Childhood cancer (including retinoblastoma)"][/sc]
  • Childhood cancers are rare, accounting for less than 1% of all cancers in the UK, with approximately 1,900 new diagnoses annually in children aged 0-14 years (Cancer Research UK).
  • Leukaemias, brain tumours, and lymphomas are the most common types, collectively representing over half of all childhood cancers.
  • Risk factors often include genetic predispositions (e.g., Down syndrome, Li-Fraumeni syndrome), certain congenital anomalies, and occasionally environmental exposures, though most cases have no identifiable cause.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Childhood cancer (including retinoblastoma)"][/sc]
  • Trigger: Genetic mutations, either inherited or acquired, lead to uncontrolled cell proliferation and differentiation, often originating from embryonic cells or rapidly dividing tissues.
  • Progression: Malignant cells evade normal growth regulatory mechanisms, forming tumours that can invade local tissues and metastasise via lymphatic or haematogenous routes.
  • Consequence: Tumour growth and spread result in organ dysfunction, systemic symptoms (e.g., fever, weight loss), and potential life-threatening complications due to mass effect or metabolic derangements.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Childhood cancer (including retinoblastoma)"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Insidious onset of non-specific symptoms, such as fatigue, pallor, or recurrent infections, which may persist for weeks to months before a specific diagnosis is considered.
  • Progressive phase: Worsening or new symptoms emerge, often related to tumour bulk or metastasis, such as persistent pain, neurological deficits, or visible masses, particularly beyond a few weeks of initial symptoms.
  • Late phase: Advanced disease with significant systemic impact, organ failure, or widespread metastatic burden, typically presenting after several months without diagnosis or effective treatment.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Childhood cancer (including retinoblastoma)"][/sc]
  • Ask about persistent, unexplained pain - often a feature of bone tumours or leukaemia, especially if worse at night or not relieved by simple analgesia.
  • Ask about unexplained fever, night sweats, or weight loss - these B symptoms are common in lymphomas and other systemic malignancies, indicating metabolic activity.
  • Ask about changes in vision or a white reflex in the eye (leukocoria) - highly suggestive of retinoblastoma, crucial for early detection and preservation of sight.
  • Ask about persistent headache, vomiting (especially morning vomiting), or gait disturbance - red flags for intracranial pathology, including brain tumours, due to raised intracranial pressure.
  • Ask about unexplained bruising, bleeding, or recurrent infections - indicative of bone marrow suppression, commonly seen in leukaemias.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Childhood cancer (including retinoblastoma)"][/sc]
  • Check for palpable masses or lymphadenopathy - a non-tender, firm, fixed mass may indicate a solid tumour or lymphoma.
  • Check for pallor, petechiae, or purpura - suggests anaemia, thrombocytopenia, or bone marrow infiltration, often seen in leukaemia.
  • Check neurological signs, including cranial nerve palsies, papilloedema, or ataxia - implies central nervous system involvement, particularly with brain tumours.
  • Check for hepatosplenomegaly - common in leukaemias and lymphomas due to extramedullary haematopoiesis or infiltration.
  • Check vital signs - persistent tachycardia, tachypnoea, or hypertension may indicate systemic illness or specific tumour effects.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Childhood cancer (including retinoblastoma)"][/sc]
  • Persistent, unexplained symptoms: Symptoms that do not resolve with usual treatments or persist for longer than expected, particularly beyond a few weeks, warrant further investigation.
  • Progressive neurological deficit: Any new or worsening neurological signs, such as focal weakness, visual changes, or persistent vomiting, suggests an evolving intracranial process.
  • Unexplained palpable mass: A firm, non-tender, or rapidly growing mass, especially in the abdomen, neck, or limbs, requires urgent oncological assessment.
  • Leukocoria or strabismus: A white pupillary reflex or new onset squint in a child is a critical sign of retinoblastoma and demands immediate ophthalmological referral.
  • Bone marrow failure signs: Unexplained anaemia, thrombocytopenia (petechiae, bruising), or neutropenia (recurrent infections) are red flags for leukaemia.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Childhood cancer (including retinoblastoma)"][/sc]
  • Infants: May present with non-specific irritability, feeding difficulties, or failure to thrive, making diagnosis challenging due to limited verbal communication.
  • Adolescents: Symptoms can be mistaken for common adolescent complaints like growing pains or viral illnesses, leading to diagnostic delays.
  • Retinoblastoma: While leukocoria is classic, some may present with strabismus or red eye, which can be initially misdiagnosed as more benign conditions.
  • Neuroblastoma: Can present with a wide range of symptoms depending on tumour location, from an abdominal mass to opsoclonus-myoclonus syndrome (dancing eyes, dancing feet).
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Childhood cancer (including retinoblastoma)"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected leukaemia/lymphoma: Full blood count with differential, peripheral blood film, and bone marrow aspirate/trephine for morphology, immunophenotyping, and cytogenetics.
  • Core for suspected solid tumour: Imaging appropriate to the suspected site (e.g., ultrasound, CT, MRI), followed by biopsy for histological diagnosis and molecular analysis.
  • Core for suspected retinoblastoma: Urgent ophthalmological examination under anaesthesia, including fundoscopy and ocular ultrasound.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Inflammatory markers (CRP, ESR), viral serology, or autoimmune screens may be considered to rule out infectious or rheumatological conditions.
  • Imaging - MRI: Preferred for brain and spinal tumours due to superior soft tissue contrast; CT for chest/abdominal masses, especially if rapid assessment is needed.
  • Validated score: The Children's Oncology Group Neuroblastoma Risk Stratification on MDCalc can guide management decisions for neuroblastoma.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Childhood cancer (including retinoblastoma)"][/sc]
  • A 4-year-old boy presents with a 6-week history of increasing lethargy, intermittent fevers, and easy bruising, alongside a new, non-tender abdominal mass.
  • A young child with progressive, non-specific systemic symptoms and a palpable mass, raising concern for an underlying malignancy.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Childhood cancer (including retinoblastoma)"][/sc]
  • Acute Leukaemia - bone marrow failure symptoms (pallor, bruising, infection) and hepatosplenomegaly.
  • Lymphoma - unexplained lymphadenopathy, B symptoms, and potential mass effect.
  • Neuroblastoma - abdominal mass, systemic symptoms, and sometimes neurological signs.
  • Wilms' Tumour (Nephroblastoma) - asymptomatic abdominal mass, often discovered incidentally.
  • Brain Tumour - persistent headache, vomiting, neurological deficits, and papilloedema.
  • Juvenile Idiopathic Arthritis - joint pain and swelling, fever, but typically without bone marrow failure or masses.
  • Infectious Mononucleosis - lymphadenopathy, fever, fatigue, but usually self-limiting and with characteristic blood film changes.
