Non-accidental injury [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Non-accidental injury"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Non-accidental injury"][/sc]
  • Non-accidental injury (NAI) refers to any injury or harm to a child that is caused by a parent, caregiver, or other person, and is not accidental. It encompasses physical abuse, neglect, sexual abuse, and emotional abuse.
  • UKMLA Learning Outcome: Recognise the signs and symptoms of non-accidental injury in children and understand the appropriate safeguarding procedures.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Non-accidental injury"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Non-accidental injury"][/sc]
  • In the UK, approximately 1 in 5 children experience some form of child abuse or neglect before the age of 16, with physical abuse being a significant component.
  • Infants and young children are particularly vulnerable to NAI, with the highest rates of serious injury and fatality occurring in those under one year old.
  • Risk factors often include parental mental health issues, substance misuse, domestic violence, poverty, and social isolation within the household.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Non-accidental injury"][/sc]
  • Trigger: An external force or neglectful act, often in a context of caregiver stress or dysfunction, initiates the injury.
  • Progression: The inflicted trauma, such as blunt force, shaking, or thermal injury, leads to tissue damage, haemorrhage, or organ dysfunction.
  • Consequence: Injuries can range from superficial bruising to severe internal damage, fractures, or brain injury, potentially resulting in long-term disability or fatality. Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Non-accidental injury"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Acute injuries may present immediately, often with vague or inconsistent histories from caregivers, or delayed presentation due to fear or concealment.
  • Progressive phase: Repeated or ongoing abuse can lead to a pattern of injuries at different stages of healing, chronic pain, developmental delays, or behavioural changes.
  • Late phase: Long-term consequences may include psychological trauma, learning difficulties, chronic health problems, and an increased risk of perpetrating abuse in adulthood.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Non-accidental injury"][/sc]
  • Ask about unexplained bruising or marks - inconsistent with developmental stage or mechanism described.
  • Ask about changes in behaviour - sudden withdrawal, aggression, fear, or anxiety, which may indicate distress.
  • Ask about feeding difficulties or poor weight gain - often warrants consideration of neglect or emotional abuse.
  • Ask about reluctance to be touched or specific movements - may suggest pain from occult fractures or soft tissue injuries.
  • Ask about unusual sleep patterns or nightmares - can be a manifestation of psychological trauma.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Non-accidental injury"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - may reveal signs of shock, infection, or neurological compromise in severe cases.
  • Check for bruising - particularly in non-mobile infants, patterned bruises, or those on the torso, ears, or neck, which are highly suspicious.
  • Check for fractures - especially metaphyseal fractures, rib fractures (posterior), or multiple fractures at different stages of healing.
  • Check for burns - with clear demarcation, unusual patterns, or immersion burns, which are rarely accidental.
  • Check for head injuries - subdural haematomas, retinal haemorrhages, or skull fractures, particularly in infants, may support a diagnosis of abusive head trauma. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Retinal haemorrhages in children: significance in non-accidental injury?" text="Retinal haemorrhages"][/sc]
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Non-accidental injury"][/sc]
  • Inconsistent history: Discrepancies between the injury and the explanation provided, or differing accounts from caregivers, are highly concerning.
  • Delay in seeking medical attention: Significant delay in presenting a child with a serious injury often warrants further investigation.
  • Bruising in non-mobile infants: Any bruising in an infant who is not yet crawling or walking is considered suspicious until proven otherwise.
  • Patterned injuries: Bruises or marks that match an object (e.g., belt, handprint) strongly suggest inflicted trauma.
  • Multiple injuries at different stages of healing: Indicates ongoing abuse rather than a single accidental event.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Non-accidental injury"][/sc]
  • Infants with osteogenesis imperfecta: Fractures may be mistaken for NAI; careful genetic and radiological assessment is crucial to differentiate.
  • Children with bleeding disorders: Easy bruising or bleeding may mimic NAI; a thorough haematological work-up is essential.
  • Children with cultural practices: Certain traditional practices (e.g., coining, cupping) can leave marks resembling abuse; cultural sensitivity and careful history taking are required.
  • Children with developmental disabilities: May be at higher risk of NAI and may present with non-specific symptoms or behavioural changes that are difficult to interpret.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Non-accidental injury"][/sc]
  • Core:
    • Full blood count, coagulation screen, liver function tests, amylase (if abdominal injury suspected).
    • Skeletal survey (for children under 2 years, or older if clinically indicated) to identify occult fractures.
  • Imaging:
    • CT head or MRI brain (if head injury suspected) to detect intracranial haemorrhage or oedema.
    • Abdominal ultrasound or CT abdomen (if abdominal injury suspected) to assess solid organ damage.
  • If alternative diagnoses considered:
    • Genetic testing for osteogenesis imperfecta.
    • Haematological investigations for bleeding disorders.
  • Serial reassessment:
    • Repeated clinical examination and imaging as indicated to monitor healing and identify new injuries.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Non-accidental injury"][/sc]
  • A young child presenting with unexplained injuries, particularly bruising in a non-mobile infant, often warrants a high index of suspicion for non-accidental injury.
  • This situation is characterised by a **vulnerable patient**, **inconsistent history**, and **suspicious injury patterns**, necessitating a comprehensive safeguarding assessment.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Non-accidental injury"][/sc]
  • Accidental injury: Typically consistent with developmental stage and history, often involving prominent areas like knees and shins.
  • Bleeding disorders (e.g., haemophilia, Von Willebrand disease): May present with easy bruising or prolonged bleeding, but usually without patterned injuries.
  • Connective tissue disorders (e.g., Ehlers-Danlos syndrome): Can cause skin fragility and bruising, but typically without specific fracture patterns seen in NAI.
  • Osteogenesis imperfecta: Characterised by brittle bones and recurrent fractures, but usually without associated soft tissue injuries or inconsistent histories.
  • Idiopathic thrombocytopenic purpura (ITP): Presents with petechiae and purpura, but not typically with focal injuries or fractures.
  • Meningococcal disease: Can cause widespread purpuric rash, but usually associated with systemic illness and fever, not isolated injuries.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Non-accidental injury"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities:
    • Ensure the child's immediate safety and remove them from any ongoing risk.
    • Address any life-threatening injuries (e.g., airway, breathing, circulation, neurological compromise).
    • Involve safeguarding services and police immediately.
  • Definitive management:
    • Treat specific injuries (e.g., fracture management, wound care, neurosurgical intervention).
    • Provide psychological support for the child and non-abusing family members.
    • Implement a comprehensive safeguarding plan, which may include child protection conferences and legal proceedings.
  • Monitoring and follow-up:
    • Regular medical and developmental reviews to assess recovery and identify any long-term sequelae.
    • Ongoing involvement with social services to ensure the child's safety and well-being.
  • Escalation and safety-netting:
    • Any concerns about ongoing risk or deterioration in the child's condition should be immediately escalated to senior medical staff and safeguarding leads.
    • Parents/carers should be advised on when and how to seek further medical attention, with clear safety-netting advice.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Non-accidental injury"][/sc]
  • Neurological impairment: Brain injury from abusive head trauma can lead to developmental delay, seizures, cerebral palsy, or cognitive deficits.
  • Physical disabilities: Untreated or severe fractures may result in limb deformities, chronic pain, or restricted mobility.
  • Psychological trauma: Children often experience anxiety, depression, post-traumatic stress disorder, and attachment difficulties, impacting their mental health.
  • Educational underachievement: Trauma and instability can disrupt schooling, leading to learning difficulties and poor academic outcomes.
  • Increased risk of future abuse: Children who experience NAI are at a higher risk of further abuse or neglect, and may perpetuate cycles of violence in adulthood.
  • Fatal outcomes: In severe cases, NAI can lead to death, particularly in very young infants.
