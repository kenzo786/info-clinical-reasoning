Hyperemesis gravidarum [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Hyperemesis gravidarum"]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Hyperemesis gravidarum"]
  • Severe form of nausea and vomiting in pregnancy (NVP) characterised by protracted NVP, more than 5% pre-pregnancy weight loss, dehydration, and electrolyte imbalance.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the diagnostic criteria for hyperemesis gravidarum and differentiate it from typical nausea and vomiting of pregnancy, understanding the implications for maternal and fetal well-being.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Hyperemesis gravidarum"]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Hyperemesis gravidarum"]
  • Affects approximately 0.3–3.6% of pregnant women in the UK, making it a significant cause of hospital admission during pregnancy.
  • Recurrence rates for hyperemesis gravidarum in subsequent pregnancies are variable, with self-reported rates as high as 81%.
  • Risk factors include multiple pregnancies, trophoblastic disease, and a history of hyperemesis gravidarum in a previous pregnancy.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Hyperemesis gravidarum"]
  • Trigger: Primarily associated with rapidly rising levels of beta-human chorionic gonadotrophin (hCG) hormone, particularly in conditions with higher hCG levels such as multiple pregnancies or trophoblastic disease.
  • Progression: Persistent nausea and vomiting lead to inadequate oral intake, resulting in fluid and electrolyte imbalances, ketosis, and significant weight loss.
  • Consequence: Prolonged severe vomiting can lead to complications such as Wernicke encephalopathy due to thiamine deficiency, oesophageal tears, and psychological distress. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Wernicke encephalopathy: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Wernicke encephalopathy"]
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Hyperemesis gravidarum"]
  • Early phase: Symptoms typically begin in the first trimester, often peaking between 8-12 weeks of gestation.
  • Progressive phase: If untreated, symptoms can worsen, leading to severe dehydration, electrolyte disturbances, and nutritional deficiencies, often necessitating hospital admission.
  • Late phase: While symptoms usually resolve by 20 weeks, a significant proportion of women may experience persistent nausea and vomiting into the late second or third trimester, requiring ongoing management.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Hyperemesis gravidarum"]
  • Ask about persistent nausea and vomiting - indicates the severity and impact on daily life, differentiating it from mild NVP.
  • Ask about inability to keep down food and fluids - suggests dehydration and potential for weight loss and electrolyte imbalance.
  • Ask about significant weight loss (more than 5% pre-pregnancy weight) - a key diagnostic criterion for hyperemesis gravidarum.
  • Ask about fatigue and weakness - often a consequence of dehydration and malnutrition.
  • Ask about symptoms of thiamine deficiency (e.g., confusion, visual disturbances) - crucial for early recognition of Wernicke encephalopathy risk.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Hyperemesis gravidarum"]
  • Check vital signs - to assess for tachycardia and hypotension indicative of dehydration.
  • Check for signs of dehydration (e.g., dry mucous membranes, reduced skin turgor) - confirms fluid depletion.
  • Check for ketonuria - indicates fat breakdown due to inadequate carbohydrate intake. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Ketonuria in pregnancy: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Ketonuria"]
  • Check for signs of Wernicke encephalopathy (e.g., nystagmus, ataxia, altered mental status) - a critical neurological complication of thiamine deficiency.
  • Check for epigastric tenderness - may suggest oesophagitis or gastritis secondary to persistent vomiting.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Hyperemesis gravidarum"]
  • Neurological changes: New onset confusion, ataxia, or visual disturbances (e.g., nystagmus) suggest Wernicke encephalopathy, requiring urgent thiamine administration.
  • Severe dehydration: Persistent inability to tolerate oral fluids, oliguria, or signs of hypovolaemic shock indicate a need for immediate intravenous rehydration.
  • Unexplained abdominal pain: May suggest an alternative or co-existing pathology requiring further investigation beyond hyperemesis gravidarum.
  • Haematemesis: Vomiting blood may indicate oesophageal tears (Mallory-Weiss tear) or severe oesophagitis, warranting prompt endoscopic evaluation.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Hyperemesis gravidarum"]
  • Later onset: While typically presenting in the first trimester, very rarely, severe nausea and vomiting may develop later in pregnancy, necessitating exclusion of other causes.
  • Absence of weight loss: In some cases, significant dehydration and electrolyte imbalance may be present without substantial weight loss, particularly if early intervention has occurred.
  • Co-existing conditions: Hyperemesis gravidarum can mask or exacerbate other conditions such as urinary tract infections or thyroid dysfunction, leading to a more complex presentation. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Thyroid dysfunction in pregnancy: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Thyroid dysfunction"]
  • Psychological overlay: Severe and prolonged symptoms can lead to significant psychological distress, including depression and anxiety, which may alter the perceived severity of physical symptoms.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Hyperemesis gravidarum"]
  • Core for suspected Hyperemesis gravidarum:
    • Urine dipstick for ketones and specific gravity - to assess for ketonuria and hydration status.
    • Full blood count - to assess for haemoconcentration (due to dehydration) and anaemia.
    • Urea and electrolytes - to identify electrolyte imbalances (e.g., hypokalaemia, hyponatraemia) and renal function.
    • Liver function tests - to exclude hepatic dysfunction.
    • Thyroid function tests - to exclude hyperthyroidism, which can mimic or exacerbate symptoms.
  • Severity/risk tools: Pregnancy-Unique Quantification of Emesis (PUQE) score on MDCalc PUQE score on MDCalc - a validated tool to classify the severity of nausea and vomiting in pregnancy. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="PUQE score: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="PUQE score"]
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible:
    • Urinalysis and culture - to exclude urinary tract infection.
    • Abdominal ultrasound - to exclude other causes of vomiting such as gallstones or pancreatitis, and to confirm viability and rule out multiple pregnancy or molar pregnancy.
  • Serial reassessment: Daily monitoring of urea and electrolytes in women requiring intravenous fluids, and regular weight checks.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Hyperemesis gravidarum"]
  • A pregnant woman in her first trimester presents with intractable nausea and vomiting, significant weight loss, and signs of dehydration and electrolyte imbalance.
  • Young woman with severe, persistent emesis and ketonuria, concerning for hyperemesis gravidarum with risk of Wernicke encephalopathy.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Hyperemesis gravidarum"]
  • Nausea and Vomiting of Pregnancy (NVP) - typically milder, without significant weight loss, dehydration, or electrolyte disturbance.
  • Gastroenteritis - usually acute onset with diarrhoea, and often a clear exposure history; symptoms are typically self-limiting.
  • Urinary Tract Infection (UTI) - may present with nausea and vomiting, but usually accompanied by dysuria, frequency, and suprapubic pain.
  • Peptic Ulcer Disease/Gastritis - often associated with epigastric pain, and symptoms may be exacerbated by food; less common in early pregnancy.
  • Cholecystitis/Pancreatitis - severe abdominal pain is a prominent feature, often radiating to the back or shoulder, and associated with specific laboratory abnormalities.
  • Thyroid storm - rare but severe hyperthyroidism can cause profound nausea and vomiting, along with tachycardia, fever, and altered mental status. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Thyroid storm: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Thyroid storm"]
  • Molar pregnancy - can cause exaggerated NVP due to very high hCG levels, often associated with vaginal bleeding and an abnormally large uterus for gestational age.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Hyperemesis gravidarum"]
  • Immediate priorities: Recognition of severity, assessment for dehydration and electrolyte imbalance, and exclusion of alternative diagnoses. Escalation to secondary care is often warranted for inpatient management.
  • Definitive management: Intravenous rehydration with normal saline and potassium chloride, antiemetic therapy, and thiamine supplementation.
  • Adjunctive management: Consideration of histamine H2 receptor antagonists or proton pump inhibitors for gastro-oesophageal reflux disease, and thromboprophylaxis with low-molecular-weight heparin for admitted patients.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Daily monitoring of urea and electrolytes, regular weight checks, and serial fetal growth scans if symptoms persist into late pregnancy. Individualised management plans upon discharge are important.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Advise women on signs of worsening dehydration, neurological symptoms, or persistent inability to tolerate fluids, and when to seek urgent medical review.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Hyperemesis gravidarum"]
  • Wernicke encephalopathy: Due to severe and prolonged thiamine deficiency, particularly if dextrose is administered without prior thiamine supplementation, leading to neurological impairment.
  • Mallory-Weiss tear: Oesophageal mucosal lacerations caused by forceful vomiting, presenting as haematemesis.
  • Electrolyte imbalances: Severe hypokalaemia or hyponatraemia can lead to cardiac arrhythmias or neurological dysfunction if not corrected.
  • Fetal growth restriction: In severe and prolonged cases, inadequate maternal nutrition and hydration can impact fetal growth, necessitating serial monitoring.
  • Psychological distress: The chronic nature and severity of symptoms can lead to significant anxiety, depression, and impact on quality of life, extending into the postnatal period.
