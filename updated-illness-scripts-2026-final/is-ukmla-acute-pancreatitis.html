Acute pancreatitis [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Acute pancreatitis"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Acute pancreatitis"][/sc]
  • Acute pancreatitis is an acute inflammatory process of the pancreas with varying involvement of local tissues or more remote organ systems [1].
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the presentation of acute pancreatitis, identify common aetiologies, and understand the principles of initial assessment and supportive management.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Acute pancreatitis"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Acute pancreatitis"][/sc]
  • The UK incidence of acute pancreatitis is estimated as 15–42 cases per 100,000 per year and is rising by 2.7% each year [2].
  • Acute pancreatitis has a mortality rate of 1%–7%, increasing to around 20% in patients with pancreatic necrosis [2].
  • Gallstone pancreatitis is more common in women over the age of 60, especially among those with microlithiasis, while alcoholic pancreatitis is more frequent in males [2].
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Acute pancreatitis"][/sc]
  • The initiating event in acute pancreatitis is often acinar cell injury, leading to impaired secretion of zymogen granules and intracellular enzyme activation.
  • Increased intracellular calcium transients potentiate co-localisation of zymogen and lysosome granules, leading to premature conversion of trypsinogen to trypsin [2].
  • This results in autodigestion of the pancreas, causing inflammation, oedema, and potentially necrosis and organ failure [2]. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Trypsinogen activation in acute pancreatitis: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Trypsinogen activation"][/sc]
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Acute pancreatitis"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Typically within the first week, characterised by systemic inflammatory response syndrome (SIRS) and/or organ failure.
  • Progressive phase: Beyond the first week, some patients may develop local complications such as fluid collections, necrosis, or pseudocysts.
  • Late phase: Persistent organ failure or infected necrosis may develop, often beyond several weeks, leading to prolonged hospital stays and increased mortality.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Acute pancreatitis"][/sc]
  • Ask about severe epigastric pain radiating to the back - this is a classic presentation and suggests pancreatic involvement [2].
  • Ask about nausea and vomiting - common accompanying symptoms that can lead to dehydration and electrolyte imbalances [2].
  • Ask about pain exacerbated by movement and alleviated by leaning forwards - a distinguishing feature that can help differentiate from other causes of abdominal pain [2].
  • Ask about recent alcohol intake or history of gallstones - these are the two most common aetiologies and guide further investigation [2].
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Acute pancreatitis"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - tachycardia, tachypnoea, and fever are common and may indicate systemic inflammatory response syndrome (SIRS) [2].
  • Check for epigastric tenderness and guarding - indicative of peritoneal irritation due to pancreatic inflammation [2].
  • Check for reduced bowel sounds - an associated ileus can occur due to inflammation [2].
  • Check for Cullen\'s or Grey-Turner\'s sign - rare but highly specific signs of retroperitoneal haemorrhage, indicating severe necrotising pancreatitis [2]. Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Acute pancreatitis"][/sc]
  • Persistent Systemic Inflammatory Response Syndrome (SIRS) - indicates increased risk of severe acute pancreatitis and higher mortality, particularly if present beyond 48 hours [2].
  • Organ failure - respiratory, renal, or cardiovascular compromise suggests severe disease and requires high dependency unit admission [2].
  • Necrotising pancreatitis - development of pancreatic necrosis, especially if infected, significantly increases morbidity and mortality, often presenting in the progressive phase [2].
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Acute pancreatitis"][/sc]
  • Older adults: May present with less severe pain or altered mental status, leading to delayed diagnosis and increased mortality [2].
  • Pregnancy: Acute pancreatitis can occur, often due to gallstones, and diagnosis can be challenging due to physiological changes; management requires careful consideration of fetal well-being [2].
  • Drug-induced pancreatitis: Certain medications (e.g., azathioprine, corticosteroids, thiazide diuretics) can cause pancreatitis, which may present without typical risk factors [2]. Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Acute pancreatitis"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected acute pancreatitis: Serum amylase or lipase levels >3 times the upper limit of normal are diagnostic [2]. Lipase is generally preferred due to higher sensitivity and specificity.
  • Severity/risk tools: Modified Glasgow score (≥3 indicates severe pancreatitis) and Ranson criteria are used for initial assessment of severity [2]. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Modified Glasgow score for acute pancreatitis: what are its components and why does it matter clinically?" text="Modified Glasgow score"][/sc] Modified Glasgow Score on MDCalc
  • Imaging - Ultrasound: Trans-abdominal ultrasound is the preferred initial study in suspected gallstone pancreatitis to identify gallstones or biliary obstruction [2].
  • Imaging - CT: Contrast-enhanced CT is the imaging modality of choice for staging acute pancreatitis and detecting complications like necrosis or fluid collections, but is not advised within the first 48 hours unless diagnosis is uncertain [2].   • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Consider other investigations such as liver function tests, triglycerides, calcium, and abdominal CT to rule out other causes of abdominal pain [2].
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Acute pancreatitis"][/sc]
  • A 55-year-old male with a history of heavy alcohol use presents with sudden-onset severe epigastric pain radiating to the back, associated with nausea, vomiting, and tenderness on examination.
  • A middle-aged male with significant alcohol history presenting with acute, severe abdominal pain and systemic inflammatory signs, concerning for acute pancreatitis.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Acute pancreatitis"][/sc]
  • Perforated peptic ulcer - sudden onset, severe abdominal pain, but often with peritonism and gas under diaphragm on imaging.
  • Acute cholecystitis - right upper quadrant pain, often radiating to the shoulder, typically without elevated amylase/lipase to the same degree.
  • Biliary colic - episodic right upper quadrant pain, usually resolving, without significant inflammatory markers.
  • Myocardial infarction - epigastric pain can be a referred symptom, especially in inferior MI; ECG and cardiac enzymes are key discriminators.
  • Mesenteric ischaemia - severe abdominal pain out of proportion to examination findings, often in patients with vascular risk factors. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Mesenteric ischaemia: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Mesenteric ischaemia"][/sc]
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Acute pancreatitis"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Early recognition, prompt escalation to senior medical staff, aggressive intravenous fluid resuscitation (e.g., Ringer\'s lactate 5-10 ml/kg/hr initially, titrated to response), effective pain control (e.g., opiates), and antiemetics [2].
  • Definitive management: Identification and treatment of the underlying cause (e.g., ERCP for gallstone pancreatitis with cholangitis, cholecystectomy for gallstone pancreatitis once inflammation settles) [2].
  • Nutrition support: Enteral nutrition should be offered to anyone with severe or moderately severe acute pancreatitis within 72 hours of presentation; parenteral nutrition only if enteral fails or is contraindicated [1].
  • Exam pharmacology reference (UKMLA/MRCGP): Prophylactic antibiotics are not routinely recommended for acute pancreatitis [1]. No doses or frequencies are cited here — always verify against your local antimicrobial/prescribing formulary before prescribing.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Acute pancreatitis"][/sc]
  • Systemic inflammatory response syndrome (SIRS): Can lead to multi-organ failure (respiratory, renal, cardiovascular) in the early phase, significantly impacting prognosis [2].
  • Pancreatic necrosis: Develops in the progressive phase, often becoming infected, which is associated with high mortality and requires careful management [2].
  • Fluid collections/pseudocysts: Local complications that may resolve spontaneously or require intervention if symptomatic or infected, typically forming in the progressive to late phases [2].
  • Type 3c diabetes: Can develop as a long-term complication in the late phase due to extensive pancreatic damage, requiring insulin or oral hypoglycaemic agents [1].
