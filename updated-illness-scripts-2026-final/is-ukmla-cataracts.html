Cataracts (Eye) [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Cataracts (Eye)"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Cataracts (Eye)"][/sc]
  • A cataract is a progressive opacification of the crystalline lens of the eye, which impairs vision by scattering and obstructing the passage of light.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical features of cataracts, differentiate between common types (age-related, congenital, secondary), and understand the principles of management, including indications for surgical referral.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Cataracts (Eye)"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Cataracts"][/sc]
  • In the UK, it is estimated that around 30% of people aged 65 or over have a visually significant cataract in one or both eyes, with over 516,000 cataract operations performed in England in the financial year ending 2023.
  • Major risk factors include increasing age, smoking, diabetes mellitus, excessive exposure to ultraviolet (UV) light, and long-term use of corticosteroid medications.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Cataracts (Eye)"][/sc]
  • Trigger: The primary trigger is oxidative stress and ageing, which leads to conformational changes and damage to the crystalline proteins within the lens.
  • Progression: Damaged proteins aggregate to form insoluble high-molecular-weight clusters, a process known as [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Lens protein aggregation: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="lens protein aggregation"][/sc], which disrupts the highly organised microarchitecture of the lens.
  • Consequence: These protein aggregates scatter and absorb light, causing progressive lens opacification and leading to the characteristic symptoms of blurred vision and glare.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Cataracts (Eye)"][/sc]
  • Early phase: The onset is typically insidious over several years, with minimal or no symptoms initially. Some individuals may notice subtle changes in vision, such as minor blurring or increased glare.
  • Progressive phase: Over months to years, the cataract matures, and visual symptoms become more pronounced, increasingly interfering with daily activities like reading, recognising faces, and driving, particularly at night.
  • Late phase: If left untreated, particularly beyond a decade, the lens can become completely opaque, leading to severe visual impairment and, ultimately, functional blindness.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Cataracts (Eye)"][/sc]
  • Ask about progressively blurred or misty vision - this is the cardinal symptom, often described as looking through a dirty window, which is not correctable with glasses.
  • Ask about glare and light sensitivity (photophobia) - this is particularly problematic in bright sunlight or when facing oncoming headlights at night, causing significant dazzle.
  • Ask about halos around lights - seeing rings or halos around light sources is a common complaint, resulting from light scattering by the opaque lens.
  • Ask about changes in colour perception - colours may appear faded, washed out, or have a yellowish/brownish tint, reducing the vibrancy of vision.
  • Ask about "second sight" (myopic shift) - some individuals experience a temporary improvement in their near vision as the cataract progresses, reducing their need for reading glasses.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Cataracts (Eye)"][/sc]
  • Check visual acuity - typically shows a gradual reduction that cannot be improved to 6/6 with a pinhole occluder.
  • Check the red reflex - using a direct ophthalmoscope, the reflex may appear diminished, irregular, or completely absent. This is a key screening test, especially for a [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Red reflex assessment in neonates: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="red reflex assessment in neonates"][/sc].
  • Check for lens opacities - on slit-lamp examination, the type and density of the cataract (e.g., nuclear, cortical, posterior subcapsular) can be directly visualised.
  • Check vital signs - these are expected to be normal as cataracts are a localised ocular process and do not cause systemic signs.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Cataracts (Eye)"][/sc]
  • Sudden, painful loss of vision - suggests an acute process such as acute angle-closure glaucoma or retinal detachment, which requires immediate ophthalmology review.
  • A painful red eye - in the context of visual disturbance, this may indicate uveitis or acute glaucoma rather than an uncomplicated cataract.
  • Leukocoria (white pupil) - particularly in a child, this can be a sign of a dense congenital cataract or a more sinister pathology like retinoblastoma, warranting urgent referral.
  • Significant visual loss in a young patient - cataracts in younger individuals are atypical and may be associated with systemic disease, trauma, or genetic conditions that require further investigation.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Cataracts (Eye)"][/sc]
  • Congenital cataracts: Present at birth or in the first few months of life, often detected by an absent red reflex during the newborn examination. They can be unilateral or bilateral and may be associated with genetic syndromes or intrauterine infections.
  • Secondary to systemic disease: Uncontrolled diabetes mellitus can lead to early-onset "snowflake" cataracts. Other conditions like myotonic dystrophy or atopic dermatitis are also associated with specific types of cataracts.
  • Secondary to medication use: Long-term systemic or topical corticosteroid use is a classic cause of a [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Posterior subcapsular cataract in steroid use: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="posterior subcapsular cataract"][/sc], which causes significant glare and reading difficulty.
  • Traumatic cataracts: A history of blunt or penetrating eye trauma can lead to the formation of a cataract, which may have a characteristic "rosette" or "stellate" appearance.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Cataracts (Eye)"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected cataracts: The diagnosis is primarily clinical, based on a comprehensive eye examination. This includes visual acuity measurement, pupillary examination, and a detailed [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Slit-lamp examination for cataracts: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="slit-lamp examination"][/sc] to assess the morphology and density of the lens opacity. A dilated fundus examination is crucial to exclude co-existing retinal pathology.
  • Severity/risk tools: There are no widely used risk calculators for cataracts on platforms like MDCalc. Severity is graded clinically using systems like the Lens Opacities Classification System III (LOCS III) during slit-lamp examination.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: If retinal or optic nerve pathology is suspected, optical coherence tomography (OCT) of the macula and optic nerve head, or formal visual field testing, may be warranted to rule out conditions like macular degeneration or glaucoma.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Cataracts (Eye)"][/sc]
  • A 72-year-old female presents with a two-year history of gradually worsening misty vision and significant glare from headlights, which now prevents her from driving at night. Examination reveals reduced visual acuity and lens opacities.
  • An elderly patient with progressive, painless, bilateral visual impairment, characterised by reduced acuity and glare, is highly suggestive of age-related cataracts, warranting referral for surgical assessment.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Cataracts (Eye)"][/sc]
  • Refractive error - can cause blurred vision but is correctable with glasses or contact lenses, and does not cause true lens opacification.
  • Age-related macular degeneration (AMD) - causes central vision loss (scotoma) and distortion (metamorphopsia), whereas cataracts typically cause more generalised blurring and glare.
  • Open-angle glaucoma - typically causes slow, painless peripheral visual field loss, which is often asymptomatic until advanced, unlike the central visual symptoms of cataracts. A key distinguishing feature is optic disc cupping.
  • Diabetic retinopathy - can cause blurred vision, but this is due to macular oedema or vitreous haemorrhage, and fundoscopy will reveal characteristic retinal microvascular changes.
  • Corneal pathology - conditions like corneal dystrophies or scarring can cause blurred vision and glare, but the opacity is located on the cornea, which can be distinguished on slit-lamp examination.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Cataracts (Eye)"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: The main priority is to establish the degree of visual impairment and its impact on the patient\'s quality of life and daily activities (e.g., driving, reading, working).
  • Definitive management: The only definitive treatment is surgical. This typically involves [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Phacoemulsification: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="phacoemulsification"][/sc], a procedure where the cataractous lens is broken up with ultrasound and removed, followed by the implantation of an artificial intraocular lens (IOL).
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Post-operative follow-up is essential to monitor for complications such as infection, inflammation, and raised intraocular pressure, and to assess the final visual outcome.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Patients are advised to seek urgent review if they experience increasing eye pain, significant reduction in vision, or purulent discharge after surgery, as these may indicate serious complications like endophthalmitis.
  • Exam pharmacology reference (UKMLA/MRCGP): Knowledge of specific drugs is not a primary focus for this condition, as management is surgical. Post-operative eye drops (antibiotics and steroids) are used, but specific regimens are outside the scope of this educational script. No doses or frequencies are cited here — always verify against your local antimicrobial/prescribing formulary before prescribing.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Cataracts (Eye)"][/sc]
  • Posterior Capsular Opacification (PCO): The most common long-term complication of surgery, where the capsule behind the IOL becomes cloudy, causing a secondary decline in vision months to years later. It is treated with a simple laser procedure.
  • Cystoid Macular Oedema (CMO): Fluid accumulation in the macula can occur weeks to months after surgery, leading to blurred or distorted central vision. It often responds to topical anti-inflammatory treatment.
  • Retinal Detachment: A rare but serious complication that can occur months to years post-operatively, presenting with flashes, floaters, and a "curtain" in the visual field, requiring urgent surgical repair.
  • Endophthalmitis: A severe and sight-threatening intraocular infection that typically presents with pain, redness, and profound vision loss within the first week after surgery. It is a true ophthalmic emergency.
  • Raised Intraocular Pressure: A transient or persistent rise in eye pressure can occur post-operatively, increasing the risk of glaucoma if not detected and managed appropriately.
