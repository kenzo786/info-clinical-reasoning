Mesenteric adenitis [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Mesenteric adenitis"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Mesenteric adenitis"][/sc]
  • Mesenteric adenitis is a self-limiting inflammatory condition characterised by enlarged mesenteric lymph nodes, typically following a viral infection, causing abdominal pain in children.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical presentation of mesenteric adenitis, differentiate it from acute appendicitis, and outline appropriate supportive management.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Mesenteric adenitis"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Mesenteric adenitis"][/sc]
  • Mesenteric adenitis is a common cause of acute abdominal pain in children under 16 years, with a higher incidence in those aged 6-10 years.
  • It is much less common in adults and often follows a recent viral upper respiratory tract infection or gastroenteritis.
  • While exact UK prevalence is unknown due to its self-limiting nature and diagnostic overlap with other conditions, it is considered more common than acute appendicitis in the first decade of life.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Mesenteric adenitis"][/sc]
  • Trigger: Often initiated by a viral infection (e.g., adenovirus, coxsackievirus) or, less commonly, bacterial pathogens like Yersinia enterocolitica, which are ingested orally and cause localised inflammation in the gastrointestinal tract.
  • Progression: Pathogens travel to the mesenteric lymph nodes, particularly those surrounding the terminal ileum, leading to their inflammation and enlargement. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Mesenteric lymph nodes: what are they and why do they matter clinically?" text="Mesenteric lymph nodes"][/sc]
  • Consequence: The swollen lymph nodes cause abdominal pain, tenderness, and systemic inflammatory responses such as fever, mimicking other acute abdominal conditions.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Mesenteric adenitis"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Symptoms typically begin abruptly, often preceded by a viral prodrome (sore throat, flu-like symptoms) within 24-48 hours.
  • Progressive phase: Abdominal pain, fever, and gastrointestinal symptoms (nausea, vomiting, diarrhoea) usually intensify over the subsequent 2-3 days.
  • Late phase: The condition is self-limiting, with symptoms generally improving within a few days and resolving completely within 1-2 weeks, particularly beyond 7-10 days.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Mesenteric adenitis"][/sc]
  • Ask about abdominal pain - typically periumbilical or in the right iliac fossa, which can be diffuse or localised, helping to differentiate from classic appendicitis pain migration.
  • Ask about fever - often present, indicating systemic inflammation, but its height and duration can vary.
  • Ask about nausea and vomiting - common, suggesting gastrointestinal involvement and can contribute to dehydration.
  • Ask about diarrhoea - frequently accompanies viral causes, providing a clue to the underlying aetiology.
  • Ask about preceding viral illness - a history of sore throat or flu-like symptoms often precedes abdominal pain, suggesting a viral trigger.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Mesenteric adenitis"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - fever is common, but other vital signs are typically stable, helping to rule out severe sepsis.
  • Check abdominal tenderness - often diffuse or localised to the right iliac fossa, but typically less severe and without rebound tenderness or guarding, distinguishing it from peritonitis.
  • Check for palpable abdominal mass - usually absent, but enlarged lymph nodes are occasionally palpable in very thin children.
  • Check for signs of dehydration - due to vomiting and diarrhoea, indicating the need for fluid management.
  • Check for signs of a viral prodrome - pharyngeal erythema or lymphadenopathy in other areas (e.g., cervical) may support a viral cause. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Viral prodrome: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Viral prodrome"][/sc]
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Mesenteric adenitis"][/sc]
  • Persistent or worsening localised right iliac fossa pain: particularly beyond 48-72 hours, which may indicate evolving appendicitis.
  • Rebound tenderness or guarding: suggestive of peritoneal irritation, necessitating urgent surgical review.
  • Haemodynamic instability: signs of shock (tachycardia, hypotension, poor perfusion) indicate severe illness requiring immediate resuscitation.
  • Bilious vomiting or abdominal distension: raising concern for intestinal obstruction or intussusception.
  • Absence of viral prodrome: in conjunction with severe abdominal pain, this may lower the likelihood of mesenteric adenitis and increase suspicion for other surgical causes.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Mesenteric adenitis"][/sc]
  • Infants and toddlers: may present with non-specific symptoms like irritability, poor feeding, or lethargy, making diagnosis challenging.
  • Older children/adolescents: symptoms can closely mimic acute appendicitis, leading to diagnostic dilemma and potential for unnecessary surgical exploration.
  • Immunocompromised children: may have more severe or prolonged illness, and atypical pathogens should be considered.
  • Yersinia infection: can cause more severe abdominal pain, bloody diarrhoea, and erythema nodosum, requiring specific microbiological investigation. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Yersinia enterocolitica infection: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Yersinia enterocolitica"][/sc]
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Mesenteric adenitis"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected mesenteric adenitis:
    • Full blood count (FBC): may show mild leucocytosis, but often normal.
    • C-reactive protein (CRP): may be mildly elevated, but typically lower than in acute appendicitis.
    • Urinalysis: to exclude urinary tract infection.
  • Imaging - Ultrasound abdomen: considered the gold standard for differentiating mesenteric adenitis from appendicitis, demonstrating enlarged mesenteric lymph nodes (≥5mm) and a normal appendix. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Ultrasound findings in mesenteric adenitis: what are they and why do they matter clinically?" text="Ultrasound findings"][/sc]
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible:
    • CT scan: if ultrasound is inconclusive or to rule out other abdominal pathologies, though radiation exposure is a consideration in children.
    • Stool culture: if Yersinia infection is suspected, particularly with severe symptoms or bloody diarrhoea.
  • Serial reassessment: close clinical observation and repeated abdominal examinations are crucial to monitor for symptom progression or resolution, especially when diagnosis is uncertain.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Mesenteric adenitis"][/sc]
  • A previously well 8-year-old boy presents with a 2-day history of periumbilical abdominal pain, fever, and mild diarrhoea, following a recent viral upper respiratory tract infection.
  • Child with acute abdominal pain and fever, concerning for mesenteric adenitis, requiring differentiation from acute appendicitis.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Mesenteric adenitis"][/sc]
  • Acute appendicitis - localised right iliac fossa pain with rebound tenderness and guarding, often progressing over hours, typically without a prominent viral prodrome. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Differentiating mesenteric adenitis from appendicitis: what are the key features?" text="Acute appendicitis"][/sc]
  • Gastroenteritis - diffuse abdominal pain, prominent vomiting and diarrhoea, usually without localised tenderness or significant fever.
  • Intussusception - intermittent, severe colicky abdominal pain, 'redcurrant jelly' stools, and a palpable sausage-shaped mass (more common in infants).
  • Urinary tract infection (UTI) - dysuria, frequency, suprapubic pain, positive urinalysis.
  • Meckel's diverticulitis - can mimic appendicitis, often diagnosed intraoperatively or with specific imaging.
  • Ovarian torsion (in females) - sudden onset severe unilateral lower abdominal pain, often with vomiting, not typically preceded by viral illness.
  • Pelvic inflammatory disease (PID) (in adolescent females) - lower abdominal pain, vaginal discharge, cervical motion tenderness, often with a history of sexual activity.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Mesenteric adenitis"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Recognition of the condition and differentiation from surgical emergencies; supportive care often includes adequate analgesia and hydration.
  • Definitive management: As mesenteric adenitis is typically self-limiting, management is primarily supportive, focusing on symptom relief.
  • Adjunctive management: Oral rehydration may support recovery from associated gastroenteritis; antipyretics for fever.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Close observation for worsening symptoms or development of red flag features is crucial; safety-netting advice for parents.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Advise parents to seek urgent medical review if pain worsens, new symptoms develop, or the child becomes more unwell.
  • Exam pharmacology reference (UKMLA/MRCGP):
    • Antibiotics are generally not indicated for viral mesenteric adenitis.
    • In suspected bacterial causes, such as Yersinia enterocolitica, specific antibiotic therapy may be considered. [sc name="jitl-medication" Q="Antibiotics for Yersinia enterocolitica infection" text="Ciprofloxacin"][/sc]

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Mesenteric adenitis"][/sc]
  • Misdiagnosis of acute appendicitis: leading to unnecessary surgical intervention, particularly in the early phase when symptoms overlap significantly.
  • Dehydration: due to persistent vomiting and diarrhoea, especially in younger children, requiring careful fluid management.
  • Prolonged abdominal pain: symptoms may persist for up to 2 weeks, impacting quality of life and requiring ongoing symptomatic relief.
  • Recurrence: although generally self-limiting, some children may experience recurrent episodes of mesenteric adenitis.
  • Bacterial complications (rare): in cases of Yersinia infection, complications such as erythema nodosum or reactive arthritis may occur in the late phase.
