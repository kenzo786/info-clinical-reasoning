Complications of fractures (including compartment syndrome, non-union, malunion, avascular necrosis) [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Complications of fractures (including compartment syndrome, non-union, malunion, avascular necrosis)"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Complications of fractures (including compartment syndrome, non-union, malunion, avascular necrosis)"][/sc]
  • A diverse group of adverse outcomes that can occur after a bone fracture, disrupting the normal healing process. These can be acute (e.g., compartment syndrome) or chronic (e.g., non-union, malunion, avascular necrosis), leading to significant pain, functional impairment, and long-term disability.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical features of common and important fracture complications and know how to investigate and manage them.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Complications of fractures (including compartment syndrome, non-union, malunion, avascular necrosis)"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Complications of fractures (including compartment syndrome, non-union, malunion, avascular necrosis)"][/sc]
  • Compartment syndrome is a surgical emergency, most commonly associated with tibial shaft fractures (1-9% incidence). Risk factors include high-energy trauma, young age, and male sex.
  • Non-union occurs in approximately 5-10% of all fractures, with higher rates in open fractures, fractures with significant soft tissue damage, and in patients who smoke or have diabetes.
  • Avascular necrosis is most common after fractures of the femoral neck, scaphoid, and talus, due to their tenuous blood supply. For displaced femoral neck fractures, the rate can be as high as 30%.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Complications of fractures (including compartment syndrome, non-union, malunion, avascular necrosis)"][/sc]
  • Trigger: A fracture disrupts bone integrity and damages surrounding soft tissues and blood vessels, initiating an inflammatory cascade and the physiological process of bone healing (inflammation, soft callus, hard callus, remodelling).
  • Progression: Complications arise from interruption of this process. For example, increased pressure within a fascial compartment (compartment syndrome) compromises perfusion; inadequate stability or blood supply leads to failed healing (non-union); or fracture displacement results in healing in an incorrect position (malunion). Disruption of critical arterial supply can lead to bone death ([sc name="jitl-query" Q="Avascular necrosis: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="avascular necrosis"][/sc]).
  • Consequence: The end result is persistent pain, loss of function, deformity, and potentially limb-threatening ischaemia or chronic disability requiring complex surgical intervention.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Complications of fractures (including compartment syndrome, non-union, malunion, avascular necrosis)"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Acute complications like compartment syndrome or nerve/vascular injury typically manifest within hours to the first few days post-fracture.
  • Progressive phase: Malunion becomes apparent as the fracture heals over weeks to months. Concerns about delayed union arise when healing is slower than expected, particularly beyond 3-6 months.
  • Late phase: Non-union is formally diagnosed when a fracture has failed to heal after 6-9 months. Avascular necrosis and post-traumatic arthritis are often late sequelae, presenting months or even years after the initial injury.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Complications of fractures (including compartment syndrome, non-union, malunion, avascular necrosis)"][/sc]
  • Ask about severe pain out of proportion to the injury - this is the cardinal symptom of compartment syndrome and requires urgent assessment.
  • Ask about new or worsening neurological symptoms (paraesthesia, numbness, weakness) - suggests nerve injury or compression, as seen in compartment syndrome.
  • Ask about persistent, deep-seated pain at the fracture site, especially with weight-bearing, months after injury - may indicate non-union or avascular necrosis.
  • Ask about visible deformity or reduced function in the limb after the expected healing period - points towards a malunion.
  • Ask about constitutional symptoms like fever or malaise - could suggest an underlying infection at the fracture site (osteomyelitis), a cause of non-union.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Complications of fractures (including compartment syndrome, non-union, malunion, avascular necrosis)"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs and overall patient status - tachycardia and hypotension may suggest significant haemorrhage or systemic illness like sepsis from an infected fracture.
  • Check the affected limb for the '6 Ps' of acute limb ischaemia - pain on passive stretch, pallor, pulselessness, paraesthesia, paralysis, and 'perishingly cold' feel. These are late and ominous signs of [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Compartment syndrome: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="compartment syndrome"][/sc].
  • Check for a tense, swollen, and tender muscle compartment - a key physical finding in compartment syndrome.
  • Check for abnormal mobility or tenderness at a fracture site long after the injury - suggests non-union.
  • Check for limb deformity, shortening, or rotational malalignment - indicates a malunion.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Complications of fractures (including compartment syndrome, non-union, malunion, avascular necrosis)"][/sc]
  • Pain out of proportion - The most sensitive early indicator of compartment syndrome.
  • New or progressive neurological deficit - Suggests evolving nerve compression or ischaemia requiring urgent intervention.
  • Absent pulses - A late and critical sign of vascular compromise or advanced compartment syndrome.
  • Signs of infection (erythema, warmth, purulent discharge, fever) - May indicate osteomyelitis, which requires aggressive management and can prevent fracture healing.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Complications of fractures (including compartment syndrome, non-union, malunion, avascular necrosis)"][/sc]
  • Older adults: May present with less intense pain and more subtle functional decline. A change in mobility or inability to weight-bear may be the primary sign of a non-union. They are also at higher risk of avascular necrosis and malunion due to poorer bone quality.
  • Patients with neuropathy: Pre-existing sensory deficits (e.g., in diabetes) can mask the severe pain of compartment syndrome, leading to delayed diagnosis. A high index of suspicion is crucial.
  • Unconscious or ventilated patients: Cannot report pain. Diagnosis of compartment syndrome relies entirely on physical signs (tense compartment, rising opioid requirements) and objective pressure measurements.
  • Children: Have greater healing potential, making non-union less common. However, fractures involving the growth plate (physeal injuries) can lead to growth arrest and limb length discrepancy, a form of malunion.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Complications of fractures (including compartment syndrome, non-union, malunion, avascular necrosis)"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected complication: Inflammatory markers (CRP, WCC) may be raised in infection (osteomyelitis) or severe inflammation. A creatine kinase (CK) level is essential if compartment syndrome is suspected; a significantly raised level indicates muscle necrosis.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: In cases of persistent pain, consider causes other than fracture complications, such as complex regional pain syndrome or referred pain.
  • Imaging - X-ray: The primary modality. Serial X-rays are used to assess healing progression. In non-union, they show persistent fracture lines and sclerotic bone ends. Malunion is diagnosed by visible angular or rotational deformity. In avascular necrosis, early X-rays may be normal, but later show sclerosis, subchondral collapse (crescent sign), and joint destruction.
  • Imaging - CT/MRI: CT is excellent for characterising bone detail, defining non-union anatomy, and planning surgery. MRI is superior for detecting early avascular necrosis (bone marrow oedema) and assessing soft tissues and infection.
  • Serial reassessment: For suspected compartment syndrome, direct intra-compartmental pressure measurement is the gold standard diagnostic test if the clinical picture is equivocal. A differential pressure (diastolic BP - compartment pressure) of <30 mmHg is highly suggestive.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Complications of fractures (including compartment syndrome, non-union, malunion, avascular necrosis)"][/sc]
  • A 24-year-old male presents with escalating pain and swelling in his lower leg, 6 hours after a tibial fracture sustained while playing football. The pain is not relieved by parenteral opioids, and he reports tingling in his foot. On examination, the calf is extremely tense and exquisitely tender to passive toe extension.
  • This is a presentation of acute, severe, localised limb pain with neurological disturbance following a long bone fracture, raising immediate concern for compartment syndrome, an acute limb-threatening complication of trauma.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Complications of fractures (including compartment syndrome, non-union, malunion, avascular necrosis)"][/sc]
  • Deep Vein Thrombosis (DVT) - Can also cause a painful, swollen limb, but pain is not typically as severe or related to passive stretch, and neurological signs are absent.
  • Cellulitis/Infection - Presents with erythema, warmth, and possibly systemic signs, but less likely to cause the extreme pain on passive stretch or tense compartment seen in compartment syndrome.
  • Nerve injury (direct trauma) - Can cause neurological symptoms, but would not typically be associated with the diffuse, tense swelling and disproportionate pain of compartment syndrome.
  • Complex Regional Pain Syndrome (CRPS) - A diagnosis of exclusion, characterised by chronic pain, autonomic dysfunction, and trophic changes, usually developing later in the time course.
  • Uncomplicated fracture pain - Pain is expected after a fracture, but it should be manageable with appropriate analgesia and improve over time, unlike the escalating pain of compartment syndrome.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Complications of fractures (including compartment syndrome, non-union, malunion, avascular necrosis)"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: For suspected compartment syndrome, immediate escalation to a senior orthopaedic surgeon is critical. All constrictive dressings, casts, or splints should be removed down to the skin. The limb should be elevated to the level of the heart, not above, to avoid compromising arterial inflow.
  • Definitive management: Confirmed compartment syndrome requires emergency fasciotomy. Non-union and malunion often require complex revision surgery to correct alignment, improve stability (e.g., with bone grafting), and promote healing. Avascular necrosis may be managed with core decompression in early stages, or joint replacement (arthroplasty) for advanced disease.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Regular clinical and radiographic follow-up is essential for all fractures to monitor for complications. Post-fasciotomy, wounds are often managed with delayed primary closure or skin grafting.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Patients discharged post-fracture should be counselled on the red flag symptoms of compartment syndrome and infection, with clear instructions on when and how to seek urgent medical attention.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Complications of fractures (including compartment syndrome, non-union, malunion, avascular necrosis)"][/sc]
  • Muscle necrosis and rhabdomyolysis: Untreated compartment syndrome leads to irreversible muscle death within 4-6 hours, releasing myoglobin and causing acute kidney injury.
  • Volkmann's ischaemic contracture: A permanent, claw-like deformity of the hand or foot resulting from muscle and nerve necrosis following compartment syndrome.
  • Chronic pain and disability: Non-union, malunion, and post-traumatic arthritis are major causes of long-term pain, stiffness, and functional limitation, often requiring long-term analgesia and rehabilitation.
  • Amputation: In severe, untreated compartment syndrome, overwhelming infection (e.g., gas gangrene), or failed limb salvage surgery, amputation may be the only option.
  • Joint destruction: Avascular necrosis and severe malunion can lead to rapid secondary osteoarthritis, necessitating joint replacement surgery.
