Deep Vein Thrombosis [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Deep Vein Thrombosis"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Deep Vein Thrombosis"][/sc]
  • Deep vein thrombosis (DVT) is the formation of a thrombus within the deep venous system — most commonly the proximal veins of the lower limb (popliteal, femoral, iliac) — arising from [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Virchow's triad: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Virchow's triad"][/sc] of venous stasis, endothelial injury, and hypercoagulability, causing local pain and swelling and carrying significant risk of thrombus embolisation to the pulmonary vasculature.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Use the Wells DVT score as a pre-test probability aid to guide D-dimer and compression ultrasound use per NICE NG158; recognise DVT and its major complication (pulmonary embolism); identify high-risk groups including pregnancy and active malignancy; understand anticoagulation treatment options, duration decisions, and the implications of unprovoked DVT.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Deep Vein Thrombosis"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Deep Vein Thrombosis"][/sc]
  • DVT affects approximately 1–2 per 1,000 people per year in the UK; venous thromboembolism (DVT and pulmonary embolism combined) is estimated to affect 25,000–30,000 hospitalised patients annually and causes approximately 25,000 deaths per year in the UK — more than breast cancer, AIDS, and road traffic accidents combined. The lifetime risk of VTE is approximately 5% in the general population. Incidence increases steeply with age, roughly doubling with each decade after 40. DVT is slightly more common in men overall; however, in women of reproductive age the incidence is higher due to oestrogen-containing contraception and pregnancy.
  • Risk factors follow Virchow's triad: stasis (prolonged immobility from long-haul travel, hospital admission, lower limb plaster cast, or paralysis; heart failure; obesity); endothelial injury (recent lower limb or pelvic surgery, trauma, central venous catheter); and hypercoagulability (active malignancy — the single highest risk factor; thrombophilia including Factor V Leiden, antiphospholipid syndrome, protein C or S deficiency; oestrogen-containing contraception or HRT; pregnancy and the puerperium; inflammatory bowel disease; nephrotic syndrome). Pregnancy carries approximately 5-fold increased risk of DVT, rising to 20-fold in the first 6 weeks postpartum.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Deep Vein Thrombosis"][/sc]
  • Thrombus formation is initiated at venous valve pockets in the lower limb, where turbulent blood flow and relative stasis create a microenvironment of reduced thrombin clearance; exposure of subendothelial collagen from venous injury activates the coagulation cascade, and hypercoagulable states (malignancy, thrombophilia, hormonal contraception) amplify thrombin generation beyond the capacity of natural anticoagulant mechanisms (protein C, protein S, antithrombin).
  • The thrombus propagates proximally from calf veins (distal DVT) towards the popliteal, femoral, and iliac veins (proximal DVT); proximal extension substantially increases the risk of thrombus dislodgement and embolisation; venous outflow obstruction causes hydrostatic pressure rise distal to the thrombus, driving fluid into the interstitium and producing the characteristic unilateral pitting oedema and limb swelling; perivenous inflammation generates localised pain, warmth, and erythema.
  • Untreated proximal DVT carries an estimated 50% risk of pulmonary embolism; even without acute PE, chronic thrombus-mediated destruction of deep vein valves leads to sustained venous hypertension — [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Post-thrombotic syndrome: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="post-thrombotic syndrome"][/sc] — which develops in approximately 20–40% of patients after proximal DVT and causes chronic limb pain, swelling, haemosiderin deposition, lipodermatosclerosis, and venous ulceration; massively obstructive iliofemoral DVT may progress to phlegmasia cerulea dolens with limb-threatening venous gangrene.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Deep Vein Thrombosis"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Onset of posterior calf aching or tightness developing over hours to 1–2 days; up to 50% of DVTs are entirely asymptomatic at this stage and discovered incidentally or during investigation for PE; mild unilateral ankle swelling and calf tightness on weight-bearing may be the only features.
  • Progressive phase: Increasing unilateral limb swelling extending from the calf to the thigh for proximal DVTs over 2–5 days; pain worsens with dependency; skin warmth and erythema develop along the deep venous distribution; the limb may feel heavy or tense; proximal propagation above the popliteal vein marks the transition to the highest-risk period for PE.
  • Late phase: Pulmonary embolism or post-thrombotic syndrome develops particularly beyond several days of untreated proximal DVT; thrombus may embolise at any point once formed, but risk accumulates with delay; massive iliofemoral thrombosis can cause phlegmasia cerulea dolens with impending venous gangrene; chronic post-thrombotic changes develop over weeks to months.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Deep Vein Thrombosis"][/sc]
  • Ask about unilateral calf or leg pain — the cardinal presenting symptom; typically a dull, aching, or throbbing posterior calf pain worsening with weight bearing and dependency; unilaterality is the key discriminating feature from bilateral physiological swelling or systemic causes of oedema; ask whether the pain came on gradually (typical of DVT) or suddenly (raises suspicion of calf muscle tear or ruptured Baker's cyst).
  • Ask about unilateral leg swelling — ask the patient to compare both limbs and note whether the swelling is limited to the calf (distal DVT) or extends to the knee, thigh, or groin (proximal DVT); proximal DVT carries substantially higher PE risk than isolated calf DVT and requires urgent imaging; entire limb swelling is a Wells score component.
  • Ask about symptoms of concurrent pulmonary embolism — dyspnoea, pleuritic chest pain, haemoptysis, palpitations, or pre-syncope; approximately 30% of patients with proximal DVT have asymptomatic PE on CTPA at presentation, and symptomatic PE may be the presenting feature prompting investigation of an underlying DVT; any respiratory symptoms alongside a swollen leg represent a medical emergency.
  • Ask about VTE risk factors — recent immobility (long-haul flight, bed rest), surgery within 12 weeks (especially lower limb orthopaedic or pelvic procedures), active cancer (new, treated, or palliative), use of oestrogen-containing contraception or HRT, pregnancy or recent delivery, personal or family history of VTE, and known thrombophilia; establishing the risk factor profile determines both the pre-test probability (Wells score) and informs the duration and nature of anticoagulation.
  • Ask about the onset pattern and any alternative explanations — sudden-onset posterior calf pain after exertion suggests musculoskeletal cause (gastrocnemius tear) or Baker's cyst rupture; diffuse limb redness with fever and systemic upset points to cellulitis; long-standing bilateral swelling without acute onset suggests chronic venous insufficiency or a systemic cause rather than acute DVT; establishing whether the current presentation represents a new, acute event or exacerbation of a chronic condition is an essential discriminating step.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Deep Vein Thrombosis"][/sc]
  • Check for unilateral pitting oedema and calf circumference asymmetry — measure the calf 10 cm below the tibial tuberosity; a difference of ≥3 cm compared to the asymptomatic leg is a Wells score component and supports DVT; assess whether the oedema is confined to the symptomatic leg (another Wells component) or bilateral (suggests systemic cause); pitting rather than non-pitting pattern favours DVT or cardiac/renal cause over lymphoedema.
  • Check for localised tenderness along the deep venous distribution — posterior calf tenderness following the course of the posterior tibial and peroneal veins (not anteriorly over the tibia, which suggests periosteal or musculoskeletal pathology); popliteal fossa tenderness for popliteal DVT; tenderness along the medial thigh for femoral DVT; this distribution of tenderness is a Wells score component.
  • Check for Homan's sign — passive dorsiflexion of the foot causing posterior calf pain; historically tested but has poor sensitivity (~50%) and specificity and is no longer recommended as a reliable standalone diagnostic sign; its presence should be considered alongside other clinical features rather than used in isolation, and a negative result does not exclude DVT.
  • Check skin temperature, colour, and collateral veins — unilateral warmth and blanching erythema over the deep venous distribution; non-varicose superficial collateral veins developing over the medial thigh or calf indicate chronic venous obstruction and are a Wells score component; mottled cyanosis with a cold, tense, swollen limb suggests phlegmasia cerulea dolens — a limb-threatening emergency; temperature and heart rate should be assessed: fever raises the differential of cellulitis; tachycardia warrants concurrent PE assessment.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Deep Vein Thrombosis"][/sc]
  • Concurrent features of pulmonary embolism — new dyspnoea, pleuritic chest pain, haemoptysis, tachycardia, or haemodynamic instability alongside a swollen leg indicates DVT with likely PE; PE mortality without treatment approaches 30% — do not defer assessment and imaging to the following day; this combination requires same-attendance CTPA or V/Q scan and urgent anticoagulation.
  • Phlegmasia cerulea dolens — massive iliofemoral thrombosis causing near-complete venous outflow obstruction; presents with a tense, grossly swollen, cyanotic or mottled lower limb with severe pain and impending venous gangrene; requires emergency vascular surgery or interventional radiology review for catheter-directed thrombolysis or mechanical thrombectomy; limb loss is a risk if untreated.
  • Unprovoked DVT in a patient over 40 — DVT without a clear provoking factor (recent surgery, immobility, hormonal therapy) significantly increases the probability of underlying occult malignancy, particularly gastrointestinal, lung, breast, or haematological cancers; NICE NG12 recommends considering appropriate investigations to exclude malignancy; this finding also changes anticoagulation duration (typically ≥6 months rather than 3).
  • Delay of several days or more before presentation with untreated proximal DVT — the cumulative risk of PE and thrombus propagation rises particularly beyond several days of untreated proximal DVT; patients presenting late with already-established DVT and no anticoagulation should not be deferred for outpatient workup in the following days — same-day clinical decision-making and anticoagulation initiation are warranted.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Deep Vein Thrombosis"][/sc]
  • Upper limb DVT (Paget-Schroetter syndrome / effort thrombosis): Thrombosis of the axillary or subclavian vein, most commonly in young adults after vigorous repetitive overhead activity (swimming, throwing sports) or related to central venous catheter or pacemaker lead; presents with unilateral arm pain, swelling, and prominence of superficial arm veins; compression ultrasound is first-line but may miss isolated subclavian or innominate thrombosis, which requires CT venography; management and anticoagulation duration parallel lower limb DVT but with specific considerations around thoracic outlet decompression.
  • Pregnancy: DVT risk rises approximately 5-fold in pregnancy and 20-fold in the early puerperium; left iliofemoral DVT predominates due to compression of the left common iliac vein by the gravid uterus and right iliac artery (May-Thurner-like anatomy); physiological leg oedema, positional discomfort, and raised D-dimer (normal in pregnancy) make clinical assessment unreliable; compression ultrasound is the imaging of choice; MR venography is preferred over CT venography to avoid fetal radiation; anticoagulant choice is restricted to low molecular weight heparin (DOACs are not recommended in pregnancy or breastfeeding).
  • Older adults: DVT may be painless and minimally swollen, discovered incidentally on imaging for another indication; multiple simultaneous Wells score risk factors from comorbid illness (malignancy, immobility, prior VTE) can elevate the score without a new acute thrombotic event, reducing score specificity; D-dimer rises with age and is more often falsely elevated; age-adjusted D-dimer thresholds (age × 10 μg/L in patients over 50 years) are validated in some clinical pathways and may help avoid unnecessary imaging.
  • Cancer-associated VTE: Malignancy is the highest-risk provoking factor for VTE; thrombosis may occur at atypical sites (mesenteric veins, hepatic veins, splenic veins, cerebral venous sinuses); bilateral or recurrent DVT warrants malignancy screening; management differs from standard VTE — low molecular weight heparin or direct oral anticoagulants (per NICE NG196 for cancer-associated VTE) are often preferred over vitamin K antagonists due to unpredictable anticoagulant control in cancer patients; indefinite anticoagulation is usually required while the cancer remains active.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Deep Vein Thrombosis"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected DVT: Wells DVT score is used first to stratify pre-test probability before ordering investigations, and should be interpreted alongside the wider clinical picture; a calculator is available at Wells DVT criteria on MDCalc. D-dimer is most useful when pre-test probability is lower, because a negative result can help exclude DVT whereas a positive result still needs imaging correlation.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: FBC and CRP (neutrophilia and markedly raised CRP support cellulitis over DVT); LFTs and serum albumin (hypoalbuminaemia as a cause of bilateral oedema); urinalysis and protein:creatinine ratio (nephrotic syndrome presenting with bilateral oedema); musculoskeletal ultrasound if gastrocnemius tear or popliteal cyst rupture is suspected (both cause acute posterior calf pain and swelling mimicking DVT); thrombophilia screen and malignancy investigation for unprovoked DVT (after discussion of timing with haematology, as acute thrombosis affects results of protein C/S and antithrombin).
  • Imaging — compression ultrasound: First-line investigation for suspected DVT (NICE NG158); positive finding is non-compressibility of the deep vein lumen under probe pressure; sensitivity ~94% for proximal DVT but only ~63% for isolated distal (calf) DVT; cannot reliably image iliac veins or inferior vena cava; NICE NG158 recommends repeat proximal ultrasound at 6–8 days if the initial scan is negative but the Wells score is ≥2 or D-dimer is positive, to detect proximal propagation from calf thrombus.
  • Imaging — CT venography or MRI venography: Indicated for suspected iliac vein or inferior vena cava thrombosis not visualised on compression ultrasound (bilateral leg swelling, retroperitoneal symptoms, or failed/inconclusive duplex); MR venography is preferred in pregnancy to avoid ionising radiation; CT venography provides the most complete anatomical delineation of iliocaval thrombosis and guides interventional decision-making in phlegmasia.
  • Serial reassessment: If initial proximal leg ultrasound is negative but Wells score ≥2 (DVT likely) or D-dimer is positive, NICE NG158 recommends repeat proximal ultrasound at 6–8 days; if second scan is also negative in a patient with positive D-dimer, DVT is effectively excluded and alternative diagnoses should be explored; repeat D-dimer is not useful for monitoring response to anticoagulation in established DVT.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Deep Vein Thrombosis"][/sc]
  • A 44-year-old woman taking combined oral contraceptive medication presents with 4 days of progressive left calf pain and swelling following a 10-hour transatlantic flight; examination reveals unilateral pitting oedema to the knee, left calf circumference 4 cm greater than the right, posterior calf tenderness along the deep venous distribution, and collateral superficial veins; Wells DVT score is 4 (DVT likely).
  • Middle-aged woman on oestrogen-containing contraception with multiple concurrent VTE risk factors (prolonged immobility from air travel, hormonal therapy) presenting with unilateral progressive calf pain and oedema, high Wells DVT probability — consistent with proximal DVT pending urgent compression ultrasound.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Deep Vein Thrombosis"][/sc]
  • Cellulitis — diffuse spreading erythema, warmth, and tenderness with systemic features (fever, raised WBC, CRP); erythema typically extends well beyond the deep venous distribution and is associated with a skin entry point; D-dimer may be mildly elevated; compression ultrasound differentiates; cellulitis and DVT can coexist.
  • Ruptured Baker's (popliteal) cyst — sudden onset of posterior calf pain and swelling in a patient with knee joint disease (osteoarthritis, rheumatoid arthritis); synovial fluid dissects distally into the calf fascia (pseudothrombophlebitis syndrome); ultrasound shows a fluid collection rather than deep vein non-compressibility; Wells score usually low; knee examination reveals joint effusion or crepitus.
  • Calf muscle haematoma or tear (gastrocnemius strain) — acute onset during or after exercise; bruising may track to the ankle or appear after a day or two; tenderness is muscular rather than along venous distribution; ultrasound shows intramuscular haematoma or disruption; no risk factors for VTE; compression ultrasound differentiates reliably.
  • Superficial thrombophlebitis — visible and palpable cord along a superficial vein (varicose or great saphenous vein) with overlying erythema and tenderness; D-dimer usually mildly elevated; compression ultrasound confirms superficial rather than deep vein involvement; importantly, approximately 25% of cases have an associated DVT, so full duplex assessment is recommended.
  • Chronic venous insufficiency — bilateral or chronic unilateral lower limb oedema; varicose veins, haemosiderin deposition, lipodermatosclerosis, or venous ulcers on examination; no acute onset; Wells score low; D-dimer not elevated unless there is intercurrent thrombosis.
  • Lymphoedema — non-pitting oedema (Stemmer's sign: inability to pinch skin over the dorsum of the second toe); typically affects the foot and toes as well as the limb; no acute onset; warmth and erythema absent unless secondary infection; Wells score low.
  • Cardiac, hepatic, or renal oedema — bilateral symmetrical dependent oedema; raised JVP and S3 in heart failure; ascites and spider naevi in cirrhosis; significant proteinuria in nephrotic syndrome; D-dimer not acutely elevated; Wells score would typically score 0 or 1.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Deep Vein Thrombosis"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Recognise DVT promptly to reduce thrombus propagation and pulmonary embolism risk, and actively look for concurrent PE if any respiratory symptoms are present. Supportive care often includes analgesia, limb elevation for comfort, and early mobilisation rather than prolonged immobility.
  • Definitive management: Therapeutic anticoagulation is the cornerstone of treatment; duration depends on whether the event is provoked, unprovoked, cancer-associated, recurrent, or linked to a persistent thrombophilic risk.
  • Adjunctive management: Compression stockings are mainly a symptom-relief tool rather than a routine post-thrombotic syndrome prevention strategy, and massive iliofemoral DVT or phlegmasia may shift the case toward specialist procedural review.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Routine repeat ultrasound is not usually needed in uncomplicated DVT; the key follow-up questions are whether symptoms are improving, whether anticoagulation remains appropriate, and whether an unprovoked event should trigger malignancy or thrombophilia review.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Any new dyspnoea, pleuritic chest pain, haemoptysis, presyncope, haemodynamic instability, or an acutely ischaemic-looking limb should prompt urgent reassessment for PE or limb-threatening venous obstruction.
  • Exam pharmacology reference (UKMLA/MRCGP): NICE NG158 describes direct oral anticoagulants as first-line treatment for acute DVT in most patients: [sc name="jitl-medication" Q="Apixaban use in Deep Vein Thrombosis" text="apixaban"][/sc] or [sc name="jitl-medication" Q="Rivaroxaban use in Deep Vein Thrombosis" text="rivaroxaban"][/sc] are commonly used; [sc name="jitl-medication" Q="Dabigatran use in Deep Vein Thrombosis" text="dabigatran"][/sc] and [sc name="jitl-medication" Q="Edoxaban use in Deep Vein Thrombosis" text="edoxaban"][/sc] are alternatives requiring initial parenteral anticoagulation. Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Deep Vein Thrombosis"][/sc]
  • Pulmonary embolism: The most immediately dangerous complication of DVT, especially with untreated proximal clot, and the main reason a swollen leg with new chest symptoms should never be treated as a routine outpatient problem.
  • Post-thrombotic syndrome: Chronic venous insufficiency from valve damage after DVT, causing ongoing pain, heaviness, oedema, skin change, and later ulceration despite apparent resolution of the acute event.
  • Recurrent VTE: Risk of recurrence is highest after unprovoked or persistent-risk DVT, so failure to identify the provoking context changes how the illness should be framed and followed up.
  • [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Phlegmasia cerulea dolens: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Phlegmasia cerulea dolens"][/sc] (rare but exam-relevant): Massive iliofemoral DVT causing near-complete venous outflow obstruction; presents with a grossly swollen, tense, cyanotic or mottled lower limb with severe pain and skin blanching; venous gangrene may follow without prompt treatment; an important diagnosis not to miss when a swollen limb appears acutely ischaemic-looking rather than merely oedematous.
