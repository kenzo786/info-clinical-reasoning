Pancreatic cancer [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Pancreatic cancer"]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Pancreatic cancer"]
  • Pancreatic cancer, most commonly pancreatic ductal adenocarcinoma (PDAC), arises from the exocrine cells of the pancreas, often presenting at an advanced stage due to its insidious onset and non-specific symptoms.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the common presentations and risk factors for pancreatic cancer, understand the principles of its investigation and multidisciplinary management, and identify red flag features necessitating urgent referral.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Pancreatic cancer"]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Pancreatic cancer"]
  • Pancreatic cancer is the 10th most common cancer in the UK, accounting for 3% of all new cancer cases, with around 10,500 new cases diagnosed each year [NICE NG85].
  • It is the 5th most common cause of cancer death in the UK, with a 5-year survival rate of approximately 7% [NICE NG85].
  • Risk factors include increasing age, smoking, obesity, chronic pancreatitis, diabetes mellitus, and a family history of pancreatic cancer (e.g., BRCA1/2, PALB2, CDKN2A mutations, Peutz-Jeghers syndrome, Lynch syndrome) [NICE NG85].
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Pancreatic cancer"]
  • Trigger: Pancreatic ductal adenocarcinoma (PDAC) typically originates from the epithelial cells lining the pancreatic ducts, often preceded by precursor lesions such as pancreatic intraepithelial neoplasia (PanIN).
  • Progression: Genetic mutations (e.g., KRAS, TP53, SMAD4, CDKN2A) drive uncontrolled cellular proliferation and invasion, leading to local tumour growth and early metastasis, particularly to regional lymph nodes and the liver.
  • Consequence: Tumour growth can obstruct the common bile duct, causing obstructive jaundice, or invade surrounding structures, leading to pain, malabsorption, and systemic symptoms. Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Pancreatic cancer"]
  • Early phase: Pancreatic cancer often remains asymptomatic or presents with vague, non-specific symptoms (e.g., indigestion, mild abdominal discomfort) in its early stages, making early diagnosis challenging.
  • Progressive phase: As the tumour grows, symptoms become more pronounced, including progressive jaundice, unexplained weight loss, new-onset diabetes, and persistent abdominal or back pain. This phase can evolve over weeks to months.
  • Late phase: Advanced disease is characterised by severe pain, significant weight loss, malabsorption, and symptoms related to metastatic spread (e.g., liver metastases, ascites), often developing over several months.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Pancreatic cancer"]
  • Ask about painless obstructive jaundice - why it matters for reasoning/discrimination: This is a classic presentation of pancreatic head tumours, often due to common bile duct compression, and is a key red flag.
  • Ask about unexplained weight loss - why it matters for reasoning/discrimination: A common and often early symptom, indicating systemic disease or malabsorption due to pancreatic exocrine insufficiency.
  • Ask about new-onset diabetes or worsening of existing diabetes - why it matters for reasoning/discrimination: The pancreas plays a crucial role in glucose regulation; tumour involvement can impair insulin production or action.
  • Ask about persistent, dull upper abdominal or back pain - why it matters for reasoning/discrimination: Often radiating to the back, this pain can indicate tumour invasion of retroperitoneal nerves and is a concerning symptom.
  • Ask about steatorrhoea - why it matters for reasoning/discrimination: Fatty stools due to pancreatic exocrine insufficiency, indicating impaired fat digestion.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Pancreatic cancer"]
  • Check vital signs - what it implies: May reveal tachycardia or hypotension in advanced disease or dehydration, but often normal in early stages.
  • Check for jaundice - what it implies: Yellowing of skin and sclera, indicating biliary obstruction, often painless in pancreatic head tumours.
  • Check for Courvoisier sign - what it implies: A palpable, non-tender gallbladder in the presence of jaundice, suggesting obstruction of the common bile duct, typically by a pancreatic head mass. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Courvoisier sign: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Courvoisier sign"]
  • Check for hepatomegaly - what it implies: Enlarged liver, potentially indicating metastatic spread to the liver.
  • Check for ascites - what it implies: Accumulation of fluid in the peritoneal cavity, suggesting peritoneal carcinomatosis or portal hypertension due to liver metastases.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Pancreatic cancer"]
  • New-onset painless jaundice: Suggests biliary obstruction, often due to a pancreatic head mass, requiring urgent investigation (NICE NG12 urgent referral).
  • Unexplained weight loss with new-onset diabetes: A highly suspicious combination, particularly in older adults, warranting urgent pancreatic imaging.
  • Persistent, unexplained upper abdominal or back pain: Especially if radiating to the back and unrelieved by analgesia, may indicate advanced pancreatic cancer.
  • NICE NG12 urgent referral: Patients aged 40 and over with jaundice should be referred urgently for suspected cancer, typically within 2 weeks [NICE NG12].
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Pancreatic cancer"]
  • Older adults: May present with more vague symptoms, such as general malaise, anorexia, or worsening frailty, making diagnosis challenging.
  • Pancreatitis-like symptoms: Some patients may present with acute pancreatitis, which can be a sentinel event for underlying pancreatic cancer, particularly in the absence of typical risk factors for pancreatitis.
  • Depression: New-onset depression, especially in older individuals without a prior psychiatric history, has been noted as a paraneoplastic phenomenon in some cases of pancreatic cancer. Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Pancreatic cancer"]
  • Core for suspected pancreatic cancer: Pancreatic protocol CT scan of the chest, abdomen, and pelvis is the primary imaging modality for diagnosis, staging, and assessing resectability [NICE NG85].
  • Imaging - MRI/MRCP: Magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) and magnetic resonance cholangiopancreatography (MRCP) can provide more detailed imaging of the biliary and pancreatic ducts, useful if CT is inconclusive or for assessing cystic lesions [NICE NG85].
  • Imaging - EUS with biopsy: Endoscopic ultrasound (EUS) with fine-needle aspiration (FNA) or biopsy is crucial for obtaining tissue diagnosis, especially for smaller lesions or when other imaging is equivocal [NICE NG85]. Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Pancreatic cancer"]
  • Choledocholithiasis - distinguished by colicky pain, fluctuating jaundice, and often a history of gallstones.
  • Cholangitis - distinguished by fever, right upper quadrant pain, and jaundice (Charcot triad), often with elevated inflammatory markers.
  • Hepatitis (viral/alcoholic) - distinguished by liver enzyme derangements, specific viral markers, and often a history of risk factors.
  • Gallbladder cancer - distinguished by imaging findings showing a mass originating from the gallbladder, though it can also cause obstructive jaundice.
  • Ampullary carcinoma - distinguished by its origin at the ampulla of Vater, often causing earlier obstructive jaundice and sometimes gastrointestinal bleeding. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Ampullary carcinoma vs pancreatic head cancer: what are the key differences?" text="Ampullary carcinoma"]
  • Chronic pancreatitis - distinguished by a history of recurrent abdominal pain, pancreatic calcifications on imaging, and often pancreatic exocrine/endocrine insufficiency, though it can be a risk factor for cancer.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Pancreatic cancer"]
  • Immediate priorities: Recognition of red flag symptoms and urgent referral to a specialist pancreatic multidisciplinary team (MDT) for prompt diagnosis and staging. Supportive care often includes pain management, nutritional support, and management of biliary obstruction (e.g., stenting).
  • Definitive management: Surgical resection (e.g., Whipple procedure for head of pancreas tumours) offers the only chance of cure for early-stage, resectable disease. Adjuvant chemotherapy is typically recommended post-surgery [NICE NG85].
  • Adjunctive management: For unresectable or metastatic disease, management focuses on palliative chemotherapy (e.g., FOLFIRINOX, gemcitabine-based regimens), pain control, nutritional support, and symptom management. Radiotherapy may be considered in some cases.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Regular follow-up with the MDT, including imaging and tumour markers, is essential to monitor for recurrence or progression and manage long-term complications.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Patients and their families should be educated on symptoms of progression or complications, with clear pathways for urgent review.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Pancreatic cancer"]
  • Biliary obstruction: Caused by tumour compression of the bile duct, leading to jaundice, pruritus, and cholangitis, often requiring endoscopic stenting or surgical bypass.
  • Gastric outlet obstruction: Tumour invasion into the duodenum can cause nausea, vomiting, and early satiety, necessitating stenting or gastrojejunostomy.
  • Pain: Often severe and debilitating, due to tumour invasion of surrounding nerves (e.g., coeliac plexus), requiring aggressive pain management strategies.
  • Malabsorption and weight loss: Pancreatic exocrine insufficiency due to tumour destruction or duct obstruction leads to impaired digestion and absorption, managed with pancreatic enzyme replacement therapy.
  • Venous thromboembolism: Pancreatic cancer is associated with a high risk of deep vein thrombosis and pulmonary embolism, often requiring prophylactic anticoagulation.
  • Metastatic disease: Spread to distant organs, most commonly the liver, lungs, and peritoneum, leading to organ-specific symptoms and significantly impacting prognosis.
