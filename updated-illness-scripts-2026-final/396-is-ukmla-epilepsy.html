Epilepsy [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Epilepsy"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Epilepsy"][/sc]
  • Epilepsy is a chronic neurological disorder characterised by recurrent, unprovoked seizures resulting from abnormal, excessive, or synchronous neuronal activity in the brain.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical features, classification, and initial management of epilepsy, including differentiating epileptic seizures from non-epileptic events.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Epilepsy"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Epilepsy"][/sc]
  • Affects approximately 1 in 100 people in the UK, with around 600,000 people living with epilepsy (Epilepsy Action, 2023). Incidence is highest in early childhood and in older adults.
  • Risk factors include genetic predisposition, structural brain abnormalities (e.g., stroke, tumour, trauma), infections (e.g., meningitis, encephalitis), and certain neurodegenerative conditions.
  • Around 50% of cases have no identifiable cause (idiopathic epilepsy).
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Epilepsy"][/sc]
  • Trigger: An imbalance between excitatory (e.g., glutamate) and inhibitory (e.g., GABA) neurotransmission, often due to genetic factors, structural brain lesions, or metabolic disturbances, leads to a lowered seizure threshold.
  • Progression: Hyperexcitable neuronal networks, either focal or generalised, undergo rapid depolarisation and repolarisation, resulting in synchronous, high-frequency discharges that propagate through neural circuits.
  • Consequence: This uncontrolled electrical activity manifests as a seizure, causing transient neurological dysfunction, which can include altered consciousness, motor, sensory, or autonomic symptoms.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Epilepsy"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Seizures typically last seconds to a few minutes, followed by a post-ictal period of confusion or fatigue that can last minutes to hours.
  • Progressive phase: In some individuals, seizure frequency may increase over time, or new seizure types may emerge, particularly beyond months or years, often influenced by underlying aetiology or inadequate treatment.
  • Late phase: Long-term epilepsy can lead to cognitive decline, mood disorders, and an increased risk of status epilepticus, particularly beyond years of uncontrolled seizures.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Epilepsy"][/sc]
  • Ask about aura - to help localise the seizure onset zone and classify seizure type (e.g., visual disturbances in occipital lobe epilepsy).
  • Ask about loss of consciousness or awareness - crucial for distinguishing focal impaired awareness seizures from focal aware seizures, and for generalised seizures.
  • Ask about involuntary movements (e.g., tonic-clonic, myoclonic, atonic) - to characterise the motor manifestations and aid in seizure classification.
  • Ask about post-ictal phenomena (e.g., confusion, headache, Todd's paralysis) - helps differentiate epileptic seizures from syncope or psychogenic non-epileptic seizures.
  • Ask about triggers (e.g., sleep deprivation, stress, flashing lights) - identifying triggers can inform lifestyle advice and management strategies.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Epilepsy"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - to rule out other causes of collapse (e.g., cardiac arrhythmia) and assess for post-ictal complications.
  • Check for tongue biting (lateral aspect) or urinary incontinence - highly suggestive of an epileptic seizure, particularly a tonic-clonic event.
  • Check neurological examination (e.g., focal weakness, sensory deficits) - may reveal underlying structural brain lesions or post-ictal deficits (Todd's paralysis).
  • Check for signs of head injury or trauma - can indicate a fall during a seizure or an underlying cause of epilepsy.
  • Check for skin stigmata of neurocutaneous syndromes (e.g., café-au-lait spots in neurofibromatosis) - suggests a genetic cause of epilepsy.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Epilepsy"][/sc]
  • First seizure in adulthood - warrants urgent investigation to identify underlying structural or metabolic causes.
  • New focal neurological deficit - suggests an acute brain lesion (e.g., stroke, tumour) requiring immediate imaging.
  • Prolonged post-ictal confusion or unresponsiveness - may indicate status epilepticus or a severe underlying brain injury.
  • Recurrent seizures without full recovery between episodes - indicative of status epilepticus, a medical emergency.
  • Signs of infection (e.g., fever, neck stiffness) - raises concern for meningitis or encephalitis as a cause of seizures.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Epilepsy"][/sc]
  • Older adults: Seizures may present subtly as confusion, falls, or transient neurological deficits, often mistaken for TIA or dementia, due to underlying cerebrovascular disease or neurodegeneration.
  • Pregnancy: Seizure control can be challenging due to hormonal changes and altered drug metabolism, impacting both maternal and foetal health.
  • Learning disabilities: Diagnosis can be delayed or missed as seizure semiology may be atypical or difficult to interpret, requiring careful observation and collateral history.
  • Psychogenic non-epileptic seizures (PNES): Clinically resemble epileptic seizures but lack electrographic correlates, often associated with psychological distress and requiring careful differentiation.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Epilepsy"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected epilepsy:
    • Electroencephalogram (EEG): To detect abnormal brain electrical activity and help classify seizure type and epilepsy syndrome.
    • Brain imaging (MRI preferred): To identify structural causes such as tumours, malformations, or hippocampal sclerosis.
    • Blood tests: Full blood count, electrolytes, glucose, liver and renal function, calcium, magnesium, toxicology screen to rule out metabolic or toxic causes.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible:
    • ECG and echocardiogram: To investigate cardiac causes of syncope or collapse.
    • Tilt table test: For suspected vasovagal syncope.
  • Imaging - [modality]:
    • CT head: For acute presentations, especially to rule out haemorrhage or acute stroke.
    • PET scan: In refractory epilepsy, to identify epileptogenic foci for surgical planning.
  • Serial reassessment: Regular clinical review and repeat EEG if initial studies are inconclusive or if seizure characteristics change.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Epilepsy"][/sc]
  • A 28-year-old male presents with a 6-month history of recurrent, sudden-onset episodes of staring, lip-smacking, and unresponsiveness lasting approximately 1 minute, followed by confusion. He denies any warning symptoms or post-episode weakness.
  • A young adult with subacute, recurrent, focal impaired awareness seizures, without clear triggers or post-ictal focal deficits, suggesting a possible temporal lobe epilepsy.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Epilepsy"][/sc]
  • Syncope - often preceded by prodromal symptoms (e.g., light-headedness, pallor) and associated with postural changes, rarely with lateral tongue biting.
  • Transient ischaemic attack (TIA) - typically presents with focal neurological deficits (e.g., weakness, speech disturbance) without loss of consciousness or post-ictal confusion.
  • Migraine with aura - visual or sensory aura usually precedes headache, and symptoms evolve over minutes, not seconds.
  • Psychogenic non-epileptic seizures (PNES) - often prolonged, fluctuating, and inconsistent with typical epileptic semiology, often occurring in the presence of others.
  • Hypoglycaemia - can cause confusion, altered consciousness, and sometimes seizure-like activity, but is typically associated with sweating, tremor, and can be rapidly reversed with glucose.
  • Movement disorders (e.g., tics, dystonia) - usually conscious and movements are often suppressible or have a different pattern than epileptic seizures.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Epilepsy"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Ensure patient safety during a seizure (e.g., protect from injury, place in recovery position). For status epilepticus, administer benzodiazepines (e.g., lorazepam, diazepam) intravenously or buccally.
  • Definitive management: Initiate appropriate anti-seizure medication (ASM) based on seizure type and epilepsy syndrome (e.g., lamotrigine, levetiracetam, sodium valproate). Consider surgical options for refractory focal epilepsy.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Regular clinical review to assess seizure control, side effects of ASMs, and adherence. Monitor drug levels if clinically indicated.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Advise on seizure first aid, driving restrictions, and provide emergency contact information. Educate on triggers and lifestyle modifications.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Epilepsy"][/sc]
  • Status epilepticus: A medical emergency defined as a seizure lasting longer than 5 minutes, or recurrent seizures without full recovery between episodes, leading to neuronal damage and systemic complications.
  • Sudden Unexpected Death in Epilepsy (SUDEP): The sudden, unexpected, non-traumatic, and non-drowning death in patients with epilepsy, with or without evidence of a seizure, and without a toxicological or anatomical cause of death.
  • Injuries: Falls, fractures, head trauma, and burns can occur during seizures, particularly tonic-clonic events, leading to significant morbidity.
  • Psychosocial impact: Stigma, anxiety, depression, and limitations on driving or employment can significantly affect quality of life and social integration.
  • Cognitive impairment: Chronic epilepsy can lead to difficulties with memory, attention, and executive function, particularly in those with frequent or prolonged seizures.
