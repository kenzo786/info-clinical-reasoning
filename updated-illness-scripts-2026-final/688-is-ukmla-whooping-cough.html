Whooping cough [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Whooping cough"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Whooping cough"][/sc]
  • Whooping cough, or pertussis, is a highly contagious bacterial respiratory tract infection caused by Bordetella pertussis, characterised by paroxysmal coughing followed by an inspiratory "whoop".
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical features and stages of whooping cough, particularly in infants and children, and understand its public health implications and management principles.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Whooping cough"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Whooping cough"][/sc]
  • Whooping cough is a notifiable disease in England and Wales, with cyclical peaks in incidence every 3-4 years.
  • Infants under 3 months of age are at highest risk of severe disease and mortality, with UK data showing significant hospitalisation rates in this age group.
  • Maternal vaccination during pregnancy (typically between 16 and 32 weeks gestation) is a key public health intervention in the UK to protect neonates through passive immunity.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Whooping cough"][/sc]
  • Trigger: Inhalation of aerosolised droplets containing Bordetella pertussis, which then colonises the ciliated epithelial cells of the respiratory tract.
  • Progression: The bacteria produce toxins, including pertussis toxin and tracheal cytotoxin, which impair ciliary function, cause local inflammation, and lead to systemic effects such as lymphocytosis.   • Consequence: Accumulation of mucus and cellular debris in the airways, leading to paroxysmal coughing, impaired gas exchange, and potential respiratory compromise. Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Whooping cough"][/sc]
  • Early phase (Catarrhal stage): Typically lasts 1-2 weeks, characterised by non-specific upper respiratory tract symptoms such as coryza, low-grade fever, and mild cough, which are indistinguishable from a common cold.
  • Progressive phase (Paroxysmal stage): Lasts 1-6 weeks (or longer), marked by severe, uncontrollable coughing fits often followed by a characteristic inspiratory "whoop" and post-tussive vomiting. This phase is particularly dangerous for infants.
  • Late phase (Convalescent stage): Gradual resolution of symptoms over weeks to months, with coughing fits becoming less frequent and severe, though can be triggered by intercurrent respiratory infections.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Whooping cough"][/sc]
  • Ask about paroxysmal cough - typically severe, repetitive coughs in a single expiration, often leading to breathlessness and exhaustion.
  • Ask about inspiratory "whoop" - a high-pitched sound heard as the patient rapidly inhales after a coughing paroxysm, though often absent in infants and vaccinated individuals.
  • Ask about post-tussive vomiting - vomiting that occurs immediately after a coughing fit, suggesting the severity of the cough.
  • Ask about apnoea or cyanosis - particularly in infants, where coughing paroxysms may be replaced by periods of not breathing or turning blue.
  • Ask about history of contact - exposure to an individual with a prolonged cough, especially if unvaccinated or recently vaccinated.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Whooping cough"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - typically normal between paroxysms, but during a coughing fit, observe for bradycardia, desaturation, or apnoea, especially in infants.
  • Check for conjunctival haemorrhage or facial petechiae - resulting from increased intrathoracic pressure during severe coughing paroxysms.
  • Check for signs of dehydration or poor feeding - particularly in infants who may struggle to feed due to coughing and vomiting.
  • Check for evidence of respiratory distress - such as tachypnoea, intercostal recession, or nasal flaring, which may indicate severe disease or complications. Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Whooping cough"][/sc]
  • Apnoea or cyanosis: Immediate concern, particularly in infants under 6 months, indicating severe respiratory compromise requiring urgent medical intervention.
  • Severe paroxysms leading to exhaustion: Suggests significant respiratory effort and potential for respiratory failure, especially if associated with poor feeding or dehydration.
  • Signs of pneumonia: New onset fever, increased respiratory rate, crackles on auscultation, or increased work of breathing, indicating a common bacterial superinfection.
  • Neurological signs (e.g., seizures, altered consciousness): May indicate hypoxic brain injury or encephalopathy, a rare but severe complication, particularly in young infants.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Whooping cough"][/sc]
  • Infants under 3 months: May present with apnoea, cyanosis, or gagging without the classic "whoop" or post-tussive vomiting, making diagnosis challenging and increasing risk of severe outcomes.
  • Vaccinated individuals: Often experience milder symptoms, a shorter course, and may not develop the characteristic "whoop", presenting instead with a persistent cough.
  • Older children and adults: Typically present with a prolonged cough that may be paroxysmal but often lacks the "whoop", leading to delayed diagnosis and continued transmission. Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Whooping cough"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected whooping cough:
    • Nasopharyngeal swab or aspirate for PCR (polymerase chain reaction) for Bordetella pertussis – highly sensitive and specific, especially in the catarrhal and early paroxysmal stages.
    • Culture of nasopharyngeal secretions – less sensitive than PCR, but allows for antimicrobial susceptibility testing.
    • Full blood count – often reveals a significant lymphocytosis (absolute lymphocyte count >10 x 109/L) in pertussis, particularly in unvaccinated infants. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Interpreting lymphocytosis in whooping cough: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Lymphocytosis in whooping cough"][/sc]
  • Severity/risk tools: No widely validated severity/risk score for whooping cough is commonly used in UK clinical practice that warrants an MDCalc link.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Consider chest X-ray if pneumonia is suspected, or viral PCR for other respiratory pathogens.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Whooping cough"][/sc]
  • A previously healthy 4-month-old infant presents with a 2-week history of coryza and mild cough, now progressing to severe coughing paroxysms followed by an inspiratory whoop and post-tussive vomiting, with intermittent apnoea.
  • An unvaccinated infant with a progressive, paroxysmal cough and post-tussive emesis, concerning for whooping cough with potential for severe respiratory complications.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Whooping cough"][/sc]
  • Bronchiolitis - typically in younger infants, often associated with viral prodrome, wheeze, and crackles, but usually without the classic whoop or prolonged paroxysms.
  • Croup (laryngotracheobronchitis) - characterised by a barking cough, inspiratory stridor, and hoarseness, often worse at night, but without the repetitive paroxysms of pertussis.
  • Asthma exacerbation - recurrent episodes of wheeze, cough, and breathlessness, often with a history of atopy, but typically responsive to bronchodilators and lacking the whoop.
  • Foreign body aspiration - sudden onset of choking, coughing, or stridor, often with a clear history of aspiration, and can cause localised wheeze or reduced air entry.
  • Cystic fibrosis - chronic cough, recurrent respiratory infections, and failure to thrive, but usually with other systemic manifestations and positive sweat test.
  • Viral tracheitis/bronchitis - common causes of cough, but generally less severe and prolonged than whooping cough, and without the characteristic whoop or post-tussive vomiting. Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Whooping cough"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Recognition of severe disease, particularly in infants, often warrants hospital admission for close monitoring of respiratory status, oxygen saturation, and hydration. Escalation to paediatric intensive care may be required for severe apnoea or respiratory failure.
  • Definitive management: Antibiotic therapy, typically macrolides (e.g., azithromycin, clarithromycin, erythromycin), is recommended to reduce infectivity and may shorten the duration/severity of symptoms if given early in the catarrhal phase.
  • Adjunctive management: Supportive care includes maintaining hydration, nutritional support, and careful suctioning of secretions. Avoid cough suppressants as they are generally ineffective and may be harmful.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Close outpatient follow-up is often warranted for infants and those with ongoing symptoms. Public health notification and contact tracing are essential.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Advise parents on red flag symptoms (e.g., worsening apnoea, cyanosis, feeding difficulties) that necessitate immediate re-assessment.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Whooping cough"][/sc]
  • Pneumonia: The most common bacterial complication, often caused by secondary bacterial infection, leading to increased respiratory distress and prolonged hospitalisation, particularly in infants.
  • Apnoea and bradycardia: Frequent and severe in young infants, directly related to the paroxysmal coughing, leading to hypoxia and potential neurological damage if prolonged.
  • Weight loss and dehydration: Due to feeding difficulties and post-tussive vomiting, especially in infants, requiring careful monitoring and nutritional support.
  • Seizures and encephalopathy: Rare but severe neurological complications, often secondary to hypoxia or cerebral haemorrhage from intense coughing, with potential for long-term neurodevelopmental sequelae.
  • Subconjunctival haemorrhage and facial petechiae: Benign but common complications resulting from increased venous pressure during severe coughing fits.
