Phaeochromocytoma [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Phaeochromocytoma"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Phaeochromocytoma"][/sc]
  • Phaeochromocytoma is a rare neuroendocrine tumour arising from chromaffin cells, typically in the adrenal medulla, that secretes excessive catecholamines.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical presentation of phaeochromocytoma and understand the initial diagnostic approach.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Phaeochromocytoma"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Phaeochromocytoma"][/sc]
  • Phaeochromocytomas are rare, with an annual incidence of 2 to 9.1 per 1 million adults. They are responsible for 0.2–0.6% of both systolic and diastolic hypertensions.
  • Approximately 35-40% of phaeochromocytomas or paragangliomas (PPGL) are associated with germline mutations in genes such as RET (MEN2), VHL, or NF1.
  • The "rule of 10s" classically describes phaeochromocytomas: 10% are bilateral, 10% are extra-adrenal, 10% are malignant, and 10% occur in children.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Phaeochromocytoma"][/sc]
  • Trigger: Tumour cells in the adrenal medulla or extra-adrenal paraganglia autonomously or intermittently release excessive amounts of catecholamines (noradrenaline, adrenaline, dopamine).
  • Progression: High circulating catecholamine levels lead to widespread activation of adrenergic receptors, causing vasoconstriction, increased heart rate, and metabolic changes. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Catecholamine excess: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Catecholamine excess"][/sc]
  • Consequence: This results in paroxysmal or sustained hypertension, tachycardia, sweating, and metabolic disturbances, potentially leading to end-organ damage and hypertensive crises.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Phaeochromocytoma"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Symptoms are often paroxysmal, lasting minutes to hours, and may occur infrequently, varying from once a month to several times a day.
  • Progressive phase: Over time, the frequency and severity of paroxysmal episodes tend to increase, and sustained hypertension may develop.
  • Late phase: Prolonged or severe catecholamine excess can lead to chronic complications such as cardiomyopathy, renal impairment, and increased risk of cardiovascular events.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Phaeochromocytoma"][/sc]
  • Ask about headache - often severe, throbbing, and sudden onset, reflecting acute hypertension.
  • Ask about profuse sweating - typically generalised and drenching, disproportionate to environmental temperature or activity.
  • Ask about palpitations - often described as a pounding heart or tachycardia, due to direct cardiac stimulation by catecholamines.
  • Ask about anxiety or sense of doom - a common feature during paroxysmal episodes, often accompanied by pallor or flushing.
  • Ask about abdominal or flank pain - may indicate tumour size or location, or less commonly, tumour haemorrhage.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Phaeochromocytoma"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - paroxysmal or sustained hypertension is characteristic, often severe, with associated tachycardia. Postural hypotension may also be present due to volume depletion.
  • Check for pallor - due to vasoconstriction, often noticeable during an attack.
  • Check for tremor - a fine tremor can be present due to adrenergic stimulation.
  • Check for neurofibromas or café au lait spots - suggestive of Neurofibromatosis type 1, a familial syndrome associated with phaeochromocytoma. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Neurofibromatosis type 1: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Neurofibromatosis type 1"][/sc]
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Phaeochromocytoma"][/sc]
  • Hypertensive crisis: Sudden, severe elevation in blood pressure (e.g., >180/120 mmHg) with new or worsening end-organ damage (e.g., encephalopathy, myocardial ischaemia, pulmonary oedema) requires immediate management.
  • Paroxysmal symptoms: Recurrent episodes of headache, sweating, and palpitations, particularly if triggered by stress, exercise, or certain medications, are highly suggestive and warrant urgent investigation.
  • Family history: A personal or family history of early-onset phaeochromocytoma, paraganglioma, or associated genetic syndromes (e.g., MEN2, VHL, NF1) indicates a higher genetic predisposition and need for screening.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Phaeochromocytoma"][/sc]
  • Normotensive phaeochromocytoma: Some tumours, particularly those secreting predominantly dopamine or metabolising catecholamines rapidly, may present without hypertension, making diagnosis challenging.
  • Phaeochromocytoma in pregnancy: Can mimic pre-eclampsia or gestational hypertension, leading to delayed diagnosis and increased maternal and foetal mortality. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Phaeochromocytoma in pregnancy: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Phaeochromocytoma in pregnancy"][/sc]
  • Incidentaloma: Phaeochromocytomas are increasingly discovered incidentally during imaging for unrelated conditions, often before symptoms develop.
  • Phaeochromocytoma crisis: A life-threatening emergency characterised by severe hypertension, circulatory failure, and multi-organ dysfunction, often precipitated by surgery, anaesthesia, or certain drugs.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Phaeochromocytoma"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected phaeochromocytoma: 24-hour urinary fractionated metanephrines and normetanephrines, or plasma free metanephrines. These are highly sensitive and specific biochemical tests for catecholamine excess.
  • Imaging - CT/MRI: After biochemical confirmation, cross-sectional imaging (CT or MRI of the abdomen/pelvis) is used to localise the tumour. MRI is preferred for children and pregnant women, and for detecting metastatic disease. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Imaging for phaeochromocytoma: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Imaging for phaeochromocytoma"][/sc]
  • Functional imaging: If initial imaging is negative or for metastatic disease, functional imaging such as MIBG scintigraphy or PET scans (e.g., with 18F-FDOPA) may be used.
  • Genetic testing: Recommended for all patients with confirmed phaeochromocytoma due to the high prevalence of germline mutations (35-40%).
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Phaeochromocytoma"][/sc]
  • A middle-aged patient presenting with paroxysmal episodes of severe headache, profuse sweating, and palpitations, often accompanied by labile hypertension.
  • Hypertension with episodic adrenergic symptoms, concerning for phaeochromocytoma.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Phaeochromocytoma"][/sc]
  • Essential hypertension - typically lacks the classic triad of headache, sweating, and palpitations, and is usually sustained rather than paroxysmal.
  • Anxiety/panic attacks - can mimic adrenergic symptoms but usually without severe hypertension or biochemical evidence of catecholamine excess.
  • Hyperthyroidism - shares symptoms like palpitations, sweating, and tremor, but typically presents with weight loss, heat intolerance, and thyroid function abnormalities.
  • Carcinoid syndrome - causes flushing, diarrhoea, and bronchospasm, but usually without severe hypertension, and is associated with elevated serotonin metabolites.
  • Drug-induced hypertension (e.g., cocaine, amphetamines, MAOIs with tyramine) - history of substance use is key.
  • Renovascular hypertension - often presents with resistant hypertension and may have abdominal bruits, but lacks the paroxysmal adrenergic symptoms. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Renovascular hypertension: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Renovascular hypertension"][/sc]
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Phaeochromocytoma"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Stabilisation of blood pressure and heart rate, typically with alpha-blockade followed by beta-blockade, to prevent hypertensive crisis and prepare for surgery.
  • Definitive management: Surgical resection of the tumour (adrenalectomy) is the primary treatment. Pre-operative alpha-blockade (e.g., phenoxybenzamine) for 7-14 days is crucial to normalise blood pressure and expand intravascular volume, followed by beta-blockade if tachycardia persists.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Lifelong annual biochemical testing (e.g., 24-hour urinary metanephrines) is recommended post-operatively to detect recurrence or metastatic disease.
  • Exam pharmacology reference (UKMLA/MRCGP): Alpha-blockers such as [sc name="jitl-medication" Q="Phenoxybenzamine use in Phaeochromocytoma" text="phenoxybenzamine"][/sc] are used pre-operatively to control hypertension and prevent intraoperative crises. Beta-blockers (e.g., [sc name="jitl-medication" Q="Propranolol use in Phaeochromocytoma" text="propranolol"][/sc]) are added only after adequate alpha-blockade to manage tachycardia. Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Phaeochromocytoma"][/sc]
  • Hypertensive crisis: Uncontrolled catecholamine release can lead to severe, life-threatening hypertension, potentially causing stroke, myocardial infarction, or aortic dissection.
  • Cardiomyopathy: Chronic exposure to high catecholamine levels can induce a catecholamine-induced cardiomyopathy, leading to heart failure.
  • Metastatic disease: Although often benign, approximately 10% of phaeochromocytomas can be malignant, with metastases to bone, liver, lung, or lymph nodes, requiring long-term surveillance and management.
  • Post-operative hypotension: Following tumour resection, a sudden drop in catecholamine levels can lead to severe hypotension, requiring careful fluid and vasopressor management.
