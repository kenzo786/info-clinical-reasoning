Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="End stage renal disease (including renal replacement therapy and transplantation)"][/sc]
  • End stage renal disease (ESRD) is the final, irreversible stage of chronic kidney disease (CKD), characterised by a glomerular filtration rate (GFR) of less than 15 mL/min/1.73 m² or the need for renal replacement therapy (dialysis or transplantation).
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical features, complications, and management principles of end stage renal disease, including indications for and types of renal replacement therapy.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="End stage renal disease (including renal replacement therapy and transplantation)"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="End stage renal disease (including renal replacement therapy and transplantation)"][/sc]
  • In the UK, over 70,000 people are living with ESRD, with approximately 7,000 new patients starting renal replacement therapy annually (NHS Digital). The prevalence is rising due to an ageing population and increased rates of diabetes and hypertension.
  • Risk factors include diabetes mellitus (most common cause), hypertension, glomerulonephritis, polycystic kidney disease, and prolonged use of nephrotoxic medications.
  • ESRD disproportionately affects individuals from South Asian, African, and Caribbean communities in the UK, often linked to higher prevalence of diabetes and hypertension.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="End stage renal disease (including renal replacement therapy and transplantation)"][/sc]
  • Initial kidney injury (e.g., diabetic nephropathy, hypertension) leads to progressive nephron loss and compensatory hyperfiltration in remaining nephrons, increasing their workload.
  • Sustained hyperfiltration and activation of inflammatory and fibrotic pathways result in glomerulosclerosis and tubulointerstitial fibrosis, causing irreversible structural damage to the kidney.
  • Extensive nephron destruction leads to a critical reduction in renal function, manifesting as accumulation of uraemic toxins, electrolyte imbalances, fluid overload, and endocrine dysfunction, necessitating renal replacement therapy.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="End stage renal disease (including renal replacement therapy and transplantation)"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Often asymptomatic or non-specific symptoms (fatigue, nocturia) over months to years, as kidney function gradually declines from CKD stages 1-3.
  • Progressive phase: Symptoms become more pronounced (oedema, breathlessness, pruritus, nausea) as GFR falls below 30 mL/min, particularly beyond several years of initial kidney injury, indicating advanced CKD.
  • Late phase: Overt uraemic symptoms (encephalopathy, pericarditis, severe fluid overload) develop rapidly over weeks to months as GFR drops below 15 mL/min, necessitating urgent initiation of renal replacement therapy to prevent life-threatening complications.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="End stage renal disease (including renal replacement therapy and transplantation)"][/sc]
  • Ask about fatigue and weakness - often due to anaemia, uraemic toxins, and poor nutrition, significantly impacting quality of life.
  • Ask about nausea, vomiting, and anorexia - common gastrointestinal manifestations of uraemia, leading to malnutrition and weight loss.
  • Ask about pruritus (itching) - a distressing symptom often related to accumulation of uraemic toxins, secondary hyperparathyroidism, and dry skin.
  • Ask about dyspnoea and orthopnoea - indicative of fluid overload leading to pulmonary oedema, or anaemia exacerbating breathlessness.
  • Ask about peripheral oedema - suggests fluid retention due to impaired renal excretion, often affecting ankles and sacrum.
  • Ask about changes in urine output - can range from polyuria in early CKD to oliguria or anuria in advanced ESRD, reflecting severe loss of renal function.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="End stage renal disease (including renal replacement therapy and transplantation)"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - hypertension is common; tachycardia may indicate anaemia or fluid overload; tachypnoea suggests metabolic acidosis or pulmonary oedema.
  • Check for peripheral oedema - pitting oedema in the ankles, sacrum, or ascites, indicating fluid retention.
  • Check for pallor - often due to anaemia of chronic disease, a common complication of ESRD.
  • Check for uraemic frost - rare white crystalline deposits on the skin, indicating very high levels of urea.
  • Check for signs of uraemic encephalopathy - altered mental status, asterixis, myoclonus, or seizures, indicating severe neurological toxicity from uraemic toxins. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Uraemic encephalopathy: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Uraemic encephalopathy"][/sc]
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="End stage renal disease (including renal replacement therapy and transplantation)"][/sc]
  • Acute pulmonary oedema - sudden onset severe breathlessness, orthopnoea, and pink frothy sputum, indicating critical fluid overload requiring immediate intervention.
  • Hyperkalaemia with ECG changes - tall tented T waves, widened QRS, or absent P waves, signifying a life-threatening electrolyte imbalance that can lead to cardiac arrest.
  • Uraemic pericarditis - pleuritic chest pain, pericardial rub, or signs of tamponade, indicating severe uraemia affecting the heart, often requiring urgent dialysis.
  • Severe metabolic acidosis - Kussmaul breathing and profound acid-base disturbance, which can lead to cardiovascular collapse if uncorrected.
  • Altered mental status or seizures - new onset or worsening neurological symptoms, suggesting uraemic encephalopathy requiring urgent renal replacement therapy.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="End stage renal disease (including renal replacement therapy and transplantation)"][/sc]
  • Older adults: May present with non-specific symptoms like falls, confusion, or functional decline, rather than classic uraemic symptoms, making diagnosis challenging.
  • Diabetic patients: Often have less severe fluid overload due to osmotic diuresis from hyperglycaemia, but may have more pronounced neuropathy and cardiovascular complications.
  • Immunosuppressed patients (post-transplant): Presentation of kidney dysfunction may be masked by immunosuppressants, and they are at higher risk of opportunistic infections or rejection, which can mimic ESRD symptoms.
  • Patients on peritoneal dialysis: May present with peritonitis (abdominal pain, cloudy dialysate) as a complication, which can lead to treatment failure and need for haemodialysis.
