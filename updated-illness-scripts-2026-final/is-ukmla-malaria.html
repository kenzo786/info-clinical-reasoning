Malaria (Infections) [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Malaria (Infections)"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Malaria (Infections)"][/sc]
  • Malaria is a protozoal infection transmitted by the bite of infected female Anopheles mosquitoes, caused by species of the Plasmodium parasite. It is characterised by fever, rigors, and sweats, and can progress to a severe, life-threatening systemic illness, particularly when caused by Plasmodium falciparum.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: The candidate must be able to recognise the clinical features of malaria in a returning traveller, initiate appropriate investigations, and understand the principles of management, including the need for urgent treatment of falciparum malaria and the recognition of severe complications.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Malaria (Infections)"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Malaria"][/sc]
  • In the UK, malaria is a travel-associated infection. Between 2012 and 2021, an average of 1,425 cases were reported annually in the UK, the majority of which were acquired in Africa. P. falciparum is the most common species imported into the UK.
  • Risk factors for acquiring malaria include travel to endemic areas (particularly sub-Saharan Africa, Southeast Asia, and South America), failure to use or adhere to appropriate chemoprophylaxis, and visiting friends and relatives in countries of origin.
  • Vulnerable groups for severe disease include young children, pregnant women, the elderly, and immunosuppressed individuals.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Malaria (Infections)"][/sc]
  • Trigger: An infected female Anopheles mosquito injects Plasmodium sporozoites into the human bloodstream during a blood meal. The sporozoites travel to the liver and infect hepatocytes.
  • Progression: Within the liver, the parasites mature into merozoites (exo-erythrocytic schizogony), which are then released into the bloodstream. In the case of P. vivax and P. ovale, a dormant liver stage ([sc name="jitl-query" Q="Hypnozoites: what are they and why do they matter clinically?" text="hypnozoites"][/sc]) can persist and cause relapsing illness months or years later.
  • Consequence: Merozoites invade red blood cells, where they multiply (erythrocytic schizogony). The cyclical rupture of infected red blood cells releases more merozoites and parasite toxins, causing the classic symptoms of fever, chills, and sweats. Sequestration of infected red cells in the microvasculature of vital organs, particularly in P. falciparum infection, leads to organ dysfunction and severe malaria.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Malaria (Infections)"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Following an incubation period of 7-30 days, a non-specific prodrome of headache, myalgia, and fatigue may occur. This is followed by the onset of fever, which may initially be irregular.
  • Progressive phase: The classic cyclical fever pattern may become established, with paroxysms of chills, high fever, and sweats occurring every 48 hours (P. falciparum, P. vivax, P. ovale) or 72 hours (P. malariae). In P. falciparum infection, rapid progression to severe malaria can occur, particularly beyond 24-48 hours of symptom onset.
  • Late phase: Without effective treatment, particularly in P. falciparum malaria, complications such as cerebral malaria, acute kidney injury, and acute respiratory distress syndrome can develop. In non-falciparum malaria, relapses can occur weeks to months after the primary infection if dormant liver stages are not treated.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Malaria (Infections)"][/sc]
  • Ask about fever, chills, and sweats - this classic triad is highly suggestive of malaria, especially if cyclical.
  • Ask about travel history - a detailed travel history, including dates, destinations, and use of prophylaxis, is the most critical piece of information in a patient with suspected malaria.
  • Ask about headache, myalgia, and fatigue - these non-specific symptoms are common in the prodromal and early stages of malaria.
  • Ask about gastrointestinal symptoms - nausea, vomiting, and diarrhoea can occur and may mimic gastroenteritis.
  • Ask about neurological symptoms - confusion, drowsiness, or seizures are red flags for severe falciparum malaria ([sc name="jitl-query" Q="Cerebral malaria: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="cerebral malaria"][/sc]).
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Malaria (Infections)"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - fever is the most common sign. Tachycardia and hypotension may indicate haemodynamic compromise and severe disease.
  • Check for jaundice - may be present due to haemolysis and liver dysfunction.
  • Check for hepatosplenomegaly - may be palpable on abdominal examination, reflecting parasitic sequestration and immune response.
  • Check neurological status - a reduced level of consciousness, seizures, or focal neurological deficits are signs of cerebral malaria.
  • Check for pallor - may indicate anaemia resulting from haemolysis.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Malaria (Infections)"][/sc]
  • Impaired consciousness or seizures - suggests cerebral malaria, a medical emergency.
  • Respiratory distress or pulmonary oedema - indicates acute respiratory distress syndrome (ARDS), a feature of severe malaria.
  • Oliguria or anuria - suggests acute kidney injury, another complication of severe malaria.
  • Spontaneous bleeding or coagulopathy - may indicate disseminated intravascular coagulation (DIC).
  • Prostration or profound weakness - a generalised sign of severe illness.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Malaria (Infections)"][/sc]
  • Pregnancy: Pregnant women are more susceptible to severe malaria and its complications, including hypoglycaemia and pulmonary oedema. Malaria in pregnancy also increases the risk of maternal anaemia, stillbirth, and low birth weight.
  • Children: Young children may present with non-specific symptoms such as irritability, poor feeding, and vomiting. Seizures are more common in children with cerebral malaria.
  • Older adults: The presentation may be less typical, with a lower-grade fever or a more insidious onset. Co-morbidities can complicate the clinical picture.
  • Non-immune travellers: Travellers with no prior exposure to malaria are at high risk of severe disease and may present with a rapid and fulminant course.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Malaria (Infections)"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected malaria:
    • Thick and thin blood films: The gold standard for diagnosis, allowing for species identification and quantification of parasitaemia.
    • Malaria rapid diagnostic test (RDT): A point-of-care test that detects parasite antigens. A negative RDT does not exclude malaria, and blood films should always be performed.
    • Full blood count: May show anaemia, thrombocytopenia, and a normal or low white cell count.
    • Urea and electrolytes: To assess for acute kidney injury.
    • Liver function tests: May show raised bilirubin and transaminases.
  • Severity/risk tools: Clinical assessment for features of severe malaria is paramount. There is no widely used scoring system for risk stratification in the UK, but the WHO criteria for severe malaria are used to guide management.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible:
    • Blood cultures: To exclude bacterial sepsis.
    • Chest X-ray: If respiratory symptoms are present, to look for pneumonia or ARDS.
    • Lumbar puncture: If there are signs of meningitis or encephalitis.
  • Serial reassessment: In patients with a high index of suspicion but negative initial tests, blood films should be repeated every 12-24 hours for 48 hours.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Malaria (Infections)"][/sc]
  • A 35-year-old male presents with a 3-day history of cyclical high-grade fevers, rigors, and sweats, associated with a severe headache and myalgia. He returned from a 2-week trip to Ghana 10 days ago, where he did not take regular antimalarial prophylaxis. On examination, he is febrile, jaundiced, and has a palpable spleen.
  • Febrile illness in a returning traveller from a malaria-endemic area, with features of haemolysis and splenomegaly, concerning for falciparum malaria.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Malaria (Infections)"][/sc]
  • Typhoid fever: Presents with fever, headache, and abdominal pain, but the fever is typically sustained rather than cyclical, and a relative bradycardia may be present.
  • Dengue fever: Characterised by high fever, severe myalgia ("break-bone fever"), headache, and often a rash. Thrombocytopenia is common, but cyclical fevers are not a feature.
  • Influenza: Presents with fever, cough, headache, and myalgia, but is not typically associated with a travel history to a malaria-endemic area or cyclical fevers.
  • Viral haemorrhagic fever (e.g., Ebola, Lassa): A rare but important differential in travellers from specific regions, presenting with fever, headache, and myalgia, which can progress to haemorrhage and shock.
  • Leptospirosis: Presents with fever, headache, myalgia, and conjunctival suffusion, often associated with exposure to contaminated water or animals.
  • Meningitis/Encephalitis: Can present with fever, headache, and altered mental status, but neck stiffness and photophobia are more prominent features.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Malaria (Infections)"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Prompt recognition and assessment for features of severe malaria. All patients with suspected or confirmed falciparum malaria should be admitted to hospital. Urgent discussion with a local infectious diseases or microbiology specialist is essential.
  • Definitive management: The choice of antimalarial therapy depends on the infecting species, the severity of the illness, and local parasite resistance patterns. Uncomplicated falciparum malaria is typically treated with an artemisinin-based combination therapy (ACT), such as artemether with lumefantrine. Severe falciparum malaria requires intravenous therapy, with intravenous artesunate being the treatment of choice.
  • Adjunctive management: Supportive care is crucial in severe malaria and may include fluid and electrolyte management, correction of hypoglycaemia, and management of seizures.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Patients with severe malaria require intensive monitoring of their clinical and laboratory parameters. For P. vivax and P. ovale infections, treatment to eradicate the dormant liver stage (radical cure) with primaquine is required after the acute illness has been treated.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Any patient with uncomplicated malaria who deteriorates or fails to respond to treatment should be reassessed for severe malaria or an alternative diagnosis.
  • Exam pharmacology reference (UKMLA/MRCGP): Knowledge of the main classes of antimalarial drugs is expected. This includes [sc name="jitl-medication" Q="Artemisinin-based combination therapy (ACT) use in Malaria" text="artemisinin-based combination therapies (ACTs)"][/sc] for uncomplicated falciparum malaria, [sc name="jitl-medication" Q="Intravenous artesunate use in Malaria" text="intravenous artesunate"][/sc] for severe malaria, and [sc name="jitl-medication" Q="Primaquine use in Malaria" text="primaquine"][/sc] for radical cure of vivax/ovale malaria. No doses or frequencies are cited here — always verify against your local antimicrobial/prescribing formulary before prescribing.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Malaria (Infections)"][/sc]
  • [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Cerebral malaria: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Cerebral malaria"][/sc]: A diffuse encephalopathy caused by sequestration of infected red blood cells in cerebral microvasculature, leading to coma, seizures, and a high mortality rate.
  • Acute kidney injury: Can result from a combination of factors including dehydration, haemolysis, and sequestration of infected red cells in the renal microvasculature.
  • Acute respiratory distress syndrome (ARDS): A form of non-cardiogenic pulmonary oedema caused by an inflammatory response in the lungs, leading to severe respiratory distress.
  • Severe anaemia: Results from the destruction of red blood cells by the parasite and bone marrow suppression.
  • [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Blackwater fever: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Blackwater fever"][/sc]: A syndrome of massive intravascular haemolysis leading to haemoglobinuria (dark urine), acute kidney injury, and shock. It is a rare but serious complication of falciparum malaria.
  • Hypoglycaemia: Can occur due to increased glucose consumption by the parasites and impaired gluconeogenesis. It can be exacerbated by quinine treatment.
