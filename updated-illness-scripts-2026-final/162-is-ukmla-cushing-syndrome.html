Cushing syndrome [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Cushing syndrome"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Cushing syndrome"][/sc]
  • Cushing syndrome is a clinical state resulting from prolonged exposure to excessive glucocorticoids, either endogenous (due to overproduction by the adrenal glands or ectopic ACTH production) or exogenous (iatrogenic from corticosteroid medication).
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical features of Cushing syndrome, differentiate between its common causes, and outline the initial diagnostic approach including screening tests.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Cushing syndrome"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Cushing syndrome"][/sc]
  • Endogenous Cushing syndrome is rare, with an estimated annual incidence of 0.7 to 2.4 cases per million population in the UK.
  • Iatrogenic Cushing syndrome, caused by exogenous corticosteroid use, is the most common form and is dose-dependent.
  • Cushing disease (pituitary-dependent Cushing syndrome) accounts for approximately 70% of endogenous cases, with a female-to-male ratio of 3-5:1.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Cushing syndrome"][/sc]
  • Trigger: Chronic excess of glucocorticoids, either from exogenous administration or endogenous overproduction due to pituitary ACTH hypersecretion (Cushing disease), ectopic ACTH production (e.g., small cell lung cancer), or primary adrenal tumours (adenoma, carcinoma).
  • Progression: Sustained high cortisol levels disrupt normal physiological processes, leading to catabolic effects on protein and bone, altered glucose and lipid metabolism, immunosuppression, and mineralocorticoid effects.
  • Consequence: Multi-systemic dysfunction manifests as characteristic clinical features, including central obesity, muscle weakness, skin fragility, hypertension, diabetes mellitus, and increased susceptibility to infection. Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Cushing syndrome"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Subtle changes such as weight gain, fatigue, and mood alterations may develop insidiously over months to a year, often attributed to other causes.
  • Progressive phase: Characteristic features like central obesity, facial plethora, striae, and muscle weakness become more apparent, typically evolving over 1-3 years.
  • Late phase: Untreated, severe hypercortisolism leads to significant comorbidities including uncontrolled hypertension, diabetes, osteoporosis with fractures, and increased risk of cardiovascular events and infections, particularly beyond several years.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Cushing syndrome"][/sc]
  • Ask about weight gain - particularly central obesity with thin limbs, as this pattern is highly suggestive of cortisol excess.
  • Ask about easy bruising and skin fragility - indicating impaired collagen synthesis due to catabolic effects of cortisol.
  • Ask about muscle weakness - especially proximal muscle weakness, making activities like climbing stairs difficult.
  • Ask about mood changes - including depression, anxiety, irritability, or cognitive dysfunction, which are common neuropsychiatric manifestations.
  • Ask about menstrual irregularities or hirsutism in women - due to androgenic effects of cortisol precursors or co-secretion from adrenal tumours.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Cushing syndrome"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - hypertension is a common finding, and blood pressure should be carefully assessed.
  • Check for facial plethora and moon facies - characteristic facial changes due to fat redistribution and capillary fragility.
  • Check for violaceous striae - purple or reddish-purple stretch marks, typically wider than 1 cm, found on the abdomen, thighs, breasts, and arms, indicating dermal atrophy.
  • Check for buffalo hump - supraclavicular and dorsocervical fat pads, another sign of fat redistribution.
  • Check for proximal myopathy - assess muscle strength, particularly in the shoulder and hip girdles, which is a direct catabolic effect of cortisol. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Proximal myopathy in Cushing syndrome: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Proximal myopathy"][/sc]
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Cushing syndrome"][/sc]
  • Rapid onset of symptoms: Suggests an aggressive underlying cause, such as ectopic ACTH production or adrenal carcinoma, warranting urgent investigation.
  • Severe hypertension or uncontrolled diabetes: Indicates significant metabolic derangement requiring prompt intervention to prevent end-organ damage.
  • Hypokalaemia: Particularly in the context of hypertension, raises suspicion for ectopic ACTH production or primary adrenal hyperplasia, necessitating immediate electrolyte correction and further workup.
  • Progressive neurological symptoms: May indicate a large pituitary adenoma with mass effect or metastatic disease from an ectopic source.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Cushing syndrome"][/sc]
  • Paediatric population: Often presents with growth retardation and obesity, rather than the classic adult features, making diagnosis challenging.
  • Older adults: Symptoms may be non-specific and attributed to ageing, such as fatigue, weakness, and cognitive changes, leading to delayed diagnosis.
  • Cyclical Cushing syndrome: Intermittent periods of hypercortisolism and normal cortisol levels, making biochemical diagnosis difficult and requiring repeated testing. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Cyclical Cushing syndrome: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Cyclical Cushing syndrome"][/sc]
  • Subclinical Cushing syndrome: Biochemical evidence of hypercortisolism without overt clinical signs, often discovered incidentally, but still associated with increased morbidity.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Cushing syndrome"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected Cushing syndrome: Initial screening tests include 24-hour urinary free cortisol (UFC), overnight 1 mg dexamethasone suppression test (ONDST), or late-night salivary cortisol.
  • Further investigations: If screening tests are abnormal, further tests to confirm hypercortisolism and determine the aetiology include low-dose and high-dose dexamethasone suppression tests, plasma ACTH levels, and imaging (pituitary MRI, adrenal CT). [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Dexamethasone suppression test interpretation: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Dexamethasone suppression test interpretation"][/sc]

  • Imaging - Pituitary MRI: To identify pituitary adenomas in suspected Cushing disease.
  • Imaging - Adrenal CT: To identify adrenal adenomas or hyperplasia in ACTH-independent Cushing syndrome.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Cushing syndrome"][/sc]
  • A 45-year-old female presents with progressive central obesity, facial plethora, easy bruising, and proximal muscle weakness over the past year, with a history of hypertension and newly diagnosed diabetes.
  • Middle-aged female with insidious onset of classic hypercortisolism features and metabolic complications, concerning for endogenous Cushing syndrome.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Cushing syndrome"][/sc]
  • Metabolic syndrome: Shares features like central obesity, hypertension, and diabetes, but typically lacks the skin fragility, proximal myopathy, and specific facial changes of Cushing syndrome.
  • Polycystic ovary syndrome (PCOS): Can present with hirsutism and menstrual irregularities, but usually without the profound catabolic features or characteristic fat redistribution of Cushing syndrome.
  • Exogenous corticosteroid use: The most common cause of Cushingoid features; a careful medication history is crucial to differentiate from endogenous causes.
  • Depression: Severe depression can mimic some symptoms of Cushing syndrome, such as weight changes and mood disturbances, but biochemical tests for hypercortisolism are typically normal.
  • Alcohol excess: Chronic heavy alcohol consumption can lead to pseudo-Cushing syndrome, with elevated cortisol levels and some physical features, but reversible with abstinence. Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Cushing syndrome"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Confirm diagnosis, identify underlying cause, and manage acute complications such as severe hypertension or hyperglycaemia.
  • Definitive management: Surgical removal of the tumour (pituitary adenoma, adrenal adenoma) is the primary treatment. For iatrogenic Cushing syndrome, gradual withdrawal of exogenous corticosteroids is crucial.
  • Adjunctive management: Pharmacological agents (e.g., metyrapone, ketoconazole, pasireotide) may be used to control hypercortisolism pre-operatively, in cases of surgical failure, or when surgery is contraindicated.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Long-term monitoring for recurrence, adrenal insufficiency, and management of comorbidities (e.g., osteoporosis, diabetes, hypertension) is essential.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Patients with severe or rapidly progressive symptoms, or those with suspected ectopic ACTH syndrome, often warrant urgent referral to a specialist endocrine centre.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Cushing syndrome"][/sc]
  • Cardiovascular disease: Chronic hypertension, dyslipidaemia, and insulin resistance significantly increase the risk of myocardial infarction and stroke, particularly beyond several years of untreated disease.
  • Diabetes mellitus: Persistent hypercortisolism leads to severe insulin resistance and impaired glucose tolerance, often progressing to type 2 diabetes.
  • Osteoporosis and fractures: Cortisol's catabolic effects on bone lead to reduced bone mineral density, increasing the risk of vertebral and other pathological fractures.
  • Increased susceptibility to infection: Immunosuppression makes patients vulnerable to bacterial, fungal, and viral infections, which can be severe and life-threatening.
  • Psychological disturbances: Untreated Cushing syndrome can lead to severe depression, anxiety, cognitive impairment, and even psychosis, impacting quality of life significantly.
  • Adrenal crisis: Can occur post-operatively if cortisol replacement is inadequate, or if exogenous steroids are withdrawn too rapidly, presenting as hypotension, hypoglycaemia, and shock.
