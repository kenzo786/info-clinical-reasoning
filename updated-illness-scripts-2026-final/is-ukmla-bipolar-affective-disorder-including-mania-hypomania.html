Bipolar affective disorder (including mania/ hypomania) [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Bipolar affective disorder (including mania/ hypomania)"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Bipolar affective disorder (including mania/ hypomania)"][/sc]
  • Bipolar disorder is a mental health condition characterised by extreme mood changes, including periods of high mood (mania or hypomania) and low mood (depression) [1].
  • UKMLA learning outcome: The candidate must recognise the clinical features, diagnostic criteria, and management principles of bipolar affective disorder, including its manic and hypomanic presentations.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Bipolar affective disorder (including mania/ hypomania)"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Bipolar affective disorder (including mania/ hypomania)"][/sc]
  • The lifetime prevalence of bipolar disorder in the UK is estimated to be around 1-2%, with Bipolar I disorder affecting approximately 1% of the population [2].
  • Onset typically occurs in late adolescence or early adulthood, with a mean age of onset between 18 and 25 years.
  • Risk factors include a strong genetic predisposition, with first-degree relatives having a significantly increased risk, and environmental factors such as childhood trauma or stressful life events.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Bipolar affective disorder (including mania/ hypomania)"][/sc]
  • Trigger: Neurobiological dysregulation involving neurotransmitter systems (e.g., dopamine, serotonin, noradrenaline), leading to imbalances in mood regulation.
  • Progression: Structural and functional brain changes, including abnormalities in areas associated with emotion processing (e.g., prefrontal cortex, amygdala), contributing to mood instability.
  • Consequence: Recurrent episodes of mania/hypomania and depression, often with progressive neurocognitive deficits and functional impairment over time.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Bipolar affective disorder (including mania/ hypomania)"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Initial episodes of depression or mania/hypomania, often presenting in late adolescence or early adulthood, which may be misdiagnosed as unipolar depression.
  • Progressive phase: Increasing frequency and severity of mood episodes, potentially leading to rapid cycling (four or more episodes in a year), particularly beyond several years without effective treatment.
  • Late phase: Chronic and recurrent course with potential for residual symptoms, cognitive impairment, and increased risk of complications if not consistently managed.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Bipolar affective disorder (including mania/ hypomania)"][/sc]
  • Ask about elevated mood or irritability - this is central to diagnosing mania or hypomania.
  • Ask about increased energy and activity - a key feature distinguishing manic/hypomanic episodes from other conditions.
  • Ask about decreased need for sleep - often reported as feeling rested after only a few hours, indicating a physiological shift.
  • Ask about racing thoughts or flight of ideas - important for assessing thought disorder and the intensity of the manic state.
  • Ask about impulsivity or engaging in risky behaviours - helps identify functional impairment and potential harm during manic episodes.
  • Ask about persistent sadness or anhedonia - crucial for identifying depressive episodes within the bipolar spectrum.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Bipolar affective disorder (including mania/ hypomania)"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - particularly heart rate and blood pressure, as agitation or medication side effects can influence these.
  • Check psychomotor agitation or retardation - observed as restlessness, pressured speech, or slowed movements, reflecting mood state.
  • Check dishevelled appearance or inappropriate dress - may indicate impaired self-care during severe mood episodes.
  • Check distractibility and poor concentration - evident during conversation, suggesting cognitive difficulties in mania or depression.
  • Check grandiosity or delusional thinking - can be elicited through mental state examination, indicating psychotic features in severe mania.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Bipolar affective disorder (including mania/ hypomania)"][/sc]
  • Psychotic features: Presence of hallucinations or delusions during manic or depressive episodes, indicating severe illness and requiring urgent psychiatric review.
  • Severe functional impairment: Inability to maintain personal safety, employment, or social relationships due to mood symptoms, necessitating immediate intervention.
  • Suicidal ideation or intent: Any expression of self-harm or suicidal thoughts, particularly during depressive or mixed episodes, requires urgent risk assessment and safety planning.
  • Rapid cycling: Four or more mood episodes within a 12-month period, which can be challenging to treat and may indicate a more severe course.
