Generalised anxiety disorder [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Generalised anxiety disorder"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Generalised anxiety disorder"][/sc]
  • Generalised anxiety disorder (GAD) is characterised by persistent, excessive, and uncontrollable worry about a range of everyday events and activities, disproportionate to actual risk, lasting for at least six months [1].
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the features of generalised anxiety disorder, distinguish it from other anxiety disorders, and outline initial management strategies including psychological and pharmacological interventions.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Generalised anxiety disorder"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Generalised anxiety disorder"][/sc]
  • In any given week in England, approximately 6 in 100 people are diagnosed with Generalised Anxiety Disorder [2].
  • The lifetime prevalence of GAD in Europe is estimated to be between 1–7% [3].
  • GAD is more common in women than men, with recent UK data showing 37.1% of women and 29.9% of men reporting high levels of anxiety in 2022/23 [2].
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Generalised anxiety disorder"][/sc]
  • Trigger: Genetic predisposition, adverse life events, and chronic stress can lead to dysregulation of neurotransmitter systems, particularly gamma-aminobutyric acid (GABA), serotonin, and noradrenaline [1].
  • Progression: This dysregulation contributes to heightened amygdala activity and impaired prefrontal cortex function, leading to exaggerated fear responses and difficulty in modulating worry [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Amygdala function in anxiety: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="amygdala activity"][/sc].
  • Consequence: Persistent activation of the hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal (HPA) axis results in chronic physiological arousal, somatic symptoms, and cognitive biases that perpetuate the cycle of anxiety and worry.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Generalised anxiety disorder"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Symptoms often develop gradually, with individuals experiencing increasing periods of worry and associated physical symptoms over several months.
  • Progressive phase: Without intervention, the worry becomes more pervasive and difficult to control, impacting daily functioning and potentially leading to avoidance behaviours and social withdrawal.
  • Late phase: GAD can become a chronic, fluctuating condition, with periods of exacerbation during stressful life events, often persisting for many years if untreated.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Generalised anxiety disorder"][/sc]
  • Ask about excessive, uncontrollable worry - this is the core feature and must be present for at least six months, distinguishing it from transient anxiety [1].
  • Ask about restlessness or feeling on edge - these are common somatic manifestations of chronic anxiety and can impact concentration.
  • Ask about fatigue - persistent worry is mentally exhausting, leading to a sense of being tired easily.
  • Ask about difficulty concentrating or mind going blank - chronic worry consumes cognitive resources, impairing focus and memory.
  • Ask about irritability - sustained anxiety can lower frustration tolerance and lead to increased emotional reactivity.
  • Ask about muscle tension - a frequent physical symptom, often presenting as headaches, neck, or back pain, reflecting physiological arousal.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Generalised anxiety disorder"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - typically normal, but may show mild tachycardia or elevated blood pressure due to autonomic arousal, especially during acute anxiety [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Autonomic arousal in anxiety: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="autonomic arousal"][/sc].
  • Check for signs of psychomotor agitation or restlessness - fidgeting, pacing, or difficulty sitting still can indicate internal tension.
  • Check for signs of muscle tension - palpation may reveal taut muscles, particularly in the neck, shoulders, and jaw.
  • Check for signs of fatigue or poor self-care - chronic anxiety can lead to sleep disturbance and neglect of personal hygiene.
  • Check for signs of hypervigilance - an exaggerated startle response or excessive scanning of the environment for threats.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Generalised anxiety disorder"][/sc]
  • Suicidal ideation or intent: Any mention of self-harm or suicide requires immediate and thorough risk assessment and appropriate safeguarding measures.
  • New onset anxiety in older adults: May indicate an underlying organic cause, such as thyroid dysfunction, cardiac disease, or medication side effects, requiring comprehensive medical evaluation.
  • Psychotic symptoms: Hallucinations, delusions, or disorganised thought processes suggest a primary psychotic disorder or severe mood disorder with psychotic features, necessitating urgent psychiatric assessment.
  • Severe functional impairment: Inability to maintain self-care, employment, or social relationships due to anxiety symptoms, indicating a need for more intensive and specialist mental health intervention.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Generalised anxiety disorder"][/sc]
  • Older adults: May present predominantly with somatic complaints (e.g., chronic pain, gastrointestinal issues) rather than overt psychological worry, making diagnosis challenging [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Somatic symptoms in older adults with anxiety: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="somatic complaints"][/sc].
  • Children and adolescents: Worry may manifest as excessive concern about school performance, social acceptance, or future events, often accompanied by physical symptoms like stomach aches or headaches.
  • Individuals with chronic physical illness: Anxiety symptoms may be attributed solely to the physical condition, leading to under-recognition and under-treatment of co-morbid GAD.
  • Men: May express anxiety as irritability, anger, or substance misuse, rather than traditional worry, potentially delaying diagnosis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Generalised anxiety disorder"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected GAD: Clinical interview focusing on the nature, duration, and impact of worry, alongside a comprehensive mental state examination.
  • Severity/risk tools: GAD-7 (Generalised Anxiety Disorder 7-item scale) is a widely used and validated self-report questionnaire for screening and assessing severity of GAD. A score of 10 or more suggests GAD [sc name="jitl-query" Q="GAD-7 scoring and interpretation: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="GAD-7"][/sc]. Consider using GAD-7 on MDCalc.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Thyroid function tests (TFTs) to exclude hyperthyroidism, full blood count (FBC) and inflammatory markers to rule out anaemia or systemic illness, and basic metabolic panel to assess electrolyte imbalances.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Generalised anxiety disorder"][/sc]
  • A 35-year-old female presents with six months of persistent, excessive, and uncontrollable worry about various aspects of her life, accompanied by fatigue, irritability, and muscle tension, significantly impacting her work and social functioning.
  • Middle-aged female with chronic, pervasive anxiety and somatic symptoms, concerning for Generalised Anxiety Disorder.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Generalised anxiety disorder"][/sc]
  • Panic Disorder: Characterised by recurrent, unexpected panic attacks, often with intense physical symptoms, whereas GAD involves persistent, free-floating worry without discrete attacks [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Distinguishing GAD from Panic Disorder: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="distinguishing from panic disorder"][/sc].
  • Social Anxiety Disorder: Fear and anxiety specifically related to social situations, contrasting with the broad, non-situational worry of GAD.
  • Health Anxiety (Illness Anxiety Disorder): Preoccupation with having or acquiring a serious illness, despite medical reassurance, distinct from general health worries in GAD.
  • Obsessive-Compulsive Disorder (OCD): Presence of obsessions (recurrent, intrusive thoughts) and compulsions (repetitive behaviours) aimed at reducing distress, which are not core features of GAD.
  • Depressive Disorder: Significant overlap in symptoms (e.g., fatigue, poor concentration), but in depression, low mood and anhedonia are primary, while anxiety is secondary; in GAD, anxiety is primary.
  • Hyperthyroidism: Can mimic anxiety symptoms (e.g., palpitations, tremor, irritability), but is distinguished by specific biochemical markers and other physical signs.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Generalised anxiety disorder"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Assess for suicidal ideation and ensure safety. Provide psychoeducation about GAD and treatment options.
  • Definitive management: Stepped-care approach, typically starting with low-intensity psychological interventions (e.g., guided self-help, psychoeducational groups based on CBT principles) [1]. If symptoms persist or are more severe, high-intensity psychological interventions (e.g., individual CBT, applied relaxation) or pharmacological treatments are considered [1].
  • Pharmacological management: Selective serotonin reuptake inhibitors (SSRIs) or serotonin-noradrenaline reuptake inhibitors (SNRIs) are often first-line pharmacological options [1].
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Regular review of symptoms, functional impairment, and treatment adherence. Monitor for side effects of medication.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: If symptoms do not improve after two interventions, referral to specialist mental health services may be warranted [1].

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Generalised anxiety disorder"][/sc]
  • Depression: GAD frequently co-occurs with major depressive disorder, exacerbating symptom burden and functional impairment, and worsening prognosis.
  • Substance misuse: Individuals may use alcohol or illicit drugs as a maladaptive coping mechanism for anxiety, leading to dependence and further health complications.
  • Functional impairment: Chronic, uncontrollable worry significantly interferes with daily activities, work productivity, social relationships, and overall quality of life.
  • Physical health problems: Persistent physiological arousal associated with GAD can contribute to or exacerbate conditions such as irritable bowel syndrome, headaches, and cardiovascular issues.
  • Suicide risk: While not as high as in major depression, GAD is associated with an increased risk of suicidal ideation and attempts, particularly when co-morbid with other mental health conditions.
