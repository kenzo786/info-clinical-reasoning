Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Neonatal jaundice (including pathological and physiological jaundice)"][/sc]
  • Neonatal jaundice is the yellow discolouration of the skin and sclera in a newborn, caused by elevated bilirubin levels in the blood (hyperbilirubinaemia). It is common, affecting up to 60% of term and 80% of preterm infants in the first week of life.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: The candidate must recognise the causes, clinical features, and management of physiological and pathological neonatal jaundice, including the identification of red flag features indicating severe hyperbilirubinaemia.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Neonatal jaundice (including pathological and physiological jaundice)"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Neonatal jaundice (including pathological and physiological jaundice)"][/sc]
  • Approximately 60% of term and 80% of preterm babies develop jaundice in the first week of life, with most cases being physiological and benign.
  • Risk factors for severe hyperbilirubinaemia include prematurity (less than 38 weeks' gestation), exclusive breastfeeding (if not feeding well), previous sibling with neonatal jaundice requiring phototherapy, visible jaundice in the first 24 hours of life, and haemolytic disease (e.g., ABO or Rh incompatibility).
  • In the UK, NICE guidelines recommend routine assessment for jaundice in all newborns, with specific thresholds for intervention based on age and bilirubin levels.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Neonatal jaundice (including pathological and physiological jaundice)"][/sc]
  • Trigger: Increased bilirubin production from the breakdown of foetal red blood cells, coupled with immature hepatic conjugation and excretion mechanisms in the newborn.
  • Progression: Unconjugated bilirubin accumulates in the bloodstream, exceeding the binding capacity of albumin, leading to its deposition in tissues, particularly the skin and sclera, causing visible jaundice.
  • Consequence: In severe cases, unconjugated bilirubin can cross the blood-brain barrier, leading to neurotoxicity, a condition known as kernicterus, which can result in permanent neurological damage. Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Neonatal jaundice (including pathological and physiological jaundice)"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Physiological jaundice typically appears after 24 hours of age, peaking at 3-5 days and resolving within 10-14 days. Jaundice appearing within the first 24 hours is always pathological.
  • Progressive phase: Pathological jaundice may persist or worsen beyond the typical physiological timeframe, particularly beyond 14 days in term infants or 21 days in preterm infants, often indicating an underlying cause.
  • Late phase: Prolonged jaundice, especially if associated with pale stools or dark urine, suggests cholestasis and requires urgent investigation to prevent long-term liver damage.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Neonatal jaundice (including pathological and physiological jaundice)"][/sc]
  • Ask about feeding difficulties - poor feeding can exacerbate jaundice by reducing bilirubin excretion and increasing enterohepatic recirculation.
  • Ask about lethargy or excessive sleepiness - these can be early signs of significant hyperbilirubinaemia affecting the central nervous system.
  • Ask about changes in stool colour - pale, chalky stools suggest cholestasis, indicating a conjugated hyperbilirubinaemia.
  • Ask about changes in urine colour - dark urine can also indicate conjugated hyperbilirubinaemia.
  • Ask about irritability or high-pitched crying - these are concerning neurological symptoms that may indicate acute bilirubin encephalopathy.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Neonatal jaundice (including pathological and physiological jaundice)"][/sc]
  • Check for yellow discolouration of the skin and sclera - typically progresses cephalocaudally (head to toe) with increasing bilirubin levels.
  • Check vital signs - fever, tachypnoea, or poor perfusion may indicate sepsis, a potential cause of pathological jaundice.
  • Check for hepatosplenomegaly - may suggest haemolytic disease, congenital infection, or cholestatic liver disease.
  • Check for neurological signs such as hypotonia, arching of the back (opisthotonus), or seizures - these are critical indicators of acute bilirubin encephalopathy. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Opisthotonus: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="opisthotonus"][/sc]
  • Check for petechiae or purpura - may indicate congenital infection or severe haemolysis.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Neonatal jaundice (including pathological and physiological jaundice)"][/sc]
  • Jaundice in the first 24 hours of life - always pathological and requires urgent investigation for haemolysis or other serious causes.
  • Pale stools and/or dark urine - suggests conjugated hyperbilirubinaemia and potential cholestatic liver disease, necessitating prompt referral.
  • Rapid rise in bilirubin levels - indicates ongoing haemolysis or impaired excretion, increasing the risk of kernicterus.
  • Signs of acute bilirubin encephalopathy - lethargy, poor feeding, hypotonia, high-pitched cry, arching, or seizures demand immediate intervention.
  • Prolonged jaundice - persisting beyond 14 days in term infants or 21 days in preterm infants, warrants investigation for underlying pathology.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Neonatal jaundice (including pathological and physiological jaundice)"][/sc]
  • Preterm infants: Jaundice is more common, more severe, and prolonged due to greater red blood cell turnover and more immature liver function, increasing their vulnerability to kernicterus at lower bilirubin levels.
  • Breastfed infants: May experience 'breast milk jaundice' which can persist for several weeks, or 'breastfeeding jaundice' due to inadequate milk intake, both typically benign but requiring careful monitoring.
  • Infants with G6PD deficiency: Can develop severe, rapid-onset hyperbilirubinaemia, often requiring aggressive treatment, as their red blood cells are more susceptible to oxidative stress and haemolysis.
  • Infants with congenital hypothyroidism: Jaundice may be prolonged due to reduced bilirubin conjugation and excretion, highlighting the importance of newborn screening.
