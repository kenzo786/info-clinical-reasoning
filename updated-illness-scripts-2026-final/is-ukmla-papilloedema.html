Papilloedema [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Papilloedema"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Papilloedema"][/sc]
  • Papilloedema is swelling of the optic disc due to raised intracranial pressure (ICP), typically bilateral but can be asymmetrical or unilateral.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical features and implications of papilloedema as a sign of raised intracranial pressure.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Papilloedema"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Papilloedema"][/sc]
  • The incidence of idiopathic intracranial hypertension (IIH), a common cause of papilloedema, is estimated at 1-3 per 100,000 in the UK, predominantly affecting obese women of childbearing age.
  • Risk factors for papilloedema include conditions causing raised ICP such as cerebral tumours, intracranial haemorrhage, meningitis, hydrocephalus, and venous sinus thrombosis.
  • Certain medications, including tetracyclines, retinoids, and growth hormone, are recognised to induce papilloedema.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Papilloedema"][/sc]
  • Raised intracranial pressure (ICP) is transmitted to the optic nerve sheath, impeding axoplasmic flow within the optic nerve.
  • This impaired flow leads to swelling of the optic nerve head (optic disc) and accumulation of fluid, causing the characteristic oedema.
  • Sustained papilloedema can result in optic nerve atrophy and irreversible vision loss due to chronic compression and ischaemia of the nerve fibres.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Papilloedema"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Symptoms such as transient visual obscurations or headache may develop over days to weeks, often correlating with the initial rise in ICP.
  • Progressive phase: Visual field defects, particularly enlargement of the blind spot, and worsening headache typically evolve over weeks to months, particularly beyond 4-6 weeks if the underlying cause of raised ICP is not addressed.
  • Late phase: Chronic papilloedema, if untreated, can lead to progressive optic atrophy and permanent severe vision loss over several months to years.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Papilloedema"][/sc]
  • Ask about headache - often diffuse, worse in the morning or with Valsalva manoeuvres, indicating raised ICP.
  • Ask about transient visual obscurations - brief, episodic dimming or loss of vision, typically lasting seconds, suggesting optic nerve ischaemia due to pressure.
  • Ask about diplopia - often horizontal, due to sixth cranial nerve palsy caused by stretching of the nerve from raised ICP.
  • Ask about nausea and vomiting - non-specific symptoms of raised ICP, which may be severe.
  • Ask about pulsatile tinnitus - a whooshing sound synchronous with the heartbeat, often associated with idiopathic intracranial hypertension.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Papilloedema"][/sc]
  • Check visual acuity - may be normal in early stages, but can decline with chronic papilloedema.
  • Check visual fields - typically reveals an enlarged blind spot initially, progressing to peripheral field constriction.
  • Check fundoscopy - reveals blurring of optic disc margins, loss of optic cup, venous engorgement, retinal haemorrhages, and Paton's lines (radial retinal folds). [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Paton's lines: what are they and why do they matter clinically?" text="Paton's lines"][/sc]
  • Check pupillary reflexes - usually normal, but an afferent pupillary defect may develop in severe, asymmetrical cases.
  • Check vital signs - particularly blood pressure, as hypertension can be a cause or consequence of some conditions leading to raised ICP.
