Hypopituitarism [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Hypopituitarism"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Hypopituitarism"][/sc]
  • Hypopituitarism is a clinical syndrome resulting from deficiency of one or more pituitary hormones, often due to damage to the pituitary gland or hypothalamus.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: recognise the clinical features and common causes of hypopituitarism, and understand the principles of its investigation and management.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Hypopituitarism"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Hypopituitarism"][/sc]
  • The prevalence of hypopituitarism in the UK is estimated to be around 30-45 cases per 100,000 population, with an incidence of 4-5 new cases per 100,000 per year.
  • Risk factors often include pituitary tumours (e.g., adenomas), cranial irradiation, head trauma, and infiltrative diseases such as sarcoidosis or haemochromatosis.
  • Postpartum haemorrhage leading to Sheehan's syndrome is a classic, though now less common, cause of hypopituitarism.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Hypopituitarism"][/sc]
  • Damage to the pituitary gland or hypothalamus, often from a mass lesion or ischaemia, leads to impaired production or release of one or more pituitary hormones.
  • The sequence of hormone loss typically follows a pattern, with growth hormone and gonadotrophins often affected first, followed by TSH and ACTH, though this can vary.
  • Consequent deficiency of target gland hormones (e.g., cortisol, thyroid hormones, sex steroids) results in a diverse range of clinical manifestations affecting multiple organ systems. Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Hypopituitarism"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Symptoms may be insidious in onset, developing over months to years, particularly beyond initial pituitary insult, with non-specific features like fatigue and weakness.
  • Progressive phase: As more hormones become deficient, or the deficiency becomes more profound, specific symptoms related to target organ failure emerge, such as menstrual irregularities, cold intolerance, or postural hypotension.
  • Late phase: If untreated, severe deficiencies, particularly of ACTH, can lead to life-threatening adrenal crisis, particularly beyond periods of physiological stress or illness.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Hypopituitarism"][/sc]
  • Ask about fatigue and lethargy - these are common, non-specific symptoms that can indicate multiple hormone deficiencies.
  • Ask about cold intolerance and weight gain - suggestive of thyroid-stimulating hormone (TSH) deficiency leading to secondary hypothyroidism.
  • Ask about menstrual irregularities, reduced libido, or erectile dysfunction - indicating gonadotrophin deficiency.
  • Ask about postural dizziness and weakness - may point towards adrenocorticotrophic hormone (ACTH) deficiency and secondary adrenal insufficiency.
  • Ask about headaches and visual field defects - these can be symptoms of a pituitary mass lesion, which is a common cause of hypopituitarism.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Hypopituitarism"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - often reveal hypotension, particularly postural, and bradycardia, reflecting adrenal and thyroid insufficiency respectively.
  • Check skin and hair - pallor, fine wrinkling around the eyes, loss of axillary and pubic hair, and dry skin can be observed due to various hormone deficiencies.
  • Check visual fields - bitemporal hemianopia may be present if a pituitary tumour is compressing the optic chiasm.
  • Check neurological examination - may reveal signs of raised intracranial pressure or cranial nerve palsies if a large pituitary mass is present. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Bitemporal hemianopia: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Bitemporal hemianopia"][/sc]
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Hypopituitarism"][/sc]
  • Adrenal crisis - acute deterioration with severe hypotension, vomiting, abdominal pain, and altered mental status, requiring immediate glucocorticoid replacement.
  • Severe hyponatraemia - can indicate profound ACTH deficiency or syndrome of inappropriate antidiuretic hormone (SIADH) in some cases, leading to neurological compromise.
  • Rapidly worsening visual field defects - suggests acute expansion of a pituitary lesion, such as pituitary apoplexy, necessitating urgent neurosurgical review.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Hypopituitarism"][/sc]
  • Older adults: Symptoms may be attributed to normal ageing, leading to delayed diagnosis; cognitive changes and frailty may be more prominent.
  • Pregnancy: Can unmask or worsen pre-existing hypopituitarism, particularly Sheehan's syndrome post-partum, with failure of lactation and amenorrhoea.
  • Immunosuppressed patients: May have atypical or exaggerated responses to stress, increasing the risk of adrenal crisis due to impaired cortisol reserve. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Sheehan's syndrome: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Sheehan's syndrome"][/sc]
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Hypopituitarism"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected hypopituitarism: Baseline pituitary hormone profile (e.g., morning cortisol, TSH, free T4, LH, FSH, prolactin, IGF-1) and dynamic tests (e.g., short synacthen test for ACTH reserve, insulin tolerance test for GH and ACTH).
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Consider tests for other endocrine disorders or systemic illnesses that can mimic hypopituitarism.
  • Imaging - MRI brain with pituitary views: Essential to identify structural abnormalities such as pituitary adenomas, craniopharyngiomas, or infiltrative lesions.
  • Serial reassessment: Regular monitoring of hormone levels and clinical status is crucial, especially during hormone replacement therapy adjustments.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Hypopituitarism"][/sc]
  • A 45-year-old female presents with a 6-month history of increasing fatigue, cold intolerance, weight gain, and irregular periods, following a history of cranial irradiation for a brain tumour 5 years prior.
  • A middle-aged female with chronic, progressive fatigue and multiple endocrine deficiencies, in the context of a history of pituitary insult, often warrants investigation for hypopituitarism.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Hypopituitarism"][/sc]
  • Primary hypothyroidism - distinguished by elevated TSH with low free T4, unlike secondary hypothyroidism where TSH is low or inappropriately normal.
  • Primary adrenal insufficiency (Addison's disease) - characterised by hyperpigmentation and elevated ACTH, absent in secondary adrenal insufficiency.
  • Depression - can mimic fatigue and low mood, but typically lacks specific endocrine signs and biochemical abnormalities.
  • Chronic fatigue syndrome - a diagnosis of exclusion, often considered after ruling out organic causes like hypopituitarism.
  • Anaemia - can cause fatigue and pallor, but a full blood count will differentiate; may coexist with hypopituitarism. Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Hypopituitarism"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: In cases of suspected adrenal crisis, administer intravenous hydrocortisone and fluid resuscitation.
  • Definitive management: Hormone replacement therapy tailored to the specific deficiencies, typically including hydrocortisone for ACTH deficiency, levothyroxine for TSH deficiency, and sex steroids for gonadotrophin deficiency. Growth hormone replacement may be considered in adults with severe deficiency.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Regular clinical assessment and biochemical monitoring of hormone levels to ensure adequate replacement and avoid over-replacement.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Educate patients on sick day rules for glucocorticoid replacement and symptoms of adrenal crisis, advising immediate medical attention if these occur.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Hypopituitarism"][/sc]
  • Adrenal crisis: Due to inadequate cortisol replacement, particularly during periods of stress or illness, leading to life-threatening cardiovascular collapse.
  • Hypothyroidism complications: Untreated or undertreated TSH deficiency can lead to myxoedema coma, cardiovascular issues, and cognitive impairment.
  • Gonadal dysfunction complications: Infertility, osteoporosis, and psychological distress due to untreated sex hormone deficiencies.
  • Cardiovascular disease: Long-term hypopituitarism, especially growth hormone deficiency, is associated with an increased risk of cardiovascular morbidity and mortality.
