Leukaemia (Blood and lymph) [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Leukaemia"]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Leukaemia"]
  • Leukaemia is a heterogeneous group of haematological malignancies characterised by the uncontrolled proliferation of abnormal leukocytes in the bone marrow and peripheral blood.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the varied presentations of acute and chronic leukaemias, differentiate between key subtypes (ALL, AML, CLL, CML), and understand the principles of initial investigation and referral for suspected cases.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Leukaemia"]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Leukaemia"]
  • Leukaemia accounts for 3% of all new cancer cases in the UK, with approximately 10,400 new cases diagnosed annually (2017-2019) [1].
  • Acute lymphoblastic leukaemia (ALL) is the most common childhood cancer, with around 790 new diagnoses annually in the UK, accounting for less than 1% of all new cancer cases [2] [3]. Incidence rates are highest in children aged 0-4 years [2].
  • Acute myeloid leukaemia (AML) is the most common acute leukaemia in adults, with almost 3,100 people diagnosed in the UK each year. 42% of cases are in those aged 75 or over [4] [5].
  • Chronic lymphocytic leukaemia (CLL) is the most common leukaemia overall in adults, with over 4,720 diagnoses annually in the UK [6] [7].
  • Chronic myeloid leukaemia (CML) is a rarer type, with around 850 new cases diagnosed annually in the UK [8] [9].
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Leukaemia"]
  • Leukaemia arises from genetic mutations in haematopoietic stem cells or progenitor cells within the bone marrow, leading to uncontrolled proliferation and impaired differentiation of abnormal leukocytes.
  • In acute leukaemias (ALL, AML), this results in the rapid accumulation of immature blast cells that crowd out normal haematopoiesis, causing bone marrow failure. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Blast cells: what are they and why do they matter clinically?" text="Blast cells"][/sc]
  • Chronic leukaemias (CLL, CML) involve the proliferation of more mature, but still dysfunctional, leukocytes. Chronic myeloid leukaemia (CML) is characterised by the Philadelphia chromosome (t(9;22)), which creates the BCR-ABL1 fusion gene, encoding a constitutively active tyrosine kinase that drives uncontrolled cell growth. Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Leukaemia"]
  • Early phase: Acute leukaemias typically present with a rapid onset of symptoms over days to weeks due to bone marrow failure. Chronic leukaemias often have an insidious onset, with patients being asymptomatic for months to years.
  • Progressive phase: Without treatment, acute leukaemias rapidly progress, leading to severe complications within weeks. Chronic leukaemias may remain stable for prolonged periods but can progress to an accelerated phase or blast crisis (in CML) over months to years.
  • Late phase: Advanced disease in both acute and chronic leukaemias is characterised by severe bone marrow failure, extramedullary involvement, and resistance to therapy, leading to life-threatening infections, haemorrhage, and organ dysfunction.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Leukaemia"]
  • Ask about fatigue and weakness - often profound and progressive, indicating anaemia due to bone marrow suppression.
  • Ask about recurrent infections or prolonged fever - suggests neutropenia or dysfunctional white blood cells, common in acute leukaemias.
  • Ask about easy bruising or bleeding (e.g., epistaxis, menorrhagia, petechiae) - points to thrombocytopenia due to impaired platelet production.
  • Ask about bone or joint pain - particularly in children with ALL, due to leukaemic infiltration of the bone marrow or periosteum.
  • Ask about unexplained weight loss and night sweats - constitutional symptoms that can be present in both acute and chronic leukaemias, particularly CML.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Leukaemia"]
  • Check vital signs - fever may indicate infection; tachycardia and tachypnoea can suggest severe anaemia or sepsis.
  • Check for pallor - a key sign of anaemia, often prominent in acute leukaemias.
  • Check for petechiae, purpura, or ecchymoses - evidence of thrombocytopenia and increased bleeding tendency.
  • Check for lymphadenopathy (cervical, axillary, inguinal) - common in ALL and CLL due to lymphoid infiltration.
  • Check for hepatosplenomegaly - often present in chronic leukaemias (especially CML) and advanced acute leukaemias due to extramedullary haematopoiesis or infiltration. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Hepatosplenomegaly in leukaemia: what does it indicate?" text="Hepatosplenomegaly"][/sc]
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Leukaemia"]
  • Persistent unexplained fatigue and pallor: Suggests significant anaemia due to bone marrow failure, particularly concerning if developing rapidly over days to weeks.
  • Recurrent or severe infections: Indicates profound neutropenia or immune dysfunction, necessitating urgent haematological assessment.
  • Unexplained bruising, petechiae, or bleeding: Signifies severe thrombocytopenia, increasing risk of spontaneous haemorrhage, especially intracranial.
  • Unexplained bone pain, especially in children: May indicate leukaemic infiltration of bone marrow, requiring urgent investigation for ALL.
  • Rapidly enlarging lymph nodes or splenomegaly: Suggests aggressive disease progression or high tumour burden, particularly if accompanied by constitutional symptoms.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Leukaemia"]
  • Older adults: May present with non-specific symptoms such as general malaise, weight loss, or recurrent infections, often delaying diagnosis. AML is more common in this age group.
  • Children: While ALL is common, presentations can be vague, including limping, irritability, or refusal to walk, mimicking musculoskeletal conditions.
  • Isolated cytopenias: Leukaemia can initially present with only one or two cell lines affected (e.g., isolated anaemia or thrombocytopenia), without overt blast cells in peripheral blood.
  • Extramedullary disease: Presentation with symptoms related to leukaemic infiltration of organs other than bone marrow, such as skin lesions (leukaemia cutis), gingival hypertrophy (AML), or central nervous system involvement.
  • CML with isolated thrombocytosis: Rarely, CML can present with a very high platelet count without significant leukocytosis, making diagnosis challenging. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Isolated thrombocytosis in CML: why does it matter clinically?" text="Isolated thrombocytosis"][/sc]
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Leukaemia"]
  • Core for suspected leukaemia:
    • Full blood count (FBC) with differential: Typically reveals anaemia, thrombocytopenia, and abnormal white cell counts (leukocytosis, leukopenia, or presence of blast cells).
    • Peripheral blood film: Essential for morphological assessment of blood cells and identification of blast cells or abnormal lymphocytes.
    • Bone marrow aspiration and trephine biopsy: Confirms diagnosis, determines leukaemia subtype, assesses blast percentage, and provides material for cytogenetics, immunophenotyping, and molecular studies.
    • Immunophenotyping (flow cytometry): Identifies cell surface markers to classify leukaemia subtype (e.g., B-ALL, T-ALL, AML subtypes, CLL).
    • Cytogenetics and molecular genetics (e.g., FISH, PCR): Detects chromosomal abnormalities (e.g., Philadelphia chromosome t(9;22) in CML and some ALL) and gene mutations (e.g., BCR-ABL1, FLT3, NPM1) crucial for diagnosis, prognosis, and targeted therapy. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Cytogenetics in leukaemia: why does it matter clinically?" text="Cytogenetics"][/sc]
  • Severity/risk tools:
    • International Prognostic Index for Chronic Lymphocytic Leukaemia (CLL-IPI): CLL-IPI on MDCalc (categorises patients into risk groups based on clinical and biological factors).
    • Sokal Index for Chronic Myelogenous Leukaemia (CML): Sokal Index on MDCalc (predicts prognosis at CML diagnosis).
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible:
    • Infection screens (e.g., viral serology, blood cultures): To rule out severe infections mimicking leukaemia symptoms.
    • Autoimmune screen: To exclude autoimmune conditions causing cytopenias.
  • Imaging - CT scan (chest, abdomen, pelvis): To assess for lymphadenopathy, hepatosplenomegaly, or extramedullary disease.
  • Serial reassessment: Regular FBCs and clinical review are essential for monitoring disease progression, treatment response, and detecting relapse, particularly in chronic leukaemias.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Leukaemia"]
  • A child presenting with persistent fatigue, unexplained bruising, and bone pain, or an older adult with insidious onset of malaise, recurrent infections, and hepatosplenomegaly.
  • A haematological malignancy characterised by uncontrolled proliferation of abnormal leukocytes, leading to bone marrow failure and systemic symptoms, concerning for leukaemia.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Leukaemia"]
  • Infectious mononucleosis: Can cause lymphadenopathy, fatigue, and atypical lymphocytes, but usually self-limiting and associated with EBV infection.
  • Aplastic anaemia: Presents with pancytopenia and fatigue, but bone marrow is hypocellular without blast infiltration.
  • Myelodysplastic syndromes (MDS): Characterised by ineffective haematopoiesis and cytopenias, with dysplastic features in bone marrow; can transform into AML.
  • Idiopathic thrombocytopenic purpura (ITP): Isolated thrombocytopenia with bleeding, but other cell lines are normal and bone marrow shows increased megakaryocytes.
  • Juvenile idiopathic arthritis (JIA): Can present with bone pain and fever in children, mimicking ALL, but typically lacks significant cytopenias or blast cells.
  • Lymphoma: Often presents with lymphadenopathy and constitutional symptoms, but typically involves solid lymphoid masses rather than diffuse bone marrow infiltration. Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Leukaemia"]
  • Immediate priorities: Recognition and urgent referral to a haematology centre. Supportive care often includes blood product transfusions, infection prophylaxis/treatment, and management of tumour lysis syndrome.
  • Definitive management:
    • Acute Leukaemias (ALL, AML): Intensive chemotherapy regimens, often followed by allogeneic stem cell transplantation for eligible patients.
    • Chronic Lymphocytic Leukaemia (CLL): Watch and wait for asymptomatic, low-risk disease. Treatment for symptomatic or high-risk disease often includes chemotherapy (e.g., fludarabine, cyclophosphamide, rituximab), targeted therapies (e.g., ibrutinib, venetoclax).
    • Chronic Myeloid Leukaemia (CML): Tyrosine kinase inhibitors (TKIs) such as imatinib are the cornerstone of treatment, targeting the BCR-ABL1 fusion protein. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Tyrosine kinase inhibitors in CML: how do they work?" text="Tyrosine Kinase Inhibitors"][/sc]
  • Adjunctive management: Central nervous system prophylaxis (especially in ALL), psychological support, and fertility preservation counselling.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Regular blood tests, bone marrow examinations, and molecular monitoring (e.g., BCR-ABL1 levels in CML) to assess treatment response and detect minimal residual disease.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Patients should be advised on symptoms requiring urgent medical attention (e.g., fever, uncontrolled bleeding).
  • Exam pharmacology reference (UKMLA/MRCGP):
    • Chemotherapy agents (e.g., cytarabine, daunorubicin, vincristine, methotrexate, asparaginase) for acute leukaemias.
    • Targeted therapies: [sc name="jitl-medication" Q="Imatinib use in CML" text="Imatinib"][/sc] (CML), [sc name="jitl-medication" Q="Rituximab use in CLL" text="Rituximab"][/sc] (CLL), [sc name="jitl-medication" Q="Venetoclax use in CLL" text="Venetoclax"][/sc] (CLL).

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Leukaemia"]
  • Severe infection: Profound neutropenia, particularly in acute leukaemias, significantly increases susceptibility to life-threatening bacterial, fungal, and viral infections, often presenting rapidly over days.
  • Haemorrhage: Severe thrombocytopenia, common in acute leukaemias, leads to increased risk of spontaneous bleeding, including intracranial haemorrhage, which can develop acutely.
  • Leukostasis: In acute leukaemias with extremely high blast counts, increased blood viscosity can lead to impaired microcirculation, causing respiratory distress, neurological symptoms, and priapism, requiring urgent intervention.
  • Tumour lysis syndrome: Rapid breakdown of leukaemic cells, especially during initial chemotherapy, can release intracellular contents, leading to hyperkalaemia, hyperphosphataemia, hypocalcaemia, and acute kidney injury, typically occurring within days of treatment initiation.
  • Blast crisis (in CML): The transformation of chronic myeloid leukaemia from its chronic phase to an aggressive acute leukaemia-like state, characterised by an increase in blast cells in the bone marrow or peripheral blood, often over months to years, leading to rapid clinical deterioration.
  • Extramedullary disease: Leukaemic infiltration of organs such as the central nervous system, skin, or testes can lead to specific organ dysfunction and is a sign of advanced or relapsed disease.
