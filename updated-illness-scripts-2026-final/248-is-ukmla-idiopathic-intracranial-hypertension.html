Idiopathic intracranial hypertension [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Idiopathic intracranial hypertension"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Idiopathic intracranial hypertension"][/sc]
  • Idiopathic intracranial hypertension (IIH) is a condition characterised by raised intracranial pressure (ICP) in the absence of an intracranial mass, hydrocephalus, or other identifiable cause, leading to symptoms such as headache and visual disturbances [1, 2].
  • UKMLA learning outcome: The candidate must recognise the clinical presentation, diagnostic approach, and initial management of idiopathic intracranial hypertension.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Idiopathic intracranial hypertension"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Idiopathic intracranial hypertension"][/sc]
  • The prevalence of IIH in the UK was reported as 10.9 per 100,000 in the general population, rising to 85.7 per 100,000 in obese women [3].
  • Incidence rates have shown a significant increase, with 7.8 per 100,000 per year in 2017, largely attributed to the rising prevalence of obesity [4].
  • Risk factors primarily include obesity, female gender (particularly women of childbearing age), and certain medications such as tetracyclines, retinoids, and growth hormone [5].
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Idiopathic intracranial hypertension"][/sc]
  • The precise trigger for IIH is unknown, but it is hypothesised to involve dysregulation of cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) dynamics, leading to impaired CSF absorption or increased CSF production [6].
  • This dysregulation results in a progressive increase in intracranial pressure, which is transmitted to the optic nerve sheaths and other intracranial structures [7].
  • The consequence is papilloedema, visual field defects, and persistent headaches, with potential for permanent vision loss if untreated [8].
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Idiopathic intracranial hypertension"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Symptoms such as headache and transient visual obscurations often develop insidiously over weeks to months, reflecting a gradual rise in intracranial pressure.
  • Progressive phase: Without intervention, papilloedema can worsen, leading to progressive visual field constriction and potentially permanent vision loss, particularly beyond several weeks of sustained elevated ICP.
  • Late phase: Chronic, unremitting headaches and severe visual impairment can become established, necessitating long-term management strategies.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Idiopathic intracranial hypertension"][/sc]
  • Ask about headache - typically diffuse, pulsatile, worse with straining, and often resistant to usual analgesia, indicating raised ICP.
  • Ask about transient visual obscurations - brief episodes of blurred or dimmed vision, often precipitated by changes in posture, suggesting intermittent optic nerve dysfunction due to pressure.
  • Ask about pulsatile tinnitus - a whooshing sound in one or both ears, synchronous with the heartbeat, caused by turbulent blood flow in venous sinuses due to altered pressure gradients.
  • Ask about visual loss - progressive blurring, reduced peripheral vision, or diplopia, indicating optic nerve compromise.
  • Ask about neck and shoulder pain - often associated with headache, possibly due to traction on pain-sensitive intracranial structures.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Idiopathic intracranial hypertension"][/sc]
  • Check visual acuity and visual fields - to detect any impairment, particularly peripheral field loss, which is a critical indicator of optic nerve damage.
  • Check fundoscopy - for papilloedema, which is swelling of the optic disc due to raised ICP, a hallmark sign of IIH. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Papilloedema: what are its grades and clinical significance?" text="Papilloedema"][/sc]
  • Check ocular motility - for abducens nerve (VI) palsy, which can cause diplopia and is a non-localising sign of raised ICP.
  • Check vital signs - blood pressure and body mass index (BMI) are crucial, as obesity is a major risk factor and hypertension can coexist.
  • Check neurological examination - to exclude focal neurological deficits that would suggest an alternative cause for raised ICP.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Idiopathic intracranial hypertension"][/sc]
  • Sudden severe headache: May indicate an acute rise in ICP or an alternative, more sinister intracranial pathology requiring urgent investigation.
  • Rapidly progressive visual loss: Suggests acute optic nerve compromise and necessitates immediate intervention to prevent permanent blindness.
  • Focal neurological deficits: Such as weakness or sensory loss, which are not typical of IIH and warrant investigation for structural brain lesions.
  • Altered consciousness: A rare but serious sign of severely elevated ICP, requiring emergency management.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Idiopathic intracranial hypertension"][/sc]
  • Men and non-obese individuals: IIH is less common in these groups, and their presentation should prompt a more thorough search for secondary causes of intracranial hypertension.
  • Children: Presentation can be more non-specific, with irritability, lethargy, and vomiting, making diagnosis challenging.
  • Older adults: Headaches may be less prominent, and visual symptoms might be attributed to other age-related conditions, delaying diagnosis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Idiopathic intracranial hypertension"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected IIH: Ophthalmoscopy to confirm papilloedema, followed by neuroimaging (MRI brain with venography) to exclude secondary causes of raised ICP and confirm normal brain parenchyma and venous sinus patency.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Further targeted imaging or blood tests based on suspected underlying conditions (e.g., thyroid function tests, autoimmune screens).
  • Imaging - MRI brain with venography: Essential to rule out structural lesions, hydrocephalus, and cerebral venous sinus thrombosis, which can mimic IIH.
  • Lumbar puncture: Performed after neuroimaging has excluded a mass lesion, to measure opening pressure (typically >25 cmH2O in adults) and analyse CSF composition (normal in IIH).
  • Serial reassessment: Regular ophthalmology reviews with visual field testing and fundoscopy are crucial to monitor disease progression and treatment response.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Idiopathic intracranial hypertension"][/sc]
  • A 32-year-old obese female presents with a 3-month history of daily, pulsatile headaches, transient visual obscurations, and pulsatile tinnitus, with fundoscopy revealing bilateral papilloedema.
  • This is an obese, young female with chronic, progressive symptoms of raised intracranial pressure, specifically headache, transient visual obscurations, and pulsatile tinnitus, objectively confirmed by papilloedema.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Idiopathic intracranial hypertension"][/sc]
  • Cerebral venous sinus thrombosis: Presents with similar symptoms of raised ICP but is caused by thrombosis within the venous sinuses, identifiable on MR venography.
  • Brain tumour: A space-occupying lesion can cause raised ICP and focal neurological deficits, excluded by MRI brain.
  • Hydrocephalus: Impaired CSF flow or absorption leading to ventricular enlargement and raised ICP, diagnosed on MRI.
  • Meningitis/Encephalitis: Inflammatory conditions of the brain and meninges causing headache, fever, and altered mental status, with CSF abnormalities.
  • Medication-induced intracranial hypertension: Certain drugs (e.g., tetracyclines, retinoids) can cause raised ICP, requiring careful drug history.
  • Secondary intracranial hypertension: Due to systemic conditions like renal failure, anaemia, or endocrine disorders.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Idiopathic intracranial hypertension"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Protect vision through prompt diagnosis and initiation of ICP-lowering therapies. Manage severe headaches symptomatically.
  • Definitive management: Weight loss is the cornerstone of long-term management. Pharmacological options include diuretics (e.g., acetazolamide) to reduce CSF production.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Regular ophthalmological assessment (visual acuity, fields, fundoscopy) and neurological review to monitor treatment efficacy and disease progression.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: If medical management fails or vision deteriorates, surgical intervention (e.g., CSF shunting, optic nerve sheath fenestration) may be considered. Patients should be advised to seek urgent medical attention for worsening vision or new neurological symptoms.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Idiopathic intracranial hypertension"][/sc]
  • Permanent visual loss: The most feared complication, resulting from chronic papilloedema and optic nerve atrophy, leading to irreversible blindness.
  • Chronic daily headache: Persistent and debilitating headaches that significantly impair quality of life, often refractory to standard treatments.
  • Recurrence: IIH can recur, particularly if weight loss is not maintained, necessitating ongoing monitoring and management.
  • Treatment-related complications: Side effects from medications (e.g., acetazolamide) or complications from surgical interventions (e.g., shunt malfunction, infection).
