Peritonitis [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Peritonitis"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Peritonitis"][/sc]
  • Peritonitis is the inflammation of the peritoneum, the serous membrane lining the abdominal cavity and covering the abdominal organs, often due to bacterial infection or chemical irritation.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: To recognise the clinical features of peritonitis, differentiate between primary and secondary causes, and understand the immediate management principles, including the need for urgent surgical assessment in suspected secondary peritonitis.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Peritonitis"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Peritonitis"][/sc]
  • Spontaneous bacterial peritonitis (SBP) is a common infection in patients with cirrhosis and ascites, with an estimated incidence of 3.5% at 1 year in outpatients with decompensated cirrhosis and 7-30% in inpatients [1].
  • Peritoneal dialysis-associated peritonitis rates in the UK range from 0.38 to 0.45 episodes per patient-year [2, 3].
  • Secondary peritonitis, often caused by perforated viscus, appendicitis, or diverticulitis, is a significant cause of surgical emergencies and morbidity globally [4].
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Peritonitis"][/sc]
  • Initial insult: Peritonitis typically begins with either bacterial contamination (e.g., gut perforation, SBP) or chemical irritation (e.g., sterile bile, pancreatic enzymes) of the peritoneal cavity.
  • Inflammatory cascade: This insult triggers a rapid inflammatory response within the peritoneum, leading to increased vascular permeability, fluid shifts into the peritoneal space, and recruitment of immune cells. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Peritoneal inflammation: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Peritoneal inflammation"][/sc]
  • Systemic consequences: Uncontrolled local inflammation can progress to systemic inflammatory response syndrome (SIRS), sepsis, and multi-organ dysfunction, particularly if the underlying cause is not addressed.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Peritonitis"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Symptoms such as acute abdominal pain, nausea, and vomiting typically develop rapidly, often within hours of the peritoneal insult.
  • Progressive phase: Over several hours to a day, the pain usually intensifies and becomes more generalised, accompanied by systemic signs of infection like fever, tachycardia, and hypotension.
  • Late phase: If untreated, peritonitis can lead to severe sepsis, septic shock, and multi-organ failure, particularly beyond 24-48 hours, necessitating urgent intervention.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Peritonitis"][/sc]
  • Ask about acute abdominal pain - typically severe, constant, and worsened by movement or coughing, indicating peritoneal irritation.
  • Ask about nausea and vomiting - common non-specific symptoms that can accompany severe abdominal pathology.
  • Ask about fever and chills - suggestive of systemic infection and inflammation.
  • Ask about changes in bowel habits - constipation or absent bowel movements may indicate ileus secondary to inflammation.
  • Ask about abdominal distension - may indicate accumulation of fluid or gas in the peritoneal cavity.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Peritonitis"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - tachycardia, hypotension, and fever are common indicators of systemic inflammatory response or sepsis.
  • Check abdominal tenderness - typically diffuse and severe, often with rebound tenderness and guarding, indicating peritoneal irritation. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Rebound tenderness: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Rebound tenderness"][/sc]
  • Check abdominal rigidity - involuntary board-like rigidity of the abdominal wall is a classic sign of generalised peritonitis, particularly in secondary peritonitis.
  • Check bowel sounds - often diminished or absent due to paralytic ileus.
  • Check for signs of underlying cause - e.g., surgical scars, ascites (in SBP), or signs of organomegaly.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Peritonitis"][/sc]
  • Board-like rigidity: Suggests widespread peritoneal inflammation, often indicative of a perforated viscus requiring urgent surgical intervention.
  • Haemodynamic instability: Hypotension and tachycardia may signify septic shock, necessitating immediate resuscitation and source control.
  • Rapidly worsening abdominal pain: Indicates progression of peritoneal inflammation or development of complications, requiring prompt reassessment.
  • Absent bowel sounds with distension: Suggests paralytic ileus, which can lead to bowel obstruction and further compromise.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Peritonitis"][/sc]
  • Older adults: May present with less pronounced pain, minimal fever, or subtle signs of peritonitis, leading to delayed diagnosis.
  • Immunosuppressed patients: (e.g., on corticosteroids, chemotherapy, HIV) can have blunted inflammatory responses, masking typical signs and symptoms of peritonitis.
  • Patients on peritoneal dialysis: Peritonitis may present with cloudy dialysate fluid, abdominal pain, and fever, but signs of peritoneal irritation can be less obvious. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Peritoneal dialysis peritonitis: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Peritoneal dialysis peritonitis"][/sc]
  • Spontaneous bacterial peritonitis (SBP): Patients with cirrhosis and ascites may have minimal abdominal pain or fever, presenting instead with altered mental status or worsening liver function.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Peritonitis"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected peritonitis: Full blood count (leukocytosis), C-reactive protein (elevated), urea and electrolytes (renal function, dehydration), liver function tests (underlying liver disease), blood cultures (sepsis).
  • Severity/risk tools: The APACHE II score or SOFA score can be used to assess severity in critically ill patients with peritonitis. APACHE II Score on MDCalc. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="APACHE II score: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="APACHE II score"][/sc]
  • Ascitic fluid analysis (for SBP): Cell count (neutrophil count >250 cells/mm³ is diagnostic), culture, protein, and glucose.
  • Imaging - CT abdomen/pelvis: Often the imaging modality of choice to identify the underlying cause of secondary peritonitis (e.g., perforation, abscess, appendicitis) and assess the extent of inflammation.
  • Imaging - Ultrasound abdomen: Can be useful for detecting free fluid, abscesses, or guiding paracentesis, particularly in SBP.
  • Serial reassessment: Clinical examination and repeat inflammatory markers are crucial to monitor response to treatment and detect deterioration.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Peritonitis"][/sc]
  • A 68-year-old male with a history of alcohol-related cirrhosis presents with acute onset, generalised abdominal pain, fever, and new-onset confusion, with examination revealing diffuse abdominal tenderness and reduced bowel sounds.
  • An elderly cirrhotic male with acute abdominal pain and altered mental status, concerning for spontaneous bacterial peritonitis.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Peritonitis"][/sc]
  • Acute appendicitis - localised right iliac fossa pain, typically migrating from periumbilical region, with less generalised peritoneal signs initially.
  • Acute diverticulitis - localised left iliac fossa pain, often with a history of diverticular disease, but can cause generalised peritonitis if perforated.
  • Acute pancreatitis - severe epigastric pain radiating to the back, often associated with elevated amylase/lipase, and less prominent peritoneal signs unless severe.
  • Bowel obstruction - colicky abdominal pain, vomiting, abdominal distension, and altered bowel sounds, but usually without signs of peritoneal irritation unless complicated by ischaemia or perforation. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Bowel obstruction: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Bowel obstruction"][/sc]
  • Ectopic pregnancy (in females) - sudden, severe unilateral lower abdominal pain, often with vaginal bleeding and haemodynamic instability, requiring urgent gynaecological assessment.
  • Pelvic inflammatory disease (PID) (in females) - lower abdominal pain, vaginal discharge, and cervical motion tenderness, typically less acute and severe than peritonitis.
  • Mesenteric ischaemia - severe abdominal pain out of proportion to physical findings, often in patients with cardiovascular risk factors, progressing to peritonitis if bowel infarction occurs.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Peritonitis"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Recognition of peritonitis, urgent escalation to senior medical and surgical teams, haemodynamic stabilisation with intravenous fluids, broad-spectrum antibiotics, and analgesia.
  • Definitive management: For secondary peritonitis, this often involves urgent surgical intervention to identify and correct the source of contamination (e.g., repair of perforation, appendicectomy, drainage of abscess). For SBP, definitive management focuses on appropriate antibiotic therapy and management of underlying liver disease.
  • Adjunctive management: May include nasogastric tube insertion for decompression in ileus, nutritional support, and management of complications such as acute kidney injury.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Close monitoring of vital signs, fluid balance, urine output, and inflammatory markers is essential. Regular clinical reassessment to detect improvement or deterioration.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Clear communication with patients and families regarding red flag symptoms and when to seek urgent medical attention.
  • Exam pharmacology reference (UKMLA/MRCGP): Empirical broad-spectrum antibiotics are often warranted, covering Gram-positive, Gram-negative, and anaerobic organisms. For SBP, a third-generation cephalosporin such as [sc name="jitl-medication" Q="Cefotaxime use in Spontaneous Bacterial Peritonitis" text="cefotaxime"][/sc] is typically considered first-line. For secondary peritonitis, combinations such as [sc name="jitl-medication" Q="Piperacillin-tazobactam use in Secondary Peritonitis" text="piperacillin-tazobactam"][/sc] or a carbapenem may be used. Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Peritonitis"][/sc]
  • Sepsis and septic shock: Uncontrolled infection and systemic inflammatory response can lead to widespread organ dysfunction and life-threatening circulatory collapse, particularly if not promptly managed within hours.
  • Abscess formation: Localised collections of pus can develop within the peritoneal cavity, requiring drainage and potentially prolonging recovery.
  • Paralytic ileus: Severe peritoneal inflammation can inhibit bowel motility, leading to functional bowel obstruction and abdominal distension, often persisting for several days.
  • Acute kidney injury: Often a consequence of hypovolaemia, sepsis, or nephrotoxic medications, developing within days of severe peritonitis.
  • Adhesions and chronic pain: Inflammation and healing processes can lead to fibrous bands forming between abdominal organs, potentially causing chronic pain or future bowel obstruction, sometimes months or years later.
