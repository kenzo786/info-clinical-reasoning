Varicella Zoster Virus (VZV) [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Varicella Zoster Virus (VZV)"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Varicella Zoster Virus (VZV)"][/sc]
  • A highly contagious DNA herpesvirus that causes two distinct clinical syndromes: primary infection results in varicella (chickenpox), a widespread vesicular rash, while reactivation of the latent virus causes herpes zoster (shingles), a painful, dermatomal vesicular rash.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical features of primary varicella and herpes zoster, distinguish them from other vesicular rashes, and initiate appropriate management, including considering antiviral therapy and recognising complications in at-risk groups.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Varicella Zoster Virus (VZV)"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Varicella Zoster Virus (VZV)"][/sc]
  • In the UK, over 90% of adults are seropositive for VZV. Chickenpox is a common childhood illness, with peak incidence in children under 5 years.
  • Herpes zoster incidence increases with age, affecting approximately 1 in 4 people in their lifetime, rising sharply in those over 50 years.
  • Risk factors for severe disease or complications include immunosuppression (e.g., from HIV, chemotherapy, or immunosuppressant medication), pregnancy, and neonates.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Varicella Zoster Virus (VZV)"][/sc]
  • Trigger: Primary infection occurs via inhalation of aerosolised respiratory droplets or direct contact with vesicle fluid, leading to viral replication in the nasopharynx and regional lymph nodes.
  • Progression: A primary viraemia disseminates the virus to the reticuloendothelial system, followed by a secondary viraemia which seeds the skin and mucous membranes, causing the characteristic vesicular rash of chickenpox. The virus then establishes lifelong latency in the dorsal root ganglia.
  • Consequence: A decline in cell-mediated immunity, often due to age or immunosuppression, allows the virus to reactivate. It then travels down a sensory nerve to the corresponding dermatome, causing the localised pain and vesicular eruption of herpes zoster (shingles).
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Varicella Zoster Virus (VZV)"][/sc]
  • Early phase: A prodrome of fever, malaise, and myalgia may occur 1-2 days before the rash in both chickenpox and shingles. Shingles is often preceded by neuropathic pain or itching in the affected dermatome for several days.
  • Progressive phase: In chickenpox, new vesicles appear in crops over 3-5 days, progressing from macules to papules, then vesicles, and finally crusting over. In shingles, the rash typically develops over 3-5 days within a single dermatome.
  • Late phase: Most chickenpox lesions have crusted by day 6, with full resolution over 2 weeks. Shingles vesicles typically crust over in 7-10 days, but the pain of [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Post-herpetic neuralgia: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="post-herpetic neuralgia"][/sc] can persist for months or years, particularly in older adults.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Varicella Zoster Virus (VZV)"][/sc]
  • Ask about rash character and distribution - a centrally distributed, pruritic rash with lesions in different stages of development ("crops") is classic for chickenpox. A unilateral, dermatomal vesicular rash is the hallmark of shingles.
  • Ask about prodromal symptoms - fever and malaise are common before the chickenpox rash. Pain, tingling, or burning in a specific area often precedes the shingles rash by several days.
  • Ask about systemic symptoms - high fever, headache, or photophobia may suggest more severe disease or complications like encephalitis.
  • Ask about immunosuppression or pregnancy - these are key risk factors for severe or disseminated VZV infection and influence management decisions.
  • Ask about facial or ear pain and facial drooping - this combination suggests [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Ramsay Hunt syndrome: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Ramsay Hunt syndrome"][/sc] (herpes zoster oticus), a serious complication requiring urgent assessment.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Varicella Zoster Virus (VZV)"][/sc]
  • Check the rash distribution and morphology - in chickenpox, look for vesicles on an erythematous base in various stages of healing, often starting on the trunk and spreading centrifugally. In shingles, confirm a unilateral dermatomal pattern that does not cross the midline.
  • Check for oral lesions - vesicles can occur on the palate and pharynx in chickenpox.
  • Check for neurological signs - assess for focal neurological deficits, altered mental state, or signs of meningism (neck stiffness, photophobia) which may indicate [sc name="jitl-query" Q="VZV encephalitis: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="VZV encephalitis"][/sc].
  • Check vital signs - pyrexia is common. Tachycardia or hypotension may indicate secondary bacterial infection or systemic illness.
  • Check the eyes - look for conjunctival injection, corneal lesions (dendritic ulcers), or visual changes, particularly if the ophthalmic division of the trigeminal nerve is involved (herpes zoster ophthalmicus).
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Varicella Zoster Virus (VZV)"][/sc]
  • Neurological symptoms - severe headache, confusion, seizures, or focal deficits suggest VZV encephalitis or meningitis, requiring urgent neurological assessment.
  • Ophthalmic involvement (Hutchinson\'s sign) - vesicles on the tip or side of the nose indicate involvement of the nasociliary branch of the trigeminal nerve and a high risk of sight-threatening herpes zoster ophthalmicus.
  • Disseminated disease - widespread vesicles crossing multiple dermatomes, or a generalised chickenpox-like rash in a patient with shingles, suggests disseminated VZV, common in the immunosuppressed.
  • Severe pain or facial palsy - may indicate Ramsay Hunt syndrome, which has a poorer prognosis for facial nerve recovery than Bell\'s palsy and requires prompt treatment.
  • Pregnant or immunosuppressed patient - these individuals are at high risk of severe complications, including pneumonia and disseminated disease, and require specialist consultation.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Varicella Zoster Virus (VZV)"][/sc]
  • Immunosuppressed patients: May present with atypical, widespread, or necrotic lesions, have prolonged viral shedding, and are at high risk of visceral dissemination (e.g., pneumonitis, hepatitis, encephalitis).
  • Older adults: More likely to experience severe and prolonged pain with shingles and have a higher incidence of post-herpetic neuralgia.
  • Pregnancy: Primary varicella infection can lead to congenital varicella syndrome if contracted in early pregnancy, or severe neonatal varicella if contracted near term.
  • Zoster sine herpete: Patients may experience the characteristic dermatomal pain of shingles but without the development of a rash, making diagnosis challenging.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Varicella Zoster Virus (VZV)"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected VZV: Diagnosis is usually clinical based on the characteristic rash. In atypical or severe cases, viral PCR from a vesicle fluid swab can confirm the diagnosis.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: VZV IgM serology can be used to confirm recent primary infection, but is less useful for shingles.
  • Imaging - Chest X-ray: Consider if respiratory symptoms are present to look for VZV pneumonitis, a serious complication more common in adults.
  • Specialist investigations: In suspected CNS infection, a lumbar puncture for CSF analysis and VZV PCR is indicated. An urgent ophthalmology review is required for suspected herpes zoster ophthalmicus.
  • Serial reassessment: Monitor for signs of secondary bacterial skin infection (e.g., increasing erythema, warmth, purulent discharge) and neurological or systemic complications.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Varicella Zoster Virus (VZV)"][/sc]
  • A 68-year-old man presents with a 3-day history of a painful, blistering rash on the right side of his chest that does not cross the midline, preceded by several days of burning pain in the same area.
  • An older adult with a painful, unilateral, dermatomal vesicular rash, consistent with reactivation of varicella zoster virus (herpes zoster).
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Varicella Zoster Virus (VZV)"][/sc]
  • Herpes simplex virus (HSV) - can cause a vesicular rash but is more commonly localised to oral or genital areas and tends to recur in the same location rather than a dermatomal pattern.
  • Impetigo - a bacterial skin infection causing vesicles or pustules that rupture to form golden-brown crusts; typically lacks a dermatomal distribution.
  • Contact dermatitis - an inflammatory skin reaction to an external agent, causing an eczematous or vesicular rash in the area of contact, not confined to a dermatome.
  • Insect bites - can cause pruritic papules or vesicles, but are usually multiple and scattered randomly rather than in a dermatomal or centripetal distribution.
  • [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Pityriasis rosea: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Pityriasis rosea"][/sc] - typically starts with a single \'herald patch\' followed by a generalised eruption of oval, pink-red macules with fine scale, often in a \'Christmas tree\' pattern on the back.
  • Hand, foot, and mouth disease - caused by an enterovirus, presents with vesicles in the mouth and on the palms and soles, typically in young children.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Varicella Zoster Virus (VZV)"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: For most children with chickenpox, management is supportive with hydration and simple analgesia. For shingles, early recognition and assessment for antiviral therapy is key, especially if presenting within 72 hours of rash onset or in at-risk individuals.
  • Definitive management: Oral antiviral therapy often warrants consideration for shingles to reduce the severity and duration of the acute illness and may reduce the risk of post-herpetic neuralgia. It is also used for chickenpox in at-risk groups (e.g., adults, the immunosuppressed).
  • Adjunctive management: Neuropathic pain agents may be considered for acute pain in shingles. Calamine lotion or emollient creams can help soothe the itch of chickenpox.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Advise on hygiene to prevent secondary bacterial infection. Patients should be advised to avoid contact with pregnant women and immunosuppressed individuals until all lesions have crusted over.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Advise patients to seek urgent review if they develop red flag features such as severe headache, eye symptoms, or signs of secondary infection.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Varicella Zoster Virus (VZV)"][/sc]
  • Post-herpetic neuralgia (PHN): The most common complication of shingles, defined as significant pain persisting for more than 90 days after rash onset. It results from nerve damage caused by the virus.
  • Secondary bacterial infection: Skin lesions can become superinfected with bacteria (e.g., Staphylococcus aureus, Streptococcus pyogenes), leading to cellulitis, impetigo, or invasive disease.
  • VZV encephalitis or meningitis: A rare but serious complication where the virus spreads to the central nervous system, causing inflammation. Can occur with both chickenpox and shingles, particularly in the immunosuppressed.
  • Herpes zoster ophthalmicus: Reactivation in the ophthalmic division of the trigeminal nerve can lead to keratitis, uveitis, and potential vision loss if not treated urgently.
  • Disseminated VZV: In immunocompromised individuals, the virus can spread haematogenously to involve multiple organs, including the lungs (pneumonitis), liver (hepatitis), and brain (encephalitis), carrying a high mortality rate.
