Spinal cord injury (Brain and spinal cord)

is-spinal-cord-injury.html

1. UKMLA Learning Outcome
  • Understand the classification, clinical presentation, and initial management of spinal cord injury, including the distinction between complete and incomplete injuries, neurogenic and spinal shock, and the recognition and management of autonomic dysreflexia.
2. Epidemiology
  • In the UK, an estimated 4,400 new cases of spinal cord injury occur each year, with approximately 50,000 people living with spinal cord injury (0.07% of the population).
3. Aetiology & Risk Factors
  • Traumatic causes: Road traffic accidents, falls, sporting accidents, violence (e.g., gunshot wounds, stabbings).
  • Non-traumatic causes: Tumours, infections (e.g., abscess), inflammation (e.g., transverse myelitis), vascular malformations, degenerative conditions.
4. Pathophysiology
  • Primary injury: Direct mechanical trauma to the spinal cord (e.g., compression, contusion, laceration, distraction).
  • Secondary injury: A cascade of biochemical and cellular events initiated by the primary injury, leading to further tissue damage (e.g., ischaemia, inflammation, excitotoxicity, apoptosis).
  • **Spinal Shock:** A transient physiological response to acute spinal cord injury, characterized by immediate loss of all voluntary motor activity, reflexes, and sensation below the level of the injury. It can last hours to weeks.
  • **Neurogenic Shock:** A distributive shock that can occur with spinal cord injuries above T6, resulting from the disruption of sympathetic outflow. This leads to unopposed parasympathetic activity, causing hypotension (due to vasodilation) and bradycardia.
5. Clinical Features
  • **Motor and Sensory Deficits:** Vary depending on the level and completeness of the injury.
  • **ASIA Impairment Scale (AIS):**
    • **AIS A (Complete):** No sensory or motor function preserved in the sacral segments S4-S5.
    • **AIS B (Sensory Incomplete):** Sensory but not motor function preserved below the neurological level and includes sacral segments S4-S5.
    • **AIS C (Motor Incomplete):** Motor function preserved below the neurological level, and more than half of key muscles below the neurological level have a muscle grade less than 3.
    • **AIS D (Motor Incomplete):** Motor function preserved below the neurological level, and at least half of key muscles below the neurological level have a muscle grade of 3 or greater.
    • **AIS E (Normal):** Sensation and motor function are normal.
  • **Complete vs Incomplete Injury:**
    • **Complete Injury:** Absence of sacral sparing (no sensory or motor function at S4-S5).
    • **Incomplete Injury:** Presence of sacral sparing (partial preservation of sensory and/or motor function at S4-S5).
  • **Autonomic Dysreflexia:** A potentially life-threatening condition in individuals with spinal cord injury at or above T6. Characterized by a sudden, severe increase in blood pressure, headache, sweating above the level of injury, and bradycardia. Triggered by noxious stimuli below the level of injury (e.g., bladder distension, bowel impaction, pressure sores).
  • **Anterior Cord Syndrome:** Characterized by motor paralysis and loss of pain and temperature sensation below the level of injury, with preservation of proprioception, vibration, and touch sensation.
6. Differential Diagnosis
  • Spinal cord compression (e.g., epidural abscess, tumour, disc herniation)
  • Transverse myelitis
  • Guillain-Barré syndrome
  • Stroke (brainstem or cerebral)
  • Conversion disorder
7. Investigations
  • **Imaging:**
    • **CT scan:** Initial imaging of choice for suspected spinal injury to assess bony trauma.
    • **MRI scan:** Essential for evaluating soft tissue injury, spinal cord oedema, haemorrhage, ligamentous injury, and to determine the extent of spinal cord involvement.
    • X-rays: May be used as a screening tool but have limited sensitivity for spinal cord injury.
  • **Neurological Assessment:** Repeated neurological examinations (e.g., ASIA Impairment Scale) to monitor changes and determine the true extent of injury after spinal shock resolves.
  • **Laboratory tests:** Full blood count, electrolytes, renal function, coagulation screen, arterial blood gas (if respiratory compromise).
8. Management
  • **Immediate Management:**
    • **Spinal immobilisation:** Crucial to prevent further injury (e.g., hard collar, spinal board, log roll).
    • **Airway, Breathing, Circulation (ABC) assessment and management:** Secure airway, ensure adequate ventilation (especially in high cervical injuries), maintain haemodynamic stability (e.g., fluid resuscitation, vasopressors for neurogenic shock).
    • **Oxygenation:** Maintain adequate oxygen saturation.
    • **Temperature control:** Prevent hypothermia.
  • **Pharmacological Management:**
    • **Methylprednisolone:** Controversial, not routinely recommended, but may be considered in specific circumstances (e.g., within 8 hours of injury) after discussion with a spinal specialist.
    • **Vasopressors:** To maintain mean arterial pressure in neurogenic shock.
    • **Analgesia:** For pain management.
  • **Surgical Management:** Indicated for unstable spinal fractures, spinal cord compression, or progressive neurological deficit.
  • **Long-term Rehabilitation:** Multidisciplinary approach including physiotherapy, occupational therapy, psychological support, and social care.
  • **Management of Autonomic Dysreflexia:** Identify and remove the noxious stimulus (e.g., empty bladder, disimpact bowel), elevate head of bed, administer antihypertensives if blood pressure remains high.
9. Complications & Prognosis
  • **Early Complications:**
    • Respiratory failure (especially high cervical injuries)
    • Neurogenic shock
    • Autonomic dysreflexia
    • Deep vein thrombosis and pulmonary embolism
    • Pressure sores
    • Urinary tract infections
    • Gastrointestinal complications (e.g., ileus, stress ulcers)
  • **Late Complications:**
    • Spasticity
    • Chronic pain
    • Heterotopic ossification
    • Syringomyelia
    • Psychological issues (e.g., depression, anxiety)
  • **Prognosis:** Highly variable, depends on the level and completeness of injury, age, and presence of complications. Incomplete injuries generally have a better prognosis for neurological recovery.
10. Key Learning Points
  • Spinal cord injury can be complete or incomplete, classified by the ASIA Impairment Scale.
  • Distinguish between spinal shock (transient loss of function) and neurogenic shock (haemodynamic instability due to sympathetic disruption).
  • Autonomic dysreflexia is a medical emergency in high spinal cord injuries, requiring prompt identification and removal of the trigger.
  • Initial management focuses on immobilisation, ABCs, and early imaging (CT for bone, MRI for soft tissue/cord).
  • Long-term care involves multidisciplinary rehabilitation and management of chronic complications.

12. Pharmacology
  • Pharmacological interventions are primarily supportive and aimed at managing complications.
  • Methylprednisolone: Its use in acute spinal cord injury is controversial and not routinely recommended.
  • Vasopressors (e.g., noradrenaline): Used to maintain mean arterial pressure in neurogenic shock.
  • Antihypertensives (e.g., nitrates, nifedipine): Used to manage severe hypertension during autonomic dysreflexia.
  • Analgesics: For pain management.
