Haemorrhoids [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Haemorrhoids"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Haemorrhoids"][/sc]
  • Haemorrhoids, commonly known as piles, are abnormally swollen vascular mucosal cushions located in the anal canal [1]. They can be internal (above the dentate line) or external (below the dentate line) [2].
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical presentation of haemorrhoids, differentiate between internal and external types, and understand initial management strategies including lifestyle modifications and when to refer for further intervention.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Haemorrhoids"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Haemorrhoids"][/sc]
  • Community-based studies in the UK report that haemorrhoids affect 13–36% of the general population [3].
  • Peak incidence occurs between 45 and 65 years of age, with prevalence decreasing in older age groups [4].
  • Risk factors include chronic constipation, prolonged straining during defecation, pregnancy, obesity, and prolonged sitting [5].
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Haemorrhoids"][/sc]
  • Trigger: Increased intra-abdominal pressure, often due to straining during defecation or pregnancy, leads to engorgement and downward displacement of the anal cushions [6].
  • Progression: The supporting connective tissue within the anal cushions degenerates, causing abnormal venous dilatation and prolapse of the vascular structures [7]. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Dentate line: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Dentate line"][/sc]
  • Consequence: This results in symptoms such as bleeding, pain, itching, and prolapse, with internal haemorrhoids originating above the dentate line and external haemorrhoids below it [8].
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Haemorrhoids"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Symptoms such as painless rectal bleeding, often noticed on toilet paper or in the toilet bowl, typically occur during or immediately after defecation. This phase may resolve spontaneously with conservative measures within a few days.
  • Progressive phase: With continued straining or predisposing factors, haemorrhoids may enlarge and prolapse, initially reducing spontaneously (Grade II) or requiring manual reduction (Grade III). Symptoms may become more persistent, including discomfort, itching, and mucus discharge.
  • Late phase: Persistent prolapse (Grade IV) or complications such as thrombosis of an external haemorrhoid can lead to severe pain, irreducible prolapse, and significant discomfort, often requiring medical intervention particularly beyond 7-10 days of acute symptoms.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Haemorrhoids"][/sc]
  • Ask about bright red rectal bleeding - often painless, occurring during or after defecation, indicating a lower gastrointestinal source.
  • Ask about anal itching (pruritus ani) - commonly due to mucus discharge from prolapsing internal haemorrhoids, leading to perianal irritation.
  • Ask about anal discomfort or pain - typically associated with external haemorrhoids, especially if thrombosed, or with severe prolapse of internal haemorrhoids.
  • Ask about prolapse or a lump around the anus - indicating internal haemorrhoids descending through the anal canal, which may reduce spontaneously, require manual reduction, or remain permanently prolapsed.
  • Ask about sensation of incomplete evacuation - a feeling that the rectum is still full after passing stool, often associated with larger internal haemorrhoids.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Haemorrhoids"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - typically normal in uncomplicated haemorrhoids; significant tachycardia or hypotension may suggest severe haemorrhage or other pathology.
  • Check perianal inspection - external haemorrhoids appear as bluish lumps if thrombosed; prolapsed internal haemorrhoids may be visible as moist, pink masses. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Goligher grading: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Goligher grading"][/sc]
  • Check digital rectal examination (DRE) - internal haemorrhoids are often not palpable unless thrombosed or severely prolapsed; DRE helps exclude other rectal pathology.
  • Check proctoscopy - visualises internal haemorrhoids, allowing assessment of their size, number, and degree of prolapse, crucial for Goligher grading.
  • Check signs of anaemia - pallor, conjunctival pallor, which may indicate chronic blood loss from haemorrhoids.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Haemorrhoids"][/sc]
  • Change in bowel habit: New onset or persistent change in bowel habit, particularly in individuals over 40, warrants investigation to exclude colorectal malignancy.
  • Unexplained weight loss: Significant unintentional weight loss associated with rectal bleeding should prompt urgent investigation for underlying malignancy or inflammatory bowel disease.
  • Abdominal pain: Persistent or severe abdominal pain, especially if associated with rectal bleeding, suggests a more proximal gastrointestinal pathology than uncomplicated haemorrhoids.
  • Anaemia: Iron deficiency anaemia, particularly if severe or unexplained by other causes, requires investigation of the entire gastrointestinal tract to identify the source of chronic blood loss.
  • Rectal bleeding mixed with stool: Blood mixed with stool, rather than separate, suggests a more proximal source of bleeding within the colon, necessitating colonoscopy.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Haemorrhoids"][/sc]
  • Pregnancy: Increased intra-abdominal pressure and hormonal changes can exacerbate haemorrhoids, often presenting with more severe prolapse and discomfort. Management focuses on conservative measures and avoiding straining.
  • Older adults: May present with less pronounced symptoms or attribute them to other age-related conditions, potentially delaying diagnosis. Co-existing comorbidities and medications (e.g., anticoagulants) can complicate management.
  • Immunosuppressed patients: May be at higher risk of perianal infections or complications following interventions, requiring careful consideration of treatment options.
  • Thrombosed external haemorrhoid: Presents with sudden onset of severe perianal pain and a tender, firm lump, often mistaken for other acute anal conditions. Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Haemorrhoids"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected haemorrhoids: Clinical history and examination, including perianal inspection and digital rectal examination. Proctoscopy is essential for visualising internal haemorrhoids and determining their grade.
  • Severity/risk tools: Goligher classification for internal haemorrhoids (Grade I-IV) guides management decisions.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Flexible sigmoidoscopy or colonoscopy may be indicated, especially in patients with red flag symptoms, age over 40 with new onset symptoms, or persistent unexplained rectal bleeding. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Flexible sigmoidoscopy: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Flexible sigmoidoscopy"][/sc]
  • Laboratory tests: Full blood count to assess for anaemia due to chronic blood loss.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Haemorrhoids"][/sc]
  • A 55-year-old male presents with a 3-month history of painless bright red rectal bleeding after defecation, associated with an occasional reducible perianal lump.
  • Middle-aged male with chronic, painless, bright red rectal bleeding and reducible prolapse, concerning for internal haemorrhoids.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Haemorrhoids"][/sc]
  • Anal fissure - characterised by severe pain during and after defecation, often with bright red bleeding on toilet paper.
  • Perianal haematoma - sudden onset of painful, tense, bluish lump at the anal verge, typically following straining.
  • Rectal prolapse - circumferential protrusion of rectal mucosa or full thickness rectal wall through the anus, often larger and more extensive than haemorrhoidal prolapse.
  • Colorectal malignancy - can present with rectal bleeding, change in bowel habit, and weight loss; requires exclusion, especially in older patients or those with red flags. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Colorelectoral malignancy: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Colorectal malignancy"][/sc]
  • Inflammatory bowel disease (IBD) - can cause rectal bleeding, abdominal pain, and altered bowel habit; often associated with systemic symptoms.
  • Diverticular disease - typically presents with painless rectal bleeding, which can be profuse, but usually without perianal symptoms.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Haemorrhoids"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Assess for significant blood loss and anaemia. Exclude red flag symptoms. Provide symptomatic relief for pain and discomfort.
  • Definitive management:
    • Lifestyle modifications: Increase dietary fibre and fluid intake, avoid prolonged straining, and regular exercise to prevent constipation [9].
    • Non-surgical interventions: Rubber band ligation is often considered for Grade I-III internal haemorrhoids [10]. Sclerotherapy or infrared coagulation may also be used [11].
    • Surgical interventions: Haemorrhoidectomy, stapled haemorrhoidopexy, or haemorrhoidal artery ligation may be considered for Grade III-IV haemorrhoids or failure of conservative/non-surgical treatments [12].
  • Adjunctive management: Topical preparations containing local anaesthetics or corticosteroids may provide temporary symptomatic relief for itching and discomfort [13].
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Review symptoms after conservative management. Follow-up after interventional procedures to assess efficacy and manage complications.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Advise patients to seek medical attention for worsening symptoms, severe pain, increased bleeding, or development of new red flag features.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Haemorrhoids"][/sc]
  • Thrombosis: Acute thrombosis of an external haemorrhoid causes sudden, severe pain and a tender, firm perianal lump, typically resolving over 7-10 days but may require excision for rapid relief.
  • Strangulation: Occurs when prolapsed internal haemorrhoids become incarcerated and their blood supply is compromised, leading to severe pain, oedema, and potential necrosis, requiring urgent intervention.
  • Anaemia: Chronic, recurrent bleeding from haemorrhoids can lead to iron deficiency anaemia, particularly if blood loss is significant and prolonged.
  • Ulceration and infection: Prolapsed or thrombosed haemorrhoids can ulcerate, increasing the risk of local infection and perianal sepsis, especially in immunocompromised individuals.
  • Skin tags: Residual skin folds may form after resolution of external haemorrhoids or thrombosed external haemorrhoids, which can cause hygiene issues and irritation.
