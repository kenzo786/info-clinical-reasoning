Pneumothorax [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Pneumothorax"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Pneumothorax"][/sc]
  • A pneumothorax is the presence of air in the pleural space, leading to partial or complete lung collapse.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical features, classify the type, and initiate appropriate emergency management for a patient presenting with pneumothorax.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Pneumothorax"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Pneumothorax"][/sc]
  • Primary spontaneous pneumothorax (PSP) typically affects tall, thin young men (aged 10-30 years), with an incidence of approximately 18-28 per 100,000 men and 1.2-6 per 100,000 women per year in the UK. Smoking is a significant risk factor.
  • Secondary spontaneous pneumothorax (SSP) occurs in individuals with underlying lung disease, most commonly COPD (up to 70% of cases), but also asthma, cystic fibrosis, and interstitial lung disease. Incidence is around 6.3 per 100,000 men and 2.0 per 100,000 women per year.
  • Connective tissue disorders such as Marfan syndrome and Ehlers-Danlos syndrome are associated with an increased risk of both PSP and SSP.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Pneumothorax"][/sc]
  • Air enters the pleural space, either from the lung (e.g., rupture of a subpleural bleb in PSP, or diseased lung in SSP) or from outside the chest wall (e.g., trauma, iatrogenic).
  • The accumulation of air disrupts the negative intrapleural pressure, leading to elastic recoil of the lung and subsequent collapse, impairing gas exchange.
  • In tension pneumothorax, a one-way valve mechanism allows air to enter the pleural space during inspiration but not escape during expiration, causing progressive accumulation of air, increasing intrapleural pressure, and leading to mediastinal shift and haemodynamic compromise. Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Pneumothorax"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Symptoms typically have an acute onset, often presenting with sudden pleuritic chest pain and dyspnoea, usually within minutes to a few hours of the event.
  • Progressive phase: Over several hours to days, the pneumothorax may expand, leading to worsening dyspnoea and potentially developing into a tension pneumothorax, particularly beyond 24-48 hours if air continues to accumulate.
  • Late phase: Resolution of a small pneumothorax can occur spontaneously over days to weeks. Larger or persistent pneumothoraces require intervention, with potential for recurrence, particularly within the first year.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Pneumothorax"][/sc]
  • Ask about sudden onset pleuritic chest pain - often sharp, unilateral, and exacerbated by inspiration or movement, suggesting pleural irritation.
  • Ask about acute dyspnoea - severity correlates with the size of the pneumothorax and underlying lung function, indicating reduced ventilatory capacity.
  • Ask about cough - a non-productive cough may be present due to irritation of the airways or pleura.
  • Ask about recent strenuous activity or changes in atmospheric pressure - these can sometimes precipitate PSP.
  • Ask about history of lung disease (e.g., COPD, asthma, cystic fibrosis) - crucial for differentiating PSP from SSP, which has different management implications.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Pneumothorax"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - tachycardia, tachypnoea, and hypoxia are common. Hypotension and profound shock suggest tension pneumothorax.
  • Check chest expansion - reduced or absent on the affected side, indicating lung collapse.
  • Check percussion note - hyper-resonant over the affected area due to air accumulation.
  • Check breath sounds - diminished or absent over the affected lung field, confirming lack of air entry.
  • Check tracheal deviation - deviation away from the affected side is a late and ominous sign of tension pneumothorax, indicating significant mediastinal shift.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Pneumothorax"][/sc]
  • Tension Pneumothorax - rapidly progressive dyspnoea, hypotension, tachycardia, tracheal deviation, and absent breath sounds on the affected side, indicating life-threatening haemodynamic compromise requiring immediate decompression. This can develop within minutes to hours of symptom onset.
  • Large Pneumothorax - significant dyspnoea, hypoxaemia, and chest pain, particularly in patients with underlying lung disease, suggesting a need for urgent intervention to prevent respiratory failure.
  • Bilateral Pneumothorax - severe respiratory distress and hypoxaemia, as both lungs are compromised, requiring immediate and careful management.
  • Haemodynamic Instability - persistent hypotension despite fluid resuscitation, indicating severe compromise, often due to tension pneumothorax or haemopneumothorax.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Pneumothorax"][/sc]
  • Elderly patients: May present with less dramatic symptoms, such as mild dyspnoea or vague chest discomfort, making diagnosis challenging, especially with co-morbidities.
  • Patients on mechanical ventilation: Pneumothorax may be difficult to detect clinically, often presenting as sudden deterioration in ventilatory parameters (e.g., increased peak airway pressure) or hypotension.
  • Post-procedural: Following thoracic procedures (e.g., central line insertion, lung biopsy), pneumothorax may be iatrogenic and present with delayed symptoms or subtle signs.
  • Catamenial pneumothorax: A rare form occurring in women of reproductive age, typically within 72 hours of menstruation, due to endometrial implants in the pleura.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Pneumothorax"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected Pneumothorax:
    • Chest X-ray (CXR): The initial investigation of choice, revealing the visceral pleural line and absence of lung markings peripheral to it. Expiratory films may increase sensitivity for small pneumothoraces.
  • Severity/risk tools:
    • BTS guidelines classify pneumothorax size based on the distance from the lung edge to the chest wall at the level of the hilum: <2cm is small, ≥2cm is large. This guides management decisions.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible:
    • CT chest: More sensitive than CXR, particularly for small or atypical pneumothoraces, and can help identify underlying lung pathology (e.g., bullae, emphysema) or differentiate from other conditions like bullous lung disease.
    • ECG: To rule out cardiac causes of chest pain, such as myocardial infarction.
  • Serial reassessment:
    • Repeat CXR: To monitor the size of the pneumothorax and assess the effectiveness of interventions (e.g., chest drain insertion).
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Pneumothorax"][/sc]
  • A young, tall, thin male presents with sudden onset unilateral pleuritic chest pain and dyspnoea, with reduced breath sounds and hyper-resonance on the affected side.
  • Acute, unilateral chest pain and dyspnoea in a young, otherwise healthy male, concerning for primary spontaneous pneumothorax.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Pneumothorax"][/sc]
  • Myocardial Infarction - typically associated with exertional retrosternal chest pain, radiation, and ECG changes, rather than pleuritic pain and respiratory signs.
  • Pulmonary Embolism - often presents with sudden dyspnoea and pleuritic chest pain, but usually accompanied by risk factors for VTE and haemoptysis, with normal chest X-ray or specific CTPA findings.
  • Acute Asthma Exacerbation - characterised by wheeze, cough, and diffuse airway obstruction, rather than unilateral signs of lung collapse.
  • Acute Exacerbation of COPD - presents with worsening cough, sputum, and dyspnoea in a patient with known COPD, but typically without the acute unilateral chest signs of pneumothorax.
  • Musculoskeletal Chest Pain - localised, reproducible pain on palpation of the chest wall, without respiratory compromise or signs of lung pathology.
  • Pericarditis - pleuritic chest pain that is often relieved by leaning forward, with characteristic ECG changes and pericardial rub, distinct from pneumothorax.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Pneumothorax"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Recognition of tension pneumothorax and immediate needle decompression (if indicated) followed by chest drain insertion. For non-tension pneumothorax, assessment of size and symptoms guides management. Escalation to senior medical staff is often warranted.
  • Definitive management:
    • Small PSP (<2cm): Often managed conservatively with observation and supplemental oxygen, as spontaneous resolution is common.
    • Large PSP (≥2cm) or symptomatic PSP: Typically requires aspiration or chest drain insertion.
    • SSP: Usually requires chest drain insertion due to underlying lung disease and higher risk of complications.
    • Recurrent pneumothorax: May warrant surgical intervention (e.g., pleurodesis, pleurectomy) to prevent further episodes.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Serial clinical assessment and chest X-rays are essential to monitor resolution or detect complications. Patients are typically advised to avoid flying and diving for a period.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Patients should be advised on symptoms of recurrence or worsening and when to seek urgent medical attention.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Pneumothorax"][/sc]
  • Tension Pneumothorax: Rapid accumulation of air in the pleural space leading to mediastinal shift, impaired venous return, and cardiovascular collapse, which can be fatal if not promptly decompressed. This is an acute, life-threatening complication that can develop within minutes to hours.
  • Recurrence: A significant risk, particularly for PSP (up to 30-50% within 1-2 years) and SSP, due to persistent underlying lung pathology or incomplete healing of the pleural defect.
  • Persistent Air Leak: Continued leakage of air into the pleural space despite chest drain insertion, prolonging hospital stay and increasing risk of infection. This can persist for several days to weeks.
  • Haemopneumothorax: Concurrent presence of blood and air in the pleural space, often due to trauma or iatrogenic injury, which can lead to significant blood loss and hypovolaemic shock.
  • Empyema: Infection of the pleural space, a potential complication of chest drain insertion or prolonged air leak, requiring antibiotic therapy and often further drainage.
