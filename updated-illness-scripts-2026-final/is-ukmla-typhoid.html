Typhoid (Infections) [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Typhoid (Infections)"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Typhoid (Infections)"][/sc]
  • Typhoid fever is a systemic bacterial infection caused by *Salmonella enterica* serovar Typhi, characterised by a gradual onset of sustained fever and multi-systemic involvement.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical features of typhoid fever, understand its transmission, and appreciate the importance of early diagnosis and public health notification to prevent further spread.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Typhoid (Infections)"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Typhoid (Infections)"][/sc]
  • Globally, there are an estimated 11 to 20 million new cases of typhoid/paratyphoid annually, with 128,000 to 161,000 deaths, predominantly in Asia and Africa [8].
  • In England, Wales, and Northern Ireland (EWNI), an average of 325 confirmed cases were reported annually between 2012 and 2018, with a notable increase to 515 cases in 2019 [683]. Most cases (93%) are travel-associated, primarily from India, Pakistan, and Bangladesh [699]. In 2024, there were 702 reported cases in England, Wales, and Northern Ireland, representing an 8% rise from the 645 cases recorded in 2023 [Vax-Before-Travel].
  • Risk factors include travel to endemic regions with poor sanitation, consumption of contaminated food or water, and close contact with carriers.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Typhoid (Infections)"][/sc]
  • Ingestion of *Salmonella Typhi* bacteria, typically through contaminated food or water, leads to invasion of the intestinal mucosa, particularly Peyer's patches, and subsequent lymphatic spread to regional lymph nodes.
  • The bacteria then disseminate haematogenously via the reticuloendothelial system (spleen, liver, bone marrow), leading to systemic infection and sustained fever. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Peyer's patches: what are they and why do they matter clinically?" text="Peyer's patches"][/sc]
  • Persistent bacteraemia and endotoxin release contribute to systemic symptoms, with potential for re-invasion of the gut, leading to ulceration and complications such as intestinal haemorrhage or perforation.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Typhoid (Infections)"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Following an incubation period of 7 to 21 days (mean), symptoms typically begin gradually with a stepwise increase in fever, malaise, headache, and anorexia.
  • Progressive phase: Over the next 1-2 weeks, fever becomes sustained, and other systemic symptoms develop, including relative bradycardia, abdominal discomfort, and potentially the appearance of rose spots on the trunk. Constipation is often observed initially, followed by diarrhoea.
  • Late phase: Without effective treatment, complications typically arise in the third week of illness, such as intestinal haemorrhage or perforation, and neurological manifestations.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Typhoid (Infections)"][/sc]
  • Ask about fever - typically a gradual, stepwise increase over several days, becoming sustained, which is a key differentiator from other acute febrile illnesses.
  • Ask about headache and malaise - common early non-specific symptoms reflecting systemic infection.
  • Ask about abdominal pain or discomfort - often diffuse, can be associated with constipation initially, followed by pea-soup diarrhoea, indicating gastrointestinal involvement.
  • Ask about travel history - crucial for epidemiological context, especially to endemic areas, as most UK cases are imported.
  • Ask about anorexia and weight loss - indicative of prolonged systemic illness.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Typhoid (Infections)"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - sustained high fever with relative bradycardia (Faget sign) is a classic, though not universally present, finding that can help differentiate from other febrile conditions. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Faget sign: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Faget sign"][/sc]
  • Check for rose spots - faint, erythematous, maculopapular rash on the trunk, typically appearing in the second week of illness in a minority of patients, reflecting dermal bacterial emboli.
  • Check for splenomegaly - palpable spleen, often soft, due to reticuloendothelial hyperplasia.
  • Check for abdominal tenderness - diffuse tenderness, particularly in the right lower quadrant, may indicate ileal involvement.
  • Check mental status - can range from apathy to delirium in severe cases, reflecting encephalopathy.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Typhoid (Infections)"][/sc]
  • Persistent high fever beyond 7 days: Suggests ongoing systemic infection and potential for complications, warranting urgent re-evaluation and treatment escalation.
  • Significant abdominal pain with guarding or rebound tenderness: May indicate intestinal perforation, a life-threatening complication requiring immediate surgical assessment.
  • Gastrointestinal haemorrhage (melaena or haematochezia): Suggests ulceration of Peyer's patches, a serious complication requiring urgent medical intervention.
  • Altered mental status or neurological signs: Could indicate typhoid encephalopathy or meningitis, necessitating prompt neurological assessment and intensive care.
  • Shock or haemodynamic instability: Signifies severe sepsis, requiring immediate resuscitation and critical care management.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Typhoid (Infections)"][/sc]
  • Children: May present with less specific symptoms, including diarrhoea more commonly than constipation, and often without relative bradycardia or rose spots, making diagnosis challenging.
  • Immunocompromised individuals: (e.g., HIV, sickle cell disease) May have a more severe or protracted course, with higher risk of complications and atypical infections like osteomyelitis. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Sickle cell disease and infection: what is the link and why does it matter clinically?" text="Sickle cell disease"][/sc]
  • Partially treated cases: Prior antibiotic use can mask typical symptoms, leading to delayed diagnosis and potential for chronic carriage.
  • Vaccinated individuals: May experience milder, attenuated illness, making clinical suspicion and diagnostic testing crucial despite vaccination history.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Typhoid (Infections)"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected Typhoid:
    • Blood cultures: Most sensitive diagnostic test, particularly in the first week of fever, with sensitivity decreasing over time. Repeat cultures may be necessary.
    • Stool cultures: Important for diagnosis, especially in later stages of illness and for identifying carriers.
    • Bone marrow culture: Highly sensitive, especially in cases with prior antibiotic use, but invasive.
    • PCR for *Salmonella Typhi*: Can be used on blood or stool samples for rapid detection.
  • Severity/risk tools:
    • No widely validated severity score for typhoid fever is routinely used in UK clinical practice. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Typhoid severity scores: what are they and why do they matter clinically?" text="Typhoid severity scores"][/sc]
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible:
    • Full blood count: May show leucopenia, anaemia, or thrombocytopenia.
    • Liver function tests: Can be deranged due to hepatitis.
    • Urine cultures: May be positive in some cases.
  • Imaging - Abdominal ultrasound/CT: May reveal splenomegaly, mesenteric lymphadenopathy, or signs of intestinal complications like perforation. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Mesenteric lymphadenopathy: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Mesenteric lymphadenopathy"][/sc]
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Typhoid (Infections)"][/sc]
  • A 30-year-old male presents with a 10-day history of gradually escalating fever, severe headache, malaise, and recent travel to India, now experiencing abdominal discomfort and relative bradycardia.
  • A febrile illness with systemic symptoms and a relevant travel history, concerning for enteric fever.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Typhoid (Infections)"][/sc]
  • Malaria - cyclical fever, often with rigors, and distinct geographical exposure.
  • Dengue fever - sudden onset high fever, severe headache, retro-orbital pain, rash, and often thrombocytopenia.
  • Paratyphoid fever - clinically similar but generally milder, caused by *Salmonella Paratyphi*.
  • Rickettsial infections - often associated with eschar, rash, and specific geographical exposure.
  • Viral haemorrhagic fevers - severe systemic illness with haemorrhagic manifestations, distinct epidemiology.
  • Brucellosis - undulating fever, arthralgia, hepatosplenomegaly, often linked to unpasteurised dairy or animal contact. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Undulating fever: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Undulating fever"][/sc]
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Typhoid (Infections)"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Recognition of suspected enteric fever, prompt initiation of appropriate empiric antibiotic therapy, and supportive care including fluid and electrolyte management. Defer to local antimicrobial guidelines and infectious disease specialist advice.
  • Definitive management: Antibiotic therapy guided by local resistance patterns and susceptibility testing. Fluoroquinolones (e.g., ciprofloxacin), azithromycin, or third-generation cephalosporins (e.g., ceftriaxone) are commonly used.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Clinical improvement, resolution of fever, and negative follow-up stool cultures to confirm eradication and exclude chronic carriage, especially in high-risk individuals.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Advise patients on red flag symptoms for complications (e.g., worsening abdominal pain, persistent fever, altered consciousness) and when to seek urgent medical attention. Public health notification is mandatory.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Typhoid (Infections)"][/sc]
  • Intestinal haemorrhage: Occurs due to ulceration of Peyer's patches, typically in the third week of illness, presenting as melaena or haematochezia, indicating significant gastrointestinal damage.
  • Intestinal perforation: A life-threatening complication resulting from full-thickness ulceration of the bowel wall, usually in the ileum, leading to peritonitis and sepsis, requiring emergency surgery.
  • Typhoid encephalopathy: Manifests as altered mental status, delirium, or coma, reflecting severe systemic toxicity and central nervous system involvement.
  • Cholecystitis: Inflammation of the gallbladder, which can occur acutely or lead to chronic carriage of *Salmonella Typhi* in the gallbladder, acting as a reservoir for infection.
  • Osteomyelitis: A rare but serious complication, particularly in individuals with pre-existing conditions like sickle cell disease, where bacteria can seed bone.
  • Relapse: Occurs in a minority of patients (5-10%) after initial clinical improvement, typically milder than the primary infection, often due to inadequate treatment or persistent foci of infection.
