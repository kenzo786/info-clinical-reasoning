Disease prevention/ screening [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Disease prevention/ screening"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Disease prevention/ screening"][/sc]
  • Disease prevention and screening encompass a range of public health interventions aimed at reducing the incidence and prevalence of disease, as well as detecting conditions early to improve outcomes.
  • UKMLA Learning Outcome: Demonstrate knowledge of the principles of disease prevention and screening, including primary, secondary, and tertiary prevention strategies, and the ethical considerations surrounding screening programmes.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Disease prevention/ screening"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Disease prevention/ screening"][/sc]
  • In England and Wales, approximately one in five deaths in 2024 were considered avoidable through effective prevention or treatment (21.3% in England, 22.8% in Wales) [1].
  • Neoplasms (cancers) and diseases of the circulatory system were the leading causes of avoidable mortality in 2023, highlighting key areas for prevention and screening efforts [1].
  • Common risk factors targeted by prevention and screening programmes, such as those assessed in the NHS Health Check, include high blood pressure, high cholesterol, obesity, and smoking, which contribute to a range of non-communicable diseases [2].
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Disease prevention/ screening"][/sc]
  • Trigger: The rationale for disease prevention and screening is often triggered by the identification of modifiable risk factors within a population or for individuals, as well as the burden of disease on public health, prompting interventions to mitigate future health issues.
  • Progression: Prevention strategies progress through primary (e.g., vaccination, lifestyle advice), secondary (e.g., screening for early detection), and tertiary (e.g., rehabilitation to prevent complications) levels. Screening involves systematic testing of asymptomatic individuals to identify those at increased risk, leading to further diagnostic investigation.
  • Consequence: Successful implementation of prevention and screening programmes leads to reduced disease incidence, earlier diagnosis, improved treatment outcomes, and decreased morbidity and mortality, ultimately enhancing population health and well-being.

Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Disease prevention/ screening"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Focus on primary prevention, targeting individuals and populations before disease onset. This involves health promotion, education on lifestyle modifications (e.g., diet, exercise, smoking cessation), and immunisation programmes to prevent exposure or susceptibility to disease.
  • Progressive phase: Implementation of secondary prevention strategies, primarily through screening programmes. This phase aims to detect disease in its asymptomatic or early stages, allowing for timely intervention and treatment to halt or slow progression.
  • Late phase: Encompasses tertiary prevention, where the disease is already established. The focus shifts to managing the condition, preventing complications, reducing disability, and improving quality of life through rehabilitation and ongoing care.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Disease prevention/ screening"][/sc]
  • Ask about family history of disease - to identify genetic predispositions or familial risk factors for conditions like certain cancers or cardiovascular disease.
  • Ask about lifestyle habits (e.g., smoking, alcohol intake, diet, physical activity) - as these are modifiable risk factors for a wide range of chronic diseases.
  • Ask about occupational and environmental exposures - to assess potential contact with carcinogens, toxins, or infectious agents.
  • Ask about vaccination history - to ensure up-to-date immunisation status for preventable infectious diseases.
  • Ask about previous screening uptake and results (e.g., cervical, breast, bowel cancer screening) - to ensure adherence to national screening programmes and identify any previous abnormal findings.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Disease prevention/ screening"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs (blood pressure, heart rate, respiratory rate, temperature) - to identify hypertension, arrhythmias, or signs of chronic disease that warrant further investigation or lifestyle modification.
  • Check Body Mass Index (BMI) and waist circumference - to assess for overweight or obesity, significant risk factors for numerous non-communicable diseases.
  • Check skin for suspicious lesions - to identify potential signs of skin cancer requiring dermatological review.
  • Check oral cavity for signs of dental disease or oral cancer - particularly in individuals with risk factors such as smoking or alcohol use.
  • [sc name="jitl-query" Q="What are the implications of an abnormal lipid profile in disease prevention?" text="Lipid profile results"] - to assess cardiovascular risk and guide dietary or pharmacological interventions.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Disease prevention/ screening"][/sc]
  • Unexplained weight loss or gain: May indicate underlying malignancy, metabolic disorder, or significant psychological distress requiring prompt medical evaluation.
  • New onset of severe, persistent pain: Could be a symptom of serious underlying pathology (e.g., malignancy, acute coronary syndrome) necessitating immediate diagnostic work-up.
  • Sudden change in bowel or bladder habits: Potentially indicative of colorectal cancer, bladder cancer, or other serious gastrointestinal/genitourinary conditions requiring urgent investigation.
  • New neurological deficit (e.g., weakness, numbness, speech disturbance): Suggests acute neurological event (e.g., stroke, transient ischaemic attack) or progressive neurological disease, demanding rapid assessment and intervention.
  • Non-healing ulcer or persistent abnormal bleeding: Raises suspicion for malignancy or significant coagulopathy, warranting urgent medical review and biopsy if indicated.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Disease prevention/ screening"][/sc]
  • Immunocompromised individuals: May present with atypical responses to vaccinations or have a higher risk of opportunistic infections, requiring tailored prevention strategies and more frequent screening for certain conditions.
  • Individuals with learning disabilities: May face barriers to understanding health information and accessing screening services, leading to delayed presentation or reduced uptake of preventive measures.
  • Ethnic minority groups: May have different genetic predispositions to certain diseases, cultural beliefs influencing health-seeking behaviours, or language barriers affecting engagement with prevention and screening programmes.
  • Socioeconomically disadvantaged populations: Often experience higher rates of chronic diseases due to poorer living conditions, limited access to healthy food, and reduced opportunities for physical activity, requiring targeted public health interventions.
  • Elderly populations: May have multiple comorbidities, polypharmacy, and altered physiological responses, necessitating a nuanced approach to screening and prevention that balances benefits against potential harms and considers frailty.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Disease prevention/ screening"][/sc]
  • Core Screening Programmes (UK):
    • **Cervical Screening:** Cervical cytology (smear test) to detect abnormal cells that could lead to cervical cancer.
    • **Breast Screening:** Mammography for women aged 50-71 to detect early breast cancer.
    • **Bowel Cancer Screening:** Faecal immunochemical test (FIT) for individuals aged 60-74 to detect blood in stool, indicating potential bowel cancer.
    • **NHS Health Check:** Cardiovascular risk assessment, including blood pressure, cholesterol, and glucose measurements, for adults aged 40-74.
    • **Diabetic Eye Screening:** Retinal photography for individuals with diabetes to detect diabetic retinopathy.
    • **Antenatal Screening:** Blood tests for infectious diseases (HIV, Hepatitis B, Syphilis) and screening for conditions like Down's syndrome.
    • **Newborn Screening:** Heel prick test for conditions like phenylketonuria, cystic fibrosis, and sickle cell disease.
  • Imaging:
    • Mammography (as part of breast screening).
    • Retinal photography (as part of diabetic eye screening).
  • If alternative:
    • Colonoscopy following a positive FIT result in bowel cancer screening.
    • Further diagnostic tests (e.g., biopsy, colposcopy) following abnormal screening results.
  • Serial reassessment:
    • Regular follow-up for individuals identified with high-risk factors (e.g., high blood pressure, dyslipidaemia) to monitor progression and effectiveness of interventions.
    • Repeat screening at recommended intervals based on national guidelines and individual risk factors.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Disease prevention/ screening"][/sc]
  • Cardiovascular Disease (CVD) - distinguished by a high prevalence, significant morbidity and mortality, and the presence of multiple modifiable risk factors (e.g., hypertension, hyperlipidaemia, smoking) amenable to primary prevention and screening (e.g., NHS Health Check).
  • Cancers (e.g., Breast, Cervical, Colorectal) - distinguished by specific screening programmes targeting early detection in asymptomatic populations, often with established evidence of screening efficacy in reducing mortality.
  • Type 2 Diabetes Mellitus - distinguished by its rising prevalence, significant long-term complications, and the ability to identify individuals at high risk through screening (e.g., fasting glucose, HbA1c) for early lifestyle interventions.
  • Infectious Diseases (e.g., HIV, Hepatitis B, Syphilis) - distinguished by their transmissibility, potential for severe outcomes, and the availability of effective vaccines (primary prevention) and screening tests (secondary prevention) to control spread and initiate early treatment.
  • Mental Health Conditions (e.g., Depression, Anxiety) - distinguished by their high prevalence and impact on quality of life, with screening often involving validated questionnaires to identify individuals who may benefit from early psychological or pharmacological interventions.
  • Osteoporosis - distinguished by its silent progression to fragility fractures, with screening (e.g., DEXA scan) targeted at at-risk populations (e.g., post-menopausal women) to enable early intervention and fracture prevention.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Disease prevention/ screening"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities:
    • Address any acute health concerns or red flag symptoms identified during assessment.
    • Provide immediate counselling on critical modifiable risk factors (e.g., smoking cessation, urgent dietary advice for severe obesity).
    • Facilitate urgent referrals for abnormal screening results (e.g., suspected cancer, critical cardiovascular risk).
  • Definitive management:
    • Implement primary prevention strategies: health education, lifestyle modification advice (diet, exercise, alcohol, smoking), and appropriate immunisations.
    • Implement secondary prevention strategies: enrolment in national screening programmes (e.g., cervical, breast, bowel, diabetic eye screening) based on age and risk factors.
    • Offer counselling on risk reduction for specific conditions (e.g., cardiovascular risk management, cancer prevention).
  • Monitoring and follow-up:
    • Schedule regular health reviews to monitor adherence to preventive advice and screening uptake.
    • Monitor key health indicators (e.g., blood pressure, cholesterol, weight) and adjust interventions as necessary.
    • Ensure timely follow-up for any abnormal screening results or emerging health concerns.
  • Escalation and safety-netting:
    • Refer to specialist services (e.g., cardiology, oncology, diabetes specialist nurse) if risk factors are poorly controlled, screening results are persistently abnormal, or disease progression is suspected.
    • Provide clear safety-netting advice regarding symptoms that warrant immediate medical attention.
  • Exam pharmacology reference:
    • Consideration of medications for primary prevention in high-risk individuals, such as statins for hyperlipidaemia or anti-hypertensives for hypertension, often identified through screening. Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Disease prevention/ screening"][/sc]
  • Advanced Disease Presentation: Failure of primary or secondary prevention can lead to diseases being diagnosed at a later, more advanced stage, often associated with poorer prognosis, more aggressive treatment requirements, and reduced survival rates.
  • Increased Morbidity and Mortality: Inadequate prevention and screening contribute to a higher burden of chronic diseases, leading to increased rates of illness, disability, and premature death within the population.
  • Avoidable Complications: Lack of early detection and intervention can result in the development of preventable complications (e.g., diabetic retinopathy, cardiovascular events, metastatic cancer), significantly impacting quality of life and increasing healthcare costs.
  • Psychological Distress: Individuals who develop preventable diseases or receive late diagnoses may experience significant psychological distress, including anxiety, depression, and regret, impacting their mental well-being.
  • Public Health Burden: Widespread failures in disease prevention and screening can overwhelm healthcare systems, leading to increased demand for services, higher expenditure, and reduced capacity to manage other health priorities.
