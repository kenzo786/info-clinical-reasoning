Blood group incompatibility in pregnancy (Pregnancy and the puerperium) [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Blood group incompatibility in pregnancy"]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Blood group incompatibility in pregnancy"]
  • Blood group incompatibility in pregnancy, primarily Rhesus D (RhD) incompatibility, occurs when an RhD-negative pregnant individual carries an RhD-positive fetus, leading to the potential for maternal alloimmunisation and subsequent haemolytic disease of the fetus and newborn (HDFN).
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the risk factors for Rhesus D incompatibility, understand the principles of anti-D prophylaxis, and interpret key diagnostic tests such as the Kleihauer test and indirect Coombs test in the context of preventing and managing HDFN.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Blood group incompatibility in pregnancy"]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Blood group incompatibility in pregnancy"]
  • Approximately 15% of the UK population is RhD negative [3]. Rhesus disease is uncommon due to effective anti-D prophylaxis, with the incidence of RhD alloimmunisation in D-negative women following two deliveries of D-positive, ABO-compatible infants dropping from approximately 16% to 2% with routine postpartum anti-D, and further to 0.17-0.28% with routine antenatal prophylaxis [2].
  • Risk factors for sensitisation include any event that allows fetal RhD-positive red blood cells to enter the maternal circulation, such as miscarriage, ectopic pregnancy, termination of pregnancy, antepartum haemorrhage, external cephalic version, abdominal trauma, or invasive prenatal diagnostic procedures [2].
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Blood group incompatibility in pregnancy"]
  • Trigger: Exposure of an RhD-negative pregnant individual to RhD-positive fetal red blood cells, typically during a sensitising event in pregnancy or at delivery, initiates a primary immune response, leading to the production of anti-D antibodies.
  • Progression: In subsequent pregnancies with an RhD-positive fetus, pre-existing maternal anti-D IgG antibodies cross the placenta and bind to fetal RhD-positive red blood cells, marking them for destruction by the fetal reticuloendothelial system. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Haemolytic disease of the fetus and newborn: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Haemolytic disease of the fetus and newborn (HDFN)"]
  • Consequence: Fetal red blood cell destruction results in haemolytic anaemia, which can lead to hydrops fetalis, stillbirth, or severe neonatal jaundice and kernicterus if untreated.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Blood group incompatibility in pregnancy"]
  • Early phase: Initial sensitisation events can occur at any point during pregnancy or at delivery, often without immediate clinical signs in the current pregnancy.
  • Progressive phase: In subsequent pregnancies, if sensitisation has occurred, maternal anti-D antibodies can cross the placenta from approximately 16-20 weeks gestation, with increasing fetal haemolysis as the pregnancy advances.
  • Late phase: The most severe manifestations of HDFN, such as hydrops fetalis, typically develop in the third trimester or perinatally, with neonatal jaundice and anaemia presenting shortly after birth.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Blood group incompatibility in pregnancy"]
  • Ask about history of previous pregnancies - to identify prior sensitising events or previous cases of HDFN.
  • Ask about any vaginal bleeding or abdominal trauma during the current pregnancy - these are potential sensitising events that warrant anti-D prophylaxis.
  • Ask about fetal movements - reduced fetal movements may indicate severe fetal anaemia or hydrops fetalis.
  • Ask about symptoms of neonatal jaundice (e.g., yellowing of skin/eyes, poor feeding, lethargy) - to identify HDFN in the newborn period.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Blood group incompatibility in pregnancy"]
  • Check vital signs (maternal) - usually normal in uncomplicated sensitisation; however, signs of maternal compromise (e.g., hypotension, tachycardia) may indicate a severe sensitising event such as placental abruption.
  • Check fetal ultrasound findings - signs of fetal anaemia (e.g., increased middle cerebral artery peak systolic velocity), hydrops fetalis (e.g., ascites, pleural effusions, skin oedema), or cardiomegaly may be present in severe HDFN.
  • Check neonatal examination - pallor, jaundice, hepatosplenomegaly, and oedema are signs of HDFN in the newborn.
  • Check Kleihauer test results - a positive Kleihauer test indicates fetomaternal haemorrhage, quantifying the volume of fetal blood in maternal circulation and guiding the dose of anti-D immunoglobulin required. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Kleihauer test: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Kleihauer test"]
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Blood group incompatibility in pregnancy"]
  • Reduced fetal movements: Suggests severe fetal compromise, potentially due to severe anaemia from HDFN, requiring urgent assessment and intervention.
  • Hydrops fetalis on ultrasound: Indicates severe fetal anaemia and cardiac decompensation, necessitating immediate specialist obstetric and neonatal review.
  • Rapidly rising bilirubin in neonate: Postnatal, this can lead to kernicterus and irreversible neurological damage if not promptly managed with phototherapy or exchange transfusion.
  • Significant fetomaternal haemorrhage: Identified by a large Kleihauer result, requiring an increased dose of anti-D immunoglobulin to prevent sensitisation.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Blood group incompatibility in pregnancy"]
  • First pregnancy: HDFN is rare in a first pregnancy unless the pregnant individual has been sensitised by a previous transfusion of RhD-positive blood or a sensitising event that went unrecognised.
  • Weak D antigen: Individuals with a weak D phenotype may be mistyped as RhD negative, potentially leading to inappropriate anti-D prophylaxis or lack thereof, which can result in sensitisation. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Weak D antigen: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Weak D antigen"]
  • ABO incompatibility co-existing: ABO incompatibility can sometimes offer a protective effect against RhD sensitisation, as maternal anti-A or anti-B antibodies may destroy fetal RhD-positive red cells before they can sensitise the mother.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Blood group incompatibility in pregnancy"]
  • Core for suspected RhD incompatibility:
    • Maternal blood group and RhD type: Essential for identifying RhD-negative pregnant individuals.
    • Maternal antibody screen (indirect Coombs test): Detects and identifies irregular red cell antibodies in maternal plasma, including anti-D, indicating sensitisation. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Indirect Coombs test: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Indirect Coombs test"]
    • Fetal RhD typing (from maternal plasma): Non-invasive prenatal testing (NIPT) can determine fetal RhD status in RhD-negative pregnant individuals.
  • If a sensitising event occurs:
    • Kleihauer test or flow cytometry: Quantifies the volume of fetomaternal haemorrhage to determine the dose of anti-D immunoglobulin required.
  • Fetal surveillance in sensitised pregnancies:
    • Serial ultrasound with Doppler assessment of middle cerebral artery peak systolic velocity (MCA-PSV): Monitors for fetal anaemia.
    • Amniocentesis for bilirubin levels: Historically used to assess severity of fetal haemolysis, now largely replaced by MCA-PSV.
  • Neonatal investigations:
    • Cord blood group and RhD type, direct Coombs test (DCT), haemoglobin, and bilirubin: Essential for diagnosing HDFN in the newborn.

  • RhD-negative pregnant individual with prior sensitisation, carrying an RhD-positive fetus, concerning for haemolytic disease of the fetus and newborn.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Blood group incompatibility in pregnancy"]
  • Other causes of fetal anaemia: Parvovirus B19 infection, alpha-thalassaemia, fetal haemorrhage from other causes - distinguished by specific serological tests or genetic analysis.
  • Other causes of neonatal jaundice: ABO incompatibility, G6PD deficiency, sepsis, congenital infections, physiological jaundice - differentiated by direct Coombs test, specific enzyme assays, infection screens, and clinical picture. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Kernicterus: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Kernicterus"]
  • Other causes of hydrops fetalis: Fetal cardiac anomalies, chromosomal abnormalities, infections, twin-to-twin transfusion syndrome - identified through detailed fetal anomaly scans and genetic testing.
  • Maternal autoimmune haemolytic anaemia: Maternal antibodies attacking maternal red cells, which can sometimes cross the placenta and affect the fetus - distinguished by maternal direct Coombs test and specific antibody identification.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Blood group incompatibility in pregnancy"]
  • Immediate priorities: For RhD-negative pregnant individuals, timely administration of anti-D immunoglobulin following any sensitising event or as routine antenatal prophylaxis is crucial to prevent sensitisation.
  • Definitive management (for sensitised pregnancies): Intensive fetal surveillance, including serial MCA-PSV Doppler scans, may indicate the need for intrauterine blood transfusions to treat severe fetal anaemia.
  • Postnatal management of HDFN: Neonates with HDFN may require phototherapy for hyperbilirubinaemia, intravenous immunoglobulin (IVIG) to reduce haemolysis, or exchange transfusion in severe cases.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Close monitoring of maternal antibody titres in sensitised pregnancies and neonatal haemoglobin and bilirubin levels post-delivery is essential.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Any signs of fetal compromise (e.g., reduced movements, abnormal Doppler findings) or severe neonatal jaundice warrant immediate specialist consultation with obstetricians, neonatologists, and haematologists.
  • Exam pharmacology reference (UKMLA/MRCGP): Anti-D immunoglobulin is a human plasma-derived product containing IgG anti-D antibodies, which bind to and clear fetal RhD-positive red blood cells from the maternal circulation before an immune response can be mounted. Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Blood group incompatibility in pregnancy"]
  • Fetal anaemia: Occurs in the progressive phase of HDFN as maternal antibodies destroy fetal red blood cells, leading to reduced oxygen-carrying capacity and potential for cardiac strain.
  • Hydrops fetalis: A severe manifestation of fetal anaemia, typically in the late phase, characterised by generalised oedema, effusions, and organ enlargement due to heart failure.
  • Stillbirth: The most severe outcome of untreated or severe HDFN, resulting from profound fetal anaemia and organ failure, often in the late phase of pregnancy.
  • Neonatal jaundice and kernicterus: Postnatally, rapid breakdown of fetal red blood cells leads to unconjugated hyperbilirubinaemia, which can cross the blood-brain barrier and cause irreversible neurological damage (kernicterus) if not promptly treated.
  • Neonatal anaemia: Persists after birth due to ongoing haemolysis and suppressed erythropoiesis, potentially requiring blood transfusions.
