Aortic Aneurysm [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Aortic Aneurysm"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Aortic Aneurysm"][/sc]
  • A permanent and irreversible dilatation of the abdominal aorta to a diameter of 3 cm or greater, or at least 50% of its normal expected diameter. Most commonly occurs below the level of the renal arteries.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the risk factors, presentation, and critical complications of abdominal aortic aneurysm, and understand the principles of screening and management.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Aortic Aneurysm"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Aortic Aneurysm"][/sc]
  • The UK national screening programme for men aged 65 suggests a prevalence of about 1.3% in this population, with the prevalence falling.
  • The prevalence of AAAs is approximately 6 times lower in women, but the rate of aneurysm rupture is significantly higher in women.
  • Risk factors include: male sex, increasing age, tobacco smoking, family history (positive in 10% of patients), severe atherosclerotic damage of the aortic wall, chronic obstructive pulmonary disease, and hyperlipidaemia.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Aortic Aneurysm"][/sc]
  • Trigger: Degradation of elastic lamellae, leukocytic infiltrate, enhanced proteolysis, and smooth muscle cell loss within the aortic wall.
  • Progression: The dilatation affects all three layers of the arterial wall, leading to a gradual increase in aortic diameter (typically 1-6 mm/year).
  • Consequence: Increased wall tension (Laplace\'s Law) as the aneurysm grows, leading to a higher risk of rupture, particularly in larger aneurysms.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Aortic Aneurysm"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Often asymptomatic, with slow, progressive dilatation of the aorta over years, detected incidentally during imaging for other conditions or through screening programmes.
  • Progressive phase: As the aneurysm enlarges, symptoms such as abdominal, back, loin, or groin pain may develop due to pressure on adjacent structures. Growth rate typically accelerates with increasing size.
  • Late phase: Critical events such as rupture can occur suddenly, leading to severe pain, hypotension, and collapse, particularly beyond a diameter of 5.5 cm.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Aortic Aneurysm"][/sc]
  • Ask about abdominal pain - often vague, deep, and central, but can radiate to the back or loin, indicating expansion or impending rupture.
  • Ask about back pain - may be due to erosion of vertebral bodies or pressure on nerves; severe, new-onset lumbar pain is a red flag for impending rupture.
  • Ask about pulsatile sensation in the abdomen - patients may notice a throbbing mass, particularly when lying down.
  • Ask about symptoms of limb ischaemia - distal embolisation from the aneurysm can cause sudden onset of pain, pallor, pulselessness, paraesthesia, and paralysis in the lower limbs.
  • Ask about syncope or collapse - indicative of rupture and significant haemorrhage.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Aortic Aneurysm"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - hypotension and tachycardia are critical indicators of rupture and hypovolaemic shock.
  • Check for a pulsatile abdominal mass - palpable in the supra-umbilical region, sensitivity decreases with increasing waist size. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Pulsatile abdominal mass: clinical significance and examination findings?" text="pulsatile mass"][/sc]
  • Check for abdominal bruit - may be heard on auscultation over the aneurysm.
  • Check for signs of retroperitoneal haemorrhage - Grey Turner\'s sign (flank bruising) or Cullen\'s sign (periumbilical bruising) may indicate rupture. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Retroperitoneal haematoma: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="retroperitoneal haematoma"][/sc]
  • Check for signs of peripheral embolisation - absent pulses, pallor, or coolness in the lower limbs.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Aortic Aneurysm"][/sc]
  • Sudden onset severe abdominal/back pain: Suggests acute expansion or rupture of the aneurysm, requiring immediate medical attention.
  • Hypotension and tachycardia: Indicate significant blood loss, often due to rupture, and necessitate urgent resuscitation and surgical review.
  • Pulsatile abdominal mass with pain: Highly suspicious for an expanding or ruptured AAA, especially in a patient with known aneurysm or risk factors.
  • Syncope or collapse: Often a consequence of massive haemorrhage from a ruptured AAA.
  • Distal ischaemia with AAA: Suggests embolisation from the aneurysm, which can lead to critical limb ischaemia.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Aortic Aneurysm"][/sc]
  • Women: AAAs are less common but rupture at smaller diameters and have a higher mortality rate compared to men.
  • Contained rupture: Rupture into the retroperitoneal space may be temporarily contained, leading to less dramatic initial symptoms and potentially delaying diagnosis.
  • Inflammatory AAA: May present with systemic inflammatory symptoms (e.g., fever, weight loss, elevated inflammatory markers) and can cause ureteric obstruction.
  • Symptoms mimicking renal colic: Pain radiating to the loin or groin can be mistaken for kidney stones, especially if haematuria is present due to ureteric involvement.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Aortic Aneurysm"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected AAA:
    • Abdominal ultrasound: Initial assessment and surveillance, accurate to 3 mm.
    • Full blood count (FBC), clotting screen, renal function, liver function: Baseline assessment and pre-operative planning.
    • Cross-match: Essential if surgery is planned due to potential for significant blood loss.
  • Severity/risk tools:
    • Aneurysm diameter is the primary risk factor for rupture. Surveillance thresholds: [sc name="jitl-query" Q="AAA surveillance thresholds: what are they and what do they mean?" text="surveillance thresholds"][/sc]
      • 3.0-4.4 cm: Annual ultrasound.
      • 4.5-5.4 cm: Three-monthly ultrasound.
      • ≥5.5 cm: Referral to vascular surgeon within 2 weeks to discuss treatment options.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible:
    • ECG and CXR: To rule out cardiac or pulmonary causes of chest/abdominal pain.
    • ESR and/or CRP: If inflammatory AAA is suspected.
  • Imaging - CT angiography: Recommended by NICE for elective patients or those with suspected rupture being considered for repair, providing detailed anatomical information including visceral arteries, mural thrombus, and signs of impending rupture (e.g., crescent sign).
  • Serial reassessment: Regular ultrasound monitoring based on aneurysm size to track growth and guide intervention timing.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Aortic Aneurysm"][/sc]
  • A 72-year-old male with a history of smoking and hypertension presents with sudden onset severe abdominal pain radiating to his back, associated with dizziness and a pulsatile abdominal mass on examination.
  • Elderly male with cardiovascular risk factors presenting with acute abdominal pain and a pulsatile mass, concerning for ruptured abdominal aortic aneurysm.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Aortic Aneurysm"][/sc]
  • Aortic dissection: Typically presents with sudden, severe tearing chest or back pain, often migrating, and may be associated with pulse deficits or new aortic regurgitation. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Aortic dissection vs Aortic Aneurysm: distinguishing features" text="Aortic dissection"][/sc]
  • Renal colic: Characterised by colicky flank pain radiating to the groin, often with haematuria, but usually without a pulsatile abdominal mass or signs of hypovolaemia.
  • Acute pancreatitis: Severe epigastric pain radiating to the back, often associated with nausea, vomiting, and elevated amylase/lipase, but typically no pulsatile mass.
  • Diverticulitis: Localised abdominal pain (often left lower quadrant), fever, and altered bowel habit, usually without signs of hypovolaemia unless complicated by perforation.
  • Mesenteric ischaemia: Severe, disproportionate abdominal pain with minimal physical findings, often in patients with cardiovascular risk factors, but typically no pulsatile mass.
  • Perforated viscus: Sudden onset severe, generalised abdominal pain with peritonism, often with a history of peptic ulcer disease or diverticulitis.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Aortic Aneurysm"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities (for suspected rupture): Recognition, urgent escalation to vascular surgery, immediate resuscitation with intravenous fluids and blood products to maintain haemodynamic stability (permissive hypotension may be considered), and pain control.
  • Definitive management:
    • For ruptured AAA: Emergency surgical repair (open repair or endovascular aneurysm repair [EVAR]) is often warranted.
    • For unruptured AAA (≥5.5 cm or rapidly expanding): Elective surgical repair (open repair or EVAR) is typically considered, balancing rupture risk against surgical risk.
  • Adjunctive management:
    • Smoking cessation: Crucial for reducing aneurysm growth and rupture risk.
    • Rigorous blood pressure control: May support reducing aneurysm expansion.
    • Statin therapy: Often advised due to increased cardiovascular disease risk in AAA patients.
    • Antiplatelet therapy: Considered where appropriate for cardiovascular risk reduction.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Regular ultrasound surveillance for unruptured AAAs (annual for 3.0-4.4 cm, three-monthly for 4.5-5.4 cm).
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Patients with known AAAs should be counselled on symptoms of rupture and advised to seek immediate medical attention if they occur.
  • Exam pharmacology reference (UKMLA/MRCGP):
    • [sc name="jitl-medication" Q="Beta-blocker use in Aortic Aneurysm" text="Beta-blockers"][/sc]: May be considered to reduce aortic wall stress, though evidence for preventing AAA expansion is limited.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Aortic Aneurysm"][/sc]
  • Rupture: The most life-threatening complication, leading to massive haemorrhage (often retroperitoneal or into the peritoneal cavity), hypovolaemic shock, and high mortality, particularly if not rapidly managed.
  • Distal embolisation: Thrombus from the aneurysm sac can dislodge and cause acute limb ischaemia or visceral infarction, presenting as sudden pain and loss of function in the affected area.
  • Aortoenteric fistula: A rare but catastrophic complication where the aneurysm erodes into the gastrointestinal tract (most commonly the duodenum), leading to massive gastrointestinal bleeding.
  • Aortic dissection: While distinct, an aneurysm can predispose to dissection, where blood enters the aortic wall, creating a false lumen and potentially compromising branch vessel perfusion.
  • Ureteric obstruction: Inflammatory AAAs can cause retroperitoneal fibrosis, encasing and obstructing the ureters, leading to hydronephrosis and renal impairment.
