Urinary tract infection (Kidneys and urinary tract) [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Urinary tract infection"]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Urinary tract infection"]
  • A urinary tract infection (UTI) is an infection of any part of the urinary system, most commonly caused by bacteria from the gastrointestinal tract, such as Escherichia coli [1].
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical features of urinary tract infection, differentiate between uncomplicated and complicated presentations, and understand initial management principles.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Urinary tract infection"]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Urinary tract infection"]
  • Urinary tract infections are common, with 1 in 2 UK women experiencing at least one UTI in their lifetime, compared to around 1 in 7 men [2].
  • Recurrent UTIs affect approximately 18% of women who have had a previous UTI [2].
  • Incidence of UTI increases with age, particularly in women aged 65–74 years [3].
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Urinary tract infection"]
  • Trigger: Uropathogenic bacteria, most commonly Escherichia coli, ascend from the periurethral area into the bladder, often facilitated by shorter urethral length in women.
  • Progression: Bacteria adhere to urothelial cells via fimbriae, multiply, and evade host defences, leading to inflammation of the bladder (cystitis) or ascending to the kidneys (pyelonephritis). [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Bacterial virulence factors in UTI: what are they and why do they matter clinically?" text="Bacterial virulence factors"]
  • Consequence: Localised inflammation results in symptoms such as dysuria and frequency; systemic infection (pyelonephritis) can lead to fever, loin pain, and potentially sepsis.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Urinary tract infection"]
  • Early phase: Symptoms of uncomplicated lower UTI typically develop over hours to a day, with dysuria and frequency being prominent.
  • Progressive phase: If untreated or if the infection ascends, symptoms may worsen over 1-3 days, potentially leading to systemic features of pyelonephritis such as fever and loin pain.
  • Late phase: Complications such as sepsis or renal damage can occur particularly beyond 3-5 days in severe or untreated cases, or in vulnerable populations.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Urinary tract infection"]
  • Ask about dysuria - often described as a burning sensation during micturition, indicating urethral or bladder irritation.
  • Ask about increased urinary frequency and urgency - reflecting bladder irritability and reduced functional capacity.
  • Ask about suprapubic pain - suggesting bladder inflammation (cystitis).
  • Ask about haematuria - visible blood in urine, which can occur due to inflammation of the urinary tract lining.
  • Ask about new onset or worsening urinary incontinence - particularly relevant in older adults where this may be the primary symptom.
  • Ask about loin pain or flank tenderness - a key discriminator for upper urinary tract involvement (pyelonephritis).
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Urinary tract infection"]
  • Check vital signs - fever, tachycardia, or hypotension may indicate systemic infection or sepsis, particularly in pyelonephritis.
  • Check abdominal examination - suprapubic tenderness may be present in cystitis.
  • Check costovertebral angle tenderness - a specific sign for pyelonephritis, indicating renal inflammation. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Costovertebral angle tenderness: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Costovertebral angle tenderness"]
  • Check urinalysis (dipstick) - positive for nitrites (bacterial presence) and leucocyte esterase (pyuria), highly suggestive of UTI. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Urinary dipstick interpretation in UTI: what are the key findings and their significance?" text="Urinary dipstick interpretation"]
  • Check urine microscopy - presence of white blood cells and bacteria confirms infection.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Urinary tract infection"]
  • Sepsis: Clinical signs of systemic inflammatory response (e.g., fever, tachycardia, tachypnoea, altered mental status) indicate severe infection requiring urgent assessment and management.
  • Acute kidney injury: Deterioration in renal function, particularly in the context of obstructive uropathy, suggests a need for urgent investigation and intervention.
  • Obstructive uropathy: Symptoms or signs suggestive of urinary tract obstruction (e.g., anuria, severe loin pain with renal angle tenderness) necessitate urgent imaging and urological review.
  • Immunosuppression: Patients who are immunocompromised (e.g., diabetes, chemotherapy, transplant recipients) are at higher risk of severe or atypical presentations and require prompt, aggressive management.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Urinary tract infection"]
  • Older adults: May present with non-specific symptoms such as confusion, falls, or general malaise without classic urinary symptoms, making diagnosis challenging.
  • Men: UTI is less common in men and often indicates an underlying structural or functional abnormality of the urinary tract, or a sexually transmitted infection.
  • Catheterised patients: Symptoms may be subtle or absent, with fever or systemic signs being the primary indicators of infection; bacteriuria is common and not always indicative of infection. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Catheter-associated UTI: what are the diagnostic criteria and management principles?" text="Catheter-associated UTI"]
  • Pregnancy: Asymptomatic bacteriuria is common and requires screening and treatment due to increased risk of pyelonephritis and adverse pregnancy outcomes.
  • Children: Infants and young children may present with non-specific symptoms like fever, vomiting, poor feeding, or irritability; diagnosis requires high index of suspicion.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Urinary tract infection"]
  • Core for suspected UTI: Urine dipstick for nitrites and leucocyte esterase; midstream urine (MSU) culture and sensitivity for confirmation and antimicrobial guidance.
  • Severity/risk tools: Consider using a severity assessment tool for pyelonephritis, such as the CURB-65 score (though primarily for pneumonia, principles of systemic infection apply) or a dedicated pyelonephritis severity score if available. CURB-65 Score on MDCalc
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Consider sexually transmitted infection (STI) screening, vaginal swabs, or imaging for renal calculi.
  • Imaging - Ultrasound: Renal ultrasound may be indicated in complicated UTI (e.g., men, recurrent UTI, pyelonephritis, suspected obstruction) to assess for structural abnormalities or complications.
  • Serial reassessment: Clinical review and repeat urine cultures may be necessary if symptoms persist or worsen despite initial treatment. Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Urinary tract infection"]
  • A 28-year-old female presents with a 24-hour history of dysuria, increased urinary frequency, and suprapubic discomfort, with a positive urine dipstick for nitrites and leucocyte esterase.
  • Young female with acute onset lower urinary tract symptoms, concerning for uncomplicated cystitis.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Urinary tract infection"]
  • Vaginitis/Cervicitis - often associated with vaginal discharge, itching, or odour, unlike UTI.
  • Sexually Transmitted Infection (STI) - may present with dysuria and frequency but often accompanied by urethral discharge or genital lesions.
  • Urethritis - inflammation of the urethra, typically causing dysuria but without significant bacteriuria or pyuria on MSU.
  • Interstitial Cystitis/Bladder Pain Syndrome - chronic bladder pain and urinary symptoms without evidence of infection.
  • Pelvic Inflammatory Disease (PID) - typically presents with lower abdominal pain, fever, and vaginal discharge, often with cervical motion tenderness.
  • Ectopic Pregnancy - severe lower abdominal pain, often unilateral, with a positive pregnancy test, requiring urgent exclusion in women of childbearing age.
  • Renal Calculi - sudden onset severe loin pain radiating to the groin, often with haematuria, but typically without infective symptoms unless complicated. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Differentiating UTI from renal colic: what are the key clinical features?" text="Renal colic"]
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Urinary tract infection"]
  • Immediate priorities: Recognition of severe infection (e.g., sepsis, pyelonephritis), escalation to senior medical staff, and supportive care (e.g., analgesia, hydration).
  • Definitive management: Antimicrobial therapy guided by local resistance patterns and urine culture results; often includes empirical antibiotics initially.
  • Adjunctive management: Adequate fluid intake, regular bladder emptying, and simple analgesia (e.g., paracetamol, ibuprofen) may support symptom relief.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Clinical review to ensure symptom resolution; repeat urine culture in specific groups (e.g., pregnant women, recurrent UTI, persistent symptoms).
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Advise patients on symptoms warranting re-consultation (e.g., worsening symptoms, fever, loin pain); consider referral for recurrent or complicated cases.
  • Exam pharmacology reference (UKMLA/MRCGP): Common antibiotics for UTI often include trimethoprim, nitrofurantoin, and pivmecillinam for uncomplicated lower UTIs. For pyelonephritis, broader spectrum agents such as co-amoxiclav or cefalexin may be considered, or ciprofloxacin if local resistance patterns allow. Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Urinary tract infection"]
  • Pyelonephritis: Ascending infection to the kidneys, presenting with fever, loin pain, and systemic symptoms, potentially leading to renal scarring or impaired renal function if recurrent or severe.
  • Sepsis: Systemic inflammatory response to infection, a life-threatening condition requiring urgent medical intervention, particularly in vulnerable patients or those with complicated UTIs.
  • Renal abscess: Localised collection of pus within the kidney, a rare but serious complication of pyelonephritis, often requiring drainage and prolonged antibiotics.
  • Recurrent UTI: Defined as two or more UTIs in six months or three or more in a year, indicating a need for further investigation and preventative strategies.
  • Preterm labour/low birth weight: In pregnant women, untreated UTI or asymptomatic bacteriuria significantly increases the risk of adverse pregnancy outcomes.
  • Chronic kidney disease: Repeated episodes of pyelonephritis, especially in childhood or with underlying structural abnormalities, can contribute to long-term renal damage.
