Human papilloma virus infection [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Human papilloma virus infection"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Human papilloma virus infection"][/sc]
  • Human papilloma virus (HPV) infection is a common viral infection that can affect the skin and mucous membranes, primarily transmitted through sexual contact.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical manifestations of HPV infection, understand its link to cervical cancer and other anogenital cancers, and be aware of national screening and vaccination programmes.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Human papilloma virus infection"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Human papilloma virus infection"][/sc]
  • HPV is the most common viral sexually transmitted infection in the UK; most sexually active individuals will acquire HPV at some point in their lives.
  • High-risk HPV types (e.g., HPV 16 and 18) are detected in 99% of cervical cancers, accounting for at least two-thirds of cases.
  • The UK HPV vaccination programme (Gardasil 9) offers protection against 9 HPV types, including high-risk types 16, 18, 31, 33, 45, 52, 58 and low-risk types 6, 11.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Human papilloma virus infection"][/sc]
  • Initial infection typically occurs through micro-abrasions in the skin or mucous membranes during sexual contact, allowing the virus to infect basal epithelial cells.
  • Persistent infection with high-risk HPV types can lead to integration of viral DNA into the host genome, expressing oncogenes (E6 and E7) that disrupt cell cycle regulation and promote uncontrolled cell growth.
  • This cellular dysregulation can progress from low-grade squamous intraepithelial lesions (LSIL) to high-grade squamous intraepithelial lesions (HSIL), and eventually to invasive carcinoma, particularly in the cervix. Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Human papilloma virus infection"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Most HPV infections are asymptomatic and resolve spontaneously within 6-24 months due to immune clearance.
  • Progressive phase: A small proportion of high-risk HPV infections persist beyond 2 years, increasing the risk of developing precancerous lesions (e.g., CIN) over several years.
  • Late phase: If persistent high-risk HPV infection and precancerous lesions are not detected and treated, progression to invasive cancer can occur over 10-20 years.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Human papilloma virus infection"][/sc]
  • Ask about visible lesions - many HPV infections are asymptomatic, but the presence of genital warts (caused by low-risk HPV types) is a common presenting symptom.
  • Ask about abnormal vaginal bleeding or discharge - these may indicate advanced cervical changes or cancer, particularly intermenstrual, post-coital, or post-menopausal bleeding.
  • Ask about pruritus or discomfort - genital warts can cause itching, irritation, or mild pain, especially if large or in sensitive areas.
  • Ask about sexual history and partners - crucial for understanding exposure risk and for contact tracing in sexually transmitted infections.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Human papilloma virus infection"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - typically normal in uncomplicated HPV infection or genital warts; abnormal vitals may suggest secondary infection or advanced malignancy.
  • Check for genital warts - characteristic fleshy, cauliflower-like, papular, or flat lesions on the vulva, penis, perineum, anus, or perianal region.
  • Check for cervical abnormalities on speculum examination - visual inspection may reveal abnormal vascular patterns, ulceration, or exophytic growths suggestive of CIN or cancer. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Colposcopy: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Colposcopy"][/sc]
  • Check for lymphadenopathy - regional lymph node enlargement may indicate metastatic spread in advanced HPV-related cancers.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Human papilloma virus infection"][/sc]
  • Persistent abnormal vaginal bleeding: particularly intermenstrual, post-coital, or post-menopausal bleeding, which can be a symptom of cervical cancer.
  • Unexplained weight loss or fatigue: may indicate advanced malignancy, especially in the context of persistent HPV infection.
  • Palpable pelvic mass: suggests advanced cervical cancer with local spread or other pelvic pathology.
  • Hydronephrosis: can occur due to ureteric compression by advanced cervical tumours, indicating significant local disease.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Human papilloma virus infection"][/sc]
  • Immunosuppressed individuals (e.g., HIV, transplant recipients): may experience more persistent HPV infections, faster progression to high-grade lesions, and more extensive or recalcitrant genital warts.
  • Men: HPV infection can cause penile, anal, and oropharyngeal cancers, often presenting with non-healing lesions or persistent symptoms in these areas.
  • Children: recurrent respiratory papillomatosis (RRP) is a rare but serious condition caused by HPV (usually types 6 and 11) acquired perinatally, presenting with hoarseness and respiratory distress.
  • Older adults: may present with advanced HPV-related cancers due to delayed diagnosis or lack of prior screening, with symptoms often attributed to other age-related conditions.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Human papilloma virus infection"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected cervical HPV-related disease: Cervical screening (smear test) with primary HPV testing for women aged 25-64 in the UK. If high-risk HPV is detected, cytology is performed.
  • Further investigation for abnormal cytology: Colposcopy with directed biopsies to assess for cervical intraepithelial neoplasia (CIN) or invasive cancer. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Cervical screening programme UK: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Cervical screening programme UK"][/sc]
  • Diagnosis of genital warts: Clinical diagnosis based on characteristic appearance; biopsy may be considered for atypical, persistent, or ulcerated lesions.
  • Imaging - MRI/CT: For staging of confirmed HPV-related cancers (e.g., cervical, anal, or oropharyngeal cancer) to assess local invasion and metastatic spread.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Human papilloma virus infection"][/sc]
  • A 32-year-old sexually active woman presents with post-coital bleeding and a recent cervical screening result showing high-risk HPV positive with abnormal cytology.
  • Sexually transmitted infection with oncogenic potential, concerning for cervical intraepithelial neoplasia.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Human papilloma virus infection"][/sc]
  • Genital warts: often confused with other benign skin lesions like skin tags, molluscum contagiosum, or sebaceous cysts.
  • Cervical cancer: other causes of abnormal vaginal bleeding include cervicitis, endometrial polyps, fibroids, or other gynaecological malignancies.
  • Vulval/vaginal intraepithelial neoplasia (VIN/VaIN): similar to CIN, these precancerous lesions can be caused by HPV and require biopsy for definitive diagnosis.
  • Squamous cell carcinoma (non-HPV related): can occur in anogenital regions but is less common than HPV-related cancers and typically affects older individuals.
  • Syphilis (secondary stage): condylomata lata can mimic genital warts but are typically flat, moist, and greyish-white, and are associated with other systemic symptoms. Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Human papilloma virus infection"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: For suspected high-grade cervical lesions or cancer, prompt referral to gynaecology/colposcopy services is essential for further assessment and management.
  • Definitive management for precancerous lesions: Treatment for CIN typically involves excisional procedures (e.g., LLETZ - Large Loop Excision of the Transformation Zone) or ablative therapies to remove abnormal cells.
  • Management of genital warts: Options include topical treatments (e.g., imiquimod, podophyllotoxin), cryotherapy, surgical excision, or laser therapy, depending on size, number, and location.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Regular cervical screening and colposcopy follow-up are crucial after treatment for CIN to detect recurrence or new lesions.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Patients should be advised on safe sexual practices, including condom use, and encouraged to complete the HPV vaccination if eligible.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Human papilloma virus infection"][/sc]
  • Cervical cancer: The most significant long-term complication of persistent high-risk HPV infection, developing over 10-20 years if precancerous lesions are untreated.
  • Other anogenital cancers: HPV can cause cancers of the vulva, vagina, penis, and anus, particularly in individuals with persistent high-risk HPV infection.
  • Oropharyngeal cancer: Increasingly recognised as an HPV-related malignancy, often presenting with persistent sore throat, dysphagia, or neck lump.
  • Psychological distress: Diagnosis of HPV infection or related conditions can lead to anxiety, depression, and impact on sexual health and relationships.
  • Recurrent respiratory papillomatosis (RRP): A rare but serious complication in children, leading to hoarseness and airway obstruction, requiring repeated surgical debulking.
