Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Transplantation (including graft rejection and immunosuppression)"][/sc]
  • Transplantation involves replacing a diseased or damaged organ or tissue with a healthy one from a donor. Graft rejection is the immune response of the recipient's body against the transplanted organ, which can be acute or chronic. Immunosuppression is the use of medications to prevent or treat graft rejection by suppressing the immune system.
  • UKMLA Learning Outcome: Understand the principles of transplantation, graft rejection, and immunosuppression, including their clinical manifestations and management.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Transplantation (including graft rejection and immunosuppression)"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Transplantation (including graft rejection and immunosuppression)"][/sc]
  • Organ transplantation rates in the UK vary by organ, with kidney transplants being the most common, followed by liver, heart, and lung transplants.
  • Acute rejection affects a significant proportion of transplant recipients, particularly in the first year, despite immunosuppressive therapy.
  • Chronic graft dysfunction, often due to chronic rejection, remains a leading cause of long-term graft loss.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Transplantation (including graft rejection and immunosuppression)"][/sc]
  • Trigger: The recipient's immune system recognises donor antigens (e.g., HLA molecules) on the transplanted organ as foreign.
  • Progression: This recognition initiates a complex immune response involving T-cells, B-cells, and antibodies, leading to inflammation and damage within the graft. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Graft rejection: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Graft rejection"][/sc]
  • Consequence: Uncontrolled immune activation results in graft dysfunction or failure, necessitating increased immunosuppression or re-transplantation. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Immunosuppression: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Immunosuppression"][/sc]
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Transplantation (including graft rejection and immunosuppression)"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Hyperacute rejection (minutes to hours, rare with modern cross-matching), acute cellular or humoral rejection (days to months post-transplant).
  • Progressive phase: Subclinical rejection or ongoing low-grade immune activity, potentially leading to chronic changes.
  • Late phase: Chronic graft dysfunction or rejection, typically developing months to years after transplantation, characterised by progressive fibrosis and organ damage.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Transplantation (including graft rejection and immunosuppression)"][/sc]
  • Ask about fever and malaise - non-specific indicators that may suggest infection or acute rejection.
  • Ask about pain or tenderness over the transplanted organ - can be a direct sign of inflammation or rejection.
  • Ask about changes in graft function (e.g., reduced urine output, jaundice, dyspnoea) - direct evidence of organ dysfunction.
  • Ask about new onset or worsening oedema - may indicate fluid retention due to renal or cardiac graft dysfunction.
  • Ask about gastrointestinal upset (nausea, vomiting, diarrhoea) - common side effects of immunosuppressants or signs of infection.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Transplantation (including graft rejection and immunosuppression)"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs (temperature, blood pressure, heart rate, respiratory rate) - to identify systemic illness, infection, or haemodynamic instability.
  • Check for graft tenderness or swelling on palpation - suggests inflammation or fluid accumulation around the transplanted organ.
  • Check for signs of organ dysfunction (e.g., jaundice, ascites, crackles on lung auscultation, peripheral oedema) - indicates impaired graft function.
  • Check for lymphadenopathy or rash - may suggest viral infection (e.g., CMV, EBV) or drug reaction.
  • Check for signs of infection (e.g., purulent discharge, cellulitis) - immunosuppressed patients are highly susceptible.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Transplantation (including graft rejection and immunosuppression)"][/sc]
  • Sudden deterioration in graft function - requires urgent investigation for acute rejection or other complications.
  • High-grade fever with systemic symptoms - suggests severe infection, potentially sepsis, in an immunocompromised patient.
  • Haemodynamic instability - indicates critical illness, possibly septic shock or severe haemorrhage.
  • New onset neurological symptoms - may signify CNS infection, post-transplant lymphoproliferative disorder (PTLD), or drug toxicity.
  • Severe pain at the graft site - could indicate graft rupture, infarction, or severe acute rejection.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Transplantation (including graft rejection and immunosuppression)"][/sc]
  • Elderly recipients: May present with more subtle symptoms of rejection or infection, often lacking typical inflammatory responses.
  • Paediatric recipients: Symptoms can be non-specific, such as irritability, poor feeding, or failure to thrive, making diagnosis challenging.
  • Patients on high-dose immunosuppression: May have blunted immune responses, leading to atypical or delayed presentation of infections or rejection.
  • Recipients of specific organs: Liver transplant rejection may present with cholestasis, while lung transplant rejection might manifest as progressive dyspnoea.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Transplantation (including graft rejection and immunosuppression)"][/sc]
  • Core investigations: Full blood count (FBC), urea and electrolytes (U&Es), liver function tests (LFTs), C-reactive protein (CRP), immunosuppressant drug levels.
  • Graft-specific function tests: Serum creatinine and urine output for kidney, bilirubin and transaminases for liver, spirometry for lung, echocardiogram for heart.
  • Infection screening: Blood cultures, urine cultures, viral PCR (e.g., CMV, EBV, BK virus), chest X-ray.
  • Imaging: Ultrasound with Doppler of the graft, CT scan, MRI to assess graft perfusion, anatomy, and rule out complications.
  • Definitive diagnosis: Graft biopsy remains the gold standard for diagnosing rejection, allowing histological assessment and grading.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Transplantation (including graft rejection and immunosuppression)"][/sc]
  • A post-transplant patient presenting with new onset fever, graft tenderness, and declining organ function, raising concern for acute rejection or infection.
  • An immunocompromised individual with a history of organ transplantation experiencing insidious fatigue and progressive graft dysfunction, suggesting chronic rejection or opportunistic infection.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Transplantation (including graft rejection and immunosuppression)"][/sc]
  • Acute graft rejection: Immune-mediated attack on the transplanted organ, often presenting with fever, pain, and declining function.
  • Infection (bacterial, viral, fungal): Common in immunosuppressed patients, can mimic rejection and cause systemic symptoms and graft dysfunction.
  • Drug toxicity (e.g., calcineurin inhibitors): Can cause nephrotoxicity, neurotoxicity, or other organ damage, mimicking rejection.
  • Recurrence of original disease: The primary disease that necessitated transplantation may recur in the graft.
  • Surgical complications: Vascular thrombosis, anastomotic leak, ureteric obstruction (in kidney transplant) can cause acute graft dysfunction.
  • Chronic graft dysfunction/rejection: Progressive decline in graft function over time due to chronic immune injury and fibrosis.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Transplantation (including graft rejection and immunosuppression)"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Stabilise the patient, assess graft function, rule out infection, and consider urgent graft biopsy if rejection is suspected.
  • Definitive management: Treatment of acute rejection often involves pulse corticosteroids, antithymocyte globulin (ATG), or other potent immunosuppressants. Adjustment of maintenance immunosuppression is crucial.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Regular monitoring of graft function, immunosuppressant drug levels, and screening for infections and malignancies.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Educate patients on red flag symptoms, ensure adherence to medication, and provide clear instructions on when to seek urgent medical attention.
  • Exam pharmacology reference: Immunosuppressive agents (e.g., ciclosporin, tacrolimus, mycophenolate mofetil, sirolimus, azathioprine, prednisolone) are critical for preventing and treating rejection. Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Transplantation (including graft rejection and immunosuppression)"][/sc]
  • Infections: Immunosuppression significantly increases the risk of bacterial, viral (e.g., CMV, EBV, BK virus), and fungal infections, which can be life-threatening.
  • Malignancies: Long-term immunosuppression is associated with an increased risk of certain cancers, including skin cancers, post-transplant lymphoproliferative disorder (PTLD), and Kaposi's sarcoma.
  • Cardiovascular disease: Transplant recipients have a higher incidence of hypertension, dyslipidaemia, and diabetes, contributing to accelerated atherosclerosis and cardiovascular events.
  • Renal dysfunction: Calcineurin inhibitor nephrotoxicity is a common long-term complication, leading to chronic kidney disease even in non-kidney transplant recipients.
  • Graft failure: Ultimately, chronic rejection or other complications can lead to irreversible graft damage and the need for re-transplantation or alternative therapies.
  • Metabolic complications: New-onset diabetes after transplant (NODAT), osteoporosis, and electrolyte disturbances are frequently observed.
