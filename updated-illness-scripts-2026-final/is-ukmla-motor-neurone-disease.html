Motor neurone disease [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Motor neurone disease"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Motor neurone disease"][/sc]
  • Motor neurone disease (MND) is a progressive neurodegenerative condition affecting upper and lower motor neurones, leading to muscle weakness, wasting, and paralysis.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical features, diagnostic approach, and multidisciplinary management of motor neurone disease, including palliative care considerations.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Motor neurone disease"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Motor neurone disease"][/sc]
  • MND has an incidence of approximately 2 per 100,000 people per year in the UK, with a prevalence of around 7-9 per 100,000 (NICE).
  • The condition typically affects individuals aged 50-70 years, with a slight male predominance, though it can occur at any age.
  • Risk factors include increasing age, male sex, and a family history of MND in about 5-10% of cases (familial MND).
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Motor neurone disease"][/sc]
  • The trigger for MND is often unknown, but involves a complex interplay of genetic and environmental factors leading to motor neurone damage.
  • Progression involves the degeneration and death of upper motor neurones (in the brain and spinal cord) and lower motor neurones (in the brainstem and spinal cord), disrupting nerve signals to muscles.
  • The consequence is progressive muscle weakness, atrophy, fasciculations, and spasticity, ultimately leading to respiratory failure.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Motor neurone disease"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Often insidious onset with focal muscle weakness, cramps, or fasciculations, which may be mistaken for benign conditions.
  • Progressive phase: Symptoms gradually spread to other muscle groups, leading to increasing disability, particularly beyond 6-12 months from onset.
  • Late phase: Characterised by severe generalised weakness, dysphagia, dysarthria, and respiratory compromise, typically within 2-5 years of diagnosis.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Motor neurone disease"][/sc]
  • Ask about muscle weakness - often asymmetrical and progressive, affecting limbs, bulbar muscles (speech, swallowing), or respiratory muscles.
  • Ask about muscle cramps and fasciculations - involuntary muscle twitching, which can be an early and persistent symptom.
  • Ask about changes in speech (dysarthria) or swallowing (dysphagia) - indicating bulbar involvement, which can lead to aspiration.
  • Ask about stiffness or spasticity - suggesting upper motor neurone involvement, contributing to difficulty with movement.
  • Ask about emotional lability - unprovoked crying or laughing, which can be a feature of bulbar MND.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Motor neurone disease"][/sc]
  • Check for a combination of upper motor neurone signs (spasticity, hyperreflexia, Babinski's sign) and lower motor neurone signs (muscle wasting, fasciculations, hypotonia) - this combination is highly suggestive of MND.
  • Check muscle power and tone - to identify focal or generalised weakness and differentiate between flaccid and spastic paralysis.
  • Check bulbar function - assessing speech clarity, tongue fasciculations, and gag reflex to detect dysphagia and dysarthria.
  • Check respiratory function - observing for signs of respiratory distress, paradoxical breathing, or reduced vital capacity.
  • Check vital signs - particularly respiratory rate and oxygen saturation, as respiratory compromise is a significant concern.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Motor neurone disease"][/sc]
  • Progressive, asymmetrical weakness - particularly if affecting multiple limbs or bulbar muscles, without sensory loss.
  • Combination of upper and lower motor neurone signs - in the same anatomical region or across different regions.
  • Persistent fasciculations - especially if widespread and associated with weakness or wasting.
  • Rapidly progressive dysphagia or dysarthria - indicating bulbar onset MND, which often has a poorer prognosis.
  • Respiratory insufficiency - presenting as orthopnoea, morning headaches, or daytime somnolence, suggesting diaphragmatic weakness.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Motor neurone disease"][/sc]
  • Primary Lateral Sclerosis (PLS): Predominantly upper motor neurone signs with spasticity and hyperreflexia, without significant muscle wasting or fasciculations. Progression is typically slower.
  • Progressive Muscular Atrophy (PMA): Predominantly lower motor neurone signs with muscle wasting and fasciculations, without significant spasticity or hyperreflexia. Can have a more benign course.
  • Progressive Bulbar Palsy (PBP): Initial presentation with dysarthria and dysphagia due to bulbar muscle weakness, often progressing rapidly to respiratory compromise.
  • Younger onset MND: Presentation before the age of 45, which may have a slower progression and a higher likelihood of genetic mutations.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Motor neurone disease"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected Motor Neurone Disease: Electromyography (EMG) and nerve conduction studies (NCS) to demonstrate widespread denervation and reinnervation, confirming lower motor neurone involvement and excluding other neuropathies.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Blood tests (e.g., creatine kinase, thyroid function, vitamin B12, autoantibodies) to exclude mimic conditions such as multifocal motor neuropathy.
  • Imaging - Magnetic Resonance Imaging (MRI) of brain and spinal cord: To exclude structural lesions (e.g., cervical myelopathy, brain tumours) that can mimic MND.
  • Serial reassessment: Clinical review over time to observe progression of symptoms and signs, which is crucial for diagnosis as there is no single definitive test.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Motor neurone disease"][/sc]
  • A 62-year-old male presents with a 6-month history of progressive weakness in his right hand, associated with muscle twitching and occasional difficulty articulating words. On examination, he has wasting and fasciculations in the right hand, brisk reflexes in the lower limbs, and a positive Babinski's sign.
  • This is a middle-aged male with subacute, progressive, asymmetrical motor weakness, combining both upper and lower motor neurone signs, raising concern for a neurodegenerative process.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Motor neurone disease"][/sc]
  • Cervical myelopathy - often presents with sensory symptoms and may have a clear spinal cord compression on MRI.
  • Multifocal motor neuropathy - characterised by conduction blocks on NCS and often responds to immunotherapy.
  • Myasthenia gravis - fluctuating weakness, typically worse with exertion, and often involves ocular or bulbar muscles, responding to anticholinesterase inhibitors.
  • Inclusion body myositis - slowly progressive muscle weakness and wasting, often asymmetrical, with characteristic pathological findings on muscle biopsy.
  • Spinal muscular atrophy (adult onset) - purely lower motor neurone syndrome, often with a more benign and slower progression.
  • Peripheral neuropathy - typically involves sensory symptoms and follows a 'glove and stocking' distribution.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Motor neurone disease"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Referral to a specialist MND multidisciplinary team for comprehensive assessment and management planning.
  • Definitive management: Riluzole may slow disease progression and extend survival by a few months. Edaravone is also an option for some patients. Symptomatic management is key.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Regular review by the MND multidisciplinary team, including neurologists, physiotherapists, occupational therapists, speech and language therapists, dietitians, and palliative care specialists.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Early discussion about advance care planning, including non-invasive ventilation and feeding options, as respiratory and swallowing difficulties progress.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Motor neurone disease"][/sc]
  • Respiratory failure: Due to progressive weakness of the diaphragm and intercostal muscles, leading to hypoventilation, particularly beyond 1-2 years from diagnosis, and is the most common cause of death.
  • Aspiration pneumonia: Resulting from dysphagia and impaired cough reflex, increasing the risk of chest infections and hospitalisation.
  • Malnutrition and dehydration: Due to severe dysphagia, leading to weight loss and electrolyte imbalances, impacting overall prognosis.
  • Deep vein thrombosis (DVT) and pulmonary embolism (PE): Increased risk due to immobility and reduced physical activity in later stages.
  • Pressure ulcers: Develop due to prolonged immobility and poor nutritional status, requiring careful skin care and repositioning.
