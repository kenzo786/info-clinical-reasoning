Septic arthritis [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Septic arthritis"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Septic arthritis"][/sc]
  • Septic arthritis is a serious infection within a joint, leading to inflammation and potential rapid joint destruction if not promptly treated [1].
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical features of septic arthritis, understand its urgent management principles, and differentiate it from other acute arthropathies.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Septic arthritis"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Septic arthritis"][/sc]
  • _Staphylococcus aureus_ is the most common causative pathogen across all age groups, primarily meticillin-sensitive strains [2].
  • The incidence of septic arthritis in the UK has been reported to be increasing, with rates rising from 5.5/100,000 in 1998 to 7.8/100,000 in 2013. Rates are increasing most rapidly in those over 75 years of age [4].
  • The incidence of prosthetic joint infection ranges from 1.5-2.5% for primary interventions and can be up to 20% for revision procedures [2].
  • Risk factors include increasing age, diabetes mellitus, pre-existing joint damage (e.g., rheumatoid arthritis, gout), recent joint surgery, prosthetic joints, skin infections, immunodeficiency (e.g., HIV), intravenous drug use, and gonorrhoea [1, 2].
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Septic arthritis"][/sc]
  • Bacteria typically gain access to the joint space via haematogenous spread from a distant infection, direct inoculation (e.g., trauma, surgery, injection), or contiguous spread from osteomyelitis [1].
  • Once in the synovial fluid, bacteria proliferate, triggering an intense inflammatory response involving neutrophils and cytokines, leading to enzymatic degradation of articular cartilage and subchondral bone [2].
  • The rapid destruction of cartilage, potentially occurring within hours, can result in irreversible joint damage, loss of function, and long-term morbidity if not treated urgently [3]. Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Septic arthritis"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Symptoms typically develop acutely over a few days, characterised by sudden onset of severe joint pain, swelling, and warmth [1].
  • Progressive phase: Without prompt intervention, joint destruction can begin within hours to days, leading to increasing pain, restricted movement, and systemic signs of infection such as fever and rigors [3].
  • Late phase: Delayed or inadequate treatment can result in irreversible joint damage, chronic pain, osteomyelitis, sepsis, and even death, particularly beyond 72 hours from symptom onset [3].
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Septic arthritis"][/sc]
  • Ask about severe, sudden-onset joint pain - often monoarticular and exquisitely tender, differentiating it from chronic arthropathies [1].
  • Ask about joint swelling and warmth - indicative of inflammation and effusion within the joint capsule [1].
  • Ask about inability or reluctance to move the affected joint (pseudoparalysis) - a key discriminator, as any movement exacerbates pain [2].
  • Ask about systemic symptoms such as fever, chills, or rigors - suggesting bacteraemia, though their absence does not rule out septic arthritis [1, 2].
  • Ask about recent trauma, surgery, or injections to the joint - potential direct routes for infection [1].
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Septic arthritis"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - fever, tachycardia, and hypotension may indicate systemic sepsis [1].
  • Check for a hot, swollen, exquisitely tender joint - the classic presentation, with pain on both active and passive movement [1, 2].
  • Check for erythema and effusion - visible redness and palpable fluid accumulation in the joint space [1].
  • Check for restricted range of motion - often severe and painful, distinguishing it from conditions like cellulitis [2]. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Synovial fluid white blood cell count in septic arthritis: what is its diagnostic significance?" text="Synovial fluid WBC >50,000/mm³"][/sc]
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Septic arthritis"][/sc]
  • Rapidly worsening joint pain and swelling: Suggests aggressive infection and impending joint destruction, requiring immediate intervention.
  • Systemic signs of sepsis (e.g., high fever, rigors, hypotension, altered mental status): Indicates potential for widespread infection and organ dysfunction, necessitating urgent resuscitation and broad-spectrum antibiotics.
  • Inability to weight-bear or move the affected limb: Points to severe joint inflammation and pain, often seen in advanced septic arthritis.
  • Immunocompromised patient with new joint symptoms: Higher risk of atypical presentation and rapid progression, requiring a lower threshold for investigation.
  • Failure to improve within 24-48 hours of initial treatment: May indicate resistant organism, inadequate drainage, or alternative diagnosis, warranting re-evaluation and further investigation.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Septic arthritis"][/sc]
  • Elderly or immunocompromised patients: May present with less pronounced pain, minimal fever, or subtle joint signs, leading to delayed diagnosis due to blunted inflammatory response [2].
  • Patients with pre-existing arthropathy (e.g., rheumatoid arthritis, gout): Superimposed septic arthritis can be challenging to diagnose as baseline inflammation masks typical features; a sudden worsening of symptoms or monoarticular flare should raise suspicion [2].
  • Polyarticular septic arthritis: While typically monoarticular, _S. aureus_ can cause polyarticular infection, mimicking inflammatory arthropathies [2].
  • Axial joint involvement (e.g., sacroiliac, sternoclavicular): Pain may be referred or non-specific, making localisation difficult and often requiring advanced imaging for diagnosis [3]. Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Septic arthritis"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected septic arthritis:
    • Blood tests: Full blood count (FBC) for leukocytosis, C-reactive protein (CRP) and erythrocyte sedimentation rate (ESR) for inflammatory markers, blood cultures before antibiotics [2, 3].
    • Synovial fluid analysis (arthrocentesis is the gold standard): Gram stain, cell count with differential (WBC >50,000/mm³ with >90% neutrophils is highly suggestive), culture, and crystal analysis to rule out crystal arthropathy [2, 3].   • Severity/risk tools:

  • Imaging - Plain Radiographs: May show soft tissue swelling or pre-existing joint disease, but often normal in early septic arthritis [3].
  • Imaging - Ultrasound: Useful for detecting joint effusions, especially in difficult-to-examine joints like the hip, and for guiding aspiration [3].
  • Imaging - MRI: Recommended for suspected axial joint involvement, to assess for osteomyelitis, or when infection spread to adjacent soft tissues is suspected [3].
  • Serial reassessment: Monitoring CRP and clinical response to treatment is crucial for guiding management and identifying complications [3].

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Septic arthritis"][/sc]
  • Gout/Pseudogout: Crystal arthropathies can mimic septic arthritis with acute pain and inflammation, but synovial fluid analysis will reveal crystals rather than bacteria [2]. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Distinguishing gout from septic arthritis: what are the key clinical and laboratory differences?" text="Gout"][/sc]
  • Rheumatoid Arthritis Flare: A sudden worsening of symptoms in a single joint in a patient with RA could be a flare, but septic arthritis must be excluded due to similar presentation and increased risk [2].
  • Osteoarthritis Flare: Acute exacerbation of chronic osteoarthritis, typically less severe and lacking systemic signs, but can be difficult to differentiate without joint aspiration.
  • Reactive Arthritis: Develops after an infection elsewhere in the body, often affecting multiple joints asymmetrically, but usually sterile joint fluid [2].
  • Cellulitis: Skin infection around a joint can cause redness, warmth, and swelling, but pain is typically less severe with passive joint movement preserved.
  • Traumatic Arthritis: History of recent injury, but usually lacks systemic signs of infection and synovial fluid is typically non-inflammatory.
  • Transient Synovitis: Common in children, particularly affecting the hip, and can mimic septic arthritis. Often self-limiting and lacks systemic signs of infection [3]. Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Septic arthritis"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Prompt recognition, urgent hospital admission, escalation to senior medical and orthopaedic teams, and supportive care including analgesia and fluid resuscitation if indicated.
  • Definitive management: Often includes urgent joint aspiration or surgical drainage (e.g., arthroscopy, open arthrotomy) to remove infected synovial fluid and debris, followed by empiric intravenous antibiotics tailored to local resistance patterns and then de-escalated based on culture results [1, 3].
  • Adjunctive management: Immobilisation of the affected joint in a position of comfort initially, followed by early mobilisation with physiotherapy once pain and infection are controlled to prevent stiffness and preserve function [3].
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Regular clinical assessment, monitoring of inflammatory markers (e.g., CRP) to assess treatment response, and prolonged course of antibiotics (typically 2-6 weeks, often with an initial intravenous phase followed by oral therapy) [3].
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Patients should be advised to seek immediate medical attention for worsening pain, fever, or new systemic symptoms.
  • Exam pharmacology reference (UKMLA/MRCGP):
    • Empiric antibiotic choices often include broad-spectrum agents covering _Staphylococcus aureus_ (e.g., flucloxacillin or vancomycin if MRSA suspected) and Gram-negative organisms (e.g., a third-generation cephalosporin) depending on patient risk factors and local guidelines. Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Septic arthritis"][/sc]
  • Irreversible joint destruction: Rapid enzymatic degradation of cartilage can lead to permanent joint damage and osteoarthritis, particularly if treatment is delayed beyond 72 hours [3].
  • Osteomyelitis: Spread of infection from the joint to adjacent bone, requiring prolonged antibiotic therapy and potentially surgical debridement [3].
  • Sepsis and Septic Shock: Systemic dissemination of infection can lead to life-threatening organ dysfunction and shock, especially in immunocompromised patients [1].
  • Chronic pain and functional impairment: Residual joint damage and inflammation can result in persistent pain, stiffness, and reduced mobility, impacting quality of life.
  • Recurrent infection: Inadequate treatment or persistent risk factors can lead to repeat episodes of septic arthritis.
