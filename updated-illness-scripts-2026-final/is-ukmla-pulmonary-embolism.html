Pulmonary Embolism [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Pulmonary Embolism"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Pulmonary Embolism"][/sc]
  • Pulmonary embolism (PE) is occlusion of one or more pulmonary arteries by thrombus — most commonly arising from deep vein thrombosis — causing impaired gas exchange, raised pulmonary vascular resistance, and potentially acute right ventricular strain.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical presentations of PE across the spectrum from sub-massive to massive, apply the Wells PE score to risk-stratify and direct investigation, interpret D-dimer and CTPA findings appropriately, and identify haemodynamically compromised PE requiring urgent escalation beyond standard anticoagulation.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Pulmonary Embolism"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Pulmonary Embolism"][/sc]
  • Venous thromboembolism (VTE), comprising DVT and PE, affects approximately 1–2 per 1,000 adults annually in the UK; PE accounts for roughly one-third of VTE events. Thirty-day case fatality is approximately 10% overall, rising to 30–50% in massive PE with haemodynamic compromise. Incidence increases with age; those over 60 account for the majority of cases.
  • Risk factors follow Virchow's triad: venous stasis (prolonged immobility, hospitalisation, long-haul travel, heart failure), hypercoagulability (active malignancy, pregnancy and puerperium, oral contraceptive pill or HRT, inherited thrombophilia, antiphospholipid syndrome), and endothelial injury (surgery, trauma, indwelling catheters). Cancer patients have a 4–7-fold increased VTE risk. Pregnancy increases risk approximately 10-fold, with the puerperium carrying the highest absolute risk.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Pulmonary Embolism"][/sc]
  • Thrombus — most commonly from the deep veins of the lower limb or pelvis, propagated by [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Virchow's triad: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Virchow's triad"][/sc] — detaches and lodges in the pulmonary arterial circulation, with clot burden and vessel calibre determining haemodynamic impact.
  • Vascular occlusion creates [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Ventilation-perfusion mismatch: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="ventilation-perfusion mismatch"][/sc], causing hypoxaemia; simultaneously, obstruction raises right ventricular afterload, leading to [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Right ventricular strain: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="RV strain"][/sc] and reduced cardiac output in more severe disease.
  • If untreated, massive PE causes acute cor pulmonale, cardiogenic shock, and cardiac arrest; incomplete thrombus resolution can later contribute to chronic thromboembolic pulmonary hypertension.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Pulmonary Embolism"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Sudden-onset dyspnoea is typically the first symptom, often at rest or with minimal exertion; unexplained tachycardia or mild oxygen desaturation may precede the full clinical picture, particularly with smaller emboli where symptoms may be dismissed as anxiety or musculoskeletal.
  • Progressive phase: Pleuritic chest pain and haemoptysis develop as peripheral pulmonary infarction occurs over hours to days; hypoxia worsens with larger clot burden; ECG and biomarker changes reflecting right heart strain emerge in submassive disease.
  • Late phase: Haemodynamic collapse — hypotension, syncope, or cardiac arrest — may occur particularly with large or bilateral emboli in the absence of treatment; untreated or under-treated PE risks CTEPH, presenting as progressive exertional dyspnoea months to years later.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Pulmonary Embolism"][/sc]
  • Ask about sudden-onset dyspnoea — the most common presenting symptom, often occurring at rest or with minimal exertion; unexplained breathlessness disproportionate to clinical findings in a patient with risk factors should raise immediate suspicion, even without pleuritic pain or haemoptysis.
  • Ask about pleuritic chest pain — sharp, localised pain that worsens with deep breathing or coughing indicates peripheral pulmonary infarction involving the pleura; its presence increases Wells score probability and helps distinguish PE from central cardiac causes of dyspnoea.
  • Ask about haemoptysis — blood-streaked sputum is a feature of pulmonary infarction, occurring in approximately 10–15% of cases; combined with pleuritic pain and dyspnoea it forms a characteristic triad of peripheral PE, though its absence does not reduce suspicion for larger or more central emboli.
  • Ask about syncope or presyncope — transient loss of consciousness or near-collapse indicates haemodynamic compromise from massive or submassive PE causing sudden impairment of cardiac output; its presence is a red flag warranting immediate haemodynamic assessment.
  • Ask about risk factor history and symptom context — recent surgery (particularly orthopaedic or abdominal), prolonged immobility, long-haul travel, current pregnancy, known malignancy, oral contraceptive use, or prior VTE are strong probabilistic modifiers; establishing whether dyspnoea or leg symptoms preceded the current presentation helps build the clinical probability picture that underpins Wells scoring.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Pulmonary Embolism"][/sc]
  • Check respiratory rate and oxygen saturations — tachypnoea (>20 breaths/min) and hypoxia are the most consistent signs; a normal SpO₂ does not exclude PE, particularly in younger patients with small peripheral emboli, so saturation should be interpreted alongside clinical probability rather than used as a standalone rule-out.
  • Check heart rate and blood pressure — tachycardia (>100 bpm) is a Wells score component and reflects haemodynamic stress; hypotension or shock index >1 (heart rate divided by systolic blood pressure) indicates massive PE with right ventricular failure and is a haemodynamic emergency requiring immediate escalation.
  • Check the lower limbs for DVT signs — asymmetric calf or thigh swelling, erythema, and tenderness indicate proximal DVT; its presence scores 3 points on Wells and, in a patient with suspected PE, is sufficient evidence to initiate anticoagulation without awaiting CTPA if the patient is haemodynamically stable.
  • Check for right heart strain signs — raised JVP, a parasternal heave (RV dilation), and a loud pulmonary component of the second heart sound (P2) indicate acute cor pulmonale; these signs, combined with troponin or BNP elevation, stratify PE as intermediate-high risk and guide decisions around escalation beyond standard anticoagulation.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Pulmonary Embolism"][/sc]
  • Haemodynamic compromise (hypotension or shock) — indicates massive PE with acute right ventricular failure; typically warrants urgent consideration of systemic thrombolysis or catheter/surgical embolectomy in addition to anticoagulation; this carries the highest short-term mortality among PE presentations.
  • Syncope or presyncope — sudden impairment of cardiac output from a large embolus; warrants immediate haemodynamic assessment, urgent CTPA or bedside echocardiography, and critical care involvement regardless of Wells score band.
  • Right ventricular strain on imaging or biomarkers — RV dilation on CTPA or echocardiography combined with troponin or BNP elevation indicates submassive (intermediate-high risk) PE; associated with significantly higher in-hospital mortality than haemodynamically stable PE and warrants close monitoring and specialist input on anticoagulation escalation.
  • Failure to improve or clinical deterioration on anticoagulation — worsening hypoxia, haemodynamic instability, or new syncope despite adequate anticoagulation should prompt reassessment for clot propagation, inadequate treatment, or an alternative or coexisting diagnosis.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Pulmonary Embolism"][/sc]
  • Saddle PE: Large thrombus straddling the main pulmonary artery bifurcation; may present with sudden cardiovascular collapse or rapidly progressive right heart failure rather than the typical peripheral triad of dyspnoea, pleuritic pain, and haemoptysis; often diagnosed on CTPA after resuscitation and carries the highest mortality among PE subtypes.
  • Pregnancy: The physiological hypercoagulable state, venous stasis from uterine compression, and increased circulating volume make PE the leading cause of maternal death in the UK; Wells criteria are not validated in pregnancy; dyspnoea is common physiologically, increasing diagnostic difficulty; D-dimer is physiologically elevated and unreliable as a rule-out test; V/Q scanning is preferred to minimise fetal radiation, with CTPA reserved where V/Q is inconclusive or unavailable; LMWH is the anticoagulant of choice in pregnancy.
  • Older adults: May present insidiously with confusion, functional decline, or worsening breathlessness attributed to cardiac failure or COPD; the absence of pleuritic pain or haemoptysis should not reduce suspicion in a frail older adult with unexplained new hypoxia and a history of recent immobility or hospitalisation.
  • Incidental PE in cancer patients: Increasingly identified on staging CT without classic symptoms; management decisions (immediate anticoagulation vs surveillance) depend on clot burden, bleeding risk, and oncology pathway guidance; cancer-associated VTE typically warrants extended anticoagulation with an LMWH or DOAC.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Pulmonary Embolism"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected PE: Wells PE score can support pre-test probability assessment and the choice between D-dimer and direct imaging; a calculator is available at Wells criteria for pulmonary embolism on MDCalc. D-dimer is highly sensitive but non-specific, so a negative result is most useful in lower-probability patients; oxygen saturations, ECG, troponin, and BNP or NT-proBNP help judge severity rather than confirm the diagnosis.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: BNP and echocardiography if acute left ventricular failure is suspected; 12-lead ECG and serial troponin if ACS is the primary concern; CXR (usually near-normal in PE, which itself heightens suspicion when combined with significant dyspnoea; Hampton's hump — wedge-shaped pleural consolidation — and Westermark sign — relative oligaemia — are classically cited but insensitive).
  • Imaging — CTPA: Definitive first-line imaging in most patients with Wells >4, or Wells ≤4 with a positive D-dimer; high sensitivity and specificity; allows direct visualisation of clot burden, RV strain, and incidental alternative diagnoses; contrast nephropathy risk should be considered in renal impairment; may under-detect sub-segmental PE.
  • Imaging — V/Q scan: Preferred over CTPA in pregnancy (lower fetal radiation), significant renal impairment, or contrast allergy; reported as normal, low, intermediate, or high probability; a normal result effectively excludes clinically significant PE; intermediate results may require further imaging or clinical reassessment; less useful where there is underlying lung disease (COPD, pleural effusion) as background V/Q inhomogeneity reduces diagnostic accuracy.
  • Serial reassessment: Repeat haemodynamic observations, oxygen saturations, and right heart biomarkers at 24–48 hours are appropriate in hospitalised submassive PE to monitor for clinical deterioration; formal echocardiography and repeat CTPA may be warranted if the clinical course worsens despite anticoagulation; compression duplex ultrasound of the lower limbs is a useful adjunct if DVT signs are present and CTPA is delayed or contraindicated.

  • Middle-aged woman with acute pleuritic dyspnoea, haemodynamic stress, and DVT signs following recent orthopaedic surgery — high-probability Wells PE score — concerning for pulmonary embolism with likely pulmonary infarction.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Pulmonary Embolism"][/sc]
  • Pneumonia — fever, productive cough, and lobar consolidation signs with raised inflammatory markers and CXR changes distinguish from PE; though PE and pneumonia can coexist, particularly in cancer or post-operative patients.
  • Acute coronary syndrome — central crushing or pressure-like chest pain with ECG ischaemic changes (ST elevation or depression) and troponin rise in the absence of right heart strain or DVT signs; pleuritic quality and Wells risk factors help differentiate.
  • Pneumothorax — sudden dyspnoea and pleuritic pain but with absent breath sounds, hyperresonance, and possible tracheal deviation (tension pneumothorax) on the affected side; confirmed by CXR or ultrasound.
  • Acute cardiac failure — bilateral basal crackles, orthopnoea, raised JVP, and peripheral oedema with cardiomegaly and interstitial shadowing on CXR; significantly elevated BNP with left ventricular rather than right ventricular predominance on echocardiography.
  • Pericarditis — sharp pleuritic chest pain relieved by sitting forward, pericardial friction rub, saddle-shaped ST elevation on ECG; echocardiography showing pericardial effusion distinguishes from PE-related right heart strain.
  • Aortic dissection — tearing or ripping chest pain radiating to the back, blood pressure differential between arms, and mediastinal widening on CXR; D-dimer may be elevated but CT aortogram is the differentiating investigation.
  • Musculoskeletal chest wall pain — reproducible tenderness on palpation of the chest wall; normal oxygen saturations at rest; no haemodynamic compromise or strong thromboembolic risk pattern; a diagnosis of exclusion in the context of new pleuritic pain.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Pulmonary Embolism"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Recognise haemodynamic instability and right ventricular failure early. Supportive care often includes oxygen, IV access, and cautious fluids, but the main implication is a shift toward urgent senior review and higher-acuity monitoring.
  • Definitive management: Anticoagulation is the cornerstone of treatment for haemodynamically stable PE; duration is shaped by whether the event is provoked, unprovoked, cancer-associated, or recurrent, alongside bleeding risk and the likely persistence of thromboembolic risk factors.
  • Adjunctive and advanced management: Massive PE or clinical deterioration despite anticoagulation should raise consideration of reperfusion strategies and specialist review, rather than routine anticoagulation alone.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Reassess haemodynamics, oxygenation, and bleeding risk over the first 24–48 hours in admitted patients; persistent exertional breathlessness or poor recovery should prompt review for post-PE syndrome or CTEPH.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Worsening dyspnoea, syncope, new hypoxia, or haemodynamic deterioration during treatment should trigger urgent reassessment for decompensation, clot progression, or an alternative explanation.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Pulmonary Embolism"][/sc]
  • Haemodynamic collapse and cardiac arrest: The acute consequence of massive PE with overwhelming right ventricular failure; rapid deterioration should always make this feel like a high-mortality emergency rather than routine VTE.
  • [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Chronic thromboembolic pulmonary hypertension: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Chronic thromboembolic pulmonary hypertension (CTEPH)"][/sc]: Presents as persistent or progressive exertional breathlessness months to years after acute PE, signalling incomplete recovery rather than simple post-illness deconditioning.
  • Post-PE syndrome: Persistent breathlessness, fatigue, and reduced exercise capacity can continue even when acute clot burden has settled, so failure to recover functionally should not be dismissed too quickly.
  • Major haemorrhage on anticoagulation: An important treatment complication that can alter the balance between recurrent VTE prevention and ongoing anticoagulation risk.
