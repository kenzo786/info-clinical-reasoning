Vulval cancer [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Vulval cancer"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Vulval cancer"][/sc]
  • Vulval cancer is a rare malignancy primarily affecting the labia majora, often presenting as a persistent vulval lesion.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical features and risk factors for vulval cancer, and understand the principles of its investigation and management.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Vulval cancer"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Vulval cancer"][/sc]
  • Approximately 1,400 new cases of vulval cancer are diagnosed in the UK each year, predominantly in postmenopausal women, with incidence increasing with age.
  • Risk factors include human papillomavirus (HPV) infection, lichen sclerosus, vulval intraepithelial neoplasia (VIN), and smoking.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Vulval cancer"][/sc]
  • Trigger: Persistent high-risk HPV infection or chronic inflammatory conditions like lichen sclerosus can lead to cellular atypia and vulval intraepithelial neoplasia (VIN).
  • Progression: Uncontrolled proliferation of abnormal squamous cells, often driven by genetic mutations, results in the development of invasive squamous cell carcinoma.
  • Consequence: Local invasion into surrounding vulval tissues and potential lymphatic spread to regional lymph nodes, impacting prognosis and treatment.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Vulval cancer"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Lesions may be asymptomatic or present with mild pruritus, often for months or even years, leading to delayed presentation.
  • Progressive phase: Symptoms such as persistent itching, pain, bleeding, or discharge typically worsen over weeks to months, particularly beyond 6-12 months from initial symptom onset.
  • Late phase: Advanced disease may manifest with ulceration, fungating masses, significant pain, and symptoms related to regional or distant metastasis, particularly beyond 1-2 years without intervention.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Vulval cancer"][/sc]
  • Ask about persistent vulval pruritus - this is the most common presenting symptom and often leads to delayed diagnosis.
  • Ask about a vulval lump, sore, or ulcer that is not healing - indicating a potential malignant lesion.
  • Ask about vulval pain or burning - suggesting local tissue invasion or inflammation.
  • Ask about abnormal vulval bleeding or discharge - which may be a sign of ulceration or advanced disease.
  • Ask about dysuria or dyspareunia - if the lesion is near the urethra or introitus.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Vulval cancer"][/sc]
  • Check for a visible vulval lesion - often a lump, ulcer, or discoloured area, typically on the labia majora.
  • Check for induration or fixation of the lesion - suggesting invasive growth.
  • Check for enlarged inguinal lymph nodes - indicating potential lymphatic spread.
  • Check vital signs - to assess for systemic signs of infection or advanced disease, though often normal in early stages.
  • Check for signs of lichen sclerosus - such as pallor, thinning of skin, or scarring, which is a risk factor.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Vulval cancer"][/sc]
  • Persistent vulval lesion: Any vulval lump, ulcer, or discoloured area that does not resolve within 4-6 weeks, particularly in older women.
  • Unexplained vulval bleeding: Bleeding not associated with menstruation or trauma, especially postmenopausal.
  • Palpable inguinal lymphadenopathy: Enlarged, firm, or fixed lymph nodes in the groin, suggesting metastatic spread.
  • Rapid growth or change in lesion: A sudden increase in size or alteration in appearance of a pre-existing vulval lesion.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Vulval cancer"][/sc]
  • Younger women: May present with HPV-related vulval cancer, often with multifocal lesions, which can be mistaken for benign conditions.
  • Immunosuppressed patients: Higher risk of aggressive disease and recurrence, requiring a lower threshold for investigation.
  • Lesions mimicking benign conditions: Can be misdiagnosed as warts, cysts, or fungal infections, leading to diagnostic delay.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Vulval cancer"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected vulval cancer: Excisional biopsy or punch biopsy of the suspicious lesion for histological confirmation.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Swabs for infection (e.g., candidiasis, herpes simplex virus) or skin scrapings for dermatological conditions.
  • Imaging - MRI: Pelvic MRI to assess local tumour extent, depth of invasion, and involvement of adjacent structures.
  • Imaging - CT: CT scan of chest, abdomen, and pelvis for distant metastasis and lymph node assessment in advanced cases.
  • Serial reassessment: Regular clinical examination and biopsy of any new or changing lesions in patients with VIN or lichen sclerosus.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Vulval cancer"][/sc]
  • A 72-year-old woman presents with a 9-month history of worsening vulval pruritus and a non-healing ulcer on her left labium majus, associated with occasional spotting.
  • Elderly female with chronic vulval pruritus and non-healing ulcer, concerning for malignancy.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Vulval cancer"][/sc]
  • Lichen sclerosus - chronic pruritus and skin changes, but typically without a discrete ulcer or mass.
  • Vulval intraepithelial neoplasia (VIN) - precursor lesion, often multifocal, but without invasion.
  • Vulval cyst (e.g., Bartholin's cyst) - typically smooth, mobile, and non-ulcerated.
  • Vulval infection (e.g., candidiasis, herpes simplex) - acute onset, often with other systemic symptoms, and typically responds to treatment.
  • Melanoma - pigmented lesion, but can be amelanotic, requiring biopsy for differentiation.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Vulval cancer"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Expedite biopsy for histological diagnosis and staging. Manage symptoms such as pain and pruritus.
  • Definitive management: Surgical excision with adequate margins is the primary treatment. Lymphadenectomy may be required based on tumour characteristics.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Regular surveillance for recurrence, particularly in the first few years post-treatment, and management of long-term side effects.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Refer to a gynaecological oncology multidisciplinary team for staging, treatment planning, and ongoing care.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Vulval cancer"][/sc]
  • Local recurrence: Occurs due to incomplete excision or new primary lesions, impacting prognosis and requiring further treatment.
  • Lymphoedema: A common long-term complication following inguinal lymphadenectomy, causing chronic swelling and discomfort.
  • Sexual dysfunction: Resulting from surgical changes to vulval anatomy, pain, or psychological impact, affecting quality of life.
  • Psychological distress: Diagnosis and treatment can lead to anxiety, depression, and body image concerns.
  • Metastatic disease: Spread to distant organs, leading to systemic symptoms and significantly poorer prognosis.
