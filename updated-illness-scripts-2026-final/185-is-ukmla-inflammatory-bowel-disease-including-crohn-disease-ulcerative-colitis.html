Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Inflammatory bowel disease including Crohn disease ulcerative colitis"][/sc]
  • Inflammatory bowel disease (IBD) is a group of chronic inflammatory conditions primarily affecting the gastrointestinal tract, encompassing Crohn\'s disease (CD) and ulcerative colitis (UC).
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical features, differentiate between Crohn\'s disease and ulcerative colitis, and understand the principles of investigation and management of inflammatory bowel disease.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Inflammatory bowel disease including Crohn disease ulcerative colitis"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Inflammatory bowel disease including Crohn disease ulcerative colitis"][/sc]
  • Approximately 540,000 people in the UK live with Crohn\'s or Colitis, with prevalence increasing to 1 in 67 for those aged 70-79 years [1].
  • Ulcerative colitis has a higher prevalence (0.4%) compared to Crohn\'s disease (0.3%) in the UK population [2].
  • Risk factors include genetics (e.g., NOD2 gene in CD), environmental factors (e.g., smoking in CD, Western diet), and altered gut microbiota.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Inflammatory bowel disease including Crohn disease ulcerative colitis"][/sc]
  • Trigger: A complex interplay of genetic predisposition, environmental factors, and dysregulated immune responses leads to chronic inflammation of the gastrointestinal tract.
  • Progression: In Crohn\'s disease, inflammation is typically transmural and can affect any part of the GI tract from mouth to anus, often with [sc name="jitl-query" Q="skip lesions: what are they and why do they matter clinically?" text="skip lesions"][/sc] and granuloma formation. Ulcerative colitis is characterised by continuous mucosal inflammation, usually starting in the rectum and extending proximally.
  • Consequence: Chronic inflammation leads to tissue damage, ulceration, strictures, fistulae (Crohn\'s), and an increased risk of colorectal cancer (both, but higher in UC).
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Inflammatory bowel disease including Crohn disease ulcerative colitis"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Often presents with insidious onset of symptoms such as abdominal pain, altered bowel habit (diarrhoea, sometimes bloody), and fatigue, which can be intermittent and mistaken for other conditions.
  • Progressive phase: Disease activity typically becomes more persistent, with increasing severity of symptoms, weight loss, and development of complications such as strictures, fistulae, or perianal disease (Crohn\'s), or severe colitis (UC).
  • Late phase: Characterised by chronic disease with periods of remission and relapse, potential for cumulative organ damage, increased risk of malignancy, and extra-intestinal manifestations, particularly beyond several years of disease duration.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Inflammatory bowel disease including Crohn disease ulcerative colitis"][/sc]
  • Ask about abdominal pain - often colicky and relieved by defaecation in UC, more constant and severe in CD, indicating inflammation or obstruction.
  • Ask about diarrhoea - persistent, often with blood and mucus in UC; can be watery or semi-formed in CD, reflecting inflammation and malabsorption.
  • Ask about weight loss and fatigue - common in both, reflecting chronic inflammation, malabsorption, and increased metabolic demand.
  • Ask about perianal symptoms - pain, swelling, discharge, or fistulae are highly suggestive of Crohn\'s disease.
  • Ask about extra-intestinal symptoms - joint pain, skin rashes (e.g., erythema nodosum), eye inflammation (e.g., uveitis), which can precede or accompany bowel symptoms.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Inflammatory bowel disease including Crohn disease ulcerative colitis"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - tachycardia, hypotension, or fever may indicate severe disease activity, dehydration, or systemic toxicity.
  • Check abdominal examination - tenderness, guarding, or rebound tenderness may indicate peritonitis or localised inflammation; a palpable mass may suggest an inflammatory phlegmon or abscess.
  • Check perianal examination - skin tags, fissures, fistulae, or abscesses are characteristic of Crohn\'s disease. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="cobblestone appearance in Crohn\'s disease: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="cobblestone appearance"][/sc]
  • Check for anaemia - pallor, conjunctival pallor, or signs of iron deficiency, reflecting chronic blood loss or malabsorption.
  • Check for clubbing - a non-specific sign of chronic inflammatory disease.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Inflammatory bowel disease including Crohn disease ulcerative colitis"][/sc]
  • Severe abdominal pain with guarding/rebound: Suggests peritonitis, perforation, or toxic megacolon, requiring urgent surgical review.
  • Persistent bloody diarrhoea with systemic toxicity: May indicate acute severe ulcerative colitis or severe Crohn\'s colitis, necessitating hospital admission and intensive medical management.
  • Unexplained weight loss and anaemia: Can be a sign of severe disease activity, malabsorption, or malignancy, warranting thorough investigation.
  • New onset perianal abscess or fistula: Strongly suggests Crohn\'s disease and requires prompt assessment and management to prevent sepsis.
  • Rapidly worsening symptoms or signs of obstruction: May indicate stricture formation or inflammatory mass, particularly in Crohn\'s disease, requiring urgent imaging and surgical consideration.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Inflammatory bowel disease including Crohn disease ulcerative colitis"][/sc]
  • Paediatric IBD: Often presents with growth failure, delayed puberty, or unexplained anaemia, with gastrointestinal symptoms being less prominent initially.
  • Older adults: May have milder symptoms, delayed diagnosis, and a higher incidence of complications such as ischaemic colitis or malignancy.
  • Isolated small bowel Crohn\'s: Can present with abdominal pain, malabsorption, and weight loss without overt diarrhoea or rectal bleeding, making diagnosis challenging.
  • Extra-intestinal manifestations: [sc name="jitl-query" Q="extra-intestinal manifestations of IBD: what are they and why do they matter clinically?" text="Extra-intestinal manifestations"][/sc] such as arthritis, skin lesions, or eye inflammation may precede bowel symptoms by months or years, delaying diagnosis of IBD.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Inflammatory bowel disease including Crohn disease ulcerative colitis"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected IBD: Full blood count (anaemia, thrombocytosis), inflammatory markers (CRP, ESR), faecal calprotectin (elevated in IBD, normal in IBS), liver function tests, renal function tests, and stool cultures (to exclude infection).
  • Severity/risk tools: Harvey-Bradshaw Index (Crohn\'s disease activity) on MDCalc, Truelove and Witts criteria (ulcerative colitis severity) on MDCalc.
  • Endoscopy with biopsy: Colonoscopy with ileoscopy is crucial for diagnosis, differentiation between UC and CD, assessing disease extent and activity, and surveillance for dysplasia. Histology confirms inflammation patterns.
  • Imaging - Cross-sectional imaging (MRI/CT enterography): Used to assess small bowel involvement, transmural inflammation, fistulae, and abscesses, particularly in Crohn\'s disease. Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Inflammatory bowel disease including Crohn disease ulcerative colitis"][/sc]
  • Irritable Bowel Syndrome (IBS) - typically no nocturnal symptoms, weight loss, or inflammatory markers; normal endoscopy.
  • Infectious Colitis (e.g., C. difficile, Salmonella, Campylobacter) - acute onset, often with fever and recent travel/antibiotic use; stool cultures positive.
  • Coeliac Disease - malabsorption, diarrhoea, weight loss, but typically no bloody stools or perianal disease; positive serology and duodenal villous atrophy.
  • Diverticulitis - localised abdominal pain, fever, altered bowel habit, more common in older adults; CT scan shows diverticula and inflammation.
  • Ischaemic Colitis - sudden onset abdominal pain and bloody diarrhoea, often in older adults with cardiovascular risk factors; colonoscopy shows segmental ischaemic changes.
  • Colorectal Cancer - insidious onset of altered bowel habit, rectal bleeding, weight loss, anaemia; endoscopy with biopsy for diagnosis.
  • Ectopic Pregnancy / Pelvic Inflammatory Disease (in females) - acute pelvic pain, vaginal bleeding/discharge; positive pregnancy test or signs of pelvic infection.
  • [sc name="jitl-query" Q="toxic megacolon: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Toxic megacolon"][/sc] - a severe complication of acute severe ulcerative colitis, requiring urgent medical and often surgical management.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Inflammatory bowel disease including Crohn disease ulcerative colitis"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Assess disease severity (e.g., Truelove and Witts criteria for UC, Harvey-Bradshaw Index for CD), identify and manage complications (e.g., toxic megacolon, perforation, obstruction), ensure adequate hydration and nutritional support.
  • Definitive management: Often includes induction and maintenance of remission using immunomodulators (e.g., azathioprine, methotrexate), biological therapies (e.g., anti-TNF agents like infliximab, adalimumab; anti-integrins like vedolizumab; JAK inhibitors like tofacitinib), or corticosteroids for acute flares. Surgical intervention (e.g., colectomy for UC, stricturoplasty/resection for CD) may be required for refractory disease or complications.
  • Adjunctive management: Nutritional support, smoking cessation (especially in CD), psychological support, and management of extra-intestinal manifestations.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Regular clinical assessment, inflammatory markers, endoscopic surveillance for dysplasia, and monitoring for drug side effects.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Patients should be advised on symptoms of worsening disease or complications and when to seek urgent medical attention.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Inflammatory bowel disease including Crohn disease ulcerative colitis"][/sc]
  • Strictures and obstruction: Chronic inflammation, particularly in Crohn\'s disease, can lead to fibrosis and narrowing of the bowel lumen, causing symptoms of obstruction, often requiring surgical intervention.
  • Fistulae and abscesses: Transmural inflammation in Crohn\'s disease can lead to abnormal connections between bowel loops, other organs, or the skin, and can form intra-abdominal or perianal abscesses, particularly in the progressive phase.
  • Colorectal cancer: Increased risk in long-standing and extensive ulcerative colitis and Crohn\'s colitis, necessitating regular endoscopic surveillance, especially in the late phase of the disease.
  • Toxic megacolon: A severe, life-threatening complication of acute severe ulcerative colitis, characterised by rapid colonic dilation and systemic toxicity, requiring urgent medical and often surgical management.
  • Malnutrition and anaemia: Chronic inflammation, malabsorption (especially in small bowel Crohn\'s), and blood loss can lead to deficiencies in iron, B12, and other nutrients, resulting in anaemia and weight loss.
  • Extra-intestinal manifestations: Systemic complications affecting joints (arthropathy), skin (erythema nodosum, pyoderma gangrenosum), eyes (uveitis, episcleritis), and liver (primary sclerosing cholangitis), which can occur at any stage of the disease.
