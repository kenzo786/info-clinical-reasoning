Menopause/ perimenopause [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Menopause/ perimenopause"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Menopause/ perimenopause"][/sc]
  • Menopause is the permanent cessation of menstruation, diagnosed retrospectively after 12 consecutive months of amenorrhoea, not due to other physiological or pathological causes. Perimenopause is the transitional period leading up to menopause, characterised by irregular menstrual cycles and fluctuating hormone levels.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: recognise the clinical features, diagnosis, and management of menopause and perimenopause, including the impact on quality of life and long-term health.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Menopause/ perimenopause"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Menopause/ perimenopause"][/sc]
  • The average age of natural menopause in the UK is 51 years, with perimenopausal symptoms often beginning in the mid-to-late 40s.
  • Approximately 1 in 100 women experience premature ovarian insufficiency (menopause before age 40), and 1 in 20 experience early menopause (before age 45).
  • Risk factors for earlier menopause include smoking, autoimmune diseases, certain genetic factors, and surgical removal of ovaries.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Menopause/ perimenopause"][/sc]
  • Reduced ovarian follicular activity leads to declining oestrogen and progesterone production, triggering a compensatory rise in follicle-stimulating hormone (FSH).
  • Fluctuating and eventually low oestrogen levels cause widespread systemic effects, impacting thermoregulation, bone density, cardiovascular health, and genitourinary tissues.
  • These hormonal changes result in the characteristic symptoms of menopause and increase the risk of long-term health conditions such as osteoporosis and cardiovascular disease.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Menopause/ perimenopause"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Perimenopause can last for several years (typically 4-8 years), characterised by irregular periods, hot flushes, and mood changes, particularly beyond 6 months of symptom onset.
  • Progressive phase: As oestrogen levels continue to decline, symptoms often intensify and new symptoms emerge, such as vaginal dryness and sleep disturbances, particularly beyond 2 years into perimenopause.
  • Late phase: Postmenopause involves the permanent cessation of menstruation and continued risk of long-term complications like osteoporosis and cardiovascular disease, particularly beyond 5 years post-menopause.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Menopause/ perimenopause"][/sc]
  • Ask about hot flushes and night sweats - these vasomotor symptoms are highly characteristic and often significantly impact quality of life.
  • Ask about menstrual cycle changes - irregular periods, changes in flow, or amenorrhoea are key indicators of perimenopause and menopause.
  • Ask about mood changes, anxiety, or depression - hormonal fluctuations can significantly affect psychological well-being.
  • Ask about vaginal dryness, dyspareunia, or recurrent urinary tract infections - these suggest genitourinary syndrome of menopause (GSM).
  • Ask about sleep disturbances - often linked to night sweats but can also be an independent symptom.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Menopause/ perimenopause"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - typically normal, but can help rule out other conditions causing similar symptoms.
  • Check for signs of vaginal atrophy - thin, pale, or dry vaginal mucosa, reduced rugae, or introital narrowing, indicating oestrogen deficiency.
  • Check for skin and hair changes - thinning skin, reduced elasticity, or hair loss can be associated with declining oestrogen.
  • Check for signs of osteoporosis (e.g., kyphosis, height loss) - important for long-term risk assessment, though usually a later sign.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Menopause/ perimenopause"][/sc]
  • Postmenopausal bleeding: any vaginal bleeding occurring 12 months or more after the last menstrual period requires urgent investigation to exclude endometrial cancer.
  • New onset severe headache with visual changes: may indicate a rare pituitary tumour or other neurological pathology, not typical menopausal symptoms.
  • Unexplained weight loss or abdominal distension: raises suspicion for malignancy, particularly ovarian cancer, and warrants prompt investigation.
  • Sudden onset unilateral leg swelling with pain: suggestive of deep vein thrombosis (DVT), especially if on hormone replacement therapy (HRT).
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Menopause/ perimenopause"][/sc]
  • Premature ovarian insufficiency (POI): menopause before age 40, presenting with menopausal symptoms at a younger age, which matters due to increased long-term health risks and psychological impact.
  • Surgical menopause: abrupt onset of severe menopausal symptoms following bilateral oophorectomy, differing from natural menopause due to the sudden cessation of ovarian function.
  • Menopause in women with pre-existing mental health conditions: symptoms like anxiety and depression may be exacerbated, making diagnosis and management more complex.
  • Asymptomatic menopause: some individuals experience no or minimal symptoms, which matters as they still face long-term health risks associated with oestrogen deficiency.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Menopause/ perimenopause"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected perimenopause/menopause (over 45 years): diagnosis is typically clinical, based on symptoms and menstrual history; routine hormone tests are not usually recommended.
  • Core for suspected premature ovarian insufficiency (under 40 years): two blood FSH levels >25 IU/L taken 4-6 weeks apart, confirming ovarian failure.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: thyroid function tests (TFTs) to exclude thyroid dysfunction, full blood count (FBC) to check for anaemia, or pregnancy test to exclude pregnancy.
  • Imaging - Pelvic ultrasound: may be considered if there are concerns about uterine or ovarian pathology, or to investigate abnormal bleeding.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Menopause/ perimenopause"][/sc]
  • A 52-year-old woman presents with a 1-year history of irregular periods, hot flushes, night sweats, and increasing anxiety, significantly impacting her sleep and daily activities.
  • A middle-aged woman with vasomotor symptoms and menstrual irregularity, concerning for hormonal transition.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Menopause/ perimenopause"][/sc]
  • Thyroid dysfunction - can cause menstrual irregularities, mood changes, and temperature intolerance.
  • Depression/anxiety disorder - can mimic mood symptoms and sleep disturbances.
  • Pregnancy - can cause amenorrhoea and hormonal changes, especially in perimenopause.
  • Premenstrual dysphoric disorder (PMDD) - severe cyclical mood symptoms, but typically resolves with menstruation.
  • Drug-induced symptoms - certain medications can cause hot flushes or menstrual changes.
  • Pituitary adenoma - rare, but can cause hormonal imbalances and menstrual irregularities.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Menopause/ perimenopause"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: provide reassurance and education about menopause, discuss lifestyle modifications (e.g., diet, exercise, smoking cessation), and explore symptom management options.
  • Definitive management: consider hormone replacement therapy (HRT) for bothersome vasomotor symptoms or genitourinary syndrome of menopause (GSM), after discussing risks and benefits.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: regular reviews to assess symptom control, adherence to treatment, and to screen for long-term complications such as osteoporosis and cardiovascular disease.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: refer to specialist menopause clinics for complex cases, premature ovarian insufficiency, or when standard treatments are ineffective or contraindicated.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Menopause/ perimenopause"][/sc]
  • Osteoporosis: reduced oestrogen leads to accelerated bone loss, increasing fracture risk, which matters for long-term morbidity and mortality, particularly beyond 5 years post-menopause.
  • Cardiovascular disease: oestrogen deficiency is associated with an increased risk of coronary heart disease and stroke, impacting long-term health outcomes.
  • Genitourinary syndrome of menopause (GSM): chronic, progressive symptoms of vaginal dryness, dyspareunia, and urinary urgency/frequency due to atrophy of urogenital tissues, significantly affecting quality of life.
  • Mood disorders: increased risk of depression and anxiety, which can be persistent and impact daily functioning.
  • Cognitive decline: some women report difficulties with memory and concentration, though the long-term impact on cognitive function is still being researched.
