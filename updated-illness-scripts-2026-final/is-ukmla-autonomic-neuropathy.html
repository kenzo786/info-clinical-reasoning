    • Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Autonomic neuropathy"][/sc]
    • Core for suspected autonomic neuropathy: Comprehensive history and physical examination, including orthostatic blood pressure measurements and assessment of heart rate variability.
    • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Blood tests (HbA1c, thyroid function, vitamin B12, autoimmune screen), urine analysis, and nerve conduction studies to rule out other neuropathies.
    • Imaging - Cardiac: Echocardiography to assess cardiac function; cardiac MRI may be considered in specific cases.
    • Imaging - Gastrointestinal: Gastric emptying studies for suspected gastroparesis.
    • Serial reassessment: Regular monitoring of symptoms and objective measures of autonomic function, particularly in progressive conditions like diabetes.

    Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Autonomic neuropathy"][/sc]
    • Diabetic neuropathy - distinguished by long-standing diabetes and often co-existing peripheral neuropathy.
    • Parkinson's disease - can cause autonomic dysfunction but typically presents with prominent motor symptoms.
    • Multiple system atrophy - a neurodegenerative disorder with severe autonomic failure and cerebellar or parkinsonian features.
    • Amyloidosis - systemic disease causing autonomic neuropathy, often with renal, cardiac, or gastrointestinal involvement.
    • Autoimmune autonomic ganglionopathy - acute or subacute onset, often associated with anti-ganglionic acetylcholine receptor antibodies.
    • Medication-induced autonomic dysfunction - history of drugs like tricyclic antidepressants or alpha-blockers.
    Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Autonomic neuropathy"][/sc]
    • Immediate priorities: Address acute symptoms such as severe orthostatic hypotension with fluid resuscitation and review of vasoactive medications.
    • Definitive management: Treat the underlying cause (e.g., optimise glycaemic control in diabetes, manage autoimmune conditions).
    • Monitoring and follow-up: Regular assessment of autonomic symptoms, blood pressure, heart rate, and glycaemic control.
    • Escalation and safety-netting: Advise on lifestyle modifications (e.g., increased fluid and salt intake for orthostatic hypotension), use of compression stockings, and awareness of symptoms requiring urgent medical attention.

    Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Autonomic neuropathy"][/sc]
    • Orthostatic syncope: Due to severe postural hypotension, leading to falls and potential injury, particularly in the progressive and late phases.
    • Gastroparesis with aspiration risk: Impaired gastric emptying can lead to nausea, vomiting, and an increased risk of aspiration pneumonia, especially in advanced disease.
    • Neurogenic bladder with recurrent UTIs: Incomplete bladder emptying predisposes to urinary tract infections and potential renal damage over time.
    • Silent myocardial ischaemia: Cardiac denervation can mask typical angina symptoms, delaying diagnosis of myocardial infarction and increasing mortality risk.
    • Sexual dysfunction: Erectile dysfunction in men and vaginal dryness in women can significantly impact quality of life throughout the progressive and late phases.
