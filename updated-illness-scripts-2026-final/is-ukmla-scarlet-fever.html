Scarlet fever (Child health) [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Scarlet fever (Child health)"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Scarlet fever (Child health)"][/sc]
  • Scarlet fever is an infectious disease caused by toxin-producing strains of Group A Streptococcus (GAS), specifically *Streptococcus pyogenes*, characterised by a distinctive rash.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical presentation of scarlet fever, understand its notifiable status, and initiate appropriate management to prevent complications.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Scarlet fever (Child health)"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Scarlet fever (Child health)"][/sc]
  • Scarlet fever is most common in children aged 2 to 8 years, though it can affect individuals of any age [1].
  • Incidence typically peaks in winter and spring, with outbreaks often observed in schools and nurseries [2].
  • It is a notifiable disease in England and Wales, requiring health professionals to report cases to local health protection teams [3].
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Scarlet fever (Child health)"][/sc]
  • Infection with toxin-producing strains of Group A Streptococcus (GAS) leads to the production of erythrogenic toxins, which are responsible for the characteristic rash.
  • The toxins cause widespread capillary damage, resulting in a diffuse erythematous rash and other mucocutaneous manifestations.
  • The body\'s immune response to GAS infection can, in some cases, lead to post-infectious complications such as acute rheumatic fever or post-streptococcal glomerulonephritis. Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Scarlet fever (Child health)"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Symptoms typically develop 2-5 days after exposure, beginning with a sudden onset of sore throat, fever, headache, and sometimes nausea or vomiting. The characteristic rash usually appears 12-48 hours after the initial symptoms.
  • Progressive phase: The rash spreads rapidly over 24 hours, becoming most prominent on the trunk and neck, then extending to the limbs. The tongue may develop a "strawberry" appearance.
  • Late phase: Without treatment, symptoms generally resolve within a week, but desquamation (peeling of skin) can occur for several weeks. Complications, if they arise, typically manifest weeks after the initial infection.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Scarlet fever (Child health)"][/sc]
  • Ask about sore throat - often sudden in onset and severe, indicating pharyngeal infection.
  • Ask about fever - typically high (above 38°C), suggesting an acute systemic inflammatory response.
  • Ask about headache and malaise - common non-specific symptoms of acute infection.
  • Ask about nausea and vomiting - particularly in younger children, can indicate systemic illness and dehydration risk.
  • Ask about characteristic rash - a fine, red, sandpaper-like rash that blanches on pressure, crucial for diagnosis.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Scarlet fever (Child health)"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - fever, tachycardia, and potentially tachypnoea may be present, indicating systemic infection.
  • Check for sandpaper rash - fine, erythematous, punctate rash, typically starting on the neck and chest, spreading to the trunk and extremities, sparing the palms and soles.
  • Check for strawberry tongue - initially white-coated with prominent red papillae, later becoming uniformly red.
  • Check for circumoral pallor - a pale area around the mouth contrasting with flushed cheeks.
  • Check for Pastia\'s lines - linear petechial streaks in skin folds (e.g., antecubital fossae, axillae). [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Pastia\'s lines: what are they and why do they matter clinically?" text="Pastia\'s lines"][/sc]
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Scarlet fever (Child health)"][/sc]
  • Signs of severe sepsis: Persistent high fever, lethargy, poor feeding, reduced urine output, or signs of shock indicate potential invasive Group A Strep (iGAS) infection.
  • Neck stiffness or altered mental status: May suggest central nervous system involvement or severe systemic toxicity.
  • Significant respiratory distress: Could indicate airway compromise due to severe pharyngitis or developing pneumonia.
  • Persistent symptoms beyond 48 hours of antibiotics: Suggests treatment failure, resistant strain, or alternative diagnosis, warranting urgent re-evaluation.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Scarlet fever (Child health)"][/sc]
  • Infants: May present with non-specific symptoms like irritability, poor feeding, or a mild rash, making diagnosis challenging.
  • Adults: Often have milder symptoms and less pronounced rash, leading to delayed diagnosis and increased risk of transmission.
  • Immunocompromised individuals: May experience more severe or prolonged illness, with a higher risk of complications.
  • Concomitant viral infections: Can mask typical scarlet fever symptoms, leading to diagnostic confusion. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Concomitant viral infections: how do they affect diagnosis of bacterial infections?" text="Concomitant viral infections"][/sc]
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Scarlet fever (Child health)"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected scarlet fever: Clinical diagnosis is often sufficient based on characteristic rash and symptoms. Throat swab for rapid antigen detection test (RADT) or bacterial culture can confirm GAS infection, particularly in equivocal cases.
  • Severity/risk tools: No specific validated severity score for scarlet fever is routinely used in UK primary care.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Consider tests for viral exanthems (e.g., measles, rubella, parvovirus B19) or other bacterial infections if clinical picture is atypical.
  • Serial reassessment: Clinical review within 24-48 hours of starting antibiotics is often warranted to assess response to treatment and exclude complications. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Serial reassessment: why is it important in acute infections?" text="Serial reassessment"][/sc]
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Scarlet fever (Child health)"][/sc]
  • A 5-year-old child presents with a 2-day history of sudden onset sore throat, high fever, headache, and a diffuse, red, sandpaper-like rash, with associated circumoral pallor and strawberry tongue.
  • A febrile child with pharyngitis and a scarlatiniform rash, concerning for Group A Streptococcus infection and potential complications.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Scarlet fever (Child health)"][/sc]
  • Measles: Rash is maculopapular and confluent, typically starting on the face and spreading downwards, often preceded by Koplik spots.
  • Rubella: Milder maculopapular rash, often with post-auricular lymphadenopathy, less systemic illness.
  • Kawasaki disease: Polymorphous rash, conjunctivitis, oral changes (strawberry tongue, cracked lips), cervical lymphadenopathy, and extremity changes; prolonged fever is a key feature. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Kawasaki disease: how does it differ from scarlet fever?" text="Kawasaki disease"][/sc]
  • Staphylococcal scalded skin syndrome (SSSS): Characterised by blistering and exfoliation, not a sandpaper-like rash, and typically affects younger children.
  • Drug eruption: History of new medication exposure, rash can be variable but typically not scarlatiniform with associated pharyngitis.
  • Viral exanthem: Many viral infections can cause rashes, but typically lack the specific features of scarlet fever (e.g., sandpaper rash, strawberry tongue, Pastia\'s lines).
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Scarlet fever (Child health)"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Prompt recognition and initiation of antibiotic treatment to prevent complications and reduce transmission. Supportive care often includes hydration and antipyretics.
  • Definitive management: A 10-day course of oral antibiotics, typically penicillin V, is recommended. For penicillin-allergic individuals, macrolides such as erythromycin may be considered.
  • Adjunctive management: Symptomatic relief for fever and sore throat with paracetamol or ibuprofen.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Clinical review is often warranted if symptoms do not improve within 24-48 hours of starting antibiotics.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Advise parents/carers on red flag symptoms requiring urgent medical attention, particularly signs of invasive GAS infection. Advise exclusion from school/nursery for 24 hours after starting antibiotics.
  • Exam pharmacology reference (UKMLA/MRCGP): Oral penicillin V [sc name="jitl-medication" Q="Penicillin V use in Scarlet fever" text="phenoxymethylpenicillin"][/sc] is the first-line antibiotic. Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Scarlet fever (Child health)"][/sc]
  • Acute rheumatic fever: A serious inflammatory condition affecting the heart, joints, brain, and skin, typically developing 2-4 weeks after untreated or inadequately treated GAS pharyngitis.
  • Post-streptococcal glomerulonephritis: A kidney disorder that can occur 1-3 weeks after GAS infection, characterised by haematuria, proteinuria, and oedema.
  • Peritonsillar abscess: A collection of pus behind the tonsil, a localised suppurative complication of streptococcal pharyngitis.
  • Otitis media and sinusitis: Common suppurative complications that can arise during the acute phase of infection.
  • Invasive Group A Streptococcus (iGAS) infection: Rare but severe complications such as necrotising fasciitis, streptococcal toxic shock syndrome, or septicaemia, which can develop rapidly and are life-threatening.
