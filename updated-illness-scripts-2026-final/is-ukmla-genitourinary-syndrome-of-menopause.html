Genitourinary syndrome of menopause [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Genitourinary syndrome of menopause"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Genitourinary syndrome of menopause"][/sc]
  • Genitourinary syndrome of menopause (GSM) is a chronic, progressive condition encompassing a collection of signs and symptoms due to oestrogen deficiency affecting the labia, clitoris, vestibule, vagina, urethra, and bladder.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the symptoms and signs of genitourinary syndrome of menopause and understand its impact on quality of life.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Genitourinary syndrome of menopause"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Genitourinary syndrome of menopause"][/sc]
  • Affects up to 50-70% of postmenopausal women in the UK, though many remain undiagnosed due to reluctance to discuss symptoms or lack of awareness (NICE).
  • Risk factors include natural menopause, surgical menopause (bilateral oophorectomy), endocrine therapies for breast cancer, and certain autoimmune conditions.
  • Prevalence increases with age and duration of oestrogen deficiency, often impacting sexual function and overall quality of life.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Genitourinary syndrome of menopause"][/sc]
  • Oestrogen deficiency leads to atrophy of oestrogen-dependent tissues in the lower genitourinary tract, reducing collagen, elastin, and hyaluronic acid content.
  • This results in thinning of the vaginal epithelium, decreased blood flow, reduced lubrication, and loss of elasticity, making tissues more fragile and susceptible to trauma.
  • Consequences include vaginal dryness, dyspareunia, urinary urgency, dysuria, and increased susceptibility to urinary tract infections due to altered vaginal pH and flora.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Genitourinary syndrome of menopause"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Symptoms typically begin within months to a few years following the onset of menopause, often presenting as mild vaginal dryness or discomfort.
  • Progressive phase: Symptoms gradually worsen over time, particularly beyond 1-2 years post-menopause, leading to more pronounced dyspareunia, urinary symptoms, and impact on daily activities.
  • Late phase: Without intervention, symptoms are chronic and progressive, persisting for decades, and can significantly impair sexual function, bladder control, and overall quality of life.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Genitourinary syndrome of menopause"][/sc]
  • Ask about vaginal dryness - often a primary complaint, indicating reduced lubrication and epithelial atrophy.
  • Ask about dyspareunia (painful intercourse) - suggests vaginal fragility, thinning, and inflammation, impacting sexual health.
  • Ask about vulvovaginal irritation, itching, or burning - points to inflammation and tissue sensitivity due to oestrogen depletion.
  • Ask about urinary urgency, frequency, or dysuria - reflects atrophy of the urethral and bladder trigone tissues, which are also oestrogen-dependent.
  • Ask about recurrent urinary tract infections - indicates altered vaginal flora and pH, creating a more favourable environment for bacterial growth.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Genitourinary syndrome of menopause"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - typically normal, but important to exclude systemic illness or infection.
  • Check external genitalia - may show pallor, loss of labial fat pads, introital narrowing, or fusion of labia minora.
  • Check vaginal mucosa - often appears pale, thin, dry, and lacks rugae; may be erythematous or show petechiae, indicating fragility.
  • Check vaginal pH - usually elevated (>4.5), reflecting the loss of lactobacilli and oestrogen effect.
  • Check for signs of prolapse or other pelvic floor dysfunction - can co-exist and exacerbate symptoms.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Genitourinary syndrome of menopause"][/sc]
  • Postmenopausal bleeding - requires urgent investigation to exclude endometrial pathology, such as hyperplasia or carcinoma.
  • New or worsening pelvic pain - may indicate other gynaecological conditions, infection, or malignancy beyond simple GSM.
  • Severe or rapidly progressive symptoms - could suggest an alternative or co-existing diagnosis, or a more aggressive underlying process.
  • Unexplained weight loss or systemic symptoms - warrants investigation for malignancy or systemic disease.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Genitourinary syndrome of menopause"][/sc]
  • Younger women with premature ovarian insufficiency: May present with GSM symptoms earlier in life, requiring careful consideration of long-term management and bone health.
  • Women on aromatase inhibitors for breast cancer: Experience severe GSM symptoms due to profound oestrogen deprivation, often requiring non-hormonal or local oestrogen therapies.
  • Women with autoimmune conditions (e.g., Sjögren's syndrome): May have exacerbated dryness and irritation due to co-existing sicca symptoms, complicating diagnosis and management.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Genitourinary syndrome of menopause"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected genitourinary syndrome of menopause: Clinical history and physical examination (pelvic examination) are usually sufficient for diagnosis.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Vaginal swab for microscopy, culture, and sensitivity to rule out infection (e.g., candidiasis, bacterial vaginosis).
  • If urinary symptoms are prominent: Urine dipstick and midstream urine (MSU) for culture to exclude urinary tract infection.
  • Imaging - Pelvic ultrasound: May be considered if there are concerns about other pelvic pathology, such as ovarian cysts or uterine fibroids, but not for GSM diagnosis itself.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Genitourinary syndrome of menopause"][/sc]
  • A 58-year-old postmenopausal woman presents with a 3-year history of increasing vaginal dryness, painful intercourse, and recurrent urinary tract infections, significantly impacting her quality of life.
  • A postmenopausal woman with chronic, progressive vulvovaginal and lower urinary tract symptoms secondary to oestrogen deficiency.
