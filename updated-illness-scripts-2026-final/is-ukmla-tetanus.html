Tetanus (Infections) [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Tetanus (Infections)"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Tetanus (Infections)"][/sc]
  • Tetanus is a serious, life-threatening neurological syndrome caused by the neurotoxin tetanospasmin, produced by the anaerobic bacterium Clostridium tetani, leading to painful muscle spasms and autonomic dysfunction [1].
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical features of tetanus, understand its pathophysiology, and outline the principles of immediate management and prevention, particularly regarding wound care and immunisation status.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Tetanus (Infections)"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Tetanus (Infections)"][/sc]
  • Tetanus is rare in the UK due to a successful national immunisation programme, with an average of fewer than 10 cases per year reported in England and Wales over the last three decades [1].
  • Between 2001 and 2022, 147 cases were reported to UKHSA, with the highest incidence in individuals over 64 years old who are at higher risk of being under-immunised [1].
  • Risk factors include inadequate immunisation, deep or contaminated wounds (especially with soil), injecting drug use, and in some cases, unnoticed minor wounds [1].
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Tetanus (Infections)"][/sc]
  • Trigger: Clostridium tetani spores, often found in soil, enter the body through a wound and germinate under anaerobic conditions, producing the potent neurotoxin [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Tetanospasmin: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="tetanospasmin"][/sc] [1].
  • Progression: Tetanospasmin travels via retrograde axonal transport to the central nervous system, where it inhibits the release of inhibitory neurotransmitters (GABA and glycine) at the neuromuscular junction [1].
  • Consequence: The unchecked excitatory impulses lead to generalised muscle rigidity, painful spasms, and autonomic instability, without affecting consciousness [1].
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Tetanus (Infections)"][/sc]
  • Early phase: The incubation period typically ranges from 3 to 21 days, but can be shorter with heavily contaminated wounds or longer, up to several months. Initial symptoms often include mild trismus and muscle stiffness [1].
  • Progressive phase: Over several days, muscle spasms intensify and become more generalised, affecting the neck, back (opisthotonus), and abdomen, leading to dysphagia and respiratory difficulties. Autonomic instability may also develop [1].
  • Late phase: Severe symptoms can persist for weeks, with recovery taking months. Complications such as respiratory failure, aspiration pneumonia, and cardiovascular instability are significant risks during this phase [1].
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Tetanus (Infections)"][/sc]
  • Ask about trismus (lockjaw) - painful muscular contractions of the jaw and neck muscles are often the earliest and most characteristic symptom, hindering mouth opening and eating [1].
  • Ask about dysphagia - difficulty swallowing due to pharyngeal muscle spasms, indicating progression and increasing risk of aspiration [1].
  • Ask about muscle stiffness or spasms - generalised tonic contractions, particularly of the trunk and limbs, can be severe and painful, leading to characteristic postures like opisthotonus [1].
  • Ask about history of wound or injury - recent or unnoticed skin breaches are the primary entry point for C. tetani, crucial for risk assessment and diagnosis [1].
  • Ask about immunisation status - incomplete or absent tetanus vaccination significantly increases susceptibility and severity of disease [1].
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Tetanus (Infections)"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Autonomic instability in Tetanus: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="autonomic instability"] can manifest as labile blood pressure, tachycardia, and fever, indicating severe disease and potential for cardiovascular collapse [1].
  • Check trismus - objective assessment of jaw rigidity and inability to open the mouth fully, confirming the characteristic lockjaw sign [1].
  • Check risus sardonicus - a peculiar, sustained spasm of the facial muscles producing a sardonic grin, indicative of facial muscle involvement [1].
  • Check opisthotonus - severe, sustained spasm of the back muscles causing arching of the back, a hallmark of generalised tetanus [1].
  • Check abdominal rigidity - tonic contraction of abdominal muscles, which can be mistaken for an acute abdomen but is part of generalised muscle involvement [1].
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Tetanus (Infections)"][/sc]
  • Progressive dysphagia - rapidly worsening difficulty swallowing suggests pharyngeal muscle involvement, increasing aspiration risk and indicating severe disease progression.
  • Respiratory distress - severe spasms of respiratory muscles can lead to acute respiratory failure, requiring urgent airway management and mechanical ventilation.
  • Autonomic instability - marked fluctuations in heart rate, blood pressure, and temperature indicate severe systemic involvement and a high risk of cardiovascular complications.
  • Short incubation period - an incubation period of less than 7 days is associated with more severe disease and a poorer prognosis, necessitating aggressive management [1].
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Tetanus (Infections)"][/sc]
  • Localised tetanus: Rigidity and spasms confined to the area around the site of infection, often in partially immunised individuals, which may precede generalised tetanus or resolve spontaneously [1].
  • Cephalic tetanus: Localised tetanus following head or neck injury, primarily affecting cranial nerve-innervated muscles, potentially leading to facial paralysis or dysphagia [1].
  • Neonatal tetanus: While eliminated in the UK, it presents in neonates with inability to suck, irritability, and spasms, typically due to infection of the umbilical stump [1].
  • Partially immunised individuals: May present with milder or localised forms of tetanus, or a prolonged incubation period, making diagnosis challenging [1].
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Tetanus (Infections)"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected Tetanus: Diagnosis is primarily clinical, based on acute onset of muscle spasms or hypertonia in the absence of a more likely diagnosis [1].
  • Laboratory support: Tissue or pus from a wound can be sent for C. tetani PCR and culture; however, a negative result does not exclude tetanus, and treatment should not be delayed awaiting results [1].
  • Severity/risk tools: Clinical grading of severity (e.g., mild, moderate, severe, very severe) guides management and prognosis. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Tetanus severity grading: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="There are various clinical grading scales for tetanus severity"] [1].
  • Immunisation status: Serum antibody levels against tetanus toxoid can be measured, but protective levels do not rule out clinical tetanus, especially in severe cases [1].
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Tetanus (Infections)"][/sc]
  • A middle-aged male presents with progressive jaw stiffness, difficulty swallowing, and generalised muscle spasms developing over several days following a contaminated puncture wound.
  • An unimmunised patient with recent wound exposure, presenting with trismus and tonic spasms, concerning for tetanus.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Tetanus (Infections)"][/sc]
  • Strychnine poisoning - presents with similar generalised spasms but typically has a more rapid onset and involves all muscles simultaneously, unlike the ascending pattern often seen in tetanus.
  • Dystonic reactions (e.g., drug-induced) - usually have a clear pharmacological trigger and respond to anticholinergic agents, and lack the autonomic instability of tetanus.
  • Meningitis/Encephalitis - may cause neck stiffness and altered mental status, but muscle spasms are less prominent and trismus is not a primary feature.
  • Severe dental infection/TMJ dysfunction - can cause trismus but typically lacks generalised spasms, dysphagia, or autonomic features.
  • Malignant hyperthermia - a rare, life-threatening reaction to certain anaesthetics, characterised by muscle rigidity, hyperthermia, and acidosis, but with a distinct trigger.
  • Status epilepticus - involves recurrent seizures, but the muscle contractions are clonic-tonic rather than sustained tonic spasms, and consciousness is typically impaired.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Tetanus (Infections)"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Recognition and escalation to critical care, early wound debridement to remove the source of toxin production, and supportive care including airway management and control of spasms [1].
  • Definitive management: Administration of human tetanus immunoglobulin (HTIG) to neutralise unbound toxin, and antimicrobials (e.g., metronidazole) to eradicate C. tetani [1].
  • Adjunctive management: Benzodiazepines (e.g., diazepam) are often used to control muscle spasms and rigidity. Management of autonomic dysfunction may require beta-blockers or magnesium sulphate [1].
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Continuous monitoring in an intensive care setting for respiratory compromise, cardiovascular instability, and ongoing spasms. Vaccination with tetanus toxoid is typically initiated or completed during recovery [1].
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Any patient with suspected tetanus warrants immediate hospital admission and specialist consultation. Patients and their families should be counselled on the prolonged recovery and potential for complications.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Tetanus (Infections)"][/sc]
  • Respiratory failure: Severe spasms of the laryngeal and respiratory muscles can lead to airway obstruction and hypoventilation, necessitating mechanical ventilation, particularly during the progressive phase.
  • Aspiration pneumonia: Dysphagia and impaired cough reflex due to muscle spasms increase the risk of aspiration, a common cause of morbidity and mortality in the progressive and late phases.
  • Cardiovascular instability: Autonomic dysfunction can cause severe and unpredictable fluctuations in heart rate and blood pressure (tachycardia, bradycardia, hypertension, hypotension), leading to cardiac arrest, especially in the late phase.
  • Fractures and muscle rupture: Violent and sustained muscle spasms can be powerful enough to cause bone fractures or muscle tears, particularly during the progressive phase.
  • Pulmonary embolism: Prolonged immobility and critical illness increase the risk of deep vein thrombosis and subsequent pulmonary embolism, a concern in the late recovery phase.
  • Secondary infections: Patients in critical care are susceptible to nosocomial infections, including ventilator-associated pneumonia and catheter-related bloodstream infections, throughout the illness.
