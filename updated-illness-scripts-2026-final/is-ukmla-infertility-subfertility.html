Infertility/ subfertility
Definition
  • Infertility is defined as the inability to conceive after 12 months of regular, unprotected sexual intercourse. Subfertility is a reduced ability to conceive.
  • UKMLA Learning Outcome: To understand the causes, investigation, and management of infertility and subfertility.
Epidemiology
  • Affects approximately 1 in 7 heterosexual couples in the UK.
  • Risk factors include increasing maternal age, obesity, smoking, and excessive alcohol consumption.
Pathophysiology
  • Trigger: Can be due to male factors (e.g., abnormal sperm), female factors (e.g., ovulatory disorders, tubal damage), or unexplained.
  • Progression: Failure of fertilisation or implantation of the embryo.
  • Consequence: Inability to achieve a clinical pregnancy.
Time Course
  • Early phase: A couple trying to conceive for over a year without success.
  • Progressive phase: Investigations are carried out to identify the cause of infertility.
  • Late phase: Management is initiated, which may include lifestyle changes, medical treatment, or assisted reproductive technologies.
Symptoms
  • Ask about menstrual cycle history - irregular or absent periods suggest ovulatory dysfunction.
  • Ask about symptoms of pelvic pain or dyspareunia - may suggest endometriosis or pelvic inflammatory disease.
  • Ask about medical history - previous pelvic surgery, chemotherapy, or radiotherapy can affect fertility.
  • Ask about lifestyle factors - smoking, alcohol, and BMI can all impact fertility.
Signs
  • Check for signs of PCOS - such as hirsutism, acne, and obesity.
  • Check for signs of thyroid disease - which can affect ovulation.
  • Check for galactorrhoea - may indicate hyperprolactinaemia.
  • Check pelvic examination - may reveal anatomical abnormalities, fibroids, or endometriosis.
  • Check male partner examination - for testicular abnormalities such as varicocele.
Red Flag Features
  • Amenorrhoea: Absence of periods requires urgent investigation.
  • Severe pelvic pain: May indicate significant pathology such as severe endometriosis or pelvic inflammatory disease.
  • History of cancer treatment: Chemotherapy or radiotherapy can cause premature ovarian insufficiency or testicular failure.
Atypical Presentations
  • Secondary infertility: Difficulty conceiving after a previous successful pregnancy.
  • Unexplained infertility: No cause is found after comprehensive investigation.
  • Male-only factor: Where the female partner has no identifiable issues.
Diagnostic Tests
  • Core investigations:
    • Mid-luteal phase progesterone to confirm ovulation.
    • Semen analysis to assess male fertility.
    • Assessment of tubal patency (e.g., hysterosalpingogram).
  • Imaging:
    • Pelvic ultrasound to assess for uterine and ovarian abnormalities.
  • If alternative causes suspected:
    • Hormone profile (FSH, LH, oestradiol, testosterone, prolactin, thyroid function).
    • Karyotyping for genetic abnormalities.
Problem Representation
  • A couple presenting with a delay in conception, seeking advice and investigation.
  • Semantic qualifiers: primary/secondary, chronic, multifactorial, distressing.
Differential Diagnosis
  • Ovulatory disorders (e.g., PCOS, premature ovarian insufficiency) - distinguished by hormone levels and ultrasound findings.
  • Tubal factor infertility (e.g., due to PID or endometriosis) - identified by hysterosalpingogram.
  • Male factor infertility - identified by semen analysis.
  • Uterine abnormalities (e.g., fibroids, polyps) - seen on pelvic ultrasound.
  • Endometriosis - diagnosed laparoscopically.
Treatment
  • Immediate priorities: Lifestyle advice (smoking, alcohol, weight) and folic acid supplementation.
  • Definitive management:
    • Medical: Ovulation induction with clomifene or letrozole.
    • Surgical: Laparoscopic treatment of endometriosis or tubal surgery.
    • Assisted conception: Intrauterine insemination (IUI), in vitro fertilisation (IVF), or intracytoplasmic sperm injection (ICSI).
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Regular review with a fertility specialist to monitor progress and adjust treatment.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Referral to tertiary centres for complex cases or if advanced reproductive technologies are required.

Complications
  • Psychological impact: Stress, anxiety, and depression are common.
  • Ovarian hyperstimulation syndrome (OHSS): A potential side effect of ovulation induction treatment.
  • Multiple pregnancy: Increased risk with ovulation induction and IVF.
  • Ectopic pregnancy: Higher risk in women with tubal factor infertility and after IVF.
