Serotonin syndrome [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Serotonin syndrome"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Serotonin syndrome"][/sc]
  • Serotonin syndrome (SS) is a severe and potentially life-threatening condition resulting from excessive serotonergic activity in the central nervous system.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: The candidate must recognise the clinical features, aetiology, and management principles of serotonin syndrome.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Serotonin syndrome"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Serotonin syndrome"][/sc]
  • The incidence of serotonin syndrome is challenging to quantify as it is a relatively uncommon drug reaction, with mild cases often going unreported [Geeky Medics].
  • It can occur in all age groups and without sex preference, with an increasing incidence due to the rising use of serotonergic drugs [Scotton et al., 2019] [Uptodate].
  • Risk factors include the use of a single serotonergic drug at a therapeutic dose, a combination of serotonergic drugs, or overdose, with most fatal cases involving an MAOI plus an SSRI [Geeky Medics].
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Serotonin syndrome"][/sc]
  • Trigger: Serotonin syndrome is caused by an excess of serotonin in the central nervous system, often due to therapeutic use or overdose of serotonergic agents [BMJ Best Practice].
  • Progression: This excess serotonin leads to overstimulation of 5-HT1A and 5-HT2A receptors, particularly in the brainstem and spinal cord, resulting in a cascade of clinical features [Scotton et al., 2019].
  • Consequence: The overstimulation manifests as a triad of altered mental state, autonomic hyperactivity, and neuromuscular abnormalities, which can range from mild to life-threatening [Geeky Medics].
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Serotonin syndrome"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Symptoms typically appear within a few hours (6 to 24 hours) of intake of a new serotonergic agent or following a change in dose [Geeky Medics].
  • Progressive phase: The severity of symptoms can rapidly progress from mild to moderate or severe within hours if the offending agent is not discontinued and supportive measures are not initiated.
  • Late phase: With prompt management, symptoms usually resolve within 24-36 hours, particularly beyond 24 hours after discontinuation of the serotonergic agent; however, severe cases can lead to prolonged complications or death [Geeky Medics].
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Serotonin syndrome"][/sc]
  • Ask about agitation and confusion - these altered mental states are key indicators of central nervous system involvement.
  • Ask about anxiety and hypervigilance - these can be early signs of increased serotonergic activity.
  • Ask about shivering and diaphoresis (sweating) - these are common autonomic features.
  • Ask about gastrointestinal symptoms such as diarrhoea - increased bowel activity is a sign of autonomic dysfunction.
  • Ask about muscle stiffness or involuntary movements - these suggest neuromuscular abnormalities.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Serotonin syndrome"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - tachycardia, hypertension, and hyperthermia are common autonomic findings.
  • Check for mydriasis (dilated pupils) - this is a sign of autonomic hyperactivity.
  • Check for ocular clonus - involuntary, rhythmic eye movements are a highly specific neuromuscular sign.
  • Check for hyperreflexia and clonus (especially in the lower limbs) - these indicate increased neuromuscular excitability.
  • Check for muscle rigidity and tremor - these are further neuromuscular abnormalities that can range from mild to severe.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Serotonin syndrome"][/sc]
  • Hyperthermia above 40°C: Indicates severe serotonin toxicity and requires immediate intervention to prevent organ damage.
  • Rapid onset of symptoms (within 6 hours of drug change/initiation): Suggests a direct drug-induced effect and high serotonergic activity.
  • Muscle rigidity and delirium: These are features of severe serotonin syndrome and are associated with increased risk of complications like rhabdomyolysis and respiratory failure.
  • Fluctuations in pulse rate and blood pressure: Signifies significant autonomic instability, requiring continuous monitoring and aggressive supportive care.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Serotonin syndrome"][/sc]
  • Mild cases: May present with only mild hypertension, tachycardia, mydriasis, diaphoresis, shivering, tremor, myoclonus, and hyperreflexia, making diagnosis challenging if the full triad is not present [Geeky Medics].
  • Older adults: May have altered drug metabolism or polypharmacy, increasing susceptibility and potentially leading to more severe or prolonged presentations even with therapeutic doses.
  • Co-ingestion with other substances: Presentation can be complicated by the effects of other drugs, masking or altering the typical features of serotonin syndrome.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Serotonin syndrome"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected Serotonin syndrome: Diagnosis is primarily clinical, based on the Hunter Serotonin Toxicity Criteria, which requires the presence of a serotonergic agent and specific clinical features like spontaneous clonus, inducible clonus, ocular clonus, tremor with hyperreflexia, or hypertonia with hyperthermia [Geeky Medics].
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Consider investigations to rule out other conditions such as neuroleptic malignant syndrome (e.g., creatine kinase), anticholinergic syndrome, or sepsis (e.g., blood cultures, FBC).
  • Imaging - Chest X-ray: If respiratory failure is suspected, a chest X-ray may be performed to assess for pulmonary complications [Geeky Medics].
  • Serial reassessment: Continuous monitoring of vital signs, neurological status, and fluid balance is crucial to assess response to treatment and detect complications.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Serotonin syndrome"][/sc]
  • A 35-year-old female presents to the emergency department with acute agitation, confusion, tachycardia, and profuse sweating, developing within hours of an increased dose of her antidepressant medication. On examination, she exhibits dilated pupils, hyperreflexia, and clonus in her lower limbs.
  • A young adult with acute mental status changes, autonomic instability, and neuromuscular hyperexcitability following recent serotonergic medication adjustment.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Serotonin syndrome"][/sc]
  • Neuroleptic malignant syndrome (NMS) - slower onset (days to weeks), severe muscular rigidity (lead pipe), hyporeflexia, normal pupils, and normal/decreased bowel sounds [Geeky Medics].
  • Anticholinergic syndrome - dry mouth, urinary retention, decreased sweating, dilated pupils, and delirium, but typically without hyperreflexia or clonus [Geeky Medics].
  • Sepsis - fever, tachycardia, and altered mental state, but typically without the prominent neuromuscular hyperexcitability of serotonin syndrome.
  • Malignant hyperthermia - triggered by anaesthetic agents, rapid rise in temperature, muscle rigidity, and acidosis, but without the specific serotonergic drug history.
  • Thyroid storm - hyperthermia, tachycardia, agitation, and tremor, but with signs of hyperthyroidism and typically without clonus or hyperreflexia.
  • Cocaine or amphetamine intoxication - can cause agitation, tachycardia, hypertension, and hyperthermia, but may lack the specific neuromuscular signs of serotonin syndrome and have a different drug history.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Serotonin syndrome"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Discontinue all serotonergic agents immediately. Provide supportive care including continuous monitoring of vital signs, intravenous fluids for volume depletion, and external cooling for hyperthermia [Geeky Medics].
  • Definitive management: Administer benzodiazepines (e.g., lorazepam) for agitation, muscular rigidity, and hypertension. If supportive measures fail, consider cyproheptadine (a serotonin antagonist) [Geeky Medics].
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Continuous cardiac monitoring and observation are essential. Following resolution of symptoms, a comprehensive assessment and thorough medication review should take place to identify the causative agent and prevent recurrence [Geeky Medics].
  • Escalation and safety-netting: For severe cases with hyperthermia above 41.1°C, dramatic swings in pulse and blood pressure, or delirium, admission to intensive care may be required, potentially involving intubation and ventilation with neuromuscular paralysis [Geeky Medics].

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Serotonin syndrome"][/sc]
  • Seizures: Result from severe central nervous system hyperexcitability, potentially leading to further neurological damage.
  • Rhabdomyolysis: Caused by severe muscle rigidity and hyperactivity, leading to muscle breakdown and release of myoglobin, which can cause acute kidney injury.
  • Acute kidney injury: A consequence of rhabdomyolysis and severe dehydration due to hyperthermia and diaphoresis.
  • Acute respiratory distress syndrome (ARDS): Can develop in severe cases due to aspiration, pulmonary oedema, or systemic inflammatory response.
  • Coma and death: The most severe outcomes, often associated with uncontrolled hyperthermia, profound autonomic instability, and multi-organ failure.
