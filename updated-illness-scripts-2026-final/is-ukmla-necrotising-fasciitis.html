Necrotising fasciitis [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Necrotising fasciitis"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Necrotising fasciitis"][/sc]
  • Necrotising fasciitis is a rare, rapidly progressive, severe bacterial infection affecting the deep soft tissues, including the fascia, leading to tissue necrosis.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the early signs and symptoms of necrotising fasciitis to facilitate prompt diagnosis and urgent surgical and medical management.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Necrotising fasciitis"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Necrotising fasciitis"][/sc]
  • The incidence of necrotising fasciitis in the UK is estimated at approximately 500 new cases each year [1] [2].
  • Risk factors include diabetes mellitus, immunosuppression (e.g., due to steroid use or HIV), malnutrition, obesity, peripheral vascular disease, chronic liver or kidney disease, and intravenous drug use [3] [4].
  • It can affect individuals of any age, but is more common in those over 50 years [4].
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Necrotising fasciitis"][/sc]
  • **Trigger:** Necrotising fasciitis typically initiates when virulent bacteria gain entry through a breach in the skin barrier, such as a cut, surgical wound, or insect bite, particularly in individuals with predisposing risk factors or immunosuppression [3].
  • **Progression:** Once established, bacteria rapidly proliferate and release potent toxins and enzymes, leading to widespread inflammation, microvascular thrombosis, and subsequent necrosis of the subcutaneous tissues and fascia. This process spreads rapidly along fascial planes, often disproportionate to external skin changes [5] [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Bacterial toxins in necrotising fasciitis: what are they and why do they matter clinically?" text="bacterial toxins"][/sc].
  • **Consequence:** The rapid and extensive tissue destruction, coupled with a severe systemic inflammatory response, can culminate in overwhelming sepsis, multi-organ dysfunction, limb loss, and a high mortality rate if not promptly diagnosed and aggressively managed [6] [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Sepsis in necrotising fasciitis: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="sepsis"][/sc].
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Necrotising fasciitis"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Initial symptoms often mimic common skin infections or flu-like illness, developing within 24-72 hours of bacterial entry. Pain is typically disproportionate to visible skin changes [6].
  • Progressive phase: Rapid deterioration occurs, often within 3-5 days, with increasing pain, swelling, erythema, and the development of bullae, skin discolouration (dusky or purplish), and signs of systemic toxicity. Tissue necrosis begins to become evident [5] [6].
  • Late phase: Beyond 5 days, extensive tissue necrosis, cutaneous gangrene, and crepitus may be present. The affected area may become numb due to nerve destruction. Systemic inflammatory response syndrome (SIRS) and sepsis are often pronounced, leading to multi-organ dysfunction [5] [6].
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Necrotising fasciitis"][/sc]
  • Ask about severe pain - often disproportionate to the apparent skin changes, which is a critical early warning sign distinguishing it from cellulitis [6] [7].
  • Ask about rapidly worsening pain - indicating rapid tissue destruction and disease progression.
  • Ask about flu-like symptoms (fever, chills, malaise) - these non-specific symptoms can mask the severity of the underlying infection in its early stages [6].
  • Ask about nausea, vomiting, or diarrhoea - suggesting systemic involvement and potential sepsis.
  • Ask about numbness or altered sensation near the affected area - indicating nerve involvement due to tissue necrosis.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Necrotising fasciitis"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - Tachycardia, hypotension, and fever are common indicators of systemic inflammatory response and impending sepsis [3].
  • Check for tenderness beyond the visible erythema - This suggests deep tissue involvement and is a highly suspicious sign for necrotising fasciitis [6] [7].
  • Check skin for rapidly progressing erythema, warmth, and swelling - These are early, non-specific signs that quickly worsen [6].
  • Check for bullae, skin discolouration (dusky, purplish), or necrosis - These are later, more specific signs of tissue death [5] [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Skin changes in necrotising fasciitis: what do they indicate?" text="skin changes"][/sc].
  • Check for crepitus on palpation - This indicates gas in the tissues, often produced by anaerobic bacteria, and is a strong indicator of necrotising fasciitis [6].
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Necrotising fasciitis"][/sc]
  • Pain disproportionate to physical findings - This is the most crucial early red flag, as the pain is often far more severe than what would be expected from the visible skin changes, distinguishing it from cellulitis [6] [7].
  • Rapid progression of skin changes - Erythema, swelling, and tenderness that spread very quickly (within hours) suggest an aggressive infection [6].
  • Systemic toxicity - Signs such as fever, tachycardia, hypotension, confusion, or malaise indicate a severe systemic inflammatory response, often preceding overt sepsis [3] [6].
  • Bullae or skin discolouration - The appearance of blisters, dusky, purplish, or necrotic skin patches signifies advanced tissue destruction [5].
  • Crepitus - A crackling sensation on palpation due to gas in the tissues is a late but highly specific sign [6].
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Necrotising fasciitis"][/sc]
  • Immunocompromised patients: The classic signs of inflammation and systemic toxicity may be blunted or absent due to a suppressed immune response, leading to delayed diagnosis and poorer outcomes [8] [9].
  • Older adults: May present with non-specific symptoms, confusion, or a less pronounced inflammatory response, making early recognition challenging and increasing the risk of misdiagnosis [10].
  • Early stages: Initial presentation can mimic common cellulitis or a musculoskeletal injury, with pain being the only significant symptom, often leading to delayed recognition of the severity [6].
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Necrotising fasciitis"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected Necrotising fasciitis:
    • Full blood count (FBC): Leukocytosis is common, but can be normal in early stages or immunocompromised patients [11].
    • C-reactive protein (CRP): Often significantly elevated and rapidly rising [11].
    • Urea and electrolytes (U&Es): May show acute kidney injury due to sepsis.
    • Creatine kinase (CK): Elevated if muscle involvement (myositis) is present [11].
    • Blood cultures: To identify causative organisms, though often negative.
    • Lactate: Elevated in severe sepsis and tissue hypoperfusion.
    • The LRINEC score on MDCalc (Laboratory Risk Indicator for Necrotising Fasciitis) can be used as an adjunct, with a score ≥6 raising suspicion, but it is not definitive and a low score does not rule out the diagnosis [12] [13].
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Consider other investigations based on differential diagnoses.
  • Imaging - Ultrasound: May show fascial thickening, fluid collection, and subcutaneous oedema, but is operator-dependent and less sensitive than CT or MRI [14].
  • Imaging - CT scan: Can reveal fascial thickening, fat stranding, gas in soft tissues, and fluid collections, aiding in defining the extent of the infection and guiding surgical debridement [15].
  • Imaging - MRI: Considered the gold standard for soft tissue imaging, offering high sensitivity for detecting fascial oedema, fluid collections, and tissue necrosis, especially in complex cases [16].
  • Serial reassessment: Frequent clinical reassessment is crucial due to the rapid progression of the disease. Deterioration in clinical signs or laboratory markers warrants urgent re-evaluation and potential surgical exploration.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Necrotising fasciitis"][/sc]
  • A 55-year-old male with a history of type 2 diabetes presents to the emergency department with rapidly worsening, excruciating pain in his left calf, disproportionate to the mild erythema and swelling observed. He reports feeling generally unwell with fever and chills for the past 24 hours, following a minor graze to his leg while gardening.
  • Acutely unwell, immunocompromised, rapidly progressive soft tissue infection with severe pain, systemic inflammatory response, and signs of tissue necrosis.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Necrotising fasciitis"][/sc]
  • Cellulitis - Pain is typically proportionate to visible skin changes, and there is usually a slower progression of symptoms without systemic toxicity or signs of deep tissue necrosis [7].
  • Erysipelas - A more superficial skin infection with well-demarcated, raised borders, often affecting the face or lower limbs, and typically lacks the severe systemic symptoms and rapid progression of necrotising fasciitis [7].
  • Deep Vein Thrombosis (DVT) - Presents with leg swelling, pain, and warmth, but typically lacks erythema, skin changes (bullae, necrosis), and severe systemic signs of infection. Doppler ultrasound can distinguish [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Deep vein thrombosis: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="DVT"][/sc].
  • Gas Gangrene (Clostridial Myonecrosis) - Characterised by rapid progression, severe pain, and crepitus, but often involves muscle tissue primarily and has a more fulminant course with distinct foul-smelling discharge and gas production [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Gas gangrene: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="gas gangrene"][/sc].
  • Pyomyositis - Bacterial infection of skeletal muscle, often presenting with localised pain and swelling, but typically without the extensive fascial involvement and characteristic skin changes of necrotising fasciitis.
  • Acute Compartment Syndrome - Severe pain, pallor, paraesthesia, pulselessness, and paralysis in a limb, often following trauma. Pain is exacerbated by passive stretching, but there are no signs of infection unless secondary.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Necrotising fasciitis"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities:
    • Urgent surgical consultation and debridement: This is the cornerstone of management and should not be delayed. Early and aggressive removal of all necrotic tissue is critical to survival [17] [18].
    • Broad-spectrum intravenous antibiotics: Commence immediately after cultures are taken, targeting Gram-positive, Gram-negative, and anaerobic organisms. Combination therapy is often required [17] [19].
    • Haemodynamic support: Aggressive fluid resuscitation, vasopressors, and inotropes as needed to manage sepsis and septic shock [17].
    • Intensive care unit (ICU) admission: For close monitoring and management of organ dysfunction.
  • Definitive management:
    • Repeated surgical debridement: Often necessary until all necrotic tissue is removed and healthy, bleeding tissue is observed [17].
    • Source control: Address any underlying source of infection.
    • Adjunctive therapies: Hyperbaric oxygen therapy may be considered in some cases, though evidence is variable.
  • Monitoring and follow-up:
    • Close monitoring of vital signs, inflammatory markers (e.g., CRP, white cell count), and lactate levels.
    • Regular wound assessment and dressing changes.
    • Nutritional support.
    • Rehabilitation following extensive debridement or amputation.
  • Escalation and safety-netting:
    • Any signs of clinical deterioration (e.g., worsening systemic inflammatory response, increasing pain, new skin changes) warrant immediate re-evaluation and consideration for further surgical intervention.
    • Educate patients and families on warning signs and the need for urgent medical attention post-discharge.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Necrotising fasciitis"][/sc]
  • Sepsis and Septic Shock: The overwhelming systemic inflammatory response to bacterial toxins and infection can lead to widespread organ dysfunction, hypotension, and shock, significantly increasing morbidity and mortality [6] [17].
  • Multi-organ Dysfunction Syndrome (MODS): As sepsis progresses, various organ systems (e.g., kidneys, lungs, liver, heart) can fail, leading to a complex and life-threatening clinical picture [6].
  • Amputation: Extensive tissue necrosis that cannot be adequately debrided, or uncontrolled infection, often necessitates amputation of the affected limb to save the patient's life [17].
  • Severe Scarring and Functional Impairment: Even with successful treatment, the extensive tissue loss requires reconstructive surgery, often resulting in significant scarring, disfigurement, and long-term functional limitations [20].
  • Death: Despite aggressive medical and surgical management, necrotising fasciitis carries a high mortality rate, ranging from 20% to 80%, depending on factors such as type of infection, patient comorbidities, and timeliness of intervention [6] [17].
