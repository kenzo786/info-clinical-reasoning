Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Miscarriage and intrauterine death"][/sc]
  • Miscarriage is the spontaneous loss of a pregnancy before 24 weeks of gestation, encompassing various types including threatened, inevitable, incomplete, complete, and missed miscarriage. Intrauterine death (IUD) refers to fetal demise at or after 24 weeks of gestation.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: To recognise the presentations of different types of miscarriage and intrauterine death, understand their initial management strategies, and identify indications for anti-D prophylaxis.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Miscarriage and intrauterine death"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Miscarriage and intrauterine death"][/sc]
  • Approximately 1 in 5 pregnancies end in miscarriage in the UK, with most occurring in the first trimester [1].
  • The risk of miscarriage increases with maternal age, with women over 40 having a significantly higher risk compared to those under 30 [1].
  • Intrauterine death (stillbirth) affects around 1 in 200 pregnancies in the UK after 24 weeks of gestation [2].
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Miscarriage and intrauterine death"][/sc]
  • Trigger: Chromosomal abnormalities (e.g., aneuploidy) are the most common cause of early miscarriage, accounting for 50-70% of cases. Other triggers include structural uterine abnormalities, endocrine disorders (e.g., uncontrolled diabetes, thyroid dysfunction), and infections.
  • Progression: The initial trigger leads to impaired embryonic or fetal development, often resulting in cessation of cardiac activity. This is followed by a cascade of events including placental detachment, uterine contractions, and cervical dilatation, leading to expulsion of pregnancy tissue. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Missed miscarriage: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Missed miscarriage"][/sc]
  • Consequence: Incomplete expulsion of pregnancy tissue can lead to ongoing bleeding, infection, and psychological distress. Intrauterine death (stillbirth) can result from placental insufficiency, pre-eclampsia, infection, or umbilical cord accidents, leading to significant maternal psychological impact and potential complications like coagulopathy if not managed promptly.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Miscarriage and intrauterine death"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Vaginal bleeding and/or abdominal pain may begin subtly, often within the first trimester for miscarriage. For intrauterine death, there may be a sudden cessation of fetal movements, which can go unnoticed initially.
  • Progressive phase: Symptoms typically intensify over hours to days for miscarriage, with increased bleeding and cramping as the cervix dilates and uterine contents are expelled. In intrauterine death, diagnosis often follows a period of reduced or absent fetal movements, with subsequent investigations confirming fetal demise.
  • Late phase: Resolution of bleeding and pain usually occurs within days to a week after complete expulsion of pregnancy tissue in miscarriage. If tissue is retained, symptoms may persist for several weeks, particularly beyond 7-14 days. For intrauterine death, management (expectant, medical, or surgical) is typically initiated within days of diagnosis, with resolution of physical symptoms occurring over days to weeks depending on the chosen approach.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Miscarriage and intrauterine death"][/sc]
  • Ask about vaginal bleeding - character (spotting, heavy, clots), duration, and association with pain helps differentiate types of miscarriage and rule out other causes.
  • Ask about abdominal pain - location (central, unilateral), character (cramping, sharp), and severity, as unilateral pain with bleeding may suggest ectopic pregnancy.
  • Ask about passage of tissue - description of any expelled tissue (clots, gestational sac) is crucial for diagnosing complete or incomplete miscarriage.
  • Ask about cessation of pregnancy symptoms - loss of nausea, breast tenderness, or fatigue may indicate a missed miscarriage or intrauterine death.
  • Ask about reduced or absent fetal movements - a key symptom for intrauterine death, particularly in the second and third trimesters.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Miscarriage and intrauterine death"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - tachycardia and hypotension may indicate significant haemorrhage or sepsis.
  • Check abdominal tenderness - diffuse or localised tenderness can suggest peritonitis or ectopic pregnancy.
  • Check cervical os - open os with visible products of conception indicates inevitable or incomplete miscarriage. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Cervical os dilation in miscarriage: what does it indicate?" text="Cervical os dilation"][/sc]
  • Check uterine size - smaller than expected for gestational age may suggest missed miscarriage or complete miscarriage.
  • Check for signs of infection - fever, purulent discharge, or uterine tenderness may indicate septic miscarriage.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Miscarriage and intrauterine death"][/sc]
  • Haemodynamic instability: Indicates significant blood loss, requiring immediate resuscitation and intervention.
  • Severe, unremitting abdominal pain: May suggest ectopic pregnancy rupture, uterine perforation, or severe infection.
  • Signs of infection (fever, purulent discharge): Suggests septic miscarriage, which is a medical emergency.
  • Heavy vaginal bleeding (soaking more than 2 pads per hour): Signifies ongoing haemorrhage, requiring urgent assessment and management.
  • Absent fetal heart activity on ultrasound with clinical suspicion of IUD: Requires prompt confirmation and discussion of management options to prevent complications like coagulopathy, particularly beyond 2-3 weeks post-demise.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Miscarriage and intrauterine death"][/sc]
  • Missed miscarriage: Often asymptomatic, diagnosed incidentally on routine ultrasound or due to loss of pregnancy symptoms. This can lead to delayed presentation and increased psychological distress.
  • Ectopic pregnancy mimicking miscarriage: Presents with vaginal bleeding and abdominal pain, but the pregnancy is implanted outside the uterus. Unilateral pain and shoulder tip pain are important discriminators. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Ectopic pregnancy vs miscarriage: how to differentiate?" text="Ectopic pregnancy"][/sc]
  • Recurrent miscarriage: Defined as three or more consecutive pregnancy losses. Requires specialist investigation to identify underlying causes such as antiphospholipid syndrome, uterine anomalies, or genetic factors.
  • Intrauterine death with retained products: Fetal demise occurs, but the fetus and placenta are not expelled spontaneously. Can lead to coagulopathy if retained for several weeks.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Miscarriage and intrauterine death"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected miscarriage/IUD: Transvaginal ultrasound scan is the gold standard for confirming intrauterine pregnancy, fetal viability, and assessing for retained products of conception.
  • Severity/risk tools: The [Maternity Early Warning Score (MEWS) on MDCalc](https://www.mdcalc.com/mews-maternity-early-warning-score) can be used to assess maternal physiological status and identify deterioration.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Serum beta-human chorionic gonadotropin (hCG) levels, particularly serial measurements, are vital for differentiating between viable intrauterine pregnancy, miscarriage, and ectopic pregnancy. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Serial hCG in early pregnancy: interpretation and significance?" text="Serial hCG levels"][/sc]
  • Imaging - Transabdominal ultrasound: May be used in later pregnancy for suspected intrauterine death to confirm fetal demise and assess fetal biometry.
  • Serial reassessment: For expectant management of miscarriage, repeat ultrasound and clinical review are necessary to ensure complete expulsion of products and resolution of symptoms.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Miscarriage and intrauterine death"][/sc]
  • Ectopic pregnancy - unilateral abdominal pain, shoulder tip pain, or haemodynamic instability.
  • Molar pregnancy - excessively high hCG levels, hyperemesis gravidarum, or characteristic "snowstorm" appearance on ultrasound.
  • Cervical or vaginal pathology (e.g., cervicitis, polyps) - bleeding without significant pain or passage of tissue, often visualised on speculum examination.
  • Implantation bleeding - light spotting occurring around the time of expected menses, typically self-limiting and not associated with pain.
  • Physiological bleeding in early pregnancy - common and usually self-resolving, but requires exclusion of more serious causes.
  • Pelvic inflammatory disease (PID) - often associated with fever, purulent discharge, and cervical motion tenderness, typically without a positive pregnancy test. Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Miscarriage and intrauterine death"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Assessment of haemodynamic stability, pain control, and exclusion of ectopic pregnancy. Escalation to senior medical staff for unstable patients.
  • Definitive management: For miscarriage, options include expectant management (wait and see), medical management (misoprostol +/- mifepristone), or surgical management (manual vacuum aspiration or surgical evacuation). For intrauterine death, induction of labour or surgical evacuation may be considered depending on gestational age and patient preference.
  • Adjunctive management: Anti-D immunoglobulin prophylaxis is often warranted for rhesus-negative women undergoing surgical management of miscarriage or IUD, or medical management of IUD.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Serial hCG measurements and repeat ultrasound scans are often required to confirm complete resolution of pregnancy and exclude retained products or ectopic pregnancy.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Patients should be advised on red flag symptoms (e.g., heavy bleeding, severe pain, fever) and provided with clear instructions on when and where to seek urgent medical attention.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Miscarriage and intrauterine death"][/sc]
  • Haemorrhage: Significant blood loss can occur, particularly with incomplete miscarriage or retained products, leading to anaemia or hypovolaemic shock.
  • Infection (septic miscarriage): Retained products of conception can become infected, leading to endometritis, sepsis, and potentially life-threatening complications if not promptly treated.
  • Psychological distress: Miscarriage and intrauterine death can have profound and lasting psychological impacts, including grief, anxiety, depression, and post-traumatic stress disorder, often extending for months to years after the event.
  • Retained products of conception: Incomplete expulsion of pregnancy tissue can lead to persistent bleeding, pain, and increased risk of infection, often requiring further medical or surgical intervention, particularly beyond 2-3 weeks post-event.
  • Coagulopathy: Prolonged retention of a dead fetus (typically beyond 3-4 weeks) can lead to disseminated intravascular coagulation (DIC), a severe bleeding disorder.
