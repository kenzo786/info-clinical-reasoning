Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Gastro-oesophageal reflux disease including Barrett oesophagus"][/sc]
  • Gastro-oesophageal reflux disease (GORD) is a condition where stomach acid and other contents flow back into the oesophagus, causing symptoms or complications.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical features of GORD and Barrett's oesophagus, identify alarm symptoms requiring urgent referral, and outline initial management strategies.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Gastro-oesophageal reflux disease including Barrett oesophagus"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Gastro-oesophageal reflux disease including Barrett oesophagus"][/sc]
  • GORD is a common condition, affecting approximately 10-20% of the UK adult population weekly.
  • Barrett's oesophagus, a metaplastic change in the oesophageal lining, affects about 1-2% of the general population in the UK and is a complication of chronic GORD.
  • Risk factors for GORD include obesity, smoking, alcohol consumption, certain medications (e.g., calcium channel blockers, nitrates), and hiatus hernia.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Gastro-oesophageal reflux disease including Barrett oesophagus"][/sc]
  • Trigger: Incompetence of the lower oesophageal sphincter (LOS) allows gastric contents, including acid and pepsin, to reflux into the oesophagus.
  • Progression: Chronic exposure to gastric contents leads to oesophageal mucosal inflammation (oesophagitis) and damage.
  • Consequence: Persistent inflammation can lead to complications such as oesophageal strictures, ulcers, and in some cases, metaplastic changes to columnar epithelium, known as Barrett's oesophagus [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Columnar metaplasia: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="columnar metaplasia"][/sc].
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Gastro-oesophageal reflux disease including Barrett oesophagus"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Intermittent symptoms of heartburn and regurgitation, often post-prandial, which may resolve with lifestyle modifications or antacids.
  • Progressive phase: Increasing frequency and severity of symptoms, potentially leading to oesophagitis and requiring regular acid-suppression therapy.
  • Late phase: Chronic, untreated GORD can lead to complications such as oesophageal stricture, ulceration, or Barrett's oesophagus, which carries a risk of malignant transformation over many years.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Gastro-oesophageal reflux disease including Barrett oesophagus"][/sc]
  • Ask about heartburn - a burning sensation in the chest, often worse after eating, lying down, or bending over, indicating oesophageal irritation.
  • Ask about regurgitation - the effortless return of gastric contents into the pharynx or mouth, which can lead to a sour taste or aspiration.
  • Ask about dysphagia - difficulty swallowing, which may suggest oesophageal stricture or malignancy, requiring urgent investigation.
  • Ask about odynophagia - painful swallowing, often associated with severe oesophagitis or ulceration.
  • Ask about chronic cough or hoarseness - atypical symptoms that may indicate extra-oesophageal reflux, particularly if worse at night.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Gastro-oesophageal reflux disease including Barrett oesophagus"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - typically normal in uncomplicated GORD, but fever or tachycardia may indicate complications like perforation or severe infection.
  • Check for epigastric tenderness - may be present due to gastric irritation, but non-specific.
  • Check for signs of anaemia - pallor, fatigue, or tachycardia may suggest chronic blood loss from oesophageal erosions or ulcers.
  • Check for dental erosions - acid reflux can cause enamel erosion, particularly on the posterior teeth [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Dental erosion in GORD: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="dental erosion"][/sc].
  • Check for signs of weight loss - unexplained weight loss is an alarm symptom and warrants urgent investigation for malignancy.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Gastro-oesophageal reflux disease including Barrett oesophagus"][/sc]
  • Dysphagia: Difficulty swallowing, suggesting oesophageal stricture or malignancy.
  • Odynophagia: Painful swallowing, indicating severe oesophagitis or ulceration.
  • Unexplained weight loss: A significant concern for underlying malignancy.
  • Upper gastrointestinal bleeding: Haematemesis or melaena, requiring urgent endoscopy to identify the source.
  • Iron deficiency anaemia: Suggests chronic blood loss from the gastrointestinal tract.
  • Persistent vomiting: Especially if new onset and unexplained, may indicate obstruction or malignancy.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Gastro-oesophageal reflux disease including Barrett oesophagus"][/sc]
  • Older adults: May present with atypical symptoms such as chest pain, chronic cough, or dysphagia, rather than classic heartburn.
  • Asthma-like symptoms: Reflux can trigger or exacerbate asthma, leading to wheezing, shortness of breath, and cough, particularly at night.
  • Laryngopharyngeal reflux (LPR): Presents with hoarseness, chronic cough, throat clearing, and globus sensation, often without typical heartburn.
  • Silent GORD: Patients with Barrett's oesophagus may have minimal or no GORD symptoms, highlighting the importance of surveillance in at-risk groups [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Barrett surveillance: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Barrett surveillance"][/sc].
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Gastro-oesophageal reflux disease including Barrett oesophagus"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected GORD: Clinical diagnosis based on typical symptoms. Empirical trial of PPI therapy is often the first step.
  • If alarm symptoms are present: Urgent upper gastrointestinal endoscopy to rule out malignancy, oesophagitis, strictures, or Barrett's oesophagus.
  • Severity/risk tools: Consider using the GORDQ questionnaire on MDCalc to assess symptom severity and response to treatment.
  • Oesophageal pH monitoring: Indicated for patients with persistent symptoms despite PPI therapy, or atypical symptoms, to confirm pathological acid reflux.
  • Oesophageal manometry: Assesses oesophageal motility and lower oesophageal sphincter function, particularly before anti-reflux surgery [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Oesophageal manometry: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="oesophageal manometry"][/sc].
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Gastro-oesophageal reflux disease including Barrett oesophagus"][/sc]
  • A 55-year-old male with a 10-year history of daily heartburn and regurgitation, now presenting with new onset dysphagia and unexplained weight loss.
  • Chronic acid reflux with alarm features, concerning for oesophageal stricture or adenocarcinoma in the context of potential Barrett's oesophagus.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Gastro-oesophageal reflux disease including Barrett oesophagus"][/sc]
  • Peptic ulcer disease - often presents with epigastric pain, but can mimic GORD; distinguished by endoscopy and H. pylori testing.
  • Oesophageal motility disorders (e.g., achalasia, diffuse oesophageal spasm) - cause dysphagia and chest pain, but typically without significant acid regurgitation.
  • Oesophageal cancer - alarm symptoms like dysphagia, weight loss, and anaemia necessitate urgent endoscopy to differentiate from benign GORD complications.
  • Cardiac chest pain - can be confused with GORD-related chest pain; distinguished by ECG, cardiac biomarkers, and response to nitrates.
  • Biliary colic - typically presents with right upper quadrant pain, often post-prandial, but can have atypical presentations.
  • Functional dyspepsia - persistent dyspeptic symptoms without an identifiable organic cause on investigation [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Functional dyspepsia: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="functional dyspepsia"][/sc].
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Gastro-oesophageal reflux disease including Barrett oesophagus"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Address alarm symptoms with urgent referral for endoscopy. Provide symptomatic relief with antacids or alginates.
  • Definitive management: Lifestyle modifications (weight loss, smoking cessation, avoiding trigger foods), and proton pump inhibitors (PPIs) are the cornerstone of GORD management.
  • Adjunctive management: H2-receptor antagonists (H2RAs) may be considered for symptom control, particularly nocturnal symptoms, or as an alternative to PPIs.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Annual review for patients on long-term PPIs. Surveillance endoscopy for Barrett's oesophagus as per NICE guidelines (e.g., every 2-5 years depending on segment length and presence of dysplasia).
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Refer to specialist gastroenterology services for persistent or atypical symptoms, alarm features, or confirmed Barrett's oesophagus. Surgical options (e.g., laparoscopic fundoplication) may be considered for selected patients with severe, refractory GORD.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Gastro-oesophageal reflux disease including Barrett oesophagus"][/sc]
  • Oesophagitis: Inflammation of the oesophageal lining due to chronic acid exposure, leading to pain and potential bleeding.
  • Oesophageal stricture: Narrowing of the oesophagus due to chronic inflammation and scarring, causing progressive dysphagia.
  • Oesophageal ulceration: Deep erosions in the oesophageal lining, which can cause pain and significant gastrointestinal bleeding.
  • Barrett's oesophagus: Metaplastic change of the squamous epithelium to columnar epithelium, a pre-malignant condition with increased risk of oesophageal adenocarcinoma over many years.
  • Oesophageal adenocarcinoma: A serious long-term complication of Barrett's oesophagus, particularly in the context of dysplasia.
  • Respiratory complications: Chronic cough, asthma exacerbation, or recurrent aspiration pneumonia due to refluxate entering the airways.
