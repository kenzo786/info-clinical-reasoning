Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Multiple pregnancy (Pregnancy and the puerperium)"][/sc]
  • A multiple pregnancy involves carrying more than one fetus simultaneously, most commonly twins, but can include triplets or more. It is associated with increased maternal and fetal risks compared to singleton pregnancies.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the increased risks associated with multiple pregnancies, differentiate between chorionicity types, and understand the principles of antenatal surveillance and management to mitigate complications.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Multiple pregnancy (Pregnancy and the puerperium)"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Multiple pregnancy (Pregnancy and the puerperium)"][/sc]
  • The incidence of multiple pregnancies has increased in recent decades, largely due to assisted reproductive technologies (ART) and increasing maternal age. In the UK, around 1 in 65 pregnancies are multiple, with twins accounting for the vast majority [1].
  • Dizygotic (non-identical) twins are more common than monozygotic (identical) twins and their incidence is influenced by genetics, maternal age, parity, and ART. Monochorionic pregnancies (sharing a placenta) occur in approximately 0.3-0.4% of all pregnancies and carry higher risks [2].
  • Risk factors include advanced maternal age (over 35 years), family history of dizygotic twins, high parity, and the use of fertility treatments such as ovulation induction or in vitro fertilisation (IVF).
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Multiple pregnancy (Pregnancy and the puerperium)"][/sc]
  • Trigger: The presence of multiple fetuses leads to an exaggerated physiological adaptation in the mother, including increased plasma volume, cardiac output, and metabolic demands, alongside mechanical stress on the uterus.
  • Progression: The shared intrauterine environment in monochorionic pregnancies can lead to vascular anastomoses in the placenta, predisposing to conditions like Twin-to-Twin Transfusion Syndrome (TTTS) or Selective Intrauterine Growth Restriction (sIUGR). [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Twin-to-Twin Transfusion Syndrome (TTTS): what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="TTTS"][/sc]
  • Consequence: Increased uterine distension and hormonal changes contribute to a higher risk of preterm labour and birth, while placental complications and fetal growth discordance can lead to significant perinatal morbidity and mortality.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Multiple pregnancy (Pregnancy and the puerperium)"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Diagnosis typically occurs in the first trimester via ultrasound, which also determines chorionicity and amnionicity. Early complications may include hyperemesis gravidarum and increased risk of miscarriage.
  • Progressive phase: Throughout the second and third trimesters, close surveillance is required due to increased risks of gestational hypertension, pre-eclampsia, gestational diabetes, and preterm labour. Fetal complications like TTTS and IUGR often manifest during this period.
  • Late phase: The risk of complications escalates towards term, necessitating careful planning for the timing and mode of birth. Postpartum, there is an increased risk of haemorrhage and psychological adjustment challenges for parents.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Multiple pregnancy (Pregnancy and the puerperium)"][/sc]
  • Ask about exaggerated pregnancy symptoms - such as severe nausea and vomiting (hyperemesis gravidarum) or profound fatigue, as these can be more pronounced due to higher hormone levels.
  • Ask about early or increased uterine contractions - indicating a heightened risk of preterm labour, which is a common complication of multiple pregnancies.
  • Ask about rapid increase in abdominal size or excessive weight gain - which may suggest polyhydramnios or fetal growth, often exceeding that of a singleton pregnancy.
  • Ask about symptoms of pre-eclampsia - including new onset headache, visual disturbances, or epigastric pain, as the risk is significantly elevated in multiple gestations.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Multiple pregnancy (Pregnancy and the puerperium)"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - for evidence of hypertension or tachycardia, which may indicate developing pre-eclampsia or other maternal complications.
  • Check fundal height - often larger than expected for gestational age, suggesting a multiple pregnancy or polyhydramnios.
  • Check for uterine tenderness or contractions - to assess for signs of preterm labour or placental abruption.
  • Check for fetal growth discordance - detected by ultrasound, where one fetus is significantly smaller than the other, potentially indicating IUGR or TTTS. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Fetal growth discordance: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Fetal growth discordance"][/sc]
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Multiple pregnancy (Pregnancy and the puerperium)"][/sc]
  • Severe abdominal pain or vaginal bleeding: May indicate placental abruption or other acute obstetric emergencies requiring immediate assessment.
  • Sudden onset of severe headache, visual changes, or epigastric pain: Suggestive of severe pre-eclampsia or HELLP syndrome, which can rapidly progress and endanger both mother and fetuses.
  • Significant reduction in fetal movements: A critical sign of fetal compromise, necessitating urgent fetal assessment and potential intervention.
  • Preterm contractions with cervical changes: Indicates active preterm labour, requiring prompt management to improve neonatal outcomes.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Multiple pregnancy (Pregnancy and the puerperium)"][/sc]
  • Monochorionic-monoamniotic (MCMA) twins: Present with a very high risk of cord entanglement and conjoined twins, requiring intensive surveillance and often early planned delivery. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Monochorionic-monoamniotic twins: what are the risks and management implications?" text="MCMA twins"][/sc]
  • Higher-order multiple pregnancies (triplets or more): Exaggerated risks of all complications seen in twin pregnancies, often necessitating multifetal pregnancy reduction in some cases to improve outcomes.
  • Vanishing twin syndrome: Early in pregnancy, one fetus may cease to develop and be reabsorbed, leading to a singleton pregnancy. This can be asymptomatic or present with light bleeding.
  • Conjoined twins: A rare and severe complication of monozygotic twinning where the fetuses are physically joined, requiring highly specialised antenatal care and delivery planning.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Multiple pregnancy (Pregnancy and the puerperium)"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected multiple pregnancy: Early first-trimester ultrasound scan (ideally 11-14 weeks) to confirm viability, determine gestational age, and establish chorionicity and amnionicity (lambda or T-sign).
  • Severity/risk tools: Quintero staging for TTTS (used to classify severity of twin-to-twin transfusion syndrome). Quintero Staging for TTTS on MDCalc
  • Imaging - Ultrasound: Regular serial ultrasound scans are crucial for monitoring fetal growth, amniotic fluid volumes, and detecting complications such as TTTS, sIUGR, or structural anomalies. Frequency depends on chorionicity (e.g., fortnightly for monochorionic, every 2-4 weeks for dichorionic).
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Consider maternal blood tests for pregnancy-associated plasma protein-A (PAPP-A) and human chorionic gonadotropin (hCG) as part of aneuploidy screening, though interpretation is adjusted for multiple pregnancies.
  • Serial reassessment: Continuous monitoring of maternal blood pressure, urine protein, and blood glucose levels throughout pregnancy due to increased risk of pre-eclampsia and gestational diabetes.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Multiple pregnancy (Pregnancy and the puerperium)"][/sc]
  • Singleton pregnancy with polyhydramnios - distinguished by ultrasound confirming only one fetus.
  • Large for gestational age (LGA) singleton - ruled out by ultrasound identifying multiple fetuses.
  • Uterine fibroids or ovarian cysts - differentiated by ultrasound imaging showing the presence of multiple gestational sacs.
  • Molar pregnancy - excluded by characteristic ultrasound findings and very high hCG levels.
  • Abdominal mass - distinguished by ultrasound demonstrating fetal heart activity and gestational sacs.
  • Preterm labour in a singleton pregnancy - differentiated by ultrasound confirming multiple fetuses and often more rapid uterine growth. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Preterm labour: what are the causes and management in multiple pregnancy?" text="Preterm labour"][/sc]
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Multiple pregnancy (Pregnancy and the puerperium)"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Early diagnosis of chorionicity, referral to a specialist multidisciplinary team, and comprehensive antenatal surveillance to detect and manage complications promptly.
  • Definitive management: Management strategies are highly dependent on chorionicity and specific complications. This often includes regular ultrasound monitoring, targeted interventions for TTTS (e.g., fetoscopic laser ablation), and careful planning for the timing and mode of birth.
  • Adjunctive management: Consideration of antenatal corticosteroids for fetal lung maturation if preterm birth is anticipated, and magnesium sulphate for neuroprotection in very preterm births.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Frequent antenatal appointments, serial ultrasound scans, and close monitoring for maternal complications such as pre-eclampsia, gestational diabetes, and anaemia.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Clear pathways for escalation to tertiary fetal medicine centres for complex cases or developing complications. Education of parents on red flag symptoms and when to seek urgent medical attention.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Multiple pregnancy (Pregnancy and the puerperium)"][/sc]
  • Preterm birth: Occurs in over 50% of twin pregnancies and nearly all higher-order multiples, significantly increasing neonatal morbidity and mortality due to immature organ systems.
  • Twin-to-Twin Transfusion Syndrome (TTTS): A severe complication of monochorionic pregnancies where unequal blood flow between fetuses leads to one donor twin becoming anaemic and growth-restricted, and one recipient twin becoming polycythaemic and hydropic. If untreated, it has high mortality.
  • Selective Intrauterine Growth Restriction (sIUGR): In monochorionic pregnancies, one twin may experience restricted growth due to unequal placental sharing, leading to significant size discordance and increased risk of adverse outcomes for both fetuses.
  • Pre-eclampsia: The risk is 2-4 times higher in multiple pregnancies, often with earlier onset and increased severity, posing significant risks to maternal and fetal health.
  • Postpartum haemorrhage: The overdistended uterus in multiple pregnancies is at higher risk of atony, leading to increased blood loss after delivery.
  • Perinatal mortality and morbidity: Overall, multiple pregnancies have a significantly higher rate of stillbirth, neonatal death, and long-term neurological impairment compared to singleton pregnancies, particularly in monochorionic gestations.
