Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Prematurity (Child health)"][/sc]
  • Prematurity, or preterm birth, is defined as birth occurring before 37 weeks of gestation. It is further categorised by gestational age: extreme (<28 weeks), very preterm (<32 weeks), and moderate to late preterm (32-36 weeks).
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the risk factors for preterm birth, anticipate common neonatal complications, and understand the principles of immediate postnatal management and long-term follow-up for premature infants.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Prematurity (Child health)"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Prematurity"][/sc]
  • Approximately 8 out of 100 babies (around 7.9% in England and Wales in 2022) are born prematurely in the UK, equating to nearly 58,000 babies annually.
  • Risk factors include previous preterm birth, multiple pregnancies, cervical insufficiency, certain uterine abnormalities, infections (e.g., urinary tract infections, periodontal disease), chronic maternal conditions (e.g., hypertension, diabetes), and lifestyle factors (e.g., smoking, substance abuse).
  • The incidence of preterm birth varies by socioeconomic status, with higher rates observed in more deprived areas.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Prematurity (Child health)"][/sc]
  • Trigger: Preterm labour can be initiated by various factors, including premature rupture of membranes, uterine overdistension (e.g., polyhydramnios, multiple gestation), infection/inflammation (e.g., chorioamnionitis), placental abnormalities, or maternal stress responses.
  • Progression: These triggers lead to a cascade of events involving activation of the maternal and fetal hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal axes, increased prostaglandin production, and cervical ripening, culminating in uterine contractions and cervical effacement and dilation.
  • Consequence: The immaturity of fetal organ systems at birth, particularly the lungs, brain, gastrointestinal tract, and immune system, results in a high susceptibility to complications such as respiratory distress syndrome, intraventricular haemorrhage, necrotising enterocolitis, and sepsis. Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Prematurity (Child health)"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Immediately after preterm birth, infants are at high risk of acute respiratory distress, hypothermia, hypoglycaemia, and early-onset sepsis, typically within the first 72 hours of life.
  • Progressive phase: Over the first few days to weeks, complications such as patent ductus arteriosus, necrotising enterocolitis, intraventricular haemorrhage, and retinopathy of prematurity may develop or become apparent, often requiring intensive neonatal care.
  • Late phase: Beyond the initial hospital stay and into infancy and childhood, premature infants, particularly those born extremely or very preterm, may experience long-term sequelae including bronchopulmonary dysplasia, neurodevelopmental impairment (e.g., cerebral palsy, learning difficulties), and chronic health issues.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Prematurity (Child health)"][/sc]
  • Ask about respiratory effort - signs of increased work of breathing (e.g., grunting, nasal flaring, retractions) indicate respiratory distress syndrome.
  • Ask about feeding tolerance - poor feeding, abdominal distension, or bilious aspirates may suggest necrotising enterocolitis.
  • Ask about activity level - lethargy, hypotonia, or irritability can be non-specific signs of sepsis, intracranial pathology, or metabolic derangement.
  • Ask about colour changes - pallor, cyanosis, or mottling may indicate poor perfusion, anaemia, or hypoxaemia.
  • Ask about temperature stability - difficulty maintaining body temperature (hypothermia) is common due to immature thermoregulation and lack of subcutaneous fat.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Prematurity (Child health)"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - tachypnoea, bradycardia, hypotension, or temperature instability are critical indicators of physiological compromise.
  • Check respiratory signs - grunting, nasal flaring, subcostal/intercostal retractions, and decreased air entry suggest respiratory distress syndrome.
  • Check neurological signs - altered consciousness, hypotonia, abnormal reflexes, or seizures may indicate intraventricular haemorrhage or hypoxic-ischaemic encephalopathy. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Intraventricular haemorrhage grading: what are the different grades of IVH and their clinical significance?" text="Intraventricular haemorrhage grading"][/sc]
  • Check abdominal signs - distension, tenderness, erythema of the abdominal wall, or absent bowel sounds are concerning for necrotising enterocolitis.
  • Check skin - jaundice, pallor, or petechiae can indicate hyperbilirubinaemia, anaemia, or sepsis respectively.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Prematurity (Child health)"][/sc]
  • Apnoea and bradycardia: Recurrent episodes, particularly if prolonged or associated with desaturation, indicate significant respiratory or neurological instability requiring urgent intervention.
  • Severe respiratory distress: Increasing oxygen requirements, persistent grunting, severe retractions, or cyanosis despite support suggest worsening lung disease (e.g., RDS, pneumonia) and potential respiratory failure.
  • Abdominal distension with bilious aspirates: Highly suspicious for necrotising enterocolitis, a life-threatening gastrointestinal emergency requiring immediate surgical review.
  • Seizures or sudden neurological deterioration: May signify intraventricular haemorrhage, periventricular leukomalacia, or severe metabolic disturbance, necessitating urgent neuroimaging and management.
  • Persistent hypothermia or hyperthermia: Despite appropriate environmental control, these can be signs of severe infection (sepsis) or metabolic crisis.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Prematurity (Child health)"][/sc]
  • Late preterm infants (34-36 weeks): Often appear healthier than their extremely preterm counterparts but are still at increased risk for respiratory distress, feeding difficulties, jaundice, and hypothermia compared to term infants, leading to delayed recognition of illness.
  • Infants with intrauterine growth restriction (IUGR): May have more severe complications for their gestational age due to chronic fetal stress and reduced physiological reserves, impacting their ability to tolerate stressors post-birth.
  • Infants of diabetic mothers: Prone to hypoglycaemia, respiratory distress syndrome (due to delayed lung maturation despite adequate gestational age), and cardiac anomalies, which can complicate the typical prematurity presentation.
  • Infants with congenital anomalies: The presence of structural abnormalities can mask or exacerbate typical prematurity-related issues, making diagnosis and management more complex. Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Prematurity (Child health)"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected complications:
    • Full blood count, C-reactive protein, blood cultures: Essential for evaluating sepsis.
    • Blood gas analysis: To assess respiratory status and metabolic balance.
    • Blood glucose: For monitoring and managing hypoglycaemia.
    • Electrolytes and renal function tests: To assess fluid balance and kidney function.
  • Imaging - Chest X-ray: Indicated for respiratory distress to diagnose respiratory distress syndrome (ground glass appearance), pneumonia, or pneumothorax.
  • Imaging - Cranial ultrasound: Routinely performed in very and extremely preterm infants to screen for and grade intraventricular haemorrhage (IVH) and periventricular leukomalacia. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Periventricular leukomalacia: what is PVL and its long-term neurological implications in preterm infants?" text="Periventricular leukomalacia"][/sc]
  • Imaging - Abdominal X-ray series: For suspected necrotising enterocolitis, looking for dilated bowel loops, pneumatosis intestinalis, or portal venous gas.
  • Severity/risk tools:
    • Apgar Score on MDCalc: Used at 1 and 5 minutes post-birth to assess immediate postnatal transition and need for resuscitation.
  • Serial reassessment: Regular clinical assessment, vital sign monitoring, and repeat investigations (e.g., blood gases, cranial ultrasounds) are crucial due to the dynamic nature of prematurity-related complications.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Prematurity (Child health)"][/sc]
  • Respiratory Distress Syndrome (RDS) - primary surfactant deficiency in immature lungs, typically presenting shortly after birth with progressive respiratory distress.
  • Transient Tachypnoea of the Newborn (TTN) - delayed clearance of fetal lung fluid, usually milder and self-resolving within 24-48 hours, more common in late preterm infants.
  • Neonatal Sepsis - systemic infection, can mimic RDS or NEC, often presenting with non-specific signs like lethargy, poor feeding, and temperature instability.
  • Congenital Heart Disease - structural cardiac anomalies can cause respiratory distress and cyanosis, but typically with specific murmurs or abnormal cardiac findings on examination.
  • Meconium Aspiration Syndrome (MAS) - aspiration of meconium into the lungs, more common in term or post-term infants, but can occur in preterm if meconium passage occurs in utero.
  • Pneumothorax - air leak from the lungs, presenting with sudden worsening of respiratory distress and asymmetry of chest expansion. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Pneumothorax in neonates: what are the causes and signs of pneumothorax in preterm infants?" text="Neonatal pneumothorax"][/sc]
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Prematurity (Child health)"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Recognition of preterm labour, administration of antenatal corticosteroids to the mother (if time permits and gestation is appropriate) to enhance fetal lung maturation, and preparation for specialist neonatal care at birth. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Antenatal corticosteroids: what is the mechanism of action and clinical benefit of antenatal corticosteroids in preventing prematurity complications?" text="Antenatal corticosteroids"][/sc]
  • Definitive management: Primarily supportive care in a neonatal intensive care unit (NICU), including respiratory support (e.g., CPAP, mechanical ventilation, surfactant administration), fluid and nutritional management (e.g., parenteral nutrition, expressed breast milk), and temperature control.
  • Adjunctive management: May include prophylactic antibiotics for suspected sepsis, management of patent ductus arteriosus (e.g., indomethacin, ibuprofen), and phototherapy for jaundice.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Continuous physiological monitoring, regular clinical assessments, and neurodevelopmental follow-up programmes are essential for early detection and intervention for long-term complications.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Clear communication with parents regarding potential complications and expected trajectory, with defined criteria for escalating care or seeking urgent medical review post-discharge.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Prematurity (Child health)"][/sc]
  • Respiratory Distress Syndrome (RDS): Due to surfactant deficiency, leading to alveolar collapse and impaired gas exchange, typically manifesting within hours of birth and requiring respiratory support.
  • Bronchopulmonary Dysplasia (BPD): Chronic lung disease resulting from lung injury (e.g., ventilation, oxygen toxicity) in the early weeks of life, characterised by persistent oxygen requirement and abnormal lung development.
  • Intraventricular Haemorrhage (IVH): Bleeding into the brain ventricles, particularly in very and extremely preterm infants, occurring within the first few days of life and carrying a risk of neurodevelopmental impairment depending on its grade.
  • Necrotising Enterocolitis (NEC): An acute inflammatory disease of the bowel, primarily affecting very low birth weight infants, presenting with abdominal distension, feeding intolerance, and bloody stools, often progressing rapidly.
  • Retinopathy of Prematurity (ROP): Abnormal blood vessel development in the retina of preterm infants, potentially leading to retinal detachment and blindness if untreated, typically screened for in infants born before 32 weeks or weighing less than 1500g.
  • Periventricular Leukomalacia (PVL): Ischaemic injury to the white matter around the cerebral ventricles, often associated with severe IVH or hypoxic-ischaemic events, leading to cerebral palsy and cognitive deficits, with long-term consequences becoming apparent in the months and years following birth.
  • Neurodevelopmental Impairment: A broad category encompassing cerebral palsy, cognitive delays, learning difficulties, and behavioural problems, which can manifest throughout childhood and adolescence, particularly in those born at earlier gestations or with significant complications.
