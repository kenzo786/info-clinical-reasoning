Raised intracranial pressure [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Raised intracranial pressure"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Raised intracranial pressure"][/sc]
  • Raised intracranial pressure (ICP) is a sustained elevation of pressure within the skull, typically above 20 mmHg, resulting from an increase in the volume of intracranial contents.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical features and potential causes of raised intracranial pressure, understanding its urgency and the need for prompt management.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Raised intracranial pressure"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Raised intracranial pressure"][/sc]
  • Incidence of conditions leading to raised ICP, such as traumatic brain injury, is significant in the UK, with an estimated 1.4 million A&E attendances annually for head injuries (NICE).
  • Risk factors include head trauma, intracranial haemorrhage, tumours, hydrocephalus, and infections like meningitis, affecting various age groups.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Raised intracranial pressure"][/sc]
  • An initial increase in intracranial volume (e.g., from a haematoma or oedema) triggers compensatory mechanisms, such as CSF displacement and venous compression, to maintain normal ICP.
  • As compensatory reserves are exhausted, further volume increases lead to a steep rise in ICP, reducing cerebral perfusion pressure and risking ischaemia.
  • Sustained high ICP can result in brain herniation, compressing vital brainstem structures and leading to irreversible neurological damage or death.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Raised intracranial pressure"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Symptoms may be subtle and non-specific, such as headache or nausea, developing over hours to days depending on the underlying cause.
  • Progressive phase: As ICP rises, symptoms worsen, including vomiting, altered mental status, and focal neurological deficits, particularly beyond 24-48 hours.
  • Late phase: Critical signs of brainstem compression, such as Cushing's triad and pupillary changes, can manifest rapidly over minutes to hours, indicating imminent herniation.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Raised intracranial pressure"][/sc]
  • Ask about headache - typically severe, generalised, worse on waking, coughing, or straining, indicating pressure changes.
  • Ask about nausea and vomiting - often projectile and not preceded by nausea, suggesting brainstem involvement.
  • Ask about visual disturbances - blurred vision, diplopia, or transient visual obscurations due to papilloedema or cranial nerve palsies.
  • Ask about altered mental status - ranging from irritability and confusion to drowsiness and coma, reflecting cerebral dysfunction.
  • Ask about seizures - new onset seizures can be a manifestation of underlying structural lesions causing raised ICP.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Raised intracranial pressure"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - look for Cushing's triad (hypertension, bradycardia, irregular respiration), a late and ominous sign of brainstem compression.
  • Check neurological examination - assess Glasgow Coma Scale (GCS) for level of consciousness, pupillary responses for asymmetry or dilation, and motor function for focal deficits.
  • Check fundoscopy - presence of papilloedema indicates chronic raised ICP, while acute changes may be absent in rapid onset.
  • Check cranial nerve examination - particularly for abducens nerve palsy (CN VI) causing diplopia, as it is sensitive to pressure changes.
  • Check neck stiffness - may indicate meningeal irritation, particularly in infective causes of raised ICP.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Raised intracranial pressure"][/sc]
  • Sudden onset severe headache - particularly "thunderclap" headache, suggesting subarachnoid haemorrhage or other acute intracranial pathology.
  • Progressive neurological deficit - worsening weakness, sensory loss, or speech disturbance, indicating an expanding lesion or increasing pressure.
  • Altered level of consciousness - any decline in GCS score, from drowsiness to coma, necessitates urgent assessment and intervention.
  • Pupillary asymmetry or non-reactivity - a critical sign of uncal herniation and brainstem compression, requiring immediate neurosurgical review.
  • Cushing's triad - hypertension, bradycardia, and irregular respiration, indicating imminent brainstem failure and herniation.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Raised intracranial pressure"][/sc]
  • Older adults: May present with subtle cognitive changes, falls, or gait disturbance rather than classic headache, making diagnosis challenging.
  • Infants: Bulging fontanelle, increased head circumference, irritability, and poor feeding are common signs, as sutures are not yet fused.
  • Idiopathic intracranial hypertension (IIH): Predominantly affects obese young women, presenting with headache and visual symptoms without an obvious cause of raised ICP.
  • Hydrocephalus: Can present acutely or chronically, with symptoms varying based on the rate of CSF accumulation and patient age.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Raised intracranial pressure"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected Raised ICP: Urgent CT head scan to identify mass lesions, haemorrhage, hydrocephalus, or signs of oedema.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Blood tests including FBC, U&Es, LFTs, coagulation screen, and inflammatory markers to rule out systemic causes or guide management.
  • Imaging - MRI brain: Provides more detailed imaging for subtle lesions, posterior fossa pathology, or venous sinus thrombosis, often following an initial CT.
  • Lumbar puncture: Contraindicated in suspected raised ICP until a mass lesion is excluded by imaging due to risk of herniation. If performed, measures opening pressure and allows CSF analysis for infection or inflammation.
  • Serial reassessment: Regular neurological observations (GCS, pupillary response, motor function) are crucial for monitoring progression and response to treatment.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Raised intracranial pressure"][/sc]
  • A 55-year-old male presents to A&E with a 3-day history of progressively worsening headache, associated with projectile vomiting and increasing drowsiness. On examination, he has bilateral papilloedema and a GCS of 12/15.
  • A middle-aged man with acute-on-chronic headache and vomiting, associated with objective neurological signs, raising concern for an urgent intracranial process.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Raised intracranial pressure"][/sc]
  • Brain tumour - often presents with progressive neurological deficits and signs of raised ICP.
  • Intracranial haemorrhage (e.g., subdural, epidural, subarachnoid) - acute onset severe headache, rapid neurological decline.
  • Hydrocephalus - impaired CSF flow leading to ventricular enlargement and raised ICP.
  • Meningitis/Encephalitis - fever, neck stiffness, altered mental status, signs of infection.
  • Idiopathic intracranial hypertension (IIH) - headache and visual symptoms, typically in obese young women, with no structural cause.
  • Cerebral venous sinus thrombosis - headache, seizures, focal deficits, often with risk factors for thrombosis.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Raised intracranial pressure"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Secure airway, breathing, and circulation (ABC). Elevate head of bed to 30 degrees. Consider hyperosmolar therapy (e.g., mannitol or hypertonic saline) to reduce cerebral oedema.
  • Definitive management: Address the underlying cause, which may involve neurosurgical intervention for mass lesions (e.g., haematoma evacuation, tumour resection) or CSF diversion (e.g., shunt placement for hydrocephalus).
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Continuous neurological monitoring, ICP monitoring in severe cases, and serial imaging to assess response to treatment and detect complications.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Urgent referral to neurosurgery or neurology for specialist assessment and management. Advise patients and carers on signs of worsening condition requiring immediate medical attention.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Raised intracranial pressure"][/sc]
  • Brain herniation: Occurs when increased pressure forces brain tissue across anatomical compartments, leading to brainstem compression and potentially fatal outcomes if not rapidly reversed.
  • Cerebral ischaemia/infarction: Reduced cerebral perfusion pressure due to high ICP can lead to inadequate blood supply to brain tissue, causing irreversible damage.
  • Permanent neurological deficit: Prolonged or severe raised ICP can result in lasting cognitive impairment, motor weakness, or sensory loss due to neuronal injury.
  • Hydrocephalus: Can be a cause or a consequence of raised ICP, where impaired CSF circulation leads to ventricular enlargement and further pressure elevation.
  • Seizures: May occur as a direct result of brain irritation from the underlying pathology or secondary to ischaemic damage.
