Nasal polyps [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Nasal polyps"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Nasal polyps"][/sc]
  • Nasal polyps are benign, painless, non-cancerous growths of the nasal or sinus mucosa, often associated with chronic inflammation.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical features of nasal polyps, differentiate between unilateral and bilateral presentations, and understand the implications for management and referral.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Nasal polyps"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Nasal polyps"][/sc]
  • Affects approximately 2-4% of the UK population, with a higher prevalence in adults, particularly those over 40 years old.
  • More common in males than females, with a male-to-female ratio of approximately 2:1.
  • Strongly associated with other atopic conditions; up to 40% of patients with nasal polyps also have asthma, and a significant proportion have aspirin sensitivity (Samter\'s triad).
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Nasal polyps"][/sc]
  • Trigger: Chronic inflammation of the sinonasal mucosa, often driven by allergic reactions, infections, or irritants, leading to oedema and fluid accumulation.
  • Progression: Sustained inflammation promotes remodelling of the extracellular matrix, resulting in prolapse of the oedematous mucosa into the nasal cavity, forming polyps. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Eosinophilic inflammation in nasal polyps: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Eosinophilic inflammation"][/sc]
  • Consequence: The polyps obstruct nasal airflow, impair mucociliary clearance, and can lead to secondary infections and further inflammation, creating a vicious cycle.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Nasal polyps"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Insidious onset of nasal congestion, reduced sense of smell, and post-nasal drip, often developing over months to years.
  • Progressive phase: Gradual enlargement of polyps leading to persistent nasal obstruction, facial pressure, and recurrent sinusitis, particularly beyond 6 months of symptoms.
  • Late phase: Established chronic rhinosinusitis with nasal polyps, characterised by significant impact on quality of life, potential for complications, and frequent recurrence even after treatment.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Nasal polyps"][/sc]
  • Ask about persistent nasal obstruction - often bilateral and progressive, leading to mouth breathing and snoring.
  • Ask about hyposmia or anosmia - a significant reduction or complete loss of the sense of smell, which can impact appetite and safety.
  • Ask about chronic post-nasal drip - often described as a sensation of mucus running down the back of the throat, leading to cough or throat clearing.
  • Ask about facial pressure or fullness - typically non-painful, located across the bridge of the nose or around the eyes, worse with bending forward.
  • Ask about recurrent episodes of acute sinusitis - due to impaired drainage and ventilation of the paranasal sinuses.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Nasal polyps"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - typically normal in uncomplicated nasal polyps; fever or tachycardia may suggest acute infection.
  • Check anterior rhinoscopy - reveals pale, glistening, mobile, grape-like masses originating from the middle meatus, often bilateral. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Unilateral nasal polyp: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Unilateral nasal polyp"][/sc]
  • Check for evidence of allergic rhinitis - such as allergic shiners, nasal crease, or pale, boggy nasal mucosa.
  • Check for signs of asthma - wheeze or prolonged expiratory phase on chest auscultation, particularly in patients with Samter\'s triad.
  • Check for facial asymmetry or proptosis - may indicate a more aggressive or malignant process, especially with unilateral polyps.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Nasal polyps"][/sc]
  • Unilateral nasal polyps: Raises concern for underlying malignancy (e.g., inverted papilloma, squamous cell carcinoma) or fungal sinusitis, warranting urgent ENT referral.
  • Epistaxis: Recurrent or severe nosebleeds, especially unilateral, may indicate an erosive or neoplastic process.
  • Orbital symptoms: Proptosis, diplopia, visual changes, or periorbital swelling suggest orbital invasion, requiring immediate specialist assessment.
  • Neurological symptoms: Headache, cranial nerve palsies, or altered mental status may indicate intracranial extension of disease.
  • Rapid growth or pain: A sudden increase in polyp size or onset of severe pain is atypical for benign polyps and warrants further investigation.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Nasal polyps"][/sc]
  • Children: Nasal polyps are rare in children; their presence should prompt investigation for cystic fibrosis, primary ciliary dyskinesia, or other underlying systemic conditions.
  • Samter\'s triad (Aspirin-Exacerbated Respiratory Disease - AERD): Patients present with asthma, recurrent nasal polyps, and aspirin sensitivity, often with severe and rapidly recurring disease. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Samter\'s triad: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Samter\'s triad"][/sc]
  • Immunocompromised individuals: May present with more aggressive or atypical infections (e.g., fungal sinusitis) mimicking or co-existing with nasal polyps.
  • Unilateral presentation: As noted, this is a red flag for malignancy or other serious pathology, particularly in older adults.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Nasal polyps"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected nasal polyps: Clinical examination with anterior rhinoscopy and flexible nasal endoscopy to visualise the polyps and assess their extent.
  • Severity/risk tools: The Sino-Nasal Outcome Test (SNOT-22) can be used to assess symptom severity and quality of life impact. SNOT-22 on MDCalc
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Biopsy of unilateral or atypical polyps to exclude malignancy or other specific pathologies.
  • Imaging - CT scan of paranasal sinuses: Provides detailed anatomical information, assesses the extent of disease, identifies bone erosion, and aids surgical planning. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Role of CT scan in nasal polyps: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="CT scan in nasal polyps"][/sc]
  • Serial reassessment: Regular endoscopic review to monitor polyp size, response to treatment, and detect recurrence.

  • A 55-year-old male presents with a 12-month history of progressive bilateral nasal obstruction, reduced sense of smell, and chronic post-nasal drip, with rhinoscopy revealing pale, glistening masses in both nasal cavities.
  • Middle-aged male with chronic, bilateral nasal obstruction and anosmia, concerning for nasal polyps.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Nasal polyps"][/sc]
  • Allergic rhinitis: Characterised by intermittent or seasonal symptoms, often with sneezing and itchy eyes, but typically without visible polyps on examination.
  • Chronic rhinosinusitis without polyps: Shares many symptoms but lacks the visible polypoid masses within the nasal cavity.
  • Turbinate hypertrophy: Enlargement of the normal nasal turbinates, which are firm and fixed, unlike the mobile and pale nasal polyps.
  • Foreign body: More common in children, typically unilateral, and often associated with purulent discharge and foul odour.
  • Inverted papilloma: A benign but locally aggressive tumour that can mimic a polyp, but is typically unilateral, friable, and carries a risk of malignant transformation. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Inverted papilloma vs nasal polyp: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Inverted papilloma"][/sc]
  • Malignancy (e.g., squamous cell carcinoma): Often unilateral, firm, friable, and associated with pain, epistaxis, or cranial nerve involvement.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Nasal polyps"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Recognition of red flag features and appropriate referral to ENT for further assessment and management.
  • Definitive management: Often includes topical intranasal corticosteroids to reduce inflammation and polyp size. Surgical polypectomy may be considered for large or refractory polyps.
  • Adjunctive management: Saline nasal irrigations may support mucociliary clearance and symptom relief. Oral corticosteroids may be used for short courses in severe cases.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Regular review to assess symptom control, polyp recurrence, and adherence to treatment.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Patients should be advised to seek urgent medical attention for worsening symptoms, new unilateral symptoms, visual changes, or severe pain.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Nasal polyps"][/sc]
  • Chronic rhinosinusitis: Persistent inflammation and obstruction can lead to recurrent bacterial infections, requiring antibiotic courses.
  • Anosmia/Hyposmia: Prolonged loss of smell can significantly impact quality of life, safety (e.g., inability to detect gas leaks), and nutritional intake.
  • Obstructive sleep apnoea: Large polyps can contribute to upper airway obstruction, leading to disturbed sleep, daytime somnolence, and cardiovascular strain.
  • Orbital complications: Rarely, severe inflammation or infection can extend into the orbit, causing periorbital cellulitis, abscess formation, or visual impairment, particularly if left untreated for an extended period.
  • Intracranial complications: Extremely rare but serious, including meningitis or brain abscess, typically arising from direct extension of infection through bone erosion, especially in cases of delayed diagnosis or inadequate management.
