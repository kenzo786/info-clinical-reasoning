Penile cancer [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Penile cancer"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Penile cancer"][/sc]
  • Penile cancer is a rare malignancy affecting the penis, typically originating from squamous cells of the glans or foreskin.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical features, risk factors, and diagnostic approach to penile cancer, including appropriate referral pathways.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Penile cancer"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Penile cancer"][/sc]
  • Incidence: Penile cancer is rare in the UK, with approximately 600 new cases diagnosed annually, predominantly affecting men over 60 years of age.
  • Risk factors: Key risk factors include phimosis, poor penile hygiene, human papillomavirus (HPV) infection (especially types 16 and 18), smoking, and a history of lichen sclerosus.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Penile cancer"][/sc]
  • Trigger: Chronic inflammation, often secondary to phimosis and poor hygiene, or persistent HPV infection, leads to cellular atypia and dysplasia in the penile epithelium.
  • Progression: Dysplastic changes can progress to carcinoma in situ and eventually invasive squamous cell carcinoma, which is the most common histological type.
  • Consequence: Local invasion into the penile shaft, corpora cavernosa, and urethra can occur, with lymphatic spread to inguinal lymph nodes being a critical prognostic factor.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Penile cancer"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Often presents as a painless lesion, ulcer, or growth on the glans or foreskin, which may be present for several months before seeking medical attention.
  • Progressive phase: The lesion may enlarge, become painful, or cause discharge, with potential for local invasion and development of palpable inguinal lymphadenopathy, particularly beyond 6-12 months.
  • Late phase: Advanced disease can lead to extensive local destruction, urethral involvement causing obstructive symptoms, and distant metastatic spread, often beyond 1-2 years without intervention.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Penile cancer"][/sc]
  • Ask about a persistent lesion or ulcer on the penis - often painless initially, which can delay presentation.
  • Ask about any abnormal discharge or bleeding from the penis - may indicate ulceration or infection within the lesion.
  • Ask about changes in urination, such as dysuria or obstructive symptoms - suggests urethral involvement or local mass effect.
  • Ask about pain in the penis or groin - typically a feature of more advanced or infected lesions, or nodal involvement.
  • Ask about any lumps or swelling in the groin - indicative of potential inguinal lymphadenopathy due to metastatic spread.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Penile cancer"][/sc]
  • Check for a visible lesion on the glans, foreskin, or shaft - may appear as an ulcer, papule, nodule, or fungating mass.
  • Check for phimosis or inability to retract the foreskin - often associated with sub-preputial lesions and poor hygiene.
  • Check for palpable inguinal lymph nodes - assess size, consistency, mobility, and tenderness, as this is crucial for staging.
  • Check for signs of local invasion, such as fixation to underlying structures or urethral involvement - may manifest as a palpable mass or stricture.
  • Check vital signs - typically normal in early disease, but fever or tachycardia may indicate infection or advanced systemic illness.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Penile cancer"][/sc]
  • Persistent penile lesion: Any non-healing ulcer, growth, or discolouration on the penis, particularly if present for more than 4-6 weeks, warrants urgent specialist referral.
  • Palpable inguinal lymphadenopathy: Enlarged, firm, or fixed lymph nodes in the groin suggest regional metastatic spread and require prompt investigation.
  • Urethral involvement: Symptoms such as haematuria, dysuria, or obstructive voiding indicate potential invasion into the urethra, necessitating urgent urological assessment.
  • Rapid growth or pain: A sudden increase in lesion size or onset of significant pain may signify aggressive tumour behaviour or secondary infection.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Penile cancer"][/sc]
  • Older adults: May present with long-standing, neglected lesions due to embarrassment or lack of awareness, leading to more advanced disease at diagnosis.
  • Immunosuppressed individuals: Patients with HIV or those on immunosuppressive therapy may have more aggressive disease or unusual histological variants.
  • Mimicking benign conditions: Early lesions can be mistaken for sexually transmitted infections, fungal infections, or benign dermatological conditions, delaying diagnosis.
  • Hidden lesions: In uncircumcised men with phimosis, the lesion may be entirely sub-preputial, making visual inspection difficult and requiring careful palpation.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Penile cancer"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected penile cancer: Biopsy of the penile lesion (incisional or excisional) is mandatory for histological confirmation and grading.
  • Imaging - Ultrasound: Ultrasound of the penis can assess local tumour extent and depth of invasion.
  • Imaging - MRI: MRI of the penis is often used for detailed local staging, particularly to assess invasion into the corpora cavernosa or urethra.
  • Imaging - CT scan: CT scan of the chest, abdomen, and pelvis is used to stage regional lymph nodes and detect distant metastases.
  • Imaging - PET-CT: May be considered for detecting occult nodal or distant metastatic disease in selected cases.
