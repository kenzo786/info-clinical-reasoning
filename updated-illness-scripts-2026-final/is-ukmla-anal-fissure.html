Anal Fissure [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Anal Fissure"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Anal Fissure"][/sc]
  • A linear tear or ulcer in the squamous epithelium of the distal anal canal, most commonly located in the posterior midline.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the characteristic presentation of an anal fissure, distinguish it from other causes of anal pain and bleeding, and initiate appropriate conservative management while knowing when to refer for specialist treatment.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Anal Fissure"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Anal Fissure"][/sc]
  • Anal fissures have an estimated incidence of 1.1 per 1000 person-years in the UK, affecting both sexes equally and peaking in young adulthood (20-40 years).
  • Key risk factors include constipation, passing hard stools, persistent diarrhoea, childbirth, and inflammatory bowel disease (particularly Crohn\'s disease).
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Anal Fissure"][/sc]
  • Trigger: Local trauma to the anoderm, typically from the passage of a hard, large stool, creates a tear in the mucosal lining.
  • Progression: The underlying [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Internal anal sphincter spasm: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="internal anal sphincter spasm"][/sc] increases resting anal pressure, which reduces blood flow to the fissure site (particularly in the poorly perfused posterior midline), impairing healing.
  • Consequence: This cycle of pain, spasm, and ischaemia prevents healing, leading to the development of a chronic fissure, often with associated features like a sentinel pile or hypertrophied anal papilla.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Anal Fissure"][/sc]
  • Early phase: An acute fissure presents with severe pain during and after defecation, lasting from minutes to a few hours, often with bright red bleeding.
  • Progressive phase: If the fissure persists beyond 6 weeks (becoming chronic), the pain may become less intense but more prolonged, and a sentinel skin tag may become noticeable.
  • Late phase: In refractory cases, the fissure becomes fibrotic and indurated, with a high likelihood of recurrence and potential for complications like stenosis if not managed effectively.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Anal Fissure"][/sc]
  • Ask about anal pain - typically described as a severe, sharp, "tearing" or "like passing glass" sensation during defecation, which helps distinguish it from the dull ache of haemorrhoids.
  • Ask about post-defecation burning or spasm - a prolonged, gnawing pain lasting for several hours after opening the bowels is highly characteristic and points towards internal sphincter hypertonia.
  • Ask about bleeding - usually a small amount of bright red blood seen on toilet paper or streaking the stool, rather than a large volume mixed in.
  • Ask about fear of defecation and constipation - patients often withhold stool to avoid the severe pain, leading to a vicious cycle of worsening constipation and harder stools.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Anal Fissure"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - usually normal, but tachycardia could indicate severe pain or anxiety. Fever is a red flag for an alternative diagnosis like an abscess.
  • Check by gentle parting of the buttocks - a linear tear is often visible in the posterior midline without an internal examination, which should be avoided if acutely painful.
  • Check for features of chronicity - a visible [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Sentinel pile: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="sentinel pile"][/sc] (a skin tag at the external end of the fissure) or a hypertrophied anal papilla internally suggests a chronic fissure.
  • Check for anal sphincter tone - digital rectal examination (if tolerated) often reveals marked hypertonia and tenderness of the internal anal sphincter.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Anal Fissure"][/sc]
  • Systemic symptoms (fever, weight loss) - may indicate an underlying inflammatory condition like Crohn\'s disease or malignancy.
  • Large volume or dark-coloured bleeding - suggests a more proximal source of bleeding in the gastrointestinal tract that requires urgent investigation.
  • Atypical location - fissures located off the midline (lateral) are suspicious for secondary causes such as Crohn\'s disease, HIV, syphilis, or tuberculosis.
  • Induration or rolled edges - raises suspicion for anal cancer (squamous cell carcinoma) and mandates a biopsy.
  • Faecal incontinence - not a typical feature of a fissure and suggests alternative neuromuscular pathology or a complication of previous surgery.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Anal Fissure"][/sc]
  • Older adults: May present with less severe pain but have a higher index of suspicion for malignancy, especially if there are changes in bowel habit.
  • Postpartum women: Fissures are common after vaginal delivery; they are more likely to be in the anterior midline and can be mistaken for perineal tears.
  • Immunosuppressed patients: May present with larger, deeper, or multiple fissures, and have a higher risk of secondary infection or atypical causes like CMV or herpes simplex virus.
  • Patients with Crohn\'s disease: Fissures are often multiple, deep, painless, and may be associated with other perianal features like large skin tags, fistulae, or abscesses. Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Anal Fissure"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected anal fissure: Diagnosis is primarily clinical, based on a characteristic history and visualisation of the fissure on gentle perianal inspection. A digital rectal examination should be deferred if pain is severe.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Consider swabs for STIs (syphilis, herpes) if there are atypical features. Blood tests (FBC, CRP, ESR) may be useful if inflammatory bowel disease is suspected.
  • Endoscopy: Proctoscopy, sigmoidoscopy, or colonoscopy may be required to exclude other pathologies like IBD or malignancy, particularly in the presence of red flags or atypical features. This is often performed under anaesthesia if pain is prohibitive.
  • Serial reassessment: A crucial diagnostic step is to review the patient after a period of conservative treatment; a healing fissure supports the diagnosis, while a non-healing fissure warrants further investigation.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Anal Fissure"][/sc]
  • A 30-year-old woman presents with a 3-week history of severe, sharp anal pain during defecation, followed by several hours of burning discomfort, and reports seeing bright red blood on the toilet paper.
  • This is a presentation of acute proctalgia with rectal bleeding, where the key features are highly suggestive of an acute anal fissure secondary to internal anal sphincter hypertonia.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Anal Fissure"][/sc]
  • Thrombosed external haemorrhoid: Presents with acute, constant, severe anal pain and a palpable, tender perianal lump, unlike the cyclical pain of a fissure.
  • Perianal abscess/fistula: Characterised by constant, throbbing pain, swelling, and potentially systemic signs like fever, which are not typical of an uncomplicated fissure.
  • Crohn\'s disease: May present with atypical (e.g., lateral, multiple, painless) fissures and is often associated with other signs like large skin tags, systemic symptoms, or diarrhoea. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Crohn\'s disease and anal fissures: what are the distinguishing features?" text="Crohn\'s disease"][/sc]
  • Sexually transmitted infections (STIs): Conditions like syphilis or herpes can cause anal ulceration, which may mimic a fissure but often have associated lesions or systemic features.
  • Anal cancer: A rare but important differential, especially in older patients or those with atypical fissures (indurated, rolled edges), requiring biopsy for definitive diagnosis.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Anal Fissure"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Focus on supportive care, including adequate analgesia and advice on stool-softening measures (high-fibre diet, increased fluid intake).
  • Definitive management: For acute fissures, conservative measures are first-line. For chronic fissures (persisting >6 weeks), topical treatments to reduce internal anal sphincter tone are often warranted, such as glyceryl trinitrate (GTN) or diltiazem ointment.
  • Adjunctive management: Warm sitz baths can provide symptomatic relief by helping to relax the anal sphincter.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Patients should be reviewed after several weeks to assess for healing. Failure to heal with topical therapy after 6-8 weeks typically warrants referral to a colorectal specialist.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Advise patients to return if pain becomes constant, if they develop a fever, or if bleeding increases significantly, as this may indicate a complication or alternative diagnosis.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Anal Fissure"][/sc]
  • Chronic fissure: Failure to heal after 6-8 weeks, leading to a persistent cycle of pain and spasm that often requires medical or surgical intervention.
  • Anal stenosis: Repeated cycles of inflammation and scarring can cause a narrowing of the anal canal, leading to worsening constipation and significant pain.
  • Fistula-in-ano: Rarely, a chronic fissure can deepen and result in the formation of an abnormal tract to the perianal skin, particularly if there is an underlying cause like Crohn\'s disease.
  • Sentinel pile: A fibrous skin tag that develops at the external aspect of a chronic fissure, which can cause irritation and hygiene difficulties.
  • Recurrence: Fissures can recur after healing, especially if underlying constipation and straining are not effectively managed long-term.
