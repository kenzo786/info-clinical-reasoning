Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Respiratory arrest (Lungs, pleura and airways)"][/sc]
  • Respiratory arrest is the cessation of effective breathing, leading to inadequate oxygenation and carbon dioxide elimination. It is a life-threatening emergency requiring immediate intervention.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the signs of impending respiratory arrest, differentiate it from cardiac arrest, and initiate appropriate immediate management including basic life support and escalation.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Respiratory arrest (Lungs, pleura and airways)"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Respiratory arrest (Lungs, pleura and airways)"][/sc]
  • Respiratory arrest is a common pathway for critical illness, particularly in conditions like severe asthma exacerbations, COPD, and drug overdose.
  • In the UK, respiratory conditions are a significant cause of emergency admissions and critical care interventions, often preceding respiratory arrest.
  • Risk factors include pre-existing lung disease (e.g., COPD, asthma), neurological conditions affecting respiratory drive, severe infections (e.g., pneumonia, sepsis), trauma, and drug toxicity.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Respiratory arrest (Lungs, pleura and airways)"][/sc]
  • Trigger: Respiratory arrest often results from progressive respiratory failure, where the body can no longer maintain adequate gas exchange. This can be due to airway obstruction, impaired respiratory drive, or neuromuscular weakness.
  • Progression: Sustained hypoventilation leads to profound hypoxaemia and hypercapnia. The body's compensatory mechanisms, such as increased respiratory effort and tachycardia, eventually fail. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Hypoxic vs hypercapnic respiratory failure: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Hypoxic vs hypercapnic respiratory failure"][/sc]
  • Consequence: Severe hypoxaemia and acidosis lead to cellular dysfunction, particularly in vital organs like the brain and heart, culminating in loss of consciousness and potentially cardiac arrest if not reversed promptly.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Respiratory arrest (Lungs, pleura and airways)"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Rapid deterioration of respiratory function, often over minutes to a few hours, characterised by increasing work of breathing, accessory muscle use, and declining oxygen saturation.
  • Progressive phase: As respiratory fatigue sets in, respiratory rate may paradoxically decrease, leading to reduced tidal volume and worsening gas exchange. This can occur over minutes.
  • Late phase: Complete cessation of breathing, typically within minutes of the progressive phase, if underlying causes are not addressed. Brain injury can occur within 4-6 minutes of complete respiratory arrest.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Respiratory arrest (Lungs, pleura and airways)"][/sc]
  • Ask about severe breathlessness - indicating significant respiratory compromise and impending failure.
  • Ask about inability to speak in full sentences - suggesting severe dyspnoea and inadequate ventilatory capacity.
  • Ask about altered mental status (confusion, drowsiness) - may signify hypoxaemia or hypercapnia, which can precede respiratory arrest.
  • Ask about recent history of choking or foreign body aspiration - a direct cause of airway obstruction.
  • Ask about drug overdose or exposure to toxins - can cause central respiratory depression.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Respiratory arrest (Lungs, pleura and airways)"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - profound bradycardia or asystole may indicate cardiac arrest secondary to respiratory arrest. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Distinction from cardiac arrest: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Distinction from cardiac arrest"][/sc]
  • Check respiratory rate and effort - absent or agonal breathing is diagnostic of respiratory arrest. Prior signs may include tachypnoea, accessory muscle use, and paradoxical abdominal breathing.
  • Check oxygen saturation - typically very low or unrecordable.
  • Check level of consciousness - unresponsiveness or coma.
  • Check airway patency - signs of obstruction (e.g., stridor, gurgling, silent chest).
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Respiratory arrest (Lungs, pleura and airways)"][/sc]
  • Absent or agonal breathing - immediate indication for basic life support and emergency medical assistance.
  • Unresponsiveness - suggests severe cerebral hypoxaemia or hypercapnia requiring urgent airway and breathing support.
  • Cyanosis - a late sign of severe hypoxaemia, indicating critical oxygen deprivation.
  • Profound bradycardia or asystole - often a consequence of prolonged respiratory arrest, necessitating advanced life support.
  • Fixed dilated pupils - may indicate severe cerebral anoxia, particularly if sustained for several minutes.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Respiratory arrest (Lungs, pleura and airways)"][/sc]
  • Opioid overdose: Respiratory depression can be profound and insidious, leading to respiratory arrest without significant initial respiratory distress. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Opioid-induced respiratory depression: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Opioid-induced respiratory depression"][/sc]
  • Neuromuscular disorders: Patients with conditions like Guillain-Barré syndrome or myasthenia gravis may develop progressive respiratory muscle weakness, leading to respiratory arrest without overt airway obstruction.
  • Children: Respiratory arrest in children often follows respiratory failure, with initial signs of distress that can rapidly progress to fatigue and cessation of breathing.
  • Elderly: May present with more subtle signs of respiratory compromise, and their physiological reserves are often diminished, leading to faster deterioration.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Respiratory arrest (Lungs, pleura and airways)"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected respiratory arrest: Clinical assessment of airway, breathing, and circulation (ABCDE approach). [sc name="jitl-query" Q="ABCDE approach in emergencies: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="ABCDE approach"][/sc]
  • Severity/risk tools: While not directly for respiratory arrest, tools like the Modified Early Warning Score (MEWS) on MDCalc can identify patients at risk of deterioration.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Arterial blood gas analysis to assess severity of hypoxaemia and hypercapnia, and to identify metabolic acidosis.
  • Imaging - Chest X-ray: May identify underlying causes such as pneumonia, pneumothorax, or pulmonary oedema.
  • Serial reassessment: Continuous monitoring of oxygen saturation, heart rate, and respiratory effort is crucial during resuscitation.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Respiratory arrest (Lungs, pleura and airways)"][/sc]
  • Cardiac arrest - distinguished by the absence of a pulse and typically different initial rhythm on ECG.
  • Severe airway obstruction (e.g., foreign body, anaphylaxis) - often presents with stridor or complete inability to vocalise, but breathing may be attempted initially.
  • Central nervous system depression (e.g., opioid overdose, stroke, head injury) - can cause respiratory arrest due to impaired respiratory drive, often with pinpoint pupils in opioid overdose. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Causes of central respiratory depression: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Central respiratory depression"][/sc]
  • Neuromuscular paralysis (e.g., Guillain-Barré, botulism) - progressive weakness leading to respiratory muscle failure, often with preserved consciousness initially.
  • Severe metabolic derangement (e.g., diabetic ketoacidosis, severe electrolyte imbalance) - can lead to altered mental status and respiratory compromise, but usually not primary respiratory arrest.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Respiratory arrest (Lungs, pleura and airways)"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Recognition of respiratory arrest, immediate call for help, initiation of basic life support (airway opening, bag-mask ventilation). [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Bag-mask ventilation technique: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Bag-mask ventilation"][/sc]
  • Definitive management: Addressing the underlying cause once immediate life threats are managed. This may involve reversal agents (e.g., naloxone for opioid overdose), treatment of anaphylaxis, or management of severe asthma/COPD exacerbations.
  • Adjunctive management: Securing the airway with advanced techniques (e.g., endotracheal intubation) and mechanical ventilation as indicated.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Continuous monitoring in a critical care setting, with ongoing assessment of respiratory function and neurological status.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Timely involvement of critical care teams and clear communication with family regarding prognosis and ongoing care.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Respiratory arrest (Lungs, pleura and airways)"][/sc]
  • Anoxic brain injury: Prolonged cerebral hypoxaemia, even for a few minutes, can lead to irreversible neurological damage and long-term cognitive impairment.
  • Cardiac arrest: Respiratory arrest frequently progresses to cardiac arrest due to severe hypoxaemia and acidosis, significantly worsening prognosis.
  • Multi-organ failure: Systemic hypoxaemia can lead to dysfunction of other vital organs, including kidneys and liver, particularly if resuscitation is delayed.
  • Aspiration pneumonia: Impaired airway reflexes during respiratory arrest or resuscitation attempts can lead to aspiration of gastric contents, resulting in pneumonia.
  • Post-resuscitation syndrome: A complex systemic inflammatory response that can occur after successful resuscitation from cardiac arrest secondary to respiratory arrest, contributing to ongoing organ dysfunction.
