Haemochromatosis [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Haemochromatosis"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Haemochromatosis"][/sc]
  • Haemochromatosis is an inherited condition characterised by excessive iron absorption and progressive iron overload, leading to iron deposition in various organs.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical features of iron overload, interpret relevant biochemical and genetic tests, and understand the principles of management for hereditary haemochromatosis.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Haemochromatosis"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Haemochromatosis"][/sc]
  • Genetic haemochromatosis (GH) is the most common inherited condition in Northern Europe, with an overall prevalence in the United Kingdom of approximately 1 in 156 individuals.
  • It predominantly affects people of white northern European background, particularly those with Celtic ancestry, due to a specific mutation in the HFE gene (p.C282Y homozygosity).
  • Clinical manifestations typically emerge between the ages of 30 and 60 years, though women may present later due to physiological blood loss from menstruation and pregnancy.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Haemochromatosis"][/sc]
  • A genetic mutation, most commonly C282Y homozygosity in the HFE gene, leads to reduced production or function of hepcidin, the master regulator of iron metabolism.
  • This hepcidin deficiency results in increased iron absorption from the diet in the small intestine and impaired iron release from macrophages, causing systemic iron overload. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Hepcidin: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="hepcidin deficiency"][/sc]
  • Excess iron accumulates in parenchymal cells of various organs, including the liver, heart, pancreas, and pituitary gland, leading to oxidative stress, tissue damage, and organ dysfunction.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Haemochromatosis"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Iron accumulation is asymptomatic, often detected incidentally through elevated transferrin saturation or serum ferritin levels, typically before significant organ damage occurs.
  • Progressive phase: As iron overload advances, non-specific symptoms such as fatigue, arthralgia, and abdominal pain may develop, often presenting insidiously over months to years.
  • Late phase: Untreated, significant iron deposition leads to irreversible organ damage, including cirrhosis, diabetes, cardiomyopathy, and hypogonadism, particularly beyond decades of disease progression.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Haemochromatosis"][/sc]
  • Ask about fatigue - often profound and persistent, disproportionate to activity, indicating early systemic effects of iron overload.
  • Ask about joint pain - particularly in the metacarpophalangeal joints (2nd and 3rd), a classic presentation of haemochromatosis-related arthropathy.
  • Ask about skin changes - bronze or greyish skin pigmentation, often subtle, resulting from melanin and iron deposition.
  • Ask about abdominal pain - non-specific, but may indicate liver enlargement or early cirrhosis.
  • Ask about sexual dysfunction - erectile dysfunction in men and irregular periods in women, suggesting pituitary or gonadal iron deposition.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Haemochromatosis"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - may be normal in early stages; however, signs of heart failure (e.g., tachycardia, peripheral oedema) may be present in advanced cardiomyopathy.
  • Check skin - for bronze or slate-grey pigmentation, particularly in sun-exposed areas and flexural creases.
  • Check liver - for hepatomegaly or signs of chronic liver disease (e.g., jaundice, ascites, spider naevi) indicating cirrhosis.
  • Check joints - for arthropathy, especially in the 2nd and 3rd metacarpophalangeal joints, characterised by swelling and tenderness.
  • Check endocrine function - for signs of hypogonadism (e.g., testicular atrophy) or diabetes (e.g., acanthosis nigricans, signs of neuropathy). [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Bronze diabetes: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="bronze diabetes"][/sc]
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Haemochromatosis"][/sc]
  • Persistent fatigue and arthralgia - in a patient with a family history of haemochromatosis or northern European ancestry, warranting investigation for iron overload.
  • Unexplained liver dysfunction - elevated liver enzymes or signs of cirrhosis, particularly in the absence of other common causes, indicating significant hepatic iron deposition.
  • New-onset diabetes with skin pigmentation - "bronze diabetes" is a classic, albeit late, manifestation of advanced haemochromatosis, requiring urgent assessment.
  • Cardiomyopathy or arrhythmias - unexplained cardiac symptoms, especially in younger individuals, may signify cardiac iron overload and risk of heart failure.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Haemochromatosis"][/sc]
  • Women: Often present later than men due to physiological iron loss through menstruation and pregnancy, delaying the onset of symptoms and diagnosis.
  • Younger individuals (juvenile haemochromatosis): A rare, severe form with earlier onset of symptoms and rapid progression of organ damage, often due to mutations in genes other than HFE.
  • Non-HFE related haemochromatosis: Caused by mutations in genes like HJV, HAMP, TFR2, or FPN1, leading to varying clinical phenotypes and iron loading patterns, which may not fit the classic HFE-related picture. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Non-HFE haemochromatosis types: what are they and how do they differ?" text="Non-HFE haemochromatosis"][/sc]
  • Asymptomatic individuals: Increasingly identified through incidental findings of elevated iron studies or cascade screening of family members, highlighting the importance of early detection before organ damage.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Haemochromatosis"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected haemochromatosis:
    • Serum ferritin: often elevated, reflecting total body iron stores.
    • Transferrin saturation (TSAT): typically elevated (>45% in men, >40% in women) and is the most sensitive initial screening test. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Transferrin saturation threshold for haemochromatosis: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="transferrin saturation threshold"][/sc]
    • HFE gene testing: confirms the diagnosis of HFE-related haemochromatosis (e.g., C282Y homozygosity).
  • Severity/risk tools:
    • Liver biopsy: historically used to quantify hepatic iron concentration and assess for fibrosis/cirrhosis, though increasingly replaced by non-invasive methods.
    • MRI liver iron quantification: a non-invasive method to accurately measure hepatic iron concentration.
    • Ferritin Index for Hemochromatosis on MDCalc: a simple calculation to assess the likelihood of HFE-related haemochromatosis.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible:
    • Full blood count, liver function tests, renal function tests, inflammatory markers, viral hepatitis serology, autoantibodies (for other causes of liver disease).
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Haemochromatosis"][/sc]
  • A 55-year-old male presents with chronic fatigue, arthralgia in his hands, and new-onset diabetes, with a family history of liver disease.
  • Middle-aged male with insidious onset of multi-systemic symptoms and biochemical evidence of iron overload, concerning for hereditary haemochromatosis.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Haemochromatosis"][/sc]
  • Non-alcoholic fatty liver disease (NAFLD) - often associated with metabolic syndrome, but typically without significantly elevated transferrin saturation or HFE gene mutations.
  • Alcoholic liver disease - history of significant alcohol intake, though can coexist with haemochromatosis and complicate diagnosis.
  • Chronic viral hepatitis (B or C) - positive serology for viral markers, distinct from genetic iron overload.
  • Porphyria cutanea tarda - presents with blistering skin lesions and photosensitivity, often associated with liver disease and iron overload, but distinct pathophysiology. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Porphyria cutanea tarda vs Haemochromatosis: how to differentiate?" text="Porphyria cutanea tarda"][/sc]
  • Metabolic syndrome - shares features like diabetes and liver steatosis, but iron overload is not the primary driver.
  • Rheumatoid arthritis - causes arthralgia, but typically with inflammatory markers and specific autoantibodies, and without systemic iron overload.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Haemochromatosis"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Early diagnosis and initiation of iron reduction therapy to prevent irreversible organ damage and improve long-term outcomes.
  • Definitive management: Therapeutic venesection (phlebotomy) is the cornerstone of treatment, involving regular removal of blood to deplete iron stores until ferritin levels are within the target range.
  • Adjunctive management: Dietary advice often includes avoiding iron-rich foods, iron and vitamin C supplements, and excessive alcohol consumption to minimise further iron loading.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Regular monitoring of serum ferritin and transferrin saturation is essential to guide venesection frequency and maintain iron levels within the therapeutic range.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Patients with advanced organ damage (e.g., cirrhosis, cardiomyopathy) require specialist multidisciplinary care and regular screening for complications like hepatocellular carcinoma.
  • Exam pharmacology reference (UKMLA/MRCGP): Iron chelation therapy (e.g., [sc name="jitl-medication" Q="Deferasirox use in Haemochromatosis" text="deferasirox"][/sc], [sc name="jitl-medication" Q="Desferrioxamine use in Haemochromatosis" text="desferrioxamine"][/sc]) may be considered for patients who cannot tolerate venesection or have specific contraindications. Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Haemochromatosis"][/sc]
  • Cirrhosis: Chronic iron overload in the liver leads to fibrosis and ultimately cirrhosis, significantly increasing the risk of liver failure and hepatocellular carcinoma, particularly after decades of untreated disease.
  • Hepatocellular carcinoma: A major long-term complication of haemochromatosis-induced cirrhosis, necessitating regular surveillance in affected individuals.
  • Diabetes mellitus: Iron deposition in the pancreas can impair insulin production and action, leading to "bronze diabetes," often developing in the progressive to late phases.
  • Cardiomyopathy: Myocardial iron deposition can cause restrictive or dilated cardiomyopathy, leading to heart failure and arrhythmias, typically in advanced stages of iron overload.
  • Arthropathy: Iron deposition in joints, especially the metacarpophalangeal joints, causes a characteristic arthritis that can be debilitating and progressive over time.
  • Hypogonadism: Iron accumulation in the pituitary gland or gonads can lead to reduced sex hormone production, resulting in erectile dysfunction, loss of libido, and amenorrhoea, often in the progressive phase.
