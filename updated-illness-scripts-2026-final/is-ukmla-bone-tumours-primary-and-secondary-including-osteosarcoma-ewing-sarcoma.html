Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Bone tumours (primary and secondary including osteosarcoma, Ewing sarcoma)"][/sc]
  • Bone tumours are abnormal growths of cells within bone tissue, which can be either benign (non-cancerous) or malignant (cancerous), originating from bone cells (primary) or spreading from other parts of the body (secondary/metastatic).
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical features and initial management of primary and secondary bone tumours, including osteosarcoma and Ewing sarcoma.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Bone tumours (primary and secondary including osteosarcoma, Ewing sarcoma)"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Bone tumours (primary and secondary including osteosarcoma, Ewing sarcoma)"][/sc]
  • Primary bone tumours are rare, accounting for less than 0.2% of all cancers in the UK, with osteosarcoma and Ewing sarcoma being the most common primary malignant types in children and young adults.
  • Secondary bone tumours are far more common, particularly in older adults, often arising from primary cancers of the breast, prostate, lung, kidney, and thyroid.
  • Risk factors for primary bone tumours can include genetic predispositions (e.g., Li-Fraumeni syndrome for osteosarcoma) and prior radiation exposure.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Bone tumours (primary and secondary including osteosarcoma, Ewing sarcoma)"][/sc]
  • Genetic mutations or chromosomal translocations (e.g., EWSR1-FLI1 fusion in Ewing sarcoma) can trigger uncontrolled proliferation of abnormal cells within bone tissue.
  • As these abnormal cells multiply, they can disrupt normal bone architecture, leading to bone destruction (lytic lesions), excessive bone formation (sclerotic lesions), or a mixed pattern.
  • This progressive disruption of bone integrity results in pain, pathological fractures, and potentially systemic symptoms due to tumour burden or metastatic spread to distant sites.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Bone tumours (primary and secondary including osteosarcoma, Ewing sarcoma)"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Insidious onset of localised bone pain, often worse at night or with activity, which may be dismissed as growing pains or musculoskeletal strain, particularly beyond a few weeks.
  • Progressive phase: Increasing severity and persistence of pain, development of a palpable mass, swelling, or tenderness over the affected bone, and potential limitation of movement, typically over several months.
  • Late phase: Pathological fractures, systemic symptoms such as weight loss, fatigue, fever, and signs of metastatic disease (e.g., respiratory symptoms from lung metastases), often beyond 6-12 months from symptom onset.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Bone tumours (primary and secondary including osteosarcoma, Ewing sarcoma)"][/sc]
  • Ask about persistent or worsening localised bone pain - often a cardinal symptom, particularly if it is worse at night or not relieved by rest, suggesting a neoplastic rather than inflammatory or mechanical cause.
  • Ask about swelling or a palpable mass - indicates local tumour growth and can be an early sign, especially in superficial bones.
  • Ask about unexplained weight loss, fatigue, or fever - these constitutional symptoms may suggest malignancy and systemic involvement.
  • Ask about recent trauma or minor injury preceding pain - a pathological fracture may occur through weakened bone, often with disproportionately severe pain for the mechanism of injury.
  • Ask about neurological symptoms (e.g., weakness, numbness) - may indicate spinal cord compression or nerve impingement from tumour growth, particularly in vertebral tumours.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Bone tumours (primary and secondary including osteosarcoma, Ewing sarcoma)"][/sc]
  • Check for a palpable, firm, and often tender mass over the affected bone - suggests local tumour presence and may be fixed to underlying structures.
  • Check for localised warmth, erythema, or oedema - can indicate increased vascularity or inflammation associated with rapidly growing tumours, particularly Ewing sarcoma.
  • Check for restricted range of motion in adjacent joints - tumour proximity or size can mechanically impede movement or cause pain on articulation.
  • Check for neurological deficits (e.g., motor weakness, sensory loss, altered reflexes) - crucial if the tumour is near the spine or peripheral nerves, indicating potential compression.
  • Check vital signs - persistent fever, tachycardia, or unexplained weight loss may be present in advanced or metastatic disease. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Constitutional symptoms in malignancy: what are they and why do they matter clinically?" text="Constitutional symptoms"][/sc]
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Bone tumours (primary and secondary including osteosarcoma, Ewing sarcoma)"][/sc]
  • Persistent, unexplained bone pain: especially if worse at night, not relieved by rest, or progressive, as this is highly suggestive of a neoplastic process.
  • Palpable, growing mass: any new or enlarging lump associated with bone should prompt urgent investigation due to the risk of malignancy.
  • Pathological fracture: a fracture occurring with minimal or no trauma, indicating underlying bone weakening, often by a tumour.
  • Systemic symptoms: unexplained weight loss, night sweats, fever, or profound fatigue can indicate advanced malignancy or metastatic disease.
  • Neurological deficits: new or worsening weakness, numbness, or bowel/bladder dysfunction, particularly with spinal involvement, necessitates urgent assessment for spinal cord compression.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Bone tumours (primary and secondary including osteosarcoma, Ewing sarcoma)"][/sc]
  • Older adults: Primary bone tumours are rare, but secondary (metastatic) bone tumours are common. Presentation may be insidious with vague aches or fatigue, and a pathological fracture might be the first sign of an unknown primary cancer.
  • Children and adolescents: Pain may be attributed to 'growing pains' or sports injuries, leading to diagnostic delay. Ewing sarcoma can mimic osteomyelitis with fever and localised inflammation.
  • Vertebral tumours: Can present primarily with back pain, which may be non-specific, before developing neurological symptoms like radiculopathy or myelopathy. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Spinal cord compression: clinical features and emergency management?" text="Spinal cord compression"][/sc]
  • Benign tumours: While not malignant, some benign tumours (e.g., osteoid osteoma) can cause severe nocturnal pain relieved by NSAIDs, mimicking features of malignancy.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Bone tumours (primary and secondary including osteosarcoma, Ewing sarcoma)"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected bone tumour: Plain film radiography of the affected area is the initial investigation, often revealing characteristic features (e.g., sunburst appearance in osteosarcoma, onion-skinning in Ewing sarcoma, lytic or sclerotic lesions).
  • Further imaging: MRI of the affected bone provides detailed soft tissue and marrow involvement, crucial for surgical planning. CT chest/abdomen/pelvis and bone scintigraphy (bone scan) are used for staging and detecting metastatic disease.
  • Biopsy: A definitive diagnosis requires a biopsy (either core needle or open) of the tumour, performed by an experienced musculoskeletal oncologist, to determine histology and grade.
  • Blood tests: Full blood count, inflammatory markers (ESR, CRP), liver and renal function tests, serum calcium, alkaline phosphatase (ALP), and lactate dehydrogenase (LDH) can provide supportive information regarding systemic involvement or tumour activity.
  • MDCalc link: The Mirels' Score on MDCalc can be used to assess the risk of impending pathological fracture in patients with bone lesions.

  • A 16-year-old male presents with a 3-month history of worsening left distal femoral pain, particularly at night, associated with a palpable, tender swelling and a recent pathological fracture after a minor fall.
  • A young male with progressive, nocturnal bone pain and a rapidly enlarging mass, complicated by a pathological fracture, suggesting an aggressive primary bone malignancy.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Bone tumours (primary and secondary including osteosarcoma, Ewing sarcoma)"][/sc]
  • Osteomyelitis - often presents with fever, acute pain, and signs of infection, but can mimic Ewing sarcoma.
  • Stress fracture - typically related to repetitive activity, pain improves with rest, and imaging may show callus formation rather than a destructive lesion.
  • Benign bone tumours (e.g., osteochondroma, fibrous dysplasia) - usually asymptomatic or cause less aggressive pain, with characteristic radiological features.
  • Metabolic bone disease (e.g., Paget's disease) - typically affects older adults, with characteristic bone remodelling on imaging and elevated alkaline phosphatase.
  • Trauma (e.g., contusion, sprain) - usually has a clear history of injury, and pain is acute and self-limiting, unlike the progressive nature of tumour pain.
  • Rheumatological conditions (e.g., juvenile idiopathic arthritis) - can cause joint pain and swelling, but bone pain is less prominent, and systemic features may differ.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Bone tumours (primary and secondary including osteosarcoma, Ewing sarcoma)"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Pain control with appropriate analgesia (e.g., NSAIDs, opioids), immobilisation of the affected limb to prevent pathological fracture, and urgent referral to a specialist musculoskeletal oncology centre.
  • Definitive management: For primary malignant bone tumours, this typically involves neoadjuvant chemotherapy, surgical resection (limb-sparing surgery where possible), and adjuvant chemotherapy. Secondary bone tumours are managed according to the primary cancer and may involve radiotherapy, chemotherapy, or surgical stabilisation.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Regular clinical review, imaging (X-rays, MRI, CT scans) to monitor for recurrence or metastatic spread, and rehabilitation to optimise function after surgery.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Advise patients to seek immediate medical attention for worsening pain, new neurological symptoms, or signs of infection. Ensure clear communication with primary care regarding ongoing pain management and surveillance.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Bone tumours (primary and secondary including osteosarcoma, Ewing sarcoma)"][/sc]
  • Pathological fracture: Occurs due to tumour-induced bone weakening, leading to acute pain and loss of function, often requiring surgical stabilisation. This can occur at any stage, particularly in the progressive phase.
  • Metastatic disease: Spread of tumour cells to distant sites, most commonly the lungs (osteosarcoma) or other bones and bone marrow (Ewing sarcoma), significantly impacting prognosis and requiring systemic treatment. This is a late-phase complication.
  • Spinal cord compression: Tumours involving the vertebrae can compress the spinal cord, leading to neurological deficits, paralysis, and bowel/bladder dysfunction, requiring urgent surgical decompression or radiotherapy. This can develop in the progressive to late phases.
  • Local recurrence: Despite surgical resection, the tumour can recur at the primary site, necessitating further treatment and impacting long-term outcomes.
  • Hypercalcaemia of malignancy: Tumours can release parathyroid hormone-related protein (PTHrP) or directly destroy bone, leading to elevated serum calcium levels, causing symptoms like nausea, confusion, and renal dysfunction.
