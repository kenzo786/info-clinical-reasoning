Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Aneurysms, ischaemic limb and occlusions"][/sc]
  • Acute limb ischaemia (ALI) is a sudden decrease in limb perfusion that threatens the viability of the limb, typically occurring within 14 days of symptom onset [1] [2].
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the signs and symptoms of acute limb ischaemia, differentiate between embolic and thrombotic causes, and understand the urgency of referral for specialist vascular assessment.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Aneurysms, ischaemic limb and occlusions"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Aneurysms, ischaemic limb and occlusions"][/sc]
  • Acute limb ischaemia affects approximately 14-20 per 100,000 people per year in the UK [3].
  • Risk factors include advanced age, smoking, diabetes mellitus, hypertension, hyperlipidaemia, and pre-existing peripheral arterial disease [1] [2].
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Aneurysms, ischaemic limb and occlusions"][/sc]
  • Trigger: Acute limb ischaemia most commonly results from an embolic event (e.g., cardiac thrombus from atrial fibrillation) or in-situ thrombosis (e.g., rupture of an atherosclerotic plaque in pre-existing peripheral arterial disease) [1] [2].
  • Progression: The sudden occlusion of an artery leads to a rapid reduction in blood flow, causing tissue hypoxia and accumulation of metabolic waste products. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Acute limb ischaemia: what is the difference between embolic and thrombotic causes and why does it matter clinically?" text="Embolic vs. thrombotic ALI"][/sc]
  • Consequence: Prolonged ischaemia results in cellular damage, muscle necrosis, nerve injury, and ultimately, if not reperfused, irreversible tissue death and potential limb loss [1].
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Aneurysms, ischaemic limb and occlusions"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Initial onset of symptoms, often characterised by pain, pallor, and paraesthesia. The limb may still be salvageable with prompt intervention, typically within the first 6 hours [1].
  • Progressive phase: As ischaemia continues, motor weakness (paralysis) develops, and sensory loss becomes more profound. This phase, particularly beyond 6-12 hours, indicates increasing threat to limb viability [2].
  • Late phase: Irreversible tissue damage, muscle necrosis, and nerve death occur. The limb becomes cold and mottled, and gangrene may develop, particularly beyond 24 hours, often necessitating amputation [1].
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Aneurysms, ischaemic limb and occlusions"][/sc]
  • Ask about Pain - often sudden onset, severe, and unrelieved by analgesia, indicating acute tissue ischaemia [1].
  • Ask about Paraesthesia - numbness or tingling sensation, signifying nerve ischaemia and impending neurological compromise [2].
  • Ask about Paralysis - weakness or inability to move the affected limb, a critical sign of severe and advanced ischaemia [1].
  • Ask about Perishingly cold - subjective feeling of coldness in the limb, reflecting severely reduced blood flow.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Aneurysms, ischaemic limb and occlusions"][/sc]
  • Check Pallor - the affected limb appears pale or mottled due to lack of blood flow [1].
  • Check Pulselessness - absence of palpable pulses distal to the occlusion, a key diagnostic finding [2]. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Pulselessness in acute limb ischaemia: what does it indicate and why is it important clinically?" text="Pulselessness"][/sc]
  • Check Temperature - the affected limb feels perishingly cold compared to the contralateral limb.
  • Check Capillary refill time - prolonged capillary refill time (>2 seconds) in the affected digits.
  • Check Vitals - assess for signs of systemic compromise such as hypotension, tachycardia, or fever, which may indicate underlying sepsis or cardiac instability.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Aneurysms, ischaemic limb and occlusions"][/sc]
  • Sudden onset severe pain: Suggests an acute event such as embolism or acute thrombosis, requiring immediate vascular assessment to prevent irreversible damage.
  • Motor weakness or paralysis: Indicates advanced ischaemia with nerve involvement, signifying a critical threat to limb viability and a need for urgent reperfusion, particularly beyond 6 hours.
  • Absent pulses and cold limb: These objective signs confirm severe arterial occlusion and profound ischaemia, necessitating emergency intervention to salvage the limb.
  • Sensory loss (anaesthesia): A sign of severe nerve ischaemia, often preceding irreversible motor deficits and indicating a time-critical window for intervention.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Aneurysms, ischaemic limb and occlusions"][/sc]
  • Older adults: May present with less pronounced pain due to neuropathy or altered pain perception, leading to delayed presentation and diagnosis.
  • Diabetic patients: Often have pre-existing neuropathy, which can mask pain and paraesthesia, making the diagnosis of acute limb ischaemia challenging.
  • Patients with chronic limb ischaemia: An acute-on-chronic presentation can occur, where a sudden worsening of symptoms is superimposed on existing claudication or rest pain, making it difficult to distinguish from purely acute events. Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Aneurysms, ischaemic limb and occlusions"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected acute limb ischaemia: Clinical assessment using the six Ps (pain, pallor, pulselessness, paraesthesia, paralysis, perishingly cold) is paramount. Doppler ultrasound to assess arterial flow and ankle-brachial pressure index (ABPI) can provide objective evidence of reduced perfusion.
  • Severity/risk tools: Rutherford classification is widely used to grade the severity of acute limb ischaemia and guide management decisions. Rutherford Classification for Acute Limb Ischemia on MDCalc [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Rutherford classification: what are the stages and how do they guide management in acute limb ischaemia?" text="Rutherford Classification"][/sc]
  • Imaging - CT angiography: Often the imaging modality of choice to delineate the level and extent of occlusion, and to differentiate between embolic and thrombotic causes.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Consider D-dimer for suspected deep vein thrombosis (DVT) or compartment syndrome.
  • Serial reassessment: Regular clinical reassessment of the limb is crucial to monitor for progression of ischaemia or response to treatment.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Aneurysms, ischaemic limb and occlusions"][/sc]
  • Acute deep vein thrombosis (DVT) with phlegmasia cerulea dolens: Characterised by severe pain, swelling, and cyanosis, but pulses are typically present, unlike ALI.
  • Cellulitis: Presents with erythema, warmth, and tenderness, usually without severe pain or neurological deficits, and pulses are intact.
  • Peripheral neuropathy: Can cause numbness and weakness, but typically has a gradual onset and is not associated with acute pallor, coldness, or pulselessness.
  • Compartment syndrome: Severe pain out of proportion to injury, pain on passive stretch, and tense compartments, often following trauma or reperfusion injury. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Compartment syndrome: what are the key features and how does it differ from acute limb ischaemia?" text="Compartment Syndrome"][/sc]
  • Musculoskeletal injury: Pain and swelling related to trauma, but usually without signs of ischaemia (pallor, pulselessness, coldness).
  • Spinal cord compression: Can cause acute limb weakness and sensory changes, but typically bilateral and associated with back pain, and peripheral pulses are preserved.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Aneurysms, ischaemic limb and occlusions"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Prompt recognition and urgent referral to a vascular specialist are crucial. Supportive care often includes pain management and fluid resuscitation.
  • Definitive management: Revascularisation strategies, such as surgical embolectomy/thrombectomy or endovascular techniques (e.g., thrombolysis, angioplasty), are typically considered based on the severity of ischaemia and patient factors.
  • Adjunctive management: Anticoagulation with unfractionated heparin is often initiated to prevent further clot propagation.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Close monitoring for reperfusion injury, compartment syndrome, and ongoing limb viability is essential.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Patients with suspected acute limb ischaemia require immediate hospital admission and specialist vascular input.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Aneurysms, ischaemic limb and occlusions"][/sc]
  • Limb loss: Irreversible tissue damage due to prolonged ischaemia, particularly beyond 24 hours, often necessitates amputation, leading to significant morbidity.
  • Reperfusion injury: Occurs after restoration of blood flow, leading to local and systemic inflammatory responses, which can cause further tissue damage and multi-organ dysfunction.
  • Compartment syndrome: Swelling within the confined fascial compartments of the limb, leading to increased pressure, reduced perfusion, and potential muscle and nerve damage, especially after reperfusion.
  • Renal failure: Release of myoglobin from damaged muscle tissue (rhabdomyolysis) can lead to acute kidney injury, a serious systemic complication.
  • Death: Severe acute limb ischaemia, especially when associated with systemic complications or delayed treatment, carries a significant mortality risk.
