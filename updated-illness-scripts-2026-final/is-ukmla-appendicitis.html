Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Appendicitis (Gastrointestinal tract)"][/sc]
  • Appendicitis is an acute inflammation of the vermiform appendix, typically initiated by luminal obstruction, leading to bacterial invasion and subsequent inflammation of the appendix wall.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the classical and atypical presentations of acute appendicitis, understand the diagnostic approach, and identify indications for urgent surgical referral.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Appendicitis (Gastrointestinal tract)"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Appendicitis (Gastrointestinal tract)"][/sc]
  • Appendicitis is the most common cause of an acute abdomen in the UK, with an estimated lifetime risk of approximately 7-8% [1, 2].
  • It is most common between the ages of 10 and 20 years, but can occur at any age, being rare in infants under 12 months [1, 2].
  • In 2021/22, over 50,000 people were treated for acute appendicitis in hospitals in England [1].
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Appendicitis (Gastrointestinal tract)"][/sc]
  • Trigger: Luminal obstruction of the appendix, often by a faecolith, lymphoid hyperplasia, or foreign body, creates a closed-loop obstruction.
  • Progression: Continued mucus secretion and bacterial proliferation within the obstructed lumen lead to increased intraluminal pressure, venous congestion, and ischaemia of the appendiceal wall. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Appendiceal ischaemia: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Appendiceal ischaemia"][/sc]
  • Consequence: Transmural inflammation, bacterial translocation, and potential perforation, leading to localised peritonitis or generalised peritonitis if the infection spreads freely within the peritoneal cavity.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Appendicitis (Gastrointestinal tract)"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Initial symptoms typically develop over 6-12 hours, characterised by vague periumbilical pain, anorexia, nausea, and sometimes vomiting.
  • Progressive phase: Over the next 12-24 hours, the pain often migrates and localises to the right iliac fossa as the inflammation involves the parietal peritoneum.
  • Late phase: If untreated, particularly beyond 24-48 hours, the risk of perforation significantly increases, leading to more severe, generalised abdominal pain and signs of peritonitis.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Appendicitis (Gastrointestinal tract)"][/sc]
  • Ask about periumbilical pain migrating to the right iliac fossa - this classic migratory pain pattern is highly suggestive of appendicitis.
  • Ask about anorexia, nausea, or vomiting - these are common early symptoms and their presence increases the likelihood of appendicitis.
  • Ask about changes in bowel habits (constipation or diarrhoea) - while less specific, these can occur and may indicate irritation of adjacent bowel loops.
  • Ask about pain exacerbated by movement, coughing, or jarring - this suggests peritoneal irritation.
  • Ask about dysuria or urinary frequency - this may indicate an inflamed appendix irritating the bladder or ureter.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Appendicitis (Gastrointestinal tract)"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - low-grade pyrexia and tachycardia may develop, indicating systemic inflammation.
  • Check for localised tenderness and guarding in the right iliac fossa - this is a key finding, indicating inflammation of the parietal peritoneum.
  • Check for rebound tenderness - pain on release of pressure, further supporting peritoneal irritation.
  • Check Rovsing sign - palpation of the left lower quadrant causes pain in the right lower quadrant, suggesting peritoneal irritation. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Rovsing sign: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Rovsing sign"][/sc]
  • Check psoas sign - pain on passive extension of the right hip or active flexion against resistance, indicating irritation of the psoas muscle by a retrocaecal appendix.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Appendicitis (Gastrointestinal tract)"][/sc]
  • Generalised peritonitis: Severe, widespread abdominal pain, rigidity, and rebound tenderness, indicating appendiceal perforation and diffuse peritoneal contamination.
  • Haemodynamic instability: Tachycardia, hypotension, and signs of shock, suggesting sepsis secondary to perforation and peritonitis.
  • Rapidly worsening pain: A sudden increase in pain intensity, particularly after a temporary improvement, may signify appendiceal rupture.
  • Palpable abdominal mass: May indicate an appendiceal phlegmon or abscess formation, requiring careful management.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Appendicitis (Gastrointestinal tract)"][/sc]
  • Young children: May present with vague abdominal pain, anorexia, vomiting, and diarrhoea, making diagnosis challenging.
  • Older adults: Often present with less severe pain, delayed presentation, and confusion without prominent abdominal pain, increasing the risk of perforation.
  • Pregnancy: The appendix is displaced upwards by the gravid uterus, leading to pain in the right upper quadrant or flank, which can mimic other conditions.
  • Retrocaecal appendix: Pain may be less localised and tenderness may be elicited only on deep palpation or rectal examination, due to the appendix being hidden behind the caecum. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Retrocaecal appendix: how does it alter presentation?" text="Retrocaecal appendix"][/sc]
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Appendicitis (Gastrointestinal tract)"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected appendicitis: Full blood count (leukocytosis, neutrophilia), C-reactive protein (elevated), urinalysis (to exclude urinary tract infection), and a pregnancy test in women of childbearing age.
  • Severity/risk tools: Alvarado score or Appendicitis Inflammatory Response (AIR) score can aid in risk stratification, though clinical judgment remains paramount. Alvarado Score for Acute Appendicitis on MDCalc
  • Imaging - Ultrasound: Often the initial imaging modality, particularly in children and pregnant women, to visualise the appendix and exclude other pathologies.
  • Imaging - CT scan: Highly sensitive and specific for diagnosing appendicitis, especially in equivocal cases or when complications are suspected.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Consider imaging appropriate for differential diagnoses, such as pelvic ultrasound for gynaecological pathology.
  • Serial reassessment: Clinical observation and repeated examinations over several hours can be crucial in cases with an evolving presentation. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Serial reassessment in appendicitis: why is it important?" text="Serial reassessment"][/sc]
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Appendicitis (Gastrointestinal tract)"][/sc]
  • A previously well 25-year-old male presents with 12 hours of vague periumbilical pain that has now localised to the right lower quadrant, associated with anorexia and low-grade fever.
  • Acute abdominal pain with migratory pattern and systemic inflammatory response, concerning for acute appendicitis.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Appendicitis (Gastrointestinal tract)"][/sc]
  • Mesenteric adenitis - often follows a viral illness, generalised abdominal pain, less localised tenderness.
  • Gastroenteritis - typically associated with vomiting and diarrhoea preceding abdominal pain, less localised tenderness.
  • Meckel\'s diverticulitis - clinically indistinguishable from appendicitis, often diagnosed intraoperatively.
  • Ectopic pregnancy - sudden onset severe unilateral lower abdominal pain, often with vaginal bleeding, positive pregnancy test.
  • Pelvic inflammatory disease (PID) - bilateral lower abdominal pain, vaginal discharge, cervical motion tenderness.
  • Urinary tract infection (UTI) / Pyelonephritis - dysuria, frequency, loin pain, positive urinalysis.
  • Crohn\'s disease (ileitis) - chronic abdominal pain, weight loss, diarrhoea, often with perianal disease. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Crohn\'s ileitis vs appendicitis: how to differentiate?" text="Crohn\'s ileitis"][/sc]
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Appendicitis (Gastrointestinal tract)"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Recognition, prompt surgical referral, adequate analgesia, antiemetics, and intravenous fluid resuscitation to correct dehydration and electrolyte imbalances.
  • Definitive management: Surgical appendicectomy (laparoscopic approach is preferred) is the standard of care for acute appendicitis.
  • Adjunctive management: Pre-operative broad-spectrum antibiotics are often administered to reduce the risk of post-operative infection, particularly in cases of suspected perforation.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Post-operative monitoring for complications such as wound infection, abscess formation, or ileus.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Patients should be advised on symptoms of worsening infection or complications and when to seek urgent medical attention.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Appendicitis (Gastrointestinal tract)"][/sc]
  • Perforation: Occurs when the inflamed appendix ruptures, typically after 24-48 hours, leading to the release of infected contents into the peritoneal cavity.
  • Peritonitis: Inflammation of the peritoneum, which can be localised (forming an abscess) or generalised (life-threatening sepsis) following perforation.
  • Appendiceal abscess: A collection of pus around the inflamed or perforated appendix, often walled off by omentum and adjacent bowel loops.
  • Sepsis: A severe, life-threatening systemic response to infection, particularly if peritonitis is generalised and untreated.
  • Ileus: Paralysis of the bowel, leading to abdominal distension, nausea, and vomiting, often a consequence of severe inflammation or peritonitis.
  • Adhesions: Bands of scar tissue that can form after inflammation or surgery, potentially leading to future bowel obstruction.
