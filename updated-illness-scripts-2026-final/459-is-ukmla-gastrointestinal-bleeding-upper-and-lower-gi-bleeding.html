Gastrointestinal bleeding [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Gastrointestinal bleeding"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Gastrointestinal bleeding"][/sc]
  • Gastrointestinal (GI) bleeding refers to any bleeding that originates from the gastrointestinal tract, broadly categorised into upper GI bleeding (proximal to the ligament of Treitz) and lower GI bleeding (distal to the ligament of Treitz).
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the signs and symptoms of upper and lower GI bleeding, initiate appropriate immediate management, and understand the principles of risk stratification and definitive treatment.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Gastrointestinal bleeding"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Gastrointestinal bleeding"][/sc]
  • Acute upper GI bleeding has an incidence of approximately 80-100 per 100,000 adults per year in the UK, with a mortality rate of 10-14% [1].
  • Acute lower GI bleeding has an estimated incidence of 33–87 per 100,000 adults per year in the UK, accounting for 3% of emergency surgical referrals [2].
  • Risk factors for GI bleeding include Helicobacter pylori infection, NSAID use, antiplatelet and anticoagulant medications, alcohol excess, liver disease, and advanced age.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Gastrointestinal bleeding"][/sc]
  • Trigger: Disruption of the mucosal integrity or vascular structures within the GI tract, often due to peptic ulcer disease (upper GI) or diverticular disease (lower GI).
  • Progression: Ongoing blood loss leads to hypovolaemia, reduced tissue perfusion, and activation of compensatory mechanisms, potentially resulting in shock.
  • Consequence: Persistent or severe bleeding can lead to anaemia, organ ischaemia, multi-organ failure, and death if not promptly managed. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Hypovolaemic shock: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Hypovolaemic shock"][/sc]
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Gastrointestinal bleeding"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Acute onset of bleeding, often presenting with haematemesis or melaena (upper GI), or haematochezia (lower GI), developing over minutes to hours.
  • Progressive phase: Continued blood loss can lead to signs of hypovolaemia and shock within hours, particularly beyond 6-12 hours if bleeding is significant and unaddressed.
  • Late phase: Complications such as re-bleeding, anaemia, and organ dysfunction may manifest over days to weeks, especially in those with underlying comorbidities or delayed intervention.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Gastrointestinal bleeding"][/sc]
  • Ask about haematemesis - indicates upper GI source, often brisk bleeding.
  • Ask about melaena - suggests upper GI bleeding, or slow bleeding from the small bowel or right colon.
  • Ask about haematochezia - typically indicates lower GI bleeding, but can be rapid upper GI bleeding.
  • Ask about abdominal pain - may suggest peptic ulcer, diverticulitis, or ischaemic colitis.
  • Ask about syncope or dizziness - points to significant blood loss and hypovolaemia.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Gastrointestinal bleeding"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - tachycardia, hypotension, and reduced capillary refill indicate hypovolaemia and shock.
  • Check abdominal examination - tenderness, distension, or masses may suggest underlying pathology.
  • Check digital rectal examination - confirms presence of blood, melaena, or fresh blood, and can identify anorectal causes. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Digital rectal examination in GI bleeding: what does it reveal and why is it important?" text="Digital rectal examination"][/sc]
  • Check stigmata of chronic liver disease - jaundice, ascites, spider naevi may suggest variceal bleeding.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Gastrointestinal bleeding"][/sc]
  • Haemodynamic instability: Indicates significant ongoing blood loss requiring immediate resuscitation and intervention.
  • Persistent or recurrent bleeding: Suggests failure of initial management or a difficult-to-control source, particularly if occurring within 24-48 hours of presentation.
  • Significant comorbidities: Patients with severe cardiac, renal, or liver disease are at higher risk of adverse outcomes.
  • Massive transfusion requirements: Implies severe, ongoing haemorrhage and increased mortality risk.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Gastrointestinal bleeding"][/sc]
  • Older adults: May present with subtle symptoms, such as fatigue or confusion, due to blunted physiological responses to blood loss.
  • Patients on anticoagulants: Bleeding may be more severe and difficult to control, even from minor lesions.
  • Occult GI bleeding: Chronic, low-grade blood loss leading to iron deficiency anaemia without overt signs of bleeding. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Occult GI bleeding: how is it diagnosed and what are its common causes?" text="Occult GI bleeding"][/sc]
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Gastrointestinal bleeding"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected GI bleeding: Full blood count, urea and electrolytes, liver function tests, coagulation screen, group and cross-match.
  • Severity/risk tools: Blatchford score (pre-endoscopy) and Rockall score (post-endoscopy) for upper GI bleeding. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Blatchford score: how is it calculated and what is its clinical utility?" text="Blatchford score"][/sc] Blatchford Score on MDCalc
  • Endoscopy - Oesophago-gastro-duodenoscopy (OGD): Primary investigation for upper GI bleeding, allowing diagnosis and therapeutic intervention.
  • Endoscopy - Colonoscopy: Primary investigation for lower GI bleeding, allowing diagnosis and therapeutic intervention.
  • Imaging - CT angiography: Useful in haemodynamically unstable patients with active bleeding to localise the source, particularly in lower GI bleeding.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Gastrointestinal bleeding"][/sc]
  • An elderly male with a history of NSAID use presents with acute onset melaena and dizziness, with initial vital signs showing tachycardia and hypotension.
  • Acute GI haemorrhage with hypovolaemic shock, concerning for peptic ulcer disease.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Gastrointestinal bleeding"][/sc]
  • Peptic ulcer disease - often associated with NSAID use or H. pylori infection, presenting with epigastric pain and melaena.
  • Oesophageal varices - typically in patients with chronic liver disease, presenting with brisk haematemesis.
  • Diverticular disease - common cause of lower GI bleeding, often painless haematochezia.
  • Angiodysplasia - vascular malformations, often in older adults, causing recurrent, sometimes occult, GI bleeding. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Angiodysplasia: what is it and how does it cause GI bleeding?" text="Angiodysplasia"][/sc]
  • Colorectal cancer - may present with chronic, low-grade bleeding, change in bowel habit, and weight loss.
  • Ischaemic colitis - abdominal pain followed by bloody diarrhoea, often in patients with cardiovascular risk factors.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Gastrointestinal bleeding"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Resuscitation with intravenous fluids and blood products, airway management if necessary, and early senior review.
  • Definitive management: Endoscopic haemostasis (e.g., clipping, thermal coagulation, band ligation) is often the first-line treatment.
  • Adjunctive management: Proton pump inhibitors (PPIs) are typically used in upper GI bleeding; terlipressin and prophylactic antibiotics are considered for variceal bleeding.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Continuous monitoring of vital signs, haemoglobin levels, and clinical status; follow-up endoscopy or imaging as indicated.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Surgical or interventional radiological intervention may be warranted for uncontrolled bleeding or re-bleeding.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Gastrointestinal bleeding"][/sc]
  • Hypovolaemic shock: Can develop rapidly with significant blood loss, leading to organ hypoperfusion and failure.
  • Re-bleeding: Occurs in a significant proportion of patients, particularly within the first 72 hours, worsening prognosis.
  • Anaemia: Chronic or severe blood loss can lead to iron deficiency anaemia, impacting quality of life and requiring transfusion.
  • Acute kidney injury: Resulting from hypovolaemia and reduced renal perfusion, especially in vulnerable patients.
  • Hepatic encephalopathy: In patients with liver disease and variceal bleeding, due to increased nitrogenous load from GI blood.
