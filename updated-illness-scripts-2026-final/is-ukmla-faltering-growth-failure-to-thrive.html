Faltering growth (failure to thrive) [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Faltering growth (failure to thrive)"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Faltering growth (failure to thrive)"][/sc]
  • Faltering growth, previously known as failure to thrive, describes a slower rate of weight gain or inadequate growth in a child than expected for their age and sex, often due to insufficient nutritional intake or underlying health conditions.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Candidates must recognise faltering growth, understand its multifactorial nature, and appreciate that conditions evolve over time, requiring early recognition and management.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Faltering growth (failure to thrive)"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Faltering growth (failure to thrive)"][/sc]
  • Faltering growth affects approximately 5% of all children in the UK, though prevalence estimates vary depending on the definition and population studied.
  • Risk factors include inadequate calorie intake (the most common cause), inadequate nutrient absorption, increased metabolic demands, low birth weight, fetal growth restriction, gastrointestinal disorders, congenital conditions, and chronic infections.
  • It can occur at any point between birth and adolescence, often presenting in early childhood.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Faltering growth (failure to thrive)"][/sc]
  • Trigger: Inadequate caloric intake, often due to insufficient supply, poor feeding practices, or medical conditions affecting appetite or ability to feed.
  • Progression: Sustained inadequate nutrition leads to a catabolic state, where the body breaks down its own tissues for energy, impairing normal growth and development.
  • Consequence: Chronic nutritional deficit results in faltering growth, characterized by poor weight gain, and potentially impacting linear growth and neurodevelopmental outcomes.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Faltering growth (failure to thrive)"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Often subtle, characterized by a slower than expected rate of weight gain, particularly beyond the first few weeks of life, which may go unnoticed initially.
  • Progressive phase: If nutritional deficits persist, weight gain continues to falter, and the child may begin to drop centiles on growth charts. This phase may become apparent particularly beyond 3-6 months of age.
  • Late phase: Prolonged faltering growth, particularly beyond 6-12 months, can lead to impaired linear growth (stunting), developmental delays, and increased susceptibility to infections, with potential long-term neurodevelopmental consequences.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Faltering growth (failure to thrive)"][/sc]
  • Ask about poor feeding or reduced appetite - important for identifying inadequate caloric intake, a primary cause.
  • Ask about lethargy or decreased activity - may indicate insufficient energy reserves and impact developmental milestones.
  • Ask about irritability or excessive crying - can be a non-specific sign of discomfort or hunger, requiring careful assessment.
  • Ask about changes in bowel habits (e.g., chronic diarrhoea, constipation) - suggests potential malabsorption or underlying gastrointestinal issues.
  • Ask about recurrent infections - may point to an underlying chronic illness or immunocompromise contributing to poor growth.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Faltering growth (failure to thrive)"][/sc]
  • Check growth parameters (weight, length/height, head circumference) - crucial for plotting on growth charts to identify faltering growth and track progress.
  • Check vital signs - may reveal underlying systemic illness (e.g., fever, tachycardia) or signs of dehydration (e.g., hypotension, poor perfusion).
  • Check for signs of malnutrition (e.g., reduced subcutaneous fat, muscle wasting, dry skin, sparse hair) - indicates chronic nutritional deficit.
  • Check for dysmorphic features or congenital anomalies - may suggest an underlying genetic syndrome contributing to faltering growth.
  • Check for signs of neglect or abuse - important to consider in cases of non-organic faltering growth.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Faltering growth (failure to thrive)"][/sc]
  • Significant weight loss or failure to gain weight over a prolonged period: Indicates severe nutritional deficit requiring urgent intervention.
  • Signs of severe malnutrition (e.g., oedema, severe muscle wasting): Suggests advanced nutritional compromise and potential for refeeding syndrome.
  • Developmental regression or significant delay: May indicate underlying neurological conditions or severe, prolonged nutritional impact on brain development.
  • Recurrent infections or unusual infections: Could point to an underlying immunodeficiency or chronic systemic illness.
  • Concerns about safeguarding (e.g., neglect, abuse): Requires immediate multi-agency assessment and intervention to ensure child safety.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Faltering growth (failure to thrive)"][/sc]
  • Premature infants: Growth charts for term infants may not accurately reflect expected growth for premature babies, leading to misinterpretation of faltering growth. Corrected gestational age should always be used for plotting to avoid over-diagnosis or under-recognition.
  • Children with chronic diseases (e.g., congenital heart disease, cystic fibrosis): Faltering growth may be an early indicator of disease progression or inadequate management, and their growth patterns may inherently differ due to increased metabolic demands or malabsorption. This necessitates careful monitoring against disease-specific growth curves if available.
  • Children with neurodevelopmental disorders (e.g., cerebral palsy, autism spectrum disorder): Feeding difficulties, oral motor dysfunction, or sensory sensitivities can contribute to faltering growth. The presentation may be subtle and require a multidisciplinary approach to identify and address underlying feeding challenges.
  • Older children/adolescents: Faltering growth in this age group may be indicative of eating disorders, chronic systemic illness, or psychosocial issues, and requires a different diagnostic approach compared to infants.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Faltering growth (failure to thrive)"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected faltering growth: Detailed history (feeding, social, developmental) and comprehensive physical examination, including accurate anthropometric measurements (weight, length/height, head circumference) plotted on UK WHO growth charts. Serial measurements are crucial for diagnosis and monitoring.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Targeted investigations based on clinical suspicion. This may include full blood count, urea and electrolytes, liver function tests, coeliac screen, thyroid function tests, urine analysis, and stool studies for malabsorption.
  • Imaging - [modality]: Imaging is not routinely indicated unless specific underlying pathology is suspected (e.g., skeletal survey for suspected non-accidental injury, abdominal ultrasound for gastrointestinal anomalies).
  • Serial reassessment: Regular monitoring of growth parameters (weekly/fortnightly initially, then monthly) and nutritional intake is essential to assess response to interventions and identify ongoing concerns.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Faltering growth (failure to thrive)"][/sc]
  • Constitutional delay of growth and puberty: Child follows a lower growth centile but maintains a normal growth velocity and is otherwise healthy, often with a family history of similar growth patterns.
  • Genetic short stature: Child is consistently small for age but grows along a normal, lower centile, with no underlying pathology and often a family history of short stature.
  • Coeliac disease: Presents with malabsorption symptoms (e.g., chronic diarrhoea, abdominal distension, poor weight gain) and responds to a gluten-free diet. Distinguishing feature: positive coeliac serology and duodenal biopsy findings.
  • Cystic fibrosis: Characterized by exocrine pancreatic insufficiency leading to malabsorption, recurrent respiratory infections, and often faltering growth. Distinguishing feature: positive sweat test and genetic testing.
  • Gastro-oesophageal reflux disease (GORD): Severe or persistent reflux can lead to feeding aversion, inadequate intake, and oesophagitis, contributing to faltering growth. Distinguishing feature: symptoms of reflux, sometimes with haematemesis.
  • Congenital heart disease: Increased metabolic demands due to cardiac workload can lead to faltering growth despite adequate caloric intake. Distinguishing feature: presence of a heart murmur, cyanosis, or signs of heart failure.
  • Renal tubular acidosis: Metabolic acidosis can impair growth. Distinguishing feature: electrolyte imbalances, particularly hyperchloraemic metabolic acidosis, and abnormal urine pH.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Faltering growth (failure to thrive)"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Address any acute medical issues (e.g., dehydration, severe infection). Optimize feeding practices, which may involve increasing caloric density of feeds, more frequent feeding, or addressing feeding difficulties. Consider referral to a dietitian for nutritional assessment and guidance.
  • Definitive management: This is guided by the underlying cause. If an organic cause is identified, specific medical or surgical treatment is initiated. For non-organic causes, a multidisciplinary approach involving health visitors, dietitians, and social services may be required to support the family and optimize the feeding environment.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Regular monitoring of growth parameters (weight, length/height, head circumference) and developmental milestones. Frequent follow-up appointments to assess progress, adjust interventions, and provide ongoing support to caregivers.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: If faltering growth persists despite interventions, or if there are concerns about safeguarding, escalate to paediatric services for further assessment. Safety-netting advice should be provided to caregivers regarding signs of deterioration or red flag features that warrant immediate medical attention.
  • Exam pharmacology reference (UKMLA/MRCGP): Nutritional supplements may be considered in some cases to support catch-up growth. For example, high-calorie oral supplements or nasogastric feeding may be required. Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Faltering growth (failure to thrive)"][/sc]
  • Developmental delay: Prolonged nutritional inadequacy, particularly during critical periods of brain development (e.g., first 1000 days), can lead to impaired cognitive, motor, and social development, significantly impacting long-term outcomes.
  • Short stature: Chronic faltering growth, especially if linear growth is affected, can result in permanent short stature, impacting physical development and potentially self-esteem.
  • Increased susceptibility to infections: Malnutrition compromises the immune system, making children more vulnerable to recurrent and severe infections, which can further exacerbate faltering growth and lead to a vicious cycle.
  • Psychosocial and behavioural problems: Faltering growth can contribute to irritability, poor social interaction, and attachment issues, impacting the child\'s overall well-being and family dynamics.
  • Hospitalization and increased healthcare utilization: Severe faltering growth often necessitates hospital admission for intensive nutritional support, investigation of underlying causes, and management of complications, leading to increased healthcare burden.
