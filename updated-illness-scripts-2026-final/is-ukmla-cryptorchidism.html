Cryptorchidism [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Cryptorchidism"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Cryptorchidism"][/sc]
  • Cryptorchidism, or undescended testis (UDT), is defined as the failure of one or both testes to descend into the scrotum. It is distinct from a retractile testis, which can be manually guided into the scrotum and remains there without tension.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise cryptorchidism, distinguish it from retractile testis, understand the implications for fertility and malignancy risk, and know the optimal timing for orchidopexy.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Cryptorchidism"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Cryptorchidism"][/sc]
  • Affects approximately 2-5% of full-term male infants at birth, with a higher incidence of up to 30% in premature infants.
  • Spontaneous descent occurs in most cases by 3 months of age, reducing the prevalence to around 1% by one year.
  • Risk factors include prematurity, low birth weight, small for gestational age, maternal exposure to oestrogens or anti-androgens, and a family history of cryptorchidism.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Cryptorchidism"][/sc]
  • Trigger: Testicular descent is a complex process involving hormonal (androgens, Insulin-like factor 3 (INSL3)) and mechanical factors (gubernaculum). Disruption in any of these pathways can lead to incomplete descent.
  • Progression: The gubernaculum, a mesenchymal cord, guides the testis from the abdominal cavity through the inguinal canal into the scrotum. Failure of the gubernaculum to develop or respond correctly, or inadequate hormonal stimulation, results in the testis arresting along this pathway. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Gubernaculum: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="gubernaculum"][/sc]
  • Consequence: The higher temperature in the abdomen or inguinal canal compared to the scrotum impairs spermatogenesis and increases the risk of germ cell malignancy. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Testicular descent: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="testicular descent"][/sc]
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Cryptorchidism"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Testicular descent typically completes by 3 months of age. Most undescended testes at birth will spontaneously descend within this period.
  • Progressive phase: If descent has not occurred by 3-6 months, spontaneous resolution becomes unlikely. Intervention is generally recommended between 6 and 18 months of age to mitigate long-term complications.
  • Late phase: Delaying orchidopexy beyond 18 months significantly increases the risk of impaired fertility and testicular malignancy.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Cryptorchidism"][/sc]
  • Ask about absence of one or both testes in the scrotum - this is the primary clinical finding, often noted by parents or during routine examinations.
  • Ask about previous palpation of testes - to differentiate between truly undescended and retractile testes, which may have been previously palpable in the scrotum.
  • Ask about any scrotal swelling or pain - while cryptorchidism is usually asymptomatic, acute pain or swelling could indicate testicular torsion, a surgical emergency.
  • Ask about family history of cryptorchidism or other genital anomalies - suggests a genetic predisposition.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Cryptorchidism"][/sc]
  • Check for absence of one or both testes in the scrotum on palpation - the definitive diagnostic sign.
  • Check for palpability of the testis in the inguinal canal - an undescended testis may be felt along its normal descent pathway. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Retractile testis vs undescended testis: what is the clinical distinction?" text="retractile testis"][/sc]
  • Check for signs of testicular atrophy or hypoplasia in the contralateral testis (if unilateral cryptorchidism) - may indicate broader testicular dysfunction.
  • Check for associated anomalies, such as inguinal hernia - often co-occurs with cryptorchidism due to a patent processus vaginalis.
  • Check vital signs - usually normal, but fever or tachycardia could suggest complications like torsion or infection (rare).
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Cryptorchidism"][/sc]
  • Non-palpable testes bilaterally: Suggests anorchia or a very high intra-abdominal testis, requiring urgent investigation and management due to implications for sex determination and hormonal function.
  • Acute scrotal pain and swelling in a child with cryptorchidism: May indicate testicular torsion, a surgical emergency requiring immediate intervention to preserve testicular viability.
  • Undescended testis persisting beyond 6 months of age: Indicates a need for surgical correction (orchidopexy) to reduce the risk of infertility and malignancy.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Cryptorchidism"][/sc]
  • Acquired undescended testis (ascending testis): Testis initially descends but later retracts out of the scrotum, often due to disproportionate growth of the spermatic cord. Requires similar management to congenital cryptorchidism.
  • Ectopic testis: Testis descends outside the normal pathway (e.g., superficial inguinal pouch, femoral region, perineum). Requires surgical repositioning.
  • Cryptorchidism in syndromic children: May be part of a broader genetic syndrome (e.g., Prader-Willi, Down syndrome), necessitating a holistic approach to care and genetic counselling. Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Cryptorchidism"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected cryptorchidism: Clinical examination remains the cornerstone of diagnosis. Palpation of the scrotum and inguinal region is crucial to locate the testis and differentiate from a retractile testis.
  • Imaging - Ultrasound: May be used to locate a non-palpable testis in the inguinal canal, but its utility for intra-abdominal testes is limited due to low sensitivity and specificity. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Ultrasound for undescended testis: what are its limitations?" text="ultrasound for UDT"][/sc]
  • Imaging - MRI/CT: Rarely indicated for routine diagnosis but may be considered for high intra-abdominal testes if surgical exploration is planned and location is uncertain.
  • Hormonal tests: In bilateral non-palpable testes, tests for testosterone, LH, and FSH may help differentiate between anorchia and intra-abdominal testes, especially if HCG stimulation test is performed.
  • Severity/risk tools: No specific validated severity or risk scores are routinely used for cryptorchidism in clinical practice.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Cryptorchidism"][/sc]
  • A 9-month-old male presents for a routine check-up, where examination reveals an empty right hemiscrotum with a palpable testis in the right inguinal canal, consistent with an undescended testis.
  • An infant with a non-scrotal testis, concerning for cryptorchidism, requiring timely intervention to prevent long-term complications.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Cryptorchidism"][/sc]
  • Retractile testis: Testis is present in the scrotum but retracts into the inguinal canal due to an overactive cremasteric reflex; can be manually brought into the scrotum and remains there. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Retractile vs undescended testis: how to differentiate clinically?" text="retractile vs undescended testis"][/sc]
  • Absent testis (anorchia/testicular agenesis): Complete absence of one or both testes, confirmed by hormonal studies and surgical exploration.
  • Testicular torsion: Acute onset of severe scrotal pain, swelling, and tenderness, often with absent cremasteric reflex; a surgical emergency.
  • Inguinal hernia: Scrotal swelling that may be reducible, often associated with a patent processus vaginalis; can coexist with cryptorchidism.
  • Ectopic testis: Testis located outside the normal path of descent, but still palpable.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Cryptorchidism"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Early recognition and referral to a paediatric surgeon or urologist for assessment. Avoidance of unnecessary imaging in palpable cases.
  • Definitive management: Orchidopexy, a surgical procedure to bring the testis into the scrotum and fix it in place, is the mainstay of treatment. This is typically performed between 6 and 18 months of age.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Regular follow-up after orchidopexy to monitor testicular position, size, and development. Lifelong self-examination education for testicular cancer screening is crucial.
  • Exam pharmacology reference (UKMLA/MRCGP): No specific drug knowledge is commonly tested for the primary management of cryptorchidism.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Cryptorchidism"][/sc]
  • Infertility: The most common long-term complication, particularly with bilateral cryptorchidism or delayed orchidopexy beyond 18 months, due to impaired spermatogenesis at higher temperatures.
  • Testicular malignancy: Increased risk of germ cell tumours (seminoma being most common), even after successful orchidopexy. Risk is higher for intra-abdominal testes and with delayed surgery.
  • Testicular torsion: Undescended testes are at a higher risk of torsion due to abnormal fixation and mobility.
  • Inguinal hernia: Often co-occurs due to a patent processus vaginalis, which can lead to bowel incarceration.
  • Psychological impact: May occur in adolescence due to cosmetic concerns or anxiety about fertility and cancer risk.
