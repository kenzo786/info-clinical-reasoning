Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Placental abruption"][/sc]
  • Placental abruption is the premature separation of a normally located placenta from the uterine wall that occurs before delivery of the fetus. It may be revealed, if blood escapes through the vagina, or concealed, if the bleeding occurs behind the placenta, with no evidence of vaginal bleeding [1] [2].
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical presentation of placental abruption, differentiate it from other causes of antepartum haemorrhage, and understand the immediate management principles to optimise maternal and fetal outcomes.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Placental abruption"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Placental abruption"][/sc]
  • Placental abruption complicates approximately 0.4–1.2% of all pregnancies in the UK [1] [2] [4].
  • It is a significant cause of maternal and perinatal morbidity and mortality. In the UK, between 2000 and 2005, placental abruption was implicated in 2-4 maternal deaths [3].
  • Key risk factors include chronic hypertension, pre-eclampsia, smoking, cocaine use, abdominal trauma, and previous placental abruption [1] [2].
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Placental abruption"][/sc]
  • **Trigger:** Placental abruption often initiates with the rupture of maternal decidual vessels within the decidua basalis, frequently associated with underlying vascular pathologies such as chronic hypertension or pre-eclampsia, or acute events like trauma or rapid uterine decompression.
  • **Progression:** Following vascular rupture, blood accumulates between the decidua and the placenta, forming a retroplacental haematoma. The expansion of this haematoma progressively dissects the placental attachment from the uterine wall. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Retroplacental haematoma: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="retroplacental haematoma"][/sc]
  • **Consequence:** The compromised uteroplacental blood flow leads to reduced oxygen and nutrient supply to the fetus, potentially resulting in fetal distress, hypoxia, and acidosis. Maternal consequences include significant haemorrhage, coagulopathy (e.g., disseminated intravascular coagulation), and hypovolaemic shock.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Placental abruption"][/sc]
  • Early phase: The onset of placental abruption is typically sudden, often presenting with vaginal bleeding and abdominal pain. Initial separation may be small and potentially concealed, making early diagnosis challenging.
  • Progressive phase: As the retroplacental haematoma expands, there is an increase in the severity of pain, uterine tenderness, and potentially more significant vaginal bleeding. Fetal distress may become evident within minutes to hours, necessitating prompt clinical assessment.
  • Late phase: Without timely intervention, severe abruption can lead to critical maternal haemorrhage, disseminated intravascular coagulation (DIC), and profound fetal compromise, including stillbirth, particularly beyond 6-12 hours from the onset of significant separation.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Placental abruption"][/sc]
  • Ask about vaginal bleeding - often dark red, but can be absent in concealed abruption, which is critical for early recognition and management.
  • Ask about abdominal pain - typically sudden onset, severe, constant, and not relieved by position changes, which helps differentiate it from labour pain.
  • Ask about uterine contractions - may be present but often differ from typical labour contractions, being more frequent, hypertonic, and less rhythmic.
  • Ask about decreased fetal movements - indicates potential fetal compromise and necessitates urgent assessment of fetal well-being.
  • Ask about lower back pain - can be a presenting symptom, particularly with posterior placental abruption, and should prompt consideration of abruption.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Placental abruption"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - maternal tachycardia and hypotension are indicative of hypovolaemia, while fever may suggest an underlying infection, a potential risk factor for abruption.
  • Check uterine tenderness and rigidity - a hallmark sign, often described as a 'woody' or 'board-like' abdomen, which is a critical indicator of significant concealed bleeding and uterine irritability. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Uterine rigidity in placental abruption: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="uterine rigidity"][/sc]
  • Check fetal heart rate - abnormalities such as bradycardia, recurrent decelerations, or reduced variability are signs of fetal distress and require immediate action.
  • Check vaginal examination (if safe and not contraindicated) - to assess cervical dilation and rule out placenta praevia, though often avoided in active bleeding to prevent exacerbation.
  • Check fundal height - a rapidly increasing fundal height may suggest a large concealed retroplacental haematoma, indicating ongoing internal bleeding.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Placental abruption"][/sc]
  • Concealed haemorrhage: Absence of visible vaginal bleeding despite significant placental separation, leading to delayed recognition of severe abruption and potentially critical maternal and fetal compromise.
  • Sudden, severe, continuous abdominal pain: This suggests ongoing placental separation and uterine irritability, necessitating immediate and thorough assessment.
  • Uterine tenderness and increased tone: A rigid, board-like uterus is a critical sign of significant retroplacental bleeding, indicating an urgent obstetric emergency.
  • Fetal distress: Any changes in fetal heart rate pattern, such as bradycardia, late decelerations, or reduced variability, indicate fetal compromise and require urgent intervention to prevent adverse outcomes.
  • Maternal hypovolaemic shock: This may be disproportionate to visible blood loss, strongly suggesting concealed haemorrhage and requiring aggressive fluid resuscitation and blood product support.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Placental abruption"][/sc]
  • Concealed Abruption: Vaginal bleeding may be absent, with symptoms limited to abdominal pain, uterine tenderness, and signs of fetal distress or maternal shock. This can delay diagnosis and worsen outcomes. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Concealed placental abruption: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="concealed abruption"][/sc]
  • Chronic Abruption: This involves recurrent, small separations over time, which can lead to complications such as oligohydramnios, fetal growth restriction, or preterm labour, rather than an acute catastrophic event.
  • Mild Abruption: May present with only mild vaginal bleeding or back pain, making differentiation from other causes of antepartum haemorrhage challenging and requiring a high index of suspicion.
  • Abruption with Coagulopathy: In severe cases, placental abruption can trigger disseminated intravascular coagulation (DIC), leading to widespread bleeding and organ dysfunction, significantly increasing maternal morbidity and mortality. Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Placental abruption"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected placental abruption:
    • **Fetal monitoring:** Continuous cardiotocography (CTG) is essential to assess fetal well-being and detect any signs of distress, guiding urgent management decisions.
    • **Full blood count (FBC):** Used to assess maternal haemoglobin and haematocrit levels, providing an indication of acute blood loss and guiding the need for transfusion.
    • **Coagulation screen:** Including prothrombin time (PT), activated partial thromboplastin time (APTT), fibrinogen, and D-dimers, to assess for coagulopathy, particularly disseminated intravascular coagulation (DIC).
    • **Kleihauer-Betke (KB) test:** Performed in Rh-negative women to quantify fetomaternal haemorrhage and determine the appropriate dose of anti-D immunoglobulin required. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Kleihauer-Betke test: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Kleihauer test"][/sc]
    • **Cross-match:** Essential for preparing blood products for potential transfusion, especially in cases of significant haemorrhage.
  • Imaging - Ultrasound: Transabdominal ultrasound may occasionally reveal a retroplacental haematoma, but its absence does not reliably rule out abruption. It is primarily useful for excluding placenta praevia and assessing fetal viability and growth.
  • Serial reassessment: Ongoing monitoring of maternal vital signs, bleeding, uterine tenderness, and fetal well-being is crucial for detecting changes and guiding management.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Placental abruption"][/sc]
  • **Placenta praevia:** Characterised by painless vaginal bleeding, often bright red, without associated uterine tenderness or increased tone, distinguishing it from the painful bleeding of abruption. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Placenta praevia vs placental abruption: what are the key distinguishing features?" text="Placenta praevia"][/sc]
  • **Vasa praevia:** Typically presents with painless vaginal bleeding, often dark red, occurring specifically at the time of membrane rupture, and is associated with rapid fetal compromise due to fetal vessel rupture.
  • **Uterine rupture:** Manifests with severe, sudden abdominal pain, often accompanied by the cessation of contractions, fetal distress, and maternal shock. A history of previous uterine surgery, such as a Caesarean section, is a significant risk factor.
  • **Local cervical/vaginal lesions:** Bleeding from conditions like cervical ectropion, polyps, or trauma is usually less severe and not associated with the uterine pain or tenderness characteristic of abruption.
  • **Bloody show:** A small amount of blood-stained mucus, typically observed at the onset of labour, which is not associated with significant pain, heavy bleeding, or fetal compromise.
  • **Preterm labour:** Involves regular painful contractions, but typically without significant vaginal bleeding or uterine tenderness, unless complicated by an underlying placental abruption. Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Placental abruption"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities:
    • Prompt recognition and urgent escalation to a multidisciplinary team, including obstetricians, anaesthetists, and neonatologists, is crucial.
    • Maternal stabilisation involves securing intravenous access (two large-bore cannulae), commencing fluid resuscitation with crystalloids, and obtaining blood samples for full blood count, coagulation screen, and cross-match.
    • Continuous fetal monitoring is essential to assess fetal well-being and guide urgent management decisions.
    • Consideration of anti-D immunoglobulin for Rh-negative women is important to prevent sensitisation.
  • Definitive management:
    • Delivery of the fetus and placenta is the definitive treatment. The mode of delivery (vaginal or Caesarean section) is determined by maternal and fetal condition, gestational age, and the severity of the abruption.
    • In cases of severe abruption with fetal distress or maternal compromise, urgent Caesarean section is often warranted to optimise outcomes.
    • For mild cases with a stable mother and fetus, and if close monitoring is feasible, a trial of vaginal delivery may be considered.
  • Adjunctive management:
    • Blood product transfusion, including packed red blood cells, fresh frozen plasma, cryoprecipitate, and platelets, should be guided by coagulation results and ongoing blood loss.
    • Consideration of corticosteroids for fetal lung maturation is appropriate if preterm and delivery is anticipated within 24-48 hours.
  • Monitoring and follow-up:
    • Close monitoring of maternal vital signs, urine output, blood loss, and coagulation parameters is essential in the immediate post-delivery period.
    • Post-delivery, continued vigilance for complications such as postpartum haemorrhage, acute kidney injury, and Sheehan's syndrome is important.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Placental abruption"][/sc]
  • Maternal haemorrhage: Significant blood loss can lead to hypovolaemic shock, requiring aggressive fluid resuscitation and blood transfusion, and is a major cause of maternal morbidity.
  • Disseminated Intravascular Coagulation (DIC): Severe abruption can trigger a consumptive coagulopathy, leading to widespread microvascular thrombosis and haemorrhage, which significantly complicates management and prognosis.
  • Acute Kidney Injury (AKI): Resulting from severe hypovolaemia and shock, AKI can occur if maternal renal perfusion is compromised, particularly in the late phase of unmanaged abruption.
  • Sheehan's Syndrome: A rare but severe complication of massive postpartum haemorrhage and shock, leading to pituitary necrosis and subsequent hypopituitarism.
  • Fetal hypoxia and acidosis: Compromised uteroplacental blood flow can lead to fetal oxygen deprivation and metabolic acidosis, increasing the risk of neurological damage or stillbirth.
  • Preterm birth: Abruption often necessitates early delivery, increasing the risks associated with prematurity for the neonate.
