Platelet disorders (including thrombocytosis, thrombocytopaenia) [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Platelet disorders (including thrombocytosis, thrombocytopaenia)"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Platelet disorders (including thrombocytosis, thrombocytopaenia)"][/sc]
  • Platelet disorders encompass a range of conditions affecting platelet number or function, leading to either excessive bleeding (thrombocytopaenia, platelet dysfunction) or thrombosis (thrombocytosis).
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical presentations and initial management principles of common platelet disorders, including thrombocytosis and thrombocytopaenia.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Platelet disorders (including thrombocytosis, thrombocytopaenia)"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Platelet disorders (including thrombocytosis, thrombocytopaenia)"][/sc]
  • Essential thrombocythaemia, a cause of thrombocytosis, has an incidence of approximately 1-2 per 100,000 population per year in the UK, often affecting individuals over 60 years of age.
  • Immune thrombocytopaenia (ITP), a common cause of thrombocytopaenia, has an estimated incidence of 2-5 per 100,000 adults per year in the UK, with a bimodal age distribution.
  • Risk factors for secondary thrombocytosis include chronic inflammation, infection, iron deficiency, and malignancy.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Platelet disorders (including thrombocytosis, thrombocytopaenia)"][/sc]
  • Trigger: Dysregulation of megakaryopoiesis (e.g., JAK2 mutation in essential thrombocythaemia) or accelerated platelet destruction/impaired production (e.g., autoantibodies in ITP, bone marrow failure).
  • Progression: Abnormal platelet counts or function lead to either an increased propensity for clot formation (thrombocytosis) or impaired haemostasis and bleeding (thrombocytopaenia).
  • Consequence: Clinical manifestations range from asymptomatic findings to life-threatening thrombotic events (stroke, myocardial infarction) or severe haemorrhage (intracranial bleed).
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Platelet disorders (including thrombocytosis, thrombocytopaenia)"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Often insidious onset, with symptoms developing gradually over weeks to months, or acute presentation following an inciting event such as infection or drug exposure.
  • Progressive phase: Symptoms may worsen or new complications emerge, particularly beyond several weeks, as the underlying pathology progresses or compensatory mechanisms fail.
  • Late phase: Chronic conditions may lead to persistent symptoms, recurrent thrombotic or bleeding episodes, and potential for myelofibrotic transformation in some myeloproliferative neoplasms.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Platelet disorders (including thrombocytosis, thrombocytopaenia)"][/sc]
  • Ask about easy bruising or petechiae - suggests impaired primary haemostasis due to low platelet count or dysfunction.
  • Ask about prolonged bleeding from minor cuts or after dental procedures - indicates a defect in platelet plug formation.
  • Ask about headaches, visual disturbances, or transient ischaemic attacks - may suggest microvascular thrombosis in thrombocytosis.
  • Ask about fatigue and shortness of breath - could be related to anaemia from chronic blood loss in bleeding disorders.
  • Ask about constitutional symptoms like weight loss or night sweats - may point towards an underlying malignancy causing secondary platelet disorder.
