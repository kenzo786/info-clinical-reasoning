Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Variceal disease including oesophageal varices"][/sc]
  • Oesophageal varices are dilated collateral blood vessels that develop as a complication of portal hypertension, usually in the setting of cirrhosis [1].
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical features of variceal bleeding, initiate immediate management, and understand the principles of preventing re-bleeding.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Variceal disease including oesophageal varices"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Variceal disease including oesophageal varices"][/sc]
  • The prevalence of oesophageal varices varies between 40% and 95% in people with cirrhosis [2].
  • Variceal haemorrhage occurs at a rate around 10-15% per year in patients with cirrhosis [3].
  • Risk factors for variceal bleeding include high portal vein pressure, large varices, and the presence of red wale marks on endoscopy [4].
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Variceal disease including oesophageal varices"][/sc]
  • The primary trigger is **portal hypertension** [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Portal hypertension: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="portal hypertension"][/sc], typically resulting from increased resistance to blood flow through the liver, most commonly due to cirrhosis. This leads to a sustained elevation in pressure within the portal venous system [5].
  • In response to the increased portal pressure, collateral vessels develop to bypass the obstructed liver. These vessels, particularly in the submucosa of the distal oesophagus, become engorged and tortuous, forming oesophageal varices.
  • These thin-walled, dilated varices are susceptible to rupture, especially with further increases in portal pressure or local erosive factors, leading to potentially life-threatening upper gastrointestinal haemorrhage.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Variceal disease including oesophageal varices"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Varices develop gradually in patients with portal hypertension, often remaining asymptomatic for an extended period. The risk of bleeding is initially low.
  • Progressive phase: Varices enlarge over time, particularly beyond several months to a year, increasing the risk of rupture. Factors such as increasing portal pressure and worsening liver function contribute to this progression.
  • Late phase: Once a variceal bleed occurs, there is a significant risk of re-bleeding, particularly within the first few days to weeks. Subsequent bleeds carry a high mortality rate, necessitating ongoing management and prophylaxis.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Variceal disease including oesophageal varices"][/sc]
  • Ask about haematemesis (vomiting blood) - to identify acute variceal rupture and the severity of bleeding, which is often significant and sudden.
  • Ask about melaena (black, tarry stools) or haematochezia (fresh blood in stools) - indicating gastrointestinal bleeding, with melaena suggesting an upper GI source, and haematochezia potentially indicating very rapid transit or a lower source.
  • Ask about symptoms of hypovolaemia such as dizziness, lightheadedness, or syncope - to assess for significant blood loss and impending or actual shock, which can rapidly lead to collapse.
  • Ask about any prior history of liver disease, particularly cirrhosis, or known varices - this is a crucial discriminator as variceal bleeding is a severe complication of portal hypertension in this context.
  • Ask about any recent changes in mental status or confusion - this could indicate hepatic encephalopathy, a complication of severe liver disease often precipitated or worsened by gastrointestinal bleeding.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Variceal disease including oesophageal varices"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - to assess for haemodynamic instability (tachycardia, hypotension) indicating significant blood loss, and to monitor for signs of shock.
  • Check for signs of chronic liver disease (e.g., jaundice, ascites, spider naevi, palmar erythema, asterixis) - these indicate underlying cirrhosis and increased risk of variceal bleeding.
  • Check for signs of hepatic encephalopathy (e.g., altered mental status, confusion, disorientation) - often precipitated or worsened by acute variceal bleeding.
  • Check for abdominal distension and tenderness - may indicate ascites or peritonitis, which can complicate variceal bleeding.
  • Check for pallor - a sign of anaemia due to chronic or acute blood loss [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Pallor in variceal bleeding: what does it indicate and why is it important?" text="pallor"][/sc].
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Variceal disease including oesophageal varices"][/sc]
  • Acute, significant haematemesis or melaena: Indicates active variceal rupture requiring immediate medical attention to prevent rapid haemodynamic compromise.
  • Haemodynamic instability (e.g., hypotension, tachycardia): Suggests severe blood loss and impending or established hypovolaemic shock, necessitating urgent resuscitation and intervention.
  • New or worsening hepatic encephalopathy: Can be precipitated by acute variceal bleeding due to increased nitrogenous load from blood in the gut, indicating a severe decompensation of liver function.
  • Signs of decompensated liver disease (e.g., increasing ascites, jaundice): These features, especially when acute, can indicate a heightened risk of variceal bleeding or a poorer prognosis following a bleed, requiring aggressive management.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Variceal disease including oesophageal varices"][/sc]
  • Older adults: Variceal bleeding in older adults may present with less overt signs of hypovolaemia due to blunted physiological responses, or with more pronounced confusion and delirium, which can mask the underlying gastrointestinal haemorrhage.
  • Pregnancy: Variceal bleeding during pregnancy, particularly in the second trimester and during delivery, carries increased risks due to physiological changes that can exacerbate portal hypertension and increase intra-abdominal pressure.
  • Isolated gastric varices: Bleeding from isolated gastric varices, which are less common than oesophageal varices, can present atypically and may be more challenging to diagnose and manage due to their location and varied morphology [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Isolated gastric varices: what are they and why do they matter clinically?" text="isolated gastric varices"][/sc].
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Variceal disease including oesophageal varices"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected variceal bleeding: Upper gastrointestinal endoscopy [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Upper gastrointestinal endoscopy: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Upper gastrointestinal endoscopy"][/sc] is the gold standard for diagnosing oesophageal varices, assessing their size, and identifying stigmata of recent haemorrhage.
  • Severity/risk tools: The Child-Pugh score and MELD score are used to assess the severity of underlying liver disease and predict prognosis. Child-Pugh Score for Cirrhosis Mortality on MDCalc; MELD Score (Model for End-Stage Liver Disease) on MDCalc.
  • Imaging - CT/MRI: Cross-sectional imaging (CT or MRI) can identify portal hypertension, assess liver morphology, and detect other causes of upper GI bleeding, though endoscopy remains primary for variceal assessment.
  • Serial reassessment: Regular endoscopic surveillance is often indicated for patients with cirrhosis to monitor for variceal development or progression, guiding prophylactic treatment decisions.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Consider further investigations such as gastric biopsy for peptic ulcer disease or angiography for obscure bleeding, depending on clinical suspicion.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Variceal disease including oesophageal varices"][/sc]
  • Peptic Ulcer Disease - often associated with NSAID use or H. pylori infection, and typically presents with epigastric pain, though bleeding can be the first symptom. Endoscopy reveals discrete ulcers rather than dilated vessels.
  • Gastritis/Oesophagitis - inflammation of the stomach or oesophagus, often due to reflux or NSAIDs, causing pain, nausea, or dysphagia. Bleeding is usually less severe and diffuse compared to variceal haemorrhage.
  • Mallory-Weiss Tear - a tear in the oesophageal lining, usually at the gastro-oesophageal junction, caused by forceful vomiting or retching. The history of preceding emesis is a key distinguishing feature.
  • Angiodysplasia - vascular malformations that can occur throughout the GI tract, leading to recurrent, often occult, bleeding. Diagnosis is made endoscopically by identifying characteristic red, flat lesions.
  • Gastric Antral Vascular Ectasia (GAVE) - also known as \'watermelon stomach\', a cause of chronic GI blood loss due to dilated capillaries in the gastric antrum. Endoscopy shows characteristic linear red streaks [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Gastric Antral Vascular Ectasia: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Gastric Antral Vascular Ectasia"][/sc].
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Variceal disease including oesophageal varices"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Recognition of variceal bleeding, rapid haemodynamic resuscitation, and early escalation to a specialist gastroenterology or hepatology team. Airway protection may be required in patients with severe haematemesis or altered mental status.
  • Definitive management: Endoscopic variceal band ligation (EVL) is often the primary endoscopic treatment for oesophageal varices. Pharmacological therapy with vasoactive agents, such as terlipressin, is typically initiated at presentation to reduce portal pressure and control bleeding.
  • Adjunctive management: Prophylactic antibiotic therapy is recommended at presentation to reduce the risk of infection, which can precipitate further bleeding and worsen outcomes. Transjugular intrahepatic portosystemic shunt (TIPSS) [sc name="jitl-query" Q="TIPSS: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="TIPSS"][/sc] may be considered if bleeding is not controlled by endoscopic and pharmacological therapies.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Close monitoring of vital signs, haemoglobin, and liver function is crucial. Regular endoscopic surveillance is often part of long-term management to detect and treat varices before re-bleeding occurs.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Patients with ongoing or recurrent bleeding despite initial measures require urgent re-evaluation and consideration for salvage therapies. Patients and carers should be advised on red flag symptoms and when to seek immediate medical attention.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Variceal disease including oesophageal varices"][/sc]
  • Acute variceal haemorrhage: The most serious and life-threatening complication, occurring when varices rupture, leading to significant blood loss and hypovolaemic shock. This can rapidly progress over minutes to hours.
  • Recurrent bleeding: Patients who have experienced one variceal bleed are at high risk of re-bleeding, particularly within the first few days to weeks, which significantly increases morbidity and mortality.
  • Hepatic encephalopathy: Acute variceal bleeding can precipitate or worsen hepatic encephalopathy due to the increased nitrogenous load from blood in the gastrointestinal tract, leading to altered mental status and neurological dysfunction.
  • Infection: Patients with variceal bleeding are at increased risk of bacterial infections, such as spontaneous bacterial peritonitis, which can further decompensate liver function and worsen prognosis, especially in the days following a bleed.
  • Oesophageal stricture: A potential long-term complication of repeated endoscopic interventions, such as band ligation, which can lead to dysphagia and require further endoscopic management.
