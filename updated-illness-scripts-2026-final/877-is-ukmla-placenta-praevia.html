Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Placenta praevia (Pregnancy and the puerperium)"][/sc]
  • Placenta praevia is a condition where the placenta implants in the lower uterine segment, either partially or completely covering the internal cervical os after 20 weeks of gestation [1].
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical presentation and potential complications of placenta praevia, including the need for timely diagnosis and appropriate management strategies to minimise maternal and fetal morbidity and mortality.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Placenta praevia (Pregnancy and the puerperium)"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Placenta praevia (Pregnancy and the puerperium)"][/sc]
  • The estimated incidence of placenta praevia at term is approximately 1 in 200 pregnancies in the UK [1].
  • Risk factors include previous caesarean section (risk increases with number of prior sections), advanced maternal age, multiparity, assisted reproductive technology, and maternal smoking [1].
  • The incidence of placenta praevia has been increasing due to rising rates of caesarean deliveries and increased maternal age [1].
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Placenta praevia (Pregnancy and the puerperium)"][/sc]
  • Trigger: Abnormal implantation of the blastocyst in the lower uterine segment, often due to previous uterine scarring or endometrial damage.
  • Progression: As the lower uterine segment thins and stretches in the third trimester, the placental attachment may detach, leading to painless bleeding. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Lower uterine segment: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Lower uterine segment physiology"][/sc]
  • Consequence: Recurrent antepartum haemorrhage, increased risk of preterm birth, and potential for placenta accreta spectrum disorders due to abnormal placental adherence to the myometrium.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Placenta praevia (Pregnancy and the puerperium)"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Placental position is typically assessed during the routine fetal anomaly scan at 18+6 to 21+6 weeks of gestation. A low-lying placenta at this stage may resolve as the uterus grows.
  • Progressive phase: If the placenta remains low-lying or praevia at 32 weeks, a follow-up scan is recommended. Symptomatic presentation, usually painless vaginal bleeding, often occurs in the third trimester, particularly beyond 28 weeks of gestation.
  • Late phase: Persistent placenta praevia necessitates planned delivery, typically via caesarean section, between 36+0 and 37+0 weeks of gestation for uncomplicated cases, or earlier if complications arise.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Placenta praevia (Pregnancy and the puerperium)"][/sc]
  • Ask about painless vaginal bleeding - this is the hallmark symptom, typically bright red and occurring in the third trimester, distinguishing it from painful bleeding associated with placental abruption.
  • Ask about onset and duration of bleeding - sudden onset without preceding trauma or activity is characteristic.
  • Ask about uterine contractions or abdominal pain - absence of pain helps differentiate from other causes of antepartum haemorrhage.
  • Ask about previous episodes of bleeding - recurrent, self-limiting episodes are common.
  • Ask about fetal movements - usually normal, unless significant maternal haemorrhage or fetal compromise has occurred.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Placenta praevia (Pregnancy and the puerperium)"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - maternal tachycardia and hypotension may indicate significant blood loss, requiring urgent resuscitation.
  • Check abdominal examination - uterus is typically soft, non-tender, and relaxed. Fetal lie may be abnormal (e.g., transverse or oblique).
  • Check fetal heart rate - usually normal, but may show signs of distress if maternal hypovolaemia or fetal compromise is present.
  • Check speculum examination - visualises the cervix and confirms vaginal bleeding, helping to exclude local causes of bleeding. Digital vaginal examination is contraindicated in suspected placenta praevia due to risk of provoking massive haemorrhage. Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Placenta praevia (Pregnancy and the puerperium)"][/sc]
  • Heavy, ongoing vaginal bleeding: Indicates significant maternal haemorrhage, potentially leading to hypovolaemic shock and requiring immediate resuscitation and delivery.
  • Maternal haemodynamic instability: Tachycardia, hypotension, or altered mental status suggests severe blood loss and impending shock.
  • Signs of fetal distress: Abnormal fetal heart rate patterns or reduced fetal movements may indicate fetal compromise due to maternal hypovolaemia or placental insufficiency.
  • Bleeding after 37+0 weeks of gestation: While planned delivery is typically between 36+0 and 37+0 weeks, any significant bleeding beyond this gestation, especially if undiagnosed, warrants urgent assessment.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Placenta praevia (Pregnancy and the puerperium)"][/sc]
  • Asymptomatic women: Placenta praevia may be diagnosed incidentally on routine antenatal ultrasound without any bleeding episodes. These women still require careful monitoring and planned delivery.
  • Placenta accreta spectrum: In cases where the placenta is abnormally adherent or invasive into the myometrium, bleeding may be less pronounced antepartum but can lead to massive haemorrhage at delivery. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Placenta accreta spectrum: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Placenta accreta spectrum"][/sc]
  • Painful bleeding: Rarely, placenta praevia can co-exist with placental abruption, leading to painful vaginal bleeding, which can complicate diagnosis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Placenta praevia (Pregnancy and the puerperium)"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected placenta praevia:
    • Transvaginal ultrasound (TVS): The gold standard for diagnosing placenta praevia, accurately determining the placental edge's relationship to the internal cervical os [1].
  • Severity/risk tools:
    • No specific severity score for placenta praevia is widely used. Risk assessment is based on clinical factors (e.g., amount of bleeding, maternal haemodynamic status) and ultrasound findings (e.g., degree of os coverage, suspicion of accreta).
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible:
    • Full blood count (FBC): To assess maternal anaemia and guide transfusion needs.
    • Coagulation screen: To assess for coagulopathy in cases of massive haemorrhage.
    • Kleihauer test: To quantify feto-maternal haemorrhage if fetal compromise is suspected.
  • Imaging - MRI: May be considered in complex cases, particularly when placenta accreta spectrum is suspected, to delineate the extent of placental invasion [1].
  • Serial reassessment:
    • Repeat TVS at 32 weeks of gestation for women with a low-lying placenta at the anomaly scan, and again at 36 weeks if still low-lying, to confirm persistence or resolution [1].
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Placenta praevia (Pregnancy and the puerperium)"][/sc]
  • A 32-year-old G2P1 woman at 34 weeks gestation presents with sudden onset, painless, bright red vaginal bleeding, without uterine contractions or abdominal pain, and normal fetal movements.
  • Gravid female with painless antepartum haemorrhage in the third trimester, concerning for placenta praevia.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Placenta praevia (Pregnancy and the puerperium)"][/sc]
  • Placental abruption - typically presents with painful vaginal bleeding, uterine tenderness, and often fetal distress.
  • Vasa praevia - painless vaginal bleeding, often scant, with associated fetal distress (e.g., sinusoidal fetal heart rate pattern) due to rupture of fetal vessels. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Vasa praevia: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Vasa praevia"][/sc]
  • Cervical ectropion/polyp - usually causes light, often post-coital, bleeding and is diagnosed on speculum examination.
  • Vaginal laceration/trauma - history of trauma, often associated with pain.
  • Cervicitis/vaginitis - associated with discharge, itching, or dyspareunia, and bleeding is usually minimal.
  • Ectopic pregnancy - presents earlier in pregnancy with abdominal pain and vaginal bleeding, typically before viability.
  • Pelvic inflammatory disease (PID) - presents with pelvic pain, fever, and vaginal discharge, usually not in the third trimester of pregnancy.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Placenta praevia (Pregnancy and the puerperium)"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Recognition of antepartum haemorrhage, urgent maternal and fetal assessment, and appropriate escalation to a senior obstetric team. Supportive care often includes intravenous fluid resuscitation and blood product transfusion if indicated.
  • Definitive management: Planned caesarean section is typically warranted for persistent placenta praevia. The timing of delivery is individualised but often considered between 36+0 and 37+0 weeks of gestation for uncomplicated cases [1].
  • Adjunctive management: Antenatal corticosteroids may be considered for fetal lung maturation if there is a risk of preterm delivery before 34 weeks of gestation [1]. Tocolysis may be used for up to 48 hours to facilitate corticosteroid administration if preterm labour is suspected [1].
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Close maternal and fetal monitoring is essential, particularly in cases of active bleeding. Women with asymptomatic placenta praevia require ongoing surveillance.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Women managed at home should be advised to return to hospital immediately if any further bleeding, contractions, or pain occur.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Placenta praevia (Pregnancy and the puerperium)"][/sc]
  • Maternal haemorrhage: The primary complication, ranging from mild spotting to massive, life-threatening antepartum or postpartum haemorrhage, particularly during delivery.
  • Placenta accreta spectrum: Increased risk of the placenta abnormally adhering to (accreta), invading (increta), or penetrating (percreta) the uterine wall, leading to severe haemorrhage and potential hysterectomy at delivery. This risk is significantly higher with previous caesarean sections.
  • Preterm birth: Often necessitated by recurrent or severe bleeding, leading to risks associated with prematurity for the neonate.
  • Fetal compromise: Can occur due to maternal hypovolaemia, placental insufficiency, or iatrogenic causes during emergency delivery.
  • Need for hysterectomy: In cases of intractable haemorrhage, especially with placenta accreta spectrum, hysterectomy may be required to save the mother's life.
  • Increased maternal and fetal morbidity and mortality: Due to the aforementioned complications, particularly if diagnosis is delayed or management is suboptimal. These risks are heightened with increasing gestational age, particularly beyond 28 weeks.
