Stevens-Johnson Syndrome/ toxic epidermal necrolysis (TEN) [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Stevens-Johnson Syndrome/ toxic epidermal necrolysis (TEN)"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Stevens-Johnson Syndrome/ toxic epidermal necrolysis (TEN)"][/sc]
  • Stevens-Johnson Syndrome (SJS) and Toxic Epidermal Necrolysis (TEN) represent a spectrum of severe, acute mucocutaneous reactions, primarily triggered by medications, characterised by extensive epidermal detachment.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical features, identify potential triggers, and understand the immediate management principles of SJS/TEN to prevent severe morbidity and mortality.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Stevens-Johnson Syndrome/ toxic epidermal necrolysis (TEN)"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Stevens-Johnson Syndrome/ toxic epidermal necrolysis (TEN)"][/sc]
  • The incidence of SJS is estimated at 1-2 cases per million person-years, while TEN is rarer, affecting 0.4-1.2 cases per million person-years in the UK.
  • Risk factors include certain medications (e.g., allopurinol, anti-epileptics, sulfonamides), HIV infection, systemic lupus erythematosus, and genetic predispositions (e.g., HLA-B*1502 with carbamazepine).
  • Older adults and immunosuppressed individuals may have a higher risk of severe outcomes due to comorbidities and reduced physiological reserve.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Stevens-Johnson Syndrome/ toxic epidermal necrolysis (TEN)"][/sc]
  • Triggering agents, typically drugs, induce an immune-mediated cytotoxic reaction targeting keratinocytes, often involving T-cells and Fas-FasL interactions.
  • This leads to widespread apoptosis of epidermal cells, resulting in subepidermal blistering and subsequent full-thickness epidermal detachment.
  • The consequence is extensive skin loss, resembling a severe burn, leading to significant fluid and electrolyte imbalance, infection risk, and multi-organ failure.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Stevens-Johnson Syndrome/ toxic epidermal necrolysis (TEN)"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Prodromal symptoms such as fever, malaise, and flu-like illness often precede skin lesions by 1-3 days, sometimes longer.
  • Progressive phase: Skin lesions, initially erythematous macules with atypical targetoid appearance, rapidly spread and coalesce, leading to blistering and epidermal detachment, particularly beyond 48-72 hours from onset.
  • Late phase: The disease can progress rapidly over several days to weeks, with continued epidermal loss and potential for severe systemic complications if not managed promptly.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Stevens-Johnson Syndrome/ toxic epidermal necrolysis (TEN)"][/sc]
  • Ask about recent medication history - crucial for identifying potential drug triggers, often within 4 days to 4 weeks of exposure.
  • Ask about fever and malaise - common prodromal symptoms indicating systemic inflammation before skin eruption.
  • Ask about painful skin and mucosal lesions - characteristic of SJS/TEN, distinguishing it from less severe rashes.
  • Ask about ocular irritation or blurred vision - suggests ocular involvement, which can lead to long-term complications.
  • Ask about difficulty swallowing or painful urination - indicative of oral or genitourinary mucosal involvement, impacting hydration and nutrition.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Stevens-Johnson Syndrome/ toxic epidermal necrolysis (TEN)"][/sc]
  • Check for widespread erythematous or purpuric macules with atypical targetoid lesions - early skin manifestations that rapidly progress.
  • Check for Nikolsky's sign (epidermal detachment with gentle lateral pressure) - a key diagnostic feature indicating epidermal fragility.
  • Check for mucosal involvement (oral, ocular, genital) - often severe and present in nearly all cases, contributing to patient discomfort and complications.
  • Check vital signs - tachycardia, hypotension, and fever are common due to systemic inflammation and fluid loss, indicating severity.
  • Check for epidermal detachment, particularly >10% body surface area for SJS/TEN overlap, and >30% for TEN - critical for classification and prognosis.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Stevens-Johnson Syndrome/ toxic epidermal necrolysis (TEN)"][/sc]
  • Rapid progression of skin lesions - indicates aggressive disease course and need for urgent intervention.
  • Extensive mucosal involvement - significantly increases morbidity due to pain, feeding difficulties, and potential for infection.
  • Positive Nikolsky's sign - a strong indicator of epidermal necrosis and detachment, requiring immediate specialist referral.
  • Systemic symptoms (e.g., high fever, haemodynamic instability) - suggest severe systemic inflammatory response and potential for multi-organ dysfunction.
  • Recent introduction of high-risk medication - provides a crucial clue for diagnosis and necessitates immediate cessation of the suspected agent.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Stevens-Johnson Syndrome/ toxic epidermal necrolysis (TEN)"][/sc]
  • Older adults: May present with less pronounced prodromal symptoms or atypical skin lesions due to altered immune responses, leading to delayed diagnosis.
  • Immunosuppressed patients: Can have an accelerated and more severe course, with increased risk of secondary infections and poorer prognosis due to compromised immune surveillance.
  • Paediatric cases: While rare, can be challenging to diagnose as initial symptoms may mimic common childhood infections, requiring a high index of suspicion.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Stevens-Johnson Syndrome/ toxic epidermal necrolysis (TEN)"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected SJS/TEN: Skin biopsy for histopathological examination, showing full-thickness epidermal necrosis with sparse dermal infiltrate, confirming the diagnosis.
  • Core for suspected SJS/TEN: Full blood count, urea and electrolytes, liver function tests, C-reactive protein, and blood cultures to assess systemic involvement and rule out sepsis.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Direct immunofluorescence studies to exclude autoimmune bullous diseases.
  • Serial reassessment: SCORTEN score on MDCalc to assess prognosis and guide management decisions, typically calculated at presentation and 24 hours later.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Stevens-Johnson Syndrome/ toxic epidermal necrolysis (TEN)"][/sc]
  • A 55-year-old male presents with a 3-day history of widespread painful erythematous rash, oral erosions, and fever, following initiation of allopurinol two weeks prior.
  • Acutely unwell, febrile, poly-mucosal, rapidly progressive, drug-induced, severe cutaneous adverse reaction.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Stevens-Johnson Syndrome/ toxic epidermal necrolysis (TEN)"][/sc]
  • Erythema multiforme major - less extensive epidermal detachment, often target lesions with three zones.
  • Staphylococcal scalded skin syndrome (SSSS) - typically affects children, positive Nikolsky's sign but superficial epidermal cleavage, no mucosal involvement.
  • Acute generalised exanthematous pustulosis (AGEP) - sterile pustules on erythematous background, rapid onset after drug exposure.
  • Drug reaction with eosinophilia and systemic symptoms (DRESS) - delayed onset, prominent eosinophilia, lymphadenopathy, and internal organ involvement.
  • Toxic shock syndrome - associated with bacterial toxins, often with hypotension and multi-organ failure, but skin changes are typically a diffuse erythroderma.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Stevens-Johnson Syndrome/ toxic epidermal necrolysis (TEN)"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Withdraw all non-essential medications, especially suspected culprits. Transfer to a specialist burn unit or intensive care unit for supportive care.
  • Definitive management: Meticulous wound care, fluid and electrolyte management, nutritional support, and pain control. Systemic corticosteroids or intravenous immunoglobulins (IVIG) may be considered, though evidence for their efficacy is mixed.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Close monitoring for infection, fluid balance, and organ function. Long-term follow-up for ocular, skin, and psychological sequelae.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Educate patient on avoiding culprit drugs and related compounds. Provide medical alert information.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Stevens-Johnson Syndrome/ toxic epidermal necrolysis (TEN)"][/sc]
  • Sepsis: Mechanism involves extensive skin barrier breakdown, leading to bacterial entry and systemic infection, significantly increasing mortality.
  • Fluid and electrolyte imbalance: Mechanism involves massive fluid loss through denuded skin, leading to hypovolaemia, electrolyte derangements, and acute kidney injury.
  • Ocular complications: Mechanism involves inflammation and scarring of conjunctiva and cornea, leading to dry eyes, photophobia, synechiae, and potentially blindness.
  • Pulmonary complications: Mechanism involves mucosal involvement of the respiratory tract, leading to pneumonia, acute respiratory distress syndrome (ARDS), and bronchiolitis obliterans.
  • Long-term skin sequelae: Mechanism involves abnormal wound healing, leading to dyspigmentation, scarring, chronic pruritus, and nail dystrophy.
