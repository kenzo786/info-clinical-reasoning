Rheumatoid arthritis (Musculoskeletal) [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Rheumatoid arthritis"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Rheumatoid arthritis"][/sc]
  • Rheumatoid arthritis (RA) is a chronic, systemic autoimmune disease primarily affecting the synovial lining of joints, leading to inflammation, pain, stiffness, and progressive joint damage.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical features of rheumatoid arthritis, understand its systemic nature, and initiate appropriate referral for specialist assessment and management.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Rheumatoid arthritis"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Rheumatoid arthritis"][/sc]
  • Rheumatoid arthritis affects approximately 0.5–1% of the adult population in the UK, with around 400,000 people living with the condition [1].
  • Incidence is higher in women than men (ratio of 2-3:1), typically presenting between 30 and 50 years of age, though it can occur at any age [1].
  • Risk factors include genetic predisposition (e.g., HLA-DRB1 alleles), smoking, obesity, and a history of periodontitis [1].
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Rheumatoid arthritis"][/sc]
  • An unknown trigger (e.g., infection, environmental factor) in genetically predisposed individuals initiates an autoimmune response, leading to the production of autoantibodies such as rheumatoid factor (RF) and anti-cyclic citrullinated peptide (anti-CCP) antibodies.
  • This immune dysregulation results in chronic inflammation of the synovial membrane, characterised by infiltration of immune cells (T-cells, B-cells, macrophages) and the release of pro-inflammatory cytokines (e.g., TNF-α, IL-6), causing synovial hypertrophy and pannus formation.
  • The proliferating pannus invades and erodes articular cartilage and subchondral bone, leading to irreversible joint damage, deformity, and loss of function, with systemic inflammation contributing to extra-articular manifestations. Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Rheumatoid arthritis"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Symptoms often develop insidiously over weeks to months, starting with morning stiffness, fatigue, and symmetrical small joint pain, particularly in the hands and feet.
  • Progressive phase: Without effective treatment, inflammation progresses, leading to persistent synovitis, joint swelling, reduced range of motion, and the development of joint deformities and erosions within months to a few years.
  • Late phase: Chronic, uncontrolled inflammation results in severe joint destruction, functional disability, and an increased risk of extra-articular manifestations, impacting quality of life and increasing morbidity.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Rheumatoid arthritis"][/sc]
  • Ask about morning stiffness - typically lasting more than 30 minutes, differentiating inflammatory from mechanical joint pain.
  • Ask about symmetrical joint pain and swelling - commonly affecting small joints of hands (MCPs, PIPs) and feet (MTPs), which helps distinguish from other arthropathies.
  • Ask about fatigue and malaise - common systemic symptoms reflecting ongoing inflammatory activity.
  • Ask about functional limitations - difficulty with daily activities like gripping, walking, or dressing, indicating disease impact and progression.
  • Ask about onset and progression - insidious onset over weeks to months is typical, distinguishing it from acute inflammatory conditions.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Rheumatoid arthritis"][/sc]
  • Check joint swelling and tenderness - typically symmetrical, affecting small joints (MCPs, PIPs, MTPs), wrists, and ankles, indicating active synovitis.
  • Check for warmth and erythema over affected joints - signs of acute inflammation.
  • Check range of motion - often reduced due to pain, swelling, and eventually joint destruction.
  • Check for rheumatoid nodules - subcutaneous nodules, typically over extensor surfaces, indicating more severe disease.
  • Check vital signs - may reveal low-grade fever in active disease, but often normal.
  • Check DAS28 score - a composite disease activity score used to monitor disease activity and treatment response. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="DAS28 score: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="DAS28 score"][/sc]
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Rheumatoid arthritis"][/sc]
  • Rapid onset of severe symptoms: Suggests a more aggressive disease course or an alternative diagnosis requiring urgent investigation.
  • Systemic features (e.g., unexplained weight loss, fever, night sweats): May indicate severe inflammatory activity, vasculitis, or other systemic complications.
  • Neurological symptoms (e.g., cervical myelopathy, mononeuritis multiplex): Suggests potential atlantoaxial subluxation or rheumatoid vasculitis affecting peripheral nerves, requiring urgent assessment.
  • New or worsening respiratory symptoms (e.g., dyspnoea, cough): May indicate interstitial lung disease (ILD) or pleural involvement, which are serious extra-articular manifestations.
  • Ocular symptoms (e.g., painful red eye, visual disturbance): Could indicate scleritis or episcleritis, which are inflammatory eye conditions associated with RA.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Rheumatoid arthritis"][/sc]
  • Older adults: May present with more acute onset, predominant large joint involvement, and less symmetrical disease, making diagnosis challenging.
  • Palindromic rheumatism: Characterised by recurrent, self-limiting episodes of joint pain and swelling, which can evolve into persistent RA in some individuals.
  • Monoarthritis: While typically polyarticular, RA can rarely present as monoarthritis, especially in early stages, requiring careful differentiation from other causes of single joint inflammation.   • Systemic onset: Rarely, RA can present with prominent systemic features like fever, weight loss, and lymphadenopathy before significant joint involvement, mimicking other systemic inflammatory conditions.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Rheumatoid arthritis"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected rheumatoid arthritis:
    • Blood tests: Rheumatoid Factor (RF), anti-CCP antibodies (most specific), ESR, CRP.
    • Imaging: X-rays of hands and feet to assess for erosions and joint space narrowing.
  • Severity/risk tools: DAS28 score DAS28 score on MDCalc.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Further imaging (e.g., ultrasound or MRI to detect synovitis or erosions earlier than X-ray), or other serological tests as indicated.
  • Serial reassessment: Regular monitoring of DAS28, ESR, and CRP to assess disease activity and treatment response. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Erythrocyte Sedimentation Rate (ESR): what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="ESR"][/sc]
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Rheumatoid arthritis"][/sc]
  • A 45-year-old female presents with a 3-month history of symmetrical small joint pain and swelling in her hands and feet, associated with morning stiffness lasting over an hour, and significant fatigue.
  • Chronic, symmetrical inflammatory polyarthritis with prominent morning stiffness and systemic features, concerning for rheumatoid arthritis.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Rheumatoid arthritis"][/sc]
  • Osteoarthritis: Typically affects weight-bearing joints and is asymmetrical, with morning stiffness lasting less than 30 minutes and absence of systemic features or inflammatory markers.
  • Psoriatic Arthritis: Often asymmetrical, associated with psoriasis, dactylitis, and enthesitis, and may involve DIP joints.
  • Systemic Lupus Erythematosus (SLE): Can cause polyarthritis, but joint involvement is typically non-erosive, and other systemic features (e.g., malar rash, serositis, renal involvement) are prominent.
  • Gout: Characterised by acute, severe monoarticular attacks, often affecting the big toe, with hyperuricaemia and crystal identification in synovial fluid.
  • Polymyalgia Rheumatica: Presents with bilateral shoulder and hip girdle pain and stiffness in older adults, without significant peripheral joint synovitis.
  • Viral Arthritis: Acute onset, often self-limiting polyarthritis following a viral infection, usually without chronic erosive changes. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Viral arthritis: how does it differ from rheumatoid arthritis?" text="Viral Arthritis"][/sc]
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Rheumatoid arthritis"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Prompt referral to a rheumatologist for early diagnosis and initiation of disease-modifying anti-rheumatic drugs (DMARDs) to prevent irreversible joint damage.
  • Definitive management: Often involves a treat-to-target strategy with conventional synthetic DMARDs (csDMARDs) such as methotrexate, leflunomide, or sulfasalazine, often combined with short-term glucocorticoids as bridging therapy.
  • Adjunctive management: May include non-pharmacological interventions such as physiotherapy, occupational therapy, and pain management strategies.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Regular assessment of disease activity (e.g., DAS28, ESR, CRP), monitoring for treatment side effects, and screening for comorbidities.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Consideration of biological DMARDs (bDMARDs) or targeted synthetic DMARDs (tsDMARDs) if csDMARDs fail to achieve treatment targets.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Rheumatoid arthritis"][/sc]
  • Joint destruction and deformity: Persistent inflammation, particularly in the progressive and late phases, leads to irreversible damage to cartilage and bone, resulting in joint deformities (e.g., ulnar deviation, swan-neck deformities) and functional disability.
  • Cardiovascular disease: Chronic systemic inflammation increases the risk of atherosclerosis, leading to higher rates of myocardial infarction, stroke, and heart failure, often manifesting in the later stages of the disease.
  • Interstitial lung disease (ILD): A significant extra-articular manifestation, often developing in the progressive or late phases, leading to progressive pulmonary fibrosis, dyspnoea, and reduced lung function.
  • Rheumatoid vasculitis: A severe, though rare, complication that can affect small to medium-sized blood vessels, leading to skin ulcers, neuropathy, and organ damage, typically in long-standing, severe disease.
  • Osteoporosis: Chronic inflammation, reduced mobility, and glucocorticoid use contribute to accelerated bone loss, increasing the risk of fractures, particularly in the progressive and late phases.
  • Felty\'s syndrome: A rare but serious complication characterised by the triad of RA, splenomegaly, and neutropenia, typically seen in individuals with long-standing, severe RA.
