Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Otitis media (Ear, nose and throat)"][/sc]
  • Otitis media encompasses a spectrum of inflammatory conditions affecting the middle ear, commonly categorised as acute otitis media (AOM) or otitis media with effusion (OME, or glue ear).
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical features of otitis media, differentiate between acute and effusive forms, and understand the principles of management, including indications for watchful waiting and referral.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Otitis media (Ear, nose and throat)"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Otitis media (Ear, nose and throat)"][/sc]
  • AOM is one of the most common childhood infections, with approximately 80% of children experiencing at least one episode by age three [1].
  • OME affects about 80% of children by the age of 10, with peak incidence between two and five years, often following an upper respiratory tract infection [2].
  • Risk factors include young age, male sex, attendance at daycare, exposure to tobacco smoke, lack of breastfeeding, and certain genetic predispositions.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Otitis media (Ear, nose and throat)"][/sc]
  • Trigger: Viral upper respiratory tract infections (URTIs) are the most common antecedent, leading to Eustachian tube dysfunction and inflammation.
  • Progression: Impaired Eustachian tube function results in negative middle ear pressure, accumulation of fluid, and subsequent bacterial or viral proliferation within the middle ear space, leading to AOM. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Eustachian tube dysfunction: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Eustachian tube dysfunction"][/sc]
  • Consequence: Persistent middle ear effusion without acute infection characterises OME, which can lead to conductive hearing loss and speech development issues if prolonged.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Otitis media (Ear, nose and throat)"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Acute otitis media typically presents with rapid onset symptoms, often peaking within 24-48 hours of onset.
  • Progressive phase: Most cases of AOM resolve spontaneously within 3 days, and up to 80% resolve within a week, even without antibiotic treatment.
  • Late phase: Middle ear effusion (OME) can persist for weeks or months after an episode of AOM, with resolution often occurring within 3 months, particularly beyond 6 weeks.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Otitis media (Ear, nose and throat)"][/sc]
  • Ask about ear pain (otalgia) - often severe and sudden in AOM, indicating middle ear inflammation.
  • Ask about fever - common in AOM, especially in younger children, suggesting systemic inflammatory response.
  • Ask about hearing loss - can be a prominent symptom in OME (glue ear) due to fluid in the middle ear, affecting sound conduction.
  • Ask about irritability or difficulty sleeping - non-specific symptoms in infants and young children with AOM, indicating discomfort.
  • Ask about recent upper respiratory tract infection - a common preceding event for both AOM and OME, highlighting the link to Eustachian tube dysfunction.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Otitis media (Ear, nose and throat)"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - fever may be present in AOM, but vital signs are typically stable unless severe systemic infection.
  • Check otoscopy findings in AOM - bulging, opaque, or erythematous tympanic membrane with loss of landmarks, indicating middle ear inflammation and effusion.
  • Check otoscopy findings in OME - dull, retracted, or amber-coloured tympanic membrane with visible fluid levels or air bubbles, indicating persistent middle ear effusion. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Otoscopy findings in otitis media: what do they indicate and why does it matter clinically?" text="Otoscopy findings"][/sc]
  • Check pneumatic otoscopy or tympanometry - reduced tympanic membrane mobility or a type B tympanogram indicates middle ear effusion.
  • Check mastoid tenderness or swelling - a rare but important sign suggesting mastoiditis, a serious complication.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Otitis media (Ear, nose and throat)"][/sc]
  • Severe systemic illness: Suggests bacterial sepsis or other serious infection requiring urgent assessment and management.
  • Mastoid tenderness or swelling: Indicates acute mastoiditis, a serious complication requiring urgent ENT referral and intravenous antibiotics.
  • Facial nerve palsy: Suggests spread of infection to the facial nerve, requiring urgent ENT assessment.
  • Meningism (neck stiffness, photophobia): Raises concern for meningitis, a life-threatening complication requiring immediate medical attention.
  • Persistent OME with significant hearing loss beyond 3 months: May warrant referral for audiological assessment and consideration of surgical intervention (e.g., grommets) to prevent developmental delays.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Otitis media (Ear, nose and throat)"][/sc]
  • Infants: May present with non-specific symptoms such as irritability, poor feeding, vomiting, or diarrhoea, without clear ear pain.
  • Immunocompromised individuals: Can have more severe or prolonged infections, with an increased risk of complications, and may not respond typically to standard treatments.
  • Children with craniofacial abnormalities (e.g., Down\\'s syndrome, cleft palate): Are at higher risk of recurrent otitis media due to anatomical differences affecting Eustachian tube function. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Risk factors for recurrent otitis media: what are they and why do they matter clinically?" text="Recurrent otitis media"][/sc]
  • Adults: AOM is less common in adults but can be more severe, with a higher risk of complications, especially in those with underlying health conditions.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Otitis media (Ear, nose and throat)"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected AOM: Clinical diagnosis based on acute onset of symptoms, presence of middle ear effusion (e.g., bulging tympanic membrane), and signs of middle ear inflammation (e.g., erythema, otalgia).
  • Core for suspected OME: Clinical diagnosis based on persistent middle ear effusion (e.g., dull, retracted tympanic membrane) and associated hearing loss, in the absence of acute infection.
  • Severity/risk tools: No routinely used validated severity/risk scores for otitis media are clinically relevant for primary care management in the UK.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Consider a throat swab for bacterial culture if streptococcal pharyngitis is suspected, or a viral PCR if specific viral infections are a concern.
  • Audiometry: Recommended for children with persistent OME (glue ear) for 3 months or more, or with concerns about hearing or speech development, to quantify hearing loss. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Audiometry in otitis media with effusion: what is its role and why does it matter clinically?" text="Audiometry"][/sc]
  • Tympanometry: Objective measure of middle ear function, useful in confirming the presence of middle ear effusion, especially in younger children where otoscopy can be challenging.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Otitis media (Ear, nose and throat)"][/sc]
  • A 3-year-old male presents with a 2-day history of severe right ear pain, fever, and irritability following a recent cold, with otoscopic examination revealing a bulging, erythematous tympanic membrane.
  • A young child with acute onset ear pain and fever, concerning for acute otitis media.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Otitis media (Ear, nose and throat)"][/sc]
  • Otitis externa - pain on tragal pressure or movement of the pinna, often with discharge, distinguishing it from middle ear pathology.
  • Referred otalgia (e.g., dental pain, temporomandibular joint dysfunction, pharyngitis) - absence of otoscopic signs of otitis media, with pain originating from other head and neck structures.
  • Foreign body in ear canal - visible foreign body on otoscopy, often with sudden onset pain or hearing loss.
  • Acute mastoiditis - post-auricular tenderness, swelling, and erythema, often displacing the pinna, indicating spread of infection beyond the middle ear.
  • Cholesteatoma - typically presents with chronic ear discharge, progressive conductive hearing loss, and sometimes vertigo or facial nerve palsy, distinguishing it from simple otitis media. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Cholesteatoma: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Cholesteatoma"][/sc]
  • Perforated tympanic membrane - sudden relief of pain with onset of discharge, visible perforation on otoscopy.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Otitis media (Ear, nose and throat)"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Recognition of severe illness or red flag features requiring urgent escalation. Supportive care with adequate analgesia (paracetamol or ibuprofen) is crucial for symptom relief.
  • Definitive management (AOM): Watchful waiting for 48-72 hours is often recommended for AOM in children without severe symptoms, as most cases resolve spontaneously. Antibiotics (e.g., amoxicillin) may be considered if symptoms do not improve or worsen, or for children under 2 years with bilateral AOM or otorrhoea.
  • Definitive management (OME): Active monitoring for 3 months is typically recommended, as OME often resolves spontaneously. If OME persists beyond 3 months with associated hearing loss or developmental concerns, referral to audiology and ENT may be warranted for consideration of grommet insertion. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Grommet insertion for otitis media with effusion: what are the indications and why does it matter clinically?" text="Grommet insertion"][/sc]
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Advise parents to seek re-assessment if symptoms worsen or do not improve as expected. For OME, regular audiological assessment and developmental monitoring are important.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Provide clear advice on when to seek further medical attention, particularly for worsening pain, high fever, or development of red flag symptoms.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Otitis media (Ear, nose and throat)"][/sc]
  • Mastoiditis: Spread of infection from the middle ear to the mastoid air cells, typically presenting with post-auricular pain, swelling, and erythema, requiring urgent surgical and medical management.
  • Intracranial complications (e.g., meningitis, brain abscess): Rare but life-threatening, occurring due to direct spread of infection from the middle ear or mastoid, often presenting with severe headache, focal neurological deficits, or altered mental status.
  • Hearing loss: Persistent conductive hearing loss due to OME can impact speech and language development, academic performance, and social interaction, particularly if prolonged beyond 3-6 months.
  • Tympanic membrane perforation: Can occur during acute infection due to increased middle ear pressure, often leading to temporary otorrhoea and pain relief, usually healing spontaneously.
  • Cholesteatoma: A destructive, expanding lesion of the middle ear and mastoid, often associated with chronic otitis media, which can lead to progressive hearing loss, vertigo, and facial nerve palsy.
