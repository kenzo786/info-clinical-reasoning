Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Gangrene (Heart and vasculature)"][/sc]
  • Gangrene is a serious and potentially life-threatening condition characterised by tissue necrosis due to insufficient blood supply (ischaemia) or severe bacterial infection.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the different types of gangrene, their clinical features, and the urgency required for management to prevent further tissue loss and systemic complications.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Gangrene (Heart and vasculature)"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Gangrene (Heart and vasculature)"][/sc]
  • Gangrene is a severe complication of peripheral arterial disease (PAD), which affects approximately 1 in 5 people aged over 60 in the UK.
  • Diabetic foot ulcers, a significant risk factor for gangrene, affect 10-15% of people with diabetes, with a lifetime risk of 15-25%.
  • Risk factors include advanced age, diabetes mellitus, peripheral arterial disease, smoking, trauma, immunosuppression, and surgical procedures.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Gangrene (Heart and vasculature)"][/sc]
  • Trigger: Insufficient blood flow (ischaemia) due to vascular occlusion (e.g., atherosclerosis, embolism) or severe bacterial infection leads to cellular hypoxia and nutrient deprivation.
  • Progression: Prolonged ischaemia results in irreversible cell injury and death (necrosis). In wet gangrene, bacterial proliferation exacerbates tissue destruction and can lead to systemic sepsis. In gas gangrene, anaerobic bacteria like Clostridium perfringens produce toxins and gas, rapidly destroying tissue.
  • Consequence: Necrotic tissue becomes non-viable, often discoloured (black in dry gangrene, swollen and discoloured in wet gangrene), and can serve as a nidus for infection, potentially leading to limb loss or systemic inflammatory response syndrome (SIRS) and death. Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Gangrene (Heart and vasculature)"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Initial ischaemia may present with pain, pallor, paraesthesia, pulselessness, and paralysis (the 5 Ps). Dry gangrene typically develops slowly over days to weeks, while wet and gas gangrene can progress rapidly within hours to days.
  • Progressive phase: Tissue necrosis becomes evident, with changes in skin colour (mottling, blackening), blistering, and foul-smelling discharge in wet gangrene. Systemic signs of infection (fever, tachycardia) may develop, particularly beyond 24-48 hours in wet or gas gangrene.
  • Late phase: Established gangrene leads to irreversible tissue damage, often requiring surgical debridement or amputation. Untreated wet or gas gangrene can rapidly lead to sepsis, multi-organ failure, and death within days.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Gangrene (Heart and vasculature)"][/sc]
  • Ask about severe pain in the affected area - this often precedes visible tissue changes and indicates severe ischaemia or infection.
  • Ask about numbness or loss of sensation - this suggests nerve damage due to prolonged ischaemia.
  • Ask about skin discolouration (e.g., red, purple, black) - indicates tissue necrosis and can differentiate types of gangrene.
  • Ask about foul-smelling discharge or pus - a key feature of wet gangrene, indicating bacterial infection.
  • Ask about fever, chills, or general malaise - these are systemic signs of infection, particularly concerning in wet and gas gangrene.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Gangrene (Heart and vasculature)"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - tachycardia, tachypnoea, fever, or hypotension may indicate systemic inflammatory response or sepsis.
  • Check for skin changes - dry gangrene presents as dry, shrunken, black tissue; wet gangrene as swollen, moist, discoloured, often blistered tissue with a foul odour.
  • Check for crepitus on palpation - this is a critical red flag for gas gangrene, indicating gas in the tissues produced by anaerobic bacteria. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Crepitus in gas gangrene: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Crepitus"] [/sc]
  • Check peripheral pulses - absent or diminished pulses distal to the affected area suggest underlying peripheral arterial disease.
  • Check for tenderness and swelling - localised tenderness and swelling are common, but rapidly spreading oedema is concerning for wet or gas gangrene.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Gangrene (Heart and vasculature)"][/sc]
  • Rapidly spreading discolouration or swelling - suggests aggressive infection (wet or gas gangrene) requiring immediate intervention.
  • Crepitus on palpation - highly indicative of gas gangrene, a surgical emergency due to rapid tissue destruction and systemic toxicity.
  • Systemic signs of sepsis (e.g., fever, hypotension, altered mental status) - indicates severe infection with systemic involvement, requiring urgent resuscitation and broad-spectrum antibiotics.
  • Absent peripheral pulses with new onset pain - suggests acute limb ischaemia, which can rapidly progress to gangrene if not revascularised within hours.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Gangrene (Heart and vasculature)"][/sc]
  • Diabetic patients: May present with neuropathic pain or painless ulcers, delaying recognition of ischaemia or infection. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Diabetic foot ulcers and gangrene: how do they relate and why does it matter clinically?" text="Diabetic foot ulcers"] [/sc]
  • Immunosuppressed patients: May have blunted inflammatory responses, leading to less pronounced systemic signs despite severe infection.
  • Older adults: Often have multiple comorbidities and may present with non-specific symptoms, making diagnosis challenging.
  • Internal gangrene: Affects internal organs (e.g., bowel) and presents with severe abdominal pain, fever, and signs of peritonitis, without external skin changes.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Gangrene (Heart and vasculature)"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected gangrene: Full blood count (leukocytosis), C-reactive protein (elevated), blood cultures (identify causative organisms in wet/gas gangrene), arterial blood gas (assess acidosis/lactate).
  • Severity/risk tools: The ABPI (Ankle-Brachial Pressure Index) can assess peripheral arterial disease severity. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Ankle-Brachial Pressure Index (ABPI): what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="ABPI"] [/sc]
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Consider imaging to rule out deep vein thrombosis or cellulitis without underlying ischaemia.
  • Imaging - X-ray: May show gas in soft tissues, highly suggestive of gas gangrene.
  • Imaging - CT angiography or Doppler ultrasound: Essential to assess vascular supply and identify occlusions in ischaemic gangrene.
  • Serial reassessment: Regular clinical examination and monitoring of inflammatory markers are crucial to assess response to treatment and detect progression.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Gangrene (Heart and vasculature)"][/sc]
  • Severe cellulitis: Typically presents with erythema, warmth, and swelling, but without the profound tissue necrosis or crepitus seen in gangrene.
  • Necrotising fasciitis: Characterised by rapidly spreading infection of the fascia, often with severe pain out of proportion to physical findings, but may not have the same degree of tissue blackening as gangrene. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Necrotising fasciitis vs gangrene: what are the key differences and why do they matter clinically?" text="Necrotising fasciitis"] [/sc]
  • Acute limb ischaemia: Presents with the 5 Ps, but tissue necrosis may not yet be established, representing a pre-gangrenous state.
  • Thrombophlebitis: Inflammation of a vein with clot formation, causing localised pain and redness, but generally not leading to widespread tissue necrosis.
  • Vasculitis: Systemic inflammation of blood vessels that can cause ischaemia and ulceration, but usually has other systemic manifestations.
  • Pressure ulcers: Develop over bony prominences due to prolonged pressure, but typically lack the acute onset and rapid progression of gangrene.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Gangrene (Heart and vasculature)"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Urgent recognition and escalation to surgical and vascular teams. Supportive care often includes pain management, fluid resuscitation, and broad-spectrum antibiotics for suspected infection.
  • Definitive management: Surgical debridement of necrotic tissue is typically required. Revascularisation procedures (e.g., bypass surgery, angioplasty) may be considered for ischaemic gangrene to restore blood flow. Amputation may be necessary for extensive, irreversible tissue loss.
  • Adjunctive management: Hyperbaric oxygen therapy may support wound healing in some cases, particularly for gas gangrene. Optimisation of underlying conditions like diabetes and peripheral arterial disease is crucial.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Close monitoring for signs of infection, sepsis, and further tissue breakdown. Regular wound care and multidisciplinary team input are essential.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Patients should be advised to seek immediate medical attention for worsening pain, spreading redness, fever, or new numbness.
  • Exam pharmacology reference (UKMLA/MRCGP): Antibiotic choices often include broad-spectrum agents covering Gram-positive, Gram-negative, and anaerobic bacteria. For gas gangrene, high-dose penicillin is typically used in combination with other agents. Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Gangrene (Heart and vasculature)"][/sc]
  • Sepsis: Particularly in wet and gas gangrene, bacterial toxins can lead to a systemic inflammatory response, progressing to septic shock and multi-organ failure within hours to days.
  • Amputation: Extensive or rapidly progressing tissue necrosis often necessitates surgical removal of the affected limb or digit, leading to significant disability.
  • Kidney injury: Systemic infection and hypotension can lead to acute kidney injury, further complicating patient management and prognosis.
  • Death: Untreated or rapidly progressive gangrene, especially wet and gas forms, carries a high mortality rate due to overwhelming sepsis and multi-organ failure.
  • Chronic pain and disability: Even after successful treatment, patients may experience persistent pain, impaired mobility, and reduced quality of life.
