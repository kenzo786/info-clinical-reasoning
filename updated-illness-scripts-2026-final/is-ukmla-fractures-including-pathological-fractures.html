Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Fracture(s) (including pathological fractures)"][/sc]
  • A fracture is a break in the continuity of bone, ranging from a hairline crack to a complete break, often resulting from trauma or underlying pathology.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical presentation, common causes, and initial management principles of fractures, including those secondary to pathological processes.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Fracture(s) (including pathological fractures)"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Fracture(s) (including pathological fractures)"][/sc]
  • Fractures are a common presentation in emergency departments across the UK, with millions occurring annually, often peaking in older adults due to falls and osteoporosis.
  • Risk factors include advanced age, osteoporosis, low bone mineral density, certain medications (e.g., long-term corticosteroids), nutritional deficiencies, and high-impact sports or occupations.
  • Pathological fractures, occurring through diseased bone, are less common but significantly impact patients with metastatic cancer, primary bone tumours, or severe metabolic bone disease.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Fracture(s) (including pathological fractures)"][/sc]
  • Excessive mechanical stress, either acute traumatic force or repetitive sub-threshold loading, exceeds the bone's structural integrity, leading to micro-damage or macroscopic discontinuity.
  • This initial injury triggers an inflammatory response, followed by haematoma formation and cellular proliferation, initiating the complex process of bone healing involving osteoblasts and osteoclasts.
  • Failure of adequate bone repair, malunion, or non-union can result if biological or mechanical factors are compromised, leading to persistent pain and functional impairment. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Bone healing: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Bone healing"][/sc]
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Fracture(s) (including pathological fractures)"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Immediate pain, swelling, and loss of function, typically within hours of injury, with potential for neurovascular compromise.
  • Progressive phase: Symptoms persist and may worsen with movement, particularly beyond the first few days, as inflammation and muscle spasm contribute to discomfort.
  • Late phase: Gradual reduction in pain and swelling over weeks to months as healing progresses, though residual stiffness or weakness may persist, particularly beyond 6-12 weeks.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Fracture(s) (including pathological fractures)"][/sc]
  • Ask about sudden, severe pain - this is the cardinal symptom and often localises to the site of injury.
  • Ask about inability to bear weight or move the affected limb - indicates significant structural compromise.
  • Ask about deformity or abnormal angulation - suggests displacement of bone fragments.
  • Ask about swelling and bruising - these develop due to local tissue damage and haematoma formation.
  • Ask about any associated numbness or tingling - crucial for assessing potential nerve involvement.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Fracture(s) (including pathological fractures)"][/sc]
  • Check for localised tenderness and swelling - direct indicators of injury at the fracture site.
  • Check for deformity, shortening, or abnormal angulation of the limb - visible signs of displaced fractures.
  • Check for crepitus on gentle palpation or movement - a grating sensation indicating bone fragments rubbing together.
  • Check distal neurovascular status (pulses, capillary refill, sensation, motor function) - essential to rule out compartment syndrome or nerve damage. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Compartment syndrome: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Compartment syndrome"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs (heart rate, blood pressure, respiratory rate, temperature) - to assess for systemic compromise, especially in polytrauma or open fractures.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Fracture(s) (including pathological fractures)"][/sc]
  • Open fracture: Skin breach over the fracture site, indicating high risk of infection and requiring urgent surgical debridement.
  • Neurovascular compromise: Absent pulses, pallor, paraesthesia, or paralysis distal to the injury, necessitating immediate reduction or surgical intervention.
  • Compartment syndrome: Disproportionate pain, pain on passive stretch, tense swelling, and paraesthesia, requiring urgent fasciotomy.
  • Spinal fracture with neurological deficit: Weakness, numbness, or bowel/bladder dysfunction, indicating potential cord compression and requiring urgent neurosurgical review.
  • Pathological fracture in known malignancy: Suggests metastatic disease, requiring oncological assessment and often prophylactic stabilisation.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Fracture(s) (including pathological fractures)"][/sc]
  • Older adults: May present with minimal trauma (e.g., fall from standing height) due to osteoporosis, and pain may be less severe or poorly localised.
  • Children: Greenstick or buckle fractures are common, and pain may be attributed to a "sprain," delaying diagnosis. Non-accidental injury must always be considered.
  • Pathological fractures: Often occur with minimal or no trauma, in a patient with known malignancy or metabolic bone disease, and may be the presenting symptom of underlying systemic illness.
  • Stress fractures: Gradual onset of pain related to activity, often in athletes or military recruits, with no acute traumatic event. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Stress fracture: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Stress fracture"][/sc]
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Fracture(s) (including pathological fractures)"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected fracture: Plain film radiography (X-ray) of the affected area, including at least two orthogonal views, to identify fracture lines, displacement, and alignment.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Blood tests such as full blood count, inflammatory markers (CRP, ESR), and bone profile (calcium, phosphate, alkaline phosphatase) may be considered, especially for pathological fractures.
  • Imaging - CT scan: Useful for complex fractures, intra-articular involvement, spinal fractures, or when plain X-rays are inconclusive, providing detailed bony anatomy.
  • Imaging - MRI scan: Indicated for suspected soft tissue injury (ligaments, menisci), occult fractures (e.g., scaphoid), stress fractures, or to evaluate bone marrow oedema and pathological lesions.
  • Imaging - Bone scan (scintigraphy): May be used to detect occult fractures, stress fractures, or metastatic bone disease, particularly when other imaging is negative.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Fracture(s) (including pathological fractures)"][/sc]
  • A 72-year-old female presents to the emergency department after a fall from standing height, complaining of severe right hip pain and inability to bear weight, with a visibly shortened and externally rotated leg.
  • An elderly female with a history of osteoporosis presenting with acute, severe pain and functional impairment in a limb following low-energy trauma, suggestive of a displaced fracture.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Fracture(s) (including pathological fractures)"][/sc]
  • Sprain/Strain - less severe pain, no bony deformity, often able to bear some weight.
  • Dislocation - joint deformity, severe pain, but bone continuity is maintained.
  • Contusion - localised pain and bruising, but no bony injury on imaging.
  • Deep vein thrombosis (DVT) - swelling and pain, but typically no acute trauma and often associated with warmth and erythema.
  • Cellulitis - erythema, warmth, swelling, and pain, usually with fever and no history of trauma.
  • Osteomyelitis - localised pain, swelling, and warmth, often with systemic signs of infection, without acute trauma.
  • Tumour (primary or metastatic) - insidious onset of pain, often worse at night, may lead to pathological fracture. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Bone tumour: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Bone tumour"][/sc]
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Fracture(s) (including pathological fractures)"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Analgesia (e.g., paracetamol, ibuprofen, opioid analgesics), immobilisation of the affected limb (splinting), neurovascular assessment and protection, and wound care for open fractures.
  • Definitive management: May involve closed reduction and casting/bracing for stable, undisplaced fractures, or open reduction and internal fixation (ORIF) with plates, screws, or rods for displaced or unstable fractures. External fixation may be used for complex open fractures.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Regular clinical and radiological assessment to ensure satisfactory healing, identify complications, and guide rehabilitation.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Advise patients to seek immediate medical attention for worsening pain, new numbness/tingling, increasing swelling, or signs of infection.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Fracture(s) (including pathological fractures)"][/sc]
  • Neurovascular injury: Damage to nerves or blood vessels adjacent to the fracture site, leading to sensory, motor, or circulatory deficits, which can result in limb ischaemia or permanent disability.
  • Compartment syndrome: Increased pressure within a closed fascial compartment, compromising blood flow to muscles and nerves, leading to irreversible tissue damage if not promptly treated.
  • Infection (osteomyelitis): Particularly in open fractures, bacterial contamination can lead to chronic bone infection, requiring prolonged antibiotic therapy and surgical debridement.
  • Malunion/Non-union: Healing in an unsatisfactory position (malunion) or failure of the bone to heal completely (non-union), leading to persistent pain, deformity, and functional impairment.
  • Deep vein thrombosis (DVT) and Pulmonary Embolism (PE): Immobility and trauma increase the risk of venous thromboembolism, a potentially life-threatening complication.
  • Fat embolism syndrome: Rare but serious complication, especially in long bone fractures, where fat globules enter the bloodstream, causing respiratory distress, neurological symptoms, and petechial rash.
