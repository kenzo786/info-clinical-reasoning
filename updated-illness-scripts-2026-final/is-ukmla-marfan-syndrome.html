Marfan syndrome [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Marfan syndrome"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Marfan syndrome"][/sc]
  • Marfan syndrome is a multisystem connective tissue disorder, primarily affecting the skeletal, ocular, and cardiovascular systems, caused by a mutation in the *FBN1* gene.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical features and potential complications of Marfan syndrome, particularly its cardiovascular manifestations.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Marfan syndrome"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Marfan syndrome"][/sc]
  • Affects approximately 1 in 3,000 to 5,000 individuals in the UK, with no significant gender or ethnic predilection.
  • Around 25% of cases are sporadic, resulting from a de novo mutation, while the remainder are inherited in an autosomal dominant pattern.
  • Risk factors primarily involve a family history of Marfan syndrome, indicating genetic predisposition.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Marfan syndrome"][/sc]
  • Trigger: A mutation in the *FBN1* gene on chromosome 15 leads to defective fibrillin-1 production, a key component of extracellular microfibrils.
  • Progression: Impaired microfibril formation weakens connective tissues throughout the body, particularly in the aorta, ligaments, and ocular lens.
  • Consequence: This structural weakness results in characteristic features such as aortic root dilatation, ectopia lentis, and skeletal abnormalities, predisposing to significant cardiovascular complications.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Marfan syndrome"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Manifestations may be subtle in infancy and early childhood, often involving skeletal features like dolichostenomelia or arachnodactyly, and sometimes early ocular involvement.
  • Progressive phase: Cardiovascular complications, such as aortic root dilatation and mitral valve prolapse, typically emerge and progress during adolescence and early adulthood, particularly beyond 10-15 years of age.
  • Late phase: Risk of aortic dissection or rupture significantly increases in later adulthood, particularly beyond 40-50 years, if not appropriately monitored and managed.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Marfan syndrome"][/sc]
  • Ask about chest pain or shortness of breath - may indicate aortic dissection or heart failure.
  • Ask about visual disturbances or sudden loss of vision - suggests ectopia lentis or retinal detachment.
  • Ask about joint pain or hypermobility - reflects widespread connective tissue laxity.
  • Ask about back pain or scoliosis - common skeletal manifestations due to vertebral abnormalities.
  • Ask about family history of similar features or sudden unexplained death - crucial for genetic risk assessment.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Marfan syndrome"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - particularly blood pressure for signs of cardiovascular compromise or hypertension.
  • Check skeletal features - arachnodactyly (long, slender fingers), pectus excavatum/carinatum, scoliosis, and increased arm span to height ratio.
  • Check ocular examination - ectopia lentis (displaced lens), myopia, or retinal detachment.
  • Check cardiovascular examination - aortic regurgitation murmur, mitral valve prolapse click, or signs of heart failure.
  • Check skin for striae atrophicae - often found on the shoulders, hips, and lower back.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Marfan syndrome"][/sc]
  • Sudden severe chest or back pain - specific implication: highly suggestive of acute aortic dissection, requiring immediate emergency assessment.
  • Acute visual loss - specific implication: may indicate retinal detachment or acute lens dislocation, necessitating urgent ophthalmological review.
  • New or worsening heart murmur - specific implication: could signify progressive aortic root dilatation or severe valvular dysfunction, requiring prompt cardiology evaluation.
  • Rapidly progressing scoliosis in childhood - specific implication: may lead to significant respiratory compromise if unaddressed.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Marfan syndrome"][/sc]
  • Neonatal Marfan syndrome: Presents with severe, rapidly progressive cardiovascular disease (e.g., severe mitral and tricuspid regurgitation, aortic dilatation) and often significant skeletal features, leading to a poorer prognosis.
  • Marfanoid habitus without full diagnostic criteria: Individuals may exhibit some features of Marfan syndrome but not meet the revised Ghent criteria, making diagnosis challenging and requiring careful monitoring.
  • Older adults: Cardiovascular complications, particularly aortic dissection, may present with less typical pain patterns or be masked by other age-related comorbidities, delaying diagnosis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Marfan syndrome"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected Marfan syndrome:
    • Echocardiogram: To assess aortic root diameter, mitral valve function, and detect aortic dissection.
    • Slit-lamp examination: To detect ectopia lentis and other ocular abnormalities.
    • Skeletal survey: To identify characteristic skeletal features and assess for scoliosis.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible:
    • Genetic testing for *FBN1* mutation: Confirms diagnosis and aids in family screening.
    • Genetic testing for other connective tissue disorders (e.g., Loeys-Dietz syndrome, Vascular Ehlers-Danlos syndrome): To differentiate from phenotypically similar conditions.
  • Imaging - MRI/CT angiography: For detailed assessment of the entire aorta, particularly in cases of suspected dissection or for surgical planning.
  • Serial reassessment: Regular echocardiograms and clinical reviews are essential for monitoring disease progression and detecting complications early.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Marfan syndrome"][/sc]
  • A 25-year-old male presents with sudden onset severe tearing chest pain radiating to his back, associated with shortness of breath, a history of tall stature, long limbs, and recent visual changes.
  • A young adult with a systemic connective tissue disorder, presenting with acute cardiovascular compromise and chronic skeletal and ocular manifestations, suggesting a genetic aetiology.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Marfan syndrome"][/sc]
  • Loeys-Dietz syndrome - distinguishing feature: more aggressive arterial aneurysms and dissections, often with bifid uvula or hypertelorism.
  • Vascular Ehlers-Danlos syndrome - distinguishing feature: extremely fragile skin, easy bruising, and propensity for arterial and organ rupture.
  • Homocystinuria - distinguishing feature: intellectual disability, thrombotic events, and downward lens dislocation (Marfan syndrome typically has upward lens dislocation).
  • Familial thoracic aortic aneurysm and dissection (FTAAD) - distinguishing feature: primarily affects the aorta without the widespread skeletal or ocular features.
  • Massive congenital scoliosis - distinguishing feature: isolated severe spinal deformity without other systemic features of connective tissue disorder.
  • Constitutional tall stature - distinguishing feature: tall height without other pathological features or systemic involvement.
