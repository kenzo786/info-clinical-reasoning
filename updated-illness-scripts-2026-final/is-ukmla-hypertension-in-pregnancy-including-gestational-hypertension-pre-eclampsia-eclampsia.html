Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Hypertension in pregnancy (including gestational hypertension, pre-eclampsia, eclampsia)"][/sc]
  • A condition characterised by new-onset hypertension during pregnancy, often accompanied by proteinuria or other signs of organ dysfunction, encompassing gestational hypertension, pre-eclampsia, and eclampsia.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the different classifications of hypertensive disorders in pregnancy, their potential complications, and the urgency required for management to prevent maternal and fetal morbidity and mortality.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Hypertension in pregnancy (including gestational hypertension, pre-eclampsia, eclampsia)"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Hypertension in pregnancy (including gestational hypertension, pre-eclampsia, eclampsia)"][/sc]
  • Hypertensive disorders complicate approximately 5-10% of pregnancies in the UK, with pre-eclampsia affecting 2-8% of pregnancies.
  • Risk factors include nulliparity, multiple pregnancy, pre-existing hypertension, diabetes, renal disease, autoimmune conditions, and a family history of pre-eclampsia.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Hypertension in pregnancy (including gestational hypertension, pre-eclampsia, eclampsia)"][/sc]
  • Trigger: Abnormal placentation leads to shallow trophoblast invasion and reduced uteroplacental perfusion, resulting in placental ischaemia.
  • Progression: The ischaemic placenta releases anti-angiogenic factors into the maternal circulation, causing widespread maternal endothelial dysfunction.
  • Consequence: Systemic vasoconstriction, increased vascular permeability, and activation of the coagulation cascade, leading to hypertension and multi-organ damage.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Hypertension in pregnancy (including gestational hypertension, pre-eclampsia, eclampsia)"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Gestational hypertension typically develops after 20 weeks' gestation, often presenting as asymptomatic elevated blood pressure.
  • Progressive phase: Pre-eclampsia can develop from gestational hypertension, with symptoms and signs of organ dysfunction emerging, particularly beyond 34 weeks' gestation, but can occur earlier.
  • Late phase: Eclampsia, the most severe manifestation, involves seizures and can occur antepartum, intrapartum, or postpartum, often within the first few days after delivery.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Hypertension in pregnancy (including gestational hypertension, pre-eclampsia, eclampsia)"][/sc]
  • Ask about headache - often frontal or occipital, persistent, and not relieved by simple analgesia, indicating cerebral oedema or vasospasm.
  • Ask about visual disturbances - such as blurred vision, flashing lights, or scotomata, suggesting retinal arteriolar spasm or oedema.
  • Ask about epigastric pain or right upper quadrant pain - often severe, indicating hepatic oedema or subcapsular haemorrhage.
  • Ask about sudden onset oedema - particularly of the face and hands, which can be a sign of increased vascular permeability, though not diagnostic on its own.
  • Ask about nausea and vomiting - which can be non-specific but may be more pronounced with severe pre-eclampsia or HELLP syndrome.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Hypertension in pregnancy (including gestational hypertension, pre-eclampsia, eclampsia)"][/sc]
  • Check blood pressure - sustained systolic BP ≥140 mmHg or diastolic BP ≥90 mmHg on two occasions at least 4 hours apart, or a single reading of systolic BP ≥160 mmHg or diastolic BP ≥110 mmHg.
  • Check proteinuria - new-onset proteinuria (e.g., urine protein:creatinine ratio ≥30 mg/mmol or 24-hour urine protein ≥0.3 g).
  • Check reflexes - hyperreflexia, particularly brisk deep tendon reflexes, can indicate central nervous system irritability.
  • Check for pulmonary oedema - crackles on lung auscultation, indicating fluid overload due to increased vascular permeability.
  • Check for fetal growth restriction - fundal height measurement or ultrasound may reveal a smaller than expected fetus, reflecting uteroplacental insufficiency.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Hypertension in pregnancy (including gestational hypertension, pre-eclampsia, eclampsia)"][/sc]
  • Severe headache - persistent, unremitting headache, unresponsive to analgesia, suggests impending eclampsia or cerebral oedema.
  • Visual disturbances - new or worsening visual symptoms like blurred vision or scotomata, indicating severe end-organ involvement.
  • Epigastric or right upper quadrant pain - severe pain in these areas may signify hepatic involvement or subcapsular haemorrhage.
  • Oliguria - urine output less than 0.5 mL/kg/hour for several hours, indicating acute kidney injury.
  • Convulsions (eclampsia) - generalised tonic-clonic seizures, a life-threatening emergency requiring immediate intervention.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Hypertension in pregnancy (including gestational hypertension, pre-eclampsia, eclampsia)"][/sc]
  • Postpartum pre-eclampsia: Can develop up to six weeks after delivery, often presenting with headache, visual changes, or epigastric pain, and is important to recognise as it can lead to eclampsia.
  • Pre-eclampsia without proteinuria: In some cases, particularly with severe features, proteinuria may be absent, making diagnosis challenging and requiring a high index of suspicion based on other signs of organ dysfunction.
  • HELLP syndrome: A severe variant of pre-eclampsia characterised by Haemolysis, Elevated Liver enzymes, and Low Platelets, which can present with non-specific symptoms like malaise and epigastric pain, often without significant hypertension.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Hypertension in pregnancy (including gestational hypertension, pre-eclampsia, eclampsia)"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected hypertension in pregnancy: Full blood count (for haemolysis, platelets), liver function tests (for hepatic involvement), renal function tests (urea, creatinine, electrolytes), and urine protein:creatinine ratio or 24-hour urine protein.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Consider tests for other causes of hypertension or symptoms, such as thyroid function tests or autoimmune screens, if clinically indicated.
  • Imaging - Ultrasound: Fetal growth and wellbeing assessment (Doppler studies if indicated) to monitor for fetal growth restriction or compromise.
  • Serial reassessment: Regular monitoring of blood pressure, urine output, and blood tests (FBC, LFTs, U&Es) to track disease progression and response to treatment.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Hypertension in pregnancy (including gestational hypertension, pre-eclampsia, eclampsia)"][/sc]
  • Chronic hypertension - pre-existing hypertension before pregnancy or diagnosed before 20 weeks' gestation.
  • Transient hypertension of pregnancy - gestational hypertension that resolves postpartum without proteinuria or other features of pre-eclampsia.
  • Secondary hypertension - hypertension due to an underlying medical condition (e.g., renal artery stenosis, phaeochromocytoma).
  • Thrombotic thrombocytopenic purpura (TTP) / Haemolytic uraemic syndrome (HUS) - can mimic HELLP syndrome but typically without significant hypertension.
  • Acute fatty liver of pregnancy - presents with liver dysfunction, jaundice, and coagulopathy, often without significant hypertension or proteinuria.
  • Migraine - headache without other features of pre-eclampsia or hypertension.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Hypertension in pregnancy (including gestational hypertension, pre-eclampsia, eclampsia)"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Stabilise maternal condition, prevent complications (e.g., eclampsia), and assess fetal wellbeing. This often includes hospital admission, regular monitoring of maternal vital signs, urine output, and blood tests.
  • Definitive management: Delivery of the fetus and placenta is the only definitive cure for pre-eclampsia. Timing of delivery depends on gestational age, severity of disease, and maternal/fetal condition.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Close monitoring postpartum for resolution of hypertension and proteinuria. Long-term follow-up for cardiovascular risk assessment is important.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Educate the patient on red flag symptoms and signs, and provide clear instructions on when to seek urgent medical attention.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Hypertension in pregnancy (including gestational hypertension, pre-eclampsia, eclampsia)"][/sc]
  • Eclampsia: Generalised tonic-clonic seizures, a direct consequence of severe cerebral oedema and vasospasm, leading to significant maternal and fetal morbidity and mortality.
  • HELLP syndrome: A severe form of pre-eclampsia involving haemolysis, elevated liver enzymes, and low platelets, indicating widespread endothelial damage and microangiopathy.
  • Placental abruption: Premature separation of the placenta from the uterine wall, often due to chronic uteroplacental ischaemia and vascular damage, leading to fetal distress and maternal haemorrhage.
  • Acute kidney injury: Renal dysfunction due to endothelial damage in the renal vasculature, leading to reduced glomerular filtration and oliguria.
  • Pulmonary oedema: Increased vascular permeability and fluid overload can lead to fluid accumulation in the lungs, causing respiratory distress.
  • Fetal growth restriction and preterm birth: Chronic uteroplacental insufficiency compromises fetal nutrient and oxygen supply, often necessitating early delivery.
