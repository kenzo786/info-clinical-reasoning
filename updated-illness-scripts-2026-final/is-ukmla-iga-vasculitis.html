IgA vasculitis [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="IgA vasculitis"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="IgA vasculitis"][/sc]
  • IgA vasculitis (formerly Henoch-Schönlein purpura) is a systemic small-vessel vasculitis characterised by IgA immune complex deposition, primarily affecting the skin, joints, gastrointestinal tract, and kidneys.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical presentation of IgA vasculitis, understand its multi-system involvement, and identify key diagnostic features and potential complications.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="IgA vasculitis"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="IgA vasculitis"][/sc]
  • IgA vasculitis is the most common vasculitis of childhood, with an incidence of approximately 20 cases per 100,000 children in the UK.
  • It typically affects children aged 3-15 years, with a peak incidence around 6 years of age.
  • Often preceded by an upper respiratory tract infection (e.g., streptococcal pharyngitis) or other infections, suggesting an immune-mediated trigger.
  • Risk factors include recent infection, male sex, and certain genetic predispositions.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="IgA vasculitis"][/sc]
  • Trigger: Often follows an infection (e.g., upper respiratory tract infection), leading to the production of abnormally glycosylated IgA1.
  • Progression: These abnormal IgA1 molecules form immune complexes that deposit in the walls of small blood vessels, activating complement and inflammatory pathways. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Immune complex deposition: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Immune complex deposition"][/sc]
  • Consequence: This results in leukocytoclastic vasculitis, causing inflammation and damage to affected organs, particularly the skin, joints, gastrointestinal tract, and kidneys.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="IgA vasculitis"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Acute onset of symptoms, typically developing over days to a few weeks, often following an upper respiratory tract infection. Initial presentation commonly includes palpable purpura, arthralgia, and abdominal pain.
  • Progressive phase: Symptoms may evolve over weeks, with potential for gastrointestinal involvement (e.g., bleeding, intussusception) and renal manifestations (e.g., haematuria, proteinuria). Recurrences are common, particularly within the first year.
  • Late phase: Most cases resolve spontaneously within a month, particularly in children. However, persistent or recurrent renal involvement can lead to chronic kidney disease, particularly beyond several months, requiring long-term monitoring.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="IgA vasculitis"][/sc]
  • Ask about rash - typically a palpable purpura, non-blanching, often on the lower limbs and buttocks, which is a key diagnostic feature.
  • Ask about joint pain - commonly affecting large joints (knees, ankles), often migratory and can be associated with swelling, indicating systemic inflammation.
  • Ask about abdominal pain - colicky, periumbilical pain due to gastrointestinal vasculitis, which can be severe and may precede the rash, mimicking an acute abdomen.
  • Ask about nausea, vomiting, or melaena - suggestive of gastrointestinal bleeding or intussusception, indicating more severe GI involvement.
  • Ask about recent infection - particularly an upper respiratory tract infection, as this often precedes the onset of IgA vasculitis.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="IgA vasculitis"][/sc]
  • Check for palpable purpura - non-blanching, raised red-purple lesions, typically on extensor surfaces of lower limbs and buttocks, confirming cutaneous vasculitis.
  • Check joints for swelling and tenderness - particularly knees and ankles, indicating inflammatory arthropathy.
  • Check abdomen for tenderness or guarding - suggesting gastrointestinal inflammation or potential complications like intussusception.
  • Check for oedema - especially of the hands, feet, or periorbital region, which may indicate systemic inflammation or early renal dysfunction.
  • Check vital signs - blood pressure for hypertension (renal involvement), temperature for fever (inflammation/infection).
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="IgA vasculitis"][/sc]
  • Severe abdominal pain: May indicate gastrointestinal haemorrhage, intussusception, or bowel ischaemia, requiring urgent surgical assessment.
  • Significant renal involvement: Rapidly progressive glomerulonephritis, severe proteinuria, or acute kidney injury warrant prompt nephrology review and potential immunosuppression.
  • Persistent purpura with systemic symptoms: Ongoing rash accompanied by fever, malaise, or significant joint pain may suggest a more severe or relapsing course.
  • Neurological symptoms: Seizures, altered mental status, or focal neurological deficits are rare but serious complications requiring immediate investigation.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="IgA vasculitis"][/sc]
  • Adult-onset IgA vasculitis: Tends to have a more severe course with a higher incidence of significant renal impairment and chronic kidney disease compared to childhood cases. Gastrointestinal involvement can also be more pronounced.
  • IgA vasculitis in pregnancy: Generally rare, and while IgAV rarely flares during pregnancy, there can be an increased risk of complications such as pre-eclampsia or premature delivery, particularly if there is pre-existing renal involvement.
  • Isolated renal involvement: Rarely, patients may present with kidney disease (haematuria, proteinuria) without the characteristic skin rash, making diagnosis challenging. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Isolated renal vasculitis: how is it diagnosed?" text="Isolated renal involvement"][/sc]
  • Severe gastrointestinal presentation: In some cases, abdominal pain and gastrointestinal bleeding can be the predominant or sole initial symptom, potentially masking the underlying vasculitis and mimicking other acute abdominal conditions.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="IgA vasculitis"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected IgA vasculitis: Clinical diagnosis based on characteristic palpable purpura, arthralgia, abdominal pain, and/or renal involvement. Urinalysis for haematuria and proteinuria. Full blood count, urea and electrolytes, C-reactive protein (CRP).
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Consider blood tests such as antinuclear antibodies (ANA), anti-neutrophil cytoplasmic antibodies (ANCA), and complement levels (C3, C4) to exclude other autoimmune conditions or vasculitides.
  • Skin biopsy: Often performed to confirm the diagnosis, showing leukocytoclastic vasculitis with IgA deposition in dermal post-capillary venules.
  • Renal biopsy: Indicated in cases of significant or progressive renal involvement (e.g., persistent proteinuria, impaired renal function) to assess severity and guide management. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Indications for renal biopsy in IgA vasculitis: what are they?" text="Renal biopsy indications"][/sc]
  • Imaging - Abdominal ultrasound: May be performed in cases of severe abdominal pain to assess for complications such as intussusception or bowel ischaemia.
  • Serial reassessment: Regular monitoring of blood pressure, urinalysis, and renal function (urea, creatinine) for at least 6 months post-diagnosis to detect delayed renal complications.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="IgA vasculitis"][/sc]
  • A 6-year-old boy presents with a 3-day history of a new non-blanching rash on his legs and buttocks, associated with painful, swollen knees and colicky abdominal pain, following a recent upper respiratory tract infection.
  • An acute, immune-mediated, small-vessel vasculitis presenting with a classic tetrad of palpable purpura, arthralgia, abdominal pain, and renal involvement, often self-limiting but with a risk of significant renal sequelae.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="IgA vasculitis"][/sc]
  • Immune Thrombocytopenic Purpura (ITP) - absence of palpable purpura and presence of thrombocytopenia.
  • Other systemic vasculitides (e.g., ANCA-associated vasculitis) - different serological markers (ANCA positive), different organ predilection, and absence of IgA deposition.
  • Acute Glomerulonephritis (primary IgA nephropathy) - isolated renal involvement without the characteristic skin, joint, or gastrointestinal manifestations.
  • Meningococcemia - rapid onset of sepsis, often with petechial or ecchymotic rash, fever, and signs of severe systemic illness.
  • Child abuse - bruising patterns may mimic purpura, but typically lack systemic symptoms and the specific characteristics of vasculitic rash.
  • Drug-induced purpura - history of recent medication exposure, often resolves upon withdrawal of the offending agent.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="IgA vasculitis"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Supportive care including adequate hydration, pain management (e.g., paracetamol), and rest. Avoid NSAIDs if there is risk of renal impairment or dehydration.
  • Definitive management: Most cases are self-limiting and require only supportive care. Corticosteroids (e.g., [sc name="jitl-medication" Q="Prednisolone use in IgA vasculitis" text="prednisolone"][/sc]) may be considered for severe abdominal pain, significant joint involvement, or progressive renal disease. Immunosuppressants may be used in severe, refractory renal involvement.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Regular monitoring of blood pressure, urinalysis for haematuria and proteinuria, and renal function for at least 6 months to detect and manage renal complications.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Advise parents/patients on red flag symptoms (e.g., severe abdominal pain, reduced urine output, new neurological symptoms) that warrant immediate medical review.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="IgA vasculitis"][/sc]
  • Renal impairment/Chronic Kidney Disease: Due to persistent IgA deposition and inflammation in the glomeruli, leading to proteinuria, haematuria, and potentially progressive loss of renal function. This is the most serious long-term complication, particularly in adults.
  • Intussusception: Invagination of one part of the intestine into another, often triggered by oedema and haemorrhage in the bowel wall due to vasculitis, presenting as severe abdominal pain and requiring urgent surgical intervention.
  • Gastrointestinal haemorrhage: Bleeding from inflamed and damaged blood vessels in the gastrointestinal tract, ranging from melaena to frank haematemesis, indicating significant GI involvement.
  • Orchitis/Epididymitis: Inflammation of the testes or epididymis, a less common but recognised complication, causing scrotal pain and swelling.
  • Recurrence: Symptoms may recur in approximately one-third of patients, typically within the first year, but usually with a milder course than the initial presentation.
