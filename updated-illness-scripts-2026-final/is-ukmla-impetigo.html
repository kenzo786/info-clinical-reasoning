Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Impetigo"][/sc]
  • Concise clinical definition of Impetigo.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise and manage Impetigo as a common bacterial skin infection.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Impetigo"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Impetigo"][/sc]
  • Impetigo is a highly contagious bacterial skin infection, particularly common in children, often peaking during warmer months.
  • Risk factors include close contact, poor hygiene, pre-existing skin conditions (e.g., eczema), and minor skin trauma.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Impetigo"][/sc]
  • Trigger: Skin barrier disruption (e.g., insect bite, scratch) allows entry of bacteria, typically Staphylococcus aureus or Streptococcus pyogenes.
  • Progression: Bacteria multiply and produce toxins, leading to superficial epidermal infection and characteristic lesions.
  • Consequence: Formation of vesicles, bullae, and pustules that rupture to form golden-brown crusts; highly contagious through direct contact.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Impetigo"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Rapid onset of lesions, often within 1-3 days of inoculation, with initial vesicles or pustules.
  • Progressive phase: Lesions quickly rupture, forming characteristic golden-brown crusts and spreading locally, particularly beyond 3-5 days if untreated.
  • Late phase: Resolution typically occurs within 7-10 days with appropriate treatment, though untreated cases may persist for weeks.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Impetigo"][/sc]
  • Ask about skin lesions - to identify the characteristic appearance (vesicles, bullae, pustules, golden crusts).
  • Ask about itchiness or discomfort - common complaints that may lead to scratching and further spread.
  • Ask about recent skin trauma or insect bites - potential entry points for bacterial infection.
  • Ask about contact with others with similar lesions - indicates contagious nature and potential source.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Impetigo"][/sc]
  • Check for characteristic lesions - vesicles, bullae, pustules, or honey-coloured crusts, typically on the face (around nose and mouth) and extremities.
  • Check for regional lymphadenopathy - indicates local inflammatory response to infection.
  • Check for signs of cellulitis - spreading erythema, warmth, tenderness, which would suggest deeper infection.
  • Check vital signs - to rule out systemic involvement or sepsis, though usually normal in uncomplicated impetigo.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Impetigo"][/sc]
  • Rapidly spreading lesions: Suggests aggressive infection or potential for deeper involvement.
  • Systemic symptoms (fever, malaise): May indicate cellulitis, sepsis, or other severe complications.
  • Failure to respond to initial treatment: Raises concern for resistant organisms or incorrect diagnosis.
  • Periorbital involvement: Risk of orbital cellulitis, requiring urgent assessment.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Impetigo"][/sc]
  • Bullous impetigo: Characterised by larger, fluid-filled blisters, primarily caused by S. aureus producing exfoliative toxins.
  • Ecthyma: A more severe, ulcerative form of impetigo extending into the dermis, leaving scars; often seen in immunocompromised individuals or those with poor hygiene.
  • Neonates: May present with widespread bullous lesions, potentially leading to staphylococcal scalded skin syndrome (SSSS).
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Impetigo"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected Impetigo: Clinical diagnosis based on characteristic lesions.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Swab of lesion for bacterial culture and sensitivity, particularly in recurrent cases or non-response to treatment.
  • Imaging - not typically indicated: Imaging is generally not required for uncomplicated impetigo.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Impetigo"][/sc]
  • A 4-year-old child presents with a 3-day history of itchy, red sores around the mouth and nose, which have developed into honey-coloured crusts.
  • Acute, contagious, superficial bacterial skin infection, characterised by vesiculopustular lesions and golden crusts, typically affecting children.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Impetigo"][/sc]
  • Herpes simplex virus (HSV) infection - vesicular lesions, but often grouped and painful, without the characteristic golden crusts.
  • Contact dermatitis - itchy rash, but usually with clear boundaries and exposure to an allergen, lacking bacterial features.
  • Fungal infection (e.g., tinea corporis) - often with annular lesions and scaling, typically slower progression.
  • Scabies - intensely itchy papules and burrows, often in specific anatomical sites, with nocturnal exacerbation.
  • Insect bites - localised reaction, but typically without the spreading and crusting seen in impetigo.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Impetigo"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Advise on hygiene measures to prevent spread (e.g., hand washing, avoiding sharing towels), and consider topical antibiotics for localised infection.
  • Definitive management: For localised impetigo, topical fusidic acid or mupirocin. For widespread or bullous impetigo, or if systemic symptoms, oral flucloxacillin or clarithromycin (if penicillin allergic).
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Review clinical response within 3-5 days; ensure resolution to prevent complications and recurrence.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Advise patients to seek medical attention if lesions worsen, spread rapidly, or if systemic symptoms develop.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Impetigo"][/sc]
  • Cellulitis: Deeper bacterial infection of the dermis and subcutaneous tissue, potentially leading to systemic illness.
  • Post-streptococcal glomerulonephritis (PSGN): A rare but serious complication of impetigo caused by Streptococcus pyogenes, typically occurring 1-3 weeks after skin infection.
  • Scarring: Particularly with ecthyma or if lesions are extensively scratched and secondarily infected.
  • Spread to others: Highly contagious, leading to outbreaks in close-contact settings like schools or families.
