Airway compromise/ management [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Airway compromise/ management"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Airway compromise/ management"][/sc]
  • Airway compromise refers to any condition that obstructs or threatens the patency of the upper or lower airway, impeding the free flow of air to and from the lungs, which is essential for adequate oxygenation and ventilation [1, 2].
  • UKMLA learning outcome: The candidate must recognise the signs and symptoms of airway compromise and initiate appropriate management to maintain a patent airway.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Airway compromise/ management"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Airway compromise/ management"][/sc]
  • Major complications of airway management in the UK are significant, with at least one in four major airway events in a hospital likely to occur in the Intensive Care Unit (ICU) or Emergency Department (ED), often with adverse outcomes [3].
  • The incidence of major complications of airway management during anaesthesia in the UK has been studied extensively, highlighting the importance of robust management strategies [4].
  • Risk factors for central airway obstruction include lung cancer, primary airway malignancy, smoking, artificial airways, tracheobronchial stents, and lung transplantation [5].
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Airway compromise/ management"][/sc]
  • Trigger: An initiating event, such as trauma, infection, foreign body aspiration, or loss of consciousness, leads to a reduction in airway patency.
  • Progression: The compromised airway results in increased work of breathing, reduced gas exchange, and potential for hypoxia and hypercapnia.
  • Consequence: Persistent airway obstruction can lead to respiratory failure, cardiac arrest, and irreversible organ damage if not promptly managed.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Airway compromise/ management"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Rapid onset of symptoms, often within minutes to hours, following an acute event such as anaphylaxis or foreign body aspiration.
  • Progressive phase: Worsening of respiratory distress and signs of hypoxia, particularly beyond 24-48 hours, in conditions like epiglottitis or severe asthma.
  • Late phase: Development of complications such as respiratory failure or aspiration pneumonia, which can occur days to weeks after the initial compromise if not effectively managed.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Airway compromise/ management"][/sc]
  • Ask about dyspnoea - to assess the severity of respiratory distress and the patient's subjective experience of breathlessness.
  • Ask about stridor or hoarseness - to identify upper airway obstruction and potential laryngeal involvement.
  • Ask about cough - to determine if it is productive or non-productive, and if it is associated with foreign body aspiration or infection.
  • Ask about difficulty swallowing (dysphagia) or drooling - to suggest potential pharyngeal or laryngeal oedema or obstruction.
  • Ask about chest pain or discomfort - to differentiate from cardiac causes or to identify associated conditions like pneumothorax.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Airway compromise/ management"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs (respiratory rate, heart rate, oxygen saturation, blood pressure) - to assess the severity of respiratory compromise and systemic response.
  • Check for accessory muscle use or tracheal tug - indicating increased work of breathing and severe respiratory distress.
  • Check for cyanosis - a late sign of significant hypoxia.
  • Check for reduced or absent breath sounds on auscultation - suggesting complete airway obstruction or pneumothorax.
  • Check for altered mental status [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Altered mental status in airway compromise: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="altered mental status"] - indicating cerebral hypoxia.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Airway compromise/ management"][/sc]
  • Rapidly worsening dyspnoea - suggests acute and severe airway compromise requiring immediate intervention.
  • Stridor at rest - indicates significant upper airway obstruction.
  • Inability to speak in full sentences - a sign of severe respiratory distress and impending respiratory failure.
  • Silent chest - in a patient with severe asthma or COPD exacerbation, this can indicate complete airway obstruction and is a pre-terminal sign.
  • Haemodynamic instability - suggests severe systemic compromise due to hypoxia or hypercapnia.
