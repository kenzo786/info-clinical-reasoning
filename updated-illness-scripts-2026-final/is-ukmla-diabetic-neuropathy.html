Diabetic neuropathy [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Diabetic neuropathy"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Diabetic neuropathy"][/sc]
  • Diabetic neuropathy encompasses a heterogeneous group of nerve disorders caused by chronic hyperglycaemia, leading to progressive damage to peripheral nerves.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the varied presentations of diabetic neuropathy, including distal symmetrical polyneuropathy, autonomic neuropathy, and mononeuropathies, and understand their clinical implications.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Diabetic neuropathy"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Diabetic neuropathy"][/sc]
  • Diabetic neuropathy affects approximately 20-30% of individuals with diabetes in the UK, with prevalence increasing with duration of diabetes and age [1].
  • Distal symmetrical polyneuropathy is the most common form, affecting up to 50% of people with long-standing diabetes [2].
  • Risk factors include poor glycaemic control, duration of diabetes, hypertension, dyslipidaemia, obesity, and smoking.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Diabetic neuropathy"][/sc]
  • Chronic hyperglycaemia triggers multiple biochemical pathways, including increased flux through the polyol pathway, advanced glycation end-product (AGE) formation, and oxidative stress, leading to neuronal damage.
  • These changes result in demyelination, axonal degeneration, and impaired nerve conduction, particularly affecting small unmyelinated and large myelinated nerve fibres.
  • The consequence is a progressive loss of nerve function, manifesting as sensory, motor, or autonomic deficits, often leading to complications such as foot ulceration and cardiovascular autonomic neuropathy. Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Diabetic neuropathy"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Often asymptomatic or subtle, with initial nerve damage occurring over years of suboptimal glycaemic control. Patients may report vague paraesthesias or reduced vibratory sensation.
  • Progressive phase: Symptoms become more noticeable and persistent, typically developing gradually over months to years. Distal symmetrical polyneuropathy often presents with a "glove and stocking" distribution. Autonomic symptoms may emerge, impacting cardiovascular, gastrointestinal, or genitourinary systems.
  • Late phase: Severe and debilitating symptoms, including chronic neuropathic pain, significant sensory loss leading to recurrent foot ulcers, Charcot arthropathy, and life-threatening autonomic dysfunction such as silent myocardial ischaemia or orthostatic hypotension.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Diabetic neuropathy"][/sc]
  • Ask about numbness or tingling - often starts in the toes and spreads upwards, indicating distal symmetrical polyneuropathy.
  • Ask about burning, shooting, or stabbing pain - characteristic neuropathic pain, which can be debilitating and worse at night.
  • Ask about muscle weakness or difficulty with balance - suggests motor nerve involvement, which can increase fall risk.
  • Ask about dizziness on standing - may indicate orthostatic hypotension due to cardiovascular autonomic neuropathy.
  • Ask about changes in bowel habits (constipation/diarrhoea) or early satiety - points towards gastrointestinal autonomic neuropathy.
  • Ask about erectile dysfunction or bladder dysfunction - common manifestations of genitourinary autonomic neuropathy.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Diabetic neuropathy"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - orthostatic hypotension (fall in systolic BP >20mmHg or diastolic BP >10mmHg on standing) suggests autonomic neuropathy.
  • Check sensation (light touch, pinprick, vibration, proprioception) - reduced sensation in a "glove and stocking" distribution is typical of distal symmetrical polyneuropathy.
  • Check ankle reflexes - often diminished or absent in diabetic neuropathy, particularly in the lower limbs.
  • Check foot deformities and skin integrity - Charcot foot (rocker-bottom deformity) or ulceration indicates advanced neuropathy and increased risk of infection. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Charcot foot: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Charcot foot"][/sc]
  • Check muscle strength and tone - distal muscle weakness and wasting may be present in motor neuropathy.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Diabetic neuropathy"][/sc]
  • Rapid onset of symptoms: Suggests an acute mononeuropathy or other non-diabetic cause requiring urgent investigation.
  • Asymmetrical weakness or sensory loss: May indicate a mononeuropathy multiplex or a compressive neuropathy, necessitating specific diagnostic workup.
  • New onset foot ulceration or deformity: High risk of infection, osteomyelitis, or Charcot arthropathy, requiring immediate specialist foot care referral.
  • Severe orthostatic hypotension with syncope: Indicates significant cardiovascular autonomic neuropathy, increasing risk of falls and cardiovascular events.
  • Progressive muscle weakness with fasciculations: Raises concern for motor neuron disease or other severe neurological conditions beyond typical diabetic neuropathy.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Diabetic neuropathy"][/sc]
  • Mononeuropathy: Affects a single nerve, e.g., oculomotor nerve palsy (diabetic ophthalmoplegia) or femoral neuropathy, presenting with acute pain and weakness in a specific distribution.
  • Diabetic amyotrophy (proximal motor neuropathy): Presents with acute or subacute onset of severe pain and weakness in the proximal thigh and hip muscles, often unilateral, leading to significant functional impairment.
  • Autonomic neuropathy: Can present with isolated symptoms such as gastroparesis (nausea, vomiting, early satiety), neurogenic bladder (urinary retention, incontinence), or gustatory sweating (sweating after eating). [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Autonomic neuropathy: what are its varied presentations and why is it often missed?" text="Autonomic neuropathy"][/sc]
  • Charcot arthropathy: A severe complication of sensory neuropathy, leading to progressive joint destruction, particularly in the foot, often without significant pain due to sensory loss.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Diabetic neuropathy"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected diabetic neuropathy:
    • Neurological examination: Assessment of sensation (10g monofilament, vibration, pinprick), reflexes (ankle jerk), and motor strength.
    • HbA1c: To assess long-term glycaemic control.
    • Urine albumin-creatinine ratio (ACR) and eGFR: To screen for diabetic nephropathy, which often coexists.
  • Severity/risk tools:
    • Michigan Neuropathy Screening Instrument (MNSI) or Diabetic Neuropathy Symptom (DNS) score: For symptom assessment.
    • Neuropathy Total Symptom Score (NTSS) on MDCalc: For quantifying neuropathic symptoms.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible:
    • Nerve conduction studies and electromyography (NCS/EMG): To confirm diagnosis, classify neuropathy type, and rule out other causes.
    • Thyroid function tests, vitamin B12 levels, serum electrophoresis: To exclude other causes of neuropathy.
  • Imaging - X-ray/MRI of foot: To assess for Charcot arthropathy or osteomyelitis in cases of foot deformity or ulceration. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Diabetic foot imaging: what are the key findings and why do they matter clinically?" text="Diabetic foot imaging"][/sc]
  • Serial reassessment: Regular foot examinations and neurological assessments are crucial for monitoring progression and preventing complications.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Diabetic neuropathy"][/sc]
  • A 62-year-old male with a 15-year history of type 2 diabetes presents with a 6-month history of burning pain and numbness in both feet, worse at night, associated with reduced vibratory sensation and absent ankle reflexes.
  • A middle-aged diabetic patient with chronic distal sensory loss and neuropathic pain, concerning for progressive diabetic polyneuropathy.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Diabetic neuropathy"][/sc]
  • Vitamin B12 deficiency: Can cause symmetrical sensory neuropathy; often associated with macrocytic anaemia and can coexist with diabetes (e.g., metformin use).
  • Alcoholic neuropathy: Distal symmetrical polyneuropathy due to chronic alcohol excess; often presents with pain, paraesthesias, and weakness.
  • Chronic inflammatory demyelinating polyneuropathy (CIDP): Autoimmune condition causing progressive weakness and sensory loss; typically more motor involvement and often responsive to immunomodulatory therapy. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="CIDP vs diabetic neuropathy: what are the key distinguishing features and why do they matter clinically?" text="CIDP"][/sc]
  • Drug-induced neuropathy: Certain medications (e.g., chemotherapy agents, amiodarone, isoniazid) can cause peripheral neuropathy; important to review medication history.
  • Hypothyroidism: Can cause a mild, often reversible, polyneuropathy; associated with other hypothyroid symptoms.
  • Compressive neuropathies (e.g., carpal tunnel syndrome): Mononeuropathies that can mimic or coexist with diabetic neuropathy; often focal symptoms and signs.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Diabetic neuropathy"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Optimisation of glycaemic control to prevent progression, comprehensive foot care to prevent ulceration, and symptomatic relief of neuropathic pain.
  • Definitive management:
    • Glycaemic control: Intensive management of blood glucose levels is crucial to slow progression, particularly in early stages.
    • Pain management: Pharmacological options for neuropathic pain often include duloxetine, pregabalin, gabapentin, or amitriptyline, as per NICE NG19 guidelines.
    • Foot care: Regular foot inspections, appropriate footwear, and prompt management of any foot lesions are essential to prevent serious complications.
  • Adjunctive management: Lifestyle modifications (diet, exercise, smoking cessation), management of cardiovascular risk factors (hypertension, dyslipidaemia), and vitamin supplementation if deficiencies are identified.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Annual foot checks, regular assessment of neuropathic symptoms, and screening for autonomic dysfunction.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Referral to specialist diabetes foot care teams for ulceration or Charcot arthropathy, pain clinics for refractory neuropathic pain, and neurology for atypical presentations or diagnostic uncertainty.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Diabetic neuropathy"][/sc]
  • Diabetic foot ulceration: Sensory neuropathy leads to loss of protective sensation, increasing risk of unnoticed trauma and subsequent ulceration, particularly in the late phase.
  • Charcot arthropathy: Progressive joint destruction, most commonly in the foot, due to repetitive microtrauma in the presence of severe sensory and autonomic neuropathy, often presenting in the late phase.
  • Amputation: A severe consequence of unmanaged foot ulceration, infection, or Charcot arthropathy, significantly impacting quality of life and mortality.
  • Cardiovascular autonomic neuropathy (CAN): Can lead to orthostatic hypotension, resting tachycardia, impaired exercise tolerance, and increased risk of silent myocardial ischaemia and sudden cardiac death, progressing over months to years.
  • Gastroparesis: Autonomic neuropathy affecting the gastrointestinal tract, causing delayed gastric emptying, nausea, vomiting, and early satiety, which can complicate glycaemic control.
  • Neurogenic bladder: Bladder dysfunction due to autonomic neuropathy, leading to urinary retention, recurrent UTIs, and overflow incontinence, typically manifesting in the progressive to late phases.
