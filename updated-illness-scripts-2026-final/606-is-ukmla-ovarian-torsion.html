Ovarian torsion [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Ovarian torsion"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Ovarian torsion"][/sc]
  • Ovarian torsion is the complete or partial rotation of the ovary on its ligamentous supports, often involving the fallopian tube, leading to compromised blood flow and potential ischaemia.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical presentation of ovarian torsion, understand its emergency nature, and outline initial diagnostic and management steps.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Ovarian torsion"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Ovarian torsion"][/sc]
  • Ovarian torsion accounts for approximately 2-3% of gynaecological emergencies in the UK [2, 6]. The incidence among women of all ages is estimated at 5.9 per 100,000 [5].
  • It is most common in women of reproductive age, with a peak incidence in the 20s and 30s, but can occur at any age, including in infants, children, adolescents, and postmenopausal women.
  • Risk factors include ovarian masses (cysts or neoplasms) greater than 5 cm, which significantly increase the likelihood of the ovary twisting on its pedicle. Pregnancy is also a risk factor due to hormonal changes and uterine enlargement.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Ovarian torsion"][/sc]
  • The ovary, and often the fallopian tube, twists around its vascular pedicle, compromising venous and lymphatic outflow, leading to congestion and oedema.
  • Continued twisting obstructs arterial inflow, resulting in ovarian ischaemia and eventual necrosis if not promptly resolved.
  • The presence of an ovarian mass, particularly a cyst, often acts as a fulcrum around which the adnexa can twist, initiating the torsion process. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Ovarian mass: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Ovarian mass"][/sc]
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Ovarian torsion"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Acute onset of severe, unilateral lower abdominal pain, often accompanied by nausea and vomiting, reflecting initial vascular compromise.
  • Progressive phase: If torsion persists, pain may become more constant, and signs of peritoneal irritation may develop as ischaemia progresses, potentially over several hours.
  • Late phase: Irreversible ovarian damage and necrosis can occur within hours if blood flow is not restored, particularly beyond 6-12 hours, leading to potential loss of the ovary.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Ovarian torsion"][/sc]
  • Ask about sudden-onset, severe, unilateral lower abdominal pain - this is the cardinal symptom, often described as sharp or stabbing, and may radiate to the back or thigh.
  • Ask about nausea and vomiting - frequently accompanies the pain, often disproportionate to other causes of abdominal pain, suggesting visceral involvement.
  • Ask about previous episodes of similar pain - intermittent torsion can occur, with spontaneous detorsion leading to recurrent symptoms.
  • Ask about any known ovarian cysts or masses - these are significant risk factors and can predispose to torsion.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Ovarian torsion"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - may reveal tachycardia and hypotension in severe pain or dehydration, but can be normal in early stages.
  • Check abdominal tenderness - typically localised to the affected adnexa, often with guarding or rebound tenderness, indicating peritoneal irritation.
  • Check for a palpable adnexal mass - may be present on abdominal or bimanual pelvic examination, though absence does not rule out torsion. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Adnexal mass: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Adnexal mass"][/sc]
  • Check for cervical motion tenderness - may be present, but is non-specific and can also indicate pelvic inflammatory disease.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Ovarian torsion"][/sc]
  • Sudden, severe, unilateral pelvic pain: Suggests an acute gynaecological emergency requiring immediate assessment to prevent ovarian ischaemia.
  • Pain with associated nausea and vomiting: Often indicates visceral pain and can be a prominent feature of ovarian torsion, warranting urgent investigation.
  • Haemodynamic instability: Tachycardia and hypotension, though rare, indicate significant pain or potential haemorrhage, necessitating rapid resuscitation and surgical review.
  • Rapidly worsening pain over several hours: Implies progressive ischaemia and increasing risk of irreversible ovarian damage, demanding prompt surgical intervention.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Ovarian torsion"][/sc]
  • Children and adolescents: May present with non-specific abdominal pain, often without a palpable mass, making diagnosis challenging. High index of suspicion is crucial.
  • Pregnancy: Symptoms can be masked by common pregnancy discomforts, and the enlarged uterus can displace the ovaries, altering pain location. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Ovarian torsion in pregnancy: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Ovarian torsion in pregnancy"][/sc]
  • Postmenopausal women: While less common, torsion can occur, often associated with ovarian masses, and may be mistaken for other causes of abdominal pain.
  • Intermittent torsion: Recurrent, self-limiting episodes of pain due to spontaneous detorsion, which can delay definitive diagnosis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Ovarian torsion"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected ovarian torsion:
    • Transvaginal ultrasound with Doppler flow: Often the first-line imaging modality, showing an enlarged, oedematous ovary, often with free fluid. Absent Doppler flow is highly suggestive but not always present, as dual blood supply can maintain some flow despite torsion. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Doppler ultrasound in ovarian torsion: what are its limitations and why does it matter clinically?" text="Doppler ultrasound limitations"][/sc]
    • Pregnancy test: Essential to rule out ectopic pregnancy, a critical differential diagnosis.
    • Full blood count and C-reactive protein: May show leucocytosis and elevated inflammatory markers, but these are non-specific.
    • Serum electrolytes and renal function tests: To assess for dehydration and exclude renal causes of pain. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Serum electrolytes in acute abdominal pain: what is their role and why does it matter clinically?" text="Serum electrolytes"][/sc]
  • Severity/risk tools: The ADNEX Model (Assessment of Different NEoplasias in the adneXa) can help characterise adnexal masses, but there is no widely validated severity/risk score specifically for ovarian torsion.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible:
    • CT abdomen/pelvis: Can be considered if ultrasound is inconclusive or other abdominal pathologies are suspected, though it is less sensitive for direct visualisation of torsion.
    • MRI abdomen/pelvis: Offers superior soft tissue contrast and can be useful in complex cases or pregnancy, but is often not readily available in an emergency setting.
  • Serial reassessment: Clinical reassessment is crucial, as symptoms and signs can evolve. Repeat imaging may be considered if initial findings are equivocal and clinical suspicion remains high.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Ovarian torsion"][/sc]
  • A 28-year-old female presents with sudden-onset, severe right-sided pelvic pain, associated with nausea and vomiting, and a history of an ovarian cyst.
  • Acute pelvic pain with systemic symptoms and a known risk factor, concerning for ovarian torsion.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Ovarian torsion"][/sc]
  • Ectopic pregnancy - distinguished by positive pregnancy test, often earlier gestational age, and different ultrasound findings (e.g., adnexal mass separate from ovary, free fluid).
  • Pelvic inflammatory disease (PID) - distinguished by often less acute onset, vaginal discharge, fever, and bilateral adnexal tenderness.
  • Appendicitis - distinguished by migratory pain (periumbilical to right lower quadrant), anorexia, and often fever, though can mimic right-sided ovarian torsion.
  • Ovarian cyst rupture/haemorrhage - distinguished by sudden pain, but often less severe and without the progressive ischaemic changes of torsion; ultrasound may show a ruptured cyst with free fluid.
  • Renal colic - distinguished by flank pain radiating to the groin, dysuria, and haematuria, though pain can be severe and mimic acute abdominal pathology.
  • Endometriosis flare - distinguished by cyclical pain, often chronic, and less acute onset, though acute exacerbations can occur. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Endometriosis: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Endometriosis"][/sc]
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Ovarian torsion"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Prompt recognition and urgent gynaecological consultation are paramount. Initial management often includes analgesia, antiemetics, and intravenous fluids for haemodynamic stability.
  • Definitive management: Laparoscopic surgery with detorsion of the affected adnexa is the preferred approach to restore blood flow and preserve ovarian function. Oophorectomy may be considered if the ovary is necrotic.
  • Adjunctive management: Management of any underlying ovarian cyst or mass, such as cystectomy, may be performed during the same surgical procedure to reduce recurrence risk.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Post-operatively, monitoring for pain resolution and ovarian viability is important. Follow-up ultrasound may be arranged to assess ovarian recovery.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Patients should be advised to seek immediate medical attention for any worsening pain, fever, or signs of infection post-discharge.
  • Exam pharmacology reference (UKMLA/MRCGP): No specific drug knowledge is commonly tested for ovarian torsion beyond general pain management.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Ovarian torsion"][/sc]
  • Ovarian necrosis: If blood supply is not restored promptly (particularly beyond 6-12 hours), the ovary can undergo irreversible damage, leading to its loss and potential impact on fertility.
  • Infection: Necrotic ovarian tissue can become a nidus for infection, potentially leading to peritonitis or abscess formation, increasing morbidity.
  • Infertility: Loss of an ovary or significant damage to ovarian function can impact future fertility, especially if the contralateral ovary is already compromised.
  • Chronic pain: Adhesions or persistent inflammation following torsion, even after detorsion, can lead to chronic pelvic pain.
  • Recurrence: Untreated underlying ovarian pathology (e.g., large cysts) can predispose to recurrent episodes of ovarian torsion.
