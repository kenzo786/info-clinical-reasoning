Bronchiectasis

1. UKMLA Learning Outcome & Summary

UKMLA Learning Outcome: Recognise the clinical features of bronchiectasis, understand its common causes, and outline the principles of investigation and management.

Bronchiectasis is a chronic respiratory condition characterized by permanent, irreversible dilatation of the bronchi and bronchioles, leading to impaired mucociliary clearance, chronic infection, and inflammation. This results in a vicious cycle of infection and airway damage, manifesting as a persistent productive cough, recurrent respiratory infections, and progressive lung function decline. Common causes include post-infective states, cystic fibrosis, primary ciliary dyskinesia, and immunodeficiencies. Diagnosis is typically confirmed by high-resolution computed tomography (HRCT) of the chest, which visualizes the bronchial dilatation. Management focuses on airway clearance techniques, antibiotics for exacerbations, and addressing underlying causes to minimize disease progression and improve quality of life.

3. Pathophysiology

Bronchiectasis develops following an initial insult to the airways, such as a severe infection or genetic defect, which disrupts the normal mucociliary clearance mechanisms. This impairment leads to the retention of thick, viscous mucus within the bronchial tree, creating a favourable environment for chronic bacterial colonization. The persistent presence of bacteria triggers a chronic inflammatory response, characterized by the release of pro-inflammatory mediators and proteases. This ongoing inflammation, coupled with recurrent infections, causes progressive damage to the bronchial walls, leading to their irreversible dilatation. This process establishes a "vicious cycle" of infection, inflammation, and further airway damage, perpetuating the disease. The bronchial dilatation, a hallmark of the condition, is typically visualized on high-resolution computed tomography (HRCT) scans.

4. Aetiology

The causes of bronchiectasis are diverse and can be broadly categorized into several groups:

  • Post-infective: This is a common cause, often following severe childhood infections such as measles, pertussis, pneumonia, or tuberculosis. The acute infection leads to airway damage that subsequently results in permanent dilatation.
  • Genetic disorders:
    • Cystic Fibrosis (CF): A leading cause of bronchiectasis, characterized by a defect in chloride transport that results in thick, sticky mucus, predisposing to chronic infection and airway damage.
    • Primary Ciliary Dyskinesia (PCD): A group of genetic disorders affecting ciliary structure and function, leading to impaired mucociliary clearance and recurrent respiratory infections.
    • Alpha-1 Antitrypsin Deficiency: A genetic condition that can lead to panacinar emphysema and bronchiectasis due to a lack of protective enzyme activity against elastase.
  • Immunodeficiency: Conditions that compromise the immune system, such as Common Variable Immunodeficiency (CVID), IgG subclass deficiencies, or HIV infection, increase susceptibility to recurrent infections that can lead to bronchiectasis.
  • Allergic Bronchopulmonary Aspergillosis (ABPA): An allergic reaction to the fungus Aspergillus fumigatus, primarily seen in individuals with asthma or cystic fibrosis, leading to airway inflammation and damage.
  • Connective tissue diseases: Systemic inflammatory conditions like rheumatoid arthritis and inflammatory bowel disease can be associated with bronchiectasis.
  • Other causes: These include aspiration of gastric contents, bronchial obstruction (e.g., by a foreign body or tumour), and certain toxic inhalations. In a significant proportion of cases, no underlying cause is identified, termed idiopathic bronchiectasis.
5. Clinical Features: Symptoms

The clinical presentation of bronchiectasis is dominated by chronic respiratory symptoms, which often fluctuate in severity and are exacerbated during periods of infection:

  • Chronic cough: A persistent cough, often lasting for months or years, is the most characteristic symptom. It is typically productive of copious amounts of sputum, which can be purulent (yellow or green) and may be produced daily. The volume and purulence of sputum often increase during exacerbations.
  • Dyspnoea (shortness of breath): This symptom is common and tends to worsen with disease progression and during acute exacerbations, reflecting reduced lung function and increased airway obstruction.
  • Recurrent chest infections: Patients frequently experience episodes of acute respiratory infection, requiring antibiotic treatment. The frequency of these infections is a key indicator of disease activity and severity.
  • Haemoptysis: Coughing up blood is a common symptom, ranging from blood-streaked sputum to, less commonly, massive haemorrhage. Any significant haemoptysis warrants urgent medical assessment.
  • Fatigue and weight loss: Non-specific systemic symptoms such as chronic fatigue, malaise, and unintentional weight loss can occur, particularly in individuals with severe or poorly controlled disease, indicating the systemic burden of chronic inflammation and infection.
  • Chest pain: Pleuritic chest pain may occur, especially during exacerbations or if there is associated pleural inflammation.
6. Clinical Features: Signs

Physical examination findings in bronchiectasis can vary depending on the severity and extent of the disease, as well as the presence of an acute exacerbation:

  • Vitals: During an acute exacerbation, patients may exhibit tachypnoea (increased respiratory rate), tachycardia (increased heart rate), and fever. In severe disease, chronic hypoxia may lead to cyanosis.
  • Chest examination: Auscultation of the chest commonly reveals coarse inspiratory crackles (crepitations), particularly over the affected lung regions, often at the lung bases. Wheeze may be present if there is co-existing airway obstruction, such as in patients with asthma or COPD. Diminished breath sounds can be noted in areas of significant consolidation or atelectasis.
  • Digital clubbing: This sign, characterized by bulbous enlargement of the fingertips and toes, can be present in individuals with severe or long-standing bronchiectasis, indicative of chronic hypoxaemia.
  • Sputum characteristics: Observation of sputum often reveals it to be purulent, mucoid, or mucopurulent, and sometimes copious in volume.

7. Clinical Course

The clinical course of bronchiectasis is typically chronic and progressive, characterized by periods of relative stability interspersed with acute exacerbations. The disease often begins subtly, with symptoms initially attributed to other common respiratory conditions. Over time, the frequency and severity of symptoms tend to increase, impacting quality of life and lung function.

  • Early phase: Patients may experience a chronic, productive cough, which might initially be dismissed as "smoker's cough" or recurrent bronchitis. Sputum production may be intermittent.
  • Progressive phase: As the disease advances, there is an increasing frequency and severity of acute exacerbations, often triggered by bacterial or viral infections. During exacerbations, sputum volume and purulence typically increase, and dyspnoea worsens. This phase is marked by a gradual decline in lung function.
  • Late phase: In advanced stages, patients may experience persistent breathlessness, chronic fatigue, and unintentional weight loss. Complications such as massive haemoptysis, respiratory failure, and cor pulmonale (right-sided heart failure due to lung disease) can develop, significantly increasing morbidity and mortality. The disease course is variable, influenced by the underlying cause, severity of airway damage, and effectiveness of management strategies.
8. Investigations

Investigations for bronchiectasis aim to confirm the diagnosis, identify the underlying cause, assess disease severity, and guide management:

  • High-Resolution Computed Tomography (HRCT) of the chest: This is the gold standard for diagnosing bronchiectasis. HRCT images reveal characteristic bronchial dilatation, lack of bronchial tapering, and bronchial wall thickening. It also helps to assess the extent and distribution of the disease.
  • Sputum microbiology: Regular sputum cultures are crucial to identify colonizing organisms (e.g., Pseudomonas aeruginosa, Haemophilus influenzae, Streptococcus pneumoniae) and their antibiotic sensitivities. This guides appropriate antibiotic therapy during exacerbations and for long-term management.
  • Lung function tests (Spirometry): Used to assess the degree of airflow obstruction and lung volume changes. FEV1 (forced expiratory volume in 1 second) is a key measure for monitoring disease progression.
  • Blood tests:
    • Full blood count (FBC): May show leukocytosis during exacerbations or chronic anaemia.
    • Inflammatory markers (CRP, ESR): Elevated during acute exacerbations.
    • Immunoglobulin levels (IgG, IgA, IgM) and specific antibody responses: To screen for immunodeficiencies.
    • Alpha-1 antitrypsin levels: To screen for alpha-1 antitrypsin deficiency.
    • Aspergillus serology (IgE, IgG) and skin prick test: To investigate for Allergic Bronchopulmonary Aspergillosis (ABPA).
  • Cystic Fibrosis (CF) screen: Sweat chloride test and/or CFTR gene mutation analysis should be considered, especially in younger patients or those with a strong clinical suspicion.
  • Nasal nitric oxide (nNO) and ciliary biopsy: For suspected Primary Ciliary Dyskinesia (PCD).
  • Bronchoscopy: May be considered in localized disease to rule out endobronchial obstruction (e.g., foreign body, tumour) or to obtain samples for microbiology and histology.

9. Management

Management of bronchiectasis is multifaceted, aiming to control symptoms, prevent exacerbations, improve quality of life, and slow disease progression. It typically involves a combination of non-pharmacological and pharmacological interventions:

  • Airway clearance techniques (ACTs): These are fundamental to management, helping to mobilize and clear retained secretions. Techniques include active cycle of breathing techniques (ACBT), postural drainage, percussion, and oscillatory positive expiratory pressure (PEP) devices. Patients should be taught appropriate techniques by a physiotherapist.
  • Antibiotics:
    • For exacerbations: Prompt initiation of antibiotics, guided by sputum microbiology and local resistance patterns, is crucial during acute exacerbations. Empirical treatment may be started while awaiting culture results.
    • Long-term prophylactic antibiotics: Considered for patients with frequent exacerbations (e.g., three or more per year) or chronic infection with problematic organisms like Pseudomonas aeruginosa. Macrolides (e.g., azithromycin) or inhaled antibiotics (e.g., colistin, gentamicin) may be used.
  • Bronchodilators: May be used to improve airflow and reduce dyspnoea, particularly in patients with co-existing asthma or COPD, or those with evidence of reversible airflow obstruction.
  • Corticosteroids: Inhaled corticosteroids may be beneficial for patients with co-existing asthma. Oral corticosteroids are specifically indicated for the treatment of Allergic Bronchopulmonary Aspergillosis (ABPA).
  • Pulmonary rehabilitation: A comprehensive program including exercise training, education, and self-management strategies, beneficial for improving exercise capacity, dyspnoea, and quality of life.
  • Vaccinations: Annual influenza vaccination and pneumococcal vaccination are recommended to prevent respiratory infections.
  • Treating underlying causes: Specific treatments for identified underlying conditions, such as immunoglobulin replacement therapy for immunodeficiencies or treatment for ABPA, are essential.
  • Surgery: Rarely indicated, but may be considered for localized disease unresponsive to medical therapy, or for managing massive haemoptysis.

10. Complications

Bronchiectasis can lead to several significant complications, particularly in advanced or poorly controlled disease:

  • Acute exacerbations: Frequent and severe episodes of worsening respiratory symptoms, often requiring hospitalization and intravenous antibiotics.
  • Respiratory failure: Chronic hypoxaemia and hypercapnia can develop due to severe lung damage, necessitating oxygen therapy or non-invasive ventilation.
  • Haemoptysis: Recurrent or massive haemoptysis can be life-threatening and may require bronchial artery embolization or, rarely, surgical intervention.
  • Cor pulmonale: Chronic hypoxaemia can lead to pulmonary hypertension and subsequent right-sided heart failure.
  • Pneumonia: Increased susceptibility to severe bacterial or viral pneumonia.
  • Empyema or lung abscess: Localized collections of pus within the pleural space or lung parenchyma, respectively, can occur as a result of severe infection.
  • Amyloidosis: A rare but serious complication of chronic inflammatory diseases, where abnormal proteins accumulate in organs.
  • Psychological impact: The chronic nature of the disease, persistent symptoms, and frequent exacerbations can significantly impact mental health, leading to anxiety and depression.
11. Prognosis

The prognosis of bronchiectasis is variable and depends on several factors, including the underlying cause, disease severity, frequency of exacerbations, and presence of co-morbidities. While it is a chronic and progressive condition, effective management can significantly improve outcomes and quality of life.

  • Factors associated with poorer prognosis: These include older age, severe airflow obstruction (low FEV1), frequent and severe exacerbations, chronic colonization with Pseudomonas aeruginosa, extensive radiological disease, and the presence of co-morbidities.
  • Severity scores: Tools like the Bronchiectasis Severity Index (BSI) and FACED score help predict mortality and hospital admissions, guiding risk stratification and management decisions.
  • Quality of life: Despite advances in treatment, many patients experience a reduced quality of life due to persistent symptoms, fatigue, and the burden of daily treatments.
  • Mortality: Mortality rates are higher in patients with severe bronchiectasis, particularly those with frequent exacerbations, chronic Pseudomonas infection, and significant co-morbidities. However, with appropriate management, many individuals can live long and relatively stable lives.

12. Pharmacology

Pharmacological management primarily focuses on controlling infection, reducing inflammation, and facilitating airway clearance:

  • Antibiotics: The cornerstone of treatment for acute exacerbations. Choice is guided by sputum culture and sensitivity. Long-term macrolides (e.g., azithromycin) or inhaled antibiotics (e.g., colistin, gentamicin) are used for prophylaxis in selected patients.
  • Bronchodilators: Short-acting and long-acting beta-agonists or anticholinergics may be used to relieve dyspnoea and improve airflow, especially if there is a reversible component to airway obstruction.
  • Corticosteroids: Inhaled corticosteroids are used for co-existing asthma. Oral corticosteroids are reserved for specific conditions like ABPA.
  • Mucolytics: Agents like carbocisteine or nebulized hypertonic saline may be used to reduce sputum viscosity and aid clearance, though evidence for their routine use is variable.
  • Other agents: Specific treatments for underlying conditions, such as antifungal agents for ABPA or immunoglobulins for immunodeficiencies.
