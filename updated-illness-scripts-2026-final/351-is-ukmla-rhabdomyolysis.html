Rhabdomyolysis [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Rhabdomyolysis"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Rhabdomyolysis"][/sc]
  • Rhabdomyolysis is a clinical syndrome characterised by the breakdown of skeletal muscle fibres, leading to the release of intracellular contents such as myoglobin, creatine kinase (CK), and electrolytes into the bloodstream. This can result in acute kidney injury (AKI), electrolyte imbalances, and other systemic complications [1].
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical features, risk factors, and potential complications of rhabdomyolysis, particularly acute kidney injury, and outline initial management strategies.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Rhabdomyolysis"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Rhabdomyolysis"][/sc]
  • The incidence of rhabdomyolysis varies widely depending on the underlying cause, with estimates ranging from 0.03 to 0.12% of hospital admissions in the UK [Source needed].
  • Risk factors include trauma (e.g., crush injuries), prolonged immobilisation, extreme exertion, certain medications (e.g., statins, antipsychotics), illicit drug use (e.g., cocaine, amphetamines), alcohol abuse, infections, and metabolic disorders [1].
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Rhabdomyolysis"][/sc]
  • Muscle injury, whether traumatic or non-traumatic, leads to sarcolemma damage and an influx of extracellular calcium into the myocytes, activating proteases and phospholipases that cause muscle cell necrosis.
  • The necrotic muscle cells release intracellular components, including myoglobin, creatine kinase, and potassium, into the circulation. Myoglobin is freely filtered by the glomeruli but can precipitate in renal tubules, leading to tubular obstruction and direct nephrotoxicity.
  • This process can result in acute kidney injury (AKI) due to renal vasoconstriction, tubular obstruction by myoglobin casts, and direct myoglobin toxicity, exacerbated by hypovolaemia and acidosis [sc name="jitl-query" Q="AKI mechanism in Rhabdomyolysis: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="AKI mechanism"][/sc].
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Rhabdomyolysis"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Symptoms such as muscle pain, weakness, and dark urine typically develop within hours to a few days following the initial muscle injury.
  • Progressive phase: If untreated, complications like acute kidney injury can manifest within 12-72 hours, with creatine kinase levels peaking within 24-72 hours.
  • Late phase: Resolution of symptoms and normalisation of laboratory parameters can take several days to weeks, particularly beyond 72 hours if complications like AKI have developed.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Rhabdomyolysis"][/sc]
  • Ask about muscle pain - often severe and diffuse, indicating widespread muscle damage.
  • Ask about muscle weakness - can range from mild to severe, affecting mobility and daily activities.
  • Ask about dark, reddish-brown urine (cola-coloured urine) - suggests myoglobinuria, a key indicator of muscle breakdown and potential AKI.
  • Ask about general malaise, fever, nausea, and vomiting - non-specific systemic symptoms that may accompany significant muscle injury.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Rhabdomyolysis"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - tachycardia, hypotension, or fever may indicate systemic inflammatory response or dehydration.
  • Check muscle tenderness and swelling - direct signs of muscle injury and inflammation.
  • Check for signs of dehydration - dry mucous membranes, reduced skin turgor, which can exacerbate AKI risk.
  • Check for neurological changes - altered mental status or confusion, particularly in severe cases or with electrolyte derangements.
  • Check for compartment syndrome - tense, painful swelling of a limb, requiring urgent surgical review [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Compartment syndrome: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Compartment syndrome"][/sc].
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Rhabdomyolysis"][/sc]
  • Acute Kidney Injury: Rapid deterioration of renal function (oliguria, anuria, rising creatinine) indicates significant myoglobin-induced renal damage, requiring aggressive fluid management and potential renal replacement therapy.
  • Hyperkalaemia: Elevated serum potassium levels due to muscle cell lysis can lead to life-threatening cardiac arrhythmias, necessitating urgent medical intervention.
  • Compartment Syndrome: Increasing pain, pallor, paraesthesia, pulselessness, and paralysis in a limb suggest muscle swelling within a confined space, demanding immediate fasciotomy to prevent irreversible tissue damage.
  • Disseminated Intravascular Coagulation (DIC): Widespread activation of the coagulation cascade can lead to both thrombosis and haemorrhage, indicating severe systemic involvement and poor prognosis.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Rhabdomyolysis"][/sc]
  • Older adults: May present with non-specific symptoms like falls, weakness, or altered mental status, often without significant muscle pain, making diagnosis challenging.
  • Children: Rhabdomyolysis can be triggered by viral infections or inherited metabolic disorders, presenting with fever, vomiting, and muscle weakness, sometimes without dark urine.
  • Post-operative patients: Prolonged immobilisation during surgery can lead to pressure-induced muscle damage, with symptoms emerging subtly in the recovery period.
  • Patients on statins: Myalgia is common, but rhabdomyolysis can occur with significantly elevated CK levels, sometimes without severe symptoms, requiring a high index of suspicion [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Statin-induced myopathy vs rhabdomyolysis: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Statin-induced rhabdomyolysis"][/sc].
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Rhabdomyolysis"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected Rhabdomyolysis:
    • Serum creatine kinase (CK) level: Typically >1000 IU/L or >5 times the upper limit of normal is diagnostic [1]. Serial measurements are crucial to monitor disease progression and response to treatment.
    • Urea and electrolytes (U&E): To assess renal function (creatinine, urea) and detect electrolyte abnormalities (hyperkalaemia, hyperphosphataemia, hypocalcaemia).
    • Urinalysis: Dipstick positive for blood but negative for red blood cells microscopically suggests myoglobinuria.
    • Arterial blood gas (ABG): To assess for metabolic acidosis.
  • Severity/risk tools: Consider using the Rhabdomyolysis Severity Score on MDCalc to stratify risk of AKI and other complications.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Consider thyroid function tests, inflammatory markers, and autoimmune screens if aetiology is unclear.
  • Serial reassessment: Regular monitoring of CK, U&E, and urine output is essential to guide fluid therapy and detect evolving complications [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Myoglobin: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Myoglobin"][/sc].
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Rhabdomyolysis"][/sc]
  • A 45-year-old male presents after prolonged immobilisation following a fall, complaining of severe muscle pain, weakness, and passing dark, cola-coloured urine, with a creatine kinase level of 15,000 IU/L.
  • Middle-aged male with significant muscle injury and myoglobinuria, concerning for rhabdomyolysis with impending acute kidney injury.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Rhabdomyolysis"][/sc]
  • Myositis (inflammatory or infectious) - distinguished by inflammatory markers, specific autoantibodies, or infectious screen, often with less dramatic CK elevation.
  • Muscle contusion/strain - typically localised pain and swelling, without systemic symptoms or significant CK elevation.
  • Acute limb ischaemia - characterised by sudden onset of pain, pallor, pulselessness, paraesthesia, and paralysis, often with a clear vascular cause.
  • Diabetic ketoacidosis (DKA) - can cause muscle pain and weakness, but distinguished by hyperglycaemia, ketosis, and metabolic acidosis.
  • Neuroleptic Malignant Syndrome (NMS) - presents with fever, rigidity, altered mental status, and autonomic dysfunction, often in patients on antipsychotics.
  • Malignant Hyperthermia - a genetic disorder triggered by anaesthetics, causing rapid rise in body temperature, muscle rigidity, and rhabdomyolysis [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Malignant Hyperthermia: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Malignant Hyperthermia"][/sc].
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Rhabdomyolysis"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Recognition of rhabdomyolysis and its complications (especially AKI and hyperkalaemia), prompt escalation to senior medical staff, and initiation of aggressive intravenous fluid resuscitation.
  • Definitive management: The cornerstone of management is aggressive intravenous fluid therapy with 0.9% sodium chloride to maintain high urine output (e.g., 200-300 mL/hour) and flush myoglobin from renal tubules.
  • Adjunctive management: Consideration of urinary alkalinisation with sodium bicarbonate to reduce myoglobin precipitation in renal tubules, though evidence is mixed. Treatment of hyperkalaemia as per local guidelines.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Close monitoring of fluid balance, urine output, serial CK levels, renal function (U&E), and electrolytes.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Early involvement of renal specialists for severe AKI or refractory electrolyte imbalances, and surgical review for suspected compartment syndrome.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Rhabdomyolysis"][/sc]
  • Acute Kidney Injury (AKI): Occurs due to myoglobin-induced tubular necrosis and obstruction, leading to impaired renal function, electrolyte derangements, and fluid overload, typically developing within 12-72 hours of muscle injury.
  • Hyperkalaemia: Rapid release of intracellular potassium from damaged muscle cells can cause life-threatening cardiac arrhythmias, often an early and critical complication.
  • Compartment Syndrome: Severe muscle swelling can increase pressure within fascial compartments, compromising blood flow and leading to ischaemia and necrosis if not promptly managed, usually within hours of injury.
  • Disseminated Intravascular Coagulation (DIC): Severe rhabdomyolysis can trigger systemic coagulation activation, resulting in microvascular thrombosis and consumption of clotting factors, leading to both bleeding and clotting complications.
  • Metabolic Acidosis: Release of acidic cellular contents and impaired renal acid excretion contribute to metabolic acidosis, which can worsen cardiac function and overall prognosis.
