Raynaud phenomenon [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Raynaud phenomenon"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Raynaud phenomenon"][/sc]
  • A vasospastic disorder causing episodic digital ischaemia, typically triggered by cold or emotional stress, leading to characteristic colour changes (white, blue, red).
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical features of Raynaud phenomenon and differentiate between primary and secondary causes.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Raynaud phenomenon"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Raynaud phenomenon"][/sc]
  • Primary Raynaud phenomenon is common, affecting up to 10% of the UK population, with a higher prevalence in women and younger individuals.
  • Secondary Raynaud phenomenon is less common but more serious, often associated with connective tissue diseases like systemic sclerosis, affecting approximately 1-2% of those with Raynaud's.
  • Risk factors include female sex, family history, living in colder climates, and underlying autoimmune conditions.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Raynaud phenomenon"][/sc]
  • Trigger: Exaggerated vasoconstrictive response of digital arteries and arterioles to cold exposure or emotional stress, often due to an imbalance in local vascular regulation.
  • Progression: Initial intense vasospasm leads to digital ischaemia (pallor), followed by cyanosis (bluish discolouration) as deoxygenated blood pools, and finally reactive hyperaemia (redness) upon reperfusion.
  • Consequence: Recurrent episodes can lead to structural changes in vessel walls, such as intimal hyperplasia and fibrosis, particularly in secondary Raynaud phenomenon, potentially causing digital ulceration or gangrene.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Raynaud phenomenon"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Episodes typically last minutes to hours, resolving spontaneously with rewarming. Symptoms are usually mild and reversible.
  • Progressive phase: With repeated or severe episodes, particularly beyond several months, symptoms may become more frequent or prolonged. Skin changes like thickening or sclerodactyly may emerge, especially in secondary forms.
  • Late phase: In severe secondary Raynaud phenomenon, particularly beyond several years, chronic ischaemia can lead to digital pitting, ulceration, or critical limb ischaemia requiring intervention.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Raynaud phenomenon"][/sc]
  • Ask about digital colour changes - to confirm the classic triphasic (white, blue, red) or biphasic pattern indicative of vasospasm.
  • Ask about triggers - cold exposure, emotional stress, or vibration are typical precipitants, helping to distinguish from other causes of digital ischaemia.
  • Ask about pain, numbness, or tingling - these sensory disturbances often accompany the ischaemic phase and reperfusion, providing insight into nerve involvement.
  • Ask about unilateral or asymmetrical involvement - unilateral symptoms or involvement of only one or two digits may suggest a secondary cause, such as an arterial occlusion.
  • Ask about associated systemic symptoms - joint pain, skin changes, dysphagia, or dry eyes/mouth may point towards an underlying connective tissue disease.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Raynaud phenomenon"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - to assess for systemic involvement (e.g., hypertension in scleroderma) or signs of critical ischaemia.
  • Check digital colour and temperature - direct observation during an episode confirms vasospasm; cool, pale digits are characteristic.
  • Check for digital pitting scars or ulceration - these indicate chronic ischaemia and are highly suggestive of secondary Raynaud phenomenon.
  • Check nailfold capillaries with a dermatoscope - abnormal capillary loops (dilated, tortuous, avascular areas) are a key indicator of secondary Raynaud phenomenon.
  • Check for sclerodactyly or other skin changes - thickened, tight skin on the fingers is a sign of systemic sclerosis, a common cause of secondary Raynaud phenomenon.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Raynaud phenomenon"][/sc]
  • New onset in older adults (>30 years) - often indicates secondary Raynaud phenomenon, warranting investigation for underlying conditions.
  • Unilateral or asymmetrical symptoms - suggests a localised arterial occlusion rather than systemic vasospasm.
  • Digital ulceration or gangrene - signifies critical ischaemia and is a severe complication, almost exclusively seen in secondary forms.
  • Abnormal nailfold capillaries - highly predictive of an underlying connective tissue disease, particularly systemic sclerosis.
  • Associated systemic symptoms - such as joint pain, rash, dysphagia, or muscle weakness, point towards a systemic autoimmune disease.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Raynaud phenomenon"][/sc]
  • Older adults: New onset Raynaud phenomenon in individuals over 30 years old is less likely to be primary and warrants thorough investigation for secondary causes, as it may be the presenting symptom of a serious underlying condition.
  • Occupational Raynaud: Caused by prolonged exposure to vibrating tools; presentation may be localised to affected digits and accompanied by neurological symptoms, highlighting the importance of occupational history.
  • Drug-induced Raynaud: Certain medications (e.g., beta-blockers, some chemotherapy agents) can induce or exacerbate Raynaud symptoms, which may resolve upon drug cessation, making medication review crucial.
  • Isolated toe involvement: While less common than digital involvement, Raynaud phenomenon can affect the toes, and this presentation should also prompt consideration of secondary causes.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Raynaud phenomenon"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected Raynaud phenomenon: Clinical history and examination are paramount. Consider full blood count, erythrocyte sedimentation rate (ESR), C-reactive protein (CRP), and antinuclear antibodies (ANA) to screen for underlying connective tissue disease.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Further specific autoantibody testing (e.g., anti-centromere, anti-Scl-70) if ANA is positive and clinical suspicion for systemic sclerosis is high.
  • Imaging - Capillaroscopy: Nailfold capillaroscopy is a non-invasive technique to visualise microvascular changes, distinguishing primary from secondary Raynaud phenomenon.
  • Imaging - Doppler ultrasound: May be used to assess for large vessel occlusive disease if unilateral or asymmetrical symptoms suggest a structural arterial problem.
  • Serial reassessment: Regular follow-up is important, especially for primary Raynaud phenomenon, to monitor for the development of features suggestive of secondary disease.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Raynaud phenomenon"][/sc]
  • A 35-year-old female presents with a 5-year history of episodic digital pallor and cyanosis, triggered by cold, affecting both hands symmetrically, without digital ulceration or systemic symptoms.
  • A young, otherwise healthy female with chronic, symmetrical, cold-induced digital vasospasm, currently uncomplicated.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Raynaud phenomenon"][/sc]
  • Acrocyanosis - persistent, symmetrical cyanosis of the hands and feet, not episodic and without pallor.
  • Chilblains (Pernio) - inflammatory response to cold, presenting as itchy, red, swollen lesions, not primarily vasospasm.
  • Erythromelalgia - episodic burning pain and redness, triggered by warmth, opposite to Raynaud phenomenon.
  • Peripheral arterial disease - typically causes claudication and chronic ischaemic changes, often unilateral and not episodic colour changes.
  • Vibration white finger - occupational disease with numbness, tingling, and blanching, but linked to specific vibration exposure.
  • Connective tissue diseases (e.g., systemic sclerosis, SLE) - secondary Raynaud phenomenon is often the initial manifestation, distinguished by systemic symptoms and abnormal nailfold capillaries.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Raynaud phenomenon"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Patient education on avoidance of triggers (cold, smoking, stress) and protection of digits (warm gloves).
  • Definitive management: For severe or secondary Raynaud phenomenon, calcium channel blockers (e.g., [sc name="jitl-medication" Q="Nifedipine use in Raynaud phenomenon" text="nifedipine"][/sc]) are often considered first-line to reduce vasospasm. Other options include phosphodiesterase-5 inhibitors or prostaglandin analogues in refractory cases.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Regular review to assess symptom control, monitor for progression to secondary Raynaud phenomenon, and screen for complications like digital ulceration.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Advise patients to seek urgent medical attention for digital ulceration, severe pain, or signs of infection. Referral to rheumatology is appropriate for suspected secondary Raynaud phenomenon.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Raynaud phenomenon"][/sc]
  • Digital pitting scars: Result from recurrent, minor ischaemic episodes causing small areas of tissue loss at the fingertips, indicating chronic microvascular damage.
  • Digital ulceration: More severe ischaemic injury leading to painful, open sores on the fingertips, a significant morbidity often seen in secondary Raynaud phenomenon.
  • Critical limb ischaemia: In rare, severe cases, particularly with underlying systemic sclerosis, prolonged and profound vasospasm can lead to irreversible tissue damage and gangrene, potentially requiring amputation.
  • Infection of ulcers: Open digital ulcers are prone to secondary bacterial infection, which can worsen tissue damage and lead to osteomyelitis if not promptly treated.
  • Functional impairment: Chronic pain, stiffness, and reduced dexterity due to recurrent episodes or complications can significantly impact daily activities and quality of life.
