Alcohol related liver disease [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Alcohol related liver disease"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Alcohol related liver disease"][/sc]
  • Alcohol-related liver disease (ARLD) encompasses a spectrum of liver damage, ranging from simple steatosis (fatty liver) to alcoholic hepatitis and advanced cirrhosis, caused by excessive alcohol consumption.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the stages of alcohol-related liver disease, identify key risk factors, and understand the principles of diagnosis and management, including the importance of alcohol abstinence.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Alcohol related liver disease"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Alcohol related liver disease"][/sc]
  • Alcohol consumption is the most common cause of liver disease in the UK, accounting for 6 in 10 cases. The incidence rate of ARLD in England increased by 65% between 2001 and 2018, reaching 56.1 per 100,000 person-years.
  • Premature deaths from alcoholic liver disease in England have increased by over 60% since 2003, with a 46% increase in related deaths across the UK in the past decade.
  • Key risk factors include prolonged heavy alcohol consumption, female sex, co-existing hepatitis C infection, obesity, and cigarette smoking.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Alcohol related liver disease"][/sc]
  • Chronic excessive alcohol intake leads to the accumulation of toxic metabolites, such as acetaldehyde, which directly damage hepatocytes and disrupt normal liver metabolism, leading to fat accumulation (steatosis).
  • Continued alcohol exposure triggers inflammation and oxidative stress, causing hepatocyte necrosis and activation of [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Hepatic stellate cells: what are they and why do they matter clinically?" text="hepatic stellate cells"][/sc], which produce collagen and initiate fibrosis.
  • Persistent fibrogenesis results in architectural distortion of the liver, forming regenerative nodules and ultimately leading to cirrhosis, impaired liver function, and [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Portal hypertension: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="portal hypertension"][/sc].
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Alcohol related liver disease"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Hepatic steatosis (fatty liver) can develop rapidly, often within days to weeks of heavy alcohol consumption. This stage is typically asymptomatic and reversible with sustained alcohol abstinence.
  • Progressive phase: Continued heavy alcohol intake, particularly beyond weeks to months, can lead to alcoholic hepatitis, characterised by inflammation and hepatocyte necrosis. This can range from mild to severe and may be life-threatening.
  • Late phase: Chronic, prolonged alcohol misuse, often over years, results in irreversible fibrosis and ultimately cirrhosis. This stage is associated with significant morbidity and mortality due to liver dysfunction and complications.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Alcohol related liver disease"][/sc]
  • Ask about fatigue and general malaise - these are often early, non-specific symptoms but can indicate underlying liver dysfunction.
  • Ask about nausea, vomiting, and loss of appetite - these can lead to significant weight loss and malnutrition, exacerbating liver damage.
  • Ask about abdominal pain or discomfort, particularly in the upper right quadrant - this may suggest liver enlargement or inflammation, as seen in steatosis or alcoholic hepatitis.
  • Ask about yellowing of the skin or eyes (jaundice) - this is a critical sign of impaired bilirubin metabolism and often indicates significant liver damage, such as alcoholic hepatitis or cirrhosis.
  • Ask about swelling of the ankles or abdomen (ascites) - these symptoms suggest fluid retention due to portal hypertension and hypoalbuminaemia, indicating advanced liver disease.
  • Ask about changes in mental status, confusion, or drowsiness - these can be signs of hepatic encephalopathy, a severe complication of advanced liver failure.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Alcohol related liver disease"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - fever and tachycardia may indicate alcoholic hepatitis or infection; tachypnoea can suggest metabolic acidosis or respiratory compromise.
  • Check for jaundice - yellow discolouration of the sclera and skin indicates hyperbilirubinaemia, a hallmark of significant liver dysfunction.
  • Check for hepatomegaly and tenderness in the right upper quadrant - an enlarged, tender liver can be present in alcoholic steatosis and hepatitis.
  • Check for [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Stigmata of chronic liver disease: what are they and why do they matter clinically?" text="stigmata of chronic liver disease"][/sc] (e.g., spider naevi, palmar erythema, gynaecomastia, clubbing) - these suggest long-standing liver damage and portal hypertension.
  • Check for ascites and peripheral oedema - fluid retention indicates decompensated liver disease, often due to portal hypertension and reduced albumin synthesis.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Alcohol related liver disease"][/sc]
  • New onset or worsening jaundice - indicates significant deterioration in liver function, often seen in acute alcoholic hepatitis or decompensated cirrhosis.
  • Development of ascites or increasing peripheral oedema - suggests fluid overload due to portal hypertension and impaired albumin synthesis, signifying decompensation.
  • Altered mental status or confusion - a critical sign of hepatic encephalopathy, requiring urgent assessment and management to prevent progression to coma.
  • Gastrointestinal bleeding (e.g., haematemesis, melaena) - often due to ruptured oesophageal varices, a life-threatening complication of portal hypertension in advanced liver disease.
  • Fever with or without abdominal pain - may indicate spontaneous bacterial peritonitis (SBP) or other severe infections, which can precipitate acute-on-chronic liver failure.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Alcohol related liver disease"][/sc]
  • Women: Women are generally more susceptible to alcohol-related liver damage than men, developing more severe disease with lower cumulative alcohol intake and shorter durations of heavy drinking. This may be due to differences in alcohol metabolism and hormonal factors.
  • Older adults: Symptoms can be vague and non-specific, such as malaise, anorexia, and abdominal pain, often attributed to other age-related conditions, leading to delayed diagnosis. Hepatomegaly may be a more prominent sign.
  • Absence of typical risk factors: ARLD can occur in individuals who do not report heavy or prolonged alcohol use, especially if co-existing liver conditions (e.g., hepatitis C, obesity) are present, making diagnosis challenging.
  • Rapid progression: In some individuals, particularly those with genetic predispositions or co-morbidities, the disease can progress rapidly from steatosis to cirrhosis without a clear stage of alcoholic hepatitis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Alcohol related liver disease"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected ARLD: Liver function tests (LFTs) including ALT, AST, GGT, ALP, bilirubin, and albumin. A full blood count (FBC) may show macrocytosis or anaemia. Coagulation screen (INR/PT) is crucial for assessing synthetic function.
  • Severity/risk tools: The [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Maddrey Discriminant Function: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Maddrey Discriminant Function"][/sc] (DF) is used to assess the severity and prognosis of alcoholic hepatitis, guiding corticosteroid therapy decisions.   • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Viral hepatitis serology (Hepatitis B and C), autoantibodies (ANA, SMA, LKM1) for autoimmune hepatitis, ferritin and transferrin saturation for haemochromatosis, and alpha-1 antitrypsin levels.
  • Imaging - Ultrasound: Often the first-line investigation, revealing features such as hepatomegaly, fatty infiltration (bright liver), signs of portal hypertension, and ruling out biliary obstruction.
  • Imaging - CT/MRI: Provides more detailed assessment of liver morphology, presence of ascites, and can help detect hepatocellular carcinoma.
  • Serial reassessment: Regular monitoring of LFTs, FBC, and coagulation is essential to track disease progression and response to interventions.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Alcohol related liver disease"][/sc]
  • A middle-aged individual with a history of chronic heavy alcohol intake presents with progressive fatigue, jaundice, abdominal distension, and lower limb oedema, alongside deranged liver function tests and coagulopathy.
  • Chronic alcohol misuse with signs of hepatic decompensation, concerning for advanced alcohol-related liver disease with complications.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Alcohol related liver disease"][/sc]
  • Non-alcoholic fatty liver disease (NAFLD)/Non-alcoholic steatohepatitis (NASH) - distinguished by the absence of significant alcohol consumption, often associated with metabolic syndrome, obesity, and type 2 diabetes.
  • Viral hepatitis (Hepatitis B, C) - identified by specific viral serology; can co-exist with ARLD and accelerate disease progression.
  • Autoimmune hepatitis - characterised by elevated autoantibodies (e.g., ANA, SMA, LKM1) and often a good response to immunosuppressive therapy.
  • Haemochromatosis - diagnosed by elevated ferritin, high transferrin saturation, and genetic testing (HFE gene mutations); can cause liver fibrosis and cirrhosis.
  • Drug-induced liver injury (DILI) - history of exposure to hepatotoxic medications, with resolution often occurring after drug withdrawal.
  • Primary biliary cholangitis (PBC) - typically affects middle-aged women, characterised by anti-mitochondrial antibodies (AMA) and cholestatic liver enzyme pattern.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Alcohol related liver disease"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Recognition of acute decompensation (e.g., severe jaundice, ascites, encephalopathy, GI bleeding) and prompt escalation for specialist assessment and supportive care. Management often includes fluid balance, nutritional support, and infection screening.
  • Definitive management: Complete and sustained alcohol abstinence is the most crucial intervention to halt disease progression and allow for liver recovery. This often requires comprehensive psychosocial support and addiction services.
  • Adjunctive management: For severe alcoholic hepatitis (Maddrey DF ≥32), corticosteroid therapy may be considered in the absence of contraindications, to reduce inflammation and improve short-term survival.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Regular clinical review, liver function tests, and imaging are typically warranted to monitor disease progression, assess treatment response, and screen for complications such as hepatocellular carcinoma.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Patients with decompensated cirrhosis should be referred for consideration of liver transplantation if they achieve sustained abstinence and meet other eligibility criteria.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Alcohol related liver disease"][/sc]
  • Portal hypertension: Increased pressure in the portal venous system due to liver fibrosis and architectural distortion, leading to complications such as oesophageal varices and ascites.
  • Oesophageal varices and bleeding: Dilated veins in the oesophagus, prone to rupture and life-threatening haemorrhage, typically occurring in the late phase of advanced cirrhosis.
  • Ascites: Accumulation of fluid in the peritoneal cavity, resulting from portal hypertension and hypoalbuminaemia, often a sign of decompensated liver disease.
  • Hepatic encephalopathy: A neuropsychiatric syndrome caused by the accumulation of toxins (e.g., ammonia) that the liver can no longer metabolise, leading to altered mental status, confusion, and coma, particularly in the late phase.
  • Hepatocellular carcinoma (HCC): A primary liver cancer that is a significant long-term complication of cirrhosis, requiring regular surveillance for early detection.
  • Spontaneous bacterial peritonitis (SBP): Infection of ascitic fluid without an obvious source, a serious complication in patients with ascites and advanced liver disease.
