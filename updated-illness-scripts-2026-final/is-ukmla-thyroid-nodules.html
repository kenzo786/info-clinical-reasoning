Thyroid nodules [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Thyroid nodules"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Thyroid nodules"][/sc]
  • A thyroid nodule is a discrete lesion within the thyroid gland, distinct from the surrounding thyroid parenchyma.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical features and initial management of thyroid nodules, including assessment for malignancy.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Thyroid nodules"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Thyroid nodules"][/sc]
  • Thyroid nodules are common, found in approximately 5% of the adult population on palpation, but in up to 60% on high-resolution ultrasound (NICE).
  • Prevalence increases with age, female sex, iodine deficiency, and radiation exposure to the head and neck.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Thyroid nodules"][/sc]
  • Trigger: Abnormal growth of thyroid cells, often due to genetic mutations or environmental factors, leading to focal hyperplasia or neoplasia.
  • Progression: Continued proliferation of these cells forms a palpable or imaging-detectable nodule, which may be benign (e.g., colloid nodule, adenoma) or malignant (e.g., papillary, follicular carcinoma).
  • Consequence: The nodule can remain asymptomatic, cause compressive symptoms, or, if malignant, metastasise. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Thyroid cancer: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Thyroid cancer"][/sc]
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Thyroid nodules"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Often asymptomatic, discovered incidentally on imaging for other conditions or during routine physical examination.
  • Progressive phase: Gradual enlargement of the nodule, particularly beyond months to years, potentially leading to compressive symptoms such as dysphagia or hoarseness.
  • Late phase: Malignant transformation or significant growth causing overt symptoms, or, if benign, stable or slow growth over many years.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Thyroid nodules"][/sc]
  • Ask about neck lump - often noticed incidentally by patient or family, or during routine examination.
  • Ask about dysphagia - suggests significant nodule size causing oesophageal compression.
  • Ask about hoarseness or voice changes - indicates potential recurrent laryngeal nerve involvement, a red flag for malignancy.
  • Ask about pain - usually absent in benign nodules; sudden pain may suggest haemorrhage into a cyst, while persistent pain can be a feature of malignancy.
  • Ask about symptoms of hyperthyroidism (e.g., palpitations, weight loss) - suggests a toxic nodule.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Thyroid nodules"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - tachycardia or hypertension may indicate hyperthyroidism associated with a toxic nodule.
  • Check for a palpable neck mass - assess size, consistency (firm/hard), mobility, and tenderness.
  • Check for cervical lymphadenopathy - suggests metastatic spread, particularly if firm and non-tender.
  • Check for tracheal deviation - indicates a large nodule causing significant mass effect.
  • Check for signs of hyperthyroidism (e.g., tremor, exophthalmos) - corroborates a toxic nodule.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Thyroid nodules"][/sc]
  • Rapid growth - particularly over weeks to months, raises suspicion for malignancy.
  • Hoarseness/voice change - suggests recurrent laryngeal nerve invasion.
  • Fixed, hard nodule - indicative of invasive malignancy.
  • Cervical lymphadenopathy - especially if ipsilateral to the nodule, points to metastatic disease.
  • History of head/neck irradiation - significant risk factor for thyroid cancer.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Thyroid nodules"][/sc]
  • Older adults: Nodules may be larger and more likely to be malignant, but symptoms of hyperthyroidism can be subtle or masked by other comorbidities.
  • Pregnancy: New or growing nodules require careful evaluation, though fine needle aspiration (FNA) is generally safe. Management may be deferred until after delivery if benign.
  • Immunosuppressed: Increased risk of lymphoma, which can present as a rapidly growing thyroid mass.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Thyroid nodules"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected thyroid nodule: Thyroid function tests (TSH, free T4) to assess for hyper- or hypothyroidism.
  • Imaging - Ultrasound: High-resolution ultrasound of the neck is the initial imaging modality to characterise the nodule (size, number, features suggestive of malignancy).
  • Fine Needle Aspiration (FNA) cytology: Performed under ultrasound guidance for nodules with suspicious features or size, to differentiate benign from malignant lesions.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Consider autoantibodies (e.g., anti-TPO) if autoimmune thyroiditis is suspected.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Thyroid nodules"][/sc]
  • A 45-year-old female presents with a slowly enlarging, non-tender lump in her neck over the past 6 months, incidentally noticed by her husband, with no associated systemic symptoms or voice changes.
  • Middle-aged female with chronic, asymptomatic, non-toxic anterior neck mass.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Thyroid nodules"][/sc]
  • Colloid nodule - most common benign cause, often soft and multi-nodular.
  • Thyroid adenoma - benign follicular neoplasm, typically solitary.
  • Thyroid cyst - fluid-filled, often smooth and compressible.
  • Thyroiditis (e.g., Hashimoto's, subacute) - diffuse or focal inflammation, often painful in subacute forms.
  • Thyroid carcinoma - typically firm, fixed, and may be associated with lymphadenopathy.
  • Parathyroid adenoma - usually posterior to the thyroid, associated with hypercalcaemia.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Thyroid nodules"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Exclude malignancy through risk stratification (ultrasound features, FNA cytology) and assess thyroid function.
  • Definitive management: For benign nodules, surveillance with ultrasound is often sufficient. Surgical excision is considered for symptomatic benign nodules or confirmed/suspected malignancy.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Regular clinical review and ultrasound surveillance for benign nodules to monitor size and characteristics.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Refer to endocrinology or endocrine surgery for suspicious nodules, confirmed malignancy, or compressive symptoms. Advise patients to seek medical attention for rapid growth, voice changes, or difficulty swallowing.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Thyroid nodules"][/sc]
  • Malignancy: The primary concern, leading to potential metastasis and requiring surgical intervention and further oncological management.
  • Compressive symptoms: Large nodules can compress the trachea (dyspnoea), oesophagus (dysphagia), or recurrent laryngeal nerve (hoarseness).
  • Hyperthyroidism: Functioning (toxic) nodules can autonomously produce thyroid hormones, leading to thyrotoxicosis.
  • Haemorrhage into a cyst: Can cause sudden pain and rapid enlargement, usually self-limiting but may require aspiration.
