Burns [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Burns"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Burns"][/sc]
  • A burn is an injury to the skin or other tissues caused by heat, electricity, chemicals, friction, or radiation.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: The candidate must recognise the different classifications of burns (superficial, partial-thickness, full-thickness) and their immediate clinical implications.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Burns"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Burns"][/sc]
  • In the UK, an estimated 250,000 people sustain burn injuries each year, with approximately 175,000 presenting to Emergency Departments annually.
  • Children account for almost 50% of severe burn injuries, with 50-80% of all childhood burns occurring in those under five years of age.
  • Key risk factors include domestic incidents (e.g., scalds from hot liquids in kitchens), workplace hazards (e.g., fire, chemical, electrical exposure), alcoholism, smoking, self-harm, and interpersonal violence.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Burns"][/sc]
  • Trigger: Direct tissue damage caused by thermal, chemical, electrical, or radiation energy, leading to protein denaturation and cell death.
  • Progression: An immediate inflammatory response results in increased capillary permeability, leading to significant fluid shifts from the intravascular to the interstitial space (oedema formation). This can progress to systemic inflammatory response syndrome (SIRS) in severe cases.
  • Consequence: Local tissue necrosis, substantial fluid and electrolyte imbalances, heightened susceptibility to infection due to compromised skin barrier and immune dysfunction, hypermetabolism, and potential multi-organ dysfunction.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Burns"][/sc]
  • Early phase: (Resuscitative/Emergent) Occurs within the first 48-72 hours post-injury, characterised by fluid shifts, oedema formation, and potential for burn shock. Focus is on immediate stabilisation and fluid resuscitation.
  • Progressive phase: (Acute) Extends from 72 hours up to several weeks, depending on burn severity. This phase involves ongoing wound care, infection prevention, and preparation for wound closure (e.g., grafting). Healing of superficial burns typically occurs within 1-2 weeks, while partial-thickness burns may take up to 3 weeks or longer.
  • Late phase: (Rehabilitative/Reconstructive) Can last for months to years. This phase focuses on scar management, functional recovery, and reconstructive surgeries. Complications such as hypertrophic scarring and contractures may develop, particularly beyond 3 weeks if wounds are not closed.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Burns"][/sc]
  • Ask about pain - to assess burn depth (superficial burns are very painful, full-thickness burns may be painless due to nerve damage) and guide analgesia.
  • Ask about blistering - presence indicates partial-thickness injury, absence in a deep burn suggests full-thickness involvement.
  • Ask about difficulty breathing or hoarseness - suggestive of inhalational injury, which can rapidly lead to airway compromise.
  • Ask about mechanism of injury (e.g., flame, scald, chemical, electrical) - helps determine potential depth, associated injuries, and systemic effects.
  • Ask about associated symptoms like headache, dizziness, or nausea - may indicate carbon monoxide poisoning in fire-related burns or systemic effects of severe burns.
  • Ask about itching or altered sensation - common during healing and in areas of nerve damage.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Burns"][/sc]
  • Check skin appearance (redness, blistering, charring) - to estimate burn depth (superficial burns are red and painful, partial-thickness have blisters, full-thickness may appear waxy white or charred and be painless).
  • Check for signs of inhalational injury (singed nasal hairs, facial burns, soot in sputum, hoarseness, stridor) - indicates potential airway compromise requiring urgent assessment.
  • Check capillary refill and peripheral pulses - to assess circulation, especially in circumferential burns which can lead to compartment syndrome.
  • Check vital signs (heart rate, blood pressure, respiratory rate, oxygen saturation) - tachycardia and hypotension may indicate hypovolaemic shock, while tachypnoea can suggest respiratory distress or metabolic acidosis.   • Check neurological status (Glasgow Coma Scale) - to assess for associated head injury, hypoxia, or carbon monoxide poisoning.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Burns"][/sc]
  • Inhalational injury: Singed nasal hairs, facial burns, hoarseness, stridor, or carbonaceous sputum imply potential airway compromise and require urgent assessment and management.
  • Circumferential burns: Burns encircling a limb or the torso can lead to compartment syndrome dueating to vascular compromise and tissue ischaemia, necessitating escharotomy.
  • Full-thickness burns: Waxy white, leathery, or charred appearance, often painless, indicates extensive tissue damage requiring surgical intervention (excision and grafting).
  • Burns to critical areas: Involvement of the face, hands, feet, perineum, or major joints carries a high risk of functional impairment and scarring, warranting specialist burn care.
  • Non-accidental injury: Suspicious burn patterns, inconsistent history, or delayed presentation should raise concerns for safeguarding issues.
  • Associated trauma or co-morbidities: Significant concurrent injuries, pre-existing medical conditions (e.g., cardiac, respiratory disease, diabetes), or extremes of age (very young or elderly) increase the complexity and risk of burn management.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Burns"][/sc]
  • Older adults: More susceptible to burn injuries due to impaired mobility, sensory deficits, and thinner skin, leading to deeper burns with less thermal exposure. They have a higher risk of complications and mortality due to co-morbidities and age-related immune dysfunction.
  • Pregnancy: Burn injuries in pregnant individuals carry increased risks for both mother and foetus, including premature labour, foetal distress, and higher mortality rates, especially with extensive burns. Management requires careful consideration of foetal well-being alongside maternal resuscitation.
  • Immunosuppressed patients: (e.g., those with diabetes, HIV, or on immunosuppressive medications) are at higher risk of infection and delayed wound healing due to compromised immune responses. Their burns may present with less pronounced inflammatory signs, making early diagnosis of infection challenging.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Burns"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected burns: Clinical assessment of burn depth and total body surface area (TBSA) is paramount. This involves visual inspection and palpation. History taking regarding the mechanism of injury is crucial.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Consider blood tests such as full blood count (FBC), urea and electrolytes (U&Es), C-reactive protein (CRP) to assess for systemic inflammation or infection. Arterial blood gas (ABG) may be needed for suspected inhalational injury or metabolic derangement.
  • Imaging - [modality]: Chest X-ray for suspected inhalational injury or pulmonary complications. Advanced imaging like CT scans or MRI are generally not routine for initial burn assessment but may be used for associated trauma or deep tissue involvement. Laser Doppler imaging or hyperspectral imaging can be used in specialist centres for more accurate burn depth assessment.
  • Serial reassessment: Regular reassessment of burn depth and extent is crucial as the appearance can evolve over the first 24-48 hours. This also applies to monitoring for signs of infection or compartment syndrome.
  • Validated scores:
    • Rule of Nines on MDCalc (for estimating TBSA in adults)
    • Parkland Formula for Burns on MDCalc (for fluid resuscitation calculation)
    • RADS (Radiologist's Score) for Smoke Inhalation Injury on MDCalc
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Burns"][/sc]
  • A 45-year-old male presented to the emergency department after sustaining a burn injury 2 hours ago while working on his car, resulting in significant pain and blistering on his right forearm. On examination, there is a painful, erythematous area with intact blisters covering approximately 5% TBSA on the right forearm. Capillary refill is brisk, and sensation is preserved.
  • An adult male with acute, localised, partial-thickness thermal injury, presenting with pain and blistering, requiring assessment for depth, extent, and potential for complications.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Burns"][/sc]
  • Stevens-Johnson Syndrome/Toxic Epidermal Necrolysis (SJS/TEN): Widespread epidermal detachment resembling severe burns, but often preceded by drug exposure and mucosal involvement is prominent.
  • Staphylococcal Scalded Skin Syndrome (SSSS): Superficial blistering and peeling of skin, primarily in children, caused by bacterial toxins, often with a sandpaper-like rash and Nikolsky's sign.
  • Cellulitis/Erysipelas: Localised skin redness, warmth, and swelling, but typically with a clear demarcation and without the initial blistering or tissue destruction characteristic of burns.
  • Contact Dermatitis: Erythema, pruritus, and sometimes blistering in areas of contact with an allergen or irritant, but usually without the immediate tissue damage of a burn.
  • Necrotising Fasciitis: Rapidly progressive soft tissue infection with severe pain, disproportionate to physical findings, and signs of systemic toxicity, often with crepitus or dusky skin changes, differentiating it from a sterile burn.
  • Child abuse (non-accidental injury): Suspicious burn patterns, inconsistent history, or delayed presentation should raise suspicion.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Burns"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Stop the burning process (e.g., cool the burn with running tap water for 20 minutes), remove constricting clothing/jewellery, assess airway, breathing, and circulation (ABCDE approach). Administer high-flow oxygen if inhalational injury is suspected. Provide adequate analgesia.
  • Definitive management: Wound care involves cleaning, debridement of non-viable tissue, and appropriate dressings. Fluid resuscitation using formulas like Parkland is crucial for significant burns (>15% TBSA in adults, >10% in children). Surgical intervention (escharotomy, fasciotomy, excision, and grafting) may be required for deep or circumferential burns.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Close monitoring of vital signs, fluid balance, urine output, and wound appearance is essential. Regular assessment for signs of infection or compartment syndrome. Follow-up care involves scar management, physiotherapy, occupational therapy, and psychological support.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Patients with major burns, inhalational injury, burns to critical areas, or those with significant co-morbidities should be referred to a specialist burn centre. Safety-netting advice should include warning signs of infection, worsening pain, or signs of circulatory compromise.
  • Exam pharmacology reference (UKMLA/MRCGP): Pain management often involves a combination of simple analgesics (e.g., paracetamol) and opioids (e.g., [sc name="jitl-medication" Q="Morphine use in Burns" text="morphine"]). Topical antimicrobials (e.g., [sc name="jitl-medication" Q="Silver sulfadiazine use in Burns" text="silver sulfadiazine"]) may be used for wound infection prophylaxis or treatment. Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Burns"][/sc]
  • Burn Shock: Occurs in the early phase (first 24-48 hours) in extensive burns due to massive fluid shifts from intravascular to interstitial space, leading to hypovolaemia, decreased cardiac output, and organ hypoperfusion. Prognostically significant as it can lead to multi-organ failure if not promptly managed.
  • Infection/Sepsis: A major risk throughout the acute phase, particularly beyond 48 hours, due to loss of skin barrier, immune dysfunction, and necrotic tissue providing a medium for bacterial growth. Diagnostically challenging, but indicated by worsening wound appearance, fever, and systemic inflammatory response syndrome (SIRS) criteria. Prognostically, sepsis is a leading cause of mortality in burn patients.
  • Acute Respiratory Distress Syndrome (ARDS): Can develop in the early phase, especially with inhalational injury, due to direct lung damage and systemic inflammatory response. Diagnostically indicated by progressive hypoxaemia and bilateral infiltrates on chest X-ray. Prognostically, it significantly increases morbidity and mortality.
  • Compartment Syndrome: Can occur in the early phase with circumferential full-thickness burns, leading to vascular compromise and tissue ischaemia in affected limbs or the torso. Diagnostically identified by increasing pain, pallor, paraesthesia, pulselessness, and paralysis. Requires urgent surgical intervention (escharotomy/fasciotomy) to prevent irreversible tissue damage.
  • Hypertrophic Scarring and Contractures: Develop in the progressive and late phases, particularly if wounds take longer than 3 weeks to heal. These are diagnostically visible and palpable, and prognostically can lead to significant functional impairment, disfigurement, and psychological distress, often requiring reconstructive surgery and long-term rehabilitation.
  • Psychological Impact: Can manifest at any phase, including anxiety, depression, post-traumatic stress disorder (PTSD), and body image issues. Diagnostically assessed through patient reporting and psychological evaluation. Prognostically, it can significantly impact long-term quality of life and social reintegration.
