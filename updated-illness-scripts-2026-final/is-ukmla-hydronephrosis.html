Hydronephrosis [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Hydronephrosis"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Hydronephrosis"][/sc]
  • Hydronephrosis is the dilation of the renal pelvis and calyces due to obstruction of urine outflow, leading to increased pressure within the kidney.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the causes, clinical features, and initial management of hydronephrosis.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Hydronephrosis"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Hydronephrosis"][/sc]
  • The prevalence of hydronephrosis in adults is estimated to be around 1-2%, often detected incidentally on imaging.
  • Risk factors include nephrolithiasis (affecting approximately 10-15% of the UK population), benign prostatic hyperplasia (BPH) in older men, and pregnancy.
  • Congenital causes, such as pelvi-ureteric junction (PUJ) obstruction, are the most common cause in children, with an incidence of about 1 in 500 live births.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Hydronephrosis"][/sc]
  • Obstruction to urine flow at any level of the urinary tract (e.g., stone, tumour, stricture) leads to a build-up of urine proximal to the blockage.
  • The increased pressure within the renal pelvis and calyces causes dilation and compression of the renal parenchyma, impairing glomerular filtration and tubular function.
  • Prolonged or severe obstruction can result in progressive renal damage, leading to renal atrophy, fibrosis, and ultimately chronic kidney disease.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Hydronephrosis"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Acute obstruction can cause rapid onset of loin pain and nausea, particularly within hours to a few days, as the kidney distends.
  • Progressive phase: If obstruction persists, symptoms may become less acute over several days to weeks, but progressive renal damage can occur silently, particularly beyond 7-10 days.
  • Late phase: Chronic, unrecognised hydronephrosis can lead to insidious onset of symptoms related to renal impairment, such as fatigue, oedema, and hypertension, particularly beyond several weeks to months.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Hydronephrosis"][/sc]
  • Ask about loin pain - often colicky and radiating to the groin, indicating acute ureteric obstruction.
  • Ask about abdominal pain or flank discomfort - may be dull and persistent, suggesting more gradual or chronic dilation.
  • Ask about changes in micturition - such as frequency, urgency, or reduced urine output, which can point to bladder outlet obstruction.
  • Ask about nausea and vomiting - common with acute renal colic due to visceral pain and vagal stimulation.
  • Ask about fever and chills - highly suggestive of superimposed infection (pyelonephritis), a medical emergency.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Hydronephrosis"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - fever, tachycardia, or hypotension may indicate sepsis, requiring urgent intervention.
  • Check abdominal examination - a palpable flank mass may be present in severe or chronic hydronephrosis, particularly in children.
  • Check costovertebral angle tenderness - elicited by percussion, suggesting renal capsule distension or inflammation.
  • Check suprapubic tenderness - may indicate bladder distension due to lower urinary tract obstruction.
  • Check for oedema - peripheral or facial oedema can be a sign of renal impairment in chronic cases.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Hydronephrosis"][/sc]
  • Fever with loin pain: Suggests infected obstructed kidney (pyonephrosis), a urological emergency requiring urgent decompression.
  • Anuria or oliguria: Indicates bilateral obstruction or obstruction in a solitary kidney, leading to acute kidney injury.
  • Rapidly worsening renal function: Points to significant obstruction causing acute kidney injury, necessitating prompt investigation and relief.
  • New onset hydronephrosis in an older adult: Raises concern for malignancy (e.g., prostate, bladder, gynaecological) as an underlying cause.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Hydronephrosis"][/sc]
  • Older adults: May present with non-specific symptoms like malaise, anorexia, or altered mental status, with pain being less prominent, often due to chronic obstruction from BPH or malignancy.
  • Pregnancy: Physiological hydronephrosis is common, particularly on the right side, due to hormonal effects and uterine compression; however, pathological obstruction (e.g., stone) must be excluded if symptoms are severe or atypical.
  • Children: Often asymptomatic and detected antenatally or incidentally; symptoms, when present, can include recurrent urinary tract infections, abdominal pain, or failure to thrive.
  • Immunosuppressed patients: May have blunted inflammatory responses, leading to delayed presentation of infected hydronephrosis, making early recognition challenging.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Hydronephrosis"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected hydronephrosis: Urinalysis (for haematuria, proteinuria, infection), urine culture, full blood count (for infection/anaemia), urea and electrolytes (for renal function), C-reactive protein (for inflammation).
  • Imaging - Renal ultrasound: The initial imaging modality of choice to confirm hydronephrosis, assess severity, and often identify the level and cause of obstruction (e.g., stones, bladder distension).
  • Imaging - CT KUB (Kidneys, Ureters, Bladder): Non-contrast CT is highly sensitive for detecting urinary tract calculi and can delineate other causes of obstruction.
  • Imaging - CT Urogram or MR Urogram: Considered for more complex cases or when malignancy is suspected, providing detailed anatomical information of the entire urinary tract.
  • Serial reassessment: Regular monitoring of renal function and imaging may be required to assess progression or resolution, particularly in asymptomatic or mild cases.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Hydronephrosis"][/sc]
  • A 55-year-old male presents with acute onset severe left loin pain radiating to the groin, associated with nausea and microscopic haematuria, developing over the past 12 hours.
  • A middle-aged man with acute, severe, colicky flank pain and associated visceral symptoms, suggesting an acute obstructive uropathy.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Hydronephrosis"][/sc]
  • Renal colic (nephrolithiasis) - often presents with acute, severe, colicky loin pain radiating to the groin, but imaging confirms stone.
  • Pyelonephritis - typically presents with fever, loin pain, and dysuria, often with systemic signs of infection.
  • Appendicitis - right-sided abdominal pain, often migrating, with anorexia and vomiting, usually without haematuria.
  • Diverticulitis - left lower quadrant pain, fever, and altered bowel habits, usually without urinary symptoms.
  • Abdominal aortic aneurysm (AAA) - sudden, severe abdominal or back pain, particularly in older adults with cardiovascular risk factors.
  • Ovarian torsion/ectopic pregnancy - acute pelvic pain in women, often with vaginal bleeding or adnexal tenderness.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Hydronephrosis"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Analgesia (e.g., NSAIDs or opioids for renal colic), antiemetics, and assessment for signs of sepsis or acute kidney injury.
  • Definitive management: Relief of obstruction, which may involve ureteric stenting, percutaneous nephrostomy, or surgical removal of the obstructing lesion (e.g., stone extraction, tumour resection).
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Regular assessment of renal function, pain control, and imaging to ensure resolution of hydronephrosis and prevent recurrence.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Advise patients to seek urgent medical attention for worsening pain, fever, reduced urine output, or signs of infection.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Hydronephrosis"][/sc]
  • Pyonephrosis: Infection of the obstructed kidney, leading to pus accumulation, which is a life-threatening condition requiring urgent drainage and antibiotics.
  • Acute kidney injury (AKI): Can result from severe or bilateral obstruction, leading to a rapid decline in renal function, particularly beyond a few days.
  • Chronic kidney disease (CKD): Prolonged or recurrent hydronephrosis can cause irreversible renal damage, leading to progressive loss of kidney function over weeks to months.
  • Sepsis: Systemic inflammatory response to infection, particularly pyonephrosis, which can lead to multi-organ dysfunction and high mortality.
  • Renal atrophy: Long-standing obstruction can cause irreversible shrinkage and damage to the kidney parenchyma.
