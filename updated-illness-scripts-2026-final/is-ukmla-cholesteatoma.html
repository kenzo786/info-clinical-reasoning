Cholesteatoma [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Cholesteatoma"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Cholesteatoma"][/sc]
  • A cholesteatoma is a benign, destructive lesion of keratinising squamous epithelium in the middle ear and/or mastoid air cell spaces, which can expand and erode surrounding bone [1, 2].
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical features of cholesteatoma, understand its potential complications, and identify when to refer for specialist ear, nose, and throat (ENT) assessment.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Cholesteatoma"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Cholesteatoma"][/sc]
  • Cholesteatoma is a rare condition, with an estimated incidence of 7-13 per 100,000 people per year in the UK [2].
  • It is more common in males than females, and typically presents in adults between 30 and 50 years old, though congenital forms exist [2].
  • Risk factors include a history of recurrent ear infections, previous ear surgery, and conditions causing chronic negative middle ear pressure, such as Eustachian tube dysfunction [2].
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Cholesteatoma"][/sc]
  • Trigger: Often initiated by chronic negative pressure in the middle ear, leading to retraction of the tympanic membrane, creating a pocket where desquamated keratinocytes accumulate [2].
  • Progression: The trapped keratinocytes form a growing mass, a "cholesteatoma pearl," which expands and secretes enzymes, causing local bone erosion of the ossicles and mastoid [2]. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Cholesteatoma pearl: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="cholesteatoma pearl"][/sc]
  • Consequence: Progressive destruction can lead to conductive hearing loss, chronic infection, and potential intracranial complications due to erosion into adjacent structures [1, 2].
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Cholesteatoma"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Often asymptomatic or presents with non-specific symptoms like mild hearing loss or intermittent ear discharge, which may be mistaken for recurrent otitis externa [2].
  • Progressive phase: As the cholesteatoma expands, symptoms become more persistent, including persistent foul-smelling otorrhea, progressive conductive hearing loss, and a sensation of ear fullness [1, 2].
  • Late phase: Untreated, the lesion can lead to significant bone erosion, potentially causing complications such as vertigo, facial nerve palsy, or intracranial infection, typically developing over months to years [1, 2].
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Cholesteatoma"][/sc]
  • Ask about persistent, foul-smelling ear discharge - often resistant to conventional antibiotic treatment, indicating chronic infection and possible cholesteatoma [1, 2].
  • Ask about progressive unilateral hearing loss - a common symptom as the cholesteatoma erodes the ossicles, impacting sound conduction [1, 2].
  • Ask about a sensation of ear fullness or pressure - due to the expanding mass within the middle ear [1, 2].
  • Ask about otalgia (ear pain) - typically mild, but can become severe if there is acute infection or significant bone erosion [2].
  • Ask about vertigo or dizziness - suggests involvement of the inner ear or semicircular canals [1, 2].
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Cholesteatoma"][/sc]
  • Check for a visible pearly-white mass or keratin debris in the middle ear, often seen through a tympanic membrane perforation or retraction pocket - this is pathognomonic for cholesteatoma [1, 2]. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Attic perforation: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="attic perforation"][/sc]
  • Check for a tympanic membrane retraction pocket, particularly in the pars flaccida (attic) - this can trap desquamated epithelium and lead to cholesteatoma formation [2].
  • Check for conductive hearing loss on audiometry - indicates ossicular chain disruption or middle ear fluid/mass [1].
  • Check for signs of inflammation or infection, such as erythema or oedema of the ear canal - may indicate secondary infection [2].
  • Check vital signs - usually normal, but fever may indicate acute infection or complications like mastoiditis [1].
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Cholesteatoma"][/sc]
  • Facial nerve palsy - indicates erosion into the facial nerve canal, requiring urgent ENT assessment [1, 2].
  • Persistent vertigo or imbalance - suggests inner ear involvement or labyrinthine fistula, necessitating prompt investigation [1, 2].
  • Severe headache, neck stiffness, or altered mental status - raises concern for intracranial complications such as meningitis or brain abscess, requiring immediate emergency referral [1, 2].
  • Post-aural swelling or tenderness - may indicate mastoiditis, a serious complication of cholesteatoma [1, 2].
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Cholesteatoma"][/sc]
  • Congenital cholesteatoma: Presents in children, often with an intact tympanic membrane and no history of ear infections; may be discovered incidentally or due to hearing loss [2].
  • Cholesteatoma in the elderly: Symptoms may be masked by age-related hearing loss or other comorbidities, leading to delayed diagnosis and potentially more advanced disease [2].
  • Painless presentation: Some cholesteatomas can be asymptomatic for extended periods, only presenting with complications or discovered incidentally during examination for other ear complaints [2]. Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Cholesteatoma"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected cholesteatoma: Otoscopy (often microscopic) to visualise the ear canal and tympanic membrane for retraction pockets, perforations, or a pearly-white mass [1, 2].
  • Imaging - CT scan: High-resolution computed tomography (HRCT) of the temporal bone is crucial to assess the extent of the disease, bone erosion, and involvement of adjacent structures [1, 2]. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="HRCT in cholesteatoma: what does it show?" text="HRCT temporal bone"][/sc]
  • Imaging - MRI scan: Magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) may be used to differentiate cholesteatoma from other middle ear masses and to detect intracranial complications [1].
  • Audiometry: Pure tone audiometry and tympanometry to assess the type and degree of hearing loss and middle ear function [1].
  • Severity/risk tools: No widely validated severity/risk score is routinely used for cholesteatoma in primary care; assessment is primarily based on clinical and imaging findings.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Cholesteatoma"][/sc]
  • A 45-year-old male presents with a 6-month history of persistent, foul-smelling left ear discharge and progressive unilateral hearing loss, unresponsive to multiple courses of oral antibiotics.
  • Chronic otorrhea with conductive hearing loss, concerning for an expanding middle ear mass.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Cholesteatoma"][/sc]
  • Chronic suppurative otitis media (CSOM) - often presents with chronic ear discharge and hearing loss, but typically lacks the pearly-white mass and bone erosion characteristic of cholesteatoma [1].
  • Otitis externa - usually presents with ear pain, itching, and discharge, but the infection is confined to the external ear canal and does not involve the middle ear or bone erosion [2].
  • Foreign body in the ear canal - can cause discharge and discomfort, but a foreign body is usually visible on otoscopy and does not cause progressive bone erosion [2].
  • Tympanosclerosis - white plaques on the tympanic membrane, often following recurrent otitis media, but these are calcified deposits and do not expand or erode bone [1].
  • Middle ear tumour (rare) - can mimic cholesteatoma with hearing loss and discharge, but typically presents with different imaging characteristics and biopsy is diagnostic [1]. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Middle ear tumour vs cholesteatoma: distinguishing features?" text="middle ear tumour"][/sc]
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Cholesteatoma"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Referral to an ENT specialist for definitive diagnosis and management planning. Management of acute infection with topical or oral antibiotics if indicated, though this will not resolve the cholesteatoma itself [1, 2].
  • Definitive management: Surgical excision is the mainstay of treatment to remove the cholesteatoma and prevent further complications. Procedures may include mastoidectomy and tympanoplasty [1, 2].
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Long-term follow-up is essential post-surgery to monitor for recurrence, which can occur even after complete excision [1, 2].
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Patients should be advised to seek urgent medical attention for new neurological symptoms (e.g., facial weakness, severe headache, altered consciousness) or signs of systemic infection [1, 2].
  • Exam pharmacology reference (UKMLA/MRCGP): Topical and oral antibiotics may be used to manage secondary infections associated with cholesteatoma, for example, ciprofloxacin or co-amoxiclav. Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Cholesteatoma"][/sc]
  • Hearing loss: Progressive conductive hearing loss is common due to ossicular chain erosion; sensorineural hearing loss can occur if the inner ear is involved [1, 2].
  • Mastoiditis: Infection and inflammation of the mastoid air cells, presenting with post-aural pain, swelling, and erythema, typically developing over days to weeks if untreated [1, 2].
  • Facial nerve palsy: Erosion of the facial nerve canal can lead to facial weakness or paralysis, which may develop gradually or acutely [1, 2].
  • Labyrinthine fistula: Erosion into the inner ear (labyrinth) can cause persistent vertigo, nystagmus, and sensorineural hearing loss [1, 2].
  • Intracranial complications: Rare but serious, including meningitis, brain abscess, or sigmoid sinus thrombosis, typically occurring in advanced, untreated cases over months to years [1, 2].
