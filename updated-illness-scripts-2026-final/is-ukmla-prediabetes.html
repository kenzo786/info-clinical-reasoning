Prediabetes [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Prediabetes"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Prediabetes"][/sc]
  • A metabolic state characterised by blood glucose levels higher than normal but not yet high enough to be diagnosed as type 2 diabetes.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the diagnostic criteria for prediabetes and its implications for future health.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Prediabetes"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Prediabetes"][/sc]
  • Affects a significant proportion of the UK adult population, with estimates suggesting around 1 in 3 adults may have prediabetes, often undiagnosed.
  • Risk factors include obesity, physical inactivity, family history of type 2 diabetes, increasing age, and certain ethnic backgrounds (e.g., South Asian, African Caribbean).
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Prediabetes"][/sc]
  • Trigger: Insulin resistance develops, often due to genetic predisposition and lifestyle factors such as excess caloric intake and sedentary behaviour.
  • Progression: Pancreatic beta cells initially compensate by increasing insulin production, maintaining normoglycaemia, but eventually become exhausted and fail to produce sufficient insulin.
  • Consequence: Impaired glucose tolerance (IGT) and/or impaired fasting glucose (IFG) result, leading to elevated blood glucose levels that do not meet the criteria for diabetes.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Prediabetes"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Often asymptomatic, with subtle metabolic changes detectable only through screening, particularly beyond several years of unhealthy lifestyle.
  • Progressive phase: Gradual worsening of insulin resistance and beta-cell dysfunction, increasing the risk of developing type 2 diabetes, particularly beyond 5-10 years without intervention.
  • Late phase: High risk of progression to overt type 2 diabetes and associated microvascular and macrovascular complications, particularly beyond 10-15 years.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Prediabetes"][/sc]
  • Ask about fatigue - often non-specific but can indicate metabolic stress.
  • Ask about increased thirst (polydipsia) - may suggest early glucose dysregulation, though less pronounced than in overt diabetes.
  • Ask about frequent urination (polyuria) - similar to thirst, can be an early, subtle sign of osmotic diuresis.
  • Ask about blurred vision - can occur due to osmotic changes in the lens, even with mild hyperglycaemia.
  • Ask about unexplained weight changes - can be a sign of metabolic imbalance.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Prediabetes"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - blood pressure may be elevated, indicating co-existing metabolic syndrome.
  • Check waist circumference - central obesity is a strong indicator of insulin resistance.
  • Check skin for acanthosis nigricans - hyperpigmented, velvety plaques, especially in skin folds, a sign of insulin resistance.
  • Check for signs of peripheral neuropathy - subtle sensory changes can occur even in prediabetes.
  • Check for signs of dyslipidaemia - xanthelasma or corneal arcus may be present, indicating associated cardiovascular risk.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Prediabetes"][/sc]
  • Rapid weight loss - may indicate progression to overt type 1 or type 2 diabetes with significant insulin deficiency.
  • Recurrent infections - particularly skin or genitourinary, suggest significant hyperglycaemia impairing immune function.
  • New onset visual disturbance - warrants urgent ophthalmological review to rule out diabetic retinopathy.
  • Symptoms of peripheral neuropathy - numbness, tingling, or pain in extremities, indicating early nerve damage.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Prediabetes"][/sc]
  • Older adults: May present with non-specific symptoms like cognitive decline or increased falls, rather than classic polyuria/polydipsia, due to blunted physiological responses.
  • Pregnancy: Gestational diabetes is a form of glucose intolerance first recognised during pregnancy, which increases the risk of developing type 2 diabetes later in life.
  • Immunosuppressed: Certain medications (e.g., corticosteroids) can induce or worsen glucose intolerance, leading to prediabetes or overt diabetes.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Prediabetes"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected Prediabetes: Fasting plasma glucose (FPG) 5.5-6.9 mmol/L or HbA1c 42-47 mmol/mol (6.0-6.4%). Oral glucose tolerance test (OGTT) with 2-hour plasma glucose 7.8-11.0 mmol/L.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Consider thyroid function tests if thyroid disease symptoms are present, or liver function tests if liver disease is suspected.
  • Imaging - Abdominal ultrasound: May be considered if non-alcoholic fatty liver disease (NAFLD) is suspected, which is often associated with insulin resistance.
  • Serial reassessment: Annual screening for progression to type 2 diabetes is recommended for individuals with prediabetes.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Prediabetes"][/sc]
  • A 55-year-old obese male presents for a routine health check, reporting mild fatigue and a family history of type 2 diabetes, with a fasting plasma glucose of 6.5 mmol/L and HbA1c of 45 mmol/mol.
  • A middle-aged, overweight male with a genetic predisposition and lifestyle risk factors, presenting with asymptomatic, chronic, mild hyperglycaemia.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Prediabetes"][/sc]
  • Type 2 Diabetes Mellitus - higher FPG (>7.0 mmol/L) or HbA1c (>48 mmol/mol).
  • Physiological stress hyperglycaemia - transient elevation due to acute illness or trauma.
  • Medication-induced hyperglycaemia - certain drugs (e.g., steroids) can elevate glucose.
  • Genetic forms of diabetes (MODY) - often presents at a younger age, with specific genetic mutations.
  • Secondary diabetes - due to pancreatic disease or endocrine disorders.
