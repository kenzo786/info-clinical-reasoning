Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Ectopic pregnancy (Pregnancy and the puerperium)"][/sc]
  • An ectopic pregnancy occurs when a fertilised ovum implants and matures outside the uterine cavity, most commonly in the fallopian tube (tubal ectopic pregnancy accounts for 95% of cases).
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical features, risk factors, and initial management of ectopic pregnancy, including the importance of early diagnosis to prevent life-threatening complications.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Ectopic pregnancy (Pregnancy and the puerperium)"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Ectopic pregnancy (Pregnancy and the puerperium)"][/sc]
  • In the UK, approximately 1 in 90 pregnancies (just over 1%) are ectopic, equating to around 11,000 cases annually [3].
  • Risk factors include previous ectopic pregnancy (10% recurrence risk), pelvic inflammatory disease (PID), previous tubal surgery (e.g., sterilisation), in vitro fertilisation (IVF), and current use of an intrauterine device (IUD) or system (IUS) [3].
  • The risk is highest for pregnant women aged over 35 and in those who smoke [3].
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Ectopic pregnancy (Pregnancy and the puerperium)"][/sc]
  • **Trigger:** Fertilised ovum fails to reach the uterine cavity due to impaired tubal transport, often secondary to structural damage (e.g., inflammation from PID, previous surgery) or functional impairment of the fallopian tube cilia.
  • **Progression:** The blastocyst implants in an ectopic site, most commonly the ampulla of the fallopian tube, where it begins to grow and invade the surrounding tissue.
  • **Consequence:** As the pregnancy grows, the ectopic site (e.g., fallopian tube) distends and can rupture, leading to significant intra-abdominal haemorrhage and haemodynamic instability. Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Ectopic pregnancy (Pregnancy and the puerperium)"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Symptoms typically develop between 4 and 12 weeks of gestation, often presenting as non-specific abdominal pain or vaginal bleeding, which can be mistaken for early miscarriage or normal pregnancy symptoms.
  • Progressive phase: As the ectopic pregnancy grows, pain may become more localised and severe. If rupture occurs, this leads to acute, sharp, sudden, and intense abdominal pain, often accompanied by signs of hypovolaemic shock.
  • Late phase: Untreated rupture results in ongoing internal haemorrhage, leading to profound haemodynamic instability, collapse, and potentially death. This progression can be rapid, particularly beyond 6-8 weeks gestation.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Ectopic pregnancy (Pregnancy and the puerperium)"][/sc]
  • Ask about missed period and other signs of pregnancy - to establish pregnancy status and gestational age.
  • Ask about unilateral lower abdominal pain - often described as a dull ache or sharp, stabbing pain, which may be persistent or intermittent and can indicate tubal distension.
  • Ask about vaginal bleeding or brown watery discharge - often lighter or darker than a normal period, and can be intermittent, indicating decidual shedding.
  • Ask about shoulder tip pain - referred pain from diaphragmatic irritation due to intra-abdominal bleeding, a key indicator of rupture.
  • Ask about discomfort with micturition or defecation - due to peritoneal irritation from blood in the Pouch of Douglas.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Ectopic pregnancy (Pregnancy and the puerperium)"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - tachycardia and hypotension indicate hypovolaemia, suggesting significant internal bleeding and impending or actual rupture.
  • Check abdominal tenderness and guarding - localised tenderness, particularly in one iliac fossa, or generalised guarding and rebound tenderness suggest peritoneal irritation or rupture.
  • Check cervical motion tenderness - pain on moving the cervix during bimanual examination, indicating peritoneal inflammation.
  • Check adnexal tenderness or mass - palpation of a tender adnexal mass on bimanual examination may indicate the ectopic pregnancy itself.
  • Check pallor - indicates significant blood loss and anaemia.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Ectopic pregnancy (Pregnancy and the puerperium)"][/sc]
  • Haemodynamic instability (tachycardia, hypotension): Indicates significant internal haemorrhage, requiring immediate resuscitation and surgical intervention to prevent cardiovascular collapse.
  • Acute, severe, sudden-onset abdominal pain: Highly suggestive of tubal rupture, a surgical emergency. This often occurs rapidly, particularly beyond 6-8 weeks gestation.
  • Peritonism (guarding, rebound tenderness): Signifies widespread peritoneal irritation due to significant intra-abdominal bleeding, necessitating urgent surgical assessment.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Ectopic pregnancy (Pregnancy and the puerperium)"][/sc]
  • Pregnancy of unknown location (PUL): A positive pregnancy test with no intrauterine or extrauterine pregnancy visualised on transvaginal ultrasound, requiring serial beta-hCG measurements and close follow-up to exclude ectopic pregnancy.
  • Asymptomatic presentation: Some ectopic pregnancies are detected incidentally during routine early pregnancy scans, highlighting the importance of systematic screening in at-risk groups.
  • Chronic ectopic pregnancy: Rare presentation where the ectopic pregnancy has been present for an extended period, often leading to a pelvic mass and vague symptoms due to slow leakage of blood.
  • Heterotopic pregnancy: Co-existence of an intrauterine and an ectopic pregnancy, often challenging to diagnose as intrauterine pregnancy symptoms can mask the ectopic. Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Ectopic pregnancy (Pregnancy and the puerperium)"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected ectopic pregnancy:
    • Serum beta-human chorionic gonadotropin (beta-hCG): Quantitative measurement to assess pregnancy viability and trend. A suboptimal rise (less than 63% in 48 hours) is highly suspicious for ectopic or failing intrauterine pregnancy.
    • Transvaginal ultrasound (TVS): Primary imaging modality to visualise intrauterine or extrauterine pregnancy. Absence of an intrauterine pregnancy with beta-hCG above the [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Discriminatory zone: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="discriminatory zone"][/sc] (typically 1500-2000 IU/L) is highly suggestive of ectopic pregnancy.
  • Severity/risk tools: No validated severity/risk score is routinely used for ectopic pregnancy in UK clinical practice.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Consider full blood count (FBC) to assess for anaemia, C-reactive protein (CRP) for infection (e.g., PID), and urine dipstick for urinary tract infection.
  • Serial reassessment: Serial beta-hCG measurements and repeat TVS are crucial for managing pregnancies of unknown location and monitoring expectant management.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Ectopic pregnancy (Pregnancy and the puerperium)"][/sc]
  • **Miscarriage:** Typically presents with central abdominal pain and heavier vaginal bleeding, often with passage of clots or tissue, and declining beta-hCG.
  • **Pelvic Inflammatory Disease (PID):** Characterised by bilateral lower abdominal pain, fever, vaginal discharge, and cervical motion tenderness, usually without a positive pregnancy test.
  • **Ovarian cyst rupture/torsion:** Sudden onset, severe unilateral lower abdominal pain, often sharp or colicky, but typically without vaginal bleeding or positive pregnancy test. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Ovarian torsion: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Ovarian torsion"][/sc]
  • **Appendicitis:** Right iliac fossa pain, often migrating from periumbilical region, associated with anorexia, nausea, vomiting, and fever, usually without vaginal bleeding.
  • **Urinary Tract Infection (UTI):** Dysuria, frequency, urgency, and suprapubic pain, typically without vaginal bleeding or shoulder tip pain.
  • **Gastroenteritis:** Diffuse abdominal pain, nausea, vomiting, and diarrhoea, usually without vaginal bleeding or signs of hypovolaemia unless severe.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Ectopic pregnancy (Pregnancy and the puerperium)"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Recognition of haemodynamic instability, urgent escalation to senior obstetric/gynaecology team, and initiation of supportive care (e.g., IV fluids, blood products if required).
  • Definitive management:
    • **Surgical management:** Often involves laparoscopic salpingectomy (removal of the affected fallopian tube) for ruptured ectopic pregnancies or those with significant symptoms/size. Salpingostomy (removal of ectopic tissue with tubal preservation) may be considered in specific cases.
    • **Medical management:** Single-dose intramuscular methotrexate may be considered for stable patients with unruptured ectopic pregnancies, small size, and low beta-hCG levels.
    • **Expectant management:** Close monitoring with serial beta-hCG may be appropriate for highly selected, asymptomatic patients with very low and declining beta-hCG levels.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Serial beta-hCG measurements until non-pregnant levels are reached, and close monitoring for symptoms of rupture or treatment failure.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Patients managed medically or expectantly must be counselled on red flag symptoms and advised to seek immediate medical attention if these develop.
  • Exam pharmacology reference (UKMLA/MRCGP):

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Ectopic pregnancy (Pregnancy and the puerperium)"][/sc]
  • Tubal rupture and haemorrhage: The most life-threatening complication, occurring rapidly as the pregnancy grows, leading to acute abdominal pain, hypovolaemic shock, and potentially death if not promptly managed.
  • Infertility: Damage to the fallopian tubes, either from the ectopic pregnancy itself or surgical management (especially salpingectomy), can increase the risk of future infertility or recurrent ectopic pregnancy.
  • Recurrent ectopic pregnancy: A significant risk (around 10%) after a previous ectopic pregnancy, highlighting the need for early pregnancy assessment in subsequent gestations.
  • Psychological distress: The loss of a pregnancy, coupled with the medical emergency and potential impact on future fertility, can lead to significant emotional and psychological trauma.
