Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="COVID-19 (Infections)"][/sc]
  • COVID-19 is an acute respiratory illness caused by infection with the SARS-CoV-2 virus, ranging from asymptomatic to severe disease with multi-organ involvement.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the varied clinical presentations of COVID-19, identify individuals at risk of severe disease, and understand the principles of management and public health implications.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="COVID-19 (Infections)"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="COVID-19 (Infections)"][/sc]
  • Globally, as of March 2026, over 700 million confirmed cases and 7 million deaths have been reported, with significant regional variations in incidence and mortality [1].
  • In the UK, seroprevalence studies indicate widespread exposure, with successive waves driven by new variants impacting different age groups and vaccination statuses [2]. Risk factors for severe disease include older age, male sex, obesity, and underlying comorbidities such as chronic respiratory disease, cardiovascular disease, diabetes, and immunosuppression [3].
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="COVID-19 (Infections)"][/sc]
  • Trigger: SARS-CoV-2 primarily infects host cells via the angiotensin-converting enzyme 2 (ACE2) receptor, leading to viral replication and direct cellular damage, particularly in the respiratory tract.
  • Progression: The infection can trigger a dysregulated immune response, leading to excessive inflammation, cytokine release, and microvascular thrombosis, contributing to acute respiratory distress syndrome (ARDS) and multi-organ dysfunction. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Cytokine storm: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Cytokine storm"][/sc]
  • Consequence: Beyond acute illness, persistent viral presence, immune dysregulation, and organ damage can lead to long-term sequelae, collectively known as Long COVID.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="COVID-19 (Infections)"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Symptoms typically appear 2-14 days post-exposure (median 5-6 days). Initial symptoms are often mild and non-specific, including fever, cough, fatigue, and anosmia.
  • Progressive phase: For a subset of individuals, symptoms may worsen around 5-10 days after onset, with increasing dyspnoea, hypoxaemia, and signs of pneumonia, particularly beyond 7 days.
  • Late phase: Recovery from acute illness can vary. Some individuals experience persistent or new symptoms for weeks or months after the initial infection, defining Long COVID, particularly beyond 4 weeks.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="COVID-19 (Infections)"][/sc]
  • Ask about fever/chills - indicates systemic inflammatory response and is a common initial symptom.
  • Ask about new continuous cough - a key respiratory symptom, often dry, indicating lower respiratory tract involvement.
  • Ask about anosmia or ageusia - highly specific symptoms, often preceding other respiratory symptoms, aiding early diagnosis.
  • Ask about dyspnoea or shortness of breath - a critical symptom indicating worsening respiratory function and potential progression to severe disease.
  • Ask about fatigue and myalgia - common systemic symptoms, often persistent in Long COVID.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="COVID-19 (Infections)"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs (temperature, heart rate, respiratory rate, oxygen saturation) - essential for assessing severity and identifying hypoxaemia or tachypnoea.
  • Check respiratory examination (crackles, wheeze, reduced breath sounds) - may indicate pneumonia or ARDS.
  • Check signs of dehydration or poor perfusion - can occur with severe illness and reduced oral intake.
  • Check for rash or skin changes - various dermatological manifestations have been reported, though less common.
  • Check for altered mental status - may indicate severe hypoxaemia, sepsis, or neurological complications. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Hypoxaemia: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Hypoxaemia"][/sc]
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="COVID-19 (Infections)"][/sc]
  • Severe dyspnoea: Indicates significant respiratory compromise requiring urgent assessment and potential advanced respiratory support.
  • Persistent chest pain or pressure: May suggest cardiac involvement (myocarditis, pericarditis) or pulmonary embolism, requiring immediate investigation.
  • New confusion or altered consciousness: Signifies severe systemic illness, hypoxaemia, or neurological complications, necessitating urgent medical attention.
  • Inability to stay awake: A critical sign of severe illness and organ dysfunction, requiring immediate hospital admission.
  • Oxygen saturation <92% on air: Objective evidence of significant hypoxaemia, indicating severe respiratory failure and need for oxygen therapy.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="COVID-19 (Infections)"][/sc]
  • Older adults: May present with non-specific symptoms like falls, delirium, or functional decline without typical fever or cough, making diagnosis challenging.
  • Children: Often asymptomatic or mild, but can present with multisystem inflammatory syndrome in children (MIS-C) or Kawasaki-like disease, requiring high index of suspicion.
  • Immunosuppressed individuals: May have prolonged viral shedding, atypical or muted inflammatory responses, and higher risk of severe or persistent disease.
  • Long COVID: Persistent symptoms (fatigue, brain fog, dyspnoea, palpitations) weeks to months after acute infection, even in those with mild initial illness. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Long COVID: definition, symptoms, and management" text="Long COVID"][/sc]
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="COVID-19 (Infections)"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected COVID-19: Nucleic acid amplification tests (NAATs), such as PCR, on respiratory samples (nasopharyngeal/oropharyngeal swabs) are the gold standard for confirming acute infection. Rapid antigen tests can be used for screening.
  • Severity/risk tools: Consider using a validated risk stratification tool to assess prognosis and guide management, such as the CURB-65 score on MDCalc for pneumonia severity, though specific COVID-19 scores are also available.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Full blood count, C-reactive protein, urea and electrolytes, liver function tests, blood cultures, and influenza/RSV testing to rule out other infections or assess organ function.
  • Imaging - Chest X-ray: May show bilateral interstitial infiltrates or consolidation in pneumonia.
  • Imaging - CT Chest: More sensitive for detecting ground-glass opacities and consolidations, especially in severe disease.
  • Serial reassessment: Regular monitoring of oxygen saturation, respiratory rate, and clinical status is crucial to detect deterioration. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Ground-glass opacities: what are they and why do they matter clinically?" text="Ground-glass opacities"][/sc]
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="COVID-19 (Infections)"][/sc]
  • A 68-year-old male with a history of type 2 diabetes presents with 5 days of fever, new continuous cough, and increasing shortness of breath, with oxygen saturation of 90% on air.
  • Acute viral pneumonitis with systemic inflammatory response, concerning for severe COVID-19 requiring hospitalisation and respiratory support.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="COVID-19 (Infections)"][/sc]
  • Influenza - similar respiratory symptoms but often with more abrupt onset and less prominent anosmia.
  • Bacterial pneumonia - typically presents with productive cough, focal chest signs, and higher procalcitonin levels.
  • Acute bronchitis - usually milder, self-limiting, and without significant dyspnoea or hypoxaemia.
  • Pulmonary embolism - sudden onset dyspnoea and pleuritic chest pain, often with risk factors for thrombosis.
  • Acute exacerbation of COPD/asthma - history of underlying disease, often triggered by infection or environmental factors. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Acute respiratory distress syndrome (ARDS): definition, causes, and clinical features" text="ARDS"][/sc]
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="COVID-19 (Infections)"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Early recognition of severe disease, escalation to appropriate care settings, supportive oxygen therapy to maintain saturations, and close monitoring of vital signs.
  • Definitive management: Antiviral treatments such as [sc name="jitl-medication" Q="Nirmatrelvir/ritonavir use in COVID-19" text="nirmatrelvir/ritonavir"] may be considered for high-risk individuals with mild-to-moderate COVID-19 to prevent progression to severe disease. Dexamethasone is often used in hospitalised patients requiring oxygen.
  • Adjunctive management: Supportive care includes fluid management, nutritional support, and management of comorbidities. Prophylactic anticoagulation may be considered in hospitalised patients.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Regular assessment for clinical deterioration, monitoring for complications, and post-discharge follow-up, particularly for those with Long COVID symptoms.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Clear advice on when to seek urgent medical review for worsening symptoms (e.g., increasing dyspnoea, chest pain, confusion).

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="COVID-19 (Infections)"][/sc]
  • Acute Respiratory Distress Syndrome (ARDS): Severe lung inflammation leading to impaired gas exchange, often requiring mechanical ventilation, particularly in the progressive phase of illness.
  • Thromboembolic events: Increased risk of deep vein thrombosis (DVT), pulmonary embolism (PE), and arterial thrombosis due to hypercoagulability, which can occur during the progressive and late phases.
  • Myocarditis/Pericarditis: Inflammation of the heart muscle or lining, leading to cardiac dysfunction, arrhythmias, or chest pain, potentially occurring in the progressive phase or as a post-acute sequela.
  • Long COVID: A constellation of persistent symptoms (e.g., fatigue, cognitive dysfunction, dyspnoea, myalgia) lasting weeks to months after acute infection, impacting quality of life, particularly beyond 4 weeks.
  • Secondary bacterial infections: Increased susceptibility to bacterial pneumonia or sepsis, especially in hospitalised patients, complicating recovery.
