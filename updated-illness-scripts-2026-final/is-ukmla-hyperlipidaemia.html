Hyperlipidaemia [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Hyperlipidaemia"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Hyperlipidaemia"][/sc]
  • Hyperlipidaemia refers to raised levels of one or more of total cholesterol (TC), low-density lipoprotein cholesterol (LDL-C) and triglycerides (TG) in the blood.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise elevated lipid levels and their association with cardiovascular risk.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Hyperlipidaemia"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Hyperlipidaemia"][/sc]
  • Approximately 6 in 10 adults in England have serum cholesterol levels above 5 mmol/L [2].
  • Heterozygous familial hypercholesterolaemia (FH) affects between 1 in 250 and 1 in 500 individuals in the UK [2].
  • Risk factors include genetic predisposition, hypothyroidism, type 2 diabetes mellitus, chronic kidney disease, abdominal obesity, and excessive alcohol consumption [2].
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Hyperlipidaemia"][/sc]
  • Elevated levels of low-density lipoprotein (LDL) cholesterol initiate the accumulation of lipids within the arterial walls, leading to endothelial dysfunction and the formation of fatty streaks.
  • Progression involves the recruitment of inflammatory cells, smooth muscle cell proliferation, and the development of atherosclerotic plaques, narrowing arterial lumens.
  • Plaque rupture and subsequent thrombosis can acutely occlude blood vessels, resulting in critical ischaemic events such as myocardial infarction or stroke.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Hyperlipidaemia"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Hyperlipidaemia often develops insidiously over years, with initial asymptomatic elevation of lipid levels, particularly beyond early adulthood.
  • Progressive phase: Over decades, sustained elevated lipid levels contribute to the gradual development and progression of atherosclerosis, often becoming clinically apparent beyond middle age.
  • Late phase: Significant cardiovascular events, such as myocardial infarction or stroke, typically manifest in later life, particularly beyond several decades of uncontrolled hyperlipidaemia.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Hyperlipidaemia"][/sc]
  • Ask about chest pain - may indicate underlying coronary artery disease, a common complication of hyperlipidaemia.
  • Ask about leg pain on exertion (claudication) - suggests peripheral arterial disease, another atherosclerotic manifestation.
  • Ask about sudden, severe abdominal pain - can be a symptom of acute pancreatitis, especially with very high triglyceride levels.
  • Ask about visual disturbances - may be associated with xanthelasma or corneal arcus, though often asymptomatic.
  • Ask about family history of early cardiovascular disease - crucial for identifying familial hyperlipidaemia.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Hyperlipidaemia"][/sc]
  • Check tendon xanthomata - hard, painless nodules, particularly over Achilles tendons, pathognomonic of familial hypercholesterolaemia.
  • Check xanthelasma - lipid-laden nodules on eyelids, indicating underlying lipid dysregulation.
  • Check premature corneal arcus - bluish rims around the irises, suggesting hyperlipidaemia in younger individuals.
  • Check vital signs and anthropometry - elevated blood pressure and increased body mass index (BMI) are common comorbidities and risk factors for cardiovascular disease in hyperlipidaemia.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Hyperlipidaemia"][/sc]
  • Sudden severe abdominal pain: May indicate acute pancreatitis, a life-threatening complication of severe hypertriglyceridaemia.
  • New onset neurological deficit: Suggests an acute cerebrovascular event (stroke or TIA) due to accelerated atherosclerosis.
  • Acute chest pain: Could signify acute coronary syndrome (myocardial infarction or unstable angina) from atherosclerotic plaque rupture.
  • Rapidly progressive peripheral ischaemia: Implies critical limb ischaemia, a severe manifestation of peripheral arterial disease.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Hyperlipidaemia"][/sc]
  • Older adults: Hyperlipidaemia may be less aggressively managed due to concerns about polypharmacy and perceived reduced benefit, despite continued cardiovascular risk.
  • Pregnancy: Physiological lipid changes can mask or exacerbate underlying hyperlipidaemia, requiring careful monitoring and management to avoid adverse maternal and foetal outcomes.
  • Immunosuppressed patients: Certain immunosuppressants can induce or worsen hyperlipidaemia, necessitating vigilant lipid monitoring and tailored management strategies.
  • Children and adolescents: Familial hypercholesterolaemia can present in childhood, often with a strong family history of early cardiovascular disease, requiring early diagnosis and intervention.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Hyperlipidaemia"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected hyperlipidaemia: Fasting lipid profile (total cholesterol, LDL-C, HDL-C, triglycerides) to assess lipid levels and calculate cardiovascular risk (e.g., using QRISK2 score).
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Thyroid function tests (TSH, T4) for hypothyroidism, liver function tests (AST, ALT, ALP, bilirubin) for liver disease, renal function tests (urea, creatinine) for chronic kidney disease, and HbA1c for diabetes mellitus.
  • Imaging - Carotid ultrasound: To assess for carotid artery atherosclerosis and plaque burden in high-risk individuals.
  • Validated scores:
    • Simon Broome Diagnostic Criteria for FH on MDCalc
    • Dutch Criteria for Familial Hypercholesterolemia (FH) on MDCalc
  • Serial reassessment: Regular monitoring of lipid profiles to assess treatment efficacy and adherence, typically every 3-6 months initially, then annually once stable.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Hyperlipidaemia"][/sc]
  • A 55-year-old male presents for a routine health check, reporting no specific symptoms but with a family history of premature coronary artery disease and a lipid profile showing elevated LDL cholesterol.
  • Asymptomatic, middle-aged male with a strong family history of cardiovascular disease and biochemical evidence of chronic lipid dysregulation, requiring risk stratification and management.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Hyperlipidaemia"][/sc]
  • Familial Hypercholesterolaemia (FH) - strong family history of premature cardiovascular disease, tendon xanthomata.
  • Secondary Hyperlipidaemia (e.g., hypothyroidism) - presence of underlying medical conditions or medication use.
  • Metabolic Syndrome - constellation of risk factors including central obesity, hypertension, insulin resistance, and dyslipidaemia.
  • Type 2 Diabetes Mellitus - often associated with dyslipidaemia, particularly hypertriglyceridaemia.
  • Nephrotic Syndrome - proteinuria and hypoalbuminaemia alongside elevated lipid levels.
  • Cholestatic Liver Disease - elevated alkaline phosphatase and bilirubin, often with hypercholesterolaemia.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Hyperlipidaemia"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Address any acute complications such as acute pancreatitis from severe hypertriglyceridaemia.
  • Definitive management:
    • Lifestyle modifications: dietary changes (low saturated fat, high fruit/vegetable intake), regular exercise (at least 150 minutes/week), reduction of alcohol intake, and smoking cessation.
    • Lipid-lowering medications: often initiated based on cardiovascular risk assessment.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Regular lipid profile monitoring to assess treatment response and adherence, with adjustments to therapy as needed.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Referral to specialist lipid clinics for complex cases, familial hypercholesterolaemia, or inadequate response to conventional therapy.
  • Exam pharmacology reference (UKMLA/MRCGP):
    • Statins (e.g., [sc name="jitl-medication" Q="Atorvastatin use in Hyperlipidaemia" text="atorvastatin"][/sc], [sc name="jitl-medication" Q="Simvastatin use in Hyperlipidaemia" text="simvastatin"][/sc]): HMG-CoA reductase inhibitors, reducing cholesterol synthesis.
    • Ezetimibe: Inhibits intestinal absorption of cholesterol.
    • Fibrates (e.g., [sc name="jitl-medication" Q="Fenofibrate use in Hyperlipidaemia" text="fenofibrate"][/sc]): Activates PPAR-alpha to increase triglyceride clearance.
    • PCSK9 inhibitors (e.g., [sc name="jitl-medication" Q="Alirocumab use in Hyperlipidaemia" text="alirocumab"][/sc], [sc name="jitl-medication" Q="Evolocumab use in Hyperlipidaemia" text="evolocumab"][/sc]): Increase LDL receptor availability, enhancing LDL clearance.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Hyperlipidaemia"][/sc]
  • Atherosclerotic cardiovascular disease: Chronic elevation of lipids leads to plaque formation, resulting in conditions like coronary artery disease, peripheral arterial disease, and cerebrovascular disease.
  • Acute pancreatitis: Severely elevated triglyceride levels can trigger acute inflammation of the pancreas, a medical emergency.
  • Xanthomatosis: Deposition of cholesterol in tissues, manifesting as tendon xanthomata or xanthelasma, indicating significant lipid accumulation.
  • Eruptive xanthomas: Small, yellowish-red papules that appear suddenly, particularly with very high triglyceride levels.
  • Hepatic steatosis: Accumulation of fat in the liver, which can progress to non-alcoholic steatohepatitis (NASH) and cirrhosis.
