Myalgic encephalomyelitis (ME)/ chronic fatigue syndrome (CFS) [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Myalgic encephalomyelitis (ME)/ chronic fatigue syndrome (CFS)"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Myalgic encephalomyelitis (ME)/ chronic fatigue syndrome (CFS)"][/sc]
  • Myalgic encephalomyelitis (ME), also known as chronic fatigue syndrome (CFS), is a complex, long-term, fluctuating condition characterised by profound fatigue not improved by rest, post-exertional malaise (PEM), unrefreshing sleep, and a range of other symptoms affecting multiple body systems.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise and approach the diagnosis and management of Myalgic encephalomyelitis (ME)/chronic fatigue syndrome (CFS), understanding its multi-systemic nature and the impact on patients' lives.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Myalgic encephalomyelitis (ME)/ chronic fatigue syndrome (CFS)"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Myalgic encephalomyelitis (ME)/ chronic fatigue syndrome (CFS)"][/sc]
  • Estimates suggest that approximately 390,000 to 404,000 people in the UK are affected by ME/CFS, with a higher prevalence in females (up to 0.92%) compared to males (up to 0.25%).
  • While the exact cause is unknown, common risk factors include post-infectious triggers (e.g., viral or bacterial infections), immune system dysfunction, genetic predisposition, and hormonal imbalances.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Myalgic encephalomyelitis (ME)/ chronic fatigue syndrome (CFS)"][/sc]
  • Trigger: ME/CFS often follows an acute trigger, most commonly a viral infection (e.g., Epstein-Barr virus, SARS-CoV-2), but can also be initiated by bacterial infections, physical trauma, or significant psychological stress.
  • Progression: The condition involves a complex interplay of immune dysfunction, neuroinflammation, mitochondrial abnormalities leading to impaired energy metabolism, and dysregulation of the autonomic nervous system. This multi-system involvement contributes to the diverse and fluctuating symptom profile. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Neuroinflammation in ME/CFS: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Neuroinflammation"] [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Mitochondrial dysfunction in ME/CFS: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Mitochondrial dysfunction"]
  • Consequence: The primary consequence is post-exertional malaise (PEM), a disproportionate and prolonged worsening of symptoms following even minor physical or mental exertion. Other consequences include persistent fatigue, unrefreshing sleep, cognitive impairment (brain fog), and orthostatic intolerance.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Myalgic encephalomyelitis (ME)/ chronic fatigue syndrome (CFS)"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Onset can be acute, often following an infection, or gradual. Initial symptoms typically include profound fatigue, flu-like symptoms, and cognitive difficulties. This phase may last from several months to up to two years.
  • Progressive phase: The illness often fluctuates, but for many, symptoms can become more severe and debilitating over time, particularly with inadequate management or repeated overexertion leading to sustained post-exertional malaise.
  • Late phase: In severe cases, individuals may experience significant functional impairment, leading to prolonged periods of bed-bound or house-bound states, with profound limitations on daily activities and quality of life.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Myalgic encephalomyelitis (ME)/ chronic fatigue syndrome (CFS)"][/sc]
  • Ask about post-exertional malaise (PEM) - a cardinal symptom where physical or mental exertion leads to a disproportionate and prolonged worsening of symptoms, often delayed by 12-48 hours.
  • Ask about profound fatigue - persistent and debilitating tiredness that is not relieved by rest and significantly impairs daily functioning.
  • Ask about unrefreshing sleep - despite adequate sleep duration, individuals do not feel rested or restored upon waking.
  • Ask about cognitive dysfunction - often described as 'brain fog', encompassing difficulties with memory, concentration, information processing, and executive function.
  • Ask about orthostatic intolerance - symptoms such as dizziness, lightheadedness, presyncope, or syncope that worsen upon standing and improve upon lying down.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Myalgic encephalomyelitis (ME)/ chronic fatigue syndrome (CFS)"][/sc]
  • Check orthostatic vital signs (blood pressure and heart rate in supine and standing positions) - to identify postural orthostatic tachycardia syndrome (POTS) or orthostatic hypotension, indicative of autonomic dysfunction. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Orthostatic intolerance in ME/CFS: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Orthostatic intolerance"]
  • Check general appearance - for signs of profound fatigue, discomfort, or distress, which may not be immediately apparent but are often reported by patients.
  • Check neurological examination - for subtle signs of cognitive slowing or difficulty with attention, which can be observed during conversation or simple tasks.
  • Check musculoskeletal system - for diffuse tenderness or myalgia, which can be a feature of the condition.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Myalgic encephalomyelitis (ME)/ chronic fatigue syndrome (CFS)"][/sc]
  • Unexplained weight loss - may indicate malignancy, malabsorption, or other severe chronic diseases that can mimic fatigue.
  • Focal neurological deficits - suggests an alternative neurological disorder such as multiple sclerosis, stroke, or a brain tumour, rather than ME/CFS.
  • New or worsening severe localised pain - warrants investigation for inflammatory conditions, infections, or malignancy, especially if not typical of ME/CFS myalgia.
  • Significant lymphadenopathy or organomegaly - these signs of systemic illness require exclusion of underlying infections, autoimmune diseases, or haematological malignancies.
  • Persistent or recurrent fever without clear explanation - necessitates investigation for ongoing infection, inflammatory conditions, or malignancy.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Myalgic encephalomyelitis (ME)/ chronic fatigue syndrome (CFS)"][/sc]
  • Children and Adolescents: Presentation may be less typical, with prominent symptoms such as recurrent abdominal pain, headaches, or school absenteeism due to fatigue and cognitive difficulties, which may be misattributed to psychological factors.
  • Elderly Patients: Symptoms of ME/CFS in older adults may be overlooked or misdiagnosed as normal ageing, sarcopenia, or exacerbations of existing chronic conditions, leading to diagnostic delays.
  • Co-morbid Conditions: The presence of co-occurring conditions such as fibromyalgia, postural orthostatic tachycardia syndrome (POTS), or irritable bowel syndrome (IBS) can complicate the clinical picture, making it challenging to delineate symptoms specific to ME/CFS.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Myalgic encephalomyelitis (ME)/ chronic fatigue syndrome (CFS)"][/sc]
  • Core investigations:
    • Full blood count, erythrocyte sedimentation rate (ESR), C-reactive protein (CRP) - to exclude inflammatory conditions or anaemia.
    • Kidney and liver function tests, thyroid function tests (TSH, free T4) - to rule out organ dysfunction or thyroid disorders.
    • Electrolytes, calcium, phosphate, glucose - to exclude metabolic disturbances.
    • Vitamin B12 and folate levels, ferritin - to assess for deficiencies.
    • Creatine kinase - to exclude muscle damage or myositis.
    • Urinalysis - to screen for renal disease or infection.
  • Imaging:
    • Routine imaging (e.g., MRI brain) is generally not recommended for ME/CFS diagnosis but may be considered if focal neurological signs or other red flags are present to exclude alternative diagnoses.
  • If alternative diagnoses are suspected:
    • Autoimmune screen (e.g., ANA, ENA, rheumatoid factor) - if connective tissue disease or autoimmune conditions are suspected.
    • Serology for specific infections (e.g., Lyme disease, HIV, hepatitis) - if exposure history or clinical picture suggests.
    • Sleep study - if primary sleep disorders (e.g., sleep apnoea) are suspected as a cause of fatigue.
  • Serial reassessment:
    • Regular clinical review to monitor symptoms, assess functional impact, and re-evaluate for emerging red flags or alternative diagnoses.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Myalgic encephalomyelitis (ME)/ chronic fatigue syndrome (CFS)"][/sc]
  • A previously active individual presents with six months of debilitating fatigue, significantly worsened by minimal exertion, unrefreshing sleep, and difficulties with concentration and memory, following a viral illness.
  • Semantic qualifiers: Chronic, debilitating, post-exertional malaise, unrefreshing sleep, cognitive impairment, multi-systemic.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Myalgic encephalomyelitis (ME)/ chronic fatigue syndrome (CFS)"][/sc]
  • Depression: While fatigue and sleep disturbances are common in both, ME/CFS is distinguished by post-exertional malaise (PEM) and physical symptoms, whereas depression often improves with activity.
  • Fibromyalgia: Characterised by widespread chronic pain and tender points, often overlapping with ME/CFS. However, ME/CFS is primarily defined by profound fatigue and PEM, while pain is the dominant feature in fibromyalgia.
  • Hypothyroidism: Presents with fatigue, weight gain, and cognitive slowing. Distinguished by abnormal thyroid function tests and resolution of symptoms with thyroid hormone replacement.
  • Sleep Apnoea: Causes daytime fatigue and unrefreshing sleep. Diagnosed via sleep study and typically improves with continuous positive airway pressure (CPAP) therapy.
  • Anaemia: Fatigue, weakness, and pallor are common. Diagnosed by full blood count showing low haemoglobin and often resolves with iron supplementation or treatment of underlying cause.
  • Long COVID: Significant overlap in symptoms with ME/CFS, particularly post-exertional malaise, fatigue, and cognitive dysfunction, often requiring careful differentiation based on onset and specific symptom clusters.
  • Adrenal Insufficiency: Fatigue, weakness, weight loss, and orthostatic hypotension. Diagnosed by abnormal cortisol levels and ACTH stimulation test.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Myalgic encephalomyelitis (ME)/ chronic fatigue syndrome (CFS)"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities:
    • Acknowledge and validate the patient's symptoms and experiences.
    • Educate on energy management and activity pacing to prevent post-exertional malaise (PEM).
    • Address urgent symptom relief, such as severe pain or sleep disturbance.
  • Definitive management:
    • Individualised symptom management plan, focusing on improving quality of life and functional capacity.
    • Activity management and pacing strategies, including rest periods and avoiding overexertion.
    • Cognitive behavioural therapy (CBT) or graded exercise therapy (GET) may be considered, but should be adapted to the individual's needs and not presented as a cure.
    • Dietary advice to ensure adequate nutrition and hydration.
  • Monitoring and follow-up:
    • Regular reviews to monitor symptom severity, functional status, and treatment effectiveness.
    • Assessment for new or worsening symptoms, and adjustment of management plan as needed.
    • Screening for mental health comorbidities, such as depression or anxiety, and offering appropriate support.
  • Escalation and safety-netting:
    • Referral to specialist ME/CFS services for complex cases or those not responding to primary care management.
    • Provide clear advice on when to seek urgent medical attention for red flag symptoms.
  • Pharmacology:
    • Medications may be considered for symptomatic relief, such as analgesics for pain, hypnotics for sleep disturbance, or antiemetics for nausea.
    • For neuropathic pain, consider agents such as amitriptyline or gabapentin.
    • For orthostatic intolerance, consider fludrocortisone or midodrine.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Myalgic encephalomyelitis (ME)/ chronic fatigue syndrome (CFS)"][/sc]
  • Significant functional impairment: The persistent and debilitating symptoms, particularly PEM, can lead to a substantial reduction in daily activities, affecting education, employment, and personal care, often resulting in severe disability.
  • Mental health impact: Chronic illness, pain, and social isolation contribute to a high prevalence of secondary depression, anxiety, and psychological distress, which can further exacerbate the physical symptoms.
  • Social isolation and economic hardship: The fluctuating and often invisible nature of ME/CFS can lead to misunderstanding from others, social withdrawal, loss of employment, and significant financial strain.
  • Deconditioning: Reduced physical activity due to severe fatigue and PEM can lead to physical deconditioning, muscle weakness, and loss of stamina, creating a vicious cycle of worsening symptoms.
  • Sleep disorders: Chronic unrefreshing sleep can lead to further cognitive impairment, mood disturbances, and overall worsening of ME/CFS symptoms.
