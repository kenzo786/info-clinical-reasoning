Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Ovarian cancer)"][/sc]
  • Ovarian cancer encompasses a group of heterogeneous malignancies arising from the ovary, fallopian tube, or primary peritoneum, primarily epithelial in origin, but also including germ cell and sex cord-stromal tumours.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the varied presentations of ovarian cancer, including non-specific symptoms, and initiate appropriate diagnostic pathways, including risk assessment and referral.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Ovarian cancer)"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Ovarian cancer)"][/sc]
  • Around 7,600 new ovarian cancer cases are diagnosed in the UK each year, making it the 16th most common cancer in women.
  • The incidence of ovarian cancer significantly increases with age, with the majority of cases occurring in postmenopausal women over 50 years.
  • Key risk factors include increasing age, nulliparity, early menarche, late menopause, endometriosis, and genetic predisposition (e.g., BRCA1/2 mutations, Lynch syndrome).
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Ovarian cancer)"][/sc]
  • Trigger: The precise origin of epithelial ovarian cancer is debated, but it is increasingly understood to arise from the fimbrial end of the fallopian tube (serous carcinoma) or ovarian surface epithelium, often linked to incessant ovulation and chronic inflammation. Germ cell tumours originate from primordial germ cells within the ovary, while sex cord-stromal tumours develop from the ovarian stroma.
  • Progression: Malignant transformation leads to uncontrolled cellular proliferation. Epithelial ovarian cancer typically spreads by direct extension within the peritoneal cavity, forming ascites and peritoneal implants. Germ cell and sex cord-stromal tumours may spread locally or via lymphatic/haematogenous routes.
  • Consequence: Advanced disease results in widespread intra-abdominal metastasis, bowel obstruction, cachexia, and organ dysfunction. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="BRCA1/2 mutations in ovarian cancer: what is their role and clinical significance?" text="BRCA1/2 mutations"] are associated with a significant proportion of high-grade serous ovarian cancers, influencing both risk and treatment response.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Ovarian cancer)"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Ovarian cancer often presents insidiously with non-specific symptoms, making early detection challenging. Symptoms like bloating, abdominal discomfort, and early satiety may be subtle and intermittent for several weeks or months.
  • Progressive phase: As the tumour grows and spreads within the peritoneal cavity, symptoms become more persistent and severe. This phase may involve increasing abdominal distension due to ascites, worsening pain, and changes in bowel or bladder habits, particularly beyond a few months of initial vague symptoms.
  • Late phase: Advanced disease is characterised by significant tumour burden, widespread metastases, and systemic effects. This can lead to severe pain, bowel obstruction, significant weight loss, and profound fatigue, often developing over several months to a year from initial symptom onset.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Ovarian cancer (Gynaymmetrical tract)"][/sc]
  • Ask about persistent abdominal distension (bloating) - often dismissed as benign, but persistent, new-onset bloating, especially in women over 50, is a key indicator.
  • Ask about feeling full quickly (early satiety) and/or loss of appetite - these non-specific gastrointestinal symptoms can reflect pressure from an ovarian mass or ascites.
  • Ask about pelvic or abdominal pain - typically vague and persistent, rather than acute, and may be mistaken for irritable bowel syndrome.
  • Ask about increased urinary urgency and/or frequency - can occur due to pressure on the bladder from an enlarging ovarian mass.
  • Ask about unexplained weight loss or fatigue - systemic symptoms that may indicate advanced disease.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Ovarian cancer)"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - may reveal tachycardia or hypotension in advanced disease, or be normal in early stages.
  • Check for ascites - accumulation of fluid in the peritoneal cavity, often presenting as abdominal distension and dullness to percussion, implying peritoneal spread. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Ascites in ovarian cancer: what is its significance and how is it managed?" text="Ascites"]
  • Check for a pelvic or abdominal mass - a palpable, often irregular, mass on abdominal or bimanual pelvic examination, suggesting an ovarian or adnexal tumour.
  • Check for lymphadenopathy - enlarged supraclavicular or inguinal lymph nodes may indicate metastatic spread.
  • Check for oedema - lower limb oedema can occur due to lymphatic obstruction or deep vein thrombosis, particularly in advanced disease.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Ovarian cancer)"][/sc]
  • New onset persistent abdominal distension: Suggests significant intra-abdominal pathology, potentially ascites or a large ovarian mass, warranting urgent investigation.
  • Palpable pelvic or abdominal mass: A new or growing mass, particularly if fixed or irregular, is highly suspicious for malignancy and requires prompt referral.
  • Unexplained weight loss or cachexia: Systemic signs of advanced malignancy, indicating a need for rapid diagnostic work-up.
  • Evidence of bowel obstruction: Suggests peritoneal carcinomatosis with involvement of the gastrointestinal tract, requiring immediate surgical or oncological assessment.
  • Elevated CA125 with abnormal imaging: A combination of a raised tumour marker and suspicious ultrasound findings strongly indicates ovarian cancer and necessitates urgent specialist referral.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Ovarian cancer)"][/sc]
  • Younger women: While rare, ovarian cancer in younger women is more likely to be [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Germ cell tumours of the ovary: characteristics and clinical presentation?" text="germ cell or sex cord-stromal tumours"], which can present with acute symptoms like abdominal pain due to torsion or rupture, or hormonal effects (e.g., precocious puberty, virilisation).
  • Postmenopausal women: Non-specific gastrointestinal symptoms (bloating, early satiety) are more concerning in this age group, as IBS rarely presents de novo after menopause.
  • Women with BRCA1/2 mutations: May present with high-grade serous carcinoma originating from the fallopian tube fimbriae, often with minimal ovarian involvement, highlighting the importance of considering extra-ovarian primary sites.
  • Incidental finding: Ovarian masses may be discovered incidentally on imaging performed for other reasons, necessitating careful evaluation to rule out malignancy.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Ovarian cancer)"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected ovarian cancer:
    • Serum CA125: A glycoprotein tumour marker, often elevated in epithelial ovarian cancer, used for initial assessment and monitoring. Levels ≥35 IU/ml warrant further investigation.
    • Transvaginal ultrasound (TVS): Primary imaging modality to assess ovarian morphology, presence of masses, and features suggestive of malignancy (e.g., solid components, septations, ascites).
  • Severity/risk tools:
    • Risk of Malignancy Index (RMI): Combines CA125 level, menopausal status, and ultrasound findings to estimate the risk of malignancy in an ovarian mass. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Risk of Malignancy Index (RMI): how is it calculated and interpreted?" text="RMI score"] ≥250 typically indicates high risk and warrants specialist referral.   • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible:
    • Other tumour markers: In women under 40, AFP and beta-hCG should be measured to screen for germ cell tumours.
  • Imaging - CT scan: CT of the abdomen and pelvis (and often thorax) is used to stage the disease, assess the extent of spread, and guide management decisions once ovarian cancer is suspected.
  • Genetic testing: [sc name="jitl-query" Q="BRCA testing in ovarian cancer: who should be tested and what are the implications?" text="BRCA testing"] is crucial for identifying inherited genetic mutations (BRCA1/2) that increase ovarian cancer risk and influence treatment choices (e.g., PARP inhibitors).

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Ovarian cancer)"][/sc]
  • Benign ovarian cysts/masses - often asymptomatic or cause mild, intermittent pain; typically resolve spontaneously or have benign features on ultrasound.
  • Uterine fibroids - commonly cause heavy menstrual bleeding, pelvic pressure, or urinary frequency, but usually distinguishable by uterine origin on examination and imaging.
  • Irritable Bowel Syndrome (IBS) - presents with chronic abdominal pain, bloating, and altered bowel habits, but rarely has new onset in postmenopausal women and lacks a palpable mass.
  • Diverticulitis - acute abdominal pain, fever, and localised tenderness, usually without a pelvic mass.
  • Ectopic pregnancy - acute abdominal pain, vaginal bleeding, and positive pregnancy test in a woman of reproductive age.
  • Pelvic Inflammatory Disease (PID) - acute pelvic pain, fever, vaginal discharge, and cervical motion tenderness, typically in sexually active younger women.
  • Gastrointestinal malignancies (e.g., colorectal cancer) - may present with similar non-specific abdominal symptoms, weight loss, and changes in bowel habit, requiring colonoscopy for differentiation.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Ovarian cancer)"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Recognition of suspected ovarian cancer, urgent referral to a gynaecological oncology multidisciplinary team, and supportive care to manage symptoms such as pain and ascites.
  • Definitive management: Often involves primary surgical debulking (cytoreduction) to remove as much tumour as possible, followed by platinum-based chemotherapy (e.g., carboplatin and paclitaxel). Neoadjuvant chemotherapy followed by interval debulking surgery may be considered for advanced disease.
  • Adjunctive management: Targeted therapies, such as PARP inhibitors (e.g., [sc name="jitl-medication" Q="Olaparib use in ovarian cancer" text="Olaparib"]) may be considered for patients with BRCA mutations or homologous recombination deficiency, particularly in maintenance settings.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Regular clinical review, CA125 monitoring, and imaging are typically employed to detect recurrence.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Patients should be advised to seek urgent medical attention for worsening symptoms, particularly severe pain, bowel obstruction, or new neurological deficits.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Ovarian cancer)"][/sc]
  • Bowel obstruction: As the disease progresses, peritoneal carcinomatosis can encase and obstruct segments of the bowel, leading to severe abdominal pain, nausea, vomiting, and constipation, often occurring in the progressive to late phases.
  • Ascites: Accumulation of fluid in the peritoneal cavity, a common complication of advanced epithelial ovarian cancer, causing abdominal distension, discomfort, and dyspnoea, typically developing in the progressive phase.
  • Venous thromboembolism (VTE): Patients with ovarian cancer are at increased risk of deep vein thrombosis and pulmonary embolism due to hypercoagulability associated with malignancy, which can occur at any stage but is more common in advanced disease.
  • Cachexia and malnutrition: Systemic effects of advanced cancer, coupled with treatment side effects, can lead to significant weight loss, muscle wasting, and nutritional deficiencies, predominantly in the late phase.
  • Renal failure: Ureteral obstruction by tumour masses can lead to hydronephrosis and acute kidney injury, a complication of advanced disease.
