Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Intestinal obstruction and ileus"][/sc]
  • Intestinal obstruction is a mechanical blockage preventing the normal passage of intestinal contents, while ileus (paralytic ileus) is a functional disruption of peristalsis without a physical obstruction.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical features of intestinal obstruction and ileus, differentiate between mechanical and functional causes, and initiate appropriate emergency management.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Intestinal obstruction and ileus"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Intestinal obstruction and ileus"][/sc]
  • Small bowel obstruction (SBO) accounts for approximately 50% of emergency laparotomies in the UK annually, with over 300,000 hospital admissions [1, 2].
  • Adhesions from previous abdominal surgery are the most common cause of SBO, accounting for 60-70% of cases [1].
  • Large bowel obstruction (LBO) is less common than SBO, with colorectal cancer being the leading cause (60-70%), followed by diverticular disease and volvulus [3].
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Intestinal obstruction and ileus"][/sc]
  • Mechanical obstruction (e.g., adhesions, hernia, tumour) leads to accumulation of fluid and gas proximal to the blockage, causing bowel distension and increased intraluminal pressure.
  • This distension compromises venous and lymphatic drainage, leading to bowel wall oedema, reduced absorption, and increased secretion, potentially resulting in fluid and electrolyte imbalances. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Closed loop obstruction: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Closed loop obstruction"][/sc]
  • Persistent distension and ischaemia can lead to bowel perforation, peritonitis, and sepsis, particularly in cases of [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Strangulation: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="strangulation"].
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Intestinal obstruction and ileus"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Initial symptoms of colicky abdominal pain, nausea, and vomiting typically develop within hours of obstruction onset.
  • Progressive phase: As distension increases, pain may become more constant, and abdominal tenderness may develop, particularly beyond 24-48 hours.
  • Late phase: Signs of dehydration, electrolyte imbalance, and potential peritonitis (e.g., fever, guarding, rigidity) may emerge, especially beyond 48-72 hours, indicating severe complications.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Intestinal obstruction and ileus"][/sc]
  • Ask about abdominal pain - typically colicky and intermittent in mechanical obstruction, often more diffuse and constant in ileus.
  • Ask about vomiting - often bilious in proximal obstruction, faeculent in distal obstruction.
  • Ask about abdominal distension - usually more prominent in distal obstructions and large bowel obstructions.
  • Ask about change in bowel habits - absolute constipation (no flatus or faeces) is characteristic of complete obstruction, while some passage of flatus/faeces may occur in partial obstruction or ileus.
  • Ask about previous abdominal surgery - a significant risk factor for adhesive small bowel obstruction.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Intestinal obstruction and ileus"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - tachycardia, hypotension, and fever may indicate dehydration, sepsis, or peritonitis.
  • Check abdominal distension - assess for generalised or localised distension.
  • Check abdominal tenderness and guarding - localised tenderness or rebound suggests peritonitis.
  • Check bowel sounds - often high-pitched and tinkling in early mechanical obstruction, absent or reduced in ileus. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Bowel sounds in obstruction vs ileus: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Bowel sounds"][/sc]
  • Check for hernias - incarcerated hernias are a common cause of external obstruction.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Intestinal obstruction and ileus"][/sc]
  • Peritonism - indicates bowel ischaemia, perforation, or peritonitis, requiring urgent surgical review.
  • Haemodynamic instability - suggests severe dehydration, sepsis, or haemorrhage, necessitating immediate resuscitation.
  • Faeculent vomiting - typically seen in distal small bowel or large bowel obstruction, indicating prolonged obstruction and bacterial overgrowth.
  • Rapidly increasing abdominal distension - may suggest closed-loop obstruction or large bowel obstruction with competent ileocaecal valve, increasing risk of perforation.
  • Lack of response to conservative management within 24-48 hours - raises concern for complete obstruction or strangulation, warranting re-evaluation for surgical intervention.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Intestinal obstruction and ileus"][/sc]
  • Older adults: May present with vague symptoms, less pronounced pain, or altered mental status, leading to delayed diagnosis.
  • Post-operative patients: Ileus is common, but differentiating from mechanical obstruction can be challenging; persistent symptoms beyond 3-5 days or worsening pain may indicate obstruction.
  • Patients on opioids: Opioid-induced constipation can mimic obstruction or exacerbate ileus, requiring careful assessment of medication history.
  • Partial obstruction: May present with intermittent symptoms and some passage of flatus or loose stools, making diagnosis more difficult. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Partial vs complete bowel obstruction: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Partial obstruction"][/sc]
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Intestinal obstruction and ileus"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected intestinal obstruction: Full blood count (FBC), urea and electrolytes (U&Es), C-reactive protein (CRP), lactate, and group and save.
  • Imaging - Abdominal X-ray (AXR): May show dilated bowel loops, air-fluid levels, and absence of gas in the colon, but has limited sensitivity and specificity.
  • Imaging - Computed Tomography (CT) scan: The gold standard for diagnosing intestinal obstruction, identifying the level, cause, and presence of complications like strangulation or perforation. Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Intestinal obstruction and ileus"][/sc]
  • Paralytic ileus - functional bowel hypomotility without mechanical blockage, often post-operative or due to systemic illness.
  • Acute appendicitis - localised right iliac fossa pain, often with anorexia and fever, typically without significant distension or absolute constipation.
  • Acute diverticulitis - localised left iliac fossa pain, fever, and altered bowel habit, usually without features of complete obstruction.
  • Gastroenteritis - diffuse abdominal pain, vomiting, and diarrhoea, typically self-limiting.
  • Mesenteric ischaemia - severe, disproportionate abdominal pain, often with minimal physical findings initially, progressing to peritonism. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Mesenteric ischaemia: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Mesenteric ischaemia"][/sc]
  • Constipation/faecal impaction - chronic history of infrequent bowel movements, often with palpable faecal masses, usually without acute, severe pain or bilious vomiting.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Intestinal obstruction and ileus"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Recognition of the condition, prompt referral to surgical team, fluid resuscitation to correct dehydration and electrolyte imbalances, and nasogastric tube insertion for decompression in vomiting or distended patients.
  • Definitive management: May involve conservative management (e.g., in partial SBO or ileus) with close monitoring, or surgical intervention (e.g., laparotomy or laparoscopy) for complete mechanical obstruction, strangulation, or perforation.
  • Adjunctive management: Pain relief (e.g., intravenous analgesia), antiemetics, and consideration of broad-spectrum antibiotics if perforation or sepsis is suspected.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Regular clinical assessment, fluid balance monitoring, and serial blood tests (e.g., U&Es, lactate) to detect deterioration or resolution.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Patients should be advised on symptoms of worsening obstruction (e.g., increasing pain, distension, persistent vomiting, fever) and when to seek urgent medical attention.
  • Exam pharmacology reference (UKMLA/MRCGP): Opioid analgesics (e.g., [sc name="jitl-medication" Q="Morphine use in pain management" text="morphine"] or [sc name="jitl-medication" Q="Fentanyl use in pain management" text="fentanyl"]) for pain management, antiemetics (e.g., [sc name="jitl-medication" Q="Ondansetron use in nausea and vomiting" text="ondansetron"] or [sc name="jitl-medication" Q="Metoclopramide use in nausea and vomiting" text="metoclopramide"]) for nausea and vomiting. Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Intestinal obstruction and ileus"][/sc]
  • Bowel ischaemia and necrosis: Occurs when blood supply to the obstructed segment is compromised (e.g., in strangulation or closed-loop obstruction), leading to tissue death and increased risk of perforation, particularly beyond 24-48 hours.
  • Bowel perforation: Resulting from prolonged distension, ischaemia, or necrosis, leading to spillage of intestinal contents into the peritoneal cavity and subsequent peritonitis and sepsis.
  • Sepsis and septic shock: Can develop from peritonitis or bacterial translocation across an ischaemic bowel wall, leading to systemic inflammatory response and organ dysfunction.
  • Fluid and electrolyte imbalance: Persistent vomiting and third-spacing of fluid into the bowel lumen can lead to severe dehydration, hypokalaemia, and metabolic alkalosis.
  • Short bowel syndrome: A long-term complication following extensive surgical resection of ischaemic or necrotic bowel, leading to malabsorption and nutritional deficiencies.
