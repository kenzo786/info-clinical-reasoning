Breast abscess/ mastitis [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Breast abscess/ mastitis"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Breast abscess/ mastitis"][/sc]
  • A localised collection of pus within the breast tissue, often a complication of mastitis.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise and manage breast abscess and mastitis, understanding their aetiology, clinical presentation, and treatment.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Breast abscess/ mastitis"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Breast abscess/ mastitis"][/sc]
  • Lactational mastitis affects up to one-third of breastfeeding women, with a small proportion (around 3%) progressing to abscess formation [1].
  • Risk factors include cracked nipples, incomplete breast emptying, and previous episodes of mastitis [2].
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Breast abscess/ mastitis"][/sc]
  • Milk stasis and nipple trauma allow bacterial entry (often Staphylococcus aureus) into breast ducts.
  • Bacterial proliferation leads to inflammation and infection (mastitis), forming a localised cellulitis.
  • Untreated or ineffective treatment of mastitis can result in pus accumulation and abscess formation within the breast parenchyma.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Breast abscess/ mastitis"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Mastitis symptoms (pain, redness, fever) typically develop rapidly over 24-48 hours.
  • Progressive phase: If mastitis is not effectively managed, an abscess may form within 5-10 days, particularly beyond 48-72 hours of initial symptoms.
  • Late phase: Resolution of an abscess can take several weeks following drainage and antibiotic therapy, particularly beyond 2 weeks for larger collections.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Breast abscess/ mastitis"][/sc]
  • Ask about localised breast pain - often severe, throbbing, and worsening with movement or feeding, indicating inflammation and pus collection.
  • Ask about breast swelling and redness - suggestive of an inflammatory process, distinguishing from simple engorgement.
  • Ask about fever and malaise - systemic symptoms indicating infection, helping to differentiate from non-infectious causes of breast pain.
  • Ask about a palpable breast lump - a discrete, tender, fluctuant mass is highly indicative of an abscess.
  • Ask about nipple discharge - purulent discharge may indicate a fistula or rupture of the abscess.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Breast abscess/ mastitis"][/sc]
  • Check for a tender, warm, erythematous, and swollen area of the breast - classic signs of inflammation and infection.
  • Check for a fluctuant mass on palpation - a key diagnostic sign for an abscess, indicating a fluid-filled cavity.
  • Check axillary lymphadenopathy - enlarged, tender lymph nodes suggest regional inflammatory response.
  • Check vital signs - fever, tachycardia, and potentially hypotension in severe cases, indicating systemic inflammatory response syndrome (SIRS).
  • Check for skin changes such as peau d\'orange or nipple retraction - important to differentiate from inflammatory breast carcinoma [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Inflammatory breast carcinoma: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="inflammatory breast carcinoma"][/sc].
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Breast abscess/ mastitis"][/sc]
  • Rapidly worsening symptoms - suggests aggressive infection or developing complications.
  • Persistent fever despite antibiotics - may indicate inadequate treatment, resistant organism, or undrained abscess.
  • Skin changes suggestive of malignancy - such as persistent oedema, peau d\'orange, or nipple retraction, requiring urgent investigation.
  • Systemic signs of sepsis - hypotension, altered mental status, tachypnoea, requiring immediate hospital admission and management.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Breast abscess/ mastitis"][/sc]
  • Non-lactational mastitis/abscess: Can occur in non-breastfeeding women, men, or adolescents, often associated with periductal mastitis or underlying conditions like diabetes. Presentation may be less acute.
  • Older adults: May present with less pronounced inflammatory signs, or a more indolent course, potentially delaying diagnosis. Consider underlying malignancy more readily.
  • Immunosuppressed patients: May have atypical pathogens or a blunted inflammatory response, leading to delayed diagnosis and more severe infection.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Breast abscess/ mastitis"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected breast abscess: Clinical examination is often sufficient for diagnosis.
  • Imaging - Ultrasound: Essential to confirm the presence of an abscess, differentiate it from cellulitis, and guide aspiration or drainage.
  • Aspiration of pus for microbiology: Crucial for identifying the causative organism and guiding antibiotic therapy, especially in recurrent or non-resolving cases.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Mammography or MRI may be considered to rule out underlying malignancy, particularly in older women or those with atypical features.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Breast abscess/ mastitis"][/sc]
  • A 32-year-old breastfeeding woman presents with a 3-day history of increasing left breast pain, redness, and swelling, associated with fever and a palpable, tender lump.
  • A lactating woman with acute, localised breast inflammation and systemic symptoms, complicated by a fluctuant mass, concerning for bacterial infection and abscess formation.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Breast abscess/ mastitis"][/sc]
  • Lactational mastitis (without abscess) - no fluctuant mass, typically responds to conservative measures and antibiotics.
  • Breast engorgement - bilateral, diffuse, no systemic signs of infection.
  • Inflammatory breast carcinoma - persistent skin changes (peau d\'orange), often without fever or acute tenderness, may mimic mastitis [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Inflammatory breast carcinoma: how to differentiate from mastitis?" text="inflammatory breast carcinoma" ][/sc].
  • Galactocele - painless, non-inflammatory cyst, usually resolves spontaneously.
  • Fat necrosis - history of trauma, often firm and irregular, can mimic malignancy.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Breast abscess/ mastitis"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Analgesia (paracetamol, ibuprofen), continued breast emptying (breastfeeding or pumping), and prompt initiation of appropriate antibiotics.
  • Definitive management: Ultrasound-guided aspiration or surgical incision and drainage of the abscess.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Regular clinical review to assess resolution of symptoms and signs, ensuring complete drainage and healing.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Advise patients to seek urgent medical attention for worsening pain, persistent fever, or signs of spreading infection.
  • Exam pharmacology reference (UKMLA/MRCGP): Common antibiotics include flucloxacillin or co-amoxiclav. Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Breast abscess/ mastitis"][/sc]
  • Recurrent abscess: Inadequate drainage or persistent risk factors can lead to repeated abscess formation, impacting patient quality of life.
  • Chronic fistula: An abscess may rupture spontaneously or after drainage, forming a persistent tract to the skin, requiring surgical intervention.
  • Sepsis: Systemic spread of infection from an untreated or severe abscess can lead to life-threatening sepsis and septic shock.
  • Deformity or scarring: Large or recurrent abscesses can cause significant tissue destruction, leading to cosmetic concerns and potential impact on future breastfeeding.
