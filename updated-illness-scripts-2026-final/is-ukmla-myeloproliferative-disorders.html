\n Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Myeloproliferative disorders"][/sc]\n
\n
    \n
  • Myeloproliferative neoplasms (MPNs) are a group of rare disorders of the bone marrow, characterised by the overproduction of one or more types of blood cells (red cells, white cells, or platelets). They are classified as a type of blood cancer.
    • \n
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical features and initial diagnostic approach to myeloproliferative disorders.
    • \n

\n Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Myeloproliferative disorders"][/sc]\n
\n
    \n
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Myeloproliferative disorders"][/sc]
    • \n
  • Myeloproliferative neoplasms (MPNs) are rare, affecting approximately 8 in every 100,000 people in the UK annually.
    • \n
  • Incidence increases with age, with most diagnoses occurring in individuals over 60 years.
    • \n
\n

\n Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Myeloproliferative disorders"][/sc]\n
    \n
  • Myeloproliferative neoplasms (MPNs) are clonal haematopoietic stem cell disorders driven by acquired somatic mutations, most commonly in *JAK2*, *CALR*, or *MPL* genes.
    • \n
  • These mutations lead to constitutive activation of the JAK-STAT signalling pathway, promoting uncontrolled proliferation and survival of myeloid cells in the bone marrow.
    • \n
  • The excessive production of mature blood cells (erythrocytes, granulocytes, or platelets) results in clinical manifestations such as thrombotic events, haemorrhage, and progression to myelofibrosis or acute myeloid leukaemia.
    • \n
\n
\n\n
\n Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Myeloproliferative disorders"][/sc]\n
    \n
  • Early phase: Often asymptomatic or with mild, non-specific symptoms. The disease may remain stable for prolonged periods, particularly in essential thrombocythaemia or polycythaemia vera.
    • \n
  • Progressive phase: Characterised by increasing symptom burden, worsening cytopenias, or development of splenomegaly. This phase may involve transformation to an accelerated phase, particularly beyond several years from diagnosis.
    • \n
  • Late phase: Risk of transformation to myelofibrosis or acute myeloid leukaemia (AML) increases significantly. This stage is associated with severe cytopenias, constitutional symptoms, and a higher incidence of life-threatening complications.
    • \n

\n Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Myeloproliferative disorders"][/sc]\n
    \n
  • Ask about fatigue - a common and often debilitating symptom that can significantly impact quality of life and may indicate disease progression.
    • \n
  • Ask about pruritus (itchy skin) - particularly after warm baths, which is characteristic of polycythaemia vera due to histamine release.
    • \n
  • Ask about night sweats and unexplained weight loss - constitutional symptoms that may suggest disease activity or progression, especially in myelofibrosis.
    • \n
  • Ask about bruising or unusual bleeding - indicative of platelet dysfunction or thrombocytopenia, which can occur in MPNs.
    • \n
  • Ask about headaches, dizziness, or visual disturbances - often related to hyperviscosity in polycythaemia vera or microvascular complications.
    • \n
\n
\n\n
\n Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Myeloproliferative disorders"][/sc]\n
    \n
  • Check for splenomegaly - a common finding, particularly in myelofibrosis, indicating extramedullary haematopoiesis or increased splenic workload.
    • \n
  • Check for facial plethora or conjunctival injection - suggestive of erythrocytosis and hyperviscosity, characteristic of polycythaemia vera.
    • \n
  • Check for pallor - may indicate anaemia, which can develop in later stages or in specific MPN subtypes.
    • \n
  • Check for bruising or petechiae - signs of abnormal bleeding, potentially due to platelet dysfunction or thrombocytopenia.
    • \n
  • Check vital signs - hypertension may be present due to increased blood volume/viscosity; tachycardia or fever may indicate infection or disease activity.
    • \n
\n
\n\n
\n Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Myeloproliferative disorders"][/sc]\n
    \n
  • Unexplained thrombosis - particularly in unusual sites (e.g., splanchnic veins), as MPNs significantly increase the risk of thrombotic events due to abnormal blood cell counts and activation.
    • \n
  • Significant or recurrent haemorrhage - may indicate severe platelet dysfunction or extreme thrombocytosis/thrombocytopenia, requiring urgent haematological assessment.
    • \n
  • Rapidly progressive splenomegaly or new onset hepatomegaly - suggests disease acceleration or transformation, especially to myelofibrosis or blast phase.
    • \n
  • Sudden worsening of constitutional symptoms (e.g., severe fatigue, drenching night sweats, unexplained weight loss) - can herald disease progression or leukemic transformation.
    • \n
  • New or worsening cytopenias (anaemia, leukopenia, thrombocytopenia) - in the context of an MPN, this may indicate bone marrow failure or progression to myelofibrosis or acute leukaemia.
    • \n
\n
\n\n
\n Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Myeloproliferative disorders"][/sc]\n
    \n
  • Older adults: MPNs typically present in older age, but symptoms may be masked by co-morbidities or attributed to normal ageing, leading to delayed diagnosis.
    • \n
  • Pregnancy: Pregnant patients with MPNs face increased risks of maternal and foetal complications, including thrombosis, haemorrhage, pre-eclampsia, and foetal growth restriction, requiring careful multidisciplinary management.
    • \n
  • Masked MPN: Some patients may present with features suggestive of myelodysplastic syndromes (MDS) or undifferentiated myeloproliferative disorders, making initial diagnosis challenging.
    • \n

\n Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Myeloproliferative disorders"][/sc]\n
    \n
  • Core for suspected Myeloproliferative disorders:\n
      \n
    • Full blood count (FBC) with differential: Essential for identifying abnormal cell counts (e.g., erythrocytosis, thrombocytosis, leukocytosis).
      • \n
    • Blood film examination: To assess cell morphology and identify immature forms or dysplastic features.
      • \n
    • Bone marrow aspirate and trephine biopsy: Crucial for morphological assessment, cellularity, fibrosis, and cytogenetics.
      • \n
    • Molecular testing for driver mutations: *JAK2* V617F, *CALR* exon 9, and *MPL* W515K/L mutations are key diagnostic markers.
      • \n
    \n
    • \n
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible:\n
      \n
    • BCR-ABL1 fusion gene testing: To exclude Chronic Myeloid Leukaemia (CML), which can present similarly.
      • \n
    • Erythropoietin (EPO) levels: Low levels support a diagnosis of Polycythaemia Vera.
      • \n
    \n
    • \n
  • Imaging - Ultrasound: Abdominal ultrasound to assess for splenomegaly or hepatomegaly.
    • \n
  • Validated scores: Consider using prognostic scoring systems like the DIPSS-Plus on MDCalc for myelofibrosis, or IPSET-thrombosis on MDCalc for essential thrombocythaemia, to guide risk stratification.
    • \n

\n Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Myeloproliferative disorders"][/sc]\n
    \n
  • A 68-year-old male presents with a 6-month history of increasing fatigue, generalised pruritus, and early satiety. On examination, he has facial plethora and palpable splenomegaly. Full blood count reveals erythrocytosis, thrombocytosis, and leukocytosis.
    • \n
  • A chronic, progressive myeloproliferative disorder characterised by panmyelosis, constitutional symptoms, and thrombotic risk, requiring haematological investigation.
    • \n

\n Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Myeloproliferative disorders"][/sc]\n
    \n
  • Chronic Myeloid Leukaemia (CML) - distinguished by the presence of the BCR-ABL1 fusion gene.
    • \n
  • Reactive Erythrocytosis/Thrombocytosis/Leukocytosis - lacks the clonal *JAK2*, *CALR*, or *MPL* mutations and is secondary to an underlying condition (e.g., hypoxia, inflammation, infection).
    • \n
  • Myelodysplastic Syndromes (MDS) - typically characterised by ineffective haematopoiesis and cytopenias, rather than proliferative features, though some overlap exists.
    • \n
  • Secondary Polycythaemia - caused by chronic hypoxia or erythropoietin-producing tumours, with normal platelet and white cell counts, and often elevated EPO levels.
    • \n
  • Secondary Thrombocytosis - often reactive to inflammation, infection, iron deficiency, or malignancy, with normal red and white cell counts and absence of MPN driver mutations.
    • \n

\n Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Myeloproliferative disorders"][/sc]\n
    \n
  • Immediate priorities: Management often includes addressing vascular risk factors and, for patients with high red blood cell counts, therapeutic phlebotomy to reduce blood viscosity. Low-dose aspirin may be considered to reduce thrombotic risk.
    • \n
  • Definitive management: Cytoreductive agents, such as hydroxycarbamide, are commonly used to control blood cell counts and manage symptoms. For specific MPN subtypes, targeted therapies like ruxolitinib (a JAK inhibitor) may be indicated, particularly in myelofibrosis. Allogeneic stem cell transplant is the only curative option but is reserved for higher-risk patients due to associated morbidity and mortality.
    • \n
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Regular clinical review, including symptom assessment and full blood counts, is essential to monitor disease activity, treatment response, and detect signs of progression.
    • \n
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Worsening symptoms, uncontrolled blood counts, or signs of disease progression (e.g., increasing splenomegaly, new cytopenias) warrant re-evaluation and potential treatment escalation. Patients should be educated on red flag symptoms requiring urgent medical attention.

\n Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Myeloproliferative disorders"][/sc]\n
    \n
  • Thrombosis: Increased risk of arterial and venous thrombotic events (e.g., DVT, PE, stroke, myocardial infarction, splanchnic vein thrombosis) due to abnormal blood cell counts, platelet activation, and endothelial dysfunction. This risk is present throughout the disease course, particularly in early and progressive phases.
    • \n
  • Haemorrhage: Bleeding complications can occur due to qualitative or quantitative platelet defects, especially in essential thrombocythaemia with extreme thrombocytosis or in advanced myelofibrosis with thrombocytopenia. This can manifest at any stage but may worsen in later phases.
    • \n
  • Myelofibrotic transformation: Progression from polycythaemia vera or essential thrombocythaemia to secondary myelofibrosis, characterised by bone marrow scarring, worsening cytopenias, and extramedullary haematopoiesis. This typically occurs in the progressive to late phases.
    • \n
  • Leukemic transformation: Evolution to acute myeloid leukaemia (AML) is a serious complication, particularly in myelofibrosis, and is associated with a poor prognosis. This is a feature of the late phase of the disease.
    • \n
  • Splenic infarction/rupture: Rapidly enlarging spleen can lead to infarction or, rarely, rupture, causing acute abdominal pain.
    • \n
