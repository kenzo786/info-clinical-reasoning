Periorbital and orbital cellulitis [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Periorbital and orbital cellulitis"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Periorbital and orbital cellulitis"][/sc]
  • Periorbital cellulitis (preseptal cellulitis) is an infection of the eyelids and periorbital tissues anterior to the orbital septum, while orbital cellulitis (postseptal cellulitis) is a more severe infection involving the orbital contents posterior to the septum.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: recognise the clinical features distinguishing periorbital from orbital cellulitis, and understand the urgency of management for orbital involvement.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Periorbital and orbital cellulitis"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Periorbital and orbital cellulitis"][/sc]
  • Periorbital cellulitis is more common in children, often following upper respiratory tract infections or local skin trauma, with a peak incidence in those under five years of age.
  • Orbital cellulitis is less common but carries significant morbidity, with a higher incidence in younger children due to anatomical factors facilitating spread from paranasal sinuses.
  • Risk factors include recent upper respiratory tract infection, sinusitis, otitis media, dental infection, local trauma, insect bites, and immunocompromise.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Periorbital and orbital cellulitis"][/sc]
  • Infection typically originates from contiguous spread from paranasal sinusitis (especially ethmoid) or from local skin trauma, insect bites, or conjunctivitis, introducing bacteria such as Staphylococcus aureus or Streptococcus pyogenes.
  • The infection progresses, with periorbital cellulitis remaining anterior to the orbital septum, causing localised inflammation, whereas orbital cellulitis breaches the septum, leading to inflammation and oedema within the confined orbital space.
  • Consequence of orbital cellulitis includes compression of orbital contents, potentially leading to optic nerve compromise, cavernous sinus thrombosis, or intracranial spread due to the valveless orbital venous system.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Periorbital and orbital cellulitis"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Symptoms such as eyelid swelling, erythema, and pain typically develop rapidly over 24-48 hours, often following a preceding upper respiratory tract infection or local skin lesion.
  • Progressive phase: Without prompt treatment, periorbital cellulitis may progress to orbital cellulitis, particularly beyond 2-3 days, with worsening symptoms and the development of proptosis, ophthalmoplegia, and vision changes.
  • Late phase: If untreated, orbital cellulitis can lead to severe complications such as vision loss, cavernous sinus thrombosis, or intracranial abscess formation, particularly beyond 5-7 days.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Periorbital and orbital cellulitis"][/sc]
  • Ask about eyelid swelling and redness - often the initial presentation, but the degree of swelling does not reliably distinguish periorbital from orbital cellulitis.
  • Ask about pain - typically localised to the eye or periorbital area, and may worsen with eye movements in orbital cellulitis.
  • Ask about fever and malaise - systemic symptoms that are more common and pronounced in orbital cellulitis, indicating a more severe infection.
  • Ask about changes in vision - blurred vision, diplopia, or reduced visual acuity are critical indicators of orbital involvement and potential optic nerve compromise.
  • Ask about proptosis (bulging of the eye) - a key sign of orbital cellulitis due to increased orbital pressure.
  • Ask about recent upper respiratory tract infection or local trauma - common predisposing factors that help establish the aetiology.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Periorbital and orbital cellulitis"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - fever, tachycardia, and hypotension may indicate sepsis, particularly in orbital cellulitis.
  • Check eyelid erythema and oedema - present in both conditions, but severe, tense oedema may obscure the globe in orbital cellulitis.
  • Check proptosis - assess for forward displacement of the globe, a definitive sign of orbital cellulitis.
  • Check ophthalmoplegia and pain on eye movements - restricted and painful eye movements are characteristic of orbital cellulitis due to inflammation of extraocular muscles.
  • Check pupillary reactions and visual acuity - afferent pupillary defect or reduced visual acuity are signs of optic nerve compression, requiring urgent intervention. Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Periorbital and orbital cellulitis"][/sc]
  • Proptosis: indicates orbital involvement, requiring urgent imaging and intravenous antibiotics.
  • Ophthalmoplegia or painful eye movements: suggests inflammation within the orbit, distinguishing it from periorbital cellulitis.
  • Reduced visual acuity or afferent pupillary defect: signs of optic nerve compression, a medical emergency requiring immediate ophthalmological review.
  • Systemic toxicity (high fever, malaise, signs of sepsis): suggests severe infection and potential for rapid deterioration.
  • Diplopia: can indicate extraocular muscle involvement or nerve compression within the orbit.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Periorbital and orbital cellulitis"][/sc]
  • Immunocompromised patients: may present with less pronounced inflammatory signs, making diagnosis challenging, and are at higher risk of fungal infections or rapid progression.
  • Neonates and infants: often present with non-specific symptoms like irritability, poor feeding, and lethargy, with eyelid swelling potentially being the only localised sign, requiring a high index of suspicion.
  • Diabetic patients: are at increased risk of aggressive infections, including mucormycosis, which can mimic bacterial orbital cellulitis but requires different management.
  • Post-surgical or post-traumatic cases: infection may be masked by initial oedema or haematoma, delaying diagnosis, and may involve unusual pathogens.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Periorbital and orbital cellulitis"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected orbital cellulitis: Full blood count (leukocytosis), C-reactive protein (elevated), blood cultures (to identify causative organism, especially if febrile).
  • Imaging - CT orbit with contrast: essential for distinguishing periorbital from orbital cellulitis, assessing the extent of orbital involvement, identifying abscess formation, and evaluating paranasal sinuses.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Consider orbital ultrasound for superficial collections or MRI for better soft tissue differentiation and intracranial extension if CT is equivocal or contraindicated.
  • Serial reassessment: Regular ophthalmological review and repeat imaging if clinical deterioration occurs despite treatment.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Periorbital and orbital cellulitis"][/sc]
  • Periorbital cellulitis - absence of proptosis, ophthalmoplegia, and visual disturbance.
  • Allergic reaction (e.g., angioedema) - typically bilateral, itchy, and not associated with fever or pain on eye movement.
  • Orbital pseudotumour - inflammatory condition, often painful, but typically chronic and lacks signs of infection.
  • Cavernous sinus thrombosis - often bilateral, severe headache, cranial nerve palsies, and proptosis, usually with a preceding facial infection.
  • Orbital tumour - usually slower onset, painless, and may cause proptosis without inflammatory signs.
  • Preseptal abscess - localised collection of pus anterior to the septum, may require incision and drainage.
  • Conjunctivitis - redness and discharge, but typically lacks significant eyelid swelling, pain, or systemic symptoms.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Periorbital and orbital cellulitis"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Urgent ophthalmological review, particularly for suspected orbital cellulitis, and initiation of broad-spectrum intravenous antibiotics.
  • Definitive management: Intravenous antibiotics (e.g., co-amoxiclav, cefotaxime, or clindamycin) covering common pathogens, with surgical drainage of any orbital or subperiosteal abscesses identified on imaging.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Close monitoring of visual acuity, pupillary reactions, eye movements, and systemic signs of infection; regular ophthalmological assessment.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Advise patients/carers on signs of worsening infection (e.g., increasing pain, vision changes, new proptosis) and the need for immediate re-evaluation.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Periorbital and orbital cellulitis"][/sc]
  • Vision loss: due to optic nerve compression from orbital oedema or abscess, or central retinal artery occlusion, which can occur rapidly and be irreversible.
  • Subperiosteal abscess: collection of pus between the orbital bone and periorbita, often requiring surgical drainage to prevent further complications.
  • Orbital abscess: collection of pus within the orbital fat, leading to increased orbital pressure and potential for vision loss.
  • Cavernous sinus thrombosis: spread of infection to the cavernous sinus, leading to severe headache, cranial nerve palsies, and high mortality.
  • Intracranial spread: meningitis, epidural abscess, or brain abscess can occur due to direct extension of infection through orbital fissures or venous drainage.
  • Sepsis: systemic inflammatory response to infection, potentially leading to multi-organ dysfunction and death if not promptly managed.
