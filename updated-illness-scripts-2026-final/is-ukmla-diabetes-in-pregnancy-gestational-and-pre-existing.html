Diabetes in pregnancy (Pregnancy and the puerperium) [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Diabetes in pregnancy"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Diabetes in pregnancy"][/sc]
  • Diabetes in pregnancy encompasses both **pre-existing diabetes** (type 1 or type 2 diabetes diagnosed before pregnancy) and **gestational diabetes mellitus (GDM)**, which is glucose intolerance first recognised during pregnancy.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the different types of diabetes in pregnancy, understand their implications for maternal and fetal health, and outline the principles of their management and monitoring.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Diabetes in pregnancy"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Diabetes in pregnancy"][/sc]
  • Gestational diabetes affects approximately 4-5% of pregnancies in the UK, with some reports suggesting prevalence between 10-20% depending on diagnostic criteria and population studied [1] [2] [3].
  • Risk factors for GDM include a BMI of 30 kg/m² or more, previous macrosomic baby (≥4.5 kg), previous GDM, family history of diabetes (first-degree relative), and certain ethnic origins (South Asian, Black Caribbean, Middle Eastern) [1].
  • Women with GDM have a significantly increased risk of developing type 2 diabetes later in life, with 15.2% progressing within 10 years [4].
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Diabetes in pregnancy"][/sc]
  • **Trigger:** During pregnancy, placental hormones (e.g., human placental lactogen, progesterone, cortisol) induce a state of **insulin resistance** to ensure adequate glucose supply to the fetus.
  • **Progression:** In women susceptible to GDM, the pancreas is unable to produce sufficient insulin to overcome this physiological insulin resistance, leading to maternal hyperglycaemia. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="What is insulin resistance in pregnancy and why does it matter clinically?" text="Insulin resistance"][/sc]
  • **Consequence:** Excess maternal glucose crosses the placenta, stimulating fetal insulin production (fetal hyperinsulinism), which promotes excessive fetal growth (macrosomia) and fat deposition, and can lead to neonatal complications. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="What is fetal hyperinsulinism and its clinical implications?" text="Fetal hyperinsulinism"][/sc]
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Diabetes in pregnancy"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Pre-existing diabetes requires careful preconception planning and early pregnancy management to minimise risks. Gestational diabetes typically develops in the second or third trimester, often detected by screening at 24-28 weeks.
  • Progressive phase: Maternal insulin resistance generally increases as pregnancy advances, necessitating adjustments in glucose-lowering therapy to maintain glycaemic control.
  • Late phase: After birth, GDM usually resolves rapidly, but women remain at high risk of developing type 2 diabetes in the long term. Neonatal complications, such as hypoglycaemia, typically manifest in the immediate postnatal period.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Diabetes in pregnancy"][/sc]
  • Ask about polyuria and polydipsia - these classic symptoms of hyperglycaemia may indicate undiagnosed or poorly controlled diabetes, though often subtle in GDM.
  • Ask about fatigue and lethargy - non-specific symptoms that can be exacerbated by hyperglycaemia or associated complications.
  • Ask about recurrent infections (e.g., thrush, urinary tract infections) - hyperglycaemia can impair immune function, increasing susceptibility to infections.
  • Ask about blurred vision - can be a symptom of acute hyperglycaemia or worsening diabetic retinopathy.
  • Ask about fetal movements - reduced fetal movements may indicate fetal compromise, which can be associated with poorly controlled diabetes.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Diabetes in pregnancy"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - blood pressure may be elevated in pre-existing hypertension or pre-eclampsia, which are more common in diabetic pregnancies.
  • Check fundal height - a larger than expected fundal height may suggest fetal macrosomia or polyhydramnios, both complications of diabetes in pregnancy. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="What is macrosomia and polyhydramnios in diabetes in pregnancy and why do they matter?" text="Macrosomia and polyhydramnios"][/sc]
  • Check for signs of peripheral neuropathy or retinopathy - these indicate long-standing diabetes and require careful monitoring during pregnancy.
  • Check for oedema - can be a sign of pre-eclampsia, which has increased incidence in diabetic pregnancies.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Diabetes in pregnancy"][/sc]
  • Diabetic ketoacidosis (DKA) - a life-threatening emergency requiring immediate admission for level 2 critical care, particularly in type 1 diabetes.
  • Severe hypoglycaemia - requires prompt treatment to prevent maternal and fetal harm, especially with impaired awareness of hypoglycaemia.
  • Reduced fetal movements - warrants urgent assessment of fetal wellbeing due to increased risk of stillbirth in diabetic pregnancies.
  • Signs of pre-eclampsia (e.g., new-onset hypertension, proteinuria) - indicates a serious maternal complication requiring close monitoring and management.
  • Rapidly worsening diabetic retinopathy - may require urgent ophthalmological review to prevent permanent vision loss.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Diabetes in pregnancy"][/sc]
  • Asymptomatic GDM: Many women with GDM are asymptomatic, highlighting the importance of routine screening via oral glucose tolerance test (OGTT) at 24-28 weeks gestation.
  • Early-onset GDM: GDM can sometimes present earlier in pregnancy, particularly in women with significant risk factors, necessitating earlier screening.
  • Pre-existing diabetes with superimposed GDM: Women with pre-existing diabetes can experience worsening insulin resistance during pregnancy, requiring intensified management strategies.
  • Postnatal persistence: While GDM typically resolves after birth, persistent hyperglycaemia may indicate undiagnosed type 2 diabetes, requiring postnatal follow-up and testing.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Diabetes in pregnancy"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected GDM: 75-g 2-hour oral glucose tolerance test (OGTT) at 24-28 weeks gestation. Diagnosis is made if fasting plasma glucose is ≥5.6 mmol/L or 2-hour plasma glucose is ≥7.8 mmol/L [1]. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="What are the NICE NG3 criteria for diagnosing gestational diabetes?" text="NICE NG3 GDM thresholds"][/sc]
  • Core for pre-existing diabetes: HbA1c levels for glycaemic control assessment.
  • Severity/risk tools: The HAPO study established associations between maternal glucose levels below diagnostic thresholds and adverse pregnancy outcomes, influencing current GDM diagnostic criteria. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="What was the significance of the HAPO study for gestational diabetes?" text="HAPO study"][/sc]
  • Imaging - Ultrasound: Serial ultrasound monitoring of fetal growth and amniotic fluid volume every 4 weeks from 28 to 36 weeks to detect macrosomia or polyhydramnios [1].
  • Serial reassessment: Self-monitoring of blood glucose (SMBG) multiple times daily (fasting and post-prandial) is crucial for guiding management and achieving glycaemic targets.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Diabetes in pregnancy"][/sc]
  • A pregnant woman in her second or third trimester, with risk factors such as obesity or a family history of diabetes, presenting with subtle symptoms of hyperglycaemia or identified through routine screening.
  • A pregnant woman with pre-existing type 1 or type 2 diabetes, requiring intensified glycaemic control and monitoring to prevent maternal and fetal complications.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Diabetes in pregnancy"][/sc]
  • **Pre-existing Type 1 Diabetes** - distinguished by presence of autoantibodies, often younger age at diagnosis, and absolute insulin deficiency.
  • **Pre-existing Type 2 Diabetes** - distinguished by diagnosis prior to pregnancy, often associated with obesity and insulin resistance, but not necessarily autoimmune.
  • **Physiological insulin resistance of pregnancy** - a normal adaptation to pregnancy, but when compensatory insulin secretion is insufficient, it progresses to GDM.
  • **Other causes of hyperglycaemia** - rare genetic forms of diabetes (e.g., MODY), pancreatic disease, or drug-induced hyperglycaemia.
  • **Polyhydramnios from other causes** - can be idiopathic or due to fetal anomalies (e.g., oesophageal atresia), not necessarily related to diabetes.
  • **Fetal macrosomia from other causes** - can be constitutional or due to other genetic syndromes, not exclusively linked to maternal diabetes.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Diabetes in pregnancy"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Recognition and prompt management of hyperglycaemia or hypoglycaemia. Escalation to a joint diabetes and antenatal care team is crucial.
  • Definitive management: Lifestyle modifications (dietary advice, regular exercise) are the first-line treatment for GDM. If glycaemic targets are not met, pharmacological therapy is initiated.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Frequent self-monitoring of blood glucose (SMBG) is essential. Regular antenatal appointments with the joint diabetes and antenatal team (every 1-2 weeks) are recommended for blood glucose control assessment and fetal monitoring.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Women should be educated on recognising and responding to symptoms of hyperglycaemia, hypoglycaemia, and reduced fetal movements. Clear pathways for urgent review should be provided.
  • Exam pharmacology reference (UKMLA/MRCGP): Metformin is often considered as an adjunct or alternative to insulin in pregnancy, particularly for GDM, when lifestyle modifications are insufficient. Insulin therapy (e.g., isophane insulin, rapid-acting analogues like aspart or lispro) is the mainstay for achieving glycaemic control in pre-existing diabetes and when oral agents are ineffective in GDM. Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Diabetes in pregnancy"][/sc]
  • Maternal complications: Increased risk of pre-eclampsia, polyhydramnios, preterm labour, operative delivery (caesarean section, instrumental delivery), and future development of type 2 diabetes.
  • Fetal complications: Macrosomia (leading to shoulder dystocia, birth trauma), intrauterine growth restriction (in severe pre-existing diabetes), congenital malformations (especially with poor preconception control), and stillbirth.
  • Neonatal complications: Neonatal hypoglycaemia, hyperbilirubinaemia, respiratory distress syndrome, polycythaemia, and cardiomyopathy. These typically manifest in the immediate postnatal period.
  • Long-term offspring complications: Increased risk of childhood obesity, impaired glucose tolerance, and type 2 diabetes in later life.
