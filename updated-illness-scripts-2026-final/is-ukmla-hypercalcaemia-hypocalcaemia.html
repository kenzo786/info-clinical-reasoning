Hypercalcaemia/ hypocalcaemia [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Hypercalcaemia/ hypocalcaemia"]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Hypercalcaemia/ hypocalcaemia"]
  • **Hypercalcaemia** is defined as a serum corrected calcium level above 2.6 mmol/L, while **hypocalcaemia** is defined as a serum corrected calcium level below 2.1 mmol/L. These conditions reflect imbalances in calcium homeostasis, a tightly regulated physiological process crucial for numerous bodily functions.
  • **UKMLA Learning Outcome:** Understand the causes, clinical features, investigations, and management of disorders of calcium metabolism, including hypercalcaemia and hypocalcaemia.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Hypercalcaemia/ hypocalcaemia"]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Hypercalcaemia/ hypocalcaemia"]
  • **Hypercalcaemia** affects approximately 1-4% of the general population in the UK, with primary hyperparathyroidism being the most common cause in outpatients and malignancy in inpatients.
  • **Hypocalcaemia** is less common in the general population but can be prevalent in hospitalised patients, particularly those in critical care, affecting up to 85% of critically ill individuals.
  • The incidence of primary hyperparathyroidism in the UK is estimated at 20-30 cases per 100,000 person-years, with a higher prevalence in post-menopausal women.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Hypercalcaemia/ hypocalcaemia"]
  • **Trigger:** Disruption of calcium homeostasis, often due to excessive parathyroid hormone (PTH) secretion (e.g., primary hyperparathyroidism), increased bone resorption (e.g., malignancy), or impaired vitamin D metabolism. Conversely, hypocalcaemia can result from PTH deficiency (e.g., post-thyroidectomy), vitamin D deficiency, or increased calcium excretion.
  • **Progression:** In hypercalcaemia, elevated calcium levels lead to increased renal calcium reabsorption, bone demineralisation, and gastrointestinal absorption. In hypocalcaemia, low calcium levels stimulate PTH release (if parathyroids are functional), leading to increased bone resorption and renal reabsorption, alongside impaired vitamin D activation.
  • **Consequence:** Sustained hypercalcaemia can cause renal calculi, bone pain, gastrointestinal upset, and neurological dysfunction. Chronic hypocalcaemia can lead to neuromuscular irritability (tetany), cardiac arrhythmias, and long-term skeletal abnormalities. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Calcium homeostasis: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Calcium homeostasis"] is critical for maintaining cellular function.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Hypercalcaemia/ hypocalcaemia"]
  • Early phase: Acute changes in calcium levels can manifest rapidly, within hours to days, often triggered by acute illness, medication changes, or surgical interventions.
  • Progressive phase: Chronic hypercalcaemia (e.g., primary hyperparathyroidism) or hypocalcaemia (e.g., chronic kidney disease) can develop insidiously over months to years, with symptoms becoming more pronounced as the imbalance persists.
  • Late phase: Untreated, severe or prolonged calcium imbalances can lead to irreversible organ damage, such as chronic kidney disease, osteoporosis, or severe cardiac dysfunction.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Hypercalcaemia/ hypocalcaemia"]
  • Ask about polyuria and polydipsia - these are common in hypercalcaemia due to impaired renal concentrating ability.
  • Ask about bone pain or fractures - indicative of increased bone resorption in hypercalcaemia or long-standing hypocalcaemia.
  • Ask about muscle cramps or spasms - a hallmark symptom of hypocalcaemia due to neuromuscular irritability.
  • Ask about fatigue, weakness, or lethargy - non-specific but common in both conditions, reflecting systemic metabolic disturbance.
  • Ask about constipation or abdominal pain - often seen in hypercalcaemia due to reduced gut motility.
  • Ask about paraesthesia (tingling around the mouth or in extremities) - a classic symptom of hypocalcaemia.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Hypercalcaemia/ hypocalcaemia"]
  • Check vital signs - hypercalcaemia can cause bradycardia and hypertension; hypocalcaemia can cause hypotension and arrhythmias.
  • Check for Chvostek's sign - facial muscle twitching upon tapping the facial nerve, indicative of hypocalcaemia.
  • Check for Trousseau's sign - carpal spasm induced by inflating a blood pressure cuff, indicative of hypocalcaemia.
  • Check for muscle weakness or hypotonia - can be present in severe hypercalcaemia.
  • Check for altered mental status - ranging from confusion to coma in severe cases of both hypercalcaemia and hypocalcaemia.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Hypercalcaemia/ hypocalcaemia"]
  • Severe Hypercalcaemia (>3.5 mmol/L): Indicates a medical emergency requiring urgent intervention due to risk of cardiac arrest, coma, and acute kidney injury.
  • Acute Symptomatic Hypocalcaemia (e.g., tetany, seizures): Requires immediate intravenous calcium replacement to prevent life-threatening complications like laryngospasm or cardiac arrhythmias.
  • New-onset Confusion or Seizures: Can be a manifestation of severe calcium imbalance, necessitating prompt investigation and management.
  • Cardiac Arrhythmias or ECG Changes: Both hypercalcaemia (shortened QT interval) and hypocalcaemia (prolonged QT interval) can cause significant cardiac instability.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Hypercalcaemia/ hypocalcaemia"]
  • Elderly Population: May present with more subtle or non-specific symptoms such as cognitive impairment or generalised weakness, making diagnosis challenging.
  • Patients with Renal Impairment: Calcium levels can be difficult to interpret due to altered vitamin D metabolism and phosphate retention, often leading to secondary hyperparathyroidism or adynamic bone disease.
  • Critically Ill Patients: Often develop hypocalcaemia due to sepsis, pancreatitis, or massive transfusions, which can be masked by other severe illness symptoms.
  • Asymptomatic Hypercalcaemia: Frequently discovered incidentally on routine blood tests, particularly in primary hyperparathyroidism, requiring careful monitoring rather than immediate aggressive treatment.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Hypercalcaemia/ hypocalcaemia"]
  • Core Investigations:
    • Serum corrected calcium, albumin, phosphate, magnesium, urea, creatinine, and electrolytes.
    • Parathyroid hormone (PTH) levels: Crucial for differentiating PTH-mediated from non-PTH-mediated causes.
    • 25-hydroxyvitamin D levels: To assess vitamin D status.
    • Alkaline phosphatase: May indicate bone turnover.
  • Imaging:
    • Renal ultrasound: To check for nephrocalcinosis or nephrolithiasis in hypercalcaemia.
    • DEXA scan: To assess bone mineral density, especially in chronic hypercalcaemia.
    • Sestamibi scan or parathyroid ultrasound: For localisation of parathyroid adenomas in primary hyperparathyroidism.
  • If alternative diagnoses considered:
    • Serum protein electrophoresis: To exclude multiple myeloma in hypercalcaemia of malignancy.
    • ECG: To assess for cardiac arrhythmias or QT interval changes.
  • Serial reassessment:
    • Regular monitoring of calcium, phosphate, and PTH levels is often warranted to assess treatment efficacy and disease progression.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Hypercalcaemia/ hypocalcaemia"]
  • A 65-year-old female presents with a 3-month history of increasing fatigue, polyuria, and generalised bone aches, alongside a recent incidental finding of elevated serum calcium.
  • This is a **gradual-onset, systemic, metabolic derangement** in an **elderly female** presenting with **non-specific symptoms** and **biochemical abnormalities**, suggesting a chronic calcium homeostasis disorder.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Hypercalcaemia/ hypocalcaemia"]
  • **Primary Hyperparathyroidism** - typically mild, asymptomatic hypercalcaemia with elevated or inappropriately normal PTH.
  • **Malignancy-associated Hypercalcaemia** - often severe hypercalcaemia with suppressed PTH, common in solid tumours (PTHrP) or haematological malignancies (bone destruction).
  • **Vitamin D Toxicity** - elevated 25-hydroxyvitamin D and hypercalcaemia, usually due to excessive supplementation.
  • **Drug-induced Hypercalcaemia** - e.g., thiazide diuretics (decreased renal calcium excretion) or lithium (alters parathyroid set point).
  • **Hypoparathyroidism** - low PTH and hypocalcaemia, often post-surgical (thyroidectomy) or autoimmune.
  • **Vitamin D Deficiency** - low 25-hydroxyvitamin D and hypocalcaemia, leading to secondary hyperparathyroidism.
  • **Chronic Kidney Disease** - complex calcium and phosphate abnormalities, often leading to secondary or tertiary hyperparathyroidism.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Hypercalcaemia/ hypocalcaemia"]
  • Immediate priorities (for severe hypercalcaemia):
    • Intravenous 0.9% sodium chloride to restore euvolaemia and promote calciuresis.
    • Consider bisphosphonates (e.g., zoledronic acid) to inhibit osteoclastic bone resorption.
    • Consider calcitonin for rapid but short-lived calcium reduction.
  • Immediate priorities (for severe hypocalcaemia):
    • Intravenous calcium gluconate for symptomatic hypocalcaemia (e.g., tetany, seizures).
    • Address underlying magnesium deficiency if present, as it can impair PTH secretion and action.
  • Definitive management:
    • **Hypercalcaemia:** Treat the underlying cause (e.g., parathyroidectomy for primary hyperparathyroidism, management of malignancy).
    • **Hypocalcaemia:** Oral calcium and vitamin D supplementation for chronic hypocalcaemia; manage underlying causes like hypoparathyroidism or malabsorption.
  • Monitoring and follow-up:
    • Regular monitoring of serum calcium, phosphate, PTH, and renal function.
    • Bone mineral density assessment for long-term complications.
  • Escalation and safety-netting:
    • Advise patients on symptoms of worsening calcium imbalance and when to seek urgent medical attention.
    • Referral to endocrinology for complex or refractory cases.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Hypercalcaemia/ hypocalcaemia"]
  • Nephrolithiasis (Kidney Stones): In hypercalcaemia, increased urinary calcium excretion can lead to calcium phosphate or calcium oxalate stone formation, causing renal colic and potential obstruction.
  • Acute Kidney Injury: Severe hypercalcaemia can impair renal concentrating ability (nephrogenic diabetes insipidus) and cause vasoconstriction, leading to reduced glomerular filtration rate.
  • Osteoporosis/Bone Demineralisation: Chronic hypercalcaemia (especially primary hyperparathyroidism) causes persistent bone resorption, increasing fracture risk. Chronic hypocalcaemia can also affect bone health if the underlying cause is not addressed.
  • Cardiac Arrhythmias: Hypercalcaemia can shorten the QT interval and cause bradycardia, while hypocalcaemia prolongs the QT interval, both increasing the risk of life-threatening arrhythmias.
  • Neurological Dysfunction: Both conditions can cause altered mental status, ranging from lethargy and confusion to seizures and coma in severe cases, due to direct effects on neuronal excitability.
  • Vascular Calcification: Chronic hypercalcaemia, particularly in the context of renal disease, can contribute to the deposition of calcium in blood vessels, increasing cardiovascular risk.
