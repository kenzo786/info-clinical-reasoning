Liver abscess [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Liver abscess"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Liver abscess"][/sc]
  • A liver abscess is a pus-filled lesion within the liver parenchyma, typically resulting from bacterial or parasitic infection.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical presentation, common causes, and initial management of a liver abscess.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Liver abscess"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Liver abscess"][/sc]
  • The crude annual incidence of liver abscess in the UK is approximately 7.0 per 100,000 population, though older data suggests rates around 2.3 per 100,000 per year [1, 2].
  • Key risk factors include diabetes mellitus, liver cirrhosis, immunocompromised states (e.g., neutropenia), underlying hepatobiliary or pancreatic disease, and chronic use of proton-pump inhibitors [3, 4, 5].

Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Liver abscess"][/sc]
  • Infection typically originates from the gastrointestinal tract, with bacteria or parasites reaching the liver via the portal vein, often secondary to conditions like appendicitis, diverticulitis, or cholangitis [1]. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Portal vein: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Portal vein"][/sc]
  • Microorganisms proliferate within the liver parenchyma, triggering an intense inflammatory response that leads to localised tissue necrosis and the formation of a pus-filled cavity. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Hepatocyte necrosis: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Hepatocyte necrosis"][/sc]
  • If untreated, the abscess can expand, potentially rupture into adjacent structures (e.g., pleura, peritoneum), or lead to systemic sepsis and multi-organ dysfunction.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Liver abscess"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Symptoms often develop insidiously over days to weeks, typically presenting with non-specific complaints such as fever, malaise, and right upper quadrant discomfort.
  • Progressive phase: Without intervention, symptoms intensify, and localised signs of inflammation become more prominent, potentially leading to significant systemic illness particularly beyond 2-3 weeks.
  • Late phase: Complications such as rupture, sepsis, or multi-organ failure can occur if diagnosis and treatment are delayed, often several weeks after initial symptom onset.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Liver abscess"][/sc]
  • Ask about fever, chills, and night sweats - these are common systemic inflammatory responses indicating infection.
  • Ask about right upper quadrant (RUQ) pain - this is a cardinal symptom due to hepatic inflammation and distension of Glisson's capsule.
  • Ask about nausea or vomiting - non-specific gastrointestinal symptoms that can accompany systemic illness.
  • Ask about malaise and fatigue - general constitutional symptoms often present with chronic or subacute infections.
  • Ask about right shoulder pain - this can be referred pain from diaphragmatic irritation due to an abscess in the superior aspect of the liver.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Liver abscess"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - fever and tachycardia are common, indicating systemic inflammatory response or sepsis.
  • Check for right upper quadrant tenderness - a key finding due to inflammation and distension of the liver capsule.
  • Check for hepatomegaly - an enlarged liver may be palpable and tender.
  • Check for jaundice - may indicate biliary obstruction or severe hepatic dysfunction, particularly in cases of cholangitis-related abscesses.
  • Check for intercostal tenderness or decreased breath sounds in the right lower lobe - suggests diaphragmatic irritation or reactive pleural effusion, especially with superiorly located abscesses.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Liver abscess"][/sc]
  • Signs of sepsis: Tachycardia, hypotension, altered mental status, or significant leukocytosis indicate systemic inflammatory response and require urgent management.
  • Sudden worsening of abdominal pain or peritonism: May suggest abscess rupture into the peritoneal cavity, a life-threatening complication.
  • Respiratory distress or pleuritic chest pain: Could indicate diaphragmatic irritation, reactive pleural effusion, or rupture into the pleural space.
  • Jaundice with worsening liver function tests: Suggests significant biliary obstruction or extensive hepatic involvement, potentially requiring urgent drainage.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Liver abscess"][/sc]
  • Older adults: May present with non-specific symptoms such as malaise, anorexia, and confusion, with less prominent fever or abdominal pain, making diagnosis challenging. Underlying biliary disease or malignancy is more common in this group.
  • Immunocompromised patients: (e.g., those with diabetes, on immunosuppressants, or with malignancy) may have blunted inflammatory responses, leading to delayed presentation and diagnosis. They are also at higher risk for fungal liver abscesses.
  • Pregnancy: Liver abscess is rare but can be a severe cause of maternal sepsis. Symptoms can be non-specific and overlap with common pregnancy-related discomforts, potentially delaying diagnosis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Liver abscess"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected liver abscess: Full blood count (leukocytosis), liver function tests (often deranged), inflammatory markers (elevated CRP/ESR), and blood cultures (positive in ~50% of cases). Abscess fluid aspiration and culture are crucial for definitive microbiological diagnosis.
  • Imaging - Ultrasound: Often the initial imaging modality, showing hypoechoic or hyperechoic lesions. Useful for guiding aspiration.
  • Imaging - CT scan: More sensitive than ultrasound (up to 97%) for detecting liver abscesses, typically showing a hypoattenuating lesion with peripheral enhancement. Essential for assessing size, number, and location, and for planning drainage.
  • Imaging - MRI: Can be used for further characterisation, especially if CT findings are equivocal or for differentiating from other liver lesions.
  • Serial reassessment: Regular clinical review, repeat inflammatory markers, and follow-up imaging (ultrasound or CT) are essential to monitor treatment response and detect complications.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Liver abscess"][/sc]
  • A 55-year-old male presents with a 1-week history of worsening right upper quadrant pain, fever, chills, and malaise, following a recent episode of diverticulitis. On examination, he is febrile, tachycardic, and has marked tenderness in the right hypochondrium.
  • Middle-aged male, subacute onset, systemic inflammatory response, localised abdominal pain, recent intra-abdominal infection, hepatic involvement.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Liver abscess"][/sc]
  • Acute Cholecystitis/Cholangitis - typically associated with gallstones and specific imaging findings of gallbladder or bile duct inflammation.
  • Hepatocellular Carcinoma (HCC) or Metastatic Liver Disease - can mimic abscess on imaging; distinguished by tumour markers, different enhancement patterns on imaging, and often lack of acute inflammatory markers.
  • Right Lower Lobe Pneumonia - presents with respiratory symptoms (cough, dyspnoea) and characteristic chest X-ray findings, though referred pain can occur.
  • Acute Appendicitis (especially retrocaecal) - pain typically localises to the right iliac fossa, though atypical positions can cause RUQ pain; distinguished by imaging of the appendix.
  • Pyelonephritis - usually presents with loin pain, dysuria, and urinary symptoms; urinalysis and renal imaging are key differentiators.
  • Viral Hepatitis - diffuse liver inflammation with specific viral serology, typically without a focal pus collection.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Liver abscess"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Stabilisation of the patient, particularly if signs of sepsis are present, including fluid resuscitation and prompt initiation of empiric broad-spectrum antibiotics.
  • Definitive management: Percutaneous drainage of the abscess, guided by ultrasound or CT, is the cornerstone of treatment for most liver abscesses. This is combined with targeted antibiotic therapy based on culture results.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Close clinical monitoring, serial inflammatory markers (e.g., CRP), and repeat imaging (ultrasound or CT) are essential to assess treatment response and ensure complete resolution.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Surgical drainage may be required if percutaneous drainage fails, for multi-loculated abscesses, or in cases of abscess rupture. Patients should be advised on red flag symptoms for recurrence or complications.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Liver abscess"][/sc]
  • Abscess rupture: Can occur into the peritoneal cavity, pleural space, or pericardium, leading to peritonitis, empyema, or pericarditis, respectively. This is a life-threatening event requiring urgent surgical intervention.
  • Sepsis/Septic shock: Systemic dissemination of bacteria from the abscess can lead to severe sepsis and septic shock, characterised by organ dysfunction and hypotension, significantly increasing mortality.
  • Pleural effusion/Empyema: Inflammation or direct extension from a superiorly located liver abscess can cause a reactive pleural effusion or, if ruptured, an empyema, leading to respiratory compromise.
  • Multi-organ failure: Prolonged sepsis or severe inflammatory response can lead to dysfunction of multiple organs, including kidneys, lungs, and cardiovascular system, particularly in delayed presentations.
