Conjunctivitis [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Conjunctivitis"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Conjunctivitis"][/sc]
  • Conjunctivitis is an inflammation of the conjunctiva, the transparent membrane lining the eyelid and covering the white part of the eye. It can be caused by infection (bacterial or viral) or allergy.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the different types of conjunctivitis, differentiate between infectious and non-infectious causes, identify red flag features, and outline appropriate initial management, including urgent referral for neonatal conjunctivitis.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Conjunctivitis"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Conjunctivitis"][/sc]
  • Conjunctivitis is a very common condition, with viral causes being the most frequent, followed by bacterial and allergic types [1].
  • Infective conjunctivitis is highly contagious and often spreads rapidly in close-contact settings such as schools and nurseries [2].
  • Allergic conjunctivitis commonly co-occurs with other atopic conditions like hay fever, asthma, and eczema, affecting a significant proportion of the UK population [3].
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Conjunctivitis"][/sc]
  • Infectious conjunctivitis is typically triggered by direct contact with viral particles (e.g., adenovirus) or bacteria (e.g., Staphylococcus aureus, Haemophilus influenzae) which colonise the conjunctiva.
  • This leads to an inflammatory response in the conjunctiva, characterised by vasodilation, oedema, and cellular infiltration, resulting in the classic signs of redness and discharge. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Conjunctival inflammation: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Conjunctival inflammation"][/sc]
  • Allergic conjunctivitis involves an IgE-mediated hypersensitivity reaction to airborne allergens, causing mast cell degranulation and release of histamine and other inflammatory mediators, leading to intense itching and watery discharge.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Conjunctivitis"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Symptoms of infective conjunctivitis typically develop rapidly over 24-72 hours. Allergic conjunctivitis symptoms can be immediate upon allergen exposure.
  • Progressive phase: Bacterial conjunctivitis often resolves spontaneously within 5-7 days, though symptoms may persist longer, particularly beyond one week, if untreated. Viral conjunctivitis can last 1-2 weeks, with some forms persisting for several weeks.
  • Late phase: Allergic conjunctivitis symptoms resolve upon removal of the allergen or with appropriate treatment, but can recur seasonally or with re-exposure. Chronic forms may persist for months.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Conjunctivitis"][/sc]
  • Ask about eye redness - distinguishing between unilateral (often bacterial/viral initially) and bilateral (often allergic or advanced infectious) presentation.
  • Ask about discharge - characterising as purulent/sticky (bacterial), watery (viral/allergic), or stringy (allergic) to guide aetiology.
  • Ask about itching - a prominent feature in allergic conjunctivitis, less so in infectious types.
  • Ask about foreign body sensation or grittiness - common in all forms, but can be more pronounced in viral conjunctivitis.
  • Ask about photophobia - mild photophobia can occur, but severe photophobia may indicate a more serious underlying condition.
  • Ask about associated symptoms - such as upper respiratory tract infection (viral), sneezing/rhinorrhoea (allergic), or recent contact with infected individuals.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Conjunctivitis"][/sc]
  • Check conjunctival injection - diffuse redness of the conjunctiva, often more prominent peripherally.
  • Check discharge - purulent (bacterial), watery (viral), or mucoid/stringy (allergic).
  • Check follicular reaction - small lymphoid follicles on the palpebral conjunctiva, characteristic of viral conjunctivitis. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Follicular conjunctivitis: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Follicular conjunctivitis"][/sc]
  • Check papillary reaction - fine vascular projections on the palpebral conjunctiva, often seen in allergic conjunctivitis.
  • Check preauricular lymphadenopathy - often present in viral conjunctivitis, particularly adenovirus.
  • Check visual acuity, pupillary reactions, and vital signs - typically normal in uncomplicated conjunctivitis; any reduction in visual acuity, abnormal pupillary reaction, or fever warrants urgent review for more serious pathology.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Conjunctivitis"][/sc]
  • Neonatal conjunctivitis (less than 30 days old): Any red or sticky eye in a neonate requires urgent assessment due to the risk of gonococcal or chlamydial infection, which can lead to severe complications like corneal perforation or blindness. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Neonatal conjunctivitis: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Neonatal conjunctivitis urgency"][/sc]
  • Reduced visual acuity: Suggests corneal involvement or other serious ocular pathology beyond simple conjunctivitis.
  • Severe eye pain or photophobia: Indicates potential keratitis, uveitis, or acute glaucoma.
  • Corneal opacity or ulceration: A serious sign requiring immediate ophthalmological review.
  • Fixed pupil or abnormal pupillary reactions: May indicate acute angle-closure glaucoma or other severe intraocular pathology.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Conjunctivitis"][/sc]
  • Contact lens wearers: Higher risk of bacterial keratitis, particularly with pseudomonas, which can mimic conjunctivitis but is sight-threatening.
  • Immunocompromised individuals: May present with more severe or prolonged infections, or with unusual pathogens.
  • Elderly patients: May have reduced inflammatory response, leading to less pronounced redness, but increased risk of complications.
  • Chlamydial conjunctivitis: Often presents as a chronic, follicular conjunctivitis that is unresponsive to standard antibiotic drops.
  • Gonococcal conjunctivitis: Characterised by hyperacute onset with copious purulent discharge, an ocular emergency requiring systemic treatment.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Conjunctivitis"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected conjunctivitis: Clinical diagnosis based on history and examination is usually sufficient. Swabs for microbiology are generally not required in uncomplicated cases.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Consider fluorescein staining to rule out corneal abrasions or ulcers, and tonometry to measure intraocular pressure if glaucoma is suspected.
  • Severity/risk tools: Consider using the [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Red Eye Algorithm: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Red Eye Algorithm"][/sc] for systematic assessment of red eye causes, available on Red Eye Algorithm on MDCalc.
  • For neonatal conjunctivitis: Urgent conjunctival swabs for bacterial culture, Gram stain, and PCR for Chlamydia trachomatis and Neisseria gonorrhoeae are crucial.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Conjunctivitis"][/sc]
  • A 28-year-old female presents with a 3-day history of bilateral red, itchy eyes with watery discharge, worse in the mornings, and associated with seasonal sneezing.
  • Bilateral ocular inflammation with prominent itching and watery discharge, concerning for allergic conjunctivitis.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Conjunctivitis"][/sc]
  • Bacterial Conjunctivitis - typically presents with purulent, sticky discharge and often unilateral onset, less itching.
  • Viral Conjunctivitis - often associated with watery discharge, follicular reaction, preauricular lymphadenopathy, and frequently a preceding upper respiratory tract infection.
  • Allergic Conjunctivitis - characterised by intense itching, bilateral involvement, watery or stringy discharge, and often a history of atopy.
  • Keratitis (Corneal Inflammation) - usually presents with more severe pain, photophobia, and reduced visual acuity; fluorescein staining may reveal corneal defects. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Keratitis vs Conjunctivitis: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Keratitis"][/sc]
  • Acute Angle-Closure Glaucoma - severe eye pain, sudden vision loss, fixed mid-dilated pupil, and a hard eye on palpation.
  • Uveitis - often presents with pain, photophobia, and ciliary flush (redness around the iris), but typically no discharge.
  • Scleritis/Episcleritis - localised redness and tenderness, often without discharge, and may be associated with systemic inflammatory conditions.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Conjunctivitis"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Exclude red flag features, provide safety-netting advice, and advise on hygiene measures to prevent spread.
  • Definitive management:
    • Bacterial: Most cases are self-limiting. Topical antibiotics (e.g., chloramphenicol, fusidic acid) may be considered for severe cases, contact lens wearers, or if symptoms do not improve after 48 hours.
    • Viral: Supportive care with lubricating eye drops and cold compresses. Antivirals are generally not indicated unless herpes simplex keratitis is suspected.
    • Allergic: Allergen avoidance, topical antihistamines (e.g., olopatadine), mast cell stabilisers (e.g., sodium cromoglicate), or dual-action agents. Systemic antihistamines may be used for severe symptoms.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Advise patients to seek review if symptoms worsen, new symptoms develop, or do not resolve within the expected timeframe (e.g., 5-7 days for bacterial, 1-2 weeks for viral).
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Urgent ophthalmological referral for any red flag features, particularly neonatal conjunctivitis.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Conjunctivitis"][/sc]
  • Corneal involvement (Keratitis): Untreated or severe infective conjunctivitis, particularly bacterial or herpetic viral forms, can extend to the cornea, leading to keratitis, ulceration, and potentially permanent vision loss.
  • Spread of infection: Highly contagious forms, especially viral, can easily spread to close contacts and other ocular structures.
  • Chronic conjunctivitis: Persistent inflammation, particularly in allergic or chlamydial forms, can lead to chronic discomfort, scarring, and visual impairment over an extended period.
  • Neonatal blindness: Untreated gonococcal or chlamydial neonatal conjunctivitis can rapidly progress to corneal damage and blindness if not managed urgently within hours to days of onset.
  • Membrane/pseudomembrane formation: Severe adenoviral conjunctivitis can cause the formation of membranes or pseudomembranes on the conjunctiva, which may require removal and can lead to scarring.
