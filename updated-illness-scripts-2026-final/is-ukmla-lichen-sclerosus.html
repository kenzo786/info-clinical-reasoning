Lichen sclerosus [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Lichen sclerosus"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Lichen sclerosus"][/sc]
  • Lichen sclerosus is a chronic inflammatory dermatosis primarily affecting the anogenital skin, characterised by patchy, discoloured, thin, and often wrinkled skin.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: The candidate must recognise the clinical features, complications, and management principles of lichen sclerosus, particularly in its anogenital presentation.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Lichen sclerosus"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Lichen sclerosus"][/sc]
  • Lichen sclerosus predominantly affects women, with a bimodal distribution peaking in prepubertal girls and postmenopausal women [1].
  • The estimated prevalence in women is around 1 in 30 to 1 in 1000, though it is likely underdiagnosed [2].
  • Risk factors include genetic predisposition, autoimmune conditions (e.g., thyroid disease, vitiligo), and possibly local trauma or infection [3].
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Lichen sclerosus"][/sc]
  • Triggering factors, such as genetic predisposition, hormonal changes, or local trauma, may initiate an autoimmune response targeting components of the extracellular matrix in the skin.
  • This leads to chronic inflammation, characterised by lymphocytic infiltration and altered collagen synthesis, resulting in epidermal atrophy and dermal sclerosis.
  • The consequence is the characteristic thin, white, and fragile skin, prone to tearing, bruising, and scarring, with an increased risk of squamous cell carcinoma.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Lichen sclerosus"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Often insidious onset with mild pruritus or discomfort, which may be dismissed or misdiagnosed for several months.
  • Progressive phase: Symptoms typically worsen over months to years, with increasing pruritus, soreness, and the development of characteristic skin changes, particularly beyond 6-12 months without treatment.
  • Late phase: Chronic, untreated disease can lead to significant architectural changes, scarring, introital stenosis, and an increased risk of malignancy, particularly beyond several years.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Lichen sclerosus"][/sc]
  • Ask about pruritus (itching) - often severe, particularly nocturnal, and can significantly impact quality of life.
  • Ask about soreness or pain - especially with sexual activity (dyspareunia) or defaecation, indicating skin fragility and fissuring.
  • Ask about skin changes - such as white patches, thinning skin, blistering, or bruising, which are characteristic features.
  • Ask about dysuria or urinary symptoms - due to involvement of the periurethral area or meatal stenosis.
  • Ask about anal discomfort or bleeding - if perianal skin is involved, leading to fissuring or pain on defaecation.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Lichen sclerosus"][/sc]
  • Check for white, atrophic, or sclerotic plaques - typically in a figure-of-eight distribution around the vulva and perianal area, indicating chronic inflammation and scarring.
  • Check for excoriations, fissures, or purpura - suggesting severe pruritus and skin fragility.
  • Check for architectural changes - such as labial resorption, clitoral hood fusion, or introital stenosis, which are late-stage complications.
  • Check for signs of secondary infection - such as candidiasis or bacterial infection, which can complicate symptoms.
  • Check vital signs - usually normal, but may reveal signs of systemic illness if co-existing autoimmune conditions are present.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Lichen sclerosus"][/sc]
  • Persistent ulceration or non-healing lesions - may indicate progression to squamous cell carcinoma, particularly in areas of chronic inflammation.
  • New onset bleeding or discharge - warrants investigation for malignancy or secondary infection.
  • Rapidly progressive architectural changes - such as severe introital stenosis or clitoral hood fusion, requiring urgent specialist assessment.
  • Unresponsive symptoms to appropriate treatment - suggests incorrect diagnosis, inadequate treatment, or underlying malignancy.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Lichen sclerosus"][/sc]
  • Children: May present with perianal soreness, constipation, or vulval itching, often misdiagnosed as sexual abuse or candidiasis.
  • Men: Typically affects the glans penis and foreskin (balanitis xerotica obliterans), leading to phimosis, dysuria, or meatal stenosis.
  • Extragenital involvement: Can occur on the trunk, neck, or limbs, presenting as white, atrophic patches, often asymptomatic.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Lichen sclerosus"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected Lichen sclerosus: Clinical diagnosis based on characteristic appearance. Biopsy is often performed to confirm diagnosis, exclude malignancy, or differentiate from other dermatoses.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Swabs for candidiasis or bacterial infection if secondary infection is suspected.
  • Imaging - Ultrasound: May be used in cases of severe architectural distortion or suspected underlying masses, though not routinely required for diagnosis.
  • Serial reassessment: Regular follow-up with clinical examination is crucial to monitor disease progression and screen for malignancy.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Lichen sclerosus"][/sc]
  • A 68-year-old postmenopausal woman presents with a 12-month history of intense vulval itching, soreness, and dyspareunia, noting white patches and thinning of the skin around her labia.
  • An elderly female with chronic, progressive vulval pruritus and pain, associated with characteristic atrophic skin changes, raising concern for a chronic inflammatory dermatosis with malignant potential.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Lichen sclerosus"][/sc]
  • Vulval candidiasis - typically acute onset, with erythema, oedema, and discharge, often responsive to antifungal treatment.
  • Eczema/Dermatitis - usually presents with more diffuse erythema, scaling, and less pronounced architectural changes.
  • Lichen planus - can affect mucous membranes and skin, often with purplish, polygonal papules and Wickham\'s striae.
  • Vulval intraepithelial neoplasia (VIN) - may present with persistent itching or lesions, but often has a more irregular, hyperkeratotic appearance, and requires biopsy for definitive diagnosis.
  • Psoriasis - well-demarcated erythematous plaques with silvery scales, often affecting other body sites.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Lichen sclerosus"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Symptomatic relief of itching and soreness with emollients and potent topical corticosteroids.
  • Definitive management: Long-term use of potent topical corticosteroids (e.g., clobetasol propionate) to reduce inflammation and prevent disease progression.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Regular clinical review to assess treatment response, monitor for complications, and screen for malignant transformation.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Referral to dermatology or gynaecology for confirmed diagnosis, unresponsive cases, or suspicion of malignancy.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Lichen sclerosus"][/sc]
  • Squamous cell carcinoma (SCC): Chronic inflammation and scarring significantly increase the risk of vulval SCC, particularly in long-standing, untreated disease.
  • Architectural distortion: Labial resorption, clitoral hood fusion, and introital stenosis can lead to dyspareunia, sexual dysfunction, and urinary symptoms.
  • Psychological impact: Chronic pain, itching, and body image concerns can lead to significant distress, anxiety, and depression.
  • Secondary infection: Skin fragility and excoriations predispose to bacterial or fungal infections, exacerbating symptoms.
