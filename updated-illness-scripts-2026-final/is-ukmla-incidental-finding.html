Incidental finding [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Incidental finding"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Incidental finding"][/sc]
  • An unexpected discovery of an abnormality on imaging or during a clinical examination, often unrelated to the primary reason for the investigation.
  • UKMLA Learning Outcome: Recognise the implications of incidental findings and formulate an appropriate management plan.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Incidental finding"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Incidental finding"][/sc]
  • Prevalence varies widely depending on the imaging modality and population screened; for example, incidental adrenal masses are found in approximately 4% of abdominal CT scans in the UK.
  • Risk factors for clinically significant incidental findings include increasing age and a history of malignancy.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Incidental finding"][/sc]
  • Trigger: The primary investigation for an unrelated condition leads to the discovery of an unexpected abnormality.
  • Progression: The significance of the incidental finding is evaluated based on its characteristics, patient history, and potential for malignancy or functional impairment.
  • Consequence: Further investigations, surveillance, or intervention may be required, leading to patient anxiety and potential iatrogenic harm.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Incidental finding"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Initial detection and communication of the finding to the patient, often causing immediate concern.
  • Progressive phase: Subsequent evaluation and follow-up to determine the nature and clinical relevance of the finding.
  • Late phase: Long-term management, which may involve surveillance, intervention, or reassurance, depending on the final diagnosis.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Incidental finding"][/sc]
  • Ask about pain - to determine if the finding is causing discomfort or is asymptomatic.
  • Ask about weight loss - to screen for potential underlying malignancy.
  • Ask about fever - to assess for infectious processes associated with the finding.
  • Ask about changes in bowel habit - relevant for abdominal incidentalomas.
  • Ask about shortness of breath - important for thoracic incidental findings.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Incidental finding"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - to identify any acute physiological derangements.
  • Check for palpable masses - to correlate with imaging findings and assess consistency.
  • Check for lymphadenopathy - suggestive of systemic disease or malignancy.
  • Check for organomegaly - to evaluate for enlargement of affected organs.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Incidental finding"][/sc]
  • Rapid growth: Suggests an aggressive or malignant process.
  • New onset symptoms: Indicates the incidental finding may now be clinically significant.
  • Systemic symptoms (e.g., unexplained weight loss, night sweats): Raises suspicion for malignancy or chronic infection.
  • Abnormal laboratory results: May point to functional activity (e.g., endocrine incidentaloma) or systemic impact.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Incidental finding"][/sc]
  • Paediatric population: Incidental findings may have different implications and require specific paediatric protocols.
  • Elderly patients: Higher prevalence of benign age-related changes, but also increased risk of malignancy, complicating assessment.
  • Immunocompromised individuals: Increased risk of opportunistic infections or unusual malignancies presenting as incidental findings.
  • Pregnant women: Management must consider potential risks to both mother and foetus, often favouring non-ionising imaging.
