Acid-base abnormality [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Acid-base abnormality"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Acid-base abnormality"][/sc]
  • A deviation from the normal physiological pH range in the body, often resulting from imbalances in bicarbonate or carbon dioxide levels.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical features and initial management of common acid-base disturbances.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Acid-base abnormality"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Acid-base abnormality"][/sc]
  • Acid-base abnormalities are common in hospitalised patients, with metabolic acidosis affecting up to 15% of admissions in the UK, often linked to renal dysfunction or sepsis.
  • Risk factors include chronic kidney disease, diabetes mellitus, severe sepsis, respiratory failure, and certain medication use (e.g., diuretics, metformin).
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Acid-base abnormality"][/sc]
  • Trigger: An initial disturbance in the balance of acid production/excretion or bicarbonate regulation, such as increased acid load from lactic acidosis or loss of bicarbonate from diarrhoea.
  • Progression: Compensatory mechanisms, primarily respiratory (altering CO2 excretion) and renal (adjusting H+ excretion and bicarbonate reabsorption), attempt to restore pH homeostasis.
  • Consequence: If compensatory mechanisms are overwhelmed, persistent pH derangement can lead to enzyme dysfunction, altered cellular metabolism, and impaired organ function.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Acid-base abnormality"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Acute changes, such as hyperventilation in metabolic acidosis, can occur within minutes to hours, particularly beyond 6 hours.
  • Progressive phase: Renal compensation, involving adjustments in bicarbonate reabsorption and acid excretion, typically develops over 12-24 hours, particularly beyond 1 day.
  • Late phase: Chronic or uncorrected abnormalities can lead to sustained physiological adaptations and potential organ damage over days to weeks, particularly beyond 3 days.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Acid-base abnormality"][/sc]
  • Ask about dyspnoea - as it may indicate respiratory compensation for metabolic acidosis (Kussmaul breathing) or primary respiratory acidosis.
  • Ask about altered mental status - as severe acidosis or alkalosis can impair cerebral function, leading to confusion or reduced consciousness.
  • Ask about muscle weakness or cramps - as electrolyte disturbances, particularly potassium, often co-exist with acid-base imbalances.
  • Ask about nausea and vomiting - as these can be both a cause (e.g., gastric acid loss) and a symptom of severe acid-base derangements.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Acid-base abnormality"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - particularly respiratory rate (tachypnoea/bradypnoea) and heart rate (tachycardia/bradycardia), which reflect compensatory efforts or severe physiological stress.
  • Check neurological status - for signs of confusion, lethargy, or seizures, indicating severe cerebral dysfunction.
  • Check respiratory pattern - for Kussmaul respirations (deep, rapid breathing) in metabolic acidosis or shallow breathing in respiratory alkalosis.
  • Check hydration status - as dehydration can contribute to metabolic acidosis, and fluid overload can exacerbate respiratory acidosis.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Acid-base abnormality"][/sc]
  • Severe pH derangement (<7.2 or >7.55) - indicates critical physiological instability requiring urgent intervention.
  • Rapidly worsening respiratory distress - suggests decompensating respiratory acidosis or severe metabolic acidosis with respiratory fatigue.
  • New onset or worsening altered consciousness - points to significant cerebral impairment due to severe acid-base disturbance.
  • Cardiac arrhythmias - severe acidosis or alkalosis can predispose to life-threatening dysrhythmias.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Acid-base abnormality"][/sc]
  • Older adults: May present with more subtle or non-specific symptoms like falls or generalised weakness, making diagnosis challenging due to reduced physiological reserve.
  • Pregnancy: Mild respiratory alkalosis is physiological, but severe acid-base disturbances can have significant implications for both mother and foetus, requiring careful interpretation.
  • Immunosuppressed: More prone to severe infections leading to lactic acidosis or renal tubular acidosis, with potentially blunted compensatory responses.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Acid-base abnormality"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected acid-base abnormality: Arterial Blood Gas (ABG) analysis is essential for immediate and accurate assessment of pH, pCO2, and bicarbonate.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Serum electrolytes (Na, K, Cl, Bicarbonate) to calculate anion gap, renal function tests (urea, creatinine), and lactate levels to identify causes of metabolic acidosis.
  • Imaging - Chest X-ray: May be indicated if respiratory pathology is suspected as the cause of respiratory acidosis or alkalosis.
  • Serial reassessment: Repeat ABGs and electrolytes are crucial to monitor response to treatment and detect evolving disturbances.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Acid-base abnormality"][/sc]
  • A 68-year-old male with a history of type 2 diabetes presents with 2 days of increasing lethargy, deep rapid breathing, and nausea, found to have a pH of 7.18 and bicarbonate of 10 mmol/L on ABG.
  • An elderly diabetic male presenting with acute decompensated metabolic acidosis, characterised by profound lethargy and compensatory hyperventilation.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Acid-base abnormality"][/sc]
  • Diabetic Ketoacidosis (DKA) - distinguished by high blood glucose, ketones in urine/blood, and a high anion gap metabolic acidosis.
  • Lactic Acidosis - distinguished by elevated lactate levels, often due to sepsis, hypoperfusion, or organ failure.
  • Renal Tubular Acidosis (RTA) - distinguished by normal anion gap metabolic acidosis with impaired renal acid excretion, often chronic.
  • Salicylate Poisoning - distinguished by mixed acid-base disorder (respiratory alkalosis and metabolic acidosis) and specific drug history.
  • Hyperventilation Syndrome - distinguished by primary respiratory alkalosis, often anxiety-induced, without significant metabolic derangement.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Acid-base abnormality"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Address the underlying cause (e.g., fluid resuscitation for hypovolaemia, insulin for DKA, oxygen for respiratory failure) and ensure adequate ventilation and oxygenation.
  • Definitive management: Correct the primary disturbance, which may involve intravenous fluids, electrolyte replacement, or mechanical ventilation, guided by serial ABG results.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Close monitoring of vital signs, neurological status, and serial blood gas analyses is essential to assess response to treatment and detect complications.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Consider early involvement of critical care for severe or rapidly deteriorating cases; advise patients on symptoms that warrant immediate medical review.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Acid-base abnormality"][/sc]
  • Cardiac arrhythmias: Severe acidosis or alkalosis can destabilise myocardial electrical activity, leading to potentially fatal dysrhythmias, particularly beyond 24 hours.
  • Impaired organ function: Profound pH changes can reduce cardiac contractility, impair cerebral autoregulation, and worsen renal perfusion, especially if uncorrected for more than 48 hours.
  • Respiratory muscle fatigue: Prolonged compensatory hyperventilation in metabolic acidosis can lead to respiratory muscle exhaustion and eventual respiratory failure, particularly beyond 2 days.
  • Electrolyte disturbances: Acid-base imbalances often cause shifts in potassium, calcium, and phosphate, leading to further clinical complications if not managed concurrently.
