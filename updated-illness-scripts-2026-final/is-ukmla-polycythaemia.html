Polycythaemia [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Polycythaemia"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Polycythaemia"][/sc]
  • Polycythaemia, also known as erythrocytosis, is a condition characterised by an abnormally high red blood cell mass, leading to increased haematocrit and haemoglobin concentration [1].
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical features and potential complications of polycythaemia, distinguishing between primary and secondary causes, and outline initial management principles.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Polycythaemia"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Polycythaemia"][/sc]
  • Polycythaemia vera (PV) is a rare myeloproliferative neoplasm with an estimated incidence of 0.7–2.6 per 100,000 people per year in Europe and North America [1].
  • It typically presents in individuals around 60 years of age and shows a slight male predominance [1] [2].
  • Risk factors for polycythaemia include the presence of the JAK2 V617F mutation (in primary polycythaemia) and conditions leading to chronic hypoxia (e.g., chronic lung disease, sleep apnoea) or erythropoietin-secreting tumours (in secondary polycythaemia) [2].
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Polycythaemia"][/sc]
  • Trigger: Primary polycythaemia, specifically Polycythaemia Vera (PV), is often triggered by an acquired somatic mutation in the Janus kinase 2 (JAK2) gene, most commonly the JAK2 V617F mutation, leading to constitutive activation of the JAK-STAT pathway [1] [2]. Secondary polycythaemia is triggered by increased erythropoietin (EPO) production, often in response to chronic hypoxia or from EPO-secreting tumours [2].
  • Progression: In PV, the activated JAK-STAT pathway results in uncontrolled proliferation of myeloid stem cells, particularly erythroid precursors, independent of EPO, leading to an overproduction of red blood cells, white blood cells, and platelets [1]. In secondary polycythaemia, sustained high EPO levels stimulate red blood cell production in the bone marrow [2].
  • Consequence: The increased red cell mass elevates blood viscosity, increasing the risk of thrombotic events such as deep vein thrombosis, pulmonary embolism, and arterial occlusions, including stroke and myocardial infarction. Long-term PV can also progress to myelofibrosis or acute myeloid leukaemia [1] [2]. Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Polycythaemia"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Polycythaemia, particularly Polycythaemia Vera (PV), often has an insidious onset and may be asymptomatic for many years, with diagnosis often made incidentally on routine blood tests showing elevated haemoglobin and haematocrit [1]. Non-specific symptoms such as fatigue, headache, dizziness, and pruritus (especially after bathing) may be present [2].
  • Progressive phase: Over time, the disease can progress, leading to an increased risk of thrombotic events (e.g., deep vein thrombosis, myocardial infarction, stroke) and microvascular disturbances. Splenomegaly may develop, and constitutional symptoms can become more pronounced, often necessitating cytoreductive therapy [1] [2].
  • Late phase: In a subset of patients, particularly beyond 10-15 years from diagnosis, PV can transform into post-PV myelofibrosis, characterised by bone marrow fibrosis, worsening anaemia, and extramedullary haematopoiesis. A small percentage may also transform into acute myeloid leukaemia, which carries a poor prognosis [1] [2].
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Polycythaemia"][/sc]
  • Ask about pruritus - often worse after hot water exposure (aquagenic pruritus), suggesting increased histamine release from basophils and mast cells, common in PV [1].
  • Ask about headache, dizziness, or visual disturbances - these microvascular symptoms are due to increased blood viscosity and impaired cerebral blood flow [1].
  • Ask about fatigue or weakness - a common, non-specific symptom that can significantly impact quality of life in polycythaemia [1].
  • Ask about erythromelalgia - burning pain and redness, typically in the hands and feet, caused by microvascular occlusion and platelet aggregation, often relieved by aspirin [1].
  • Ask about abdominal discomfort or early satiety - may indicate splenomegaly, a common finding in PV due to extramedullary haematopoiesis [1].
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Polycythaemia"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - particularly blood pressure, as hypertension is common and contributes to thrombotic risk [1].
  • Check for splenomegaly - a palpable spleen is a common finding in PV, indicating extramedullary haematopoiesis [1] [2]. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Splenomegaly in polycythaemia: what does it imply?" text="Splenomegaly"][/sc]
  • Check for plethora or facial flushing - due to increased red blood cell mass and capillary engorgement [1].
  • Check for hepatomegaly - less common than splenomegaly, but can occur due to extramedullary haematopoiesis [1].
  • Check for signs of thrombosis - such as swelling, tenderness, or warmth in a limb (DVT), or neurological deficits (stroke) [1].
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Polycythaemia"][/sc]
  • New neurological deficit: Suggests an acute thrombotic event such as stroke or transient ischaemic attack, requiring urgent neurological assessment and management [1].
  • Acute chest pain or dyspnoea: May indicate myocardial infarction or pulmonary embolism, necessitating immediate cardiac or respiratory evaluation [1].
  • Sudden, severe abdominal pain: Could signify splanchnic vein thrombosis (e.g., Budd-Chiari syndrome), a serious complication requiring prompt investigation and intervention [1].
  • Unexplained bleeding or bruising: While thrombotic events are more common, extreme thrombocytosis can paradoxically lead to acquired von Willebrand disease and bleeding, requiring haematological review [2].
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Polycythaemia"][/sc]
  • Masked Polycythaemia Vera: Patients, particularly males with borderline haematocrit levels (e.g., 0.48-0.52), may have a true erythrocytosis despite not meeting standard diagnostic thresholds, potentially leading to delayed diagnosis and management of thrombotic risk [2].
  • JAK2-negative Polycythaemia Vera: A very rare variant where the JAK2 V617F mutation is absent, requiring more extensive investigation to exclude secondary causes and other myeloproliferative neoplasms. Diagnostic criteria include raised red cell mass, absence of JAK2 mutation, no secondary cause, and specific bone marrow histology [2].
  • Polycythaemia in pregnancy: Diagnosis can be challenging due to physiological haemodilution. Gestation-specific haematocrit ranges should be used, and management requires careful consideration of fetal and maternal risks, often involving venesection and low-dose aspirin [2].
  • Iron-deficient Polycythaemia Vera: Chronic iron deficiency, often due to repeated venesections or occult bleeding, can mask the true extent of erythrocytosis by limiting red cell production, leading to a misleadingly low haematocrit [2]. Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Polycythaemia"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected Polycythaemia: Full blood count (FBC) to assess haemoglobin, haematocrit, white blood cell count, and platelet count. A blood film should be reviewed for atypical features. Serum erythropoietin (EPO) levels are crucial to differentiate primary (low EPO) from secondary (normal/high EPO) polycythaemia. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Erythropoietin levels in polycythaemia: what do they indicate?" text="Serum Erythropoietin"][/sc]
  • Genetic testing: JAK2 V617F mutational analysis in peripheral blood is recommended for suspected PV, as it is present in over 95% of cases. If negative, JAK2 exon 12 analysis should be considered [2].
  • Further investigations for JAK2 V617F-negative erythrocytosis: Red cell mass studies can confirm absolute erythrocytosis. Abdominal ultrasound can detect splenomegaly and exclude renal/hepatic pathology as secondary causes [2].
  • Severity/risk tools: Risk stratification for thrombosis in PV is based on age (≥65 years) and prior history of thrombosis. Cardiovascular risk factors (smoking, diabetes mellitus, arterial hypertension, hypercholesterolaemia) also contribute to thrombotic risk [2]. Consider using the ASCVD (Atherosclerotic Cardiovascular Disease) 2013 Risk Calculator on MDCalc for overall cardiovascular risk assessment.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Polycythaemia"][/sc]
  • A 68-year-old male presents with a several-month history of increasing fatigue, generalised pruritus exacerbated by hot baths, and a recent incidental finding of elevated haemoglobin and haematocrit on routine blood tests, with a palpable spleen on examination.
  • Elderly male with chronic, progressive myeloproliferative symptoms and signs of hyperviscosity, concerning for Polycythaemia Vera.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Polycythaemia"][/sc]
  • Secondary Polycythaemia (Hypoxia-driven): Elevated red cell mass due to chronic hypoxia (e.g., chronic obstructive pulmonary disease, sleep apnoea, high altitude) or conditions causing local renal hypoxia (e.g., renal artery stenosis). Distinguished by normal or elevated EPO levels and absence of JAK2 mutation [2].
  • Secondary Polycythaemia (EPO-secreting tumour): Elevated red cell mass due to autonomous EPO production from tumours (e.g., hepatocellular carcinoma, renal cell carcinoma, cerebellar haemangioblastoma). Distinguished by very high EPO levels and absence of JAK2 mutation [2].
  • Apparent/Relative Polycythaemia: Elevated haematocrit due to reduced plasma volume (e.g., dehydration, severe burns) rather than an increase in red cell mass. Red cell mass studies will be normal [2].
  • Essential Thrombocythaemia (ET): A myeloproliferative neoplasm primarily characterised by elevated platelet count. While JAK2 V617F mutation can be present, haemoglobin and haematocrit levels are typically below PV diagnostic thresholds, and erythrocytosis is not the primary feature [2].
  • Chronic Myeloid Leukaemia (CML): A myeloproliferative neoplasm characterised by the Philadelphia chromosome (BCR-ABL1 fusion gene) and marked leucocytosis. While it can present with increased cell counts, the genetic marker and predominant white cell involvement distinguish it from PV [2].
  • Familial Erythrocytosis: Rare inherited conditions causing increased red cell production, often due to mutations in genes involved in oxygen sensing or transport (e.g., VHL, EGLN1, EPAS1, high oxygen affinity haemoglobin). Distinguished by family history and specific genetic testing [2]. Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Polycythaemia"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Recognition of acute thrombotic events (e.g., stroke, myocardial infarction) and prompt escalation for emergency management. Supportive care, including hydration, may be considered [1].
  • Definitive management: The primary goal is to reduce the risk of thromboembolic events. This typically involves venesection to maintain haematocrit below 0.45, and low-dose aspirin (e.g., 75-100 mg daily) for most patients [1] [2].
  • Cytoreductive therapy: Considered for high-risk patients (age ≥65 years or prior thrombosis) or those with uncontrolled haematocrit despite venesection, progressive splenomegaly, severe symptoms, or extreme thrombocytosis. Hydroxycarbamide is often the first-line cytoreductive agent [1] [2].
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Regular monitoring of FBC, symptoms, and assessment of cardiovascular risk factors. Patients on cytoreductive therapy require close monitoring for side effects [1].
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Patients should be educated on symptoms of thrombosis or bleeding and advised to seek urgent medical attention if these occur. Referral to haematology is essential for diagnosis and ongoing management [1].

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Polycythaemia"][/sc]
  • Thrombotic events: Increased blood viscosity and platelet dysfunction significantly elevate the risk of arterial (e.g., myocardial infarction, stroke) and venous (e.g., deep vein thrombosis, pulmonary embolism, Budd-Chiari syndrome) thromboses, which can occur at any stage of the disease and are the leading cause of morbidity and mortality [1] [2].
  • Haemorrhage: Paradoxically, extreme thrombocytosis can lead to acquired von Willebrand disease, increasing the risk of bleeding, particularly from the gastrointestinal tract [2].
  • Myelofibrotic transformation: In the progressive phase, a subset of PV patients may develop post-PV myelofibrosis, characterised by progressive bone marrow fibrosis, extramedullary haematopoiesis, worsening anaemia, and increasing splenomegaly [1] [2].
  • Leukaemic transformation: A rare but serious late-phase complication where PV transforms into acute myeloid leukaemia, carrying a very poor prognosis [1] [2].
  • Gout and hyperuricaemia: Increased cell turnover leads to elevated uric acid levels, predisposing patients to gouty arthritis and renal calculi [1].
