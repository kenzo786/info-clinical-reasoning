Lung cancer [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Lung cancer"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Lung cancer"][/sc]
  • Lung cancer is a malignant tumour arising from the respiratory epithelium within the lung parenchyma or bronchi.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the varied presentations of lung cancer, differentiate between NSCLC and SCLC, identify key diagnostic investigations, and understand the principles of management and palliative care.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Lung cancer"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Lung cancer"][/sc]
  • Lung cancer is the 3rd most common cancer in the UK, accounting for 13% of all new cancer cases (2017-2019 data from Cancer Research UK).
  • Around 48,000 new cases are diagnosed annually in the UK (Cancer Research UK).
  • Incidence and prevalence have increased, particularly in females and older populations (Corby et al., 2024).
  • Risk factors include smoking (85-90% of cases), occupational exposure (e.g., asbestos, radon, silica), air pollution, family history, and previous lung disease (e.g., COPD, pulmonary fibrosis).
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Lung cancer"][/sc]
  • Trigger: Chronic exposure to carcinogens (e.g., tobacco smoke) leads to genetic mutations in bronchial epithelial cells.
  • Progression: Accumulation of mutations drives uncontrolled cell proliferation, leading to the formation of a primary tumour.
  • Consequence: Local invasion into surrounding lung tissue, pleura, and mediastinum, followed by distant metastasis via lymphatic and haematogenous spread. Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Lung cancer"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Often asymptomatic, or non-specific symptoms such as persistent cough, dyspnoea, or fatigue. Tumour growth is typically slow, particularly for NSCLC.
  • Progressive phase: Symptoms become more pronounced and persistent, including haemoptysis, chest pain, weight loss, and recurrent infections. Local invasion and regional lymph node involvement may occur.
  • Late phase: Development of paraneoplastic syndromes, superior vena cava obstruction, pleural effusion, and distant metastases (e.g., brain, bone, liver, adrenal glands). Prognosis is poor at this stage.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Lung cancer"][/sc]
  • Ask about persistent cough - a new or changed cough lasting >3 weeks, often productive or dry, indicating bronchial irritation or obstruction.
  • Ask about haemoptysis - coughing up blood, even small amounts, suggests bronchial mucosal erosion or tumour necrosis.
  • Ask about dyspnoea - shortness of breath, which may be due to airway obstruction, pleural effusion, or lymphangitic carcinomatosis.
  • Ask about unexplained weight loss and fatigue - systemic effects of malignancy, indicating advanced disease.
  • Ask about chest pain - persistent, localised pain, especially if pleuritic, may indicate pleural or chest wall invasion.
  • Ask about hoarseness - due to recurrent laryngeal nerve palsy from mediastinal lymph node involvement.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Lung cancer"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - tachypnoea, tachycardia, and hypoxaemia may indicate respiratory compromise or systemic illness.
  • Check for finger clubbing - a non-specific sign associated with chronic hypoxia and paraneoplastic syndromes.
  • Check for supraclavicular or cervical lymphadenopathy - palpable, firm, non-tender nodes suggest metastatic spread.
  • Check for Horner syndrome - unilateral ptosis, miosis, and anhidrosis, indicative of Pancoast tumour invading the sympathetic chain. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Pancoast tumour: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Pancoast tumour"][/sc]
  • Check for superior vena cava obstruction - facial oedema, plethora, and dilated neck veins, suggesting mediastinal compression.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Lung cancer"][/sc]
  • Haemoptysis - warrants urgent investigation to exclude malignancy, particularly in smokers or those with risk factors.
  • Persistent unexplained chest pain - especially if new onset and unresponsive to simple analgesia, may indicate chest wall invasion or pleural involvement.
  • Unexplained weight loss >10% body weight over 6 months - a significant indicator of underlying malignancy.
  • New onset neurological symptoms (e.g., seizures, focal weakness) - suggestive of brain metastases, requiring urgent imaging.
  • Stridor - indicates significant central airway obstruction, requiring immediate assessment and intervention.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Lung cancer"][/sc]
  • Older adults: May present with non-specific symptoms like fatigue, anorexia, or general decline, often attributed to age or comorbidities, delaying diagnosis.
  • Women: Historically, lung cancer was less common in women, but incidence is rising. May present with adenocarcinoma, often without a smoking history.
  • Non-smokers: Can develop lung cancer, often adenocarcinoma, due to genetic predisposition, environmental factors (e.g., radon, secondhand smoke), or occupational exposures.
  • Paraneoplastic syndromes: Can precede the diagnosis of lung cancer, presenting with endocrine, neurological, dermatological, or haematological manifestations (e.g., SIADH, Eaton-Lambert syndrome). Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Lung cancer"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected lung cancer: Contrast-enhanced chest CT scan (including liver, adrenals, and lower neck) to assess tumour extent and staging. PET-CT for all people with curative intent.
  • Severity/risk tools: ECOG Performance Status on MDCalc (for assessing functional status and guiding treatment decisions).
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Sputum cytology (if central lesion and cannot tolerate invasive tests), bronchoscopy with biopsy, image-guided biopsy for peripheral lesions.
  • Imaging - CT: Essential for initial diagnosis, staging, and monitoring response to treatment.
  • Imaging - PET-CT: Used for staging and detecting metastatic disease, particularly before curative intent treatment.
  • Imaging - MRI: Used to assess the extent of disease in superior sulcus tumours or for suspected brain metastases.
  • Histopathological diagnosis: Biopsy (bronchoscopic, image-guided, or surgical) for tissue diagnosis, tumour subtyping (NSCLC vs SCLC), and molecular marker assessment (e.g., EGFR, ALK, PD-L1). Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Lung cancer"][/sc]
  • A 68-year-old male smoker presents with a 3-month history of persistent cough, haemoptysis, and unintentional weight loss, alongside new onset right arm pain and ptosis.
  • An elderly smoker with progressive respiratory symptoms and signs of local invasion, concerning for advanced lung cancer with paraneoplastic features.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Lung cancer"][/sc]
  • Chronic Obstructive Pulmonary Disease (COPD) exacerbation - typically a history of smoking, but lung cancer should be excluded in new or worsening symptoms. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Distinguishing features of lung cancer from COPD: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Distinguishing lung cancer from COPD"][/sc]
  • Pneumonia - acute onset, fever, productive cough, and consolidation on imaging, but persistent or recurrent pneumonia in the same lobe should raise suspicion for underlying malignancy.
  • Tuberculosis - chronic cough, weight loss, night sweats, and apical lung lesions, but often associated with specific risk factors and positive microbiology.
  • Metastatic disease from other primary - history of other cancer, but lung cancer can be a primary or secondary cause of lung lesions.
  • Benign lung nodule - often asymptomatic, stable on imaging, but requires careful follow-up and may need biopsy to exclude malignancy.
  • Mesothelioma - history of asbestos exposure, often presents with pleural effusion and chest pain, but distinct from lung cancer in cell type and origin.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Lung cancer"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Recognition of red flag features, urgent referral to a lung cancer multidisciplinary team (MDT), and symptomatic management (e.g., pain control, oxygen therapy).
  • Definitive management: Treatment approach is individualised based on cancer type (NSCLC vs SCLC), stage, performance status, and molecular markers. Options often include surgery, radiotherapy, chemotherapy, targeted therapy, and immunotherapy.
  • Adjunctive management: Smoking cessation support, nutritional support, psychological support, and management of comorbidities.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Regular clinical review, imaging (CT scans), and symptom assessment to monitor disease progression, treatment response, and identify complications.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Clear instructions for patients on when to seek urgent medical attention for worsening symptoms or new concerns.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Lung cancer"][/sc]
  • Superior vena cava obstruction: Occurs due to external compression or direct invasion of the SVC by tumour or lymph nodes, leading to facial and upper limb oedema, dyspnoea, and headache. This can develop acutely and requires urgent intervention.
  • Pleural effusion: Accumulation of fluid in the pleural space, often malignant, causing dyspnoea and pleuritic chest pain. This can be a progressive complication.
  • Brain metastases: Spread of cancer cells to the brain, leading to neurological deficits, seizures, and cognitive impairment. These can develop at any stage, particularly in late phase disease.
  • Paraneoplastic syndromes: Remote effects of the tumour not due to direct invasion or metastasis, such as SIADH, Eaton-Lambert syndrome, or hypercalcaemia. These can manifest early in the disease course and may precede cancer diagnosis.
  • Spinal cord compression: Due to vertebral metastases, causing back pain, neurological deficits, and bladder/bowel dysfunction. This is a medical emergency requiring urgent assessment.
