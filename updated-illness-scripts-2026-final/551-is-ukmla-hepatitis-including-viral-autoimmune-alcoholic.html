Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Hepatitis (including viral autoimmune alcoholic)"][/sc]
  • Hepatitis is an inflammation of the liver, often caused by viral infections (Hepatitis A, B, C), autoimmune processes, or alcohol misuse, leading to hepatocellular damage and dysfunction.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the common causes and clinical presentations of hepatitis, differentiate between acute and chronic forms, and understand the principles of initial investigation and management.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Hepatitis (including viral autoimmune alcoholic)"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Hepatitis (including viral autoimmune alcoholic)"][/sc]
  • Chronic hepatitis B affects approximately 180,000 people in the UK, with higher prevalence in certain ethnic minority groups [1]. Risk factors include vertical transmission, unprotected sexual contact, and intravenous drug use.
  • Hepatitis C is the most common cause of chronic viral hepatitis in the UK, with an estimated 118,000 people chronically infected [2]. Risk factors include past or current intravenous drug use, blood transfusions before 1991, and needlestick injuries.
  • Alcoholic hepatitis incidence is strongly linked to alcohol consumption patterns, with a rising trend in the UK [3]. Risk factors include prolonged heavy alcohol consumption, female sex, and genetic predisposition.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Hepatitis (including viral autoimmune alcoholic)"][/sc]
  • Trigger: Viral infection (HBV, HCV, HAV), immune dysregulation (autoimmune hepatitis), or toxic metabolites of alcohol initiate hepatocellular injury.
  • Progression: Inflammation and hepatocyte necrosis lead to varying degrees of fibrosis and architectural distortion. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Liver fibrosis: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Liver fibrosis"][/sc]
  • Consequence: Persistent inflammation can result in cirrhosis, liver failure, and hepatocellular carcinoma.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Hepatitis (including viral autoimmune alcoholic)"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Acute hepatitis (viral or alcoholic) typically manifests within days to weeks of exposure, characterised by initial inflammatory response.
  • Progressive phase: Chronic hepatitis (HBV, HCV, autoimmune) can persist for months to years, often asymptomatic, with gradual progression of liver damage, particularly beyond 6 months.
  • Late phase: Advanced liver disease, including cirrhosis and its complications, may develop over years to decades if underlying hepatitis is not controlled.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Hepatitis (including viral autoimmune alcoholic)"][/sc]
  • Ask about fatigue - a common, non-specific symptom that can be debilitating in chronic hepatitis, impacting quality of life.
  • Ask about nausea and vomiting - often present in acute hepatitis, indicating systemic inflammation and liver dysfunction.
  • Ask about right upper quadrant pain - suggests liver inflammation or distension of Glisson\'s capsule.
  • Ask about jaundice - indicates impaired bilirubin metabolism, a key sign of significant liver injury.
  • Ask about dark urine and pale stools - further evidence of impaired bilirubin excretion and cholestasis.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Hepatitis (including viral autoimmune alcoholic)"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - fever, tachycardia, and hypotension may indicate severe inflammation or systemic complications.
  • Check scleral icterus - a visible sign of jaundice, indicating elevated bilirubin levels.
  • Check hepatomegaly - an enlarged liver, often tender, suggesting inflammation or congestion.
  • Check asterixis - a flapping tremor, indicative of hepatic encephalopathy in severe liver dysfunction. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Asterixis: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Asterixis"][/sc]
  • Check spider naevi - vascular lesions that suggest chronic liver disease and portal hypertension.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Hepatitis (including viral autoimmune alcoholic)"][/sc]
  • Acute liver failure: Rapid onset of severe liver dysfunction with encephalopathy and coagulopathy in a patient without pre-existing liver disease, often within days to weeks.
  • Hepatic encephalopathy: Altered mental status ranging from subtle cognitive changes to coma, indicating severe liver dysfunction and impaired detoxification.
  • Variceal haemorrhage: Acute upper gastrointestinal bleeding from ruptured oesophageal or gastric varices, a life-threatening complication of portal hypertension.
  • Spontaneous bacterial peritonitis: Infection of ascitic fluid without an obvious source, common in cirrhotic patients and associated with high mortality.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Hepatitis (including viral autoimmune alcoholic)"][/sc]
  • Older adults: May present with non-specific symptoms like malaise or confusion, delaying diagnosis and increasing risk of complications.
  • Immunosuppressed patients: Can have fulminant hepatitis or reactivation of chronic viral hepatitis with minimal symptoms, requiring a high index of suspicion.
  • Pregnancy: Viral hepatitis can impact maternal and foetal outcomes; autoimmune hepatitis may flare or remit, requiring careful management.
  • Children: Acute viral hepatitis is often asymptomatic or mild; chronic hepatitis can lead to cirrhosis and liver failure if not identified and managed early. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Neonatal hepatitis: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Neonatal hepatitis"][/sc]
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Hepatitis (including viral autoimmune alcoholic)"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected viral hepatitis: Liver function tests (ALT, AST, bilirubin), coagulation screen (INR), full blood count.
  • Specific viral serology:
    • Hepatitis B: HBsAg, anti-HBs, HBeAg, anti-HBe, anti-HBc (total and IgM). [sc name="jitl-query" Q="HBV serology interpretation: what do the markers mean?" text="HBV serology interpretation"][/sc]
    • Hepatitis C: Anti-HCV antibodies, HCV RNA.
    • Hepatitis A: Anti-HAV IgM, Anti-HAV IgG.
  • Core for suspected autoimmune hepatitis: Autoantibodies (ANA, anti-SMA, anti-LKM1), IgG levels.
  • Severity/risk tools: MELD Score on MDCalc - used to assess prognosis in chronic liver disease and prioritise for transplant.
  • Imaging - Ultrasound: To assess liver size, morphology, and exclude biliary obstruction.
  • Liver biopsy: May be considered for definitive diagnosis, staging of fibrosis, and assessment of inflammation, particularly in autoimmune hepatitis.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Hepatitis (including viral autoimmune alcoholic)"][/sc]
  • A 45-year-old male presents with 3 days of worsening fatigue, nausea, and jaundice, with a history of heavy alcohol consumption.
  • Middle-aged male with acute-on-chronic liver injury, concerning for alcoholic hepatitis.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Hepatitis (including viral autoimmune alcoholic)"][/sc]
  • Drug-induced liver injury (DILI) - often a clear temporal relationship to new medication or overdose; can mimic viral or autoimmune hepatitis.
  • Acute cholangitis - typically presents with Charcot\'s triad (fever, RUQ pain, jaundice) and evidence of biliary obstruction on imaging.
  • Ischaemic hepatitis - usually follows a hypotensive episode, with a rapid and significant rise in transaminases followed by quick resolution.
  • Wilson\'s disease - rare genetic disorder, consider in younger patients with neuropsychiatric symptoms and Kayser-Fleischer rings. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Wilson\'s disease: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Wilson\'s disease"][/sc]
  • Haemochromatosis - genetic iron overload disorder, consider in patients with bronze diabetes, cardiomyopathy, and arthropathy.
  • Alpha-1 antitrypsin deficiency - genetic disorder, consider in patients with early-onset emphysema and liver disease.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Hepatitis (including viral autoimmune alcoholic)"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Recognition of acute liver failure or severe alcoholic hepatitis often warrants urgent specialist assessment and supportive care, including fluid management and nutritional support.
  • Definitive management:
    • Viral hepatitis: Antiviral therapy may be considered for chronic HBV (e.g., [sc name="jitl-medication" Q="Tenofovir use in Hepatitis B" text="Tenofovir"][/sc], [sc name="jitl-medication" Q="Entecavir use in Hepatitis B" text="Entecavir"][/sc]) or HCV (e.g., direct-acting antivirals).
    • Autoimmune hepatitis: Immunosuppression with corticosteroids (e.g., [sc name="jitl-medication" Q="Prednisolone use in Autoimmune Hepatitis" text="Prednisolone"][/sc]) often with azathioprine.
    • Alcoholic hepatitis: Complete alcohol abstinence is crucial; nutritional support and corticosteroids may be considered in severe cases.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Regular assessment of liver function, viral load (for viral hepatitis), and imaging to monitor disease progression and screen for complications.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Patients with decompensated liver disease or acute deterioration often require hospital admission and urgent specialist input.
  • Exam pharmacology reference (UKMLA/MRCGP): Antiviral agents for HBV (e.g., Tenofovir, Entecavir) and HCV (direct-acting antivirals), and immunosuppressants for autoimmune hepatitis (e.g., Prednisolone, Azathioprine) are commonly tested. Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Hepatitis (including viral autoimmune alcoholic)"][/sc]
  • Cirrhosis: Irreversible scarring of the liver, developing over months to years of chronic inflammation, leading to impaired liver function and portal hypertension.
  • Hepatocellular carcinoma (HCC): A primary liver cancer, a significant long-term risk in chronic viral hepatitis (HBV, HCV) and cirrhosis, often developing years to decades after initial infection.
  • Portal hypertension: Increased pressure in the portal venous system due to impedance of blood flow through the liver, leading to complications like varices, ascites, and splenomegaly.
  • Hepatorenal syndrome: Progressive renal failure in patients with advanced liver disease, typically cirrhosis with ascites, due to severe renal vasoconstriction.
  • Acute-on-chronic liver failure (ACLF): Acute decompensation of pre-existing chronic liver disease, often triggered by an acute insult (e.g., infection, alcoholic binge), with high short-term mortality.
