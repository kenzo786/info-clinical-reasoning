Vitamin B12 and/ or folate deficiency [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Vitamin B12 and/ or folate deficiency"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Vitamin B12 and/ or folate deficiency"][/sc]
  • Vitamin B12 (cobalamin) and folate are essential vitamins involved in DNA synthesis and red blood cell formation. Deficiency can lead to megaloblastic anaemia and neurological complications.
  • UKMLA Learning Outcome: Understand the causes, clinical features, diagnosis, and management of vitamin B12 and folate deficiencies.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Vitamin B12 and/ or folate deficiency"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Vitamin B12 and/ or folate deficiency"][/sc]
  • In the UK, vitamin B12 deficiency is estimated to affect around 1 in 10 of those aged over 75, often linked to pernicious anaemia or dietary factors.
  • Folate deficiency is less common but may affect up to 1 in 20 individuals, particularly those with poor diet, malabsorption, or increased demand such as in pregnancy.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Vitamin B12 and/ or folate deficiency"][/sc]
  • Deficiency of vitamin B12 or folate impairs DNA synthesis, leading to ineffective erythropoiesis and the production of large, immature red blood cells (megaloblasts).
  • Vitamin B12 is also crucial for myelin synthesis; its deficiency can lead to demyelination and neurological damage. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Myelin: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Myelin"][/sc]
  • The consequence is megaloblastic anaemia, which may manifest with fatigue, pallor, and glossitis, alongside potential neurological symptoms in B12 deficiency.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Vitamin B12 and/ or folate deficiency"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Subtle symptoms such as fatigue and reduced exercise tolerance may develop gradually over months to years due to slow depletion of body stores.
  • Progressive phase: As deficiency worsens, megaloblastic anaemia becomes evident, with increasing pallor, breathlessness, and potentially neurological symptoms like paraesthesia.
  • Late phase: Severe, prolonged deficiency can lead to significant neurological impairment, including subacute combined degeneration of the spinal cord, which may be irreversible.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Vitamin B12 and/ or folate deficiency"][/sc]
  • Ask about fatigue and lethargy - these are common, non-specific symptoms of anaemia.
  • Ask about breathlessness and palpitations - indicative of reduced oxygen-carrying capacity due to anaemia.
  • Ask about glossitis or a sore tongue - a classic symptom of megaloblastic anaemia.
  • Ask about paraesthesia or numbness/tingling in extremities - a key neurological symptom of vitamin B12 deficiency.
  • Ask about changes in memory or mood - cognitive and psychiatric symptoms can occur with B12 deficiency.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Vitamin B12 and/ or folate deficiency"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - tachycardia and tachypnoea may indicate severe anaemia.
  • Check for pallor - particularly in mucous membranes and conjunctiva, suggesting anaemia.
  • Check the tongue for glossitis - a smooth, red, painful tongue is characteristic.
  • Check neurological examination for signs of peripheral neuropathy or subacute combined degeneration - impaired proprioception, vibration sense, and ataxia.
  • Check for jaundice - a mild icterus may be present due to ineffective erythropoiesis.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Vitamin B12 and/ or folate deficiency"][/sc]
  • Severe neurological symptoms: Rapidly progressive paraesthesia, ataxia, or cognitive decline warrant urgent investigation to prevent irreversible damage.
  • Profound anaemia: Haemoglobin levels significantly below normal, especially with signs of cardiac compromise, require immediate assessment and management.
  • Suspicion of underlying malignancy: Unexplained weight loss, night sweats, or lymphadenopathy alongside deficiency may indicate a more serious underlying cause.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Vitamin B12 and/ or folate deficiency"][/sc]
  • Elderly population: May present predominantly with cognitive impairment or psychiatric symptoms without significant anaemia.
  • Vegetarians/Vegans: Can develop B12 deficiency due to dietary exclusion, often without overt symptoms until advanced stages.
  • Patients on certain medications: Metformin or proton pump inhibitors can mask or contribute to B12 deficiency, leading to delayed diagnosis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Vitamin B12 and/ or folate deficiency"][/sc]
  • Core: Full blood count (FBC) showing macrocytosis (MCV > 100 fL), serum vitamin B12 levels, serum folate levels, red cell folate (more reflective of tissue stores).
  • If alternative: Intrinsic factor antibodies and parietal cell antibodies for suspected pernicious anaemia. Methylmalonic acid (MMA) and homocysteine levels can differentiate B12 from folate deficiency.
  • Imaging: Not typically required for diagnosis, but MRI brain/spinal cord may be considered in severe neurological presentations to rule out other causes.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Vitamin B12 and/ or folate deficiency"][/sc]
  • A middle-aged individual presenting with progressive fatigue, breathlessness, and paraesthesia, alongside macrocytic anaemia on blood tests.
  • This represents a chronic, multi-systemic deficiency state affecting haematological and neurological systems, likely due to impaired absorption or inadequate intake.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Vitamin B12 and/ or folate deficiency"][/sc]
  • Pernicious anaemia - autoimmune destruction of parietal cells leading to B12 malabsorption.
  • Dietary deficiency - particularly in vegans or individuals with restrictive diets.
  • Malabsorption syndromes - such as Crohn's disease or coeliac disease affecting terminal ileum.
  • Gastric surgery - gastrectomy or bariatric surgery can impair intrinsic factor production or B12 absorption.
  • Drug-induced - certain medications like metformin or proton pump inhibitors.
  • Alcohol excess - can lead to folate deficiency and sometimes B12 deficiency.
  • Myelodysplastic syndromes - can cause macrocytic anaemia not responsive to vitamin supplementation.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Vitamin B12 and/ or folate deficiency"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Address severe anaemia with blood transfusion if haemodynamically unstable; initiate replacement therapy promptly.
  • Definitive management: For vitamin B12 deficiency, typically intramuscular hydroxocobalamin injections (e.g., [sc name="jitl-medication" Q="Hydroxocobalamin use in Vitamin B12 deficiency" text="hydroxocobalamin"][/sc]). For folate deficiency, oral folic acid supplementation.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Regular blood tests to monitor response to treatment (FBC, B12, folate levels) and assess for resolution of symptoms.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Advise patients on the importance of adherence to treatment and monitoring for recurrence of symptoms. Refer to haematology if diagnosis is unclear or response to treatment is inadequate.
  • Exam pharmacology reference: [sc name="jitl-medication" Q="Folic acid use in Folate deficiency" text="Folic acid"][/sc]. No doses or frequencies are cited here — always verify against your local antimicrobial/prescribing formulary before prescribing.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Vitamin B12 and/ or folate deficiency"][/sc]
  • Irreversible neurological damage: Prolonged vitamin B12 deficiency can lead to permanent neurological deficits, even after treatment.
  • Cardiac complications: Severe anaemia may exacerbate or precipitate heart failure, particularly in individuals with pre-existing cardiac conditions.
  • Infertility and adverse pregnancy outcomes: Folate deficiency is linked to neural tube defects in offspring and can contribute to infertility.
  • Increased risk of certain cancers: Chronic folate deficiency has been implicated in an increased risk of some malignancies.
