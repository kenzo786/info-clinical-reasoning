Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Primary headache disorders (including migraine, cluster headache, tension headache)"][/sc]
  • Primary headache disorders are headaches that are not caused by an underlying disease or condition, but rather by dysfunction or over-activity of pain-sensitive structures in the head. They include migraine, tension-type headache, and cluster headache.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise and differentiate between common primary headache disorders, including migraine, tension-type headache, and cluster headache, based on their characteristic features.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Primary headache disorders (including migraine, cluster headache, tension headache)"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Primary headache disorders (including migraine, cluster headache, tension headache)"][/sc]
  • Migraine affects approximately 1 in 7 adults in the UK, with a higher prevalence in women [NICE CKS Migraine].
  • Tension-type headache is the most common primary headache disorder, with a lifetime prevalence of up to 89.4% and a one-year prevalence of 86.5% in the general population [NICE CKS Tension-type Headache].
  • Cluster headache is rare, with a pooled lifetime prevalence of 124 per 100,000 and a one-year prevalence of 53 per 100,000 adult population in the UK, showing a male preponderance [NICE CKS Cluster Headache].
  • Risk factors vary by type but can include genetics, stress, sleep disturbances, and certain dietary triggers for migraine, and stress and muscle tension for tension-type headache.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Primary headache disorders (including migraine, cluster headache, tension headache)"][/sc]
  • Trigger: In migraine, various internal or external stimuli can activate the trigeminal nervous system and brainstem nuclei, leading to cortical spreading depression and activation of the trigeminal-autonomic reflex.
  • Progression: This activation results in the release of neuropeptides, such as calcitonin gene-related peptide (CGRP), causing neurogenic inflammation and vasodilation of intracranial blood vessels, contributing to the pain sensation. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Calcitonin gene-related peptide (CGRP): what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="CGRP"][/sc]
  • Consequence: The sustained activation and sensitisation of central pain pathways lead to the characteristic throbbing pain, often accompanied by associated symptoms like photophobia, phonophobia, and nausea.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Primary headache disorders (including migraine, cluster headache, tension headache)"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Migraine often begins with a prodrome hours or even days before the headache, involving subtle changes like mood shifts, fatigue, or neck stiffness. Tension-type headaches can be episodic, lasting from 30 minutes to several days. Cluster headaches typically have an abrupt onset, with attacks lasting 15 to 180 minutes.
  • Progressive phase: For migraine, this involves the aura (if present) followed by the headache phase, which can last 4 to 72 hours. Frequent episodic tension-type headaches occur on fewer than 15 days per month. Cluster headaches occur in bouts (cluster periods) lasting weeks to months, followed by remission.
  • Late phase: The postdrome phase of migraine can last for a day or two after the headache subsides, characterised by fatigue or difficulty concentrating. Chronic tension-type headache evolves from frequent episodic forms, occurring on 15 or more days per month. Chronic cluster headache involves cluster periods lasting for more than a year without remission, or with remissions lasting less than one month.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Primary headache disorders (including migraine, cluster headache, tension headache)"][/sc]
  • Ask about headache quality and location - a throbbing, unilateral pain suggests migraine, whereas a bilateral, pressing or tightening sensation is more typical of tension-type headache.
  • Ask about associated symptoms - photophobia, phonophobia, nausea, or vomiting are common in migraine and indicate central sensitisation.
  • Ask about visual or sensory disturbances - transient visual (e.g., zig-zag lines, flashing lights) or sensory (e.g., numbness, tingling) aura can precede migraine headaches.
  • Ask about autonomic features - unilateral lacrimation, conjunctival injection, ptosis, or rhinorrhoea ipsilateral to the headache are characteristic of cluster headache.
  • Ask about exacerbating and relieving factors - physical activity often worsens migraine, while rest may alleviate it.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Primary headache disorders (including migraine, cluster headache, tension headache)"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - typically normal in primary headache disorders, but essential to assess for signs of systemic illness or hypertension that may suggest a secondary cause.
  • Check general neurological examination - usually normal in primary headaches; however, focal neurological deficits would raise suspicion for a secondary cause.
  • Check for cranial nerve abnormalities - specifically, for cluster headache, look for ipsilateral ptosis, miosis, lacrimation, conjunctival injection, or rhinorrhoea, indicating trigeminal autonomic activation.
  • Check for pericranial tenderness - palpation of the head, neck, and shoulder muscles may reveal tenderness, particularly in tension-type headache.
  • Check for photophobia and phonophobia - observe for aversion to light or sound, which are common non-specific signs but highly characteristic of migraine attacks.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Primary headache disorders (including migraine, cluster headache, tension headache)"][/sc]
  • Sudden onset, thunderclap headache: Reaching maximum intensity within one minute, this may indicate subarachnoid haemorrhage.
  • New onset headache in patient over 50 years: Raises suspicion for giant cell arteritis or other serious underlying pathology.
  • Focal neurological deficit: Suggests structural brain pathology such as a tumour, stroke, or other neurological lesion.
  • Headache with fever, neck stiffness, or rash: May indicate meningitis or encephalitis.
  • Headache associated with trauma: Could signify intracranial haemorrhage or other post-traumatic complications.
  • Change in established headache pattern: A significant alteration in character, frequency, or severity of a chronic headache may signal a new underlying cause.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Primary headache disorders (including migraine, cluster headache, tension headache)"][/sc]
  • Migraine aura without headache: Patients experience typical migraine aura symptoms (e.g., visual disturbances, sensory changes) but without the subsequent headache phase, which can be confusing for diagnosis.
  • Chronic daily headache: Any primary headache type can transform from an episodic to a chronic form, where headaches occur on 15 or more days per month, often leading to medication overuse headache.
  • Older adults: New onset headache in older adults is more likely to be secondary (e.g., giant cell arteritis, stroke, tumour) even if it resembles a primary headache type. Primary headaches may also present with less typical features or be less severe.
  • Pregnancy: Headaches during pregnancy require careful evaluation to rule out secondary causes such as pre-eclampsia, cerebral venous thrombosis, or pituitary apoplexy, even if they appear to be primary headaches.
  • Immunosuppressed patients: Headaches in this population warrant thorough investigation for opportunistic infections (e.g., toxoplasmosis, cryptococcosis), CNS lymphoma, or other immunocompromise-related pathologies, as primary headaches are less likely to be the sole diagnosis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Primary headache disorders (including migraine, cluster headache, tension headache)"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected primary headache disorders: Diagnosis is primarily clinical, based on a detailed history and neurological examination. No specific laboratory tests or imaging are routinely required for uncomplicated primary headaches.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible (presence of red flags): Consider blood tests such as ESR/CRP (for suspected giant cell arteritis), full blood count, and biochemistry if systemic illness is suspected.
  • Imaging - CT or MRI brain: Not indicated for typical primary headache presentations. Neuroimaging is reserved for cases with red flag features (e.g., sudden onset, focal neurological deficits, new onset in older age) to exclude secondary causes such as intracranial haemorrhage, tumour, or structural abnormalities.
  • Serial reassessment: Regular follow-up is important to monitor headache frequency, severity, response to treatment, and to identify any evolving red flag features or changes in headache pattern.

  • A 35-year-old female presents with a 24-hour history of severe, throbbing, unilateral headache, associated with nausea, photophobia, and phonophobia, which is worsened by movement. She reports similar episodes occurring approximately twice a month for the past five years.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Primary headache disorders (including migraine, cluster headache, tension headache)"][/sc]
  • Subarachnoid Haemorrhage - Characterised by a sudden onset, severe "thunderclap" headache, often described as the worst headache of one's life, typically reaching peak intensity within one minute.
  • Meningitis/Encephalitis - Headache accompanied by fever, neck stiffness (meningism), photophobia, and altered mental status.
  • Giant Cell Arteritis - New onset headache in patients over 50 years, often associated with temporal tenderness, jaw claudication, and visual disturbances; requires urgent assessment.
  • Brain Tumour - Typically presents with a progressive headache, often worse in the morning, and may be associated with focal neurological deficits, seizures, or papilloedema.
  • Medication Overuse Headache - A chronic daily or near-daily headache that develops or worsens with regular overuse of acute headache medications.
  • Cervicogenic Headache - Headache originating from the cervical spine, often triggered or exacerbated by neck movements or sustained postures, and associated with neck pain and restricted range of motion.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Primary headache disorders (including migraine, cluster headache, tension headache)"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities:
    • For acute migraine: Offer a triptan (e.g., [sc name="jitl-medication" Q="Sumatriptan use in migraine" text="sumatriptan"]) together with an NSAID (e.g., [sc name="jitl-medication" Q="Naproxen use in migraine" text="naproxen"]) or paracetamol. Consider antiemetics if nausea/vomiting is prominent.
    • For acute tension-type headache: Simple analgesics such as paracetamol, aspirin, or NSAIDs (e.g., [sc name="jitl-medication" Q="Ibuprofen use in tension headache" text="ibuprofen"]).
    • For acute cluster headache: Offer high-flow oxygen via a non-rebreather mask and/or subcutaneous triptans (e.g., [sc name="jitl-medication" Q="Sumatriptan use in cluster headache" text="sumatriptan"]).
  • Definitive management:
    • For migraine prophylaxis: Consider beta-blockers (e.g., [sc name="jitl-medication" Q="Propranolol use in migraine prophylaxis" text="propranolol"]), topiramate, or amitriptyline. CGRP monoclonal antibodies may be considered for chronic migraine.
    • For chronic tension-type headache prophylaxis: Consider a course of acupuncture or a tricyclic antidepressant (e.g., [sc name="jitl-medication" Q="Amitriptyline use in chronic tension headache" text="amitriptyline"]).
    • For cluster headache prophylaxis: Verapamil is often used as a first-line preventive treatment.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Monitor headache frequency, severity, and impact on daily life using headache diaries. Review treatment effectiveness and side effects regularly.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Advise patients to seek urgent medical attention for any red flag features. Refer to a headache specialist or neurologist if primary headache diagnosis is uncertain, treatments are ineffective, or for complex cases.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Primary headache disorders (including migraine, cluster headache, tension headache)"][/sc]
  • Chronic Migraine: Frequent episodic migraine can transform into chronic migraine (≥15 headache days/month), often due to central sensitisation and medication overuse, significantly impacting quality of life and requiring different management strategies.
  • Medication Overuse Headache (MOH): Regular overuse of acute headache medications (e.g., triptans, opioids, simple analgesics) can paradoxically lead to an increase in headache frequency and severity, making diagnosis and treatment challenging.
  • Migrainous Infarction: A rare complication where migraine with aura is associated with an ischaemic lesion in the brain, typically in the posterior circulation, highlighting the vascular component of migraine pathophysiology.
  • Status Migrainosus: A debilitating migraine attack lasting for more than 72 hours, often requiring hospitalisation for aggressive treatment to break the cycle of pain.
  • Chronic Cluster Headache: Episodic cluster headache can evolve into a chronic form, where patients experience cluster periods lasting for more than a year without remission, or with remissions lasting less than one month, leading to persistent severe pain and disability.
  • Psychological Comorbidities: Chronic headache disorders are frequently associated with depression, anxiety, and sleep disturbances, which can exacerbate headache symptoms and complicate management, underscoring the need for a holistic approach.
