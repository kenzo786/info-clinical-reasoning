Acute cholangitis [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Acute cholangitis"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Acute cholangitis"][/sc]
  • Acute cholangitis is an infection of the biliary tree, typically caused by bacterial ascent from the duodenum secondary to biliary outflow obstruction. It is a life-threatening condition.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical presentation, key diagnostic features, and principles of initial management for acute cholangitis.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Acute cholangitis"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Acute cholangitis"][/sc]
  • Acute cholangitis is an uncommon condition, affecting approximately 1% of patients with gallstone disease [1].
  • Up to 9% of patients admitted to hospital with gallstone disease may develop acute cholangitis [2].
  • The median age of presentation is typically 50-60 years, with an equal sex distribution [1]. Risk factors include gallstone disease (e.g., choledocholithiasis, Mirizzi syndrome), iatrogenic biliary injury (e.g., post-ERCP), tumours (pancreatic, cholangiocarcinoma, hepatic), sclerosing cholangitis, biliary strictures, and parasitic infections [1].
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Acute cholangitis"][/sc]
  • Trigger: Biliary outflow obstruction (e.g., gallstones, strictures, tumours) leads to stasis of bile within the biliary tree.
  • Progression: Stasis promotes bacterial overgrowth (commonly E. coli, Klebsiella, Enterococcus) and increased intraductal pressure, facilitating bacterial translocation into the bloodstream. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Biliary stasis: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Biliary stasis"][/sc]
  • Consequence: Ascending bacterial infection of the biliary tree results in inflammation and systemic sepsis if untreated. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Bacterial translocation: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Bacterial translocation"][/sc]
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Acute cholangitis"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Symptoms such as fever, right upper quadrant pain, and jaundice (Charcot\'s triad) typically develop acutely over hours to a few days following biliary obstruction and infection.
  • Progressive phase: Without prompt intervention, systemic signs of infection, such as hypotension and confusion (Reynold\'s pentad), may develop, indicating severe sepsis, particularly beyond 24-48 hours.
  • Late phase: If untreated, complications such as hepatic abscess, acute kidney injury, and multi-organ failure can manifest, particularly beyond several days to a week.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Acute cholangitis"][/sc]
  • Ask about fever - often high-grade and associated with rigors, indicating systemic inflammation and bacteraemia.
  • Ask about right upper quadrant (RUQ) pain - typically constant and severe, localising to the inflamed biliary tree.
  • Ask about jaundice - yellowing of the skin and sclera, signifying biliary obstruction and impaired bilirubin excretion.
  • Ask about pruritus - generalised itching due to accumulation of bile salts, often accompanying jaundice.
  • Ask about pale stools and dark urine - indicative of complete or significant biliary obstruction.
  • Ask about nausea and vomiting - common non-specific symptoms associated with acute abdominal pathology and systemic illness.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Acute cholangitis"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - fever, tachycardia, and hypotension are common, indicating systemic inflammatory response syndrome (SIRS) or sepsis.
  • Check for jaundice - visible yellow discolouration of the sclera and skin, confirming biliary obstruction.
  • Check right upper quadrant tenderness - elicited on palpation, often with guarding, localising the inflammation.
  • Check mental status - confusion or altered consciousness may indicate severe sepsis or hepatic encephalopathy. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Hepatic encephalopathy: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Hepatic encephalopathy"][/sc]
  • Check for signs of peritonism - typically absent; if present, consider alternative or co-existing diagnoses.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Acute cholangitis"][/sc]
  • Reynold\'s Pentad: Charcot\'s triad (fever, RUQ pain, jaundice) plus hypotension and altered mental status, indicating severe cholangitis with impending septic shock.
  • Rapidly worsening jaundice: Suggests severe or complete biliary obstruction requiring urgent decompression.
  • Signs of systemic inflammatory response syndrome (SIRS): Two or more of fever/hypothermia, tachycardia, tachypnoea, or abnormal white cell count, indicating a significant inflammatory response.
  • Evidence of organ dysfunction: Acute kidney injury, coagulopathy, or respiratory compromise, signifying progression to severe sepsis or septic shock.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Acute cholangitis"][/sc]
  • Older adults: May present with vague symptoms, less pronounced fever, or altered mental status without classic Charcot’s triad, leading to delayed diagnosis.
  • Immunosuppressed patients: (e.g., those with HIV, on chemotherapy, or post-transplant) may have attenuated inflammatory responses, masking typical signs and symptoms.
  • Post-ERCP cholangitis: Can present within hours to days of the procedure, sometimes with pancreatitis-like pain, and requires a high index of suspicion.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Acute cholangitis"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected acute cholangitis:
    • Full blood count (FBC): typically shows leucocytosis with neutrophilia.
    • Urea and electrolytes (U&Es): may show acute kidney injury in severe cases.
    • Liver function tests (LFTs): elevated bilirubin (conjugated), alkaline phosphatase (ALP), and gamma-glutamyl transferase (GGT) indicate cholestasis.
    • C-reactive protein (CRP): significantly elevated, reflecting systemic inflammation.
    • Blood cultures: essential to identify causative organisms and guide antibiotic therapy.
    • Amylase/Lipase: may be elevated if there is concomitant pancreatitis.
  • Imaging - Transabdominal ultrasound: Often the initial imaging modality, may reveal gallstones, common bile duct (CBD) dilatation, or cholecystitis.
  • Imaging - CT abdomen: Useful if ultrasound is inconclusive, to identify the cause of obstruction (e.g., tumour, stricture) and assess for complications like hepatic abscess.
  • Imaging - Magnetic Resonance Cholangiopancreatography (MRCP): Non-invasive, highly sensitive for visualising the biliary tree and identifying stones or strictures, often performed before ERCP.
  • Imaging - Endoscopic Retrograde Cholangiopancreatography (ERCP): Gold standard for diagnosis and therapeutic intervention (e.g., stone extraction, stent placement) in acute cholangitis.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Consider further imaging or investigations based on the differential diagnosis.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Acute cholangitis"][/sc]
  • A 65-year-old female presents to the emergency department with a 2-day history of high-grade fever, severe right upper quadrant abdominal pain radiating to her back, and yellowing of her eyes and skin. She has a known history of gallstones.
  • An acutely unwell, febrile, jaundiced, elderly female with right upper quadrant pain and a history of cholelithiasis, concerning for ascending cholangitis.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Acute cholangitis"][/sc]
  • Acute cholecystitis - typically presents with RUQ pain and fever but usually without jaundice unless Mirizzi syndrome is present.
  • Acute pancreatitis - severe epigastric pain radiating to the back, often with nausea/vomiting; amylase/lipase elevation is key.
  • Hepatitis (viral/alcoholic/drug-induced) - may cause jaundice and RUQ pain, but typically without the acute, severe systemic illness of cholangitis.
  • Hepatic abscess - fever and RUQ pain, but jaundice is less common and imaging will show a focal liver lesion.
  • Biliary colic - episodic RUQ pain, often post-prandial, without fever or signs of infection.
  • Peptic ulcer disease - epigastric pain, often relieved by antacids, without jaundice or fever.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Acute cholangitis"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities:
    • Resuscitation following an ABCDE approach.
    • Administer broad-spectrum intravenous antibiotics promptly (e.g., piperacillin/tazobactam or a carbapenem), guided by local antimicrobial guidelines.
    • Provide adequate fluid resuscitation and oxygen therapy as needed.
    • Correct any coagulopathy if present.
  • Definitive management:
    • Biliary decompression is crucial and typically achieved via ERCP with sphincterotomy and stone extraction or stent placement.
    • Percutaneous transhepatic cholangiography (PTC) with biliary drainage may be considered if ERCP is unsuccessful or contraindicated.
    • Surgical decompression is a last resort if endoscopic or percutaneous methods fail.
  • Monitoring and follow-up:
    • Close monitoring of vital signs, inflammatory markers, and liver function tests.
    • Repeat imaging as necessary to assess the effectiveness of decompression.
  • Escalation and safety-netting:
    • Escalate care to a higher level (e.g., intensive care) if there are signs of septic shock or multi-organ failure.
    • Advise patients on symptoms of worsening infection or complications and when to seek urgent medical attention.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Acute cholangitis"][/sc]
  • Sepsis and septic shock: Systemic inflammatory response to infection, leading to organ dysfunction and potentially life-threatening circulatory collapse.
  • Hepatic abscess: Localised collection of pus within the liver, often a consequence of untreated or inadequately treated cholangitis.
  • Acute kidney injury: Can result from severe sepsis and hypoperfusion, requiring careful fluid management and monitoring.
  • Acute pancreatitis: May occur if the biliary obstruction also affects the pancreatic duct, leading to pancreatic inflammation.
  • Recurrent cholangitis: If the underlying cause of biliary obstruction is not definitively addressed, leading to repeated episodes of infection.
  • Multi-organ failure: The most severe complication of uncontrolled sepsis, affecting multiple organ systems and carrying a high mortality.
