Liver Failure [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Liver Failure"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Liver Failure"][/sc]
  • Liver failure occurs when extensive damage to liver cells impairs the liver's ability to perform its essential functions, leading to systemic dysfunction.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical features of acute and chronic liver failure, and outline the initial diagnostic and management steps.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Liver Failure"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Liver Failure"][/sc]
  • In the UK, over 12,000 deaths annually are attributed to liver disease, with a four-fold increase in death rates over the last 50 years [1].
  • Liver disease deaths are nearly twice as high among men compared to women, and half of all deaths occur in mid-life (age 45 to 64) [1].
  • Key risk factors include alcohol misuse, obesity (leading to non-alcoholic fatty liver disease), and undiagnosed viral hepatitis infections [1].
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Liver Failure"][/sc]
  • Acute liver injury, often from paracetamol overdose or viral hepatitis, leads to massive hepatocyte necrosis, triggering a systemic inflammatory response.
  • Progressive loss of functional liver mass results in impaired detoxification, reduced synthetic capacity (e.g., clotting factors, albumin), and altered metabolism.
  • Consequences include coagulopathy, jaundice, hepatic encephalopathy due to accumulation of toxins like ammonia, and multi-organ dysfunction, particularly renal failure. Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Liver Failure"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Often asymptomatic or vague symptoms such as fatigue, malaise, and nausea, making early diagnosis challenging.
  • Progressive phase: Rapid onset of jaundice, coagulopathy, and hepatic encephalopathy, typically within days to weeks in acute liver failure, or gradual decompensation over months to years in chronic liver disease.
  • Late phase: Development of severe complications such as cerebral oedema, renal failure, sepsis, and gastrointestinal bleeding, particularly beyond the initial weeks of acute presentation or with advanced chronic disease.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Liver Failure"][/sc]
  • Ask about fatigue and malaise - these are common early, non-specific symptoms that can indicate underlying liver dysfunction.
  • Ask about nausea and vomiting - often present in acute liver injury and can contribute to dehydration and electrolyte imbalances.
  • Ask about jaundice (yellowing of skin/eyes) - a key indicator of impaired bilirubin metabolism, suggesting significant liver dysfunction.
  • Ask about abdominal pain or discomfort, particularly in the right upper quadrant - may indicate liver inflammation or distension.
  • Ask about changes in mental status (confusion, disorientation, somnolence) - highly suggestive of hepatic encephalopathy, a critical complication.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Liver Failure"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - hypotension, tachycardia, and fever may indicate sepsis or circulatory dysfunction, common in liver failure.
  • Check for jaundice - visible yellow discolouration of the sclera and skin, indicating hyperbilirubinaemia.
  • Check for asterixis (flapping tremor) - a classic sign of hepatic encephalopathy, reflecting impaired neurological function. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Asterixis: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Asterixis"][/sc]
  • Check for ascites and peripheral oedema - signs of fluid retention due to portal hypertension and hypoalbuminaemia.
  • Check for spider naevi and palmar erythema - cutaneous manifestations of chronic liver disease, indicating altered hormone metabolism.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Liver Failure"][/sc]
  • Acute encephalopathy: Rapidly worsening confusion, disorientation, or reduced consciousness in a patient with acute liver dysfunction, indicating severe hepatic impairment and risk of cerebral oedema.
  • Significant coagulopathy: Prolonged prothrombin time (INR >1.5) or active bleeding, reflecting severely impaired hepatic synthesis of clotting factors and increased risk of haemorrhage.
  • Hypoglycaemia: Impaired hepatic gluconeogenesis can lead to dangerously low blood glucose levels, requiring urgent correction.
  • Renal dysfunction: Rising creatinine or oliguria, suggesting hepatorenal syndrome or acute kidney injury, a common and serious complication.
  • Persistent acidosis: Arterial pH <7.30 despite fluid resuscitation, indicating severe metabolic derangement and poor prognosis, particularly in paracetamol-induced liver failure.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Liver Failure"][/sc]
  • Older adults: May present with more subtle or non-specific symptoms, such as increased fatigue or mild confusion, making diagnosis challenging and potentially delaying intervention.
  • Immunosuppressed patients: Aetiology of liver failure may be atypical (e.g., opportunistic infections), and symptoms of liver dysfunction may be masked or attributed to other conditions.
  • Acute-on-chronic liver failure (ACLF): Patients with pre-existing chronic liver disease may experience an acute decompensation triggered by an event (e.g., infection, alcohol binge), presenting with rapid worsening of jaundice, ascites, or encephalopathy. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Acute-on-chronic liver failure: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="ACLF"][/sc]
  • Early stage chronic liver disease: Often asymptomatic, with liver dysfunction only detected incidentally on routine blood tests, highlighting the "silent" nature of progression.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Liver Failure"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected liver failure: Full blood count, liver function tests (bilirubin, ALT, AST, ALP, albumin), coagulation screen (INR, prothrombin time), urea and electrolytes, glucose, arterial blood gas.
  • Aetiological investigations: Paracetamol levels, viral hepatitis serology (A, B, C, E), autoimmune screen (ANA, SMA, LKM1), ceruloplasmin (for Wilson's disease), alpha-1 antitrypsin levels.
  • Severity/risk tools: King's College Criteria for liver transplant eligibility in acute liver failure. King's College Criteria for Acetaminophen Toxicity on MDCalc; King's College Criteria for Non-Acetaminophen Toxicity on MDCalc. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="King-College Criteria: what are they and why do they matter clinically?" text="King's College Criteria"][/sc]
  • Imaging - Ultrasound abdomen with Doppler: To assess liver size, morphology, presence of ascites, and patency of hepatic vasculature (e.g., portal vein thrombosis, Budd-Chiari syndrome).
  • Serial reassessment: Regular monitoring of liver function tests, coagulation profile, renal function, and neurological status is crucial to track disease progression and response to treatment.

  • An acutely unwell patient with jaundice, coagulopathy, and encephalopathy, concerning for acute liver failure.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Liver Failure"][/sc]
  • Severe Hepatitis (Viral/Autoimmune/Drug-induced): Can cause significant liver inflammation and dysfunction, mimicking liver failure, but may not progress to full failure or have different management.
  • Biliary Obstruction: Presents with jaundice and elevated liver enzymes, but typically involves cholestatic pattern and absence of severe coagulopathy or encephalopathy unless prolonged.
  • Sepsis with Liver Dysfunction: Systemic infection can cause secondary liver injury, but primary focus is on identifying and treating the underlying sepsis.
  • Budd-Chiari Syndrome: Hepatic venous outflow obstruction leading to liver congestion and dysfunction, often presenting with abdominal pain, ascites, and hepatomegaly.
  • Wilson's Disease: A genetic disorder of copper metabolism, can present with acute liver failure, particularly in younger patients, and requires specific chelation therapy. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Wilson's Disease: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Wilson's Disease"][/sc]
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Liver Failure"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Recognition and urgent referral to a specialist liver unit, aggressive supportive care including fluid management, correction of hypoglycaemia, and management of coagulopathy.
  • Definitive management: May involve specific antidotes (e.g., N-acetylcysteine for paracetamol overdose), treatment of underlying cause (e.g., antiviral therapy for viral hepatitis), or liver transplantation in selected cases.
  • Adjunctive management: Management of hepatic encephalopathy (e.g., lactulose, rifaximin), prevention and treatment of infections, renal replacement therapy for acute kidney injury.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Close monitoring in an intensive care setting, with serial assessment of liver function, coagulation, renal function, and neurological status.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Early discussion with a liver transplant centre for patients meeting King's College Criteria or other poor prognostic indicators.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Liver Failure"][/sc]
  • Cerebral Oedema: A critical complication of acute liver failure, particularly in the progressive phase, leading to intracranial hypertension and brain herniation.
  • Renal Failure (Hepatorenal Syndrome): Development of acute kidney injury in the setting of advanced liver disease, often in the late phase, due to splanchnic vasodilation and renal vasoconstriction.
  • Sepsis and Infections: Patients with liver failure are highly susceptible to bacterial and fungal infections, which can precipitate or worsen decompensation, especially in the progressive and late phases.
  • Gastrointestinal Haemorrhage: Due to portal hypertension (variceal bleeding) or coagulopathy (gastric erosions), a significant cause of morbidity and mortality, particularly in the late phase.
  • Multi-organ Failure: Progressive dysfunction of other organ systems (e.g., respiratory, cardiovascular) due to systemic inflammation and metabolic derangements, common in the late phase.
