Acute coronary syndromes [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Acute coronary syndromes"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Acute coronary syndromes"][/sc]
  • Acute coronary syndromes (ACS) represent a spectrum of conditions resulting from acute myocardial ischaemia, encompassing unstable angina, non-ST-elevation myocardial infarction (NSTEMI), and ST-elevation myocardial infarction (STEMI).
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical features of acute coronary syndromes, interpret relevant investigations including ECG and cardiac biomarkers, and initiate immediate management to minimise myocardial damage and improve patient outcomes.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Acute coronary syndromes"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Acute Coronary Syndromes"][/sc]
  • Coronary heart disease is a leading cause of death in the UK, with over 80,000 hospitalisations annually for acute myocardial infarction [1].
  • Key risk factors include smoking, hypertension, dyslipidaemia, diabetes mellitus, obesity, physical inactivity, and a family history of premature coronary artery disease [2].
  • Incidence of acute coronary syndromes has shown a decline in recent years, though it remains a significant public health burden [3].
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Acute coronary syndromes"][/sc]
  • The primary trigger is typically the rupture or erosion of an atherosclerotic plaque in a coronary artery, exposing thrombogenic material to the bloodstream.
  • This leads to platelet aggregation and activation of the coagulation cascade, forming a thrombus that partially or completely occludes the coronary artery, resulting in myocardial ischaemia or infarction. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Coronary artery thrombus: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Coronary artery thrombus"][/sc]
  • The consequence is an imbalance between myocardial oxygen supply and demand, leading to cardiomyocyte injury and necrosis, manifesting as chest pain and other symptoms.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Acute coronary syndromes"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Symptoms typically develop acutely, often over minutes to hours, with rapid progression of myocardial ischaemia if reperfusion is not established.
  • Progressive phase: Without intervention, myocardial damage can extend over several hours, particularly beyond the first 6-12 hours, leading to larger infarct size and increased risk of complications.
  • Late phase: Recovery and remodelling occur over weeks to months, but ongoing risk of recurrent events and heart failure persists, necessitating long-term management.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Acute coronary syndromes"][/sc]
  • Ask about chest pain - typically retrosternal, constricting, heavy, or crushing, often radiating to the left arm, neck, jaw, or epigastrium, indicating myocardial ischaemia.
  • Ask about shortness of breath - may occur due to impaired left ventricular function or pulmonary oedema secondary to myocardial damage.
  • Ask about nausea and vomiting - common accompanying symptoms, possibly due to vagal stimulation or diaphragmatic irritation from inferior wall ischaemia.
  • Ask about sweating and pallor - signs of sympathetic activation in response to pain and physiological stress.
  • Ask about pre-syncope or syncope - suggests significant haemodynamic compromise or arrhythmia.
  • Ask about fatigue - a non-specific but common symptom, particularly in women and older adults, which can be a sole presenting feature.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Acute coronary syndromes"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - tachycardia, tachypnoea, hypotension or hypertension may indicate the severity of myocardial dysfunction or compensatory mechanisms.
  • Check for pallor and diaphoresis - signs of sympathetic activation due to pain and physiological stress.
  • Check for evidence of impaired myocardial function - raised jugular venous pressure (JVP), peripheral oedema, or a third heart sound (S3) may suggest heart failure.
  • Check for new murmurs - a new pan-systolic murmur may indicate mitral regurgitation due to papillary muscle dysfunction or rupture. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="New cardiac murmur in ACS: what does it imply?" text="New cardiac murmur"][/sc]
  • Check lung fields - crackles on auscultation may indicate pulmonary oedema.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Acute coronary syndromes"][/sc]
  • Persistent chest pain: Pain unresponsive to nitrates or lasting longer than 20 minutes, suggesting ongoing myocardial ischaemia and potential infarction.
  • Haemodynamic instability: Hypotension, syncope, or signs of shock (e.g., cool peripheries, altered mental status) indicating severe cardiac compromise.
  • New or worsening heart failure: Development of dyspnoea, orthopnoea, or pulmonary oedema, reflecting significant myocardial damage.
  • Recurrent ischaemia post-treatment: Re-emergence of symptoms after initial stabilisation, indicating ongoing risk and need for further intervention.
  • High-risk ECG changes: New ST-segment elevation or depression, T-wave inversion, or arrhythmias, particularly if dynamic.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Acute coronary syndromes"][/sc]
  • Women: Often present with non-chest pain symptoms such as fatigue, dyspnoea, nausea, or epigastric pain, leading to delayed diagnosis. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Atypical ACS presentation in women: why does it matter?" text="Atypical ACS in women"][/sc]
  • Older adults: May present with confusion, syncope, stroke, or unexplained breathlessness without typical chest pain, increasing diagnostic challenge.
  • Diabetic patients: Due to autonomic neuropathy, may experience \'silent\' ischaemia or present with only dyspnoea or fatigue, masking classic symptoms.
  • Immunosuppressed patients: Can have blunted inflammatory responses, leading to less severe pain or non-specific symptoms, complicating early recognition.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Acute coronary syndromes"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected ACS: 12-lead electrocardiogram (ECG) for ST-segment changes, T-wave inversion, or new left bundle branch block; serial high-sensitivity troponin measurements to detect myocardial injury.
  • Severity/risk tools: GRACE 2.0 score on MDCalc GRACE 2.0 Score on MDCalc for risk stratification in NSTEMI and unstable angina. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="GRACE score: what is it and how is it used in ACS?" text="GRACE score"][/sc]
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Chest X-ray for pulmonary oedema or other lung pathology; D-dimer for pulmonary embolism; echocardiography for valvular disease or pericardial effusion.
  • Serial reassessment: Repeat ECGs and troponin levels are crucial to monitor for evolving ischaemia or infarction, particularly in patients with initial non-diagnostic findings.

  • A 65-year-old male with a history of hypertension and smoking presents to the emergency department with 3 hours of crushing retrosternal chest pain radiating to his left arm, associated with nausea and diaphoresis.
  • An elderly male with cardiovascular risk factors presenting with acute, severe, radiating chest pain and autonomic symptoms, concerning for acute coronary syndrome.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Acute coronary syndromes"][/sc]
  • Aortic dissection - sudden onset, tearing chest pain radiating to the back, often associated with pulse deficit or new murmur.
  • Pulmonary embolism - acute onset dyspnoea, pleuritic chest pain, and risk factors for venous thromboembolism.
  • Pericarditis - sharp, pleuritic chest pain, often relieved by leaning forward, with widespread ST elevation on ECG.
  • Oesophageal spasm/reflux - burning retrosternal pain, often relieved by antacids, may mimic cardiac pain.
  • Musculoskeletal chest pain - localised, reproducible pain with palpation, often exacerbated by movement.
  • Pneumothorax - sudden onset pleuritic chest pain and dyspnoea, with reduced breath sounds on affected side. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Pneumothorax: how to differentiate from ACS?" text="Pneumothorax"][/sc]
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Acute coronary syndromes"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Recognition and prompt escalation to cardiology, pain relief, antiplatelet therapy, and assessment for reperfusion strategy (e.g., primary percutaneous coronary intervention for STEMI).
  • Definitive management: Often includes revascularisation (PCI or CABG) for significant coronary artery disease, alongside optimal medical therapy.
  • Adjunctive management: May support with oxygen if hypoxic, nitrates for ongoing ischaemic pain, and beta-blockers to reduce myocardial oxygen demand.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Continuous cardiac monitoring, serial ECGs and troponins, and comprehensive secondary prevention strategies including lifestyle modification and pharmacotherapy.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Patients with suspected ACS often warrant admission for observation and further investigation; clear safety-netting advice should be provided on discharge.
  • Exam pharmacology reference (UKMLA/MRCGP): Antiplatelet agents such as [sc name="jitl-medication" Q="Aspirin use in Acute Coronary Syndromes" text="Aspirin"][/sc] and P2Y12 inhibitors (e.g., clopidogrel, ticagrelor), anticoagulants (e.g., fondaparinux, enoxaparin), beta-blockers (e.g., bisoprolol), ACE inhibitors (e.g., ramipril), and statins (e.g., atorvastatin) are typically used. Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Acute coronary syndromes"][/sc]
  • Arrhythmias: Common in the acute phase, particularly ventricular arrhythmias, due to myocardial ischaemia and irritability, which can lead to sudden cardiac death.
  • Heart failure: May develop acutely due to extensive myocardial damage or chronically due to ventricular remodelling, leading to reduced cardiac output and pulmonary congestion.
  • Cardiogenic shock: A severe form of heart failure where the heart cannot pump enough blood to meet the body\'s needs, often occurring with large anterior MIs, leading to multi-organ dysfunction.
  • Mechanical complications: Rare but life-threatening, including ventricular septal rupture, papillary muscle rupture (leading to severe mitral regurgitation), or free wall rupture, typically occurring within days of infarction.
  • Recurrent ischaemia/reinfarction: Ongoing or new episodes of myocardial ischaemia, particularly beyond the initial presentation, indicating unstable coronary disease or incomplete revascularisation.
  • Pericarditis: Inflammation of the pericardium, either early (peri-infarction pericarditis) or late (Dressler\'s syndrome), causing chest pain and sometimes pericardial effusion.
