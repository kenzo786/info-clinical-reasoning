Perforated eardrum [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Perforated eardrum"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Perforated eardrum"][/sc]
  • A perforated eardrum, or tympanic membrane perforation, is a hole or tear in the thin tissue separating the ear canal from the middle ear.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: recognise the causes, clinical features, and initial management of a perforated eardrum.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Perforated eardrum"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Perforated eardrum"][/sc]
  • Acute otitis media is a common cause, affecting approximately 80% of children by age three in the UK, with perforation occurring in a subset of these cases.
  • Traumatic perforations, such as from cotton buds or barotrauma, are also prevalent, particularly in younger adults and those engaging in diving or flying.
  • Risk factors include recurrent ear infections, previous ear surgery, and activities involving rapid pressure changes.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Perforated eardrum"][/sc]
  • Trigger: Increased pressure in the middle ear due to infection (e.g., acute otitis media) or direct trauma (e.g., foreign body, barotrauma) [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Acute otitis media: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="acute otitis media"][/sc].
  • Progression: The tympanic membrane, a thin and delicate structure, is unable to withstand the excessive pressure or direct insult, leading to a tear or hole.
  • Consequence: Disruption of sound transmission to the ossicles, potential entry of pathogens into the middle ear, and subsequent conductive hearing loss and increased risk of infection.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Perforated eardrum"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Acute pain and sudden hearing loss, often accompanied by discharge, typically resolving within hours to a few days.
  • Progressive phase: Spontaneous healing of the perforation often occurs within a few weeks, particularly beyond 2-4 weeks, with resolution of symptoms.
  • Late phase: Persistent perforation beyond 6-8 weeks may indicate a chronic issue, potentially leading to ongoing hearing loss or recurrent infections.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Perforated eardrum"][/sc]
  • Ask about sudden ear pain - indicates the moment of perforation, often followed by relief if due to pressure build-up.
  • Ask about hearing loss - suggests disruption of sound conduction, varying in severity.
  • Ask about ear discharge (otorrhoea) - may indicate middle ear infection or fluid draining through the perforation.
  • Ask about tinnitus - a ringing or buzzing sound, which can accompany hearing changes.
  • Ask about vertigo or dizziness - suggests potential involvement of the inner ear, a more serious complication.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Perforated eardrum"][/sc]
  • Check otoscopy findings - direct visualisation of a hole or tear in the tympanic membrane is diagnostic [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Otoscopy: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="otoscopy" ][/sc].
  • Check ear discharge - purulent or serosanguinous discharge may be visible in the external auditory canal.
  • Check hearing assessment (e.g., Weber and Rinne tests) - typically reveals conductive hearing loss on the affected side.
  • Check vital signs - usually normal unless there is a systemic infection or significant pain.
  • Check for signs of facial nerve palsy - suggests potential involvement of the facial nerve, a rare but serious complication.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Perforated eardrum"][/sc]
  • Persistent vertigo: Suggests inner ear involvement or labyrinthine fistula, requiring urgent ENT assessment.
  • Facial nerve weakness or paralysis: Indicates potential damage to the facial nerve, necessitating immediate specialist review.
  • Signs of mastoiditis (e.g., post-auricular swelling, erythema, tenderness): Implies spread of infection beyond the middle ear, requiring urgent medical attention.
  • Severe, unremitting pain despite analgesia: May indicate developing complications or an alternative diagnosis.
  • New onset or worsening sensorineural hearing loss: Suggests inner ear damage, requiring prompt audiological and ENT assessment.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Perforated eardrum"][/sc]
  • Young children: May present with non-specific symptoms like irritability, fever, or pulling at the ear, making diagnosis challenging.
  • Older adults: Hearing loss may be attributed to presbycusis, potentially delaying diagnosis of a perforation.
  • Immunosuppressed individuals: May have less pronounced inflammatory signs despite significant infection, increasing risk of complications.
  • Chronic perforations: May be asymptomatic apart from hearing loss, with occasional discharge during upper respiratory tract infections.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Perforated eardrum"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected perforated eardrum: Otoscopy is the primary diagnostic tool, allowing direct visualisation of the tympanic membrane.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Audiometry to quantify hearing loss and differentiate conductive from sensorineural components.
  • Imaging - CT temporal bones: Considered if complications like mastoiditis, cholesteatoma, or intracranial spread are suspected.
  • Serial reassessment: Repeat otoscopy to monitor healing and assess for complications, particularly if symptoms persist beyond 4-6 weeks.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Perforated eardrum"][/sc]
  • A 35-year-old male presents with sudden onset right ear pain and reduced hearing for 2 days, following a recent upper respiratory tract infection, with subsequent discharge. Otoscopy reveals a visible perforation.
  • An acute, unilateral, inflammatory ear condition in a young adult, characterised by pain, hearing impairment, and discharge, suggesting a breach in the tympanic membrane.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Perforated eardrum"][/sc]
  • Acute otitis media without perforation - intact tympanic membrane, bulging and inflamed.
  • Otitis externa - inflammation of the ear canal, pain on tragal movement, normal tympanic membrane.
  • Cholesteatoma - often associated with chronic discharge, hearing loss, and a pearly white mass on otoscopy.
  • Foreign body in ear canal - visible on otoscopy, may cause pain and hearing loss.
  • Barotrauma without perforation - history of pressure changes, pain, but intact tympanic membrane.
  • Traumatic injury to ear canal - may cause bleeding and pain, but tympanic membrane may be intact.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Perforated eardrum"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Provide adequate analgesia (e.g., paracetamol, ibuprofen) and advise on ear care (keep ear dry, avoid swimming).
  • Definitive management: Most acute perforations heal spontaneously. Consider a short course of topical antibiotic ear drops if there is ongoing infection and discharge.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Review in 4-6 weeks to confirm healing. Refer to ENT if perforation persists beyond 8 weeks or if complications arise.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Advise patients to seek urgent medical attention for worsening pain, fever, facial weakness, or vertigo.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Perforated eardrum"][/sc]
  • Persistent perforation: Failure of the tympanic membrane to heal, leading to chronic conductive hearing loss and increased risk of recurrent infections.
  • Chronic otitis media: Ongoing inflammation and infection of the middle ear, potentially leading to ossicular erosion and cholesteatoma formation.
  • Cholesteatoma: An abnormal, non-cancerous skin growth in the middle ear, which can erode bone and cause significant damage to the ear structures.
  • Hearing loss: Can be conductive due to the perforation itself, or sensorineural if the inner ear is affected by severe infection or trauma.
  • Labyrinthitis: Inflammation of the inner ear, causing vertigo, dizziness, and potentially sensorineural hearing loss.
