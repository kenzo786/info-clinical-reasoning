Subdural haemorrhage [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Subdural haemorrhage"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Subdural haemorrhage"][/sc]
  • A collection of blood in the subdural space, between the dura mater and the arachnoid mater, typically resulting from trauma.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical features and risk factors for acute and chronic subdural haemorrhage, and understand the principles of immediate management and investigation.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Subdural haemorrhage"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Subdural haemorrhage"][/sc]
  • Acute subdural haemorrhage (ASDH) accounts for approximately 10-20% of all traumatic brain injuries requiring hospital admission in the UK, with an incidence of around 1.7-2.0 per 100,000 population per year [1].
  • Chronic subdural haemorrhage (CSDH) incidence is estimated at 8-10 per 100,000 population per year in the UK, rising significantly in those over 65 years to 50-100 per 100,000, and up to 170 per 100,000 in those over 80 years [2].
  • Risk factors include advanced age, anticoagulant or antiplatelet medication use, alcohol misuse, epilepsy, and previous head injury [3].
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Subdural haemorrhage"][/sc]
  • Traumatic injury, often minor in older adults, causes sudden acceleration-deceleration forces on the brain, leading to tearing of the bridging veins that traverse the subdural space [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Bridging veins: what is their anatomy and why are they vulnerable in head injury?" text="bridging veins"][/sc].
  • Blood accumulates in the subdural space, forming a haematoma that can exert mass effect on the brain parenchyma, leading to increased intracranial pressure (ICP) and neurological dysfunction.
  • In chronic cases, repeated minor trauma or brain atrophy (common in elderly) stretches bridging veins, making them more susceptible to rupture, with subsequent slow accumulation of blood and breakdown products, leading to an encapsulated collection.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Subdural haemorrhage"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Acute subdural haemorrhage typically presents within 72 hours of significant head trauma, with rapid neurological deterioration due to acute bleeding and mass effect.
  • Progressive phase: Subacute subdural haemorrhage develops between 3 days and 3 weeks post-injury, often with fluctuating or delayed onset of symptoms as the haematoma expands more slowly.
  • Late phase: Chronic subdural haemorrhage manifests more than 3 weeks after often minor or unrecognised head injury, with insidious onset of symptoms that can fluctuate over weeks to months.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Subdural haemorrhage"][/sc]
  • Ask about headache - often severe and persistent in acute cases, or fluctuating and less specific in chronic presentations.
  • Ask about altered consciousness - ranging from mild confusion or drowsiness to coma, indicating significant intracranial pressure or brain injury.
  • Ask about focal neurological deficits - such as unilateral weakness, speech disturbance, or visual field defects, localising the area of brain compression.
  • Ask about nausea and vomiting - non-specific symptoms that can indicate raised intracranial pressure.
  • Ask about history of head injury - crucial for acute cases, but often minor or forgotten in chronic subdural haemorrhage.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Subdural haemorrhage"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - particularly blood pressure and heart rate, which may show Cushing\\\'s reflex (hypertension, bradycardia, irregular respiration) in severe raised ICP.
  • Check Glasgow Coma Scale (GCS) - a critical indicator of neurological status and severity of brain injury.
  • Check pupillary responses - asymmetry or sluggishness can indicate uncal herniation or direct cranial nerve compression.
  • Check focal neurological signs - such as hemiparesis, dysphasia, or papilloedema (late sign of raised ICP) [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Papilloedema: what is its significance in raised intracranial pressure?" text="papilloedema"][/sc].
  • Check signs of external trauma - scalp lacerations, bruising, or Battle\\\'s sign, which may indicate the mechanism of injury.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Subdural haemorrhage"][/sc]
  • Decreased GCS: Indicates worsening neurological function and potentially rapidly increasing intracranial pressure, requiring urgent neurosurgical review.
  • New focal neurological deficit: Suggests progressive brain compression or injury, necessitating immediate imaging and specialist assessment.
  • Pupillary asymmetry or non-reactivity: A sign of uncal herniation, a life-threatening complication requiring emergency intervention.
  • Seizures: Can be a manifestation of cortical irritation from the haematoma, indicating significant neurological compromise.
  • Signs of non-accidental injury in children: Bruising, retinal haemorrhages, or other suspicious injuries in infants or young children warrant safeguarding concerns and urgent investigation.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Subdural haemorrhage"][/sc]
  • Elderly patients: Often present with chronic subdural haemorrhage after minor or unremembered trauma, with vague symptoms like fluctuating cognition, gait disturbance, or mild headache, mimicking dementia or stroke [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Chronic subdural haematoma in elderly: why is diagnosis challenging?" text="chronic subdural haematoma in elderly"][/sc].
  • Anticoagulated patients: Have an increased risk of developing subdural haemorrhage even after trivial head injury, and may present with less severe initial symptoms that rapidly deteriorate.
  • Children (non-accidental injury): Subdural haemorrhage in infants, particularly without clear history of significant trauma, should raise suspicion for non-accidental injury, often associated with retinal haemorrhages.
  • Alcohol misuse: Chronic alcoholics are prone to brain atrophy and falls, increasing their risk of chronic subdural haemorrhage with often subtle or delayed presentation.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Subdural haemorrhage"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected subdural haemorrhage: Initial assessment includes full neurological examination and urgent blood tests (full blood count, coagulation screen, renal function, group and save).
  • Imaging - CT head: Non-contrast CT head is the gold standard for initial diagnosis, typically showing a crescent-shaped hyperdensity in acute subdural haemorrhage, which becomes isodense in subacute and hypodense in chronic stages [sc name="jitl-query" Q="CT findings in subdural haemorrhage: how do they evolve over time?" text="CT findings in subdural haemorrhage"][/sc].
  • Imaging - MRI brain: May be used in subacute or chronic cases, or when CT findings are equivocal, providing better characterisation of the haematoma and underlying brain injury.
  • Severity/risk tools: The Markwalder Grading Scale or GCS can be used to assess neurological severity, but no specific MDCalc link is routinely used for risk stratification in UK practice.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Consider further investigations such as lumbar puncture (if meningitis/encephalitis suspected and no contraindications) or toxicology screen.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Subdural haemorrhage"][/sc]
  • Epidural haemorrhage: Typically arterial bleed, often associated with skull fracture and lucid interval, appearing biconvex on CT, unlike the crescent shape of subdural haemorrhage.
  • Subarachnoid haemorrhage: Usually presents with sudden onset severe headache ("thunderclap"), often due to aneurysm rupture, with blood seen in the basal cisterns on CT.
  • Intracerebral haemorrhage: Bleeding within the brain parenchyma, often associated with hypertension or amyloid angiopathy, presenting with focal neurological deficits.
  • Ischaemic stroke: Acute neurological deficits due to arterial occlusion, typically without headache or history of trauma, and different imaging characteristics.
  • Transient ischaemic attack (TIA): Transient neurological symptoms resolving within 24 hours, without evidence of acute infarction on imaging.
  • Dementia: Progressive cognitive decline, but typically without acute fluctuations or focal neurological signs unless complicated by other pathology [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Dementia vs chronic subdural haematoma: how to differentiate?" text="dementia vs chronic subdural haematoma"][/sc].
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Subdural haemorrhage"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Secure airway, breathing, and circulation (ABC); manage intracranial pressure; reverse anticoagulation if present; urgent neurosurgical consultation.
  • Definitive management: Surgical evacuation of the haematoma (e.g., craniotomy for acute, burr hole drainage for chronic) is often warranted, depending on haematoma size, mass effect, and neurological status.
  • Adjunctive management: May include seizure prophylaxis, osmotic therapy (e.g., mannitol) for raised ICP, and strict blood pressure control.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Close neurological observation, serial imaging, and rehabilitation are often required post-operatively.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Patients and carers should be advised on symptoms of neurological deterioration and when to seek urgent medical attention.
  • Exam pharmacology reference (UKMLA/MRCGP): Consideration of agents to reverse anticoagulation (e.g., prothrombin complex concentrate for warfarin, idarucizumab for dabigatran, andexanet alfa for factor Xa inhibitors) and seizure prophylaxis (e.g., [sc name="jitl-medication" Q="Levetiracetam use in subdural haemorrhage" text="levetiracetam"][/sc]). Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Subdural haemorrhage"][/sc]
  • Brain herniation: Occurs when increased intracranial pressure forces brain tissue across anatomical barriers, leading to severe neurological dysfunction and often death, particularly in acute, rapidly expanding haematomas.
  • Seizures: Can develop acutely or chronically due to cortical irritation from the haematoma or underlying brain injury, impacting long-term neurological outcome.
  • Re-accumulation of haematoma: Especially common in chronic subdural haemorrhage, requiring repeat surgical intervention and often associated with ongoing anticoagulation or coagulopathy.
  • Infection: A risk following surgical intervention, leading to meningitis, encephalitis, or wound infection, which can worsen neurological prognosis.
  • Post-concussion syndrome: Persistent symptoms such as headache, dizziness, and cognitive difficulties can occur for weeks to months after the initial injury, even after haematoma resolution.
