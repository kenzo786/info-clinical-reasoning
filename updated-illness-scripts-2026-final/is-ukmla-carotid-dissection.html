Carotid dissection [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Carotid dissection"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Carotid dissection"][/sc]
  • Carotid artery dissection involves a tear in the inner lining of the carotid artery wall, allowing blood to separate the layers and form a false lumen, which can compromise blood flow to the brain [1, 2, 3].
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise carotid dissection as a cause of acute neurological symptoms, particularly stroke or transient ischaemic attack (TIA) in younger adults, and understand the importance of prompt diagnosis and initial management.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Carotid dissection"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Carotid dissection"][/sc]
  • The incidence of carotid artery dissection is estimated to be 2.5-3.0 per 100,000 population per year, though the true incidence may be higher due to underdiagnosis [4, 5].
  • It is a significant cause of stroke in individuals under 50 years of age, accounting for a notable proportion of ischaemic strokes in this demographic [1, 2, 6].
  • Risk factors include minor trauma (e.g., neck manipulation, sudden head movements), connective tissue disorders (e.g., Ehlers-Danlos syndrome, Marfan syndrome), fibromuscular dysplasia, and hypertension [7, 8].
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Carotid dissection"][/sc]
  • A tear in the arterial intima (innermost layer) allows blood from the vessel lumen to penetrate the arterial wall, creating a false lumen within the media layer [9].
  • The expanding intramural haematoma within the false lumen can compress the true lumen, leading to stenosis or occlusion of the artery, thereby reducing cerebral blood flow [9].
  • Thrombus formation within the false lumen or on the disrupted intimal surface can embolise distally to the cerebral circulation, causing ischaemic stroke or TIA [10]. Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Carotid dissection"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Symptoms such as headache, neck pain, and Horner\'s syndrome often develop acutely, within minutes to hours of the dissection event.
  • Progressive phase: Neurological symptoms, including TIA or stroke, may develop hours to days after the initial pain, as the intramural haematoma expands or thromboembolism occurs.
  • Late phase: Resolution of symptoms can occur over weeks to months, but some patients may experience persistent neurological deficits or develop complications such as pseudoaneurysm formation, particularly beyond the first few weeks.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Carotid dissection"][/sc]
  • Ask about unilateral headache or neck pain - often severe and sudden onset, which can precede neurological symptoms by hours or days.
  • Ask about transient monocular vision loss (amaurosis fugax) - suggesting retinal ischaemia due to emboli from the carotid artery.
  • Ask about focal neurological deficits - such as unilateral weakness, numbness, or speech disturbance, indicative of cerebral ischaemia (TIA or stroke).
  • Ask about symptoms of Horner\'s syndrome - including drooping eyelid (ptosis), constricted pupil (miosis), and decreased sweating (anhidrosis) on the affected side of the face, due to sympathetic chain involvement.
  • Ask about pulsatile tinnitus - a less common symptom caused by altered blood flow dynamics within the dissected artery.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Carotid dissection"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - to assess for systemic signs of distress or complications, though often normal in isolated dissection.
  • Check for Horner\'s syndrome - specifically ptosis and miosis on the ipsilateral side, which is a classic sign of carotid dissection. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Horner syndrome mechanism in carotid dissection: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Horner\'s syndrome"][/sc]
  • Check for carotid bruit - a whooshing sound heard over the carotid artery, indicating turbulent blood flow due to stenosis, though its absence does not rule out dissection.
  • Check for focal neurological signs - such as hemiparesis, hemisensory loss, or dysphasia, consistent with TIA or stroke.
  • Check for cranial nerve palsies - particularly lower cranial nerves (IX, X, XI, XII) due to local compression by the expanding haematoma.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Carotid dissection"][/sc]
  • Sudden severe headache or neck pain - especially if unilateral and associated with neurological symptoms, warrants urgent investigation for dissection.
  • Acute onset focal neurological deficit - new weakness, sensory loss, or speech disturbance, indicating cerebral ischaemia requiring immediate assessment and management.
  • Horner\'s syndrome - particularly if new and painful, as it is a strong indicator of carotid dissection and requires urgent imaging.
  • Stroke or TIA in young adults - carotid dissection is a leading cause in this demographic, necessitating a high index of suspicion.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Carotid dissection"][/sc]
  • Isolated headache or neck pain: Dissection can present solely with pain, without overt neurological deficits, making diagnosis challenging.
  • Delayed neurological symptoms: Stroke or TIA may occur days after the initial pain, requiring careful follow-up and patient education.
  • Spontaneous dissection: Occurs without significant trauma, often in individuals with underlying arteriopathy, highlighting the need for consideration even in the absence of injury. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Spontaneous carotid dissection: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Spontaneous dissection"][/sc]
  • Bilateral dissection: Although rare, simultaneous or sequential dissection of both carotid arteries can occur, leading to more severe and complex presentations.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Carotid dissection"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected carotid dissection:
    • `Imaging - CT Angiography (CTA) or MR Angiography (MRA):` These are the primary diagnostic modalities, demonstrating the intimal flap, intramural haematoma, and luminal narrowing or occlusion. MRA is often preferred for its ability to visualise the arterial wall and avoid radiation exposure. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="MRI/MRA diagnosis of carotid dissection: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="MRI/MRA diagnosis"][/sc]
  • Severity/risk tools:
    • No specific validated severity or risk score is routinely used for carotid dissection on MDCalc, but general stroke severity scales (e.g., NIHSS) are used for neurological assessment.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible:
    • `CT head (non-contrast):` To rule out intracranial haemorrhage or other acute intracranial pathology, especially in patients presenting with sudden severe headache.
  • Imaging - Duplex Ultrasound: Can be used as an initial screening tool or for follow-up, but its sensitivity for dissection, especially in the distal internal carotid artery, is lower than CTA or MRA.
  • Serial reassessment: Clinical and imaging reassessment may be required to monitor for progression, resolution, or complications such as pseudoaneurysm formation.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Carotid dissection"][/sc]
  • A young adult presenting with acute onset unilateral neck pain, headache, and new neurological deficits such as Horner\'s syndrome or TIA-like symptoms, potentially following minor trauma.
  • A patient with acute ischaemic stroke in the setting of recent neck pain and a history of connective tissue disorder, concerning for carotid artery dissection.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Carotid dissection"][/sc]
  • Migraine with aura - typically recurrent, with a characteristic aura, and usually resolves without focal neurological deficits.
  • Tension headache - generally bilateral, dull, and not associated with focal neurological signs or Horner\'s syndrome.
  • Cluster headache - severe unilateral pain, but typically periorbital and associated with autonomic features (e.g., lacrimation, conjunctival injection) rather than Horner\'s syndrome due to dissection.
  • Cervical spondylosis or radiculopathy - pain is usually exacerbated by neck movements and may be associated with dermatomal sensory loss or myotomal weakness, but not typically Horner\'s syndrome or acute stroke.
  • Stroke of other aetiology (e.g., cardioembolic, atherosclerotic) - often presents with similar neurological deficits but typically lacks the preceding neck pain or Horner\'s syndrome. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Stroke/TIA in young adults differential diagnosis: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Stroke/TIA in young adults"][/sc]
  • Subarachnoid haemorrhage - presents with sudden severe "thunderclap" headache, but usually without focal neurological signs unless complicated by vasospasm or secondary ischaemia.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Carotid dissection"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Recognition of acute neurological symptoms and prompt referral to a stroke unit or specialist neurological care for urgent imaging and assessment. Supportive care often includes blood pressure management and pain control.
  • Definitive management: Antithrombotic therapy (antiplatelets or anticoagulants) is typically initiated to prevent ischaemic stroke. The choice between antiplatelets (e.g., aspirin) and anticoagulants (e.g., heparin followed by warfarin) is often individualised based on stroke risk and bleeding risk, with current evidence suggesting similar efficacy for both in preventing recurrent stroke [11].
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Regular clinical assessment and repeat imaging (e.g., MRA at 3-6 months) are often warranted to monitor for arterial healing, resolution of stenosis, or development of pseudoaneurysm.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Patients should be advised on recognising worsening neurological symptoms and seeking immediate medical attention.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Carotid dissection"][/sc]
  • Ischaemic stroke: The most serious complication, occurring due to arterial occlusion or distal embolisation, leading to permanent neurological deficits. This risk is highest in the early phase following dissection.
  • Transient ischaemic attack (TIA): Temporary neurological dysfunction due to transient cerebral ischaemia, serving as a warning sign for impending stroke.
  • Pseudoaneurysm formation: A contained rupture of the arterial wall that can develop weeks to months after the initial dissection, potentially leading to mass effect or rupture, though rare.
  • Recurrent dissection: Although uncommon, a repeat dissection can occur in the same or contralateral carotid artery, particularly in individuals with underlying arteriopathies.
  • Cranial nerve palsies: Persistent palsies of cranial nerves (e.g., IX, X, XI, XII) can result from direct compression by the haematoma, leading to dysphagia or dysphonia.
