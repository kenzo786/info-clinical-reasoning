Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Bradyarrhythmias including AV block"][/sc]
  • Bradyarrhythmias are abnormal heart rhythms characterised by a heart rate slower than normal, typically below 60 beats per minute, which can be physiological or pathological.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise and differentiate types of bradyarrhythmias, including sick sinus syndrome and atrioventricular blocks, and understand their clinical significance and initial management.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Bradyarrhythmias including AV block"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Bradyarrhythmias including AV block"][/sc]
  • Sick sinus syndrome prevalence is approximately 0.03% of the general population in the UK, increasing with age.
  • Atrioventricular block prevalence estimates range from 0.015% to 0.1% in the UK, with coexisting sinus node and AV node abnormalities being common.
  • Risk factors include increasing age, ischaemic heart disease, structural heart disease, electrolyte disturbances, and certain medications (e.g., beta-blockers, calcium channel blockers, digoxin).
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Bradyarrhythmias including AV block"][/sc]
  • Trigger: Dysfunction in electrical impulse generation (e.g., sick sinus syndrome affecting the sinoatrial node) or abnormal conduction (e.g., atrioventricular block affecting the AV node or His-Purkinje system).
  • Progression: Impaired impulse formation or conduction leads to a reduced ventricular rate, which can be intermittent or permanent. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Wenckebach phenomenon: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Wenckebach phenomenon"][/sc]
  • Consequence: Insufficient cardiac output to meet metabolic demands, resulting in symptoms such as syncope, dizziness, fatigue, and dyspnoea due to cerebral and systemic hypoperfusion.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Bradyarrhythmias including AV block"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Symptoms may be intermittent and subtle, such as occasional dizziness or fatigue, often correlated with transient bradycardic episodes.
  • Progressive phase: As underlying conduction abnormalities worsen, symptoms become more frequent and severe, potentially including presyncope or syncope, particularly beyond a few weeks to months.
  • Late phase: Persistent and profound bradycardia can lead to chronic symptoms, heart failure, or recurrent syncopal episodes (e.g., Stokes-Adams attacks), necessitating intervention.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Bradyarrhythmias including AV block"][/sc]
  • Ask about faints or syncope - indicates significant cerebral hypoperfusion and potential for serious underlying bradyarrhythmia.
  • Ask about dizziness or lightheadedness - suggests transient reduction in cerebral blood flow, often an early symptom.
  • Ask about fatigue or reduced exercise tolerance - reflects inadequate cardiac output to meet metabolic demands, particularly on exertion.
  • Ask about palpitations - may indicate compensatory tachyarrhythmias in sick sinus syndrome (tachy-brady syndrome) or awareness of irregular beats.
  • Ask about dyspnoea or chest pain - can signify cardiac ischaemia or heart failure due to prolonged bradycardia.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Bradyarrhythmias including AV block"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - bradycardia (heart rate <60 bpm) is the defining feature; hypotension may be present in symptomatic cases.
  • Check for signs of hypoperfusion - cool peripheries, prolonged capillary refill time, altered mental status.
  • Check for signs of heart failure - elevated JVP, peripheral oedema, pulmonary crackles, suggesting chronic or severe bradycardia.
  • Check for cannon A waves in the JVP - indicates AV dissociation, particularly in complete heart block. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Cannon A waves: what are they and why do they matter clinically?" text="Cannon A waves"][/sc]
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Bradyarrhythmias including AV block"][/sc]
  • Syncope or presyncope: Indicates critical cerebral hypoperfusion requiring urgent assessment for high-grade AV block or severe sinus node dysfunction.
  • New-onset chest pain or dyspnoea: Suggests myocardial ischaemia or heart failure precipitated by bradycardia, requiring immediate cardiac evaluation.
  • Altered mental status: Points to significant cerebral hypoperfusion, particularly in older adults, necessitating rapid intervention.
  • Stokes-Adams attack: Sudden collapse without warning due to transient asystole or severe bradycardia, indicating a high risk of sudden cardiac death and requiring urgent pacing.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Bradyarrhythmias including AV block"][/sc]
  • Older adults: May present with non-specific symptoms like falls, confusion, or general weakness, rather than classic syncope, due to reduced physiological reserve.
  • Athletes: Physiological bradycardia is common; however, symptomatic bradycardia or high-grade AV block in athletes warrants investigation to differentiate from benign causes.
  • Post-cardiac surgery: Bradyarrhythmias, including AV block, can occur due to surgical trauma or inflammation, requiring careful monitoring. Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Bradyarrhythmias including AV block"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected bradyarrhythmia: 12-lead ECG to identify type of bradyarrhythmia (e.g., sinus bradycardia, AV block type), PR interval, QRS duration, and signs of ischaemia.
  • Core for suspected bradyarrhythmia: Ambulatory ECG monitoring (e.g., 24-hour Holter, event recorder) to correlate symptoms with rhythm disturbances, especially for intermittent bradycardia.
  • Severity/risk tools: CHADS2-VASc score for stroke risk in atrial fibrillation, though not directly for bradyarrhythmias, may be relevant if tachy-brady syndrome is present.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Electrolytes (potassium, magnesium, calcium), thyroid function tests, and cardiac enzymes to rule out reversible causes or myocardial injury.
  • Serial reassessment: Continuous cardiac monitoring in hospital for symptomatic patients or those with high-grade AV block to detect progression or life-threatening events. Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Bradyarrhythmias including AV block"][/sc]
  • Vasovagal syncope - often triggered by specific events, prodromal symptoms, and rapid recovery, unlike persistent bradycardia.
  • Orthostatic hypotension - symptoms primarily on standing, with a drop in blood pressure without significant bradycardia.
  • Medication side effects - review drug history for agents known to cause bradycardia (e.g., beta-blockers, calcium channel blockers, amiodarone).
  • Hypothyroidism - can cause generalised slowing of physiological processes, including heart rate, but typically with other systemic symptoms.
  • Hyperkalaemia - severe hyperkalaemia can lead to bradycardia and conduction blocks, often with characteristic ECG changes. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Hyperkalaemia and bradycardia: what is the link and why does it matter clinically?" text="Hyperkalaemia and bradycardia"][/sc]
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Bradyarrhythmias including AV block"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Recognition of symptomatic bradycardia, escalation to senior medical staff, and supportive care including oxygen and IV access.
  • Definitive management: For symptomatic bradycardia due to sick sinus syndrome or high-grade AV block, permanent pacemaker implantation is often indicated.
  • Adjunctive management: Reversible causes, such as electrolyte imbalances or offending medications, should be addressed and corrected.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Regular follow-up post-pacemaker implantation to ensure device function and monitor for complications.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Patients should be advised on symptoms requiring urgent medical attention, such as recurrent syncope or worsening dizziness.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Bradyarrhythmias including AV block"][/sc]
  • Syncope and falls: Due to transient cerebral hypoperfusion, leading to injury and reduced quality of life, particularly in older adults.
  • Heart failure: Prolonged severe bradycardia can lead to reduced cardiac output and subsequent development or exacerbation of heart failure.
  • Sudden cardiac death: High-grade AV blocks, especially Mobitz II and complete heart block, carry a significant risk of asystole and sudden cardiac death if untreated.
  • Pacemaker syndrome: Can occur post-pacemaker implantation if AV synchrony is lost, leading to symptoms like fatigue, dyspnoea, and palpitations, typically in the progressive phase.
  • Tachy-brady syndrome: In sick sinus syndrome, alternating bradycardia and tachycardia can lead to symptoms related to both slow and fast heart rates, increasing stroke risk if atrial fibrillation is present.
