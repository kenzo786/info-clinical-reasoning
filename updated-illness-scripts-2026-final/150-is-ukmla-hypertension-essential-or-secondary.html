Hypertension (essential or secondary) [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Hypertension (essential or secondary)"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Hypertension (essential or secondary)"][/sc]
  • Hypertension is defined as persistently elevated arterial blood pressure, diagnosed when clinic blood pressure is consistently 140/90 mmHg or higher, or ambulatory/home blood pressure monitoring (ABPM/HBPM) average is 135/85 mmHg or higher [1].
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the diagnostic criteria for hypertension and understand its significance as a major modifiable risk factor for cardiovascular disease, necessitating early identification and management.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Hypertension (essential or secondary)"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Hypertension (essential or secondary)"][/sc]
  • Approximately one in three adults in the UK has high blood pressure, with prevalence increasing significantly with age [2].
  • An estimated 5 million adults in the UK have undiagnosed hypertension, highlighting a substantial public health challenge [3].
  • Key risk factors include increasing age, family history, ethnicity (higher risk in Black African and South Asian populations), and lifestyle factors such as smoking, excessive alcohol intake, high dietary salt, unhealthy diet, obesity, and physical inactivity [4, 5].
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Hypertension (essential or secondary)"][/sc]
  • Essential hypertension often begins with a complex interplay of genetic predisposition and environmental factors, leading to subtle dysregulation of blood pressure control mechanisms.
  • This progresses through increased sympathetic nervous system activity, activation of the renin-angiotensin-aldosterone system (RAAS), and endothelial dysfunction, causing increased peripheral vascular resistance and fluid retention [6].
  • The consequence is sustained elevation of arterial blood pressure, leading to structural and functional changes in target organs such as the heart, kidneys, brain, and vasculature, increasing the risk of cardiovascular events. Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Hypertension (essential or secondary)"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Hypertension often develops insidiously over many years, with individuals remaining asymptomatic despite gradually rising blood pressure. This phase may be detected through routine screening.
  • Progressive phase: Without intervention, sustained high blood pressure leads to progressive damage to blood vessels and target organs. This can manifest as symptoms related to organ dysfunction, such as headaches, visual changes, or early signs of renal impairment.
  • Late phase: Prolonged uncontrolled hypertension culminates in significant cardiovascular, cerebrovascular, and renal complications, often requiring intensive medical management and leading to substantial morbidity and mortality. This phase is particularly evident beyond several years of unmanaged hypertension.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Hypertension (essential or secondary)"][/sc]
  • Ask about headaches - particularly persistent or severe headaches, especially in the morning, which may indicate significantly elevated blood pressure or hypertensive crisis.
  • Ask about blurred vision or visual disturbances - these can be signs of hypertensive retinopathy, suggesting end-organ damage.
  • Ask about nosebleeds (epistaxis) - recurrent or severe nosebleeds can be associated with uncontrolled hypertension.
  • Ask about shortness of breath or chest pain - these symptoms may suggest cardiac involvement, such as angina or heart failure, as a complication of hypertension.
  • Ask about dizziness or light-headedness - while often non-specific, these can occur with very high blood pressure or orthostatic changes.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Hypertension (essential or secondary)"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - persistently elevated blood pressure (clinic BP ≥140/90 mmHg, ABPM/HBPM average ≥135/85 mmHg) is the cardinal sign. Tachycardia or other arrhythmias may also be present.
  • Check fundoscopy - for signs of hypertensive retinopathy (e.g., arteriolar narrowing, AV nipping, haemorrhages, exudates, papilloedema), indicating chronic or severe hypertension. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Hypertensive retinopathy: what are its stages and clinical significance?" text="Hypertensive retinopathy"][/sc]
  • Check cardiovascular examination - for signs of left ventricular hypertrophy (e.g., displaced apex beat, S4 gallop), murmurs (e.g., aortic stenosis), or peripheral oedema, suggesting cardiac complications.
  • Check abdominal examination - for renal bruits (suggesting renal artery stenosis) or palpable kidneys (suggesting polycystic kidney disease), which could indicate secondary hypertension.
  • Check neurological examination - for focal neurological deficits, altered mental status, or seizures, which are red flags for hypertensive encephalopathy or stroke.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Hypertension (essential or secondary)"][/sc]
  • Hypertensive crisis/emergency - severe hypertension (typically >180/120 mmHg) with new or worsening target organ damage (e.g., papilloedema, acute kidney injury, acute heart failure, neurological deficits) warrants immediate specialist assessment and blood pressure reduction.
  • Sudden onset or rapidly worsening hypertension - particularly in younger individuals or those previously well-controlled, may indicate a secondary cause such as renal artery stenosis or phaeochromocytoma.
  • Symptoms suggestive of secondary hypertension - e.g., muscle weakness/cramps (Conn's syndrome), sweating/palpitations/episodic headaches (phaeochromocytoma), or symptoms of kidney disease (renal parenchymal disease).
  • Neurological symptoms - new or sudden severe headache, visual disturbances, seizures, or focal neurological deficits may indicate hypertensive encephalopathy or stroke, requiring urgent investigation.
  • Chest pain or acute shortness of breath - these can be signs of acute coronary syndrome or acute heart failure precipitated by severe hypertension, necessitating emergency evaluation.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Hypertension (essential or secondary)"][/sc]
  • White-coat hypertension: Elevated clinic blood pressure readings with normal ambulatory or home blood pressure measurements. This is important to recognise to avoid unnecessary treatment and its associated risks. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="White-coat hypertension: how is it diagnosed and managed?" text="White-coat hypertension"][/sc]
  • Masked hypertension: Normal clinic blood pressure readings but elevated ambulatory or home blood pressure measurements. This carries similar cardiovascular risk to sustained hypertension and requires active detection.
  • Elderly patients: May present with isolated systolic hypertension due to arterial stiffness, and can be more susceptible to orthostatic hypotension and adverse effects of antihypertensive medications.
  • Pregnancy: Hypertension can be gestational, pre-existing, or pre-eclampsia, each with distinct diagnostic criteria, management, and risks to both mother and foetus.
  • Secondary hypertension: Hypertension caused by an underlying, potentially treatable condition (e.g., renal artery stenosis, primary aldosteronism, phaeochromocytoma, Cushing's syndrome, coarctation of the aorta). Clues include early onset, sudden worsening, resistance to treatment, or specific associated symptoms.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Hypertension (essential or secondary)"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected hypertension:
    • Confirm diagnosis with ABPM or HBPM if clinic BP is 140/90 mmHg or higher [1].
    • Blood tests: HbA1c, electrolytes, creatinine, eGFR, total cholesterol, HDL cholesterol [1].
    • Urine tests: albumin:creatinine ratio (ACR) and haematuria using a reagent strip [1].
    • 12-lead electrocardiogram (ECG) to assess for left ventricular hypertrophy or other cardiac abnormalities [1].
    • Fundoscopy to check for hypertensive retinopathy [1].
  • Severity/risk tools: Consider using a cardiovascular risk assessment tool such as the ASCVD Risk Calculator on MDCalc to estimate 10-year risk of atherosclerotic cardiovascular disease events [7].
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible (secondary hypertension investigations):
    • Renal artery stenosis: Doppler ultrasound of renal arteries, CT angiography, or MR angiography [8].
    • Primary aldosteronism (Conn's syndrome): Plasma aldosterone-to-renin ratio (ARR), followed by confirmatory tests like saline suppression test or fludrocortisone suppression test [9].
    • Phaeochromocytoma: 24-hour urinary metanephrines and normetanephrines, or plasma free metanephrines [10].
    • Coarctation of the aorta: Echocardiography, CT angiography, or MRI of the aorta [11].

  • A middle-aged male with asymptomatic, sustained hypertension and a family history of cardiovascular disease, concerning for increased long-term cardiovascular risk.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Hypertension (essential or secondary)"][/sc]
  • White-coat hypertension - consistently elevated clinic blood pressure but normal out-of-office readings (ABPM/HBPM), distinguishing it from true sustained hypertension.
  • Masked hypertension - normal clinic blood pressure but elevated out-of-office readings, often requiring ABPM/HBPM for diagnosis.
  • Renal artery stenosis - often presents with resistant hypertension, flash pulmonary oedema, or worsening renal function after ACE inhibitor initiation; may have an abdominal bruit.
  • Primary aldosteronism (Conn's syndrome) - typically associated with hypokalaemia, metabolic alkalosis, and resistant hypertension; diagnosed by elevated aldosterone-to-renin ratio.
  • Phaeochromocytoma - characterised by paroxysmal hypertension, headaches, palpitations, and sweating; diagnosed by elevated catecholamines/metanephrines.
  • Cushing's syndrome - associated with central obesity, moon facies, striae, and muscle weakness; diagnosed by cortisol excess.
  • Coarctation of the aorta - presents with differential blood pressure between upper and lower limbs, and often a murmur; diagnosed by imaging of the aorta. Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Hypertension (essential or secondary)"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: For severe hypertension or hypertensive crisis, immediate specialist assessment and careful blood pressure reduction are often warranted to prevent or limit target organ damage.
  • Definitive management: Lifestyle modifications (dietary changes, regular exercise, reduced salt intake, moderation of alcohol, smoking cessation) are fundamental for all patients. Pharmacological treatment typically follows a stepwise approach based on age, ethnicity, and comorbidities, often initiating with ACE inhibitors/ARBs, calcium channel blockers, or thiazide-like diuretics [1].
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Regular blood pressure monitoring is essential to assess treatment effectiveness and adjust medications as needed. Annual reviews should include assessment of cardiovascular risk factors and screening for target organ damage.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Patients with resistant hypertension (uncontrolled despite optimal doses of three antihypertensive agents, including a diuretic) or suspected secondary causes should be considered for referral to a specialist hypertension clinic.
  • Exam pharmacology reference (UKMLA/MRCGP): Knowledge of common antihypertensive drug classes and their mechanisms of action is often tested. Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Hypertension (essential or secondary)"][/sc]
  • Stroke (ischaemic or haemorrhagic): Sustained high blood pressure damages cerebral blood vessels, increasing the risk of rupture (haemorrhagic stroke) or occlusion (ischaemic stroke), leading to acute neurological deficits. This risk significantly increases with prolonged uncontrolled hypertension.
  • Myocardial infarction and heart failure: Chronic hypertension leads to left ventricular hypertrophy and increased myocardial oxygen demand, predisposing to coronary artery disease, myocardial infarction, and eventually heart failure due to impaired cardiac function. These complications typically manifest after years of sustained hypertension.
  • Chronic kidney disease: Hypertension causes progressive damage to renal arterioles, leading to nephrosclerosis, reduced glomerular filtration, and ultimately end-stage renal disease, often developing over many years.
  • Hypertensive retinopathy: Damage to retinal blood vessels can lead to visual impairment or blindness, ranging from mild changes to severe papilloedema, reflecting the severity and duration of hypertension.
  • Peripheral artery disease: Atherosclerosis accelerated by hypertension can narrow peripheral arteries, leading to claudication, critical limb ischaemia, and increased risk of amputation, particularly after prolonged exposure to high blood pressure.
  • Aortic dissection/aneurysm: High shear stress on the aortic wall due to uncontrolled hypertension can weaken the vessel, increasing the risk of dissection or aneurysm formation, which are acute, life-threatening events.
