Hyperthermia and hypothermia [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Hyperthermia and hypothermia"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Hyperthermia and hypothermia"][/sc]
  • Hyperthermia: A condition where the body's temperature regulation fails, leading to an uncontrolled rise in core body temperature above the normal range, often due to excessive heat production or impaired heat dissipation.
  • Hypothermia: A condition where the body loses heat faster than it can produce it, resulting in a dangerously low core body temperature, typically below 35°C.
  • UKMLA Learning Outcome: Recognise and manage disorders of thermoregulation, including hyperthermia and hypothermia, and their associated complications.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Hyperthermia and hypothermia"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Hyperthermia and hypothermia"][/sc]
  • In the UK, heat-related illnesses, including hyperthermia, see a significant increase during summer heatwaves, particularly affecting the elderly and those with chronic conditions.
  • Hypothermia is more prevalent in the colder months, with an estimated 300-600 deaths annually in England and Wales directly attributed to cold exposure.
  • Vulnerable populations, such as rough sleepers, infants, and individuals with mental health conditions, are disproportionately affected by both extremes of temperature.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Hyperthermia and hypothermia"][/sc]
  • Hyperthermia: Environmental heat stress or increased metabolic heat production (e.g., exercise, drugs) overwhelms the body's thermoregulatory mechanisms, leading to a rise in core temperature. This triggers a systemic inflammatory response, cellular damage, and organ dysfunction.
  • Hypothermia: Prolonged exposure to cold environments or impaired heat production (e.g., malnutrition, endocrine disorders) results in excessive heat loss. This causes a cascade of physiological changes, including vasoconstriction, reduced metabolic rate, and eventually cardiac and neurological compromise.
  • Consequence: Both conditions can lead to multi-organ failure, cardiac arrhythmias, and neurological impairment if not promptly recognised and managed.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Hyperthermia and hypothermia"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Initial compensatory mechanisms are activated, such as sweating and vasodilation in hyperthermia, or shivering and vasoconstriction in hypothermia, to maintain normothermia.
  • Progressive phase: Compensatory mechanisms become overwhelmed, leading to a progressive rise or fall in core body temperature, with initial symptoms like fatigue, dizziness, or confusion.
  • Late phase: Severe thermoregulatory failure ensues, resulting in profound organ dysfunction, altered mental status, and life-threatening complications such as cardiac arrest or rhabdomyolysis.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Hyperthermia and hypothermia"][/sc]
  • Ask about headache - *may indicate dehydration or cerebral oedema in hyperthermia, or early sign of hypothermia.*
  • Ask about confusion or altered mental status - *a critical indicator of severe thermoregulatory dysfunction requiring urgent intervention.*
  • Ask about muscle cramps or weakness - *suggests electrolyte imbalance, particularly in heat exhaustion.*
  • Ask about nausea or vomiting - *common in both heat-related illness and early hypothermia.*
  • Ask about feeling unusually hot or cold - *direct patient perception of temperature dysregulation.*
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Hyperthermia and hypothermia"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs (temperature, heart rate, blood pressure, respiratory rate) - *essential for assessing severity and guiding management. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Normal vital signs: what are they and why do they matter clinically?" text="Vitals"][/sc]*
  • Check skin for warmth, dryness, or clamminess - *dry, hot skin suggests heat stroke; cold, pale skin suggests hypothermia.*
  • Check neurological status (GCS, pupillary response) - *to identify encephalopathy or cerebral oedema.*
  • Check for shivering - *a key compensatory mechanism in early hypothermia.*
  • Check for signs of dehydration (e.g., reduced skin turgor, dry mucous membranes) - *often associated with hyperthermia.*
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Hyperthermia and hypothermia"][/sc]
  • Altered mental status: Indicates severe cerebral dysfunction and requires immediate cooling/warming.
  • Seizures: A sign of severe neurological compromise in both hyperthermia and hypothermia.
  • Cardiac arrhythmias: Can be life-threatening, particularly in severe hypothermia or heat stroke.
  • Core temperature >40°C (hyperthermia) or <32°C (hypothermia): Suggests critical illness and high risk of organ damage.
  • Evidence of multi-organ dysfunction: Such as acute kidney injury, liver dysfunction, or rhabdomyolysis.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Hyperthermia and hypothermia"][/sc]
  • Elderly: May present with subtle changes in mental status or functional decline without classic symptoms like sweating (hyperthermia) or shivering (hypothermia) due to impaired thermoregulation.
  • Infants: Can rapidly develop severe thermoregulatory dysfunction; may present with lethargy, poor feeding, or irritability rather than specific temperature-related symptoms.
  • Patients on certain medications (e.g., anticholinergics, diuretics): May have an exaggerated response to temperature extremes, masking typical signs or accelerating progression.
  • Individuals with spinal cord injuries: May have impaired autonomic thermoregulation, leading to poikilothermia and difficulty maintaining core body temperature.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Hyperthermia and hypothermia"][/sc]
  • Core:
    • Rectal temperature measurement: Most accurate for core body temperature.
    • Full blood count: May show haemoconcentration (hyperthermia) or signs of infection.
    • Urea and electrolytes: To assess hydration status and electrolyte imbalances (e.g., hyponatraemia, hyperkalaemia).
    • Renal function tests: To check for acute kidney injury.
    • Liver function tests: To assess for liver damage.
    • Creatine kinase: Elevated in rhabdomyolysis, a complication of both conditions.
    • Blood glucose: To rule out hypoglycaemia or hyperglycaemia as contributing factors.
    • Arterial blood gas: To assess acid-base status and oxygenation.
    • Coagulation screen: To detect disseminated intravascular coagulation (DIC).
  • Imaging:
    • Chest X-ray: To rule out pneumonia or pulmonary oedema.
    • CT head: If neurological symptoms are prominent or to rule out other intracranial pathology.
  • If alternative diagnosis suspected:
    • Thyroid function tests: To investigate thyroid storm (hyperthermia) or myxoedema coma (hypothermia).
    • Toxicology screen: To identify drug-induced thermoregulatory disorders.
  • Serial reassessment:
    • Continuous core temperature monitoring.
    • Repeat blood tests as clinically indicated to monitor organ function and electrolyte balance.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Hyperthermia and hypothermia"][/sc]
  • An elderly patient presenting with altered mental status and a core body temperature of 40.5°C after prolonged exposure to a hot environment, with dry, flushed skin and tachycardia.
  • A young homeless individual found unresponsive with a core body temperature of 30°C, bradycardia, and shallow respirations, following a night of cold exposure.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Hyperthermia and hypothermia"][/sc]
  • Sepsis: Can cause fever or hypothermia, but typically with a clear source of infection and systemic inflammatory response.
  • Thyroid storm/Myxoedema coma: Endocrine emergencies with profound temperature dysregulation, accompanied by other signs of thyroid dysfunction.
  • Neuroleptic Malignant Syndrome/Serotonin Syndrome: Drug-induced conditions presenting with hyperthermia, muscle rigidity, and autonomic instability.
  • Adrenal crisis: Can cause hypothermia, hypotension, and electrolyte disturbances.
  • Hypoglycaemia: May mimic hypothermia with altered mental status, but typically responds to glucose administration.
  • Stroke/Intracranial haemorrhage: Can cause central thermoregulatory dysfunction, leading to fever or hypothermia.
  • Drug overdose/Intoxication: Many substances can impair thermoregulation, leading to either hyperthermia or hypothermia.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Hyperthermia and hypothermia"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities (Hyperthermia):
    • Rapid cooling: Move to a cooler environment, remove clothing, apply cool water/ice packs to groin, axillae, neck.
    • Fluid resuscitation: Intravenous fluids to correct dehydration and electrolyte imbalances.
    • Manage complications: Treat seizures, arrhythmias, and organ dysfunction.
  • Immediate priorities (Hypothermia):
    • Rewarming: Remove wet clothing, insulate, provide warm blankets. Passive external rewarming for mild hypothermia; active external or internal rewarming for moderate to severe cases.
    • Supportive care: Maintain airway, breathing, and circulation.
    • Cardiac monitoring: For arrhythmias.
  • Definitive management:
    • Address underlying cause: Treat infection, manage endocrine disorders, discontinue causative medications.
    • Pharmacological interventions: Consider dantrolene for malignant hyperthermia or neuroleptic malignant syndrome.
    • Supportive organ care: Dialysis for renal failure, mechanical ventilation for respiratory failure.
  • Monitoring and follow-up:
    • Continuous core temperature monitoring until stable.
    • Serial blood tests to monitor electrolytes, renal, and liver function.
    • Neurological assessment.
  • Escalation and safety-netting:
    • Escalate to critical care for severe cases or those with multi-organ failure.
    • Educate patients and carers on prevention strategies and recognition of early signs of thermoregulatory dysfunction.
  • Pharmacology reference:
    • Consider paracetamol for fever reduction if not contraindicated, though it is less effective in true hyperthermia.
    • [sc name="jitl-medication" Q="Dantrolene use in Malignant Hyperthermia" text="Dantrolene"][/sc] may be indicated for specific hyperthermic syndromes.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Hyperthermia and hypothermia"][/sc]
  • Rhabdomyolysis: Muscle breakdown releasing myoglobin, which can lead to acute kidney injury due to tubular obstruction.
  • Disseminated Intravascular Coagulation (DIC): Widespread activation of the clotting cascade, leading to both thrombosis and haemorrhage.
  • Acute Kidney Injury (AKI): Due to dehydration, rhabdomyolysis, or direct thermal injury to renal cells.
  • Cardiac Arrhythmias: Especially ventricular fibrillation in severe hypothermia, or tachycardia/atrial fibrillation in hyperthermia.
  • Cerebral Oedema/Encephalopathy: Brain swelling or dysfunction due to direct thermal injury, electrolyte imbalances, or hypoxia.
  • Multi-organ Failure: The ultimate consequence of uncontrolled thermoregulatory dysfunction, affecting cardiovascular, respiratory, renal, hepatic, and neurological systems.
