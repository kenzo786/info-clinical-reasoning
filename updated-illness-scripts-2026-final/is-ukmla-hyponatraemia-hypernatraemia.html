Hyponatraemia/ hypernatraemia [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Hyponatraemia/ hypernatraemia"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Hyponatraemia/ hypernatraemia"][/sc]
  • **Hyponatraemia:** A serum sodium concentration below 135 mmol/L, often reflecting an imbalance in water and sodium homeostasis.
  • **UKMLA learning outcome:** Recognise the clinical features, causes, and initial management of hyponatraemia and hypernatraemia.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Hyponatraemia/ hypernatraemia"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Hyponatraemia/ hypernatraemia"][/sc]
  • Hyponatraemia is the most common electrolyte abnormality, affecting approximately 5% of adults and up to 35% of hospitalised patients in the UK. [1]
  • Risk factors for hyponatraemia include advanced age, heart failure, kidney disease, liver cirrhosis, and certain medications (e.g., thiazide diuretics, SSRIs).
  • Hypernatraemia is less common but carries a higher mortality, particularly in older adults and those with impaired thirst mechanisms or limited access to water.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Hyponatraemia/ hypernatraemia"][/sc]
  • **Trigger:** Hyponatraemia results from an excess of total body water relative to sodium (impaired water excretion/increased intake), while hypernatraemia stems from a deficit of water relative to sodium (inadequate intake/excessive loss).
  • **Progression:** In hyponatraemia, water shifts into cells causing swelling (cerebral oedema); in hypernatraemia, water shifts out of cells causing dehydration (cerebral shrinkage).
  • **Consequence:** Both conditions can lead to severe neurological dysfunction, including seizures and coma, with hyponatraemia risking cerebral oedema and hypernatraemia risking intracranial haemorrhage.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Hyponatraemia/ hypernatraemia"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Acute changes in sodium levels (within 24-48 hours) are more likely to cause severe symptoms due to rapid osmotic shifts and the brain\'s inability to adapt.
  • Progressive phase: Gradual changes allow for cerebral adaptation, reducing the risk of severe symptoms, particularly beyond 48 hours, but still requiring careful management.
  • Late phase: Chronic imbalances, if uncorrected, can lead to persistent neurological dysfunction and increased mortality.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Hyponatraemia/ hypernatraemia"][/sc]
  • Ask about nausea and vomiting - often early, non-specific indicators of electrolyte disturbance.
  • Ask about headache and confusion - suggest cerebral oedema in hyponatraemia or dehydration in hypernatraemia.
  • Ask about lethargy and fatigue - common in both conditions, reflecting general malaise and neurological impairment.
  • Ask about muscle cramps or weakness - can occur due to altered nerve and muscle function with significant sodium derangements.
  • Ask about thirst - prominent in hypernatraemia, indicating water deficit, but may be absent in some hyponatraemia types.
  • Ask about seizures - a critical symptom indicating severe, life-threatening electrolyte imbalance requiring urgent intervention.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Hyponatraemia/ hypernatraemia"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - hypotension and tachycardia may indicate hypovolaemia (e.g., hypovolaemic hyponatraemia or hypernatraemia with dehydration); hypertension and bradycardia may suggest increased intracranial pressure.
  • Check neurological status - altered mental state, reduced GCS, focal neurological deficits, or seizures indicate severe electrolyte disturbance.
  • Check fluid status - signs of dehydration (dry mucous membranes, reduced skin turgor) in hypernatraemia or hypovolaemic hyponatraemia; signs of fluid overload (oedema, crackles) in hypervolaemic hyponatraemia.
  • Check for signs of underlying cause - e.g., stigmata of liver disease, heart failure, or kidney disease.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Hyponatraemia/ hypernatraemia"][/sc]
  • Severe neurological symptoms - seizures, coma, or signs of cerebral herniation, indicating critical hyponatraemia or hypernatraemia.
  • Rapid onset of symptoms - suggests acute electrolyte disturbance, which carries a higher risk of severe complications.
  • Extreme sodium levels - serum sodium <120 mmol/L (hyponatraemia) or >160 mmol/L (hypernatraemia) are associated with high mortality and morbidity.
  • Haemodynamic instability - severe hypotension or shock, requiring immediate fluid resuscitation and correction of electrolyte imbalance.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Hyponatraemia/ hypernatraemia"][/sc]
  • Older adults: May present with subtle or non-specific symptoms like falls, confusion, or gait disturbance, making diagnosis challenging.
  • Psychiatric patients: Polydipsia can lead to dilutional hyponatraemia, often presenting with psychiatric symptom exacerbation.
  • Post-operative patients: Often receive hypotonic fluids, increasing the risk of hyponatraemia, particularly in women and children.
  • Diabetic ketoacidosis (DKA): Hyperglycaemia can cause factitious hyponatraemia due to osmotic shift of water from cells to extracellular fluid.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Hyponatraemia/ hypernatraemia"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected hyponatraemia/hypernatraemia: Serum electrolytes (sodium, potassium, urea, creatinine), serum osmolality, urine electrolytes (sodium, potassium), urine osmolality.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Thyroid function tests, adrenal function tests (short synacthen test), liver function tests, glucose, lipid profile.
  • Imaging - CT head: Consider in severe neurological symptoms (seizures, coma) to rule out cerebral oedema or other intracranial pathology.
  • Serial reassessment: Regular monitoring of serum sodium, fluid balance, and clinical status is crucial during management.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Hyponatraemia/ hypernatraemia"][/sc]
  • A 78-year-old female presents to the emergency department with a 3-day history of increasing confusion, lethargy, and a recent fall at home. She has a history of heart failure and is on a thiazide diuretic. On examination, she is disorientated to time and place, with dry mucous membranes and reduced skin turgor.
  • An elderly female with chronic heart failure on diuretics presenting with acute-on-chronic neurological decline and signs of hypovolaemia, suggestive of an electrolyte disturbance.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Hyponatraemia/ hypernatraemia"][/sc]
  • **Hyponatraemia:**
  • Syndrome of Inappropriate Antidiuretic Hormone (SIADH) - euvolaemic hyponatraemia with concentrated urine.
  • Hypovolaemic hyponatraemia (e.g., gastrointestinal losses, diuretics) - signs of dehydration, low urine sodium.
  • Hypervolaemic hyponatraemia (e.g., heart failure, cirrhosis) - signs of fluid overload, high urine sodium.
  • Adrenal insufficiency - associated with hypovolaemia and hyperkalaemia.
  • Hypothyroidism - can cause mild hyponatraemia due to impaired water excretion.
  • **Hypernatraemia:**
  • Dehydration (e.g., inadequate water intake, excessive insensible losses) - signs of hypovolaemia, concentrated urine.
  • Diabetes insipidus (central or nephrogenic) - polyuria, polydipsia, dilute urine.
  • Osmotic diuresis (e.g., uncontrolled diabetes, mannitol) - polyuria, elevated glucose or other osmolytes.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Hyponatraemia/ hypernatraemia"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Address life-threatening symptoms (seizures, coma) with cautious correction of sodium, typically with hypertonic saline for severe symptomatic hyponatraemia. For hypernatraemia, administer intravenous fluids to correct water deficit.
  • Definitive management: Treat the underlying cause. For hyponatraemia, this may involve fluid restriction, discontinuation of offending medications, or specific therapies for SIADH. For hypernatraemia, this involves replacing free water deficit, often orally or via nasogastric tube, or intravenously with 5% dextrose.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Close monitoring of serum sodium, fluid balance, and neurological status is essential to prevent overcorrection or undercorrection, which can lead to severe complications.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Refer to endocrinology or nephrology for complex cases or those not responding to initial management. Educate patients on symptoms of recurrence and when to seek medical attention.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Hyponatraemia/ hypernatraemia"][/sc]
  • Cerebral oedema (Hyponatraemia): Occurs due to rapid water shift into brain cells, leading to increased intracranial pressure, seizures, and potentially brain herniation.
  • Osmotic demyelination syndrome (Hyponatraemia): A severe neurological complication resulting from overly rapid correction of chronic hyponatraemia, causing demyelination in the brainstem.
  • Cerebral shrinkage/intracranial haemorrhage (Hypernatraemia): Occurs due to rapid water shift out of brain cells, leading to brain shrinkage and potential tearing of bridging veins.
  • Seizures and coma (Both): Severe electrolyte disturbances can lead to profound neurological dysfunction, including seizures and loss of consciousness.
  • Increased mortality (Both): Both hyponatraemia and hypernatraemia are independently associated with increased morbidity and mortality, particularly in hospitalised patients.
