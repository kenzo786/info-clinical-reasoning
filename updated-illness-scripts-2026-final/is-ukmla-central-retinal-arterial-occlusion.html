Central retinal arterial occlusion [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Central retinal arterial occlusion"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Central retinal arterial occlusion"][/sc]
  • A sudden, painless, and severe loss of vision in one eye due to an obstruction of the central retinal artery.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the acute presentation of central retinal arterial occlusion and initiate appropriate emergency management.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Central retinal arterial occlusion"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Central retinal arterial occlusion"][/sc]
  • Central retinal arterial occlusion (CRAO) has an estimated annual incidence of 1-2 per 100,000 population in the UK, predominantly affecting individuals over 60 years of age [1].
  • Risk factors include systemic atherosclerosis, hypertension, diabetes mellitus, hyperlipidaemia, smoking, and cardiac emboli (e.g., from atrial fibrillation or carotid artery disease).
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Central retinal arterial occlusion"][/sc]
  • Embolic occlusion of the central retinal artery, often originating from carotid atheroma or cardiac sources, leads to acute cessation of blood flow to the inner retina.
  • Retinal ischaemia rapidly ensues, causing metabolic disruption and irreversible damage to retinal ganglion cells and nerve fibres within minutes to hours.
  • Consequence is profound and typically permanent monocular vision loss due to widespread retinal infarction.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Central retinal arterial occlusion"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Sudden, painless, and complete or near-complete monocular vision loss, often noticed upon waking or within seconds.
  • Progressive phase: Retinal ischaemia progresses rapidly, with irreversible damage occurring within 90-120 minutes, particularly beyond 4-6 hours.
  • Late phase: Permanent visual impairment is common, with potential for neovascularisation and glaucoma if not managed acutely.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Central retinal arterial occlusion"][/sc]
  • Ask about sudden, painless vision loss - this is the cardinal symptom and helps differentiate from painful ocular conditions.
  • Ask about monocular involvement - typically affects one eye, distinguishing it from bilateral neurological causes of vision loss.
  • Ask about duration and onset - sudden onset is characteristic, indicating an acute vascular event.
  • Ask about preceding transient visual obscurations (amaurosis fugax) - may suggest carotid artery disease as an embolic source.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Central retinal arterial occlusion"][/sc]
  • Check visual acuity - typically severely reduced to counting fingers or hand movements in the affected eye.
  • Check pupillary reflexes - a relative afferent pupillary defect (RAPD) is usually present in the affected eye.
  • Check fundoscopy - classic findings include a pale, oedematous retina with a cherry-red spot at the fovea, and attenuated retinal arterioles.
  • Check vital signs - assess for hypertension, arrhythmias (e.g., atrial fibrillation), and signs of systemic vascular disease.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Central retinal arterial occlusion"][/sc]
  • Sudden, profound, painless monocular vision loss: Indicates an acute vascular emergency requiring immediate ophthalmological assessment.
  • Cherry-red spot on fundoscopy: Pathognomonic sign of CRAO, confirming the diagnosis and urgency.
  • Associated neurological symptoms (e.g., transient ischaemic attack): Suggests a common embolic source (e.g., carotid artery disease) and increased risk of stroke.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Central retinal arterial occlusion"][/sc]
  • Younger patients: Consider hypercoagulable states, vasculitis, or cardiac anomalies (e.g., patent foramen ovale) as potential causes.
  • Giant cell arteritis: May present with CRAO, often accompanied by headache, jaw claudication, and scalp tenderness.
  • Incomplete occlusion: May present with less severe vision loss or altitudinal field defects, potentially delaying diagnosis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Central retinal arterial occlusion"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected central retinal arterial occlusion: Urgent fundoscopy is essential to identify the characteristic cherry-red spot and retinal oedema.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Consider fluorescein angiography to confirm retinal artery occlusion and assess retinal perfusion.
  • Imaging - carotid Doppler ultrasound and echocardiogram: To investigate for embolic sources in the carotid arteries and heart.
  • Serial reassessment: Regular monitoring of intraocular pressure is important due to the risk of neovascular glaucoma.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Central retinal arterial occlusion"][/sc]
  • A 68-year-old male with a history of hypertension and smoking presents with sudden, painless loss of vision in his right eye, noticed upon waking this morning. On examination, visual acuity is reduced to hand movements, and fundoscopy reveals a pale retina with a cherry-red spot at the fovea.
  • An elderly patient with cardiovascular risk factors presenting with acute, profound, painless monocular vision loss and a relative afferent pupillary defect, with fundoscopic findings of retinal ischaemia, is highly suggestive of central retinal arterial occlusion.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Central retinal arterial occlusion"][/sc]
  • Retinal detachment - often preceded by floaters and flashes of light, with a visual field defect described as a curtain or shadow.
  • Vitreous haemorrhage - may cause sudden vision loss, but fundoscopy shows blood in the vitreous cavity, obscuring retinal details.
  • Optic neuritis - typically affects younger patients, associated with pain on eye movement and often improves with time.
  • Giant cell arteritis - can cause CRAO but usually has associated systemic symptoms like headache, jaw claudication, and scalp tenderness.
  • Branch retinal artery occlusion - presents with a sectoral visual field defect rather than complete vision loss.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Central retinal arterial occlusion"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Urgent referral to an ophthalmologist is crucial. Ocular massage and measures to lower intraocular pressure (e.g., anterior chamber paracentesis) may be attempted to dislodge the embolus, but evidence for their effectiveness is limited.
  • Definitive management: No treatment has been proven to reliably restore vision. Management focuses on identifying and managing the underlying cause to reduce the risk of further vascular events.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Regular ophthalmological review is necessary to monitor for complications such as neovascular glaucoma.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Patients should be advised to seek immediate medical attention if they experience any new neurological symptoms, as they are at high risk of stroke.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Central retinal arterial occlusion"][/sc]
  • Permanent severe vision loss: The most common and devastating complication, resulting from irreversible retinal damage.
  • Neovascular glaucoma: A painful and difficult-to-treat form of glaucoma that can develop weeks to months after the initial event due to retinal ischaemia.
  • Stroke: Patients with CRAO are at significantly increased risk of ischaemic stroke, particularly in the weeks following the ocular event.
  • Amaurosis fugax: Transient monocular vision loss may occur in the contralateral eye, indicating ongoing embolic activity.
