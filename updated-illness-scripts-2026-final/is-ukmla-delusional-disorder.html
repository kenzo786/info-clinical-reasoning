Delusional disorder [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Delusional disorder"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Delusional disorder"][/sc]
  • A delusional disorder is a mental health condition characterised by the presence of one or more non-bizarre delusions that persist for at least one month, without other prominent psychotic symptoms.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the core features of delusional disorder, differentiate it from other psychotic disorders, and understand its impact on a patient's life.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Delusional disorder"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Delusional disorder"][/sc]
  • The lifetime prevalence of delusional disorder is estimated to be around 0.02%, making it considerably rarer than other psychotic disorders. In the UK, a broader category of psychotic disorder has an estimated past-year prevalence of 0.7% in adults aged 16 and over in England.
  • Risk factors include a family history of psychotic disorders, significant psychosocial stress, and substance use disorders. Older age may also be a contributing factor.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Delusional disorder"][/sc]
  • Trigger: While the exact aetiology is unclear, a combination of genetic predisposition, neurobiological factors (e.g., limbic system and basal ganglia involvement), and environmental stressors (e.g., significant psychosocial stress) are thought to contribute to the onset.
  • Progression: The disorder is characterised by the development of fixed, false beliefs (delusions) that are non-bizarre and often plausible, leading to a misinterpretation of reality. These delusions persist despite contradictory evidence.
  • Consequence: The sustained delusional beliefs can lead to significant distress, functional impairment in social and occupational settings, and potential legal or interpersonal difficulties due to actions driven by the delusions.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Delusional disorder"][/sc]
  • Early phase: The onset of delusional disorder is often insidious, with the development of a single, non-bizarre delusion that persists for at least one month. The individual's functioning is typically not markedly impaired, and behaviour is not obviously odd or bizarre, apart from the direct impact of the delusion.
  • Progressive phase: If untreated, the delusion can become more entrenched and elaborate, potentially leading to increasing preoccupation and a gradual impact on social, occupational, and interpersonal functioning, particularly beyond several months to a year.
  • Late phase: In the late phase, the disorder often becomes chronic, with persistent delusions that may be resistant to treatment. This can lead to significant social isolation, legal issues, or even violence in rare cases, especially if the delusions are persecutory or jealous in nature.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Delusional disorder"][/sc]
  • Ask about the nature of the delusion - to understand its content (e.g., persecutory, grandiose, jealous, somatic, erotomanic) and assess its impact on daily life.
  • Ask about the duration and stability of the delusion - to differentiate from transient psychotic symptoms or other mental health conditions.
  • Ask about any associated distress or functional impairment - as while delusions are the primary feature, their impact on the individual's well-being and ability to function is crucial for diagnosis and management.
  • Ask about any behavioural changes related to the delusion - to identify potential risks to self or others, or significant disruptions in social or occupational spheres.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Delusional disorder"][/sc]
  • Check general appearance and behaviour - to observe for any unusual or guarded behaviour, social withdrawal, or signs of agitation that might be related to the delusion.
  • Check affect and mood - to assess for irritability, suspiciousness, anxiety, or low mood, which are often secondary to the delusional beliefs.
  • Check thought content during mental state examination - to identify the presence, nature, and conviction of the delusional beliefs, ensuring they are non-bizarre and fixed.
  • Check vital signs - to rule out any underlying physical health issues that might mimic or exacerbate psychiatric symptoms, and to establish a baseline, especially if medication is considered.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Delusional disorder"][/sc]
  • Risk of harm to self or others: Delusions, particularly persecutory or jealous types, can lead to aggressive or violent behaviour if the individual perceives a threat or injustice.
  • Emergence of other psychotic symptoms: The development of prominent hallucinations, disorganised speech, or negative symptoms suggests a diagnosis other than pure delusional disorder, such as schizophrenia or schizoaffective disorder.
  • Severe functional impairment: While typically less impairing, significant deterioration in occupational, social, or personal functioning due to the delusion warrants urgent assessment and intervention.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Delusional disorder"][/sc]
  • Older adults: Delusional disorder may be more prevalent in older age, particularly in women. Delusions in this population can sometimes be mistaken for cognitive decline or dementia, especially if the content of the delusion is related to memory or theft. It is crucial to differentiate from paranoia associated with dementia.
  • Pregnancy/Postpartum: While rare, somatic delusions such as delusion of pregnancy (pseudocyesis) can occur, where a woman has a fixed, false belief that she is pregnant despite medical evidence to the contrary. This requires careful assessment to distinguish from other psychiatric conditions.
  • Immunosuppressed individuals: While not directly linked to delusional disorder, certain medical conditions or treatments causing immunosuppression can lead to organic brain syndromes or infections that may present with psychotic symptoms, including delusions. A thorough medical workup is essential to rule out secondary causes.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Delusional disorder"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected Delusional disorder: A comprehensive psychiatric evaluation, including a detailed history of the presenting symptoms, mental state examination, and collateral history from family or carers (with patient consent). This aims to establish the presence, duration, and nature of delusions, and to rule out other psychotic symptoms.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Blood tests (e.g., full blood count, electrolytes, liver and renal function, thyroid function tests, B12/folate, inflammatory markers, toxicology screen) to exclude medical conditions, substance misuse, or medication side effects that can mimic psychotic symptoms.
  • Imaging - Brain MRI/CT: May be considered to rule out organic brain pathology (e.g., tumours, infections, cerebrovascular disease, neurodegenerative conditions) if there are atypical neurological signs or symptoms, or an unusual presentation for delusional disorder.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Delusional disorder"][/sc]
  • A 55-year-old man presents with a 9-month history of a fixed belief that his neighbours are actively trying to poison him through the ventilation system, despite no objective evidence. He has become increasingly isolated, frequently checks his air vents, and has reported his neighbours to the police multiple times. His wife reports he is otherwise functioning well at work, and denies hallucinations or disorganised thought.
  • A middle-aged male with a chronic, encapsulated, non-bizarre delusional system, leading to social withdrawal and preoccupation, but without other overt psychotic features or significant global functional decline.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Delusional disorder"][/sc]
  • Schizophrenia: Distinguished by the presence of bizarre delusions, prominent hallucinations, disorganised thought/speech, and significant functional impairment, which are typically absent or less pronounced in delusional disorder.
  • Mood disorders with psychotic features: Delusions occur exclusively during a mood episode (depressive or manic) and are typically mood-congruent. In delusional disorder, mood symptoms are usually secondary to the delusion or absent.
  • Obsessive-Compulsive Disorder (OCD): Characterised by obsessions and compulsions, where the individual often has some insight into the irrationality of their thoughts, unlike the fixed, unshakeable beliefs in delusional disorder.
  • Substance-induced psychotic disorder: Delusions are directly attributable to the physiological effects of a substance, and typically resolve with substance cessation.
  • Psychotic disorder due to another medical condition: Delusions are a direct physiological consequence of a general medical condition, requiring a thorough medical workup to identify.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Delusional disorder"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Establish a therapeutic alliance and ensure safety. Assess for risk of harm to self or others, especially if the delusions involve violence or self-neglect. Address any acute distress or agitation with appropriate de-escalation techniques.
  • Definitive management: Antipsychotic medication is the mainstay of treatment, with atypical antipsychotics often preferred due to a more favourable side-effect profile. Psychological interventions, particularly cognitive behavioural therapy (CBT) adapted for psychosis, can help individuals cope with the distress caused by delusions and improve social functioning. Family interventions can also be beneficial.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Regular clinical reviews to assess symptom response, monitor for side effects of medication, and evaluate functional status. Long-term follow-up is often required due to the chronic nature of the disorder. Encourage adherence to treatment and engagement with support services.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: If there is an escalation of risk (e.g., increased aggression, self-harm ideation) or worsening of symptoms despite treatment, consider inpatient admission for intensive management. Safety-netting involves providing clear advice on when and how to seek urgent help, and involving carers in the safety plan.Exam pharmacology reference (UKMLA/MRCGP): Antipsychotics are the primary pharmacological intervention. Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Delusional disorder"][/sc]
  • Social isolation and relationship breakdown: The fixed, unshakeable nature of delusions often leads to suspicion, mistrust, and conflict with family, friends, and colleagues, resulting in significant social withdrawal and isolation. This can further entrench the delusions and worsen prognosis.
  • Occupational and financial difficulties: Preoccupation with delusions can impair work performance, lead to job loss, and create financial instability, significantly impacting the individual's quality of life and independence.
  • Legal problems: Particularly with persecutory or jealous delusions, individuals may engage in behaviours (e.g., harassment, false accusations) that lead to legal intervention, arrests, or incarceration, which can have severe long-term consequences.
  • Secondary mood disorders: The chronic stress, social isolation, and functional impairment associated with delusional disorder can lead to the development of depression, anxiety disorders, or substance misuse, further complicating management and impacting overall prognosis.
  • Risk of harm: In rare cases, especially with persecutory delusions, individuals may act on their beliefs, leading to aggression or violence towards perceived threats. There is also an increased risk of self-harm due to the distress caused by the delusions.
