Pyrexia of unknown origin (Infections) [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Pyrexia of unknown origin (Infections)"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Pyrexia of unknown origin (Infections)"][/sc]
  • Pyrexia of unknown origin (PUO) is defined as a temperature greater than 38.3°C on multiple occasions, lasting for more than three weeks, with no diagnosis reached after one week of inpatient investigation [1].
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the criteria for pyrexia of unknown origin and initiate appropriate broad diagnostic investigations to identify underlying infectious, inflammatory, or neoplastic causes.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Pyrexia of unknown origin (Infections)"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Pyrexia of unknown origin (Infections)"][/sc]
  • In adults, infections and malignancy each account for 25-40% of pyrexia of unknown origin (PUO) cases, with autoimmune disorders contributing 10-20% [1].
  • In children, infectious diseases are the leading cause (37.6%), followed by malignancy (17.2%) and collagen vascular diseases (14.0%) [1].
  • Risk factors for PUO include immunosuppression, recent travel, animal exposure, and certain occupational hazards, which guide diagnostic pathways [1].
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Pyrexia of unknown origin (Infections)"][/sc]
  • Trigger: An underlying disease process (infection, malignancy, or autoimmune disease) activates the host's immune system, leading to a sustained inflammatory response.
  • Progression: Activated macrophages and other immune cells release endogenous pyrogens, such as [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Pyrogenic cytokines: what are they and why do they matter clinically?" text="pyrogenic cytokines"] [/sc] (e.g., IL-1, IL-6, TNF-α), which cross the blood-brain barrier and act on the [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Hypothalamic thermoregulation: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="hypothalamic thermoregulatory centre"] [/sc].
  • Consequence: The hypothalamus increases the body's thermoregulatory set point, causing a persistent fever that does not resolve until the underlying cause is identified and treated.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Pyrexia of unknown origin (Infections)"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Initial presentation of fever (>38.3°C) on multiple occasions over several days, often accompanied by non-specific symptoms such as malaise, fatigue, and myalgia.
  • Progressive phase: Fever persists for more than three weeks without a clear diagnosis despite initial investigations, indicating the need for more extensive diagnostic work-up.
  • Late phase: Beyond one week of inpatient investigation, the fever remains unexplained, necessitating a re-evaluation of the diagnostic approach and consideration of less common etiologies.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Pyrexia of unknown origin (Infections)"][/sc]
  • Ask about night sweats - often indicative of chronic infection (e.g., tuberculosis) or malignancy (e.g., lymphoma).
  • Ask about unexplained weight loss - a non-specific but significant symptom that can point towards chronic inflammatory conditions, infections, or malignancy.
  • Ask about localised pain or swelling - may suggest an occult abscess, osteomyelitis, or vasculitis, even in the absence of overt signs.
  • Ask about recent travel history or animal exposure - crucial for identifying exotic infections (e.g., malaria, brucellosis) that may not be common in the UK.
  • Ask about drug history - certain medications can cause drug-induced fever, which can mimic PUO.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Pyrexia of unknown origin (Infections)"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - persistent fever (>38.3°C), tachycardia, and tachypnoea are expected; hypotension may indicate sepsis.
  • Check for lymphadenopathy - generalised or localised lymph node enlargement can indicate infection (e.g., HIV, TB), malignancy (e.g., lymphoma), or autoimmune conditions.
  • Check for hepatosplenomegaly - may suggest systemic infection, haematological malignancy, or chronic liver disease.
  • Check for new murmurs or signs of embolic phenomena - indicative of [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Infective endocarditis: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="infective endocarditis"] [/sc], a critical cause of PUO.
  • Check for rashes or skin lesions - can be a clue to drug reactions, vasculitis, or specific infections.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Pyrexia of unknown origin (Infections)"][/sc]
  • Haemodynamic instability: Persistent hypotension or signs of shock indicate severe sepsis or other critical illness requiring immediate resuscitation and investigation.
  • New neurological deficit: Sudden onset of focal neurological signs, seizures, or altered mental status suggests central nervous system infection (e.g., meningitis, encephalitis) or vasculitis.
  • Unexplained bleeding or bruising: May indicate haematological malignancy, severe infection with coagulopathy, or vasculitis, particularly in the progressive phase of PUO.
  • Rapidly worsening symptoms: A swift deterioration in clinical status, especially beyond one week of investigation, necessitates urgent re-evaluation for aggressive infections or malignancies.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Pyrexia of unknown origin (Infections)"][/sc]
  • Older adults: May present with subtle or non-specific symptoms, often lacking prominent fever, making diagnosis challenging; consider conditions like giant cell arteritis or polymyalgia rheumatica.
  • Immunosuppressed patients: Broadened differential diagnosis including opportunistic infections (e.g., fungal, viral, parasitic) and drug-induced fevers; [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Neutropenic fever: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="neutropenic fever"] [/sc] requires urgent management.
  • HIV-associated PUO: Etiology varies with CD4 count; opportunistic infections are common at lower CD4 counts, while lymphomas and drug reactions are more prevalent at higher counts.
  • Post-operative patients: Consider healthcare-associated infections, deep vein thrombosis, pulmonary embolism, or drug fever as causes of PUO.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Pyrexia of unknown origin (Infections)"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected PUO: Full blood count, ESR, CRP, liver function tests, renal function tests, urinalysis, blood cultures (multiple sets), HIV serology, and chest X-ray.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Consider serology for specific infections (e.g., CMV, EBV, toxoplasmosis, brucellosis), autoimmune screen (ANA, RF, ANCA), and protein electrophoresis.
  • Imaging - CT: CT chest, abdomen, and pelvis is often a first-line imaging modality to identify occult infections, lymphadenopathy, or malignancy.
  • Imaging - PET-CT: [sc name="jitl-query" Q="PET-CT in PUO: what is its role and why does it matter clinically?" text="PET-CT"] [/sc] is a valuable tool in localising inflammation or malignancy when initial investigations are inconclusive, particularly in cases of prolonged PUO [12, 13, 14].
  • Serial reassessment: Regular clinical review and repeat inflammatory markers are essential to monitor disease progression and guide further investigations.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Pyrexia of unknown origin (Infections)"][/sc]
  • A 65-year-old male presents with a three-week history of daily fevers exceeding 38.5°C, associated with night sweats and a 5kg unintentional weight loss, despite initial outpatient investigations being inconclusive.
  • Elderly male with prolonged, unexplained fever and constitutional symptoms, concerning for occult infection, malignancy, or autoimmune disease.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Pyrexia of unknown origin (Infections)"][/sc]
  • Tuberculosis: Often presents with constitutional symptoms (night sweats, weight loss) and can have varied organ involvement, making it difficult to diagnose without specific microbiological or histological confirmation.
  • Infective Endocarditis: Characterised by persistent fever, new or changing heart murmurs, and embolic phenomena; often requires multiple blood cultures and echocardiography for diagnosis.
  • Lymphoma: Can cause prolonged fever, night sweats, and weight loss, often with lymphadenopathy or hepatosplenomegaly; diagnosis relies on biopsy.
  • Giant Cell Arteritis/Polymyalgia Rheumatica: Typically affects older adults, presenting with headache, jaw claudication, visual disturbances (GCA) or proximal muscle pain/stiffness (PMR), along with elevated inflammatory markers.
  • Systemic Lupus Erythematosus (SLE): A multi-system autoimmune disease with diverse manifestations including fever, arthralgia, rash, and serological abnormalities (e.g., positive ANA).
  • Drug-induced fever: A diagnosis of exclusion, where fever resolves rapidly upon withdrawal of the offending medication; often accompanied by a rash.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Pyrexia of unknown origin (Infections)"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Recognition of potential severity, escalation to senior medical staff, and initiation of supportive care, including fluid management and antipyretics, while deferring to local pathways for critical care.
  • Definitive management: Primarily involves identifying and treating the underlying cause; empirical antimicrobial or corticosteroid therapy is generally not recommended unless there is strong clinical suspicion of a specific treatable condition or the patient is critically unwell or immunocompromised.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Close monitoring of vital signs, inflammatory markers, and clinical progression is essential; ongoing review of investigations and consideration of further specialist input may be warranted.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Clear communication with patients regarding warning signs and when to seek further medical attention; prompt re-evaluation if clinical condition deteriorates or new symptoms emerge.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Pyrexia of unknown origin (Infections)"][/sc]
  • Organ damage: Prolonged, uncontrolled fever, particularly in the progressive and late phases, can lead to direct thermal injury or immune-mediated damage to vital organs such as the brain, kidneys, and liver, impacting long-term prognosis.
  • Sepsis and septic shock: If the underlying cause of PUO is an undiagnosed severe infection, it can progress to sepsis, characterised by life-threatening organ dysfunction, and potentially septic shock, requiring urgent critical care intervention.
  • Disseminated intravascular coagulation (DIC): Severe infections or malignancies causing PUO can trigger widespread activation of coagulation, leading to both thrombosis and haemorrhage, significantly increasing morbidity and mortality.
  • Functional decline: Chronic illness associated with PUO, especially if diagnosis is delayed beyond one week of inpatient investigation, can result in significant deconditioning, muscle wasting, and impaired functional status, particularly in older or frail patients.
  • Psychological distress: The uncertainty and prolonged diagnostic process associated with PUO can lead to significant anxiety, depression, and reduced quality of life for patients and their families.
