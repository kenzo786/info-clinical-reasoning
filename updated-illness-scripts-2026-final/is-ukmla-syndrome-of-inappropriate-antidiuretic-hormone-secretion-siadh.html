Syndrome of inappropriate antidiuretic hormone secretion (SIADH) [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Syndrome of inappropriate antidiuretic hormone secretion (SIADH)"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Syndrome of inappropriate antidiuretic hormone secretion (SIADH)"][/sc]
  • SIADH is a condition characterised by the excessive release of antidiuretic hormone (ADH) from the posterior pituitary or ectopic sources, leading to impaired free water excretion, water retention, and dilutional hyponatraemia despite normal or increased plasma volume.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical features, common causes, and initial management of SIADH.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Syndrome of inappropriate antidiuretic hormone secretion (SIADH)"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Syndrome of inappropriate antidiuretic hormone secretion (SIADH)"][/sc]
  • Hyponatraemia (serum sodium <135 mmol/L) is common, affecting up to 30% of hospitalised patients in the UK, with SIADH being the most frequent cause of euvolaemic hyponatraemia in this setting.
  • Risk factors include age >50 years, pulmonary conditions (e.g., pneumonia, tuberculosis), malignancy (especially small cell lung cancer), central nervous system disorders (e.g., stroke, haemorrhage), postoperative state, and certain medications (e.g., antidepressants, anticonvulsants).
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Syndrome of inappropriate antidiuretic hormone secretion (SIADH)"][/sc]
  • Trigger: Inappropriate, non-osmotic release of ADH (vasopressin) from the posterior pituitary or ectopic sources (e.g., malignancy, CNS disorders, pulmonary disease, drugs).
  • Progression: Excessive ADH acts on V2 receptors in the renal collecting ducts, increasing water reabsorption and leading to impaired free water excretion and water retention. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="V2 receptors: what are they and why do they matter clinically?" text="V2 receptors"]
  • Consequence: Increased total body water dilutes serum sodium, resulting in euvolaemic hypotonic hyponatraemia. This can lead to intracellular fluid shifts and cerebral oedema, particularly if hyponatraemia develops rapidly.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Syndrome of inappropriate antidiuretic hormone secretion (SIADH)"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Symptoms typically develop gradually over days to weeks, often subtle and non-specific, as hyponatraemia progresses. Acute onset can occur with rapid fluid shifts or severe underlying causes.
  • Progressive phase: As hyponatraemia worsens, neurological symptoms such as headache, confusion, and gait disturbance become more prominent, particularly beyond 48-72 hours.
  • Late phase: Severe, rapidly developing hyponatraemia can lead to seizures, coma, respiratory arrest, and brain herniation, particularly beyond 72 hours if untreated.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Syndrome of inappropriate antidiuretic hormone secretion (SIADH)"][/sc]
  • Ask about nausea and vomiting - these are common early non-specific symptoms that can indicate worsening hyponatraemia.
  • Ask about headache - a frequent symptom of cerebral oedema due to osmotic shifts.
  • Ask about muscle cramps or weakness - often due to electrolyte imbalance affecting neuromuscular function.
  • Ask about confusion, memory problems, or personality changes - these mental status changes are critical indicators of neurological compromise.
  • Ask about problems with balance or falls - reflecting neurological impairment and increased risk of injury.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Syndrome of inappropriate antidiuretic hormone secretion (SIADH)"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - assess for euvolaemia (normal blood pressure, heart rate) and rule out hypovolaemia or hypervolaemia. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Euvolaemia: what is it and why does it matter in SIADH?" text="Euvolaemia"]
  • Check neurological status - assess for altered mental state, disorientation, or focal neurological deficits.
  • Check for signs of fluid overload - absence of peripheral oedema, ascites, or pulmonary oedema is characteristic of euvolaemic hyponatraemia.
  • Check for muscle fasciculations or tremors - may indicate severe electrolyte disturbance.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Syndrome of inappropriate antidiuretic hormone secretion (SIADH)"][/sc]
  • Acute onset severe hyponatraemia: Rapid decline in serum sodium (<120 mmol/L within 48 hours) significantly increases risk of cerebral oedema.
  • Seizures or coma: Indicate severe neurological compromise requiring immediate intervention.
  • Respiratory arrest: A life-threatening complication of severe cerebral oedema.
  • Brain herniation: The most severe and often fatal complication of untreated cerebral oedema.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Syndrome of inappropriate antidiuretic hormone secretion (SIADH)"][/sc]
  • Older adults: May present with more subtle cognitive impairment, gait disturbance, or falls, often attributed to age-related changes, delaying diagnosis. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Hyponatraemia in older adults: why is it often atypical?" text="Hyponatraemia in older adults"]
  • Postoperative patients: SIADH can be triggered by stress, pain, and administration of hypotonic fluids, often presenting with non-specific symptoms in a complex clinical picture.
  • Psychiatric patients on psychotropic medications: Certain antidepressants and antipsychotics can induce SIADH, making it challenging to differentiate from underlying psychiatric symptoms.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Syndrome of inappropriate antidiuretic hormone secretion (SIADH)"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected SIADH: Serum electrolytes (sodium, potassium, urea, creatinine), serum osmolality, urine electrolytes (sodium, potassium), urine osmolality. These confirm hypotonic hyponatraemia with inappropriately concentrated urine and elevated urine sodium.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Thyroid function tests (TSH, free T4) to rule out hypothyroidism; morning cortisol to rule out adrenal insufficiency.
  • Imaging - Chest X-ray/CT: To investigate underlying pulmonary pathology (e.g., pneumonia, malignancy). Imaging - Brain CT/MRI: To investigate CNS pathology (e.g., stroke, tumour, infection).
  • Serial reassessment: Regular monitoring of serum sodium and clinical status is crucial during management.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Syndrome of inappropriate antidiuretic hormone secretion (SIADH)"][/sc]
  • A 72-year-old female presents with a 3-day history of increasing confusion, headache, and nausea following a recent chest infection. On examination, she is euvolaemic with no signs of dehydration or fluid overload. Her serum sodium is 118 mmol/L.
  • An elderly female with acute-on-chronic confusion and hypotonic euvolaemic hyponatraemia secondary to pulmonary infection, raising suspicion for SIADH.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Syndrome of inappropriate antidiuretic hormone secretion (SIADH)"][/sc]
  • Hypovolaemic hyponatraemia - distinguished by clinical signs of dehydration (e.g., reduced skin turgor, orthostatic hypotension) and low urine sodium if renal cause, or high urine sodium if extra-renal cause.
  • Hypervolaemic hyponatraemia (e.g., heart failure, cirrhosis) - distinguished by clinical signs of fluid overload (e.g., oedema, ascites) and low urine sodium.
  • Adrenal insufficiency - distinguished by hyperkalaemia, hypotension, and often hyperpigmentation; confirmed by low cortisol.
  • Hypothyroidism - distinguished by other features of hypothyroidism (e.g., fatigue, weight gain, bradycardia) and abnormal thyroid function tests.
  • Primary polydipsia - distinguished by very dilute urine (urine osmolality <100 mOsm/kg) and history of excessive fluid intake.
  • Cerebral salt wasting syndrome - distinguished by hypovolaemia and high urine sodium, typically seen in patients with intracranial pathology.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Syndrome of inappropriate antidiuretic hormone secretion (SIADH)"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Address severe symptomatic hyponatraemia (e.g., seizures, coma) with slow, controlled intravenous hypertonic saline to prevent osmotic demyelination syndrome. Fluid restriction is paramount.
  • Definitive management: Treat the underlying cause of SIADH (e.g., discontinue causative medications, treat infection, manage malignancy). Continued fluid restriction is key.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Close monitoring of serum sodium, fluid balance, and neurological status. Gradual correction of hyponatraemia is essential.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Educate patients and carers on symptoms of worsening hyponatraemia and the importance of fluid restriction. Advise immediate medical attention for severe symptoms.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Syndrome of inappropriate antidiuretic hormone secretion (SIADH)"][/sc]
  • Cerebral oedema: Mechanism: Rapidly falling serum sodium causes water to shift into brain cells. Why it matters: Leads to neurological symptoms, seizures, coma, and potentially brain herniation.
  • Osmotic demyelination syndrome (ODS): Mechanism: Too rapid correction of chronic hyponatraemia causes demyelination of pontine and extrapontine neurons. Why it matters: Can result in irreversible neurological deficits (e.g., locked-in syndrome).
  • Seizures: Mechanism: Severe cerebral oedema increases intracranial pressure and disrupts neuronal function. Why it matters: Indicates critical neurological compromise requiring urgent intervention.
  • Respiratory arrest: Mechanism: Brainstem compression due to severe cerebral oedema. Why it matters: A life-threatening emergency requiring ventilatory support.
  • Falls and fractures: Mechanism: Chronic hyponatraemia can cause gait instability, cognitive impairment, and bone demineralisation. Why it matters: Increased morbidity and mortality, particularly in older adults.
