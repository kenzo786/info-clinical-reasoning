Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Interstitial lung disease including fibrotic lung disease"][/sc]
  • Interstitial lung disease (ILD) encompasses a diverse group of chronic lung disorders characterised by inflammation and/or fibrosis of the lung interstitium, leading to impaired gas exchange.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical features and key investigations for common interstitial lung diseases, including idiopathic pulmonary fibrosis, and understand the principles of their management.

  • The incidence of idiopathic pulmonary fibrosis (IPF), the most common fibrotic ILD, is estimated at 3-9 cases per 100,000 person-years in the UK, predominantly affecting individuals over 50 years old.
  • Hypersensitivity pneumonitis (HP) prevalence varies significantly, with chronic HP estimated at 1-2 per 100,000, often linked to environmental antigen exposure (e.g., bird fancier's lung).
  • Sarcoidosis, which can cause ILD, has an estimated prevalence of 10-20 per 100,000 in the UK, with a higher incidence in individuals of African or Afro-Caribbean descent.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Interstitial lung disease including fibrotic lung disease"][/sc]
  • Trigger: Various triggers, including genetic predisposition, environmental exposures (e.g., asbestos, silica, organic dusts), certain medications, and autoimmune diseases, can initiate chronic inflammation or direct injury to the alveolar epithelium.
  • Progression: Persistent injury and aberrant repair mechanisms lead to fibroblast activation and proliferation, excessive extracellular matrix deposition, and distortion of lung architecture, often manifesting as a [sc name="jitl-query" Q="UIP pattern: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="usual interstitial pneumonia (UIP) pattern" ][/sc] in IPF.
  • Consequence: Progressive fibrosis results in stiffening of the lung parenchyma, reduced lung volumes, impaired gas exchange (leading to hypoxaemia), and ultimately respiratory failure.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Interstitial lung disease including fibrotic lung disease"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Symptoms may be insidious, developing over months to years, often presenting as a persistent dry cough and progressive exertional dyspnoea, which can be initially attributed to other conditions.
  • Progressive phase: The disease typically advances gradually, with increasing dyspnoea, reduced exercise tolerance, and worsening lung function, though acute exacerbations can occur, particularly in IPF.
  • Late phase: Characterised by severe respiratory impairment, chronic hypoxaemia, pulmonary hypertension, and cor pulmonale, significantly impacting quality of life and leading to increased mortality.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Interstitial lung disease including fibrotic lung disease"][/sc]
  • Ask about progressive exertional dyspnoea - this is the most common and often earliest symptom, indicating reduced lung capacity and impaired gas exchange.
  • Ask about persistent dry cough - often non-productive and can be debilitating, sometimes worsening with activity.
  • Ask about fatigue and malaise - common non-specific symptoms reflecting chronic illness and respiratory burden.
  • Ask about chest discomfort or pain - may occur due to pleural involvement or pulmonary hypertension, though less common than dyspnoea.
  • Ask about symptoms of connective tissue disease (e.g., arthralgia, Raynaud's phenomenon, skin changes) - to identify potential underlying systemic causes of ILD.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Interstitial lung disease including fibrotic lung disease"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - may reveal tachypnoea, tachycardia, and oxygen desaturation on exertion or at rest, indicating respiratory compromise.
  • Check fine inspiratory crackles (velcro crackles) at lung bases - a characteristic auscultatory finding, particularly prominent in fibrotic ILDs like IPF, often associated with [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Honeycombing: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="honeycombing" ][/sc] on imaging.
  • Check finger clubbing - a common sign in IPF and other fibrotic ILDs, though non-specific.
  • Check signs of pulmonary hypertension (e.g., loud P2, right ventricular heave, peripheral oedema) - indicating advanced disease and increased pulmonary vascular resistance.
  • Check signs of underlying systemic disease (e.g., skin rashes, joint swelling, muscle weakness) - to identify associated conditions like connective tissue diseases or sarcoidosis.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Interstitial lung disease including fibrotic lung disease"][/sc]
  • Rapidly worsening dyspnoea: Suggests acute exacerbation of ILD, particularly IPF, or a superimposed infection, requiring urgent assessment and management.
  • Significant oxygen desaturation at rest or with minimal exertion: Indicates severe respiratory impairment and potential need for supplemental oxygen or higher level respiratory support.
  • Haemoptysis: While rare, can indicate complications such as pulmonary hypertension, infection, or malignancy, warranting prompt investigation.
  • New or worsening peripheral oedema with elevated JVP: May signify developing or worsening cor pulmonale due to severe pulmonary hypertension.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Interstitial lung disease including fibrotic lung disease"][/sc]
  • Older adults: Symptoms may be misattributed to normal ageing or other comorbidities (e.g., heart failure, COPD), delaying diagnosis.
  • Early disease: Patients may be asymptomatic or have very mild symptoms, with ILD only detected incidentally on imaging performed for other reasons.
  • Connective tissue disease-associated ILD (CTD-ILD): Pulmonary symptoms may precede or occur concurrently with systemic features of the underlying CTD, requiring a high index of suspicion.
  • Acute exacerbation of IPF: Can present with sudden, severe respiratory deterioration, mimicking pneumonia or acute respiratory distress syndrome, often without clear precipitating factors.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Interstitial lung disease including fibrotic lung disease"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected ILD: Full blood count, renal and liver function tests, inflammatory markers (CRP, ESR), autoantibody screen (ANA, ENA, RF, anti-CCP) to screen for CTD-ILD, and HIV serology if risk factors present.
  • Severity/risk tools: The GAP (Gender, Age, Physiology) index for IPF can predict mortality, available on GAP Index for IPF on MDCalc.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Consider sputum culture, echocardiogram (to assess for pulmonary hypertension or cardiac dysfunction), and alpha-1 antitrypsin levels if emphysema is prominent.
  • Imaging - High-resolution computed tomography (HRCT) of the chest: Essential for diagnosis, characterising the pattern of ILD (e.g., UIP, NSIP), assessing extent of fibrosis, and guiding further management.
  • Imaging - Echocardiogram: To assess for pulmonary hypertension and right ventricular function, which can be a complication of advanced ILD.
  • Serial reassessment: Regular pulmonary function tests (spirometry, lung volumes, gas transfer), 6-minute walk test, and symptom questionnaires to monitor disease progression and treatment response.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Interstitial lung disease including fibrotic lung disease"][/sc]
  • Chronic Obstructive Pulmonary Disease (COPD) - typically presents with productive cough, smoking history, and obstructive pattern on spirometry, though can co-exist.
  • Heart Failure - often associated with orthopnoea, paroxysmal nocturnal dyspnoea, and peripheral oedema, with cardiomegaly on chest X-ray and elevated BNP.
  • Bronchiectasis - characterised by chronic productive cough, recurrent infections, and dilated airways on HRCT.
  • Pulmonary Embolism - acute onset dyspnoea, pleuritic chest pain, and hypoxaemia, often with risk factors for VTE.
  • Lung Cancer - can present with cough, dyspnoea, weight loss, and haemoptysis, requiring imaging and biopsy for diagnosis.
  • Gastro-oesophageal Reflux Disease (GORD) - can cause chronic cough and may exacerbate ILD, but typically without the characteristic crackles or fibrotic changes.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Interstitial lung disease including fibrotic lung disease"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Recognition of respiratory distress and escalation to specialist respiratory services; supportive care often includes oxygen therapy to maintain adequate saturation.
  • Definitive management: For IPF, antifibrotic agents such as [sc name="jitl-medication" Q="Pirfenidone use in Interstitial lung disease including fibrotic lung disease" text="pirfenidone"][/sc] or [sc name="jitl-medication" Q="Nintedanib use in Interstitial lung disease including fibrotic lung disease" text="nintedanib"][/sc] may slow disease progression. For other ILDs, management often involves identifying and removing causative agents (e.g., in HP) or immunosuppression (e.g., in CTD-ILD, sarcoidosis).
  • Adjunctive management: Pulmonary rehabilitation programmes may support exercise tolerance and quality of life.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Regular specialist review, pulmonary function tests, and symptom assessment are typically warranted to monitor disease activity and treatment effectiveness.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Patients should be advised on symptoms of acute exacerbation and when to seek urgent medical attention; advance care planning is an important consideration in progressive fibrotic ILDs.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Interstitial lung disease including fibrotic lung disease"][/sc]
  • Acute exacerbation of ILD: A sudden, clinically significant worsening of respiratory symptoms and lung function, particularly beyond a few days, often requiring hospitalisation and associated with high mortality.
  • Pulmonary hypertension: Develops as a consequence of chronic hypoxaemia and vascular remodelling, leading to increased right ventricular afterload and eventually cor pulmonale.
  • Cor pulmonale: Right-sided heart failure due to chronic pulmonary hypertension, manifesting as peripheral oedema, elevated JVP, and hepatomegaly, typically in the late phase of disease.
  • Respiratory failure: Progressive decline in lung function leads to chronic hypoxaemia and hypercapnia, necessitating long-term oxygen therapy or, in some cases, lung transplantation consideration.
  • Increased risk of lung cancer: Patients with fibrotic ILDs, especially IPF, have a higher incidence of lung malignancy, warranting vigilance for new or changing respiratory symptoms.
