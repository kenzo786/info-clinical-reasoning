Infective endocarditis [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Infective endocarditis"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Infective endocarditis"][/sc]
  • Infective endocarditis is an infection of the endocardium, the inner lining of the heart chambers and valves.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical features of infective endocarditis, initiate appropriate investigations including blood cultures and echocardiography, and understand the principles of prolonged antibiotic therapy and surgical intervention.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Infective endocarditis"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Infective endocarditis"][/sc]
  • Incidence is approximately 1.7–6.2 cases per 100,000 patient-years, with a mortality rate of around 40%.
  • More common in men (>2:1 ratio), elderly, multimorbid patients, patients with internal cardiac devices, and intravenous drug users.
  • Prosthetic valve endocarditis accounts for 20% of all cases and carries a poorer prognosis.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Infective endocarditis"][/sc]
  • Transient bacteraemia, often from dental procedures or intravenous drug use, introduces pathogens into the bloodstream.
  • Pre-existing valvular damage or prosthetic valves create turbulent flow and exposed endothelium, leading to platelet and fibrin aggregation, forming a non-bacterial thrombotic endocarditis (NBTE).
  • Circulating pathogens adhere to the NBTE, proliferate, and become encased in a fibrin-platelet matrix, forming vegetations that are protected from host immune responses and antibiotics.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Infective endocarditis"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Symptoms may be insidious, developing over days to weeks, often presenting as non-specific malaise, fever, and fatigue.
  • Progressive phase: As vegetations grow and embolise, more specific symptoms and signs emerge, including new murmurs, embolic phenomena, and immune-mediated manifestations, typically over weeks.
  • Late phase: Untreated, the condition progresses to severe valvular damage, heart failure, and systemic complications, particularly beyond 4-6 weeks, leading to significant morbidity and mortality.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Infective endocarditis"][/sc]
  • Ask about fever and chills - common initial symptoms, often intermittent, indicating systemic infection.
  • Ask about malaise and fatigue - non-specific but persistent, reflecting chronic inflammatory state.
  • Ask about new or worsening shortness of breath - suggests developing heart failure due to valvular dysfunction.
  • Ask about unexplained weight loss and night sweats - constitutional symptoms indicating chronic infection.
  • Ask about focal neurological symptoms (e.g., weakness, speech changes) - concerning for embolic stroke.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Infective endocarditis"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - persistent fever, tachycardia, and hypotension may indicate sepsis.
  • Check for new or changing heart murmur - indicative of valvular damage or dysfunction.
  • Check for splinter haemorrhages - non-specific but can suggest microemboli.
  • Check for Osler\'s nodes, Janeway lesions, and Roth spots - classic but rare immune-mediated or embolic phenomena. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Osler nodes, Janeway lesions, Roth spots: what are they and why do they matter clinically?" text="Osler nodes/Janeway lesions/Roth spots"][/sc]
  • Check for clubbing - a late sign of chronic hypoxaemia, though less common now.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Infective endocarditis"][/sc]
  • New onset or worsening heart murmur - suggests acute valvular damage or perforation, requiring urgent cardiac assessment.
  • Signs of heart failure - such as acute pulmonary oedema, indicating severe valvular dysfunction and impending cardiovascular collapse.
  • Embolic phenomena - sudden neurological deficits (stroke), acute limb ischaemia, or splenic/renal infarcts, signifying systemic spread of vegetations.
  • Persistent fever despite antibiotics - may indicate inadequate treatment, resistant organism, or undrained abscess, necessitating re-evaluation of antimicrobial therapy and imaging.
  • Perivalvular extension (e.g., aortic root abscess) - suggested by new conduction abnormalities (e.g., PR prolongation), requiring urgent surgical consideration.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Infective endocarditis"][/sc]
  • Elderly patients: May present with non-specific symptoms like confusion, falls, or general decline, often without fever, making diagnosis challenging.
  • Patients with prosthetic valves: Early prosthetic valve endocarditis (within 1 year of surgery) is often acute and severe, commonly caused by *Staphylococcus aureus* due to perioperative contamination. Late prosthetic valve endocarditis (after 1 year) resembles native valve endocarditis.
  • Intravenous drug users: Often present with right-sided endocarditis (tricuspid valve), commonly caused by *Staphylococcus aureus*, with pulmonary septic emboli being a frequent complication.
  • HACEK organisms: (*Haemophilus*, *Aggregatibacter*, *Cardiobacterium*, *Eikenella*, *Kingella*) are fastidious Gram-negative bacteria that can cause subacute endocarditis, often blood culture-negative, and are associated with large vegetations and embolic events.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Infective endocarditis"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected infective endocarditis:
    • Blood cultures: three sets from different sites, at least 30 minutes apart, before initiating antibiotics.
    • Echocardiography: transthoracic echocardiogram (TTE) as initial imaging; transoesophageal echocardiogram (TOE) for higher sensitivity, especially in prosthetic valves or if TTE is inconclusive.
    • Full blood count (FBC), C-reactive protein (CRP), erythrocyte sedimentation rate (ESR), urea and electrolytes (U&E), liver function tests (LFTs).
  • Severity/risk tools: Diagnosis is based on the modified Duke criteria, which integrates clinical, microbiological, and imaging findings. Duke Criteria for Infective Endocarditis on MDCalc
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Consider other causes of fever of unknown origin or systemic inflammatory response.
  • Imaging - Chest X-ray: To assess for pulmonary oedema or septic emboli.
  • Imaging - CT scan: May be used to identify septic emboli in other organs (e.g., brain, spleen, kidneys). [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Embolic phenomena in infective endocarditis: what are they and why do they matter clinically?" text="Embolic phenomena"][/sc]
  • Serial reassessment: Regular clinical review, repeat blood cultures, and echocardiography as guided by clinical course.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Infective endocarditis"][/sc]
  • A 45-year-old male with a history of intravenous drug use presents with a two-week history of intermittent fevers, malaise, and a new heart murmur, accompanied by splinter haemorrhages.
  • A febrile patient with a new murmur and embolic phenomena, concerning for infective endocarditis.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Infective endocarditis"][/sc]
  • Sepsis of unknown origin - lacks cardiac involvement or embolic phenomena, though can present with similar systemic inflammatory response.
  • Acute rheumatic fever - typically follows streptococcal infection, presents with migratory polyarthritis, carditis, chorea, erythema marginatum, and subcutaneous nodules, often in younger patients.
  • Systemic lupus erythematosus (SLE) - can cause Libman-Sacks endocarditis (non-infective vegetations), but typically associated with other systemic autoimmune features and negative blood cultures.
  • Atrial myxoma - a benign cardiac tumour that can mimic endocarditis with constitutional symptoms and embolic events, but blood cultures are negative and echocardiography reveals a mass rather than vegetations.
  • Vasculitis - can cause systemic inflammation and organ damage, but usually involves small or medium vessels and lacks the characteristic vegetations on heart valves.
  • Malignancy - can cause constitutional symptoms and anaemia, but typically without valvular lesions or positive blood cultures for bacterial pathogens.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Infective endocarditis"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Prompt recognition, urgent referral to cardiology and infectious diseases specialists, and initiation of empirical intravenous antibiotics after blood cultures are taken. Supportive care for complications such as heart failure.
  • Definitive management: Prolonged course (typically 2-6 weeks) of targeted intravenous antibiotics based on culture sensitivities. Surgical intervention (valve repair or replacement) may be required for severe valvular dysfunction, uncontrolled infection, large vegetations, or recurrent emboli.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Serial echocardiography to monitor vegetation size and valvular function, regular blood tests (CRP, FBC) to assess treatment response, and clinical assessment for complications.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Advise patients on symptoms of worsening infection or complications, and provide clear instructions for seeking urgent medical attention.
  • Exam pharmacology reference (UKMLA/MRCGP): Empirical antibiotic regimens often include a combination of agents such as flucloxacillin and gentamicin, or vancomycin for penicillin-allergic patients or those with suspected MRSA. For prosthetic valve endocarditis, regimens may include vancomycin, gentamicin, and rifampicin. Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Infective endocarditis"][/sc]
  • Heart failure: Develops due to severe valvular regurgitation or obstruction, particularly in the progressive and late phases, significantly worsening prognosis.
  • Systemic emboli: Fragments of vegetations can dislodge and travel to various organs (brain, spleen, kidneys, limbs), leading to stroke, organ infarction, or acute limb ischaemia, often occurring in the progressive phase.
  • Mycotic aneurysms: Infection of arterial walls by septic emboli can lead to aneurysm formation, which may rupture, particularly in the late phase if untreated.
  • Perivalvular abscess: Extension of infection beyond the valve annulus into surrounding myocardial tissue, leading to conduction disturbances (e.g., heart block) or fistulae, typically seen in progressive to late phases.
  • Renal complications: Glomerulonephritis due to immune complex deposition or renal infarction from septic emboli, manifesting in the progressive or late phases.
  • Recurrence: Despite successful treatment, patients remain at risk of recurrent infective endocarditis, especially if underlying predisposing factors persist.
