Trigeminal neuralgia [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Trigeminal neuralgia"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Trigeminal neuralgia"][/sc]
  • Trigeminal neuralgia is a chronic pain condition characterised by sudden, severe, short-lived, unilateral, electric shock-like pain in the distribution of one or more branches of the trigeminal nerve (cranial nerve V) [1].
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical features of trigeminal neuralgia, differentiate it from other facial pain syndromes, and outline initial management strategies.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Trigeminal neuralgia"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Trigeminal neuralgia"][/sc]
  • Incidence is estimated to be between 4 and 13 per 100,000 people per year in the UK, with high-quality epidemiological data being scarce [1].
  • Typically affects individuals over 50 years of age, with incidence increasing with age, and is more common in women than men [1].
  • Risk factors include vascular compression of the trigeminal nerve root, and demyelinating conditions such as multiple sclerosis [2].
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Trigeminal neuralgia"][/sc]
  • Most commonly, a blood vessel (e.g., superior cerebellar artery) compresses the trigeminal nerve root as it exits the brainstem, leading to focal demyelination [3].
  • Demyelination results in aberrant nerve impulses, ephaptic transmission (cross-talk between demyelinated axons), and neuronal hyperexcitability within the trigeminal sensory pathways [3].
  • This hyperexcitability manifests as sudden, severe, paroxysmal pain attacks triggered by light touch or movement, characteristic of trigeminal neuralgia. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Microvascular decompression: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Microvascular decompression"][/sc]
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Trigeminal neuralgia"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Initial presentation often involves infrequent, brief, and mild episodes of electric shock-like pain, which may be triggered by specific stimuli.
  • Progressive phase: Over time, the frequency, intensity, and duration of pain attacks typically increase, with pain-free intervals becoming shorter or less predictable.
  • Late phase: In advanced stages, pain may become more constant, with persistent background aching or burning sensations, upon which paroxysmal attacks are superimposed, potentially leading to refractory pain.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Trigeminal neuralgia"][/sc]
  • Ask about sudden, severe, unilateral facial pain - crucial for distinguishing from other facial pain syndromes and identifying the affected trigeminal nerve branch.
  • Ask about electric shock-like, stabbing, or burning quality of pain - characteristic description that helps confirm the diagnosis.
  • Ask about trigger zones - specific areas on the face (e.g., around the nose, lips, or gums) where light touch, chewing, talking, or even a cool breeze can precipitate an attack. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Trigeminal neuralgia trigger zones: what are they and why do they matter clinically?" text="trigger zones"][/sc]
  • Ask about short duration of pain episodes (seconds to 2 minutes) - helps differentiate from continuous pain conditions.
  • Ask about pain-free intervals between attacks - indicative of classical trigeminal neuralgia, though these may shorten over time.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Trigeminal neuralgia"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - typically normal, as trigeminal neuralgia is a pain syndrome without systemic inflammatory response.
  • Check neurological examination - usually normal, with no objective sensory loss or motor weakness in the trigeminal nerve distribution, which helps differentiate from secondary causes.
  • Check for trigger points - light touch or palpation of specific facial areas may elicit a pain attack, confirming the presence of trigger zones.
  • Check for pain distribution - typically unilateral, affecting the V2 (maxillary) and/or V3 (mandibular) divisions of the trigeminal nerve. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Trigeminal nerve V2/V3 distribution: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="V2/V3 distribution"][/sc]
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Trigeminal neuralgia"][/sc]
  • Sensory loss or motor weakness - objective neurological deficits in the trigeminal nerve distribution (e.g., facial numbness, weakness of mastication) suggest a structural lesion compressing or damaging the nerve, rather than classical trigeminal neuralgia [1].
  • Bilateral symptoms - trigeminal neuralgia is almost exclusively unilateral; bilateral pain, especially if synchronous, raises suspicion for underlying systemic conditions like multiple sclerosis or other central nervous system pathology [1].
  • Optic neuritis or other focal neurological signs - concurrent neurological symptoms or signs elsewhere in the body may indicate a demyelinating disease (e.g., multiple sclerosis) or other intracranial pathology affecting the trigeminal nerve [2].
  • Pain that is constant or lacks trigger zones - classical trigeminal neuralgia is characterised by paroxysmal pain with distinct trigger zones; constant pain or absence of triggers may suggest atypical facial pain or other conditions [1].
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Trigeminal neuralgia"][/sc]
  • Atypical Trigeminal Neuralgia (TN2): Characterised by a more constant, dull, aching, or burning pain, often with superimposed paroxysmal attacks, and may be less responsive to typical treatments [1]. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Atypical Trigeminal Neuralgia (TN2): what is it and how does it differ from classical trigeminal neuralgia?" text="Atypical Trigeminal Neuralgia (TN2)"][/sc]
  • Trigeminal Neuralgia in Multiple Sclerosis: Occurs in a younger population and is often bilateral, due to demyelinating plaques affecting the trigeminal nerve root entry zone in the brainstem [2].
  • Younger Patients: While rare, trigeminal neuralgia in individuals under 40 should prompt investigation for underlying causes such as multiple sclerosis or structural lesions, as classical vascular compression is less common [1].
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Trigeminal neuralgia"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected Trigeminal neuralgia: Clinical diagnosis based on characteristic history of pain and a typically normal neurological examination.
  • Severity/risk tools: Neuropathic Pain Scale (NPS) on MDCalc can quantify pain intensity and quality, aiding in monitoring treatment response.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Magnetic Resonance Imaging (MRI) of the brain and trigeminal nerve is essential to exclude secondary causes such as tumors, vascular malformations, or demyelinating plaques (e.g., multiple sclerosis). [sc name="jitl-query" Q="MRI in trigeminal neuralgia: what does it show and why does it matter clinically?" text="MRI in trigeminal neuralgia"][/sc]
  • Imaging - MRI: High-resolution MRI with specific sequences (e.g., FIESTA or CISS) is the gold standard for visualising neurovascular compression of the trigeminal nerve root.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Trigeminal neuralgia"][/sc]
  • **Dental pain** - typically constant or throbbing, often exacerbated by hot/cold stimuli, and usually localised to a specific tooth, unlike the paroxysmal, electric shock-like pain of trigeminal neuralgia [1].
  • **Temporomandibular joint (TMJ) dysfunction** - pain is often associated with jaw movement, clicking, or locking, and tenderness over the joint, which is not characteristic of trigeminal neuralgia [1].
  • **Migraine with facial pain** - usually accompanied by other migraine features (e.g., photophobia, phonophobia, aura), and the pain is typically throbbing and longer-lasting than trigeminal neuralgia attacks [1].
  • **Cluster headache** - characterised by severe, unilateral orbital, supraorbital, or temporal pain, often with autonomic features (e.g., lacrimation, ptosis, rhinorrhoea), and typically lasts 15-180 minutes, much longer than trigeminal neuralgia attacks [1].
  • **Post-herpetic neuralgia** - persistent burning or aching pain in a dermatomal distribution following a herpes zoster outbreak, often with associated dysaesthesia or allodynia, and a history of vesicular rash [1].
  • **Atypical facial pain (Persistent Idiopathic Facial Pain)** - chronic, continuous facial pain that does not fit the criteria for other facial pain syndromes, often described as a deep ache or burning, and lacks the paroxysmal nature and trigger zones of trigeminal neuralgia. Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Trigeminal neuralgia"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Recognition of the characteristic pain to avoid misdiagnosis and inappropriate interventions (e.g., unnecessary dental procedures).
  • Definitive management: Initial management often involves pharmacological therapy; if medical management fails or is not tolerated, surgical options may be considered [1].
  • Exam pharmacology reference (UKMLA/MRCGP): Carbamazepine is typically considered first-line pharmacological treatment for classical trigeminal neuralgia, aiming to reduce neuronal hyperexcitability. Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Trigeminal neuralgia"][/sc]
  • Psychological distress: The chronic, severe, and unpredictable nature of trigeminal neuralgia pain can lead to significant anxiety, depression, social isolation, and a reduced quality of life [1].
  • Nutritional deficiencies and weight loss: Fear of triggering pain during eating or drinking can lead to inadequate oral intake, resulting in malnutrition and unintended weight loss [1].
  • Medication side effects: Long-term use of anticonvulsants like carbamazepine can lead to adverse effects such as drowsiness, dizziness, hyponatraemia, and haematological abnormalities, requiring careful monitoring [1].
  • Treatment failure or recurrence: Despite initial success with medical or surgical interventions, pain may recur or become refractory over time, necessitating further treatment adjustments or alternative therapies [1].
