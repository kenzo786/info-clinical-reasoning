Hyperthyroidism/ thyrotoxicosis [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Hyperthyroidism/ thyrotoxicosis"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Hyperthyroidism/ thyrotoxicosis"][/sc]
  • Hyperthyroidism is a condition where the thyroid gland produces excessive amounts of thyroid hormones, leading to a hypermetabolic state. Thyrotoxicosis is the clinical syndrome resulting from elevated thyroid hormone levels in the body, regardless of their origin [1].
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical features, investigations, and initial management of hyperthyroidism and thyrotoxicosis.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Hyperthyroidism/ thyrotoxicosis"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Hyperthyroidism/ thyrotoxicosis"][/sc]
  • Hyperthyroidism affects approximately 1 in 50 women and is about 10 times more common in women than men, typically presenting between 20 and 40 years of age [2, 3].
  • The annual incidence of hyperthyroidism in the UK is estimated to be between 100-200 cases per 100,000 population [4].
  • Risk factors include a family history of thyroid disease (especially Graves\' disease), other autoimmune conditions (e.g., type 1 diabetes, pernicious anaemia), and increased iodine intake [5, 6].
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Hyperthyroidism/ thyrotoxicosis"][/sc]
  • Trigger: In Graves\' disease, an autoimmune response leads to the production of thyroid-stimulating immunoglobulins (TSIs) that bind to and activate the thyroid-stimulating hormone (TSH) receptor on thyroid follicular cells [7]. In toxic multinodular goitre or solitary toxic adenoma, autonomous thyroid nodules develop that produce thyroid hormones independently of TSH regulation [8].
  • Progression: The persistent stimulation of TSH receptors by TSIs or the unregulated activity of autonomous nodules results in increased synthesis and release of thyroid hormones (thyroxine (T4) and triiodothyronine (T3)) [7, 8].
  • Consequence: Elevated levels of T4 and T3 accelerate metabolic processes throughout the body, leading to a hypermetabolic state and the characteristic signs and symptoms of thyrotoxicosis [1].
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Hyperthyroidism/ thyrotoxicosis"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Symptoms may develop gradually over weeks to months, often subtle initially, including mild anxiety, increased appetite, and slight weight loss. Patients may attribute these to stress or other factors, delaying presentation [9].
  • Progressive phase: As thyroid hormone levels rise, symptoms become more pronounced and diverse, affecting multiple organ systems. This phase can persist for several months, particularly beyond 6-12 weeks, leading to significant impact on quality of life if untreated [10].
  • Late phase: If untreated or inadequately managed, hyperthyroidism can lead to severe complications such as atrial fibrillation, osteoporosis, and in extreme cases, thyroid storm, which is a life-threatening exacerbation of thyrotoxicosis [11].
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Hyperthyroidism/ thyrotoxicosis"][/sc]
  • Ask about unintentional weight loss despite increased appetite - this suggests a hypermetabolic state and can be a key diagnostic clue [12].
  • Ask about palpitations or a racing heart - these indicate cardiovascular involvement due to increased adrenergic sensitivity and can be a presenting complaint [12].
  • Ask about heat intolerance and increased sweating - these reflect the elevated metabolic rate and altered thermoregulation [12].
  • Ask about anxiety, irritability, and difficulty sleeping - these neuropsychiatric symptoms are common due to central nervous system stimulation by excess thyroid hormones [12].
  • Ask about tremor - a fine tremor is a classic sign of sympathetic overactivity [12].
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Hyperthyroidism/ thyrotoxicosis"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - tachycardia (resting heart rate >100 bpm), systolic hypertension with a wide pulse pressure, and sometimes a low-grade fever are common due to increased metabolic rate and cardiac output [13].
  • Check for a fine tremor, particularly of the outstretched hands - this is a classic sign of increased sympathetic activity [12].
  • Check the skin - it is often warm, moist, and smooth due to increased peripheral blood flow and sweating [12].
  • Check the eyes - lid lag (upper eyelid lags behind the globe on downward gaze) and a characteristic stare are common. In Graves\' disease, exophthalmos (protrusion of the eyeballs) may be present [12].
  • Check the thyroid gland - a diffuse goitre may be palpable, and a bruit may be audible over the thyroid in Graves\' disease, indicating increased vascularity [14].
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Hyperthyroidism/ thyrotoxicosis"][/sc]
  • Fever and altered mental status: High fever, severe agitation, confusion, delirium, or psychosis can indicate impending or established thyroid storm, a life-threatening medical emergency [15].
  • Significant cardiovascular compromise: New-onset or worsening atrial fibrillation, severe tachycardia (e.g., >140 bpm), or signs of heart failure (e.g., dyspnoea, oedema) suggest severe thyrotoxicosis requiring urgent intervention [15].
  • Gastrointestinal dysfunction: Profound nausea, vomiting, diarrhoea, or unexplained abdominal pain, especially when accompanied by other systemic symptoms, can be a feature of severe thyrotoxicosis or thyroid storm [15].
  • Jaundice: The presence of jaundice indicates severe hepatic dysfunction, which can occur in severe thyrotoxicosis and is a poor prognostic sign [15].
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Hyperthyroidism/ thyrotoxicosis"][/sc]
  • Older adults (Apathetic Hyperthyroidism): May present with subtle or atypical symptoms such as fatigue, apathy, depression, weight loss, and anorexia, rather than the classic hyperkinetic symptoms. This can delay diagnosis as symptoms may be mistaken for normal ageing or other comorbidities [16].
  • Pregnancy: Many physiological changes in normal pregnancy (e.g., increased heart rate, heat intolerance, fatigue) can mimic symptoms of hyperthyroidism, making diagnosis challenging. Careful interpretation of thyroid function tests is crucial, as TSH levels are naturally lower in early pregnancy [17].
  • Co-existing illness: In patients with significant comorbidities, the symptoms of hyperthyroidism may be masked or attributed to their existing conditions, leading to delayed recognition and treatment [16].
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Hyperthyroidism/ thyrotoxicosis"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected hyperthyroidism:
    • Thyroid Function Tests (TFTs): Low or undetectable Thyroid-Stimulating Hormone (TSH) and elevated free Thyroxine (fT4) and/or free Triiodothyronine (fT3) confirm the diagnosis of hyperthyroidism [18].
    • Thyroid-stimulating hormone receptor antibodies (TRAb): Positive TRAb strongly suggests Graves\' disease as the underlying cause [19].
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible or to determine aetiology:
    • Thyroid peroxidase antibodies (TPOAb) and Thyroglobulin antibodies (TgAb): May be elevated in autoimmune thyroid conditions, but are not specific for hyperthyroidism [19].
    • Erythrocyte Sedimentation Rate (ESR) and C-reactive protein (CRP): May be elevated in thyroiditis [20].
  • Imaging - Thyroid Scintigraphy (Radioactive Iodine Uptake and Scan): Used to differentiate causes of hyperthyroidism. High uptake is seen in Graves\' disease and toxic nodular goitre, while low uptake is characteristic of thyroiditis or iodine-induced hyperthyroidism [21].
  • Imaging - Thyroid Ultrasound: Useful for assessing thyroid size, nodularity, and vascularity. Can identify toxic nodules or multinodular goitre, and assess for features suggestive of malignancy if nodules are present [22].
  • Serial reassessment: Regular monitoring of TFTs is essential to assess treatment response and detect recurrence or progression of the condition [18].
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Hyperthyroidism/ thyrotoxicosis"][/sc]
  • A 35-year-old female presents with a 3-month history of unintentional weight loss despite increased appetite, palpitations, anxiety, and heat intolerance. On examination, she has a fine tremor, warm and moist skin, tachycardia, and a palpable diffuse goitre.
  • A young adult female presenting with a subacute onset of hypermetabolic symptoms, including weight loss, cardiovascular manifestations, and neuropsychiatric features, alongside physical signs of thyroid overactivity.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Hyperthyroidism/ thyrotoxicosis"][/sc]
  • Graves\' disease - presence of TRAb antibodies, diffuse goitre, and often ophthalmopathy or dermopathy [19].
  • Toxic multinodular goitre - palpable nodules on thyroid examination, often in older patients, and patchy uptake on thyroid scintigraphy [8].
  • Toxic adenoma - solitary palpable nodule, with increased uptake confined to the nodule on scintigraphy [8].
  • Thyroiditis (e.g., subacute, postpartum, drug-induced) - often a painful thyroid, transient thyrotoxic phase followed by hypothyroidism, and low uptake on scintigraphy [20].
  • Iodine-induced hyperthyroidism (Jod-Basedow phenomenon) - history of recent iodine exposure (e.g., contrast media, amiodarone), particularly in patients with pre-existing thyroid disease [21].
  • Factitious thyrotoxicosis - exogenous thyroid hormone intake, undetectable thyroglobulin, and low uptake on scintigraphy [18].
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Hyperthyroidism/ thyrotoxicosis"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities:
    • Symptomatic relief: Beta-blockers (e.g., propranolol) are often initiated to control adrenergic symptoms such as palpitations, tremor, and anxiety [23].
    • Initiation of antithyroid drugs (ATDs): Carbimazole or propylthiouracil are used to reduce thyroid hormone synthesis [23].
  • Definitive management:
    • Antithyroid drugs (ATDs): Carbimazole is typically the first-line treatment in the UK. Propylthiouracil is an alternative, particularly in the first trimester of pregnancy or in thyroid storm [23, 24].
    • Radioactive iodine (RAI) therapy: A definitive treatment that ablates overactive thyroid cells. It is generally not suitable for pregnant or breastfeeding women [23].
    • Surgery (thyroidectomy): Surgical removal of part or all of the thyroid gland. This is considered for large goitres, obstructive symptoms, or when ATDs and RAI are contraindicated or unsuccessful [23].
  • Monitoring and follow-up:
    • Regular monitoring of thyroid function tests (TFTs), including TSH, fT4, and fT3, is essential to assess treatment response and guide medication dose adjustments [25].
    • Patients should be monitored for adverse effects of ATDs, such as rash, arthralgia, and rare but serious complications like agranulocytosis or hepatotoxicity [23].
  • Escalation and safety-netting:
    • Patients on ATDs must be advised to seek urgent medical attention if they develop symptoms such as fever, sore throat, or mouth ulcers, as these may indicate agranulocytosis [23].
    • Educate patients about the symptoms of thyroid storm and the importance of seeking emergency care if these develop [15].
  • Exam pharmacology reference (UKMLA/MRCGP):
    • [sc name="jitl-medication" Q="Carbimazole use in Hyperthyroidism" text="Carbimazole"][/sc]: Blocks thyroid hormone synthesis by inhibiting thyroid peroxidase.
    • [sc name="jitl-medication" Q="Propylthiouracil use in Hyperthyroidism" text="Propylthiouracil"][/sc]: Blocks thyroid hormone synthesis and also inhibits the peripheral conversion of T4 to T3. It is preferred in early pregnancy.
    • [sc name="jitl-medication" Q="Propranolol use in Hyperthyroidism" text="Propranolol"][/sc]: A non-selective beta-blocker used for symptomatic relief of adrenergic manifestations of thyrotoxicosis.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Hyperthyroidism/ thyrotoxicosis"][/sc]
  • Thyroid Storm (Thyrotoxic Crisis): A rare but life-threatening exacerbation of thyrotoxicosis, characterised by severe systemic decompensation, including high fever, profound tachycardia, altered mental status, and multi-organ dysfunction. It is a medical emergency requiring immediate recognition and aggressive management [15, 26].
  • Cardiac Complications: Chronic hyperthyroidism can lead to atrial fibrillation, congestive heart failure, and increased risk of other arrhythmias due to the sustained hyperdynamic circulatory state and increased myocardial oxygen demand [13, 27].
  • Osteoporosis: Excess thyroid hormones accelerate bone turnover, leading to increased bone resorption and a reduction in bone mineral density, increasing the risk of fractures, particularly in post-menopausal women [28].
  • Thyroid Eye Disease (Graves\' Ophthalmopathy): In Graves\' disease, autoimmune inflammation can affect the orbital tissues, leading to symptoms such as proptosis, diplopia, pain, and in severe cases, optic nerve compression and vision loss [12].
  • Infertility and Obstetric Complications: Hyperthyroidism can disrupt menstrual cycles and reduce fertility. During pregnancy, uncontrolled hyperthyroidism is associated with increased risks of miscarriage, preterm birth, pre-eclampsia, and foetal growth restriction [17].
