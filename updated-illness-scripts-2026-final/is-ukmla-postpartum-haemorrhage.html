Postpartum haemorrhage (Pregnancy and the puerperium) [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Postpartum haemorrhage (Pregnancy and the puerperium)"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Postpartum haemorrhage (Pregnancy and the puerperium)"][/sc]
  • Postpartum haemorrhage (PPH) is defined as blood loss of 500 ml or more within 24 hours of birth (primary PPH) or any abnormal or excessive bleeding from 24 hours up to 12 weeks postpartum (secondary PPH).
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the signs and symptoms of primary and secondary postpartum haemorrhage, identify key risk factors, and understand the immediate management principles to prevent maternal morbidity and mortality.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Postpartum haemorrhage (Pregnancy and the puerperium)"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Postpartum haemorrhage (Pregnancy and the puerperium)"][/sc]
  • Primary PPH affects approximately 5% of women giving birth in the UK, with severe PPH (blood loss ≥1500 ml) occurring in about 1.4% of births (NICE).
  • Secondary PPH is less common, affecting around 1% of women, often due to retained placental tissue or infection.
  • Key risk factors include previous PPH, multiple pregnancy, prolonged labour, instrumental delivery, placenta praevia, and pre-eclampsia.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Postpartum haemorrhage (Pregnancy and the puerperium)"][/sc]
  • Primary PPH often results from the "4 Ts": Tone (uterine atony, most common cause), Trauma (genital tract lacerations), Tissue (retained placental fragments), and Thrombin (coagulopathy).
  • Uterine atony, a failure of the uterus to contract effectively after birth, leads to persistent bleeding from placental site vessels due to inadequate compression [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Uterine atony: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="uterine atony"][/sc].
  • Uncontrolled bleeding can rapidly lead to hypovolaemic shock, organ dysfunction, and disseminated intravascular coagulation (DIC) if not promptly managed.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Postpartum haemorrhage (Pregnancy and the puerperium)"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Primary PPH typically manifests within the first 24 hours following childbirth, often immediately after placental delivery.
  • Progressive phase: If initial measures are ineffective, blood loss can continue to accumulate rapidly, leading to a worsening clinical picture within minutes to a few hours.
  • Late phase: Secondary PPH occurs from 24 hours up to 12 weeks postpartum, often presenting with intermittent or persistent bleeding, sometimes associated with infection.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Postpartum haemorrhage (Pregnancy and the puerperium)"][/sc]
  • Ask about heavy vaginal bleeding - to quantify blood loss and assess its onset and progression.
  • Ask about feelings of dizziness or faintness - indicating potential hypovolaemia and reduced cerebral perfusion.
  • Ask about palpitations or shortness of breath - suggesting compensatory mechanisms for blood loss.
  • Ask about abdominal pain or uterine tenderness - which may point towards retained products of conception or uterine infection in secondary PPH.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Postpartum haemorrhage (Pregnancy and the puerperium)"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs (heart rate, blood pressure, respiratory rate, oxygen saturation) - to assess for signs of hypovolaemic shock, such as tachycardia and hypotension. The Shock Index (HR/SBP) can be a useful early indicator of hypovolaemia [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Shock Index: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Shock Index"][/sc].
  • Check uterine tone and size - a boggy, enlarged uterus suggests uterine atony.
  • Check for active bleeding from the vagina - to quantify blood loss and identify its source (e.g., lacerations).
  • Check for signs of genital tract trauma (e.g., perineal tears, vaginal lacerations) - as a potential cause of primary PPH.
  • Check for pallor and capillary refill time - indicating peripheral perfusion and severity of blood loss.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Postpartum haemorrhage (Pregnancy and the puerperium)"][/sc]
  • Heavy, continuous vaginal bleeding: Indicates ongoing significant blood loss requiring immediate intervention.
  • Signs of hypovolaemic shock: Tachycardia, hypotension, altered mental status, and oliguria suggest critical blood volume depletion.
  • Failure of uterine contraction: A persistently soft, boggy uterus despite massage and uterotonic agents points to severe uterine atony.
  • Rapidly expanding haematoma: Suggests concealed bleeding, particularly after instrumental delivery or episiotomy.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Postpartum haemorrhage (Pregnancy and the puerperium)"][/sc]
  • Concealed haemorrhage: Bleeding into the broad ligament or a large vaginal haematoma may not be immediately obvious externally, leading to delayed recognition of hypovolaemia.
  • Coagulopathy-related PPH: Women with pre-existing bleeding disorders or those developing DIC may have persistent oozing despite adequate uterine tone and no obvious trauma [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Disseminated Intravascular Coagulation (DIC) in PPH: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="DIC in PPH"][/sc].
  • Delayed presentation of secondary PPH: Can occur several weeks postpartum, often presenting with intermittent bleeding, foul-smelling lochia, or fever, indicating retained products or endometritis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Postpartum haemorrhage (Pregnancy and the puerperium)"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected PPH: Full blood count (FBC) for haemoglobin and haematocrit, coagulation screen (prothrombin time, activated partial thromboplastin time, fibrinogen), and cross-match for blood products.
  • Severity/risk tools: The Shock Index (HR/SBP) can be used for rapid assessment of hypovolaemia. The RCOG Green-top Guideline 52 provides a comprehensive risk assessment for PPH.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Pelvic ultrasound to assess for retained placental tissue or uterine abnormalities in secondary PPH.
  • Serial reassessment: Regular monitoring of vital signs, blood loss, and urine output to assess response to treatment and detect ongoing haemorrhage.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Postpartum haemorrhage (Pregnancy and the puerperium)"][/sc]
  • A 30-year-old primiparous woman, 2 hours post-vaginal delivery, presents with heavy, continuous vaginal bleeding, dizziness, and a boggy uterus on examination.
  • Postpartum haemorrhage with signs of hypovolaemia, concerning for uterine atony.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Postpartum haemorrhage (Pregnancy and the puerperium)"][/sc]
  • Uterine atony - most common cause, characterised by a soft, poorly contracted uterus.
  • Genital tract trauma (lacerations, haematomas) - often presents with bright red bleeding despite a well-contracted uterus.
  • Retained placental tissue - can cause both primary and secondary PPH, often with an incompletely delivered placenta or persistent bleeding.
  • Coagulopathy - bleeding diathesis leading to persistent oozing, often with abnormal coagulation screen results.
  • Uterine inversion - rare but life-threatening, characterised by a sudden onset of pain and a mass in the vagina.
  • Placenta accreta spectrum - abnormal adherence of the placenta to the uterine wall, leading to difficulty with placental separation and massive haemorrhage.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Postpartum haemorrhage (Pregnancy and the puerperium)"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Summon senior help, initiate ABCDE approach, establish large bore IV access, commence fluid resuscitation, and send blood for FBC, coagulation screen, and cross-match.
  • Definitive management: Uterine massage and administration of uterotonic agents (e.g., oxytocin, carboprost, misoprostol) to promote uterine contraction. Examination of the genital tract for lacerations and removal of retained placental tissue.
  • Adjunctive management: Consider tranexamic acid to reduce blood loss. Balloon tamponade or uterine compression sutures may be considered for refractory uterine atony.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Close monitoring of vital signs, blood loss, and urine output. Post-PPH counselling and iron supplementation often warrants consideration.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Early involvement of obstetric, anaesthetic, and haematology teams. Patients should be advised on symptoms of secondary PPH and when to seek urgent medical attention.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Postpartum haemorrhage (Pregnancy and the puerperium)"][/sc]
  • Hypovolaemic shock: Due to significant blood loss, leading to inadequate organ perfusion and potential multi-organ failure.
  • Anaemia: Resulting from acute blood loss, often requiring iron supplementation or blood transfusion, and impacting maternal recovery.
  • Sheehan\'s syndrome: A rare but severe complication of massive PPH, leading to pituitary infarction and subsequent hypopituitarism, often presenting weeks to months postpartum.
  • Disseminated Intravascular Coagulation (DIC): A life-threatening condition where widespread activation of the clotting cascade leads to consumption of clotting factors and platelets, exacerbating bleeding.
  • Infection: Increased risk of endometritis or wound infection, particularly with retained products of conception or prolonged labour.
  • Psychological impact: Can lead to post-traumatic stress disorder, anxiety, and depression in the postpartum period.
