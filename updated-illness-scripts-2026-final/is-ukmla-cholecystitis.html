Cholecystitis [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Cholecystitis"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Cholecystitis"][/sc]
  • Cholecystitis is an inflammation of the gallbladder, most commonly caused by obstruction of the cystic duct by gallstones (calculous cholecystitis), leading to bile stasis and subsequent inflammation, often with secondary bacterial infection [1] [2]. A less common but more severe form, acalculous cholecystitis, occurs without gallstones, typically in critically ill patients [2].
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical presentation of acute cholecystitis, differentiate it from other causes of acute abdominal pain, and understand the principles of its initial management and definitive treatment.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Cholecystitis"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Cholecystitis"][/sc]
  • Gallstones, the primary cause of calculous cholecystitis, affect over 1 in 10 adults in the UK, though most remain asymptomatic [2].
  • Acute cholecystitis is a common surgical emergency, with calculous cholecystitis accounting for approximately 90% of cases [1] [2].
  • Acalculous cholecystitis is less common (5-10% of cases) but carries a higher mortality rate, typically affecting critically ill patients [1] [2].
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Cholecystitis"][/sc]
  • Obstruction: The initial trigger is often the impaction of a gallstone in the cystic duct or Hartmann\'s pouch, leading to a build-up of bile within the gallbladder [1].
  • Inflammation: Bile stasis and increased intraluminal pressure cause chemical irritation and inflammation of the gallbladder wall. This can be exacerbated by secondary bacterial infection, commonly by enteric organisms [1]. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Bile stasis: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Bile stasis"][/sc]
  • Consequence: Persistent inflammation can lead to gallbladder distension, oedema, ischaemia, and potentially necrosis, perforation, or abscess formation if untreated [1].
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Cholecystitis"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Symptoms typically begin acutely, with right upper quadrant pain often developing within minutes to a few hours after a fatty meal, persisting for more than 3-6 hours [1] [2].
  • Progressive phase: If obstruction and inflammation persist, symptoms may worsen over 24-72 hours, with increasing pain, fever, and systemic signs of infection. Complications such as gangrene or perforation become more likely beyond 72 hours [1].
  • Late phase: Without intervention, complications can lead to peritonitis, sepsis, or abscess formation, requiring urgent surgical management. Chronic cholecystitis may develop from recurrent acute episodes [1].
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Cholecystitis"][/sc]
  • Ask about sudden, severe right upper quadrant pain - this is the cardinal symptom, often radiating to the right shoulder or back, and is typically persistent [2].
  • Ask about duration of pain - pain lasting more than 3-6 hours, unlike biliary colic which is usually self-limiting [1].
  • Ask about associated symptoms - nausea, vomiting, anorexia, and fever are common, indicating systemic inflammation [2].
  • Ask about precipitating factors - symptoms may be triggered or exacerbated by fatty meals due to gallbladder contraction [2].
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Cholecystitis"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - fever, tachycardia, and tachypnoea may indicate systemic inflammatory response [1].
  • Check for right upper quadrant tenderness - often marked, with guarding or rebound tenderness [1].
  • Check for Murphy\'s sign - inspiratory arrest during palpation of the right subcostal area, highly suggestive of acute cholecystitis [2]. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Murphy\'s sign: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Murphy\'s sign"][/sc]
  • Check for abdominal mass - a palpable, tender gallbladder may be present in some cases [1].
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Cholecystitis"][/sc]
  • Sepsis: Signs of systemic inflammatory response syndrome (SIRS) or sepsis, such as hypotension, altered mental status, or organ dysfunction, indicate severe disease and require urgent escalation [1].
  • Peritonism: Generalised abdominal tenderness, rigidity, or rebound suggests gallbladder perforation and peritonitis, a surgical emergency [2].
  • Jaundice: Yellowing of the skin or sclera may indicate obstruction of the common bile duct (choledocholithiasis) or ascending cholangitis, complicating the presentation [2].
  • Persistent pain beyond 72 hours: Prolonged symptoms despite initial conservative management may suggest ongoing inflammation, potential complications, or an atypical presentation requiring reassessment [1].
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Cholecystitis"][/sc]
  • Older adults: May present with vague symptoms, such as anorexia, malaise, or confusion, with less pronounced pain or fever, leading to delayed diagnosis [1].
  • Diabetic patients: Have an increased risk of complications, including emphysematous cholecystitis, and may have an attenuated inflammatory response [1].
  • Immunosuppressed patients: May present with minimal symptoms and signs despite severe inflammation, increasing the risk of perforation [1].
  • Acalculous cholecystitis: Occurs in critically ill patients (e.g., trauma, burns, sepsis) without gallstones, often presenting with unexplained fever, jaundice, or abdominal distension, and carries a higher mortality [2]. Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Cholecystitis"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected cholecystitis:
    • Blood tests: Full blood count (leukocytosis), C-reactive protein (elevated), liver function tests (may be normal or show mild derangement) [1] [2].
    • Ultrasound scan of the right upper quadrant: Initial investigation of choice, demonstrating gallstones, gallbladder wall thickening (>3mm), pericholecystic fluid, and sonographic Murphy\'s sign [1] [2].
  • Severity/risk tools: Tokyo Guidelines severity grading for acute cholecystitis can guide management decisions [1]. Tokyo Guidelines Severity Grading for Acute Cholecystitis on MDCalc [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Tokyo Guidelines: what are they and why do they matter clinically?" text="Tokyo Guidelines"][/sc]
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Consider amylase/lipase for pancreatitis, or further imaging for other causes of abdominal pain [1].
  • Imaging - CT scan: May be used if ultrasound is inconclusive or to assess for complications such as perforation or abscess [1].
  • Imaging - HIDA scan (Cholescintigraphy): Used when ultrasound is equivocal, demonstrating cystic duct obstruction [1].
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Cholecystitis"][/sc]
  • A 45-year-old female presents with sudden onset, severe right upper quadrant pain radiating to her right shoulder, associated with nausea, vomiting, and a low-grade fever, exacerbated after a fatty meal.
  • Middle-aged female with acute, persistent right upper quadrant pain and systemic inflammatory response, concerning for acute cholecystitis.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Cholecystitis"][/sc]
  • Biliary colic - typically shorter duration of pain (<6 hours), resolves spontaneously, and lacks signs of inflammation [1].
  • Acute pancreatitis - epigastric pain radiating to the back, often associated with elevated amylase/lipase [1].
  • Acute cholangitis - Charcot\'s triad (fever, jaundice, RUQ pain) or Reynold\'s pentad (Charcot\'s triad + hypotension, altered mental status) indicates ascending infection of the bile ducts [1]. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Acute cholangitis: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Acute cholangitis"][/sc]
  • Peptic ulcer disease - epigastric pain, often relieved by antacids, may be associated with dyspepsia [1].
  • Hepatitis - diffuse right upper quadrant pain, often with jaundice and elevated transaminases [1].
  • Right lower lobe pneumonia - pleuritic chest pain, cough, and dyspnoea, may have referred pain to the right upper quadrant [1].
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Cholecystitis"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Recognition, assessment of severity (e.g., Tokyo Guidelines), escalation to surgical team, supportive care including nil by mouth, intravenous fluids, and analgesia [1].
  • Definitive management: Laparoscopic cholecystectomy is the definitive treatment, ideally performed within 72 hours of symptom onset for acute calculous cholecystitis to reduce complications and conversion to open surgery [1] [2].
  • Adjunctive management: Broad-spectrum intravenous antibiotics are typically initiated empirically to cover common enteric pathogens, especially if infection is suspected or confirmed [1].
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Close monitoring for clinical deterioration or development of complications. Post-cholecystectomy, patients are typically advised on dietary modifications [2].
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Patients with severe cholecystitis, signs of sepsis, or complications require urgent surgical intervention and intensive care support [1].
  • Exam pharmacology reference (UKMLA/MRCGP): Antimicrobial agents often include broad-spectrum antibiotics such as co-amoxiclav or cephalosporins with metronidazole. Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Cholecystitis"][/sc]
  • Gallbladder gangrene: Occurs when severe inflammation leads to ischaemia and necrosis of the gallbladder wall, typically within 72 hours of symptom onset, increasing the risk of perforation and sepsis [1].
  • Gallbladder perforation: Can result in localised abscess formation or generalised peritonitis, a life-threatening condition requiring emergency surgery [2].
  • Pericholecystic abscess: A collection of pus around the gallbladder, often a consequence of contained perforation, presenting with persistent fever and localised tenderness [1].
  • Fistula formation: Chronic inflammation can lead to the formation of a fistula between the gallbladder and adjacent organs, such as the duodenum, potentially leading to gallstone ileus [1].
  • Sepsis: Systemic inflammatory response to infection, particularly if complicated by gangrene or perforation, can lead to multi-organ dysfunction and high mortality [1].
