Pleural effusion [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Pleural effusion"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Pleural effusion"][/sc]
  • A pleural effusion is an abnormal accumulation of fluid in the pleural space, which can impair lung expansion and gas exchange.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical features and common causes of pleural effusion, and outline initial diagnostic steps and management principles.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Pleural effusion"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Pleural effusion"][/sc]
  • Pleural effusions are common, with an estimated incidence of 320 cases per 100,000 population per year in the UK. [1]
  • Malignancy (41.9%), infection (21%), and heart/renal failure (13.1%) are the commonest causes of pleural effusion in a large UK multicentre cohort. [2]
  • Risk factors include heart failure, liver cirrhosis, renal failure, malignancy, pneumonia, pulmonary embolism, and autoimmune diseases.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Pleural effusion"][/sc]
  • Trigger: An imbalance in fluid production and absorption within the pleural space, often due to increased hydrostatic pressure, decreased oncotic pressure, increased capillary permeability, or impaired lymphatic drainage.
  • Progression: Accumulation of fluid compresses the adjacent lung parenchyma, leading to reduced lung volumes, ventilation-perfusion mismatch, and dyspnoea.
  • Consequence: Significant effusions can cause respiratory distress, hypoxaemia, and in severe cases, mediastinal shift and cardiovascular compromise. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Transudate vs Exudate: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Transudate vs Exudate"][/sc]
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Pleural effusion"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Symptoms such as dyspnoea, cough, and pleuritic chest pain may develop gradually over days to weeks, depending on the underlying cause and rate of fluid accumulation.
  • Progressive phase: As the effusion enlarges, dyspnoea typically worsens, and patients may experience orthopnoea or reduced exercise tolerance. This can occur over weeks to months.
  • Late phase: Untreated large effusions can lead to chronic respiratory compromise, recurrent infections, or complications such as trapped lung, particularly beyond several weeks.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Pleural effusion"][/sc]
  • Ask about dyspnoea - often progressive and related to the size of the effusion, indicating lung compression.
  • Ask about pleuritic chest pain - sharp, localised pain exacerbated by deep breathing or coughing, suggesting pleural inflammation.
  • Ask about cough - typically dry and non-productive, caused by irritation of the pleura or compression of airways.
  • Ask about constitutional symptoms (e.g., weight loss, fever, night sweats) - may indicate underlying malignancy or infection.
  • Ask about orthopnoea - difficulty breathing when lying flat, suggesting significant effusion or associated heart failure.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Pleural effusion"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - tachypnoea and tachycardia may indicate respiratory compromise or systemic illness.
  • Check reduced chest expansion - unilateral reduction on the affected side due to fluid accumulation.
  • Check stony dullness to percussion - a classic sign over the area of fluid, indicating significant effusion.
  • Check diminished or absent breath sounds - over the effusion, as fluid impedes sound transmission. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Light criteria: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Light criteria"][/sc]
  • Check tracheal deviation - away from the effusion in very large effusions, indicating mediastinal shift.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Pleural effusion"][/sc]
  • Rapidly accumulating effusion - suggests active bleeding, severe infection, or rapidly progressing malignancy, requiring urgent investigation and drainage.
  • Haemoptysis - raises suspicion of malignancy, pulmonary embolism with infarction, or severe infection.
  • Fever and purulent sputum with effusion - highly suggestive of empyema, requiring urgent antibiotic therapy and drainage.
  • Unilateral effusion with significant weight loss - strong indicator of underlying malignancy, necessitating prompt diagnostic work-up.
  • Sudden onset severe pleuritic chest pain with dyspnoea - may indicate pulmonary embolism, especially if associated with risk factors.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Pleural effusion"][/sc]
  • Older adults: May present with non-specific symptoms like fatigue or confusion, with dyspnoea being less prominent, delaying diagnosis.
  • Immunosuppressed patients: Increased risk of unusual infections (e.g., fungal, mycobacterial) causing effusions, which may be more aggressive.
  • Post-cardiac surgery: Effusions are common and often benign, but can rarely indicate chylothorax or haemothorax. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Chylothorax: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Chylothorax"][/sc]
  • Subpulmonic effusion: Fluid collects between the lung base and diaphragm, mimicking an elevated diaphragm on chest X-ray, making diagnosis challenging.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Pleural effusion"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected pleural effusion: Chest X-ray (PA and lateral) to confirm presence and size. Ultrasound of the chest is crucial for guiding thoracentesis and identifying loculations.
  • Pleural fluid analysis: Diagnostic thoracentesis with fluid sent for biochemistry (protein, LDH, glucose), microbiology (Gram stain, culture, TB culture), cytology, and cell count. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Pleural fluid LDH and protein: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Pleural fluid LDH and protein"][/sc]
  • Severity/risk tools: Light\'s Criteria on MDCalc for differentiating transudates from exudates.
  • Imaging - CT Thorax: Indicated for further characterisation of the effusion, underlying lung pathology, pleural abnormalities, and guiding further interventions.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Consider echocardiography for suspected cardiac causes, or D-dimer/CT pulmonary angiogram for suspected pulmonary embolism.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Pleural effusion"][/sc]
  • A 68-year-old male with a history of heart failure presents with progressively worsening dyspnoea, non-productive cough, and reduced chest expansion on the right, with stony dullness to percussion.
  • Elderly male with chronic cardiac comorbidity and subacute respiratory symptoms, concerning for transudative pleural effusion secondary to heart failure.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Pleural effusion"][/sc]
  • Pneumonia - often associated with productive cough, fever, and focal consolidation on chest X-ray, though can cause parapneumonic effusion.
  • Heart failure - typically presents with bilateral effusions, elevated JVP, and peripheral oedema, but can be unilateral.
  • Pulmonary embolism - sudden onset dyspnoea and pleuritic chest pain, often with clear lung fields or small effusions, and risk factors for VTE.
  • Malignancy (e.g., lung cancer, mesothelioma, metastatic disease) - often insidious onset, constitutional symptoms, and may present with recurrent effusions. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Malignant pleural effusion: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Malignant pleural effusion"][/sc]
  • Liver cirrhosis - usually transudative, often right-sided, associated with ascites and signs of chronic liver disease.
  • Nephrotic syndrome - typically bilateral transudative effusions, associated with generalised oedema and heavy proteinuria.
  • Connective tissue disease (e.g., rheumatoid arthritis, SLE) - effusions may be chronic, often small, and associated with other systemic features of the disease.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Pleural effusion"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Ensure adequate oxygenation and respiratory support. Consider urgent therapeutic thoracentesis for large, symptomatic effusions causing respiratory distress. Defer to local pathways for escalation.
  • Definitive management: Directed at the underlying cause. For transudates, manage heart failure, liver cirrhosis, or renal disease. For exudates, treat infection (antibiotics), malignancy (chemotherapy, radiotherapy, pleurodesis, indwelling pleural catheter), or inflammatory conditions.
  • Adjunctive management: Analgesia for pleuritic pain. Nutritional support for chronic conditions.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Regular clinical assessment, repeat imaging (chest X-ray) to monitor effusion size and resolution. Follow-up with specialist respiratory services is often warranted.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Advise patients to seek urgent medical attention for worsening dyspnoea, fever, increasing pain, or new haemoptysis.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Pleural effusion"][/sc]
  • Empyema: Infection of the pleural fluid, leading to pus formation, often presenting with fever, pleuritic pain, and systemic signs of sepsis, particularly if untreated for several days.
  • Trapped lung: The visceral pleura becomes encased by fibrous tissue, preventing lung re-expansion after fluid drainage, leading to chronic dyspnoea and restrictive lung disease, often developing over weeks to months.
  • Pneumothorax: Can occur as a complication of thoracentesis or spontaneously, causing sudden onset pleuritic chest pain and acute dyspnoea.
  • Haemothorax: Accumulation of blood in the pleural space, often due to trauma or iatrogenic injury during procedures, leading to acute respiratory distress and hypovolaemic shock.
  • Bronchopleural fistula: An abnormal communication between the bronchial tree and the pleural space, often a complication of severe infection or surgery, leading to persistent air leak and recurrent empyema.
