Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Benign skin lesion (including melanocytic naevi, epidermal cyst, dermatofibroma, seborrhoeic keratosis, actinic keratosis)"][/sc]
  • Benign skin lesions are common, non-cancerous growths or abnormalities of the skin, encompassing a wide range of conditions such as melanocytic naevi (moles), epidermal cysts, dermatofibromas, seborrhoeic keratoses, and actinic keratoses.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: The candidate must recognise and differentiate common benign skin lesions from malignant or pre-malignant conditions, understanding their typical presentation and when referral for further assessment is warranted.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Benign skin lesion (including melanocytic naevi, epidermal cyst, dermatofibroma, seborrhoeic keratosis, actinic keratosis)"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Benign skin lesion (including melanocytic naevi, epidermal cyst, dermatofibroma, seborrhoeic keratosis, actinic keratosis)"][/sc]
  • Melanocytic naevi are extremely common, with most adults having 10-40 moles. Their prevalence is influenced by genetics and sun exposure.
  • Seborrhoeic keratoses are highly prevalent, affecting over 30% of individuals over 40 years old, increasing with age.
  • Actinic keratoses affect approximately 15-20% of the UK population over 60, representing a pre-malignant condition linked to chronic sun exposure.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Benign skin lesion (including melanocytic naevi, epidermal cyst, dermatofibroma, seborrhoeic keratosis, actinic keratosis)"][/sc]
  • Trigger: Genetic predisposition, chronic sun exposure, or minor skin trauma can initiate abnormal cellular proliferation or differentiation in the epidermis or dermis.
  • Progression: This leads to the formation of localised collections of specific cell types, such as melanocytes in naevi, keratinocytes in seborrhoeic keratoses, or fibroblasts in dermatofibromas.
  • Consequence: The result is a visible, palpable skin lesion with characteristic features, which, while typically benign, may rarely undergo malignant transformation (e.g., actinic keratosis to squamous cell carcinoma).
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Benign skin lesion (including melanocytic naevi, epidermal cyst, dermatofibroma, seborrhoeic keratosis, actinic keratosis)"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Lesions may appear gradually over months to years, often unnoticed, or as a new growth that slowly enlarges.
  • Progressive phase: Many benign lesions remain stable in size and appearance for decades, though some, like seborrhoeic keratoses, may slowly increase in size or number, particularly beyond several years.
  • Late phase: While generally stable, some lesions may become symptomatic (e.g., itchy, inflamed) or undergo subtle changes, necessitating reassessment, particularly beyond several decades.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Benign skin lesion (including melanocytic naevi, epidermal cyst, dermatofibroma, seborrhoeic keratosis, actinic keratosis)"][/sc]
  • Ask about change in size, shape, or colour - important for differentiating benign from potentially malignant lesions.
  • Ask about itching or tenderness - can indicate inflammation, irritation, or rarely, malignancy.
  • Ask about bleeding or crusting - concerning features that may suggest ulceration or malignancy.
  • Ask about recent trauma or irritation - can cause benign lesions to become inflamed or symptomatic.
  • Ask about sun exposure history - a significant risk factor for actinic keratoses and melanoma.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Benign skin lesion (including melanocytic naevi, epidermal cyst, dermatofibroma, seborrhoeic keratosis, actinic keratosis)"][/sc]
  • Check for asymmetry, irregular borders, varied colour, diameter >6mm, and evolution (ABCDEs) - crucial for melanoma screening.
  • Check for a "stuck-on" appearance, waxy texture, and horn cysts - characteristic of seborrhoeic keratoses.
  • Check for a firm, dimpling nodule when pinched - typical of a dermatofibroma.
  • Check for a rough, scaly patch on sun-exposed skin - indicative of actinic keratosis.
  • Check vital signs - generally normal in benign skin lesions, but may be relevant if infection or systemic illness is suspected.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Benign skin lesion (including melanocytic naevi, epidermal cyst, dermatofibroma, seborrhoeic keratosis, actinic keratosis)"][/sc]
  • Rapid change in size or appearance - particularly concerning for malignant transformation, especially in melanocytic lesions.
  • New lesion in adulthood with atypical features - warrants careful assessment to rule out melanoma.
  • Bleeding, ulceration, or persistent crusting - suggests a breach in the skin barrier, potentially due to malignancy or significant irritation.
  • Inflammation or pain without clear cause - can be a sign of underlying malignancy or infection.
  • Multiple or widespread actinic keratoses - indicates significant sun damage and increased risk of squamous cell carcinoma.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Benign skin lesion (including melanocytic naevi, epidermal cyst, dermatofibroma, seborrhoeic keratosis, actinic keratosis)"][/sc]
  • Older adults: May present with numerous seborrhoeic keratoses or actinic keratoses, sometimes making it challenging to identify a single concerning lesion amidst many benign ones.
  • Immunosuppressed patients: Have an increased risk of developing squamous cell carcinoma from actinic keratoses, and lesions may behave more aggressively.
  • Darker skin types: Melanocytic naevi may be less visible, and melanoma can present in acral (palms, soles, nail beds) or mucosal sites, which are often overlooked.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Benign skin lesion (including melanocytic naevi, epidermal cyst, dermatofibroma, seborrhoeic keratosis, actinic keratosis)"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected benign lesion: Clinical examination with dermoscopy is often sufficient for diagnosis.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Excisional biopsy with histological examination is the gold standard for definitive diagnosis and to rule out malignancy.
  • Imaging - Photography: Serial photography can be used to monitor suspicious lesions for changes over time.
  • Serial reassessment: For lesions with equivocal features, regular follow-up appointments to observe for changes are often recommended.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Benign skin lesion (including melanocytic naevi, epidermal cyst, dermatofibroma, seborrhoeic keratosis, actinic keratosis)"][/sc]
  • A 68-year-old male presents with a slowly enlarging, pigmented, waxy "stuck-on" lesion on his back that has been present for several years, occasionally itchy but not bleeding.
  • An elderly male with a chronic, stable, non-ulcerating, pigmented epidermal lesion, suggestive of a benign aetiology.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Benign skin lesion (including melanocytic naevi, epidermal cyst, dermatofibroma, seborrhoeic keratosis, actinic keratosis)"][/sc]
  • Melanoma - rapid change in size/colour, irregular borders, asymmetry.
  • Basal Cell Carcinoma - pearly nodule with rolled borders, telangiectasia, central ulceration.
  • Squamous Cell Carcinoma - rapidly growing, scaly, erythematous nodule, often with ulceration.
  • Dermatofibrosarcoma Protuberans - slow-growing, firm, nodular lesion, often with an atrophic plaque.
  • Atypical Naevus - shares some features with melanoma but lacks definitive malignant criteria.
  • Lentigo Maligna - flat, asymmetric, variegated pigmented macule on sun-damaged skin.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Benign skin lesion (including melanocytic naevi, epidermal cyst, dermatofibroma, seborrhoeic keratosis, actinic keratosis)"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Reassurance for clearly benign lesions. For suspicious lesions, prompt referral to dermatology for expert assessment and potential biopsy.
  • Definitive management: Excision for symptomatic benign lesions (e.g., irritated epidermal cysts, dermatofibromas) or for cosmetic reasons. Cryotherapy or curettage may be used for seborrhoeic keratoses or actinic keratoses.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Regular self-skin examination and professional skin checks for individuals with multiple moles or a history of sun damage.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Advise patients to seek urgent medical attention for any rapid changes in existing lesions or the appearance of new, concerning lesions.
  • Exam pharmacology reference (UKMLA/MRCGP): Topical fluorouracil or imiquimod may be considered for widespread actinic keratoses. Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Benign skin lesion (including melanocytic naevi, epidermal cyst, dermatofibroma, seborrhoeic keratosis, actinic keratosis)"][/sc]
  • Malignant transformation: Actinic keratoses can progress to squamous cell carcinoma, and rarely, melanocytic naevi can transform into melanoma, highlighting the importance of monitoring.
  • Inflammation and infection: Benign lesions, particularly epidermal cysts, can become inflamed or infected, leading to pain, redness, and discharge.
  • Cosmetic concern: Large or prominent benign lesions can cause significant distress or impact quality of life due to their appearance.
  • Bleeding or irritation: Lesions in areas of friction or trauma can become repeatedly irritated or bleed, causing discomfort and potential for secondary infection.
