Polymyositis and dermatomyositis [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Polymyositis and dermatomyositis"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Polymyositis and dermatomyositis"][/sc]
  • Polymyositis and dermatomyositis are chronic inflammatory myopathies characterized by muscle weakness and inflammation.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical features and initial diagnostic approach to inflammatory myopathies.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Polymyositis and dermatomyositis"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Polymyositis and dermatomyositis"][/sc]
  • Affects approximately 1 in 100,000 people in the UK, with a slight female predominance.
  • Peak incidence in adults aged 40-60 years, but can occur at any age.
  • Associated with increased risk in individuals with certain autoimmune conditions and malignancies.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Polymyositis and dermatomyositis"][/sc]
  • Trigger: Autoimmune response targeting muscle fibres (polymyositis) or muscle and skin (dermatomyositis).
  • Progression: Infiltration of muscle tissue by inflammatory cells, leading to myonecrosis and regeneration attempts.
  • Consequence: Progressive proximal muscle weakness, fatigue, and systemic manifestations.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Polymyositis and dermatomyositis"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Insidious onset of muscle weakness, particularly beyond several weeks, often mistaken for fatigue.
  • Progressive phase: Worsening weakness over months, impacting daily activities, with potential for skin manifestations in dermatomyositis.
  • Late phase: Chronic muscle damage, atrophy, and potential for organ involvement, particularly beyond several months to years.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Polymyositis and dermatomyositis"][/sc]
  • Ask about proximal muscle weakness - difficulty with activities like climbing stairs or raising arms.
  • Ask about skin changes (dermatomyositis) - Gottron's papules, heliotrope rash, V-sign, shawl sign.
  • Ask about dysphagia - involvement of pharyngeal muscles can lead to aspiration risk.
  • Ask about fatigue - a common and often debilitating symptom impacting quality of life.
  • Ask about arthralgia - joint pain can occur, though typically non-erosive.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Polymyositis and dermatomyositis"][/sc]
  • Check muscle strength - proximal muscle weakness (e.g., deltoids, hip flexors) is characteristic.
  • Check skin for characteristic rashes - Gottron's papules (over knuckles), heliotrope rash (periorbital oedema), V-sign, shawl sign.
  • Check for muscle tenderness or atrophy - may indicate active inflammation or chronic damage.
  • Check vital signs - assess for fever, tachycardia, or respiratory compromise if severe.
  • Check for interstitial lung disease signs - crackles on auscultation, clubbing.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Polymyositis and dermatomyositis"][/sc]
  • Rapidly progressive weakness - suggests aggressive disease or potential for acute respiratory failure.
  • Dysphagia or dysphonia - indicates pharyngeal or laryngeal muscle involvement, high aspiration risk.
  • Respiratory distress - diaphragmatic or intercostal muscle weakness, requiring urgent assessment.
  • New onset of skin lesions with muscle weakness - highly suggestive of dermatomyositis, often associated with malignancy.
  • Constitutional symptoms (e.g., weight loss, fever) - may indicate underlying malignancy or severe systemic inflammation.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Polymyositis and dermatomyositis"][/sc]
  • Amyopathic dermatomyositis: Characteristic skin findings without clinical muscle weakness, though subclinical myositis may be present.
  • Juvenile dermatomyositis: Presents in childhood, often with calcinosis and vasculitis, differing from adult forms.
  • Overlap syndromes: Co-occurrence with other connective tissue diseases (e.g., systemic sclerosis, lupus), complicating diagnosis.
  • Malignancy-associated myositis: Older adults with new onset myositis, particularly dermatomyositis, warrant screening for underlying cancer.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Polymyositis and dermatomyositis"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected polymyositis/dermatomyositis: Serum creatine kinase (CK) levels (often significantly elevated), ESR/CRP, autoantibody panel (ANA, anti-Jo-1, anti-Mi-2, anti-MDA5).
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Thyroid function tests, vitamin D levels, electromyography (EMG) to differentiate myopathic from neuropathic weakness.
  • Imaging - MRI: Muscle MRI can show oedema and inflammation, guiding biopsy site selection.
  • Imaging - Chest CT: For assessment of interstitial lung disease, especially with anti-MDA5 antibodies.
  • Biopsy - Muscle biopsy: Confirmatory test, showing inflammatory infiltrates and muscle fibre necrosis/regeneration.
  • Screening for malignancy: Age-appropriate cancer screening, particularly in dermatomyositis, including colonoscopy, mammography, and CT chest/abdomen/pelvis.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Polymyositis and dermatomyositis"][/sc]
  • A 55-year-old female presents with a 3-month history of progressive difficulty climbing stairs and raising her arms, accompanied by a reddish-purple rash around her eyes and over her knuckles.
  • Subacute, progressive, proximal muscle weakness with characteristic dermatological manifestations, suggestive of an inflammatory myopathy.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Polymyositis and dermatomyositis"][/sc]
  • Inclusion body myositis - typically affects older males, distal and asymmetric weakness, often involves quadriceps and forearm flexors.
  • Drug-induced myopathy (e.g., statins) - history of medication use, often resolves with drug cessation.
  • Muscular dystrophies - typically genetic, onset often in childhood or adolescence, family history.
  • Neuromuscular junction disorders (e.g., myasthenia gravis) - fluctuating weakness, fatigability, often ocular or bulbar symptoms.
  • Hypothyroidism - fatigue, muscle aches, but typically less severe weakness and distinct thyroid symptoms.
  • Polymyalgia rheumatica - older age, shoulder and hip girdle stiffness (not weakness), elevated ESR, good response to low-dose steroids.
  • Infectious myositis - acute onset, fever, recent infection, often viral.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Polymyositis and dermatomyositis"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Assess for dysphagia and respiratory compromise; consider hospital admission for severe cases.
  • Definitive management: High-dose corticosteroids (e.g., prednisolone) as first-line therapy, often combined with immunosuppressants (e.g., methotrexate, azathioprine) for steroid-sparing effect or refractory disease.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Regular assessment of muscle strength, CK levels, and pulmonary function; screening for malignancy in dermatomyositis.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Refer to rheumatology or neurology; educate on warning signs of respiratory compromise or severe dysphagia.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Polymyositis and dermatomyositis"][/sc]
  • Aspiration pneumonia: Due to pharyngeal muscle weakness, increasing risk of infection and respiratory compromise.
  • Interstitial lung disease: A significant cause of morbidity and mortality, particularly in certain autoantibody profiles (e.g., anti-Jo-1, anti-MDA5).
  • Cardiac involvement: Myocarditis, conduction abnormalities, or heart failure can occur, impacting prognosis.
  • Malignancy: Increased risk, especially with dermatomyositis, requiring vigilant screening.
  • Muscle atrophy and contractures: Chronic inflammation and damage can lead to irreversible muscle loss and joint immobility.
  • Corticosteroid-induced side effects: Long-term use can lead to osteoporosis, diabetes, and increased infection risk.
