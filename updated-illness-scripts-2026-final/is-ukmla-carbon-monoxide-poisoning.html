Carbon monoxide poisoning [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Carbon monoxide poisoning"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Carbon monoxide poisoning"][/sc]
  • A systemic toxicity resulting from the inhalation of carbon monoxide (CO), which binds to haemoglobin with high affinity, impairing oxygen transport and utilisation.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the sources, symptoms, signs, and immediate management of carbon monoxide poisoning.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Carbon monoxide poisoning"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Carbon monoxide poisoning"][/sc]
  • Carbon monoxide poisoning is a significant cause of accidental and intentional death in the UK, with hundreds of hospital admissions annually (ONS data).
  • Risk factors often include faulty heating appliances, blocked flues, or the use of portable generators in enclosed spaces, particularly during colder months.
  • Exposure can occur in various settings, including homes, workplaces, and vehicles, often affecting multiple individuals simultaneously.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Carbon monoxide poisoning"][/sc]
  • Trigger: Inhalation of carbon monoxide (CO), a colourless, odourless gas, often from incomplete combustion of carbon-containing fuels.
  • Progression: CO binds to haemoglobin with an affinity 200-250 times greater than oxygen, forming carboxyhaemoglobin (COHb), which reduces oxygen-carrying capacity and shifts the oxygen dissociation curve to the left, impairing oxygen release to tissues. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Carboxyhaemoglobin: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Carboxyhaemoglobin"][/sc]
  • Consequence: Cellular hypoxia and metabolic acidosis ensue, particularly affecting organs with high oxygen demand such as the brain and heart, leading to widespread organ dysfunction.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Carbon monoxide poisoning"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Symptoms typically develop within minutes to hours of exposure, depending on CO concentration and duration, often presenting as non-specific flu-like illness.
  • Progressive phase: As COHb levels rise, symptoms worsen over several hours, particularly beyond 6-12 hours of continuous exposure, leading to neurological and cardiovascular compromise.
  • Late phase: Delayed neurological sequelae, such as cognitive impairment, personality changes, and movement disorders, can manifest days to weeks after acute exposure, particularly in severe cases.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Carbon monoxide poisoning"][/sc]
  • Ask about headache - often described as dull, throbbing, and persistent, easily mistaken for tension headache or migraine.
  • Ask about nausea and vomiting - non-specific gastrointestinal symptoms that can mimic viral gastroenteritis.
  • Ask about dizziness and weakness - can lead to falls and impaired mobility, often attributed to fatigue or dehydration.
  • Ask about confusion or altered mental status - indicates cerebral hypoxia and can range from mild disorientation to coma.
  • Ask about chest pain - suggests myocardial ischaemia due to reduced oxygen delivery to the heart.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Carbon monoxide poisoning"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - often normal initially, but tachycardia, tachypnoea, and hypotension may develop in severe cases. Oxygen saturation by pulse oximetry is unreliable as it cannot differentiate COHb from oxyhaemoglobin.
  • Check neurological status - may reveal altered consciousness, focal neurological deficits, or seizures in severe poisoning.
