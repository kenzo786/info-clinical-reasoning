Hyposplenism/ splenectomy [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Hyposplenism/ splenectomy"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Hyposplenism/ splenectomy"][/sc]
  • A condition characterised by reduced or absent splenic function, often due to surgical removal (splenectomy) or congenital absence.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical implications and management strategies for patients with hyposplenism or post-splenectomy.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Hyposplenism/ splenectomy"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Hyposplenism/ splenectomy"][/sc]
  • Splenectomy is performed for various indications, including trauma, haematological disorders (e.g., idiopathic thrombocytopenic purpura, hereditary spherocytosis), and certain cancers.
  • The incidence of splenectomy for trauma has decreased with non-operative management, but elective splenectomy remains significant.
  • Risk factors for hyposplenism include autoimmune diseases, coeliac disease, and sickle cell disease, even without surgical removal of the spleen.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Hyposplenism/ splenectomy"][/sc]
  • Trigger: Loss of splenic function, either surgical (splenectomy) or functional (e.g., sickle cell disease, coeliac disease).
  • Progression: Impaired clearance of encapsulated bacteria, abnormal red blood cell morphology, and reduced immune surveillance.
  • Consequence: Increased susceptibility to overwhelming post-splenectomy infection (OPSI) and other immune-related complications.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Hyposplenism/ splenectomy"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Immediate post-splenectomy period, focusing on surgical recovery and initial vaccination.
  • Progressive phase: Lifelong risk of infection, particularly beyond the first few years, requiring ongoing vigilance and prophylactic measures.
  • Late phase: Potential for long-term complications such as pulmonary hypertension or thrombocytosis, necessitating regular monitoring.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Hyposplenism/ splenectomy"][/sc]
  • Ask about fever - often the first and sometimes only sign of serious infection in an asplenic patient.
  • Ask about malaise or fatigue - may indicate an underlying infection or other complication.
  • Ask about abdominal pain - could suggest an accessory spleen infarction or other acute abdominal pathology.
  • Ask about recent travel or exposure to illness - important for assessing infection risk.
  • Ask about vaccination status - crucial for assessing protection against encapsulated bacteria.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Hyposplenism/ splenectomy"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - fever, tachycardia, or hypotension may indicate sepsis.
  • Check for signs of infection - e.g., localised erythema, lymphadenopathy, or respiratory distress.
  • Check for abdominal tenderness or guarding - could suggest an acute abdomen.
  • Check for jaundice or pallor - may indicate haemolysis or anaemia.
  • Check for signs of thrombocytosis - e.g., peripheral ischaemia, although often asymptomatic.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Hyposplenism/ splenectomy"][/sc]
  • Sudden onset high fever: Suggests rapid progression of infection, potentially OPSI.
  • Hypotension or signs of shock: Indicates severe sepsis requiring immediate resuscitation.
  • Altered mental status: May signify severe infection with central nervous system involvement.
  • Petechial or purpuric rash: Highly concerning for meningococcal sepsis or other severe bacterial infections.
  • Rapidly deteriorating clinical condition: Points to a life-threatening infection in an immunocompromised patient.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Hyposplenism/ splenectomy"][/sc]
  • Older adults: May present with less pronounced fever or non-specific symptoms, making diagnosis challenging.
  • Immunosuppressed patients: Higher risk of severe and rapidly progressive infections, requiring a lower threshold for investigation and treatment.
  • Partial splenectomy/functional hyposplenism: Symptoms and signs may be less severe but still carry significant infection risk.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Hyposplenism/ splenectomy"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected infection: Full blood count (often shows thrombocytosis, Howell-Jolly bodies), blood cultures, C-reactive protein.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Liver function tests, renal function tests, urinalysis.
  • Imaging - Abdominal ultrasound: May confirm absence of spleen or identify accessory splenic tissue.
  • Serial reassessment: Close monitoring of vital signs and clinical status in suspected infection.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Hyposplenism/ splenectomy"][/sc]
  • A 45-year-old male, post-splenectomy for trauma 10 years ago, presents with sudden onset of high fever, rigors, and malaise for 12 hours. On examination, he is tachycardic and hypotensive.
  • An asplenic adult with acute febrile illness and signs of systemic inflammatory response, suggesting overwhelming infection.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Hyposplenism/ splenectomy"][/sc]
  • Overwhelming Post-Splenectomy Infection (OPSI) - rapid onset, severe sepsis, often due to encapsulated bacteria.
  • Viral infection (e.g., influenza, common cold) - typically less severe, self-limiting, but can be a trigger for bacterial superinfection.
  • Bacterial pneumonia - respiratory symptoms often prominent, may be a focus of sepsis.
  • Urinary tract infection - dysuria, frequency, suprapubic pain, but can also lead to urosepsis.
  • Malaria (if travel history) - cyclical fevers, chills, anaemia, hepatosplenomegaly (though spleen absent here).
  • Drug-induced fever - history of new medications, rash may be present.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Hyposplenism/ splenectomy"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Rapid assessment, broad-spectrum intravenous antibiotics (e.g., co-amoxiclav or ceftriaxone), fluid resuscitation, oxygen therapy if hypoxic.
  • Definitive management: Identification of causative organism via cultures, tailoring antibiotic therapy, management of complications.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Close observation in a high-dependency setting, regular review of clinical parameters and inflammatory markers.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Early involvement of critical care, clear instructions to the patient regarding symptoms of infection and when to seek urgent medical attention.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Hyposplenism/ splenectomy"][/sc]
  • Overwhelming Post-Splenectomy Infection (OPSI): Rapidly progressive and potentially fatal sepsis, often caused by encapsulated bacteria (e.g., Streptococcus pneumoniae, Haemophilus influenzae, Neisseria meningitidis) due to impaired splenic immune function.
  • Thrombocytosis: Elevated platelet count due to loss of splenic sequestration, increasing the risk of thrombotic events, particularly in the early post-splenectomy period.
  • Pulmonary hypertension: A long-term complication, mechanism not fully understood but thought to involve altered red blood cell dynamics and endothelial dysfunction.
  • Venous thromboembolism: Increased risk of deep vein thrombosis and pulmonary embolism, especially in the perioperative period and with underlying prothrombotic states.
  • Accessory spleen infarction: If present, an accessory spleen can undergo infarction, causing acute abdominal pain.
