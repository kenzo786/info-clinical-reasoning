Abscess [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Abscess"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Abscess"][/sc]
  • A localised collection of pus, often due to bacterial infection, within tissues or organs.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical presentation, common causes, and initial management principles of an abscess.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Abscess"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Abscess"][/sc]
  • Hospital admissions for abscesses, carbuncles, furuncles, and cellulitis in the UK almost doubled from 123 per 100,000 in 1998/1999 to 236 per 100,000 in 2010/2011 [1].
  • Approximately 10% of patients with a boil or abscess experience recurrence within 12 months, with risk factors including obesity, diabetes, younger age, smoking, and recent antibiotic use [1].
  • People who inject drugs (PWID) in urban areas like London have a high prevalence of abscesses and cellulitis [2].
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Abscess"][/sc]
  • An initial trigger, such as bacterial invasion through compromised skin or an obstructed duct, leads to a localised infection.
  • The body\"s immune response mobilises neutrophils and other inflammatory cells, forming pus which accumulates and becomes walled off by surrounding granulation tissue.
  • This expanding collection of pus creates a cavity, causing tissue destruction and local pressure effects, which can lead to systemic inflammation if not contained.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Abscess"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Initial bacterial inoculation and inflammatory response, often presenting as cellulitis or a tender, erythematous nodule, typically over a few days.
  • Progressive phase: As pus accumulates, the lesion becomes fluctuant and more painful, with systemic symptoms such as fever and malaise potentially developing, particularly beyond the first week.
  • Late phase: Resolution following drainage (spontaneous or surgical), with healing occurring over several weeks, though complications can prolong recovery.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Abscess"][/sc]
  • Ask about localised pain and tenderness - indicates inflammation and helps pinpoint the site of infection.
  • Ask about swelling, redness, and warmth - classic signs of inflammation (rubor, tumor, calor) suggesting an active infectious process.
  • Ask about discharge of pus or fluid - direct evidence of purulent material, confirming an abscess and potentially identifying the causative organism.
  • Ask about fever, chills, or general malaise - systemic inflammatory response, suggesting a more widespread infection or potential sepsis.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Abscess"][/sc]
  • Check for localised erythema, warmth, and swelling - these are cardinal signs of inflammation and infection.
  • Check for fluctuance on palpation - indicates a collection of fluid (pus) within the lesion, suggesting a mature abscess ready for drainage. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Fluctuance: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Fluctuance"][/sc]
  • Check for regional lymphadenopathy - suggests lymphatic spread of infection from the abscess site.
  • Check vital signs (temperature, heart rate, blood pressure) - fever and tachycardia may indicate systemic infection or sepsis, while hypotension suggests severe sepsis or septic shock.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Abscess"][/sc]
  • Systemic signs of sepsis: High fever, tachycardia, hypotension, or altered mental status, indicating widespread infection and potential organ dysfunction.
  • Rapidly spreading cellulitis or erythema: Suggests an aggressive infection that is not contained and may be progressing quickly.
  • Abscess in a dangerous area: Such as the face (e.g., periorbital, peritonsillar), neck, or near major vessels/nerves, implying risk of airway compromise, neurological deficit, or vascular complications.
  • Immunocompromised patient: Patients with diabetes, HIV, or on immunosuppressants may have blunted inflammatory responses and rapid progression of infection.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Abscess"][/sc]
  • Older adults: May present with non-specific symptoms such as fatigue, confusion, or general malaise, with absent or blunted fever and inflammatory signs, making diagnosis challenging.
  • Pregnancy: Abscesses can present atypically or in unusual locations (e.g., psoas abscess), and symptoms may be masked by physiological changes of pregnancy, delaying diagnosis.
  • Immunocompromised patients: (e.g., diabetes, HIV, chemotherapy) may have minimal inflammatory response, leading to less pronounced pain, redness, and swelling, and can develop unusual or rapidly progressive infections.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Abscess"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected Abscess: Clinical assessment (history and physical examination) is paramount. Blood tests including full blood count (leukocytosis), C-reactive protein (CRP), and erythrocyte sedimentation rate (ESR) to assess for systemic inflammation and infection.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Consider specific investigations based on the differential diagnosis (e.g., biopsy for suspected malignancy, specific cultures for unusual pathogens).
  • Imaging - Ultrasound: Often the first-line imaging for superficial soft tissue abscesses, useful for confirming the presence of a fluid collection and guiding drainage.
  • Imaging - CT scan: Preferred for deeper or intra-abdominal abscesses, providing detailed anatomical information and assessing the extent of infection.
  • Imaging - MRI: More sensitive than CT for certain locations (e.g., brain, spinal epidural abscess) and for delineating soft tissue involvement.
  • Serial reassessment: Regular clinical review and monitoring of inflammatory markers (CRP) to assess response to treatment and detect complications.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Abscess"][/sc]
  • A 45-year-old male presents with a 5-day history of a progressively painful, red, and swollen lump in his right axilla, associated with fevers and chills. On examination, the lesion is fluctuant and tender, with surrounding erythema.
  • Middle-aged male with acute, progressive, localised inflammatory mass and systemic symptoms, suggestive of a contained infection requiring intervention.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Abscess"][/sc]
  • Cellulitis - lacks central fluctuance and is a more diffuse infection of the dermis and subcutaneous tissue.
  • Furuncle (boil) - a deep folliculitis, typically smaller than an abscess and centred around a hair follicle.
  • Carbuncle - a cluster of interconnected furuncles, often with multiple drainage points.
  • Cyst (e.g., sebaceous cyst) - typically non-tender unless infected, and often present for a longer duration.
  • Lipoma - a benign fatty tumour, soft and mobile, not typically painful or inflamed.
  • Lymphadenitis - inflammation of a lymph node, which can be painful and swollen but is usually mobile and not fluctuant.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Abscess"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Analgesia for pain relief. Assessment for systemic infection (sepsis screening) and stabilisation if indicated.
  • Definitive management: Incision and drainage (I&D) of the abscess to evacuate pus and relieve pressure. This is the cornerstone of abscess management.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Regular wound care, including packing if appropriate, and monitoring for signs of healing or worsening infection. Follow-up to ensure complete resolution and address any complications.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Advise patients to seek urgent medical attention if symptoms worsen (e.g., increasing pain, spreading redness, new fever), or if new systemic symptoms develop.
  • Exam pharmacology reference (UKMLA/MRCGP): Consider adjunctive antibiotics (e.g., [sc name="jitl-medication" Q="Flucloxacillin use in Abscess" text="flucloxacillin"][/sc]) in cases of significant surrounding cellulitis, systemic inflammatory response, immunocompromise, or abscesses in specific anatomical locations. Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Abscess"][/sc]
  • Sepsis: Uncontrolled systemic inflammatory response to infection, leading to organ dysfunction and potentially life-threatening within days if untreated, aligning with red flag features.
  • Spread of infection: Local extension (e.g., cellulitis, osteomyelitis) or haematogenous dissemination to distant sites (e.g., endocarditis, brain abscess), which can occur if the abscess is not adequately contained or treated, particularly in the progressive phase.
  • Fistula formation: Chronic drainage tract from the abscess to a body surface or another organ, indicating persistent infection and requiring surgical intervention.
  • Scarring and disfigurement: Particularly with large or recurrent abscesses, leading to cosmetic concerns and potential functional impairment depending on the location.
  • Recurrence: Inadequate drainage, underlying predisposing factors (e.g., diabetes, immunosuppression), or persistent source of infection can lead to repeated abscess formation, often within weeks to months.
