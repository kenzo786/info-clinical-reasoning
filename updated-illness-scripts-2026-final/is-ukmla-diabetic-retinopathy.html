Diabetic retinopathy [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Diabetic retinopathy"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Diabetic retinopathy"][/sc]
  • Diabetic retinopathy is a microvascular complication of diabetes mellitus affecting the retina, caused by damage to the blood vessels.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the stages of diabetic retinopathy and the importance of early detection and management to prevent vision loss.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Diabetic retinopathy"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Diabetic retinopathy"][/sc]
  • Diabetic retinopathy is a leading cause of preventable sight loss in the working-age population in the UK.
  • Approximately one-third of people with diabetes have some form of diabetic retinopathy, with prevalence increasing with duration of diabetes and poor glycaemic control.
  • Risk factors include prolonged duration of diabetes, poor glycaemic control (high HbA1c), hypertension, dyslipidaemia, and pregnancy.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Diabetic retinopathy"][/sc]
  • Trigger: Chronic hyperglycaemia leads to damage of retinal microvasculature, causing pericyte loss, basement membrane thickening, and endothelial cell dysfunction.
  • Progression: This damage results in increased vascular permeability, microaneurysms, haemorrhages, and cotton wool spots (non-proliferative retinopathy). Ischaemia can trigger the release of vascular endothelial growth factor (VEGF). [sc name="jitl-query" Q="VEGF: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="VEGF"][/sc]
  • Consequence: VEGF induces neovascularisation (new, fragile blood vessel growth) on the retinal surface or optic disc (proliferative retinopathy), which can lead to vitreous haemorrhage and tractional retinal detachment.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Diabetic retinopathy"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Often asymptomatic, with microvascular changes detectable on retinal examination (e.g., microaneurysms, dot and blot haemorrhages). This can persist for years without significant visual impairment.
  • Progressive phase: Worsening microvascular damage leads to more extensive haemorrhages, hard exudates, cotton wool spots, and venous beading. Macular oedema may develop at any stage, causing visual distortion or blurring.
  • Late phase: Characterised by proliferative changes, including neovascularisation, vitreous haemorrhage, and potential tractional retinal detachment, leading to severe and irreversible vision loss if untreated.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Diabetic retinopathy"][/sc]
  • Ask about blurred vision - often insidious onset, may indicate macular oedema or vitreous haemorrhage.
  • Ask about floaters or cobwebs - sudden onset may suggest vitreous haemorrhage from proliferative disease.
  • Ask about sudden, painless vision loss - a red flag for vitreous haemorrhage or retinal detachment.
  • Ask about distorted vision (metamorphopsia) - particularly central vision, indicative of macular oedema.
  • Ask about difficulty with night vision or adapting to low light - can be an early non-specific symptom.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Diabetic retinopathy"][/sc]
  • Check visual acuity - may be reduced, especially if macular oedema or vitreous haemorrhage is present.
  • Check fundoscopy for microaneurysms, dot and blot haemorrhages, hard exudates, cotton wool spots - signs of non-proliferative diabetic retinopathy.
  • Check fundoscopy for neovascularisation (new vessels on disc or elsewhere), pre-retinal or vitreous haemorrhage - signs of proliferative diabetic retinopathy. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Neovascularisation in diabetic retinopathy: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Neovascularisation"][/sc]
  • Check for macular oedema - retinal thickening or hard exudates within one disc diameter of the fovea.
  • Check vital signs - blood pressure and HbA1c control are crucial systemic indicators.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Diabetic retinopathy"][/sc]
  • Sudden, painless vision loss: Suggests acute vitreous haemorrhage or retinal detachment, requiring urgent ophthalmological review.
  • New onset floaters or flashes: Can indicate vitreous haemorrhage or traction on the retina, particularly in the progressive phase.
  • Rapid decline in visual acuity: Implies significant macular oedema or progression to proliferative disease.
  • Red reflex abnormality: May indicate dense vitreous haemorrhage, obscuring retinal view.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Diabetic retinopathy"][/sc]
  • Pregnancy: Rapid progression of diabetic retinopathy can occur due to hormonal changes and metabolic shifts, necessitating more frequent screening. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Diabetic retinopathy in pregnancy: how does it differ and why does it matter clinically?" text="Diabetic retinopathy in pregnancy"][/sc]
  • Rapid glycaemic control: A sudden, significant drop in HbA1c can paradoxically worsen retinopathy temporarily, known as early worsening phenomenon.
  • Unilateral presentation: While typically bilateral, asymmetric disease can occur, sometimes masking progression in one eye.
  • Masked by cataract: Significant cataracts can obscure the view of the retina, making retinopathy assessment difficult until cataract surgery.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Diabetic retinopathy"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected diabetic retinopathy: Dilated fundoscopy (slit lamp biomicroscopy with Volk lens) by an experienced ophthalmologist or optometrist.
  • Imaging - Fundus photography: Standard for screening and monitoring, capturing detailed images of the retina.
  • Imaging - Optical Coherence Tomography (OCT): Essential for detecting and quantifying macular oedema, and assessing vitreoretinal interface. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Optical Coherence Tomography (OCT) in diabetic retinopathy: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="OCT"][/sc]
  • Imaging - Fluorescein angiography: Used to identify areas of capillary non-perfusion, microaneurysms, and neovascularisation, particularly in proliferative disease.
  • Severity/risk tools: Early Treatment Diabetic Retinopathy Study (ETDRS) classification for severity.
  • Serial reassessment: Regular follow-up examinations and imaging are crucial to monitor disease progression and treatment response.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Diabetic retinopathy"][/sc]
  • A 55-year-old male with a 15-year history of type 2 diabetes and poorly controlled HbA1c presents with gradually worsening blurred vision and new floaters in his right eye. Fundoscopy reveals extensive retinal haemorrhages, cotton wool spots, and new vessel formation on the optic disc.
  • Chronic microvascular damage with progressive retinal ischaemia, concerning for proliferative diabetic retinopathy with potential vitreous haemorrhage.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Diabetic retinopathy"][/sc]
  • Retinal vein occlusion: Presents with sudden, painless vision loss and retinal haemorrhages, but typically unilateral and not associated with systemic diabetes microvascular changes.
  • Hypertensive retinopathy: Retinal changes (e.g., arteriolar narrowing, AV nipping, cotton wool spots) due to chronic hypertension, but lacks specific microaneurysms and neovascularisation of diabetic retinopathy.
  • Age-related macular degeneration (AMD): Causes central vision loss, but typically in older non-diabetic individuals, with drusen and RPE changes rather than widespread microvascular pathology.
  • Posterior vitreous detachment (PVD): Causes sudden onset floaters, but usually without retinal haemorrhages or neovascularisation unless complicated by a retinal tear.
  • Retinal detachment (non-tractional): Presents with sudden vision loss, flashes, and floaters, often with a visual field defect, but the underlying cause is usually a retinal tear, not diabetic microvascular disease.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Diabetic retinopathy"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Recognition of vision-threatening retinopathy (e.g., proliferative disease, severe macular oedema) and urgent referral to ophthalmology. Optimisation of glycaemic control, blood pressure, and lipids.
  • Definitive management:
    • For proliferative diabetic retinopathy: Panretinal photocoagulation (PRP) laser treatment to ablate ischaemic retina and reduce VEGF drive. Intravitreal anti-VEGF injections (e.g., ranibizumab, aflibercept) may also be considered, particularly if macular oedema is present.
    • For diabetic macular oedema: Intravitreal anti-VEGF injections are often the first-line treatment. Macular laser (focal or grid) may be considered in specific cases.
    • Vitrectomy may be required for non-clearing vitreous haemorrhage or tractional retinal detachment.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Regular ophthalmological review is essential, with frequency determined by disease severity and treatment response.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Patients should be advised to seek urgent review for any sudden changes in vision, new floaters, or flashes.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Diabetic retinopathy"][/sc]
  • Vitreous haemorrhage: Bleeding into the vitreous cavity from fragile new vessels, leading to sudden, painless vision loss, particularly in the late phase of proliferative disease.
  • Tractional retinal detachment: Fibrovascular proliferation on the retinal surface contracts, pulling the retina away from the underlying choroid, resulting in severe and often irreversible vision loss.
  • Neovascular glaucoma: New vessels grow on the iris and in the anterior chamber angle, obstructing aqueous outflow and causing a painful rise in intraocular pressure.
  • Macular oedema: Fluid leakage from damaged capillaries at the macula, causing retinal thickening and central vision impairment, which can occur at any stage of retinopathy.
  • Permanent vision loss/blindness: The ultimate consequence of untreated or advanced diabetic retinopathy, particularly from extensive retinal detachment or severe macular damage.
