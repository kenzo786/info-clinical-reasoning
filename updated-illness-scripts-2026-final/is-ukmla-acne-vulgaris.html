Acne vulgaris [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Acne vulgaris"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Acne vulgaris"][/sc]
  • Acne vulgaris is a chronic inflammatory skin condition affecting pilosebaceous units, characterised by comedones, papules, pustules, nodules, and cysts.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical features, common presentations, and differential diagnoses of acne vulgaris.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Acne vulgaris"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Acne vulgaris"][/sc]
  • Acne vulgaris commonly affects adolescents, with an estimated prevalence of over 80% in those aged 12-24 years in the UK.
  • Risk factors include genetic predisposition, hormonal fluctuations (androgens), stress, and certain medications.
  • The condition can persist into adulthood, affecting approximately 5% of women and 1% of men over 25 years of age.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Acne vulgaris"][/sc]
  • Trigger: Increased sebum production and follicular hyperkeratinisation lead to obstruction of the pilosebaceous unit, forming microcomedones. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Sebum production: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Sebum production"][/sc]
  • Progression: Proliferation of Cutibacterium acnes (formerly Propionibacterium acnes) within the obstructed follicle, leading to inflammation.
  • Consequence: Rupture of the inflamed follicle, resulting in inflammatory lesions such as papules, pustules, nodules, and cysts.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Acne vulgaris"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Onset typically in early adolescence, presenting with comedones and mild inflammatory lesions, often lasting for several months.
  • Progressive phase: Untreated or severe cases may progress to more widespread and deeper inflammatory lesions, particularly beyond several months, leading to potential scarring.
  • Late phase: Resolution often occurs by late adolescence or early adulthood, though some individuals experience persistent or adult-onset acne, potentially with residual scarring or post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Acne vulgaris"][/sc]
  • Ask about skin lesions - to understand the primary complaint and assess the type and distribution of lesions.
  • Ask about pain or tenderness - to gauge the inflammatory component and impact on quality of life.
  • Ask about cosmetic concerns - to understand the psychological impact and guide treatment goals.
  • Ask about previous treatments and their efficacy - to inform current management strategies and avoid ineffective approaches.
  • Ask about menstrual cycle regularity and hirsutism in females - to screen for underlying hormonal conditions like PCOS.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Acne vulgaris"][/sc]
  • Check for comedones (blackheads and whiteheads) - indicative of follicular obstruction, a hallmark of acne. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Comedones: what are they and why do they matter clinically?" text="Comedones"][/sc]
  • Check for inflammatory lesions (papules, pustules, nodules, cysts) - to assess the severity and inflammatory burden of the condition.
  • Check for scarring (atrophic, hypertrophic, keloidal) and post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation - to evaluate long-term sequelae and guide management.
  • Check vital signs - typically normal in uncomplicated acne, but may be relevant if systemic symptoms are present or if considering isotretinoin.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Acne vulgaris"][/sc]
  • Sudden onset severe acne in pre-pubertal children - may indicate underlying endocrine abnormalities.
  • Acne associated with virilisation (hirsutism, irregular menses, deepening voice) - suggests hyperandrogenism requiring further investigation.
  • Acne fulminans - rare, severe ulcerative acne with systemic symptoms (fever, arthralgia), requiring urgent systemic treatment.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Acne vulgaris"][/sc]
  • Adult-onset acne: Often presents with inflammatory lesions predominantly on the jawline, chin, and neck in women, potentially linked to hormonal factors.
  • Acne mechanica: Caused by friction, pressure, or occlusion of the skin, such as from helmets, tight clothing, or musical instruments, leading to localised breakouts.
  • Drug-induced acne: Certain medications (e.g., corticosteroids, lithium, some anticonvulsants) can trigger or exacerbate acne-like eruptions, which may differ in morphology or distribution from typical acne vulgaris.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Acne vulgaris"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected acne vulgaris: Diagnosis is primarily clinical, based on the characteristic lesions. No routine laboratory tests are typically required.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Consider hormonal assays (e.g., testosterone, DHEAS, prolactin) if virilisation signs are present in females or if adult-onset acne is severe and unresponsive to conventional treatment.
  • Microbiology: Swabs for bacterial culture are generally not recommended unless there is suspicion of secondary bacterial infection (e.g., cellulitis) or folliculitis not responding to standard therapy.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Acne vulgaris"][/sc]
  • A 16-year-old female presents with a 6-month history of progressively worsening facial spots, including blackheads, whiteheads, and painful red bumps, primarily on her forehead and cheeks.
  • Adolescent female with chronic, progressive, inflammatory facial dermatosis.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Acne vulgaris"][/sc]
  • Rosacea - lacks comedones, often presents with erythema, telangiectasias, and papulopustules, typically affecting the central face.
  • Perioral dermatitis - small papules and pustules around the mouth, nose, and eyes, often sparing the vermilion border.
  • Folliculitis - inflammation of hair follicles, often monomorphic papules/pustules, can be bacterial or fungal.
  • Keratosis pilaris - follicular papules with surrounding erythema, typically on the upper arms and thighs, not associated with comedones or deep cysts.
  • Drug-induced acneiform eruption - sudden onset, monomorphic lesions, often on the trunk, history of new medication.
  • Hidradenitis suppurativa - recurrent abscesses, sinus tracts, and scarring in intertriginous areas, not typically on the face.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Acne vulgaris"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Provide education on skin care, avoid harsh scrubbing, and manage expectations regarding treatment duration.
  • Definitive management: Topical retinoids (e.g., adapalene), topical antimicrobials (e.g., benzoyl peroxide, clindamycin), oral antibiotics (e.g., doxycycline, lymecycline), and oral isotretinoin for severe or resistant cases.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Regular review to assess treatment response, manage side effects, and adjust therapy as needed. Psychological support may be considered.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Refer to dermatology for severe acne, acne unresponsive to primary care management, or if significant scarring or psychological distress is present.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Acne vulgaris"][/sc]
  • Scarring: Can be atrophic (ice-pick, boxcar, rolling) or hypertrophic/keloidal, resulting from severe inflammation and tissue destruction, significantly impacting self-esteem.
  • Post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation (PIH): Darkening of the skin at sites of previous acne lesions, particularly common in individuals with darker skin types, which can persist for months to years.
  • Psychological distress: Acne can lead to significant anxiety, depression, social isolation, and reduced quality of life, especially in severe or persistent cases.
  • Acne excoriée: A psychological condition where individuals compulsively pick and scratch at acne lesions, leading to excoriations and secondary infections.
