Visual field defects [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Visual field defects"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Visual field defects"][/sc]
  • A visual field defect is a loss of part of the usual field of vision, occurring when any part of the complex visual pathway from the eye to the brain's occipital lobe is damaged by disease or injury.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise and interpret visual field defects as indicators of underlying ocular or neurological pathology.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Visual field defects"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Visual field defects"][/sc]
  • In England, large-scale data from glaucoma clinics indicate an association between socioeconomic deprivation and the severity of glaucoma at presentation, with 18% (293/1608) of patients in the most deprived decile presenting with advanced visual field loss compared to 11% (771/6929) in the least deprived decile [1].
  • Risk factors for visual field deterioration in glaucoma include bilateral disease, higher intraocular pressure, and disc haemorrhage [2].
  • Vigabatrin-associated visual field loss (VAVFL) occurs in approximately 45% of exposed individuals, and once established, is generally accepted to be permanent [3].
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Visual field defects"][/sc]
  • Trigger: Damage or dysfunction to any part of the visual pathway, including the retina, optic nerve, optic chiasm, or visual cortex, often due to conditions such as glaucoma, stroke, or compressive lesions.
  • Progression: This damage leads to impaired neural signal transmission, axonal degeneration, or nerve fibre layer thinning, depending on the aetiology.
  • Consequence: The disruption of visual information processing results in characteristic patterns of visual field loss, corresponding to the location and extent of the pathology.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Visual field defects"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Visual field defects may be asymptomatic or subtle, often detected incidentally during routine eye examinations, particularly in conditions like early glaucoma.
  • Progressive phase: Defects can gradually worsen over months to years, with increasing area and density of visual field loss, as seen in advancing neurodegenerative conditions or uncontrolled glaucoma.
  • Late phase: Severe and extensive visual field loss can lead to significant functional impairment and legal blindness, particularly beyond several years of unmanaged progression.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Visual field defects"][/sc]
  • Ask about bumping into objects - indicates loss of peripheral vision, impacting mobility and safety.
  • Ask about difficulty reading - suggests central visual field loss, affecting tasks requiring fine visual acuity.
  • Ask about problems with balance or depth perception - can be a consequence of visual field loss, increasing fall risk.
  • Ask about a progressive expansion of a shadow or curtain over the visual field - characteristic of retinal detachment or other acute ocular pathologies requiring urgent assessment.
  • Ask about objects appearing crooked or distorted - may indicate macular involvement, crucial for differentiating from optic nerve or pathway lesions.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Visual field defects"][/sc]
  • Check visual field confrontation testing - to identify gross visual field deficits and their patterns (e.g., homonymous hemianopsia, quadrantanopia).
  • Check fundoscopy - to assess for optic disc pallor, oedema, or cupping, which can indicate optic nerve pathology or raised intracranial pressure.
  • Check pupillary reflexes - to detect relative afferent pupillary defect (RAPD), suggesting significant unilateral optic nerve dysfunction.
  • Check vital signs - to identify systemic abnormalities such as hypertension (stroke risk), or signs of inflammation (e.g., in temporal arteritis).
  • Check visual acuity and colour vision - to assess for associated visual impairment and optic nerve dysfunction (e.g., colour desaturation).
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Visual field defects"][/sc]
  • Sudden vision loss - requires urgent investigation to identify and manage acute pathologies such as retinal detachment, optic neuropathy, or stroke.
  • Headache with vision loss - may indicate a neurological cause such as optic neuritis, giant cell arteritis, or a compressive lesion requiring prompt neuroimaging.
  • Visual field defect respecting the vertical midline - strongly suggests a post-chiasmal lesion in the visual pathway, often due to stroke or tumour.
  • Sudden onset of flashes and floaters with a 'curtain' sensation - highly suggestive of retinal detachment, necessitating immediate ophthalmological assessment.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Visual field defects"][/sc]
  • Older adults: Visual field dependence can be exacerbated in older adults with central field loss and dense binocular scotomata, impacting daily functioning and increasing fall risk [4].
  • Brain tumours: Visual field defects can be the presenting symptom of brain tumours, particularly those compressing the optic apparatus, and may present atypically without other neurological signs [5].
  • Functional visual loss: Patients may present with non-organic visual field defects, where no underlying organic pathology is found, often requiring careful history taking and examination to differentiate from true organic causes.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Visual field defects"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected visual field defects: Formal perimetry (e.g., Humphrey Visual Field Analyzer) to precisely map the extent and pattern of visual field loss.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Comprehensive ophthalmic examination, including fundoscopy, to rule out ocular causes such as retinal detachment or optic neuropathy.
  • Imaging - MRI brain/orbits: Indicated for suspected neurological causes (e.g., pituitary adenoma, stroke, multiple sclerosis) to visualise lesions along the visual pathway. CT may be used as a first-line imaging modality in acute settings or trauma.
  • Serial reassessment: Regular visual field testing is crucial for monitoring progression of conditions like glaucoma or assessing response to treatment for neurological causes.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Visual field defects"][/sc]
  • A 68-year-old male presents with a 3-month history of gradually worsening difficulty seeing objects in his left peripheral vision, often bumping into doorframes on his left side. He denies pain, redness, or flashes of light. His medical history includes hypertension and type 2 diabetes.
  • An elderly male with chronic comorbidities presenting with insidious, progressive, unilateral peripheral visual field loss, suggestive of a neurological or chronic ocular pathology.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Visual field defects"][/sc]
  • Glaucoma - progressive peripheral visual field loss with characteristic optic disc changes.
  • Macular degeneration - central vision loss, metamorphopsia, affecting fine visual acuity.
  • Retinal detachment - sudden onset of flashes, floaters, and a 'curtain' sensation, typically unilateral.
  • Optic neuritis - acute, painful, unilateral vision loss, often associated with demyelinating disease.
  • Stroke (occipital lobe) - sudden onset homonymous hemianopsia, often with other neurological deficits.
  • Pituitary adenoma - gradual bitemporal hemianopsia due to compression of the optic chiasm, potentially with endocrine symptoms.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Visual field defects"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Address any acute, life-threatening underlying causes such as stroke (e.g., thrombolysis if indicated) or giant cell arteritis (e.g., high-dose systemic corticosteroids).
  • Definitive management: Treatment is directed at the underlying cause. This may include medical management (e.g., intraocular pressure-lowering drops for glaucoma, immunosuppression for inflammatory conditions), surgical intervention (e.g., tumour resection, retinal reattachment), or supportive care.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Regular visual field testing and clinical review are essential to monitor disease progression, treatment efficacy, and detect new or worsening defects.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Patients should be advised to seek urgent medical attention for sudden worsening of vision, new neurological symptoms, or severe headache.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Visual field defects"][/sc]
  • Functional impairment: Visual field loss, particularly in the peripheral or central fields, can significantly impair daily activities such as reading, driving, and mobility, leading to reduced quality of life and independence.
  • Increased risk of falls: Patients with visual field defects, especially older adults, have a higher propensity for falls due to reduced spatial awareness and difficulty navigating obstacles [4].
  • Psychological impact: Chronic visual field loss can lead to significant psychological distress, including anxiety, depression, and social isolation, impacting overall well-being.
  • Permanent vision loss/blindness: If the underlying cause of the visual field defect is not identified and managed effectively, progressive damage to the visual pathway can result in irreversible and profound vision loss or legal blindness.
  • Road traffic accidents: Undiagnosed or unmanaged visual field defects can compromise driving safety, increasing the risk of road traffic accidents due to impaired hazard perception.
