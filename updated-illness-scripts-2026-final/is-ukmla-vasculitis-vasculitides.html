Vasculitis/ vasculitides [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Vasculitis/ vasculitides"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Vasculitis/ vasculitides"][/sc]
  • Vasculitis refers to inflammation of blood vessels, which can affect any organ system and present with a wide range of clinical manifestations.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the varied presentations of vasculitis and understand the importance of early diagnosis and management to prevent organ damage.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Vasculitis/ vasculitides"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Vasculitis/ vasculitides"][/sc]
  • The incidence of primary systemic vasculitides in the UK varies by type, with ANCA-associated vasculitis (AAV) having an estimated incidence of 10-20 per million per year.
  • Risk factors include genetic predisposition (e.g., HLA-DRB1*04 in GPA), environmental triggers (e.g., silica exposure, infections), and certain medications.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Vasculitis/ vasculitides"][/sc]
  • Triggering event (e.g., infection, drug exposure) initiates an immune response, often involving autoantibodies like ANCA, or immune complex deposition.
  • Progression involves immune cell infiltration and activation within the vessel wall, leading to inflammation and damage to endothelial cells.
  • Consequence is vessel narrowing, occlusion, or rupture, resulting in ischaemia, infarction, haemorrhage, and organ dysfunction depending on the size and location of affected vessels.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Vasculitis/ vasculitides"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Non-specific systemic symptoms such as fever, malaise, and weight loss, often preceding organ-specific manifestations by weeks.
  • Progressive phase: Rapid development of organ-specific symptoms, such as rapidly progressive glomerulonephritis or mononeuritis multiplex, particularly beyond 2-4 weeks without treatment.
  • Late phase: Chronic organ damage and potential for relapses, requiring long-term immunosuppression and monitoring, particularly beyond 6 months from diagnosis.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Vasculitis/ vasculitides"][/sc]
  • Ask about constitutional symptoms (fever, weight loss, fatigue) - these are common but non-specific, indicating systemic inflammation.
  • Ask about new onset headache, visual disturbance, or jaw claudication - may indicate giant cell arteritis, requiring urgent assessment.
  • Ask about skin rashes (palpable purpura, livedo reticularis, ulcers) - these are direct manifestations of cutaneous vasculitis and can guide diagnosis.
  • Ask about muscle weakness, numbness, or tingling - suggestive of peripheral neuropathy due to vasa nervorum involvement.
  • Ask about haemoptysis, dyspnoea, or pleuritic chest pain - may indicate pulmonary haemorrhage or capillaritis.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Vasculitis/ vasculitides"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - fever, tachycardia, and hypertension are common, indicating systemic illness and potential renal involvement.
  • Check for palpable purpura, skin ulcers, or digital ischaemia - direct evidence of small or medium vessel vasculitis.
  • Check for tenderness or reduced pulses in temporal arteries - highly suggestive of giant cell arteritis.
  • Check for mononeuritis multiplex (e.g., foot drop, wrist drop) - indicates nerve ischaemia due to vasculitis.
  • Check for oedema, especially facial or lower limb - may indicate renal dysfunction or cardiac involvement.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Vasculitis/ vasculitides"][/sc]
  • Rapidly progressive glomerulonephritis - indicates urgent need for immunosuppression to preserve renal function.
  • Pulmonary haemorrhage - life-threatening complication requiring immediate intensive care and treatment.
  • Acute visual loss - in suspected giant cell arteritis, mandates immediate high-dose steroids to prevent permanent blindness.
  • New onset neurological deficits - suggests central nervous system involvement, requiring prompt investigation and management.
  • Digital ischaemia with impending gangrene - indicates severe peripheral vascular involvement, potentially requiring surgical intervention.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Vasculitis/ vasculitides"][/sc]
  • Older adults: May present with non-specific symptoms, making diagnosis challenging; consider vasculitis in unexplained constitutional symptoms.
  • Children: Kawasaki disease is a common vasculitis in children, presenting with fever, rash, and lymphadenopathy; early recognition prevents coronary artery aneurysms.
  • Immunosuppressed patients: Vasculitis may be masked or atypical due to altered immune response, requiring a high index of suspicion.
  • Drug-induced vasculitis: Can mimic primary vasculitis, requiring careful drug history and withdrawal of the offending agent.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Vasculitis/ vasculitides"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected vasculitis: Full blood count (anaemia, thrombocytosis), ESR/CRP (inflammation), urea and electrolytes (renal function), liver function tests, urinalysis (haematuria, proteinuria), ANCA (MPO/PR3), complement levels (C3/C4), cryoglobulins.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Blood cultures (sepsis), viral serology (Hepatitis B/C, HIV), autoantibodies (ANA, RF, anti-CCP).
  • Imaging - Chest X-ray/CT: To assess for pulmonary involvement (e.g., infiltrates, nodules, haemorrhage).
  • Imaging - MRI/MRA: For CNS vasculitis or large vessel involvement.
  • Biopsy: Gold standard for diagnosis (e.g., skin, kidney, nerve, temporal artery).
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Vasculitis/ vasculitides"][/sc]
  • A 68-year-old female presents with a 3-week history of malaise, weight loss, new-onset headache, and jaw claudication, with examination revealing a tender, non-pulsatile right temporal artery.
  • An elderly female with subacute systemic inflammatory symptoms and focal neurological signs, concerning for large vessel vasculitis.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Vasculitis/ vasculitides"][/sc]
  • Infection (e.g., endocarditis, sepsis) - often lacks specific organ involvement patterns of vasculitis.
  • Malignancy (e.g., lymphoma, solid tumours) - can cause constitutional symptoms and elevated inflammatory markers, but usually without vasculitic rash or specific organ damage patterns.
  • Connective tissue diseases (e.g., SLE, rheumatoid arthritis) - may have overlapping features but typically have distinct autoantibody profiles and organ involvement.
  • Drug reactions - careful drug history is crucial to differentiate from primary vasculitis.
  • Atherosclerosis - can cause ischaemic symptoms but typically affects larger vessels and lacks systemic inflammatory features.
  • Embolic phenomena - acute onset of symptoms, often with a clear source, unlike the more subacute presentation of vasculitis.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Vasculitis/ vasculitides"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Stabilise life-threatening organ involvement (e.g., pulmonary haemorrhage, rapidly progressive glomerulonephritis), initiate high-dose glucocorticoids.
  • Definitive management: Induction of remission with immunosuppressants (e.g., cyclophosphamide, rituximab) followed by maintenance therapy (e.g., azathioprine, methotrexate).
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Regular clinical assessment, inflammatory markers, and organ function tests to detect relapse or treatment side effects.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Advise patients on symptoms of relapse or complications, and provide clear instructions for seeking urgent medical attention.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Vasculitis/ vasculitides"][/sc]
  • Organ damage: Due to chronic inflammation and ischaemia, leading to renal failure, lung fibrosis, or peripheral neuropathy, particularly if diagnosis and treatment are delayed beyond weeks.
  • Relapse: Recurrence of disease activity, often triggered by reduction in immunosuppression or intercurrent infection, requiring re-induction therapy.
  • Infection: Increased risk due to immunosuppressive therapy, requiring vigilance for opportunistic infections.
  • Cardiovascular events: Accelerated atherosclerosis and increased risk of myocardial infarction and stroke, especially in long-standing disease.
  • Malignancy: Increased risk of certain cancers (e.g., bladder cancer with cyclophosphamide), necessitating long-term surveillance.
