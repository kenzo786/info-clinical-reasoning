Central retinal vein occlusion (Eye) [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Central retinal vein occlusion (Eye)"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Central retinal vein occlusion (Eye)"][/sc]
  • Central retinal vein occlusion (CRVO) is a blockage of the main retinal vein, typically due to thrombus formation, leading to impaired venous drainage from the retina.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical features and risk factors for central retinal vein occlusion, differentiate it from other causes of sudden vision loss, and understand the principles of initial management and referral.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Central retinal vein occlusion (Eye)"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Central retinal vein occlusion (Eye)"][/sc]
  • Retinal vein occlusion (RVO) is the second most common retinal vascular disorder, affecting approximately 0.40% of the population, with CRVO accounting for about 0.08% globally.
  • Typically affects individuals over 65 years, with a slightly higher incidence in men for RVO overall.
  • Key risk factors include systemic hypertension, glaucoma, diabetes mellitus, hyperlipidaemia, and hyperviscosity syndromes.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Central retinal vein occlusion (Eye)"][/sc]
  • Thrombus formation within the central retinal vein, often at the level of the lamina cribrosa, leads to obstruction of venous outflow from the retina.
  • Increased intraluminal pressure and capillary non-perfusion result in retinal haemorrhages, oedema, and ischaemia, causing damage to retinal cells. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Retinal ischaemia: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Retinal ischaemia"][/sc]
  • Chronic ischaemia can trigger neovascularisation and macular oedema, leading to severe and potentially irreversible vision loss. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Macular oedema: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Macular oedema"][/sc]
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Central retinal vein occlusion (Eye)"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Sudden, painless unilateral vision loss, often noticed upon waking, typically developing over hours to a few days.
  • Progressive phase: Visual acuity may fluctuate or further deteriorate over weeks to months due to persistent macular oedema or development of ischaemic complications.
  • Late phase: Potential for chronic visual impairment, with risks of neovascular glaucoma or vitreous haemorrhage particularly beyond 3-6 months if ischaemia is significant and untreated.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Central retinal vein occlusion (Eye)"][/sc]
  • Ask about sudden, painless loss of vision in one eye - this is the hallmark symptom and helps differentiate from painful conditions like acute glaucoma or optic neuritis.
  • Ask about blurred or distorted vision - indicative of macular oedema, a common consequence of CRVO affecting central vision.
  • Ask about floaters or flashes of light - may suggest vitreous haemorrhage or retinal detachment, particularly in ischaemic CRVO with neovascularisation.
  • Ask about any preceding transient visual obscurations - could indicate impending vascular event, though less common than in carotid disease.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Central retinal vein occlusion (Eye)"][/sc]
  • Check visual acuity - typically reduced, ranging from mild blurring to no perception of light, depending on the severity and macular involvement.
  • Check pupillary reflexes - a relative afferent pupillary defect (RAPD) may be present in ischaemic CRVO, indicating significant optic nerve dysfunction. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Relative afferent pupillary defect: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="RAPD"][/sc]
  • Check fundoscopy - classic findings include widespread retinal haemorrhages (flame-shaped and dot-blot) in all four quadrants, dilated and tortuous retinal veins, and optic disc oedema.
  • Check intraocular pressure - may be elevated, particularly if secondary neovascular glaucoma develops.
  • Check vital signs - systemic hypertension is a common comorbidity and risk factor.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Central retinal vein occlusion (Eye)"][/sc]
  • Sudden, profound vision loss: Suggests acute ischaemic event or macular involvement, requiring urgent ophthalmological assessment to preserve vision.
  • Presence of a relative afferent pupillary defect (RAPD): Indicates significant retinal ischaemia and a higher risk of neovascular complications, necessitating prompt management.
  • New onset ocular pain or redness: May signal the development of neovascular glaucoma, a severe complication requiring immediate intervention to prevent irreversible vision loss.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Central retinal vein occlusion (Eye)"][/sc]
  • Non-ischaemic CRVO: More common and less severe, with better visual prognosis and less risk of neovascularisation compared to ischaemic CRVO.
  • Younger patients: CRVO in individuals under 50 years may suggest underlying thrombophilia, vasculitis, or other systemic conditions, warranting a more extensive systemic workup. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Thrombophilia: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Thrombophilia"][/sc]
  • Hemi-retinal vein occlusion: Involves only a portion of the retina, presenting with visual field defects rather than diffuse vision loss, but with similar underlying pathophysiology.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Central retinal vein occlusion (Eye)"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected CRVO: Comprehensive ophthalmological examination including visual acuity, pupillary assessment, slit-lamp biomicroscopy, and dilated fundoscopy.
  • Imaging - Optical Coherence Tomography (OCT): Essential for assessing macular oedema and guiding anti-VEGF treatment decisions.
  • Imaging - Fluorescein Angiography (FA): Used to differentiate ischaemic from non-ischaemic CRVO, identify areas of capillary non-perfusion, and detect neovascularisation. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Fluorescein angiography: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Fluorescein Angiography"][/sc]
  • Systemic workup: Blood pressure measurement, fasting glucose/HbA1c, lipid profile, full blood count, and consideration of thrombophilia screen in younger patients.

  • Elderly male with acute, unilateral, painless vision loss and diffuse retinal haemorrhages, concerning for central retinal vein occlusion.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Central retinal vein occlusion (Eye)"][/sc]
  • Branch Retinal Vein Occlusion (BRVO) - haemorrhages and oedema are confined to a sector of the retina, not all four quadrants.
  • Central Retinal Artery Occlusion (CRAO) - presents with profound, sudden vision loss and a cherry-red spot on fundoscopy, rather than diffuse haemorrhages.
  • Vitreous Haemorrhage - sudden vision loss with floaters, but fundal view is often obscured, and no specific retinal vascular changes are seen.
  • Retinal Detachment - typically presents with flashes, floaters, and a 'curtain' coming across vision, with specific fundoscopic signs of detachment.
  • Diabetic Retinopathy - can cause vision loss due to macular oedema or vitreous haemorrhage, but usually has a more gradual onset and history of diabetes. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Diabetic retinopathy: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Diabetic retinopathy"][/sc]
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Central retinal vein occlusion (Eye)"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Urgent ophthalmological referral for assessment and confirmation of diagnosis, particularly to differentiate ischaemic from non-ischaemic CRVO.
  • Definitive management: Often involves intravitreal anti-VEGF injections (e.g., ranibizumab, aflibercept) to reduce macular oedema and improve visual acuity.
  • Adjunctive management: Panretinal photocoagulation (PRP) may be considered for neovascularisation in ischaemic CRVO to prevent vitreous haemorrhage or neovascular glaucoma.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Regular ophthalmological review is essential to monitor macular oedema, neovascularisation, and intraocular pressure.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Patients should be advised to seek immediate medical attention for any sudden worsening of vision, new pain, or redness.
  • Exam pharmacology reference (UKMLA/MRCGP): Intravitreal anti-VEGF agents [sc name="jitl-medication" Q="Anti-VEGF mechanism in CRVO" text="(e.g., ranibizumab, aflibercept)"][/sc] are commonly used to inhibit vascular endothelial growth factor, thereby reducing vascular permeability and macular oedema. Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Central retinal vein occlusion (Eye)"][/sc]
  • Chronic macular oedema: Persistent fluid accumulation at the macula, leading to ongoing visual impairment if not adequately treated, particularly beyond the initial weeks.
  • Neovascular glaucoma: Development of abnormal blood vessels in the iris and angle of the eye, leading to a severe and painful rise in intraocular pressure, typically occurring months after an ischaemic CRVO.
  • Vitreous haemorrhage: Bleeding into the vitreous cavity from fragile new blood vessels, causing sudden, profound vision loss and floaters, often a late complication of ischaemic CRVO.
  • Retinal detachment: Can occur secondary to vitreous haemorrhage or traction from neovascular membranes, leading to severe and potentially irreversible vision loss, particularly in the late phase.
