Self-harm [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Self-harm"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Self-harm"][/sc]
  • Self-harm is defined as any act of self-poisoning or self-injury carried out by an individual, irrespective of the apparent motivation.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise self-harm as a complex behaviour often linked to underlying mental health conditions, requiring sensitive assessment and management.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Self-harm"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Self-harm"][/sc]
  • Self-harm is a significant public health concern in the UK, with rates particularly high among young people, especially females aged 16-24.
  • Risk factors include a history of mental health disorders (e.g., depression, anxiety, personality disorders), substance misuse, social isolation, and adverse childhood experiences.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Self-harm"][/sc]
  • Trigger: Overwhelming emotional distress, often stemming from complex psychological factors or acute stressors, leads to intense negative affect.
  • Progression: Self-harm acts as a maladaptive coping mechanism, providing temporary relief from emotional pain or a sense of control, reinforcing the behaviour.
  • Consequence: The behaviour can escalate in severity and frequency, leading to physical injury, increased psychological distress, and a higher risk of suicide. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Emotional dysregulation: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Emotional dysregulation"][/sc]
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Self-harm"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Often characterised by acute emotional distress or a precipitating event, leading to an impulsive or planned act of self-harm.
  • Progressive phase: Without effective intervention, self-harm can become a recurrent coping strategy, particularly beyond weeks, with increasing frequency or severity.
  • Late phase: Chronic self-harm may indicate entrenched psychological difficulties, often associated with personality disorders and a heightened risk of future suicide attempts.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Self-harm"][/sc]
  • Ask about feelings of hopelessness or worthlessness - these are core features of depression often associated with self-harm.
  • Ask about intense emotional pain or distress - understanding the underlying affect helps in identifying triggers and motivations.
  • Ask about previous episodes of self-harm or suicidal ideation - a strong predictor of future risk and indicates a pattern of behaviour.
  • Ask about coping mechanisms currently employed - to identify both adaptive and maladaptive strategies.
  • Ask about social isolation or lack of support - these factors can exacerbate distress and reduce protective factors.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Self-harm"][/sc]
  • Check for visible injuries (e.g., cuts, burns, ligatures) - these are direct evidence of self-harm and indicate the method used.
  • Check for signs of neglect or poor self-care - often co-occurs with severe mental distress and can indicate chronicity.
  • Check vital signs (heart rate, blood pressure, respiratory rate, temperature) - to assess for physiological compromise, especially in cases of self-poisoning or significant injury.
  • Check for signs of intoxication (e.g., alcohol, illicit drugs) - substance use can disinhibit and increase the risk of self-harm.
  • Check for signs of agitation or restlessness - may indicate acute distress or withdrawal. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Borderline personality disorder: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Emotional lability"][/sc]
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Self-harm"][/sc]
  • Suicidal intent: Any explicit or implicit statement of intent to end life, indicating a high immediate risk.
  • Lethality of method: Use of highly dangerous methods (e.g., jumping from height, significant overdose) suggests serious intent and higher risk of fatality.
  • Lack of protective factors: Absence of social support, future plans, or reasons for living increases vulnerability.
  • Persistent ideation: Ongoing thoughts of self-harm or suicide, particularly beyond the immediate aftermath of an event, warrants urgent assessment.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Self-harm"][/sc]
  • Older adults: Self-harm may present as medication non-adherence or refusal to eat, often overlooked due to assumptions about age-related decline.
  • Learning disabilities: Individuals may express distress through challenging behaviours that are not immediately recognised as self-harm, requiring careful interpretation.
  • Culturally specific presentations: Some cultures may have unique expressions of distress or self-harm that differ from Western norms, necessitating cultural sensitivity in assessment.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Self-harm"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected self-poisoning: Full blood count, urea and electrolytes, liver function tests, paracetamol and salicylate levels (if relevant), and toxicology screen to assess for specific substances and organ damage.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Consider thyroid function tests if mood disturbance is prominent, or B12/folate levels if nutritional deficiencies are suspected in chronic cases.
  • Serial reassessment: Regular mental state examinations and risk assessments are crucial, particularly in the days following an episode, to monitor for changes in intent or protective factors.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Self-harm"][/sc]
  • A 22-year-old female presents to the emergency department after intentionally cutting her forearm with a razor blade following a relationship breakdown, reporting overwhelming sadness and a desire to escape her feelings.
  • A young, distressed female with acute self-inflicted injury, exhibiting maladaptive coping mechanisms in response to significant psychosocial stressors.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Self-harm"][/sc]
  • Accidental injury - distinguishing feature: lack of intent or planning, often inconsistent with reported mechanism.
  • Factitious disorder - distinguishing feature: intentional production of symptoms for primary gain (e.g., attention), often with a deceptive element.
  • Malingering - distinguishing feature: intentional production of symptoms for secondary gain (e.g., avoiding work, obtaining drugs).
  • Psychotic episode - distinguishing feature: self-harm may occur in response to command hallucinations or delusions, requiring specific psychiatric assessment.
  • Substance intoxication/withdrawal - distinguishing feature: altered mental state directly attributable to substance use, which may lead to impulsive self-harm.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Self-harm"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Ensure physical safety, assess and manage any injuries or overdose, conduct a comprehensive risk assessment for suicide, and provide a safe environment.
  • Definitive management: Focus on psychological interventions such as dialectical behaviour therapy (DBT) or cognitive behavioural therapy (CBT), addressing underlying mental health conditions, and developing adaptive coping strategies.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Close outpatient follow-up with mental health services, regular risk assessments, and engagement with social support networks.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Inpatient psychiatric admission may be required for high-risk individuals or those with severe mental illness. Provide clear safety plans and emergency contact information.
