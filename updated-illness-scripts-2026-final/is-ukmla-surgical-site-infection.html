Surgical site infection [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Surgical site infection"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Surgical site infection"][/sc]
  • A surgical site infection (SSI) is an infection that occurs after surgery in the part of the body where the surgery took place, ranging from superficial skin infections to more severe infections involving deep tissues or organs/spaces.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the classification of surgical site infections (superficial, deep incisional, organ/space), identify key risk factors, and understand the principles of prevention and initial management.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Surgical site infection"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Surgical site infection"][/sc]
  • In the UK, surgical site infections remain a significant healthcare-associated infection. The UKHSA reported 591 SSIs across 17 surgical categories from 64,111 procedures in NHS hospitals in England during 2020-2021 [2].
  • The incidence of SSI varies considerably by surgical category; for example, in 2020-2021, hip and knee replacement SSIs were around 0.4%, while large bowel surgery had an SSI risk of 9.9% [2].
  • Key risk factors include patient-related factors (e.g., diabetes, obesity, smoking, immunosuppression, MRSA carriage) and procedure-related factors (e.g., wound classification, duration of surgery, emergency procedures, insertion of prosthetic material) [1] [3].
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Surgical site infection"][/sc]
  • **Trigger:** Surgical incision breaches the skin\'s natural barrier, allowing microorganisms (often from the patient\'s own flora, e.g., *Staphylococcus aureus*) to enter the sterile surgical field.
  • **Progression:** Contamination, coupled with factors like tissue trauma, foreign bodies (sutures, implants), haematoma formation, and impaired host defences, allows bacteria to proliferate and establish infection. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Wound classification: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Wound classification"][/sc]
  • **Consequence:** The inflammatory response to bacterial invasion leads to the classic signs of infection (redness, swelling, pain, heat, pus formation), potentially resulting in wound dehiscence, delayed healing, systemic sepsis, and organ dysfunction.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Surgical site infection"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Symptoms typically manifest within 5-10 days post-operatively, though superficial SSIs can appear within 30 days.
  • Progressive phase: Deep incisional SSIs and organ/space SSIs may present later, often within 30 days, but up to 90 days if an implant is involved.
  • Late phase: Persistent or recurrent infections, particularly beyond 90 days, may indicate underlying issues such as retained foreign material, chronic osteomyelitis, or prosthetic joint infection.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Surgical site infection"][/sc]
  • Ask about increasing pain at the surgical site - disproportionate pain may indicate a deep infection or necrotising fasciitis.
  • Ask about purulent discharge or cloudy fluid from the wound - a key indicator of bacterial infection, distinguishing it from serous drainage.
  • Ask about fever or general malaise - systemic symptoms suggest a more widespread inflammatory response or sepsis.
  • Ask about localised swelling, redness, or warmth around the incision - classic signs of inflammation and infection, helping to delineate the extent of the SSI.
  • Ask about wound dehiscence or delayed healing - indicates a failure of the wound to close properly, often due to underlying infection.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Surgical site infection"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - tachycardia, tachypnoea, fever, or hypotension may indicate systemic inflammatory response syndrome (SIRS) or sepsis.
  • Check for erythema, warmth, and tenderness around the incision - these local signs suggest inflammation and infection, helping to differentiate from normal post-operative healing.
  • Check for purulent discharge - direct visualisation of pus confirms infection and helps classify the SSI (e.g., superficial vs. deep).
  • Check for wound oedema and induration - swelling and hardening of tissues around the wound can indicate fluid accumulation or cellulitis.
  • Check for wound dehiscence or gaping - separation of wound edges, potentially exposing deeper tissues, is a serious sign of infection or poor healing. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="SSI risk factors: what are they and how do they influence patient management?" text="SSI risk factors"][/sc]
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Surgical site infection"][/sc]
  • Systemic Sepsis: Rapidly deteriorating vital signs (e.g., persistent tachycardia, hypotension, high fever, altered mental status) indicate systemic infection requiring immediate escalation.
  • Necrotising Fasciitis: Severe pain out of proportion to clinical findings, rapidly spreading erythema, crepitus, skin blistering, or necrosis, often within 24-72 hours post-op, necessitates urgent surgical debridement.
  • Deep Incisional or Organ/Space SSI: Evidence of infection extending beyond the superficial layers, such as deep tenderness, unexplained organ dysfunction, or purulent drainage from a deep drain, requires prompt investigation and intervention.
  • Wound Dehiscence with Evisceration: Protrusion of internal organs through a surgical wound is a surgical emergency.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Surgical site infection"][/sc]
  • Immunosuppressed patients: May present with minimal inflammatory signs (e.g., less erythema, warmth, or fever) despite severe infection, making diagnosis challenging.
  • Older adults: Often have blunted inflammatory responses, presenting with non-specific symptoms like confusion, lethargy, or functional decline rather than classic local signs.
  • Diabetic patients: Impaired wound healing and neuropathy can mask pain and delay recognition of infection, leading to more advanced disease at presentation.
  • Obese patients: Deep incisional SSIs can be difficult to detect due to thick adipose tissue, and wound healing is often compromised, increasing infection risk.
  • Patients on antibiotics: May have partially treated infections with modified or suppressed symptoms, leading to delayed diagnosis and potential for resistant organisms.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Surgical site infection"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected SSI:
    • Full blood count (FBC) - leucocytosis may indicate infection.
    • C-reactive protein (CRP) - elevated levels suggest inflammation/infection.
    • Wound swab for microbiology (culture and sensitivity) - to identify causative organism and guide antibiotic therapy.
    • Blood cultures - if systemic sepsis is suspected.
  • Severity/risk tools: [sc name="jitl-query" Q="MRSA screening: when is it indicated for surgical patients?" text="MRSA screening"][/sc]
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Consider imaging (e.g., ultrasound, CT) to rule out other causes of pain or inflammation, or to identify collections.
  • Imaging - Ultrasound: Useful for identifying fluid collections (abscesses, haematomas) in superficial and deep tissues.
  • Imaging - CT scan: Provides detailed anatomical information, crucial for diagnosing deep incisional or organ/space SSIs, especially intra-abdominal or pelvic collections.
  • Serial reassessment: Regular clinical review of the wound and patient\'s systemic status is essential to monitor response to treatment and detect deterioration.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Surgical site infection"][/sc]
  • **Normal post-operative inflammation:** Typically resolves within a few days, lacks purulent discharge, and systemic signs of infection are absent.
  • **Haematoma/Seroma:** Localised swelling and discomfort, but usually non-erythematous, non-tender, and without systemic signs of infection or purulent discharge.
  • **Wound dehiscence (non-infectious):** Separation of wound edges without signs of infection, often due to excessive tension or poor healing.
  • **Allergic reaction (e.g., to dressing material):** Pruritic rash, often extending beyond the wound edges, without systemic signs of infection or purulent discharge.
  • **Deep vein thrombosis (DVT):** Leg swelling, pain, and tenderness, but typically in the lower limb and not directly related to the surgical incision itself.
  • **Cellulitis (non-SSI related):** Spreading skin infection, but not originating from the surgical incision or involving deeper structures. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Surgical wound classification: how does it impact SSI risk?" text="Surgical wound classification"][/sc]
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Surgical site infection"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Recognition of SSI, assessment for systemic involvement (e.g., sepsis), and escalation to the surgical team. Supportive care often includes fluid resuscitation and analgesia.
  • Definitive management: Often involves wound exploration, debridement of necrotic tissue, drainage of pus or fluid collections, and appropriate antibiotic therapy guided by microbiology results.
  • Adjunctive management: Optimisation of patient factors (e.g., glycaemic control in diabetics, nutritional support) and wound care (e.g., appropriate dressings, negative pressure wound therapy) may support healing.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Regular wound assessment, monitoring of inflammatory markers (e.g., CRP), and clinical review to ensure resolution of infection and wound healing progression.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Patients should be advised on signs of worsening infection or systemic deterioration and when to seek urgent medical review.
  • Exam pharmacology reference (UKMLA/MRCGP): Empirical antibiotic choices often include broad-spectrum agents covering common skin flora and gut organisms (e.g., co-amoxiclav, metronidazole, flucloxacillin, gentamicin), adjusted based on culture results. Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Surgical site infection"][/sc]
  • Sepsis and Septic Shock: Uncontrolled infection can lead to systemic inflammatory response, organ dysfunction, and potentially life-threatening shock, particularly if not recognised and treated promptly.
  • Wound Dehiscence and Incisional Hernia: Infection compromises wound integrity, increasing the risk of wound breakdown and subsequent development of an incisional hernia, often requiring further surgical intervention.
  • Abscess Formation: Localised collections of pus can form within the wound or deeper tissues, necessitating drainage and potentially prolonging recovery.
  • Chronic Osteomyelitis: In cases involving bone (e.g., orthopaedic surgery), SSI can lead to persistent bone infection, which is challenging to treat and may require long-term antibiotics or further surgery.
  • Implant Failure: Infection around prosthetic implants (e.g., joint replacements, vascular grafts) can lead to implant loosening, failure, and the need for removal or revision surgery, significantly impacting patient outcomes.
