Vaginal prolapse [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Vaginal prolapse"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Vaginal prolapse"][/sc]
  • Vaginal prolapse occurs when one or more pelvic organs (such as the bladder, uterus, or bowel) descend from their normal anatomical position and bulge into the vaginal canal, due to weakened pelvic floor muscles and connective tissues [1].
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the presentation, risk factors, and initial management principles of vaginal prolapse.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Vaginal prolapse"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Vaginal prolapse"][/sc]
  • In the UK, the prevalence of pelvic organ prolapse symptoms (vaginal bulge or lump) in primary care is reported to be 8.4% [2].
  • Risk factors include increasing age, parity (especially vaginal delivery), obesity, chronic constipation, persistent heavy lifting, and conditions causing chronic cough [3, 4].
  • The lifetime risk of undergoing surgery for pelvic organ prolapse is estimated to be around 10-20% [5].
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Vaginal prolapse"][/sc]
  • The primary trigger is damage or weakening of the pelvic floor muscles, fascia, and supporting ligaments, often due to factors such as childbirth trauma, chronic increases in intra-abdominal pressure, or age-related collagen degradation [1, 6].
  • This structural compromise leads to a loss of support for the pelvic organs, allowing them to descend from their normal anatomical positions within the pelvic cavity.
  • The consequence is the protrusion of one or more pelvic organs (e.g., bladder, uterus, rectum) into or beyond the vaginal canal, manifesting as a palpable bulge and associated symptoms.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Vaginal prolapse"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Often asymptomatic or subtle, with mild descent of pelvic organs. Symptoms may be intermittent and noticed only during periods of increased intra-abdominal pressure, such as coughing or straining.
  • Progressive phase: Gradual worsening of symptoms and increasing descent of the prolapse, particularly beyond several months or years, as pelvic floor support structures continue to weaken. Symptoms become more persistent and bothersome.
  • Late phase: Significant prolapse with organs protruding beyond the vaginal introitus, leading to more severe symptoms and potential complications such as ulceration or urinary retention.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Vaginal prolapse"][/sc]
  • Ask about a sensation of a vaginal bulge or \'something coming down\' - this directly indicates organ descent and helps grade severity.
  • Ask about pelvic pressure or heaviness - a common non-specific symptom that can worsen with prolonged standing or activity.
  • Ask about urinary symptoms such as frequency, urgency, or incomplete bladder emptying - these suggest anterior compartment prolapse (cystocele) affecting bladder function.
  • Ask about bowel symptoms like constipation or difficulty with defecation, sometimes requiring digital splinting - indicative of posterior compartment prolapse (rectocele).
  • Ask about lower back pain - can be associated with pelvic organ descent, though often non-specific.
  • Ask about discomfort or pain during sexual intercourse (dyspareunia) - may be due to anatomical changes or vaginal dryness associated with prolapse.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Vaginal prolapse"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - typically normal in uncomplicated prolapse, but important to rule out systemic illness or infection if symptoms are acute.
  • Check for a visible or palpable bulge at the vaginal introitus, especially with Valsalva manoeuvre - directly indicates the presence and degree of organ descent.
  • Check pelvic floor muscle strength and tone on bimanual examination - assesses the integrity and function of supporting structures.
  • Check for evidence of ulceration, excoriation, or dryness on the prolapsed tissue - suggests chronic exposure and potential for local complications.
  • Check for residual urine volume post-voiding [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Post-void residual volume: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="post-void residual volume"][/sc] - may indicate bladder outlet obstruction in cases of cystocele.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Vaginal prolapse"][/sc]
  • Irreducible prolapse - inability to manually reduce the prolapsed tissue, potentially indicating incarceration or strangulation, requiring urgent assessment.
  • Ulceration or bleeding of the prolapsed tissue - suggests chronic exposure, friction, and potential for infection, necrosis, or significant discomfort.
  • Acute urinary retention - can occur with severe cystocele, indicating significant bladder outlet obstruction and requiring immediate intervention.
  • New or worsening severe pain - while prolapse is often painless, acute severe pain may signal complications such as infection, tissue ischaemia, or nerve compression.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Vaginal prolapse"][/sc]
  • Older adults: May present with more advanced prolapse due to cumulative effects of aging, menopause-related tissue changes, and comorbidities. Symptoms might be masked by other conditions or attributed to \'old age\', leading to delayed presentation.
  • Pregnancy: Prolapse during pregnancy is uncommon but can occur due to increased intra-abdominal pressure and hormonal changes. It may present with cervical ulceration, infection, or preterm labour, and often resolves or improves postpartum.
  • Asymptomatic prolapse: A significant proportion of women have some degree of pelvic organ descent on examination but report no symptoms. This highlights that treatment should be guided by symptoms, not just anatomical findings.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Vaginal prolapse"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected vaginal prolapse: Comprehensive history and physical examination, including a speculum and bimanual pelvic examination, performed in both supine and standing positions, with and without Valsalva manoeuvre, to assess the type and degree of prolapse using systems like the Pelvic Organ Prolapse Quantification (POP-Q) system [7].
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Urinalysis and urine culture to rule out urinary tract infection if urinary symptoms are prominent.
  • Imaging - Pelvic ultrasound: Can be used to assess pelvic organ anatomy and rule out other pelvic pathology, though not routinely required for diagnosis of uncomplicated prolapse.
  • Imaging - Dynamic MRI or MR defecography: Considered in complex cases, recurrent prolapse, or when surgical planning requires detailed anatomical assessment of all pelvic compartments, particularly for posterior compartment defects [8].
  • Serial reassessment: For mild or asymptomatic prolapse, regular clinical review may be appropriate to monitor progression and symptom development.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Vaginal prolapse"][/sc]
  • A 62-year-old multiparous woman presents with a 6-month history of a sensation of a vaginal bulge, worsening with prolonged standing, and associated with difficulty completely emptying her bladder. On examination, a reducible bulge is noted at the introitus, consistent with a cystocele.
  • An elderly multiparous female with chronic, progressive pelvic discomfort and obstructive urinary symptoms, demonstrating a reducible anterior vaginal wall prolapse.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Vaginal prolapse"][/sc]
  • Vaginal cyst (e.g., Gartner\'s duct cyst, Bartholin\'s cyst) - typically a discrete, often mobile, fluid-filled or solid mass that does not reduce with repositioning, unlike prolapsed organs [9].
  • Urethral diverticulum - a localized outpouching of the urethra that can present as an anterior vaginal wall mass, often associated with dysuria, post-void dribbling, and dyspareunia, confirmed by imaging [10].
  • Uterine fibroid (prolapsed) - a pedunculated fibroid that has prolapsed through the cervix can present as a vaginal mass, differentiated by its firm consistency and attachment to the uterus.
  • Rectal prolapse (full-thickness) - involves the protrusion of the rectal wall through the anus, which can coexist with or be mistaken for a rectocele, but is distinguished by the concentric folds of rectal mucosa.
  • Other pelvic masses (e.g., ovarian cyst, tumour) - may cause pelvic pressure or a palpable mass, but are typically identified on bimanual examination or imaging as distinct from the vaginal walls or uterus.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Vaginal prolapse"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: For symptomatic prolapse, initial management often involves lifestyle modifications (e.g., weight management, treating chronic constipation, avoiding heavy lifting) and pelvic floor muscle training (PFMT) with a specialist physiotherapist [11].
  • Definitive management:
    • Conservative: Vaginal pessaries are a common and effective non-surgical option for many women, providing mechanical support to the prolapsed organs [12].
    • Surgical: Various surgical procedures exist, tailored to the type and severity of prolapse and patient preference, aiming to restore anatomical support and alleviate symptoms. These include native tissue repairs (e.g., anterior or posterior colporrhaphy) or reconstructive procedures using mesh (e.g., sacrocolpopexy) [13].
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Regular review is essential to assess symptom control, pessary fit (if used), and to monitor for any progression of prolapse or complications.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Patients should be advised to seek medical attention for worsening symptoms, new pain, difficulty with urination or defecation, or signs of infection or ulceration.
  • Exam pharmacology reference (UKMLA/MRCGP):
    • Oestrogen replacement therapy (topical vaginal oestrogen) may be considered in postmenopausal women to improve vaginal tissue health and reduce symptoms, particularly when using pessaries [sc name="jitl-medication" Q="Oestrogen use in Vaginal prolapse" text="Oestrogen"][/sc]. Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Vaginal prolapse"][/sc]
  • Vaginal ulceration and infection: Chronic exposure and friction of prolapsed tissue can lead to ulceration, which increases the risk of local infection and discomfort, impacting quality of life.
  • Urinary tract infections (UTIs): Incomplete bladder emptying due to cystocele can lead to urinary stasis, predisposing to recurrent UTIs, which can be diagnostically challenging to differentiate from other urinary symptoms.
  • Acute urinary retention: Severe anterior vaginal wall prolapse (cystocele) can cause significant kinking of the urethra, leading to acute urinary retention, a medical emergency requiring prompt decompression.
  • Faecal impaction and obstructive defecation: Posterior vaginal wall prolapse (rectocele) can impede normal defecation, leading to chronic constipation, faecal impaction, and the need for manual assistance, affecting bowel health and comfort.
  • Sexual dysfunction: Prolapse can cause dyspareunia, reduced sensation, or body image concerns, leading to avoidance of sexual activity and impacting intimate relationships.
  • Progression of prolapse: Without intervention, prolapse can worsen over time, leading to more severe symptoms and potentially requiring more complex surgical management.
