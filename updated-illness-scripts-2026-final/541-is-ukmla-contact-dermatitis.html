Contact dermatitis [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Contact dermatitis"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Contact dermatitis"][/sc]
  • Contact dermatitis is an inflammatory skin condition that occurs as a result of exposure to an external irritant or allergen.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical features and differentiate between irritant and allergic contact dermatitis, understanding the principles of investigation and management.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Contact dermatitis"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Contact dermatitis"][/sc]
  • Contact dermatitis accounts for 4–7% of dermatological consultations in secondary care in the UK [9, 14].
  • The prevalence of dermatitis in the UK is estimated to be about 20% [14].
  • Occupational contact dermatitis represents 70-90% of all occupational skin diseases [13].
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Contact dermatitis"][/sc]
  • Trigger: Exposure to an irritant (e.g., chemicals, solvents, detergents) or an allergen (e.g., nickel, fragrances, preservatives).
  • Progression: Irritant contact dermatitis involves direct toxic damage to keratinocytes, while allergic contact dermatitis is a [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Type IV hypersensitivity reaction: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Type IV hypersensitivity reaction"] [/sc] mediated by T-lymphocytes, requiring prior sensitisation.
  • Consequence: Release of pro-inflammatory cytokines and chemokines, leading to inflammation, erythema, oedema, and pruritus.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Contact dermatitis"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Irritant contact dermatitis can manifest within minutes to hours of exposure to a strong irritant, or gradually over days to weeks with repeated exposure to weaker irritants.
  • Progressive phase: Allergic contact dermatitis typically develops 24-72 hours after re-exposure to an allergen in a sensitised individual.
  • Late phase: If exposure continues, both types can progress to chronic dermatitis with lichenification, scaling, and fissuring, particularly beyond 72 hours.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Contact dermatitis"][/sc]
  • Ask about itching (pruritus) - often intense and can significantly impact quality of life, distinguishing it from non-itchy rashes.
  • Ask about redness (erythema) and swelling (oedema) - key indicators of inflammation, helping to localise the affected area.
  • Ask about blistering (vesicles/bullae) or weeping - suggests an acute and more severe inflammatory reaction.
  • Ask about dry, cracked, or scaly skin - characteristic of chronic exposure and inflammation, indicating a prolonged course.
  • Ask about burning or stinging sensation - more commonly associated with irritant contact dermatitis due to direct tissue damage.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Contact dermatitis"][/sc]
  • Check vitals - usually normal; fever may suggest secondary bacterial infection.
  • Check erythema and oedema - often well-demarcated to the area of contact, providing clues to the causative agent.
  • Check vesicles, bullae, weeping, and crusting - indicative of acute dermatitis, particularly in allergic reactions.
  • Check lichenification, scaling, and fissuring - signs of chronic dermatitis due to persistent inflammation and scratching.
  • Check distribution pattern - the location and shape of the rash can strongly suggest exposure to a specific irritant or allergen (e.g., jewellery, clothing, gloves). Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Contact dermatitis"][/sc]
  • Widespread or rapidly spreading rash: May indicate a severe allergic reaction or systemic involvement requiring urgent assessment.
  • Signs of secondary infection: Increasing pain, purulent discharge, spreading erythema, or fever warrant prompt antibiotic consideration.
  • Facial or genital involvement: Can cause significant discomfort, functional impairment, and potential for severe oedema, especially around the eyes or airway.
  • Failure to respond to initial treatment: Suggests an incorrect diagnosis, ongoing exposure to the causative agent, or a severe reaction requiring specialist dermatological review, particularly beyond 72 hours of appropriate therapy.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Contact dermatitis"][/sc]
  • Older adults: May present with less pronounced inflammatory signs due to age-related skin changes, making diagnosis challenging. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Age-related skin changes: what are they and why do they matter clinically?" text="Age-related skin changes"] [/sc]
  • Children: Often presents on the face, hands, and nappy area, and can be difficult to differentiate from atopic eczema.
  • Occupational contact dermatitis: The distribution of the rash often correlates with work-related exposure, highlighting the importance of a detailed occupational history.
  • Airborne contact dermatitis: Rash appears on exposed areas (e.g., face, neck, upper chest) with sparing of areas covered by clothing, suggesting an airborne allergen or irritant.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Contact dermatitis"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected contact dermatitis: Thorough history taking focusing on exposure to potential irritants or allergens, onset, location, and progression of the rash. Clinical examination to assess morphology and distribution.
  • If allergic contact dermatitis is suspected: [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Patch testing: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Patch testing"] [/sc] is the gold standard investigation to identify specific allergens.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Skin scrapings for microscopy and culture to rule out fungal infections (e.g., tinea corporis).
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Contact dermatitis"][/sc]
  • A 45-year-old female presents with a 3-day history of an intensely itchy, erythematous, vesicular rash on her hands after using a new cleaning product.
  • Acute eczematous eruption in a contact distribution, concerning for contact dermatitis.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Contact dermatitis"][/sc]
  • Atopic eczema: Often a personal or family history of atopy, typically affects flexural areas, and has a chronic relapsing course, unlike the localised and acute onset of contact dermatitis. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Atopic eczema vs contact dermatitis: what are the key differences?" text="Atopic eczema"] [/sc]
  • Psoriasis: Characterised by well-demarcated erythematous plaques with silvery scales, and a different histological pattern.
  • Seborrhoeic dermatitis: Affects sebum-rich areas such as the scalp, face, and chest, presenting with greasy scales.
  • Tinea corporis (ringworm): Presents as annular lesions with an active, raised border, and can be confirmed by positive fungal scrapings.
  • Scabies: Causes intense pruritus, especially at night, with characteristic burrows, often affecting web spaces, wrists, and ankles.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Contact dermatitis"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Identification and removal of the causative agent is paramount. Symptomatic relief often includes emollients and topical corticosteroids.
  • Definitive management: Strict avoidance of identified irritants or allergens. Patient education on skin protection and barrier creams may support prevention.
  • Adjunctive management: Oral antihistamines may be considered for severe pruritus. A short course of oral corticosteroids may be warranted for severe acute reactions.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Regular assessment of treatment response and reinforcement of avoidance strategies. Referral to dermatology for patch testing is typically indicated if allergic contact dermatitis is suspected or if the condition is persistent/severe.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Patients should be advised to seek medical attention for worsening rash, signs of secondary infection, or systemic symptoms.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Contact dermatitis"][/sc]
  • Secondary bacterial infection: Disruption of the skin barrier and scratching can lead to impetiginisation or cellulitis, particularly beyond 72 hours of untreated inflammation.
  • Lichenification: Chronic scratching and rubbing results in skin thickening and accentuation of skin markings, indicating prolonged inflammation.
  • Post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation/hypopigmentation: Changes in skin colour can occur after resolution of inflammation, especially in individuals with darker skin types.
  • Psychological impact: Persistent itching and visible skin changes can lead to significant anxiety, depression, and reduced quality of life.
  • Occupational disability: Chronic or severe occupational contact dermatitis can necessitate job modification or cessation, impacting livelihood.
