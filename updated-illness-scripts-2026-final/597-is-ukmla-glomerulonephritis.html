Glomerulonephritis [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Glomerulonephritis"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Glomerulonephritis"][/sc]
  • Glomerulonephritis (GN) is a group of kidney diseases characterised by inflammation and damage to the glomeruli, leading to impaired kidney function, often presenting with haematuria and proteinuria.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical features and common causes of glomerulonephritis, distinguish between nephritic and nephrotic syndromes, and outline initial management principles.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Glomerulonephritis"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Glomerulonephritis"][/sc]
  • IgA nephropathy is the most common primary glomerulonephritis worldwide, affecting approximately 1 in 50,000 people in the UK, and is a significant cause of chronic kidney disease and kidney failure.
  • Minimal change glomerulonephritis accounts for 80% of nephrotic syndrome cases in children and 20% in adults.
  • Risk factors include a history of viral or bacterial infections (e.g., Group A Streptococcus, Hepatitis B/C), autoimmune diseases (e.g., Systemic Lupus Erythematosus), and certain genetic predispositions.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Glomerulonephritis"][/sc]
  • Initial trigger often involves immune complex deposition or autoantibody formation targeting glomerular structures, frequently following an infection or in the context of systemic autoimmune disease.
  • This immune activation leads to inflammation and proliferation of glomerular cells, causing damage to the glomerular basement membrane and podocytes, disrupting the kidney\'s filtration barrier. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Glomerular filtration barrier: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="glomerular filtration barrier"][/sc]
  • Consequence is the leakage of proteins (proteinuria) and red blood cells (haematuria) into the urine, leading to reduced glomerular filtration rate and potentially progressive renal impairment. Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Glomerulonephritis"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Acute presentations, such as post-streptococcal glomerulonephritis, typically manifest 1-3 weeks after an infection, with symptoms like oedema, hypertension, and haematuria developing rapidly.
  • Progressive phase: Some forms, like rapidly progressive glomerulonephritis, can lead to a significant decline in renal function over weeks to months, particularly beyond 3 months, often requiring urgent intervention.
  • Late phase: Chronic glomerulonephritis can progress insidiously over years, with gradual loss of kidney function, eventually leading to end-stage renal disease, particularly beyond 5 years without effective management.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Glomerulonephritis"][/sc]
  • Ask about oedema (swelling) - particularly around the eyes (periorbital) or in the ankles, indicating fluid retention due to impaired kidney function.
  • Ask about changes in urine colour (dark, smoky, or cola-coloured) - suggesting haematuria, a key feature of glomerular inflammation.
  • Ask about reduced urine output (oliguria) - reflecting a significant decrease in glomerular filtration rate.
  • Ask about fatigue and generalised weakness - common non-specific symptoms of chronic kidney disease and uraemia.
  • Ask about shortness of breath - potentially due to fluid overload leading to pulmonary oedema.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Glomerulonephritis"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - hypertension is common due to fluid retention and activation of the renin-angiotensin-aldosterone system.
  • Check for oedema - particularly pitting oedema in dependent areas, indicating significant fluid overload.
  • Check for signs of fluid overload in the lungs (e.g., crackles on auscultation) - suggesting pulmonary oedema.
  • Check for pallor - indicative of anaemia, which can be a complication of chronic kidney disease. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Anaemia of chronic disease: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Anaemia of chronic disease"][/sc]
  • Check for skin rashes or joint swelling - may suggest an underlying systemic autoimmune disease (e.g., SLE, vasculitis) causing secondary glomerulonephritis.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Glomerulonephritis"][/sc]
  • Rapidly worsening renal function: Suggests rapidly progressive glomerulonephritis, requiring urgent nephrology review and immunosuppressive therapy to prevent irreversible kidney damage, particularly within weeks.
  • Severe hypertension with neurological symptoms: Indicates hypertensive encephalopathy, a medical emergency requiring immediate blood pressure control to prevent stroke or further organ damage.
  • Pulmonary haemorrhage with renal involvement: May suggest Goodpasture\'s syndrome or ANCA-associated vasculitis, both life-threatening conditions requiring prompt diagnosis and aggressive immunosuppression.
  • Anuria or severe oliguria: Points to severe acute kidney injury, necessitating urgent investigation and potentially renal replacement therapy.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Glomerulonephritis"][/sc]
  • Older adults: May present with non-specific symptoms like fatigue, anorexia, or cognitive changes, making diagnosis challenging; often have underlying comorbidities that complicate management.
  • Children: Minimal change glomerulonephritis is common, presenting primarily with nephrotic syndrome (severe oedema, heavy proteinuria) without significant hypertension or haematuria. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Minimal Change Disease: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Minimal Change Disease"][/sc]
  • Immunosuppressed patients: Glomerulonephritis can be triggered by opportunistic infections or as a manifestation of their underlying condition, with altered immune responses affecting presentation and treatment.
  • Asymptomatic proteinuria or haematuria: Often discovered incidentally on routine urine dipstick, requiring further investigation to determine the underlying cause and risk of progression.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Glomerulonephritis"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected Glomerulonephritis:
    • Urine dipstick and microscopy: To detect haematuria (dysmorphic red blood cells, red cell casts) and proteinuria.
    • 24-hour urine protein collection or urine protein:creatinine ratio: To quantify proteinuria.
    • Blood tests: Urea, creatinine, electrolytes (to assess renal function), albumin (hypoalbuminaemia in nephrotic syndrome), FBC (anaemia), CRP/ESR (inflammation).
    • Immunological screen: ANA, ANCA, anti-GBM antibodies, complement levels (C3, C4), ASO titre (post-streptococcal GN), Hepatitis B/C serology. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="ANCA: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="ANCA"][/sc]
  • Severity/risk tools: International IgA Nephropathy Risk Score on MDCalc (if applicable for IgA nephropathy).
  • Imaging - Renal ultrasound: To assess kidney size, rule out obstruction, and identify chronic changes.
  • Renal biopsy: Gold standard for diagnosis, classification, and prognostication of glomerulonephritis, guiding specific treatment.

  • A 35-year-old male presents with new-onset periorbital oedema, dark urine, and elevated blood pressure following a recent upper respiratory tract infection.
  • Young adult with acute nephritic syndrome, post-infectious, concerning for glomerulonephritis.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Glomerulonephritis"][/sc]
  • Urinary Tract Infection (UTI) - typically presents with dysuria, frequency, and suprapubic pain, often without significant proteinuria or red cell casts.
  • Nephrolithiasis (Kidney Stones) - characterised by severe flank pain radiating to the groin, often with macroscopic haematuria but usually without proteinuria or oedema.
  • Hypertensive Nephrosclerosis - chronic kidney damage due to long-standing hypertension, usually with bland urine sediment and slow progression, unlike acute glomerulonephritis.
  • Diabetic Nephropathy - gradual onset of proteinuria in a patient with diabetes, often without haematuria in early stages, and typically a different immunological profile.
  • Acute Interstitial Nephritis - often drug-induced, presenting with acute kidney injury, rash, and eosinophilia, but typically with white cell casts rather than red cell casts. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Acute Interstitial Nephritis: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Acute Interstitial Nephritis"][/sc]
  • Systemic Lupus Erythematosus (SLE) - a multi-system autoimmune disease that can cause lupus nephritis, often with other systemic manifestations like arthritis, rash, and serositis.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Glomerulonephritis"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Recognition of acute kidney injury or rapidly progressive disease, urgent nephrology referral, and supportive care including blood pressure control and fluid management.
  • Definitive management: Often involves immunosuppressive therapy (e.g., corticosteroids, cyclophosphamide, rituximab) tailored to the specific type of glomerulonephritis and its severity, aiming to halt immune-mediated damage.
  • Adjunctive management: Includes angiotensin-converting enzyme (ACE) inhibitors or angiotensin receptor blockers (ARBs) to reduce proteinuria and control blood pressure, and diuretics for oedema.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Regular assessment of renal function, proteinuria, blood pressure, and disease activity markers is crucial to monitor treatment response and detect relapses.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Patients should be advised on symptoms of worsening renal function, fluid overload, or infection, and when to seek urgent medical attention.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Glomerulonephritis"][/sc]
  • Acute Kidney Injury (AKI): Can develop rapidly, particularly in aggressive forms of glomerulonephritis, leading to fluid overload, electrolyte imbalances, and uraemia, often within days to weeks.
  • Chronic Kidney Disease (CKD) and End-Stage Renal Disease (ESRD): Prolonged or recurrent glomerular damage can lead to irreversible scarring and progressive loss of kidney function over months to years, particularly beyond 5 years.
  • Hypertension: A common and significant complication, contributing to cardiovascular morbidity and accelerating CKD progression if not adequately controlled.
  • Cardiovascular Disease: Patients with glomerulonephritis and CKD have a significantly increased risk of heart attack, stroke, and heart failure due to chronic inflammation, hypertension, and dyslipidaemia.
  • Infection: Immunosuppressive therapy used to treat glomerulonephritis increases susceptibility to various infections, which can be severe and life-threatening.
  • Thromboembolism: Particularly in nephrotic syndrome, the loss of anticoagulant proteins in the urine increases the risk of deep vein thrombosis and pulmonary embolism.
