Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Venous thromboembolism (VTE) in pregnancy and puerperium"][/sc]
  • Venous thromboembolism (VTE) in pregnancy and the puerperium refers to the formation of blood clots within the venous system, including deep vein thrombosis (DVT) and pulmonary embolism (PE), occurring during pregnancy or up to 6 weeks postpartum.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the risk factors, clinical presentation, and initial management of VTE in pregnant and postpartum women.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Venous thromboembolism (VTE) in pregnancy and puerperium"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Venous thromboembolism (VTE) in pregnancy and puerperium"][/sc]
  • VTE is a leading direct cause of maternal death in the UK, with an overall incidence of 1–2 per 1000 pregnancies and puerperium.
  • The absolute incidence of VTE in pregnancy and the puerperium in the UK is 107 per 100,000 person-years. The risk is five-fold higher postpartum compared to antepartum, peaking in the first 3 weeks postpartum (421 per 100,000 person-years).
  • Key risk factors include increasing gestational age, caesarean section, and a history of previous VTE.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Venous thromboembolism (VTE) in pregnancy and puerperium"][/sc]
  • Trigger: Pregnancy induces a hypercoagulable state, primarily due to increased levels of procoagulant factors (e.g., fibrinogen, factors VII, VIII, X) and decreased levels of natural anticoagulants (e.g., protein S), alongside reduced fibrinolytic activity.
  • Progression: This hypercoagulable state, combined with venous stasis (caused by uterine compression of pelvic veins and hormonal-induced venodilation) and endothelial damage (e.g., during delivery), constitutes Virchow\'s triad, promoting thrombus formation.
  • Consequence: Thrombi can form in deep veins, most commonly in the lower limbs (DVT), and may embolise to the pulmonary circulation (PE), leading to potentially life-threatening cardiorespiratory compromise.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Venous thromboembolism (VTE) in pregnancy and puerperium"][/sc]
  • Early phase: VTE can occur at any stage of pregnancy, with some studies suggesting a significant proportion of antenatal VTE events occur in the first trimester.
  • Progressive phase: The risk of VTE progressively increases with gestational age, becoming highest in the third trimester, particularly beyond 28 weeks.
  • Late phase: The puerperium, especially the first 3 weeks postpartum, represents the period of highest risk for VTE, with a significantly elevated daily risk compared to both the antenatal period and the non-pregnant state.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Venous thromboembolism (VTE) in pregnancy and puerperium"][/sc]
  • Ask about unilateral leg pain and swelling - to identify potential deep vein thrombosis (DVT), noting that bilateral oedema is common in pregnancy.
  • Ask about sudden onset shortness of breath - a key symptom of pulmonary embolism (PE), which can be subtle and attributed to normal pregnancy changes.
  • Ask about chest pain, especially if pleuritic or worse on inspiration - indicative of PE, requiring differentiation from other causes of chest discomfort in pregnancy.
  • Ask about haemoptysis (coughing up blood) - a less common but highly concerning symptom of PE, suggesting pulmonary infarction.
  • Ask about dizziness or syncope - may indicate significant haemodynamic compromise due to a large PE.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Venous thromboembolism (VTE) in pregnancy and puerperium"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - for tachycardia, tachypnoea, hypotension, or hypoxia, which may indicate pulmonary embolism or haemodynamic compromise.
  • Check unilateral leg swelling, warmth, tenderness, and erythema - suggestive of deep vein thrombosis (DVT), noting that bilateral oedema is common in pregnancy.
  • Check for calf pain on dorsiflexion (Homan\'s sign) - while historically taught, this sign is unreliable and non-specific for DVT.
  • Check lung auscultation - for crackles or reduced breath sounds, which may be present in PE but are often non-specific.
  • Check for signs of right heart strain (e.g., elevated JVP, loud P2) - in severe PE, indicating acute cor pulmonale.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Venous thromboembolism (VTE) in pregnancy and puerperium"][/sc]
  • Sudden severe dyspnoea or chest pain: Suggests a large or haemodynamically significant pulmonary embolism (PE).
  • Haemodynamic instability (e.g., hypotension, syncope): Indicates massive PE with right ventricular dysfunction and impending cardiovascular collapse.
  • Unilateral leg swelling with severe pain and pallor (Phlegmasia cerulea dolens): A rare but severe form of DVT with extensive venous occlusion, risking limb ischaemia and gangrene.
  • New onset neurological deficit: Could indicate paradoxical embolism through a patent foramen ovale, a rare but serious complication of VTE.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Venous thromboembolism (VTE) in pregnancy and puerperium"][/sc]
  • Proximal DVT (iliac or femoral vein): May present with pain in the thigh, buttock, or lower back, and swelling of the entire leg, which can be masked by common pregnancy discomforts.
  • Isolated pelvic vein thrombosis: Can manifest as unexplained abdominal or pelvic pain, which might be mistaken for obstetric or gynaecological conditions.
  • Small pulmonary emboli: May cause only mild, non-specific symptoms like increased fatigue or slight breathlessness, easily dismissed as normal physiological changes of pregnancy.
  • Postpartum VTE: Symptoms can be attributed to the physiological changes and discomforts of the puerperium, delaying diagnosis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Venous thromboembolism (VTE) in pregnancy and puerperium"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected DVT: Clinical assessment using a modified Wells score (though its utility is limited in pregnancy due to physiological changes). D-dimer testing is often elevated in normal pregnancy, reducing its specificity, but a normal D-dimer can still help rule out DVT in some cases (e.g., using pregnancy-adapted YEARS algorithm).
  • Core for suspected PE: Clinical assessment (e.g., using modified Wells score for PE, though again, limited utility in pregnancy). D-dimer has reduced specificity in pregnancy.
  • Imaging - DVT: First-line investigation is compression ultrasonography of the symptomatic leg.
  • Imaging - PE: Ventilation-perfusion (V/Q) scan or CT pulmonary angiography (CTPA) are the primary imaging modalities. The choice depends on local expertise, patient factors, and radiation exposure considerations.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Consider other investigations based on differential diagnoses (e.g., musculoskeletal pain, cellulitis for DVT; pneumonia, cardiac causes for PE).
  • Serial reassessment: Clinical reassessment is crucial, especially if initial tests are inconclusive or symptoms persist/worsen.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Venous thromboembolism (VTE) in pregnancy and puerperium"][/sc]
  • Physiological dyspnoea of pregnancy - typically gradual onset, not associated with pleuritic pain or haemodynamic instability.
  • Peripartum cardiomyopathy - presents with dyspnoea, fatigue, and oedema, but usually with signs of heart failure (e.g., cardiomegaly, elevated BNP).
  • Pneumonia/bronchitis - often accompanied by cough with sputum, fever, and localised crackles on auscultation.
  • Musculoskeletal pain (e.g., costochondritis, rib fracture) - localised chest wall tenderness, often reproducible with palpation.
  • Leg cramps or varicose veins - common in pregnancy, usually bilateral, and not associated with unilateral swelling, warmth, or tenderness indicative of DVT.
  • Cellulitis - presents with localised erythema, warmth, and tenderness, often with a clear portal of entry for infection.
  • Pelvic girdle pain - localised to the pelvis, often exacerbated by movement, without signs of DVT.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Venous thromboembolism (VTE) in pregnancy and puerperium"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Stabilisation of the patient, particularly in cases of suspected pulmonary embolism (PE) with haemodynamic compromise. Oxygen support and fluid resuscitation may be required.
  • Definitive management: Therapeutic anticoagulation with low molecular weight heparin (LMWH) is the treatment of choice during pregnancy and the puerperium. Treatment typically continues throughout pregnancy and for at least 6 weeks postpartum, or for a minimum of 3 months in total.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Regular clinical assessment for resolution of symptoms and signs, and monitoring for potential complications or adverse effects of anticoagulation.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Patients should be advised on symptoms of worsening VTE or bleeding and instructed to seek immediate medical attention if these occur. Referral to a haematologist with expertise in haemostasis and pregnancy is often indicated.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Venous thromboembolism (VTE) in pregnancy and puerperium"][/sc]
  • Pulmonary Embolism (PE): The most serious acute complication, occurring when a DVT dislodges and travels to the lungs, potentially causing respiratory distress, haemodynamic instability, and death.
  • Post-thrombotic Syndrome (PTS): A long-term complication of DVT, resulting from damage to venous valves and walls, leading to chronic leg pain, swelling, skin changes, and ulceration, significantly impacting quality of life.
  • Recurrent VTE: Women with a history of VTE, especially pregnancy-related, are at increased risk of future VTE events in subsequent pregnancies or even outside of pregnancy.
  • Chronic Thromboembolic Pulmonary Hypertension (CTEPH): A rare but severe long-term complication of PE, where organised thrombi lead to persistent pulmonary vascular obstruction and progressive right heart failure.
  • Anticoagulation-related bleeding: While essential for treatment, anticoagulation carries a risk of bleeding complications, which can be significant in the peripartum period.
