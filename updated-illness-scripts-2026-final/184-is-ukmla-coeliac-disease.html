Coeliac disease (Gastrointestinal tract) [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Coeliac disease (Gastrointestinal tract)"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Coeliac disease"][/sc]
  • Coeliac disease is a common, lifelong autoimmune condition where the ingestion of gluten leads to immune-mediated damage to the small intestinal mucosa in genetically predisposed individuals.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical features, diagnostic pathway, and management principles of coeliac disease, including the importance of a strict gluten-free diet and monitoring for complications.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Coeliac disease epidemiology"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Coeliac disease"][/sc]
  • Prevalence in the UK is estimated to be around 1% (1 in 100 people), though many remain undiagnosed [2].
  • Incidence has been increasing, with 50,416 people newly diagnosed in the UK in 2020 [3].
  • Risk factors include a genetic predisposition (HLA-DQ2/DQ8), first-degree relatives with coeliac disease, and other autoimmune conditions such as Type 1 diabetes and autoimmune thyroid disease [1].
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Coeliac disease pathophysiology"][/sc]
  • Trigger: In genetically susceptible individuals (HLA-DQ2/DQ8), gluten (specifically gliadin) peptides are deamidated by tissue transglutaminase (tTG) in the small intestine.
  • Progression: These deamidated peptides are presented to T-cells, initiating an immune response that leads to inflammation and damage to the small intestinal lining.
  • Consequence: Chronic inflammation results in villous atrophy, crypt hyperplasia, and increased intraepithelial lymphocytes, impairing nutrient absorption and causing diverse clinical manifestations. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Villous atrophy: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Villous atrophy"][/sc]
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Coeliac disease time course"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Symptoms can develop gradually over weeks to months after gluten introduction, often presenting with non-specific gastrointestinal complaints or nutritional deficiencies.
  • Progressive phase: If untreated, ongoing gluten exposure leads to worsening intestinal damage, potentially causing malabsorption, weight loss, and systemic complications over months to years.
  • Late phase: Long-term untreated coeliac disease can lead to severe complications, including refractory coeliac disease, intestinal lymphoma, and increased mortality, particularly beyond several years of undiagnosed disease.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Coeliac disease symptoms"][/sc]
  • Ask about chronic diarrhoea or steatorrhoea - indicative of malabsorption and intestinal damage.
  • Ask about abdominal pain, bloating, and distension - common gastrointestinal manifestations, often non-specific.
  • Ask about unexplained weight loss or faltering growth in children - key indicators of malabsorption and nutritional impact.
  • Ask about fatigue and lethargy - often due to anaemia (iron, B12, folate deficiency) secondary to malabsorption.
  • Ask about severe or persistent mouth ulcers - a common extra-intestinal manifestation.
  • Ask about dermatitis herpetiformis - a highly specific, intensely itchy blistering rash, often symmetrical, on extensor surfaces.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Coeliac disease signs"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - typically normal, but may show signs of dehydration in severe diarrhoea or pallor in anaemia.
  • Check for signs of malnutrition (e.g., muscle wasting, reduced subcutaneous fat) - indicates significant malabsorption.
  • Check for abdominal distension and tenderness - non-specific but can be present.
  • Check for pallor of mucous membranes and conjunctiva - suggestive of anaemia.
  • Check skin for dermatitis herpetiformis - pathognomonic for coeliac disease. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Dermatitis herpetiformis: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Dermatitis herpetiformis"][/sc]
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Coeliac disease red flags"][/sc]
  • Unexplained iron deficiency anaemia: A common and often the sole presenting feature, warranting investigation for coeliac disease.
  • Persistent unexplained gastrointestinal symptoms: Especially if unresponsive to conventional treatments for conditions like IBS.
  • Faltering growth in children or unexpected weight loss in adults: Suggests significant malabsorption requiring urgent assessment.
  • Dermatitis herpetiformis: Highly specific skin manifestation that should prompt immediate investigation for coeliac disease.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Coeliac disease atypical presentations"][/sc]
  • Older adults: May present with subtle symptoms, such as anaemia, fatigue, or osteoporosis, without classic gastrointestinal complaints.
  • Children: Can present with irritability, dental enamel defects, short stature, or delayed puberty, in addition to or instead of gastrointestinal symptoms.
  • Silent coeliac disease: Individuals with positive serology and villous atrophy but no overt symptoms; often discovered during screening of high-risk groups. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Silent coeliac disease: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Silent coeliac disease"][/sc]
  • Potential coeliac disease: Individuals with positive serology but normal small intestinal mucosa; may develop villous atrophy over time.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Coeliac disease diagnostic tests"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected Coeliac disease:
    • Serological testing: Total IgA and IgA tissue transglutaminase (tTG) are the first-line tests. If IgA tTG is weakly positive, IgA endomysial antibodies (EMA) should be used. If IgA deficient, IgG deamidated gliadin peptide (DGP) or IgG tTG should be considered [1]. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Anti-tTG IgA: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Anti-tTG IgA"][/sc]
    • Endoscopic intestinal biopsy: Confirms diagnosis by demonstrating villous atrophy, crypt hyperplasia, and increased intraepithelial lymphocytes in the small intestine. Patients must be on a gluten-containing diet for at least 6 weeks prior to testing for accurate results [1].
  • Severity/risk tools: No widely validated severity or risk scores are routinely used in clinical practice for coeliac disease.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Consider tests for other causes of malabsorption or gastrointestinal symptoms, such as stool microscopy for parasites, faecal elastase for pancreatic insufficiency, or hydrogen breath tests for lactose intolerance.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Coeliac disease problem representation"][/sc]
  • A middle-aged female presents with chronic diarrhoea, abdominal bloating, and fatigue for several months, alongside unexplained iron deficiency anaemia.
  • Chronic malabsorptive enteropathy with systemic manifestations, concerning for coeliac disease.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Coeliac disease differential diagnosis"][/sc]
  • Irritable Bowel Syndrome (IBS) - distinguished by absence of villous atrophy and negative coeliac serology; symptoms often overlap.
  • Lactose intolerance - symptoms (bloating, diarrhoea) triggered by lactose, not gluten; no intestinal damage.
  • Crohn\\'s disease - inflammatory bowel disease with patchy inflammation, often transmural, and granulomas; distinguished by endoscopy and histology.
  • Microscopic colitis - chronic watery diarrhoea, normal colonoscopy but characteristic microscopic changes on biopsy.
  • Small intestinal bacterial overgrowth (SIBO) - can cause bloating, diarrhoea, and malabsorption; diagnosed by breath test or jejunal aspirate culture. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Small intestinal bacterial overgrowth: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="SIBO"][/sc]
  • Tropical sprue - malabsorption syndrome affecting residents or visitors to tropical regions; diagnosis of exclusion after ruling out other causes.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Coeliac disease treatment"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Recognition of symptoms and initiation of diagnostic pathway while maintaining gluten intake.
  • Definitive management: Lifelong strict gluten-free diet (GFD) is the cornerstone of treatment, leading to mucosal healing and symptom resolution.
  • Adjunctive management: Nutritional supplementation (e.g., iron, folate, vitamin D, calcium) may be required to correct deficiencies.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Regular follow-up with a dietitian and annual review by a healthcare professional to assess adherence to GFD, monitor symptoms, and screen for complications.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Refer to a gastrointestinal specialist for non-responsive or refractory coeliac disease, or if complications are suspected.
  • Exam pharmacology reference (UKMLA/MRCGP): [sc name="jitl-medication" Q="Corticosteroids use in refractory coeliac disease" text="Corticosteroids"][/sc] may be considered in cases of refractory coeliac disease, under specialist guidance. Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Coeliac disease complications"][/sc]
  • Refractory coeliac disease: Persistent villous atrophy and symptoms despite strict adherence to a gluten-free diet for over 12 months; requires specialist management.
  • Osteoporosis/osteopenia: Due to chronic malabsorption of calcium and vitamin D, increasing fracture risk, particularly if undiagnosed for prolonged periods.
  • Anaemia: Persistent iron, folate, or B12 deficiency due to malabsorption, even after GFD initiation if adherence is poor or other causes exist.
  • Small bowel malignancy: Increased risk of enteropathy-associated T-cell lymphoma (EATL) and adenocarcinoma of the small bowel, especially in long-standing, untreated disease.
  • Hyposplenism: Reduced splenic function, increasing susceptibility to encapsulated bacterial infections.
  • Infertility and adverse pregnancy outcomes: Untreated coeliac disease can be associated with subfertility, miscarriage, and low birth weight.
