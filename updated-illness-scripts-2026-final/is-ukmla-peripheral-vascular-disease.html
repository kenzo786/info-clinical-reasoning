Peripheral vascular disease [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Peripheral vascular disease"]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Peripheral vascular disease"]
  • Peripheral vascular disease (PVD), more commonly referred to as peripheral arterial disease (PAD), is a condition characterised by the narrowing or occlusion of arteries supplying the limbs, most commonly the lower limbs, due to atherosclerosis [1].
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical features of peripheral arterial disease, including intermittent claudication and critical limb ischaemia, and understand the principles of diagnosis and cardiovascular risk factor management.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Peripheral vascular disease"]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Peripheral vascular disease"][/sc]
  • The prevalence of PAD increases with age, affecting approximately 20% of people over 60 years in the UK [1].
  • Key risk factors include smoking, diabetes mellitus, hypertension, hyperlipidaemia, obesity, and a family history of cardiovascular disease [1].
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Peripheral vascular disease"]
  • Atherosclerosis, triggered by endothelial damage from cardiovascular risk factors, leads to plaque formation within the arterial walls, causing progressive narrowing and hardening of the arteries [1].
  • This arterial stenosis or occlusion reduces blood flow to the distal limbs, resulting in an imbalance between oxygen supply and metabolic demand, particularly during exercise [1].
  • Consequence is ischaemia, manifesting as intermittent claudication (pain on exertion) or, in severe cases, critical limb ischaemia (rest pain, ulcers, or gangrene) [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Critical limb ischaemia: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="critical limb ischaemia"][/sc].
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Peripheral vascular disease"]
  • Early phase: Often asymptomatic, or presents with mild, non-specific leg discomfort that is easily dismissed.
  • Progressive phase: Development of intermittent claudication, where muscle pain occurs consistently with exercise and is relieved by rest, gradually worsening over months to years.
  • Late phase: Critical limb ischaemia, characterised by chronic ischaemic rest pain, non-healing ulcers, or gangrene, indicating severe and persistent tissue hypoperfusion, particularly beyond 6 months without intervention.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Peripheral vascular disease"]
  • Ask about intermittent claudication - to assess for exercise-induced muscle pain (typically calf, thigh, or buttock) that resolves with rest, indicating arterial insufficiency.
  • Ask about rest pain - to identify severe ischaemia, often worse at night and relieved by hanging the leg over the bed or standing.
  • Ask about non-healing wounds or ulcers - to detect signs of critical limb ischaemia and tissue loss.
  • Ask about erectile dysfunction - to screen for pelvic arterial involvement, particularly in men with other cardiovascular risk factors.
  • Ask about coldness or numbness in the affected limb - to evaluate for reduced perfusion and nerve ischaemia.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Peripheral vascular disease"]
  • Check vital signs - to identify co-existing hypertension, tachycardia, or other cardiovascular instability.
  • Check peripheral pulses (femoral, popliteal, dorsalis pedis, posterior tibial) - for absence, diminution, or asymmetry, indicating arterial obstruction.
  • Check skin changes (pallor, dependent rubor, hair loss, brittle nails, shiny skin) - to assess chronic ischaemic changes.
  • Check capillary refill time - for delayed return of colour, suggesting poor peripheral perfusion.
  • Check for Buerger\'s angle - an angle of less than 20 degrees indicates severe ischaemia [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Buerger\'s angle: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Buerger\'s angle"][/sc].
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Peripheral vascular disease"]
  • Acute limb ischaemia: Sudden onset of severe limb pain, pallor, pulselessness, paraesthesia, paralysis, and perishingly cold limb, indicating an urgent need for revascularisation to prevent limb loss within hours.
  • Critical limb ischaemia: Chronic rest pain, non-healing ulcers, or gangrene, signifying severe and persistent tissue hypoperfusion requiring urgent vascular assessment to prevent amputation.
  • Rapidly worsening claudication: A sudden and significant decrease in walking distance or onset of rest pain over days to weeks, suggesting rapid disease progression or acute thrombosis.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Peripheral vascular disease"]
  • Diabetic patients: May present with atypical pain or neuropathic symptoms masking ischaemia, and a falsely elevated ABPI due to arterial calcification [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Falsely elevated ABPI in diabetes: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="falsely elevated ABPI"][/sc].
  • Elderly patients: May have reduced mobility or cognitive impairment, leading to delayed presentation or attribution of symptoms to other age-related conditions.
  • Women: May present with more diffuse symptoms or atypical pain patterns, potentially leading to delayed diagnosis compared to men.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Peripheral vascular disease"]
  • Core for suspected PAD: Ankle-brachial pressure index (ABPI) measurement, with an ABPI <0.9 indicating PAD and >1.3 suggesting arterial calcification [sc name="jitl-query" Q="ABPI interpretation: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="ABPI interpretation"][/sc].
  • Severity/risk tools: Fontaine classification (Stages I-IV) and Rutherford classification (Grades 0-III, Categories 0-6) are used to classify severity of PAD. Rutherford Classification of PAD on MDCalc
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Duplex ultrasound to visualise arterial anatomy, assess flow, and identify stenoses or occlusions.
  • Imaging - CTA/MRA: Computed tomography angiography (CTA) or magnetic resonance angiography (MRA) for detailed anatomical mapping prior to revascularisation, especially if duplex ultrasound is inconclusive or complex intervention is planned.
  • Serial reassessment: Regular clinical review and repeat ABPI measurements to monitor disease progression and treatment effectiveness.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Peripheral vascular disease"]
  • A 68-year-old male with a history of smoking and type 2 diabetes presents with a 6-month history of bilateral calf pain on walking 100 metres, relieved by rest, and a non-healing ulcer on his right great toe.
  • Elderly male with multiple cardiovascular risk factors presenting with chronic exertional leg pain and ischaemic tissue loss, concerning for advanced peripheral arterial disease.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Peripheral vascular disease"]
  • Spinal stenosis (neurogenic claudication) - pain is often position-dependent, relieved by sitting or bending forward, and not consistently related to walking distance.
  • Osteoarthritis of the hip or knee - pain is typically joint-specific, exacerbated by movement, and may not resolve rapidly with rest.
  • Venous claudication - leg discomfort or swelling that worsens with standing and improves with elevation, often associated with varicose veins or chronic venous insufficiency.
  • Peripheral neuropathy - burning, tingling, or numbness in a stocking-glove distribution, often symmetrical, without exertional pain.
  • Musculoskeletal pain (e.g., calf strain, shin splints) - localised tenderness, often related to specific movements or injury, not typically relieved by rest in the same way as claudication.
  • Deep vein thrombosis (DVT) - acute onset of unilateral leg swelling, pain, and tenderness, without signs of chronic ischaemia [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Deep vein thrombosis: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Deep vein thrombosis"][/sc].
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Peripheral vascular disease"]
  • Immediate priorities: Recognition of critical limb ischaemia or acute limb ischaemia, urgent referral to vascular services, and pain management.
  • Definitive management: Lifestyle modifications (smoking cessation, supervised exercise programme), optimal medical therapy for cardiovascular risk factors (antiplatelets, statins, antihypertensives, diabetes control), and revascularisation (angioplasty, stenting, bypass surgery) for severe or limb-threatening disease [1].
  • Adjunctive management: Wound care for ulcers, foot protection, and pain relief.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Regular clinical review, ABPI assessment, and ongoing cardiovascular risk factor management.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Advise patients to seek urgent medical attention for sudden worsening of pain, new numbness, weakness, or changes in skin colour.
  • Exam pharmacology reference (UKMLA/MRCGP): Antiplatelet agents such as [sc name="jitl-medication" Q="Aspirin use in Peripheral vascular disease" text="aspirin"][/sc] and [sc name="jitl-medication" Q="Clopidogrel use in Peripheral vascular disease" text="clopidogrel"][/sc] are often considered for secondary prevention of cardiovascular events. Statins (e.g., [sc name="jitl-medication" Q="Atorvastatin use in Peripheral vascular disease" text="atorvastatin"][/sc]) are used for lipid modification. Naftidrofuryl oxalate may be considered for symptomatic relief of intermittent claudication [1]. Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Peripheral vascular disease"]
  • Critical limb ischaemia: Persistent severe pain, non-healing ulcers, and gangrene, leading to increased risk of amputation and significant morbidity if not managed urgently.
  • Acute limb ischaemia: Sudden and severe reduction in limb perfusion, often due to embolism or thrombosis, which can result in irreversible tissue damage and limb loss within hours if not promptly treated.
  • Cardiovascular events: Increased risk of myocardial infarction, stroke, and cardiovascular death due to systemic atherosclerosis, highlighting the importance of aggressive risk factor modification.
  • Infection: Ischaemic ulcers are prone to infection, which can spread rapidly in compromised tissue, potentially leading to cellulitis, osteomyelitis, or sepsis.
  • Amputation: The ultimate consequence of uncontrolled critical limb ischaemia or severe acute limb ischaemia, resulting in significant functional impairment and reduced quality of life.
