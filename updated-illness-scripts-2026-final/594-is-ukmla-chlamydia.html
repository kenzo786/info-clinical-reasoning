Chlamydia [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Chlamydia"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Chlamydia"][/sc]
  • Chlamydia is a bacterial infection caused by *Chlamydia trachomatis*, representing one of the most common sexually transmitted infections (STIs) in the UK.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the varied presentations of Chlamydia, initiate appropriate diagnostic testing, and outline management strategies including contact tracing and complication prevention.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Chlamydia"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Chlamydia"][/sc]
  • Genital chlamydia is the most frequently diagnosed bacterial STI in the UK, with a high prevalence particularly among sexually active individuals under 25 years.
  • In 2024, over 600,000 chlamydia tests were conducted in England among young women aged 15-24, highlighting targeted screening efforts [1].
  • Key risk factors include age under 25, new or multiple sexual partners, inconsistent condom use, and a history of other STIs.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Chlamydia"][/sc]
  • Trigger: Infection begins with the elementary body of *Chlamydia trachomatis*, an obligate intracellular bacterium, attaching to and invading columnar epithelial cells, primarily in the genitourinary tract.
  • Progression: Inside the host cell, elementary bodies transform into metabolically active reticulate bodies, which replicate extensively within a protective vacuole called an inclusion, manipulating host cell processes for survival and multiplication [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Chlamydia inclusion bodies: what are they and why do they matter clinically?" text="inclusion bodies"][/sc].
  • Consequence: Release of new elementary bodies from ruptured host cells perpetuates the infection and triggers inflammatory responses, which, if untreated, can lead to tissue damage, scarring, and serious complications such as pelvic inflammatory disease (PID) or epididymo-orchitis [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Chlamydia-induced inflammation: what is its role in complications?" text="inflammatory response"][/sc].
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Chlamydia"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Incubation period typically ranges from 1 to 3 weeks post-exposure, often remaining asymptomatic.
  • Progressive phase: If untreated, infection can persist for months or even years, potentially ascending to cause complications such as pelvic inflammatory disease (PID) in women or epididymo-orchitis in men, particularly beyond 3-4 weeks.
  • Late phase: Chronic or recurrent infections, especially if untreated, can lead to long-term sequelae including infertility, ectopic pregnancy, and chronic pelvic pain, often manifesting months to years after initial exposure.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Chlamydia"][/sc]
  • Ask about dysuria - may indicate urethritis in both sexes or cervicitis in women.
  • Ask about unusual vaginal or penile discharge - suggests inflammation or infection of the genital tract.
  • Ask about intermenstrual or post-coital bleeding - can be a sign of cervicitis in women.
  • Ask about lower abdominal or pelvic pain - may indicate ascending infection such as pelvic inflammatory disease (PID) in women.
  • Ask about testicular pain or swelling - suggests epididymo-orchitis in men.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Chlamydia"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - typically normal in uncomplicated cases, but fever and tachycardia may indicate systemic spread or complications like PID.
  • Check for mucopurulent cervicitis on speculum examination - suggests cervical inflammation in women.
  • Check for cervical motion tenderness on bimanual examination - a key sign of pelvic inflammatory disease (PID) [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Cervical motion tenderness: what is its significance in pelvic inflammatory disease?" text="cervical motion tenderness"][/sc].
  • Check for epididymal tenderness or swelling on scrotal examination - indicative of epididymo-orchitis in men.
  • Check for conjunctival injection or discharge - may suggest ocular chlamydia (conjunctivitis).
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Chlamydia"][/sc]
  • Severe lower abdominal pain with fever: Suggests acute pelvic inflammatory disease (PID), requiring urgent assessment and treatment to prevent long-term reproductive complications.
  • Acute testicular pain and swelling with systemic symptoms: May indicate severe epididymo-orchitis, necessitating prompt management to preserve fertility.
  • Signs of disseminated infection (e.g., rash, migratory arthralgia): Could point towards reactive arthritis, a systemic complication that can develop weeks to months after initial infection.
  • Symptoms in pregnancy: Untreated chlamydia can lead to premature rupture of membranes, preterm birth, and neonatal conjunctivitis or pneumonia, requiring immediate attention.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Chlamydia"][/sc]
  • Asymptomatic infection: The majority of chlamydia infections are asymptomatic, particularly in women, leading to delayed diagnosis and increased risk of transmission and complications.
  • Older adults: Symptoms may be non-specific or attributed to other conditions, leading to underdiagnosis and potential for severe complications, especially if sexually active.
  • Pharyngeal or rectal infection: Often asymptomatic but can cause pharyngitis or proctitis, requiring specific testing based on sexual history.
  • Reactive arthritis: A sterile inflammatory arthritis that can occur weeks to months after a chlamydial infection, presenting with oligoarthritis, conjunctivitis, and urethritis [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Reactive arthritis (Reiter syndrome): what is its clinical presentation and association with Chlamydia?" text="reactive arthritis"][/sc].
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Chlamydia"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected Chlamydia: Nucleic Acid Amplification Tests (NAATs) on first-pass urine (men) or vulvovaginal swab (women) are the gold standard due to high sensitivity and specificity.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Consider testing for other STIs (gonorrhoea, syphilis, HIV) and bacterial vaginosis, especially in symptomatic individuals.
  • Severity/risk tools: For suspected PID, consider clinical assessment using criteria such as lower abdominal pain, cervical motion tenderness, and fever; no single validated severity score is routinely used for uncomplicated chlamydia.
  • Serial reassessment: Repeat NAAT testing 3-6 months post-treatment is recommended for all individuals diagnosed with chlamydia due to high rates of re-infection.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Chlamydia"][/sc]
  • A 22-year-old sexually active female presents with 2 weeks of dysuria and new vaginal discharge, with cervical motion tenderness on examination.
  • Young female with subacute genitourinary symptoms and signs of cervicitis, concerning for sexually transmitted infection, likely Chlamydia.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Chlamydia"][/sc]
  • Gonorrhoea - often presents with more purulent discharge and can co-exist with chlamydia.
  • Bacterial vaginosis - typically causes a fishy-smelling discharge without significant inflammation.
  • Trichomoniasis - may cause frothy, yellow-green discharge and vulvovaginal irritation.
  • Pelvic Inflammatory Disease (PID) - a complication of untreated STIs, presenting with lower abdominal pain, fever, and cervical motion tenderness.
  • Ectopic pregnancy - must be considered in sexually active women with lower abdominal pain and abnormal bleeding, especially if pregnant.
  • Urinary Tract Infection (UTI) - primarily causes dysuria and frequency, usually without vaginal or penile discharge.
  • Non-gonococcal urethritis (NGU) - can be caused by various organisms, including *Mycoplasma genitalium*, presenting similarly to chlamydial urethritis.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Chlamydia"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Prompt diagnosis and initiation of appropriate antibiotic therapy to prevent complications and reduce transmission; partner notification and treatment are crucial.
  • Definitive management: First-line treatment typically involves a short course of oral antibiotics, such as doxycycline or azithromycin, guided by national (e.g., BASHH) guidelines.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Abstinence from sexual activity until 7 days after completion of treatment by both the patient and partners; repeat testing for re-infection is recommended 3-6 months post-treatment.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Advise patients on symptoms of complications (e.g., worsening pain, fever) and when to seek urgent medical review; ensure access to sexual health services for ongoing support.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Chlamydia"][/sc]
  • Pelvic Inflammatory Disease (PID): Ascending infection in women, occurring weeks to months after untreated chlamydia, leading to chronic pelvic pain, ectopic pregnancy, and infertility due to tubal damage.
  • Epididymo-orchitis: Ascending infection in men, causing testicular pain and swelling, potentially leading to subfertility if severe or recurrent.
  • Reactive Arthritis (Reiter Syndrome): A sterile inflammatory arthritis that can develop weeks to months after chlamydial infection, characterised by the classic triad of urethritis, conjunctivitis, and arthritis.
  • Ectopic Pregnancy: Increased risk in women with previous PID due to tubal scarring, a serious and potentially life-threatening complication.
  • Infertility: Both male and female infertility can result from chronic inflammation and scarring caused by untreated chlamydial infections.
  • Neonatal Conjunctivitis and Pneumonia: Transmitted vertically from an infected mother to her newborn during childbirth, manifesting in the first few weeks of life.
