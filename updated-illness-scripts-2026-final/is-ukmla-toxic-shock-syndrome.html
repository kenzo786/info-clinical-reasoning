Toxic shock syndrome [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Toxic shock syndrome"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Toxic shock syndrome"][/sc]
  • Toxic shock syndrome (TSS) is a rare but life-threatening, rapidly progressive illness caused by bacterial exotoxins, primarily from *Staphylococcus aureus* (staphylococcal TSS) or *Streptococcus pyogenes* (streptococcal TSS), leading to systemic inflammation, multiorgan dysfunction, and shock.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical features of toxic shock syndrome, differentiate between staphylococcal and streptococcal forms, and initiate appropriate urgent management, including supportive care and antimicrobial therapy.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Toxic shock syndrome"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Toxic shock syndrome"][/sc]
  • TSS is rare; in the UK, the incidence of menstrual TSS (m-TSS) was estimated at 0.09/100,000 persons, and non-menstrual TSS (nm-TSS) at 0.04/100,000 persons.
  • Staphylococcal TSS is often associated with tampon use, particularly highly absorbent types, or other retained foreign bodies and surgical wound infections.
  • Streptococcal TSS (STSS) typically follows an invasive Group A Streptococcus (GAS) infection, such as necrotising fasciitis or bacteraemia, and has a higher mortality rate (up to 64% in the UK) compared to staphylococcal TSS.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Toxic shock syndrome"][/sc]
  • Trigger: Localised infection with toxin-producing strains of *Staphylococcus aureus* (e.g., TSST-1) or *Streptococcus pyogenes* (e.g., SpeA, SpeC) leads to the release of bacterial superantigens.
  • Progression: These superantigens bypass conventional antigen presentation, directly binding to MHC class II molecules on antigen-presenting cells and the Vβ region of T-cell receptors. This leads to a massive, non-specific activation of T-cells and an uncontrolled release of pro-inflammatory cytokines (cytokine storm). [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Superantigen mechanism: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Superantigen mechanism"][/sc]
  • Consequence: The overwhelming systemic inflammatory response results in widespread endothelial damage, capillary leak, hypotension, and ultimately multiorgan dysfunction and shock. Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Toxic shock syndrome"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Initial symptoms, often non-specific and flu-like (fever, myalgia, malaise), typically develop rapidly within hours to 1-2 days following bacterial colonisation or infection.
  • Progressive phase: Rapid deterioration with onset of hypotension, diffuse erythematous rash, and signs of organ dysfunction (e.g., vomiting, diarrhoea, altered mental status) usually within 24-48 hours of symptom onset.
  • Late phase: Established multiorgan failure, severe shock, and potential for irreversible damage or death, particularly beyond 48-72 hours if not promptly recognised and treated.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Toxic shock syndrome"][/sc]
  • Ask about sudden onset high fever - indicating a systemic inflammatory response.
  • Ask about diffuse macular erythroderma (sunburn-like rash) - a key diagnostic criterion, often followed by desquamation.
  • Ask about vomiting or diarrhoea - common gastrointestinal manifestations of systemic toxicity.
  • Ask about myalgia and severe pain out of proportion to apparent injury - particularly in streptococcal TSS, suggesting deep tissue infection.
  • Ask about headache, confusion, or altered mental status - reflecting central nervous system involvement due to systemic inflammation and hypoperfusion.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Toxic shock syndrome"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - for hypotension (systolic BP < 90 mmHg or orthostatic drop), tachycardia, and tachypnoea, indicating shock and systemic compromise.
  • Check for diffuse macular erythroderma - a widespread, blanching, sunburn-like rash, often sparing palms and soles initially.
  • Check for signs of desquamation - particularly on palms and soles, typically occurring 1-2 weeks after rash onset.
  • Check for mucous membrane hyperaemia - affecting conjunctivae, oral cavity, or vaginal mucosa, suggesting widespread inflammation.
  • Check for soft tissue infection (e.g., necrotising fasciitis) - in streptococcal TSS, look for severe pain, swelling, erythema, and crepitus, which may be out of proportion to initial findings. Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Toxic shock syndrome"][/sc]
  • Rapid clinical deterioration: A sudden and rapid worsening of symptoms and signs, particularly within hours, indicating a fulminant disease course.
  • Persistent hypotension: Systolic blood pressure less than 90 mmHg despite adequate fluid resuscitation, signifying circulatory shock.
  • Evidence of multiorgan dysfunction: Acute kidney injury, liver dysfunction, coagulopathy, or acute respiratory distress syndrome developing within 24-48 hours.
  • Diffuse erythematous rash with desquamation: A characteristic sunburn-like rash followed by skin peeling, especially on palms and soles, which is highly suggestive of TSS.
  • Severe pain out of proportion to physical findings: Especially in streptococcal TSS, this can indicate underlying necrotising soft tissue infection requiring urgent surgical assessment.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Toxic shock syndrome"][/sc]
  • Children: May present with less pronounced hypotension or rash, making diagnosis challenging; often associated with varicella or other skin infections.
  • Post-surgical patients: TSS can develop from surgical wound infections, with symptoms potentially masked by post-operative pain or fever, delaying recognition.
  • Non-menstrual staphylococcal TSS: Can occur in any age or gender, often associated with skin and soft tissue infections, burns, or foreign bodies (e.g., nasal packing), requiring a high index of suspicion.
  • Early stage: Initial symptoms can mimic common viral illnesses, leading to delayed diagnosis; the absence of a classic rash does not rule out TSS. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Early TSS presentation: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Early TSS presentation"][/sc]
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Toxic shock syndrome"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected TSS:
    • Full blood count (FBC): often shows leucocytosis with left shift, thrombocytopenia.
    • C-reactive protein (CRP) and erythrocyte sedimentation rate (ESR): markedly elevated, indicating severe inflammation.
    • Renal function tests (urea, creatinine, electrolytes): to assess for acute kidney injury.
    • Liver function tests (LFTs): to assess for hepatic dysfunction.
    • Coagulation screen (PT, APTT, fibrinogen): to detect coagulopathy.
    • Blood cultures: to identify causative organism (positive in ~60% of STSS, <5% of staphylococcal TSS).
    • Swabs from potential sites of infection (e.g., wound, vagina, throat): for microscopy, culture, and sensitivity.
  • Severity/risk tools: Consider using a severity score for sepsis, as TSS shares features with severe sepsis. qSOFA Score on MDCalc [sc name="jitl-query" Q="qSOFA score in TSS: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="qSOFA score"][/sc]
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible:
    • Lumbar puncture: if meningitis is suspected.
    • Imaging (e.g., CT scan): to identify occult sources of infection or complications.
  • Serial reassessment: Regular monitoring of bloods (FBC, U&Es, LFTs, CRP, coagulation) to track disease progression and response to treatment.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Toxic shock syndrome"][/sc]
  • A previously healthy 25-year-old female presents with sudden onset high fever, diffuse erythematous rash, vomiting, and hypotension, 3 days after starting menstruation with tampon use.
  • A young adult with acute febrile illness, mucocutaneous findings, and circulatory shock, concerning for toxin-mediated systemic inflammatory response.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Toxic shock syndrome"][/sc]
  • Sepsis: While TSS is a form of sepsis, it is specifically toxin-mediated with characteristic rash and desquamation, often without positive blood cultures for the causative organism. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Distinguishing TSS from severe sepsis: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Sepsis vs TSS"][/sc]
  • Meningococcaemia: Can present with fever, rash (purpuric/petechial), and shock, but typically lacks the diffuse erythematous rash and subsequent desquamation of TSS.
  • Kawasaki disease: Primarily affects children, presenting with fever, rash, conjunctivitis, and mucositis, but typically lacks the profound hypotension and multiorgan failure of TSS.
  • Drug reaction with eosinophilia and systemic symptoms (DRESS) syndrome: A severe adverse drug reaction with rash, fever, and organ involvement, but usually has a slower onset and eosinophilia.
  • Rocky Mountain spotted fever: Tick-borne illness with fever, headache, and rash (often petechial), but geographically specific and typically lacks the rapid progression and toxin-mediated features of TSS.
  • Measles: Presents with fever, cough, coryza, conjunctivitis, and a maculopapular rash, but typically less severe systemic illness and no hypotension or desquamation.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Toxic shock syndrome"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Prompt recognition and escalation to critical care, aggressive fluid resuscitation to manage hypotension, and removal of any foreign body or source of infection (e.g., tampon).
  • Definitive management: Early initiation of empiric broad-spectrum antibiotics, often including agents targeting *Staphylococcus aureus* and *Streptococcus pyogenes*, with subsequent de-escalation based on culture results. Surgical debridement may be warranted for necrotising soft tissue infections.
  • Adjunctive management: Consideration of intravenous immunoglobulin (IVIG) in severe cases, particularly STSS, to neutralise superantigens. Vasopressors may be required for refractory shock.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Close monitoring of vital signs, urine output, and organ function in an intensive care setting. Serial blood tests to track inflammatory markers and organ function.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Any patient with suspected TSS warrants immediate hospital admission and senior medical review due to the rapid progression and high mortality risk. Patients should be advised on signs of recurrence and preventative measures.
  • Exam pharmacology reference (UKMLA/MRCGP): Antimicrobial therapy often includes a beta-lactam antibiotic (e.g., flucloxacillin for staphylococcal, benzylpenicillin for streptococcal) combined with clindamycin. Clindamycin is considered for its antitoxin effect by inhibiting bacterial protein synthesis. Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Toxic shock syndrome"][/sc]
  • Acute Respiratory Distress Syndrome (ARDS): Can develop rapidly within 24-48 hours due to widespread capillary leak and pulmonary oedema, leading to severe hypoxaemia.
  • Acute Kidney Injury (AKI): Common due to hypoperfusion and direct toxin-mediated renal damage, often manifesting within 48-72 hours and potentially requiring renal replacement therapy.
  • Disseminated Intravascular Coagulation (DIC): A severe coagulopathy that can occur in the progressive phase, leading to both thrombotic and haemorrhagic complications.
  • Myocardial dysfunction: Direct cardiotoxicity from bacterial toxins and systemic inflammation can lead to reduced cardiac output and cardiogenic shock, often evident within the first few days.
  • Limb loss: Particularly in severe streptococcal TSS with necrotising fasciitis, extensive tissue necrosis can necessitate amputation if not managed aggressively in the early phase.
  • Recurrence: Staphylococcal TSS can recur, especially if the initial source of infection is not completely eradicated or if predisposing factors (e.g., tampon use) are reintroduced.
