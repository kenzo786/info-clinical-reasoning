Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Asthma-chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD) overlap"][/sc]
  • A clinical syndrome characterised by persistent respiratory symptoms and airflow limitation due to features of both asthma and COPD.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the overlapping features of asthma and COPD, and understand the implications for diagnosis and management.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Asthma-chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD) overlap"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Asthma-chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD) overlap"][/sc]
  • Prevalence of Asthma-COPD Overlap (ACO) is estimated to be around 15-20% in patients with obstructive airway disease in the UK, according to NICE guidelines.
  • Risk factors include a history of asthma, significant smoking exposure, and occupational irritant exposure.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Asthma-chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD) overlap"][/sc]
  • Chronic exposure to irritants (e.g., cigarette smoke) or allergens triggers airway inflammation and remodelling, leading to both reversible and irreversible airflow obstruction.
  • Progressive inflammation involves eosinophils, neutrophils, and mast cells, contributing to bronchial hyperresponsiveness and emphysema.
  • Consequence is persistent respiratory symptoms, exacerbations, and accelerated decline in lung function due to combined asthmatic and COPD processes.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Asthma-chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD) overlap"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Symptoms often present with features of asthma in younger adulthood, such as episodic wheeze and breathlessness, particularly beyond 2-3 days.
  • Progressive phase: Over years, persistent symptoms and fixed airflow obstruction develop, often with a history of smoking or significant environmental exposure.
  • Late phase: Established ACO with frequent exacerbations, significant impact on quality of life, and potential for respiratory failure, particularly beyond 5-10 years of progressive disease.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Asthma-chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD) overlap"][/sc]
  • Ask about breathlessness - often worse on exertion, but can also be episodic and nocturnal, indicating both fixed and variable obstruction.
  • Ask about wheeze - can be persistent or intermittent, and may respond partially to bronchodilators, reflecting asthmatic component.
  • Ask about cough - often chronic and productive, suggesting a COPD phenotype, but can also be dry and irritative.
  • Ask about chest tightness - a common complaint in both conditions, but its variability and response to treatment can help differentiate.
  • Ask about smoking history and environmental exposures - crucial for assessing the COPD contribution to the overlap syndrome.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Asthma-chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD) overlap"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - may show tachypnoea, tachycardia, or hypoxaemia during exacerbations, indicating respiratory distress.
  • Check chest auscultation - diffuse polyphonic wheeze and prolonged expiratory phase are common, sometimes with crackles if heart failure or infection is present.
  • Check signs of hyperinflation - barrel chest, reduced cricosternal distance, and Hoover's sign, indicative of chronic air trapping.
  • Check peripheral oedema - may suggest cor pulmonale in advanced disease.
  • Check for use of accessory muscles of respiration - indicates increased work of breathing, especially during exacerbations.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Asthma-chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD) overlap"][/sc]
  • Rapidly worsening breathlessness - suggests acute exacerbation or other acute respiratory pathology requiring urgent assessment.
  • New onset chest pain - may indicate pulmonary embolism, pneumonia, or cardiac ischaemia, especially in older patients.
  • Haemoptysis - warrants investigation for malignancy, bronchiectasis, or other serious lung pathology.
  • Cyanosis or altered consciousness - signs of severe hypoxaemia or hypercapnia, necessitating immediate medical intervention.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Asthma-chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD) overlap"][/sc]
  • Older adults: May present with less prominent wheeze and more chronic cough and breathlessness, often attributed solely to COPD, delaying recognition of the asthmatic component.
  • Non-smokers: ACO can occur in individuals without a smoking history, often linked to occupational exposures, severe childhood asthma, or biomass fuel exposure.
  • Frequent exacerbators: Patients with frequent exacerbations despite optimal therapy for either asthma or COPD alone may have undiagnosed ACO.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Asthma-chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD) overlap"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected ACO: Spirometry with bronchodilator reversibility testing is essential to demonstrate airflow obstruction and its partial reversibility.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Chest X-ray to exclude other lung pathologies; full blood count for eosinophilia; alpha-1 antitrypsin levels if early-onset emphysema.
  • Imaging - CT Thorax: High-resolution CT may be considered to assess for emphysema, bronchiectasis, or other structural lung disease, aiding in characterising the COPD component.
  • Serial reassessment: Regular spirometry and clinical review are crucial to monitor disease progression and response to treatment, as features can evolve over time.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Asthma-chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD) overlap"][/sc]
  • A 68-year-old male with a 40-pack-year smoking history presents with chronic productive cough, exertional breathlessness, and intermittent wheeze that partially responds to salbutamol, worsening over the past 5 years.
  • An older male with significant smoking exposure and chronic obstructive symptoms, exhibiting features of both fixed and variable airflow limitation, suggesting a combined aetiology.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Asthma-chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD) overlap"][/sc]
  • Asthma - significant bronchodilator reversibility, less smoking history, often allergic triggers.
  • COPD - minimal bronchodilator reversibility, strong smoking history, progressive and persistent symptoms.
  • Bronchiectasis - chronic productive cough, recurrent infections, characteristic CT findings.
  • Heart failure - orthopnoea, paroxysmal nocturnal dyspnoea, peripheral oedema, cardiac signs.
  • Pulmonary embolism - acute onset breathlessness, pleuritic chest pain, risk factors for VTE.
  • Lung cancer - weight loss, haemoptysis, persistent cough, focal chest signs.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Asthma-chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD) overlap"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Assess severity of exacerbation, administer oxygen if hypoxic, bronchodilators (short-acting beta-agonists and anticholinergics), and systemic corticosteroids.
  • Definitive management: Optimise inhaled corticosteroid (ICS) with long-acting beta-agonist (LABA) and/or long-acting muscarinic antagonist (LAMA) therapy, tailored to the predominant phenotype.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Regular review of symptoms, lung function, exacerbation frequency, and inhaler technique; consider pulmonary rehabilitation.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Provide a personalised action plan for exacerbations, advise on when to seek urgent medical attention, and ensure smoking cessation support.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Asthma-chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD) overlap"][/sc]
  • Frequent exacerbations: Due to combined inflammatory pathways, patients experience more severe and frequent acute worsening of respiratory symptoms, impacting quality of life and accelerating lung function decline.
  • Accelerated lung function decline: The presence of both asthmatic and COPD processes leads to a more rapid and irreversible loss of lung function compared to either condition alone, particularly beyond 5-10 years.
  • Cor pulmonale: Chronic hypoxaemia and pulmonary hypertension can lead to right-sided heart failure, a late-stage complication with significant morbidity and mortality.
  • Respiratory failure: Severe airflow obstruction and gas exchange abnormalities can result in acute or chronic respiratory failure, necessitating ventilatory support.
  • Reduced quality of life: Persistent symptoms, activity limitation, and psychological distress significantly impair daily living and overall well-being.
