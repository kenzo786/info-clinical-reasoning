Soft tissue injury [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Soft tissue injury"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Soft tissue injury"][/sc]
  • A common injury involving damage to muscles, ligaments, and tendons, often resulting from trauma or overuse.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the presentation and initial management of common soft tissue injuries.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Soft tissue injury"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Soft tissue injury"][/sc]
  • Soft tissue injuries are highly prevalent in the UK, with millions of new cases annually, particularly affecting active individuals and older adults.
  • Risk factors include participation in sports, occupational hazards, previous injury, and inadequate warm-up.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Soft tissue injury"][/sc]
  • Trigger: Direct trauma or repetitive strain leads to micro-tears or macroscopic disruption of connective tissues.
  • Progression: An inflammatory cascade is initiated, involving cellular infiltration and release of cytokines, leading to pain and swelling.
  • Consequence: Impaired tissue function, reduced range of motion, and potential for chronic pain or instability if not managed appropriately.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Soft tissue injury"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Acute pain and swelling typically resolve within the first few days to a week.
  • Progressive phase: Healing and rehabilitation can take several weeks, particularly beyond 2-3 weeks, with gradual restoration of function.
  • Late phase: Full recovery may extend to several months, with persistent symptoms or re-injury possible particularly beyond 6 weeks if rehabilitation is incomplete.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Soft tissue injury"][/sc]
  • Ask about pain - often localised, sharp or aching, and exacerbated by movement or weight-bearing.
  • Ask about swelling - may indicate fluid accumulation or inflammation around the injured area.
  • Ask about bruising - suggests underlying vascular damage and bleeding into the tissues.
  • Ask about reduced range of motion - important for assessing the severity of injury and functional impairment.
  • Ask about mechanism of injury - crucial for understanding the forces involved and potential structures affected.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Soft tissue injury"][/sc]
  • Check for localised tenderness - implies direct injury to underlying structures.
  • Check for swelling and ecchymosis - indicates inflammation and haemorrhage.
  • Check for reduced active and passive range of motion - suggests mechanical restriction or pain inhibition.
  • Check for instability on stress testing - may imply ligamentous laxity or rupture.
  • Check vital signs - important to rule out systemic involvement or significant blood loss in severe trauma.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Soft tissue injury"][/sc]
  • Severe, unremitting pain: May indicate fracture, compartment syndrome, or nerve impingement.
  • Inability to weight-bear: Suggests significant structural damage, such as a fracture or complete ligamentous tear.
  • Rapidly expanding swelling or pulsatile mass: Could signify vascular injury or haematoma requiring urgent intervention.
  • Fever or systemic signs of infection: Raises concern for septic arthritis or osteomyelitis, particularly after penetrating injury.
  • Neurological deficit distal to injury: Implies nerve damage or compression.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Soft tissue injury"][/sc]
  • Older adults: May present with less pronounced pain or swelling due to reduced inflammatory response, but with higher risk of fracture.
  • Children: Often present with non-specific symptoms; growth plate injuries must be considered, which can mimic soft tissue injuries.
  • Immunosuppressed patients: Increased risk of infection complicating soft tissue injuries, leading to delayed healing or atypical inflammatory signs.
  • Chronic pain syndromes: Previous soft tissue injuries can contribute to or exacerbate chronic regional pain, making diagnosis challenging.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Soft tissue injury"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected Soft tissue injury: Clinical examination is paramount, assessing tenderness, swelling, range of motion, and stability.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: X-ray to exclude fracture, particularly if red flags are present or mechanism suggests high impact.
  • Imaging - Ultrasound: May support diagnosis of tendon or ligament tears, fluid collections, or muscle haematomas.
  • Imaging - MRI: Considered for complex injuries, suspected complete tears, or when surgical planning is required.
  • Serial reassessment: Crucial to monitor for evolving symptoms, signs of complications, or delayed healing.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Soft tissue injury"][/sc]
  • A 35-year-old male presents with acute, localised pain and swelling in his ankle after twisting it during a football match yesterday, now struggling to bear weight.
  • Acute, traumatic, localised musculoskeletal pain with functional impairment, suggesting inflammatory or structural injury.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Soft tissue injury"][/sc]
  • Fracture - bony tenderness, crepitus, severe pain on weight-bearing.
  • Dislocation - obvious deformity, severe pain, complete loss of function.
  • Septic arthritis - fever, warmth, severe pain on passive movement, systemic signs of infection.
  • Gout/Pseudogout - acute monoarthritis, severe pain, redness, often in specific joints, elevated inflammatory markers.
  • Deep vein thrombosis (DVT) - unilateral leg swelling, pain, warmth, often without clear traumatic injury.
  • Cellulitis - spreading erythema, warmth, tenderness, often with a portal of entry for infection.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Soft tissue injury"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Pain relief, RICE (Rest, Ice, Compression, Elevation) principles, and assessment for red flags.
  • Definitive management: Gradual mobilisation and rehabilitation, often involving physiotherapy to restore strength and function.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Regular review to assess healing progress, pain control, and functional recovery.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Advise patient to seek urgent medical attention for worsening pain, new neurological symptoms, or signs of infection.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Soft tissue injury"][/sc]
  • Chronic pain: Persistent discomfort beyond the expected healing time, often due to incomplete rehabilitation or nerve sensitisation.
  • Joint instability: Resulting from inadequate healing of ligaments, leading to recurrent sprains or dislocations.
  • Reduced range of motion: Due to scar tissue formation, muscle guarding, or inadequate stretching during recovery.
  • Re-injury: Increased susceptibility to further injury if the affected area is not fully rehabilitated and strengthened.
  • Compartment syndrome: A rare but serious complication, particularly in severe trauma, where increased pressure within a muscle compartment compromises blood flow.
