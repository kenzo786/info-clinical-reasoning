Brain tumours (primary and secondary) [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Brain tumours (primary and secondary)"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Brain tumours (primary and secondary)"][/sc]
  • A brain tumour is an abnormal growth of cells within the brain or central nervous system. Primary brain tumours originate in the brain, while secondary (metastatic) brain tumours spread to the brain from a cancer elsewhere in the body [1].
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the varied presentations of primary and secondary brain tumours, understand their potential impact on neurological function, and identify features warranting urgent investigation.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Brain tumours (primary and secondary)"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Brain tumours (primary and secondary)"][/sc]
  • Approximately 9,000 new primary brain and central nervous system cancers are diagnosed each year in the UK [2].
  • Risk factors include increasing age, certain genetic conditions (e.g., neurofibromatosis, tuberous sclerosis), and previous radiotherapy [3, 4].
  • Secondary brain tumours (metastases) are more common than primary tumours, particularly in older adults, and originate from primary cancers such as lung, breast, melanoma, renal, and colorectal [5].
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Brain tumours (primary and secondary)"][/sc]
  • Trigger: Primary brain tumours arise from uncontrolled proliferation of glial cells or neurons within the brain, often due to genetic mutations. Secondary brain tumours result from metastatic spread of malignant cells from a primary cancer site elsewhere in the body [6].
  • Progression: Tumour growth leads to mass effect, causing compression of adjacent brain tissue, increased intracranial pressure, and disruption of normal cerebral blood flow. Tumours can also secrete factors (e.g., VEGF) that compromise the blood-brain barrier, leading to oedema [7].
  • Consequence: Localised brain dysfunction and global neurological deficits occur due to direct tissue destruction, compression, oedema, and altered neurotransmission, manifesting as a range of symptoms including seizures, focal weakness, and cognitive impairment.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Brain tumours (primary and secondary)"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Symptoms may be subtle and non-specific, developing gradually over weeks to months, particularly with slow-growing tumours. These can include mild headaches, personality changes, or subtle neurological deficits that may be initially overlooked.
  • Progressive phase: As the tumour grows, mass effect and oedema lead to worsening and more focal neurological symptoms. Headaches become more severe and persistent, seizures may occur, and specific deficits (e.g., weakness, visual field loss, speech difficulties) become more apparent, often progressing over days to weeks.
  • Late phase: Advanced disease is characterised by significant neurological impairment, increased intracranial pressure, and systemic decline. This may include profound cognitive dysfunction, severe motor deficits, intractable seizures, and reduced conscious level, typically progressing over days to a few weeks.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Brain tumours (primary and secondary)"][/sc]
  • Ask about headaches - particularly new-onset, worsening, or atypical headaches, especially if associated with nausea, vomiting, or neurological symptoms, as these may indicate raised intracranial pressure [8].
  • Ask about seizures - new-onset seizures in an adult, especially focal seizures, are a significant indicator of underlying structural brain pathology [9].
  • Ask about focal neurological deficits - such as unilateral weakness, sensory changes, visual field defects, or speech difficulties (dysphasia/aphasia), which help to localise the tumour [10].
  • Ask about cognitive or personality changes - including memory impairment, confusion, apathy, or behavioural alterations, which can be subtle and often attributed to other causes, particularly in frontal or temporal lobe tumours [11].
  • Ask about nausea and vomiting - especially if projectile or worse in the morning, suggesting increased intracranial pressure.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Brain tumours (primary and secondary)"][/sc]
  • Check neurological examination - focal neurological deficits (e.g., hemiparesis, sensory loss, cranial nerve palsies) indicate the anatomical location of the tumour.
  • Check fundoscopy - papilloedema suggests raised intracranial pressure, a critical sign requiring urgent attention.
  • Check visual fields - visual field defects (e.g., hemianopia, quadrantanopia) can localise tumours affecting the optic pathways.
  • Check gait and coordination - ataxia or dysmetria may indicate cerebellar involvement.
  • Check vital signs - Cushing's triad (hypertension, bradycardia, irregular respiration) is a late and ominous sign of severely raised intracranial pressure.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Brain tumours (primary and secondary)"][/sc]
  • New or worsening headache - particularly if persistent, severe, associated with nausea/vomiting, or worse in the morning, suggesting raised intracranial pressure [8].
  • New-onset seizure - in an adult with no prior history of epilepsy, this is a significant indicator of underlying structural brain pathology requiring urgent investigation [9].
  • Progressive focal neurological deficit - such as rapidly developing unilateral weakness, sensory loss, or speech disturbance, indicating an expanding lesion [10].
  • Papilloedema - detected on fundoscopy, is a direct sign of raised intracranial pressure and necessitates immediate neurological assessment [12].
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Brain tumours (primary and secondary)"][/sc]
  • Older adults: May present with insidious cognitive decline, personality changes, or gait disturbance, often misattributed to age-related conditions or dementia. Seizures may be less frequent at presentation compared to younger adults [13].
  • Pregnancy: Symptoms such as headaches, nausea, and vomiting can be masked by common pregnancy-related complaints, delaying diagnosis. Hormonal changes during pregnancy may also accelerate tumour growth [14].
  • Immunosuppressed individuals: Have an increased risk of certain brain tumours, such as primary central nervous system lymphoma (PCNSL). Presentation can be rapid and aggressive, with focal neurological deficits or altered mental status, and may mimic opportunistic infections.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Brain tumours (primary and secondary)"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected brain tumour: Initial assessment typically involves neurological examination and consideration of urgent imaging. Definitive diagnosis often requires a biopsy for histological analysis.
  • Imaging - CT: Often the first-line imaging modality due to its rapid acquisition, particularly in emergency settings or when MRI is contraindicated. It can identify mass effect, hydrocephalus, and haemorrhage.
  • Imaging - MRI: The most sensitive and specific imaging modality for brain tumours, providing high-resolution images that delineate tumour size, location, and relationship to critical structures. Contrast-enhanced MRI is crucial for characterising tumour vascularity and blood-brain barrier disruption [15].
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Depending on the clinical presentation, further investigations may include inflammatory markers, autoimmune screens, or CSF analysis to rule out conditions such as infection, demyelination, or autoimmune encephalitis.
  • Serial reassessment: Regular MRI scans are fundamental for monitoring tumour growth, assessing response to treatment, and detecting recurrence [16].
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Brain tumours (primary and secondary)"][/sc]
  • A 68-year-old male presents with a 3-month history of progressively worsening headaches, associated with intermittent nausea and recent onset of left-sided arm weakness. On examination, he has mild left hemiparesis and papilloedema.
  • An elderly male with subacute, progressive neurological deficits and signs of raised intracranial pressure.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Brain tumours (primary and secondary)"][/sc]
  • Stroke (Ischaemic or Haemorrhagic) - typically acute onset of focal neurological deficits, often with vascular risk factors.
  • Brain Abscess - usually presents with fever, headache, and focal neurological deficits, often with a history of infection or immunosuppression.
  • Multiple Sclerosis - relapsing-remitting or progressive neurological symptoms disseminated in space and time, often with optic neuritis or sensory disturbances.
  • Migraine with Aura - recurrent headaches with transient neurological symptoms, but typically without progressive deficits or signs of raised intracranial pressure.
  • Encephalitis - acute or subacute onset of fever, headache, altered mental status, and seizures, often with CSF pleocytosis.
  • Subdural Haematoma - can present insidiously, particularly in older adults, with fluctuating consciousness, headache, and focal neurological signs, often following minor head trauma.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Brain tumours (primary and secondary)"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Stabilisation of the patient, management of raised intracranial pressure (e.g., with corticosteroids), and control of seizures (e.g., with antiepileptic drugs).
  • Definitive management: Often involves a multidisciplinary approach including neurosurgery (resection or biopsy), radiotherapy, and chemotherapy, tailored to the tumour type, grade, and location.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Regular clinical review and serial imaging (typically MRI) are essential to monitor for tumour progression or recurrence and to manage treatment-related side effects [16].
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Patients and carers should be advised on symptoms of worsening neurological function or raised intracranial pressure, and when to seek urgent medical attention.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Brain tumours (primary and secondary)"][/sc]
  • Raised Intracranial Pressure (ICP) and Herniation: Tumour growth and associated oedema can lead to dangerously elevated ICP, risking brain herniation, which is a life-threatening event with severe neurological sequelae or death [17].
  • Seizures: Brain tumours are a common cause of symptomatic epilepsy. Seizures can be debilitating, impact quality of life, and indicate tumour irritation of cortical tissue [9].
  • Progressive Neurological Deficits: Ongoing tumour expansion or recurrence can lead to worsening focal neurological deficits (e.g., hemiparesis, dysphasia, visual loss), significantly impacting functional independence and prognosis [10].
  • Cognitive Impairment: Tumour location, mass effect, and treatment side effects can result in significant cognitive dysfunction, including memory loss, executive dysfunction, and personality changes, which are often progressive and have a major impact on daily living [11].
  • Hydrocephalus: Tumours obstructing cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) pathways can cause hydrocephalus, leading to further increases in ICP and neurological deterioration if not managed promptly.
