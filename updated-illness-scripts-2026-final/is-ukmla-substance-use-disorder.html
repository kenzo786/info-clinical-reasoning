Substance use disorder [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Substance use disorder"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Substance use disorder"][/sc]
  • A problematic pattern of substance use leading to clinically significant impairment or distress, characterised by uncontrolled use despite harmful consequences.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise and manage common presentations of substance use disorder, understanding its impact on physical and mental health, and social functioning.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Substance use disorder"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Substance use disorder"][/sc]
  • In England and Wales, approximately 6.7% of people aged 16 to 64 years showed signs of drug dependence in the year ending March 2025 (ONS), an increase from previous years. Men (4.3%) are more likely to be dependent on illegal drugs than women (1.9%).
  • Risk factors include a family history of addiction, adverse childhood experiences (e.g., homelessness, disrupted family life), co-occurring mental health problems, and peer substance use.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Substance use disorder"][/sc]
  • Initial substance exposure triggers a powerful activation of the brain\'s reward system, particularly the mesolimbic dopamine pathway, leading to intense pleasure and reinforcement of the behaviour.
  • Chronic substance use induces significant neuroadaptations in brain regions governing reward, motivation, memory, and executive function, resulting in tolerance, withdrawal symptoms, and an escalating, compulsive drive for the substance. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Neuroadaptations in addiction: what are they and why do they matter clinically?" text="neuroadaptations"][/sc]
  • These long-lasting alterations in neural circuitry lead to a dysfunctional reward system, impaired decision-making, and reduced inhibitory control, perpetuating substance-seeking behaviours despite adverse physical, psychological, and social consequences. Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Substance use disorder"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Characterised by experimentation and regular, often social, use of a substance. Individuals may experience initial positive reinforcement and a perceived ability to control their use.
  • Progressive phase: Marked by increasing frequency and quantity of substance use, leading to tolerance and the emergence of withdrawal symptoms upon cessation. The individual may start to experience negative consequences but continues use, particularly beyond several weeks or months of regular engagement.
  • Late phase: Defined by compulsive substance-seeking behaviour, loss of control over use, and significant functional impairment across multiple life domains. This phase often involves chronic relapse and severe health, social, and psychological complications, particularly beyond prolonged periods of uncontrolled use.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Substance use disorder"][/sc]
  • Ask about intense cravings - indicates psychological dependence and a strong predictor of relapse.
  • Ask about loss of control over substance use (e.g., using more or for longer than intended) - a cardinal feature reflecting impaired inhibitory control.
  • Ask about continued use despite negative consequences (e.g., health problems, relationship issues, legal troubles) - highlights the compulsive nature of the disorder and impaired judgment.
  • Ask about neglect of important activities (e.g., work, social, recreational) due to substance use - signifies functional impairment and the prioritisation of substance use.
  • Ask about withdrawal symptoms when attempting to cut down or stop - suggests physiological dependence and can complicate cessation efforts.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Substance use disorder"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - may reveal tachycardia, hypertension, or hyperthermia during withdrawal, or bradycardia and hypotension in acute intoxication, indicating physiological stress or overdose risk.
  • Check for track marks, skin abscesses, or cellulitis - suggests intravenous drug use and associated complications, requiring urgent medical attention.
  • Check pupillary changes (e.g., miosis or mydriasis) - can be indicative of specific substance intoxication or withdrawal, aiding in differential diagnosis.
  • Check for tremors, ataxia, or seizures - common neurological signs of acute intoxication or withdrawal syndromes, particularly with alcohol or benzodiazepines. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Withdrawal syndromes: what are they and why do they matter clinically?" text="Withdrawal syndromes"][/sc]
  • Check general appearance and hygiene - often reflects self-neglect, malnutrition, or chronic health issues associated with prolonged substance use.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Substance use disorder"][/sc]
  • Signs of overdose: Indicates an immediate life-threatening emergency requiring urgent medical intervention (e.g., respiratory depression, altered consciousness, pinpoint pupils with opioid use).
  • Severe withdrawal symptoms: Suggests significant physiological dependence and a high risk of severe complications such as seizures, delirium tremens, or cardiac arrhythmias, necessitating supervised medical detoxification.
  • Suicidal ideation or attempts: Highlights the high comorbidity of substance use disorder with mental health conditions and the urgent need for comprehensive mental health assessment and crisis intervention.
  • Acute physical complications: Such as gastrointestinal haemorrhage, endocarditis, or severe liver dysfunction, indicating organ damage directly attributable to substance use and requiring immediate medical management.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Substance use disorder"][/sc]
  • Older Adults: Substance use disorder may be overlooked or misdiagnosed, as symptoms can be attributed to normal ageing, co-morbid medical conditions, or polypharmacy. This can lead to delayed intervention and increased risk of adverse outcomes. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Substance use disorder in older adults: what are the challenges in diagnosis and why does it matter clinically?" text="challenges in diagnosis"][/sc]
  • Pregnancy: Presentation may be concealed due to fear of stigma or legal consequences, delaying access to crucial antenatal care and treatment. Substance use during pregnancy carries significant risks for both maternal and foetal health, including neonatal abstinence syndrome.
  • Immunosuppressed Patients: Chronic substance use, especially intravenous drug use, can lead to compromised immune function. Patients may present with severe or atypical infections, complicating the clinical picture and requiring careful consideration of underlying substance use.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Substance use disorder"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected Substance use disorder: Comprehensive clinical assessment, including a detailed history of substance use patterns, consequences, and attempts to cut down. Standardised screening tools such as the AUDIT-C for Alcohol Use on MDCalc, CAGE Questions for Alcohol Use on MDCalc, or Drug Abuse Screening Test-10 (DAST-10) on MDCalc can aid in identification.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Consider blood tests (e.g., liver function tests, renal function tests, full blood count) to assess for organ damage or co-morbidities. Urine or blood toxicology screens may confirm recent substance use but do not diagnose a disorder. Mental health assessment to rule out primary psychiatric conditions.
  • Imaging - [modality]: Neuroimaging (e.g., MRI, CT brain) is not typically used for diagnosing SUD itself but may be indicated to investigate neurological complications (e.g., stroke, infection, Wernicke-Korsakoff syndrome) or to rule out other causes of presenting symptoms. Chest X-ray may be used to investigate respiratory complications like aspiration pneumonia.
  • Serial reassessment: Ongoing clinical interviews and repeated use of screening tools are crucial to monitor progress, identify relapse, and adjust treatment plans.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Substance use disorder"][/sc]
  • A 35-year-old male presents with a 6-month history of escalating alcohol consumption, experiencing daily cravings, recurrent unsuccessful attempts to cut down, and increasing neglect of professional responsibilities, alongside episodes of tremulousness and anxiety when abstinent.
  • Semantic qualifiers: Chronic, relapsing, compulsive, impairing, biopsychosocial, progressive.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Substance use disorder"][/sc]
  • Primary Depressive Disorder: While depression can co-occur with SUD, primary depression may present with anhedonia, low mood, and fatigue without the compulsive substance-seeking behaviour or loss of control characteristic of SUD.
  • Primary Anxiety Disorder: Anxiety symptoms can be a prominent feature of withdrawal or intoxication, but in primary anxiety disorders, the anxiety is not directly attributable to substance effects or withdrawal and often precedes substance use.
  • Bipolar Disorder: Manic or hypomanic episodes can involve impulsive behaviours and increased risk-taking that might mimic aspects of SUD, but the cyclical mood shifts and other core features of bipolar disorder distinguish it.
  • Schizophrenia and other Psychotic Disorders: Psychotic symptoms (hallucinations, delusions) can be induced by certain substances (e.g., stimulants, hallucinogens). In primary psychotic disorders, these symptoms occur independently of acute substance intoxication or withdrawal.
  • Attention-Deficit/Hyperactivity Disorder (ADHD): Impulsivity and difficulty with executive function in ADHD can contribute to substance use. However, ADHD symptoms are typically lifelong and not solely linked to substance use, and the primary goal is not substance-seeking.
  • Personality Disorders (e.g., Borderline Personality Disorder, Antisocial Personality Disorder): These disorders often involve impulsive behaviour, emotional dysregulation, and interpersonal difficulties that can lead to or exacerbate substance use. The pervasive and enduring patterns of thought and behaviour across multiple contexts distinguish them from SUD alone.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Substance use disorder"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Stabilisation of acute intoxication or withdrawal syndromes, management of co-occurring medical emergencies (e.g., overdose, trauma, infection), and addressing acute psychiatric crises (e.g., suicidality, psychosis).
  • Definitive management: A comprehensive, individualised, and multidisciplinary approach often includes psychosocial interventions (e.g., cognitive behavioural therapy, motivational interviewing, contingency management), pharmacotherapy where indicated, and engagement with mutual aid groups.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Regular assessment of substance use patterns, physical and mental health status, social functioning, and treatment adherence. Relapse prevention planning and ongoing support are crucial.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Clear pathways for escalation to more intensive support (e.g., inpatient detoxification, residential rehabilitation) if outpatient treatment is insufficient or unsafe. Safety-netting advice includes crisis contact numbers and overdose prevention strategies.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Substance use disorder"][/sc]
  • Liver Disease: Chronic alcohol use can lead to alcoholic hepatitis, steatosis, and cirrhosis, while intravenous drug use increases the risk of viral hepatitis (B and C). These conditions can progress to liver failure, portal hypertension, and hepatocellular carcinoma, significantly impacting prognosis.
  • Cardiovascular Complications: Stimulant use can cause acute myocardial infarction, arrhythmias, and cardiomyopathy. Intravenous drug use is a major risk factor for infective endocarditis, which can lead to valvular damage, heart failure, and systemic emboli.
  • Neurological Damage: Prolonged alcohol use can result in Wernicke-Korsakoff syndrome, peripheral neuropathy, and cerebellar degeneration. Stimulant use can lead to strokes and seizures.
  • Mental Health Disorders: High comorbidity with depression, anxiety disorders, psychosis, and personality disorders. Substance use can exacerbate existing mental health conditions or induce new ones.
  • Social and Legal Consequences: Impaired social functioning, unemployment, homelessness, incarceration, and strained relationships are common.
