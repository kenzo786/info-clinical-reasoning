Turner syndrome [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Turner syndrome"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Turner syndrome"][/sc]
  • Turner syndrome is a genetic condition that affects females, resulting from the complete or partial absence of one of the two X chromosomes. It is characterised by short stature, ovarian dysfunction, and a range of other physical features and medical issues [1, 2, 3].
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical features, genetic basis, and common complications associated with Turner syndrome to guide appropriate investigation and management [4].
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Turner syndrome"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Turner syndrome"][/sc]
  • Affects approximately 1 in 2,000 to 1 in 2,500 live female births in the UK [5, 6].
  • It is the only viable monosomy syndrome in humans [5].
  • Risk factors are not typically associated with parental age or environmental exposures, as the chromosomal abnormality usually occurs randomly during the formation of reproductive cells or early fetal development [7].
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Turner syndrome"][/sc]
  • Trigger: Turner syndrome arises from the complete or partial absence of one X chromosome (monosomy X, 45,X karyotype), typically occurring randomly during gamete formation or early embryonic development [5, 7, 8, 9].
  • Progression: This chromosomal abnormality results in haploinsufficiency of critical genes on the X chromosome, which are essential for normal female growth and development across multiple organ systems [8].
  • Consequence: The lack of these genes leads to characteristic features such as short stature, ovarian dysgenesis (causing primary amenorrhoea and infertility), and an increased risk of congenital anomalies, particularly affecting the cardiovascular and renal systems [1, 8, 9].
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Turner syndrome"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Features such as nuchal translucency or hydrops fetalis may be detected prenatally. At birth, some infants may present with oedema of the hands and feet, or a webbed neck. However, many cases are not diagnosed until later childhood or adolescence [10, 11].
  • Progressive phase: Short stature typically becomes evident by approximately five years of age, and growth deceleration continues throughout childhood. Puberty is often delayed or absent, particularly beyond 12-13 years, due to ovarian dysfunction [1, 10].
  • Late phase: Adulthood is characterised by infertility in most affected individuals. There is an increased risk of long-term complications, including cardiovascular disease (e.g., aortic coarctation, bicuspid aortic valve), renal anomalies, hypertension, and autoimmune conditions, necessitating lifelong monitoring [9, 10, 12].
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Turner syndrome"][/sc]
  • Ask about growth trajectory - to identify short stature or growth deceleration, a cardinal feature of Turner syndrome [1, 10].
  • Ask about pubertal development - delayed or absent puberty is common due to ovarian insufficiency, impacting fertility and bone health [1, 10].
  • Ask about cardiac symptoms (e.g., chest pain, breathlessness, palpitations) - to screen for congenital heart defects like coarctation of the aorta or bicuspid aortic valve, which require early detection and management [9, 12].
  • Ask about recurrent ear infections or hearing difficulties - increased susceptibility to otitis media and sensorineural hearing loss are common [13].
  • Ask about learning difficulties or specific cognitive challenges - individuals may experience difficulties with visuospatial tasks, mathematics, and executive function [14].
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Turner syndrome"][/sc]
  • Check height and growth velocity - to confirm short stature and monitor growth patterns against Turner syndrome-specific growth charts [1, 10].
  • Check for characteristic dysmorphic features - such as a webbed neck, low posterior hairline, low-set ears, and a broad chest with widely spaced nipples, which are suggestive of the condition [1, 15].
  • Check for peripheral oedema - particularly of the hands and feet (lymphoedema), which may be present at birth or develop later [11, 15].
  • Check vital signs - regular blood pressure monitoring is crucial due to the increased risk of hypertension, often associated with renal or cardiovascular anomalies [12, 16].
  • Check for cubitus valgus - an increased carrying angle of the elbow, which is a common skeletal manifestation [15]. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Cubitus valgus: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="cubitus valgus"][/sc]
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Turner syndrome"][/sc]
  • Congenital cardiac defects: Particularly coarctation of the aorta or bicuspid aortic valve, which can lead to serious cardiovascular complications if undiagnosed [9, 12].
  • Significant growth deceleration: A persistent drop in growth centiles or severe short stature, especially if noted early in childhood, warrants investigation for Turner syndrome [1, 10].
  • Primary amenorrhoea or absent pubertal development: Lack of menarche or development of secondary sexual characteristics by the expected age, indicating ovarian failure [1, 10].
  • Severe or persistent lymphedema: Oedema of the hands and feet, particularly in infancy, which may indicate lymphatic system abnormalities associated with Turner syndrome [11, 15].
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Turner syndrome"][/sc]
  • Mosaic Turner syndrome: Occurs when some cells have the 45,X karyotype, while others have a normal 46,XX karyotype or other variations. This can lead to a milder phenotype with fewer or less severe clinical features, and sometimes even spontaneous puberty or fertility [17, 18].
  • Partial X chromosome deletions: Instead of a complete missing X chromosome, a portion of one X chromosome is deleted. The clinical presentation can vary depending on the size and location of the deleted segment, potentially leading to a less typical constellation of symptoms [19].
  • Late diagnosis in adulthood: Some individuals with milder forms of Turner syndrome, particularly those with mosaicism, may not be diagnosed until adulthood, often presenting with infertility or premature ovarian insufficiency as the primary concern, with other features being subtle or overlooked [10, 17].
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Turner syndrome"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected Turner syndrome: Karyotyping (chromosomal analysis) is the definitive diagnostic test, confirming the presence of 45,X or a variant. This can be performed postnatally via a blood test or prenatally through amniocentesis or chorionic villus sampling [20, 21]. Fluorescent in situ hybridisation (FISH) and chromosomal microarray (CMA) may also be used [22].
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: While karyotyping is diagnostic for Turner syndrome, other investigations may be considered to rule out other causes of short stature, delayed puberty, or congenital anomalies, such as thyroid function tests or pituitary hormone assays if endocrine dysfunction is suspected [23].
  • Imaging - Cardiac: Echocardiogram is essential to screen for congenital heart defects, such as bicuspid aortic valve, coarctation of the aorta, and partial anomalous pulmonary venous return, which are common in Turner syndrome [12, 24].
  • Imaging - Renal: Renal ultrasound is recommended to identify structural abnormalities of the kidneys, such as horseshoe kidney or collecting system anomalies, which are also frequently associated with the condition [12, 24].
  • Serial reassessment: Regular monitoring of growth, pubertal development, and blood pressure is crucial. Periodic cardiac imaging may be required depending on initial findings and clinical course [12].
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Turner syndrome"][/sc]
  • A 14-year-old female presents with short stature, lack of breast development, and primary amenorrhoea. She has a history of recurrent ear infections and a webbed neck noted in early childhood.
  • A young female with a congenital chromosomal anomaly, presenting with growth failure, primary gonadal dysfunction, and characteristic dysmorphic features, requiring comprehensive multidisciplinary management.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Turner syndrome"][/sc]
  • Noonan syndrome: Shares features like short stature, webbed neck, and cardiac defects (pulmonary stenosis is common, unlike aortic coarctation in Turner syndrome), but affects both sexes and has a normal karyotype [25].
  • 46,XX gonadal dysgenesis: Presents with primary amenorrhoea and lack of secondary sexual characteristics due to ovarian failure, but individuals have a normal female karyotype (46,XX) and typically do not have the somatic features of Turner syndrome [26].
  • 46,XY complete gonadal dysgenesis (Swyer syndrome): Individuals are phenotypically female with primary amenorrhoea and streak gonads, but have a male karyotype (46,XY) and lack the typical somatic features of Turner syndrome [27].
  • SHOX-related short stature: Caused by mutations in the SHOX gene, which is located on both X and Y chromosomes. It leads to short stature and skeletal abnormalities (e.g., Madelung deformity), but without the gonadal dysfunction or other dysmorphic features of Turner syndrome [28].
  • Multiple pterygium syndrome (Escobar syndrome): Can present with webbing of the neck and joint contractures, mimicking some features of Turner syndrome, but is an autosomal recessive disorder with a normal karyotype and distinct facial features and musculoskeletal anomalies [29].
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Turner syndrome"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Early diagnosis and comprehensive assessment are crucial to identify and address congenital anomalies, particularly cardiac (e.g., coarctation of the aorta) and renal abnormalities, which may require immediate intervention [12, 30].
  • Definitive management:
    • Growth hormone therapy: Often initiated in childhood to improve final adult height [30, 31].
    • Oestrogen replacement therapy (ERT): Typically commenced around the age of normal puberty to induce secondary sexual characteristics, maintain bone mineral density, and support uterine development. This usually involves a gradual increase in oestrogen dosage [30, 32].
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Lifelong multidisciplinary care is essential, including regular monitoring of cardiovascular health (echocardiograms, blood pressure), renal function, thyroid function, bone health, and hearing. Psychological support and counselling are also important [30, 33].
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Any new or worsening cardiac symptoms (e.g., chest pain, syncope), significant changes in blood pressure, or signs of new organ dysfunction should prompt urgent specialist review. Patients should be educated on the importance of adherence to hormone therapy and regular follow-up appointments [30].

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Turner syndrome"][/sc]
  • Cardiovascular disease: Congenital heart defects (e.g., bicuspid aortic valve, coarctation of the aorta) are common and can lead to aortic dissection, heart failure, and hypertension, significantly impacting long-term morbidity and mortality [12, 34].
  • Ovarian failure and infertility: The primary gonadal dysfunction results in premature ovarian insufficiency, leading to primary amenorrhoea and infertility in the vast majority of affected individuals, necessitating hormone replacement therapy [1, 10].
  • Renal anomalies: Structural abnormalities of the kidneys (e.g., horseshoe kidney, duplicated collecting systems) are frequent, increasing the risk of urinary tract infections, hypertension, and renal dysfunction [12, 24].
  • Osteoporosis: Chronic oestrogen deficiency due to ovarian failure contributes to reduced bone mineral density and an increased risk of osteoporosis and fractures, particularly if hormone replacement therapy is not initiated or adhered to [30, 35].
  • Autoimmune conditions: There is an increased predisposition to autoimmune disorders, including Hashimoto\'s thyroiditis, coeliac disease, and inflammatory bowel disease, which require vigilant screening and management [30, 36].
