Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Thyroid cancer (Ear, nose and throat)"][/sc]
  • Thyroid cancer is a malignancy arising from the follicular or parafollicular cells of the thyroid gland, with varying histological types and prognoses.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical features suggestive of thyroid malignancy, initiate appropriate urgent referral pathways, and understand the principles of initial investigation and management.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Thyroid cancer (Ear, nose and throat)"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Thyroid cancer"][/sc]
  • Thyroid cancer is relatively rare, accounting for about 1% of all cancers in the UK, with approximately 3,900 new cases diagnosed annually [1].
  • It is more common in women than men, with a female-to-male ratio of approximately 3:1, and typically affects individuals between 20 and 60 years of age, though anaplastic thyroid cancer often presents in older adults [1].
  • Risk factors include exposure to radiation, a family history of thyroid cancer or certain genetic syndromes (e.g., MEN2 for medullary thyroid cancer), and certain benign thyroid conditions [1].
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Thyroid cancer (Ear, nose and throat)"][/sc]
  • Genetic mutations (e.g., BRAF in papillary, RAS in follicular, RET in medullary) or environmental factors (e.g., radiation exposure) can trigger abnormal proliferation of thyroid cells.
  • This leads to the uncontrolled growth and differentiation of thyroid cells, forming a nodule or mass within the gland, which may invade local structures or metastasise.
  • The consequence is a functional or structural compromise of the thyroid gland and surrounding tissues, potentially leading to symptoms such as dysphagia, dyspnoea, or hoarseness, and systemic effects if distant metastases occur. Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Thyroid cancer (Ear, nose and throat)"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Often asymptomatic, with a thyroid nodule discovered incidentally on imaging or routine physical examination. Growth is typically slow for differentiated thyroid cancers.
  • Progressive phase: As the tumour enlarges, local symptoms such as a palpable neck lump, dysphagia, or hoarseness may develop over months to years. Aggressive variants like anaplastic thyroid cancer progress rapidly over weeks to months.
  • Late phase: Advanced disease may involve significant local invasion, leading to severe airway compromise or recurrent laryngeal nerve palsy, and distant metastases, particularly beyond months for aggressive types.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Thyroid cancer (Ear, nose and throat)"][/sc]
  • Ask about a painless neck lump - often the presenting symptom, but most thyroid nodules are benign; rapid growth or firmness raises suspicion.
  • Ask about changes in voice (hoarseness) - suggests possible recurrent laryngeal nerve involvement due to local tumour invasion.
  • Ask about difficulty swallowing (dysphagia) - indicates compression of the oesophagus by a large tumour or local invasion.
  • Ask about difficulty breathing (dyspnoea) - a red flag symptom suggesting tracheal compression, particularly with rapidly growing tumours like anaplastic thyroid cancer.
  • Ask about a history of radiation exposure to the head and neck - a significant risk factor for differentiated thyroid cancers.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Thyroid cancer (Ear, nose and throat)"][/sc]
  • Check for a palpable thyroid nodule - assess size, consistency (firm/hard), mobility (fixed to surrounding structures), and presence of associated lymphadenopathy.
  • Check vital signs - usually normal in early differentiated thyroid cancer; may show signs of respiratory distress in advanced disease or anaplastic variants.
  • Check for cervical lymphadenopathy - particularly firm, non-tender, enlarged nodes, which may indicate metastatic spread.
  • Check vocal cord mobility via laryngoscopy - to assess for recurrent laryngeal nerve palsy, which can be a sign of local invasion. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Recurrent laryngeal nerve palsy in thyroid cancer: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Recurrent laryngeal nerve palsy"][/sc]
  • Check for signs of MEN2 (e.g., pheochromocytoma, parathyroid hyperplasia) - relevant for medullary thyroid cancer.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Thyroid cancer (Ear, nose and throat)"][/sc]
  • Rapidly enlarging neck lump: Suggests aggressive tumour growth, particularly anaplastic thyroid cancer, requiring urgent investigation.
  • Fixed, hard thyroid nodule: Indicates local invasion into surrounding structures, increasing the likelihood of malignancy.
  • Hoarseness or dysphagia of new onset: Points to recurrent laryngeal nerve involvement or oesophageal compression, necessitating prompt evaluation.
  • Cervical lymphadenopathy: Enlarged, firm, non-tender lymph nodes in the neck can signify metastatic disease.
  • NICE NG12 urgent referral criteria: An unexplained thyroid lump or a thyroid mass associated with hoarseness, stridor, or dysphagia warrants urgent suspected cancer pathway referral to a thyroid surgeon or endocrinologist [2].
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Thyroid cancer (Ear, nose and throat)"][/sc]
  • Older adults: May present with more aggressive disease or anaplastic variants, often with rapid progression and poorer prognosis.
  • Children: Thyroid cancer is rare but tends to be more aggressive, with a higher incidence of lymph node and distant metastases at presentation.
  • Medullary thyroid cancer (MTC): Can present with diarrhoea or flushing due to calcitonin secretion, especially in the context of Multiple Endocrine Neoplasia type 2 (MEN2). [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Medullary thyroid cancer presentation: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Medullary thyroid cancer"][/sc]
  • Incidentaloma: Thyroid nodules discovered incidentally on imaging (e.g., carotid ultrasound, CT neck) performed for unrelated reasons, often asymptomatic.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Thyroid cancer (Ear, nose and throat)"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected thyroid cancer:
    • Thyroid function tests (TSH, free T4, free T3) to assess thyroid status, though often normal in malignancy.
    • Ultrasound of the neck to characterise the nodule (size, echogenicity, margins, calcifications, vascularity) and assess for lymphadenopathy [2].
    • Fine-needle aspiration cytology (FNAC) with ultrasound guidance for suspicious nodules, reported using the British Thyroid Association (BTA) classification system (Thy1-Thy5) [2].
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible:
    • Calcitonin measurement if medullary thyroid cancer is suspected (family history, MEN2, suspicious ultrasound features) [2]. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Calcitonin in medullary thyroid cancer: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Calcitonin"][/sc]
    • Genetic testing for RET proto-oncogene mutations if medullary thyroid cancer is confirmed or strongly suspected, especially in familial cases or MEN2.
  • Imaging - CT/MRI: For assessing local invasion or distant metastases in advanced disease, particularly for anaplastic thyroid cancer or large differentiated tumours [2].
  • Severity/risk tools: ATA Risk Stratification System on MDCalc ATA Risk Stratification System for Differentiated Thyroid Cancer on MDCalc

  • Middle-aged female with a progressive, firm thyroid mass and recurrent laryngeal nerve symptoms, concerning for thyroid malignancy.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Thyroid cancer (Ear, nose and throat)"][/sc]
  • Benign thyroid nodule: Most thyroid nodules are benign; distinguished by ultrasound features (e.g., cystic components, smooth margins) and benign FNAC results (Thy2).
  • Multinodular goitre: Multiple thyroid nodules, often asymptomatic or causing compressive symptoms; distinguished by multiple nodules on ultrasound and typically benign FNAC.
  • Thyroiditis (e.g., Hashimoto\'s, subacute): Inflammatory conditions causing thyroid enlargement and tenderness; distinguished by pain, systemic symptoms, and characteristic thyroid function tests/autoantibodies.
  • Thyroid cyst: Fluid-filled lesions within the thyroid; distinguished by cystic appearance on ultrasound and aspiration of fluid.
  • Lymphoma of the thyroid: Rare, rapidly growing thyroid mass, often associated with Hashimoto\'s thyroiditis; distinguished by biopsy. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Thyroid lymphoma: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Thyroid lymphoma"][/sc]
  • Metastatic disease to the thyroid: Rare, but other primary cancers (e.g., renal cell carcinoma, breast cancer) can metastasise to the thyroid; distinguished by history of primary malignancy and biopsy.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Thyroid cancer (Ear, nose and throat)"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Urgent referral to a specialist multidisciplinary team (MDT) for evaluation and management, especially if red flag features are present (NICE NG12).
  • Definitive management:
    • Surgery (hemithyroidectomy or total thyroidectomy) is the primary treatment for most thyroid cancers, with the extent depending on tumour type, size, and stage [2].
    • Radioactive iodine (RAI) therapy may be considered post-surgery for differentiated thyroid cancers to ablate residual thyroid tissue or treat metastatic disease [2].
    • External beam radiotherapy or chemotherapy may be used for aggressive or anaplastic thyroid cancers, or in cases of extensive local invasion or distant metastases [2].
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Lifelong monitoring with thyroid function tests, thyroglobulin levels (for differentiated thyroid cancer), and imaging is typically required to detect recurrence [2].

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Thyroid cancer (Ear, nose and throat)"][/sc]
  • Recurrent laryngeal nerve injury: Can occur during surgery, leading to hoarseness or vocal cord paralysis, impacting speech and airway protection.
  • Hypoparathyroidism: Damage to parathyroid glands during surgery can result in hypocalcaemia, requiring calcium and vitamin D supplementation.
  • Local recurrence: Persistence or regrowth of cancer in the thyroid bed or regional lymph nodes, often detected during follow-up months to years post-treatment.
  • Distant metastases: Spread to lungs, bones, or other organs, particularly in advanced or aggressive forms, impacting prognosis and requiring systemic treatment.
  • Thyroid storm: Although rare, can be precipitated by excessive thyroid hormone replacement or manipulation of the gland, leading to life-threatening hypermetabolic state.
