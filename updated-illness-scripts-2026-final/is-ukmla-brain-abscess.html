Brain abscess [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Brain abscess"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Brain abscess"][/sc]
  • A brain abscess is a focal collection of pus within the brain parenchyma, typically resulting from an infection elsewhere in the body or direct inoculation.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical features, risk factors, and diagnostic approach to a patient presenting with suspected brain abscess.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Brain abscess"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Brain abscess"][/sc]
  • Brain abscesses are relatively rare, with an incidence of approximately 1-2 per 100,000 population in the UK, though higher in immunocompromised individuals.
  • Risk factors often include otitis media, sinusitis, dental infections, pulmonary infections, endocarditis, and recent neurosurgery or head trauma.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Brain abscess"][/sc]
  • Infection typically originates from a contiguous source (e.g., paranasal sinuses, middle ear) or haematogenous spread from a distant site, leading to cerebritis.
  • Progressive inflammation and necrosis within the brain parenchyma result in the formation of a central necrotic core surrounded by a collagen capsule.
  • The expanding mass effect and surrounding oedema can lead to increased intracranial pressure and focal neurological deficits.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Brain abscess"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Symptoms may be insidious, developing over days to weeks, often presenting with non-specific signs of infection or headache.
  • Progressive phase: As the abscess enlarges, symptoms of raised intracranial pressure and focal neurological deficits typically become more pronounced, particularly beyond two weeks.
  • Late phase: Without intervention, rapid neurological deterioration, seizures, or rupture into the ventricles can occur, particularly beyond four weeks.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Brain abscess"][/sc]
  • Ask about headache - often severe, progressive, and unresponsive to simple analgesia, indicating raised intracranial pressure.
  • Ask about fever and chills - may be present, but can be absent in up to 50% of cases, especially in immunocompromised patients.
  • Ask about focal neurological deficits - such as weakness, sensory changes, or speech difficulties, localising the lesion.
  • Ask about altered mental status - ranging from lethargy to confusion or coma, suggesting significant intracranial pathology.
  • Ask about seizures - new-onset seizures can be a presenting feature due to cortical irritation.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Brain abscess"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - fever may be present, but can be absent; observe for signs of sepsis or haemodynamic instability.
  • Check neurological examination - look for focal deficits such as hemiparesis, aphasia, or cranial nerve palsies.
  • Check fundoscopy - papilloedema may indicate raised intracranial pressure, though its absence does not rule it out.
  • Check neck stiffness - may be present if there is meningeal irritation, but is not a consistent finding.
  • Check for signs of primary infection - such as otitis media, sinusitis, or dental abscess, which may be the source.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Brain abscess"][/sc]
  • Rapid neurological deterioration - suggests acute worsening of mass effect or complications like rupture.
  • New-onset focal neurological deficit - indicates an evolving intracranial lesion requiring urgent investigation.
  • Signs of raised intracranial pressure - such as papilloedema, Cushing's triad, or persistent vomiting, necessitating immediate neurosurgical review.
  • Immunocompromised state with neurological symptoms - significantly increases the risk of opportunistic infections including brain abscess.
