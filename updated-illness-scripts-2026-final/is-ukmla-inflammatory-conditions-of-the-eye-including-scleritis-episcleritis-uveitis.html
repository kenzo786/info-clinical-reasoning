Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Inflammatory conditions of the eye (including scleritis, episcleritis, uveitis)"][/sc]
  • Inflammatory conditions of the eye encompass a spectrum of disorders characterised by immune-mediated inflammation affecting various ocular structures.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise and differentiate between common inflammatory eye conditions, understanding their clinical presentation, potential complications, and initial management.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Inflammatory conditions of the eye (including scleritis, episcleritis, uveitis)"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Inflammatory conditions of the eye (including scleritis, episcleritis, uveitis)"][/sc]
  • Episcleritis is a common, benign condition, with an estimated incidence of 41 per 100,000 person-years in the UK, often affecting young to middle-aged adults [1].
  • Scleritis is less common but more severe, with an incidence of 5.2 per 100,000 person-years, and is frequently associated with systemic autoimmune diseases in 30-50% of cases [2].
  • Uveitis has a global incidence ranging from 17 to 52 per 100,000 person-years, with anterior uveitis being the most frequent form, and can be idiopathic or linked to systemic conditions like spondyloarthropathies [3].
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Inflammatory conditions of the eye (including scleritis, episcleritis, uveitis)"][/sc]
  • Immune dysregulation or external triggers (e.g., infection, trauma) initiate an inflammatory cascade within ocular tissues, leading to cellular infiltration and mediator release.
  • Persistent inflammation causes tissue damage, oedema, and potentially structural changes, such as scleral thinning in scleritis or synechiae formation in uveitis.
  • Uncontrolled inflammation can result in irreversible vision loss due to complications like glaucoma, cataract, or retinal damage.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Inflammatory conditions of the eye (including scleritis, episcleritis, uveitis)"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Acute onset of redness and discomfort, often localised, which may resolve spontaneously (episcleritis) or progress rapidly (scleritis, acute anterior uveitis).
  • Progressive phase: Increasing pain, photophobia, and visual disturbance, particularly beyond a few days, indicating deeper or more widespread inflammation.
  • Late phase: Chronic or recurrent inflammation, particularly beyond several weeks, leading to structural damage, visual impairment, and potential complications if not adequately managed.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Inflammatory conditions of the eye (including scleritis, episcleritis, uveitis)"][/sc]
  • Ask about eye pain - to differentiate between mild discomfort (episcleritis) and severe, boring pain radiating to the temple/jaw (scleritis), or photophobia (uveitis).
  • Ask about redness - to determine onset, location (diffuse vs. localised), and character (bright red vs. violaceous hue), which helps distinguish superficial from deep inflammation.
  • Ask about visual changes - blurred vision, floaters, or decreased acuity may indicate posterior segment involvement or complications like macular oedema.
  • Ask about photophobia - a prominent symptom in uveitis, suggesting ciliary body spasm and inflammation of the iris.
  • Ask about tearing/discharge - typically watery in inflammatory conditions, purulent discharge suggests bacterial conjunctivitis.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Inflammatory conditions of the eye (including scleritis, episcleritis, uveitis)"][/sc]
  • Check visual acuity - to assess impact on vision and monitor progression; any reduction warrants urgent ophthalmological review.
  • Check conjunctival/scleral injection - episcleritis shows superficial, movable vessels that blanch with phenylephrine; scleritis shows deep, non-blanching, violaceous vessels.
  • Check pupil reaction and shape - irregular pupil (synechiae) or sluggish reaction to light may indicate uveitis.
  • Check intraocular pressure (IOP) - can be elevated in some forms of uveitis (e.g., Fuchs heterochromic iridocyclitis) or scleritis, or reduced in severe anterior uveitis.
  • Check vitals - to identify systemic inflammatory response (fever, tachycardia) or signs of associated systemic disease.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Inflammatory conditions of the eye (including scleritis, episcleritis, uveitis)"][/sc]
  • Severe, boring eye pain - highly suggestive of scleritis, indicating deep inflammation and potential for tissue destruction.
  • Reduced visual acuity - implies involvement of the visual axis, such as cornea, lens, retina, or optic nerve, requiring urgent assessment.
  • Photophobia and ciliary flush - classic signs of uveitis, particularly anterior uveitis, which can lead to synechiae and glaucoma.
  • Irregular pupil or hypopyon - indicates severe intraocular inflammation (uveitis) and potential for rapid vision loss.
  • Associated systemic symptoms - fever, rash, joint pain, or neurological symptoms may point to an underlying systemic autoimmune or infectious cause.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Inflammatory conditions of the eye (including scleritis, episcleritis, uveitis)"][/sc]
  • Older adults: May present with less pronounced pain or redness, leading to delayed diagnosis, particularly in conditions like chronic uveitis or posterior scleritis.
  • Immunosuppressed patients: Can have attenuated inflammatory responses, masking typical signs and symptoms, making diagnosis challenging and increasing risk of severe complications.
  • Children: Often present with subtle signs like decreased vision or strabismus, as they may not verbalise pain or photophobia effectively, especially in juvenile idiopathic arthritis-associated uveitis.
  • Posterior scleritis: May present with minimal external signs, but with pain on eye movement, proptosis, or visual disturbance, requiring imaging for diagnosis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Inflammatory conditions of the eye (including scleritis, episcleritis, uveitis)"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected inflammatory eye conditions: Slit-lamp examination to assess anterior chamber cells and flare, keratic precipitates, synechiae, and scleral/episcleral vessel characteristics.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Conjunctival swab for microbiology if infectious conjunctivitis is suspected; corneal scraping if infectious keratitis is considered.
  • Imaging - Ultrasound B-scan: For suspected posterior scleritis or vitritis, to visualise scleral thickening, T-sign, or vitreous opacities.
  • Imaging - Optical Coherence Tomography (OCT): To assess macular oedema in uveitis or optic disc swelling.
  • Serial reassessment: Regular follow-up to monitor response to treatment, assess for complications, and adjust management.
  • Systemic work-up: Consider blood tests (e.g., ESR, CRP, ANA, HLA-B27, ACE, syphilis serology) if an underlying systemic condition is suspected, guided by clinical context.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Inflammatory conditions of the eye (including scleritis, episcleritis, uveitis)"][/sc]
  • Conjunctivitis (bacterial/viral/allergic) - typically less pain, more discharge, superficial redness, no photophobia (unless severe).
  • Acute angle-closure glaucoma - very painful, red eye, fixed mid-dilated pupil, significantly raised IOP, blurred vision with haloes.
  • Keratitis (infectious/non-infectious) - pain, photophobia, foreign body sensation, corneal involvement (e.g., ulcer, infiltrate).
  • Episcleritis - mild discomfort, bright red, superficial vessels that blanch with phenylephrine, no visual changes.
  • Scleritis - severe, boring pain, violaceous non-blanching redness, often associated with systemic disease, potential for vision loss.
  • Uveitis (anterior) - pain, photophobia, ciliary flush, small/irregular pupil, cells/flare in anterior chamber.
  • Orbital cellulitis - severe pain, proptosis, ophthalmoplegia, lid swelling, systemic signs of infection.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Inflammatory conditions of the eye (including scleritis, episcleritis, uveitis)"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Prompt ophthalmological referral for accurate diagnosis and initiation of appropriate therapy to prevent vision loss and manage pain.
  • Definitive management: Depends on the specific diagnosis. For episcleritis, often supportive (lubricants, cool compresses) or topical NSAIDs. For scleritis and uveitis, systemic corticosteroids, immunosuppressants, or biologics may be required, alongside topical corticosteroids and cycloplegics for uveitis.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Regular ophthalmological review to assess disease activity, response to treatment, and screen for complications like glaucoma, cataract, or macular oedema.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Advise patients to seek urgent medical attention for worsening pain, redness, or visual changes.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Inflammatory conditions of the eye (including scleritis, episcleritis, uveitis)"][/sc]
  • Vision loss: Due to direct inflammation affecting the visual axis (e.g., corneal scarring, macular oedema, optic neuropathy) or secondary complications like glaucoma and cataract.
  • Glaucoma: Can develop secondary to chronic inflammation (uveitic glaucoma) or corticosteroid use, leading to irreversible optic nerve damage.
  • Cataract: Often a complication of chronic uveitis or prolonged corticosteroid therapy, causing progressive blurring of vision.
  • Scleral thinning/perforation: A severe complication of necrotising scleritis, which can lead to globe rupture and profound vision loss.
  • Retinal detachment: Can occur in posterior uveitis or severe scleritis, leading to acute visual impairment.
