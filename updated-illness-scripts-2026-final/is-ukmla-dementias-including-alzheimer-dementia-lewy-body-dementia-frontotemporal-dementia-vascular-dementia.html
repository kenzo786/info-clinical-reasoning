Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Dementias (including Alzheimer dementia, Lewy-Body dementia, frontotemporal dementia, vascular dementia)"][/sc]
  • Dementia is a syndrome characterised by a progressive decline in cognitive function, severe enough to interfere with daily life, encompassing memory, thinking, language, problem-solving, and social abilities [1]. It is not a normal part of ageing.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical features, common causes (including Alzheimer's, Lewy body, frontotemporal, and vascular dementias), and initial assessment of dementia.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Dementias (including Alzheimer dementia, Lewy-Body dementia, frontotemporal dementia, vascular dementia)"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Dementias (including Alzheimer dementia, Lewy-Body dementia, frontotemporal dementia, vascular dementia)"][/sc]
  • Approximately 982,000 people are living with dementia in the UK, a figure projected to rise to 1.4 million by 2040 [2, 3]. It is estimated that one in three people will develop dementia in their lifetime [3].
  • Dementia, including Alzheimer's disease, is the leading cause of death for women and the second leading cause for men in the UK, accounting for 11.8% of all deaths in 2025 [4].
  • Key risk factors include increasing age, mild cognitive impairment, genetics, Parkinson's disease, cerebrovascular disease, and cardiovascular disease [5].
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Dementias (including Alzheimer dementia, Lewy-Body dementia, frontotemporal dementia, vascular dementia)"][/sc]
  • The pathophysiology of dementias is diverse, often initiated by either the abnormal accumulation and misfolding of specific proteins (known as [sc name="jitl-query" Q="proteinopathies: what are they and why do they matter clinically?" text="proteinopathies"]) or cerebrovascular insults leading to brain injury.
  • Progression involves synaptic dysfunction, neuroinflammation, and neuronal damage, with the specific pathology spreading through distinct brain regions depending on the type of dementia.
  • The consequence is widespread neurodegeneration and brain atrophy, leading to progressive loss of cognitive and functional abilities.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Dementias (including Alzheimer dementia, Lewy-Body dementia, frontotemporal dementia, vascular dementia)"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Subtle cognitive changes, often attributed to normal ageing or stress, may be present for several years before a formal diagnosis. Memory difficulties, particularly for recent events, are common.
  • Progressive phase: Symptoms become more pronounced, interfering significantly with daily activities. This phase can last for several years, with increasing dependency and behavioural changes, particularly beyond a few months of initial noticeable decline.
  • Late phase: Severe cognitive and functional impairment, requiring extensive care. Individuals may lose the ability to communicate, recognise loved ones, and perform basic self-care, often extending over many years.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Dementias (including Alzheimer dementia, Lewy-Body dementia, frontotemporal dementia, vascular dementia)"][/sc]
  • Ask about memory loss - particularly recent events, as this is often an early and prominent feature in Alzheimer's dementia, differentiating it from benign forgetfulness.
  • Ask about changes in language or communication - such as difficulty finding words (anomia), understanding speech, or producing fluent sentences, which can be a primary presentation in frontotemporal dementia.
  • Ask about behavioural or personality changes - including apathy, disinhibition, or obsessive behaviours, which are characteristic of frontotemporal dementia and can also occur in Lewy body dementia.
  • Ask about visual hallucinations - often vivid and detailed, which are a core feature of Lewy body dementia and help distinguish it from other dementia types.
  • Ask about motor symptoms - such as parkinsonism (tremor, rigidity, bradykinesia) or gait disturbance, which are prominent in Lewy body dementia and can indicate vascular dementia.
  • Ask about executive dysfunction - difficulties with planning, problem-solving, and decision-making, which impact daily functioning and are common across all dementia types, but may be more pronounced early in vascular or frontotemporal dementias.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Dementias (including Alzheimer dementia, Lewy-Body dementia, frontotemporal dementia, vascular dementia)"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - to identify systemic causes of cognitive impairment (e.g., hypertension in vascular dementia, fever in delirium) and establish baseline physiological status.
  • Check neurological examination - to identify focal neurological deficits (suggestive of vascular dementia), extrapyramidal signs like bradykinesia or rigidity (common in Lewy body dementia and some frontotemporal dementias), or other motor abnormalities.
  • Check cognitive function (e.g., using [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Mini-Mental State Examination (MMSE) or Montreal Cognitive Assessment (MoCA): what are they and why do they matter clinically?" text="MMSE or MoCA"]) - to objectively quantify cognitive impairment and track progression.
  • Check gait and balance - abnormalities such as shuffling gait or frequent falls are common in Lewy body dementia and vascular dementia, indicating motor involvement.
  • Check for primitive reflexes - reappearance of primitive reflexes (e.g., grasp, glabellar tap) can indicate diffuse cerebral dysfunction, particularly in advanced stages.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Dementias (including Alzheimer dementia, Lewy-Body dementia, frontotemporal dementia, vascular dementia)"][/sc]
  • Rapid cognitive decline over weeks to months - suggests potentially reversible causes (e.g., infection, metabolic disturbance, normal pressure hydrocephalus) or rapidly progressive neurodegenerative conditions.
  • Sudden onset of symptoms or step-wise deterioration - highly indicative of vascular dementia due to acute cerebrovascular events, rather than a gradual neurodegenerative process.
  • Prominent behavioural changes or disinhibition in individuals under 65 - a key indicator for frontotemporal dementia, where personality and conduct changes often precede significant memory loss.
  • Recurrent, well-formed visual hallucinations, fluctuating cognition, and spontaneous parkinsonism - the diagnostic triad for Lewy body dementia, requiring specific management considerations.
  • Focal neurological deficits on examination - such as unilateral weakness or sensory loss, strongly point towards a vascular aetiology or other structural brain pathology.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Dementias (including Alzheimer dementia, Lewy-Body dementia, frontotemporal dementia, vascular dementia)"][/sc]
  • Young-onset dementia (under 65): Often presents with prominent behavioural changes or language difficulties rather than memory loss, leading to delayed diagnosis or misdiagnosis as psychiatric conditions.
  • Posterior Cortical Atrophy (PCA): An atypical variant of Alzheimer's disease where initial symptoms are visual processing deficits (e.g., difficulty reading, object recognition) rather than memory impairment, due to atrophy in the posterior brain regions.
  • Primary Progressive Aphasia (PPA): A form of frontotemporal dementia characterised by isolated and progressive language impairment, with other cognitive functions relatively preserved initially, impacting communication significantly.
  • Dementia in older adults with multimorbidity: Atypical presentations may include acute confusion (delirium) superimposed on chronic cognitive decline, or a rapid functional decline without clear cognitive complaints, making diagnosis challenging.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Dementias (including Alzheimer dementia, Lewy-Body dementia, frontotemporal dementia, vascular dementia)"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected Dementias:
    • Comprehensive clinical assessment, including detailed history from patient and collateral, and neurological examination.
    • Cognitive screening tests: Mini-Mental State Examination (MMSE) on MDCalc or Montreal Cognitive Assessment (MoCA) on MDCalc to quantify cognitive impairment.
    • Routine blood tests: Full blood count, electrolytes, renal and liver function, thyroid function tests, vitamin B12 and folate levels to exclude reversible causes.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible:
    • Further investigations based on clinical suspicion, e.g., CSF analysis for specific biomarkers (amyloid-beta, tau) in atypical presentations or younger patients.
    • Screening for infections (e.g., syphilis, HIV) if risk factors are present.
  • Imaging - Structural (CT/MRI):
    • Brain CT or MRI to rule out structural causes (e.g., tumour, subdural haematoma, normal pressure hydrocephalus) and identify features suggestive of vascular dementia (infarcts, white matter changes) or atrophy patterns characteristic of Alzheimer's or frontotemporal dementia.
  • Imaging - Functional (PET):
    • Amyloid PET or Tau PET scans may be used in specialist settings to confirm the presence of Alzheimer's pathology, particularly in atypical cases or for research.
  • Serial reassessment:
    • Regular follow-up and repeat cognitive assessments to monitor disease progression and adjust management strategies.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Dementias (including Alzheimer dementia, Lewy-Body dementia, frontotemporal dementia, vascular dementia)"][/sc]
  • Alzheimer dementia - insidious onset, progressive memory impairment as the predominant early symptom, often with visuospatial and language difficulties later.
  • Vascular dementia - often abrupt onset or step-wise decline, associated with cerebrovascular disease risk factors and focal neurological signs.
  • Lewy-Body dementia - prominent visual hallucinations, fluctuating cognition, and spontaneous parkinsonism, often preceding or concurrent with cognitive decline.
  • Frontotemporal dementia - early and prominent behavioural changes or language disturbance, with relative preservation of memory in the initial stages.
  • Delirium - acute onset, fluctuating course, impaired attention, and altered consciousness, often triggered by an underlying medical condition.
  • Depression (Pseudodementia) - prominent mood disturbance, often with rapid onset of cognitive complaints, but typically preserved insight and effort on cognitive testing.
  • Normal Pressure Hydrocephalus (NPH) - classic triad of gait disturbance, urinary incontinence, and dementia, potentially reversible with CSF shunting.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Dementias (including Alzheimer dementia, Lewy-Body dementia, frontotemporal dementia, vascular dementia)"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities:
    • Address reversible causes of cognitive impairment (e.g., delirium, metabolic disturbances, infections).
    • Ensure patient safety, including assessment of driving ability, risk of falls, and capacity for self-care.
    • Provide support and education to the patient and their carers.
  • Definitive management:
    • Non-pharmacological interventions: Cognitive stimulation therapy, occupational therapy, regular physical activity, and social engagement are often recommended.
    • Pharmacological management for Alzheimer's and Lewy body dementias: Cholinesterase inhibitors (e.g., [sc name="jitl-medication" Q="Donepezil use in Alzheimer's dementia" text="donepezil"], [sc name="jitl-medication" Q="Rivastigmine use in Alzheimer's dementia" text="rivastigmine"], [sc name="jitl-medication" Q="Galantamine use in Alzheimer's dementia" text="galantamine"]) may be considered to improve cognitive symptoms. Memantine may be used in moderate to severe Alzheimer's dementia.
    • Pharmacological management for vascular dementia: Management of cardiovascular risk factors (e.g., hypertension, diabetes, hyperlipidaemia) is crucial.
    • Pharmacological management for frontotemporal dementia: Symptomatic management of behavioural disturbances, as specific disease-modifying treatments are not currently available.
  • Monitoring and follow-up:
    • Regular clinical reviews to monitor cognitive function, behavioural symptoms, and functional abilities.
    • Assessment of carer burden and provision of ongoing support.
  • Escalation and safety-netting:
    • Referral to specialist memory services or geriatric psychiatry for complex cases, diagnostic uncertainty, or challenging behavioural symptoms.
    • Advance care planning discussions, including lasting power of attorney and end-of-life care preferences.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Dementias (including Alzheimer dementia, Lewy-Body dementia, frontotemporal dementia, vascular dementia)"][/sc]
  • Infections (e.g., pneumonia, UTIs): Impaired immunity, reduced mobility, and poor self-care in later stages increase susceptibility, often leading to acute deterioration and increased mortality.
  • Falls and fractures: Gait disturbance, balance impairment, and cognitive deficits increase the risk of falls, particularly in the progressive phase, leading to significant morbidity and reduced quality of life.
  • Malnutrition and dehydration: Forgetting to eat or drink, difficulty with feeding, and dysphagia can lead to significant weight loss and electrolyte imbalances, impacting overall health and prognosis.
  • Behavioural and Psychological Symptoms of Dementia (BPSD): Agitation, aggression, psychosis, and depression are common, particularly in the progressive phase, increasing caregiver burden and often precipitating institutionalisation.
  • Aspiration pneumonia: Dysphagia, common in advanced dementia, increases the risk of aspirating food or liquids into the lungs, a frequent cause of morbidity and mortality in the late phase.
