Malnutrition [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Malnutrition"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Malnutrition"][/sc]
  • A state of nutrition where a deficiency, excess, or imbalance of energy, protein, or other nutrients causes measurable adverse effects on body composition, function, and clinical outcome.
  • UKMLA Learning Outcome: Recognise and manage malnutrition, including its causes, consequences, and appropriate nutritional support strategies.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Malnutrition"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Malnutrition"][/sc]
  • In the UK, malnutrition affects approximately 3 million people, with up to 30% of hospital patients and 40% of care home residents at risk.
  • Older adults, particularly those over 65, are disproportionately affected, with prevalence rates increasing with age and co-morbidities.
  • Socioeconomic deprivation and chronic diseases, such as cancer, chronic obstructive pulmonary disease, and inflammatory bowel disease, are significant risk factors in the UK population.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Malnutrition"][/sc]
  • Triggered by inadequate nutrient intake, increased metabolic demands (e.g., infection, trauma), or impaired nutrient absorption, leading to a negative energy balance.
  • Progression involves the body mobilising endogenous stores (glycogen, fat, protein) to meet energy needs, resulting in muscle wasting and depletion of visceral proteins.
  • Consequences include impaired immune function, delayed wound healing, reduced muscle strength, and increased susceptibility to infections, ultimately impacting recovery and mortality.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Malnutrition"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Characterised by reduced dietary intake or increased metabolic stress, often unnoticed, leading to gradual weight loss and subtle functional decline over weeks.
  • Progressive phase: Sustained negative energy balance results in significant depletion of body reserves, marked by visible muscle wasting, fatigue, and impaired organ function over months.
  • Late phase: Severe, prolonged malnutrition leads to profound physiological derangements, including oedema, immune suppression, and multi-organ dysfunction, potentially culminating in refeeding syndrome upon re-nutrition.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Malnutrition"][/sc]
  • Ask about unintentional weight loss - this is a key indicator of negative energy balance and can be quantified.
  • Ask about reduced appetite or early satiety - these suggest underlying issues affecting food intake or digestion.
  • Ask about fatigue and weakness - these reflect muscle wasting and reduced energy reserves.
  • Ask about changes in bowel habits (e.g., diarrhoea, constipation) - these may indicate malabsorption or altered gut function.
  • Ask about poor wound healing or recurrent infections - these are common consequences of impaired immune function and tissue repair.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Malnutrition"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - may reveal bradycardia, hypotension, or hypothermia in severe cases, indicating metabolic slowing.
  • Check for muscle wasting (e.g., temporal, deltoid, quadriceps) - this is a visible sign of protein catabolism.
  • Check for subcutaneous fat loss (e.g., orbital, triceps, ribs) - indicates depletion of energy stores.
  • Check for oedema (e.g., ankle, sacral) - may suggest hypoalbuminaemia, particularly in protein-energy malnutrition.
  • Check skin and hair changes (e.g., dry, flaky skin; sparse, brittle hair) - these can be signs of micronutrient deficiencies.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Malnutrition"][/sc]
  • Rapid, unintentional weight loss: Suggests severe catabolism or underlying malignancy, requiring urgent investigation.
  • Severe dysphagia or odynophagia: Indicates significant difficulty in eating, posing a high risk of aspiration and further nutritional decline.
  • Persistent vomiting or diarrhoea: Leads to rapid fluid and electrolyte imbalances, and severe malabsorption, necessitating immediate intervention.
  • Clinical signs of refeeding syndrome risk: Electrolyte disturbances (hypophosphataemia, hypokalaemia, hypomagnesaemia) in severely malnourished patients starting re-nutrition, which can be life-threatening.
  • Impaired consciousness or severe lethargy: May indicate profound metabolic derangement or underlying systemic illness, requiring urgent medical assessment.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Malnutrition"][/sc]
  • Obese individuals: Can be malnourished despite a high BMI, often due to micronutrient deficiencies or sarcopenic obesity, where muscle mass is lost but fat mass is retained.
  • Patients with fluid retention (e.g., heart failure, renal failure): Weight may be stable or increased, masking underlying muscle and fat loss, requiring careful assessment of body composition.
  • Children with chronic illness: May present with growth faltering or developmental delay rather than overt weight loss, necessitating growth chart monitoring.
  • Individuals with cognitive impairment: May not report symptoms effectively, with malnutrition manifesting as behavioural changes, increased falls, or recurrent infections.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Malnutrition"][/sc]
  • Core:
    • Full blood count (FBC) - may show anaemia (iron, B12, folate deficiency).
    • Urea and electrolytes (U&Es) - to assess renal function and electrolyte balance, particularly for refeeding risk.
    • Liver function tests (LFTs) - to assess synthetic function and identify liver disease.
    • C-reactive protein (CRP) - to identify inflammation, which can influence nutritional status.
    • Albumin - a marker of inflammatory response and severity of illness, not a direct indicator of nutritional status in acute settings.
    • Thyroid function tests (TFTs) - to exclude thyroid dysfunction as a cause of weight changes.
  • Imaging:
    • Dual-energy X-ray absorptiometry (DEXA) scan - can assess body composition (fat and lean mass) more accurately than BMI alone.
  • If alternative diagnosis considered:
    • Endoscopy/colonoscopy - if malabsorption or gastrointestinal pathology is suspected.
    • Imaging (CT/MRI) - to investigate for underlying malignancy or structural abnormalities.
  • Serial reassessment:
    • Regular weight monitoring and nutritional screening (e.g., MUST score) to track progress and identify deterioration.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Malnutrition"][/sc]
  • A 78-year-old cachectic female, with a history of chronic obstructive pulmonary disease, presents with a three-month history of unintentional weight loss, reduced appetite, and increasing fatigue, alongside recurrent chest infections.
  • An elderly, frail patient with chronic respiratory disease and significant catabolism, experiencing progressive functional decline and immune compromise due to inadequate nutritional intake.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Malnutrition"][/sc]
  • Malignancy: Often associated with cachexia, anorexia, and increased metabolic demands.
  • Chronic infections (e.g., TB, HIV): Can lead to sustained inflammatory responses, increased energy expenditure, and nutrient malabsorption.
  • Gastrointestinal disorders (e.g., Crohn's disease, coeliac disease): Result in malabsorption, nutrient loss, and reduced intake due to symptoms.
  • Hyperthyroidism: Causes increased metabolic rate and weight loss despite adequate or increased food intake.
  • Depression or other mental health conditions: Can lead to reduced appetite, poor food choices, and social isolation affecting nutritional intake.
  • Dementia: Impairs ability to prepare food, remember to eat, or recognise hunger cues, leading to inadequate intake.
  • Chronic kidney disease: Associated with anorexia, metabolic acidosis, and protein-energy wasting.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Malnutrition"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities:
    • Identify and address underlying causes of malnutrition (e.g., treat infection, manage dysphagia).
    • Initiate nutritional support cautiously, especially in those at risk of refeeding syndrome, with careful monitoring of electrolytes.
  • Definitive management:
    • Dietary advice and oral nutritional supplements (ONS) - first-line for most patients, aiming for nutrient-dense, high-calorie, high-protein foods.
    • Enteral tube feeding (e.g., nasogastric, gastrostomy) - considered when oral intake is insufficient or unsafe, ensuring appropriate formula and delivery rates.
    • Parenteral nutrition (PN) - reserved for patients with non-functional or inaccessible gastrointestinal tracts, requiring central venous access and meticulous monitoring.
    • [sc name="jitl-medication" Q="Appetite stimulants use in Malnutrition" text="Mirtazapine"][/sc] or [sc name="jitl-medication" Q="Appetite stimulants use in Malnutrition" text="Dronabinol"] [/sc] may be considered in specific contexts, such as palliative care, to improve appetite.   • Monitoring and follow-up:
    • Regular weight, BMI, and nutritional screening (e.g., MUST score) to assess response to intervention.
    • Monitoring of biochemical parameters (electrolytes, LFTs, renal function) to detect complications or guide adjustments.
    • Dietetic review and ongoing support to optimise nutritional intake and address evolving needs.
  • Escalation and safety-netting:
    • Referral to specialist nutrition teams for complex cases or those requiring advanced nutritional support.
    • Educate patients and carers on signs of deterioration and when to seek medical attention.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Malnutrition"][/sc]
  • Increased susceptibility to infection: Due to impaired immune function, leading to higher rates of pneumonia, urinary tract infections, and sepsis.
  • Delayed wound healing: Inadequate protein and micronutrient availability compromises collagen synthesis and tissue repair, prolonging recovery.
  • Muscle weakness and functional decline: Sarcopenia increases the risk of falls, reduces mobility, and impairs activities of daily living.
  • Refeeding syndrome: A potentially fatal shift in fluids and electrolytes that can occur in malnourished patients receiving aggressive re-nutrition, leading to cardiac, respiratory, and neurological complications.
  • Pressure ulcers: Reduced subcutaneous fat and muscle mass, combined with immobility, increase vulnerability to skin breakdown.
  • Anaemia: Often multifactorial, resulting from deficiencies in iron, folate, or vitamin B12, further contributing to fatigue and weakness.
