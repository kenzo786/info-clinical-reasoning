Chronic kidney disease [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Chronic kidney disease"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Chronic kidney disease"][/sc]
  • Chronic kidney disease (CKD) is defined as abnormalities of kidney function or structure, or a sustained reduction in glomerular filtration rate (GFR), present for more than three months, with implications for health [1] [2] [3].
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the presentation, risk factors, and long-term implications of chronic kidney disease, and understand the principles of its investigation and management [4].
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Chronic kidney disease"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Chronic kidney disease"][/sc]
  • In the UK, over 1.9 million adults have been diagnosed with CKD, with many more remaining undiagnosed [5]. An estimated 7.2 million people, over 10% of the population, have CKD stages 1-5 [6].
  • Key risk factors include diabetes, hypertension, cardiovascular disease, obesity, smoking, and a family history of kidney disease [7] [8].
  • Prevalence increases with age, with 22% of adults aged 35 and over in England having CKD stages 1-5 [9].
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Chronic kidney disease"][/sc]
  • Initial injury to the kidneys from conditions like diabetes or hypertension leads to nephron loss and compensatory hyperfiltration in remaining nephrons [10].
  • Progressive damage involves a vicious cycle of glomerular hypertension, proteinuria, and tubulointerstitial fibrosis, driven by various cytokines and growth factors [11] [12].
  • Consequence is a gradual decline in renal function, leading to accumulation of waste products, electrolyte imbalances, and systemic complications such as cardiovascular disease and anaemia [13] [14]. Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Chronic kidney disease"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Often asymptomatic, with kidney damage or reduced function present for at least three months, but without significant symptoms. Detection is typically through screening for risk factors like diabetes or hypertension [15].
  • Progressive phase: Gradual decline in kidney function, particularly beyond several months to years, leading to accumulation of waste products and subtle symptoms such as fatigue or changes in urination. Complications may begin to emerge [16].
  • Late phase: Significant loss of kidney function, particularly beyond several years, resulting in overt symptoms of uraemia, electrolyte imbalances, and severe complications requiring renal replacement therapy or palliative care [17].
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Chronic kidney disease"][/sc]
  • Ask about fatigue and weakness - often an early, non-specific symptom, but persistent and worsening fatigue can indicate accumulating uraemic toxins and anaemia, impacting daily function [18].
  • Ask about swelling (oedema) - peripheral oedema (ankles, feet, hands) or periorbital oedema suggests fluid retention due to impaired kidney function and sodium excretion [19].
  • Ask about changes in urination - nocturia (waking at night to urinate) or polyuria (increased urine volume) can indicate impaired concentrating ability in early CKD, while oliguria (reduced urine volume) suggests advanced disease [20].
  • Ask about nausea, vomiting, or loss of appetite - these are common symptoms of uraemia, indicating significant accumulation of waste products that irritate the gastrointestinal tract [21].
  • Ask about itching (pruritus) - often a distressing symptom in advanced CKD, typically due to the accumulation of uraemic toxins and phosphate, impacting quality of life [22].
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Chronic kidney disease"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - particularly blood pressure, as hypertension is both a cause and consequence of CKD, and its control is crucial for slowing progression [23].
  • Check for oedema - peripheral (ankles, sacrum) or pulmonary oedema suggests fluid overload due to impaired renal excretion [24].
  • Check for pallor - often indicative of anaemia, a common complication of CKD due to reduced erythropoietin production [25].
  • Check for uraemic fetor - a distinctive urine-like odour on the breath, signifying severe uraemia and accumulation of nitrogenous waste products [26]. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Uraemic fetor: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Uraemic fetor"][/sc]
  • Check for skin changes - such as excoriations from pruritus, or rarely, uraemic frost in very advanced disease [27].
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Chronic kidney disease"][/sc]
  • Unexplained haematuria: May indicate glomerular disease or other structural kidney pathology requiring urgent investigation [28].
  • Sudden worsening of oedema or breathlessness: Suggests acute fluid overload, potentially leading to pulmonary oedema, requiring immediate medical attention [29].
  • Severe, uncontrolled hypertension: Can signify accelerated hypertension or renovascular disease, which can rapidly worsen kidney function and increase cardiovascular risk [30].
  • Symptoms of uraemia (e.g., altered mental status, pericarditis): Indicates advanced kidney failure and the need for urgent renal replacement therapy [31].
  • Hyperkalaemia: A life-threatening electrolyte imbalance that can cause cardiac arrhythmias, requiring prompt management [32].
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Chronic kidney disease"][/sc]
  • Older adults: Often present asymptomatically or with non-specific symptoms like cognitive impairment, falls, or functional decline, rather than classic uraemic symptoms. Co-morbidities can mask or mimic CKD symptoms [33] [34].
  • Pregnancy: CKD can worsen during pregnancy, increasing risks of pre-eclampsia, preterm birth, and accelerated decline in renal function. Conversely, pregnancy can unmask underlying CKD [35] [36].
  • Immunosuppressed patients: The underlying condition requiring immunosuppression (e.g., autoimmune disease, transplant) can directly cause or exacerbate CKD. Immunosuppressive medications themselves can be nephrotoxic, leading to a complex and often rapidly progressive course [37] [38].
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Chronic kidney disease"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected Chronic kidney disease:
    • Urine dipstick and albumin:creatinine ratio (ACR) - to detect proteinuria, a key marker of kidney damage and progression [39].
    • Blood tests: Urea, creatinine, estimated GFR (eGFR) - to assess kidney function and stage CKD. Electrolytes (Na, K, Ca, PO4) - to detect imbalances [40].
    • Full blood count (FBC) - to assess for anaemia of CKD [41].
    • Renal ultrasound - to assess kidney size, rule out obstruction, and identify structural abnormalities [42].
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible:
    • Autoimmune screen (ANA, ANCA, complement levels) - if glomerulonephritis is suspected [43].
    • Serum and urine electrophoresis - if myeloma kidney is suspected [44].
  • Imaging - Renal ultrasound: Essential for assessing kidney size, identifying hydronephrosis (obstruction), and detecting other structural abnormalities. Small, echogenic kidneys are typical of chronic damage [45].
  • Serial reassessment: Regular monitoring of eGFR, ACR, blood pressure, and electrolytes is crucial to track disease progression and guide management. Frequency depends on CKD stage and risk of progression [46].

Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Chronic kidney disease"][/sc]
  • A 68-year-old male with a 15-year history of type 2 diabetes and hypertension presents with increasing fatigue, bilateral ankle swelling, and reduced appetite over the last six months. Examination reveals elevated blood pressure, bilateral pitting oedema to the mid-shins, and pallor. Blood tests show an eGFR of 25 mL/min/1.73m² and elevated creatinine.
  • A chronic, progressive, systemic illness characterised by insidious onset, multi-systemic symptoms, and biochemical evidence of significantly impaired renal function, likely secondary to long-standing metabolic and vascular comorbidities.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Chronic kidney disease"][/sc]
  • Acute Kidney Injury (AKI) - Rapid onset (hours to days) of kidney dysfunction, often reversible, frequently triggered by acute events like sepsis, dehydration, or nephrotoxic drugs. CKD is defined by chronicity (>3 months) [47].
  • Congestive Heart Failure (CHF) - Can cause renal impairment (cardiorenal syndrome) and fluid retention, mimicking some CKD symptoms. Distinguishing features include prominent cardiac symptoms (orthopnoea, PND) and echocardiographic findings [48].
  • Liver Cirrhosis - Leads to fluid retention (ascites, oedema) and can cause hepatorenal syndrome, a form of AKI. Jaundice, stigmata of chronic liver disease, and liver function test abnormalities are key differentiators [49].
  • Essential Hypertension - A major risk factor and consequence of CKD. Differentiating primary hypertension from hypertension secondary to CKD requires careful assessment of renal function and imaging [50].
  • Diabetic Nephropathy - A common cause of CKD. The presence of long-standing diabetes, retinopathy, and characteristic renal biopsy findings help confirm this specific aetiology of CKD [51].
  • Glomerulonephritis - Can cause acute or chronic kidney disease. Often presents with haematuria, significant proteinuria, and may have systemic features. Diagnosis typically requires renal biopsy [52].
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Chronic kidney disease"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Address acute complications such as hyperkalaemia, fluid overload, or severe acidosis. This may involve emergency dialysis or specific pharmacological interventions [53].
  • Definitive management:
    • Optimise blood pressure control: Aim for targets typically <140/90 mmHg (or <130/80 mmHg if albuminuria present) using ACE inhibitors or ARBs as first-line agents, unless contraindicated [54].
    • Glycaemic control in diabetes: Strict control to prevent progression of diabetic nephropathy [55].
    • Dietary modifications: Low salt, low phosphate, low potassium diet as appropriate for CKD stage. Protein restriction may be considered in advanced CKD [56].
    • Management of anaemia: Iron supplementation and erythropoiesis-stimulating agents (ESAs) if indicated [57].
    • Management of mineral and bone disorders: Phosphate binders and vitamin D analogues [58].
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Regular assessment of eGFR, ACR, electrolytes, blood pressure, and symptoms. Frequency depends on CKD stage and risk of progression [59].
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Education on symptoms of worsening kidney function or complications. Referral to nephrology services for advanced CKD (eGFR <30 mL/min/1.73m²), rapid decline, or complex management [60]. Discussion of renal replacement therapy options (dialysis, transplantation) as disease progresses [61].

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Chronic kidney disease"][/sc]
  • Cardiovascular disease: CKD is an independent risk factor for cardiovascular events (heart attack, stroke, heart failure) due to accelerated atherosclerosis, hypertension, and fluid overload, significantly impacting prognosis [62].
  • Anaemia: Common in CKD due to reduced erythropoietin production by damaged kidneys, iron deficiency, and shortened red blood cell lifespan. Contributes to fatigue and reduced quality of life, and can exacerbate cardiovascular disease [63].
  • Mineral and bone disorders (CKD-MBD): Characterised by abnormalities in calcium, phosphate, PTH, and vitamin D metabolism, leading to renal osteodystrophy, vascular calcification, and increased fracture risk [64].
  • Hyperkalaemia: Impaired potassium excretion by the kidneys can lead to dangerously high potassium levels, especially with certain medications (e.g., ACE inhibitors) or dietary indiscretion, posing a risk of life-threatening arrhythmias [65].
  • Metabolic acidosis: Reduced kidney capacity to excrete acid and regenerate bicarbonate leads to chronic metabolic acidosis, which can worsen bone disease, protein catabolism, and inflammation [66].
  • End-stage kidney disease (ESKD): The ultimate complication, where kidney function is insufficient to sustain life, requiring renal replacement therapy (dialysis or transplantation) [67].
