Irritable bowel syndrome [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Irritable bowel syndrome"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Irritable bowel syndrome definition"][/sc]
  • Irritable bowel syndrome (IBS) is a chronic, relapsing functional gastrointestinal disorder characterised by recurrent abdominal pain, often associated with defaecation, and a change in bowel habit (constipation, diarrhoea, or mixed).
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the diagnostic criteria for IBS, differentiate it from inflammatory bowel disease, and outline initial management strategies including lifestyle and dietary modifications.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Irritable bowel syndrome epidemiology"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Irritable bowel syndrome"][/sc]
  • IBS affects an estimated 10–20% of the general population in the UK, with prevalence often higher in women than men (NICE CG61).
  • Onset typically occurs between 20 and 30 years of age, though it can present at any age, including in older adults (NICE CG61).
  • Risk factors include female sex, younger age, family history of IBS, and a history of gastroenteritis or psychological stressors such as anxiety and depression.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Irritable bowel syndrome pathophysiology"][/sc]
  • Trigger: A complex interplay of factors including altered gut motility, visceral hypersensitivity, gut microbiota dysbiosis, and genetic predisposition, often exacerbated by psychological stress.
  • Progression: Chronic low-grade inflammation and immune activation in the gut, coupled with dysregulation of the gut-brain axis, leads to persistent abdominal symptoms.
  • Consequence: Disrupted communication between the central nervous system and the enteric nervous system results in altered bowel habits and heightened perception of pain, impacting quality of life. Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Irritable bowel syndrome time course"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Symptoms typically emerge gradually, often following a period of stress or gastrointestinal infection, with intermittent abdominal discomfort and changes in bowel habits.
  • Progressive phase: Symptoms become more frequent and severe, potentially impacting daily activities and quality of life, with periods of exacerbation and remission.
  • Late phase: IBS is a chronic, relapsing condition that can persist for many years or a lifetime, requiring long-term management strategies to control symptoms.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Irritable bowel syndrome symptoms"][/sc]
  • Ask about abdominal pain or discomfort - this is a key diagnostic criterion, often relieved by defaecation or associated with altered bowel frequency/form.
  • Ask about altered bowel habit - presenting as constipation (IBS-C), diarrhoea (IBS-D), or a mixed pattern (IBS-M), which helps classify the subtype.
  • Ask about bloating and abdominal distension - these are common and often distressing symptoms, particularly in women, contributing to discomfort.
  • Ask about urgency or incomplete evacuation - these indicate disordered bowel function and can significantly impact daily activities.
  • Ask about symptoms made worse by eating - this suggests a gastrocolic reflex hypersensitivity or food intolerance, common in IBS.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Irritable bowel syndrome signs"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - typically normal in IBS, but important to exclude systemic illness or acute complications.
  • Check for abdominal tenderness - non-specific tenderness may be present, but localised or rebound tenderness suggests an alternative diagnosis.
  • Check for abdominal distension - often visible or palpable, correlating with patient-reported bloating.
  • Check for passage of mucus per rectum - a supportive feature of IBS, but absence does not rule it out. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Mucus in stool: what does it indicate in IBS?" text="Mucus in stool"][/sc]
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Irritable bowel syndrome red flags"][/sc]
  • Unexplained weight loss - suggests malabsorption, malignancy, or inflammatory bowel disease, requiring urgent investigation.
  • Rectal bleeding - indicates mucosal damage, potentially from inflammatory bowel disease, polyps, or malignancy, necessitating colonoscopy.
  • New onset of symptoms in patients over 60 years - increases the likelihood of organic disease, particularly colorectal cancer, warranting prompt investigation.
  • Family history of bowel cancer or inflammatory bowel disease - raises suspicion for genetic predisposition to more serious conditions.
  • Anaemia - may indicate chronic blood loss from the gastrointestinal tract, requiring investigation for underlying pathology.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Irritable bowel syndrome atypical presentations"][/sc]
  • Older adults: New onset IBS symptoms in this age group should prompt thorough investigation to exclude organic pathology, as malignancy risk increases with age.
  • IBS-U (Unclassified): Patients who do not fit into IBS-C, IBS-D, or IBS-M categories, highlighting the heterogeneity of the condition and the need for individualized management.
  • Post-infectious IBS (PI-IBS): Develops after an acute episode of gastroenteritis, suggesting a potential trigger for gut-brain axis dysfunction. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Post-infectious IBS: what is its significance?" text="Post-infectious IBS"][/sc]
  • Severe, refractory IBS: Characterised by persistent and debilitating symptoms despite conventional treatments, often requiring multidisciplinary input including psychological therapies.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Irritable bowel syndrome diagnostic tests"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected IBS: Full blood count (FBC), erythrocyte sedimentation rate (ESR) or plasma viscosity, C-reactive protein (CRP), and antibody testing for coeliac disease (endomysial antibodies [EMA] or tissue transglutaminase [TTG]) to exclude other diagnoses (NICE CG61).
  • Diagnostic criteria: Rome IV criteria for IBS diagnosis, which require recurrent abdominal pain on average at least 1 day per week in the last 3 months, associated with two or more of the following: related to defaecation, associated with a change in frequency of stool, or associated with a change in form (appearance) of stool. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Rome IV criteria for IBS: what are the key components?" text="Rome IV criteria for IBS"][/sc]
  • Severity/risk tools: The IBS Severity Scoring System (IBS-SSS) can quantify symptom severity and impact on quality of life. IBS Severity Scoring System on MDCalc
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Faecal calprotectin to screen for inflammatory bowel disease, and hydrogen breath test for lactose intolerance or small intestinal bacterial overgrowth (SIBO) if clinically indicated.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Irritable bowel syndrome problem representation"][/sc]
  • A 35-year-old female presents with a 9-month history of intermittent lower abdominal pain, relieved by defaecation, associated with alternating constipation and diarrhoea, and significant bloating, with no red flag symptoms.
  • Chronic functional gastrointestinal disorder with fluctuating bowel habits and abdominal discomfort, concerning for irritable bowel syndrome.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Irritable bowel syndrome differential diagnosis"][/sc]
  • Inflammatory Bowel Disease (Crohn\'s disease/Ulcerative colitis) - distinguished by presence of red flag symptoms (e.g., rectal bleeding, weight loss, anaemia), elevated inflammatory markers (CRP, faecal calprotectin), and endoscopic findings.
  • Coeliac Disease - distinguished by positive antibody tests (TTG, EMA) and duodenal biopsy findings, often presenting with malabsorption symptoms.
  • Lactose Intolerance - distinguished by symptom resolution on lactose-free diet or positive hydrogen breath test, typically without significant abdominal pain.
  • Small Intestinal Bacterial Overgrowth (SIBO) - distinguished by positive hydrogen breath test and often responds to antibiotics, symptoms can mimic IBS. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Small intestinal bacterial overgrowth (SIBO): how does it mimic IBS?" text="SIBO"][/sc]
  • Colorectal Cancer - distinguished by new onset symptoms in older patients, rectal bleeding, weight loss, and positive faecal occult blood test, requiring colonoscopy.
  • Diverticular Disease - distinguished by localised abdominal pain, often in the left lower quadrant, and imaging findings of diverticula, typically in older patients.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Irritable bowel syndrome treatment"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Reassurance, education about the chronic nature of IBS, and initial lifestyle and dietary advice, including regular meals, adequate fluid intake, and limiting caffeine/alcohol (NICE CG61).
  • Definitive management: Dietary modifications such as a low-FODMAP diet, often guided by a dietitian, can significantly improve symptoms in many patients.
  • Symptom-targeted pharmacological therapy: Antispasmodics (e.g., mebeverine, peppermint oil) for abdominal pain, laxatives (excluding lactulose) for constipation, and loperamide for diarrhoea (NICE CG61).
  • Adjunctive management: Consider low-dose tricyclic antidepressants (TCAs) like amitriptyline as second-line treatment for persistent symptoms, or selective serotonin reuptake inhibitors (SSRIs) if TCAs are ineffective (NICE CG61).
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Regular review of symptoms and treatment effectiveness, with adjustment of therapies as needed.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Referral to secondary care for patients with red flag symptoms or those whose symptoms are refractory to primary care management, potentially for psychological interventions (CBT, hypnotherapy).

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Irritable bowel syndrome complications"][/sc]
  • Reduced quality of life: Chronic and unpredictable symptoms can significantly impair daily activities, social interactions, and overall well-being, leading to psychological distress.
  • Psychological comorbidities: High rates of anxiety and depression are associated with IBS, which can exacerbate symptoms and complicate management.
  • Nutritional deficiencies: Restrictive diets, particularly self-imposed ones without professional guidance, can lead to inadequate intake of essential nutrients over time.
  • Impact on work/education: Frequent or severe symptoms can result in absenteeism or reduced productivity, affecting professional and academic performance.
  • Increased healthcare utilisation: Patients with IBS often seek frequent medical attention, leading to numerous investigations and consultations, increasing healthcare costs.
