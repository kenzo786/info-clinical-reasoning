Lower respiratory tract infection [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Lower respiratory tract infection"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Lower respiratory tract infection"][/sc]
  • Lower respiratory tract infection (LRTI) encompasses acute infections affecting the airways below the larynx, including acute bronchitis, pneumonia, and exacerbations of chronic respiratory conditions like COPD or asthma.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical features of common lower respiratory tract infections, differentiate between viral and bacterial causes, and understand the principles of appropriate antimicrobial stewardship.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Lower respiratory tract infection"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Lower respiratory tract infection"][/sc]
  • Acute cough, often due to LRTI, is one of the most common reasons for presentation to primary care in the UK, accounting for millions of consultations annually.
  • Pneumonia affects approximately 5-11 per 1,000 adults annually in the UK, with higher incidence in older adults and those with comorbidities.
  • Risk factors for severe LRTI include advanced age, immunosuppression, chronic lung disease (e.g., COPD, asthma), heart failure, diabetes, and smoking.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Lower respiratory tract infection"][/sc]
  • Infection typically initiates when pathogens (viruses or bacteria) overcome host defence mechanisms in the upper airway, leading to their aspiration or inhalation into the lower respiratory tract.
  • Local inflammation and immune responses are triggered, causing bronchial oedema, increased mucus production, and impaired mucociliary clearance, which can lead to airway obstruction and ventilation-perfusion mismatch.
  • Progression can result in alveolar inflammation and consolidation (pneumonia), or persistent bronchial inflammation (acute bronchitis), potentially leading to respiratory compromise and systemic effects. Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Lower respiratory tract infection"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Symptoms such as cough, fever, and malaise typically develop over hours to a few days following exposure, often indistinguishable from upper respiratory tract infections.
  • Progressive phase: If the infection progresses, symptoms may worsen over 3-7 days, with increasing breathlessness, chest pain, and systemic signs of infection, particularly in pneumonia.
  • Late phase: Resolution of symptoms can take 1-3 weeks for acute bronchitis, and cough may persist for several weeks. Pneumonia recovery can be protracted, with fatigue lasting for months, particularly beyond 7-10 days if complications arise.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Lower respiratory tract infection"][/sc]
  • Ask about cough - often productive of sputum, indicating inflammation or infection in the lower airways.
  • Ask about breathlessness (dyspnoea) - suggests significant lung involvement or impaired gas exchange, especially concerning in pneumonia.
  • Ask about chest pain - pleuritic chest pain may indicate pleural involvement, common in pneumonia.
  • Ask about fever and chills - systemic inflammatory response to infection, though may be absent in older adults or immunocompromised individuals.
  • Ask about fatigue and malaise - non-specific but common systemic symptoms of infection, impacting functional status.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Lower respiratory tract infection"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - tachycardia, tachypnoea, fever, and hypoxia are key indicators of severity and systemic illness, particularly in pneumonia.
  • Check chest auscultation - crackles or bronchial breathing may indicate alveolar consolidation, while wheeze may suggest bronchospasm, as seen in acute bronchitis or COPD exacerbation.
  • Check chest percussion - dullness to percussion can indicate underlying consolidation or pleural effusion.
  • Check signs of respiratory distress - use of accessory muscles, nasal flaring, or cyanosis indicate severe respiratory compromise. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Accessory muscle use: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Accessory muscle use"][/sc]
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Lower respiratory tract infection"][/sc]
  • Severe breathlessness: Rapidly worsening dyspnoea or inability to complete sentences suggests significant respiratory compromise requiring urgent assessment.
  • Haemoptysis: Coughing up blood may indicate severe infection, tissue necrosis, or an alternative diagnosis such as pulmonary embolism or malignancy.
  • Altered mental status: New confusion or reduced consciousness can signify severe sepsis, hypoxia, or hypercapnia, particularly in older adults.
  • Persistent high fever: Fever >38°C persisting beyond 48-72 hours despite initial management may indicate treatment failure or complications.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Lower respiratory tract infection"][/sc]
  • Older adults: May present with non-specific symptoms such as confusion, falls, or reduced mobility, with minimal cough or fever, making diagnosis challenging.
  • Immunocompromised individuals: Symptoms may be blunted, and the infection can progress rapidly with unusual pathogens, requiring a high index of suspicion.
  • Children: Viral LRTIs are common; presentation can range from mild cough to severe bronchiolitis or pneumonia, with rapid deterioration possible in infants.
  • Exacerbation of COPD/Asthma: A change in baseline cough, sputum production, or breathlessness may indicate an LRTI, often without classic signs of pneumonia. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="COPD exacerbation: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="COPD exacerbation"][/sc]
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Lower respiratory tract infection"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected pneumonia: Chest X-ray to confirm consolidation and rule out other pathologies.
  • Severity/risk tools: CURB-65 score for community-acquired pneumonia to guide management decisions and site of care. CURB-65 Score on MDCalc
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: D-dimer for suspected pulmonary embolism, or ECG for cardiac causes of breathlessness.
  • Blood tests: C-reactive protein (CRP) can help differentiate bacterial from viral infection and guide antibiotic prescribing, particularly in acute bronchitis.   • Microbiology: Sputum culture and sensitivity if severe pneumonia or treatment failure, or if atypical pathogens are suspected.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Lower respiratory tract infection"][/sc]
  • A 68-year-old male presents with a 5-day history of productive cough, increasing breathlessness, and fever, with crackles noted on chest auscultation.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Lower respiratory tract infection"][/sc]
  • Upper respiratory tract infection (URTI) - typically lacks signs of lower airway involvement (e.g., crackles, significant dyspnoea) and systemic illness is often milder.
  • Heart failure exacerbation - often presents with bilateral crackles and dyspnoea, but typically without fever or productive cough, and with signs of fluid overload.
  • Pulmonary embolism - acute onset dyspnoea and pleuritic chest pain, but often without fever or productive cough, and may have risk factors for VTE.
  • Asthma/COPD exacerbation - history of underlying disease, with wheeze and breathlessness, but fever and new focal chest signs are less common unless complicated by infection.
  • Pneumothorax - sudden onset of sharp pleuritic chest pain and dyspnoea, with reduced breath sounds and hyper-resonance on percussion.
  • Lung malignancy - chronic cough, weight loss, and haemoptysis may mimic LRTI, but symptoms are typically more insidious and persistent. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Lung malignancy presentation: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Lung malignancy"][/sc]
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Lower respiratory tract infection"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Recognition of severity (e.g., using CURB-65), escalation of care for severe cases, and supportive measures such as oxygen therapy for hypoxia and adequate hydration.
  • Definitive management: Antibiotic therapy is considered for bacterial pneumonia, guided by local antimicrobial guidelines and severity assessment. Viral LRTIs are typically managed symptomatically.
  • Adjunctive management: Bronchodilators may support symptom relief in patients with underlying obstructive airway disease or significant bronchospasm.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Clinical reassessment to ensure improvement, with consideration for repeat chest imaging if there is slow resolution or worsening symptoms.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Patients should be advised on symptoms that warrant re-evaluation, suchs as worsening breathlessness, persistent high fever, or new confusion.
  • Exam pharmacology reference (UKMLA/MRCGP): Common antibiotics for LRTI often include amoxicillin, doxycycline, or clarithromycin, depending on local resistance patterns and patient factors. Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Lower respiratory tract infection"][/sc]
  • Pleural effusion: Accumulation of fluid in the pleural space, often parapneumonic, which can cause worsening breathlessness and chest pain, particularly beyond 5-7 days of illness.
  • Empyema: Purulent fluid in the pleural space, a severe complication of pneumonia requiring drainage and prolonged antibiotics, often developing if pleural effusion is untreated.
  • Sepsis and septic shock: Systemic inflammatory response to infection leading to organ dysfunction, a life-threatening complication that can develop rapidly within hours to days.
  • Acute respiratory failure: Inability of the respiratory system to maintain adequate gas exchange, requiring ventilatory support, often seen in severe pneumonia or ARDS.
  • Lung abscess: Localised collection of pus within the lung parenchyma, typically a late complication of bacterial pneumonia, especially with aspiration or virulent pathogens.
