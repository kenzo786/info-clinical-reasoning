Epiglottitis [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Epiglottitis"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Epiglottitis"][/sc]
  • Acute inflammation of the epiglottis and surrounding supraglottic structures, which can rapidly progress to severe airway obstruction.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the signs and symptoms of acute epiglottitis, initiate immediate emergency management, and understand the importance of securing the airway in a controlled environment.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Epiglottitis"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Epiglottitis"][/sc]
  • The incidence of epiglottitis significantly decreased in children following the introduction of the Haemophilus influenzae type b (Hib) vaccine in the early 1990s in the UK [1].
  • Adult cases are now more common than paediatric cases, with an estimated incidence of 1.9 cases per 100,000 adults per year [2].
  • Risk factors include unvaccinated status (for Hib), immunocompromise, and direct trauma to the throat.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Epiglottitis"][/sc]
  • Trigger: Bacterial infection, most commonly *Haemophilus influenzae type b* (Hib), or other pathogens such as *Streptococcus pneumoniae* or Group A streptococci, leads to acute inflammation of the epiglottis.
  • Progression: The inflamed epiglottis and surrounding supraglottic tissues swell rapidly, obstructing the laryngeal inlet. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Epiglottic swelling: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Epiglottic swelling"][/sc]
  • Consequence: Airway obstruction can lead to respiratory distress, hypoxia, and potentially complete airway collapse, constituting a medical emergency.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Epiglottitis"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Symptoms often develop rapidly over a few hours, typically presenting with a severe sore throat, fever, and difficulty swallowing.
  • Progressive phase: Within hours, airway compromise can become evident with muffled voice, drooling, and signs of respiratory distress such as stridor, particularly beyond 6-12 hours without intervention.
  • Late phase: If untreated, complete airway obstruction and respiratory arrest can occur rapidly, often within 24 hours of symptom onset.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Epiglottitis"][/sc]
  • Ask about severe sore throat - often disproportionate to clinical findings on oral examination, suggesting a deeper pathology.
  • Ask about muffled voice - often described as a "hot potato voice," indicating supraglottic swelling affecting vocal resonance.
  • Ask about drooling - due to painful swallowing (odynophagia) and inability to manage secretions, a critical sign of impending airway compromise, especially in children.
  • Ask about difficulty swallowing (dysphagia) - a prominent symptom that can rapidly worsen, leading to complete inability to swallow.
  • Ask about respiratory distress - including rapid breathing, noisy breathing (stridor), or leaning forward (tripod position) to maximise airway patency.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Epiglottitis"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - often reveal tachycardia, tachypnoea, and fever; hypoxia is a late and concerning sign.
  • Check for tripod position - the patient sits upright, leaning forward with neck extended and mouth open, to optimise airflow. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Tripod position: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Tripod position"][/sc]
  • Check for drooling - indicates severe odynophagia and inability to clear secretions, suggesting significant airway obstruction.
  • Check for muffled voice - a characteristic sign, often described as a "hot potato voice," due to swelling around the vocal cords.
  • Check for stridor - an inspiratory high-pitched sound, which can be a late sign of advanced upper airway obstruction.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Epiglottitis"][/sc]
  • Rapid onset of symptoms - suggests a rapidly progressive condition with potential for acute airway compromise.
  • Drooling and inability to swallow secretions - indicates severe pharyngeal inflammation and impending airway obstruction.
  • Stridor or respiratory distress - signs of significant airway narrowing requiring immediate intervention.
  • Tripod position - a compensatory posture to maintain airway patency, signalling severe respiratory compromise.
  • Muffled voice - suggests significant swelling of the supraglottic structures.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Epiglottitis"][/sc]
  • Adults: May present with a slower onset of symptoms and less severe respiratory distress compared to children, often with only a severe sore throat and odynophagia. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Adult epiglottitis: how does it differ from paediatric presentation?" text="Adult epiglottitis"][/sc]
  • Immunocompromised patients: May have an atypical pathogen or a more fulminant course, with rapid progression to severe airway compromise.
  • Partial vaccination: Individuals with incomplete Hib vaccination may present with milder symptoms, potentially delaying diagnosis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Epiglottitis"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected epiglottitis: Direct flexible laryngoscopy in a controlled environment (e.g., operating theatre) with ENT and anaesthetic support to visualise the epiglottis and assess swelling.
  • Severity/risk tools: No validated severity or risk scores are routinely used for epiglottitis.
  • Imaging - Lateral neck X-ray: May reveal a "thumb sign" due to a swollen epiglottis, but should only be performed in stable patients where it will not delay definitive airway management. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Thumb sign on lateral neck X-ray: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Thumb sign"][/sc]
  • Imaging - CT scan: Can delineate the extent of inflammation and identify abscess formation, typically reserved for stable patients or when an alternative diagnosis is suspected.
  • Laboratory investigations: Blood tests (FBC, CRP) and blood cultures may be performed once the airway is secure to identify the causative organism and guide antibiotic therapy.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Epiglottitis"][/sc]
  • A previously healthy 4-year-old child presents with a sudden onset of high fever, severe sore throat, drooling, and inspiratory stridor, preferring to sit in a tripod position.
  • Acute supraglottic inflammation with impending airway obstruction, concerning for epiglottitis.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Epiglottitis"][/sc]
  • Croup (Laryngotracheobronchitis) - distinguished by a barking cough, hoarse voice, and typically viral aetiology, with less acute airway compromise.
  • Bacterial tracheitis - presents with a brassy cough, high fever, and stridor, but often has a more toxic appearance and purulent secretions.
  • Peritonsillar abscess (Quinsy) - characterised by unilateral sore throat, trismus, and deviation of the uvula, without the rapid airway compromise of epiglottitis.
  • Retropharyngeal abscess - presents with neck stiffness, dysphagia, and fever, but typically without the prominent drooling and tripod position.
  • Foreign body aspiration - sudden onset of choking, coughing, and respiratory distress, often with a clear history of aspiration. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Foreign body aspiration: how to differentiate from epiglottitis?" text="Foreign body aspiration"][/sc]
  • Anaphylaxis - rapid onset of widespread allergic reaction, often with urticaria, angioedema, and bronchospasm, in addition to airway swelling.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Epiglottitis"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Recognition of a medical emergency, immediate escalation to senior medical staff (ENT, anaesthetics), maintaining the patient in an upright position, and preparing for definitive airway management in a controlled environment.
  • Definitive management: Securing the airway, typically via endotracheal intubation, in an operating theatre with experienced personnel. A surgical airway (tracheostomy) may be considered if intubation is not possible.
  • Adjunctive management: Intravenous broad-spectrum antibiotics (e.g., a third-generation cephalosporin like ceftriaxone) to cover common pathogens, corticosteroids (e.g., dexamethasone) to reduce inflammation, and humidified oxygen.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Patients often require admission to a critical care setting for close monitoring of the airway and respiratory status, with extubation performed in a controlled environment once swelling has resolved.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Any signs of worsening respiratory distress, increasing stridor, or difficulty swallowing should prompt immediate re-evaluation and escalation of care.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Epiglottitis"][/sc]
  • Airway obstruction: The most critical complication, leading to respiratory arrest and death if not promptly managed, often developing rapidly within hours.
  • Epiglottic abscess: Localised collection of pus within the epiglottis, which can exacerbate airway obstruction and may require surgical drainage.
  • Pneumonia: A potential complication due to aspiration or spread of infection to the lower respiratory tract.
  • Sepsis: Systemic inflammatory response to infection, which can lead to multi-organ dysfunction and is a significant cause of morbidity and mortality.
  • Meningitis: Although rare with widespread Hib vaccination, it is a serious complication if the infection spreads to the central nervous system.
