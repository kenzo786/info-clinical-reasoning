Vitamin D deficiency [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Vitamin D deficiency"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Vitamin D deficiency"][/sc]
  • Vitamin D deficiency is a common condition characterised by inadequate levels of vitamin D, essential for calcium homeostasis and bone metabolism.
  • **UKMLA Learning Outcome:** Understand the causes, clinical features, diagnosis, and management of Vitamin D deficiency.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Vitamin D deficiency"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Vitamin D deficiency"][/sc]
  • Approximately 1 billion people worldwide are affected by vitamin D deficiency, with nearly 50% of some populations experiencing insufficiency.
  • In the UK, it is advised that everyone takes a daily supplement containing 10 micrograms (400 international units) of vitamin D during autumn and winter months.
  • Around 20% of adults in the UK may have low vitamin D status.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Vitamin D deficiency"][/sc]
  • **Trigger:** Inadequate dietary intake, reduced sun exposure, or malabsorption leads to decreased vitamin D levels.
  • **Progression:** Low vitamin D results in reduced intestinal absorption of calcium and phosphorus, triggering parathyroid hormone (PTH) production and secondary hyperparathyroidism.
  • **Consequence:** Elevated PTH stimulates osteoclast activity, accelerating bone turnover and causing thinning and porosity of cortical bone, leading to osteomalacia and osteoporosis in adults, and rickets in children.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Vitamin D deficiency"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Often asymptomatic, with subclinical vitamin D deficiency linked to increased risk of osteoporosis, falls, and fractures.
  • Progressive phase: Prolonged and severe deficiency can lead to symptoms related to secondary hyperparathyroidism, such as bone pain, arthralgia, myalgia, fatigue, and muscle weakness.
  • Late phase: Chronic vitamin D deficiency may result in fragility fractures and established osteoporosis in adults, and rickets with bone changes and developmental delay in children.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Vitamin D deficiency"][/sc]
  • Ask about fatigue that won't go away - *may indicate chronic deficiency and impact daily activities*.
  • Ask about muscle aches and weakness - *common manifestations of osteomalacia or secondary hyperparathyroidism*.
  • Ask about bone pain or tenderness - *suggests skeletal involvement due to demineralisation*.
  • Ask about mood changes or depression - *potential association with vitamin D deficiency, often warrants further investigation*.
  • Ask about frequent illnesses or infections - *vitamin D plays a role in immune function*.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Vitamin D deficiency"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - *typically normal, but severe cases may show signs of hypocalcaemia*.
  • Check for bone tenderness or deformities - *may indicate osteomalacia or rickets*.
  • Check muscle strength - *proximal muscle weakness can be a sign*.
  • Check for gait abnormalities - *waddling gait may be present in severe osteomalacia*.
  • Check for Chvostek's or Trousseau's sign - *if hypocalcaemia is severe, indicating neuromuscular irritability*.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Vitamin D deficiency"][/sc]
  • Severe bone pain: May indicate significant osteomalacia or impending pathological fracture.
  • Recurrent fractures with minimal trauma: Suggests underlying bone fragility, such as osteoporosis.
  • Proximal muscle weakness affecting mobility: Can lead to falls and functional decline, particularly in older adults.
  • Symptoms of hypocalcaemia (e.g., paraesthesia, tetany, seizures): Requires urgent medical attention to prevent life-threatening complications.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Vitamin D deficiency"][/sc]
  • Older adults: May present with non-specific symptoms like generalised weakness, fatigue, and increased falls risk, often without overt bone pain.
  • Individuals with malabsorption syndromes: Symptoms may be masked by or attributed to their primary gastrointestinal condition, delaying diagnosis.
  • Dark-skinned individuals: Due to reduced cutaneous vitamin D synthesis, they may have severe deficiency despite adequate sun exposure, often presenting with more pronounced skeletal symptoms.
  • Patients on certain medications (e.g., anticonvulsants): May develop deficiency due to increased hepatic catabolism of vitamin D, presenting with unexplained musculoskeletal pain.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Vitamin D deficiency"][/sc]
  • Core: Serum 25-hydroxyvitamin D (25(OH)D) is the primary diagnostic test. Levels below 20 ng/mL (50 nmol/L) indicate deficiency, while 20-30 ng/mL (50-75 nmol/L) suggest insufficiency.
  • Core: Serum calcium, phosphate, alkaline phosphatase (ALP), and parathyroid hormone (PTH) to assess for secondary hyperparathyroidism and bone metabolism.
  • Imaging: X-rays of long bones may show features of osteomalacia (e.g., Looser's zones) or rickets (e.g., widened growth plates, cupping, fraying).
  • If alternative diagnoses considered: Bone mineral density (BMD) measurement via DEXA scan to assess for osteoporosis.
  • Serial reassessment: Repeat 25(OH)D levels after initiation of treatment to ensure repletion.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Vitamin D deficiency"][/sc]
  • A patient presenting with chronic widespread musculoskeletal pain, fatigue, and a history of limited sun exposure, often warrants investigation for Vitamin D deficiency.
  • This is a metabolic bone disorder characterised by inadequate mineralisation of bone, leading to skeletal fragility and systemic symptoms.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Vitamin D deficiency"][/sc]
  • Osteoporosis - *distinguished by normal vitamin D and calcium levels, primary bone density loss*.
  • Fibromyalgia - *characterised by widespread pain and tenderness, but without specific biochemical markers of bone disease*.
  • Hypothyroidism - *fatigue and muscle weakness are common, but typically associated with thyroid hormone abnormalities*.
  • Chronic fatigue syndrome - *diagnosis of exclusion, no specific biochemical markers*.
  • Rheumatoid arthritis - *inflammatory joint disease with specific serological markers and joint involvement patterns*.
  • Polymyalgia rheumatica - *presents with shoulder and hip girdle pain and stiffness, elevated inflammatory markers*.
  • Metastatic bone disease - *history of malignancy, focal bone pain, and lytic/blastic lesions on imaging*.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Vitamin D deficiency"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Address severe symptoms like hypocalcaemia if present, which may require intravenous calcium.
  • Definitive management: Supplementation with vitamin D3 (cholecalciferol) is preferred due to its higher efficacy. Dosing depends on severity of deficiency and patient factors.
  • Definitive management: Lifestyle advice including safe sun exposure and dietary sources (e.g., oily fish, fortified foods).
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Reassess vitamin D levels after 3-6 months of supplementation to ensure repletion and adjust dosing as needed.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Consider bone mineral density screening if osteoporosis is suspected.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Vitamin D deficiency"][/sc]
  • Osteomalacia: Impaired bone mineralisation in adults, leading to bone pain, muscle weakness, and increased fracture risk.
  • Rickets: Impaired bone mineralisation in children, causing bone deformities, growth retardation, and skeletal pain.
  • Osteoporosis: Reduced bone density and microarchitectural deterioration, significantly increasing the risk of fragility fractures.
  • Secondary hyperparathyroidism: Chronic elevation of PTH due to persistent hypocalcaemia, leading to increased bone turnover and further bone loss.
  • Increased risk of falls: Due to muscle weakness and bone fragility, particularly in older adults.
  • Hypocalcaemic seizures/tetany: Rare but severe complication of profound vitamin D deficiency and hypocalcaemia, requiring urgent intervention.
