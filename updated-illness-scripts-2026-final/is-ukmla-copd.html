Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD)"][/sc]
  • Chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD) is a common, preventable, and treatable disease characterized by persistent respiratory symptoms and airflow limitation due to airway and/or alveolar abnormalities, usually caused by significant exposure to noxious particles or gases.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical features, risk factors, and diagnostic criteria for COPD, including the interpretation of spirometry, and outline the principles of its long-term management and acute exacerbation.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD)"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD)"][/sc]
  • COPD affects approximately 1.2 million people in the UK, making it the second most common lung disease after asthma. [1]
  • Prevalence increases with age, with the majority of diagnoses occurring in individuals over 40 years. [1]
  • Smoking is the primary risk factor, accounting for around 90% of cases, but exposure to occupational dusts, chemicals, and air pollution also contribute. [1]
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD)"][/sc]
  • Chronic exposure to noxious particles (e.g., cigarette smoke) triggers a chronic inflammatory response in the airways and lung parenchyma, leading to structural changes.
  • This inflammation causes small airway remodelling (bronchiolitis) and parenchymal destruction (emphysema), resulting in irreversible airflow limitation and gas trapping. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Air trapping in COPD: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="air trapping"][/sc]
  • The consequence is progressive dyspnoea, chronic cough, sputum production, and reduced exercise capacity, often complicated by acute exacerbations.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD)"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Insidious onset over years to decades, often asymptomatic or with mild, intermittent cough and sputum production, particularly in smokers.
  • Progressive phase: Gradual worsening of symptoms, including persistent dyspnoea on exertion, chronic cough, and increased frequency of respiratory infections, typically developing over several years.
  • Late phase: Severe and persistent airflow limitation, with significant impact on daily activities, frequent acute exacerbations requiring hospitalisation, and potential development of cor pulmonale, often occurring after many years of disease progression.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD)"][/sc]
  • Ask about exertional breathlessness - often the most debilitating symptom, limiting physical activity and quality of life.
  • Ask about chronic cough - typically daily, productive of sputum, and often worse in the mornings.
  • Ask about regular sputum production - often clear or white, but can become purulent during exacerbations.
  • Ask about frequent winter \'bronchitis\' - recurrent episodes of increased cough and sputum, indicating increased susceptibility to respiratory infections.
  • Ask about wheeze - a whistling sound during breathing, often intermittent but can be persistent, particularly during exacerbations.
  • Ask about reduced exercise tolerance - a key indicator of disease progression and impact on functional capacity.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD)"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - may reveal tachypnoea, tachycardia, and hypoxaemia, especially during exacerbations.
  • Check for prolonged expiratory phase - indicative of airflow obstruction and air trapping. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Prolonged expiratory phase in COPD: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="prolonged expiratory phase"][/sc]
  • Check for use of accessory respiratory muscles - suggests increased work of breathing and respiratory distress.
  • Check for barrel chest - a sign of hyperinflation due to chronic air trapping.
  • Check for cyanosis - a late sign of severe hypoxaemia.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD)"][/sc]
  • Acute worsening of dyspnoea: Suggests an acute exacerbation, potentially requiring urgent medical attention and hospitalisation.
  • New onset or worsening peripheral oedema: May indicate right heart failure (cor pulmonale) due to chronic pulmonary hypertension.
  • Altered mental status: Can be a sign of severe hypoxaemia or hypercapnia, requiring immediate assessment and intervention.
  • Haemoptysis: While uncommon in COPD, it raises suspicion for alternative diagnoses such as lung cancer or bronchiectasis, warranting further investigation.
  • Unexplained weight loss: May indicate systemic inflammation, malignancy, or severe disease progression.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD)"][/sc]
  • Non-smokers: COPD can occur in individuals with significant exposure to biomass fuel smoke, occupational dusts, or genetic factors like alpha-1 antitrypsin deficiency.
  • Younger patients: While typically a disease of older adults, early-onset COPD can occur, particularly with severe alpha-1 antitrypsin deficiency or heavy smoking from a young age.
  • Predominant cough and sputum: Some patients may present primarily with chronic cough and sputum production, with less prominent dyspnoea, leading to delayed diagnosis.
  • Frequent exacerbations with mild airflow limitation: A subset of patients may experience frequent acute exacerbations despite relatively preserved spirometry, indicating a phenotype prone to exacerbations.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD)"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected COPD: Spirometry with post-bronchodilator testing is essential to confirm persistent airflow limitation (FEV1/FVC ratio < 0.7). [sc name="jitl-query" Q="FEV1/FVC ratio in COPD: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="FEV1/FVC ratio"][/sc]
  • Severity/risk tools: Global Initiative for Chronic Obstructive Lung Disease (GOLD) classification for severity assessment. GOLD COPD Assessment on MDCalc
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Chest X-ray to exclude other pathologies (e.g., lung cancer, heart failure) and full blood count to identify polycythaemia or anaemia.
  • Imaging - CT scan of the thorax: To investigate symptoms disproportionate to spirometric impairment, signs suggesting other lung diagnoses (e.g., bronchiectasis), or to assess suitability for lung volume reduction procedures.
  • Serial reassessment: Regular spirometry to monitor disease progression and assess response to treatment.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD)"][/sc]
  • A middle-aged or older patient with a significant smoking history presenting with progressive exertional dyspnoea, chronic productive cough, and recurrent respiratory infections.
  • Chronic, progressive airflow limitation with acute exacerbations, concerning for chronic obstructive pulmonary disease.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD)"][/sc]
  • Asthma - often presents earlier in life, with diurnal variability in symptoms and significant reversibility on spirometry.
  • Bronchiectasis - characterised by chronic cough with copious purulent sputum and recurrent infections, often with characteristic CT findings.
  • Heart failure - presents with dyspnoea, cough, and fatigue, but typically with orthopnoea, paroxysmal nocturnal dyspnoea, and signs of fluid overload.
  • Lung cancer - new or changing cough, haemoptysis, unexplained weight loss, and focal chest signs warrant investigation.
  • Tuberculosis - chronic cough, weight loss, night sweats, and haemoptysis, especially in endemic areas or immunocompromised individuals.
  • Alpha-1 antitrypsin deficiency - early-onset emphysema, especially in non-smokers or those with a family history. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Alpha-1 antitrypsin deficiency: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="alpha-1 antitrypsin deficiency"][/sc]
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD)"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Recognition of acute exacerbation, assessment of severity, oxygen therapy (controlled to target SpO2 88-92%), bronchodilators, systemic corticosteroids, and antibiotics if bacterial infection is suspected.
  • Definitive management: Smoking cessation is paramount. Pharmacological management often includes bronchodilators (short-acting and long-acting beta-agonists and muscarinic antagonists) and inhaled corticosteroids for frequent exacerbators. Pulmonary rehabilitation is a key non-pharmacological intervention.
  • Adjunctive management: Annual influenza and pneumococcal vaccinations, nutritional support, and management of comorbidities.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Regular review of symptoms, spirometry, exacerbation frequency, and inhaler technique.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Patient education on self-management plans for exacerbations, including when to seek urgent medical advice. Referral to specialist services for severe disease or complex management.
  • Exam pharmacology reference (UKMLA/MRCGP): Bronchodilators (e.g., [sc name="jitl-medication" Q="Salbutamol use in COPD" text="salbutamol"][/sc], [sc name="jitl-medication" Q="Tiotropium use in COPD" text="tiotropium"][/sc]) and inhaled corticosteroids (e.g., [sc name="jitl-medication" Q="Fluticasone use in COPD" text="fluticasone"][/sc], [sc name="jitl-medication" Q="Budesonide use in COPD" text="budesonide"][/sc]) are commonly used. Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD)"][/sc]
  • Acute exacerbations: Characterised by acute worsening of respiratory symptoms, often triggered by infection or environmental factors, leading to increased morbidity and mortality.
  • Respiratory failure: Chronic hypoxaemia and hypercapnia can develop in advanced disease, requiring long-term oxygen therapy or non-invasive ventilation.
  • Cor pulmonale: Chronic pulmonary hypertension due to hypoxic vasoconstriction and vascular remodelling can lead to right ventricular hypertrophy and failure.
  • Pneumonia: Increased susceptibility to bacterial and viral respiratory infections, which can trigger exacerbations and worsen lung function.
  • Pneumothorax: Bullae in emphysematous lungs can rupture, leading to spontaneous pneumothorax, a medical emergency.
  • Cachexia and muscle wasting: Systemic inflammation and increased metabolic demand can lead to significant weight loss and sarcopenia, impacting prognosis.
