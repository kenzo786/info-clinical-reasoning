Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Folliculitis and Hidradenitis suppurativa"][/sc]
  • Folliculitis is an inflammatory condition affecting hair follicles, presenting as tender red spots or pustules, which can be superficial or deep and occur in any hair-bearing area.
  • Hidradenitis suppurativa (HS) is a chronic, recurrent inflammatory disease of apocrine follicles, typically affecting intertriginous areas like the axillae and groin, characterised by nodules, abscesses, sinus tracts, and scarring.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise and differentiate common inflammatory skin conditions affecting hair follicles and apocrine glands, including their typical presentations and associated risk factors.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Folliculitis and Hidradenitis suppurativa"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Folliculitis and Hidradenitis suppurativa"][/sc]
  • Hidradenitis suppurativa affects approximately 1% of the European population, with an average age of onset around 21 years.
  • HS is more prevalent in women and individuals of Afro-Caribbean descent, and rare in those of Asian descent.
  • Risk factors for HS include cigarette smoking, obesity, and a family history in about 30% of cases.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Folliculitis and Hidradenitis suppurativa"][/sc]
  • Trigger: Folliculitis often arises from infection, commonly by Staphylococcus aureus, or from physical irritation and occlusion of hair follicles. HS is initiated by follicular occlusion within apocrine gland-bearing skin, leading to inflammation.
  • Progression: In folliculitis, inflammation can be superficial or extend deeper, potentially forming furuncles or carbuncles. In HS, chronic occlusion and inflammation lead to rupture of follicles, extending into the dermis and subcutaneous tissue, forming abscesses and interconnected sinus tracts.
  • Consequence: Untreated folliculitis may resolve or progress to deeper infections, potentially causing scarring. HS is characterised by chronic relapsing inflammation, mucopurulent discharge, progressive scarring, and induration, significantly impacting quality of life. Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Folliculitis and Hidradenitis suppurativa"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Folliculitis typically presents acutely, with individual lesions developing over hours to a few days. HS often begins with isolated, painful nodules that may persist for weeks or months.
  • Progressive phase: Folliculitis may resolve spontaneously or, if untreated, progress to deeper infections like furuncles. HS is characterised by recurrent flares, with nodules developing into pustules that rupture and discharge, often in the same region.
  • Late phase: While most folliculitis resolves without sequelae, severe or recurrent cases can lead to scarring and permanent hair loss. HS is a chronic condition marked by dense fibrosis, extensive scarring, and the formation of chronic sinus tracts, particularly beyond several months to years of disease activity.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Folliculitis and Hidradenitis suppurativa"][/sc]
  • Ask about pain and tenderness - to differentiate from asymptomatic lesions and assess severity.
  • Ask about itch - common in superficial folliculitis and can indicate fungal aetiology (e.g., Malassezia folliculitis).
  • Ask about discharge - purulent discharge is characteristic of deeper folliculitis and HS, indicating infection or inflammation.
  • Ask about recurrence and location - recurrent lesions in intertriginous areas are highly suggestive of HS.
  • Ask about aggravating factors - such as shaving, tight clothing, or heat, which can exacerbate both conditions.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Folliculitis and Hidradenitis suppurativa"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - usually normal in uncomplicated folliculitis; fever may indicate systemic infection in severe cases of folliculitis or HS.
  • Check skin lesions - folliculitis presents as erythematous papules or pustules centred on hair follicles. HS manifests as inflammatory nodules, abscesses, comedones, and sinus tracts, often with scarring.
  • Check distribution - folliculitis can occur anywhere hair grows; HS typically affects axillae, groin, perineum, buttocks, and submammary regions.
  • Check for scarring and sinus tracts - indicative of chronic inflammation and a hallmark of HS, particularly in later stages. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Hidradenitis suppurativa: what are the key dermatological signs?" text="Sinus tracts in HS"][/sc]
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Folliculitis and Hidradenitis suppurativa"][/sc]
  • Systemic signs of infection - fever, tachycardia, or hypotension suggest sepsis, particularly with extensive or rapidly spreading lesions.
  • Rapid progression or extensive involvement - indicates a more aggressive infection or inflammatory process requiring urgent assessment.
  • Deep-seated, fluctuant lesions - may require incision and drainage, especially if associated with significant pain or systemic symptoms.
  • Failure to respond to initial treatment - warrants re-evaluation for resistant organisms, alternative diagnoses, or underlying systemic issues.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Folliculitis and Hidradenitis suppurativa"][/sc]
  • Children: Folliculitis is common, but HS is rare before puberty; its presence should prompt investigation for underlying conditions.
  • Immunosuppressed patients: May present with more severe, widespread, or unusual forms of folliculitis (e.g., fungal, viral) or rapidly progressive HS.
  • Pseudofolliculitis barbae: An irritant folliculitis-like condition caused by ingrowing hairs after shaving, particularly in individuals with curly hair, often mistaken for bacterial folliculitis.
  • Gram-negative folliculitis: A rare form of folliculitis, typically seen after long-term antibiotic use, caused by gram-negative organisms.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Folliculitis and Hidradenitis suppurativa"][/sc]
  • Furunculosis/Carbunculosis - typically solitary or clustered deep infections, often resolving with treatment, lacking the chronic, recurrent, and scarring nature of HS.
  • Acne vulgaris - primarily affects the face, chest, and back, characterised by comedones, papules, pustules, and cysts, but without the deep sinus tracts and extensive scarring seen in HS.
  • Abscess - usually a solitary collection of pus, often acute, lacking the chronic recurrence and specific anatomical distribution of HS.
  • Crohn's disease (cutaneous manifestations) - can present with perianal lesions resembling HS, but typically associated with gastrointestinal symptoms and specific histological findings.
  • Lymphadenitis - acute inflammation of lymph nodes, often painful, but usually distinct from the follicular involvement and sinus tract formation of HS.
  • Pilonidal sinus - typically located in the sacrococcygeal region, presenting as a sinus tract with discharge, but distinct from the widespread apocrine gland involvement of HS.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Folliculitis and Hidradenitis suppurativa"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Recognition of potential systemic infection and appropriate escalation. Supportive care may include pain management and wound care.
  • Definitive management: For folliculitis, often involves topical or oral antibiotics depending on severity and causative organism. For HS, management is multidisciplinary, often including lifestyle modifications (smoking cessation, weight management), topical or systemic antibiotics, anti-inflammatory agents, and biologics (e.g., adalimumab, secukinumab for moderate to severe disease). Surgical intervention may be considered for chronic lesions or extensive sinus tracts.
  • Adjunctive management: Consideration of psychological support due to the significant impact on quality of life.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Regular assessment of disease activity, response to treatment, and screening for associated comorbidities.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Patients should be advised on when to seek further medical attention for worsening symptoms or signs of complications.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Folliculitis and Hidradenitis suppurativa"][/sc]
  • Cellulitis/Abscess formation: Localised spread of infection from folliculitis or acute exacerbation of HS, potentially leading to systemic infection if not managed promptly.
  • Scarring and disfigurement: Chronic inflammation and recurrent lesions, particularly in HS, result in significant scarring, which can cause functional impairment and psychological distress.
  • Sinus tract and fistula formation: A hallmark of advanced HS, leading to persistent discharge, pain, and potential connection to other structures (e.g., bowel, bladder) over months to years.
  • Psychological impact: Chronic pain, disfigurement, and recurrent flares can lead to depression, anxiety, and social isolation, significantly impacting quality of life.
  • Squamous cell carcinoma: A rare but serious long-term complication of chronic, non-healing HS lesions, particularly in areas of persistent inflammation and scarring.
