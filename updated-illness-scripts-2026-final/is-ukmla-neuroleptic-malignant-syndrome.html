Neuroleptic Malignant Syndrome [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Neuroleptic Malignant Syndrome"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Neuroleptic Malignant Syndrome"][/sc]
  • A rare, life-threatening idiosyncratic reaction to dopamine-receptor antagonist medications (antipsychotics) or abrupt withdrawal of dopamine agonists, characterised by hyperthermia, muscle rigidity, altered consciousness, and autonomic instability.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the features of neuroleptic malignant syndrome, distinguish it from other causes of hyperthermia and altered mental status (especially serotonin syndrome), and initiate immediate supportive management.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Neuroleptic Malignant Syndrome"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Neuroleptic Malignant Syndrome"][/sc]
  • The incidence is estimated to be 0.01-0.02% of patients treated with antipsychotics. It can occur at any age, but is more common in young adult males.
  • Risk factors include use of high-potency antipsychotics, rapid dose escalation, parenteral administration, and a prior history of NMS.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Neuroleptic Malignant Syndrome"][/sc]
  • Trigger: Abrupt, significant reduction in central dopamine activity, most commonly due to D2 receptor blockade by antipsychotic medication.
  • Progression: Dopamine blockade in the hypothalamus, nigrostriatal pathways, and corpus striatum leads to dysregulated thermoregulation, profound muscle rigidity, and autonomic dysfunction.
  • Consequence: Uncontrolled muscle contraction generates excessive heat, leading to hyperthermia and rhabdomyolysis, while autonomic instability causes cardiovascular compromise. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Rhabdomyolysis: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Rhabdomyolysis"][/sc]
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Neuroleptic Malignant Syndrome"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Altered mental status and muscle rigidity often develop within hours to days of starting or increasing the dose of a dopamine antagonist.
  • Progressive phase: Hyperthermia and autonomic instability (tachycardia, labile blood pressure, diaphoresis) typically follow over the next 24-72 hours.
  • Late phase: Complications such as rhabdomyolysis, acute kidney injury, and arrhythmias may develop, particularly beyond 72 hours if untreated.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Neuroleptic Malignant Syndrome"][/sc]
  • Ask about confusion or agitation - this is often the first sign and can progress to stupor or coma.
  • Ask about muscle stiffness or "lead-pipe" rigidity - a core feature that distinguishes NMS from other conditions.
  • Ask about fever and sweating - hyperthermia is a key diagnostic criterion and can be severe.
  • Ask about recent medication changes - specifically the initiation or dose increase of an antipsychotic, or withdrawal of a dopamine agonist.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Neuroleptic Malignant Syndrome"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - for hyperthermia (>38°C), tachycardia, and labile blood pressure.
  • Check for generalised muscle rigidity - often described as "lead-pipe" rigidity, which is a key sign.
  • Check for altered mental status - ranging from confusion and agitation to delirium and coma. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Altered mental status: what are the causes and clinical significance?" text="Altered mental status"][/sc]
  • Check for signs of autonomic instability - such as diaphoresis, pallor, and incontinence.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Neuroleptic Malignant Syndrome"][/sc]
  • Hyperthermia >40°C - indicates severe thermoregulatory failure and high risk of multi-organ damage.
  • Profound muscle rigidity - can lead to respiratory compromise and rhabdomyolysis.
  • Rapidly deteriorating consciousness - suggests severe central nervous system dysfunction.
  • Autonomic collapse - marked by haemodynamic instability and cardiovascular collapse.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Neuroleptic Malignant Syndrome"][/sc]
  • Older adults: May present with less pronounced fever and rigidity, but with more prominent confusion and dehydration.
  • Patients on atypical antipsychotics: May have a less severe presentation with lower peak temperatures and less rigidity.
  • Patients with pre-existing neurological conditions: The presentation can be confounded by underlying motor or cognitive deficits. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Atypical NMS presentations: how do they impact diagnosis?" text="Atypical NMS presentations"][/sc]
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Neuroleptic Malignant Syndrome"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected Neuroleptic Malignant Syndrome: Markedly elevated creatine kinase (CK) is a hallmark finding, often >1000 IU/L. Also check FBC (leukocytosis), U&Es (for AKI), and LFTs. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Creatine kinase elevation: what does it indicate and why is it important?" text="Elevated CK"][/sc]
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Consider blood cultures, lumbar puncture, and toxicology screen to exclude sepsis, meningitis, and other drug-induced syndromes.
  • Imaging - Brain CT/MRI: May be useful to exclude structural brain pathology, but is typically normal in NMS.
  • Serial reassessment: Regular monitoring of CK, renal function, and vital signs is crucial to assess progression and response to treatment.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Neuroleptic Malignant Syndrome"][/sc]
  • A patient presenting with acute onset of altered mental status, severe muscle rigidity, hyperthermia, and autonomic dysfunction following initiation of an antipsychotic medication.
  • A medical emergency characterised by a tetrad of altered mental status, muscle rigidity, autonomic instability, and hyperthermia, concerning for Neuroleptic Malignant Syndrome.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Neuroleptic Malignant Syndrome"][/sc]
  • Serotonin syndrome - distinguished by the presence of hyperreflexia, myoclonus, and tremor, and a history of serotonergic agent use. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="NMS vs serotonin syndrome: what are the key differences?" text="NMS vs serotonin syndrome"][/sc]
  • Malignant catatonia - can mimic NMS, but often lacks clear exposure to dopamine antagonists and may respond to benzodiazepines. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Malignant catatonia: how does it differ from NMS?" text="Malignant catatonia"][/sc]
  • Malignant hyperthermia - occurs in the context of general anaesthesia and is characterised by a rapid rise in end-tidal CO2.
  • Meningitis/Encephalitis - usually associated with meningism, focal neurological signs, and specific CSF findings.
  • Sepsis - a source of infection is usually identifiable, and the pattern of autonomic dysfunction may differ.
  • Heatstroke - related to environmental exposure and lack of diaphoresis is a key feature.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Neuroleptic Malignant Syndrome"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Recognition, immediate cessation of the offending antipsychotic agent, and urgent transfer to a medical facility for supportive care.
  • Definitive management: Supportive care is the mainstay of treatment, including aggressive fluid resuscitation, cooling measures, and monitoring in an intensive care setting.
  • Adjunctive management: In severe cases, specific pharmacological agents such as dantrolene or bromocriptine may be considered, but their efficacy is not definitively established.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Close monitoring of vital signs, fluid balance, renal function, and CK levels is essential.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Patients require intensive care if they have severe hyperthermia, cardiovascular instability, or respiratory failure.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Neuroleptic Malignant Syndrome"][/sc]
  • Rhabdomyolysis: Muscle breakdown releases myoglobin, leading to myoglobinuria and acute kidney injury.
  • Acute kidney injury: Can result from rhabdomyolysis, dehydration, and hypotension.
  • Aspiration pneumonia: Altered consciousness and dysphagia increase the risk of aspiration.
  • Venous thromboembolism: Immobility and dehydration are significant risk factors.
  • Arrhythmias and cardiovascular collapse: Can occur due to autonomic instability and electrolyte disturbances.
