Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Lung abscess"][/sc]
  • A lung abscess is a localized collection of pus within the lung parenchyma, leading to cavitation, typically greater than 2 cm in diameter.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: To recognise the clinical features, risk factors, and radiological findings of a lung abscess, and to outline initial management principles.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Lung abscess"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Lung abscess"][/sc]
  • While specific UK incidence and prevalence figures for lung abscess are not well-established, it is considered an uncommon but serious condition.
  • Risk factors include conditions predisposing to aspiration, such as altered consciousness (e.g., alcoholism, drug overdose, general anaesthesia), dysphagia, poor oral hygiene, and gastro-oesophageal reflux disease.
  • Secondary lung abscesses can arise from pneumonia, bronchial obstruction (e.g., tumour, foreign body), or septic emboli.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Lung abscess"][/sc]
  • Trigger: Aspiration of oropharyngeal or gastric contents, often containing anaerobic bacteria, into the lower respiratory tract, particularly the right lower lobe due to anatomical considerations.
  • Progression: The aspirated material leads to pneumonitis, followed by tissue necrosis and liquefaction, forming a pus-filled cavity. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Liquefactive necrosis: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Liquefactive necrosis"][/sc]
  • Consequence: The abscess cavity may communicate with the bronchial tree, leading to expectoration of purulent sputum and the formation of an air-fluid level visible on imaging.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Lung abscess"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Initial symptoms are often insidious, mimicking pneumonia, with cough, fever, and malaise developing over days to weeks.
  • Progressive phase: As tissue necrosis and cavitation occur, symptoms may worsen, including productive cough with foul-smelling sputum, pleuritic chest pain, and weight loss, particularly beyond 1-2 weeks.
  • Late phase: Without effective treatment, the abscess can enlarge, leading to significant lung damage, systemic complications, and prolonged recovery, potentially extending over several weeks to months.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Lung abscess"][/sc]
  • Ask about productive cough - often purulent and foul-smelling, indicating anaerobic infection and communication with the bronchial tree.
  • Ask about fever and chills - common systemic inflammatory responses, often persistent.
  • Ask about pleuritic chest pain - may indicate involvement of the pleura adjacent to the abscess.
  • Ask about weight loss and night sweats - constitutional symptoms suggesting chronic infection.
  • Ask about dysphagia or recent aspiration events - crucial for identifying aspiration as a primary cause.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Lung abscess"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - fever, tachycardia, and tachypnoea are common, indicating systemic infection.
  • Check chest auscultation - diminished breath sounds, crackles, or bronchial breath sounds over the affected area.
  • Check for digital clubbing - a sign of chronic lung suppuration, though less common in acute presentations.
  • Check for signs of poor oral hygiene - indicative of a potential source for anaerobic aspiration. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Poor oral hygiene: how does it contribute to lung abscess?" text="Poor oral hygiene"][/sc]
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Lung abscess"][/sc]
  • Haemoptysis: Suggests erosion into a blood vessel, requiring urgent assessment for potential massive haemorrhage.
  • Rapid clinical deterioration: Indicates sepsis or extensive lung involvement, necessitating immediate escalation of care.
  • Lack of improvement after 72 hours of antibiotics: Raises concern for resistant organisms, incorrect diagnosis, or complications, warranting re-evaluation and further investigation.
  • Large abscess size (>6 cm): Associated with higher risk of complications and poorer prognosis, often requiring more aggressive management.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Lung abscess"][/sc]
  • Immunosuppressed patients: May present with less pronounced inflammatory signs and symptoms, and can be caused by unusual pathogens (e.g., fungi, Nocardia). [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Immunosuppression and lung infections: what are the key considerations?" text="Immunosuppression"][/sc]
  • Older adults: Symptoms may be vague and non-specific, such as general weakness or confusion, delaying diagnosis.
  • Patients with underlying malignancy: A lung abscess may mask or be mistaken for a cavitating tumour, requiring careful differentiation.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Lung abscess"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected lung abscess: Chest X-ray (CXR) to identify cavitation and air-fluid levels.
  • Imaging - CT Chest: Computed tomography (CT) of the chest provides better anatomical detail, delineating the size and location of the abscess, and differentiating it from empyema or cavitating malignancy. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Air-fluid level on CXR: what does it indicate in lung abscess?" text="Air-fluid level on CXR"][/sc]

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Lung abscess"][/sc]
  • Necrotising pneumonia - often more diffuse infiltrates, less well-defined cavitation.
  • Empyema - typically presents as a collection within the pleural space, often without significant lung parenchymal cavitation. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Empyema vs lung abscess: what are the differentiating features?" text="Empyema"][/sc]
  • Cavitating malignancy (e.g., squamous cell carcinoma) - often has thicker, irregular walls and may be associated with other signs of malignancy.
  • Tuberculosis - can cause cavitating lesions, but typically has a more indolent course and specific microbiological findings.
  • Fungal infection (e.g., aspergilloma) - may present with cavitating lesions, particularly in immunocompromised patients.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Lung abscess"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Recognition of severe infection, escalation of care if indicated, and supportive measures including oxygen therapy and fluid management.
  • Definitive management: Prolonged course of appropriate antibiotics, typically targeting anaerobic bacteria (e.g., co-amoxiclav, clindamycin, or metronidazole in combination with a beta-lactam). Treatment duration often extends for several weeks (e.g., 4-6 weeks or until radiological resolution).
  • Adjunctive management: Percutaneous drainage or surgical resection may be considered for large abscesses, those not responding to antibiotics, or in cases of complications.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Regular clinical assessment and repeat imaging (CXR or CT) to monitor response to treatment and resolution of the abscess.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Patients should be advised on symptoms of worsening infection or complications, and when to seek urgent medical attention.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Lung abscess"][/sc]
  • Empyema: Rupture of the abscess into the pleural space, leading to a collection of pus in the pleural cavity, often requiring drainage.
  • Bronchopleural fistula: An abnormal communication between the bronchial tree and the pleural space, which can lead to persistent air leak and empyema.
  • Haemorrhage: Erosion of the abscess into a blood vessel, potentially causing significant haemoptysis.
  • Sepsis: Systemic inflammatory response to infection, which can lead to multi-organ dysfunction if not promptly managed.
  • Chronic lung disease: Persistent cavitation or fibrosis can lead to long-term respiratory impairment, particularly if treatment is delayed or inadequate.
