Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Macular degeneration (Eye)"][/sc]
  • Age-related macular degeneration (AMD) is a common, progressive condition affecting the macula, the central part of the retina responsible for sharp, detailed vision.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical features of age-related macular degeneration, differentiate between dry and wet forms, and understand the principles of management and referral pathways.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Macular degeneration (Eye)"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Macular degeneration (Eye)"][/sc]
  • Age-related macular degeneration (AMD) is the leading cause of sight loss in the UK, affecting nearly 1.5 million people (Macular Society, 2018).
  • The prevalence of late AMD in the UK population aged 50 years or more is estimated at 2.4%, increasing significantly with age to 21.2% in those over 90 years (Owen et al., 2012; Wilde et al., 2017).
  • Key risk factors include advancing age, smoking, high blood pressure, obesity, and a family history of AMD.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Macular degeneration (Eye)"][/sc]
  • Trigger: Accumulation of cellular debris and extracellular material beneath the retinal pigment epithelium (RPE) leads to the formation of [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Drusen: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="drusen" ][/sc].
  • Progression: In dry AMD, drusen accumulation and RPE dysfunction lead to gradual atrophy of the RPE and photoreceptors, known as [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Geographic atrophy: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="geographic atrophy" ][/sc]. In wet AMD, abnormal new blood vessels (choroidal neovascularisation) grow from the choroid beneath the retina.
  • Consequence: These new vessels are fragile and prone to leakage of fluid and blood, causing rapid damage to the macula and severe central vision loss.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Macular degeneration (Eye)"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Dry AMD typically progresses slowly over several years, with gradual worsening of central vision.
  • Progressive phase: The conversion from dry to wet AMD can occur at any stage, but is more likely in intermediate and advanced dry AMD. Wet AMD can manifest with a rapid decline in central vision, often over days to weeks, due to leakage from new blood vessels.
  • Late phase: Without intervention, significant and irreversible central vision loss can occur within months in wet AMD, particularly beyond a few weeks from symptom onset. Dry AMD, if progressing to geographic atrophy, also leads to severe, irreversible vision loss over many years.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Macular degeneration (Eye)"][/sc]
  • Ask about blurred or distorted central vision - this is the hallmark symptom, affecting tasks like reading and recognising faces.
  • Ask about metamorphopsia (straight lines appearing wavy or bent) - a key discriminator for wet AMD, often detected using an Amsler grid.
  • Ask about difficulty adapting to low light levels or needing brighter light for tasks - common in both dry and wet AMD due to photoreceptor dysfunction.
  • Ask about reduced intensity or brightness of colours - indicates impaired cone function in the macula.
  • Ask about a scotoma (dark or empty patch) in the central visual field - can occur in advanced dry AMD (geographic atrophy) or wet AMD due to RPE and photoreceptor loss.
  • Ask about sudden onset or rapid worsening of symptoms - highly suggestive of conversion to wet AMD, requiring urgent assessment.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Macular degeneration (Eye)"][/sc]
  • Check visual acuity - often reduced, particularly central vision. This is a primary measure of macular function.
  • Check Amsler grid - distortion or missing areas on the grid indicate metamorphopsia or scotoma, highly suggestive of macular pathology, especially wet AMD.
  • Check fundoscopy - presence of [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Drusen: what are they and what do they indicate in AMD?" text="drusen"] [/sc] (yellow deposits) is a hallmark of dry AMD. Pigmentary changes, geographic atrophy, or subretinal haemorrhage/fluid (in wet AMD) may also be observed.
  • Check vital signs - generally normal in uncomplicated AMD, but hypertension is a risk factor and should be assessed.
  • Check optical coherence tomography (OCT) - can reveal retinal oedema, subretinal fluid, haemorrhage, drusen, and choroidal neovascularisation, crucial for differentiating dry from wet AMD and monitoring progression.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Macular degeneration (Eye)"][/sc]
  • Sudden onset or rapid worsening of central vision loss: Implies conversion to wet AMD, which requires urgent ophthalmological assessment within days to weeks to preserve vision.
  • New or acutely worsening metamorphopsia: Suggests active choroidal neovascularisation with fluid or blood leakage, necessitating prompt referral for potential anti-VEGF treatment.
  • New or rapidly expanding central scotoma: Indicates acute macular damage, often due to wet AMD, requiring immediate evaluation.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Macular degeneration (Eye)"][/sc]
  • Juvenile Macular Dystrophies: While AMD is age-related, inherited macular dystrophies (e.g., Stargardt disease) can present in childhood or young adulthood with similar central vision loss, but with distinct genetic causes and progression patterns.
  • Earlier onset AMD: Although typically affecting those over 50, individuals with strong genetic predispositions or significant risk factors (e.g., heavy smoking) may develop AMD at a younger age, necessitating earlier screening.
  • AMD in pregnancy: While pregnancy does not typically cause AMD, pre-existing AMD may be influenced by physiological changes, such as fluid retention or altered vascular dynamics, potentially affecting symptoms or progression, though direct evidence of atypical presentation is limited.
  • Rapid progression in dry AMD: Although dry AMD is usually slow, some cases can progress more rapidly to geographic atrophy, leading to quicker vision decline than typically expected.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Macular degeneration (Eye)"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected macular degeneration:
    • Dilated fundoscopy: To visualise the macula for drusen, pigmentary changes, geographic atrophy, or signs of choroidal neovascularisation (e.g., subretinal fluid, haemorrhage).
    • Optical Coherence Tomography (OCT): Essential for detailed cross-sectional imaging of the retina, identifying fluid, drusen, RPE detachment, and choroidal neovascularisation, crucial for diagnosis and monitoring.
    • Amsler grid: A simple self-monitoring tool to detect metamorphopsia or scotomas, particularly useful for early detection of wet AMD conversion.
  • Severity/risk tools: The AREDS Simplified Severity Scale can be used to stratify the 5-year risk of progression to advanced AMD. AREDS Simplified Severity Scale on MDCalc
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Fluorescein angiography (FA) or OCT angiography (OCTA) may be used to confirm choroidal neovascularisation in wet AMD or to differentiate from other macular pathologies.
  • Serial reassessment: Regular follow-up with visual acuity testing, Amsler grid, and OCT is vital to monitor disease progression and detect conversion to wet AMD.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Macular degeneration (Eye)"][/sc]
  • Diabetic Macular Oedema (DMO) - distinguished by a history of diabetes mellitus and characteristic retinal changes (e.g., microaneurysms, haemorrhages, hard exudates) on fundoscopy and OCT.
  • Retinal Vein Occlusion (RVO) - presents with sudden, painless vision loss, often with characteristic flame-shaped haemorrhages, cotton wool spots, and dilated tortuous veins in a specific retinal quadrant.
  • Macular Hole - causes central vision loss and metamorphopsia, but OCT reveals a full-thickness defect in the fovea.
  • Central Serous Chorioretinopathy (CSCR) - typically affects younger to middle-aged individuals, often associated with stress or corticosteroid use, and characterised by serous detachment of the neurosensory retina and/or RPE on OCT.
  • Epiretinal Membrane (ERM) - causes distorted vision and metamorphopsia, but OCT shows a thin, fibrous membrane on the inner retinal surface, often with associated retinal folds.
  • Optic Neuropathy - vision loss is typically associated with optic disc abnormalities (e.g., pallor, swelling) and visual field defects that respect the vertical midline, unlike macular conditions.
  • Inherited Macular Dystrophies - present at a younger age, often with a family history, and specific genetic testing can confirm the diagnosis. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Stargardt disease: what is it and how does it differ from AMD?" text="Stargardt disease"] is an example.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Macular degeneration (Eye)"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Recognition of symptoms, particularly sudden changes suggestive of wet AMD, and prompt referral to ophthalmology for urgent assessment and management to preserve vision.
  • Definitive management:
    • Dry AMD: Currently, there is no specific medical treatment for dry AMD. Management focuses on lifestyle modifications (e.g., smoking cessation, healthy diet), nutritional supplements (AREDS2 formulation) for intermediate to advanced dry AMD to slow progression, and low vision aids.
    • Wet AMD: The primary treatment involves regular intravitreal injections of anti-vascular endothelial growth factor (anti-VEGF) agents to inhibit abnormal blood vessel growth and leakage.
  • Adjunctive management: Low vision services and rehabilitation are crucial for both dry and wet AMD to help individuals maximise their remaining vision and maintain independence.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Regular ophthalmological review, including OCT imaging, is essential to monitor disease activity, particularly in wet AMD, and to detect progression or recurrence.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Patients should be educated on self-monitoring using an Amsler grid and advised to seek urgent review for any sudden worsening of vision or new visual distortions.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Macular degeneration (Eye)"][/sc]
  • Severe, irreversible central vision loss: This is the most significant complication, particularly in advanced wet AMD or geographic atrophy, leading to profound impact on daily activities like reading, driving, and facial recognition.
  • Depression and social isolation: The psychological impact of significant vision loss can lead to mood disorders and reduced social engagement, highlighting the importance of holistic patient support.
  • Falls and injuries: Impaired central vision increases the risk of falls and other injuries, especially in older adults, due to reduced ability to perceive obstacles and navigate environments safely.
  • Progression to wet AMD: In individuals with dry AMD, the development of choroidal neovascularisation (wet AMD) is a critical complication that can lead to rapid and severe vision loss if not promptly treated.
  • Geographic atrophy: The advanced form of dry AMD, characterised by sharply demarcated areas of RPE and photoreceptor loss, results in irreversible blind spots in the central vision that expand over time.
