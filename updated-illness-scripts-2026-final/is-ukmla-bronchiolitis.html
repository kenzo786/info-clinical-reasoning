Bronchiolitis [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Bronchiolitis"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Bronchiolitis"][/sc]
  • A common acute viral lower respiratory tract infection primarily affecting infants under two years of age, characterised by inflammation of the small airways.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical presentation of bronchiolitis, differentiate it from other respiratory illnesses, and understand appropriate management strategies, including when to escalate care.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Bronchiolitis"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Bronchiolitis"][/sc]
  • Bronchiolitis is the most common cause of lower respiratory tract infection in infants, with around 1 in 3 infants developing the condition in their first year of life in the UK.
  • Peak incidence occurs between 3 and 6 months of age, with seasonal outbreaks typically from October to March.
  • Risk factors include prematurity, chronic lung disease, congenital heart disease, immunodeficiency, and exposure to tobacco smoke.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Bronchiolitis"][/sc]
  • Trigger: Viral infection, most commonly Respiratory Syncytial Virus (RSV), leads to direct damage to the respiratory epithelial cells in the small airways.
  • Progression: Inflammation, oedema, and increased mucus production cause narrowing and obstruction of the bronchioles, leading to air trapping and atelectasis.
  • Consequence: Impaired gas exchange results in tachypnoea, increased work of breathing, and potential hypoxaemia, particularly in vulnerable infants.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Bronchiolitis"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Initial upper respiratory tract symptoms (rhinorrhoea, cough) lasting for a few days, followed by the onset of lower respiratory tract signs.
  • Progressive phase: Respiratory symptoms typically worsen over 3-5 days, characterised by increased work of breathing, wheeze, and crackles, often requiring supportive care.
  • Late phase: Symptoms gradually improve over 7-10 days, though a cough may persist for several weeks, particularly beyond two weeks.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Bronchiolitis"][/sc]
  • Ask about coryzal symptoms - often precedes respiratory distress, indicating a viral prodrome.
  • Ask about cough - typically persistent and may be paroxysmal, reflecting airway irritation and mucus.
  • Ask about difficulty feeding - reduced oral intake due to tachypnoea and increased work of breathing, a key indicator of severity.
  • Ask about fever - usually low-grade, but high fever may suggest bacterial co-infection or an alternative diagnosis.
  • Ask about irritability or lethargy - signs of increasing respiratory distress or hypoxaemia, warranting urgent assessment.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Bronchiolitis"][/sc]
  • Check respiratory rate and effort - tachypnoea, nasal flaring, subcostal/intercostal recession, and grunting indicate increased work of breathing.
  • Check auscultation - widespread fine inspiratory crackles and expiratory wheeze are characteristic findings.
  • Check oxygen saturation - hypoxaemia is a key indicator of severity and need for oxygen therapy.
  • Check hydration status - assess for signs of dehydration due to reduced oral intake and increased insensible losses.
  • Check vital signs - assess heart rate, temperature, and capillary refill time to evaluate for systemic compromise.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Bronchiolitis"][/sc]
  • Apnoea: Particularly in infants under 2-3 months, indicates severe respiratory compromise and requires immediate medical attention.
  • Persistent hypoxaemia: Oxygen saturation consistently below 92% in air, suggesting significant gas exchange impairment.
  • Severe respiratory distress: Marked tachypnoea, severe recession, and grunting, indicating impending respiratory failure.
  • Inability to maintain hydration: Reduced oral intake leading to clinical signs of dehydration, necessitating intravenous fluids.
  • Cyanosis: A late and serious sign of hypoxaemia, requiring urgent intervention.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Bronchiolitis"][/sc]
  • Premature infants: May present with apnoea as the primary symptom rather than overt respiratory distress, due to immature respiratory control.
  • Infants with underlying cardiac or respiratory disease: Can experience more severe and prolonged illness, with a higher risk of respiratory failure and hospitalisation.
  • Older infants (6-12 months): May have a milder course, sometimes resembling viral-induced wheeze, but still require careful assessment for severity.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Bronchiolitis"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected Bronchiolitis: Clinical diagnosis based on history and examination; no routine laboratory tests are recommended.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Consider a full blood count and C-reactive protein to rule out bacterial infection, especially if high fever or focal signs are present.
  • Imaging - Chest X-ray: Not routinely recommended for typical bronchiolitis; consider if severe or atypical presentation, or to rule out complications like pneumonia or pneumothorax.
  • Serial reassessment: Regular monitoring of respiratory rate, oxygen saturation, work of breathing, and feeding tolerance is crucial to assess progression and response to supportive care.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Bronchiolitis"][/sc]
  • A 4-month-old male infant presents with a 3-day history of coryzal symptoms, followed by increasing cough, tachypnoea, and difficulty feeding, with widespread wheeze and crackles on auscultation.
  • An acutely unwell, febrile, tachypnoeic infant with signs of increased work of breathing and diffuse lower airway obstruction, consistent with a viral respiratory infection.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Bronchiolitis"][/sc]
  • Asthma/Viral-induced wheeze - recurrent episodes, older age, family history of atopy.
  • Pneumonia - focal chest signs, higher fever, poor response to supportive care.
  • Croup - barking cough, stridor, hoarseness, typically older infants/toddlers.
  • Foreign body aspiration - sudden onset of choking, unilateral signs, no viral prodrome.
  • Heart failure - hepatomegaly, cardiomegaly on CXR, poor feeding, failure to thrive.
  • Sepsis - more profound systemic illness, poor perfusion, lethargy, often without prominent respiratory signs initially.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Bronchiolitis"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Assess severity, ensure adequate oxygenation, and maintain hydration.
  • Definitive management: Primarily supportive care, including humidified oxygen to maintain saturations >92%, and nasogastric or intravenous fluids for hydration if oral intake is insufficient.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Close observation of respiratory effort, oxygen requirements, and feeding. Discharge criteria include stable oxygen saturations in air, adequate oral intake, and parental confidence in home care.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Advise parents on signs of worsening condition (e.g., increased work of breathing, reduced feeding, apnoea) and when to seek urgent medical review.
  • Exam pharmacology reference (UKMLA/MRCGP): No specific pharmacological treatments are routinely recommended for bronchiolitis. Bronchodilators, steroids, and antibiotics are generally not effective. No doses or frequencies are cited here — always verify against your local antimicrobial/prescribing formulary before prescribing.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Bronchiolitis"][/sc]
  • Respiratory failure: Due to severe airway obstruction and exhaustion, requiring ventilatory support.
  • Dehydration: From reduced oral intake and increased insensible losses, leading to electrolyte imbalances.
  • Apnoea: Especially in young infants, can lead to hypoxaemia and bradycardia.
  • Secondary bacterial pneumonia: A potential complication, though less common, indicated by persistent high fever or focal chest signs.
  • Recurrent wheezing: Some infants may experience post-bronchiolitis wheezing for several months or years, particularly beyond the initial illness.
