kb-accordion kb-parent"> Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Rosacea"][/sc]
  • Triggering factors (e.g., UV radiation, heat, stress, certain foods) activate neurovascular pathways and innate immune responses in genetically predisposed individuals.
  • This leads to dysregulation of the cutaneous vascular system, resulting in persistent vasodilation, increased vascular permeability, and chronic inflammation.
  • Progression involves the development of inflammatory lesions (papules, pustules) and connective tissue changes, potentially leading to permanent erythema, telangiectasia, and tissue hypertrophy (e.g., rhinophyma).
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Rosacea"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Transient flushing episodes, often triggered by specific stimuli, which resolve completely.
  • Progressive phase: Persistent facial erythema develops, with visible telangiectasia, and recurrent inflammatory papules and pustules, particularly beyond several months.
  • Late phase: Permanent skin changes such as severe telangiectasia, oedema, and potentially phymatous changes (e.g., rhinophyma) may occur over years if untreated.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Rosacea"][/sc]
  • Ask about facial flushing - often triggered by heat, spicy food, alcohol, or stress, indicating vascular instability.
  • Ask about persistent facial redness - typically central face (cheeks, nose, forehead), distinguishing it from transient blushes.
  • Ask about papules and pustules - resembling acne but without comedones, crucial for differentiating rosacea from acne vulgaris.
  • Ask about burning or stinging sensation - common with rosacea, suggesting skin barrier dysfunction and inflammation.
  • Ask about ocular symptoms - such as gritty sensation, dryness, or blepharitis, as ocular rosacea can precede or accompany cutaneous symptoms.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Rosacea"][/sc]
  • Check for persistent central facial erythema - hallmark sign, often symmetrical across the nose and cheeks.
  • Check for telangiectasia - visible small blood vessels, particularly on the nose and cheeks, indicating chronic vascular damage.
  • Check for inflammatory papules and pustules - typically dome-shaped, erythematous, and without associated comedones.
  • Check for phymatous changes - thickening of the skin, especially on the nose (rhinophyma), more common in men and severe cases.
  • Check vital signs - usually normal, but rule out systemic causes of flushing like carcinoid syndrome or phaeochromocytoma.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Rosacea"][/sc]
  • Sudden onset of severe facial redness with systemic symptoms: May indicate an acute allergic reaction, cellulitis, or a systemic inflammatory condition requiring urgent assessment.
  • Unilateral facial swelling or pain: Suggests an alternative diagnosis such as infection (e.g., erysipelas) or a deeper inflammatory process.
  • Lesions extending beyond the face and neck: Rosacea is typically confined to the face; widespread lesions warrant investigation for other dermatological or systemic diseases.
  • Rapid progression of ocular symptoms with vision changes: Ocular rosacea can lead to corneal complications; urgent ophthalmological review is needed to prevent vision loss.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Rosacea"][/sc]
  • Older adults: May present with less prominent flushing but more persistent erythema and telangiectasia, sometimes with significant phymatous changes.
  • Darker skin types: Erythema may be less obvious, presenting instead with dusky brown or violaceous discolouration, papules, and pustules, leading to delayed diagnosis.
  • Ocular rosacea without cutaneous involvement: Patients may present solely with eye symptoms like conjunctivitis, blepharitis, or keratitis, making diagnosis challenging without a high index of suspicion.
  • Steroid-induced rosacea: Prolonged use of topical corticosteroids on the face can induce a rosacea-like eruption, often with more uniform erythema and papules, and a history of steroid use is key.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Rosacea"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected Rosacea: Diagnosis is primarily clinical, based on characteristic signs and symptoms. No specific diagnostic tests are routinely required.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Skin biopsy may be considered in atypical cases to rule out other conditions like lupus erythematosus or sarcoidosis.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Blood tests (e.g., FBC, ESR, CRP, ANA) are generally not indicated for rosacea but may be used to exclude systemic inflammatory conditions or autoimmune diseases if the presentation is unusual.
  • Imaging - Ophthalmic examination: If ocular symptoms are present, referral to ophthalmology for slit-lamp examination is crucial to assess for corneal involvement.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Rosacea"][/sc]
  • A 45-year-old woman presents with a 6-month history of recurrent facial flushing, persistent redness on her cheeks and nose, and small red bumps that sometimes contain pus, exacerbated by hot drinks and stress.
  • A middle-aged female with chronic, relapsing-remitting facial erythema and inflammatory papulopustules, triggered by environmental factors, suggestive of a primary dermatological condition.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Rosacea"][/sc]
  • Acne vulgaris - presence of comedones (blackheads/whiteheads) and typical distribution in adolescents.
  • Seborrhoeic dermatitis - greasy scales, involvement of nasolabial folds, eyebrows, and scalp.
  • Systemic lupus erythematosus (SLE) - malar rash typically spares the nasolabial folds, often associated with systemic symptoms and positive autoantibodies.
  • Contact dermatitis - history of exposure to an irritant or allergen, often with itching and well-demarcated lesions.
  • Perioral dermatitis - papules and pustules concentrated around the mouth, nose, and eyes, often with a clear zone around the vermilion border.
  • Carcinoid syndrome - episodic flushing, often with diarrhoea and bronchospasm, typically associated with neuroendocrine tumours.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Rosacea"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Patient education on trigger avoidance (e.g., sun protection, avoiding hot/spicy foods, alcohol, stress) and gentle skincare.
  • Definitive management: Topical treatments such as metronidazole, azelaic acid, or ivermectin are often considered for mild to moderate papulopustular rosacea. Oral antibiotics (e.g., doxycycline) may be used for more severe inflammatory lesions.
  • Definitive management: For persistent erythema, topical brimonidine or oxymetazoline may be used. Vascular laser therapy is an option for prominent telangiectasia.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Regular review to assess treatment response, manage side effects, and reinforce trigger avoidance.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Refer to dermatology for severe or refractory cases, diagnostic uncertainty, or significant phymatous changes. Urgent ophthalmology referral for severe ocular symptoms.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Rosacea"][/sc]
  • Ocular rosacea: Can lead to chronic blepharitis, conjunctivitis, keratitis, and potentially vision impairment if corneal involvement is severe and untreated.
  • Rhinophyma: Progressive thickening and enlargement of the nose, primarily in men, causing disfigurement and psychological distress, typically occurring in the late phase.
  • Psychological impact: Chronic visible facial lesions can lead to significant anxiety, depression, reduced self-esteem, and impaired quality of life.
  • Persistent facial oedema: Chronic inflammation can lead to lymphatic dysfunction and persistent swelling, particularly on the forehead and cheeks.
