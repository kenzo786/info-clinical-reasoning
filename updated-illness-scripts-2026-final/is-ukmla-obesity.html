Obesity [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Obesity"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Obesity"][/sc]
  • A chronic, relapsing disease characterised by excessive or abnormal fat accumulation that presents a risk to health, typically defined by a Body Mass Index (BMI) of 30 kg/m² or greater.
  • UKMLA Learning Outcome: Understand the definition, classification, and health consequences of obesity, including its impact on various organ systems.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Obesity"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Obesity"][/sc]
  • In the UK, approximately 26% of adults are living with obesity, with a higher prevalence in more deprived areas.
  • The prevalence of obesity has steadily increased over the past three decades, posing a significant public health challenge.
  • Risk factors include genetic predisposition, sedentary lifestyle, high-calorie diet, socioeconomic status, and certain medications.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Obesity"][/sc]
  • Triggered by a sustained positive energy balance, where caloric intake exceeds energy expenditure, leading to adipose tissue expansion.
  • Progression involves complex neurohormonal dysregulation, including leptin resistance and altered ghrelin secretion, which disrupt satiety and appetite control.
  • Consequence is a state of chronic low-grade inflammation, insulin resistance, and increased mechanical stress on joints and organs, contributing to multi-system complications.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Obesity"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Gradual weight gain over months to years, often unnoticed or attributed to lifestyle changes, with minimal immediate health impact.
  • Progressive phase: Sustained weight gain leading to overt obesity, with increasing risk of metabolic dysfunction, cardiovascular strain, and musculoskeletal issues.
  • Late phase: Established obesity with multiple co-morbidities, such as type 2 diabetes, hypertension, dyslipidaemia, obstructive sleep apnoea, and osteoarthritis, requiring complex multi-disciplinary management.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Obesity"][/sc]
  • Ask about fatigue - may indicate obstructive sleep apnoea or metabolic burden.
  • Ask about breathlessness on exertion - may suggest reduced exercise tolerance or cardiac strain.
  • Ask about joint pain - particularly in weight-bearing joints like knees and hips, indicating osteoarthritis.
  • Ask about increased thirst and urination - may be symptoms of developing type 2 diabetes.
  • Ask about heartburn or reflux - often associated with increased intra-abdominal pressure.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Obesity"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - blood pressure may be elevated (hypertension), heart rate may be increased.
  • Check Body Mass Index (BMI) - calculated from weight (kg) / height (m)², a key diagnostic criterion.
  • Check waist circumference - an indicator of central adiposity and metabolic risk.
  • Check for acanthosis nigricans - darkened, thickened skin, often in skin folds, suggesting insulin resistance.
  • Check for oedema - particularly in lower limbs, may indicate venous insufficiency or cardiac compromise.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Obesity"][/sc]
  • Rapid unexplained weight gain: May suggest an underlying endocrine disorder (e.g., Cushing's syndrome) or medication side effect rather than primary obesity.
  • New onset or worsening breathlessness at rest: Could indicate acute cardiac decompensation or severe obstructive sleep apnoea.
  • Sudden onset severe abdominal pain: May point to acute pancreatitis or gallstone disease, which are more common in obesity.
  • Neurological symptoms (e.g., headache, visual changes): Consider idiopathic intracranial hypertension, a known complication of obesity.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Obesity"][/sc]
  • Elderly population: Weight loss or stable weight may mask underlying sarcopenic obesity, where muscle mass is lost but fat mass remains high, increasing frailty.
  • Children and adolescents: Presentation may involve psychosocial issues, early onset puberty, or orthopaedic complications like slipped capital femoral epiphysis.
  • Individuals with certain genetic syndromes (e.g., Prader-Willi syndrome): Present with severe hyperphagia and early-onset extreme obesity, requiring specialised management.
  • Patients on specific medications (e.g., antipsychotics, corticosteroids): May experience significant weight gain as a side effect, necessitating careful medication review.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Obesity"][/sc]
  • Core investigations:
    • Full blood count (FBC) - to assess for anaemia.
    • Urea and electrolytes (U&Es) - to assess renal function.
    • Liver function tests (LFTs) - to screen for non-alcoholic fatty liver disease (NAFLD).
    • Fasting glucose and HbA1c - to screen for pre-diabetes or type 2 diabetes.
    • Lipid profile - to assess for dyslipidaemia.
    • Thyroid function tests (TFTs) - to exclude hypothyroidism as a secondary cause of weight gain.
  • Imaging:
    • Abdominal ultrasound - if NAFLD is suspected.
    • Echocardiogram - if cardiac dysfunction is suspected.
  • If alternative diagnosis suspected:
    • Cortisol levels (e.g., overnight dexamethasone suppression test) - if Cushing's syndrome is suspected.
  • Serial reassessment:
    • Regular monitoring of BMI, waist circumference, blood pressure, and metabolic parameters to track progress and manage co-morbidities.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Obesity"][/sc]
  • A middle-aged individual presents with a long history of increasing weight, now experiencing breathlessness on exertion and knee pain, with a BMI of 35 kg/m².
  • This represents a chronic, multi-factorial metabolic disorder, characterised by excessive adiposity, leading to significant cardiovascular and musculoskeletal morbidity.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Obesity"][/sc]
  • Hypothyroidism: Often presents with weight gain, fatigue, and cold intolerance, but typically has other systemic features like bradycardia and dry skin.
  • Cushing's Syndrome: Characterised by central obesity, but also includes moon facies, buffalo hump, striae, and proximal muscle weakness.
  • Polycystic Ovary Syndrome (PCOS): Common in women with obesity, but also features hirsutism, irregular menses, and infertility.
  • Medication-induced weight gain: Certain drugs (e.g., antipsychotics, corticosteroids, some antidepressants) can cause significant weight gain, which should be considered in the history.
  • Fluid retention (e.g., heart failure, renal failure): Can cause weight gain, but typically associated with significant oedema and other signs of organ dysfunction.
  • Genetic syndromes (e.g., Prader-Willi): Rare, but present with characteristic dysmorphic features and severe hyperphagia from early childhood.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Obesity"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities:
    • Address acute complications such as severe obstructive sleep apnoea or uncontrolled hypertension.
    • Initiate lifestyle modifications including dietary changes and increased physical activity.
  • Definitive management:
    • Structured weight management programmes, often involving dietary advice, exercise prescription, and behavioural therapy.
    • Pharmacological interventions: Consider medications such as orlistat or liraglutide for eligible patients, in conjunction with lifestyle changes. No doses or frequencies are cited here — always verify against your local antimicrobial/prescribing formulary before prescribing.
    • Bariatric surgery: May be an option for individuals with severe obesity (BMI >40 kg/m² or >35 kg/m² with significant co-morbidities) where other interventions have been unsuccessful.
  • Monitoring and follow-up:
    • Regular review of weight, BMI, waist circumference, blood pressure, and metabolic parameters.
    • Screening for and management of co-morbidities (e.g., type 2 diabetes, dyslipidaemia, hypertension).
  • Escalation and safety-netting:
    • Referral to specialist obesity services for complex cases or those requiring bariatric surgery assessment.
    • Educate patients on symptoms of complications and when to seek urgent medical attention.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Obesity"][/sc]
  • Type 2 Diabetes Mellitus: Insulin resistance and pancreatic beta-cell dysfunction due to chronic metabolic stress.
  • Cardiovascular Disease: Hypertension, dyslipidaemia, coronary artery disease, and heart failure due to increased cardiac workload and systemic inflammation.
  • Non-alcoholic Fatty Liver Disease (NAFLD): Hepatic steatosis and inflammation, potentially progressing to cirrhosis and liver failure.
  • Obstructive Sleep Apnoea (OSA): Upper airway obstruction due to increased soft tissue in the neck and pharynx, leading to nocturnal hypoxia and daytime somnolence.
  • Osteoarthritis: Increased mechanical stress on weight-bearing joints, leading to cartilage degradation and chronic pain.
  • Certain Cancers: Increased risk of colorectal, breast (post-menopausal), endometrial, kidney, and oesophageal adenocarcinomas due to chronic inflammation and hormonal changes.
