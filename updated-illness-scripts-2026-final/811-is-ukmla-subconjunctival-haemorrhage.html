Subconjunctival haemorrhage (Eye) [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Subconjunctival haemorrhage (Eye)"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Subconjunctival haemorrhage (Eye)"][/sc]
  • A subconjunctival haemorrhage is a benign, self-limiting extravasation of blood beneath the conjunctiva, often presenting as a bright red patch on the sclera.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise subconjunctival haemorrhage, differentiate it from more serious causes of red eye, and identify potential underlying systemic causes such as hypertension or coagulopathy.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Subconjunctival haemorrhage (Eye)"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Subconjunctival haemorrhage (Eye)"][/sc]
  • Common, with an estimated incidence of 1.9% in general practice annually, increasing with age.
  • Risk factors include Valsalva manoeuvres (e.g., coughing, sneezing, straining), minor trauma, eye rubbing, hypertension, diabetes, and anticoagulant use.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Subconjunctival haemorrhage (Eye)"][/sc]
  • Increased venous pressure, often due to a [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Valsalva manoeuvre: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Valsalva manoeuvre"], causes rupture of small, fragile conjunctival or episcleral capillaries.
  • Blood extravasates into the potential space between the conjunctiva and the sclera, becoming visible as a bright red patch.
  • The blood is typically reabsorbed over 1-2 weeks as part of the body\'s natural healing process, similar to a bruise.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Subconjunctival haemorrhage (Eye)"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Sudden onset of a bright red patch on the white of the eye, often noticed incidentally or by others, typically asymptomatic.
  • Progressive phase: The haemorrhage may initially appear to spread or enlarge slightly within the first 24-48 hours as blood disperses under the conjunctiva.
  • Late phase: Gradual resolution over 1-2 weeks, with the colour changing from bright red to orange/yellow before complete disappearance.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Subconjunctival haemorrhage (Eye)"][/sc]
  • Ask about painless red eye - to distinguish from painful causes of red eye (e.g., acute glaucoma, scleritis).
  • Ask about sudden onset - typically noticed upon waking or after a specific event like coughing/straining.
  • Ask about visual acuity changes - vision should be unaffected, helping to rule out corneal or intraocular pathology.
  • Ask about history of trauma or eye rubbing - to identify potential precipitating factors.
  • Ask about associated symptoms like headache, nausea, or photophobia - absence suggests a benign cause.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Subconjunctival haemorrhage (Eye)"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - particularly blood pressure, as [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Hypertension: how does it relate to subconjunctival haemorrhage and why does it matter clinically?" text="hypertension"] is a common association and risk factor.
  • Check a well-demarcated, bright red patch under the conjunctiva - typically unilateral, not extending to the limbus, and without corneal involvement.
  • Check for normal pupillary reflexes and visual acuity - indicating no compromise to deeper ocular structures.
  • Check for absence of discharge, foreign body, or other signs of inflammation - to differentiate from conjunctivitis or keratitis.
  • Check for signs of systemic bleeding (e.g., bruising, petechiae) - especially if recurrent or bilateral, raising concern for coagulopathy.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Subconjunctival haemorrhage (Eye)"][/sc]
  • Significant eye pain or visual disturbance: Suggests a more serious underlying ocular pathology, such as acute angle-closure glaucoma or corneal abrasion, requiring urgent ophthalmological review.
  • Recurrent or bilateral haemorrhages without clear cause: Raises suspicion for an underlying bleeding disorder, uncontrolled hypertension, or systemic vasculitis, warranting further investigation.
  • Associated signs of systemic bleeding (e.g., new bruising, epistaxis): Indicates a potential coagulopathy or platelet dysfunction, necessitating haematological assessment.
  • History of recent significant eye trauma: May indicate a globe rupture or other severe injury, requiring immediate ophthalmological evaluation.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Subconjunctival haemorrhage (Eye)"][/sc]
  • Newborns: Often occurs spontaneously during vaginal delivery due to pressure changes, usually benign and self-resolving.
  • Patients on anticoagulants: Haemorrhages may be larger, more frequent, or take longer to resolve, necessitating review of anticoagulation control.
  • Elderly patients: More prone due to fragile blood vessels and higher prevalence of hypertension, often spontaneous.
  • Patients with severe coughing/vomiting: Can present with multiple petechial haemorrhages around the eyes and face, in addition to subconjunctival haemorrhage, due to extreme Valsalva.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Subconjunctival haemorrhage (Eye)"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected subconjunctival haemorrhage:
    • Clinical examination: Assessment of visual acuity, pupillary reflexes, extraocular movements, and careful slit-lamp examination (if available) to exclude other causes of red eye.
    • Blood pressure measurement: Essential to identify or monitor [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Hypertension and subconjunctival haemorrhage: what is the link and why is it important?" text="hypertension"] as a contributing factor.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible or recurrent/bilateral:
    • Full blood count (FBC) and coagulation screen (PT/APTT): To investigate for underlying bleeding disorders or coagulopathy.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Subconjunctival haemorrhage (Eye)"][/sc]
  • Acute conjunctivitis - typically presents with diffuse redness, discharge, and often irritation/grittiness, unlike the localised, asymptomatic bleed of subconjunctival haemorrhage.
  • Acute anterior uveitis - characterised by ciliary flush (perilimbal redness), pain, photophobia, and often reduced vision, which are absent in subconjunctival haemorrhage.
  • Acute angle-closure glaucoma - presents with severe pain, blurred vision, fixed mid-dilated pupil, and a hazy cornea, distinct from the painless, clear-vision subconjunctival haemorrhage.
  • Episcleritis/Scleritis - involves inflammation of the episclera or sclera, causing localised or diffuse redness, often with tenderness and pain, which is not seen in subconjunctival haemorrhage.
  • Corneal abrasion/foreign body - usually causes significant pain, foreign body sensation, lacrimation, and photophobia, often with visible corneal defect, unlike subconjunctival haemorrhage.
  • Orbital cellulitis - presents with painful proptosis, ophthalmoplegia, and systemic signs of infection, which are absent in subconjunctival haemorrhage.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Subconjunctival haemorrhage (Eye)"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Reassurance that the condition is usually benign and self-limiting. Exclude red flag features and measure blood pressure.
  • Definitive management: No specific treatment is typically required as the haemorrhage resolves spontaneously.
  • Adjunctive management: Consider artificial tears if mild irritation is present.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Advise patients that resolution usually occurs within 1-2 weeks. If recurrent, bilateral, or associated with other bleeding, further investigation for systemic causes is warranted.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Advise to seek medical attention if pain develops, vision changes, or if new symptoms of systemic bleeding occur.
  • Exam pharmacology reference (UKMLA/MRCGP): No specific drug knowledge is commonly tested for the direct treatment of subconjunctival haemorrhage itself. Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Subconjunctival haemorrhage (Eye)"][/sc]
  • Anxiety and cosmetic concern: The dramatic appearance of a bright red eye can cause significant patient distress, though it is clinically benign.
  • Recurrence: Patients with underlying risk factors (e.g., hypertension, coagulopathy, frequent Valsalva) may experience repeated episodes, prompting further investigation.
  • Masking of more serious pathology: A large haemorrhage could potentially obscure signs of a more severe ocular injury or condition, particularly in the early phase.
  • Delayed resolution: While typically resolving within 1-2 weeks, larger haemorrhages or those in patients on anticoagulants may take longer to clear, extending the late phase.
