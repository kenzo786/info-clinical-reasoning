Gastrointestinal perforation [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Gastrointestinal perforation"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Gastrointestinal perforation"][/sc]
  • A breach in the integrity of the gastrointestinal wall, allowing luminal contents to leak into the peritoneal cavity or retroperitoneum.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical features and initial management of gastrointestinal perforation.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Gastrointestinal perforation"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Gastrointestinal perforation"][/sc]
  • The incidence of perforated peptic ulcer, a common cause, is approximately 3–10 per 100,000 population per year in the UK. Risk factors include NSAID and corticosteroid use, smoking, and H. pylori infection.
  • Other causes include diverticulitis, malignancy, inflammatory bowel disease, and iatrogenic injury during endoscopy.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Gastrointestinal perforation"][/sc]
  • Trigger: An insult such as ulceration, ischaemia, or trauma breaches the full thickness of the gastrointestinal wall.
  • Progression: Gastric acid, digestive enzymes, bacteria, and food content spill into the sterile peritoneal cavity, causing chemical and then bacterial [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Peritonitis: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="peritonitis"][/sc].
  • Consequence: A massive systemic inflammatory response (SIRS) is triggered, leading to fluid sequestration, hypovolaemia, sepsis, and potentially multi-organ dysfunction syndrome.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Gastrointestinal perforation"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Sudden, severe, constant abdominal pain begins within minutes of the perforation. Localised peritonism is present.
  • Progressive phase: Over several hours, the pain becomes generalised as peritonitis spreads. Systemic signs like tachycardia and fever develop.
  • Late phase: Without intervention, particularly beyond 12-24 hours, the patient develops septic shock and multi-organ failure, with a high mortality rate.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Gastrointestinal perforation"][/sc]
  • Ask about abdominal pain - characteristically sudden in onset, severe, and constant. It may start localised but quickly becomes generalised.
  • Ask about previous dyspepsia or NSAID use - these are significant risk factors for peptic ulcer disease, a primary cause of perforation.
  • Ask about vomiting - this is common due to peritoneal irritation and developing ileus.
  • Ask about fever or rigors - suggests a systemic inflammatory response and developing sepsis.
  • Ask about inability to pass flatus or faeces - indicates the onset of paralytic ileus.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Gastrointestinal perforation"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - tachycardia, hypotension, and fever are signs of sepsis. Tachypnoea may be present due to pain and metabolic acidosis.
  • Check for abdominal tenderness with guarding - this is a key sign of peritonitis. It is often generalised.
  • Check for percussion tenderness and rebound tenderness - these signs also point towards peritoneal inflammation.
  • Check for a rigid, "board-like" abdomen - this is a classic, late sign of established, diffuse peritonitis.
  • Check for absent bowel sounds - indicates a paralytic ileus has developed.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Gastrointestinal perforation"][/sc]
  • Sudden, severe, generalised abdominal pain: The cardinal symptom of a visceral perforation.
  • Board-like rigidity: A critical finding indicating diffuse peritonitis that requires immediate surgical referral.
  • Haemodynamic instability: Hypotension and tachycardia are ominous signs of septic shock.
  • Reduced urine output: Suggests significant hypovolaemia and the onset of acute kidney injury.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Gastrointestinal perforation"][/sc]
  • Older adults: May present with confusion, lethargy, or a fall, with less prominent abdominal pain and fever, leading to diagnostic delays.
  • Immunosuppressed patients: Patients on corticosteroids or other immunosuppressants may have a blunted inflammatory response, masking the classic signs of peritonitis.
  • Contained perforation: If the perforation is small and walled off by omentum or adjacent structures, the presentation may be subacute with localised pain and abscess formation rather than diffuse peritonitis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Gastrointestinal perforation"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected perforation: An urgent erect chest X-ray is crucial to look for pneumoperitoneum (free air under the diaphragm), which is diagnostic. Bloods should include FBC (leukocytosis), U&Es (for AKI), LFTs, amylase (to exclude pancreatitis), CRP (inflammation), and a group and save. An arterial blood gas will often show metabolic acidosis.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: An ECG and troponin may be warranted to exclude myocardial infarction if the presentation is atypical.
  • Imaging - CT scan: A CT scan of the abdomen and pelvis with intravenous contrast is the gold standard investigation. It is highly sensitive for detecting even small amounts of free air or fluid, can often identify the site of perforation, and helps to rule out other pathologies.
  • Serial reassessment: If the diagnosis is uncertain, repeated clinical examination is vital. Worsening signs or haemodynamic instability should prompt urgent escalation.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Gastrointestinal perforation"][/sc]
  • A 75-year-old woman with a history of NSAID use for osteoarthritis presents with a 4-hour history of sudden, severe, generalised abdominal pain. On examination, she is febrile, tachycardic, and has a rigid abdomen with widespread tenderness.
  • An elderly patient with risk factors for peptic ulcer disease presenting with an acute abdomen, signs of peritonism, and systemic inflammatory response, highly suspicious for a perforated viscus.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Gastrointestinal perforation"][/sc]
  • Acute pancreatitis - pain often radiates to the back; serum amylase or lipase is typically markedly elevated.
  • Ruptured abdominal aortic aneurysm - may present with back pain and haemodynamic collapse; a pulsatile mass may be felt.
  • Mesenteric ischaemia - pain is often described as "out of proportion" to the clinical signs in the early stages.
  • Acute cholecystitis - pain is typically localised to the right upper quadrant, with a positive Murphy\'s sign.
  • Myocardial infarction - an inferior MI can present with epigastric pain, but will have ECG changes and a raised troponin.
  • Appendicitis - pain typically migrates to the right iliac fossa, and signs are localised there initially.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Gastrointestinal perforation"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: This is a surgical emergency. An ABCDE approach should be adopted, with high-flow oxygen, intravenous fluid resuscitation, and insertion of a urinary catheter to monitor urine output. The patient should be made nil by mouth and an urgent surgical opinion sought.
  • Definitive management: This almost always involves emergency surgery. A laparotomy is performed to wash out the peritoneal cavity and identify and repair the source of the perforation (e.g., a Graham patch for a duodenal ulcer). In some cases, a resection of the affected bowel may be necessary.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Post-operatively, patients often require admission to a high dependency or intensive care unit for haemodynamic monitoring and support.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Any patient with suspected perforation requires immediate senior review and referral to the on-call general surgery team.
  • Exam pharmacology reference (UKMLA/MRCGP): Intravenous [sc name="jitl-medication" Q="Antibiotic use in Gastrointestinal Perforation" text="broad-spectrum antibiotics"][/sc] should be started as soon as possible to cover Gram-negative and anaerobic organisms. Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Gastrointestinal perforation"][/sc]
  • Sepsis and septic shock: The most immediate and life-threatening complication, resulting from the overwhelming systemic inflammatory response.
  • Intra-abdominal abscess: A collection of pus may form in the peritoneal cavity, which can be a persistent source of sepsis and may require radiological or surgical drainage.
  • Multi-organ failure: The septic response can lead to acute kidney injury, acute respiratory distress syndrome (ARDS), and disseminated intravascular coagulation.
  • Anastomotic leak: If a bowel resection and anastomosis is performed, this is a serious post-operative complication that can lead to further peritonitis.
  • Adhesions: The inflammatory process can lead to the formation of fibrous bands (adhesions) in the abdomen, which can cause bowel obstruction in the future.
