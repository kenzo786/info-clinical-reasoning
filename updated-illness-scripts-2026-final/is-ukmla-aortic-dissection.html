Aortic dissection [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Aortic dissection"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Aortic dissection"][/sc]
  • A life-threatening condition involving a tear in the aortic intima, allowing blood to dissect between the intimal and medial layers, forming a true and false lumen.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical features of aortic dissection, differentiate between Stanford A and B classifications, and understand the immediate management principles.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Aortic dissection"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Aortic dissection"][/sc]
  • Incidence in the UK is estimated at 2.5-3.5 cases per 100,000 person-years, with a male predominance (2-3:1).
  • Peak incidence occurs between 60-80 years of age, though it can occur at any age, particularly in those with predisposing conditions.
  • Key risk factors include uncontrolled hypertension (most common), pre-existing aortic aneurysm, bicuspid aortic valve, Marfan syndrome, Ehlers-Danlos syndrome, and cocaine use.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Aortic dissection"][/sc]
  • An intimal tear, often in the ascending aorta or near the left subclavian artery, allows pulsatile blood flow into the aortic media.
  • The force of blood creates a [sc name="jitl-query" Q="False lumen: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="false lumen"] within the aortic wall, propagating either proximally or distally, compromising blood flow to branch vessels.
  • This dissection can lead to organ ischaemia, aortic rupture, or acute aortic regurgitation, resulting in rapid haemodynamic compromise and high mortality.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Aortic dissection"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Symptoms typically present abruptly, often with maximal pain intensity at onset, evolving over minutes to a few hours.
  • Progressive phase: Dissection can extend rapidly, leading to new symptoms such as syncope, neurological deficits, or limb ischaemia, particularly beyond the initial 24-48 hours.
  • Late phase: If untreated, complications such as aortic rupture, cardiac tamponade, or irreversible organ damage can develop within days to weeks, significantly increasing mortality.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Aortic dissection"][/sc]
  • Ask about sudden onset severe tearing or ripping pain - often radiating to the back, indicating the propagation of the dissection.
  • Ask about chest pain - typically sharp and severe, often mimicking myocardial infarction but usually without pleuritic or positional features.
  • Ask about abdominal pain - may suggest mesenteric ischaemia if the dissection extends to the abdominal aorta.
  • Ask about neurological symptoms (e.g., syncope, stroke-like symptoms, paraplegia) - implies involvement of carotid, vertebral, or spinal arteries.
  • Ask about limb pain or paraesthesia - suggests ischaemia due to involvement of peripheral arteries.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Aortic dissection"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - often reveal hypertension (especially in distal dissections), hypotension (suggesting rupture or tamponade), or tachycardia.
  • Check for pulse deficit or blood pressure asymmetry - a difference of >20 mmHg between arms is highly suggestive of proximal aortic involvement.
  • Check for new aortic regurgitation murmur - indicates involvement of the aortic valve, particularly in Stanford A dissections.
  • Check for neurological deficits - focal weakness, sensory loss, or altered consciousness may point to cerebral or spinal cord ischaemia.
  • Check for signs of peripheral ischaemia - cool, pale limbs with absent pulses.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Aortic dissection"][/sc]
  • Sudden onset severe tearing chest or back pain: Indicates an acute vascular emergency requiring immediate assessment and intervention.
  • Pulse deficit or blood pressure differential: Suggests compromise of major arterial branches, often associated with Type A dissection and higher mortality.
  • New neurological deficit: Implies cerebral or spinal cord ischaemia, necessitating urgent imaging and neurosurgical consultation.
  • Signs of cardiac tamponade (e.g., muffled heart sounds, jugular venous distension, hypotension): Indicates aortic rupture into the pericardium, a rapidly fatal complication.
  • Acute limb ischaemia: Suggests critical compromise of blood flow to an extremity, requiring prompt vascular assessment.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Aortic dissection"][/sc]
  • Older adults: May present with less severe pain or non-specific symptoms like syncope, heart failure, or renal failure, leading to diagnostic delay.
  • Women: Often experience atypical pain patterns, such as abdominal pain or pain radiating to the jaw, which can be misdiagnosed.
  • Marfan syndrome/Ehlers-Danlos syndrome: Younger patients with connective tissue disorders are at higher risk and may present with dissection at an earlier age.
  • Painless dissection: A rare but critical presentation where severe pain is absent, often associated with neurological deficits or hypotension.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Aortic dissection"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected aortic dissection: Immediate CT angiography of the chest, abdomen, and pelvis is the gold standard for diagnosis, delineating the extent of dissection and identifying branch vessel involvement.
  • Severity/risk tools: The [sc name="jitl-query" Q="DeBakey classification: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="DeBakey classification"] and Stanford classification are crucial for guiding management decisions. The ADAPT score can aid in risk stratification for acute aortic syndromes. ADAPT Score for Acute Aortic Syndromes on MDCalc
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: ECG to rule out myocardial infarction, D-dimer (if low probability), and chest X-ray (may show widened mediastinum).
  • Imaging - Echocardiography: Transoesophageal echocardiography (TOE) can rapidly diagnose proximal dissections and assess aortic valve involvement, especially in unstable patients.
  • Serial reassessment: Regular clinical review and repeat imaging (e.g., CT scan) are essential to monitor dissection progression, organ perfusion, and response to treatment.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Aortic dissection"][/sc]
  • A 68-year-old male with a history of hypertension presents with sudden onset severe tearing chest pain radiating to his back, associated with a new pulse deficit in his left arm.
  • An elderly male with acute, severe, tearing chest pain and pulse asymmetry, concerning for an acute aortic syndrome, likely aortic dissection.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Aortic dissection"][/sc]
  • Myocardial Infarction - typically presents with crushing chest pain, often with ECG changes and troponin elevation, but usually without pulse deficits or BP asymmetry.
  • Pulmonary Embolism - presents with pleuritic chest pain, dyspnoea, and sometimes haemoptysis, but usually without the tearing quality or pulse deficits.
  • Oesophageal Rupture (Boerhaave Syndrome) - severe retrosternal chest pain after forceful vomiting, often with subcutaneous emphysema, but lacks vascular signs.
  • Pericarditis - sharp, pleuritic chest pain relieved by leaning forward, often with a pericardial rub, but typically less severe and without acute vascular compromise.
  • Musculoskeletal Chest Pain - localised, reproducible pain with palpation, often related to movement, lacking systemic features of aortic dissection.
  • Aortic Aneurysm Rupture - similar severe pain, but typically without a false lumen and often with more rapid haemodynamic collapse.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Aortic dissection"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Prompt recognition and urgent escalation to a specialist cardiothoracic or vascular team. Initial management often includes aggressive blood pressure and heart rate control to reduce aortic shear stress.
  • Definitive management: Stanford Type A dissections typically warrant emergency surgical repair. Stanford Type B dissections are often managed medically with strict blood pressure control, unless complicated by malperfusion, rupture, or refractory pain, which may necessitate endovascular repair (TEVAR).
  • Adjunctive management: Pain control with opioids, close monitoring in a high-dependency or intensive care setting, and management of complications such as organ ischaemia.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Regular imaging surveillance (e.g., CT scans) is crucial to monitor aortic diameter, detect aneurysm formation, and assess for re-dissection.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Patients and their families should be counselled on the signs of worsening condition and the importance of immediate medical attention.
  • Exam pharmacology reference (UKMLA/MRCGP): [sc name="jitl-medication" Q="Beta-blockers use in Aortic dissection" text="Beta-blockers"] (e.g., labetalol, esmolol) are often used to reduce heart rate and blood pressure, thereby decreasing aortic shear stress. Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Aortic dissection"][/sc]
  • Aortic rupture: A critical and often fatal complication that can occur rapidly, leading to massive haemorrhage and hypovolaemic shock.
  • Cardiac tamponade: Results from rupture into the pericardial sac, causing compression of the heart and impaired cardiac output, often developing within hours to days.
  • Acute aortic regurgitation: Occurs when the dissection extends to the aortic valve annulus, leading to severe valvular incompetence and acute heart failure.
  • Organ ischaemia: Dissection can compromise blood flow to major branch vessels, leading to stroke, renal failure, mesenteric ischaemia, or limb ischaemia, potentially developing within the first few days.
  • Spinal cord ischaemia: A rare but devastating complication resulting in paraplegia, due to involvement of spinal arteries.
  • Re-dissection or aneurysm formation: Long-term complications that necessitate ongoing surveillance and may require further intervention, particularly beyond the initial acute phase.
