Hyperprolactinaemia [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Hyperprolactinaemia"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Hyperprolactinaemia"][/sc]
  • Hyperprolactinaemia is a condition characterised by abnormally elevated levels of prolactin in the blood, outside of physiological states such as pregnancy or lactation.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the common causes and clinical manifestations of hyperprolactinaemia, including its impact on reproductive function and potential underlying pituitary pathology.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Hyperprolactinaemia"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Hyperprolactinaemia"][/sc]
  • The prevalence of hyperprolactinaemia in a UK study was reported as 0.23%, with rising incidence attributed to increased testing and drug-related causes [8].
  • Overall incidence is 3.5 times higher in women than in men, with the highest rates observed in women aged 25-44 [8].
  • Prolactinomas, benign pituitary tumours, are the most common cause, accounting for approximately 40% of all pituitary tumours [2].
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Hyperprolactinaemia"][/sc]
  • Trigger: Prolactin secretion from lactotroph cells in the anterior pituitary is primarily under tonic inhibitory control by dopamine from the hypothalamus. Disruption of this inhibitory pathway, or direct stimulation of prolactin secretion, leads to elevated prolactin levels.
  • Progression: Common mechanisms include prolactin-secreting pituitary adenomas (prolactinomas), hypothalamic-pituitary stalk compression (e.g., [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Pituitary stalk effect: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="stalk effect"][/sc]), or dopamine receptor blockade by certain medications.
  • Consequence: Chronically elevated prolactin inhibits gonadotrophin-releasing hormone (GnRH) secretion, leading to reduced luteinising hormone (LH) and follicle-stimulating hormone (FSH) release, resulting in hypogonadism and its associated clinical features.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Hyperprolactinaemia"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Symptoms such as menstrual irregularities (oligomenorrhoea, amenorrhoea) or galactorrhoea in women, and reduced libido or erectile dysfunction in men, may develop insidiously over weeks to months.
  • Progressive phase: If untreated, symptoms of hypogonadism may worsen, leading to infertility and potential bone density loss (osteoporosis) over several months to years. Tumour-related symptoms like headache or visual disturbances may also progress over this period.
  • Late phase: Long-standing, untreated hyperprolactinaemia, particularly beyond several years, can lead to significant complications including severe osteoporosis, persistent infertility, and, in the case of macroprolactinomas, irreversible visual field defects or other neurological deficits due to mass effect.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Hyperprolactinaemia"][/sc]
  • Ask about menstrual irregularities (oligomenorrhoea, amenorrhoea) - important for assessing reproductive impact and guiding investigation in women.
  • Ask about galactorrhoea (spontaneous milky nipple discharge) - a classic symptom, though not always present, and helps differentiate from other causes of menstrual dysfunction.
  • Ask about reduced libido and erectile dysfunction - key indicators of hypogonadism in men, often presenting subtly and leading to delayed diagnosis.
  • Ask about infertility - a common presenting complaint in both sexes, prompting investigation into underlying endocrine causes.
  • Ask about headaches - may indicate a pituitary macroadenoma causing mass effect, particularly if severe or persistent.
  • Ask about visual disturbances (e.g., blurred vision, visual field loss) - a critical symptom suggesting optic chiasm compression by a large pituitary tumour, requiring urgent assessment.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Hyperprolactinaemia"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - typically normal, but may reveal signs of associated conditions like hypothyroidism (e.g., bradycardia).
  • Check for galactorrhoea - inspect nipples for spontaneous or expressible milky discharge, confirming a direct effect of hyperprolactinaemia.
  • Check visual fields (e.g., by confrontation) - bitemporal hemianopia suggests optic chiasm compression by a pituitary macroadenoma.
  • Check for signs of hypogonadism - reduced secondary sexual characteristics (e.g., reduced beard growth in men, vaginal atrophy in women) indicate chronic prolactin excess.
  • Check for gynaecomastia in men - often associated with hyperprolactinaemia-induced hypogonadism. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Gynaecomastia in hyperprolactinaemia: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Gynaecomastia"] is a common sign in men with hypogonadism.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Hyperprolactinaemia"][/sc]
  • Sudden onset severe headache: May indicate pituitary apoplexy (haemorrhage into a pituitary tumour), a medical emergency requiring immediate neurosurgical assessment.
  • Acute visual loss or rapidly progressive visual field defects: Suggests acute compression of the optic chiasm by a growing pituitary tumour, necessitating urgent imaging and neuro-ophthalmological review.
  • Signs of hypopituitarism (e.g., postural hypotension, severe fatigue): Can occur with large pituitary tumours compromising other pituitary hormone axes, requiring urgent endocrine evaluation and hormone replacement.
  • Neurological deficits (e.g., cranial nerve palsies): Indicate significant tumour expansion beyond the sella turcica, requiring prompt neurological and neurosurgical assessment.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Hyperprolactinaemia"][/sc]
  • Men: Often present later with subtle symptoms like reduced libido or erectile dysfunction, leading to delayed diagnosis and potentially larger tumours (macroprolactinomas) with mass effects.
  • Postmenopausal women: May present with non-specific symptoms or solely with tumour mass effects (headache, visual disturbance) as menstrual irregularities are no longer a diagnostic clue.
  • Children and adolescents: Can present with growth failure, delayed puberty, or primary amenorrhoea, requiring careful evaluation to differentiate from other causes of pubertal delay.
  • Macroprolactinaemia: A benign condition where prolactin circulates as large, biologically inactive complexes, leading to elevated assay results but often asymptomatic presentation. Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Hyperprolactinaemia"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected hyperprolactinaemia:
    • Serum prolactin level: Initial test, ideally repeated on at least two separate occasions, at least two hours after rising and when rested, to confirm elevation and exclude transient physiological rises. Levels >5000 mU/L often indicate a prolactinoma [patient.info]. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Serum prolactin levels in hyperprolactinaemia: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Interpreting serum prolactin levels"] is crucial for diagnosis.
    • Thyroid function tests (TFTs): To exclude primary hypothyroidism, a reversible cause of hyperprolactinaemia.
    • Pregnancy test: To exclude physiological hyperprolactinaemia.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible:
    • Renal function tests: To assess for chronic kidney disease.
    • Liver function tests: To assess for liver cirrhosis.
  • Imaging - Pituitary MRI: Essential for identifying pituitary adenomas (prolactinomas) and assessing their size, location, and relationship to surrounding structures, particularly the optic chiasm.
  • Severity/risk tools:
    • Visual field assessment: Formal perimetry is indicated for macroadenomas or any visual symptoms to detect optic chiasm compression.
    • Bone mineral density (BMD) scan (DEXA): Recommended for patients with chronic hypogonadism to assess for osteoporosis risk.
  • MDCalc link: FRAX Fracture Risk Assessment Tool on MDCalc (for osteoporosis risk assessment).
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Hyperprolactinaemia"][/sc]
  • A 32-year-old woman presents with a 6-month history of irregular periods, galactorrhoea, and difficulty conceiving, with a persistently elevated serum prolactin level on repeat testing.
  • A young woman with chronic menstrual dysfunction and galactorrhoea, concerning for hyperprolactinaemia, potentially due to a prolactinoma or medication effect.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Hyperprolactinaemia"][/sc]
  • Pregnancy/Lactation: Physiological causes, excluded by history and pregnancy test.
  • Primary Hypothyroidism: Elevated TRH stimulates prolactin; distinguished by abnormal thyroid function tests.
  • Drug-induced Hyperprolactinaemia: Caused by dopamine antagonists (e.g., antipsychotics, metoclopramide); distinguished by medication history.
  • Non-functional Pituitary Adenoma: Can cause hyperprolactinaemia via [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Pituitary stalk compression: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="stalk compression"]; distinguished from prolactinoma by lower prolactin levels and lack of prolactin-secreting cells on histology.
  • Macroprolactinaemia: Biologically inactive prolactin complexes; distinguished by polyethylene glycol (PEG) precipitation test.
  • Polycystic Ovary Syndrome (PCOS): Can cause menstrual irregularities and infertility, but typically without galactorrhoea and with normal prolactin levels.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Hyperprolactinaemia"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Recognition of potential mass effect symptoms (e.g., severe headache, visual changes) warrants urgent referral to endocrinology and neurosurgery. Supportive care often includes managing symptoms of hypogonadism.
  • Definitive management:
    • Dopamine agonists (e.g., [sc name="jitl-medication" Q="Cabergoline use in Hyperprolactinaemia" text="cabergoline"], bromocriptine) are the first-line treatment for prolactinomas, effectively reducing prolactin levels and shrinking tumour size.
    • Management of drug-induced hyperprolactinaemia often involves reviewing and potentially adjusting causative medications, if clinically appropriate and safe.
    • Treatment of underlying hypothyroidism will resolve hyperprolactinaemia in those cases.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Regular monitoring of prolactin levels, symptom resolution, and pituitary imaging (MRI) is typically required to assess treatment response and detect recurrence.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Patients with macroadenomas or those unresponsive to medical therapy may warrant consideration for surgical intervention (transsphenoidal surgery) or radiotherapy.
  • Exam pharmacology reference (UKMLA/MRCGP): [sc name="jitl-medication" Q="Cabergoline use in Hyperprolactinaemia" text="Cabergoline"] is a long-acting dopamine agonist, highly effective in reducing prolactin levels and tumour size in prolactinomas, with a favourable side-effect profile compared to bromocriptine. Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Hyperprolactinaemia"][/sc]
  • Hypogonadism and Infertility: Chronic suppression of gonadotrophins leads to reduced sex hormone production, causing menstrual dysfunction, anovulation, and infertility in women, and reduced libido, erectile dysfunction, and impaired spermatogenesis in men.
  • Osteoporosis: Long-term hypogonadism, particularly beyond several years, significantly increases the risk of reduced bone mineral density and osteoporosis, leading to increased fracture risk.
  • Visual Field Defects: Pituitary macroadenomas can compress the optic chiasm, leading to characteristic bitemporal hemianopia, which can progress to irreversible vision loss if untreated.
  • Pituitary Apoplexy: A rare but serious complication involving haemorrhage or infarction of a pituitary tumour, presenting with sudden severe headache, visual loss, and signs of hypopituitarism, requiring emergency management.
  • Psychological Impact: Symptoms such as galactorrhoea, infertility, and changes in sexual function can lead to significant psychological distress, anxiety, and depression.
