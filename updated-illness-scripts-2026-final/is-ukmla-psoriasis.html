Psoriasis [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Psoriasis"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Psoriasis"][/sc]
  • A chronic, immune-mediated inflammatory skin condition characterised by well-demarcated erythematous plaques with silvery scales, often affecting extensor surfaces.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: The candidate must recognise the clinical features and common presentations of psoriasis, understanding its chronic nature and potential systemic associations.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Psoriasis"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Psoriasis"][/sc]
  • Psoriasis affects approximately 2-3% of the UK population, with prevalence varying by age and ethnicity.
  • Risk factors include genetic predisposition (e.g., HLA-Cw6), family history, obesity, smoking, alcohol consumption, and certain medications (e.g., beta-blockers, lithium).
  • Peak incidence often occurs in two age groups: 20-30 years and 50-60 years, though it can manifest at any age.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Psoriasis"][/sc]
  • Triggering factors (e.g., infection, trauma, stress) activate immune cells, leading to the release of pro-inflammatory cytokines.
  • This results in keratinocyte hyperproliferation and abnormal differentiation, forming thickened skin plaques.
  • The consequence is chronic inflammation, epidermal hyperplasia, and characteristic psoriatic lesions, often with systemic implications.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Psoriasis"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Initial appearance of small, red papules that gradually enlarge and coalesce, often in response to a specific trigger.
  • Progressive phase: Lesions expand and become more numerous, with characteristic silvery scales developing, particularly beyond a few weeks.
  • Late phase: Chronic, relapsing-remitting course with periods of flare and remission, potentially leading to widespread skin involvement and associated comorbidities over months to years.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Psoriasis"][/sc]
  • Ask about skin lesions - to understand their distribution, morphology, and evolution, which aids in diagnosis.
  • Ask about pruritus (itching) - often a significant and distressing symptom, impacting quality of life and indicating disease activity.
  • Ask about joint pain or stiffness - to screen for psoriatic arthritis, a common comorbidity requiring specific management.
  • Ask about nail changes - such as pitting, onycholysis, or discolouration, which are characteristic features and can indicate disease severity.
  • Ask about impact on daily activities and psychological well-being - to assess the overall burden of the disease and guide holistic care.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Psoriasis"][/sc]
  • Check for well-demarcated erythematous plaques with silvery scales - the hallmark lesions, often found on extensor surfaces (elbows, knees), scalp, and lower back.
  • Check for Auspitz sign - pinpoint bleeding after removal of scales, indicative of epidermal thinning over dermal papillae.
  • Check for Koebner phenomenon - new psoriatic lesions appearing at sites of skin trauma, suggesting disease activity.
  • Check nails for pitting, onycholysis, subungual hyperkeratosis, or oil drop signs - these are highly suggestive of psoriatic nail disease.
  • Check vital signs - to assess for systemic inflammation or potential complications, particularly in severe or erythrodermic psoriasis.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Psoriasis"][/sc]
  • Erythrodermic psoriasis - widespread erythema and scaling affecting >90% of body surface area, indicating severe systemic inflammation and risk of complications like hypothermia and fluid loss.
  • Generalised pustular psoriasis - sudden onset of widespread sterile pustules on erythematous skin, often with fever and systemic symptoms, requiring urgent hospital admission.
  • Rapidly worsening or extensive psoriasis - suggests uncontrolled disease, potential triggers, or inadequate treatment, necessitating prompt specialist review.
  • Severe joint involvement - progressive joint damage or significant functional impairment due to psoriatic arthritis, requiring early rheumatology assessment.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Psoriasis"][/sc]
  • Flexural/Inverse Psoriasis: Affects skin folds (axillae, groin, inframammary), presenting as smooth, erythematous patches without typical scaling due to moisture, which can be misdiagnosed as fungal infection.
  • Guttate Psoriasis: Characterised by small, drop-like lesions, often triggered by streptococcal infection, which can be mistaken for viral exanthems.
  • Psoriatic Arthritis: May precede skin lesions in a minority of cases, presenting solely with inflammatory joint pain, making diagnosis challenging without a skin history.
  • Scalp Psoriasis: Can be severe and mimic seborrhoeic dermatitis, but with thicker, more adherent scales and often extending beyond the hairline.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Psoriasis"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected Psoriasis: Clinical diagnosis based on characteristic skin lesions; no specific blood tests are diagnostic.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Skin biopsy may be considered to differentiate from other papulosquamous disorders (e.g., eczema, lichen planus).
  • Imaging - X-rays/MRI: For suspected psoriatic arthritis, to assess for joint erosions, enthesitis, or sacroiliitis.
  • Screening for comorbidities: Consider blood tests for metabolic syndrome components (lipids, glucose) and liver function tests if systemic treatments are planned.
  • Validated scores: Psoriasis Area and Severity Index (PASI) or Dermatology Life Quality Index (DLQI) are used to assess severity and impact, but are not diagnostic.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Psoriasis"][/sc]
  • A 45-year-old male presents with a 6-month history of itchy, red, scaly patches on his elbows, knees, and scalp, which have gradually worsened and are now affecting his quality of life.
  • A middle-aged man presenting with chronic, symmetrical, erythematous, and scaly plaques on extensor surfaces, consistent with a chronic inflammatory dermatosis.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Psoriasis"][/sc]
  • Eczema (Dermatitis) - often poorly demarcated, intensely itchy, and typically affects flexural surfaces.
  • Seborrhoeic Dermatitis - greasy, yellowish scales, primarily affecting sebaceous areas like the scalp, face, and chest.
  • Tinea Corporis (Ringworm) - annular lesions with active borders and central clearing, often unilateral and itchy.
  • Lichen Planus - purplish, polygonal, pruritic papules, often with Wickham's striae, affecting skin, hair, nails, and mucous membranes.
  • Drug Eruptions - sudden onset, often widespread, and related to new medication use.
  • Cutaneous T-cell Lymphoma - persistent, slowly progressive patches or plaques that may be resistant to conventional treatments.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Psoriasis"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Provide symptomatic relief for pruritus and address any acute flares or complications like erythrodermic psoriasis.
  • Definitive management: Tailored approach based on severity, location, and patient preference, often starting with topical therapies (e.g., corticosteroids, vitamin D analogues).
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Regular assessment of disease activity, treatment response, and screening for comorbidities (e.g., psoriatic arthritis, metabolic syndrome).
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Refer to dermatology for moderate-to-severe disease, inadequate response to topical therapy, or significant impact on quality of life.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Psoriasis"][/sc]
  • Psoriatic Arthritis: An inflammatory arthropathy affecting up to 30% of individuals with psoriasis, leading to joint pain, stiffness, and potential irreversible joint damage if not managed early.
  • Metabolic Syndrome: Increased risk of obesity, type 2 diabetes, hypertension, and dyslipidaemia, contributing to cardiovascular morbidity.
  • Cardiovascular Disease: Psoriasis is an independent risk factor for myocardial infarction, stroke, and peripheral vascular disease, linked to chronic systemic inflammation.
  • Psychological Impact: Significant burden of depression, anxiety, and social isolation due to visible skin lesions and chronic nature of the disease.
  • Erythrodermic Psoriasis: A severe, generalised form that can lead to hypothermia, fluid and electrolyte imbalance, and high-output cardiac failure, particularly in the progressive and late phases.
