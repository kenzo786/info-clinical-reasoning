Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Skin ulcers (including arterial, venous and neuropathic ulcers)"][/sc]
  • A skin ulcer is a break in the skin or mucous membrane, often extending into deeper tissues, resulting from a loss of epidermis and dermis. They are typically caused by underlying vascular insufficiency (arterial or venous), neuropathy, or pressure.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: The candidate must recognise the clinical features, risk factors, and initial management principles of common skin ulcers, including arterial, venous, and neuropathic types.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Skin ulcers (including arterial, venous and neuropathic ulcers)"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Skin ulcers (including arterial, venous and neuropathic ulcers)"][/sc]
  • Venous leg ulcers are the most common type, affecting approximately 1-3% of the adult population in the UK, with prevalence increasing with age (NICE).
  • Arterial ulcers account for about 10-20% of leg ulcers, often associated with peripheral arterial disease, which affects around 10% of adults over 55 in the UK (CKS).
  • Neuropathic ulcers, particularly diabetic foot ulcers, affect 15-25% of individuals with diabetes during their lifetime, a significant concern given the rising prevalence of diabetes in the UK (Diabetes UK).
  • Risk factors include advanced age, obesity, immobility, history of deep vein thrombosis, varicose veins, peripheral arterial disease, diabetes mellitus, and smoking.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Skin ulcers (including arterial, venous and neuropathic ulcers)"][/sc]
  • Trigger: Chronic underlying conditions such as venous hypertension, arterial insufficiency, or peripheral neuropathy lead to compromised tissue perfusion, oxygenation, or sensation.
  • Progression: Sustained tissue hypoxia, impaired waste product removal, or repetitive unrecognised trauma results in cellular damage, inflammation, and breakdown of the skin's protective barrier.
  • Consequence: The damaged tissue fails to heal, leading to persistent ulceration, often complicated by infection, pain, and impaired mobility.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Skin ulcers (including arterial, venous and neuropathic ulcers)"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Initial skin changes, such as erythema, oedema, or hyperpigmentation, may precede overt ulceration, particularly beyond a few weeks of underlying vascular compromise or pressure.
  • Progressive phase: Once an ulcer forms, it may expand in size and depth over several weeks to months if the underlying cause is not addressed, often becoming chronic.
  • Late phase: Chronic ulcers, particularly beyond 3 months, are prone to complications such as infection, cellulitis, and in severe cases, osteomyelitis or amputation.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Skin ulcers (including arterial, venous and neuropathic ulcers)"][/sc]
  • Ask about pain - to differentiate between arterial (often severe, worse at night, relieved by dependency) and venous (aching, relieved by elevation) or neuropathic (often painless).
  • Ask about leg swelling or heaviness - may indicate venous insufficiency and contribute to venous ulcer formation.
  • Ask about claudication or rest pain in the legs - suggests peripheral arterial disease, a precursor to arterial ulcers.
  • Ask about numbness, tingling, or loss of sensation - points towards neuropathy, a key factor in neuropathic ulcers.
  • Ask about previous history of DVT, varicose veins, or diabetes - provides crucial context for ulcer aetiology.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Skin ulcers (including arterial, venous and neuropathic ulcers)"][/sc]
  • Check ulcer location and characteristics - arterial ulcers are typically on toes/heels, punched-out, pale base; venous ulcers are usually gaiter region, irregular, shallow, granulating base; neuropathic ulcers are often on pressure points, calloused edges.
  • Check peripheral pulses (dorsalis pedis, posterior tibial) - diminished or absent pulses strongly suggest arterial insufficiency.
  • Check for signs of venous insufficiency - oedema, varicose veins, haemosiderin staining, lipodermatosclerosis.
  • Check for signs of neuropathy - reduced sensation to light touch, pinprick, vibration, or proprioception, often using a 10g monofilament.
  • Check vital signs - particularly temperature and heart rate, to assess for systemic infection or sepsis.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Skin ulcers (including arterial, venous and neuropathic ulcers)"][/sc]
  • Rapidly expanding ulcer with surrounding cellulitis - suggests aggressive infection requiring urgent antibiotic therapy and potentially debridement.
  • Systemic signs of infection (fever, rigors, confusion) - indicates potential sepsis, necessitating immediate medical review and management.
  • Severe, unremitting pain with absent pulses - highly concerning for critical limb ischaemia, requiring urgent vascular assessment.
  • Deep ulcer exposing bone or tendon - raises suspicion for osteomyelitis, requiring imaging and potentially surgical intervention.
  • New neurological deficit in the affected limb - may indicate nerve compression or severe ischaemia.
