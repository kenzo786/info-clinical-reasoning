Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Prostatitis, epididymitis and orchitis"][/sc]
  • Prostatitis is an inflammation of the prostate gland, epididymitis is inflammation of the epididymis, and orchitis is inflammation of the testis, often occurring together as epididymo-orchitis.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: The candidate must recognise the clinical features, common causes, and initial management of acute and chronic genitourinary infections in males.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Prostatitis, epididymitis and orchitis"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Prostatitis, epididymitis and orchitis"][/sc]
  • Acute prostatitis affects approximately 2-10% of men in the UK, with chronic forms being more prevalent.
  • Epididymitis and orchitis are common, particularly in sexually active men under 35 (often due to STIs) and older men with urinary tract issues.
  • Risk factors include unprotected sexual intercourse, urinary tract infections, recent urological procedures, and benign prostatic hyperplasia.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Prostatitis, epididymitis and orchitis"][/sc]
  • Trigger: Ascending infection from the urethra or reflux of infected urine into prostatic ducts or ejaculatory ducts often initiates inflammation.
  • Progression: Bacterial colonisation leads to an inflammatory response within the prostate, epididymis, or testis, causing tissue damage and swelling.
  • Consequence: Localised pain, swelling, and systemic symptoms like fever may develop, potentially leading to abscess formation or chronic pain syndromes.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Prostatitis, epididymitis and orchitis"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Acute symptoms of pain, dysuria, and fever typically develop over hours to a few days, particularly within the first 72 hours of infection.
  • Progressive phase: If untreated, symptoms may worsen over several days to a week, with increasing pain, swelling, and potential for systemic spread, particularly beyond 7 days.
  • Late phase: Resolution of acute symptoms usually occurs within 2-4 weeks with appropriate treatment; however, chronic forms can persist for months or years, particularly beyond 6 weeks.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Prostatitis, epididymitis and orchitis"][/sc]
  • Ask about perineal or suprapubic pain - indicates prostatic involvement and helps localise the discomfort.
  • Ask about scrotal pain and swelling - suggests epididymal or testicular inflammation, crucial for differentiating from other scrotal pathologies.
  • Ask about dysuria, frequency, or urgency - points towards lower urinary tract irritation, common in both prostatitis and epididymitis.
  • Ask about fever and chills - systemic symptoms that suggest a more severe infection or bacteraemia.
  • Ask about recent sexual activity and partners - essential for assessing risk of sexually transmitted infections as a cause.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Prostatitis, epididymitis and orchitis"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - fever, tachycardia, or hypotension may indicate sepsis, requiring urgent management.
  • Check for a tender, boggy prostate on digital rectal examination - characteristic of acute prostatitis, though caution is needed.
  • Check for unilateral scrotal swelling, erythema, and tenderness - typical findings in epididymitis or orchitis.
  • Check for a positive Prehn's sign (relief of pain with scrotal elevation) - may help differentiate epididymitis from testicular torsion.
  • Check for urethral discharge - suggests a sexually transmitted infection, which is a common cause.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Prostatitis, epididymitis and orchitis"][/sc]
  • Sepsis: High fever, rigors, hypotension, or altered mental status indicate severe infection and require immediate hospital admission.
  • Testicular torsion: Sudden onset severe scrotal pain, absent cremasteric reflex, and high-riding testis require urgent surgical exploration to preserve testicular viability.
  • Urinary retention: Inability to pass urine, particularly in acute prostatitis, necessitates catheterisation.
  • Perineal abscess: Fluctuant, tender mass in the perineum suggests abscess formation, requiring surgical drainage.
  • Immunocompromised patient: Increased risk of severe or atypical infections, requiring a lower threshold for aggressive investigation and management.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Prostatitis, epididymitis and orchitis"][/sc]
  • Older adults: May present with non-specific symptoms like malaise, confusion, or falls, rather than classic genitourinary pain, due to altered inflammatory response.
  • Immunosuppressed patients: Can have fulminant infections with minimal localising symptoms, or infections with unusual pathogens, necessitating broad-spectrum antibiotics.
  • Chronic prostatitis: Presents with persistent pelvic pain, urinary symptoms, and ejaculatory pain without acute systemic signs, often a diagnostic challenge.
  • Pre-pubertal boys: Epididymitis/orchitis is rare and often non-infectious (e.g., trauma, torsion of appendix testis), requiring careful differentiation from testicular torsion.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Prostatitis, epididymitis and orchitis"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected genitourinary infection: Urine dipstick and midstream urine (MSU) for culture and sensitivity to identify bacterial pathogens and guide antibiotic choice.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Full blood count (leukocytosis), C-reactive protein (inflammation), and sexually transmitted infection (STI) screen (gonorrhoea, chlamydia) in sexually active individuals.
  • Imaging - Ultrasound: Scrotal ultrasound to differentiate epididymitis/orchitis from testicular torsion, hydrocele, or tumour; transrectal ultrasound (TRUS) for prostatic abscess if suspected.
  • Serial reassessment: Clinical review to monitor response to treatment and ensure resolution of symptoms, particularly if initial management is conservative.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Prostatitis, epididymitis and orchitis"][/sc]
  • A 32-year-old male presents with a 3-day history of gradually worsening left scrotal pain, swelling, and dysuria, accompanied by a low-grade fever.
  • A young, sexually active male with acute, unilateral, inflammatory scrotal pain and lower urinary tract symptoms, suggesting an infectious aetiology.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Prostatitis, epididymitis and orchitis"][/sc]
  • Testicular torsion - sudden onset, severe pain, absent cremasteric reflex, often in younger males.
  • Testicular tumour - painless lump, often with a heavy sensation, typically not associated with acute inflammatory signs.
  • Inguinal hernia - reducible scrotal swelling, often with a cough impulse, pain may be less acute.
  • Hydrocele - transilluminable scrotal swelling, usually painless, not associated with acute infection.
  • Kidney stones - flank pain radiating to the groin, haematuria, often without scrotal swelling or fever.
  • Urinary tract infection (UTI) - dysuria, frequency, urgency, but typically without significant scrotal or perineal pain.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Prostatitis, epididymitis and orchitis"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Assess for sepsis and testicular torsion; provide adequate analgesia and anti-inflammatory agents.
  • Definitive management: Initiate appropriate empiric antibiotics covering common pathogens (e.g., enterobacteriaceae, STIs) based on local guidelines and patient risk factors.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Advise rest, scrotal support, and review in 2-3 days to assess response to antibiotics and exclude alternative diagnoses.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Advise patient to seek urgent medical attention for worsening pain, fever, new urinary retention, or signs of sepsis.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Prostatitis, epididymitis and orchitis"][/sc]
  • Chronic pain syndrome: Persistent perineal or scrotal pain, particularly beyond 6 weeks, significantly impacting quality of life due to ongoing inflammation or neuropathic changes.
  • Abscess formation: Localised collection of pus within the prostate or epididymis, requiring surgical drainage, often due to inadequate antibiotic penetration or delayed treatment.
  • Infertility: Bilateral epididymo-orchitis can lead to obstruction of the epididymis or testicular damage, impairing sperm transport and production.
  • Sepsis: Systemic inflammatory response to infection, potentially leading to organ dysfunction and mortality, especially if infection spreads to the bloodstream.
  • Recurrent infections: Incomplete treatment or underlying predisposing factors (e.g., BPH, strictures) can lead to repeated episodes of infection.
