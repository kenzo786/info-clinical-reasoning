Panic disorder [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Panic disorder"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Panic disorder"][/sc]
  • Recurrent unexpected panic attacks, which are discrete periods of intense fear or discomfort that develop abruptly and reach a peak within minutes.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the features of panic disorder, distinguish it from other conditions with similar presentations, and outline initial management strategies.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Panic disorder"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Panic disorder"][/sc]
  • The 12-month prevalence of panic disorder with or without agoraphobia in the UK general population is approximately 1.70% (Skapinakis et al., 2011).
  • Panic attacks during the past month, a core symptom, had a prevalence of 4.42% in the UK (Skapinakis et al., 2011).
  • More common in women than men, with onset typically in late adolescence or early adulthood.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Panic disorder"][/sc]
  • Trigger: A complex interplay of genetic predisposition, neurobiological factors (e.g., dysregulation of neurotransmitter systems like serotonin and noradrenaline), and psychological vulnerabilities (e.g., anxiety sensitivity).
  • Progression: Initial panic attacks can lead to anticipatory anxiety about future attacks, often resulting in avoidance behaviours and the development of agoraphobia. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Anticipatory anxiety: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Anticipatory anxiety"][/sc]
  • Consequence: The fear of fear creates a vicious cycle, reinforcing avoidance and increasing the likelihood of further panic attacks, significantly impacting quality of life. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Panic cycle: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Panic cycle"][/sc]
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Panic disorder"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Characterised by sudden, unexpected panic attacks that typically peak within 10 minutes, followed by a period of residual anxiety.
  • Progressive phase: As attacks recur, individuals often develop anticipatory anxiety between episodes and may begin to avoid situations where previous attacks occurred, leading to agoraphobia over weeks to months.
  • Late phase: Without effective intervention, panic disorder can become chronic, with persistent anxiety, avoidance behaviours, and significant functional impairment, potentially lasting for years.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Panic disorder"][/sc]
  • Ask about palpitations, chest pain, or shortness of breath - these somatic symptoms are often mistaken for cardiac or respiratory emergencies, requiring careful discrimination.
  • Ask about feelings of choking, dizziness, or faintness - these can lead to fears of impending doom or loss of control, driving emergency presentations.
  • Ask about derealisation or depersonalisation - these dissociative symptoms are common during intense panic and can be highly distressing.
  • Ask about fear of losing control, going crazy, or dying - these cognitive symptoms are central to the experience of panic and differentiate it from generalised anxiety.
  • Ask about avoidance of specific places or situations (e.g., crowded places, public transport) - this indicates the development of agoraphobia, a common complication.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Panic disorder"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - typically normal between attacks, but during an attack, tachycardia, tachypnoea, and elevated blood pressure may be observed due to sympathetic activation.
  • Check for signs of hyperventilation - may include carpopedal spasm or perioral tingling, which can exacerbate panic symptoms. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Hyperventilation: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Hyperventilation"][/sc]
  • Check general appearance - may show signs of anxiety, restlessness, or agitation during an attack, but often unremarkable otherwise.
  • Check for signs of avoidance behaviours - individuals may appear uncomfortable or distressed in specific environments, suggesting agoraphobia.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Panic disorder"][/sc]
  • New onset cardiac symptoms: Chest pain or palpitations, particularly in individuals with cardiac risk factors, warrant investigation to exclude acute coronary syndromes or arrhythmias.
  • Neurological deficits: Sudden onset focal neurological signs (e.g., weakness, visual changes) suggest alternative diagnoses such as transient ischaemic attack or stroke, rather than panic.
  • Unexplained weight loss or fever: These systemic symptoms are not typical of panic disorder and should prompt investigation for underlying physical illness.
  • Suicidal ideation or intent: Any expression of self-harm requires immediate mental health assessment and safety planning, as panic disorder can co-occur with depression.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Panic disorder"][/sc]
  • Older adults: Panic attacks may present predominantly with somatic symptoms, leading to misdiagnosis as cardiac or respiratory conditions. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Panic disorder in older adults: how does presentation differ and why does it matter?" text="Older adults"][/sc]
  • Children and adolescents: Symptoms may be less clearly articulated, presenting as irritability, school refusal, or unexplained physical complaints.
  • Nocturnal panic attacks: Occur during sleep, waking the individual abruptly with intense fear and physical symptoms, often without an obvious trigger.
  • Limited symptom attacks: Panic attacks where fewer than four characteristic symptoms are present, still causing significant distress and impairment.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Panic disorder"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected panic disorder: Clinical history and mental state examination are paramount. Use of a structured diagnostic interview (e.g., MINI, SCID) can aid diagnosis.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Consider investigations to rule out physical causes, such as ECG for cardiac arrhythmias, thyroid function tests for hyperthyroidism, and full blood count for anaemia. Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Panic disorder"][/sc]
  • A 32-year-old female presents with a 6-month history of recurrent, unexpected episodes of intense fear, palpitations, and shortness of breath, often accompanied by a fear of dying, leading to avoidance of public places.
  • Young female with recurrent, unprovoked episodes of acute autonomic arousal and cognitive distress, concerning for panic disorder with agoraphobia.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Panic disorder"][/sc]
  • Cardiac arrhythmia (e.g., paroxysmal supraventricular tachycardia) - distinguished by objective ECG changes during episodes and often a more regular, sudden onset/offset of palpitations.
  • Hyperthyroidism - distinguished by other systemic symptoms (e.g., weight loss, heat intolerance, tremor) and abnormal thyroid function tests.
  • Phaeochromocytoma - distinguished by paroxysmal hypertension, sweating, and headache, with elevated catecholamines and metabolites.
  • Generalised anxiety disorder - distinguished by persistent, excessive worry about multiple events or activities, rather than discrete panic attacks.
  • Asthma/COPD exacerbation - distinguished by objective signs of respiratory distress, wheeze, and history of chronic respiratory disease.
  • Drug-induced anxiety (e.g., caffeine, stimulants) - distinguished by temporal relationship to substance use. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Drug-induced anxiety: what substances can cause it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Drug-induced anxiety"][/sc]
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Panic disorder"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Reassurance, de-escalation, and exclusion of life-threatening physical causes. Supportive care often includes breathing control techniques during an acute attack.
  • Definitive management: Cognitive Behavioural Therapy (CBT) is recommended as a first-line psychological intervention.
  • Adjunctive management: Pharmacological options, typically Selective Serotonin Reuptake Inhibitors (SSRIs), may be considered, often initiated at a low dose and gradually increased.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Regular review of symptoms, side effects, and functional improvement. Treatment response may take several weeks.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Individuals with severe symptoms, significant functional impairment, or co-morbid conditions may warrant referral to secondary mental health services.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Panic disorder"][/sc]
  • Agoraphobia: Develops over weeks to months, leading to significant restriction of activities and social isolation due to fear of panic attacks in certain situations.
  • Depression: A common comorbidity, often developing after prolonged experience of panic disorder, impacting overall prognosis and treatment response.
  • Substance misuse: Individuals may use alcohol or illicit drugs as a maladaptive coping mechanism to manage anxiety symptoms, leading to further health problems.
  • Functional impairment: Significant impact on occupational, social, and personal functioning, potentially leading to unemployment and relationship difficulties, persisting for years without effective intervention.
  • Increased healthcare utilisation: Frequent presentations to emergency departments or primary care due to somatic symptoms of panic attacks, often leading to unnecessary investigations.
