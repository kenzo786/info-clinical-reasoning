Meniere disease [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Meniere disease"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Meniere disease"][/sc]
  • Meniere disease is a chronic disorder of the inner ear characterised by spontaneous, recurrent episodes of vertigo, fluctuating sensorineural hearing loss, tinnitus, and aural fullness.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical features of Meniere disease and understand its episodic nature and impact on balance and hearing.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Meniere disease"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Meniere disease"][/sc]
  • Meniere disease is a rare condition, with an estimated incidence in the UK of 13.1 per 100,000 person-years [2].
  • It most commonly affects individuals between 40 and 60 years of age, with a slight female predominance [2].
  • Risk factors may include genetic susceptibility, autoimmune conditions, and migraine [1].
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Meniere disease"][/sc]
  • Trigger: The hallmark of Meniere disease is endolymphatic hydrops, an increase in the volume and pressure of endolymph within the membranous labyrinth of the inner ear [1].
  • Progression: This increased pressure leads to distension of the membranous labyrinth, which can cause rupture of the membrane, mixing of endolymph and perilymph, and depolarisation block of the vestibular and cochlear hair cells [1]. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Endolymphatic hydrops mechanism: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="endolymphatic hydrops mechanism"][/sc]
  • Consequence: The resulting dysfunction of the vestibular and cochlear systems manifests as episodic vertigo, hearing loss, and tinnitus [1].
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Meniere disease"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Characterised by sudden, unpredictable attacks of vertigo, often lasting minutes to hours (typically 2-3 hours). Hearing loss is fluctuating, and tinnitus increases. Remissions can last days to years [1].
  • Progressive phase: Vertigo attacks continue, with possible giddiness before and after episodes. Sensorineural hearing loss and tinnitus progress. Remission periods vary [1].
  • Late phase: Hearing loss becomes more pronounced and often constant. Vertigo attacks may lessen in severity or frequency, but balance difficulties, particularly in the dark, can persist. Tinnitus is often constant [1].
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Meniere disease"][/sc]
  • Ask about vertigo - feeling like you or everything around you is spinning, which is a key diagnostic criterion and often debilitating [1].
  • Ask about fluctuating hearing loss - typically low-frequency, worsening during attacks and improving between episodes, crucial for diagnosis [1].
  • Ask about tinnitus - ringing or buzzing sounds in the affected ear, often preceding or accompanying vertigo attacks [1].
  • Ask about aural fullness - a sensation of pressure or blockage in the affected ear, a common symptom that can precede other symptoms [1].
  • Ask about nausea and vomiting - often accompany severe vertigo attacks due to vestibular system overstimulation [1].
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Meniere disease"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - usually normal, but may show signs of anxiety or dehydration during acute attacks.
  • Check nystagmus - often present during acute vertigo attacks, typically horizontal or rotatory, indicating vestibular dysfunction [1]. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Nystagmus in Meniere disease: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="nystagmus"][/sc]
  • Check Romberg\'s test and gait - may reveal imbalance or ataxia during and immediately after acute episodes [1].
  • Check ear examination - usually normal, ruling out other causes of ear symptoms.
  • Check tuning fork tests (Weber\'s and Rinne\'s) - may indicate sensorineural hearing loss in the affected ear [1].
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Meniere disease"][/sc]
  • Focal neurological deficits: Suggests a central cause of vertigo (e.g., stroke, TIA) rather than Meniere disease, requiring urgent neurological assessment [1].
  • Sudden onset severe headache: May indicate intracranial pathology, such as haemorrhage, necessitating immediate investigation [1].
  • Vertigo without auditory symptoms: While Meniere disease can have atypical presentations, isolated vertigo should prompt consideration of other vestibular disorders [1].
  • Drop attacks (Tumarkin\'s otolithic crisis): Sudden falls without loss of consciousness, though rare in Meniere disease, can indicate severe vestibular dysfunction and risk of injury [1].
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Meniere disease"][/sc]
  • Lermoyez variant: Characterised by hearing loss and tinnitus preceding vertigo, with hearing improving after the vertigo attack. This reversal of typical symptoms is important for differentiation.
  • Cochlear Meniere: Presents primarily with fluctuating hearing loss and tinnitus, without significant vertigo. This variant highlights the spectrum of inner ear involvement. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Cochlear Meniere: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Cochlear Meniere"][/sc]
  • Vestibular Meniere: Presents primarily with recurrent vertigo, without significant hearing loss or tinnitus. This can make differentiation from other vestibular disorders challenging.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Meniere disease"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected Meniere disease: Clinical history and examination, including otoscopy and neurological assessment, to identify characteristic symptoms and rule out other causes [1].
  • Audiometry: Essential for documenting fluctuating low- to medium-frequency sensorineural hearing loss in the affected ear, a key diagnostic criterion [1]. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Audiometry in Meniere disease: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="audiometry"][/sc]
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Blood tests (e.g., FBC, ESR, thyroid function, syphilis screen) to exclude systemic illnesses that can mimic Meniere disease [1].
  • Imaging - MRI brain scan: Advised for unilateral cases to exclude other causes of unilateral vertigo and hearing loss, such as acoustic neuroma. Should include views of the internal auditory canal with and without contrast [1].
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Meniere disease"][/sc]
  • A 55-year-old female presents with recurrent, spontaneous episodes of severe spinning vertigo, associated with fluctuating hearing loss, tinnitus, and aural fullness in her right ear, lasting several hours.
  • Episodic vestibular dysfunction with progressive auditory symptoms, concerning for Meniere disease.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Meniere disease"][/sc]
  • Vestibular migraine: Often associated with headache and visual aura, and auditory symptoms are less prominent or absent compared to Meniere disease.
  • Benign paroxysmal positional vertigo (BPPV): Characterised by brief episodes of vertigo triggered by specific head movements, without associated hearing loss or tinnitus.
  • Labyrinthitis/Vestibular neuritis: Typically a single, prolonged episode of vertigo, often preceded by a viral illness, with or without hearing loss, but usually not recurrent.
  • Acoustic neuroma (Vestibular schwannoma): Can cause progressive unilateral hearing loss, tinnitus, and imbalance, but vertigo is usually less episodic and severe than in Meniere disease. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Acoustic neuroma vs Meniere disease: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="acoustic neuroma"][/sc]
  • Transient ischaemic attack (TIA) / Stroke: May present with sudden vertigo, but typically accompanied by other focal neurological deficits (e.g., diplopia, dysarthria, weakness).
  • Autoimmune inner ear disease: Can cause bilateral, progressive sensorineural hearing loss and vestibular symptoms, but often responds to immunosuppression.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Meniere disease"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Supportive care during acute attacks, including antiemetics for nausea/vomiting and vestibular suppressants (e.g., prochlorperazine) to alleviate vertigo.
  • Definitive management: Lifestyle modifications such as a low-salt diet and avoidance of caffeine and alcohol are often recommended to reduce symptom frequency and severity [1].
  • Adjunctive management: Betahistine may support symptom control, particularly reducing vertigo frequency and severity [1]. Vestibular rehabilitation can help improve balance and reduce dizziness between attacks.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Regular audiology assessments to monitor hearing loss progression and clinical review to assess symptom control and adjust management strategies.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Referral to ENT specialist for consideration of intratympanic injections (e.g., corticosteroids, gentamicin) or surgical interventions (e.g., endolymphatic sac decompression) in refractory cases. Patients should be advised on driving restrictions during acute episodes.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Meniere disease"][/sc]
  • Progressive hearing loss: Over time, the fluctuating hearing loss can become permanent and severe, significantly impacting quality of life and communication.
  • Chronic imbalance and falls: Persistent vestibular dysfunction, even between acute attacks, can lead to chronic imbalance, increasing the risk of falls and injury, particularly in the late phase.
  • Anxiety and depression: The unpredictable nature of attacks and the chronic symptoms can lead to significant psychological distress, including anxiety and depression.
  • Impact on daily activities: Vertigo attacks and associated symptoms can severely disrupt daily activities, including work, driving, and social engagements, leading to social isolation.
