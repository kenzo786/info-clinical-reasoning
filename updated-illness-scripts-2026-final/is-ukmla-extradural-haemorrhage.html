Extradural haemorrhage (Brain and spinal cord) [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Extradural haemorrhage (Brain and spinal cord)"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Extradural haemorrhage (Brain and spinal cord)"][/sc]
  • Extradural haemorrhage (EDH) is a collection of blood in the potential space between the dura mater and the inner surface of the skull or spinal column.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the classical presentation of extradural haemorrhage, including the lucid interval, and understand the urgency of diagnosis and surgical intervention.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Extradural haemorrhage (Brain and spinal cord)"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Extradural haemorrhage (Brain and spinal cord)"][/sc]
  • Extradural haemorrhage (EDH) occurs in approximately 2% of all head injuries, but accounts for 5-15% of fatal head injuries [1].
  • Males are affected more frequently than females, with a ratio of 4:1 [1].
  • It is less common in children due to skull plasticity and in adults over 60 due to tighter dural adherence [1].
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Extradural haemorrhage (Brain and spinal cord)"][/sc]
  • Trigger: Typically results from severe head trauma, often involving a fracture of the temporal or parietal bone, leading to laceration of the [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Middle meningeal artery: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="middle meningeal artery"] or a dural venous sinus.
  • Progression: Arterial bleeding (or less commonly venous) causes rapid accumulation of blood in the extradural space, stripping the dura from the inner table of the skull and forming a characteristic biconvex haematoma.
  • Consequence: The expanding haematoma increases intracranial pressure, leading to cerebral compression, neurological deterioration, and potentially brain herniation if not promptly managed. This often manifests as a [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Lucid interval mechanism: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="lucid interval"] followed by rapid decline.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Extradural haemorrhage (Brain and spinal cord)"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Initial loss of consciousness immediately following head trauma, often brief, followed by a period of apparent neurological improvement (the lucid interval).
  • Progressive phase: Rapid neurological deterioration typically occurring within minutes to hours after the lucid interval, characterised by worsening headache, vomiting, altered consciousness, and focal neurological deficits.
  • Late phase: If untreated, progressive mass effect and rising intracranial pressure can lead to brainstem compression, herniation, and death, particularly beyond 6-12 hours from injury onset.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Extradural haemorrhage (Brain and spinal cord)"][/sc]
  • Ask about headache - a rapidly worsening headache, particularly after a head injury, suggests increasing intracranial pressure.
  • Ask about nausea or vomiting - often associated with raised intracranial pressure and can indicate neurological deterioration.
  • Ask about altered level of consciousness - a fluctuating or deteriorating conscious level, especially after an initial recovery, is highly suggestive of an expanding intracranial lesion like an EDH.
  • Ask about a lucid interval - a period of apparent normality between an initial brief loss of consciousness and subsequent neurological decline is a classic, though not universal, feature of EDH.
  • Ask about focal neurological deficits - such as limb weakness, speech disturbance (aphasia), or visual field defects, which indicate localised brain compression.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Extradural haemorrhage (Brain and spinal cord)"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - bradycardia and hypertension (Cushing's reflex) are indicative of significantly raised intracranial pressure.
  • Check for evidence of head trauma - scalp haematomas, lacerations, or palpable skull fractures may indicate the site of injury.
  • Check Glasgow Coma Scale (GCS) - a deteriorating GCS score is a critical sign of worsening neurological status.
  • Check pupils - [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Unequal pupils in head injury: what do they indicate and why does it matter clinically?" text="unequal pupils"] (anisocoria), particularly a dilated and unreactive pupil, suggests uncal herniation.
  • Check focal neurological deficits - such as hemiparesis or hemiplegia, indicating compression of motor pathways.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Extradural haemorrhage (Brain and spinal cord)"][/sc]
  • Deteriorating GCS - indicates worsening neurological function and increasing intracranial pressure, necessitating urgent neurosurgical review.
  • Unequal or fixed/dilated pupils - suggests uncal herniation and brainstem compression, a life-threatening emergency.
  • Focal neurological deficit - new or worsening weakness, sensory loss, or speech disturbance points to progressive brain compression.
  • Seizures - can be a sign of cortical irritation due to the expanding haematoma, requiring immediate assessment and management.
  • Cushing's triad (bradycardia, hypertension, irregular respiration) - a late and ominous sign of severely raised intracranial pressure and impending brainstem failure.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Extradural haemorrhage (Brain and spinal cord)"][/sc]
  • Paediatric population: Children, especially infants, may not have an associated skull fracture due to skull plasticity, and symptoms can be non-specific, making diagnosis challenging. Posterior fossa EDH may be more common in children and can lead to very rapid deterioration [1].
  • Older adults: EDH is less common over the age of 60 due to tighter dural adherence, but when it occurs, prognosis can be poorer. Conservative management may be considered in selected cases [1].
  • Spinal extradural haemorrhage: Can occur in the spinal column, presenting with radicular symptoms or complete cord compression, including weakness, numbness, altered reflexes, and urinary/faecal incontinence [1].
  • Absence of lucid interval: The 'classical' lucid interval occurs in less than a third of cases, meaning its absence does not rule out EDH [1].
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Extradural haemorrhage (Brain and spinal cord)"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected extradural haemorrhage: Urgent computed tomography (CT) scan of the head is the gold standard for diagnosis, typically revealing a [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Biconvex hyperdense lesion on CT: what does it indicate and why does it matter clinically?" text="biconvex hyperdense lesion"] that does not cross suture lines.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Consider full blood count (FBC), urea and electrolytes (U&E), and coagulation studies if there is suspicion of a spontaneous haemorrhage or coagulopathy.
  • Imaging - CT: Provides rapid and definitive diagnosis, showing the location, size, and mass effect of the haematoma. Repeat CT may be necessary if there is neurological deterioration.
  • Imaging - MRI: Magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) offers more detailed imaging but is often not suitable for unstable patients in the acute setting.

Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Extradural haemorrhage (Brain and spinal cord)"][/sc]
  • A young male presenting after head trauma with an initial brief loss of consciousness followed by a lucid interval, now rapidly deteriorating with a worsening headache and unilateral pupillary dilation.
  • A previously healthy individual with acute neurological decline following head injury, concerning for an expanding intracranial mass lesion.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Extradural haemorrhage (Brain and spinal cord)"][/sc]
  • Acute Subdural Haematoma (ASDH) - typically results from tearing of bridging veins, often crescent-shaped on CT and crosses suture lines, with a more insidious onset of symptoms.
  • Intracerebral Haemorrhage (ICH) - bleeding within the brain parenchyma, often associated with hypertension or vascular malformations, appearing as an irregular hyperdensity on CT.
  • Subarachnoid Haemorrhage (SAH) - bleeding into the subarachnoid space, often presenting with a sudden severe headache ("thunderclap headache"), and blood visible in the basal cisterns on CT.
  • Concussion - a mild traumatic brain injury with transient neurological dysfunction, but without structural brain damage or progressive neurological deterioration.
  • Cerebral Contusion - bruising of the brain tissue, often seen in areas adjacent to bony prominences, which can cause focal neurological deficits but typically does not expand as rapidly as an EDH.
  • Ischaemic Stroke - sudden onset of focal neurological deficits due to interruption of blood supply to the brain, usually without a history of trauma and appearing as hypodensity on CT after several hours.
  • Intracranial Abscess or Tumour - can present with progressive neurological symptoms and signs of raised intracranial pressure, but typically has a slower onset and different imaging characteristics.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Extradural haemorrhage (Brain and spinal cord)"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Recognition of the condition, urgent escalation to neurosurgical services, and supportive care including airway management, breathing support, and maintenance of circulation to preserve cerebral perfusion.
  • Definitive management: Surgical evacuation of the haematoma via craniotomy is often warranted, particularly for large or rapidly expanding haematomas, or in patients with neurological deterioration.
  • Adjunctive management: Management of raised intracranial pressure may involve osmotic diuretics (e.g., intravenous mannitol or hypertonic saline) and head elevation.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Continuous neurological monitoring, repeat imaging as indicated, and close observation for complications such as seizures or delayed neurological deficits.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Any signs of neurological deterioration, such as a drop in GCS, pupillary changes, or new focal deficits, should prompt immediate re-evaluation and escalation of care.
  • Exam pharmacology reference (UKMLA/MRCGP): No specific drug knowledge is commonly tested for the definitive management of extradural haemorrhage, which is primarily surgical.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Extradural haemorrhage (Brain and spinal cord)"][/sc]
  • Brain herniation: As the haematoma expands, it can cause significant mass effect, leading to displacement of brain tissue and potentially fatal brain herniation, particularly beyond 6-12 hours if untreated.
  • Neurological deficits: Depending on the location and extent of brain compression, patients may experience temporary or permanent neurological deficits, including weakness, sensory loss, or cognitive impairment.
  • Post-traumatic seizures: Cortical irritation from the haematoma or subsequent brain injury can lead to seizures, which may develop weeks or months after the initial injury.
  • Death: Despite surgical intervention, extradural haemorrhage carries a significant mortality risk, especially in cases of rapid clinical progression, large haematoma volume, or delayed presentation.
