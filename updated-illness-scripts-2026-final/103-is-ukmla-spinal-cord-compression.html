Spinal cord compression [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Spinal cord compression"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Spinal cord compression"][/sc]
  • Spinal cord compression is a neurological emergency caused by any lesion that reduces the diameter of the spinal canal, leading to direct pressure on the spinal cord.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the signs and symptoms of spinal cord compression and initiate appropriate urgent investigation and management.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Spinal cord compression"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Spinal cord compression"][/sc]
  • Spinal cord compression affects approximately 1 in 20 patients with cancer, with metastatic disease being the most common cause in adults.
  • Risk factors include a history of malignancy (especially breast, lung, prostate, kidney, and myeloma), trauma, and degenerative spinal conditions.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Spinal cord compression"][/sc]
  • Trigger: A space-occupying lesion (e.g., tumour, disc herniation, haematoma) encroaches upon the spinal canal, directly compressing the spinal cord.
  • Progression: Mechanical compression leads to ischaemia, oedema, and demyelination of neural tissue, impairing axonal conduction.
  • Consequence: Progressive neurological deficits, including motor weakness, sensory loss, and autonomic dysfunction, can result in permanent disability if not promptly managed.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Spinal cord compression"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Often insidious onset of back pain, which may be localised or radicular, progressing over days to weeks.
  • Progressive phase: Neurological deficits such as motor weakness, sensory changes, and gait disturbance typically develop over hours to days, particularly beyond 48 hours of symptom onset.
  • Late phase: Rapid deterioration to paraplegia, bladder/bowel dysfunction, and loss of sensation can occur within hours if compression is severe and untreated.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Spinal cord compression"][/sc]
  • Ask about back pain - often the earliest symptom, may be severe, localised, and exacerbated by movement, coughing, or lying down.
  • Ask about limb weakness - typically progressive, affecting one or more limbs, and may be accompanied by clumsiness or difficulty walking.
  • Ask about sensory changes - numbness, tingling, or altered sensation in a dermatomal distribution, often ascending.
  • Ask about bladder or bowel dysfunction - urinary retention, incontinence, or faecal incontinence are late but critical signs.
  • Ask about saddle anaesthesia - loss of sensation in the perineal area, indicating cauda equina involvement.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Spinal cord compression"][/sc]
  • Check motor power - assess for asymmetrical or symmetrical weakness, often with upper motor neuron signs (e.g., spasticity, hyperreflexia, Babinski sign).
  • Check sensory level - identify a distinct level of sensory loss to light touch, pinprick, and temperature, which helps localise the lesion.
  • Check reflexes - hyperreflexia below the level of compression and absent reflexes at the level of compression are common findings.
  • Check vitals - typically normal, but may show signs of systemic illness if an underlying cause like infection or malignancy is present.
  • Check anal tone - reduced or absent anal tone is a critical sign of cauda equina syndrome, requiring urgent intervention.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Spinal cord compression"][/sc]
  • New or worsening back pain - especially if nocturnal, unrelieved by rest, or associated with a history of malignancy.
  • Progressive neurological deficit - rapidly developing motor weakness, sensory loss, or gait disturbance.
  • Bladder or bowel dysfunction - new onset urinary retention, overflow incontinence, or faecal incontinence.
  • Saddle anaesthesia - numbness or altered sensation in the perineal region.
  • Bilateral leg weakness - particularly if associated with upper motor neuron signs.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Spinal cord compression"][/sc]
  • Older adults: May present with insidious onset of gait disturbance and non-specific weakness, often attributed to degenerative changes, delaying diagnosis.
  • Early disease: Can manifest solely as radicular pain without overt motor or sensory deficits, making differentiation from simple disc prolapse challenging.
  • Cervical compression: May present with upper limb weakness and sensory changes, sometimes mimicking peripheral neuropathy or stroke.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Spinal cord compression"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected spinal cord compression: Urgent MRI of the whole spine is the gold standard, providing detailed imaging of the spinal cord, nerve roots, and surrounding structures.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Blood tests including FBC, U&Es, LFTs, inflammatory markers (ESR, CRP), and tumour markers if malignancy is suspected.
  • Imaging - CT myelography: Considered if MRI is contraindicated or unavailable, involving injection of contrast into the subarachnoid space followed by CT imaging.
  • Serial reassessment: Regular neurological examination is crucial to monitor for progression of deficits while awaiting definitive imaging or treatment.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Spinal cord compression"][/sc]
  • A 65-year-old male presents with a 3-week history of progressive bilateral leg weakness, worsening back pain, and recent onset urinary hesitancy, following a diagnosis of prostate cancer.
  • Elderly male with subacute, progressive, multifocal neurological deficits and autonomic dysfunction, in the context of known malignancy, concerning for spinal cord compression.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Spinal cord compression"][/sc]
  • Cauda Equina Syndrome - involves nerve roots below L1/L2, typically presenting with severe back pain, saddle anaesthesia, and bladder/bowel dysfunction, often without upper motor neuron signs.
  • Transverse Myelitis - acute inflammation of the spinal cord, often post-infectious or autoimmune, leading to rapid onset of motor, sensory, and autonomic dysfunction at a specific level.
  • Guillain-Barré Syndrome - ascending flaccid paralysis with areflexia, typically post-infectious, affecting peripheral nerves rather than the spinal cord directly.
  • Acute Disc Prolapse - can cause radicular pain and focal neurological deficits, but severe cord compression with bilateral signs and bladder/bowel involvement is less common.
  • Spinal Stroke (Spinal Cord Infarction) - sudden onset of neurological deficits, often with a clear vascular risk factor, typically sparing posterior column function.
  • Peripheral Neuropathy - diffuse or focal nerve dysfunction, usually affecting distal limbs, with sensory and motor symptoms that do not typically have a clear spinal level.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Spinal cord compression"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: High-dose corticosteroids (e.g., dexamethasone) to reduce oedema and preserve neurological function, urgent neurosurgical or oncological review.
  • Definitive management: Surgical decompression (laminectomy, tumour excision) or radiotherapy, depending on the underlying cause, extent of compression, and patient's overall condition.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Close neurological monitoring, pain management, rehabilitation, and management of underlying disease.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Advise patients to seek immediate medical attention for any worsening neurological symptoms, new bladder/bowel dysfunction, or increasing pain.
  • Exam pharmacology reference (UKMLA/MRCGP): Dexamethasone [sc name="jitl-medication" Q="Dexamethasone use in Spinal cord compression" text="dexamethasone"][/sc] is often used to reduce peritumoural oedema. Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Spinal cord compression"][/sc]
  • Permanent neurological deficit: Irreversible damage to the spinal cord can lead to persistent weakness, sensory loss, and paralysis, particularly if decompression is delayed.
  • Bladder and bowel dysfunction: Chronic urinary retention, incontinence, and constipation can significantly impact quality of life and increase risk of UTIs and skin breakdown.
  • Pressure ulcers: Immobility and sensory loss increase the risk of developing pressure injuries, requiring meticulous skin care.
  • Deep vein thrombosis (DVT) and pulmonary embolism (PE): Reduced mobility predisposes to venous thromboembolism, necessitating prophylactic measures.
  • Chronic pain: Persistent neuropathic or mechanical pain can be a long-term sequela, requiring ongoing pain management strategies.
