Otitis externa [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Otitis externa"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Otitis externa"][/sc]
  • Inflammation of the external ear canal, often associated with infection.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical features of otitis externa, differentiate it from otitis media, and outline initial management strategies.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Otitis externa"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Otitis externa"][/sc]
  • Otitis externa is a common condition, affecting approximately 1 in 10 people at some point in their lives [1].
  • It is slightly more prevalent in women than men and most frequently diagnosed in adults aged 45 to 75 years [1].
  • Risk factors include regular water exposure (e.g., swimming, often termed 'swimmer's ear'), trauma to the ear canal (e.g., scratching, cotton bud use), and underlying skin conditions such as eczema or dermatitis [1].
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Otitis externa"][/sc]
  • Trigger: Disruption of the protective skin barrier in the external ear canal due to moisture, trauma, or dermatological conditions, creating a favourable environment for microbial growth.
  • Progression: Bacterial (most commonly Pseudomonas aeruginosa or Staphylococcus aureus) or fungal (e.g., Candida, Aspergillus) proliferation leads to inflammation, oedema, and exudate within the ear canal [2]. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Pseudomonas aeruginosa: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Pseudomonas aeruginosa"][/sc]
  • Consequence: The inflammatory response results in pain, itching, and potential narrowing of the ear canal, which can further trap debris and perpetuate the infection.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Otitis externa"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Symptoms typically develop rapidly over 48 hours, often following exposure to predisposing factors such as swimming or minor ear trauma.
  • Progressive phase: Without intervention, inflammation and pain can intensify over several days, potentially leading to significant ear canal oedema and discharge.
  • Late phase: With appropriate treatment, symptoms usually begin to resolve within a few days, though complete resolution may take up to a week or longer. In some cases, particularly beyond 3 months, the condition can become chronic or, rarely, progress to malignant otitis externa.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Otitis externa"][/sc]
  • Ask about ear pain - often severe and exacerbated by movement of the tragus or pinna, distinguishing it from otitis media.
  • Ask about itching and irritation - a prominent early symptom, particularly with fungal infections.
  • Ask about discharge from the ear - can range from thin and watery to thick and purulent, indicating infection severity.
  • Ask about hearing loss - typically conductive, due to swelling and debris in the ear canal.
  • Ask about recent water exposure or ear trauma - key predisposing factors.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Otitis externa"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - usually normal in uncomplicated cases; fever or systemic signs may suggest spreading infection or complications.
  • Check tragal tenderness and pain on movement of the pinna - highly characteristic of otitis externa.
  • Check for oedema and erythema of the external ear canal - visible on otoscopy, often obscuring the tympanic membrane.
  • Check for discharge and debris in the ear canal - purulent discharge suggests bacterial infection, while white/black debris may indicate fungal aetiology.
  • Check for regional lymphadenopathy - tender pre-auricular or cervical lymph nodes may be present in more severe cases. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Malignant otitis externa: what are the risk factors and clinical implications?" text="Malignant otitis externa risk factors"][/sc]
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Otitis externa"][/sc]
  • Unremitting pain: Suggests potential spread beyond the ear canal or development of malignant otitis externa, particularly in high-risk groups.
  • Systemic symptoms: Fever, malaise, or spreading cellulitis indicate a more severe infection requiring urgent assessment.
  • Cranial nerve palsies: Especially facial nerve palsy, are a critical sign of malignant otitis externa and require immediate specialist referral.
  • Immunocompromised status: Patients with diabetes, HIV, or on immunosuppressants are at higher risk of severe or complicated infection, including malignant otitis externa.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Otitis externa"][/sc]
  • Immunocompromised/Elderly/Diabetic: May present with less pronounced local symptoms but rapid progression to severe infection, including malignant otitis externa, which is an osteomyelitis of the skull base. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Malignant otitis externa: clinical features and management principles?" text="Malignant otitis externa"][/sc]
  • Fungal otitis externa: Often presents with intense itching, less pain, and a characteristic appearance of fungal hyphae or spores on otoscopy.
  • Chronic otitis externa: Persistent symptoms (itching, discomfort, discharge) lasting over 3 months, often due to underlying dermatological conditions or inadequate treatment.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Otitis externa"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected otitis externa: Clinical diagnosis based on history and otoscopic examination.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Consider ear swab for microbiology (bacterial and fungal culture) if severe, recurrent, chronic, or unresponsive to initial treatment. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Ear swab in otitis externa: indications and interpretation?" text="Ear swab indications"][/sc]
  • Severity/risk tools: No widely used severity/risk scores for uncomplicated otitis externa. For suspected malignant otitis externa, imaging (CT/MRI) and inflammatory markers (ESR/CRP) are crucial.
  • Imaging - CT/MRI: Indicated in suspected malignant otitis externa to assess for bone erosion and spread of infection.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Otitis externa"][/sc]
  • A 55-year-old male presents with acute onset, severe right ear pain, itching, and discharge for 3 days, exacerbated by tragal movement, following regular swimming.
  • Acute, painful otitis externa in a middle-aged male with recent water exposure, concerning for bacterial aetiology.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Otitis externa"][/sc]
  • Acute Otitis Media (AOM): Typically presents with ear pain and often fever, but pain is not exacerbated by tragal movement, and otoscopy reveals a bulging or retracted tympanic membrane, not an inflamed ear canal.
  • Furunculosis: Localised infection of a hair follicle in the ear canal, causing intense localised pain; often a visible pustule or swelling.
  • Contact Dermatitis: Itching, redness, and scaling of the ear canal due to irritant or allergic exposure (e.g., ear drops, jewellery); less severe pain and discharge than infective otitis externa.
  • Referred Pain: Pain originating from other structures (e.g., temporomandibular joint, teeth, pharynx) can be perceived in the ear; absence of ear canal inflammation on otoscopy.
  • Malignant Otitis Externa: Severe, persistent otitis externa with deep-seated pain, often in immunocompromised patients; may involve cranial nerve palsies and requires urgent investigation. Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Otitis externa"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Pain relief (e.g., paracetamol, ibuprofen), aural toilet (gentle cleaning of the ear canal by a healthcare professional to remove debris), and avoidance of aggravating factors (e.g., water exposure).
  • Definitive management: Topical ear drops containing antibiotics (e.g., a fluoroquinolone or aminoglycoside with a steroid) are the mainstay of treatment. Oral antibiotics are generally reserved for severe cases, spreading infection, or immunocompromised patients.
  • Adjunctive management: Consideration of an ear wick if the ear canal is severely oedematous to facilitate penetration of topical medication.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Review typically within 1 week to assess response to treatment and ensure resolution of symptoms.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Advise patients to seek urgent medical attention for worsening pain, fever, spreading redness, or new neurological symptoms (e.g., facial weakness).
  • Exam pharmacology reference (UKMLA/MRCGP): Topical ciprofloxacin [sc name="jitl-medication" Q="Ciprofloxacin use in Otitis externa" text="ciprofloxacin"][/sc] or gentamicin [sc name="jitl-medication" Q="Gentamicin use in Otitis externa" text="gentamicin"][/sc] ear drops are commonly used. Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Otitis externa"][/sc]
  • Chronic Otitis Externa: Persistence of inflammation and symptoms beyond 3 months, often due to inadequate treatment, recurrent predisposing factors, or underlying dermatological conditions.
  • Cellulitis/Perichondritis: Spread of infection to the surrounding skin (cellulitis) or cartilage (perichondritis) of the external ear, leading to increased pain, redness, and swelling.
  • Malignant Otitis Externa: A severe, potentially life-threatening osteomyelitis of the skull base, typically occurring in elderly, diabetic, or immunocompromised patients. It is characterised by persistent, severe pain, granulation tissue in the ear canal, and potential cranial nerve involvement, particularly facial nerve palsy. This complication can develop insidiously over weeks to months.
  • Hearing Loss: Usually temporary and conductive due to ear canal obstruction, but can be permanent in severe cases with structural damage.
