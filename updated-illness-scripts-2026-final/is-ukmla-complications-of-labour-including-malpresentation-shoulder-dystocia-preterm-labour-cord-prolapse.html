Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Complications of labour (including malpresentation, shoulder-dystocia, preterm labour, cord prolapse)"][/sc]
  • Complications of labour encompass a range of adverse events that deviate from the physiological process of childbirth, potentially endangering maternal or foetal well-being.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise and manage common complications of labour, including malpresentation, shoulder dystocia, preterm labour, and cord prolapse, understanding their immediate and long-term implications.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Complications of labour (including malpresentation, shoulder-dystocia, preterm labour, cord prolapse)"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Complications of labour (including malpresentation, shoulder-dystocia, preterm labour, cord prolapse)"][/sc]
  • Preterm labour affects approximately 7-8% of live births in the UK, contributing significantly to neonatal morbidity and mortality (ONS data).
  • Shoulder dystocia is a relatively rare but critical obstetric emergency, occurring in about 0.5-1.5% of vaginal births, with increased incidence in cases of foetal macrosomia and maternal diabetes.
  • Risk factors often include previous complicated pregnancies, multiple gestations, maternal obesity, and certain uterine anomalies.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Complications of labour (including malpresentation, shoulder-dystocia, preterm labour, cord prolapse)"][/sc]
  • Trigger: Various factors, such as uterine overdistension, infection, or structural abnormalities, can initiate abnormal uterine contractions or compromise foetal descent.
  • Progression: These triggers lead to a cascade of events, including inefficient cervical dilation, malpositioning of the foetus, or mechanical obstruction, disrupting the normal progression of labour.
  • Consequence: The result is often prolonged labour, foetal distress, maternal injury, or the need for urgent obstetric intervention, such as instrumental delivery or Caesarean section.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Complications of labour (including malpresentation, shoulder-dystocia, preterm labour, cord prolapse)"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Subtle signs such as irregular contractions or changes in foetal movement may precede overt complications, particularly beyond 24-48 hours before active labour.
  • Progressive phase: During active labour, complications can manifest rapidly, with events like cord prolapse requiring immediate intervention within minutes, or shoulder dystocia developing during the second stage.
  • Late phase: Postpartum complications, such as haemorrhage or infection, may emerge hours to days after delivery, influenced by the nature and management of intrapartum events.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Complications of labour (including malpresentation, shoulder-dystocia, preterm labour, cord prolapse)"][/sc]
  • Ask about uterine contractions - frequency, intensity, and regularity can indicate preterm labour or ineffective labour progression.
  • Ask about vaginal discharge or bleeding - may suggest membrane rupture, placental abruption, or cervical changes.
  • Ask about foetal movements - decreased or absent movements can be a sign of foetal distress.
  • Ask about maternal pain - location and character can help differentiate between normal labour pain and pain associated with complications like uterine rupture.
