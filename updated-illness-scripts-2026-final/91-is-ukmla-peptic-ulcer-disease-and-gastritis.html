Peptic ulcer disease and gastritis [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Peptic ulcer disease and gastritis"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Peptic ulcer disease and gastritis"][/sc]
  • Peptic ulcer disease (PUD) involves breaches in the gastric or duodenal mucosa extending through the muscularis mucosae, typically caused by an imbalance between aggressive factors (acid, pepsin, H. pylori, NSAIDs) and protective mechanisms (mucus, bicarbonate, prostaglandins). Gastritis refers to inflammation of the gastric mucosa, which may or may not be associated with ulceration.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical features, risk factors, and common complications of peptic ulcer disease and gastritis, and understand the principles of investigation and initial management, including H. pylori eradication.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Peptic ulcer disease and gastritis"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Peptic ulcer disease and gastritis"][/sc]
  • The annual incidence of peptic ulcer disease in the UK has been reported to be around 0.1–0.3% in the general population, with a decreasing trend in recent decades due to reduced H. pylori infection rates and changes in NSAID prescribing [1].
  • H. pylori infection is the most common cause of PUD, accounting for approximately 90% of duodenal ulcers and 70-80% of gastric ulcers. NSAID use is the second most common cause, particularly in older adults [2].
  • Risk factors include H. pylori infection, chronic NSAID use, smoking, excessive alcohol consumption, stress, and certain genetic predispositions.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Peptic ulcer disease and gastritis"][/sc]
  • Trigger: H. pylori infection or NSAID use disrupts the protective gastric or duodenal mucosal barrier. H. pylori directly damages epithelial cells and induces inflammation, while NSAIDs inhibit prostaglandin synthesis, reducing mucus and bicarbonate production and mucosal blood flow.
  • Progression: The compromised mucosal defence allows gastric acid and pepsin to cause direct injury to the underlying tissue, leading to inflammation (gastritis) and eventually ulcer formation. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="H. pylori virulence factors: what are they and why do they matter clinically?" text="H. pylori virulence factors"][/sc]
  • Consequence: Persistent exposure to acid and pepsin prevents healing and can lead to deepening of the ulcer, potentially causing complications such as bleeding, perforation, or obstruction.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Peptic ulcer disease and gastritis"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Symptoms such as epigastric pain, dyspepsia, or nausea may develop gradually over days to weeks, often intermittently, as gastritis or superficial ulceration occurs.
  • Progressive phase: If untreated, symptoms may become more persistent and severe over weeks to months, indicating deeper ulceration. Periods of remission and exacerbation are common, particularly with H. pylori infection.
  • Late phase: Chronic, untreated ulcers can lead to complications developing over months to years, such as anaemia from chronic blood loss, or acute events like perforation or haemorrhage, which can manifest suddenly.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Peptic ulcer disease and gastritis"][/sc]
  • Ask about epigastric pain - often described as burning or gnawing, which may be relieved by food or antacids in duodenal ulcers, or exacerbated by food in gastric ulcers, helping to differentiate ulcer type.
  • Ask about dyspepsia - including bloating, early satiety, or nausea, which are common but non-specific symptoms of upper gastrointestinal irritation.
  • Ask about heartburn or acid regurgitation - suggesting associated gastro-oesophageal reflux, which can co-exist or mimic ulcer symptoms.
  • Ask about weight loss or appetite changes - these can be indicators of chronic disease or potential malignancy, particularly with gastric ulcers.
  • Ask about symptoms of anaemia (fatigue, pallor) - indicating chronic, occult gastrointestinal bleeding, a common complication.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Peptic ulcer disease and gastritis"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - to assess for signs of acute complications such as tachycardia or hypotension in significant bleeding, or fever in perforation.
  • Check abdominal tenderness - localised epigastric tenderness on palpation is a common finding, but diffuse tenderness or guarding suggests perforation.
  • Check pallor - may indicate chronic blood loss and anaemia, especially in long-standing or occult bleeding.
  • Check signs of peritonism - rebound tenderness, guarding, or rigidity suggest peritoneal irritation, a critical sign of perforation. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Peritonism: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Peritonism"][/sc]
  • Check for succussion splash - a rare sign of gastric outlet obstruction, which can be a complication of chronic peptic ulceration.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Peptic ulcer disease and gastritis"][/sc]
  • Haematemesis or melaena: Indicates acute upper gastrointestinal bleeding, requiring urgent endoscopic assessment and management within hours.
  • Sudden, severe abdominal pain with guarding/rigidity: Highly suggestive of peptic ulcer perforation, a surgical emergency requiring immediate intervention.
  • Persistent vomiting, particularly undigested food: May signify gastric outlet obstruction due to chronic ulcer scarring, which can develop over weeks to months.
  • Unexplained weight loss or dysphagia: Raises suspicion for gastric malignancy, especially in older patients with new-onset dyspepsia, warranting urgent investigation.
  • Iron deficiency anaemia without obvious cause: Points to chronic, occult gastrointestinal blood loss, necessitating investigation for a bleeding source, including ulceration.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Peptic ulcer disease and gastritis"][/sc]
  • Older adults: May present with less pronounced pain, or primarily with complications such as bleeding or perforation, often due to NSAID use and reduced pain perception.
  • NSAID users: Can develop ulcers with minimal or no dyspeptic symptoms, presenting acutely with haemorrhage or perforation, highlighting the importance of risk factor assessment.
  • Critically ill patients: Stress-induced gastritis and ulcers can occur without typical symptoms, often presenting with overt bleeding, necessitating prophylactic strategies.
  • Zollinger-Ellison syndrome: A rare condition causing severe, refractory PUD due to gastrinoma-induced hypersecretion of acid, often presenting with multiple or unusual ulcer locations. Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Peptic ulcer disease and gastritis"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected peptic ulcer disease: Upper gastrointestinal endoscopy with biopsy is the gold standard for diagnosing ulcers, differentiating gastric from duodenal ulcers, and excluding malignancy, especially for gastric ulcers.
  • H. pylori testing: Urea breath test or stool antigen test are preferred non-invasive methods for detecting H. pylori infection. Rapid urease test (CLO test) is performed on biopsy samples during endoscopy. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Rapid urease test (CLO test): what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Rapid urease test (CLO test)"][/sc]
  • Severity/risk tools: The Blatchford Score or Rockall Score can be used to stratify risk in patients with upper GI bleeding, guiding management decisions. Blatchford Score on MDCalc, Rockall Score on MDCalc
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Blood tests (FBC, U&Es, LFTs, amylase) to rule out other causes of abdominal pain or assess for complications.
  • Imaging - CT abdomen: May be used in suspected perforation to identify free air or fluid, or in cases of suspected gastric outlet obstruction.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Peptic ulcer disease and gastritis"][/sc]
  • Gastro-oesophageal reflux disease (GORD) - typically presents with heartburn and regurgitation, often worse on lying down, without the deep gnawing pain characteristic of ulcers.
  • Functional dyspepsia - chronic dyspeptic symptoms without an identifiable organic cause on investigation, often diagnosed after excluding PUD and other conditions.
  • Gastric malignancy - can mimic ulcer symptoms, but often associated with progressive weight loss, anorexia, and dysphagia, requiring endoscopic differentiation.
  • Biliary colic/cholecystitis - pain is typically in the right upper quadrant, often radiating to the back or shoulder, and precipitated by fatty meals.
  • Pancreatitis - severe epigastric pain radiating to the back, often associated with nausea, vomiting, and elevated amylase/lipase.
  • Myocardial ischaemia - atypical chest pain can sometimes present as epigastric discomfort, especially in women or diabetics, necessitating cardiac assessment. Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Peptic ulcer disease and gastritis"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Recognition and escalation of acute complications (e.g., bleeding, perforation), supportive care including fluid resuscitation and blood transfusion if indicated, and prompt referral for endoscopic or surgical intervention.
  • Definitive management: Eradication of H. pylori infection (if present) with a combination of antibiotics and proton pump inhibitors (PPIs). Discontinuation or reduction of NSAID use, with consideration of gastroprotection (e.g., PPIs) if NSAIDs are essential.
  • Adjunctive management: Acid suppression with PPIs or H2-receptor antagonists to promote ulcer healing and alleviate symptoms. Lifestyle modifications, including avoidance of smoking, alcohol, and known dietary triggers.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Repeat endoscopy for gastric ulcers to confirm healing and exclude malignancy. Follow-up H. pylori testing to confirm eradication.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Advise patients on red flag symptoms requiring urgent medical attention (e.g., severe pain, vomiting blood, black stools).

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Peptic ulcer disease and gastritis"][/sc]
  • Haemorrhage: Occurs when an ulcer erodes into a blood vessel, leading to haematemesis, melaena, or occult bleeding, which can manifest acutely over hours or chronically over weeks, causing anaemia.
  • Perforation: A life-threatening complication where the ulcer erodes through the entire gastric or duodenal wall, leading to peritonitis and requiring immediate surgical intervention, typically presenting as sudden, severe pain.
  • Gastric outlet obstruction: Caused by oedema, inflammation, or scarring from chronic ulcers near the pylorus, leading to persistent vomiting, early satiety, and weight loss, developing over weeks to months.
  • Malignant transformation: While duodenal ulcers are rarely malignant, gastric ulcers, particularly those that are large or refractory to treatment, carry a risk of malignancy and require careful follow-up with biopsies.
