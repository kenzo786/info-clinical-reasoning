Transfusion reactions [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Transfusion reactions"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Transfusion reactions"][/sc]
  • A clinical syndrome occurring during or after blood product transfusion, characterised by adverse signs and symptoms.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the types, causes, and immediate management of transfusion reactions to ensure patient safety.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Transfusion reactions"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Transfusion reactions"][/sc]
  • The incidence of transfusion reactions varies by type, with allergic reactions being the most common (affecting approximately 1-3% of transfusions), while severe reactions like TRALI or acute haemolytic reactions are rare but life-threatening (e.g., 1 in 50,000 to 1 in 100,000 transfusions in the UK).
  • Risk factors include patient sensitisation to donor antigens, underlying medical conditions, and specific blood product characteristics.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Transfusion reactions"][/sc]
  • Trigger: Introduction of foreign antigens (e.g., red cell, plasma, platelet, white cell antigens) or vasoactive substances from transfused blood products into the recipient's circulation.
  • Progression: Activation of the recipient's immune system (e.g., antibody-antigen reactions, cytokine release) or direct non-immune effects (e.g., volume overload, bacterial contamination).
  • Consequence: Systemic inflammatory response, organ dysfunction, or haemolysis, leading to a spectrum of clinical manifestations.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Transfusion reactions"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Reactions typically occur within minutes to a few hours of starting the transfusion, often within the first 15-30 minutes.
  • Progressive phase: Symptoms may escalate over several hours, particularly beyond 6 hours, if the reaction is not recognised and managed promptly.
  • Late phase: Delayed reactions can manifest days to weeks after the transfusion, such as delayed haemolytic transfusion reactions or transfusion-associated graft-versus-host disease.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Transfusion reactions"][/sc]
  • Ask about fever/chills - may indicate an immune response or bacterial contamination.
  • Ask about rash/urticaria/pruritus - suggestive of an allergic reaction.
  • Ask about dyspnoea/chest tightness - could signify anaphylaxis, TRALI, or TACO.
  • Ask about loin pain/dark urine - classic signs of acute haemolytic transfusion reaction.
  • Ask about nausea/vomiting - non-specific but can accompany various types of reactions.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Transfusion reactions"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs (temperature, pulse, blood pressure, respiratory rate, oxygen saturation) - essential for assessing severity and type of reaction.
  • Check skin for flushing, urticaria, angioedema - indicates allergic or anaphylactic reaction.
  • Check respiratory effort for tachypnoea, wheeze, crackles - suggests pulmonary involvement (e.g., TRALI, TACO).
  • Check for signs of circulatory compromise like hypotension or tachycardia - indicative of severe systemic reaction or shock.
  • Check urine output and colour - for evidence of haemoglobinuria in haemolytic reactions.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Transfusion reactions"][/sc]
  • Sudden onset dyspnoea and hypoxaemia: May indicate transfusion-related acute lung injury (TRALI) or transfusion-associated circulatory overload (TACO), requiring immediate respiratory support.
  • Acute hypotension and shock: Suggests severe anaphylaxis, acute haemolytic reaction, or septic shock from bacterial contamination, necessitating urgent resuscitation.
  • Haemoglobinuria and acute kidney injury: Highly indicative of an acute haemolytic transfusion reaction, requiring prompt investigation and renal protection.
  • Rapidly spreading urticaria with angioedema: Signifies severe allergic reaction or anaphylaxis, requiring immediate administration of adrenaline and antihistamines.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Transfusion reactions"][/sc]
  • Immunosuppressed patients: May present with attenuated or delayed immune responses, making diagnosis challenging.
  • Patients under general anaesthesia: Signs like hypotension, tachycardia, or haemoglobinuria may be the only indicators, as conscious symptoms are masked.
  • Older adults: More susceptible to transfusion-associated circulatory overload (TACO) due to reduced cardiac reserve, and symptoms may be non-specific or attributed to underlying comorbidities.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Transfusion reactions"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected transfusion reaction: Stop transfusion immediately, maintain IV access, notify blood bank. Send post-transfusion blood samples (EDTA, clotted) for direct antiglobulin test (DAT), repeat group and crossmatch, and plasma for haemolysis markers (e.g., bilirubin, LDH, haptoglobin). Send urine for haemoglobinuria.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Consider chest X-ray for dyspnoea (TRALI/TACO), blood cultures for suspected bacterial contamination, and ECG/cardiac enzymes for chest pain.
  • Serial reassessment: Monitor full blood count, renal function, and coagulation profile, especially in severe reactions.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Transfusion reactions"][/sc]
  • A 68-year-old female, receiving a red cell transfusion for anaemia, develops sudden onset fever, chills, and loin pain within 15 minutes of transfusion initiation, accompanied by dark urine.
  • An acutely unwell, elderly female with new-onset systemic inflammatory response and evidence of haemolysis immediately following blood product administration.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Transfusion reactions"][/sc]
  • Acute Haemolytic Transfusion Reaction (AHTR) - positive DAT, haemoglobinuria, renal impairment.
  • Febrile Non-Haemolytic Transfusion Reaction (FNHTR) - fever and chills without haemolysis, often due to cytokine release.
  • Allergic Reaction - urticaria, pruritus, angioedema, typically without fever or haemolysis.
  • Anaphylactic Reaction - severe allergic reaction with hypotension, bronchospasm, angioedema.
  • Transfusion-Related Acute Lung Injury (TRALI) - acute hypoxaemia and bilateral pulmonary oedema within 6 hours, non-cardiogenic.
  • Transfusion-Associated Circulatory Overload (TACO) - acute pulmonary oedema within 6 hours, cardiogenic, elevated BNP.
  • Bacterial Contamination/Septic Transfusion Reaction - high fever, rigors, hypotension, positive blood cultures.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Transfusion reactions"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Stop the transfusion immediately, maintain intravenous access with normal saline, notify the blood bank, and assess the patient (ABCDE approach).
  • Definitive management: Management is supportive and specific to the type of reaction. This may include antihistamines for allergic reactions, adrenaline for anaphylaxis, diuretics for TACO, and respiratory support for TRALI.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Close monitoring of vital signs, fluid balance, and urine output. Further investigations as guided by the blood bank.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Escalate to senior medical staff and critical care if signs of severe reaction, haemodynamic instability, or respiratory compromise.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Transfusion reactions"][/sc]
  • Acute Kidney Injury: Mechanism is acute tubular necrosis due to haemoglobinuria from severe haemolysis, which matters prognostically due to potential for long-term renal impairment.
  • Disseminated Intravascular Coagulation (DIC): Mechanism is widespread activation of coagulation and fibrinolysis, often triggered by severe immune reactions or sepsis, which matters diagnostically due to bleeding and thrombotic complications.
  • Acute Respiratory Distress Syndrome (ARDS): Mechanism is severe lung inflammation and increased capillary permeability (e.g., TRALI), which matters prognostically due to high mortality and prolonged ventilation requirements.
  • Multi-organ Failure: Mechanism is uncontrolled systemic inflammatory response leading to dysfunction of multiple organ systems, which matters prognostically due to very high mortality.
