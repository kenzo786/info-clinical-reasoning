Acute bronchitis [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Acute bronchitis"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Acute bronchitis"][/sc]
  • Acute bronchitis is a self-limiting inflammation of the large airways, typically viral in origin, presenting with cough and often sputum production, without evidence of pneumonia.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical features of acute bronchitis, differentiate it from pneumonia, and understand the principles of symptomatic management and antibiotic stewardship.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Acute bronchitis"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Acute bronchitis"][/sc]
  • Acute bronchitis is one of the most common reasons for primary care consultations, particularly during autumn and winter, affecting up to 5% of adults annually in the UK.
  • Risk factors include exposure to respiratory viruses, smoking, chronic lung conditions (e.g., asthma, COPD), and occupational exposure to irritants.
  • The vast majority (90-95%) of cases are caused by viruses, with bacterial co-infection being rare, highlighting the importance of judicious antibiotic use.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Acute bronchitis"][/sc]
  • Viral infection of the tracheobronchial tree initiates an inflammatory response, leading to mucosal oedema, increased mucus production, and desquamation of epithelial cells.
  • This inflammation and irritation of airway receptors trigger a cough reflex, which may be productive of clear, yellow, or green sputum due to inflammatory exudates and cellular debris.
  • The consequence is transient airway hyperresponsiveness and a persistent cough, which can last for several weeks even after the viral infection has resolved.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Acute bronchitis"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Symptoms typically develop over 1-2 days, often preceded by an upper respiratory tract infection, with initial presentation of cough, sore throat, and nasal congestion.
  • Progressive phase: The cough usually becomes the predominant symptom, often productive, and can persist for 1-3 weeks, sometimes accompanied by mild fever, chest discomfort, and wheezing.
  • Late phase: Resolution of symptoms is gradual, with the cough slowly improving over 3-4 weeks, though in some individuals, a post-infectious cough may linger for up to 8 weeks, particularly beyond 4 weeks.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Acute bronchitis"][/sc]
  • Ask about cough - often the most prominent symptom, which can be dry or productive, and its duration is key to distinguishing from common cold.
  • Ask about sputum production - typically clear, white, yellow, or green; purulent sputum alone does not indicate bacterial infection in acute bronchitis.
  • Ask about chest discomfort - often described as a burning sensation behind the sternum, exacerbated by coughing, due to tracheal irritation.
  • Ask about dyspnoea - usually mild and associated with coughing; significant breathlessness or pleuritic chest pain may suggest pneumonia.
  • Ask about fever - low-grade fever may be present, but high fever (≥38°C) or rigors are more suggestive of pneumonia or influenza.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Acute bronchitis"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - typically normal or mildly elevated temperature; significant tachycardia, tachypnoea, or hypoxaemia are red flags for more severe illness.
  • Check auscultation - often reveals scattered rhonchi or wheezes that may clear with coughing, indicating airway inflammation and secretions.
  • Check percussion - usually resonant; dullness suggests consolidation (pneumonia) or pleural effusion.
  • Check palpation - no focal tenderness; chest wall tenderness may indicate musculoskeletal pain from coughing.
  • Check for signs of respiratory distress - absence of accessory muscle use, nasal flaring, or intercostal recession helps differentiate from severe lower respiratory tract infection.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Acute bronchitis"][/sc]
  • High fever (≥38°C) or rigors: Suggests a more severe infection such as pneumonia or influenza, warranting further investigation.
  • Significant breathlessness or tachypnoea (>20 breaths/min): Indicates impaired gas exchange or increased work of breathing, raising concern for pneumonia or exacerbation of underlying lung disease.
  • Haemoptysis: While rare, this symptom requires urgent assessment to exclude serious underlying pathology such as pulmonary embolism or malignancy.
  • Focal chest signs (e.g., crackles, dullness to percussion): Highly suggestive of pneumonia, requiring chest radiography and potentially antibiotic treatment.
  • Symptoms persisting beyond 3-4 weeks: Raises suspicion for alternative diagnoses such as asthma, post-infectious cough, or less commonly, underlying malignancy or tuberculosis.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Acute bronchitis"][/sc]
  • Older adults: May present with less prominent cough, more general malaise, or confusion, making differentiation from pneumonia challenging.
  • Immunocompromised individuals: Symptoms may be more severe and prolonged, with a higher risk of progression to pneumonia or other opportunistic infections.
  • Patients with underlying chronic lung disease (e.g., COPD, asthma): Acute bronchitis can trigger an exacerbation of their underlying condition, leading to more severe breathlessness and wheezing.
  • Children: Often present with wheezing and mild respiratory distress, which can be difficult to distinguish from bronchiolitis or asthma.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Acute bronchitis"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected acute bronchitis: Diagnosis is primarily clinical, based on history and examination, with no routine investigations required.
  • Severity/risk tools: Consider using the CRB-65 score to assess severity and guide management if pneumonia is suspected; CRB-65 Score for Pneumonia Severity on MDCalc.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Chest X-ray is indicated if red flag features suggest pneumonia, such as focal chest signs, high fever, or significant breathlessness.
  • Microbiology: Sputum culture is generally not recommended unless there is suspicion of specific bacterial infection (e.g., pertussis) or in immunocompromised patients.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Acute bronchitis"][/sc]
  • A 35-year-old non-smoking male presents with a 10-day history of productive cough, mild chest discomfort, and no significant fever or focal chest signs on examination.
  • Otherwise healthy adult with a self-limiting respiratory illness, concerning for acute bronchitis, but requiring exclusion of pneumonia.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Acute bronchitis"][/sc]
  • Pneumonia - distinguished by focal chest signs, higher fever, significant systemic upset, and often abnormal chest X-ray findings.
  • Common cold/Upper respiratory tract infection - typically shorter duration of cough (usually <7 days) and more prominent upper respiratory symptoms like sneezing and nasal discharge.
  • Asthma exacerbation - history of asthma, recurrent wheezing, and response to bronchodilators are key differentiating features.
  • COPD exacerbation - history of COPD, increased sputum volume/purulence, and worsening dyspnoea are characteristic.
  • Pertussis (whooping cough) - prolonged cough (>2 weeks) with paroxysms, inspiratory whoop, and post-tussive vomiting, especially if unvaccinated.
  • Gastro-oesophageal reflux disease (GORD) - chronic cough, often worse at night or after meals, associated with heartburn or regurgitation.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Acute bronchitis"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Reassurance, education on the self-limiting nature of the condition, and advice on symptomatic relief.
  • Definitive management: Primarily supportive care, including adequate hydration, rest, and simple analgesia (e.g., paracetamol, ibuprofen) for fever and discomfort.
  • Adjunctive management: Cough suppressants or expectorants may be considered for symptomatic relief, though evidence of efficacy is limited.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Advise patients to seek re-evaluation if symptoms worsen, new symptoms develop, or do not improve within 3-4 weeks.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Provide clear instructions on red flag symptoms (e.g., increasing breathlessness, high fever, chest pain) that warrant urgent medical review.
  • Exam pharmacology reference (UKMLA/MRCGP): Antibiotics are generally not indicated for acute bronchitis due to its viral aetiology and lack of benefit in most cases. Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Acute bronchitis"][/sc]
  • Pneumonia: The most significant complication, occurring when the infection spreads from the bronchi to the lung parenchyma, particularly if symptoms worsen or persist beyond 3-4 weeks.
  • Exacerbation of underlying lung disease: In patients with asthma or COPD, acute bronchitis can trigger a severe worsening of their baseline respiratory function, requiring intensified treatment.
  • Protracted cough: A persistent cough lasting more than 8 weeks can significantly impact quality of life and may require investigation for other causes, such as post-nasal drip or asthma.
  • Antibiotic resistance: Over-prescription of antibiotics for viral bronchitis contributes to the development of antimicrobial resistance, a major public health concern.
