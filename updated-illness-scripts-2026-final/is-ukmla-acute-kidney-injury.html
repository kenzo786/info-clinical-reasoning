Acute kidney injury [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Acute kidney injury"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Acute kidney injury"][/sc]
  • A rapid decline in renal function, often leading to accumulation of nitrogenous waste products and dysregulation of fluid and electrolyte balance.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the causes, clinical features, and initial management of acute kidney injury.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Acute kidney injury"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Acute kidney injury"][/sc]
  • Affects approximately 1 in 5 hospitalised patients in the UK, with higher incidence in critical care settings (NICE).
  • Risk factors include advanced age, pre-existing chronic kidney disease, heart failure, diabetes, sepsis, and exposure to nephrotoxic drugs.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Acute kidney injury"][/sc]
  • Trigger: An acute insult, such as hypovolaemia, sepsis, or nephrotoxic agents, leads to reduced renal perfusion or direct kidney damage.
  • Progression: Decreased glomerular filtration rate (GFR) results in impaired excretion of waste products and inability to regulate fluid and electrolytes.
  • Consequence: Accumulation of urea, creatinine, potassium, and fluid overload, potentially leading to systemic complications and organ dysfunction.
