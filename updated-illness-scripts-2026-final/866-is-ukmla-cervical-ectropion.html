Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Cervical ectropion"][/sc]
  • Cervical ectropion is a benign physiological condition where the columnar epithelium, normally lining the endocervical canal, is everted onto the ectocervix, appearing as a reddish area around the external os.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise cervical ectropion as a common, benign gynaecological finding, differentiate it from pathological cervical conditions, and understand its typical presentations and management.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Cervical ectropion"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Cervical ectropion"][/sc]
  • Cervical ectropion is a common finding, particularly in women of reproductive age, with prevalence estimates ranging from 17-50% in various populations [1, 2].
  • It is strongly associated with conditions of high oestrogen levels, including adolescence, pregnancy, and the use of combined oral contraceptive pills [3].
  • Post-coital bleeding, a common symptom of cervical ectropion, affects 0.7-9.0% of menstruating women in the UK, with benign causes like ectropion being the most frequent [4].
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Cervical ectropion"][/sc]
  • Trigger: Elevated oestrogen levels, as seen in adolescence, pregnancy, or with combined oral contraceptive use, stimulate the eversion of the endocervical columnar epithelium onto the ectocervix.
  • Progression: The delicate columnar epithelium, which is typically found within the endocervical canal, becomes exposed to the acidic vaginal environment, making it more susceptible to irritation and trauma. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Squamocolumnar junction: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Squamocolumnar junction"][/sc]
  • Consequence: The exposed columnar cells contain mucus-secreting glands, leading to increased vaginal discharge, and their superficial, fine blood vessels are prone to rupture, causing post-coital or intermenstrual bleeding. Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Cervical ectropion"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Often asymptomatic, ectropion may be present from adolescence or develop shortly after starting combined oral contraceptives, with symptoms such as discharge or post-coital bleeding emerging gradually.
  • Progressive phase: Symptoms may persist or worsen with continued oestrogen exposure (e.g., throughout pregnancy or prolonged contraceptive use), potentially leading to ongoing discomfort or anxiety.
  • Late phase: Ectropion often resolves spontaneously with decreasing oestrogen levels, such as after childbirth or discontinuation of hormonal contraception, or with age, becoming uncommon in post-menopausal women.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Cervical ectropion"][/sc]
  • Ask about post-coital bleeding - this is the most common symptomatic presentation, often light and self-limiting, but requires exclusion of more serious pathology.
  • Ask about intermenstrual bleeding - irregular bleeding between periods may occur due to the friable nature of the exposed columnar epithelium.
  • Ask about increased vaginal discharge - the mucus-secreting columnar cells can produce a non-purulent, clear or whitish discharge.
  • Ask about dyspareunia - discomfort or pain during sexual intercourse may be reported due to irritation of the ectropion.
  • Ask about contact bleeding during cervical screening - the delicate ectropion can bleed easily upon instrumentation, which can be a source of patient anxiety.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Cervical ectropion"][/sc]
  • Check speculum examination - reveals a characteristic reddish, velvety appearance around the external os, representing the everted columnar epithelium.
  • Check for contact bleeding - gentle touching of the ectropion with a swab may induce bleeding, confirming its friable nature.
  • Check for purulent discharge - absence of purulent discharge helps differentiate ectropion from cervicitis, which often presents with inflammatory exudate.
  • Check vital signs - typically normal, as ectropion is a localised, benign condition and does not cause systemic illness.
  • Check bimanual examination - usually unremarkable, with no cervical tenderness or masses, further supporting a benign diagnosis. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Cervical appearance on speculum: what does it imply?" text="Cervical appearance"][/sc]
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Cervical ectropion"][/sc]
  • Persistent or heavy post-coital bleeding: May indicate underlying cervical pathology such as cervical cancer or high-grade cervical intraepithelial neoplasia (CIN), particularly if symptoms are new onset or worsening.
  • Abnormal cervical appearance on speculum: Any suspicious lesions, ulceration, or induration of the cervix warrants urgent colposcopy to exclude malignancy.
  • Unexplained intermenstrual or post-menopausal bleeding: These bleeding patterns, especially in older women, require thorough investigation to rule out endometrial or cervical cancer.
  • Systemic symptoms: Unexplained weight loss, persistent pelvic pain, or changes in bowel/bladder habits in conjunction with cervical symptoms could suggest advanced malignancy.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Cervical ectropion"][/sc]
  • Post-menopausal women: Cervical ectropion is uncommon in this group due to lower oestrogen levels; its presence should prompt careful consideration of other causes of bleeding, including malignancy.
  • Women not on hormonal contraception: While often associated with exogenous oestrogen, ectropion can occur physiologically, but other causes of symptoms should be rigorously excluded.
  • Asymptomatic ectropion: Many women have ectropion without any symptoms, highlighting that its presence alone does not necessitate intervention unless symptomatic.
  • Persistent symptoms despite treatment: If symptoms like post-coital bleeding persist after discontinuing hormonal contraception or ablative therapy, further investigation is warranted to exclude other pathologies.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Cervical ectropion"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected cervical ectropion:
    • Speculum examination: Visualisation of the cervix to identify the characteristic reddish appearance of the ectropion.
    • Cervical cytology (smear test): To screen for cervical intraepithelial neoplasia (CIN) or cervical cancer, especially if there are abnormal bleeding patterns.
    • Swabs (endocervical and high vaginal): If infection (e.g., cervicitis) is suspected due to purulent discharge or other symptoms.
    • Pregnancy test: To exclude pregnancy as a cause of bleeding or as a physiological cause of ectropion.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible:
    • Colposcopy: Indicated if cervical cytology is abnormal, or if there are suspicious features on speculum examination (e.g., ulceration, mass) to allow for targeted biopsies. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Colposcopy indications: when is it performed and what does it show?" text="Colposcopy"][/sc]
    • Biopsy: Taken during colposcopy if abnormal areas are identified, to provide a definitive histological diagnosis.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Cervical ectropion"][/sc]
  • A 24-year-old female presents with a 3-month history of intermittent post-coital bleeding and increased vaginal discharge, with a speculum examination revealing a reddish, velvety area around the external cervical os.
  • Young female with chronic post-coital bleeding and non-purulent discharge, associated with a characteristic cervical appearance, concerning for cervical ectropion.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Cervical ectropion"][/sc]
  • Cervical cancer: Distinguished by persistent, often heavier, irregular bleeding, abnormal cytology, and suspicious lesions on examination requiring biopsy.
  • Cervical intraepithelial neoplasia (CIN): Typically asymptomatic but detected by abnormal cervical cytology; colposcopy and biopsy are diagnostic.
  • Cervicitis: Often presents with purulent or foul-smelling discharge, cervical tenderness, and may be associated with sexually transmitted infections.
  • Cervical polyp: A benign growth on the cervix that can cause intermenstrual or post-coital bleeding, usually visible as a distinct lesion on speculum examination.
  • Vaginitis: Inflammation of the vagina, often causing discharge, itching, and irritation, but typically without the characteristic cervical appearance of ectropion.
  • Ectopic pregnancy: A critical differential for any woman of reproductive age with abdominal pain and vaginal bleeding, requiring urgent assessment and exclusion.
  • Pelvic inflammatory disease (PID): Characterised by lower abdominal pain, deep dyspareunia, purulent vaginal discharge, and cervical motion tenderness. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Causes of post-coital bleeding: what are the common and serious etiologies?" text="Post-coital bleeding causes"][/sc]
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Cervical ectropion"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Exclude serious pathology such as cervical cancer or high-grade CIN through appropriate investigations (e.g., cervical cytology, colposcopy if indicated).
  • Definitive management: For symptomatic ectropion, initial management often involves discontinuing hormonal contraception if it is a contributing factor.
  • Adjunctive management: If symptoms persist and are bothersome, ablative therapies such as cryotherapy or electrocautery may be considered to destroy the everted columnar epithelium.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Asymptomatic ectropion typically requires no treatment or specific follow-up beyond routine cervical screening. Symptomatic cases should be reviewed after intervention to ensure resolution.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Patients should be advised to seek further medical attention if symptoms worsen, new symptoms develop, or if there is any concern for persistent or heavy bleeding.
  • Exam pharmacology reference (UKMLA/MRCGP): No specific drug knowledge is commonly tested for the direct treatment of cervical ectropion itself, as management is primarily non-pharmacological or procedural. Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Cervical ectropion"][/sc]
  • Persistent symptoms: Ongoing post-coital bleeding, intermenstrual bleeding, or excessive discharge can lead to significant patient distress and impact quality of life if not effectively managed.
  • Anxiety and psychological impact: Unexplained vaginal bleeding can cause considerable anxiety about underlying serious conditions like cancer, necessitating clear communication and reassurance.
  • Misdiagnosis or delayed diagnosis: Failure to differentiate ectropion from more serious cervical pathologies can lead to delayed diagnosis and treatment of conditions like cervical cancer.
  • Recurrence after treatment: Symptoms may recur after ablative therapies, particularly if underlying hormonal factors persist, potentially requiring repeat treatment or alternative approaches.
