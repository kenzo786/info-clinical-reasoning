Febrile convulsions [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Febrile convulsions"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Febrile convulsions"][/sc]
  • A febrile convulsion is a seizure occurring in a child aged 6 months to 5 years, associated with a fever (temperature ≥38°C), without evidence of intracranial infection or other defined cause.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical features of a febrile convulsion and differentiate between simple and complex presentations to guide initial management and referral.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Febrile convulsions"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Febrile convulsions"][/sc]
  • Febrile convulsions are common, affecting approximately 2-4% of children in the UK, typically between 6 months and 5 years of age [NHS].
  • Peak incidence is between 12 and 18 months of age [NHS].
  • Risk factors include a family history of febrile convulsions, prematurity, and viral infections (e.g., human herpesvirus 6, influenza) [NHS].
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Febrile convulsions"][/sc]
  • Trigger: Rapid rise in body temperature, often associated with viral infections, leading to neuronal hyperexcitability in the immature brain.
  • Progression: The immature brain\'s susceptibility to fever-induced neuronal discharge results in generalised or focal seizure activity.
  • Consequence: Typically self-limiting, but prolonged or recurrent seizures may rarely lead to neuronal injury or increased risk of epilepsy later in life. Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Febrile convulsions"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Seizure onset is typically rapid, occurring within the first 24 hours of a febrile illness, often as the temperature is rising.
  • Progressive phase: Most simple febrile convulsions resolve spontaneously within 5 minutes, particularly beyond 15 minutes, requiring urgent medical review.
  • Late phase: Post-ictal drowsiness or irritability is common, usually resolving within an hour. Recurrence risk is highest within 12-24 months of the initial episode.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Febrile convulsions"][/sc]
  • Ask about generalised tonic-clonic movements - to distinguish from focal seizures or non-epileptic events.
  • Ask about duration of seizure - crucial for differentiating simple (<15 minutes) from complex (>15 minutes) febrile convulsions.
  • Ask about associated fever - confirms the febrile nature and excludes afebrile seizures.
  • Ask about post-ictal state - drowsiness or irritability is typical, prolonged altered consciousness may suggest other pathology.
  • Ask about recurrence within 24 hours - indicates a complex febrile convulsion.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Febrile convulsions"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - fever (temperature ≥38°C) is a prerequisite; assess for signs of sepsis or meningitis (e.g., tachycardia, tachypnoea, hypotension).
  • Check neurological examination - assess for focal neurological deficits post-ictally, which may suggest a complex seizure or underlying pathology.
  • Check for signs of meningism - neck stiffness, photophobia, or non-blanching rash are red flags for meningitis. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Signs of meningism in children: what are they and why do they matter clinically?" text="Signs of meningism"][/sc]
  • Check for evidence of injury - tongue biting or incontinence may confirm a true seizure.
  • Check for source of infection - examine ears, throat, chest, and skin for a focus of infection causing the fever.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Febrile convulsions"][/sc]
  • Focal seizure activity: Suggests a complex febrile convulsion or alternative neurological pathology, requiring further investigation.
  • Prolonged seizure (>15 minutes): Indicates a complex febrile convulsion and increases the risk of status epilepticus, necessitating urgent intervention.
  • Recurrence within 24 hours: Defines a complex febrile convulsion and warrants further assessment.
  • Signs of meningitis/encephalitis: Neck stiffness, bulging fontanelle, altered consciousness, or non-blanching rash demand immediate exclusion of central nervous system infection.
  • Age <6 months or >5 years: Febrile seizures outside this typical age range require investigation for other causes of seizures.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Febrile convulsions"][/sc]
  • Infants <6 months: Seizures with fever in this age group are less likely to be simple febrile convulsions and require thorough investigation to exclude serious underlying conditions. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Differential diagnosis of seizures with fever in infants under 6 months: what are they and why do they matter clinically?" text="Seizures with fever in infants <6 months"][/sc]
  • Children >5 years: Febrile seizures are uncommon in this age group; consider alternative diagnoses such as epilepsy or other neurological disorders.
  • Afebrile seizures: Seizures without fever are not febrile convulsions and necessitate a full neurological workup to identify the cause.
  • Febrile status epilepticus: A prolonged febrile seizure (>30 minutes) or recurrent seizures without recovery of consciousness, requiring urgent medical management.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Febrile convulsions"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected febrile convulsion: Clinical assessment to differentiate simple from complex febrile convulsions and exclude central nervous system infection.
  • Severity/risk tools: Consider using a risk stratification tool for recurrent febrile seizures, though no universally adopted MDCalc score exists for initial presentation. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Risk factors for recurrent febrile convulsions: what are they and why do they matter clinically?" text="Risk factors for recurrent febrile convulsions"][/sc]
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Lumbar puncture to exclude meningitis/encephalitis (especially in infants <12 months or those with meningeal signs), blood tests (e.g., FBC, CRP, electrolytes, glucose) to identify infection or metabolic derangement.
  • Imaging - MRI brain: Rarely indicated in simple febrile convulsions; considered in complex cases with focal neurological signs or suspicion of underlying structural brain abnormality.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Febrile convulsions"][/sc]
  • A 15-month-old male presents with a 5-minute generalised tonic-clonic seizure, associated with a temperature of 39.5°C, following a viral upper respiratory tract infection, with rapid post-ictal recovery.
  • A toddler with a viral illness and fever, presenting with a brief, generalised seizure, concerning for a simple febrile convulsion.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Febrile convulsions"][/sc]
  • Meningitis/Encephalitis - presence of meningeal signs, altered consciousness beyond post-ictal phase, or focal neurological deficits.
  • Epilepsy (afebrile seizure) - seizure occurring without fever, or recurrent seizures that are not triggered by fever.
  • Rigors - shivering with fever, but without loss of consciousness or tonic-clonic movements.
  • Breath-holding spells - typically triggered by pain or emotion, followed by apnoea and cyanosis, without true seizure activity.
  • Syncope - transient loss of consciousness due to cerebral hypoperfusion, often with a clear trigger and rapid recovery.
  • Metabolic derangement (e.g., hypoglycaemia, electrolyte imbalance) - identified by blood tests, may present with seizures. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Hypoglycaemia in children presenting with seizures: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Hypoglycaemia in children"][/sc]
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Febrile convulsions"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Ensure airway patency, place child in recovery position, protect from injury, record seizure duration. Assess for red flags and escalate to senior medical staff if complex or prolonged.
  • Definitive management: For simple febrile convulsions, no specific anti-epileptic treatment is usually required. Management focuses on treating the underlying cause of fever.
  • Adjunctive management: Antipyretics (e.g., paracetamol, ibuprofen) may be considered to improve comfort, but do not prevent recurrence of febrile convulsions.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Observe for recurrence or development of red flag features. Advise parents on fever management and what to do if another seizure occurs.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Provide clear safety-netting advice to parents regarding signs of serious illness, recurrence, and when to seek urgent medical attention.
  • Exam pharmacology reference (UKMLA/MRCGP): Benzodiazepines (e.g., buccal midazolam, rectal diazepam) may be considered for prolonged (>5 minutes) or recurrent febrile seizures. Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Febrile convulsions"][/sc]
  • Recurrence: Approximately one-third of children will experience a recurrent febrile convulsion, most commonly within 12-24 months of the initial episode.
  • Febrile status epilepticus: Prolonged febrile seizures (>30 minutes) are rare but can lead to neuronal injury and are associated with a higher risk of subsequent epilepsy.
  • Increased risk of epilepsy: While most children with febrile convulsions do not develop epilepsy, complex febrile convulsions, recurrent episodes, or pre-existing neurological abnormalities slightly increase this risk.
  • Developmental delay: Rarely, very prolonged or frequent febrile seizures, particularly in children with pre-existing developmental issues, may be associated with subtle developmental regression.
