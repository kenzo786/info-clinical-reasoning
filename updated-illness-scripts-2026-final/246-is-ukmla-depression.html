Depression (Mental health) [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Depression (Mental health)"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Depression (Mental health)"][/sc]
  • A common mental health condition characterised by persistent low mood, anhedonia (loss of pleasure), and a range of associated emotional, cognitive, physical, and behavioural symptoms lasting at least two weeks.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the symptoms and signs of depression, assess severity and risk (including suicide), and outline initial management strategies in primary care, including referral pathways.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Depression (Mental health)"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Depression (Mental health)"][/sc]
  • Approximately 1 in 6 adults in the UK experience a common mental health problem like depression or anxiety in any given week (NHS Digital).
  • Women are nearly twice as likely to be diagnosed with depression as men (ONS).
  • Prevalence of moderate to severe depressive symptoms was around 16% in adults aged 16 and over in Great Britain in autumn 2022 (ONS).
  • Risk factors include a history of mental illness, chronic physical health conditions, adverse life events (e.g., bereavement, job loss), social isolation, and certain medications.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Depression (Mental health)"][/sc]
  • Trigger: A complex interplay of genetic predisposition, neurobiological factors (e.g., neurotransmitter dysregulation, particularly serotonin, noradrenaline, and dopamine), psychological stressors, and environmental influences.
  • Progression: Chronic stress can lead to neuroendocrine changes (e.g., HPA axis dysfunction), reduced neuroplasticity, and structural alterations in brain regions involved in mood regulation (e.g., hippocampus, prefrontal cortex). [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Neurotransmitter hypothesis of depression: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Neurotransmitter dysregulation"][/sc]
  • Consequence: These changes manifest as the characteristic symptoms of depression, impacting emotional processing, cognitive function, and physical well-being.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Depression (Mental health)"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Symptoms typically develop gradually over weeks to months, often triggered by a stressful life event or accumulating pressures.
  • Progressive phase: Without intervention, symptoms can become more entrenched and severe, leading to significant functional impairment and increased risk of chronicity, particularly beyond 6 months.
  • Late phase: Episodes can last for several months to years. Recurrence is common, with each subsequent episode increasing the risk of further episodes.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Depression (Mental health)"][/sc]
  • Ask about persistent low mood - differentiating from transient sadness, noting its pervasive nature.
  • Ask about anhedonia (loss of pleasure or interest) - a core symptom, indicating a significant impact on daily life.
  • Ask about changes in sleep patterns (insomnia or hypersomnia) - often an early indicator and contributor to distress.
  • Ask about changes in appetite or weight (increase or decrease) - reflecting neurovegetative symptoms.
  • Ask about fatigue or loss of energy - a common and debilitating symptom, impacting daily functioning.
  • Ask about feelings of worthlessness, guilt, or hopelessness - key cognitive distortions associated with depression, often escalating with severity.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Depression (Mental health)"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - typically normal, but rule out underlying physical illness.
  • Check psychomotor agitation or retardation - observable slowing of movement and speech, or restlessness.
  • Check poor self-care or neglect of appearance - may indicate severity and functional impairment.
  • Check reduced eye contact or flat affect - non-verbal cues reflecting emotional state.
  • Check evidence of self-harm or suicidal ideation - crucial for immediate risk assessment. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Assessing suicide risk in depression: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Suicide risk assessment"][/sc]
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Depression (Mental health)"][/sc]
  • Active suicidal ideation with plan: Indicates immediate and severe risk, requiring urgent mental health assessment and intervention.
  • Psychotic symptoms (e.g., delusions, hallucinations): Suggests severe depressive episode with psychotic features, necessitating specialist psychiatric care.
  • Rapidly worsening symptoms or functional decline: May indicate an escalating crisis, particularly over days to weeks, requiring prompt review.
  • Severe self-neglect or inability to care for self: Suggests a high level of impairment and risk, potentially requiring inpatient admission.
  • New onset depression in older adults: May be a presentation of an underlying physical illness or cognitive decline, warranting thorough investigation.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Depression (Mental health)"][/sc]
  • Older adults: May present with more somatic complaints (e.g., pain, fatigue), cognitive impairment (pseudodementia), or social withdrawal rather than overt sadness.
  • Children and adolescents: Can manifest as irritability, behavioural problems, school refusal, or physical symptoms (e.g., headaches, stomach aches) rather than classic low mood.
  • Perinatal depression: Occurs during pregnancy or within one year postpartum, often complicated by hormonal changes, sleep deprivation, and the demands of new parenthood.
  • Masked depression: Patients may present with anxiety, anger, or physical symptoms, unconsciously or consciously avoiding the emotional aspects of depression. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Pseudodementia vs dementia: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Pseudodementia"][/sc]
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Depression (Mental health)"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected Depression: Clinical assessment based on DSM-5 or ICD-10 criteria. Use of validated screening tools like PHQ-9 or GAD-7 can aid in symptom quantification and monitoring.
  • Severity/risk tools: PHQ-9 (Patient Health Questionnaire-9) for severity and response to treatment. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="PHQ-9 scoring and interpretation: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="PHQ-9 scoring"][/sc] PHQ-9 on MDCalc.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Full blood count, thyroid function tests, electrolytes, B12/folate, liver and renal function tests to rule out organic causes mimicking depression.
  • Serial reassessment: Regular use of PHQ-9 to monitor symptom change and treatment effectiveness.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Depression (Mental health)"][/sc]
  • A 45-year-old female presents with a 3-month history of persistent low mood, anhedonia, significant fatigue, and difficulty sleeping, following a recent bereavement.
  • A middle-aged female with chronic low mood, anhedonia, and neurovegetative symptoms, concerning for a major depressive episode.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Depression (Mental health)"][/sc]
  • Anxiety disorders: Often co-exist with depression, but primary anxiety disorders feature prominent worry and fear rather than pervasive low mood.
  • Bipolar disorder: Characterised by episodes of both depression and mania/hypomania; careful history taking is crucial to identify past elevated mood episodes.
  • Adjustment disorder: Emotional or behavioural symptoms in response to an identifiable stressor, typically resolving within 6 months of the stressor's cessation, and less severe than major depression.
  • Bereavement: Normal grief can present with depressive symptoms, but typically resolves over time and does not meet full criteria for a major depressive episode.
  • Hypothyroidism: Can mimic depressive symptoms (fatigue, low mood, weight gain); thyroid function tests are essential to differentiate.
  • Chronic fatigue syndrome/ME: Shares symptoms like profound fatigue, sleep disturbance, and cognitive difficulties, but typically without the pervasive low mood and anhedonia of depression.
  • Substance misuse: Alcohol or drug use can cause or exacerbate depressive symptoms; a thorough substance use history is vital. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Differentiating depression from adjustment disorder: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Adjustment disorder"][/sc]
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Depression (Mental health)"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Assess and manage immediate risks, particularly suicide risk. Provide supportive listening and safety-netting. Defer to local mental health crisis pathways for urgent assessment if high risk.
  • Definitive management: For mild depression, guided self-help, CBT, or exercise are often considered first-line. For moderate to severe depression, a combination of antidepressant medication (e.g., SSRIs) and high-intensity psychological interventions (e.g., CBT) is typically recommended by NICE.
  • Adjunctive management: Consider social prescribing, support groups, and lifestyle interventions (e.g., sleep hygiene, diet, physical activity) to complement primary treatments.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Regular review of symptoms, side effects, and risk is essential, especially in the initial weeks of treatment.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Patients should be advised on when and how to seek urgent help if symptoms worsen or new risks emerge. Referral to secondary mental health services is often warranted for severe, complex, or refractory depression.
  • Exam pharmacology reference (UKMLA/MRCGP): Selective Serotonin Reuptake Inhibitors (SSRIs) are often considered first-line pharmacological treatment for moderate to severe depression. Other classes include Serotonin-Noradrenaline Reuptake Inhibitors (SNRIs) like [sc name="jitl-medication" Q="Venlafaxine use in Depression" text="venlafaxine"][/sc], tricyclic antidepressants (TCAs), and mirtazapine. Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Depression (Mental health)"][/sc]
  • Suicide: The most serious complication, arising from profound hopelessness and despair, particularly when symptoms are severe or rapidly escalating.
  • Self-harm: Non-fatal self-injurious behaviour, often used as a coping mechanism for intense emotional pain, indicating significant distress and increased risk.
  • Chronic depression (Dysthymia/Persistent Depressive Disorder): Prolonged depressive symptoms lasting for years, leading to significant impairment in quality of life and functioning.
  • Comorbidity: Increased risk of developing other mental health conditions (e.g., anxiety disorders, substance use disorders) and physical health problems (e.g., cardiovascular disease, diabetes).
  • Functional impairment: Significant difficulties in maintaining employment, education, social relationships, and daily activities, leading to social isolation and economic hardship.
