Infective keratitis [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Infective keratitis"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Infective keratitis"][/sc]
  • Infective keratitis is an inflammation of the cornea caused by various microorganisms, including bacteria, fungi, viruses, and parasites, often leading to significant ocular morbidity and potential vision loss.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical features, risk factors, and potential severity of infective keratitis to facilitate timely diagnosis and appropriate initial management.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Infective keratitis"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Infective keratitis"][/sc]
  • In the UK, the incidence of infective keratitis associated with contact lens wear ranges between 1 and 85 per million wearers, with regional variations [4].
  • Contact lens wear is a significant risk factor, accounting for 19-42% of culture-proven cases, particularly due to poor hygiene and extended wear [9, 12].
  • Other important risk factors include corneal trauma, pre-existing ocular surface diseases, and previous corneal surgery [9, 14].
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Infective keratitis"][/sc]
  • Infective keratitis is triggered by the invasion and proliferation of microorganisms (bacteria, fungi, viruses, or parasites) into the corneal stroma, often following a breach in the corneal epithelium due to trauma, contact lens wear, or pre-existing ocular surface disease.
  • The invading pathogens elicit a robust inflammatory response, leading to corneal oedema, infiltration of immune cells, and tissue destruction, which can rapidly progress to .
  • The consequence of uncontrolled infection and inflammation is progressive corneal damage, potentially resulting in corneal scarring, thinning, perforation, and irreversible vision loss if not promptly and effectively treated.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Infective keratitis"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Symptoms typically develop rapidly, often within 24-48 hours of infection, with initial signs of inflammation and discomfort.
  • Progressive phase: Without prompt and effective treatment, the infection can progress quickly, leading to worsening corneal inflammation, ulceration, and potential stromal involvement, particularly beyond a few days.
  • Late phase: If untreated or poorly managed, the condition can lead to severe complications such as corneal scarring, thinning, or perforation, with irreversible vision loss potentially occurring within weeks.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Infective keratitis"][/sc]
  • Ask about eye pain - often sudden and intense, indicating corneal nerve involvement and severity of inflammation.
  • Ask about blurred or decreased vision - a critical symptom suggesting central corneal involvement and potential threat to sight.
  • Ask about photophobia - severe light sensitivity is common and reflects significant corneal irritation and inflammation.
  • Ask about excessive tearing or discharge - purulent discharge is highly suggestive of bacterial infection, while watery discharge may indicate viral aetiology.
  • Ask about foreign body sensation - a persistent feeling of something in the eye can be an early indicator of corneal surface disruption.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Infective keratitis"][/sc]
  • Check visual acuity - decreased acuity implies central corneal involvement and potential for permanent vision loss.
  • Check conjunctival injection and lid oedema - indicates inflammation and irritation of the ocular surface.
  • Check corneal appearance - presence of an infiltrate, ulceration, or suggests active infection and severity.
  • Check ocular discharge - character (purulent, watery, mucoid) can help differentiate between bacterial, viral, or other causes.
  • Check vital signs (temperature, pulse, blood pressure) - typically normal in isolated keratitis, but fever may indicate severe infection or systemic involvement, warranting broader investigation.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Infective keratitis"][/sc]
  • Marked eye pain out of proportion to clinical signs - suggests significant corneal nerve involvement or potentially a more aggressive pathogen like Acanthamoeba.
  • Rapidly worsening vision - indicates central corneal involvement and a high risk of permanent visual impairment.
  • Presence of a corneal infiltrate or ulceration - a direct sign of active infection and tissue destruction, requiring urgent ophthalmological assessment.
  • Contact lens wear in a symptomatic patient - significantly increases the risk of bacterial or Acanthamoeba keratitis, which can be rapidly sight-threatening.
  • Hypopyon - indicates severe intraocular inflammation and a high bacterial load, often associated with severe keratitis.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Infective keratitis"][/sc]
  • Immunocompromised patients: May present with infections from unusual pathogens (e.g., fungi like Candida albicans, or viruses like VZV/HSV) and experience more severe, rapidly progressive disease due to impaired immune response.
  • Older adults: Often present with more severe forms of keratitis due to age-related changes in corneal structure and immune function, potentially leading to delayed diagnosis and poorer outcomes.
  • Acanthamoeba keratitis: Characterised by disproportionate pain compared to clinical signs, often with a prolonged course and can be misdiagnosed, particularly in contact lens wearers.
  • Polymicrobial infections: Co-infection with multiple bacterial species or a combination of bacteria and viruses can lead to a more complex and resistant clinical picture, challenging standard treatment approaches.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Infective keratitis"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected Infective keratitis: Comprehensive ophthalmic examination including visual acuity, slit-lamp biomicroscopy with fluorescein staining to assess corneal integrity and identify infiltrates or ulcers. Corneal scraping for microscopy (Gram stain, KOH wet mount) and culture (bacterial, fungal, viral) is crucial for pathogen identification and guiding targeted therapy.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Consider anterior segment optical coherence tomography (AS-OCT) to evaluate corneal depth and morphology of infiltrates, particularly for differentiating infectious from non-infectious causes or identifying fungal/Acanthamoeba cysts.
  • Imaging - In vivo confocal microscopy (IVCM): May be used in specialist centres to directly visualise pathogens like Acanthamoeba cysts or fungal elements, especially when cultures are negative or diagnosis is challenging.
  • Serial reassessment: Regular clinical review (daily initially) is essential to monitor treatment response, assess for progression or complications, and adjust therapy as needed.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Infective keratitis"][/sc]
  • A 28-year-old female contact lens wearer presents with acute onset of severe left eye pain, redness, photophobia, and blurred vision for the past 24 hours, noting a yellowish discharge. Examination reveals significant conjunctival injection, a central corneal infiltrate, and reduced visual acuity in the affected eye.
  • Acute, unilateral, painful, vision-threatening corneal inflammation in a contact lens wearer, likely infectious in aetiology.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Infective keratitis"][/sc]
  • Viral Keratitis (e.g., Herpes Simplex Keratitis) - often presents with characteristic dendritic ulcers on fluorescein staining and a history of recurrent episodes.
  • Fungal Keratitis - typically has a slower onset, feathery-edged infiltrates, and may be associated with trauma involving vegetative matter.
  • Acanthamoeba Keratitis - characterised by severe pain disproportionate to clinical signs, radial keratoneuritis, and a ring infiltrate in later stages, often in contact lens wearers.
  • Sterile Corneal Ulcer/Marginal Keratitis - usually peripheral, less severe pain, and often associated with staphylococcal blepharitis without active infection.
  • Corneal Abrasion/Foreign Body - history of acute trauma, sudden onset of pain, and linear or punctate staining without significant infiltrate unless secondarily infected.
  • Dry Eye Disease - chronic symptoms of irritation and fluctuating vision, diffuse punctate staining, but typically lacks focal infiltrates or severe photophobia.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Infective keratitis"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Urgent referral to ophthalmology for assessment and management. Discontinuation of contact lens wear. Corneal scraping for microscopy and culture should be considered prior to initiating antimicrobial therapy to guide targeted treatment.
  • Definitive management: Initiation of topical antimicrobial therapy based on suspected pathogen and local resistance patterns. This often includes broad-spectrum topical antibiotics (e.g., fluoroquinolones or fortified antibiotics) administered frequently (e.g., hourly initially). Antifungal or antiviral agents may be used if fungal or viral aetiology is suspected.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Close clinical monitoring is essential, typically with daily ophthalmological review in the initial stages, to assess treatment response, identify complications, and adjust therapy as indicated.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Escalation to inpatient care or surgical intervention (e.g., corneal transplant) may be required for severe, non-responsive, or perforating cases. Patients should be advised on red flag symptoms for immediate re-presentation.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Infective keratitis"][/sc]
  • Corneal scarring: Persistent inflammation and tissue remodelling can lead to permanent corneal opacities, significantly impairing vision and potentially requiring corneal transplantation. This can develop over weeks to months if the initial infection is not adequately controlled.
  • Corneal perforation: Severe, untreated keratitis can lead to thinning and eventual perforation of the cornea, a true ophthalmic emergency that can result in endophthalmitis and permanent loss of the eye.
  • Endophthalmitis: Intraocular extension of the infection, which can occur with corneal perforation, leading to severe inflammation within the eye and a very poor prognosis for vision.
  • Glaucoma: Secondary glaucoma can develop due to inflammation and obstruction of the trabecular meshwork, leading to elevated intraocular pressure and optic nerve damage if not managed.
  • Cataract formation: Chronic inflammation or the use of long-term topical steroids can accelerate the development of cataracts, further compromising vision.
