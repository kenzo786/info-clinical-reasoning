Breast cysts [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Breast cysts"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Breast cysts"][/sc]
  • Breast cysts are fluid-filled sacs within the breast tissue, typically benign, and a common cause of breast lumps in women.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical presentation and initial management of benign breast conditions, including breast cysts.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Breast cysts"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Breast cysts"][/sc]
  • Breast cysts are most common in women aged 35-50 years, with prevalence estimated to be around 7% in the UK adult female population (ONS data).
  • Risk factors include hormonal fluctuations, particularly during perimenopause, and a family history of benign breast disease.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Breast cysts"][/sc]
  • Trigger: Hormonal imbalances, particularly oestrogen dominance, lead to proliferation of mammary ducts and glands.
  • Progression: Blockage of these ducts results in fluid accumulation and the formation of cysts, which can vary in size and number.
  • Consequence: The expanding fluid-filled sacs can cause localised pain and palpable lumps within the breast tissue.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Breast cysts"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Often presents as a rapidly appearing, tender lump, particularly in the premenstrual phase.
  • Progressive phase: Cysts may fluctuate in size and tenderness with the menstrual cycle, persisting for several weeks or months.
  • Late phase: Larger or persistent cysts may cause ongoing discomfort and require intervention, particularly beyond 6-8 weeks.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Breast cysts"][/sc]
  • Ask about breast lump - often described as smooth, mobile, and well-defined, which helps differentiate from malignancy.
  • Ask about breast pain or tenderness - commonly cyclical, worsening before menstruation, indicating hormonal influence.
  • Ask about nipple discharge - typically clear or straw-coloured, which can be associated with benign breast changes.
  • Ask about changes in lump size - fluctuation with menstrual cycle is characteristic of cysts.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Breast cysts"][/sc]
  • Check for a palpable, smooth, rubbery, or firm lump - often mobile and non-fixed to surrounding tissue.
  • Check for tenderness on palpation - localised pain upon examination is common.
  • Check for skin changes - typically no overlying skin tethering, dimpling, or redness, distinguishing from inflammatory or malignant processes.
  • Check vital signs - usually normal, as breast cysts are benign and do not cause systemic illness.
  • Check for axillary lymphadenopathy - typically absent, as cysts are not associated with lymphatic spread [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Axillary lymphadenopathy: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="axillary lymphadenopathy"][/sc].
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Breast cysts"][/sc]
  • Fixed or irregular lump - suggests potential malignancy rather than a simple cyst.
  • Skin tethering or dimpling - indicates possible underlying malignancy invading the skin.
  • Bloody nipple discharge - a concerning feature that warrants urgent investigation for malignancy.
  • Persistent lump post-menstruation - a lump that does not resolve or change with the menstrual cycle, particularly beyond 6 weeks.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Breast cysts"][/sc]
  • Older adults: Postmenopausal women can still develop cysts, though less commonly, and any new lump warrants careful investigation due to increased malignancy risk.
  • Pregnancy/Lactation: Hormonal changes can lead to rapid cyst formation or enlargement, which may be confused with other pregnancy-related breast changes.
  • Large or multiple cysts: Can present as diffuse nodularity or a dominant lump, making clinical differentiation from fibroadenoma or malignancy challenging.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Breast cysts"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected Breast cysts: Triple assessment (clinical examination, imaging, and biopsy if indicated).
  • Imaging - Ultrasound: Primary imaging modality for women under 40 or pregnant/lactating, to differentiate solid from cystic lesions.
  • Imaging - Mammography: Used for women over 40 or if ultrasound is inconclusive, to screen for malignancy.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Fine needle aspiration (FNA) or core biopsy for cytological or histological confirmation, especially if the lesion is complex or suspicious.
  • Serial reassessment: Clinical review and repeat imaging may be considered for simple cysts that are symptomatic or large, to monitor for changes.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Breast cysts"][/sc]
  • A 42-year-old female presents with a 3-week history of a mobile, tender lump in her left breast, which fluctuates with her menstrual cycle. Clinical examination reveals a smooth, well-defined, non-fixed lump with no associated skin changes or lymphadenopathy.
  • Middle-aged female with cyclical, mobile, tender breast lump, suggestive of benign aetiology.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Breast cysts"][/sc]
  • Fibroadenoma - typically firm, rubbery, mobile, but non-tender and does not fluctuate with menstrual cycle.
  • Breast cancer - often presents as a hard, irregular, fixed lump, with potential skin changes or lymphadenopathy.
  • Breast abscess - associated with signs of infection like redness, warmth, and fever, often in lactating women.
  • Fat necrosis - history of trauma, often presents as a firm, irregular lump, but typically painless.
  • Duct ectasia - may cause nipple discharge and periareolar lump, but usually not a discrete cyst.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Breast cysts"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Reassurance and education regarding the benign nature of most breast cysts.
  • Definitive management: Aspiration of symptomatic cysts for pain relief and diagnostic confirmation; simple cysts often do not require intervention.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Regular self-breast examination and clinical review for persistent or recurrent cysts.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Refer to breast clinic for triple assessment if features are atypical, persistent, or suspicious for malignancy.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Breast cysts"][/sc]
  • Infection: Rarely, a cyst can become infected, leading to pain, redness, and warmth, requiring antibiotic treatment.
  • Recurrence: Cysts can recur after aspiration, necessitating repeat procedures or ongoing monitoring.
  • Anxiety: The presence of breast lumps, even benign ones, can cause significant patient anxiety and distress.
  • Cosmetic concerns: Large or multiple cysts can cause visible lumps or asymmetry, leading to cosmetic dissatisfaction.
