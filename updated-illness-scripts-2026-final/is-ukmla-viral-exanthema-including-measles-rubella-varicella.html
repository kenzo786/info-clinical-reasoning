kb-accordion kb-parent"> Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Viral exanthema (including measles, rubella, varicella)"][/sc]
  • Initial viral replication in the respiratory tract or local lymph nodes leads to viraemia and systemic spread.
  • The virus then infects skin cells, triggering an immune response that results in the characteristic rash.
  • Immune-mediated inflammation and cellular damage in the skin contribute to the appearance of macules, papules, or vesicles.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Viral exanthema (including measles, rubella, varicella)"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Prodromal symptoms such as fever, malaise, and catarrhal symptoms typically precede the rash by several days.
  • Progressive phase: The characteristic rash emerges and spreads, often becoming more prominent over a few days, particularly beyond 3-5 days.
  • Late phase: Resolution of the rash and systemic symptoms usually occurs within 7-10 days, though complications may manifest later.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Viral exanthema (including measles, rubella, varicella)"][/sc]
  • Ask about fever - to assess the severity of systemic illness and potential for complications.
  • Ask about rash onset and progression - to help differentiate between various viral exanthems and other dermatological conditions.
  • Ask about cough, coryza, and conjunctivitis - these are classic prodromal symptoms of measles (the '3 Cs') and aid in early diagnosis.
  • Ask about pruritus - to gauge discomfort and differentiate from non-viral rashes, as some viral exanthems are intensely itchy (e.g., varicella).
  • Ask about recent contacts and vaccination status - crucial for epidemiological assessment and public health management.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Viral exanthema (including measles, rubella, varicella)"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - to identify signs of systemic illness, dehydration, or potential sepsis.
  • Check rash morphology and distribution - maculopapular, vesicular, or petechial patterns and their spread are key discriminators.
  • Check for Koplik's spots - pathognomonic for measles, appearing as small white spots on the buccal mucosa.
  • Check for lymphadenopathy - particularly posterior auricular and suboccipital nodes in rubella.
  • Check for signs of respiratory distress or neurological changes - indicating potential severe complications.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Viral exanthema (including measles, rubella, varicella)"][/sc]
  • Severe respiratory distress - suggests viral pneumonia, a serious complication of measles or varicella.
  • Altered consciousness or seizures - may indicate encephalitis, a rare but severe neurological complication.
  • Haemorrhagic rash - can signify severe thrombocytopenia or disseminated intravascular coagulation, particularly in atypical presentations.
  • Persistent high fever - beyond the expected course, potentially indicating secondary bacterial infection or other complications.
  • Immunocompromised status - individuals are at higher risk of severe or prolonged illness and atypical presentations.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Viral exanthema (including measles, rubella, varicella)"][/sc]
  • Immunocompromised patients: May present with an attenuated or absent rash, but severe visceral involvement, making diagnosis challenging.
  • Vaccinated individuals (modified measles): Can develop a milder form of measles with fewer spots and less severe systemic symptoms, often due to waning immunity.
  • Neonates: Varicella can be severe in neonates if the mother develops chickenpox around delivery, potentially leading to congenital varicella syndrome or neonatal varicella.
  • Adults: Viral exanthems, particularly varicella, tend to be more severe in adults with a higher risk of complications like pneumonia.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Viral exanthema (including measles, rubella, varicella)"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected viral exanthema: Clinical diagnosis is often sufficient, but viral swabs (throat, nasopharyngeal) or urine samples for PCR can confirm measles or rubella.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Full blood count, C-reactive protein, and blood cultures may be considered to rule out bacterial sepsis.
  • Imaging - Chest X-ray: Indicated if there are signs of respiratory compromise to assess for pneumonia.
  • Validated score: The Paediatric Early Warning Score (PEWS) on MDCalc can be used for risk stratification in unwell children.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Viral exanthema (including measles, rubella, varicella)"][/sc]
  • A 4-year-old child presents with a 3-day history of fever, cough, and conjunctivitis, followed by a spreading maculopapular rash that started on the face and is now generalised.
  • A febrile, unvaccinated child with a characteristic prodrome and a centrifugal, erythematous, maculopapular eruption.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Viral exanthema (including measles, rubella, varicella)"][/sc]
  • Scarlet fever - often associated with a sandpaper-like rash and strawberry tongue, typically bacterial.
  • Kawasaki disease - characterised by persistent fever, conjunctivitis, rash, and lymphadenopathy, but often with mucocutaneous changes.
  • Drug eruption - usually lacks a prodrome and may have a more abrupt onset, often linked to new medication.
  • Erythema infectiosum (Fifth disease) - presents with a 'slapped cheek' appearance and a lacy rash on the trunk and limbs.
  • Roseola infantum (Sixth disease) - high fever for 3-5 days followed by a rash as the fever subsides, typically in infants.
  • Meningococcal disease - rapidly progressive purpuric or petechial rash, often with signs of sepsis, a medical emergency.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Viral exanthema (including measles, rubella, varicella)"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Ensure adequate hydration and manage fever with antipyretics such as paracetamol or ibuprofen.
  • Definitive management: Most viral exanthems are self-limiting and require supportive care; specific antiviral agents may be considered for severe varicella in at-risk groups.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Observe for signs of complications such as pneumonia, encephalitis, or secondary bacterial infections.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Advise parents on red flag symptoms requiring urgent medical review, such as worsening respiratory distress or altered consciousness.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Viral exanthema (including measles, rubella, varicella)"][/sc]
  • Pneumonia: A common and serious complication of measles and varicella, often viral but can be complicated by bacterial superinfection, impacting respiratory function.
  • Encephalitis: A rare but severe neurological complication, particularly of measles and varicella, leading to inflammation of the brain parenchyma and potential long-term neurological sequelae.
  • Otitis media: A frequent bacterial complication of measles, occurring due to impaired immune function and mucosal damage, leading to ear pain and hearing impairment.
  • Dehydration: Can result from fever, reduced oral intake, and vomiting/diarrhoea, especially in younger children, necessitating fluid management.
  • Congenital Rubella Syndrome: If rubella infection occurs during early pregnancy, it can lead to severe birth defects, including cardiac anomalies, deafness, and cataracts.
