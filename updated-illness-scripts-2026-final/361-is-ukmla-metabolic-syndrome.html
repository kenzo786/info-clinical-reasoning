Metabolic syndrome [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Metabolic syndrome"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Metabolic syndrome"][/sc]
  • Metabolic syndrome is a cluster of conditions occurring together that increase the risk of heart disease, stroke, and type 2 diabetes, primarily driven by insulin resistance and central obesity.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the components of metabolic syndrome, understand its underlying pathophysiology, and identify patients at increased risk of cardiovascular disease and type 2 diabetes, advising on appropriate lifestyle modifications.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Metabolic syndrome"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Metabolic syndrome"][/sc]
  • Approximately one in four adults in the UK are thought to have metabolic syndrome, with prevalence increasing with age. (Heart UK)
  • Insulin resistance is commonly associated with metabolic syndrome, which significantly increases the risk of type 2 diabetes and cardiovascular disease. (NICE CKS)
  • Key risk factors include obesity (especially central adiposity), physical inactivity, unhealthy diet, increasing age, and genetic predisposition.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Metabolic syndrome"][/sc]
  • Trigger: Central obesity, particularly increased [sc name="jitl-query" Q="visceral adiposity: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="visceral adiposity" ][/sc], leads to chronic low-grade inflammation and altered adipokine secretion.
  • Progression: This often results in [sc name="jitl-query" Q="insulin resistance: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="insulin resistance" ][/sc], where target cells fail to respond adequately to insulin, leading to compensatory hyperinsulinemia and impaired glucose uptake.
  • Consequence: The combination of insulin resistance, dyslipidaemia (high triglycerides, low HDL), hypertension, and impaired glucose metabolism creates a pro-inflammatory and pro-thrombotic state, accelerating atherosclerosis and increasing the risk of type 2 diabetes and cardiovascular events.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Metabolic syndrome"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Often asymptomatic, developing insidiously over years with gradual weight gain, particularly around the abdomen, and subtle changes in blood pressure, glucose, and lipid profiles.
  • Progressive phase: As insulin resistance worsens, individuals may develop pre-diabetes or type 2 diabetes, and hypertension and dyslipidaemia become more pronounced, increasing the risk of end-organ damage.
  • Late phase: Sustained metabolic dysregulation leads to macrovascular complications such as coronary artery disease, stroke, and peripheral artery disease, as well as microvascular complications associated with diabetes.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Metabolic syndrome"][/sc]
  • Ask about increased abdominal girth - often the most visible sign, indicating central adiposity which is a core component.
  • Ask about fatigue or lethargy - non-specific but can be associated with insulin resistance and developing glucose intolerance.
  • Ask about increased thirst and urination - suggestive of developing hyperglycaemia, a component of metabolic syndrome.
  • Ask about blurred vision - a potential symptom of poorly controlled hyperglycaemia or early diabetic retinopathy.
  • Ask about family history of diabetes or cardiovascular disease - important for risk stratification and early screening.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Metabolic syndrome"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - particularly elevated blood pressure (hypertension is a diagnostic criterion).
  • Check waist circumference - a key measure of central obesity, correlating with [sc name="jitl-query" Q="abdominal adiposity: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="abdominal adiposity" ][/sc] and insulin resistance.
  • Check for acanthosis nigricans - darkened, velvety skin patches, especially in skin folds, indicating severe insulin resistance.
  • Check for signs of dyslipidaemia - such as xanthelasma or corneal arcus, though less common.
  • Check for peripheral oedema - may indicate early cardiovascular or renal compromise, often associated with long-standing hypertension.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Metabolic syndrome"][/sc]
  • Acute chest pain or dyspnoea: Suggestive of acute coronary syndrome or heart failure, which are serious complications of metabolic syndrome.
  • Sudden neurological deficit: Indicative of a cerebrovascular event (stroke), a major complication in individuals with metabolic syndrome.
  • Rapid onset of polyuria, polydipsia, and weight loss: May signify new-onset or decompensated type 2 diabetes, requiring urgent management.
  • Severe hypertension (e.g., >180/110 mmHg) with end-organ damage: Hypertensive crisis, necessitating immediate intervention to prevent further damage.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Metabolic syndrome"][/sc]
  • Lean individuals: Metabolic syndrome can occur in individuals with a normal BMI but high [sc name="jitl-query" Q="visceral fat: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="visceral fat" ][/sc] and insulin resistance, often termed 'TOFI' (Thin Outside, Fat Inside).
  • Children and adolescents: Increasing prevalence, often presenting with obesity, acanthosis nigricans, and early signs of dyslipidaemia or hypertension, requiring early intervention.
  • Women with Polycystic Ovary Syndrome (PCOS): High incidence of insulin resistance and central obesity, predisposing them to metabolic syndrome components at a younger age.
  • Individuals on certain medications: Atypical antipsychotics or corticosteroids can induce weight gain, dyslipidaemia, and glucose intolerance, leading to drug-induced metabolic syndrome.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Metabolic syndrome"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected metabolic syndrome:
    • Fasting plasma glucose (or HbA1c) - to assess for impaired glucose regulation or diabetes.
    • Lipid profile (fasting triglycerides, HDL cholesterol) - to identify dyslipidaemia.
    • Blood pressure measurement - to detect hypertension.
    • Waist circumference measurement - to assess central obesity.
  • Severity/risk tools:
    • QRISK3 or JBS3 - for cardiovascular risk assessment.
    • HOMA-IR on MDCalc - Homeostatic Model Assessment for Insulin Resistance (research tool, not routinely used clinically).
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible:
    • Thyroid function tests - to exclude hypothyroidism, which can mimic some metabolic symptoms.
    • Liver function tests - to assess for non-alcoholic fatty liver disease (NAFLD), a common comorbidity.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Metabolic syndrome"][/sc]
  • A 55-year-old male with a 5-year history of increasing abdominal obesity, recently diagnosed hypertension, and elevated fasting glucose presents for routine review, expressing concerns about his cardiovascular health.
  • Middle-aged male with central obesity, hypertension, and dysglycaemia, concerning for metabolic syndrome and increased cardiovascular risk.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Metabolic syndrome"][/sc]
  • Cushing's Syndrome - distinguished by characteristic physical features (e.g., moon facies, buffalo hump, striae) and specific endocrine tests, though it can present with metabolic derangements.
  • Hypothyroidism - can cause weight gain, fatigue, and dyslipidaemia, but typically presents with low thyroid hormone levels and is managed with hormone replacement.
  • Type 2 Diabetes Mellitus (T2DM) - while a component of metabolic syndrome, T2DM is a distinct diagnosis when glucose criteria are met, often requiring specific anti-diabetic medications.
  • Essential Hypertension - hypertension is a component, but isolated essential hypertension lacks the cluster of other metabolic abnormalities.
  • Familial Hyperlipidaemia - genetic disorders causing severe dyslipidaemia, often without the full spectrum of metabolic syndrome components or insulin resistance.
  • Non-alcoholic Fatty Liver Disease (NAFLD) - often co-exists with metabolic syndrome, but can occur independently, diagnosed by imaging and liver biopsy.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Metabolic syndrome"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Recognition of the cluster of risk factors and initiation of comprehensive lifestyle interventions to mitigate progression and reduce cardiovascular risk.
  • Definitive management: Primarily lifestyle modification focusing on dietary changes (e.g., Mediterranean diet, reduced saturated fats and refined carbohydrates) and increased physical activity (e.g., 150 minutes moderate-intensity aerobic activity per week).
  • Adjunctive management: Pharmacological interventions may support management of individual components, such as antihypertensives for blood pressure control, statins for dyslipidaemia, and metformin for impaired glucose tolerance or type 2 diabetes.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Regular monitoring of weight, waist circumference, blood pressure, fasting glucose/HbA1c, and lipid profile to assess response to interventions and detect progression.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Patients should be advised on symptoms of acute cardiovascular events or worsening diabetes, and when to seek urgent medical attention.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Metabolic syndrome"][/sc]
  • Cardiovascular Disease (CVD): The cumulative effect of hypertension, dyslipidaemia, and insulin resistance significantly accelerates atherosclerosis, leading to coronary artery disease, myocardial infarction, and stroke over years.
  • Type 2 Diabetes Mellitus (T2DM): Chronic insulin resistance eventually overwhelms pancreatic beta-cell function, resulting in overt T2DM, typically developing over several years if not managed.
  • Non-alcoholic Fatty Liver Disease (NAFLD) / Non-alcoholic Steatohepatitis (NASH): Often co-exists with metabolic syndrome, progressing from simple steatosis to inflammation and fibrosis over decades, potentially leading to cirrhosis and hepatocellular carcinoma.
  • Chronic Kidney Disease (CKD): Long-standing hypertension and diabetes contribute to progressive renal damage, manifesting as proteinuria and declining glomerular filtration rate over many years.
  • Obstructive Sleep Apnoea (OSA): Strongly associated with obesity, exacerbating metabolic dysfunction and increasing cardiovascular risk.
