Vasa praevia (Pregnancy and the puerperium) [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Vasa praevia (Pregnancy and the puerperium)"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Vasa praevia (Pregnancy and the puerperium)"][/sc]
  • Vasa praevia is a rare but serious obstetric complication where unprotected fetal blood vessels cross the internal cervical os, lying between the presenting fetal part and the cervix. These vessels are vulnerable to rupture during labour or membrane rupture, leading to rapid fetal exsanguination [1].
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the risk factors for vasa praevia, understand its antenatal diagnosis and the critical importance of planned delivery to prevent catastrophic fetal haemorrhage.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Vasa praevia (Pregnancy and the puerperium)"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Vasa praevia"][/sc]
  • Vasa praevia is a rare condition, affecting approximately 1 in 1,200 to 1 in 5,000 pregnancies globally [2]. Precise UK-specific incidence data is limited, with the UK National Screening Committee noting that the exact number of affected babies in the UK is unknown [3].
  • Risk factors for vasa praevia include velamentous cord insertion, bilobed or succenturiate-lobed placentas, low-lying placenta (placenta praevia), multiple pregnancies, and pregnancies conceived via in vitro fertilisation (IVF) [2].
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Vasa praevia (Pregnancy and the puerperium)"][/sc]
  • Vasa praevia is characterised by fetal blood vessels traversing the fetal membranes over the internal cervical os, unprotected by placental tissue or Wharton\'s jelly, making them vulnerable to compression or rupture [1].
  • During labour, or with spontaneous or artificial rupture of membranes, these unprotected vessels are susceptible to tearing or compression, particularly if located directly over the cervix or under the fetal presenting part [1].
  • Rupture of these vessels leads to rapid fetal exsanguination, resulting in acute fetal compromise, severe anaemia, and potentially death due to the small total blood volume of the fetus [1]. Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Vasa praevia (Pregnancy and the puerperium)"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Vasa praevia is typically asymptomatic during the antenatal period. Diagnosis is usually made incidentally during routine or targeted ultrasound scans, particularly transvaginal Doppler ultrasound, often in the second or third trimester [2].
  • Progressive phase: If undiagnosed antenatally, the condition manifests acutely during labour, especially with spontaneous or artificial rupture of membranes. This event can lead to the tearing of the unprotected fetal vessels, initiating rapid fetal blood loss [1].
  • Late phase: Following vessel rupture, rapid fetal exsanguination occurs, leading to acute fetal distress, severe bradycardia, and potentially fetal death within minutes if not immediately identified and managed [1].
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Vasa praevia (Pregnancy and the puerperium)"][/sc]
  • Ask about vaginal bleeding - particularly if it is painless and occurs immediately following rupture of membranes, as this is highly suggestive of vasa praevia and fetal vessel rupture [1].
  • Ask about the colour of any vaginal fluid - dark-red or port-wine coloured fluid after membrane rupture indicates fetal blood loss, a critical discriminator [1].
  • Ask about fetal movements - a sudden decrease or absence of fetal movements may indicate acute fetal compromise due to exsanguination [1].
  • Ask about any prior risk factors - such as a history of IVF, multiple pregnancy, low-lying placenta, or known velamentous cord insertion, as these increase the index of suspicion [2].
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Vasa praevia (Pregnancy and the puerperium)"][/sc]
  • Check fetal heart rate - acute fetal bradycardia or a sinusoidal heart rate pattern immediately following membrane rupture is a critical sign of fetal exsanguination and distress [1]. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Fetal bradycardia: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="fetal bradycardia"][/sc]
  • Check for pulsating vessels on vaginal examination - rarely, pulsating fetal vessels may be palpable through the membranes or in the vagina after membrane rupture, which is diagnostic [1].
  • Check maternal vital signs - while the primary blood loss is fetal, severe fetal compromise can indirectly affect maternal well-being, though direct maternal hypovolaemia is uncommon unless there is co-existing placental abruption or other causes of maternal haemorrhage.
  • Check cardiotocography (CTG) - to identify signs of acute fetal distress, such as prolonged decelerations or a sinusoidal pattern, which necessitate immediate intervention [1].
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Vasa praevia (Pregnancy and the puerperium)"][/sc]
  • Painless vaginal bleeding after membrane rupture: Indicates fetal vessel rupture and immediate threat to fetal life, requiring urgent intervention [1].
  • Acute fetal bradycardia or sinusoidal CTG pattern: Suggests severe fetal compromise due to exsanguination, demanding immediate delivery [1].
  • Palpable pulsating vessels on vaginal examination: Direct evidence of vasa praevia, necessitating urgent delivery [1].
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Vasa praevia (Pregnancy and the puerperium)"][/sc]
  • Antenatal diagnosis: Vasa praevia is often asymptomatic and diagnosed incidentally during routine or targeted antenatal ultrasound scans, particularly transvaginal Doppler ultrasound. This allows for planned delivery and significantly improves fetal outcomes [1].
  • Undiagnosed cases presenting in labour: In cases where vasa praevia is not diagnosed antenatally, the first presentation is typically during labour with sudden, painless vaginal bleeding and acute fetal distress (e.g., bradycardia) immediately following spontaneous or artificial rupture of membranes. This is a medical emergency [1].
  • Associated with placental abnormalities: The presentation may be atypical when associated with other placental anomalies such as bilobed or succenturiate-lobed placentas, or velamentous cord insertion, which are risk factors for vasa praevia and may influence the timing and nature of symptoms [2]. Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Vasa praevia (Pregnancy and the puerperium)"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected vasa praevia: Transvaginal colour Doppler ultrasound is the most accurate method for antenatal diagnosis, allowing visualisation of fetal vessels crossing the internal cervical os [1]. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Transvaginal Doppler ultrasound in pregnancy: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Transvaginal Doppler ultrasound"][/sc]
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Further imaging, such as transabdominal ultrasound, may be used to assess placental location and cord insertion, especially if initial scans are inconclusive or suggest other placental abnormalities [1].
  • Serial reassessment: In cases of suspected or confirmed vasa praevia, serial ultrasound scans may be performed to monitor cervical length and the position of the vasa praevia relative to the cervix, particularly in the third trimester [1].

  • Gravid female with antepartum haemorrhage and acute fetal distress, concerning for vasa praevia.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Vasa praevia (Pregnancy and the puerperium)"][/sc]
  • Placenta praevia: Typically presents with painless vaginal bleeding, but usually without acute fetal distress unless there is significant maternal haemorrhage or abruption. Bleeding is maternal in origin [1].
  • Placental abruption: Characterised by painful vaginal bleeding, uterine tenderness, and often maternal compromise (e.g., shock) and fetal distress. Bleeding is maternal in origin [1].
  • Local cervical/vaginal causes (e.g., cervical ectropion, cervicitis, vaginal trauma): Bleeding is usually less profuse, often associated with intercourse or examination, and not typically accompanied by acute fetal distress [1].
  • Uterine rupture: Presents with sudden severe abdominal pain, maternal shock, and acute fetal distress (e.g., sudden bradycardia). Fetal vessels are not typically involved in the bleeding [1].
  • Marginal sinus rupture: Painless vaginal bleeding, but usually self-limiting and not associated with acute fetal distress or vessel rupture over the os [1].
  • Velamentous cord insertion without vasa praevia: Fetal vessels insert into the membranes but do not cross the internal os. While a risk factor for vasa praevia, it does not cause bleeding unless the vessels are torn [2]. Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Vasa praevia (Pregnancy and the puerperium)"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: If vasa praevia is diagnosed during labour with membrane rupture and fetal distress, immediate recognition and urgent delivery via caesarean section is paramount to prevent catastrophic fetal haemorrhage [1]. Supportive care for the mother may be required.
  • Definitive management: For antenatally diagnosed vasa praevia, planned elective caesarean section is typically scheduled between 34 and 36 weeks of gestation to minimise the risk of spontaneous labour and membrane rupture [2].
  • Adjunctive management: Antenatal corticosteroids are often administered to promote fetal lung maturity if preterm delivery is anticipated (e.g., before 34 weeks gestation) [2].
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Close maternal and fetal monitoring is warranted, particularly in the third trimester. Hospitalisation from around 30-32 weeks may be considered for high-risk pregnancies (e.g., multiple pregnancies, vaginal bleeding, risk of preterm labour) [2].
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Patients should be counselled on red flag symptoms (e.g., painless vaginal bleeding, rupture of membranes) and advised to seek immediate medical attention if these occur. Preparation for neonatal resuscitation and blood transfusion should be in place for delivery [2].

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Vasa praevia (Pregnancy and the puerperium)"][/sc]
  • Fetal exsanguination: Rupture of the unprotected fetal vessels, particularly during labour or membrane rupture, leads to rapid and severe fetal blood loss. This can result in acute fetal anaemia, hypovolaemic shock, and death due to the small total blood volume of the fetus [1].
  • Fetal hypoxia and neurological injury: Significant fetal blood loss compromises oxygen delivery to fetal tissues, leading to hypoxia. Prolonged or severe hypoxia can cause irreversible neurological damage, including cerebral palsy [1].
  • Stillbirth or neonatal death: In cases of undiagnosed vasa praevia, the high fetal mortality rate (at least 60%) is primarily due to acute blood loss at the time of membrane rupture. Even with antenatal diagnosis, complications can arise if planned delivery is delayed or if spontaneous labour occurs prematurely [1].
  • Need for emergency caesarean section: The acute nature of fetal compromise following vessel rupture necessitates immediate delivery, often via emergency caesarean section, which carries its own risks for both mother and baby [2].
  • Neonatal anaemia and need for transfusion: Babies born after a vasa praevia rupture often require immediate resuscitation, including blood transfusions, to correct severe anaemia and hypovolaemia [2].
