Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Cardiomyopathy (Heart and vasculature)"][/sc]
  • Cardiomyopathy refers to a group of diseases affecting the heart muscle, making it harder for the heart to pump blood to the rest of the body, often leading to heart failure.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the different types of cardiomyopathy, their clinical presentations, and the principles of their investigation and management, including genetic implications and risk stratification for sudden cardiac death.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Cardiomyopathy (Heart and vasculature)"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Cardiomyopathy (Heart and vasculature)"][/sc]
  • Dilated cardiomyopathy (DCM) affects approximately 1 in 250 people in the UK, making it the most common form of cardiomyopathy. [1]
  • Hypertrophic cardiomyopathy (HCM) is estimated to affect 1 in 500 people in the UK, often inherited in an autosomal dominant pattern. [2]
  • Arrhythmogenic cardiomyopathy (ACM), previously known as ARVC, is a rare genetic condition with an estimated prevalence of 1 in 2,000 to 1 in 5,000 in the general population. [3]
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Cardiomyopathy (Heart and vasculature)"][/sc]
  • **Trigger:** Genetic mutations or acquired factors (e.g., viral infection, toxins) lead to structural and functional abnormalities in myocardial cells.
  • **Progression:** These abnormalities result in diverse pathological changes, such as ventricular dilation and impaired contractility (DCM), myocardial hypertrophy and diastolic dysfunction (HCM), or fibrofatty replacement of myocardium (ARVC).
  • **Consequence:** The altered cardiac structure and function lead to reduced cardiac output, increased filling pressures, and electrical instability, culminating in symptoms of heart failure, arrhythmias, and potential sudden cardiac death. Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Cardiomyopathy (Heart and vasculature)"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Often asymptomatic, particularly in inherited forms, with disease progression occurring silently over years. Initial manifestations may be subtle, such as mild exercise intolerance or incidental findings on imaging.
  • Progressive phase: Symptoms gradually emerge and worsen, including dyspnoea, fatigue, and oedema, reflecting increasing cardiac dysfunction. This phase can span months to years, with varying rates of progression depending on the specific cardiomyopathy type and individual factors.
  • Late phase: Characterised by advanced heart failure symptoms, refractory arrhythmias, and significant functional limitation. The risk of life-threatening events, such as sudden cardiac death, increases substantially, particularly beyond several years of symptomatic disease.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Cardiomyopathy (Heart and vasculature)"][/sc]
  • Ask about shortness of breath - often progressive, initially on exertion, later at rest, indicating impaired cardiac output and pulmonary congestion.
  • Ask about fatigue - a common non-specific symptom reflecting reduced cardiac output and systemic hypoperfusion.
  • Ask about palpitations - sensations of a rapid, fluttering, or pounding heart, suggesting underlying arrhythmias which are common in all cardiomyopathies.
  • Ask about chest pain - may be atypical, exertional, or at rest, particularly in HCM due to myocardial ischaemia from hypertrophy and LVOTO.
  • Ask about syncope or pre-syncope - transient loss of consciousness or near-fainting, a critical symptom indicating reduced cerebral perfusion, often due to arrhythmias or outflow tract obstruction.
  • Ask about oedema - swelling in the ankles, legs, or abdomen, indicating fluid retention due to right-sided heart failure.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Cardiomyopathy (Heart and vasculature)"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - may reveal tachycardia, hypotension (especially in advanced disease or with LVOTO), or tachypnoea.
  • Check jugular venous pressure (JVP) - often elevated, particularly in restrictive cardiomyopathy or right ventricular involvement in ARVC, indicating increased right atrial pressure.
  • Check cardiac auscultation - murmurs (e.g., systolic ejection murmur in HCM with LVOTO, which intensifies with Valsalva manoeuvre), gallop rhythms (S3 in DCM, S4 in HCM), or muffled heart sounds.
  • Check peripheral oedema - pitting oedema in the lower limbs or sacrum, indicating systemic fluid overload.
  • Check hepatomegaly - an enlarged liver, often tender, due to passive venous congestion in right heart failure. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Epsilon wave: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Epsilon wave"][/sc]
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Cardiomyopathy (Heart and vasculature)"][/sc]
  • Syncope or pre-syncope: Suggests significant haemodynamic compromise, such as severe outflow tract obstruction or life-threatening arrhythmias, requiring urgent evaluation.
  • Sudden onset severe dyspnoea: May indicate acute decompensated heart failure or pulmonary oedema, necessitating immediate medical attention.
  • Sustained ventricular arrhythmias: High risk for sudden cardiac death, particularly in HCM and ARVC, requiring prompt antiarrhythmic management or device therapy.
  • Progressive symptoms despite optimal medical therapy: Signals advanced disease and potential need for escalation of care, including consideration for advanced therapies like transplantation or mechanical circulatory support.
  • Family history of sudden cardiac death at a young age: Raises suspicion for inherited cardiomyopathies, warranting cascade screening and aggressive risk stratification.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Cardiomyopathy (Heart and vasculature)"][/sc]
  • Athletes: May present with sudden cardiac death during exertion, particularly in HCM and ARVC, where physiological hypertrophy can mask underlying pathology. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Sudden death in athletes: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Sudden death in athletes"][/sc]
  • Women: May present with more subtle symptoms or later in the disease course, potentially leading to delayed diagnosis and worse outcomes.
  • Older adults: Symptoms may be attributed to normal ageing or comorbidities, delaying diagnosis. Restrictive cardiomyopathy may be more prevalent in this group due to amyloidosis.
  • Peripartum cardiomyopathy: A form of DCM presenting in late pregnancy or early postpartum, often with rapid onset heart failure symptoms.
  • Non-cardiac symptoms: Rarely, initial presentation may involve systemic manifestations of underlying genetic syndromes (e.g., neuromuscular disorders in some DCMs) before overt cardiac symptoms.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Cardiomyopathy (Heart and vasculature)"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected cardiomyopathy:
    • Electrocardiogram (ECG): May show abnormalities such as left ventricular hypertrophy (HCM), conduction delays, or arrhythmias (e.g., T-wave inversion in ARVC).
    • Echocardiography: Essential for diagnosis, assessing ventricular size and function, wall thickness, and valvular abnormalities.
    • Cardiac MRI: Provides detailed anatomical and functional assessment, including myocardial fibrosis (late gadolinium enhancement), crucial for differentiating types and assessing prognosis.
    • Blood tests: Including B-type natriuretic peptide (BNP) for heart failure severity, troponin for myocardial injury, and genetic testing for inherited forms.
  • Severity/risk tools:
    • HCM Risk-SCD Score: HCM Risk-SCD Score on MDCalc [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Valsalva manoeuvre in HCM: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Valsalva manoeuvre"][/sc]
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible:
    • Coronary angiography: To rule out ischaemic heart disease, especially in patients with chest pain or risk factors.
  • Serial reassessment:
    • Regular clinical review, ECG, and echocardiography to monitor disease progression and treatment response.

  • A 45-year-old male presents with progressive exertional dyspnoea, palpitations, and lower limb oedema over the past six months, with echocardiography revealing left ventricular dilation and severely impaired systolic function.
  • Progressive heart failure with dilated cardiomyopathy, concerning for advanced disease and potential for arrhythmias.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Cardiomyopathy (Heart and vasculature)"][/sc]
  • Ischaemic heart disease - distinguished by evidence of coronary artery disease on angiography and typical angina symptoms.
  • Hypertensive heart disease - differentiated by long-standing uncontrolled hypertension and concentric left ventricular hypertrophy without genetic predisposition.
  • Valvular heart disease - identified by specific murmurs and echocardiographic evidence of significant valvular stenosis or regurgitation.
  • Pericardial disease (e.g., constrictive pericarditis) - distinguished by characteristic imaging findings (pericardial thickening/calcification) and often normal ventricular function.
  • Athlete\'s heart - differentiated from HCM by normal diastolic function, regression of hypertrophy with de-training, and absence of genetic mutations.   • Sarcoidosis - can mimic various cardiomyopathies; distinguished by extra-cardiac manifestations and biopsy findings.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Cardiomyopathy (Heart and vasculature)"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Recognition of acute decompensation, escalation to specialist cardiac services, and supportive care including oxygen, diuretics, and vasopressors as indicated by local pathways.
  • Definitive management: Often includes pharmacological therapy (e.g., ACE inhibitors, beta-blockers, mineralocorticoid receptor antagonists for heart failure), device therapy (e.g., ICD for primary/secondary prevention of sudden cardiac death, pacemakers for bradyarrhythmias), and lifestyle modifications.
  • Adjunctive management: May support symptom control and improve quality of life, such as cardiac rehabilitation, dietary sodium restriction, and fluid management.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Regular clinical assessment, echocardiography, and ECG monitoring are typically warranted to track disease progression and treatment efficacy.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Patients should be advised on symptoms of worsening heart failure or arrhythmias and when to seek urgent medical review. Consideration for advanced therapies (e.g., heart transplantation, LVAD) in refractory cases.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Cardiomyopathy (Heart and vasculature)"][/sc]
  • Heart failure: Progressive deterioration of cardiac function leading to symptoms of congestion and low cardiac output, often developing over months to years.
  • Arrhythmias: Both atrial and ventricular arrhythmias are common, ranging from benign to life-threatening (e.g., ventricular tachycardia/fibrillation), with risk increasing over the disease course.
  • Sudden cardiac death: A critical and often tragic complication, particularly in HCM and ARVC, which can occur at any stage of the disease, sometimes as the first manifestation.
  • Thromboembolism: Increased risk of clot formation, especially in dilated atria (e.g., in atrial fibrillation) or severely dysfunctional ventricles, leading to stroke or systemic emboli.
  • Valvular regurgitation: Often secondary to ventricular dilation (e.g., mitral regurgitation in DCM) or structural changes (e.g., mitral regurgitation in HCM), exacerbating heart failure symptoms.
