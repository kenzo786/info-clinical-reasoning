Osteomyelitis (Musculoskeletal) [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Osteomyelitis"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Osteomyelitis"][/sc]
  • Osteomyelitis is an inflammatory condition of bone caused by an infecting organism, most commonly Staphylococcus aureus.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical features, risk factors, and diagnostic approach to osteomyelitis, including its common variants and complications.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Osteomyelitis"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Osteomyelitis"][/sc]
  • Incidence of acute haematogenous osteomyelitis in children is approximately 1 in 5,000 to 1 in 10,000 per year in developed countries.
  • Diabetic foot osteomyelitis affects approximately 15-20% of individuals with diabetic foot ulcers, with a lifetime risk of up to 25% for diabetic patients developing a foot ulcer.
  • Risk factors include diabetes mellitus, peripheral vascular disease, immunosuppression, intravenous drug use, trauma, and recent orthopaedic surgery.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Osteomyelitis"][/sc]
  • Trigger: Infection typically arises from haematogenous spread (e.g., in children from bacteraemia), contiguous spread from adjacent soft tissue infection (e.g., diabetic foot ulcers, pressure sores), or direct inoculation (e.g., trauma, surgery).
  • Progression: Bacteria colonise bone, leading to an inflammatory response, oedema, increased intraosseous pressure, and impaired blood flow, which can result in bone necrosis and the formation of a sequestrum (dead bone) and involucrum (new bone formation around the sequestrum). [sc name="jitl-query" Q="What is involucrum and sequestrum in osteomyelitis and why do they matter clinically?" text="involucrum/sequestrum"][/sc]
  • Consequence: Untreated or chronic infection can lead to persistent pain, pathological fractures, sinus tract formation, amyloidosis, and in severe cases, amputation or sepsis.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Osteomyelitis"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Acute osteomyelitis typically develops over days to a few weeks, characterised by rapid onset of pain, fever, and local inflammatory signs.
  • Progressive phase: If untreated, acute infection can progress to subacute or chronic forms over weeks to months, with symptoms becoming less severe but persistent, and bone destruction becoming more evident.
  • Late phase: Chronic osteomyelitis can persist for months to years, often with intermittent exacerbations, sinus tract formation, and significant bone remodelling, particularly beyond 6-8 weeks from initial infection.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Osteomyelitis"][/sc]
  • Ask about localised bone pain - often severe and constant, not relieved by rest, which helps differentiate from muscular pain.
  • Ask about fever and chills - indicative of systemic inflammation and bacteraemia, particularly in acute haematogenous forms.
  • Ask about reduced weight-bearing or limping (in children) - suggests significant pain or dysfunction in an affected limb.
  • Ask about malaise and fatigue - non-specific systemic symptoms that can accompany any significant infection.
  • Ask about a history of recent trauma, surgery, or skin infection - provides clues to the route of infection, especially in contiguous osteomyelitis.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Osteomyelitis"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - fever, tachycardia, and hypotension may indicate systemic inflammatory response or sepsis.
  • Check for localised tenderness, warmth, erythema, and swelling over the affected bone - classic signs of inflammation and infection.
  • Check for reduced range of motion in adjacent joints - due to pain or inflammatory exudate.
  • Check for draining sinus tracts or non-healing wounds - highly suggestive of chronic osteomyelitis, especially in diabetic foot infections.
  • Check for a Brodie abscess - a subacute or chronic form of osteomyelitis presenting as a localised abscess in bone, often with minimal systemic symptoms. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="What is a Brodie abscess and how does it present clinically?" text="Brodie abscess"][/sc]
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Osteomyelitis"][/sc]
  • Systemic inflammatory response syndrome (SIRS): Indicates severe infection or sepsis, requiring urgent medical attention and broad-spectrum antibiotics.
  • Rapidly worsening pain or neurological deficit (spinal osteomyelitis): Suggests spinal cord compression or epidural abscess, necessitating emergency imaging and surgical decompression.
  • New onset or worsening limb ischaemia: Can be a complication of severe infection, particularly in diabetic foot osteomyelitis, requiring urgent vascular assessment.
  • Pathological fracture: Implies significant bone destruction due to chronic infection, often requiring surgical stabilisation and debridement.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Osteomyelitis"][/sc]
  • Diabetic foot osteomyelitis: Often presents with minimal pain due to neuropathy, with infection masked by a chronic ulcer; probe-to-bone test may be positive.
  • Vertebral osteomyelitis: May present as isolated back pain without fever or local signs, making diagnosis challenging and often delayed.
  • Neonates and infants: Can present with pseudoparalysis, irritability, and refusal to move a limb, often without significant fever.
  • Immunocompromised patients: Symptoms may be blunted or atypical, with less pronounced inflammatory markers, leading to delayed diagnosis. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="How does osteomyelitis present atypically in immunocompromised patients?" text="Immunocompromised presentation"][/sc]
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Osteomyelitis"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected osteomyelitis: Full blood count (leukocytosis), erythrocyte sedimentation rate (ESR), C-reactive protein (CRP) – elevated inflammatory markers are common but can be normal in chronic or diabetic forms.
  • Severity/risk tools: Consider the VIRSTA Score on MDCalc for vertebral osteomyelitis risk stratification. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="What is the VIRSTA score and how is it used in vertebral osteomyelitis?" text="VIRSTA Score"][/sc]
  • Severity/risk tools: The PEDIS Score on MDCalc can be used to assess severity of diabetic foot infections, including bone involvement. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="What is the PEDIS score and how is it used in diabetic foot ulcers?" text="PEDIS Score"][/sc]
  • Imaging - Plain radiographs: Initial imaging, may show periosteal reaction, cortical destruction, or lucency after 10-14 days of infection; often normal in early stages.
  • Imaging - Magnetic Resonance Imaging (MRI): Gold standard for diagnosis, demonstrating bone marrow oedema, cortical disruption, and soft tissue involvement with high sensitivity and specificity.
  • Imaging - Ultrasound: Useful in children for detecting subperiosteal abscesses or soft tissue collections.
  • Imaging - CT scan: Provides excellent bony detail, useful for surgical planning and detecting sequestra, but less sensitive for early bone marrow changes than MRI.
  • Microbiological diagnosis: Bone biopsy with culture is crucial for definitive diagnosis and guiding targeted antibiotic therapy; ideally performed before antibiotic initiation.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Osteomyelitis"][/sc]
  • A 6-year-old boy presents with a 5-day history of right thigh pain, fever, and refusal to bear weight, following a recent upper respiratory tract infection.
  • A middle-aged man with poorly controlled diabetes presents with a chronic, non-healing foot ulcer and localised warmth and swelling, concerning for osteomyelitis.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Osteomyelitis"][/sc]
  • Septic arthritis - distinguished by primary involvement of the joint space with effusion and severe pain on passive movement, though co-occurrence with osteomyelitis is possible.
  • Cellulitis - superficial soft tissue infection without bone involvement; lacks deep bone pain and systemic features of osteomyelitis.
  • Transient synovitis (in children) - self-limiting inflammatory condition of the hip, typically less severe systemic symptoms and normal inflammatory markers compared to osteomyelitis.
  • Gout/Pseudogout - acute inflammatory arthritis, often monoarticular, with crystal identification in synovial fluid being diagnostic.
  • Stress fracture - history of repetitive trauma, pain exacerbated by activity, typically no systemic signs of infection.
  • Bone tumour (e.g., Ewing sarcoma, osteosarcoma) - can mimic osteomyelitis with pain and swelling, but usually lacks infectious symptoms and has characteristic imaging features. Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Osteomyelitis"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Recognition of severe infection, escalation to senior medical staff, supportive care (analgesia, fluid resuscitation), and early microbiological sampling before empiric antibiotic initiation where possible.
  • Definitive management: Often includes prolonged courses of targeted antibiotics (typically 4-6 weeks for acute, longer for chronic) guided by bone culture results, and surgical debridement to remove necrotic bone and drain abscesses.
  • Adjunctive management: Immobilisation of the affected limb, pain management, and management of underlying conditions such as diabetes or peripheral vascular disease.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Regular clinical assessment, monitoring of inflammatory markers (ESR, CRP), and repeat imaging to assess response to treatment and detect complications.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Patients should be advised to seek urgent medical review for worsening pain, fever, new neurological symptoms, or signs of spreading infection.
  • Exam pharmacology reference (UKMLA/MRCGP): Empiric antibiotic choices often include flucloxacillin or clindamycin for suspected Staphylococcus aureus, with broader spectrum agents for polymicrobial infections (e.g., diabetic foot). Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Osteomyelitis"][/sc]
  • Chronic osteomyelitis: Persistence of infection beyond 6-8 weeks, often due to inadequate debridement or antibiotic penetration, leading to recurrent flares and sinus tracts.
  • Pathological fracture: Weakening of bone due to infection and necrosis can predispose to fracture with minimal trauma.
  • Sepsis: Systemic spread of infection, particularly from acute osteomyelitis, can lead to life-threatening organ dysfunction.
  • Amputation: In severe, refractory cases, especially in diabetic foot osteomyelitis, amputation may be necessary to control infection and preserve life.
  • Squamous cell carcinoma: A rare, long-term complication arising in chronic draining sinus tracts.
