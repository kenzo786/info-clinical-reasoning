Chorioamnionitis (Pregnancy and the puerperium) [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Chorioamnionitis (Pregnancy and the puerperium)"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Chorioamnionitis (Pregnancy and the puerperium)"][/sc]
  • An acute inflammation of the fetal membranes (amnion and chorion), often with infection of the amniotic fluid and fetus, typically arising from an ascending bacterial infection from the lower genital tract. It is also termed intra-amniotic infection (IAI).
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical features of chorioamnionitis, understand its implications for maternal and fetal wellbeing, and initiate appropriate diagnostic and management pathways, including timely administration of antibiotics and planning for delivery.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Chorioamnionitis (Pregnancy and the puerperium)"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Chorioamnionitis (Pregnancy and the puerperium)"][/sc]
  • Chorioamnionitis complicates approximately 1–4% of term births in the UK. The incidence is significantly higher in preterm deliveries, particularly following preterm prelabour rupture of membranes (PPROM), where it can affect up to 40-70% of cases.
  • Key risk factors include PPROM, prolonged labour, multiple digital vaginal examinations, nulliparity, and the presence of lower genital tract pathogens (e.g., Group B Streptococcus, bacterial vaginosis).
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Chorioamnionitis (Pregnancy and the puerperium)"][/sc]
  • Trigger: Ascending migration of vaginal or cervical micro-organisms (commonly polymicrobial, including Ureaplasma, Mycoplasma, Gardnerella vaginalis, and E. coli) into the uterine cavity, often facilitated by ruptured membranes.
  • Progression: Bacterial proliferation within the amniotic fluid triggers a maternal and fetal inflammatory response, involving the release of cytokines and chemokines, leading to neutrophilic infiltration of the chorioamniotic membranes.
  • Consequence: The inflammatory cascade can stimulate prostaglandin production, leading to uterine contractions, cervical ripening, and preterm labour. It can also lead to systemic infection in both mother (sepsis) and fetus ([sc name="jitl-query" Q="Neonatal sepsis: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="neonatal sepsis"][/sc]).
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Chorioamnionitis (Pregnancy and the puerperium)"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Often insidious or subclinical, particularly in the context of PPROM, where colonisation can exist for days before overt clinical signs appear.
  • Progressive phase: Clinical features such as maternal fever, uterine tenderness, and tachycardia typically develop rapidly over several hours once the bacterial load and inflammatory response cross a clinical threshold.
  • Late phase: Without intervention, maternal sepsis, fetal distress, and neonatal infection can develop, with adverse outcomes escalating significantly, particularly beyond 12-24 hours of established clinical signs.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Chorioamnionitis (Pregnancy and the puerperium)"][/sc]
  • Ask about fever and chills - a core sign of systemic infection and a key diagnostic criterion.
  • Ask about lower abdominal or uterine pain/tenderness - suggests inflammation of the myometrium and surrounding tissues.
  • Ask about changes in vaginal discharge - specifically a purulent or foul-smelling discharge (malodorous liquor), indicating bacterial activity.
  • Ask about general malaise or feeling unwell - a non-specific but important symptom of systemic illness.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Chorioamnionitis (Pregnancy and the puerperium)"][/sc]
  • Check maternal vital signs - pyrexia (temperature ≥38.0°C), persistent tachycardia (>120 bpm), and hypotension are critical signs.
  • Check for uterine tenderness on abdominal palpation - a classic sign of uterine inflammation.
  • Check fetal heart rate - persistent fetal tachycardia (>160 bpm for >10 minutes) is an early and sensitive indicator of fetal stress or infection.
  • Check for purulent or foul-smelling fluid draining from the cervix on speculum examination - highly suggestive of [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Intra-amniotic infection: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="intra-amniotic infection"][/sc].
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Chorioamnionitis (Pregnancy and the puerperium)"][/sc]
  • Maternal pyrexia ≥39.0°C - indicates a significant systemic inflammatory response and high risk of sepsis.
  • Sustained fetal tachycardia - suggests fetal compromise and an urgent need for intervention.
  • Maternal hypotension or altered mental state - concerning for progression to severe sepsis or septic shock.
  • Foul-smelling liquor - strongly correlates with established, often polymicrobial, infection requiring immediate action.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Chorioamnionitis (Pregnancy and the puerperium)"][/sc]
  • Subclinical or histological chorioamnionitis: The patient may be asymptomatic, with no fever or tenderness. The diagnosis is made on placental histology post-delivery, but it still carries risks for neonatal complications.
  • With epidural analgesia: The pyrexia associated with infection can be difficult to distinguish from epidural-related maternal hyperthermia, potentially delaying diagnosis.
  • Immunosuppressed patients: May not mount a significant febrile response, presenting with more subtle signs like general malaise or fetal tachycardia as the primary indicator.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Chorioamnionitis (Pregnancy and the puerperium)"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected chorioamnionitis: Diagnosis is primarily clinical. Blood tests support the diagnosis: Full Blood Count (may show leucocytosis >15 x 10⁹/L) and C-Reactive Protein (often elevated >40 mg/L). Blood cultures should be taken if sepsis is suspected.
  • Severity/risk tools: The concept of [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Triple I (intrauterine infection, inflammation, or both): what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Triple I (intrauterine infection, inflammation, or both)"][/sc] is used to categorise the condition. A diagnosis of 'suspected' IAI is based on maternal fever plus fetal tachycardia, uterine tenderness, or purulent/foul-smelling fluid.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: A mid-stream urine (MSU) sample to exclude urinary tract infection. High vaginal and endocervical swabs may identify specific pathogens but do not confirm IAI.
  • Imaging - Ultrasound: Not routinely used for diagnosis, but may show reduced amniotic fluid volume or echogenic sludge, which can be associated with infection.
  • Serial reassessment: Regular monitoring of maternal temperature, heart rate, blood pressure, and continuous electronic fetal monitoring are crucial to assess progression and response to treatment.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Chorioamnionitis (Pregnancy and the puerperium)"][/sc]
  • A 30-year-old woman at 34 weeks gestation with a 12-hour history of ruptured membranes presents with a fever of 38.5°C, uterine tenderness, and a fetal heart rate of 170 bpm.
  • A febrile pregnant woman with prolonged membrane rupture, uterine tenderness, and fetal tachycardia, concerning for intra-amniotic infection and impending preterm delivery.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Chorioamnionitis (Pregnancy and the puerperium)"][/sc]
  • Urinary tract infection / Pyelonephritis - distinguished by urinary symptoms (dysuria, frequency) and flank pain/tenderness, with a positive urine culture.
  • Placental abruption - typically presents with a painful, tense ('woody') uterus and vaginal bleeding; fever is usually absent.
  • Labour - uterine contractions are present, but signs of infection (fever, foul discharge) are absent unless chorioamnionitis is the underlying cause.
  • Appendicitis - pain is often localised to the right iliac fossa (though may be higher and more diffuse in pregnancy), with associated gastrointestinal symptoms.
  • Pelvic Inflammatory Disease (PID) - rare in an established pregnancy but presents with similar lower abdominal pain and discharge; distinguished by cervical motion tenderness.
  • Ectopic pregnancy - only a differential in early pregnancy, presenting with pain and bleeding, not a consideration at gestations where chorioamnionitis is common.
  • Endometritis (postpartum) - typically occurs after delivery, presenting with fever and uterine tenderness; rarely seen antenatally. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Endometritis: what is it and how does it differ from chorioamnionitis?" text="Endometritis"][/sc]
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Chorioamnionitis (Pregnancy and the puerperium)"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Prompt recognition, escalation to senior obstetric and neonatal teams, and initiation of broad-spectrum intravenous antibiotics and antipyretics, as per sepsis care bundles.
  • Definitive management: Expedited delivery of the fetus and placenta is the definitive treatment. The mode of delivery is based on standard obstetric indications; chorioamnionitis itself is not a mandatory indication for caesarean section.
  • Adjunctive management: Supportive care includes intravenous fluid resuscitation to maintain maternal haemodynamic stability and continuous electronic fetal monitoring.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: After delivery, the mother requires ongoing monitoring for signs of sepsis. The neonate requires close observation and investigation for early-onset sepsis.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: A low threshold for involving the critical care team for maternal deterioration is essential. Clear communication with the neonatal team is vital to prepare for the delivery of a potentially septic infant.
  • Exam pharmacology reference (UKMLA/MRCGP): UK guidelines (e.g., RCOG, NICE) recommend broad-spectrum intravenous antibiotics. Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Chorioamnionitis (Pregnancy and the puerperium)"][/sc]
  • Maternal sepsis: Can progress rapidly to severe sepsis or septic shock, particularly if diagnosis and antibiotic administration are delayed beyond a few hours.
  • Postpartum haemorrhage: Increased risk due to uterine atony resulting from prolonged labour and infection-induced inflammation of the myometrium.
  • Neonatal sepsis: A major risk, as infection is transmitted to the fetus in utero or during birth. It is a leading cause of early-onset neonatal mortality and morbidity, often manifesting within 72 hours of birth.
  • Preterm birth: Chorioamnionitis is a primary driver of spontaneous preterm labour, contributing significantly to the morbidity and mortality associated with prematurity.
  • Perinatal brain injury: The fetal inflammatory response to infection is linked to an increased risk of neurological damage, including periventricular leukomalacia and cerebral palsy.
  • Increased operative delivery rates: Fetal distress or dysfunctional labour associated with chorioamnionitis often leads to an increased likelihood of emergency caesarean section.
