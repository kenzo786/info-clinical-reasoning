Intestinal ischaemia [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Intestinal ischaemia"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Intestinal ischaemia"][/sc]
  • Intestinal ischaemia is a serious condition resulting from inadequate blood flow to a segment of the bowel, leading to cellular injury and potentially necrosis.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the varied presentations of intestinal ischaemia, differentiate acute from chronic forms, and understand the urgency of diagnosis and management.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Intestinal ischaemia"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Intestinal ischaemia"][/sc]
  • Acute mesenteric ischaemia is rare but carries high mortality, affecting approximately 1 in 100,000 people annually in the UK, often in older adults with cardiovascular comorbidities.
  • Chronic mesenteric ischaemia is less common, often seen in patients with extensive atherosclerotic disease, with an incidence estimated at 1 in 200,000.
  • Risk factors include atrial fibrillation, recent myocardial infarction, heart failure, peripheral vascular disease, and hypercoagulable states.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Intestinal ischaemia"][/sc]
  • Reduced blood flow to the bowel, often due to arterial occlusion (e.g., embolism, thrombosis) or non-occlusive mechanisms (e.g., low cardiac output), leads to oxygen deprivation in enterocytes.
  • Prolonged ischaemia causes cellular damage, loss of mucosal integrity, and release of inflammatory mediators, potentially leading to bacterial translocation and systemic inflammatory response.
  • If reperfusion occurs after significant ischaemia, further injury can result from reactive oxygen species and inflammatory cascades, ultimately leading to bowel necrosis, perforation, and peritonitis.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Intestinal ischaemia"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Acute presentations typically involve sudden, severe abdominal pain, often disproportionate to physical findings, developing over hours.
  • Progressive phase: Symptoms may evolve over several days, particularly beyond 24-48 hours, with increasing signs of peritonitis, systemic sepsis, and organ dysfunction as bowel necrosis progresses.
  • Late phase: Chronic forms present with post-prandial abdominal pain (abdominal angina) developing over weeks to months, often leading to food aversion and weight loss.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Intestinal ischaemia"][/sc]
  • Ask about sudden, severe abdominal pain - often out of proportion to examination findings in acute mesenteric ischaemia, which is a critical diagnostic clue.
  • Ask about post-prandial abdominal pain - typically occurring 15-60 minutes after eating in chronic mesenteric ischaemia, leading to food fear.
  • Ask about changes in bowel habit - diarrhoea, constipation, or bloody stools may indicate mucosal damage or infarction.
  • Ask about nausea and vomiting - common non-specific symptoms that can accompany severe abdominal pain.
  • Ask about weight loss - often a feature of chronic mesenteric ischaemia due to food aversion.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Intestinal ischaemia"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - tachycardia, hypotension, and fever may indicate systemic inflammatory response or sepsis.
  • Check abdominal tenderness - initially minimal in acute ischaemia, progressing to generalised tenderness, guarding, and rigidity with bowel infarction and peritonitis.
  • Check for abdominal distension - can be a sign of ileus or bowel obstruction secondary to ischaemia.
  • Check for absent bowel sounds - may indicate advanced ischaemia and paralytic ileus.
  • Check for signs of peripheral vascular disease - bruits or diminished pulses may suggest a systemic atherosclerotic burden.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Intestinal ischaemia"][/sc]
  • Pain out of proportion to examination: Suggests acute mesenteric ischaemia, demanding urgent investigation.
  • Peritonism (guarding, rigidity, rebound tenderness): Indicates bowel infarction and perforation, requiring immediate surgical intervention.
  • Haemodynamic instability (hypotension, tachycardia): Signifies severe sepsis or hypovolaemia, necessitating aggressive resuscitation.
  • Bloody diarrhoea: Can be a late sign of mucosal ischaemia and necrosis.
  • Sudden onset severe abdominal pain in a patient with atrial fibrillation: Highly suspicious for embolic mesenteric ischaemia.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Intestinal ischaemia"][/sc]
  • Older adults: May present with less pronounced pain or atypical symptoms due to altered pain perception, leading to delayed diagnosis.
  • Immunosuppressed patients: Can have blunted inflammatory responses, masking typical signs of peritonitis and making diagnosis challenging.
  • Patients on beta-blockers: May not exhibit the expected tachycardia in response to hypovolaemia or sepsis, potentially obscuring the severity of illness.
  • Chronic mesenteric ischaemia: Can mimic other gastrointestinal conditions like irritable bowel syndrome or peptic ulcer disease, leading to misdiagnosis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Intestinal ischaemia"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected acute intestinal ischaemia: Urgent CT angiography of the abdomen and pelvis is the gold standard for visualising mesenteric vessels and bowel wall changes.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Blood tests including full blood count, urea and electrolytes, liver function tests, C-reactive protein, and lactate are essential; a rising lactate is a concerning but non-specific marker.
  • Imaging - plain abdominal X-ray: May show non-specific findings like ileus or thumbprinting, but is not sensitive or specific for ischaemia.
  • Imaging - duplex ultrasound: Can be used for chronic mesenteric ischaemia to assess flow in mesenteric arteries, but is operator-dependent.
  • Serial reassessment: Close monitoring of clinical status, abdominal examination, and serial lactate levels is crucial, particularly in equivocal cases.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Intestinal ischaemia"][/sc]
  • A 78-year-old male with a history of atrial fibrillation presents to the emergency department with sudden onset, severe, generalised abdominal pain for 4 hours, which is disproportionate to the mild tenderness on palpation.
  • An elderly male with cardiovascular comorbidities presenting with acute, severe abdominal pain and minimal initial physical findings, rapidly progressing to signs of systemic illness and peritonism.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Intestinal ischaemia"][/sc]
  • Acute pancreatitis - typically presents with epigastric pain radiating to the back, often with elevated amylase/lipase.
  • Perforated viscus - sudden, severe abdominal pain with rigid abdomen and often free air on imaging.
  • Bowel obstruction - colicky abdominal pain, vomiting, distension, and altered bowel sounds, often with dilated loops on imaging.
  • Acute appendicitis - localised right iliac fossa pain, often with migration from periumbilical region, fever, and localised tenderness.
  • Diverticulitis - localised left lower quadrant pain, fever, and inflammatory markers, often in older patients.
  • Myocardial infarction - can present with epigastric pain, but typically associated with cardiac risk factors and ECG changes.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Intestinal ischaemia"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Aggressive fluid resuscitation, broad-spectrum antibiotics to cover gut flora, and urgent pain control.
  • Definitive management: Revascularisation (surgical or endovascular) for occlusive mesenteric ischaemia, or addressing underlying cause for non-occlusive forms. Surgical resection of necrotic bowel may be required.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Intensive care monitoring, serial abdominal examinations, and lactate levels. Long-term management involves addressing cardiovascular risk factors and anticoagulation if indicated.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Early involvement of surgical and interventional radiology teams is paramount. Patients should be advised to seek immediate medical attention for worsening pain, fever, or signs of peritonism.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Intestinal ischaemia"][/sc]
  • Bowel infarction and necrosis: Direct consequence of prolonged ischaemia, leading to irreversible tissue damage and loss of bowel function.
  • Bowel perforation: Occurs when necrosis extends through the bowel wall, resulting in spillage of intestinal contents into the peritoneal cavity and peritonitis.
  • Sepsis and multi-organ failure: Systemic inflammatory response due to bacterial translocation from damaged bowel and release of inflammatory mediators, leading to widespread organ dysfunction.
  • Short bowel syndrome: May result from extensive surgical resection of necrotic bowel, leading to malabsorption and nutritional deficiencies.
  • Stricture formation: Healing of ischaemic segments can lead to fibrotic narrowing of the bowel lumen, causing obstruction.
