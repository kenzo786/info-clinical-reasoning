Orthostatic/postural hypotension [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Orthostatic/postural hypotension"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Orthostatic/postural hypotension"][/sc]
  • A sustained reduction in systolic blood pressure (BP) of at least 20 mmHg or diastolic BP of 10 mmHg that occurs within three minutes of standing.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical features and diagnostic criteria for orthostatic hypotension, and understand its impact on patient safety and quality of life.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Orthostatic/postural hypotension"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Orthostatic/postural hypotension"][/sc]
  • In the community, the prevalence of orthostatic hypotension in adults aged over 65 years is estimated to be approximately 16-22.2%, rising to 23.9-50% for those living in long-term care settings.
  • Risk factors include advanced age (over 65), polypharmacy (especially antihypertensives, diuretics, vasodilators), autonomic dysfunction (e.g., Parkinson's disease, multiple system atrophy, diabetes mellitus), dehydration, and prolonged bed rest.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Orthostatic/postural hypotension"][/sc]
  • Upon standing, gravity causes blood to pool in the lower extremities, leading to decreased venous return to the heart.
  • The autonomic nervous system fails to adequately compensate for this pooling by increasing heart rate, cardiac contractility, and vascular tone, or skeletal muscle contraction is insufficient.
  • This inadequate physiological response results in an abnormally large drop in blood pressure upon standing, leading to cerebral hypoperfusion and associated symptoms. Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Orthostatic/postural hypotension"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Symptoms typically manifest within seconds to 3 minutes of standing or changing from a supine to an upright position.
  • Progressive phase: Symptoms may worsen or persist if the upright posture is maintained without adequate compensatory mechanisms.
  • Late phase: Resolution of symptoms usually occurs rapidly upon returning to a seated or supine position, as blood pressure normalises.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Orthostatic/postural hypotension"][/sc]
  • Ask about lightheadedness or dizziness - indicative of cerebral hypoperfusion upon standing.
  • Ask about blurring of vision - a common manifestation of reduced cerebral blood flow.
  • Ask about weakness or fatigue - often experienced due to systemic hypoperfusion.
  • Ask about syncope (fainting) - a critical symptom indicating severe cerebral hypoperfusion and risk of injury.
  • Ask about headache or neck pain (coat-hanger pain) - may occur due to hypoperfusion of cervical and occipital muscles.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Orthostatic/postural hypotension"][/sc]
  • Check lying and standing blood pressure - a sustained drop of >20mmHg systolic or >10mmHg diastolic within 3 minutes of standing is diagnostic.
  • Check heart rate - may show a compensatory tachycardia, but this response can be blunted in autonomic dysfunction. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Compensatory tachycardia in orthostatic hypotension: what does it indicate?" text="Compensatory tachycardia"][/sc]
  • Check for pallor or clamminess - general signs of sympathetic activation and hypoperfusion.
  • Check neurological signs - to rule out other causes of syncope or dizziness, especially in atypical presentations.
  • Check hydration status - assess for signs of dehydration (e.g., dry mucous membranes, reduced skin turgor) as a reversible cause.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Orthostatic/postural hypotension"][/sc]
  • Syncope with injury: Any episode of fainting that results in trauma or injury, indicating a significant risk of harm. Timing: can occur acutely with any episode of symptomatic orthostatic hypotension.
  • New neurological deficits: Development of new focal neurological signs (e.g., weakness, numbness, speech changes) alongside orthostatic symptoms, suggesting a central nervous system cause or complication. Timing: acute onset.
  • Chest pain or shortness of breath: These symptoms, especially if new or worsening with postural changes, may indicate underlying cardiac or pulmonary pathology requiring urgent assessment. Timing: acute onset.
  • Severe or persistent symptoms: Orthostatic symptoms that are unusually severe, prolonged, or unresponsive to conservative measures, warranting further investigation for underlying causes. Timing: ongoing.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Orthostatic/postural hypotension"][/sc]
  • Older adults: May present with non-specific symptoms like falls, cognitive impairment, or generalised weakness rather than classic dizziness or lightheadedness. Asymptomatic orthostatic hypotension is also common.
  • Diabetic autonomic neuropathy: Patients with long-standing diabetes may have impaired autonomic reflexes, leading to significant orthostatic drops in blood pressure without a compensatory increase in heart rate. Symptoms can be masked or attributed to other diabetic complications.
  • Parkinson's disease and Multiple System Atrophy (MSA): Neurogenic orthostatic hypotension is common. In Parkinson's, it often develops later in the disease course. In MSA, it can be a prominent early feature and is typically more severe, often accompanied by other autonomic failures (e.g., urinary incontinence, erectile dysfunction) and cerebellar or pyramidal signs. The absence of compensatory tachycardia is a key differentiating feature in neurogenic OH. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Autonomic failure in MSA vs Parkinson disease: how to differentiate?" text="autonomic failure in MSA vs Parkinson disease"][/sc]
  • Post-prandial hypotension: A drop in blood pressure occurring 1-2 hours after a meal, particularly in older adults, which can exacerbate orthostatic symptoms.
  • Delayed orthostatic hypotension: A blood pressure drop that occurs more than 3 minutes after standing, which may not meet the classic diagnostic criteria but can still cause significant symptoms.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Orthostatic/postural hypotension"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected orthostatic hypotension: Lying and standing blood pressure measurements (after 5 minutes supine, then at 1 and 3 minutes after standing). A drop of ≥20 mmHg systolic or ≥10 mmHg diastolic is diagnostic. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Lying and standing blood pressure test: how to perform and interpret?" text="Lying and standing blood pressure test"][/sc]
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Full blood count (FBC) to assess for anaemia; Urea and electrolytes (U&Es) to evaluate hydration and renal function; Glucose to screen for diabetes; Electrocardiogram (ECG) to exclude cardiac causes.
  • Further investigation for autonomic dysfunction: Tilt table testing or autonomic function tests (e.g., Valsalva manoeuvre, deep breathing, sudomotor tests) may be performed to characterise the type and severity of autonomic dysfunction. Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Orthostatic/postural hypotension"][/sc]
  • An elderly patient presenting with recurrent falls and dizziness upon standing, exacerbated by polypharmacy, is concerning for orthostatic hypotension.
  • Elderly patient with syncope and falls, likely secondary to impaired autonomic reflexes and medication effects, consistent with orthostatic hypotension.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Orthostatic/postural hypotension"][/sc]
  • Vasovagal syncope: Often triggered by specific events (e.g., pain, fear, prolonged standing) and associated with prodromal symptoms like nausea, sweating, and pallor. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Vasovagal syncope: how to differentiate from orthostatic hypotension?" text="Vasovagal syncope"][/sc]
  • Postural Orthostatic Tachycardia Syndrome (PoTS): Characterised by an excessive increase in heart rate upon standing without significant orthostatic hypotension. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="PoTS vs Orthostatic Hypotension: key differences?" text="PoTS"][/sc]
  • Cardiac syncope: Due to structural heart disease or arrhythmias. Often occurs without warning and can be associated with chest pain or palpitations.
  • Seizure: Characterised by tonic-clonic movements, post-ictal confusion, and often a lack of clear trigger.
  • Hypoglycaemia: Can cause dizziness, confusion, weakness, and altered consciousness, typically associated with diabetes.
  • Anaemia: Severe anaemia can lead to reduced oxygen delivery to the brain, causing dizziness and fatigue.
  • Dehydration/Hypovolaemia: Reduced intravascular volume can lead to orthostatic symptoms due to insufficient blood volume.
  • Drug-induced hypotension: Many medications (e.g., antihypertensives, diuretics) can cause or worsen orthostatic hypotension.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Orthostatic/postural hypotension"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Recognising the condition, assessing for reversible causes (e.g., dehydration, offending medications), and ensuring patient safety to prevent falls and injury. Encouraging gradual changes in posture.
  • Definitive management: Non-pharmacological measures are first-line, including patient education (avoiding sudden postural changes, prolonged standing, hot environments, large carbohydrate-rich meals, excessive alcohol), increasing fluid and salt intake (if not contraindicated), physical counter-manoeuvres (e.g., leg crossing, squatting), and raising the head of the bed. Review and adjust medications that may exacerbate orthostatic hypotension.
  • Adjunctive management: Compression stockings or abdominal binders may be considered to reduce venous pooling. Carefully controlled and individualised exercise training can improve cardiovascular fitness.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Regular monitoring of blood pressure (lying and standing) and symptom assessment to evaluate treatment effectiveness and adjust management as needed. Monitoring for supine hypertension is important.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: If symptoms are severe, persistent, or associated with red flag features, further specialist assessment (e.g., cardiology, neurology, geriatrics) is often warranted. Patients should be advised on when to seek urgent medical attention.
  • Exam pharmacology reference (UKMLA/MRCGP): Fludrocortisone (mineralocorticoid, increases blood volume) and midodrine (alpha-1 adrenergic agonist, causes vasoconstriction) are pharmacological options for refractory cases, particularly in neurogenic orthostatic hypotension. Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Orthostatic/postural hypotension"][/sc]
  • Falls and injury: A primary and significant complication, particularly in older adults, leading to fractures, head injuries, and reduced quality of life. Timing: can occur acutely with any episode of symptomatic orthostatic hypotension.
  • Syncope: Transient loss of consciousness due to cerebral hypoperfusion, posing a risk for injury and often causing significant distress. Timing: typically occurs within minutes of standing.
  • Reduced quality of life: Persistent symptoms such as dizziness, fatigue, and fear of falling can significantly impact daily activities, independence, and overall well-being. Timing: chronic and ongoing.
  • Cardiovascular events: Orthostatic hypotension is associated with an increased risk of myocardial infarction, stroke, heart failure, and all-cause mortality, particularly in those with underlying cardiovascular disease. Timing: long-term risk.
  • Cognitive impairment: Chronic cerebral hypoperfusion may contribute to cognitive decline, especially in vulnerable populations. Timing: long-term and progressive.
  • Supine hypertension: A common complication of pharmacological treatments for orthostatic hypotension, which can increase the risk of cardiovascular events during recumbency. Timing: can develop with initiation or dose escalation of certain medications.
