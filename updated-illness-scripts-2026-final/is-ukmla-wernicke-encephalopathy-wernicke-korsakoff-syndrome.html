Wernicke encephalopathy/ Wernicke-Korsakoff syndrome [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Wernicke encephalopathy/ Wernicke-Korsakoff syndrome"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Wernicke encephalopathy/ Wernicke-Korsakoff syndrome"][/sc]
  • Wernicke encephalopathy (WE) is an acute neuropsychiatric syndrome caused by thiamine (vitamin B1) deficiency, characterised by a classic triad of encephalopathy, oculomotor dysfunction, and gait ataxia.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical features, risk factors, and acute management of Wernicke encephalopathy and its progression to Korsakoff syndrome.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Wernicke encephalopathy/ Wernicke-Korsakoff syndrome"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Wernicke encephalopathy/ Wernicke-Korsakoff syndrome"][/sc]
  • The prevalence of Wernicke encephalopathy in the UK is estimated to be around 0.8-2.8% in the general population, rising significantly in specific at-risk groups such as those with alcohol use disorder (up to 12.5%).
  • Risk factors include chronic alcohol use disorder, prolonged vomiting, bariatric surgery, malignancy, hyperemesis gravidarum, and other conditions causing malnutrition or malabsorption.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Wernicke encephalopathy/ Wernicke-Korsakoff syndrome"][/sc]
  • Thiamine deficiency, often due to inadequate dietary intake or impaired absorption, leads to reduced activity of thiamine-dependent enzymes crucial for glucose metabolism in the brain.
  • This metabolic disruption primarily affects brain regions with high metabolic demand, such as the mammillary bodies, thalamus, periaqueductal grey matter, and cerebellum, causing focal neuronal damage.
  • The resulting neuronal dysfunction and damage manifest as the classic triad of WE, and if untreated, can progress to irreversible memory deficits characteristic of Korsakoff syndrome.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Wernicke encephalopathy/ Wernicke-Korsakoff syndrome"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Acute onset of symptoms, typically developing over days to weeks, often precipitated by an acute illness or nutritional stress in a predisposed individual.
  • Progressive phase: Without prompt thiamine replacement, symptoms can rapidly worsen, leading to coma or death, particularly beyond 48-72 hours of symptom onset.
  • Late phase: Persistent neurological deficits, especially severe anterograde and retrograde amnesia, may develop into Korsakoff syndrome, particularly if treatment is delayed or inadequate.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Wernicke encephalopathy/ Wernicke-Korsakoff syndrome"][/sc]
  • Ask about confusion or disorientation - crucial for identifying encephalopathy, which can range from mild lethargy to profound stupor.
  • Ask about double vision or blurred vision - indicates oculomotor dysfunction, a key diagnostic feature, often presenting as nystagmus or ophthalmoplegia.
  • Ask about unsteadiness or difficulty walking - points to gait ataxia, a cerebellar sign that contributes to falls and impaired mobility.
  • Ask about memory problems, particularly recent events - suggests progression towards Korsakoff syndrome, characterised by severe amnesia and confabulation.
  • Ask about nausea and vomiting - common in conditions predisposing to thiamine deficiency, such as chronic alcohol use or hyperemesis.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Wernicke encephalopathy/ Wernicke-Korsakoff syndrome"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - particularly pulse and blood pressure, as autonomic dysfunction can lead to hypotension or tachycardia.
  • Check eye movements - for nystagmus (horizontal more common than vertical) or ophthalmoplegia (e.g., lateral rectus palsy), which are highly suggestive.
  • Check gait - for ataxia, characterised by a broad-based, unsteady walk, often with difficulty tandem walking.
  • Check mental state examination - for disorientation, apathy, or global confusion, indicating encephalopathy.
  • Check for peripheral neuropathy - although not part of the classic triad, co-existing thiamine deficiency can cause sensory or motor deficits in the extremities.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Wernicke encephalopathy/ Wernicke-Korsakoff syndrome"][/sc]
  • New onset confusion or altered mental status - indicates acute encephalopathy requiring urgent assessment and thiamine administration.
  • Oculomotor abnormalities (e.g., nystagmus, ophthalmoplegia) - highly specific signs that, even in isolation, warrant immediate consideration of WE.
  • Gait ataxia in a patient with risk factors - suggests cerebellar involvement and the need for prompt intervention to prevent irreversible damage.
  • Hypothermia or hypotension - signs of severe autonomic dysfunction, indicating advanced disease and increased mortality risk.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Wernicke encephalopathy/ Wernicke-Korsakoff syndrome"][/sc]
  • Subclinical or partial presentations: Patients may present with only one or two components of the classic triad, making diagnosis challenging, particularly in non-alcoholic populations.
  • Post-bariatric surgery: Rapid onset of symptoms due to acute thiamine malabsorption, often presenting with more severe neurological deficits than typical alcoholic WE.
  • Hyperemesis gravidarum: Pregnant individuals with severe, prolonged vomiting can develop WE, often presenting with profound confusion and oculomotor signs.
  • Malignancy: Patients with advanced cancer, especially those undergoing chemotherapy or with significant weight loss, are at risk due to poor nutritional intake.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Wernicke encephalopathy/ Wernicke-Korsakoff syndrome"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected Wernicke encephalopathy: Clinical diagnosis based on presence of two out of four Caine criteria (dietary deficiency, oculomotor abnormalities, cerebellar dysfunction, altered mental status).
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Full blood count, electrolytes, liver function tests, renal function tests, glucose, thyroid function tests, B12/folate to exclude other causes of neurological symptoms.
  • Imaging - MRI brain: May show characteristic lesions in the mammillary bodies, thalamus, or periaqueductal grey matter, though a normal MRI does not exclude the diagnosis.
  • Thiamine levels: While low thiamine levels support the diagnosis, normal levels do not rule out WE, especially in acute settings, as treatment should not be delayed awaiting results.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Wernicke encephalopathy/ Wernicke-Korsakoff syndrome"][/sc]
  • A 55-year-old male with a history of chronic alcohol use presents to the emergency department with acute onset confusion, difficulty walking, and double vision that developed over the past three days. On examination, he is disoriented, has horizontal nystagmus, and a broad-based ataxic gait.
  • Elderly, malnourished male with subacute encephalopathy, oculomotor dysfunction, and cerebellar ataxia.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Wernicke encephalopathy/ Wernicke-Korsakoff syndrome"][/sc]
  • Alcohol intoxication/withdrawal - absence of nystagmus and specific gait ataxia, often resolves with time.
  • Stroke - focal neurological deficits, often sudden onset, typically unilateral.
  • Encephalitis/Meningitis - fever, headache, neck stiffness, CSF abnormalities.
  • Hypoglycaemia - rapid response to glucose, often no oculomotor signs.
  • Hepatic encephalopathy - asterixis, fluctuating consciousness, elevated ammonia.
  • Drug overdose - specific toxidrome, often pinpoint or dilated pupils.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Wernicke encephalopathy/ Wernicke-Korsakoff syndrome"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Prompt administration of parenteral thiamine (intravenous or intramuscular) is critical to prevent progression and irreversible neurological damage.
  • Definitive management: Continue high-dose thiamine replacement until clinical improvement or for at least 5-7 days, followed by oral thiamine supplementation. Address underlying causes such as alcohol use disorder.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Close neurological monitoring for improvement or deterioration. Long-term follow-up for cognitive rehabilitation if Korsakoff syndrome develops.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: If no improvement or deterioration, consider alternative diagnoses or complications. Educate patients and carers on the importance of continued thiamine and abstinence from alcohol.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Wernicke encephalopathy/ Wernicke-Korsakoff syndrome"][/sc]
  • Korsakoff syndrome: Irreversible severe anterograde and retrograde amnesia, often with confabulation, due to persistent damage to memory circuits, particularly beyond the acute phase of WE.
  • Coma and death: Without prompt and adequate thiamine treatment, WE can rapidly progress to profound neurological impairment and be fatal.
  • Persistent neurological deficits: Residual ataxia, nystagmus, or cognitive impairment may persist even after treatment, impacting quality of life.
  • Aspiration pneumonia: Due to impaired consciousness and swallowing reflexes in severe encephalopathy.
