Cervical cancer [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Cervical cancer"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Cervical cancer"][/sc]
  • Cervical cancer arises from abnormal cell growth in the lining of the cervix, typically progressing from precancerous changes to invasive disease.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical presentation of cervical cancer, understand its aetiology, particularly the role of HPV, and identify appropriate screening and referral pathways.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Cervical cancer"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Cervical cancer"][/sc]
  • Cervical cancer is the 14th most common cancer in females in the UK, with approximately 3,300 new cases diagnosed annually, equating to around 9 cases per day [1].
  • Incidence rates are highest in females aged 30 to 34 years in the UK, accounting for 15% of all new cervical cancer cases [1].
  • The primary risk factor is persistent infection with high-risk human papillomavirus (HPV), particularly types 16 and 18, which are responsible for most cases [2]. Other risk factors include immunosuppression (e.g., HIV), smoking, and long-term oral contraceptive use.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Cervical cancer"][/sc]
  • Trigger: Persistent infection with high-risk human papillomavirus (HPV), predominantly types 16 and 18, leads to integration of viral DNA into host cell genome, expressing oncogenic proteins E6 and E7.
  • Progression: HPV [sc name="jitl-query" Q="HPV oncoproteins E6/E7: what are they and why do they matter clinically?" text="E6 and E7 oncoproteins"] inactivate tumour suppressor proteins p53 and pRB, respectively, disrupting cell cycle control and promoting uncontrolled cellular proliferation. This typically occurs in the transformation zone of the cervix, where squamous and glandular epithelia meet.
  • Consequence: Uncontrolled proliferation of abnormal cells leads to the development of cervical intraepithelial neoplasia (CIN), which can progress to invasive [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Squamous cell carcinoma vs adenocarcinoma of cervix: what is the difference and why does it matter clinically?" text="squamous cell carcinoma"] (80-90% of cases) or adenocarcinoma (10-20% of cases) [3].
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Cervical cancer"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Initial HPV infection is often asymptomatic and may clear spontaneously within 1-2 years. Persistent infection, particularly beyond 2 years, is a prerequisite for precancerous changes.
  • Progressive phase: The development from persistent high-risk HPV infection to high-grade cervical intraepithelial neoplasia (CIN2/3) and then to invasive cervical cancer is typically slow, often taking 10-20 years.
  • Late phase: Once invasive cancer develops, progression can be more rapid, with potential for local spread to adjacent pelvic structures and distant metastasis, particularly beyond the initial diagnostic period.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Cervical cancer"][/sc]
  • Ask about unusual vaginal bleeding - particularly postcoital bleeding, intermenstrual bleeding, or postmenopausal bleeding, as these are often the earliest indicators of cervical pathology [4].
  • Ask about changes to vaginal discharge - noting any increase in volume, foul odour, or blood-staining, which may suggest infection or tumour necrosis [4].
  • Ask about pain during sexual intercourse (dyspareunia) - indicating potential local tumour invasion or inflammation [4].
  • Ask about persistent pelvic or lower back pain - suggesting more advanced disease with local spread to nerves or surrounding structures [4].
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Cervical cancer"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - noting any tachycardia or hypotension in cases of significant vaginal bleeding, or pyrexia if secondary infection is present.
  • Check for a visibly abnormal cervix on speculum examination - such as an exophytic lesion, ulceration, or increased friability, which warrants urgent investigation.
  • Check for cervical motion tenderness or parametrial induration on bimanual examination - indicating potential local tumour extension beyond the cervix.
  • Check for [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Palpable pelvic masses: what do they indicate in cervical cancer?" text="palpable pelvic masses"] or lymphadenopathy - suggesting advanced disease and metastatic spread.
  • Check for signs of anaemia - such as pallor, which may result from chronic blood loss.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Cervical cancer"][/sc]
  • Visible cervical lesion: Any suspicious appearance of the cervix on examination, consistent with cervical cancer, warrants an urgent suspected cancer pathway referral (NICE NG12) [5].
  • Persistent unexplained vaginal bleeding: Including postcoital, intermenstrual, or postmenopausal bleeding, particularly if new onset or worsening, necessitates urgent investigation.
  • Unexplained persistent vaginal discharge: Especially if blood-stained, offensive, or watery, as this can be a symptom of underlying malignancy.
  • Unexplained palpable pelvic mass: Suggesting advanced local disease or metastatic involvement, requiring urgent imaging and specialist assessment.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Cervical cancer"][/sc]
  • Older adults: Cervical cancer may be diagnosed at a later stage in women over 65 due to cessation of routine screening, often presenting with advanced disease and poorer prognosis [6].
  • Immunosuppressed individuals: Patients with HIV or those on immunosuppressive therapy have a higher incidence of cervical cancer and may experience more rapid progression or less typical histological subtypes [7].
  • Early stage asymptomatic disease: Many early cervical cancers are asymptomatic and detected incidentally during routine cervical screening, highlighting the importance of regular screening.
  • Post-treatment surveillance: Recurrence can present with vague symptoms or be asymptomatic, requiring vigilance during follow-up.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Cervical cancer"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected cervical cancer:
    • Colposcopy with directed biopsies of any suspicious lesions, which is essential for histological confirmation and assessment of invasion.
    • HPV testing and cytology (smear test) are screening tools, not diagnostic for invasive cancer.
  • Severity/risk tools:
    • [sc name="jitl-query" Q="FIGO staging for cervical cancer: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="FIGO staging"] (International Federation of Gynecology and Obstetrics) is the globally accepted system for clinical staging of cervical cancer, guiding prognosis and treatment decisions.
    • The Swede Score on MDCalc can aid in colposcopic assessment of high-grade lesions, but biopsy remains crucial.
  • Imaging - MRI: Pelvic MRI is crucial for local staging, assessing tumour size, parametrial invasion, and lymph node involvement.
  • Imaging - PET-CT: May be used for detecting distant metastases or equivocal lymph node involvement in advanced disease.
  • Serial reassessment: Regular follow-up post-treatment with clinical examination, cytology, and imaging as indicated to monitor for recurrence.
  • Biomarkers: Emerging role of [sc name="jitl-query" Q="HPV oncoproteins E6/E7 as biomarkers in cervical cancer: what is their clinical significance?" text="HPV oncoproteins E6/E7"] as potential biomarkers for monitoring disease progression and recurrence.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Cervical cancer"][/sc]
  • A 45-year-old female presents with a 3-month history of postcoital bleeding and persistent, foul-smelling vaginal discharge, with speculum examination revealing an exophytic cervical lesion.
  • A middle-aged woman with persistent abnormal vaginal bleeding and a visible cervical lesion, concerning for cervical cancer.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Cervical cancer"][/sc]
  • Cervical polyp: Benign growths on the cervix that can cause intermenstrual or postcoital bleeding, but typically appear smooth and mobile on examination.
  • Cervicitis: Inflammation of the cervix, often due to infection, causing vaginal discharge and bleeding, but usually without a discrete mass.
  • Vaginitis: Inflammation of the vagina, causing discharge, itching, and sometimes bleeding, but typically diffuse rather than localised to the cervix.
  • Endometrial cancer: Can present with abnormal uterine bleeding, particularly postmenopausal, but originates from the uterine lining rather than the cervix.
  • Pelvic Inflammatory Disease (PID): Presents with pelvic pain, vaginal discharge, and fever, but typically lacks a visible cervical lesion.
  • Ectopic pregnancy: Should always be considered in reproductive-aged women with abnormal vaginal bleeding and pelvic pain, especially if unilateral.
  • Vaginal cancer: Rare, but can present with abnormal bleeding or a mass in the vagina, distinct from the cervix.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Cervical cancer"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Recognition of suspicious symptoms and urgent referral to a gynaecological oncology specialist for definitive diagnosis and staging. Supportive care may include managing pain and bleeding.
  • Definitive management: Treatment options vary by stage but often include surgery (e.g., [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Wertheim hysterectomy: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Wertheim hysterectomy"] for early-stage invasive disease), radiotherapy, chemotherapy, or a combination (chemoradiotherapy).
  • Adjunctive management: Palliative care for advanced disease, fertility preservation counselling, and psychological support are important considerations.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Regular surveillance after treatment is essential to detect recurrence early, typically involving clinical examination, cytology, and imaging.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Patients should be advised to seek urgent medical attention for any new or worsening symptoms, particularly bleeding or pain.
  • Exam pharmacology reference (UKMLA/MRCGP): No specific drug knowledge is commonly tested for cervical cancer treatment at this level beyond general oncology principles. No doses or frequencies are cited here — always verify against your local antimicrobial/prescribing formulary before prescribing.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Cervical cancer"][/sc]
  • Local invasion: Tumour extension into adjacent structures such as the bladder, rectum, or parametrium, leading to symptoms like haematuria, rectal bleeding, or hydronephrosis, particularly in advanced stages.
  • Metastasis: Spread to regional lymph nodes (pelvic, para-aortic) or distant organs (lungs, liver, bone), significantly worsening prognosis and requiring systemic treatment.
  • Haemorrhage: Tumour ulceration can lead to significant vaginal bleeding, potentially requiring transfusions or interventional radiology.
  • Fistula formation: In advanced or recurrent disease, communication between the vagina and bladder (vesicovaginal fistula) or rectum (rectovaginal fistula) can occur, causing distressing symptoms.
  • Treatment-related complications: Side effects from surgery (e.g., lymphedema, sexual dysfunction), radiotherapy (e.g., radiation cystitis/proctitis, vaginal stenosis), and chemotherapy (e.g., myelosuppression, neuropathy).
