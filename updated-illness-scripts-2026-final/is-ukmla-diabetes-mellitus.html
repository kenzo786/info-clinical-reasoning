Diabetes mellitus [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Diabetes mellitus"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Diabetes mellitus"][/sc]
  • Diabetes mellitus is a group of metabolic disorders characterized by persistent hyperglycaemia resulting from defects in insulin secretion, insulin action, or both [1] [2].
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical features, diagnostic criteria, and initial management principles for both type 1 and type 2 diabetes mellitus, including their acute and chronic complications.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Diabetes mellitus"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Diabetes mellitus"][/sc]
  • In the UK, approximately 4.6 million people have a diagnosis of diabetes, with around 8% having type 1 diabetes and 90% having type 2 diabetes [3].
  • The prevalence of type 2 diabetes in adults aged 17 and over in England was 7.0% at March 2024, an increase from 6.8% in the previous year [4].
  • Risk factors for type 2 diabetes include being overweight or obese, physical inactivity, unhealthy diet, family history, ethnicity (South Asian, African-Caribbean, and Black African populations are at higher risk), and increasing age [5] [6].
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Diabetes mellitus"][/sc]
  • Trigger: Type 1 diabetes involves autoimmune destruction of pancreatic beta cells, leading to absolute insulin deficiency. Type 2 diabetes begins with insulin resistance, where target cells do not respond effectively to insulin, often compounded by impaired insulin secretion from beta cells.
  • Progression: In type 1 diabetes, the lack of insulin leads to unchecked glucose production by the liver and impaired glucose uptake by peripheral tissues, resulting in hyperglycaemia. In type 2 diabetes, chronic insulin resistance and compensatory hyperinsulinemia eventually lead to beta-cell exhaustion and progressive decline in insulin production.
  • Consequence: Persistent hyperglycaemia in both types leads to microvascular (retinopathy, nephropathy, neuropathy) and macrovascular (cardiovascular disease, stroke, peripheral arterial disease) complications. Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Diabetes mellitus"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Type 1 diabetes typically presents acutely with symptoms developing rapidly over days to weeks. Type 2 diabetes often has an insidious onset, with hyperglycaemia developing gradually over months to years, often asymptomatic in its early stages.
  • Progressive phase: In type 1 diabetes, the autoimmune destruction is ongoing, leading to complete insulin dependence. In type 2 diabetes, beta-cell dysfunction progresses, requiring escalating therapeutic interventions to maintain glycaemic control.
  • Late phase: Both types, if poorly controlled, lead to chronic complications. The timing of these complications can vary, but they typically manifest after years of sustained hyperglycaemia.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Diabetes mellitus"][/sc]
  • Ask about polyuria - due to osmotic diuresis from high blood glucose exceeding renal threshold.
  • Ask about polydipsia - compensatory response to fluid loss from polyuria.
  • Ask about unexplained weight loss - particularly in type 1, due to catabolism of fat and muscle for energy in the absence of insulin.
  • Ask about fatigue - due to poor cellular glucose uptake and metabolic derangements.
  • Ask about blurred vision - osmotic effects on the lens of the eye.
  • Ask about recurrent infections (e.g., thrush, UTIs) - hyperglycaemia impairs immune function and promotes microbial growth.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Diabetes mellitus"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - Tachycardia and hypotension may indicate dehydration, particularly in DKA or HHS. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Diabetic Ketoacidosis (DKA): what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Diabetic Ketoacidosis (DKA)"][/sc]
  • Check BMI and waist circumference - indicators of obesity, a major risk factor for type 2 diabetes.
  • Check skin for acanthosis nigricans - a sign of insulin resistance, often seen in type 2 diabetes.
  • Check feet for signs of neuropathy or peripheral vascular disease - crucial for early detection of complications.
  • Check fundoscopy - to screen for diabetic retinopathy.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Diabetes mellitus"][/sc]
  • Diabetic Ketoacidosis (DKA) symptoms: Rapid onset polyuria, polydipsia, nausea, vomiting, abdominal pain, Kussmaul breathing, and altered mental status, indicating severe insulin deficiency and metabolic acidosis, requiring urgent medical attention.
  • Hyperosmolar Hyperglycaemic State (HHS) symptoms: Gradual onset polyuria, polydipsia, profound dehydration, and altered mental status, typically in type 2 diabetes, indicating severe hyperglycaemia and hyperosmolarity without significant ketosis, requiring urgent medical attention.
  • Severe hypoglycaemia: Confusion, seizures, or loss of consciousness, requiring immediate glucose administration to prevent neurological damage.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Diabetes mellitus"][/sc]
  • Older adults: May present with non-specific symptoms like fatigue, confusion, or recurrent infections, rather than classic polyuria/polydipsia. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Atypical presentations of diabetes in older adults: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Atypical presentations in older adults"][/sc]
  • Lean individuals with type 2 diabetes: May be misdiagnosed as type 1, but often have significant insulin resistance despite normal weight.
  • Monogenic diabetes (MODY): Often misdiagnosed as type 1 or type 2, presenting in younger individuals with a strong family history, but without autoimmune markers or severe insulin resistance.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Diabetes mellitus"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected diabetes: Fasting plasma glucose (≥7.0 mmol/L), random plasma glucose (≥11.1 mmol/L with symptoms), or HbA1c (≥48 mmol/mol [6.5%]) [7]. Oral glucose tolerance test (OGTT) may be used if results are equivocal.
  • Severity/risk tools: Consider using a cardiovascular risk assessment tool like QRISK3 for primary prevention in individuals with diabetes. QRISK3 on MDCalc [sc name="jitl-query" Q="HbA1c interpretation: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="HbA1c interpretation"][/sc]
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Autoantibody testing (e.g., GAD antibodies, islet cell antibodies) for type 1 diabetes, C-peptide levels to assess endogenous insulin production.
  • Monitoring: Regular HbA1c, lipid profile, renal function tests (eGFR, ACR), and urine albumin-to-creatinine ratio.

  • Middle-aged male with metabolic syndrome and chronic hyperglycaemia, concerning for type 2 diabetes with evolving complications.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Diabetes mellitus"][/sc]
  • Type 1 Diabetes Mellitus: Distinguished by autoimmune destruction of beta cells, leading to absolute insulin deficiency, often presenting acutely with DKA. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Type 1 vs Type 2 Diabetes: what are the key differences and why do they matter clinically?" text="Type 1 vs Type 2 Diabetes"][/sc]
  • Gestational Diabetes Mellitus: Glucose intolerance first recognised during pregnancy, typically resolving postpartum but increasing future risk of type 2 diabetes.
  • Drug-induced hyperglycaemia: Certain medications (e.g., corticosteroids, thiazide diuretics) can cause elevated blood glucose levels.
  • Pancreatic disease: Conditions like pancreatitis or pancreatic cancer can impair insulin production.
  • Other endocrine disorders: Cushing\'s syndrome or acromegaly can lead to secondary diabetes.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Diabetes mellitus"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Recognition and management of acute complications such as DKA or HHS. Patient education on self-management, including diet, exercise, and blood glucose monitoring.
  • Definitive management: Individualised glycaemic control targets. Lifestyle modifications (dietary changes, increased physical activity). Pharmacological interventions, initially often metformin for type 2 diabetes. Insulin therapy for type 1 diabetes and often for advanced type 2 diabetes.
  • Adjunctive management: Management of cardiovascular risk factors (hypertension, dyslipidaemia) with appropriate medications (e.g., ACE inhibitors, statins). [sc name="jitl-medication" Q="SGLT2 inhibitors use in Diabetes Mellitus" text="SGLT2 inhibitors"][/sc] No doses or frequencies are cited here — always verify against your local antimicrobial/prescribing formulary before prescribing.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Regular review of glycaemic control (HbA1c), blood pressure, lipid profile, renal function, and foot examination. Annual screening for retinopathy.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Referral to specialist diabetes services for complex management, recurrent hypoglycaemia, or advanced complications. Education on sick day rules and when to seek urgent medical advice.
  • Exam pharmacology reference (UKMLA/MRCGP): Metformin, SGLT2 inhibitors, GLP-1 receptor agonists, sulfonylureas, insulin.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Diabetes mellitus"][/sc]
  • Diabetic Ketoacidosis (DKA): Acute, life-threatening complication of severe insulin deficiency, leading to hyperglycaemia, ketosis, and metabolic acidosis, typically occurring in type 1 diabetes but can occur in type 2 under severe stress. Requires urgent fluid resuscitation, insulin, and electrolyte correction.
  • Hyperosmolar Hyperglycaemic State (HHS): Severe hyperglycaemia, hyperosmolarity, and dehydration without significant ketosis, primarily in type 2 diabetes, often triggered by illness or infection. Develops gradually over days to weeks.
  • Hypoglycaemia: Blood glucose levels below 4.0 mmol/L, a common complication of insulin or sulfonylurea therapy, requiring prompt glucose intake to prevent neuroglycopenic symptoms and potential loss of consciousness.
  • Diabetic Retinopathy: Damage to the blood vessels in the retina, leading to vision impairment and blindness if untreated. Develops progressively over years of hyperglycaemia.
  • Diabetic Nephropathy: Progressive kidney disease due to damage to renal microvasculature, leading to proteinuria and eventual renal failure. Develops over years.
  • Diabetic Neuropathy: Nerve damage affecting peripheral nerves (sensory, motor) or autonomic nerves, leading to symptoms like numbness, pain, gastroparesis, or erectile dysfunction. Develops gradually over years.
