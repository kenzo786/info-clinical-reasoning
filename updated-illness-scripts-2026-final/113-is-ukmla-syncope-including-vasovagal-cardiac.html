Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Syncope including vasovagal cardiac"][/sc]
  • Syncope is a transient loss of consciousness due to global cerebral hypoperfusion, characterised by rapid onset, short duration, and spontaneous complete recovery.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the common causes of transient loss of consciousness, differentiate syncope from other causes, and identify red flag features indicating serious underlying pathology.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Syncope including vasovagal cardiac"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Syncope including vasovagal cardiac"][/sc]
  • Syncope is a common presentation, accounting for 3% of emergency department attendances and 1% of hospital admissions in the UK.
  • Vasovagal syncope is the most common cause, particularly in younger individuals, while cardiac causes become more prevalent with increasing age and comorbidities.
  • Recurrence rates are high, with up to a third of individuals experiencing another syncopal episode within 3 years.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Syncope including vasovagal cardiac"][/sc]
  • Syncope results from a temporary reduction in cerebral blood flow, typically due to a transient fall in systemic blood pressure or cardiac output.
  • Vasovagal syncope involves an exaggerated autonomic reflex leading to peripheral vasodilation and/or bradycardia, reducing venous return and cardiac output. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Vasovagal reflex: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Vasovagal reflex"][/sc]
  • Cardiac syncope arises from structural heart disease or arrhythmias that impair the heart\'s ability to maintain adequate cerebral perfusion.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Syncope including vasovagal cardiac"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Prodromal symptoms such as light-headedness, nausea, sweating, or visual disturbance may precede vasovagal syncope by seconds to minutes. Cardiac syncope often has an abrupt onset with no prodrome.
  • Progressive phase: Loss of consciousness is typically brief, lasting seconds to a few minutes, with rapid and complete recovery.
  • Late phase: Post-syncopal confusion or fatigue may persist for several minutes to hours, particularly beyond 10 minutes, but focal neurological deficits are usually absent.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Syncope including vasovagal cardiac"][/sc]
  • Ask about prodromal symptoms - to differentiate vasovagal syncope (often preceded by nausea, sweating, pallor) from cardiac syncope (typically abrupt onset).
  • Ask about triggers - specific situations like prolonged standing, emotional stress, or pain suggest vasovagal causes.
  • Ask about posture at onset - syncope occurring from a supine position is highly suspicious for a cardiac cause.
  • Ask about exertional syncope - loss of consciousness during or immediately after physical exertion is a key red flag for cardiac syncope.
  • Ask about palpitations or chest pain - these may indicate an underlying arrhythmia or structural heart disease.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Syncope including vasovagal cardiac"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - bradycardia, hypotension, or significant orthostatic drop may be present, but often normal post-recovery.
  • Check for cardiac murmurs - new or changed murmurs may indicate structural heart disease (e.g., aortic stenosis).
  • Check for signs of heart failure - peripheral oedema, raised JVP, or crackles may suggest reduced cardiac output.
  • Check neurological examination - focal neurological deficits are not typical of syncope and suggest an alternative diagnosis.
  • Check for signs of injury - falls during syncope can result in trauma, which may be the presenting complaint. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Post-syncopal injury: what is its significance?" text="Post-syncopal injury"][/sc]
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Syncope including vasovagal cardiac"][/sc]
  • Syncope during exertion: Highly suggestive of a cardiac cause, such as outflow tract obstruction or arrhythmia.
  • Syncope in supine position: Almost always indicates a cardiac aetiology, as orthostatic mechanisms are excluded.
  • Palpitations or chest pain preceding syncope: May signify an underlying arrhythmia or acute coronary syndrome.
  • Family history of sudden cardiac death: Raises suspicion for inherited cardiac conditions.
  • New or unexplained structural heart disease: Indicates increased risk of cardiac syncope.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Syncope including vasovagal cardiac"][/sc]
  • Older adults: Syncope may present with less clear prodromal symptoms, and often has multifactorial causes including polypharmacy and orthostatic hypotension.
  • Children and adolescents: Vasovagal syncope is common, but cardiac causes, particularly inherited arrhythmias, must be considered.
  • Convulsive syncope: Brief myoclonic jerks can occur during syncope due to cerebral hypoxia, which may be mistaken for an epileptic seizure. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Convulsive syncope vs seizure: how to differentiate?" text="Convulsive syncope"][/sc]
  • Psychogenic pseudosyncope: Characterised by apparent loss of consciousness without true cerebral hypoperfusion, often with inconsistent features and eye closure.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Syncope including vasovagal cardiac"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected syncope: Full history and examination, lying and standing blood pressure, 12-lead ECG.
  • Severity/risk tools: The ROSE rule (San Francisco Syncope Rule equivalent) helps identify patients at high risk of serious outcomes. ROSE Rule on MDCalc [sc name="jitl-query" Q="ROSE rule: what are its components and utility?" text="ROSE rule"][/sc]
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Consider blood tests (e.g., FBC, U&Es, glucose, troponin if cardiac concern), echocardiogram if structural heart disease suspected.
  • Imaging - Echocardiography: Indicated if there is suspicion of structural heart disease.
  • Serial reassessment: Patients with unclear aetiology or recurrent episodes may benefit from prolonged ECG monitoring (e.g., Holter, implantable loop recorder).

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Syncope including vasovagal cardiac"][/sc]
  • Seizure: Often associated with tonic-clonic movements, post-ictal confusion lasting longer than syncope, and urinary incontinence.
  • Orthostatic Hypotension: Loss of consciousness upon standing, but often without the specific triggers or prodrome of vasovagal syncope. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Orthostatic hypotension: causes and diagnostic criteria?" text="Orthostatic hypotension"][/sc]
  • Hypoglycaemia: Typically has a slower onset, associated with sweating, tremor, and confusion, and responds to glucose administration.
  • Transient Ischaemic Attack (TIA): Usually presents with focal neurological deficits, not global loss of consciousness.
  • Psychogenic Pseudosyncope: Characterised by inconsistent features, often occurring in specific social contexts, and without objective signs of cerebral hypoperfusion.
  • Vertebrobasilar TIA: Can cause transient loss of consciousness but usually accompanied by other brainstem symptoms like diplopia, vertigo, or ataxia.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Syncope including vasovagal cardiac"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Ensure patient safety, assess for injuries, perform a thorough history and examination including ECG to identify high-risk features.
  • Definitive management: Directed by the underlying cause; for vasovagal syncope, education on avoidance of triggers and counter-pressure manoeuvres is key. Cardiac syncope requires management of the specific cardiac condition (e.g., antiarrhythmics, pacing, valve replacement).
  • Adjunctive management: For recurrent vasovagal syncope, increased fluid and salt intake may be considered.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Patients with unclear aetiology or recurrent episodes may benefit from specialist cardiology review and further investigation.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Advise patients on recognising prodromal symptoms, lying down promptly, and seeking urgent medical attention for recurrent or exertional syncope.
  • Exam pharmacology reference (UKMLA/MRCGP): Beta-blockers may be considered in some cases of recurrent vasovagal syncope, though evidence is mixed. Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Syncope including vasovagal cardiac"][/sc]
  • Physical injury: Falls during syncope can lead to fractures, head injuries, or soft tissue trauma, particularly in older adults or those with sudden onset syncope.
  • Recurrence: Syncope has a high recurrence rate, impacting quality of life and increasing the risk of future injury.
  • Underlying cardiac pathology: Unrecognised cardiac syncope carries a significant risk of sudden cardiac death, particularly if associated with structural heart disease or inherited arrhythmias.
  • Psychological impact: Fear of recurrence can lead to anxiety, depression, and avoidance behaviours, affecting daily activities and independence.
