Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Herpes simplex virus (Infections)"][/sc]
  • Herpes simplex virus (HSV) infection is a common viral infection that can cause a range of clinical manifestations, most commonly orolabial herpes (cold sores) and genital herpes, but also more severe conditions such as encephalitis and neonatal herpes.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the varied presentations of HSV infection, differentiate between HSV-1 and HSV-2, and identify red flag features such as neonatal herpes and HSV encephalitis requiring urgent management.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Herpes simplex virus (Infections)"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Herpes simplex virus (Infections)"][/sc]
  • HSV-1 is highly prevalent, with over 90% of adults globally infected, often acquired in childhood and primarily associated with orolabial lesions.
  • HSV-2 is predominantly sexually transmitted, causing genital herpes, with an estimated 205 million people aged 15–49 (5.3%) experiencing at least one symptomatic episode globally in 2020.
  • Neonatal herpes is rare in the UK, affecting approximately 1 in 50,000 live births, but carries significant morbidity and mortality.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Herpes simplex virus (Infections)"][/sc]
  • Initial infection with HSV involves viral entry into epithelial cells, followed by replication and establishment of latency in sensory nerve ganglia (e.g., trigeminal ganglion for orolabial, sacral ganglia for genital).
  • Reactivation of latent virus, triggered by factors such as stress, immunosuppression, or ultraviolet light, leads to viral replication and migration along nerve axons to the skin or mucous membranes, causing recurrent lesions.
  • In rare cases, HSV can spread to the central nervous system, causing conditions like HSV encephalitis, often involving the temporal lobes, or disseminate systemically in neonates due to an immature immune system. Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Herpes simplex virus (Infections)"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Primary HSV infection typically manifests 2-12 days after exposure, with symptoms such as painful vesicles, ulcers, and systemic features like fever and malaise, resolving within 2-4 weeks.
  • Progressive phase: Following primary infection, the virus establishes latency. Recurrent episodes can occur with varying frequency, often preceded by prodromal symptoms like tingling or itching, with lesions typically healing within 7-10 days.
  • Late phase: In severe manifestations like HSV encephalitis, symptoms can progress rapidly over hours to days, leading to neurological deterioration. Neonatal herpes can also present within the first few weeks of life with rapid progression if untreated.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Herpes simplex virus (Infections)"][/sc]
  • Ask about painful blisters or sores - these are characteristic of active HSV infection and help differentiate from other dermatological conditions.
  • Ask about tingling, itching, or burning sensations - these prodromal symptoms often precede lesion development, indicating viral reactivation.
  • Ask about location of lesions (oral, genital, other) - helps distinguish between HSV-1 and HSV-2 predominant sites and guides further investigation.
  • Ask about fever, malaise, or lymphadenopathy - systemic symptoms are more common in primary infections and can indicate a more widespread viral response.
  • Ask about headache, altered consciousness, or focal neurological deficits - these are critical symptoms suggesting potential HSV encephalitis, requiring urgent assessment.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Herpes simplex virus (Infections)"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - fever may be present, especially in primary infections or severe systemic manifestations like encephalitis.
  • Check for grouped vesicles on an erythematous base - this classic appearance is highly suggestive of HSV infection.
  • Check for ulcerations or crusting lesions - these represent later stages of vesicular lesions and indicate active viral shedding.
  • Check for regional lymphadenopathy - often present in primary infections, indicating an immune response to the viral presence.
  • Check neurological examination for focal deficits, confusion, or seizures - these signs are crucial for identifying HSV encephalitis. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Temporal lobe involvement in HSV encephalitis: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Temporal lobe involvement"][/sc]
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Herpes simplex virus (Infections)"][/sc]
  • Neonatal herpes - rapid progression to severe systemic disease, including encephalitis, skin lesions, and disseminated infection, requiring immediate antiviral treatment.
  • HSV encephalitis - acute onset of fever, headache, altered mental status, and focal neurological signs (e.g., aphasia, seizures), indicating severe brain inflammation.
  • Ocular herpes - corneal involvement (dendritic ulcer) can lead to vision loss if not promptly treated.
  • Disseminated HSV in immunocompromised patients - widespread mucocutaneous lesions or visceral organ involvement, indicating impaired immune control.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Herpes simplex virus (Infections)"][/sc]
  • Immunocompromised patients: May present with chronic, non-healing ulcers, extensive mucocutaneous disease, or atypical visceral involvement (e.g., oesophagitis, pneumonitis) due to impaired immune response.
  • Women: Genital herpes can present with atypical symptoms such as vulval pain, dysuria, or vaginal discharge without overt vesicular lesions, leading to misdiagnosis.
  • Neonates: May present with non-specific symptoms like lethargy, poor feeding, irritability, or fever without characteristic skin lesions, making early diagnosis challenging. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Non-specific presentation of neonatal herpes: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Non-specific neonatal presentation"][/sc]
  • HSV encephalitis: Can rarely present with normal CSF findings or atypical imaging, delaying diagnosis and treatment.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Herpes simplex virus (Infections)"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected mucocutaneous HSV: Viral swab for HSV PCR from lesion base or vesicle fluid offers high sensitivity and specificity for active infection.
  • Core for suspected HSV encephalitis: Lumbar puncture for CSF analysis, including HSV PCR, is the gold standard. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="CSF PCR for HSV encephalitis: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="CSF PCR for HSV"][/sc]
  • Imaging - MRI brain: For suspected HSV encephalitis, MRI is preferred over CT to visualise characteristic temporal lobe involvement and oedema.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Serology for HSV antibodies can confirm past exposure but does not distinguish between active and latent infection, and is not useful for acute diagnosis.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Herpes simplex virus (Infections)"][/sc]
  • A 35-year-old male presents with a 3-day history of painful genital blisters, dysuria, and bilateral inguinal lymphadenopathy, following a new sexual contact one week prior.
  • A 68-year-old female with a history of recurrent cold sores presents with acute onset of fever, headache, confusion, and expressive dysphasia over 24 hours, concerning for viral encephalitis.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Herpes simplex virus (Infections)"][/sc]
  • Varicella-zoster virus (VZV) infection - typically presents with dermatomal distribution and often more widespread lesions, distinguishing it from localised HSV.
  • Syphilis (primary) - presents with a single, painless chancre, unlike the painful, grouped vesicles of HSV.
  • Chancroid - causes painful genital ulcers with ragged borders and often suppurative lymphadenopathy, but lesions are typically larger and deeper than HSV.
  • Behçet\'s disease - recurrent oral and genital ulceration, but also involves ocular and skin lesions, and systemic inflammation.
  • Fixed drug eruption - solitary or few lesions that recur at the same site with drug re-exposure, lacking the prodromal symptoms and typical vesicular morphology of HSV.
  • Other causes of encephalitis (e.g., bacterial, autoimmune) - distinguished by CSF findings, specific imaging patterns, and serological markers. Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Herpes simplex virus (Infections)"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: For suspected HSV encephalitis or neonatal herpes, prompt initiation of empirical intravenous antiviral therapy (e.g., aciclovir) is crucial to minimise morbidity and mortality, deferring to local neurological or paediatric infectious disease pathways.
  • Definitive management: Antiviral medications such as aciclovir, valaciclovir, or famciclovir are often used to reduce the duration and severity of primary and recurrent mucocutaneous HSV infections.
  • Adjunctive management: Symptomatic relief for mucocutaneous lesions may include analgesia, topical anaesthetics (e.g., lidocaine), and maintaining good hygiene.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: For genital herpes, BASHH guidelines recommend counselling on transmission risk, suppressive therapy for frequent recurrences, and partner notification.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Patients with severe or atypical presentations, immunocompromise, or suspected CNS involvement often warrant urgent specialist review and inpatient management.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Herpes simplex virus (Infections)"][/sc]
  • Post-encephalitic neurological sequelae: Cognitive impairment, epilepsy, and behavioural changes can persist after HSV encephalitis, particularly with delayed treatment.
  • Neonatal mortality and morbidity: Untreated neonatal herpes can lead to severe neurological damage, developmental delay, or death, often progressing rapidly within days.
  • Recurrent painful lesions: Frequent recurrences of orolabial or genital herpes can significantly impact quality of life due to pain, discomfort, and psychological distress.
  • Herpes keratitis: HSV infection of the eye can cause corneal scarring and vision loss if not managed promptly, often requiring topical antiviral agents.
  • Psychological impact: The stigma and recurrent nature of genital herpes can lead to significant psychological distress, anxiety, and impact on sexual health.
