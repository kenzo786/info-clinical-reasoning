Adverse drug effects (Drugs and drug effects) [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Adverse drug effects"]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Adverse drug effects"]
  • An adverse drug effect (ADE) is an appreciably harmful or unpleasant reaction, resulting from an intervention related to the use of a medicinal product, which is noxious and unintended [1].
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise and differentiate between various types of adverse drug reactions, including Type A (predictable, dose-related) and Type B (idiosyncratic, unpredictable), and understand their clinical implications for patient safety and management.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Adverse drug effects"]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Adverse drug effects"] [/sc]
  • Adverse drug reactions (ADRs) are a significant cause of morbidity and mortality, with approximately 5% of all hospital admissions in the UK attributed to ADRs [1].
  • The prevalence of ADRs causing hospital admission in the UK has been reported between 6.5% and 16.5% in various studies [4, 5, 8].
  • Within hospitals, ADRs are thought to occur in 10-20% of inpatients [10]. In primary care, the reported prevalence is 8.3%, with one-fifth being preventable [6].
  • Risk factors include polypharmacy, extremes of age (very young and elderly), multiple comorbidities, genetic predispositions, and impaired organ function (e.g., renal or hepatic impairment).
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Adverse drug effects"]
  • Trigger: Adverse drug effects are initiated by drug exposure, either through normal pharmacological action (Type A reactions) or idiosyncratic responses (Type B reactions) [1].
  • Progression: Type A reactions, such as dose-dependent side effects, progress as an exaggeration of the drug\'s intended action, while Type B reactions involve unpredictable immunological or genetic mechanisms leading to tissue damage or systemic dysfunction [1]. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Type A ADRs: what are they and why do they matter clinically?" text="Type A ADRs"][/sc] [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Type B ADRs: what are they and why do they matter clinically?" text="Type B ADRs"][/sc]
  • Consequence: The ultimate consequence ranges from mild, self-limiting symptoms to severe, life-threatening conditions such as anaphylaxis, Stevens-Johnson syndrome, or organ failure, depending on the type and severity of the reaction [1].
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Adverse drug effects"]
  • Early phase: Immediate reactions, such as anaphylaxis, can manifest within minutes to a few hours of drug exposure. Other reactions, like some Type A effects, may appear within hours to a few days, often correlating with drug concentration.
  • Progressive phase: If the offending drug is continued or the reaction is severe, symptoms can worsen over several days. This phase may involve the development of more systemic signs, such as organ dysfunction in severe cases of SJS/TEN or escalating neurological symptoms in serotonin syndrome.
  • Late phase: Some adverse drug effects, particularly Type B reactions, can have a delayed onset, appearing days to weeks after initial exposure. Chronic adverse effects may persist for weeks or months after drug discontinuation, particularly beyond the acute resolution of symptoms.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Adverse drug effects"]
  • Ask about skin changes (rash, blistering, itching) - to differentiate between common allergic reactions, drug eruptions, and severe cutaneous adverse reactions like SJS/TEN.
  • Ask about swelling (face, lips, tongue, throat) - to identify angioedema or early signs of anaphylaxis, which can rapidly compromise the airway.
  • Ask about respiratory symptoms (shortness of breath, wheezing, stridor) - to assess for bronchospasm or laryngeal oedema, critical features of anaphylaxis.
  • Ask about mental state changes (confusion, agitation, anxiety, tremor) - to screen for neurological involvement, particularly in conditions like serotonin syndrome.
  • Ask about gastrointestinal upset (nausea, vomiting, diarrhoea, abdominal pain) - as these can be non-specific but also prominent in many drug reactions, including early anaphylaxis or drug-induced enteropathy.
  • Ask about fever or flu-like symptoms (malaise, myalgia, arthralgia) - as these can be prodromal symptoms for severe adverse drug reactions such as SJS/TEN.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Adverse drug effects"]
  • Check vital signs (heart rate, blood pressure, respiratory rate, temperature, oxygen saturation) - to identify systemic compromise such as hypotension in anaphylaxis, tachycardia/hypertension in serotonin syndrome, or fever in SJS/TEN.
  • Check skin for lesions (erythema, macules, papules, blisters, epidermal detachment) - to assess the morphology and extent of cutaneous reactions, crucial for diagnosing conditions like SJS/TEN. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Epidermal detachment: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Epidermal detachment"][/sc]
  • Check mucous membranes (oral, ocular, genital) for erosions or blistering - as involvement of these sites is a key diagnostic feature of SJS/TEN.
  • Check neurological status (reflexes, muscle tone, clonus, agitation) - to detect signs of central nervous system excitation or depression, particularly relevant for serotonin syndrome.
  • Check for angioedema (facial, periorbital, laryngeal) - to identify swelling that may indicate a severe allergic reaction or anaphylaxis, potentially leading to airway obstruction.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Adverse drug effects"]
  • Rapid onset of symptoms: Suggests an immediate hypersensitivity reaction, such as anaphylaxis, requiring urgent intervention.
  • Widespread blistering or epidermal detachment: Indicates severe cutaneous adverse reactions like Stevens-Johnson syndrome (SJS) or toxic epidermal necrolysis (TEN), necessitating immediate hospitalisation and specialist care.
  • Airway compromise (stridor, wheeze, hoarseness): Points to laryngeal oedema or bronchospasm, critical features of anaphylaxis that can rapidly lead to respiratory arrest.
  • Haemodynamic instability (hypotension, tachycardia): Suggests systemic shock, commonly seen in severe anaphylaxis or other severe systemic reactions.
  • Altered mental status with neuromuscular hyperactivity (clonus, hyperreflexia): Highly indicative of serotonin syndrome, especially in patients on serotonergic medications, requiring prompt drug discontinuation and supportive care.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Adverse drug effects"]
  • Older adults: May present with non-specific symptoms, cognitive impairment, or falls, making ADRs harder to recognise due to polypharmacy and altered drug metabolism. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Polypharmacy in older adults: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Polypharmacy"][/sc]
  • Children: Symptoms can be non-verbal or mimic common childhood illnesses, leading to delayed diagnosis. Dose-related errors are also more common due to weight-based dosing.
  • Immunosuppressed patients: May have altered immune responses, leading to unusual or exaggerated Type B reactions, or present with opportunistic infections mimicking drug reactions.
  • Patients with pre-existing organ dysfunction (renal/hepatic): Reduced drug clearance can lead to accumulation and dose-dependent (Type A) reactions at therapeutic doses, necessitating careful dose adjustments.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Adverse drug effects"]
  • Core for suspected adverse drug effect: Detailed drug history (including over-the-counter, herbal, and illicit drugs), timing of symptom onset relative to drug initiation, and assessment of drug withdrawal/rechallenge response.
  • Causality assessment: The Naranjo algorithm can be used to assess the probability of a drug causing an adverse reaction, aiding in clinical decision-making and reporting. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Naranjo algorithm: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Naranjo algorithm"][/sc]
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Blood tests (e.g., full blood count, electrolytes, renal and liver function tests) to rule out other systemic illnesses or organ dysfunction. Inflammatory markers (CRP, ESR) may be elevated in some reactions.
  • Imaging - Chest X-ray: If respiratory symptoms are prominent, to assess for pulmonary involvement (e.g., drug-induced pneumonitis).
  • Biopsy - Skin biopsy: In severe cutaneous reactions (e.g., SJS/TEN) to confirm diagnosis and assess the depth of epidermal necrosis.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Adverse drug effects"]
  • Infection (e.g., viral exanthem, sepsis) - often lacks clear drug exposure history, may have different rash morphology or systemic features not typical of ADRs.
  • Autoimmune conditions (e.g., lupus, vasculitis) - typically chronic, may have specific autoantibodies, and often lack acute drug-related onset.
  • Other allergic reactions (e.g., contact dermatitis, urticaria) - usually localised or transient, without systemic features or mucosal involvement seen in severe ADRs.
  • Toxic exposures (e.g., chemical burns, heavy metal poisoning) - history of exposure to toxins rather than therapeutic drugs.
  • Organ dysfunction from underlying disease (e.g., liver failure from hepatitis, renal failure from glomerulonephritis) - may mimic drug-induced organ damage but with different aetiology. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Drug-induced liver injury: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Drug-induced liver injury"][/sc]
  • Neuroleptic Malignant Syndrome - distinguished from serotonin syndrome by slower onset, \'lead-pipe\' rigidity, and typically associated with dopamine antagonists.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Adverse drug effects"]
  • Immediate priorities: Recognition and prompt discontinuation of the suspected causative drug. Escalation to appropriate specialist care (e.g., intensive care, dermatology, allergy) and initiation of supportive measures (e.g., airway management, fluid resuscitation, temperature control).
  • Definitive management: Primarily involves withdrawal of the offending agent. Specific interventions may include antihistamines for urticaria, corticosteroids for some inflammatory reactions, or specific antidotes where available (e.g., cyproheptadine for severe serotonin syndrome).
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Close monitoring for resolution of symptoms and complications. Education of the patient regarding the identified drug allergy/reaction and provision of an alert card or similar documentation.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Advise patients on symptoms requiring urgent medical review and ensure clear communication with primary care and other specialists involved in the patient\'s care. Reporting of suspected ADRs to the Yellow Card scheme is crucial for pharmacovigilance.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Adverse drug effects"]
  • Organ failure: Severe adverse drug reactions can lead to acute kidney injury, liver failure, or respiratory failure, particularly in the progressive and late phases if the offending agent is not withdrawn promptly.
  • Secondary infections: Extensive skin damage in conditions like SJS/TEN significantly increases the risk of bacterial or fungal superinfections, which can lead to sepsis and further morbidity.
  • Cardiovascular collapse: In severe anaphylaxis, profound hypotension and shock can lead to cardiovascular collapse, requiring immediate resuscitation and potentially prolonged critical care.
  • Neurological sequelae: Prolonged or severe serotonin syndrome can result in seizures, coma, and potentially long-term neurological deficits if not managed aggressively in the early and progressive phases.
  • Psychological impact: Experiencing a severe adverse drug reaction can lead to significant psychological distress, including anxiety, depression, and post-traumatic stress disorder, affecting long-term quality of life.
