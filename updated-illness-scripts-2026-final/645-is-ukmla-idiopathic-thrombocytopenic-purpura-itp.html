Idiopathic thrombocytopenic purpura (ITP) [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Idiopathic thrombocytopenic purpura (ITP)"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Idiopathic thrombocytopenic purpura (ITP)"][/sc]
  • Idiopathic thrombocytopenic purpura (ITP) is an acquired autoimmune disorder characterised by isolated thrombocytopenia (platelet count <100 x 109/L) in the absence of other causes, leading to an increased risk of bleeding.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical features of ITP, differentiate it from other causes of thrombocytopenia, and outline initial management strategies including indications for specialist referral.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Idiopathic thrombocytopenic purpura (ITP)"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Idiopathic thrombocytopenic purpura (ITP)"][/sc]
  • ITP has a bimodal age distribution, with peaks in childhood (2-5 years) and adulthood (20-50 years, and >60 years). The annual incidence in the UK is approximately 2-5 per 100,000 adults and 4-5 per 100,000 children.
  • In adults, ITP is more common in females, particularly in younger age groups, with a female-to-male ratio of approximately 2-3:1. In children, there is no significant sex predominance.
  • Secondary ITP, accounting for about 20% of adult cases, is associated with conditions such as Helicobacter pylori infection, HIV, hepatitis C, systemic lupus erythematosus, and lymphoproliferative disorders.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Idiopathic thrombocytopenic purpura (ITP)"][/sc]
  • Trigger: Autoantibodies, primarily IgG, are directed against platelet surface glycoproteins (e.g., GPIIb/IIIa), leading to their opsonisation.
  • Progression: Opsonised platelets are recognised by Fc receptors on macrophages, predominantly in the spleen, leading to their premature destruction. There is also impaired platelet production in the bone marrow due to antibody-mediated megakaryocyte damage and suppression.
  • Consequence: The combined effect of increased platelet destruction and inadequate production results in isolated thrombocytopenia, increasing the risk of mucocutaneous bleeding. Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Idiopathic thrombocytopenic purpura (ITP)"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Acute ITP, particularly in children, often follows a viral infection and typically resolves spontaneously within 6 months. Platelet counts can drop rapidly, leading to sudden onset of bleeding symptoms.
  • Progressive phase: If thrombocytopenia persists beyond 6 months, it is classified as persistent ITP. This phase may involve fluctuating platelet counts and recurrent bleeding episodes, often requiring ongoing management.
  • Late phase: Chronic ITP is diagnosed when thrombocytopenia lasts for more than 12 months. This form is more common in adults and often requires long-term treatment to maintain safe platelet levels and prevent severe haemorrhage.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Idiopathic thrombocytopenic purpura (ITP)"][/sc]
  • Ask about easy bruising or purpura - indicates capillary fragility and extravasation of red blood cells due to low platelet count.
  • Ask about petechiae - pinpoint, non-blanching red spots, often on dependent areas, suggesting superficial capillary bleeding.
  • Ask about mucosal bleeding (epistaxis, gingival bleeding, menorrhagia) - signifies impaired primary haemostasis and is a common presentation.
  • Ask about fatigue - can be a non-specific symptom, but may indicate underlying anaemia from chronic blood loss or the impact of the autoimmune process itself.
  • Ask about recent viral illness or new medications - helps differentiate primary ITP from secondary causes or drug-induced thrombocytopenia.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Idiopathic thrombocytopenic purpura (ITP)"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - assess for tachycardia and hypotension, which may indicate significant haemorrhage, particularly in the context of active bleeding.
  • Check for purpura and petechiae - visible evidence of bleeding into the skin, often most prominent on the lower limbs.
  • Check for ecchymoses - larger areas of bruising, indicating more extensive subcutaneous bleeding.
  • Check for signs of mucosal bleeding - inspect the oral cavity for gingival bleeding, epistaxis, or blood blisters (wet purpura). [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Wet purpura: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Wet purpura"][/sc]
  • Check for splenomegaly or lymphadenopathy - typically absent in primary ITP; their presence suggests a secondary cause of thrombocytopenia.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Idiopathic thrombocytopenic purpura (ITP)"][/sc]
  • Wet purpura: Presence of blood blisters in the mouth or other mucosal surfaces indicates a higher risk of severe haemorrhage, including intracranial bleeding.
  • Neurological symptoms: Headache, visual changes, or altered mental status suggest potential intracranial haemorrhage, a life-threatening complication requiring immediate intervention.
  • Significant active bleeding: Gastrointestinal bleeding, haematuria, or heavy menorrhagia that is ongoing or causing haemodynamic instability necessitates urgent assessment and management.
  • Rapidly falling platelet count: A swift decline in platelet count, particularly to very low levels (<10 x 109/L), increases the immediate risk of spontaneous severe bleeding.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Idiopathic thrombocytopenic purpura (ITP)"][/sc]
  • Elderly patients: May present with more severe bleeding at higher platelet counts, or have concomitant comorbidities and medications that complicate management.
  • ITP in pregnancy: Can pose risks to both mother and foetus. Management requires careful consideration of treatments that are safe in pregnancy and monitoring for neonatal alloimmune thrombocytopenia. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="ITP in pregnancy: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="ITP in pregnancy"][/sc]
  • Secondary ITP: Presentation may be dominated by symptoms of the underlying condition (e.g., systemic lupus erythematosus, chronic lymphocytic leukaemia), making the diagnosis of ITP less obvious.
  • Asymptomatic ITP: Incidental finding of low platelet count on routine blood tests, often with platelet counts >30 x 109/L and no bleeding manifestations.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Idiopathic thrombocytopenic purpura (ITP)"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected ITP:
    • Full blood count (FBC) with blood film: Reveals isolated thrombocytopenia. The blood film is crucial to exclude pseudothrombocytopenia and other causes of thrombocytopenia (e.g., schistocytes in TTP).
    • Coagulation screen (PT, APTT, fibrinogen): Typically normal, confirming isolated platelet defect.
    • Liver and renal function tests: To assess for underlying organ dysfunction or secondary causes.
    • Thyroid function tests: To exclude thyroid disease as a secondary cause.
    • Direct antiglobulin test (DAT): To exclude autoimmune haemolytic anaemia (Evans syndrome).
    • HIV, Hepatitis C, Helicobacter pylori serology: To screen for common infectious causes of secondary ITP.
  • Severity/risk tools: Platelet count alone does not always correlate with bleeding risk. Clinical bleeding scores (e.g., ITP-BAT) can help guide management.   • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible:
    • Bone marrow aspirate and trephine biopsy: Considered in older patients (>60 years), those not responding to first-line treatment, or if other cytopenias or abnormal cells are present, to exclude myelodysplastic syndromes or leukaemia.
    • Autoantibody screen (ANA, anti-dsDNA): If systemic lupus erythematosus is suspected.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Idiopathic thrombocytopenic purpura (ITP)"][/sc]
  • Drug-induced thrombocytopenia: Certain medications (e.g., heparin, quinine, sulphonamides) can cause platelet destruction; often resolves upon drug withdrawal.
  • Thrombotic thrombocytopenic purpura (TTP): Characterised by microangiopathic haemolytic anaemia, thrombocytopenia, neurological symptoms, renal impairment, and fever; requires urgent differentiation due to high mortality. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Thrombotic thrombocytopenic purpura: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="Thrombotic thrombocytopenic purpura"][/sc]
  • Haemolytic uraemic syndrome (HUS): Similar to TTP but often associated with diarrhoeal illness (E. coli O157:H7) and more prominent renal involvement.
  • Systemic lupus erythematosus (SLE): Autoimmune disease that can cause thrombocytopenia as one of its manifestations, often with other systemic features.
  • Myelodysplastic syndromes (MDS): Clonal haematopoietic stem cell disorders that can present with isolated cytopenias, including thrombocytopenia, particularly in older adults.
  • Viral infections (e.g., HIV, Hepatitis C, EBV): Can cause thrombocytopenia through various mechanisms, including direct bone marrow suppression or immune-mediated destruction.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Idiopathic thrombocytopenic purpura (ITP)"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Assessment of bleeding severity and risk. Patients with significant bleeding or very low platelet counts (<20-30 x 109/L) often warrant urgent intervention.
  • Definitive management:
    • First-line: Corticosteroids (e.g., prednisolone or dexamethasone) are typically used to suppress autoantibody production and reduce platelet destruction. Intravenous immunoglobulin (IVIG) may be considered for rapid platelet count increase in severe bleeding.
    • Second-line: For patients refractory to or relapsing after first-line therapy, options include rituximab (anti-CD20 monoclonal antibody), thrombopoietin receptor agonists (TPO-RAs) such as eltrombopag or romiplostim, or splenectomy.
  • Adjunctive management: Avoidance of antiplatelet drugs (e.g., aspirin, NSAIDs) and intramuscular injections. Management of underlying secondary causes if identified.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Regular monitoring of platelet counts and clinical bleeding. Long-term follow-up with haematology is often required for chronic ITP.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Patients should be advised on symptoms of severe bleeding (e.g., severe headache, melaena) and when to seek urgent medical attention.
  • Exam pharmacology reference (UKMLA/MRCGP):
    • [sc name="jitl-medication" Q="Corticosteroids use in ITP" text="Corticosteroids"][/sc]: Immunosuppressive and anti-inflammatory effects, reducing antibody production and platelet destruction.
    • [sc name="jitl-medication" Q="IVIG use in ITP" text="Intravenous immunoglobulin (IVIG)"][/sc]: Blocks Fc receptors on macrophages, preventing platelet destruction, and modulates immune response.
    • [sc name="jitl-medication" Q="Rituximab use in ITP" text="Rituximab"][/sc]: Monoclonal antibody targeting CD20 B-lymphocytes, reducing autoantibody production.
    • [sc name="jitl-medication" Q="TPO-RAs use in ITP" text="Thrombopoietin receptor agonists (TPO-RAs)"][/sc]: Stimulate megakaryopoiesis and platelet production.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Idiopathic thrombocytopenic purpura (ITP)"][/sc]
  • Severe haemorrhage: The most serious complication, including gastrointestinal bleeding, haematuria, and potentially life-threatening intracranial haemorrhage, particularly with very low platelet counts (<10 x 109/L) or wet purpura. This risk is highest in the early phase of acute ITP or during severe relapses of chronic ITP.
  • Anaemia: Can develop due to chronic blood loss from recurrent bleeding episodes, contributing to fatigue and reduced quality of life.
  • Treatment-related side effects: Long-term corticosteroid use can lead to osteoporosis, diabetes, hypertension, and increased infection risk. Splenectomy carries risks of infection (e.g., overwhelming post-splenectomy infection) and thrombosis.
  • Reduced quality of life: Chronic fatigue, fear of bleeding, and side effects of treatment can significantly impact a patient\'s physical and psychological well-being, particularly in the progressive and late phases of the disease.
