Retinal detachment [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Retinal detachment"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Retinal detachment"][/sc]
  • A retinal detachment occurs when the neurosensory retina separates from the underlying retinal pigment epithelium. This can lead to vision loss if not treated promptly.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the signs and symptoms of retinal detachment and initiate appropriate referral.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Retinal detachment"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Retinal detachment"][/sc]
  • Incidence in the UK is approximately 1 in 10,000 per year, with a higher prevalence in older adults and myopic individuals [1].
  • Risk factors include high myopia, previous cataract surgery, trauma, family history, and lattice degeneration [2].
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Retinal detachment"][/sc]
  • Trigger: A break or tear in the retina, often due to vitreous detachment, allows fluid to pass from the vitreous cavity into the subretinal space.
  • Progression: Accumulation of subretinal fluid separates the neurosensory retina from the retinal pigment epithelium, disrupting photoreceptor function.
  • Consequence: Ischaemia and degeneration of photoreceptors lead to progressive visual field loss and, if untreated, permanent blindness.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Retinal detachment"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Sudden onset of floaters and flashes, indicating acute posterior vitreous detachment which may precede a retinal tear.
  • Progressive phase: Gradual expansion of a visual field defect, often described as a "curtain" or "shadow" spreading across vision, particularly beyond a few hours to days.
  • Late phase: Macular involvement leading to central vision loss, which can become permanent if not reattached within days to weeks.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Retinal detachment"][/sc]
  • Ask about sudden onset of new floaters - often described as "cobwebs" or "specks", which may indicate vitreous haemorrhage or posterior vitreous detachment.
  • Ask about flashes of light (photopsia) - typically in the peripheral vision, suggesting vitreoretinal traction.
  • Ask about a dark curtain or shadow spreading across the vision - this is highly suggestive of a spreading retinal detachment.
  • Ask about blurred or distorted vision - particularly if central vision is affected, indicating macular involvement.
  • Ask about any recent eye trauma or surgery - these are significant risk factors for retinal detachment.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Retinal detachment"][/sc]
  • Check visual acuity - often reduced, especially if the macula is detached.
  • Check visual fields - a corresponding visual field defect, often superior, is characteristic.
  • Check pupillary reflexes - a relative afferent pupillary defect (RAPD) may be present in extensive detachments.
  • Check fundoscopy - grey, elevated retina with folds, often with a visible retinal tear. Vitreous pigment (Shafer\\'s sign) may be present.
  • Check vital signs - typically normal, as retinal detachment is a localised ocular condition.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Retinal detachment"][/sc]
  • Sudden, significant vision loss: Indicates potential macular involvement or extensive detachment requiring urgent assessment.
  • New onset of floaters and flashes: Suggests acute posterior vitreous detachment with possible retinal tear, necessitating prompt dilated fundal examination.
  • Visual field defect (curtain/shadow): Highly indicative of retinal detachment and requires immediate ophthalmological referral.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Retinal detachment"][/sc]
  • Inferior retinal detachment: May progress more slowly as gravity can hold the retina in place longer, but still requires urgent treatment.
  • Paediatric retinal detachment: Often associated with trauma, genetic conditions (e.g., Marfan syndrome), or prematurity, and can be challenging to diagnose due to non-verbal children.
  • Post-operative retinal detachment: Can occur after cataract surgery or other intraocular procedures, requiring vigilance in the post-operative period.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Retinal detachment"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected retinal detachment: Dilated fundal examination with indirect ophthalmoscopy and slit-lamp biomicroscopy with a Volk lens to visualise the entire retina and identify tears or detachment.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Optical Coherence Tomography (OCT) may differentiate between retinal detachment and other macular pathologies like retinoschisis or macular oedema.
  • Imaging - B-scan ultrasonography: Useful when the view to the retina is obscured (e.g., dense cataract, vitreous haemorrhage) to confirm and characterise the detachment.
  • Serial reassessment: For suspected but unconfirmed cases, repeat dilated fundal examination within 24-48 hours may be necessary, especially if symptoms are progressing.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Retinal detachment"][/sc]
  • A 68-year-old male with a history of high myopia presents with a sudden increase in floaters, flashes of light, and a new "curtain" obscuring his superior visual field, developing over the past 24 hours.
  • An elderly, myopic male with acute, progressive monocular visual field loss and photopsia, concerning for rhegmatogenous retinal detachment.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Retinal detachment"][/sc]
  • Posterior Vitreous Detachment (PVD) - typically causes floaters and flashes but no visual field defect or retinal elevation.
  • Vitreous Haemorrhage - sudden onset of floaters or blurred vision, but fundoscopy may show blood rather than a detached retina.
  • Retinoschisis - a splitting of the retinal layers, usually asymptomatic and stable, but can be confused with detachment on imaging.
  • Retinal Tear without Detachment - causes flashes and floaters, but no fluid in the subretinal space or visual field defect.
  • Migraine Aura - transient visual disturbances, often geometric patterns, usually bilateral and resolve completely.
  • Ischaemic Optic Neuropathy - sudden, painless vision loss, often with an altitudinal field defect, but fundoscopy shows optic disc oedema rather than retinal detachment.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Retinal detachment"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Urgent referral to ophthalmology for assessment and surgical planning. Keep the patient supine with the head turned to encourage the retina to settle, especially if the macula is threatened.
  • Definitive management: Surgical repair, which may include scleral buckling, vitrectomy, or pneumatic retinopexy, depending on the type and extent of detachment.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Post-operative monitoring for re-detachment, infection, or other complications. Regular follow-up appointments are essential.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Advise patients to seek immediate medical attention for any new or worsening visual symptoms.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Retinal detachment"][/sc]
  • Permanent vision loss: If the macula is detached for a prolonged period (days to weeks), photoreceptor damage can become irreversible, leading to permanent central vision loss.
  • Proliferative vitreoretinopathy (PVR): Formation of scar tissue on the retinal surface, which can cause traction and re-detachment, making subsequent repairs more challenging.
  • Vitreous haemorrhage: Bleeding into the vitreous cavity, which can obscure vision and make diagnosis and treatment more difficult.
  • Glaucoma: Secondary glaucoma can develop due to inflammation or mechanical obstruction of aqueous outflow.
  • Cataract: Can be a complication of vitrectomy surgery or prolonged retinal detachment.
