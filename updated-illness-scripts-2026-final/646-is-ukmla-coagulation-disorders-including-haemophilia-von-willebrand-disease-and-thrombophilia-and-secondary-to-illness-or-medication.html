Coagulation disorders [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Coagulation disorders"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Coagulation disorders"][/sc]
  • A group of conditions characterised by impaired blood clotting, leading to excessive bleeding or thrombosis.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical presentation and initial management of common coagulation disorders.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Coagulation disorders"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Coagulation disorders"][/sc]
  • Haemophilia A affects approximately 1 in 5,000 males in the UK, with haemophilia B being less common.
  • Von Willebrand disease is the most common inherited bleeding disorder, affecting up to 1% of the UK population, though often undiagnosed.
  • Risk factors for acquired coagulation disorders include severe liver disease, vitamin K deficiency, and certain medications.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Coagulation disorders"][/sc]
  • Trigger: Genetic mutations or acquired factors lead to deficiencies or dysfunction of clotting factors or platelets.
  • Progression: Impaired primary or secondary haemostasis results in an unstable clot formation or excessive clot breakdown.
  • Consequence: Manifests as either prolonged bleeding (e.g., haemophilia) or inappropriate thrombosis (e.g., thrombophilia).
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Coagulation disorders"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Acute bleeding episodes or thrombotic events, often presenting within hours to days of an inciting factor.
  • Progressive phase: Recurrent or worsening symptoms if underlying disorder is untreated, particularly beyond weeks.
  • Late phase: Chronic complications such as arthropathy in haemophilia or recurrent venous thromboembolism in thrombophilia.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Coagulation disorders"][/sc]
  • Ask about easy bruising - may indicate platelet dysfunction or capillary fragility.
  • Ask about prolonged bleeding after minor cuts or dental procedures - suggests a defect in primary haemostasis.
  • Ask about spontaneous joint or muscle bleeds - highly suggestive of severe clotting factor deficiency like haemophilia.
  • Ask about heavy menstrual bleeding (menorrhagia) - a common presentation of Von Willebrand disease in females.
  • Ask about recurrent deep vein thrombosis or pulmonary embolism - points towards a thrombophilic state.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Coagulation disorders"][/sc]
  • Check for petechiae or purpura - implies platelet disorder or vasculitis.
  • Check for ecchymoses or haematomas - indicates a defect in coagulation factors.
  • Check for haemarthrosis (swollen, painful joints) - classic sign of haemophilia.
  • Check vital signs - assess for signs of hypovolaemic shock in acute bleeding.
  • Check for signs of deep vein thrombosis (e.g., unilateral leg swelling, tenderness) - relevant in thrombophilia.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Coagulation disorders"][/sc]
  • Intracranial haemorrhage - life-threatening bleeding requiring urgent neurosurgical and haematological intervention.
  • Airway compromise due to bleeding - e.g., neck haematoma, necessitates immediate airway management.
  • Haemodynamic instability - indicates significant blood loss and impending shock.
  • New onset severe bleeding with no obvious cause - may suggest an acquired coagulation disorder or severe underlying pathology.
  • Recurrent thrombotic events in unusual sites - e.g., cerebral venous sinus thrombosis, mesenteric ischaemia, suggests a strong thrombophilic tendency.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Coagulation disorders"][/sc]
  • Older adults: May present with bleeding secondary to polypharmacy (e.g., anticoagulants) or underlying malignancy, rather than inherited disorders.
  • Pregnancy: Coagulation factors naturally increase, but some disorders (e.g., Von Willebrand disease) can worsen or first manifest with obstetric haemorrhage.
  • Mild haemophilia: May only present with bleeding after significant trauma or surgery, often going undiagnosed until adulthood.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Coagulation disorders"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected bleeding disorder: Full blood count (FBC), prothrombin time (PT), activated partial thromboplastin time (APTT), thrombin time (TT), fibrinogen.
  • If Von Willebrand disease suspected: Von Willebrand factor antigen, ristocetin cofactor activity, factor VIII activity.
  • If thrombophilia suspected: Antithrombin, Protein C, Protein S, Factor V Leiden mutation, Prothrombin gene mutation.
  • Imaging - Ultrasound/CT: To identify sites of occult bleeding or thrombosis.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Coagulation disorders"][/sc]
  • A 28-year-old male presents with recurrent, painful swelling in his right knee for the past three days, following a minor twist during football, with a history of easy bruising since childhood.
  • Young male with chronic, recurrent haemarthrosis and ecchymoses, suggestive of an inherited bleeding diathesis.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Coagulation disorders"][/sc]
  • Platelet dysfunction - normal coagulation screen but prolonged bleeding time.
  • Vitamin K deficiency - prolonged PT and APTT, responsive to vitamin K.
  • Disseminated intravascular coagulation (DIC) - prolonged PT/APTT, low platelets, elevated D-dimer.
  • Liver disease - prolonged PT/APTT, low clotting factors, often with other signs of liver dysfunction.
  • Non-accidental injury - inconsistent history, multiple injuries of varying ages.
  • Connective tissue disorders (e.g., Ehlers-Danlos syndrome) - skin hyperextensibility, joint hypermobility, easy bruising.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Coagulation disorders"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Control acute bleeding, maintain haemodynamic stability, replace deficient clotting factors or platelets as indicated.
  • Definitive management: Factor replacement therapy (e.g., recombinant factor VIII for haemophilia A), desmopressin (for type 1 Von Willebrand disease), anticoagulation (for thrombophilia).
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Regular haematology review, monitoring for complications, patient education on self-management and emergency protocols.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Advise patients on when to seek urgent medical attention for bleeding or thrombotic symptoms.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Coagulation disorders"][/sc]
  • Chronic arthropathy: Repeated haemarthroses in haemophilia lead to joint damage and functional impairment, particularly beyond weeks of recurrent bleeds.
  • Inhibitor development: Immune response to replacement factors in haemophilia, making treatment challenging.
  • Recurrent venous thromboembolism: In thrombophilic states, leading to post-thrombotic syndrome or pulmonary hypertension.
  • Intracranial haemorrhage: A severe and life-threatening complication of severe bleeding disorders, often presenting acutely.
  • Anaemia: Chronic blood loss can lead to iron deficiency anaemia, impacting quality of life.
