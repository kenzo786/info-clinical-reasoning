Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Giant Cell Arteritis"][/sc]
  • Giant cell arteritis (GCA), also known as temporal arteritis, is a chronic inflammatory vasculitis primarily affecting medium and large arteries, most commonly those in the head and neck.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the key clinical features of giant cell arteritis, including headache, jaw claudication, and visual loss, and understand the urgency of high-dose steroid treatment to prevent irreversible complications such as blindness.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Giant Cell Arteritis"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Giant Cell Arteritis"][/sc]
  • GCA predominantly affects individuals over 50 years of age, with the incidence increasing with advancing age.
  • It is more common in women than in men, with a female-to-male ratio of approximately 2.5-3:1.
  • The estimated annual incidence in the UK ranges from 14.6 to 43.6 per 100,000 people.
  • Risk factors include increasing age, female sex, and Northern European ancestry.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Giant Cell Arteritis"][/sc]
  • Trigger: The exact trigger for Giant Cell Arteritis (GCA) is unknown, but it is understood to be an immune-mediated process involving activation of dendritic cells and T-lymphocytes within the arterial wall.
  • Progression: This leads to a transmural, granulomatous inflammation of medium and large arteries, characterized by the infiltration of macrophages and giant cells, causing structural damage, intimal hyperplasia, and narrowing of the arterial lumen. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Granulomatous inflammation: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="granulomatous inflammation"][/sc]
  • Consequence: The progressive luminal narrowing and potential thrombosis result in ischaemia of the tissues supplied by the affected arteries, leading to symptoms such as headache, jaw claudication, and critically, irreversible vision loss due to anterior ischaemic optic neuropathy. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Anterior ischaemic optic neuropathy: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="anterior ischaemic optic neuropathy"][/sc]
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Giant Cell Arteritis"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Symptoms can develop subacutely over several weeks, or in some cases, abruptly over a few days. Initial symptoms may be non-specific, such as malaise, fatigue, and low-grade fever, often preceding more classic cranial symptoms.
  • Progressive phase: Without prompt treatment, inflammation progresses, leading to arterial narrowing and potential occlusion. This can manifest as worsening headaches, jaw claudication, and critically, acute visual disturbances, particularly beyond a few days of symptom onset, which can rapidly lead to irreversible vision loss.
  • Late phase: With appropriate high-dose glucocorticoid treatment, symptoms typically improve within days. However, the disease often requires a prolonged course of treatment, usually 1-2 years, with gradual tapering of medication. Relapses are common during tapering, and some individuals may require long-term low-dose therapy.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Giant Cell Arteritis"][/sc]
  • Ask about new-onset headache - often severe, persistent, and localised to the temporal region, which can be the presenting symptom and a key indicator of cranial artery involvement.
  • Ask about jaw claudication - pain or fatigue in the jaw muscles that occurs during chewing or talking and resolves with rest, highly suggestive of ischaemia in the masticatory muscles due to affected arteries.
  • Ask about visual disturbances - including transient monocular vision loss (amaurosis fugax), diplopia, or sudden, painless, permanent vision loss in one or both eyes, which is a medical emergency indicating ophthalmic artery involvement.
  • Ask about scalp tenderness - pain or sensitivity when touching the scalp, combing hair, or wearing glasses/hats, reflecting inflammation of superficial temporal arteries.
  • Ask about constitutional symptoms - such as unexplained fever, malaise, fatigue, anorexia, and unintentional weight loss, which are common systemic manifestations of the inflammatory process.
  • Ask about symptoms of polymyalgia rheumatica (PMR) - bilateral aching and stiffness in the shoulders, neck, and hip girdles, particularly prominent in the morning, as PMR frequently co-exists with GCA.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Giant Cell Arteritis"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - for low-grade fever, which can indicate systemic inflammation, and assess for any signs of cardiovascular compromise.
  • Check temporal arteries - for tenderness, thickening, nodularity, or reduced/absent pulsation, which are direct signs of arterial inflammation.
  • Check visual acuity and fundoscopy - for signs of anterior ischaemic optic neuropathy (AION), such as optic disc pallor and oedema, which signify irreversible damage and impending vision loss. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Anterior ischaemic optic neuropathy: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="anterior ischaemic optic neuropathy"][/sc]
  • Check for scalp tenderness - palpation of the scalp may elicit pain, particularly over the temporal arteries.
  • Check for bruits or blood pressure discrepancies - in cases of large vessel involvement, bruits may be audible over affected arteries, and blood pressure differences between limbs may be present.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Giant Cell Arteritis"][/sc]
  • New-onset, severe headache: Particularly if localised to the temporal region, this can indicate active cranial arteritis and the potential for ischaemic complications.
  • Acute visual disturbance: Including transient monocular vision loss (amaurosis fugax), diplopia, or sudden, painless, permanent vision loss, as these are ophthalmic emergencies requiring immediate treatment to prevent irreversible blindness.
  • Jaw claudication: Pain in the jaw muscles during chewing, which signifies ischaemia of the masticatory muscles and is a strong predictor of visual complications.
  • Scalp tenderness or temporal artery abnormalities: Tenderness, thickening, or absence of pulsation in the temporal arteries suggests active inflammation and a high risk of progression to visual loss.
  • Unexplained fever and systemic symptoms: While non-specific, these, in conjunction with other cranial symptoms, can indicate active systemic inflammation requiring urgent assessment.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Giant Cell Arteritis"][/sc]
  • Large Vessel GCA: Presentation may involve inflammation of the aorta and its major branches, leading to symptoms such as limb claudication, bruits, or blood pressure discrepancies between limbs, often without classic cranial symptoms. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Large vessel vasculitis: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="large vessel vasculitis"][/sc]
  • Occult GCA: Some individuals may present primarily with constitutional symptoms (fever of unknown origin, weight loss, malaise) and elevated inflammatory markers, without any cranial symptoms, making diagnosis challenging.
  • Neurological variants: Rarely, GCA can present with neurological symptoms such as stroke, transient ischaemic attacks, or peripheral neuropathy, which can be misdiagnosed as other neurological conditions.
  • Isolated ophthalmic GCA: In some cases, GCA may present solely with visual symptoms, such as sudden vision loss, without preceding headache or other systemic features, highlighting the importance of considering GCA in any acute visual loss in older adults.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Giant Cell Arteritis"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected Giant Cell Arteritis:
    • Blood tests: Erythrocyte sedimentation rate (ESR) and C-reactive protein (CRP) are typically elevated, reflecting systemic inflammation. A full blood count may show thrombocytosis.
    • Temporal artery biopsy (TAB): Considered the gold standard for diagnosis, showing transmural inflammation with mononuclear cell infiltrates and often giant cells. Should be performed within 1-2 weeks of starting steroids.
  • Severity/risk tools:
    • Clinical probability scores, such as the GCA Probability Score (GCAPS), can aid in assessing the likelihood of GCA. Giant Cell Arteritis Probability Score (GCAPS) on MDCalc
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible:
    • Consider investigations for conditions mimicking GCA, such as tension headache, migraine, trigeminal neuralgia, or other forms of vasculitis, based on clinical presentation.
  • Imaging - Ultrasound: Duplex ultrasonography of the temporal arteries can reveal a 'halo sign' (hypoechoic wall thickening) and compression sign, which are highly suggestive of GCA. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Halo sign in temporal artery ultrasound: what is it and why does it matter clinically?" text="halo sign"][/sc]
  • Imaging - MRI/CT/PET: Magnetic Resonance Imaging (MRI) or Computed Tomography (CT) angiography may be used to assess for large vessel involvement (e.g., aorta, subclavian arteries). Positron Emission Tomography (PET) scanning can also detect inflammation in large vessels.
  • Serial reassessment: Regular monitoring of inflammatory markers (ESR, CRP) and clinical symptoms is crucial to assess treatment response and detect relapses.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Giant Cell Arteritis"][/sc]
  • Migraine/Tension Headache - typically lacks systemic inflammatory markers, jaw claudication, and visual symptoms, and often has a history of similar headaches.
  • Polymyalgia Rheumatica (PMR) - shares systemic inflammatory features and musculoskeletal pain but typically lacks cranial symptoms (headache, jaw claudication, visual loss) and temporal artery abnormalities.
  • Trigeminal Neuralgia - characterised by sudden, severe, unilateral facial pain in the distribution of the trigeminal nerve, distinct from the exertional jaw pain of GCA.
  • Other Vasculitides - differentiation relies on specific vessel involvement, histological findings, and associated clinical syndromes (e.g., ANCA-associated vasculitis affecting small vessels).
  • Cervical Spondylosis/Osteoarthritis - can cause neck and shoulder pain, but inflammatory markers are usually normal, and cranial symptoms of GCA are absent.
  • Systemic Infection - can cause fever and elevated inflammatory markers, but typically lacks the specific cranial and visual symptoms, and temporal artery signs of GCA.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Giant Cell Arteritis"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Urgent recognition and initiation of high-dose glucocorticoid therapy (e.g., prednisolone) is crucial to prevent irreversible vision loss. This should not be delayed while awaiting confirmatory tests or specialist review.
  • Definitive management: High-dose oral glucocorticoids, typically prednisolone, form the cornerstone of treatment. The initial high dose is maintained until symptoms resolve and inflammatory markers normalise, followed by a gradual tapering regimen over several months to years.
  • Adjunctive management:
    • Low-dose aspirin may be considered to reduce the risk of ischaemic events such as stroke or myocardial infarction.
    • Proton pump inhibitors (PPIs) are often co-prescribed to mitigate the gastrointestinal side effects of glucocorticoids.
    • Bisphosphonates and calcium/vitamin D supplementation are typically initiated to prevent glucocorticoid-induced osteoporosis.
    • Immunosuppressants, such as methotrexate or tocilizumab, may be considered in cases of refractory disease, frequent relapses, or inability to taper glucocorticoids due to side effects.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Regular clinical review and monitoring of inflammatory markers (ESR, CRP) are essential to assess disease activity, treatment response, and to guide glucocorticoid tapering. Monitoring for glucocorticoid-related adverse effects is also vital.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Patients should be advised to seek urgent medical attention for any new or worsening symptoms, particularly visual changes or severe headache, as this may indicate a relapse requiring immediate re-escalation of glucocorticoid therapy.

Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Giant Cell Arteritis"][/sc]
  • Irreversible vision loss: The most feared complication, typically occurring acutely in the progressive phase due to anterior ischaemic optic neuropathy (AION) from ophthalmic artery involvement. This is a medical emergency as vision loss is usually permanent if not treated promptly.
  • Aortic aneurysm and dissection: A significant long-term complication, particularly affecting the thoracic aorta, resulting from chronic inflammation and weakening of the arterial wall. This can develop years after the initial diagnosis and warrants ongoing monitoring.
  • Stroke or transient ischaemic attack (TIA): Can occur if cerebral arteries are affected, leading to neurological deficits. The risk is highest in the early phase of untreated disease.
  • Relapse: Common during the late phase, particularly during glucocorticoid tapering, necessitating re-escalation of treatment and careful monitoring to prevent further complications.
  • Glucocorticoid-related side effects: Prolonged high-dose steroid therapy can lead to complications such as osteoporosis, diabetes, hypertension, and increased susceptibility to infections, which require careful management throughout the disease course.
