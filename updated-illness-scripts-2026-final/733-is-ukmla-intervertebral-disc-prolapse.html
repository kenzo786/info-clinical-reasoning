Intervertebral disc prolapse (Musculoskeletal) [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Intervertebral disc prolapse"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Intervertebral disc prolapse"][/sc]
  • Intervertebral disc prolapse, commonly known as a "slipped disc" or herniated disc, occurs when the soft inner nucleus pulposus of an intervertebral disc protrudes through a tear in the outer annulus fibrosus, often compressing adjacent nerve roots.
  • UKMLA learning outcome: Recognise the clinical features of intervertebral disc prolapse, differentiate it from other causes of back pain and radiculopathy, and understand the principles of initial conservative management and indications for specialist referral.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Intervertebral disc prolapse"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Intervertebral disc prolapse"][/sc]
  • The lifetime risk for lumbar disc herniation is approximately 30%, with symptomatic cases occurring in 1–3% of the population.
  • Most commonly affects individuals aged 30-50 years, with a slight male predominance.
  • Risk factors include obesity, physically demanding occupations involving lifting or twisting, prolonged sitting, and a history of previous back injury.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Intervertebral disc prolapse"][/sc]
  • Degenerative changes in the intervertebral disc, often exacerbated by mechanical stress or trauma, can lead to weakening and tearing of the annulus fibrosus.
  • The nucleus pulposus then extrudes or prolapses through the annular tear, often impinging on nearby spinal nerve roots or the spinal cord itself.
  • This compression and associated inflammatory response results in radicular pain, sensory deficits, and/or motor weakness in the distribution of the affected nerve root. Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Intervertebral disc prolapse"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Acute onset of severe localised back pain, often radiating into a limb, typically following a precipitating event such as lifting or twisting. Symptoms may be intermittent initially.
  • Progressive phase: Over days to weeks, radicular symptoms (pain, numbness, tingling) may become more prominent than back pain, often worsening with specific movements or positions. Neurological deficits like weakness or reflex changes may develop.
  • Late phase: The majority of symptoms often improve spontaneously within 6-12 weeks with conservative management, as the herniated disc material may regress or the inflammatory response subsides. Persistent or worsening neurological deficits, particularly beyond 6 weeks, may warrant further investigation or intervention.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Intervertebral disc prolapse"][/sc]
  • Ask about radiating limb pain - typically sharp, shooting, or burning pain following a dermatomal distribution, indicating nerve root compression.
  • Ask about paraesthesia or numbness - sensory disturbances in the affected dermatome, which can help localise the level of disc prolapse.
  • Ask about exacerbating and relieving factors - pain often worsens with coughing, sneezing, straining (Valsalva manoeuvre), or prolonged sitting, and may be relieved by lying down.
  • Ask about motor weakness - difficulty with specific movements or a feeling of "giving way" in the affected limb, suggesting motor nerve involvement.
  • Ask about saddle anaesthesia or bowel/bladder dysfunction - these are critical red flag symptoms indicating potential cauda equina syndrome.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Intervertebral disc prolapse"][/sc]
  • Check vital signs - typically normal in uncomplicated disc prolapse, but may reveal signs of severe pain or systemic illness if an alternative diagnosis is present.
  • Check neurological examination - assess motor power, sensation (light touch, pinprick), and deep tendon reflexes in the affected limb to identify specific nerve root involvement.
  • Check straight leg raise (SLR) test - a positive test (radicular pain reproduced between 30-70 degrees of hip flexion) is highly suggestive of lumbar nerve root compression. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Straight leg raise test: how is it performed and what does a positive result indicate?" text="straight leg raise test"][/sc]
  • Check gait - observe for antalgic gait or foot drop, which may indicate significant motor weakness.
  • Check spinal range of motion - often restricted and painful, particularly with flexion or extension, but this is non-specific.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Intervertebral disc prolapse"][/sc]
  • Cauda Equina Syndrome: New onset of saddle anaesthesia, bilateral sciatica, bladder or bowel dysfunction (e.g., urinary retention, faecal incontinence), or severe progressive motor weakness in both legs. This constitutes a surgical emergency requiring urgent assessment, typically within hours.
  • Spinal Cord Compression: Upper motor neuron signs (e.g., spasticity, hyperreflexia, extensor plantar responses), gait disturbance, or bilateral neurological deficits, suggesting compression of the spinal cord (more common in cervical or thoracic disc prolapse). Requires urgent specialist review.
  • Progressive Neurological Deficit: Rapidly worsening motor weakness or sensory loss in a limb, particularly over days, indicating ongoing nerve root damage. Warrants prompt re-evaluation.
  • Suspected Infection/Malignancy: Unexplained weight loss, fever, night sweats, history of cancer, intravenous drug use, or immunosuppression, especially with unremitting pain not relieved by rest. Requires urgent investigation.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Intervertebral disc prolapse"][/sc]
  • Older adults: Symptoms may be less typical, with less prominent radicular pain and more diffuse back pain or neurological deficits due to co-existing spinal stenosis or degenerative changes.
  • Thoracic disc prolapse: Less common, often presenting with non-specific back pain, chest wall pain, or myelopathic symptoms (e.g., gait disturbance, spasticity) if spinal cord compression occurs.
  • Cervical disc prolapse: Presents with neck pain radiating to the arm, often with sensory or motor deficits in the upper limb corresponding to the affected cervical nerve root.
  • Contained disc bulge: May cause back pain without radiculopathy if there is no significant nerve root compression, making diagnosis more challenging. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Contained disc bulge vs herniation: what is the difference and clinical significance?" text="contained disc bulge"][/sc]
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Intervertebral disc prolapse"][/sc]
  • Core for suspected intervertebral disc prolapse: Clinical history and neurological examination are paramount. Imaging is not routinely required in the absence of red flags or persistent symptoms.
  • Imaging - MRI: Magnetic Resonance Imaging (MRI) of the spine is the gold standard for confirming disc prolapse and nerve root compression, particularly if symptoms persist beyond 6 weeks, there are progressive neurological deficits, or red flags are present. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="MRI in intervertebral disc prolapse: indications and findings?" text="MRI findings"][/sc]
  • Imaging - X-ray: Plain X-rays are generally not useful for diagnosing disc prolapse but may help exclude other bony pathologies.
  • Severity/risk tools: The Oswestry Disability Index or Roland-Morris Disability Questionnaire may be used to assess functional impact and monitor progress.
  • If an alternative diagnosis is plausible: Blood tests (e.g., FBC, ESR, CRP) may be considered to rule out inflammatory, infective, or malignant causes if suggested by red flags or atypical features.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Intervertebral disc prolapse"][/sc]
  • Spinal stenosis: Typically presents with neurogenic claudication (leg pain with walking, relieved by sitting), often bilateral, and less acute onset than disc prolapse.
  • Piriformis syndrome: Sciatic-like pain due to piriformis muscle spasm, often without true dermatomal distribution or neurological deficits on examination.
  • Sacroiliac joint dysfunction: Localised pain over the SI joint, often radiating to the buttock or posterior thigh, usually without true radiculopathy.
  • Facet joint arthropathy: Localised back pain, often worse with extension, and may refer pain to the buttock or thigh but typically not below the knee.
  • Spondylolisthesis: Forward slippage of one vertebra over another, which can cause back pain and radiculopathy, often identifiable on X-ray.
  • Peripheral neuropathy: Sensory disturbances and weakness in a stocking-glove distribution, not dermatomal, and often bilateral. [sc name="jitl-query" Q="Peripheral neuropathy vs radiculopathy: key differences in presentation?" text="peripheral neuropathy"][/sc]
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Intervertebral disc prolapse"][/sc]
  • Immediate priorities: Recognition of red flag symptoms (e.g., cauda equina syndrome) and urgent referral to neurosurgery or orthopaedics. Otherwise, initial management often involves reassurance, advice on activity modification, and pain control.
  • Definitive management: Conservative management is the first-line approach for most patients, including analgesia, physiotherapy, and continued activity modification. The majority of patients experience resolution of symptoms within 6-12 weeks.
  • Adjunctive management: Epidural steroid injections may be considered for short-term pain relief in selected patients with persistent radicular pain, but their long-term efficacy is debated.
  • Monitoring and follow-up: Regular review to assess symptom progression, neurological status, and functional improvement. Patients should be advised on self-management strategies and when to seek further medical attention.
  • Escalation and safety-netting: Specialist referral to spinal services may be warranted for persistent severe radicular pain despite adequate conservative management, progressive neurological deficits, or red flag symptoms.
  • Exam pharmacology reference (UKMLA/MRCGP): Non-steroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs) may be considered for pain relief. Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Intervertebral disc prolapse"][/sc]
  • Chronic pain: Persistent back pain or radicular pain beyond 3-6 months, which can significantly impact quality of life and functional ability.
  • Persistent neurological deficit: Irreversible nerve damage leading to ongoing motor weakness, sensory loss, or reflex abnormalities, particularly if nerve compression is prolonged or severe.
  • Cauda Equina Syndrome: A rare but severe complication involving compression of the cauda equina nerves, leading to saddle anaesthesia, bladder/bowel dysfunction, and lower limb weakness, requiring emergency surgical decompression to prevent permanent neurological damage.
  • Recurrent disc prolapse: Re-herniation of the same or an adjacent disc, which can occur spontaneously or following activity, potentially requiring further intervention.
