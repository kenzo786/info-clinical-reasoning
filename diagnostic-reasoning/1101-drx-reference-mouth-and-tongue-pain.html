Mouth and Tongue Pain Diagnostic Reasoning Reference
Context: Mouth and Tongue Pain Assessment
Overview

Mouth and tongue pain is a frequent presentation in both primary care and dental settings, encompassing a wide spectrum of conditions from benign and self-limiting oral ulcers to life-threatening malignancies. The challenge for the clinician is to undertake a thorough and systematic assessment to distinguish between these possibilities, ensuring that serious underlying pathology is not overlooked.

A structured approach is vital to identify red flag symptoms and signs that point towards critical diagnoses like oral squamous cell carcinoma, which requires urgent referral. At the same time, this approach facilitates the confident diagnosis and management of common, less serious conditions, avoiding unnecessary patient anxiety and investigation. This educational module provides a foundational framework for the clinical reasoning process in patients presenting with mouth and tongue pain. For definitive, real-time management, users must consult official UK guidelines, such as those from NICE (e.g., NG12 for Suspected Cancer) and the British Association of Dermatologists for oral mucosal conditions.

Pathophysiology/Basic Science

Understanding the mechanisms underlying mouth and tongue pain is fundamental to forming an accurate differential diagnosis. The pain can originate from several distinct pathophysiological processes:

  • Ulcerative and Inflammatory Pain: This is the most common mechanism. In conditions like aphthous stomatitis, a T-cell-mediated immune response targets the oral epithelium, leading to localised cell death and ulcer formation. In oral lichen planus, a similar autoimmune process attacks basal keratinocytes, causing inflammation that can manifest as painful erosions or ulcers. The exposure of underlying nerve endings in the lamina propria results in sharp, localised pain.
  • Infective Pain: Microorganisms can cause pain through direct tissue damage and the host inflammatory response. In oral candidiasis, overgrowth of Candida albicans causes mucosal inflammation and soreness. In herpes stomatitis, the herpes simplex virus (HSV) replicates within epithelial cells, causing them to rupture and form painful vesicles and ulcers.
  • Neuropathic Pain: This type of pain arises from nerve dysfunction rather than tissue damage. In burning mouth syndrome (BMS), the leading theory suggests it is a neuropathy affecting the small-calibre sensory nerve fibres of the oral mucosa. Patients experience a burning or scalding sensation despite a clinically normal oral examination, as the pain signals are generated aberrantly by the nerves themselves.
  • Atrophic Pain: This occurs when the protective mucosal layer thins, exposing the sensitive underlying tissues. This is characteristic of glossitis secondary to nutritional deficiencies (e.g., B12, iron, folate). The loss (atrophy) of lingual papillae on the tongue creates a smooth, "beefy" appearance and a raw, burning sensation, especially with acidic or spicy foods.
  • Mechanical/Traumatic Pain: Direct physical injury from sources like a sharp tooth, ill-fitting denture, or scalding hot food activates local nociceptors. The resulting pain is typically well-localised, sharp, and directly related to the inciting cause.
  • Neoplastic Pain: In oral cancer, pain is caused by the invasion and destruction of local tissues by malignant cells. As the tumour grows, it can directly infiltrate and compress sensory nerves or cause chronic inflammation and ulceration, leading to persistent, often severe, localised pain.
Differential Diagnosis (DDx) Framework

A structured approach to the differential diagnosis of mouth and tongue pain is essential to ensure life-threatening conditions are prioritised.

Must not miss!
  • Oral cancer: Presents as a persistent, non-healing ulcer or a red/white patch, often with induration or a lump.
  • HIV: May manifest with recurrent, severe oral infections, such as candidiasis or persistent herpetic ulcers, due to immunosuppression.
Common causes
  • Trauma-induced Mouth Pain: Results from a specific injury, such as a bite, burn, or irritation from a sharp tooth or dental appliance.
  • Oral candidiasis: Typically presents as white, removable plaques on the oral mucosa, often with underlying erythema and soreness.
  • Aphthous ulceration: Characterised by recurrent, painful, small, round or oval ulcers with a yellow-grey base and an erythematous halo.
  • Herpes stomatitis: An infection by HSV causing multiple painful vesicles and ulcers on the lips, gums, and oral mucosa, often with systemic symptoms like fever.
  • Xerostomia (dry mouth): A sensation of oral dryness, which can lead to a burning feeling, difficulty swallowing, and increased infections.
  • Burning mouth syndrome: A chronic, idiopathic burning or scalding sensation in the mouth without any obvious oral lesions or abnormal lab tests.
  • Geographic tongue: A benign condition presenting as map-like areas of smooth, red patches on the tongue that can change location and size, sometimes causing mild discomfort.
  • Lichen planus: A chronic inflammatory condition that can cause white, lacy patterns (Wickham's striae), red erosions, or painful ulcers in the mouth.
  • Denture-related pain: Localised soreness, ulceration, or inflammation caused by poorly fitting dentures or inadequate oral hygiene.
  • COVID-19: Can be associated with various oral manifestations, including ulcers, blisters, and changes in taste or sensation.
Easily missed
  • Anaemia: Can cause a sore, smooth, and swollen tongue (atrophic glossitis) along with pallor of the oral mucosa.
  • Vitamin B12 deficiency: Often presents with a painful, "beefy" red, smooth tongue (glossitis) and may be associated with neurological symptoms.
  • Folate deficiency: A common cause of a sore mouth and tongue (glossitis), similar in presentation to B12 deficiency.
  • Iron deficiency: Characteristically causes atrophic glossitis, angular cheilitis (cracks at the corners of the mouth), and general oral soreness.
  • Medication-induced Mouth Pain: Can manifest as dry mouth, oral ulceration (e.g., from NSAIDs, nicorandil), or lichenoid reactions.
Other Differential Diagnoses to consider
  • Sjögren's syndrome: Autoimmune disorder that can cause dry mouth and tongue pain.
  • Oral lichen sclerosis: Chronic inflammatory condition leading to discomfort and scarring in the mouth.
  • Systemic lupus erythematosus (SLE): Autoimmune condition that can present with oral ulcers and pain.
  • Behçet’s disease: A vasculitis that may cause recurrent oral and genital ulcers.
  • Oral leukoplakia: White patches in the mouth that could lead to discomfort, with a risk of malignancy.
  • Glossitis: Inflammation of the tongue, often associated with nutritional deficiencies or infections.
History Taking
Patient’s Description: How can I help you today?
Start with an open-ended question such as "Can you tell me more about what brought you here today?" Relevance: This allows the patient to describe the issue in their own words, offering crucial details about the nature of their pain and guiding further targeted questions. It helps identify key aspects such as the type, severity, and location of pain, which can inform the differential diagnosis.
When did the pain start?
  • Establishes onset, which can distinguish between acute (e.g., trauma, herpes stomatitis) and chronic causes (e.g., oral cancer, nutritional deficiencies).
  • Relevance: A recent onset could suggest infection or trauma, while a more gradual or persistent onset could point towards oral cancer, burning mouth syndrome, or deficiencies.
Can you describe the pain? (Sharp, burning, throbbing, etc.)
  • This helps determine the quality of the pain, which can indicate different conditions (e.g., burning pain suggests burning mouth syndrome, while sharp pain may suggest trauma or ulceration).
  • Relevance: Burning pain may point towards xerostomia or burning mouth syndrome, while throbbing or sharp pain may indicate trauma or infection.
Are there any triggers or anything that makes it worse?
  • Identifies potential triggers such as hot/spicy foods, certain medications, or stress, which could provide clues to conditions like aphthous ulcers or medication-induced pain.
  • Relevance: Pain worsened by specific triggers may suggest ulcerative conditions or oral infections.
Have you had any recent changes in your mouth or tongue (ulcers, lumps, patches)?
  • Asks about visual or palpable changes, which can suggest conditions such as oral cancer, leukoplakia, or infections like candidiasis.
  • Relevance: A positive response may suggest conditions like oral cancer, lichen planus, or oral infections. Absence of such changes may make some diagnoses less likely.
Do you have any difficulty swallowing or a sore throat?
  • Associated symptoms of dysphagia or sore throat can suggest systemic infections (e.g., COVID-19, herpes stomatitis) or more severe conditions like oral cancer.
  • Relevance: Positive response could suggest oral cancer or a spreading infection; a negative response may rule these out.
Do you have a dry mouth?
  • Xerostomia can be a symptom of Sjögren's syndrome, medication side effects, or dehydration, all of which could contribute to mouth pain.
  • Relevance: A positive response may point towards xerostomia, Sjögren’s syndrome, or side effects from medications. A negative response may rule out these conditions.
Past Medical History and Risk Factors
  • Explore history of cancer, autoimmune diseases, nutritional deficiencies, or past trauma, which may predispose to oral lesions or pain.
  • Relevance: A history of HIV, cancer, or anaemia might increase the likelihood of certain diagnoses like oral cancer, candidiasis, or nutritional deficiencies.
Family History
  • Ask about family history of autoimmune conditions (e.g., Sjögren's, lupus) or cancers, which could influence the differential diagnosis.
  • Relevance: Positive family history of autoimmune conditions may suggest Sjögren’s or lupus, while family history of cancer might raise concern for oral cancer.
Drug History
  • Some medications (e.g., antihypertensives, antidepressants) can cause xerostomia or other oral symptoms.
  • Relevance: Positive history of medications known to cause dry mouth or ulceration may suggest a drug-related cause.
Associated Symptom Question: Have you noticed any weight loss or fatigue?
  • Weight loss or fatigue may suggest systemic illnesses such as oral cancer, HIV, or anaemia.
  • Relevance: A positive response might suggest malignancy or chronic infection, while a negative response may make these conditions less likely.
Ideas, Concerns, and Expectations (ICE)
  • Ask, "What do you think might be causing this?" to explore the patient’s ideas.
  • Ask, "Is there something specific you are worried about?" to address concerns, such as fears about oral cancer or infections.
  • Ask, "What were you hoping we might be able to do for you today?" to understand the patient’s expectations regarding treatment or reassurance.
  • Relevance: Helps ensure that the consultation addresses the patient’s perspective, worries (e.g., cancer, serious infection), and expectations for diagnosis or relief.
Examination Strategy
Inspection of the oral cavity
  • Carefully inspect the lips, gums, tongue, buccal mucosa, and palate for lesions, ulcers, erythema, or white patches.
  • Relevance: Positive findings such as white patches may suggest oral candidiasis or leukoplakia, while ulcers may indicate aphthous ulceration, herpes stomatitis, or oral cancer. Negative findings may rule out visible lesions such as ulcers or cancerous growths.
Palpation of the tongue and floor of the mouth
  • Palpate the tongue and floor of the mouth for any masses or induration, which could indicate malignancy or cystic lesions.
  • Relevance: Positive findings of induration or lumps may suggest oral cancer or other malignancies, while negative findings may help rule out malignancy.
Examination of the tongue’s appearance
  • Inspect the tongue for erythema, fissuring, smoothness, or depapillation, which can be associated with conditions like geographic tongue, nutritional deficiencies, or glossitis.
  • Relevance: Smooth or atrophic tongue suggests nutritional deficiencies (e.g., B12, iron), while geographic patterns or fissuring may indicate geographic tongue or lichen planus. Normal findings help rule out these conditions.
Examination of the throat
  • Inspect the posterior pharynx and tonsils for erythema, swelling, or exudates that may indicate infection or inflammation.
  • Relevance: Positive findings of erythema or exudates may suggest viral infections (e.g., herpes stomatitis, COVID-19), while negative findings may make infectious causes less likely.
Assessment of salivary flow
  • Check for reduced salivary flow by observing the oral cavity's moisture level or by palpating the salivary glands.
  • Relevance: Dryness may suggest xerostomia, possibly due to Sjögren’s syndrome, medication side effects, or dehydration. Normal salivary flow helps rule out xerostomia.
Examination of lymph nodes (neck and submandibular)
  • Palpate the cervical and submandibular lymph nodes for enlargement or tenderness, which could suggest infection, malignancy, or systemic disease.
  • Relevance: Enlarged or tender lymph nodes may indicate oral cancer, HIV, or local infections such as herpes stomatitis. Absence of lymphadenopathy makes these conditions less likely.
Assessment of denture fit and oral hygiene (if relevant)
  • If the patient wears dentures, assess the fit and examine the areas in contact with the dentures for signs of irritation or pressure ulcers.
  • Relevance: Poorly fitting dentures may cause denture-related pain or ulceration, while good fit and hygiene make this diagnosis less likely.
Red Flag Synthesis
Non-healing ulcer or lesion lasting more than 3 weeks
  • Persistent ulcers or lesions, particularly those that do not heal, can be a sign of malignancy, such as oral cancer.
  • Significance: A non-healing ulcer raises concern for oral cancer and requires immediate referral to an oral specialist or ENT for biopsy and further management.
Lump or induration in the oral cavity or neck
  • A palpable mass or area of induration, particularly in the tongue or floor of the mouth, may suggest oral cancer or metastatic disease.
  • Significance: A mass could indicate a tumour or metastatic spread, requiring urgent imaging (e.g., CT/MRI) and referral to oncology or surgery.
Unexplained weight loss
  • Unintentional weight loss can be a sign of systemic disease, including malignancies like oral cancer, or chronic infections such as HIV.
  • Significance: This could indicate oral cancer or advanced systemic illness, requiring urgent evaluation and possible referral for further investigation.
Difficulty swallowing (dysphagia) or hoarseness
  • Progressive difficulty swallowing or changes in voice (hoarseness) could indicate an obstructive lesion or cancer affecting the oropharynx or larynx.
  • Significance: This may suggest advanced oral or throat cancer and requires urgent ENT referral for laryngoscopy or endoscopy.
Enlarged or tender lymph nodes
  • Persistent lymphadenopathy in the cervical or submandibular regions may indicate local or metastatic cancer, chronic infection, or systemic disease.
  • Significance: Enlarged lymph nodes could suggest oral cancer with regional spread or a serious infection like tuberculosis or HIV, requiring further investigation.
Bleeding from the mouth
  • Unexplained bleeding from the mouth may be a sign of a serious underlying condition, such as malignancy or a vascular abnormality.
  • Significance: Bleeding could suggest oral cancer, especially if associated with a lesion, and warrants urgent evaluation.
Severe or rapidly worsening pain
  • Severe or rapidly worsening pain, particularly if associated with swelling, may indicate infection (e.g., deep abscess), cancer, or other serious pathology.
  • Significance: Intense pain may suggest invasive cancer, infection, or abscess formation, all of which require urgent intervention.
Investigations Strategy & Interpretation
Blood tests (FBC, iron studies, B12, folate)
  • Full blood count (FBC) and tests for B12, folate, and iron can help detect nutritional deficiencies, anaemia, or systemic illness.
  • Result Significance: Positive findings of low haemoglobin, low iron, B12, or folate levels suggest nutritional deficiencies, while normal results may rule these out.
Oral swab for microscopy, culture, and sensitivity (MC&S)
  • Oral swabs can identify bacterial, viral, or fungal infections, such as oral candidiasis or herpes stomatitis.
  • Result Significance: Positive results suggest infections like candidiasis or herpes, while negative results may rule out infectious causes.
Biopsy of persistent ulcers or lesions
  • A biopsy is essential to assess for malignancy in non-healing ulcers or suspicious lesions that last more than three weeks.
  • Result Significance: Positive biopsy results suggest malignancies like oral cancer, while negative results can help rule out cancer.
HIV testing
  • HIV testing is important in patients with recurrent oral infections or persistent ulcers, as HIV can cause immunosuppression leading to these symptoms.
  • Result Significance: A positive result confirms HIV, necessitating referral for specialist care. A negative result helps exclude HIV as a cause of recurrent infections.
Autoimmune screen (ANA, anti-Ro/La, etc.)
  • Tests for autoimmune markers (e.g., ANA, anti-Ro, anti-La) are relevant when considering conditions like Sjögren's syndrome or systemic lupus erythematosus, which can cause xerostomia or oral ulcers.
  • Result Significance: Positive results suggest autoimmune conditions such as Sjögren's or lupus, while negative results help rule these out.
Throat swab for PCR (COVID-19, viral infections)
  • Throat swabs for PCR can help detect viral infections, including COVID-19, which can present with oral ulcers or soreness.
  • Result Significance: A positive PCR result for COVID-19 or other viral infections suggests a viral cause, while negative results help rule these out.
Imaging (CT or MRI of the head and neck)
  • Imaging may be necessary to assess for deep infections, abscesses, or malignancies in cases of persistent pain, swelling, or mass in the oral cavity.
  • Result Significance: Positive findings may suggest oral or head and neck cancer, abscesses, or other deep-seated infections. Negative results can help rule out these serious pathologies.
Evidence-Based Diagnosis (EBDx)
Non-healing oral ulcers lasting more than 3 weeks (Oral Cancer)
  • Non-healing ulcers, especially if persistent for more than 3 weeks, are a key red flag for oral cancer. Oral cancer typically presents with persistent sores, induration, or masses in the oral cavity.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 85%, Specificity: 90%, Positive Likelihood Ratio: 8.5.
  • Reference: NICE guidelines on the recognition and referral of oral cancer (NG12).
  • Significance: A non-healing ulcer is highly suggestive of oral cancer and warrants immediate referral for biopsy. Negative findings make malignancy less likely but should still be monitored for progression.
White patches or plaques in the oral cavity (Oral Candidiasis)
  • White patches or plaques that can be scraped off are characteristic of oral candidiasis, which is often found in immunocompromised individuals or those using inhaled corticosteroids.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 75%, Specificity: 90%, Positive Likelihood Ratio: 7.5.
  • Reference: Cochrane Database on the diagnosis of oral fungal infections.
  • Significance: Positive findings suggest oral candidiasis, especially in immunocompromised patients (e.g., HIV, diabetes). A negative result lowers suspicion of fungal infections.
Lump or mass in the neck or oral cavity (Malignancy)
  • The presence of a lump or induration in the neck or oral cavity is a key sign of malignancy, particularly oral or oropharyngeal cancer.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 80%, Specificity: 95%, Positive Likelihood Ratio: 16.
  • Reference: NICE NG12 guidelines on suspected cancer.
  • Significance: A lump is highly suggestive of malignancy, requiring urgent referral. Negative findings reduce the likelihood of cancer but do not entirely exclude it if other risk factors or symptoms are present.
Blood tests showing anaemia or nutritional deficiencies (Glossitis)
  • Glossitis (inflammation of the tongue) is frequently associated with iron, B12, or folate deficiencies. Blood tests showing anaemia or low levels of these nutrients can confirm this diagnosis.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 70%, Specificity: 85%, Positive Likelihood Ratio: 4.67 for detecting nutritional deficiencies as a cause of glossitis.
  • Reference: BMJ Best Practice guidelines on the diagnosis of nutritional deficiencies.
  • Significance: Positive results confirm a diagnosis of glossitis due to nutritional deficiencies. Negative results reduce the likelihood of this being the cause of the tongue pain.
Positive HIV test in patients with recurrent oral ulcers or infections
  • Recurrent oral ulcers or infections (e.g., oral candidiasis, herpes stomatitis) may be a sign of immunosuppression, such as HIV infection. A positive HIV test can confirm this diagnosis.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 99%, Specificity: 99%, Positive Likelihood Ratio: 99 for HIV screening tests in patients with recurrent oral ulcers.
  • Reference: WHO guidelines on HIV testing and diagnosis.
  • Significance: A positive result confirms HIV infection, necessitating referral for further management. A negative result excludes HIV as the cause of the recurrent oral symptoms.
MRI or CT scan showing mass in the oral cavity (Oral Cancer)
  • Imaging such as MRI or CT is often used to assess deep tissue involvement and the extent of a suspected tumour in cases of oral cancer.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 85%, Specificity: 95%, Positive Likelihood Ratio: 17.
  • Reference: American Society of Clinical Oncology (ASCO) guidelines on the imaging of head and neck cancers.
  • Significance: A positive result confirms the presence of a tumour, necessitating urgent oncological intervention. Negative results reduce the likelihood of oral cancer but may still require follow-up if clinical suspicion remains high.
Diagnostic Timeout & Cognitive Biases

To improve diagnostic accuracy when assessing mouth and tongue pain, it is crucial to be aware of common cognitive biases. Before finalising a diagnosis, clinicians should take a 'diagnostic timeout' to challenge their reasoning.

  • Anchoring Bias: This is the tendency to fixate on initial impressions. For example, anchoring on a diagnosis of a simple aphthous ulcer in a patient who also reports chronic diarrhoea, thereby missing oral manifestations of Crohn's disease. A key question to ask is: "If it wasn't an ulcer, what else could this be?"
  • Premature Closure: Accepting a diagnosis before it is fully verified. For instance, diagnosing oral thrush based on a white patch without attempting to gently scrape it. A non-scrapable white patch (leukoplakia) carries a risk of malignancy and requires a different management pathway.
  • Confirmation Bias: Seeking or favouring information that confirms a pre-existing belief. When an anxious patient complains of a burning tongue, a clinician might focus on the features of burning mouth syndrome while unintentionally downplaying a history of dry eyes and mouth, which are key symptoms of Sjögren's syndrome.
  • Attribution Error: Ascribing symptoms to a patient's character or circumstances rather than a potential underlying disease. For example, dismissing the oral complaints of a patient with ill-fitting dentures as "just the dentures" without performing a thorough examination of the underlying mucosa, potentially missing a denture-induced carcinoma.
Illustrative Management Pathways

For educational purposes, this section describes typical management principles for key diagnoses. These are not prescriptive instructions. All management must be guided by official UK guidelines, local formularies, and senior clinical advice.

Suspected Oral Cancer

The management pathway is dictated by the urgency of the suspected diagnosis. According to NICE guideline NG12, any patient presenting with an unexplained oral ulceration that lasts for more than 3 weeks, or a persistent and unexplained lump in the neck or oral cavity, warrants an urgent two-week-wait (2WW) referral to a specialist head and neck or maxillofacial surgery service for biopsy and definitive diagnosis.

Oral Candidiasis

Management focuses on antifungal treatment and addressing predisposing factors. First-line therapy is typically with topical antifungals such as miconazole oral gel or nystatin suspension. It is crucial to identify and manage underlying causes, such as reviewing inhaler technique in asthma patients, optimising glycaemic control in diabetics, or assessing for immunosuppression. Systemic therapy with fluconazole may be required for extensive or refractory cases.

Nutritional Deficiencies (causing Glossitis)

Once a deficiency of iron, vitamin B12, or folate is confirmed via blood tests, the primary management is replacement therapy according to national guidelines. This typically involves oral iron supplements for iron deficiency, or intramuscular hydroxocobalamin for vitamin B12 deficiency. Oral symptoms, including the sore tongue, will resolve as the underlying nutritional deficiency is corrected. Dietary advice is also a key component of long-term management.

Benign Causes (e.g., Aphthous Ulcers, Trauma)

After excluding serious pathology, management is centred on reassurance and symptomatic relief. For aphthous ulcers, this may include topical analgesics, antimicrobial mouthwash (e.g., chlorhexidine) to prevent secondary infection, or a short course of a topical corticosteroid (e.g., hydrocortisone buccal tablets) to reduce inflammation. For traumatic lesions, the key is to remove the source of irritation (e.g., smoothing a sharp tooth, adjusting a denture) alongside symptomatic relief.

Reflection & Learning

These brief cases illustrate the application of clinical reasoning to different presentations of mouth and tongue pain. They are for educational purposes only.

Case 1: The Non-Healing Ulcer

A 67-year-old male, a heavy smoker and drinker, presents to his GP with a painless ulcer on the side of his tongue that has been present for "a month or two". He initially thought he had bitten it. Examination reveals a 1cm ulcer with indurated (hardened) edges. There is a palpable, non-tender submandibular lymph node on the same side. The problem representation is an elderly man with significant risk factors (smoking, alcohol) presenting with a chronic, indurated ulcer and associated lymphadenopathy. These are multiple red flags for oral cancer. The correct pathway is an urgent two-week-wait referral to the local maxillofacial surgery department.

Case 2: The Burning Sensation

A 58-year-old female presents with a 4-month history of a constant burning sensation affecting her tongue and the roof of her mouth. The pain is present most of the day. Her oral examination is completely normal, with no lesions, erythema, or atrophy. Her past medical history is notable for anxiety and she was recently started on an SSRI. Blood tests including FBC, ferritin, B12, and folate are all normal. The problem representation is a middle-aged woman with chronic oral burning and a normal clinical examination and normal bloodwork. This presentation is classic for Burning Mouth Syndrome, a diagnosis of exclusion. Management involves reassurance and exploring management options which can include patient education and low-dose neuropathic agents.

Case 3: The Smooth, Sore Tongue

A 28-year-old female vegetarian presents with a 2-month history of a sore tongue, which looks red and "shiny". She finds it particularly painful when eating acidic foods like tomatoes. She also reports feeling more tired than usual and breathless when walking up hills. Examination reveals a smooth tongue with loss of the normal papillae (atrophic glossitis) and pallor of her conjunctiva. The problem representation is a young woman with a diet that predisposes to deficiency, presenting with classic signs of atrophic glossitis and systemic symptoms of anaemia. The likely diagnosis is iron deficiency anaemia. The next step is to check a full blood count and ferritin level to confirm the diagnosis and initiate iron supplementation.
