Anxiety and Depression Diagnostic Reasoning Reference
Context: Anxiety and Depression Assessment
Overview & Links to Key Guidelines

Anxiety and depression are the most common mental health problems in the UK, frequently presenting in both primary and secondary care. They represent a spectrum of conditions ranging from mild, self-limiting distress to severe, disabling illness with significant morbidity and mortality. The clinical challenge lies in recognising the pattern of symptoms, differentiating between primary psychiatric disorders and symptoms secondary to organic disease, assessing risk, and initiating appropriate management. A systematic and empathetic approach is essential to make an accurate diagnosis and avoid misattributing physical symptoms to a pre-existing mental health condition (diagnostic overshadowing).

This educational module is designed to provide a foundational understanding of the clinical reasoning process for patients presenting with symptoms of anxiety and depression. For definitive, real-time management protocols, users must refer to official UK guidelines from authoritative bodies such as NICE (e.g., NG222 for Depression in Adults, NG224 for Social Anxiety Disorder, CG113 for Generalised Anxiety Disorder) and the British Association for Psychopharmacology (BAP).

Pathophysiology/Basic Science

The pathophysiology of anxiety and depression is complex and multifactorial, involving an interplay of genetic, neurobiological, environmental, and psychological factors. No single mechanism provides a complete explanation.

  • Neurotransmitter Imbalance: This is a cornerstone theory, suggesting that dysregulation of key neurotransmitter systems in the brain is central to mood disorders.
    • Serotonin (5-HT): Plays a crucial role in mood, sleep, appetite, and cognition. Reduced serotonin activity is strongly implicated in both depression and anxiety, and is the primary target for SSRI medications.
    • Norepinephrine (Noradrenaline): Involved in the 'fight or flight' response, alertness, and stress. Dysregulation can contribute to the fatigue and low motivation seen in depression, and the hyperarousal of anxiety.
    • Dopamine: Key to the brain's reward and motivation pathways. Reduced dopamine function is associated with anhedonia (the inability to feel pleasure), a core symptom of depression.
  • Neuroendocrine Dysregulation (HPA Axis): The Hypothalamic-Pituitary-Adrenal (HPA) axis is the body's central stress response system. In many individuals with depression and chronic anxiety, this system is hyperactive, leading to elevated levels of the stress hormone cortisol. Prolonged high cortisol can be neurotoxic, particularly to the hippocampus, a brain region vital for memory and mood regulation.
  • Structural and Functional Brain Changes: Neuroimaging studies have identified alterations in brain regions involved in emotional processing. In depression, there is often reduced activity in the prefrontal cortex (involved in executive function and emotional control) and changes in the size and activity of the amygdala (the brain's 'fear centre') and hippocampus.
  • Inflammation: There is growing evidence for a link between chronic low-grade inflammation and depression. Pro-inflammatory cytokines can alter neurotransmitter metabolism and HPA axis function, contributing to depressive symptoms. This helps explain the high co-morbidity of depression with chronic inflammatory medical conditions.
  • Genetic Predisposition: Both anxiety and depression have a significant heritable component. While no single gene is responsible, multiple genes interacting with environmental factors can increase an individual's vulnerability.
  • Organic Disease: Numerous medical conditions can directly cause or mimic psychiatric symptoms. For example, hypothyroidism can cause classic depressive symptoms, hyperthyroidism can mimic anxiety, and cardiac conditions like arrhythmias can present as panic attacks.
Differential Diagnosis (DDx) Framework

A structured approach to the differential diagnosis is essential to differentiate primary psychiatric illness from organic causes.

Must not miss!
  • Psychosis and schizophrenia: Presents with altered thought processes, delusions, or hallucinations alongside mood disturbances.
  • Bipolar disorder: Characterised by distinct episodes of mania or hypomania in addition to depressive episodes.
  • Anorexia nervosa: Involves severe weight loss, an intense fear of gaining weight, and a disturbed body image.
  • Arrhythmia: Can cause sudden episodes of palpitations, dizziness, and a sense of dread mimicking a panic attack.
  • Myocardial Infarction: May present with atypical symptoms like overwhelming anxiety, sweating, and epigastric discomfort, especially in women and older adults.
  • Heart failure: Often presents with breathlessness, fatigue, and orthopnoea which can be associated with significant anxiety.
  • Pulmonary Embolism: Characterised by a sudden onset of breathlessness, pleuritic chest pain, and a sense of impending doom.
  • Hyperthyroidism: Presents with weight loss, heat intolerance, tremor, and tachycardia, closely mimicking symptoms of anxiety.
  • Electrolyte disturbance: Imbalances in calcium, sodium, or potassium can cause confusion, fatigue, and neuropsychiatric symptoms.
  • Phaeochromocytoma: A rare adrenal tumour causing paroxysmal release of catecholamines, leading to episodes of severe headache, sweating, and palpitations.
  • Subdural haematoma: Can present with a gradual decline in cognitive function, headache, and personality change, especially in the elderly after a fall.
  • SOL (Space-Occupying Lesion): A brain tumour can present with new-onset mood or personality changes, headache, or focal neurological signs.
Common causes
  • General Anxiety Disorder: Defined by excessive, uncontrollable worry about multiple issues, accompanied by physical symptoms like restlessness and fatigue.
  • Panic disorder: Characterised by recurrent, unexpected panic attacks and persistent worry about having further attacks.
  • Depression: Presents with persistent low mood, anhedonia (loss of interest or pleasure), and other emotional and physical symptoms.
  • Alcohol abuse: Can cause or exacerbate both anxiety and depression, and withdrawal can precipitate severe anxiety symptoms.
  • Medication-induced anxiety: Can be a side effect of various drugs, such as corticosteroids, stimulants, or bronchodilators.
  • Drug abuse: Use of substances like cannabis, cocaine, or amphetamines can induce anxiety, psychosis, or depressive syndromes.
  • COVID-19: Can be associated with a wide range of neuropsychiatric symptoms including anxiety, depression, and 'brain fog', both acutely and as part of 'long COVID'.
Easily missed
  • Bipolar disorder: Often misdiagnosed as unipolar depression if hypomanic episodes are not specifically asked about.
  • Early Dementia: Can present with apathy, anxiety, and depressive symptoms before significant memory loss becomes apparent.
  • Electrolyte disturbance: Gradual changes in electrolytes can cause non-specific symptoms like fatigue and mood changes that are easily overlooked.
  • Anaemia: Can cause persistent fatigue, poor concentration, and low energy, which can be mistaken for primary depression.
  • Hypothyroidism: Classic mimic of depression, presenting with fatigue, weight gain, cognitive slowing, and low mood.
Other Differential Diagnoses to consider
  • COVID-19: Can present with atypical symptoms including anxiety and mood changes.
  • Sleep Disorders: Chronic sleep disturbances can exacerbate or mimic symptoms of anxiety and depression.
  • Chronic Pain Conditions: Conditions like fibromyalgia or chronic fatigue syndrome often have overlapping mood symptoms.
  • Obsessive-Compulsive Disorder (OCD): May present with anxiety, especially if compulsions or intrusive thoughts cause distress.
  • Post-Traumatic Stress Disorder (PTSD): Often presents with anxiety and depressive symptoms related to past trauma.
  • Personality Disorders: Certain personality disorders, such as borderline personality disorder, can have episodic anxiety and mood disturbances.
  • Menopause or Premenstrual Dysphoric Disorder (PMDD): Hormonal changes can induce mood changes and anxiety.
  • Vitamin D Deficiency: Linked to mood disturbances, including depression and anxiety symptoms.
  • Wilson’s Disease: Rare but can cause psychiatric symptoms, including mood changes.
  • Parkinson’s Disease: Early stages may include anxiety and depression before motor symptoms become prominent.
History Taking
**Patient’s Description**: How can I help you today?
Beginning with an open-ended question, such as "Can you tell me more about what brought you here today?", invites the patient to express their experiences in their own words. Relevance: Allows the patient to articulate their concerns, which guides further specific questions and promotes a patient-centred approach.
Can you describe when you first noticed these symptoms?
  • Understanding the onset of symptoms (e.g., acute, gradual) helps distinguish between different causes.
  • Relevance: A gradual onset may suggest a primary mood disorder such as depression or generalised anxiety, whereas an acute onset could indicate a response to a specific stressor, substance use, or an acute medical cause like arrhythmia.
How have these symptoms changed over time?
  • Questions on progression and fluctuation of symptoms help in identifying episodic versus chronic conditions.
  • Relevance: Fluctuating symptoms may suggest bipolar disorder or a cyclical medical condition, while persistent symptoms are more likely in conditions like depression or generalised anxiety disorder.
Have you noticed any specific triggers or anything that worsens or alleviates your symptoms?
  • Exploring triggers and relieving factors helps to identify psychological, environmental, or physical influences.
  • Relevance: Specific triggers like social situations might suggest social anxiety, while physical activity worsening symptoms could point to cardiac or respiratory causes such as arrhythmia or pulmonary embolism.
Associated Symptom: Do you experience chest pain or palpitations with your symptoms?
  • Asking about chest pain or palpitations is crucial in ruling out cardiac causes, which may present with similar symptoms to anxiety.
  • Relevance: A positive response raises suspicion of cardiac causes like arrhythmia or myocardial infarction, while a negative response may favour a primary anxiety disorder.
Associated Symptom: Have you noticed any significant weight changes or changes in appetite?
  • Changes in weight or appetite can indicate various mood disorders, endocrine conditions, or chronic diseases.
  • Relevance: Weight loss might suggest conditions such as anorexia nervosa, hyperthyroidism, or severe depression, while weight gain could indicate depression or hypothyroidism.
Past Medical History: Have you been diagnosed with any other conditions?
  • Identifying previous diagnoses helps to assess the risk of co-existing conditions that may contribute to current symptoms.
  • Relevance: A history of endocrine disorders, neurological conditions, or cardiovascular disease increases the likelihood of secondary anxiety and depressive symptoms.
Family History: Is there any family history of mood disorders or medical conditions such as thyroid disease?
  • Family history helps in identifying genetic predispositions to certain psychiatric or medical conditions.
  • Relevance: A positive family history of bipolar disorder, major depression, or thyroid disease increases the likelihood of these in the patient.
Drug History: Are you currently taking any medications or using any recreational drugs?
  • Medication and substance use can contribute to anxiety and depressive symptoms.
  • Relevance: Certain medications (e.g., corticosteroids, stimulants) and substances (e.g., alcohol, cannabis) are known to induce or exacerbate these symptoms.
**Ideas, Concerns, and Expectations (ICE)**
  • ICE questions ensure the clinician understands the patient’s perspective on their symptoms and what they hope to achieve from the consultation.
  • Relevance: Helps align management with the patient’s ideas (e.g., concern it may be a heart problem), specific worries (e.g., fear of a serious condition like cancer or heart disease), and expectations (e.g., desire for symptom relief or referral).
Examination Strategy
General Appearance and Behaviour
  • Observing the patient’s general appearance, level of distress, and behaviour can provide insight into their mental state and level of functioning.
  • Relevance: Restlessness or agitation may suggest anxiety disorders or possible stimulant drug use, while poor grooming and slow movements may be indicative of major depression. A calm or withdrawn demeanour may help rule out active anxiety symptoms.
Vital Signs (Heart Rate, Blood Pressure, Respiratory Rate, Temperature)
  • Evaluating vital signs can help identify or rule out medical causes of anxiety and depressive symptoms, particularly cardiovascular or endocrine disorders.
  • Relevance: Tachycardia, hypertension, or tachypnoea may suggest hyperthyroidism, arrhythmia, or phaeochromocytoma, while hypotension could indicate adrenal insufficiency. Stable vitals may make these secondary causes less likely.
Thyroid Examination (Palpation of Thyroid Gland)
  • Palpating the thyroid can help detect thyroid abnormalities such as goitre, which may be associated with hyperthyroidism or hypothyroidism, both of which can contribute to anxiety or depressive symptoms.
  • Relevance: A positive finding of thyroid enlargement or tenderness may suggest thyroid dysfunction, which warrants further investigation. A normal thyroid examination may reduce the likelihood of a thyroid-related cause.
Cardiovascular Examination (Auscultation and Peripheral Pulses)
  • Cardiovascular examination helps detect signs of underlying cardiac issues such as arrhythmias or heart failure, which can present with anxiety-like symptoms.
  • Relevance: Detection of an irregular heartbeat may suggest arrhythmia as a cause of anxiety-like symptoms. Normal heart sounds and pulses help rule out significant cardiac abnormalities as contributors.
Respiratory Examination (Inspection, Percussion, and Auscultation)
  • Examining the respiratory system can help identify conditions like pulmonary embolism or chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD), which may present with anxiety due to dyspnoea.
  • Relevance: Reduced breath sounds, crackles, or wheezing could suggest respiratory pathology such as PE or COPD. Normal respiratory findings make primary respiratory causes for symptoms less likely.
Neurological Examination (Mental Status, Cranial Nerves, Motor and Sensory Assessment)
  • A neurological exam is essential to rule out organic neurological causes, including early dementia or space-occupying lesions that may present with behavioural changes.
  • Relevance: Abnormal findings (e.g., cognitive impairment, asymmetrical reflexes) could indicate neurological causes. A normal neurological exam would help rule out these conditions, supporting a primary psychiatric cause.
Abdominal Examination (Palpation for Hepatomegaly, Splenomegaly)
  • Palpating the abdomen for organomegaly may help identify systemic issues like liver disease or adrenal gland disorders, which could contribute to anxiety and mood disturbances.
  • Relevance: Hepatomegaly or abdominal tenderness may indicate liver disease, affecting mood. Normal findings make an organic abdominal cause less likely.
Skin Examination (Inspection for Pallor, Jaundice, or Signs of Nutritional Deficiencies)
  • Examining the skin may reveal signs of nutritional deficiencies, anaemia, or chronic illness, all of which can manifest as fatigue or mood disturbances.
  • Relevance: Pallor suggests anaemia, while jaundice may indicate hepatic dysfunction, both contributing to mood symptoms. A healthy appearance of the skin suggests lower likelihood of these causes.
Red Flag Synthesis
Sudden onset of severe anxiety symptoms
  • A sudden, intense onset of anxiety could indicate a serious underlying cause, such as an acute cardiovascular or neurological event.
  • Significance: May suggest diagnoses such as myocardial infarction, arrhythmia, or pulmonary embolism, all of which require immediate assessment and intervention.
Chest pain or palpitations
  • Chest pain and palpitations, especially if associated with anxiety, could signal a cardiac issue that mimics or exacerbates anxiety symptoms.
  • Significance: Potentially life-threatening causes, such as myocardial infarction, arrhythmia, or aortic dissection, need to be excluded urgently.
Severe weight loss or failure to thrive
  • Severe, unexplained weight loss is a concerning sign, particularly if seen alongside mood changes or anxiety, as it can indicate systemic illness or malignancy.
  • Significance: Could signify cancer, severe depression, or endocrine disorders like hyperthyroidism or adrenal insufficiency, all of which require prompt investigation.
Altered mental state or psychotic features
  • Changes in mental state, including hallucinations, delusions, or significant confusion, may suggest a serious psychiatric condition or organic brain pathology.
  • Significance: Could indicate psychosis, bipolar disorder, or even neurological conditions like brain tumour or encephalopathy, all necessitating urgent evaluation.
Neurological deficits (e.g., weakness, numbness, altered vision)
  • Neurological symptoms may accompany mood changes and anxiety if a neurological condition is the underlying cause.
  • Significance: Possible signs of stroke, space-occupying lesion, or multiple sclerosis, all of which warrant immediate assessment.
Severe or prolonged sleep disturbance
  • Prolonged sleep disturbance, particularly if accompanied by mood instability, can signal severe psychiatric or organic disease.
  • Significance: May indicate severe depression, mania in bipolar disorder, or even thyroid dysfunction. Prolonged sleep issues increase the risk of worsening psychiatric symptoms and physical health complications.
Suicidal ideation or self-harm
  • Suicidal thoughts or self-harm behaviours are critical signs of severe mental health distress and indicate a high risk of self-injury or fatality.
  • Significance: Immediate intervention is essential to protect patient safety and initiate urgent mental health support for conditions such as major depressive disorder or borderline personality disorder.
Severe agitation or risk of violence
  • Severe agitation or aggression may indicate a high risk of harm to the patient or others and may be due to underlying psychiatric or neurological conditions.
  • Significance: This could suggest acute psychosis, substance intoxication, or delirium, necessitating urgent intervention to ensure safety.
Investigations Strategy & Interpretation
Full Blood Count (FBC)
  • Assesses for anaemia and infection, both of which can present with symptoms similar to fatigue or mood disturbances.
  • Result Significance: A positive result indicating anaemia or infection suggests a potential physical contributor to the symptoms, whereas normal findings make these less likely causes.
Thyroid Function Tests (TFTs)
  • Assesses for thyroid dysfunction, which can manifest with anxiety, irritability, fatigue, or depressive symptoms.
  • Result Significance: Abnormal results indicating hyperthyroidism or hypothyroidism would support thyroid disease as a cause, while normal results would make thyroid disease less likely.
Electrolytes and Renal Function
  • Assesses for electrolyte imbalances, which can cause mood and cognitive changes, as well as contribute to overall systemic health.
  • Result Significance: Abnormal electrolyte levels, such as sodium or calcium abnormalities, would suggest a metabolic or renal cause for symptoms, whereas normal results may rule out such causes.
Liver Function Tests (LFTs)
  • Assesses liver health, as chronic liver disease can contribute to depressive symptoms and fatigue.
  • Result Significance: Abnormal liver enzymes could indicate liver disease, supporting a hepatic origin for symptoms, while normal findings reduce the likelihood of liver-related issues.
ECG (Electrocardiogram)
  • Useful to evaluate for arrhythmias or cardiac conditions that may present with anxiety-like symptoms or palpitations.
  • Result Significance: Abnormalities, such as arrhythmias or conduction issues, could indicate a cardiac source for anxiety or mood symptoms, whereas a normal ECG reduces the likelihood of a cardiac cause.
Cortisol Level or Dexamethasone Suppression Test
  • Assesses adrenal function, as conditions like Cushing’s syndrome or adrenal insufficiency can present with mood changes, anxiety, or depressive symptoms.
  • Result Significance: Elevated cortisol may suggest Cushing’s syndrome, whereas low cortisol could indicate adrenal insufficiency; normal results help exclude these adrenal causes.
Urine Drug Screen
  • Detects recreational drugs, as substance use or withdrawal can mimic or worsen anxiety and depression symptoms.
  • Result Significance: A positive result suggests drug-induced symptoms, while a negative result makes substance use as a primary cause less likely.
Vitamin B12 and Folate Levels
  • Vitamin deficiencies, particularly B12 and folate, can lead to neuropsychiatric symptoms, including fatigue, cognitive impairment, and mood disturbances.
  • Result Significance: Low levels may suggest vitamin deficiencies as a contributory factor, while normal levels make this less likely.
Imaging (CT or MRI of the Brain)
  • Reserved for patients with focal neurological signs or significant changes in cognitive function, imaging can help exclude intracranial pathologies.
  • Result Significance: Abnormal findings, such as a space-occupying lesion, could explain psychiatric symptoms, while normal imaging would help rule out structural brain pathology.
Evidence-Based Diagnosis (EBDx)
Low Thyroid Stimulating Hormone (TSH) for Hyperthyroidism
  • Hyperthyroidism can present with symptoms similar to anxiety, such as restlessness, palpitations, and mood changes.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 95%, Specificity: 90% for hyperthyroidism in patients with low TSH levels.
  • Reference: Biondi B., Cooper DS. "The Clinical Significance of Subclinical Thyroid Dysfunction." Endocrine Reviews, 2008.
  • Significance: A low TSH level strongly suggests hyperthyroidism, helping to identify or rule out an endocrine cause for anxiety symptoms.
ECG Findings for Arrhythmias in Anxiety Presentations
  • An ECG is useful to assess for arrhythmias, which can mimic or exacerbate anxiety symptoms.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 75%, Specificity: 98% for detecting atrial fibrillation in patients with palpitations and anxiety.
  • Reference: January CT et al., "2019 AHA/ACC/HRS Focused Update on Atrial Fibrillation." Circulation, 2019.
  • Significance: Positive findings, such as atrial fibrillation, can indicate a cardiac cause, while a normal ECG can reduce the likelihood of a cardiac-related anxiety presentation.
Elevated Cortisol Levels for Cushing’s Syndrome
  • High cortisol can cause mood disturbances, irritability, and anxiety symptoms, often seen in Cushing’s syndrome.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 87%, Specificity: 95% for diagnosing Cushing’s syndrome with elevated cortisol levels.
  • Reference: Nieman LK, "The Diagnosis of Cushing's Syndrome: An Endocrine Society Clinical Practice Guideline." JCEM, 2008.
  • Significance: Elevated cortisol indicates a likely diagnosis of Cushing’s syndrome, while normal levels make this diagnosis less probable.
Patient Health Questionnaire-9 (PHQ-9) for Depression Screening
  • The PHQ-9 is a validated tool for diagnosing and assessing the severity of depression in primary care settings.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 88%, Specificity: 85% for diagnosing major depressive disorder with a score of ≥10.
  • Reference: Kroenke K, Spitzer RL, Williams JB, "The PHQ-9: Validity of a Brief Depression Severity Measure." J Gen Intern Med, 2001.
  • Significance: A high PHQ-9 score suggests depression, guiding appropriate mental health intervention, while a low score reduces the likelihood of major depressive disorder.
Low Vitamin B12 Levels and Mood Symptoms
  • Vitamin B12 deficiency can cause fatigue, irritability, and mood changes, contributing to depressive symptoms.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 80%, Specificity: 75% for vitamin B12 deficiency in patients with neuropsychiatric symptoms.
  • Reference: Reynolds E, "Vitamin B12, folic acid, and the nervous system." The Lancet, 2006.
  • Significance: Low vitamin B12 suggests a contributory factor to mood changes, and supplementation may improve symptoms; normal levels reduce the likelihood of deficiency as a cause.
CRP and Inflammatory Markers in Depression and Chronic Illness
  • Elevated inflammatory markers like CRP can be seen in chronic inflammatory states, which have been linked with increased risk of depressive symptoms.
  • Evidence: Elevated CRP has been associated with an odds ratio of 1.6 for depression, suggesting inflammatory contribution in patients with chronic symptoms.
  • Reference: Howren MB, Lamkin DM, Suls J, "Associations of Depression With C-Reactive Protein, IL-1, and IL-6: A Meta-Analysis." Psychosom Med, 2009.
  • Significance: Elevated CRP supports an inflammatory basis for mood symptoms, while normal levels suggest a lower likelihood of inflammatory-driven depression.
Diagnostic Timeout & Cognitive Biases

Awareness of common cognitive errors is crucial to avoid misdiagnosis when assessing a patient with symptoms of anxiety or depression. Before finalising a diagnosis, clinicians should take a 'diagnostic timeout' to challenge their reasoning.

  • Diagnostic Overshadowing: This is the most significant bias in this context. It is the tendency to attribute all of a patient's symptoms (including new physical ones) to their existing mental health diagnosis. A patient with known anxiety who presents with palpitations might have their symptoms dismissed as 'just anxiety' without considering a new cardiac arrhythmia. A key question to ask is: "If this patient did not have a history of anxiety/depression, what would my differential diagnosis be?"
  • Premature Closure: The tendency to accept a diagnosis before it has been fully verified. A clinician might quickly label a patient with 'depression' based on low mood and fatigue, without performing the basic blood tests that would have revealed hypothyroidism as the true underlying cause.
  • Confirmation Bias: Actively seeking out or overvaluing evidence that supports an initial belief while ignoring contradictory data. For example, focusing on a recent social stressor to confirm a diagnosis of adjustment disorder, while downplaying the significance of a new headache or weight loss which could be red flags for organic disease.
  • Attribution Error: A tendency to blame the patient's character or choices for their illness. This can lead to dismissing symptoms in patients with a history of alcohol or substance abuse, assuming their presentation is solely due to their lifestyle rather than considering other co-morbidities.
Illustrative Management Pathways

For educational purposes, this section describes the typical principles of management for key diagnoses. These are not prescriptive instructions. All management must be guided by official UK guidelines (e.g., NICE), local formularies, and senior clinical advice.

Depression (Primary Care)

Management follows a stepped-care model. For mild depression, watchful waiting, psychoeducation, and computerised CBT may be appropriate. For persistent mild-to-moderate or moderate-to-severe depression, NICE recommends offering a choice of high-intensity psychological interventions (e.g., CBT, interpersonal therapy) or an antidepressant medication. SSRIs (e.g., sertraline, citalopram) are typically first-line. Regular review is crucial to monitor for response, side effects, and risk.

Generalised Anxiety Disorder (GAD)

The management of GAD also follows a stepped-care model. Step 1 involves identification and psychoeducation. Step 2 focuses on low-intensity psychological interventions (e.g., guided self-help). Step 3 involves offering either a high-intensity psychological intervention (CBT) or a pharmacological treatment. Sertraline is the first-line SSRI recommended by NICE. If ineffective, other SSRIs, SNRIs (e.g., venlafaxine), or pregabalin can be considered. Benzodiazepines should be avoided for long-term treatment due to the risk of dependence.

Acute Psychiatric Emergency (e.g., Suicidal Ideation)

Any patient presenting with active suicidal ideation, intent, or a plan requires immediate and urgent assessment. The priority is to ensure their safety. This involves a comprehensive risk assessment, exploring the nature of their thoughts, plans, access to means, and protective factors. Management involves creating a safety plan with the patient, providing details for crisis support services, and making an urgent referral to the local NHS Mental Health Crisis Team or considering admission to a place of safety under the Mental Health Act if necessary.

Symptoms Secondary to Organic Disease (e.g., Hypothyroidism)

When anxiety or depression symptoms are clearly attributable to an underlying physical health problem, the primary focus of management is to treat that condition. For example, in a patient with new-onset depression and abnormal TFTs consistent with hypothyroidism, the appropriate management is thyroid hormone replacement (e.g., levothyroxine). The mood symptoms will often resolve as the underlying condition is treated. Reassurance and psychoeducation about the link between the physical and mental symptoms are key.

Reflection & Learning

These brief cases illustrate the application of clinical reasoning to different presentations of anxiety and depression. They are for educational purposes only.

Case 1: Suspected GAD in Primary Care

A 28-year-old postgraduate student presents to their GP with a 6-month history of constant worry, poor sleep, muscle tension, and irritability. They worry excessively about their studies, finances, and health. They have no significant past medical history. The problem representation is a young adult with chronic, multi-faceted worry and associated somatic symptoms, with no clear red flags. The likely diagnosis is Generalised Anxiety Disorder. The GP's pathway involves using a screening tool (e.g., GAD-7), performing baseline blood tests (FBC, TFTs) to exclude organic mimics, providing psychoeducation, and discussing stepped-care management options, starting with a referral to IAPT for low-intensity psychological therapy.

Case 2: Low Mood in an Older Patient

A 72-year-old woman is brought in by her daughter, who is concerned about a 4-month history of apathy, social withdrawal, poor appetite with 5kg weight loss, and tearfulness. The patient says she feels "slowed down" and has no energy. The problem representation is an older adult with a subacute presentation of neurovegetative symptoms, where the differential is broad. This could be major depression, but red flags for underlying organic disease (weight loss, apathy) are present. The urgent pathway is a thorough physical examination and a 'senior screen' of blood tests (FBC, U&E, LFT, TFT, B12, Folate, HbA1c, Calcium) and a chest X-ray to screen for malignancy, hypothyroidism, or dementia before concluding a primary psychiatric diagnosis.

Case 3: Acute Panic in the Emergency Department

A 40-year-old man calls an ambulance for a sudden episode of intense fear, palpitations, shortness of breath, and a feeling he was "going to die". The episode started 20 minutes ago while he was watching TV. On arrival in the ED, his vital signs are HR 110, RR 24, SpO2 99%, but are now settling. His ECG and initial troponin are normal. The problem representation is a middle-aged man with a sudden-onset episode of cardiorespiratory symptoms and overwhelming fear. While this has features of a panic attack, life-threatening causes like PE and ACS must be formally excluded given the acute presentation. The pathway involves a full assessment of PE risk (e.g., Wells' score), and consideration of a D-dimer. Once serious pathology is excluded, the diagnosis becomes Panic Disorder, and management involves reassurance, explanation, and discharge with advice to see his GP for long-term management.
