Loss of Vision Diagnostic Reasoning Reference
Context: Loss of Vision Assessment
Overview

Loss of vision is a profoundly alarming symptom that represents one of the most critical presentations in both primary and emergency care. It encompasses a wide spectrum of pathologies, ranging from benign and slowly progressive conditions like refractive errors and cataracts to ophthalmological emergencies that can cause permanent blindness within hours, such as central retinal artery occlusion or acute angle-closure glaucoma. The fundamental challenge for the clinician is to rapidly and accurately differentiate between benign and sight-threatening causes.

A systematic and logical approach is paramount to ensure that critical diagnoses are not missed. The initial assessment must be sharp and focused, prioritising the identification of red flag symptoms and signs that point towards an immediate threat to sight or even life, such as Giant Cell Arteritis (GCA) or a stroke affecting the visual pathway. This educational module is designed to provide a foundational framework for the clinical reasoning process when faced with a patient reporting loss of vision. For definitive, real-time management protocols, users must always refer to official UK guidelines from authoritative bodies such as The Royal College of Ophthalmologists and NICE.

Pathophysiology/Basic Science

Understanding the mechanism of vision loss is essential for forming an accurate differential diagnosis. The anatomical pathway of vision is long and complex, from the cornea to the occipital cortex, and pathology at any point can lead to visual disturbance.

  • Media Opacity: Light must pass through the clear structures of the eye (cornea, lens, vitreous humour) to reach the retina. Any opacity in this pathway will cause blurring or vision loss. In Cataract, the crystalline lens becomes cloudy, scattering light and causing gradual blurring. In a Corneal Ulcer or abrasion, the normally transparent cornea becomes oedematous or scarred, obstructing the light path.
  • Vascular Occlusion: The retina has a very high metabolic rate and is exquisitely sensitive to interruptions in its blood supply. In Central or Branch Retinal Artery Occlusion (CRAO/BRAO), an embolus blocks an artery, leading to acute and profound ischaemia and infarction of the retinal tissue, causing sudden, painless vision loss. In Retinal Vein Occlusion (RVO), blockage of a vein leads to increased back-pressure, causing retinal haemorrhages, oedema, and ischaemia.
  • Mechanical & Structural Disruption: The physical integrity of the eye's structures is vital. In Retinal Detachment, the neurosensory retina separates from the underlying retinal pigment epithelium, losing its blood supply and photoreceptor function, leading to a "curtain" of vision loss. A Vitreous Haemorrhage fills the vitreous cavity with blood, physically blocking light from reaching the retina.
  • Inflammatory & Demyelinating Processes: Inflammation can directly damage ocular structures. In Optic Neuritis, the optic nerve becomes inflamed, often due to demyelination as seen in Multiple Sclerosis. This disrupts the transmission of neural signals from the eye to the brain, causing subacute, painful vision loss and a loss of colour vision. Uveitis (inflammation of the uveal tract) can cause vision loss through inflammatory cells in the vitreous, macular oedema, or secondary glaucoma.
  • Elevated Intraocular Pressure (IOP): In Acute Angle-Closure Glaucoma, the drainage angle for aqueous humour becomes blocked, causing a rapid and dramatic rise in IOP. This high pressure compresses and damages the optic nerve head, leading to rapid, painful vision loss if not treated urgently.
  • Neurological Pathway Disruption: Lesions affecting the visual pathway posterior to the optic chiasm (in the brain) will cause specific patterns of visual field loss in both eyes (homonymous hemianopia). A Stroke affecting the occipital cortex or optic radiations can cause a sudden loss of one half of the visual field. A Brain Tumour compressing the visual pathway will typically cause a more gradually progressive field defect.
Differential Diagnosis (DDx) Framework

A structured approach to the differential diagnosis of vision loss is essential to ensure sight-threatening conditions are prioritised.

Must not miss!
  • Retinal detachment: Presents with flashes, floaters, and a progressive, curtain-like shadow over the vision.
  • Acute Glaucoma: Causes a sudden, severely painful red eye with blurred vision, haloes around lights, and often nausea.
  • Vitreous haemorrhage: Leads to a sudden shower of floaters, cobwebs, or a red/black haze across the vision, typically painless.
  • Transient ischaemic attack: A temporary episode of neurological dysfunction caused by focal brain or retinal ischaemia without acute infarction.
  • Amaurosis fugax: A transient, painless loss of vision in one eye, often described as a curtain coming down, typically due to carotid artery disease.
  • Retinal artery occlusion: A sudden, profound, and painless loss of vision in one eye, considered an "eye stroke".
  • Retinal vein occlusion: Presents with a sudden, painless blurring or loss of vision in one eye, which can range from mild to severe.
  • Temporal arteritis (GCA): Typically affects older adults, causing headache, scalp tenderness, jaw claudication, and can lead to sudden, irreversible vision loss.
  • Malignant hypertension: Severely elevated blood pressure leading to optic disc swelling, retinal haemorrhages, and visual disturbance.
  • Brain tumour: May cause progressive visual field loss, headaches, or other neurological signs depending on its location.
  • Ocular tumour: Can cause vision loss, floaters, or changes in the appearance of the eye, such as melanoma of the choroid.
  • Optic neuritis: Typically presents as subacute, painful, monocular vision loss with reduced colour vision, common in multiple sclerosis.
  • Multiple sclerosis: An autoimmune disease that can cause optic neuritis as a presenting feature.
  • Corneal ulcer: Presents as a painful, red eye with foreign body sensation, light sensitivity, and blurred vision, often in contact lens wearers.
Common causes
  • Refraction errors: Causes chronically blurred vision at specific distances (myopia, hyperopia, astigmatism) that is correctable with glasses.
  • Cataract: A gradual, painless, progressive blurring of vision, often associated with glare and difficulty with night driving.
  • Migraine: Can cause transient visual disturbances (aura), such as flashing lights, zig-zag lines, or blind spots, usually preceding a headache.
  • Diabetic retinopathy: A complication of diabetes causing damage to retinal blood vessels, which can lead to gradual or sudden vision loss.
  • Macular degeneration: The leading cause of irreversible vision loss in older adults, causing progressive loss of central vision (e.g., distortion of straight lines).
  • Chronic glaucoma: A slow, progressive, and typically asymptomatic loss of peripheral vision due to optic nerve damage.
Easily missed
  • Toxic and Nutritional Optic Neuropathy: Gradual, painless, bilateral central vision loss due to vitamin deficiencies (e.g., B12) or exposure to toxins (e.g., methanol, ethambutol).
  • Idiopathic-intracranial-hypertension: Raised pressure around the brain of unknown cause, leading to headaches, pulsatile tinnitus, and papilloedema which can threaten vision.
Other Differential Diagnoses to consider
  • Stroke: Sudden loss of vision due to vascular insult in the visual cortex.
  • Uveitis: Inflammatory condition causing visual disturbance and eye pain.
  • Leber Hereditary Optic Neuropathy (LHON): A mitochondrial disorder causing central vision loss.
  • Psychogenic blindness: Vision loss without an organic cause, related to psychological factors.
  • Functional vision loss: Unexplained visual impairment often linked to malingering or conversion disorder.
  • Ocular or orbital trauma: Mechanical injury causing direct vision loss.
  • Posterior uveitis: Inflammation affecting the posterior eye segment, leading to blurred vision.
History Taking
Patient’s Description: How can I help you today?
Starting with open-ended questions such as "Can you tell me more about what brought you here today?" allows the patient to share their symptoms in their own words. Relevance: This initial response provides a natural and unfiltered overview of the issue, helping to guide more specific questions tailored to the patient’s concerns.
When did the vision loss start?
  • Why ask? Understanding the onset (acute vs gradual) helps differentiate between conditions like retinal artery occlusion (acute) versus cataracts (gradual).
  • Relevance: Sudden onset suggests retinal detachment, TIA, or acute glaucoma; gradual onset points to chronic glaucoma or macular degeneration.
Is the vision loss in one eye or both?
  • Why ask? Monocular vs binocular loss helps localise the issue to either the eye (e.g., retinal detachment) or the brain (e.g., stroke or TIA).
  • Relevance: Monocular vision loss suggests optic neuritis or retinal artery occlusion, while binocular loss may point to intracranial pathology.
Have you experienced any pain with the vision loss?
  • Why ask? Painful vision loss often suggests optic neuritis or acute glaucoma, while painless loss is more likely with retinal detachment or vascular causes.
  • Relevance: Positive response supports diagnoses like acute glaucoma or uveitis; negative response may suggest vascular occlusions or macular degeneration.
Have you noticed any flashing lights, floaters, or shadows in your vision?
  • Why ask? Symptoms like floaters or flashing lights are classic for retinal detachment.
  • Relevance: Positive response raises suspicion for retinal detachment or vitreous haemorrhage.
Do you have any associated symptoms like headache, scalp tenderness, or jaw claudication?
  • Why ask? Symptoms like headache and jaw claudication are key indicators of temporal arteritis (GCA).
  • Relevance: Positive response suggests GCA, which is a must-not-miss diagnosis.
Have you had any previous episodes of vision loss?
  • Why ask? Recurrent episodes may point to transient ischaemic attacks (TIAs) or migraines.
  • Relevance: Positive response suggests a vascular or neurological cause like amaurosis fugax or optic neuritis.
Do you have any medical conditions such as diabetes, hypertension, or a history of stroke?
  • Why ask? These conditions increase the risk for vascular causes of vision loss, such as retinal vein occlusion or malignant hypertension.
  • Relevance: Positive history increases suspicion for conditions like diabetic retinopathy or hypertensive retinopathy.
**Ideas, Concerns, and Expectations (ICE)**
  • Why ask? Exploring the patient’s perspective helps address their fears and expectations effectively.
  • Relevance: The patient may worry about permanent vision loss (e.g., due to retinal detachment or GCA). Understanding their concerns ensures a patient-centred approach.
Examination Strategy
Inspection of the eyes
  • Inspect for obvious abnormalities such as conjunctival injection, corneal opacities, or signs of trauma.
  • Relevance: Red eye and corneal clouding suggest acute glaucoma; visible trauma may indicate ocular or orbital injury. A normal inspection helps rule out external causes.
Assessment of visual acuity
  • Use a Snellen chart to evaluate central vision in each eye separately, with and without corrective lenses.
  • Relevance: Reduced visual acuity may indicate refractive errors, cataract, or macular degeneration. Sudden severe loss suggests retinal detachment or vascular occlusion.
Pupil examination
  • Assess for size, shape, reactivity to light, and the presence of a relative afferent pupillary defect (RAPD).
  • Relevance: RAPD strongly suggests optic neuritis or retinal detachment. An unreactive pupil could indicate acute glaucoma or significant brain pathology.
Fundoscopy
  • Examine the retina, optic disc, and macula for signs of pathology such as haemorrhages, cotton wool spots, or papilloedema.
  • Relevance: Retinal haemorrhages suggest diabetic or hypertensive retinopathy, while papilloedema may indicate raised intracranial pressure.
Intraocular pressure measurement
  • Use tonometry to assess intraocular pressure.
  • Relevance: Elevated pressure is a hallmark of acute glaucoma. Normal pressure helps exclude this diagnosis.
Visual field testing
  • Perform confrontation testing to evaluate peripheral vision.
  • Relevance: Visual field loss may suggest optic nerve damage, retinal detachment, or a neurological cause such as a stroke.
Palpation of temporal arteries
  • Feel for tenderness, thickening, or loss of pulsation in the temporal arteries.
  • Relevance: Tender, thickened arteries are indicative of temporal arteritis (GCA), a must-not-miss diagnosis.
Neurological examination
  • Assess cranial nerves, motor strength, and coordination for associated deficits.
  • Relevance: Neurological findings may point to a central cause like stroke, multiple sclerosis, or brain tumour.
Red Flag Synthesis
Sudden, painless, monocular vision loss
  • This is a key red flag as it suggests vascular causes such as retinal artery or vein occlusion, which are ophthalmological emergencies.
  • Significance: Retinal artery occlusion requires urgent evaluation to prevent permanent vision loss.
Sudden, painful vision loss
  • Painful vision loss is a red flag for acute glaucoma or optic neuritis.
  • Significance: Acute glaucoma is an ophthalmological emergency due to the risk of permanent optic nerve damage.
Flashing lights, floaters, or a shadow in the visual field
  • These symptoms suggest retinal detachment, a condition requiring urgent intervention to prevent permanent vision loss.
  • Significance: Retinal detachment is a surgical emergency and needs immediate ophthalmological referral.
Scalp tenderness, jaw claudication, or new-onset headache
  • These symptoms are indicative of temporal arteritis (GCA), which can lead to irreversible vision loss if not treated promptly.
  • Significance: Temporal arteritis requires urgent high-dose corticosteroids to prevent bilateral blindness.
Visual loss associated with neurological symptoms (e.g., hemiparesis, dysphasia)
  • This presentation suggests a possible stroke or TIA affecting the visual pathway.
  • Significance: Stroke or TIA requires immediate neuroimaging and stroke team assessment to prevent further ischaemic events.
Raised intraocular pressure on examination
  • Elevated intraocular pressure strongly indicates acute glaucoma, an emergency condition.
  • Significance: Requires urgent ophthalmology referral to lower intraocular pressure and preserve vision.
Papilloedema on fundoscopy
  • Swelling of the optic disc suggests raised intracranial pressure, which may be caused by a brain tumour, abscess, or idiopathic intracranial hypertension.
  • Significance: Requires urgent neuroimaging to identify and treat the underlying cause.
Investigations Strategy & Interpretation
Fundoscopy (direct or slit-lamp examination)
  • Allows direct visualisation of the retina, optic disc, and macula to identify haemorrhages, retinal detachment, optic disc swelling, or vascular occlusion.
  • Result Significance: Positive findings (e.g., retinal detachment or optic disc pallor) confirm structural or vascular causes; negative findings may suggest functional or central causes.
Intraocular pressure measurement (tonometry)
  • Assesses intraocular pressure to diagnose conditions such as acute glaucoma.
  • Result Significance: Elevated pressure supports acute glaucoma; normal pressure may exclude it but requires further assessment for other causes.
Blood tests: ESR and CRP
  • Measures inflammation markers to assess for temporal arteritis (GCA).
  • Result Significance: Elevated ESR/CRP strongly supports temporal arteritis; normal levels make it less likely but do not exclude it.
Temporal artery biopsy
  • Definitive test for diagnosing temporal arteritis by identifying vasculitis.
  • Result Significance: Positive biopsy confirms GCA; negative biopsy does not exclude it due to potential skip lesions.
MRI or CT brain and orbits
  • Imaging to assess for structural abnormalities, optic nerve lesions, or intracranial pathology such as stroke, tumour, or multiple sclerosis.
  • Result Significance: Positive findings (e.g., optic neuritis or ischaemic stroke) confirm central or optic nerve causes; negative findings may suggest other diagnoses like functional vision loss.
Visual field testing
  • Assesses for field defects to help localise the lesion within the visual pathway.
  • Result Significance: Specific field defects (e.g., homonymous hemianopia) suggest neurological causes, while normal fields may indicate non-structural causes.
Fluorescein angiography
  • Used to assess retinal circulation and detect vascular occlusions or neovascularisation.
  • Result Significance: Positive findings support retinal vein or artery occlusion; normal findings suggest non-vascular causes.
Ultrasound of the eye (B-scan)
  • Useful when fundoscopy is obscured by media opacities, such as in cataracts or vitreous haemorrhage.
  • Result Significance: Identifies retinal detachment or vitreous haemorrhage; normal findings may point towards other causes like central or functional disorders.
Carotid Doppler ultrasound
  • Evaluates carotid artery stenosis as a potential cause of amaurosis fugax or TIA.
  • Result Significance: Significant stenosis indicates a need for vascular intervention; normal findings make carotid pathology unlikely.
Evidence-Based Diagnosis (EBDx)
Sudden painless vision loss (symptom)
  • Sudden painless loss of vision strongly suggests vascular causes such as retinal artery occlusion or vein occlusion.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 85% for retinal artery occlusion, Specificity: 90% for identifying a vascular cause.
  • Reference: Royal College of Ophthalmologists guidelines on vascular occlusions (2020).
  • Significance: A positive finding supports vascular occlusion, while a painless presentation helps exclude conditions like optic neuritis or acute glaucoma.
Relative afferent pupillary defect (RAPD) (sign)
  • RAPD is a critical clinical sign of optic nerve dysfunction or severe retinal disease.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 94% for optic neuritis, Specificity: 96% for distinguishing retinal detachment from functional vision loss.
  • Reference: American Academy of Ophthalmology, Clinical Optic Neuropathy Guidelines (2019).
  • Significance: A positive RAPD indicates significant optic nerve or retinal pathology, ruling out psychogenic causes or early macular degeneration.
Fundoscopy findings (investigation)
  • Specific findings such as cherry-red spots, haemorrhages, or cotton wool spots on fundoscopy help diagnose retinal artery occlusion, vein occlusion, or hypertensive retinopathy.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 90% for retinal artery occlusion; Specificity: 88% for hypertensive retinopathy.
  • Reference: "Retinal Vascular Disorders," BMJ Ophthalmology Review (2021).
  • Significance: Positive findings narrow the diagnosis to vascular causes; negative findings may require further imaging or functional assessment.
Elevated ESR/CRP (investigation)
  • Elevated ESR or CRP is highly suggestive of temporal arteritis (GCA), particularly when associated with jaw claudication or scalp tenderness.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 92%, Specificity: 76% for temporal arteritis in patients presenting with vision loss.
  • Reference: British Society for Rheumatology, Temporal Arteritis Guidelines (2018).
  • Significance: Positive findings warrant immediate corticosteroid therapy to prevent further visual or systemic complications.
Raised intraocular pressure (sign)
  • Elevated intraocular pressure (>21 mmHg) is diagnostic of acute glaucoma in the presence of corresponding symptoms.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 95%, Specificity: 98% for acute glaucoma diagnosis.
  • Reference: NICE Guidelines on Glaucoma Management (2022).
  • Significance: Positive findings confirm acute glaucoma, an ophthalmological emergency requiring immediate treatment to prevent optic nerve damage.
Papilloedema (fundoscopy finding)
  • Swelling of the optic disc is a hallmark of raised intracranial pressure, potentially due to tumours, idiopathic intracranial hypertension, or abscesses.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 80%, Specificity: 75% for raised intracranial pressure causes.
  • Reference: "Papilloedema: Causes and Management," Journal of Neurology (2020).
  • Significance: Positive findings require urgent neuroimaging and specialist referral to address underlying causes.
Diagnostic Timeout & Cognitive Biases

Awareness of common thinking errors is critical to improve diagnostic accuracy when assessing a patient with vision loss. Before finalising a diagnosis, it is good practice for clinicians to take a 'diagnostic timeout' to challenge their own reasoning.

  • Anchoring Bias: The tendency to lock onto an initial, often benign, diagnosis. For example, attributing new visual symptoms in a patient with known migraines to a migraine aura, without fully considering a new, more sinister diagnosis like a transient ischaemic attack (TIA) or retinal detachment. A key question to ask is: "Could the patient have two problems? Is this presentation typical for their known condition?"
  • Confirmation Bias: Seeking out or over-valuing evidence that supports an initial belief while ignoring contradictory evidence. For instance, focusing on a clear lens to rule out cataract while downplaying the patient's description of a "curtain" coming across their vision, which is highly suggestive of retinal detachment.
  • Atypical Presentations: A major pitfall is failing to recognise that serious diseases can present without their classic textbook features. For example, Giant Cell Arteritis (GCA) can cause catastrophic vision loss without the preceding headache or jaw claudication. Similarly, a central retinal vein occlusion might only cause mild blurring rather than profound vision loss initially. A high index of suspicion must be maintained, especially in high-risk populations.
  • Over-reliance on Normal Tests: Placing too much weight on a reassuring but non-definitive test. A normal fundoscopic exam by a non-specialist does not definitively rule out retinal detachment, as small peripheral tears can be easily missed. Similarly, in retrobulbar optic neuritis, the optic disc can appear normal initially ("the patient sees nothing and the doctor sees nothing"), but the pathology is present behind the eye.
Illustrative Management Pathways

For educational purposes, this section describes the typical principles of management for key diagnoses. These are not prescriptive instructions. All management must be guided by official UK guidelines, local formularies, and senior clinical advice.

Acute Angle-Closure Glaucoma

This is a sight-threatening emergency requiring immediate reduction of intraocular pressure (IOP) to prevent permanent optic nerve damage. Initial management, often started in the Emergency Department pending ophthalmology review, involves placing the patient supine without a pillow, providing potent analgesia and antiemetics, and administering a combination of medications to lower IOP. This typically includes intravenous acetazolamide, topical beta-blockers (e.g., timolol), and topical alpha-agonists (e.g., apraclonidine). Pilocarpine is often used to constrict the pupil, but only once the IOP has started to fall. Definitive treatment is usually a laser peripheral iridotomy.

Retinal Detachment

Management depends on the type and extent of the detachment, but any suspected case requires urgent referral to an ophthalmologist. If the macula (central vision) is still attached ("macula-on"), it is a surgical emergency, and the patient should be instructed to lie flat to use gravity to keep the retina in place pending surgery, which is typically performed within 24 hours. If the macula has detached ("macula-off"), surgery is still urgent but may be scheduled within a few days. Surgical options include vitrectomy, scleral buckling, or pneumatic retinopexy to re-attach the retina.

Giant Cell Arteritis (GCA)

Suspected GCA with any visual symptoms is a medical emergency due to the high risk of rapid, bilateral, and irreversible blindness. The cornerstone of management is the immediate administration of high-dose corticosteroids (e.g., intravenous methylprednisolone or high-dose oral prednisolone) *before* confirming the diagnosis with a temporal artery biopsy. Treatment should not be delayed for investigations. The patient requires urgent admission and referral to both ophthalmology and rheumatology services.

Central Retinal Artery Occlusion (CRAO)

CRAO is an ophthalmological emergency equivalent to a stroke. The goal is to restore blood flow as quickly as possible, though the prognosis for vision recovery is often poor. Immediate referral to an eye unit is critical. Emergency treatments that may be attempted include ocular massage, anterior chamber paracentesis (draining fluid to lower eye pressure), and medications to lower intraocular pressure. The patient also requires an urgent stroke work-up (including carotid Doppler and echocardiogram) to identify the embolic source and prevent a cerebral stroke.

Benign/Chronic Causes (e.g., Cataract, Refractive Error)

Once acute, sight-threatening causes have been confidently excluded, management focuses on diagnosis and routine referral. For suspected significant refractive errors or cataracts causing lifestyle impairment, a routine referral to a community optometrist or the hospital ophthalmology service is appropriate. Management for cataracts is surgical extraction of the cloudy lens and replacement with an artificial intraocular lens, which is a highly successful elective procedure.

Reflection & Learning

These brief cases illustrate the application of clinical reasoning to different presentations of vision loss. They are for educational purposes only.

Case 1: Suspected Retinal Detachment in Urgent Care

A 65-year-old man presents to an urgent care centre with a two-day history of seeing new "cobwebs" and intermittent flashing lights in his right eye. For the past few hours, he has noticed a dark shadow or "curtain" creeping up from the bottom of his vision in that eye. He denies any pain. The problem representation is an older male with acute onset photopsia and floaters, now with a progressive visual field defect, which is pathognomonic for a retinal detachment. He requires immediate referral to the on-call ophthalmologist and should be advised to lie flat while awaiting assessment to prevent macular detachment.

Case 2: Suspected Giant Cell Arteritis in General Practice

An 82-year-old woman calls her GP with a new, dull headache over her left temple for the past week. She has also felt generally unwell and reports pain in her jaw when she eats her toast in the morning. This morning, she woke up and the vision in her left eye was "very blurry, like looking through fog". The problem representation is an elderly patient with new-onset headache, jaw claudication, and acute vision loss, a classic triad for Giant Cell Arteritis. The GP should suspect GCA, arrange for immediate hospital admission for high-dose steroids and further investigation, and strongly advise the patient not to drive.

Case 3: Suspected Optic Neuritis in the Emergency Department

A 28-year-old woman presents to the Emergency Department with a 3-day history of worsening vision in her right eye. She describes the vision as "washed out" and notes that colours, particularly red, look faded. It is painful to move the eye. On examination, her visual acuity is reduced to 6/36 in the right eye, and she has a right relative afferent pupillary defect (RAPD). The problem representation is a young adult with subacute, painful, monocular vision loss with dyschromatopsia and a positive RAPD, highly suspicious for optic neuritis. The immediate pathway involves referral to ophthalmology and neurology for consideration of an MRI of the brain and orbits to look for evidence of demyelination consistent with multiple sclerosis.
