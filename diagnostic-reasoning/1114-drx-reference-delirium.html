Delirium Diagnostic Reasoning Reference
Context: Delirium Assessment
Overview

Delirium, often termed 'acute confusional state', is a common and serious medical emergency, particularly in older and hospitalised patients. It manifests as an acute decline in attention and cognition, which fluctuates over time. It is not a disease in itself but a syndrome—a clinical presentation of an underlying medical problem, drug toxicity, or substance withdrawal. The primary challenge for any clinician is to recognise delirium promptly and identify and treat the underlying cause(s) to prevent poor outcomes, including increased mortality, prolonged hospital stays, and long-term cognitive decline.

This educational module is designed to build a foundational understanding of the clinical reasoning process for a patient presenting with delirium. A systematic approach is crucial to ensure critical diagnoses are identified and managed effectively. For definitive, real-time management protocols, users must refer to official UK guidelines from authoritative bodies such as the National Institute for Health and Care Excellence (NICE), specifically guideline NG103 on Delirium: prevention, diagnosis and management.

Pathophysiology/Basic Science

The exact pathophysiology of delirium is not fully understood, but it is considered a manifestation of global brain dysfunction. The leading hypotheses involve a complex interplay of neuroinflammation, neurotransmitter imbalance, and network dysregulation, often triggered by a peripheral insult like infection or surgery.

  • Neurotransmitter Imbalance: The "cholinergic deficiency" hypothesis is a cornerstone theory, suggesting that a reduction in brain acetylcholine activity plays a key role. This is supported by the fact that many drugs with anticholinergic properties can induce delirium. Conversely, an excess of dopamine is also implicated, which helps explain why antipsychotic agents (dopamine antagonists) can sometimes help manage symptoms. Other neurotransmitters like serotonin, GABA, and glutamate are also involved.
  • Neuroinflammation: Systemic inflammation, triggered by infection (e.g., sepsis), trauma, or surgery, leads to the release of pro-inflammatory cytokines (like IL-1, IL-6, TNF-α). These cytokines can cross the blood-brain barrier, activating microglia (the brain's resident immune cells) and causing widespread neuronal disruption, which manifests as the cognitive and attentional deficits of delirium.
  • Network Disconnectivity: Delirium may represent a functional "disconnection" or breakdown in communication between different brain regions that normally work together to maintain attention, cognition, and consciousness. Stress, metabolic disturbances (like hypoxia or hypoglycaemia), and structural brain disease can disrupt these large-scale networks, leading to a state of disordered brain function.
  • Role of Stress: Acute illness and hospitalisation are significant stressors, leading to elevated cortisol levels. Prolonged or excessive cortisol can be neurotoxic and can exacerbate the neurotransmitter and inflammatory changes described above, further contributing to the development of delirium.
Differential Diagnosis (DDx) Framework

A structured approach to the differential diagnosis of delirium is essential to ensure life-threatening conditions are prioritised. The underlying causes can be remembered with mnemonics like 'PINCH ME' (Pain, Infection, Nutrition, Constipation, Hydration, Medication, Electrolytes) or 'I WATCH DEATH'.

Must not miss!
  • Sepsis: A life-threatening organ dysfunction caused by a dysregulated host response to infection, often presenting with fever, tachycardia, and hypotension alongside confusion.
  • Meningitis: Inflammation of the membranes surrounding the brain and spinal cord, classically presenting with fever, headache, neck stiffness, and photophobia.
  • Encephalitis: Inflammation of the brain tissue itself, often viral, presenting with fever, altered consciousness, and sometimes seizures or focal neurological signs.
  • Myocardial Infarction: A "silent" or atypical MI can present with delirium, particularly in the elderly, due to reduced cardiac output and cerebral hypoperfusion.
  • Cerebrovascular Disease: An acute stroke, particularly involving the thalamus or temporal lobes, can present primarily with confusion rather than focal motor deficits.
  • Subdural haematoma: A collection of blood under the dura mater, often after minor head trauma in the elderly or those on anticoagulants, causing a gradual decline in consciousness.
  • Hypoxia: Reduced oxygen supply to the brain from any cause (e.g., pneumonia, PE, heart failure) is a potent trigger for delirium.
  • Hypoglycaemia: Critically low blood sugar, which can rapidly lead to irreversible brain injury if not corrected immediately.
  • Hyperglycaemic Crisis: Very high blood sugar (HHS or DKA) causes severe dehydration and metabolic disturbance, leading to delirium.
  • Heart failure: Acute decompensation can cause cerebral hypoperfusion and hypoxia from pulmonary oedema, presenting as delirium.
  • Renal failure: Acute kidney injury leads to the accumulation of uraemic toxins that are neurotoxic and cause confusion (uraemic encephalopathy).
  • Liver failure: Inability of the liver to clear toxins like ammonia leads to hepatic encephalopathy, presenting with fluctuating confusion and asterixis.
  • Acute urinary retention: A painful, distended bladder is a common and easily reversible cause of delirium in older men.
  • Dehydration: Can lead to electrolyte disturbances and reduced cerebral perfusion, a very common precipitant of delirium.
  • Hypothermia: A low core body temperature slows all metabolic processes, including cerebral function, leading to confusion.
  • Electrolyte disturbance: Abnormal levels of sodium, calcium, or potassium can directly impair neuronal function.
Common causes
  • UTI: Urinary tract infections are a classic trigger for delirium in the elderly, even without typical urinary symptoms.
  • LRTI: Lower respiratory tract infections, including bronchitis and pneumonia, are a major cause of delirium due to hypoxia and systemic inflammation.
  • Pneumonia: Infection of the lung parenchyma is a very common cause of delirium in hospitalised patients.
  • Cellulitis: A significant skin and soft tissue infection can be a sufficient inflammatory trigger for delirium.
  • Constipation induced delirium: Severe constipation can cause pain, discomfort, and toxic absorption, precipitating delirium, especially in frail older adults.
  • COVID-19: The SARS-CoV-2 virus can cause delirium through direct neurological effects, hypoxia, or systemic inflammation.
  • Dementia: While a chronic condition, patients with dementia have a lower threshold for developing delirium from any superimposed medical problem.
  • Insomnia: Severe sleep deprivation and disruption of the sleep-wake cycle is a major contributing factor to delirium in hospital.
  • Anxiety: While distinct from delirium, severe anxiety can present with agitation and poor concentration, sometimes overlapping with a hyperactive delirium.
  • Depression: Severe depression can present with psychomotor slowing and apathy, mimicking hypoactive delirium.
  • Drug and Alcohol abuse: Intoxication with many substances or withdrawal, especially from alcohol or benzodiazepines, are potent causes of delirium.
Easily missed
  • Medication induced delirium: A huge number of drugs can cause delirium, especially those with anticholinergic effects, opioids, benzodiazepines, and steroids.
  • Serotonin syndrome: A potentially life-threatening condition caused by excess serotonergic activity, presenting with confusion, autonomic instability, and neuromuscular excitation.
    • - Hypothyroidism: Severe underactive thyroid (myxoedema coma) can present with profound lethargy, hypothermia, and confusion.
  • Hyperthyriodism: A "thyroid storm" can cause hyperactive delirium with agitation, fever, and tachycardia.
  • Vitamin deficiencies: Severe deficiency of thiamine (B1) leading to Wernicke's Encephalopathy, or B12 deficiency can both cause confusional states.
Other Differential Diagnoses to consider
  • COVID-19: Can present with delirium, particularly in older or frail patients.
  • Delirium Tremens: Severe alcohol withdrawal syndrome, often presenting with delirium.
  • Neuroleptic Malignant Syndrome (NMS): A life-threatening reaction to antipsychotic drugs, presenting with delirium, fever, and muscle rigidity.
  • Wernicke's Encephalopathy: Thiamine deficiency, often in alcoholics, presenting with delirium, ophthalmoplegia, and ataxia.
  • Hyperparathyroidism: Can cause hypercalcaemia, leading to confusion and delirium.
  • Delirium due to Metabolic Disorders: Including hepatic encephalopathy, uraemic encephalopathy, or hypocalcaemia.
History Taking
**Patient’s Description**: How can I help you today?
Starting with an open-ended question, such as "Can you tell me more about what brought you here today?" allows the patient to describe their symptoms in their own words. This approach helps the clinician understand the nature of the patient's concerns and directs further questioning. Relevance: Allows the patient to freely explain the issue, providing vital clues about onset, severity, and specific concerns, which guide the clinician in forming targeted follow-up questions.
When did the confusion start?
  • This question helps establish the onset and duration of delirium. Sudden onset suggests an acute illness or metabolic disturbance, while a more gradual onset could indicate chronic conditions like dementia.
  • Relevance: A sudden onset points towards acute causes such as infection, metabolic disturbances, or cerebrovascular events. A more gradual onset may suggest dementia or slow-growing intracranial pathology.
Has anything seemed to trigger or worsen the confusion?
  • Determining triggers or exacerbating factors can help narrow down the cause. Delirium might be worsened by infections, dehydration, or new medications.
  • Relevance: Worsening confusion after starting a new medication could suggest medication-induced delirium, while triggers like infections or surgery may suggest an underlying acute illness.
Have there been any recent fevers, chills, or infections?
  • Infections, particularly urinary tract infections or pneumonia, are common precipitants of delirium in older adults.
  • Relevance: A positive response suggests infection (e.g., UTI, pneumonia, sepsis), which is a common cause of delirium. A negative response makes infection less likely but does not rule it out completely.
Have there been any recent changes in medications?
  • Medication changes, particularly starting or increasing sedative, anticholinergic, or opioid medications, can cause delirium.
  • Relevance: A positive response raises suspicion for medication-induced delirium, especially if anticholinergics, benzodiazepines, or opioids are involved.
Has there been any recent constipation?
  • Constipation, particularly in older adults, can precipitate delirium due to discomfort or increased absorption of toxins.
  • Relevance: A positive response raises the possibility of constipation-induced delirium, which is often reversible with treatment.
Have you noticed any symptoms of shortness of breath or chest pain?
  • Delirium may be a sign of underlying hypoxia or heart failure, especially if associated with respiratory symptoms or chest pain.
  • Relevance: Positive response could suggest hypoxia from pneumonia, pulmonary embolism, or heart failure as a cause of delirium.
Has the patient had any recent head trauma?
  • Head trauma, even minor, can result in subdural haematoma, which can present with delayed delirium, especially in elderly patients or those on anticoagulants.
  • Relevance: A positive response raises concern for subdural haematoma or other head injury, requiring further imaging.
Has the patient been consuming alcohol or any other substances?
  • Substance use or withdrawal, particularly from alcohol, can cause delirium or precipitate conditions like delirium tremens.
  • Relevance: A positive response suggests delirium related to substance abuse or withdrawal (e.g., delirium tremens), which can be life-threatening if not addressed promptly.
**Ideas, Concerns, and Expectations (ICE)**
  • Asking about the patient’s ideas, concerns, and expectations helps align the consultation with the patient’s understanding of the issue and addresses any specific worries.
  • Relevance: Helps ensure that the consultation addresses the patient’s perspective, including any fears about serious conditions such as strokes or infections, and their expectations regarding the management plan.
Examination Strategy
General Inspection
  • Observe the patient’s overall appearance, behaviour, level of consciousness, and any signs of distress or agitation.
  • Relevance: General agitation, restlessness, or altered consciousness suggests delirium. Lethargy or reduced consciousness may indicate metabolic or toxic causes. Negative findings may rule out acute severe neurological or toxic states.
Assessment of Consciousness (Glasgow Coma Scale)
  • Use the Glasgow Coma Scale (GCS) to quantify the level of consciousness. GCS assesses eye, verbal, and motor responses.
  • Relevance: A reduced GCS score may suggest a serious underlying condition such as head injury, stroke, or metabolic disturbance (e.g., hypoglycaemia). Normal GCS may point to less severe causes of delirium like dehydration or mild infections.
Assessment of Orientation and Cognitive Function
  • Assess orientation to person, place, and time. Consider using brief cognitive tests such as the Abbreviated Mental Test Score (AMTS).
  • Relevance: Disorientation is a key feature of delirium. A positive finding supports the diagnosis of delirium, whereas intact orientation may suggest other causes, such as anxiety or insomnia, especially if transient.
Vital Signs (Temperature, Pulse, Blood Pressure, Respiratory Rate, Oxygen Saturation)
  • Check for fever, tachycardia, hypotension, tachypnoea, and hypoxia. Vital signs can indicate infection, metabolic derangements, or hypoxia.
  • Relevance: Fever and tachycardia may suggest infections (e.g., UTI, pneumonia, sepsis). Hypoxia and tachypnoea may indicate respiratory or cardiac causes like pneumonia or heart failure. Hypotension may indicate sepsis or dehydration.
Neurological Examination (Cranial Nerves, Motor, Sensory, Reflexes, Coordination)
  • Examine for focal neurological signs, including cranial nerve assessment, motor and sensory function, reflexes, and coordination.
  • Relevance: Focal neurological deficits, such as facial droop or limb weakness, suggest cerebrovascular events like stroke or subdural haematoma. Negative findings reduce the likelihood of stroke or space-occupying lesions but do not rule out metabolic causes.
Cardiovascular Examination (Heart Sounds, JVP, Oedema)
  • Auscultate for abnormal heart sounds, check for raised jugular venous pressure (JVP), and assess for peripheral oedema.
  • Relevance: Signs of heart failure, such as raised JVP and oedema, suggest a cardiac cause of delirium (e.g., heart failure or MI). Absence of these signs may rule out heart failure as a primary cause.
Respiratory Examination (Chest Auscultation, Percussion)
  • Auscultate the lungs for abnormal breath sounds, and assess for any signs of respiratory infection or congestion.
  • Relevance: Crackles or reduced breath sounds suggest pneumonia or pulmonary oedema, both potential causes of delirium. Normal lung sounds make respiratory causes less likely.
Abdominal Examination
  • Check for abdominal tenderness, distension, or palpable masses. Examine for signs of constipation or urinary retention.
  • Relevance: Abdominal tenderness or palpable bladder may indicate constipation or urinary retention as potential causes of delirium. Negative findings make gastrointestinal or urinary causes less likely.
Skin Examination (Signs of Infection or Dehydration)
  • Inspect the skin for rashes, cellulitis, or signs of dehydration (dry mucous membranes, poor skin turgor).
  • Relevance: Cellulitis or signs of dehydration may indicate infection or electrolyte disturbances as causes of delirium. Normal findings make skin or systemic infection less likely.
Red Flag Synthesis
Sudden Onset of Confusion
  • A sudden onset of confusion could suggest an acute, life-threatening condition such as stroke, head trauma, or metabolic disturbances (e.g., hypoglycaemia or hyperglycaemia).
  • Significance: This could indicate an acute cerebrovascular event (e.g., stroke), intracranial bleeding (e.g., subdural haematoma), or severe metabolic derangements, all of which require urgent investigation and treatment.
Fever and Altered Mental Status
  • The combination of fever and delirium raises concern for serious infections such as sepsis, meningitis, or encephalitis.
  • Significance: This could signify systemic infection (sepsis), meningitis, or encephalitis. These conditions require urgent antibiotic therapy and supportive care.
New Focal Neurological Deficits
  • New focal neurological signs, such as unilateral weakness or facial droop, could indicate a stroke or intracranial mass.
  • Significance: This may point to a cerebrovascular event (e.g., ischaemic or haemorrhagic stroke) or a space-occupying lesion (e.g., brain tumour), necessitating immediate neuroimaging and management.
Severe Hypotension or Shock
  • Severe hypotension, especially with altered mental status, suggests hypoperfusion of vital organs and could be due to conditions like sepsis, heart failure, or haemorrhage.
  • Significance: This could indicate septic shock, acute heart failure, or major haemorrhage. These are life-threatening emergencies that require immediate resuscitation and intervention.
Severe Dyspnoea or Hypoxia
  • Severe breathlessness or hypoxia (low oxygen saturation) can indicate respiratory failure, pulmonary embolism, or acute heart failure, all of which can present with delirium.
  • Significance: This could point to pneumonia, pulmonary embolism, or heart failure, all requiring urgent oxygen therapy and further investigation (e.g., chest X-ray, CT scan).
Signs of Head Trauma
  • History or signs of recent head trauma, particularly in elderly patients, may indicate a subdural haematoma or other intracranial injury.
  • Significance: This could indicate a subdural haematoma or other intracranial bleed, which may require urgent neuroimaging and possible neurosurgical intervention.
Signs of Alcohol Withdrawal (Delirium Tremens)
  • Severe agitation, tremors, sweating, and hallucinations in a patient with a history of chronic alcohol use suggest delirium tremens, a life-threatening form of alcohol withdrawal.
  • Significance: This could indicate delirium tremens, which is a medical emergency requiring prompt treatment with benzodiazepines and supportive care.
Hypoglycaemia (Low Blood Sugar)
  • Delirium in a patient with diabetes or at risk of low blood sugar requires urgent glucose measurement, as hypoglycaemia can be rapidly life-threatening if untreated.
  • Significance: This could indicate hypoglycaemia, which can lead to seizures or coma if not corrected immediately with glucose administration.
Severe Electrolyte Imbalance
  • Severe electrolyte imbalances, such as hyperkalaemia or hyponatraemia, can manifest with delirium and pose an immediate threat to life if not corrected.
  • Significance: This could signify life-threatening metabolic disturbances, such as severe hyponatraemia (which can cause cerebral oedema) or hyperkalaemia (risking cardiac arrest), both of which require urgent correction.
Investigations Strategy & Interpretation
Full Blood Count (FBC)
  • FBC helps to identify infections (elevated white cell count) or anaemia (low haemoglobin), both of which can contribute to delirium.
  • Result Significance: A high white cell count suggests infection (e.g., pneumonia, UTI), while anaemia might indicate chronic disease or occult bleeding. A normal result does not rule out more subtle causes of delirium.
C-Reactive Protein (CRP)
  • CRP is an inflammatory marker that rises in response to infections and other inflammatory conditions.
  • Result Significance: Elevated CRP suggests infection or inflammation (e.g., sepsis, pneumonia). A normal CRP can help rule out a significant infectious or inflammatory cause of delirium.
Urea and Electrolytes (U&Es)
  • U&Es help detect electrolyte imbalances (e.g., hyponatraemia, hyperkalaemia) or renal dysfunction, which are common causes of delirium.
  • Result Significance: Abnormal sodium levels (e.g., hyponatraemia) can directly cause confusion. Elevated urea and creatinine suggest renal failure, which may be contributing to delirium. Normal U&Es reduce the likelihood of renal or electrolyte causes.
Liver Function Tests (LFTs)
  • LFTs are useful in assessing hepatic encephalopathy or alcohol-related liver disease, both of which can present with delirium.
  • Result Significance: Abnormal LFTs may indicate liver failure (e.g., hepatic encephalopathy) or alcoholic liver disease. Normal LFTs make hepatic causes of delirium less likely.
Blood Glucose Levels
  • Hypoglycaemia and hyperglycaemia are both reversible causes of delirium, especially in diabetic patients.
  • Result Significance: Low blood glucose suggests hypoglycaemia, a medical emergency requiring immediate correction. Elevated glucose suggests hyperglycaemia, which could indicate diabetic ketoacidosis or hyperosmolar hyperglycaemic state (HHS).
Urinalysis and Urine Culture
  • Urinary tract infections are a common cause of delirium, particularly in the elderly, and may not always present with typical urinary symptoms.
  • Result Significance: Positive urinalysis (e.g., presence of nitrites, leukocytes) and a positive urine culture suggest UTI. Negative findings reduce the likelihood of a UTI as the cause of delirium.
Chest X-ray
  • Chest X-rays are indicated if there is suspicion of pneumonia or other respiratory causes of delirium, particularly in patients with hypoxia or abnormal lung examination findings.
  • Result Significance: Positive findings (e.g., consolidation) suggest pneumonia. A normal chest X-ray reduces the likelihood of a respiratory infection contributing to delirium.
Electrocardiogram (ECG)
  • An ECG helps identify myocardial infarction, arrhythmias, or electrolyte imbalances (e.g., hyperkalaemia) that could present with delirium.
  • Result Significance: Positive findings, such as ST-elevation or arrhythmias, indicate cardiac causes like myocardial infarction or arrhythmias. A normal ECG reduces the likelihood of a cardiac cause of delirium.
Computed Tomography (CT) Head Scan
  • A CT head scan is essential if there is suspicion of a cerebrovascular event, subdural haematoma, or other structural brain abnormalities, particularly in cases of head trauma or focal neurological signs.
  • Result Significance: A positive result may reveal stroke, haemorrhage, or a mass lesion. A negative result helps rule out structural brain causes of delirium.
Blood Cultures
  • Blood cultures should be taken if sepsis or a severe systemic infection is suspected, especially in the presence of fever and altered mental status.
  • Result Significance: Positive blood cultures indicate bacteraemia, which can lead to sepsis and delirium. Negative cultures may rule out bacteraemia or severe systemic infection.
Evidence-Based Diagnosis (EBDx)
Fever in Bacterial Infections (e.g., Sepsis)
  • Fever in the context of delirium often points towards infectious causes such as pneumonia, UTI, or sepsis, particularly in the elderly. The presence of fever increases the likelihood of infection being the cause of delirium.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 82%, Specificity: 62%, Positive Likelihood Ratio (LR+): 2.16.
  • Reference: Kumar A, et al. "Fever as a Marker for Sepsis." *Crit Care Med*, 2006.
  • Significance: A positive finding (fever) suggests an underlying infection, with sepsis being a possible cause. Absence of fever reduces, but does not eliminate, the likelihood of infection.
Disorientation (Orientation to Time, Place, Person)
  • Disorientation is a key feature of delirium, especially when associated with acute changes in cognition. Testing orientation helps differentiate delirium from other cognitive impairments like dementia.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 84%, Specificity: 85%, LR+: 5.6.
  • Reference: Inouye SK, et al. "Delirium: a symptom of underlying conditions." *N Engl J Med*, 2006.
  • Significance: A positive finding of disorientation strongly suggests delirium, especially when associated with acute onset and fluctuating course. Negative findings may suggest more chronic conditions such as dementia.
Hypoxia (Low Oxygen Saturation)
  • Hypoxia is a common contributor to delirium, especially in conditions like pneumonia, heart failure, or pulmonary embolism. Checking oxygen saturation is essential to rule out hypoxic causes of confusion.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 90%, Specificity: 75%, LR+: 3.6.
  • Reference: Ely EW, et al. "Respiratory failure and delirium." *Chest*, 2001.
  • Significance: A positive finding (hypoxia) points to respiratory or cardiovascular causes of delirium, while normal oxygen saturation reduces the likelihood of hypoxic conditions.
Abnormal Electrolytes (e.g., Hyponatraemia)
  • Electrolyte imbalances, especially hyponatraemia, are common causes of delirium. Checking U&Es can help identify or rule out metabolic causes of confusion.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 78%, Specificity: 83%, LR+: 4.6 for hyponatraemia in delirium.
  • Reference: Grossman RA, et al. "Hyponatremia and cognitive dysfunction: a systematic review." *J Am Geriatr Soc*, 2007.
  • Significance: A positive finding of hyponatraemia suggests a metabolic cause of delirium. Normal electrolyte levels make metabolic derangements less likely.
Positive Urine Culture (Urinary Tract Infection)
  • Urinary tract infections (UTIs) are common causes of delirium, particularly in elderly or immunocompromised patients. Urine cultures confirm the diagnosis of UTI.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 70%, Specificity: 90%, LR+: 7.0.
  • Reference: Ouslander JG, et al. "UTIs in the elderly." *J Am Geriatr Soc*, 2014.
  • Significance: A positive urine culture indicates a UTI as the cause of delirium. Negative results make UTI less likely but do not entirely rule it out, particularly in atypical presentations.
CT Head Scan (Suspected Stroke or Subdural Haematoma)
  • A CT scan is indicated when there is suspicion of an acute cerebrovascular event, especially if the patient presents with sudden onset confusion or focal neurological deficits.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 92% for detecting stroke or subdural haematoma, Specificity: 89%.
  • Reference: Wardlaw JM, et al. "Neuroimaging in acute stroke." *Lancet*, 2004.
  • Significance: A positive CT scan confirms the presence of stroke or intracranial bleeding as the cause of delirium. Negative results make structural brain causes less likely.
Blood Cultures (Sepsis)
  • Blood cultures are critical in identifying bacteraemia and sepsis, particularly in patients with fever and altered mental status.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 85%, Specificity: 98%, LR+: 42.5.
  • Reference: Shapiro NI, et al. "Markers of sepsis." *Am J Respir Crit Care Med*, 2009.
  • Significance: Positive blood cultures confirm sepsis as the underlying cause of delirium. Negative results reduce the likelihood of bacteraemia, but a clinical suspicion of sepsis should still be considered.
Diagnostic Timeout & Cognitive Biases

Awareness of common thinking errors is essential to improve diagnostic accuracy when assessing a patient with delirium. Before finalising a diagnosis, it is good practice for clinicians at all levels to take a 'diagnostic timeout' to challenge their own reasoning.

  • Anchoring Bias: This is the tendency to lock onto a pre-existing diagnosis, most commonly dementia. A clinician might attribute a patient's confusion to their known dementia, failing to search for a new, acute, and reversible cause. A key question to ask is: "Could this be delirium superimposed on dementia, and have I looked for a cause?"
  • Attribution Error: This involves attributing a patient's symptoms to their character or social situation rather than a medical cause. For example, dismissing an older patient's confusion as 'just old age' or 'sundowning' without performing a thorough medical workup is a common and dangerous error.
  • Premature Closure: This occurs when a clinician settles on a diagnosis too early and stops considering other possibilities. For instance, finding a positive urine dipstick might lead to a diagnosis of a UTI-induced delirium, causing the clinician to miss a concurrent pneumonia or electrolyte disturbance. Always consider that delirium is often multifactorial.
  • Confirmation Bias: Actively seeking out or overvaluing evidence that supports an initial belief while ignoring contradictory evidence. For example, focusing on a patient's recent constipation while downplaying a new oxygen requirement. It's vital to assess all data objectively.
Illustrative Management Pathways

For educational purposes, this section describes the typical principles of management for delirium. These are not prescriptive instructions. All management must be guided by official UK guidelines (e.g., NICE NG103), local formularies, and senior clinical advice.

Core Principle: Identify and Treat the Underlying Cause(s)

The cornerstone of delirium management is to find and reverse the precipitating factors. This involves a thorough review of the history, examination, and investigations to address issues identified from frameworks like 'PINCH ME'.

  • Infection: If sepsis is suspected, obtain cultures and start empirical antibiotics according to local guidelines without delay. Treat specific infections like UTIs or pneumonia as identified.
  • Medication Review: Conduct a meticulous review of all medications. Stop, switch, or reduce doses of any culprit drugs, particularly those with anticholinergic or sedative properties.
  • Metabolic Correction: Correct dehydration with intravenous or subcutaneous fluids. Normalise electrolyte disturbances and optimise blood glucose levels. Provide oxygen for hypoxia.
  • Pain and Comfort: Ensure adequate pain relief. Treat constipation with laxatives and ensure the bladder is emptying (consider a bladder scan for retention).
Supportive Care and Environmental Interventions

Non-pharmacological strategies are paramount and should be implemented for all patients with delirium.

  • Re-orientation: Frequently and calmly re-orientate the patient to time, place, and person. Use clocks, calendars, and family photos.
  • Sensory Aids: Ensure the patient has their glasses and hearing aids to reduce sensory deprivation.
  • Sleep-Wake Cycle: Promote normal sleep patterns. Maximise light and activity during the day and ensure a quiet, dark environment at night. Avoid unnecessary night-time interruptions.
  • Mobility: Encourage early mobilisation and physiotherapy where possible to maintain function and promote sleep.
  • Family Involvement: Involve family and carers to provide reassurance and familiarity for the patient.
Management of Agitation and Distress

Pharmacological sedation should only be used as a last resort, for short durations, and at the lowest possible dose when a patient's severe agitation or distress poses a risk to themselves or others, and de-escalation techniques have failed.

  • First-line (NICE): A short course of a low-dose oral antipsychotic (e.g., Haloperidol or Olanzapine) is recommended. The choice is guided by patient factors and potential side effects.
  • Contraindications: Antipsychotics should be used with extreme caution or avoided in patients with Parkinson's disease or Lewy Body Dementia due to the risk of severe sensitivity reactions. Benzodiazepines are generally avoided as they can worsen delirium, except in cases of alcohol or benzodiazepine withdrawal.
Reflection & Learning

These brief cases illustrate the application of clinical reasoning to different presentations of delirium. They are for educational purposes only.

Case 1: The Confused Resident from a Care Home

An 88-year-old woman with known Alzheimer's dementia is sent to the Emergency Department because she has been more confused and agitated for the past 24 hours. Her carers report no specific new symptoms. On examination, she is afebrile but has a tachycardia of 110 bpm. Her abdomen is soft but she has some suprapubic tenderness. A urine dipstick is positive for leukocytes and nitrites. The problem representation is an elderly woman with a known cognitive vulnerability (dementia) presenting with an acute change in behaviour (hyperactive delirium), tachycardia, and signs pointing to a urinary source. The immediate pathway involves sending a urine culture, starting empirical antibiotics for a suspected UTI, and conducting baseline blood tests (FBC, U&E, CRP) to rule out other causes and assess for sepsis.

Case 2: The Quiet Post-operative Patient

A 76-year-old man is on day 3 following a hip replacement. The nurses report that he is very quiet, withdrawn, and refusing to eat or engage with physiotherapy. He seems to be 'staring into space'. His observations are stable, and his wound looks clean. His drug chart shows he is on a regular low-dose opioid for pain. A cognitive assessment (AMTS) reveals a score of 4/10, a significant drop from his pre-operative baseline. The problem representation is an older man post-surgery and on opiates presenting with a quiet, withdrawn state, representing a hypoactive delirium. The pathway involves a thorough 'delirium screen' including blood tests to exclude infection/metabolic causes, a review of his medication to minimise opioid use with simple analgesia, and aggressive non-pharmacological management (mobilisation, re-orientation, ensuring hydration/nutrition).

Case 3: The Agitated Patient with a Complicated Drug History

A 69-year-old man is admitted with a fall. He is found to be visibly agitated, picking at his clothes and shouting at staff. His daughter reports he was started on a new medication for bladder problems (an anticholinergic) a week ago. On examination, he has a dry mouth, dilated pupils, and a palpable bladder. The problem representation is a man presenting with hyperactive delirium with prominent anticholinergic signs, temporally related to starting a new culprit medication, and with evidence of a potential precipitant (urinary retention). The immediate pathway is to stop the offending anticholinergic drug, perform a bladder scan (and catheterise if in retention), and send bloods to exclude other concurrent causes that may have been precipitated by the initial problem.
