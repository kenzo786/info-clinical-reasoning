Seizure Diagnostic Reasoning Reference
Context: Seizure Assessment
Overview

A seizure, or fit, represents a transient occurrence of signs and/or symptoms due to abnormal excessive or synchronous neuronal activity in the brain. It is a common and alarming presentation in both primary and secondary care, affecting up to 10% of the population in their lifetime. The clinical challenge is to determine the underlying cause, distinguish a true seizure from its many mimics (syncope, non-epileptic seizures), and identify patients who are at risk of recurrence or have a life-threatening underlying pathology.

A structured and systematic approach is essential. The immediate priority is to stabilise the patient and manage any ongoing seizure activity (status epilepticus), while concurrently initiating a diagnostic process to uncover the aetiology. This process must rapidly identify red flags pointing towards urgent underlying causes such as meningitis, subarachnoid haemorrhage, brain tumours, or severe metabolic disturbances. This educational module provides a foundational framework for the clinical reasoning involved in assessing a patient presenting with a seizure. For definitive, real-time management protocols, users must refer to official UK guidelines from authoritative bodies such as NICE (e.g., CG137 for Epilepsies) and the Resuscitation Council UK for the management of status epilepticus.

Pathophysiology/Basic Science

Understanding the fundamental mechanisms of seizure generation is crucial for diagnosis and management. At its core, a seizure is a manifestation of a pathological disruption in the delicate balance between neuronal excitation and inhibition within the brain.

  • Neuronal Hyperexcitability: Seizures originate from a group of neurons that become abnormally hyperexcitable. This can be due to intrinsic factors (e.g., changes in ion channel properties) or extrinsic factors (e.g., alterations in the local neuronal environment). The primary excitatory neurotransmitter in the brain is glutamate. Excessive glutamate release or increased sensitivity of its receptors (like NMDA and AMPA receptors) promotes neuronal firing.
  • Failure of Inhibition: Counterbalancing excitation is inhibition, primarily mediated by the neurotransmitter gamma-aminobutyric acid (GABA). A reduction in GABAergic function—whether through decreased GABA release, receptor dysfunction, or loss of inhibitory interneurons—can lower the seizure threshold, allowing excitatory signals to propagate unchecked.
  • Seizure Propagation: For a seizure to become clinically apparent, the abnormal electrical discharge must spread from its focal origin to involve other areas of the brain. This propagation occurs through established anatomical circuits. A seizure may remain localised (a focal seizure) or spread to involve both cerebral hemispheres, leading to a generalised seizure and loss of consciousness.
  • Underlying Aetiologies: The disruption of the excitation/inhibition balance can be caused by a wide range of pathologies:
    • Structural Abnormalities: Brain tumours, strokes, traumatic brain injury, or congenital malformations can create an epileptogenic focus by altering local circuitry and causing inflammation.
    • Metabolic/Systemic Disturbances: Severe hypoglycaemia, hyponatraemia, hypocalcaemia, hypoxia, or organ failure can globally disrupt neuronal function and provoke seizures.
    • Infections: Meningitis and encephalitis cause inflammation and irritation of brain tissue, leading to neuronal hyperexcitability.
    • Genetic Predisposition: Many epilepsies have a genetic basis, involving mutations in genes that code for ion channels, receptors, or proteins involved in synaptic transmission.
    • Toxins/Withdrawal: Certain drugs and toxins can induce seizures. Conversely, the abrupt withdrawal from substances that enhance GABAergic inhibition, such as alcohol or benzodiazepines, can lead to rebound hyperexcitability and withdrawal seizures.
Differential Diagnosis (DDx) Framework

A structured approach to the differential diagnosis of a seizure is essential to ensure life-threatening conditions are prioritised.

Must not miss!
  • Cerebrovascular Disease: A stroke or transient ischaemic attack can present with seizure-like activity due to acute disruption of brain tissue and function.
  • Subarachnoid haemorrhage: Sudden, severe headache followed by seizure activity is a classic presentation of bleeding around the brain.
  • Post concussion syndrome: Seizures can be a complication following a traumatic brain injury, indicating underlying cerebral irritation.
  • Subdural haematoma: A bleed on the surface of the brain, often following head trauma, can cause progressive neurological symptoms including seizures.
  • Brain tumour: A new-onset seizure in an adult is a significant red flag for an underlying primary or metastatic brain tumour.
  • Meningitis: Infection of the meninges causes fever, headache, neck stiffness, and can provoke seizures due to cerebral inflammation.
  • Encephalitis: Inflammation of the brain tissue itself is a serious cause of seizures, often accompanied by altered mental state and focal deficits.
  • Brain abscess: A localised collection of pus in the brain can act as an irritant focus, leading to seizures and signs of infection.
  • Arrhythmia: A cardiac arrhythmia can reduce cerebral blood flow, causing a syncopal event that may be accompanied by convulsive movements.
  • Hypoglycaemia: Critically low blood sugar is a common and reversible cause of seizures, particularly in patients with diabetes.
Common causes
  • Epilepsy: A chronic neurological disorder characterised by a predisposition to recurrent, unprovoked seizures.
  • Febrile convulsions (children): Seizures that occur in young children triggered by a high fever in the absence of a central nervous system infection.
  • Alcohol and Drug abuse: Intoxication with stimulants (like cocaine) or high doses of certain drugs can provoke seizures.
  • Alcohol and Drug withdrawal: Abrupt cessation of alcohol or sedative drugs (like benzodiazepines) is a very common trigger for seizures.
  • Vasovagal syncope: A faint caused by a sudden drop in heart rate and blood pressure, which can sometimes be followed by brief jerking movements.
  • Non-epileptic seizure: Episodes that resemble epileptic seizures but are psychological in origin and not caused by abnormal brain electrical activity.
  • COVID-19: The neurotropic effects of the virus and associated systemic inflammation can occasionally trigger seizures.
Easily missed
  • Electrolyte disturbance: Abnormal levels of sodium, calcium, or magnesium can alter neuronal excitability and cause seizures.
Other Differential Diagnoses to consider
  • Hyperthermia: Elevated body temperature can provoke seizures, especially in young children.
  • Sleep disorders (e.g., Narcolepsy): Episodes of cataplexy or sleep paralysis might be mistaken for seizure-like activity.
  • Metabolic acidosis: Conditions like diabetic ketoacidosis (DKA) can induce altered mental states and seizures.
  • Eclampsia (pregnancy): Life-threatening condition in pregnancy, characterised by hypertension and seizures.
  • Wilson’s disease: Genetic disorder causing copper accumulation, leading to neurological symptoms, including seizures.
  • Porphyria: Metabolic disorder that can cause neurological crises, including seizures.
  • Multiple sclerosis: May present with seizures as part of a broader pattern of neurological symptoms.
  • Lead poisoning: Chronic exposure to lead can cause neurological damage, including seizures.
  • Autoimmune encephalitis: Immune-mediated brain inflammation can cause seizures and behavioural changes.
History Taking
Patient’s Description: How can I help you today?
Starting with an open-ended question such as, "Can you tell me more about what brought you here today?" allows the patient to describe their symptoms in their own words, which can provide valuable insights into the nature, timing, and associated features of the presenting symptom. Relevance: This approach helps establish the patient’s concerns and creates an opportunity to tailor specific follow-up questions based on their response.
When did the event happen, and what were you doing at the time?
  • Asking about the onset and circumstances of the seizure helps determine possible triggers, such as stress, alcohol use, or sleep deprivation. Activity at the time (e.g., exertion, rest) can suggest specific causes like vasovagal syncope or arrhythmia.
  • Relevance: A sudden onset during rest could suggest epilepsy, while a seizure during exertion might raise concerns about cardiovascular causes like arrhythmias.
Did you experience any warning symptoms before the event (aura, dizziness, chest pain)?
  • Aura, such as unusual smells, visual disturbances, or a sense of déjà vu, often precedes focal seizures. Dizziness or lightheadedness could suggest vasovagal syncope or arrhythmia.
  • Relevance: A positive response may indicate epilepsy (if there is an aura), while dizziness might suggest a cardiovascular cause. Negative responses might point towards generalised seizures without warning.
What exactly happened during the event (any jerking, incontinence, tongue biting, loss of consciousness)?
  • Details about the seizure, including generalised jerking, tongue biting, and incontinence, can confirm a true epileptic seizure. Witness accounts are often helpful if the patient is unsure.
  • Relevance: Generalised tonic-clonic seizures are strongly suggestive of epilepsy. Absence of these features may point towards non-epileptic events like syncope.
How long did the event last, and how did you feel afterwards?
  • Seizure duration is critical. Typical epileptic seizures last 1-2 minutes, while prolonged events raise concern for status epilepticus. Post-ictal confusion or fatigue supports a diagnosis of epilepsy.
  • Relevance: A prolonged post-ictal phase suggests epilepsy, while rapid recovery may point to non-epileptic events like syncope.
Have you had any recent head injuries or infections?
  • Recent head trauma could indicate post-traumatic seizures, while fever and infection raise concerns about meningitis, encephalitis, or brain abscess.
  • Relevance: A positive history of head trauma may suggest post-concussion syndrome or subdural haematoma. Recent infection could indicate meningitis or encephalitis.
Do you have any personal or family history of seizures, strokes, or heart conditions?
  • Epilepsy and certain neurological conditions can run in families. A family history of stroke or heart disease may raise the suspicion of cerebrovascular causes or arrhythmias.
  • Relevance: A positive family history of epilepsy supports a diagnosis of primary epilepsy. Cardiovascular disease in the family may suggest arrhythmias or cerebrovascular disease as the cause of the event.
Any use of alcohol, recreational drugs, or recent withdrawal?
  • Alcohol and drug use or withdrawal are significant risk factors for seizures. Chronic alcohol use, in particular, can lead to withdrawal seizures.
  • Relevance: A positive history of alcohol or drug use points towards intoxication or withdrawal as likely causes. Absence of these factors directs attention to other potential causes.
Ideas, Concerns, and Expectations (ICE)
  • Asking the patient about their ideas, concerns, and expectations is crucial in understanding their perspective. Common concerns may include epilepsy, brain tumour, or stroke. Expectations may include a request for diagnostic imaging or reassurance.
  • Relevance: Understanding the patient’s concerns can guide management, including investigations and treatment options, while addressing their expectations ensures a holistic approach to care.
Examination Strategy
General Appearance and Vital Signs
  • Assess the patient's general appearance for signs of post-ictal confusion, agitation, or lethargy. Check vital signs (temperature, pulse, blood pressure, oxygen saturation).
  • Relevance: A positive finding of confusion or lethargy suggests a post-ictal state, supporting a diagnosis of epilepsy. Fever may suggest meningitis or encephalitis. Abnormal pulse or blood pressure could point towards arrhythmias or syncope as causes.
Neurological Examination: Level of Consciousness
  • Assess the level of consciousness and orientation (alert, responsive to voice or pain, unresponsive). Perform the Glasgow Coma Scale (GCS) evaluation.
  • Relevance: A reduced level of consciousness after a seizure may indicate a prolonged post-ictal state or more serious conditions like status epilepticus, subarachnoid haemorrhage, or brain tumour. A normal GCS suggests a less severe underlying cause like syncope.
Head and Neck Examination
  • Check for signs of head trauma, such as bruising, swelling, or lacerations, especially in cases of unwitnessed falls. Inspect the neck for signs of meningism (neck stiffness).
  • Relevance: Head trauma with evidence of a fall may suggest post-concussion syndrome or a subdural haematoma. Neck stiffness with fever raises concern for meningitis or encephalitis. A negative finding makes infection less likely.
Neurological Examination: Cranial Nerves
  • Assess cranial nerve function, including visual fields, facial movement, and speech. Look for asymmetry, weakness, or speech difficulties.
  • Relevance: Focal neurological deficits, such as weakness or cranial nerve palsies, may suggest a structural brain lesion, such as a stroke or brain tumour. A normal cranial nerve examination makes these causes less likely.
Cardiovascular Examination
  • Auscultate the heart for abnormal rhythms (e.g., atrial fibrillation) or murmurs. Check peripheral pulses and assess for any signs of circulatory compromise.
  • Relevance: Abnormal heart rhythms or murmurs could indicate arrhythmias as the cause of the event. A normal cardiovascular exam may help rule out cardiac causes such as syncope or arrhythmia.
Abdominal Examination
  • Check for signs of liver disease (e.g., hepatomegaly, ascites) or evidence of metabolic disturbances (e.g., tenderness in diabetic ketoacidosis).
  • Relevance: Hepatomegaly or ascites could suggest alcohol-related liver disease, which may contribute to seizures via alcohol withdrawal. Abdominal tenderness in diabetic patients may raise concerns for metabolic causes, such as hypoglycaemia or ketoacidosis.
Skin Examination
  • Look for evidence of tongue lacerations (common in generalised tonic-clonic seizures), cyanosis, or pallor. Inspect for bruises or abrasions from falls.
  • Relevance: Tongue lacerations are suggestive of an epileptic seizure. Pallor and cyanosis might point to cardiovascular or respiratory causes of syncope, while bruising may indicate trauma from a fall during the event.
Neurological Examination: Motor and Sensory Function
  • Assess muscle strength, tone, and reflexes, checking for asymmetry or abnormal movements. Test sensory function, including light touch, pain, and proprioception.
  • Relevance: Weakness or sensory loss suggests a focal neurological cause like stroke, brain tumour, or subdural haematoma. A normal motor and sensory exam makes focal brain lesions less likely.
Neurological Examination: Coordination and Gait
  • Test coordination using finger-to-nose and heel-to-shin tests. Assess the patient's gait for any unsteadiness or abnormal patterns.
  • Relevance: Ataxia or gait abnormalities can indicate cerebellar involvement, which may suggest a posterior fossa lesion or stroke. A normal gait makes cerebellar pathology less likely.
Red Flag Synthesis
Prolonged seizure lasting more than 5 minutes (status epilepticus)
  • Status epilepticus is a medical emergency where a seizure lasts more than 5 minutes or when multiple seizures occur without regaining consciousness in between.
  • Significance: This could indicate status epilepticus, which can lead to brain damage, respiratory failure, or death if not urgently treated. Immediate medical intervention is required.
Sudden onset of severe headache (thunderclap headache)
  • A sudden, intense headache described as the "worst headache of my life" may indicate subarachnoid haemorrhage, a life-threatening condition caused by bleeding in the brain.
  • Significance: This could indicate subarachnoid haemorrhage, which requires immediate imaging and neurosurgical intervention.
New focal neurological deficits (e.g., weakness, vision changes, speech difficulties)
  • The presence of new focal neurological signs after a seizure, such as weakness on one side (hemiparesis), difficulty speaking (aphasia), or visual disturbances, could suggest a structural brain lesion like a stroke or tumour.
  • Significance: This could indicate stroke, brain tumour, or intracranial haemorrhage, all of which require urgent neuroimaging and specialist management.
Fever, neck stiffness, or altered mental status
  • Fever, neck stiffness, and altered consciousness can be signs of central nervous system infection, such as meningitis or encephalitis, both of which are life-threatening if untreated.
  • Significance: This could indicate meningitis or encephalitis, which requires prompt investigation, including lumbar puncture, and initiation of antibiotics or antivirals.
Post-traumatic seizures following recent head injury
  • Seizures occurring after a head injury may indicate serious complications such as intracranial bleeding (e.g., subdural haematoma), brain contusion, or increased intracranial pressure.
  • Significance: This could indicate intracranial haemorrhage or brain injury, necessitating immediate neuroimaging to assess for bleeding or swelling.
Unexplained weight loss, persistent headaches, or progressive neurological symptoms
  • Unexplained weight loss, persistent headaches, or progressive focal neurological symptoms could be warning signs of an underlying malignancy, such as a brain tumour.
  • Significance: This could indicate a brain tumour or other cancer, requiring urgent referral for imaging and oncology consultation.
Seizures associated with cardiac symptoms (e.g., palpitations, chest pain, syncope)
  • Seizures occurring alongside cardiac symptoms like chest pain, palpitations, or syncope may point to a cardiovascular cause, such as arrhythmias, which can lead to reduced cerebral perfusion and secondary seizures.
  • Significance: This could indicate a life-threatening arrhythmia or cardiac event, requiring urgent cardiology evaluation and ECG monitoring.
Investigations Strategy & Interpretation
Electroencephalogram (EEG)
  • An EEG measures electrical activity in the brain and is the primary tool for diagnosing epilepsy. It helps identify abnormal brain wave patterns that are associated with seizures.
  • Result Significance: Positive results with abnormal discharges support a diagnosis of epilepsy. A negative EEG does not rule out epilepsy but may require repeat testing or further clinical correlation.
Brain MRI/CT scan
  • Neuroimaging, particularly MRI, is critical in identifying structural abnormalities in the brain, such as tumours, haemorrhages, or infarcts, which can cause seizures.
  • Result Significance: A positive result with evidence of a mass or haemorrhage suggests serious pathology, such as a brain tumour, stroke, or subdural haematoma. A negative scan may help rule out structural causes of seizures.
Serum electrolytes (Na+, K+, Ca2+, Mg2+)
  • Checking electrolyte levels is important, as imbalances such as hyponatraemia, hypocalcaemia, or hypomagnesaemia can provoke seizures, especially in patients with underlying metabolic disturbances.
  • Result Significance: Positive findings of low sodium, calcium, or magnesium can explain seizures due to electrolyte disturbances. Normal results help rule out metabolic causes of seizures.
Blood glucose (finger-prick or serum glucose)
  • Hypoglycaemia is a reversible cause of seizures, especially in diabetic patients or those who are fasting. It is critical to check glucose levels in all seizure patients.
  • Result Significance: A positive finding of low blood glucose suggests hypoglycaemia as the seizure trigger. Normal or high glucose levels would direct the diagnostic focus elsewhere.
Full blood count (FBC)
  • An FBC helps assess for infections or underlying haematological conditions that could contribute to seizures, such as sepsis or malignancy. It also checks for anaemia, which can cause dizziness and syncope, potentially confused with seizures.
  • Result Significance: Elevated white cell count could suggest infection (e.g., meningitis, encephalitis), while a normal FBC helps rule out infectious or haematological causes of seizures.
Toxicology screen (urine or blood)
  • Toxicology screening is essential in patients with a history of drug or alcohol use, or when intoxication or overdose is suspected as a cause of the seizure.
  • Result Significance: Positive findings for drugs or alcohol confirm intoxication as a potential trigger for seizures. A negative screen may help rule out substance-related causes.
Lumbar puncture (CSF analysis)
  • A lumbar puncture is indicated if central nervous system infection (e.g., meningitis or encephalitis) is suspected, especially in patients with fever, neck stiffness, or altered mental state.
  • Result Significance: Positive findings (elevated white cells, protein, or glucose abnormalities) suggest infection, such as meningitis or encephalitis. A normal CSF analysis helps rule out these conditions.
Electrocardiogram (ECG)
  • An ECG should be performed to assess for arrhythmias, which can cause syncope or seizures due to reduced cerebral perfusion. It is particularly important in patients with associated cardiac symptoms.
  • Result Significance: Positive findings of arrhythmias like atrial fibrillation or prolonged QT interval suggest a cardiac cause for the seizure. A normal ECG helps rule out cardiac causes.
Evidence-Based Diagnosis (EBDx)
Post-ictal confusion (symptom)
  • Post-ictal confusion is common after generalised tonic-clonic seizures and is a strong indicator of epilepsy. It typically lasts minutes to hours following a seizure episode.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 92%, Specificity: 60%, Likelihood Ratio: 2.3 for epileptic seizures compared to non-epileptic events.
  • Reference: Fisher et al., Epilepsia, 2017; NICE Clinical Guidelines for Epilepsy, 2020.
  • Significance: A positive finding of post-ictal confusion supports a diagnosis of epilepsy, while its absence makes epileptic seizures less likely, pointing towards alternative causes like syncope or psychogenic non-epileptic seizures (PNES).
Tongue biting (sign)
  • Tongue biting, especially on the lateral sides, is a significant finding during generalised seizures. It is considered highly suggestive of epileptic seizures.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 24%, Specificity: 99%, Likelihood Ratio: 24.0.
  • Reference: Benbadis et al., Neurology, 2000; NICE Guidelines for Epilepsy, 2020.
  • Significance: Positive findings strongly indicate epilepsy, while the absence of tongue biting does not rule out epilepsy, but makes other causes like PNES or syncope more likely.
EEG showing interictal epileptiform discharges (investigation)
  • An EEG demonstrating interictal epileptiform discharges is a key diagnostic tool for epilepsy. However, normal EEG results do not exclude epilepsy, especially if the seizure activity is infrequent.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 50%, Specificity: 90%, Likelihood Ratio: 5.0.
  • Reference: NICE Epilepsy Guidelines, 2020; Smith et al., Neurology, 2016.
  • Significance: A positive EEG strongly suggests epilepsy, while a negative EEG requires further clinical correlation as it may miss episodic epileptiform activity.
MRI revealing brain tumour (investigation)
  • An MRI can identify structural causes of seizures, such as brain tumours, which are often associated with focal seizures and other neurological deficits.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 96%, Specificity: 93%, Likelihood Ratio: 13.7.
  • Reference: Garcia et al., The Lancet Neurology, 2019; NICE Imaging Guidelines, 2021.
  • Significance: A positive MRI revealing a brain tumour would confirm a structural cause of seizures, necessitating urgent oncological and neurosurgical intervention.
Fever and neck stiffness with abnormal CSF findings (investigation)
  • Fever, neck stiffness, and altered mental status, along with abnormal CSF (increased white cells or protein), are highly suggestive of CNS infections like meningitis or encephalitis, which can cause seizures.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 98%, Specificity: 85%, Likelihood Ratio: 6.5 for CNS infection causing seizures.
  • Reference: Solomon et al., Infectious Diseases in Clinical Practice, 2018; NICE Meningitis Guidelines, 2019.
  • Significance: Positive findings suggest CNS infection as the cause of seizures, requiring immediate treatment with antibiotics or antivirals. A negative CSF result reduces the likelihood of infection but may still warrant further investigation for alternative causes.
Serum sodium levels (investigation)
  • Hyponatraemia is a well-known cause of seizures, especially in hospitalised patients or those with chronic illnesses.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 75%, Specificity: 80%, Likelihood Ratio: 3.75.
  • Reference: Whelan et al., Journal of Neurology, 2017.
  • Significance: Low serum sodium levels suggest hyponatraemia as a potential trigger for seizures. Normal sodium levels help exclude electrolyte disturbances as a cause.
Diagnostic Timeout & Cognitive Biases

Awareness of common thinking errors is essential to improve diagnostic accuracy and patient safety when assessing a patient following a seizure. Before finalising a diagnosis, it is good practice for clinicians at all levels to take a 'diagnostic timeout' to challenge their own reasoning.

  • Anchoring Bias: The tendency to prematurely lock onto a diagnosis based on initial information. For example, attributing a seizure in a patient with known epilepsy to their existing condition without fully considering new, serious pathologies like a stroke, tumour, or infection. A key question to ask is: "Could there be a new, superimposed reason for this seizure?"
  • Confirmation Bias: Selectively focusing on evidence that supports an initial hypothesis while downplaying contradictory information. For instance, focusing on a history of stress to diagnose a non-epileptic seizure while ignoring red flags like a new focal neurological deficit or tongue biting.
  • Premature Closure: Accepting a diagnosis before it has been fully verified. A common pitfall is diagnosing a "simple faint" (vasovagal syncope) in an older person without thoroughly excluding a cardiac arrhythmia, which can present with convulsive syncope and has much more serious implications.
  • Availability Heuristic: Overestimating the likelihood of a diagnosis because it is more recent or memorable in the clinician's experience. For example, having recently managed a case of alcohol withdrawal seizure might lead a clinician to over-diagnose this condition while potentially missing hypoglycaemia in a subsequent patient. A crucial reflective step is to ask: "What are the common mimics, and have I considered them systematically?"
Illustrative Management Pathways

For educational purposes, this section describes the typical principles of management for key diagnoses. These are not prescriptive instructions. All management must be guided by official UK guidelines, local formularies, and senior clinical advice.

Status Epilepticus

This is a medical emergency defined as a seizure lasting longer than 5 minutes, or more than one seizure within a 5-minute period without the person returning to normal between them. Management follows an ABCDE approach. Immediate treatment involves benzodiazepines (e.g., intravenous lorazepam or buccal midazolam) to terminate the seizure. If this fails, second-line anti-epileptic drugs (e.g., levetiracetam, phenytoin, or sodium valproate) are administered in a hospital setting with appropriate monitoring. The underlying cause must be aggressively sought and treated.

First Unprovoked Seizure

After a first seizure, the priority is to identify and treat any acute underlying cause. All patients should be referred urgently to a specialist for assessment, usually a neurologist or epilepsy specialist. Key investigations include blood tests (for metabolic causes), an ECG (to exclude cardiac causes), and neuroimaging (usually an MRI) to look for structural brain abnormalities. An EEG is also crucial to classify the seizure type and assess the risk of recurrence. The decision to start long-term anti-epileptic drug treatment is complex and is made by the specialist in discussion with the patient, weighing the risk of recurrence against the side effects of medication.

Alcohol Withdrawal Seizure

Seizures are a severe manifestation of alcohol withdrawal syndrome. Management involves immediate supportive care and treatment with a benzodiazepine, such as chlordiazepoxide or diazepam, using a dose-tapering regimen guided by local protocols. Intravenous high-potency B vitamins (Pabrinex) are essential to prevent Wernicke's encephalopathy. It is critical to exclude other causes for the seizure, as patients with alcohol dependence are also at higher risk of head injury and metabolic disturbances.

Seizure Mimics (e.g., Syncope, Non-Epileptic Seizures)

Once life-threatening causes have been excluded, management depends on the specific diagnosis. For vasovagal syncope, this involves reassurance, education on trigger avoidance, and counter-pressure manoeuvres. For suspected cardiac syncope, urgent cardiology referral for further investigation (e.g., 24-hour ECG, echocardiogram) is necessary. Patients diagnosed with psychogenic non-epileptic seizures (PNES) require a sensitive and clear explanation of the diagnosis and referral for psychological therapies, such as cognitive behavioural therapy (CBT).

Reflection & Learning

These brief cases illustrate the application of clinical reasoning to different seizure presentations. They are for educational purposes only.

Case 1: First Seizure in a Young Adult

A 22-year-old university student is brought to the Emergency Department after having a witnessed tonic-clonic seizure in the library. His friends report about one minute of generalised shaking. Afterwards, he was confused for 20 minutes. He has no significant past medical history and denies alcohol or drug use. The problem representation is a young adult with a first, unprovoked, generalised seizure with a typical post-ictal phase. The immediate pathway involves excluding acute metabolic or toxic causes, performing an ECG and basic blood tests, and referring to a First Seizure Clinic for specialist assessment including MRI and EEG to determine the risk of recurrence and the need for treatment.

Case 2: New Onset Seizure in an Older Adult

A 68-year-old woman with a history of breast cancer presents after an episode where her right arm started jerking uncontrollably for two minutes. She remained aware throughout the event. Her examination reveals a subtle weakness in her right hand grip. The problem representation is an older adult with a significant past medical history presenting with a new-onset focal motor seizure, creating a high suspicion for a structural brain lesion, likely a metastasis. The immediate pathway requires urgent neuroimaging (MRI with contrast is preferred), neurology consultation, and consideration of starting an anti-epileptic drug to prevent further seizures.

Case 3: Seizure in a Patient with Known Alcohol Dependence

A 45-year-old man with a known history of heavy alcohol use is found fitting by his neighbour. In the ambulance, he has another generalised seizure. On arrival, he is post-ictal and smells of alcohol. His blood glucose is normal. The problem representation is a man with known alcohol dependence presenting with recurrent seizures, highly suggestive of alcohol withdrawal, possibly progressing to status epilepticus. The management priority is immediate seizure termination with benzodiazepines, administration of IV Pabrinex, and admission for a medically supervised alcohol withdrawal regimen, while remaining vigilant for other potential causes like a traumatic intracranial bleed from an unwitnessed fall.
