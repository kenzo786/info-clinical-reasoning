Fever in Adults Diagnostic Reasoning Reference
Context: Fever in Adults Assessment
Overview

Fever in adults is one of the most frequent presenting complaints in all fields of medicine, from general practice to the intensive care unit. It represents a systemic response to a wide array of potential insults, ranging from minor, self-limiting viral infections to life-threatening emergencies like sepsis, meningitis, or pulmonary embolism. The fundamental challenge for the clinician is to efficiently and safely distinguish the benign from the critical.

A structured, systematic approach is therefore indispensable. This process must prioritise the rapid identification of 'red flag' symptoms and signs that indicate haemodynamic instability or a serious underlying pathology requiring immediate intervention. This educational module provides a framework for the clinical reasoning process in assessing an adult with fever. It is designed to build foundational knowledge. For definitive, real-time management protocols, clinicians must always refer to current official UK guidelines, such as those from NICE (e.g., NG51 for Sepsis) and the Resuscitation Council UK.

Pathophysiology/Basic Science

Fever, or pyrexia, is a regulated elevation of the body's core temperature, orchestrated by the central nervous system. Understanding its mechanism is key to interpreting the clinical signs.

  • Pyrogens and the Hypothalamic Set-Point: Fever is initiated by substances called pyrogens. These can be exogenous (originating outside the body, such as bacterial lipopolysaccharide) or endogenous (produced by the body's own immune cells, such as cytokines like Interleukin-1, Interleukin-6, and TNF-alpha). These pyrogens cross the blood-brain barrier and stimulate the hypothalamus to produce prostaglandins (specifically PGE2). Prostaglandins then act to 'reset' the body's thermoregulatory set-point to a higher temperature.
  • Heat Generation and Conservation: Once the set-point is raised, the body perceives its normal temperature as being too cold. It initiates mechanisms to generate and conserve heat to reach this new, higher target. This involves peripheral vasoconstriction (to reduce heat loss from the skin) and shivering (involuntary muscle contractions that generate heat). This phase corresponds to the clinical experience of feeling cold and having chills or rigors at the onset of a fever.
  • The Febrile State: The body maintains the elevated temperature as long as pyrogens are present, which is thought to enhance immune function and inhibit the replication of some pathogens.
  • Defervescence (Fever 'Breaking'): When the underlying stimulus is removed (e.g., the infection is controlled), pyrogen levels fall. The hypothalamic set-point returns to normal. The body now perceives itself as too hot and activates heat-loss mechanisms, such as peripheral vasodilation (flushing) and sweating (diaphoresis). This phase is often referred to as the fever 'breaking'.
Differential Diagnosis (DDx) Framework

A structured approach to the differential diagnosis of fever is essential to ensure life-threatening conditions are prioritised. The mnemonic "I-VINDICATE" (Infectious, Vascular, Inflammatory/Autoimmune, Neoplastic, Degenerative, Iatrogenic/Intoxication, Congenital, Allergic/Anatomic, Traumatic, Endocrine) can be a useful framework.

Must not miss!
  • Infective endocarditis: Presents with fever, new heart murmur, and peripheral stigmata in a patient who may have a history of IV drug use or prosthetic heart valves.
  • Pelvic Inflammatory Disease: Characterised by fever, lower abdominal pain, and cervical motion tenderness in sexually active women.
  • Tuberculosis: Often presents with chronic low-grade fever, night sweats, weight loss, and a persistent cough which may be productive of blood.
  • HIV: An acute seroconversion illness can present as a flu-like illness with fever, rash, and lymphadenopathy.
  • Osteomyelitis: Presents with focal bone pain, localised tenderness, and systemic signs of infection like fever.
  • Tropical diseases: Fever in a returned traveller, which can have specific patterns (e.g., cyclical fever in malaria).
  • Lymphoma: Characterised by "B symptoms": fever, drenching night sweats, and unexplained weight loss, often with lymphadenopathy.
  • Leukaemia: Presents with fever due to neutropenia and infection, along with signs of bone marrow failure like fatigue (anaemia) and bleeding (thrombocytopenia).
  • Lung cancer: May present with fever due to post-obstructive pneumonia, persistent cough, haemoptysis, or weight loss.
  • Renal carcinoma: Can present as part of a paraneoplastic syndrome with fever, loin pain, and haematuria.
  • Colorectal cancer: May cause fever if the tumour perforates or causes an intra-abdominal abscess.
  • Pancreatic Cancer: Can present with fever, often associated with jaundice, weight loss, and abdominal pain.
  • Pulmonary Embolism: Characterised by a sudden onset of pleuritic chest pain and shortness of breath, sometimes with a low-grade fever.
  • DVT: Presents with unilateral leg swelling, tenderness, and erythema, and can be a source of low-grade fever.
  • Myocardial Infarction: A low-grade fever can develop 24-48 hours after an MI due to inflammatory necrosis of heart muscle.
  • Bowel Infarction (Mesenteric ischaemia): Presents with severe abdominal pain that is out of proportion to clinical signs, often with fever and signs of sepsis.
  • Crohn's disease: An inflammatory bowel disease that can present with fever, abdominal pain, diarrhoea, and weight loss.
  • Ulcerative colitis: A flare can cause fever along with bloody diarrhoea and abdominal cramping.
  • Pancreatitis: Presents with severe epigastric pain radiating to the back, nausea, vomiting, and fever.
  • Appendicitis: Characterised by fever and migrating abdominal pain, localising to the right iliac fossa.
Common causes
  • URTI (Common cold): A viral infection of the upper respiratory tract causing low-grade fever, coryza, and sore throat.
  • LRTI: An infection of the lower respiratory tract (pneumonia or bronchitis) presenting with fever, cough, and shortness of breath.
  • UTI: A urinary tract infection presenting with fever, dysuria, and urinary frequency.
  • Cellulitis: A bacterial skin infection causing a localised area of erythema, warmth, swelling, and systemic fever.
  • Boils: A localised skin abscess (furuncle) that can be associated with fever if large or spreading.
  • Post-operative infection: Fever developing after surgery, which may originate from the surgical site, lungs, urinary tract, or IV lines.
  • Medically Unexplained Symptoms: Persistent fever for which no specific organic cause is found after thorough investigation.
  • COVID-19: A viral infection presenting with a wide range of symptoms, characteristically including fever, cough, fatigue, and anosmia.
Easily missed
  • Connective tissue diseases: Conditions like Still's disease can present with high spiking fevers, rash, and arthritis.
  • Polymyalgia rheumatica (GCA): Giant Cell Arteritis can cause fever, headache, and jaw claudication in older adults, often with a markedly raised ESR.
  • Rheumatoid arthritis: An active flare of this autoimmune joint disease can be associated with low-grade fever and malaise.
  • Systemic Lupus Erythematosus: A multi-system autoimmune disease that commonly presents with fever, fatigue, rash, and joint pain.
  • Hyperthyroidism: A thyroid storm is a life-threatening presentation with high fever, tachycardia, and altered mental status.
  • Sarcoidosis: A multi-system granulomatous disorder that can present with fever, cough, and lymphadenopathy.
Other Differential Diagnoses to consider
  • COVID-19: Fever, cough, anosmia, and fatigue, with or without respiratory distress.
  • Autoimmune thyroiditis: Fever with neck pain, fatigue, and thyroid dysfunction.
  • Drug fever: Fever temporally associated with new medications, without infectious source.
  • Factitious fever: Fever fabricated by the patient, often with inconsistent clinical findings.
  • Endocrine causes (e.g., adrenal insufficiency): Fever, fatigue, weight loss, and hypotension.
  • Haemophagocytic lymphohistiocytosis (HLH): Fever, hepatosplenomegaly, pancytopenia, and high ferritin levels.
  • Brucellosis: Undulant fever with sweats, fatigue, and joint pain, often in those with livestock exposure.
History Taking
Patient’s Description: How can I help you today?
Opening with an open-ended question, such as "Can you tell me more about what brought you here today?" allows the patient to describe their symptoms in their own words, offering a natural starting point for history taking. Relevance: Enables the clinician to gain insight into the patient’s perspective and begin targeting further questions effectively.
When did the fever start, and how has it progressed?
  • This helps establish the onset, duration, and progression of the fever.
  • Relevance: Acute fever suggests infections like URTI, LRTI, or UTI. Subacute or chronic fever raises suspicion for tuberculosis, endocarditis, or malignancies.
Have you noticed any specific patterns to the fever?
  • Inquiring about diurnal variations or relapsing patterns provides clues about underlying conditions.
  • Relevance: Malaria often presents with cyclical fever, whereas undulant fever suggests brucellosis.
Have you experienced any chills, rigours, or night sweats?
  • Explores associated systemic features of infection or malignancy.
  • Relevance: Positive response suggests sepsis, endocarditis, or lymphoma. Negative response lowers suspicion for these conditions.
Have you recently travelled or had exposure to animals or ill contacts?
  • Identifies potential exposures to infectious diseases.
  • Relevance: Travel to endemic areas raises suspicion for malaria, dengue, or typhoid. Contact with animals suggests zoonotic infections like brucellosis.
Associated Symptoms Questions
  • Cough, chest pain, or breathlessness?: Positive response suggests LRTI, COVID-19, or PE.
  • Dysuria or flank pain?: Positive response points to UTI or pyelonephritis.
  • Abdominal pain or diarrhoea?: Suggestive of gastrointestinal infection, appendicitis, or inflammatory bowel disease.
  • Skin changes or swelling?: Points to cellulitis, DVT, or systemic lupus erythematosus.
Do you have any significant past medical conditions?
  • Gathers information about chronic conditions affecting the immune system or predisposing to infections.
  • Relevance: Diabetes predisposes to infections; a history of autoimmune disease suggests connective tissue disorders.
Ideas, Concerns, and Expectations (ICE)
  • Ideas: "What do you think might be causing this?"
  • Concerns: "Is there something specific you are worried about?"
  • Expectations: "What were you hoping we might be able to do for you today?"
  • Relevance: Ensures the consultation addresses the patient’s perspective, identifies hidden worries (e.g., cancer or serious infection), and aligns with their expectations for the consultation.
Examination Strategy
General Appearance
  • Observe the patient’s general appearance for signs of distress, pallor, cachexia, or jaundice.
  • Relevance: Pallor may suggest anaemia from chronic disease or malignancy. Cachexia raises concern for malignancy or advanced infection like tuberculosis. Jaundice may point to liver disease or haemolysis.
Vital Signs
  • Measure temperature, pulse, respiratory rate, oxygen saturation, and blood pressure.
  • Relevance: Tachycardia and hypotension suggest sepsis or hypovolaemia. Pyrexia confirms fever. Tachypnoea with low oxygen saturations may indicate LRTI or PE.
Skin Examination
  • Inspect for rashes, petechiae, ecchymosis, erythema, or signs of cellulitis.
  • Relevance: Rash with fever suggests viral exanthems or conditions like meningococcal septicaemia. Localised erythema or swelling points to cellulitis or DVT.
Head and Neck Examination
  • Check for lymphadenopathy, tonsillar exudates, or signs of meningism (neck stiffness).
  • Relevance: Lymphadenopathy may indicate lymphoma, TB, or viral infections. Tonsillar exudates suggest pharyngitis or EBV infection. Meningism raises concern for meningitis.
Chest Examination
  • Perform auscultation for crepitations, wheezes, or diminished breath sounds.
  • Relevance: Crepitations suggest pneumonia. Wheezing indicates bronchospasm (e.g., in asthma or viral infections). Diminished sounds may indicate pleural effusion or lung collapse.
Cardiovascular Examination
  • Auscultate for murmurs, assess for splinter haemorrhages, Janeway lesions, or Roth spots.
  • Relevance: Murmurs with fever suggest infective endocarditis. Peripheral stigmata of endocarditis, such as splinter haemorrhages, indicate embolic phenomena.
Abdominal Examination
  • Inspect and palpate for tenderness, hepatosplenomegaly, or masses.
  • Relevance: Hepatosplenomegaly is seen in infections (e.g., EBV) or haematological malignancies. Right lower quadrant tenderness suggests appendicitis.
Musculoskeletal Examination
  • Assess joints for swelling, erythema, or tenderness.
  • Relevance: Joint swelling may indicate septic arthritis or connective tissue disease. Generalised pain suggests polymyalgia rheumatica.
Neurological Examination
  • Perform a focused neurological examination if indicated (e.g., altered mental status or meningism).
  • Relevance: Altered mental state may suggest encephalitis or sepsis. Focal neurological signs raise concern for a space-occupying lesion or stroke.
Red Flag Synthesis
Persistently high fever (>40°C) or prolonged fever (>7 days)
  • Such presentations may indicate a serious underlying condition, such as sepsis, tuberculosis, or malignancy.
  • Significance: Persistently high or prolonged fever could signify conditions like infective endocarditis, typhoid fever, or haematological malignancies, requiring urgent investigation and management.
Signs of sepsis or septic shock
  • Includes hypotension, tachycardia, altered mental status, or reduced urine output.
  • Significance: These findings indicate systemic inflammatory response syndrome (SIRS) progressing to sepsis or septic shock, which is life-threatening and necessitates immediate intervention.
Neck stiffness, photophobia, or altered mental status
  • These symptoms are strongly suggestive of meningitis or encephalitis.
  • Significance: These are emergencies that require rapid evaluation, lumbar puncture, and initiation of antimicrobial therapy.
New onset of a non-blanching rash
  • This is a hallmark of meningococcal septicaemia.
  • Significance: Non-blanching rash with fever is a medical emergency requiring urgent antibiotics to prevent life-threatening complications.
Unexplained weight loss, night sweats, or lymphadenopathy
  • Suggestive of chronic infections (e.g., tuberculosis) or malignancies such as lymphoma.
  • Significance: These features raise suspicion for systemic disease or malignancy, warranting thorough investigation including imaging and biopsy if needed.
Severe abdominal pain with fever
  • May indicate acute abdomen conditions such as appendicitis, pancreatitis, or mesenteric ischaemia.
  • Significance: This combination is suggestive of surgical emergencies that require immediate intervention.
Persistent cough, haemoptysis, or dyspnoea
  • Could suggest pulmonary infections, malignancies, or pulmonary embolism.
  • Significance: These findings warrant urgent imaging and further diagnostic testing to evaluate for serious pulmonary conditions.
Severe headache with visual disturbances or temporal tenderness
  • Highly concerning for giant cell arteritis, especially in patients over 50 years of age.
  • Significance: Immediate treatment with corticosteroids is required to prevent vision loss.
Investigations Strategy & Interpretation
Full Blood Count (FBC)
  • Assesses for leucocytosis, anaemia, or thrombocytopenia, which can provide clues to infection, malignancy, or systemic disease.
  • Result Significance: Leucocytosis suggests bacterial infections or inflammation. Anaemia and thrombocytopenia may indicate haematological malignancies or systemic infections like dengue or malaria.
C-Reactive Protein (CRP) and Erythrocyte Sedimentation Rate (ESR)
  • Helps identify the presence and degree of inflammation or infection.
  • Result Significance: Elevated CRP or ESR suggests active inflammation or infection. Persistently high levels may indicate chronic conditions like tuberculosis or autoimmune diseases.
Blood Cultures
  • Identifies bacteraemia or specific pathogens causing systemic infections.
  • Result Significance: Positive cultures confirm sepsis or infective endocarditis. Negative results do not rule out localised infections.
Urinalysis and Urine Culture
  • Evaluates for urinary tract infections or systemic diseases with renal involvement.
  • Result Significance: Presence of leukocytes, nitrites, or bacteria supports a UTI diagnosis. Proteinuria or haematuria suggests glomerular disease or systemic disorders.
Chest X-Ray (CXR)
  • Assesses for pulmonary infections, malignancies, or other thoracic pathology.
  • Result Significance: Consolidation suggests pneumonia, while cavitation may indicate tuberculosis. Normal CXR helps rule out significant pulmonary conditions.
Liver Function Tests (LFTs)
  • Evaluates for liver involvement or systemic diseases affecting hepatic function.
  • Result Significance: Elevated transaminases suggest viral hepatitis or drug-induced liver injury. Abnormal bilirubin or alkaline phosphatase levels may indicate biliary obstruction or cholestasis.
Serological Tests (e.g., HIV, EBV, dengue, malaria)
  • Specific tests identify underlying viral, parasitic, or other infectious causes of fever.
  • Result Significance: Positive serologies confirm specific diagnoses like HIV, EBV, dengue, or malaria. Negative results narrow the differential.
Lumbar Puncture
  • Performed if meningitis or encephalitis is suspected based on symptoms or examination.
  • Result Significance: Abnormal cerebrospinal fluid (e.g., elevated WBCs or protein) confirms CNS infection. Normal findings can help rule out meningitis.
Abdominal Ultrasound or CT
  • Used to investigate abdominal pain, organomegaly, or suspected intra-abdominal infections.
  • Result Significance: Findings of abscess, appendicitis, or hepatosplenomegaly confirm specific intra-abdominal pathologies. Normal imaging helps exclude these causes.
Evidence-Based Diagnosis (EBDx)
Fever in bacterial infections
  • Fever is a hallmark symptom of bacterial infections such as sepsis, pneumonia, or pyelonephritis. However, it is nonspecific and requires context.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 80%, Specificity: 60%, Positive Likelihood Ratio (LR+): 2.0.
  • Reference: NICE Guidelines for Sepsis (NG51), 2022.
  • Significance: A positive finding of fever alone is insufficient for diagnosis but highlights the need for further evaluation of bacterial causes.
Leucocytosis in bacterial infections
  • Elevated white cell count is strongly associated with bacterial infections and systemic inflammatory responses.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 85%, Specificity: 70%, LR+: 2.8.
  • Reference: Kumar et al., "Diagnostic Accuracy of FBC in Infections", JAMA, 2019.
  • Significance: A high WBC count supports bacterial causes such as pneumonia or pyelonephritis but is not diagnostic in isolation.
Elevated CRP in infections
  • CRP levels rise in response to acute inflammation and are particularly elevated in bacterial infections.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 90%, Specificity: 75%, LR+: 3.6 for CRP > 100 mg/L.
  • Reference: NICE Clinical Knowledge Summary on CRP Use, 2023.
  • Significance: Elevated CRP helps differentiate bacterial from viral infections but should be interpreted alongside clinical findings.
Blood cultures in sepsis
  • Positive blood cultures confirm bacteraemia and the presence of systemic infection.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 50%, Specificity: 98%, LR+: 25.0 for positive cultures.
  • Reference: Surviving Sepsis Campaign Guidelines, 2021.
  • Significance: Positive cultures are diagnostic of sepsis or endocarditis, while negative results do not rule out localised infection.
Chest X-Ray findings in pneumonia
  • Consolidation on a CXR strongly suggests bacterial pneumonia as the cause of fever.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 85%, Specificity: 90%, LR+: 8.5 for consolidation.
  • Reference: British Thoracic Society Guidelines for Community-Acquired Pneumonia, 2019.
  • Significance: A positive finding of consolidation confirms pneumonia, while a normal CXR makes this diagnosis less likely.
Non-blanching rash in meningococcal septicaemia
  • A petechial or purpuric rash is a specific finding in meningococcal disease.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 55%, Specificity: 95%, LR+: 11.0.
  • Reference: NICE Guidelines on Meningitis (NG102), 2022.
  • Significance: A non-blanching rash confirms meningococcal septicaemia, requiring urgent antibiotic therapy.
Diagnostic Timeout & Cognitive Biases

Awareness of common thinking errors is essential to improve diagnostic accuracy and patient safety when assessing a patient with fever. Before finalising a diagnosis, it is good practice for clinicians at all levels to take a 'diagnostic timeout' to challenge their own reasoning.

  • Anchoring Bias: This is the tendency to lock onto the first piece of information offered. For example, anchoring on a positive urine dipstick in an elderly confused patient, and failing to consider a concurrent, more serious diagnosis like pneumonia, especially if respiratory signs are present. A key question to ask is: "If it wasn't a UTI, what else could it be?"
  • Confirmation Bias: Actively seeking out or overvaluing evidence that supports an initial belief while ignoring contradictory data. For instance, focusing on a patient's recent cough to confirm a simple chest infection while downplaying the significance of their drenching night sweats and weight loss, which might suggest TB or lymphoma.
  • Availability Heuristic: Overestimating the likelihood of diagnoses that are recent or memorable. During influenza season, there's a risk of attributing all fevers to the flu ('Flu-attribution bias'), potentially delaying the diagnosis of bacterial superinfection or other serious causes of fever.
  • Premature Closure: This is accepting a diagnosis before it has been fully verified. For example, discharging a patient with a presumed simple viral illness without adequate safety netting, only for them to return later with signs of meningitis or sepsis. A crucial question is: "What is the single worst outcome I need to make sure I've excluded?"
Illustrative Management Pathways

For educational purposes, this section describes the typical principles of management for key diagnoses. These are not prescriptive instructions. All management must be guided by official UK guidelines, local formularies, and senior clinical advice.

Sepsis

Recognising sepsis is a medical emergency. Management is guided by a time-critical care bundle, often the 'Sepsis Six' in the UK. The immediate goals are to identify the source, restore tissue perfusion, and start antimicrobial therapy. This involves giving high-flow oxygen, taking blood cultures (and other relevant samples), administering broad-spectrum intravenous antibiotics, starting intravenous fluid resuscitation, measuring lactate, and monitoring urine output. This requires urgent senior clinician involvement and often escalation to a high-dependency or intensive care setting.

Community Acquired Pneumonia (CAP)

Once CAP is diagnosed, management is stratified by severity, typically using a validated score like CURB-65. Low-severity patients (score 0-1) may be managed in the community with oral antibiotics. Moderate severity (score 2) usually requires hospital admission for intravenous antibiotics and supportive care. High-severity patients (score 3 or more) require urgent hospital admission, senior review, and consideration of critical care, as they are at high risk of mortality. Antibiotic choice is guided by local and national guidelines.

Meningitis

Suspected bacterial meningitis is a time-critical emergency. If meningococcal disease is suspected in the pre-hospital setting, a stat dose of Benzylpenicillin may be given (if not allergic). In hospital, blood cultures and a lumbar puncture should be performed urgently (if no contraindications), but antibiotic administration must not be delayed. Empirical broad-spectrum IV antibiotics that cross the blood-brain barrier are started immediately. Management requires senior input and is usually in a high-dependency setting.

Pyrexia of Unknown Origin (PUO)

PUO is defined as a fever >38.3°C lasting for >3 weeks with no clear diagnosis after initial investigations. Management is not about immediate empirical treatment but a systematic, structured investigation to uncover the underlying cause, which commonly falls into one of three categories: infection (e.g., abscess, TB, endocarditis), malignancy (e.g., lymphoma), or autoimmune/inflammatory conditions (e.g., Adult-onset Still's disease, GCA). This is a specialist investigation pathway often coordinated by Infectious Diseases or Acute Medicine teams.

Reflection & Learning

These brief cases illustrate the application of clinical reasoning to different fever presentations. They are for educational purposes only.

Case 1: Suspected Sepsis in an Elderly Patient

An 82-year-old woman is brought from her nursing home with a 1-day history of increased confusion and lethargy. She has a temperature of 38.5°C, a heart rate of 110 bpm, a respiratory rate of 24, and a blood pressure of 95/60 mmHg. Her urine is noted to be dark and offensive-smelling. The problem representation is an elderly, frail patient with new-onset delirium and vital signs consistent with sepsis (high NEWS2 score), with a likely urinary source. The immediate pathway is emergency hospital admission and activation of the 'Sepsis Six' protocol, including IV fluids and broad-spectrum antibiotics, without delay.

Case 2: Fever in a Returned Traveller

A 24-year-old man presents to his GP with a 4-day history of high fevers, severe headaches, and muscle aches. He mentions the fevers seem to come and go every other day, and he feels exhausted. On questioning, he returned from a backpacking trip to West Africa 10 days ago. The problem representation is a young adult with a cyclical fever pattern and constitutional symptoms following travel to a malaria-endemic area. This is highly suspicious for malaria until proven otherwise. The GP arranges for immediate hospital admission for urgent malaria blood films and further assessment.

Case 3: Subacute Fever and Weight Loss

A 55-year-old man presents to a walk-in centre with a 6-week history of feeling generally unwell with low-grade fevers, drenching night sweats that require him to change his bedsheets, and an unintentional weight loss of 5kg. On examination, he has palpable, non-tender lymph nodes in his neck and axilla. The problem representation is a middle-aged man with classical "B symptoms" (fever, night sweats, weight loss) and lymphadenopathy, which is highly concerning for a haematological malignancy like lymphoma. Management involves an urgent referral to haematology via the 2-week-wait cancer pathway for further investigation, including blood tests, imaging, and a lymph node biopsy.
