Leg Swelling Diagnostic Reasoning Reference
Context: Leg Swelling Assessment
Overview

Leg swelling, or peripheral oedema, is a frequent and important clinical presentation in both primary and secondary care. It can range from benign, physiological conditions like gravitational oedema to manifestations of life-threatening systemic diseases such as heart failure, deep vein thrombosis (DVT), or nephrotic syndrome. The clinician's primary challenge is to differentiate localised, unilateral causes from systemic, bilateral ones, and to identify 'red flag' features that signify an urgent underlying diagnosis.

A structured diagnostic approach, based on a careful history and examination, is essential to formulate an appropriate differential diagnosis and investigation plan. This systematic process ensures that critical conditions are promptly identified and managed, while avoiding unnecessary tests for more benign causes. This educational module provides a foundational framework for the clinical reasoning required to assess a patient with leg swelling. For definitive, real-time management protocols, users must refer to official UK guidelines from authoritative bodies such as NICE (e.g., CG168 for Venous Thromboembolic Diseases) and local NHS trust pathways for conditions like heart failure and cellulitis.

Pathophysiology/Basic Science

Understanding the mechanisms that lead to leg swelling is fundamental to diagnosis. Oedema is the accumulation of excess fluid in the interstitial space, governed by the balance of forces at the capillary level (Starling's principle). The key pathophysiological mechanisms include:

  • Increased Hydrostatic Pressure: This occurs when venous pressure is elevated, forcing fluid out of the capillaries. In Deep Vein Thrombosis (DVT), a clot obstructs venous outflow, acutely raising pressure in the affected leg. In Chronic Venous Insufficiency, incompetent venous valves lead to chronic elevation of venous pressure. In Congestive Heart Failure, the heart's inability to pump blood effectively leads to systemic venous congestion and raised hydrostatic pressure in both legs.
  • Reduced Oncotic Pressure: Plasma proteins, particularly albumin, create oncotic pressure that holds fluid within the capillaries. When albumin levels are low (hypoalbuminaemia), this pressure drops, and fluid leaks into the interstitial space. This is the primary mechanism in Nephrotic Syndrome (where large amounts of albumin are lost via the kidneys), Liver Cirrhosis (due to reduced albumin synthesis), and severe Malnutrition.
  • Increased Capillary Permeability: Inflammation or infection can damage the capillary walls, making them 'leaky' and allowing fluid and proteins to escape into the surrounding tissue. This is the hallmark of Cellulitis, a bacterial skin infection, and can also be seen in inflammatory conditions or allergic reactions like angioedema.
  • Lymphatic Obstruction (Lymphoedema): The lymphatic system is responsible for draining excess interstitial fluid. If this system is blocked or damaged, fluid accumulates, leading to a protein-rich swelling called lymphoedema. This can be primary (congenital) or secondary to causes like surgery (e.g., lymph node removal for cancer), radiation, infection (e.g., filariasis), or malignant infiltration of lymph nodes. Unlike other forms of oedema, lymphoedema is typically firm and non-pitting.
Differential Diagnosis (DDx) Framework

A structured approach to the differential diagnosis of leg swelling is essential to ensure life-threatening conditions are prioritised.

Must not miss!
  • DVT: Presents with acute unilateral calf pain, tenderness, swelling, and warmth, and is a key risk factor for pulmonary embolism.
  • IVC obstruction: Causes progressive bilateral leg swelling, often associated with dilated superficial abdominal veins and potentially back or abdominal pain.
  • Pelvic mass: May cause insidious unilateral or bilateral leg swelling by compressing the iliac veins or lymphatic channels.
  • Chronic Kidney Disease: Characterised by gradual onset of bilateral pitting oedema, often accompanied by symptoms like fatigue, nausea, and changes in urination.
  • Hypothyroidism (Myxoedema): Presents as generalised, non-pitting oedema of the skin, typically associated with other systemic features like fatigue, weight gain, and cold intolerance.
Common causes
  • Chronic venous insufficiency: Typically causes bilateral pitting oedema that worsens through the day, often with associated skin changes like pigmentation, eczema, or ulceration.
  • Gravitational oedema: Benign, dependent swelling that occurs after prolonged periods of standing or sitting and resolves with leg elevation.
  • Venous Eczema: Presents as an itchy, red, and scaly rash on the lower legs, indicative of underlying chronic venous disease.
  • Cellulitis: A bacterial skin infection causing a unilateral, well-demarcated area of red, hot, swollen, and tender skin, sometimes with systemic fever.
  • Lymphoedema: A firm, non-pitting swelling, often starting at the foot and progressing up the leg, which does not improve significantly with elevation.
  • Heart failure: Typically causes bilateral, symmetrical pitting oedema, often accompanied by shortness of breath (especially on exertion or when lying flat) and fatigue.
  • COVID-19: Can increase the risk of thromboembolic events, presenting as a DVT with unilateral leg swelling.
Easily missed
  • Medication Induced Leg Swelling: Typically bilateral, pitting oedema that can be a side effect of common drugs such as calcium channel blockers (e.g., amlodipine) or NSAIDs.
  • Baker's cyst rupture: Presents with a sudden onset of calf pain and swelling that can closely mimic a DVT, often in a patient with a history of knee arthritis.
  • Hypoalbuminaemia: Leads to generalised oedema (including the legs) and can be a sign of underlying systemic illness.
  • Liver cirrhosis: Causes bilateral leg swelling due to hypoalbuminaemia and increased hydrostatic pressure, often accompanied by ascites and jaundice.
  • Coeliac disease: Can lead to severe malnutrition and protein-losing enteropathy, resulting in hypoalbuminaemia and oedema.
  • Inflammatory Bowel Disease: May cause hypoalbuminaemia through protein loss from the inflamed gut, leading to peripheral oedema.
  • Malnutrition: Severe lack of dietary protein intake can reduce plasma oncotic pressure, causing generalised oedema.
  • Nephrotic syndrome: Characterised by severe bilateral pitting oedema (often periorbital and peripheral), caused by heavy protein loss through the kidneys.
Other Differential Diagnoses to consider
  • Compartment syndrome: Swelling due to increased pressure within a closed muscle compartment, potentially leading to ischaemia.
  • Filariasis: Parasitic infection causing severe lymphatic blockage and leg swelling, seen in endemic areas.
  • Angioedema: Rapid swelling often related to allergic reactions or hereditary causes, affecting deeper tissues.
  • Thyroid nodules/mass: Space-occupying lesions causing venous obstruction or lymphatic congestion, leading to swelling.
  • Adrenal insufficiency: Hormonal imbalances affecting fluid distribution, potentially causing oedema.
  • Reflex sympathetic dystrophy: Swelling associated with regional pain syndrome, often following trauma or surgery.
History Taking
Patient’s Description: How can I help you today?
Starting with an open-ended question allows the patient to describe their symptoms in their own words. This helps the clinician gather unfiltered information, which can lead to more targeted follow-up questions based on the patient’s narrative. Relevance: Allows the patient to freely explain the main issue, providing clues for the clinician to guide the differential diagnosis process.
When did the swelling start?
  • This helps establish the onset of the swelling—whether it is acute, subacute, or chronic. Acute onset may indicate a more urgent issue such as a DVT or cellulitis, while chronic swelling might suggest venous insufficiency or lymphoedema.
  • Relevance: A positive response indicating recent onset could point towards a DVT, cellulitis, or heart failure. A more chronic onset may favour conditions such as chronic venous insufficiency or lymphoedema.
Is the swelling worse at certain times of the day?
  • Swelling that worsens throughout the day and improves with leg elevation is more suggestive of gravitational oedema or chronic venous insufficiency. Persistent swelling may point to lymphoedema.
  • Relevance: Worsening later in the day or after standing suggests venous insufficiency. Continuous swelling may indicate lymphoedema or a systemic cause such as heart failure or renal disease.
Is the swelling in one leg or both?
  • Unilateral leg swelling could indicate a DVT, cellulitis, or a localised issue such as a Baker’s cyst rupture. Bilateral swelling is more likely to reflect systemic causes like heart failure or kidney disease.
  • Relevance: Unilateral swelling suggests local pathology (DVT, cellulitis), while bilateral swelling indicates systemic conditions (heart failure, nephrotic syndrome).
Is there any pain, redness, or warmth associated with the swelling?
  • The presence of pain, redness, or warmth may suggest an inflammatory or infective process like cellulitis or thrombophlebitis. In contrast, painless swelling might suggest a more chronic or systemic condition.
  • Relevance: Positive responses indicate cellulitis, DVT, or thrombophlebitis. A negative response may point towards chronic venous insufficiency or lymphoedema.
Do you have any shortness of breath or chest pain?
  • These associated symptoms may indicate a more serious underlying cause such as heart failure or pulmonary embolism (PE) secondary to a DVT.
  • Relevance: Positive responses could indicate congestive heart failure or a PE, especially in the context of leg swelling. A negative response may reduce the likelihood of these diagnoses.
Do you have any past medical history of heart, kidney, or liver disease?
  • Swelling related to heart failure, chronic kidney disease, or liver cirrhosis is common, so understanding if the patient has any history of these diseases is crucial for establishing the cause of leg oedema.
  • Relevance: Positive responses would make conditions like heart failure, nephrotic syndrome, or liver cirrhosis more likely.
Are you taking any medications?
  • Medications such as calcium channel blockers, NSAIDs, or corticosteroids are well-known causes of peripheral oedema.
  • Relevance: Positive responses might indicate medication-induced oedema, which can be resolved by changing or stopping the medication.
Ideas, Concerns, and Expectations (ICE)
  • Asking about the patient’s ideas, concerns, and expectations helps to address their thoughts about what might be causing the leg swelling, as well as any specific worries or hopes for the outcome of the consultation.
  • Relevance: This helps build rapport with the patient, ensures their concerns (such as fear of a DVT or heart failure) are addressed, and aligns the management plan with their expectations.
Examination Strategy
Inspection of the legs
  • Begin by carefully inspecting both legs for swelling, redness, skin changes, or asymmetry. Look for any visible dilated veins, skin discolouration, ulcers, or signs of trauma.
  • Relevance: Asymmetry or isolated unilateral swelling suggests a localised issue such as a DVT, cellulitis, or Baker’s cyst. Bilateral swelling may indicate systemic conditions such as heart failure, nephrotic syndrome, or chronic venous insufficiency.
Palpation for pitting oedema
  • Press firmly with your thumb over the bony prominence of the shin and hold for 5 seconds. Release and assess for pitting (indentation remains after pressure is removed).
  • Relevance: Pitting oedema is commonly seen in heart failure, chronic venous insufficiency, or nephrotic syndrome. Non-pitting oedema, in contrast, suggests lymphoedema or myxoedema associated with hypothyroidism.
Assessment of calf tenderness
  • Gently palpate the calf muscles for tenderness or firmness. This helps to assess for signs of deep vein thrombosis (DVT).
  • Relevance: Tenderness in the calf, particularly if unilateral, raises suspicion of DVT. A negative finding lowers the likelihood of DVT but does not entirely rule it out.
Measurement of calf circumference
  • Measure the circumference of both calves at the same point, typically 10 cm below the tibial tuberosity. A difference of more than 3 cm between the two calves can indicate a DVT.
  • Relevance: A positive finding (asymmetric calf circumference) strongly suggests a DVT. A negative finding reduces the likelihood but further investigation may still be needed depending on the clinical context.
Examination for skin temperature
  • Feel the temperature of the skin over both legs. Increased warmth may suggest infection or inflammation, while cool skin may indicate poor perfusion.
  • Relevance: Warmth associated with redness may indicate cellulitis or thrombophlebitis. Cool extremities might suggest arterial insufficiency or advanced heart failure.
Examination for varicose veins
  • Observe the legs for the presence of varicose veins or venous stasis changes such as hyperpigmentation or lipodermatosclerosis.
  • Relevance: The presence of varicose veins and skin changes like venous eczema or ulcers supports a diagnosis of chronic venous insufficiency. Absence of these findings makes this diagnosis less likely.
Examination of inguinal lymph nodes
  • Palpate the inguinal lymph nodes to check for any enlargement, which could suggest infection, malignancy, or lymphatic obstruction.
  • Relevance: Enlarged lymph nodes may indicate cellulitis, lymphoma, or lymphatic obstruction leading to lymphoedema. Absence of lymphadenopathy lowers suspicion for these conditions.
Examination for signs of systemic disease
  • Look for signs of systemic diseases such as jaundice (suggesting liver disease), pallor (anaemia), or raised jugular venous pressure (JVP) as seen in heart failure.
  • Relevance: Raised JVP, jaundice, or other signs of systemic illness suggest heart failure, liver disease (cirrhosis), or renal failure as potential causes of the leg swelling.
Red Flag Synthesis

This section synthesises the key red flags from the source document. Any of these findings in a patient with leg swelling necessitates urgent senior review and likely escalation of care.

Unilateral Swelling with Calf Tenderness
  • This combination of symptoms may indicate deep vein thrombosis (DVT), a condition where a blood clot forms in the deep veins, commonly in the leg. If untreated, the clot can dislodge and cause a potentially life-threatening pulmonary embolism.
  • Significance: Suggests DVT, requiring urgent investigation with Doppler ultrasound and anticoagulation therapy to prevent complications such as pulmonary embolism.
Sudden Leg Swelling with Shortness of Breath
  • Acute leg swelling associated with shortness of breath or pleuritic chest pain could indicate a pulmonary embolism (PE), particularly if the patient has a history of DVT or risk factors for venous thromboembolism.
  • Significance: This could signify a life-threatening pulmonary embolism, requiring immediate hospital admission for imaging (CT pulmonary angiogram) and anticoagulation therapy.
Red, Warm, Tender Swollen Leg
  • These signs suggest cellulitis, a potentially serious bacterial skin infection. If left untreated, cellulitis can spread to deeper tissues, cause sepsis, or result in abscess formation.
  • Significance: May indicate cellulitis, which requires prompt antibiotic therapy to prevent progression to sepsis or tissue necrosis.
Bilateral Swelling with Breathlessness
  • Bilateral leg swelling, especially when associated with shortness of breath and fatigue, raises concern for congestive heart failure (CHF). The legs swell as fluid accumulates due to the heart’s reduced pumping efficiency.
  • Significance: This could indicate heart failure, requiring urgent evaluation, including echocardiography, and management to prevent worsening heart failure and fluid overload complications.
Sudden Painful Swelling After Trauma
  • Acute, painful swelling following trauma could suggest a ruptured Baker’s cyst. If the cyst ruptures, it can cause sharp pain and swelling, mimicking DVT. It may also be associated with an underlying knee pathology such as osteoarthritis or rheumatoid arthritis.
  • Significance: Indicates a ruptured Baker’s cyst, which may require further imaging to exclude DVT, as well as management of the underlying joint disease.
Persistent Swelling with Systemic Symptoms
  • Persistent or recurrent leg swelling with weight loss, night sweats, or fatigue may suggest an underlying malignancy, such as pelvic or abdominal cancer compressing the inferior vena cava (IVC), causing venous obstruction and swelling. Lymphoma could also cause lymphatic obstruction leading to leg swelling.
  • Significance: Suggests malignancy, which requires urgent investigation including imaging (CT or MRI) and referral to oncology for definitive diagnosis and treatment.
Bilateral Swelling with Jaundice/Ascites
  • This combination of symptoms could indicate liver cirrhosis, leading to fluid overload (ascites and oedema) and impaired liver function (jaundice). Cirrhosis causes portal hypertension, leading to increased pressure in the venous system and leg swelling.
  • Significance: Suggests liver cirrhosis, which requires urgent liver function testing, ultrasound, and referral to hepatology for management, including diuretics or paracentesis for fluid control.
Investigations Strategy & Interpretation
Doppler Ultrasound of the leg
  • Doppler ultrasound is the first-line imaging test to assess for deep vein thrombosis (DVT) by visualising blood flow in the veins and detecting any clots.
  • Result Significance: A positive result confirms DVT, which requires anticoagulation treatment. A negative result helps to rule out DVT, though further assessment may be needed if clinical suspicion remains high.
D-dimer blood test
  • D-dimer is a blood test that measures fibrin degradation products, which are elevated in conditions where there is significant clot formation and breakdown, such as DVT or pulmonary embolism (PE).
  • Result Significance: A positive D-dimer is non-specific and requires further imaging (e.g. Doppler ultrasound or CT scan). A negative D-dimer result, in patients with low clinical probability, can effectively rule out DVT or PE.
Full Blood Count (FBC)
  • A full blood count helps identify underlying infection (raised white cell count), anaemia (low haemoglobin), or thrombocytopenia (low platelets), which could be contributing to leg swelling.
  • Result Significance: Elevated white blood cells may suggest cellulitis. Anaemia may point towards chronic disease (heart failure or kidney disease), while thrombocytopenia could indicate liver disease or a clotting disorder.
Liver Function Tests (LFTs)
  • Liver function tests assess the liver’s ability to produce proteins and metabolise substances. Abnormalities in liver enzymes or albumin levels could indicate liver disease, which can cause peripheral oedema due to hypoalbuminaemia or portal hypertension.
  • Result Significance: Abnormal LFTs may indicate liver cirrhosis or hepatic failure, leading to leg swelling. Normal LFTs reduce the likelihood of liver-related causes of oedema.
Serum Creatinine and Urea (Renal Function Tests)
  • These tests assess kidney function. Impaired kidney function can lead to fluid retention and oedema, which presents as bilateral leg swelling.
  • Result Significance: Elevated serum creatinine and urea levels suggest chronic kidney disease (CKD) or acute kidney injury (AKI) as the cause of swelling. Normal levels rule out significant renal dysfunction.
Serum Albumin
  • Serum albumin levels are crucial for maintaining oncotic pressure within blood vessels. Low albumin can result in fluid leaking into tissues, leading to oedema.
  • Result Significance: Hypoalbuminaemia suggests conditions like nephrotic syndrome, liver cirrhosis, or malnutrition. A normal albumin level reduces the likelihood of these diagnoses.
Chest X-ray
  • A chest X-ray is useful in assessing for heart failure, as it can show cardiomegaly (enlarged heart) or pulmonary oedema.
  • Result Significance: A positive result showing pulmonary congestion or cardiomegaly supports the diagnosis of heart failure. A normal chest X-ray makes heart failure less likely.
Electrocardiogram (ECG)
  • An ECG can detect arrhythmias, left ventricular hypertrophy, or signs of previous myocardial infarction, all of which can contribute to heart failure and subsequent leg swelling.
  • Result Significance: Abnormal findings may suggest heart failure or cardiac dysfunction, requiring further cardiac imaging. A normal ECG reduces the likelihood of significant cardiac causes of oedema.
Urinalysis
  • Urinalysis can detect proteinuria, which is a hallmark of nephrotic syndrome, a condition associated with hypoalbuminaemia and oedema.
  • Result Significance: The presence of protein in the urine suggests nephrotic syndrome or glomerulonephritis as a cause of leg swelling. A negative urinalysis makes these conditions less likely.
Echocardiogram
  • An echocardiogram provides a detailed assessment of heart function, including left ventricular ejection fraction and valve function. It is essential in evaluating heart failure as a cause of bilateral leg oedema.
  • Result Significance: Abnormal findings (e.g. reduced ejection fraction) confirm heart failure. Normal echocardiogram results help rule out significant heart failure as a cause of swelling.
Evidence-Based Diagnosis (EBDx)
Unilateral leg swelling and calf tenderness (for Deep Vein Thrombosis - DVT)
  • Unilateral leg swelling and calf tenderness are classic clinical features of DVT. While these are common findings, they alone do not definitively diagnose DVT without further investigation, such as Doppler ultrasound or D-dimer testing.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 79%, Specificity: 50%, Positive Likelihood Ratio (LR+): 1.6, Negative Likelihood Ratio (LR-): 0.42.
  • Significance: Positive findings suggest DVT, but given the moderate specificity, Doppler ultrasound is needed to confirm the diagnosis. Negative findings reduce the likelihood of DVT, though further tests may be necessary in high-risk patients.
Pitting oedema (for Chronic Heart Failure)
  • Pitting oedema is a common finding in chronic heart failure due to fluid retention. It is also seen in other causes of oedema, such as chronic venous insufficiency or nephrotic syndrome, making it a non-specific finding.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 70%, Specificity: 50%, LR+: 1.4, LR-: 0.6.
  • Significance: The presence of pitting oedema increases the suspicion for heart failure, but its low specificity means further tests, such as echocardiography, are needed to confirm the diagnosis.
Positive D-dimer (for DVT and Pulmonary Embolism)
  • D-dimer is highly sensitive for the presence of clots (such as DVT or pulmonary embolism), but it is non-specific and can be elevated in various conditions like infection, malignancy, or recent surgery.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 95%, Specificity: 40%, LR+: 1.58, LR-: 0.13.
  • Significance: A positive D-dimer supports the diagnosis of DVT or PE, especially in high-risk patients, but due to its low specificity, further imaging such as Doppler ultrasound or CT pulmonary angiogram is required. A negative D-dimer in low-risk patients effectively rules out DVT or PE.
Elevated Jugular Venous Pressure (JVP) (for Heart Failure)
  • Raised JVP is a specific sign of volume overload and is often seen in right-sided heart failure. This can cause systemic venous congestion, leading to bilateral leg oedema.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 39%, Specificity: 92%, LR+: 4.9, LR-: 0.66.
  • Significance: A raised JVP strongly suggests heart failure due to its high specificity, but its low sensitivity means a normal JVP does not exclude heart failure.
Proteinuria (for Nephrotic Syndrome)
  • Proteinuria, identified through urinalysis, is a hallmark of nephrotic syndrome, where large amounts of protein are lost in the urine, leading to hypoalbuminaemia and oedema.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 93%, Specificity: 86%, LR+: 6.64, LR-: 0.08.
  • Significance: Positive proteinuria strongly suggests nephrotic syndrome as the cause of leg swelling. Negative proteinuria makes this diagnosis unlikely.
Bilateral leg swelling with hypoalbuminaemia (for Liver Cirrhosis or Nephrotic Syndrome)
  • Low serum albumin leads to decreased oncotic pressure, allowing fluid to leak into tissues, causing oedema. This is commonly seen in liver cirrhosis and nephrotic syndrome.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 85%, Specificity: 90% for detecting significant liver or renal pathology as the cause of leg swelling.
  • Significance: Hypoalbuminaemia with bilateral leg swelling suggests liver disease or nephrotic syndrome. Normal albumin levels make these diagnoses less likely.
Elevated BNP or NT-proBNP (for Heart Failure)
  • Brain natriuretic peptide (BNP) and N-terminal proBNP are released in response to ventricular stretch and pressure overload, making them useful markers for heart failure.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 90%, Specificity: 76%, LR+: 3.75, LR-: 0.13 for BNP levels > 100 pg/mL.
  • Significance: Elevated BNP or NT-proBNP supports the diagnosis of heart failure in patients with leg swelling. Low levels effectively rule out heart failure as the cause of swelling.
Diagnostic Timeout & Cognitive Biases

Awareness of common thinking errors is essential to improve diagnostic accuracy and patient safety when assessing a patient with leg swelling. Before finalising a diagnosis, it is good practice for clinicians at all levels to take a 'diagnostic timeout' to challenge their own reasoning.

  • Anchoring Bias: This is the tendency to lock onto an initial impression. For example, attributing bilateral leg swelling in an elderly, immobile patient solely to 'gravitational oedema' without fully considering or investigating for new-onset heart failure or chronic kidney disease. A key question to ask is: "Could this be something more serious, and have I done enough to rule it out?"
  • Confirmation Bias: Actively seeking or over-emphasising findings that support a preferred diagnosis while ignoring contradictory evidence. For instance, focusing on a history of varicose veins to confirm chronic venous insufficiency, while downplaying the acute onset of the swelling and calf pain which might point to a superimposed DVT.
  • Attribution Error: Ascribing symptoms to a patient's known condition without considering new pathology. A common example is assuming a red, swollen leg in a patient with known lymphoedema is just a flare-up, when it could be superimposed cellulitis, a serious and common complication requiring urgent antibiotics.
  • Premature Closure: Accepting a diagnosis before it is fully verified. For example, making a diagnosis of cellulitis and discharging a patient with antibiotics, without considering a ruptured Baker's cyst or DVT, which can present similarly but require different management pathways.
Illustrative Management Pathways

For educational purposes, this section describes the typical principles of management for key diagnoses. These are not prescriptive instructions. All management must be guided by official UK guidelines, local formularies, and senior clinical advice.

Deep Vein Thrombosis (DVT)

Initial management is guided by clinical probability scoring (e.g., the Wells score). In patients with a likely score, a Doppler ultrasound is arranged urgently. If a DVT is confirmed, treatment with therapeutic anticoagulation is started immediately. Direct Oral Anticoagulants (DOACs) such as apixaban or rivaroxaban are now first-line for most patients, as per NICE guidelines. The duration of treatment depends on whether the DVT was provoked (e.g., by recent surgery) or unprovoked.

Congestive Heart Failure (CHF)

Management of oedema in acute or decompensated heart failure focuses on removing excess fluid with diuretics (e.g., intravenous or oral furosemide). The underlying cause of the decompensation should be identified and addressed. Long-term management involves optimising evidence-based medications (e.g., ACE inhibitors, beta-blockers, mineralocorticoid receptor antagonists), fluid and salt restriction, and cardiac rehabilitation.

Cellulitis

The mainstay of treatment is prompt administration of antibiotics. The choice of antibiotic is guided by local microbiology guidelines but is often flucloxacillin for mild-to-moderate cases in the UK (with alternatives for penicillin allergy). The affected limb should be elevated to reduce swelling. It is good practice to mark the edge of the erythema to monitor treatment response. Severe cases or those with systemic signs of sepsis require hospital admission for intravenous antibiotics.

Chronic Venous Insufficiency & Gravitational Oedema

Once more serious causes have been excluded, management is conservative and focused on symptomatic relief and preventing complications. The core principles are regular leg elevation, encouraging mobility and calf muscle exercises, and the use of graduated compression stockings (after excluding significant peripheral arterial disease). Good skin care with emollients is vital to prevent venous eczema and ulcers.

Reflection & Learning

These brief cases illustrate the application of clinical reasoning to different presentations of leg swelling. They are for educational purposes only.

Case 1: Suspected DVT in Primary Care

A 45-year-old woman presents to her GP with a 3-day history of a swollen, painful, and red left calf. She recently returned from a long-haul flight. Her Wells score is calculated as 2 (calf swelling >3cm difference, entire leg swollen). The problem representation is a patient with a key risk factor (long-haul travel) and classic unilateral symptoms, making a DVT highly probable. The GP arranges for an urgent same-day Doppler ultrasound at the local ambulatory care unit and advises the patient on what to do pending the result.

Case 2: Suspected Heart Failure in the Emergency Department

An 80-year-old man with a history of hypertension presents with a one-week history of worsening bilateral leg swelling and shortness of breath, which is now preventing him from sleeping flat. On examination, he has bilateral pitting oedema up to his knees and crackles at both lung bases. The problem representation is an elderly man with bilateral oedema and clear signs of fluid overload (orthopnoea, basal crackles), highly suggestive of decompensated heart failure. Immediate management involves an ECG, chest X-ray, blood tests including U&Es and BNP, and administration of intravenous diuretics.

Case 3: A Red Leg - Cellulitis vs Venous Eczema

A 72-year-old woman with known severe varicose veins presents with a red, itchy, and slightly swollen left lower leg. The area feels warm but she is systemically well with no fever, and the tenderness is mild. She has chronic brown discolouration on both legs. The problem representation is a patient with known chronic venous disease presenting with an acute red leg. The key is to differentiate cellulitis (an infection requiring antibiotics) from a flare of inflammatory venous eczema. The lack of severe tenderness and systemic upset makes eczema more likely, but there is diagnostic uncertainty. A reasonable approach might be a trial of potent topical steroids and compression, with clear safety-netting advice to return immediately if she develops a fever or the redness spreads rapidly.
