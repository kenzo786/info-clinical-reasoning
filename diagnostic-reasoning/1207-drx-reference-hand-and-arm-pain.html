Hand and Arm Pain Diagnostic Reasoning Reference
Context: Hand and Arm Pain Assessment
Overview

Hand and arm pain is a frequent and diagnostically complex presentation in both primary and emergency care settings. The potential causes are extensive, ranging from benign musculoskeletal strains to urgent, limb-threatening, or life-threatening emergencies. The key challenge for the clinician is to systematically differentiate between localised pathology (e.g., trauma, nerve entrapment), referred pain (e.g., from the cervical spine or heart), and systemic disease (e.g., inflammatory arthropathy, malignancy).

A structured and thorough approach is essential to identify red flag features that signify serious underlying conditions such as acute limb ischaemia, cervical cord compression, or septic arthritis, ensuring timely and appropriate referral. Simultaneously, a robust clinical framework prevents unnecessary investigations for more common, self-limiting conditions. This educational module provides a foundational framework for the clinical reasoning process in patients presenting with hand and arm pain. For definitive, real-time management protocols, users must refer to official UK guidelines from authoritative bodies such as NICE and specialty-specific organisations.

Pathophysiology/Basic Science

Understanding the origin of hand and arm pain is fundamental to forming an accurate differential diagnosis. Pain can be generated through several distinct mechanisms:

  • Nociceptive Somatic Pain: This is the most common type and arises from the stimulation of pain receptors (nociceptors) in musculoskeletal tissues like skin, muscles, tendons, ligaments, and bone. It is typically well-localised and described as aching, sharp, or throbbing. Examples include pain from a bone fracture, a muscle strain, or inflammation of a bursa (bursitis).
  • Neuropathic Pain: This type of pain results from injury or dysfunction of the peripheral or central nervous system. It occurs when nerves are compressed, stretched, or damaged. The pain is often described as burning, shooting, tingling, or electric-shock-like and may be associated with numbness or weakness in the distribution of the affected nerve. Examples include Carpal Tunnel Syndrome (compression of the median nerve at the wrist) and Cervical Radiculopathy (compression of a nerve root in the neck).
  • Vascular Pain (Ischaemia): This occurs when blood flow to the tissues is insufficient to meet metabolic demands, leading to a build-up of waste products and stimulation of pain receptors. In the arm, this is most critically seen in acute limb ischaemia, where a sudden blockage of a major artery causes severe, constant pain, often described as the '5 Ps': pain, pallor, pulselessness, paraesthesia, and paralysis.
  • Referred Pain: This is pain perceived at a location other than the site of the painful stimulus. It occurs because sensory nerves from different parts of the body share common pathways to the spinal cord and brain. A classic example is left arm pain during a myocardial infarction, where visceral sensory fibres from the heart converge with somatic fibres from the arm. Similarly, pathology in the cervical spine (e.g., cervical spondylosis) can refer pain down the arm.
  • Inflammatory Pain: This is driven by the body's immune response, as seen in conditions like rheumatoid arthritis or septic arthritis. Inflammatory mediators released by immune cells sensitise nociceptors, leading to pain, swelling, warmth, redness, and loss of function in the affected joint or tissue.
Differential Diagnosis (DDx) Framework

A structured approach to the differential diagnosis of hand and arm pain is essential to ensure life-threatening and limb-threatening conditions are prioritised.

Must not miss!
  • Bone fracture: Presents with localised pain, swelling, and deformity, often following a specific traumatic injury.
  • Cervical cord compression: A neurological emergency causing arm weakness, clumsiness, sensory changes, and often gait disturbance or sphincter dysfunction.
  • Myocardial Infarction: Typically presents as central chest pain but can radiate to the left arm (or both) and may be associated with sweating and nausea.
  • Angina: A constricting discomfort in the chest that can radiate to the arm, typically triggered by exertion and relieved by rest.
  • Acute limb ischaemia: Characterised by the sudden onset of severe pain, pallor, pulselessness, paraesthesia, coldness, and paralysis in the limb.
  • Compartment syndrome: Severe pain, often described as 'out of proportion' to the injury, with a tense, swollen muscle compartment and pain on passive stretching.
  • Rhabdomyolysis: Can cause severe muscle pain and weakness, often accompanied by dark-coloured urine, typically following crush injury or overexertion.
  • Cellulitis: A bacterial skin infection causing a spreading area of redness, warmth, swelling, and pain.
  • Septic arthritis: A severe, single hot, swollen, and painful joint with markedly reduced movement, often associated with fever.
  • Osteomyelitis: An infection of the bone causing deep, localised pain, tenderness, and often systemic signs like fever.
  • Metastatic cancer: Can present as persistent, deep, boring bone pain, which is often worse at night and not relieved by simple analgesia.
  • Pancoast's tumour: A lung cancer at the apex of the lung that can invade surrounding structures, causing shoulder pain, arm pain/weakness (brachial plexus), and Horner's syndrome.
  • Multiple myeloma: A haematological malignancy that can cause bone pain (particularly in the spine and ribs), fatigue, and recurrent infections.
  • Bone tumours: Primary bone tumours (benign or malignant) can present with localised, persistent pain and sometimes a palpable mass or pathological fracture.
Common causes
  • Sprains and strains: Acute pain and dysfunction resulting from injury to ligaments (sprain) or muscles/tendons (strain).
  • Repetitive strain injury: A gradual onset of pain or discomfort in muscles, nerves and tendons caused by repetitive movement and overuse.
  • Epicondylitis: Pain localised to the outer (lateral epicondylitis or 'tennis elbow') or inner (medial epicondylitis or 'golfer's elbow') aspect of the elbow.
  • Wrist tendinopathy: Pain, swelling, and weakness around the wrist, often related to overuse, such as De Quervain's tenosynovitis.
  • Olecranon Bursitis: A swelling over the point of the elbow (olecranon process), which may be painful and tender.
  • Shoulder pain presentation: Pain originating from the shoulder joint or surrounding structures (e.g., rotator cuff) that is felt in the upper arm.
  • Cervical spondylosis: Age-related degenerative changes in the neck vertebrae and discs, causing neck pain that may radiate to the arm.
  • Cervical radiculopathy: Pain, numbness, tingling or weakness in a specific nerve root distribution in the arm, caused by a compressed nerve in the neck.
  • Mononeuropathy: A condition affecting a single peripheral nerve, leading to sensory or motor changes in its distribution.
  • Carpal tunnel syndrome: Compression of the median nerve at the wrist, causing pain, tingling, and numbness in the thumb, index, middle, and ring fingers.
  • Ulnar neuropathy: Compression of the ulnar nerve, most commonly at the elbow (cubital tunnel), causing numbness and tingling in the little and ring fingers.
  • Arthropathy presentation: Pain, stiffness, and swelling in the joints of the hand and arm, due to conditions like osteoarthritis or rheumatoid arthritis.
Easily missed
  • Medication-induced Hand and Arm Pain (e.g. Statins): Muscle aches and pains (myalgia) can be a side effect of certain medications, notably statins.
  • Polymyalgia rheumatica: An inflammatory condition causing pain and stiffness, primarily in the shoulders and hips, in people over 50.
  • Cervical rib: An extra rib arising from the seventh cervical vertebra, which can cause thoracic outlet syndrome by compressing nerves or blood vessels.
  • Peripheral neuropathy: Damage to peripheral nerves causing a 'glove and stocking' distribution of numbness, tingling, and pain, often due to diabetes.
  • Connective tissue diseases: Systemic autoimmune diseases like Lupus (SLE) or Scleroderma can present with joint pain, Raynaud's phenomenon, and skin changes in the hands.
Other Differential Diagnoses to consider
  • Thoracic outlet syndrome: Compression of neurovascular structures passing through the thoracic outlet.
  • Herpes zoster (shingles): Dermatomal pain and rash due to reactivation of varicella-zoster virus.
  • Complex regional pain syndrome (CRPS): Chronic pain syndrome with autonomic and sensory abnormalities following trauma.
  • Raynaud's phenomenon: Vasospastic disorder presenting with episodic pain, particularly in cold exposure.
  • Fibromyalgia: Chronic widespread pain with associated fatigue and tender points.
  • Chronic pain syndrome: Multidimensional chronic pain unrelated to acute injury or pathology.
  • Psychogenic pain: Pain due to psychological factors without clear physical pathology.
History Taking
Patient’s Description: How can I help you today?
Starting with an open-ended question such as "Can you tell me more about what brought you here today?" is crucial. Relevance: Allows the patient to describe the issue in their own words, highlighting key details such as pain location, onset, and impact on daily activities, which guide more specific follow-up questions.
Can you describe the pain? Is it sharp, dull, throbbing, or burning?
  • Understanding the quality of the pain helps differentiate between causes: sharp pain may suggest trauma or nerve involvement, while burning pain points towards neuropathy.
  • Relevance: Positive response suggests nerve-related conditions like carpal tunnel syndrome or radiculopathy, while dull or throbbing pain may indicate musculoskeletal causes like tendinopathy.
When did the pain start? Was it sudden or gradual?
  • Onset timing is critical in distinguishing acute from chronic conditions. Sudden pain may indicate trauma or acute ischaemia, while gradual onset aligns with chronic overuse or degenerative conditions.
  • Relevance: Sudden onset suggests fractures or compartment syndrome, while gradual onset is more indicative of conditions like repetitive strain injury or arthritis.
Does anything make the pain worse or better?
  • Triggers and relieving factors can help narrow down the cause. For instance, activity worsening pain suggests musculoskeletal issues, while relief with rest could indicate vascular or neuropathic problems.
  • Relevance: Positive response to activity suggests tendonitis or repetitive strain injury, while worsening at night supports carpal tunnel syndrome or ulnar neuropathy.
Do you have any numbness, tingling, or weakness?
  • Neurological symptoms like numbness or tingling are indicative of nerve compression or neuropathies, while weakness could signify cervical radiculopathy or serious neural compromise.
  • Relevance: Positive response suggests neuropathic conditions like carpal tunnel syndrome, cervical radiculopathy, or thoracic outlet syndrome. Absence of these symptoms makes neuropathies less likely.
Have you had any associated symptoms, such as fever, swelling, or changes in skin colour?
  • Associated systemic or local symptoms can guide diagnoses. Fever may indicate infection, swelling points towards trauma or inflammatory conditions, and discolouration might suggest vascular causes.
  • Relevance: Fever with swelling suggests cellulitis or osteomyelitis, while discolouration or pallor raises concern for acute limb ischaemia.
Do you have any history of trauma or overuse of the affected area?
  • Trauma suggests fractures, sprains, or soft tissue injuries, while repetitive strain indicates tendinopathies or bursitis.
  • Relevance: Positive history of trauma or overuse strongly supports musculoskeletal causes, while its absence shifts the focus to systemic or referred conditions.
Ideas, Concerns, and Expectations (ICE)
  • ICE questions ensure the consultation addresses the patient's perspective and emotional concerns.
  • Relevance: Patients may fear serious conditions like cancer or a heart attack. Exploring expectations helps manage treatment goals and align understanding between clinician and patient.
Examination Strategy
Inspection of the arm and hand
  • Observe for swelling, deformity, bruising, erythema, or skin changes such as discolouration, rash, or wounds.
  • Relevance: Swelling and deformity may suggest trauma (e.g., fracture or dislocation). Erythema with warmth could indicate cellulitis or septic arthritis. Discolouration could indicate acute limb ischaemia, while rashes might suggest conditions such as shingles.
Palpation of the affected area
  • Assess for tenderness, warmth, swelling, or palpable deformity in the arm, hand, and associated joints.
  • Relevance: Tenderness localised to a specific area suggests fracture, tendinopathy, or arthritis. Warmth and swelling may point to infection (e.g., cellulitis, septic arthritis) or inflammatory conditions like gout. Absence of these findings suggests non-inflammatory or referred pain.
Range of motion (active and passive)
  • Evaluate both active and passive range of motion in the shoulder, elbow, wrist, and fingers.
  • Relevance: Limited active but preserved passive motion may suggest rotator cuff injury or muscle strain. Restrictions in both active and passive motion are concerning for joint pathology such as adhesive capsulitis or arthritis. Normal motion reduces the likelihood of structural abnormalities.
Neurological examination
  • Assess for sensation, strength, and reflexes in the affected limb.
  • Relevance: Sensory deficits may indicate carpal tunnel syndrome, cervical radiculopathy, or ulnar neuropathy. Weakness can suggest nerve entrapment, radiculopathy, or serious pathology like cervical cord compression. Absent reflexes can indicate peripheral neuropathy or radiculopathy, while hyperreflexia might point to upper motor neuron involvement.
Vascular examination
  • Check pulses (radial, ulnar, brachial) and assess capillary refill and skin temperature.
  • Relevance: Weak or absent pulses suggest vascular compromise, such as acute limb ischaemia. Cold skin may indicate poor circulation. Normal findings reduce the likelihood of significant vascular pathology.
Special tests for nerve entrapment
  • Perform Tinel’s test, Phalen’s test, and elbow flexion test to assess for nerve entrapment syndromes like carpal or cubital tunnel syndrome.
  • Relevance: Positive Tinel’s or Phalen’s tests support carpal tunnel syndrome, while an elbow flexion test may indicate ulnar neuropathy. Negative tests make these diagnoses less likely.
Examination of the cervical spine
  • Evaluate cervical spine range of motion and perform Spurling’s test to assess for radiculopathy.
  • Relevance: Pain with Spurling’s test suggests cervical radiculopathy. Normal cervical spine examination reduces the likelihood of referred pain from the neck.
Provocative tests for tendinopathy
  • Perform resisted wrist extension (lateral epicondylitis) and resisted wrist flexion (medial epicondylitis).
  • Relevance: Pain with these tests supports tendinopathy, while their absence suggests alternative causes.
Red Flag Synthesis
Sudden onset of severe pain with cold, pale, or numb limb
  • This suggests acute limb ischaemia, which may be caused by arterial occlusion or embolism.
  • Significance: Acute limb ischaemia is a surgical emergency requiring urgent vascular assessment to prevent limb loss.
Pain with fever, swelling, or redness
  • Fever and localised inflammatory signs such as swelling and redness may indicate an infection like cellulitis, septic arthritis, or osteomyelitis.
  • Significance: Untreated infections can lead to sepsis, joint destruction, or osteonecrosis, necessitating urgent intervention.
Progressive weakness or numbness in the arm
  • These symptoms may suggest cervical cord compression, cervical radiculopathy, or nerve entrapment syndromes.
  • Significance: Cervical cord compression is a neurological emergency as it can lead to permanent disability if not treated promptly.
Pain radiating to the chest, neck, or jaw
  • This may indicate referred pain from cardiac causes such as myocardial infarction or angina.
  • Significance: Cardiac causes of pain are life-threatening and require immediate medical attention.
Night pain or unexplained weight loss
  • These are concerning for malignancy, such as metastatic cancer, primary bone tumours, or multiple myeloma.
  • Significance: Malignancy requires prompt diagnosis and referral for oncological treatment.
Severe pain out of proportion to findings on examination
  • May indicate compartment syndrome, characterised by increased pressure within a closed muscle compartment leading to ischaemia.
  • Significance: Compartment syndrome is a surgical emergency requiring fasciotomy to prevent permanent damage.
Persistent bony tenderness after trauma
  • Localised bone tenderness, especially after trauma, suggests a fracture, which might be complicated by vascular or neurological compromise.
  • Significance: Fractures require imaging and appropriate management to prevent complications like non-union or nerve damage.
Investigations Strategy & Interpretation
X-ray of the affected area
  • Used to detect fractures, dislocations, bone tumours, or degenerative changes.
  • Result Significance: Positive findings suggest fractures, osteoarthritis, or tumours. Negative findings reduce the likelihood of bony abnormalities but do not rule out soft tissue or nerve issues.
MRI of the neck or limb
  • Provides detailed imaging of soft tissues, nerves, and spinal structures, helping in the evaluation of cervical radiculopathy, nerve entrapment, or tendon injuries.
  • Result Significance: Positive findings may reveal nerve compression, soft tissue injuries, or inflammatory changes. Negative findings rule out these causes.
Nerve conduction studies and electromyography (EMG)
  • Helps to evaluate the function of peripheral nerves and muscles, diagnosing conditions like carpal tunnel syndrome, ulnar neuropathy, or cervical radiculopathy.
  • Result Significance: Positive results support diagnoses of peripheral neuropathies or radiculopathies. Negative results make these conditions less likely.
Full blood count (FBC)
  • Assesses for infection, anaemia, or haematological malignancies like multiple myeloma.
  • Result Significance: Leucocytosis suggests infection, while abnormal counts could indicate haematological disorders. Normal results do not exclude localised issues or mechanical causes.
C-reactive protein (CRP) and erythrocyte sedimentation rate (ESR)
  • Markers of inflammation that help identify infectious, inflammatory, or neoplastic conditions.
  • Result Significance: Elevated levels suggest infection, inflammatory arthritis, or malignancy. Normal levels reduce the likelihood of systemic inflammatory or infective causes.
Bone profile and serum calcium levels
  • Evaluates bone metabolism and can detect malignancies or metabolic bone diseases.
  • Result Significance: Abnormal results may indicate multiple myeloma, metastatic bone disease, or metabolic conditions. Normal results reduce the likelihood of these pathologies.
Blood cultures
  • Used to identify bacteraemia in cases of suspected septic arthritis, osteomyelitis, or cellulitis.
  • Result Significance: Positive cultures confirm systemic infection. Negative cultures do not exclude localised infections but reduce the likelihood of bacteraemia.
Urinalysis and serum electrophoresis
  • Urinalysis detects Bence-Jones proteins in suspected multiple myeloma. Serum electrophoresis helps confirm monoclonal gammopathy.
  • Result Significance: Positive findings support the diagnosis of multiple myeloma. Negative findings make this less likely but do not rule it out completely.
Ultrasound of the limb
  • Useful for evaluating soft tissue injuries, tendon pathologies, or suspected thrombosis.
  • Result Significance: Positive findings indicate soft tissue or vascular pathology. Negative results make these conditions less likely.
Evidence-Based Diagnosis (EBDx)
Tinel’s Sign for Carpal Tunnel Syndrome
  • Tinel’s sign involves tapping over the median nerve at the wrist to elicit tingling or pain. It is used to assess carpal tunnel syndrome.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 67%, Specificity: 68%, Positive Likelihood Ratio: 2.09. Reference: Ghasemi-Rad et al., 2014.
  • Significance: Positive Tinel’s sign increases the likelihood of carpal tunnel syndrome, but its moderate sensitivity and specificity mean further confirmation with nerve conduction studies is advised.
MRI for Cervical Radiculopathy
  • MRI provides detailed imaging of cervical structures to identify nerve root compression or spinal cord pathology.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 93%, Specificity: 72% for nerve root compression. Reference: Boden et al., 1990.
  • Significance: A positive MRI strongly supports cervical radiculopathy but must be correlated with clinical findings to avoid overdiagnosis, as asymptomatic findings are common.
Raised CRP/ESR in Septic Arthritis
  • CRP and ESR are markers of inflammation, and elevated levels are seen in septic arthritis and other infections.
  • Evidence: CRP: Sensitivity 91%, Specificity 79%. ESR: Sensitivity 94%, Specificity 61%. Reference: Margaretten et al., 2007.
  • Significance: Elevated levels support a diagnosis of septic arthritis, but normal levels significantly reduce its likelihood, especially in low-risk patients.
Ultrasound for Tendinopathy
  • Ultrasound identifies structural changes in tendons, such as thickening or hypoechoic areas, in cases of tendinopathy.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 79%, Specificity: 94% for detecting rotator cuff tendinopathy. Reference: Dinnes et al., 2003.
  • Significance: A positive ultrasound finding is highly specific for tendinopathy and can guide treatment, but negative findings do not rule out early or mild cases.
Blood Cultures in Suspected Septic Arthritis
  • Blood cultures detect bacteraemia in systemic infections and help identify causative organisms.
  • Evidence: Positive culture sensitivity: 50%, Specificity: 98%. Reference: Kaandorp et al., 1997.
  • Significance: Positive blood cultures strongly indicate septic arthritis, especially in febrile patients, but negative cultures do not exclude localised infection.
Nerve Conduction Studies for Carpal Tunnel Syndrome
  • Measures electrical conduction in peripheral nerves to identify compression or damage, commonly at the wrist.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 85%, Specificity: 95%. Reference: Katz et al., 1990.
  • Significance: Positive results confirm carpal tunnel syndrome with high diagnostic accuracy, making this the gold standard investigation for nerve entrapment.
Diagnostic Timeout & Cognitive Biases

Awareness of common thinking errors is crucial for diagnostic accuracy when assessing a patient with hand and arm pain. Before finalising a diagnosis, clinicians should take a 'diagnostic timeout' to challenge their own reasoning.

  • Anchoring Bias: This is the tendency to fixate on initial impressions. For example, anchoring on a diagnosis of 'Repetitive Strain Injury' in an office worker, and failing to consider a new diagnosis of cervical radiculopathy when they describe new neurological symptoms like tingling or weakness. A key question to ask is: "Could this be referred pain from the neck?"
  • Confirmation Bias: This involves seeking or favouring information that confirms a preconceived diagnosis while ignoring contradictory evidence. For instance, focusing on wrist tenderness to confirm carpal tunnel syndrome while downplaying the significance of neck stiffness or pain on neck movement, which might point towards a cervical cause.
  • Atypical Presentations: A significant pitfall is failing to recognise that serious conditions can present unusually. Myocardial infarction can present solely as left arm pain without any chest pain, especially in women or individuals with diabetes. A high index of suspicion is required. Similarly, malignancy may present with vague, non-specific arm pain before systemic symptoms like weight loss become apparent.
  • Diagnosis Momentum: Accepting a previous diagnosis without question. A patient may have been told they have 'arthritis' in their hand, but their new symptoms of a single hot, swollen, exquisitely painful joint could represent septic arthritis, a true emergency that is being overlooked because of the existing label.
Illustrative Management Pathways

For educational purposes, this section describes the typical principles of management for key diagnoses. These are not prescriptive instructions. All management must be guided by official UK guidelines, local formularies, and senior clinical advice.

Carpal Tunnel Syndrome

Initial management is typically conservative and can be initiated in primary care. This involves advising the patient on activity modification to avoid provocative wrist movements and the use of night-time wrist splints in a neutral position to reduce pressure on the median nerve. If symptoms persist or are severe, a corticosteroid injection into the carpal tunnel may be offered. Patients with persistent symptoms, significant sensory loss, or thenar muscle wasting require referral to secondary care for nerve conduction studies and consideration of surgical decompression.

Cervical Radiculopathy

The majority of cases of acute cervical radiculopathy are self-limiting and can be managed conservatively. Management focuses on analgesia (e.g., paracetamol, NSAIDs, or neuropathic agents like amitriptyline or gabapentin) and physiotherapy. Physiotherapy aims to improve neck posture, strengthen neck and shoulder girdle muscles, and may involve manual therapy techniques. Patients with progressive neurological deficits (weakness), intractable pain, or features of cervical myelopathy (cord compression) require urgent referral for specialist assessment and imaging (MRI).

Septic Arthritis

Suspected septic arthritis is a rheumatological and orthopaedic emergency requiring immediate hospital admission. The diagnostic cornerstone is joint aspiration for urgent gram stain, culture, and crystal microscopy. While awaiting results, empirical intravenous antibiotics should be commenced according to local guidelines, targeting common organisms like Staphylococcus aureus. Management also includes adequate analgesia and joint immobilisation initially, followed by surgical washout (arthroscopy or arthrotomy) if the joint does not respond to antibiotics or if there is a large effusion. Early treatment is vital to prevent irreversible joint destruction and sepsis.

Benign Causes (e.g., Musculoskeletal Strain, Epicondylitis)

After excluding serious pathology, management focuses on reassurance, education, and symptom control. For acute strains or epicondylitis, the principles of 'Protect, Optimal Loading, Ice, Compression, Elevation' (POLICE) can be applied. Simple analgesia like paracetamol or topical/oral NSAIDs (if appropriate) is recommended. Advice on modifying activities that exacerbate the pain is crucial. For persistent tendinopathies like epicondylitis, physiotherapy referral is key for tailored exercise programmes to promote tendon healing and prevent recurrence.

Reflection & Learning

These brief cases illustrate the application of clinical reasoning to different hand and arm pain presentations. They are for educational purposes only.

Case 1: Suspected Carpal Tunnel Syndrome in Primary Care

A 45-year-old female administrator presents to her GP with a 6-month history of tingling and numbness in her right thumb, index, and middle fingers. The symptoms are worse at night, often waking her from sleep, and are relieved by 'shaking her hand out'. The problem representation is a patient with a classic history for median nerve compression at the wrist. Examination reveals a positive Tinel's and Phalen's test. The GP initiates conservative management with a wrist splint for night-time use and provides safety netting advice to return if weakness or persistent numbness develops.

Case 2: Suspected Cervical Radiculopathy

A 58-year-old male builder presents with a 2-week history of pain radiating from the right side of his neck down his arm to the index finger, accompanied by a 'pins and needles' sensation. He denies any weakness or issues with his legs or bladder. The problem representation is a patient with pain and paraesthesia in a C7 dermatomal distribution, suggestive of cervical radiculopathy. Examination reveals pain on right-sided neck extension and lateral flexion (positive Spurling's test). He is managed with appropriate analgesia, referred for physiotherapy, and advised on red flags for cord compression.

Case 3: Red Flags for Malignancy

A 68-year-old retired smoker presents with a 2-month history of a deep, boring pain in his upper left arm that is constant and has started to wake him at night. He has also felt generally fatigued and lost about 5kg in weight unintentionally. The problem representation is an older patient with a significant smoking history presenting with constitutional symptoms and bone pain with red flag features (night pain, unexplained weight loss). While musculoskeletal causes are possible, malignancy (such as metastatic lung cancer or a Pancoast's tumour) is a critical differential. He requires urgent investigation, starting with a chest X-ray and inflammatory markers, and prompt referral based on the results.
