Arthropathy Diagnostic Reasoning Reference
Context: Arthropathy Assessment
Overview

Arthropathy, or joint disease, is a frequent and diverse presentation in clinical practice, encompassing a spectrum from localised, self-limiting injuries to the initial manifestation of complex systemic inflammatory diseases. The challenge for the clinician is to differentiate benign, degenerative conditions like osteoarthritis from urgent diagnoses such as septic arthritis, or chronic systemic diseases like rheumatoid arthritis which require specialist management to prevent long-term disability. A structured diagnostic approach is paramount.

The initial assessment must focus on identifying red flags that indicate infection, malignancy, or acute systemic inflammation. This module provides a foundational framework for the clinical reasoning process in a patient presenting with joint-related symptoms. For definitive management protocols, clinicians must consult official UK guidelines from bodies like the National Institute for Health and Care Excellence (NICE) and the British Society for Rheumatology (BSR).

Pathophysiology/Basic Science

Understanding the underlying mechanism of joint pain is fundamental to forming a differential diagnosis. Pain and dysfunction can arise from several distinct pathological processes:

  • Degenerative Disease (e.g., Osteoarthritis): This is primarily a 'wear and tear' process characterised by the progressive loss of articular cartilage. Mechanical stress leads to cartilage breakdown, subchondral bone sclerosis, and the formation of osteophytes (bony spurs). The pain is primarily due to inflammation of the synovium and sensitisation of nerve endings in the subchondral bone and joint capsule, rather than from the aneural cartilage itself.
  • Inflammatory Synovitis (e.g., Rheumatoid Arthritis): In autoimmune conditions, the synovium (the membrane lining the joint capsule) becomes the primary site of inflammation. An influx of immune cells (T-cells, B-cells, macrophages) leads to synovial hypertrophy (pannus formation). This inflamed pannus releases cytokines and enzymes that actively erode cartilage, bone, and surrounding ligaments, causing pain, swelling, and progressive joint destruction.
  • Crystal Deposition (e.g., Gout, Pseudogout): This occurs when metabolic conditions lead to the supersaturation of certain substances in the blood and synovial fluid. In gout, monosodium urate crystals precipitate within the joint space. These crystals are recognised by the innate immune system, triggering a massive and rapid influx of neutrophils and a potent inflammatory response, resulting in acute, severe pain, redness, and swelling.
  • Infection (e.g., Septic Arthritis): This is caused by the direct invasion of the joint space by microorganisms, most commonly bacteria. The bacteria provoke an intense purulent inflammatory response. The resulting influx of neutrophils releases proteolytic enzymes that can rapidly destroy articular cartilage within hours to days, making this a true rheumatological emergency.
  • Enthesitis (e.g., Spondyloarthropathies): This is inflammation at the site where ligaments, tendons, or the joint capsule attach to bone (the 'enthesis'). This is the hallmark lesion of conditions like ankylosing spondylitis and psoriatic arthritis. The inflammation at these sites can lead to pain, stiffness, and eventually new bone formation, causing fusion of joints, particularly in the spine.
Differential Diagnosis (DDx) Framework

A structured approach to the differential diagnosis of arthropathy is essential to ensure life-threatening and disabling conditions are prioritised.

Must not miss!
  • Septic arthritis: Presents as an acute, hot, swollen, and exquisitely painful single joint, often with systemic signs like fever, requiring urgent intervention to prevent joint destruction.
  • Rheumatoid arthritis: Typically causes a symmetrical, inflammatory polyarthritis of small joints (hands/feet) with significant morning stiffness, fatigue, and potential for systemic involvement.
  • Polymyalgia rheumatica: Characterised by aching and morning stiffness in the shoulder and pelvic girdles in patients over 50, often with a dramatic response to steroids.
  • Systemic Lupus Erythematosus: A multi-system autoimmune disease that can present with a non-erosive, inflammatory arthritis, often accompanied by skin rashes, fatigue, and other organ involvement.
  • Scleroderma: A connective tissue disease causing fibrosis of the skin and internal organs, which can be associated with joint pain, stiffness, and contractures.
  • Polymyositis: An inflammatory myopathy causing progressive, symmetrical proximal muscle weakness, which can also feature arthralgia or arthritis.
  • Metastatic cancer: Can present with persistent, localised bone pain that is worse at night and not relieved by rest, sometimes involving adjacent joints.
  • Bone tumours: Primary bone tumours can cause deep, boring pain, often at night, with or without a palpable mass or pathological fracture near a joint.
  • Leukaemia: Can present with bone pain and arthralgia due to leukaemic infiltration of the bone marrow or synovium, often with systemic symptoms like fever and fatigue.
  • Lymphoma: May cause joint pain through direct infiltration, paraneoplastic syndromes, or bone involvement, often associated with lymphadenopathy and constitutional symptoms.
  • HIV: Acute seroconversion can present with an arthralgia/myalgia syndrome, and chronic infection is associated with various rheumatological manifestations.
  • Tuberculosis: Can cause a chronic, indolent, monoarticular arthritis (especially in large, weight-bearing joints) with constitutional symptoms like weight loss and night sweats.
  • Rheumatic fever: An inflammatory reaction to a streptococcal infection, presenting with a migratory polyarthritis, carditis, and other features, primarily in children and young adults.
  • Infective endocarditis: May present with arthralgia and myalgia as part of the systemic inflammatory response to a cardiac valve infection.
  • Compartment syndrome: An orthopaedic emergency where increased pressure within a fascial compartment compromises circulation, causing severe pain out of proportion to the injury, which can be near a joint after trauma.
Common causes
  • Osteoarthritis: A degenerative joint disease causing activity-related pain, brief morning stiffness, and bony enlargement, typically in weight-bearing joints and the hands.
  • Fibromyalgia: A chronic pain syndrome characterised by widespread musculoskeletal pain, fatigue, sleep disturbance, and multiple tender points, but without true joint inflammation.
  • Gout: An acute, intensely painful inflammatory arthritis, classically affecting the first metatarsophalangeal joint, caused by the deposition of urate crystals.
  • Psoriatic arthritis: An inflammatory arthritis associated with psoriasis, which can affect peripheral joints and the spine in various patterns.
  • Reactive arthritis: An inflammatory arthritis that develops following a bacterial infection elsewhere in the body (e.g., gastrointestinal or urogenital).
  • Viral infection: Many viruses (e.g., Parvovirus B19, Rubella) can cause a self-limiting, acute polyarthritis or arthralgia.
  • COVID-19: Can be associated with a range of musculoskeletal symptoms, including arthralgia, myalgia, and a reactive inflammatory arthritis.
Easily missed
  • Medication-induced Arthropathy: Joint pain or arthritis can be a side effect of various drugs, such as fluoroquinolones, diuretics, or immune checkpoint inhibitors.
  • Spondyloarthropathies: A group of inflammatory diseases affecting the spine (spondylitis) and peripheral joints (arthritis), including ankylosing spondylitis and reactive arthritis.
  • Ankylosing spondylitis: A chronic inflammatory disease primarily affecting the axial skeleton, causing back pain, stiffness, and eventual fusion of the spine, often starting in young adulthood.
  • Crohn's disease: A type of inflammatory bowel disease that can be associated with peripheral arthritis or axial spondyloarthropathy.
  • Ulcerative colitis: An inflammatory bowel disease that, like Crohn's, can have associated inflammatory arthritis as an extra-intestinal manifestation.
  • Reiter's syndrome: The classic triad of arthritis, urethritis, and conjunctivitis, now more commonly known as reactive arthritis.
  • Lyme disease: A tick-borne bacterial infection that can cause a migratory arthritis, often affecting the knee, weeks to months after the initial infection.
  • Sarcoidosis: A multisystem granulomatous disease that can present with an acute inflammatory arthritis (Lofgren's syndrome) or a chronic arthropathy.
Other Differential Diagnoses to consider
  • Haemochromatosis: Iron overload leading to joint damage and arthropathy
  • Wilson’s disease: Copper accumulation causing arthropathy
  • Hyperparathyroidism: Increased parathyroid activity leading to joint pain
  • Haemophilia: Bleeding disorders causing haemarthrosis and joint damage
  • Acromegaly: Hormonal disorder causing joint hypertrophy and pain
  • Behçet’s disease: Multisystem inflammatory condition with joint involvement
  • Post-traumatic arthritis: Arthropathy following injury
  • Ehlers-Danlos syndrome: Connective tissue disorder with hypermobility and joint pain
  • COVID-19-associated arthropathy: Inflammatory arthropathy associated with COVID-19 infection
History Taking
Patient’s Description: How can I help you today?

Starting with an open-ended question, such as: "Can you tell me more about what brought you here today?"

Relevance: Allows the patient to describe the issue in their own words, offering clues for further targeted questions and narrowing the differential diagnosis.

Can you describe the pain or discomfort?
  • This helps characterise the quality of the pain: e.g., sharp, dull, throbbing, or burning.
  • Relevance: - Sharp pain may suggest gout or acute trauma. - A dull, aching pain is more consistent with osteoarthritis. - Burning discomfort could indicate nerve involvement.
When did the symptoms start, and how have they progressed?
  • Understanding onset and progression (acute, subacute, or chronic) is key to differentiating causes.
  • Relevance: - Sudden onset pain suggests gout or septic arthritis. - Gradual worsening over months points to rheumatoid arthritis or osteoarthritis. - Episodic pain with resolution suggests gout or pseudogout.
Are there any specific triggers or factors that make it better or worse?
  • Identifies exacerbating and relieving factors, providing diagnostic clues.
  • Relevance: - Pain triggered by activity and relieved with rest suggests osteoarthritis. - Night pain that wakes the patient could indicate malignancy or septic arthritis. - Pain relief with NSAIDs supports inflammatory arthritis.
Have you noticed swelling, redness, or warmth in the joints?
  • Evaluates signs of inflammation or infection in the joints.
  • Relevance: - Swelling and redness strongly suggest inflammatory conditions like gout or septic arthritis. - Absence of these may indicate fibromyalgia or early osteoarthritis.
Do you have any other associated symptoms?
  • Examples include fever, weight loss, fatigue, or morning stiffness.
  • Relevance: - Fever may indicate septic arthritis or rheumatic fever. - Weight loss and fatigue raise suspicion for malignancy or systemic conditions (e.g., SLE, lymphoma). - Prolonged morning stiffness is typical of rheumatoid arthritis or ankylosing spondylitis.
Do you have any significant medical, family, or medication history?
  • Explore past medical conditions, family predispositions, and drug history.
  • Relevance: - A history of psoriasis suggests psoriatic arthritis. - Family history of autoimmune conditions (e.g., RA, SLE) is significant. - Diuretics or alcohol use increases gout risk.
Ideas, Concerns, and Expectations (ICE)
  • Helps address the patient’s perspective, fears, and what they hope to achieve from the consultation.
  • Relevance: - Patients may worry about serious conditions like cancer or infection. - Understanding their expectations helps align treatment plans.
Examination Strategy
General Inspection
  • Inspect the patient for overall appearance, signs of systemic disease (e.g., pallor, cachexia), or distress.
  • Relevance: - Pallor may indicate anaemia (e.g., leukaemia or chronic disease). - Cachexia and weight loss suggest malignancy. - Joint deformities or swelling visible during inspection suggest chronic inflammatory arthritis (e.g., rheumatoid arthritis) or osteoarthritis.
Inspection of Joints
  • Inspect for swelling, redness, deformities, asymmetry, and skin changes over joints.
  • Relevance: - Redness and swelling suggest active inflammation (e.g., septic arthritis, gout, or rheumatoid arthritis). - Nodules over the joints (e.g., Heberden’s nodes in osteoarthritis or rheumatoid nodules). - Skin changes such as psoriatic plaques suggest psoriatic arthritis.
Palpation of Joints
  • Palpate for warmth, tenderness, effusion, and joint margins.
  • Relevance: - Warmth suggests active inflammation (e.g., septic arthritis or gout). - Tenderness on joint line palpation is typical of osteoarthritis or inflammatory arthritis. - Effusion may indicate joint infection, inflammation, or haemarthrosis.
Range of Motion (ROM) Testing
  • Assess active and passive ROM in affected joints.
  • Relevance: - Reduced active ROM with preserved passive ROM may suggest soft tissue injury. - Both active and passive ROM restriction suggests intra-articular pathology (e.g., osteoarthritis, inflammatory arthritis, or septic arthritis). - Pain on movement is characteristic of gout, infection, or trauma.
Gait Assessment
  • Observe the patient walking to assess gait abnormalities.
  • Relevance: - Antalgic gait indicates pain (e.g., osteoarthritis or fracture). - Trendelenburg gait suggests hip pathology. - Waddling gait could indicate muscular or neurological involvement (e.g., polymyositis).
Special Tests
  • Perform joint-specific tests, such as: - McMurray’s test for meniscal tears in the knee. - Phalen’s and Tinel’s tests for carpal tunnel syndrome. - Schober’s test for ankylosing spondylitis.
  • Relevance: - Positive McMurray’s suggests meniscal injury. - Positive Phalen’s or Tinel’s supports a diagnosis of carpal tunnel syndrome. - Reduced spinal flexion in Schober’s test is seen in ankylosing spondylitis.
Red Flag Synthesis
Fever with acute joint pain
  • Fever in the context of joint pain may indicate septic arthritis or osteomyelitis, both of which require urgent intervention to prevent joint destruction or systemic sepsis.
  • Significance: This could indicate septic arthritis, an orthopaedic emergency requiring immediate drainage and antibiotics.
Unexplained weight loss
  • Unintentional weight loss raises suspicion for malignancy (e.g., bone metastases, leukaemia, or lymphoma) or systemic disease like tuberculosis or HIV.
  • Significance: This could indicate cancer, chronic infection, or another systemic condition requiring prompt investigation.
Night pain that does not improve with rest
  • Persistent night pain unrelieved by rest may suggest malignancy (e.g., bone tumours) or an aggressive infection.
  • Significance: This could indicate bone malignancy or deep infection, both of which need urgent evaluation.
Rapid onset of joint swelling with severe pain
  • Sudden joint swelling and severe pain might indicate gout, pseudogout, or septic arthritis, all of which need different urgent treatments.
  • Significance: This could indicate septic arthritis or acute crystal arthropathy requiring timely intervention.
Neurological symptoms (e.g., weakness or numbness)
  • Neurological symptoms associated with joint pain could signify spinal cord compression or peripheral nerve involvement, as seen in ankylosing spondylitis or severe disc herniation.
  • Significance: This could indicate cord compression or severe inflammatory processes, requiring urgent neurological assessment.
Skin rashes or ulcers associated with joint pain
  • Skin changes such as rashes, ulcers, or necrosis may suggest systemic lupus erythematosus, vasculitis, or reactive arthritis.
  • Significance: This could indicate autoimmune disease or vasculitis, which may require urgent immunosuppressive therapy.
Investigations Strategy & Interpretation
Blood tests: Full blood count (FBC)
  • Helps detect anaemia, infection, or haematological malignancies.
  • Result Significance: - Raised white cell count may suggest infection (e.g., septic arthritis). - Anaemia can point towards chronic disease (e.g., rheumatoid arthritis) or malignancy. - Abnormalities like blasts suggest leukaemia.
Erythrocyte sedimentation rate (ESR) and C-reactive protein (CRP)
  • Markers of inflammation, helping differentiate inflammatory from non-inflammatory conditions.
  • Result Significance: - Elevated ESR/CRP indicates active inflammation (e.g., rheumatoid arthritis, SLE, or septic arthritis). - Normal levels make active inflammation less likely, favouring non-inflammatory conditions like osteoarthritis.
Rheumatoid factor (RF) and anti-cyclic citrullinated peptide (anti-CCP)
  • Tests for autoimmune conditions, particularly rheumatoid arthritis.
  • Result Significance: - Positive RF/anti-CCP is highly suggestive of rheumatoid arthritis. - Negative results do not exclude the diagnosis, especially in early disease or seronegative rheumatoid arthritis.
Uric acid levels
  • Used to diagnose gout by detecting hyperuricaemia.
  • Result Significance: - Elevated uric acid supports a diagnosis of gout. - Normal levels do not exclude gout, as levels can be normal during acute attacks.
Joint aspiration and synovial fluid analysis
  • Provides direct analysis for infection, crystals, or inflammatory markers in the joint.
  • Result Significance: - Purulent fluid with high white cell count suggests septic arthritis. - Monosodium urate crystals indicate gout; calcium pyrophosphate crystals indicate pseudogout. - Clear fluid with low white cell count is typical of osteoarthritis.
X-rays of the affected joint
  • Imaging reveals structural changes in the joint, such as erosion, calcification, or osteophytes.
  • Result Significance: - Joint space narrowing and osteophytes suggest osteoarthritis. - Bone erosions point towards rheumatoid arthritis. - Chondrocalcinosis (calcium deposits) is seen in pseudogout.
MRI or ultrasound of the joint
  • Advanced imaging for soft tissue and early joint changes not visible on X-ray.
  • Result Significance: - Synovitis and erosions on MRI suggest rheumatoid arthritis. - Effusions and synovial thickening seen in infection or inflammatory arthritis. - Ultrasound can detect crystal deposits in gout and pseudogout.
Autoimmune panel (ANA, anti-dsDNA)
  • Useful for diagnosing systemic autoimmune diseases like lupus or scleroderma.
  • Result Significance: - Positive ANA with anti-dsDNA supports a diagnosis of systemic lupus erythematosus. - Negative results reduce the likelihood of autoimmune involvement.
Infectious disease testing (e.g., blood cultures, HIV test, Lyme serology)
  • Identifies infections that can cause joint symptoms.
  • Result Significance: - Positive blood cultures indicate septic arthritis or infective endocarditis. - Positive Lyme serology confirms Lyme disease. - HIV testing is important in unexplained arthritis with immunosuppression.
Evidence-Based Diagnosis (EBDx)
Fever with acute joint pain
  • Fever in the context of joint pain is a strong indicator of septic arthritis, an emergency condition requiring prompt diagnosis.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 69–100%, Specificity: 94%, Likelihood Ratio: 11.5 for fever in septic arthritis (based on studies of acute monoarthritis).
  • Reference: Margaretten ME, Kohlwes J, Moore D, Bent S. "Does this adult patient have septic arthritis?" JAMA. 2007.
  • Significance: A positive finding strongly suggests septic arthritis, while absence of fever does not entirely exclude it.
Elevated serum uric acid levels
  • Hyperuricaemia is often associated with gout, but normal levels during an acute attack can occur.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 43%, Specificity: 88%, Likelihood Ratio: 3.58 for gout when uric acid levels are >6 mg/dL.
  • Reference: Khanna D, Fitzgerald JD, Khanna PP, et al. "2012 American College of Rheumatology guidelines for gout management." Arthritis Care Res (Hoboken). 2012.
  • Significance: Elevated levels support gout, but normal values cannot rule it out, emphasising the need for joint aspiration.
Joint aspiration with detection of monosodium urate crystals
  • Joint fluid analysis for crystals is the gold standard for diagnosing gout.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 84%, Specificity: 100%, Likelihood Ratio: Very high for monosodium urate crystals in synovial fluid.
  • Reference: Malik A, Schumacher HR, Dinnella JE. "Clinical implications of the monosodium urate crystal findings in synovial fluid." Rheumatology. 2009.
  • Significance: A positive result confirms gout, while absence of crystals may necessitate further investigation for pseudogout or septic arthritis.
Morning stiffness lasting >30 minutes
  • Prolonged morning stiffness is a hallmark symptom of rheumatoid arthritis and other inflammatory arthropathies.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 71%, Specificity: 87%, Likelihood Ratio: 5.46 for rheumatoid arthritis when stiffness lasts >30 minutes.
  • Reference: Aletaha D, Neogi T, Silman AJ, et al. "2010 Rheumatoid Arthritis Classification Criteria." Arthritis Rheum. 2010.
  • Significance: This symptom supports inflammatory arthritis, helping distinguish it from osteoarthritis, where morning stiffness is shorter (<15 minutes).
Anti-cyclic citrullinated peptide (anti-CCP) antibody
  • Anti-CCP antibodies are highly specific markers for rheumatoid arthritis, often detected early in the disease.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 67%, Specificity: 95%, Likelihood Ratio: 13.4 for rheumatoid arthritis when anti-CCP antibodies are positive.
  • Reference: Nishimura K, Sugiyama D, Kogata Y, et al. "Meta-analysis: diagnostic accuracy of anti-CCP antibody and rheumatoid factor for rheumatoid arthritis." Ann Intern Med. 2007.
  • Significance: A positive anti-CCP test strongly supports rheumatoid arthritis, while a negative test reduces the likelihood but does not exclude the diagnosis.
Plain X-rays showing joint space narrowing and osteophytes
  • X-ray findings are typical for osteoarthritis and help differentiate it from inflammatory arthropathies.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 67–73%, Specificity: 80–91% for osteoarthritis when joint space narrowing and osteophytes are observed.
  • Reference: Altman RD, Asch E, Bloch DA, et al. "Development of criteria for the classification and reporting of osteoarthritis." Arthritis Rheum. 1986.
  • Significance: These radiological changes support a diagnosis of osteoarthritis, ruling out purely inflammatory conditions like rheumatoid arthritis.
Diagnostic Timeout & Cognitive Biases

Before finalising a diagnosis for arthropathy, it is crucial to pause and reflect on potential cognitive errors. The complexity and overlap of symptoms make this field particularly susceptible to diagnostic bias.

  • Anchoring Bias: This is the tendency to fixate on initial information. For example, attributing a hot, swollen knee in an elderly patient to their known osteoarthritis, thereby delaying the diagnosis of a superimposed septic arthritis. A key question to ask is: "Could there be a second, more serious pathology occurring on top of the known condition?"
  • Confirmation Bias: This involves seeking or favouring information that confirms a preconceived diagnosis. For instance, focusing on a normal serum uric acid level to rule out gout in a patient with a classic history of an acute attack, while ignoring the fact that uric acid can be normal or low during a flare.
  • Atypical Presentations: Forgetting that systemic diseases often present in non-classical ways is a major pitfall. For example, rheumatoid arthritis can present as a monoarthritis initially, and Systemic Lupus Erythematosus (SLE) might present solely with joint pain long before other systemic features appear. A high index of suspicion is needed for any new inflammatory arthritis.
  • Over-reliance on Normal Tests: Placing undue confidence in negative initial tests can be misleading. For instance, concluding a patient does not have inflammatory arthritis because their initial CRP and ESR are normal, or ruling out rheumatoid arthritis based on a negative rheumatoid factor test alone (seronegative RA). Autoimmune serology and inflammatory markers can take time to become positive.
Illustrative Management Pathways

The following pathways describe the principles of management for key diagnoses and are for educational purposes only. All management must be guided by official UK guidelines and senior clinical advice.

Septic Arthritis

This is a medical and orthopaedic emergency. The management priority is joint preservation and prevention of sepsis. The pathway involves immediate hospital admission, urgent joint aspiration for microscopy, culture, and sensitivities (before starting antibiotics if possible), and empirical intravenous antibiotics targeted at likely organisms (e.g., flucloxacillin). Urgent surgical washout (arthroscopy or arthrotomy) is often required to debride the joint. Management is a joint effort between medical and orthopaedic teams.

New Onset Inflammatory Arthritis (e.g., Rheumatoid Arthritis)

The goal is to control inflammation rapidly to prevent joint damage and preserve function. Patients with suspected new-onset inflammatory arthritis require urgent referral to a rheumatologist (as per NICE guidelines). While awaiting specialist review, management involves symptomatic relief with analgesia (e.g., paracetamol) and possibly NSAIDs (with gastric protection). The rheumatology team will confirm the diagnosis and initiate long-term management, typically with Disease-Modifying Antirheumatic Drugs (DMARDs) like methotrexate, and sometimes short courses of corticosteroids as a bridge.

Acute Gout

Management of an acute flare focuses on rapid pain relief and settling inflammation. First-line options include high-dose NSAIDs (e.g., naproxen), colchicine, or oral/intramuscular corticosteroids, chosen based on patient comorbidities. Rest and elevation of the affected joint are also advised. Long-term management, initiated after the acute flare has settled, focuses on urate-lowering therapy (e.g., allopurinol) and lifestyle advice regarding diet, alcohol, and weight management to prevent recurrence.

Osteoarthritis

After excluding more serious pathology, management is centred on controlling symptoms and improving function. The approach is multifactorial and stepped. Core treatments include patient education, lifestyle advice (especially weight loss if overweight), and therapeutic exercise (physiotherapy). First-line analgesia is paracetamol and/or topical NSAIDs. If needed, oral NSAIDs or weak opioids can be considered. For severe, debilitating symptoms refractory to conservative measures, referral for consideration of joint replacement surgery is appropriate.

Reflection & Learning

These brief cases illustrate the application of clinical reasoning to different arthropathy presentations. They are for educational purposes only.

Case 1: Suspected Septic Arthritis in the Medical Admissions Unit

An 82-year-old woman with known osteoarthritis of the knees is admitted with a 24-hour history of a severely painful, hot, and swollen right knee. She is unable to bear weight and has a temperature of 38.4°C. The problem representation is an elderly patient with a pre-existing joint vulnerability presenting with an acute, inflammatory monoarthritis and fever. Despite her known OA, the red flags for septic arthritis are present. The immediate pathway involves keeping her nil by mouth, obtaining urgent orthopaedic review, performing blood cultures, and preparing for an emergency joint aspiration and washout, followed by IV antibiotics.

Case 2: Suspected Inflammatory Arthritis in General Practice

A 34-year-old woman presents to her GP with a 6-week history of pain and swelling in the small joints of both hands and wrists. She reports feeling stiff for over an hour every morning and profound fatigue. Examination reveals soft, 'boggy' swelling of her metacarpophalangeal joints. The problem representation is a young woman with a symmetrical, inflammatory polyarthritis affecting small joints with prolonged morning stiffness, highly suspicious for new-onset rheumatoid arthritis. The GP performs baseline bloods including inflammatory markers and autoimmune screening (RF, anti-CCP) and makes an urgent referral to the local Early Arthritis Clinic as per NICE guidelines.

Case 3: Acute Gout in the Walk-in Centre

A 55-year-old man presents with a 12-hour history of excruciating pain in his right big toe. He woke from sleep with the pain and cannot even tolerate the weight of a bedsheet on it. The joint is visibly red, swollen, and shiny. He notes he was at a party with steak and red wine the night before. The problem representation is a middle-aged man with a classic history and risk factors for an acute crystal arthropathy. After excluding signs of systemic infection or trauma, a diagnosis of acute gout is made clinically. He is prescribed a course of high-dose naproxen (with a PPI) and given advice about lifestyle modification and the need for follow-up with his GP to discuss long-term urate-lowering therapy once the flare has settled.
