Haematuria Diagnostic Reasoning Reference
Context: Haematuria Assessment
Overview & Links to Key Guidelines

Haematuria, the presence of blood in the urine, is a common and important clinical sign that demands careful evaluation. It can be categorised as visible (macroscopic or 'gross') or non-visible (microscopic), with the former often being more alarming to the patient and clinician. The diagnostic challenge lies in its wide range of causes, from benign and self-limiting conditions like a simple urinary tract infection (UTI) to life-threatening malignancies such as bladder or renal cancer. A systematic and logical approach is therefore crucial to differentiate serious underlying pathology from more common, less urgent causes.

The initial assessment must stratify the patient's risk, particularly focusing on red flags for malignancy. This educational module provides a foundational framework for the clinical reasoning process in a patient presenting with haematuria. For definitive, real-time management protocols, clinicians must consult official UK guidelines, primarily from NICE, including 'Suspected cancer: recognition and referral' (NG12) and guidelines on urinary tract infections.

Pathophysiology/Basic Science

Understanding the origin of haematuria is fundamental to formulating a differential diagnosis. Blood can enter the urinary tract at any point from the glomerulus to the urethral meatus, and the underlying mechanism provides clues to the aetiology:

  • Glomerular Bleeding: This occurs when inflammation or damage to the glomerular basement membrane (as seen in glomerulonephritis) permits red blood cells (RBCs) to pass from the bloodstream into the renal tubule. As these RBCs traverse the nephron, they are often misshapen by osmotic and enzymatic stresses, becoming 'dysmorphic'. They may also form clumps within the tubules, leading to the pathognomonic finding of 'red cell casts' in the urine sediment. This typically results in brownish, 'cola-coloured' urine.
  • Non-Glomerular (Urological) Bleeding: This arises from pathology within the urinary tract lining (urothelium) of the renal pelvis, ureters, bladder, or urethra. The RBCs are of normal shape ('isomorphic') as they have not passed through the nephron. Common mechanisms include:
    • Neoplasia: Malignant tumours (e.g., bladder or renal cell carcinoma) develop fragile new blood vessels (neoangiogenesis) that are prone to rupture and bleed. Ulceration of the tumour surface is another common source of bleeding.
    • Infection/Inflammation: The inflammation associated with a urinary tract infection (e.g., cystitis, prostatitis) causes the mucosal lining to become hyperaemic and friable, leading to bleeding.
    • Urolithiasis (Stones): A urinary tract stone can cause direct mechanical trauma to the urothelium as it moves or lodges within the tract, causing bleeding and often significant pain.
    • Trauma: Direct blunt or penetrating trauma to the abdomen, flank, or pelvis can injure the kidneys, bladder, or urethra, leading to haematuria.
Differential Diagnosis (DDx) Framework

A structured approach to the differential diagnosis of haematuria is essential to ensure life-threatening conditions are prioritised.

Must not miss!
  • Renal carcinoma: Often presents with the classic triad of haematuria, flank pain, and a palpable mass, though is now frequently detected incidentally on imaging.
  • Bladder cancer: Typically causes painless, visible haematuria, particularly in older adults with a history of smoking.
  • Prostate cancer: May present with haematuria, although lower urinary tract symptoms like hesitancy and frequency are more common presentations.
  • Urethritis: Inflammation of the urethra, often due to an STI, presenting with dysuria and potentially a bloody discharge or haematuria.
  • Gonorrhoeae: A sexually transmitted infection that is a common cause of urethritis and can lead to associated haematuria and purulent discharge.
  • Chlamydia: Another common STI causing urethritis, which can present with dysuria, discharge, and occasionally haematuria.
  • Prostatitis: Inflammation or infection of the prostate gland can cause pain, significant urinary symptoms, and haematuria.
  • Urinary tract Tuberculosis: A rare cause in the UK that should be considered in at-risk populations, often presenting with sterile pyuria and constitutional symptoms.
  • Infective endocarditis: Can cause haematuria via the deposition of immune complexes in the glomeruli, leading to an acute glomerulonephritis.
  • Glomerulonephritis: Inflammation of the kidney's filtering units, characteristically presenting with 'smoky' or 'cola-coloured' urine, proteinuria, hypertension, and oedema.
  • Renovascular disease: Conditions such as renal artery stenosis or thrombosis can, on rare occasions, lead to haematuria due to renal ischaemia or infarction.
Common causes
  • UTI: A bacterial infection of the bladder (cystitis), typically causing dysuria, frequency, urgency, and haematuria.
  • Pyelonephritis: An infection of the kidney itself, presenting more systemically with fever, loin pain, and vomiting, in addition to haematuria.
  • Urinary tract stone: Characterised by severe, colicky flank pain that may radiate to the groin, often accompanied by visible haematuria.
  • Prostatitis: Inflammation of the prostate, which can be acute or chronic, causing pain and urinary symptoms including haematuria.
  • Menstruation (contaminating urine): A frequent cause of a positive urine dipstick for blood in women; it represents contamination rather than true haematuria.
  • Trauma-induced Haematuria: Results from direct injury to the kidneys, ureters, or bladder, often in the context of pelvic fractures or abdominal trauma.
  • COVID-19: Has been associated with a range of renal complications, including acute kidney injury and haematuria.
Easily missed
  • Medication-induced Haematuria: Can be caused by anticoagulants (e.g., warfarin, DOACs) unmasking a lesion, or by drugs like cyclophosphamide causing haemorrhagic cystitis.
  • Exercise-induced Haematuria: A benign and self-limiting condition that can occur after strenuous physical activity, thought to be due to minor bladder trauma.
  • PCKD: Autosomal Dominant Polycystic Kidney Disease is an inherited condition where cyst rupture, infection, or bleeding can cause episodes of haematuria.
  • HSP: Henoch-Schönlein purpura is a systemic vasculitis, primarily in children, that can cause glomerulonephritis with haematuria alongside its characteristic rash.
Other Differential Diagnoses to consider
  • COVID-19 related haematuria: Potential renal involvement leading to haematuria in the context of systemic infection.
  • Benign prostatic hyperplasia (BPH): Common in older males, may cause haematuria due to enlarged prostate and irritation.
  • Endometriosis: Endometrial tissue within the urinary tract can present with cyclical haematuria.
  • Vasculitis (e.g., ANCA-associated): Inflammation of blood vessels can lead to renal involvement and haematuria.
  • Schistosomiasis: Chronic infection causing bladder inflammation and haematuria, endemic in certain regions.
  • Hypercalciuria or hyperuricosuria: Metabolic abnormalities causing microscopic or macroscopic haematuria.
  • Sickle cell disease or trait: Renal papillary necrosis or microvascular occlusion can lead to haematuria.
  • Radiation cystitis: Post-radiotherapy, bladder inflammation can lead to haematuria.
History Taking
Patient’s Description: How can I help you today?
Asking an open-ended question such as, "Can you tell me more about what brought you here today?" invites the patient to explain their symptoms in their own words. Relevance: This approach provides insight into the primary concerns and the patient’s perception of the symptom, guiding the focus of the consultation.
Onset, duration, and frequency of haematuria
  • Ask: “When did you first notice the blood in your urine?” and “Has it been constant or does it come and go?”
  • Relevance: Acute onset could suggest trauma, infection, or stones, while chronic or intermittent haematuria may point towards malignancy, benign prostatic hyperplasia, or glomerulonephritis.
Colour and quantity of blood
  • Ask: “Is the blood bright red, or is the urine tea-coloured or smoky?” and “Is it a small amount or quite a lot?”
  • Relevance: Bright red blood typically suggests a lower urinary tract source (e.g., bladder or urethra), whereas dark or brownish urine may indicate glomerular disease.
Pain associated with haematuria
  • Ask: “Do you experience any pain when you notice blood in the urine?” or “Do you have any back or abdominal pain?”
  • Relevance: Painful haematuria suggests infection, stones, or trauma, while painless haematuria could indicate malignancy or a vascular cause.
Associated symptoms: Urinary frequency, urgency, dysuria
  • Ask: “Have you noticed any changes in how often you urinate, an urgent need to go, or any pain when passing urine?”
  • Relevance: These symptoms are highly indicative of a urinary tract infection or bladder pathology; their absence may direct the differential away from infectious causes.
Systemic symptoms: Fever, weight loss, night sweats
  • Ask: “Have you had any fevers, unexplained weight loss, or night sweats?”
  • Relevance: Fever might indicate infection (e.g., pyelonephritis or endocarditis). Weight loss and night sweats could suggest malignancy or systemic diseases like tuberculosis.
History of similar episodes
  • Ask: “Have you had episodes like this in the past?”
  • Relevance: Recurrent haematuria might indicate a chronic or recurring condition such as nephritis, polycystic kidney disease, or benign prostatic hyperplasia.
Medication history
  • Ask: “Are you on any medications, particularly blood thinners or pain relievers?”
  • Relevance: Certain medications (e.g., anticoagulants, NSAIDs) can cause haematuria or worsen bleeding, suggesting a potential medication-related cause.
Past medical history and risk factors
  • Ask: “Do you have any history of kidney disease, bladder issues, or conditions like hypertension or diabetes?”
  • Relevance: A history of renal or systemic diseases, as well as cardiovascular risk factors, may suggest glomerular causes, renovascular disease, or chronic kidney conditions.
Family history of renal disease or cancer
  • Ask: “Is there any family history of kidney disease, polycystic kidney disease, or cancer?”
  • Relevance: Family history can highlight inherited conditions like polycystic kidney disease or a genetic predisposition to certain cancers.
Ideas, Concerns, and Expectations (ICE)
  • Ask: “What do you think might be causing this?”, “Is there something specific you’re worried about?”, and “What were you hoping we could help with today?”
  • Relevance: Understanding the patient’s perspective and addressing any underlying fears (such as cancer or serious kidney disease) can improve communication and ensure that their expectations for the consultation are managed appropriately.
Examination Strategy
General Inspection
  • Observe for signs of pallor, jaundice, oedema, cachexia, or any visible distress.
  • Relevance: Pallor may indicate anaemia (secondary to chronic blood loss or renal disease), jaundice may suggest hepatic involvement or haemolysis, and oedema could be a sign of nephrotic syndrome or glomerulonephritis. Cachexia might raise suspicion of malignancy.
Vital Signs
  • Assess temperature, blood pressure, pulse, and respiratory rate.
  • Relevance: Fever suggests infection (e.g., pyelonephritis or endocarditis), while elevated blood pressure could indicate glomerulonephritis or chronic kidney disease. Tachycardia might be present with significant blood loss, and abnormal respiratory rate may suggest metabolic acidosis in severe kidney disease.
Abdominal Examination
  • Inspect and palpate for masses, tenderness, or distension, especially in the suprapubic and flank regions.
  • Relevance: Suprapubic tenderness may suggest cystitis, while flank tenderness can indicate pyelonephritis or renal stones. Palpable abdominal or flank masses might suggest a renal or bladder tumour, polycystic kidney disease, or hydronephrosis.
Percussion of the Kidneys
  • Gently percuss over the costovertebral angles to elicit tenderness.
  • Relevance: Costovertebral angle tenderness (positive Murphy’s punch sign) is a hallmark of pyelonephritis or acute renal obstruction (e.g., from a stone).
Genitourinary Examination
  • Examine external genitalia and perform a digital rectal examination (DRE) in males if prostatitis or prostate pathology is suspected.
  • Relevance: In males, a tender or enlarged prostate on DRE suggests prostatitis or benign prostatic hyperplasia. Genital inspection may reveal urethritis or trauma that could account for haematuria.
Assessment for Peripheral Oedema
  • Check for bilateral lower limb oedema.
  • Relevance: Peripheral oedema may indicate nephrotic syndrome, glomerulonephritis, or chronic kidney disease, as these conditions can lead to hypoalbuminaemia or fluid retention.
Skin Examination
  • Inspect for any rashes, purpura, or other skin lesions.
  • Relevance: Palpable purpura, especially on the lower limbs, could suggest Henoch-Schönlein purpura (HSP) or another form of vasculitis associated with renal involvement. Malar rash might indicate systemic lupus erythematosus, a potential cause of glomerulonephritis.
Cardiac Examination
  • Auscultate for any murmurs or signs of infective endocarditis.
  • Relevance: Heart murmurs or other signs of infective endocarditis, such as Janeway lesions or Osler nodes, may explain haematuria due to renal emboli or glomerulonephritis.
Red Flag Synthesis
Painless Haematuria
  • Painless haematuria, especially in older adults, can be a warning sign of malignancy within the urinary tract, particularly bladder cancer or renal carcinoma.
  • Significance: This could indicate underlying cancer in the urinary system and warrants urgent imaging and urological assessment.
Visible Haematuria with Clots
  • Haematuria with clots may suggest significant bleeding from the urinary tract, often associated with tumours, severe infections, or trauma.
  • Significance: This can indicate serious pathology such as bladder cancer, requiring prompt evaluation with cystoscopy and imaging to assess the source of bleeding.
Constitutional Symptoms (Weight Loss, Night Sweats, Fever)
  • Unintentional weight loss, persistent night sweats, and fever suggest a possible systemic disease, including malignancy or chronic infections like tuberculosis.
  • Significance: These symptoms raise suspicion for cancer or chronic infection such as renal tuberculosis and necessitate comprehensive investigation.
Severe or Persistent Flank Pain
  • Severe flank pain is often associated with renal stones but could also signify obstruction, infection, or tumour in the kidneys or ureters.
  • Significance: Persistent or severe pain may indicate obstructive uropathy or pyelonephritis, both of which may require urgent imaging and management to prevent kidney damage.
New-Onset Hypertension with Haematuria
  • New or worsening hypertension in the presence of haematuria could indicate glomerulonephritis, renovascular disease, or chronic kidney disease.
  • Significance: This finding is concerning for renal pathology requiring urgent nephrology input to prevent renal function deterioration.
Oedema (Especially in the Lower Limbs)
  • Oedema, particularly when accompanied by haematuria, suggests nephrotic syndrome or advanced glomerulonephritis, where kidney function is significantly compromised.
  • Significance: This may indicate nephrotic syndrome or severe kidney disease, necessitating urgent workup to assess renal function and manage complications.
Signs of Infection (e.g., Fever, Malaise, Rigors)
  • Fever and systemic signs of infection in a patient with haematuria suggest a serious urinary tract infection or possible urosepsis, particularly if associated with flank pain.
  • Significance: This could indicate pyelonephritis or urosepsis, which requires prompt antibiotic therapy and potential hospitalisation for intravenous antibiotics and monitoring.
Haematuria in Patients on Anticoagulant Therapy
  • Haematuria in anticoagulated patients may indicate a serious bleeding complication or an underlying lesion such as a tumour that is exacerbated by the anticoagulant.
  • Significance: This could signify uncontrolled bleeding or expose an occult malignancy. Urgent review of anticoagulation therapy and imaging is recommended.
Investigations Strategy & Interpretation
Urine Dipstick and Microscopy
  • A urine dipstick is a quick, initial test for detecting haematuria and any associated proteinuria, leukocytes, or nitrites.
  • Result Significance: Presence of blood confirms haematuria, while associated findings like nitrites and leukocytes suggest infection. Proteinuria may indicate a glomerular cause, and the absence of haematuria on microscopy could imply haemoglobinuria or myoglobinuria instead.
Urine Culture
  • This test checks for bacterial growth to confirm or rule out urinary tract infection as a cause of haematuria.
  • Result Significance: A positive result indicates UTI, requiring antibiotic treatment, whereas a negative result helps rule out infection and shifts the differential towards other causes like stones or malignancy.
Urine Cytology
  • Urine cytology assesses for malignant cells in patients with haematuria, especially if risk factors for bladder cancer are present (e.g., age over 40, smoking history).
  • Result Significance: Positive cytology suggests bladder or upper tract malignancy, while negative cytology does not entirely exclude cancer, often prompting further evaluation.
Renal Function Tests (e.g., Urea, Creatinine, Electrolytes)
  • These blood tests evaluate kidney function and can identify underlying renal disease contributing to haematuria.
  • Result Significance: Abnormal results, such as elevated creatinine, suggest renal impairment or chronic kidney disease, while normal results do not rule out renal or urological causes.
Full Blood Count (FBC)
  • An FBC can help identify anaemia due to chronic blood loss and signs of infection or inflammation.
  • Result Significance: Low haemoglobin may indicate anaemia secondary to blood loss, while raised white cell count suggests infection. A normal FBC may reduce suspicion of systemic or infectious causes.
Coagulation Profile
  • This test assesses the coagulation status, especially in patients taking anticoagulants or with signs of coagulopathy.
  • Result Significance: Abnormal results suggest a bleeding disorder that could exacerbate haematuria, while normal findings allow consideration of other structural causes.
Renal Ultrasound
  • Renal ultrasound is a non-invasive imaging modality to assess for structural abnormalities, hydronephrosis, stones, or masses.
  • Result Significance: Findings like hydronephrosis indicate obstruction, stones suggest urolithiasis, and masses may indicate renal carcinoma. A normal ultrasound does not exclude glomerular causes of haematuria.
CT Urogram
  • A CT urogram is a comprehensive imaging test for evaluating both the upper and lower urinary tracts, especially in cases of painless haematuria or suspected malignancy.
  • Result Significance: A positive finding may indicate stones, tumours, or anatomical abnormalities, while a negative CT reduces the likelihood of malignancy or stones.
Cystoscopy
  • Cystoscopy allows direct visualisation of the bladder and urethra to identify sources of bleeding, especially in cases with risk factors for bladder cancer.
  • Result Significance: Detection of bladder tumours, stones, or other structural abnormalities confirms the source of haematuria, while normal findings may redirect the diagnostic focus to the upper urinary tract or renal causes.
Autoimmune and Glomerulonephritis Screen
  • This screen includes tests for autoimmune conditions (e.g., ANCA, anti-GBM antibodies) to detect glomerulonephritis or systemic vasculitis associated with haematuria.
  • Result Significance: Positive results indicate conditions such as glomerulonephritis or vasculitis, warranting nephrology referral. Negative results help exclude autoimmune causes of haematuria.
Evidence-Based Diagnosis (EBDx)
Painless Haematuria as an Indicator of Bladder Cancer
  • Painless haematuria, especially in adults over 40, is a significant indicator of urinary tract malignancy, particularly bladder cancer.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 75%, Specificity: 68%, Positive Likelihood Ratio: 2.3.
  • Significance: A positive finding (painless haematuria) increases the likelihood of bladder cancer, while absence may reduce its probability, prompting alternative causes like infection or stones.
Dipstick Positive for Haematuria
  • A dipstick test for haematuria provides a rapid indication of blood presence but requires confirmation with microscopy to rule out false positives.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 91%, Specificity: 65% for detecting haematuria.
  • Significance: A positive result increases suspicion for UTI, stones, or other bleeding sources, while a negative dipstick result is reliable to exclude haematuria and may shift focus to other conditions.
Urine Cytology for Detection of Bladder Cancer
  • Urine cytology is used to detect malignant cells in cases where bladder cancer is suspected, especially in high-risk individuals.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 40-60%, Specificity: 90-98%, High Specificity for high-grade cancers.
  • Significance: A positive cytology strongly indicates malignancy, while a negative result may still necessitate further evaluation due to low sensitivity.
Ultrasound for Detection of Renal Stones or Masses
  • Renal ultrasound is non-invasive and helpful in detecting stones, masses, or other structural abnormalities in patients presenting with haematuria.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 45-94% (stones), Specificity: 86-100% for renal masses.
  • Significance: A positive finding confirms structural pathology like stones or tumours, while a negative result shifts focus to glomerular or lower urinary tract causes.
CT Urogram for Evaluation of Urinary Tract
  • A CT urogram is highly sensitive for detecting tumours, stones, and structural abnormalities in patients with unexplained haematuria.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 97% for urological cancers, High sensitivity for stones and anatomical abnormalities.
  • Significance: Positive findings confirm anatomical or malignant causes, while negative findings may allow for conservative management or further nephrological evaluation.
Autoimmune Screen (e.g., ANCA) in Suspected Glomerulonephritis
  • An autoimmune screen, including ANCA testing, is useful to detect or exclude vasculitis or glomerulonephritis in haematuria cases where autoimmune pathology is suspected.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity for ANCA-associated vasculitis: 81%, Specificity: 99%.
  • Significance: Positive ANCA results support a diagnosis of vasculitis or glomerulonephritis, while negative findings help rule out autoimmune causes.
Cystoscopy for Direct Visualisation of Bladder Pathology
  • Cystoscopy provides direct visualisation of the bladder and is a key investigation for identifying lesions in patients with haematuria.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 91-97%, High specificity for bladder cancer detection.
  • Significance: A positive cystoscopy finding confirms bladder pathology (e.g., tumour, stones), while negative results can help exclude malignancy in high-risk patients.
Diagnostic Timeout & Cognitive Biases

Awareness of common cognitive errors is essential to improve diagnostic accuracy when assessing a patient with haematuria. Before finalising a diagnosis, it is good practice for clinicians to take a 'diagnostic timeout' to challenge their own reasoning.

  • Anchoring Bias: The tendency to lock onto an initial diagnosis, such as diagnosing a urinary tract infection in a young woman with dysuria and haematuria, while failing to reconsider the diagnosis if the urine culture comes back negative or symptoms persist post-treatment. This may delay investigation for other causes like bladder pathology or stones.
  • Confirmation Bias: Attributing haematuria solely to a patient's known warfarin therapy without further investigation. This bias involves seeking to confirm that the INR is high while ignoring the possibility that the anticoagulation has simply 'unmasked' a new, underlying pathology like a bladder tumour. Haematuria in an anticoagulated patient should always prompt a full investigation.
  • Premature Closure: Concluding that a common benign diagnosis, such as benign prostatic hyperplasia (BPH) in an older man, is the definitive cause of haematuria without completing the necessary workup to exclude a co-existing malignancy. Both BPH and bladder cancer are common in this demographic.
  • Over-reliance on Normal Tests: Placing too much faith in a single negative test. For example, a normal renal tract ultrasound does not rule out bladder cancer (which requires cystoscopy for definitive assessment) or glomerulonephritis. Similarly, a negative urine cytology has poor sensitivity for low-grade tumours and cannot be used to exclude cancer. A systematic approach considering the limitations of each test is vital.
Illustrative Management Pathways

For educational purposes, this section describes the typical principles of management for key diagnoses. These are not prescriptive instructions. All management must be guided by official UK guidelines, local formularies, and senior clinical advice.

Urothelial (Bladder) Cancer

Patients with suspected bladder cancer, particularly those with painless visible haematuria and aged over 45 (as per NICE guidelines), require an urgent two-week wait referral to a urology service. The diagnostic pathway involves imaging of the upper urinary tracts (usually a CT urogram) and direct visualisation of the lower tract via flexible cystoscopy. If a tumour is identified, the patient proceeds to a trans-urethral resection of bladder tumour (TURBT) for staging and histology. Subsequent management is guided by the tumour grade and stage and may include intravesical chemotherapy/BCG, radical cystectomy, or radiotherapy.

Urinary Tract Infection (UTI)

The management of an uncomplicated lower UTI is typically with a short course of oral antibiotics, guided by local microbiology resistance patterns (e.g., nitrofurantoin or trimethoprim). Patients should be advised on adequate fluid intake. If symptoms persist or recur, a mid-stream urine (MSU) sample for culture and sensitivities is essential. Importantly, if haematuria (visible or non-visible) persists after successful treatment of the infection, the patient requires further investigation as per the haematuria pathway to exclude other underlying causes.

Renal Stones (Urolithiasis)

Management of acute renal colic focuses on providing adequate analgesia, most effectively with non-steroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs) if not contraindicated. Small stones (<5mm) are likely to pass spontaneously with conservative management (hydration and analgesia). Patients with signs of obstruction, infection, or intractable pain require urgent hospital admission and urological assessment. Definitive treatment for larger or obstructive stones includes extracorporeal shock wave lithotripsy (ESWL), ureteroscopy with laser fragmentation, or percutaneous nephrolithotomy (PCNL).

Suspected Glomerulonephritis

A patient with features suggestive of glomerulonephritis (e.g., haematuria with proteinuria, hypertension, oedema, and declining renal function) requires urgent referral to a nephrology service. The initial workup involves a comprehensive set of blood tests (including an autoimmune screen and complement levels), quantification of proteinuria, and urine microscopy. A renal biopsy is often required to establish a definitive histological diagnosis, which then guides specific management. Treatment is highly specialised and may involve immunosuppressive agents like high-dose steroids or cyclophosphamide, alongside aggressive blood pressure control.

Reflection & Learning

These brief cases illustrate the application of clinical reasoning to different haematuria presentations. They are for educational purposes only.

Case 1: Painless Haematuria in Primary Care

A 68-year-old male, a lifelong smoker, presents to his GP after his wife noticed his urine was red. He has no pain or other urinary symptoms. A urine dipstick is positive for blood and protein. The problem representation is an older male with a major risk factor (smoking) presenting with painless, visible haematuria, a cardinal red flag for urothelial cancer. Following NICE NG12 guidelines, the GP makes an urgent two-week wait referral to urology for a flexible cystoscopy and upper tract imaging.

Case 2: Haematuria with Flank Pain in the Emergency Department

A 42-year-old woman presents to the ED with a 4-hour history of sudden-onset, severe, colicky pain in her right loin radiating to her groin. She feels nauseated and has seen blood in her urine. She is afebrile but in visible distress. The problem representation is acute, severe flank pain with haematuria, a classic presentation for renal colic due to a ureteric stone. Immediate management involves strong analgesia (e.g., IM diclofenac) and arranging an urgent non-contrast CT of the kidneys, ureters, and bladder (CT KUB) to confirm the diagnosis and check for hydronephrosis.

Case 3: Incidental Finding in a Young Adult

A 25-year-old man is found to have non-visible haematuria and 2+ of protein on a urine dipstick during a pre-employment medical. On questioning, he reports feeling more tired than usual and mentions his ankles have been slightly puffy. His blood pressure is noted to be 155/95 mmHg. The problem representation is a young man with a nephritic syndrome (haematuria, proteinuria, hypertension, oedema), raising high suspicion for glomerulonephritis. He requires urgent referral to a nephrologist for a full workup, including an autoimmune screen and probable renal biopsy.
