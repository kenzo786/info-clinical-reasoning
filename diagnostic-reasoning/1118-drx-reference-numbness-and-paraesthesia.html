Numbness and Paraesthesia Diagnostic Reasoning Reference
Context: Numbness and Paraesthesia Assessment
Overview

Numbness and paraesthesia (abnormal sensations like tingling, pins and needles, or burning) are among the most common neurological symptoms encountered in clinical practice. They represent a significant diagnostic challenge because their causes range from benign and transient, such as anxiety-induced hyperventilation, to severe and life-threatening neurological emergencies like stroke or spinal cord compression. The key clinical task is to differentiate between central and peripheral nervous system pathology and to identify red flags that signal the need for urgent investigation and intervention.

A meticulous, systematic approach based on a detailed history and a focused neurological examination is paramount. This allows the clinician to localise the lesion anatomically—is it in the brain, spinal cord, nerve root, peripheral nerve, or is it a systemic process? This anatomical localisation is the cornerstone of an effective diagnostic strategy. This educational module provides a structured framework for the clinical reasoning process, from differential diagnosis to investigation and management principles. For definitive, real-time management, clinicians must consult up-to-date UK guidelines from bodies such as the National Institute for Health and Care Excellence (NICE), particularly NG127 on suspected neurological conditions and NG217 on multiple sclerosis.

Pathophysiology/Basic Science

Understanding the underlying neurophysiological mechanisms of numbness and paraesthesia is essential for localising the cause and formulating a differential diagnosis. These sensations arise from dysfunction in the sensory pathways at any point from the peripheral receptors to the cerebral cortex.

  • Peripheral Nerve Dysfunction: This is the most common origin.
    • Compression/Entrapment: Mechanical pressure on a nerve (e.g., in carpal tunnel syndrome or radiculopathy) can impair its blood supply (vasa nervorum), leading to local ischaemia, altered ion channel function, and ectopic nerve impulses that are perceived as tingling or numbness.
    • Axonal Degeneration: In many peripheral neuropathies (e.g., diabetic, alcohol-related, toxic), the nerve axons themselves are damaged. This disrupts the normal transmission of sensory signals, often leading to a "dying-back" process that starts in the longest nerves, causing the classic "glove and stocking" pattern of sensory loss.
    • Demyelination: Conditions like Guillain-Barré syndrome attack the myelin sheath, which insulates the nerve axon. This severely slows or blocks nerve conduction, resulting in sensory loss and weakness.
  • Spinal Cord (Myelopathy) Dysfunction:
    • Compression: An extrinsic mass (e.g., tumour, prolapsed disc) or intrinsic process (e.g., syringomyelia) can damage specific sensory tracts. For example, damage to the dorsal columns classically impairs vibration and proprioception, while damage to the spinothalamic tracts affects pain and temperature sensation.
    • Inflammation/Demyelination: In multiple sclerosis or transverse myelitis, inflammatory lesions disrupt signal transmission within the spinal cord tracts, causing patches of numbness or paraesthesia, often accompanied by upper motor neuron signs like hyperreflexia.
  • Central Nervous System (Brain) Dysfunction:
    • Ischaemia/Infarction (Stroke/TIA): A sudden loss of blood supply to sensory areas of the thalamus or somatosensory cortex in the parietal lobe will cause a sudden onset of contralateral numbness.
    • Space-Occupying Lesions: Brain tumours or abscesses can compress or destroy these same sensory pathways, leading to a more gradual onset of symptoms.
  • Metabolic/Systemic Mechanisms:
    • Electrolyte Imbalance: Hypocalcaemia, often resulting from hyperventilation-induced respiratory alkalosis, increases neuronal membrane excitability, leading to perioral and peripheral paraesthesias.
    • Nutritional Deficiencies: Vitamin B12 deficiency causes defective myelin synthesis, particularly affecting the dorsal columns of the spinal cord and peripheral nerves, leading to sensory ataxia and paraesthesia.
Differential Diagnosis (DDx) Framework

A structured approach to the differential diagnosis of numbness and paraesthesia is essential to ensure that potentially serious neurological conditions are prioritised and identified promptly.

Must not miss!
  • Cerebrovascular Disease: Presents with a sudden onset of unilateral numbness, often with associated weakness or speech disturbance.
  • Brain tumour: Typically causes a gradual progression of focal neurological deficits, including sensory changes, often with signs of raised intracranial pressure.
  • Spinal cord tumour: Leads to progressive sensory and motor symptoms below the level of the lesion, often with back pain and sphincter dysfunction.
  • Spinal cord compression: A neurological emergency presenting with sensory level changes, limb weakness, and bowel/bladder dysfunction.
  • Multiple sclerosis: Often presents in younger adults with episodes of focal, transient neurological deficits, such as numbness or optic neuritis, disseminated in time and space.
  • HIV: Can cause various neurological complications, including a painful distal sensory neuropathy.
  • Guillain-Barré syndrome: Characterised by rapidly progressive, ascending, symmetrical weakness and sensory disturbance, often following an infection.
Common causes
  • Anxiety (Hyperventilation): Causes acute, bilateral paraesthesias, typically affecting the hands, feet, and perioral region, often with dizziness or light-headedness.
  • Diabetic neuropathy: A very common cause, presenting with a gradual onset of symmetrical "glove and stocking" numbness and burning pain.
  • Mononeuropathy (e.g. CTS): Sensory changes confined to the distribution of a single nerve, such as median nerve paraesthesia in Carpal Tunnel Syndrome.
  • Radiculopathy: Numbness or tingling in a specific dermatomal pattern, often with associated neck or back pain radiating into a limb.
  • Migraine with aura: Transient, spreading sensory symptoms (often visual, but can be unilateral numbness) that typically precede a headache.
  • Herpes Zoster: Presents with a burning, unilateral, dermatomal pain and tingling which precedes the appearance of the characteristic vesicular rash.
  • COVID-19: Can be associated with a wide range of neurological symptoms, including peripheral paraesthesias.
Easily missed
  • Peripheral neuropathy: Can have numerous causes beyond diabetes, requiring a thorough search for underlying systemic disease.
  • Alcohol abuse: A common cause of a painful, dose-dependent axonal neuropathy due to both direct toxicity and nutritional deficiencies.
  • Vitamin B12 deficiency: Causes a combination of peripheral neuropathy and spinal cord (dorsal column) dysfunction, leading to sensory loss and ataxia.
  • Metabolic neuropathy: Can be caused by conditions like hypothyroidism or chronic kidney disease, presenting with a gradual sensory neuropathy.
  • Connective tissue diseases: Conditions like Lupus (SLE) or Rheumatoid Arthritis can cause neuropathy through vasculitis or nerve compression.
  • Heavy metal poisoning: Chronic exposure to lead, mercury, or arsenic can cause a progressive and often painful sensory-motor neuropathy.
  • Paraneoplastic syndrome: A rare but important cause where a remote cancer triggers an autoimmune attack on the nervous system, often a sensory neuronopathy.
  • Medication induced neuropathy: Can be caused by various drugs, including certain chemotherapies (e.g., vincristine), antibiotics (e.g., isoniazid), and cardiovascular drugs (e.g., amiodarone).
Other Differential Diagnoses to consider
  • Syringomyelia: A rare condition characterised by the formation of a fluid-filled cyst in the spinal cord.
  • Lymphoma: Lymphomas affecting the nervous system can cause numbness and paraesthesia.
  • Amyloidosis: Infiltrative disease that can lead to peripheral nerve damage.
  • Sarcoidosis: A granulomatous disease which can involve the nervous system (neurosarcoidosis).
  • Vasculitis: Inflammatory blood vessel diseases can damage peripheral nerves.
  • Thyroid dysfunction: Both hypo- and hyperthyroidism can result in neuropathy.
History Taking
**Patient’s Description**: How can I help you today?
Starting with an open-ended question, such as "Can you tell me more about what brought you here today?", allows the patient to describe their symptoms in their own words. This provides valuable information about the quality, timing, and nature of the symptoms and helps guide more specific questions later. Relevance: Allows the patient to provide unprompted details that may offer key diagnostic clues, such as sudden onset (suggestive of stroke or Guillain-Barré syndrome) versus gradual onset (suggestive of diabetic neuropathy or peripheral neuropathy).
When did you first notice the numbness or tingling?
  • This question helps to determine the onset of symptoms, whether they started suddenly or gradually.
  • Relevance: A sudden onset suggests cerebrovascular disease, Guillain-Barré syndrome, or trauma, while a gradual onset points towards conditions like diabetic neuropathy, multiple sclerosis, or B12 deficiency.
Is the numbness or tingling constant, or does it come and go?
  • Explores the pattern of symptoms, whether persistent or intermittent.
  • Relevance: Constant symptoms might suggest peripheral neuropathy or radiculopathy, while episodic symptoms could indicate migraines or hyperventilation syndrome.
Are there any triggers or anything that makes the symptoms worse or better?
  • This question identifies any exacerbating or relieving factors, which helps narrow the diagnosis.
  • Relevance: Worsening with movement may suggest radiculopathy, while improvement with rest may indicate compression neuropathies such as carpal tunnel syndrome.
Have you noticed any associated weakness, balance problems, or difficulty walking?
  • Associated symptoms like weakness or balance issues can indicate more severe neurological involvement.
  • Relevance: Positive responses raise suspicion for multiple sclerosis, stroke, or Guillain-Barré syndrome, while a negative response makes these less likely.
Do you have any pain, either at rest or when moving?
  • Presence of pain, particularly radicular pain or pain in a dermatomal distribution, can suggest a radiculopathy or nerve compression.
  • Relevance: Pain may point towards causes like herpes zoster, radiculopathy, or peripheral neuropathy, while absence of pain might suggest other aetiologies such as multiple sclerosis or B12 deficiency.
Have you experienced any changes in bowel or bladder control?
  • Bowel or bladder changes indicate possible involvement of the spinal cord or cauda equina syndrome.
  • Relevance: Positive responses raise concern for spinal cord compression, spinal tumour, or cauda equina syndrome, while a negative response makes these diagnoses less likely.
Do you have any medical conditions such as diabetes, HIV, or any history of alcohol use?
  • Explores risk factors in the past medical history that might contribute to peripheral neuropathy or immunosuppression.
  • Relevance: Diabetes suggests diabetic neuropathy, HIV raises concern for HIV-associated neuropathy, and alcohol use is a known risk factor for peripheral neuropathy.
**Ideas, Concerns, and Expectations (ICE)**
  • Ask the patient, "What do you think might be causing this?" (Ideas), "Is there something specific you are worried about?" (Concerns), and "What were you hoping we might be able to do for you today?" (Expectations).
  • Relevance: Helps ensure the consultation addresses the patient's thoughts about possible causes (e.g. stroke, tumour), worries about serious diagnoses, and expectations for diagnostic tests or reassurance.
Examination Strategy
General inspection
  • Observe for any obvious abnormalities such as muscle wasting, fasciculations, abnormal posture, or asymmetry.
  • Relevance: Muscle wasting or fasciculations suggest peripheral nerve or lower motor neuron disease (e.g. peripheral neuropathy, motor neuron disease), while absence of these findings makes these diagnoses less likely.
Cranial nerve examination
  • Assess all cranial nerves (I-XII) to check for any abnormalities, focusing on eye movements (III, IV, VI), facial sensation and movements (V, VII), and tongue movements (XII).
  • Relevance: Cranial nerve deficits may indicate central causes like stroke, multiple sclerosis, or brain tumour. Negative findings reduce the likelihood of central nervous system (CNS) involvement.
Sensory examination of the affected area
  • Test sensation (light touch, pinprick, temperature, vibration, and proprioception) in the distribution of the symptoms and compare with unaffected areas.
  • Relevance: Sensory deficits in a dermatomal pattern suggest radiculopathy, while a stocking-glove pattern suggests peripheral neuropathy. If sensory loss is absent, it makes these less likely.
Reflexes
  • Assess deep tendon reflexes (biceps, triceps, knee, ankle). Note whether they are diminished, normal, or hyperactive.
  • Relevance: Diminished reflexes suggest peripheral neuropathy, Guillain-Barré syndrome, or radiculopathy. Hyperreflexia suggests upper motor neuron lesions such as multiple sclerosis, stroke, or spinal cord compression.
Muscle strength testing
  • Test strength in relevant muscle groups, focusing on areas related to the affected nerves.
  • Relevance: Weakness in a specific muscle group may suggest mononeuropathy or radiculopathy, while more generalised weakness could indicate conditions like Guillain-Barré syndrome or multiple sclerosis.
Gait assessment
  • Observe the patient's gait, including normal walking, heel-to-toe walking, and tandem gait.
  • Relevance: Abnormal gait, such as ataxia or foot drop, may suggest cerebellar disease, spinal cord lesions, or peripheral neuropathy. A normal gait reduces the likelihood of these conditions.
Romberg test
  • Have the patient stand with feet together, eyes closed, and observe for swaying.
  • Relevance: A positive Romberg test (increased swaying with eyes closed) suggests sensory ataxia due to peripheral neuropathy or posterior column disease (e.g. B12 deficiency, multiple sclerosis).
Lhermitte’s sign
  • Ask the patient to flex their neck forward and note any electric shock-like sensations radiating down the spine or limbs.
  • Relevance: A positive Lhermitte’s sign suggests spinal cord involvement, commonly seen in multiple sclerosis, cervical spondylosis, or spinal cord compression.
Tinel's sign and Phalen's test
  • Tinel’s sign: Tap over the carpal tunnel. Phalen’s test: Have the patient hold wrists in flexion for 60 seconds.
  • Relevance: Positive tests suggest carpal tunnel syndrome or another mononeuropathy. Negative results decrease the likelihood of these diagnoses.
Red Flag Synthesis

This section synthesises the key red flags from the patient's history and examination. The presence of any of these features should prompt a high index of suspicion for a serious underlying cause and trigger urgent investigation and/or specialist referral.

Sudden onset of numbness or weakness, especially unilateral
  • Abrupt onset of unilateral numbness or weakness may indicate a cerebrovascular event such as a transient ischaemic attack (TIA) or stroke.
  • Significance: This could signify a stroke or TIA, which are medical emergencies requiring immediate intervention to prevent permanent neurological damage.
Numbness or tingling associated with loss of bowel or bladder control
  • Loss of bowel or bladder function alongside sensory changes raises concern for cauda equina syndrome or spinal cord compression.
  • Significance: This could indicate spinal cord compression, cauda equina syndrome, or a spinal tumour, which are surgical emergencies that require urgent decompression.
Progressive numbness with weakness and difficulty walking
  • Progressive neurological symptoms affecting motor function and mobility suggest a serious underlying condition involving the central nervous system or peripheral nerves.
  • Significance: This could signify multiple sclerosis, Guillain-Barré syndrome, or spinal cord compression, which require urgent investigation and treatment.
Bilateral numbness in a “glove and stocking” distribution
  • This pattern of sensory loss may indicate a peripheral neuropathy, which could be caused by diabetes, chronic alcohol use, or nutritional deficiencies.
  • Significance: This could signify diabetic neuropathy, B12 deficiency, or chronic alcohol-induced neuropathy, which are conditions requiring prompt management to prevent further complications.
Numbness associated with recent trauma
  • Recent trauma, particularly spinal or head injury, could result in spinal cord damage or peripheral nerve injury.
  • Significance: This could indicate a spinal fracture or nerve injury, requiring urgent imaging and potentially surgical intervention.
Numbness associated with a history of cancer
  • A history of malignancy raises concern for metastatic disease, particularly spinal cord or brain metastases causing neurological symptoms.
  • Significance: This could signify spinal cord compression or brain metastases, both of which require urgent oncological assessment and treatment.
Unexplained weight loss, fatigue, or night sweats
  • These systemic symptoms raise concern for an underlying malignancy, autoimmune disease, or chronic infection.
  • Significance: This could signify a malignancy (e.g., lymphoma, leukaemia) or a chronic infection such as HIV or tuberculosis, all of which require thorough investigation.
Fever with neurological symptoms
  • Fever in the context of neurological symptoms suggests an infectious cause, such as meningitis, encephalitis, or spinal abscess.
  • Significance: This could indicate a serious infection such as meningitis or encephalitis, requiring urgent antimicrobial therapy and possibly hospitalisation.
Investigations Strategy & Interpretation
Investigations: Numbness and Paraesthesia
Full blood count (FBC)
  • Assesses for anaemia, infection, and other haematological abnormalities which can contribute to fatigue, infections, or neurological symptoms.
  • Result Significance: Anaemia may suggest B12 deficiency, and abnormal white cell count may point towards infection or chronic disease like HIV.
Serum Vitamin B12 and folate levels
  • Low B12 or folate levels can lead to peripheral neuropathy and neurological symptoms, including paraesthesia.
  • Result Significance: Positive results suggest B12 or folate deficiency neuropathy, while normal levels help rule out these as causes.
HbA1c
  • Measures long-term blood glucose control and helps diagnose diabetes mellitus, a common cause of peripheral neuropathy.
  • Result Significance: Elevated HbA1c suggests diabetic neuropathy, while normal levels reduce the likelihood of diabetes as a cause.
Electromyography (EMG) and Nerve Conduction Studies (NCS)
  • Tests the electrical activity of muscles and the speed of nerve signals to evaluate nerve and muscle function.
  • Result Significance: Abnormal results suggest peripheral nerve disorders, such as carpal tunnel syndrome, radiculopathy, or Guillain-Barré syndrome.
Magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) of the brain and/or spine
  • Provides detailed images of the brain and spinal cord to identify structural causes of neurological symptoms, such as lesions, tumours, or demyelination.
  • Result Significance: Positive findings can indicate multiple sclerosis, spinal cord compression, brain tumours, or stroke. Negative results help rule out central nervous system pathology.
Lumbar puncture (LP) for cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) analysis
  • Analyses CSF for infections, inflammation, or malignancy-related changes, and helps assess for conditions such as multiple sclerosis and Guillain-Barré syndrome.
  • Result Significance: Positive results (e.g. elevated protein, oligoclonal bands) may indicate multiple sclerosis or Guillain-Barré syndrome. Normal CSF findings can rule out these conditions.
Screening for HIV and syphilis
  • Both HIV and syphilis can cause neurological complications, including peripheral neuropathy and other neurological syndromes.
  • Result Significance: Positive tests suggest HIV-associated neuropathy or neurosyphilis, while negative tests help rule out these infectious causes.
Autoimmune panel (e.g. ANA, anti-dsDNA)
  • Tests for autoimmune diseases that can cause neurological symptoms, such as lupus or vasculitis.
  • Result Significance: Positive results suggest autoimmune conditions like systemic lupus erythematosus (SLE) or vasculitic neuropathy, while negative results reduce the likelihood of autoimmune causes.
Toxicology screen (e.g. for heavy metals like lead or arsenic)
  • Detects the presence of toxic substances that can cause neuropathy, such as heavy metal poisoning.
  • Result Significance: Positive results indicate heavy metal toxicity, which can be a cause of peripheral neuropathy. Negative results help rule out these causes.
Evidence-Based Diagnosis (EBDx)
Sudden onset of unilateral numbness (suggesting stroke)
  • Sudden onset of unilateral numbness or weakness strongly indicates a vascular event, such as a stroke or transient ischaemic attack (TIA). In stroke diagnosis, history of sudden neurological deficit is highly predictive.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 80%, Specificity: 90%, Likelihood Ratio (LR) positive: 8.0.
  • Significance: A positive history of sudden unilateral numbness increases the likelihood of a cerebrovascular event, while absence reduces but does not rule out stroke.
Stocking-glove distribution of numbness (suggesting peripheral neuropathy)
  • A stocking-glove distribution of sensory loss is characteristic of peripheral neuropathy, often due to conditions like diabetes or alcohol abuse.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 70%, Specificity: 80%, LR positive: 3.5.
  • Significance: A positive finding of stocking-glove distribution suggests peripheral neuropathy, while a negative finding suggests further central causes should be explored.
Hyperreflexia (suggesting upper motor neuron lesion)
  • Hyperreflexia is a sign of an upper motor neuron lesion, indicating possible central nervous system pathology, such as multiple sclerosis, stroke, or spinal cord compression.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 65%, Specificity: 85%, LR positive: 4.3.
  • Significance: Positive hyperreflexia increases the likelihood of CNS lesions, while negative results point towards peripheral causes such as radiculopathy or peripheral neuropathy.
Decreased vibration sense (suggesting B12 deficiency neuropathy)
  • Loss of vibration sense is a key finding in B12 deficiency and other conditions affecting the dorsal columns of the spinal cord.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 75%, Specificity: 80%, LR positive: 3.75.
  • Significance: A positive finding suggests B12 deficiency or other causes of posterior column damage, while negative findings suggest other peripheral neuropathies.
Elevated HbA1c (suggesting diabetic neuropathy)
  • An elevated HbA1c indicates poor glycaemic control, which is a common cause of diabetic peripheral neuropathy.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 80%, Specificity: 85%, LR positive: 5.3.
  • Significance: A positive HbA1c result supports the diagnosis of diabetic neuropathy, while normal HbA1c levels may reduce the likelihood of this cause but do not exclude it.
Oligoclonal bands in CSF (suggesting multiple sclerosis)
  • The presence of oligoclonal bands in cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) is a hallmark of multiple sclerosis, indicating inflammation within the central nervous system.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 85%, Specificity: 90%, LR positive: 8.5.
  • Significance: Positive oligoclonal bands are highly indicative of multiple sclerosis, while their absence reduces the likelihood of this condition.
Nerve conduction studies (suggesting carpal tunnel syndrome)
  • Nerve conduction studies are crucial in diagnosing carpal tunnel syndrome by identifying slowed conduction velocity in the median nerve.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 85%, Specificity: 95%, LR positive: 17.0.
  • Significance: Positive nerve conduction studies strongly suggest carpal tunnel syndrome, while negative findings can rule it out.
Diagnostic Timeout & Cognitive Biases

Even with a systematic approach, the diagnostic process for numbness and paraesthesia is susceptible to cognitive errors. Before finalising a diagnosis, it is good practice for clinicians to take a 'diagnostic timeout' to challenge their reasoning and consider potential biases.

  • Anchoring Bias: The tendency to fixate on an early piece of information or a known diagnosis. For example, attributing a patient's new leg numbness to their known diabetic neuropathy without considering the possibility of a superimposed, acute radiculopathy or spinal cord pathology. A key question is: "Could this be something new and different?"
  • Confirmation Bias: Seeking or over-valuing evidence that supports an initial hypothesis while ignoring contradictory findings. For instance, focusing on a positive Tinel's sign to confirm carpal tunnel syndrome while downplaying the significance of neck pain or weakness in the arm, which might suggest a cervical radiculopathy.
  • Diagnosis Momentum: Accepting a previous diagnosis without question. A patient may carry a label of "anxiety" for their tingling, causing clinicians to overlook the gradual development of objective neurological signs that point towards an organic cause like multiple sclerosis or a vitamin deficiency.
  • Atypical Presentations: Forgetting that serious conditions can present in non-classical ways. For example, spinal cord compression may present with vague sensory changes or gait disturbance without the classic triad of pain, weakness, and sphincter disturbance. Vitamin B12 deficiency can cause significant neurological symptoms long before any haematological changes (like macrocytic anaemia) appear.
  • Over-reliance on Normal Tests: Placing undue confidence in a negative test result. A normal brain MRI does not rule out small fibre neuropathy, which requires a skin biopsy. Similarly, normal nerve conduction studies can miss early or intermittent radiculopathy, which may require an MRI of the spine to diagnose.
Illustrative Management Pathways

For educational purposes, this section describes the typical principles of management for key diagnoses. These are not prescriptive instructions. All management must be guided by official UK guidelines, local formularies, and senior clinical advice.

Suspected Stroke / TIA

A patient presenting with a sudden onset of unilateral numbness, with or without other focal neurological deficits, requires immediate emergency hospital admission. The initial management pathway focuses on rapid assessment and imaging. This includes an urgent non-contrast CT head to exclude haemorrhage, followed by further imaging (e.g., CT angiography or MRI with diffusion-weighted imaging) as indicated. Management is coordinated by the stroke team, with treatment (e.g., thrombolysis, thrombectomy, or antiplatelet therapy) depending on the type of stroke, time of onset, and patient factors.

Suspected Spinal Cord Compression / Cauda Equina Syndrome

The development of bilateral leg numbness, saddle anaesthesia, and/or bladder or bowel dysfunction are red flags for spinal cord compression or cauda equina syndrome. This constitutes a neurosurgical emergency. The patient requires immediate hospital admission for an urgent MRI of the whole spine. The primary goal of management is to relieve the compression as quickly as possible to prevent permanent neurological damage. This usually involves high-dose corticosteroids (e.g., dexamethasone) to reduce oedema and urgent surgical decompression.

Peripheral Neuropathy (e.g., due to Diabetes or B12 deficiency)

Once a diagnosis of peripheral neuropathy is established and serious causes have been excluded, management focuses on two key areas. First, treating the underlying cause, which involves optimising glycaemic control (HbA1c) in diabetes or administering vitamin B12 replacement therapy. Second, managing the symptoms of neuropathic pain according to NICE guidelines (NG127). First-line pharmacological options typically include amitriptyline, duloxetine, gabapentin, or pregabalin. Patient education on foot care is also critical, especially in diabetic neuropathy.

Mononeuropathy (e.g., Carpal Tunnel Syndrome)

For mild to moderate carpal tunnel syndrome, initial management is often conservative and can be managed in primary care. This includes advising the patient on activity modification and providing wrist splints for nocturnal use. If symptoms persist or are severe, corticosteroid injections into the carpal tunnel can provide temporary relief. For patients with significant symptoms, evidence of muscle wasting (thenar eminence), or nerve damage on nerve conduction studies, referral for surgical decompression is indicated.

Reflection & Learning

These brief cases illustrate the application of clinical reasoning to different presentations of numbness and paraesthesia. They are for educational purposes only.

Case 1: Suspected Cauda Equina Syndrome in A&E

A 48-year-old man presents to the Emergency Department with a 3-day history of lower back pain and a 24-hour history of numbness in both legs. On further questioning, he reports difficulty knowing if his bladder is full and feels numb when he wipes after using the toilet. The problem representation is a middle-aged male with new back pain and clear red flag symptoms (bilateral leg numbness, bladder disturbance, saddle anaesthesia). This is highly suspicious for cauda equina syndrome, a neurosurgical emergency. The immediate pathway involves keeping the patient nil-by-mouth, requesting an urgent MRI of the spine, and making an immediate referral to the on-call neurosurgery or spinal surgery team.

Case 2: Suspected Peripheral Neuropathy in General Practice

A 65-year-old woman with a history of heavy alcohol intake presents to her GP complaining of a 6-month history of burning and "pins and needles" in both feet, which is gradually ascending up her shins. She has also felt increasingly unsteady on her feet. On examination, she has reduced sensation to light touch and pinprick in a "stocking" distribution up to her mid-calves and absent ankle reflexes. The problem representation is an older woman with a key risk factor (alcohol) presenting with a symmetrical, length-dependent sensory neuropathy and sensory ataxia. The GP arranges blood tests to screen for other causes (B12, folate, glucose, thyroid function) and initiates treatment for neuropathic pain while providing advice on alcohol cessation.

Case 3: Suspected Multiple Sclerosis in a Young Adult

A 28-year-old woman presents to her GP with a 2-week history of numbness and tingling affecting her left hand and forearm. She recalls a similar episode of numbness in her right leg about a year ago which resolved on its own after a month. She also mentions an episode of painful, blurred vision in one eye two years ago. The problem representation is a young woman with a history of relapsing and remitting neurological symptoms affecting different parts of the central nervous system at different times (optic neuritis, leg numbness, arm numbness). This history is highly suggestive of Multiple Sclerosis. The GP performs a neurological examination looking for upper motor neuron signs and refers her urgently to a neurologist for further assessment, likely including an MRI of the brain and spine.
