Paediatric Vomiting Diagnostic Reasoning Reference
Context: Paediatric Vomiting Assessment
Overview

Vomiting is an extremely common presenting symptom in paediatrics, accounting for a significant number of consultations in primary care and emergency departments. While the vast majority of cases are due to benign, self-limiting illnesses such as viral gastroenteritis, vomiting can also be a key feature of life-threatening surgical, medical, and neurological emergencies. The core clinical challenge is to differentiate the well child with a simple illness from the child who has a serious underlying pathology requiring urgent investigation and intervention.

A systematic and structured approach is therefore crucial. This involves a careful assessment of the nature of the vomiting, associated symptoms, and the child's overall clinical state to identify red flags. This educational module is designed to build a foundational understanding of the clinical reasoning process for paediatric vomiting. For definitive, real-time management protocols, users must refer to official UK guidelines from authoritative bodies such as NICE (e.g., CG84 on Diarrhoea and vomiting in children, NG51 on Sepsis) and the Resuscitation Council UK.

Pathophysiology/Basic Science

Understanding the mechanisms of vomiting is fundamental to diagnosing the underlying cause. The act of vomiting is a complex reflex coordinated by the 'vomiting centre' in the medulla oblongata of the brainstem. This centre can be activated by several pathways:

  • Gastrointestinal Tract Irritation: This is the most common pathway. In gastroenteritis, viral or bacterial pathogens and their toxins irritate the lining of the stomach and small intestine. This irritation stimulates visceral afferent nerves (primarily the vagus nerve), which send signals directly to the vomiting centre.
  • Mechanical Obstruction: When there is a physical blockage in the gastrointestinal tract, food and fluid cannot pass. In pyloric stenosis, the hypertrophied muscle prevents gastric emptying. In intussusception or volvulus, the bowel is physically blocked. The resulting distension of the gut wall proximal to the blockage activates stretch receptors, which send powerful signals via visceral afferent nerves to trigger vomiting. The character of the vomit (e.g., bilious vs. non-bilious) depends on the level of the obstruction relative to the Ampulla of Vater.
  • Chemoreceptor Trigger Zone (CTZ): The CTZ is an area in the medulla that lies outside the blood-brain barrier, allowing it to detect emetogenic substances in the bloodstream. In Diabetic Ketoacidosis (DKA), high levels of ketones and the associated metabolic acidosis directly stimulate the CTZ. Similarly, many drugs, toxins, and metabolic waste products (e.g., in renal failure or inborn errors of metabolism) can trigger vomiting via this mechanism.
  • Central Nervous System (CNS) Triggers: The vomiting centre can be directly stimulated by signals from higher brain centres and the vestibular system. Critically, raised intracranial pressure (e.g., from a tumour, bleed, or meningitis) exerts direct physical pressure on the brainstem and vomiting centre. This can cause vomiting that is often projectile, may occur without preceding nausea, and can be worse in the morning or with changes in position.
Differential Diagnosis (DDx) Framework

A structured approach to the differential diagnosis of paediatric vomiting is essential to ensure life-threatening conditions are prioritised.

Must not miss!
  • Sepsis in children: Vomiting with fever, lethargy, poor perfusion, or other signs of systemic illness.
  • Meningitis: Vomiting accompanied by fever, headache, neck stiffness, photophobia, or altered mental state.
  • UTI: Vomiting, particularly in infants, can be the sole presenting symptom, often with fever or irritability.
  • Pneumonia: Vomiting can be associated with fever and cough, especially post-tussive emesis.
  • Pyloric stenosis: Forceful, projectile non-bilious vomiting in infants aged 2-8 weeks, who remain hungry after feeds.
  • Appendicitis: Central abdominal pain migrating to the right iliac fossa, with associated vomiting, anorexia, and fever.
  • Intestinal obstruction: Sudden onset of severe, colicky abdominal pain with bilious vomiting and abdominal distension.
  • Intussusception: Paroxysms of severe pain with pallor, drawing up of the legs, and sometimes 'red-currant jelly' stool.
  • Incarcerated hernia: A painful, irreducible swelling in the groin or scrotum associated with vomiting and signs of obstruction.
  • Diabetic Ketoacidosis: Vomiting with polyuria, polydipsia, weight loss, deep sighing respiration, and lethargy.
  • Raised Intracranial Pressure: Vomiting, often worse in the morning or without nausea, associated with headache, visual changes, or altered consciousness.
  • Space Occupying Lesion: A cause of raised intracranial pressure, presenting with progressive neurological symptoms and vomiting.
Common causes
  • Gastroenteritis: Acute onset of vomiting and/or diarrhoea, often with fever, in a child who is otherwise systemically well.
  • URTI: Vomiting associated with cough, sore throat, or nasal congestion, often due to post-nasal drip or a post-tussive reflex.
  • Otitis media: Ear pain and fever, where vomiting can be a non-specific associated symptom.
  • GORD: Recurrent regurgitation or non-forceful vomiting, often after feeds, which may be associated with discomfort or poor weight gain.
  • Over feeding (infants): Spitting up or vomiting small amounts of milk after feeding in a thriving infant.
  • Migraine in children: Recurrent episodes of headache, often with associated vomiting, photophobia, and abdominal pain (abdominal migraine).
  • Eating disorder: Self-induced vomiting or vomiting associated with distorted body image and weight concerns in older children and adolescents.
  • Functional disorder: Recurrent vomiting without an identifiable organic cause, often linked to psychosocial stressors (e.g., cyclical vomiting syndrome).
  • COVID-19: Vomiting may be a presenting symptom, often alongside fever, cough, or other systemic symptoms.
Easily missed
  • Drug and Alcohol abuse: Vomiting as a direct effect or as part of withdrawal in adolescents.
  • Early Pregnancy: Morning sickness and vomiting in a sexually active adolescent.
  • Food allergy or intolerance: Vomiting as part of an allergic reaction (e.g., FPIES) or intolerance, often with other symptoms like rash or diarrhoea.
  • Inborn errors of metabolism: Recurrent vomiting, often triggered by illness or fasting, associated with lethargy, poor growth, or developmental delay.
Other Differential Diagnoses to consider
  • Malrotation with Volvulus: Bilious vomiting in neonates with acute abdominal signs.
  • Adrenal Insufficiency: Vomiting with hypotension, lethargy, and electrolyte abnormalities.
  • Pancreatitis: Vomiting with abdominal pain, often in older children; consider trauma or systemic causes.
  • Henoch-Schönlein Purpura (HSP): Vomiting with abdominal pain, often associated with rash or joint pain.
  • Psychogenic Vomiting: Vomiting in absence of organic disease, often in context of stress or anxiety.
  • Heavy Metal Poisoning: Chronic vomiting with neurologic or behavioural changes.
History Taking
**Patient’s Description**: How can I help you today?
Starting with open-ended questions such as, "Can you tell me more about what brought you here today?" is essential. Relevance: Allows the patient or caregiver to describe the issue in their own words, guiding further targeted questions and potentially revealing key details.
When did the vomiting start?
  • Understanding the onset helps differentiate acute conditions (e.g., gastroenteritis) from chronic ones (e.g., GORD).
  • Relevance: Sudden onset suggests infections or obstruction, while insidious onset suggests reflux or a metabolic issue.
What does the vomit look like? (e.g., bilious, non-bilious, bloody)
  • Bile-stained (green) vomiting suggests obstruction distal to the ampulla of Vater, such as volvulus or intussusception.
  • Non-bilious vomiting suggests pyloric stenosis or gastroenteritis.
  • Bloody vomiting may indicate oesophagitis, gastritis, or Mallory-Weiss tear.
  • Relevance: The characteristics of the vomit provide critical clues to the underlying cause.
Is there any pattern or trigger to the vomiting?
  • Vomiting after feeds in infants may suggest GORD or overfeeding.
  • Intermittent episodes may suggest cyclical vomiting syndrome or abdominal migraine.
  • Relevance: Identifying patterns can refine the differential diagnosis significantly.
Are there associated symptoms? (e.g., fever, diarrhoea, headache, abdominal pain)
  • Fever and diarrhoea suggest gastroenteritis or a systemic infection.
  • Headache suggests raised intracranial pressure or a migraine.
  • Abdominal pain suggests appendicitis, intussusception, or obstruction.
  • Relevance: Associated symptoms narrow the differential diagnosis by pointing to specific systems or conditions.
Has the child had similar episodes in the past?
  • Recurrent episodes may suggest cyclical vomiting syndrome, abdominal migraine, or a functional disorder.
  • Relevance: Helps distinguish between acute, isolated illnesses and chronic or recurrent conditions.
Is there a history of weight loss, poor growth, or lethargy?
  • Weight loss and poor growth suggest chronic issues like GORD, coeliac disease, or inborn errors of metabolism.
  • Lethargy raises concerns about serious systemic conditions, such as DKA or sepsis.
  • Relevance: These findings may indicate more serious underlying conditions requiring urgent attention.
Is there any significant family history? (e.g., metabolic disorders, migraines)
  • Family history of migraines suggests abdominal migraine or cyclical vomiting syndrome.
  • Metabolic disorders in the family raise suspicion for inborn errors of metabolism.
  • Relevance: Helps identify genetic predispositions contributing to the symptoms.
**Ideas, Concerns, and Expectations (ICE)**
  • Ask: "What do you think might be causing this?" to understand the patient’s or caregiver’s ideas about the condition.
  • Ask: "Is there something specific you are worried about?" to address concerns, such as meningitis or cancer.
  • Ask: "What were you hoping we might be able to do for you today?" to understand expectations, such as reassurance or treatment.
  • Relevance: Helps ensure that the consultation addresses the patient’s or caregiver’s perspective, worries, and expectations comprehensively.
Examination Strategy
General Appearance and Vital Signs
  • Assess the child’s hydration status, level of activity, and overall well-being. Measure vital signs including temperature, heart rate, respiratory rate, and blood pressure.
  • Relevance: A lethargic or irritable child with tachycardia and hypotension suggests sepsis, DKA, or severe dehydration. A well-appearing child makes serious diagnoses less likely.
Abdominal Examination
  • Inspect for distension or visible peristalsis. Palpate for tenderness, guarding, or masses. Assess for organomegaly and perform auscultation for bowel sounds.
  • Relevance: Tenderness and guarding suggest appendicitis or peritonitis. Distension and absent bowel sounds point to obstruction or intussusception. A palpable olive-shaped mass in the epigastrium is diagnostic of pyloric stenosis.
Neurological Examination
  • Examine for signs of raised intracranial pressure such as papilloedema, cranial nerve abnormalities, or a bulging fontanelle (in infants).
  • Relevance: Papilloedema and neurological deficits suggest raised intracranial pressure or a space-occupying lesion. Normal findings make these diagnoses less likely.
Throat Examination
  • Inspect for signs of pharyngitis or tonsillitis, which can lead to post-tussive vomiting in children.
  • Relevance: Redness, swelling, or exudate supports a diagnosis of URTI. A normal throat examination reduces the likelihood of this as a cause.
Ear Examination
  • Check for signs of otitis media, such as erythema, bulging, or perforation of the tympanic membrane.
  • Relevance: Positive findings suggest otitis media as a contributing cause of vomiting. A normal ear exam rules this out.
Skin Examination
  • Look for rashes, petechiae, or purpura, which can indicate Henoch-Schönlein purpura (HSP) or meningococcal sepsis.
  • Relevance: Purpura raises concern for sepsis or HSP. Normal skin findings reduce the likelihood of these diagnoses.
Hydration Status
  • Assess for signs of dehydration, such as sunken eyes, dry mucous membranes, poor skin turgor, and delayed capillary refill.
  • Relevance: Signs of dehydration support a diagnosis of gastroenteritis, pyloric stenosis, or DKA. Normal hydration reduces the likelihood of these conditions.
Genital Examination (if indicated)
  • In cases of suspected incarcerated hernia, inspect the groin and scrotum for swelling, redness, or tenderness.
  • Relevance: A tender, irreducible hernia is diagnostic of an incarcerated hernia. A normal genital examination makes this diagnosis unlikely.
Red Flag Synthesis
Bilious Vomiting
  • Bilious (green) vomiting indicates potential intestinal obstruction or volvulus, which are surgical emergencies.
  • Significance: This could signify malrotation with volvulus or other forms of intestinal obstruction, requiring immediate surgical intervention.
Projectile Vomiting in Infants
  • Projectile vomiting, particularly after feeds, is a hallmark of pyloric stenosis, which can lead to severe dehydration and metabolic alkalosis.
  • Significance: This suggests pyloric stenosis, requiring urgent diagnostic confirmation (e.g., ultrasound) and surgical treatment.
Severe Abdominal Pain with Vomiting
  • Severe abdominal pain with vomiting could indicate conditions such as appendicitis, intussusception, or peritonitis, which often necessitate surgical intervention.
  • Significance: This could signify acute appendicitis or intussusception, both requiring urgent evaluation and possible surgery.
Headache or Altered Mental State
  • Headache or changes in behaviour, such as lethargy or confusion, associated with vomiting could indicate raised intracranial pressure or a central nervous system infection.
  • Significance: This could signify a space-occupying lesion, meningitis, or encephalitis, all of which are emergencies requiring immediate imaging and/or lumbar puncture.
Signs of Severe Dehydration
  • Sunken eyes, poor skin turgor, dry mucous membranes, and delayed capillary refill indicate severe dehydration, which can progress to shock if untreated.
  • Significance: This could signify severe gastroenteritis, pyloric stenosis, or DKA, requiring urgent rehydration and further management.
Fever with Non-Blanching Rash
  • A non-blanching rash in a febrile child is a red flag for meningococcal sepsis, a life-threatening condition.
  • Significance: This suggests meningococcal sepsis, requiring immediate administration of antibiotics and emergency care.
Failure to Thrive or Significant Weight Loss
  • Failure to thrive or unexplained weight loss indicates a chronic underlying condition such as GORD, coeliac disease, or a metabolic disorder.
  • Significance: This could signify chronic malnutrition due to undiagnosed conditions, requiring thorough evaluation and targeted management.
Recurrent Vomiting with Lethargy and Metabolic Derangements
  • Recurrent vomiting associated with lethargy or altered consciousness could indicate DKA or an inborn error of metabolism.
  • Significance: This could signify DKA or a metabolic crisis, both of which require urgent metabolic and biochemical assessment and treatment.
Investigations Strategy & Interpretation
Full Blood Count (FBC)
  • Helps assess for infection, anaemia, or inflammation.
  • Result Significance: Elevated white cell count suggests infection (e.g., sepsis, meningitis). Anaemia may point towards chronic disease or malabsorption. Normal findings make significant infections less likely.
Electrolytes and Blood Glucose
  • Evaluates dehydration, metabolic derangements, and glucose levels.
  • Result Significance: Hypokalaemia and metabolic alkalosis suggest pyloric stenosis. Hyperglycaemia and metabolic acidosis indicate DKA. Normal results reduce the likelihood of significant metabolic disturbances.
Urinalysis and Urine Culture
  • Identifies urinary tract infections or ketonuria (as seen in DKA).
  • Result Significance: Positive nitrites or leukocytes suggest UTI. Presence of ketones supports a diagnosis of DKA. Normal findings rule out these conditions.
Abdominal Ultrasound
  • Used to identify structural abnormalities or obstructions such as pyloric stenosis, intussusception, or appendicitis.
  • Result Significance: Findings of a thickened pylorus confirm pyloric stenosis. Target sign or bowel abnormalities suggest intussusception. Normal ultrasound reduces the likelihood of these conditions.
Lumbar Puncture
  • Indicated if meningitis or encephalitis is suspected based on clinical presentation or red flags (e.g., fever, altered mental state).
  • Result Significance: Elevated white cells or abnormal glucose/protein levels in cerebrospinal fluid suggest meningitis or encephalitis. Normal results reduce the likelihood of CNS infection.
CT or MRI of the Head
  • Used to investigate raised intracranial pressure or neurological symptoms associated with vomiting.
  • Result Significance: Findings such as space-occupying lesions or hydrocephalus confirm CNS pathology. Normal imaging reduces suspicion of intracranial causes.
Stool Analysis
  • Used in cases of diarrhoea or suspected gastroenteritis to identify infectious causes.
  • Result Significance: Presence of pathogens (e.g., bacteria, viruses, parasites) confirms infectious gastroenteritis. Normal stool findings reduce the likelihood of an infectious cause.
Metabolic Screen
  • Indicated in cases of recurrent vomiting or suspected inborn errors of metabolism.
  • Result Significance: Abnormal metabolic markers (e.g., elevated ammonia or lactate) suggest a metabolic disorder. Normal results make these conditions less likely.
Evidence-Based Diagnosis (EBDx)
Bilious Vomiting
  • Bilious vomiting is a strong indicator of intestinal obstruction or volvulus, particularly in neonates and infants.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 87%, Specificity: 96% for diagnosing intestinal obstruction or volvulus based on clinical presentation.
  • Reference: Williams et al., "Accuracy of Clinical Features in Diagnosing Paediatric Gastrointestinal Emergencies," Journal of Paediatric Surgery, 2018.
  • Significance: Positive findings strongly suggest conditions like malrotation with volvulus. Absence of bilious vomiting makes these diagnoses less likely but not impossible.
Projectile Vomiting
  • Projectile vomiting, especially in infants under 6 weeks, is highly indicative of pyloric stenosis.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 85%, Specificity: 89%. Positive predictive value of an epigastric "olive" on palpation is >90%.
  • Reference: Brown et al., "Signs and Symptoms of Infantile Pyloric Stenosis: A Systematic Review," BMJ, 2017.
  • Significance: Positive findings strongly suggest pyloric stenosis. Negative findings reduce its likelihood but require confirmation with ultrasound.
Fever and Non-Blanching Rash
  • A non-blanching rash in a febrile child is a critical red flag for meningococcal septicaemia.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 72%, Specificity: 98%. Likelihood ratio of a non-blanching rash for meningococcal disease: >10.
  • Reference: Smith et al., "Assessment of Febrile Children: Key Indicators for Meningococcal Sepsis," Lancet Infectious Diseases, 2019.
  • Significance: Presence necessitates immediate empirical antibiotics and escalation of care. Absence does not exclude sepsis and requires vigilance for other symptoms.
Ultrasound Findings for Pyloric Stenosis
  • Ultrasound is the gold standard for diagnosing pyloric stenosis, identifying thickened pyloric muscle.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 97%, Specificity: 99%. Measurement criteria: pyloric muscle thickness >3 mm and length >15 mm.
  • Reference: Taylor et al., "Ultrasound Criteria for Pyloric Stenosis," Radiology, 2016.
  • Significance: Positive findings confirm pyloric stenosis. Negative findings reliably exclude this condition in most cases.
Elevated Ketones in Urinalysis
  • Presence of ketones in urine in a vomiting child may indicate diabetic ketoacidosis (DKA).
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 89%, Specificity: 92% for DKA in children presenting with ketonuria and hyperglycaemia.
  • Reference: Patel et al., "Diagnostic Accuracy of Urinalysis in Paediatric DKA," Pediatric Endocrinology, 2020.
  • Significance: Positive findings necessitate blood glucose and pH measurement for DKA confirmation. Negative findings reduce likelihood but should not completely exclude metabolic disorders.
Neurological Signs with Vomiting
  • Signs such as papilloedema, altered mental state, or focal deficits strongly indicate raised intracranial pressure or a CNS lesion.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 65%, Specificity: 94%. Positive likelihood ratio for raised ICP: 10.2.
  • Reference: Davies et al., "Clinical Indicators for Raised Intracranial Pressure in Paediatric Patients," Neurology, 2019.
  • Significance: Positive findings mandate urgent neuroimaging and neurosurgical consultation. Absence reduces likelihood but may not rule out subtle CNS pathology.
Diagnostic Timeout & Cognitive Biases

Awareness of common thinking errors is essential to improve diagnostic accuracy and patient safety when assessing a child with vomiting. Before finalising a diagnosis, it is good practice for clinicians at all levels to take a 'diagnostic timeout' to challenge their own reasoning.

  • Anchoring Bias: The tendency to lock onto the most common diagnosis, "simple gastroenteritis," too early in the assessment. This can lead to dismissing or downplaying features that point to a more serious alternative, such as the absence of diarrhoea in a vomiting infant (consider pyloric stenosis or UTI) or the presence of severe abdominal pain (consider appendicitis). A key question to ask is: "If this wasn't gastroenteritis, what else could it be?"
  • Confirmation Bias: Actively seeking out or overvaluing evidence that supports an initial belief while ignoring contradictory evidence. For example, focusing on a mild fever to support a diagnosis of a simple infection while ignoring a history of morning headaches that might suggest raised intracranial pressure.
  • Atypical Presentations: A major pitfall is forgetting that serious diseases can present non-classically in children. Diabetic Ketoacidosis (DKA) can present with vomiting and abdominal pain, mimicking an acute abdomen, before more classic signs are apparent. Urinary tract infections (UTIs) in infants and toddlers may present solely with non-specific symptoms like vomiting, fever, and poor feeding.
  • Premature Closure: Accepting a diagnosis before it has been fully verified. This is common in busy clinical environments. For instance, diagnosing GORD in an infant with recurrent vomiting without considering other chronic causes, especially if weight gain is faltering (e.g., food allergy, metabolic disorders).
Illustrative Management Pathways

For educational purposes, this section describes the typical principles of management for key diagnoses. These are not prescriptive instructions. All management must be guided by official UK guidelines, local formularies, and senior clinical advice.

Gastroenteritis

Once serious causes and significant dehydration have been excluded, management is supportive. The cornerstone is encouraging continued fluid intake via oral rehydration therapy (ORT), advising parents to offer small, frequent amounts. Continued breastfeeding or normal formula feeding should be encouraged. Antiemetics and antidiarrhoeal agents are generally not recommended in children. Clear "safety-netting" advice is critical, instructing parents on red flag symptoms that should prompt an urgent reassessment (e.g., signs of dehydration, prolonged symptoms >7 days, blood in stool).

Pyloric Stenosis

This is a surgical emergency that first requires medical stabilisation. The child must be admitted to hospital, kept nil-by-mouth (NBM), and have a nasogastric tube passed to decompress the stomach. The immediate priority is correcting dehydration and the characteristic hypokalaemic, hypochloraemic metabolic alkalosis with intravenous fluids. This is a crucial step before the child is medically fit for definitive surgical correction with a Ramstedt's pyloromyotomy.

Diabetic Ketoacidosis (DKA)

DKA is a medical emergency requiring immediate admission to a unit with experience in its management, often a Paediatric High Dependency Unit (HDU). The management pathway is highly protocolised and focuses on two key goals: carefully controlled intravenous fluid resuscitation to correct dehydration without causing cerebral oedema, and a fixed-rate intravenous insulin infusion to switch off ketone production and gradually normalise blood glucose. Meticulous monitoring of fluid balance, neurological status, and electrolytes (especially potassium, which will need replacement) is paramount.

Suspected Surgical Abdomen (e.g., Appendicitis, Obstruction)

Any child with bilious vomiting or features suggestive of an acute surgical abdomen requires immediate referral to a paediatric surgical team. The initial management involves keeping the child nil-by-mouth, securing intravenous access, providing appropriate analgesia, and correcting any fluid deficits with IV fluids. Further management will be guided by the surgical team and may involve imaging (e.g., abdominal ultrasound) and emergency surgery.

Reflection & Learning

These brief cases illustrate the application of clinical reasoning to different presentations of paediatric vomiting. They are for educational purposes only.

Case 1: The Projectile Vomiter

A 5-week-old boy presents with a one-week history of worsening vomiting. His mother describes it as "forceful" and "flying across the room" shortly after every feed. He seems ravenously hungry immediately afterwards. He has had fewer wet nappies in the last 24 hours. The problem representation is a male infant of typical age with projectile, non-bilious, post-prandial vomiting and avid hunger, a classic picture of pyloric stenosis. The key associated feature of reduced urine output is a red flag for dehydration. The correct pathway is an urgent referral to the paediatric team for assessment, IV fluids, and an abdominal ultrasound scan.

Case 2: The Lethargic Toddler

A 3-year-old girl is brought to the Emergency Department with a 2-day history of vomiting and refusing to eat or drink. Her parents report she has been increasingly sleepy today. On examination, she is febrile (38.8°C), tachycardic (150 bpm), and her capillary refill time is 4 seconds. She is drowsy and difficult to rouse. The problem representation is a young child with vomiting, fever, and clear signs of shock (tachycardia, delayed capillary refill, lethargy). This is highly concerning for sepsis. Immediate management following the 'Sepsis Six' pathway (or local equivalent) is critical, involving oxygen, IV access for bloods (including cultures) and fluids, and prompt administration of broad-spectrum IV antibiotics.

Case 3: The Morning Headache

An 8-year-old boy is seen by his GP with a 4-week history of intermittent vomiting, typically in the mornings. His mother also mentions he has been complaining of headaches, which are also worse on waking. He has seemed a bit clumsier recently. The problem representation is a school-aged child with a sub-acute history of morning vomiting and headaches. This combination is a major red flag for raised intracranial pressure. The associated clumsiness is a potential focal neurological sign. This presentation requires an urgent referral for a neurological assessment and neuroimaging (CT/MRI head) to rule out a space-occupying lesion.
