Back Pain Diagnostic Reasoning Reference
Context: Back Pain Assessment
Overview

Back pain is one of the most frequent reasons for consulting a healthcare professional in the UK, affecting people of all ages and representing a significant cause of disability and work absence. The clinical challenge lies in differentiating common, self-limiting mechanical back pain from the small but significant proportion of cases caused by serious underlying pathologies such as spinal cord compression, cancer, infection, or aortic aneurysm.

A systematic and structured approach to assessment is paramount. The initial priority is to identify "red flag" symptoms and signs that indicate the need for urgent investigation or specialist referral. This ensures that time-critical diagnoses are not missed, while also preventing over-investigation in the majority of patients with non-specific, benign back pain.

This educational module is designed to build a foundational understanding of the clinical reasoning process for back pain. For definitive, real-time management protocols, users must refer to official UK guidelines from authoritative bodies such as NICE (e.g., NG59 for Low back pain and sciatica in over 16s) and the Resuscitation Council UK.

Pathophysiology/Basic Science

Understanding the origin of back pain is key to diagnosis. Pain can be generated through several distinct mechanisms, each pointing towards a different set of differential diagnoses:

  • Mechanical (Nociceptive) Pain: This is the most common type and originates from the structures of the spine itself—muscles, ligaments, facet joints, and the outer layer of the intervertebral discs. It's caused by strain, sprain, or degenerative changes that activate local nociceptors. The pain is typically dull, aching, localised to the back, and often varies with posture and activity (e.g., worse on movement, better with rest).
  • Radicular Pain: This occurs when a spinal nerve root is compressed or inflamed, most commonly by a herniated intervertebral disc or bony stenosis (foraminal narrowing). This generates a sharp, shooting, or electric-shock-like pain that radiates from the back down the leg in the territory of the affected nerve root (a dermatome). Sciatica is the classic example, affecting the L4, L5, or S1 nerve roots.
  • Neurogenic Claudication: This is a specific type of pain caused by compression of the spinal canal itself (spinal stenosis), often due to generalised degenerative changes. The pain, cramping, or weakness affects the buttocks and legs and is characteristically brought on by standing or walking and relieved by sitting or leaning forward (which opens up the spinal canal).
  • Inflammatory Pain: This results from systemic inflammatory conditions, most notably axial spondyloarthropathies like Ankylosing Spondylitis. The inflammation targets the sacroiliac joints and spine, causing chronic pain and stiffness that is characteristically worse with rest (especially in the early morning) and improves with activity and NSAIDs.
  • Referred Pain: Pain felt in the back that originates from visceral organs. For example, pathology in the aorta (aneurysm), pancreas (pancreatitis), kidneys (pyelonephritis, stones), or pelvic organs can be perceived as back pain due to shared nerve pathways. The quality of the pain often reflects its origin (e.g., tearing for aortic dissection, colicky for renal stones).
  • Malignant/Infectious Pain: This pain is caused by the destruction of vertebral bone by a primary tumour, metastatic deposit, or infection (e.g., discitis, osteomyelitis, spinal abscess). This process is unrelenting and independent of activity, often leading to constant, deep, boring pain that is characteristically worse at night.
Differential Diagnosis (DDx) Framework

A structured approach to the differential diagnosis of back pain is essential to ensure life-threatening conditions are prioritised.

Must not miss!
  • Cauda Equina Syndrome: A surgical emergency presenting with bilateral leg weakness, saddle anaesthesia, and bladder/bowel dysfunction.
  • Spinal cord compression: Can cause progressive limb weakness, sensory changes, and a distinct sensory level on examination.
  • Metastatic cancer: Often presents with unrelenting pain, worse at night, in a patient with a history of cancer or unexplained weight loss.
  • Multiple myeloma: A haematological malignancy that can present with bone pain, fractures, and systemic symptoms like fatigue.
  • Bone tumours: Primary bone tumours can cause localised, severe pain and may present with a palpable mass or pathological fracture.
  • Discitis: Infection of the intervertebral disc space causing severe, localised back pain and often associated with fever and raised inflammatory markers.
  • Spinal abscess: A collection of pus in the epidural space, causing severe pain, fever, and rapidly progressing neurological deficits.
  • Osteomyelitis: Infection of the vertebra itself, leading to deep, constant pain, tenderness, and systemic signs of infection.
  • Spinal Tuberculosis: Also known as Pott's disease, a destructive form of skeletal tuberculosis causing chronic pain and spinal deformity.
  • Aortic aneurysm: A leaking or dissecting aneurysm can cause severe, tearing back pain, often with signs of haemodynamic collapse.
  • Acute pancreatitis: Typically presents with severe epigastric pain boring through to the back, associated with nausea and vomiting.
  • Pneumonia: Basal pneumonia can cause referred pain to the upper back or flank, usually with associated cough, fever, and respiratory signs.
  • Pelvic Inflammatory Disease: Infection of the female reproductive organs causing lower abdominal and lower back pain, often with fever and discharge.
  • Pelvic abscess: Can cause lower back pain, fever, and systemic illness, often as a complication of surgery or other pelvic infection.
  • Pulmonary Embolism: Can occasionally present with pleuritic back pain, particularly if the embolus affects the lower lobes of the lungs.
  • Atypical Myocardial Infarction: An inferior MI can sometimes present with epigastric or lower back pain rather than classic chest pain.
Common causes
  • Spinal sprains and strains: Acute, self-limiting pain due to injury of muscles or ligaments, often following a specific incident of lifting or twisting.
  • Disc disease: Degenerative changes or herniation of an intervertebral disc, causing mechanical back pain or radicular symptoms (sciatica).
  • Lumbar radiculopathy: Pain, numbness, or weakness radiating down the leg due to compression or irritation of a lumbar nerve root.
  • Spondylosis: Age-related degenerative changes of the spine, including the discs and facet joints, causing chronic mechanical pain and stiffness.
  • Spondylolisthesis: The forward slip of one vertebra over another, which can cause mechanical back pain or nerve root compression.
  • Spinal stenosis: Narrowing of the spinal canal, leading to neurogenic claudication—leg pain and weakness on walking, relieved by sitting.
  • Osteoporotic vertebral fracture: Sudden onset of severe thoracic or lumbar pain, often after minimal trauma, in an older person with osteoporosis.
  • Chronic pain syndromes: Persistent back pain where the pain experience becomes disconnected from the initial injury, involving central sensitisation.
  • Fibromyalgia: A condition characterised by widespread musculoskeletal pain accompanied by fatigue, sleep, memory and mood issues.
  • Psychogenic: Back pain that is influenced or caused by psychological factors such as stress, anxiety, or depression.
  • COVID-19: Can present with myalgia and back pain as part of the systemic viral illness, usually alongside fever and respiratory symptoms.
Easily missed
  • Peptic Ulcer Disease: A posterior penetrating duodenal ulcer can cause pain referred to the mid-thoracic back.
  • Chronic Pancreatitis: Can cause persistent epigastric and back pain, often related to alcohol intake.
  • UTI: A simple urinary tract infection or pyelonephritis can cause flank and lower back pain, usually with urinary symptoms.
  • Prostatitis: Inflammation of the prostate gland can cause pain in the lower back, perineum, and genitalia in men.
  • Endometriosis: Ectopic endometrial tissue can cause cyclical lower back pain that varies with the menstrual cycle.
  • Ankylosing spondylitis: An inflammatory arthritis causing chronic back pain and stiffness, characteristically worse in the morning and improved with exercise.
  • Crohn's disease: Inflammatory bowel disease can be associated with sacroiliitis, causing inflammatory lower back pain.
  • Ulcerative colitis: Like Crohn's, this IBD can be associated with inflammatory arthritis affecting the spine.
  • Psoriatic arthritis: A form of inflammatory arthritis that can affect the spine (spondylitis) in patients with psoriasis.
  • Reiter's syndrome: Also known as reactive arthritis, can cause back pain as part of a triad with urethritis and conjunctivitis following an infection.
Other Differential Diagnoses to consider
  • COVID-19: Can occasionally present with myalgia or back pain as an atypical symptom, especially in cases with other systemic signs (e.g., fever, respiratory symptoms).
  • Herpes Zoster (Shingles): May present with unilateral back pain prior to rash onset, especially in older adults or immunocompromised patients.
  • Abdominal Aortic Aneurysm (AAA) - chronic: May present with dull, lower back pain; a key consideration in older adults with risk factors.
  • Paget's Disease of Bone: Can lead to chronic back pain due to vertebral involvement; often identified by changes on imaging.
  • Hyperparathyroidism: May lead to back pain due to metabolic bone disease and vertebral fractures.
  • Psoas Abscess: Can present with back pain and systemic symptoms, often associated with recent infection or immunocompromised status.
  • Endocrine causes (e.g., Cushing's Disease, Osteomalacia): Endocrine disorders affecting bone health can contribute to back pain.
  • Depression and Somatisation: Psychological factors can contribute to chronic or recurrent back pain without a clear physical source.
  • Thoracic or Lumbar Spondylodiscitis: An infection of the intervertebral disc space, which can lead to severe, unrelenting back pain and systemic symptoms.
History Taking
**Patient’s Description**: How can I help you today?
Starting with an open-ended question like "Can you tell me more about what brought you here today?" allows the patient to describe the nature and quality of their pain in their own words, providing insight into symptoms and guiding further specific questions. Relevance: This approach facilitates a natural flow and highlights key concerns for the patient, helping to prioritise relevant differential diagnoses.
Can you describe the pain quality (e.g., dull, sharp, burning)?
  • Identifying the pain quality can help distinguish between musculoskeletal, neuropathic, or inflammatory pain.
  • Relevance: A sharp, localised pain may suggest conditions like disc disease or fracture, whereas a burning pain may point to neuropathic causes such as lumbar radiculopathy.
When did the pain start, and has it changed over time?
  • Understanding the onset, duration, and progression can differentiate acute injuries from chronic conditions.
  • Relevance: Sudden onset may suggest an acute strain, fracture, or aneurysm, whereas gradual onset with morning stiffness could indicate inflammatory causes like ankylosing spondylitis.
Does anything make the pain better or worse?
  • Exploring exacerbating and relieving factors can reveal the underlying pathology or functional component.
  • Relevance: Pain worsened by activity may suggest musculoskeletal strain, while pain relieved by leaning forward is often seen in spinal stenosis.
Associated Symptom: Do you have any numbness, weakness, or changes in bowel or bladder function?
  • These neurological symptoms are red flags for cauda equina syndrome and spinal cord compression, which require urgent intervention.
  • Relevance: Positive responses suggest conditions such as cauda equina syndrome or severe radiculopathy, while their absence makes these less likely.
Associated Symptom: Have you experienced any fever, weight loss, or night sweats?
  • These systemic symptoms can indicate an infection, malignancy, or inflammatory condition.
  • Relevance: Positive findings may suggest discitis, abscess, tuberculosis, or metastatic cancer, while a negative response may reduce suspicion for these conditions.
Associated Symptom: Have you had any recent infections or known cancer history?
  • A recent infection or cancer history can increase the likelihood of metastatic disease, discitis, or an abscess.
  • Relevance: Positive responses raise the suspicion for metastatic cancer or infectious causes of back pain.
**Past Medical History, Family History, Drug History**
  • Understanding relevant medical background helps identify risk factors such as osteoporosis, previous trauma, family history of autoimmune disease, or long-term steroid use.
  • Relevance: Conditions like osteoporosis, autoimmune disorders, or malignancy in the family increase risk for spinal fractures or inflammatory back pain.
**Ideas, Concerns, and Expectations (ICE)**
  • Understanding the patient’s perspective, concerns, and hopes for management aligns the consultation with their needs.
  • Relevance: Patients may fear severe conditions (e.g., cancer, paralysis) and may expect specific tests or treatments (e.g., imaging or referral). Addressing these concerns early can improve satisfaction and compliance.
Examination Strategy
General Inspection
  • Inspect the patient's general appearance, posture, and any signs of discomfort, deformity, or muscle wasting. Note any abnormal curvature or alignment of the spine.
  • Relevance: Observing muscle wasting or deformity may suggest chronic conditions such as muscular atrophy or scoliosis, while a normal inspection reduces suspicion of severe deformity or long-standing pathology.
Palpation of the Spine
  • Palpate along the vertebral column for tenderness, deformities, or step-offs, which may indicate a fracture or spinal infection.
  • Relevance: Tenderness over a specific vertebra may suggest fractures or infection, whereas generalised tenderness without focal pain is more typical of muscle strain.
Assessment of Range of Motion (ROM)
  • Ask the patient to perform flexion, extension, and lateral movements of the spine. Limited ROM may indicate musculoskeletal injury or spinal pathology.
  • Relevance: Reduced ROM in flexion and extension may indicate mechanical back pain or degenerative disease. Pain with lateral bending might suggest lumbar radiculopathy or facet joint pathology.
Straight Leg Raise (SLR) Test
  • With the patient lying supine, raise each leg individually to 60 degrees. Reproduction of radicular pain during this manoeuvre suggests nerve root irritation, such as in sciatica or lumbar radiculopathy.
  • Relevance: A positive SLR test is suggestive of lumbar disc herniation or radiculopathy. A negative test reduces the likelihood of nerve root compression.
Reflex Testing (Knee and Ankle Reflexes)
  • Assess knee (L3-L4) and ankle (S1) reflexes, which can be diminished in cases of nerve root compression or neuropathy.
  • Relevance: Decreased reflexes may indicate lumbar radiculopathy or peripheral neuropathy, whereas normal reflexes reduce the likelihood of nerve root involvement.
Sensory Examination (Lower Extremities)
  • Assess sensory function in the dermatomes of the lower limbs. Reduced or altered sensation may indicate nerve root involvement.
  • Relevance: Sensory changes in a specific dermatome may suggest lumbar radiculopathy or peripheral neuropathy. Absence of sensory deficits reduces suspicion of significant nerve root involvement.
Muscle Strength Testing (Lower Extremities)
  • Test muscle strength in the lower limbs, particularly hip flexion, knee extension, and ankle dorsiflexion, as weakness may indicate nerve compression.
  • Relevance: Weakness in specific muscle groups (e.g., ankle dorsiflexion for L5 radiculopathy) can point to nerve root compression, whereas normal strength reduces the likelihood of motor involvement.
Abdominal Palpation
  • Palpate the abdomen to rule out causes of referred back pain such as aortic aneurysm, pancreatitis, or renal disease.
  • Relevance: Abdominal tenderness or a pulsatile mass may suggest an abdominal aortic aneurysm, renal pathology, or pancreatitis. A non-tender abdomen reduces the likelihood of these intra-abdominal causes.
Rectal Examination (if clinically indicated)
  • If red flags are present (e.g., neurological symptoms, saddle anaesthesia), a rectal examination can help assess for cauda equina syndrome.
  • Relevance: Decreased anal tone or saddle anaesthesia is highly suggestive of cauda equina syndrome, requiring urgent intervention. Normal findings reduce the likelihood of this severe condition.
Red Flag Synthesis

This section synthesises the key red flags from the source document. Any of these findings in a patient with back pain necessitates urgent senior review and likely escalation of care.

New onset bladder or bowel incontinence, or urinary retention

Significance: This could indicate cauda equina syndrome, a surgical emergency requiring urgent decompression to prevent permanent neurological damage.

Saddle anaesthesia

Significance: Loss of sensation in the perineal area ("saddle" region) is also highly suggestive of cauda equina syndrome, necessitating urgent assessment and intervention.

Progressive limb weakness or sensory changes

Significance: This could indicate spinal cord compression from a tumour, disc herniation, or other lesion, requiring urgent imaging and possibly surgical intervention.

Severe, unrelenting pain, especially at night or at rest

Significance: This could suggest spinal metastases or spinal infections (e.g., osteomyelitis, discitis, abscess), both of which require prompt investigation and management.

Unexplained weight loss or history of cancer

Significance: This could indicate metastatic spinal disease or other systemic malignancy that requires urgent imaging and oncology input.

Fever, chills, or recent infection

Significance: This could indicate a spinal infection (e.g., epidural abscess or discitis), which may require urgent imaging and intravenous antibiotics.

Pulsatile abdominal mass

Significance: This could indicate a rupturing or expanding abdominal aortic aneurysm, a life-threatening emergency requiring urgent vascular surgical intervention.

History of trauma, especially in older patients or those with osteoporosis

Significance: This could suggest an osteoporotic vertebral fracture requiring pain management and possibly orthopaedic evaluation, especially if pain is severe and of sudden onset.

Investigations Strategy & Interpretation
Full Blood Count (FBC)
  • An FBC can help detect signs of infection, inflammation, or malignancy, such as elevated white cell count or anaemia.
  • Result Significance: Elevated white cell count may suggest infection (e.g., spinal abscess or discitis), while anaemia could be a sign of malignancy or chronic disease. A normal result may make infection or inflammation less likely.
Erythrocyte Sedimentation Rate (ESR) and C-Reactive Protein (CRP)
  • Both ESR and CRP are markers of inflammation that can help identify infection or inflammatory conditions.
  • Result Significance: Elevated ESR or CRP may indicate infection (e.g., discitis or osteomyelitis), malignancy, or inflammatory disease. Normal results make infection or inflammation less likely but do not entirely exclude them.
Plain X-ray of the Spine
  • Spinal X-rays are helpful in evaluating structural abnormalities, fractures, and signs of degenerative changes or malignancy.
  • Result Significance: Positive findings such as vertebral fractures or lytic lesions may indicate osteoporotic fractures or metastatic disease. However, a normal X-ray does not rule out soft tissue or nerve-related conditions.
Magnetic Resonance Imaging (MRI) of the Spine
  • MRI provides detailed images of soft tissues, nerves, and intervertebral discs, making it the preferred investigation for suspected nerve compression, malignancy, or infection.
  • Result Significance: MRI can reveal disc herniation, spinal stenosis, nerve root compression, or evidence of infection (e.g., discitis, abscess). A normal MRI may help exclude serious causes like malignancy or nerve compression.
Serum Calcium, Alkaline Phosphatase, and Parathyroid Hormone (PTH)
  • These tests assess bone metabolism, which can help identify metabolic bone diseases contributing to back pain, such as osteoporosis or hyperparathyroidism.
  • Result Significance: Elevated calcium and PTH levels may suggest hyperparathyroidism, while high alkaline phosphatase could indicate Paget's disease or bone turnover. Normal levels reduce the likelihood of metabolic bone diseases.
Urine Dipstick and Urinalysis
  • Urinalysis can help identify infections or kidney disease, which may cause referred back pain, particularly in cases of pyelonephritis.
  • Result Significance: Positive findings (e.g., white cells or nitrites) may suggest urinary tract infection or pyelonephritis, while a normal result reduces suspicion for renal causes of back pain.
Prostate-Specific Antigen (PSA) in Males
  • PSA levels can be helpful in men with unexplained back pain and a history or suspicion of prostate cancer, as prostate cancer can metastasise to the spine.
  • Result Significance: Elevated PSA may raise suspicion for prostate cancer, warranting further imaging and urological assessment. A normal PSA, however, does not rule out prostate cancer entirely.
Bone Scan or Positron Emission Tomography (PET) Scan
  • Bone scans or PET scans can detect bone metastases or other lesions in patients with a known history of cancer or unexplained severe back pain.
  • Result Significance: Positive findings indicate possible metastatic disease or other bone pathology. A negative scan reduces suspicion for these conditions.
Evidence-Based Diagnosis (EBDx)
Saddle anaesthesia in Cauda Equina Syndrome
  • Saddle anaesthesia (numbness in the perineal area) is a significant clinical finding for cauda equina syndrome, as it indicates sacral nerve root involvement.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 75%, Specificity: 95% for cauda equina syndrome.
  • Reference: Gardner A, et al. "Cauda Equina Syndrome: A Review of the Current Clinical and Medicolegal Position." Eur Spine J. 2011.
  • Significance: A positive finding of saddle anaesthesia is highly suggestive of cauda equina syndrome, necessitating urgent surgical evaluation, while a negative finding makes this diagnosis less likely.
Straight Leg Raise (SLR) Test in Lumbar Radiculopathy
  • The SLR test, when positive (reproduction of radicular pain between 30-70 degrees), indicates lumbar nerve root irritation.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 91%, Specificity: 26% for lumbar disc herniation causing radiculopathy.
  • Reference: Vroomen, PC, et al. "The Accuracy of History, Physical Examination, and Imaging in the Diagnosis of Lumbar Disc Herniation." Spine (Phila Pa 1976), 1999.
  • Significance: A positive SLR test supports the diagnosis of lumbar radiculopathy due to disc herniation, though it has limited specificity; a negative result reduces the likelihood of radiculopathy.
MRI for Detection of Spinal Infection or Malignancy
  • MRI is the imaging modality of choice for assessing soft tissue structures, allowing detection of infections like discitis or malignancies compressing the spinal cord.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 96%, Specificity: 92% for spinal infection; 93% Sensitivity for detecting malignancy in spine.
  • Reference: Patel, ND, et al. "Diagnostic Accuracy of MRI in Suspected Spinal Infection." AJR Am J Roentgenol, 2014.
  • Significance: A positive MRI result confirming infection or malignancy prompts urgent management, while a negative MRI greatly reduces the likelihood of these conditions.
Elevated ESR and CRP in Spinal Infection
  • Raised inflammatory markers (ESR and CRP) are frequently seen in spinal infections, although they lack specificity and must be interpreted alongside other clinical findings.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: ESR 94%, CRP 88% for spinal infections such as discitis and osteomyelitis.
  • Reference: Carragee EJ. "Diagnostic and Treatment Challenges in Pyogenic Vertebral Osteomyelitis." J Bone Joint Surg Am, 1997.
  • Significance: Elevated ESR/CRP in the presence of back pain and fever is suggestive of infection; however, normal values do not exclude infection entirely.
Unexplained Weight Loss in Spinal Malignancy
  • Unintentional weight loss in a patient with back pain and a history of cancer increases the probability of metastatic spinal disease.
  • Evidence: Positive Predictive Value: 0.16 (16%) for malignancy in patients presenting with back pain and weight loss.
  • Reference: Deyo RA, et al. "Red Flags for Low Back Pain." Arch Intern Med, 1992.
  • Significance: Weight loss, when present with back pain and other systemic symptoms, raises suspicion for malignancy, prompting further imaging and oncology assessment.
Pulsatile Abdominal Mass in Abdominal Aortic Aneurysm (AAA)
  • The presence of a pulsatile mass in a patient with back pain can indicate an AAA, particularly in older adults or those with risk factors.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 68%, Specificity: 75% for detection of AAA.
  • Reference: Lederle FA, et al. "Prevalence of Abdominal Aortic Aneurysms in Older Adults." JAMA, 2000.
  • Significance: A pulsatile mass suggests possible AAA and necessitates urgent ultrasound or CT imaging, as rupture risk is high.
Diagnostic Timeout & Cognitive Biases

Awareness of common thinking errors is essential to improve diagnostic accuracy and patient safety when assessing a patient with back pain. Before finalising a diagnosis, it is good practice for clinicians at all levels to take a 'diagnostic timeout' to challenge their own reasoning.

  • Anchoring Bias: The tendency to lock onto a patient's pre-existing diagnosis of 'chronic back pain' or 'slipped disc' too early. This may cause a clinician to miss new red flag symptoms suggesting a different, more serious pathology, like an infection or malignancy, has developed. A key question to ask is: "Is this just their usual back pain, or is there something different this time?"
  • Confirmation Bias: Actively seeking out or overvaluing evidence that supports a benign, mechanical diagnosis while ignoring contradictory evidence. For example, focusing on tenderness to palpation to confirm a muscle strain, while downplaying a new report of night pain or a history of unexplained weight loss.
  • Viscerogenic Neglect: Forgetting that back pain can be referred from abdominal or pelvic organs. It is a common pitfall to focus exclusively on the spine and musculoskeletal system, thereby missing diagnoses like pancreatitis, peptic ulcer disease, pyelonephritis, or a leaking aortic aneurysm.
  • Ageism and Stereotyping: Unconsciously attributing an older patient's new back pain to 'just arthritis' or 'wear and tear' without seriously considering more sinister causes like an osteoporotic fracture, multiple myeloma, or metastatic cancer, which are more prevalent in this age group.
Illustrative Management Pathways

For educational purposes, this section describes the typical principles of management for key diagnoses. These are not prescriptive instructions. All management must be guided by official UK guidelines, local formularies, and senior clinical advice.

Cauda Equina Syndrome / Spinal Cord Compression

This is a neurosurgical emergency. The immediate pathway involves urgent admission to hospital, an emergency MRI of the whole spine to confirm the diagnosis and locate the level of compression, and immediate referral to the on-call spinal surgery team. Intravenous high-dose corticosteroids (e.g., dexamethasone) may be given on senior advice to reduce oedema around the spinal cord while awaiting definitive surgical decompression.

Spinal Infection (e.g., Discitis, Abscess) or Malignancy

Patients with suspected spinal infection or malignancy require urgent hospital admission for investigation. This includes blood tests (FBC, CRP, ESR, blood cultures), urgent MRI, and often a CT-guided biopsy to obtain tissue for histology and microbiology. Management involves close liaison with spinal surgeons, infectious disease specialists, or oncologists. Treatment typically consists of long-term intravenous antibiotics for infection or specific oncology treatment (radiotherapy, chemotherapy, surgery) for cancer.

Inflammatory Back Pain (e.g., Ankylosing Spondylitis)

If an inflammatory cause is suspected based on history (early morning stiffness, improvement with exercise), the patient should be referred to a rheumatologist. Initial management in primary care involves advising regular exercise and a trial of NSAIDs. In secondary care, diagnosis may be confirmed with imaging (X-ray or MRI of sacroiliac joints) and blood tests (e.g., HLA-B27). Specialist management may include advanced therapies like biologic agents (e.g., anti-TNF drugs).

Non-specific Mechanical Back Pain

Once red flags have been excluded, management focuses on reassurance and symptomatic control, guided by the NICE NG59 pathway. Key components include advising the patient to remain active and continue normal activities as much as possible, providing simple analgesia (paracetamol, or short-course NSAIDs/codeine), and considering referral for physiotherapy if symptoms are not improving. Imaging is not routinely recommended. Crucially, providing clear "safety netting" advice—explaining which red flag symptoms should prompt an urgent re-consultation—is essential.

Reflection & Learning

These brief cases illustrate the application of clinical reasoning to different back pain presentations. They are for educational purposes only.

Case 1: Suspected Cauda Equina Syndrome in A&E

A 45-year-old man presents to the Emergency Department with a 24-hour history of severe central low back pain after lifting a heavy box. He also reports numbness in his groin and buttocks and states he hasn't been able to pass urine for 12 hours, despite feeling a full bladder. The problem representation is a middle-aged man with an acute history of severe back pain accompanied by clear red flags for cauda equina syndrome (saddle anaesthesia, urinary retention). The immediate pathway involves urgent bladder scan, keeping the patient nil-by-mouth, and an emergency discussion with the on-call spinal surgery team for an urgent MRI.

Case 2: Suspected Malignancy in General Practice

A 72-year-old woman with a history of breast cancer treated 8 years ago presents to her GP. For the past month, she has had constant, worsening thoracic back pain that is present even at rest and wakes her from sleep. She has also lost about 5kg in weight without trying. The problem representation is an older woman with a history of cancer presenting with insidious-onset, unrelenting back pain with constitutional symptoms. This is highly concerning for spinal metastases. The GP arranges urgent blood tests (FBC, ESR, calcium) and makes an urgent cancer referral for investigation, which will include an urgent MRI of the spine.

Case 3: Mechanical Back Pain in an Urgent Treatment Centre

A 28-year-old builder attends an urgent treatment centre after feeling a 'pop' in his lower back while lifting cement bags two days ago. He has localised pain in his lower back that is worse when he bends forward and is relieved by lying down. He denies any leg pain, numbness, or changes to his bladder or bowel function. Examination reveals tenderness over the lumbar paraspinal muscles but a full neurological exam of the lower limbs is normal. The problem representation is a young, low-risk man with acute-onset, mechanical-sounding back pain following a clear injury, with no red flags. Management involves reassurance, advice on appropriate analgesia and staying active, and safety netting advice on when to seek further help.
