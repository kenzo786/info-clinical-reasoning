Pruritus Diagnostic Reasoning Reference
Context: Pruritus Assessment
Overview

Pruritus, or itching, is one of the most common symptoms in medicine, prompting consultations in both primary and specialist care. It can range from a localised, minor nuisance associated with a simple skin condition to a severe, generalised symptom heralding a serious underlying systemic disease, such as malignancy or liver failure. The diagnostic challenge lies in distinguishing benign dermatological causes from these more sinister conditions.

A systematic and structured approach is essential to avoid missing red flags while also preventing unnecessary and extensive investigations for common, self-limiting causes. The initial assessment must focus on identifying features that point towards systemic disease, such as jaundice, weight loss, or lymphadenopathy. This educational module is designed to provide a foundational understanding of the clinical reasoning process for pruritus. For definitive, real-time management protocols, users must refer to official UK guidelines from authoritative bodies such as the British Association of Dermatologists (BAD) and NICE.

Pathophysiology/Basic Science

Understanding the origin of pruritus is key to diagnosis. The sensation of itch is transmitted by slow-conducting, unmyelinated C-fibres in the skin, which are distinct from those that transmit pain, although there is significant overlap. Several mechanisms can trigger these nerve fibres:

  • Pruritoceptive Itch: This is the most common type, originating in the skin due to inflammation, dryness, or other damage. It is triggered by pruritogens such as histamine (e.g., in urticaria), serotonin, proteases, and cytokines released from inflammatory cells, keratinocytes, and microbes. This is typical of conditions like eczema, scabies, and insect bites.
  • Neuropathic Itch: This arises from pathology at any point along the afferent nervous pathway. It can be caused by damage to peripheral nerves (e.g., post-herpetic neuralgia, brachioradial pruritus) or the central nervous system (e.g., multiple sclerosis, tumours). The itch is often accompanied by other neurological symptoms like burning or tingling.
  • Neurogenic Itch: This is centrally mediated itch without evidence of neural damage. It is caused by centrally acting mediators, such as opioids or bile salts, that trigger itch pathways in the central nervous system. This is the mechanism behind the pruritus seen in systemic conditions like cholestasis (liver disease) and chronic kidney disease (uraemia).
  • Psychogenic Itch: This is related to primary psychological or psychiatric conditions. The itch may be a primary manifestation of a psychiatric illness (e.g., delusional parasitosis) or an exacerbation of itch due to conditions like anxiety or depression. The diagnosis is one of exclusion after a thorough workup for organic causes.
Differential Diagnosis (DDx) Framework

A structured approach to the differential diagnosis of pruritus is essential to ensure systemic and serious conditions are prioritised.

Must not miss!
  • Cholestasis: Generalised pruritus, often worse on the palms and soles, which may be accompanied by jaundice and dark urine, indicating liver dysfunction.
  • Obstetric cholestasis: Severe pruritus, particularly in the third trimester of pregnancy, which poses risks to the foetus and requires urgent obstetric review.
  • Lymphoma: Persistent, generalised pruritus which may be associated with "B symptoms" such as night sweats, fever, and unexplained weight loss.
  • Leukaemia: Can present with generalised pruritus as a constitutional symptom, often alongside fatigue, easy bruising, and signs of infection.
  • Renal failure: Chronic, intractable pruritus (uraemic pruritus) in patients with advanced kidney disease, often worsening during or after dialysis.
  • HIV: Generalised pruritus can be an early manifestation of HIV infection or a symptom of advanced immunosuppression.
Common causes
  • Dry skin (Xerosis): A very common cause, especially in older adults or in low-humidity environments, presenting as flaky, rough skin that feels tight and itchy.
  • Atopic dermatitis (Eczema): A chronic inflammatory skin condition causing intensely itchy, red, and sometimes weeping patches of skin, typically in flexural areas.
  • Contact dermatitis: An itchy rash caused by direct contact with an allergen (allergic) or an irritant (irritant), localised to the area of exposure.
  • Scabies: Intense, widespread itching that is classically worse at night, caused by a mite infestation, with characteristic burrows often seen between fingers.
  • Urticaria (Hives): Presents with transient, raised, itchy wheals on the skin, often as part of an allergic reaction.
  • Lichen simplex chronicus: A localised area of thickened, leathery skin (lichenification) resulting from persistent scratching and rubbing of a primary itchy patch.
  • Lichen planus: An inflammatory condition causing purplish, flat-topped, itchy bumps, which can affect the skin, mouth, and nails.
  • Psychogenic: Pruritus for which no organic cause is found after thorough investigation, often associated with underlying psychological stress or psychiatric conditions.
  • COVID-19: Can present with a wide range of dermatological symptoms, including generalised pruritus, sometimes without a visible rash.
Easily missed
  • Medication-induced Pruritus: Itching can be a side effect of many common drugs, such as opioids, ACE inhibitors, statins, and certain antibiotics, and may occur without a rash.
  • Hypothyroidism: An underactive thyroid can cause severe dry skin (xerosis), leading to generalised pruritus, often with other signs like fatigue and weight gain.
  • Hyperthyroidism: An overactive thyroid can cause pruritus due to increased skin blood flow and a lowered itch threshold, often alongside symptoms like sweating and anxiety.
  • Iron deficiency anaemia: Pruritus can be a presenting symptom of iron deficiency, which may be subtle and easily overlooked if not specifically tested for.
  • Polycythaemia: A rare blood disorder that can cause aquagenic pruritus, a characteristic itching or stinging sensation triggered by contact with water.
Other Differential Diagnoses to consider
  • COVID-19: Can present with pruritus as a generalised symptom, even without a typical rash.
  • Diabetes Mellitus: Often associated with pruritus, particularly in cases of poorly controlled blood sugar levels.
  • Primary Biliary Cholangitis (PBC): A chronic liver disease that frequently leads to pruritus, especially in females.
  • Multiple Myeloma: Rarely, pruritus can present as a symptom in patients with underlying plasma cell disorders.
  • Psoriasis: While commonly associated with plaques, pruritus can occur, especially in inverse psoriasis.
  • Parasitic infections (e.g., strongyloidiasis): Often missed, especially in patients with a travel history, and may lead to generalised pruritus.
  • Systemic Lupus Erythematosus (SLE): Can occasionally cause pruritus, even in the absence of skin lesions.
  • Cutaneous T-cell Lymphoma (CTCL): A rare type of lymphoma that primarily affects the skin and can lead to persistent pruritus.
History Taking
Patient’s Description: How can I help you today?

Starting with open-ended questions allows the patient to describe their symptoms in their own words. This approach can reveal specific language or nuances that guide further, more targeted questions.

Relevance: An open-ended start provides clues to the symptom's duration, progression, and impact on the patient’s life, helping shape a personalised approach.
Can you describe how the itching feels?
  • This question helps to establish the quality of the pruritus, such as if it is burning, tingling, or crawling. Descriptive language may suggest specific causes; for example, "crawling" or "biting" sensations might point towards parasitic infestations, while burning might suggest neuropathic origins.
  • Relevance: Descriptions like “burning” could indicate neuropathy or irritant dermatitis, whereas “crawling” may suggest parasitic causes or psychological conditions.
When did you first notice the itching? Has it changed over time?
  • Understanding onset and progression helps differentiate acute from chronic causes. Acute onset might suggest allergic reactions or irritant contact dermatitis, while chronic progression could indicate systemic issues like renal failure or cholestasis.
  • Relevance: A recent onset might suggest acute dermatitis or allergic reactions, while chronic pruritus could be linked to systemic causes such as renal or liver disease.
Are there any factors that make it better or worse?
  • Learning about aggravating or alleviating factors can pinpoint triggers like soaps, temperature changes, or stress, which are more typical of dermatological conditions. Pruritus that worsens at night may suggest scabies.
  • Relevance: Worsening at night could indicate scabies, while triggers related to certain fabrics or soaps might suggest contact dermatitis.
Have you noticed any associated symptoms, such as a rash, jaundice, or weight loss?
  • Associated symptoms can be critical for narrowing the differential. Rash may indicate dermatitis or scabies; jaundice could suggest liver disease; weight loss might raise suspicion for malignancy.
  • Relevance: A positive for rash could point towards dermatological causes, while jaundice is a red flag for liver-related pruritus, and weight loss could indicate malignancy.
Do you have any long-term medical conditions, or have you recently started any new medications?
  • Long-term conditions like renal failure or diabetes can predispose to pruritus. Medication-induced pruritus is also common, particularly with drugs like opioids, ACE inhibitors, and statins.
  • Relevance: Chronic illnesses (e.g., renal failure) can explain systemic causes of pruritus. New medications can suggest a drug-induced reaction.
Has anyone in your family had similar issues with itching?
  • Family history can highlight conditions like atopic dermatitis, which may have a genetic component. Additionally, certain systemic diseases like cholestasis can occur in familial patterns.
  • Relevance: A family history of atopy increases the likelihood of atopic dermatitis; a familial occurrence of pruritus may also indicate hereditary cholestasis.
Ideas, Concerns, and Expectations (ICE)
  • Exploring ICE allows the clinician to address the patient’s thoughts, worries, and what they hope to achieve from the visit, enhancing patient-centred care.
  • Relevance: Helps clinicians identify any fears (e.g., worry about cancer or infection) or expectations (e.g., wanting relief, testing) to tailor the consultation accordingly.
Examination Strategy
General Inspection of Skin
  • A thorough inspection of the skin is essential to identify any visible lesions, rash, excoriations, lichenification, or jaundice. This step helps determine if the pruritus is due to a primary dermatological condition or secondary to systemic illness.
  • Relevance: Positive findings of lesions or rash may suggest eczema, contact dermatitis, or scabies. Jaundice would raise suspicion of liver disease, while a lack of visible skin changes might suggest systemic or psychogenic causes.
Palpation of Skin
  • Palpation assesses for skin texture changes, warmth, or areas of thickening, such as lichenification. Skin that feels dry might indicate xerosis (dry skin), which is a common cause of pruritus in older adults.
  • Relevance: Dry or rough skin suggests xerosis, while thickened skin indicates chronic scratching, often seen in lichen simplex chronicus. Warmth or erythema may suggest infection or inflammation.
Examination of the Nails
  • Checking the nails for signs of excessive scratching (e.g., nail ridges) can provide clues about the chronicity and severity of the pruritus. Ridged or damaged nails from scratching may point toward psychogenic pruritus or chronic dermatological conditions.
  • Relevance: Positive findings of damaged nails support a chronic itching behaviour, often seen in psychological or dermatological conditions. Normal nails might suggest an acute cause.
Inspection for Signs of Jaundice
  • Inspecting for jaundice (yellowing of the skin or sclera) is crucial, as pruritus can be an early symptom of cholestasis and liver dysfunction.
  • Relevance: The presence of jaundice suggests liver-related pruritus (e.g., cholestasis, hepatitis), while its absence reduces the likelihood of liver disease as the underlying cause.
Lymph Node Examination
  • Palpating for lymphadenopathy (swollen lymph nodes) can help identify possible systemic causes like lymphoma or leukaemia, both of which can present with pruritus.
  • Relevance: Enlarged lymph nodes suggest haematological malignancies or infections, while the absence of lymphadenopathy may make these conditions less likely.
Thyroid Examination
  • Assessing for thyroid enlargement or nodules, along with associated symptoms like weight changes or fatigue, can help identify thyroid disease as a potential cause of pruritus.
  • Relevance: A palpable thyroid enlargement could suggest hyperthyroidism or hypothyroidism, while a normal thyroid exam reduces the likelihood of thyroid-related causes.
Abdominal Examination
  • An abdominal examination can help detect hepatosplenomegaly, which may indicate underlying liver disease, haematological malignancies, or systemic diseases associated with pruritus.
  • Relevance: Positive findings of hepatomegaly or splenomegaly could suggest liver disease or lymphoma. A normal abdominal examination might reduce the likelihood of these systemic conditions.
Red Flag Synthesis
Unexplained Weight Loss
  • Unintentional weight loss alongside pruritus may indicate an underlying malignancy, such as lymphoma or other systemic cancers, where pruritus is a paraneoplastic symptom.
  • Significance: This could indicate a hidden malignancy or other serious systemic disease, warranting thorough investigation.
Night Sweats and Fever
  • Night sweats and fever with pruritus may suggest a haematological malignancy, such as lymphoma or leukaemia, where these "B symptoms" are common features.
  • Significance: The presence of these symptoms raises suspicion for a haematological malignancy and warrants urgent evaluation.
Jaundice (Yellowing of the Skin or Eyes)
  • Pruritus associated with jaundice suggests hepatobiliary disease, including cholestasis, hepatitis, or even a hepatic malignancy. This combination often points towards significant liver dysfunction.
  • Significance: Jaundice with pruritus may indicate liver disease or obstructive pathology, such as biliary obstruction, necessitating urgent investigation.
Generalised Lymphadenopathy
  • Enlarged lymph nodes, especially when generalised, may indicate an underlying haematological malignancy such as lymphoma or leukaemia, both of which can present with pruritus.
  • Significance: Generalised lymphadenopathy could signify haematological cancers or infectious diseases, requiring prompt workup.
Severe Pruritus in Pregnancy
  • Severe pruritus in pregnancy, especially in the third trimester, can be a sign of obstetric cholestasis, a liver condition that can increase the risk of preterm birth and foetal distress.
  • Significance: Obstetric cholestasis is a significant condition that requires close monitoring and intervention to reduce perinatal risks.
Severe, Persistent Pruritus with No Skin Findings
  • Persistent pruritus without any visible skin findings can point towards systemic diseases such as chronic renal failure, thyroid disorders, or hidden malignancies.
  • Significance: This presentation necessitates a workup for systemic causes, as it may indicate an underlying serious disease.
Investigations Strategy & Interpretation
Full Blood Count (FBC)
  • An FBC can help identify anaemia, polycythaemia, and signs of infection or malignancy. Raised eosinophils might suggest an allergic or parasitic cause, while other abnormalities could indicate haematological diseases.
  • Result Significance: Raised white cell count with eosinophilia may suggest allergies or parasitic infection, while anaemia or abnormal white cell counts may indicate malignancies or systemic diseases.
Liver Function Tests (LFTs)
  • LFTs assess for liver dysfunction, a common cause of pruritus in cholestatic conditions, hepatitis, and other liver pathologies. Elevated bilirubin and alkaline phosphatase are particularly associated with cholestasis.
  • Result Significance: Elevated bilirubin and alkaline phosphatase suggest cholestasis, which may indicate liver disease or obstruction, while normal LFTs make hepatic causes less likely.
Renal Function Tests (U&Es)
  • U&Es assess kidney function, as chronic renal failure can be associated with pruritus. Raised urea or creatinine levels would indicate renal insufficiency.
  • Result Significance: Elevated urea or creatinine indicates renal impairment, supporting a diagnosis of uraemic pruritus. Normal renal function reduces the likelihood of kidney-related causes.
Thyroid Function Tests (TFTs)
  • Abnormal thyroid function (hyperthyroidism or hypothyroidism) can lead to pruritus. TFTs help assess thyroid hormone levels to identify or exclude thyroid disorders.
  • Result Significance: Abnormal TSH or thyroid hormone levels indicate thyroid disease, which can cause pruritus, while normal levels rule out thyroid-related causes.
Serum Ferritin and Iron Studies
  • Iron deficiency anaemia can present with pruritus. Ferritin and iron studies can identify or exclude iron deficiency as an underlying cause.
  • Result Significance: Low ferritin levels suggest iron deficiency, which could contribute to pruritus. Normal levels make iron deficiency less likely.
HIV Testing
  • Pruritus can be an early symptom of HIV. Testing is indicated for patients with risk factors or unexplained chronic pruritus.
  • Result Significance: Positive HIV test may explain pruritus in the context of immunodeficiency. A negative test rules out HIV as a cause.
Chest X-ray (CXR)
  • A CXR is useful to assess for mediastinal lymphadenopathy or lung lesions, which could suggest lymphoma, a potential cause of systemic pruritus.
  • Result Significance: Mediastinal lymphadenopathy or lung masses raise suspicion for lymphoma or other malignancies, while a normal CXR makes these causes less likely.
Skin Biopsy
  • If there is a persistent rash or suspected dermatological condition (e.g., lichen planus, psoriasis), a skin biopsy can aid diagnosis by providing histological information.
  • Result Significance: Positive histopathological findings can confirm dermatological diagnoses, while normal histology may suggest systemic causes.
Evidence-Based Diagnosis (EBDx)
Jaundice and Liver Function Tests (LFTs) for Cholestatic Pruritus
  • Cholestasis is a common cause of pruritus associated with liver dysfunction. Elevated levels of bilirubin and alkaline phosphatase are highly indicative of cholestasis.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 80%, Specificity: 95% for cholestasis with elevated bilirubin and alkaline phosphatase.
  • Significance: Positive results with jaundice strongly suggest cholestatic liver disease, while normal LFTs reduce the likelihood of a hepatic origin.
Night Sweats, Weight Loss, and Lymphadenopathy for Haematological Malignancies
  • Night sweats, weight loss, and lymphadenopathy are concerning for malignancies like lymphoma, which is a potential cause of pruritus. The presence of all three symptoms significantly raises suspicion.
  • Evidence: Combined sensitivity for B symptoms (night sweats, weight loss, fever) in lymphoma: 75%, Specificity: 85%.
  • Significance: The presence of B symptoms and lymphadenopathy strongly suggests lymphoma, while their absence lowers the probability of haematological malignancy.
Skin Changes and Positive Skin Biopsy for Dermatological Conditions
  • Skin changes such as erythema, scaling, or lichenification, along with biopsy findings, can confirm specific dermatological conditions like psoriasis or lichen simplex chronicus.
  • Evidence: Biopsy for diagnosis of lichen planus: Sensitivity 90%, Specificity 92%.
  • Significance: A positive biopsy supports a dermatological cause of pruritus, while absence of skin changes and a normal biopsy could suggest a systemic or psychogenic origin.
Elevated Eosinophil Count for Allergic or Parasitic Causes
  • An elevated eosinophil count is associated with allergic reactions and parasitic infections, both of which can cause pruritus.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity of eosinophilia for parasitic infections: 70%, Specificity: 80%.
  • Significance: Elevated eosinophils suggest allergic or parasitic causes, while a normal eosinophil count decreases the likelihood of these conditions.
Abnormal Thyroid Function Tests (TFTs) for Thyroid Disease-Associated Pruritus
  • Both hyperthyroidism and hypothyroidism can cause pruritus. Abnormal TSH or thyroid hormone levels help confirm thyroid dysfunction as a contributing factor.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity of abnormal TFTs in pruritus caused by thyroid disease: 60-70%, Specificity: 85%.
  • Significance: Positive findings on TFTs support thyroid disease as a cause of pruritus, while normal TFTs reduce the likelihood of thyroid-related pruritus.
Diagnostic Timeout & Cognitive Biases

Awareness of common thinking errors is essential to improve diagnostic accuracy when assessing a patient with pruritus. Before finalising a diagnosis, it is good practice for clinicians to take a 'diagnostic timeout' to challenge their own reasoning.

  • Anchoring Bias: The tendency to lock onto an early, common diagnosis like 'dry skin' or 'eczema', especially in an older patient, while failing to adequately explore red flag symptoms like weight loss or fatigue that might suggest a systemic cause. A key question to ask is: "What is the most serious cause of this itch, and have I confidently excluded it?"
  • Confirmation Bias: Actively seeking out or overemphasising evidence that supports an initial belief. For example, focusing on excoriation marks to confirm a primary skin condition while downplaying the significance of a new nocturnal itch in a patient's household contacts, which might point towards scabies.
  • Attribution Error: Prematurely attributing pruritus to a patient's known anxiety or psychological state ('psychogenic itch') without first conducting a thorough investigation to rule out all potential organic causes. This is a diagnosis of exclusion and should be made with caution.
  • Over-reliance on a Normal Skin Exam: A major pitfall is forgetting that severe systemic diseases can cause intense, generalised pruritus with no primary skin findings at all (pruritus sine materia). The absence of a rash does not rule out serious pathology like lymphoma, liver disease, or renal failure.
Illustrative Management Pathways

For educational purposes, this section describes the typical principles of management for key diagnoses. These are not prescriptive instructions. All management must be guided by official UK guidelines, local formularies, and senior clinical advice.

Systemic Disease (e.g., Cholestasis, Renal Failure)

The cornerstone of management is to treat the underlying condition. This requires referral to the appropriate specialty (e.g., gastroenterology, nephrology). Symptomatic management is also crucial. For cholestatic pruritus, treatments may include bile acid sequestrants (e.g., colestyramine) or rifampicin. For uraemic pruritus, optimising dialysis, using emollients, and considering treatments like gabapentin or phototherapy are key strategies.

Dermatological Conditions (e.g., Eczema, Scabies)

Management focuses on treating the specific skin disease. For inflammatory conditions like eczema, this involves a stepwise approach with frequent emollient use, topical corticosteroids of appropriate potency, and sometimes calcineurin inhibitors. For infestations like scabies, treatment with topical permethrin or oral ivermectin for the patient and all close contacts is required, alongside advice on decontaminating bedding and clothing.

Generalised Idiopathic Pruritus

Once systemic and dermatological causes have been thoroughly excluded, management focuses on breaking the itch-scratch cycle and providing symptomatic relief. This includes regular use of emollients, avoiding irritants (like soaps and hot water), and using sedating antihistamines (e.g., hydroxyzine) at night to aid sleep. For severe, refractory cases, referral to dermatology for consideration of treatments like phototherapy, gabapentin, or antidepressants with antihistaminergic properties (e.g., doxepin) may be necessary.

Reflection & Learning

These brief cases illustrate the application of clinical reasoning to different pruritus presentations. They are for educational purposes only.

Case 1: Suspected Obstetric Cholestasis

A 32-year-old woman, 34 weeks pregnant, presents to her GP with a 1-week history of intense, widespread itching that is keeping her awake at night. The itching is particularly severe on the palms of her hands and soles of her feet. There is no rash. The problem representation is a woman in the third trimester of pregnancy with severe pruritus of a cholestatic pattern, highly suspicious for obstetric cholestasis. The immediate pathway involves urgent blood tests (LFTs and bile acids) and same-day referral to the maternity assessment unit for further evaluation and monitoring due to potential risks to the fetus.

Case 2: Suspected Lymphoma

An 80-year-old man presents with a 3-month history of debilitating, generalised pruritus. His skin is covered in scratch marks, but there is no primary rash. He mentions feeling unusually tired and reports that his pyjamas are often damp with sweat overnight. He thinks he may have lost some weight. The problem representation is an older man with pruritus sine materia associated with constitutional 'B symptoms' (fatigue, night sweats, weight loss), raising a strong suspicion of an underlying haematological malignancy like lymphoma. The pathway involves urgent blood tests (FBC, U&Es, LFTs, LDH), a chest X-ray, and an urgent cancer pathway referral for further investigation.

Case 3: Scabies in a Care Home

A practice nurse is asked to see a 78-year-old resident of a care home with a very itchy rash. The itching is severe and worse at night. On examination, there are excoriated papules on her trunk and limbs, with some fine, grey lines visible in the web spaces of her fingers. The nurse learns that two other residents and one staff member have also been complaining of a similar itch. The problem representation is an individual in a residential setting with a classic history of nocturnal itch and typical lesions (burrows), with other contacts affected, pathognomonic for scabies. Management involves confirming the diagnosis, prescribing treatment for the patient and all residents/staff, and implementing institutional infection control measures.
