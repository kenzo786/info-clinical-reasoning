Muscle Pain and Weakness Diagnostic Reasoning Reference
Context: Muscle Pain and Weakness Assessment
Overview

Muscle pain (myalgia) and weakness are frequent and challenging clinical presentations, encountered across all medical specialties. The diagnostic spectrum is vast, ranging from benign, self-limiting conditions like viral myalgia to severe, life-threatening emergencies such as rhabdomyolysis or Guillain-Barré syndrome. The primary clinical challenge is to effectively differentiate between primary muscle diseases (myopathies), nerve disorders (neuropathies), neuromuscular junction defects, and systemic illnesses with musculoskeletal manifestations.

A systematic and structured approach is essential to identify red flags, risk-stratify patients, and guide appropriate investigation. The initial assessment must focus on distinguishing between the common and benign, and the rare but critical diagnoses that require urgent intervention. This ensures patient safety while avoiding unnecessary investigation for self-limiting causes.

This educational module is designed to build a foundational understanding of the clinical reasoning process for muscle pain and weakness. For definitive, real-time management protocols, users must refer to official UK guidelines from authoritative bodies such as NICE (e.g., NG192 for Motor Neurone Disease, NG12 for Fibromyalgia) and specialised clinical societies.

Pathophysiology/Basic Science

Understanding the origin of muscle pain and weakness is key to diagnosis. Symptoms can be generated through several distinct pathophysiological mechanisms, each pointing towards a different set of differential diagnoses:

  • Direct Muscle Injury and Inflammation: This occurs when muscle fibres (myocytes) are damaged directly. In trauma or overuse injuries, mechanical stress leads to micro-tears and a localised inflammatory response. In inflammatory myopathies (e.g., polymyositis), an autoimmune attack on muscle tissue causes widespread inflammation. In rhabdomyolysis, extensive myocyte necrosis leads to the release of intracellular contents like creatine kinase (CK) and myoglobin into the circulation, which can cause systemic effects like acute kidney injury.
  • Neuropathic Dysfunction: Weakness can arise from pathology anywhere along the motor neuron, from the spinal cord to the axon terminal. In peripheral neuropathies (e.g., due to diabetes or vitamin B12 deficiency), axonal damage or demyelination impairs signal conduction, resulting in distal weakness, sensory loss, and reduced reflexes. In Guillain-Barré syndrome, an acute autoimmune attack on the myelin sheath of peripheral nerves causes rapidly ascending paralysis.
  • Neuromuscular Junction (NMJ) Failure: Conditions like Myasthenia Gravis interfere with the transmission of nerve impulses across the NMJ. Autoantibodies block or destroy acetylcholine receptors on the muscle cell surface, preventing muscle contraction and causing characteristic fatigable weakness (strength decreases with repeated effort).
  • Metabolic and Endocrine Disruption: Muscle function is highly dependent on a stable internal environment. Electrolyte disturbances (e.g., hypokalaemia, hypocalcaemia) alter the resting membrane potential of muscle cells, impairing their ability to contract. Endocrine disorders like hypothyroidism slow down cellular metabolism, including energy production and protein synthesis within muscle cells, leading to a generalised, non-inflammatory myopathy with weakness and fatigue.
  • Impaired Energy Metabolism: Inborn errors of metabolism or mitochondrial diseases directly affect the ability of muscle cells to produce ATP, the energy currency for contraction and relaxation. This results in exercise intolerance, profound fatigue, and myalgia. Similarly, severe Vitamin D deficiency is thought to impair mitochondrial function and calcium handling within the muscle cell.
  • Central Sensitisation: In conditions like fibromyalgia and chronic fatigue syndrome, the primary pathology is not thought to be in the peripheral nerves or muscles themselves. Instead, these conditions are associated with neurobiological changes in the central nervous system (CNS). This leads to an amplification of pain signals (allodynia and hyperalgesia) and a perception of profound fatigue and weakness, even in the absence of objective muscle pathology.
Differential Diagnosis (DDx) Framework

A structured approach to the differential diagnosis of muscle pain and weakness is essential to ensure life-threatening conditions are prioritised.

Must not miss!
  • Statin related (inc Rhabdomyolysis): Presents as symmetrical, proximal muscle aches and weakness, which in rare cases can progress to severe rhabdomyolysis with dark urine and acute kidney injury.
  • Cancer cachexia: Profound muscle wasting and weakness associated with an underlying malignancy, often accompanied by significant weight loss and anorexia.
  • Peripheral Arterial Disease: Causes cramp-like muscle pain (claudication) in the lower limbs on exertion, which is relieved by rest, due to arterial insufficiency.
  • Guillain-Barré syndrome: An acute, rapidly progressive, symmetrical ascending weakness, often following an infection, which can lead to respiratory failure.
Common causes
  • Sprains and strains (including overuse): Localised muscle pain and weakness following acute injury or repetitive activity, with tenderness and pain on movement.
  • Viral infections: Generalised muscle aches (myalgia) and fatigue are common during acute viral illnesses like influenza or COVID-19.
  • Arthropathy presentation: Joint pain and inflammation from arthritis can lead to secondary muscle weakness and disuse atrophy around the affected joint.
  • Disuse atrophy: A gradual loss of muscle mass and strength resulting from prolonged immobility, bed rest, or a sedentary lifestyle.
  • Vitamin D deficiency: Can cause diffuse bone pain, muscle aches (myalgia), and proximal muscle weakness.
  • Mononeuropathy: Weakness and sensory changes confined to the distribution of a single nerve, often due to compression (e.g., carpal tunnel syndrome).
  • Hypothyroidism: Typically causes gradual onset of fatigue, lethargy, and proximal muscle weakness, often associated with other signs of a slow metabolism.
  • Fibromyalgia: Characterised by chronic widespread pain, fatigue, unrefreshing sleep, and multiple tender points on examination, without objective weakness.
  • Chronic fatigue syndrome: A primary complaint of severe, disabling fatigue, often accompanied by myalgia, post-exertional malaise, and cognitive difficulties.
  • Psychogenic: Weakness that is inconsistent on examination, with a non-organic pattern, often in the context of significant psychological distress.
  • COVID-19: Can present with acute myalgia and fatigue, and can be followed by prolonged post-viral fatigue and weakness.
Easily missed
  • Connective tissue diseases: Conditions like lupus (SLE) or Sjögren's syndrome can present with fatigue, myalgia, and arthralgia as part of a systemic inflammatory process.
  • Polymyalgia rheumatica: Typically causes pain and stiffness in the shoulder and pelvic girdles in patients over 50, which is worse in the mornings, and associated with a high ESR/CRP.
  • Polymyositis: An inflammatory myopathy causing progressive, symmetrical proximal muscle weakness, making tasks like climbing stairs or rising from a chair difficult.
  • Peripheral neuropathy: Symmetrical, distal ("glove and stocking") sensory loss, numbness, and weakness, often caused by diabetes or alcohol excess.
  • Diabetic neuropathy: A common complication of diabetes, leading to sensory and motor nerve damage, typically affecting the feet and legs first.
  • Alcohol abuse: Can cause an acute or chronic myopathy with muscle pain and weakness, in addition to a peripheral neuropathy.
  • Vitamin B12 deficiency: May present with neurological symptoms, including peripheral neuropathy and subacute combined degeneration of the cord, leading to weakness and sensory ataxia.
  • Metabolic: Electrolyte disturbances (e.g., hypokalaemia, hypercalcaemia) can cause acute muscle weakness.
  • Heavy metal poisoning: Chronic exposure (e.g., to lead or mercury) can cause a range of neurological symptoms, including a motor neuropathy and weakness.
  • Paraneoplastic syndrome: Neuromuscular syndromes (e.g., Lambert-Eaton myasthenic syndrome) can be the first presentation of an underlying cancer.
  • Medication-induced Muscle Pain and Weakness: A side effect of various drugs, most notably statins but also including steroids, fibrates, and some antiretrovirals.
Other Differential Diagnoses to consider
  • Myasthenia gravis: Autoimmune condition causing fatigable muscle weakness.
  • Electrolyte imbalances: Hypokalaemia or hypocalcaemia resulting in muscle weakness.
  • Infectious myositis: Muscle inflammation due to bacterial or parasitic infection.
  • Chronic kidney disease (CKD): Muscle weakness linked to uraemic myopathy.
  • Mitochondrial disorders: Genetic conditions affecting muscle energy metabolism.
  • Sarcoidosis: Granulomatous inflammation potentially involving muscles.
  • Hyperparathyroidism: Myopathy due to altered calcium homeostasis.
  • COVID-19: Post-viral myalgia or associated myopathy in severe cases.
History Taking
Patient’s Description: How can I help you today?

Starting with an open-ended question like, "Can you tell me more about what brought you here today?" allows the patient to describe their symptoms in their own words. This helps gather a clear picture of their experience and sets the stage for targeted follow-up questions.

Relevance: Gives the clinician insight into the symptom quality, severity, and patient priorities, which are essential for refining the differential diagnosis.
Can you describe the nature of the muscle pain and/or weakness?
  • Ask about the quality (e.g., cramping, burning, dull ache) and whether weakness is localised or generalised.
  • Relevance: Cramping or burning pain might suggest neuropathy or rhabdomyolysis. Generalised weakness could suggest hypothyroidism or vitamin deficiency, while focal weakness might indicate a neuropathy or mononeuropathy.
When did the symptoms start, and have they changed over time?
  • Explore onset (acute vs gradual), duration, and progression of symptoms.
  • Relevance: Sudden onset could suggest Guillain-Barré syndrome or an acute inflammatory myopathy. Gradual onset may indicate hypothyroidism, vitamin deficiencies, or chronic illnesses like fibromyalgia.
Are there any triggers or activities that exacerbate or relieve your symptoms?
  • Ask about associations with physical activity, rest, or specific positions.
  • Relevance: Symptoms worsened by activity may point to PAD or overuse injuries. Relieved by rest may suggest disuse atrophy or chronic fatigue syndrome.
Do you experience any associated symptoms, such as fever, fatigue, or numbness?
  • Ask about systemic symptoms, neurological symptoms, or joint involvement.
  • Relevance: Fever may suggest infectious myositis. Fatigue can indicate hypothyroidism or fibromyalgia. Numbness could point to peripheral neuropathy or diabetic neuropathy.
Have you had any recent infections, injuries, or significant lifestyle changes?
  • Determine if recent viral illnesses, trauma, or immobility are present.
  • Relevance: Viral infections may explain post-viral myalgia. Injuries can suggest strains or rhabdomyolysis. Lifestyle changes may point to disuse atrophy or psychogenic causes.
Do you have a history of chronic conditions such as diabetes, thyroid disorders, or autoimmune diseases?
  • Review the patient’s past medical history for contributing conditions.
  • Relevance: Diabetes raises suspicion for diabetic neuropathy. Hypothyroidism or autoimmune conditions could explain chronic or inflammatory myopathies.
Have you noticed any changes in medications, or are you taking any medications like statins?
  • Ask about new prescriptions, over-the-counter drugs, or changes to existing regimens.
  • Relevance: Statins are a major cause of medication-induced myopathy. Corticosteroids or colchicine might also lead to muscle weakness.
Ideas, Concerns, and Expectations (ICE)
  • Ask the patient: "What do you think might be causing this?", "Is there something specific you are worried about?", and "What were you hoping we might be able to do for you today?"
  • Relevance: Helps understand the patient’s perspective, addresses specific concerns (e.g., cancer, neurological disease), and aligns management with their expectations.
Examination Strategy
General Inspection
  • Observe the patient for signs of muscle wasting, asymmetry, abnormal posture, or visible tremors.
  • Relevance: Muscle wasting may indicate disuse atrophy, cancer cachexia, or chronic neurological conditions. Asymmetry can point towards focal neuropathies or mononeuropathies. Absence of these findings may steer towards systemic causes like fibromyalgia or hypothyroidism.
Palpation of Muscles
  • Palpate muscles for tenderness, tone, and bulk.
  • Relevance: Tenderness might suggest inflammation (e.g., polymyositis) or local trauma (e.g., strain). Decreased bulk suggests atrophy due to disuse or neurogenic causes, while increased tone may indicate spasticity seen in upper motor neuron lesions.
Assessment of Muscle Strength
  • Test muscle strength in major muscle groups, grading it on the Medical Research Council (MRC) scale (0-5).
  • Relevance: Proximal weakness suggests conditions like polymyositis, hypothyroidism, or statin-related myopathy. Distal weakness might indicate peripheral neuropathy or Guillain-Barré syndrome. Symmetrical weakness supports systemic causes, while asymmetry may suggest focal neuropathies.
Neurological Examination: Reflexes
  • Assess deep tendon reflexes, such as the patellar and Achilles reflexes.
  • Relevance: Reduced reflexes may indicate peripheral neuropathy or Guillain-Barré syndrome. Hyperreflexia suggests upper motor neuron involvement, such as in a stroke or spinal cord lesion.
Neurological Examination: Sensory Testing
  • Check for sensory deficits (light touch, vibration, proprioception, pinprick).
  • Relevance: Sensory loss supports peripheral neuropathy (e.g., diabetic neuropathy, B12 deficiency). A normal sensory exam helps rule out neuropathic causes, narrowing the focus to myopathies.
Gait Assessment
  • Observe the patient walking and assess their ability to rise from a chair without using their arms.
  • Relevance: Difficulty rising from a chair suggests proximal muscle weakness, typical in myopathies like polymyositis. A waddling gait indicates hip girdle weakness, while a foot drop gait points to peripheral nerve damage.
Cardiovascular Examination
  • Check for peripheral pulses, signs of claudication, or bruits.
  • Relevance: Absent or diminished pulses suggest peripheral arterial disease. Normal findings help rule out vascular causes of muscle pain.
Joint Examination
  • Examine for joint swelling, tenderness, or reduced range of motion.
  • Relevance: Joint involvement may indicate an inflammatory arthritis or polymyalgia rheumatica. A normal joint exam helps rule out arthropathies as a cause.
Red Flag Synthesis
Severe, rapidly progressing muscle weakness
  • This symptom could indicate Guillain-Barré syndrome, a neurological emergency characterised by ascending weakness and potential respiratory compromise.
  • Significance: Guillain-Barré syndrome requires immediate evaluation and potential hospitalisation for monitoring and treatment.
Dark urine or reduced urine output
  • Dark urine may suggest rhabdomyolysis, a condition caused by muscle breakdown, which can lead to acute kidney injury if not promptly treated.
  • Significance: Rhabdomyolysis requires urgent evaluation with blood tests (e.g., CK levels) and management to prevent renal failure.
Persistent, unexplained weight loss
  • Unexplained weight loss in the context of muscle weakness may indicate an underlying malignancy such as cancer cachexia or a paraneoplastic syndrome.
  • Significance: Requires urgent investigation, including imaging and blood tests, to rule out cancer or other systemic conditions.
Severe pain out of proportion to findings
  • This could indicate a vascular emergency such as acute limb ischaemia or compartment syndrome.
  • Significance: Both conditions are surgical emergencies requiring immediate intervention to restore circulation or relieve pressure.
Proximal muscle weakness with systemic symptoms (e.g., fever, rash)
  • May suggest an inflammatory myopathy like polymyositis or dermatomyositis, which can also be associated with malignancy or autoimmune disease.
  • Significance: Needs early diagnosis and treatment to prevent complications and assess for associated malignancies or systemic conditions.
Neurological deficits (e.g., numbness, tingling, or difficulty with coordination)
  • Neurological symptoms alongside muscle weakness may indicate peripheral neuropathy, spinal cord compression, or central nervous system pathology.
  • Significance: Urgent imaging and neurological assessment are required to determine the cause and prevent long-term deficits.
Signs of systemic illness (e.g., night sweats, fever, lymphadenopathy)
  • These may point towards an underlying malignancy, systemic infection, or autoimmune condition.
  • Significance: Requires thorough systemic evaluation to identify the cause and guide treatment.
Rapid onset of severe muscle pain after medication changes
  • Suggests statin-induced myopathy or other medication-related myopathies such as those caused by corticosteroids or colchicine.
  • Significance: Requires immediate discontinuation of the causative agent and supportive care.
Investigations Strategy & Interpretation
Blood Tests: Creatine Kinase (CK)
  • CK levels help assess muscle damage and are elevated in conditions like rhabdomyolysis, polymyositis, or statin-induced myopathy.
  • Result Significance: High levels suggest muscle breakdown or inflammation, supporting diagnoses such as rhabdomyolysis or polymyositis. Normal levels may help rule out these conditions.
Blood Tests: Thyroid Function Tests (TFTs)
  • TFTs are important to detect hypothyroidism, a common reversible cause of muscle weakness and myopathy.
  • Result Significance: Low thyroid hormone levels (e.g., T3, T4) with high TSH indicate hypothyroidism. Normal levels rule out thyroid-related causes.
Electromyography (EMG) and Nerve Conduction Studies
  • Used to evaluate muscle and nerve function, helping distinguish between myopathies and neuropathies.
  • Result Significance: Myopathic patterns suggest conditions like polymyositis, while neuropathic patterns support diagnoses like peripheral neuropathy or Guillain-Barré syndrome.
Blood Tests: Vitamin D and B12 Levels
  • These tests help identify deficiencies that can cause muscle pain and weakness.
  • Result Significance: Low levels indicate vitamin deficiencies as the likely cause, while normal levels help rule out nutritional aetiologies.
Inflammatory Markers: ESR and CRP
  • Elevated ESR or CRP indicates systemic inflammation, which is seen in polymyositis, dermatomyositis, or polymyalgia rheumatica.
  • Result Significance: High levels support an inflammatory or autoimmune condition. Normal levels suggest against these diagnoses.
Autoantibody Testing
  • Tests for specific autoantibodies (e.g., ANA, anti-Jo-1) help diagnose autoimmune conditions such as polymyositis or lupus.
  • Result Significance: Positive results confirm autoimmune involvement, while negative results make such conditions less likely.
Liver and Kidney Function Tests
  • These tests assess for end-organ damage or metabolic conditions contributing to muscle symptoms, such as rhabdomyolysis or chronic kidney disease.
  • Result Significance: Abnormal results indicate systemic involvement. Normal results rule out organ dysfunction-related causes.
MRI or CT Imaging of Affected Areas
  • Useful to detect soft tissue abnormalities, inflammation, or tumours affecting muscles.
  • Result Significance: Positive findings suggest focal conditions like tumours or myositis. Negative imaging helps exclude structural causes.
Biopsy of Muscle Tissue
  • Provides definitive diagnosis in cases of suspected inflammatory myopathies, infections, or neoplasms.
  • Result Significance: Pathological findings confirm inflammatory or neoplastic aetiologies. Normal findings rule out these conditions.
Evidence-Based Diagnosis (EBDx)
Elevated Creatine Kinase (CK) Levels
  • CK levels are a marker of muscle injury or breakdown, elevated in conditions like rhabdomyolysis, polymyositis, and statin-induced myopathy.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 94%, Specificity: 87% for detecting inflammatory myopathies (e.g., polymyositis). Likelihood Ratio (LR): Positive: 7.2, Negative: 0.07.
  • Reference: Dalakas MC, "Inflammatory Muscle Diseases," NEJM, 2015.
  • Significance: High CK supports diagnoses involving muscle damage; normal CK levels help rule out significant myopathies.
Proximal Muscle Weakness
  • Proximal weakness is characteristic of myopathies, including polymyositis, dermatomyositis, and hypothyroidism-induced myopathy.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 78%, Specificity: 92% for inflammatory myopathies when associated with systemic symptoms.
  • Reference: Bohan A and Peter JB, "Diagnostic Criteria for Polymyositis and Dermatomyositis," Arthritis & Rheumatism, 1975.
  • Significance: Positive findings strongly suggest systemic myopathies; absence may indicate non-muscle causes of weakness.
Low Vitamin D Levels
  • Vitamin D deficiency contributes to myopathy and can cause widespread muscle pain and weakness.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 80%, Specificity: 75% for diagnosing vitamin D deficiency-related myopathy. LR: Positive: 3.2, Negative: 0.27.
  • Reference: Holick MF, "Vitamin D Deficiency," NEJM, 2007.
  • Significance: A positive result supports the diagnosis of deficiency-related muscle symptoms; normal levels rule out this cause.
Electromyography (EMG) Findings
  • EMG identifies patterns of muscle or nerve dysfunction, helping differentiate myopathy from neuropathy.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 85%, Specificity: 90% for detecting inflammatory myopathies. LR: Positive: 8.5, Negative: 0.17.
  • Reference: Preston DC, Shapiro BE, "Electromyography and Neuromuscular Disorders," Elsevier, 2019.
  • Significance: Myopathic patterns support conditions like polymyositis, while neuropathic patterns point to peripheral neuropathy.
Autoantibody Positivity (e.g., Anti-Jo-1)
  • Specific autoantibodies are markers for autoimmune myopathies and connective tissue diseases.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 60%, Specificity: 95% for polymyositis and dermatomyositis. LR: Positive: 12, Negative: 0.42.
  • Reference: Targoff IN, "Autoantibodies in Polymyositis and Dermatomyositis," Curr Rheumatol Rep, 2008.
  • Significance: Positive results confirm autoimmune involvement, while negative results reduce the likelihood of these conditions.
Reduced Deep Tendon Reflexes
  • Decreased reflexes are indicative of neuropathies, including Guillain-Barré syndrome and peripheral neuropathy.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 90%, Specificity: 88% for peripheral neuropathy. LR: Positive: 7.5, Negative: 0.11.
  • Reference: Hughes RA et al., "Guillain-Barré Syndrome," Lancet, 2005.
  • Significance: Reduced reflexes support neurological causes of weakness, while normal reflexes favour myopathies.
MRI Findings of Muscle Oedema
  • MRI can detect inflammation or structural abnormalities in muscles, aiding in the diagnosis of myopathies or focal lesions.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 95%, Specificity: 92% for inflammatory myopathies. LR: Positive: 11.9, Negative: 0.05.
  • Reference: Reimers CD, "MRI of Skeletal Muscle in Inflammatory Myopathies," Annals of Neurology, 1994.
  • Significance: Positive findings strongly support inflammatory conditions, while negative results help rule out significant pathology.
Diagnostic Timeout & Cognitive Biases

Awareness of common thinking errors is essential to improve diagnostic accuracy when assessing a patient with muscle pain and weakness. Before finalising a diagnosis, it is good practice for clinicians at all levels to take a 'diagnostic timeout' to challenge their own reasoning.

  • Anchoring Bias: The tendency to lock onto an initial benign feature, such as the patient's age or a recent minor injury, and attribute all symptoms to it. For example, dismissing an older patient's girdle weakness as "just old age" or "arthritis" and failing to consider Polymyalgia Rheumatica or a myopathy. A key question to ask is: "Could these symptoms represent a new, serious underlying process?"
  • Confirmation Bias: Actively seeking out or overvaluing evidence that supports an initial belief while ignoring contradictory findings. For instance, focusing on tender points to confirm a fibromyalgia diagnosis while downplaying objective signs like muscle wasting or reflex changes that point to a neurological condition.
  • Atypical Presentations: A major pitfall is forgetting that diseases can present non-classically. Statin myopathy can manifest as mild aches and fatigue without significant weakness. Hypothyroidism may present with primarily muscular symptoms long before other classic signs like weight gain or hair loss become apparent. A high index of suspicion is crucial.
  • Diagnosis Momentum: The tendency to accept a previous label without question. A patient may have been told they have "sciatica" for years, but a change in their symptoms, such as new bilateral weakness, necessitates a full re-evaluation to rule out more serious spinal pathology rather than just continuing the established diagnosis.
  • Over-reliance on Normal Tests: Placing too much weight on a single reassuring test. A normal CK level does not rule out all muscle disease (e.g., steroid or mitochondrial myopathy) or a neuropathy. Likewise, inflammatory markers (ESR/CRP) can be normal in a significant minority of patients with active Polymyalgia Rheumatica. Clinical correlation is paramount.
Illustrative Management Pathways

For educational purposes, this section describes the typical principles of management for key diagnoses. These are not prescriptive instructions. All management must be guided by official UK guidelines, local formularies, and senior clinical advice.

Statin-Induced Myopathy

When statin-induced myopathy is suspected, the immediate step is to advise the patient to stop the statin. Blood tests for Creatine Kinase (CK) and renal function (U&Es) are essential to assess the degree of muscle damage and rule out rhabdomyolysis. In most cases, symptoms resolve within weeks of cessation. Management then focuses on reassessing the patient's cardiovascular risk and considering alternative lipid-lowering strategies (e.g., ezetimibe, a lower potency statin, or referral to a lipid clinic) once symptoms have fully resolved.

Polymyalgia Rheumatica (PMR)

Once a diagnosis of PMR is made based on clinical features and raised inflammatory markers, the cornerstone of treatment is a therapeutic trial of oral corticosteroids. A starting dose of 15mg prednisolone daily typically produces a rapid and dramatic improvement in symptoms within days. Management involves a slow, structured tapering regimen over 1-2 years, guided by clinical symptoms and inflammatory markers. Patients must also be co-prescribed bone protection (e.g., calcium, vitamin D, and a bisphosphonate like alendronic acid) to prevent steroid-induced osteoporosis.

Hypothyroidism

The management of myopathy secondary to hypothyroidism is to treat the underlying condition. This involves initiating thyroid hormone replacement with levothyroxine. The dose is started low and titrated upwards every 4-6 weeks based on monitoring of Thyroid Stimulating Hormone (TSH) levels, aiming for a target TSH within the normal reference range. As the patient becomes biochemically and clinically euthyroid, the muscle weakness, aches, and fatigue should gradually resolve over several weeks to months.

Fibromyalgia

Management of fibromyalgia is holistic and multifactorial, focusing on non-pharmacological strategies as first-line treatment. Key components include patient education about the condition, encouragement of a structured, graded exercise programme (to improve function and not just 'push through' pain), and strategies to improve sleep hygiene. For persistent distressing symptoms, pharmacological options recommended by NICE include low-dose amitriptyline, duloxetine, or pregabalin. The goal is to improve function and quality of life through self-management strategies, rather than to completely eliminate pain.

Reflection & Learning

These brief cases illustrate the application of clinical reasoning to different presentations of muscle pain and weakness. They are for educational purposes only.

Case 1: Suspected PMR in Primary Care

A 72-year-old woman presents with a 6-week history of severe bilateral shoulder and hip stiffness, which is worst on waking and lasts for several hours. She reports difficulty getting out of a chair and brushing her hair. On examination, there is no objective weakness or joint swelling, but active movements of her shoulders and hips are restricted by pain. The problem representation is an older woman with new-onset, symmetrical inflammatory-type pain in the shoulder and pelvic girdles with significant functional impairment. The GP arranges urgent blood tests, which show a markedly raised ESR of 85 mm/hr. The high pre-test probability and blood results make PMR the leading diagnosis, and a trial of prednisolone is initiated, leading to a dramatic improvement.

Case 2: Statin Myopathy in a Pharmacy Review

A 58-year-old man, who was started on atorvastatin 80mg three months ago after a heart attack, complains of new-onset aching in his thighs and upper arms during a routine medication review. He feels his legs are "weaker" when he climbs stairs. He denies any other symptoms. The problem representation is a patient with new, symmetric, proximal myalgia and weakness that temporally coincides with the initiation of a high-dose statin. The pharmacist advises the patient to contact his GP immediately, who arranges an urgent CK level (which returns at 10x the upper limit of normal) and advises him to stop the statin. The pathway involves monitoring for symptom resolution and renal function before considering a future lipid management strategy.

Case 3: Neuropathic Weakness in a Diabetes Clinic

A 65-year-old man with a long history of poorly controlled type 2 diabetes mentions that he has been tripping over things more frequently. On direct questioning, he reports a 2-month history of his right foot "not clearing the ground properly". Examination reveals clear weakness of dorsiflexion and eversion of the right foot (MRC grade 3/5). Sensory testing shows reduced light touch and vibration sense bilaterally up to the mid-shin. Ankle reflexes are absent. The problem representation is a patient with a key risk factor (diabetes) presenting with subacute focal weakness (foot drop) superimposed on a background of symmetrical, distal sensory loss, highly consistent with a diabetic mononeuropathy and peripheral neuropathy. The management pathway involves urgent referral for nerve conduction studies and to a specialist diabetic foot clinic.
