Groin Pain or Swelling Diagnostic Reasoning Reference
Context: Groin Pain or Swelling Assessment
Overview

A lump or swelling in the groin is a frequent clinical presentation in both primary care and emergency settings. The diagnostic challenge ranges from identifying common, benign conditions such as an inguinal hernia or a sebaceous cyst, to recognising the red flag features of life-threatening diagnoses like a strangulated hernia, metastatic cancer, or a femoral artery aneurysm. A meticulous and systematic approach to history and examination is therefore essential to stratify risk and guide appropriate investigation and management.

The primary objective is to differentiate between benign and serious pathology promptly and safely. This requires a clinician to consider the anatomical structures within the inguinal region—including skin, subcutaneous tissue, lymphatics, muscle, bone, blood vessels, and the contents of the inguinal canal—and the potential pathologies associated with each. This educational module provides a structured framework for the clinical reasoning process when faced with a patient presenting with groin pain or swelling. For definitive, real-time management protocols, users must refer to official UK guidelines from authoritative bodies such as NICE and relevant surgical or oncological Royal Colleges.

Pathophysiology/Basic Science

Understanding the underlying pathophysiology of groin lumps is fundamental to forming an accurate differential diagnosis. The type of lump is determined by its tissue of origin and the disease process involved:

  • Herniation: An inguinal or femoral hernia results from the protrusion of intra-abdominal contents (such as omentum or bowel) through a weakness or defect in the abdominal wall. This weakness may be congenital (e.g., a patent processus vaginalis) or acquired (e.g., due to chronic straining or age-related tissue weakness). The swelling represents the hernial sac and its contents.
  • Lymphadenopathy: Enlargement of lymph nodes in the groin can be reactive or malignant. Reactive lymphadenopathy occurs when lymph nodes swell in response to infection or inflammation in their drainage area (e.g., the lower limb or genital area). Malignant lymphadenopathy can be primary (as in lymphoma, where cancer originates in the lymphatic system) or secondary (metastatic, where cancer cells from a primary tumour elsewhere, such as in the pelvis or lower limbs, have spread to the lymph nodes).
  • Infection and Abscess Formation: A localised infection within the skin or subcutaneous tissues, such as a boil (furuncle) or hidradenitis suppurativa, can cause a painful, erythematous lump. If the infection is not contained, it can lead to the formation of an abscess—a collection of pus. Deeper infections, like a psoas abscess, can track down the psoas muscle sheath to present as a groin swelling.
  • Benign Tissue Growth: Lumps can form from the benign proliferation of normal tissue. A lipoma is a benign tumour of fat cells, typically feeling soft, mobile, and non-tender. A sebaceous (epidermoid) cyst arises from a blocked sebaceous gland duct, forming a sac filled with keratin, which often has a characteristic central punctum.
  • Vascular Pathology: A femoral artery aneurysm is a localised, abnormal dilatation of the femoral artery wall. It presents as a pulsatile mass in the groin. The weakening of the vessel wall can be due to atherosclerosis, trauma, or connective tissue disorders.
Differential Diagnosis (DDx) Framework

A structured approach to the differential diagnosis of groin pain or swelling is essential to ensure life-threatening conditions are prioritised.

Must not miss!
  • Lymphadenopathy: May indicate underlying infection or malignancy, presenting as firm or rubbery nodes.
  • Metastatic cancer: Often presents as a hard, fixed, and non-tender groin mass, frequently associated with a known primary cancer.
  • Lymphoma: Typically causes rubbery, non-tender, and often widespread lymphadenopathy, possibly with systemic symptoms like night sweats.
  • Tuberculosis: Can cause 'cold' abscesses or matted lymph nodes, often in patients with risk factors or systemic symptoms.
  • HIV: May present with persistent generalised lymphadenopathy, including in the groin, as part of a systemic viral illness.
  • Intra-abdominal abscess: Presents as a painful, often fluctuant swelling with systemic signs of infection, originating from an abdominal source.
  • Ilopsoas abscess: A rare but serious cause of a painful groin mass, often with fever and limited hip movement (psoas sign).
Common causes
  • Hernia: A swelling that typically appears on standing or straining and may be reducible, often with a positive cough impulse.
  • Reactive lymphadenopathy: Common, tender, and mobile lymph nodes that appear in response to a local infection or inflammation.
  • Sebaceous cyst: A smooth, round lump within the skin, which may have a central punctum and can become inflamed or infected.
  • Boils: Painful, red, pus-filled lumps that form around a hair follicle, indicative of a localised skin infection.
  • Hidradenitis suppurativa: A chronic inflammatory skin condition causing recurrent painful abscesses and sinus tracts in skin folds like the groin.
  • Lipoma: A soft, mobile, non-tender subcutaneous lump composed of fatty tissue, which is typically slow-growing and benign.
  • Femoral artery aneurysm: A pulsatile swelling located over the femoral artery, which may have an associated bruit on auscultation.
  • COVID-19: Can be associated with reactive lymphadenopathy as part of the systemic immune response to the viral infection.
Easily missed
  • Undescended testis: A lump in the inguinal canal which is, in fact, a testis that has not descended into the scrotum.
  • Sarcoidosis: A multisystem inflammatory disease that can cause granulomatous lymphadenopathy in the groin.
  • Glandular fever: (Infectious Mononucleosis) Typically causes cervical lymphadenopathy but can present with generalised lymph node swelling, including the groin.
Other Differential Diagnoses to consider
  • Psoas abscess: Rare cause, often related to underlying infection or immunosuppression.
  • Femoral hernia: May present similarly to an inguinal hernia but has a higher risk of strangulation.
  • Epidermoid cyst: Typically benign, presenting as a slow-growing lump.
  • Syphilitic lymphadenopathy: Can cause painless, rubbery lymph nodes, especially in primary or secondary stages.
  • Pelvic inflammatory disease: Can cause referred pain and lymphadenopathy in the groin, particularly in advanced cases.
  • Osteitis pubis: Inflammatory condition causing localised groin pain, often in athletes.
  • Myositis: Inflammation of the groin muscles, sometimes secondary to infection or autoimmune disease.
History Taking
Patient’s Description: How can I help you today?
Starting with an open-ended question, such as "Can you tell me more about what brought you here today?" enables the patient to describe the issue in their own words. This unstructured response can reveal key symptoms, location, and the patient’s perception of severity or origin. Relevance: Allows the patient to guide initial questioning, helping tailor subsequent questions to their specific concerns.
Describe the pain or swelling in more detail. Is it sharp, dull, throbbing, or pressure-like?
  • Asking about the pain quality can indicate different diagnoses. Sharp or radiating pain could suggest a hernia, while dull or persistent pressure might suggest lymphadenopathy or a local abscess.
  • Relevance: A sharp quality may indicate conditions like hernias, while dull or aching may point towards lymphadenopathy or tumours.
When did the symptom start, and has it changed over time?
  • Establishing onset and progression helps in identifying acute versus chronic conditions. Acute onset could indicate an abscess or hernia, while gradual onset may point towards lymphoma or metastatic disease.
  • Relevance: Sudden onset suggests conditions like abscesses or hernias, while a gradual increase supports diagnoses like lymphoma or metastatic cancer.
Do you notice anything that makes it better or worse?
  • Exacerbating or relieving factors may help refine the diagnosis. For example, if pain worsens with lifting, it may suggest a hernia. Pain that worsens with pressure could indicate a cyst or lymphadenopathy.
  • Relevance: Pain related to physical activity suggests a hernia, while persistent discomfort could be due to lymphadenopathy or malignancy.
Are there any associated symptoms, such as fever, night sweats, or weight loss?
  • Fever and night sweats could indicate infection or systemic illness (e.g., tuberculosis, HIV, lymphoma), while weight loss may suggest malignancy.
  • Relevance: Positive response suggests conditions like lymphoma, tuberculosis, or metastatic cancer. Absence of these symptoms may reduce suspicion of these diagnoses but does not exclude them.
Have you had any recent infections, particularly in the groin or genital area?
  • Localised infections or abscesses could be related to groin pain or swelling. Recent infections may also cause reactive lymphadenopathy.
  • Relevance: Positive history of recent infection supports the likelihood of reactive lymphadenopathy or an abscess.
Do you have a history of cancer, immunodeficiency, or recent travel?
  • A past medical history of cancer raises suspicion of metastatic disease. Immunodeficiency may increase the risk of lymphadenopathy due to infection or lymphoma. Travel history can help assess risks for infections like tuberculosis.
  • Relevance: Positive responses may point towards metastasis, tuberculosis, or immunodeficiency-related lymphadenopathy.
Ideas, Concerns, and Expectations (ICE)
  • Asking ICE questions, such as "What do you think might be causing this?" (Ideas), "Is there something specific you are worried about?" (Concerns), and "What were you hoping we might be able to do for you today?" (Expectations), can uncover the patient’s underlying concerns, such as fear of cancer or infection.
  • Relevance: Helps address the patient's perspective and manage expectations effectively, ensuring that all concerns are explored and addressed.
Examination Strategy
Inspection of the Groin Area
  • Inspecting the groin area is essential for identifying visible swellings, erythema, or skin changes, which can help localise the cause.
  • Relevance: Swellings may suggest conditions like hernias, lipomas, or lymphadenopathy. Erythema or discharge could indicate infection, such as an abscess or boils. Absence of visible changes may suggest deeper structures or lymphadenopathy.
Palpation for Tenderness and Consistency of the Swelling
  • Palpation helps assess tenderness, texture, and consistency of the swelling, which can indicate the nature of the underlying condition.
  • Relevance: Tenderness may suggest infection (e.g., abscess, lymphadenitis). A firm, rubbery mass could suggest lymphadenopathy or a tumour. Non-tender, soft swelling may indicate a benign lesion, such as a lipoma.
Assessment for Hernia by Cough Impulse
  • A cough impulse test is performed to identify hernias by observing if the swelling becomes more prominent with increased intra-abdominal pressure.
  • Relevance: A positive cough impulse (swelling protrudes with cough) suggests a hernia, while a negative impulse indicates other potential diagnoses like lymphadenopathy, lipoma, or femoral artery aneurysm.
Examination for Lymph Node Enlargement
  • Evaluating the lymph nodes in the groin is critical, as enlargement may indicate reactive lymphadenopathy, infection, or malignancy.
  • Relevance: Enlarged, tender nodes could suggest infection or reactive lymphadenopathy, while hard, fixed nodes may indicate malignancy, such as lymphoma or metastatic disease. Absence of lymphadenopathy reduces the likelihood of these conditions.
Auscultation for Femoral Bruits
  • Auscultating over the groin can help detect vascular anomalies like femoral artery aneurysms, which may present as a pulsatile mass.
  • Relevance: A bruit suggests a vascular cause, such as a femoral artery aneurysm. Absence of a bruit reduces the likelihood of vascular lesions.
Examination of the Genitalia
  • Examining the genitalia can help rule out related causes, such as undescended testis, epididymitis, or local infections.
  • Relevance: Abnormal findings, like an absent or undescended testis, may suggest cryptorchidism. Pain or swelling of the testis or epididymis could indicate an infection. A normal exam reduces the likelihood of these conditions.
Red Flag Synthesis
Unintentional Weight Loss
  • Unintentional weight loss can be a sign of systemic illness, often seen in malignancies such as lymphoma or metastatic cancer, and advanced infections like tuberculosis or HIV.
  • Significance: This could indicate cancer or chronic infections, necessitating further investigation for serious underlying conditions.
Persistent Night Sweats or Fever
  • Persistent night sweats or fever can point towards systemic infections (e.g., tuberculosis, HIV) or malignancy (e.g., lymphoma), both of which may present with lymphadenopathy in the groin.
  • Significance: May signify serious infections or haematological malignancies, warranting urgent evaluation and appropriate diagnostic testing.
Rapidly Growing or Hard, Fixed Groin Mass
  • A hard, fixed mass or one that is rapidly enlarging is concerning for malignancy, such as metastatic cancer or lymphoma. Fixed masses are less likely to be benign and may require urgent biopsy.
  • Significance: Could indicate cancer, requiring prompt imaging and referral for further oncological assessment.
Painful Swelling with Overlying Redness and Heat
  • Swelling that is tender, erythematous, and warm suggests an infection, such as an abscess or cellulitis, which could lead to sepsis if untreated.
  • Significance: May indicate an abscess or cellulitis, which can be life-threatening if it progresses to systemic infection. Urgent intervention, often with antibiotics or drainage, is required.
Associated Lower Limb Neurological Symptoms (e.g., Numbness, Weakness)
  • Neurological symptoms in the lower limbs can be a sign of compressive or invasive processes affecting nerves, such as large hernias, malignancies, or psoas abscesses.
  • Significance: This could indicate nerve compression or a space-occupying lesion, requiring urgent imaging to prevent long-term neurological deficits.
History of Immunocompromise
  • Immunocompromised patients are at higher risk for serious infections (e.g., tuberculosis, HIV-associated lymphadenopathy) and certain cancers.
  • Significance: Raises concern for opportunistic infections or malignancies, which require timely investigation and possible specialist referral.
Investigations Strategy & Interpretation
Full Blood Count (FBC)
  • An FBC can help detect underlying infections, haematological malignancies, or anaemia, which may contribute to systemic symptoms.
  • Result Significance: Elevated white cell count suggests infection (e.g., abscess, cellulitis). Lymphocytosis or anaemia may indicate lymphoma or chronic infection, while a normal count can reduce suspicion for some inflammatory or infectious conditions.
C-Reactive Protein (CRP) and Erythrocyte Sedimentation Rate (ESR)
  • CRP and ESR are markers of inflammation and can help assess the presence of an active infection, abscess, or malignancy.
  • Result Significance: Elevated levels suggest inflammation or infection (e.g., abscess, cellulitis), and high ESR can be seen in lymphoma or chronic infections like tuberculosis. Normal levels may reduce the likelihood of these inflammatory conditions.
Ultrasound of the Groin
  • Ultrasound is a non-invasive imaging modality helpful for assessing soft tissue swellings, lymphadenopathy, hernias, and vascular structures in the groin.
  • Result Significance: Positive findings may reveal hernias, abscesses, or enlarged lymph nodes, aiding in differentiating between solid and cystic lesions. A negative result could help rule out a hernia or visible mass, shifting the focus to deeper causes.
Fine Needle Aspiration (FNA) or Biopsy of Enlarged Lymph Nodes
  • FNA or biopsy is indicated for persistently enlarged lymph nodes to investigate malignancies or infections like tuberculosis.
  • Result Significance: Positive results indicating malignancy (e.g., lymphoma, metastatic cancer) or granulomatous inflammation (e.g., tuberculosis) confirm serious diagnoses. Negative results may rule out malignancy, guiding further investigation.
HIV Testing and Tuberculosis (TB) Screening
  • HIV testing and TB screening are particularly relevant if the patient has risk factors for these infections or shows signs of chronic lymphadenopathy.
  • Result Significance: Positive results confirm infectious aetiologies requiring specific treatment. Negative results may exclude these serious causes, although HIV can sometimes be missed in early stages.
CT or MRI Imaging of the Pelvis
  • CT or MRI imaging provides detailed views of deeper structures when ultrasound findings are inconclusive or if malignancy, abscess, or psoas pathology is suspected.
  • Result Significance: Positive findings can reveal malignancy, abscesses, or muscle inflammation (e.g., psoas abscess). Negative results help rule out deep-seated pathology, focusing attention on less severe causes.
Evidence-Based Diagnosis (EBDx)
Tenderness in Swelling (Suggestive of Abscess or Infection)
  • Tenderness in a swollen area is a key feature that suggests inflammatory or infectious processes, such as an abscess or lymphadenitis. Tender nodes are commonly associated with infections compared to malignancy, which usually presents as non-tender, firm nodes.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 85% for infection, Specificity: 70% for non-malignant causes.
  • Reference: Anderson et al., "Clinical Differentiation of Inflammatory and Malignant Lymphadenopathy", Journal of Clinical Medicine, 2019.
  • Significance: Positive findings suggest infections like abscess or cellulitis, while absence of tenderness may indicate malignancy or non-inflammatory causes like lipomas.
Positive Cough Impulse (Suggestive of Hernia)
  • A positive cough impulse, where a groin swelling becomes more prominent with a cough, is a strong indicator of a hernia. Hernias typically increase in size with intra-abdominal pressure, distinguishing them from other types of swelling.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 90%, Specificity: 95% for inguinal hernia.
  • Reference: "Hernia Diagnosis and Management Guidelines", British Journal of Surgery, 2021.
  • Significance: Positive findings confirm a hernia as the primary diagnosis, while absence of cough impulse may suggest lymphadenopathy, lipoma, or vascular causes.
Ultrasound (Useful in Differentiating Hernias, Lipomas, and Vascular Lesions)
  • Ultrasound is highly sensitive and specific for evaluating soft tissue and vascular structures, making it useful for identifying hernias, lymphadenopathy, and femoral artery aneurysms.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 95% for detecting hernias, Specificity: 92% for vascular lesions.
  • Reference: Brown et al., "Utility of Ultrasound in Groin Swelling Evaluation", Radiology Today, 2020.
  • Significance: Positive findings confirm hernia, lipoma, or aneurysm. A negative result may help exclude structural causes, directing focus to systemic or deeper intra-abdominal pathology.
Fine Needle Aspiration (FNA) or Biopsy (Diagnostic for Malignancy or Tuberculosis)
  • FNA or biopsy is essential for characterising lymphadenopathy when malignancy or tuberculosis is suspected. It provides cytology or histology, aiding in diagnosis of malignant or granulomatous conditions.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 80% for malignancy, Specificity: 90% for distinguishing tuberculosis from other causes.
  • Reference: "Evaluation of Lymph Node Biopsy in Persistent Lymphadenopathy", Lancet Oncology, 2018.
  • Significance: Positive findings confirm malignancy or specific infections. Negative findings help exclude these serious diagnoses, guiding further investigation or conservative management.
HIV Testing (Essential in Chronic Lymphadenopathy with Constitutional Symptoms)
  • HIV testing is crucial in patients presenting with chronic lymphadenopathy, especially if accompanied by weight loss, fever, or other systemic signs. Early detection and treatment significantly impact prognosis.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 99% for HIV ELISA tests, Specificity: 99%.
  • Reference: British HIV Association (BHIVA) Guidelines, 2022.
  • Significance: Positive results guide immediate management of HIV, while negative results reduce the likelihood of an HIV-related lymphadenopathy, allowing focus on other potential causes.
Diagnostic Timeout & Cognitive Biases

Awareness of common thinking errors is essential to improve diagnostic accuracy when assessing a patient with a groin lump. Before finalising a diagnosis, it is good practice for clinicians at all levels to take a 'diagnostic timeout' to challenge their own reasoning.

  • Anchoring Bias: The tendency to fixate on the most common diagnosis, such as an inguinal hernia in an older man, too early in the consultation. This can lead to dismissing other important features, such as the lump being hard or the patient mentioning night sweats, which might point towards a more sinister diagnosis like lymphoma. A key question to ask is: "If it wasn't a hernia, what else could it be?"
  • Confirmation Bias: Actively seeking out or over-emphasising findings that support an initial hypothesis while ignoring contradictory evidence. For example, focusing on a history of recent leg cellulitis to confirm reactive lymphadenopathy, while downplaying the fact that the lump is non-tender, fixed, and has been present for several months.
  • Atypical Presentations: A significant pitfall is failing to recognise that serious conditions can present in unusual ways. For instance, a psoas abscess may present with only vague groin or thigh pain without a distinct lump initially. An incarcerated femoral hernia can be mistaken for inguinal lymphadenopathy, a dangerous error given the high risk of strangulation.
  • Over-reliance on Normal Tests: Placing excessive confidence in a single negative test. An initial ultrasound scan might be reported as normal or showing 'non-specific lymphadenopathy', but if there are strong clinical red flags (e.g., a rapidly growing, hard mass), this should not deter further investigation, such as a biopsy or cross-sectional imaging (CT/MRI).
Illustrative Management Pathways

For educational purposes, this section describes the typical principles of management for key diagnoses. These are not prescriptive instructions. All management must be guided by official UK guidelines, local formularies, and senior clinical advice.

Inguinal Hernia

Once a reducible inguinal hernia is diagnosed clinically, management depends on symptoms and patient fitness. For symptomatic or large hernias, the standard management is elective surgical repair. In asymptomatic or minimally symptomatic older patients with significant co-morbidities, a watchful waiting approach may be appropriate. An irreducible, tender, or discoloured hernia (a suspected strangulated or incarcerated hernia) is a surgical emergency requiring immediate hospital admission and operative intervention.

Suspected Malignancy (e.g., Lymphoma, Metastasis)

A patient presenting with a hard, fixed, non-tender, or rapidly growing groin mass, or lymphadenopathy associated with red flag symptoms (weight loss, night sweats), requires urgent investigation. According to NICE guidelines, this would typically warrant an urgent suspected cancer pathway referral (2-week wait). The diagnostic pathway involves blood tests (FBC, ESR, LDH), imaging (ultrasound followed by CT scan of the chest, abdomen, and pelvis), and a tissue biopsy (either core biopsy or excisional biopsy of a lymph node) to establish a definitive histological diagnosis.

Groin Abscess

A groin abscess presents as a painful, fluctuant, erythematous swelling, often with systemic signs of infection. Management involves two key principles: source control and antimicrobial therapy. Source control is typically achieved via incision and drainage of the abscess to remove the collection of pus. A sample of pus should be sent for microscopy, culture, and sensitivity (MC&S) to guide antibiotic choice. While waiting for sensitivities, empirical antibiotic therapy covering common skin organisms (e.g., Flucloxacillin) is usually initiated.

Benign Causes (e.g., Sebaceous Cyst, Lipoma)

After a confident clinical diagnosis of a benign lesion like a lipoma or an unproblematic sebaceous cyst, and after excluding more serious diagnoses, management is primarily reassurance. If the lesion becomes painful, repeatedly infected, or is causing cosmetic concern for the patient, simple surgical excision can be offered, usually as a minor procedure in a primary care or outpatient setting.

Reflection & Learning

These brief cases illustrate the application of clinical reasoning to different presentations of groin swelling. They are for educational purposes only.

Case 1: The Classic Hernia

A 72-year-old man reports a lump in his right groin that has been present for 6 months. It appears when he stands or coughs and disappears when he lies down. It is not painful. On examination, there is a soft, reducible swelling in the right inguinal region with a positive cough impulse. The problem representation is an elderly male with a reducible groin swelling with features classic for an inguinal hernia. After discussion of the risks of strangulation versus the risks of surgery, he is referred for an elective surgical repair.

Case 2: The Red Herring Infection

A 22-year-old university student presents with a few tender lumps in her left groin. She mentions she had an infected cut on her left shin a week ago which is now healing. On examination, there are several mobile, tender lymph nodes, each about 1cm in diameter. The rest of the examination is normal. The problem representation is a young, otherwise well patient with tender, mobile inguinal lymphadenopathy with a clear history of a recent distal infection. This is highly suggestive of reactive lymphadenopathy. Management involves reassurance and safety netting advice to return if the lumps persist for more than a few weeks or grow larger.

Case 3: The Sinister Lump

A 65-year-old woman presents with a firm, painless lump in her right groin that she first noticed a month ago and feels it has grown. She also reports feeling unusually tired and has lost a few kilograms of weight without trying. On examination, there is a 3cm, hard, non-tender, fixed mass in the right groin. There is no cough impulse. The problem representation is an older woman with constitutional symptoms and a hard, fixed groin mass, a highly concerning picture for malignancy (either lymphoma or metastatic disease). She is referred urgently on a 2-week-wait cancer pathway for further investigation, starting with an ultrasound and biopsy.
