Hypertension Diagnostic Reasoning Reference
Context: Hypertension Assessment
Overview

Hypertension is a leading cause of cardiovascular disease worldwide and a major modifiable risk factor for myocardial infarction, stroke, heart failure, and chronic kidney disease. In the UK, it is one of the most common long-term conditions managed in primary care. The primary challenge for clinicians is the accurate diagnosis and risk stratification of patients, distinguishing between common essential hypertension and less common but important secondary causes. A systematic approach is crucial to identify individuals requiring specialist investigation and to implement effective management to prevent long-term complications.

This educational module is designed to build a foundational understanding of the clinical reasoning process for hypertension. For definitive, real-time management protocols, users must refer to official UK guidelines from authoritative bodies such as NICE (e.g., NG136 for Hypertension in adults: diagnosis and management).

Pathophysiology/Basic Science

Understanding the origin of hypertension is key to diagnosis. Blood pressure is determined by cardiac output and systemic vascular resistance. Hypertension arises from a persistent dysregulation of one or both of these factors through several distinct mechanisms:

  • Essential (Primary) Hypertension: This accounts for over 90% of cases and is a multifactorial condition without a single identifiable cause. It results from a complex interplay between genetic predisposition and environmental factors (e.g., high salt intake, obesity, stress). Key mechanisms include overactivity of the sympathetic nervous system, inappropriate activation of the Renin-Angiotensin-Aldosterone System (RAAS), and endothelial dysfunction leading to increased systemic vascular resistance.
  • Renal Hypertension: This is the most common cause of secondary hypertension. It can be due to:
    • Renovascular disease: Stenosis (narrowing) of one or both renal arteries, typically due to atherosclerosis or fibromuscular dysplasia. This reduces blood flow to the kidney, which is sensed as systemic hypotension, triggering the RAAS cascade and leading to severe, often treatment-resistant hypertension.
    • Parenchymal disease: Conditions like Chronic Kidney Disease (CKD) or glomerulonephritis impair the kidney's ability to excrete sodium and water, leading to volume expansion and hypertension.
  • Endocrine Hypertension: Hormonal imbalances can cause hypertension:
    • Primary Aldosteronism (Conn's Syndrome): Autonomous overproduction of aldosterone from the adrenal glands causes excessive sodium and water retention and potassium excretion, leading to volume expansion and hypertension, classically with hypokalaemia.
    • Phaeochromocytoma: A rare catecholamine-secreting tumour of the adrenal medulla. The excess adrenaline and noradrenaline cause potent vasoconstriction and increased cardiac output, resulting in sustained or paroxysmal (episodic) severe hypertension, often with headaches, palpitations, and sweating.
    • Cushing's Syndrome: Excess cortisol (endogenous or exogenous) increases vascular sensitivity to catecholamines and has mineralocorticoid effects, leading to fluid retention and hypertension.
  • Coarctation of the Aorta: A congenital narrowing of the aorta, usually just distal to the left subclavian artery. This creates high pressure in the upper body (arms and head) and low pressure in the lower body, leading to upper limb hypertension and a cascade of compensatory mechanisms including RAAS activation.
Differential Diagnosis (DDx) Framework

A structured approach to the differential diagnosis of hypertension is essential to ensure life-threatening conditions are prioritised.

Must not miss!
  • Malignant hypertension: Presents with very high blood pressure and signs of end-organ damage, such as papilloedema, renal failure, or encephalopathy.
  • Pre-eclampsia: New-onset hypertension after 20 weeks of gestation, often with proteinuria and other signs of maternal organ dysfunction.
  • Aortic coarctation: Typically presents in younger individuals with hypertension in the arms, diminished femoral pulses, and a radio-femoral delay.
Common causes
  • Essential hypertension: The most common form, usually asymptomatic and diagnosed on routine screening in adults.
  • 'White coat' hypertension: Elevated blood pressure readings in a clinical setting but normal readings at home, confirmed by ambulatory or home monitoring.
  • Chronic Kidney Disease: Hypertension is both a cause and a consequence of progressive loss of kidney function.
  • Diabetic nephropathy: A complication of diabetes leading to kidney damage and secondary hypertension.
  • Gestational hypertension: Hypertension developing after 20 weeks of pregnancy without proteinuria, which typically resolves post-partum.
  • COVID-19: Can be associated with acute blood pressure fluctuations and an increased risk of developing persistent hypertension.
Easily missed
  • Medication Induced Hypertension: Elevated blood pressure caused by common medications such as NSAIDs, corticosteroids, or the combined oral contraceptive pill.
  • Hyperthyroidism: Overactive thyroid function leading to a high-output state with increased heart rate and systolic hypertension.
  • PCKD (Polycystic Kidney Disease): An inherited condition causing multiple cysts in the kidneys, leading to enlarged kidneys, impaired function, and hypertension.
  • Hyperaldosteronism (Conn's syndrome): Adrenal overproduction of aldosterone, causing hypertension which is often resistant to standard treatment and may be associated with low potassium levels.
  • Phaeochromocytoma: A rare adrenal tumour causing episodic or sustained release of catecholamines, leading to severe hypertension, headaches, palpitations, and sweating.
  • Cushing's syndrome: Excess cortisol production causing hypertension along with characteristic features like central obesity, facial plethora, and skin striae.
Other Differential Diagnoses to consider
  • Obstructive Sleep Apnoea (OSA): Recurrent episodes of upper airway obstruction during sleep, contributing to daytime hypertension.
  • Renovascular hypertension: Hypertension secondary to renal artery stenosis, often presenting with sudden-onset or resistant hypertension.
  • Primary hyperparathyroidism: Excess parathyroid hormone, leading to hypercalcaemia and hypertension.
  • Coarctation of the aorta (in adults): Hypertension in young adults, with diminished femoral pulses.
  • Lead poisoning: Chronic exposure leading to hypertension through renal impairment.
  • Neurogenic hypertension: Caused by disorders affecting the autonomic nervous system, such as Guillain-Barré syndrome or multiple sclerosis.
  • Post-transplant hypertension: Often occurs in renal transplant recipients due to immunosuppressants like cyclosporine or tacrolimus.
  • Liddle syndrome: A rare genetic disorder causing severe hypertension due to excessive sodium reabsorption in the kidneys.
History Taking
**Onset and Duration**: When did you first notice your blood pressure was raised, and how long has it been an issue?
Asking about the onset helps to determine if the hypertension is a new issue or if it is part of a chronic condition, while understanding the duration helps assess its severity and progression. Relevance: Sudden onset of hypertension might suggest secondary causes (e.g., renal artery stenosis, phaeochromocytoma), whereas longstanding hypertension is more likely to be essential hypertension.
**Blood Pressure Monitoring and Symptoms**: Have you been regularly monitoring your blood pressure at home, and if so, what have your readings been like?
This question assesses whether the patient has objective evidence of hypertension and helps evaluate the accuracy of previous measurements. Relevance: Consistently high readings confirm hypertension, whereas 'white coat' hypertension is suggested if readings are high only in clinical settings but normal at home.
**Headaches**: Have you experienced any headaches recently?
Headaches can be a sign of poorly controlled or severe hypertension, particularly in the context of hypertensive urgency or emergencies. Relevance: A positive response may indicate malignant hypertension or a hypertensive crisis, while the absence of headaches does not exclude chronic hypertension.
**Visual Disturbances**: Have you had any episodes of blurred vision or visual disturbances?
Visual disturbances may indicate hypertensive retinopathy or an acute hypertensive emergency affecting the optic nerve. Relevance: Positive findings suggest a more severe hypertensive state, possibly malignant hypertension or a hypertensive emergency.
**Chest Pain**: Have you experienced chest pain or discomfort?
Chest pain could indicate cardiovascular complications of hypertension, such as myocardial infarction or aortic dissection. Relevance: A positive response raises concern for serious conditions such as coronary artery disease or aortic dissection, while a negative response reduces the likelihood of these diagnoses.
**Past Medical History**: Do you have a history of kidney disease, diabetes, or thyroid problems?
Certain comorbid conditions like chronic kidney disease, diabetes, and hyperthyroidism are risk factors for secondary hypertension. Relevance: A history of these conditions increases the likelihood of secondary causes of hypertension, such as diabetic nephropathy or renal artery stenosis.
**Family History**: Does anyone in your family have a history of high blood pressure, heart disease, or kidney disease?
A family history of hypertension or cardiovascular disease suggests a genetic predisposition to essential hypertension or other cardiovascular issues. Relevance: Positive family history increases the likelihood of essential hypertension or early-onset cardiovascular complications.
**Drug History**: Are you taking any medications, including over-the-counter drugs or herbal supplements?
Certain medications (e.g., NSAIDs, corticosteroids, oral contraceptives) and supplements can raise blood pressure or interfere with antihypertensive therapy. Relevance: Identifying medication-induced hypertension is crucial, as stopping the offending drug may resolve the raised blood pressure.
**Ideas, Concerns, and Expectations (ICE)**
Asking about the patient’s ideas, concerns, and expectations allows the clinician to understand the patient’s perspective, ensuring the consultation is patient-centred. Relevance: Helps to address any specific concerns the patient has, such as fear of complications, and clarifies their expectations for treatment or further investigations.
Examination Strategy
**General Inspection**: Inspect for signs of distress, obesity, or physical characteristics like cushingoid appearance.
  • General inspection helps identify signs of secondary hypertension causes, such as obesity (linked to metabolic syndrome), or Cushingoid features like moon face and central obesity (indicative of Cushing's syndrome).
  • Relevance: Positive findings of obesity may suggest essential hypertension or Cushing's syndrome. Absence of obvious physical signs does not rule out hypertension but makes certain secondary causes less likely.
**Blood Pressure Measurement**: Measure blood pressure in both arms and consider ambulatory blood pressure monitoring.
  • Blood pressure measurement in both arms helps detect conditions like aortic coarctation or subclavian artery stenosis, which may present with differing BP readings between arms. Ambulatory BP monitoring can help identify white coat hypertension and assess true BP control.
  • Relevance: Significant differences between arm readings suggest aortic coarctation or vascular stenosis, while consistently elevated BP indicates hypertension. Normal BP or lack of difference between arms may suggest 'white coat' hypertension or better control.
**Pulse and Heart Rate**: Check for rate, rhythm, and volume of the pulse.
  • An irregular pulse may indicate arrhythmias like atrial fibrillation, which can complicate hypertension. A bounding pulse might suggest hyperthyroidism or a high-output state like aortic regurgitation.
  • Relevance: An irregular pulse suggests arrhythmias, while a bounding pulse may indicate high-output conditions or hyperthyroidism. A normal pulse makes these diagnoses less likely.
**Fundoscopy**: Examine the retina for signs of hypertensive retinopathy (e.g., cotton wool spots, haemorrhages).
  • Fundoscopy can reveal end-organ damage due to chronic hypertension, such as hypertensive retinopathy, which reflects damage to small blood vessels in the eye.
  • Relevance: Positive findings (e.g., retinal haemorrhages, cotton wool spots) suggest long-standing or poorly controlled hypertension. Absence of retinopathy suggests well-controlled or recent-onset hypertension.
**Palpation of the Abdomen**: Palpate for enlarged kidneys or a palpable abdominal aorta.
  • Abdominal examination can help identify signs of secondary causes of hypertension such as polycystic kidney disease (enlarged kidneys) or abdominal aortic aneurysm (palpable aorta).
  • Relevance: Enlarged kidneys suggest PCKD, while a pulsatile mass may indicate an abdominal aortic aneurysm. Absence of these findings makes these conditions less likely.
**Auscultation of the Abdomen**: Listen for renal or abdominal bruits.
  • Auscultation for bruits can reveal vascular abnormalities such as renal artery stenosis, which is a common secondary cause of hypertension.
  • Relevance: Positive findings of renal or abdominal bruits suggest renovascular hypertension, while their absence does not rule out other causes of secondary hypertension.
**Peripheral Oedema**: Check for bilateral pitting oedema in the lower limbs.
  • Peripheral oedema can be a sign of fluid overload and heart failure, which may be a complication of long-standing hypertension.
  • Relevance: Positive findings of pitting oedema suggest heart failure or renal dysfunction, while negative findings make these complications less likely.
**Body Mass Index (BMI)**: Measure the patient's height and weight to calculate BMI.
  • Obesity is a major risk factor for essential hypertension, and calculating BMI helps assess the overall cardiovascular risk.
  • Relevance: A high BMI suggests obesity-related hypertension, while a normal BMI makes this less likely. Obesity also increases the risk of complications like heart failure.
**Thyroid Examination**: Palpate for thyroid enlargement or nodules.
  • Thyroid examination can help identify hyperthyroidism or hypothyroidism, both of which can cause secondary hypertension.
  • Relevance: A palpable goitre or thyroid nodules suggest thyroid disease (e.g., hyperthyroidism) as a potential cause of hypertension. Absence of these signs reduces the likelihood of thyroid-related hypertension.
Red Flag Synthesis
**Severe Headache**
  • A sudden, severe headache in a hypertensive patient may indicate a hypertensive crisis or impending cerebrovascular event.
  • Significance: This could indicate malignant hypertension or intracranial haemorrhage, requiring urgent evaluation and blood pressure management to prevent stroke or brain damage.
**Visual Disturbances**
  • Blurred vision, seeing spots, or other visual disturbances can indicate hypertensive retinopathy or papilloedema, which are signs of end-organ damage.
  • Significance: This could signify malignant hypertension, raised intracranial pressure, or hypertensive retinopathy, all of which require urgent treatment.
**Chest Pain**
  • Chest pain in a hypertensive patient may suggest cardiovascular complications such as myocardial infarction or aortic dissection.
  • Significance: This could indicate acute coronary syndrome or aortic dissection, both of which are medical emergencies requiring immediate intervention.
**Breathlessness**
  • Dyspnoea in a hypertensive patient may be a sign of acute heart failure or pulmonary oedema, potentially caused by uncontrolled hypertension.
  • Significance: This could indicate left ventricular failure or hypertensive heart disease, requiring urgent treatment to prevent respiratory failure.
**Neurological Symptoms (e.g., confusion, weakness)**
  • Confusion, weakness, or focal neurological deficits may indicate a hypertensive emergency, including stroke or encephalopathy.
  • Significance: This could signify hypertensive encephalopathy or stroke, both of which require immediate intervention to prevent permanent neurological damage.
**Palpitations**
  • Palpitations may suggest underlying arrhythmias or secondary causes of hypertension such as phaeochromocytoma or hyperthyroidism.
  • Significance: This could indicate arrhythmias like atrial fibrillation or a secondary cause of hypertension such as phaeochromocytoma, both of which require further investigation and management.
**Rapidly Rising Blood Pressure in Pregnancy**
  • In pregnant women, rapidly rising blood pressure, especially when associated with symptoms like oedema or headaches, can indicate pre-eclampsia.
  • Significance: This could signify pre-eclampsia or eclampsia, which can lead to life-threatening complications for both mother and baby. Immediate evaluation and management are essential.
Investigations Strategy & Interpretation
**Blood Pressure Measurement** (Ambulatory or Home Monitoring)
  • Ambulatory blood pressure monitoring (ABPM) or home BP measurement helps confirm hypertension by eliminating white coat effect and providing a clearer picture of BP control.
  • Result Significance: Persistent high readings confirm hypertension. Normal readings may suggest white coat hypertension.
**Full Blood Count (FBC)**
  • FBC can assess for anaemia, infection, or other haematological abnormalities that may contribute to secondary causes of hypertension.
  • Result Significance: Anaemia may suggest underlying kidney disease or chronic illness, while a raised white cell count suggests infection or inflammation.
**Urea, Electrolytes, and Creatinine (U&Es)**
  • U&Es assess renal function, as hypertension can be secondary to renal disease or cause renal impairment.
  • Result Significance: Elevated creatinine suggests chronic kidney disease (CKD), while electrolyte imbalances (e.g., low potassium) may point to conditions like hyperaldosteronism.
**Thyroid Function Tests (TFTs)**
  • TFTs assess for hyperthyroidism or hypothyroidism, both of which can contribute to secondary hypertension.
  • Result Significance: Positive findings for hyperthyroidism may suggest a secondary cause of hypertension, while normal thyroid function reduces the likelihood of thyroid-related hypertension.
**Fasting Blood Glucose and HbA1c**
  • These tests screen for diabetes, which is a major risk factor for hypertension and its complications.
  • Result Significance: Elevated fasting glucose or HbA1c suggests diabetes or prediabetes, contributing to secondary causes of hypertension like diabetic nephropathy. Normal values reduce the likelihood of diabetes.
**Lipid Profile**
  • Lipid profile measures cholesterol levels and assesses cardiovascular risk, as hypertension is often part of metabolic syndrome.
  • Result Significance: Elevated LDL cholesterol or triglycerides suggest an increased risk of cardiovascular disease. A normal lipid profile does not exclude hypertension but suggests lower cardiovascular risk.
**Renal Ultrasound**
  • Renal ultrasound evaluates kidney structure and size, helping to identify secondary causes of hypertension such as polycystic kidney disease (PCKD) or renal artery stenosis.
  • Result Significance: Enlarged or cystic kidneys suggest PCKD, while small or scarred kidneys suggest chronic kidney disease. A normal ultrasound reduces suspicion of renal causes of hypertension.
**ECG (Electrocardiogram)**
  • An ECG is used to detect left ventricular hypertrophy or arrhythmias, which can be complications of long-standing hypertension.
  • Result Significance: Signs of left ventricular hypertrophy (LVH) suggest chronic hypertension, while arrhythmias like atrial fibrillation may complicate hypertension. A normal ECG does not exclude hypertension but reduces the likelihood of cardiac complications.
**Urine Dipstick and Albumin:Creatinine Ratio (ACR)**
  • Urine dipstick and ACR help assess for proteinuria or microalbuminuria, which may indicate hypertensive nephropathy or diabetic nephropathy.
  • Result Significance: Positive findings of proteinuria or microalbuminuria suggest underlying renal disease or damage due to hypertension. Negative results make significant renal involvement less likely.
**Plasma Renin and Aldosterone Levels**
  • This test assesses for primary hyperaldosteronism (Conn's syndrome), a common cause of secondary hypertension.
  • Result Significance: Low renin and high aldosterone levels suggest primary hyperaldosteronism, while normal levels reduce the likelihood of this condition as the cause of hypertension.
**Echocardiogram**
  • An echocardiogram evaluates the structure and function of the heart, helping to assess for left ventricular hypertrophy, valvular disease, or heart failure related to hypertension.
  • Result Significance: Positive findings of left ventricular hypertrophy or reduced ejection fraction suggest chronic hypertensive heart disease, while a normal echocardiogram reduces the likelihood of significant cardiac involvement.
Evidence-Based Diagnosis (EBDx)
**Headache** in Malignant Hypertension
  • Headaches in the context of hypertension can indicate malignant hypertension or hypertensive crises, often associated with increased intracranial pressure.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 22%, Specificity: 92% (headaches are often absent in early stages but specific for more severe hypertension).
  • Significance: A positive finding may suggest hypertensive emergency, while absence of headache does not rule out high blood pressure but reduces suspicion of hypertensive crisis.
**Blood Pressure Measurement** in Hypertension Diagnosis
  • Ambulatory blood pressure monitoring (ABPM) or home BP measurements are used to diagnose hypertension and reduce the influence of white coat hypertension.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 80%, Specificity: 92%, Positive Predictive Value (PPV) for detecting true hypertension with ABPM: 91%.
  • Significance: High readings on ABPM confirm the diagnosis of hypertension, while normal readings suggest white coat hypertension or well-controlled blood pressure.
**ECG** in Detecting Left Ventricular Hypertrophy (LVH)
  • ECG is commonly used to detect left ventricular hypertrophy (LVH), a complication of long-standing hypertension that indicates end-organ damage.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 55%, Specificity: 95%, Positive Likelihood Ratio (LR+): 11.0.
  • Significance: A positive finding of LVH on ECG strongly suggests long-standing or poorly controlled hypertension. A negative finding does not exclude LVH, as ECG sensitivity is low.
**Plasma Aldosterone/Renin Ratio** in Diagnosing Primary Hyperaldosteronism
  • An elevated aldosterone/renin ratio is a highly specific test for primary hyperaldosteronism, a common secondary cause of hypertension.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 80%, Specificity: 90%, LR+: 8.0.
  • Significance: A positive result suggests primary hyperaldosteronism, while a negative result reduces the likelihood of this secondary cause of hypertension.
**Serum Creatinine** in Assessing Kidney Function
  • Serum creatinine assesses renal function and is crucial for identifying chronic kidney disease (CKD), which can be both a cause and complication of hypertension.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 90% (for detecting CKD), Specificity: 80%, PPV for CKD: 85%.
  • Significance: Elevated creatinine levels suggest CKD, while normal levels make significant renal impairment less likely but do not fully exclude early renal disease.
**Echocardiogram** in Detecting Left Ventricular Hypertrophy
  • An echocardiogram is the gold standard for detecting left ventricular hypertrophy (LVH), a common finding in patients with long-standing hypertension.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 90%, Specificity: 90%, PPV: 88%, NPV: 92%.
  • Significance: A positive finding of LVH confirms hypertensive heart disease, while a negative finding rules out significant cardiac structural changes related to hypertension.
**Proteinuria** in Diagnosing Hypertensive Nephropathy
  • Proteinuria, detected by urine dipstick or albumin:creatinine ratio (ACR), is a marker of renal damage and hypertensive nephropathy.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 75%, Specificity: 85% for detecting chronic kidney disease.
  • Significance: Positive proteinuria indicates renal damage, often secondary to hypertension or diabetic nephropathy. Negative findings make renal involvement less likely but not impossible.
Diagnostic Timeout & Cognitive Biases

Awareness of common thinking errors is essential to improve diagnostic accuracy and patient safety when assessing a patient with hypertension. Before finalising a diagnosis, it is good practice for clinicians at all levels to take a 'diagnostic timeout' to challenge their own reasoning.

  • Anchoring Bias: The tendency to attribute a high reading to 'white coat hypertension' without adequately considering or investigating for true sustained hypertension or secondary causes, especially if the patient is anxious. A key question to ask is: "Have I confirmed this with out-of-office measurements?"
  • Premature Closure: Diagnosing essential hypertension (the most common cause) without considering red flags for secondary causes, such as a young age of onset, resistant hypertension, or electrolyte abnormalities (e.g., hypokalaemia in Conn's syndrome).
  • Confirmation Bias: Focusing on lifestyle factors like diet and stress to confirm a diagnosis of essential hypertension, while unintentionally ignoring signs that point elsewhere, such as a new renal bruit or a family history of polycystic kidney disease.
  • Attribution Error: Incorrectly attributing symptoms like fatigue or headaches to stress or other life events, rather than considering them as potential manifestations of severe hypertension or an underlying secondary cause like phaeochromocytoma. A key question to ask is: "Could hypertension be the cause of these symptoms, rather than an incidental finding?"
Illustrative Management Pathways

For educational purposes, this section describes the typical principles of management for key diagnoses. These are not prescriptive instructions. All management must be guided by official UK guidelines, local formularies, and senior clinical advice.

Essential Hypertension

The management pathway, guided by NICE NG136, begins with lifestyle advice (reducing salt, healthy diet, exercise, limiting alcohol). Pharmacological treatment is staged, starting with an ACE inhibitor or an ARB (A) for patients under 55 and not of Black African or African-Caribbean family origin, or a calcium-channel blocker (C) for others. Subsequent steps involve combining A or C with a thiazide-like diuretic (D) and further escalation if needed, including consideration of spironolactone for resistant hypertension.

Hypertensive Urgency/Emergency (Malignant Hypertension)

This is a medical emergency requiring immediate hospital admission. The goal is a controlled reduction of blood pressure to prevent end-organ damage, not rapid normalisation which can cause ischaemia. This is typically achieved in a high-dependency setting with intravenous agents like labetalol or sodium nitroprusside, alongside urgent investigation for and management of complications like encephalopathy, acute kidney injury, or heart failure.

Secondary Hypertension (e.g., Renal Artery Stenosis)

Management targets the underlying cause. For renal artery stenosis, this may involve medical management of blood pressure (often with ACE inhibitors, used cautiously) or revascularisation with angioplasty/stenting. For Conn's syndrome, treatment is with mineralocorticoid receptor antagonists (e.g., spironolactone) or surgical adrenalectomy if a unilateral adenoma is identified.

Reflection & Learning

These brief cases illustrate the application of clinical reasoning to different hypertension presentations. They are for educational purposes only.

Case 1: Primary Care Diagnosis of Essential Hypertension

A 58-year-old male smoker attends for a routine check-up. His clinic blood pressure is 164/98 mmHg. He is asymptomatic. Examination is unremarkable. The problem representation is an older male with a cardiovascular risk factor (smoking) and Stage 2 hypertension. The GP arranges for ambulatory blood pressure monitoring, which confirms sustained hypertension. Management involves counselling on lifestyle changes (especially smoking cessation) and starting treatment with a calcium-channel blocker as per NICE guidelines.

Case 2: Investigating Secondary Hypertension

A 38-year-old woman presents with treatment-resistant hypertension, requiring three agents for control. Her blood tests, requested by her GP, reveal a low potassium level of 3.1 mmol/L. The problem representation is a young patient with resistant hypertension and hypokalaemia, raising high suspicion for primary aldosteronism (Conn's syndrome). She is referred urgently to an endocrinologist for measurement of renin and aldosterone levels and further investigation.

Case 3: Hypertensive Emergency

A 45-year-old man is brought to the Emergency Department with a severe occipital headache and blurred vision for the past 12 hours. His blood pressure is 220/130 mmHg. Fundoscopy reveals bilateral papilloedema. The problem representation is a patient with severely elevated blood pressure and evidence of end-organ damage (papilloedema), diagnostic of malignant hypertension. He is admitted immediately for controlled intravenous blood pressure reduction and urgent investigation to assess for other organ damage.
