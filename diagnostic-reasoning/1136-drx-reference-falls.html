Falls Diagnostic Reasoning Reference
Context: Falls Assessment
Overview

Falls are a major clinical presentation, particularly in older adults, and are often referred to as a "geriatric giant". They represent a significant cause of morbidity and mortality, leading to injuries such as fractures, loss of confidence, fear of falling, and reduced independence. A fall is rarely a simple accident; it is often a symptom of an underlying acute illness or a manifestation of multiple interacting, chronic health impairments.

The primary challenge for any clinician is to move beyond simply treating the injury and to systematically investigate and manage the underlying cause(s) of the fall. This requires a multifactorial assessment to identify and address risk factors, thereby preventing future falls. The initial assessment must focus on identifying red flags that point towards immediate threats like major trauma, acute cardiovascular or neurological events.

This educational module is designed to build a foundational understanding of the clinical reasoning process for falls. For definitive, real-time management protocols, users must refer to official UK guidelines from authoritative bodies such as NICE (e.g., CG161 for Falls in older people: assessing risk and prevention) and the Resuscitation Council UK.

Pathophysiology/Basic Science

Understanding why people fall is key to diagnosis and management. Maintaining balance is a complex process requiring the coordinated function of several physiological systems. A fall occurs when this system fails.

  • Sensory Input Failure: The brain relies on three main sensory inputs to know where the body is in space. Decline in any of these increases fall risk.
    • Visual System: Age-related changes (e.g., cataracts, glaucoma) impair vision, reducing the ability to detect and avoid environmental hazards.
    • Vestibular System: The inner ear's balance organs can dysfunction (e.g., in BPPV or labyrinthitis), causing vertigo and a profound sense of imbalance.
    • Proprioception: The sensation of limb and joint position, transmitted from nerves in the feet and legs. This is commonly impaired in peripheral neuropathy (e.g., from diabetes or B12 deficiency), leading to unsteadiness.
  • Central Processing Failure: The brain must integrate sensory inputs and coordinate a motor response. Cognitive impairment (e.g., delirium or dementia) disrupts this process, leading to poor judgement, inattention to hazards, and slowed reaction times.
  • Effector (Musculoskeletal) Failure: An appropriate motor response requires adequate muscle strength, joint range of motion, and reaction time. Sarcopenia (age-related muscle loss) and arthritis directly impair the ability to maintain balance and to recover from a trip.
  • Cardiovascular Homeostasis Failure: Maintaining adequate blood flow to the brain is critical.
    • Orthostatic Hypotension: A failure of the baroreceptor reflex to adequately constrict blood vessels on standing causes a drop in blood pressure, leading to cerebral hypoperfusion, dizziness, and syncope (fainting). This is a very common cause of falls, often exacerbated by medications.
    • Cardiac Arrhythmias: An abnormally slow (bradycardia) or fast (tachycardia) heart rate can compromise cardiac output, leading to syncope and a fall, often with no warning.
Differential Diagnosis (DDx) Framework

A structured approach to the differential diagnosis of falls is essential to ensure life-threatening conditions are prioritised.

Must not miss!
  • Cerebrovascular Disease: Can cause falls through stroke or TIA, leading to focal neurological deficits, vertigo, or ataxia.
  • Arrhythmia: May present with syncope or pre-syncope leading to a fall due to a sudden drop in cardiac output.
  • Myocardial Infarction: Can present atypically in older adults with syncope or a fall as the primary symptom, rather than chest pain.
  • Vertebrobasilar insufficiency: Transient ischaemia of the posterior circulation can cause vertigo, drop attacks, and falls.
  • Hypoglycaemia: Low blood glucose can cause dizziness, confusion, or loss of consciousness, leading to a fall, particularly in patients on diabetic medication.
Common causes
  • Frailty: A state of increased vulnerability to stressors due to cumulative decline in multiple physiological systems, making falls more likely from minor insults.
  • Acute infections (UTI etc): Can precipitate delirium in older adults, leading to confusion, inattention, and an increased risk of falls.
  • Delirium presentation: An acute state of confusion that is a common cause of falls in hospitalised or unwell older people.
  • Visual problems: Age-related changes like cataracts, glaucoma, or macular degeneration impair hazard perception and increase fall risk.
  • Orthostatic hypotension: A significant drop in blood pressure on standing, causing cerebral hypoperfusion, dizziness, and falls.
  • ‘Dizziness’ presentation: A non-specific symptom that can be a manifestation of various underlying vestibular, cardiovascular, or neurological conditions leading to falls.
  • Parkinson's disease: Characterised by bradykinesia, rigidity, and postural instability, which significantly increase the risk of falls.
  • COVID-19: Can present with weakness, delirium, or dizziness, particularly in older adults, leading to an increased risk of falls.
Easily missed
  • Medication-induced Falls: Commonly caused by polypharmacy, particularly drugs affecting alertness (sedatives), blood pressure (antihypertensives), or balance (anticholinergics).
  • Alcohol abuse: Acute intoxication impairs coordination and judgement, while chronic abuse can cause cerebellar dysfunction and peripheral neuropathy.
  • Peripheral neuropathy: Damage to peripheral nerves, often due to diabetes or B12 deficiency, impairs proprioception and sensation in the feet, leading to instability.
Other Differential Diagnoses to consider
  • COVID-19: May present with weakness or dizziness, increasing fall risk, particularly in older adults
  • Seizures: Can result in falls due to sudden loss of consciousness or postictal confusion
  • Meniere's disease: Inner ear disorder causing vertigo and balance issues
  • Diabetic neuropathy: Can lead to impaired sensation in the feet and instability
  • Depression: Associated with decreased activity levels and poor balance
  • Spinal stenosis: Can cause neurogenic claudication and imbalance due to sensory loss in the legs
  • Normal pressure hydrocephalus: May present with the classic triad of gait disturbance, urinary incontinence, and cognitive impairment
  • Musculoskeletal disorders: Conditions such as arthritis or muscle weakness can impair balance and mobility
  • Vasovagal syncope: Sudden drop in heart rate and blood pressure can lead to fainting and falls
History Taking
**Patient’s Description**: How can I help you today?
Starting with an open-ended question like "Can you tell me more about what brought you here today?" allows the patient to describe the fall in their own words, offering insight into key details, such as the circumstances of the fall, frequency, and potential triggers. Relevance: This approach provides a natural flow to the consultation and helps tailor subsequent, more specific questions.
Could you describe the nature of the fall? Did you feel dizzy, weak, or lightheaded beforehand?
  • This question explores whether symptoms like dizziness, weakness, or lightheadedness preceded the fall, which can help identify underlying causes.
  • Relevance: A positive response might suggest orthostatic hypotension, dehydration, or hypoglycaemia. A negative response could rule out these causes, shifting focus to other potential triggers.
Was there a loss of consciousness at any point?
  • Assessing for syncope is crucial, as it can indicate underlying cardiac, neurological, or vasovagal causes.
  • Relevance: Loss of consciousness may suggest causes such as arrhythmia or vertebrobasilar insufficiency, while no loss of consciousness shifts focus to other explanations like musculoskeletal instability or peripheral neuropathy.
Have you experienced any recent infections or been feeling unwell in general?
  • This question helps identify systemic factors like infections, which can contribute to delirium or weakness, especially in older adults.
  • Relevance: A recent infection may suggest underlying delirium or frailty as contributing factors, while absence of infection might steer towards other causes.
Do you have any visual problems?
  • Vision changes can impair balance and contribute to falls, particularly in older adults.
  • Relevance: Positive response might indicate visual impairment as a primary factor, while a negative response helps rule out visual causes.
Have you started any new medications, or has there been a change in your dosage recently?
  • Medication changes can increase fall risk, especially those affecting blood pressure, sedation, or balance.
  • Relevance: A recent change may suggest medication-induced falls, particularly from antihypertensives, sedatives, or anticholinergics.
Are there any activities or positions that make you more likely to fall, or that help prevent falls?
  • Explores positional triggers or relievers, potentially indicating orthostatic hypotension or balance-related issues.
  • Relevance: Falls that occur upon standing or sitting up may suggest orthostatic hypotension, while falls during activities could suggest balance or muscular issues.
Associated Symptoms: Do you have any weakness, numbness, or tingling in your legs?
  • These symptoms may indicate neurological causes of imbalance, such as peripheral neuropathy or diabetic neuropathy.
  • Relevance: Positive findings suggest neuropathic or muscular conditions, while absence of symptoms shifts attention to other causes.
Associated Symptoms: Have you experienced any chest pain, palpitations, or shortness of breath?
  • These symptoms could suggest a cardiovascular cause of the fall, such as myocardial infarction or arrhythmia.
  • Relevance: Positive findings increase suspicion for cardiac causes, while a negative response makes these less likely.
**Ideas, Concerns, and Expectations (ICE)**
  • Asking about ICE allows insight into the patient's perspective, their worries about potentially serious conditions (e.g., stroke, heart issues), and their expectations for the consultation.
  • Relevance: Ensures a patient-centred approach, addressing both their concerns and expectations, which may include diagnostic clarity, reassurance, or management strategies.
Examination Strategy
Assessment of Vital Signs (including postural blood pressure)
  • Vital signs, including blood pressure, heart rate, and respiratory rate, help evaluate potential causes of falls. Postural blood pressure checks are crucial to identify orthostatic hypotension.
  • Relevance: A positive finding of postural hypotension (significant drop in blood pressure upon standing) suggests orthostatic hypotension as a potential cause, while stable postural blood pressure reduces the likelihood of this condition.
Cardiovascular Examination
  • Evaluates the heart and vascular system, focusing on any irregularities in rhythm, murmurs, or signs of heart failure.
  • Relevance: Abnormal findings like irregular heart rhythms may suggest arrhythmia as a contributing factor to falls. A normal cardiovascular exam lowers the probability of cardiac causes, such as arrhythmias or heart failure.
Neurological Examination
  • Assesses motor function, sensation, reflexes, and coordination. Key elements include gait assessment, Romberg’s test, and cerebellar function tests.
  • Relevance: Positive findings (e.g., abnormal gait, reduced sensation, impaired coordination) can indicate neurological causes like peripheral neuropathy, Parkinson’s disease, or stroke. Normal findings may help exclude these causes.
Assessment of Gait and Balance
  • Involves observing the patient’s gait and balance, identifying any difficulties in coordination or instability.
  • Relevance: An unsteady gait or poor balance may point towards musculoskeletal issues, neurological conditions, or frailty. Normal gait suggests that these are less likely causes.
Vision Assessment
  • Includes checking visual acuity, fields, and eye movements, as visual impairment can significantly impact balance and increase fall risk.
  • Relevance: Poor visual acuity or restricted fields may indicate visual impairment as a primary factor in falls. Normal vision can help exclude visual problems as a likely cause.
Musculoskeletal Examination
  • Examines joint stability, muscle strength, and range of motion, particularly in the lower limbs. It also includes assessing for pain or tenderness that may impact mobility.
  • Relevance: Weakness, joint instability, or pain may suggest musculoskeletal causes of falls. Normal findings may reduce the likelihood of musculoskeletal issues contributing to the fall.
Cognitive Assessment (e.g., Mini-Mental State Examination)
  • Evaluates cognitive function to detect potential delirium or dementia, as both can impair judgement, coordination, and increase fall risk.
  • Relevance: Impaired cognition may indicate delirium or dementia, which are significant risk factors for falls. Normal cognition can help rule out these as contributing factors.
Red Flag Synthesis
Loss of consciousness
  • Loss of consciousness (LOC) preceding or during a fall can indicate serious underlying conditions such as arrhythmias, stroke, or vertebrobasilar insufficiency.
  • Significance: LOC may signify a cardiac or neurological emergency and requires urgent assessment to rule out life-threatening causes.
New or worsening chest pain
  • Chest pain accompanying a fall raises concern for cardiovascular causes, such as myocardial infarction, which can lead to syncope or falls, especially in older adults.
  • Significance: Chest pain may indicate an acute coronary syndrome, requiring immediate evaluation and possible emergency intervention.
Acute focal neurological deficit (e.g., limb weakness, facial droop, slurred speech)
  • New neurological deficits could indicate a stroke or transient ischaemic attack, which may present with falls in older adults.
  • Significance: This presentation is highly suggestive of a cerebrovascular event, necessitating urgent evaluation and intervention to prevent further complications.
Severe headache, especially if sudden or “thunderclap” in nature
  • A sudden, severe headache preceding a fall can be a sign of subarachnoid haemorrhage or other intracranial pathology.
  • Significance: This type of headache is a red flag for serious intracranial events, such as haemorrhage, and requires immediate neurological assessment.
Palpitations or irregular heart rate
  • Palpitations or an irregular pulse may indicate arrhythmias, which can lead to syncope or falls due to reduced cardiac output.
  • Significance: Arrhythmias can pose serious risks, including sudden cardiac arrest, and require prompt cardiological assessment.
Unexplained weight loss
  • Significant, unexplained weight loss may suggest underlying malignancy, which can contribute to falls due to generalised weakness or metastatic complications.
  • Significance: Weight loss is a concerning sign for potential cancer, requiring a thorough workup for malignancy or other chronic disease.
Sudden onset of confusion or delirium
  • Acute confusion or delirium can increase fall risk and often indicates an underlying acute medical issue, such as infection, metabolic imbalance, or drug toxicity.
  • Significance: Delirium requires prompt investigation as it may signal serious, potentially reversible causes, including infection or metabolic derangements.
Severe lower limb pain or inability to bear weight
  • Inability to bear weight or severe pain following a fall could indicate fractures, such as hip fractures, which are common in older adults and require urgent treatment.
  • Significance: This finding suggests potential fractures or soft tissue injury, needing urgent imaging and intervention to prevent complications.
Investigations Strategy & Interpretation
Full Blood Count (FBC)
  • An FBC is useful to check for anaemia, infection, or other haematological abnormalities, which can contribute to weakness or dizziness, leading to falls.
  • Result Significance: Anaemia or signs of infection may suggest causes like systemic infection or frailty. A normal FBC makes these less likely as primary causes of the fall.
Electrolytes, Urea, and Creatinine (EUC)
  • Evaluates for electrolyte imbalances or dehydration, which can lead to confusion, weakness, or orthostatic hypotension and contribute to falls.
  • Result Significance: Abnormal results (e.g., hypokalaemia or hyponatraemia) could indicate an increased risk of falls due to electrolyte imbalance. Normal results rule out these metabolic causes.
Blood Glucose
  • Assesses for hypoglycaemia, which can cause dizziness, weakness, and syncope, leading to falls, especially in diabetic patients.
  • Result Significance: Low blood glucose levels indicate hypoglycaemia as a likely cause, while normal levels make it less likely.
ECG (Electrocardiogram)
  • An ECG is essential to assess for arrhythmias or myocardial infarction, both of which can lead to syncope and falls, particularly in older adults.
  • Result Significance: Abnormal findings, such as arrhythmias or signs of acute myocardial infarction, suggest a cardiac cause. A normal ECG lowers suspicion for these issues.

Orthostatic Blood Pressure Measurement
  • Assesses for orthostatic hypotension, which can cause lightheadedness or syncope upon standing, increasing fall risk.
  • Result Significance: A significant drop in blood pressure on standing confirms orthostatic hypotension as a potential cause. Normal results reduce the likelihood of this as a contributing factor.
Cranial CT or MRI
  • Neuroimaging may be indicated if neurological signs are present or if there is a suspicion of head injury, stroke, or intracranial pathology following a fall.
  • Result Significance: Positive findings, such as haemorrhage or stroke, confirm an intracranial cause. Negative imaging can help rule out these serious conditions.
Vitamin B12 and Folate Levels
  • Low levels of Vitamin B12 or folate can contribute to neuropathy and balance issues, potentially increasing fall risk.
  • Result Significance: Deficiencies may indicate neuropathic causes contributing to falls, while normal levels make this less likely.
Urinalysis
  • Urinalysis can help identify urinary tract infections (UTIs), which are common in older adults and can contribute to delirium, weakness, and falls.
  • Result Significance: Positive findings for infection suggest a UTI as a contributing factor, while a negative result makes this less likely.
Echocardiography
  • Echocardiography can evaluate cardiac function, identifying structural heart disease, valve pathology, or heart failure, which can predispose to syncope and falls.
  • Result Significance: Abnormal findings may indicate heart failure or valvular disease, increasing fall risk. Normal findings make these cardiac issues less likely.
Evidence-Based Diagnosis (EBDx)
Orthostatic Hypotension as a Cause of Falls
  • Orthostatic hypotension is a common cause of falls in older adults, often identified through postural blood pressure measurements.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: ~67%, Specificity: ~90% for diagnosing orthostatic hypotension as a cause of falls when blood pressure drops ≥20 mmHg systolic or ≥10 mmHg diastolic on standing.
  • Reference: Finucane et al., 2020, European Heart Journal Guidelines.
  • Significance: A positive postural BP drop suggests orthostatic hypotension, guiding appropriate management. A negative finding reduces the likelihood of this condition as a cause.
Abnormal ECG Findings in Falls Due to Arrhythmias
  • An ECG can detect arrhythmias, a significant risk factor for syncope and falls, especially in older patients.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 75-85%, Specificity: 95% for detecting arrhythmias related to syncope and falls.
  • Reference: Shen et al., 2017, ACC/AHA/HRS Guideline for the Evaluation and Management of Syncope.
  • Significance: Positive findings, such as bradycardia or tachyarrhythmia, strongly suggest a cardiac cause. A normal ECG makes arrhythmias less likely but does not fully exclude them.
Cognitive Impairment in Falls (MMSE)
  • Cognitive impairment, often measured by the Mini-Mental State Examination (MMSE), is associated with increased fall risk, particularly in dementia and delirium.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 80%, Specificity: 70% for predicting fall risk in patients with MMSE scores below 24.
  • Reference: Montero-Odasso et al., 2012, Journal of the American Geriatrics Society.
  • Significance: An MMSE score <24 indicates increased fall risk due to impaired cognition, suggesting potential causes like dementia or delirium.
Blood Glucose Testing for Hypoglycaemia
  • Hypoglycaemia is a known cause of dizziness and falls, particularly in diabetic patients. Blood glucose levels below 3.9 mmol/L are diagnostic.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 85%, Specificity: 90% for identifying hypoglycaemia-related falls when glucose levels are <3.9 mmol/L.
  • Reference: Seaquist et al., 2013, American Diabetes Association Position Statement.
  • Significance: Low glucose confirms hypoglycaemia as a likely cause of the fall, while normal glucose suggests this is less likely.
Cranial CT Scan for Suspected Intracranial Injury or Stroke
  • Cranial CT is essential for identifying intracranial injury or stroke in patients with falls accompanied by neurological symptoms.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: ~95%, Specificity: ~90% for detecting acute intracranial pathology in patients with head trauma or neurological deficits.
  • Reference: NICE Guidelines (Head Injury: Assessment and Early Management, 2019).
  • Significance: Positive CT findings confirm intracranial pathology, necessitating urgent intervention. Negative findings can rule out many structural causes of falls.
Urinalysis for Urinary Tract Infection (UTI)
  • UTI is a common cause of delirium in older adults, which can present with falls. Urinalysis with positive leukocyte esterase and nitrites supports a UTI diagnosis.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 75%, Specificity: 82% for diagnosing UTI in patients with positive nitrites and leukocyte esterase.
  • Reference: Nicolle et al., 2019, Infectious Diseases Society of America Guidelines.
  • Significance: A positive urinalysis suggests UTI-related delirium as a contributing factor to falls. A negative result helps rule out UTI as a cause.
Diagnostic Timeout & Cognitive Biases

Awareness of common thinking errors is essential to improve diagnostic accuracy and patient safety when assessing a patient who has fallen. Before finalising a diagnosis, it is good practice for clinicians at all levels to take a 'diagnostic timeout' to challenge their own reasoning.

  • Anchoring Bias: The tendency to lock onto the initial narrative (e.g., "I just tripped over the rug") too early, failing to consider new, more serious possibilities even when red flags like pre-syncope or palpitations are mentioned. A key question to ask is: "What is the most dangerous thing this could be, and have I ruled it out?"
  • Attribution Error (Ageism): This is a major pitfall in geriatrics. It is the error of attributing a fall simply to the patient's "old age" or "frailty," leading to premature diagnostic closure. This prevents a thorough investigation for specific, treatable underlying causes like arrhythmia, orthostatic hypotension, or infection.
  • Confirmation Bias: Actively seeking out or overvaluing evidence that supports an initial belief (e.g., focusing on a history of arthritis to confirm a mechanical fall) while ignoring or downplaying evidence that refutes it (e.g., a new medication known to cause dizziness).
  • Over-reliance on Normal Tests: Placing too much weight on a single reassuring but non-definitive test result. A single normal ECG does not definitively rule out a cardiac arrhythmia as a cause of a fall, as the arrhythmia may be paroxysmal. Prolonged monitoring (e.g., a 24-hour Holter monitor) may be required if the history is suspicious.
Illustrative Management Pathways

For educational purposes, this section describes the typical principles of management for key diagnoses. These are not prescriptive instructions. All management must be guided by official UK guidelines, local formularies, and senior clinical advice.

Multifactorial Falls Prevention

For older patients with recurrent falls or frailty, management is multifactorial, guided by a Comprehensive Geriatric Assessment (CGA). This is a holistic, multidisciplinary process. Key interventions include a personalised strength and balance training programme from a physiotherapist, a structured medication review to reduce or stop problematic drugs (polypharmacy), and a home environment assessment by an occupational therapist to identify and remove hazards. Management of specific medical issues like visual impairment or osteoporosis is also crucial.

Orthostatic Hypotension

First-line management involves patient education on standing up slowly, maintaining hydration, and using counter-pressure manoeuvres (e.g., crossing legs, clenching fists). This is followed by a thorough medication review to reduce or stop causative drugs (e.g., antihypertensives, alpha-blockers, diuretics). If symptoms persist despite these measures, a specialist may advise on increasing salt intake or consider mineralocorticoids like fludrocortisone.

Cardiac Syncope / Arrhythmia

If a significant cardiac arrhythmia (e.g., complete heart block, ventricular tachycardia) is identified as the cause of a fall with syncope, this constitutes a medical emergency. Management requires urgent cardiology referral and admission for cardiac monitoring. Depending on the specific rhythm disturbance, treatment may involve permanent pacemaker insertion, implantable cardioverter-defibrillator (ICD), or specific anti-arrhythmic medications.

Vestibular Causes (e.g., BPPV)

If benign paroxysmal positional vertigo (BPPV) is diagnosed based on a characteristic history (brief, rotational vertigo triggered by head movements) and a positive Dix-Hallpike test, the primary treatment is a canalith repositioning procedure. The Epley manoeuvre is highly effective and can often be performed at the bedside in primary or secondary care, providing immediate relief. Vestibular rehabilitation exercises may also be taught to help the brain adapt.

Reflection & Learning

These brief cases illustrate the application of clinical reasoning to different presentations of falls. They are for educational purposes only.

Case 1: Delirium in a Care Home

An 88-year-old care home resident with known Alzheimer's dementia has had two falls today. The staff report she is more confused than usual and is not eating. Her vital signs show a temperature of 37.9°C, a heart rate of 110 bpm, and a blood pressure of 105/60 mmHg. A urine dipstick is positive for nitrites and leucocytes. The problem representation is an older person with baseline cognitive impairment presenting with an acute functional decline (falls) and confusion, consistent with delirium precipitated by a urinary tract infection (UTI). The immediate pathway involves treating the underlying infection with antibiotics as per local guidelines and ensuring adequate hydration.

Case 2: Polypharmacy in the GP Clinic

A 79-year-old man presents to his GP after a fall when getting out of bed at night. He felt very lightheaded just beforehand but did not lose consciousness. His past medical history includes hypertension and benign prostatic hyperplasia. Review of his records shows that an alpha-blocker (Tamsulosin) was added for his prostate symptoms two weeks ago. A lying and standing blood pressure check in the clinic shows his systolic BP drops from 140 mmHg to 105 mmHg on standing. The problem representation is a patient with a pre-syncopal fall secondary to iatrogenic orthostatic hypotension. Management involves stopping the offending drug and providing education on postural symptoms and slow positional changes.

Case 3: Syncope in the Emergency Department

A 75-year-old woman presents to the ED after a sudden fall while shopping. She had no warning symptoms and lost consciousness for approximately 30 seconds, according to a bystander. She has a past medical history of ischaemic heart disease. Her examination is unremarkable and a CT head shows no acute injury. Her ECG, however, shows a new Left Bundle Branch Block (LBBB). The problem representation is a patient with a high-risk, no-warning syncopal fall with a new conduction abnormality on ECG. This is highly concerning for an underlying cardiac arrhythmia (e.g., intermittent complete heart block). This requires urgent admission for continuous cardiac monitoring and cardiology referral.
