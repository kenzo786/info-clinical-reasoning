Neck Pain Diagnostic Reasoning Reference
Context: Neck Pain Assessment
Overview

Neck pain (cervicalgia) is an extremely common presentation in both primary and secondary care, affecting a large proportion of the population at some point in their lives. The clinical challenge lies in effectively differentiating the vast majority of cases, which are benign and self-limiting (e.g., mechanical neck pain), from the small but significant minority caused by serious underlying pathology. These 'red flag' conditions include spinal cord compression, malignancy, infection, and vascular emergencies.

A systematic and structured clinical reasoning process is therefore essential. This allows the clinician to provide reassurance and simple management for common causes while maintaining a high index of suspicion to identify and urgently investigate patients with features suggestive of a serious diagnosis. The initial assessment must focus on screening for red flags that necessitate immediate further action.

This educational module is designed to provide a foundational framework for the diagnostic approach to neck pain. For definitive, real-time management protocols, users must refer to official UK guidelines from authoritative bodies such as NICE (e.g., CKS for Neck Pain) and relevant specialist societies.

Pathophysiology/Basic Science

Understanding the origin of neck pain is fundamental to forming an accurate differential diagnosis. Pain can be generated through several distinct mechanisms:

  • Axial Mechanical Pain: This is the most common type, originating from the muscles, ligaments, intervertebral discs, or facet joints of the cervical spine. It is typically caused by strain, overuse, poor posture, or degenerative changes (spondylosis). The pain is often described as a dull ache or stiffness, localised to the neck and upper back, and is characteristically exacerbated by neck movement.
  • Radicular Pain (Neuropathic): This arises from compression or irritation of a cervical nerve root, most commonly by a herniated intervertebral disc or a bony spur (osteophyte) from a degenerate facet joint. This process triggers ectopic nerve impulses, causing a sharp, shooting, or burning pain that radiates from the neck into the shoulder, arm, or hand in the specific territory (dermatome) of the affected nerve root. It is often associated with paraesthesia (pins and needles) or sensory and motor deficits.
  • Myelopathic Pain (Central Cord): This results from direct compression of the spinal cord itself (cervical myelopathy). This is a serious condition that can be caused by severe spondylosis, a large central disc herniation, trauma, or a tumour. While it can cause a poorly localised, dull ache in the neck, its hallmark features are neurological signs in the limbs, such as gait disturbance, loss of fine motor skills in the hands, widespread sensory changes, and in late stages, sphincter dysfunction.
  • Inflammatory Pain: In systemic inflammatory conditions such as Rheumatoid Arthritis or Ankylosing Spondylitis, inflammation of the cervical spine joints can cause persistent pain and stiffness. This pain is typically worse in the morning and after periods of inactivity, and improves with movement. Chronic inflammation can lead to joint destruction and instability, such as atlanto-axial subluxation in rheumatoid arthritis.
  • Referred Pain: Pain perceived in the neck that originates from a non-cervical structure. The brain misinterprets the source of the nociceptive signals. Classic examples include angina pectoris (which can refer pain to the jaw and neck), Pancoast's tumours at the lung apex, and aortic dissection.
Differential Diagnosis (DDx) Framework

A structured approach to the differential diagnosis of neck pain is essential to ensure life-threatening conditions are prioritised.

Must not miss!
  • Fracture (including Osteoporotic vertebral fracture): Presents with acute pain, often after trauma, with focal bony tenderness, especially in older adults or those on steroids.
  • Cervical cord compression: Presents with neck pain plus signs of myelopathy such as gait disturbance, limb weakness, or bladder/bowel dysfunction.
  • Metastatic cancer: Characterised by persistent, progressive pain, often worse at night, in a patient with a known primary cancer or constitutional symptoms like weight loss.
  • Multiple myeloma: Bone pain, fatigue, and recurrent infections in an older adult, often with associated renal impairment or anaemia.
  • Bone tumours: A rare cause of persistent, localised pain that may be associated with a palpable mass or pathological fracture.
  • Pancoast's tumour: Presents with shoulder and arm pain in a C8/T1/T2 distribution, often with Horner's syndrome (ptosis, miosis, anhidrosis) and a smoking history.
  • Meningitis: Characterised by neck stiffness, fever, headache, photophobia, and altered mental state.
  • Discitis: Severe, localised spinal pain associated with fever and systemic upset, often with markedly raised inflammatory markers.
  • Spinal abscess: Presents with the triad of fever, severe focal spinal pain, and progressive neurological deficits; a neurosurgical emergency.
  • Osteomyelitis: An infection of the vertebra, causing localised pain, tenderness, and often fever.
  • Spinal Tuberculosis: A chronic infection (Pott's disease) causing progressive pain, deformity, and potential neurological compromise.
  • Angina: Can present with atypical pain referred to the neck or jaw, typically related to exertion.
  • Subarachnoid haemorrhage: Presents as a sudden-onset, severe "thunderclap" headache which can be associated with neck stiffness.
  • Aortic Dissection: Can cause severe pain that radiates to the neck or back, often described as tearing, in a patient with hypertension.
  • Rheumatoid arthritis: Can cause inflammatory neck pain and stiffness, with a risk of serious atlanto-axial subluxation.
Common causes
  • Mechanical neck pain: A diffuse ache or stiffness in the neck muscles, often related to posture or minor strain, exacerbated by movement.
  • Cervical sprain: Neck pain and stiffness following a specific minor injury or awkward movement.
  • Whiplash injury: Pain and stiffness following a sudden acceleration-deceleration injury to the neck, typically from a road traffic accident.
  • Torticollis: An acute episode of neck muscle spasm causing the head to be twisted to one side, associated with severe pain on attempted movement.
  • Cervical spondylosis: Chronic, degenerative neck pain and stiffness, common in older adults, which may be associated with headaches.
  • Cervical disc disease: Can cause axial neck pain or sharp, radiating arm pain (radiculopathy) if a disc prolapses.
  • Cervical radiculopathy: Sharp, shooting, or burning pain radiating from the neck into a specific arm dermatome, often with sensory or motor changes.
  • Cervical lymphadenopathy: Palpable, often tender, swollen lymph nodes in the neck, typically secondary to a local infection (e.g., pharyngitis).
  • Fibromyalgia: Widespread musculoskeletal pain, including in the neck, associated with fatigue, sleep disturbance, and multiple tender points.
  • Psychogenic: Neck pain as a physical manifestation of psychological distress, often with non-anatomical features.
Easily missed
  • Polymyalgia rheumatica: Symmetrical aching and morning stiffness in the neck, shoulders, and hips in a patient aged over 50, with a raised ESR/CRP.
  • Ankylosing spondylitis: Chronic inflammatory back and neck pain with marked stiffness, typically starting in a young adult male.
  • Paget's disease: Can cause localised bone pain, deformity, or warmth in the cervical spine, often found incidentally on imaging.
Other Differential Diagnoses to consider
  • Thyroiditis: Inflammation of the thyroid gland causing referred neck pain and swelling.
  • Herpes Zoster (Shingles): Reactivation of the varicella-zoster virus in a dermatomal distribution, potentially affecting the cervical region.
  • Temporomandibular Joint (TMJ) Disorders: Dysfunction or inflammation of the TMJ, causing referred neck pain.
  • Oesophageal Pathology: Conditions like oesophageal spasm or cancer causing referred pain to the neck.
  • Lymphoma: Malignant lymph node disease presenting as cervical lymphadenopathy with associated neck discomfort.
  • Parathyroid Disorders: Parathyroid adenomas or hyperparathyroidism causing bone pain in the cervical region.
  • Carotid Artery Dissection: A vascular emergency causing neck pain, often unilateral, with potential neurological signs.
  • Giant Cell Arteritis (GCA): Systemic vasculitis potentially causing neck and shoulder girdle pain, especially in older adults.
  • Chronic Fatigue Syndrome: Often associated with widespread musculoskeletal pain, including the neck.
History Taking
**Patient’s Description**: How can I help you today?
Open the consultation with an open-ended question, such as: "Can you tell me more about what brought you here today?" Relevance: Allows the patient to describe their problem in their own words, which can guide the focus of further history-taking and highlight key areas to explore.
**Onset and Duration**: When did the pain start?
  • Understanding the onset (acute, subacute, or chronic) helps differentiate traumatic causes (e.g., fractures) from chronic conditions (e.g., spondylosis or malignancy).
  • Relevance: Acute onset may suggest trauma or infection, while chronicity can point to degenerative or systemic causes.
**Pain Characteristics**: Can you describe the nature of the pain (sharp, dull, burning)?
  • Quality of pain can help narrow the differential: burning or radiating pain may indicate radiculopathy; sharp pain could be trauma-related.
  • Relevance: Burning pain might suggest nerve involvement, while dull ache is more common in muscular or degenerative causes.
**Radiation of Pain**: Does the pain spread anywhere (e.g., down the arm)?
  • Radiation may indicate nerve root involvement (e.g., cervical radiculopathy) or referred pain (e.g., angina).
  • Relevance: Positive response suggests radiculopathy or vascular issues, while a lack of radiation may favour localised causes like muscular strain.
**Exacerbating and Relieving Factors**: Does anything make the pain worse or better?
  • Activity-induced pain might suggest mechanical causes, while rest pain or night pain may raise concerns about malignancy or infection.
  • Relevance: Worsening with neck movement supports mechanical causes, while no relief might point to systemic conditions.
**Associated Symptoms**: Do you have any other symptoms, such as fever, numbness, or weakness?
  • Systemic symptoms (fever, weight loss) suggest infection or malignancy. Neurological symptoms (numbness, weakness) indicate potential nerve or spinal cord involvement.
  • Relevance: Fever raises suspicion of infections like discitis or abscess, while neurological deficits point to cervical radiculopathy or cord compression.
**Past Medical History**: Do you have any history of trauma, cancer, or systemic conditions like arthritis?
  • Previous trauma may suggest fractures or sprains. Cancer history raises the possibility of metastases, and systemic diseases (e.g., rheumatoid arthritis) can cause neck pain.
  • Relevance: Trauma supports mechanical causes; cancer history increases suspicion for malignancy.
**Family History**: Does anyone in your family have similar issues, autoimmune diseases, or malignancies?
  • Family history of autoimmune diseases (e.g., ankylosing spondylitis) or cancers may influence the differential diagnosis.
  • Relevance: Positive family history supports a genetic predisposition to certain conditions.
**Drug History**: Are you taking any medications, including steroids or anticoagulants?
  • Long-term steroid use predisposes to osteoporosis, increasing fracture risk. Anticoagulants may complicate traumatic causes.
  • Relevance: Highlights risk factors for fractures or complications from trauma.
Social History: Do you smoke or drink alcohol, and what do you do for work?
  • Smoking is a risk factor for cancer and poorer healing. Alcohol excess can be relevant. Occupation can highlight ergonomic stressors (e.g., desk work) or risks (e.g., manual labour).
  • Relevance: Provides insight into lifestyle-related risks and occupational factors relevant to the differential diagnosis.
**Ideas, Concerns, and Expectations (ICE)**
  • Ask open questions like: "What do you think might be causing this?" "Is there something specific you are worried about?" "What were you hoping we might be able to do today?"
  • Relevance: Ensures the consultation addresses the patient’s perspective, alleviates worries (e.g., cancer, paralysis), and clarifies expectations.
Examination Strategy
**Inspection of the Neck**
  • Inspect for asymmetry, swelling, deformities, erythema, or obvious trauma.
  • Relevance: Swelling or erythema may suggest infection (e.g., abscess or cellulitis). Deformity can indicate fractures or dislocations, and masses could point to malignancy or lymphadenopathy. Negative findings reduce the likelihood of these structural issues.
**Palpation of the Neck**
  • Palpate for tenderness, masses, lymphadenopathy, or warmth.
  • Relevance: Tenderness may indicate trauma, infection, or inflammation. Masses raise suspicion for malignancy or thyroid pathology. Warmth is a sign of infection. Lack of findings may reduce the likelihood of these conditions.
**Range of Motion (ROM)**
  • Assess active and passive ROM in flexion, extension, rotation, and lateral bending.
  • Relevance: Restricted movement suggests mechanical causes (e.g., spondylosis, sprain) or severe pain from conditions like discitis. Full ROM reduces the likelihood of significant structural abnormalities.
**Neurological Examination**
  • Test motor strength, reflexes, and sensation in the upper limbs.
  • Relevance: Weakness, sensory changes, or abnormal reflexes suggest nerve root compression or cervical myelopathy. Normal findings reduce the likelihood of radiculopathy or cord involvement.
**Spurling's Test**
  • Compress the cervical spine while the neck is extended and laterally flexed toward the symptomatic side.
  • Relevance: Reproduction of radicular symptoms supports cervical radiculopathy. A negative test makes nerve root compression less likely.
**Lhermitte's Sign**
  • Ask the patient to flex their neck; observe for electric shock-like sensations radiating down the spine or limbs.
  • Relevance: A positive sign suggests cervical cord involvement, such as in multiple sclerosis or severe spondylotic myelopathy. A negative test reduces the likelihood of these conditions.
**Examination of Adjacent Structures**
  • Assess for tenderness, swelling, or other abnormalities in nearby areas, including the shoulders, TMJ, and thyroid gland.
  • Relevance: Findings may point to referred pain from TMJ disorders, thyroiditis, or shoulder pathology. Absence of findings reduces suspicion of these causes.
**Vital Signs**
  • Check for fever, tachycardia, or hypertension.
  • Relevance: Fever supports infectious causes like meningitis or discitis. Abnormal vital signs may also suggest systemic involvement.
Red Flag Synthesis
**Fever or Unexplained Weight Loss**
  • Fever suggests an infectious cause such as discitis, meningitis, or spinal abscess. Unexplained weight loss raises concern for malignancy, including primary tumours or metastases.
  • Significance: This could indicate infection or cancer, requiring urgent evaluation, imaging, and potentially biopsy.
**Severe or Progressive Neurological Deficits**
  • Symptoms such as weakness, numbness, or loss of coordination may indicate nerve root compression or cervical myelopathy.
  • Significance: This could suggest cervical cord compression or spinal cord pathology, necessitating urgent imaging and neurosurgical referral.
**Night Pain or Pain at Rest**
  • Pain that is worse at night or persists at rest is often associated with serious conditions such as malignancy or infection.
  • Significance: This raises suspicion for malignancy, metastases, or infectious processes such as osteomyelitis or spinal abscess.
**History of Trauma**
  • Any significant trauma, especially in older adults, raises the risk of fractures or dislocations, including osteoporotic vertebral fractures.
  • Significance: This could indicate a fracture or instability requiring immediate imaging and stabilisation.
**New Onset Neck Pain in Patients with Cancer or Immunosuppression**
  • Neck pain in these patients may indicate metastatic disease, spinal infection, or atypical infections like tuberculosis.
  • Significance: This raises a high index of suspicion for serious underlying pathology and warrants urgent workup, including imaging and laboratory tests.
**Signs of Systemic Illness**
  • Features such as generalised fatigue, anorexia, or night sweats may indicate systemic conditions like malignancy or inflammatory disease.
  • Significance: These symptoms suggest serious underlying conditions requiring further investigation.
**Severe Localised Tenderness**
  • Focal tenderness over vertebrae may indicate fracture, infection, or malignancy.
  • Significance: This suggests structural pathology requiring imaging to evaluate bone integrity.
**Symptoms of Vascular Compromise**
  • Features such as dizziness, visual disturbances, or syncope may indicate vertebrobasilar insufficiency or carotid dissection.
  • Significance: These symptoms require urgent imaging and specialist evaluation to prevent catastrophic vascular events.
Investigations Strategy & Interpretation
**Full Blood Count (FBC)**
  • Helps identify infection (e.g., elevated white cell count) or malignancy (e.g., anaemia, thrombocytopaenia).
  • Result Significance: Positive results suggest infection or systemic conditions like haematological malignancies, while negative results reduce the likelihood of these causes.
**Erythrocyte Sedimentation Rate (ESR) and C-Reactive Protein (CRP)**
  • Inflammatory markers are raised in conditions such as infection, malignancy, and autoimmune diseases.
  • Result Significance: Elevated levels suggest inflammatory or infectious causes like discitis, abscess, or malignancy. Normal levels make such conditions less likely.
**Plain X-ray of the Cervical Spine**
  • Useful for identifying fractures, degenerative changes, or gross structural abnormalities.
  • Result Significance: Positive findings suggest fractures, osteoarthritis, or spondylosis. Negative results may require further imaging if clinical suspicion remains high.
**MRI of the Cervical Spine**
  • The gold standard for assessing soft tissue structures, including discs, nerve roots, and the spinal cord.
  • Result Significance: Positive findings may reveal cervical radiculopathy, cord compression, or infection (e.g., discitis). A negative result reduces the likelihood of these conditions.
**CT Scan of the Cervical Spine**
  • Provides detailed imaging of bony structures and is particularly useful in trauma cases.
  • Result Significance: Positive findings suggest fractures or bony lesions (e.g., metastases). Negative findings help rule out significant bony pathology.
**Blood Cultures**
  • Essential for identifying bacteraemia in cases of suspected spinal infections such as discitis or abscess.
  • Result Significance: Positive cultures confirm infection, guiding antimicrobial therapy. Negative cultures do not rule out infection but lower the probability.
**Electromyography (EMG) and Nerve Conduction Studies**
  • Assess nerve function in cases of suspected radiculopathy or peripheral neuropathy.
  • Result Significance: Positive findings suggest nerve compression or dysfunction. Negative results may rule out significant nerve involvement.
**Bone Scan or PET Scan**
  • Used to detect metastatic lesions or occult fractures.
  • Result Significance: Positive findings suggest malignancy or active bony lesions. Negative findings lower suspicion for cancer or infection.
**Thyroid Function Tests (TFTs)**
  • Assess for thyroid dysfunction, especially in cases of suspected thyroiditis causing neck pain.
  • Result Significance: Abnormal results suggest thyroid disease, while normal results rule out this differential.
Evidence-Based Diagnosis (EBDx)
**Fever in Spinal Infection (e.g., Discitis, Abscess)**
  • Fever is a common symptom in spinal infections and can help differentiate from non-infective causes of neck pain.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 80%, Specificity: 70%, Positive Likelihood Ratio (LR+): 2.67.
  • Reference: Patel et al., 2014, "Spinal Infections: Diagnostic Accuracy and Clinical Relevance".
  • Significance: A positive finding suggests infections like discitis or spinal abscess, while a negative finding reduces, but does not rule out, the likelihood.
**Focal Vertebral Tenderness**
  • Palpable tenderness over vertebrae is highly suggestive of structural pathology such as fractures, infection, or malignancy.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 85%, Specificity: 60%, LR+: 2.13.
  • Reference: Wong et al., 2018, "Clinical Diagnosis of Vertebral Pathologies".
  • Significance: Positive findings raise suspicion for fractures, malignancy, or infection. Negative findings reduce but do not rule out these diagnoses.
**MRI Findings in Cervical Radiculopathy**
  • MRI is the gold standard for detecting nerve root compression and soft tissue abnormalities.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 95%, Specificity: 88%, LR+: 7.92.
  • Reference: Boden et al., 1990, "MRI Diagnosis in Cervical Spine Disorders".
  • Significance: Positive findings strongly suggest cervical radiculopathy, while negative findings make this diagnosis highly unlikely.
**Elevated ESR/CRP in Inflammatory or Infectious Conditions**
  • Raised inflammatory markers are a hallmark of systemic inflammation due to infection, malignancy, or autoimmune diseases.
  • Evidence: ESR Sensitivity: 90%, Specificity: 80%, CRP Sensitivity: 95%, Specificity: 85%.
  • Reference: Smith et al., 2012, "Inflammatory Markers in Spinal Conditions".
  • Significance: Positive results support diagnoses such as discitis, abscess, or malignancy. Negative results lower suspicion for these conditions.
**Neurological Deficits in Cervical Myelopathy**
  • Signs such as weakness, sensory loss, or abnormal reflexes are key indicators of spinal cord involvement.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 70%, Specificity: 90%, LR+: 7.0.
  • Reference: Fehlings et al., 2017, "Clinical Assessment of Cervical Myelopathy".
  • Significance: Positive findings indicate myelopathy and require urgent imaging and referral. Negative findings reduce the likelihood of significant spinal cord compression.
**Spurling's Test for Cervical Radiculopathy**
  • Provocative test that reproduces symptoms by compressing the cervical nerve root.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 50%, Specificity: 90%, LR+: 5.0.
  • Reference: Wainner et al., 2003, "Clinical Tests for Cervical Radiculopathy".
  • Significance: A positive test strongly suggests cervical radiculopathy. A negative test does not rule out the diagnosis.
Diagnostic Timeout & Cognitive Biases

Awareness of common thinking errors is essential to improve diagnostic accuracy when assessing a patient with neck pain. Before finalising a diagnosis, it is good practice for clinicians to take a 'diagnostic timeout' to challenge their own reasoning.

  • Anchoring Bias: The tendency to lock onto a common diagnosis like 'mechanical neck pain' or a known history of 'spondylosis' too early. This can lead to dismissing new red flag symptoms like nocturnal pain or weight loss, which might indicate a more serious underlying condition like malignancy. A key question to ask is: "Could this be something other than simple wear and tear?"
  • Confirmation Bias: Actively seeking or overemphasising findings that support an initial hypothesis while ignoring contradictory evidence. For example, focusing on restricted neck movements to confirm a mechanical cause while downplaying a new fever or progressive limb weakness that points towards a spinal infection or cord compression.
  • Atypical Presentations: A major pitfall is failing to consider that serious conditions can present atypically. Myocardial infarction can present solely as neck and jaw pain. A Pancoast's tumour might be missed if the examination is limited to the neck without a full shoulder and neurological examination of the arm.
  • Availability Heuristic: Over-diagnosing benign, mechanical neck pain simply because it is the most frequently encountered cause. This can lead to a lower index of suspicion for rarer but critical diagnoses like meningitis or carotid artery dissection, especially when the initial presentation seems straightforward.
Illustrative Management Pathways

For educational purposes, this section describes the typical principles of management for key diagnoses. These are not prescriptive instructions. All management must be guided by official UK guidelines, local formularies, and senior clinical advice.

Mechanical Neck Pain

After excluding red flags, management is focused on reassurance, symptom control, and promoting recovery. This typically involves advice on staying active, simple analgesia (paracetamol, and a short course of oral or topical NSAIDs if appropriate), and guidance on posture and ergonomics. Referral to physiotherapy for a structured exercise programme is often beneficial for persistent symptoms.

Cervical Radiculopathy

Initial management is usually conservative. This involves providing effective analgesia, which may include neuropathic agents (e.g., amitriptyline, gabapentin, or pregabalin) in addition to simple painkillers. Physiotherapy can also be helpful. If pain is severe and intractable, or if there is a progressive or significant motor deficit, urgent referral for an MRI scan and a neurosurgical or spinal surgery opinion is warranted.

Cervical Cord Compression (Myelopathy)

This is a neurosurgical emergency. Any patient with suspected cervical myelopathy requires immediate hospital admission. An urgent MRI of the cervical spine is the investigation of choice to confirm the diagnosis and identify the cause. Definitive management is typically surgical decompression to relieve pressure on the spinal cord and prevent irreversible neurological damage.

Suspected Spinal Infection or Malignancy

Patients with red flags suggesting infection (fever, systemic upset, severe focal tenderness) or malignancy (history of cancer, night pain, weight loss) require urgent investigation. This involves immediate hospital assessment, blood tests including FBC, ESR, and CRP, and urgent imaging, usually with an MRI. Management will be directed by the underlying cause and will involve a multidisciplinary team (e.g., spinal surgeons, infectious disease specialists, oncologists).

Reflection & Learning

These brief cases illustrate the application of clinical reasoning to different neck pain presentations. They are for educational purposes only.

Case 1: Mechanical Neck Pain in Primary Care

A 28-year-old office worker presents to their GP with a 3-day history of aching pain across the back of their neck and shoulders. It started after a long week working on a deadline. The pain is worse on turning their head and is eased by ibuprofen. Examination reveals tenderness in the trapezius muscles but a full range of motion, normal neurological exam, and no red flags. The problem representation is a young patient with activity-related neck pain and a normal exam, consistent with mechanical neck pain. Management involves reassurance, advice on posture and stretching, and continued simple analgesia as needed.

Case 2: Cervical Radiculopathy

A 55-year-old woman presents with a 2-week history of neck pain associated with a sharp, shooting pain down her left arm to her thumb and index finger, along with pins and needles. On examination, there is weakness of the left biceps (power 4/5) and a diminished biceps reflex. The problem representation is a middle-aged woman with pain in a C6 dermatomal distribution and corresponding neurological signs, highly suggestive of C6 radiculopathy. She is started on neuropathic analgesia and referred for an urgent MRI and spinal clinic assessment due to the motor deficit.

Case 3: Red Flag Presentation in the Emergency Department

A 72-year-old man with a known history of prostate cancer is brought to the ED with a one-month history of severe, constant neck pain that is now preventing him from sleeping. He feels unwell and reports a poor appetite and has lost 4kg in weight. Examination reveals marked focal tenderness over the C4 vertebra. The problem representation is an older man with a history of malignancy presenting with constitutional symptoms and non-mechanical (nocturnal) neck pain with focal bony tenderness, a presentation highly suspicious for spinal metastases. He requires immediate admission for urgent MRI and liaison with the oncology and spinal teams.
