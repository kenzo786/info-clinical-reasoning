Headache Diagnostic Reasoning Reference
Context: Headache Assessment
Overview

Headache is one of the most frequent reasons for patients to seek medical attention, presenting across the full spectrum of clinical settings from general practice to the emergency department. The causes range from common, benign primary headache syndromes like tension-type headache and migraine, to rare but life-threatening secondary causes such as subarachnoid haemorrhage, meningitis, or giant cell arteritis. The fundamental clinical challenge is to reliably distinguish between these categories, providing reassurance and appropriate management for the former, while ensuring timely and life-saving intervention for the latter.

This educational module provides a structured framework for the clinical reasoning process involved in assessing a patient with a headache. It is designed to build a systematic approach to diagnosis. For definitive, real-time management protocols, users must refer to official UK guidelines from authoritative bodies such as NICE (e.g., CG150 for Headaches in over 12s) and the British Association for the Study of Headache (BASH).

Pathophysiology/Basic Science

Understanding the origin of head pain is fundamental to forming a differential diagnosis. Pain-sensitive structures in the head include the scalp, blood vessels (arteries and venous sinuses), meninges, and cranial nerves. The brain parenchyma itself is insensitive to pain. Different headache types are generated by distinct pathophysiological mechanisms:

  • Vascular Headache (e.g., Migraine): The leading theory involves the activation of the trigeminal vascular system. A triggering event leads to the release of vasoactive neuropeptides like Calcitonin Gene-Related Peptide (CGRP), causing painful inflammation and vasodilation of cranial blood vessels. This process stimulates trigeminal nerve fibres, resulting in the characteristic throbbing pain. Aura is thought to be caused by a wave of cortical spreading depression, a transient wave of neuronal and glial depolarisation.
  • Tension-Type Headache: The pathophysiology is less well understood but is thought to involve both peripheral and central mechanisms. Pericranial myofascial tenderness is a common finding, suggesting that peripheral activation of muscle nociceptors contributes. Central mechanisms likely involve sensitisation of trigeminal pathways in the brainstem and thalamus, leading to a lower pain threshold and the perception of non-painful stimuli as painful (allodynia).
  • Neuropathic Pain (e.g., Trigeminal Neuralgia): This is typically caused by compression of the trigeminal nerve root, often by an aberrant blood vessel. The resulting demyelination leads to ectopic nerve impulses and hyperexcitability, causing the classic brief, electric shock-like pains in the nerve's distribution.
  • Pain from Raised Intracranial Pressure (ICP): A space-occupying lesion (e.g., tumour, abscess, haematoma) or obstruction of cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) flow increases pressure within the rigid skull. This pressure causes traction and distortion of pain-sensitive structures like the dura mater, blood vessels, and cranial nerves, leading to a progressive, often dull headache that is characteristically worse with coughing, straining, or lying down (Valsalva manoeuvres).
  • Meningeal Irritation (e.g., Meningitis, Subarachnoid Haemorrhage): Inflammation or the presence of blood in the subarachnoid space irritates the pain-sensitive meninges and nerve roots. This triggers a generalised, severe headache, often accompanied by photophobia and neck stiffness (meningism) as a reflex protective mechanism.
Differential Diagnosis (DDx) Framework

A structured approach to the differential diagnosis of headache is essential to ensure life-threatening conditions are prioritised.

Must not miss!
  • Raised Intracranial Pressure: Presents with a persistent, worsening headache, often worse on waking or with coughing, and may be associated with visual changes or vomiting.
  • Subarachnoid haemorrhage: A sudden, severe 'thunderclap' headache, often described as the "worst headache of my life," with rapid onset to maximum intensity.
  • Subdural haematoma: Can present with a fluctuating level of consciousness and a progressive headache, often following a head injury which may have been minor, particularly in the elderly or those on anticoagulants.
  • Brain tumour: Typically causes a new-onset, progressive headache that worsens over time, is present on waking, and may be associated with focal neurological deficits or seizures.
  • Brain abscess: Presents as a triad of headache, fever, and focal neurological signs, often in a patient with a recent infection (e.g., sinusitis, otitis media) or in an immunocompromised state.
  • Meningitis: Characterised by the classic triad of headache, fever, and neck stiffness, often accompanied by photophobia and a non-blanching rash in meningococcal disease.
  • Encephalitis: Involves headache and fever but is distinguished by altered brain function, such as confusion, personality change, or seizures.
  • Malignant hypertension: A severe, throbbing headache associated with markedly elevated blood pressure, often accompanied by visual disturbances or signs of end-organ damage.
  • Pre-eclampsia: A new-onset headache after 20 weeks of pregnancy, associated with hypertension and proteinuria, and can be a sign of impending eclampsia.
  • Temporal arteritis (GCA): A new headache in a patient over 50, typically unilateral and temporal, often associated with scalp tenderness, jaw claudication, and visual symptoms.
  • Acute Glaucoma: A severe, painful red eye with a fixed, mid-dilated pupil, blurred vision or seeing haloes, and a headache often localised to the periorbital region.
Common causes
  • Viral infection: A generalised, often mild-to-moderate headache accompanying systemic viral symptoms like fever, malaise, and myalgia.
  • Tension type headache: Typically a bilateral, non-pulsating, band-like pressure of mild to moderate intensity, not aggravated by routine physical activity.
  • Migraine: A recurrent, unilateral, throbbing headache of moderate to severe intensity, often associated with nausea, photophobia, phonophobia, and sometimes preceded by a visual or sensory aura.
  • Sinusitis: A dull, aching pain or pressure over the frontal or maxillary sinuses, often worse on bending forward, and associated with nasal congestion or discharge.
  • Referred neck pain: Cervicogenic headache originating from the neck, often unilateral and triggered by neck movements or sustained postures, with pain radiating from the neck to the head.
  • Medication overuse headache: A chronic daily headache developing in a patient with a pre-existing primary headache disorder who is overusing acute headache medication.
  • Caffeine withdrawal: A dull, diffuse headache that typically starts 12-24 hours after cessation of caffeine intake in a regular consumer.
  • Trigeminal neuralgia: Brief, electric shock-like, unilateral facial pain in the distribution of the trigeminal nerve, triggered by light touch, chewing, or talking.
  • TMJ dysfunction: Pain in the jaw joint and surrounding muscles that can radiate to the temples, causing a headache, often associated with jaw clicking or locking.
  • Cluster headache: A severe, strictly unilateral, periorbital or temporal pain lasting 15-180 minutes, associated with autonomic features on the same side (e.g., eye-watering, nasal congestion).
  • Post concussion headache: A headache that develops within 7 days of a head injury, often with features of tension-type headache or migraine.
  • COVID-19: Can present with a new-onset headache, often described as a pressing or pulsing sensation, alongside other typical symptoms like fever, cough, and anosmia.
Easily missed
  • Drug withdrawal: Headache can be a symptom of withdrawal from various substances, including alcohol, opioids, or benzodiazepines, not just caffeine.
  • Depression: Can present with somatic symptoms, including a persistent, non-specific headache, often with features of tension-type headache.
  • Medically Unexplained Symptoms: A persistent headache for which no specific organic cause is found after thorough investigation, often associated with significant functional impairment.
  • Dental pain: Pain from dental abscesses or other dental pathology can be referred and present as a headache or facial pain.
  • HIV: Headache can be a presenting feature of primary HIV infection (seroconversion illness) or a symptom of opportunistic infections in advanced disease.
  • Idiopathic-intracranial-hypertension: A condition of raised intracranial pressure without a clear cause, typically affecting younger women who are overweight, presenting with a headache and visual disturbances like papilloedema.
Other Differential Diagnoses to consider
  • COVID-19: Can present with headache, often alongside fever, fatigue, and respiratory symptoms.
  • Carbon monoxide poisoning: May present with headache, confusion, dizziness, and nausea, often related to indoor heating sources.
  • Acute sinus vein thrombosis: Rare, but may present with severe headache, often with focal neurological signs.
  • Chronic daily headache: Persistent headaches, typically associated with tension-type headache or transformed migraine.
  • Arteriovenous malformation (AVM): Rare cause of headache that can present with intracranial haemorrhage or seizure.
  • Hypoglycaemia: Can result in headache, especially when associated with confusion, diaphoresis, or altered consciousness.
  • Phaeochromocytoma: Episodic headaches with hypertension, sweating, and palpitations.
History Taking
**Patient’s Description**: How can I help you today?
Starting with an open-ended question like "Can you tell me more about what brought you here today?" is essential to allow the patient to express their main concerns without direction. Relevance: This allows the patient to describe the headache in their own words, guiding further, more targeted questions. It gives insight into the patient’s perception of the issue and helps to uncover potentially important details that a closed question might miss.
**Location of the headache**: Where is the headache located?
  • Determining the location of the headache can help distinguish between different types of headache (e.g., unilateral vs. bilateral).
  • Relevance: A unilateral headache may suggest migraine or cluster headache, while a bilateral headache might indicate tension-type headache. A facial location could point towards sinusitis, while pain around the eyes may suggest acute glaucoma.
**Quality of the headache**: How would you describe the pain?
  • The quality of pain can provide diagnostic clues (e.g., throbbing, sharp, dull).
  • Relevance: Throbbing or pulsatile pain may suggest migraine, while sharp, electric shock-like pain is characteristic of trigeminal neuralgia. A dull, constant ache could indicate tension-type headache.
**Onset**: When did the headache start?
  • Understanding the onset helps to differentiate between acute and chronic causes of headache.
  • Relevance: Sudden onset ("thunderclap" headache) may indicate subarachnoid haemorrhage, while a more gradual onset might suggest tension-type headache or sinusitis. Chronic daily headaches are more consistent with medication overuse or idiopathic intracranial hypertension.
**Duration**: How long does the headache last?
  • Duration helps distinguish between episodic and chronic headache conditions.
  • Relevance: Migraines may last hours to days, cluster headaches are shorter but recur in bouts, while tension-type headaches can be more persistent. Chronic daily headaches could suggest medication overuse or idiopathic intracranial hypertension.
**Triggers and Exacerbating Factors**: What seems to bring on or worsen the headache?
  • Identifying triggers can be key in diagnosing certain types of headache.
  • Relevance: Stress or poor posture may worsen tension-type headaches, while certain foods, stress, or bright lights could trigger migraines. Cluster headaches may be worsened by alcohol.
**Relieving Factors**: Is there anything that helps alleviate the headache?
  • Understanding what relieves the headache can also aid diagnosis.
  • Relevance: Migraines may be relieved by lying in a dark, quiet room, while tension-type headaches might improve with rest or over-the-counter analgesics. Cluster headaches often improve with oxygen therapy.
**Associated Symptoms**: Do you have any other symptoms with the headache?
  • Associated symptoms can provide critical clues to the underlying diagnosis.
  • Relevance: Fever, neck stiffness, and photophobia may suggest meningitis. Nausea and vomiting could indicate a migraine or raised intracranial pressure. Visual changes may suggest acute glaucoma or temporal arteritis, and focal neurological symptoms might raise concerns about a brain tumour or stroke.
**Past Medical History**: Do you have any history of medical conditions?
  • Past medical conditions can provide context for the headache.
  • Relevance: A history of hypertension may suggest malignant hypertension, while previous head trauma could point to a subdural haematoma. Autoimmune diseases like giant cell arteritis should also be considered in older patients with relevant symptoms.
**Drug History**: Are you on any medications?
  • Drug history can help identify medication overuse or withdrawal-related headaches.
  • Relevance: Overuse of analgesics or caffeine withdrawal can contribute to chronic daily headaches. Additionally, patients on anticoagulants may be at higher risk for intracranial haemorrhage.
**Family History**: Does anyone in your family suffer from headaches?
  • Family history can point towards hereditary conditions such as migraines.
  • Relevance: A positive family history of migraines or other headache syndromes can increase the likelihood of a similar diagnosis in the patient.
Ideas, Concerns, and Expectations (ICE)
  • ICE questions ensure that the consultation addresses the patient’s perspective, concerns, and expectations about their symptoms.
  • Relevance: Asking the patient what they think might be causing the headache (Ideas) can provide insight into their understanding of the problem. Understanding if they are worried about serious conditions like brain tumours (Concerns) is important to address fears. Expectations help clarify what the patient hopes to achieve from the consultation, such as reassurance, pain relief, or further investigation.
Examination Strategy
**General Inspection**
  • Start by observing the patient’s general appearance for signs of distress, discomfort, or altered consciousness.
  • Relevance: A distressed or drowsy appearance may suggest meningitis, encephalitis, or raised intracranial pressure. A well-appearing patient may point towards more benign causes such as tension-type headache or migraine.
**Vital Signs**
  • Check temperature, blood pressure, heart rate, respiratory rate, and oxygen saturation.
  • Relevance: Fever suggests infection (e.g., meningitis, encephalitis), while hypertension may point towards malignant hypertension or pre-eclampsia. Tachycardia may suggest sepsis or raised intracranial pressure.
**Palpation of the scalp and temporal arteries**
  • Palpate the scalp for tenderness and the temporal arteries for thickening or tenderness.
  • Relevance: Tenderness over the scalp may indicate temporal arteritis (GCA), particularly in older patients. A negative finding may help rule out GCA but does not completely exclude it.
**Examination of the neck (Meningism)**
  • Check for neck stiffness, specifically testing for signs of meningism by assessing passive neck flexion.
  • Relevance: Neck stiffness, particularly if accompanied by fever and photophobia, suggests meningitis. A negative result makes meningitis less likely but does not exclude it, especially in elderly or immunocompromised patients.
**Fundoscopy**
  • Examine the eyes for papilloedema, which indicates raised intracranial pressure.
  • Relevance: Papilloedema strongly suggests raised intracranial pressure, which can be due to a mass lesion, haemorrhage, or idiopathic intracranial hypertension. A normal fundoscopic exam reduces the likelihood of these diagnoses.
**Cranial Nerve Examination**
  • Examine all 12 cranial nerves for any abnormalities, focusing on visual fields, eye movements, and facial asymmetry.
  • Relevance: Cranial nerve deficits, such as visual field loss, diplopia, or facial weakness, may suggest a space-occupying lesion, stroke, or intracranial infection. A normal cranial nerve exam would make these less likely.
**Visual Acuity and Visual Field Testing**
  • Assess visual acuity using a Snellen chart and check for visual field defects via confrontation testing.
  • Relevance: Reduced visual acuity or visual field defects could suggest raised intracranial pressure, optic neuritis, or temporal arteritis. A normal visual exam makes these diagnoses less likely.
**Pupil Examination**
  • Assess pupil size, symmetry, and reaction to light, looking for anisocoria or abnormal light reflexes.
  • Relevance: Anisocoria or a sluggish pupillary response may indicate raised intracranial pressure, acute glaucoma, or third nerve palsy. Normal findings would make these conditions less likely.
**Sinus Tenderness**
  • Palpate over the frontal and maxillary sinuses for tenderness.
  • Relevance: Sinus tenderness, particularly with nasal congestion or facial pain, suggests sinusitis. A lack of tenderness reduces the likelihood of sinusitis.
**Jaw Examination (TMJ)**
  • Examine the temporomandibular joint (TMJ) for tenderness, crepitus, or restricted movement.
  • Relevance: TMJ dysfunction can cause referred headache pain. Tenderness or restricted movement at the TMJ supports this diagnosis, while a normal exam suggests other causes.
**Cardiovascular and Respiratory Examination**
  • Perform a basic cardiovascular and respiratory exam, checking for hypertension, arrhythmias, and signs of infection.
  • Relevance: Hypertension, especially if severe, may cause a headache (e.g., malignant hypertension). Arrhythmias might raise concerns of embolic stroke, while signs of infection may point towards an infectious cause such as meningitis or encephalitis.
Red Flag Synthesis
**Thunderclap onset headache**
  • A sudden, severe headache described as the "worst ever" or a "thunderclap" onset, reaching maximum intensity within seconds to minutes.
  • Significance: This may indicate a subarachnoid haemorrhage, which is a medical emergency requiring immediate neuroimaging and evaluation.
**New-onset headache in patients over 50**
  • Headache starting in a patient over the age of 50, especially if associated with other systemic symptoms.
  • Significance: This may indicate temporal arteritis (GCA), which can lead to blindness if not treated urgently with corticosteroids. It also raises concern for malignancy or a space-occupying lesion.
**Headache with fever and neck stiffness**
  • The combination of headache, fever, and neck stiffness, potentially with photophobia, points to meningitis.
  • Significance: Meningitis is a life-threatening infection that requires urgent investigation and immediate antibiotic therapy.
**Headache with focal neurological signs**
  • Presence of focal neurological deficits such as weakness, sensory loss, visual field defects, or speech disturbances accompanying the headache.
  • Significance: This may indicate a space-occupying lesion (e.g., brain tumour or abscess), stroke, or intracranial haemorrhage, all of which require urgent neuroimaging and management.
**Headache with papilloedema**
  • Papilloedema (optic disc swelling) on fundoscopy is a sign of raised intracranial pressure.
  • Significance: Raised intracranial pressure can be caused by conditions such as a brain tumour, idiopathic intracranial hypertension, or intracranial haemorrhage, all of which require urgent investigation with neuroimaging.
**Headache triggered by physical activity or coughing**
  • Headache that is exacerbated by physical activity, coughing, sneezing, or bending over.
  • Significance: This could suggest raised intracranial pressure or a posterior fossa lesion, such as a brain tumour or Chiari malformation, and requires further investigation.
**Headache in an immunocompromised patient**
  • Any new-onset headache in a patient who is immunocompromised, such as those with HIV, chemotherapy, or organ transplant recipients.
  • Significance: This raises concern for opportunistic infections (e.g., toxoplasmosis, cryptococcal meningitis) or malignancy (e.g., CNS lymphoma), both of which require urgent evaluation and treatment.
**Headache with visual disturbances or jaw claudication**
  • Headache associated with transient visual loss, double vision, or pain when chewing (jaw claudication).
  • Significance: These are classic symptoms of temporal arteritis (GCA), which requires immediate treatment with corticosteroids to prevent permanent vision loss.
**Headache with progressively worsening severity**
  • A headache that is steadily worsening in intensity over time, particularly if it’s unresponsive to standard treatments.
  • Significance: This may suggest a space-occupying lesion such as a brain tumour, or idiopathic intracranial hypertension, and warrants urgent neuroimaging.
Investigations Strategy & Interpretation
**Full Blood Count (FBC)**
  • A full blood count can help detect infection, inflammation, or anaemia.
  • Result Significance: Elevated white cell count may suggest an infection such as meningitis or encephalitis. Anaemia may point towards temporal arteritis, while normal results would reduce the likelihood of systemic infection or inflammation.
**Erythrocyte Sedimentation Rate (ESR) and C-Reactive Protein (CRP)**
  • ESR and CRP are markers of inflammation and can be elevated in conditions such as temporal arteritis or infection.
  • Result Significance: A significantly raised ESR, particularly in patients over 50 with headache and visual symptoms, strongly suggests temporal arteritis. Raised CRP also supports this diagnosis, while normal levels reduce the likelihood of inflammatory conditions.
**Blood cultures**
  • Blood cultures are essential in patients with suspected meningitis or encephalitis to identify the causative pathogen.
  • Result Significance: Positive cultures indicate a bacterial infection, supporting diagnoses such as meningitis or sepsis. Negative results do not rule out these infections but may suggest viral or aseptic causes.
**Lumbar Puncture (LP)**
  • LP is crucial for diagnosing meningitis, encephalitis, and subarachnoid haemorrhage. It allows for the analysis of cerebrospinal fluid (CSF).
  • Result Significance: Elevated white blood cells in the CSF suggest infection (meningitis or encephalitis), while xanthochromia (yellowish CSF) indicates subarachnoid haemorrhage. A normal LP reduces the likelihood of these conditions.
**Non-contrast CT head scan**
  • A non-contrast CT scan is often the first-line imaging investigation for acute headache, particularly to rule out haemorrhage or mass effect.
  • Result Significance: Positive findings, such as intracranial haemorrhage or a mass lesion (e.g., tumour, abscess), require urgent management. A normal CT scan can help exclude haemorrhagic causes but does not rule out smaller bleeds or other pathologies like meningitis.
**Magnetic Resonance Imaging (MRI) of the brain**
  • MRI is more sensitive than CT for detecting soft tissue abnormalities, tumours, abscesses, or demyelinating lesions.
  • Result Significance: Positive results may show structural abnormalities such as tumours, abscesses, or signs of multiple sclerosis. A normal MRI reduces the likelihood of these conditions but may still warrant further investigation based on clinical suspicion.
**CT or MR Venography**
  • Venography is used to evaluate venous structures in the brain, particularly if cerebral venous sinus thrombosis (CVST) is suspected.
  • Result Significance: Positive findings of thrombosis confirm CVST, requiring anticoagulation. A normal venography reduces the likelihood of venous thrombosis but does not rule out other vascular abnormalities.
**Temporal Artery Biopsy**
  • A temporal artery biopsy is the gold standard for diagnosing temporal arteritis (GCA).
  • Result Significance: Positive biopsy results, showing granulomatous inflammation, confirm temporal arteritis. A negative result does not completely exclude GCA, as the disease can skip sections of the artery, but makes the diagnosis less likely.
**Electrolytes, urea, and creatinine**
  • Checking electrolyte balance can help identify metabolic causes of headache, such as hyponatraemia or uraemia.
  • Result Significance: Electrolyte imbalances may indicate an underlying cause, such as hyponatraemia, dehydration, or renal impairment. Normal results reduce the likelihood of metabolic causes but do not rule out neurological conditions.
**Autoimmune screen**
  • An autoimmune screen can help identify conditions such as lupus or vasculitis, which can cause secondary headache.
  • Result Significance: Positive autoantibodies suggest autoimmune involvement, such as systemic lupus erythematosus (SLE) or vasculitis. A negative screen makes these diagnoses less likely but does not completely exclude them.
Evidence-Based Diagnosis (EBDx)
**Thunderclap headache and Subarachnoid Haemorrhage (SAH)**
  • Thunderclap headache, characterised by a sudden onset, is a key symptom for subarachnoid haemorrhage.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 97%, Specificity: 38%, Positive Likelihood Ratio (LR+): 1.6, Negative Likelihood Ratio (LR-): 0.08.
  • Reference: Perry JJ et al., "Clinical decision rules to rule out subarachnoid haemorrhage for acute headache," JAMA, 2013.
  • Significance: A positive finding of a thunderclap headache increases the likelihood of SAH, but a negative finding (absence of sudden onset) significantly reduces the probability of SAH.
**Fever and Meningitis**
  • Fever, combined with headache, raises suspicion for meningitis, particularly if associated with neck stiffness or photophobia.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 53%, Specificity: 83%, LR+: 3.1, LR-: 0.57.
  • Reference: Attia J et al., "Does this adult patient have acute meningitis?", JAMA, 1999.
  • Significance: The presence of fever increases the likelihood of meningitis, though it is neither highly sensitive nor specific alone. The absence of fever moderately decreases the likelihood but does not rule out meningitis.
**Neck Stiffness and Meningitis**
  • Neck stiffness is a classic feature of meningitis and is often assessed with passive neck flexion.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 70%, Specificity: 68%, LR+: 2.2, LR-: 0.44.
  • Reference: Thomas KE et al., "The diagnostic accuracy of physical signs for meningitis in adults," JAMA, 2002.
  • Significance: Neck stiffness moderately increases the probability of meningitis, but its absence does not rule it out, especially in elderly or immunocompromised patients.
**Papilloedema and Raised Intracranial Pressure (ICP)**
  • Papilloedema on fundoscopy is a key sign of raised intracranial pressure, which may occur with conditions like a brain tumour or intracranial haemorrhage.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 75%, Specificity: 90%, LR+: 7.5, LR-: 0.28.
  • Reference: Trobe JD et al., "Papilledema: prognosis of visual acuity in patients with intracranial hypertension," JAMA, 1980.
  • Significance: The presence of papilloedema greatly increases the likelihood of raised ICP, while its absence makes it less likely but does not fully exclude it.
**CT Head for Subarachnoid Haemorrhage (SAH)**
  • A non-contrast CT head scan is the first-line imaging modality for suspected subarachnoid haemorrhage.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 92% (within the first 24 hours), Specificity: 100%, LR+: >10, LR-: 0.08.
  • Reference: Edlow JA et al., "Diagnosis of subarachnoid hemorrhage in the emergency department," Ann Emerg Med, 2008.
  • Significance: A positive CT scan confirms SAH with high specificity, while a negative scan within 24 hours significantly reduces the likelihood but may still necessitate further investigation such as a lumbar puncture.
**Lumbar Puncture for Subarachnoid Haemorrhage (SAH)**
  • A lumbar puncture is performed to detect xanthochromia in cerebrospinal fluid, which confirms subarachnoid haemorrhage.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 95%, Specificity: 98%, LR+: 47.5, LR-: 0.05.
  • Reference: Vermeulen MJ et al., "Lumbar puncture in subarachnoid hemorrhage: a diagnostic study," BMJ, 2007.
  • Significance: A positive lumbar puncture showing xanthochromia confirms SAH, particularly if CT is negative. A negative LP significantly reduces the probability of SAH.
**Elevated ESR and Temporal Arteritis (Giant Cell Arteritis - GCA)**
  • Elevated ESR is a key marker for temporal arteritis, especially in patients over 50 with headache, visual symptoms, or jaw claudication.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 92%, Specificity: 86%, LR+: 6.6, LR-: 0.09.
  • Reference: Smetana GW et al., "Does this patient have temporal arteritis?", JAMA, 2002.
  • Significance: A raised ESR strongly suggests temporal arteritis, while a normal ESR significantly lowers the likelihood but does not entirely rule it out.
Diagnostic Timeout & Cognitive Biases

Awareness of common thinking errors is essential to improve diagnostic accuracy and patient safety when assessing a patient with a headache. Before finalising a diagnosis, it is good practice for clinicians at all levels to take a 'diagnostic timeout' to challenge their own reasoning.

  • Anchoring Bias: The tendency to latch onto a patient's pre-existing diagnosis, such as migraine, and attribute any new headache to it without full consideration of new features. For example, a patient with known migraines who presents with a headache that is now associated with fever and neck stiffness might have meningitis, not just a severe migraine. A key question to ask is: "Could this be something else?"
  • Confirmation Bias: Actively seeking out or overemphasising features that support an initial hypothesis while downplaying contradictory evidence. For instance, focusing on a patient's stress levels to confirm a diagnosis of tension-type headache while ignoring a new subtle weakness in one arm that could point to a space-occupying lesion.
  • Atypical Presentations: A significant pitfall is failing to recognise that serious diseases can present without their classic textbook features. Subarachnoid haemorrhage might not always be a 'thunderclap' headache, and meningitis can present without significant neck stiffness, particularly in the elderly or immunocompromised. A high index of suspicion is crucial.
  • Over-reliance on Normal Tests: Placing undue confidence in a single normal test result. A normal non-contrast CT head scan does not definitively rule out a subarachnoid haemorrhage (especially after 24 hours), nor does it exclude conditions like cerebral venous sinus thrombosis or meningitis. Similarly, a normal ESR does not completely rule out Giant Cell Arteritis. Clinical judgement must prevail over isolated negative results.
Illustrative Management Pathways

For educational purposes, this section describes the typical principles of management for key diagnoses. These are not prescriptive instructions. All management must be guided by official UK guidelines, local formularies, and senior clinical advice.

Subarachnoid Haemorrhage (SAH)

Suspected SAH is a time-critical medical emergency requiring immediate hospital admission. Management is focused on confirming the diagnosis (usually with a non-contrast CT head, followed by lumbar puncture if the CT is negative and suspicion remains high) and preventing re-bleeding. This involves urgent referral to a regional neurosurgical centre, strict blood pressure control, and administration of nimodipine to prevent cerebral vasospasm.

Giant Cell Arteritis (GCA)

If there is a high clinical suspicion of GCA, particularly if there are visual symptoms, treatment should be initiated immediately without waiting for investigations to prevent irreversible vision loss. This involves starting high-dose oral corticosteroids (e.g., prednisolone 40-60mg daily). The patient requires urgent referral to a rheumatologist for confirmation of the diagnosis (usually via temporal artery biopsy or ultrasound) and long-term management.

Migraine

Management involves a stepped-care approach. Acute treatment focuses on simple analgesia (e.g., paracetamol, NSAIDs), often with an anti-emetic. If these are ineffective, specific migraine treatments like triptans (e.g., sumatriptan) are used. For frequent or debilitating attacks, prophylactic (preventative) treatment with medications such as propranolol, amitriptyline, or topiramate should be considered, alongside lifestyle advice on trigger avoidance.

Tension-Type Headache (TTH)

After confidently excluding secondary causes, management focuses on reassurance and symptomatic relief. Acute episodes are managed with simple analgesia like paracetamol or ibuprofen, but patients should be warned about the risk of medication-overuse headache. For chronic TTH, a course of low-dose amitriptyline can be effective as a prophylactic measure. Lifestyle factors such as stress management, sleep hygiene, and physiotherapy for any associated neck issues are also important.

Reflection & Learning

These brief cases illustrate the application of clinical reasoning to different headache presentations. They are for educational purposes only.

Case 1: Suspected Migraine in General Practice

A 28-year-old female presents to her GP with recurrent episodes of severe, throbbing left-sided headaches lasting about a day. She feels nauseated during the attacks and prefers to lie down in a dark, quiet room. Occasionally, she sees flashing zig-zag lines for about 20 minutes before the headache starts. Her examination is normal. The problem representation is a young woman with episodic, unilateral, throbbing headaches with associated features and a typical aura, consistent with a diagnosis of migraine with aura. Management would involve discussing acute treatments (analgesia, triptans) and lifestyle trigger factors.

Case 2: Suspected Giant Cell Arteritis in the Emergency Department

A 75-year-old woman presents to the Emergency Department with a new-onset headache for the past two weeks, localised to her right temple. The area is tender to touch, and she reports pain in her jaw when she chews. This morning, she experienced a brief 'curtain' coming down over the vision in her right eye. The problem representation is an elderly patient with a new headache, classic features of jaw claudication and scalp tenderness, and a transient monocular visual disturbance. This is highly suspicious for Giant Cell Arteritis. The immediate management pathway involves starting high-dose steroids and arranging urgent ophthalmology and rheumatology review.

Case 3: Tension-Type Headache in a Student Health Clinic

A 20-year-old university student presents during his exam period with a 3-week history of a near-constant, dull headache. He describes it as a 'tight band' around his entire head. It is not associated with nausea or photophobia and does not stop him from revising, although it is bothersome. His vital signs and neurological examination are normal. The problem representation is a young man in a period of high stress with a featureless, bilateral, non-disabling headache, most consistent with a tension-type headache. After excluding red flags, management would involve reassurance, advice on simple analgesia (with caution about overuse), and discussion of stress management techniques.
