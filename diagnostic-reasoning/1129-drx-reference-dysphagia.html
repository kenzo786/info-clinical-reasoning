Dysphagia Diagnostic Reasoning Reference
Context: Dysphagia Assessment
Overview & Links to Key Guidelines

Dysphagia, the subjective sensation of difficulty in swallowing, is a common and serious clinical symptom that requires careful and systematic evaluation. It can result from a wide range of conditions, from benign and self-limiting issues to severe, life-threatening diseases such as oesophageal cancer or neurological emergencies. The clinical challenge is to differentiate between structural (obstructive) and functional (motility) disorders, and to distinguish between oropharyngeal and oesophageal causes.

An initial assessment must focus on identifying red flag symptoms and signs, such as progressive dysphagia, unintentional weight loss, odynophagia, or neurological deficits, which may indicate malignancy or a serious underlying systemic disease. A misdiagnosis or delayed diagnosis can have profound consequences for patient outcomes, including malnutrition, dehydration, aspiration pneumonia, and mortality from untreated cancer.

This educational module provides a foundational framework for the clinical reasoning process in patients presenting with dysphagia. For definitive, real-time management protocols, clinicians must consult official UK guidelines from authoritative bodies such as NICE (e.g., NG12 for Suspected Cancer, CG189 for Head and Neck Cancer) and relevant professional organisations like the British Society of Gastroenterology (BSG).

Pathophysiology/Basic Science

Understanding the pathophysiology of dysphagia is essential for localising the problem and forming a differential diagnosis. The swallowing process is a complex sequence involving voluntary and involuntary actions coordinated by multiple nerves and muscles. Dysphagia can be broadly classified based on the anatomical location of the disruption (oropharyngeal or oesophageal) and the underlying mechanism (mechanical obstruction or motility disorder).

  • Oropharyngeal (Transfer) Dysphagia: This arises from problems in the oral cavity, pharynx, or upper oesophageal sphincter. The patient has difficulty initiating a swallow, often pointing to the neck as the site of the problem. It is frequently caused by neuromuscular diseases that disrupt the coordination of the tongue, pharyngeal muscles, and larynx. This can lead to misdirection of the food bolus into the airway, resulting in coughing, choking, or aspiration. Common causes include stroke, Parkinson's disease, motor neurone disease, and myasthenia gravis.
  • Oesophageal Dysphagia: This is felt after the swallow has been initiated, typically described as a sensation of food getting 'stuck' or 'held up' in the chest or lower neck. It can be further subdivided:
    • Mechanical Obstruction: This occurs when there is a physical narrowing of the oesophageal lumen. Patients typically report dysphagia primarily for solids, which progresses over time. If the obstruction becomes severe, liquids may also become difficult to swallow. Causes include oesophageal strictures (benign or malignant), oesophageal cancer, webs, rings, and external compression from masses like a goitre or mediastinal lymphoma.
    • Motility (Motor) Disorder: This results from disordered oesophageal peristalsis or abnormal function of the lower oesophageal sphincter (LES). It typically causes dysphagia for both solids and liquids from the outset. The symptoms can be intermittent and may be associated with chest pain. Examples include achalasia (failure of LES relaxation and absent peristalsis), diffuse oesophageal spasm, and ineffective oesophageal motility seen in conditions like scleroderma.
Differential Diagnosis (DDx) Framework

A structured approach to the differential diagnosis of dysphagia is essential to ensure that life-threatening conditions are prioritised. The following framework categorises potential causes based on clinical urgency and frequency.

Must not miss!
  • Epiglottitis: A rapidly progressing infection of the epiglottis causing severe throat pain, muffled voice, drooling, and potential airway obstruction.
  • Retropharyngeal abscess: A deep neck space infection presenting with fever, neck pain, and a bulge in the posterior pharyngeal wall, which can compromise the airway.
  • Oropharyngeal cancer: Malignancy of the tonsil, base of tongue, or pharynx, often presenting with persistent sore throat, a neck lump, or referred ear pain.
  • Oesophageal cancer: Typically presents with progressive dysphagia to solids, weight loss, and anorexia in older patients.
  • Gastric cancer: Cancer of the stomach, especially at the cardia, can obstruct the gastro-oesophageal junction, causing progressive dysphagia.
  • Mediastinal Lymphoma: Enlarged lymph nodes in the mediastinum can externally compress the oesophagus, leading to dysphagia and sometimes respiratory symptoms.
  • Cerebrovascular disease: A stroke affecting the brainstem or cranial nerves can cause sudden-onset oropharyngeal dysphagia with associated neurological deficits.
Common causes
  • Pharyngitis: Inflammation of the pharynx, usually viral, causing a sore throat and pain on swallowing (odynophagia) rather than true dysphagia.
  • Tonsillitis: Infection of the tonsils leading to swelling, throat pain, and odynophagia.
  • Pharyngeal pouch: (Zenker's diverticulum) An outpouching of the pharyngeal mucosa where food can collect, causing regurgitation of undigested food and gurgling.
  • Globus Hystericus: A persistent sensation of a lump in the throat, which is typically not related to swallowing and may even be relieved by eating.
  • GORD: Gastro-oesophageal reflux disease can cause dysphagia through inflammation (oesophagitis) or the development of a benign stricture.
  • Oesophageal spasm: Uncoordinated contractions of the oesophagus causing intermittent dysphagia and central chest pain, often triggered by certain foods.
  • Achalasia: A motor disorder characterised by failure of the lower oesophageal sphincter to relax, causing progressive dysphagia to both liquids and solids.
  • Oesophageal strictures and webs: Narrowing of the oesophagus due to scar tissue (e.g., from long-standing reflux) or thin mucosal folds, typically causing solid food dysphagia.
  • COVID-19: Can be associated with dysphagia, either due to severe pharyngitis or as a neurological complication.
Easily missed
  • Foreign body: An impacted food bolus or foreign object can cause acute-onset dysphagia and must be considered, especially in children or adults with underlying oesophageal issues.
  • Parkinson's disease: A progressive neurodegenerative disorder where oropharyngeal dysphagia is a common motor symptom, leading to drooling and aspiration risk.
  • Multiple sclerosis: Demyelination affecting brainstem pathways can lead to dysphagia as part of a wider pattern of neurological dysfunction.
  • Motor Neurone Disease: Progressive degeneration of motor neurons leading to muscle weakness, with bulbar involvement causing severe oropharyngeal dysphagia and dysarthria.
  • Myasthenia gravis: An autoimmune condition causing fatigable muscle weakness, which can affect the pharyngeal and oesophageal muscles, worsening with repeated swallowing.
  • Scleroderma: A systemic autoimmune disease that can cause fibrosis and atrophy of oesophageal smooth muscle, leading to severe dysmotility and reflux.
  • Sjogren's syndrome: An autoimmune condition causing severe dryness of the mouth (xerostomia), which can make bolus formation and swallowing difficult.
Other Differential Diagnoses to consider
  • Zenker’s diverticulum: Outpouching of the pharyngeal mucosa, often leading to regurgitation and dysphagia.
  • Esophageal candidiasis: Common in immunocompromised patients; presents with odynophagia.
  • Diffuse oesophageal spasm: Can cause intermittent dysphagia with chest pain, often triggered by cold or hot food.
  • Thyroid enlargement (goitre): Can compress the oesophagus and trachea, causing a sensation of obstruction.
  • Systemic lupus erythematosus (SLE): Autoimmune disease that can cause oesophageal motility dysfunction.
  • Radiation-induced oesophagitis: Occurs post-radiotherapy to head, neck, or chest; presents with dysphagia and odynophagia.
  • HIV/AIDS-related dysphagia: Dysphagia in HIV may be due to opportunistic infections like candidiasis or CMV oesophagitis.
  • Botulism: Neurotoxin can lead to progressive dysphagia and neuromuscular weakness.
  • Chagas disease: Parasitic infection causing oesophageal achalasia, primarily seen in endemic regions.
History Taking
**Patient’s Description**: How can I help you today?
Starting with an open-ended question allows the patient to describe the problem in their own words, which can reveal key characteristics such as sensation (e.g., "food getting stuck") or pain during swallowing, and help guide further targeted questions. Relevance: This initial response can help determine if the dysphagia is oropharyngeal or oesophageal in origin and if the primary issue is a structural blockage or a functional motility problem.
Can you describe when and how the swallowing problem started?
  • Understanding onset (acute or gradual) and duration helps in distinguishing sudden causes (e.g., CVA, epiglottitis) from more insidious, progressive conditions like oesophageal cancer or achalasia.
  • Relevance: Acute onset may suggest foreign body, stroke, or infection, whereas gradual onset with progression could point to malignancies or motility disorders.
Is swallowing solids, liquids, or both problematic?
  • Dysphagia to solids suggests an obstruction (e.g., stricture, tumour), while difficulty swallowing both solids and liquids from the start suggests a motility disorder (e.g., achalasia).
  • Relevance: Positive for solids alone suggests structural causes; difficulty with both points towards motility issues like achalasia.
Have you experienced any associated pain or discomfort while swallowing (odynophagia)?
  • Odynophagia often indicates inflammation or infection (e.g., oesophagitis, pharyngitis), or malignancy.
  • Relevance: Positive response suggests inflammatory causes like oesophagitis or malignancy, whereas a negative answer leans towards structural or functional dysphagia.
Have you had any associated symptoms like weight loss, night sweats, or fever?
  • Weight loss, night sweats, or fever can be red flags for malignancy, chronic infection, or systemic diseases (e.g., lymphoma, tuberculosis).
  • Relevance: Positive response heightens suspicion of cancer, systemic infection, or chronic inflammatory conditions.
Have you noticed any changes in your voice, like hoarseness?
  • Hoarseness may indicate involvement of the recurrent laryngeal nerve, often associated with thyroid pathology, lung malignancies, or a mediastinal mass (e.g., mediastinal lymphoma).
  • Relevance: Positive for hoarseness suggests neurological involvement or local tumour invasion; negative response lowers suspicion of these conditions.
Have you had any recent respiratory symptoms, such as cough or shortness of breath?
  • Respiratory symptoms may indicate an underlying respiratory infection, mediastinal mass, or, in some cases, dysphagia-related aspiration.
  • Relevance: Positive for respiratory symptoms raises suspicion of mediastinal pathology or an infectious cause like epiglottitis, while negative decreases these concerns.
Do you have any history of autoimmune disease, such as scleroderma or Sjögren’s syndrome?
  • Autoimmune conditions, particularly scleroderma and Sjögren’s syndrome, can cause oesophageal dysmotility or dryness, leading to dysphagia.
  • Relevance: Positive for autoimmune history suggests potential motility disorder; negative response reduces likelihood of autoimmune dysphagia.
**Ideas, Concerns, and Expectations (ICE)**
  • Exploring the patient’s perspective is key to addressing any anxieties, ensuring an understanding of their beliefs about the cause (e.g., worry about cancer) and expectations for treatment or investigation.
  • Relevance: Addresses the patient’s main concerns (e.g., fear of malignancy, hope for diagnostic testing) and informs shared decision-making.
Examination Strategy
Inspection of the Throat and Oral Cavity
  • Inspecting the oral cavity, pharynx, and tonsils can reveal signs of infection (e.g., tonsillitis, pharyngitis), obstruction, or lesions. Swelling, redness, or asymmetry may indicate infection or mass lesions.
  • Relevance: Positive findings such as erythema, swelling, or asymmetry suggest infection (tonsillitis, epiglottitis) or malignancy, while negative findings help rule out localised oropharyngeal causes of dysphagia.
Neck Palpation
  • Palpate for any neck masses, lymphadenopathy, or thyroid enlargement, which could suggest malignancy, infection, or a goitre. Assessing the mobility and consistency of any masses found can help differentiate between benign and malignant causes.
  • Relevance: Positive for masses or lymphadenopathy suggests malignancy (e.g., oropharyngeal cancer) or infection, while a negative finding lowers suspicion of these conditions.
Assessment of Cranial Nerves
  • Evaluating cranial nerves (particularly IX, X, XI, and XII) is critical in identifying neuromuscular causes of dysphagia. Weakness or asymmetry in tongue movement, palate elevation, or gag reflex may indicate neurological pathology.
  • Relevance: Positive findings for cranial nerve dysfunction point towards neurological causes such as stroke, multiple sclerosis, or motor neurone disease, while intact function helps exclude these.
Chest Examination
  • Listen for wheezing, stridor, or signs of infection. A chest exam can reveal potential aspiration pneumonia in cases of chronic aspiration, which may occur in patients with severe dysphagia.
  • Relevance: Positive for crackles or wheezing may suggest aspiration pneumonia or tracheal compression by mediastinal masses, while a clear chest lowers suspicion of these complications.
Abdominal Examination
  • Assess for any abdominal masses, particularly in the epigastric region, which may indicate malignancies of the stomach or oesophagus that can present with dysphagia.
  • Relevance: Positive for palpable masses could indicate malignancy or other gastrointestinal pathology, while a negative exam makes intra-abdominal causes less likely.
Assessment of Skin and Mucosal Membranes
  • Look for signs of systemic disease, such as dry mucous membranes in Sjögren’s syndrome or scleroderma, which could cause oesophageal dysmotility.
  • Relevance: Positive for dry mucous membranes or skin changes suggests autoimmune causes, such as Sjögren’s syndrome or scleroderma, while negative findings lower the likelihood of these systemic diseases.
Observation for Myasthenic Symptoms
  • Look for signs of muscle fatigue, ptosis, or other muscle weakness that could suggest myasthenia gravis, a condition that can affect swallowing muscles and lead to dysphagia.
  • Relevance: Positive for ptosis or muscle weakness raises suspicion for myasthenia gravis or other neuromuscular conditions, while a negative exam may rule out neuromuscular dysphagia.
Red Flag Synthesis
Severe difficulty swallowing or breathing
  • Sudden severe dysphagia or difficulty breathing may indicate acute airway compromise, possibly due to epiglottitis, anaphylaxis, or a large obstructing foreign body.
  • Significance: This could indicate an airway emergency requiring immediate intervention to secure the airway.
Progressive dysphagia, especially starting with solids and advancing to liquids
  • Dysphagia that begins with difficulty swallowing solids and progresses to liquids over time may suggest an obstructive process, such as oesophageal or gastric cancer.
  • Significance: This pattern raises suspicion for malignancy, particularly oesophageal cancer, and necessitates urgent endoscopic evaluation.
Unintentional weight loss and anorexia
  • Significant, unintentional weight loss or a lack of appetite often signals a serious underlying condition such as malignancy.
  • Significance: Weight loss in the setting of dysphagia could indicate oesophageal, gastric, or oropharyngeal cancer and warrants prompt investigation.
Odynophagia (painful swallowing)
  • Painful swallowing can suggest inflammation or infection (e.g., oesophagitis, candida infection) or even malignancy. In immunocompromised patients, it may indicate serious infections like CMV or HSV oesophagitis.
  • Significance: Odynophagia, particularly in high-risk patients, requires thorough investigation to rule out infection or malignancy.
Persistent hoarseness or voice changes
  • Persistent hoarseness may result from recurrent laryngeal nerve involvement, commonly seen in malignancies affecting the upper aerodigestive tract or mediastinum.
  • Significance: Persistent voice changes raise suspicion for malignancy, such as lung or thyroid cancer, requiring imaging and ENT referral.
Coughing or choking during meals
  • Frequent coughing or choking while eating may suggest aspiration, often due to neuromuscular disorders affecting swallowing coordination, such as stroke or motor neurone disease.
  • Significance: This sign is concerning for aspiration risk and associated complications like aspiration pneumonia, especially in patients with neuromuscular disease.
Neurological symptoms (e.g., weakness, numbness)
  • Neurological signs associated with dysphagia may indicate a central nervous system pathology such as stroke, multiple sclerosis, or motor neurone disease.
  • Significance: Presence of neurological symptoms alongside dysphagia requires urgent assessment for possible neurological or cerebrovascular pathology.
Investigations Strategy & Interpretation
Throat Swab for Culture and Sensitivity
  • Throat swabs help identify bacterial or fungal pathogens, particularly useful in patients with symptoms of infection such as sore throat, odynophagia, or visible oral lesions.
  • Result Significance: Positive results may indicate bacterial or fungal pharyngitis, while negative results reduce the likelihood of infectious causes.
Full Blood Count (FBC)
  • An FBC can help detect anaemia (often linked to malignancy or chronic disease) and raised white cell count or inflammatory markers, which may indicate infection or malignancy.
  • Result Significance: Anaemia raises suspicion for malignancy, while leukocytosis may suggest infection. A normal FBC does not exclude structural or functional causes of dysphagia.
Chest X-ray (CXR)
  • A CXR can reveal mediastinal masses, pulmonary aspiration signs, or lung pathology that may contribute to dysphagia (e.g., mediastinal lymphoma or lung cancer compressing the oesophagus).
  • Result Significance: Positive findings of a mass or aspiration indicate malignancy or neuromuscular dysfunction, while a normal CXR helps exclude thoracic structural pathology.
Barium Swallow
  • This imaging technique can reveal structural abnormalities like strictures, webs, diverticula (e.g., Zenker’s diverticulum), or motility disorders such as achalasia.
  • Result Significance: Positive findings for obstruction or dysmotility help narrow the diagnosis to structural or functional causes, while negative results may suggest a non-structural aetiology.
Oesophagogastroduodenoscopy (OGD)
  • OGD is a key diagnostic tool for visualising the oesophagus, stomach, and duodenum, allowing direct visualisation of tumours, strictures, inflammation, or other structural abnormalities.
  • Result Significance: Positive findings of masses, strictures, or oesophagitis guide further management, while negative findings can be reassuring and help rule out certain structural abnormalities.
Oesophageal Manometry
  • Oesophageal manometry measures pressure within the oesophagus and is essential for diagnosing motility disorders, such as achalasia and diffuse oesophageal spasm.
  • Result Significance: Positive for abnormal motility (e.g., high pressure in achalasia) confirms functional disorders; negative findings reduce suspicion of motility disorders.
CT Neck and Chest
  • CT imaging provides detailed views of the neck and chest structures and is useful in identifying masses, lymphadenopathy, or other pathologies that may compress or invade the oesophagus.
  • Result Significance: Positive for masses or lymphadenopathy supports malignancy or significant structural pathology, while negative results decrease the likelihood of such issues.
Autoimmune Antibody Screen (e.g., ANA, anti-Ro, anti-La)
  • An autoimmune screen can help identify conditions like scleroderma or Sjögren’s syndrome, which are associated with oesophageal dysmotility and dryness, respectively.
  • Result Significance: Positive findings support an autoimmune cause for dysphagia, while negative results make autoimmune causes less likely.
Evidence-Based Diagnosis (EBDx)
Progressive Dysphagia to Solids and Liquids
  • Progressive dysphagia starting with solids and advancing to liquids is highly indicative of mechanical obstruction (e.g., oesophageal cancer) or motility disorders (e.g., achalasia).
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 91% for oesophageal cancer in patients over 50 with progressive dysphagia; Specificity: 89% for distinguishing between malignant and benign causes of obstruction.
  • Reference: Talley NJ et al., "Oesophageal Cancer Diagnosis and Management," BMJ, 2013.
  • Significance: Positive findings strongly suggest malignancy, while absence of progression lessens likelihood of serious obstruction.
Weight Loss in Patients with Dysphagia
  • Unintentional weight loss with dysphagia is a significant red flag for malignancy, particularly in older adults or those with chronic symptoms.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 87%, Specificity: 92% for upper GI malignancies in dysphagia with weight loss.
  • Reference: Katzka DA et al., "Gastroesophageal Reflux and the Risk of Cancer," Lancet Gastroenterology, 2017.
  • Significance: Positive for weight loss increases suspicion of cancer, necessitating urgent endoscopic evaluation.
Positive OGD Findings (e.g., Stricture or Mass)
  • Endoscopic visualisation via OGD is the gold standard for assessing oesophageal or gastric pathology, allowing direct visual and biopsy for definitive diagnosis.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 98% and Specificity: 95% for detecting oesophageal cancer with visualised mass/stricture.
  • Reference: Cancer Research UK, "Oesophageal Cancer Screening and Diagnosis," 2019.
  • Significance: Positive findings strongly confirm structural or malignant aetiologies, while negative findings reduce suspicion of upper GI pathology.
Abnormal Manometry Results (e.g., High LES Pressure in Achalasia)
  • High lower oesophageal sphincter (LES) pressure is diagnostic for achalasia and other motility disorders, differentiating functional dysphagia from structural causes.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 93%, Specificity: 98% for diagnosing achalasia based on LES pressure on manometry.
  • Reference: Pandolfino JE et al., "Achalasia and Oesophageal Manometry," Gastroenterology Clinics of North America, 2015.
  • Significance: Positive high LES pressure on manometry confirms achalasia, while normal pressure suggests functional or non-motility-related dysphagia.
CXR Showing Mediastinal Mass or Aspiration
  • A mediastinal mass on CXR may compress the oesophagus, causing dysphagia, while signs of aspiration may indicate neuromuscular causes leading to swallowing dysfunction.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 75%, Specificity: 90% for identifying mediastinal masses causing dysphagia symptoms.
  • Reference: Allen MS et al., "Mediastinal Masses and Dysphagia," American Journal of Medicine, 2016.
  • Significance: Positive for mass or aspiration in dysphagia supports structural or neuromuscular causes; absence lowers suspicion of these issues.
Autoimmune Antibodies in Suspected Sjögren’s or Scleroderma
  • Autoimmune antibodies (e.g., anti-Ro, anti-La) can indicate Sjögren’s or scleroderma, which may present with oesophageal motility issues and dysphagia.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 80%, Specificity: 95% for diagnosing Sjögren’s syndrome in patients with dysphagia and positive anti-Ro/anti-La antibodies.
  • Reference: Mariette X et al., "Diagnosis and Management of Sjögren's Syndrome," BMJ, 2013.
  • Significance: Positive antibodies increase suspicion for autoimmune causes, while negative findings lower the likelihood of Sjögren’s or scleroderma.
Diagnostic Timeout & Cognitive Biases

Awareness of common thinking errors is essential to improve diagnostic accuracy and patient safety when assessing a patient with dysphagia. Before finalising a diagnosis, it is good practice for clinicians at all levels to take a 'diagnostic timeout' to challenge their own reasoning.

  • Anchoring Bias: This is the tendency to latch onto an initial piece of information, such as a patient's known history of GORD, and attribute the dysphagia to a benign stricture without fully considering more sinister causes. This can lead to a failure to investigate red flags like weight loss or progressive symptoms. A key question to ask is: "Could this be cancer, and have I done enough to rule it out?"
  • Premature Closure: This bias involves accepting a diagnosis before it has been fully verified. For example, finding mild oesophagitis on an OGD might lead a clinician to stop searching for a cause, potentially missing a subtle underlying motility disorder like achalasia that requires manometry for diagnosis.
  • Atypical Presentations: A major pitfall is failing to recognise that serious diseases can present non-classically. Oropharyngeal dysphagia from a stroke may be subtle in a patient with pre-existing dementia. So-called 'globus' (a feeling of a lump in the throat) is usually benign, but can occasionally be the presenting symptom of a laryngeal or pharyngeal cancer, especially if other red flags like hoarseness or a neck lump are present.
  • Confirmation Bias: This is the tendency to focus on evidence that supports a preferred diagnosis while ignoring contradictory information. For instance, a clinician might focus on a patient's anxiety to support a diagnosis of globus hystericus, while downplaying the significance of a new, slight difficulty swallowing solid foods.
Illustrative Management Pathways

For educational purposes, this section describes the typical principles of management for key diagnoses. These are not prescriptive instructions. All management must be guided by official UK guidelines, local formularies, and senior clinical advice.

Suspected Oesophageal or Gastric Cancer

Patients presenting with dysphagia or those aged 55 and over with weight loss and upper abdominal pain, reflux, or dyspepsia should be referred urgently under the '2-week wait' rule for endoscopy (OGD), according to NICE guideline NG12. If cancer is confirmed, management will be determined by a specialist multidisciplinary team (MDT) and may involve surgery, chemotherapy, radiotherapy, or palliative stenting to relieve the obstruction.

Neurological Dysphagia (e.g., Post-Stroke)

Any patient with a new neurological event or progressive neurological condition who develops dysphagia requires an urgent swallowing assessment by a Speech and Language Therapist (SALT). This is critical to assess the safety of their swallow and risk of aspiration. Management is focused on reducing this risk through modified food and fluid consistencies (e.g., thickened liquids, pureed diet), compensatory swallowing strategies, and consideration of non-oral feeding, such as a nasogastric (NG) tube or percutaneous endoscopic gastrostomy (PEG).

Oesophageal Motility Disorders (e.g., Achalasia)

If a motility disorder like achalasia is suspected (dysphagia to liquids and solids), the patient should be referred to a gastroenterologist. The diagnosis is confirmed with oesophageal manometry after an endoscopy has ruled out mechanical obstruction (or 'pseudoachalasia' from a tumour). Management aims to lower the pressure in the lower oesophageal sphincter and may include endoscopic pneumatic dilatation, injection of botulinum toxin into the sphincter, or a surgical procedure known as a Heller's myotomy.

Benign Structural Causes (e.g., Peptic Stricture)

For benign causes like an oesophageal stricture secondary to chronic GORD, management involves two goals. First, treating the underlying reflux with high-dose proton pump inhibitors (PPIs) to prevent recurrence. Second, relieving the obstruction via endoscopic dilatation, where a balloon or bougie is used to stretch the narrowed segment. Patients with oesophageal webs or rings can also be effectively treated with dilatation.

Reflection & Learning

These brief cases illustrate the application of clinical reasoning to different dysphagia presentations. They are for educational purposes only.

Case 1: The Elderly Man with Food Sticking

A 72-year-old man presents to his GP with a 3-month history of food "getting stuck" in his chest. It started with just bread and meat, but now even soft foods are becoming difficult. He reports having lost about 5kg in weight without trying. The problem representation is an older man with progressive solid food dysphagia and an associated red flag of unintentional weight loss. This clinical picture is highly suspicious for oesophageal cancer, and the GP makes an urgent 2-week wait referral for an OGD.

Case 2: The Young Woman with Difficulty Swallowing Everything

A 28-year-old woman presents with a year-long history of difficulty swallowing. She finds that both food and liquids "get held up" behind her breastbone. She sometimes regurgitates undigested food hours after a meal and has learned to arch her back to help things pass. The problem representation is a young patient with long-standing, paradoxical dysphagia (affecting liquids and solids from the start) and regurgitation, classic for a motility disorder. The next step is referral to gastroenterology for an OGD to rule out obstruction, followed by oesophageal manometry, which would likely confirm achalasia.

Case 3: The Nursing Home Resident with a Cough

A nurse at a care home calls the GP about an 85-year-old resident with advanced dementia who has developed a persistent cough, especially during mealtimes. She has had two chest infections in the last three months. On review, she has a weak, gurgly-sounding cough after being given a sip of water. The problem representation is an elderly, frail patient with a neurological comorbidity developing respiratory symptoms linked to eating, concerning for oropharyngeal dysphagia with aspiration. The immediate action is to make her nil-by-mouth and refer urgently for a Speech and Language Therapy (SALT) assessment to prevent further aspiration pneumonia.
