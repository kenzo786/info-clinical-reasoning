Dyspnoea Diagnostic Reasoning Reference
Context: Dyspnoea Assessment
Overview

Dyspnoea, the subjective sensation of breathlessness, is a cardinal symptom of cardiorespiratory disease and one of the most common reasons for seeking emergency medical care. It can range from a chronic, slowly progressive symptom in conditions like COPD or heart failure, to an acute, life-threatening emergency such as a pulmonary embolism or anaphylaxis. The challenge for the clinician is to rapidly differentiate between these causes, identify immediate threats to life, and initiate appropriate management.

A systematic and structured approach is paramount. The initial assessment must focus on evaluating airway, breathing, and circulation, and identifying red flag features that signify critical illness. This educational module provides a foundational framework for the clinical reasoning process involved in assessing a patient with dyspnoea. For definitive, real-time management protocols, users must always refer to current UK guidelines from authoritative bodies such as NICE (e.g., NG115 for COPD, NG80 for Asthma, NG187 for Acute Heart Failure) and the British Thoracic Society (BTS).

Pathophysiology/Basic Science

The sensation of dyspnoea is a complex phenomenon arising from a mismatch between the central respiratory drive from the brainstem and the feedback received from mechanoreceptors and chemoreceptors throughout the body. Understanding the underlying mechanisms is key to diagnosis:

  • Increased Work of Breathing: This is a primary driver of dyspnoea. In obstructive diseases like Asthma or COPD, airflow resistance is high, requiring greater effort to exhale. In restrictive diseases like Interstitial Lung Disease or conditions affecting chest wall movement (e.g., obesity, neuromuscular weakness), lung compliance is reduced, requiring more effort to inflate the lungs.
  • Ventilation/Perfusion (V/Q) Mismatch: Efficient gas exchange requires that ventilated areas of the lung are also well-perfused with blood. In conditions like Pneumonia, alveoli are filled with inflammatory exudate (perfusion without ventilation). In Pulmonary Embolism, blood flow is obstructed to a ventilated area of the lung (ventilation without perfusion). Both scenarios lead to hypoxaemia, which stimulates peripheral chemoreceptors and drives the sensation of breathlessness.
  • Impaired Gas Exchange: Conditions that thicken the alveolar-capillary membrane, such as Pulmonary Oedema (due to heart failure) or Interstitial Lung Disease, impede the diffusion of oxygen from the alveoli into the bloodstream. The resulting hypoxia is a potent stimulus for dyspnoea.
  • Increased Respiratory Drive: The respiratory centre can be stimulated by factors other than hypoxia. In Metabolic Acidosis (e.g., Diabetic Ketoacidosis), the body attempts to compensate by expelling CO2, leading to deep, rapid breathing (Kussmaul breathing). In Anxiety or panic attacks, the central respiratory drive can be directly stimulated, leading to hyperventilation and a sensation of air hunger despite normal gas exchange.
  • Reduced Oxygen-Carrying Capacity: In severe Anaemia, the blood's ability to carry oxygen is diminished. While arterial oxygen saturation (SpO2) may be normal, total oxygen delivery to the tissues is reduced. The body compensates with an increased cardiac output and respiratory rate, which can be perceived as dyspnoea, particularly on exertion.
Differential Diagnosis (DDx) Framework

A structured approach to the differential diagnosis of dyspnoea is essential to ensure life-threatening conditions are prioritised.

Must not miss!
  • Anaphylaxis: A severe, life-threatening allergic reaction causing rapid onset of airway swelling, wheeze, and hypotension.
  • FBAO (Foreign Body Airway Obstruction): Sudden onset of choking, coughing, and stridor, often after eating or in young children.
  • Epiglottitis: Presents with a rapid onset of fever, sore throat, drooling, and stridor, requiring immediate airway management.
  • Pneumonia: An infection of the lung parenchyma presenting with productive cough, fever, pleuritic pain, and dyspnoea.
  • Pulmonary Embolism: Characterised by a sudden onset of dyspnoea and pleuritic chest pain, often with risk factors for venous thromboembolism.
  • Pneumothorax: Sudden onset of unilateral pleuritic chest pain and dyspnoea, with reduced breath sounds on the affected side.
  • Myocardial Infarction: Can present with dyspnoea as an anginal equivalent, often alongside chest pain, sweating, and nausea.
  • Heart failure: Often presents with progressive exertional dyspnoea, orthopnoea, and paroxysmal nocturnal dyspnoea, with signs of fluid overload.
  • Arrhythmia: Can cause dyspnoea due to an inefficient cardiac output, often associated with palpitations or dizziness.
  • Pleural effusion: An accumulation of fluid in the pleural space causing progressive breathlessness and dullness to percussion.
  • Tuberculosis: Presents with chronic cough, fever, night sweats, weight loss, and progressive dyspnoea.
  • Lung cancer: May cause progressive dyspnoea due to airway obstruction, effusion, or lymphatic spread.
  • Diabetic ketoacidosis: A metabolic acidosis causing deep, sighing respiration (Kussmaul breathing) in a patient with diabetes.
Common causes
  • Asthma: A common chronic inflammatory disease of the airways causing episodic wheeze, cough, and breathlessness.
  • COPD: A progressive lung disease, typically in smokers, causing persistent cough and exertional dyspnoea.
  • LRTI (Lower Respiratory Tract Infection): Often a viral bronchitis causing cough and mild breathlessness, which is usually self-limiting.
  • Anaemia: A reduced haemoglobin level causing exertional dyspnoea, fatigue, and pallor.
  • Obesity: Increased work of breathing due to excess chest wall weight and reduced lung volumes.
  • COVID-19: A viral illness that can cause a range of respiratory symptoms from mild breathlessness to severe pneumonia and ARDS.
Easily missed
  • Medication Induced Dyspnoea: Can be a side effect of drugs like beta-blockers, amiodarone, or NSAIDs.
  • Hyperventilation: Often related to anxiety, causing a sensation of breathlessness despite over-breathing, sometimes with paraesthesia.
  • Obstructive Sleep Apnoea: Causes daytime sleepiness and can contribute to chronic dyspnoea due to associated cardiorespiratory strain.
  • Interstitial Lung Disease: A group of disorders causing progressive scarring of lung tissue, leading to exertional dyspnoea and dry cough.
  • Pulmonary hypertension: Elevated pressure in the pulmonary arteries causing progressive exertional dyspnoea and signs of right heart failure.
  • Sarcoidosis: A multi-system inflammatory disease that can affect the lungs, causing dry cough and exertional dyspnoea.
  • Hyperthyroidism: Increased metabolic rate and oxygen consumption can lead to a sensation of breathlessness, often with palpitations.
Other Differential Diagnoses to consider
  • Guillain-Barré syndrome: May present with respiratory muscle weakness, causing dyspnoea in severe cases.
  • Myasthenia gravis: Muscle weakness, including the respiratory muscles, leading to breathlessness.
  • Pneumocystis jirovecii pneumonia (PCP): An opportunistic infection, causing progressive dyspnoea in immunocompromised patients.
  • Vocal cord dysfunction: Paradoxical movement of vocal cords leading to dyspnoea, often misdiagnosed as asthma.
  • Bronchiolitis obliterans: A rare inflammatory condition affecting small airways, causing progressive dyspnoea.
  • Thyrotoxic crisis: A severe hyperthyroid state, presenting with acute breathlessness, tachycardia, and fever.
  • Metabolic acidosis: Often associated with diabetic ketoacidosis (DKA) or renal failure, leading to Kussmaul breathing (deep, laboured respirations).
  • Large pulmonary arteriovenous malformation: Abnormal vascular connections in the lungs, causing hypoxia and dyspnoea.
  • Chronic thromboembolic pulmonary hypertension (CTEPH): Unresolved pulmonary embolism leading to persistent dyspnoea and pulmonary hypertension.
History Taking
Onset and Duration: When did the breathlessness start, and did it come on suddenly or gradually?
  • Clarifying the timeline is critical for diagnosis. A sudden onset suggests acute events like a Pulmonary Embolism, Pneumothorax, or Anaphylaxis. Gradual onset over weeks to months is more typical of chronic conditions like COPD, Heart Failure, or Interstitial Lung Disease.
  • Relevance: The acuity of onset is one of the most important features for distinguishing between life-threatening emergencies and chronic conditions.
Severity and Progression: How severe is it? Can you speak in full sentences? Has it been getting worse?
  • Assessing severity helps determine the urgency of the situation. Inability to speak in full sentences is a sign of significant respiratory distress. Understanding the trajectory (improving, worsening, or fluctuating) provides diagnostic clues; for instance, fluctuating symptoms are characteristic of Asthma.
  • Relevance: Helps to triage the patient and understand the natural history of the underlying cause.
Triggers and Exacerbating/Relieving Factors: What makes it worse? Is it worse on exertion, lying flat, or with allergens?
  • Exertional dyspnoea is a hallmark of Heart Failure, COPD, and Anaemia.
  • Orthopnoea (breathlessness on lying flat) and Paroxysmal Nocturnal Dyspnoea (PND) are highly suggestive of left-sided heart failure.
  • Positional changes can also worsen dyspnoea in large pleural effusions.
  • Triggers like dust, pollen, or cold air point towards Asthma.
  • Relevance: Specific triggers and relieving factors can be highly specific for certain diagnoses.
Associated Symptoms: Have you had any chest pain, cough, sputum, fever, wheeze, or leg swelling?
  • Chest pain: Pleuritic pain suggests PE or Pneumonia; crushing central pain suggests Myocardial Infarction.
  • Cough and Sputum: Productive cough with purulent sputum suggests an infective cause like Pneumonia or a COPD exacerbation. A dry cough is typical of Interstitial Lung Disease.
  • Haemoptysis (coughing blood) is a red flag for PE, Lung Cancer, or Tuberculosis.
  • Fever points towards an infectious or inflammatory cause.
  • Wheeze is characteristic of obstructive airway diseases like Asthma or COPD.
  • Unilateral leg swelling is a key sign of Deep Vein Thrombosis (DVT), increasing the probability of a PE. Bilateral swelling suggests Heart Failure.
  • Relevance: The constellation of associated symptoms is crucial for building a diagnostic picture.
Past Medical History (PMH): Do you have any existing conditions like asthma, COPD, heart disease, or cancer?
  • A known history of cardiorespiratory disease significantly increases the likelihood of an exacerbation being the cause of acute dyspnoea. Risk factors for ischaemic heart disease (diabetes, hypertension, hypercholesterolaemia) are important. A history of cancer or recent surgery/immobility increases the risk of PE.
  • Relevance: PMH is essential for risk stratification and helps to place the current presentation in context.
Drug History: Are you taking any new medications? Are you compliant with your inhalers or diuretics?
  • Certain drugs can cause dyspnoea (e.g., beta-blockers, amiodarone-induced lung toxicity). Poor compliance with medications for asthma, COPD, or heart failure is a common cause of deterioration.
  • Relevance: A thorough medication review can reveal the cause or contributing factors.
Social History: Do you smoke? What is your occupation? Are there any pets or birds at home?
  • Smoking history is the single most important risk factor for COPD and Lung Cancer. Occupational exposures (e.g., to asbestos) can cause Interstitial Lung Disease. Exposure to birds or mould can cause hypersensitivity pneumonitis.
  • Relevance: Provides clues to chronic respiratory diagnoses and key risk factors.
Ideas, Concerns, and Expectations (ICE)
  • Ask the patient: "What are you most worried this could be?" and "What were you hoping we could do for you today?" Understanding the patient's perspective is key to a holistic assessment and shared decision-making, particularly as dyspnoea can be a very frightening experience.
  • Relevance: Fosters a patient-centred approach and helps address specific fears, such as a fear of cancer or a heart attack.
Examination Strategy
**General Inspection**: Inspect for signs of respiratory distress (use of accessory muscles, cyanosis, etc.)
  • General inspection provides initial clues about the severity of dyspnoea and its potential cause.
  • Relevance: Use of accessory muscles, nasal flaring, or intercostal recession suggests significant respiratory distress, pointing to conditions like asthma exacerbation, COPD, or pneumonia. Cyanosis indicates hypoxia, which may be seen in pulmonary embolism or severe COPD.
**Respiratory Rate and Pattern**: Measure the respiratory rate and observe the pattern of breathing.
  • Tachypnoea or abnormal breathing patterns can indicate underlying pathology.
  • Relevance: Tachypnoea (increased respiratory rate) may suggest conditions such as pulmonary embolism, pneumonia, or metabolic acidosis. Cheyne-Stokes breathing may be seen in heart failure, while Kussmaul breathing indicates metabolic acidosis (e.g., diabetic ketoacidosis).
**Palpation**: Check for tracheal deviation.
  • Tracheal deviation can signal significant pathology involving the lungs or pleura.
  • Relevance: A trachea deviating away from the affected side suggests tension pneumothorax or large pleural effusion. Deviation towards the affected side may indicate lung collapse.
**Percussion of the Chest**: Percuss the chest wall to assess for hyperresonance or dullness.
  • Percussion helps identify changes in air content or fluid within the lungs or pleura.
  • Relevance: Hyperresonance may suggest pneumothorax, while dullness could indicate pleural effusion, consolidation (as in pneumonia), or lung collapse.
**Auscultation of the Chest**: Listen for breath sounds, wheezing, crackles, or absent breath sounds.
  • Auscultation provides key information on the presence of airway obstruction, fluid, or consolidation in the lungs.
  • Relevance: Wheezing suggests obstructive airway diseases such as asthma or COPD. Crackles may indicate pulmonary oedema (heart failure) or pneumonia. Absent breath sounds may indicate pneumothorax, pleural effusion, or lung collapse.
**Jugular Venous Pressure (JVP)**: Assess JVP for elevation.
  • An elevated JVP can indicate increased central venous pressure, often linked to cardiac causes of dyspnoea.
  • Relevance: Raised JVP is typically seen in heart failure, pulmonary embolism, or tension pneumothorax. A normal JVP can help rule out these conditions.
**Peripheral Oedema**: Inspect and palpate for signs of oedema in the lower limbs.
  • Oedema can indicate fluid overload, usually due to cardiac or renal causes.
  • Relevance: Bilateral oedema may suggest heart failure or chronic venous insufficiency, while unilateral oedema raises suspicion for deep vein thrombosis (DVT) with possible pulmonary embolism.
**Cardiovascular Examination**: Listen for murmurs, gallops, or irregular heart rhythms.
  • Cardiac auscultation can reveal structural or functional abnormalities that may cause dyspnoea.
  • Relevance: A third heart sound (S3) suggests heart failure, while murmurs may indicate valvular heart disease (e.g., mitral stenosis or aortic stenosis). Irregular rhythms could suggest arrhythmias like atrial fibrillation.
**Inspection**: Inspect for pallor, cyanosis, or clubbing.
  • Signs such as pallor, cyanosis, or clubbing give clues to both acute and chronic disease processes.
  • Relevance: Pallor may indicate anaemia, while central cyanosis suggests hypoxia from conditions like COPD, pneumonia, or pulmonary embolism. Clubbing can be seen in chronic lung diseases like lung cancer, interstitial lung disease, or bronchiectasis.
**Abdominal Examination**: Palpate for hepatomegaly or ascites.
  • An abdominal examination can help identify signs of fluid overload or underlying systemic disease.
  • Relevance: Hepatomegaly and ascites suggest right-sided heart failure or liver disease, both of which can cause or contribute to dyspnoea.
Red Flag Synthesis
**Sudden Onset of Severe Dyspnoea**
  • Sudden and severe shortness of breath is a critical symptom that suggests an acute and potentially life-threatening cause.
  • Significance: This could indicate conditions such as pulmonary embolism, pneumothorax, or acute myocardial infarction, all of which require immediate medical intervention.
**Chest Pain with Dyspnoea**
  • Chest pain associated with dyspnoea may suggest serious cardiovascular or respiratory conditions.
  • Significance: This could indicate myocardial infarction, pulmonary embolism, aortic dissection, or pneumothorax, all of which are medical emergencies.
**Haemoptysis**
  • Haemoptysis is a concerning symptom that may indicate underlying pathology within the lungs or airways.
  • Significance: This could signify lung cancer, tuberculosis, pulmonary embolism, or severe infection such as pneumonia. Haemoptysis always warrants further investigation.
**Stridor or Wheezing with Dyspnoea**
  • Stridor or wheezing can indicate upper or lower airway obstruction, respectively.
  • Significance: Stridor may suggest anaphylaxis, foreign body aspiration, or epiglottitis, while wheezing can indicate severe asthma or anaphylaxis. Both conditions are potential emergencies requiring prompt treatment.
**Marked Cyanosis**
  • Cyanosis indicates significant hypoxia and is a critical sign of respiratory or cardiovascular failure.
  • Significance: This could indicate life-threatening conditions such as severe pneumonia, pulmonary embolism, heart failure, or advanced COPD.
**Altered Mental State (Confusion or Drowsiness)**
  • Changes in mental state suggest that hypoxia or hypercapnia (high CO2 levels) are severely affecting cerebral function.
  • Significance: This could indicate respiratory failure from conditions like severe COPD, pneumonia, or pulmonary embolism, necessitating immediate medical intervention.
**Severe Tachycardia or Bradycardia with Dyspnoea**
  • Severe changes in heart rate may indicate cardiovascular instability, especially when associated with dyspnoea.
  • Significance: Tachycardia may indicate pulmonary embolism, sepsis, or arrhythmias, while bradycardia can be a sign of advanced heart block or impending respiratory failure.
**Unilateral Leg Swelling or Pain with Dyspnoea**
  • Unilateral leg swelling or pain, particularly with dyspnoea, raises concern for a deep vein thrombosis (DVT) with potential pulmonary embolism.
  • Significance: This could indicate venous thromboembolism, a condition that can result in pulmonary embolism and sudden death without prompt treatment.
**Fever with Dyspnoea**
  • Fever in a patient with dyspnoea may indicate an underlying infectious cause, particularly in the lungs.
  • Significance: This could suggest pneumonia, tuberculosis, or COVID-19. Such infections can cause severe respiratory compromise and require immediate treatment.
Investigations Strategy & Interpretation
**Pulse Oximetry**
  • Pulse oximetry measures the oxygen saturation in the blood and is a quick and non-invasive way to assess for hypoxaemia.
  • Result Significance: A low oxygen saturation (< 92%) suggests hypoxia, which may indicate conditions like pneumonia, COPD exacerbation, or pulmonary embolism. Normal oxygen saturation helps rule out severe hypoxaemia.
**Arterial Blood Gas (ABG)**
  • An ABG is critical for assessing gas exchange, acid-base status, and detecting hypoxaemia and hypercapnia.
  • Result Significance: Hypoxaemia suggests pulmonary causes such as pulmonary embolism or pneumonia. Hypercapnia is seen in COPD exacerbations or respiratory failure. ABGs also help detect metabolic disturbances such as in diabetic ketoacidosis.
**Chest X-Ray (CXR)**
  • A chest X-ray is a first-line investigation to assess the lungs, pleura, and heart size.
  • Result Significance: Consolidation may indicate pneumonia, while hyperinflation suggests COPD. A pneumothorax, pleural effusion, or pulmonary oedema (seen in heart failure) can also be detected. A normal CXR does not exclude pulmonary embolism but can help rule out other causes.
**ECG (Electrocardiogram)**
  • An ECG is important for assessing cardiac causes of dyspnoea, especially arrhythmias and myocardial infarction.
  • Result Significance: Abnormalities such as atrial fibrillation, signs of right heart strain (S1Q3T3 pattern) in pulmonary embolism, or ischaemic changes suggest myocardial infarction or PE. A normal ECG makes these less likely but does not exclude them.
**Full Blood Count (FBC)**
  • FBC assesses for anaemia, infection, and other haematological abnormalities.
  • Result Significance: Anaemia (low haemoglobin) may explain chronic dyspnoea, while an elevated white cell count suggests infection (e.g., pneumonia). Eosinophilia might suggest asthma exacerbation or allergic reactions. A normal FBC helps exclude these conditions.
**D-Dimer**
  • D-Dimer is used to help rule out thromboembolic events, such as pulmonary embolism, in low to intermediate-risk patients.
  • Result Significance: A positive D-dimer suggests the presence of a clot and necessitates further imaging (e.g., CT pulmonary angiography). A negative D-dimer in a low-risk patient can effectively rule out PE or DVT.
**CT Pulmonary Angiography (CTPA)**
  • CTPA is the gold standard for diagnosing pulmonary embolism by visualising pulmonary arteries.
  • Result Significance: A positive CTPA confirms a pulmonary embolism. A negative result rules out PE with high accuracy.
**B-Type Natriuretic Peptide (BNP)**
  • BNP is a biomarker that helps assess heart failure, especially when dyspnoea is of uncertain cardiac or respiratory origin.
  • Result Significance: Elevated BNP suggests heart failure. Normal BNP levels make heart failure less likely as the cause of dyspnoea.
**Spirometry**
  • Spirometry is used to assess lung function, particularly in obstructive or restrictive lung diseases.
  • Result Significance: Obstructive patterns (reduced FEV1/FVC ratio) suggest asthma or COPD. Restrictive patterns suggest interstitial lung disease. Normal spirometry results help rule out significant lung disease.
**Echocardiography**
  • Echocardiography assesses the structure and function of the heart and is crucial in evaluating cardiac causes of dyspnoea.
  • Result Significance: Echocardiogram findings such as left ventricular dysfunction or valvular disease suggest heart failure or valvular heart disease. A normal echocardiogram helps exclude significant cardiac causes of dyspnoea.
Evidence-Based Diagnosis (EBDx)
**Sudden onset of dyspnoea** in Pulmonary Embolism
  • Sudden onset of dyspnoea is a key presenting symptom of pulmonary embolism (PE). It reflects acute vascular obstruction in the lungs.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 73%, Specificity: 60%, Positive Likelihood Ratio (LR+): 1.8, Negative Likelihood Ratio (LR-): 0.45.
  • Reference: Wells PS et al. (2001), Clinical prediction rule for pulmonary embolism.
  • Significance: Sudden onset of dyspnoea increases suspicion of pulmonary embolism, particularly when combined with other risk factors, but its absence does not rule out the diagnosis.
**Exertional dyspnoea** in Heart Failure
  • Exertional dyspnoea is a hallmark symptom of heart failure, caused by increased pulmonary venous pressure due to left ventricular dysfunction.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 84%, Specificity: 70%, LR+: 2.8, LR-: 0.23.
  • Reference: McMurray JJ et al. (2012), ESC guidelines for the diagnosis and treatment of acute and chronic heart failure.
  • Significance: Positive findings of exertional dyspnoea strongly suggest heart failure, while a negative response makes heart failure less likely.
**Wheezing** in Asthma
  • Wheezing is a common physical sign of asthma due to bronchoconstriction and airway inflammation.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 80%, Specificity: 90%, LR+: 8.0, LR-: 0.22.
  • Reference: British Thoracic Society Guidelines for the management of asthma (2019).
  • Significance: Wheezing on auscultation is highly specific for asthma and supports the diagnosis, while its absence reduces the likelihood but does not exclude asthma.
**D-Dimer** in Pulmonary Embolism
  • D-Dimer is a fibrin degradation product that becomes elevated during thrombus formation and is used to rule out PE in low-risk patients.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 95%, Specificity: 40%, LR+: 1.6, LR-: 0.13.
  • Reference: Kearon C et al. (2016), Clinical practice guideline for diagnosis of PE.
  • Significance: A negative D-Dimer effectively rules out pulmonary embolism in low-risk patients, while a positive result necessitates further imaging (e.g., CTPA).
**Chest X-Ray** in Pneumonia
  • A chest X-ray is critical in diagnosing pneumonia, showing areas of consolidation and infiltrates in the lung parenchyma.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 85%, Specificity: 90%, LR+: 8.5, LR-: 0.17.
  • Reference: Metlay JP et al. (2019), IDSA/ATS guidelines for management of community-acquired pneumonia.
  • Significance: A positive chest X-ray confirms pneumonia, while a normal chest X-ray makes the diagnosis less likely but does not entirely rule it out, especially in early or mild cases.
**B-Type Natriuretic Peptide (BNP)** in Heart Failure
  • BNP is a biomarker for heart failure, released in response to increased ventricular pressure and volume overload.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 90%, Specificity: 76%, LR+: 3.75, LR-: 0.13.
  • Reference: Maisel AS et al. (2002), Utility of BNP in the diagnosis of heart failure.
  • Significance: Elevated BNP supports the diagnosis of heart failure, while normal levels make heart failure unlikely, especially in acute presentations of dyspnoea.
Diagnostic Timeout & Cognitive Biases

The differential for dyspnoea is broad, making it susceptible to cognitive errors. Before finalising a diagnosis, it is good practice for clinicians at all levels to take a 'diagnostic timeout' to challenge their own reasoning and consider potential biases.

  • Anchoring Bias: This is the tendency to lock onto the first piece of information or a known diagnosis. For example, attributing acute dyspnoea in a patient with known COPD to an exacerbation, while failing to consider a new, alternative diagnosis like a pulmonary embolism or heart failure, even if features like pleuritic pain or peripheral oedema are present. A key question to ask is: "If the patient didn't have [known diagnosis], what else could this be?"
  • Confirmation Bias: The tendency to seek out and overvalue evidence that supports an initial hypothesis while ignoring contradictory signs. For instance, focusing on a history of anxiety to confirm a diagnosis of hyperventilation, while downplaying a new tachycardia or a subtle drop in oxygen saturation.
  • Premature Closure: Accepting a diagnosis before it has been fully verified. A common pitfall is diagnosing a "chest infection" without radiological confirmation, potentially missing a non-infectious cause like heart failure or a pulmonary embolism which can mimic infective symptoms.
  • Attribution Error: Attributing symptoms to a patient's character or previous behaviour rather than a new pathology. For example, dismissing dyspnoea in a patient with high anxiety levels as "just another panic attack" without performing a full workup to exclude organic disease. Dyspnoea is a frightening symptom that will naturally cause anxiety, which can confound the clinical picture.
Illustrative Management Pathways

For educational purposes, this section describes the typical principles of management for key diagnoses. These are not prescriptive instructions. All management must be guided by official UK guidelines, local formularies, and senior clinical advice.

Acute Exacerbation of COPD/Asthma

The cornerstone of management for acute obstructive airway disease is immediate administration of high-flow oxygen (titrated to target saturations, e.g., 88-92% in known COPD) and bronchodilators. This typically involves nebulised salbutamol (a short-acting beta-agonist) and ipratropium bromide (a short-acting muscarinic antagonist). Systemic corticosteroids (e.g., oral prednisolone or intravenous hydrocortisone) are given to reduce airway inflammation. For infective exacerbations of COPD, antibiotics are prescribed according to local guidelines. In severe cases unresponsive to initial therapy, escalation to intravenous bronchodilators (e.g., magnesium sulphate, aminophylline) or non-invasive ventilation (NIV) may be required.

Acute Pulmonary Oedema (Heart Failure)

The management of acute cardiogenic pulmonary oedema aims to reduce cardiac preload and afterload, and improve oxygenation. Patients are sat upright to reduce venous return. High-flow oxygen is administered, and in cases of severe respiratory distress, continuous positive airway pressure (CPAP) or non-invasive ventilation (NIV) is often used. The mainstay of medical treatment is intravenous loop diuretics (e.g., furosemide) to promote fluid removal. Vasodilators, such as intravenous glyceryl trinitrate (GTN), are used to reduce both preload and afterload, decreasing the work of the heart. Opiates may be used for anxiolysis and their vasodilatory effects, but with caution due to the risk of respiratory depression.

Pulmonary Embolism (PE)

Once PE is suspected or confirmed, the primary treatment is anticoagulation to prevent further clot formation and propagation. In haemodynamically stable patients, treatment is typically initiated with a Direct Oral Anticoagulant (DOAC), such as apixaban or rivaroxaban, or with Low Molecular Weight Heparin (LMWH). The duration of treatment depends on whether the PE was provoked or unprovoked. In massive PE causing haemodynamic instability (hypotension), urgent reperfusion therapy with systemic thrombolysis ('clot-busting' drugs like alteplase) is the treatment of choice, often delivered in a high-dependency or intensive care setting.

Pneumonia

Management of community-acquired pneumonia (CAP) involves supportive care and prompt antibiotic therapy. The severity of pneumonia is typically assessed using a scoring system like CURB-65 (Confusion, Urea >7, Respiratory rate ≥30, Blood pressure <90 systolic or ≤60 diastolic, age ≥65) to guide the place of care (home, hospital ward, or ICU) and the choice of antibiotics. Antibiotic choice is guided by local microbiology guidelines but often starts with amoxicillin for low-severity CAP, with the addition of a macrolide (e.g., clarithromycin) for moderate-to-severe cases. Supportive care includes oxygen therapy to maintain adequate saturations, intravenous fluids if dehydrated, and analgesia.

Reflection & Learning

These brief cases illustrate the application of clinical reasoning to different dyspnoea presentations. They are for educational purposes only.

Case 1: Suspected Heart Failure in the Community

A 78-year-old woman with a history of hypertension and a previous myocardial infarction presents to her GP with a 2-month history of worsening breathlessness on exertion. She now needs three pillows to sleep and has woken up gasping for air twice in the last week. On examination, her JVP is elevated, she has bilateral pitting oedema to her knees, and fine crackles at both lung bases. The problem representation is an elderly woman with cardiovascular risk factors presenting with classic symptoms (exertional dyspnoea, orthopnoea, PND) and signs (raised JVP, oedema, crackles) of congestive heart failure. The GP arranges urgent blood tests including a BNP and refers for an echocardiogram to confirm the diagnosis and initiate appropriate management.

Case 2: Suspected Pneumothorax in the Emergency Department

A 22-year-old tall, slim man presents to the Emergency Department with a sudden onset of sharp, left-sided chest pain and shortness of breath that started two hours ago while he was watching television. He is a smoker but has no other medical history. On examination, his respiratory rate is 24, and oxygen saturation is 95% on air. There is reduced air entry and a hyperresonant percussion note on the left side of his chest. The problem representation is a young man with a classic phenotype presenting with sudden pleuritic pain and unilateral examination findings highly suspicious for a primary spontaneous pneumothorax. An urgent chest X-ray is requested to confirm the diagnosis and determine the size of the pneumothorax to guide management.

Case 3: Suspected Anaphylaxis

A 40-year-old man with a known bee sting allergy is brought in by ambulance after being stung. He complains of feeling dizzy, having difficulty breathing, and his voice sounds hoarse. On examination, he is pale and clammy, his heart rate is 130 bpm, his blood pressure is 85/50 mmHg, and an audible wheeze is present on auscultation. There is widespread urticaria on his chest and arms. The problem representation is a patient with a known allergen exposure presenting with rapid onset of airway compromise (hoarseness), breathing difficulty (wheeze), and circulation problems (hypotension), defining anaphylaxis. The immediate management is an intramuscular injection of adrenaline, followed by securing the airway and administering high-flow oxygen, IV fluids, antihistamines, and steroids.
