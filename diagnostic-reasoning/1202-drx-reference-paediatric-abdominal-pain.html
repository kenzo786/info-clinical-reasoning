Paediatric Abdominal Pain Diagnostic Reasoning Reference
Context: Paediatric Abdominal Pain Assessment
Overview

Abdominal pain in children is one of the most frequent reasons for consultation in both primary care and emergency medicine. The diagnostic challenge is significant, as the causes range from benign and self-limiting conditions like constipation and viral gastroenteritis to acute surgical emergencies such as appendicitis or intussusception. The child's age, developmental stage, and ability to describe symptoms add layers of complexity to the assessment.

A systematic and structured approach is essential to differentiate between serious and non-serious causes effectively. The initial priority is to identify "red flag" symptoms and signs that indicate a need for urgent investigation and specialist referral. This framework aims to provide a foundational approach to the clinical reasoning required when faced with a child presenting with abdominal pain. For definitive, real-time management protocols, clinicians must consult official UK guidelines from bodies such as NICE (e.g., CG99 for Constipation in children and young people) and local paediatric surgical pathways.

Pathophysiology/Basic Science

Understanding the origin and type of abdominal pain is fundamental to forming a differential diagnosis. Pain can be generated through several distinct mechanisms:

  • Visceral Pain: This arises from the stretching or distension of abdominal organs (e.g., intestines, gallbladder) or from ischaemia. It is typically dull, poorly localised, and often felt in the midline. The level of pain corresponds to the embryological origin of the affected organ (foregut, midgut, or hindgut). For example, appendicitis initially presents as poorly localised periumbilical visceral pain (midgut) before becoming a localised somatic pain.
  • Somatic (Parietal) Pain: This occurs when the parietal peritoneum becomes inflamed. The pain is sharp, intense, and precisely localised to the area of inflammation. It is aggravated by movement, coughing, or palpation (e.g., the classic right iliac fossa pain in appendicitis after the inflammation spreads to the peritoneum).
  • Referred Pain: This is pain perceived at a site distant from the affected organ. It occurs because nerves from the organ and the distant site share the same spinal cord level. A classic example is the shoulder tip pain seen in diaphragmatic irritation (e.g., from a ruptured spleen or pneumonia), as both are supplied by the phrenic nerve (C3, C4, C5).
  • Obstructive Pain: This is caused by the forceful peristaltic contractions of a hollow organ trying to push its contents past an obstruction. The pain is typically colicky (coming in waves). This is the characteristic pain pattern of intussusception or bowel obstruction.
  • Inflammatory Pain: Conditions like inflammatory bowel disease (IBD) or gastritis cause pain through mucosal inflammation, leading to a constant, burning, or gnawing sensation.
  • Functional Pain: In conditions like irritable bowel syndrome (IBS) or functional abdominal pain, the pain is real but not caused by a discernible structural or biochemical abnormality. It is thought to result from visceral hypersensitivity (an amplified pain response to normal stimuli like gas or stool) and altered gut-brain axis communication.
Differential Diagnosis (DDx) Framework

A structured approach to the differential diagnosis of paediatric abdominal pain is essential. Diagnoses can be categorised based on urgency and prevalence.

Must not miss!
  • Appendicitis: Inflammation of the appendix, typically causing periumbilical pain that migrates to the right lower quadrant, with fever and anorexia.
  • Pyloric stenosis: Presents in infants (2-8 weeks) with projectile non-bilious vomiting, palpable olive-like mass, and dehydration.
  • Intussusception: Telescoping of one segment of bowel into another, causing colicky pain, redcurrant jelly stool, and a sausage-shaped abdominal mass.
  • Incarcerated hernia: A hernia that cannot be reduced, causing a painful, firm lump in the groin or scrotum, potentially leading to bowel obstruction.
  • UTI: Urinary tract infection can present with abdominal pain, fever, vomiting, and urinary symptoms like frequency or dysuria.
  • Pneumonia: Lower lobe pneumonia can irritate the diaphragm and present as referred upper abdominal pain, often with cough and fever.
  • Diabetic Ketoacidosis: A life-threatening complication of diabetes, causing abdominal pain, vomiting, dehydration, and altered mental status.
  • Leukaemia: May present with abdominal pain due to organ infiltration (hepatosplenomegaly) or lymphadenopathy, alongside pallor and bruising.
  • Lymphoma: Can cause abdominal pain from enlarged lymph nodes or intestinal involvement, often associated with weight loss and fever.
  • Testicular torsion: A surgical emergency causing acute, severe scrotal or lower abdominal pain and swelling.
  • Ovarian torsion: Twisting of the ovary causing sudden, severe unilateral lower abdominal pain, often with nausea and vomiting.
  • Ectopic pregnancy: In post-menarchal females, this presents with lower abdominal pain, often with vaginal bleeding and a positive pregnancy test.
  • Miscarriage: Presents with suprapubic cramping pain and vaginal bleeding in a confirmed or suspected pregnancy.
  • Pelvic Inflammatory Disease: An infection of the female reproductive organs in sexually active adolescents, causing lower abdominal pain and vaginal discharge.
Common causes
  • Viral gastroenteritis: An infection of the gut causing diarrhoea, vomiting, and crampy abdominal pain, often with a low-grade fever.
  • Mesenteric adenitis: Inflammation of the abdominal lymph nodes, often following a viral illness, causing pain that can mimic appendicitis.
  • Infantile colic: Episodes of intense, unexplained crying and fussiness in an otherwise healthy infant, often in the late afternoon or evening.
  • GORD: Gastro-oesophageal reflux disease, causing retrosternal or epigastric pain (heartburn), often worse after meals or when lying down.
  • Gastritis: Inflammation of the stomach lining, presenting as epigastric pain, nausea, and sometimes vomiting.
  • Constipation: A very common cause, presenting as intermittent or chronic abdominal pain, often relieved by passing stool.
  • Functional abdominal pain syndrome: Recurrent abdominal pain without an identifiable organic cause, often stress-related.
  • Irritable bowel syndrome (Adolescents): A functional disorder causing crampy pain, bloating, and altered bowel habits (diarrhoea, constipation, or both).
  • Dysmenorrhoea: Painful menstrual cramps, typically in the lower abdomen, occurring around the time of menstruation.
  • COVID-19: Can present with a range of gastrointestinal symptoms, including abdominal pain, often alongside fever and respiratory symptoms.
Easily missed
  • Lactose intolerance: Difficulty digesting lactose, leading to bloating, diarrhoea, and crampy abdominal pain after consuming dairy products.
  • Coeliac disease: An autoimmune reaction to gluten, causing chronic abdominal pain, bloating, diarrhoea, and failure to thrive.
  • Crohn's disease: A type of inflammatory bowel disease that can affect any part of the GI tract, causing pain, diarrhoea, weight loss, and fatigue.
  • Ulcerative colitis: A type of inflammatory bowel disease affecting the colon, causing bloody diarrhoea, abdominal cramps, and urgency.
  • Eating disorder: Conditions like anorexia nervosa or bulimia can be associated with various abdominal complaints.
  • HSP: Henoch-Schönlein purpura, a vasculitis causing a characteristic rash, joint pain, and colicky abdominal pain.
Other Differential Diagnoses to consider
  • Gallstones: Rare in children but possible, especially in adolescents with risk factors
  • Pancreatitis: Consider in cases with severe, persistent pain and vomiting
  • Intestinal obstruction: Can present with severe pain, vomiting, and abdominal distension
  • Malrotation with volvulus: Consider in neonates and infants with bilious vomiting and pain
  • Meckel’s diverticulum: Can present with bleeding or acute pain if inflamed or obstructed
  • Porphyria: Rare, episodic abdominal pain in adolescents, with neurologic symptoms
  • Acute porphyria: Consider in adolescents with unexplained, episodic pain and neurologic symptoms
  • Heavy metal poisoning: Rare, presents with abdominal pain and systemic signs (e.g., lead poisoning)
History Taking
**Patient’s Description**: How can I help you today?
Opening the consultation with an open-ended question allows the child (or their caregiver) to describe the symptom in their own words, offering insight into its nature, severity, and impact. Relevance: Guides the flow of targeted questions and helps build rapport by addressing patient concerns holistically.
When did the pain start? (Onset)
  • Establishes acute vs chronic nature of the pain.
  • Relevance: Acute onset may suggest appendicitis, intussusception, or testicular torsion. Chronic symptoms may indicate IBS, constipation, or IBD.
Can you describe the pain? (Character)
  • Identifies the type of pain (e.g., sharp, colicky, dull).
  • Relevance: Colicky pain suggests intussusception or biliary colic. Sharp, localised pain may indicate appendicitis or testicular torsion.
Is the pain constant, or does it come and go? (Pattern)
  • Helps differentiate between ongoing inflammation and intermittent obstructive or functional issues.
  • Relevance: Intermittent pain may suggest colic or intussusception, while constant pain is more likely in appendicitis or gastritis.
Does anything make the pain better or worse? (Relieving/Exacerbating factors)
  • Explores triggers and alleviating factors (e.g., food, movement, rest).
  • Relevance: Pain worsened by movement may suggest peritonitis, while improvement after defecation suggests constipation or IBS.
Are there any associated symptoms? (Associated features)
  • Ask about fever, vomiting, diarrhoea, urinary symptoms, or weight loss.
  • Relevance: Fever suggests infection (UTI, gastroenteritis). Vomiting with bilious content indicates obstruction. Weight loss raises suspicion of IBD or malignancy.
Has the child had similar episodes before? (History)
  • Reveals patterns or chronic conditions (e.g., IBS, coeliac disease).
  • Relevance: A history of recurrent symptoms may favour functional or chronic conditions over acute causes like appendicitis.
Does anyone in the family have similar issues? (Family history)
  • Explores genetic predispositions (e.g., coeliac disease, IBD).
  • Relevance: Family history of IBD or coeliac disease increases the likelihood of these diagnoses.
Has the child been on any medications or experienced recent changes? (Drug history)
  • Explores the role of medications or dietary changes in symptom onset.
  • Relevance: Recent antibiotic use may suggest C. difficile infection, while NSAIDs may cause gastritis.
**Ideas, Concerns, and Expectations (ICE)**
  • Understand the patient's or caregiver's perspective on the symptom's cause, any specific worries, and what they hope to achieve from the consultation.
  • Relevance: For example, parents may worry about appendicitis or cancer, influencing how the discussion is framed and managed.
Examination Strategy
General appearance and vital signs
  • Assess the child's overall appearance (well, unwell, pale, lethargic) and measure vital signs (temperature, heart rate, respiratory rate, blood pressure).
  • Relevance: A febrile, lethargic child with tachycardia may indicate infection (e.g., appendicitis, UTI). Pallor and lethargy suggest anaemia or malignancy (e.g., leukaemia). Normal vital signs in a well-appearing child favour functional or mild conditions like constipation.
Abdominal inspection
  • Observe for distension, visible peristalsis, scars, or bruising.
  • Relevance: Distension and visible peristalsis may indicate obstruction. Bruising could suggest trauma. Surgical scars might provide clues to previous conditions.
Palpation of the abdomen
  • Palpate systematically for tenderness, guarding, rigidity, and masses.
  • Relevance: Localised tenderness in the right lower quadrant suggests appendicitis. Guarding and rigidity may indicate peritonitis. A palpable mass could signify intussusception or malignancy.
Assessment of rebound tenderness
  • Gently assess for pain upon release of pressure (Blumberg’s sign).
  • Relevance: Positive rebound tenderness strongly suggests peritonitis, commonly caused by appendicitis or perforation.
Percussion of the abdomen
  • Check for areas of dullness, tympany, or peritonitis-induced pain.
  • Relevance: Dullness in specific regions may indicate fluid or a mass. Generalised tenderness on percussion supports peritonitis.
Auscultation of bowel sounds
  • Listen for the presence and character of bowel sounds.
  • Relevance: Absent bowel sounds suggest ileus or obstruction, while hyperactive sounds may be heard in gastroenteritis or obstruction.
Genitourinary examination
  • Inspect and palpate for hernias, testicular torsion, or ovarian pathology as appropriate.
  • Relevance: A tender testicle with abnormal position strongly suggests torsion. Tenderness in the adnexal area in females may indicate ovarian torsion or ectopic pregnancy in adolescents.
Rectal examination (if clinically indicated)
  • Evaluate for faecal impaction, masses, or rectal bleeding.
  • Relevance: Hard stool supports constipation. Blood may suggest intussusception, HSP, or IBD.
Systemic examination
  • Assess for signs of systemic illness, including pallor, lymphadenopathy, or hepatosplenomegaly.
  • Relevance: Hepatosplenomegaly or lymphadenopathy suggests malignancy or systemic infection (e.g., lymphoma, leukaemia).
Red Flag Synthesis
Persistent or severe abdominal pain
  • Severe or worsening pain, especially localised to one area, may indicate conditions such as appendicitis, intussusception, or peritonitis.
  • Significance: This could indicate an acute surgical abdomen, requiring immediate intervention.
Fever with abdominal pain
  • The combination of fever and abdominal pain raises suspicion of infection, such as appendicitis, UTI, or systemic sepsis.
  • Significance: This could indicate serious infections or inflammatory conditions that require urgent management.
Bilious vomiting
  • Bilious vomiting (green or yellow vomit) strongly suggests an obstruction distal to the duodenum, such as volvulus or intussusception.
  • Significance: This is a surgical emergency and requires urgent imaging and intervention.
Haematemesis or rectal bleeding
  • Vomiting blood (haematemesis) or passing blood per rectum could indicate gastrointestinal bleeding due to causes like intussusception, Meckel’s diverticulum, or gastritis.
  • Significance: This could indicate life-threatening haemorrhage or serious gastrointestinal pathology.
Pallor and lethargy
  • Marked pallor and lethargy may indicate anaemia, significant blood loss, or systemic illness such as leukaemia.
  • Significance: This could suggest malignancy or severe systemic disease requiring urgent investigation.
Signs of shock
  • Tachycardia, hypotension, prolonged capillary refill time, or cold extremities suggest circulatory collapse.
  • Significance: This indicates hypovolaemic or septic shock, requiring immediate resuscitation.
Abdominal distension
  • Distension may suggest obstruction, volvulus, or perforation, especially if associated with pain and vomiting.
  • Significance: This is a surgical emergency, necessitating urgent imaging and treatment.
Testicular or groin pain in boys
  • Acute pain in the scrotum or groin may indicate testicular torsion or an incarcerated inguinal hernia.
  • Significance: These are surgical emergencies and require immediate evaluation and management to prevent complications.
Weight loss
  • Unintentional weight loss in children raises suspicion for chronic conditions such as IBD, coeliac disease, or malignancy.
  • Significance: This may indicate serious underlying pathology and requires comprehensive investigation.
Investigations Strategy & Interpretation
Full blood count (FBC)
  • Assesses for anaemia, infection, or haematological malignancies.
  • Result Significance: Elevated white cell count suggests infection (e.g., appendicitis, UTI), while anaemia and abnormal white cells or platelets may indicate leukaemia or chronic disease.
C-reactive protein (CRP) and erythrocyte sedimentation rate (ESR)
  • Markers of inflammation that help assess for acute or chronic inflammatory processes.
  • Result Significance: Elevated levels support inflammatory or infectious conditions such as appendicitis or IBD. Normal results make significant inflammation less likely.
Urinalysis
  • Detects urinary tract infections, haematuria, or ketones.
  • Result Significance: Positive nitrites or leucocytes suggest UTI. Ketones indicate possible DKA. Haematuria may point to HSP or renal issues.
Abdominal ultrasound
  • Provides visualisation of the appendix, intussusception, gallbladder, ovaries, or masses.
  • Result Significance: Appendicitis may appear as an enlarged, non-compressible appendix. Target or doughnut sign suggests intussusception. Ovarian or testicular torsion shows characteristic findings.
Blood glucose and venous blood gas
  • Assesses for metabolic derangements such as DKA.
  • Result Significance: Hyperglycaemia and metabolic acidosis confirm DKA. Normal glucose and pH help exclude metabolic causes.
Coeliac serology
  • Detects antibodies associated with coeliac disease.
  • Result Significance: Positive anti-tissue transglutaminase (anti-tTG) antibodies support coeliac disease. Negative results make coeliac disease unlikely, but consider biopsy if symptoms persist.
Stool studies
  • Evaluate for infection (bacterial, viral, parasitic) or markers of inflammation (faecal calprotectin).
  • Result Significance: Pathogen identification supports gastroenteritis. Elevated faecal calprotectin suggests IBD.
Chest X-ray
  • Assesses for referred pain from lower lobe pneumonia.
  • Result Significance: Consolidation in the lower lobes indicates pneumonia. Normal findings reduce likelihood of pulmonary causes.
CT or MRI abdomen and pelvis
  • Advanced imaging for detailed assessment of intra-abdominal pathology.
  • Result Significance: Identifies complex or unclear conditions such as malignancies, abscesses, or obstruction. Negative findings are reassuring in diagnostic uncertainty.
Evidence-Based Diagnosis (EBDx)
Rebound tenderness for appendicitis
  • Rebound tenderness is a classic sign of peritonitis, often due to appendicitis.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 50-85%, Specificity: 68-91%, Positive Likelihood Ratio (LR+): 3.6.
  • Reference: Andersson et al., 2004, "Diagnostic Accuracy of Peritonitis Signs in Appendicitis".
  • Significance: A positive finding increases the likelihood of appendicitis, while a negative result makes peritonitis less likely but does not exclude appendicitis.
Bilious vomiting in bowel obstruction
  • Bilious vomiting is strongly indicative of intestinal obstruction distal to the duodenum.
  • Evidence: Specificity: ~95%, LR+: 10 for volvulus or intussusception.
  • Reference: Emil et al., 2007, "Paediatric Intestinal Obstruction: Clinical and Radiographic Predictors".
  • Significance: Highly predictive of conditions such as intussusception or malrotation with volvulus.
Elevated white cell count (WCC) in appendicitis
  • An elevated WCC is a common laboratory finding in appendicitis, reflecting systemic inflammation.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 83-92%, Specificity: 60-70%, LR+: 2.4-3.0.
  • Reference: Samuel, M., 2002, "Paediatric Appendicitis Score Validation Study".
  • Significance: Elevated WCC supports a diagnosis of appendicitis but is not diagnostic on its own; it must be correlated with clinical signs and imaging.
Faecal calprotectin for inflammatory bowel disease (IBD)
  • Elevated faecal calprotectin is a sensitive marker of intestinal inflammation, useful in diagnosing IBD.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 93%, Specificity: 96%, LR+: 23.25.
  • Reference: van Rheenen et al., 2010, "Calprotectin in the Diagnosis of IBD in Children".
  • Significance: A high level strongly suggests IBD, while normal levels make IBD unlikely, favouring functional conditions like IBS.
Ultrasound findings in intussusception
  • Ultrasound is highly sensitive and specific for intussusception, with findings such as the "target sign".
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 97-100%, Specificity: 88-98%, LR+: ~50.
  • Reference: Shiels et al., 2007, "Ultrasound Diagnosis of Paediatric Intussusception".
  • Significance: Positive findings strongly confirm intussusception, necessitating urgent management.
Ketones in urine for diabetic ketoacidosis (DKA)
  • The presence of ketones in urine is a hallmark finding in DKA.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 97%, Specificity: 95%, LR+: 19.4.
  • Reference: Wolfsdorf et al., 2009, "ISPAD Guidelines for the Diagnosis of DKA".
  • Significance: Positive results confirm DKA, while their absence effectively rules out this condition.
Diagnostic Timeout & Cognitive Biases

Diagnosing abdominal pain in children is fraught with potential cognitive errors. Taking a 'diagnostic timeout' to reflect on one's reasoning is a crucial step to enhance patient safety.

  • Anchoring Bias: This is the tendency to fixate on initial impressions. For example, anchoring on a common diagnosis like 'constipation' in a child with recurrent pain might cause a clinician to overlook new 'red flag' features like weight loss or fever that suggest a more serious underlying condition like IBD or malignancy. A key question to ask is: "Could this be something else?"
  • Confirmation Bias: This involves seeking or over-interpreting evidence that supports a preferred diagnosis while ignoring contradictory findings. A clinician might focus on a vague history of loose stools to support a diagnosis of gastroenteritis, while downplaying localised right iliac fossa tenderness that points towards appendicitis.
  • Atypical Presentations: A significant pitfall is failing to recognise that diseases do not always follow textbook patterns in children. Appendicitis may present without fever; a UTI may present without urinary symptoms, only abdominal pain and vomiting. A high index of suspicion is required, especially in very young or non-verbal children.
  • Over-reliance on 'Normal' Investigations: Placing undue confidence in a single normal test result can be misleading. An initial ultrasound for appendicitis can be negative or equivocal, but the diagnosis may still be correct if the clinical picture is highly suggestive. Similarly, inflammatory markers like CRP may be normal in the very early stages of a serious infection. Clinical correlation and serial assessment are paramount.
Illustrative Management Pathways

For educational purposes, this section describes the typical principles of management for key diagnoses. These are not prescriptive instructions. All management must be guided by official UK guidelines, local formularies, and senior clinical advice.

Suspected Appendicitis

A child with suspected appendicitis requires immediate referral to a paediatric surgical team. The initial management involves keeping the patient nil by mouth (NBM), securing intravenous access, and administering IV fluids to correct any dehydration. Analgesia is crucial for comfort. Diagnosis is confirmed through clinical assessment, supported by blood tests (FBC, CRP) and often imaging (typically ultrasound or, in some cases, MRI/CT). Definitive treatment is an appendicectomy (surgical removal of the appendix).

Intussusception

This is a paediatric emergency. Once suspected, the child should be urgently transferred to a hospital with paediatric surgical and radiological services. Management involves fluid resuscitation and nasogastric tube insertion for decompression. The primary treatment is non-operative reduction via a therapeutic air or contrast enema, performed under radiological guidance. If this fails, or if there are signs of peritonitis or perforation, surgical reduction or resection is required.

Diabetic Ketoacidosis (DKA)

DKA is a medical emergency requiring management in a high-dependency setting, following strict national protocols. The cornerstones of treatment are carefully controlled intravenous fluid resuscitation to correct dehydration and electrolyte imbalances, and an intravenous insulin infusion to switch off ketone production and normalise blood glucose. Frequent monitoring of electrolytes, glucose, and neurological status is vital to prevent complications like cerebral oedema.

Benign Causes (e.g., Constipation, Functional Pain)

Once serious pathology has been confidently excluded, management focuses on explanation, reassurance, and symptomatic treatment. For constipation, this involves education, lifestyle advice, and the use of laxatives (e.g., macrogols) according to NICE guidelines. For functional abdominal pain, the key is a positive diagnosis, reassuring the family that the pain is real but not dangerous, and developing coping strategies. This may involve input from psychologists, dietitians, and physiotherapists as part of a multidisciplinary approach.

Reflection & Learning

These brief cases illustrate the application of clinical reasoning to different paediatric abdominal pain presentations. They are for educational purposes only.

Case 1: Suspected Appendicitis in A&E

An 8-year-old boy presents to the Emergency Department with a 24-hour history of central abdominal pain that is now localised in the right lower quadrant. He has refused to eat, has vomited twice, and looks flushed with a temperature of 38.2°C. On examination, he has marked tenderness and guarding in the right iliac fossa. The problem representation is a school-aged child with a classic migratory pain, anorexia, fever, and localised peritonism, making acute appendicitis the primary diagnosis. The immediate pathway is urgent referral to the paediatric surgeons for assessment and likely appendicectomy.

Case 2: Intussusception in an Infant

A 9-month-old infant is brought to the GP by his parents. They describe episodes where he suddenly becomes pale and screams inconsolably, drawing his legs up to his chest. These episodes last a few minutes and are followed by periods where he seems lethargic. The GP notes a nappy containing blood-stained mucus ("redcurrant jelly"). The problem representation is an infant with a classic triad of intermittent, severe colicky pain, lethargy, and redcurrant jelly stool. This is highly suspicious for intussusception. The GP arranges for immediate emergency hospital admission for urgent ultrasound and pneumatic reduction.

Case 3: Functional Pain in a Clinic

A 10-year-old girl is seen in a paediatric clinic for recurrent, non-specific periumbilical abdominal pain that has been occurring for 6 months. The pain is not related to food or bowel movements and has not caused any weight loss. She is missing school frequently. Multiple previous investigations, including blood tests and an abdominal ultrasound, have all been normal. Physical examination today is entirely unremarkable. The problem representation is a child with chronic, non-localising pain, normal growth, and negative investigations, with significant functional impact. This is most consistent with a diagnosis of functional abdominal pain, and management should focus on reassurance, exploring psychosocial stressors, and developing coping strategies rather than further invasive tests.
