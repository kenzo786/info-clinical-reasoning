Jaundice Diagnostic Reasoning Reference
Context: Jaundice Assessment
Overview

Jaundice (or icterus) is the yellow discolouration of the skin and sclera resulting from elevated levels of bilirubin in the blood (hyperbilirubinaemia). It is not a disease in itself, but rather a cardinal sign of an underlying pathological process involving the liver, biliary system, or red blood cell breakdown. The clinical challenge is to systematically determine the cause, which can range from benign, hereditary conditions to life-threatening emergencies like ascending cholangitis or fulminant liver failure. A structured approach is essential to differentiate between pre-hepatic, hepatocellular, and post-hepatic (obstructive) causes.

This educational module provides a foundational framework for the clinical reasoning process in a patient presenting with jaundice. For definitive, real-time management protocols, clinicians must consult official UK guidelines from authoritative bodies such as NICE (e.g., CG166 for Liver Disease, NG50 for Gallstone Disease) and the British Society of Gastroenterology (BSG).

Pathophysiology/Basic Science

Understanding bilirubin metabolism is fundamental to diagnosing jaundice. The pathophysiology is best understood by categorising the cause based on the site of the primary problem:

  • Pre-hepatic Jaundice: This occurs when there is excessive production of bilirubin, overwhelming the liver's capacity to conjugate it. The primary cause is haemolysis, the rapid breakdown of red blood cells. This releases large amounts of haemoglobin, which is converted to unconjugated bilirubin. Conditions like sickle cell anaemia, hereditary spherocytosis, or autoimmune haemolytic anaemia are typical causes. The resulting hyperbilirubinaemia is predominantly unconjugated.
  • Hepatocellular Jaundice: This results from damage to the liver cells (hepatocytes), impairing their ability to take up, conjugate, or excrete bilirubin. This leads to a build-up of both unconjugated and conjugated bilirubin in the blood. Causes include viral hepatitis (e.g., Hepatitis A, B, C), alcoholic liver disease, drug-induced liver injury (DILI), and chronic conditions like autoimmune hepatitis or haemochromatosis. The damage can be acute or chronic, leading to cirrhosis.
  • Post-hepatic (Obstructive) Jaundice: This is caused by a blockage in the biliary system, which prevents the drainage of conjugated bilirubin from the liver into the intestine. The obstruction leads to a "back-up" of conjugated bilirubin into the bloodstream. Common causes include gallstones lodged in the common bile duct (choledocholithiasis), tumours of the head of the pancreas or bile duct (cholangiocarcinoma), or strictures. The classic clinical signs are dark urine (due to renal excretion of conjugated bilirubin) and pale stools (due to the absence of stercobilin in the gut).
Differential Diagnosis (DDx) Framework

A structured approach to the differential diagnosis of jaundice is essential to ensure life-threatening conditions are prioritised.

Must not miss!
  • Hepatocellular carcinoma: A primary liver cancer typically arising in the context of chronic liver disease, often presenting with weight loss, abdominal pain, and decompensation.
  • Metastatic disease: Cancer spread to the liver from another primary site (e.g., colorectal, breast, lung), which can cause obstructive jaundice or liver failure.
  • Cholangiocarcinoma: A cancer of the bile ducts that typically presents with painless obstructive jaundice and significant weight loss.
  • Pancreatic Cancer: Cancer of the head of the pancreas classically causes painless obstructive jaundice by compressing the common bile duct.
  • Viral hepatitis: Acute infection (e.g., Hepatitis A, B, E) or chronic infection (B, C) causing hepatocellular inflammation and damage.
  • Cholangitis: A bacterial infection of the biliary tree, often secondary to obstruction, presenting with Charcot's triad of fever, right upper quadrant pain, and jaundice.
  • Acute pancreatitis: Inflammation of the pancreas, which can cause jaundice if a gallstone obstructs the biliary system or if oedema compresses the bile duct.
  • Obstetric cholestasis: A condition of pregnancy causing intense itching (pruritus) and impaired bile flow, leading to maternal and fetal risks.
Common causes
  • Gallstone disease: Stones forming in the gallbladder which can cause biliary colic or move to obstruct the common bile duct, causing painful jaundice.
  • Alcoholic Liver Disease: Liver damage from excessive alcohol consumption, ranging from fatty liver to acute alcoholic hepatitis and chronic cirrhosis.
  • Gilbert’s syndrome: A common, benign hereditary condition causing a mild, fluctuating unconjugated hyperbilirubinaemia, often noticeable during illness or stress.
  • COVID-19: Can cause a mild hepatitis or cholestatic picture as part of the systemic inflammatory response to the viral infection.
Easily missed
  • Medication-induced Jaundice: Drug-induced liver injury (DILI) from a wide range of medications (e.g., paracetamol overdose, certain antibiotics, anti-epileptics) causing hepatocellular or cholestatic damage.
  • Heart failure: Severe right-sided heart failure can cause congestive hepatopathy due to back-pressure on the liver, leading to jaundice.
  • Haemolytic anaemia: Increased red blood cell destruction leading to pre-hepatic jaundice and anaemia.
  • Primary Biliary Cirrhosis: An autoimmune condition causing progressive destruction of small bile ducts within the liver, leading to cholestatic jaundice.
  • Autoimmune hepatitis: A condition where the body's immune system attacks the liver cells, causing inflammation and damage.
  • Haemochromatosis: A genetic disorder causing excessive iron accumulation in the body, leading to damage in the liver, pancreas, and heart.
  • Wilson’s disease: A rare genetic disorder of copper metabolism, leading to copper deposition in the liver and brain.
Other Differential Diagnoses to consider
  • COVID-19: Rarely associated with cholestatic or hepatocellular jaundice due to viral hepatotoxicity or inflammatory processes
  • Biliary atresia: Congenital obstruction of the biliary tree, seen in infants
  • Crigler-Najjar syndrome: Rare hereditary disorder affecting bilirubin metabolism
  • Dubin-Johnson syndrome: Genetic disorder leading to chronic conjugated hyperbilirubinaemia
  • Ischaemic hepatitis: Also known as shock liver, occurs due to reduced blood flow to the liver, often in critical illness
  • Primary sclerosing cholangitis (PSC): Chronic cholestatic disease involving inflammation and fibrosis of bile ducts, often associated with IBD
  • Sarcoidosis: Systemic granulomatous disease that can cause liver involvement and jaundice
History Taking
Patient’s Description: How can I help you today?
Starting with an open-ended question such as "Can you tell me more about what brought you here today?" Relevance: This approach allows the patient to describe their symptoms in their own words, which helps the clinician capture subjective details that might guide further, more targeted questioning.
Colour Change: Can you describe the colour change in your skin or eyes?
  • This question helps determine if the patient has noticed yellowing (jaundice) and if it affects the sclera and/or skin, supporting the assessment of the jaundice severity.
  • Relevance: A positive response may suggest conditions like liver disease or haemolytic anaemia. Absence of visible jaundice might suggest an alternative cause for their symptoms.
Onset and Duration: When did the yellowing start, and has it changed over time?
  • This question helps establish the onset, duration, and progression, which can differentiate between acute and chronic causes of jaundice.
  • Relevance: A sudden onset might suggest viral hepatitis, drug-induced jaundice, or acute biliary obstruction, while a gradual onset could be more consistent with chronic liver disease or conditions like haemochromatosis.
Associated Pain: Have you had any abdominal pain, particularly in the upper right side?
  • Upper right abdominal pain may point towards hepatobiliary causes such as gallstone disease, cholangitis, or hepatic congestion.
  • Relevance: A positive response may indicate gallstone-related jaundice or hepatic congestion. Absence of pain might suggest causes such as Gilbert’s syndrome or viral hepatitis.
Urine and Stool Colour: Have you experienced any dark urine or pale stools?
  • Dark urine and pale stools are classic signs of obstructive jaundice or hepatocellular disease.
  • Relevance: A positive response may indicate bile duct obstruction or liver disease. A negative response may lean away from obstructive pathology.
Systemic Symptoms: Any recent fevers, chills, or feeling unwell?
  • Fever and systemic symptoms may suggest an infectious cause, such as viral hepatitis or cholangitis.
  • Relevance: A positive response may indicate viral hepatitis or cholangitis. Lack of systemic symptoms may make infections less likely.
Past Medical History: Do you have any history of liver disease or heavy alcohol use?
  • This is important for identifying risk factors for chronic liver disease or alcoholic liver disease, which can cause jaundice.
  • Relevance: A positive history of alcohol use or liver disease can point towards alcoholic liver disease or chronic liver conditions as the cause. Absence of these factors may make these diagnoses less likely.
Drug History: Have you taken any new medications or supplements?
  • Some medications can cause drug-induced liver injury or cholestasis, leading to jaundice.
  • Relevance: A positive history of recent medication or supplement use could suggest drug-induced jaundice. No new medications or supplements may reduce this likelihood.
Family History: Any family history of liver disease or blood disorders?
  • Familial liver or blood disorders, such as haemochromatosis or Gilbert’s syndrome, may present with jaundice.
  • Relevance: A positive family history may indicate genetic causes such as Wilson's disease, haemochromatosis, or Gilbert’s syndrome. Lack of family history reduces the likelihood of hereditary causes.
Ideas, Concerns, and Expectations (ICE)
  • Exploring the patient’s ideas, concerns, and expectations is essential to understand their perspective and any specific fears or expectations for their care.
  • Relevance: This helps ensure that the consultation addresses the patient’s worries (e.g., fears about cancer or serious liver disease), their thoughts on potential causes, and what they hope to achieve from the consultation.
Examination Strategy
General Inspection: Inspect the skin and sclera for yellowing
  • Inspecting the skin and sclera provides an initial assessment of jaundice. Yellowing, particularly of the sclera, is a hallmark of jaundice due to elevated bilirubin levels.
  • Relevance: Visible jaundice suggests possible liver or biliary disease. If absent, it may indicate mild or fluctuating bilirubin levels, as seen in conditions like Gilbert’s syndrome.
Abdominal Palpation (Liver): Palpate the abdomen for hepatomegaly
  • Assessing for liver enlargement helps identify potential liver pathology. Hepatomegaly may indicate chronic liver disease, hepatitis, or malignancy.
  • Relevance: A positive finding (enlarged liver) could suggest hepatocellular causes, such as hepatitis or hepatocellular carcinoma. A negative finding may reduce the likelihood of significant liver disease.
Abdominal Palpation (Tenderness): Palpate the abdomen for tenderness, particularly in the right upper quadrant
  • Right upper quadrant tenderness can indicate hepatic or biliary inflammation, as seen in gallstone disease, cholangitis, or hepatitis.
  • Relevance: Pain upon palpation may suggest acute conditions like cholangitis, acute hepatitis, or cholecystitis. Absence of tenderness may lean towards non-inflammatory causes, such as chronic liver disease or Gilbert’s syndrome.
Ascites Assessment: Assess for ascites (abdominal distension and fluid wave test)
  • Ascites suggests advanced liver disease or malignancy, as fluid accumulation occurs with significant liver dysfunction or intra-abdominal cancer spread.
  • Relevance: A positive fluid wave may indicate decompensated liver disease or malignancy. A negative finding makes these severe complications less likely.
Hands Examination: Examine the hands for palmar erythema and clubbing
  • Palmar erythema and clubbing are associated with chronic liver disease and other systemic illnesses.
  • Relevance: Presence of palmar erythema or clubbing may indicate chronic liver conditions like cirrhosis. Their absence makes these chronic liver diagnoses less likely.
Eye Examination: Assess the eyes for Kayser-Fleischer rings
  • Kayser-Fleischer rings are deposits of copper in the cornea, associated with Wilson’s disease, a genetic condition that can cause hepatic dysfunction and jaundice.
  • Relevance: Positive finding strongly suggests Wilson’s disease, especially in younger patients with liver disease. Absence of this sign does not rule out other liver pathologies.
Chest and Skin Inspection: Inspect for spider naevi and gynaecomastia
  • Spider naevi and gynaecomastia are associated with chronic liver disease, especially cirrhosis due to hormonal imbalance.
  • Relevance: The presence of these signs may point towards chronic liver disease, especially cirrhosis. Their absence may make advanced liver disease less likely.
Lower Limb Examination: Assess the legs for peripheral oedema
  • Peripheral oedema may occur in advanced liver disease due to hypoalbuminaemia and portal hypertension.
  • Relevance: Positive findings may indicate chronic liver disease or heart failure. Absence of oedema makes these conditions less likely, particularly decompensated liver disease.
Red Flag Synthesis
Fever, chills, and right upper quadrant pain
  • Fever, chills, and right upper quadrant pain in a patient with jaundice can indicate ascending cholangitis, an infection of the bile ducts that can lead to sepsis if untreated.
  • Significance: This could indicate acute cholangitis, a potentially life-threatening emergency requiring urgent intervention, typically with IV antibiotics and possible biliary drainage.
Sudden onset of severe abdominal pain
  • Severe, acute abdominal pain in a jaundiced patient could suggest acute pancreatitis, particularly if radiating to the back, or a perforated viscus.
  • Significance: This presentation may signify acute pancreatitis, gallstone complications, or perforation, all of which may require urgent imaging and management.
Unintentional weight loss and anorexia
  • Weight loss and loss of appetite can indicate a malignancy, especially in the liver, pancreas, or biliary system.
  • Significance: This could signify a possible underlying malignancy, such as hepatocellular carcinoma, cholangiocarcinoma, or pancreatic cancer, necessitating prompt imaging and oncological assessment.
Altered mental status or confusion
  • Confusion or altered mental status in a jaundiced patient can indicate hepatic encephalopathy due to liver failure or a build-up of toxins, especially ammonia.
  • Significance: This could signify decompensated liver disease or fulminant hepatic failure, requiring urgent investigation and management to prevent further neurological decline.
Signs of bleeding or easy bruising
  • Spontaneous bruising or signs of bleeding, such as gum bleeding, suggest coagulopathy, which can occur in liver failure as a result of decreased clotting factors.
  • Significance: This could indicate advanced liver disease with impaired coagulation, which requires prompt correction and assessment to prevent serious haemorrhage.
Dark urine and pale stools
  • Dark urine and pale stools often suggest obstructive jaundice, commonly caused by biliary obstruction or malignancy in the liver or pancreas.
  • Significance: This finding can indicate biliary obstruction from conditions such as pancreatic cancer or cholangiocarcinoma, which need further investigation through imaging and possible surgical intervention.
Investigations Strategy & Interpretation
Liver function tests (LFTs)
  • LFTs measure bilirubin, liver enzymes (ALT, AST, ALP, GGT), and albumin levels, helping to determine if jaundice is hepatocellular, cholestatic, or haemolytic in origin.
  • Result Significance: Elevated ALT and AST suggest hepatocellular injury, while elevated ALP and GGT indicate cholestasis. Bilirubin elevation confirms jaundice, and isolated raised bilirubin may indicate Gilbert’s syndrome.
Full Blood Count (FBC)
  • A FBC can reveal signs of anaemia, haemolysis, or infection, which may all contribute to jaundice.
  • Result Significance: Low haemoglobin with reticulocytosis may indicate haemolytic anaemia. Leukocytosis could suggest infection or cholangitis. Normal findings help exclude these causes.
Coagulation profile (INR, PT)
  • A coagulation profile assesses clotting function, often impaired in liver disease due to reduced synthesis of clotting factors.
  • Result Significance: Prolonged PT/INR may indicate liver failure. Normal findings may reduce suspicion of severe hepatic dysfunction.
Viral hepatitis serology
  • Hepatitis A, B, and C serology is crucial to rule out infectious causes of jaundice, as viral hepatitis is a common aetiology.
  • Result Significance: Positive serology suggests viral hepatitis, guiding infection control and potential antiviral treatment. Negative results reduce the likelihood of viral hepatitis.
Autoimmune markers (ANA, ASMA, AMA)
  • Testing for autoimmune markers helps identify autoimmune hepatitis or primary biliary cirrhosis, especially in unexplained chronic liver disease.
  • Result Significance: Positive results for ANA or ASMA suggest autoimmune hepatitis, while AMA positivity indicates primary biliary cirrhosis. Negative markers help exclude these diagnoses.
Abdominal ultrasound
  • Ultrasound is a non-invasive imaging tool that assesses liver texture, biliary dilation, and gallbladder pathology, helping to identify obstructive causes or malignancy.
  • Result Significance: Findings of biliary dilation suggest obstruction, possibly due to gallstones or pancreatic masses. Hepatic lesions may indicate malignancy. A normal ultrasound may help exclude significant obstruction.
CT abdomen
  • CT imaging provides detailed visualisation of the liver, biliary tree, pancreas, and nearby structures, helpful in assessing malignancies or complex hepatobiliary disease.
  • Result Significance: Positive findings may include tumours, biliary dilation, or evidence of liver disease. A clear CT scan may help rule out significant structural abnormalities.
Serum ceruloplasmin and 24-hour urinary copper
  • These tests are performed to investigate Wilson’s disease, particularly in younger patients presenting with unexplained jaundice or liver dysfunction.
  • Result Significance: Low ceruloplasmin and elevated urinary copper are suggestive of Wilson’s disease. Normal levels make Wilson’s disease less likely.
Genetic testing for hereditary haemochromatosis (HFE gene)
  • HFE gene testing is useful in cases of iron overload or suspected haemochromatosis, which can cause hepatic iron accumulation and subsequent jaundice.
  • Result Significance: A positive HFE mutation indicates hereditary haemochromatosis. A negative result helps rule out this genetic disorder.
Evidence-Based Diagnosis (EBDx)
Fever, chills, and right upper quadrant pain (suggestive of Cholangitis)
  • Charcot's triad—fever, jaundice, and right upper quadrant pain—has a high specificity for cholangitis, though sensitivity is limited. If present, it supports a diagnosis of acute cholangitis.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 36%, Specificity: 95%, Positive Likelihood Ratio (LR+): 7.2.
  • Significance: Positive findings increase the likelihood of cholangitis, necessitating urgent treatment. Negative findings, however, do not completely rule out this diagnosis.
Dark urine and pale stools (suggestive of Obstructive Jaundice)
  • These symptoms indicate a reduced excretion of bilirubin in the bile, common in obstructive jaundice due to biliary obstruction or hepatobiliary malignancy.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 90%, Specificity: 70% in cases of biliary obstruction.
  • Significance: Positive findings strongly suggest an obstructive aetiology. The absence of these symptoms makes obstructive jaundice less likely, particularly if bilirubin levels are minimally raised.
Elevated ALT and AST (suggestive of Hepatocellular Injury)
  • ALT and AST elevations are highly sensitive markers for hepatocellular injury. ALT is typically higher than AST in viral hepatitis, while AST is elevated disproportionately in alcoholic liver disease.
  • Evidence: ALT Sensitivity: ~90%, Specificity: 80% for acute hepatocellular injury.
  • Significance: Elevated levels support hepatocellular causes such as hepatitis or hepatocellular carcinoma. Normal levels make these diagnoses less likely.
Elevated Alkaline Phosphatase (ALP) and Gamma-Glutamyl Transferase (GGT) (suggestive of Cholestasis)
  • ALP and GGT are commonly raised in cholestasis. ALP elevations combined with jaundice suggest cholestatic liver disease or biliary obstruction, especially if GGT is also elevated.
  • Evidence: ALP Sensitivity: ~80%, Specificity: 70% for cholestatic jaundice.
  • Significance: Positive findings support cholestasis or biliary obstruction. Absence of raised ALP or GGT can make these causes less likely.
Positive Viral Hepatitis Serology
  • Positive hepatitis serology (e.g., HBsAg, anti-HCV antibodies) is diagnostic for viral hepatitis as a cause of jaundice.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity and Specificity >95% for hepatitis B and C serologies.
  • Significance: Positive serology confirms viral hepatitis, while negative results rule out hepatitis as the cause of jaundice, guiding further investigations.
Abdominal Ultrasound (assessment for biliary obstruction or malignancy)
  • Ultrasound is a first-line imaging study to assess for biliary ductal dilation, gallstones, and liver texture. It is useful in distinguishing intrahepatic vs. extrahepatic causes of jaundice.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 85%, Specificity: 90% for biliary obstruction, with strong predictive value for gallstones if visualised.
  • Significance: Positive findings such as biliary dilation suggest obstructive causes, while a normal ultrasound reduces suspicion of significant biliary obstruction or malignancy.
Serum Ceruloplasmin (for Wilson’s Disease)
  • Low serum ceruloplasmin, particularly in younger patients with jaundice, suggests Wilson’s disease, a rare cause of hepatic failure and neuropsychiatric symptoms.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: ~85%, Specificity: 95% for Wilson’s disease.
  • Significance: A low ceruloplasmin level supports the diagnosis of Wilson’s disease, especially if urinary copper is elevated. Normal ceruloplasmin makes this diagnosis less likely.
Diagnostic Timeout & Cognitive Biases

Awareness of common thinking errors is essential to improve diagnostic accuracy and patient safety when assessing a patient with jaundice. Before finalising a diagnosis, it is good practice for clinicians at all levels to take a 'diagnostic timeout' to challenge their own reasoning.

  • Anchoring Bias: The tendency to anchor on a patient's known history, such as heavy alcohol use, and prematurely conclude alcoholic liver disease is the sole cause. This might lead to missing a superimposed, acute process like gallstone-induced pancreatitis or a newly developed hepatocellular carcinoma. A key question to ask is: "Could there be a second, more acute problem here?"
  • Confirmation Bias: Selectively focusing on evidence that supports an initial hypothesis. For example, in a young patient with mild jaundice, a clinician might focus on the lack of pain and normal LFTs to confirm Gilbert's syndrome, while downplaying a new family history of autoimmune disease that might suggest autoimmune hepatitis.
  • Search Satisficing: Stopping the diagnostic process once a plausible cause is found. For instance, identifying gallstones on an ultrasound might lead to a diagnosis of choledocholithiasis, but this could cause the clinician to miss a concurrent, more sinister finding like a small pancreatic head tumour which is the true cause of the obstruction.
  • Over-reliance on Normal Tests: Placing excessive faith in a single negative test. A normal initial abdominal ultrasound does not definitively rule out a small common bile duct stone or early malignancy. If clinical suspicion for obstruction remains high (e.g., due to a cholestatic LFT pattern and pale stools), further imaging like an MRCP may be warranted.
Illustrative Management Pathways

For educational purposes, this section describes the typical principles of management for key diagnoses. These are not prescriptive instructions. All management must be guided by official UK guidelines, local formularies, and senior clinical advice.

Obstructive Jaundice (e.g., Gallstones, Malignancy)

The management of obstructive jaundice is focused on identifying the level and cause of the obstruction and relieving it. Initial investigation is with an abdominal ultrasound. If this shows dilated bile ducts, further imaging with Magnetic Resonance Cholangiopancreatography (MRCP) or CT is usually required to define the anatomy. Patients with suspected gallstone obstruction are referred to gastroenterology for Endoscopic Retrograde Cholangiopancreatography (ERCP) to remove the stone. If malignancy is suspected, the patient is referred urgently via the two-week wait pathway to the relevant multidisciplinary team (MDT), usually the hepatobiliary (HPB) team.

Ascending Cholangitis

This is a medical emergency requiring immediate hospital admission. Management is guided by sepsis protocols ('Sepsis Six' in the UK), including taking blood cultures, administering broad-spectrum intravenous antibiotics, and providing intravenous fluid resuscitation. The definitive treatment is to relieve the biliary obstruction, which is almost always performed via urgent ERCP once the patient is stabilised.

Hepatocellular Jaundice (e.g., Viral, Alcoholic, Drug-Induced)

Management is centred on identifying and treating the underlying cause of the liver injury. This involves a detailed history (especially alcohol and drugs), viral serology, and an autoimmune screen. For drug-induced liver injury, the offending drug must be stopped. For alcoholic hepatitis, treatment involves supportive care and complete alcohol cessation, with some severe cases considered for corticosteroid therapy. Acute viral hepatitis is typically managed supportively, with referral to hepatology or infectious disease specialists. All cases of significant hepatocellular jaundice require specialist input.

Pre-hepatic Jaundice (e.g., Haemolysis, Gilbert's Syndrome)

Once life-threatening causes have been excluded, management focuses on the underlying cause. If haemolysis is suspected (indicated by anaemia, reticulocytosis, and raised unconjugated bilirubin), referral to a haematologist is necessary to investigate the cause. For Gilbert's syndrome, once confirmed by the characteristic pattern of isolated unconjugated hyperbilirubinaemia (often with normal LFTs and FBC), the key management step is providing a clear explanation and reassurance about the benign and hereditary nature of the condition.

Reflection & Learning

These brief cases illustrate the application of clinical reasoning to different presentations of jaundice. They are for educational purposes only.

Case 1: Painless Jaundice in the GP Surgery

A 72-year-old man presents to his GP with a 3-week history of progressive, painless yellowing of his skin and eyes. He has also noticed his urine is very dark, his stools are pale, and he has lost about 5kg in weight without trying. The problem representation is painless obstructive jaundice with constitutional symptoms in an elderly male. This presentation is highly concerning for a malignancy, most likely pancreatic cancer or cholangiocarcinoma. The GP makes an urgent two-week wait referral to the hepatobiliary service and arranges initial bloods, including LFTs, and an urgent abdominal ultrasound.

Case 2: Painful Jaundice in the Emergency Department

A 45-year-old woman presents to the Emergency Department with severe right upper quadrant pain, fever, and new-onset jaundice. On examination, she is febrile (38.5°C), tachycardic, and tender in the RUQ. The problem representation is a patient with Charcot's triad (RUQ pain, fever, jaundice), which is the classic presentation of acute ascending cholangitis, a medical emergency. The immediate pathway involves sepsis management with IV fluids and antibiotics, urgent blood tests, and an immediate referral to the on-call gastroenterology and surgical teams for consideration of emergency biliary decompression via ERCP.

Case 3: Incidental Finding in a Young Adult

A 21-year-old university student presents for a routine check-up. He mentions that his friends sometimes say he looks 'a bit yellow', especially when he has a cold or is stressed during exams. He feels otherwise perfectly well. Blood tests show normal FBC, normal ALT, AST, and ALP, but a mildly elevated isolated unconjugated bilirubin. The problem representation is intermittent, asymptomatic, isolated unconjugated hyperbilirubinaemia in a healthy young person. This is the classic picture of Gilbert's syndrome. Management involves reassurance, explaining the benign nature of the condition, and advising that no further investigation or treatment is required.
