Facial Pain Diagnostic Reasoning Reference
Context: Facial Pain Assessment
Overview

Facial pain is a frequent and diagnostically challenging presentation in both primary and secondary care. The sensory innervation of the face is complex, and the potential causes of pain are numerous, ranging from common, self-limiting conditions like sinusitis to life-threatening emergencies such as Temporal (Giant Cell) Arteritis. The clinician's primary challenge is to systematically differentiate between these possibilities, ensuring that critical diagnoses are identified promptly while avoiding over-investigation for more benign aetiologies.

The initial assessment must be thorough, focusing on a detailed history and a targeted physical examination to elicit red flag features. These features help to identify patients who require urgent specialist referral and intervention to prevent serious morbidity, such as permanent vision loss from temporal arteritis or neurological complications from a space-occupying lesion. This educational module provides a structured framework for the clinical reasoning process in a patient presenting with facial pain. For definitive, real-time management protocols, users must always consult official UK guidelines from authoritative bodies such as NICE and relevant Royal Colleges.

Pathophysiology/Basic Science

Understanding the underlying mechanism of pain generation is fundamental to forming a differential diagnosis for facial pain. The pain can originate from various structures and pathways:

  • Neuropathic Pain: This arises from damage or dysfunction of the nerves themselves. The classic example is Trigeminal Neuralgia, where vascular compression of the trigeminal nerve root is thought to cause focal demyelination. This leads to ectopic nerve impulses and hyperexcitability, resulting in paroxysms of severe, "electric shock-like" pain. A similar mechanism can occur from demyelinating plaques in Multiple Sclerosis.
  • Inflammatory Pain: Inflammation of facial structures is a very common cause. In Sinusitis, inflammation of the sinus mucosa and obstruction of the sinus ostia lead to a build-up of pressure and fluid, causing a dull, constant ache. In a Dental Abscess, bacterial infection leads to pulpitis and inflammation of the periapical tissues, stimulating nociceptors and causing intense, throbbing pain.
  • Vascular Pain: Inflammation of blood vessels (vasculitis) can cause pain through ischaemia of the tissues they supply. In Temporal (Giant Cell) Arteritis, granulomatous inflammation of medium and large arteries (like the temporal artery) compromises blood flow, leading to symptoms like headache, scalp tenderness, and jaw claudication (pain on chewing).
  • Referred Pain: Pain can be perceived in a location distant from its origin. While uncommon, Acute Coronary Syndrome can sometimes cause pain referred to the jaw. More commonly, dysfunction of the Temporomandibular Joint (TMJ), involving the joint capsule, ligaments, or muscles of mastication, can cause pain that is felt more diffusely across the face.
  • Neoplastic Pain: Pain from a tumour can be caused by direct infiltration or compression of sensory nerves or other pain-sensitive structures. For example, a Nasopharyngeal Carcinoma or a Salivary Gland Tumour can invade the trigeminal nerve or its branches, causing persistent, progressive facial pain or numbness.
Differential Diagnosis (DDx) Framework

A structured approach to the differential diagnosis of facial pain is essential to ensure life-threatening conditions are prioritised.

Must not miss!
  • Temporal arteritis (GCA): New-onset headache and jaw claudication in patients over 50, posing a risk of blindness.
  • Orbital cellulitis: A serious infection of the tissues behind the orbital septum, presenting with pain, swelling, proptosis, and reduced eye movements.
  • Acute Glaucoma: Sudden, severe eye pain with blurred vision, halos around lights, and a red eye.
  • Acute Coronary Syndrome: Can rarely present with referred pain to the jaw or face, typically with other ischaemic symptoms.
  • Nasopharyngeal cancer: May present with unilateral facial pain, numbness, or ear symptoms and is often a late diagnosis.
  • Salivary gland tumour: Can cause localised swelling and pain, sometimes with facial nerve involvement.
Common causes
  • Sinusitis: Facial pressure or a dull ache, often worse on bending forward, and may be associated with nasal congestion or discharge.
  • Dental pain: A very common cause, often described as throbbing, which may be triggered by hot, cold, or sweet stimuli.
  • Herpes Zoster: Presents with a burning, unilateral, dermatomal pain which typically precedes the characteristic vesicular rash.
  • Trigeminal neuralgia: Characterised by sudden, severe, electric shock-like pains in the distribution of the trigeminal nerve, often triggered by light touch.
  • COVID-19: Can be associated with headache and facial pain as part of the viral prodrome.
Easily missed
  • Psychogenic: Facial pain without an identifiable organic cause, often with features of a somatoform disorder.
  • Salivary gland disorder: Non-neoplastic causes like sialadenitis or sialolithiasis, causing pain and swelling, especially around mealtimes.
  • Multiple sclerosis: Can cause trigeminal neuralgia, especially in younger patients, due to demyelination in the brainstem.
Other Differential Diagnoses to consider
  • Cluster headache: Often presents with unilateral facial pain, typically around the eye.
  • Migraine: Facial pain can be associated with migrainous neuralgia.
  • Carotid artery dissection: Rare but important cause of facial pain due to vascular involvement.
  • Temporomandibular joint (TMJ) disorder: Associated with jaw pain and referred facial pain.
  • Neoplasm (e.g., parotid tumour): Could present with swelling and facial pain.
  • Peripheral nerve injury: Post-traumatic or iatrogenic facial nerve involvement.
History Taking
**Patient’s Description**: How can I help you today?

Open-ended questions such as “Can you tell me more about what brought you here today?” allow the patient to describe their symptoms in their own words. This helps gather initial clues and guide further questions.

Relevance: Allows the patient to highlight their main concern, which may reveal key symptoms like the severity, location, and quality of the pain.
When did the pain start?
  • Understanding the onset of the pain helps determine if the condition is acute or chronic.
  • Relevance: Acute onset may suggest conditions like sinusitis, acute dental infections, or acute glaucoma, while a chronic or insidious onset could indicate trigeminal neuralgia, neoplasms, or psychogenic pain.
Can you describe the type of pain? (sharp, dull, throbbing, electric-like)
  • The quality of pain helps differentiate between causes.
  • Relevance: Sharp or stabbing pain could indicate trigeminal neuralgia or herpes zoster. Throbbing pain may point towards sinusitis or dental pain, while electric-like pain is classically seen in neuralgias.
Do you notice any specific triggers for the pain? (e.g., chewing, cold exposure, light)
  • Identifying triggers can narrow down the cause of facial pain.
  • Relevance: Pain triggered by chewing may suggest TMJ disorder or dental causes. Light sensitivity may point to acute glaucoma, while pain triggered by cold exposure could suggest trigeminal neuralgia.
Have you had any associated symptoms? (e.g., visual changes, fever, rash)
  • Associated symptoms can provide crucial clues for differentiating between diagnoses.
  • Relevance: Visual changes could suggest acute glaucoma or temporal arteritis, while fever may indicate sinusitis, orbital cellulitis, or dental abscess. A rash, especially in a dermatomal pattern, strongly suggests herpes zoster.
Do you have any history of sinus problems, dental issues, or autoimmune diseases?
  • Past medical history can help identify predisposing conditions.
  • Relevance: A history of sinusitis or dental problems increases the likelihood of these being the cause. Autoimmune conditions may raise suspicion of vasculitis or temporal arteritis.
**Ideas, Concerns, and Expectations (ICE)**
  • Asking about the patient's ideas, concerns, and expectations helps to address their perspective and ensure their concerns are acknowledged during the consultation.
  • Relevance: Helps explore if the patient is worried about serious conditions like cancer or an infection, and clarifies if they are expecting treatment such as antibiotics or pain relief. This ensures patient-centred care.
Examination Strategy
Inspection of the face
  • Observe for visible signs such as swelling, redness, asymmetry, rashes, or vesicular eruptions.
  • Relevance: Swelling and erythema suggest infections like sinusitis or dental abscesses. Vesicular rash in a dermatomal distribution is characteristic of herpes zoster. Facial asymmetry may indicate a tumour or nerve involvement (e.g., Bell’s palsy).
Palpation of the sinuses (frontal and maxillary)
  • Gently palpate the frontal and maxillary sinuses for tenderness, which indicates possible inflammation or infection.
  • Relevance: Tenderness over the sinuses suggests sinusitis. A lack of tenderness makes sinusitis less likely but doesn’t completely rule it out.
Examination of the temporomandibular joint (TMJ)
  • Palpate the TMJ while the patient opens and closes their mouth, listening for clicks or crepitus and asking about pain.
  • Relevance: Pain, crepitus, or clicking may indicate TMJ dysfunction, a potential cause of facial pain. If the TMJ examination is normal, other causes of pain should be considered.
Examination of the cranial nerves (particularly V, VII)
  • Examine the trigeminal (V) and facial (VII) nerves. For trigeminal nerve, check sensation across all three branches (ophthalmic, maxillary, mandibular). For the facial nerve, assess symmetry of facial movements.
  • Relevance: Trigeminal nerve dysfunction may suggest trigeminal neuralgia or multiple sclerosis. Facial nerve weakness may indicate Bell’s palsy or a space-occupying lesion affecting the facial nerve.
Inspection of the oral cavity and teeth
  • Inspect the teeth, gums, and oral mucosa for signs of dental infection, abscesses, or other lesions.
  • Relevance: Dental pain or abscesses are common causes of facial pain. Oral ulcers or lesions could indicate malignancy or infections like herpes simplex.
Examination of the eyes (visual acuity, fundoscopy, and checking for pupil reactivity)
  • Assess visual acuity, inspect the optic disc with fundoscopy, and check for pupil reactivity to light to rule out eye-related causes of facial pain.
  • Relevance: Reduced visual acuity, an abnormal optic disc, or a sluggish pupil response may indicate acute glaucoma or optic neuritis. These findings would necessitate urgent ophthalmological review.
Palpation of the temporal arteries
  • Palpate the temporal arteries for tenderness, thickening, or reduced pulsation.
  • Relevance: Tender, thickened, or pulseless temporal arteries raise suspicion of temporal arteritis (Giant Cell Arteritis), which requires urgent treatment to prevent complications such as vision loss.
Palpation of the salivary glands
  • Palpate the parotid and submandibular glands for swelling, tenderness, or the presence of stones (sialoliths).
  • Relevance: Swelling and tenderness may indicate sialadenitis or a salivary gland tumour. The presence of a stone may suggest sialolithiasis as the cause of the pain.
Examination for neck stiffness
  • Assess for any neck stiffness or resistance to passive neck flexion.
  • Relevance: Neck stiffness could indicate meningitis, particularly if associated with fever or other systemic symptoms, though this is a rare cause of facial pain.
Red Flag Synthesis
Visual disturbances (blurred vision, loss of vision)
  • Visual disturbances accompanying facial pain are concerning for conditions affecting the eyes or optic nerves.
  • Significance: This could indicate acute angle-closure glaucoma or optic neuritis, both of which are ophthalmic emergencies requiring urgent intervention to prevent permanent vision loss.
Sudden, severe headache or "thunderclap" headache
  • A sudden, severe headache may be associated with facial pain in life-threatening conditions like subarachnoid haemorrhage.
  • Significance: This presentation could indicate a subarachnoid haemorrhage, a neurosurgical emergency, or temporal arteritis, both requiring immediate assessment.
Fever, swelling, or erythema over the face or periorbital region
  • Facial swelling and redness with fever suggests an infectious process involving deeper structures.
  • Significance: These symptoms could indicate orbital cellulitis, which can spread to the brain and requires urgent antibiotic treatment, or a dental abscess progressing to cellulitis or sepsis.
Pain on palpation of the temporal arteries or new-onset headache in older patients
  • Tenderness over the temporal arteries with new headache in individuals over 50 suggests inflammation of the arteries.
  • Significance: This could indicate temporal arteritis (Giant Cell Arteritis), which requires urgent treatment with steroids to prevent complications such as permanent vision loss.
Unexplained weight loss or night sweats
  • Unintentional weight loss or night sweats raise concerns about systemic conditions or malignancy.
  • Significance: This could be a sign of nasopharyngeal cancer, salivary gland tumours, or other malignancies, necessitating further investigation such as imaging or biopsy.
Neurological symptoms (weakness, numbness, difficulty speaking)
  • Facial pain accompanied by neurological symptoms suggests involvement of the central nervous system or cranial nerves.
  • Significance: This could indicate a stroke, multiple sclerosis, or a space-occupying lesion like a brain tumour, all of which require urgent evaluation.
Jaw claudication or difficulty chewing
  • Jaw claudication is pain or fatigue in the jaw muscles while chewing, associated with vascular insufficiency.
  • Significance: This is a key symptom of temporal arteritis (Giant Cell Arteritis), which requires prompt treatment to prevent serious complications such as vision loss.
Unilateral facial paralysis or drooping
  • Unilateral facial paralysis may indicate damage to the facial nerve or a more serious underlying pathology.
  • Significance: This could signify Bell’s palsy, but more concerningly, it could be due to a stroke, tumour, or infection (e.g., Lyme disease, herpes zoster). Immediate investigation is needed to rule out serious causes.
Investigations Strategy & Interpretation
Full blood count (FBC)
  • A full blood count can help identify signs of infection or inflammation.
  • Result Significance: Raised white cell count (leukocytosis) suggests an infectious cause such as sinusitis or orbital cellulitis. A normal result makes infection less likely.
Erythrocyte sedimentation rate (ESR) and C-reactive protein (CRP)
  • Both ESR and CRP are markers of inflammation and can help identify inflammatory or infectious causes of facial pain.
  • Result Significance: Elevated ESR and CRP suggest systemic inflammation, which can point to conditions such as temporal arteritis, orbital cellulitis, or a deep-seated infection like dental abscess. A normal ESR or CRP makes these less likely.
Temporal artery biopsy
  • Temporal artery biopsy is indicated if temporal arteritis (Giant Cell Arteritis) is suspected.
  • Result Significance: A positive biopsy (showing inflammation of the temporal artery) confirms the diagnosis of temporal arteritis, while a negative result may suggest alternative diagnoses but does not completely rule out the condition.
Sinus X-ray or CT scan of the sinuses
  • Imaging of the sinuses can help identify sinusitis, particularly if the diagnosis is unclear from the clinical presentation.
  • Result Significance: Positive findings (such as mucosal thickening or fluid levels) confirm sinusitis, while negative findings may help rule it out and prompt consideration of other causes.
MRI or CT scan of the brain
  • Neuroimaging is essential if there is suspicion of serious neurological conditions such as tumours, multiple sclerosis, or stroke.
  • Result Significance: A positive MRI or CT finding may indicate a mass lesion (e.g., brain tumour), demyelination (e.g., multiple sclerosis), or vascular abnormalities (e.g., stroke). A normal scan helps rule out these causes and may point towards benign conditions.
Fundoscopy
  • A fundoscopy should be performed if there is concern about eye involvement or raised intracranial pressure.
  • Result Significance: Papilloedema suggests raised intracranial pressure, possibly due to a mass lesion or intracranial pathology. A normal fundus examination reduces the likelihood of these diagnoses.
Lumbar puncture
  • A lumbar puncture may be indicated if meningitis, subarachnoid haemorrhage, or other central nervous system infections are suspected.
  • Result Significance: Positive cerebrospinal fluid findings (e.g., elevated white cells, xanthochromia) may suggest meningitis or subarachnoid haemorrhage. A negative result reduces the likelihood of these serious conditions.
Blood cultures
  • Blood cultures are indicated if sepsis or bacteraemia is suspected, particularly in cases of severe infection such as orbital cellulitis or deep dental abscess.
  • Result Significance: Positive cultures confirm bacteraemia and necessitate urgent antibiotic treatment. Negative cultures make systemic infection less likely.
Electrolytes, renal function, and blood glucose
  • Basic metabolic panel and blood glucose levels can help assess for systemic causes of facial pain, especially in patients with diabetes or electrolyte imbalances.
  • Result Significance: Abnormal results may indicate underlying conditions such as diabetic ketoacidosis or renal insufficiency, which may exacerbate other causes of facial pain.
Evidence-Based Diagnosis (EBDx)
Fever in Orbital Cellulitis
  • Fever is a common symptom of orbital cellulitis, a serious infection of the soft tissues surrounding the eye.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 83%, Specificity: 89%, Likelihood Ratio: 7.5.
  • Significance: A positive finding of fever increases the likelihood of orbital cellulitis, while its absence makes the diagnosis less likely but does not rule it out.
Jaw Claudication in Temporal Arteritis (Giant Cell Arteritis)
  • Jaw claudication, or pain with chewing, is a classic symptom of temporal arteritis, related to ischemia of the masseter muscles.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 34%, Specificity: 99%, Likelihood Ratio: 34.0.
  • Significance: The high specificity and likelihood ratio mean that the presence of jaw claudication is highly suggestive of temporal arteritis, though its absence does not exclude the diagnosis.
Tenderness Over Sinuses in Sinusitis
  • Tenderness over the frontal or maxillary sinuses is a key clinical sign in diagnosing sinusitis.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 70%, Specificity: 76%, Likelihood Ratio: 2.9.
  • Significance: Positive findings increase the likelihood of sinusitis, while negative findings suggest other causes of facial pain should be considered.
Elevated ESR in Temporal Arteritis
  • An elevated ESR is commonly associated with temporal arteritis, reflecting the inflammatory nature of the condition.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 85%, Specificity: 30%, Likelihood Ratio: 1.2.
  • Significance: A raised ESR supports the diagnosis of temporal arteritis, though it is not specific and may be raised in other inflammatory or infectious conditions.
MRI Findings in Trigeminal Neuralgia
  • MRI can reveal compression of the trigeminal nerve by vascular loops, which is often the cause of trigeminal neuralgia.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 95%, Specificity: 88%, Likelihood Ratio: 7.9.
  • Significance: A positive MRI result strongly supports the diagnosis of trigeminal neuralgia, while a negative result makes it less likely but does not completely exclude it.
CT Scan in Acute Glaucoma
  • A CT scan can help identify features of acute angle-closure glaucoma, such as increased intraocular pressure or anatomical predisposition.
  • Significance: A positive result on CT supports the diagnosis of acute glaucoma, while negative results suggest that alternative causes of facial pain should be considered.
Diagnostic Timeout & Cognitive Biases

Awareness of common thinking errors is essential to improve diagnostic accuracy when assessing a patient with facial pain. Before finalising a diagnosis, it is good practice for clinicians at all levels to take a 'diagnostic timeout' to challenge their own reasoning.

  • Anchoring Bias: The tendency to lock onto an early, common diagnosis (e.g., "dental pain" or "sinusitis") and fail to reconsider when new information arises. For instance, attributing an elderly patient's facial pain to their known sinus issues while downplaying new-onset visual symptoms that could indicate temporal arteritis. A key question to ask is: "What is the most dangerous cause of this presentation, and have I confidently excluded it?"
  • Confirmation Bias: Selectively seeking or over-emphasising evidence that supports an initial hypothesis. For example, focusing on tenderness over the maxillary sinus to confirm sinusitis, while ignoring a subtle dermatomal rash that would point towards pre-eruptive herpes zoster.
  • Atypical Presentations: A significant pitfall is forgetting that serious diseases can present in non-classical ways. Temporal arteritis can occur without a headache or with a normal ESR. Trigeminal neuralgia can initially present as a dull ache before evolving into the classic lancinating pain. A high index of suspicion is crucial, especially in high-risk populations.
  • Over-reliance on Normal Tests: Placing undue confidence in a negative test result. A normal dental X-ray does not rule out pain of dental origin (e.g., cracked tooth, pulpitis), and a normal ESR/CRP does not definitively exclude GCA, particularly if the patient has already started taking steroids. Clinical correlation is paramount.
Illustrative Management Pathways

For educational purposes, this section describes the typical principles of management for key diagnoses. These are not prescriptive instructions. All management must be guided by official UK guidelines, local formularies, and senior clinical advice.

Temporal Arteritis (Giant Cell Arteritis)

This is a medical emergency. If there is a high clinical suspicion of GCA (e.g., patient >50, new-onset headache, jaw claudication, tender temporal artery), treatment should be initiated immediately, without waiting for investigation results, to prevent irreversible vision loss. The management pathway involves immediate high-dose oral prednisolone (e.g., 60mg daily) and urgent referral to a rheumatologist or the appropriate local specialist service for confirmation (e.g., temporal artery biopsy or ultrasound) and ongoing management.

Trigeminal Neuralgia

First-line treatment, as per NICE guidelines, is the anticonvulsant carbamazepine. It should be started at a low dose and titrated up slowly to an effective level. Patients should be warned about potential side effects and the importance of not stopping the medication abruptly. If first-line treatment is ineffective or not tolerated, referral to a neurologist or a specialist pain management service is indicated for consideration of other medical therapies or surgical options.

Acute Bacterial Sinusitis

The majority of cases are viral and self-limiting. Management focuses on reassurance, adequate analgesia (paracetamol, ibuprofen), and advising on nasal saline or decongestants. According to NICE guidelines, a prescription for antibiotics (e.g., phenoxymethylpenicillin) should only be considered for patients with symptoms for over 10 days, purulent nasal discharge, severe localised pain, or systemic signs of severe infection. Hospital admission should be considered if there are signs of periorbital or orbital cellulitis.

Acute Dental Pain / Abscess

The mainstay of management is analgesia and prompt referral to a dentist for definitive treatment. Clinicians can provide advice on simple analgesia (paracetamol and NSAIDs). Antibiotics are not routinely indicated unless there is evidence of spreading infection (cellulitis) or systemic symptoms. The definitive treatment will be provided by a dental professional and may involve drainage, root canal treatment, or tooth extraction.

Reflection & Learning

These brief cases illustrate the application of clinical reasoning to different facial pain presentations. They are for educational purposes only.

Case 1: Suspected GCA in the Community

A 76-year-old woman calls her GP complaining of a 1-week history of a new headache on the right side of her head and pain in her jaw when she eats her toast in the morning. She also mentions her scalp feels tender when she brushes her hair. The problem representation is an elderly patient with a new-onset headache, jaw claudication, and scalp tenderness. This is highly suspicious for Giant Cell Arteritis. The GP advises the patient to attend the surgery immediately for assessment and blood tests (ESR/CRP) and arranges for same-day discussion with the on-call rheumatologist, starting high-dose steroids immediately after the consultation.

Case 2: Suspected Trigeminal Neuralgia

A 60-year-old man presents describing a 3-month history of sudden, intensely painful "electric shocks" on the left side of his face, lasting a few seconds each time. They are triggered by smiling, talking, and a cold breeze on his face. He is otherwise well. The problem representation is a man over 50 with classic paroxysmal, triggered, lancinating facial pain in a nerve distribution, highly consistent with trigeminal neuralgia. After a normal neurological examination, he is started on a low dose of carbamazepine with a plan for slow titration and follow-up.

Case 3: A Diagnostic Re-evaluation

A 45-year-old patient presents with a persistent, dull ache over their right cheek for two months. They have seen their dentist twice, who could find no clear cause. The pain is now associated with a feeling of numbness over the cheek. The problem representation is a middle-aged patient with progressive, persistent facial pain and new neurological signs (numbness) that has not responded to primary care treatment for a presumed dental cause. The presence of neurological signs is a red flag, prompting an urgent referral for specialist assessment and neuroimaging (MRI), which could reveal an underlying cause such as a neoplasm compressing a branch of the trigeminal nerve.
