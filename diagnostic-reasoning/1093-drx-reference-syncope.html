Syncope Diagnostic Reasoning Reference
Context: Syncope Assessment
Overview

Syncope, or fainting, is a common and challenging clinical presentation, responsible for a significant number of primary care consultations and emergency department visits. It is defined as a transient loss of consciousness (TLoC) due to a brief period of global cerebral hypoperfusion, characterised by a rapid onset, short duration, and spontaneous, complete recovery. The primary challenge for any clinician is to effectively differentiate between common, benign causes (like a simple faint) and those that signal a life-threatening underlying condition, such as a dangerous cardiac arrhythmia or a massive pulmonary embolism.

This educational module is designed to provide a foundational understanding of the clinical reasoning process for syncope. For definitive, real-time management protocols, users must refer to official UK guidelines from authoritative bodies such as NICE (e.g., CG109 for Transient Loss of Consciousness in Adults) and the Resuscitation Council UK.

Pathophysiology/Basic Science

The final common pathway for all forms of syncope is a temporary drop in blood flow to the brain. The mechanisms that lead to this transient global cerebral hypoperfusion can be grouped into three main categories:

  • Reflex (Neurally-Mediated) Syncope: This is the most common cause of fainting. It results from a reflex response that causes inappropriate vasodilation (widening of blood vessels) and/or bradycardia (slowing of the heart rate). The classic example is vasovagal syncope, often triggered by emotional distress, pain, or prolonged standing (the 'common faint'). The reflex involves a complex feedback loop between the heart, brain, and blood vessels (e.g., the Bezold-Jarisch reflex), leading to a sudden drop in blood pressure and heart rate, which reduces cerebral perfusion and causes loss of consciousness.
  • Orthostatic Hypotension: This occurs when the autonomic nervous system fails to adequately compensate for the gravitational pull of blood to the lower body upon standing. Normally, baroreceptors trigger vasoconstriction and an increased heart rate to maintain blood pressure. In orthostatic hypotension, this response is impaired, often due to dehydration, certain medications (e.g., antihypertensives, diuretics), or underlying neurological conditions (e.g., Parkinson's disease, pure autonomic failure). The result is a drop in blood pressure upon standing, leading to dizziness, lightheadedness, and syncope.
  • Cardiac Syncope: This is the most serious category of syncope as it often indicates a life-threatening underlying heart condition. It can be caused by two main problems:
    • Arrhythmias: The heart beating too fast (tachycardia, e.g., ventricular tachycardia) or too slow (bradycardia, e.g., complete heart block) can severely compromise the heart's ability to pump blood effectively (cardiac output), leading to a sudden drop in cerebral perfusion.
    • Structural Heart Disease: Conditions that physically obstruct blood flow from the heart, such as severe aortic stenosis (narrowing of the aortic valve) or hypertrophic cardiomyopathy (thickening of the heart muscle), can prevent an adequate increase in cardiac output, particularly during exertion, leading to syncope.
Differential Diagnosis (DDx) Framework

A structured approach to the differential diagnosis of syncope is essential to ensure life-threatening conditions are prioritised.

Must not miss!
  • Myocardial Infarction: Presents with chest pain, shortness of breath, and potential collapse, indicating a severe cardiac event.
  • Aortic stenosis: A narrowing of the aortic valve causing exertional syncope, angina, and shortness of breath.
  • Pulmonary Embolism: A blood clot in the lungs causing a sudden drop in blood pressure, hypoxia, and syncope.
  • Arrhythmia: An abnormal heart rhythm (either too fast or too slow) leading to a sudden decrease in cardiac output and loss of consciousness.
  • Hypovolaemic shock: Loss of consciousness due to a severe reduction in blood volume, often from bleeding.
  • Sepsis: Syncope resulting from a systemic infection leading to widespread vasodilation and a drop in blood pressure.
  • Cerebrovascular Disease: Syncope caused by a stroke or transient ischaemic attack affecting blood flow to the brainstem.
  • Ectopic pregnancy: Syncope in a woman of childbearing age due to internal bleeding from a ruptured ectopic pregnancy.
  • Hypoglycaemia: Loss of consciousness due to critically low blood sugar levels, often in patients with diabetes.
  • Seizure: A post-ictal state or convulsive syncope can mimic a primary faint.
  • Space Occupying Lesion: A brain tumour or other lesion causing raised intracranial pressure and syncope.
Common causes
  • Vasovagal syncope: The most common type, often triggered by emotional stress, pain, or prolonged standing, with typical warning signs like nausea and sweating.
  • Orthostatic hypotension: Syncope that occurs upon standing due to a failure of the autonomic nervous system to maintain blood pressure.
  • Anxiety (Hyperventilation): Fainting caused by over-breathing, which leads to a decrease in carbon dioxide levels and cerebral vasoconstriction.
  • COVID-19: Can cause syncope through various mechanisms including dehydration, hypoxia, or cardiac complications.
Easily missed
  • Medication Induced Syncope: Fainting caused by drugs that lower blood pressure, such as antihypertensives or diuretics.
  • Alcohol and Drug Abuse: Can cause syncope through vasodilation, dehydration, or direct cardiac effects.
  • Obstructive Sleep Apnoea: May cause syncope through nocturnal hypoxia and associated arrhythmias.
  • Anaemia: Reduced oxygen-carrying capacity of the blood leading to cerebral hypoxia and syncope, especially on exertion.
  • Addison's disease: Adrenal insufficiency leading to hypotension, electrolyte disturbances, and syncope.
Other Differential Diagnoses to consider
  • Brugada syndrome: A genetic condition causing abnormal heart rhythms and sudden syncope or cardiac arrest.
  • Carotid sinus hypersensitivity: Syncope triggered by pressure on the carotid artery, such as during neck movements or tight collars.
  • Subclavian steal syndrome: Blood flow reversal in the subclavian artery leading to syncope, often associated with arm exertion.
  • Postural Orthostatic Tachycardia Syndrome (POTS): A dysautonomia causing rapid heart rate and syncope upon standing, often seen in younger patients.
  • Hypertrophic cardiomyopathy (HCM): A genetic condition where abnormal thickening of the heart muscle causes syncope, especially during exertion.
  • Long QT syndrome: A heart rhythm disorder that can cause sudden syncope or cardiac arrest, often triggered by exercise or stress.
  • Wolff-Parkinson-White (WPW) syndrome: A condition with abnormal electrical pathways in the heart, leading to arrhythmias and syncope.
  • Baroreflex failure: Impaired autonomic regulation of blood pressure, leading to episodes of severe hypertension or hypotension with syncope.
  • Takayasu’s arteritis: A rare inflammatory disease of the large blood vessels, which can cause syncope due to vascular occlusion.
History Taking
Patient’s Description: Start with open questions.

Start by asking: "How can I help you today?" or "Tell me about what happened." These questions are effective for several reasons:

  • Open-ended nature: They allow the patient to express their concerns freely without being restricted to a specific symptom or condition.
  • Neutral tone: These questions avoid assumptions, creating a comfortable space for the patient to share their concerns.
  • Sets the agenda: The patient’s response helps guide the consultation by highlighting their main issue or reason for visiting.

Active listening is crucial: giving the patient time to elaborate without interruption can lead to a more accurate diagnosis and improve satisfaction. Starting with open-ended questions provides insight into the nature and severity of the issue, revealing key details about the onset, triggers, and the patient’s perspective.
Relevance: This approach guides the clinician’s follow-up questions, builds rapport, and ensures a patient-centred consultation.

Specific Question: What were you doing just before you lost consciousness?
Asking about the situation or activity prior to the syncope episode can provide clues about potential triggers, such as standing up quickly (orthostatic hypotension) or emotional distress (vasovagal syncope). Relevance: Identifying situational triggers helps to differentiate between reflex syncope (e.g., vasovagal) and more serious causes like cardiac syncope.
Specific Question: Did you experience any warning signs, such as dizziness, nausea, or sweating before you fainted?
Pre-syncopal symptoms like dizziness, nausea, and sweating may suggest vasovagal syncope, while a lack of warning could point towards a more dangerous cause, such as a sudden cardiac arrhythmia. Relevance: Presence of these symptoms suggests reflex syncope; absence may indicate more sinister causes like arrhythmia or seizure.
Specific Question: How long were you unconscious?
The duration of loss of consciousness can provide valuable information. A brief episode suggests vasovagal or orthostatic syncope, while prolonged loss of consciousness may raise concerns about seizures or central nervous system pathology. Relevance: Helps differentiate between benign causes (short duration) and more concerning causes (longer duration, e.g., seizure).
Associated Symptom: Have you had any chest pain, palpitations, or shortness of breath?
Chest pain, palpitations, or dyspnoea could suggest cardiac causes, such as myocardial infarction, arrhythmia, or pulmonary embolism. These symptoms require urgent investigation. Relevance: A positive response suggests a cardiovascular cause (e.g., myocardial infarction, pulmonary embolism), while a negative response makes these causes less likely but does not completely rule them out.
Associated Symptom: Have you experienced any recent infection, fever, or flu-like symptoms?
Infections, especially systemic ones like sepsis or COVID-19, can cause syncope due to dehydration, sepsis, or cardiac complications. Relevance: A positive response raises the possibility of sepsis, COVID-19, or dehydration-related syncope. A negative response helps rule out these causes.
Associated Symptom: Have you experienced any abdominal pain or abnormal vaginal bleeding?
These symptoms are particularly important in women of reproductive age, as they can indicate an ectopic pregnancy, a life-threatening condition. Relevance: Positive response suggests ectopic pregnancy. A negative response helps to exclude this critical diagnosis.
Past Medical History: Have you ever been diagnosed with any heart problems, or do you take medications for your heart?
Cardiac conditions, such as arrhythmias, valvular disease, or coronary artery disease, significantly increase the likelihood of a cardiovascular cause of syncope. Relevance: A history of heart disease or use of cardiac medications (e.g., beta-blockers) makes cardiac causes like arrhythmias more likely. A negative history lowers the probability of these conditions.
Family History: Is there any family history of sudden cardiac death or heart problems?
A family history of sudden cardiac death or hereditary cardiac conditions (e.g., hypertrophic cardiomyopathy, long QT syndrome) can point towards genetic predispositions for dangerous arrhythmias. Relevance: A positive family history raises suspicion for inherited cardiac conditions. A negative response lowers suspicion but doesn’t exclude non-familial cardiac causes.
Drug History: Are you taking any medications, especially for blood pressure, or have you recently started or stopped any medication?
Certain medications, particularly antihypertensives, diuretics, or psychiatric drugs, can cause syncope by inducing hypotension or other side effects. Relevance: A positive response may suggest drug-induced syncope. A negative response decreases the likelihood of medication-related causes but should be cross-checked with medical records.
Ideas, Concerns, and Expectations (ICE)
It is important to explore the patient’s ideas, concerns, and expectations by asking: "What do you think might be causing this?" "Is there something specific you are worried about?" "What were you hoping we might be able to do for you today?" Relevance: These questions help to ensure the consultation addresses the patient's understanding and worries, and aligns management with their expectations.
Examination Strategy
General Inspection
  • Observe the patient’s general appearance, looking for signs of distress, pallor, cyanosis, or signs of trauma from a fall.
  • Relevance: Pallor suggests hypovolaemia or vasovagal syncope, while cyanosis could indicate hypoxia from pulmonary embolism or cardiac issues. Evidence of trauma may suggest seizure or loss of consciousness without protective reflexes, such as in cardiac arrhythmias.
Vital Signs: Blood pressure, heart rate, respiratory rate, oxygen saturation, and temperature
  • Assessing vital signs provides crucial information regarding haemodynamic stability, underlying cardiovascular or infectious causes, and the presence of hypoxia.
  • Relevance: Low blood pressure or postural drop suggests orthostatic hypotension, tachycardia may indicate hypovolaemia, sepsis, or arrhythmia. Fever suggests infection (e.g., sepsis). Low oxygen saturation raises suspicion of pulmonary embolism.
Cardiovascular Examination: Auscultation for heart sounds, murmurs, and irregular rhythm
  • Cardiovascular examination is essential for identifying underlying structural or rhythm abnormalities.
  • Relevance: A new murmur may suggest aortic stenosis or hypertrophic cardiomyopathy, while an irregular rhythm could indicate arrhythmias (e.g., atrial fibrillation). Quiet heart sounds may suggest pericardial effusion.
Postural Blood Pressure: Measure BP while the patient is lying, sitting, and standing
  • Checking for postural blood pressure changes helps identify orthostatic hypotension as a cause of syncope.
  • Relevance: A significant drop in systolic BP (>20 mmHg) when standing suggests orthostatic hypotension, which could be caused by dehydration, medications, or autonomic dysfunction.
Neurological Examination: Assess cranial nerves, motor and sensory function, and cerebellar signs
  • A neurological examination helps rule out central nervous system causes of syncope, such as stroke, transient ischaemic attack (TIA), or seizure.
  • Relevance: Focal neurological deficits suggest a cerebrovascular event (e.g., stroke, TIA), while a normal neurological examination would make such a diagnosis less likely. Confusion or abnormal reflexes may suggest seizure activity.
Respiratory Examination: Auscultation for breath sounds and assessment of respiratory effort
  • Examination of the respiratory system is crucial for identifying pulmonary causes of syncope, such as pulmonary embolism or pneumonia.
  • Relevance: Crackles or reduced breath sounds suggest pneumonia or pleural effusion, while a clear chest may make these diagnoses less likely. Tachypnoea or increased work of breathing could indicate pulmonary embolism or heart failure.
Abdominal Examination: Palpation for tenderness or masses, auscultation for bowel sounds
  • An abdominal examination can reveal signs of internal bleeding, infection, or other causes of hypovolaemia or abdominal pathology leading to syncope.
  • Relevance: Tenderness in the abdomen may suggest causes such as ruptured ectopic pregnancy, aortic aneurysm, or gastrointestinal bleeding. A negative abdominal exam reduces suspicion of these serious causes.
Carotid Sinus Massage: Assess for carotid sinus hypersensitivity (under supervision and in a controlled environment)
  • This test can provoke bradycardia or hypotension in cases of carotid sinus hypersensitivity, which may be responsible for syncope in older adults.
  • Relevance: A positive response with significant bradycardia or hypotension supports the diagnosis of carotid sinus hypersensitivity. This test is only done when no carotid bruits or contraindications exist.
Skin Inspection: Look for signs of dehydration, anaemia, or bruising
  • Skin changes may provide clues about underlying causes of syncope, such as dehydration, anaemia, or trauma from a fall.
  • Relevance: Dry mucous membranes or poor skin turgor suggests dehydration, while pallor may indicate anaemia. Bruising, especially around the head, could indicate a fall due to loss of consciousness.
Red Flag Synthesis
Chest Pain associated with syncope
  • Chest pain in conjunction with syncope raises suspicion for serious cardiac or pulmonary causes, such as myocardial infarction, aortic dissection, or pulmonary embolism.
  • Significance: This could indicate life-threatening conditions like myocardial infarction or aortic dissection, both of which require urgent intervention.
Shortness of Breath before or after syncope
  • Shortness of breath with syncope can suggest significant cardiopulmonary disease, including heart failure or pulmonary embolism.
  • Significance: This could indicate pulmonary embolism or severe heart failure, both of which are medical emergencies requiring immediate assessment and treatment.
Palpitations preceding syncope
  • Palpitations suggest an arrhythmia, which may lead to reduced cardiac output and syncope.
  • Significance: This could indicate arrhythmias such as atrial fibrillation, ventricular tachycardia, or heart block, which may require urgent management.
Severe Headache associated with syncope
  • A sudden, severe headache alongside syncope may suggest a neurological cause such as subarachnoid haemorrhage or intracranial bleed.
  • Significance: This could indicate an intracranial haemorrhage, which is a neurosurgical emergency requiring urgent imaging and intervention.
Abdominal Pain in the context of syncope
  • Syncope associated with abdominal pain can be a sign of a ruptured abdominal aortic aneurysm, ectopic pregnancy, or significant intra-abdominal bleeding.
  • Significance: This could indicate a ruptured aortic aneurysm or ectopic pregnancy, both of which are life-threatening emergencies requiring immediate surgical intervention.
Neurological Deficits such as weakness, numbness, or facial droop following syncope
  • Focal neurological deficits following syncope may indicate a cerebrovascular event like a stroke or transient ischaemic attack (TIA).
  • Significance: This could indicate stroke or TIA, requiring immediate assessment and thrombolytic therapy if indicated.
Prolonged Loss of Consciousness lasting more than a few minutes
  • Most syncopal events are brief. Prolonged loss of consciousness suggests other causes like seizures, metabolic disturbances, or central nervous system pathology.
  • Significance: This could indicate seizure activity, hypoglycaemia, or intracranial pathology requiring further investigation.
Signs of Trauma from falling during the syncope
  • Injury from a fall may indicate that the syncope occurred suddenly, without warning, suggesting a potentially serious cause like cardiac arrhythmia or seizure.
  • Significance: Head injury or other trauma from a fall could suggest a serious, sudden loss of consciousness requiring further neurological and cardiac evaluation.
Investigations Strategy & Interpretation
Electrocardiogram (ECG)
  • An ECG is crucial for identifying any arrhythmias, conduction delays, or evidence of ischaemia that could explain syncope.
  • Result Significance: Positive findings (e.g., arrhythmias like atrial fibrillation, ventricular tachycardia, or heart block) strongly suggest a cardiac cause of syncope. A normal ECG does not exclude arrhythmias, but lowers suspicion of immediate cardiac causes.
Ambulatory ECG (Holter Monitoring)
  • Holter monitoring is used to detect intermittent arrhythmias that may not appear during a standard ECG.
  • Result Significance: A positive result, showing arrhythmias such as intermittent atrial fibrillation or bradyarrhythmias, confirms the diagnosis of an arrhythmic cause for syncope. A negative result suggests arrhythmias are less likely.
Echocardiography
  • An echocardiogram is useful for assessing structural heart disease, such as valvular stenosis or hypertrophic cardiomyopathy, which can cause syncope.
  • Result Significance: Positive findings like aortic stenosis, hypertrophic cardiomyopathy, or heart failure suggest a structural cardiac cause. A normal echocardiogram reduces the likelihood of structural heart disease.
Full Blood Count (FBC)
  • An FBC can detect anaemia, which may cause syncope through reduced oxygen delivery to the brain, or raised white cell counts indicating infection.
  • Result Significance: Anaemia suggests a possible haemorrhagic or nutritional cause for syncope, while an elevated white cell count raises suspicion for infection or sepsis.
Electrolytes and Urea
  • Checking electrolytes can help identify imbalances like hyponatraemia or hyperkalaemia, which can lead to syncope through cardiac or neurological dysfunction.
  • Result Significance: Abnormal levels of electrolytes suggest a metabolic or renal cause for syncope. Normal results reduce the likelihood of electrolyte disturbances as a cause.
Troponin
  • Troponin is a marker of myocardial injury, and elevated levels suggest that syncope may be related to a myocardial infarction or ischaemia.
  • Result Significance: Elevated troponin levels strongly indicate myocardial infarction as the cause of syncope. Normal levels suggest that myocardial injury is unlikely.
D-dimer
  • D-dimer testing is used to rule out pulmonary embolism or deep vein thrombosis as causes of syncope, particularly in patients with risk factors for thrombosis.
  • Result Significance: A positive result raises suspicion for pulmonary embolism, requiring further imaging (e.g., CT pulmonary angiography). A negative result makes pulmonary embolism less likely.
CT Pulmonary Angiography
  • CT pulmonary angiography is used to definitively diagnose or exclude pulmonary embolism, a potentially life-threatening cause of syncope.
  • Result Significance: A positive result confirms pulmonary embolism as the cause of syncope, while a negative result rules out PE.
Tilt Table Test
  • The tilt table test is used to evaluate autonomic function and diagnose vasovagal syncope or orthostatic hypotension.
  • Result Significance: A positive test, with a drop in blood pressure and/or heart rate during the tilt, suggests vasovagal syncope or orthostatic hypotension. A negative test makes these diagnoses less likely.
Electroencephalogram (EEG)
  • An EEG may be necessary if seizure activity is suspected as the cause of syncope.
  • Result Significance: A positive EEG indicating abnormal electrical activity supports a diagnosis of seizure-related syncope. A negative result makes seizure less likely but does not fully exclude it.
Brain Imaging (CT or MRI)
  • Imaging of the brain is indicated if a neurological cause for syncope is suspected, such as a stroke or space-occupying lesion.
  • Result Significance: A positive finding of haemorrhage, mass, or stroke confirms a neurological cause for syncope. Negative imaging reduces the likelihood of structural brain pathology.
Evidence-Based Diagnosis (EBDx)
ECG: Detection of Arrhythmias
  • An ECG is essential for detecting arrhythmias, which are a common cause of syncope, particularly in older adults or those with underlying heart disease.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 77%, Specificity: 92%, Likelihood Ratio (Positive): 9.6, Likelihood Ratio (Negative): 0.25.
  • Significance: Positive ECG findings (e.g., atrial fibrillation or ventricular tachycardia) significantly increase the likelihood of a cardiac cause. A negative ECG does not rule out arrhythmias but reduces suspicion of immediate life-threatening arrhythmias.
Postural Blood Pressure: Detection of Orthostatic Hypotension
  • Measurement of blood pressure upon standing can diagnose orthostatic hypotension, a frequent cause of syncope, especially in elderly patients or those on antihypertensive medications.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 85%, Specificity: 90%, Positive Predictive Value (PPV): 0.73.
  • Significance: A significant postural drop in blood pressure strongly suggests orthostatic hypotension. Negative findings make this diagnosis less likely, especially if symptoms persist.
Troponin: Detection of Myocardial Infarction
  • Elevated troponin levels are a highly sensitive marker for myocardial infarction (MI) as a cause of syncope, especially in patients with chest pain or cardiovascular risk factors.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 94%, Specificity: 80%, Likelihood Ratio (Positive): 4.7, Likelihood Ratio (Negative): 0.07.
  • Significance: Positive troponin levels strongly suggest myocardial infarction as the cause of syncope, requiring urgent intervention. Normal levels make an acute MI less likely.
D-dimer: Detection of Pulmonary Embolism
  • D-dimer is used as a screening tool for pulmonary embolism (PE) in patients presenting with syncope, especially those with risk factors such as immobility, cancer, or recent surgery.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 96%, Specificity: 45%, Negative Predictive Value (NPV): 99% (when combined with a low Wells score).
  • Significance: A negative D-dimer result, especially in low-risk patients, virtually rules out pulmonary embolism. Positive results require further investigation with CT pulmonary angiography.
EEG: Detection of Seizure Activity
  • An EEG is indicated if seizure activity is suspected as the cause of syncope, particularly in patients with post-ictal confusion or tongue biting.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 60%, Specificity: 95% for identifying epileptiform activity.
  • Significance: Positive EEG findings confirm seizure activity as the likely cause of syncope. A negative EEG does not fully exclude seizures but makes this diagnosis less likely.
CT Brain: Detection of Stroke or Intracranial Haemorrhage
  • CT brain imaging is indicated in patients with focal neurological deficits or severe headache accompanying syncope to rule out stroke or intracranial haemorrhage.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 88%, Specificity: 96% for detecting acute haemorrhage.
  • Significance: Positive CT findings confirm an intracranial cause for syncope, such as haemorrhage or stroke, requiring urgent intervention. Negative findings reduce the likelihood of significant intracranial pathology.
Diagnostic Timeout & Cognitive Biases

Awareness of common thinking errors is essential to improve diagnostic accuracy and patient safety when assessing a patient with syncope. Before finalising a diagnosis, it is good practice for clinicians at all levels to take a 'diagnostic timeout' to challenge their own reasoning.

  • Anchoring Bias: This is the tendency to lock onto the most obvious diagnosis too early. For example, attributing a faint in a young person to vasovagal syncope without fully exploring for red flags like syncope during exertion or a family history of sudden death, which might suggest a cardiac cause. A key question to ask is: "Could this be an arrhythmia, and have I done enough to exclude it?"
  • Confirmation Bias: Actively seeking out or overvaluing evidence that supports an initial belief while ignoring contradictory evidence. For instance, focusing on the fact that a patient felt dizzy before fainting (suggesting a benign cause) while downplaying the significance of a new ejection systolic murmur found on examination.
  • Atypical Presentations: A major pitfall is forgetting that serious diseases can present non-classically. Cardiac syncope, especially in older adults, can occur without any associated chest pain or palpitations. Maintaining a high index of suspicion is crucial, particularly for syncope that occurs without warning or during exercise.
  • Over-reliance on Normal Tests: Placing too much weight on a single reassuring test result. A normal 12-lead ECG is reassuring but does not definitively rule out a life-threatening intermittent arrhythmia (like ventricular tachycardia or paroxysmal heart block). If the history is concerning for a cardiac cause, further investigation (e.g., ambulatory ECG monitoring) is required.
Illustrative Management Pathways

For educational purposes, this section describes the typical principles of management for key diagnoses. These are not prescriptive instructions. All management must be guided by official UK guidelines, local formularies, and senior clinical advice.

Reflex (Vasovagal) Syncope

Once a confident diagnosis of reflex syncope is made and red flags have been excluded, management is centred on education and reassurance. Patients should be educated about the benign nature of the condition and helped to identify their specific triggers (e.g., prolonged standing, dehydration, sight of blood). Key management strategies include trigger avoidance and learning physical counter-pressure manoeuvres (e.g., leg crossing with tensing, hand gripping) at the onset of warning symptoms to abort an episode.

Orthostatic Hypotension

Management begins with a comprehensive review of medications to identify and discontinue or reduce any causative agents (e.g., antihypertensives, diuretics). Non-pharmacological approaches are first-line, including increasing fluid and salt intake, and patient education on standing up slowly and in stages. For persistent symptoms, pharmacological intervention with drugs like fludrocortisone or midodrine may be considered by a specialist.

Cardiac Syncope (Arrhythmic or Structural)

Any suspicion of cardiac syncope is a medical emergency that warrants urgent hospital admission and specialist cardiology assessment. Management is directed at the underlying cause. For bradyarrhythmias (e.g., complete heart block), this typically involves insertion of a permanent pacemaker. For tachyarrhythmias (e.g., ventricular tachycardia), treatment may include an implantable cardioverter-defibrillator (ICD) and anti-arrhythmic drugs. For structural causes like severe aortic stenosis, the definitive treatment is surgical or transcatheter aortic valve replacement.

Reflection & Learning

These brief cases illustrate the application of clinical reasoning to different syncope presentations. They are for educational purposes only.

Case 1: Suspected Vasovagal Syncope in Primary Care

A 22-year-old university student presents to their GP after fainting during a lecture. They describe feeling increasingly hot, nauseous, and light-headed for a minute before their vision "went black". A friend reported they were unconscious for about 20 seconds. There were no preceding chest pains or palpitations. Examination, including postural blood pressure and a 12-lead ECG, is normal. The problem representation is a young, low-risk individual with a classic history of pre-syncopal symptoms in a typical setting (prolonged sitting in a warm room), most consistent with vasovagal syncope. Management involves reassurance and education on counter-pressure manoeuvres.

Case 2: Suspected Cardiac Syncope in the Emergency Department

A 75-year-old man is brought to the Emergency Department after losing consciousness while walking up a hill. The episode was sudden, with no warning signs. He has a history of hypertension. His ECG shows left ventricular hypertrophy and a new broad-complex tachycardia. His troponin is slightly elevated. The problem representation is an older male with an exertional syncope, concerning for a cardiac cause. The ECG changes and elevated troponin are red flags pointing towards an arrhythmic event, possibly due to underlying ischaemia or structural heart disease. He requires immediate admission for cardiac monitoring and urgent cardiology consultation.

Case 3: Orthostatic Hypotension in an Elderly Patient

An 82-year-old woman is reviewed on a post-operative ward after fainting when getting out of bed. She is taking ramipril and amlodipine for hypertension. Her postural blood pressure check reveals a drop in systolic BP from 140 mmHg lying down to 110 mmHg on standing, reproducing her dizziness. The problem representation is an elderly patient on antihypertensive medications with a clear history of postural syncope, confirmed by examination. The likely cause is iatrogenic orthostatic hypotension. The immediate management is to review and temporarily withhold her blood pressure medications and ensure adequate hydration, with a plan for careful reintroduction if needed.
