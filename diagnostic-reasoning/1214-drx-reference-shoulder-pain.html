Shoulder Pain Diagnostic Reasoning Reference
Context: Shoulder Pain Assessment
Overview

Shoulder pain is an extremely common presentation in both primary and secondary care, affecting up to one in three adults at some point in their lives. The diagnostic challenge lies in differentiating benign, self-limiting musculoskeletal conditions from referred pain originating from serious cervical, thoracic, or abdominal pathology. A systematic and structured clinical reasoning process is essential to avoid missing red flag conditions such as malignancy, infection, or myocardial ischaemia, while ensuring common issues like rotator cuff disorders are managed appropriately.

This educational module is designed to provide a foundational framework for the clinical assessment of shoulder pain. For definitive, real-time management protocols, users must refer to official UK guidelines from authoritative bodies such as NICE (e.g., for rheumatoid arthritis or osteoarthritis) and specialty societies like the British Elbow and Shoulder Society (BESS).

Pathophysiology/Basic Science

Understanding the origin of shoulder pain is fundamental to diagnosis. The shoulder complex is anatomically intricate, and pain can be generated by local structures or referred from distant sites.

  • Intrinsic Shoulder Pain (Glenohumeral or Periscapular): This arises directly from the structures of the shoulder girdle. A common mechanism is subacromial impingement, where rotator cuff tendons and the subacromial bursa are compressed in the space beneath the acromion during arm elevation, leading to inflammation and pain (tendinopathy, bursitis). In osteoarthritis, the gradual degradation of articular cartilage within the glenohumeral joint leads to friction, inflammation, and pain on movement. In adhesive capsulitis (frozen shoulder), chronic inflammation leads to fibrosis and thickening of the joint capsule, severely restricting both active and passive movement.
  • Referred Pain: This occurs when pain is perceived in the shoulder but originates elsewhere, due to shared neural pathways. Cervical radiculopathy is a classic example, where compression of C5 or C6 nerve roots in the neck causes pain to be referred to the shoulder and arm along the corresponding dermatome. Diaphragmatic irritation (from causes like a subphrenic abscess, splenic rupture, or gallstone disease) can stimulate the phrenic nerve (C3, C4, C5 nerve roots). Because these nerve roots also supply sensation to the shoulder tip area, the brain misinterprets the visceral pain as originating from the shoulder. Similarly, myocardial ischaemia can refer pain to the left shoulder and arm via shared spinal cord segments in the brachial plexus.
  • Neuropathic Pain: This pain originates from nerve tissue itself. Conditions like brachial neuritis (Parsonage-Turner syndrome) involve acute inflammation of the brachial plexus, causing severe, sudden-onset shoulder pain followed by muscle weakness and sensory changes. Herpes zoster (shingles) involves the reactivation of the varicella-zoster virus in a dorsal root ganglion, causing a burning, dermatomal pain that often precedes a vesicular rash.
Differential Diagnosis (DDx) Framework

A structured approach to the differential diagnosis of shoulder pain is essential to ensure life-threatening and serious conditions are prioritised.

Must not miss!
  • Fracture or dislocation: Typically occurs after significant trauma, presenting with acute severe pain, deformity, and inability to move the arm.
  • Myocardial Infarction: Presents as a deep, aching left shoulder pain, often associated with central chest discomfort, shortness of breath, and sweating, particularly on exertion.
  • Angina: A constricting discomfort in the chest that may radiate to the shoulder, predictably triggered by exertion and relieved by rest.
  • Rheumatoid arthritis: An inflammatory polyarthritis causing symmetrical, painful, swollen, and stiff joints, often worse in the morning.
  • Pneumonia: Can cause referred shoulder pain, especially if the infection is in the upper lobes, and is associated with cough, fever, and breathlessness.
  • Septic arthritis: A severe, constant pain with fever and systemic illness, where the patient is unable to tolerate any movement of the joint.
  • Osteomyelitis: An infection of the bone causing deep, constant, boring pain, often accompanied by fever and localised tenderness.
  • Metastatic cancer: Often presents with persistent, worsening pain that is unrelieved by rest and particularly severe at night, possibly with a history of primary cancer.
  • Multiple myeloma: A haematological malignancy that can cause bone pain, pathological fractures, fatigue, and recurrent infections.
  • Bone tumours: Primary bone tumours can cause localised, persistent pain and swelling, often worse at night and not related to activity.
  • Pancoast's tumour: A tumour in the lung apex causing shoulder pain, often with neurological symptoms in the arm (Horner's syndrome, weakness) due to invasion of the brachial plexus.
Common causes
  • Rotator cuff disorder: A common cause of shoulder pain, typically worse with overhead activities and lying on the affected side, often involving an impingement mechanism.
  • Adhesive capsulitis: Characterised by a gradual onset of severe pain followed by profound stiffness, with global restriction of both active and passive shoulder movement.
  • Shoulder osteoarthritis: Presents with activity-related deep joint pain and progressive stiffness, often with crepitus on movement.
  • AC joint disorder: Pain localised to the top of the shoulder, exacerbated by reaching across the body or lying on the affected side.
  • Cervical radiculopathy: Neck pain that radiates into the shoulder and arm in a specific dermatomal pattern, often with associated numbness or weakness.
  • Arthropathy presentation: A general term for joint disease which can encompass inflammatory or degenerative conditions affecting the shoulder.
Easily missed
  • Polymyalgia rheumatica: An inflammatory condition in older adults causing symmetrical aching and stiffness in the shoulders, neck, and pelvic girdle, typically worse in the morning.
  • Gallstone disease (Referred): Can cause referred pain to the right shoulder tip (Boas' sign) due to diaphragmatic irritation, often associated with right upper quadrant pain after fatty meals.
  • Connective tissue diseases: Systemic conditions like lupus or scleroderma can present with shoulder arthralgia as part of a wider pattern of symptoms.
Other Differential Diagnoses to consider
  • Thoracic outlet syndrome: Compression of nerves or vessels at the thoracic outlet, causing pain and sensory changes in the shoulder and arm.
  • Subacromial bursitis: Inflammation of the bursa in the subacromial space, often presenting with pain and limited movement.
  • Parsonage-Turner syndrome: Brachial neuritis leading to acute shoulder pain followed by muscle weakness and atrophy.
  • Herpes zoster (shingles): Viral infection causing dermatomal pain and possible vesicular rash over the shoulder region.
  • Diaphragmatic irritation: Referred pain to the shoulder from intra-abdominal pathology such as subphrenic abscess or splenic rupture.
  • Fibromyalgia: Widespread musculoskeletal pain including the shoulder, associated with fatigue and tender points.
  • Post-stroke pain syndrome: Central pain after a cerebrovascular event, affecting the shoulder and upper limb.
History Taking
Patient’s Description: How can I help you today?
Starting with open-ended questions allows the patient to describe their symptoms in their own words, which can reveal valuable information about the nature, severity, and context of their concern. Relevance: Guides the clinician in tailoring subsequent targeted questions.
When did the pain start?
  • This helps determine if the pain is acute, subacute, or chronic.
  • Relevance: Acute onset may suggest trauma, dislocation, myocardial infarction, or septic arthritis. Chronic pain is more consistent with conditions like rotator cuff disorders, adhesive capsulitis, or osteoarthritis.
Can you describe the pain? (e.g., sharp, dull, aching, burning)
  • Pain quality can provide clues about the underlying cause.
  • Relevance: Sharp pain may suggest a rotator cuff tear or fracture. Burning pain could indicate nerve involvement, such as cervical radiculopathy or brachial neuritis.
Does anything make the pain worse or better?
  • Exploring triggers and relieving factors helps narrow down the differential diagnosis.
  • Relevance: Pain aggravated by movement could indicate rotator cuff pathology or AC joint dysfunction. Pain relieved by rest may suggest cardiac or referred pain.
Do you have any associated symptoms? (e.g., fever, chest pain, weakness, numbness, swelling)
  • Identifying associated symptoms can highlight systemic or serious conditions.
  • Relevance: Fever or swelling may indicate septic arthritis. Chest pain or breathlessness suggests a cardiac or pulmonary cause. Weakness and numbness may point to cervical radiculopathy or brachial plexus injury.
Do you have any history of trauma or injury?
  • A history of trauma provides essential context for structural injuries.
  • Relevance: A positive response could indicate a fracture, dislocation, or soft tissue injury.
Have you had similar symptoms before?
  • Previous episodes may indicate a recurrent condition or chronic underlying pathology.
  • Relevance: A history of recurrent symptoms may suggest adhesive capsulitis or osteoarthritis.
Do you have any medical conditions, or take any regular medications?
  • Identifying relevant medical history or medications can uncover risk factors for specific diagnoses.
  • Relevance: Conditions like diabetes (risk of adhesive capsulitis) or immunosuppression (risk of septic arthritis) may influence the presentation.
Ideas, Concerns, and Expectations (ICE)
  • Exploring the patient’s ideas, concerns, and expectations ensures a holistic understanding of their perspective.
  • Relevance: For example, the patient may worry about cancer or a heart condition, influencing their stress and engagement with treatment. Their expectations may clarify their goals for the consultation (e.g., pain relief, diagnostic clarity).
Examination Strategy
Inspection of the Shoulder
  • Inspect for visible asymmetry, muscle wasting, deformity, swelling, or erythema.
  • Relevance: Muscle wasting suggests chronic conditions like rotator cuff tear or brachial neuritis. Swelling or erythema could indicate septic arthritis or inflammatory arthropathy. A visible deformity may indicate dislocation or fracture.
Palpation of the Shoulder
  • Palpate for tenderness, warmth, or bony abnormalities in the shoulder and surrounding areas.
  • Relevance: Localised tenderness over the AC joint suggests AC joint dysfunction. General warmth and tenderness may point to septic arthritis or inflammatory conditions like rheumatoid arthritis.
Range of Motion (Active and Passive)
  • Assess active and passive range of motion in all planes (flexion, extension, abduction, adduction, internal rotation, and external rotation).
  • Relevance: Restricted active and passive movement may suggest adhesive capsulitis. Limited active but normal passive movement points towards rotator cuff injury. Pain during movement may indicate osteoarthritis or tendinopathy.
Special Tests for Rotator Cuff Pathology
  • Neer’s test: Pain during forward elevation with internal rotation suggests impingement.
  • Empty can test: Weakness or pain with resisted abduction indicates supraspinatus tendinopathy or tear.
  • Relevance: Positive findings confirm rotator cuff involvement, while negative results make impingement or rotator cuff tear less likely.
Neurological Examination
  • Assess motor strength, reflexes (e.g., biceps, triceps), and sensation in the upper limb to evaluate for nerve involvement.
  • Relevance: Weakness, altered reflexes, or sensory deficits suggest cervical radiculopathy, brachial plexopathy, or Parsonage-Turner syndrome.
Spurling’s Test (For Cervical Radiculopathy)
  • Compress the cervical spine in lateral flexion to reproduce radicular symptoms.
  • Relevance: A positive test indicates cervical radiculopathy as a cause of shoulder pain. Negative findings make nerve root compression less likely.
Cardiopulmonary Examination
  • Auscultate the heart and lungs for abnormal sounds and examine for signs of cardiac or pulmonary pathology.
  • Relevance: Abnormal findings like a murmur or crackles may point to myocardial infarction, pneumonia, or a Pancoast tumour causing referred shoulder pain.
Examination of Adjacent Joints
  • Evaluate the cervical spine, elbow, and thoracic region to assess for referred pain or associated pathology.
  • Relevance: Cervical spine tenderness or restricted movement may indicate radiculopathy. Pain referred from the thorax may indicate gallbladder disease or diaphragmatic irritation.
Red Flag Synthesis
Unexplained weight loss
  • Unintentional weight loss may be a sign of malignancy, including metastatic cancer, multiple myeloma, or Pancoast tumour.
  • Significance: This could indicate a serious underlying condition, such as cancer, requiring prompt investigation.
Night pain that disturbs sleep
  • Night pain is often associated with malignancy or significant inflammation, such as bone tumours or osteomyelitis.
  • Significance: Persistent night pain warrants urgent evaluation for serious conditions like cancer or infection.
Fever or chills
  • Fever or chills may indicate systemic infection, such as septic arthritis or osteomyelitis.
  • Significance: This could suggest an urgent need to address infection, which may be life-threatening if untreated.
Severe or progressive neurological deficits
  • Symptoms like limb weakness, numbness, or significant sensory loss may indicate nerve compression, cervical radiculopathy, or brachial plexopathy.
  • Significance: Progressive neurological deficits could signify a spinal cord or nerve root lesion requiring immediate evaluation.
Chest pain or shortness of breath
  • Chest pain or dyspnoea may reflect a cardiac or pulmonary cause, such as myocardial infarction, angina, or a Pancoast tumour.
  • Significance: These symptoms require urgent investigation to rule out life-threatening conditions.
Swelling, erythema, or warmth over the joint
  • Localised swelling, redness, and warmth suggest inflammatory or infectious conditions such as septic arthritis.
  • Significance: This could indicate an acute infection that requires immediate treatment.
History of significant trauma
  • A history of significant trauma raises suspicion for fractures, dislocations, or ligamentous injuries.
  • Significance: This could signify structural damage requiring urgent imaging and intervention.
Investigations Strategy & Interpretation
X-ray of the shoulder
  • An X-ray can identify fractures, dislocations, and degenerative changes such as osteoarthritis.
  • Result Significance: Positive findings like fractures or joint space narrowing confirm structural abnormalities, while negative findings make such conditions less likely.
MRI of the shoulder
  • MRI is useful for assessing soft tissue injuries, including rotator cuff tears, labral injuries, and ligamentous damage.
  • Result Significance: Positive findings help diagnose soft tissue pathology, while negative findings suggest the pain may not be due to structural damage.
Ultrasound of the shoulder
  • Ultrasound is a cost-effective way to evaluate rotator cuff pathology, bursitis, or effusions in real time.
  • Result Significance: Positive findings confirm conditions like tendon tears or bursitis, while negative findings make these diagnoses less likely.
Full blood count (FBC)
  • An FBC can detect signs of infection or systemic inflammation.
  • Result Significance: Elevated white cell count may indicate infection (e.g., septic arthritis), while normal results make infection less likely.
C-reactive protein (CRP) and erythrocyte sedimentation rate (ESR)
  • These tests are markers of inflammation and can help identify infection, autoimmune conditions, or malignancy.
  • Result Significance: Elevated levels suggest conditions like septic arthritis or rheumatoid arthritis, while normal results may help exclude significant inflammation.
Electrocardiogram (ECG) and troponins
  • These tests are essential if referred shoulder pain is suspected to be of cardiac origin.
  • Result Significance: Positive findings, such as ST changes or elevated troponins, confirm cardiac ischaemia, while negative findings may rule out myocardial infarction or unstable angina.
Chest X-ray
  • Useful for assessing pulmonary conditions that may refer pain to the shoulder, such as Pancoast tumour or pneumonia.
  • Result Significance: Positive findings like a mass or consolidation indicate underlying pulmonary pathology, while negative findings rule out these causes.
Bone scan or PET scan
  • These imaging modalities are used to detect metastatic cancer or bone tumours.
  • Result Significance: Positive findings support a diagnosis of malignancy, while negative findings make such conditions less likely.
Evidence-Based Diagnosis (EBDx)
Night Pain and Sleep Disturbance
  • Night pain that disturbs sleep is a significant red flag for malignancy, such as metastatic cancer or a Pancoast tumour, and requires further investigation.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 70%, Specificity: 85% for malignancy-related pain. Likelihood Ratio: 4.7.
  • Reference: Henschke et al., "Red flags to screen for malignancy in low back pain: systematic review," BMJ 2013.
  • Significance: Positive findings strongly suggest malignancy, while absence of night pain reduces the probability of cancer as a diagnosis.
Rotator Cuff Special Tests (Empty Can Test)
  • The empty can test evaluates supraspinatus function, a key rotator cuff muscle. Weakness or pain suggests supraspinatus pathology.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 89%, Specificity: 54%, Positive Likelihood Ratio: 1.9 for rotator cuff tears.
  • Reference: Park et al., "Diagnostic accuracy of clinical tests for rotator cuff tears," Am J Sports Med 2005.
  • Significance: A positive test increases the likelihood of rotator cuff injury, but further imaging is needed to confirm.
C-reactive Protein (CRP)
  • CRP is a sensitive marker for infection or significant inflammation, such as septic arthritis or osteomyelitis.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 91%, Specificity: 87% for detecting septic arthritis. Positive Likelihood Ratio: 7.0.
  • Reference: Mathews et al., "Laboratory investigations in the diagnosis of septic arthritis," Ann Rheum Dis 2007.
  • Significance: A raised CRP strongly supports infectious or inflammatory causes, while a normal CRP makes these less likely.
MRI for Rotator Cuff Tears
  • MRI is the gold standard for detecting rotator cuff pathology, including full and partial tears.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 91%, Specificity: 95%, Positive Likelihood Ratio: 18.2 for full-thickness rotator cuff tears.
  • Reference: Tuite et al., "Imaging techniques for rotator cuff injury," J Bone Joint Surg Am 1997.
  • Significance: Positive findings confirm the diagnosis, while negative findings rule out significant rotator cuff pathology.
Electrocardiogram (ECG)
  • An ECG is critical to rule out cardiac causes of referred shoulder pain, particularly myocardial ischaemia or infarction.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 85%, Specificity: 90% for acute coronary syndrome in appropriate clinical contexts.
  • Reference: Amsterdam et al., "2014 AHA/ACC guideline for the management of patients with non-ST-elevation acute coronary syndromes," J Am Coll Cardiol 2014.
  • Significance: Abnormal ECG findings increase the likelihood of cardiac origin, while a normal ECG reduces it but does not exclude it entirely.
Chest X-ray for Pancoast Tumour
  • A chest X-ray can identify masses in the lung apex, suggestive of a Pancoast tumour, which may cause referred shoulder pain.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 70%, Specificity: 85% for detecting lung masses.
  • Reference: Rivera et al., "Initial diagnosis of lung cancer: ACCP evidence-based guidelines," Chest 2013.
  • Significance: Positive findings strongly suggest lung pathology, while negative findings warrant further investigation with CT or MRI if clinical suspicion persists.
Diagnostic Timeout & Cognitive Biases

Awareness of common cognitive errors is essential to improve diagnostic accuracy when assessing a patient with shoulder pain. Before finalising a diagnosis, it is good practice for clinicians to take a 'diagnostic timeout' to challenge their own reasoning.

  • Anchoring Bias: The tendency to lock onto the most obvious diagnosis, often a musculoskeletal one, based on initial information (e.g., patient mentions lifting something). This may cause a clinician to fail to consider red flags for a more serious condition, like a Pancoast tumour, even if the patient mentions night pain and weight loss later in the consultation. A key question to ask is: "Could this pain be referred, and have I ruled out cardiac or malignant causes?"
  • Confirmation Bias: Actively seeking out or overvaluing evidence that supports an initial hypothesis while ignoring contradictory findings. For example, focusing on a positive Neer's test to confirm impingement syndrome while downplaying associated neck stiffness and arm paraesthesia that point towards cervical radiculopathy.
  • Availability Heuristic: Overestimating the likelihood of common diagnoses like rotator cuff disorders simply because they are frequently encountered. This can lead to under-consideration of rarer but critical diagnoses like septic arthritis, especially in a patient who presents atypically without a high fever.
  • Search Satisficing: This is the tendency to stop searching for causes once one plausible explanation has been found. For instance, an X-ray might show mild glenohumeral osteoarthritis, and the diagnostic process is halted there. This might lead to missing a concurrent, more significant diagnosis like polymyalgia rheumatica, which is the true driver of the patient's severe bilateral symptoms.
Illustrative Management Pathways

For educational purposes, this section describes the typical principles of management for key diagnoses. These are not prescriptive instructions. All management must be guided by official UK guidelines, local formularies, and senior clinical advice.

Rotator Cuff Disorder

The management is typically conservative in the first instance. It focuses on patient education, activity modification to avoid aggravating movements, and simple analgesia (paracetamol and/or NSAIDs, if appropriate). A structured, supervised physiotherapy programme is crucial to strengthen the rotator cuff and periscapular muscles and improve biomechanics. If conservative measures fail after several months, a subacromial corticosteroid injection can be considered for short-term pain relief. Surgical referral for arthroscopic subacromial decompression or tendon repair is reserved for persistent, debilitating symptoms refractory to non-operative treatment.

Adhesive Capsulitis (Frozen Shoulder)

Management is centred on pain control and maintaining as much range of motion as possible, while educating the patient about the condition's natural history of gradual resolution over 1-3 years. Analgesia is the first step. Physiotherapy is key, but must be gentle to avoid exacerbating pain. Intra-articular corticosteroid injections can be very effective for pain relief, particularly in the early, painful phase. For patients with persistent severe stiffness, hydrodilatation (injecting a large volume of saline and steroid into the joint to stretch the capsule) or surgical capsular release may be considered by specialists.

Septic Arthritis

This is a rheumatological and orthopaedic emergency requiring immediate hospital admission. The core principles of management are prompt joint aspiration for microscopy, culture, and sensitivities, followed immediately by empiric intravenous antibiotics according to local guidelines. Urgent surgical intervention (arthroscopic washout or open arthrotomy) is required to debride the joint and remove infected material. The joint should be rested initially, followed by physiotherapy to restore function once the infection is controlled.

Referred Pain (e.g., Cardiac or Cervical)

The management pathway is dictated entirely by the underlying cause. If myocardial ischaemia is suspected, the patient must be managed as a cardiac emergency with immediate transfer to hospital for an ACS workup (ECG, troponins, cardiology review). If cervical radiculopathy is diagnosed, management is typically conservative with analgesia, neck physiotherapy, and nerve-modulating agents (e.g., amitriptyline, gabapentin). Urgent specialist referral is needed if there are signs of myelopathy or progressive neurological deficit.

Reflection & Learning

These brief cases illustrate the application of clinical reasoning to different shoulder pain presentations. They are for educational purposes only.

Case 1: Suspected Rotator Cuff Disorder in Primary Care

A 55-year-old painter presents to his GP with a 3-month history of right shoulder pain. It's a dull ache, worse when he works overhead, and he struggles to sleep on his right side. Examination reveals a painful arc of abduction between 70-120 degrees and weakness on the 'empty can' test. The problem representation is a middle-aged man with an occupation involving overhead work, presenting with chronic, activity-related shoulder pain and classic signs of impingement and supraspinatus weakness, most consistent with a rotator cuff disorder. The GP provides advice on analgesia and activity modification and refers him to physiotherapy.

Case 2: Suspected Referred Cardiac Pain in A&E

A 68-year-old woman with type 2 diabetes presents to the Emergency Department with a 4-hour history of a deep, heavy ache in her left shoulder and jaw. The pain started while she was walking up a hill. She denies any injury, and examination of the shoulder joint itself is unremarkable, with a full, pain-free range of passive movement. The problem representation is an older woman with a key cardiovascular risk factor presenting with exertional shoulder and jaw pain with no mechanical features, highly suspicious for an atypical presentation of ACS. An urgent ECG is performed, which shows ST depression, confirming the need for an ACS management pathway.

Case 3: Suspected Cervical Radiculopathy in an MSK Clinic

A 42-year-old office worker is referred to a musculoskeletal clinic with left shoulder pain. She describes the pain as a burning sensation that radiates from the side of her neck, over the top of her shoulder, and down to her thumb. She has also noticed tingling in her thumb and index finger. Examination of the shoulder is normal, but she has reduced neck rotation to the left. Spurling's test (head tilted to the left and gentle downward pressure) reproduces her arm pain. The problem representation is a patient with a sedentary job presenting with neuropathic pain in a C6 dermatomal distribution, with clinical signs pointing to a cervical rather than a shoulder origin. The diagnosis is cervical radiculopathy, and she is managed with neck physiotherapy and nerve-modulating medication.
