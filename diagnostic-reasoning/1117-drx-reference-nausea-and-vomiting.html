Nausea and Vomiting Diagnostic Reasoning Reference
Context: Nausea and Vomiting Assessment
Overview

Nausea and vomiting are among the most frequent and distressing symptoms encountered in clinical practice, affecting patients across all age groups and medical specialties. They can represent a wide spectrum of conditions, from benign and self-limiting illnesses like viral gastroenteritis to indicators of severe, life-threatening pathologies such as intestinal obstruction, diabetic ketoacidosis (DKA), or raised intracranial pressure. The core clinical challenge is to differentiate these causes efficiently, ensuring that urgent conditions are promptly identified and managed while providing appropriate symptomatic relief for less severe ailments.

This educational module is designed to provide a systematic framework for the clinical reasoning process when faced with a patient presenting with nausea and vomiting. It is intended for educational purposes only. For definitive, real-time management protocols, users must consult official UK guidelines from authoritative bodies such as NICE (e.g., CG184 for Acute Heart Failure, NG28 for Type 1 Diabetes in adults) and local NHS trust protocols.

Pathophysiology/Basic Science

The act of vomiting (emesis) is a complex reflex coordinated by the "vomiting centre" in the medulla oblongata of the brainstem. This centre does not initiate the reflex itself but receives inputs from several key areas, leading to the sensation of nausea and the physical act of vomiting:

  • The Chemoreceptor Trigger Zone (CTZ): Located in the area postrema on the floor of the fourth ventricle, the CTZ is outside the blood-brain barrier. This allows it to detect emetogenic (vomit-inducing) substances in the bloodstream, such as drugs (e.g., opioids, chemotherapy), toxins (e.g., bacterial toxins), and metabolic products (e.g., ketones in DKA, urea in renal failure). It is rich in dopamine (D2) and serotonin (5-HT3) receptors.
  • Visceral Afferent Nerves from the Gastrointestinal (GI) Tract: Vagal and sympathetic nerve fibres from the GI tract are activated by mechanical stretch (e.g., in bowel obstruction or gastroparesis), irritation (e.g., from infection or inflammation in gastroenteritis or appendicitis), or toxins. These signals travel to the vomiting centre.
  • The Vestibular System: Located in the inner ear, the vestibular system is crucial for balance and detecting motion. Overstimulation, as in motion sickness or in diseases like labyrinthitis or Meniere's disease, sends strong signals to the vomiting centre via histamine (H1) and muscarinic acetylcholine receptors.
  • Higher Cortical Centres: The cerebral cortex can trigger nausea and vomiting in response to psychological stimuli, such as intense pain, emotional distress (anxiety), or repulsive sights and smells. This pathway explains psychogenic vomiting.

Understanding which pathway is likely being stimulated is fundamental to diagnosing the underlying cause and selecting appropriate anti-emetic therapy, as different drugs target different receptors in these pathways.

Differential Diagnosis (DDx) Framework

A structured approach to the differential diagnosis of nausea and vomiting is essential to ensure life-threatening conditions are prioritised.

Must not miss!
  • Acute abdomen: Presents with severe abdominal pain, tenderness, and rigidity, indicating a surgical emergency.
  • Appendicitis: Typically starts with central abdominal pain that migrates to the right iliac fossa, associated with fever and anorexia.
  • Intestinal obstruction: Characterised by colicky abdominal pain, absolute constipation, and abdominal distension.
  • Pancreatitis: Involves severe epigastric pain radiating to the back, often relieved by leaning forward.
  • Cholecystitis: Presents with right upper quadrant pain, fever, and a positive Murphy's sign on examination.
  • Mesenteric ischaemia: An excruciating abdominal pain that is disproportionate to clinical signs in an older patient with vascular risk factors.
  • Raised Intracranial Pressure: Associated with headache (often worse in the mornings), visual changes, and altered mental status.
  • Brain tumour: Can present with persistent headache, focal neurological deficits, seizures, and personality changes.
  • Pancreatic Cancer: Often presents insidiously with painless jaundice, weight loss, and new-onset diabetes.
  • Gastric cancer: May present with persistent dyspepsia, dysphagia, weight loss, and signs of anaemia.
  • Silent Myocardial Infarction: Atypical presentation of a heart attack, particularly in elderly or diabetic patients, with nausea and fatigue instead of chest pain.
  • Hyperglycaemic Crisis: A metabolic emergency in diabetes with symptoms of profound dehydration and altered consciousness.
  • Diabetic Ketoacidosis: Characterised by hyperglycaemia, ketosis, and acidosis, leading to nausea, vomiting, and Kussmaul breathing.
  • HHS: Severe hyperglycaemia and dehydration without significant ketosis, typically in type 2 diabetes.
  • Acute Glaucoma: Presents with a severely painful, red eye, blurred vision, and seeing haloes around lights.
Common causes
  • Gastroenteritis: An acute onset of diarrhoea and vomiting, often with abdominal cramps and sometimes fever.
  • URTI: Upper respiratory tract infections can cause nausea, particularly in children, alongside typical cold or flu symptoms.
  • Labyrinthitis: Sudden onset of vertigo, nausea, and vomiting, often following a viral illness.
  • UTI: Urinary tract infection can present with nausea and vomiting, especially in the elderly, alongside urinary symptoms.
  • Gastritis: Inflammation of the stomach lining causing epigastric pain, nausea, and a feeling of fullness.
  • Peptic Ulcer Disease: Presents with a gnawing or burning epigastric pain, often related to meals.
  • Migraine: A severe, throbbing headache often accompanied by nausea, vomiting, and sensitivity to light and sound.
  • Eating disorder: Can involve self-induced vomiting or gastroparesis as a complication of disordered eating patterns.
  • Psychogenic: Vomiting related to psychological stress or anxiety with no identifiable organic cause.
  • COVID-19: Can present with a range of symptoms, including nausea and vomiting, often with fever, cough, and fatigue.
Easily missed
  • Medication induced nausea and vomiting: A common side effect of many drugs, including opioids, antibiotics, and NSAIDs.
  • Hyperemesis gravidarum: Severe and persistent nausea and vomiting during pregnancy, leading to dehydration and weight loss.
  • Chronic Kidney Disease: Advanced kidney failure can cause uraemia, leading to persistent nausea and a metallic taste.
  • Hypercalcaemia: High calcium levels, often due to malignancy or hyperparathyroidism, causing "bones, stones, groans, and psychiatric moans".
  • Addison's disease: Adrenal insufficiency presenting with fatigue, weakness, weight loss, and hyperpigmentation.
  • Diabetic Gastroparesis: Delayed gastric emptying in patients with long-standing diabetes, causing early satiety and post-prandial vomiting.
Other Differential Diagnoses to consider
  • Meniere’s disease: Episodic vertigo with nausea and vomiting, associated with hearing loss and tinnitus.
  • Subarachnoid haemorrhage: Sudden severe headache, nausea, vomiting, and neurological symptoms.
  • Phaeochromocytoma: Paroxysmal hypertension, headache, sweating, and palpitations alongside nausea.
  • Metabolic acidosis: A complication of renal or gastrointestinal disorders, presenting with nausea and general malaise.
  • Small bowel obstruction: Colicky abdominal pain, vomiting, and constipation in a post-surgical or obstructive setting.
  • Cyclic vomiting syndrome: Recurrent, severe episodes of nausea and vomiting with symptom-free intervals.
  • Lead poisoning: Chronic exposure leading to nausea, abdominal pain, and neurological signs.
History Taking
Patient’s Description: How can I help you today?
Start by asking an open-ended question such as "Can you tell me more about what brought you here today?" Relevance: This allows the patient to describe their symptoms in their own words. It gives insight into how they perceive their illness and helps guide further specific questions based on the details they provide.
When did the nausea and vomiting start?
  • This question helps establish the onset, whether the symptoms are acute or chronic, and identifies potential triggers or precipitating events.
  • Relevance: An acute onset might suggest gastroenteritis, appendicitis, or acute abdomen. A more insidious onset could point towards chronic conditions like gastric cancer or gastroparesis.
How frequent is the vomiting? How severe is it?
  • Understanding the frequency and severity of vomiting helps assess the degree of dehydration and the impact on the patient’s health.
  • Relevance: Frequent, severe vomiting may suggest conditions like hyperemesis gravidarum or obstruction, while intermittent episodes could indicate migraine or psychogenic causes.
Have you noticed any patterns or triggers?
  • Identify factors that worsen or relieve symptoms, such as meals, stress, or certain positions.
  • Relevance: Vomiting after meals could suggest peptic ulcer disease or gastritis, while stress-related triggers might suggest psychogenic causes.
Have you experienced any other symptoms? (e.g., fever, headache, abdominal pain)
  • Associated symptoms can help differentiate between causes.
  • Relevance: Fever suggests infection (e.g., gastroenteritis, UTI), headache might suggest raised intracranial pressure or migraine, and abdominal pain could indicate appendicitis, cholecystitis, or pancreatitis.
Any recent changes in your bowel habits? (diarrhoea, constipation)
  • Bowel habit changes can provide clues to underlying gastrointestinal disorders.
  • Relevance: Diarrhoea may suggest gastroenteritis, while constipation with vomiting could indicate bowel obstruction.
Do you have any history of migraines, diabetes, or chronic kidney disease?
  • Explore past medical history for conditions that may predispose to nausea and vomiting.
  • Relevance: Migraines may explain nausea as part of the prodrome, while diabetes raises the suspicion of gastroparesis or DKA. Chronic kidney disease could indicate uraemia as a cause.
Are you taking any medications, including over-the-counter or herbal remedies?
  • Medication history is crucial to rule out drug-induced nausea and vomiting.
  • Relevance: Drugs such as NSAIDs, opiates, and antibiotics are common culprits for gastrointestinal upset.
Ideas, Concerns, and Expectations (ICE)
  • Asking about the patient’s ideas, concerns, and expectations ensures their perspective is heard, and it helps build rapport.
  • Relevance: It may reveal hidden fears, such as concerns about cancer or heart problems, and clarify what the patient hopes to achieve from the consultation (e.g., reassurance, further testing).
Examination Strategy
General inspection and observation
  • Start with an overall assessment of the patient's appearance, checking for signs of dehydration (dry mucous membranes, sunken eyes) or distress.
  • Relevance: General malaise and signs of dehydration may suggest conditions like gastroenteritis, DKA, or hyperemesis gravidarum. A normal general appearance may argue against acute, severe pathology.
Vital signs (temperature, heart rate, blood pressure, respiratory rate)
  • Check for fever, tachycardia, hypotension, and respiratory abnormalities.
  • Relevance: Fever may indicate infections (gastroenteritis, UTI). Tachycardia and hypotension may point to dehydration or sepsis. Tachypnoea and Kussmaul breathing can suggest metabolic acidosis, such as DKA.
Abdominal examination (inspection, palpation, percussion, auscultation)
  • Inspect for any distension, scars, or hernias. Palpate to check for tenderness, guarding, or masses. Percuss for abnormal fluid or tympany, and auscultate bowel sounds.
  • Relevance: Localised tenderness could suggest appendicitis, cholecystitis, or pancreatitis. Diffuse tenderness might indicate gastroenteritis. Absence of bowel sounds or significant distension may suggest bowel obstruction.
Neurological examination (focusing on mental status and cranial nerves)
  • Assess the patient’s level of consciousness, and check for any focal neurological deficits, including cranial nerve examination.
  • Relevance: Altered mental status, headache, and papilloedema may point to raised intracranial pressure or brain tumours. Cranial nerve abnormalities can suggest posterior fossa tumours or labyrinthitis.
Cardiovascular examination
  • Auscultate for heart murmurs, check for peripheral pulses, and assess for signs of fluid overload or shock.
  • Relevance: Signs of fluid overload or a weak pulse may suggest heart failure. A silent myocardial infarction can present with nausea and vomiting in elderly patients.
Eye examination (fundoscopy and checking for signs of acute glaucoma)
  • Examine the eyes for signs of papilloedema and assess for raised intraocular pressure if acute glaucoma is suspected.
  • Relevance: Papilloedema may suggest raised intracranial pressure, while a red, painful eye with vision loss could indicate acute glaucoma.
Skin examination
  • Inspect the skin for rashes, pallor, jaundice, or signs of chronic disease (e.g., hyperpigmentation).
  • Relevance: Jaundice may suggest liver disease or pancreatitis. Hyperpigmentation can be a sign of Addison’s disease. Pallor could suggest anaemia or chronic kidney disease.
Red Flag Synthesis
Persistent vomiting with weight loss
  • Persistent vomiting accompanied by significant weight loss is concerning for malignancy or chronic gastrointestinal diseases.
  • Significance: This could indicate gastric cancer, pancreatic cancer, or chronic gastrointestinal obstruction, requiring urgent evaluation, including endoscopy or imaging.
Severe abdominal pain with vomiting
  • Severe abdominal pain associated with vomiting suggests serious intra-abdominal pathology such as acute surgical emergencies.
  • Significance: This could indicate acute abdomen conditions like appendicitis, intestinal obstruction, pancreatitis, or mesenteric ischaemia, all of which require urgent surgical or medical intervention.
Headache with nausea, vomiting, and neurological signs
  • The combination of headache, nausea, and vomiting, especially with neurological signs (e.g., confusion, visual disturbances), is concerning for raised intracranial pressure.
  • Significance: This could indicate raised intracranial pressure due to a brain tumour, intracranial haemorrhage, or other space-occupying lesions, requiring urgent neuroimaging.
Haematemesis (vomiting blood)
  • Vomiting blood is a critical symptom, indicating a possible upper gastrointestinal bleed.
  • Significance: This could indicate peptic ulcer disease, gastric cancer, oesophageal varices, or Mallory-Weiss tear, all of which require urgent intervention, including endoscopy and resuscitation if needed.
Altered mental status with vomiting
  • Altered consciousness or confusion in a patient with vomiting can indicate systemic or neurological emergencies.
  • Significance: This could suggest DKA, hyperosmolar hyperglycaemic state (HHS), sepsis, or meningitis, all requiring immediate diagnostic work-up and treatment.
Severe, acute onset headache ("thunderclap" headache)
  • A sudden, severe headache with vomiting may be a sign of a subarachnoid haemorrhage or other serious neurological conditions.
  • Significance: This could indicate a subarachnoid haemorrhage, which is life-threatening and requires immediate neuroimaging and specialist intervention.
Severe eye pain with vomiting
  • Acute, severe eye pain with nausea and vomiting is suggestive of acute angle-closure glaucoma, a vision-threatening emergency.
  • Significance: This could indicate acute angle-closure glaucoma, which requires urgent ophthalmological evaluation and management to prevent permanent vision loss.
Chest pain with nausea and vomiting
  • Chest pain accompanied by nausea and vomiting, especially in high-risk patients, raises concern for cardiovascular emergencies.
  • Significance: This could indicate a myocardial infarction (silent or atypical presentation), especially in elderly or diabetic patients. Immediate assessment and intervention are required.
Investigations Strategy & Interpretation
Full blood count (FBC)
  • FBC helps assess for infection (raised white cell count), anaemia (low haemoglobin), or other haematological abnormalities.
  • Result Significance: Raised white cell count suggests infection (e.g., gastroenteritis, UTI), while low haemoglobin could indicate gastrointestinal bleeding or malignancy. Normal results may help rule out these conditions.
Urea and electrolytes (U&E)
  • U&E tests are useful to assess hydration status, kidney function, and electrolyte imbalances, which can contribute to nausea and vomiting.
  • Result Significance: Electrolyte disturbances (e.g., hypokalaemia, hyponatraemia) may suggest dehydration, vomiting-induced losses, or renal impairment. Abnormal renal function could point to chronic kidney disease or acute kidney injury.
Liver function tests (LFTs) and amylase
  • LFTs assess liver function and biliary obstruction, while amylase is used to diagnose pancreatitis.
  • Result Significance: Elevated LFTs suggest hepatitis, cholecystitis, or liver metastases, while raised amylase points to acute pancreatitis. Normal results may help rule out these conditions.
Blood glucose and ketones
  • Blood glucose and ketones are critical for evaluating metabolic causes such as diabetic ketoacidosis (DKA) and hyperosmolar hyperglycaemic state (HHS).
  • Result Significance: Elevated blood glucose and ketones suggest DKA or HHS, which require immediate treatment. Normal levels may exclude these diagnoses.
Urinalysis
  • Urinalysis can help identify infections, glucose, ketones, or blood in the urine.
  • Result Significance: The presence of glucose and ketones may suggest DKA, while blood or leucocytes may indicate a UTI. Negative findings could help rule out these causes.
Abdominal ultrasound
  • An ultrasound is a non-invasive way to assess the gallbladder, liver, pancreas, and other abdominal structures.
  • Result Significance: Positive findings may show cholecystitis, pancreatitis, or liver masses. A normal ultrasound helps exclude these conditions.
CT abdomen and pelvis
  • CT imaging is crucial for diagnosing abdominal emergencies and malignancies when clinical suspicion is high.
  • Result Significance: A positive result may identify intestinal obstruction, appendicitis, tumours, or pancreatitis. A negative scan reduces the likelihood of serious abdominal pathology.
Endoscopy (upper gastrointestinal)
  • Endoscopy is indicated if there is suspicion of upper gastrointestinal bleeding, cancer, or peptic ulcer disease.
  • Result Significance: Positive findings may show gastric cancer, peptic ulcers, or oesophagitis. Negative findings reduce the likelihood of these upper GI pathologies.
ECG (electrocardiogram)
  • An ECG is important to rule out cardiac causes of nausea and vomiting, such as myocardial infarction (MI).
  • Result Significance: Positive findings may suggest silent MI, particularly in diabetic or elderly patients. A normal ECG makes a cardiac cause less likely.
Evidence-Based Diagnosis (EBDx)
Fever in bacterial infections (e.g., gastroenteritis, UTI)
  • Fever is a common sign in infections causing nausea and vomiting, such as gastroenteritis or urinary tract infections. However, fever alone is not highly specific for infection, as it can be present in other inflammatory or malignant conditions.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 55-75%, Specificity: 70-80%, Likelihood Ratio: 2.2 for bacterial infections.
  • Significance: A positive fever increases the likelihood of infection (e.g., gastroenteritis, UTI), but its absence does not exclude these diagnoses.
Abdominal tenderness in acute abdomen (e.g., appendicitis, cholecystitis)
  • Abdominal tenderness is a key sign in acute abdominal conditions like appendicitis or cholecystitis. Specific tenderness on palpation, especially rebound tenderness, is highly suggestive of an acute surgical abdomen.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 82%, Specificity: 85%, Positive Likelihood Ratio: 5.5 for appendicitis.
  • Significance: Abdominal tenderness, especially with rebound, is highly indicative of acute surgical conditions. The absence of tenderness makes an acute abdomen less likely.
Ketones in urine or blood for DKA
  • Elevated ketones in the urine or blood are critical for diagnosing diabetic ketoacidosis (DKA) in patients presenting with nausea and vomiting, especially in known diabetics or hyperglycaemic patients.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 98%, Specificity: 85%, Positive Likelihood Ratio: 6.5 for DKA.
  • Significance: Positive ketones strongly suggest DKA, which is a medical emergency. Absence of ketones largely excludes this diagnosis.
Raised amylase for acute pancreatitis
  • Serum amylase levels are often elevated in acute pancreatitis and are used in conjunction with clinical signs and imaging to diagnose this condition in patients with nausea and vomiting.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 90%, Specificity: 75%, Likelihood Ratio: 3.6 for acute pancreatitis.
  • Significance: Raised amylase strongly indicates acute pancreatitis, particularly when associated with typical abdominal pain. Normal amylase levels do not entirely rule out the condition.
Electrolyte disturbances in chronic vomiting (e.g., hypokalaemia)
  • Electrolyte disturbances, particularly hypokalaemia and hyponatraemia, can result from chronic vomiting, indicating the need for correction and further evaluation of the underlying cause.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 70%, Specificity: 90%, Positive Likelihood Ratio: 7.0 for electrolyte imbalance in chronic vomiting.
  • Significance: Electrolyte imbalances are common in chronic vomiting and suggest dehydration or renal impairment. Normal levels make chronic vomiting-induced electrolyte issues less likely.
Abdominal ultrasound for gallbladder disease (e.g., cholecystitis)
  • Abdominal ultrasound is a first-line imaging test for detecting gallbladder disease, particularly cholecystitis, in patients presenting with nausea, vomiting, and upper abdominal pain.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 88%, Specificity: 94%, Likelihood Ratio: 14.6 for cholecystitis on ultrasound.
  • Significance: A positive ultrasound for gallstones or gallbladder inflammation is highly indicative of cholecystitis. A negative scan reduces the likelihood of gallbladder disease but may not rule it out completely.
CT scan for intestinal obstruction
  • CT scanning is highly sensitive for detecting intestinal obstruction, particularly in patients with nausea, vomiting, and abdominal distension.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 94%, Specificity: 96%, Likelihood Ratio: 23.5 for intestinal obstruction on CT.
  • Significance: A positive CT scan is highly diagnostic for bowel obstruction, while a negative scan nearly excludes the diagnosis.
Diagnostic Timeout & Cognitive Biases

Recognising and mitigating cognitive biases is critical for accurate diagnosis in patients with non-specific symptoms like nausea and vomiting. Before settling on a diagnosis, it is vital to take a 'diagnostic timeout' to challenge initial assumptions.

  • Anchoring Bias: This is the tendency to fixate on the first piece of information received. For example, latching onto a history of recent travel to diagnose gastroenteritis, while potentially overlooking red flags for a surgical abdomen like localised tenderness or guarding. A key question to ask is: "If it wasn't gastroenteritis, what else could it be?"
  • Confirmation Bias: This involves selectively seeking or overvaluing information that confirms a pre-existing belief. For instance, focusing on a patient's known anxiety to diagnose psychogenic vomiting while downplaying new-onset headaches that might suggest raised intracranial pressure.
  • Atypical Presentations: A major pitfall is failing to consider that serious conditions can present unusually. Myocardial infarction can present solely with nausea and sweating, particularly in women, the elderly, or patients with diabetes. Likewise, a urinary tract infection in an older person may present with vomiting and confusion without any urinary symptoms. A high index of suspicion is essential.
  • Premature Closure: This is accepting a diagnosis before it has been fully verified. A common example is diagnosing "gastritis" in a patient with epigastric pain and vomiting without considering more serious mimics like pancreatitis or even inferior myocardial infarction. Always ask: "What is the most dangerous thing this could be, and have I done enough to rule it out?"
Illustrative Management Pathways

For educational purposes, this section describes the typical principles of management for key diagnoses. These are not prescriptive instructions. All management must be guided by official UK guidelines, local formularies, and senior clinical advice.

Viral Gastroenteritis

Management is primarily supportive. The focus is on preventing and treating dehydration through oral rehydration solutions. Anti-emetics (e.g., cyclizine or ondansetron) may be considered for symptomatic relief if vomiting is severe, but are often not necessary. Antidiarrhoeals are generally not recommended. Patients are advised on hygiene measures to prevent transmission. Hospital admission is considered for those with signs of severe dehydration or who are unable to tolerate oral fluids.

Diabetic Ketoacidosis (DKA)

DKA is a medical emergency requiring immediate hospital admission, often to a high-dependency setting. The core principles of management are fluid resuscitation with intravenous fluids (typically 0.9% sodium chloride), correction of hyperglycaemia with a fixed-rate intravenous insulin infusion, and replacement of electrolytes, particularly potassium. Close monitoring of blood glucose, ketones, and electrolytes is critical. The underlying trigger for the DKA (e.g., infection) must also be identified and treated.

Intestinal Obstruction

Suspected intestinal obstruction requires urgent hospital admission under the care of the surgical team. Initial management involves making the patient 'nil by mouth', inserting a nasogastric tube to decompress the stomach ('drip and suck'), providing intravenous fluids to correct dehydration and electrolyte imbalances, and administering analgesia. The definitive management depends on the cause and severity of the obstruction and may range from conservative management to emergency surgery.

Raised Intracranial Pressure

This is a neurological emergency requiring immediate specialist assessment. Management is focused on identifying and treating the underlying cause (e.g., tumour, haemorrhage). Immediate measures in a critical care setting aim to reduce intracranial pressure and may include head elevation, controlled hyperventilation, and administration of osmotic agents like mannitol or hypertonic saline. Definitive treatment may involve neurosurgery (e.g., to evacuate a haematoma or resect a tumour) or medical management.

Reflection & Learning

These brief cases illustrate the application of clinical reasoning to different presentations of nausea and vomiting. They are for educational purposes only.

Case 1: Suspected Gastroenteritis in Primary Care

A 22-year-old university student calls their GP with a 24-hour history of profuse vomiting and watery diarrhoea. They also complain of generalised abdominal cramps. They have no significant past medical history. The problem representation is a young, otherwise healthy individual with acute onset vomiting and diarrhoea, most consistent with viral gastroenteritis. After confirming no red flags (e.g., severe localised pain, signs of significant dehydration), management involves advice on oral rehydration and hygiene, with safety netting instructions to seek further help if symptoms worsen or do not resolve.

Case 2: Suspected Bowel Obstruction in the Emergency Department

An 80-year-old woman with a history of previous abdominal surgery presents to the Emergency Department with a 2-day history of worsening abdominal pain, vomiting, and not having opened her bowels for 3 days. On examination, her abdomen is distended and tender, with high-pitched bowel sounds. The problem representation is an elderly patient with a key risk factor (previous surgery) presenting with the classic triad of vomiting, abdominal pain, and absolute constipation, highly suspicious for intestinal obstruction. The immediate pathway involves urgent surgical review, intravenous fluids, a nasogastric tube, and an urgent CT scan of the abdomen.

Case 3: Suspected DKA in a Young Adult

A 19-year-old known to have type 1 diabetes is brought in by family, feeling unwell for 2 days with nausea, vomiting, and increasing drowsiness. They have been unable to keep their insulin down. On examination, they are tachycardic, their mucous membranes are dry, and they are breathing deeply and rapidly. A capillary blood glucose is critically high and ketones are present in their urine. The problem representation is a young person with type 1 diabetes presenting with vomiting and altered mental status, classic for DKA. This is a medical emergency requiring immediate hospital admission for fluid and insulin therapy according to established DKA protocols.
