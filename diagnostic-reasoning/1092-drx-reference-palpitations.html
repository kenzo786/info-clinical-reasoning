Palpitations Diagnostic Reasoning Reference
Context: Palpitations Assessment
Overview

Palpitations, the sensation of a forceful, rapid, or irregular heartbeat, are a very common reason for patients to seek medical advice in both primary and secondary care. The clinical challenge lies in distinguishing between benign, physiological causes (like anxiety or caffeine intake) and potentially life-threatening cardiac arrhythmias. A misdiagnosis can have severe consequences, including stroke in undiagnosed atrial fibrillation or sudden cardiac death from ventricular tachycardia.

A systematic and structured approach is therefore essential to identify red flag features, risk stratify patients appropriately, and guide investigation and management. This ensures that serious underlying conditions are promptly identified and treated, while avoiding unnecessary anxiety and investigation for those with benign causes. For definitive, real-time management protocols, users must refer to official UK guidelines from authoritative bodies such as NICE (e.g., CG180 for Atrial Fibrillation) and the Resuscitation Council UK.

Pathophysiology/Basic Science

Understanding the origin of palpitations is key to diagnosis. The sensation can be generated by several distinct electrophysiological mechanisms:

  • Enhanced Automaticity: This occurs when a group of cardiac cells outside of the sinoatrial (SA) node begins to depolarise and fire impulses faster than the SA node itself, taking over as the heart's primary pacemaker. This can be caused by factors like ischaemia, electrolyte disturbances (e.g., hypokalaemia), or stimulant drugs, leading to ectopic beats or sustained tachycardias.
  • Re-entry Circuits: This is the most common mechanism for clinically significant arrhythmias. It involves an electrical impulse that does not terminate after one pass but instead circulates repeatedly around a path of cardiac tissue. This can occur in small circuits (micro-re-entry) or large circuits (macro-re-entry). Examples include Atrioventricular Nodal Re-entrant Tachycardia (AVNRT), Atrioventricular Re-entrant Tachycardia (AVRT) involving an accessory pathway (e.g., Wolff-Parkinson-White syndrome), Atrial Flutter, and Ventricular Tachycardia (VT).
  • Triggered Activity: This occurs when abnormal electrical impulses arise from preceding cardiac action potentials. These are known as afterdepolarisations. Early afterdepolarisations (EADs) occur during the repolarisation phase and are associated with conditions that prolong the QT interval (e.g., certain drugs, Long QT syndrome), potentially leading to Torsades de Pointes. Delayed afterdepolarisations (DADs) occur after repolarisation is complete and are often associated with high intracellular calcium levels (e.g., digoxin toxicity, catecholaminergic states).
  • Physiological Sinus Tachycardia: This is not a pathological arrhythmia but a normal physiological response where the SA node fires at a rate >100 bpm. It is driven by the autonomic nervous system in response to stimuli such as exercise, stress, anxiety, or systemic conditions like fever, anaemia, hyperthyroidism, or hypovolaemia. While the mechanism is normal, the rapid rate can be perceived as palpitations.
Differential Diagnosis (DDx) Framework

A structured approach to the differential diagnosis of palpitations is essential to ensure life-threatening conditions are prioritised.

Must not miss!
  • Acute Coronary Syndrome: Can present with palpitations due to an ischaemia-induced arrhythmia, often accompanied by chest pain.
  • Narrow complex tachycardia: A rapid rhythm originating above the ventricles; includes Atrial Flutter, PSVT (e.g., AVNRT), and WPW syndrome, often causing sudden-onset palpitations.
  • Broad complex tachycardia: A life-threatening rapid rhythm from the ventricles, including Ventricular Tachycardia and Torsades de Pointes, often causing haemodynamic instability.
  • Bradycardia: An abnormally slow heart rate (e.g., from AV block or Sick Sinus Syndrome) can cause palpitations due to forceful escape beats.
  • Valvular heart disease: Structural issues like stenosis or regurgitation can precipitate arrhythmias or forceful heartbeats.
  • Pulmonary Embolism: May cause sinus tachycardia or atrial fibrillation due to acute right heart strain, often with pleuritic chest pain and hypoxia.
  • Hypoglycaemia: The adrenergic (adrenaline) surge in response to low blood sugar causes a rapid heartbeat and sweating.
Common causes
  • Anxiety: The physiological 'fight or flight' response causes sinus tachycardia and a heightened awareness of a normal heartbeat.
  • Sinus tachycardia: A normal increase in heart rate due to systemic causes like fever, hyperthyroidism, or anaemia.
  • Premature ectopic beats: Benign extra heartbeats originating from the atria or ventricles, often felt as a 'skipped' or 'extra' beat.
  • Atrial fibrillation: A very common cause of an irregular, often rapid, pulse, particularly in older adults.
  • COVID-19: Can be associated with palpitations due to fever, hypoxia, anxiety, or direct inflammatory effects on the heart.
Easily missed
  • Drug Induced Palpitations: Often caused by stimulants (caffeine, nicotine, cocaine), sympathomimetic drugs, or as a side effect of prescribed medication.
  • Electrolyte disturbance: Imbalances, particularly low potassium or magnesium, can provoke ectopic beats and arrhythmias.
Other Differential Diagnoses to consider
  • Phaeochromocytoma: Rare tumour releasing catecholamines, causing episodic hypertension, headaches, and palpitations.
  • Mitral valve prolapse: Can cause palpitations, atypical chest pain, and clicks on auscultation due to valve abnormalities.
  • Pericarditis: Inflammation of the pericardium may lead to sinus tachycardia or atrial arrhythmias.
  • Hypertrophic cardiomyopathy: An inherited structural heart disease that is a key cause of arrhythmias and sudden death in the young.
  • Hypovolaemia: Reduced blood volume from dehydration or bleeding results in a compensatory tachycardia.
History Taking
**Patient’s Description**: How can I help you today?
It’s important to start with open-ended questions such as, "Can you tell me more about what brought you here today?" This allows the patient to describe the issue in their own words, which can guide more targeted questions later in the consultation. Relevance: This open-ended approach may reveal details about the nature, timing, and perceived severity of palpitations, giving you clues about potential triggers or associated conditions.
When did the palpitations start?
  • This helps determine the onset and acute versus chronic nature of the symptoms.
  • Relevance: A sudden onset may suggest arrhythmia or acute coronary syndrome, while a gradual onset could indicate anxiety or thyroid disorders. Chronic palpitations could be related to anaemia or atrial fibrillation.
How long do the palpitations last?
  • This helps in identifying the duration of episodes, distinguishing between transient and sustained arrhythmias.
  • Relevance: Brief episodes could suggest premature ectopic beats, whereas prolonged episodes might indicate atrial fibrillation or ventricular tachycardia.
What does it feel like? Is there a fast or irregular heartbeat?
  • This explores the quality of the palpitations, whether they are rapid, irregular, or pounding.
  • Relevance: A rapid and regular heartbeat may suggest supraventricular tachycardia (SVT), while a fast and irregular sensation is classic for atrial fibrillation. A slow or pounding sensation could indicate bradycardia or anxiety.
Are there any triggers or anything that worsens or relieves the palpitations?
  • This helps identify factors that precipitate or alleviate the symptoms.
  • Relevance: Palpitations triggered by exertion might point to cardiac causes (e.g., valvular heart disease or ischaemia), while those relieved by rest may suggest anxiety or benign causes like premature beats. Caffeine or stimulant use might suggest drug-induced palpitations.
Do you have any associated symptoms such as chest pain, breathlessness, dizziness, or syncope?
  • These symptoms are important in evaluating the severity and potential cause of the palpitations.
  • Relevance: Chest pain may indicate acute coronary syndrome or pulmonary embolism, while breathlessness could suggest heart failure or valvular disease. Dizziness or syncope (fainting) are red flags for dangerous arrhythmias such as ventricular tachycardia or AV block.
Any past medical history of thyroid disease, heart disease, or anxiety?
  • Exploring past medical history helps identify predisposing conditions.
  • Relevance: A history of thyroid disease may indicate hyperthyroidism, and heart disease raises the suspicion of arrhythmias or structural heart problems. Anxiety can also be a frequent cause of palpitations.
**Ideas, Concerns, and Expectations (ICE)**
  • Asking about the patient's ideas, concerns, and expectations ensures that you address their perspective and fears.
  • Relevance: For example, the patient may be worried about a heart attack or other serious causes. It is essential to explore these concerns and manage their expectations around treatment and investigation.
Examination Strategy
Inspection: General appearance
  • Observe the patient for signs of distress, anxiety, or pallor, which could suggest underlying systemic causes such as anaemia or anxiety.
  • Relevance: A pale appearance may indicate anaemia, while signs of distress might point towards more serious cardiovascular conditions like acute coronary syndrome.
Palpation: Pulse rate and rhythm
  • Palpate the radial pulse to assess the rate, regularity, and character.
  • Relevance: An irregularly irregular pulse is the cardinal sign of atrial fibrillation. A fast and regular pulse could indicate sinus tachycardia or supraventricular tachycardia, while bradycardia may point to heart block.
Auscultation: Heart sounds
  • Auscultate the heart to identify abnormal sounds such as murmurs, extra heart sounds, or rubs.
  • Relevance: A murmur may indicate valvular heart disease, a known cause of palpitations. Extra sounds such as an S3 may suggest heart failure, while a pericardial rub could indicate pericarditis.
Inspection: Jugular venous pressure (JVP)
  • Assess the JVP to check for signs of raised venous pressure, which may indicate cardiac dysfunction.
  • Relevance: A raised JVP suggests heart failure or conditions causing right heart strain, which can both cause palpitations. A normal JVP reduces the likelihood of these diagnoses.
Palpation: Peripheral oedema
  • Check for signs of peripheral oedema in the ankles and lower limbs, which may suggest fluid overload.
  • Relevance: Pitting peripheral oedema is often seen in congestive heart failure, which is a common cause of palpitations.
Auscultation: Lung fields
  • Auscultate the lungs for signs of fluid overload or pulmonary complications.
  • Relevance: Bibasal inspiratory crackles may suggest heart failure (pulmonary oedema). Clear lung fields reduce the likelihood of this diagnosis.
Blood pressure measurement
  • Check the patient’s blood pressure to assess for hypotension, hypertension, or orthostatic changes.
  • Relevance: Hypotension in the context of palpitations is a major red flag suggesting a life-threatening arrhythmia. Hypertension is a key risk factor for atrial fibrillation and structural heart disease.
Red Flag Synthesis

This section synthesises the key red flags from the source document. Any of these findings in a patient with palpitations necessitates urgent senior review and likely hospital admission.

Chest pain

Significance: Chest pain accompanying palpitations, particularly if crushing or radiating, could indicate an acute coronary syndrome (ACS) where the arrhythmia is a consequence of myocardial ischaemia. This is a medical emergency.

Syncope or near-syncope

Significance: Fainting or feeling about to faint suggests that the arrhythmia is causing a significant drop in cardiac output and cerebral hypoperfusion. This may indicate a dangerous underlying rhythm such as ventricular tachycardia or a high-grade AV block.

Severe shortness of breath

Significance: The sudden onset of severe breathlessness with palpitations can indicate the development of acute heart failure or a massive pulmonary embolism, both of which require immediate hospital treatment.

Irregular pulse

Significance: A new finding of an irregularly irregular pulse is highly suggestive of atrial fibrillation, which carries a significant risk of stroke and requires urgent assessment for anticoagulation and rate/rhythm control.

Family history of sudden cardiac death

Significance: A family history of sudden, unexplained death, particularly in a young relative, raises the possibility of an inherited cardiac condition like hypertrophic cardiomyopathy or an ion channelopathy (e.g., Long QT syndrome), which can cause lethal arrhythmias.

Severe dizziness or lightheadedness

Significance: While mild lightheadedness can be benign, severe or persistent dizziness suggests haemodynamic compromise and could indicate a life-threatening arrhythmia requiring urgent evaluation.

Investigations Strategy & Interpretation
Electrocardiogram (ECG)
  • An ECG is the essential first-line investigation. It records the heart's electrical activity and can diagnose ongoing arrhythmias, or show features suggestive of underlying structural disease or predisposition to arrhythmia (e.g., long QT, delta wave of WPW).
  • Result Significance: A positive result could confirm atrial fibrillation, ventricular tachycardia, or other arrhythmias. A normal result during a palpitations-free period is common and does not rule out an intermittent arrhythmia, often necessitating ambulatory monitoring.
Holter monitor (24-48 hours)
  • This is an ambulatory ECG that monitors the heart’s rhythm continuously over 24 to 48 hours, aiming to capture intermittent symptoms and correlate them with the underlying rhythm.
  • Result Significance: Positive findings may reveal paroxysmal arrhythmias such as atrial fibrillation or supraventricular tachycardia. A negative result reduces the likelihood of a frequent arrhythmic cause for the palpitations.
Echocardiogram
  • An ultrasound scan of the heart used to assess cardiac structure and function. It is crucial for identifying underlying causes like valvular heart disease, hypertrophic cardiomyopathy, or impaired left ventricular function.
  • Result Significance: A positive result could reveal structural heart disease (e.g., mitral valve prolapse, hypertrophic cardiomyopathy), which would require specialist management. A normal echo is reassuring and reduces the likelihood of a structural cause.
Thyroid function tests
  • A blood test to measure levels of TSH, T3, and T4, as hyperthyroidism is a common and reversible cause of sinus tachycardia and atrial fibrillation.
  • Result Significance: Elevated free T3/T4 with suppressed TSH confirms hyperthyroidism. Normal results make a thyroid-related cause unlikely.
Full blood count (FBC)
  • A full blood count is performed to identify anaemia, which can cause a compensatory sinus tachycardia leading to palpitations.
  • Result Significance: A low haemoglobin level indicates anaemia, which, when treated, may resolve the palpitations. A normal result rules out anaemia as a primary cause.
Serum electrolytes
  • A blood test to check levels of key electrolytes, as imbalances (particularly in potassium, magnesium, and calcium) can predispose a patient to arrhythmias.
  • Result Significance: Abnormal electrolyte levels (e.g., hypokalaemia, hypomagnesaemia) suggest a reversible cause for arrhythmias. Normal results make an electrolyte disturbance less likely.
D-dimer
  • A blood test used in patients with a low clinical probability of venous thromboembolism. Palpitations can be a symptom of a pulmonary embolism (PE).
  • Result Significance: A negative result in a low-risk patient helps to rule out PE. A positive result is non-specific and requires further imaging (e.g., CTPA) to confirm or exclude PE.
Toxicology screen (if drug-induced palpitations are suspected)
  • A urine or blood test may be considered in cases where palpitations are suspected to be caused by recreational substance use (e.g., cocaine, amphetamines).
  • Result Significance: A positive result suggests a drug-induced cause, guiding management towards counselling and cessation of the substance.
Evidence-Based Diagnosis (EBDx)
Irregularly Irregular Pulse (Atrial Fibrillation)
  • An irregularly irregular pulse is a key clinical sign of atrial fibrillation (AF), and its presence significantly increases the likelihood of this diagnosis.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: ~87%, Specificity: ~92%, Positive Likelihood Ratio: 10.9.
  • Reference: Cook, D. A. et al. (2009). The relation between clinical examination and atrial fibrillation. A systematic review. J Gen Intern Med.
  • Significance: A positive finding on palpation is a strong indicator of atrial fibrillation, mandating an ECG for confirmation.
ECG findings for Atrial Fibrillation
  • An ECG showing an absence of P waves, a chaotic baseline, and an irregularly irregular ventricular response is considered the gold standard for diagnosing atrial fibrillation.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity and Specificity approach 100% for diagnosing AF when classic features are present on a 12-lead ECG.
  • Reference: NICE guideline [CG180] Atrial fibrillation: diagnosis and management (2021).
  • Significance: A positive ECG confirms the diagnosis, guiding treatment towards anticoagulation and rhythm or rate control. A negative ECG does not rule out paroxysmal AF.
Thyroid Function Tests (Hyperthyroidism)
  • Thyroid function tests can reveal hyperthyroidism, which is a common and treatable cause of palpitations and atrial fibrillation.
  • Evidence: Hyperthyroidism is found in approximately 1-2% of patients presenting with new-onset atrial fibrillation.
  • Reference: NICE Clinical Knowledge Summary (CKS) for Atrial Fibrillation.
  • Significance: A positive result confirms hyperthyroidism as a likely cause, requiring management of the thyroid disorder alongside the arrhythmia.
D-dimer for Pulmonary Embolism
  • The D-dimer test is highly sensitive for ruling out thromboembolic disease in patients with a low pre-test probability (e.g., low Wells score).
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: >95%, Specificity: ~50%, Negative Predictive Value: >99% for ruling out PE when used in low-risk patients.
  • Reference: NICE guideline [NG158] Venous thromboembolic diseases: diagnosis, management and thrombophilia testing (2020).
  • Significance: A negative D-dimer in a low-risk patient effectively excludes PE as the cause of palpitations. A positive result is non-specific and mandates further imaging.
Holter Monitor (Paroxysmal Arrhythmias)
  • A 24-48 hour Holter monitor is key for detecting intermittent arrhythmias that are not captured on a resting ECG.
  • Evidence: The diagnostic yield of a 24-hour Holter monitor in patients with palpitations is typically between 35% and 55%, increasing with longer monitoring periods.
  • Reference: Giada, F., et al. (2007). The diagnostic yield of a new external wearable event recorder in patients with palpitations. Journal of Electrocardiology.
  • Significance: A positive result that correlates symptoms with a specific arrhythmia confirms the diagnosis. A negative result reduces, but does not eliminate, the likelihood of an arrhythmic cause.
Diagnostic Timeout & Cognitive Biases

Awareness of common thinking errors is essential to improve diagnostic accuracy and patient safety when assessing a patient with palpitations. Before finalising a diagnosis, it is good practice for clinicians to take a 'diagnostic timeout' to challenge their own reasoning.

  • Anchoring Bias: The tendency to lock onto an early, often benign, diagnosis, especially in younger patients. For example, attributing palpitations in a young, anxious woman solely to 'anxiety' without adequately considering and excluding paroxysmal Supraventricular Tachycardia (SVT). A key question to ask is: "If not anxiety, what else could this be?"
  • Confirmation Bias: Actively seeking out or overvaluing evidence that supports an initial belief while ignoring contradictory data. For example, focusing on a patient's high caffeine intake to confirm a benign diagnosis while downplaying a new symptom of exertional breathlessness, which might point to underlying heart disease.
  • Atypical Presentations: A major pitfall is forgetting that serious diseases can present non-classically. For instance, an older patient might describe their new-onset Atrial Fibrillation simply as 'feeling tired' or 'a bit off', rather than as classic palpitations. A high index of suspicion must be maintained, especially in elderly patients or those with multiple comorbidities.
  • Over-reliance on Normal Tests: Placing too much weight on a single reassuring but non-definitive test result. A single normal 12-lead ECG in a patient with a history of intermittent, sudden-onset palpitations is often falsely reassuring. It does not rule out a paroxysmal arrhythmia, and the clinician must consider the need for ambulatory monitoring (e.g., a Holter monitor) if the history is compelling.
Illustrative Management Pathways

For educational purposes, this section describes the typical principles of management for key diagnoses. These are not prescriptive instructions. All management must be guided by official UK guidelines, local formularies, and senior clinical advice.

Atrial Fibrillation (AF)

Once AF is confirmed on an ECG, management is twofold. First, control the ventricular rate using medications like beta-blockers or calcium channel blockers. Second, assess the patient's stroke risk using the CHA₂DS₂-VASc score. Most patients will require long-term anticoagulation (typically with a Direct-Acting Oral Anticoagulant - DOAC) to reduce this risk. Rhythm control strategies (e.g., cardioversion, anti-arrhythmic drugs) may be considered in some patients, particularly if they are younger or highly symptomatic.

Supraventricular Tachycardia (SVT)

In a haemodynamically stable patient, management begins with teaching and attempting vagal manoeuvres (e.g., the modified Valsalva manoeuvre) to terminate the arrhythmia. If these fail, management in an acute setting involves intravenous adenosine, which transiently blocks the AV node. For patients with recurrent episodes, long-term management options include beta-blockers, calcium channel blockers, or referral for catheter ablation, which is often curative.

Ventricular Tachycardia (VT)

This is a medical emergency. If the patient is haemodynamically unstable (hypotensive, reduced consciousness), immediate synchronised DC cardioversion is required. In a stable patient, intravenous anti-arrhythmic drugs (e.g., amiodarone) are used. All patients require urgent cardiology assessment to investigate the underlying cause (e.g., ischaemia, cardiomyopathy) and will likely need an implantable cardioverter-defibrillator (ICD) for secondary prevention.

Benign Causes (e.g., Ectopic Beats, Anxiety)

Once life-threatening causes have been confidently excluded through a thorough assessment (including history, examination, ECG, and sometimes an Echo/Holter), management focuses on explanation and reassurance. For benign ectopic beats, explaining the mechanism and advising on lifestyle modifications (e.g., reducing caffeine, alcohol, and stress) is often sufficient. If symptoms are very intrusive, a low-dose beta-blocker may be trialled. For anxiety, addressing the underlying cause with psychoeducation and psychological therapies is key.

Reflection & Learning

These brief cases illustrate the application of clinical reasoning to different palpitation presentations. They are for educational purposes only.

Case 1: Suspected SVT in the Emergency Department

A 28-year-old woman presents to the ED with a 30-minute history of abrupt-onset, rapid, regular palpitations. She feels lightheaded but denies chest pain or syncope. Her heart rate is 180 bpm and regular. Her ECG shows a regular, narrow complex tachycardia. The problem representation is a young, haemodynamically stable patient with a classic history and ECG findings of SVT. The immediate pathway involves attempting a modified Valsalva manoeuvre. If unsuccessful, intravenous access is secured in preparation for treatment with IV adenosine under continuous ECG monitoring.

Case 2: New Atrial Fibrillation in General Practice

A 74-year-old man with hypertension presents to his GP, reporting feeling tired and breathless for a few weeks, with an occasional 'fluttering' in his chest. His pulse is palpated and is irregularly irregular at approximately 115 bpm. The problem representation is an older man with a key risk factor (hypertension) and classic symptoms and signs of new-onset atrial fibrillation with a rapid ventricular response. The GP performs an urgent ECG which confirms AF. The pathway involves organising baseline bloods (FBC, U&E, TFTs), initiating a rate-controlling agent (e.g., bisoprolol), performing a CHA₂DS₂-VASc score, and starting anticoagulation, likely with a DOAC.

Case 3: Benign Ectopics in a Walk-in Centre

A 42-year-old man presents complaining of intermittent 'skipped heartbeats' for the last few months. He notices them more at night when resting. He denies any red flag symptoms like chest pain, syncope, or breathlessness. His examination is unremarkable, with a regular pulse, and his vital signs are normal. A 12-lead ECG is performed and is normal sinus rhythm. The problem representation is a low-risk man with a history highly suggestive of benign ectopic beats. After excluding red flags, management involves providing a clear explanation of what ectopic beats are, offering reassurance that they are not dangerous, advising on lifestyle factors like caffeine reduction, and providing safety netting advice.
