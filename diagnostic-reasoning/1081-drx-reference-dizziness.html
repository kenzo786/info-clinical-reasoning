Dizziness Diagnostic Reasoning Reference
Context: Dizziness Assessment
Overview & Links to Key Guidelines

Dizziness is one of the most frequent reasons for patients to seek medical advice in both primary and secondary care. It represents a significant diagnostic challenge as it encompasses a wide spectrum of underlying pathologies, from benign, self-limiting conditions to indicators of severe, life-threatening emergencies. The term 'dizziness' itself is often used by patients to describe a variety of sensations, including vertigo (a sense of spinning), presyncope (light-headedness or feeling faint), and disequilibrium (unsteadiness).

A systematic and structured approach is essential for any clinician to safely and effectively navigate the differential diagnosis. The primary goal of the initial assessment is to identify red flag symptoms and signs that point towards serious underlying causes, such as a posterior circulation stroke, cardiac arrhythmia, or significant haemorrhage. This allows for prompt escalation and investigation while providing reassurance and appropriate management for more benign causes.

This educational module is designed to provide a foundational framework for the clinical reasoning process in patients presenting with dizziness. For definitive, real-time management protocols, users must refer to official UK guidelines from authoritative bodies such as NICE (e.g., NG197 for Managing falls in older people, CG65 for Transient loss of consciousness in over 16s) and relevant Royal Colleges.

Pathophysiology/Basic Science

Understanding the underlying physiology of balance is crucial for diagnosing the cause of dizziness. The sense of equilibrium is maintained by a complex interplay of sensory information from three main systems: the vestibular system (inner ear), the visual system, and the proprioceptive system (sensory receptors in muscles and joints). The brainstem and cerebellum integrate this information to maintain balance and spatial orientation.

Dizziness arises when there is a mismatch or disruption in the sensory input from these systems, or in the central processing of this information. The main pathophysiological mechanisms can be categorised as follows:

  • Peripheral Vestibular Dysfunction (Vertigo): This is the most common cause of true vertigo. It results from a problem within the inner ear's labyrinth or the vestibular nerve. Conditions like Benign Paroxysmal Positional Vertigo (BPPV) are caused by displaced otoconia (calcium carbonate crystals) within the semicircular canals, leading to false signals of head movement. In Vestibular Neuritis or Labyrinthitis, inflammation of the vestibular nerve or labyrinth (often post-viral) disrupts the baseline firing rate, creating a profound asymmetry in vestibular input that the brain interprets as constant motion.
  • Central Vestibular Dysfunction (Vertigo): This occurs when the problem lies within the central nervous system (CNS), specifically the brainstem or cerebellum, which are responsible for processing vestibular signals. A posterior circulation stroke (affecting the vertebrobasilar arteries that supply these areas) can cause acute vertigo by damaging these central pathways. Other central causes include multiple sclerosis (demyelinating plaques) and tumours (e.g., acoustic neuroma compressing the brainstem).
  • Cardiovascular/Haemodynamic (Presyncope): This type of dizziness, often described as light-headedness or a feeling of 'nearly fainting', is caused by a temporary reduction in cerebral blood flow (hypoperfusion). This can be due to a sudden drop in blood pressure (Orthostatic Hypotension), a cardiac arrhythmia that reduces cardiac output, or significant valvular heart disease. Vasovagal syncope is a common reflex-mediated cause involving a sudden drop in both heart rate and blood pressure.
  • Disequilibrium: This is a sense of imbalance or unsteadiness, particularly when walking, without a sensation of spinning or faintness. It often results from a loss of proprioceptive input (e.g., due to peripheral neuropathy in diabetes) or a failure of central integration from cerebellar degeneration or other neurological disorders. It can also be caused by musculoskeletal problems affecting gait and stability.
  • Non-specific/Psychophysiological Dizziness: This category includes dizziness that doesn't fit neatly into the other categories. Anxiety and panic disorders can cause a sensation of dizziness or unsteadiness through mechanisms like hyperventilation, which leads to a temporary reduction in cerebral blood flow. Persistent Postural-Perceptual Dizziness (PPPD) is a chronic functional disorder often triggered by an initial vestibular or medical event, resulting in a persistent sensation of non-vertiginous dizziness and unsteadiness.
Differential Diagnosis (DDx) Framework

A structured approach to the differential diagnosis of dizziness is essential to ensure life-threatening conditions are prioritised.

Must not miss!
  • Hypoglycaemia: Presents with sweating, tremor, and confusion, often in patients with diabetes, which resolves rapidly with glucose.
  • Cerebrovascular Disease: Sudden onset of dizziness, often with other neurological signs like weakness, ataxia, or speech disturbance, indicating a stroke or TIA.
  • Arrhythmia: May cause presyncope or syncope, often with palpitations, and can be intermittent, requiring cardiac monitoring for diagnosis.
  • Myocardial Infarction: Can present atypically with dizziness, particularly in older adults or women, alongside chest pain, shortness of breath, or nausea.
  • Pulmonary Embolism: Can cause sudden dizziness or syncope due to acute right heart strain, often associated with pleuritic chest pain and hypoxia.
  • Valvular heart disease: Severe aortic stenosis or other valvular issues can limit cardiac output, causing exertional dizziness or syncope.
  • Brain tumour or SOL: Typically causes progressive, persistent dizziness or imbalance, often with headache and focal neurological signs.
  • Acoustic neuroma: A benign tumour on the vestibulocochlear nerve, presenting with unilateral hearing loss, tinnitus, and gradual onset of imbalance.
  • Cholesteatoma: An abnormal skin growth in the middle ear that can erode bony structures, leading to hearing loss, discharge, and dizziness.
  • Seizure: Can manifest with dizziness as an aura or a post-ictal symptom, often with altered awareness or stereotypical movements.
  • Multiple sclerosis: May present with an acute episode of vertigo or chronic imbalance due to demyelinating plaques in the brainstem or cerebellum.
Common causes
  • Viral infection: A common trigger for vestibular neuritis or labyrinthitis, leading to acute, severe vertigo.
  • Labyrinthitis: Inflammation of the inner ear labyrinth, causing sudden vertigo, nausea, and hearing loss.
  • Vestibular neuritis: Inflammation of the vestibular nerve, causing acute vertigo and nausea but typically without hearing loss.
  • BPPV: Characterised by brief, intense episodes of rotational vertigo triggered by specific head movements (e.g., rolling over in bed).
  • Meniere’s disease: Presents with episodic attacks of vertigo, fluctuating hearing loss, tinnitus, and a sensation of aural fullness.
  • Anxiety Induced Dizziness: A common cause of non-specific, often persistent or situational dizziness, sometimes associated with panic attacks.
  • Vasovagal syncope: A common faint triggered by emotional distress or prolonged standing, preceded by light-headedness, pallor, and nausea.
  • Orthostatic hypotension: A drop in blood pressure on standing, causing light-headedness, common in older adults and those on certain medications.
  • Vestibular migraine: Episodic vertigo or dizziness associated with other migrainous features like headache, photophobia, or aura.
  • Anaemia: Can cause persistent light-headedness and fatigue due to reduced oxygen-carrying capacity of the blood.
  • Hypothyroidism: May contribute to dizziness, lethargy, and cognitive slowing as part of a generalised metabolic slowdown.
  • COVID-19: Can present with a wide range of neurological symptoms, including dizziness and vertigo, both acutely and as part of 'long COVID'.
Easily missed
  • Medication Induced Dizziness: A frequent and often overlooked cause, associated with antihypertensives, antidepressants, sedatives, and many other drugs.
  • Drug abuse and Alcohol abuse: Can cause acute or chronic dizziness and imbalance through direct CNS effects or withdrawal syndromes.
  • Vertebrobasilar insufficiency: Transient episodes of dizziness, often with visual or other neurological symptoms, due to reduced blood flow in the posterior circulation.
  • Autonomic neuropathy: Damage to autonomic nerves (e.g., in diabetes) can lead to profound orthostatic hypotension and dizziness.
  • Addison's disease: Adrenal insufficiency can cause postural dizziness, weakness, and fatigue due to volume depletion and hypotension.
Other Differential Diagnoses to consider
  • Perilymphatic fistula: Sudden onset of dizziness, often following trauma or barotrauma, due to abnormal connection between the middle and inner ear.
  • Cervical vertigo: Dizziness associated with neck pain or restricted neck movements, commonly after trauma or degeneration.
  • Hyperventilation syndrome: Often linked to anxiety, causing dizziness due to reduced CO2 levels in the blood.
  • Hyperthyroidism: Can cause dizziness due to increased metabolic rate, tachycardia, and possible atrial fibrillation.
  • Dehydration: Common cause of dizziness, especially in the elderly or those with gastrointestinal fluid loss.
  • Central nervous system infections: Such as meningitis or encephalitis, presenting with dizziness alongside fever and altered mental status.
  • Chiari malformation: Congenital condition where part of the brain extends into the spinal canal, causing dizziness, headaches, and coordination issues.
  • Basilar migraine: A rare form of migraine with dizziness, visual disturbances, and ataxia, often mistaken for a stroke.
History Taking
Patient’s Description: Start with open questions.

Start by asking: "How can I help you today?" or "Tell me about the dizziness you've been experiencing." These questions are effective for several reasons:

  • Open-ended nature: They allow the patient to express their concerns freely without being restricted to a specific symptom or condition.
  • Neutral tone: These questions avoid assumptions, creating a comfortable space for the patient to share their concerns.
  • Sets the agenda: The patient’s response helps guide the consultation by highlighting their main issue or reason for visiting.

Active listening is crucial: giving the patient time to elaborate without interruption can lead to a more accurate diagnosis and improve satisfaction. Starting with open-ended questions provides insight into the nature and severity of the issue, revealing key details about the onset, triggers, and the patient’s perspective.
Relevance: This approach guides the clinician’s follow-up questions, builds rapport, and ensures a patient-centred consultation.

Can you describe the sensation in more detail? Is it more like the room spinning, feeling faint, or being unsteady?
Clarifying the quality of the dizziness helps distinguish between types of dizziness, such as vertigo (spinning sensation), pre-syncope (feeling faint), or disequilibrium (imbalance). Relevance: Vertigo suggests vestibular causes (e.g., BPPV, labyrinthitis), faintness points towards cardiovascular causes (e.g., orthostatic hypotension, arrhythmia), and unsteadiness might suggest a central neurological cause (e.g., stroke, multiple sclerosis).
When did it start, and how long does it last?
Understanding the onset, duration, and progression of dizziness can help differentiate between acute vs chronic conditions, and episodic vs continuous symptoms. Relevance: A sudden onset with a short duration may suggest BPPV, whereas persistent dizziness could point towards vestibular neuritis or central causes like stroke. Gradual progression may indicate tumours or neurodegenerative conditions.
What triggers or worsens the dizziness?
Determining triggers, such as positional changes, exertion, or stress, helps in identifying specific conditions. Relevance: Dizziness triggered by head movements is typical of BPPV, while exertional dizziness may indicate cardiovascular causes. Anxiety-induced dizziness can occur during stressful events.
Do you have any hearing loss, tinnitus, or ear fullness?
These symptoms suggest an inner ear problem. Relevance: Positive responses may indicate Meniere's disease or labyrinthitis. A negative response makes these less likely and raises suspicion of central causes.
Do you feel nauseous or have you vomited?
Nausea and vomiting are commonly associated with vestibular disorders. Relevance: These symptoms support vestibular causes like BPPV or vestibular neuritis. Their absence may suggest non-vestibular causes.
Have you had any visual disturbances, weakness, numbness, or difficulty with speech?
These are symptoms of neurological dysfunction that can accompany dizziness. Relevance: Positive answers raise concern for serious conditions like stroke, multiple sclerosis, or brain tumours. Negative answers make these diagnoses less likely.
Do you have any history of heart disease, diabetes, or thyroid problems?
A history of these conditions can predispose patients to dizziness through different mechanisms. Relevance: Cardiovascular disease raises suspicion of arrhythmias or myocardial infarction, while diabetes could suggest hypoglycaemia or autonomic neuropathy. Thyroid dysfunction, particularly hypothyroidism, may contribute to dizziness.
Ideas, Concerns, and Expectations (ICE)
Asking about the patient's thoughts, worries, and hopes regarding their dizziness allows you to better understand their perspective and manage their expectations. Relevance: These questions help align the consultation with the patient's concerns and ensure that any underlying anxieties are addressed, which can also provide insight into anxiety-related causes of dizziness.
Examination Strategy
General Inspection: Observe for signs of distress, pallor, or dehydration
  • General appearance can provide clues about systemic causes. Pallor may indicate anaemia, while signs of dehydration (dry mucous membranes, reduced skin turgor) may suggest volume depletion causing orthostatic hypotension.
  • Relevance: Positive findings of pallor suggest anaemia, and signs of dehydration suggest orthostatic hypotension. Negative findings rule out these causes.
Vital Signs: Measure blood pressure, pulse, and oxygen saturation
  • Blood pressure and pulse are crucial in assessing for orthostatic hypotension, arrhythmias, or cardiovascular instability. Oxygen saturation helps evaluate for hypoxia, which may suggest pulmonary embolism or other cardiorespiratory causes.
  • Relevance: Low blood pressure or a significant drop when standing suggests orthostatic hypotension. Irregular pulse suggests arrhythmias, and low oxygen saturation points towards cardiorespiratory causes like pulmonary embolism.
Orthostatic Blood Pressure and Pulse: Measure BP and pulse lying down and then standing
  • This test assesses for orthostatic hypotension. A drop in systolic blood pressure of more than 20 mmHg or diastolic blood pressure of more than 10 mmHg upon standing, alongside an increase in pulse, indicates orthostatic hypotension.
  • Relevance: Positive findings confirm orthostatic hypotension as a cause of dizziness. Negative findings make this diagnosis unlikely.
Cardiovascular Examination: Auscultate for heart sounds, murmurs, and irregular rhythms
  • A thorough cardiovascular examination helps identify arrhythmias, murmurs (indicating valvular heart disease), or signs of heart failure. These are important causes of dizziness, especially in older patients or those with cardiac risk factors.
  • Relevance: Positive findings of arrhythmias or murmurs suggest causes like atrial fibrillation or valvular disease. Normal heart sounds and rhythm make cardiac causes less likely.
Ear Examination: Inspect ear canals and tympanic membranes
  • Middle ear pathology, such as otitis media, cholesteatoma, or acoustic neuroma, can present with dizziness. The presence of ear discharge, tympanic membrane perforation, or signs of infection may indicate a middle or inner ear disorder.
  • Relevance: Positive findings like tympanic membrane perforation or discharge suggest conditions such as cholesteatoma or labyrinthitis. Normal findings rule out these middle ear causes.
Eye Movements: Check for nystagmus or gaze palsies
  • Nystagmus (involuntary eye movements) indicates vestibular dysfunction, commonly seen in BPPV, vestibular neuritis, or Meniere’s disease. Gaze palsies could suggest a central nervous system cause, such as a stroke or brain tumour.
  • Relevance: Positive nystagmus indicates vestibular causes like BPPV or Meniere’s disease. Gaze palsies may point to a stroke or central lesion. Absence of these findings reduces the likelihood of these diagnoses.
Dix-Hallpike Maneuver: Perform to assess for BPPV
  • The Dix-Hallpike manoeuvre is used to diagnose BPPV. A positive test results in vertigo and nystagmus when the patient’s head is moved into a specific position, indicating the presence of displaced otoliths in the semicircular canals.
  • Relevance: A positive test confirms BPPV as the cause of dizziness. A negative test makes BPPV less likely, but does not completely rule it out.
Cranial Nerve Examination: Test cranial nerves II-XII
  • Cranial nerve examination can identify neurological causes of dizziness, such as brainstem lesions, multiple sclerosis, or acoustic neuroma. Involvement of cranial nerves III, IV, and VI (eye movements) or VIII (vestibulocochlear nerve) is particularly relevant.
  • Relevance: Positive findings suggest central nervous system causes such as stroke, brain tumour, or multiple sclerosis. Normal findings reduce the likelihood of these diagnoses.
Romberg Test: Assess for proprioceptive or vestibular dysfunction
  • The Romberg test assesses balance with the patient standing, feet together, and eyes closed. A positive Romberg sign (loss of balance) indicates proprioceptive or vestibular dysfunction, seen in conditions like BPPV or peripheral neuropathy.
  • Relevance: A positive Romberg test suggests vestibular or proprioceptive dysfunction, such as BPPV or peripheral neuropathy. A negative test reduces the likelihood of these diagnoses.
Gait Examination: Observe gait for ataxia or unsteadiness
  • Abnormal gait can indicate cerebellar or vestibular dysfunction. Ataxia, broad-based gait, or unsteadiness may suggest central causes of dizziness like cerebellar stroke or multiple sclerosis, or peripheral causes like vestibular neuritis.
  • Relevance: Positive findings of ataxia suggest central causes (e.g., stroke or multiple sclerosis). A normal gait reduces the likelihood of these diagnoses.
Neurological Examination: Assess motor and sensory function
  • A detailed neurological examination can help identify signs of cerebrovascular disease, multiple sclerosis, or peripheral neuropathy. Weakness, sensory loss, or hyperreflexia may suggest a neurological cause for dizziness.
  • Relevance: Positive findings like weakness or sensory loss suggest a central or peripheral neurological cause. Normal findings make these less likely.
Red Flag Synthesis
Sudden Onset of Severe Dizziness (thunderclap dizziness)
  • This red flag refers to the abrupt and intense onset of dizziness, which may suggest a serious neurological or vascular event, such as a stroke, transient ischaemic attack (TIA), or subarachnoid haemorrhage.
  • Significance: Sudden severe dizziness can indicate a cerebrovascular event such as stroke or subarachnoid haemorrhage, requiring immediate emergency evaluation.
Dizziness with Chest Pain, Palpitations, or Dyspnoea
  • This combination of symptoms suggests a cardiovascular cause of dizziness, such as arrhythmias (e.g., atrial fibrillation), myocardial infarction, or pulmonary embolism.
  • Significance: This presentation may indicate life-threatening conditions such as myocardial infarction or pulmonary embolism, requiring urgent intervention.
Neurological Deficits (e.g., weakness, speech difficulties, visual loss)
  • The presence of neurological symptoms, such as unilateral weakness, dysarthria, or visual disturbances, may indicate a cerebrovascular event like a stroke or TIA. These signs require immediate action to prevent long-term neurological damage.
  • Significance: Neurological deficits strongly suggest a stroke or TIA, which are medical emergencies requiring rapid assessment and treatment.
Severe Headache Accompanying Dizziness
  • A new, severe headache in the context of dizziness could indicate a serious condition like subarachnoid haemorrhage, cerebral venous sinus thrombosis, or a brain tumour. These require urgent imaging and evaluation.
  • Significance: A severe headache may indicate subarachnoid haemorrhage or a brain tumour, both of which are emergencies.
Hearing Loss or Unilateral Tinnitus
  • Unilateral sensorineural hearing loss or tinnitus in association with dizziness could point to an acoustic neuroma or Meniere’s disease. Acoustic neuromas are slow-growing tumours but require timely referral and management.
  • Significance: Hearing loss or tinnitus, particularly if unilateral, may indicate acoustic neuroma or Meniere's disease, necessitating further investigation with imaging and specialist referral.
Fever with Dizziness
  • Dizziness accompanied by fever raises concern for infectious causes, such as labyrinthitis or meningitis. Meningitis can present with dizziness due to raised intracranial pressure, and it requires immediate intervention.
  • Significance: Fever with dizziness could indicate an infectious process like labyrinthitis or meningitis, with the latter requiring urgent antibiotic therapy.
Persistent Dizziness with Unintentional Weight Loss
  • Persistent dizziness associated with unexplained weight loss may suggest a neoplastic process, such as a brain tumour or paraneoplastic syndrome. This requires further investigation with imaging.
  • Significance: Unintentional weight loss alongside dizziness could indicate cancer, such as a brain tumour, and warrants prompt referral for imaging and evaluation.
Unsteady Gait or Ataxia
  • Ataxia or unsteadiness, especially if progressive, can suggest central nervous system causes such as cerebellar stroke, multiple sclerosis, or brain tumours. These require urgent neuroimaging and neurological referral.
  • Significance: Ataxia or gait disturbances suggest cerebellar stroke, brain tumours, or multiple sclerosis, requiring rapid neuroimaging and specialist referral.
Recent Head Trauma
  • Recent head trauma followed by dizziness may indicate a serious injury, such as a subdural haematoma, concussion, or skull fracture. These require immediate imaging and evaluation for potential life-threatening complications.
  • Significance: Recent head trauma with dizziness suggests subdural haematoma or other intracranial injuries, necessitating urgent imaging.
Investigations Strategy & Interpretation
Full Blood Count (FBC)
  • An FBC can identify anaemia, which is a common cause of dizziness. It can also detect infections or leukaemia, which may present with dizziness in conjunction with other symptoms.
  • Result Significance: A low haemoglobin level suggests anaemia as the cause of dizziness, while a normal result rules this out. Elevated white cell counts may indicate infection.
Electrolyte Panel (U&Es)
  • Electrolyte imbalances, such as hyponatraemia or hyperkalaemia, can cause dizziness, particularly in elderly patients or those taking diuretics or other medications affecting fluid balance.
  • Result Significance: Abnormal sodium or potassium levels may suggest an underlying metabolic cause for dizziness, such as dehydration or renal dysfunction. Normal results rule out these causes.
Glucose Levels (Fasting or Random)
  • Hypoglycaemia is a common cause of dizziness, particularly in diabetic patients or those on hypoglycaemic medications.
  • Result Significance: Low glucose levels suggest hypoglycaemia, a potentially reversible cause of dizziness. Normal glucose levels rule this out.
12-Lead ECG
  • An ECG is essential for detecting arrhythmias (e.g., atrial fibrillation) or other cardiac abnormalities (e.g., myocardial infarction, prolonged QT interval) that may present with dizziness.
  • Result Significance: Positive findings, such as atrial fibrillation or ST-segment changes, suggest a cardiac cause for dizziness. A normal ECG helps rule out arrhythmias or acute myocardial infarction.
Orthostatic Blood Pressure Measurement
  • This test involves measuring blood pressure while the patient is lying down and then again after standing, to assess for orthostatic hypotension.
  • Result Significance: A significant drop in blood pressure when standing suggests orthostatic hypotension as the cause of dizziness. Normal results rule out this diagnosis.
CT or MRI of the Brain
  • Neuroimaging is indicated when there are neurological red flags (e.g., focal neurological deficits, severe headache, ataxia) to rule out stroke, brain tumour, or other central nervous system pathology.
  • Result Significance: Positive findings may indicate stroke, brain tumour, or multiple sclerosis. Normal imaging can help rule out central causes of dizziness.
Audiometry
  • Audiometry is useful in cases of dizziness associated with hearing loss or tinnitus, as it helps diagnose Meniere’s disease or acoustic neuroma.
  • Result Significance: Abnormal audiometry findings, such as sensorineural hearing loss, suggest Meniere's disease or acoustic neuroma. Normal results rule out these conditions.
D-dimer Test
  • A D-dimer test is used to assess for the presence of a blood clot. It is particularly relevant in cases of dizziness with chest pain or shortness of breath, where pulmonary embolism is a concern.
  • Result Significance: A positive D-dimer result suggests the possibility of a pulmonary embolism, which requires further imaging. A negative result lowers the likelihood of clot formation.
Thyroid Function Tests
  • Hypothyroidism can cause dizziness, lethargy, and weight gain. Thyroid function tests (TFTs) are necessary to assess thyroid hormone levels.
  • Result Significance: Abnormal TFTs, such as low T3/T4 and elevated TSH, suggest hypothyroidism. Normal thyroid function tests rule out thyroid-related causes of dizziness.
Tilt Table Test
  • This test assesses for vasovagal syncope or autonomic dysfunction as causes of dizziness by monitoring blood pressure and heart rate responses to positional changes.
  • Result Significance: A positive test suggests vasovagal syncope or autonomic dysfunction. Negative findings make these diagnoses less likely.
Carotid Doppler Ultrasound
  • Carotid Doppler ultrasound assesses for carotid artery stenosis, which can cause transient ischaemic attacks (TIA) and dizziness.
  • Result Significance: Positive findings suggest carotid artery stenosis, increasing the risk of TIA or stroke. Normal results rule out significant carotid disease.
Evidence-Based Diagnosis (EBDx)
Dix-Hallpike Manoeuvre for BPPV
  • The Dix-Hallpike manoeuvre is highly diagnostic for benign paroxysmal positional vertigo (BPPV) when it elicits vertigo and nystagmus. It is considered the gold standard for diagnosing BPPV.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 79%, Specificity: 75%, Positive Likelihood Ratio: 3.16.
  • Reference: Bhattacharyya N, et al. Clinical practice guideline: benign paroxysmal positional vertigo (Update). Otolaryngol Head Neck Surg. 2017.
  • Significance: A positive test strongly suggests BPPV, while a negative test lowers the probability but does not rule it out completely.
Nystagmus for Vestibular Neuritis
  • Spontaneous, unidirectional horizontal nystagmus is a key feature of vestibular neuritis. The presence of nystagmus that suppresses with visual fixation supports this diagnosis.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 86%, Specificity: 100%, Positive Likelihood Ratio: 14.1.
  • Reference: Kerber KA, et al. Vestibular testing in patients with dizziness: sensitivity and specificity of nystagmus characteristics in a population-based study. Mayo Clin Proc. 2010.
  • Significance: Positive findings confirm vestibular neuritis, while absence of nystagmus reduces the likelihood of this diagnosis.
Orthostatic Hypotension (Drop in Blood Pressure)
  • Orthostatic hypotension, defined by a drop in systolic blood pressure of ≥20 mmHg or diastolic blood pressure of ≥10 mmHg upon standing, is a common cause of dizziness, especially in older adults and those on antihypertensive medications.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 65%, Specificity: 98%, Positive Likelihood Ratio: 32.5.
  • Reference: Freeman R, et al. Consensus statement on the definition of orthostatic hypotension, pure autonomic failure, and multiple system atrophy. Clin Auton Res. 2011.
  • Significance: A positive test strongly suggests orthostatic hypotension, especially in elderly patients or those on medications, while a negative test rules out this cause.
MRI Brain for Acoustic Neuroma
  • MRI of the brain with gadolinium contrast is the gold standard for diagnosing acoustic neuroma, a common cause of dizziness associated with unilateral hearing loss and tinnitus.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 98%, Specificity: 95%, Positive Likelihood Ratio: 19.6.
  • Reference: Fortnum HM, et al. The role of MRI in the detection of acoustic neuromas. Clin Otolaryngol. 2009.
  • Significance: A positive MRI confirms the presence of an acoustic neuroma, while a negative MRI effectively rules it out.
12-Lead ECG for Arrhythmias
  • ECG is essential for diagnosing arrhythmias, such as atrial fibrillation, which can cause dizziness due to decreased cardiac output. This test is quick and non-invasive.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 68%, Specificity: 98%, Positive Likelihood Ratio: 34.0 for diagnosing atrial fibrillation.
  • Reference: Svennberg E, et al. Screening for atrial fibrillation in the elderly population: a randomized controlled trial. Circulation. 2015.
  • Significance: A positive ECG (e.g., atrial fibrillation) confirms a cardiac cause for dizziness, while a negative result lowers the likelihood of arrhythmias.
D-dimer for Pulmonary Embolism
  • A raised D-dimer level is highly sensitive for pulmonary embolism (PE), which may present with dizziness, particularly when associated with chest pain and shortness of breath.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 95%, Specificity: 50%, Negative Predictive Value: 99% for ruling out PE in low-risk patients.
  • Reference: Wells PS, et al. Evaluation of D-dimer in the diagnosis of suspected pulmonary embolism. N Engl J Med. 2003.
  • Significance: A positive D-dimer requires further imaging (CT pulmonary angiography), while a negative result effectively rules out PE in low-risk patients.
Cranial Nerve Deficits for Stroke
  • Cranial nerve abnormalities (e.g., facial asymmetry, gaze palsy) are key indicators of stroke, particularly posterior circulation strokes affecting the brainstem or cerebellum.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 85%, Specificity: 97%, Positive Likelihood Ratio: 28.3.
  • Reference: Wardlaw JM, et al. Neuroimaging in acute stroke: review and recommendations. Lancet Neurol. 2014.
  • Significance: Positive findings suggest stroke, especially when combined with other neurological deficits, necessitating urgent neuroimaging. Negative findings reduce the likelihood of stroke but do not completely rule it out.
Diagnostic Timeout & Cognitive Biases

Awareness of common thinking errors is essential to improve diagnostic accuracy and patient safety when assessing a patient with dizziness. Before finalising a diagnosis, it is good practice for clinicians at all levels to take a 'diagnostic timeout' to challenge their own reasoning.

  • Anchoring Bias: The tendency to lock onto the most obvious diagnosis too early. For instance, attributing dizziness in a young, anxious patient solely to anxiety without thoroughly considering other causes like vestibular migraine or an arrhythmia. A key question to ask is: "If it wasn't anxiety, what else could it be? Have I ruled out red flags?"
  • Confirmation Bias: Actively seeking out or overvaluing evidence that supports an initial belief while ignoring contradictory findings. For example, focusing on a positive Romberg's test to diagnose a vestibular issue while downplaying new-onset ataxia and headache which could indicate a central cause. A key question to ask is: "What finding doesn't fit with my current diagnosis?"
  • Atypical Presentations: A major pitfall is forgetting that serious diseases can present non-classically, especially in older adults. A posterior circulation stroke may present with isolated dizziness without the classic unilateral weakness. Myocardial infarction can present as dizziness and nausea without chest pain. A high index of suspicion must be maintained in high-risk populations. A key question to ask is: "Could my patient's risk factors (e.g., age, diabetes, vascular disease) make a serious diagnosis more likely, even with an unusual presentation?"
  • Over-reliance on Normal Tests: Placing too much weight on a single reassuring test result. A normal ECG in the clinic does not rule out an intermittent, paroxysmal arrhythmia. Similarly, a normal initial neurological exam does not exclude a developing stroke. A key question to ask is: "Does this normal test result definitively exclude the dangerous conditions on my list, or do I need further observation or investigation?"
Illustrative Management Pathways

For educational purposes, this section describes the typical principles of management for key diagnoses. These are not prescriptive instructions. All management must be guided by official UK guidelines, local formularies, and senior clinical advice.

Benign Paroxysmal Positional Vertigo (BPPV)

Once BPPV is confirmed with a positive Dix-Hallpike manoeuvre, the primary treatment is a physical repositioning manoeuvre. The Epley manoeuvre is the most common and effective technique, designed to move the displaced otoconia out of the posterior semicircular canal. This can often be performed in the clinic and provides immediate relief for many patients. Patients are given advice on avoiding provocative movements and information on how to perform self-treatment manoeuvres (e.g., Brandt-Daroff exercises) if symptoms recur.

Vestibular Neuritis / Labyrinthitis

Management is primarily supportive and focuses on symptom control during the acute phase. A short course of a vestibular suppressant (e.g., prochlorperazine or cyclizine) may be prescribed for severe nausea and vomiting, but use should be limited to the first 2-3 days to avoid hindering central compensation. If a bacterial cause is suspected for labyrinthitis, antibiotics may be required. The cornerstone of long-term recovery is vestibular rehabilitation therapy (VRT), a specialised exercise programme designed to help the brain compensate for the vestibular deficit.

Posterior Circulation Stroke

This is a medical emergency requiring immediate hospital admission, usually via a stroke pathway. The patient requires urgent neuroimaging (often MRI is preferred over CT for posterior fossa visualisation) to confirm the diagnosis. Management in a dedicated stroke unit focuses on supportive care, secondary prevention (antiplatelet therapy, statins, blood pressure control), and assessment by a multidisciplinary team including physiotherapists and speech and language therapists to manage neurological deficits and plan rehabilitation.

Orthostatic Hypotension

Management begins with a thorough medication review to identify and adjust or stop any causative drugs (e.g., antihypertensives, diuretics). Lifestyle advice is crucial, including increasing fluid and salt intake (if not contraindicated), performing counter-pressure manoeuvres (e.g., leg crossing, tensing muscles) before standing, and rising slowly in stages. In more severe cases, medications like fludrocortisone or midodrine may be considered under specialist guidance.

Reflection & Learning

These brief cases illustrate the application of clinical reasoning to different dizziness presentations. They are for educational purposes only.

Case 1: Suspected BPPV in Primary Care

A 58-year-old woman presents to her GP complaining of brief (under a minute) but intense episodes of the "room spinning" for the past week. She notes it happens when she rolls over to her right side in bed or tilts her head back to get something from a high shelf. She has no hearing loss, tinnitus, or other neurological symptoms. The problem representation is a middle-aged woman with episodic, positionally-triggered true vertigo, highly suggestive of BPPV. A positive right-sided Dix-Hallpike manoeuvre confirms the diagnosis, and the GP performs an Epley manoeuvre in the surgery, resolving her symptoms.

Case 2: Suspected Posterior Stroke in the Emergency Department

A 72-year-old man with a history of hypertension and atrial fibrillation is brought to the ED with a sudden onset of persistent dizziness, vomiting, and an inability to walk unaided for the past three hours. He describes the world as "wobbling" rather than spinning. On examination, he has nystagmus that changes direction with gaze, significant ataxia (he cannot stand with his feet together), and mild dysarthria. The problem representation is an older man with vascular risk factors presenting with acute, continuous dizziness and clear central neurological signs (gaze-evoked nystagmus, ataxia, dysarthria), highly suspicious for a posterior circulation (cerebellar) stroke. He is managed as an acute stroke and sent for an urgent MRI brain.

Case 3: Orthostatic Hypotension in an Elderly Patient

An 85-year-old man reports feeling "light-headed and woozy" every time he gets out of his armchair, which sometimes makes him grab onto furniture to avoid falling. This has been getting worse over the last few months since his GP increased his dose of ramipril for blood pressure. He denies any spinning sensation, chest pain or palpitations. The problem representation is an elderly man on an antihypertensive medication with classic presyncopal symptoms on standing. His lying blood pressure is 145/80 mmHg, and on standing for three minutes, it drops to 110/70 mmHg and he reports his typical symptoms. The diagnosis is orthostatic hypotension, likely exacerbated by his medication.
