Knee Pain Diagnostic Reasoning Reference
Context: Knee Pain Assessment
Overview

Knee pain is an extremely common presentation in both primary and emergency care, affecting individuals of all ages. The diagnostic challenge lies in distinguishing between benign mechanical issues, chronic degenerative conditions, and urgent pathologies that threaten the limb or life, such as septic arthritis or deep vein thrombosis (DVT). A systematic and structured approach is essential to accurately identify the underlying cause, rule out red flags, and guide appropriate management, while avoiding unnecessary investigations for self-limiting problems.

This educational module is designed to build a foundational understanding of the clinical reasoning process for knee pain. For definitive, real-time management protocols, users must refer to official UK guidelines from authoritative bodies such as NICE (e.g., NG226 for Osteoarthritis, CKS guidance on Knee Pain Assessment) and local musculoskeletal pathways.

Pathophysiology/Basic Science

Understanding the origin of knee pain is key to diagnosis. Pain can be generated through several distinct mechanisms, each pointing towards a different set of differential diagnoses:

  • Mechanical/Structural Pain: This arises from damage or stress to the anatomical structures of the knee. In osteoarthritis, the progressive loss of articular cartilage leads to bone-on-bone friction, causing deep, aching pain that is typically worse with weight-bearing. Acute trauma can cause ligament sprains (e.g., ACL tear) or meniscal tears, resulting in instability, locking, and sharp pain on specific movements.
  • Inflammatory Pain: Inflammation of the synovial membrane (synovitis) is a hallmark of conditions like rheumatoid arthritis or psoriatic arthritis. The inflammatory cascade releases cytokines and prostaglandins, causing constant pain, swelling, warmth, and prolonged morning stiffness. This pain is often present at rest and may improve with gentle activity.
  • Crystal-Induced Arthropathy: In conditions like gout and pseudogout, the deposition of monosodium urate or calcium pyrophosphate crystals within the joint space triggers an intense, acute inflammatory response. This leads to a sudden onset of severe pain, redness, swelling, and extreme tenderness, often rendering the joint immobile.
  • Infective Pain: Septic arthritis occurs when microorganisms (usually bacteria) seed the joint, leading to a rapid and destructive purulent inflammation. The bacterial toxins and resulting inflammatory response cause excruciating pain, a large effusion, fever, and can lead to irreversible cartilage destruction and sepsis if not treated as an emergency.
  • Neuropathic/Referred Pain: Pain felt in the knee does not always originate from the joint itself. Nerve root compression in the lumbar spine (radiculopathy), for instance from a herniated disc at L3/L4, can refer pain down the thigh to the anterior knee, often described as burning or shooting. Similarly, pathology of the hip, such as osteoarthritis, can present as referred pain in the knee.
  • Soft Tissue Pain: Pain can originate from the structures surrounding the joint. Prepatellar bursitis ('housemaid's knee') is inflammation of the bursa over the kneecap, causing localised swelling and pain. Patellar tendinopathy ('jumper's knee') involves micro-tears and inflammation of the patellar tendon, typically causing pain localised to the inferior pole of the patella, exacerbated by activities like jumping or running.
Differential Diagnosis (DDx) Framework

A structured approach to the differential diagnosis of knee pain is essential to ensure life-threatening conditions are prioritised.

Must not miss!
  • Knee fracture or dislocation: Presents with severe pain, deformity, and inability to weight-bear following significant trauma.
  • Cruciate ligament injury: Often occurs during sports with a "popping" sensation, rapid swelling, and a feeling of instability.
  • Patellar tendon rupture: Characterised by a sudden tearing sensation during forceful quadriceps contraction, with an inability to extend the knee.
  • Rheumatoid arthritis: Typically causes symmetrical, inflammatory pain and swelling in multiple joints, with prolonged morning stiffness.
  • Septic arthritis: A hot, swollen, and exquisitely painful joint, often with fever and systemic illness, requiring urgent intervention.
  • Malignancy: Can present with persistent, deep, boring pain, often worse at night, and may be associated with systemic symptoms like weight loss.
  • DVT: Presents with calf swelling, pain, and tenderness, which can be mistaken for a knee problem or a ruptured Baker's cyst.
Common causes
  • Osteoarthritis: Typically a chronic, aching pain, worse with activity and relieved by rest, often with morning stiffness lasting less than 30 minutes.
  • Patellofemoral pain syndrome: Common in young adults, presenting as diffuse anterior knee pain, worse with climbing stairs or prolonged sitting.
  • Soft tissue injury: A broad category including muscle strains or contusions from minor trauma, usually with localised pain and tenderness.
  • Ligament injury: Sprains of the collateral ligaments (MCL, LCL) typically result from varus or valgus stress, causing localised pain and instability.
  • Cartilage injury: Meniscal tears can cause joint line pain, locking, or a sensation of the knee "giving way."
  • Prepatellar Bursitis: Presents as a well-defined swelling and tenderness over the anterior aspect of the patella.
  • Radiculopathy: Referred pain from the lumbar spine, which may be described as sharp or burning and can be associated with back pain.
  • Gout: Characterised by episodes of intensely painful, red, hot, and swollen joints, most classically the big toe but common in the knee.
  • Psoriatic arthritis: An inflammatory arthritis associated with psoriasis, which can affect the knees asymmetrically.
  • Arthropathy presentation: A general term for joint disease, which can encompass various inflammatory or degenerative conditions.
Easily missed
  • Baker's cyst: A fluid-filled sac in the popliteal fossa causing a feeling of tightness or a bulge, which can rupture and mimic a DVT.
  • Hip pain & Back pain: Conditions like hip osteoarthritis or spinal stenosis can refer pain to the knee, misleading the diagnostic process.
  • Connective tissue diseases: Systemic lupus erythematosus (SLE) or other autoimmune diseases can present with arthralgia or arthritis in the knee.
Other Differential Diagnoses to consider
  • COVID-19-associated arthritis: Post-viral arthropathy seen in some cases of viral infections, including COVID-19.
  • Reactive arthritis: Arthritis following an infection, often post-urethritis or gastroenteritis.
  • Meniscal tear: Often traumatic, causing joint line pain and mechanical symptoms.
  • Fibromyalgia: Chronic widespread pain syndrome, may involve the knees as part of a systemic pattern.
  • Haemarthrosis: Bleeding into the knee joint, commonly seen in haemophilia or trauma.
  • Juvenile idiopathic arthritis: A possible cause in younger patients, presenting with joint swelling and pain.
  • Osgood-Schlatter disease: Anterior knee pain in adolescents due to overuse.
  • Paget’s disease of bone: Rare, but may cause knee pain due to underlying bony changes.
History Taking
Patient’s Description: How can I help you today?
It’s essential to begin with an open-ended question such as, "Can you tell me more about what brought you here today?" This approach allows the patient to freely describe their symptoms in their own words without leading them. Relevance: Helps guide the clinician toward specific lines of questioning, ensuring no critical details are missed early in the consultation.
When did the pain start?
  • Establishes the onset (acute, subacute, or chronic) of the pain.
  • Relevance: Acute onset may suggest trauma, septic arthritis, or DVT; chronic pain might indicate osteoarthritis, rheumatoid arthritis, or patellofemoral pain syndrome.
What does the pain feel like (sharp, dull, aching)?
  • Understanding the quality of the pain can help distinguish between musculoskeletal, inflammatory, or neuropathic causes.
  • Relevance: Sharp pain might suggest ligament or meniscal injury, dull aching is more typical of osteoarthritis, and burning pain might indicate radiculopathy.
Is there any swelling or redness?
  • Swelling and erythema may indicate an inflammatory or infective process.
  • Relevance: Positive findings suggest septic arthritis, gout, or bursitis; absence of these symptoms makes an inflammatory or infective process less likely.
Does anything make the pain worse or better?
  • Understanding exacerbating or relieving factors provides clues about the underlying condition.
  • Relevance: Weight-bearing pain suggests osteoarthritis or meniscal injury; pain relieved by rest is consistent with mechanical issues, while no relief might suggest an inflammatory process.
Have you noticed any associated symptoms, such as fever or systemic upset?
  • Explores systemic symptoms that could suggest an infective or inflammatory aetiology.
  • Relevance: Fever raises suspicion of septic arthritis or systemic inflammatory conditions; absence makes such diagnoses less likely.
Any past injuries to the knee?
  • Identifies pre-existing damage or susceptibility to certain conditions.
  • Relevance: A history of trauma raises the possibility of ligament or cartilage injuries, which might lead to chronic conditions like osteoarthritis.
Ideas, Concerns, and Expectations (ICE)
  • Exploring the patient’s perspective about their symptoms ensures their concerns are addressed during the consultation.
  • Relevance: Helps identify patient worries about serious conditions like cancer or septic arthritis and aligns expectations for diagnostic work-up and management plans.
Examination Strategy
Inspection of the knee
  • Inspect for swelling, erythema, deformity, or muscle wasting. Compare both knees for asymmetry.
  • Relevance: Swelling or erythema suggests inflammatory or infective causes (e.g., septic arthritis, gout). Deformity might indicate fractures or dislocations. Muscle wasting can occur in chronic conditions like osteoarthritis or rheumatoid arthritis.
Palpation of the knee
  • Palpate for warmth, tenderness, or joint effusion. Check specific areas such as the joint line, patella, and tibial tuberosity.
  • Relevance: Warmth suggests inflammation or infection. Tenderness along the joint line might indicate meniscal injury. Effusion supports diagnoses like osteoarthritis, septic arthritis, or haemarthrosis.
Range of motion (ROM) assessment
  • Assess active and passive movement of the knee, including flexion and extension.
  • Relevance: Restricted ROM suggests effusion or mechanical obstruction (e.g., meniscal tear). Pain during movement could indicate soft tissue injuries or arthritis.
Lachman test
  • Used to assess the integrity of the anterior cruciate ligament (ACL).
  • Relevance: Positive findings (increased laxity or soft endpoint) indicate ACL injury. A negative result helps rule out ACL tears.
McMurray test
  • Evaluate for meniscal tears by rotating the tibia on the femur while flexing and extending the knee.
  • Relevance: A click or pain during the test suggests a meniscal tear. Negative findings make a meniscal injury less likely.
Patellar tap test
  • Assesses for a significant knee effusion.
  • Relevance: A positive test (patella floats) suggests joint effusion, commonly seen in inflammatory or traumatic conditions.
Check for distal neurovascular status
  • Assess pulses, capillary refill, and sensation in the lower leg and foot.
  • Relevance: Ensures no compromise of vascular or neurological function. Crucial in cases of trauma or suspected DVT.
Red Flag Synthesis
Fever with a hot, swollen knee
  • A red, swollen knee with systemic symptoms such as fever suggests septic arthritis.
  • Significance: Septic arthritis is a medical emergency requiring urgent aspiration and antibiotics to prevent joint destruction and systemic sepsis.
Inability to weight bear
  • Inability to weight bear after trauma may indicate fractures or ligamentous injuries such as ACL rupture.
  • Significance: Fractures and significant ligament injuries need urgent imaging and possibly surgical management to prevent long-term disability.
Rapid onset of severe pain and swelling
  • Acute and severe pain with swelling may indicate haemarthrosis or a ruptured Baker’s cyst.
  • Significance: Haemarthrosis might be seen in coagulopathy or trauma and requires urgent assessment. A ruptured Baker’s cyst can mimic DVT and warrants careful evaluation.
Progressive night pain or weight loss
  • Night pain or unexplained weight loss associated with knee pain raises concern for malignancy.
  • Significance: Could indicate primary bone malignancy or metastasis, necessitating urgent imaging and oncology referral.
Symptoms of distal neurovascular compromise
  • Signs like cold limb, absent pulses, or numbness below the knee may indicate neurovascular compromise due to trauma or compartment syndrome.
  • Significance: These conditions require emergency intervention to preserve limb viability and prevent permanent damage.
Calf pain, swelling, or erythema
  • Calf symptoms in association with knee pain might signal a deep vein thrombosis (DVT).
  • Significance: DVT is a potentially life-threatening condition due to the risk of pulmonary embolism, requiring prompt anticoagulation treatment.
Investigations Strategy & Interpretation
Plain X-ray of the knee
  • Useful for identifying fractures, joint space narrowing, osteophytes, or other bony abnormalities.
  • Result Significance: Positive findings may indicate osteoarthritis, fractures, or malignancy. Negative results do not rule out soft tissue injuries or early-stage arthritis.
Magnetic Resonance Imaging (MRI)
  • Provides detailed imaging of soft tissues, including ligaments, tendons, and menisci.
  • Result Significance: Positive findings might identify ACL tears, meniscal injuries, or soft tissue tumours. Negative results can help exclude significant ligament or cartilage damage.
Joint aspiration and synovial fluid analysis
  • Essential for assessing joint effusions, particularly in suspected septic arthritis or gout.
  • Result Significance: Positive findings of purulent fluid or high white cell count suggest infection. The presence of urate crystals confirms gout, while negative findings help exclude these conditions.
Blood tests (FBC, CRP, ESR)
  • Inflammatory markers and white cell count help identify infection, inflammation, or systemic conditions.
  • Result Significance: Raised CRP/ESR and leukocytosis suggest inflammatory or infective processes, such as septic arthritis or rheumatoid arthritis. Normal results reduce the likelihood of active inflammation.
Ultrasound of the knee
  • Non-invasive and useful for assessing effusions, bursitis, or soft tissue abnormalities.
  • Result Significance: Positive findings may indicate joint effusions, synovitis, or Baker’s cyst. Negative results make significant effusions or cysts less likely.
D-dimer and Doppler ultrasound (if DVT suspected)
  • D-dimer testing is useful for screening, while Doppler ultrasound confirms venous thrombosis.
  • Result Significance: Positive Doppler ultrasound confirms DVT, while negative findings with a low clinical probability can effectively rule it out.
Rheumatological tests (e.g., rheumatoid factor, anti-CCP antibodies)
  • Investigates autoimmune causes of knee pain, such as rheumatoid arthritis.
  • Result Significance: Positive findings suggest autoimmune arthritis. Negative results reduce the likelihood of such conditions but do not fully exclude them.
Evidence-Based Diagnosis (EBDx)
Fever with a hot, swollen knee (indicative of septic arthritis)
  • Fever combined with a swollen, warm, and painful knee is a critical indicator of septic arthritis.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 85%, Specificity: 85%, Positive Likelihood Ratio (LR+): 5.67, Negative Likelihood Ratio (LR-): 0.18.
  • Reference: Mathews CJ et al., *BMJ*, 2010.
  • Significance: Positive findings strongly suggest septic arthritis, necessitating urgent joint aspiration and management. Negative findings make septic arthritis less likely but do not rule it out completely.
Joint effusion on MRI
  • Detects the presence of fluid in the joint, aiding in the diagnosis of inflammatory, traumatic, or infective conditions.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 95%, Specificity: 88%, LR+: 7.92, LR-: 0.06 for detecting intra-articular pathology.
  • Reference: Rubin DA et al., *Radiology*, 2000.
  • Significance: A positive MRI finding supports diagnoses such as septic arthritis, haemarthrosis, or synovitis. A negative MRI reduces the likelihood of significant joint effusion or intra-articular damage.
Positive Lachman test (for ACL injury)
  • The Lachman test is highly reliable for assessing anterior cruciate ligament (ACL) integrity.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 85%, Specificity: 94%, LR+: 14.17, LR-: 0.16.
  • Reference: Benjaminse A et al., *Br J Sports Med*, 2006.
  • Significance: A positive result strongly suggests ACL injury. A negative result significantly reduces the likelihood of ACL rupture.
Presence of urate crystals in synovial fluid
  • Definitive diagnostic marker for gout.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 84%, Specificity: 100%, LR+: Very high (infinity), LR-: 0.16.
  • Reference: Pascual E et al., *Ann Rheum Dis*, 2007.
  • Significance: Positive findings confirm gout. Negative results reduce the likelihood of gout but warrant consideration of other inflammatory arthritis.
Elevated CRP/ESR
  • Markers of systemic inflammation, useful in detecting septic arthritis, inflammatory arthritis, or other systemic inflammatory conditions.
  • Evidence: CRP: Sensitivity: 87%, Specificity: 77%; ESR: Sensitivity: 92%, Specificity: 61% for inflammatory arthritis.
  • Reference: Margaretten ME et al., *Arthritis Care Res*, 2007.
  • Significance: Elevated CRP/ESR supports inflammatory or infective diagnoses. Normal levels reduce the likelihood but do not fully exclude such conditions.
Calf ultrasound for suspected DVT
  • Non-invasive imaging to confirm the presence of thrombus in the deep venous system.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 97%, Specificity: 94%, LR+: 16.17, LR-: 0.03 for diagnosing proximal DVT.
  • Reference: Wells PS et al., *The Lancet*, 1997.
  • Significance: Positive findings confirm DVT, a potentially life-threatening condition. Negative results in a low-probability patient effectively rule out DVT.
Diagnostic Timeout & Cognitive Biases

Awareness of common thinking errors is essential to improve diagnostic accuracy when assessing a patient with knee pain. Before finalising a diagnosis, it is good practice for clinicians to take a 'diagnostic timeout' to challenge their own reasoning.

  • Anchoring Bias: The tendency to lock onto an initial feature, such as the patient's age or a past history of osteoarthritis, and attribute new symptoms to it. This can lead to missing a superimposed septic arthritis or a new meniscal tear in a patient with known chronic knee pain. A key question is: "Could this be something new and different, rather than just a flare of their old problem?"
  • Confirmation Bias: Actively seeking out or overvaluing evidence that supports an initial belief. For example, focusing on a history of minor trauma to diagnose a sprain while downplaying red flags like fever or severe, unrelenting pain that might point to septic arthritis or malignancy.
  • Diagnosis Momentum: Accepting a previous (and potentially incorrect) diagnosis without question. A patient may have been told they have 'arthritis' years ago, but this label could be masking a different, more active pathology. It's crucial to reassess the diagnosis from first principles.
  • Search Satisficing: Stopping the search for causes once one has been found. A patient may have clear X-ray evidence of osteoarthritis, but this doesn't rule out a concurrent DVT or a ruptured Baker's cyst causing their acute increase in pain and swelling. Always consider if there could be more than one diagnosis at play.
Illustrative Management Pathways

For educational purposes, this section describes the typical principles of management for key diagnoses. These are not prescriptive instructions. All management must be guided by official UK guidelines, local formularies, and senior clinical advice.

Septic Arthritis

This is a rheumatological and orthopaedic emergency requiring immediate hospital admission. The core principles are to identify the organism, eradicate the infection, and preserve joint function. Management involves urgent aspiration of the knee joint for gram stain, culture, and crystal analysis. Empirical intravenous antibiotics should be started immediately after aspiration, guided by local microbiology protocols. Surgical drainage and washout of the joint by an orthopaedic surgeon is often required to remove purulent material and reduce pressure.

Acute Ligamentous/Meniscal Injury

Initial management in an acute setting focuses on the PRICE principles: Protection, Rest, Ice, Compression, and Elevation. Simple analgesia (paracetamol, NSAIDs if appropriate) is key for pain control. The patient should be assessed for mechanical instability or a locked knee, which may require urgent orthopaedic review. Most patients benefit from an early referral to physiotherapy for rehabilitation and muscle strengthening. Further imaging, typically an MRI, may be arranged on an outpatient basis to confirm the diagnosis and guide further management, which can range from conservative therapy to surgical reconstruction.

Osteoarthritis (OA)

Management of OA is multimodal and centred on a chronic disease management model. Core treatments recommended by NICE include patient education, lifestyle advice (especially weight loss if overweight), and therapeutic exercise programmes delivered by physiotherapists. First-line analgesia is typically paracetamol and/or topical NSAIDs. If this is insufficient, oral NSAIDs or COX-2 inhibitors can be considered, taking into account patient comorbidities. Intra-articular steroid injections can provide temporary relief for flare-ups. For severe, end-stage OA with disabling pain and functional loss, referral for consideration of total knee replacement surgery is appropriate.

Gout

Management is split into treating the acute attack and long-term prevention. An acute flare is managed with high-dose NSAIDs (e.g., naproxen), colchicine, or oral/intra-articular corticosteroids, depending on patient comorbidities and preference. Long-term management focuses on urate-lowering therapy (ULT) to prevent future attacks and joint damage. The first-line agent is typically allopurinol, started once the acute flare has settled. This is combined with lifestyle advice regarding diet, alcohol intake, and weight management.

Reflection & Learning

These brief cases illustrate the application of clinical reasoning to different knee pain presentations. They are for educational purposes only.

Case 1: Suspected Septic Arthritis in the Emergency Department

A 72-year-old man with type 2 diabetes and rheumatoid arthritis presents with a 24-hour history of a severely painful, red, and swollen right knee. He is unable to weight-bear and feels generally unwell with a temperature of 38.4°C. The problem representation is an immunocompromised patient with a pre-existing joint disease presenting with an acute, hot, swollen monoarthritis and systemic signs of infection. This presentation is highly suspicious for septic arthritis until proven otherwise. The immediate pathway involves urgent hospital admission, blood tests (FBC, CRP, cultures), and an emergency call to the on-call orthopaedic and rheumatology teams for joint aspiration before starting empirical IV antibiotics.

Case 2: Suspected Meniscal Tear in General Practice

A 45-year-old woman presents to her GP after twisting her knee while gardening a week ago. She describes intermittent sharp pain along the inner (medial) side of her knee, and a sensation of her knee 'catching' or 'locking' when she straightens it. There was some mild swelling initially which has now subsided. On examination, there is specific tenderness over the medial joint line. The problem representation is a patient with a clear traumatic mechanism presenting with mechanical symptoms (locking) and localised joint line pain, consistent with a medial meniscal tear. Management involves analgesia, referral to physiotherapy for initial management, and safety netting advice regarding signs of DVT or infection. An MRI may be considered if symptoms persist to confirm the diagnosis.

Case 3: Osteoarthritis Flare in a Walk-in Centre

A 68-year-old man with a known history of knee osteoarthritis presents with a 3-day history of increased aching and stiffness in his left knee after a long walk. The pain is worse on stairs and at the end of the day. There is no significant redness or heat, and he is systemically well. On examination, there is some crepitus on movement and a small effusion, but the joint is not hot or exquisitely tender. The problem representation is an older patient with known degenerative joint disease presenting with an exacerbation of typical mechanical symptoms following overuse, consistent with a flare of his osteoarthritis. After ruling out red flags (septic arthritis, fracture), management involves reassurance, advice on relative rest, appropriate analgesia (e.g., topical NSAID), and reinforcing the importance of his long-term physiotherapy exercises.
