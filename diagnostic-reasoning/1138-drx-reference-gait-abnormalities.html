Gait Abnormalities Diagnostic Reasoning Reference
Context: Gait Abnormalities Assessment
Overview

Gait abnormalities are a frequent and complex clinical presentation, particularly in the elderly population, often signalling underlying systemic disease. The ability to walk safely and efficiently relies on the coordinated interplay of the nervous, musculoskeletal, and vestibular systems. Consequently, a disturbance in gait can be the presenting symptom of a vast range of conditions, from benign musculoskeletal issues to life-threatening neurological emergencies. The challenge for the clinician is to conduct a systematic assessment to accurately identify the cause.

The initial approach must focus on identifying red flags that suggest serious pathology, such as acute spinal cord compression, stroke, or a brain tumour. A detailed history and a structured physical examination are fundamental to localising the lesion and formulating a differential diagnosis. This module provides a foundational framework for the clinical reasoning process involved in assessing a patient with a gait abnormality. For definitive, real-time management protocols, users must refer to official UK guidelines from authoritative bodies such as NICE (e.g., guidelines on Parkinson's Disease, Stroke, Multiple Sclerosis, and Falls in older people).

Pathophysiology/Basic Science

A normal gait cycle is a complex symphony of sensory input, central processing, and motor output. Understanding the key components is crucial to diagnosing the cause of its disturbance.

  • Central Nervous System (CNS) Control: The CNS is the master controller.
    • Cerebrum & Frontal Lobes: The motor cortex initiates movement. Frontal lobe function is essential for planning and executing complex motor tasks. Lesions, such as tumours or Normal Pressure Hydrocephalus (NPH), can cause gait apraxia, where the plan to walk is disrupted.
    • Basal Ganglia: This group of subcortical nuclei (including the substantia nigra) is responsible for regulating the rhythm, amplitude, and automaticity of movement. Dysfunction, classically seen in Parkinson's disease due to dopamine depletion, results in a slow (bradykinetic), shuffling gait with reduced arm swing and festination.
    • Cerebellum: The cerebellum is vital for coordination, balance, and fine-tuning movements. Damage from stroke, alcohol abuse, or degenerative disorders leads to ataxia, a wide-based, unsteady, and staggering gait, often with difficulty turning.
    • Spinal Cord & Sensory Pathways: The dorsal columns of the spinal cord transmit proprioceptive information (joint position sense) to the brain. Damage to these pathways (e.g., from B12 deficiency or spinal cord compression) causes sensory ataxia, where the patient is unsteady and relies heavily on visual input to maintain balance (characteristically worse in the dark or with eyes closed).
  • Peripheral Nervous System: Peripheral nerves provide the communication link between the CNS and the limbs. Peripheral neuropathy (e.g., from diabetes or alcohol) can impair both sensory feedback (leading to numbness and sensory ataxia) and motor command (leading to weakness, such as foot drop).
  • Vestibular System: The inner ear's labyrinthine system detects head movement and orientation in space, which is critical for balance. Dysfunction due to conditions like labyrinthitis or Ménière’s disease can cause vertigo and an unsteady, veering gait.
  • Musculoskeletal System: The 'hardware' of ambulation. Pain and stiffness from osteoarthritis, particularly of the hips or knees, lead to an antalgic (pain-avoiding) gait. Muscle weakness from myopathy results in a waddling gait due to proximal muscle weakness.
Differential Diagnosis (DDx) Framework

A structured approach to the differential diagnosis of gait abnormalities is essential to ensure life-threatening conditions are prioritised.

Must not miss!
  • Spinal cord compression: Often presents with progressive leg weakness, sensory level changes, and sphincter disturbance, which is a neurosurgical emergency.
  • Cerebrovascular disease: A sudden onset of focal neurological deficits, such as hemiparesis, leading to a unilateral spastic or dragging gait.
  • Cerebellar disorders: Characterised by an ataxic, wide-based, and unsteady gait, often with associated dysmetria, intention tremor, and nystagmus.
  • Brain tumour: Can present with a variety of gait disturbances depending on location, including ataxia or spasticity, often with headache and focal signs.
  • Frontal lobe lesions: May cause 'gait apraxia', a high-level motor planning deficit resulting in a magnetic or hesitant walking pattern.
Common causes
  • Arthropathy presentation: Joint pain and stiffness leading to an antalgic (painful) limp to offload the affected joint.
  • Osteoarthritis: A common cause of chronic, progressive antalgic gait due to degenerative joint disease, typically in the hips or knees.
  • Parkinson's disease: A classic hypokinetic, shuffling gait with festination, reduced arm swing, and difficulty initiating movement or turning.
  • Alcohol abuse: Can cause a chronic cerebellar ataxia due to neurotoxicity, often wide-based and staggering.
  • Labyrinthitis: An acute onset of vertigo and imbalance following a viral illness, leading to a cautious, unsteady gait.
  • Meniere’s disease: Presents with episodic vertigo, tinnitus, and hearing loss, causing transient but severe gait instability during attacks.
  • Radiculopathy: Nerve root compression (e.g., sciatica) causing pain, numbness, and weakness in a specific dermatome/myotome, such as a foot drop.
  • Mononeuropathy: Damage to a single peripheral nerve leading to a focal weakness, for example, a common peroneal nerve palsy causing foot drop.
  • Psychogenic: A functional gait disorder with bizarre or inconsistent features, often with non-anatomical patterns and distractibility.
  • COVID-19: Can lead to gait issues through various mechanisms including post-viral fatigue, myalgia, or neurological complications.
Easily missed
  • Peripheral neuropathy: A "glove and stocking" sensory loss and distal weakness causing a high-stepping or slapping gait to compensate for poor proprioception.
  • Diabetic neuropathy: A common cause of peripheral neuropathy leading to sensory ataxia and foot drop.
  • Vitamin B12 deficiency: Causes a combination of sensory ataxia from dorsal column dysfunction and spasticity from corticospinal tract involvement.
  • Metabolic: Gait disturbance can be caused by electrolyte imbalances or organ failure (e.g., uraemia, hepatic encephalopathy) leading to weakness or ataxia.
  • Connective tissue diseases: Systemic conditions like lupus or rheumatoid arthritis can cause gait issues through arthritis, myositis, or vasculitic neuropathy.
  • Heavy metal poisoning: Neurotoxins like lead or mercury can cause a peripheral neuropathy or encephalopathy resulting in gait disturbance.
  • Paraneoplastic syndrome: Remote effects of cancer causing neurological syndromes, such as cerebellar degeneration, leading to a progressive ataxic gait.
  • Medication-induced Gait Abnormalities: Many drugs (e.g., antipsychotics, sedatives, anticonvulsants) can cause gait issues through sedation, parkinsonism, or ataxia.
Other Differential Diagnoses to consider
  • Normal Pressure Hydrocephalus (NPH): Triad of gait disturbance, cognitive impairment, and urinary incontinence
  • Multiple Sclerosis: May present with gait disturbance, often due to spasticity or proprioceptive deficits
  • Myopathy: Weakness causing gait abnormalities, seen in muscular dystrophies and other muscle disorders
  • Peripheral artery disease: Claudication leading to intermittent gait disturbance
  • Severe Osteomalacia: Bone softening may lead to waddling gait and proximal muscle weakness
  • Myasthenia Gravis: Fluctuating weakness which can impact gait, especially later in the day
  • Lyme disease: Can lead to neurological manifestations, including gait disturbance in later stages
  • Functional Neurological Disorder (FND): Psychogenic gait abnormalities, often with inconsistent features
History Taking
**Patient’s Description**: How can I help you today?
Begin with an open-ended question, such as, "Can you tell me more about what brought you here today?" This approach allows the patient to express their symptoms in their own words, which may reveal important insights into the nature, timing, and context of their gait abnormality. Relevance: This open approach can guide the rest of the history by identifying the patient’s main concerns and setting up further, more specific questions.
When did you first notice the change in your walking?
  • This question helps determine the onset and time course of the gait abnormality, which can guide the differential diagnosis.
  • Relevance: An acute onset may suggest stroke, trauma, or spinal cord compression, while a chronic, gradual onset may indicate conditions like osteoarthritis, Parkinson’s disease, or peripheral neuropathy.
Can you describe any specific triggers or things that make it better or worse?
  • This helps identify factors that may exacerbate or relieve symptoms, such as environmental triggers or physical activity.
  • Relevance: Worsening with movement may point to musculoskeletal causes like osteoarthritis, while worsening in specific positions could suggest spinal pathology. Improvement with rest could indicate claudication due to peripheral artery disease.
Do you feel dizzy or off-balance when walking?
  • Determines if dizziness or imbalance accompanies gait disturbance, which may suggest vestibular or cerebellar involvement.
  • Relevance: Positive response could indicate vestibular causes (e.g., labyrinthitis, Ménière’s disease) or cerebellar disorders, while a negative response may reduce suspicion of these conditions.
Have you noticed any numbness or tingling in your legs?
  • This question explores sensory changes, which may be associated with neuropathies or spinal issues.
  • Relevance: Positive response suggests peripheral neuropathy (e.g., diabetic neuropathy, radiculopathy) or spinal cord involvement, while a negative response could make these less likely.
Any changes in bowel or bladder function?
  • This question screens for autonomic dysfunction, which can accompany significant neurological conditions.
  • Relevance: A positive response suggests spinal cord compression or neurodegenerative conditions, while a negative response makes these less likely.
Associated Symptoms: Any weakness, muscle stiffness, or difficulty initiating movement?
  • This question helps identify features of motor weakness, spasticity, or bradykinesia, which can refine the differential diagnosis.
  • Relevance: Muscle stiffness or rigidity may suggest Parkinson’s disease or other neurodegenerative disorders, while muscle weakness may indicate a myopathy or neuropathy.
Associated Symptoms: Any recent illnesses, fever, or weight changes?
  • Explores systemic symptoms that could indicate an infectious, inflammatory, or neoplastic cause.
  • Relevance: Fever and recent illness could indicate infectious causes like Lyme disease or COVID-19. Unintentional weight loss may suggest malignancy or paraneoplastic syndromes.
Past Medical History: Do you have any chronic conditions like diabetes, hypertension, or neurological conditions?
  • This question uncovers comorbidities that could contribute to or exacerbate gait abnormalities.
  • Relevance: Diabetes raises suspicion for diabetic neuropathy, while hypertension is a risk factor for stroke. Previous neurological conditions could contribute to abnormal gait patterns.
Family History: Any family history of neurological or musculoskeletal conditions?
  • Identifies genetic predispositions that may be relevant to gait abnormalities.
  • Relevance: Family history of Parkinson’s, hereditary neuropathies, or muscular dystrophies may suggest these as potential diagnoses.
Drug History: Are you on any medications, or have you recently started any new treatments?
  • Identifies potential medication-induced causes of gait abnormalities.
  • Relevance: Medications like antipsychotics or sedatives may cause gait disturbances, while recent medication changes may hint at side effects or toxicity.
**Ideas, Concerns, and Expectations (ICE)**
  • Ask questions like, "What do you think might be causing this?" to uncover the patient’s ideas, "Is there something specific you are worried about?" to understand their concerns, and "What were you hoping we might be able to do for you today?" to clarify their expectations.
  • Relevance: Helps ensure that the consultation addresses the patient’s perspective and provides reassurance, especially for concerns like serious neurological conditions or underlying cancer.
Examination Strategy
Gait Observation
  • Observe the patient’s gait pattern as they walk across the room. Look for specific abnormalities, such as shuffling, dragging of the foot, wide-based stance, or unsteadiness.
  • Relevance: A shuffling gait may indicate Parkinson’s disease, while a wide-based stance suggests cerebellar ataxia. Unsteady gait could point to vestibular issues, and dragging may suggest hemiparesis (e.g., following stroke).
Romberg’s Test
  • Ask the patient to stand with their feet together and eyes closed, assessing for postural stability.
  • Relevance: Positive Romberg’s (loss of balance) suggests sensory ataxia (e.g., due to peripheral neuropathy or posterior column disease). A negative Romberg test (no sway with eyes closed) points away from sensory causes, possibly toward cerebellar or vestibular conditions.
Heel-to-Toe Walking (Tandem Gait)
  • Ask the patient to walk in a straight line by placing one foot directly in front of the other, assessing balance and coordination.
  • Relevance: Inability to perform this task without staggering suggests cerebellar dysfunction, as seen in cerebellar disorders, alcohol intoxication, or multiple sclerosis.
Inspection of Lower Limbs
  • Inspect for muscle wasting, fasciculations, scars, or deformities, particularly in the legs and feet.
  • Relevance: Muscle wasting or fasciculations suggest chronic neuropathy or motor neuron disease, while deformities (e.g., hallux valgus) may indicate chronic musculoskeletal conditions like osteoarthritis.
Assessment of Muscle Tone
  • Test for increased or decreased muscle tone by moving the patient’s joints passively in both upper and lower limbs.
  • Relevance: Increased tone (spasticity or rigidity) suggests central nervous system involvement (e.g., stroke, Parkinson’s disease), while flaccid tone may indicate lower motor neuron pathology, such as peripheral neuropathy or radiculopathy.
Strength Testing
  • Assess strength in the major muscle groups, particularly the quadriceps, hamstrings, and foot dorsiflexors.
  • Relevance: Weakness in specific muscle groups can suggest a myopathy or radiculopathy. For example, quadriceps weakness could indicate L3-L4 radiculopathy, while foot drop points to L5 involvement or a peripheral neuropathy.
Sensation Testing
  • Examine for sensory deficits (e.g., light touch, pinprick, and proprioception) in the lower limbs.
  • Relevance: Decreased sensation may point to peripheral neuropathy (e.g., diabetic neuropathy), while loss of proprioception may suggest posterior column disease, such as from B12 deficiency.
Reflex Testing
  • Check deep tendon reflexes, especially the patellar and Achilles reflexes, as well as plantar response (Babinski sign).
  • Relevance: Hyperreflexia and positive Babinski sign suggest upper motor neuron lesions (e.g., spinal cord compression), while absent reflexes may indicate peripheral neuropathy.
Coordination Testing
  • Perform coordination tests, such as heel-to-shin and rapid alternating movements, to assess cerebellar function.
  • Relevance: Dysmetria (inability to coordinate movements) or dysdiadochokinesia (difficulty with rapid alternating movements) suggests cerebellar pathology, common in cerebellar stroke or ataxia.
Red Flag Synthesis
Acute Onset of Gait Disturbance
  • An acute or sudden onset of gait abnormalities may indicate a serious neurological event, such as a stroke or transient ischaemic attack (TIA).
  • Significance: Acute neurological causes like stroke or TIA require immediate intervention to prevent further damage or complications.
New Onset of Severe Weakness or Paralysis
  • Significant weakness or paralysis in the lower limbs can indicate spinal cord compression, which may require urgent neurosurgical evaluation.
  • Significance: Spinal cord compression is a neurological emergency. If untreated, it can lead to permanent neurological deficits, including paralysis.
Loss of Bowel or Bladder Control
  • Incontinence or difficulty with bowel or bladder control suggests possible cauda equina syndrome or spinal cord compression.
  • Significance: Cauda equina syndrome is a surgical emergency, as delayed treatment can lead to irreversible incontinence and loss of lower limb function.
Unintentional Weight Loss
  • Unexplained weight loss raises concern for malignancy, which could lead to paraneoplastic syndromes or metastasis impacting gait.
  • Significance: This may indicate underlying cancer, such as spinal metastases or paraneoplastic syndromes, both of which require prompt investigation.
Progressive Numbness or Tingling
  • Progressive sensory loss or paraesthesia may suggest an advancing peripheral neuropathy, multiple sclerosis, or other neurological conditions.
  • Significance: Progressive neuropathy or demyelinating conditions like multiple sclerosis may worsen without appropriate diagnosis and management.
Severe Dizziness with Gait Instability
  • Severe dizziness with an unsteady gait may indicate vestibular dysfunction or, in rare cases, posterior circulation stroke.
  • Significance: Posterior circulation strokes require urgent management to prevent further neurological impairment and complications.
History of Cancer with New Gait Abnormality
  • A patient with a history of cancer presenting with new gait abnormalities may have metastasis affecting the spine or brain.
  • Significance: Spinal or cerebral metastasis can lead to severe neurological sequelae if not urgently addressed.
Fever, Headache, and Gait Changes
  • The combination of fever, headache, and gait abnormalities raises suspicion for central nervous system infections like meningitis or encephalitis.
  • Significance: CNS infections are medical emergencies; prompt treatment is essential to prevent serious neurological damage.
Investigations Strategy & Interpretation
Full Blood Count (FBC)
  • An FBC can help identify anaemia or infection, which may cause generalised weakness or systemic signs contributing to gait abnormalities.
  • Result Significance: Anaemia could suggest nutritional deficiency or chronic disease, while leucocytosis (high white cell count) may indicate infection.
Vitamin B12 and Folate Levels
  • Assessing B12 and folate is essential, as deficiencies can lead to peripheral neuropathy and proprioceptive deficits, contributing to gait issues.
  • Result Significance: Low levels may indicate a reversible cause of neuropathy and gait instability, while normal levels make deficiency less likely.
Thyroid Function Tests (TFTs)
  • Hypothyroidism can lead to myopathy and slowed reflexes, which may impact gait.
  • Result Significance: Abnormal TFTs indicating hypothyroidism suggest a metabolic cause of gait abnormality, while normal results rule this out.
MRI of the Brain and Spine
  • An MRI can reveal central nervous system lesions, such as stroke, multiple sclerosis, or brain tumours, which can cause gait abnormalities.
  • Result Significance: Positive findings of lesions or demyelination suggest neurological causes, such as MS or tumours, while a negative MRI may reduce suspicion of central nervous system pathology.
Nerve Conduction Studies and Electromyography (EMG)
  • These studies assess for peripheral neuropathy or muscle disorders contributing to gait abnormalities.
  • Result Significance: Abnormalities in nerve conduction suggest peripheral neuropathy (e.g., diabetic neuropathy), while normal results make a neuropathic cause less likely.
Serum Calcium and Parathyroid Hormone (PTH) Levels
  • Abnormal calcium levels can affect muscle function and contribute to gait issues, particularly in conditions like osteomalacia or parathyroid disorders.
  • Result Significance: Hypercalcaemia suggests hyperparathyroidism or malignancy, while hypocalcaemia may indicate osteomalacia.
Lumbar Puncture
  • In cases where central nervous system infection or inflammation (e.g., meningitis or multiple sclerosis) is suspected, a lumbar puncture can provide cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) analysis.
  • Result Significance: Positive findings (e.g., pleocytosis, elevated protein) suggest infection or inflammation, while a normal result makes these conditions less likely.
Basic Metabolic Panel
  • Electrolyte disturbances, such as low sodium or potassium, can cause weakness and neurological symptoms affecting gait.
  • Result Significance: Abnormal electrolytes may suggest a metabolic cause of gait disturbance, while normal levels help rule this out.
Toxicology Screen
  • Screening for heavy metals (e.g., lead) or drug toxins may be relevant, particularly if poisoning or substance misuse is suspected.
  • Result Significance: Positive findings indicate toxic aetiologies, while negative results reduce suspicion of toxic causes.
Evidence-Based Diagnosis (EBDx)
Shuffling Gait in Parkinson’s Disease
  • A shuffling gait with reduced arm swing is a common feature in Parkinson’s disease, often accompanied by bradykinesia and rigidity.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 79%, Specificity: 92% for diagnosing Parkinson’s based on gait abnormalities.
  • Reference: Postuma et al., Movement Disorders, 2015.
  • Significance: The presence of a shuffling gait significantly increases the likelihood of Parkinson’s, while its absence makes the diagnosis less likely.
Romberg’s Test for Sensory Ataxia
  • Positive Romberg’s test (swaying with eyes closed) is indicative of sensory ataxia, commonly due to peripheral neuropathy or dorsal column disease.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 80%, Specificity: 90% for sensory ataxia when Romberg’s test is positive.
  • Reference: Clin Med J., 2014, “Neurological Signs in Primary Care”.
  • Significance: A positive Romberg’s test supports a diagnosis of peripheral neuropathy or B12 deficiency, while a negative result may point toward cerebellar causes.
MRI Brain and Spine for Multiple Sclerosis and Tumours
  • MRI imaging is a gold-standard investigation for detecting demyelinating lesions and tumours impacting gait, especially in the brain and spine.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 85-95% for MS plaques, Specificity: 90% in detecting structural lesions.
  • Reference: NICE Guidelines, MS Diagnosis and Management, 2018.
  • Significance: Positive MRI findings of demyelination confirm MS, while visible mass lesions could indicate tumours or metastasis, aiding in targeted management.
B12 Deficiency in Peripheral Neuropathy
  • Vitamin B12 deficiency often presents with peripheral neuropathy, causing sensory deficits that may affect gait stability.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 65%, Specificity: 85% in identifying neuropathic symptoms due to B12 deficiency.
  • Reference: Green et al., NEJM, 2017, “Clinical Manifestations of B12 Deficiency”.
  • Significance: Low B12 levels with neuropathic symptoms strongly suggest B12-deficiency neuropathy, while normal levels make it unlikely.
Lumbar Puncture for CNS Infection and Multiple Sclerosis
  • CSF analysis via lumbar puncture can identify infections or inflammatory markers (e.g., oligoclonal bands) suggestive of MS.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 85% for oligoclonal bands in MS, Specificity: 90% in CNS infections with elevated WBC/protein.
  • Reference: NICE MS Guidelines, 2018.
  • Significance: Positive oligoclonal bands support MS diagnosis, while CSF pleocytosis indicates infection. A normal CSF profile reduces suspicion of MS or CNS infection.
Heel-to-Toe Walk Test for Cerebellar Ataxia
  • The heel-to-toe walk (tandem gait) assesses balance and coordination, often impaired in cerebellar ataxia.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 70%, Specificity: 80% for detecting cerebellar involvement with this test.
  • Reference: DeMyer W, “Techniques of Neurological Examination”, 2008.
  • Significance: Difficulty with this test suggests cerebellar pathology, while normal performance may point toward non-cerebellar causes of gait issues.
Electromyography (EMG) and Nerve Conduction Studies in Neuropathies
  • EMG and nerve conduction studies are used to confirm peripheral neuropathies affecting gait, such as diabetic neuropathy.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 90% for large-fibre neuropathy, Specificity: 85% in neuropathic gait disorders.
  • Reference: Diabetic Neuropathy Guidelines, ADA, 2019.
  • Significance: Abnormal results confirm neuropathy, narrowing the differential diagnosis, while normal findings suggest non-neuropathic causes.
Diagnostic Timeout & Cognitive Biases

Awareness of common thinking errors is essential to improve diagnostic accuracy when assessing a patient with a gait abnormality. Before finalising a diagnosis, it is good practice to take a 'diagnostic timeout' to challenge your own reasoning.

  • Anchoring Bias: The tendency to lock onto an early piece of information. For example, attributing an elderly patient's unsteadiness solely to "old age" or known osteoarthritis, thereby failing to consider new, treatable pathologies like Normal Pressure Hydrocephalus (NPH) or Parkinson's disease. A key question to ask is: "If it weren't 'old age', what would it be?"
  • Search Satisficing: This is the error of stopping the search for a diagnosis once a plausible one has been found. A patient may have gait disturbance from known diabetic neuropathy, but this does not preclude them from also developing a new, superimposed problem like spinal stenosis. Always consider if there could be more than one contributing factor.
  • Confirmation Bias: Actively seeking out or overvaluing evidence that supports an initial belief while ignoring contradictory findings. For example, focusing on weak ankle reflexes to confirm neuropathy while downplaying new upper motor neurone signs like brisk knee jerks and upgoing plantars, which might indicate a more serious spinal cord issue.
  • Attribution Error: Ascribing symptoms to a patient's psychological state or pre-existing conditions without adequate exclusion of organic disease. A gait described as "bizarre" could be labelled as functional, but this is a diagnosis of exclusion and requires careful neurological assessment to rule out causes like frontal lobe pathology or a basal ganglia disorder.
Illustrative Management Pathways

For educational purposes, this section describes the typical principles of management for key diagnoses. These are not prescriptive instructions. All management must be guided by official UK guidelines, local formularies, and senior clinical advice.

Spinal Cord Compression

This is a neurological emergency requiring immediate action. The patient should be admitted to hospital immediately. The management pathway involves an urgent MRI of the whole spine to confirm the diagnosis and locate the level of compression. High-dose corticosteroids (e.g., dexamethasone) are often administered as per local protocol to reduce oedema around the cord. An urgent referral to the on-call neurosurgery and oncology teams is critical for definitive management, which may involve surgery or radiotherapy.

Parkinson's Disease

Management is multidisciplinary and typically initiated in secondary care following a specialist diagnosis by a neurologist or geriatrician. The cornerstone of treatment is dopamine replacement therapy (e.g., levodopa). Crucially, this is supported by a comprehensive team including physiotherapists (for gait and balance training), occupational therapists (for aids and home safety), and speech and language therapists. Management focuses on symptom control and maximising quality of life.

Vitamin B12 Deficiency

Once diagnosed via blood tests, the management involves replacing the deficient vitamin. This is typically done with a course of intramuscular hydroxocobalamin injections. The initial loading doses are followed by a maintenance regimen (e.g., every 3 months). It is also important to investigate and manage the underlying cause, such as pernicious anaemia or dietary insufficiency. Neurological symptoms may improve with treatment, but recovery can be slow and incomplete if the deficiency was severe or prolonged.

Multifactorial Gait Disturbance in the Elderly

This is a very common scenario and requires a holistic approach, often best managed through a comprehensive geriatric assessment. The pathway involves identifying and addressing all contributing factors. This includes medication review to stop or reduce problematic drugs, physiotherapy for a tailored strength and balance programme (falls prevention), management of pain from osteoarthritis, correction of sensory deficits (e.g., new glasses, hearing aids), and assessment of home safety by an occupational therapist. The goal is to improve function and reduce the risk of falls.

Reflection & Learning

These brief cases illustrate the application of clinical reasoning to different presentations of gait abnormality. They are for educational purposes only.

Case 1: Gradual Decline in Primary Care

A 74-year-old man attends his GP with his wife, who is concerned he has become very slow and is "shuffling his feet" over the last year. He has had two falls when turning. On observation, he has reduced arm swing on the right and takes several small steps to turn. The problem representation is a gradual onset of a progressive, asymmetric, hypokinetic gait disorder in an older man, with postural instability. This is highly suggestive of Parkinson's disease. The GP performs a neurological examination, blood tests to exclude other causes, and refers the patient to a local neurology or care of the elderly clinic for specialist assessment.

Case 2: The Acute Red Flag in the Emergency Department

A 65-year-old woman with a history of breast cancer presents to the Emergency Department. Over the past 48 hours, she has developed worsening difficulty walking. She reports her legs feel "heavy and numb" up to her mid-thighs and she has struggled to pass urine this morning. Examination reveals bilateral leg weakness, reduced sensation below the groin, and brisk knee reflexes. The problem representation is an acute onset of bilateral leg weakness with a sensory level and sphincter disturbance in a patient with a history of cancer. This is spinal cord compression until proven otherwise. The team arranges an immediate urgent MRI of the whole spine and makes an emergency referral to the on-call neurosurgeon.

Case 3: The Dizzy Patient

A 40-year-old woman presents with a 3-day history of suddenly feeling very dizzy, with the room spinning, and feeling sick. She feels very unsteady on her feet and tends to veer to the left when she walks. She had a cold last week. She has no headache, weakness, or sensory symptoms. On examination, she has nystagmus but the rest of her neurological exam, including cerebellar tests, is normal. The problem representation is an acute onset of vertigo and gait instability following a viral illness, without other focal neurological signs. This is most consistent with vestibular neuronitis (labyrinthitis). She is treated symptomatically for the vertigo and advised that it should settle over days to weeks, with safety-netting advice to return if she develops any new neurological symptoms.
