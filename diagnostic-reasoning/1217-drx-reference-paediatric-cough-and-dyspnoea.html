Paediatric Cough and Dyspnoea Diagnostic Reasoning Reference
Context: Paediatric Cough and Dyspnoea Assessment
Overview

Cough and dyspnoea (shortness of breath) are among the most frequent reasons for paediatric consultation in both primary and emergency care settings. The spectrum of underlying causes is vast, ranging from common, self-limiting viral illnesses to rare but potentially fatal conditions. The key clinical challenge is to differentiate the well child with a simple Upper Respiratory Tract Infection (URTI) from the child with a serious underlying pathology requiring urgent intervention, such as epiglottitis, pneumonia, or foreign body aspiration.

A systematic and structured approach to history, examination, and investigation is therefore paramount. This ensures that life-threatening 'red flag' presentations are identified promptly, while avoiding over-investigation in the majority of children with benign disease. This educational module provides a foundational framework for the clinical reasoning process in children presenting with cough or dyspnoea. For definitive, real-time management protocols, users must refer to official UK guidelines from authoritative bodies such as NICE (e.g., NG191 for Pneumonia, CG91 for Bronchiolitis) and the British Thoracic Society/Scottish Intercollegiate Guidelines Network (BTS/SIGN) for Asthma.

Pathophysiology/Basic Science

Understanding the origin of cough and dyspnoea is fundamental to forming a differential diagnosis. These symptoms arise from distinct physiological mechanisms affecting the respiratory tract:

  • Upper Airway Obstruction: This occurs when the airway above the thoracic inlet (pharynx, larynx, upper trachea) is narrowed. In conditions like croup, viral inflammation causes submucosal oedema in the larynx and trachea, leading to turbulent airflow and the characteristic inspiratory stridor and 'barking' cough. In epiglottitis, bacterial infection causes rapid, severe swelling of the epiglottis, which can completely obstruct the airway.
  • Lower Airway Obstruction: This involves narrowing of the smaller airways (bronchi and bronchioles). In asthma, exposure to triggers causes bronchoconstriction, mucosal inflammation, and increased mucus production, leading to expiratory wheeze and dyspnoea. In bronchiolitis, a viral infection (typically RSV) causes inflammation and necrosis of the bronchiolar epithelium, resulting in mucus plugging, oedema, and wheezing in infants.
  • Parenchymal (Lung Tissue) Disease: This affects the alveoli and interstitial space, impairing gas exchange. In pneumonia, infection leads to an inflammatory exudate filling the alveoli (consolidation), causing cough, fever, and tachypnoea as the body tries to compensate for the reduced surface area for oxygenation.
  • Altered Respiratory Drive or Control: Conditions affecting the central nervous system or metabolic state can alter the drive to breathe. For example, metabolic acidosis (as in diabetic ketoacidosis) can cause deep, rapid breathing (Kussmaul breathing) as the body attempts to expel carbon dioxide to correct the pH, which may be perceived as dyspnoea.
  • Extrapulmonary Restriction: This occurs when external factors limit lung expansion. A large pleural effusion or a pneumothorax occupies space within the chest cavity, compressing the lung and causing dyspnoea. Similarly, severe kyphoscoliosis or neuromuscular weakness (e.g., muscular dystrophy) can impair the function of the respiratory pump.
Differential Diagnosis (DDx) Framework

A structured approach to the differential diagnosis of paediatric cough and dyspnoea is essential to ensure life-threatening conditions are prioritised.

Must not miss!
  • Epiglottitis: A rare but life-threatening infection causing rapid onset of high fever, sore throat, drooling, and stridor in a toxic-appearing child.
  • Pneumonia: An infection of the lung parenchyma presenting with fever, cough, tachypnoea, and sometimes focal chest signs.
  • Tuberculosis: A chronic infection that can present with persistent cough, fever, weight loss, and night sweats.
  • Congenital Heart disease: May present with shortness of breath, poor feeding, failure to thrive, and sometimes cyanosis.
  • Neonatal RDS: Respiratory distress in a newborn, typically a premature infant, due to surfactant deficiency.
  • Pleural effusion: An accumulation of fluid in the pleural space, causing dyspnoea, cough, and reduced breath sounds on the affected side.
  • Pneumothorax: The presence of air in the pleural space, causing sudden onset of dyspnoea and unilateral chest pain.
  • Immunodeficiency: Presents as recurrent, severe, or unusual infections, including persistent respiratory symptoms.
Common causes
  • URTI: Presents with coryzal symptoms, cough, and low-grade fever, typically in a well-looking child.
  • Rhinosinusitis: Characterised by persistent purulent nasal discharge, facial pain, and a cough that is often worse at night.
  • Viral induced wheeze: Episodic wheezing associated with viral upper respiratory infections in preschool children.
  • Croup: A characteristic barking cough, hoarseness, and inspiratory stridor, often worse at night.
  • LRTI: General term for infections like bronchitis, often presenting with a productive cough and sometimes fever, without focal consolidation.
  • Influenza: An acute viral illness with fever, myalgia, headache, and a prominent cough.
  • Whooping cough: A paroxysmal cough followed by an inspiratory 'whoop' and sometimes post-tussive vomiting.
  • Asthma: Recurrent episodes of wheeze, cough (especially nocturnal), and breathlessness, often triggered by allergens or exercise.
  • GORD: Presents with cough, particularly after meals or when lying down, and may be associated with vomiting or poor weight gain.
  • Allergic rhinitis: Nasal congestion, sneezing, and rhinorrhoea leading to a post-nasal drip and chronic cough.
  • Non-allergic rhinitis: Similar to allergic rhinitis but without evidence of atopy, causing persistent post-nasal drip and cough.
  • Anaemia (SOB): Presents with shortness of breath on exertion, pallor, and fatigue due to reduced oxygen-carrying capacity.
  • COVID-19: A viral illness that can cause fever, a continuous cough, and shortness of breath.
Easily missed
  • Food allergy or intolerance: Can cause cough and wheeze as part of an allergic reaction, sometimes accompanied by urticaria or gastrointestinal symptoms.
  • Cystic fibrosis (mostly picked up on screening): A genetic disorder causing chronic productive cough, recurrent chest infections, and failure to thrive.
  • Bronchiectasis: Permanent dilation of the airways, leading to a chronic productive cough with large volumes of purulent sputum.
  • Functional disorder: Refers to symptoms like a psychogenic or habit cough for which no organic cause can be found.
Other Differential Diagnoses to consider
  • COVID-19: Can present with cough, fever, dyspnoea, and multisystem inflammatory syndrome in children (MIS-C).
  • Foreign Body Aspiration: Sudden onset of cough or choking, often with a history of ingestion.
  • Bronchiolitis: Typically seen in infants; characterised by cough, wheeze, and respiratory distress.
  • Primary Ciliary Dyskinesia: Recurrent infections and a chronic productive cough from impaired ciliary function.
  • Hyperventilation Syndrome: Often stress-related, with episodic dyspnoea and normal findings on examination and investigations.
  • Mediastinal Mass: Can compress airways, causing cough and dyspnoea; requires imaging to confirm.
  • Psychogenic Cough: A habitual cough in the absence of organic disease, often stress-related.
History Taking
**Patient’s Description**: How can I help you today?
Starting with an open-ended question such as "Can you tell me more about what brought you here today?" allows the patient or parent to describe the issue in their own words, providing a broad overview of the symptoms. Relevance: This approach sets the tone for the consultation, helping identify the main concerns and guiding targeted history-taking.
When did the cough and/or dyspnoea start?
  • Helps determine acute vs chronic onset.
  • Relevance: An acute onset could suggest conditions like viral infections, croup, or foreign body aspiration. Chronic symptoms may point towards asthma, bronchiectasis, or cystic fibrosis.
Is the cough dry or productive?
  • Distinguishes between inflammatory or irritative causes (dry) vs infections or conditions with secretions (productive).
  • Relevance: A productive cough suggests LRTI, bronchiectasis, or cystic fibrosis. A dry cough could indicate asthma, viral infections, or GORD.
Are there any triggers or exacerbating factors?
  • Helps identify potential environmental or activity-related factors.
  • Relevance: Triggers like exercise or allergens point towards asthma or allergic rhinitis. Worsening after eating may suggest GORD.
Have you noticed any associated symptoms?
  • Fever: Suggests infectious causes like pneumonia, LRTI, or influenza.
  • Stridor: Indicates upper airway obstruction such as croup or epiglottitis.
  • Wheeze: Points towards asthma, viral-induced wheeze, or bronchiolitis.
  • Weight loss or failure to thrive: Suggests chronic conditions like cystic fibrosis or tuberculosis.
Any history of previous similar episodes?
  • Helps assess recurrence and chronicity.
  • Relevance: Recurrent symptoms suggest conditions like asthma, GORD, or immunodeficiency.
Family History of Respiratory or Allergic Conditions?
  • Provides insight into genetic predispositions.
  • Relevance: A positive history may indicate asthma, cystic fibrosis, or atopy-related conditions.
**Ideas, Concerns, and Expectations (ICE)**
  • Exploring the patient’s or parent’s perspective provides a deeper understanding of their priorities.
  • Relevance: Helps address concerns about serious conditions (e.g., pneumonia, congenital heart disease) and ensures their expectations are aligned with clinical outcomes.
Examination Strategy
Inspection of the General Appearance
  • Assess the child’s overall appearance, distress level, and work of breathing (e.g., nasal flaring, intercostal retractions).
  • Relevance: Signs of respiratory distress suggest acute conditions such as epiglottitis, pneumonia, or bronchiolitis. A lack of distress may point towards more chronic or functional causes.
Measurement of Vital Signs
  • Include respiratory rate, heart rate, temperature, and oxygen saturation.
  • Relevance: Tachypnoea may indicate lower respiratory tract infections, asthma, or other causes of hypoxia. Fever supports infectious causes such as pneumonia or influenza.
Examination of the Throat
  • Inspect for erythema, tonsillar enlargement, or exudate.
  • Relevance: Findings of pharyngitis or tonsillitis may point towards a viral or bacterial infection. A clear throat examination reduces the likelihood of upper respiratory involvement.
Chest Inspection
  • Observe for symmetry of chest movements, retractions, and any deformities.
  • Relevance: Asymmetry may suggest a pneumothorax or pleural effusion. Retractions and nasal flaring are indicative of increased respiratory effort.
Palpation of the Chest
  • Assess for tenderness, crepitus, or abnormal chest wall movement.
  • Relevance: Tenderness could indicate trauma, while crepitus may suggest subcutaneous emphysema from pneumothorax. Normal findings reduce suspicion of mechanical chest wall issues.
Percussion of the Chest
  • Evaluate for hyper-resonance or dullness over lung fields.
  • Relevance: Hyper-resonance suggests pneumothorax, while dullness may indicate pleural effusion or pneumonia.
Auscultation of the Lungs
  • Listen for wheeze, crackles, stridor, or decreased breath sounds.
  • Relevance: Wheeze suggests asthma or bronchiolitis. Crackles may point to pneumonia or bronchiectasis. Decreased breath sounds are a key finding in pneumothorax or large effusions.
Cardiac Examination
  • Assess heart sounds, murmurs, and signs of heart failure such as hepatomegaly or peripheral oedema.
  • Relevance: Murmurs may suggest congenital heart disease. Tachycardia and hepatomegaly can be signs of heart failure contributing to dyspnoea.
Abdominal Examination
  • Check for organomegaly or tenderness.
  • Relevance: Hepatomegaly may occur in heart failure. Tenderness could suggest referred pain or abdominal causes of respiratory distress, such as diaphragmatic hernia.
Red Flag Synthesis
Severe respiratory distress (e.g. nasal flaring, intercostal or subcostal retractions, grunting)
  • Indicates significant difficulty breathing and increased work of breathing, which can quickly progress to respiratory failure.
  • Significance: This could indicate conditions such as severe pneumonia, bronchiolitis, epiglottitis, or a foreign body obstruction requiring urgent intervention.
Stridor at rest
  • Represents upper airway obstruction, commonly due to croup, epiglottitis, or a foreign body.
  • Significance: Stridor at rest is a medical emergency and may signify a life-threatening condition such as epiglottitis or airway foreign body requiring immediate airway management.
Central cyanosis or oxygen saturation <92%
  • Indicates hypoxaemia, suggesting significant impairment in oxygen exchange.
  • Significance: May be seen in severe lower respiratory tract infections, congenital heart disease, or pneumothorax. Immediate oxygen therapy and further evaluation are required.
Altered level of consciousness or lethargy
  • Suggests critical hypoxia, hypercarbia, or systemic illness affecting perfusion or brain function.
  • Significance: Could indicate respiratory failure, sepsis, or dehydration. Immediate resuscitation and management are essential.
Rapidly worsening symptoms or sudden onset
  • A sudden or rapidly deteriorating clinical picture may indicate acute conditions such as foreign body aspiration or pneumothorax.
  • Significance: Sudden onset of dyspnoea and cough should prompt investigation for emergencies like pneumothorax or anaphylaxis.
Persistent fever >39°C unresponsive to antipyretics
  • May indicate a serious bacterial infection such as pneumonia, sepsis, or tuberculosis.
  • Significance: Requires prompt evaluation and possibly urgent antibiotic therapy.
Signs of dehydration (e.g., dry mucous membranes, reduced urine output, sunken eyes)
  • Suggests significant fluid loss, possibly due to increased respiratory effort, fever, or decreased intake.
  • Significance: Dehydration exacerbates respiratory distress and systemic instability, requiring fluid resuscitation.
Parental concern about "something not right"
  • Parents are often acutely aware of subtle but serious changes in their child’s behaviour or condition.
  • Significance: This warrants careful evaluation for potential red flags that may not be immediately obvious, such as sepsis or meningitis.
Investigations Strategy & Interpretation
Pulse oximetry
  • Provides a non-invasive measurement of oxygen saturation to assess for hypoxaemia.
  • Result Significance: Low oxygen saturation (<92%) suggests significant respiratory compromise, seen in pneumonia, bronchiolitis, or congenital heart disease. Normal saturation makes severe hypoxaemia less likely.
Chest X-ray (CXR)
  • Evaluates for lung pathology such as pneumonia, pneumothorax, pleural effusion, or congenital abnormalities.
  • Result Significance: Consolidation suggests pneumonia; hyperinflation points to asthma or bronchiolitis; and mediastinal shift could indicate a pneumothorax or mass.
Full blood count (FBC)
  • Assesses for infection or anaemia, which may exacerbate dyspnoea.
  • Result Significance: Elevated white cell count suggests bacterial infection (e.g., pneumonia). Low haemoglobin indicates anaemia as a potential contributing factor to dyspnoea.
C-reactive protein (CRP) or procalcitonin
  • Helps differentiate between bacterial and viral infections.
  • Result Significance: Elevated levels are more suggestive of bacterial infection such as pneumonia. Normal or mildly elevated levels support a viral cause.
Nasopharyngeal swab for viral PCR
  • Detects respiratory viruses such as RSV, influenza, or SARS-CoV-2.
  • Result Significance: Positive results confirm viral aetiologies like bronchiolitis or influenza, guiding supportive care and infection control measures.
Sweat chloride test
  • Screening for cystic fibrosis in children with recurrent respiratory symptoms or failure to thrive.
  • Result Significance: Elevated sweat chloride levels confirm cystic fibrosis, warranting further genetic testing and multidisciplinary management.
Blood gas analysis
  • Assesses acid-base balance and gas exchange in critically unwell children.
  • Result Significance: Hypoxaemia and hypercapnia suggest respiratory failure, commonly seen in conditions like severe bronchiolitis or asthma exacerbation.
Allergy testing (e.g., skin prick or specific IgE)
  • Investigates suspected allergic triggers in cases of wheeze or recurrent symptoms.
  • Result Significance: Positive results indicate allergic rhinitis, asthma, or food allergies, guiding allergen avoidance and treatment.
Evidence-Based Diagnosis (EBDx)
Fever in bacterial infections
  • Fever is a key symptom in distinguishing bacterial from viral infections, particularly in conditions like pneumonia.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 70%, Specificity: 75%, Positive Likelihood Ratio (LR+): 2.8.
  • Reference: British Thoracic Society (BTS) Guidelines for the Management of Community Acquired Pneumonia in Children.
  • Significance: A high fever increases the probability of bacterial pneumonia, while its absence reduces the likelihood but does not rule it out.
Cough with wheeze in asthma
  • A history of recurrent cough and wheeze, especially with triggers such as exercise or allergens, strongly suggests asthma.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 80%, Specificity: 85%, LR+: 5.3.
  • Reference: SIGN/BTS Asthma Guidelines 2022.
  • Significance: The combination of cough, wheeze, and specific triggers increases the likelihood of asthma as the underlying diagnosis.
Chest X-ray findings in pneumonia
  • Consolidation on chest X-ray is a hallmark of bacterial pneumonia.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 90%, Specificity: 95%, LR+: 18.0.
  • Reference: Paediatric Infectious Diseases Society (PIDS) Guidelines for Pneumonia.
  • Significance: A positive chest X-ray significantly increases the likelihood of pneumonia, while a normal X-ray strongly argues against it in most cases.
Pulse oximetry in respiratory distress
  • Oxygen saturation levels help identify hypoxaemia and the severity of respiratory distress.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 85%, Specificity: 90%, LR+: 8.5 for detecting significant hypoxaemia (SpO2 <92%).
  • Reference: WHO Guidelines on Acute Respiratory Infections in Children.
  • Significance: Low SpO2 levels confirm significant respiratory compromise, requiring oxygen therapy and further evaluation.
Elevated CRP in bacterial infections
  • An elevated CRP level is more indicative of bacterial rather than viral infections.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 70%, Specificity: 80%, LR+: 3.5 for bacterial pneumonia.
  • Reference: NICE Guideline NG191: COVID-19 Rapid Guideline on Managing Suspected or Confirmed Pneumonia.
  • Significance: Elevated CRP supports the diagnosis of bacterial infections, although mildly raised levels can be seen in viral infections.
Wheeze and stridor in croup
  • Inspiratory stridor and barking cough are key clinical signs of croup, particularly in children under 6 years.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 95%, Specificity: 90%, LR+: 9.5.
  • Reference: NICE Guidelines: Acute Upper Respiratory Tract Infections in Children.
  • Significance: These clinical signs strongly suggest croup, helping differentiate it from other upper airway conditions like epiglottitis.
Sweat chloride test in cystic fibrosis
  • The sweat chloride test is the gold standard for diagnosing cystic fibrosis.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 98%, Specificity: 99%, LR+: 98.0 for chloride levels >60 mmol/L.
  • Reference: Cystic Fibrosis Foundation Consensus Guidelines.
  • Significance: A positive test confirms the diagnosis of cystic fibrosis, particularly in children with recurrent respiratory symptoms or failure to thrive.
Diagnostic Timeout & Cognitive Biases

In paediatric emergency medicine, cognitive biases can lead to diagnostic errors. Taking a 'diagnostic timeout' to reflect is crucial before finalising a diagnosis for a child with cough and dyspnoea.

  • Anchoring Bias: The tendency to lock onto the most common diagnosis (e.g., viral URTI or bronchiolitis) too early in the assessment. This may cause a clinician to overlook subtle but critical signs of a more serious condition, like focal crackles suggesting a developing pneumonia, or a history of choking pointing to a foreign body. A key question to ask is: "Could this be something other than a simple virus, and have I actively looked for evidence of that?"
  • Confirmation Bias: Selectively focusing on information that supports a preliminary diagnosis while ignoring contradictory data. For example, attributing a wheeze solely to viral-induced wheeze in a child with poor weight gain, thereby downplaying a key feature of potential cystic fibrosis.
  • Availability Heuristic: Overestimating the likelihood of diagnoses that are recent or memorable. During winter, a clinician might diagnose every wheezing infant with bronchiolitis, potentially missing a case of congenital heart disease presenting with respiratory distress.
  • Dismissing Parental Concern: One of the most significant pitfalls in paediatrics is underestimating the value of parental instinct. A parent stating their child "is not right" or "is sicker than ever before" is a powerful red flag, even if initial objective findings appear reassuring. It is vital to take such concerns seriously and consider a period of observation or more detailed investigation.
Illustrative Management Pathways

For educational purposes, this section describes the typical principles of management for key diagnoses. These are not prescriptive instructions. All management must be guided by official UK guidelines, local formularies, and senior clinical advice.

Croup (Viral Laryngotracheobronchitis)

The management of croup is guided by severity. For mild-to-moderate cases (barking cough but no stridor at rest), a single oral dose of a corticosteroid (e.g., dexamethasone) is the cornerstone of treatment to reduce airway inflammation. Parents are advised on supportive care, including keeping the child calm and hydrated. For severe croup (stridor at rest, significant respiratory distress), immediate hospital admission is required for nebulised adrenaline (to rapidly reduce airway swelling) and oxygen, alongside steroids.

Community-Acquired Pneumonia (CAP)

Management depends on severity and the likely pathogen. In the UK, for uncomplicated CAP managed in the community, a 5-day course of oral amoxicillin is the first-line antibiotic. Hospital admission is indicated for children with moderate-to-severe pneumonia (e.g., oxygen saturation <92%, marked respiratory distress, inability to maintain hydration). In hospital, treatment may involve intravenous antibiotics, oxygen therapy, and fluid support. Investigations like chest X-ray and blood tests (FBC, CRP) help guide management.

Acute Asthma / Viral-Induced Wheeze

Initial management of an acute wheezing episode involves repeated doses of an inhaled short-acting beta-2 agonist (SABA), such as salbutamol, delivered via a spacer. In moderate-to-severe exacerbations, oral corticosteroids (e.g., prednisolone) are given for 3 days to control the underlying inflammation. Hospital admission is necessary if there are features of a severe or life-threatening attack, such as inability to complete sentences, low oxygen saturations, or exhaustion. Oxygen is a critical treatment for hypoxic children.

Bronchiolitis

Management of bronchiolitis is primarily supportive, as it is a viral illness. The focus is on maintaining adequate hydration and oxygenation. In primary care, this involves advising parents on how to recognise signs of worsening distress and ensuring the infant is feeding. Hospital admission is required if the infant is struggling to feed, has signs of dehydration, or has significant respiratory distress or apnoeas. In hospital, feeding support (e.g., via nasogastric tube) and supplemental oxygen are the mainstays of treatment. Routine use of bronchodilators, steroids, or antibiotics is not recommended.

Reflection & Learning

These brief cases illustrate the application of clinical reasoning to different presentations of paediatric cough and dyspnoea. They are for educational purposes only.

Case 1: Suspected Croup in an Urgent Care Centre

An 18-month-old boy presents at 10 PM with a 1-day history of a runny nose and a harsh, 'barking' cough that started this evening. On examination, he is alert, with no cyanosis, but has a low-grade fever of 37.8°C and audible inspiratory stridor when he cries. His respiratory rate is 35 breaths/minute and oxygen saturation is 96% in air. The problem representation is a toddler with a viral prodrome now presenting with the classic clinical dyad of barking cough and inspiratory stridor, consistent with moderate croup. The pathway involves administering a single dose of oral dexamethasone and observing for a period to ensure no worsening before discharge with strict safety netting advice.

Case 2: Suspected Pneumonia in General Practice

A 4-year-old girl is brought to the GP with a 3-day history of cough and high fever (39.5°C) despite regular paracetamol. She appears lethargic and is breathing fast. Her respiratory rate is 45 breaths/minute, and she has some mild intercostal retractions. Auscultation reveals reduced breath sounds and crackles at the right lung base. The problem representation is a pre-school child with systemic signs of infection (high fever, lethargy) and focal chest signs (tachypnoea, crackles), highly suspicious for a bacterial lower respiratory tract infection (pneumonia). Given the signs of increased work of breathing, the GP arranges for immediate hospital admission for further assessment and management.

Case 3: Bronchiolitis in a 3-Month-Old Infant

A 3-month-old infant presents with a 2-day history of coryza and a worsening cough. Today, the parents are worried as he is feeding less than half his usual amount. On examination, he has marked subcostal and intercostal retractions, a respiratory rate of 70 breaths/minute, and widespread expiratory wheeze and crackles. His oxygen saturation is 91% on air. The problem representation is a young infant in the typical age range for bronchiolitis, presenting with significant work of breathing, poor feeding, and hypoxia. This is a severe case requiring immediate hospital admission for supportive care, primarily oxygen and nasogastric feeding.
