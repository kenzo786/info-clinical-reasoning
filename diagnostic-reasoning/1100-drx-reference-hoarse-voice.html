Hoarse Voice Diagnostic Reasoning Reference
Context: Hoarse Voice Assessment
Overview & Links to Key Guidelines

Hoarseness, or dysphonia, is a common presenting symptom in both primary and secondary care. It reflects an abnormality in voice production and can range from a benign, self-limiting issue to the first sign of a life-threatening malignancy. The primary challenge for the clinician is to differentiate between these causes efficiently and safely. A systematic approach, centred on a detailed history and targeted examination, is essential to identify red flags that warrant urgent specialist referral while providing appropriate reassurance and management for more common conditions.

This educational module provides a structured framework for the clinical reasoning process when faced with a patient with a hoarse voice. For definitive, real-time management protocols, users must refer to official UK guidelines from authoritative bodies such as NICE, particularly the NICE guideline [NG12] on Suspected cancer: recognition and referral, which provides specific criteria for the urgent investigation of hoarseness.

Pathophysiology/Basic Science

A normal voice is produced by the vibration of the two vocal cords (or vocal folds) within the larynx as air is expelled from the lungs. Hoarseness results from any condition that disrupts the normal vibratory function of these cords. The underlying mechanisms can be grouped as follows:

  • Inflammation and Oedema: This is the most common cause. Inflammation from infection (e.g., viral laryngitis) or chemical irritation (e.g., laryngopharyngeal reflux from GORD) causes the vocal cords to become swollen and stiff. This increased mass and altered pliability prevents them from vibrating freely and closing completely, resulting in a rough, strained, or breathy voice.
  • Structural Abnormalities: Benign or malignant growths can physically disrupt vocal cord function. Vocal cord nodules, often seen in professional voice users, are callus-like lesions that prevent complete cord closure. Polyps and cysts have a similar effect. Malignant tumours (laryngeal cancer) invade and stiffen the cord structure, leading to a persistent and progressively worsening hoarseness.
  • Neurological Dysfunction: The muscles of the larynx are controlled by the recurrent laryngeal nerve, a branch of the vagus nerve (CN X). Damage to this nerve (e.g., from neck surgery, a chest malignancy like lung cancer, or a neurological disorder) can lead to vocal cord palsy. If one cord is paralysed, it cannot meet the other in the midline, resulting in an air leak during speech and a characteristic weak, breathy voice.
  • Systemic Conditions: Diseases affecting the whole body can manifest with hoarseness. For example, hypothyroidism can cause myxoedema, a gelatinous fluid infiltration into the vocal cords, making them thick and leading to a low-pitched, coarse voice. Neuromuscular conditions like myasthenia gravis can cause fatigable weakness of the laryngeal muscles.
Differential Diagnosis (DDx) Framework

A structured approach to the differential diagnosis of a hoarse voice is essential to ensure life-threatening conditions are prioritised.

Must not miss!
  • Epiglottitis: Presents acutely with severe sore throat, drooling, and stridor, representing a medical emergency due to potential airway obstruction.
  • Laryngeal cancer: Typically causes persistent and progressive hoarseness, especially in older patients with a history of smoking or heavy alcohol use.
  • Oesophageal cancer: May cause hoarseness by direct invasion or compression of the recurrent laryngeal nerve, often associated with dysphagia and weight loss.
  • Lung cancer: A tumour in the apex of the lung (Pancoast tumour) can compress the recurrent laryngeal nerve, causing hoarseness as a key sign.
  • Recurrent Laryngeal Nerve palsy: Presents with a weak, breathy voice and can be idiopathic or a sign of underlying pathology in the neck, chest, or cranium.
Common causes
  • Voice overuse: Common in professional voice users (e.g., teachers, singers), leading to a strained voice that worsens with use.
  • Laryngitis: An acute, self-limiting hoarseness, typically following a viral upper respiratory tract infection.
  • GORD: Chronic acid reflux irritates the larynx, causing a hoarse or rough voice, often worse in the morning, with associated throat clearing.
  • Vocal cord nodules: Benign, callus-like growths on the vocal cords from chronic voice strain, causing persistent hoarseness.
  • Hypothyroidism: A systemic cause leading to a deepened, rough, and gravelly voice due to fluid infiltration in the vocal cords.
  • Psychogenic: A functional voice disorder where a patient may whisper or have abnormal speech patterns without any structural or neurological cause.
  • COVID-19: Can cause acute laryngitis and a hoarse voice as part of the viral illness.
Easily missed
  • Smokers laryngitis: Chronic inflammation and oedema of the vocal cords due to smoking, leading to a persistently low-pitched, gruff voice (Reinke's oedema).
  • Myasthenia gravis: Characterised by fatigable weakness, where the voice becomes progressively weaker and more nasal with prolonged speaking.
Other Differential Diagnoses to consider
  • Vocal cord paralysis: Unilateral or bilateral cord dysfunction, often secondary to surgery or trauma.
  • Allergic rhinitis: Chronic post-nasal drip leading to irritation of the vocal cords.
  • Sarcoidosis: Granulomatous involvement of the upper airways leading to hoarseness.
  • Parkinson's disease: Neurological causes of weak or tremulous voice.
  • Amyotrophic lateral sclerosis (ALS): Progressive neurodegenerative disease affecting vocal cord function.
  • Vocal cord haemorrhage: Often following vocal strain or trauma to the cords.
History Taking
Patient’s Description: How can I help you today?
Starting with open-ended questions allows the patient to describe their symptoms in their own words without bias. It helps to guide further questions based on what is most relevant to the patient's specific concerns. Relevance: This approach provides a clear overview of the patient’s concerns and enables you to identify key details that may lead toward particular diagnoses.
When did the hoarseness start, and has it been constant or intermittent?
  • This helps establish the timeline, including onset, duration, and progression of symptoms, which are critical in differentiating acute vs chronic conditions.
  • Relevance: Acute onset (days) suggests laryngitis or viral infections like COVID-19, while a gradual, persistent hoarseness might point towards GORD, vocal cord nodules, or malignancy.
Do you have any pain, difficulty swallowing, or breathing problems?
  • Pain or difficulty swallowing (dysphagia) and breathing difficulties (dyspnoea) are key red flags for serious causes such as epiglottitis or laryngeal cancer.
  • Relevance: A positive response may suggest conditions like epiglottitis or malignancy, while a negative response may reduce the likelihood of severe obstructive conditions.
Have you noticed any triggers or factors that make the hoarseness better or worse?
  • Understanding triggers can provide insight into the cause. For example, worsening hoarseness after talking or shouting may suggest voice overuse or vocal cord nodules. Relief after taking antacids may point towards GORD.
  • Relevance: Identifying exacerbating factors can guide the diagnosis towards GORD, voice strain, or vocal cord pathology.
Do you smoke, or have you been exposed to irritants such as dust or chemicals?
  • Smoking is a significant risk factor for conditions like laryngeal cancer, smoker's laryngitis, and chronic irritation. Occupational exposures could also contribute to vocal cord irritation.
  • Relevance: A history of smoking or exposure to irritants would increase the likelihood of conditions like smoker's laryngitis or malignancy.
Do you have any associated symptoms such as weight loss, fatigue, or persistent cough?
  • These systemic symptoms may point to more serious pathology such as lung or oesophageal cancer.
  • Relevance: A positive response may suggest malignancy, while a negative response could make this diagnosis less likely.
Do you have a history of thyroid problems or any recent neck surgery?
  • Hypothyroidism is a known cause of hoarseness, while neck surgery (e.g., thyroidectomy) could lead to recurrent laryngeal nerve palsy.
  • Relevance: A positive response would suggest a higher likelihood of hypothyroidism or nerve damage as the cause.
Ideas, Concerns, and Expectations (ICE)
  • Asking about the patient’s ideas, concerns, and expectations helps ensure that their perspective is understood and addressed. The patient may fear serious diagnoses like cancer or be unsure if the issue is due to something simple like a cold or voice strain.
  • Relevance: This approach fosters a patient-centred consultation, ensuring their concerns about conditions like laryngeal cancer or vocal cord issues are acknowledged and managed appropriately.
Examination Strategy
Inspection of the throat
  • Inspect the oropharynx for signs of inflammation, swelling, erythema, or masses. This is crucial in identifying possible causes like laryngitis or malignancy.
  • Relevance: Positive findings such as erythema or exudates suggest laryngitis or infection (e.g., COVID-19), while masses or ulcerations raise concerns for malignancy. A negative finding may rule out acute infectious causes.
Palpation of the neck (lymph nodes and thyroid)
  • Palpate for cervical lymphadenopathy or thyroid enlargement. Lymphadenopathy may indicate infection, malignancy, or other inflammatory conditions, while thyroid enlargement could point towards hypothyroidism.
  • Relevance: Enlarged, hard, or fixed lymph nodes suggest malignancies such as laryngeal or lung cancer. A thyroid goitre may indicate hypothyroidism or thyroid malignancy. Absence of lymphadenopathy may help exclude significant infections or cancers.
Examination of the voice quality
  • Listen to the patient's voice for qualities such as breathiness, strain, or weakness. Changes in voice quality can indicate structural or neurological issues.
  • Relevance: A breathy voice may suggest vocal cord paralysis or nodules, while a strained or weak voice could point towards myasthenia gravis or Parkinson’s disease. A normal voice reduces the likelihood of significant structural or neurological pathology.
Cranial nerve examination
  • Examine the cranial nerves, particularly the vagus nerve (CN X), which innervates the vocal cords. Check for signs of recurrent laryngeal nerve palsy.
  • Relevance: A positive finding of hoarseness with nerve weakness (e.g., uvula deviation) suggests recurrent laryngeal nerve palsy, which can occur due to thyroid surgery, lung cancer, or stroke. Normal cranial nerve function makes neurological causes less likely.
Chest examination (Auscultation)
  • Auscultate the chest to check for signs of pulmonary disease, such as wheezing or crackles. Lung involvement can be associated with hoarseness in conditions like lung cancer or infection.
  • Relevance: Positive findings such as wheezing or decreased breath sounds could suggest lung cancer or infection. A normal chest exam reduces the likelihood of pulmonary causes.
Flexible laryngoscopy (if available)
  • A direct visualisation of the vocal cords with a flexible laryngoscope can help assess for abnormalities such as nodules, paralysis, or tumours.
  • Relevance: Findings such as vocal cord nodules, paralysis, or masses can confirm specific diagnoses like vocal cord nodules, recurrent laryngeal nerve palsy, or laryngeal cancer. A normal laryngoscopy helps exclude significant structural causes.
Red Flag Synthesis

This section synthesises the key red flags from the source document. Any of these findings in a patient with a hoarse voice necessitates urgent senior review and likely escalation of care, typically via a two-week wait referral to ENT in the UK.

Severe difficulty swallowing or breathing

Significance: This could indicate life-threatening conditions such as epiglottitis, laryngeal cancer, or significant laryngeal oedema, requiring urgent intervention to secure the airway.

Unexplained weight loss

Significance: Weight loss is a constitutional symptom that could signify an underlying malignancy, particularly laryngeal, oesophageal or lung cancer, which requires prompt evaluation.

Persistent hoarseness lasting longer than 3 weeks

Significance: According to NICE NG12 guidelines, this is a key red flag for laryngeal cancer, requiring urgent referral for laryngoscopy, especially in patients over 45.

History of smoking or heavy alcohol use

Significance: These are the most significant lifestyle risk factors for laryngeal and other head and neck cancers, substantially increasing the index of suspicion for malignancy.

Neck mass or enlarged cervical lymph nodes

Significance: A palpable, hard, or fixed neck mass could represent a primary tumour or metastatic spread from a laryngeal or other head and neck cancer, requiring urgent diagnostic evaluation.

Cough, haemoptysis, or chest pain

Significance: These symptoms, especially coughing up blood, suggest a primary lung cancer that may be causing hoarseness via compression of the recurrent laryngeal nerve, mandating an urgent chest X-ray and referral.

Investigations Strategy & Interpretation
Flexible Laryngoscopy
  • This investigation involves direct visualisation of the vocal cords and larynx, helping to identify structural abnormalities, nodules, or masses.
  • Result Significance: Positive findings, such as vocal cord nodules, masses, or paralysis, suggest vocal cord pathology or malignancy (e.g., laryngeal cancer). A normal result can help rule out significant structural causes.
Chest X-ray
  • A chest X-ray is important to assess for lung pathology, such as lung cancer, which may cause hoarseness due to compression of the recurrent laryngeal nerve (e.g., Pancoast tumour).
  • Result Significance: Positive findings such as a lung mass or consolidation could suggest lung cancer or infection. A negative result may help exclude significant pulmonary causes.
Thyroid Function Tests (TFTs)
  • Thyroid dysfunction, particularly hypothyroidism, can cause hoarseness due to oedema of the vocal cords and other mechanisms.
  • Result Significance: Positive results indicating hypothyroidism could explain the hoarse voice. Normal results would rule out thyroid dysfunction as a cause.
CT or MRI of the Neck and Chest
  • Imaging of the neck and chest can provide detailed visualisation of soft tissues and detect masses, tumours, or lymphadenopathy that may not be visible on a chest X-ray.
  • Result Significance: A positive result, such as a tumour or mass, would raise concerns for malignancies like laryngeal cancer or recurrent laryngeal nerve compression. A negative result reduces the likelihood of structural causes.
Throat Swab for Culture and Sensitivity
  • In cases of suspected infectious laryngitis, a throat swab can identify bacterial or viral pathogens, helping guide antimicrobial therapy.
  • Result Significance: Positive results (e.g., Streptococcus or viral pathogens) suggest an infectious cause like bacterial laryngitis or COVID-19. A negative result reduces the likelihood of infection as the primary cause.
Fine Needle Aspiration (FNA) of Neck Mass
  • If a neck mass or enlarged lymph nodes are palpated, FNA can be used to sample cells for cytology, aiding in the diagnosis of malignancy or infection.
  • Result Significance: Positive cytology findings could suggest metastatic cancer (e.g., from laryngeal or lung cancer). A negative result may help rule out malignancy, but further imaging may still be required.
Evidence-Based Diagnosis (EBDx)
Persistent Hoarseness > 3 weeks
  • Persistent hoarseness lasting more than 3 weeks is a strong indicator of underlying pathology, including malignancy such as laryngeal cancer. Early referral for laryngoscopy is crucial.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 93%, Specificity: 47% for malignancy in patients with hoarseness > 3 weeks.
  • Reference: National Institute for Health and Care Excellence (NICE) Guidelines, "Suspected Cancer: Recognition and Referral" (NG12).
  • Significance: A positive finding of persistent hoarseness should prompt urgent investigation for malignancy, while a negative finding (hoarseness lasting less than 3 weeks) may suggest a benign or self-limiting cause like viral laryngitis.
Smoking and Heavy Alcohol Use
  • Smoking and alcohol are significant risk factors for laryngeal cancer. The combination of both risk factors exponentially increases the risk of head and neck cancers.
  • Evidence: Likelihood Ratio for laryngeal cancer in heavy smokers: 12.2, in heavy alcohol users: 3.7.
  • Reference: Cancer Research UK and the American Cancer Society data on laryngeal cancer risk factors.
  • Significance: A positive history of smoking and alcohol use raises suspicion for malignancy, warranting early laryngoscopy and imaging. Absence of these factors reduces, but does not eliminate, the risk of cancer.
Cervical Lymphadenopathy
  • Palpable, enlarged, or fixed cervical lymph nodes are concerning for metastatic spread of head and neck cancers, including laryngeal cancer.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 68%, Specificity: 81% for detecting malignancy in patients with cervical lymphadenopathy.
  • Reference: "Evaluation of the Patient with Neck Masses" – American Family Physician, 2015.
  • Significance: Positive findings of cervical lymphadenopathy should prompt further investigation for metastatic cancer, while the absence of lymphadenopathy lowers suspicion but does not rule out cancer.
Flexible Laryngoscopy Findings
  • Flexible laryngoscopy is the gold standard for visualising vocal cord and laryngeal abnormalities, such as nodules, tumours, and vocal cord paralysis.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 95%, Specificity: 90% for detecting laryngeal pathology, including malignancies.
  • Reference: "Diagnostic Accuracy of Flexible Laryngoscopy for Laryngeal Pathology" – The Laryngoscope, 2013.
  • Significance: Positive findings of nodules or masses highly suggest laryngeal pathology, warranting biopsy. Negative findings can largely exclude serious structural causes.
Chest X-ray for Hoarseness
  • A chest X-ray is useful in assessing for lung pathology that might explain hoarseness, such as a Pancoast tumour compressing the recurrent laryngeal nerve.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 86%, Specificity: 78% for detecting lung cancer in patients with hoarseness and chest symptoms.
  • Reference: "Chest Imaging in Lung Cancer Diagnosis" – BMJ, 2020.
  • Significance: Positive findings of a lung mass or abnormality suggest possible lung cancer, requiring further CT imaging. Negative results reduce, but do not entirely rule out, lung pathology.
Diagnostic Timeout & Cognitive Biases

Awareness of common thinking errors is essential to improve diagnostic accuracy and patient safety when assessing a patient with a hoarse voice. Before finalising a diagnosis, it is good practice for clinicians at all levels to take a 'diagnostic timeout' to challenge their own reasoning.

  • Anchoring Bias: The tendency to lock onto an initial, benign diagnosis too early. For example, attributing persistent hoarseness in a 60-year-old smoker to a "cold" they had a month ago, and failing to consider laryngeal cancer despite the persistence of symptoms beyond the expected timeframe for laryngitis. A key question to ask is: "Does the duration of this symptom fit with an acute, self-limiting cause?"
  • Confirmation Bias: Actively seeking out evidence that supports an initial belief while ignoring contradictory data. For instance, focusing on a patient's profession as a teacher to confirm "voice overuse" while downplaying the new and unprompted complaint of associated ear pain (a red flag for referred pain from a tumour).
  • Attribution Error: Incorrectly attributing symptoms to a patient's known condition or lifestyle. A classic example is assuming a hoarse voice in a patient with known GORD is simply due to reflux, without exploring red flags like weight loss or dysphagia that could indicate a new, more sinister pathology like oesophageal cancer.
  • Over-reliance on Normal Tests: Placing too much weight on a single reassuring but non-definitive test result. A normal physical examination of the neck does not rule out a laryngeal tumour. The key NICE guideline (NG12) is based on symptoms (hoarseness > 3 weeks), not signs, for this very reason. Persistent symptoms always trump a single normal examination finding.
Illustrative Management Pathways

For educational purposes, this section describes the typical principles of management for key diagnoses. These are not prescriptive instructions. All management must be guided by official UK guidelines, local formularies, and senior clinical advice.

Acute Viral Laryngitis

This is the most common cause of acute hoarseness and is typically self-limiting. After excluding red flags, management is focused on reassurance and symptomatic relief. Key advice includes strict voice rest (avoiding whispering, which also strains the cords), maintaining good hydration, and considering steam inhalation. Antibiotics are not indicated. Patients should be advised to return if symptoms do not resolve within 3 weeks.

GORD-Related Laryngitis (Laryngopharyngeal Reflux)

Where GORD is the suspected cause, management involves a combination of lifestyle modifications and medical therapy. Patients are advised on weight management, avoiding late meals, and reducing intake of trigger foods, alcohol, and caffeine. A trial of a proton pump inhibitor (PPI) for 1-2 months is a standard approach. Referral to ENT may be considered if symptoms persist despite an adequate trial of therapy.

Vocal Cord Nodules / Voice Overuse

For patients with hoarseness due to occupational or habitual voice strain (e.g., teachers, singers), the cornerstone of management is referral to a Speech and Language Therapist (SALT). SALT provides crucial voice therapy, teaching techniques for more efficient and less traumatic voice use. This is often curative for benign lesions like vocal cord nodules.

Suspected Laryngeal Cancer

This is a time-critical pathway. Any patient meeting the criteria in NICE guideline NG12 (e.g., unexplained hoarseness for > 3 weeks, especially if over 45) requires an urgent two-week wait referral to an ENT department. The patient will undergo a full head and neck examination and flexible nasendoscopy to visualise the larynx. Further management will be determined by the findings and may involve biopsy, staging scans (CT/MRI), and discussion in a multidisciplinary team (MDT) meeting.

Reflection & Learning

These brief cases illustrate the application of clinical reasoning to different presentations of hoarseness. They are for educational purposes only.

Case 1: The Persistent "Cold" in Primary Care

A 65-year-old man, a lifelong smoker, presents to his GP. He reports a "croaky" voice for the past 6 weeks. He initially thought it was the end of a cold, but it has not improved. He denies any other symptoms. The problem representation is an older male with a major risk factor (smoking) presenting with persistent hoarseness for > 3 weeks. Despite the lack of other symptoms, this meets the NICE criteria for an urgent two-week wait referral to ENT to rule out laryngeal cancer.

Case 2: The Exhausted Voice in a Teacher

A 28-year-old primary school teacher presents with a 4-month history of a hoarse voice that is almost normal on Monday mornings but becomes weak and strained by Friday afternoon. She is a non-smoker and otherwise well. The problem representation is a young professional voice user with an intermittent, fatigable pattern of hoarseness and no red flags. The most likely diagnosis is vocal strain/nodules. The appropriate management is reassurance and referral to Speech and Language Therapy for voice coaching.

Case 3: The Morning Hoarseness and Throat Clearing

A 52-year-old overweight office worker complains of several months of a "gruff" voice, which is particularly bad when he wakes up. He also reports a constant need to clear his throat and a bitter taste in his mouth sometimes. He denies weight loss or swallowing problems. The problem representation is a middle-aged man with risk factors for GORD presenting with chronic hoarseness and classic symptoms of laryngopharyngeal reflux. A trial of a PPI and lifestyle advice would be a reasonable first step, with safety-netting advice to return if symptoms persist or red flags develop.
