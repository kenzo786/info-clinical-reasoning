Anorectal Symptoms Diagnostic Reasoning Reference
Context: Anorectal Symptoms Assessment
Overview & Links to Key Guidelines

Anorectal symptoms, such as pain, bleeding, and changes in bowel habits, are extremely common presentations in both primary care and emergency settings. While many cases are due to benign conditions like haemorrhoids or anal fissures, these symptoms can also herald serious underlying pathology, including colorectal cancer, inflammatory bowel disease, or significant infection. The clinician's primary challenge is to differentiate benign from life-threatening causes through a careful and systematic approach, ensuring that red flags are identified promptly while avoiding unnecessary anxiety and investigation for self-limiting issues.

This educational module provides a structured framework for the clinical reasoning process when faced with a patient presenting with anorectal symptoms. It is designed to build foundational knowledge for students and serve as a refresher for experienced clinicians. For definitive, real-time management protocols, users must refer to official UK guidelines from authoritative bodies such as NICE (e.g., NG12 for Suspected Cancer, CG147 for Inflammatory Bowel Disease) and relevant professional bodies.

Pathophysiology/Basic Science

A fundamental understanding of anorectal pathophysiology is essential for accurate diagnosis. Pain and bleeding in this region can arise from several distinct mechanisms:

  • Vascular Engorgement & Prolapse: The anal canal contains vascular cushions, which are normal structures that contribute to continence. When these cushions become pathologically engorged, friable, and displaced, they are termed haemorrhoids. Increased intra-abdominal pressure (e.g., from straining, pregnancy) is a key contributing factor. Bleeding occurs when the fragile surface is traumatised by passing stool, typically resulting in painless, bright red blood.
  • Mucosal Tear & Sphincter Spasm: An anal fissure is a longitudinal tear in the anoderm, the specialised skin lining the lower anal canal. It is most commonly caused by trauma from passing a hard stool. The tear exposes the underlying internal anal sphincter muscle, leading to intense spasm. This spasm is not only excruciatingly painful but also reduces blood flow to the area, impairing healing and creating a vicious cycle of pain and non-healing.
  • Infection and Abscess Formation: The anal canal contains several small glands. If the duct of one of these glands becomes blocked, bacteria can multiply, leading to a localised infection and the formation of an anorectal abscess—a collection of pus. This process causes intense, throbbing, constant pain, localised swelling, and systemic signs of infection like fever.
  • Malignant Transformation: Colorectal cancer arises from the uncontrolled proliferation of epithelial cells lining the colon or rectum. As a tumour grows, it can become fragile and ulcerated, leading to bleeding that may be bright red or mixed with stool. If the tumour grows large enough to obstruct the lumen, it can cause changes in bowel habit, such as constipation or the passage of pencil-thin stools. Systemic effects like weight loss are due to the metabolic demands of the cancer.
  • Inflammation: In inflammatory bowel disease (IBD), such as Crohn's disease or ulcerative colitis, the immune system inappropriately attacks the gastrointestinal tract. In ulcerative colitis, this inflammation is confined to the colon and rectum, leading to diffuse ulceration that causes bloody diarrhoea, urgency, and tenesmus. Crohn's disease can affect the perianal area directly, causing deep, complex fissures and fistulae.
Differential Diagnosis (DDx) Framework

A structured approach to the differential diagnosis of anorectal symptoms is essential to ensure life-threatening conditions are prioritised.

Must not miss!
  • Colorectal cancer: Malignant growth in the colon or rectum, often presenting with persistent rectal bleeding, change in bowel habit, and weight loss.
  • Anorectal abscess: A painful, pus-filled cavity near the anus or rectum, causing constant throbbing pain, swelling, and often fever.
  • Crohn's disease: A type of inflammatory bowel disease that can affect any part of the digestive tract, potentially causing perianal disease like fissures or fistulas.
  • Ulcerative colitis: A type of inflammatory bowel disease that causes inflammation and ulcers in the large intestine and rectum, leading to bloody diarrhoea and urgency.
  • Severe upper Gastrointestinal bleeding: Bleeding from the oesophagus, stomach, or duodenum, which can sometimes present with altered blood (melaena) passed per rectum.
  • Mesenteric ischaemia: A condition caused by reduced blood flow to the intestines, presenting with severe abdominal pain that is often out of proportion to clinical signs.
Common causes
  • Haemorrhoids: Swollen veins in the anus and lower rectum, often presenting with painless bright red bleeding or a palpable lump.
  • Anal fissure: A small tear in the lining of the anus, causing severe sharp pain and bleeding during and after bowel movements.
  • Gastroenteritis: Inflammation of the stomach and intestines, typically causing diarrhoea which can lead to anal irritation and sometimes minor bleeding.
  • Diverticular disease: Small bulges or pockets (diverticula) in the lining of the intestine, which can become inflamed or bleed, causing pain and a change in bowel habit.
  • Colonic polyp: A small growth on the inner lining of the colon or rectum, which is often asymptomatic but can cause bleeding.
  • Proctalgia fugax: Fleeting, severe episodes of anorectal pain caused by spasm of the pelvic floor muscles, with no underlying pathology.
  • COVID-19: Can occasionally present with gastrointestinal symptoms including diarrhoea, which may lead to secondary anal irritation.
Easily missed
  • Bleeding disorders: Conditions that impair the body's ability to form blood clots, leading to easy or excessive bleeding from minor trauma, including from the anorectal area.
  • Angiodysplasia: Abnormal, fragile blood vessels in the gastrointestinal tract that can rupture and cause bleeding, often in older adults.
Other Differential Diagnoses to consider
  • Anal cancer: Rare malignant growth in the anal region, typically presenting with rectal bleeding and discomfort.
  • Rectal prolapse: Descent of the rectal wall, causing discomfort, bleeding, and a sensation of mass.
  • Ischaemic colitis: Insufficient blood flow to the colon, causing pain, bloody stools, and diarrhoea.
  • Sexually transmitted infections (STIs): Infections such as gonorrhoea, syphilis, or chlamydia that can cause proctitis or anorectal discomfort.
  • Radiation proctitis: Inflammation and damage to the rectum following pelvic radiation therapy, often presenting with bleeding and discomfort.
  • Levator ani syndrome: Chronic anorectal pain associated with spasm of the levator ani muscle, often without obvious pathology.
History Taking
**Patient’s Description**: How can I help you today?
Starting with open-ended questions such as "Can you tell me more about what brought you here today?" allows the patient to explain their symptoms in their own words. This often leads to valuable clues and helps guide subsequent focused questions. Relevance: Allows the patient to describe the issue from their perspective, which can reveal key details about the symptom's nature, severity, and impact on daily life, thus narrowing the differential diagnosis.
When did the symptoms start?
  • Helps establish the onset of symptoms, whether they are acute, subacute, or chronic.
  • Relevance: A sudden onset may suggest acute causes such as anorectal abscess or acute anal fissure. Chronic symptoms may indicate haemorrhoids, colorectal cancer, or inflammatory bowel disease.
Can you describe the nature of the pain or discomfort?
  • Explores the quality of pain (sharp, throbbing, burning, etc.), which can differentiate between conditions.
  • Relevance: Sharp pain during defecation may point towards anal fissure, whereas dull or aching pain that worsens with sitting could suggest haemorrhoids or proctalgia fugax. Throbbing pain may indicate an anorectal abscess.
Are there any associated symptoms such as bleeding?
  • Asks about rectal bleeding, which is a key symptom that may accompany anorectal conditions.
  • Relevance: Bright red blood may suggest haemorrhoids or anal fissure. Darker blood, mixed with stool, could be a sign of colorectal cancer or inflammatory bowel disease.
Have you noticed any lumps or swelling?
  • Assesses for the presence of external or internal swelling, possibly indicating haemorrhoids or anal abscess.
  • Relevance: A palpable lump near the anus may suggest external haemorrhoids, while an internal lump or mass could be indicative of a more serious condition like a rectal tumour or prolapse.
Do you have any changes in your bowel habits (constipation, diarrhoea)?
  • Evaluates whether the patient has had any changes in the frequency or consistency of bowel movements, which could be related to anorectal pathology.
  • Relevance: Diarrhoea may suggest gastroenteritis, Crohn’s disease, or ulcerative colitis, while constipation might indicate a colorectal polyp or haemorrhoids. Changes in stool shape (pencil-thin) could point to colorectal cancer.
Have you had any recent trauma or injury to the area?
  • Investigates if the patient has experienced any direct trauma, which could result in symptoms such as anal fissures or bruising.
  • Relevance: A history of trauma, especially following childbirth or surgery, may be relevant for conditions like anal fissures or pelvic floor dysfunction.
Do you have any history of similar symptoms?
  • Asks about a previous history of anorectal symptoms, which might suggest recurrent or chronic conditions.
  • Relevance: Recurrent symptoms may point towards conditions like haemorrhoids or proctalgia fugax, whereas new or worsening symptoms may indicate a more serious diagnosis like colorectal cancer or inflammatory bowel disease.
Have you noticed any other associated symptoms like weight loss or fatigue?
  • Explores for systemic symptoms that could indicate more serious conditions.
  • Relevance: Unexplained weight loss or fatigue may be a red flag for colorectal cancer, inflammatory bowel disease, or chronic infection.
**Ideas, Concerns, and Expectations (ICE)**
  • Ask ICE questions such as "What do you think might be causing this?", "Is there something specific you are worried about?" and "What were you hoping we might be able to do for you today?".
  • Relevance: These questions help to understand the patient’s perspective and any fears they may have. For example, the patient may be concerned about cancer or an infection. It also helps manage expectations regarding treatment and diagnosis.
Examination Strategy
General Inspection
  • Begin by observing the patient’s general appearance, noting any signs of discomfort, pallor, or weight loss.
  • Relevance: Pallor may suggest anaemia, which could be secondary to chronic gastrointestinal bleeding (e.g., colorectal cancer or angiodysplasia). Weight loss might be a red flag for malignancy or chronic inflammatory bowel disease. The absence of these findings may make severe systemic causes less likely.
Abdominal Examination
  • Palpate the abdomen for tenderness, distension, masses, or organomegaly.
  • Relevance: Tenderness in the lower abdomen may suggest diverticular disease or inflammatory bowel disease (Crohn’s disease, ulcerative colitis). A mass could indicate a tumour, such as colorectal cancer. A negative abdominal examination does not rule out localised anorectal pathology like haemorrhoids or fissures.
Inspection of the Perianal Area
  • Visually inspect the anus and perianal area for any signs of external haemorrhoids, fissures, swelling, erythema, or discharge.
  • Relevance: Visible haemorrhoids or fissures confirm a local cause of symptoms. Erythema, swelling, or discharge may indicate an anorectal abscess or infection (e.g., sexually transmitted infections or bacterial abscess). A lack of external findings may suggest internal pathology, such as internal haemorrhoids or deeper anorectal causes like proctitis.
Digital Rectal Examination (DRE)
  • Perform a DRE to assess for tenderness, masses, sphincter tone, and the presence of blood.
  • Relevance: A palpable mass may indicate colorectal cancer or a rectal polyp. Blood on the glove suggests active bleeding, possibly from haemorrhoids or more serious causes such as colorectal cancer or an upper gastrointestinal source. Increased sphincter tone may be seen in anal fissure, while reduced tone could indicate neurological conditions. A normal DRE with no abnormalities may make anorectal abscess, cancer, or severe fissure less likely.
Anoscopy/Proctoscopy (if available)
  • Anoscopy or proctoscopy allows direct visualisation of the anal canal and rectum for internal haemorrhoids, fissures, polyps, or other mucosal abnormalities.
  • Relevance: Internal haemorrhoids, polyps, or areas of inflammation (e.g., in proctitis) can be directly observed. This helps confirm the diagnosis of local causes such as haemorrhoids or rectal polyps. The absence of findings may point to more proximal causes of symptoms (e.g., colorectal cancer or inflammatory bowel disease).
Pelvic Examination (if indicated)
  • If the patient is female and symptoms could be gynaecological in origin (e.g., pelvic pain, masses), consider a pelvic examination to assess for uterine or adnexal masses, tenderness, or discharge.
  • Relevance: Pelvic examination may help identify gynaecological causes of anorectal discomfort such as endometriosis, pelvic inflammatory disease, or tumours. A normal pelvic exam helps rule out these gynaecological causes.
Neurological Examination (if indicated)
  • Assess for neurological deficits, particularly in the lower limbs or sphincter control, to rule out spinal cord or cauda equina syndrome, which may present with bowel dysfunction and anorectal symptoms.
  • Relevance: Neurological signs such as decreased anal tone or saddle anaesthesia are red flags for cauda equina syndrome or spinal cord pathology. A normal neurological examination makes these diagnoses less likely.
Red Flag Synthesis
Unexplained weight loss
  • Unintentional weight loss is a significant red flag, particularly when associated with gastrointestinal symptoms, as it may indicate a serious underlying pathology.
  • Significance: This could indicate malignancy, such as colorectal cancer, which requires urgent investigation, including imaging and possible referral for colonoscopy.
Rectal bleeding (especially dark or mixed with stool)
  • Rectal bleeding can range from benign causes (e.g., haemorrhoids) to more serious conditions. Dark blood or blood mixed with stool raises suspicion for a higher gastrointestinal source or malignancy.
  • Significance: This could suggest colorectal cancer, angiodysplasia, or significant gastrointestinal bleeding, all of which require urgent evaluation, including imaging and endoscopy.
Change in bowel habits (especially to diarrhoea or constipation lasting > 6 weeks)
  • A persistent change in bowel habits, particularly in older adults, is concerning for a potential obstructive process or malignancy within the bowel.
  • Significance: This could indicate colorectal cancer or inflammatory bowel disease, both of which require further investigation, including colonoscopy and biopsy.
Severe anal pain with fever
  • Severe anal pain accompanied by fever suggests an infectious or inflammatory process, which can quickly escalate if untreated.
  • Significance: This could indicate an anorectal abscess, which may require urgent surgical intervention to prevent progression to sepsis.
Painless rectal mass
  • A painless mass felt on digital rectal examination or by the patient is highly concerning for malignancy, especially if associated with other red flags such as bleeding or weight loss.
  • Significance: This could indicate colorectal cancer, necessitating urgent referral for further imaging and biopsy to confirm the diagnosis.
Anaemia (especially iron-deficiency)
  • Unexplained anaemia, particularly iron-deficiency anaemia, may be a sign of chronic gastrointestinal blood loss.
  • Significance: This could indicate colorectal cancer, angiodysplasia, or another cause of chronic bleeding that requires investigation with blood tests and endoscopy.
Perianal numbness or incontinence
  • Perianal numbness, loss of sphincter control, or faecal incontinence raises concern for a neurological emergency, especially if combined with lower back pain or lower limb weakness.
  • Significance: This could indicate cauda equina syndrome, a spinal emergency that requires immediate imaging and surgical intervention to prevent permanent neurological damage.
Investigations Strategy & Interpretation
Full Blood Count (FBC)
  • A full blood count is important to assess for anaemia, signs of infection, or inflammation.
  • Result Significance: Anaemia, especially iron-deficiency, may suggest chronic gastrointestinal bleeding from conditions like colorectal cancer or angiodysplasia. Elevated white cell count may indicate infection, such as anorectal abscess or inflammatory bowel disease. A normal result may help rule out systemic conditions.
C-Reactive Protein (CRP) or Erythrocyte Sedimentation Rate (ESR)
  • CRP and ESR are markers of inflammation, useful in detecting inflammatory conditions.
  • Result Significance: Elevated levels may suggest inflammatory bowel disease (e.g., Crohn’s disease or ulcerative colitis) or an acute infection, such as an anorectal abscess. Normal levels may help rule out active inflammation or infection.
Faecal Occult Blood Test (FOBT)
  • FOBT is used to detect hidden (occult) blood in the stool, which may indicate gastrointestinal bleeding.
  • Result Significance: A positive result suggests the presence of bleeding, which may be due to colorectal cancer, angiodysplasia, or inflammatory bowel disease. A negative result decreases the likelihood of significant gastrointestinal bleeding but does not completely rule it out.
Stool Cultures
  • Stool cultures are useful in identifying infectious causes of diarrhoea and anorectal symptoms, such as bacterial or parasitic infections.
  • Result Significance: Positive results may indicate infectious gastroenteritis, which could explain diarrhoea or rectal discomfort. Negative results suggest non-infectious causes, such as inflammatory bowel disease or irritable bowel syndrome.
Flexible Sigmoidoscopy or Colonoscopy
  • Endoscopic evaluation is critical for visualising the rectum and colon, allowing direct identification of lesions, inflammation, or bleeding sources.
  • Result Significance: Positive findings such as colorectal cancer, polyps, or inflammatory changes (as in Crohn’s disease or ulcerative colitis) help establish the diagnosis. A negative result can exclude structural abnormalities and localise pathology elsewhere.
Anoscopy or Proctoscopy
  • Anoscopy or proctoscopy allows direct visualisation of the anal canal and lower rectum to assess for haemorrhoids, fissures, or other local abnormalities.
  • Result Significance: Positive findings may include haemorrhoids, fissures, or rectal tumours. A normal examination suggests the need to look for more proximal gastrointestinal causes.
Pelvic MRI or CT Scan
  • MRI or CT imaging is useful for evaluating complex anorectal conditions, such as abscesses, fistulas, or pelvic masses, that may not be visible with endoscopy.
  • Result Significance: Positive findings such as pelvic masses, deep abscesses, or fistulas may point towards malignancy, infection, or inflammatory bowel disease. A negative result can rule out these conditions.
Serum Iron Studies
  • Iron studies can help identify iron-deficiency anaemia, which may be due to chronic blood loss from the gastrointestinal tract.
  • Result Significance: Low iron levels suggest chronic blood loss, possibly due to conditions like colorectal cancer or angiodysplasia. Normal iron levels may rule out significant chronic bleeding.
Evidence-Based Diagnosis (EBDx)
Unexplained Weight Loss in Colorectal Cancer
  • Unexplained weight loss is a key red flag symptom in patients presenting with gastrointestinal complaints, particularly for identifying malignancies such as colorectal cancer.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 45%, Specificity: 95%, Positive Likelihood Ratio: 9.0.
  • Reference: Hamilton W et al., BMJ 2005: “Risk of colorectal cancer in symptomatic patients in primary care: a case-control study of electronic records”.
  • Significance: A positive finding (weight loss) significantly increases the likelihood of colorectal cancer, prompting urgent referral for colonoscopy. A negative finding (absence of weight loss) does not rule out colorectal cancer but lowers the probability.
Rectal Bleeding in Colorectal Cancer
  • Rectal bleeding, particularly when mixed with stool or dark in colour, is a key symptom for detecting colorectal cancer.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 71%, Specificity: 67%, Positive Likelihood Ratio: 2.15.
  • Reference: Astin M et al., BMC Med 2011: “The diagnostic value of symptoms for colorectal cancer in primary care: a systematic review.”
  • Significance: A positive finding of rectal bleeding, especially in older adults or with associated red flag symptoms, strongly suggests colorectal cancer and warrants further investigation. A negative finding does not exclude cancer but makes it less likely.
Iron-Deficiency Anaemia in Gastrointestinal Bleeding
  • Iron-deficiency anaemia is often a sign of chronic gastrointestinal blood loss and is particularly concerning for malignancy or angiodysplasia.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 32%, Specificity: 92%, Positive Likelihood Ratio: 4.0.
  • Reference: British Society of Gastroenterology guidelines 2011: “Guidelines for the management of iron-deficiency anaemia”.
  • Significance: A positive finding of iron-deficiency anaemia increases the suspicion for gastrointestinal malignancies such as colorectal cancer. Negative findings suggest other causes of anaemia but do not completely rule out occult bleeding.
Positive Faecal Occult Blood Test (FOBT) in Colorectal Cancer
  • The FOBT is used as a screening tool to detect hidden blood in the stool, which can be an early sign of colorectal cancer or other sources of bleeding.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 79%, Specificity: 94%, Positive Likelihood Ratio: 13.2.
  • Reference: Guaiac FOBT Screening Study Group, NEJM 1993: “The effect of fecal occult-blood screening on the incidence of colorectal cancer”.
  • Significance: A positive FOBT result strongly suggests colorectal cancer or another source of gastrointestinal bleeding and should lead to urgent endoscopic evaluation. A negative result reduces the likelihood of gastrointestinal bleeding but does not exclude it.
CRP in Inflammatory Bowel Disease (IBD)
  • CRP is a non-specific marker of inflammation, commonly elevated in inflammatory conditions such as Crohn's disease or ulcerative colitis.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 49%, Specificity: 92%, Positive Likelihood Ratio: 6.1.
  • Reference: Solem CA et al., Am J Gastroenterol 2005: “Correlation of C-reactive protein with clinical, endoscopic, histologic, and radiographic activity in inflammatory bowel disease”.
  • Significance: An elevated CRP supports the diagnosis of active inflammatory bowel disease (IBD) and may help differentiate it from functional disorders like irritable bowel syndrome (IBS). A normal CRP does not rule out IBD but makes active inflammation less likely.
Digital Rectal Examination (DRE) in Anorectal Pathology
  • The DRE is a crucial part of the physical exam for detecting anorectal masses, haemorrhoids, or other abnormalities.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity for detecting rectal cancer: 50%, Specificity: 85%, Positive Likelihood Ratio: 3.3.
  • Reference: Jellema P et al., BMJ 2010: “The diagnostic value of digital rectal examination in primary care screening for rectal cancer”.
  • Significance: A positive DRE finding of a mass is highly suggestive of rectal cancer and warrants urgent referral for colonoscopy. A negative DRE does not rule out rectal or colorectal cancer but makes it less likely.
Diagnostic Timeout & Cognitive Biases

To improve diagnostic accuracy in patients with anorectal symptoms, it is vital to be aware of common cognitive errors. Before finalising a diagnosis, clinicians should take a 'diagnostic timeout' to challenge their own reasoning.

  • Anchoring Bias: This is the most common pitfall. It is the tendency to lock onto a benign diagnosis like haemorrhoids too early, especially in a younger patient with rectal bleeding. This can cause the clinician to ignore or downplay subsequent red flags like a change in bowel habit or weight loss. Always ask: "Could this be cancer, and have I done enough to rule it out?"
  • Confirmation Bias: Actively seeking out features that confirm a suspected benign diagnosis. For example, focusing on a history of constipation to support a diagnosis of anal fissure, while failing to adequately explore the nature of rectal bleeding which might be atypical for a fissure and more concerning for IBD or malignancy.
  • Availability Heuristic: Overestimating the likelihood of a diagnosis because it is more recent or memorable. A clinician who has recently seen several cases of benign haemorrhoids might be less inclined to consider a more serious diagnosis in the next patient with similar symptoms.
  • Premature Closure: Accepting a diagnosis before it has been fully verified. A classic example is attributing all rectal bleeding to visually confirmed external haemorrhoids without considering the possibility of a concurrent, more proximal lesion like a rectal cancer. The presence of one pathology does not exclude another.
Illustrative Management Pathways

For educational purposes, this section describes the typical principles of management for key diagnoses. These are not prescriptive instructions. All management must be guided by official UK guidelines, local formularies, and senior clinical advice.

Haemorrhoids

Management is typically stepwise. Initial advice focuses on conservative measures: increasing dietary fibre and fluid intake to soften stools and avoid straining. Topical preparations containing local anaesthetics and corticosteroids can provide symptomatic relief. For persistent or prolapsing haemorrhoids, referral may be needed for procedures like rubber band ligation.

Anal Fissure

The primary goal is to relax the internal anal sphincter to improve blood flow and allow healing. This involves ensuring soft stools with fibre and laxatives. The first-line medical treatment is a topical calcium channel blocker (e.g., diltiazem) or glyceryl trinitrate (GTN) ointment. If medical management fails, referral for botulinum toxin injection or surgery (lateral sphincterotomy) may be required.

Anorectal Abscess

This is a surgical emergency. The definitive treatment is incision and drainage of the pus collection under anaesthesia. Antibiotics are often given, particularly if the patient is systemically unwell, immunocompromised, or has significant cellulitis. A significant proportion of abscesses can be associated with an underlying fistula-in-ano, which may require further treatment.

Suspected Colorectal Cancer

If a patient meets the criteria for an urgent two-week wait referral (e.g., based on age and symptom combination as per NICE NG12 guidelines), they must be referred immediately. The diagnostic pathway involves urgent colonoscopy and biopsy of any suspicious lesions. Subsequent management is determined by the multidisciplinary team (MDT) based on cancer staging and involves surgery, chemotherapy, and/or radiotherapy.

Reflection & Learning

These brief cases illustrate the application of clinical reasoning to different anorectal presentations. They are for educational purposes only.

Case 1: Suspected Malignancy in Primary Care

A 65-year-old man presents to his GP with a 6-week history of rectal bleeding. He describes darker blood mixed in with his stool and notes his bowels have become looser. He feels more tired than usual but denies weight loss. The problem representation is an older male with a persistent change in bowel habit and rectal bleeding concerning for colorectal cancer. Despite the absence of weight loss, the combination of symptoms and his age mandates an urgent two-week wait referral for colonoscopy.

Case 2: Classic Benign Presentation

A 30-year-old woman presents with severe anal pain during and after defecation, which she describes as "like passing glass". She has noticed small amounts of bright red blood on the toilet paper. She has a history of constipation. The problem representation is a young woman with a classic history for an anal fissure (severe pain on defecation, bright red blood). Examination reveals a posterior fissure. Management would involve stool softeners, dietary advice, and topical GTN ointment.

Case 3: Anorectal Emergency

A 45-year-old man with type 2 diabetes presents to the Emergency Department with a 3-day history of worsening, constant, throbbing perianal pain and is unable to sit comfortably. He feels hot and shivery. On examination, he has a temperature of 38.5°C and there is a tender, fluctuant, erythematous swelling next to his anus. The problem representation is a patient with risk factors for infection presenting with systemic sepsis and localised signs of a perianal abscess. This is a surgical emergency requiring immediate hospital admission for intravenous antibiotics and urgent incision and drainage.
