Anaemia Diagnostic Reasoning Reference
Context: Anaemia Assessment
Overview

Anaemia is one of the most common clinical findings in medicine, affecting a significant portion of the global population. It is not a diagnosis in itself but a sign of an underlying disease process. The presentation can range from asymptomatic, discovered incidentally on a routine blood test, to a life-threatening emergency with severe symptoms. The challenge for the clinician is to systematically identify the cause, which can span nutritional deficiencies, chronic diseases, genetic conditions, and malignancies.

A structured approach is crucial to correctly classify the anaemia, investigate its root cause efficiently, and initiate appropriate management. This process must differentiate between common, easily treatable causes like iron deficiency and 'must not miss' diagnoses such as gastrointestinal bleeding or haematological cancers. This educational module provides a foundational framework for the clinical reasoning process in diagnosing anaemia. For definitive, real-time management protocols, users must refer to official UK guidelines from authoritative bodies like the British Society for Haematology (BSH) and NICE (e.g., NG231 for Myelodysplastic Syndromes, CG13 for Coeliac Disease).

Pathophysiology/Basic Science

Understanding the underlying pathophysiology of anaemia is essential for its classification and diagnosis. Anaemia is defined as a reduction in haemoglobin concentration, red blood cell (RBC) count, or haematocrit below the normal range for an individual's age and sex. The causes can be broadly categorised into three main mechanisms:

  • Impaired Red Blood Cell (RBC) Production: This is the most common mechanism and arises when the bone marrow fails to produce an adequate number of RBCs. This can be due to a deficiency in essential building blocks or a primary failure of the marrow itself.
    • Microcytic Anaemia (MCV <80 fL): Characterised by small RBCs, this is most commonly caused by iron deficiency, where insufficient iron is available for haem synthesis. Other causes include thalassaemia, an inherited defect in globin chain synthesis, and the anaemia of chronic disease (though it is more commonly normocytic).
    • Normocytic Anaemia (MCV 80-100 fL): Characterised by normal-sized RBCs, this can be caused by a reduced production of erythropoietin in chronic kidney disease, suppression of erythropoiesis by inflammatory cytokines in anaemia of chronic disease, or by bone marrow failure, as seen in aplastic anaemia or infiltration by malignancy.
    • Macrocytic Anaemia (MCV >100 fL): Characterised by large RBCs, this is typically caused by impaired DNA synthesis, a process highly dependent on Vitamin B12 and Folate. Deficiency of either vitamin leads to megaloblastic anaemia. Other causes include alcohol excess, liver disease, and hypothyroidism.
  • Increased Red Blood Cell Destruction (Haemolysis): This occurs when RBCs are prematurely destroyed, either within the circulation (intravascular) or by the reticuloendothelial system (extravascular). The bone marrow increases production to compensate, leading to a high reticulocyte count. Causes can be intrinsic (defects within the RBC itself, e.g., hereditary spherocytosis, G6PD deficiency, sickle cell disease) or extrinsic (factors outside the RBC, e.g., autoimmune haemolytic anaemia, mechanical heart valves).
  • Blood Loss: Loss of whole blood from the circulation is a straightforward cause of anaemia. Acute blood loss (e.g., from trauma or major gastrointestinal bleeding) leads to a rapid drop in haemoglobin and haemodynamic instability. Chronic blood loss (e.g., slow gastrointestinal bleeding from a polyp or heavy menstrual bleeding) is more insidious and typically leads to iron deficiency anaemia as iron stores are gradually depleted.
Differential Diagnosis (DDx) Framework

A structured approach to the differential diagnosis of anaemia is essential to ensure life-threatening conditions are prioritised.

Must not miss!
  • Acute Gastrointestinal bleeding: Presents with haematemesis (vomiting blood) or melaena (black, tarry stools), often with signs of haemodynamic instability.
  • Crohn's disease: Can cause anaemia through chronic inflammation, malabsorption of nutrients (iron, B12, folate), and gastrointestinal blood loss.
  • Ulcerative colitis: Typically causes iron deficiency anaemia due to chronic bloody diarrhoea.
  • Multiple myeloma: A bone marrow cancer presenting with anaemia, bone pain, fatigue, and renal impairment.
  • Lymphoma: Can cause anaemia through bone marrow infiltration or autoimmune haemolysis, often associated with lymphadenopathy and B-symptoms (fever, night sweats, weight loss).
  • Leukaemia: Presents with features of bone marrow failure, including anaemia (fatigue, pallor), thrombocytopenia (bleeding, bruising), and neutropenia (infections).
Common causes
  • Iron deficiency anaemia: The most common cause of anaemia worldwide, often presenting with fatigue, shortness of breath on exertion, and pallor.
  • Thalassaemia: An inherited condition causing microcytic anaemia, with severity ranging from asymptomatic carrier to transfusion-dependent anaemia.
  • Sickle cell disease: An inherited haemoglobinopathy causing chronic haemolytic anaemia and painful vaso-occlusive crises.
  • Sideroblastic Anaemia: A rare disorder where the body has iron available but cannot incorporate it into haemoglobin, leading to anaemia.
  • Anaemia of chronic disease: A common cause in patients with long-term inflammatory conditions, infections, or cancer, presenting as a mild to moderate normocytic anaemia.
  • Haemolytic anaemia: Caused by premature destruction of red blood cells, presenting with anaemia, jaundice, and splenomegaly.
  • Myelodysplastic syndrome: A group of bone marrow disorders, typically in older adults, causing ineffective production of blood cells, leading to anaemia and other cytopenias.
  • Vitamin B12 deficiency: Often presents with macrocytic anaemia and can cause neurological symptoms (e.g., peripheral neuropathy, cognitive changes).
  • Folate deficiency: Causes macrocytic anaemia, often linked to poor diet, alcohol excess, or malabsorption.
  • Alcohol abuse: Can cause anaemia through multiple mechanisms, including folate deficiency, liver disease, and direct bone marrow suppression.
  • Hypothyroidism: Can cause a mild normocytic or macrocytic anaemia due to reduced metabolic rate and erythropoietin production.
  • COVID-19: Can be associated with anaemia of inflammation/chronic disease during or after the acute infection.
Easily missed
  • Medication-induced anaemia: Can be caused by various drugs (e.g., NSAIDs causing GI bleed, some antibiotics causing haemolysis) and is often overlooked.
  • Coeliac disease: An autoimmune disorder triggered by gluten, often presenting with iron deficiency anaemia due to malabsorption, sometimes without prominent GI symptoms.
Other Differential Diagnoses to consider
  • Chronic kidney disease: Associated with reduced erythropoietin production leading to anaemia.
  • Lead poisoning: Causes microcytic anaemia, often overlooked in paediatric and adult cases with exposure risks.
  • Aplastic anaemia: A rare bone marrow failure syndrome causing pancytopenia including anaemia.
  • Systemic lupus erythematosus (SLE): Autoimmune condition where chronic inflammation can cause anaemia of chronic disease or haemolysis.
  • Hyperthyroidism: Can occasionally cause anaemia through increased erythrocyte turnover.
  • Paroxysmal nocturnal haemoglobinuria (PNH): A rare cause of haemolytic anaemia with haemoglobinuria.
  • Chronic infection (e.g. tuberculosis): Chronic infections can result in anaemia of chronic disease or nutrient malabsorption.
History Taking
Patient’s Description: How can I help you today?
Starting with an open-ended question such as, "Can you tell me more about what brought you here today?" allows the patient to describe their symptoms in their own words. This initial description often reveals valuable information, such as the severity, duration, and impact of symptoms, without directing the patient too early. Relevance: It gives insight into the patient's primary concerns, helping guide subsequent targeted questions. It also builds rapport and helps to direct the flow of the consultation.
When did you first notice your symptoms?
  • Asking about the onset of symptoms helps to differentiate between acute and chronic causes of anaemia. For example, acute onset may suggest gastrointestinal bleeding or haemolysis, whereas a more gradual onset might point to nutritional deficiencies or chronic disease.
  • Relevance: A rapid onset could indicate acute blood loss (e.g., GI bleeding) or haemolytic anaemia. A slower, more insidious onset may suggest iron deficiency anaemia or anaemia of chronic disease.
Have you noticed any fatigue or weakness?
  • Fatigue is one of the most common symptoms of anaemia and is often the reason patients seek medical advice. This question helps to gauge the impact of anaemia on the patient’s daily life and functioning.
  • Relevance: A positive response supports the diagnosis of anaemia, particularly if other systemic symptoms like dizziness or shortness of breath are also present. Fatigue in the context of anaemia could be related to iron deficiency, vitamin B12 deficiency, or anaemia of chronic disease.
Have you had any shortness of breath or chest pain?
  • This question is crucial because shortness of breath, particularly on exertion, is a common symptom of anaemia, as oxygen delivery to tissues is reduced. Chest pain may suggest cardiac involvement, especially in cases of severe anaemia.
  • Relevance: A positive response suggests more severe anaemia, possibly related to acute blood loss, haemolysis, or even underlying cardiac issues. Negative findings may help rule out some life-threatening complications like heart failure secondary to anaemia.
Have you experienced any unusual bruising or bleeding?
  • Unexplained bruising or bleeding may suggest haematological disorders such as leukaemia, multiple myeloma, or thrombocytopenia, which are important to consider when evaluating anaemia.
  • Relevance: A positive response may point towards bone marrow disorders like leukaemia or coagulopathies that could be contributing to the anaemia. A negative response helps to exclude these more severe diagnoses.
Have you had any changes in your diet or weight loss?
  • Dietary changes, particularly a vegetarian or vegan diet, may lead to iron or vitamin B12 deficiencies. Unexplained weight loss could be a red flag for malignancy, gastrointestinal diseases like coeliac disease, or chronic infections such as tuberculosis.
  • Relevance: A positive response suggesting poor nutritional intake could point towards deficiencies, while weight loss could indicate a more serious underlying condition like malignancy or chronic infection.
Have you had any recent infections or illnesses?
  • Recent infections, particularly viral infections like COVID-19, can cause anaemia either through inflammation, chronic illness, or haemolysis.
  • Relevance: A positive history of recent infection, including COVID-19, may indicate anaemia of chronic disease or haemolysis. A negative response helps rule out these causes.
Ideas, Concerns, and Expectations (ICE)
  • Ask, "What do you think might be causing this?" to explore the patient's ideas and understand their perspective on their symptoms.
  • Ask, "Is there something specific you are worried about?" to address the patient’s concerns, which may include fear of a diagnosis like cancer, leukaemia, or a chronic illness.
  • Ask, "What were you hoping we might be able to do for you today?" to align with the patient’s expectations, such as seeking reassurance, diagnosis, or a management plan.
  • Relevance: Understanding the patient’s thoughts helps ensure the consultation addresses their worries (e.g., concerns about serious illnesses like cancer or gastrointestinal bleeding) and their expectations for treatment or reassurance.
Examination Strategy
Inspection of the conjunctiva and sclera
  • Inspecting the conjunctiva for pallor can help detect anaemia. Pale conjunctiva is often seen when haemoglobin levels are low. Additionally, examining the sclera for jaundice can suggest haemolytic anaemia, as bilirubin levels increase due to the breakdown of red blood cells.
  • Relevance: Conjunctival pallor indicates anaemia, particularly when haemoglobin is <9 g/dL. Scleral icterus (jaundice) may point towards haemolysis, such as in haemolytic anaemia, while a negative finding may help rule out these causes.
Palpation of the lymph nodes
  • Palpating for lymphadenopathy is important, as it can be associated with conditions like lymphoma, leukaemia, or infection. Enlarged lymph nodes in the cervical, axillary, or inguinal regions may indicate haematological malignancies or systemic disease contributing to anaemia.
  • Relevance: Positive findings of lymphadenopathy may suggest lymphoma, leukaemia, or chronic infection (e.g., tuberculosis). A negative finding decreases the likelihood of these diagnoses.
Examination of the nails (Koilonychia)
  • Koilonychia (spoon-shaped nails) is a clinical sign of chronic iron deficiency anaemia. It results from changes in the structure of the nail bed due to long-term iron deficiency.
  • Relevance: The presence of koilonychia strongly suggests chronic iron deficiency anaemia, especially if accompanied by other symptoms like fatigue and pallor. A negative finding helps to rule out long-standing iron deficiency as a cause.
Palpation of the abdomen for hepatosplenomegaly
  • Enlargement of the liver or spleen (hepatosplenomegaly) can indicate several conditions that cause anaemia, such as haemolytic anaemias, leukaemia, lymphoma, or infections like malaria. The spleen may become enlarged due to increased breakdown of red blood cells.
  • Relevance: A positive finding of hepatosplenomegaly may suggest haemolysis, malignancy (such as leukaemia or lymphoma), or chronic infection. A negative finding helps rule out these causes, especially haemolytic anaemias.
Inspection of the tongue for glossitis or angular stomatitis
  • Glossitis (inflammation of the tongue) and angular stomatitis (cracks at the corners of the mouth) can be seen in cases of iron deficiency, vitamin B12 deficiency, or folate deficiency anaemias. These signs are related to malnutrition or malabsorption syndromes.
  • Relevance: A positive finding of glossitis or angular stomatitis suggests nutritional anaemias such as iron, folate, or vitamin B12 deficiency. Negative findings may rule out malnutrition-related causes of anaemia.
Cardiovascular examination (tachycardia, murmur)
  • In anaemia, the heart compensates for reduced oxygen-carrying capacity by increasing the heart rate (tachycardia). A flow murmur may also be heard in severe anaemia due to increased blood flow. Additionally, signs of heart failure (e.g., raised jugular venous pressure) can occur in longstanding or severe anaemia.
  • Relevance: Tachycardia and a flow murmur are often present in severe anaemia. Signs of heart failure may indicate a more serious, chronic anaemia. Negative cardiovascular findings make severe anaemia and its complications less likely.
Examination of the skin for pallor, petechiae, or ecchymosis
  • Skin pallor is a classic sign of anaemia. Petechiae (small red or purple spots) or ecchymosis (bruising) may indicate a platelet or coagulation disorder, which can be associated with haematological causes of anaemia such as leukaemia or aplastic anaemia.
  • Relevance: Pallor confirms the suspicion of anaemia, while petechiae or ecchymosis suggest haematological disorders like leukaemia or thrombocytopenia. A negative finding of petechiae or ecchymosis helps to rule out bleeding disorders.
Red Flag Synthesis
Unintentional weight loss
  • Unexplained weight loss can indicate malignancy, particularly gastrointestinal cancers (e.g., colorectal or gastric cancer) or haematological malignancies such as leukaemia and lymphoma. It may also be associated with chronic conditions like tuberculosis.
  • Significance: This could suggest cancer or other serious chronic diseases and warrants further investigation, including imaging and possibly endoscopy.
Haematemesis or melaena
  • Haematemesis (vomiting blood) and melaena (black, tarry stools) are signs of upper gastrointestinal bleeding, which can lead to life-threatening blood loss and acute anaemia. These symptoms are often due to peptic ulcers, gastric cancer, or oesophageal varices.
  • Significance: This suggests gastrointestinal bleeding, which is a medical emergency. Urgent endoscopy and blood transfusion may be required to manage this condition.
Haematuria
  • Haematuria (blood in the urine) may indicate genitourinary malignancies, such as bladder or kidney cancer, or other serious conditions like glomerulonephritis or urinary tract infections. Haematuria with anaemia should raise concern for underlying renal pathology.
  • Significance: This could suggest genitourinary cancer or significant renal disease, both requiring further investigation, including imaging and possibly cystoscopy.
Severe fatigue and dyspnoea at rest
  • Severe fatigue and shortness of breath at rest are concerning for significant anaemia, which may result from acute blood loss, haemolysis, or bone marrow failure. This could indicate an urgent need for transfusion or investigation of underlying causes.
  • Significance: This may signify life-threatening anaemia, potentially requiring immediate blood transfusion and urgent investigation of the underlying cause.
Lymphadenopathy with night sweats and fevers
  • Night sweats, fevers, and lymphadenopathy (enlarged lymph nodes) are concerning for haematological malignancies such as lymphoma or leukaemia. These symptoms may also occur in chronic infections such as tuberculosis.
  • Significance: This could indicate lymphoma, leukaemia, or chronic infection, which requires urgent investigation, including blood tests and imaging, and possibly a biopsy of the lymph nodes.
Bone pain or fractures
  • Bone pain, particularly in the back or ribs, or unexplained fractures may suggest multiple myeloma, a malignancy that affects plasma cells and can cause significant anaemia, hypercalcaemia, and bone lesions.
  • Significance: This could indicate multiple myeloma, a serious condition requiring urgent investigation with blood tests (e.g., serum protein electrophoresis) and imaging (e.g., skeletal survey or MRI).
Neurological symptoms (e.g., paraesthesia, ataxia)
  • Neurological symptoms like paraesthesia (tingling), ataxia (unsteady gait), or cognitive changes may be associated with vitamin B12 deficiency, which can cause subacute combined degeneration of the spinal cord. Early detection and treatment are essential to prevent permanent neurological damage.
  • Significance: This could suggest severe vitamin B12 deficiency or other neurological causes of anaemia, requiring immediate investigation and treatment to prevent irreversible damage.
Investigations Strategy & Interpretation
Full blood count (FBC) and blood film
  • The full blood count is the most crucial initial test in anaemia, providing information about haemoglobin levels, mean corpuscular volume (MCV), and red cell distribution width (RDW). A blood film can also reveal abnormal red blood cell morphology, such as target cells or sickle cells, aiding in the diagnosis.
  • Result Significance: A low haemoglobin level confirms anaemia. Microcytic anaemia (low MCV) suggests iron deficiency or thalassaemia, while macrocytic anaemia (high MCV) points to vitamin B12 or folate deficiency. Normocytic anaemia may indicate anaemia of chronic disease. Abnormalities on the blood film, such as spherocytes or schistocytes, may suggest haemolysis.
Iron studies (serum ferritin, transferrin, and iron levels)
  • Iron studies help assess the body's iron stores and are essential when evaluating microcytic anaemia. Serum ferritin indicates total body iron stores, while transferrin and iron levels help assess iron transport and availability.
  • Result Significance: Low ferritin and iron levels suggest iron deficiency anaemia. A normal or elevated ferritin level in the presence of microcytic anaemia could indicate anaemia of chronic disease. High transferrin saturation might suggest haemochromatosis or ineffective erythropoiesis.
Vitamin B12 and folate levels
  • These tests are essential in the workup of macrocytic anaemia. Vitamin B12 and folate are necessary for DNA synthesis in red blood cells, and deficiencies can lead to megaloblastic anaemia.
  • Result Significance: Low levels of vitamin B12 or folate confirm deficiency anaemia. Normal levels may rule out nutritional causes of macrocytic anaemia. It's also important to consider the possibility of malabsorption, as seen in pernicious anaemia or coeliac disease.
Reticulocyte count
  • Reticulocytes are immature red blood cells. A reticulocyte count helps determine if the bone marrow is responding adequately to anaemia by increasing red blood cell production.
  • Result Significance: A high reticulocyte count suggests active red blood cell production, seen in haemolysis or blood loss. A low reticulocyte count suggests bone marrow suppression, which may occur in conditions like aplastic anaemia or bone marrow infiltration (e.g., leukaemia or myelofibrosis).
Coeliac serology
  • Coeliac disease is a common cause of iron deficiency anaemia due to malabsorption. Testing for tissue transglutaminase antibodies (tTG) or endomysial antibodies is useful in suspected cases of coeliac disease.
  • Result Significance: A positive result confirms coeliac disease as a potential cause of anaemia due to malabsorption. A negative result makes coeliac disease less likely, but further testing may be needed if there is still clinical suspicion.
Liver function tests (LFTs) and lactate dehydrogenase (LDH)
  • Liver function tests and LDH are important in evaluating haemolytic anaemia. Haemolysis leads to the breakdown of red blood cells, resulting in elevated bilirubin and LDH levels, while low haptoglobin may also be noted.
  • Result Significance: Elevated LDH and bilirubin levels, along with reduced haptoglobin, suggest haemolytic anaemia. Normal LFTs and LDH levels may rule out haemolysis as a cause of the anaemia.
Bone marrow biopsy
  • A bone marrow biopsy is indicated when bone marrow failure or infiltration is suspected, such as in leukaemia, lymphoma, myelofibrosis, or myelodysplastic syndromes. It provides direct information about marrow cellularity and any abnormal cells.
  • Result Significance: Abnormal findings, such as blasts in leukaemia or fibrosis in myelofibrosis, confirm bone marrow pathology. A normal bone marrow result would rule out marrow infiltration or primary haematological malignancies.
Stool occult blood test
  • A stool occult blood test can detect hidden gastrointestinal bleeding, a potential cause of iron deficiency anaemia. It is particularly useful in older adults where colon cancer or peptic ulcer disease may be suspected.
  • Result Significance: A positive result suggests gastrointestinal bleeding, warranting further investigation with endoscopy or colonoscopy. A negative result reduces the likelihood of GI bleeding but does not entirely rule it out.
Evidence-Based Diagnosis (EBDx)
Conjunctival Pallor for Detecting Anaemia
  • Conjunctival pallor is commonly assessed in patients with suspected anaemia. It is a quick bedside sign but has varying diagnostic accuracy depending on the severity of the anaemia.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 69%, Specificity: 96%, Positive Likelihood Ratio: 17.4.
  • Reference: Nardone DA, et al. JAMA. 1990;263(19):2752-2755.
  • Significance: A positive finding (pallor) significantly increases the likelihood of moderate to severe anaemia, but a negative result does not entirely rule it out, especially in mild cases.
Low Mean Corpuscular Volume (MCV) in Microcytic Anaemia
  • MCV is an important part of the full blood count that helps differentiate types of anaemia. Low MCV is a key feature of microcytic anaemias, such as iron deficiency or thalassaemia.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 97%, Specificity: 84% for iron deficiency anaemia when MCV <80 fL.
  • Reference: Guyatt GH, et al. J Gen Intern Med. 1992;7(2):145-153.
  • Significance: A low MCV strongly suggests microcytic anaemia, likely due to iron deficiency or thalassaemia. Normal or high MCV would point to other causes like normocytic or macrocytic anaemia.
Low Serum Ferritin for Diagnosing Iron Deficiency Anaemia
  • Serum ferritin is the best initial test for assessing iron stores. A low ferritin level is diagnostic of iron deficiency, even in the absence of anaemia.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 92%, Specificity: 98% for iron deficiency anaemia when ferritin is <15 µg/L.
  • Reference: Worwood M, et al. Br J Haematol. 1980;45(3):451-460.
  • Significance: A low ferritin confirms iron deficiency as the cause of anaemia, while a normal or high ferritin level, especially in the presence of inflammation, points towards other causes such as anaemia of chronic disease.
Elevated Reticulocyte Count in Haemolytic Anaemia
  • An elevated reticulocyte count suggests increased red blood cell production, typically seen in haemolytic anaemias where the bone marrow responds to the destruction of red blood cells.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 85%, Specificity: 90% for haemolytic anaemia with an elevated reticulocyte count.
  • Reference: Kaushansky K, et al. Williams Hematology. 9th Edition.
  • Significance: A raised reticulocyte count supports a diagnosis of haemolysis, suggesting conditions like autoimmune haemolytic anaemia or hereditary spherocytosis. A low reticulocyte count suggests impaired bone marrow function, pointing to conditions like aplastic anaemia or myelodysplasia.
Positive Stool Occult Blood Test for Gastrointestinal Bleeding
  • A stool occult blood test can detect gastrointestinal bleeding, which may be a source of chronic blood loss leading to iron deficiency anaemia. It is especially important in older patients where malignancy must be considered.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 50-80%, Specificity: 90-98% for detecting gastrointestinal bleeding.
  • Reference: Allison JE, et al. N Engl J Med. 1996;334(3):155-159.
  • Significance: A positive result increases the likelihood of gastrointestinal bleeding, indicating a need for further investigation such as endoscopy. A negative result does not completely rule out GI bleeding but reduces its likelihood.
Bone Marrow Biopsy for Diagnosing Bone Marrow Disorders
  • A bone marrow biopsy is the definitive investigation for diagnosing bone marrow disorders such as leukaemia, myelodysplasia, and aplastic anaemia. It provides direct insight into the structure and cellularity of the bone marrow.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 95%, Specificity: 98% for diagnosing haematological malignancies.
  • Reference: Bain BJ, et al. Br J Haematol. 2001;114(5):746-758.
  • Significance: A positive biopsy indicating bone marrow pathology confirms a diagnosis of conditions like leukaemia, multiple myeloma, or myelodysplasia. A negative result helps rule out bone marrow infiltration or primary haematological malignancies.
Diagnostic Timeout & Cognitive Biases

Awareness of common thinking errors is essential to improve diagnostic accuracy and patient safety when assessing a patient with anaemia. Before finalising a diagnosis, it is good practice for clinicians at all levels to take a 'diagnostic timeout' to challenge their own reasoning.

  • Anchoring Bias: The tendency to lock onto the most common diagnosis too early. For example, assuming any young woman with fatigue and anaemia has iron deficiency from menorrhagia, potentially overlooking coeliac disease, even if there are subtle GI symptoms. A key question to ask is: "Could this be something other than simple iron deficiency?"
  • Confirmation Bias: Actively seeking out or overvaluing evidence that supports an initial belief. For instance, focusing on a low MCV to confirm iron deficiency while downplaying a family history of anaemia that might point towards thalassaemia trait.
  • Atypical Presentations: A major pitfall is forgetting that diseases can present non-classically. For example, coeliac disease can present with isolated iron deficiency anaemia without any significant gastrointestinal symptoms. Similarly, B12 deficiency can present with predominantly neurological symptoms before any significant macrocytic anaemia develops.
  • Over-reliance on Normal Tests: Placing too much weight on a single reassuring test result. A normal ferritin level does not rule out iron deficiency in the context of co-existing inflammation (as ferritin is an acute-phase reactant). In such cases, further iron studies like transferrin saturation are required.
Illustrative Management Pathways

For educational purposes, this section describes the typical principles of management for key diagnoses. These are not prescriptive instructions. All management must be guided by official UK guidelines, local formularies, and senior clinical advice.

Iron Deficiency Anaemia (IDA)

The core principle is twofold: replace the missing iron and identify and treat the underlying cause of the deficiency. Initial management involves prescribing oral iron supplements (e.g., ferrous sulphate or fumarate). It is crucial to investigate the cause, especially in men and post-menopausal women, where gastrointestinal blood loss (e.g., from peptic ulcers or colorectal cancer) must be excluded, often requiring endoscopy and/or colonoscopy. In cases of malabsorption (e.g., coeliac disease) or intolerance to oral iron, intravenous iron infusion may be required.

Vitamin B12 / Folate Deficiency

Management involves replacing the deficient vitamin. For B12 deficiency, this typically requires a course of intramuscular hydroxycobalamin injections, followed by maintenance doses every 2-3 months, particularly for pernicious anaemia. It is critical to check folate levels and treat any concurrent deficiency, but never treat with folic acid alone if B12 deficiency is suspected, as this can precipitate irreversible neurological damage (subacute combined degeneration of the cord). Folic acid deficiency is managed with daily oral folic acid.

Anaemia of Chronic Disease (ACD)

The primary management goal is to treat the underlying inflammatory, infectious, or malignant condition. The anaemia itself is often mild to moderate and may not require specific treatment. Iron studies are important to rule out a co-existing true iron deficiency. In some cases, particularly in chronic kidney disease or chemotherapy-induced anaemia, erythropoiesis-stimulating agents (ESAs) like erythropoietin may be considered by a specialist. Blood transfusions are reserved for patients with severe, symptomatic anaemia.

Acute Blood Loss

This is a medical emergency requiring immediate resuscitation. The priority is to achieve haemodynamic stability with intravenous fluids and, where necessary, blood transfusion according to local protocols (often using a restrictive transfusion threshold). Simultaneously, urgent efforts must be made to identify and stop the source of bleeding, which may involve emergency endoscopy, interventional radiology, or surgery.

Reflection & Learning

These brief cases illustrate the application of clinical reasoning to different anaemia presentations. They are for educational purposes only.

Case 1: Suspected Iron Deficiency Anaemia in Primary Care

A 32-year-old woman presents to her GP complaining of persistent fatigue and feeling breathless when climbing stairs. She reports heavy menstrual periods. Her FBC shows a microcytic anaemia (Hb 98 g/L, MCV 74 fL) and blood film confirms microcytic, hypochromic cells. The problem representation is a young woman with a clear risk factor for iron loss presenting with symptoms and blood results classic for iron deficiency anaemia. The GP starts oral iron, provides dietary advice, and considers gynaecological referral to manage the heavy periods.

Case 2: Suspected B12 Deficiency in an Older Adult

A 78-year-old man is brought in by his family who are concerned about his increasing confusion and unsteadiness over the past few months. He also complains of "pins and needles" in his feet. An FBC reveals a macrocytic anaemia (Hb 105 g/L, MCV 112 fL). The problem representation is an elderly patient with a combination of neurological symptoms and macrocytic anaemia, highly suspicious for Vitamin B12 deficiency causing subacute combined degeneration of the cord. Urgent blood tests for B12, folate and intrinsic factor antibodies are sent, and a loading course of intramuscular B12 is started immediately pending results.

Case 3: Anaemia of Chronic Disease in a Hospital Inpatient

A 65-year-old woman with a long history of severe rheumatoid arthritis is admitted with a flare of her condition. Routine bloods show a normocytic anaemia (Hb 101 g/L, MCV 88 fL), which is similar to previous results. Her inflammatory markers (CRP, ESR) are markedly elevated. Iron studies show a normal ferritin and low total iron binding capacity. The problem representation is a patient with a known, active inflammatory condition and a mild, stable normocytic anaemia with an iron profile consistent with inflammation. The diagnosis is anaemia of chronic disease, and management is focused on controlling the arthritis flare.
