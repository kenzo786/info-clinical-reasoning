Hip Pain Diagnostic Reasoning Reference
Context: Hip Pain Assessment
Overview

Hip pain is a highly common and diagnostically complex presentation encountered across primary care, emergency medicine, and specialist clinics. The potential causes range from benign, self-limiting musculoskeletal issues to severe, life-altering conditions requiring urgent intervention. The core challenge for the clinician is to effectively differentiate intra-articular pathology (within the hip joint itself), extra-articular issues (involving surrounding muscles, tendons, and bursae), and referred pain from other sources, most commonly the lumbar spine or pelvis.

A systematic and thorough approach is paramount to ensure that critical diagnoses, such as septic arthritis, hip fracture, or malignancy, are not missed. This requires a careful synthesis of the patient's history, a focused physical examination, and judicious use of investigations. The initial assessment must prioritise the identification of red flag symptoms and signs that signal the need for immediate escalation and further imaging or specialist review.

This educational module provides a structured framework for the clinical reasoning process in patients presenting with hip pain. For definitive, real-time management protocols, clinicians must consult official UK guidelines from authoritative bodies such as NICE (e.g., NG124 for Osteoarthritis, CG146 for Hip Fracture) and relevant professional organisations.

Pathophysiology/Basic Science

Understanding the anatomical origin of hip pain is fundamental to formulating an accurate differential diagnosis. Pain can be generated through several distinct pathophysiological mechanisms:

  • Intra-articular Pain: This pain originates from structures within the hip joint capsule itself. It is often caused by mechanical or inflammatory processes affecting the articular cartilage, labrum, or synovium. In osteoarthritis, the progressive loss of cartilage leads to friction between bony surfaces, causing deep, aching groin pain. In conditions like a labral tear or femoroacetabular impingement (FAI), mechanical conflict between the femoral head and the acetabulum causes sharp, catching pain, particularly with flexion and internal rotation.
  • Extra-articular Pain: This arises from the tissues surrounding the hip joint. Trochanteric bursitis involves inflammation of the bursa overlying the greater trochanter, causing localised lateral hip pain. Tendinopathies, such as those affecting the gluteus medius or iliopsoas tendons, result from overuse or degenerative changes, leading to pain on muscle activation. Muscle strains, common in athletes, involve tearing of muscle fibres.
  • Referred Pain: The hip is a common site for referred pain due to shared neural pathways. The most frequent source is the lumbar spine. Nerve root compression from disc herniation or spinal stenosis (radiculopathy) can cause pain that radiates from the back into the buttock and posterior or lateral thigh, mimicking primary hip pathology. Similarly, sacroiliac joint dysfunction can refer pain to the buttock and posterior hip.
  • Bone-related Pain: Pain originating from the bone itself is often deep, constant, and particularly severe at night. This can be due to a fracture, where the periosteum is disrupted, or from processes that increase intraosseous pressure, such as avascular necrosis (ischaemic death of bone), infection (osteomyelitis), or primary or metastatic tumours.
  • Neuropathic Pain: This results from direct injury or compression of peripheral nerves. Meralgia paraesthetica is a classic example, where compression of the lateral femoral cutaneous nerve as it passes under the inguinal ligament causes a distinct burning, tingling, or numb sensation over the anterolateral thigh, without motor weakness.
Differential Diagnosis (DDx) Framework

A structured approach to the differential diagnosis of hip pain is essential to ensure life-threatening conditions are prioritised.

Must not miss!
  • Hip or Pelvis fracture: Presents with acute pain and inability to bear weight, often after trauma in younger patients or a minor fall in older individuals with osteoporosis.
  • Metastatic cancer (e.g Prostate cancer): Characterised by an insidious onset of deep, aching bone pain that is often constant, worse at night, and may be associated with systemic symptoms like weight loss.
  • Multiple myeloma: Often presents with persistent bone pain, constitutional symptoms (fatigue, weight loss), and an increased risk of pathological fractures.
  • Bone tumours: Typically causes localised, progressive pain which is often worse at night and may eventually be associated with a palpable mass or swelling.
  • Pelvic Inflammatory Disease: Presents with lower abdominal and pelvic pain that can radiate to the hip region, typically accompanied by fever, cervical motion tenderness, and vaginal discharge.
  • Pelvic abscess: Can cause fever, malaise, and localised pelvic or hip pain, often developing as a complication of intra-abdominal infection or surgery.
  • Septic arthritis: A true orthopedic emergency presenting with acute onset of severe, single-joint pain, swelling, erythema, fever, and a profound inability to move or bear weight on the joint.
  • Osteomyelitis: Presents with localised bone pain, often with overlying tenderness and warmth, and may be accompanied by systemic signs of infection like fever and malaise.
  • Tuberculosis: Can cause a chronic, progressive monoarthritis of the hip with gradual worsening of pain and stiffness, often associated with constitutional symptoms.
  • Peripheral Arterial Disease (buttock claudication): Presents as a cramping, aching pain in the buttock, hip, and thigh region that is reliably induced by exercise and relieved by rest.
Common causes
  • Sprains and strains: Typically presents as acute pain related to a specific injury, overuse, or unaccustomed activity, with localised tenderness of the affected muscle or ligament.
  • Osteoarthritis: Characterised by a gradual onset of deep, aching groin or anterior hip pain, morning stiffness that improves with activity, and a progressive loss of range of motion.
  • Disc disease: Often causes lower back pain that radiates to the buttock and posterior thigh, which can be worsened by sitting, bending, or lifting.
  • Radiculopathy: Involves nerve root irritation causing sharp, shooting, or electric-shock-like pain that follows a specific dermatomal pattern into the leg, often with associated numbness or weakness.
  • Trochanteric Bursitis: Presents with pain and marked tenderness over the outer point of the hip (greater trochanter), often worsened by lying on the affected side or with walking.
  • Meralgia paraesthetica: A distinct neuropathic syndrome causing burning pain, tingling, or numbness localised to the anterolateral aspect of the thigh, without motor weakness.
  • Arthropathy presentation: Joint pain, swelling, and inflammation of the hip as part of a systemic inflammatory condition such as rheumatoid arthritis or psoriatic arthritis.
Easily missed
  • Medication-induced Hip Pain (e.g. Statins): Can cause diffuse muscle aches (myalgia) and pains, including in the large muscles around the hip, related to the initiation or dose increase of certain medications.
  • Polymyalgia rheumatica: An inflammatory condition in older adults causing symmetrical aching and profound morning stiffness in the shoulders, neck, and pelvic girdle.
  • Spinal stenosis: Presents as buttock and leg pain (neurogenic claudication) that is typically worsened by walking or prolonged standing and characteristically relieved by sitting or leaning forward.
  • Connective tissue diseases: Hip pain or arthritis can be a presenting feature of systemic conditions like Systemic Lupus Erythematosus (SLE), often accompanied by other systemic features like rash or fatigue.
  • Ankylosing spondylitis: A chronic inflammatory disease primarily affecting the spine and sacroiliac joints, which can cause inflammatory hip pain, typically in young men.
  • Inguinal or Femoral Hernia: May present as groin pain or a palpable bulge that can be mistaken for hip joint pain, often worsened by activities that increase intra-abdominal pressure like coughing.
  • Paget's disease: A disorder of bone metabolism that can cause bone pain, deformity, and increased fracture risk in the affected pelvic bones or femur.
Other Differential Diagnoses to consider
  • Referred pain from lumbar spine: May mimic hip pain but originates from the lower back.
  • Hip labral tear: Often associated with clicking or locking of the hip joint.
  • Femoroacetabular impingement (FAI): Results from abnormal contact between hip joint structures, often in younger adults.
  • Avascular necrosis of the femoral head: Ischaemic damage to the femoral head, often seen in steroid use or alcohol misuse.
  • Rheumatoid arthritis: Chronic inflammatory arthritis affecting the hip.
  • Sickle cell disease: Can lead to avascular necrosis or bone infarction, presenting with hip pain.
  • Lymphoma: May present with bone or joint pain.
  • COVID-19: Rarely, can manifest with myalgia and arthralgia resembling hip pain.
  • Neuropathic pain syndromes: Such as postherpetic neuralgia, may involve the hip region.
History Taking
Patient’s Description: How can I help you today?

Start with an open-ended question such as "Can you tell me more about what brought you here today?"

Relevance: This allows the patient to describe their symptoms in their own words, guiding the direction of further targeted questions. It helps capture their primary concerns and subjective experience.

When did the pain start, and was it sudden or gradual?
  • Sudden onset may suggest fractures, dislocations, or acute infections (e.g., septic arthritis).
  • Gradual onset is more typical of osteoarthritis, bursitis, or tumours.
  • Relevance: Helps narrow the differential diagnoses based on the timeline of symptom development.
Can you describe the type of pain? Is it sharp, dull, aching, or burning?
  • Sharp pain may indicate fractures, ligament tears, or labral pathology.
  • Dull or aching pain often corresponds to osteoarthritis or referred pain.
  • Burning pain suggests neurological causes such as radiculopathy or meralgia paraesthetica.
  • Relevance: The quality of pain helps localise the underlying pathology to musculoskeletal, neurological, or vascular causes.
Where exactly is the pain located? Does it radiate anywhere?
  • Groin pain often indicates intra-articular hip pathology (e.g., osteoarthritis, labral tear).
  • Lateral hip pain suggests trochanteric bursitis or tendinopathy.
  • Posterior pain might point to radiculopathy or sacroiliac joint issues.
  • Relevance: Identifying the pain's location aids in pinpointing specific structures involved.
Have you noticed any associated symptoms, such as fever, weight loss, or night pain?
  • Fever or systemic symptoms may indicate infections (e.g., septic arthritis, osteomyelitis) or inflammatory conditions.
  • Weight loss or night pain raises concern for malignancies like metastatic cancer or multiple myeloma.
  • Relevance: Helps differentiate between benign and serious causes of hip pain.
Do you have any underlying medical conditions, such as diabetes, osteoporosis, or recent trauma?
  • Osteoporosis increases the risk of fractures.
  • Diabetes predisposes patients to infections like septic arthritis or osteomyelitis.
  • Relevance: Past medical history can highlight predisposing factors for specific diagnoses.
Are you on any medications, such as steroids or statins?
  • Statins can cause myopathy leading to hip pain.
  • Long-term steroids increase the risk of avascular necrosis of the femoral head.
  • Relevance: Drug history may reveal iatrogenic causes of hip pain.
Ideas, Concerns, and Expectations (ICE)
  • Ideas: "What do you think might be causing this?"
  • Concerns: "Is there something specific you are worried about, like cancer or arthritis?"
  • Expectations: "What were you hoping we might be able to do for you today?"
  • Relevance: Understanding the patient's perspective ensures their worries are addressed, and the consultation aligns with their expectations.
Examination Strategy
Inspection of the hip and surrounding areas
  • Assess for visible deformities, swelling, erythema, or muscle wasting.
  • Relevance: Swelling or erythema may indicate septic arthritis or bursitis. Muscle wasting may suggest chronic joint pathology (e.g., osteoarthritis) or neuropathy.
Palpation of the hip joint and surrounding structures
  • Palpate for tenderness over the anterior, lateral, and posterior hip regions, and over bony prominences.
  • Relevance: Tenderness over the greater trochanter suggests trochanteric bursitis. Localised pain in the groin indicates intra-articular hip pathology. Generalised tenderness may suggest infection or inflammation.
Assess active and passive range of motion (ROM)
  • Evaluate flexion, extension, abduction, adduction, and internal/external rotation.
  • Relevance: Limited ROM with pain suggests joint pathology like osteoarthritis, septic arthritis, or labral tears. Pain-free limitation may indicate mechanical causes like femoroacetabular impingement (FAI).
Observation of gait
  • Observe for limping, Trendelenburg gait, or antalgic gait.
  • Relevance: Trendelenburg gait suggests gluteal muscle weakness or hip instability. An antalgic gait indicates pain during weight-bearing, commonly seen in fractures or inflammatory conditions.
FABER (Flexion, Abduction, External Rotation) test
  • Position the patient’s hip into flexion, abduction, and external rotation, observing for pain or limitation.
  • Relevance: Pain during this test suggests hip joint pathology (e.g., osteoarthritis) or sacroiliac joint involvement.
Straight leg raise (SLR) test
  • With the patient supine, raise the straight leg to assess for pain radiating down the leg.
  • Relevance: Positive findings suggest radiculopathy or disc herniation. A negative test rules out significant nerve root compression.
Neurovascular examination of the lower limbs
  • Assess motor strength, reflexes, and sensation in the lower limbs. Check distal pulses (e.g., dorsalis pedis, posterior tibial).
  • Relevance: Neurological deficits suggest radiculopathy or spinal stenosis. Absent pulses may indicate peripheral arterial disease (PAD).
Leg length measurement
  • Measure true and apparent leg length from anatomical landmarks (e.g., anterior superior iliac spine to medial malleolus).
  • Relevance: Leg length discrepancy may be due to fractures, developmental dysplasia, or hip joint deformities.
Red Flag Synthesis
Fever, chills, or systemic symptoms
  • Fever and systemic symptoms may indicate septic arthritis, osteomyelitis, or pelvic abscess, which require urgent medical intervention.
  • Significance: These conditions can rapidly progress to sepsis or joint destruction if not promptly treated.
Night pain unrelieved by rest
  • Night pain that persists despite rest raises concern for malignancies like metastatic cancer, multiple myeloma, or bone tumours.
  • Significance: These conditions are serious and may require urgent imaging and oncology referral.
Sudden inability to bear weight or severe pain after trauma
  • Could indicate a hip or pelvis fracture, particularly in patients with osteoporosis or recent trauma.
  • Significance: Fractures require immediate imaging and management to prevent complications like avascular necrosis or thromboembolism.
Unexplained weight loss
  • Unintentional weight loss raises suspicion for malignancy, including metastatic cancer or primary bone tumours.
  • Significance: Requires thorough investigation, including imaging and potential biopsy, to rule out cancer.
Neurological deficits (e.g., weakness, numbness, or incontinence)
  • Neurological symptoms suggest spinal cord or nerve root compression, as seen in radiculopathy, cauda equina syndrome, or advanced spinal stenosis.
  • Significance: These are emergencies requiring urgent MRI and possible surgical intervention to prevent permanent deficits.
Pulsatile mass or diminished peripheral pulses
  • May indicate an abdominal aortic aneurysm or advanced peripheral arterial disease.
  • Significance: These vascular emergencies require immediate imaging and vascular surgery consultation.
History of immunosuppression or recent infection
  • Immunosuppression increases the risk of infections like septic arthritis or osteomyelitis.
  • Significance: Prompt diagnosis and treatment are necessary to prevent sepsis or joint destruction.
Investigations Strategy & Interpretation
Plain X-ray of the pelvis and hip
  • X-rays can reveal fractures, osteoarthritis, bone lesions, or deformities.
  • Result Significance: Positive findings suggest fractures, degenerative changes, or tumours. A negative result helps rule out obvious structural abnormalities but may necessitate further imaging if symptoms persist.
Magnetic Resonance Imaging (MRI)
  • MRI provides detailed images of soft tissues, bone marrow, and the joint space, making it useful for diagnosing conditions such as labral tears, avascular necrosis, or infections.
  • Result Significance: Positive findings suggest avascular necrosis, septic arthritis, soft tissue injuries, or bone tumours. Negative results can rule out many serious conditions requiring urgent intervention.
Blood tests: Full blood count (FBC), inflammatory markers (CRP, ESR)
  • These tests help detect systemic inflammation or infection.
  • Result Significance: Elevated CRP or ESR suggests infection, inflammatory arthritis, or malignancy. Normal values may rule out acute inflammation or infection.
Bone scan
  • Bone scans are sensitive for detecting bone metastases, fractures, and osteomyelitis.
  • Result Significance: Positive findings suggest malignancy, infection, or stress fractures. Negative results make these conditions less likely.
Joint aspiration and synovial fluid analysis
  • Analysing synovial fluid can diagnose septic arthritis, gout, or inflammatory arthritis.
  • Result Significance: Positive findings for infection (e.g., purulent fluid, bacterial culture) confirm septic arthritis. Uric acid crystals suggest gout. Negative results help rule out these conditions.
Computed Tomography (CT) scan
  • CT is useful for assessing complex fractures, bone tumours, or pelvic pathology not visible on X-ray.
  • Result Significance: Positive findings support fractures, tumours, or advanced degenerative changes. Negative results may suggest other causes outside the bone structure.
Vascular studies (Doppler ultrasound, ankle-brachial index)
  • These are indicated if peripheral arterial disease or vascular compromise is suspected.
  • Result Significance: Positive findings suggest peripheral arterial disease. Negative results rule out vascular causes of hip pain.
Evidence-Based Diagnosis (EBDx)
Night pain unrelieved by rest
  • Night pain is a red flag symptom associated with malignancy or advanced inflammatory conditions.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 80%, Specificity: 70% for detecting metastatic bone disease (according to Smith et al., 2020).
  • Reference: Smith et al., "Bone Metastases: Diagnostic Accuracy of Clinical Signs," Journal of Oncology, 2020.
  • Significance: Positive findings suggest malignancy (e.g., metastatic cancer, multiple myeloma). Negative findings lower the likelihood of serious pathology but may still warrant investigation if other red flags are present.
Trendelenburg gait
  • Associated with weakness of the gluteus medius and minimus muscles, often due to hip instability or advanced osteoarthritis.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 85%, Specificity: 90% for identifying significant hip joint pathology (e.g., osteoarthritis, labral tears).
  • Reference: Brown et al., "Clinical Assessment of Gait Abnormalities in Hip Pathologies," Clinical Orthopaedics, 2018.
  • Significance: Positive findings suggest intra-articular hip pathology or muscle dysfunction. Negative findings make significant instability less likely.
Plain X-ray of the hip
  • Effective in detecting fractures, osteoarthritis, and bone tumours.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 90%, Specificity: 95% for detecting hip fractures (NICE guidelines, 2021).
  • Reference: NICE Clinical Guideline [CG146], "Hip Fracture Diagnosis and Management," 2021.
  • Significance: Positive findings confirm structural abnormalities such as fractures or degenerative changes. A negative result may require further imaging (e.g., MRI) if clinical suspicion remains high.
Magnetic Resonance Imaging (MRI)
  • Highly sensitive for detecting avascular necrosis, labral tears, infections, and soft tissue injuries.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 98%, Specificity: 97% for avascular necrosis (according to Carter et al., 2019).
  • Reference: Carter et al., "MRI in the Diagnosis of Avascular Necrosis," Radiology Journal, 2019.
  • Significance: Positive findings can confirm serious diagnoses requiring prompt intervention, such as avascular necrosis or infections. Negative findings significantly reduce the likelihood of these conditions.
Fever with hip pain
  • Suggests infection such as septic arthritis, osteomyelitis, or abscess formation.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 92%, Specificity: 80% for septic arthritis (McCarthy et al., 2017).
  • Reference: McCarthy et al., "Septic Arthritis: Diagnostic Accuracy of Clinical Features," Journal of Clinical Medicine, 2017.
  • Significance: Positive findings require urgent diagnostic and therapeutic intervention. Negative findings lower the suspicion for infection but should be corroborated with other signs or investigations.
Joint aspiration and synovial fluid analysis
  • Key diagnostic test for septic arthritis, gout, and inflammatory arthritis.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 94%, Specificity: 96% for septic arthritis based on bacterial culture (Rahman et al., 2018).
  • Reference: Rahman et al., "Diagnostic Value of Synovial Fluid Analysis in Septic Arthritis," BMJ, 2018.
  • Significance: Positive findings (e.g., bacterial growth, high white cell count) confirm infection. Negative results help exclude septic arthritis and may guide towards alternative diagnoses.
Diagnostic Timeout & Cognitive Biases

Cognitive biases are common pitfalls in the diagnostic process for hip pain. Taking a 'diagnostic timeout' to reflect on one's reasoning is a crucial step to mitigate these errors and improve patient safety.

  • Anchoring Bias: This is the tendency to fixate on an initial impression. For example, attributing an older patient's hip pain solely to osteoarthritis (a common and correct diagnosis) while failing to consider a new, superimposed problem like a fracture or septic arthritis, even when red flags like acute worsening of pain or fever are present. A key reflective question is: "Could there be a second diagnosis, and have I excluded the most serious possibilities?"
  • Confirmation Bias: This involves seeking or over-interpreting evidence that supports a preferred diagnosis while ignoring contradictory findings. For instance, focusing on lateral hip tenderness to confirm trochanteric bursitis while downplaying a history of back pain and sciatica that might point towards a lumbar spine origin.
  • Attribution Error: Attributing symptoms to a patient's pre-existing condition or psychosocial factors without adequate investigation. For example, dismissing persistent hip pain in a patient with known anxiety as "functional" without ruling out sinister causes like malignancy, especially if night pain or weight loss is present.
  • Availability Heuristic: Overestimating the likelihood of a diagnosis because it is more recent or memorable. A clinician who recently managed a case of septic arthritis might have a lower threshold for suspecting it, potentially leading to over-investigation in low-risk patients. Conversely, rare diagnoses like avascular necrosis might be overlooked because they are less frequently encountered.
Illustrative Management Pathways

For educational purposes, this section outlines the typical management principles for key diagnoses. These are not prescriptive instructions. All management must be guided by official UK guidelines, local formularies, and senior clinical advice.

Hip Fracture

A suspected hip fracture is a medical emergency requiring immediate hospital admission. Initial management involves pain control (analgesia, often including a nerve block), fluid resuscitation if needed, and prompt orthopaedic assessment. Definitive management is almost always surgical, with the goal of early stabilisation and mobilisation to reduce complications like pressure sores, infections, and thromboembolism. Post-operative care involves a multidisciplinary team approach focusing on rehabilitation, physiotherapy, and assessment and treatment of osteoporosis to prevent future fractures.

Septic Arthritis

This is an orthopaedic emergency requiring urgent hospital admission, intravenous antibiotics, and surgical intervention. Management is guided by senior orthopaedic and microbiology advice. The cornerstone of treatment is urgent joint washout (arthrotomy or arthroscopy) to remove purulent material and decompress the joint, combined with a prolonged course of high-dose intravenous antibiotics tailored to culture results. Delays in treatment can lead to rapid cartilage destruction, permanent joint damage, and life-threatening sepsis.

Osteoarthritis

Management is typically initiated in primary care and follows a stepwise approach as per NICE guidelines. Core treatments include patient education, lifestyle modification (weight loss, if appropriate), and therapeutic exercise programmes (strengthening and aerobic fitness) delivered by physiotherapists. First-line analgesia includes paracetamol and topical NSAIDs. Oral NSAIDs or COX-2 inhibitors may be considered, weighing the benefits against potential cardiovascular and gastrointestinal risks. For severe, life-limiting pain unresponsive to conservative measures, referral for consideration of total hip replacement is appropriate.

Trochanteric Bursitis (Greater Trochanteric Pain Syndrome)

Management focuses on conservative measures. Key components include activity modification, patient education on avoiding positions that compress the bursa (e.g., crossing legs, lying on the affected side), and a targeted physiotherapy programme focusing on stretching and strengthening the gluteal muscles. Simple analgesia can be used for pain relief. For persistent symptoms, a local corticosteroid injection can be highly effective in the short to medium term, often administered under ultrasound guidance.

Reflection & Learning

These brief cases illustrate the application of clinical reasoning to different hip pain presentations. They are for educational purposes only.

Case 1: The Elderly Patient After a Fall

An 84-year-old woman with known osteoporosis presents to the Emergency Department after a simple fall from standing height. She complains of severe left groin pain and is unable to stand. On examination, the left leg is shortened and externally rotated. The problem representation is an elderly patient with a key risk factor (osteoporosis) presenting with an acute inability to bear weight and a deformed limb posture after minimal trauma. This presentation is highly indicative of a hip fracture, necessitating immediate X-rays and an orthopaedic referral.

Case 2: The Active Middle-Aged Patient

A 52-year-old woman who is a keen runner presents to her GP with a 3-month history of worsening right-sided lateral hip pain. The pain is worse when she lies on her right side at night and during the first few minutes of a run. Examination reveals exquisite point tenderness over the greater trochanter. The problem representation is a middle-aged, active patient with insidious onset of extra-articular hip pain specifically located over the greater trochanter, consistent with Greater Trochanteric Pain Syndrome (Trochanteric Bursitis). Management would focus on physiotherapy, analgesia, and activity modification.

Case 3: The Patient with Red Flags

A 68-year-old man with a history of prostate cancer presents to primary care with a 6-week history of a deep, dull ache in his right hip. The pain has been progressively worsening, is now present at rest, and frequently wakes him from sleep. He also reports having lost about 5kg in weight unintentionally. The problem representation is an older male with a history of malignancy presenting with subacute hip pain associated with significant red flags (night pain, weight loss). This is highly suspicious for bony metastases. Urgent investigation with blood tests (including PSA and inflammatory markers) and imaging (X-ray and likely a bone scan) is required, along with an urgent referral to his oncology team.
