Axillary Swelling Diagnostic Reasoning Reference
Context: Axillary Swelling Assessment
Overview

Axillary swelling is a common clinical presentation that requires careful evaluation in both primary and secondary care. The differential diagnosis is broad, ranging from benign and self-limiting conditions, such as reactive lymph nodes or simple cysts, to serious underlying pathology, including metastatic cancer and lymphoma. The primary clinical challenge is to accurately distinguish between these possibilities in a timely and safe manner.

A systematic approach, grounded in a thorough history and physical examination, is essential to identify red flag features that suggest malignancy or significant systemic disease. This allows the clinician to stratify risk and direct investigations appropriately, ensuring that urgent conditions are managed promptly while avoiding unnecessary anxiety and testing for benign causes. The initial assessment must focus on characterising the lump and searching for associated local or systemic symptoms.

This educational module is designed to provide a foundational understanding of the clinical reasoning process for a patient presenting with axillary swelling. For definitive, real-time management protocols, users must refer to official UK guidelines from authoritative bodies such as NICE, particularly the 'Suspected cancer: recognition and referral' guideline (NG12).

Pathophysiology/Basic Science

Understanding the underlying pathophysiology of axillary swelling is key to forming an accurate differential diagnosis. The causes can be grouped into several distinct mechanisms:

  • Reactive Lymphadenopathy: This is the most common cause of axillary lymph node enlargement. The lymphatic system is a critical component of the body's immune response. When infection or inflammation occurs in the areas drained by the axillary nodes (the arm, chest wall, and breast), lymphocytes and macrophages within the nodes proliferate in response to antigens. This cellular proliferation and increased blood flow cause the nodes to enlarge, often becoming tender. This is a normal physiological response to challenges like local skin infections (cellulitis), cat scratch disease, or systemic viral illnesses like glandular fever.
  • Infective/Abscess Formation: Direct bacterial infection of structures within the axilla can lead to swelling. This can occur in a hair follicle or sweat gland (folliculitis, leading to a boil or furuncle) or within a lymph node itself (lymphadenitis). If the infection is not controlled, central necrosis can occur, leading to the formation of a pus-filled cavity known as an abscess. Conditions like Hidradenitis Suppurativa involve a chronic, recurrent inflammatory process of the apocrine sweat glands, leading to painful nodules and abscesses.
  • Neoplastic Infiltration: The axillary lymph nodes can become enlarged due to cancer. This occurs via two main pathways. Metastatic cancer involves cancer cells breaking away from a primary tumour (most commonly breast cancer, but also melanoma, lung, or gastrointestinal cancers) and travelling through the lymphatic vessels to the axillary nodes, where they establish a secondary tumour. Primary haematological malignancy, such as lymphoma (e.g., Hodgkin's or Non-Hodgkin's Lymphoma) or leukaemia, originates from the cells of the lymphatic or blood system itself, causing nodes to become enlarged with malignant lymphocytes. Malignant nodes are typically hard, non-tender, and may become matted together and fixed to underlying tissues as the cancer grows.
  • Benign Growths: Non-cancerous growths can also present as axillary lumps. A lipoma is a common, benign tumour of mature fat cells (adipocytes), which typically presents as a soft, mobile, non-tender, and slow-growing subcutaneous mass. A sebaceous cyst (epidermoid or pilar cyst) is a sac-like lump under the skin that forms when a hair follicle or skin gland becomes blocked. It is filled with a cheesy, malodorous substance called keratin. While benign, these can become inflamed or infected, causing pain and redness.
Differential Diagnosis (DDx) Framework

A structured approach to the differential diagnosis of axillary swelling is essential to ensure life-threatening conditions are prioritised.

Must not miss!
  • Metastatic cancer: The spread of cancer cells from a primary site (e.g., breast, lung) to the axillary lymph nodes.
  • Breast cancer: Can present with an axillary lump as its first sign, due to metastasis to regional lymph nodes.
  • Lung cancer: An uncommon but possible cause of axillary lymphadenopathy due to metastatic spread.
  • Lymphoma: A cancer of the lymphatic system, which can manifest as firm, rubbery, non-tender lymph nodes in the axilla.
  • Leukaemia: A cancer of blood-forming tissues, which can cause generalised lymphadenopathy.
  • Tuberculosis lymphadenopathy: A form of extrapulmonary TB where axillary lymph nodes become enlarged and may form a cold abscess.
  • HIV: Can cause persistent generalised lymphadenopathy, including in the axilla, as a feature of the infection.
Common causes
  • Lymphadenopathy: Benign enlargement of lymph nodes, often due to a reactive response to local or systemic infection or inflammation.
  • Sebaceous cyst: A non-cancerous lump arising from a blocked sebaceous gland, which can become inflamed or infected.
  • Boils: Painful, pus-filled lumps that form under the skin when bacteria infect one or more hair follicles.
  • Lipoma: A benign tumour composed of adipose tissue, typically presenting as a soft, mobile, and painless lump.
  • Hidradenitis suppurativa: A chronic skin condition featuring painful, recurrent abscesses and nodules in sweat-gland-bearing areas like the axilla.
  • COVID-19: Can cause reactive lymphadenopathy as part of the systemic immune response to infection or vaccination.
Easily missed
  • Sarcoidosis: An inflammatory disease that can cause granulomas to form in lymph nodes, leading to firm, non-tender swelling.
  • Glandular fever: An infection (infectious mononucleosis) that commonly causes widespread, tender lymphadenopathy in young adults.
Other Differential Diagnoses to consider
  • COVID-19-associated lymphadenopathy: rare but possible lymphadenopathy secondary to immune response or vaccination.
  • Cat scratch disease: lymphadenopathy with history of cat contact, often with associated fever and malaise.
  • Brucellosis: occupational or travel exposure; may present with lymphadenopathy, fever, and malaise.
  • Silicone breast implant rupture: axillary swelling due to inflammatory reaction to leaked material.
  • Autoimmune diseases: diseases like lupus can involve lymphadenopathy with other systemic symptoms.
History Taking
**Patient’s Description**: How can I help you today?
Starting with an open-ended question such as “Can you tell me more about what brought you here today?” encourages the patient to describe their symptom in their own words. Relevance: This initial response helps to gauge the patient’s main concerns and priorities, enabling the clinician to tailor further questioning effectively.
When did you first notice the swelling?
  • This question clarifies the onset of the axillary swelling, distinguishing acute from chronic causes.
  • Relevance: An acute onset may suggest an infection (e.g., a boil or hidradenitis), while a more gradual onset could indicate chronic causes like lymphadenopathy or malignancy.
Have you noticed any changes in the size of the swelling?
  • This helps assess progression, which can indicate the aggressiveness of the underlying pathology.
  • Relevance: Rapid growth could suggest infection or malignancy, whereas stability over time may point toward benign conditions like lipoma.
Is the swelling painful or tender to touch?
  • This question assesses the presence of pain, which can differentiate inflammatory or infectious causes from non-painful conditions like malignancy.
  • Relevance: Pain suggests conditions like a boil, infected cyst, or hidradenitis; lack of pain may raise suspicion for malignancy or lymphoma.
Have you had any recent infections or injuries near this area?
  • This question helps to identify any potential causes of localised lymphadenopathy or reactive inflammation.
  • Relevance: A recent infection in the nearby skin, arm, or breast may point toward reactive lymphadenopathy; recent trauma could indicate a haematoma.
Do you have any other symptoms, such as fever, night sweats, weight loss, or fatigue?
  • Systemic symptoms may indicate an infectious or neoplastic process and help guide further investigations.
  • Relevance: Fever or night sweats may indicate infection (e.g., tuberculosis or HIV) or lymphoma, while weight loss and fatigue are red flags for malignancy or chronic infection.
Have you had similar swellings in other areas, or a history of similar symptoms?
  • This helps identify whether the lymphadenopathy is localised or generalised, influencing differential diagnosis.
  • Relevance: Localised axillary swelling might indicate local infection or neoplasm, while generalised swelling could suggest HIV, leukaemia, or autoimmune causes.
Do you have a history of cancer or family history of cancers?
  • Personal or family history of cancer raises the suspicion of metastatic disease or primary malignancy in the breast or lymph nodes.
  • Relevance: History of breast, lung, or lymphatic cancer increases risk of malignant axillary swelling, necessitating further evaluation.
**Ideas, Concerns, and Expectations (ICE)**
  • These questions help uncover the patient’s perspective on their condition and address any underlying fears or expectations.
  • Relevance: This is critical for addressing concerns like cancer, particularly if the patient is worried about a lump or swelling. Understanding their expectations ensures that the consultation aligns with their priorities, whether for reassurance, further investigation, or treatment options.
Examination Strategy
Inspection of the axilla
  • Begin by visually inspecting the axilla for swelling, skin changes, erythema, or scarring.
  • Relevance: Redness and inflammation may suggest an infectious process, such as a boil or hidradenitis suppurativa, while skin puckering, dimpling, or a tethered appearance could indicate malignancy. A lack of skin changes may support benign conditions like lipoma or cyst.
Palpation of the axillary swelling
  • Assess the size, consistency, mobility, and tenderness of any palpable mass in the axilla.
  • Relevance: Tender, soft, mobile nodes suggest reactive lymphadenopathy or infection, while hard, fixed, or matted nodes raise concern for malignancy or lymphoma. A fluctuant mass could indicate an abscess, whereas a soft, non-tender, mobile mass might point to a benign lipoma.
Examination for systemic lymphadenopathy
  • Palpate other lymph node groups (e.g., cervical, supraclavicular, inguinal) to assess for generalised lymphadenopathy.
  • Relevance: Generalised lymphadenopathy may indicate systemic conditions like lymphoma, HIV, or infectious mononucleosis. Isolated axillary lymphadenopathy is more likely to be a local reaction or infection.
Breast examination (in both males and females)
  • Perform a breast examination to identify any primary breast mass or nipple changes that may suggest breast cancer.
  • Relevance: A primary breast lesion, skin changes, or nipple retraction supports a diagnosis of breast cancer, which can present with axillary lymphadenopathy due to metastasis. Absence of breast pathology decreases the likelihood of breast cancer as the cause.
Chest examination
  • Auscultate and inspect the chest for signs of respiratory distress, abnormal breath sounds, or masses.
  • Relevance: Findings such as decreased breath sounds, dullness, or wheezing could suggest an underlying lung pathology that may have spread to the axilla. Clear lung fields reduce the likelihood of primary pulmonary malignancy.
Skin examination (specifically of the arms and nearby areas)
  • Inspect the skin of the arm, shoulder, and upper chest for rashes, signs of recent trauma, or infection.
  • Relevance: Evidence of a recent scratch, cut, or infection can suggest reactive lymphadenopathy. Pustules or erythematous nodules in the axillary region are associated with hidradenitis suppurativa. Absence of skin findings may suggest systemic causes of lymphadenopathy.
Red Flag Synthesis
Non-tender, hard, fixed lymph node
  • A lymph node that is hard, fixed (non-mobile), and non-tender is highly suspicious for malignancy. Benign inflammatory or infective nodes are typically mobile and tender.
  • Significance: This is the most important clinical sign for malignancy, such as metastatic breast cancer or lymphoma, and requires urgent investigation, usually via a two-week wait referral in the UK.
Unintentional weight loss, night sweats, and fever
  • This trio of systemic symptoms (known as 'B symptoms') is classically associated with haematological malignancies and other chronic diseases.
  • Significance: These symptoms are a major red flag for conditions like lymphoma or tuberculosis. Their presence alongside any lymphadenopathy necessitates prompt and thorough investigation.
Generalised lymphadenopathy
  • The presence of swollen lymph nodes in two or more non-contiguous areas (e.g., axilla and neck) points towards a systemic disease process rather than a localised issue.
  • Significance: This finding significantly raises the suspicion for systemic conditions such as leukaemia, lymphoma, HIV, or widespread metastatic disease, warranting urgent blood tests and further work-up.
Breast lump or skin changes
  • The discovery of a concurrent breast lump, skin dimpling ('peau d'orange'), nipple retraction, or discharge alongside an axillary lump is highly concerning.
  • Significance: These signs strongly suggest a primary breast cancer that has metastasised to the axillary lymph nodes. This requires immediate referral to a breast clinic for imaging and biopsy.
Persistent swelling > 4-6 weeks
  • While reactive nodes are common, they should typically resolve as the underlying cause (e.g., a local infection) improves. A lump that persists or grows is a cause for concern.
  • Significance: A persistent swelling, especially in the absence of a clear benign cause or one that fails to resolve despite treatment (e.g., with antibiotics), raises the possibility of malignancy and should prompt further investigation.
Investigations Strategy & Interpretation
Full blood count (FBC) and differential
  • FBC can reveal evidence of infection, anaemia, or abnormal white cell counts suggestive of haematological malignancies.
  • Result Significance: Leukocytosis may indicate infection or inflammation, while abnormalities in white cell count could suggest leukaemia or lymphoma. A normal FBC may reduce the likelihood of systemic infection or haematological malignancy.
Erythrocyte sedimentation rate (ESR) and C-reactive protein (CRP)
  • These inflammatory markers help detect systemic inflammation, which may support a diagnosis of infection, malignancy, or autoimmune disease.
  • Result Significance: Elevated ESR or CRP may indicate infection, inflammatory conditions, or cancer. Normal values suggest a lower likelihood of systemic inflammation.
Ultrasound of the axilla
  • Ultrasound is a non-invasive imaging tool that provides information on the characteristics of the axillary swelling, including size, consistency, and vascularity.
  • Result Significance: Findings of a solid, irregular, or vascular mass are concerning for malignancy, while cystic or benign-appearing findings suggest a benign condition like a lipoma or sebaceous cyst.
Fine needle aspiration (FNA) or core biopsy
  • FNA or biopsy can provide cytological or histological diagnosis, which is essential for distinguishing benign from malignant causes of axillary swelling.
  • Result Significance: Malignant cells suggest lymphoma or metastatic disease, whereas benign findings may indicate reactive lymphadenopathy or benign lesions like lipomas.
Chest X-ray
  • A chest X-ray can help assess for primary lung pathology, especially if metastatic disease or tuberculosis is suspected.
  • Result Significance: Findings of a lung mass or hilar lymphadenopathy suggest primary or metastatic malignancy, while clear findings lower the likelihood of pulmonary involvement.
HIV testing
  • HIV testing is indicated if there is a high suspicion of HIV-related lymphadenopathy, especially in patients with risk factors or unexplained generalised lymphadenopathy.
  • Result Significance: A positive HIV test suggests HIV-related lymphadenopathy, which can present with persistent, generalised lymph node enlargement. A negative test rules out HIV as the cause.
Tuberculin skin test (TST) or interferon-gamma release assay (IGRA)
  • These tests help detect latent or active tuberculosis, which can present with lymphadenopathy, including axillary lymph nodes.
  • Result Significance: A positive test may suggest tuberculosis as a possible cause, especially if other signs and symptoms support the diagnosis. A negative result reduces the likelihood of tuberculosis.
Evidence-Based Diagnosis (EBDx)
Non-tender, firm, fixed lymph nodes in suspected malignancy
  • Non-tender, firm, and fixed lymph nodes are strongly suggestive of malignancy compared to soft, mobile, or tender nodes, which are more commonly reactive or infectious.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 72%, Specificity: 91% for malignancy diagnosis when nodes are firm and fixed.
  • Reference: "Evaluation of Peripheral Lymphadenopathy in Adults," American Family Physician, 2002.
  • Significance: Positive findings raise suspicion for malignancy (e.g., lymphoma or metastatic breast cancer), while mobile, tender nodes make malignancy less likely.
Weight loss and night sweats in lymphoproliferative disorders
  • Systemic symptoms such as unintentional weight loss and night sweats are often seen in lymphoproliferative disorders and have been associated with more advanced disease.
  • Evidence: Night sweats and weight loss are present in approximately 50% of patients with advanced lymphoma.
  • Reference: NICE Guidelines on "Hodgkin Lymphoma: Recognition and Referral", 2019.
  • Significance: These findings are significant red flags that suggest malignancy, particularly lymphoma, especially when accompanied by persistent lymphadenopathy.
Fine needle aspiration (FNA) for distinguishing benign from malignant lymphadenopathy
  • FNA is useful for initial evaluation of lymphadenopathy and can differentiate reactive from malignant nodes, especially when other risk factors are present.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 87%, Specificity: 97% for detecting malignancy with FNA of lymph nodes.
  • Reference: "Efficacy of Fine-Needle Aspiration in Diagnosing Lymph Node Malignancies," Journal of Clinical Oncology, 2005.
  • Significance: A positive result on FNA provides strong evidence of malignancy, aiding early diagnosis and reducing the need for more invasive procedures.
Elevated ESR and CRP in infectious and inflammatory causes
  • ESR and CRP are non-specific markers of inflammation but can support an infectious or inflammatory cause of axillary swelling.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 85%, Specificity: 65% for elevated CRP in bacterial infections.
  • Reference: "The Diagnostic Utility of C-Reactive Protein in Patients with Infection," Annals of Internal Medicine, 2004.
  • Significance: Elevated ESR or CRP supports a diagnosis of infection or inflammatory disease, especially when malignancy is less likely.
HIV testing for generalised lymphadenopathy
  • HIV testing is a vital diagnostic tool for patients presenting with persistent, unexplained generalised lymphadenopathy.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 99% for HIV ELISA testing in diagnosing HIV infection.
  • Reference: WHO Guidelines on "HIV Testing Services," 2016.
  • Significance: A positive HIV test in the context of lymphadenopathy indicates HIV-related lymphadenopathy, while a negative result helps exclude HIV as the cause.
Ultrasound characteristics for differentiating benign from malignant nodes
  • Ultrasound can assess lymph node morphology, size, and vascularity, differentiating between benign and malignant features (e.g., irregular borders and increased central vascularity).
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 82%, Specificity: 92% for ultrasound in diagnosing malignant lymph nodes based on morphological characteristics.
  • Reference: "Ultrasound Evaluation of Lymph Nodes in Malignancy," Radiology Clinics, 2014.
  • Significance: Suspicious ultrasound features strongly suggest malignancy, while benign characteristics support a non-cancerous cause, guiding further diagnostic steps.
Diagnostic Timeout & Cognitive Biases

Awareness of common thinking errors is essential to improve diagnostic accuracy when assessing a patient with an axillary lump. Before finalising a diagnosis, clinicians should take a 'diagnostic timeout' to challenge their own reasoning.

  • Anchoring Bias: The tendency to lock onto the most obvious or initial diagnosis, such as a simple boil, and fail to consider alternatives. For example, attributing a painful, red lump in a young woman to a simple infection while failing to perform a breast exam, potentially missing a rare inflammatory breast cancer. A key question to ask is: "What is the most serious thing this could be, and have I confidently excluded it?"
  • Confirmation Bias: Actively seeking out or overvaluing evidence that supports an initial belief. For instance, focusing on the fact that a lump is tender to confirm an infectious cause, while downplaying other 'red flag' features like a family history of cancer or the lump's firm consistency.
  • Satisficing (Search Satisfaction): The error of stopping the diagnostic process once one finding has been identified. For example, identifying a reactive-looking axillary node and concluding the assessment, without proceeding to a full examination of all lymph node groups and the breast, thereby missing signs of generalised lymphadenopathy or a primary tumour.
  • Availability Heuristic: Overestimating the likelihood of a diagnosis because it is more recent or memorable in the clinician’s experience. After seeing a series of benign lipomas, a clinician might be less inclined to investigate a new soft lump, even if it has some atypical features. It's crucial to assess each case on its own merits.
Illustrative Management Pathways

For educational purposes, this section describes the typical principles of management for key diagnoses. These are not prescriptive instructions. All management must be guided by official UK guidelines, local formularies, and senior clinical advice.

Suspected Malignancy (e.g., Hard, Fixed Node)

A patient with an axillary lump that is suspicious for malignancy (e.g., hard, non-tender, fixed, >2cm, or associated with red flag symptoms) requires urgent action. The standard UK pathway is an urgent two-week wait (2WW) referral to a specialist clinic, typically a breast clinic for both men and women. The patient will undergo triple assessment: clinical examination, imaging (usually ultrasound of the axilla and mammogram/ultrasound of the breast), and tissue sampling (fine-needle aspiration or core biopsy) to establish a histological diagnosis.

Infected Cyst or Abscess

If a lump is clearly an infected sebaceous cyst or boil (i.e., red, hot, tender, and possibly fluctuant), management is focused on treating the infection. If there is significant surrounding cellulitis, a course of oral antibiotics (e.g., flucloxacillin, following local guidelines) is appropriate. If an abscess has formed (a collection of pus confirmed by fluctuance on examination or on ultrasound), it will often require incision and drainage. Simple warm compresses can help smaller, non-fluctuant lesions to resolve or localise.

Reactive Lymphadenopathy

When a patient presents with a tender, mobile lymph node and a clear source of local infection or inflammation (e.g., a cut on the hand, recent vaccination), the most likely diagnosis is reactive lymphadenopathy. The management is to treat the underlying cause if necessary and provide reassurance. The patient should be 'safety-netted' with clear advice to return for review in 4-6 weeks if the lump has not resolved or if it is getting larger, to ensure a more persistent or serious cause is not missed.

Benign Lesions (e.g., Lipoma)

If a clinical diagnosis of a simple lipoma is made (soft, mobile, non-tender, slow-growing, with classic features) and the patient is asymptomatic and not concerned, a period of watchful waiting is a reasonable approach. If there is any diagnostic uncertainty, or if the lesion is large, causing symptoms, or the patient desires removal for cosmetic reasons, an ultrasound can confirm the diagnosis and routine referral for surgical excision can be considered.

Reflection & Learning

These brief cases illustrate the application of clinical reasoning to different presentations of axillary swelling. They are for educational purposes only.

Case 1: The Worried Student

A 19-year-old student presents with a painful right axillary lump that appeared over the last two days. It is red and very tender to touch. There is no history of trauma. On examination, there is a 2cm, tender, erythematous, firm nodule with no fluctuance. The remainder of the examination, including breast and other lymph node groups, is normal. The problem representation is a young patient with an acute, inflammatory lump consistent with an infected follicle or cyst (a boil). After excluding red flags, management involves reassurance, advice on warm compresses, and a prescription for oral antibiotics due to the degree of inflammation, with clear safety-netting advice to return if it worsens or fails to improve.

Case 2: The Subtle Finding

A 68-year-old woman attends for a routine blood pressure check and mentions in passing that she's had a "small pea" under her left arm for a couple of months. It is not painful. She denies any other symptoms. On examination, a 1.5cm, firm, non-tender, and non-mobile lump is palpated deep in the axilla. The breast examination is unremarkable. The problem representation is an older woman with a persistent, non-tender, fixed axillary lymph node. This is a significant red flag for malignancy (either lymphoma or metastatic disease, most likely from an occult breast primary). The correct pathway is an urgent two-week wait referral to the breast clinic for triple assessment.

Case 3: The Systemic Clues

A 25-year-old man presents with a one-week history of feeling generally unwell, with fatigue, a sore throat, and a low-grade fever. He has noticed tender lumps in his armpits and on both sides of his neck. Examination confirms multiple small (<1cm), mobile, tender lymph nodes in the bilateral axillary and cervical chains. The pharynx is red with some tonsillar exudate. The problem representation is a young adult with a viral-type illness and generalised, tender lymphadenopathy. The constellation of symptoms is highly suggestive of glandular fever (infectious mononucleosis). Management involves blood tests (FBC, monospot test) to confirm the diagnosis, supportive advice for symptomatic relief, and safety-netting regarding the small risk of splenic rupture.
