Fatigue Diagnostic Reasoning Reference
Context: Fatigue Assessment
Overview

Fatigue is one of the most frequent and diagnostically challenging symptoms encountered in clinical practice, affecting patients across all demographics and clinical settings. It ranges from a normal physiological response to exertion or stress to a debilitating feature of a serious underlying systemic disease. The core challenge for the clinician is to differentiate between benign, lifestyle-related fatigue and pathological fatigue that signals conditions such as malignancy, endocrine disorders, or chronic infections.

A systematic and structured approach is essential to avoid missing critical diagnoses while preventing over-investigation in patients where the cause is likely benign. The initial assessment must focus on identifying red flag symptoms and signs that point towards immediate threats. This educational module is designed to provide a foundational framework for the clinical reasoning process in assessing a patient with fatigue. For definitive management protocols, clinicians must consult official UK guidelines from bodies such as NICE (e.g., NG206 for Myalgic encephalomyelitis/chronic fatigue syndrome).

Pathophysiology/Basic Science

Understanding the origin of fatigue is fundamental to diagnosis. The sensation of fatigue is complex and can be generated by several distinct pathophysiological mechanisms, each pointing towards a different group of differential diagnoses:

  • Impaired Energy Metabolism & Oxygen Delivery: This is a core mechanism where cellular energy production cannot meet demand. In anaemia, reduced oxygen-carrying capacity leads to tissue hypoxia and fatigue. In heart failure, diminished cardiac output has a similar effect. Endocrine disorders like hypothyroidism slow the body's entire metabolic rate, while diabetes mellitus involves inefficient glucose utilisation, starving cells of their primary energy source.
  • Systemic Inflammation & Immune Activation: Pro-inflammatory cytokines (e.g., TNF-α, IL-1, IL-6), released during chronic infections (like HIV or TB), autoimmune diseases (like SLE or Rheumatoid Arthritis), and malignancy, have a direct effect on the central nervous system, inducing sickness behaviour, of which fatigue is a cardinal feature. They disrupt neurotransmitter function and hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal (HPA) axis regulation.
  • Neurotransmitter & HPA Axis Disruption: This is central to fatigue in psychological and some neurological conditions. In depression and anxiety, dysregulation of neurotransmitters like serotonin, dopamine, and norepinephrine is thought to contribute significantly to mental and physical exhaustion. Chronic stress can lead to HPA axis dysfunction (as seen in Addison's disease), impairing cortisol production and the body's ability to manage energy and stress.
  • Neuromuscular Dysfunction: This involves a failure at the level of the motor unit. In conditions like myasthenia gravis, autoantibodies block acetylcholine receptors at the neuromuscular junction, causing activity-dependent muscle weakness (fatigability). In multiple sclerosis, demyelination impairs nerve signal conduction, requiring greater effort for muscle activation, which is perceived as fatigue.
  • Disrupted Sleep Architecture: Lack of restorative sleep is a major cause of daytime fatigue. In obstructive sleep apnoea, repeated episodes of hypoxia and arousal throughout the night prevent entry into deep sleep stages. Insomnia and poor sleep hygiene, often linked to stress or psychiatric conditions, similarly disrupt the restorative processes of sleep.
Differential Diagnosis (DDx) Framework

A structured approach to the differential diagnosis of fatigue is essential to ensure life-threatening and treatable conditions are prioritised.

Must not miss!
  • Lymphoma: Often presents with persistent fatigue alongside B-symptoms such as fever, drenching night sweats, and unexplained weight loss.
  • Leukaemia: Can cause profound fatigue due to anaemia, abnormal white cell function, and bone marrow infiltration.
  • GI tract cancer: May cause fatigue through chronic blood loss leading to iron deficiency anaemia, or due to cancer-related cachexia.
  • Pancreatic Cancer: Typically presents with fatigue, significant weight loss, and sometimes jaundice or new-onset diabetes.
  • Diabetes Type 1: Can present with fatigue, polydipsia, polyuria, and weight loss due to an absolute deficiency of insulin.
  • Heart failure: Causes fatigue and breathlessness on exertion due to the heart's inability to pump blood effectively to meet metabolic demands.
  • Renal failure: Leads to fatigue through a combination of anaemia (due to reduced erythropoietin production) and uraemia.
Common causes
  • Anxiety: Often causes mental and physical fatigue associated with worry, tension, and poor sleep.
  • Depression: Characterised by low mood, anhedonia, and a profound lack of energy or motivation.
  • Insomnia: Leads to non-restorative sleep and significant daytime tiredness.
  • Stress and overwork: A common cause of both mental and physical exhaustion in modern life.
  • Anaemia: Presents with fatigue, pallor, and exertional dyspnoea due to reduced oxygen-carrying capacity of the blood.
  • Hypothyroidism: A common endocrine cause of fatigue, associated with weight gain, cold intolerance, and constipation.
  • Chronic fatigue syndrome: A complex condition characterised by profound, disabling fatigue lasting more than 6 months, not explained by other conditions.
  • Chronic infections: Conditions like glandular fever (EBV) or hepatitis can cause prolonged periods of fatigue.
  • COVID-19: Can lead to acute fatigue during the illness and persistent post-viral fatigue ("Long COVID") in some individuals.
Easily missed
  • Medication-induced Fatigue: A common side effect of many drugs, including beta-blockers, antidepressants, and sedatives.
  • Obstructive Sleep Apnoea: Presents with daytime sleepiness, snoring, and witnessed apnoeas, due to repeated airway collapse during sleep.
  • Addison's disease: Adrenal insufficiency causing fatigue, weakness, weight loss, and characteristic skin hyperpigmentation.
  • Cushing's syndrome: Excess cortisol can cause fatigue along with central obesity, muscle weakness, and hypertension.
  • Connective tissue diseases: Conditions like Systemic Lupus Erythematosus (SLE) often have fatigue as a primary and debilitating symptom.
  • Polymyalgia rheumatica: An inflammatory condition in older adults causing fatigue, stiffness, and pain in the shoulder and pelvic girdles.
  • Myasthenia gravis: An autoimmune disorder causing fluctuating muscle weakness and fatigability that worsens with activity.
  • Coeliac disease: An autoimmune reaction to gluten causing fatigue, often through malabsorption and resulting nutritional deficiencies.
  • HIV: Can present with non-specific fatigue during the seroconversion phase or as a symptom of advanced disease.
Other Differential Diagnoses to consider
  • Fibromyalgia: Chronic widespread pain often with fatigue, poor sleep, and mood symptoms
  • Hypopituitarism: Multiple hormone deficiencies leading to fatigue and weakness
  • Vitamin D deficiency: Common cause of fatigue and musculoskeletal discomfort
  • Hypogonadism: Low testosterone in men, leading to fatigue and reduced vitality
  • Chronic kidney disease (early stages): Often asymptomatic until advanced stages, with early fatigue
  • Multiple sclerosis: Neurological disease that can cause early fatigue before other neurological symptoms appear
  • Post-viral fatigue syndrome: Persistent fatigue following viral infections
  • Systemic lupus erythematosus: Autoimmune condition causing fatigue, joint pain, and rashes
History Taking
**Patient’s Description**: How can I help you today?
Starting with an open-ended question like “Can you tell me more about what brought you here today?” allows the patient to describe their symptoms in their own words. Relevance: This approach helps gather an initial sense of the patient’s experience of fatigue, providing insight into symptom character and potential key features for further exploration.
Onset, Duration, and Timing
  • “When did you first notice feeling unusually tired?” or “How long have you been feeling this way?”
  • Relevance: Acute onset may indicate infections (e.g., COVID-19, recent viral illness) or sudden metabolic issues (e.g., adrenal insufficiency), whereas chronic or progressive fatigue could suggest chronic conditions like hypothyroidism, anaemia, or malignancy.
Quality and Description of Fatigue
  • “Can you describe how the fatigue feels? Is it physical tiredness, mental exhaustion, or both?”
  • Relevance: Physical fatigue may indicate cardiovascular or endocrine issues, while mental fatigue might suggest anxiety, depression, or chronic fatigue syndrome.
Exacerbating and Relieving Factors
  • “Does anything make your fatigue better or worse?”
  • Relevance: Fatigue worsening with activity and relieved by rest could suggest heart failure or pulmonary issues. Fatigue relieved by sleep might indicate insomnia or anxiety.
Sleep Quality and Patterns
  • “How is your sleep? Do you feel rested in the morning?”
  • Relevance: Poor sleep quality is common in obstructive sleep apnoea, depression, or chronic insomnia. Restful sleep, yet persistent fatigue, may suggest a metabolic or systemic condition like hypothyroidism.
Associated Symptoms
  • Ask about symptoms such as weight loss, night sweats, fever, joint pain, skin changes, and gastrointestinal symptoms.
  • Relevance: Weight loss and night sweats might suggest malignancy or HIV; joint pain could indicate connective tissue disease; GI symptoms could point towards coeliac disease or chronic infection.
Medical History
  • “Have you had any significant illnesses or surgeries in the past?”
  • Relevance: Previous history of autoimmune diseases, thyroid disorders, or diabetes can provide clues to underlying fatigue causes.
Medication and Drug History
  • “Are you taking any regular medication, including over-the-counter drugs or supplements?”
  • Relevance: Certain medications, such as beta-blockers or sedatives, may contribute to fatigue. Additionally, interactions or polypharmacy could exacerbate symptoms in elderly patients.
Family History
  • “Does anyone in your family have a history of similar symptoms or conditions like thyroid disease, diabetes, or autoimmune disorders?”
  • Relevance: Family history of autoimmune diseases, metabolic issues, or malignancy can indicate genetic predisposition and direct further workup.
**Ideas, Concerns, and Expectations (ICE)**
  • “What do you think might be causing this? Is there something you are particularly worried about?”
  • Relevance: Understanding the patient’s perspective helps address concerns they may have about serious conditions (e.g., cancer, heart disease) and clarifies what they hope to achieve from the consultation.
Examination Strategy
General Inspection
  • Observe for signs of pallor, weight loss, jaundice, or general distress.
  • Relevance: Pallor may indicate anaemia; weight loss could suggest malignancy, HIV, or thyroid disease; jaundice might point toward liver disease; general distress or poor grooming could hint at depression or chronic fatigue syndrome.
Vital Signs
  • Measure blood pressure, pulse, respiratory rate, and temperature.
  • Relevance: Hypotension may indicate Addison’s disease or heart failure, while tachycardia could suggest anaemia, hyperthyroidism, or infection. Fever may suggest infection or malignancy.
Skin Examination
  • Check for hyperpigmentation, rashes, or bruising.
  • Relevance: Hyperpigmentation may suggest Addison’s disease; a butterfly rash could indicate systemic lupus erythematosus; easy bruising might point toward leukaemia or Cushing’s syndrome.
Thyroid Examination
  • Inspect and palpate the thyroid gland for enlargement, nodules, or tenderness.
  • Relevance: A palpable goitre or nodules may suggest hyperthyroidism or hypothyroidism. Thyroid tenderness may indicate thyroiditis, potentially causing fatigue.
Cardiovascular Examination
  • Auscultate the heart, check for peripheral oedema, and assess jugular venous pressure (JVP).
  • Relevance: Signs of heart failure, such as raised JVP or peripheral oedema, could explain fatigue. Heart murmurs may suggest anaemia, endocarditis, or heart valve pathology.
Respiratory Examination
  • Auscultate the lungs and assess for signs of respiratory distress.
  • Relevance: Basal crackles might suggest heart failure; reduced breath sounds or crackles could indicate chronic lung disease or infection, both of which can cause fatigue.
Abdominal Examination
  • Palpate for hepatomegaly, splenomegaly, or abdominal masses.
  • Relevance: Hepatosplenomegaly might suggest infections (e.g., HIV), haematologic malignancies, or liver disease. An abdominal mass could indicate a GI malignancy, contributing to fatigue.
Muscle Power and Neurological Examination
  • Test muscle strength and tone, focusing on proximal muscle groups.
  • Relevance: Muscle weakness may suggest myasthenia gravis, polymyalgia rheumatica, or other neuromuscular disorders. Neurological signs like diminished reflexes or sensory loss may point to multiple sclerosis or vitamin B12 deficiency.
Mental Status Examination
  • Assess for mood, cognition, and orientation.
  • Relevance: Low mood or poor concentration may suggest depression or anxiety as a cause of fatigue. Cognitive changes could indicate chronic fatigue syndrome or a systemic issue such as hypothyroidism.
Red Flag Synthesis

This section synthesises the key red flags from the source document. Any of these findings in a patient with fatigue necessitates urgent senior review and further investigation.

Unexplained Weight Loss

Significance: This could indicate serious diagnoses like cancer (e.g., lymphoma, GI malignancies), chronic infections (e.g. HIV, TB) or advanced chronic illness, requiring urgent investigation.

Persistent Fever or Night Sweats

Significance: Persistent fever or drenching night sweats could suggest lymphoma, tuberculosis, or endocarditis, all of which may necessitate urgent workup.

New or Severe Pain, Especially Bone Pain

Significance: This could suggest multiple myeloma, metastatic cancer, or a serious inflammatory condition, requiring immediate imaging and further investigation.

Lymphadenopathy (Enlarged Lymph Nodes)

Significance: Could indicate lymphoma, leukaemia, or metastatic cancer, warranting urgent referral and biopsy consideration, especially if nodes are hard, fixed, and non-tender.

Signs of Anaemia (e.g., Pallor, Dizziness, Breathlessness)

Significance: Anaemia, particularly if severe or with rapid onset, could indicate GI malignancy (from chronic bleeding), renal failure, or leukaemia, and requires urgent testing and management.

Jaundice

Significance: This could indicate hepatobiliary malignancy (e.g., pancreatic cancer), liver failure, or cholestasis, necessitating urgent liver function tests and imaging.

Neurological Symptoms (e.g., Weakness, Paresthesia, Vision Changes)

Significance: This could indicate serious neurological conditions, such as multiple sclerosis, myasthenia gravis, or brain metastases, and warrants prompt neuroimaging and neurology referral.

Persistent Abdominal Pain or Mass

Significance: This could indicate GI malignancy (e.g., colorectal or pancreatic cancer) and requires urgent abdominal imaging and further GI evaluation.

Investigations Strategy & Interpretation
Full Blood Count (FBC)
  • A full blood count can help identify anaemia, infection, or haematological abnormalities, all of which can cause fatigue.
  • Result Significance: Low haemoglobin levels suggest anaemia, which may be due to GI bleeding, iron deficiency, or chronic disease. Elevated white cells may indicate infection or haematologic malignancy, while abnormal platelet counts could point to bone marrow pathology or leukaemia.
Thyroid Function Tests (TFTs)
  • Thyroid function tests assess levels of thyroid hormones, which can reveal hypo- or hyperthyroidism, both of which can present with fatigue.
  • Result Significance: Elevated TSH with low T4 suggests hypothyroidism, a common cause of fatigue. Conversely, low TSH with high T4 may indicate hyperthyroidism, which can also lead to fatigue.
Liver Function Tests (LFTs)
  • Liver function tests help assess for liver disease, which can contribute to fatigue due to metabolic dysfunction and toxin buildup.
  • Result Significance: Elevated liver enzymes or bilirubin may indicate liver disease (e.g., hepatitis, cirrhosis) or biliary obstruction, and would require further evaluation with imaging.
Renal Function Tests (Urea and Electrolytes)
  • Assessing renal function is essential, as chronic kidney disease can present with fatigue due to impaired filtration and toxin buildup.
  • Result Significance: Elevated creatinine and urea levels suggest renal impairment. Abnormal electrolyte levels may also suggest kidney dysfunction or adrenal issues.
Blood Glucose and HbA1c
  • Glucose testing, including HbA1c, helps identify diabetes mellitus, a condition commonly associated with fatigue.
  • Result Significance: Elevated fasting glucose or HbA1c confirms diabetes, which could explain symptoms. Normal glucose helps rule out diabetes as a cause of fatigue.
Inflammatory Markers (CRP and ESR)
  • These markers help detect inflammation or infection, which could be contributing to fatigue.
  • Result Significance: Elevated CRP or ESR suggests inflammation, which may be due to autoimmune disease, infection, or malignancy. Normal values make an inflammatory cause less likely.
Ferritin and Iron Studies
  • Iron studies help evaluate iron deficiency anaemia, a common cause of fatigue.
  • Result Significance: Low ferritin indicates iron deficiency, while high ferritin can indicate chronic disease or liver pathology.
Vitamin B12 and Folate Levels
  • B12 and folate deficiencies can lead to anaemia and neurological symptoms, which may manifest as fatigue.
  • Result Significance: Low levels suggest deficiency, often associated with malabsorption or dietary issues. Normal levels make deficiency less likely.
HIV Test
  • HIV can present with fatigue, especially in the context of chronic infection or early seroconversion illness.
  • Result Significance: A positive HIV test confirms diagnosis, prompting specialist referral and management. Negative results rule out HIV as a cause of fatigue.
Coeliac Screening (Tissue Transglutaminase Antibodies)
  • Screening for coeliac disease is important in unexplained fatigue, as malabsorption can lead to anaemia and nutritional deficiencies.
  • Result Significance: Positive antibodies suggest coeliac disease, warranting further diagnostic workup with endoscopy. Negative antibodies reduce the likelihood of coeliac disease.
Autoantibody Screen (e.g., ANA, RF)
  • Autoantibodies can help detect autoimmune diseases like lupus or rheumatoid arthritis, both of which can cause fatigue.
  • Result Significance: Positive results suggest an autoimmune condition, requiring specialist referral and management. Negative results make autoimmune causes less likely.
Evidence-Based Diagnosis (EBDx)
Unintentional Weight Loss
  • Unintentional weight loss, especially >5% body weight over 6-12 months, is a strong predictor of malignancy, chronic infections, and some endocrine disorders.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 74% for malignancy, Specificity: 85% for chronic diseases.
  • Significance: Positive findings support the need to evaluate for malignancy or chronic infection, while absence reduces likelihood but does not exclude these conditions.
Pallor in Anaemia
  • Pallor, especially conjunctival, is commonly associated with anaemia and has high clinical significance in fatigue assessment.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 59%, Specificity: 87% for haemoglobin <10 g/dL.
  • Significance: Positive findings increase the likelihood of anaemia, prompting further testing with full blood count; absence of pallor reduces, but does not rule out, anaemia.
Raised C-Reactive Protein (CRP)
  • Elevated CRP is a non-specific marker of inflammation, but when elevated significantly, it supports inflammatory or infectious aetiologies of fatigue.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 76%, Specificity: 67% for systemic inflammation in chronic disease.
  • Significance: Elevated CRP supports inflammatory conditions or chronic infection; normal CRP makes these less likely but does not fully exclude them.
Low Serum Ferritin in Iron Deficiency Anaemia
  • Low ferritin is a reliable marker for iron deficiency anaemia, commonly associated with fatigue symptoms.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 92%, Specificity: 98% for iron deficiency with ferritin <15 µg/L.
  • Significance: Low ferritin strongly suggests iron deficiency as a cause of fatigue, while normal levels (if above 100 µg/L) make iron deficiency highly unlikely.
Positive Anti-Tissue Transglutaminase Antibodies for Coeliac Disease
  • Anti-TTG antibodies are a reliable screening test for coeliac disease, which can cause fatigue due to malabsorption and nutritional deficiency.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 94%, Specificity: 97% for coeliac disease.
  • Significance: Positive result indicates high likelihood of coeliac disease, guiding further diagnostic workup with endoscopy; negative result makes coeliac unlikely.
Elevated TSH and Low Free T4 in Hypothyroidism
  • High TSH with low free T4 is the hallmark of primary hypothyroidism, a common cause of fatigue.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 85%, Specificity: 99% for primary hypothyroidism.
  • Significance: Positive findings confirm hypothyroidism; normal TSH and T4 levels make thyroid disease an unlikely cause of fatigue.
Anaemia Detected on Full Blood Count
  • Anaemia is a well-established cause of fatigue, with fatigue severity often correlating with haemoglobin level.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 89% for fatigue with Hb <11 g/dL, Specificity: 75%.
  • Significance: Low Hb levels confirm anaemia as a fatigue cause, with further investigation to identify underlying aetiology; normal Hb reduces likelihood of anaemia-related fatigue.
Diagnostic Timeout & Cognitive Biases

Awareness of common thinking errors is essential to improve diagnostic accuracy and patient safety when assessing a patient with fatigue. Before finalising a diagnosis, it is good practice for clinicians at all levels to take a 'diagnostic timeout' to challenge their own reasoning.

  • Anchoring Bias: The tendency to attribute fatigue to a known, common diagnosis like depression or stress too early in the consultation. This can lead to dismissing new or subtle symptoms (e.g., night sweats, new joint pain) that point towards a different, organic cause. A key question to ask is: "If it wasn't depression, what else could this be?"
  • Confirmation Bias: Selectively focusing on evidence that supports an initial hypothesis while downplaying contradictory information. For example, focusing on a history of poor sleep to confirm a diagnosis of insomnia while ignoring a finding of lymphadenopathy or unintentional weight loss.
  • Availability Heuristic: Overestimating the likelihood of diagnoses that are recent or memorable. For example, attributing all post-viral fatigue to "Long COVID" due to its high prevalence, while potentially missing less common but important diagnoses like post-EBV lymphoma or Addison's disease.
  • Diagnostic Momentum: Uncritically accepting a previous, often vague, label of 'tired all the time' (TATT) from the patient's record without undertaking a full, first-principles reassessment. This is particularly risky when a patient's clinical picture has evolved.
Illustrative Management Pathways

For educational purposes, this section describes the typical principles of management for key diagnoses. These are not prescriptive instructions. All management must be guided by official UK guidelines, local formularies, and senior clinical advice.

Iron Deficiency Anaemia

Once diagnosed via FBC and ferritin levels, the management pathway has two parallel aims. The first is to identify and treat the underlying cause of iron loss (e.g., managing heavy menstrual bleeding, investigating for GI blood loss via endoscopy if indicated). The second is iron replacement. This is typically achieved with oral ferrous sulphate or fumarate, with advice on taking it with Vitamin C to enhance absorption and manage side effects. For patients who cannot tolerate oral iron or require more rapid replacement, intravenous iron infusions are used in a hospital or community setting.

Hypothyroidism

The management of primary hypothyroidism is based on hormone replacement. According to NICE guidelines, this involves starting treatment with levothyroxine, with the initial dose depending on the patient's age and cardiovascular health. The goal is to restore the patient to a euthyroid state, monitored by regular Thyroid Function Tests. The dose of levothyroxine is titrated gradually (usually every 3-4 months initially) based on the TSH level, aiming to bring it within the normal reference range.

Depression with Associated Fatigue

Management should follow a stepped-care model. For mild to moderate depression, initial management may focus on non-pharmacological approaches, including psychoeducation, sleep hygiene, structured exercise programmes, and referral for psychological therapies like Cognitive Behavioural Therapy (CBT). For moderate to severe depression, or when psychological therapies are not effective or available, an SSRI antidepressant is typically considered first-line, with regular review to assess efficacy and side effects.

Fatigue with No Identified Organic Cause

After a thorough history, examination, and appropriate screening blood tests have excluded red flag conditions and common organic causes, management focuses on validation, reassurance, and lifestyle modification. This includes providing clear advice on sleep hygiene, diet, and the importance of graded exercise therapy (avoiding boom-and-bust cycles). If fatigue is profound, persistent (>3 months), and meets specific criteria (e.g., post-exertional malaise), a diagnosis of Myalgic Encephalomyelitis/Chronic Fatigue Syndrome (ME/CFS) should be considered, which requires referral to a specialist service for multidisciplinary management, as outlined in NICE guideline NG206.

Reflection & Learning

These brief cases illustrate the application of clinical reasoning to different fatigue presentations. They are for educational purposes only.

Case 1: Suspected Anaemia in General Practice

A 28-year-old female presents to her GP complaining of feeling "drained" for the past 6 months. She finds it hard to get through her workday and feels breathless climbing stairs. On questioning, she reports her periods have become much heavier recently. Examination reveals conjunctival pallor. The problem representation is a young woman with chronic fatigue, exertional breathlessness, and a clear source of potential blood loss. The GP's initial investigation is an FBC and ferritin, which subsequently confirms iron deficiency anaemia, leading to treatment with oral iron and a referral to gynaecology.

Case 2: Suspected Malignancy in an Older Patient

A 68-year-old man presents with a 3-month history of worsening fatigue. He has unintentionally lost 5kg in weight and mentions his clothes feel looser. He also reports some vague abdominal discomfort and notes his stools seem darker than usual. He has a history of smoking. The problem representation is an older man with constitutional symptoms (fatigue, weight loss) and a change in bowel habit, raising a high suspicion of gastrointestinal malignancy. The GP makes an urgent 2-week-wait cancer referral for colonoscopy and arranges urgent blood tests including FBC, LFTs, and U&Es.

Case 3: Suspected Endocrine Disorder in A&E

A 45-year-old woman is brought to the Emergency Department feeling generally unwell, weak, and dizzy. She has been feeling profoundly tired for weeks. She appears tanned despite it being winter. Her blood pressure is 90/55 mmHg. Blood tests reveal hyponatraemia and hyperkalaemia. The problem representation is a patient with chronic fatigue, postural hypotension, a suggestive skin sign, and characteristic electrolyte abnormalities. This is highly suspicious for Addison's disease (adrenal insufficiency), representing a medical emergency requiring immediate fluid resuscitation and intravenous hydrocortisone whilst awaiting a definitive diagnosis with a short synacthen test.
