Chest Pain Diagnostic Reasoning Reference
Context: Chest Pain Assessment
Overview & Links to Key Guidelines

Chest pain is one of the most common and challenging presentations in medicine, spanning from benign, self-limiting conditions to life-threatening emergencies. The primary challenge for any clinician, from medical student to consultant, is to rapidly and safely distinguish between these possibilities. A systematic approach is crucial to ensure critical diagnoses are not missed while avoiding unnecessary investigations for benign causes. The initial assessment, whether in primary care or an Emergency Department, must focus on identifying red flags that point towards immediate threats like Acute Coronary Syndrome (ACS), Pulmonary Embolism (PE), or Aortic Dissection.

This educational module is designed to build a foundational understanding of the clinical reasoning process for chest pain. For definitive, real-time management protocols, users must refer to official UK guidelines from authoritative bodies such as NICE (e.g., NG185 for Acute Coronary Syndromes, CG144 for Venous Thromboembolic Diseases) and the Resuscitation Council UK.

Pathophysiology/Basic Science

Understanding the origin of chest pain is key to diagnosis. Pain can be generated through several distinct mechanisms, each pointing towards a different set of differential diagnoses:

  • Myocardial Ischaemia: This occurs when the oxygen demand of the heart muscle exceeds the available supply, typically due to coronary artery disease. The resulting accumulation of metabolic byproducts like lactate and adenosine stimulates cardiac nerve endings, causing the characteristic visceral, crushing, or heavy pain of angina or myocardial infarction.
  • Pleuritic Pain: Inflammation of the pleura (the lining of the lungs and chest cavity) causes sharp, well-localised pain that is characteristically exacerbated by inspiration, coughing, or movement. The parietal pleura is richly innervated with somatic pain fibres, leading to this distinct pain pattern in conditions like PE, pneumonia, and pleuritis.
  • Vascular Stretch/Tear: The wall of the aorta is intensely innervated. In an Aortic Dissection, a tear in the intimal layer allows high-pressure blood to enter and split the layers of the aortic wall. This rapid, forceful stretching causes an excruciating, 'tearing' or 'ripping' pain, which classically radiates to the back.
  • Oesophageal Pain: This can arise from two main mechanisms. Chemical irritation of the oesophageal lining from acid reflux (GORD) causes a burning retrosternal pain (heartburn). In contrast, powerful, uncoordinated smooth muscle contractions (oesophageal spasm) can cause a squeezing or constricting pain that can closely mimic angina pectoris.
  • Somatic Pain: This pain originates from the chest wall itself, including muscles, bones, and cartilage. Direct injury or inflammation of these somatic structures activates local nociceptors, resulting in pain that is typically well-localised, often described as sharp or aching, and characteristically reproducible on palpation or with specific movements.
Differential Diagnosis (DDx) Framework

A structured approach to the differential diagnosis of chest pain is essential to ensure life-threatening conditions are prioritised.

Must not miss!
  • Myocardial Infarction: Presents with severe, crushing central chest pain which may radiate to the arms or jaw, often associated with sweating and nausea.
  • Angina: Typically a constricting discomfort on exertion, which is predictable and relieved by rest or nitrates.
  • Pulmonary Embolism: Characterised by a sudden onset of sharp, pleuritic chest pain, often accompanied by shortness of breath and tachycardia.
  • Dissecting aortic aneurysm: An excruciating, 'tearing' or 'ripping' pain, usually of sudden onset and radiating to the back between the shoulder blades.
  • Pericarditis: A sharp, central chest pain that is characteristically relieved by sitting forward and worsened by lying down or deep inspiration.
  • Pneumonia: Presents with pleuritic chest pain, fever, productive cough, and shortness of breath.
  • Pneumothorax: Sudden onset of unilateral pleuritic chest pain and shortness of breath, with reduced breath sounds on the affected side.
  • Lung cancer: May present with persistent chest pain, cough, haemoptysis, and unexplained weight loss.
  • Oesophageal cancer: Often presents with pain or difficulty swallowing (dysphagia), persistent heartburn, and weight loss.
  • Spinal cord compression: Can cause a band-like chest pain with associated neurological symptoms in the limbs, such as weakness or sensory changes.
Common causes
  • Musculoskeletal pain: A common cause, typically described as an ache or sharp pain that is reproducible on palpation or with specific movements.
  • Costochondritis: Localised pain and tenderness over the costochondral joints of the chest wall, without visible swelling.
  • GORD: A burning retrosternal discomfort (heartburn), often worse after meals or when lying flat, sometimes relieved by antacids.
  • Oesophageal spasm: A squeezing, central chest pain that can mimic angina but is often related to swallowing.
  • Peptic Ulcer Disease: Typically presents as a gnawing or burning pain in the epigastrium, which may be related to meals.
  • Anxiety: Can cause atypical, variable chest pain or tightness, often associated with palpitations, dizziness, and a sense of panic.
  • Medically Unexplained Symptoms: Chest pain for which no specific organic cause is found after thorough investigation.
  • COVID-19: Can present with a wide range of symptoms, including central chest pain, often alongside fever, cough, and fatigue.
Easily missed
  • Biliary colic: Severe, constant pain in the right upper quadrant or epigastrium that can radiate to the chest, often triggered by fatty meals.
  • Herpes Zoster: A burning, unilateral, dermatomal pain which precedes the characteristic vesicular rash by several days.
  • Rib fracture: Localised, sharp pain and tenderness over a rib, often following trauma or severe coughing, with pain on inspiration.
Other Differential Diagnoses to consider
  • Mitral valve prolapse: Typically presents with non-exertional chest pain, palpitations, and dizziness.
  • Boerhaave syndrome: Oesophageal rupture presenting with severe, sudden onset chest pain often after vomiting or retching.
  • Pleuritis: Inflammation of the pleura, often causing sharp, pleuritic chest pain, especially on deep inspiration.
  • Panic attack: Sudden onset chest tightness with palpitations, dyspnoea, and a feeling of impending doom.
  • Perforated peptic ulcer: Sudden severe epigastric or chest pain, often in a patient with a history of ulcers.
  • Sarcoidosis: Rare cause of chest pain with systemic signs of granulomatous inflammation, often accompanied by respiratory symptoms.
  • Tietze's syndrome: Localised pain and swelling at the costochondral junction, similar to costochondritis but with visible swelling.
  • Thoracic outlet syndrome: Neurovascular compression that may cause chest pain radiating to the arm with associated numbness or weakness.
  • Myocarditis: Inflammatory condition often presenting with chest pain, fatigue, and heart failure symptoms, commonly following a viral illness.
History Taking
Patient’s Description: Start with open questions.

Start by asking: "How can I help you today?" or "What brings you in today?" These questions are effective for several reasons:

  • Open-ended nature: They allow the patient to express their concerns freely without being restricted to a specific symptom or condition.
  • Neutral tone: These questions avoid assumptions, creating a comfortable space for the patient to share their concerns.
  • Sets the agenda: The patient’s response helps guide the consultation by highlighting their main issue or reason for visiting.

Active listening is crucial: giving the patient time to elaborate without interruption can lead to a more accurate diagnosis and improve satisfaction. Starting with open-ended questions provides insight into the nature and severity of the issue, revealing key details about the onset, triggers, and the patient’s perspective.
Relevance: This approach guides the clinician’s follow-up questions, builds rapport, and ensures a patient-centred consultation.

Quality of Pain: Can you describe what the pain feels like?
  • The description of pain quality (e.g., sharp, dull, burning, crushing) can provide critical diagnostic insights. For example, a sharp, pleuritic pain may suggest a pulmonary cause, while crushing or squeezing pain may indicate myocardial ischaemia.
  • Relevance: Helps differentiate between musculoskeletal, cardiac, gastrointestinal, and other causes of chest pain.
Location and Radiation: Where exactly is the pain, and does it spread anywhere else?
  • Asking about pain location and whether it radiates can narrow the differential diagnosis. For example, chest pain radiating to the left arm or jaw is more suggestive of myocardial ischaemia, while pain confined to the chest wall may indicate musculoskeletal origins.
  • Relevance: Radiation patterns help identify or exclude certain diagnoses, such as myocardial infarction (radiation to arm/jaw) or aortic dissection (radiation to back).
Onset and Duration: When did the pain start, and how long does it last?
  • Clarifying the onset (sudden or gradual) and duration of the chest pain is essential for diagnosis. Acute onset pain, particularly if severe and associated with collapse, may suggest aortic dissection or pulmonary embolism. Pain lasting for seconds is rarely cardiac in origin.
  • Relevance: Onset and duration help identify acute emergencies (e.g., PE, MI) versus chronic or benign causes (e.g., GORD).
Triggers and Exacerbating/Relieving Factors: Is there anything that makes the pain better or worse?
  • Understanding what triggers or relieves the pain is crucial. Pain that worsens with exertion and improves with rest is often cardiac, whereas pain worsened by movement or palpation may be musculoskeletal. Gastrointestinal pain might improve with antacids or worsen after eating.
  • Relevance: Helps distinguish between cardiac, musculoskeletal, and gastrointestinal causes based on typical exacerbating or relieving factors.
Associated Symptoms: Have you noticed any other symptoms like shortness of breath, palpitations, fever, or a rash?
  • Exploring associated symptoms is vital. Shortness of breath could suggest a pulmonary or cardiac cause (e.g., PE, MI). Fever might point towards an infectious process like pneumonia or COVID-19. A rash, particularly in a dermatomal pattern, might suggest herpes zoster.
  • Relevance: Positive findings can indicate diagnoses such as pneumonia or pulmonary embolism, while their absence may help rule out these conditions.
Past Medical History (PMH): Have you had any previous heart, lung, or gastrointestinal problems?
  • A patient’s past medical history is often key to risk stratification. A history of coronary artery disease raises the likelihood of myocardial ischaemia, while a history of deep vein thrombosis may point towards pulmonary embolism. Gastro-oesophageal reflux in the past might suggest GORD.
  • Relevance: Relevant comorbidities help estimate the likelihood of recurrent conditions such as MI, PE, or GORD.
Family History (FH): Does anyone in your family have heart disease, blood clots, or cancer?
  • Family history can reveal predispositions to certain conditions. A family history of early-onset coronary artery disease increases the risk of myocardial ischaemia. Likewise, inherited hypercoagulable states can increase the risk of PE, and familial patterns of certain cancers can suggest oesophageal or lung cancer.
  • Relevance: Helps identify inherited risks for conditions like myocardial infarction, pulmonary embolism, or cancer.
Drug History: Are you taking any regular medications, or have you recently started any new medications?
  • Certain medications can cause chest pain or influence underlying conditions. For example, beta-blockers may mask tachycardia in pulmonary embolism, and anticoagulants may increase the risk of bleeding. New medications could suggest drug-induced oesophagitis or side effects like angina.
  • Relevance: Medication history is vital for assessing drug side effects, interactions, and contraindications, especially in cardiovascular conditions.
Social History: Do you smoke or drink alcohol, and what do you do for work?
  • Smoking and alcohol use are significant risk factors for coronary artery disease, lung cancer, and gastrointestinal conditions like GORD or oesophageal cancer. Occupational hazards, such as exposure to asbestos or other chemicals, could predispose to lung disease.
  • Relevance: Provides insight into lifestyle-related risks and potential environmental exposures relevant to the differential diagnosis.
Ideas, Concerns, and Expectations (ICE)
  • ICE questions, such as "What do you think might be causing this?", "Is there something specific you are worried about?", and "What were you hoping we might be able to do for you today?" help to explore the patient’s perspective.
  • Relevance: Helps align the management plan with the patient’s concerns and expectations, ensuring their worries are addressed and fostering shared decision-making.
Examination Strategy
General Inspection: Inspect the patient for general signs of distress, pallor, or sweating
  • Observation of the patient's general appearance can give important clues about the severity of the condition.
  • Relevance: Positive findings such as pallor, diaphoresis, or signs of distress suggest a potentially life-threatening cause, such as myocardial infarction or pulmonary embolism. Negative findings help to exclude more acute, severe conditions.
Vital Signs: Measure blood pressure, heart rate, respiratory rate, oxygen saturation, and temperature
  • Vital signs are critical in assessing the stability of the patient and guiding the differential diagnosis.
  • Relevance: Tachycardia, hypotension, or hypoxia may suggest pulmonary embolism or pneumothorax. Fever could indicate pneumonia or pericarditis. Stable vital signs reduce the likelihood of an acute life-threatening event.
Jugular Venous Pressure (JVP) Assessment: Evaluate the jugular venous pressure for signs of fluid overload or heart failure
  • Raised JVP is a key sign of right heart failure or pulmonary embolism.
  • Relevance: An elevated JVP suggests conditions such as heart failure, pulmonary embolism, or tension pneumothorax. A normal JVP makes these diagnoses less likely.
Cardiovascular Examination: Palpate the precordium, assess heart sounds (murmurs, rubs), and check for peripheral oedema
  • A thorough cardiovascular examination can help to identify or exclude cardiac causes of chest pain.
  • Relevance: Positive findings such as a new murmur or pericardial rub suggest pericarditis, endocarditis, or valvular disease. Absent heart sounds or muffled sounds may indicate pericardial effusion. Peripheral oedema may point towards congestive heart failure.
Inspection for Skin Changes: Look for vesicular rash or erythema
  • Inspection of the skin is important to identify conditions like herpes zoster (shingles).
  • Relevance: A vesicular rash along a dermatome suggests herpes zoster, which can present with chest pain before the rash appears. Absence of skin changes helps to rule out this diagnosis.
Palpation of the Chest Wall: Palpate the chest wall for tenderness
  • Palpation of the chest wall is important for identifying musculoskeletal causes of chest pain.
  • Relevance: Localised tenderness over the ribs or costochondral junction suggests musculoskeletal pain or costochondritis. The absence of tenderness helps to rule out these causes.
Respiratory Examination: Assess for chest expansion, breath sounds, percussion notes, and added sounds (crackles, wheeze, pleural rub)
  • Examination of the respiratory system helps identify or exclude pulmonary causes of chest pain.
  • Relevance: Reduced breath sounds and hyper-resonant percussion may suggest pneumothorax. Crackles could indicate pneumonia or pulmonary oedema. A pleural rub might suggest pleuritis or pulmonary embolism.
Abdominal Examination: Palpate the abdomen for tenderness, particularly in the right upper quadrant
  • Abdominal examination can help identify gastrointestinal causes of chest pain.
  • Relevance: Right upper quadrant tenderness may indicate biliary colic, while epigastric tenderness could suggest GORD or peptic ulcer disease. The absence of abdominal tenderness reduces the likelihood of these gastrointestinal causes.
Examination of the Lower Limbs: Check for signs of deep vein thrombosis (DVT), such as unilateral leg swelling or tenderness
  • Examining the lower limbs is essential to assess for DVT, which may lead to pulmonary embolism.
  • Relevance: Positive findings of unilateral leg swelling, erythema, or tenderness suggest a possible DVT. Negative findings lower the suspicion for a pulmonary embolism.
Red Flag Synthesis

This section synthesises the key red flags from the source document. Any of these findings in a patient with chest pain necessitates urgent senior review and likely escalation of care.

Severe, Crushing Chest Pain

Significance: This could indicate a myocardial infarction, requiring urgent hospitalisation and intervention.

Sudden Onset Chest Pain

Significance: This could indicate aortic dissection or pulmonary embolism, both of which are life-threatening conditions requiring immediate assessment.

Pain Associated with Severe Shortness of Breath

Significance: This could signify pulmonary embolism, pneumothorax, or acute heart failure, all of which require urgent intervention.

Hypotension or Syncope

Significance: This could indicate conditions such as myocardial infarction, massive pulmonary embolism, or aortic dissection, which are medical emergencies requiring urgent evaluation.

Fever and Productive Cough

Significance: This could signify bacterial pneumonia or empyema, which may lead to sepsis if untreated, requiring immediate antibiotic therapy and possibly hospitalisation.

Hemoptysis (Coughing up Blood)

Significance: This could suggest pulmonary embolism, lung cancer, or tuberculosis, all of which are potentially life-threatening and require prompt diagnostic workup.

Neurological Deficit (e.g., Weakness, Numbness)

Significance: This could indicate spinal cord compression or aortic dissection with spinal involvement, both of which are surgical emergencies.

Unexplained Weight Loss or Dysphagia

Significance: This could indicate lung cancer or oesophageal cancer, both requiring urgent diagnostic investigation, including imaging and biopsy.

Investigations Strategy & Interpretation
Electrocardiogram (ECG)
  • An ECG is crucial in assessing for acute coronary syndrome (ACS) and other cardiac pathologies. It helps identify myocardial infarction, arrhythmias, and ischaemia.
  • Result Significance: Positive findings such as ST-segment elevation suggest myocardial infarction, while non-specific changes or a normal ECG may reduce suspicion for ACS but do not rule it out.
Troponin Levels
  • Troponins are cardiac enzymes released during myocardial injury, and their measurement is critical in diagnosing myocardial infarction.
  • Result Significance: Elevated troponins indicate myocardial infarction or cardiac damage. A negative result after serial testing can help rule out myocardial infarction.
Chest X-ray
  • A chest X-ray is important for evaluating pulmonary causes of chest pain, such as pneumonia, pneumothorax, and pleural effusion. It also helps assess for signs of heart failure.
  • Result Significance: Positive findings such as consolidation suggest pneumonia, while a normal chest X-ray can help rule out pneumothorax and pneumonia.
D-dimer
  • D-dimer testing is useful in assessing patients for thromboembolic events like pulmonary embolism (PE) and deep vein thrombosis (DVT).
  • Result Significance: A positive D-dimer result increases suspicion for PE or DVT, while a negative D-dimer in a low-risk patient can effectively rule out these diagnoses.
CT Pulmonary Angiography (CTPA)
  • CTPA is the gold standard for diagnosing pulmonary embolism, particularly in patients with elevated D-dimer or high clinical suspicion.
  • Result Significance: Positive findings confirm the presence of a pulmonary embolism, while a negative result rules out significant PE.
Arterial Blood Gas (ABG)
  • ABG testing is useful for evaluating patients with respiratory distress, providing information about oxygenation and acid-base balance. It is especially helpful in assessing conditions like pulmonary embolism and severe pneumonia.
  • Result Significance: Hypoxaemia and respiratory alkalosis may indicate pulmonary embolism, while acidosis may suggest underlying respiratory failure.
Full Blood Count (FBC)
  • FBC is important for identifying infections, anaemia, or other haematological causes of chest pain.
  • Result Significance: Leukocytosis suggests infection (e.g., pneumonia), while anaemia may exacerbate cardiac conditions or suggest gastrointestinal bleeding in the case of haematemesis.
Serum Electrolytes and Urea
  • Electrolytes are measured to assess kidney function, electrolyte imbalances, and their potential impact on cardiac function.
  • Result Significance: Abnormal electrolytes (e.g., hyperkalaemia) may precipitate arrhythmias. Elevated urea or creatinine can indicate underlying kidney disease, which may contribute to fluid overload or hypertension.
Oesophagogastroduodenoscopy (OGD)
  • An OGD may be indicated in patients with chest pain suggestive of gastrointestinal origin, particularly when GORD, peptic ulcer disease, or oesophageal cancer is suspected.
  • Result Significance: Positive findings may include oesophagitis, ulcers, or malignancy. A normal result helps rule out gastrointestinal causes of chest pain.
Echocardiography
  • Echocardiography provides visualisation of cardiac structure and function, making it essential for evaluating suspected heart failure, valvular disease, or pericardial effusion.
  • Result Significance: Positive findings such as reduced ejection fraction suggest heart failure, while pericardial fluid suggests pericarditis or effusion.
Evidence-Based Diagnosis (EBDx)
Elevated Troponin Levels in Myocardial Infarction
  • Elevated cardiac troponin levels are a critical biomarker for myocardial infarction (MI), indicating myocardial cell injury.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: ~79%, Specificity: ~93%, Positive Likelihood Ratio: 11.3.
  • Significance: A positive troponin result strongly suggests MI, necessitating urgent management. Negative results, especially when repeated after a few hours, make MI less likely but do not completely exclude it.
ST-Segment Elevation on ECG in Myocardial Infarction
  • ST-segment elevation indicates transmural ischaemia and is characteristic of STEMI (ST-Elevation Myocardial Infarction).
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: ~45%, Specificity: ~90%, Positive Likelihood Ratio: 4.5.
  • Significance: Presence of ST-segment elevation supports a diagnosis of STEMI, requiring immediate reperfusion therapy. Absence does not rule out MI, as NSTEMI may present without ST elevation.
D-dimer Testing in Suspected Pulmonary Embolism
  • D-dimer levels are elevated in the presence of a thrombus due to fibrin degradation.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: ~95%, Specificity: ~40%, Negative Likelihood Ratio: 0.13.
  • Significance: A negative D-dimer test in a low-risk patient effectively rules out pulmonary embolism. A positive result is non-specific and requires imaging for confirmation.
Wells Score for Assessing Pulmonary Embolism Risk
  • Clinical prediction tool estimating pre-test probability of PE.
  • Evidence: Wells score >6 indicates high risk; Sensitivity: ~40%, Specificity: ~80%.
  • Significance: High score prompts immediate investigation. Low score with negative D-dimer can rule out PE.
Chest X-ray Showing Consolidation in Pneumonia
  • Radiographic evidence of lung consolidation supports infection.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: ~75%, Specificity: ~90%.
  • Significance: Positive findings confirm pneumonia. A normal X-ray does not exclude early or mild cases.
Chest Wall Tenderness in Musculoskeletal Pain
  • Pain reproduced by palpation suggests musculoskeletal origin.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: ~50%, Specificity: ~85%.
  • Significance: Positive tenderness increases likelihood of musculoskeletal pain; absence does not rule out other causes.
Diagnostic Timeout & Cognitive Biases

Awareness of common thinking errors is essential to improve diagnostic accuracy and patient safety when assessing a patient with chest pain. Before finalising a diagnosis, it is good practice for clinicians at all levels to take a 'diagnostic timeout' to challenge their own reasoning.

  • Anchoring Bias: The tendency to lock onto initial features or a known diagnosis (e.g., a patient's known GORD) too early, failing to consider new, more serious possibilities even when contradictory evidence like exertional symptoms emerges. A key question to ask is: "What is the most dangerous thing this could be, and have I ruled it out?"
  • Confirmation Bias: Actively seeking out or overvaluing evidence that supports an initial belief while ignoring or downplaying evidence that refutes it (e.g., focusing on reproducible chest wall tenderness to confirm a musculoskeletal diagnosis while ignoring a new tachycardia).
  • Atypical Presentations: A major pitfall is forgetting that serious diseases can present non-classically. For example, ACS can present as epigastric pain or fatigue, especially in older patients, women, or people with diabetes. A high index of suspicion must be maintained in these groups.
  • Over-reliance on Normal Tests: Placing too much weight on a single reassuring but non-definitive test result. A single normal ECG or an initial normal troponin level does not definitively rule out ACS, as serial testing is often required to detect dynamic changes.
Illustrative Management Pathways

For educational purposes, this section describes the typical principles of management for key diagnoses. These are not prescriptive instructions. All management must be guided by official UK guidelines, local formularies, and senior clinical advice.

Acute Coronary Syndrome (ACS)

The initial management pathway for suspected ACS focuses on reducing myocardial oxygen demand, preventing further thrombus formation, and providing analgesia. This typically involves administering a loading dose of aspirin, often combined with another antiplatelet (e.g., clopidogrel or ticagrelor), providing sublingual or intravenous nitrates, and considering intravenous opiates for severe pain. Patients with ST-elevation MI (STEMI) require immediate reperfusion therapy, usually via primary percutaneous coronary intervention (PCI) in a dedicated heart attack centre.

Pulmonary Embolism (PE)

Once PE is confirmed, the mainstay of treatment is anticoagulation to prevent further clot formation. The choice of agent (e.g., a Direct Oral Anticoagulant like apixaban or rivaroxaban, or Low Molecular Weight Heparin) is guided by clinical guidelines and patient factors. In massive PE causing haemodynamic collapse, systemic thrombolysis ('clot busting' drugs) or surgical embolectomy is considered urgently in a secondary care setting.

Aortic Dissection

This is a surgical emergency requiring immediate transfer to a specialist cardiothoracic centre. The initial medical management, performed in an intensive care or resuscitation setting, is critical to prevent propagation of the dissection. It focuses on aggressive heart rate and blood pressure control. Intravenous beta-blockers are typically used first-line to reduce the heart rate and the force of cardiac contraction, followed by vasodilators if blood pressure remains high. This is a crucial bridge to definitive surgical repair.

Benign Causes (e.g., Musculoskeletal Pain, GORD)

Once life-threatening causes have been confidently excluded through a thorough assessment, management focuses on reassurance and symptomatic relief. For musculoskeletal pain, simple analgesia (e.g., paracetamol, topical or oral NSAIDs if appropriate) and advice on rest and gentle stretching are key. For suspected GORD, a trial of a proton pump inhibitor (PPI) and lifestyle advice regarding diet, alcohol, smoking, and weight management is a standard approach.

Reflection & Learning

These brief cases illustrate the application of clinical reasoning to different chest pain presentations. They are for educational purposes only.

Case 1: Suspected ACS in the Emergency Department

A 62-year-old man with type 2 diabetes presents to the Emergency Department with a 2-hour history of central chest tightness radiating to his jaw, associated with nausea and sweating. His ECG shows ST depression in leads V4-V6. The problem representation is an older male with cardiovascular risk factors presenting with ischaemic-sounding chest pain and dynamic ECG changes, highly suspicious for an NSTEMI. The immediate pathway involves aspirin, analgesia, serial troponins, and urgent cardiology referral for consideration of angiography.

Case 2: Suspected PE in General Practice

A 35-year-old woman on the combined oral contraceptive pill presents to her GP with a 1-day history of sudden-onset, sharp right-sided chest pain, worse on inspiration, and breathlessness. Her heart rate is 105 bpm and oxygen saturation is 93% on air. The problem representation is a young woman with a key risk factor (OCP) presenting with pleuritic chest pain and hypoxia, concerning for a PE. The GP arranges for immediate hospital admission for urgent assessment, likely with a CTPA.

Case 3: Musculoskeletal Pain in a Walk-in Centre

A 25-year-old man presents to a walk-in centre with localised, sharp left-sided chest pain after heavy lifting. The pain is worse on twisting and is reproducible on palpation over the 4th left costochondral junction. Vital signs and ECG are normal. The problem representation is a young, low-risk man with reproducible chest wall pain, most consistent with a musculoskeletal cause. After excluding red flags through a thorough history and examination, management involves reassurance, simple analgesia, and safety netting advice.
