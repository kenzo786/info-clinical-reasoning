Nasal Symptoms Diagnostic Reasoning Reference
Context: Nasal Symptoms Assessment
Overview

Nasal symptoms, including congestion, rhinorrhoea, sneezing, and epistaxis, are extremely common presentations in both primary care and emergency medicine. They range from self-limiting viral infections and allergic reactions to indicators of serious systemic or malignant disease. The central clinical challenge is to differentiate benign, common conditions from rare but critical 'must not miss' diagnoses through a structured and systematic approach. This requires a careful history and targeted examination to identify red flags that warrant further investigation or urgent referral.

This educational module is designed to build a foundational understanding of the clinical reasoning process for nasal symptoms. For definitive, real-time management protocols, users must refer to official UK guidelines from authoritative bodies such as NICE (e.g., NG80 for Allergic Rhinitis, NG79 for Sinusitis) and relevant professional bodies.

Pathophysiology/Basic Science

Understanding the origin of nasal symptoms is key to diagnosis. Symptoms can be generated through several distinct mechanisms, each pointing towards a different set of differential diagnoses:

  • Allergic Inflammatory Cascade: In allergic rhinitis, exposure to an allergen (e.g., pollen, dust mites) triggers IgE-mediated degranulation of mast cells in the nasal mucosa. This releases inflammatory mediators like histamine, leukotrienes, and prostaglandins, leading to vasodilation (causing congestion), increased vascular permeability (causing rhinorrhoea), and sensory nerve stimulation (causing itching and sneezing).
  • Infectious Processes: Viral upper respiratory tract infections (URTIs) cause direct cytopathic damage to the respiratory epithelium and trigger an acute inflammatory response. This results in mucosal oedema, increased mucus production, and initially clear rhinorrhoea. Secondary bacterial infection, often in the sinuses (sinusitis), can occur when drainage is obstructed, leading to the accumulation of purulent discharge and more pronounced inflammatory signs like facial pain and fever.
  • Vasomotor and Non-Allergic Mechanisms: Non-allergic rhinitis involves nasal nerve dysregulation. Triggers like temperature changes, strong odours, or hormonal shifts can cause parasympathetic overactivity, leading to vasodilation and glandular secretion without an allergic inflammatory basis. This results in persistent congestion and rhinorrhoea.
  • Structural Obstruction: Anatomical issues can physically impede airflow and mucus drainage. A deviated nasal septum, hypertrophied adenoids or turbinates, or the presence of nasal polyps (oedematous outgrowths of mucosa, often linked to chronic inflammation) can cause a sensation of blockage and contribute to recurrent sinusitis.
  • Vascular Fragility (Epistaxis): The nasal septum, particularly the anterior part (Kiesselbach's plexus), has a rich and superficial blood supply. Bleeding (epistaxis) can be caused by local trauma (e.g., nose-picking), mucosal dryness, inflammation, or underlying systemic issues like hypertension, coagulopathies (e.g., thrombocytopenia, liver disease), or the presence of highly vascular tumours.
  • Neoplastic Processes: Malignant tumours, such as nasopharyngeal carcinoma, cause symptoms by local invasion and mass effect. They can erode surrounding tissues, leading to persistent unilateral obstruction, blood-stained discharge, epistaxis, facial pain, or cranial nerve palsies as the tumour expands.
Differential Diagnosis (DDx) Framework

A structured approach to the differential diagnosis of nasal symptoms is essential to ensure life-threatening conditions are prioritised.

Must not miss!
  • Nasopharyngeal cancer: Often presents with persistent unilateral nasal obstruction, epistaxis, and sometimes associated with neck lumps or cranial nerve palsies.
  • Leukaemia: May present with recurrent or severe epistaxis and easy bruising due to thrombocytopenia as part of bone marrow failure.
  • Septal haematoma: A collection of blood under the perichondrium of the nasal septum, usually following trauma, which can lead to cartilage necrosis if not drained urgently.
  • Liver cirrhosis: Can cause recurrent epistaxis due to impaired production of clotting factors and thrombocytopenia from hypersplenism.
  • Thrombocytopenia: A low platelet count from any cause, leading to an increased risk of spontaneous or prolonged bleeding, including severe epistaxis.
Common causes
  • URTI: An acute viral infection of the upper respiratory tract causing nasal congestion, clear rhinorrhoea, sneezing, and often a mild cough or sore throat.
  • Allergic rhinitis: An IgE-mediated inflammation of the nasal passages causing paroxysms of sneezing, itching, rhinorrhoea, and congestion, often with a seasonal or specific trigger.
  • Non-allergic rhinitis: A condition characterised by chronic sneezing or a congested, drippy nose without an apparent allergic reaction.
  • Nasal polyps: Benign, soft, noncancerous growths on the lining of the nasal passages or sinuses, often associated with chronic inflammation and causing obstruction.
  • Adenoid hyperplasia: Enlargement of the adenoid tissue in the nasopharynx, common in children, leading to chronic nasal obstruction and mouth breathing.
  • Sinusitis: Inflammation of the paranasal sinuses, which can be acute or chronic, presenting with purulent nasal discharge, facial pain/pressure, and obstruction.
  • Nasal injury/septal deviation: Trauma to the nose can cause a deviation of the septum, leading to chronic unilateral or bilateral nasal obstruction.
  • COVID-19: Can present with a wide range of symptoms, including nasal congestion, rhinorrhoea, and loss of smell (anosmia), often with fever and cough.
Easily missed
  • Medication induced Nasal Symptoms: Can be caused by overuse of decongestant sprays (rhinitis medicamentosa) or as a side effect of drugs like beta-blockers or NSAIDs.
  • Bleeding disorders: Inherited or acquired conditions like von Willebrand disease that can present with recurrent and difficult-to-control epistaxis.
  • Foreign body: Most common in children, presenting with unilateral, foul-smelling nasal discharge and obstruction.
Other Differential Diagnoses to consider
  • Wegener’s granulomatosis (Granulomatosis with Polyangiitis): Systemic vasculitis often presenting with nasal and sinus involvement
  • Sarcoidosis: Multisystem disease, can cause granulomas in the nasal mucosa
  • Hypothyroidism: Can cause nasal congestion due to mucosal oedema
  • Cystic fibrosis: Can lead to chronic sinusitis and nasal polyps
  • Autoimmune disorders: Conditions like Sjogren’s syndrome can present with nasal dryness and crusting
  • Vascular malformations: Rare causes of recurrent epistaxis
History Taking
Patient’s Description: How can I help you today?
Starting with open-ended questions such as “Can you tell me more about what brought you here today?” helps the patient describe their nasal symptoms in their own words. Relevance: Allows the patient to focus on what they perceive as the main issue, which can guide more specific history-taking questions. It helps identify key characteristics such as onset, duration, or associated factors.
When did the nasal symptoms start?
  • This question helps to establish the timing of the symptoms. Acute symptoms may suggest an URTI or acute sinusitis, whereas chronic symptoms could indicate allergic rhinitis or nasal polyps.
  • Relevance: Acute onset suggests an infection like URTI or COVID-19, while chronic onset may indicate allergic rhinitis, polyps, or malignancy.
Have you noticed anything that seems to trigger or worsen your symptoms?
  • Explores potential triggers such as pollen, dust, or weather changes that may suggest allergic rhinitis. Alternatively, certain medications or irritants could exacerbate non-allergic rhinitis.
  • Relevance: Positive response with allergens suggests allergic rhinitis, while irritants or medications suggest non-allergic rhinitis or medication-induced symptoms.
Do you have any other associated symptoms like cough, fever, or facial pain?
  • Helps to differentiate between upper respiratory tract infections, sinusitis, and allergic conditions. Facial pain and pressure are more suggestive of sinusitis, while fever might indicate an infection like URTI or COVID-19.
  • Relevance: Fever suggests infection (URTI, sinusitis, or COVID-19). Facial pain and pressure suggest sinusitis. Cough might be seen in URTI or postnasal drip due to rhinitis.
Have you experienced any nosebleeds or changes in the appearance of your nose?
  • Nosebleeds (epistaxis) can be linked to trauma, nasal polyps, septal deviation, or serious conditions like malignancy, thrombocytopenia, or cirrhosis. Changes in the nasal structure or appearance can raise suspicion for malignancy.
  • Relevance: Nosebleeds could indicate hypertension, nasal injury, or malignancy. Structural changes might be linked to nasopharyngeal cancer or granulomatous conditions like Wegener’s.
Any recent medication changes?
  • Relevant to rule out medication-induced nasal symptoms, such as from nasal decongestants (causing rebound congestion), anticoagulants (epistaxis), or NSAIDs (rhinitis).
  • Relevance: Positive response could indicate medication-induced symptoms, especially nasal decongestant overuse (rhinitis medicamentosa) or anticoagulant-associated epistaxis.
Do you have a family history of allergies, nasal polyps, or sinus problems?
  • Exploring family history can help identify hereditary conditions like allergic rhinitis or conditions such as cystic fibrosis, which can present with nasal polyps.
  • Relevance: Family history of allergies suggests allergic rhinitis, while polyps or cystic fibrosis in the family can indicate a hereditary cause of nasal polyps or chronic sinusitis.
Associated Symptom Question: Do you feel congested, or do you have difficulty breathing through your nose?
  • Congestion or nasal obstruction is a common symptom of rhinitis (allergic or non-allergic), sinusitis, or nasal polyps.
  • Relevance: Positive response suggests allergic rhinitis, sinusitis, or nasal polyps. Negative response may steer towards less obstructive conditions like mild URTI.
Ideas, Concerns, and Expectations (ICE)
  • Exploring ICE questions helps to address the patient's beliefs, worries, and hopes regarding their nasal symptoms.
  • Relevance: This can reveal if the patient is concerned about conditions like cancer (if nosebleeds or structural changes are present), serious infection (fever, fatigue), or if they expect simple relief for rhinitis or sinusitis. Addressing ICE can enhance shared decision-making and patient satisfaction.
Examination Strategy
Inspection of the External Nose
  • Examine the external structure of the nose for any visible deformity, swelling, or trauma. Check for nasal deviation, redness, or obvious masses.
  • Relevance: Positive findings such as nasal deformity or septal deviation suggest traumatic injury or congenital deformity. A negative finding may rule out significant external pathology.
Palpation of the Nasal Bridge and Sinuses
  • Palpate the nasal bridge and maxillary and frontal sinuses to assess for tenderness or swelling, which can indicate sinusitis or trauma.
  • Relevance: Tenderness over the sinuses suggests sinusitis, while a lack of tenderness can rule out significant sinus involvement.
Anterior Rhinoscopy
  • Using a nasal speculum or otoscope, inspect the nasal mucosa for erythema, oedema, nasal discharge, or polyps. Assess the nasal septum for deviation or perforation.
  • Relevance: Erythema and oedema suggest rhinitis (allergic or non-allergic). Polyps may indicate chronic inflammation or nasal polyposis. A deviated septum could explain obstructive symptoms. Clear discharge might suggest allergic rhinitis, while purulent discharge is consistent with infection.
Transillumination of the Sinuses
  • Place a light source against the maxillary or frontal sinuses to check for normal transillumination, which can be reduced in cases of fluid retention or thickened mucosa (e.g., in sinusitis).
  • Relevance: Reduced or absent transillumination suggests sinusitis. Normal transillumination may help rule out significant sinus congestion or fluid build-up.
Inspection of the Oropharynx and Throat
  • Inspect the throat for signs of postnasal drip, erythema, or enlarged tonsils, which could suggest ongoing infection or allergic rhinitis.
  • Relevance: Postnasal drip and erythema suggest allergic rhinitis or sinusitis. Enlarged tonsils with exudate may indicate bacterial infection, such as streptococcal pharyngitis.
Examination of the Cervical Lymph Nodes
  • Palpate the cervical lymph nodes for enlargement or tenderness, which could suggest an infection or, less commonly, malignancy.
  • Relevance: Enlarged and tender lymph nodes suggest infection (e.g., viral URTI or bacterial sinusitis), while painless and firm nodes might suggest malignancy or a chronic condition such as lymphoma or leukaemia.
Examination of the Ears
  • Inspect the tympanic membranes for signs of effusion or infection, as eustachian tube dysfunction can accompany nasal congestion.
  • Relevance: Middle ear effusion or retraction suggests eustachian tube dysfunction, which is commonly associated with rhinitis or sinusitis.
Examination of the Skin (Face and Nose)
  • Look for rashes, erythema, or nasal ulcers, which may suggest systemic conditions such as sarcoidosis, Wegener's granulomatosis, or lupus.
  • Relevance: Skin changes like ulcers or rashes may point towards systemic conditions such as vasculitis (e.g., Wegener's), sarcoidosis, or autoimmune disorders.
Red Flag Synthesis
Unilateral Nasal Obstruction or Bleeding
  • Unilateral symptoms, especially persistent obstruction or bleeding, may suggest a serious underlying condition such as malignancy or a nasopharyngeal tumour.
  • Significance: This could indicate nasopharyngeal cancer or other malignancies, which require prompt referral for imaging and biopsy.
Persistent or Recurrent Nosebleeds (Epistaxis)
  • Frequent or prolonged epistaxis may indicate a bleeding disorder, thrombocytopenia, or even malignancies such as leukaemia. It may also be a sign of uncontrolled hypertension or liver disease.
  • Significance: Recurrent or severe nosebleeds can suggest conditions like leukaemia, thrombocytopenia, or liver cirrhosis, all of which require urgent evaluation and management.
Facial Pain, Swelling, or Double Vision
  • Severe facial pain, swelling, or visual disturbances suggest invasive sinus disease, including malignancy or orbital complications of sinusitis such as orbital cellulitis or abscess.
  • Significance: These symptoms can indicate an orbital complication of sinusitis or tumour invasion, both of which require immediate specialist referral and imaging.
Neurological Symptoms (e.g., Vision Changes, Headache)
  • Headaches, vision changes, or other neurological symptoms alongside nasal symptoms can suggest intracranial extension of infection or malignancy (e.g., invasion of the cranial nerves or brain).
  • Significance: This could indicate life-threatening conditions like intracranial extension of a sinus infection (e.g., meningitis, abscess) or malignancy, requiring immediate neuroimaging and intervention.
Hard Palate or Nasal Ulcers That Do Not Heal
  • Persistent or non-healing ulcers in the nasal cavity or hard palate could indicate granulomatous diseases (e.g., Wegener’s granulomatosis) or malignancies.
  • Significance: These symptoms may suggest an aggressive malignancy or granulomatous disease, both of which require urgent investigation and possible biopsy.
Weight Loss or Night Sweats
  • Constitutional symptoms like unexplained weight loss or night sweats alongside nasal symptoms raise suspicion for systemic illnesses such as cancer (e.g., lymphoma, leukaemia) or chronic infection (e.g., tuberculosis).
  • Significance: These systemic symptoms could indicate malignancy, including haematological cancers such as lymphoma or leukaemia, warranting urgent full blood count and imaging.
Proptosis or Bulging Eye
  • Proptosis or a bulging eye alongside nasal symptoms can suggest orbital invasion from an adjacent malignancy or severe orbital complications of sinusitis.
  • Significance: This could indicate an aggressive malignancy or an orbital abscess, both of which require urgent imaging and intervention.
Investigations Strategy & Interpretation
Nasal Endoscopy
  • Direct visualisation of the nasal cavity and sinuses using a flexible endoscope can help identify structural abnormalities, nasal polyps, masses, or other obstructions.
  • Result Significance: Positive findings of masses or polyps suggest nasal polyposis, malignancy, or chronic rhinosinusitis. A negative result may help rule out these structural causes of nasal obstruction or discharge.
Full Blood Count (FBC)
  • FBC is useful for identifying signs of infection, inflammation, or haematological disorders such as leukaemia or thrombocytopenia.
  • Result Significance: Elevated white cell count suggests infection or inflammation (e.g., sinusitis or URTI). Thrombocytopenia could explain recurrent nosebleeds and may suggest a bleeding disorder or leukaemia.
CT Scan of the Sinuses
  • A CT scan provides detailed images of the sinuses, useful for identifying sinusitis, structural abnormalities, or masses not visible on endoscopy. It is typically indicated for chronic or recurrent sinusitis and suspected malignancy.
  • Result Significance: Positive findings such as fluid levels, mucosal thickening, or masses suggest sinusitis, nasal polyps, or malignancy. A negative result may help rule out significant structural causes of nasal symptoms.
Allergy Testing (Skin Prick Test or Specific IgE Blood Test)
  • Allergy testing is important in suspected allergic rhinitis. It helps to identify specific allergens causing the nasal symptoms, such as pollen, dust mites, or pet dander.
  • Result Significance: Positive results confirm allergic rhinitis and guide allergen avoidance or immunotherapy. A negative result makes allergic rhinitis less likely, pointing towards other causes like non-allergic rhinitis or structural issues.
Nasal Swab for Microbial Culture
  • Nasal swabs can be taken to identify bacterial or viral pathogens in cases of suspected infectious rhinitis or sinusitis. They are especially useful when symptoms are prolonged or recurrent.
  • Result Significance: Positive results for pathogens like Staphylococcus aureus or Haemophilus influenzae support a diagnosis of bacterial sinusitis. Negative results might suggest non-infectious causes of nasal symptoms.
Coagulation Profile (PT, APTT)
  • Coagulation studies assess clotting function and are important in patients presenting with recurrent or unexplained epistaxis. They help identify bleeding disorders or the effects of anticoagulant therapy.
  • Result Significance: Abnormal results suggest coagulopathies, such as von Willebrand disease or liver dysfunction, both of which can lead to recurrent nosebleeds. Normal results make bleeding disorders less likely.
Biopsy of Suspicious Nasal Lesions
  • In cases where a mass or ulcer is found on nasal examination, biopsy is crucial to determine whether it is malignant or benign.
  • Result Significance: Positive biopsy findings for malignancy confirm nasopharyngeal cancer or other tumours. Negative results may rule out cancer but require further evaluation to determine the cause of the lesion.
COVID-19 PCR or Lateral Flow Test
  • COVID-19 testing should be considered in patients with nasal congestion, rhinorrhoea, and other symptoms such as fever or cough, given the possibility of atypical presentations.
  • Result Significance: A positive test confirms COVID-19 infection, which may present with mild nasal symptoms, while a negative result helps rule out this increasingly common differential.
Evidence-Based Diagnosis (EBDx)
Purulent Nasal Discharge in Sinusitis
  • Purulent nasal discharge is a key indicator of acute bacterial sinusitis, especially when it persists for more than 10 days or worsens after initial improvement (double-sickening).
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 77%, Specificity: 73%, Likelihood Ratio (LR) of a positive test: 2.8.
  • Reference: Rosenfeld RM, et al. Clinical practice guideline: adult sinusitis. Otolaryngol Head Neck Surg. 2015.
  • Significance: A positive finding increases the likelihood of bacterial sinusitis, while its absence suggests a non-infectious cause like allergic rhinitis or viral URTI.
Facial Pain or Pressure in Acute Sinusitis
  • Facial pain, especially when it worsens with bending forward, is a classic sign of sinusitis. It helps differentiate sinusitis from other causes of nasal symptoms like rhinitis.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 64%, Specificity: 77%, LR of a positive test: 2.8.
  • Reference: Hansen JG, et al. Diagnosis of acute rhinosinusitis: the role of clinical criteria and imaging. Eur J Clin Microbiol Infect Dis. 2004.
  • Significance: Positive findings suggest acute bacterial sinusitis, while their absence may indicate a less severe or non-infectious condition.
Nasal Polyps in Chronic Rhinosinusitis
  • The presence of nasal polyps is strongly associated with chronic rhinosinusitis, especially when combined with symptoms like nasal obstruction, loss of smell, and facial pain.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 65%, Specificity: 85%, LR of a positive test: 4.3.
  • Reference: Fokkens WJ, et al. European Position Paper on Rhinosinusitis and Nasal Polyps 2020. Rhinology. 2020.
  • Significance: The detection of nasal polyps increases the likelihood of chronic rhinosinusitis, especially with prolonged symptoms. Absence of polyps suggests other causes such as allergic rhinitis or viral URTI.
Positive Skin Prick Test in Allergic Rhinitis
  • A positive skin prick test or specific IgE test confirms sensitisation to allergens in patients with nasal symptoms, helping differentiate allergic from non-allergic rhinitis.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 85%, Specificity: 90%, LR of a positive test: 8.5.
  • Reference: Bousquet J, et al. Allergic rhinitis and its impact on asthma (ARIA) guidelines. J Allergy Clin Immunol. 2010.
  • Significance: Positive findings confirm allergic rhinitis, while negative results make allergic rhinitis unlikely, pointing towards other causes like non-allergic rhinitis or sinusitis.
Abnormal CT Sinus Findings in Chronic Sinusitis
  • A CT scan is the gold standard for diagnosing chronic sinusitis, revealing mucosal thickening, air-fluid levels, or sinus opacification.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 93%, Specificity: 85%, LR of a positive test: 6.2.
  • Reference: DeMarcantonio MA, Han JK. The role of sinus computed tomography in the diagnosis of chronic rhinosinusitis. Curr Opin Otolaryngol Head Neck Surg. 2011.
  • Significance: Positive findings strongly suggest chronic sinusitis, while negative findings can help rule out sinusitis as the cause of chronic nasal symptoms.
Septal Deviation on Nasal Endoscopy
  • Septal deviation is a structural cause of nasal obstruction and can be identified during nasal endoscopy. This helps to distinguish mechanical causes of nasal symptoms from inflammatory or infectious causes.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 75%, Specificity: 70%, LR of a positive test: 2.5.
  • Reference: Hol M, et al. Evaluation of nasal obstruction: patient and clinician perspectives. Ear Nose Throat J. 2010.
  • Significance: Positive findings suggest that septal deviation is contributing to the patient's symptoms, and surgical correction may be needed. Negative results make structural causes less likely.
Diagnostic Timeout & Cognitive Biases

Awareness of common thinking errors is essential to improve diagnostic accuracy and patient safety when assessing a patient with nasal symptoms. Before finalising a diagnosis, it is good practice for clinicians at all levels to take a 'diagnostic timeout' to challenge their own reasoning.

  • Anchoring Bias: The tendency to lock onto a common diagnosis like allergic rhinitis too early in a patient with chronic nasal symptoms, failing to consider other possibilities like chronic rhinosinusitis, nasal polyps, or structural issues, even when symptoms don't respond to antihistamines. A key question is: "Could this be something other than an allergy?"
  • Confirmation Bias: Actively seeking out or overvaluing evidence that supports an initial belief while ignoring contradictory information. For example, focusing on a history of recent cold exposure to diagnose a simple URTI while downplaying the significance of persistent unilateral, blood-stained discharge, which is a red flag for malignancy.
  • Premature Closure: Accepting a diagnosis before it is fully verified. For instance, diagnosing acute sinusitis based on facial pain and purulent discharge without considering that a foreign body in a child, or a dental abscess in an adult, can present similarly. Have all relevant red flags been actively excluded?
  • Availability Heuristic: Overestimating the likelihood of diseases that are more recent or memorable. For example, attributing all new-onset nasal congestion and anosmia to COVID-19 without taking a full history that might suggest an alternative diagnosis, especially if other cardinal COVID-19 symptoms are absent.
Illustrative Management Pathways

For educational purposes, this section describes the typical principles of management for key diagnoses. These are not prescriptive instructions. All management must be guided by official UK guidelines, local formularies, and senior clinical advice.

Allergic Rhinitis

Management focuses on patient education, allergen avoidance, and pharmacological treatment. First-line therapy is typically an intranasal corticosteroid spray, which is highly effective for congestion, rhinorrhoea, and sneezing. Non-sedating oral antihistamines can be added for prominent itching or sneezing. Patients with severe or refractory symptoms may be referred for consideration of immunotherapy.

Acute Bacterial Rhinosinusitis

Most cases of acute sinusitis are viral and self-limiting. Management is primarily symptomatic with simple analgesia and advice on nasal saline irrigation. According to NICE guidelines, antibiotics are not recommended for symptoms lasting less than 10 days unless the patient is systemically unwell or at high risk of complications. If antibiotics are indicated, a 5-day course of phenoxymethylpenicillin is the typical first-line choice.

Epistaxis (Anterior Bleed)

Initial management involves first aid measures: the patient should sit upright, lean forward, and apply firm, continuous pressure to the soft fleshy part of the nose for 10-15 minutes. If bleeding persists, hospital assessment is required. In the emergency setting, treatment may involve topical vasoconstrictors, chemical cautery with a silver nitrate stick if a bleeding point is visible, or anterior nasal packing.

Nasal Foreign Body

This is a common presentation in young children and requires prompt removal to prevent complications like sinusitis, pressure necrosis, or aspiration. Removal is often attempted in a primary care or emergency setting using techniques like the 'parent's kiss' or fine instruments (e.g., forceps or a hook) if the object is clearly visible and accessible. If initial attempts fail or the object is not visible, urgent ENT referral is required for removal under controlled conditions.

Reflection & Learning

These brief cases illustrate the application of clinical reasoning to different nasal symptom presentations. They are for educational purposes only.

Case 1: Suspected Allergic Rhinitis in Primary Care

A 22-year-old university student presents to her GP in late spring with a 3-week history of itchy, watery eyes, episodes of intense sneezing, and a constantly running, clear nose. She has a history of childhood eczema. The problem representation is a young adult with a history of atopy presenting with seasonal, bilateral nasal and ocular symptoms, highly consistent with allergic rhinitis. Management would focus on advising a regular intranasal corticosteroid spray and a non-sedating antihistamine.

Case 2: Acute Sinusitis post-URTI

A 45-year-old man presents to a walk-in centre. He developed a typical head cold 12 days ago which seemed to be improving, but for the past 3 days he has felt much worse, with a fever of 38.5°C, severe pain and pressure over his right cheek which is worse on bending forward, and thick, green nasal discharge. The problem representation is a middle-aged man with 'double-sickening' following a URTI, now with systemic symptoms and classic features of acute bacterial rhinosinusitis. Following NICE guidelines, a prescription for antibiotics would be appropriate alongside advice on analgesia.

Case 3: Unilateral Symptoms requiring Urgent Referral

A 68-year-old man with a 40-pack-year smoking history presents to his GP with a 3-month history of a completely blocked left nostril and occasional blood-streaked discharge from that side. He has not had any trauma. On examination, his left nasal passage appears obstructed by a fleshy mass. The problem representation is an older man with a significant risk factor for malignancy presenting with persistent, progressive, unilateral red flag symptoms. This presentation is highly suspicious for nasopharyngeal cancer, and an urgent two-week-wait referral to an ENT specialist is mandatory.
