Sore Throat Diagnostic Reasoning Reference
Context: Sore Throat Assessment
Overview

Sore throat is an extremely common presentation in both primary care and emergency settings, accounting for a significant proportion of GP consultations. While the vast majority of cases are caused by benign, self-limiting viral infections, the primary clinical challenge lies in identifying the small but significant number of patients with more serious pathology. This includes severe bacterial infections that may lead to systemic illness or airway compromise, and underlying malignancies that can present with persistent throat symptoms.

A systematic and structured approach is therefore crucial. The clinician must efficiently differentiate between common viral upper respiratory tract infections and conditions requiring specific intervention, such as bacterial tonsillitis, peritonsillar abscess (quinsy), or epiglottitis. Furthermore, a high index of suspicion for red flag symptoms is necessary to ensure timely referral and investigation for potential cancers of the head and neck. This educational module provides a foundational framework for the clinical reasoning process in assessing a patient with a sore throat. For definitive management, clinicians must consult current UK guidelines, such as NICE guideline [NG84] on Sore Throat (acute): antimicrobial prescribing.

Pathophysiology/Basic Science

Understanding the underlying mechanisms of sore throat is fundamental to forming an accurate differential diagnosis. The sensation of pain can originate from several processes:

  • Infectious Inflammation: This is the most common cause. Viruses (like rhinovirus, adenovirus, influenza, Epstein-Barr virus) or bacteria (primarily Group A Streptococcus) invade the mucosal lining of the pharynx and/or tonsils. The host immune response triggers the release of inflammatory mediators (e.g., prostaglandins, bradykinin), leading to vasodilation, increased vascular permeability, and oedema. This inflammatory cascade directly stimulates local nociceptors, causing erythema, swelling, and the characteristic pain, which is often worsened by swallowing.
  • Abscess Formation: In some bacterial infections, such as tonsillitis, the infection can spread from the tonsillar capsule into the surrounding soft tissues, forming a collection of pus. This is known as a peritonsillar abscess or 'quinsy'. The expanding abscess creates significant localised pressure and intense inflammation, causing severe unilateral pain, dysphagia, and trismus (difficulty opening the mouth) due to inflammation of the nearby pterygoid muscles. A similar process in the deep neck spaces can lead to a retropharyngeal abscess.
  • Chemical Irritation: Non-infectious inflammation can occur due to chemical irritants. In Gastro-Oesophageal Reflux Disease (GORD) or more specifically Laryngopharyngeal Reflux (LPR), stomach acid and pepsin reflux into the pharynx and larynx. This directly irritates the delicate mucosa, which is not adapted to such a low pH, causing a chronic, often burning sensation, particularly in the mornings.
  • Mucosal Ulceration: A break in the mucosal surface can directly expose underlying nerves, causing sharp pain. This can be seen in viral infections like Herpes Simplex (stomatitis) or Coxsackie virus (Hand, Foot, and Mouth Disease), or in autoimmune conditions such as aphthous ulceration or Behçet's disease.
  • Neoplastic Invasion: Malignant tumours (e.g., squamous cell carcinoma) in the oropharynx, nasopharynx, or larynx cause pain through several mechanisms. The tumour can directly invade and destroy local tissue, cause ulcerative lesions that become secondarily infected, or compress or invade sensory nerves, leading to persistent, localised, or referred pain. Associated mass effect can also cause dysphagia and a feeling of a lump in the throat.
Differential Diagnosis (DDx) Framework

A structured approach to the differential diagnosis of sore throat is essential to ensure life-threatening conditions are prioritised.

Must not miss!
  • Peritonsillar abscess: A collection of pus behind the tonsil, causing severe unilateral pain, fever, and difficulty opening the mouth (trismus).
  • Retropharyngeal abscess: A deep neck space infection, often in children, presenting with fever, neck stiffness, and potential airway compromise.
  • Epiglottitis: A life-threatening infection causing inflammation and swelling of the epiglottis, leading to rapid onset of stridor and breathing difficulties.
  • HIV (acute retroviral syndrome): Can present with a flu-like or glandular fever-like illness, including a sore throat, fever, rash, and lymphadenopathy.
  • Oral cancer: May present as a persistent, non-healing ulcer or lump in the mouth or throat, often associated with smoking and alcohol use.
  • Nasopharyngeal cancer: Can cause persistent sore throat, unilateral ear symptoms, nasal blockage, or enlarged neck lymph nodes.
  • Agranulocytosis (from DMARDs): A severe drop in white blood cells caused by certain medications, leading to a severe sore throat and risk of overwhelming infection.
Common causes
  • URTI (common cold): A mild viral infection of the upper respiratory tract, typically causing a sore throat alongside coryzal symptoms.
  • Pharyngitis: Inflammation of the pharynx, usually viral, causing a diffuse sore throat and redness.
  • Tonsillitis: Inflammation of the tonsils, which can be viral or bacterial, causing sore throat, fever, and sometimes tonsillar exudates.
  • COVID-19: A viral infection that commonly presents with a sore throat, often accompanied by cough, fever, and loss of taste or smell.
  • Influenza: A viral respiratory illness causing a sore throat along with prominent systemic symptoms like fever, muscle aches, and fatigue.
  • Rhinosinusitis: Inflammation of the sinuses causing post-nasal drip, which can irritate the throat and lead to soreness.
  • GORD: Stomach acid refluxing into the throat, causing a burning sensation or chronic irritation, often worse at night.
  • Aphthous ulceration: The presence of common, painful, recurrent mouth ulcers on the oral mucosa.
  • Oral candidiasis: A fungal infection (thrush) causing white patches in the mouth and throat, common in infants or the immunosuppressed.
  • Herpes stomatitis: A primary herpes simplex virus infection causing painful vesicles and ulcers throughout the oral cavity and pharynx.
Easily missed
  • Glandular fever: An infection caused by the Epstein-Barr virus, presenting with severe sore throat, fever, fatigue, and widespread lymphadenopathy.
Other Differential Diagnoses to consider
  • COVID-19: Common viral infection that can present with sore throat, fever, and systemic symptoms.
  • Post-nasal drip: Irritation from chronic rhinitis or sinusitis causing throat discomfort.
  • Allergic rhinitis: Allergic inflammation causing throat irritation and symptoms like sneezing or nasal congestion.
  • Laryngopharyngeal reflux (LPR): Reflux of stomach acid into the throat, causing irritation and soreness.
  • Scarlet fever: Bacterial infection (often caused by Streptococcus) presenting with sore throat and characteristic rash.
  • Thyroiditis: Inflammation of the thyroid gland causing throat pain and swelling, often with systemic symptoms.
  • Kawasaki disease: Rare vasculitis primarily in children, presenting with sore throat, fever, and mucosal changes.
  • Stevens-Johnson syndrome: Severe hypersensitivity reaction with mucosal involvement, including sore throat and mouth ulcers.
  • Behçet's disease: Rare inflammatory condition characterised by recurrent oral and genital ulcers, including sore throat.
History Taking
When did the sore throat start? Has it been getting better or worse?
Asking about the onset and progression of symptoms helps to determine whether the sore throat is acute, subacute, or chronic. A rapid onset might indicate viral or bacterial infections, while a more prolonged course could suggest GORD or chronic irritants. Relevance: An acute onset with fever may point towards infections like tonsillitis or glandular fever. Gradual worsening with weight loss could raise suspicion of malignancy.
Have you noticed any triggers, or does anything make it better or worse?
Exploring exacerbating or relieving factors helps differentiate between infectious and non-infectious causes. For instance, sore throat worsened by lying flat may indicate GORD. Relevance: Aggravation by swallowing could suggest pharyngitis or tonsillitis, while improvement with antacids points towards reflux-related causes.
Do you have a fever or chills?
Infectious causes of sore throat, such as tonsillitis or glandular fever, are often associated with fever. Relevance: A positive response suggests an infection, while a lack of fever may point to non-infectious causes like GORD or thyroiditis.
Do you have any difficulty swallowing or breathing?
Dysphagia or dyspnoea can be red flags indicating serious conditions like epiglottitis, peritonsillar abscess, or malignancy. Relevance: Difficulty swallowing may point to peritonsillar abscess or advanced pharyngitis, while difficulty breathing could be a sign of airway compromise, requiring urgent intervention.
Have you noticed any associated symptoms like a cough, nasal congestion, or ear pain?
A cough or nasal congestion may indicate a viral upper respiratory infection, while referred ear pain could suggest tonsillitis or peritonsillar abscess. Relevance: A cough suggests viral causes or GORD, while referred ear pain could point towards a localised infection in the throat, such as tonsillitis or abscess formation.
Any recent contact with someone who has been ill or any recent travel?
Travel history or contact with ill individuals can help identify infectious causes, such as viral pharyngitis, glandular fever, or COVID-19. Relevance: Recent contact with ill individuals supports an infectious aetiology, while travel history may raise the possibility of less common infections.
Do you have any known medical conditions or take any regular medications?
Understanding the patient’s past medical history and drug history helps identify risk factors such as immunosuppression (which increases the risk of oral candidiasis) or drug-induced conditions (e.g., agranulocytosis from DMARDs). Relevance: Immunosuppressed patients may be at risk for opportunistic infections like oral candidiasis, and those on medications like methotrexate or clozapine are at risk of agranulocytosis.
Ideas, Concerns, and Expectations (ICE)
Exploring the patient's Ideas, Concerns, and Expectations helps address their underlying worries and ensures the consultation is patient-centred. Relevance: Understanding the patient’s thoughts on the cause of their symptoms (Ideas), specific worries (Concerns), and what they hope to achieve from the consultation (Expectations) helps in managing both the medical and emotional aspects of the case.
Examination Strategy
Inspection of the throat
  • Direct inspection of the throat helps to visualise the tonsils, uvula, soft palate, and posterior pharynx. Look for erythema, exudates, swelling, or ulceration.
  • Relevance: Erythema and tonsillar exudates suggest bacterial pharyngitis or tonsillitis. Unilateral tonsillar swelling and uvular deviation may indicate a peritonsillar abscess. Negative findings can rule out more severe local infections like peritonsillar abscess or epiglottitis.
Palpation of the neck (Lymph nodes)
  • Palpating for lymphadenopathy helps identify enlarged or tender lymph nodes in the cervical and submandibular areas.
  • Relevance: Tender, enlarged lymph nodes are common in bacterial or viral pharyngitis (e.g., glandular fever). Lack of lymphadenopathy reduces the likelihood of glandular fever. Persistent, hard, non-tender lymphadenopathy may raise concern for malignancies such as nasopharyngeal cancer.
Examination of the oral mucosa and tongue
  • Examine the buccal mucosa, gingivae, and tongue for ulcers, plaques, or vesicles.
  • Relevance: White plaques may suggest oral candidiasis, especially in immunocompromised patients. Vesicles or ulcers could indicate herpes stomatitis or aphthous ulcers. A clean mucosa makes infections like oral candidiasis or herpes less likely.
Assess for stridor or respiratory distress
  • Stridor is a high-pitched inspiratory sound indicating upper airway obstruction, while signs of respiratory distress (e.g., use of accessory muscles, tachypnoea) may signal airway compromise.
  • Relevance: Stridor or respiratory distress suggests serious conditions like epiglottitis or retropharyngeal abscess. Absence of these signs can help rule out life-threatening upper airway obstruction.
Palpation of the thyroid gland
  • Palpating the thyroid helps detect any enlargement, nodules, or tenderness, which may be relevant if subacute thyroiditis or thyroid masses are considered in the differential.
  • Relevance: A tender thyroid may indicate subacute thyroiditis, while a firm mass could suggest malignancy. A normal thyroid palpation reduces the likelihood of thyroid-related causes.
Ear examination (otoscopy)
  • Examine the tympanic membranes and external auditory canals to rule out concurrent ear infections or referred pain from a throat infection.
  • Relevance: Red, bulging tympanic membranes may suggest otitis media, which can present with referred throat pain. A normal otoscopy makes ear-related causes of the sore throat less likely.
Abdominal palpation (spleen and liver)
  • Palpating for splenomegaly or hepatomegaly is important in cases of glandular fever (infectious mononucleosis), which often presents with these findings.
  • Relevance: Splenomegaly or hepatomegaly strongly suggests glandular fever. Absence of these findings makes infectious mononucleosis less likely, but not impossible.
Red Flag Synthesis
Severe difficulty swallowing or breathing
  • Severe dysphagia (difficulty swallowing) or dyspnoea (difficulty breathing) can suggest significant upper airway obstruction.
  • Significance: This could indicate life-threatening conditions such as epiglottitis, peritonsillar abscess, or retropharyngeal abscess, all of which require urgent intervention to prevent airway compromise.
Stridor
  • Stridor is a high-pitched sound heard during inspiration, indicating upper airway obstruction.
  • Significance: Stridor is a red flag for conditions like epiglottitis or a large peritonsillar abscess. These conditions can rapidly progress to complete airway obstruction, necessitating emergency management.
Trismus (inability to open the mouth fully)
  • Trismus, or restricted mouth opening, is a sign of significant inflammation or infection spreading into the deep spaces of the throat.
  • Significance: This is commonly associated with a peritonsillar abscess (quinsy) and may indicate the need for drainage or surgical intervention.
Unilateral tonsillar swelling with uvular deviation
  • Asymmetry of the tonsils, particularly if associated with uvular deviation, is a key sign of a peritonsillar abscess.
  • Significance: A peritonsillar abscess can cause significant airway obstruction and pain. Prompt surgical drainage is often required.
Weight loss, night sweats, or persistent hoarseness
  • These are systemic symptoms that may indicate a chronic or serious underlying disease, such as malignancy.
  • Significance: Weight loss and night sweats could suggest nasopharyngeal or oropharyngeal cancer, while persistent hoarseness may point to laryngeal cancer, especially in smokers or patients with a history of alcohol use. Referral for urgent investigation is warranted.
Non-tender, persistent lymphadenopathy
  • Lymph nodes that are hard, non-tender, and persist for several weeks raise concern for malignancy.
  • Significance: Persistent, non-tender lymphadenopathy could suggest cancers such as lymphoma or metastatic head and neck cancers. Urgent referral for biopsy or imaging is necessary.
Oral ulcers that do not heal after two weeks
  • Persistent oral ulcers may indicate malignant changes or chronic infections.
  • Significance: Non-healing ulcers, especially in high-risk individuals (e.g., smokers, heavy alcohol users), could suggest oral cancer. Biopsy is often needed for diagnosis.
Investigations Strategy & Interpretation
Throat swab for culture and sensitivity
  • A throat swab is taken to identify bacterial pathogens, particularly Group A Streptococcus (GAS), which is a common cause of bacterial pharyngitis and tonsillitis.
  • Result Significance: A positive result for GAS confirms bacterial tonsillitis, guiding antibiotic therapy. A negative result suggests a viral aetiology or non-infectious cause, reducing the need for antibiotics.
Rapid antigen test (for Group A Streptococcus)
  • This test detects antigens from Group A Streptococcus within minutes and is often used in primary care settings for quick diagnosis of streptococcal pharyngitis.
  • Result Significance: A positive result indicates streptococcal pharyngitis, warranting antibiotic treatment. A negative result is less reliable but can reduce the likelihood of bacterial infection, especially if clinical suspicion is low.
Monospot test (for infectious mononucleosis)
  • The Monospot test detects heterophile antibodies associated with Epstein-Barr Virus (EBV) infection, which causes glandular fever (infectious mononucleosis).
  • Result Significance: A positive Monospot test confirms infectious mononucleosis, especially in a patient with sore throat, lymphadenopathy, and splenomegaly. A negative result does not completely rule out the condition, particularly early in the disease course.
Full blood count (FBC)
  • A full blood count helps assess for infection, immune status, and other haematological conditions. It can identify leukocytosis, neutropenia, or lymphocytosis.
  • Result Significance: Leukocytosis with neutrophilia suggests a bacterial infection, while lymphocytosis may indicate viral infections like infectious mononucleosis. Neutropenia could suggest agranulocytosis, which may be drug-induced.
C-reactive protein (CRP)
  • CRP is an inflammatory marker used to assess the severity of infection or inflammation. It is often elevated in bacterial infections.
  • Result Significance: Elevated CRP supports the diagnosis of bacterial pharyngitis or tonsillitis, helping to differentiate from viral causes. A low CRP level makes a bacterial infection less likely.
Lateral flow or PCR test for COVID-19
  • A sore throat can be an early symptom of COVID-19, and testing is crucial to prevent spread and confirm diagnosis.
  • Result Significance: A positive test confirms COVID-19, which may present with mild symptoms such as sore throat. A negative result, especially with typical features, reduces the likelihood of COVID-19 but may need retesting if suspicion remains high.
Blood cultures
  • Blood cultures are taken if there is a suspicion of systemic infection, such as sepsis, which may arise from conditions like peritonsillar or retropharyngeal abscess.
  • Result Significance: Positive blood cultures indicate bacteraemia, requiring prompt antibiotic treatment. Negative results reduce the likelihood of systemic infection but do not entirely rule it out.
Neck ultrasound or CT scan
  • Imaging is used to assess for abscess formation (e.g., peritonsillar, retropharyngeal) or to evaluate masses such as thyroid lesions or lymphadenopathy.
  • Result Significance: Positive findings of fluid collections suggest abscess formation, requiring drainage. Detection of masses or enlarged lymph nodes may point towards malignancy, warranting further investigation.
Evidence-Based Diagnosis (EBDx)
Fever in bacterial infections (e.g., Group A Streptococcus)
  • Fever is a common symptom in patients with bacterial pharyngitis, especially Group A Streptococcal (GAS) infections. It helps distinguish between viral and bacterial causes.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 46%, Specificity: 82%, Positive Likelihood Ratio (LR+): 2.6, Negative Likelihood Ratio (LR-): 0.66.
  • Reference: Centor RM, et al. "The diagnosis of strep throat in adults in the emergency room." Med Decis Making. 1981.
  • Significance: A positive finding of fever increases the likelihood of GAS pharyngitis, while the absence of fever reduces but does not rule out bacterial causes.
Tonsillar exudates in bacterial infections
  • Tonsillar exudates are a hallmark of bacterial pharyngitis, particularly in streptococcal and diphtheritic infections.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 54%, Specificity: 92%, LR+: 6.8, LR-: 0.5 for streptococcal pharyngitis.
  • Reference: McIsaac WJ, et al. "A clinical score to reduce unnecessary antibiotic use in patients with sore throat." CMAJ. 2004.
  • Significance: The presence of exudates strongly suggests bacterial infection (e.g., GAS), while their absence does not entirely rule out bacterial causes but lowers suspicion.
Cervical lymphadenopathy in infectious mononucleosis
  • Cervical lymphadenopathy is often seen in viral infections like infectious mononucleosis, making it a key physical sign in patients with sore throat.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 88%, Specificity: 52%, LR+: 1.83, LR-: 0.23.
  • Reference: Ebell MH, et al. "Does this patient have infectious mononucleosis?" JAMA. 2016.
  • Significance: The presence of lymphadenopathy strongly supports the diagnosis of infectious mononucleosis, while its absence significantly lowers the likelihood.
Positive throat swab for Group A Streptococcus
  • A throat swab for culture is considered the gold standard for diagnosing streptococcal pharyngitis. It has high diagnostic accuracy.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 90-95%, Specificity: 95-98%, LR+: 18.0, LR-: 0.1.
  • Reference: Choby BA. "Diagnosis and treatment of streptococcal pharyngitis." Am Fam Physician. 2009.
  • Significance: A positive throat swab confirms streptococcal pharyngitis, warranting antibiotic therapy. A negative result virtually rules out bacterial infection, favouring viral aetiologies.
Positive Monospot test for infectious mononucleosis
  • The Monospot test detects heterophile antibodies in patients with Epstein-Barr virus (EBV) infection, which causes infectious mononucleosis.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 85%, Specificity: 100%, LR+: 34.0, LR-: 0.15.
  • Reference: Horwitz CA, et al. "The clinical utility of heterophile antibody tests for infectious mononucleosis in the emergency department." Am J Clin Pathol. 2014.
  • Significance: A positive Monospot test strongly supports the diagnosis of infectious mononucleosis. A negative result, particularly early in the disease course, may still warrant repeat testing or other serological tests.
Elevated C-reactive protein (CRP) in bacterial infections
  • CRP is an acute phase reactant that increases in bacterial infections, helping differentiate bacterial from viral causes of sore throat.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 73%, Specificity: 69%, LR+: 2.35, LR-: 0.39 for diagnosing bacterial infections in sore throat.
  • Reference: van der Meer V, et al. "Procalcitonin and C-reactive protein for diagnosing pneumonia in primary care." Eur Respir J. 2005.
  • Significance: Elevated CRP supports a bacterial aetiology, especially when clinical suspicion is high. Low CRP levels favour viral infections, reducing the need for antibiotics.
Diagnostic Timeout & Cognitive Biases

Awareness of common thinking errors is essential to improve diagnostic accuracy when assessing a patient with a sore throat. Before finalising a diagnosis, it is good practice for clinicians at all levels to take a 'diagnostic timeout' to challenge their own reasoning.

  • Anchoring Bias: This is the tendency to latch onto the most common diagnosis—a simple viral URTI—too early in the consultation. This may cause the clinician to inadequately explore red flags or atypical features that might point towards a more serious condition like a peritonsillar abscess or malignancy. A key question to ask is: "Could this be something other than a simple cold, and have I done enough to rule it out?"
  • Confirmation Bias: This occurs when a clinician selectively focuses on features that support their initial hypothesis while ignoring contradictory evidence. For example, a clinician who believes the patient has bacterial tonsillitis might focus on the presence of fever and tender lymph nodes but downplay the significance of a prominent cough and coryza, which would make a viral cause more likely. Using validated scores like FeverPAIN or Centor can help mitigate this by forcing a more objective assessment.
  • Atypical Presentations: A major diagnostic pitfall is failing to consider that serious diseases can present in non-classical ways. For example, Glandular Fever can present without significant lymphadenopathy, especially early on. Likewise, nasopharyngeal cancer may present with only a persistent, mild sore throat and unilateral serous otitis media before other signs become apparent. A high index of suspicion is required for persistent or unusual symptoms.
  • Over-reliance on Scoring Tools: While clinical prediction rules like the FeverPAIN score are valuable for guiding antibiotic prescribing, they are not infallible. A low score significantly reduces the likelihood of a streptococcal infection but does not eliminate it. Clinical judgement must always be paramount, and if there are features concerning for severe illness or complications (e.g., marked systemic upset, unilateral swelling), these must be acted upon regardless of the score.
Illustrative Management Pathways

For educational purposes, this section describes the typical principles of management for key diagnoses. These are not prescriptive instructions. All management must be guided by official UK guidelines (e.g., NICE), local formularies, and senior clinical advice.

Acute Bacterial Tonsillitis/Pharyngitis

Management is guided by clinical scoring systems (FeverPAIN or Centor) to assess the likelihood of a streptococcal infection and thus the potential benefit of antibiotics. For patients with low scores, a no-antibiotic or delayed-antibiotic prescription strategy is recommended, along with advice on simple analgesia (paracetamol, ibuprofen) and self-care. For those with high scores indicating a likely bacterial cause, a course of antibiotics (typically phenoxymethylpenicillin for 10 days, or a macrolide if penicillin-allergic) is appropriate to shorten the illness and reduce the risk of complications.

Glandular Fever (Infectious Mononucleosis)

As this is a viral infection, management is primarily supportive. This includes ensuring adequate hydration and analgesia. A crucial aspect of management is providing safety-netting advice. Patients should be counselled to avoid alcohol to reduce the risk of hepatitis and to refrain from contact sports for at least one month (and until splenomegaly has resolved) to prevent the rare but life-threatening complication of splenic rupture.

Peritonsillar Abscess (Quinsy)

This is a hospital emergency requiring immediate referral to an Ear, Nose, and Throat (ENT) specialist. Management involves a combination of intravenous antibiotics, steroids to reduce swelling, and analgesia. The definitive treatment is drainage of the abscess, which is typically performed under local anaesthetic via needle aspiration or incision and drainage. Most patients require a short hospital admission for observation and intravenous therapy.

Suspected Malignancy

Any patient with red flag symptoms suggesting a possible head and neck cancer requires urgent investigation. This involves referral via the two-week wait pathway in the UK. The patient will be seen by an ENT specialist for a full clinical examination, which will include nasendoscopy to visualise the pharynx and larynx. Further investigations may include imaging (CT/MRI) and a biopsy of any suspicious lesions to establish a definitive diagnosis.

Reflection & Learning

These brief cases illustrate the application of clinical reasoning to different sore throat presentations. They are for educational purposes only.

Case 1: Suspected Glandular Fever in Primary Care

A 19-year-old university student presents with a 5-day history of a very severe sore throat, high fevers, and profound fatigue. On examination, they have grossly enlarged, exudative tonsils, significant tender cervical lymphadenopathy, and palpable splenomegaly. The problem representation is a young adult with a severe exudative tonsillitis and systemic features including fatigue and splenomegaly, highly suspicious for Glandular Fever. The GP performs blood tests including an FBC (showing atypical lymphocytes) and a positive Monospot test, confirming the diagnosis and provides supportive care and safety-netting advice regarding contact sports.

Case 2: Suspected Peritonsillar Abscess in the Emergency Department

A 28-year-old presents to the ED unable to swallow their saliva due to severe throat pain. They have a fever of 38.8°C, a muffled 'hot potato' voice, and can only open their mouth by about 1cm. On inspection, there is marked unilateral swelling of the right tonsil, pushing the uvula across the midline. The problem representation is a patient with severe systemic upset, trismus, and unilateral tonsillar swelling with uvular deviation, which is pathognomonic for a peritonsillar abscess (quinsy). The ED physician makes an immediate referral to the on-call ENT team for urgent assessment and drainage.

Case 3: Red Flags for Malignancy in a GP Clinic

A 65-year-old man, who is a lifelong smoker, presents with a 6-week history of a persistent sore throat localised to the right side, associated with a new-onset hoarse voice and a 4kg weight loss. The pain is dull but constant. Examination of the oropharynx is unremarkable, but a hard, non-tender 2cm lymph node is palpable in the right anterior cervical chain. The problem representation is an older patient with significant risk factors presenting with persistent, constitutional symptoms (weight loss) and signs (hoarseness, fixed lymphadenopathy), raising a high suspicion of laryngeal or pharyngeal cancer. The GP makes an urgent two-week wait cancer referral to ENT.
