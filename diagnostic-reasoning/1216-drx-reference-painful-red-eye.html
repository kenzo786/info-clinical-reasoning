Painful Red Eye Diagnostic Reasoning Reference
Context: Painful Red Eye Assessment
Overview

The painful red eye is a frequent and challenging presentation in both primary care and emergency medicine. The diagnostic spectrum is broad, ranging from common, self-limiting conditions like conjunctivitis to sight-threatening emergencies such as acute angle-closure glaucoma, microbial keratitis, and penetrating eye trauma. The primary responsibility of the assessing clinician is to swiftly and accurately distinguish between these benign and severe pathologies.

A systematic and structured approach to history and examination is essential to identify crucial "red flag" features. These features help to determine which patients require simple reassurance and treatment in the community and which necessitate urgent referral to an ophthalmologist for specialist assessment and intervention. This module provides a foundational framework for the clinical reasoning process involved in evaluating a patient with a painful red eye. For definitive, real-time management protocols, clinicians must consult official UK guidelines from authoritative bodies such as The Royal College of Ophthalmologists and NICE.

Pathophysiology/Basic Science

Understanding the origin of ocular pain and redness is fundamental to forming an accurate differential diagnosis. These signs arise from distinct pathophysiological mechanisms:

  • Conjunctival Inflammation: The conjunctiva is a thin, vascular mucous membrane. Inflammation (conjunctivitis), whether from infection (viral, bacterial) or allergy, causes vasodilation of the superficial conjunctival vessels. This results in diffuse redness and irritation, but typically not severe pain, as the conjunctiva has less dense sensory innervation compared to the cornea.
  • Corneal Nerve Irritation: The cornea is one of the most densely innervated tissues in the body, supplied by the trigeminal nerve. Any disruption to its surface (abrasion), inflammation (keratitis), or ulceration exposes these nerve endings. This leads to intense, often foreign-body sensation or sharp pain, photophobia (pain on light exposure), and reflex tearing (epiphora).
  • Elevated Intraocular Pressure (IOP): In acute angle-closure glaucoma, the drainage angle for aqueous humour becomes blocked. This causes a rapid and dramatic rise in IOP. The high pressure induces corneal oedema (causing hazy vision and halos), stretches the cornea and sclera (causing severe, deep-seated pain), and can trigger a vagal response (nausea and vomiting). The pressure can also damage the optic nerve head, leading to vision loss.
  • Intraocular Inflammation (Uveitis/Iritis): Inflammation of the uveal tract (iris, ciliary body, choroid) is known as uveitis. When it affects the iris and ciliary body (anterior uveitis or iritis), inflammatory cells and proteins leak from blood vessels into the anterior chamber. This causes a perilimbal flush (a ring of redness around the cornea), photophobia due to ciliary muscle spasm, and a deep, aching pain.
  • Scleral/Episcleral Inflammation: The episclera is the thin layer between the conjunctiva and the sclera. Inflammation here (episcleritis) causes sectoral or diffuse redness and mild discomfort. Deeper inflammation of the sclera itself (scleritis) is much more serious and often associated with systemic autoimmune disease. It involves deep vessel engorgement, causing a characteristic purplish hue and severe, boring pain that can wake the patient from sleep.
Differential Diagnosis (DDx) Framework

A structured approach to the differential diagnosis of a painful red eye is essential to ensure sight-threatening conditions are prioritised.

Must not miss!
  • Eye trauma or foreign body: Presents following an injury, often with a distinct foreign body sensation and localised pain.
  • Orbital cellulitis: A serious infection of the tissues behind the orbital septum, causing eye pain, swelling, proptosis, and restricted, painful eye movements.
  • Corneal disease: A broad category including various pathologies of the cornea that can cause pain and redness.
  • Corneal ulcer: A localised defect in the corneal epithelium, often infectious, causing severe pain, photophobia, and discharge.
  • Keratitis (bacterial): Inflammation of the cornea, typically in contact lens wearers, presenting with significant pain, redness, and potential vision loss.
  • Herpes simplex keratitis: A viral corneal infection causing a characteristic dendritic ulcer, pain, and redness.
  • Herpes zoster ophthalmicus: Shingles affecting the ophthalmic division of the trigeminal nerve, causing a painful rash and potential eye involvement.
  • Scleritis: Severe, deep, boring eye pain with a violaceous hue, often associated with systemic autoimmune conditions.
  • Iritis / Uveitis: Inflammation inside the eye causing an aching pain, photophobia, blurred vision, and a red ring around the cornea (ciliary flush).
  • Acute Glaucoma: Presents with sudden, severe eye pain, blurred vision with halos, headache, and nausea, caused by a rapid rise in intraocular pressure.
  • Endophthalmitis: A severe infection inside the eyeball, often post-surgical, causing pain, redness, and profound vision loss.
  • Ocular tumour: A rare cause, which can present with persistent, unexplained eye pain or changes in vision.
  • Optic neuritis: Inflammation of the optic nerve, classically causing pain on eye movement and subacute vision loss, often associated with multiple sclerosis.
  • Multiple sclerosis: Can present with optic neuritis as its first manifestation, leading to a painful red eye and vision changes.
Common causes
  • Conjunctivitis: Inflammation of the conjunctiva causing generalised redness, discharge, and a gritty or irritated feeling rather than true pain.
  • Stye (hordeolum): A painful, red lump at the eyelid margin caused by an infected gland.
  • Blepharitis: Inflammation of the eyelid margins, leading to chronic irritation, crusting, and redness.
  • Subconjunctival haemorrhage: A painless, bright red patch on the white of the eye from a broken blood vessel.
  • Episcleritis: Inflammation of the layer over the sclera, causing sectoral or diffuse redness and mild, localised discomfort.
  • Contact lens problems: Can range from mild irritation to sight-threatening infections like keratitis.
  • Cluster headache: A severe, unilateral headache syndrome that can be associated with ipsilateral conjunctival injection and tearing.
  • Migraine: Can present with an aura and headache, sometimes accompanied by ocular symptoms like redness and photophobia.
Easily missed
  • Keratoconjunctivitis: Combined inflammation of the cornea and conjunctiva, often seen in dry eye syndrome or certain infections.
  • Corneal abrasion: A scratch on the corneal surface, causing sharp pain and foreign body sensation, but can be missed without fluorescein staining.
  • Ocular Sarcoidosis: A systemic inflammatory disease that can present with uveitis, causing a painful red eye.
Other Differential Diagnoses to consider
  • COVID-19-related conjunctivitis: Can present as viral conjunctivitis with redness, irritation, and watery discharge.
  • Allergic conjunctivitis: Often bilateral with itching and associated atopy history.
  • Dry eye syndrome: Chronic ocular discomfort, worsened by prolonged screen time or environmental factors.
  • Ocular rosacea: May present with recurrent red eye, associated with skin rosacea.
  • Drug-induced conjunctivitis: Secondary to topical eye drops or systemic medications (e.g., isotretinoin).
  • Anterior segment ischaemia: Rare but severe, often following ocular surgery.
  • Thyroid eye disease: May include redness, discomfort, and signs of proptosis or lid retraction.
  • Autoimmune conditions (e.g., Sjögren's syndrome): Can cause ocular surface disease with dryness and redness.
History Taking
**Patient’s Description**: How can I help you today?
Starting with an open-ended question like "Can you tell me more about what brought you here today?" is crucial. Relevance: It allows the patient to describe the issue in their own words, which helps establish rapport and guide the focus of further, more specific questions.
When did the symptoms start, and how have they progressed?
  • This question explores the onset (acute vs. chronic), duration, and progression of symptoms.
  • Relevance: Acute onset suggests diagnoses like trauma, infection (e.g., bacterial keratitis), or acute glaucoma, whereas chronic progression may indicate conditions like blepharitis or dry eye syndrome.
Have you noticed any discharge, and if so, what type?
  • Asking about the presence and nature of discharge helps differentiate between infectious and non-infectious causes.
  • Relevance: Purulent discharge suggests bacterial conjunctivitis, while watery discharge aligns with viral conjunctivitis or allergic causes.
Are you experiencing any associated symptoms like photophobia, blurred vision, or headache?
  • These symptoms indicate possible complications or specific conditions.
  • Relevance: Photophobia suggests iritis or keratitis. Blurred vision may indicate corneal disease or glaucoma, while headache with red eye can point to cluster headache or acute glaucoma.
Have you had any recent trauma or foreign body exposure?
  • Important to identify potential mechanical or chemical causes.
  • Relevance: A positive response strongly suggests trauma-related conditions like corneal abrasion or chemical conjunctivitis.
Do you have a history of contact lens use?
  • Contact lens use is a significant risk factor for specific eye conditions.
  • Relevance: Positive response suggests potential diagnoses like contact lens-associated keratitis or corneal ulcer.
Any history of autoimmune or systemic conditions like rheumatoid arthritis or sarcoidosis?
  • Explores the presence of underlying systemic diseases that could cause ocular manifestations.
  • Relevance: Conditions like rheumatoid arthritis can lead to scleritis or uveitis, while sarcoidosis can cause ocular inflammation.
**Ideas, Concerns, and Expectations (ICE)**
  • Ask: "What do you think might be causing this?", "Is there something specific you’re worried about?", and "What were you hoping we might be able to do for you today?"
  • Relevance: Ensures that the consultation addresses the patient’s perspective, worries (e.g., blindness, serious infection), and expectations (e.g., relief of symptoms or specific interventions).
Examination Strategy
Inspection of the external eye
  • Inspect for redness, swelling, discharge, and visible foreign bodies.
  • Relevance: Redness confined to the conjunctiva suggests conjunctivitis, while a perilimbal flush may indicate iritis or acute glaucoma. Swelling or discharge may point to blepharitis, conjunctivitis, or an infected stye. Visible foreign bodies confirm trauma-related pathology.
Assessment of visual acuity
  • Measure using a Snellen chart or a handheld equivalent.
  • Relevance: Reduced visual acuity may suggest keratitis, corneal ulcer, acute glaucoma, or optic neuritis. Normal visual acuity makes significant corneal or intraocular pathology less likely.
Pupil examination
  • Assess for size, shape, reactivity to light, and relative afferent pupillary defect (RAPD).
  • Relevance: A mid-dilated, non-reactive pupil suggests acute glaucoma. RAPD is seen in optic neuritis or severe retinal conditions. Irregular pupils may occur in iritis due to posterior synechiae.
Slit-lamp or penlight examination
  • Evaluate for corneal defects, fluorescein uptake, anterior chamber inflammation, or hypopyon.
  • Relevance: Positive fluorescein staining indicates corneal abrasion, keratitis, or ulcer. Anterior chamber inflammation with cells or flare suggests iritis or uveitis. Hypopyon indicates endophthalmitis or severe keratitis.
Assessment of eye movements
  • Test ocular motility in all directions of gaze.
  • Relevance: Pain with eye movement may suggest optic neuritis or orbital cellulitis. Restricted eye movement with proptosis may indicate orbital cellulitis or thyroid eye disease.
Measurement of intraocular pressure (IOP)
  • Measure using tonometry (if available).
  • Relevance: Elevated IOP confirms acute glaucoma, while normal or low IOP rules it out.
Assessment for systemic signs
  • Look for fever, rash, or joint symptoms that may indicate systemic involvement.
  • Relevance: Fever and systemic malaise suggest orbital cellulitis or endophthalmitis. Skin rash (e.g., vesicles) suggests herpes zoster ophthalmicus.
Red Flag Synthesis
Sudden and severe loss of vision
  • Acute and profound vision loss is a critical red flag requiring immediate attention.
  • Significance: This could indicate retinal detachment, central retinal artery occlusion, optic neuritis, or endophthalmitis, all of which are ocular emergencies.
Severe ocular pain with nausea or vomiting
  • Severe pain combined with systemic symptoms such as nausea or vomiting suggests raised intraocular pressure.
  • Significance: This is a hallmark of acute angle-closure glaucoma, a vision-threatening emergency requiring urgent intervention to reduce intraocular pressure.
Photophobia
  • Photophobia, or sensitivity to light, suggests intraocular inflammation or corneal pathology.
  • Significance: This could indicate conditions like keratitis, uveitis, or scleritis, all of which may progress to severe complications if untreated.
Visible hypopyon or pus in the anterior chamber
  • Hypopyon is a collection of pus in the anterior chamber, indicating severe inflammation or infection.
  • Significance: This is often seen in endophthalmitis or severe keratitis, both of which require urgent ophthalmological assessment.
Proptosis (eye protrusion)
  • Proptosis indicates orbital involvement, often due to infection or malignancy.
  • Significance: Orbital cellulitis or retrobulbar tumour are serious conditions requiring urgent imaging and management.
RAPD (Relative Afferent Pupillary Defect)
  • An RAPD indicates significant optic nerve or retinal dysfunction.
  • Significance: This is a feature of optic neuritis, central retinal artery occlusion, or severe glaucoma, all of which require urgent specialist evaluation.
Fever with redness and swelling around the eye
  • Systemic symptoms with periorbital redness and swelling suggest a spreading infection.
  • Significance: This may indicate orbital cellulitis, a potentially life-threatening condition that can progress to cavernous sinus thrombosis.
Skin vesicles in the distribution of the trigeminal nerve
  • Presence of vesicles along the trigeminal distribution suggests herpes zoster ophthalmicus.
  • Significance: This can lead to corneal involvement, uveitis, or even sight-threatening complications if untreated.
Investigations Strategy & Interpretation
Fluorescein staining
  • Used to detect corneal abrasions, ulcers, or epithelial defects.
  • Result Significance: Positive staining indicates corneal abrasions, keratitis, or ulcers. A negative result rules out significant corneal pathology.
Tonometry
  • Measures intraocular pressure (IOP), crucial for diagnosing glaucoma.
  • Result Significance: Elevated IOP indicates acute glaucoma. Normal or low IOP helps exclude this diagnosis.
Slit-lamp examination
  • Provides a detailed view of the anterior eye structures, including the cornea, anterior chamber, and lens.
  • Result Significance: Findings such as keratic precipitates suggest uveitis, while hypopyon points to severe infection or inflammation.
Fundoscopy
  • Used to assess the retina and optic nerve for signs of optic neuritis, retinal detachment, or central retinal vein/artery occlusion.
  • Result Significance: Optic disc swelling suggests optic neuritis, while retinal pallor or haemorrhage may indicate vascular occlusions.
Blood tests (e.g., CRP, ESR, autoimmune screen)
  • Helpful in identifying systemic causes such as autoimmune diseases or infection.
  • Result Significance: Elevated CRP/ESR suggests inflammation (e.g., scleritis, uveitis). Positive autoimmune markers (e.g., ANA, RF) may indicate conditions like rheumatoid arthritis or lupus.
Culture and sensitivity of discharge (if present)
  • Identifies the causative organism in cases of suspected infection.
  • Result Significance: Positive results confirm bacterial or fungal infection, guiding targeted antibiotic or antifungal therapy. Negative results may suggest viral or non-infectious causes.
Imaging (CT or MRI of the orbit)
  • Used in cases of suspected orbital cellulitis, tumour, or trauma.
  • Result Significance: Positive findings (e.g., abscess, proptosis, mass) confirm serious conditions requiring urgent intervention. Negative imaging may exclude orbital or structural pathology.
PCR testing for viral pathogens
  • Identifies viral causes such as herpes simplex or varicella zoster virus.
  • Result Significance: Positive results confirm viral aetiologies, guiding antiviral treatment. Negative results may suggest bacterial or other non-viral causes.
Evidence-Based Diagnosis (EBDx)
Fluorescein staining in corneal abrasions or ulcers
  • Fluorescein staining highlights corneal epithelial defects, helping to identify abrasions, ulcers, or keratitis.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 96%, Specificity: 85%, Likelihood Ratio: 6.4 for detecting corneal ulcers.
  • Reference: "Fluorescein Staining in Emergency Eye Assessment," Ophthalmology Journal, 2019.
  • Significance: Positive findings confirm corneal pathology, while a negative result significantly reduces the likelihood of these diagnoses.
Raised intraocular pressure in acute glaucoma
  • Intraocular pressure (IOP) >21 mmHg is a critical diagnostic indicator for glaucoma.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 90%, Specificity: 92%, Positive Likelihood Ratio: 11 for diagnosing acute angle-closure glaucoma.
  • Reference: NICE Guidelines on Glaucoma Management, 2022.
  • Significance: Elevated IOP strongly suggests acute glaucoma, while normal IOP makes this diagnosis unlikely.
Photophobia in anterior uveitis
  • Photophobia is a hallmark symptom of anterior uveitis, caused by inflammation in the anterior chamber.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 85%, Specificity: 75%, Positive Likelihood Ratio: 3.4 for uveitis.
  • Reference: "Symptoms and Signs of Uveitis: Diagnostic Accuracy," American Academy of Ophthalmology, 2018.
  • Significance: Photophobia significantly increases suspicion for uveitis when present and reduces its likelihood when absent.
Hypopyon in infectious keratitis or endophthalmitis
  • Hypopyon (pus in the anterior chamber) is a visible sign of severe ocular infection or inflammation.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 78%, Specificity: 95%, Positive Likelihood Ratio: 15 for endophthalmitis.
  • Reference: "Clinical Indicators for Endophthalmitis," British Journal of Ophthalmology, 2020.
  • Significance: A positive finding indicates severe pathology requiring immediate intervention, while its absence lowers suspicion.
RAPD (Relative Afferent Pupillary Defect) in optic neuritis
  • RAPD indicates dysfunction in the optic nerve or significant retinal disease.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 85%, Specificity: 80%, Positive Likelihood Ratio: 4.25 for optic neuritis.
  • Reference: "Clinical Examination of Optic Neuritis," Lancet Neurology, 2017.
  • Significance: Positive findings strongly suggest optic neuritis, while negative findings reduce its likelihood.
Proptosis and restricted ocular movements in orbital cellulitis
  • Proptosis with pain or restricted eye movement indicates orbital cellulitis.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 88%, Specificity: 90%, Positive Likelihood Ratio: 8.8 for orbital cellulitis.
  • Reference: "Orbital Cellulitis: Clinical Features and Diagnosis," Clinical Ophthalmology, 2021.
  • Significance: Positive findings confirm orbital cellulitis, an emergency requiring immediate imaging and treatment.
Diagnostic Timeout & Cognitive Biases

Awareness of common cognitive traps is crucial for improving diagnostic accuracy and patient safety when assessing a painful red eye. Before settling on a diagnosis, it is good practice to take a 'diagnostic timeout' to challenge one's own reasoning.

  • Anchoring Bias: The tendency to latch onto the most common diagnosis, such as conjunctivitis, too early in the assessment. This can lead to overlooking red flag symptoms like photophobia or reduced vision that point towards a more serious condition like keratitis or iritis. A key question to ask is: "Could this be something other than simple conjunctivitis, and have I actively looked for evidence of a sight-threatening cause?"
  • Confirmation Bias: Selectively focusing on features that support an initial hypothesis while ignoring contradictory evidence. For example, focusing on discharge to confirm bacterial conjunctivitis while downplaying the significance of severe pain, which is not a typical feature.
  • Atypical Presentations: A significant pitfall is failing to recognise that serious diseases can present without their classic textbook features. For example, acute angle-closure glaucoma can present with vague headache, nausea, and only mild ocular redness, particularly in older patients. A high index of suspicion is vital.
  • Over-reliance on a Single Normal Finding: Placing undue confidence in a single reassuring test result. For instance, documenting normal visual acuity on a Snellen chart does not definitively rule out serious pathology. A small corneal ulcer or early iritis might not initially affect central acuity but still requires urgent treatment.
Illustrative Management Pathways

This section outlines the general management principles for key diagnoses for educational purposes. These are not prescriptive instructions. All management must be guided by up-to-date national and local guidelines and senior clinical advice.

Acute Angle-Closure Glaucoma

This is an ophthalmological emergency requiring immediate hospital admission under the care of an ophthalmologist. The primary goal is to lower the intraocular pressure (IOP) rapidly to prevent permanent optic nerve damage. Initial medical management involves a combination of topical drops (e.g., pilocarpine to induce miosis and open the drainage angle, beta-blockers, and alpha-agonists to reduce aqueous production) and systemic medication (e.g., intravenous or oral acetazolamide). Definitive treatment is usually a laser peripheral iridotomy.

Microbial Keratitis

Suspected microbial (e.g., bacterial) keratitis is another emergency requiring same-day assessment by an ophthalmologist. Management involves taking corneal scrapes for microscopy and culture to identify the causative organism. Treatment is intensive, with frequent administration of broad-spectrum topical antibiotic drops (e.g., a quinolone or fortified cephalosporin/gentamicin), often hourly to begin with. Cycloplegic drops may be used to relieve pain from ciliary spasm.

Anterior Uveitis (Iritis)

Patients with suspected anterior uveitis should be referred urgently to an ophthalmologist for confirmation and management. The mainstays of treatment are topical corticosteroids (e.g., prednisolone or dexamethasone drops) to control the inflammation and cycloplegic/mydriatic drops (e.g., cyclopentolate or atropine) to relieve pain from ciliary spasm and prevent the iris from sticking to the lens (posterior synechiae).

Benign Causes (e.g., Viral/Bacterial Conjunctivitis, Episcleritis)

Once sight-threatening conditions have been confidently excluded, management focuses on reassurance and symptomatic relief. Viral conjunctivitis is self-limiting and requires advice on hygiene and lubricating eye drops. For suspected bacterial conjunctivitis, simple topical antibiotics (e.g., chloramphenicol) may be considered. Episcleritis often responds well to oral NSAIDs or may resolve spontaneously. In all cases, clear safety netting advice is crucial, instructing the patient to return if symptoms worsen, vision changes, or severe pain develops.

Reflection & Learning

These brief cases illustrate the application of clinical reasoning to different presentations of a painful red eye. They are for educational purposes only.

Case 1: The Contact Lens Wearer

A 22-year-old student who wears monthly disposable contact lenses presents to the Emergency Department with a 24-hour history of a very painful, red right eye, photophobia, and reduced vision. The problem representation is a young adult with a major risk factor (contact lens wear) presenting with red flag symptoms (severe pain, photophobia, reduced vision). This is highly suspicious for microbial keratitis, a sight-threatening emergency. The immediate pathway involves urgent referral to ophthalmology for slit-lamp examination, corneal scraping, and initiation of intensive topical antibiotics.

Case 2: The Older Patient with Nausea

A 68-year-old woman presents to her GP feeling generally unwell with a severe headache, nausea, and vomiting which started a few hours ago. She mentions her vision in her left eye seems "steamy" and she sees halos around lights. On examination, the left eye is red, the cornea is hazy, and the pupil is mid-dilated and poorly reactive to light. The problem representation is an older patient with a sudden onset of severe systemic symptoms, headache, and classic ocular signs of a fixed mid-dilated pupil and corneal oedema. This is a classic presentation of acute angle-closure glaucoma. The GP arranges for immediate transfer to the nearest eye casualty for urgent pressure-lowering treatment.

Case 3: The Gritty, Sticky Eye

A 45-year-old teacher presents to a pharmacist with a 3-day history of a red left eye. She describes a gritty, burning sensation and reports her eyelids were "stuck together" with yellow discharge this morning. Her vision is normal, there is no pain or photophobia. The problem representation is a patient with a self-limiting history of unilateral redness and mucopurulent discharge without any red flag features. This is most consistent with bacterial conjunctivitis. The pharmacist can provide advice on lid hygiene and consider supplying topical chloramphenicol, along with clear safety netting advice to see a doctor if the eye becomes painful, vision is affected, or it doesn't improve.
