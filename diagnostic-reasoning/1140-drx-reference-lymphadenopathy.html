Lymphadenopathy Diagnostic Reasoning Reference
Context: Lymphadenopathy Assessment
Overview

Lymphadenopathy, or the enlargement of lymph nodes, is a very common clinical finding in both primary and secondary care. It represents a diagnostic challenge because its causes range from benign, self-limiting viral infections to life-threatening malignancies. The core task for the clinician is to systematically and safely distinguish patients who require urgent investigation and referral from the vast majority who can be managed with reassurance and observation.

A structured approach, grounded in a sound understanding of anatomy and pathophysiology, is essential. The initial assessment must focus on identifying red flag features from the history and examination that point towards serious underlying pathology such as lymphoma, metastatic cancer, or systemic infections like tuberculosis. This educational module provides a foundational framework for the clinical reasoning process in assessing a patient with lymphadenopathy. For definitive, real-time management protocols, users must refer to official UK guidelines from authoritative bodies such as NICE (e.g., NG12 for Suspected Cancer: recognition and referral, and C82 for Haematological Cancers).

Pathophysiology/Basic Science

Understanding the mechanisms of lymph node enlargement is fundamental to forming a differential diagnosis. Pain and other features are generated through several distinct processes:

  • Reactive Lymphoid Hyperplasia: This is the most common cause of lymphadenopathy. In response to antigenic stimulation (e.g., from a local or systemic infection or inflammation), lymphocytes and other immune cells within the node proliferate rapidly. This causes the node to enlarge. The stretching of the nodal capsule often results in tenderness. These nodes are typically soft or firm, mobile, and resolve as the underlying immune stimulus recedes.
  • Infiltrative Disease: This occurs when the normal architecture of the lymph node is replaced by extrinsic cells. This is most commonly seen with malignant cells, either from a primary haematological cancer (e.g., lymphoma, chronic lymphocytic leukaemia) or from metastatic solid tumours (e.g., breast cancer spreading to axillary nodes). The infiltration makes the nodes feel hard, non-tender, and often "fixed" or matted to surrounding tissues as the disease breaches the capsule.
  • Granulomatous Inflammation: In certain chronic infections (like Tuberculosis) and systemic inflammatory conditions (like Sarcoidosis), the immune response is characterised by the formation of granulomas—organised collections of macrophages (histiocytes). This process leads to firm, often matted, and typically non-tender lymphadenopathy. In TB, these granulomas may undergo central necrosis, a process known as caseation.
  • Suppurative Lymphadenitis: This refers to a direct bacterial infection within the lymph node itself, leading to the formation of pus and a potential abscess. The node becomes acutely inflamed, very tender, warm, and erythematous, often with overlying cellulitis. This is common in staphylococcal or streptococcal infections.
Differential Diagnosis (DDx) Framework

A structured approach to the differential diagnosis of lymphadenopathy is essential to ensure life-threatening conditions are prioritised.

Must not miss!
  • Sepsis: A life-threatening organ dysfunction caused by a dysregulated host response to infection, which can be associated with widespread lymphadenopathy.
  • HIV: A viral infection attacking the immune system, often presenting with persistent generalised lymphadenopathy in its early stages.
  • Tuberculosis: A bacterial infection that can cause chronic, non-tender, and often matted lymph nodes, particularly in the cervical region (scrofula).
  • Metastatic cancer: The spread of cancer cells from a primary tumour to regional lymph nodes, which characteristically become hard, fixed, and non-tender.
  • Lymphoma: A primary cancer of the lymphatic system, typically presenting with firm, rubbery, non-tender lymph nodes, often accompanied by systemic "B symptoms".
  • Leukaemia: A cancer of the blood-forming tissues that can infiltrate lymph nodes, causing generalised lymphadenopathy alongside signs of bone marrow failure.
Common causes
  • URTI (Common cold): A viral respiratory infection that commonly causes reactive, tender cervical lymphadenopathy.
  • Skin infection: Localised bacterial infections of the skin that lead to tender enlargement of the regional draining lymph nodes.
  • Boils: A type of skin abscess that causes a significant local inflammatory response and regional lymphadenopathy.
  • Cellulitis: A bacterial infection of the deeper layers of skin that results in painful, tender regional lymph node swelling.
  • Varicella: The chickenpox virus, which can cause generalised lymphadenopathy in association with its characteristic vesicular rash.
  • Glandular fever: An infection, most commonly with Epstein-Barr Virus (EBV), characterised by prominent cervical lymphadenopathy, fever, sore throat, and fatigue.
  • Cytomegalovirus: A common herpesvirus that can cause a glandular fever-like illness with generalised lymphadenopathy, especially in the immunocompromised.
  • Measles: A highly contagious viral illness presenting with a characteristic rash, cough, coryza, and conjunctivitis, along with generalised lymphadenopathy.
  • Rubella: A viral infection causing a distinctive rash and prominent post-auricular and sub-occipital lymphadenopathy.
  • Dermatitis: Chronic skin inflammation from any cause can lead to reactive enlargement of regional lymph nodes.
  • COVID-19: Can cause lymphadenopathy as part of the acute viral illness or in the context of multisystem inflammatory syndrome.
Easily missed
  • Medication-induced Lymphadenopathy: A rare adverse reaction to certain drugs (e.g., phenytoin, allopurinol) that can mimic lymphoma.
  • Cat scratch disease: A bacterial infection (Bartonella henselae) transmitted by a cat scratch, leading to tender regional lymphadenopathy over several weeks.
  • Sarcoidosis: A multisystem inflammatory condition of unknown cause that creates non-caseating granulomas, often presenting with bilateral hilar lymphadenopathy.
  • Systemic Lupus Erythematosus: A systemic autoimmune disease that can cause non-tender, generalised lymphadenopathy along with other features like rash and arthritis.
  • Rheumatoid arthritis: A chronic autoimmune disease primarily affecting joints, which can also be associated with lymphadenopathy, particularly near affected joints.
Other Differential Diagnoses to consider
  • COVID-19: Can cause lymphadenopathy, particularly in multisystem inflammatory syndrome.
  • Toxoplasmosis: Often presents with generalised lymphadenopathy, especially in immunocompromised patients.
  • Kawasaki disease: Paediatric vasculitis that can cause cervical lymphadenopathy.
  • Castleman disease: Rare disorder causing abnormal lymph node growth, sometimes mimicking lymphoma.
  • Brucellosis: Zoonotic infection that may present with lymphadenopathy in endemic areas or from animal contact.
  • Histoplasmosis: Fungal infection with potential for lymphadenopathy, especially in immunosuppressed individuals.
  • Whipple's disease: Rare bacterial infection causing systemic symptoms and mesenteric lymphadenopathy.
  • Silicosis: Occupational lung disease with lymphadenopathy linked to chronic inhalation of silica dust.
History Taking
**Patient’s Description**: How can I help you today?
Starting with an open-ended question such as, "Can you tell me more about what brought you here today?" is key. Relevance: This allows the patient to explain their symptom in their own words, highlighting any descriptors or details that may suggest specific diagnoses. The clinician can then follow up with more targeted questions, making history-taking more efficient and comprehensive.
Can you describe the swelling or lumps?
  • Encourage the patient to describe the lymphadenopathy’s location, size, and texture (e.g., hard, soft, mobile, tender).
  • Relevance: Generalised lymphadenopathy may suggest systemic conditions (e.g., HIV, TB), whereas localised swelling may indicate local infection or neoplasia.
When did you first notice the swelling?
  • Assess onset and duration—whether it’s acute, subacute, or chronic.
  • Relevance: An acute onset might suggest infections (URTI, cellulitis), whereas chronic or progressive enlargement may raise concern for malignancies (lymphoma, metastatic cancer).
Have you noticed any change in size over time?
  • Understanding any progression can guide whether the lymphadenopathy is likely reactive or malignant.
  • Relevance: A progressively enlarging lymph node may be more suggestive of malignancy, while stable or fluctuating nodes often indicate benign or infectious causes.
Is there anything that makes the swelling worse or better?
  • Exploring for exacerbating or alleviating factors, including rest, movement, or temperature.
  • Relevance: Increased swelling after certain exposures or activity may support inflammatory causes, while no alleviating factors might be more typical of malignancy.
Associated Symptom Question: Fever, night sweats, and weight loss?
  • These “B symptoms” can indicate systemic illness or malignancy.
  • Relevance: Positive response is concerning for lymphoma, TB, or metastatic disease; a negative response might support a local or benign condition.
Associated Symptom Question: Sore throat or recent infection?
  • Upper respiratory symptoms are relevant in evaluating infectious causes of lymphadenopathy, like viral URTI or glandular fever.
  • Relevance: A positive response supports an infectious cause; a negative response may guide focus to non-infectious aetiologies.
Associated Symptom Question: Fatigue and joint pain?
  • Fatigue and arthralgia may indicate systemic autoimmune conditions or chronic infections.
  • Relevance: Positive response raises suspicion for conditions like SLE, rheumatoid arthritis, or chronic infections (e.g., HIV, TB); negative response makes autoimmune aetiologies less likely.
Past Medical History
  • Ask about past infections, immunocompromising conditions, malignancies, and any known autoimmune conditions.
  • Relevance: Past infections or malignancies can increase risk of certain aetiologies (e.g., lymphoma in those with past EBV or immunosuppression); chronic diseases (e.g., diabetes) may predispose to recurrent infections.
Family History
  • Inquire about family history of malignancies, autoimmune diseases, or chronic infections like TB.
  • Relevance: A positive family history can raise suspicion for genetic predispositions to certain conditions, such as lymphoma or autoimmune disorders.
Drug History
  • Review any recent medications, particularly those known to cause lymphadenopathy (e.g., phenytoin, certain antibiotics).
  • Relevance: Medication-induced lymphadenopathy can be managed by discontinuing the offending drug; a negative history would make drug-related causes less likely.
**Ideas, Concerns, and Expectations (ICE)**
  • Ask about the patient’s ideas regarding the cause of the lymphadenopathy, any specific worries (e.g., cancer), and their expectations for the consultation or investigations.
  • Relevance: This ensures the patient’s perspective is understood and that their fears, like concerns about cancer or chronic disease, are addressed. It also clarifies if the patient is seeking reassurance, testing, or specific interventions.
Examination Strategy
Inspection of the lymph nodes
  • Visually inspect the regions where lymph nodes are typically palpable (cervical, axillary, inguinal) for signs of swelling or erythema.
  • Relevance: Visible swelling can indicate lymphadenopathy. Redness or warmth over a lymph node may suggest an infection (e.g., cellulitis), while absence of such findings may point away from acute infection.
Palpation of lymph nodes (size, consistency, mobility, tenderness)
  • Palpate the lymph nodes in the cervical, axillary, and inguinal areas, noting size, texture, mobility, and tenderness.
  • Relevance: Enlarged, hard, fixed nodes may suggest malignancy (e.g., lymphoma, metastatic cancer). Tender, soft, mobile nodes are often reactive and may point to benign or infectious causes. Non-tender, rubbery nodes can be associated with lymphoma. A lack of lymph node enlargement reduces suspicion for conditions like lymphoma or systemic infections.
Examination of the spleen and liver (Hepatosplenomegaly)
  • Examine for hepatosplenomegaly by palpating the liver and spleen edges.
  • Relevance: Hepatosplenomegaly may be indicative of systemic diseases such as viral infections (e.g., EBV, CMV), haematological malignancies (e.g., lymphoma, leukaemia), or chronic infections like TB. A lack of hepatosplenomegaly decreases the likelihood of these systemic conditions.
Examination of the throat (pharyngitis, tonsillar enlargement)
  • Inspect the throat for signs of pharyngitis, tonsillar enlargement, and exudates.
  • Relevance: Pharyngitis with tonsillar enlargement and exudates may suggest infectious mononucleosis (glandular fever) or streptococcal infection. A clear throat examination reduces the likelihood of a primary throat infection causing lymphadenopathy.
Examination of the skin (rashes, lesions, erythema)
  • Inspect the skin for any rashes, lesions, or erythema, particularly around areas with lymphadenopathy.
  • Relevance: A rash may indicate systemic conditions such as SLE, measles, or rubella. Skin lesions near lymphadenopathy could suggest local infections (boils, cellulitis). Absence of skin findings helps rule out dermatological or localised skin infections as causes of lymphadenopathy.
General observation (fever, cachexia, pallor)
  • Observe for signs of systemic illness, such as fever, weight loss, or pallor.
  • Relevance: Fever and cachexia (weight loss) may suggest malignancy or chronic infection (e.g., TB, lymphoma). Pallor could indicate anaemia associated with leukaemia or chronic infection. Absence of these signs might suggest a more benign cause of lymphadenopathy.
Joint examination (swelling, tenderness)
  • Examine joints for any swelling, tenderness, or deformities.
  • Relevance: Joint findings may indicate autoimmune conditions like rheumatoid arthritis or SLE, which can present with lymphadenopathy. A normal joint examination reduces the likelihood of an autoimmune cause.
Red Flag Synthesis
Unintentional weight loss
  • Significant, unexplained weight loss may indicate a systemic or malignant process.
  • Significance: This is concerning for conditions such as lymphoma, metastatic cancer, or chronic infections like tuberculosis, all of which require prompt investigation and management.
Night sweats and fever
  • Persistent night sweats and fever, particularly when accompanied by lymphadenopathy, may point to serious infections or malignancies.
  • Significance: These symptoms are often associated with lymphoma, tuberculosis, or systemic infections such as HIV, all of which require urgent evaluation.
Generalised lymphadenopathy
  • Enlargement of lymph nodes in multiple regions can suggest systemic disease rather than a localised infection.
  • Significance: Generalised lymphadenopathy may be indicative of conditions such as lymphoma, leukaemia, HIV, or autoimmune diseases, warranting further investigation to rule out these serious causes.
Fixed, hard, or matted lymph nodes
  • Nodes that are hard, immobile, or feel matted (stuck together) are concerning for malignancy.
  • Significance: These characteristics are often associated with metastatic cancer or lymphoma, necessitating urgent referral and further investigation.
Severe or persistent pain in lymph nodes, especially after alcohol consumption
  • While pain in lymph nodes may occur with benign infections, pain specifically triggered by alcohol consumption is concerning.
  • Significance: This phenomenon is classically associated with Hodgkin lymphoma and warrants further investigation if present.
Pallor, easy bruising, or frequent infections
  • Signs of anaemia, bleeding tendency, or frequent infections may suggest a haematological disorder.
  • Significance: These findings could indicate leukaemia or other bone marrow infiltrative diseases, and they require urgent haematological evaluation.
Rapidly enlarging lymph nodes
  • Nodes that increase in size quickly can suggest an aggressive process.
  • Significance: Rapid growth raises suspicion for aggressive lymphoma or metastatic cancer, and it may also indicate infection or abscess formation, requiring prompt intervention.
Systemic symptoms: fatigue, weakness, or significant loss of appetite
  • Systemic symptoms such as fatigue or loss of appetite are often associated with chronic diseases or malignancy.
  • Significance: These symptoms may accompany lymphoma, metastatic disease, or chronic infections (e.g., TB or HIV), necessitating further investigation to determine the underlying cause.
Investigations Strategy & Interpretation
Full Blood Count (FBC) with differential
  • Assesses white cell count, haemoglobin, and platelets, providing information on possible infections, haematological malignancies, and anaemia.
  • Result Significance: Leucocytosis may indicate an infection, while lymphocytosis could suggest viral infections or lymphoproliferative disorders. Abnormal cells or cytopenias may raise suspicion for leukaemia or lymphoma. A normal FBC may make haematological malignancies less likely.
Inflammatory markers (CRP and ESR)
  • Measures systemic inflammation, which can indicate infection, autoimmune disease, or malignancy.
  • Result Significance: Elevated CRP or ESR may suggest an underlying infection, malignancy, or inflammatory condition (e.g., SLE or rheumatoid arthritis). A normal ESR and CRP do not rule out malignancy but make systemic inflammation less likely.
Serology for Epstein-Barr Virus (EBV) and Cytomegalovirus (CMV)
  • Helps diagnose infectious mononucleosis, often caused by EBV or CMV, which commonly presents with lymphadenopathy.
  • Result Significance: Positive serology indicates recent or current infection with EBV or CMV, explaining lymphadenopathy. Negative results may point toward other causes.
HIV test
  • Important for diagnosing HIV, which can cause generalised lymphadenopathy, particularly in early and advanced stages.
  • Result Significance: A positive HIV test indicates an HIV infection, which could be responsible for lymphadenopathy. A negative test rules out HIV as a cause.
Tuberculosis (TB) testing (e.g., Mantoux test, IGRA, or chest X-ray)
  • Screening for TB is relevant, especially if there is a history of exposure or travel to endemic areas, as TB can present with lymphadenopathy.
  • Result Significance: A positive test suggests TB infection, which can cause lymphadenopathy, particularly in the cervical region. Negative results make TB less likely but do not completely exclude it in high-risk cases.
Autoimmune screening (e.g., ANA, RF)
  • Tests for autoimmune markers can help identify conditions such as systemic lupus erythematosus (SLE) or rheumatoid arthritis, which may present with lymphadenopathy.
  • Result Significance: Positive ANA or RF may suggest autoimmune conditions like SLE or rheumatoid arthritis, while negative results reduce the likelihood of these conditions.
Chest X-ray
  • Assesses for mediastinal lymphadenopathy, lung pathology, or granulomatous disease (e.g., sarcoidosis or TB).
  • Result Significance: Enlarged hilar or mediastinal nodes can indicate sarcoidosis, TB, or malignancy. A clear chest X-ray may help rule out pulmonary causes of lymphadenopathy.
Ultrasound of lymph nodes
  • Non-invasive imaging to evaluate lymph node characteristics, such as size, shape, and internal structure, helping to distinguish benign from suspicious nodes.
  • Result Significance: Abnormal findings, like irregular borders or internal vascularity, may suggest malignancy, while benign nodes typically appear oval and well-defined. Normal findings can reassure or prompt observation rather than invasive testing.
Lymph node biopsy (fine needle aspiration or excisional biopsy)
  • Biopsy provides histological analysis, which is the gold standard for diagnosing malignancy or specific infections in cases of persistent or suspicious lymphadenopathy.
  • Result Significance: Positive biopsy results for malignancy confirm diagnoses like lymphoma or metastatic cancer. Negative or benign findings may indicate reactive or infectious causes, helping to exclude malignancy.
Evidence-Based Diagnosis (EBDx)
Generalised lymphadenopathy and the likelihood of systemic disease
  • Generalised lymphadenopathy (involving more than one region) increases the likelihood of systemic causes such as HIV, TB, and haematologic malignancies.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 85% for lymphoma in patients with generalised lymphadenopathy; Specificity: 72% for excluding localised infections.
  • Reference: National Institute for Health and Care Excellence (NICE) guidelines for haematological cancers, 2022.
  • Significance: Positive findings suggest a systemic disease, whereas localised lymphadenopathy may indicate a reactive or local infection.
Presence of B symptoms (fever, night sweats, weight loss)
  • B symptoms are associated with lymphomas and other malignancies, as well as chronic infections like TB.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 65% for diagnosing lymphoma in the presence of B symptoms; Specificity: 88%.
  • Reference: British Journal of Haematology, 2021, clinical practice guidelines.
  • Significance: B symptoms significantly increase suspicion for malignancy or TB. Absence of these symptoms lowers the likelihood of these diagnoses but does not rule them out.
Hard, fixed, or matted lymph nodes and malignancy risk
  • Firm, fixed, or matted lymph nodes raise concern for malignancy, especially metastatic cancer or lymphoma.
  • Evidence: Likelihood Ratio: 4.7 for malignancy when lymph nodes are hard or fixed.
  • Reference: Clinical features of Hodgkin and non-Hodgkin lymphoma, Cochrane Review, 2020.
  • Significance: These physical characteristics increase the probability of malignancy and warrant further diagnostic evaluation, including imaging and biopsy.
Full blood count (FBC) abnormalities in lymphadenopathy
  • Abnormal FBC findings, such as cytopenias or atypical cells, may indicate haematologic malignancies (e.g., lymphoma, leukaemia) or viral infections.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 76% for diagnosing haematologic malignancies with FBC abnormalities; Specificity: 82%.
  • Reference: NICE Haematology guidelines, 2022.
  • Significance: An abnormal FBC result increases suspicion for leukaemia, lymphoma, or viral infections, whereas a normal FBC reduces but does not exclude these diagnoses.
Ultrasound of lymph nodes and distinguishing benign from malignant features
  • Ultrasound can assess lymph node structure and vascularity, helping to differentiate reactive from malignant nodes.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 85% for identifying malignant nodes with irregular borders or high vascularity; Specificity: 78% for ruling out malignancy with benign ultrasound findings.
  • Reference: Radiology of lymphadenopathy, European Journal of Radiology, 2021.
  • Significance: Suspicious ultrasound findings should prompt biopsy to confirm malignancy, while benign features support a conservative approach.
Lymph node biopsy findings for definitive diagnosis
  • Biopsy provides histological evidence that can confirm malignancy, granulomatous disease, or infection.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 95% for lymphoma diagnosis; Specificity: 100% for excluding lymphoma with a benign biopsy.
  • Reference: World Health Organisation (WHO) lymph node biopsy guidelines, 2019.
  • Significance: Positive biopsy results confirm diagnoses such as lymphoma or metastatic cancer, while benign findings effectively exclude malignancy, guiding appropriate management.
Diagnostic Timeout & Cognitive Biases

Awareness of common thinking errors is essential to improve diagnostic accuracy when assessing a patient with lymphadenopathy. Before finalising a diagnosis, it is good practice for clinicians at all levels to take a 'diagnostic timeout' to challenge their own reasoning.

  • Anchoring Bias: The tendency to lock onto an early, obvious diagnosis, often a benign one. For example, attributing new lymphadenopathy in a young adult to a recent cold, and failing to reconsider the diagnosis when the node persists for weeks or other red flags like weight loss emerge. A key question to ask is: "If it wasn't this common infection, what is the worst thing it could be?"
  • Confirmation Bias: Actively seeking out or over-emphasising evidence that supports an initial belief while ignoring contradictory data. For example, focusing on the presence of tenderness to confirm a reactive node, while downplaying the fact that it feels unusually firm or is rapidly enlarging.
  • Attribution Error & Stereotyping: Making assumptions based on the patient's age. For instance, too readily dismissing lymphadenopathy in a young person as infectious ("it's always glandular fever") or, conversely, assuming any enlarged node in an older person must be cancer. Lymphoma has peaks in both young adults and older populations.
  • Base Rate Neglect: Forgetting that while malignancy is the most feared cause, it is not the most common. The vast majority of presentations of lymphadenopathy, particularly in primary care, are due to benign, reactive causes. The cognitive challenge is to maintain vigilance for rare but serious conditions without over-investigating every benign case.
Illustrative Management Pathways

For educational purposes, this section describes the typical principles of management for key diagnoses. These are not prescriptive instructions. All management must be guided by official UK guidelines, local formularies, and senior clinical advice.

Acute Reactive Lymphadenopathy (e.g., secondary to URTI)

When a patient presents with tender, mobile lymphadenopathy in the context of a clear, self-limiting infection (like a sore throat or cellulitis), the management focuses on treating the primary cause and providing reassurance. Simple analgesia (e.g., paracetamol, ibuprofen) can be used for discomfort. The key element is safety netting: advising the patient to return if the nodes do not resolve within an expected timeframe (e.g., 3-4 weeks), if they continue to enlarge, or if new symptoms (especially red flags) develop.

Suspected Infectious Mononucleosis (Glandular Fever)

In a patient (typically an adolescent or young adult) with significant cervical lymphadenopathy, fever, sore throat, and profound fatigue, the diagnosis of glandular fever is likely. Management involves confirming the diagnosis (e.g., with a Monospot test and EBV serology) and providing symptomatic support with rest and analgesia. Crucially, patients should be advised to avoid alcohol and contact sports for at least one month to minimise the risk of splenic rupture, a rare but serious complication.

Unexplained Persistent Lymphadenopathy or Suspicion of Malignancy

This is a critical pathway that requires prompt action. Any patient with an unexplained lymph node that is non-tender, firm/hard, >2cm, fixed, or associated with red flags (B symptoms, hepatosplenomegaly) requires an urgent two-week wait referral to a specialist (e.g., Haematology or ENT), as per NICE guidelines. The workup in secondary care will typically involve further blood tests, imaging (CT/PET-CT), and a lymph node biopsy (preferably an excisional biopsy) to establish a definitive histological diagnosis.

Reflection & Learning

These brief cases illustrate the application of clinical reasoning to different presentations of lymphadenopathy. They are for educational purposes only.

Case 1: Suspected Glandular Fever in a University Student

A 19-year-old student presents to their GP with a one-week history of extreme tiredness, high fevers, and a very sore throat. On examination, they have bilaterally enlarged and tender posterior cervical lymph nodes, tonsillar exudate, and palpable splenomegaly. The problem representation is a young adult with the classic triad of fever, pharyngitis, and lymphadenopathy, highly suggestive of infectious mononucleosis. The GP performs blood tests, including FBC and EBV serology, and provides crucial safety netting advice regarding the risk of splenic rupture.

Case 2: Suspected Lymphoma in an Older Adult

A 72-year-old woman presents with a persistent, painless lump in her right axilla that she noticed six weeks ago. She reports feeling more tired than usual and has been waking up with her nightclothes damp from sweat. On examination, there is a 3cm, firm, rubbery, non-tender lymph node in the axilla. The problem representation is an older adult with a persistent, non-tender lymph node and associated B symptoms (fatigue, night sweats), which is highly concerning for lymphoma. An urgent two-week wait referral to Haematology is made for biopsy.

Case 3: Reactive Node in a Child

A 4-year-old boy is brought to the walk-in centre by his mother due to a "lump behind his ear". The lump appeared two days ago and is sore. On examination, there is a 1cm, tender, mobile posterior auricular lymph node. A thorough skin examination reveals a small, healing scratch from the family cat on his scalp on the same side. The problem representation is a child with a tender, localised lymph node with a clear, benign, and proximate cause (skin breach). This is consistent with reactive lymphadenopathy (or potentially early cat-scratch disease). The management involves reassurance, simple analgesia, and advice to monitor for resolution, with instructions to return if it enlarges significantly or fails to resolve.
