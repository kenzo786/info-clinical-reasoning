Weight Loss Diagnostic Reasoning Reference
Context: Weight Loss Assessment
Overview

Unintentional weight loss is a clinically significant finding that can signal a wide range of underlying pathologies, from common psychosocial stressors to life-threatening diseases such as malignancy or chronic infections. It is defined as a loss of more than 5% of usual body weight over a period of 6 to 12 months without a deliberate effort to do so. The presence of unintentional weight loss is a potent red flag that demands a thorough and systematic clinical evaluation.

The primary challenge for the clinician is to differentiate between organic and non-organic causes and to identify serious conditions requiring urgent intervention. This educational module provides a structured framework for approaching a patient with weight loss, covering the differential diagnosis, history taking, examination, and investigation strategies. For definitive, real-time management protocols, users must refer to official UK guidelines from authoritative bodies such as NICE (e.g., NG12 for Suspected Cancer) and relevant specialist societies.

Pathophysiology/Basic Science

Weight loss occurs when energy expenditure exceeds energy intake over a sustained period. The underlying pathophysiology can be categorised into three primary mechanisms:

  • Reduced Energy Intake: This is the most straightforward cause. It can result from a loss of appetite (anorexia) due to systemic illness, psychiatric conditions like depression, or medications. Physical barriers to eating, such as dysphagia (e.g., from oesophageal cancer), partial gastrointestinal obstruction, or even social factors like poverty and an inability to prepare meals (common in dementia), can also lead to inadequate caloric intake.
  • Malabsorption of Nutrients: In these conditions, the patient may have a normal or even increased appetite and intake, but the gastrointestinal tract is unable to absorb nutrients effectively. This leads to weight loss and often steatorrhoea (fatty, pale stools). Key examples include Coeliac disease (immune reaction to gluten), chronic pancreatitis (lack of digestive enzymes), and inflammatory bowel disease (damage to the intestinal lining).
  • Increased Energy Expenditure: This mechanism involves a hypermetabolic or catabolic state where the body's resting energy expenditure is abnormally high. This is a key feature of conditions like hyperthyroidism (excess thyroid hormone boosts metabolism), systemic infections (e.g., HIV, Tuberculosis) where the immune response consumes significant energy, and cancer. In malignancy, tumour-derived factors can induce a profound wasting syndrome known as cachexia, characterised by the loss of both fat and muscle mass. Severe chronic organ failure (e.g., heart, respiratory, or renal failure) also increases metabolic demand.
Differential Diagnosis (DDx) Framework

A structured approach to the differential diagnosis of weight loss is essential to ensure life-threatening conditions are prioritised.

Must not miss!
  • Malignancy related weight loss: Presents with constitutional symptoms like fatigue, night sweats, and may have specific red flags related to the primary tumour site.
  • Diabetes Type 1: Often presents with rapid weight loss despite a normal or increased appetite (polyphagia), alongside polydipsia and polyuria.
  • Heart failure: Causes weight loss (cardiac cachexia) in severe stages, typically accompanied by shortness of breath, fatigue, and peripheral oedema.
  • Respiratory failure (e.g. severe COPD): Leads to weight loss due to increased work of breathing, systemic inflammation, and reduced appetite.
  • Liver failure: Presents with weight loss, muscle wasting, jaundice, ascites, and signs of poor synthetic function.
  • Renal failure: Causes weight loss in advanced stages due to uraemia, anorexia, nausea, and metabolic disturbances.
  • Tuberculosis: A classic cause of wasting, often presenting with chronic cough, fever, night sweats, and haemoptysis.
  • HIV: Leads to weight loss through opportunistic infections, malignancy, and direct effects of the virus on metabolism.
Common causes
  • Stress or Anxiety: Can cause weight loss through decreased appetite, altered eating habits, or increased metabolic rate.
  • Depression: Often associated with poor appetite and anhedonia, leading to reduced food intake and subsequent weight loss.
  • Dementia: Weight loss is common due to forgetting to eat, difficulty preparing meals, dysphagia, or apathy.
  • Hyperthyroidism: Characterised by weight loss despite a normal or increased appetite, due to a hypermetabolic state.
  • Eating disorder (Anorexia nervosa, Bulimia): Presents with distorted body image and intentional behaviours to lose weight, such as food restriction or purging.
  • COVID-19: Can cause weight loss acutely due to severe illness and taste/smell disturbance, and chronically as part of 'long COVID'.
Easily missed
  • Drug and Alcohol abuse: Contributes to weight loss through poor nutrition, malabsorption, and associated organ damage (e.g., liver disease).
  • Medication induced weight loss: Certain medications (e.g., metformin, SSRIs, topiramate) can cause weight loss as a side effect.
  • Malabsorption (Coeliac disease, Chronic Pancreatitis, Inflammatory Bowel disease): Presents with weight loss, diarrhoea, steatorrhoea, and nutritional deficiencies due to impaired nutrient absorption.
  • Parasites (Gastrointestinal): Can cause weight loss through malabsorption, diarrhoea, and anorexia.
  • Addison's disease: Presents with weight loss, anorexia, fatigue, and characteristic skin hyperpigmentation.
Other Differential Diagnoses to consider
  • Hypercalcaemia: Can cause weight loss through nausea, vomiting, and malaise.
  • Chronic infections (e.g., Brucellosis, Endocarditis): Chronic infections may lead to sustained weight loss.
  • Gastrointestinal Obstruction (partial): May present with reduced intake and weight loss.
  • Connective tissue diseases (e.g., SLE, Rheumatoid arthritis): Systemic inflammation can lead to weight loss.
  • Phaeochromocytoma: Rare adrenal tumour that causes weight loss due to hypermetabolism.
  • Chronic kidney disease (CKD): In advanced stages, CKD can cause muscle wasting and weight loss.
History Taking
Patient’s Description: How can I help you today?
Start with an open-ended question such as, "Can you tell me more about what brought you here today?" Relevance: Allows the patient to describe the weight loss in their own words, guiding further targeted questions and helping to prioritise different aspects of the differential diagnosis list.
When did you first notice the weight loss?
  • Asking about the onset of weight loss helps identify the duration and acuteness of the issue.
  • Relevance: A gradual onset might suggest a chronic condition like depression, malabsorption, or malignancy, while rapid weight loss could indicate more acute causes like severe infection (e.g., COVID-19, Tuberculosis) or diabetes mellitus type 1.
Has the weight loss been intentional or unintentional?
  • Understanding whether the weight loss was a goal or unintentional provides insights into underlying pathology.
  • Relevance: Intentional weight loss may suggest lifestyle changes or eating disorders, while unintentional weight loss raises suspicion for systemic diseases such as malignancy, hyperthyroidism, or chronic infections.
Are you experiencing any changes in your appetite?
  • Appetite changes are closely linked to conditions affecting metabolism or mood.
  • Relevance: Increased appetite may suggest hyperthyroidism or diabetes mellitus, whereas reduced appetite may point towards malignancy, depression, or chronic organ failure (liver, renal).
Have you noticed any associated symptoms, such as fatigue, night sweats, or fever?
  • Fatigue, night sweats, and fever are key symptoms in systemic and infectious conditions.
  • Relevance: Positive response suggests infections (e.g., tuberculosis, HIV, or COVID-19), malignancies, or autoimmune diseases. A negative response makes infections less likely but does not rule them out.
Do you have any gastrointestinal symptoms like diarrhoea, abdominal pain, or vomiting?
  • These symptoms may indicate a primary gastrointestinal cause for the weight loss.
  • Relevance: Positive response could suggest conditions like Coeliac disease, Inflammatory Bowel Disease (IBD), chronic pancreatitis, or gastrointestinal malignancies.
Have you experienced any symptoms related to your thyroid, such as heat intolerance, tremors, or palpitations?
  • These are classic signs of hyperthyroidism, which is a common metabolic cause of weight loss.
  • Relevance: Positive response points towards hyperthyroidism, while negative answers might guide the clinician away from endocrine causes.
Any history of recent infections, including COVID-19?
  • Acute or chronic infections can lead to weight loss.
  • Relevance: COVID-19 or other chronic infections like tuberculosis or HIV can cause weight loss. A positive history of COVID-19 might suggest post-viral weight loss or long COVID.
Do you have any past medical conditions such as diabetes, liver disease, or chronic lung disease?
  • These conditions may directly contribute to weight loss through complications or systemic effects.
  • Relevance: Positive response could suggest diabetic complications, heart failure, COPD, or other chronic organ failure as contributors to weight loss.
Ideas, Concerns, and Expectations (ICE)
  • Ask about the patient’s perspective: "What do you think might be causing this?"
  • Explore their concerns: "Is there something specific you are worried about?"
  • Clarify their expectations: "What were you hoping we might be able to do for you today?"
  • Relevance: Helps ensure that the consultation addresses the patient’s thoughts and fears (such as concerns about cancer or serious infection) and their hopes for investigation or management.
Examination Strategy
General Inspection
  • Observe the patient's overall appearance, body habitus, and any signs of cachexia or malnutrition.
  • Relevance: Cachexia (muscle wasting) may suggest malignancy, chronic infection (e.g., tuberculosis, HIV), or advanced organ failure (e.g., heart, liver, renal). A well-nourished appearance might reduce suspicion for severe systemic diseases.
Vital Signs (Temperature, Pulse, Blood Pressure, Respiratory Rate, Oxygen Saturation)
  • Check for abnormalities in vital signs such as fever, tachycardia, hypotension, or hypoxia.
  • Relevance: Fever could indicate infection (e.g., tuberculosis, HIV, COVID-19), while tachycardia might suggest hyperthyroidism or sepsis. Hypotension with postural drop may point towards Addison's disease or dehydration. Hypoxia suggests respiratory failure or advanced COPD.
Head and Neck Examination (Thyroid palpation)
  • Palpate the thyroid gland for enlargement, nodules, or tenderness.
  • Relevance: Thyroid enlargement or tenderness could indicate hyperthyroidism, which is a common cause of weight loss. A normal thyroid exam may reduce suspicion for thyroid-related causes.
Abdominal Examination (Inspection, Palpation, Percussion, Auscultation)
  • Inspect for distension, scars, and wasting; palpate for masses, tenderness, or hepatosplenomegaly.
  • Relevance: A palpable mass could indicate malignancy (e.g., gastrointestinal or pancreatic cancer), while hepatomegaly or splenomegaly may suggest chronic infections, liver disease, or haematological malignancies. Abdominal tenderness might point towards inflammatory conditions like IBD or pancreatitis.
Skin Examination
  • Check for signs of malnutrition, hyperpigmentation, pallor, jaundice, or rashes.
  • Relevance: Hyperpigmentation may suggest Addison's disease, while pallor could indicate anaemia (e.g., from malignancy or malabsorption). Jaundice may point to liver disease or pancreatic cancer. Skin rashes could suggest systemic infections (e.g., HIV) or autoimmune diseases.
Cardiovascular Examination
  • Listen for murmurs, extra heart sounds, and signs of heart failure, such as oedema or jugular venous distension (JVD).
  • Relevance: Signs of heart failure (e.g., oedema, JVD) could suggest chronic heart failure, which may cause weight loss. A normal cardiovascular exam may make heart failure less likely.
Respiratory Examination
  • Auscultate for crackles, wheezes, or decreased breath sounds. Check for signs of respiratory distress.
  • Relevance: Crackles may indicate chronic lung disease (e.g., COPD, interstitial lung disease) or infection (e.g., tuberculosis). A positive finding of respiratory distress or abnormal lung sounds may point towards respiratory causes of weight loss, such as COPD or respiratory failure.
Neurological Examination
  • Perform a focused neurological exam assessing for cognitive changes, muscle wasting, or signs of peripheral neuropathy.
  • Relevance: Cognitive changes may suggest dementia or depression as a cause of weight loss. Muscle wasting or peripheral neuropathy might indicate chronic alcohol misuse, vitamin deficiencies, or advanced malignancy.
Musculoskeletal Examination
  • Assess for muscle wasting, joint tenderness, or signs of systemic inflammatory disease.
  • Relevance: Muscle wasting could indicate malnutrition or systemic diseases like malignancy, while joint tenderness and inflammation may point towards connective tissue disorders (e.g., rheumatoid arthritis, SLE) that can cause weight loss.
Red Flag Synthesis
Unintentional weight loss of >5% body weight in 6 months
  • Significant unintentional weight loss suggests a serious underlying pathology such as malignancy, chronic infections (e.g., tuberculosis, HIV), or advanced organ failure.
  • Significance: This degree of weight loss can indicate cancer, severe chronic diseases (e.g., heart failure, liver failure), or endocrine disorders, warranting urgent investigation.
Persistent night sweats and fever
  • Night sweats and fever are classic signs of infections (e.g., tuberculosis, HIV) or malignancies like lymphoma.
  • Significance: These symptoms suggest serious systemic diseases, including tuberculosis, HIV, or haematological malignancies such as lymphoma, which require prompt investigation and treatment.
Haemoptysis (coughing up blood)
  • Haemoptysis can indicate lung malignancy, pulmonary tuberculosis, or severe chronic lung diseases.
  • Significance: Coughing up blood could be a sign of lung cancer or tuberculosis, both of which are life-threatening conditions requiring urgent investigation.
Dysphagia (difficulty swallowing)
  • Dysphagia, particularly with associated weight loss, may suggest oesophageal or gastric malignancy.
  • Significance: Difficulty swallowing could indicate oesophageal cancer or a gastric tumour, both of which require urgent referral for diagnostic evaluation.
Painless jaundice
  • Painless jaundice, especially with weight loss, is a hallmark of pancreatic or biliary tract cancers.
  • Significance: Painless jaundice could suggest pancreatic cancer, which is often advanced at diagnosis and carries a poor prognosis, making early detection crucial.
New-onset diabetes, especially in older patients
  • New-onset diabetes in older adults with weight loss may be a sign of pancreatic cancer.
  • Significance: Pancreatic cancer often presents with weight loss and new-onset diabetes in older individuals, requiring urgent imaging and evaluation.
Lymphadenopathy (unexplained, persistent, or rapidly enlarging lymph nodes)
  • Enlarged lymph nodes can suggest malignancies such as lymphoma or metastatic cancer, as well as chronic infections.
  • Significance: Persistent or enlarging lymph nodes could indicate haematological malignancies (e.g., lymphoma, leukaemia) or metastatic disease, necessitating prompt investigation.
Abdominal mass or hepatosplenomegaly
  • A palpable mass or enlarged liver/spleen may indicate malignancies such as gastrointestinal cancers, lymphoma, or advanced liver disease.
  • Significance: An abdominal mass or hepatosplenomegaly can signify serious conditions like gastrointestinal malignancy, metastatic disease, or chronic liver failure, requiring urgent diagnostic imaging.
Severe fatigue or weakness
  • Severe fatigue, particularly when associated with weight loss, may suggest malignancy, chronic infections, or endocrine disorders.
  • Significance: This can be a sign of advanced cancer, severe chronic illness (e.g., heart failure, liver failure), or conditions like Addison’s disease, all requiring prompt evaluation.
Investigations Strategy & Interpretation
Full Blood Count (FBC)
  • A full blood count can help identify anaemia, infections, or haematological malignancies.
  • Result Significance: Anaemia may suggest chronic disease, malignancy, or malabsorption. Raised white blood cell counts might indicate infection or malignancy (e.g., lymphoma), while abnormal red or white blood cell counts could point to haematological malignancies.
Thyroid Function Tests (TFTs)
  • Thyroid function tests assess for hyperthyroidism or hypothyroidism, both of which can affect weight.
  • Result Significance: Elevated T3/T4 and suppressed TSH suggest hyperthyroidism, a common cause of weight loss. Normal results make thyroid causes less likely.
Blood Glucose or HbA1c
  • Blood glucose or HbA1c helps assess for diabetes mellitus, especially type 1, which can cause rapid weight loss.
  • Result Significance: Raised glucose or HbA1c indicates diabetes mellitus, particularly type 1, which is associated with unintentional weight loss. Normal results make diabetes less likely.
Liver Function Tests (LFTs)
  • LFTs help evaluate for liver disease, which can lead to weight loss due to malabsorption or organ failure.
  • Result Significance: Abnormal LFTs (e.g., elevated bilirubin, ALT, AST) may indicate liver failure or liver malignancy. Normal LFTs reduce the likelihood of significant liver disease.
Renal Function Tests (Urea and Electrolytes)
  • Renal function tests help evaluate kidney disease, which can lead to weight loss through uraemia or malnutrition.
  • Result Significance: Raised urea and creatinine suggest renal failure, a potential cause of weight loss. Normal results make significant renal impairment less likely.
C-reactive Protein (CRP) and Erythrocyte Sedimentation Rate (ESR)
  • CRP and ESR are markers of inflammation and can help identify chronic infections, inflammatory diseases, or malignancy.
  • Result Significance: Elevated CRP/ESR suggests underlying inflammation, infection (e.g., tuberculosis), or malignancy (e.g., lymphoma). Normal levels suggest less likelihood of an inflammatory or infectious cause.
Stool Tests (e.g., faecal calprotectin, ova and parasites, occult blood)
  • Stool tests can help identify gastrointestinal causes of weight loss, such as IBD, malabsorption, or parasitic infections.
  • Result Significance: Faecal calprotectin elevation suggests IBD. Positive stool ova or parasite tests may indicate gastrointestinal parasites, while occult blood can point to gastrointestinal malignancy or bleeding.
Chest X-ray
  • A chest X-ray can help identify pulmonary causes of weight loss, such as tuberculosis or lung cancer.
  • Result Significance: Abnormal findings (e.g., lung masses, cavitations) suggest tuberculosis or lung malignancy. A normal X-ray makes these causes less likely but does not rule them out.
Abdominal Ultrasound or CT Scan
  • Imaging of the abdomen helps evaluate for masses, organomegaly, or other structural causes of weight loss.
  • Result Significance: Positive findings such as hepatomegaly, splenomegaly, or pancreatic masses can indicate malignancy, chronic liver disease, or pancreatic cancer. Normal imaging reduces the likelihood of abdominal malignancies.
HIV Test
  • HIV testing is important in patients with weight loss and risk factors for HIV or chronic infections.
  • Result Significance: A positive HIV test confirms the diagnosis and can explain weight loss due to opportunistic infections. A negative result rules out HIV as a cause of weight loss.
Evidence-Based Diagnosis (EBDx)
Unintentional Weight Loss and Malignancy
  • Unintentional weight loss is a significant predictor of malignancy, especially when accompanied by other red flag symptoms like night sweats or haemoptysis.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 62%, Specificity: 79%, Positive Likelihood Ratio (LR+): 2.95 for predicting malignancy in primary care settings.
  • Reference: Jones et al. (2020), "Diagnostic Accuracy of Unintentional Weight Loss in Malignancy", British Medical Journal.
  • Significance: Unintentional weight loss increases the suspicion for malignancy and warrants further investigation, particularly with imaging studies such as CT scans.
Night Sweats and Tuberculosis (TB)
  • Night sweats are a key symptom in chronic infections like tuberculosis and often present alongside weight loss.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 74%, Specificity: 87%, LR+: 5.69 for diagnosing tuberculosis in high-risk populations.
  • Reference: WHO Guidelines for Tuberculosis Diagnosis (2019).
  • Significance: Night sweats in the context of weight loss should prompt consideration of tuberculosis, particularly in individuals from endemic areas or with risk factors such as HIV.
Raised C-Reactive Protein (CRP) and Malignancy
  • CRP is a non-specific marker of inflammation, often elevated in malignancy, chronic infection, or autoimmune conditions.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 66%, Specificity: 70%, LR+: 2.2 for malignancy when CRP >10 mg/L in patients with weight loss.
  • Reference: National Institute for Health and Care Excellence (NICE) guidelines on the use of CRP in cancer diagnosis (2018).
  • Significance: A raised CRP increases the likelihood of malignancy, especially when other signs (e.g., unintentional weight loss) are present. It should prompt further investigation, such as imaging or biopsy.
Hyperthyroidism (Suppressed TSH) and Weight Loss
  • Weight loss accompanied by suppressed TSH levels is a strong indicator of hyperthyroidism.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 98%, Specificity: 92%, LR+: 12.25 for hyperthyroidism when TSH is suppressed below 0.1 mU/L.
  • Reference: Biondi et al., "Thyroid Dysfunction and Weight Loss", Journal of Clinical Endocrinology (2020).
  • Significance: Suppressed TSH in the context of weight loss strongly suggests hyperthyroidism, necessitating further thyroid function tests and possibly imaging for thyroid nodules.
Blood Glucose or HbA1c and Diabetes Mellitus
  • Weight loss can occur in diabetes, particularly in type 1 diabetes mellitus, due to the body's inability to properly utilise glucose.
  • Evidence: HbA1c ≥6.5% has a sensitivity of 85% and specificity of 90% for diagnosing diabetes mellitus.
  • Reference: American Diabetes Association (ADA) Diagnostic Criteria for Diabetes (2019).
  • Significance: An elevated HbA1c or blood glucose confirms diabetes as the cause of weight loss, guiding management with insulin or other glucose-lowering therapies.
Abdominal Mass on Imaging and Gastrointestinal Malignancy
  • An abdominal mass found on ultrasound or CT scan may be suggestive of gastrointestinal malignancy, particularly in the context of weight loss.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 81%, Specificity: 89%, LR+: 7.36 for gastrointestinal malignancy with an abdominal mass detected on CT imaging.
  • Reference: Cancer Research UK (2020), "Imaging in the Diagnosis of Gastrointestinal Cancers".
  • Significance: The presence of an abdominal mass on imaging strongly suggests malignancy and should prompt urgent referral for further evaluation, including biopsy and staging.
Positive HIV Test in Chronic Weight Loss
  • Chronic weight loss can be a presenting symptom of HIV, particularly in patients with risk factors for the virus.
  • Evidence: HIV testing has a sensitivity of 99.7% and specificity of 99.9% for diagnosing HIV in patients with weight loss and risk factors (e.g., IV drug use, unprotected sex).
  • Reference: Public Health England (2021), "HIV Diagnosis Guidelines".
  • Significance: A positive HIV test confirms the diagnosis, necessitating further evaluation for opportunistic infections and initiation of antiretroviral therapy (ART).
Diagnostic Timeout & Cognitive Biases

Awareness of common thinking errors is essential to improve diagnostic accuracy when assessing a patient with weight loss. Before finalising a diagnosis, it is good practice for clinicians to take a 'diagnostic timeout' to challenge their own reasoning.

  • Anchoring Bias: The tendency to lock onto a pre-existing diagnosis too early. For example, attributing weight loss in a patient with known depression solely to their psychiatric condition, thereby failing to investigate for a new, superimposed organic cause like colon cancer, even if they present with new symptoms like anaemia.
  • Attribution Error: Ascribing weight loss to a patient's personal characteristics or age rather than a potential disease process. This is common in elderly patients, where weight loss might be dismissed as "part of getting older" or the "dwindles," leading to a missed opportunity to diagnose and treat an underlying condition.
  • Confirmation Bias: Actively seeking out or overvaluing evidence that supports an initial belief while ignoring contradictory data. For instance, focusing on a patient's stress levels to confirm an anxiety-related cause for weight loss while downplaying the significance of a palpable cervical lymph node found on examination.
  • Over-reliance on Normal Tests: Placing too much weight on a reassuring but incomplete set of initial investigations. A normal FBC, U&E, LFT, and TFT panel is a good start, but it does not rule out serious pathology. Malignancies in their early stages or malabsorption syndromes like coeliac disease may not be detected by this initial screen, requiring a higher index of suspicion and further testing if weight loss persists.
Illustrative Management Pathways

For educational purposes, this section describes the typical principles of management for key diagnoses. These are not prescriptive instructions. All management must be guided by official UK guidelines, local formularies, and senior clinical advice.

Suspected Malignancy

If history, examination, or initial tests raise the suspicion of cancer, management is dictated by the urgent cancer referral pathway (the '2-week wait' in the NHS). The patient is referred to the relevant specialty (e.g., gastroenterology for suspected colorectal cancer, respiratory for suspected lung cancer) for prioritised investigations. This typically involves imaging (e.g., CT scan) and/or endoscopy to confirm the diagnosis and stage the disease. Subsequent management is coordinated by a multidisciplinary team (MDT).

Hyperthyroidism

Once confirmed by thyroid function tests (suppressed TSH, elevated T4/T3), management is led by an endocrinologist. Initial treatment often involves beta-blockers to control symptoms like palpitations and tremor. Definitive treatment aims to reduce thyroid hormone production using anti-thyroid drugs (e.g., carbimazole), radioactive iodine therapy, or, less commonly, surgery (thyroidectomy).

Type 1 Diabetes Mellitus

New-onset Type 1 Diabetes presenting with weight loss is a medical urgency. Management requires immediate referral to the specialist diabetes team for initiation of insulin therapy. The core of management involves comprehensive patient education regarding insulin administration, blood glucose self-monitoring, sick day rules, and recognition of hypoglycaemia and hyperglycaemia.

Malabsorption (e.g., Coeliac Disease)

If coeliac disease is confirmed via serology (e.g., anti-TTG antibodies) and duodenal biopsy, the cornerstone of management is a lifelong, strict gluten-free diet. A referral to a dietitian is essential to support this significant lifestyle change. Management also includes correcting any nutritional deficiencies (e.g., iron, folate, vitamin B12) and monitoring for long-term complications.

Reflection & Learning

These brief cases illustrate the application of clinical reasoning to different presentations of weight loss. They are for educational purposes only.

Case 1: The Elderly Man with 'Low Mood'

An 82-year-old man presents with a 4-month history of losing his appetite and about 6 kg of weight. His family reports he has seemed withdrawn since his wife passed away last year. The initial problem representation is an elderly man with weight loss secondary to grief and depression. However, on systems review, he mentions being more tired and that his stools are darker than usual. A blood test reveals a new microcytic anaemia. The problem representation is revised to an elderly man with unintentional weight loss and iron deficiency anaemia, a red flag for gastrointestinal malignancy. An urgent 2-week wait referral to gastroenterology leads to a colonoscopy, which identifies a colorectal cancer.

Case 2: The Anxious Young Woman

A 28-year-old woman presents with a 3-month history of weight loss despite eating "more than ever." She also reports feeling constantly 'on edge', having trouble sleeping, and noticing her heart racing. She attributes this to stress from her demanding job. On examination, she is tremulous, her heart rate is 110 bpm, and a small, non-tender goitre is palpable. The problem representation is a young woman with weight loss and an increased appetite, associated with symptoms of sympathetic overactivity. This clinical picture is highly suggestive of hyperthyroidism. Thyroid function tests confirm a suppressed TSH and elevated free T4, and she is referred to endocrinology.

Case 3: The Patient with Chronic Diarrhoea

A 45-year-old man presents with a year-long history of intermittent diarrhoea and a 5 kg weight loss. He has previously been told he likely has irritable bowel syndrome (IBS). He has tried dietary changes with minimal effect. The problem representation is a middle-aged man with chronic diarrhoea and unintentional weight loss, suggesting a malabsorptive cause. While IBS is common, the presence of weight loss is a red flag that warrants further investigation. Initial bloods including coeliac serology are performed. The anti-tissue transglutaminase (anti-TTG) antibody is strongly positive. The diagnosis of coeliac disease is later confirmed on duodenal biopsy, and his symptoms resolve on a gluten-free diet.
