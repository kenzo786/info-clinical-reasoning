Abnormal Vaginal Bleeding Diagnostic Reasoning Reference
Context: Abnormal Vaginal Bleeding Assessment
Overview

Abnormal vaginal bleeding (AVB) is a frequent and important presentation in both primary and emergency care, encompassing a wide spectrum of underlying pathologies. The causes range from benign physiological variations and hormonal dysfunctions to life-threatening emergencies like ectopic pregnancy and malignancy. For the clinician, the core challenge is to systematically differentiate between these possibilities, ensuring that urgent conditions are identified promptly while avoiding unnecessary investigation for less serious causes. The assessment must be tailored to the patient's age and reproductive status, as the differential diagnosis shifts significantly between pre-menarchal, reproductive-aged, and post-menopausal women.

This educational module provides a structured framework for the clinical reasoning process involved in assessing a patient with abnormal vaginal bleeding. It is designed for educational purposes only and is not a substitute for clinical judgment or official guidelines. For definitive, real-time management protocols, clinicians must consult authoritative UK guidance from bodies such as the National Institute for Health and Care Excellence (NICE), the Royal College of Obstetricians and Gynaecologists (RCOG), and the Faculty of Sexual & Reproductive Healthcare (FSRH).

Pathophysiology/Basic Science

Understanding the mechanisms behind abnormal vaginal bleeding is fundamental to forming an accurate differential diagnosis. The bleeding can originate from the uterus, cervix, vagina, or, rarely, from extra-gynaecological sites. Key pathophysiological mechanisms include:

  • Structural Uterine Abnormalities: Conditions like uterine fibroids (leiomyomas), endometrial polyps, and adenomyosis disrupt the normal architecture of the uterus. They can cause bleeding by distorting the endometrial cavity, increasing the surface area of the endometrium, impairing normal uterine contractility (preventing vasoconstriction of spiral arteries during menstruation), or through ulceration of the overlying endometrium.
  • Hormonal Dysregulation (Anovulatory Bleeding): The regular, cyclical shedding of the endometrium is governed by the coordinated rise and fall of oestrogen and progesterone, controlled by the Hypothalamic-Pituitary-Ovarian (HPO) axis. In anovulatory cycles (common in PCOS, perimenopause, and thyroid disorders), there is a continuous production of oestrogen without the opposing effect of progesterone that normally follows ovulation. This "unopposed oestrogen" leads to excessive proliferation of the endometrium, which becomes unstable and eventually sheds irregularly and often heavily.
  • Pregnancy-Related Complications: In early pregnancy, bleeding can result from implantation, threatened or actual miscarriage (due to detachment of the gestational sac), or an ectopic pregnancy (where implantation outside the uterus can erode local blood vessels). In later pregnancy, placental causes like placenta praevia (placenta covering the cervix) or placental abruption (premature separation of the placenta) are major causes of severe bleeding.
  • Inflammation and Infection: Pelvic Inflammatory Disease (PID), endometritis, and cervicitis cause inflammation of the genital tract. This leads to increased blood flow (hyperaemia), micro-ulceration of mucosal surfaces, and endometrial instability, all of which can manifest as intermenstrual or post-coital bleeding.
  • Malignancy and Premalignancy: Endometrial, cervical, and vaginal cancers cause bleeding through the erosion of tissue and the formation of fragile, abnormal blood vessels (neovascularisation) that are prone to rupture. Endometrial hyperplasia, a precursor to endometrial cancer, involves excessive proliferation of the endometrium which can also lead to abnormal bleeding.
  • Systemic and Iatrogenic Causes: Systemic conditions like coagulation disorders (e.g., von Willebrand disease) directly impair the blood clotting process, leading to heavy menstrual bleeding. Medications, particularly hormonal contraceptives, can alter the stability of the endometrium causing breakthrough bleeding, while anticoagulants can exacerbate any underlying bleeding tendency.
Differential Diagnosis (DDx) Framework

A structured approach to the differential diagnosis of abnormal vaginal bleeding is essential to ensure life-threatening conditions are prioritised based on the patient's age and clinical context.

Must not miss!
  • Ectopic pregnancy: Typically presents in early pregnancy with abdominal pain, vaginal bleeding, and potential haemodynamic collapse.
  • Miscarriage: Presents with crampy abdominal pain and vaginal bleeding, often with the passage of products of conception.
  • Placental abruption: A pregnancy emergency with sudden-onset painful vaginal bleeding and uterine tenderness.
  • Placenta previa: Characterised by painless, bright red vaginal bleeding in the second or third trimester of pregnancy.
  • Endometritis: Uterine infection, often postpartum or related to PID, causing bleeding, pelvic pain, and fever.
  • Pelvic Inflammatory Disease: Infection of the upper genital tract presenting with pelvic pain, deep dyspareunia, abnormal discharge, and bleeding.
  • Cervicitis: Inflammation of the cervix, often due to infection, causing post-coital or intermenstrual bleeding and discharge.
  • Vaginitis: Inflammation of the vagina causing discharge, soreness, and sometimes spotting.
  • Cervical cancer: Often presents with post-coital bleeding, intermenstrual bleeding, or an abnormal cervical appearance.
  • Endometrial cancer: The classic presentation is postmenopausal bleeding, but can occur in younger women with risk factors.
  • Vaginal cancer: A rare malignancy that can present with abnormal bleeding, a mass, or discharge.
  • Ovarian cancer: May present with persistent bloating, abdominal pain, and sometimes abnormal bleeding as a late sign.
  • Molar pregnancy: A gestational trophoblastic disease presenting with exaggerated signs of pregnancy and vaginal bleeding.
  • Sexual abuse: Must always be considered as a cause of unexplained genital bleeding, particularly in children and vulnerable adults.
Common causes
  • Menorrhagia: Clinically defined as heavy or prolonged menstrual bleeding that interferes with a woman's quality of life.
  • Fibroids: Benign uterine tumours that are a very common cause of heavy menstrual bleeding and pressure symptoms.
  • Contraceptive related bleeding: Irregular bleeding or spotting is a common side effect of hormonal contraception and IUDs.
  • PCOS: Polycystic Ovarian Syndrome often causes irregular, infrequent periods, but can also present with heavy, erratic bleeding.
  • Endometriosis: Can cause heavy, painful periods, as well as premenstrual spotting or intermenstrual bleeding.
  • Endometrial Hyperplasia: Pre-cancerous thickening of the uterine lining, typically causing heavy or irregular bleeding.
  • Cervical ectropion: A benign condition where glandular cells from the endocervix are present on the ectocervix, often causing post-coital bleeding.
  • Polyps: Benign growths (cervical or endometrial) that can cause intermenstrual or post-coital spotting.
  • DUB: Dysfunctional Uterine Bleeding is a diagnosis of exclusion for abnormal bleeding with no identifiable structural or systemic cause.
Easily missed
  • Hypothyroidism: An underactive thyroid can disrupt the HPO axis, leading to heavy or irregular periods.
  • Hyperthyroidism: An overactive thyroid can cause light or irregular periods.
  • Medications-induced Vaginal Bleeding: Bleeding can be a side effect of anticoagulants, SSRIs, and some herbal supplements.
  • Bleeding disorders: Inherited coagulopathies like von Willebrand disease are an important underlying cause of heavy menstrual bleeding from menarche.
  • Hyperprolactinaemia: Elevated prolactin levels can suppress ovulation and cause menstrual irregularities and bleeding.
Other Differential Diagnoses to consider
  • COVID-19: Rare cases of abnormal vaginal bleeding have been reported in association with COVID-19 infection or its treatment.
  • Stress-induced amenorrhoea: Chronic stress affecting the hypothalamic-pituitary axis can lead to menstrual irregularities.
  • Coagulation disorders: Less common inherited clotting disorders, such as haemophilia carriers, may present with abnormal bleeding.
  • Adrenal disorders: Cushing’s syndrome or adrenal insufficiency can disrupt menstrual cycles.
  • Diabetes mellitus: Poorly controlled diabetes can impact menstrual patterns due to effects on metabolism and hormonal regulation.
History Taking
History taking for Abnormal Vaginal Bleeding presentation
**Patient’s Description**: How can I help you today?
Starting with an open-ended question allows the patient to describe their symptoms in their own words, without being influenced by specific medical terminology. It helps build rapport and may provide clues about the severity or context of the problem. Relevance: Allows the patient to describe the issue in their own words, guiding further targeted questions.
When did the bleeding start?
  • This question helps establish the timing of the bleeding, which is crucial for determining the acuity of the condition. Acute onset suggests causes such as miscarriage or ectopic pregnancy, while more chronic symptoms might indicate fibroids or hormonal imbalance.
  • Relevance: Sudden onset might suggest pregnancy-related complications or infection, whereas a more gradual onset points towards structural causes like fibroids or endometriosis.
Is the bleeding heavy, light, or spotting? How does it compare to your normal period?
  • Understanding the volume and pattern of bleeding can help differentiate between causes. Heavy bleeding could indicate fibroids or hormonal imbalances, whereas spotting may suggest cervical ectropion, infection, or early pregnancy complications.
  • Relevance: Heavy bleeding might suggest fibroids or endometrial pathology, while light or spotting could point to hormonal issues or infection. Helps differentiate abnormal bleeding from typical menstrual variation.
Are there any triggers or factors that make the bleeding worse or better?
  • Triggers, such as intercourse-related bleeding, could suggest cervical causes (e.g., cervical ectropion, polyps, or malignancy). Understanding exacerbating factors can help narrow the differential.
  • Relevance: Post-coital bleeding suggests cervical causes, while relief from hormonal contraceptives may suggest a hormonal aetiology.
Have you experienced any associated pain, cramps, or discomfort?
  • Pain associated with abnormal bleeding could indicate ectopic pregnancy, miscarriage, endometriosis, or infection such as pelvic inflammatory disease (PID).
  • Relevance: Painful bleeding is concerning for ectopic pregnancy or PID, whereas painless bleeding might indicate a structural cause or malignancy.
Have you had any recent changes in weight, diet, or exercise levels?
  • Changes in weight or lifestyle can affect the hypothalamic-pituitary-ovarian axis, potentially leading to menstrual irregularities or bleeding.
  • Relevance: Recent weight loss or excessive exercise could point to hypothalamic amenorrhoea, while obesity is a risk factor for endometrial hyperplasia or cancer.
Have you noticed any unusual vaginal discharge, fever, or pelvic pain?
  • These are important indicators of infection, such as pelvic inflammatory disease, cervicitis, or endometritis, which may also present with abnormal bleeding.
  • Relevance: A positive response suggests infection (PID, endometritis), while the absence of these symptoms could help rule out infectious causes.
Are you currently using any form of contraception, or have you changed contraception recently?
  • Hormonal contraception can affect bleeding patterns, and intrauterine devices (IUDs) are commonly associated with spotting or heavy periods.
  • Relevance: A positive response suggests a contraceptive-related cause of the bleeding, while lack of contraception use could indicate pregnancy-related causes or other factors.
Is there any chance that you could be pregnant?
  • Pregnancy must be considered in all women of childbearing age with abnormal vaginal bleeding. Ectopic pregnancy or miscarriage are serious conditions that require urgent attention.
  • Relevance: A positive answer requires urgent pregnancy testing to rule out ectopic pregnancy or miscarriage. A negative answer helps focus on other non-pregnancy-related causes.
**Ideas, Concerns, and Expectations (ICE)**
  • Ideas: Asking the patient what they think might be causing the problem can reveal underlying fears or misconceptions. It may also provide insight into how informed they are about their health.
  • Concerns: Identifying the patient's worries can help guide the consultation. For instance, they may be concerned about cancer, a common fear in cases of abnormal bleeding.
  • Expectations: Understanding the patient’s expectations ensures that their needs are addressed, whether it’s symptom relief, further investigation, or reassurance.
  • Relevance: Helps ensure that the consultation addresses the patient’s perspective, worries (such as cancer or pregnancy complications), and expectations.
Examination Strategy
General Inspection
  • Begin by assessing the patient's general appearance, including signs of pallor, fatigue, or weight loss, which may suggest anaemia or malignancy. Note any signs of distress or discomfort.
  • Relevance: Pallor may indicate anaemia due to chronic blood loss, suggesting conditions like fibroids or malignancy. Absence of these signs might rule out severe anaemia but does not exclude the possibility of less acute causes.
Abdominal Examination
  • Palpate for tenderness, masses, or organomegaly. Tenderness might suggest infection (e.g., PID or endometritis), while a palpable mass could indicate fibroids, an ovarian cyst, or pregnancy-related complications.
  • Relevance: Tenderness over the lower abdomen suggests PID, ectopic pregnancy, or endometriosis. A mass may indicate fibroids or an ovarian mass. Absence of tenderness and masses helps rule out these conditions.
Pelvic Examination - Inspection
  • Inspect the external genitalia, vaginal walls, and cervix for lesions, discharge, or signs of infection. Look for cervical ectropion, polyps, or malignancy.
  • Relevance: Visible discharge suggests infection (e.g., cervicitis or vaginitis), while abnormal cervical appearance (e.g., bleeding on contact) could indicate ectropion or malignancy. Normal inspection might suggest a more internal cause like fibroids.
Bimanual Examination
  • This step assesses the size, shape, and mobility of the uterus, as well as tenderness in the adnexal regions. It helps identify masses or tenderness suggestive of infection or structural abnormalities.
  • Relevance: Uterine enlargement may suggest fibroids or pregnancy-related conditions, while adnexal tenderness raises concern for ectopic pregnancy, ovarian cysts, or PID. Lack of findings suggests a more subtle hormonal or systemic cause.
Speculum Examination
  • Use a speculum to inspect the cervix and vaginal walls. Assess for contact bleeding, cervical discharge, polyps, or cervical lesions. This is essential for visualising causes of local bleeding.
  • Relevance: Contact bleeding from the cervix may indicate malignancy, cervical ectropion, or infection. Cervical polyps can be a benign cause of spotting. Absence of these findings may direct attention towards uterine or hormonal causes.
Vaginal Discharge Assessment
  • Assess the character, colour, and odour of any vaginal discharge. Abnormal discharge may point towards infection, such as cervicitis or vaginitis.
  • Relevance: Purulent or foul-smelling discharge suggests infection (e.g., PID, bacterial vaginosis), while a lack of discharge might suggest a non-infectious cause of abnormal bleeding.
Rectal Examination
  • A rectal examination may be useful in cases where pelvic masses are suspected or where there is concern for rectal involvement in malignancy.
  • Relevance: Palpable masses in the rectum or pelvic region may indicate advanced malignancy or severe pelvic disease. A normal exam helps rule out rectal involvement.
Red Flag Synthesis
Heavy bleeding with signs of hypovolaemia (dizziness, fainting, tachycardia, hypotension)
  • Heavy vaginal bleeding, especially when accompanied by symptoms of shock such as dizziness, fainting, rapid heart rate, or low blood pressure, may indicate significant blood loss.
  • Significance: This could indicate life-threatening haemorrhage, such as from a ruptured ectopic pregnancy, placental abruption, or severe fibroid-related bleeding, requiring urgent intervention.
Severe abdominal pain with bleeding
  • Severe abdominal pain alongside abnormal vaginal bleeding can signal a surgical emergency, particularly in pregnancy-related conditions or pelvic infections.
  • Significance: This is a red flag for ectopic pregnancy, ovarian torsion, or severe pelvic inflammatory disease (PID), all of which require urgent evaluation and treatment.
Postmenopausal bleeding
  • Bleeding after menopause is abnormal and is a red flag for endometrial or cervical pathology, including malignancy.
  • Significance: Postmenopausal bleeding is a significant red flag for endometrial cancer, which requires prompt investigation with transvaginal ultrasound and biopsy.
Intermenstrual bleeding or post-coital bleeding
  • Bleeding between periods or following sexual intercourse may indicate cervical pathology, including malignancy or cervical ectropion.
  • Significance: This could suggest cervical cancer or other significant cervical lesions, which require further investigation with a speculum examination and referral for colposcopy.
Fever, purulent discharge, and pelvic pain
  • Fever, foul-smelling vaginal discharge, and pelvic pain suggest an active infection, such as pelvic inflammatory disease (PID) or endometritis, which may lead to severe complications if untreated.
  • Significance: These signs may indicate a pelvic infection, such as PID or endometritis, which can cause infertility or sepsis if not promptly treated.
Unintentional weight loss, fatigue, and abnormal bleeding
  • Systemic symptoms like weight loss, fatigue, and abnormal vaginal bleeding may be signs of malignancy or advanced systemic illness.
  • Significance: This is a red flag for malignancies, including cervical, endometrial, or ovarian cancer, requiring urgent diagnostic work-up.
Signs of sexual trauma or abuse
  • Unexplained vaginal bleeding with physical or emotional signs of trauma may indicate sexual abuse, which requires a sensitive and urgent response.
  • Significance: Sexual abuse can present with vaginal bleeding and requires immediate safeguarding, psychological support, and forensic evaluation.
Investigations Strategy & Interpretation
Pregnancy Test (Urine or Serum hCG)
  • This is a crucial first-line investigation for any woman of childbearing age presenting with abnormal vaginal bleeding. It helps to confirm or rule out pregnancy, which could explain the bleeding.
  • Result Significance: A positive result suggests early pregnancy, ectopic pregnancy, or miscarriage. A negative result helps rule out pregnancy-related causes of bleeding.
Full Blood Count (FBC)
  • FBC helps assess the degree of blood loss and can reveal anaemia, which is common in cases of heavy or prolonged bleeding.
  • Result Significance: Low haemoglobin indicates anaemia, often due to chronic blood loss (e.g., from fibroids or endometrial hyperplasia). A normal result does not exclude significant blood loss if bleeding is acute or intermittent.
Coagulation Profile
  • A coagulation profile assesses for underlying bleeding disorders, especially in women with heavy menstrual bleeding or a history of easy bruising or prolonged bleeding after minor injuries.
  • Result Significance: Abnormal results may indicate a bleeding disorder (e.g., von Willebrand disease or thrombocytopenia), while normal results help rule out coagulopathies as a cause.
Transvaginal Ultrasound (TVUS)
  • TVUS is a key imaging modality to assess the uterus, ovaries, and endometrium. It helps detect structural causes of bleeding, such as fibroids, polyps, or thickened endometrium.
  • Result Significance: Positive findings, such as fibroids, ovarian cysts, or a thickened endometrium, suggest a structural cause of the bleeding. A normal result might point towards hormonal or systemic causes.
Endometrial Biopsy
  • This test is important in women over 45 or with risk factors for endometrial cancer. It helps to identify endometrial hyperplasia or malignancy.
  • Result Significance: Positive findings of hyperplasia or malignancy necessitate further management. A negative result helps exclude endometrial cancer as a cause of the bleeding.
Cervical Smear (Pap Smear)
  • A cervical smear helps detect cervical dysplasia or malignancy, which can present with abnormal bleeding, especially post-coital or intermenstrual bleeding.
  • Result Significance: Positive results suggest cervical cancer or pre-cancerous changes, while negative results lower the likelihood of cervical malignancy as a cause of bleeding.
High Vaginal and Endocervical Swabs
  • These swabs are used to assess for infections, such as pelvic inflammatory disease (PID), cervicitis, or vaginitis, which can cause abnormal bleeding.
  • Result Significance: Positive results for pathogens (e.g., chlamydia, gonorrhoea, or bacterial vaginosis) indicate infection, which requires antibiotic treatment. Negative results help exclude infectious causes of bleeding.
Thyroid Function Tests (TFTs)
  • Thyroid dysfunction, both hypo- and hyperthyroidism, can disrupt the menstrual cycle and cause abnormal vaginal bleeding.
  • Result Significance: Abnormal results (elevated TSH in hypothyroidism, suppressed TSH in hyperthyroidism) suggest thyroid dysfunction as the underlying cause of bleeding. Normal results help rule out thyroid-related causes.
Serum Prolactin
  • Elevated prolactin levels can indicate hyperprolactinaemia, which may cause irregular or absent menstrual cycles and abnormal bleeding.
  • Result Significance: A raised prolactin level suggests hyperprolactinaemia as a cause of bleeding. A normal result makes this diagnosis unlikely.
Evidence-Based Diagnosis (EBDx)
Positive Pregnancy Test (Urine or Serum hCG) in reproductive-aged women
  • A positive pregnancy test in a woman of reproductive age presenting with abnormal bleeding is highly suggestive of a pregnancy-related cause, such as miscarriage or ectopic pregnancy. This is one of the first investigations done in these cases.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 99%, Specificity: 99% for pregnancy. A serum hCG level that does not rise appropriately is suggestive of ectopic pregnancy or failing pregnancy.
  • Reference: Royal College of Obstetricians and Gynaecologists (RCOG) Ectopic Pregnancy Guidelines.
  • Significance: A positive test suggests pregnancy-related causes (e.g., miscarriage, ectopic pregnancy), while a negative result rules out these causes.
Transvaginal Ultrasound (TVUS) for detecting uterine fibroids or polyps
  • TVUS is highly effective in identifying structural abnormalities such as fibroids, polyps, or endometrial thickening. It is the gold standard imaging tool for assessing abnormal uterine bleeding.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 90%, Specificity: 89% for detecting uterine fibroids. Likelihood Ratio: 8.1 for fibroids with positive findings on ultrasound.
  • Reference: ACOG Practice Bulletin on the Management of Abnormal Uterine Bleeding.
  • Significance: A positive finding suggests fibroids or polyps, while a normal result helps rule out structural causes of abnormal bleeding.
Endometrial Biopsy for detecting endometrial hyperplasia or cancer
  • An endometrial biopsy is highly accurate for diagnosing endometrial hyperplasia and endometrial cancer, especially in postmenopausal women or those with prolonged, heavy bleeding.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 91%, Specificity: 98% for detecting endometrial cancer. Likelihood Ratio: 45.5 for malignancy in abnormal biopsy results.
  • Reference: NICE Guidelines on Heavy Menstrual Bleeding and Endometrial Cancer Risk.
  • Significance: A positive biopsy result indicates endometrial hyperplasia or cancer, requiring further management. A negative result helps exclude malignancy as a cause.
Cervical Smear (Pap Smear) for cervical cancer
  • Cervical smears are highly effective for screening and early detection of cervical dysplasia or cervical cancer, particularly in women with post-coital or intermenstrual bleeding.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 70-80% for detecting cervical abnormalities. Specificity: 90-95% for detecting high-grade cervical lesions.
  • Reference: NHS Cervical Screening Programme Guidelines.
  • Significance: A positive result suggests cervical dysplasia or cancer, necessitating colposcopy. A negative result reduces the likelihood of cervical malignancy.
Thyroid Function Tests (TFTs) for abnormal bleeding related to thyroid dysfunction
  • Thyroid dysfunction, particularly hypothyroidism, can cause abnormal menstrual bleeding due to hormonal imbalances. TFTs are important in cases of unexplained or chronic bleeding.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 90% for diagnosing hypothyroidism. Specificity: 85% for distinguishing thyroid-related menstrual issues.
  • Reference: British Thyroid Association Guidelines.
  • Significance: Abnormal TFTs suggest thyroid dysfunction as the cause of abnormal bleeding. Normal results help rule out thyroid-related causes.
Coagulation Profile for bleeding disorders
  • Coagulation tests help diagnose inherited or acquired bleeding disorders, such as von Willebrand disease, which may cause heavy or prolonged menstrual bleeding.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 95% for detecting bleeding disorders in women with menorrhagia. Specificity: 90% for ruling out coagulopathies.
  • Reference: RCOG Guidelines on the Management of Menorrhagia.
  • Significance: Abnormal results suggest a bleeding disorder, while normal results help exclude coagulopathies as a cause of bleeding.
Diagnostic Timeout & Cognitive Biases

In the assessment of abnormal vaginal bleeding, cognitive biases can lead to diagnostic errors. A 'diagnostic timeout' to reflect on one's reasoning is a crucial step to enhance patient safety.

  • Anchoring Bias: This is the tendency to fixate on initial information. For example, attributing a young woman's intermenstrual bleeding solely to her new hormonal contraceptive (a common cause) without performing a speculum examination or considering sexually transmitted infections or the possibility of pregnancy. A crucial counter-question is: "If it wasn't the pill, what else could it be?"
  • Availability Heuristic: Overestimating the likelihood of diagnoses that are more recent or memorable. A clinician who has recently managed a case of endometrial cancer might be more inclined to suspect it in other patients, potentially leading to unnecessary anxiety and investigation for what is a more common benign cause.
  • Atypical Presentations: A significant pitfall is failing to recognise that serious conditions can present unusually. Endometrial cancer is most common post-menopause, but it can occur in younger women, especially those with risk factors like obesity or PCOS. Similarly, a bleeding disorder may present for the first time as heavy menstrual bleeding at menarche. Always consider the 'worst-case scenario' relevant to the patient's specific risk profile.
  • Normalisation Bias: The error of explaining away or downplaying findings. Dismissing post-coital bleeding as 'probably just an ectropion' without a proper examination, or reassuring a postmenopausal woman that 'a little bit of spotting is probably nothing' are dangerous assumptions. In the UK, postmenopausal bleeding is considered endometrial cancer until proven otherwise and requires urgent investigation.
Illustrative Management Pathways

The following pathways are for educational illustration only. Management must always be guided by current national and local guidelines, and senior clinical advice.

Heavy Menstrual Bleeding (HMB) in Primary Care

For a reproductive-aged woman presenting with HMB without red flags, after excluding pregnancy, the initial focus is on assessment and medical management. This includes a full blood count to check for anaemia. First-line medical treatments, as per NICE guidelines, often include the levonorgestrel-releasing intrauterine system (LNG-IUS). Other options are tranexamic or mefenamic acid, or combined hormonal contraception. If initial treatment fails, or if there are clinical findings such as a palpable pelvic mass, referral for a transvaginal ultrasound is indicated to look for structural causes like fibroids or polyps. Subsequent referral to gynaecology may be needed for further management like hysteroscopy or endometrial ablation.

Suspected Ectopic Pregnancy

A woman of reproductive age presenting with bleeding, abdominal pain, and a positive pregnancy test requires immediate exclusion of an ectopic pregnancy. This constitutes a medical emergency. The patient should be referred urgently to an Early Pregnancy Assessment Unit (EPAU). The diagnostic pathway involves serial serum hCG measurements and a transvaginal ultrasound. An hCG level rising sub-optimally or the visualisation of an adnexal mass with an empty uterus is highly suggestive of an ectopic pregnancy. Management depends on clinical stability, hCG levels, and ultrasound findings, and can be expectant, medical (with methotrexate), or surgical (salpingectomy or salpingotomy).

Postmenopausal Bleeding (PMB)

Any vaginal bleeding in a woman more than 12 months after her last menstrual period is considered postmenopausal bleeding and requires urgent investigation to rule out endometrial cancer. Under UK NICE guidelines, this triggers an urgent 'two-week wait' referral to a gynaecology service. The standard initial investigation is a transvaginal ultrasound to measure the endometrial thickness. A thickened endometrium (typically >4-5mm in a postmenopausal woman) necessitates further sampling via a pipelle biopsy in clinic or a hysteroscopy and curettage under anaesthetic.

Suspected Pelvic Inflammatory Disease (PID)

A sexually active young woman presenting with pelvic pain, deep dyspareunia, abnormal bleeding (intermenstrual or post-coital), and abnormal discharge should be treated for PID. A low threshold for empirical treatment is recommended as untreated PID can lead to infertility. High vaginal and endocervical swabs should be taken for chlamydia and gonorrhoea. Broad-spectrum antibiotics covering likely pathogens are started immediately, following local or national guidelines. Contact tracing for sexual partners is essential. Admission for intravenous antibiotics is considered if the patient is systemically unwell, pregnant, or has signs of a pelvic abscess.

Reflection & Learning

These brief cases are for educational purposes to illustrate clinical reasoning. They are not prescriptive management plans.

Case 1: Heavy Periods in Primary Care

A 42-year-old woman presents to her GP with a one-year history of increasingly heavy and painful periods, now lasting 8 days. She uses double protection and passes large clots. She feels tired but has no intermenstrual or post-coital bleeding. A full blood count reveals iron deficiency anaemia. Bimanual examination reveals a bulky, non-tender uterus. The problem representation is a perimenopausal woman with progressive heavy menstrual bleeding causing anaemia, likely due to a structural cause like fibroids or adenomyosis. The GP initiates iron replacement, provides initial medical treatment (e.g., tranexamic acid), and refers her for a transvaginal ultrasound to clarify the diagnosis and guide further management.

Case 2: Post-Coital Bleeding in a Young Woman

A 23-year-old woman presents to a sexual health clinic with spotting after intercourse for the last two months. She is on the combined oral contraceptive pill and has a new partner. She has no other symptoms. The problem representation is a young, sexually active woman with post-coital bleeding, raising suspicion for cervical pathology or infection. A speculum examination is essential. It reveals a cervical ectropion which bleeds on contact. Swabs are taken for STIs. After her negative swab results return, she is reassured about the benign nature of the ectropion. This case highlights the importance of examination to differentiate common benign causes from more serious pathology like cervicitis or cervical cancer.

Case 3: Urgent Referral for Postmenopausal Bleeding

A 68-year-old woman presents to her GP after noticing a single episode of blood on the toilet paper after urinating. She has been postmenopausal for 15 years and takes medication for hypertension. She feels otherwise well. The problem representation is a postmenopausal woman with vaginal bleeding. This is a red flag symptom. Despite the minimal amount of bleeding and lack of other symptoms, this warrants an urgent two-week wait referral to gynaecology as per NICE guidelines to exclude endometrial cancer. The case underscores the principle that the volume of postmenopausal bleeding does not correlate with the seriousness of the underlying cause.
