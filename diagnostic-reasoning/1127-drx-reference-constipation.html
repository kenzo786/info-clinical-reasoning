Constipation Diagnostic Reasoning Reference
Context: Constipation Assessment
Overview

Constipation is an exceptionally common symptom encountered across all fields of medicine, from general practice to specialist surgical and medical wards. While often perceived as a benign issue, it can significantly impair quality of life and, more importantly, can be the presenting feature of serious underlying pathology, such as colorectal cancer. The prevalence of constipation increases with age and is a frequent comorbidity in patients with neurological conditions or those on multiple medications.

The core clinical challenge is to effectively differentiate between functional constipation, which can be managed with lifestyle advice and laxatives, and constipation caused by a secondary, potentially life-threatening condition. A systematic and structured approach to history, examination, and investigation is therefore crucial to provide reassurance and appropriate treatment for benign causes while ensuring urgent referral and management for patients with red flag features.

This educational module is designed to build a foundational understanding of the clinical reasoning process for constipation. For definitive, real-time management protocols, users must refer to official UK guidelines from authoritative bodies such as NICE (e.g., Clinical Knowledge Summary for Constipation; NG12 for Suspected Cancer) and the British Society of Gastroenterology.

Pathophysiology/Basic Science

Understanding the underlying mechanism of constipation is fundamental to effective diagnosis and management. Constipation can be broadly classified into primary (functional) and secondary causes.

  • Primary (Functional) Constipation: This occurs when there is no underlying structural or systemic disease. It is typically subdivided into three overlapping types:
    • Slow-Transit Constipation: Characterised by reduced colonic propulsive activity. The movement of stool through the colon is delayed, leading to infrequent, hard stools. This can be idiopathic or associated with autonomic nervous system dysfunction.
    • Normal-Transit Constipation: This is the most common subtype. Despite stool moving through the colon at a normal speed, patients perceive difficulty with evacuation or report hard stools. It often overlaps significantly with Irritable Bowel Syndrome with Constipation (IBS-C).
    • Defecatory or Evacuation Disorders: This involves dysfunction of the pelvic floor muscles and/or anal sphincters. Instead of relaxing to allow stool to pass, the muscles may paradoxically contract (dyssynergic defecation), or there may be structural issues (e.g., rectocele) that physically impede evacuation. Patients often describe a sensation of incomplete emptying or prolonged straining.
  • Secondary Causes: Constipation arises as a consequence of another medical condition, medication, or dietary factor.
    • Structural Abnormalities: Mechanical obstruction, either intrinsic (e.g., colorectal cancer, inflammatory strictures) or extrinsic (e.g., compression from an ovarian tumour), can prevent the passage of stool.
    • Metabolic and Endocrine Disorders: Conditions like hypothyroidism decrease gut motility as part of a general slowing of metabolic processes. Hypercalcaemia can impair the function of smooth muscle in the colonic wall, leading to constipation. Diabetes mellitus can cause autonomic neuropathy affecting the nerves that control gut transit.
    • Neurological Conditions: Diseases affecting the central or peripheral nervous system, such as Parkinson's disease, multiple sclerosis, and spinal cord injuries, frequently disrupt autonomic control of the colon, leading to severe slow-transit constipation.
    • Medication-Induced: A vast number of common medications can cause constipation. Opioids are a classic example, as they bind to mu-receptors in the gut wall, reducing peristalsis. Other major culprits include anticholinergic drugs, calcium channel blockers, and iron supplements.
Differential Diagnosis (DDx) Framework

A structured approach to the differential diagnosis of constipation is essential to ensure life-threatening conditions are prioritised.

Must not miss!
  • Colorectal cancer: Often presents with a persistent change in bowel habit, rectal bleeding, abdominal pain, and unexplained weight loss, particularly in older adults.
  • Intestinal obstruction: Characterised by the inability to pass stools or flatus, abdominal distension, colicky pain, and vomiting.
  • Hirschsprung's (children): A congenital condition causing functional obstruction, typically presenting in neonates with failure to pass meconium.
Common causes
  • Simple Constipation: Often related to lifestyle factors like low fibre diet, poor fluid intake, and lack of physical activity.
  • Irritable bowel syndrome: A functional disorder characterised by chronic abdominal pain, bloating, and altered bowel habit, with constipation being the predominant feature (IBS-C).
  • Diverticular disease: Can cause altered bowel habit, including constipation, and is often associated with left lower quadrant pain.
  • Painful ano-rectal conditions: Conditions like anal fissures or haemorrhoids can lead to withholding of stool due to pain on defecation, causing secondary constipation.
  • Hypothyroidism: Reduced metabolic rate can lead to slowed gut motility and chronic constipation.
  • Behavioural (children): Stool withholding is a common cause in children, often triggered by painful defecation or toilet training issues.
  • COVID-19: Can present with gastrointestinal symptoms, including constipation, alongside typical respiratory signs.
Easily missed
  • Medication-induced Constipation: A very common side effect of drugs such as opioids, anticholinergics, and iron supplements.
  • Hypercalcaemia: Elevated serum calcium levels can impair colonic motility, leading to constipation, and may indicate an underlying condition like hyperparathyroidism or malignancy.
Other Differential Diagnoses to consider
  • Parkinson's disease: Autonomic dysfunction leading to slow gut motility.
  • Multiple sclerosis: Neurological dysfunction affecting bowel motility.
  • Diabetes mellitus: Autonomic neuropathy can cause delayed gastrointestinal transit.
  • Depression: Reduced gut motility associated with psychological factors or medication side effects.
  • Coeliac disease: Malabsorption can occasionally present with constipation instead of diarrhoea.
  • Ovarian cancer: Pelvic masses causing bowel obstruction or compression.
  • Chronic kidney disease: Electrolyte disturbances, particularly hyperkalaemia, affecting bowel function.
History Taking
Patient’s Description: Can you tell me more about what brought you here today?
Starting with an open-ended question allows the patient to describe their symptom in their own words, giving insight into their main concerns and priorities. It also helps establish rapport and a natural conversational flow. Relevance: This open format can uncover details about the nature, onset, and patient’s perceptions, which guide further targeted questioning.
When did the constipation start, and has it been consistent or variable?
  • Helps assess whether the constipation is chronic or acute and whether it is episodic or continuous, which can narrow the differential diagnosis.
  • Relevance: Chronic constipation may suggest irritable bowel syndrome, hypothyroidism, or a functional issue, while sudden onset may indicate obstruction or other urgent issues like colorectal cancer.
Have you noticed any factors that make it worse or better?
  • Exploring triggers or alleviating factors (such as diet changes, fluid intake, stress, or physical activity) helps identify lifestyle factors, behavioural causes, or conditions like IBS.
  • Relevance: Diet-related improvement might point to simple constipation, while worsened symptoms with certain foods or stress may indicate IBS.
Are you experiencing any associated symptoms such as abdominal pain, bloating, nausea, or rectal bleeding?
  • These symptoms can indicate specific causes, such as colorectal cancer (rectal bleeding), IBS (bloating, abdominal pain), or diverticular disease (pain and bloating).
  • Relevance: A positive response to rectal bleeding raises suspicion of malignancy, while bloating and pain may suggest IBS or diverticular disease. Absence of these symptoms may suggest functional constipation.
Do you experience any changes in bowel movements (colour, consistency) or have noticed any weight loss?
  • Changes in stool characteristics can help identify possible structural or metabolic causes, while weight loss may raise suspicion of malignancy.
  • Relevance: A positive response could indicate colorectal cancer, coeliac disease, or obstruction, while a negative response could favour functional causes or simple constipation.
Past Medical History, Family History, and Drug History
  • Conditions such as hypothyroidism, diabetes, and neurological disorders (e.g., Parkinson’s disease) have a strong association with constipation. Family history of colorectal cancer is crucial for early screening.
  • Relevance: A history of these conditions raises the likelihood of secondary causes, and a family history of colorectal cancer might warrant further evaluation. Drug history may reveal medications causing constipation (e.g., opioids, anticholinergics).
Ideas, Concerns, and Expectations (ICE)
  • Understanding the patient’s perspective on their condition helps identify any specific worries (such as cancer concerns) and whether they have certain expectations, like testing or immediate treatment options.
  • Relevance: Addressing the patient’s Ideas, Concerns, and Expectations ensures a patient-centred approach, which is essential for providing reassurance and aligning management with their goals and concerns.
Examination Strategy
General Inspection
  • Observe for general appearance, signs of distress, or cachexia, which may indicate a more serious underlying condition such as malignancy.
  • Relevance: Positive findings like cachexia or pallor could suggest malignancy or anaemia, while a generally healthy appearance may support a benign cause such as functional constipation.
Abdominal Inspection
  • Observe for abdominal distension or visible masses. Distension may indicate bowel obstruction or ascites, while masses could be suggestive of malignancy or other structural causes.
  • Relevance: Distension may suggest intestinal obstruction, while a lack of visible abnormalities may support a diagnosis like IBS or hypothyroidism.
Palpation of the Abdomen
  • Gentle palpation can help detect tenderness, guarding, or palpable masses. Tenderness in the left lower quadrant may suggest diverticular disease, while diffuse tenderness may indicate IBS or obstruction.
  • Relevance: A palpable mass could indicate a tumour, especially in older adults, while a non-tender abdomen supports functional causes.
Percussion of the Abdomen
  • Performing percussion can help assess for tympany or dullness. Tympany over a distended abdomen can indicate gas-filled intestines, while dullness may suggest an underlying mass or fluid.
  • Relevance: Tympany may suggest an obstruction, whereas dullness over a mass could indicate a neoplasm or stool impaction.
Auscultation of Bowel Sounds
  • Listening for bowel sounds can provide clues to motility issues. High-pitched, tinkling sounds suggest obstruction, while decreased or absent sounds may indicate ileus.
  • Relevance: Hyperactive bowel sounds suggest obstruction, while normal sounds may support functional constipation or hypothyroidism.
Rectal Examination
  • A digital rectal exam can detect faecal impaction, anal fissures, haemorrhoids, or masses. It also assesses anal tone, which can be affected by neurological conditions.
  • Relevance: A positive finding for masses raises suspicion for malignancy, while normal findings may support functional causes. Faecal impaction may indicate simple constipation.
Thyroid Examination
  • Assess the thyroid gland for signs of hypothyroidism, such as a goitre, which can be associated with constipation as a symptom of reduced metabolic rate.
  • Relevance: A goitre or other signs of hypothyroidism support hypothyroid-related constipation, while normal thyroid findings reduce the likelihood of this as a primary cause.
Red Flag Synthesis
Unintentional Weight Loss
  • Unintentional weight loss in a patient with constipation raises suspicion of malignancy, such as colorectal cancer, or other serious underlying conditions like chronic infection or systemic disease.
  • Significance: This could indicate colorectal cancer or another serious pathology, requiring urgent diagnostic evaluation, including possible imaging and referral for colonoscopy.
Rectal Bleeding
  • Rectal bleeding, particularly if associated with altered bowel habits, can indicate colorectal cancer or other significant lower gastrointestinal conditions such as inflammatory bowel disease or anal pathology.
  • Significance: Rectal bleeding could suggest malignancy, inflammatory bowel disease, or haemorrhoidal disease, necessitating investigation to determine the cause and prevent complications.
Persistent Abdominal Pain
  • Persistent or severe abdominal pain with constipation could indicate bowel obstruction, malignancy, or diverticulitis, particularly if the pain is localised or progressive.
  • Significance: This could suggest intestinal obstruction or advanced malignancy, both of which are medical emergencies requiring prompt evaluation and management.
Iron-Deficiency Anaemia
  • Iron-deficiency anaemia may present as fatigue or pallor and, when seen with constipation, can raise suspicion of gastrointestinal bleeding due to colorectal cancer or other conditions.
  • Significance: Anaemia in the setting of constipation can suggest gastrointestinal malignancy, especially in older patients, requiring further investigation.
Acute-Onset Constipation in the Elderly
  • Sudden-onset constipation in an elderly patient, especially if severe or with other symptoms, may signal an underlying malignancy, obstruction, or other acute pathology.
  • Significance: This could indicate colorectal cancer or bowel obstruction and may require urgent imaging and referral.
Family History of Colorectal Cancer
  • A family history of colorectal cancer, especially in a first-degree relative, increases the patient’s risk of developing the disease.
  • Significance: Family history of colorectal cancer is an important risk factor and may necessitate earlier or more frequent screening, such as colonoscopy, to detect potential malignancy at an early stage.
Investigations Strategy & Interpretation
Full Blood Count (FBC)
  • Assesses for anaemia, which could be associated with gastrointestinal bleeding, especially in cases of suspected colorectal cancer.
  • Result Significance: Anaemia, particularly iron-deficiency anaemia, may suggest colorectal cancer, while a normal FBC makes this diagnosis less likely.
Serum Electrolytes, Urea, and Creatinine
  • Helps assess for electrolyte imbalances, particularly hypercalcaemia, which can cause constipation, as well as renal function, which may affect medication management.
  • Result Significance: Hypercalcaemia could indicate a parathyroid disorder or malignancy, while normal levels may support functional causes of constipation.
Thyroid Function Tests
  • Hypothyroidism is a common metabolic cause of constipation, so assessing thyroid function is essential to rule out this condition.
  • Result Significance: Hypothyroidism would support a metabolic cause of constipation, while normal thyroid function tests make this less likely.
Serum Calcium Levels
  • Hypercalcaemia can cause constipation and may be due to primary hyperparathyroidism, malignancy, or other metabolic disorders.
  • Result Significance: Elevated calcium levels suggest hyperparathyroidism or malignancy, while normal levels reduce the likelihood of these conditions.
Faecal Occult Blood Test (FOBT)
  • Detects microscopic blood in the stool, which can be a sign of colorectal cancer, polyps, or other gastrointestinal pathologies.
  • Result Significance: A positive FOBT raises suspicion for colorectal cancer, requiring further investigation, while a negative result makes malignancy less likely but does not exclude it.
Abdominal X-ray
  • Can provide information on bowel gas patterns, stool burden, and may help identify obstruction or significant faecal impaction.
  • Result Significance: Findings of bowel dilatation suggest obstruction, while significant stool burden supports a diagnosis of simple constipation or impaction.
Colonoscopy
  • Recommended for patients with red flag symptoms such as weight loss, rectal bleeding, or unexplained anaemia. Colonoscopy allows direct visualisation of the bowel and detection of malignancies, polyps, or inflammatory changes.
  • Result Significance: Positive findings (e.g., masses, polyps) support diagnoses such as colorectal cancer or polyps, while a normal result can rule out many serious causes of constipation.
MRI or CT Abdomen
  • Useful in cases where there is concern for malignancy, obstruction, or complex diverticular disease, particularly if initial imaging is inconclusive.
  • Result Significance: A mass or obstruction found on imaging raises suspicion for malignancy or structural abnormalities, while normal imaging may help exclude these.
Evidence-Based Diagnosis (EBDx)
Unintentional Weight Loss
  • Unintentional weight loss in a patient with constipation significantly raises suspicion for malignancy, especially colorectal cancer. Studies show that weight loss, when combined with other symptoms like rectal bleeding or anaemia, has a higher predictive value for gastrointestinal cancers.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 45%, Specificity: 85%, Positive Likelihood Ratio (LR+): 3.0.
  • Reference: NICE Guidelines on Suspected Cancer Recognition and Referral (NG12), 2015.
  • Significance: Positive findings of weight loss increase the likelihood of colorectal cancer, while the absence of weight loss does not rule out malignancy but lowers its probability.
Rectal Bleeding
  • Rectal bleeding is a significant red flag in patients over 50 presenting with constipation, as it increases suspicion for colorectal cancer. It has moderate sensitivity but good specificity for malignancy when combined with other symptoms.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 50-60%, Specificity: 80%, LR+: 2.8.
  • Reference: American College of Gastroenterology Guidelines on Colorectal Cancer Screening, 2021.
  • Significance: Positive findings of rectal bleeding significantly raise suspicion for colorectal cancer and necessitate further investigation. Absence of bleeding, however, does not exclude malignancy, especially if other symptoms are present.
Faecal Occult Blood Test (FOBT)
  • FOBT is a useful screening tool for detecting occult blood in the stool, often associated with colorectal cancer. It has moderate sensitivity but high specificity, making it more reliable for ruling in the diagnosis if positive.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 70%, Specificity: 92%, LR+: 8.8.
  • Reference: Cochrane Review on Faecal Occult Blood Testing for Colorectal Cancer Screening, 2019.
  • Significance: A positive FOBT is highly suggestive of colorectal cancer, warranting further investigation, while a negative result reduces but does not eliminate the probability of cancer.
Iron-Deficiency Anaemia
  • Iron-deficiency anaemia is a key red flag in patients presenting with constipation and may indicate gastrointestinal blood loss, commonly due to colorectal cancer.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 50%, Specificity: 85%, LR+: 3.3.
  • Reference: BSG Guidelines on Diagnosis and Management of Anaemia, 2020.
  • Significance: Presence of anaemia raises suspicion for colorectal cancer, while normal haemoglobin levels slightly reduce its likelihood but are not definitive.
Thyroid Function Tests (Hypothyroidism)
  • Hypothyroidism is a known metabolic cause of constipation, particularly in older adults, and testing for TSH and free T4 levels is essential for diagnosis.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 85%, Specificity: 95%, LR+: 5.7 for elevated TSH and low free T4.
  • Reference: NICE Guidelines on Thyroid Disease, 2019.
  • Significance: Positive findings support a diagnosis of hypothyroidism, while normal levels effectively exclude it as a cause of constipation.
Abdominal X-ray (for Obstruction)
  • Abdominal X-ray is useful in detecting obstruction or significant faecal loading. Its diagnostic accuracy is particularly high in acute settings.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 80%, Specificity: 95%, LR+: 16.0 for obstruction.
  • Reference: European Society of Radiology Guidelines, 2018.
  • Significance: Positive findings of bowel dilatation and air-fluid levels strongly suggest obstruction, necessitating urgent intervention, while a normal X-ray reduces the likelihood of obstruction.
Diagnostic Timeout & Cognitive Biases

Awareness of common thinking errors is essential to improve diagnostic accuracy and patient safety when assessing a patient with constipation. Before finalising a diagnosis, it is good practice for clinicians at all levels to take a 'diagnostic timeout' to challenge their own reasoning.

  • Anchoring Bias: The tendency to anchor on a benign diagnosis like "simple constipation," especially in a younger patient or someone with known IBS. This can lead to dismissing new, concerning symptoms like rectal bleeding or weight loss, which should prompt a re-evaluation for a more serious cause. A key question to ask is: "Could this be colorectal cancer, and have I done enough to rule it out based on the patient's age and symptoms?"
  • Attribution Error: Incorrectly attributing constipation to a patient's known chronic condition (e.g., depression, Parkinson's disease, opioid use) without considering that a new, independent pathology could be developing. For example, while a patient on morphine is expected to be constipated, the development of anaemia and abdominal pain must be investigated independently.
  • Confirmation Bias: Actively seeking out or overemphasising information that supports an initial hypothesis while ignoring contradictory evidence. For instance, focusing on a patient's low-fibre diet to confirm functional constipation while downplaying a family history of colorectal cancer.
  • Premature Closure: Accepting a diagnosis too early in the assessment process and failing to complete a full evaluation. A classic example is failing to perform a digital rectal examination (DRE) once a diagnosis of "constipation" has been made, thereby missing critical findings like faecal impaction, a rectal mass, or lack of anal tone.
Illustrative Management Pathways

For educational purposes, this section describes the typical principles of management for key diagnoses. These are not prescriptive instructions. All management must be guided by official UK guidelines, local formularies, and senior clinical advice.

Simple/Functional Constipation

After excluding secondary causes and red flags, management is stepwise. The first line is lifestyle modification, including increasing dietary fibre, ensuring adequate fluid intake, and promoting physical activity. If these measures are insufficient, laxatives are introduced. A bulk-forming laxative (e.g., ispaghula husk) is often tried first, followed by or combined with an osmotic laxative (e.g., macrogol, lactulose). If stools remain hard, a stimulant laxative (e.g., senna, bisacodyl) can be added for short-term use.

Opioid-Induced Constipation (OIC)

Prophylaxis with laxatives should be considered for all patients starting opioids. The mainstay of treatment is a combination of an osmotic and a stimulant laxative. If conventional laxatives are ineffective or poorly tolerated, peripherally acting mu-opioid receptor antagonists (PAMORAs), such as naloxegol, may be considered to counteract the constipating effects of opioids in the gut without affecting central analgesia.

Irritable Bowel Syndrome with Constipation (IBS-C)

Management principles are similar to functional constipation, focusing on lifestyle and laxatives. However, pain and bloating are often prominent. Antispasmodics (e.g., mebeverine) may be helpful. For refractory constipation in IBS-C, NICE guidelines recommend considering linaclotide, which can improve both stool frequency and abdominal symptoms. A low FODMAP diet under dietetic supervision may also be beneficial.

Suspected Colorectal Cancer

Any patient whose symptoms and signs meet the criteria in the NICE NG12 guidelines (e.g., a person aged 40 or over with unexplained weight loss and abdominal pain, or aged 50 or over with unexplained rectal bleeding) requires an urgent two-week wait (2WW) referral to a colorectal team for further investigation, which is typically a colonoscopy. Management is then dictated by the findings of the investigation and multidisciplinary team discussion.

Reflection & Learning

These brief cases illustrate the application of clinical reasoning to different presentations of constipation. They are for educational purposes only.

Case 1: Red Flag Presentation in Primary Care

A 68-year-old man presents to his GP with a 2-month history of worsening constipation and a feeling of incomplete emptying. He has also noticed a 4kg weight loss over the same period despite no change in diet. He denies rectal bleeding. The problem representation is an older male with a new, progressive change in bowel habit accompanied by a constitutional red flag symptom (unintentional weight loss). This presentation is highly suspicious for colorectal cancer. The correct pathway is an urgent two-week wait referral for colonoscopy, as per NICE NG12 guidelines.

Case 2: Secondary Cause Identified on Investigation

A 55-year-old woman presents with several months of constipation, fatigue, and feeling cold. On questioning, she reports her skin has become dry and she has gained some weight. Her diet and activity levels are unchanged. Examination is unremarkable. The problem representation is chronic constipation associated with systemic symptoms of a slowed metabolism. This should prompt consideration of a secondary endocrine cause. The GP arranges blood tests, which include Thyroid Function Tests, revealing a high TSH and low T4, confirming a diagnosis of hypothyroidism. Treatment with levothyroxine is initiated.

Case 3: Functional Constipation in a Younger Adult

A 29-year-old woman complains of lifelong constipation, opening her bowels twice a week with straining. She often feels bloated but has no abdominal pain, rectal bleeding, or weight loss. Her past medical history is clear, and she takes no regular medication. A full history reveals a low-fibre diet and sedentary lifestyle. Examination, including a digital rectal exam, is normal. The problem representation is a young, low-risk patient with chronic symptoms and no red flags, consistent with functional constipation. Management involves reassurance, education about lifestyle and diet, and a trial of a macrogol-based osmotic laxative.
