Haematemesis and Melaena Diagnostic Reasoning Reference
Context: Haematemesis and Melaena Assessment
Overview

Haematemesis (vomiting of blood) and melaena (black, tarry stools) are cardinal signs of upper gastrointestinal (GI) bleeding and represent a major medical emergency. This presentation carries a significant mortality rate, and the primary challenge for the clinician is to rapidly assess the patient's haemodynamic stability, initiate resuscitation, identify the likely source of bleeding, and arrange urgent specialist intervention. A systematic approach is crucial to distinguish between common causes, such as peptic ulcer disease, and life-threatening emergencies like bleeding oesophageal varices, which require a different immediate management pathway.

This educational module is designed to build a foundational understanding of the clinical reasoning process for a patient presenting with haematemesis or melaena. For definitive, real-time management protocols, users must refer to official UK guidelines from authoritative bodies such as the British Society of Gastroenterology (BSG) and NICE (e.g., NG188 for Upper Gastrointestinal Bleeding).

Pathophysiology/Basic Science

Understanding the origin and appearance of blood in the upper GI tract is key to diagnosis. Bleeding can arise from several distinct mechanisms:

  • Peptic Ulceration: This is the most common cause. Chronic inflammation, typically driven by Helicobacter pylori infection or NSAID use, erodes the mucosal lining of the stomach or duodenum. If this erosion penetrates a submucosal artery or vein, significant bleeding occurs. The appearance of the vomitus depends on the bleed's speed and the blood's transit time in the stomach; rapid arterial bleeding causes fresh red haematemesis, while slower bleeding allows gastric acid to alter haemoglobin, resulting in dark, "coffee-ground" emesis.
  • Portal Hypertension and Varices: In patients with chronic liver disease (cirrhosis), scarring obstructs blood flow through the liver. This increases pressure in the portal vein (portal hypertension), forcing blood to divert through collateral vessels. These vessels, particularly at the gastro-oesophageal junction, become engorged and fragile (varices). They have thin walls and are under high pressure, making them prone to rupture, which can lead to catastrophic, high-volume, bright red haematemesis.
  • Mucosal Erosive Disease (Gastritis/Oesophagitis): Widespread inflammation of the stomach lining (gastritis) or oesophagus (oesophagitis) can cause diffuse, superficial bleeding. This is often less dramatic than a bleeding ulcer or varix and typically presents with coffee-ground vomiting or melaena. Common causes include NSAIDs, alcohol, and severe acid reflux.
  • Mallory-Weiss Tear: This is a longitudinal tear in the mucosa at the gastro-oesophageal junction. It is caused by a sudden, forceful increase in intra-abdominal pressure, classically from repeated, violent retching or vomiting after an alcohol binge. The tear disrupts submucosal blood vessels, leading to haematemesis.
  • Malignancy: Oesophageal or gastric cancers can bleed. As tumours grow, they develop a fragile network of blood vessels (neovascularisation) and can also ulcerate or erode into adjacent vessels, causing chronic occult blood loss (leading to anaemia), melaena, or acute haematemesis.
  • Formation of Melaena: When blood enters the upper GI tract, it is exposed to digestive enzymes and bacteria as it travels through the intestines. Haemoglobin is broken down into haematin and other pigments. This process takes several hours and transforms the blood into the characteristic black, tarry, and foul-smelling stool known as melaena. A minimum of 50-100ml of blood loss is usually required to produce melaena.
Differential Diagnosis (DDx) Framework

A structured approach to the differential diagnosis of haematemesis and melaena is essential to ensure life-threatening conditions are prioritised.

Must not miss!
  • Oesophageal varices: Presents with profuse, bright red haematemesis in a patient with signs or history of chronic liver disease.
  • Oesophageal cancer: Often presents with progressive difficulty swallowing (dysphagia), weight loss, and later, haematemesis.
  • Gastric cancer: May present with persistent epigastric pain, unexplained weight loss, and signs of chronic bleeding like anaemia or melaena.
Common causes
  • Gastritis: Often causes "coffee-ground" vomiting or melaena, associated with epigastric pain, and linked to NSAID use or H. pylori.
  • Peptic Ulcer Disease: Presents with epigastric pain related to meals, and can cause bleeding ranging from melaena to significant haematemesis.
  • Oesophagitis: Typically causes retrosternal pain and can lead to minor haematemesis, often in the context of severe GORD.
  • Mallory-Weiss tear: Characterised by haematemesis following a period of forceful retching or vomiting.
  • COVID-19: Can rarely cause GI bleeding, particularly in severe illness associated with systemic coagulopathy.
Easily missed
  • Medications-induced Haematemesis & Melaena: Bleeding caused by drugs like NSAIDs, steroids, or anticoagulants, which may not be immediately obvious.
  • Bleeding disorders: Can present with spontaneous GI bleeding in patients with known or undiagnosed coagulopathies.
  • Epistaxis: Swallowed blood from a posterior nosebleed can present as haematemesis, mimicking an upper GI source.
  • Haemoptysis: Coughed-up blood from the lungs can be swallowed and then vomited, causing confusion with a primary GI bleed.
Other Differential Diagnoses to consider
  • COVID-19: Possible in severe cases with coagulopathy leading to GI bleeding
  • Dieulafoy’s lesion: A rare but significant cause of massive GI bleeding from a submucosal artery
  • Aortoenteric fistula: Rare, often following abdominal aortic aneurysm repair, presenting as life-threatening GI bleeding
  • Angiodysplasia: Vascular malformation in the GI tract, typically causing intermittent bleeding
  • Gastric antral vascular ectasia (GAVE): “Watermelon stomach,” causes upper GI bleeding, associated with liver disease
  • Portal hypertensive gastropathy: Due to portal hypertension, common in liver disease and varices
  • Oesophageal ulcer: Erosions in oesophagus, often related to severe gastro-oesophageal reflux disease
  • Splenic artery aneurysm rupture: Rare cause of GI bleeding; rupture can lead to massive haemorrhage
History Taking
**Patient’s Description**: How can I help you today?
Starting with an open-ended question like "Can you tell me more about what brought you here today?" allows the patient to provide a broad, unfiltered description of their symptoms. Relevance: This approach helps reveal initial details of the complaint and gives direction for specific, targeted questions.
When did you first notice the blood in your vomit or stool?
  • Establishing the onset is essential to differentiate acute versus chronic causes.
  • Relevance: Sudden onset suggests an acute cause, such as Mallory-Weiss tear or oesophageal varices, while a gradual onset may indicate chronic issues like peptic ulcer disease or gastritis.
Is the bleeding fresh, bright red, or dark, coffee-ground-like in appearance?
  • Understanding the nature of the blood provides clues about the location and severity of the bleed.
  • Relevance: Fresh red blood suggests active upper GI bleeding (oesophageal varices or Mallory-Weiss tear), while dark, coffee-ground-like blood suggests older blood from the stomach, as in gastritis or peptic ulcer disease.
Have you noticed any abdominal pain? If so, can you describe it?
  • Abdominal pain is an important associated symptom that can guide diagnosis.
  • Relevance: Epigastric pain could indicate peptic ulcer disease or gastritis, while severe upper abdominal pain could suggest a perforated ulcer. The absence of pain might make gastric varices more likely.
Have you experienced weight loss, fatigue, or loss of appetite?
  • These systemic symptoms help evaluate for malignancies and chronic disease.
  • Relevance: Positive responses may raise suspicion of gastric or oesophageal cancer. Fatigue might also suggest anaemia secondary to chronic blood loss.
Are there any known triggers or factors that worsen or relieve the bleeding episodes?
  • Determining triggers and relievers can help narrow down functional versus structural causes.
  • Relevance: Symptoms worsened by alcohol intake might indicate oesophageal varices, especially in a background of liver disease. Relief with antacids may suggest a gastritis or peptic ulcer origin.
Associated Symptom: Have you had any black, tarry stools (melaena)?
  • Presence of melaena indicates upper GI bleeding and helps localise the bleed.
  • Relevance: Melaena is suggestive of upper GI bleeding, as seen in peptic ulcer disease or gastritis. Absence of melaena may make a lower GI source or haemoptysis more likely.
Associated Symptom: Have you experienced any nosebleeds or coughing up blood?
  • Helps rule out non-GI sources of bleeding, such as epistaxis or haemoptysis.
  • Relevance: Positive answer may suggest epistaxis with blood swallowing or haemoptysis rather than a primary GI bleed.
Past Medical History, Family History, and Drug History
  • Explore previous diagnoses (e.g., liver disease, peptic ulcer, malignancies) and family history of GI disorders.
  • Relevance: Past GI bleeding or liver disease history increases suspicion of varices; NSAID use or anticoagulation therapy increases bleeding risk; a family history of GI malignancies may suggest cancer.
**Ideas, Concerns, and Expectations (ICE)**
  • Understanding the patient’s perspective can guide reassurance and tailored education.
  • Relevance: Helps identify specific concerns such as cancer or severe bleeding, ensuring these worries are addressed. Understanding expectations (e.g., need for immediate relief, diagnostics) aids in aligning care with patient’s hopes.
Examination Strategy
General Inspection
  • Observe for general signs of anaemia (pallor, fatigue), jaundice, or distress. These can provide clues about chronic blood loss or liver disease.
  • Relevance: Pallor suggests anaemia, potentially from chronic GI bleeding (e.g., peptic ulcer, gastritis). Jaundice may indicate underlying liver disease, increasing suspicion for oesophageal varices.
Vital Signs Assessment
  • Assess blood pressure, heart rate, and respiratory rate to identify signs of haemodynamic instability or shock, which may suggest active bleeding.
  • Relevance: Tachycardia and hypotension indicate significant blood loss, possibly from severe upper GI bleeding (e.g., variceal bleed, peptic ulcer). Stable vitals may suggest a more chronic, less acute bleed.
Abdominal Examination: Inspection
  • Look for signs of liver disease, such as ascites, visible veins, or abdominal distension, which can point to portal hypertension as a cause of bleeding.
  • Relevance: Signs of liver disease increase the likelihood of oesophageal varices. Absence of these findings makes varices less likely.
Abdominal Palpation
  • Palpate for hepatomegaly, tenderness, or masses. Hepatomegaly could indicate liver disease, while tenderness may suggest an inflammatory process like gastritis or a perforated ulcer.
  • Relevance: Hepatomegaly supports a diagnosis of liver disease with portal hypertension, while epigastric tenderness may indicate peptic ulcer disease or gastritis.
Rectal Examination
  • Perform a rectal examination to assess stool colour, which can confirm the presence of melaena (black, tarry stool), indicating upper GI bleeding.
  • Relevance: Melaena suggests upper GI bleeding sources like peptic ulcers or varices. Absence of melaena may suggest alternative bleeding sources or less severe bleeding.
Examination for Signs of Chronic Liver Disease
  • Assess for features of chronic liver disease such as spider naevi, palmar erythema, and gynaecomastia. These may indicate underlying liver pathology linked to portal hypertension.
  • Relevance: Findings suggestive of chronic liver disease increase the likelihood of variceal bleeding. Absence of these signs may lower the probability of varices as a source of bleeding.
Oral and Pharyngeal Inspection
  • Examine the mouth and throat for signs of bleeding from other sources, such as posterior epistaxis or haemoptysis, which could mimic GI bleeding.
  • Relevance: Positive findings of blood or lesions in the pharynx might suggest a non-GI source, such as epistaxis or haemoptysis. A normal examination here supports a GI source of bleeding.
Red Flag Synthesis
Haemodynamic Instability (e.g., hypotension, tachycardia)
  • Signs of shock, such as low blood pressure and rapid heart rate, suggest significant blood loss and potential haemodynamic compromise.
  • Significance: This may indicate severe upper GI bleeding (e.g., variceal bleed, large peptic ulcer bleed), requiring immediate resuscitation and urgent intervention.
Fresh, Profuse Vomiting of Bright Red Blood
  • Vomiting fresh blood in large quantities suggests active, possibly severe upper GI bleeding.
  • Significance: This finding is highly concerning for massive GI haemorrhage, as seen in variceal rupture or bleeding peptic ulcer, necessitating urgent endoscopy and stabilisation.
Persistent, Severe Abdominal Pain
  • Severe pain, particularly in the epigastric region, can signal an acute abdominal condition that requires urgent evaluation.
  • Significance: This could indicate a perforated peptic ulcer or other surgical emergency, which may require immediate imaging and surgical consultation.
Signs of Chronic Liver Disease (e.g., jaundice, ascites)
  • Chronic liver disease increases the likelihood of portal hypertension and oesophageal varices, which are prone to catastrophic bleeding.
  • Significance: Signs of liver disease raise the suspicion of oesophageal varices as a bleeding source, which can be life-threatening if untreated.
Unintentional Weight Loss and Fatigue
  • Weight loss and fatigue may indicate an underlying malignancy, such as gastric or oesophageal cancer.
  • Significance: These symptoms increase suspicion for GI malignancies, warranting urgent referral for endoscopic evaluation to rule out cancer.
Dark, Tarry Stools (Melaena)
  • Melaena suggests bleeding from the upper GI tract, which may be substantial and requires prompt investigation.
  • Significance: This could indicate upper GI bleeding from peptic ulcer disease, varices, or malignancy, and often requires endoscopy to identify and manage the bleeding source.
History of NSAID or Anticoagulant Use
  • NSAIDs and anticoagulants are common causes of GI bleeding, especially when combined with other risk factors like age or comorbidities.
  • Significance: Patients on these medications are at high risk of GI bleeding, which may require cessation of the medication, endoscopy, and potentially reversal of anticoagulation.
Dysphagia or Progressive Difficulty Swallowing
  • Difficulty swallowing may suggest an obstructive lesion in the oesophagus, such as cancer.
  • Significance: Dysphagia warrants urgent evaluation for oesophageal malignancy, especially if associated with haematemesis, as it could indicate bleeding from an oesophageal tumour.
Investigations Strategy & Interpretation
Full Blood Count (FBC)
  • FBC is used to assess haemoglobin levels to detect anaemia, and to check for elevated white cell count or platelet abnormalities.
  • Result Significance: Low haemoglobin suggests significant blood loss, potentially from a GI bleed. A normal result may indicate either a small or recent bleed. Elevated white cells may indicate an infectious or inflammatory cause.
Urea and Electrolytes (U&Es)
  • This test assesses for dehydration and renal function. A raised urea level may be associated with upper GI bleeding due to blood digestion.
  • Result Significance: Elevated urea with normal creatinine can indicate an upper GI bleed, as blood digestion increases urea. Normal urea may suggest a lower GI source or a less significant bleed.
Liver Function Tests (LFTs)
  • Abnormal LFTs may indicate underlying liver disease, increasing the likelihood of variceal bleeding or portal hypertensive gastropathy.
  • Result Significance: Elevated bilirubin, ALT, or AST suggests liver dysfunction, potentially due to chronic liver disease. Normal LFTs make varices or portal hypertension less likely.
Coagulation Profile (INR, PT, aPTT)
  • A coagulation profile is important to evaluate bleeding risk, especially in patients on anticoagulants or with liver disease.
  • Result Significance: Prolonged INR/PT indicates an increased bleeding risk, commonly seen in liver disease or due to anticoagulant use. A normal profile suggests bleeding is less likely due to coagulation abnormalities.
Crossmatch and Group & Save
  • Crossmatching prepares for blood transfusion if significant blood loss is suspected, particularly in patients with haemodynamic instability.
  • Result Significance: Availability of compatible blood is critical in managing acute blood loss from a severe GI bleed. No need for transfusion preparation if the patient is stable and anaemia is minimal.
Upper GI Endoscopy
  • Endoscopy is the gold-standard diagnostic tool to visualise the source of bleeding within the upper GI tract.
  • Result Significance: Findings such as ulcers, varices, or malignancy directly confirm the source of bleeding. Normal findings may prompt investigation of other causes like epistaxis or haemoptysis.
Helicobacter pylori Testing
  • Testing for H. pylori infection is relevant in patients with suspected peptic ulcer disease, as it is a common cause of ulcers.
  • Result Significance: Positive results support a diagnosis of H. pylori-associated ulcer disease, guiding specific eradication therapy. Negative results may suggest other ulcer causes (e.g., NSAID-related).
CT Abdomen (if endoscopy inconclusive or contraindicated)
  • CT can help identify non-visible or obscure bleeding sources and is particularly useful when endoscopy is not possible or the patient remains unstable.
  • Result Significance: Positive findings may reveal alternative causes like aortoenteric fistula or large tumours. Negative findings reduce the likelihood of a structural source within the abdomen.
Evidence-Based Diagnosis (EBDx)
Presence of Melaena
  • Melaena, characterised by black, tarry stools, is a highly specific indicator of upper GI bleeding. This is due to the digestion of blood as it passes through the GI tract.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 50-70%, Specificity: 90-95%, Positive Likelihood Ratio: 10-15.
  • Reference: Savides et al., 1995, *Journal of Clinical Gastroenterology*.
  • Significance: The presence of melaena increases the likelihood of an upper GI source, such as peptic ulcer disease or varices. Absence of melaena lowers the probability of a significant upper GI bleed, though it does not completely exclude it.
Haemodynamic Instability (Hypotension and Tachycardia)
  • Hypotension and tachycardia are signs of haemodynamic instability and can indicate significant acute blood loss, especially in GI bleeding cases.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 60%, Specificity: 75%, Positive Likelihood Ratio: 2.4 for ongoing bleeding.
  • Reference: Barkun et al., 2019, *Management of Upper Gastrointestinal Bleeding*, *BMJ Clinical Guidelines*.
  • Significance: Presence of these signs strongly suggests an active or recent large volume GI bleed, necessitating urgent resuscitation and investigation to identify the bleeding source.
Endoscopic Findings of Peptic Ulcers
  • Endoscopy is considered the gold standard for diagnosing upper GI bleeding and identifying the source. Peptic ulcers are common findings and may show stigmata of recent bleeding, such as visible vessels or adherent clots.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 95%, Specificity: 90% for detecting active bleeding sources in peptic ulcer disease.
  • Reference: Laine et al., 2008, *American Journal of Gastroenterology*.
  • Significance: Positive findings on endoscopy confirm the source and allow for therapeutic interventions, like cauterisation or clipping, to control bleeding.
Elevated Urea in Blood Tests
  • Elevated urea levels relative to creatinine in the context of GI bleeding are thought to be due to the absorption of blood proteins in the GI tract, which increases urea production.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 70%, Specificity: 65% for identifying upper GI sources.
  • Reference: Golin et al., 2015, *World Journal of Gastroenterology*.
  • Significance: Elevated urea levels suggest an upper GI source, though other causes for increased urea (e.g., renal impairment) should also be considered.
Positive Helicobacter pylori Test
  • H. pylori infection is a major causative factor for peptic ulcer disease, which can result in upper GI bleeding if an ulcer erodes a blood vessel.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 90%, Specificity: 95% for diagnosing H. pylori infection with urea breath test.
  • Reference: Malfertheiner et al., 2017, *Maastricht V/Florence Consensus Report on Helicobacter pylori infection*, *Gut Journal*.
  • Significance: A positive test in patients with ulcers guides treatment toward eradication therapy, significantly reducing the risk of rebleeding.
Low Haemoglobin on Full Blood Count
  • Low haemoglobin levels indicate anaemia, often due to blood loss in the context of GI bleeding. Repeated measurements can help assess ongoing bleeding and response to treatment.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 85%, Specificity: 70% for detecting anaemia in chronic or acute blood loss scenarios.
  • Reference: UK NICE Guidelines, Anaemia in GI Bleeding, 2020.
  • Significance: A low haemoglobin result indicates significant blood loss and the potential need for blood transfusion, especially if ongoing bleeding is suspected.
Diagnostic Timeout & Cognitive Biases

Awareness of common thinking errors is essential to improve diagnostic accuracy and patient safety when assessing a patient with upper GI bleeding. Before finalising a diagnosis, it is good practice for clinicians at all levels to take a 'diagnostic timeout' to challenge their own reasoning.

  • Anchoring Bias: The tendency to lock onto an initial, often benign, feature. For example, attributing a bleed solely to known NSAID use in an elderly patient and failing to consider a new, underlying malignancy, even if red flags like weight loss are present. A key question to ask is: "Could there be more than one cause for this bleed?"
  • Confirmation Bias: Actively seeking out evidence that supports an initial belief while ignoring contradictory findings. For example, focusing on a history of indigestion to confirm gastritis while downplaying the significance of jaundice, which would point towards a variceal cause.
  • Premature Closure: Accepting a diagnosis before it is fully verified. A common pitfall is assuming haematemesis in a young person after vomiting is always a Mallory-Weiss tear, without fully assessing their haemodynamic status or considering a less common but more severe cause like a Dieulafoy's lesion.
  • Distinguishing Swallowed Blood: A crucial error is to miss a non-GI source of bleeding. Significant epistaxis (nosebleed) or even haemoptysis (coughed-up blood) can be swallowed and then vomited. Failure to inspect the nose and throat or to ask about coughing can lead to an unnecessary and invasive GI investigation. Always ask: "Could this blood have come from anywhere else?"
Illustrative Management Pathways

For educational purposes, this section describes the typical principles of management for key diagnoses. These are not prescriptive instructions. All management must be guided by official UK guidelines, local formularies, and senior clinical advice.

Initial Resuscitation (All Causes)

The immediate priority is an ABCDE assessment. This involves securing wide-bore intravenous access (e.g., 2 x 16G cannulae), taking urgent bloods (FBC, U&Es, LFTs, Coagulation, Group & Save/Crossmatch), and starting fluid resuscitation with crystalloids for shocked patients. Oxygen is given if hypoxic. The decision to transfuse red blood cells is guided by the patient's haemodynamic status, evidence of active bleeding, and risk factors, with a restrictive transfusion threshold (e.g., target Hb of 70-80 g/L) often applied in non-variceal bleeds.

Suspected Non-Variceal Bleed (e.g., Peptic Ulcer)

Once the patient is stabilised, risk stratification using a validated score (e.g., Glasgow-Blatchford Score at presentation) helps determine the need for inpatient endoscopy. Patients with high-risk features (e.g., haemodynamic instability, melaena) require urgent upper GI endoscopy (within 24 hours). This allows for both diagnosis and therapeutic intervention, such as adrenaline injection, thermal coagulation, or clipping of the bleeding point. Intravenous proton pump inhibitors (PPIs) may be started while awaiting endoscopy in some cases.

Suspected Variceal Bleed

A suspected variceal bleed (in a patient with known or suspected chronic liver disease) is a medical emergency requiring a different approach. Alongside resuscitation, management involves immediate administration of a vasoactive agent (e.g., terlipressin) to reduce portal pressure and a broad-spectrum prophylactic antibiotic to prevent infection. These patients require urgent endoscopy (ideally with a consultant gastroenterologist) for definitive treatment with variceal band ligation. Blood transfusion thresholds are typically more liberal. Management should occur in a setting with access to intensive care.

Post-Endoscopy Management

After successful haemostasis, management focuses on preventing re-bleeding. For peptic ulcer disease, this involves testing for and eradicating Helicobacter pylori and stopping offending medications like NSAIDs. For variceal bleeding, patients are established on non-selective beta-blockers (e.g., carvedilol) and entered into a surveillance/banding programme.

Reflection & Learning

These brief cases illustrate the application of clinical reasoning to different presentations of upper GI bleeding. They are for educational purposes only.

Case 1: The Elderly Patient on NSAIDs

A 78-year-old woman with osteoarthritis takes regular ibuprofen. She presents to the Emergency Department feeling dizzy and reports one episode of vomiting dark material that "looked like coffee grounds." She has also had black, foul-smelling stools for two days. On examination, she is pale, her heart rate is 110 bpm, and her blood pressure is 100/60 mmHg. Her abdomen is soft with mild epigastric tenderness. The problem representation is an elderly patient on NSAIDs with features of both haematemesis and melaena, along with haemodynamic instability (tachycardia, relative hypotension), highly suspicious for a bleeding peptic ulcer. Immediate management involves resuscitation, urgent blood tests including crossmatch, and referral to gastroenterology for an inpatient endoscopy.

Case 2: The Patient with Known Liver Disease

A 55-year-old man with a known history of alcohol-related liver disease is brought in by ambulance after vomiting a large amount of fresh, bright red blood. He is confused and agitated. His pulse is 125 bpm and his blood pressure is 85/50 mmHg. His abdomen is distended with a positive fluid thrill (ascites). The problem representation is a patient with decompensated chronic liver disease presenting with massive haematemesis and shock. This is a suspected variceal bleed until proven otherwise. The immediate pathway is aggressive resuscitation, urgent administration of terlipressin and prophylactic antibiotics, and an emergency call to the on-call gastroenterologist and anaesthetist for an immediate endoscopy.

Case 3: The Young Man After a Night Out

A 22-year-old student presents to a walk-in centre. He explains that after a night of heavy drinking, he spent the morning vomiting repeatedly. The last time he vomited, he noticed streaks of bright red blood in the vomit. He feels otherwise well. His vital signs are normal and his examination is unremarkable. The problem representation is a young, stable patient with a classic history of haematemesis following a bout of forceful retching. This is most consistent with a Mallory-Weiss tear. Given his haemodynamic stability and minimal bleeding, he can likely be managed with observation and lifestyle advice, with clear safety-netting instructions to return if bleeding becomes heavier or he feels unwell.
