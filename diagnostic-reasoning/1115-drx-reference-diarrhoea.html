Diarrhoea Diagnostic Reasoning Reference
Context: Diarrhoea Assessment
Overview

Diarrhoea is an extremely common presenting symptom in both primary and emergency care, representing a wide spectrum of underlying pathologies. The causes range from self-limiting viral illnesses to chronic, debilitating conditions and life-threatening emergencies. The core challenge for the clinician is to differentiate between benign and serious causes efficiently, identifying red flags that warrant urgent investigation while providing appropriate reassurance and management for less severe conditions. A systematic and structured approach is essential to ensure conditions like inflammatory bowel disease (IBD), colorectal cancer, or severe infections are not missed.

This educational module aims to provide a robust framework for the clinical reasoning process when faced with a patient with diarrhoea. For definitive, real-time management protocols, users must refer to official UK guidelines from authoritative bodies such as NICE (e.g., NG151 for Colorectal Cancer, NG131 for Crohn's Disease, NG130 for Ulcerative Colitis, and CG184 for Diarrhoea and Vomiting in children under 5) and the British Society of Gastroenterology (BSG).

Pathophysiology/Basic Science

Understanding the mechanism of diarrhoea is fundamental to forming a differential diagnosis. Diarrhoea is not a disease itself but a symptom resulting from an imbalance in the normal physiological processes of intestinal water and electrolyte absorption and secretion. The primary mechanisms include:

  • Secretory Diarrhoea: This occurs when there is a net secretion of electrolytes (primarily chloride ions) and water into the intestinal lumen, overwhelming its absorptive capacity. The classic example is cholera, where bacterial toxins stimulate intestinal cells to actively pump out fluid. This type of diarrhoea is typically large-volume, watery, and persists even when the patient is fasting.
  • Osmotic Diarrhoea: This is caused by the presence of poorly absorbed, osmotically active solutes in the gut lumen, which draw water from the body into the intestine. Common causes include lactose intolerance (undigested lactose) and the use of certain laxatives (e.g., lactulose, magnesium salts). This type of diarrhoea typically stops when the patient fasts or stops ingesting the offending substance.
  • Inflammatory (Exudative) Diarrhoea: This results from damage to the intestinal mucosa due to inflammation or infection. The damaged lining leaks mucus, serum proteins, and blood into the gut lumen. Conditions like Inflammatory Bowel Disease (Crohn's, Ulcerative Colitis) and infections with invasive bacteria (e.g., Campylobacter, Shigella) are prime examples. It is often associated with fever, abdominal pain, and bloody or mucoid stools (dysentery).
  • Dysmotility-Related Diarrhoea: This is caused by abnormally rapid intestinal transit, which reduces the time available for water and electrolytes to be absorbed. Conditions like Irritable Bowel Syndrome (IBS), hyperthyroidism, and diabetic autonomic neuropathy can lead to this type of diarrhoea.
Differential Diagnosis (DDx) Framework

A structured approach to the differential diagnosis of diarrhoea is essential to ensure life-threatening conditions are prioritised.

Must not miss!
  • Colorectal cancer: Often presents with a persistent change in bowel habit, blood in the stool, and may be associated with weight loss or abdominal pain in older adults.
  • Inflammatory Bowel Disease: Typically presents in young adults with chronic diarrhoea, which is often bloody, along with abdominal pain, weight loss, and fatigue.
  • Diverticulitis: Presents with left-sided abdominal pain, fever, and a change in bowel habit to diarrhoea or constipation, particularly in older patients.
  • Intussusception: A paediatric emergency presenting with colicky abdominal pain, vomiting, and characteristic 'redcurrant jelly' stool.
  • Pelvic abscess: Can cause diarrhoea, fever, and localised pelvic or abdominal pain, often following surgery or a condition like appendicitis or diverticulitis.
  • HIV: Chronic diarrhoea can be a presenting feature of primary HIV infection or an opportunistic infection in advanced disease.
Common causes
  • Gastroenteritis: Acute onset of watery diarrhoea, often accompanied by vomiting, nausea, and abdominal cramps, typically due to a viral or bacterial infection.
  • Food poisoning: Acute diarrhoea and vomiting that occurs within hours of consuming contaminated food.
  • Traveller’s diarrhoea: Acute diarrhoea that develops during or shortly after travel to a foreign country, usually due to infection with local pathogens.
  • Toddler’s diarrhoea (paediatrics): Chronic, painless, watery diarrhoea in a young child who is otherwise healthy and growing well, often related to diet.
  • Irritable bowel syndrome: A functional disorder characterised by chronic, relapsing abdominal pain associated with diarrhoea, constipation, or a mix of both.
  • Lactose intolerance: Diarrhoea, bloating, and gas that occurs after consuming dairy products due to a deficiency in the lactase enzyme.
  • Faecal impaction (overflow diarrhoea): Leakage of liquid stool around a hard, impacted stool mass, commonly seen in the elderly or immobile.
  • COVID-19: Can present with gastrointestinal symptoms, including acute diarrhoea, sometimes in the absence of respiratory symptoms.
Easily missed
  • Medication induced diarrhoea: A common side effect of many drugs, including antibiotics, metformin, NSAIDs, and proton pump inhibitors.
  • Hyperthyroidism: Increased gut motility caused by excess thyroid hormone can lead to chronic diarrhoea and weight loss.
  • Diabetic Autonomic neuropathy: Damage to the nerves supplying the gut in long-standing diabetes can cause erratic bowel function, including bouts of diarrhoea.
  • Coeliac disease: An autoimmune condition triggered by gluten, causing malabsorption, chronic diarrhoea, bloating, and weight loss.
  • Short bowel syndrome: Malabsorptive diarrhoea following surgical resection of a significant length of the small intestine.
  • Pancreatic insufficiency: Failure of the pancreas to produce digestive enzymes, leading to malabsorption of fat (steatorrhoea) and chronic diarrhoea.
  • Addison's disease: Adrenal insufficiency can present with a variety of vague symptoms, including chronic diarrhoea, fatigue, and weight loss.
Other Differential Diagnoses to consider
  • Small intestinal bacterial overgrowth (SIBO): Chronic diarrhoea due to bacterial colonisation of the small intestine.
  • Chronic pancreatitis: Leads to pancreatic enzyme deficiency causing malabsorption and diarrhoea.
  • Whipple's disease: A rare bacterial infection causing chronic diarrhoea and malabsorption.
  • Ischaemic colitis: Occurs due to reduced blood flow to the colon, leading to pain and diarrhoea.
  • Microscopic colitis: An inflammation of the colon's lining, causing chronic watery diarrhoea.
  • Radiation enteritis: A delayed complication of abdominal or pelvic radiotherapy, resulting in chronic diarrhoea.
  • Carcinoid syndrome: A neuroendocrine tumour causing diarrhoea due to excessive serotonin production.
History Taking
**Patient’s Description**: How can I help you today?
Asking the patient to describe their symptoms in their own words helps to capture the full extent of their concerns and guides the consultation. Relevance: Allows the patient to provide information that may not be revealed through specific questioning, helping to identify key aspects of their condition.
When did the diarrhoea start?
  • Understanding the onset is important for determining whether the diarrhoea is acute (e.g., gastroenteritis) or chronic (e.g., IBS, IBD).
  • Relevance: Acute diarrhoea may point towards infection or food poisoning, while a chronic duration suggests conditions such as IBS, coeliac disease, or colorectal cancer.
Is it continuous or does it come and go?
  • Helps differentiate between ongoing diarrhoea (inflammatory or malabsorptive conditions) and intermittent diarrhoea (e.g., IBS).
  • Relevance: Continuous diarrhoea suggests more serious or persistent causes (e.g., IBD, pancreatic insufficiency), whereas episodic diarrhoea could indicate IBS or lactose intolerance.
Can you describe the diarrhoea? (e.g., watery, bloody, greasy, etc.)
  • Watery diarrhoea is often seen in viral gastroenteritis, while greasy stool can indicate fat malabsorption (pancreatic insufficiency).
  • Relevance: Bloody diarrhoea raises concerns for IBD, colorectal cancer, or severe bacterial infections (e.g., Campylobacter or Shigella).
Are there any foods or activities that make the diarrhoea worse or better?
  • Helps to assess if food intolerances (e.g., lactose intolerance) or functional causes (e.g., IBS) may be involved.
  • Relevance: Worsening after meals could suggest malabsorption syndromes like coeliac disease, while no clear triggers might lean towards infectious causes.
Have you had any nausea, vomiting, or fever?
  • Fever with diarrhoea often points to an infectious cause (e.g., gastroenteritis), whereas nausea and vomiting might indicate food poisoning.
  • Relevance: A positive response is more indicative of acute infections or inflammatory processes, while the absence of these symptoms suggests a non-infectious cause like IBS.
Have you noticed any weight loss, fatigue, or night sweats?
  • Unintentional weight loss and fatigue can suggest more serious pathology such as colorectal cancer, IBD, or malabsorptive conditions like coeliac disease.
  • Relevance: Positive findings may indicate malignancy, chronic inflammation, or endocrine issues (e.g., Addison’s disease). A negative response could point towards benign causes like IBS.
Have you travelled abroad recently?
  • Essential for identifying traveller’s diarrhoea, which is often due to bacterial, viral, or parasitic infections contracted in endemic areas.
  • Relevance: A positive travel history increases suspicion for specific pathogens, while no recent travel may lead the clinician to focus on other causes like IBS or food intolerance.
Do you have any other medical conditions?
  • Comorbidities such as diabetes (autonomic neuropathy), thyroid disease, or previous abdominal surgery (short bowel syndrome) can influence the development of diarrhoea.
  • Relevance: Identifying underlying chronic conditions helps to narrow down the differential diagnosis and direct investigations.
Are you taking any medications?
  • Certain medications, such as antibiotics, metformin, or laxatives, can cause diarrhoea.
  • Relevance: A positive history of medication use may point towards drug-induced diarrhoea, and discontinuing the drug could relieve symptoms.
**Ideas, Concerns, and Expectations (ICE)**
  • These questions help uncover the patient’s thoughts on their symptoms, any underlying fears (e.g., cancer, IBD), and their hopes for the consultation.
  • Relevance: Addressing the patient’s concerns directly helps manage anxiety, ensure shared decision-making, and tailor the management plan to their expectations.
Examination Strategy
General inspection of the patient
  • Observe for signs of dehydration (dry mucous membranes, sunken eyes), weight loss, or generalised weakness. Look for any visible signs of distress.
  • Relevance: Dehydration may suggest acute diarrhoea from infectious causes, while significant weight loss can indicate chronic conditions such as IBD, coeliac disease, or malignancy. A well-appearing patient may suggest functional causes like IBS.
Assessment of vital signs (temperature, pulse, blood pressure)
  • Check for fever, tachycardia, hypotension, or other abnormalities in vital signs.
  • Relevance: Fever may indicate infectious gastroenteritis, while hypotension or tachycardia could signal dehydration or adrenal insufficiency (Addison’s disease). A lack of abnormalities could suggest a more chronic or benign cause like IBS.
Inspection of the abdomen
  • Observe for abdominal distension, visible masses, or surgical scars, which may provide clues to the underlying cause of diarrhoea.
  • Relevance: Abdominal distension might suggest obstruction, while surgical scars could point towards short bowel syndrome. A flat abdomen with no visible abnormalities may be more consistent with functional causes.
Palpation of the abdomen
  • Check for tenderness, masses, or organomegaly. Palpation is particularly important in the assessment of the liver, spleen, and any palpable masses.
  • Relevance: Generalised tenderness may indicate gastroenteritis, while localised tenderness could suggest diverticulitis or appendicitis. Hepatomegaly may point towards liver disease (e.g., cirrhosis, hepatitis), while a palpable mass could raise suspicion for malignancy or abscess.
Auscultation of bowel sounds
  • Listen for normal, hyperactive, or absent bowel sounds using a stethoscope.
  • Relevance: Hyperactive bowel sounds may suggest gastroenteritis or malabsorption, while absent bowel sounds could indicate bowel obstruction or paralytic ileus. Normal bowel sounds may suggest a functional cause like IBS.
Digital rectal examination (DRE)
  • Assess for faecal impaction, rectal masses, and check for the presence of blood or mucous in the stool.
  • Relevance: Faecal impaction suggests overflow diarrhoea, while palpable rectal masses may raise concern for colorectal cancer. The presence of blood on examination might indicate IBD, malignancy, or ischaemic colitis.
Inspection of the skin
  • Look for signs of dehydration (dry skin, decreased skin turgor), pigmentation changes (e.g., in Addison’s disease), or any rashes.
  • Relevance: Hyperpigmentation may suggest Addison’s disease, while dry skin could indicate dehydration. A dermatitis herpetiformis rash may suggest coeliac disease.
Examination of the thyroid gland
  • Palpate for thyroid enlargement or nodules, as well as check for signs of thyrotoxicosis (e.g., tremor, exophthalmos).
  • Relevance: A goitre or signs of hyperthyroidism suggest hyperthyroidism as a potential cause of diarrhoea, which can often be associated with increased bowel motility.
Red Flag Synthesis
Blood in stool (haematochezia or melena)
  • Haematochezia (bright red blood) or melena (black, tarry stools) can indicate gastrointestinal bleeding, which may arise from various sources, such as inflammatory bowel disease, colorectal cancer, diverticulitis, or an upper GI bleed.
  • Significance: This could indicate colorectal cancer, ischaemic colitis, or severe infection. Urgent investigation such as endoscopy or colonoscopy is warranted.
Unexplained weight loss
  • Unintentional weight loss, especially when associated with chronic diarrhoea, suggests a serious underlying pathology, such as cancer, malabsorption syndromes (e.g., coeliac disease, pancreatic insufficiency), or chronic infections.
  • Significance: Weight loss raises concern for malignancy (e.g., colorectal cancer), chronic infections (e.g., HIV), or advanced IBD. This requires prompt evaluation and further investigation.
Persistent fever
  • Fever associated with diarrhoea may suggest an infectious cause, but when persistent, it raises concern for systemic infections, inflammatory processes, or abscesses.
  • Significance: A persistent fever may indicate intra-abdominal abscess, HIV infection, or inflammatory bowel disease flare-up, which requires further investigation to prevent complications.
Severe abdominal pain
  • Severe pain, particularly if sudden or associated with peritonitis, may suggest serious conditions such as bowel perforation, ischaemic colitis, or an acute abdomen.
  • Significance: This could indicate an acute surgical emergency like perforation, bowel ischaemia, or diverticulitis with abscess formation, requiring urgent surgical assessment.
Signs of dehydration (e.g., dry mucous membranes, low urine output, dizziness)
  • Severe diarrhoea, especially in the elderly or very young, can lead to significant fluid loss and dehydration, which can become life-threatening without prompt treatment.
  • Significance: Dehydration requires immediate fluid replacement, and in cases of electrolyte imbalance or shock, hospitalisation and intravenous fluids may be necessary.
Family history of colorectal cancer
  • A strong family history of colorectal cancer increases the risk of this condition, especially in those with new-onset diarrhoea or change in bowel habits.
  • Significance: Patients with a family history of colorectal cancer should be assessed urgently, as early diagnosis of colorectal cancer significantly improves prognosis.
Nocturnal diarrhoea
  • Diarrhoea that occurs at night, disrupting sleep, is typically more concerning for organic pathology (e.g., IBD, malignancy) compared to functional disorders like IBS, which rarely causes nocturnal symptoms.
  • Significance: Nocturnal diarrhoea raises concern for inflammatory or neoplastic conditions such as IBD or colorectal cancer, and warrants further investigation.
Recent travel to endemic areas
  • Diarrhoea following travel, particularly to regions with poor sanitation, raises suspicion for infections such as parasitic, bacterial, or viral gastroenteritis.
  • Significance: Travel-related diarrhoea could indicate serious infections like amoebiasis or cholera. Urgent stool tests and possible hospitalisation may be required.
Investigations Strategy & Interpretation
Stool microscopy, culture, and sensitivity (MC&S)
  • Stool MC&S is essential for identifying bacterial, viral, or parasitic causes of diarrhoea, particularly in cases of acute onset or recent travel.
  • Result Significance: Positive results suggest infectious causes such as Salmonella, Shigella, Campylobacter, or parasitic infections. Negative results may help rule out infectious diarrhoea and suggest non-infectious causes such as IBS or IBD.
Stool for ova, cysts, and parasites (OCP)
  • This test is particularly important for patients with a history of recent travel or immunocompromised status, as parasitic infections can be a cause of persistent diarrhoea.
  • Result Significance: Positive results suggest parasitic infections such as Giardia, Entamoeba histolytica, or Cryptosporidium. Negative results would rule out parasitic causes of diarrhoea.
Full blood count (FBC)
  • FBC can help identify anaemia or infection. Anaemia could indicate chronic blood loss or malabsorption, while a raised white cell count may suggest infection or inflammation.
  • Result Significance: A raised white cell count suggests infection or inflammatory conditions like IBD. Low haemoglobin could indicate chronic blood loss (e.g., colorectal cancer) or malnutrition (e.g., coeliac disease). A normal result would point towards non-inflammatory causes such as IBS.
C-reactive protein (CRP)
  • CRP is a marker of inflammation and can help differentiate between inflammatory causes of diarrhoea (e.g., IBD) and functional causes (e.g., IBS).
  • Result Significance: Elevated CRP suggests active inflammation, which may point towards IBD, diverticulitis, or infection. A normal CRP would be more consistent with non-inflammatory causes like IBS or malabsorption syndromes.
Thyroid function tests (TFTs)
  • Hyperthyroidism is a potential cause of diarrhoea due to increased bowel motility. TFTs help assess thyroid hormone levels to rule in or out thyroid-related causes of diarrhoea.
  • Result Significance: Abnormal TFTs, particularly elevated free T4 and suppressed TSH, suggest hyperthyroidism as a cause of diarrhoea. Normal results would rule out thyroid dysfunction.
Coeliac serology (tTG-IgA)
  • This test detects tissue transglutaminase antibodies, which are typically elevated in coeliac disease, a common cause of chronic diarrhoea due to malabsorption.
  • Result Significance: Positive coeliac serology strongly suggests coeliac disease, which would require further confirmation via duodenal biopsy. Negative results rule out coeliac disease as a cause of diarrhoea.
Faecal calprotectin
  • Faecal calprotectin is a marker of intestinal inflammation and is particularly useful in distinguishing between IBD and IBS in patients with chronic diarrhoea.
  • Result Significance: Elevated faecal calprotectin suggests IBD, while a normal result points towards functional causes such as IBS.
Colonoscopy
  • A colonoscopy allows direct visualisation of the colon and can detect inflammatory changes, polyps, tumours, or other structural abnormalities. It is crucial for patients with red flag symptoms such as blood in the stool or weight loss.
  • Result Significance: Positive findings, such as polyps or tumours, may indicate colorectal cancer or other significant pathology. A normal colonoscopy helps rule out structural causes of diarrhoea, such as cancer or diverticulitis.
Abdominal CT scan
  • CT scans can help identify intra-abdominal pathology such as tumours, abscesses, or signs of inflammation (e.g., diverticulitis, ischaemic colitis).
  • Result Significance: Positive results may indicate masses, abscesses, or significant inflammatory processes. A negative result reduces the likelihood of structural causes, focusing attention on functional or infectious causes of diarrhoea.
Evidence-Based Diagnosis (EBDx)
Blood in stool (haematochezia or melena)
  • Blood in the stool, particularly in the form of haematochezia, is a key finding that may indicate colorectal cancer, IBD, or ischaemic colitis. Melena is more often associated with upper GI bleeding.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity for colorectal cancer: 55%, Specificity: 90%, Positive Likelihood Ratio: 5.5.
  • Reference: National Institute for Health and Care Excellence (NICE) Guidelines on Colorectal Cancer (NG151).
  • Significance: Positive findings strongly suggest malignancy or inflammatory disease, while the absence of blood reduces the likelihood of colorectal cancer or severe inflammatory conditions.
Faecal calprotectin
  • Faecal calprotectin is a biomarker of intestinal inflammation, widely used to differentiate IBD from IBS in patients presenting with chronic diarrhoea.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity for IBD: 93%, Specificity: 96%, Positive Likelihood Ratio: 23.25.
  • Reference: Van Rheenen PF, et al. "Faecal calprotectin for screening of patients with suspected inflammatory bowel disease: diagnostic meta-analysis." BMJ (2010).
  • Significance: A positive result strongly indicates the presence of IBD, while a negative result makes IBD very unlikely, supporting a diagnosis of IBS or other non-inflammatory causes.
Unexplained weight loss
  • Unexplained weight loss, especially in combination with chronic diarrhoea, is a red flag symptom that may indicate malignancy, chronic infection, or malabsorption syndromes.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity for colorectal cancer: 37%, Specificity: 98%, Positive Likelihood Ratio: 18.5.
  • Reference: Hamilton W, et al. "The risk of colorectal cancer with symptoms at different ages and between the sexes: a case-control study." BMC Medicine (2009).
  • Significance: Weight loss significantly increases the likelihood of serious conditions such as colorectal cancer, requiring prompt investigation. Absence of weight loss does not fully rule out these conditions but lowers suspicion.
Stool culture
  • Stool culture is performed to identify bacterial pathogens responsible for diarrhoea, particularly in cases of acute onset or after travel.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity for bacterial pathogens: 45-60%, Specificity: >95%, Positive Likelihood Ratio: 12.
  • Reference: Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC) Guidelines for the Diagnosis and Management of Gastroenteritis (2017).
  • Significance: A positive stool culture identifies specific infectious causes (e.g., Salmonella, Shigella, Campylobacter), while a negative culture points towards non-infectious causes such as IBS or IBD.
C-reactive protein (CRP)
  • CRP is an inflammatory marker used to help distinguish between inflammatory and non-inflammatory causes of diarrhoea.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity for IBD: 50-70%, Specificity: 85%, Positive Likelihood Ratio: 3.3.
  • Reference: NICE Clinical Knowledge Summary on Inflammatory Bowel Disease (2022).
  • Significance: Elevated CRP suggests an inflammatory process such as IBD or infection. A normal CRP reduces the likelihood of IBD, pointing towards functional causes like IBS.
Colonoscopy
  • Colonoscopy is the gold standard investigation for direct visualisation of the colon, allowing for biopsy and diagnosis of colorectal pathology.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity for colorectal cancer: 95%, Specificity: 99%, Positive Likelihood Ratio: 95.
  • Reference: Rex DK, et al. "Quality indicators for colonoscopy." Gastrointestinal Endoscopy (2006).
  • Significance: A positive colonoscopy can confirm diagnoses such as colorectal cancer, polyps, or IBD, while a normal colonoscopy effectively rules out structural causes of diarrhoea.
Diagnostic Timeout & Cognitive Biases

Awareness of common cognitive traps is crucial for diagnostic accuracy in patients presenting with diarrhoea. Before settling on a diagnosis, clinicians should take a 'diagnostic timeout' to reflect on their reasoning and challenge potential biases.

  • Anchoring Bias: This is the tendency to fixate on initial information. For example, anchoring on a diagnosis of Irritable Bowel Syndrome (IBS) in a young person and failing to consider new red flags like weight loss or rectal bleeding that might suggest Inflammatory Bowel Disease (IBD). A key reflective question is: "Could this common symptom be masking a serious new diagnosis?"
  • Premature Closure: Accepting a diagnosis before it is fully verified. For instance, diagnosing simple gastroenteritis in an elderly patient with diarrhoea and abdominal pain without considering more serious alternatives like ischaemic colitis or diverticulitis, especially if the symptoms persist.
  • Confirmation Bias: The inclination to seek or interpret information that confirms a pre-existing belief. For example, attributing a patient's diarrhoea to a new medication while downplaying other concerning features like nocturnal symptoms, which are more suggestive of organic disease.
  • Availability Heuristic: Overestimating the likelihood of a diagnosis because it is more recent or memorable in the clinician's experience, such as diagnosing multiple cases of traveller's diarrhoea after a holiday period, potentially overlooking a domestic cause of infectious colitis. It is vital to ask: "Have I considered the full range of possibilities, not just the most obvious or recent ones?"
Illustrative Management Pathways

For educational purposes, this section describes the typical principles of management for key diagnoses. These are not prescriptive instructions. All management must be guided by official UK guidelines, local formularies, and senior clinical advice.

Acute Infectious Diarrhoea

The primary goal is to prevent and treat dehydration. Most cases are self-limiting. Management includes encouraging oral fluid intake, with oral rehydration solution (ORS) recommended for vulnerable patients (elderly, young children). Symptomatic relief with loperamide may be used cautiously in adults if there are no signs of systemic infection or bloody stools. Stool samples are sent if the diarrhoea is severe, persistent, bloody, or if there is a suspicion of a public health risk. Antibiotics are generally not required for viral gastroenteritis and are reserved for specific bacterial infections (e.g., Campylobacter in severe cases, or Giardia) based on culture results and clinical context.

Inflammatory Bowel Disease (IBD)

Once suspected, patients require urgent referral to a gastroenterology specialist. Initial investigations include blood tests (FBC, CRP), stool tests (calprotectin, MC&S), and endoscopic evaluation (colonoscopy or sigmoidoscopy) with biopsies to confirm the diagnosis. Management aims to induce and maintain remission. This typically involves a stepwise approach starting with corticosteroids to control acute flare-ups, followed by long-term maintenance therapy with aminosalicylates (e.g., mesalazine) or immunomodulators (e.g., azathioprine). Biologic therapies (e.g., infliximab) are reserved for more severe or refractory disease. This is all managed under specialist care.

Colorectal Cancer

Patients presenting with red flag symptoms (e.g., persistent change in bowel habit, rectal bleeding, unexplained weight loss, iron deficiency anaemia, especially in older adults) require an urgent "two-week wait" referral to secondary care for investigation, as per NICE guidelines. The primary investigation is a colonoscopy to visualise the colon and obtain biopsies. If cancer is confirmed, further staging is done with CT scans. Management is decided by a multidisciplinary team (MDT) and typically involves surgical resection of the tumour, often combined with chemotherapy or radiotherapy depending on the stage and location of the cancer.

Irritable Bowel Syndrome (IBS)

The diagnosis of IBS is made based on positive symptom criteria (e.g., Rome IV criteria) after excluding red flags and relevant differential diagnoses through history, examination, and first-line investigations (FBC, CRP, coeliac serology, faecal calprotectin). Management is focused on education, reassurance, and a holistic approach. First-line advice includes lifestyle and dietary modifications (e.g., regular meals, adequate fluid intake, managing fibre intake). If symptoms persist, a trial of a low FODMAP diet under dietetic supervision may be beneficial. Symptomatic treatment includes antispasmodics for pain, loperamide for diarrhoea-predominant IBS, and laxatives for constipation-predominant IBS. Low-dose amitriptyline can be considered for persistent symptoms.

Reflection & Learning

These brief cases illustrate the application of clinical reasoning to different presentations of diarrhoea. They are for educational purposes only.

Case 1: Acute Diarrhoea in Primary Care

A 22-year-old university student presents to their GP with a 48-hour history of profuse watery diarrhoea, abdominal cramps, and one episode of vomiting. He mentions eating a chicken kebab from a van two nights ago. He has no significant past medical history and denies any rectal bleeding or weight loss. On examination, he is afebrile, his abdomen is soft with mild generalised tenderness, and bowel sounds are hyperactive. The problem representation is a young, healthy adult with acute onset of watery diarrhoea and vomiting with a clear link to a possible source of food poisoning. Management involves reassurance, advice on oral hydration, and safety netting to return if symptoms worsen, persist beyond a week, or if he develops fever or bloody stool.

Case 2: Chronic Diarrhoea in a Young Adult

A 28-year-old woman presents with a 6-month history of intermittent diarrhoea, often containing mucus and occasional streaks of blood. She reports abdominal cramping, which is relieved by opening her bowels, and feeling tired all the time. She thinks she has lost some weight. There is no travel history. The problem representation is a young adult with chronic, bloody diarrhoea and constitutional symptoms. This raises high suspicion for Inflammatory Bowel Disease (IBD). Initial investigations in primary care would include blood tests (FBC, U&Es, LFTs, CRP, coeliac screen) and stool tests (MC&S and faecal calprotectin). An urgent referral to gastroenterology for colonoscopy is warranted.

Case 3: Change in Bowel Habit in an Older Adult

A 68-year-old man presents with a 3-month history of looser and more frequent stools. He previously opened his bowels once daily but now goes three to four times a day. He has seen a small amount of dark red blood mixed in with the stool on a few occasions. He denies abdominal pain but feels his appetite is reduced. His wife thinks he has lost weight. The problem representation is an older adult with a persistent change in bowel habit, rectal bleeding, and unexplained weight loss. These are red flag symptoms for colorectal cancer. He requires an urgent two-week wait referral for a colonoscopy, as per NICE guidelines.
