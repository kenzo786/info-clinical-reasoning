Ankle and Foot Pain Diagnostic Reasoning Reference
Context: Ankle and Foot Pain Assessment
Overview

Ankle and foot pain is an exceedingly common presentation in both primary and emergency care, encompassing a vast spectrum of conditions from minor mechanical injuries to limb-threatening emergencies. The foot is a complex structure of bones, ligaments, tendons, and joints, making it susceptible to a wide range of traumatic, inflammatory, degenerative, and systemic pathologies.

The core challenge for the clinician is to differentiate benign, self-limiting problems from serious conditions that require urgent investigation and management. A systematic and structured approach is essential to identify red flag features that may indicate diagnoses such as septic arthritis, acute limb ischaemia, or an unrecognised fracture. This module provides a foundational framework for the diagnostic reasoning process for ankle and foot pain. For definitive, real-time management protocols, users must consult official UK guidelines from bodies such as NICE (e.g., NG194 for Diabetic Foot Problems, NG223 for Gout) and local clinical pathways.

Pathophysiology/Basic Science

Understanding the origin of ankle and foot pain is fundamental to forming an accurate differential diagnosis. Pain can be generated through several distinct mechanisms:

  • Traumatic/Mechanical Pain: This arises from direct injury to anatomical structures. An acute inversion injury can stretch or tear ligaments (sprain), while greater forces can cause fractures of the malleoli, tarsals, or metatarsals. Overuse and repetitive microtrauma lead to stress fractures or inflammatory conditions like plantar fasciitis and Achilles tendinopathy, where repetitive strain causes micro-tears and a degenerative or inflammatory response in the tissue.
  • Inflammatory Pain: This is driven by the body's immune response. In crystal arthropathies like gout, the deposition of monosodium urate crystals in a joint (classically the first metatarsophalangeal joint) triggers an intense, acute inflammatory cascade, resulting in severe pain, swelling, and erythema. In septic arthritis, bacterial invasion of the synovial space leads to a purulent inflammatory response that can rapidly destroy cartilage.
  • Degenerative Pain: This is characteristic of osteoarthritis, where progressive wear and tear of articular cartilage leads to the loss of joint space, osteophyte formation, and subchondral bone changes. The pain is often described as a deep, aching sensation that is typically worse with weight-bearing and activity, and associated with stiffness.
  • Neuropathic Pain: This originates from damage or dysfunction of the peripheral nerves. In diabetic neuropathy, chronic hyperglycaemia leads to nerve damage, which can paradoxically cause a loss of protective sensation (increasing injury risk) or positive symptoms like burning, tingling, or shooting pains. Nerve compression, such as in Morton's neuroma (interdigital nerve) or tarsal tunnel syndrome (posterior tibial nerve), causes localised pain and paraesthesia in the nerve's distribution.
  • Vascular/Ischaemic Pain: This occurs when blood supply is insufficient to meet metabolic demands. In acute limb ischaemia, a sudden occlusion of an artery leads to severe pain, pallor, pulselessness, and a cold extremity (the "6 Ps"). Chronic peripheral arterial disease can cause intermittent claudication, a cramp-like pain in the calf or foot on exertion, which is relieved by rest.
Differential Diagnosis (DDx) Framework

A structured approach to the differential diagnosis of ankle and foot pain is essential to ensure limb-threatening and systemic conditions are prioritised.

Must not miss!
  • Ankle or Foot Bone fracture: Presents with acute pain, swelling, and inability to bear weight, often following a specific traumatic event.
  • Achilles tendon rupture: Characterised by a sudden 'snap' or sensation of being kicked in the back of the ankle, resulting in weakness of plantar flexion.
  • Septic arthritis: An infection within a joint causing severe pain, swelling, erythema, and systemic fever, with marked pain on any passive movement.
  • Acute limb ischaemia: A vascular emergency presenting with the "6 Ps": pain, pallor, pulselessness, paraesthesia, perishingly cold, and paralysis.
  • Non-venous ulceration: Includes arterial or neuropathic ulcers, often indicating severe underlying peripheral arterial disease or uncontrolled diabetes.
  • Diabetic foot (painless): A complex presentation due to neuropathy, ischaemia, and immunodeficiency, where a significant ulcer or infection may be present without pain.
  • Osteomyelitis: An infection of the bone, which can present with localised, deep, boring pain, and may be associated with a non-healing ulcer or sinus tract.
Common causes
  • Soft tissue injury: A general term for sprains and strains of ligaments and muscles around the ankle and foot.
  • Ankle sprain: Typically an inversion injury causing pain, swelling, and tenderness over the lateral ligaments.
  • Achilles tendinopathy: Pain and stiffness at the back of the heel, which is often worse in the morning and after periods of inactivity.
  • Plantar fasciitis: Sharp, stabbing pain on the underside of the heel, classically worst with the first steps in the morning.
  • Warts and verrucae: Viral skin lesions, typically on the sole of the foot, which can be painful on weight-bearing.
  • Corns and calluses: Thickened areas of skin caused by pressure or friction, commonly over bony prominences.
  • Ingrown toenail: Occurs when the edge of a toenail grows into the surrounding skin, causing pain, inflammation, and potential infection.
  • Osteoarthritis: A degenerative joint disease causing aching pain and stiffness, commonly affecting the ankle or big toe joint.
  • Gout: An inflammatory arthritis causing sudden, severe episodes of pain, redness, and swelling, most often in the big toe.
  • Metatarsalgia: Pain and inflammation in the ball of the foot, often described as feeling like there is a pebble in the shoe.
  • Foot deformities: Structural abnormalities like flat feet (pes planus) or high arches (pes cavus) that can cause pain.
  • Hallux valgus: A bony bump (bunion) that forms on the joint at the base of the big toe, causing it to deviate outwards.
  • Morton's neuroma: A thickening of the tissue around a nerve leading to the toes, causing sharp, burning pain in the ball of the foot and toes.
  • March fracture: A stress fracture of a metatarsal bone, common in runners or soldiers, presenting with gradual onset forefoot pain.
  • Lumbar Radiculopathy: Referred pain from a compressed nerve root in the lower back (e.g., sciatica), which can radiate down to the foot.
  • Venous leg ulcer: Typically located around the malleolar area, associated with other signs of chronic venous insufficiency like oedema and haemosiderin staining.
  • Cellulitis: A bacterial skin infection causing a spreading area of redness, swelling, and warmth, often with systemic symptoms.
  • Arthropathy presentation: Joint pain and inflammation as a manifestation of a systemic inflammatory disease like rheumatoid or psoriatic arthritis.
Easily missed
  • Painful Peripheral neuropathy: Can present with burning, stabbing, or tingling pain, often in a "stocking" distribution, without an obvious injury or inflammatory cause.
Other Differential Diagnoses to consider
  • Tarsal tunnel syndrome: Compression of the posterior tibial nerve causing medial ankle pain and paraesthesia.
  • Reflex sympathetic dystrophy (complex regional pain syndrome): Chronic pain condition often following trauma or surgery.
  • Osteochondritis dissecans: Localised bone necrosis causing persistent ankle pain and stiffness.
  • Erythema nodosum: Painful nodules over the shins or feet associated with systemic diseases or infections.
  • Psoriatic arthritis: May present with joint pain and characteristic psoriatic skin changes.
  • Sickle cell crisis: Vaso-occlusive episodes affecting joints and bones, particularly in individuals with sickle cell disease.
  • ?COVID-19-related arthropathy: Post-viral arthropathy or associated inflammatory syndromes.
History Taking
Patient’s Description: How can I help you today?
Starting with an open-ended question allows the patient to describe their symptoms in their own words, which provides insight into their perspective and guides further targeted history taking. Relevance: Helps identify the primary concern, its impact, and any red flag features warranting urgent investigation.
Can you describe the pain? (e.g., sharp, dull, throbbing)
  • Understanding the quality of pain can help differentiate between conditions. For instance, sharp pain may suggest a fracture or acute ligamentous injury, while dull or throbbing pain may point to osteoarthritis or chronic overuse injuries.
  • Relevance: Sharp pain suggests conditions like a fracture or acute sprain; dull, aching pain may suggest chronic tendinopathy or arthritis.
When did the pain start? Was it sudden or gradual?
  • The onset and progression provide important diagnostic clues. Sudden onset may indicate trauma or rupture, while gradual onset often points to overuse injuries or degenerative conditions.
  • Relevance: Sudden onset with trauma suggests fractures or ruptures, whereas gradual onset supports conditions like plantar fasciitis or Achilles tendinopathy.
What makes the pain better or worse? (e.g., movement, rest, footwear)
  • Exploring aggravating and relieving factors helps refine the differential diagnosis. For example, pain worsened by activity may indicate tendinopathy, while pain that improves with activity might suggest inflammatory arthritis.
  • Relevance: Pain worsened by movement suggests mechanical causes (e.g., sprain), while pain relieved by rest might suggest inflammatory or vascular conditions.
Have you noticed any associated symptoms? (e.g., swelling, redness, fever)
  • Associated symptoms like swelling and redness suggest inflammatory or infectious causes, while fever might point to systemic infection (e.g., cellulitis, septic arthritis).
  • Relevance: Positive response suggests infections or inflammation; absence may favour mechanical or neuropathic causes.
Do you have a history of trauma or injury to the area?
  • Direct trauma suggests fractures or sprains. A history of repetitive trauma may point to stress fractures or overuse injuries.
  • Relevance: Positive history of trauma strongly supports mechanical causes; absence of trauma may suggest systemic or degenerative conditions.
Do you have a history of conditions like diabetes, gout, or vascular disease?
  • Past medical history provides critical context, as conditions like diabetes increase the risk of diabetic foot complications, and gout predisposes to acute joint pain.
  • Relevance: Positive history raises suspicion for diabetic foot, gout, or peripheral vascular disease as causes of symptoms.
Have you experienced numbness, tingling, or weakness?
  • Neurological symptoms like numbness or tingling suggest peripheral neuropathy, lumbar radiculopathy, or tarsal tunnel syndrome.
  • Relevance: Positive response supports neurological causes; absence may favour musculoskeletal or inflammatory conditions.
Ideas, Concerns, and Expectations (ICE)
  • Asking ICE questions ensures the consultation addresses the patient’s understanding and worries. For example, a patient with diabetes may worry about limb loss, while another may expect simple pain relief.
  • Relevance: Helps uncover patient priorities and fears, which may highlight conditions (e.g., fracture, infection) they suspect or are anxious about.
Examination Strategy
Inspection of the foot and ankle
  • Inspect for deformity, swelling, erythema, bruising, or wounds. Look for signs of trauma (e.g., bruising) or infection (e.g., erythema, discharge).
  • Relevance: Swelling and erythema suggest inflammatory or infectious causes (e.g., cellulitis, septic arthritis), while deformity may indicate fracture or dislocation. Absence of visible abnormalities may point to neuropathic or referred pain.
Palpation of the ankle and foot
  • Palpate bony landmarks (e.g., medial and lateral malleoli, metatarsals) and soft tissues for tenderness. Note warmth, swelling, or crepitus.
  • Relevance: Localised tenderness suggests fractures, sprains, or specific inflammatory conditions (e.g., gout). Generalised warmth and swelling may suggest systemic conditions like cellulitis or septic arthritis.
Range of motion (ROM) assessment
  • Assess active and passive movements at the ankle and subtalar joints. Look for pain, stiffness, or restricted movement.
  • Relevance: Reduced ROM with pain suggests mechanical or inflammatory conditions (e.g., osteoarthritis, tendon injuries). Normal ROM with pain may suggest referred or neuropathic causes.
Special tests: Anterior drawer test (ankle instability)
  • Perform to assess the integrity of the anterior talofibular ligament (ATFL). Excessive forward displacement indicates ATFL rupture.
  • Relevance: Positive test supports ankle sprain with ATFL involvement. Negative findings reduce the likelihood of ligament rupture.
Thompson test (Achilles tendon integrity)
  • Squeeze the calf while the patient is prone with feet hanging. Absence of plantar flexion suggests Achilles tendon rupture.
  • Relevance: Positive test strongly supports Achilles tendon rupture. Normal findings suggest intact tendon integrity.
Neurological examination
  • Assess sensory and motor function of the lower limb, including reflexes and strength testing.
  • Relevance: Reduced sensation or strength suggests lumbar radiculopathy or peripheral neuropathy. Normal findings help rule out neurological causes.
Vascular assessment
  • Check for peripheral pulses (dorsalis pedis, posterior tibial) and assess capillary refill time. Inspect for ischaemic changes.
  • Relevance: Absent pulses or delayed capillary refill suggests peripheral vascular disease or acute limb ischaemia. Normal findings reduce suspicion of vascular causes.
Gait analysis
  • Observe the patient’s gait for abnormalities such as limping or asymmetry. Look for signs of pain during weight-bearing.
  • Relevance: Abnormal gait suggests mechanical issues like fractures, sprains, or deformities. Normal gait may point to referred or mild causes of pain.
Red Flag Synthesis
Severe pain with inability to bear weight
  • Inability to bear weight may indicate a significant structural injury, such as a fracture or Achilles tendon rupture, or severe soft tissue damage.
  • Significance: Suggests a fracture or tendon rupture, requiring urgent imaging and possible orthopaedic intervention.
Erythema, warmth, and systemic symptoms (e.g., fever)
  • These symptoms raise suspicion of infection, such as cellulitis, septic arthritis, or osteomyelitis.
  • Significance: Suggests an infectious process requiring urgent antibiotics and possible surgical intervention.
Cool, pale, or cyanotic foot
  • These signs indicate compromised vascular supply to the limb, which could be due to acute limb ischaemia or severe peripheral arterial disease.
  • Significance: Suggests acute limb ischaemia, a vascular emergency requiring immediate intervention to prevent limb loss.
Non-healing or painless ulceration
  • Painless ulcers, particularly in diabetic patients, may indicate neuropathy or underlying ischaemia. Non-healing ulcers raise concerns about infection or malignancy.
  • Significance: Suggests diabetic foot complications, peripheral arterial disease, or squamous cell carcinoma, requiring multidisciplinary management.
Rapidly progressive swelling with severe pain
  • This combination suggests a possible compartment syndrome or necrotising fasciitis, both of which are surgical emergencies.
  • Significance: Suggests compartment syndrome or necrotising fasciitis, requiring immediate intervention to prevent permanent damage or sepsis.
Neurological symptoms (e.g., numbness, weakness, or paraesthesia)
  • Neurological deficits may suggest lumbar radiculopathy, peripheral neuropathy, or tarsal tunnel syndrome.
  • Significance: Suggests nerve impingement or systemic neurological conditions, requiring further evaluation and imaging.
History of trauma with deformity
  • Visible deformity following trauma strongly indicates a fracture or dislocation, requiring urgent reduction or surgical management.
  • Significance: Suggests fracture or dislocation, necessitating imaging and orthopaedic intervention.
Investigations Strategy & Interpretation
Plain X-ray of the foot and ankle
  • Plain radiographs are essential for identifying fractures, dislocations, and degenerative changes such as osteoarthritis.
  • Result Significance: Positive findings confirm fractures, joint dislocations, or degenerative conditions, while negative results help rule out structural abnormalities.
MRI of the foot and ankle
  • MRI is ideal for assessing soft tissue injuries, including ligament or tendon tears, and for detecting subtle bone marrow oedema in conditions like stress fractures.
  • Result Significance: Positive findings suggest tendon ruptures, ligament injuries, or stress fractures. A normal MRI reduces the likelihood of soft tissue or occult bony injuries.
Ultrasound of the foot and ankle
  • Useful for evaluating soft tissue structures, such as tendons and ligaments, and for guiding aspiration of suspected joint effusions.
  • Result Significance: Positive findings support diagnoses like Achilles tendinopathy or joint effusions. Negative results may rule out soft tissue abnormalities.
Blood tests: Full blood count (FBC), C-reactive protein (CRP), erythrocyte sedimentation rate (ESR)
  • These tests help identify systemic inflammation or infection, which can indicate cellulitis, septic arthritis, or osteomyelitis.
  • Result Significance: Elevated CRP or ESR suggests infection or inflammatory arthritis. Normal results may help rule out systemic inflammatory or infectious causes.
Joint aspiration and synovial fluid analysis
  • Indicated if septic arthritis or gout is suspected. Analysis includes microscopy, culture, and crystal examination.
  • Result Significance: Positive culture suggests septic arthritis, while urate crystals confirm gout. Negative findings reduce suspicion of infectious or crystal arthropathy.
Doppler ultrasound or ankle-brachial pressure index (ABPI)
  • Used to assess vascular supply if peripheral arterial disease or acute limb ischaemia is suspected.
  • Result Significance: Abnormal findings suggest vascular insufficiency. Normal results help rule out significant arterial compromise.
Nerve conduction studies
  • Helpful for assessing suspected peripheral neuropathy or nerve entrapment syndromes like tarsal tunnel syndrome.
  • Result Significance: Positive findings confirm nerve involvement. Normal results make neuropathic causes less likely.
Evidence-Based Diagnosis (EBDx)
Tenderness at bony landmarks (Ottawa Ankle Rules)
  • The Ottawa Ankle Rules are a clinical decision tool to identify the need for X-ray in suspected ankle injuries, focusing on tenderness over bony prominences and inability to bear weight.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 98%, Specificity: 32%, Negative Predictive Value: High (rule-out tool).
  • Reference: Stiell et al., "Decision Rules for the Use of Radiography in Acute Ankle Injuries," JAMA, 1993.
  • Significance: High sensitivity means a negative result effectively rules out significant fractures, avoiding unnecessary imaging.
Absence of plantar flexion on the Thompson test
  • The Thompson test assesses for Achilles tendon rupture. Lack of plantar flexion when squeezing the calf indicates a complete rupture.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 96%, Specificity: 93%.
  • Reference: Maffulli et al., "Clinical Tests in Diagnosis of Achilles Tendon Ruptures," Clinical Orthopaedics and Related Research, 2001.
  • Significance: Positive test strongly indicates Achilles rupture, guiding urgent orthopaedic referral.
Elevated CRP and ESR in suspected infection
  • Inflammatory markers like CRP and ESR are elevated in infectious and inflammatory conditions, including cellulitis, septic arthritis, and osteomyelitis.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: CRP ~90% for septic arthritis, Specificity: ~70%; ESR Sensitivity: ~85% for osteomyelitis.
  • Reference: Margaretten et al., "Does This Adult Patient Have Septic Arthritis?" JAMA, 2007.
  • Significance: Elevated values support infection, guiding further imaging or synovial fluid analysis.
Joint aspiration showing urate crystals
  • The presence of monosodium urate crystals in joint fluid is diagnostic of gout. Analysis includes polarised light microscopy.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 85%, Specificity: ~100% for gout diagnosis.
  • Reference: Pascual et al., "Synovial Fluid Analysis for Diagnosis of Crystal-Induced Arthritis," Arthritis Research & Therapy, 2012.
  • Significance: Positive findings confirm gout, guiding targeted treatment with urate-lowering therapies.
Absent dorsalis pedis or posterior tibial pulses
  • Loss of palpable peripheral pulses suggests peripheral arterial disease or acute limb ischaemia. Additional tests like Doppler ultrasound or ABPI confirm diagnosis.
  • Evidence: ABPI Sensitivity: 95%, Specificity: 99% for detecting peripheral arterial disease.
  • Reference: Norgren et al., "Inter-Society Consensus for the Management of Peripheral Arterial Disease," European Journal of Vascular and Endovascular Surgery, 2007.
  • Significance: Positive findings confirm vascular compromise, necessitating urgent vascular referral.
MRI showing bone marrow oedema
  • Bone marrow oedema on MRI is a hallmark of stress fractures or early-stage osteomyelitis. MRI is the gold standard for detecting subtle bony or soft tissue changes.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: ~99% for stress fractures, Specificity: ~90%.
  • Reference: Banal et al., "Sensitivity and Specificity of MRI for Stress Fractures," European Radiology, 2008.
  • Significance: Positive findings guide treatment decisions, such as rest for stress fractures or antibiotics for osteomyelitis.
Diagnostic Timeout & Cognitive Biases

Awareness of common cognitive errors is vital to improve diagnostic accuracy when assessing a patient with ankle or foot pain. Before finalising a diagnosis, it is good practice to take a 'diagnostic timeout' to challenge initial assumptions.

  • Anchoring Bias: The tendency to fixate on the most obvious diagnosis, such as an ankle sprain following a fall, while failing to consider a less common but more serious injury, like a fracture of the talar dome or syndesmotic injury, especially if pain is atypical or recovery is delayed. A key question is: "Could this be something other than a simple sprain?"
  • Confirmation Bias: Seeking evidence that supports an initial hypothesis while ignoring contradictory signs. For example, focusing on a patient's history of gout to explain a hot, swollen joint, while downplaying a high fever or disproportionate pain that might point towards septic arthritis instead.
  • Attribution Error: Ascribing symptoms to a patient's existing condition without due diligence. A common pitfall is to attribute any new foot problem in a patient with diabetes to "just their diabetes," thereby missing an acute, treatable cause like a new ulcer, cellulitis, or Charcot neuroarthropathy.
  • Availability Heuristic: Overestimating the likelihood of diagnoses that are more common or more recent in one's experience (e.g., plantar fasciitis) while underestimating less frequent but critical diagnoses (e.g., osteomyelitis underlying a foot ulcer). One must consciously consider the 'worst-case scenario' for every presentation.
Illustrative Management Pathways

For educational purposes, this section describes the typical principles of management for key diagnoses. These are not prescriptive instructions. All management must be guided by official UK guidelines, local formularies, and senior clinical advice.

Acute Ankle Sprain

Once a fracture is excluded (e.g., by applying the Ottawa Ankle Rules), management centres on the principles of PRICE (Protection, Rest, Ice, Compression, Elevation) for the first 48-72 hours to reduce swelling and pain. Simple analgesia like paracetamol and NSAIDs (oral or topical) is recommended. Early mobilisation, as pain allows, is encouraged to prevent stiffness. Patients should be advised on the expected recovery timeline and given safety netting advice on when to seek re-assessment if symptoms do not improve.

Septic Arthritis

This is a rheumatological and orthopaedic emergency requiring immediate hospital admission. Management is focused on urgent joint aspiration to obtain synovial fluid for microscopy, culture, and sensitivities (MC&S) before starting antibiotics. Empirical intravenous antibiotics, guided by local microbiology protocols, should be commenced immediately after aspiration. Surgical washout of the joint may be required. The limb should be immobilised in a functional position and analgesia provided.

Diabetic Foot Ulcer

Management of a diabetic foot ulcer is complex and requires a multidisciplinary team (MDT) approach, including diabetologists, podiatrists, vascular surgeons, and tissue viability nurses. Key principles include urgent assessment of infection, neuropathy, and ischaemia. If infection is present, wound swabs and tissue samples should be taken and antibiotics commenced. Debridement of the ulcer and effective pressure offloading (e.g., with a cast or specialised footwear) are crucial to promote healing. Vascular supply must be assessed, and revascularisation considered if significant peripheral arterial disease is present.

Acute Gout

The primary goal is to settle the acute inflammation and relieve pain. According to NICE guidelines, the first-line treatment is typically a high-dose oral NSAID (if no contraindications exist), colchicine, or oral corticosteroids. The choice depends on patient comorbidities and preference. Rest and elevation of the affected joint are advised. Once the acute flare has settled, long-term management strategies, including lifestyle advice and consideration of urate-lowering therapy (e.g., allopurinol), should be discussed to prevent future attacks.

Reflection & Learning

These brief cases illustrate the application of clinical reasoning to different ankle and foot pain presentations. They are for educational purposes only.

Case 1: Suspected Fracture in the Urgent Treatment Centre

A 28-year-old woman presents after twisting her ankle playing netball. She has significant pain and is unable to bear weight. On examination, there is marked tenderness over the posterior edge of the lateral malleolus. The problem representation is a young adult with an acute traumatic injury and positive findings on the Ottawa Ankle Rules (inability to bear weight and bony tenderness). This presentation mandates an urgent X-ray to rule out a fracture, rather than a presumptive diagnosis of a simple sprain.

Case 2: The Hot Swollen Joint in the Emergency Department

A 70-year-old man with a history of knee osteoarthritis presents with a 24-hour history of a severely painful, red, and swollen right ankle. He feels unwell and has a temperature of 38.2°C. Examination reveals a tense effusion and exquisite pain on any passive movement of the ankle joint. The problem representation is an older patient with an acute monoarthritis and systemic signs of infection. While gout is a possibility, the overriding concern must be septic arthritis, a limb-threatening emergency. The immediate pathway involves urgent hospital admission, blood tests (FBC, CRP), and orthopaedic consultation for joint aspiration.

Case 3: A Painless Ulcer in the GP Clinic

A 65-year-old man with a 15-year history of type 2 diabetes is seen for a routine review. When asked to remove his shoes and socks, a 2cm ulcer is noted under the head of the first metatarsal on his right foot. The patient was unaware of its presence as he has no pain. The problem representation is a patient with a major risk factor (diabetes) presenting with a painless neuropathic ulcer. This is a high-risk diabetic foot problem. Management involves assessing for signs of infection, assessing vascular supply (palpating foot pulses), and making an urgent referral to the local multidisciplinary diabetic foot service for specialist assessment and management.
