Cough Diagnostic Reasoning Reference
Context: Cough Assessment
Overview

Cough is one of the most frequent reasons for patients to seek medical advice in both primary and emergency care settings. It ranges from a trivial, self-limiting symptom of a viral upper respiratory tract infection (URTI) to the presenting feature of a life-threatening illness like lung cancer or pulmonary embolism. The clinical challenge lies in distinguishing the benign from the serious, using a structured and systematic approach.

This educational module is designed to provide a foundational framework for the clinical reasoning process when assessing a patient with a cough. For definitive, real-time management protocols, users must refer to official UK guidelines from authoritative bodies such as NICE (e.g., NG191 for respiratory tract infections, NG12 for suspected cancer) and the British Thoracic Society (BTS) guidelines on cough.

Pathophysiology/Basic Science

Understanding the mechanisms that trigger a cough is fundamental to forming a differential diagnosis. The cough reflex is a protective mechanism designed to clear the airways of secretions, irritants, and foreign bodies. It involves a complex reflex arc that can be triggered by various stimuli:

  • The Cough Reflex Arc: This consists of afferent, central, and efferent pathways. Afferent sensory nerves (primarily via the vagus nerve) in the pharynx, larynx, and airways detect triggers. These signals are processed in the brainstem's cough centre, which then coordinates a response via efferent pathways to the diaphragm, abdominal wall, and laryngeal muscles, resulting in the forceful expulsion of air.
  • Inflammatory Triggers: Inflammation from infections (e.g., viruses, bacteria) or allergic conditions (e.g., asthma) releases mediators like bradykinin and prostaglandins. These substances sensitise the afferent nerve endings in the airways, lowering the threshold for the cough reflex to be triggered. This is the primary mechanism in conditions like bronchitis and pneumonia.
  • Mechanical Triggers: Direct physical stimulation of cough receptors can occur. This can be caused by inhaled foreign bodies, excessive mucus (as in bronchiectasis), or external compression of the airways from a tumour or enlarged lymph nodes.
  • Chemical Triggers: Inhaled irritants like cigarette smoke or environmental pollutants can directly activate cough receptors. Internally, acid from gastro-oesophageal reflux disease (GORD) can reflux into the oesophagus and even the larynx, causing chemical irritation and triggering a chronic cough. Certain medications, most notably ACE inhibitors, can cause a cough by increasing levels of bradykinin.
  • Cough Receptor Hypersensitivity: In many patients with chronic cough, the underlying issue is a sensitised reflex. Following an initial insult (like an infection), the cough receptors remain "hyper-responsive," leading to a persistent cough triggered by stimuli that would not affect a healthy individual, such as changes in temperature or talking. This is a key concept in understanding chronic refractory cough.
Differential Diagnosis (DDx) Framework

A structured approach to the differential diagnosis of cough is essential to ensure life-threatening conditions are prioritised.

Must not miss!
  • Tuberculosis: Often presents with a persistent productive cough, haemoptysis, night sweats, and weight loss, particularly in at-risk populations.
  • Pneumonia: Typically causes an acute cough with purulent sputum, fever, and pleuritic chest pain, with focal signs on examination.
  • HIV (pneumocystis): Presents with a persistent dry cough and progressive breathlessness on exertion in an immunocompromised individual.
  • Left ventricular failure: Can cause a chronic cough, often worse when lying flat (orthopnoea), sometimes with frothy pink sputum.
  • Lung cancer: May present with a new cough lasting over three weeks, haemoptysis, or a change in a chronic cough in a smoker.
Common causes
  • URTI (Common cold): A self-limiting acute dry or productive cough associated with nasal congestion, sore throat, and malaise.
  • Allergic rhinitis: Causes a persistent cough due to post-nasal drip, often accompanied by sneezing, nasal itching, and watery eyes.
  • Non-allergic rhinitis: Similar to allergic rhinitis with post-nasal drip causing cough, but without evidence of allergy.
  • LRTI: Acute lower respiratory tract infection (bronchitis) presents with a prominent cough that can be productive, often following a URTI.
  • Bronchitis: Inflammation of the bronchi typically causing an acute, productive cough, usually viral in origin.
  • Influenza: Characterised by the sudden onset of fever, myalgia, headache, and a prominent dry cough.
  • Asthma: Often presents with a chronic dry cough, particularly at night, with triggers like cold air or exercise, and associated wheeze.
  • COPD: A chronic productive cough is a hallmark feature in patients with a significant smoking history.
  • GORD: Gastro-oesophageal reflux disease can cause a chronic dry cough due to acid irritating the upper airway.
  • Whooping cough: Caused by Bordetella pertussis, presenting with severe bouts of coughing (paroxysms) followed by an inspiratory 'whoop'.
  • COVID-19: Commonly presents with a new, continuous cough, often accompanied by fever, fatigue, and loss of taste or smell.
Easily missed
  • ACE-I induced cough: A persistent, dry, and often tickly cough developing weeks to months after starting an ACE inhibitor medication.
  • Interstitial Lung Disease: Presents with a persistent dry cough and progressive exertional breathlessness, with fine inspiratory crackles on auscultation.
  • Sarcoidosis: An inflammatory condition that can cause a persistent dry cough, breathlessness, and systemic symptoms like fatigue.
  • Bronchiectasis: Characterised by a chronic daily productive cough with large volumes of purulent sputum due to permanent airway damage.
  • Psychogenic: A habitual cough that is typically absent during sleep and may be a diagnosis of exclusion after a thorough workup.
Other Differential Diagnoses to consider
  • COVID-19: Can present with acute or subacute cough, often accompanied by fever and systemic symptoms.
  • Post-viral cough: Persistent cough following resolution of an acute viral illness.
  • Occupational lung disease: Chronic exposure to irritants such as silica, asbestos, or other environmental pollutants.
  • Cystic fibrosis: Often presents with chronic cough and sputum production, especially in younger patients.
  • Chronic aspiration: Recurrent micro-aspiration, commonly in patients with swallowing disorders.
  • Foreign body aspiration: Particularly in children or the elderly, associated with a sudden onset of symptoms.
  • Mediastinal masses: Can cause cough due to compression of airways or recurrent laryngeal nerve involvement.
  • Vocal cord dysfunction: Paradoxical vocal cord movement leading to symptoms resembling asthma or cough.
  • Anxiety-related hyperventilation: Cough associated with psychogenic causes and hyperventilation episodes.
History Taking
**Patient’s Description**: How can I help you today?
Starting with an open-ended question such as "Can you tell me more about what brought you here today?" is vital. Relevance: Allows the patient to provide their narrative, which may include critical details about the cough’s characteristics, associated symptoms, and impact on their life, guiding more focused questioning.
When did the cough start?
  • Helps determine acute (<3 weeks), subacute (3-8 weeks), or chronic (>8 weeks) cough, narrowing the differential diagnosis.
  • Relevance: Acute onset may suggest infections like URTI, pneumonia, or COVID-19. Chronic cough raises suspicion for conditions like asthma, GORD, or bronchiectasis.
Do you produce any sputum? If so, what does it look like?
  • Explores whether the cough is productive (sputum-producing) or dry.
  • Relevance: Green/yellow sputum may indicate bacterial infection (e.g., pneumonia, bronchitis), while blood-tinged sputum raises concern for tuberculosis, lung cancer, or bronchiectasis. A dry cough may suggest viral illness, asthma, or ACE inhibitor use.
Are there any triggers or factors that make the cough worse or better?
  • Identifies environmental or situational factors influencing the cough.
  • Relevance: Worsening with exercise or cold air may suggest asthma, while lying flat could indicate GORD or heart failure.
Have you experienced any associated symptoms?
  • Associated symptoms like fever, chest pain, breathlessness, weight loss, or night sweats provide diagnostic clues.
  • Relevance: Fever may suggest infection (e.g., pneumonia, COVID-19); chest pain could point to pleurisy or GORD; weight loss and night sweats are red flags for cancer or tuberculosis.
Do you have a history of allergies, asthma, or reflux?
  • Identifies predisposing conditions for chronic cough.
  • Relevance: Allergies may point to allergic rhinitis; asthma is a common cause of chronic cough, and reflux (GORD) can trigger cough via oesophageal irritation.
Have you recently started or changed any medications?
  • Drug history is critical in evaluating for medication-related causes.
  • Relevance: ACE inhibitors (e.g., ramipril) are a well-known cause of dry cough, which resolves on discontinuation of the drug.
Have you travelled recently or had contact with anyone unwell?
  • Explores risk factors for infectious causes.
  • Relevance: Travel may raise suspicion for tuberculosis or COVID-19, while contact with sick individuals increases the likelihood of viral or bacterial infections.
Social History: Do you smoke or drink alcohol, and what do you do for work?
  • Smoking is the single most important risk factor for chronic bronchitis, COPD, and lung cancer. Alcohol can worsen GORD.
  • Relevance: A detailed smoking history is crucial. Occupational history might reveal exposure to dusts (e.g., asbestos, silica) or allergens that cause lung disease.
**Ideas, Concerns, and Expectations (ICE)**
  • Ask: "What do you think might be causing this?" (Ideas), "Is there something specific you are worried about?" (Concerns), and "What were you hoping we might do today?" (Expectations).
  • Relevance: Addresses the patient’s perspective and specific worries, such as fear of cancer or COVID-19, and aligns management with their expectations, ensuring a patient-centred approach.
Examination Strategy
General appearance and vital signs
  • Assess for signs of respiratory distress (e.g., use of accessory muscles, cyanosis) and measure vital signs such as respiratory rate, oxygen saturation, heart rate, blood pressure, and temperature.
  • Relevance: Tachypnoea and hypoxia suggest pneumonia, COVID-19, or severe asthma/COPD exacerbation. Fever supports infection (e.g., LRTI, pneumonia). Cyanosis may indicate severe hypoxaemia due to heart failure, pulmonary embolism, or pneumonia.
Inspection of the chest
  • Observe for chest wall deformities, asymmetry, or scars from prior surgeries.
  • Relevance: Asymmetry suggests structural lung disease (e.g., pneumothorax, pleural effusion). Deformities or previous surgery may hint at bronchiectasis or other chronic lung conditions.
Palpation of the chest
  • Assess chest expansion and tactile vocal fremitus.
  • Relevance: Reduced chest expansion or fremitus may indicate consolidation (e.g., pneumonia), pleural effusion, or pneumothorax. Normal findings suggest less likelihood of structural abnormalities.
Percussion of the chest
  • Evaluate for hyperresonance, dullness, or normal resonance over lung fields.
  • Relevance: Dullness suggests consolidation or pleural effusion. Hyperresonance points to pneumothorax. Normal percussion rules out major structural abnormalities.
Auscultation of the chest
  • Listen for breath sounds (normal, reduced, or absent), added sounds (e.g., wheezing, crackles), and vocal resonance.
  • Relevance: Crackles suggest pneumonia, pulmonary oedema, or bronchiectasis. Wheezing is seen in asthma, COPD, or an obstructive lesion. Reduced breath sounds may indicate effusion or pneumothorax.
Examination of the throat and oral cavity
  • Inspect for signs of pharyngitis, tonsillitis, or postnasal drip.
  • Relevance: Redness or swelling suggests URTI or postnasal drip. Normal findings reduce the likelihood of upper airway pathology.
Cardiovascular examination
  • Assess for signs of heart failure, including jugular venous pressure, peripheral oedema, and heart sounds.
  • Relevance: Raised JVP, S3 gallop, and peripheral oedema suggest left ventricular failure. Normal findings make heart failure less likely.
Examination for lymphadenopathy
  • Palpate cervical, supraclavicular, and axillary lymph nodes.
  • Relevance: Enlarged supraclavicular nodes may suggest malignancy (e.g., lung cancer, lymphoma). Normal findings reduce the likelihood of metastatic disease.
Examination of extremities
  • Check for signs of clubbing, cyanosis, or peripheral oedema.
  • Relevance: Clubbing suggests chronic lung disease (e.g., bronchiectasis, lung cancer). Peripheral cyanosis supports hypoxaemia, while oedema may indicate heart failure.
Red Flag Synthesis
Haemoptysis (coughing up blood)
  • Haemoptysis is a concerning symptom that may indicate significant pathology, such as lung cancer, tuberculosis, or pulmonary embolism.
  • Significance: Requires urgent evaluation with chest imaging and further investigations to exclude life-threatening causes.
Unexplained weight loss or night sweats
  • These symptoms may suggest systemic diseases such as malignancy (e.g., lung cancer) or infections like tuberculosis.
  • Significance: Indicates the need for a thorough systemic workup to rule out serious underlying conditions.
Persistent cough >8 weeks
  • Chronic cough, particularly when unexplained, can be an indicator of conditions such as lung cancer, interstitial lung disease, or bronchiectasis.
  • Significance: Should prompt detailed history, examination, and imaging to investigate for chronic or serious causes.
Dyspnoea (difficulty breathing) at rest
  • Resting dyspnoea can indicate severe pulmonary or cardiac conditions, such as pneumonia, heart failure, pulmonary embolism, or severe asthma exacerbation.
  • Significance: Requires immediate evaluation and potentially life-saving interventions.
Chest pain
  • Chest pain associated with cough could signify pleuritis, pulmonary embolism, or pneumonia.
  • Significance: May indicate serious thoracic pathology requiring prompt investigation.
Stridor or wheezing with respiratory distress
  • Stridor or significant wheezing, particularly with respiratory distress, suggests upper airway obstruction or severe lower airway conditions such as asthma or anaphylaxis.
  • Significance: This is a medical emergency requiring immediate airway assessment and intervention.
Cervical or supraclavicular lymphadenopathy
  • Enlarged lymph nodes in these regions may indicate malignancy, such as lung cancer or lymphoma, or infections like tuberculosis.
  • Significance: Warrants further diagnostic evaluation, including imaging and biopsy if necessary.
Investigations Strategy & Interpretation
Full blood count (FBC)
  • Assesses for infection or other systemic illnesses by evaluating white cell count, haemoglobin, and platelets.
  • Result Significance: Elevated white cell count suggests infection (e.g., pneumonia). Anaemia may indicate chronic disease or malignancy. Normal findings make systemic conditions less likely.
C-reactive protein (CRP)
  • CRP is a marker of inflammation and is useful in assessing the severity of infection or inflammation.
  • Result Significance: Elevated CRP supports bacterial infections (e.g., pneumonia). A normal CRP may rule out significant infection or inflammation.
Chest X-ray
  • Essential imaging to evaluate for structural abnormalities, infection, or malignancy in the chest.
  • Result Significance: Consolidation indicates pneumonia; cavitation may suggest tuberculosis. A mass could indicate lung cancer. A normal X-ray reduces the likelihood of serious pulmonary pathology.
Sputum culture and sensitivity
  • Helps identify bacterial pathogens in patients with productive cough and guides antibiotic therapy.
  • Result Significance: Positive culture indicates bacterial infection (e.g., pneumonia). Negative findings may suggest non-infectious causes.
Spirometry
  • Assesses lung function and identifies obstructive or restrictive airway diseases.
  • Result Significance: Obstructive patterns suggest asthma or COPD. Restrictive patterns may indicate interstitial lung disease. Normal spirometry helps rule out these conditions.
Mantoux test or interferon-gamma release assay (IGRA)
  • Used to screen for latent or active tuberculosis, particularly in high-risk patients.
  • Result Significance: Positive results indicate TB exposure or active disease. Negative results reduce the likelihood of TB but do not completely exclude it.
CT Chest
  • Provides detailed imaging of the lungs and mediastinum, useful in diagnosing malignancy, interstitial lung disease, or complex infections.
  • Result Significance: Identifies lung masses, fibrosis, bronchiectasis, or other abnormalities that may not be visible on X-ray. Normal findings make these conditions unlikely.
Bronchoscopy
  • Allows direct visualisation of the airways and collection of tissue or fluid samples for diagnosis.
  • Result Significance: Identifies malignancy, foreign bodies, or infections (e.g., fungal or tuberculosis). Normal findings help exclude intraluminal pathology.
pH monitoring or oesophageal manometry
  • Assesses for gastro-oesophageal reflux as a cause of chronic cough.
  • Result Significance: Abnormal results confirm GORD. Normal findings suggest that GORD is not contributing to the cough.
Evidence-Based Diagnosis (EBDx)
Fever in bacterial infections
  • Fever is a common symptom of bacterial infections, such as pneumonia, but can also occur in viral conditions like influenza or COVID-19.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 85%, Specificity: 77% for bacterial pneumonia (source: BMJ Best Practice).
  • Reference: BMJ Best Practice: Diagnosis and Management of Community-Acquired Pneumonia.
  • Significance: Presence of fever increases the likelihood of pneumonia or other infections, but absence does not fully rule out these conditions.
Haemoptysis in lung cancer
  • Haemoptysis is a red flag symptom of lung cancer but is not specific and may also occur in infections like tuberculosis or bronchiectasis.
  • Evidence: Positive Likelihood Ratio (LR+): 7.5 for lung cancer in patients with haemoptysis (source: British Thoracic Society guidelines).
  • Reference: British Thoracic Society Guideline for Investigation and Management of Haemoptysis (2022).
  • Significance: Strongly suggests the need for imaging to exclude malignancy, especially in high-risk patients (e.g., smokers).
Wheezing in asthma
  • Wheezing is a hallmark of asthma but can also occur in COPD or airway obstruction.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 80%, Specificity: 85% for asthma when combined with episodic cough (source: GINA guidelines).
  • Reference: Global Initiative for Asthma (GINA) 2023 guidelines.
  • Significance: Episodic wheezing significantly increases the likelihood of asthma, particularly when associated with a reversible obstructive spirometry pattern.
CRP in differentiating bacterial from viral infections
  • CRP is commonly used to distinguish between bacterial and viral infections in patients with acute cough.
  • Evidence: CRP >100 mg/L has a sensitivity of 70% and specificity of 90% for bacterial pneumonia (source: NICE guidelines).
  • Reference: NICE Guideline NG191: Diagnosis and Management of Respiratory Tract Infections (2022).
  • Significance: Elevated CRP strongly suggests bacterial infection, supporting the decision to prescribe antibiotics.
Chest X-ray in pneumonia
  • Chest X-ray is the gold standard for diagnosing pneumonia, revealing consolidation or infiltrates.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 85%, Specificity: 90% for detecting pneumonia (source: Radiology Evidence-Based Practice).
  • Reference: Radiology Evidence-Based Practice: Pneumonia Imaging Guidelines (2021).
  • Significance: A positive finding confirms pneumonia, while a normal X-ray reduces the likelihood but does not completely rule it out, particularly in early stages.
Spirometry in asthma and COPD
  • Spirometry helps distinguish obstructive airway diseases like asthma and COPD from other causes of chronic cough.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 88%, Specificity: 92% for diagnosing COPD with FEV1/FVC <0.7 (source: GOLD guidelines).
  • Reference: Global Initiative for Chronic Obstructive Lung Disease (GOLD) 2023 guidelines.
  • Significance: An obstructive pattern with reversibility suggests asthma, while fixed obstruction supports COPD.
Diagnostic Timeout & Cognitive Biases

Awareness of common thinking errors is essential to improve diagnostic accuracy and patient safety when assessing a patient with a cough. Before finalising a diagnosis, it is good practice for clinicians at all levels to take a 'diagnostic timeout' to challenge their own reasoning.

  • Anchoring Bias: The tendency to prematurely label a cough based on the most obvious feature. For example, attributing a persistent cough in a lifelong smoker to a "smoker's cough" without considering red flags for lung cancer, such as weight loss or a change in the cough's character. A key question to ask is: "Could this be something other than their usual COPD?"
  • Confirmation Bias: Seeking out or giving more weight to evidence that supports an initial hypothesis while ignoring contradictory data. For example, focusing on a history of heartburn to confirm GORD as the cause of a chronic cough, while downplaying the significance of exertional breathlessness which might point to interstitial lung disease.
  • Availability Heuristic: Overestimating the likelihood of diagnoses that are more recent or memorable. During flu season, a clinician might attribute every cough to influenza, potentially missing a bacterial superinfection or another serious diagnosis like pulmonary embolism presenting with a cough.
  • Premature Closure: Accepting a diagnosis before it is fully verified. For instance, diagnosing an acute URTI and failing to arrange follow-up. The patient may return weeks later with a persistent cough that has now evolved to reveal a more serious underlying condition that could have been investigated earlier. It's crucial to ask: "What's the worst-case scenario, and have I safely excluded it?"
Illustrative Management Pathways

For educational purposes, this section describes the typical principles of management for key diagnoses. These are not prescriptive instructions. All management must be guided by official UK guidelines, local formularies, and senior clinical advice.

Acute Bronchitis / URTI

For most patients with an acute cough due to a viral infection, management is supportive. This includes advising rest, ensuring adequate fluid intake, and using simple analgesia like paracetamol for associated symptoms. The evidence for over-the-counter cough medicines is weak. Crucially, this involves providing clear safety netting advice on when to seek further help (e.g., if symptoms worsen, breathlessness develops, or the cough persists beyond 3-4 weeks) and explaining why antibiotics are not indicated, in line with antibiotic stewardship principles.

Community-Acquired Pneumonia (CAP)

The management of suspected CAP depends on severity assessment, often using the CRB-65 score in primary care. A score of 0 suggests low severity, and treatment with oral antibiotics (e.g., amoxicillin, or a macrolide if penicillin-allergic) at home is appropriate. A score of 1-2 indicates intermediate risk, requiring hospital assessment. A score of 3-4 indicates high risk and necessitates urgent hospital admission. Management in hospital involves prompt antibiotic administration, oxygen therapy if hypoxic, and supportive care.

Chronic Cough (Initial Approach)

In patients with a chronic cough (>8 weeks) and no red flags, the initial approach involves a chest X-ray to exclude sinister pathology. If the X-ray is normal, the next step is to systematically address the common "triple therapy" causes: asthma/eosinophilic bronchitis (with a trial of inhaled corticosteroids), GORD (with a trial of a proton pump inhibitor), and upper airway cough syndrome/post-nasal drip (with a trial of an intranasal steroid). If an ACE inhibitor is the suspected cause, it should be stopped and replaced with an alternative.

Reflection & Learning

These brief cases illustrate the application of clinical reasoning to different cough presentations. They are for educational purposes only.

Case 1: The Elderly Smoker

A 68-year-old male with a 40-pack-year smoking history presents to his GP with a cough that has changed in character over the past 6 weeks. It is now more persistent and associated with blood-streaked sputum on one occasion and a 4kg weight loss. The problem representation is an elderly man with a major risk factor (smoking) and red flag symptoms (haemoptysis, weight loss, persistent cough >3 weeks), highly suspicious for lung cancer. The immediate pathway involves an urgent two-week-wait referral for a chest X-ray and specialist assessment, as per NICE guidelines.

Case 2: The Young Adult with a Night Cough

A 22-year-old university student presents with a dry cough that has persisted for three months. It is noticeably worse at night, sometimes waking her from sleep, and is also triggered by running for the bus. She has a history of childhood eczema. Auscultation is clear. The problem representation is a young adult with a history of atopy presenting with a chronic cough with features suggestive of bronchial hyper-responsiveness (nocturnal worsening, exercise-induced), making cough-variant asthma a leading diagnosis. The management plan would involve spirometry with reversibility testing or a trial of an inhaled corticosteroid.

Case 3: The Patient on New Medication

A 55-year-old woman presents with a frustratingly persistent, dry, tickly cough that started about two months ago. She feels otherwise well, with no other respiratory or systemic symptoms. Her medical history is notable for hypertension, for which she was started on Ramipril three months ago. Examination and a chest X-ray are normal. The problem representation is a middle-aged woman who has developed a characteristic dry cough shortly after starting an ACE inhibitor. This is a classic "easily missed" diagnosis. The management involves substituting the ACE inhibitor with an alternative antihypertensive (like an Angiotensin II Receptor Blocker) and advising the patient that the cough should resolve within 1-4 weeks.
