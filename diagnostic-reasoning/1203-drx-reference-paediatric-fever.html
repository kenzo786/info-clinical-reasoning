Paediatric Fever Diagnostic Reasoning Reference
Context: Paediatric Fever Assessment
Overview

Paediatric fever is one of the most frequent reasons for children to present to healthcare services, accounting for a significant proportion of consultations in general practice and visits to the Emergency Department. While the majority of febrile episodes are due to benign, self-limiting viral infections, fever can also be the presenting sign of a life-threatening illness, such as sepsis, meningitis, or pneumonia. The core challenge for the clinician is to reliably distinguish the small number of children with serious bacterial infections (SBIs) from the large majority with non-serious illness, while avoiding unnecessary investigations and antibiotic use.

A structured, systematic approach is therefore crucial. This involves a thorough history and examination focused on identifying "red flag" features that indicate a higher risk of serious illness. This educational module is designed to provide a foundational framework for the clinical reasoning process in assessing a febrile child. For definitive, real-time management protocols, users must refer to official UK guidelines from authoritative bodies such as NICE (specifically NG143: Fever in under 5s) and local paediatric sepsis pathways.

Pathophysiology/Basic Science

Understanding the mechanisms underlying fever is key to interpreting clinical signs. Fever is not a disease itself, but a regulated rise in the body's core temperature, controlled by the hypothalamus. It is a fundamental part of the host's acute phase response to a perceived threat.

  • Fever Generation: The process begins with the introduction of pyrogens (fever-inducing substances). Exogenous pyrogens, such as bacterial components (e.g., lipopolysaccharide) or viruses, stimulate host immune cells (like macrophages) to release endogenous pyrogens. These are pro-inflammatory cytokines, including Interleukin-1 (IL-1), Interleukin-6 (IL-6), and Tumour Necrosis Factor-alpha (TNF-α).
  • Hypothalamic Response: These cytokines travel to the brain and stimulate the production of Prostaglandin E2 (PGE2) in the hypothalamus. PGE2 acts on the thermoregulatory centre, effectively 'resetting' the body's thermostat to a higher temperature. The body then initiates mechanisms like shivering (to generate heat) and vasoconstriction (to conserve heat) to raise the core temperature to this new set point.
  • Systemic Inflammatory Conditions: In non-infectious inflammatory diseases such as Kawasaki disease or Juvenile Idiopathic Arthritis (JIA), the inflammatory process is driven by the body's own immune system (auto-inflammation or autoimmunity). This process also generates the same endogenous cytokines (IL-1, IL-6), leading to fever in the absence of any infection.
  • Malignancy: In conditions like leukaemia and lymphoma, fever can be caused either by the cancer cells themselves producing pyrogenic cytokines or by the body's inflammatory response to the malignancy. Fever can also result from secondary infections due to a compromised immune system.
Differential Diagnosis (DDx) Framework

A structured approach to the differential diagnosis of paediatric fever is essential to ensure life-threatening conditions are prioritised.

Must not miss!
  • Sepsis: A life-threatening systemic illness with signs of organ dysfunction (e.g., persistent tachycardia, altered mental state, poor perfusion).
  • Meningitis: Central nervous system infection presenting with fever, headache, neck stiffness, or non-specific signs in infants like irritability and poor feeding.
  • UTI: Infection of the urinary system, often causing fever with non-specific symptoms such as vomiting or lethargy in young children.
  • Pneumonia: Infection of the lung parenchyma, typically presenting with fever, cough, and increased work of breathing.
  • Septic arthritis: Bacterial infection within a joint, presenting with fever, severe joint pain, swelling, and refusal to move the affected limb.
  • Intra-abdominal abscess: A collection of pus within the abdomen, which can present with persistent fever, abdominal pain, and systemic upset.
  • Tuberculosis: A chronic bacterial infection that can present with prolonged fever, weight loss, night sweats, and cough.
  • Lymphoma: A malignancy of the lymphatic system that may present with persistent fever, night sweats, weight loss, and lymphadenopathy.
  • Leukaemia: Cancer of the blood-forming tissues, which can present with fever, pallor, bruising, bone pain, and hepatosplenomegaly.
  • Juvenile idiopathic arthritis: An autoimmune condition causing joint inflammation, which can present with systemic features including high-spiking fevers and rash.
  • Crohn's disease: A type of inflammatory bowel disease that can present with fever, abdominal pain, diarrhoea, and weight loss.
  • Ulcerative colitis: An inflammatory bowel disease that may present with fever and bloody diarrhoea, particularly during acute flare-ups.
  • Appendicitis: Inflammation of the appendix, typically presenting with fever, migrating abdominal pain, vomiting, and loss of appetite.
Common causes
  • URTI (Common cold): A self-limiting viral infection of the upper respiratory tract causing coryza, cough, and low-grade fever.
  • Rhinosinusitis: Inflammation of the nasal passages and sinuses, presenting with facial pain, purulent nasal discharge, and fever.
  • Stomatitis: Inflammation and sores inside the mouth, often viral (e.g., herpangina, hand-foot-and-mouth), causing fever and poor feeding.
  • Pharyngitis: Inflammation of the pharynx, commonly viral, causing a sore throat and fever.
  • Tonsillitis: Inflammation of the tonsils, which can be viral or bacterial (Strep A), presenting with fever, sore throat, and swollen tonsils.
  • Laryngitis: Inflammation of the larynx, typically causing a hoarse voice, barking cough, and fever.
  • Croup: A viral infection of the upper airway causing a characteristic barking cough, stridor, and fever.
  • Otitis media: Infection of the middle ear, very common in young children, presenting with fever and ear pain or tugging at the ear.
  • Influenza: A viral respiratory illness characterised by a sudden onset of high fever, muscle aches, headache, and cough.
  • LRTI: Lower respiratory tract infection is a broad term that includes bronchitis and pneumonia.
  • Bronchitis: Inflammation of the large airways, typically viral, causing a productive cough that can be associated with fever.
  • Bronchiolitis: A common viral infection in infants affecting the small airways, causing cough, wheeze, increased work of breathing, and fever.
  • Gastroenteritis: Viral or bacterial infection of the gut causing vomiting and diarrhoea, often accompanied by fever and abdominal cramps.
  • Post vaccination reactions: A common and expected reaction to childhood immunisations, typically causing a mild, short-lived fever within 48 hours.
  • COVID-19: A viral infection that can present with a wide range of symptoms, including fever, cough, fatigue, and loss of taste or smell.
Easily missed
  • Glandular fever: An infection caused by the Epstein-Barr virus (EBV), typically presenting with prolonged fever, severe sore throat, profound fatigue, and lymphadenopathy.
  • Cat scratch disease: A bacterial infection (Bartonella henselae) that typically causes a prolonged low-grade fever and localised, tender lymphadenopathy following a cat scratch.
  • Tropical diseases: Infections such as malaria, dengue fever, or typhoid should be considered in any febrile child with a history of recent travel to an endemic area.
Other Differential Diagnoses to consider
  • Kawasaki disease: A vasculitis that often presents with prolonged fever and rash, particularly in young children.
  • Roseola infantum: A viral infection characterised by a high fever followed by a rash as the fever resolves.
  • Malaria: A possible cause if there is recent travel to endemic areas, presenting with cyclical fever patterns.
  • Rheumatic fever: Typically follows a streptococcal infection, presenting with fever, joint pain, and possibly carditis.
  • Henoch-Schönlein purpura: A small vessel vasculitis that may present with fever, abdominal pain, and purpuric rash.
  • Autoimmune diseases (e.g., systemic lupus erythematosus): Can present with fever, especially in the presence of other systemic symptoms.
  • Endocarditis: Although rare, it may present in certain high-risk populations with fever and new murmur.
History Taking
Patient’s Description: How can I help you today?
Starting with an open-ended question like “Can you tell me more about what brought you here today?” is crucial. Relevance: Allows the patient or their parent to provide an unfiltered account of symptoms, which guides more targeted questioning based on their perspective and priorities.
When did the fever start, and how has it progressed?
  • This helps establish the onset, duration, and trajectory of the fever (acute, subacute, or chronic).
  • Relevance: A sudden onset with high fever suggests infections like URTI, tonsillitis, or meningitis, while a prolonged fever might suggest TB, lymphoma, or autoimmune conditions.
Have you noticed any pattern to the fever?
  • Ask about intermittent, remittent, or cyclic patterns (e.g., malarial fever has a periodic pattern).
  • Relevance: Cyclic fever suggests malaria or rheumatic fever, whereas sustained fever may point to sepsis or meningitis.
Are there any associated symptoms?
  • Examples include cough, difficulty breathing, rash, vomiting, or joint pain.
  • Relevance: Cough or breathing difficulty may suggest LRTI or croup; rash might suggest Kawasaki disease or viral exanthems; vomiting could point to meningitis or gastroenteritis.
Have you noticed any rashes?
  • Determine location, timing, and appearance of the rash.
  • Relevance: A rash that develops with fever may indicate roseola, meningococcal sepsis, or Kawasaki disease.
Does the child have any difficulty drinking or feeding?
  • Poor oral intake can suggest dehydration or a serious systemic illness.
  • Relevance: Reduced intake is an early sign of sepsis or significant infections like UTI or pneumonia.
Is there any history of recent travel or exposure to sick contacts?
  • Travel to malaria-endemic areas or exposure to individuals with infections may guide diagnostic considerations.
  • Relevance: Travel history raises suspicion for tropical diseases like malaria, while sick contacts suggest viral infections or TB.
Does the child have any underlying medical conditions?
  • Consider immunodeficiencies, autoimmune conditions, or a history of frequent infections.
  • Relevance: Chronic conditions may predispose to recurrent infections or atypical presentations of common illnesses.
Ideas, Concerns, and Expectations (ICE)
  • Ideas: “What do you think might be causing this?” Helps identify patient/parent perspective and potential causes they suspect.
  • Concerns: “Is there something specific you are worried about?” Helps uncover fears, e.g., meningitis or sepsis.
  • Expectations: “What were you hoping we might be able to do for you today?” Ensures the consultation aligns with patient or parent priorities, such as reassurance or specific tests.
  • Relevance: Helps ensure a patient-centred approach, addressing both medical and psychological aspects of care.
Examination Strategy
General appearance and vital signs
  • Assess the child’s overall appearance, activity level, and distress. Check vital signs including temperature, heart rate, respiratory rate, oxygen saturation, and blood pressure.
  • Relevance: A toxic appearance (lethargy, pallor, poor response) raises concern for sepsis or meningitis. Fever above 39°C is common in bacterial infections, while tachypnoea may suggest pneumonia or bronchiolitis. Hypotension or prolonged capillary refill indicates possible sepsis.
Inspection of the throat
  • Look for erythema, exudates, ulcers, or swelling in the pharynx and tonsils.
  • Relevance: Tonsillar exudates with erythema suggest streptococcal pharyngitis, while ulcers may indicate herpangina or stomatitis. A normal throat reduces the likelihood of pharyngitis as the cause.
Examination of the ears
  • Inspect the tympanic membrane for redness, bulging, or perforation and check the external ear for discharge or swelling.
  • Relevance: Redness or bulging of the tympanic membrane suggests acute otitis media. A normal ear exam helps rule out ear infections as a source of fever.
Respiratory examination
  • Assess work of breathing, auscultate for crackles, wheezes, or reduced breath sounds.
  • Relevance: Tachypnoea, intercostal recession, and crackles suggest pneumonia or bronchiolitis. Clear breath sounds with no distress reduce the likelihood of a respiratory cause.
Cardiovascular examination
  • Check for murmurs, capillary refill, and peripheral pulses.
  • Relevance: New murmurs may suggest infective endocarditis. Prolonged capillary refill and weak pulses indicate possible septic shock.
Abdominal examination
  • Palpate for tenderness, rebound, guarding, and organomegaly.
  • Relevance: Tenderness in the right lower quadrant suggests appendicitis, while hepatosplenomegaly may indicate infections such as glandular fever or leukaemia.
Skin examination
  • Inspect for rashes, petechiae, or purpura.
  • Relevance: Petechiae or purpura raise concerns for meningococcal sepsis. A blanching rash might suggest viral exanthems like roseola or measles.
Neurological examination
  • Check for neck stiffness, irritability, or altered consciousness. Perform Kernig’s and Brudzinski’s signs if meningitis is suspected.
  • Relevance: Neck stiffness, photophobia, or altered consciousness strongly suggest meningitis or encephalitis. A normal neurological exam reduces the likelihood of central nervous system involvement.
Red Flag Synthesis
Prolonged fever (>5 days)
  • A fever lasting more than 5 days is concerning for conditions like Kawasaki disease, tuberculosis, or malignancies such as lymphoma or leukaemia.
  • Significance: This could indicate a systemic inflammatory or malignant process requiring further investigation and urgent specialist input.
Severe lethargy or unresponsiveness
  • Severe lethargy, reduced interaction, or unresponsiveness can indicate sepsis, meningitis, or encephalitis.
  • Significance: These are signs of potentially life-threatening central nervous system or systemic infections requiring immediate intervention.
Petechial or purpuric rash
  • Non-blanching rashes, particularly petechiae or purpura, are concerning for meningococcal septicaemia.
  • Significance: This could indicate a severe bacterial infection requiring urgent antibiotic treatment.
Bulging fontanelle or neck stiffness in infants
  • Bulging fontanelle or neck stiffness may indicate raised intracranial pressure due to meningitis or encephalitis.
  • Significance: These are signs of serious central nervous system involvement, requiring urgent neuroimaging and lumbar puncture.
Signs of respiratory distress
  • Tachypnoea, nasal flaring, intercostal or subcostal retractions, and grunting are red flags for severe respiratory infections such as pneumonia or bronchiolitis.
  • Significance: These signs indicate a compromised airway or oxygenation, necessitating immediate stabilisation and treatment.
Cold extremities or prolonged capillary refill (>2 seconds)
  • These findings suggest hypoperfusion, a hallmark of sepsis or septic shock in children.
  • Significance: Hypoperfusion is life-threatening and requires urgent fluid resuscitation and critical care support.
Persistent vomiting and/or severe headache
  • These symptoms may indicate raised intracranial pressure, meningitis, or encephalitis.
  • Significance: These are critical signs that require urgent imaging and lumbar puncture to determine the underlying cause.
Hepatosplenomegaly
  • Enlarged liver or spleen can indicate malignancy (e.g., leukaemia), glandular fever, or systemic infections.
  • Significance: Hepatosplenomegaly is a red flag for systemic diseases requiring prompt diagnostic evaluation.
Investigations Strategy & Interpretation
Full Blood Count (FBC)
  • Provides information on white blood cell count (WBC), haemoglobin, and platelets, which can indicate infection, inflammation, or haematological malignancy.
  • Result Significance: Raised WBC with neutrophilia suggests bacterial infection; lymphocytosis may indicate viral infection. Pancytopenia raises concern for leukaemia.
C-reactive Protein (CRP) and Erythrocyte Sedimentation Rate (ESR)
  • Markers of inflammation that help distinguish between bacterial and viral infections.
  • Result Significance: Elevated CRP/ESR supports bacterial infection or inflammatory conditions like Kawasaki disease. Normal levels favour viral infections.
Blood culture
  • Essential for identifying bacteraemia in suspected sepsis or severe infections.
  • Result Significance: Positive results confirm bloodstream infection (e.g., meningococcaemia or other sepsis). Negative results do not fully exclude bacteraemia.
Urinalysis and urine culture
  • Key to identifying urinary tract infections (UTIs), particularly in infants and young children where symptoms may be non-specific.
  • Result Significance: Positive nitrites and leucocytes suggest UTI. Negative culture helps exclude bacterial UTI.
Chest X-ray
  • Indicated if respiratory symptoms like tachypnoea, cough, or hypoxia are present.
  • Result Significance: Positive findings of consolidation suggest pneumonia. Normal findings reduce the likelihood of lower respiratory tract infection.
Lumbar puncture (LP)
  • Used to evaluate cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) for meningitis or encephalitis in febrile children with neurological signs.
  • Result Significance: Elevated WBC and protein or low glucose in CSF suggest bacterial meningitis. Viral meningitis shows elevated lymphocytes with normal glucose.
Throat swab for culture
  • Indicated for suspected streptococcal pharyngitis or other throat infections.
  • Result Significance: Positive culture confirms streptococcal infection. Negative results may suggest viral aetiology.
Peripheral blood smear
  • Helpful in evaluating for haematological disorders like leukaemia or infections like malaria.
  • Result Significance: Presence of blasts suggests leukaemia, while malarial parasites confirm malaria. Normal smear helps exclude these conditions.
Viral PCR (e.g., for COVID-19, influenza)
  • Confirms viral infections that present with fever, such as COVID-19 or influenza.
  • Result Significance: Positive results confirm viral aetiology. Negative results necessitate exploring other infectious or non-infectious causes.
Evidence-Based Diagnosis (EBDx)
Fever in bacterial infections
  • Fever is a common presentation in bacterial infections such as pneumonia, meningitis, or sepsis. Persistent high-grade fever (>39°C) increases the likelihood of bacterial aetiology over viral causes.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 83%, Specificity: 77% for bacterial infections in febrile children with high fever (Source: NICE fever guidelines).
  • Reference: NICE Clinical Guideline NG143: Fever in under 5s: assessment and initial management.
  • Significance: Persistent high fever supports bacterial infection, while the absence of fever may reduce the likelihood of bacterial causes but not entirely exclude them.
Petechial or purpuric rash in meningococcal disease
  • A non-blanching rash is a critical finding in meningococcal sepsis, with a strong association with life-threatening bacterial infections.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 70%, Specificity: 92%, Positive Likelihood Ratio: 8.75 for meningococcaemia (Source: Thompson et al., Lancet, 2006).
  • Reference: Thompson MJ, Ninis N, Perera R, et al. Clinical recognition of meningococcal disease in children and adolescents. Lancet. 2006.
  • Significance: Presence of a petechial or purpuric rash strongly suggests meningococcal disease, necessitating urgent treatment, while its absence does not fully rule out the condition.
Neutrophilia on Full Blood Count (FBC)
  • Neutrophilia is indicative of bacterial infections such as pneumonia or sepsis, while lymphocytosis may favour viral infections.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 82%, Specificity: 75% for bacterial infection in febrile children (Source: National Paediatric Fever Guidelines).
  • Reference: NICE Clinical Guideline NG143: Fever in under 5s: assessment and initial management.
  • Significance: Elevated neutrophils strongly suggest bacterial infection. A normal FBC does not exclude significant infections but reduces their likelihood.
Lumbar puncture in suspected meningitis
  • Analysis of cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) is crucial in distinguishing bacterial meningitis from viral or other causes of fever with neurological signs.
  • Evidence: Elevated WBC and protein with low glucose in CSF has a sensitivity of 96% and specificity of 95% for bacterial meningitis (Source: IDSA Guidelines).
  • Reference: Tunkel AR, et al. Practice Guidelines for the Management of Bacterial Meningitis. Clinical Infectious Diseases. 2004.
  • Significance: Abnormal CSF findings confirm meningitis, and specific patterns help differentiate bacterial from viral causes.
CRP in distinguishing bacterial vs viral infections
  • CRP is an inflammatory marker that rises significantly in bacterial infections but may remain low in viral illnesses.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 89%, Specificity: 73% for bacterial infections with a CRP >20 mg/L (Source: van den Bruel et al., BMJ 2011).
  • Reference: van den Bruel A, Haj-Hassan T, Thompson M, et al. Diagnostic value of laboratory tests in identifying serious infections in febrile children: a systematic review. BMJ. 2011.
  • Significance: Elevated CRP supports bacterial infection but should be interpreted in context with other findings. A normal CRP reduces the likelihood but does not exclude bacterial infections.
Chest X-ray in suspected pneumonia
  • Chest X-ray identifies consolidation, a hallmark of bacterial pneumonia, which can be challenging to diagnose clinically in young children.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 85%, Specificity: 90% for bacterial pneumonia (Source: Harris et al., Paediatrics 2011).
  • Reference: Harris M, Clark J, Coote N, et al. British Thoracic Society guidelines for the management of community-acquired pneumonia in children. Thorax. 2011.
  • Significance: Radiographic findings of consolidation confirm pneumonia, while a normal chest X-ray reduces but does not exclude it, particularly in early disease stages.
Diagnostic Timeout & Cognitive Biases

Awareness of common thinking errors is essential to improve diagnostic accuracy and patient safety when assessing a febrile child. Before finalising a diagnosis, it is good practice for clinicians at all levels to take a 'diagnostic timeout' to challenge their own reasoning.

  • Anchoring Bias: The tendency to lock onto an initial, benign diagnosis (e.g., a simple URTI) too early, failing to reconsider more serious possibilities when new information or red flags emerge. For example, attributing a child's lethargy solely to the fever from a "cold" while missing early signs of sepsis. A key question to ask is: "What is the most dangerous thing this could be, and have I confidently ruled it out?"
  • Confirmation Bias: Actively seeking out or overvaluing evidence that supports an initial belief while downplaying contradictory findings. For instance, focusing on a clear chest examination to confirm a viral illness while ignoring a high heart rate and reduced fluid intake that might point to a UTI or dehydration.
  • Atypical Presentations: A major pitfall is forgetting that serious bacterial infections can present non-classically, especially in the very young. Infants under 3 months with sepsis or meningitis may present only with fever, poor feeding, or irritability, without any specific localising signs. A high index of suspicion must be maintained in this age group.
  • Premature Closure: Accepting a common diagnosis too quickly without considering the full differential, especially when a child is not improving as expected. For example, diagnosing tonsillitis in an adolescent but failing to consider a peritonsillar abscess (quinsy) when they present with a muffled voice and difficulty opening their mouth.
  • Over-reliance on Normal Tests: Placing too much weight on a single reassuring but non-definitive test result. A normal urinalysis dipstick does not definitively rule out a UTI (culture is required), and a normal CRP at the beginning of an illness does not exclude a developing serious bacterial infection.
Illustrative Management Pathways

For educational purposes, this section describes the typical principles of management for key diagnoses. These are not prescriptive instructions. All management must be guided by official UK guidelines (e.g., NICE NG143), local formularies, and senior clinical advice.

Simple Febrile Illness (e.g., Viral URTI)

Once serious causes have been confidently excluded through a thorough assessment, management focuses on parental education, reassurance, and symptomatic relief. Key advice includes encouraging fluid intake to prevent dehydration and using antipyretics like paracetamol or ibuprofen for the child's comfort (not just to lower the temperature). Crucially, clear "safety netting" advice must be provided, outlining specific red flag symptoms (e.g., worsening lethargy, non-blanching rash, difficulty breathing) that should trigger an urgent review.

Suspected Sepsis or Meningitis

This is a medical emergency requiring immediate hospitalisation. The initial management follows an ABCDE approach to stabilise the child. High-flow oxygen should be administered, and urgent intravenous access obtained for blood tests (including FBC, CRP, and blood culture) and fluid resuscitation. According to NICE guidelines, broad-spectrum intravenous antibiotics (e.g., ceftriaxone or cefotaxime) should be administered at the earliest opportunity, without delaying for investigations if the child is critically unwell. A lumbar puncture should be considered to analyse cerebrospinal fluid once the child is stable, if there are no contraindications.

Urinary Tract Infection (UTI)

Management depends on the child's age and clinical condition. Any child under 3 months with a suspected UTI requires admission for intravenous antibiotics pending culture results. For children over 3 months who are systemically well and can tolerate oral intake, management with oral antibiotics in the community may be appropriate. A clean urine sample for microscopy and culture is essential to confirm the diagnosis and guide antibiotic choice. Follow-up imaging may be required for younger children or those with recurrent UTIs, as per NICE guidelines.

Kawasaki Disease

A child with suspected Kawasaki disease requires urgent referral to and management by a paediatric specialist team. The primary goal of treatment is to reduce inflammation and prevent the development of coronary artery aneurysms, the most serious complication. The standard treatment is a single infusion of high-dose intravenous immunoglobulin (IVIG), alongside high-dose aspirin which is later reduced to a low, antiplatelet dose. An echocardiogram is essential at baseline and during follow-up to monitor the coronary arteries.

Reflection & Learning

These brief cases illustrate the application of clinical reasoning to different presentations of paediatric fever. They are for educational purposes only.

Case 1: Reassuring Viral Illness in General Practice

A 3-year-old girl presents to her GP with a 2-day history of a runny nose, cough, and a temperature of 38.2°C. Her mother reports she is still eating and drinking reasonably well and is playful between periods of sleep. Examination reveals a happy, alert child with a clear chest and a normal-looking throat and eardrums. The problem representation is a well, interactive child with symptoms and signs confined to the upper respiratory tract, highly consistent with a simple viral URTI. After excluding red flags, management involves reassurance, advice on symptomatic relief and hydration, and clear safety netting instructions.

Case 2: Suspected Sepsis in an Infant

A 7-week-old boy is brought to the Emergency Department with a 12-hour history of poor feeding and being 'not himself'. His temperature is 38.6°C. On examination, he is lethargic, has a heart rate of 190 bpm, a respiratory rate of 65, and his skin appears pale and mottled. The problem representation is a febrile infant under 3 months with tachycardia, tachypnoea, and altered appearance, meeting the criteria for high-risk of serious illness (NICE traffic light system). This presentation is highly concerning for sepsis. The immediate pathway involves an urgent call for senior help, activating the hospital's sepsis pathway, and commencing emergency treatment with oxygen, IV fluids, and antibiotics.

Case 3: Prolonged Fever in a Toddler

A 4-year-old boy presents with a 6-day history of a high, spiking fever (up to 40°C) that has not responded to a course of oral antibiotics for a presumed tonsillitis. Over the past 2 days, he has developed bright red eyes (without discharge), a widespread red rash, and dry, cracked lips. He is extremely irritable. The problem representation is a young child with a prolonged fever (>5 days) and characteristic mucocutaneous signs. This clinical picture is highly suspicious for Kawasaki disease. The correct action is an urgent referral to the on-call paediatric team for admission, investigation, and consideration of treatment with IVIG.
