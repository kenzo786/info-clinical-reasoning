Lower Abdominal Pain Diagnostic Reasoning Reference
Context: Lower Abdominal Pain Assessment
Overview

Lower abdominal pain is a frequent and diagnostically complex presentation in both primary and emergency care. The potential causes are extensive, ranging from benign and self-limiting conditions like gastroenteritis to acute, life-threatening surgical emergencies such as a ruptured ectopic pregnancy or appendicitis. The clinician's primary responsibility is to systematically differentiate between these possibilities, ensuring that critical diagnoses are identified promptly while avoiding excessive investigation for less serious issues.

A structured approach, grounded in a thorough understanding of anatomy and pathophysiology, is essential. The initial assessment must prioritise the identification of "red flag" symptoms and signs that signal immediate danger. This educational module provides a foundational framework for the clinical reasoning process involved in assessing a patient with lower abdominal pain. For definitive, real-time management protocols, clinicians must consult official UK guidelines from authoritative bodies such as NICE (e.g., NG128 for Diverticular Disease, CG151 for Irritable Bowel Syndrome in Adults) and the Royal College of Obstetricians and Gynaecologists (RCOG).

Pathophysiology/Basic Science

Understanding the origin and type of pain is fundamental to forming a differential diagnosis. Pain in the lower abdomen can be classified into three main types:

  • Visceral Pain: This originates from the abdominal organs (viscera), which are innervated by autonomic nerve fibres. The pain is typically dull, poorly localised, and often felt in the midline because of the bilateral sensory supply to the spinal cord. For example, the initial visceral pain of appendicitis arises from distension of the appendix and is felt in the peri-umbilical region (as it's a midgut structure).
  • Somatic Pain: This arises from the parietal peritoneum, the lining of the abdominal cavity, which is innervated by somatic nerves that also supply the skin and muscles. This pain is sharp, intense, and well-localised to the area of inflammation. As appendicitis progresses, inflammation irritates the overlying parietal peritoneum, causing the classic shift of pain to the right iliac fossa (McBurney's point). This irritation also causes localised guarding and rebound tenderness on examination.
  • Referred Pain: This is pain perceived at a location other than the site of the painful stimulus. It occurs when nerve fibres from different regions converge on the same spinal level. For example, diaphragmatic irritation can be referred to the shoulder tip (C3, C4, C5 nerve roots), and ureteric colic from a kidney stone can radiate from the loin to the groin and into the testicle or labia.

Specific pathophysiological mechanisms include:

  • Inflammation/Infection: Conditions like appendicitis, diverticulitis, or pelvic inflammatory disease (PID) cause pain through peritoneal irritation.
  • Obstruction: Blockage of a hollow viscus, such as the intestine (bowel obstruction) or the ureter (renal colic), leads to distension and forceful smooth muscle contraction, causing a spasmodic, colicky pain.
  • Ischaemia: Reduced blood flow to an organ, as seen in mesenteric ischaemia or ovarian/testicular torsion, causes severe pain due to tissue hypoxia and accumulation of metabolic products.
  • Perforation/Rupture: A ruptured ectopic pregnancy or a perforated diverticulum can lead to the spillage of blood or bowel contents into the peritoneal cavity, causing sudden, severe, generalised pain and peritonitis.
Differential Diagnosis (DDx) Framework

A structured approach to the differential diagnosis of lower abdominal pain is essential to ensure life-threatening conditions are prioritised.

Must not miss!
  • Acute abdomen: A general term for any sudden, severe abdominal pain requiring urgent assessment, often indicating a need for surgery.
  • Appendicitis: Typically presents as peri-umbilical pain migrating to the right iliac fossa, associated with anorexia, nausea, and low-grade fever.
  • Intestinal obstruction: Characterised by colicky abdominal pain, vomiting, abdominal distension, and absolute constipation.
  • Peritonitis: Presents with severe, constant abdominal pain worsened by any movement, with board-like rigidity on examination, often due to perforation or infection.
  • Ectopic pregnancy: A gynaecological emergency in women of childbearing age, presenting with lower abdominal pain, vaginal bleeding, and potential haemodynamic collapse.
  • Aortic aneurysm (Rupture or Dissection): Can present with severe, tearing abdominal or back pain and haemodynamic shock, primarily in older patients with vascular risk factors.
  • Mesenteric ischaemia: Presents with severe abdominal pain that is disproportionately greater than the physical examination findings, especially in patients with atrial fibrillation or vascular disease.
  • Pelvic Inflammatory Disease: An infection of the female upper genital tract, presenting with bilateral lower abdominal pain, vaginal discharge, and cervical motion tenderness.
  • Diverticulitis: Inflammation of diverticula, typically causing left iliac fossa pain, fever, and altered bowel habits.
  • Pyelonephritis: An infection of the kidney causing loin or flank pain, fever, rigors, and urinary symptoms.
  • Colorectal cancer: May present insidiously with altered bowel habit, rectal bleeding, weight loss, and sometimes a mass or obstruction.
  • Ovarian cancer: Often presents with vague, non-specific symptoms such as abdominal bloating, pelvic pain, and early satiety.
  • Endometrial cancer: Typically presents with postmenopausal bleeding, but can cause lower abdominal pain or pressure.
  • Cervical cancer: Advanced disease may cause pelvic pain, but the classic presentation is post-coital or intermenstrual bleeding.
  • Acute urinary retention: Presents with a sudden inability to urinate, causing severe suprapubic pain and a palpable bladder.
  • Testicular torsion: A urological emergency in males, presenting with sudden, severe unilateral testicular pain, which may be referred to the lower abdomen.
  • Diabetic Ketoacidosis: Can present with significant abdominal pain, vomiting, and dehydration in patients with diabetes.
Common causes
  • Gastroenteritis: Often presents with diffuse, crampy abdominal pain accompanied by diarrhoea and vomiting.
  • Mesenteric adenitis: Inflammation of mesenteric lymph nodes, common in children, often mimicking appendicitis following a viral illness.
  • UTI: Presents with suprapubic pain, dysuria, frequency, and urgency; cystitis is infection of the bladder.
  • Constipation: A very common cause of lower abdominal pain, often described as colicky and relieved by a bowel movement.
  • Dysmenorrhoea: Painful menstruation, typically causing crampy, cyclical lower abdominal pain.
  • Mittelschmerz: Unilateral, mid-cycle pelvic pain associated with ovulation.
  • Endometriosis: A chronic condition causing cyclical or chronic pelvic pain, dysmenorrhoea, and dyspareunia.
  • Ovarian cyst: Can cause a dull ache, but may present with sudden, severe pain if it ruptures or causes torsion.
  • Threatened Miscarriage: Presents with vaginal bleeding and crampy lower abdominal pain in a confirmed pregnancy with a closed cervix.
  • Renal colic (Urinary tract stone): Severe, colicky pain radiating from the loin to the groin, often associated with haematuria.
  • Hernia: An incarcerated or strangulated hernia (e.g., inguinal or femoral) can cause localised pain and a palpable lump.
  • Psychogenic: Abdominal pain related to psychological factors or stress, a diagnosis of exclusion.
  • COVID-19: Can present with gastrointestinal symptoms, including abdominal pain, often alongside fever and respiratory symptoms.
Easily missed
  • Crohn's disease: A type of inflammatory bowel disease causing chronic, relapsing-remitting abdominal pain, diarrhoea, and weight loss.
  • Ulcerative colitis: An inflammatory bowel disease primarily affecting the colon, causing bloody diarrhoea, tenesmus, and lower abdominal pain.
  • Sickle cell crisis: Vaso-occlusive crises in patients with sickle cell disease can cause severe abdominal pain.
  • Addison's disease: Adrenal insufficiency can present with a non-specific abdominal pain, weakness, and hyperpigmentation.
  • Herpes Zoster: Shingles can cause a unilateral, dermatomal burning pain in the lower abdomen, which precedes the characteristic rash.
Other Differential Diagnoses to consider
  • Psoas abscess: May present with unilateral lower abdominal pain, fever, and difficulty with hip extension.
  • Diverticulosis: Can cause chronic lower abdominal pain, particularly on the left side, with or without inflammation.
  • Pelvic congestion syndrome: Chronic pain, often worse after prolonged standing, linked to pelvic venous insufficiency.
  • Ureteropelvic junction (UPJ) obstruction: Intermittent pain due to blockage at the junction of the ureter and renal pelvis, often with hydronephrosis.
  • Lead poisoning: Can cause diffuse abdominal pain, with neurologic and haematologic abnormalities.
  • Porphyria: Rare metabolic disorder causing abdominal pain, typically with neuropsychiatric symptoms and red or dark urine.
  • Familial Mediterranean fever: Genetic disorder causing recurrent bouts of fever and serositis, often with lower abdominal pain.
  • Rectus sheath haematoma: Sudden onset of lower abdominal pain, often due to trauma or anticoagulation, with palpable mass.
  • COVID-19: May present with atypical symptoms, including abdominal pain, particularly in severe or prolonged cases.
  • Functional abdominal pain: Chronic or recurrent lower abdominal pain without an identifiable organic cause, often linked to IBS or functional dyspepsia.
History Taking
**Patient’s Description**: How can I help you today?
Starting with an open-ended question like, "Can you tell me more about what brought you here today?" allows the patient to describe the symptom in their own words, without initial interruption. Relevance: This approach provides an unfiltered view of the patient’s experience, often offering clues on symptom descriptors, context, and concerns, which guides further targeted questions.
Can you describe the pain? (e.g., sharp, cramping, dull)
  • The quality of pain (e.g., cramping, sharp, dull) may narrow the differential: colicky pain suggests gastrointestinal causes (e.g., renal colic, gastroenteritis), while sharp pain may indicate acute abdomen or rupture.
  • Relevance: Specific pain types can suggest diagnoses, e.g., sharp pain suggests appendicitis or ovarian torsion, while cramping pain might suggest gastroenteritis or dysmenorrhoea.
When did the pain start, and has it changed over time?
  • Determining the onset and progression (e.g., sudden vs. gradual, worsening, or episodic) helps assess acuteness. A sudden onset may suggest acute conditions (e.g., ectopic pregnancy, appendicitis), while chronic pain could indicate endometriosis or IBD.
  • Relevance: A sudden onset can point toward emergent causes like ruptured AAA, while gradual onset may suggest less acute or inflammatory processes.
Are there any factors that make the pain better or worse?
  • Asking about triggers or relieving factors (e.g., eating, movement, urination) can clarify functional, gastrointestinal, or gynaecological causes.
  • Relevance: Pain relief with urination suggests UTI, while worsening with movement suggests peritonitis or appendicitis. Pain relieved by bowel movements may indicate IBS or constipation.
Have you noticed any associated symptoms (e.g., fever, nausea, vomiting, bleeding)?
  • Associated symptoms provide critical clues. Fever could indicate infection (e.g., UTI, pyelonephritis, PID); nausea and vomiting are common in appendicitis, gastroenteritis, or bowel obstruction.
  • Relevance: Positive responses suggest infections or inflammatory conditions. For example, fever and nausea increase suspicion for appendicitis, while blood in urine suggests renal causes like stones.
Have you experienced changes in bowel habits or urinary symptoms?
  • Alterations in bowel or urinary habits can indicate specific diagnoses. Diarrhoea or constipation is common in IBS and inflammatory bowel disease, while dysuria or haematuria suggests UTI or urinary stones.
  • Relevance: Changes in bowel habits support IBD or IBS, while urinary symptoms increase suspicion of UTI, pyelonephritis, or renal colic.
Do you have any recent or relevant medical history (e.g., previous abdominal surgeries, infections)?
  • Previous abdominal surgeries can cause adhesions, predisposing to bowel obstruction. History of gynaecological issues may suggest conditions like endometriosis or PID.
  • Relevance: Identifying prior medical conditions helps assess risk factors for specific conditions, e.g., prior PID increases risk of ectopic pregnancy.
Does anyone in your family have a history of similar pain or conditions?
  • Family history may increase suspicion for genetic or familial disorders, such as IBD or sickle cell disease.
  • Relevance: Family history of IBD, for instance, would raise suspicion for Crohn’s disease or ulcerative colitis.
**Ideas, Concerns, and Expectations (ICE)**
  • Asking about ICE (Ideas, Concerns, Expectations) ensures understanding of the patient’s perspective, worries, and goals for the consultation, potentially uncovering unspoken anxieties.
  • Relevance: Helps guide empathetic care. For instance, if the patient fears appendicitis, addressing this concern may help reduce anxiety while focusing the assessment.
Examination Strategy
General Observation
  • Observe the patient’s general appearance, including distress, pallor, sweating, or signs of dehydration, which can indicate pain severity or underlying pathology.
  • Relevance: A distressed appearance may indicate acute or severe pain (e.g., appendicitis, ruptured ectopic pregnancy), while pallor or signs of shock might suggest a ruptured aortic aneurysm or acute haemorrhage.
Vital Signs
  • Check heart rate, blood pressure, respiratory rate, and temperature. Tachycardia, hypotension, or fever may indicate serious conditions.
  • Relevance: Tachycardia and hypotension suggest hypovolaemic shock (e.g., ruptured AAA, ectopic pregnancy), while fever may indicate infection (e.g., PID, pyelonephritis).
Abdominal Inspection
  • Observe for abdominal distension, visible masses, scars, or hernias.
  • Relevance: Abdominal distension may indicate bowel obstruction; scars may hint at previous surgeries leading to adhesions; visible masses could suggest hernias or tumours.
Abdominal Palpation
  • Gently palpate all four quadrants to identify areas of tenderness, guarding, or masses. Localised tenderness can help identify the affected area and potential cause.
  • Relevance: Right lower quadrant tenderness increases suspicion for appendicitis, while suprapubic tenderness suggests UTI or gynaecological causes.
Rebound Tenderness
  • Assess rebound tenderness by applying pressure to the abdomen and quickly releasing. Pain on release suggests peritoneal irritation.
  • Relevance: Positive rebound tenderness suggests peritonitis, which may occur in appendicitis, perforated ulcer, or other acute abdomen conditions.
Percussion of the Abdomen
  • Percuss to assess for tenderness, fluid, or distension. Tympany indicates gas, while dullness may indicate fluid or mass.
  • Relevance: Dullness over a distended abdomen could suggest ascites or a mass, while generalised tympany suggests bowel obstruction.
Auscultation of Bowel Sounds
  • Listen for bowel sounds in all four quadrants. Absent bowel sounds can suggest ileus or peritonitis, while high-pitched sounds may indicate obstruction.
  • Relevance: Absent sounds suggest peritonitis, while high-pitched or tinkling sounds are associated with bowel obstruction.
Rectal Examination
  • Perform a rectal exam to assess for tenderness, masses, or blood. This may also help identify causes of lower abdominal pain with rectal involvement.
  • Relevance: Positive findings of tenderness or mass could suggest colorectal cancer or inflammatory bowel disease, while rectal bleeding indicates a possible gastrointestinal bleed.
Pelvic Examination (in appropriate cases)
  • In female patients, perform a pelvic exam to assess for cervical motion tenderness, adnexal tenderness, or masses, which may help diagnose gynaecological causes.
  • Relevance: Cervical motion tenderness suggests PID; adnexal mass may suggest an ovarian cyst or ectopic pregnancy.
Red Flag Synthesis
Severe, Sudden-Onset Abdominal Pain
  • Sudden, intense pain is highly concerning, particularly if described as the “worst pain ever,” which may indicate a ruptured abdominal aortic aneurysm (AAA) or bowel perforation.
  • Significance: This could indicate a life-threatening condition such as a ruptured AAA, necessitating immediate surgical intervention.
Signs of Shock (e.g., tachycardia, hypotension, pallor, diaphoresis)
  • Shock signs suggest haemodynamic instability, potentially from severe haemorrhage or sepsis, and require urgent management to prevent organ failure.
  • Significance: Could indicate ruptured ectopic pregnancy, septic shock, or massive internal bleeding, all of which are emergencies needing rapid resuscitation and intervention.
Rebound Tenderness and Guarding
  • Rebound tenderness and involuntary guarding suggest peritoneal irritation, indicating a high likelihood of acute abdomen conditions such as peritonitis or appendicitis.
  • Significance: These findings suggest conditions like appendicitis, bowel perforation, or ruptured ovarian cyst, all of which may require surgical intervention.
Persistent Vomiting and Inability to Tolerate Oral Intake
  • Persistent vomiting leads to dehydration, electrolyte imbalances, and may indicate bowel obstruction or intra-abdominal pathology.
  • Significance: This could indicate a bowel obstruction or severe infection (e.g., peritonitis), necessitating hospitalisation and possible surgery.
Unexplained Weight Loss
  • Unintentional weight loss suggests a possible underlying malignancy, chronic infection, or inflammatory disease.
  • Significance: May indicate colorectal, ovarian, or another abdominal malignancy requiring further investigation.
Blood in Stool or Dark, Tarry Stools (Melena)
  • Haematochezia (bright red blood) or melena (dark, tarry stools) suggests gastrointestinal bleeding, possibly from malignancy, IBD, or diverticular disease.
  • Significance: Could indicate colorectal cancer, severe IBD, or GI bleeding needing urgent evaluation and treatment.
Fever with Lower Abdominal Pain
  • Fever suggests infection, and when associated with abdominal pain, raises concern for sepsis, PID, or pyelonephritis.
  • Significance: This could indicate infections like PID, diverticulitis, or sepsis, requiring prompt antibiotic treatment and close monitoring.
New Onset Constipation or Altered Bowel Habits in Older Adults
  • New bowel habit changes, especially with other red flags, suggest an underlying structural or neoplastic cause.
  • Significance: Could indicate colorectal cancer or other malignancies needing further investigation through imaging or colonoscopy.
Haematuria (Blood in Urine)
  • Haematuria can indicate urinary tract stones, infections, or genitourinary cancers.
  • Significance: Haematuria may signify urinary stones, renal cancer, or bladder cancer, necessitating urinalysis, imaging, and further urological assessment.
Investigations Strategy & Interpretation
Full Blood Count (FBC)
  • FBC helps identify anaemia, leukocytosis, or thrombocytosis, which may indicate infection, inflammation, or haematological issues contributing to the pain.
  • Result Significance: Elevated white cell count supports infection (e.g., appendicitis, PID) or inflammation (e.g., IBD). Anaemia could indicate chronic disease or GI bleeding.
C-Reactive Protein (CRP) and Erythrocyte Sedimentation Rate (ESR)
  • CRP and ESR are markers of inflammation and can help confirm inflammatory or infectious causes of pain.
  • Result Significance: Elevated CRP/ESR suggests inflammation or infection, supporting diagnoses such as diverticulitis, IBD, or PID.
Urinalysis and Urine Culture
  • Urinalysis can identify signs of infection, haematuria, or crystals, pointing towards a UTI, renal stones, or other urinary tract issues.
  • Result Significance: Positive findings of nitrites, leukocytes, or bacteria support UTI; haematuria may indicate renal colic or malignancy.
Pregnancy Test (for female patients of childbearing age)
  • Ruling out pregnancy is crucial in women of reproductive age to exclude ectopic pregnancy or miscarriage.
  • Result Significance: Positive test raises suspicion of intrauterine or ectopic pregnancy; a negative result may rule out pregnancy-related causes.
Abdominal and Pelvic Ultrasound
  • Ultrasound is non-invasive and effective for assessing the appendix, ovaries, bladder, and other pelvic structures.
  • Result Significance: Positive findings of appendicitis, ovarian cysts, or ectopic pregnancy help diagnose specific causes. A negative scan may help exclude structural abnormalities.
CT Abdomen and Pelvis
  • CT is highly sensitive for detecting intra-abdominal pathology such as diverticulitis, bowel obstruction, and abscesses.
  • Result Significance: Positive findings may reveal diverticulitis, bowel obstruction, or perforation. A normal CT scan can help rule out significant structural disease.
Stool Culture and Occult Blood Testing
  • Stool tests can reveal infections, inflammation, or GI bleeding, which may cause lower abdominal pain.
  • Result Significance: Positive stool culture suggests infection (e.g., gastroenteritis); occult blood may indicate malignancy, IBD, or diverticular disease.
Liver and Renal Function Tests
  • Assessing liver and renal function is essential if there is a risk of systemic illness or if abdominal pain may be referred.
  • Result Significance: Abnormal results may indicate systemic conditions affecting these organs or underlying chronic disease.
Lactate Levels
  • In cases of severe or acute abdominal pain, lactate levels help assess for hypoperfusion or ischaemia, such as in bowel ischaemia.
  • Result Significance: Elevated lactate suggests tissue hypoxia, raising suspicion for mesenteric ischaemia or sepsis.
Evidence-Based Diagnosis (EBDx)
Rebound Tenderness in Acute Appendicitis
  • Rebound tenderness is a clinical sign indicative of peritoneal irritation, commonly associated with acute appendicitis.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 68%, Specificity: 93%, Positive Likelihood Ratio: 9.7.
  • Reference: Andersson RE. Meta-analysis of the clinical and laboratory diagnosis of appendicitis. Br J Surg. 2004;91(1):28-37.
  • Significance: Positive rebound tenderness significantly increases suspicion for appendicitis or peritonitis, while its absence reduces the likelihood of an acute abdominal emergency.
White Blood Cell Count (WBC) in Appendicitis
  • An elevated WBC count, particularly with neutrophilia, is frequently observed in appendicitis and can help support a diagnosis when clinical findings are equivocal.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 74-84%, Specificity: 62-68% for elevated WBC in appendicitis.
  • Reference: Howell JM, Eddy OL, Lukens TW, et al. Clinical policy: Critical issues in the evaluation and management of emergency department patients with suspected appendicitis. Ann Emerg Med. 2010;55(1):71-116.
  • Significance: Elevated WBC strengthens the suspicion of appendicitis, but normal levels do not exclude it, as some cases may present without leukocytosis.
Pregnancy Test in Suspected Ectopic Pregnancy
  • A positive β-hCG in a woman of childbearing age with lower abdominal pain should raise suspicion of pregnancy-related causes, including ectopic pregnancy.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: Nearly 100% for pregnancy; specificity depends on clinical context and imaging confirmation.
  • Reference: American College of Obstetricians and Gynecologists (ACOG) Practice Bulletin No. 193: Tubal Ectopic Pregnancy. Obstet Gynecol. 2018.
  • Significance: Positive β-hCG indicates pregnancy, requiring prompt imaging to rule out ectopic pregnancy, a potential surgical emergency.
Ultrasound in Pelvic Inflammatory Disease (PID)
  • Ultrasound can reveal tubo-ovarian abscesses or other inflammatory changes in PID, aiding diagnosis and severity assessment.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 81%, Specificity: 78% for PID with abscesses; general sensitivity and specificity depend on clinical criteria.
  • Reference: Workowski KA, Bolan GA. Sexually Transmitted Diseases Treatment Guidelines, 2015. MMWR Recomm Rep. 2015;64(RR-03):1-137.
  • Significance: Positive findings of abscesses or fluid collections on ultrasound strongly suggest PID, guiding the need for IV antibiotics or drainage.
CT Abdomen in Suspected Diverticulitis
  • CT is the preferred imaging modality for diverticulitis, detecting complications such as abscesses, perforation, and obstruction.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 93%, Specificity: 100% for CT in diagnosing diverticulitis.
  • Reference: Hjern F, et al. CT scan for diagnosis of left-sided acute colonic diverticulitis: a systematic review. Eur Radiol. 2007;17(9):2545-58.
  • Significance: Positive CT findings confirm diverticulitis and assess severity, assisting in management planning, particularly for complicated cases.
Cervical Motion Tenderness (CMT) in PID
  • CMT is a highly suggestive sign of PID in sexually active women with lower abdominal pain and vaginal symptoms.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 92%, Specificity: 47% for CMT in PID.
  • Reference: Haggerty CL, et al. Diagnosis and treatment of pelvic inflammatory disease: a systematic review. JAMA. 2013;289(3):865-877.
  • Significance: Positive CMT supports PID diagnosis, but low specificity means additional signs and symptoms should be assessed.
Serum Lactate in Suspected Mesenteric Ischaemia
  • Elevated serum lactate is a marker of tissue hypoxia, potentially indicating mesenteric ischaemia in patients with abdominal pain and shock.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 86%, Specificity: 44%, but with a high positive predictive value for severe disease.
  • Reference: Oldenburg WA, et al. Acute mesenteric ischemia: a clinical review. Arch Intern Med. 2004;164(10):1054-1062.
  • Significance: Elevated lactate strongly suggests ischaemia or shock, prompting urgent imaging and surgical evaluation.
Diagnostic Timeout & Cognitive Biases

Diagnosing lower abdominal pain is prone to cognitive errors. Taking a 'diagnostic timeout' to challenge initial assumptions is a crucial step to enhance patient safety.

  • Anchoring Bias: This is the tendency to fixate on the first piece of information or an initial diagnosis. For example, anchoring on a diagnosis of a urinary tract infection (UTI) in a young woman with dysuria and suprapubic pain, and failing to consider a more serious diagnosis like an ectopic pregnancy or PID when other signs (e.g., adnexal tenderness, tachycardia) are present. A key reflective question is: "Have I considered gynaecological causes in this female of childbearing age?"
  • Confirmation Bias: This involves seeking out or overvaluing evidence that supports a preferred diagnosis while ignoring contradictory findings. For instance, focusing on altered bowel habit to confirm a diagnosis of IBS while downplaying red flags like rectal bleeding or weight loss that might suggest inflammatory bowel disease or colorectal cancer.
  • Atypical Presentations: A significant pitfall is failing to recognise that diseases can present non-classically. Appendicitis in the very young or elderly can present with vague symptoms without the classic pain migration. Similarly, diverticulitis can occur on the right side of the colon, presenting with right iliac fossa pain and mimicking appendicitis. Maintaining a high index of suspicion in these demographic groups is vital.
  • Premature Closure: This is accepting a diagnosis before it is fully verified. For example, diagnosing gastroenteritis in a patient with diffuse pain and vomiting, without considering a potentially evolving small bowel obstruction, especially if there is a history of prior abdominal surgery. A crucial check is to ask, "Could something else explain these findings?"
Illustrative Management Pathways

For educational purposes, this section describes the typical principles of management for key diagnoses. These are not prescriptive instructions. All management must be guided by official UK guidelines, local formularies, and senior clinical advice.

Acute Appendicitis

Once suspected, patients require urgent hospital admission for surgical review. The initial management involves keeping the patient nil by mouth (NBM), securing intravenous access, administering intravenous fluids to correct any dehydration, and providing analgesia. Inflammatory markers (FBC, CRP) are checked. The diagnosis is often clinical but may be supported by ultrasound or CT imaging. Definitive treatment is an appendicectomy, which is now most commonly performed laparoscopically. Intravenous antibiotics are given peri-operatively according to local trust guidelines.

Ectopic Pregnancy

Any woman of childbearing age with lower abdominal pain and a positive pregnancy test requires an urgent ultrasound to determine the location of the pregnancy. If an ectopic pregnancy is confirmed or strongly suspected, the patient needs immediate referral to an Early Pregnancy Assessment Unit or Gynaecology service. Management depends on clinical stability, serum β-hCG levels, and ultrasound findings. Options include expectant management, medical management with methotrexate, or surgical management (usually a laparoscopic salpingectomy). In cases of rupture with haemodynamic instability, this is a surgical emergency requiring immediate resuscitation and laparotomy.

Acute Diverticulitis

Management depends on severity. Uncomplicated diverticulitis in a well patient can often be managed in the community with oral antibiotics (if indicated), analgesia, and advice to reintroduce fluids and then solids gradually. Patients who are systemically unwell, have signs of complicated diverticulitis (e.g., abscess, perforation), or are unable to tolerate oral intake require hospital admission. In hospital, management includes IV fluids, IV antibiotics, and analgesia. A CT scan of the abdomen and pelvis is the investigation of choice to confirm the diagnosis and rule out complications. Abscesses may require radiological drainage, and perforation requires emergency surgery.

Benign Causes (e.g., Constipation, UTI)

Once serious pathology has been reasonably excluded, management is focused on treating the underlying cause and providing reassurance. For constipation, advice on increasing dietary fibre, fluid intake, and exercise is key, with laxatives prescribed as needed according to the local formulary. For a simple, uncomplicated UTI in non-pregnant women, a short course of oral antibiotics is typically prescribed based on local antimicrobial guidance. Safety netting advice is crucial, instructing the patient to seek further medical attention if symptoms worsen or do not resolve.

Reflection & Learning

These brief cases illustrate the application of clinical reasoning to different presentations of lower abdominal pain. They are for educational purposes only.

Case 1: Suspected Ectopic Pregnancy in the Emergency Department

A 24-year-old woman presents to the ED with a 6-hour history of worsening right iliac fossa pain and light vaginal spotting. Her last menstrual period was 7 weeks ago. She feels dizzy. On examination, she is tachycardic (110 bpm), her blood pressure is 100/60 mmHg, and she has marked tenderness in the RIF. The problem representation is a young woman of childbearing age with amenorrhoea, lower abdominal pain, and signs of haemodynamic compromise, highly suspicious for a ruptured ectopic pregnancy. The immediate pathway involves securing wide-bore IV access, taking bloods (including FBC, group & save, and β-hCG), urgent resuscitation with fluids, and an immediate referral to the on-call gynaecology team for emergency surgical intervention.

Case 2: Suspected Diverticulitis in General Practice

A 72-year-old man with a history of hypertension presents to his GP with a 3-day history of constant left iliac fossa pain, associated with a fever. He reports feeling constipated. On examination, he has a temperature of 38.2°C and localised tenderness in the LIF. His observations are otherwise stable, and he appears well. The problem representation is an older man with localised left-sided abdominal pain and fever, most consistent with uncomplicated acute diverticulitis. The GP may decide to manage him in the community with oral antibiotics, analgesia, and clear safety netting advice to attend the ED if the pain worsens, he is unable to tolerate fluids, or he develops new symptoms.

Case 3: Non-Specific Pain in a Young Man

A 19-year-old university student presents to a walk-in centre with a 24-hour history of generalised lower abdominal cramping and two episodes of diarrhoea. He feels nauseous but has not vomited. Examination reveals mild, diffuse lower abdominal tenderness but no guarding or rebound tenderness. His vital signs are normal. The problem representation is a young, otherwise healthy man with a short history of crampy abdominal pain and diarrhoea, most consistent with gastroenteritis. After excluding red flags for more serious pathology like appendicitis (e.g., no migration of pain, no focal tenderness), management involves reassurance, advice on oral rehydration, and safety netting advice to return if the pain localises, becomes severe, or he is unable to keep fluids down.
