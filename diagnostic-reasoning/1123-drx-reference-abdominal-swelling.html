Abdominal Swelling Diagnostic Reasoning Reference
Context: Abdominal Swelling Assessment
Overview

Abdominal swelling is a common clinical presentation that poses a significant diagnostic challenge, as its causes range from benign and functional disorders to life-threatening malignancies and organ failure. The key task for the clinician is to differentiate between these possibilities through a systematic and logical approach. The assessment must prioritise the identification of "red flag" features that suggest serious underlying pathology, such as malignancy, advanced liver disease, or acute intestinal obstruction.

This educational module provides a foundational framework for the clinical reasoning process involved in assessing a patient with abdominal swelling. It is designed for educational purposes only. For definitive, real-time management protocols, clinicians must consult official UK guidelines from bodies such as NICE (e.g., NG12 for Suspected Cancer, CG149 for Ovarian Cancer) and relevant professional organisations.

Pathophysiology/Basic Science

Understanding the mechanisms causing abdominal swelling is fundamental to formulating a differential diagnosis. The classic mnemonic of the "6 Fs" provides a useful starting point:

  • Fluid (Ascites): This refers to the accumulation of excess fluid within the peritoneal cavity. It most commonly results from portal hypertension (e.g., in liver cirrhosis), where increased pressure in the portal vein forces fluid out of the vasculature. Other causes include hypoalbuminaemia (reducing plasma oncotic pressure, as in nephrotic syndrome), peritoneal inflammation or malignancy (increasing capillary permeability), and right-sided heart failure (causing systemic venous congestion).
  • Flatus: An excess of intestinal gas can cause significant bloating and visible distension. This can be due to functional disorders like Irritable Bowel Syndrome (IBS), where gut motility and visceral sensitivity are altered, or a physical blockage (intestinal obstruction) preventing the passage of gas and leading to proximal bowel dilatation.
  • Faeces: Severe constipation or faecal impaction can lead to a loaded colon that causes generalised abdominal distension and discomfort. This occurs when stool remains in the colon for a prolonged period, leading to increased water absorption and hardening of the faecal mass.
  • Foetus (Pregnancy): A growing uterus during pregnancy is a physiological cause of progressive abdominal swelling.
  • Fat (Obesity): The accumulation of adipose tissue in the abdominal wall and within the omentum and mesentery is a common cause of increased abdominal girth.
  • 'Fatal Growth' / Fulminant Mass (Neoplasm/Organomegaly): Abdominal swelling can be the presenting sign of a large tumour or significant organ enlargement. This includes malignancies like ovarian, colorectal, or gastric cancer, as well as hepatomegaly (enlarged liver) or splenomegaly (enlarged spleen) due to various underlying conditions such as liver disease, haematological disorders, or infiltrative diseases. Large benign masses, such as fibroids or polycystic kidneys, can also cause marked distension.
Differential Diagnosis (DDx) Framework

A structured approach to the differential diagnosis of abdominal swelling is essential to ensure life-threatening conditions are prioritised.

Must not miss!
  • Ascites: The accumulation of fluid in the peritoneal cavity, often presenting with progressive, painless swelling and potential shortness of breath.
  • Liver cirrhosis: End-stage liver disease causing ascites, jaundice, and other stigmata of chronic liver failure.
  • Malignancy: Cancers such as ovarian, colorectal, gastric, or pancreatic cancer can present with a palpable mass, ascites, and constitutional symptoms like weight loss.
  • Heart failure: Right-sided heart failure can cause fluid retention leading to ascites and bilateral lower limb oedema.
  • Nephrotic syndrome: A kidney disorder causing massive protein loss, leading to generalised oedema and ascites due to low serum albumin.
  • Aortic aneurysm: A localised bulge in the aorta which may present as a pulsatile abdominal mass.
  • Colorectal cancer: Can cause swelling due to a large tumour mass or secondary to bowel obstruction.
  • Gastric cancer: May present with a palpable epigastric mass, ascites, and early satiety.
  • Renal carcinoma: A large kidney tumour can present as a palpable flank mass and abdominal distension.
  • Pancreatic Cancer: Often presents with painless jaundice and may cause ascites or a palpable epigastric mass.
  • Lymphoma: Can cause massive lymphadenopathy or splenomegaly, leading to abdominal swelling and systemic symptoms like fever and night sweats.
  • Metastatic cancer: The spread of cancer to the peritoneum (peritoneal carcinomatosis) is a common cause of malignant ascites.
  • Acute urinary retention: A painfully enlarged bladder causing suprapubic swelling and an inability to pass urine.
  • Intra-abdominal abscess: A localised collection of pus which can present as a painful, tender mass, often with fever.
Common causes
  • Constipation: Presents with infrequent bowel movements, straining, and a feeling of bloating or fullness.
  • Irritable bowel syndrome: A functional disorder characterised by fluctuating abdominal bloating, pain, and altered bowel habits.
  • Pregnancy: A physiological cause of progressive abdominal enlargement over several months.
  • Fibroids: Benign uterine tumours that can cause a firm, irregular mass in the lower abdomen and heavy menstrual bleeding.
  • Hepatomegaly: An enlarged liver which can be palpated below the right costal margin, often due to liver disease.
  • Splenomegaly: An enlarged spleen that can cause fullness in the left upper quadrant, often related to liver disease or haematological conditions.
  • Polycystic Kidney Disease (PCKD): A genetic disorder causing massively enlarged, palpable kidneys.
  • COVID-19: While not a direct cause of swelling, gastrointestinal symptoms including bloating can be associated with the infection.
Easily missed
  • Ovarian cancer: Often presents with vague, non-specific symptoms like bloating, pelvic pain, and early satiety, which can be easily missed.
  • Ovarian cyst: A large benign cyst can mimic more serious causes of abdominal or pelvic masses.
  • Diverticular disease: Inflammation or abscess formation can cause a localised, tender mass, typically in the left lower quadrant.
Other Differential Diagnoses to consider
  • Endometriosis: Can present with pelvic masses, causing abdominal distension.
  • Intestinal obstruction: Results from a mechanical or functional block in the bowel, leading to abdominal distension.
  • Peritoneal carcinomatosis: Advanced malignancy with seeding of cancer on the peritoneum.
  • Meigs syndrome: A rare syndrome of ovarian tumour, ascites, and pleural effusion.
  • Massive lymphadenopathy: Can cause abdominal swelling, particularly in lymphoma or metastatic disease.
  • Tuberculous peritonitis: A rare cause of ascites associated with tuberculosis.
  • Retroperitoneal fibrosis: A rare cause of abdominal swelling involving fibrous tissue growth in the retroperitoneum.
  • Pelvic inflammatory disease (PID): In severe cases, PID can cause tubo-ovarian abscesses and abdominal swelling.
History Taking
Patient’s Description: How can I help you today?

It is important to begin with open-ended questions, such as “Can you tell me more about what brought you here today?”. Relevance: This allows the patient to describe their symptoms in their own words. It guides the consultation, helping the clinician decide which areas need further exploration and sets the tone for a patient-centred approach.

Timing: When did you first notice the swelling?
  • Asking about onset helps establish whether the swelling is acute, subacute, or chronic.
  • Relevance: Acute onset may suggest conditions like ascites, obstruction, or ovarian torsion. Gradual onset might suggest chronic liver disease, malignancy, or pregnancy. A sudden onset could also indicate complications of an existing condition.
Progression: Has the swelling been getting worse, staying the same, or improving?
  • Understanding progression helps assess disease severity and course.
  • Relevance: Progressive swelling could indicate malignancy, heart failure, or ascites, while stable or fluctuating swelling could suggest IBS, fibroids, or other benign conditions.
Associated Pain: Have you experienced any pain with the swelling?
  • Determining if pain accompanies the swelling helps distinguish between different causes.
  • Relevance: Painful swelling could suggest ovarian torsion, ruptured cyst, or bowel obstruction, while painless swelling is more consistent with ascites, fibroids, or hepatosplenomegaly.
Triggers/Relieving Factors: Have you noticed anything that makes the swelling better or worse?
  • Identifying triggers or relieving factors can help pinpoint the underlying cause.
  • Relevance: Swelling worsened by meals may suggest gastrointestinal causes, while relief with rest could suggest heart failure. IBS often improves after bowel movements.
Bowel and Urinary Symptoms: Have you noticed any changes in your bowel or urinary habits?
  • Changes in bowel or urinary habits are important in diagnosing abdominal causes of swelling.
  • Relevance: Constipation or diarrhoea may suggest IBS or malignancy, while urinary changes could indicate ascites, ovarian or renal pathology. Acute urinary retention can also cause abdominal swelling.
Weight Changes: Have you noticed any weight gain or loss?
  • Sudden weight changes may be associated with fluid retention or malignancy.
  • Relevance: Weight gain may point to ascites or heart failure, while unexplained weight loss can suggest malignancy.
Fever/Night Sweats: Have you had any fevers or night sweats?
  • Systemic symptoms like fever can suggest an underlying infection or malignancy.
  • Relevance: A positive response could suggest peritoneal infection, intra-abdominal abscess, lymphoma, or tuberculosis, while a negative response may reduce the likelihood of infectious causes.
Past Medical History: Do you have any other medical conditions?
  • Assessing past medical history is crucial to identify pre-existing conditions that may contribute to abdominal swelling.
  • Relevance: A history of liver disease, heart failure, or malignancy raises the index of suspicion for ascites, right heart failure, or metastatic disease. Previous abdominal surgeries may suggest adhesions or bowel obstruction.
Family History: Does anyone in your family have a history of similar conditions?
  • Family history can help assess genetic predispositions.
  • Relevance: A family history of polycystic kidney disease (PCKD), gastrointestinal cancers, or ovarian cancer may indicate a hereditary risk for these conditions.
Drug History: Are you taking any medications?
  • Medications can contribute to or alleviate abdominal swelling.
  • Relevance: Diuretics may indicate pre-existing fluid overload or heart failure, while medications like NSAIDs could worsen conditions like liver cirrhosis.
Ideas, Concerns, and Expectations (ICE)
  • It is essential to ask the patient about their ideas, concerns, and expectations to understand their perspective on the symptoms and address their worries.
  • Relevance: This helps ensure the consultation is holistic. Patients may fear cancer, heart failure, or severe liver disease, and understanding these concerns can guide reassurance and management.
Examination Strategy
General Inspection: Observe the patient's general appearance and position
  • Start by observing the patient’s general appearance, demeanour, and whether they appear distressed or comfortable. Note their body position, which could indicate discomfort or pain.
  • Relevance: A distressed patient may have an acute condition like bowel obstruction, peritonitis, or ascites causing discomfort. Cachexia or weight loss might suggest malignancy, while signs of chronic illness may suggest heart failure or liver disease.
Abdominal Inspection: Inspect the abdomen for visible abnormalities
  • Visually inspect the abdomen for distension, scars, visible veins, or abnormal movement such as pulsations.
  • Relevance: Distension may suggest ascites, pregnancy, or masses. Visible veins (caput medusae) suggest portal hypertension from liver cirrhosis. Surgical scars may indicate past abdominal surgery, which can lead to adhesions and obstruction. A pulsatile mass may indicate an aortic aneurysm.
Palpation: Light and deep palpation of the abdomen
  • Palpation helps assess for tenderness, masses, organomegaly, or abnormal fluid collection (ascites). Perform light palpation first, followed by deep palpation.
  • Relevance: Tenderness could suggest peritonitis, appendicitis, or bowel obstruction. A palpable mass may indicate malignancy, organomegaly, or a cyst. Hepatomegaly could suggest liver disease, while splenomegaly might point to haematological conditions or portal hypertension.
Percussion: Percuss the abdomen to assess for fluid or masses
  • Percussion helps distinguish between solid masses, fluid, and gas in the abdomen. It is especially useful in detecting ascites or hepatosplenomegaly.
  • Relevance: A shifting dullness on percussion suggests ascites. Tympany (high-pitched resonance) may indicate bowel obstruction, while dullness could point to masses, organomegaly, or fluid collection.
Fluid Wave Test: Assess for ascites
  • The fluid wave test helps confirm the presence of ascites by feeling for a transmitted fluid wave across the abdomen.
  • Relevance: A positive fluid wave indicates ascites, which could be due to cirrhosis, malignancy, heart failure, or nephrotic syndrome. A negative test makes ascites less likely.
Auscultation: Listen for bowel sounds
  • Auscultation helps assess bowel motility and vascular sounds. Normal, increased, or absent bowel sounds can provide clues to the underlying condition.
  • Relevance: Hyperactive bowel sounds (high-pitched or tinkling) may suggest bowel obstruction. Absent bowel sounds could indicate ileus or peritonitis. Bruits may indicate vascular abnormalities like an aortic aneurysm or renal artery stenosis.
Assessment of Lower Limbs: Inspect for oedema and venous signs
  • Inspect and palpate the lower limbs for signs of oedema or venous congestion, which could suggest systemic causes of abdominal swelling.
  • Relevance: Bilateral pitting oedema may suggest heart failure, liver cirrhosis (with hypoalbuminaemia), or nephrotic syndrome. Unilateral oedema might indicate deep vein thrombosis (DVT) or lymphatic obstruction.
Hepatojugular Reflux: Assess for right heart failure
  • Press on the upper abdomen while observing the jugular venous pressure (JVP) for signs of a rise, indicating hepatojugular reflux.
  • Relevance: A positive hepatojugular reflux indicates right-sided heart failure, which may contribute to ascites and abdominal swelling. A negative test helps rule out heart failure as a cause of the swelling.
Skin Examination: Check for jaundice, bruising, or spider naevi
  • Examine the skin for signs of liver disease or systemic conditions, such as jaundice, bruising, or spider naevi.
  • Relevance: Jaundice may suggest liver disease or malignancy, bruising may indicate coagulopathy (e.g., due to liver failure), and spider naevi are associated with chronic liver disease.
Pelvic Examination (if appropriate): Check for masses or tenderness
  • A pelvic examination is crucial in patients with suspected gynaecological causes of abdominal swelling, such as ovarian masses or fibroids.
  • Relevance: A palpable pelvic mass may indicate ovarian cancer, large ovarian cysts, or fibroids. Tenderness could suggest pelvic inflammatory disease (PID) or ovarian torsion.
Red Flag Synthesis
Unintentional Weight Loss
  • Significant, unexplained weight loss is a red flag, especially when associated with abdominal swelling.
  • Significance: This could indicate malignancy (e.g., gastrointestinal or ovarian cancer), systemic illness, or chronic infection (e.g., tuberculosis). It requires prompt investigation to rule out cancer or other serious conditions.
Severe Abdominal Pain with Guarding or Rebound Tenderness
  • Severe abdominal pain, particularly with guarding or rebound tenderness on palpation, suggests peritoneal irritation or an acute abdomen.
  • Significance: This may indicate conditions such as bowel perforation, peritonitis, or ruptured abdominal aneurysm, which are life-threatening and require emergency surgical intervention.
Jaundice (Yellowing of Skin or Eyes)
  • Jaundice suggests liver dysfunction or biliary obstruction and is a significant red flag when associated with abdominal swelling.
  • Significance: This may indicate liver cirrhosis, liver malignancy, or pancreatic cancer obstructing the bile ducts. Urgent evaluation is needed to assess liver function and investigate potential malignant causes.
Haematemesis or Melaena (Vomiting Blood or Black Stools)
  • Gastrointestinal bleeding, whether presenting as vomiting blood (haematemesis) or black, tarry stools (melaena), is a red flag in the context of abdominal swelling.
  • Significance: This could indicate upper gastrointestinal bleeding due to peptic ulcers, oesophageal varices (linked to cirrhosis), or malignancy. Immediate evaluation and treatment are required to prevent life-threatening haemorrhage.
New-Onset Ascites
  • The development of ascites (fluid in the abdomen) in a patient with no known history of liver disease or heart failure is a red flag.
  • Significance: This may indicate advanced liver cirrhosis, malignancy (e.g., ovarian or gastrointestinal), or heart failure. Urgent investigation is required to determine the underlying cause and initiate appropriate management.
Lower Limb Oedema with Abdominal Swelling
  • Bilateral pitting oedema in the legs, particularly with abdominal swelling, is a concerning finding.
  • Significance: This could indicate right-sided heart failure, nephrotic syndrome, or severe hypoalbuminaemia (e.g., due to liver failure or protein-losing enteropathy). It requires timely assessment and management to prevent progression.
Palpable Mass in Abdomen or Pelvis
  • The presence of a firm, irregular, or immobile mass during palpation is a red flag for malignancy.
  • Significance: This could indicate an abdominal or pelvic malignancy, such as colorectal cancer, ovarian cancer, or lymphoma. Urgent imaging and biopsy may be necessary to confirm the diagnosis and initiate treatment.
Persistent Fever and Night Sweats
  • Fever and night sweats, particularly when persistent, are signs of systemic infection or malignancy.
  • Significance: This could indicate intra-abdominal infection (e.g., abscess, peritonitis), tuberculosis, or lymphoma. These symptoms warrant prompt investigation and possible urgent intervention.
Investigations Strategy & Interpretation
Full Blood Count (FBC)
  • FBC is important for assessing anaemia, infection, or thrombocytopenia, which can give clues about underlying conditions.
  • Result Significance: Anaemia may suggest malignancy, chronic disease, or bleeding. Elevated white cell count suggests infection or inflammation, while thrombocytopenia could indicate liver disease or malignancy.
Liver Function Tests (LFTs)
  • LFTs assess liver enzymes and bilirubin levels, which are crucial in detecting liver dysfunction or biliary obstruction.
  • Result Significance: Elevated ALT/AST suggests liver injury (e.g., hepatitis, cirrhosis, or malignancy), while raised bilirubin indicates jaundice or biliary obstruction. Low albumin suggests chronic liver disease or nephrotic syndrome.
Urea and Electrolytes (U&Es)
  • U&Es assess kidney function and electrolyte balance, which can help identify causes such as renal disease or dehydration.
  • Result Significance: Abnormal urea or creatinine levels suggest kidney disease or acute kidney injury (e.g., in nephrotic syndrome or heart failure). Electrolyte imbalances may occur with liver disease, diuretics, or other systemic conditions.
Abdominal Ultrasound
  • An ultrasound is a non-invasive imaging modality that helps visualise abdominal organs and fluid collections (e.g., ascites).
  • Result Significance: A positive finding of ascites, masses, or hepatosplenomegaly can point towards liver cirrhosis, malignancy, or polycystic kidney disease. A negative result may rule out significant organomegaly or large masses.
CT Abdomen and Pelvis
  • A CT scan provides detailed imaging of the abdominal and pelvic structures, useful for detecting masses, malignancy, or vascular abnormalities.
  • Result Significance: A positive finding of a mass may suggest malignancy (e.g., ovarian, colorectal, or renal cancer). It also aids in detecting aortic aneurysms, lymphadenopathy, or bowel obstruction.
Serum Amylase/Lipase
  • These enzymes are crucial in diagnosing acute pancreatitis, which may present with abdominal swelling due to inflammation and fluid accumulation.
  • Result Significance: Elevated amylase or lipase levels suggest acute pancreatitis, while normal levels help rule out pancreatic involvement.
Paracentesis with Ascitic Fluid Analysis
  • In cases of ascites, fluid analysis (cytology, albumin, and cell count) helps determine the underlying cause.
  • Result Significance: High white cell count suggests infection (e.g., spontaneous bacterial peritonitis), while cytology can detect malignant cells, indicating peritoneal carcinomatosis. A high serum-ascites albumin gradient (SAAG) suggests portal hypertension.
CA-125 (Tumour Marker)
  • CA-125 is a tumour marker often elevated in ovarian cancer, a key consideration in women presenting with abdominal swelling.
  • Result Significance: An elevated CA-125 level suggests ovarian malignancy or, less commonly, other malignancies like peritoneal carcinomatosis. However, it can also be elevated in benign conditions (e.g., endometriosis), so it must be interpreted in context.
Echocardiogram
  • An echocardiogram is used to assess heart function, especially in cases where right-sided heart failure may be contributing to abdominal swelling through fluid retention.
  • Result Significance: A positive finding of right ventricular dysfunction or elevated pressures would suggest heart failure as a cause of ascites. A normal result helps rule out cardiac causes of swelling.
D-Dimer
  • D-Dimer testing is useful when considering venous thromboembolism (VTE) in patients with abdominal swelling and risk factors for deep vein thrombosis or pulmonary embolism.
  • Result Significance: A positive D-Dimer may indicate a thromboembolic event requiring further imaging (e.g., CT pulmonary angiogram or Doppler ultrasound). A negative result can help rule out VTE in low-risk patients.
Evidence-Based Diagnosis (EBDx)
Shifting Dullness on Percussion for Ascites
  • Shifting dullness is a clinical sign used to detect ascites by percussing for fluid in the abdomen. When the patient changes position, dullness shifts due to free fluid moving within the peritoneal cavity.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 83%, Specificity: 56%, Positive Likelihood Ratio: 1.9.
  • Reference: Fong et al. (2016) “Clinical evaluation for ascites: Systematic review,” JAMA.
  • Significance: A positive finding increases the likelihood of ascites, commonly due to cirrhosis, malignancy, or heart failure, while a negative result lowers suspicion for large-volume ascites.
Fluid Wave Test for Ascites
  • The fluid wave test assesses for the presence of free fluid in the abdomen. It involves generating a fluid wave by tapping one side of the abdomen while feeling for the transmission of the wave on the opposite side.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 62%, Specificity: 92%, Positive Likelihood Ratio: 7.75.
  • Reference: Williams JW et al. (2017) “Accuracy of physical examination for ascites,” American Journal of Medicine.
  • Significance: A positive fluid wave test strongly suggests ascites, while a negative test reduces its likelihood, particularly in smaller volumes of fluid.
CA-125 for Ovarian Cancer
  • CA-125 is a tumour marker often used in the diagnosis of ovarian cancer, especially in post-menopausal women presenting with abdominal swelling.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 78%, Specificity: 78%, Positive Likelihood Ratio: 3.55.
  • Reference: Buys SS et al. (2011) “Ovarian cancer screening with CA-125,” Journal of Clinical Oncology.
  • Significance: Elevated CA-125 levels suggest ovarian cancer, though benign conditions like endometriosis can also cause raised levels. A negative result reduces the likelihood of malignancy.
Ultrasound for Detecting Liver Cirrhosis and Ascites
  • Abdominal ultrasound is a non-invasive imaging modality that can assess liver size, surface nodularity, and detect the presence of ascites, all of which suggest liver cirrhosis.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 85%, Specificity: 95%, Positive Likelihood Ratio: 17.00 for detecting cirrhosis.
  • Reference: Colli et al. (2014) “Ultrasound detection of cirrhosis and complications,” Hepatology Journal.
  • Significance: A positive ultrasound finding of ascites and cirrhotic features supports the diagnosis of liver cirrhosis or malignancy. Negative results make these diagnoses less likely.
CT Scan for Detecting Intra-Abdominal Malignancy
  • CT scanning provides high-resolution imaging of abdominal and pelvic structures, making it a key tool in identifying malignancies such as ovarian, colorectal, and pancreatic cancers.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 89%, Specificity: 93% for detecting intra-abdominal malignancy.
  • Reference: Griffin et al. (2018) “CT accuracy in diagnosing abdominal malignancies,” Radiology.
  • Significance: A positive finding on CT scan strongly suggests malignancy, guiding further diagnostics or staging. A negative CT scan can help exclude significant masses or advanced disease.
Serum-Ascites Albumin Gradient (SAAG) for Ascites Cause
  • The SAAG helps distinguish between causes of ascites. A high gradient (>1.1 g/dL) suggests portal hypertension (e.g., cirrhosis, heart failure), while a low gradient (<1.1 g/dL) suggests non-portal causes like malignancy or infection.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 97%, Specificity: 90% for identifying portal hypertension.
  • Reference: Runyon BA (2013) “Ascites diagnosis with SAAG,” Hepatology Journal.
  • Significance: A high SAAG suggests cirrhosis or heart failure, while a low SAAG points to malignancy or infectious causes such as peritoneal tuberculosis.
Diagnostic Timeout & Cognitive Biases

To improve diagnostic accuracy when assessing a patient with abdominal swelling, it is crucial to be aware of common cognitive errors. A 'diagnostic timeout' to reflect on one's reasoning can prevent misdiagnosis.

  • Anchoring Bias: This is the tendency to fixate on an initial impression. For example, attributing persistent bloating in a middle-aged woman to her known Irritable Bowel Syndrome (IBS) without adequately considering new, more sinister causes like ovarian cancer, especially if red flags like weight loss or new-onset symptoms are present. Ask yourself: "Could this be something else?"
  • Confirmation Bias: This involves seeking or over-emphasising information that supports a favoured diagnosis while ignoring contradictory evidence. For instance, focusing on a history of constipation to diagnose faecal impaction while downplaying the significance of a newly palpable, firm mass found on examination.
  • Attribution Error: Attributing symptoms to a patient's pre-existing condition or lifestyle (e.g., assuming swelling in a patient with obesity is just due to their weight) without a full workup. This can lead to a delayed diagnosis of conditions like ascites from liver disease.
  • Vague Symptom Dismissal: A major pitfall is dismissing non-specific symptoms like bloating, fatigue, or early satiety, particularly in women. These can be the only early signs of ovarian cancer, and a high index of suspicion is required to avoid delaying a critical diagnosis.
Illustrative Management Pathways

This section outlines educational management principles for key diagnoses. These are not prescriptive instructions. All management must be guided by official UK guidelines, local protocols, and senior clinical advice.

Malignant Ascites (e.g., from Ovarian Cancer)

The management is palliative and focuses on symptom control and treating the underlying cancer. Therapeutic paracentesis (draining fluid from the abdomen) provides significant symptomatic relief from distension and breathlessness. For recurrent ascites, options include repeated drainage, indwelling catheters for home drainage, or diuretics (like spironolactone), although their efficacy can be limited. The definitive management involves systemic chemotherapy or targeted therapy for the primary malignancy, coordinated by an oncology team.

Ascites from Liver Cirrhosis

Management aims to control fluid retention and prevent complications. The primary approach is dietary sodium restriction and diuretic therapy, typically with spironolactone (an aldosterone antagonist) and furosemide (a loop diuretic). The dose is titrated based on weight loss and renal function. Large-volume paracentesis is used for tense or diuretic-refractory ascites, often with an infusion of human albumin solution to prevent circulatory dysfunction. All patients should be evaluated for liver transplantation.

Intestinal Obstruction

This is a surgical emergency. Initial management involves making the patient "nil by mouth," providing intravenous fluids to correct dehydration and electrolyte imbalances, and inserting a nasogastric tube to decompress the stomach ('drip and suck'). Analgesia is given for pain. The underlying cause determines further action. Obstruction due to adhesions may resolve with conservative management, whereas obstruction from a malignancy or strangulated hernia requires urgent surgical intervention.

Benign Causes (e.g., IBS, Constipation)

After excluding serious pathology, management focuses on reassurance, education, and symptom control. For IBS-related bloating, lifestyle and dietary advice (e.g., a low FODMAP diet) is first-line, with pharmacological options like antispasmodics. For constipation, management includes increasing dietary fibre, ensuring adequate fluid intake, and using laxatives (e.g., osmotic laxatives like macrogol or stimulant laxatives like senna) as needed. Reassurance that there is no life-threatening cause is a key component of the consultation.

Reflection & Learning

These brief cases illustrate the application of clinical reasoning to different presentations of abdominal swelling. They are for educational purposes only.

Case 1: Suspected Ovarian Cancer in Primary Care

A 65-year-old woman presents to her GP with a 6-week history of persistent abdominal bloating, feeling full quickly when eating, and needing to urinate more frequently. She has also lost about 3kg in weight unintentionally. The problem representation is a post-menopausal woman with persistent, new-onset abdominal symptoms and weight loss, which are red flags for ovarian cancer. The GP arranges urgent blood tests including a CA-125 and an urgent pelvic ultrasound, referring her under the 2-week-wait pathway for suspected cancer.

Case 2: Suspected Decompensated Liver Disease in the Acute Medical Unit

A 58-year-old man with a history of heavy alcohol use is admitted with two months of progressive abdominal swelling, yellowing of his eyes, and swollen ankles. On examination, he is jaundiced, has spider naevi, and a tense, distended abdomen with shifting dullness. The problem representation is a patient with risk factors for liver disease presenting with new-onset ascites and stigmata of chronic liver disease, highly suggestive of decompensated cirrhosis. Immediate management involves blood tests (LFTs, U&Es, coagulation), an abdominal ultrasound, and planning for a diagnostic paracentesis to analyse the ascitic fluid.

Case 3: Functional Bloating in a Young Adult

A 22-year-old university student presents with a 1-year history of abdominal bloating that comes and goes. It is often worse towards the end of the day and after eating certain foods, and is associated with alternating constipation and diarrhoea. The bloating is relieved by passing wind or opening her bowels. Her weight is stable and examination is unremarkable. The problem representation is a young patient with chronic, fluctuating gastrointestinal symptoms consistent with Irritable Bowel Syndrome (IBS). After excluding red flags and performing baseline blood tests, management focuses on lifestyle advice, dietary modification, and reassurance.
