Presentation Overview & Initial Triage

Chest pain is a common presenting complaint that can signify conditions ranging from benign to immediately life-threatening. The primary goal of the initial assessment is to rapidly identify and manage any critical pathology using a structured approach to ensure patient safety.

  • A systematic ABCDE assessment is the recommended first step for any patient with acute chest pain to identify and treat immediate life-threats. (standard UK practice)
  • Immediate priorities include recording baseline vital signs: heart rate, respiratory rate, oxygen saturation, temperature, and blood pressure in both arms if aortic dissection is a possibility.
  • An ECG should be acquired and interpreted within 10 minutes of presentation for any patient with suspected Acute Coronary Syndrome (ACS).[1]
  • Consider obtaining intravenous access, particularly in patients who are haemodynamically unstable or may require IV medications. (standard UK practice)
  • Pain relief should be considered early. The choice of analgesia should be guided by the suspected cause, pain severity, and clinical guidelines. (standard UK practice)
  • According to NICE guideline NG185, supplemental oxygen should not be administered routinely and is typically only initiated if oxygen saturations fall below 94%.[1]
Differential Diagnosis Framework

A structured differential diagnosis framework is essential to ensure all potential causes of chest pain are considered, prioritising life-threatening conditions. The following lists represent a clinically pragmatic approach, search for more DDx lists.

  • Must-Not-Miss Diagnoses
    • Myocardial Infarction
    • Pulmonary Embolism
    • Aortic Dissection
    • Pericarditis (especially with tamponade)
    • Pneumonia (especially severe)
    • Pneumothorax (especially tension)
    • Oesophageal Cancer or Rupture
    • Lung Cancer
    • Spinal Cord Compression
  • Common Diagnoses
    • Musculoskeletal Chest Pain
    • Gastro-oesophageal reflux disease (GORD)
    • Anxiety-related chest pain
    • Costochondritis
    • Stable Angina
    • Medically Unexplained Chest Pain
  • Often-Missed Diagnoses
    • Biliary Colic
    • Herpes Zoster (Shingles)
    • Peptic Ulcer Disease
    • Oesophageal Spasm
    • Rib Fracture
Red Flags & "Must-Not-Miss" Scenarios

Recognising specific symptom and sign clusters is critical for the timely diagnosis of life-threatening conditions. Any patient exhibiting these features requires urgent assessment and senior clinician involvement.

Myocardial Infarction (MI / ACS)
  • Key Symptoms: Central, crushing, or heavy chest pain, often described as pressure or tightness. Pain may radiate to the left arm, jaw, or neck and can be associated with sweating (diaphoresis), nausea, and shortness of breath.
  • Key Signs: The patient may appear pale, sweaty, and distressed. An ECG may show ST-segment elevation/depression, new T-wave inversion, or a new Left Bundle Branch Block (LBBB).
Pulmonary Embolism (PE)
  • Key Symptoms: Sudden onset of sharp, pleuritic chest pain, often accompanied by shortness of breath (dyspnoea) and sometimes haemoptysis. There may be a history of recent surgery, immobility, or active cancer.
  • Key Signs: Tachycardia, tachypnoea, and hypoxia are common findings. There may be signs of a deep vein thrombosis (DVT) in the lower limbs, such as unilateral calf swelling or tenderness.
Aortic Dissection
  • Key Symptoms: Abrupt onset of extremely severe, tearing or ripping chest pain, which classically radiates to the back between the shoulder blades.
  • Key Signs: A significant blood pressure differential (>20mmHg) between the arms is a critical sign. There may be pulse deficits, a new aortic regurgitation murmur, or associated neurological deficits.
Tension Pneumothorax
  • Key Symptoms: Sudden onset of unilateral pleuritic chest pain with severe and rapidly worsening respiratory distress.
  • Key Signs: Tracheal deviation away from the affected side, hypotension, tachycardia, unilaterally hyper-resonant percussion note, and absent breath sounds are hallmark signs of this medical emergency.
Oesophageal Rupture (Boerhaave Syndrome)
  • Key Symptoms: Severe retrosternal chest pain often following an episode of forceful vomiting or an oesophageal procedure.
  • Key Signs: The patient may be febrile and tachycardic, appearing septic. Subcutaneous emphysema (crepitus) in the neck or chest wall is a highly suggestive sign.
History Taking

A systematic and targeted history is the most powerful diagnostic tool. The goal is to elicit features that increase or decrease the probability of the key differential diagnoses.

  • Character of Pain
    • Questions: "Can you describe what the pain feels like? Is it sharp, dull, heavy, tight, burning, or tearing?"
    • Clinical Relevance: A description of "crushing," "heavy," or "tight" pressure increases suspicion for Myocardial Infarction. Sharp, stabbing pain worsened by inspiration (pleuritic) points towards Pulmonary Embolism, Pneumonia, or Pericarditis. "Burning" pain suggests GORD, while "tearing" is a red flag for Aortic Dissection.
  • Location and Radiation
    • Questions: "Can you point with one finger to where the pain is worst? Does it move or spread anywhere else, like your arms, neck, jaw, or back?"
    • Clinical Relevance: Central chest pain radiating to the left arm or jaw is classic for Myocardial Infarction. Radiation to the interscapular region of the back is a red flag for Aortic Dissection. Localised, reproducible pain points towards a Musculoskeletal cause.
  • Exacerbating and Relieving Factors
    • Questions: "Is there anything that makes the pain better or worse? For example, movement, breathing in, exertion, food, or rest?"
    • Clinical Relevance: Pain worsened by exertion and relieved by rest is highly suggestive of Angina. Pain reproducible on palpation strongly suggests a Musculoskeletal cause. Worsening on deep inspiration suggests a pleuritic cause like **PE** or **Pneumonia**. Relief with sitting forward is classic for **Pericarditis**.
  • Associated Symptoms
    • Questions: "Along with the pain, have you experienced any shortness of breath, sweating, nausea, fever, palpitations, or leg swelling?"
    • Clinical Relevance: Dyspnoea is a critical symptom suggesting **PE**, **Pneumonia**, or **Myocardial Infarction**. Sweating (diaphoresis) and nausea are common with **Myocardial Infarction**. Fever suggests an infectious cause like **Pneumonia**. Unilateral leg swelling increases the likelihood of **Pulmonary Embolism** from a DVT.
  • Essential Patient Context
    • Questions: Enquire about past medical history (especially previous **MI**, **PE**, or **GORD**), family history (early heart disease), and social history (smoking is a major risk factor for **MI** and **Lung Cancer**; recent long-haul travel for **PE**).
    • Clinical Relevance: Risk factors significantly alter pre-test probability. A history of IHD makes ACS more likely. Recent surgery or immobility increases the risk of PE. Smoking is a powerful risk factor for multiple serious diagnoses.
Examination Strategy

A focused physical examination aims to find objective signs that support or refute the differential diagnoses generated from the history.

  • General Inspection & Vital Signs
    • Technique: Observe the patient for distress, pallor, or sweatiness (diaphoresis). Measure RR, SpO2, HR, temperature, and BP in both arms.
    • Interpretation & Significance: Diaphoresis and distress are red flags for **MI**, **PE**, or **Aortic Dissection**. Hypoxia (SpO2 <94%) points towards **PE** or **Pneumonia**. Tachycardia is non-specific but common in serious illness. Hypotension is a sign of shock (e.g., from massive **PE** or cardiogenic shock in **MI**). A BP differential >20mmHg suggests **Aortic Dissection**.
  • Cardiovascular Examination
    • Technique: Assess the JVP. Listen for heart sounds, new murmurs, or pericardial rubs. Check for peripheral oedema.
    • Interpretation & Significance: A raised JVP suggests right heart strain (seen in large **PE**) or tamponade. A new murmur could indicate a mechanical complication of **MI** or aortic regurgitation from **Aortic Dissection**. A pericardial friction rub is highly specific for **Pericarditis**.
  • Respiratory Examination
    • Technique: Palpate for tracheal deviation and chest expansion. Percuss and auscultate the lung fields.
    • Interpretation & Significance: Unilateral reduced breath sounds and hyper-resonance suggest **Pneumothorax**. Dullness to percussion and bronchial breath sounds point to **Pneumonia**. A pleural rub can be heard in **PE**, **Pneumonia**, or pleurisy.
  • Chest Wall & Abdominal Palpation
    • Technique: Systematically palpate the chest wall, including ribs and costochondral junctions. Palpate the abdomen, especially the epigastrium and right upper quadrant.
    • Interpretation & Significance: Pain reproducible on chest wall palpation strongly suggests a **Musculoskeletal** cause. Epigastric tenderness may point to **GORD** or **Peptic Ulcer Disease**. Right upper quadrant tenderness suggests **Biliary Colic**.
Investigations: Strategy & Interpretation

Investigations are chosen strategically to confirm or exclude key differentials, with results interpreted in the full clinical context.

  • Electrocardiogram (ECG)
    • Role: First-line investigation to detect cardiac ischaemia, arrhythmia, or signs of pericarditis.
    • Interpretation: ST-elevation confirms **STEMI**. ST-depression or T-wave inversion suggests **NSTEMI** or unstable **Angina**. Widespread concave ST elevation suggests **Pericarditis**. The S1Q3T3 pattern can be seen in **PE**. A normal ECG does not rule out serious causes like NSTEMI or PE.
  • High-Sensitivity Troponin
    • Role: Key blood test to detect myocardial injury.
    • Interpretation: A rise and/or fall in troponin levels confirms acute myocardial injury, consistent with **Myocardial Infarction** in the right clinical context. Troponin can also be mildly elevated in **PE**, severe sepsis, or myocarditis. Normal serial troponins, especially using a 0/1h or 0/3h pathway, have a very high negative predictive value for ruling out MI.[1]
  • Chest X-ray (CXR)
    • Role: To identify pulmonary or other thoracic causes of chest pain.
    • Interpretation: Can reveal consolidation in **Pneumonia**, loss of lung markings in **Pneumothorax**, or signs of heart failure. A widened mediastinum is a red flag for **Aortic Dissection**. The CXR is often normal in ACS and PE.
  • D-dimer
    • Role: A blood test used to help rule out venous thromboembolism in low-risk patients.
    • Interpretation: A negative D-dimer in a patient with a low pre-test probability (e.g., low Wells' score) effectively rules out **Pulmonary Embolism**. It is not specific; a positive result requires further imaging (e.g., CTPA) as it can be elevated in many conditions.
  • CT Imaging
    • Role: Definitive imaging for suspected PE or Aortic Dissection.
    • Interpretation: A **CT Pulmonary Angiogram (CTPA)** is the gold standard for diagnosing **PE**. A **CT Aortogram** is the investigation of choice for suspected **Aortic Dissection**.
Management Principles & Safety Netting

Initial management focuses on stabilising the patient and treating life-threatening conditions according to established UK guidelines, followed by robust safety netting for all patients.

  • Suspected Acute Coronary Syndrome (ACS)
    • An approach consistent with NICE guideline NG185 includes offering a single loading dose of 300 mg aspirin (unless contraindicated), providing pain relief (e.g., nitrates, morphine), and considering further antiplatelet therapy. Urgent referral to cardiology is indicated, especially for STEMI.[1]
  • Suspected Pulmonary Embolism (PE)
    • NICE guideline CG144 suggests offering immediate anticoagulation (e.g., with apixaban, rivaroxaban, or low molecular weight heparin) to patients with a high clinical probability of PE, or confirmed PE. Oxygen should be offered to hypoxic patients.[2]
  • Suspected Aortic Dissection
    • This is a surgical emergency requiring immediate senior input. Management principles include aggressive blood pressure and heart rate control (e.g., with intravenous beta-blockers) and urgent surgical review.
  • Suspected Tension Pneumothorax
    • Immediate management involves needle decompression to relieve pressure, followed by definitive chest drain insertion. This is a life-saving intervention performed before imaging confirmation. (standard UK practice)
  • Common, Less Serious Causes
    • Management for conditions like **GORD** may involve a trial of a proton pump inhibitor. **Musculoskeletal pain** often responds to simple analgesia and reassurance after serious causes are excluded.
  • Critical Safety Netting Advice
    • All patients discharged, especially those with diagnostic uncertainty, must be given clear instructions on when and where to seek urgent medical attention.
    • Specific red flag symptoms to watch for should be explained (e.g., "If the pain becomes severe and crushing, or you become very short of breath, you must call 999 immediately").
    • Ensure appropriate follow-up is arranged (e.g., with their GP). Document the advice given in the patient's record.
Pathophysiology & Mechanisms

Understanding the underlying mechanism of pain generation is key to interpreting clinical features and linking them to a diagnosis.

  • Myocardial Ischaemia (in MI/Angina)
    • Imbalance between myocardial oxygen supply and demand leads to the accumulation of metabolic byproducts like adenosine and lactate. These substances stimulate cardiac nociceptors, causing the characteristic visceral pain described as heavy, tight, or crushing.
  • Pleuritic & Pericardial Pain (in PE, Pneumonia, Pericarditis)
    • Inflammation of the parietal pleura or pericardium irritates densely packed somatic pain fibres. This results in a sharp, stabbing pain that is well-localised and characteristically worsens with any movement that causes the inflamed surfaces to rub, such as deep inspiration or coughing.
  • Vascular Wall Stretch (in Aortic Dissection)
    • A tear in the aortic intima allows high-pressure blood to enter and split the vessel wall (media). This rapid and forceful stretching of the intensely innervated aortic wall tissue causes excruciatingly severe, "tearing" pain.
  • Oesophageal Pain (in GORD, Spasm)
    • Pain can be generated by two main mechanisms: chemical irritation of the oesophageal lining from acid reflux (**GORD**), causing a "burning" sensation; or abnormal, forceful smooth muscle contractions (**Oesophageal Spasm**), causing a "squeezing" or "tight" pain that can mimic angina.
  • Somatic Pain (in Musculoskeletal causes)
    • Direct inflammation or injury to chest wall structures (muscles, cartilage, bone) activates local nociceptors in somatic tissues. This pain is typically well-localised and reproducible by direct pressure or movement, as this directly stimulates the affected tissues.
Evidence-Based Diagnosis (EBDx)

This section synthesizes evidence on the diagnostic value of key clinical findings to aid reasoning. Statistics should be interpreted as illustrative of the evidence, not as absolute values.

  • Finding: Crushing Chest Pain Radiating to Arm(s)
    • Significance: A classic feature of Myocardial Infarction.
    • Evidence Bite: The presence of pain radiating to the arms or shoulders significantly increases the likelihood of ACS. Some studies report Positive Likelihood Ratios (LR+) ranging from approximately 2 to 7 for these features, though the exact values vary. A history of cardiac pain is a powerful indicator.
  • Finding: Pain Reproducible on Chest Wall Palpation
    • Significance: Strongly suggests a Musculoskeletal cause.
    • Evidence Bite: This sign has a high specificity (often >85%) for a musculoskeletal diagnosis. However, its sensitivity is variable, and it can occasionally co-exist with ACS, so it should not be used in isolation to rule out serious pathology.
  • Finding: Pleuritic Chest Pain
    • Significance: Points towards pulmonary or pleural causes like **PE**, **Pneumonia**, or **Pericarditis**.
    • Evidence Bite: While a key feature of PE, pleuritic pain in isolation has a modest diagnostic value (LR+ often <2). Its value increases when combined with other features in clinical prediction rules like the Wells' score.
  • Finding: New ST-Segment Elevation on ECG
    • Significance: Diagnostic hallmark of an **ST-Elevation Myocardial Infarction (STEMI)**.
    • Evidence Bite: ST-elevation meeting standard criteria is highly specific (>90%) for acute coronary occlusion, though it has moderate sensitivity (~50%) for all MIs, as many are NSTEMIs. It is a time-critical emergency signal.
  • Finding: A Negative D-dimer Result
    • Significance: Used to rule out **Pulmonary Embolism**.
    • Evidence Bite: When combined with a low clinical pre-test probability, a negative D-dimer has a very high Negative Predictive Value (>99%), making it an excellent rule-out test for PE and allowing clinicians to safely avoid further imaging.[2]
Cognitive Biases & Pitfalls

Awareness of common cognitive errors is essential to improve diagnostic accuracy and patient safety when assessing a patient with chest pain.

  • Anchoring Bias: Prematurely locking onto a diagnosis and failing to adjust.
    • Example: Attributing chest pain in a patient with known GORD to their reflux without adequately considering a new cardiac cause, especially if the symptoms are atypical.
  • Confirmation Bias: Seeking out or overvaluing evidence that supports an initial belief while ignoring contradictory evidence.
    • Example: Focusing on reproducible chest wall tenderness to confirm a musculoskeletal diagnosis while downplaying the significance of exertional shortness of breath or a non-specific ECG change.
  • Atypical Presentation Pitfall: Failing to recognise that serious diseases can present non-classically.
    • Example: Missing an ACS in an older female patient or a person with diabetes whose primary symptom is fatigue, nausea, or epigastric discomfort rather than classic crushing chest pain.
  • Over-reliance on Normal Investigations: Placing too much weight on reassuring but non-definitive test results.
    • Example: Discharging a patient with a compelling history for ACS based on a single normal ECG and an initial normal troponin, without appreciating the need for serial testing.
Illustrative Case Vignettes

These brief cases illustrate the application of clinical reasoning to different chest pain presentations. They are for educational purposes only.

  • Case 1: Suspected ACS
    • Presentation: A 62-year-old man with type 2 diabetes presents with a 2-hour history of central chest tightness radiating to his jaw, associated with nausea and sweating.
    • Key Findings: Appears pale and clammy. HR 98, BP 155/90. ECG shows ST depression in leads V4-V6.
    • Problem Representation: An older male with cardiovascular risk factors presents with ischaemic-sounding chest pain and dynamic ECG changes, highly suspicious for an Acute Coronary Syndrome (NSTEMI).
    • Typical Next-Step Considerations: Immediate management as per ACS pathway (e.g., aspirin 300mg), serial troponins, cardiology referral, risk stratification (e.g., GRACE score).
  • Case 2: Suspected PE
    • Presentation: A 35-year-old woman on the combined oral contraceptive pill presents with a 1-day history of sudden-onset, sharp right-sided chest pain, worse on inspiration. She also feels breathless.
    • Key Findings: Anxious but alert. HR 105, RR 22, SpO2 93% on air. Chest is clear on auscultation. No calf swelling.
    • Problem Representation: A young woman with a key risk factor (OCP) presents with acute pleuritic chest pain, breathlessness, tachycardia, and hypoxia, concerning for a Pulmonary Embolism.
    • Typical Next-Step Considerations: Calculate Wells' score (likely moderate-to-high risk). Given the hypoxia, proceed directly to imaging with a CTPA rather than starting with a D-dimer. Consider starting therapeutic anticoagulation.
  • Case 3: Musculoskeletal Pain
    • Presentation: A 25-year-old man presents with 3 days of localised, sharp left-sided chest pain after helping a friend move house. The pain is worse when he twists his torso or takes a deep breath.
    • Key Findings: Appears comfortable at rest. All vital signs are normal. Cardiovascular and respiratory exams are normal. There is distinct tenderness to palpation over the 4th left costochondral junction, which reproduces his pain.
    • Problem Representation: A young, low-risk man with a history of recent physical strain presents with localised, reproducible chest wall pain and no red flag features, most consistent with a musculoskeletal cause.
    • Typical Next-Step Considerations: After a thorough history and examination have excluded red flags, and with a normal ECG, reassurance is key. Advise simple analgesia (e.g., NSAIDs if appropriate) and provide clear safety netting advice about when to return.
