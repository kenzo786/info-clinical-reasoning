Upper Abdominal Pain Diagnostic Reasoning Reference
Context: Upper Abdominal Pain Assessment
Overview

Upper abdominal pain is a frequent and diagnostically challenging presentation in both primary and emergency care. The spectrum of possible causes is wide, ranging from benign, self-limiting conditions like gastroenteritis or indigestion, to severe, life-threatening emergencies such as a ruptured aortic aneurysm, perforated peptic ulcer, or acute pancreatitis. The key clinical challenge is to effectively and safely differentiate between these possibilities, ensuring that critical diagnoses are identified promptly while avoiding excessive investigation for more benign causes.

A structured and systematic approach, grounded in a thorough history and physical examination, is essential. This initial assessment must prioritise the identification of "red flag" symptoms and signs that indicate the need for immediate investigation or referral. This educational module provides a foundational framework for the clinical reasoning process involved in assessing a patient with upper abdominal pain. For definitive, real-time management protocols, clinicians must consult official UK guidelines from bodies such as the National Institute for Health and Care Excellence (NICE) (e.g., CG184 for Gastro-oesophageal reflux disease and dyspepsia, CG143 for Gallstone disease) and local hospital trusts.

Pathophysiology/Basic Science

Understanding the mechanisms that generate upper abdominal pain is fundamental to forming an accurate differential diagnosis. Pain can originate from different structures and through distinct pathways:

  • Visceral Pain: This arises from the abdominal organs themselves (the viscera). It occurs when organ capsules are stretched (e.g., by inflammation in hepatitis or pancreatitis) or when hollow organs (like the stomach or intestines) contract forcefully or are distended. Visceral pain is typically dull, gnawing, or cramping and is poorly localised to the epigastrium, periumbilical, or suprapubic regions, corresponding to the embryological origin of the organ (foregut, midgut, or hindgut).
  • Somatic (Parietal) Pain: This originates from the parietal peritoneum, the lining of the abdominal cavity. It is caused by direct irritation from inflammation, infection, or chemical substances (e.g., gastric acid from a perforated ulcer or pus from an abscess). Unlike visceral pain, somatic pain is sharp, intense, and precisely localised to the area of irritation. It is often associated with tenderness to palpation and is exacerbated by movement, leading to guarding and rigidity.
  • Referred Pain: This is pain perceived at a location distant from the actual site of pathology. It occurs because visceral and somatic nerve fibres from different areas converge at the same spinal cord level. A classic example is the diaphragmatic irritation from a gallbladder abscess or splenic injury causing referred pain to the right shoulder tip (via the phrenic nerve, C3-C5), as this spinal segment also supplies sensation to the shoulder area. Similarly, pancreatitis classically refers pain to the back.
  • Chemical and Inflammatory Pain: Conditions like gastro-oesophageal reflux disease (GORD) and peptic ulcer disease cause pain through direct chemical irritation of the mucosal lining by gastric acid. The resulting inflammation activates local nociceptors, causing a characteristic burning sensation. In pancreatitis, the release of digestive enzymes causes auto-digestion and intense inflammation of the pancreas and surrounding tissues, leading to severe pain.
Differential Diagnosis (DDx) Framework

A structured approach to the differential diagnosis of upper abdominal pain is essential to ensure life-threatening conditions are prioritised.

Must not miss!
  • Aortic aneurysm (Rupture or Dissection): Presents with sudden-onset, severe epigastric or back pain, often described as 'tearing', and may be associated with haemodynamic collapse.
  • Myocardial Infarction: Can present atypically with epigastric pain, nausea, and diaphoresis, particularly in women, older adults, or patients with diabetes.
  • Mesenteric ischaemia: Characterised by severe, diffuse abdominal pain that is classically out of proportion to the findings on physical examination.
  • Oesophageal cancer: May cause persistent epigastric pain, progressive difficulty swallowing (dysphagia), and unintentional weight loss.
  • Gastric cancer: Often presents with persistent dyspepsia, anorexia, weight loss, and sometimes signs of gastrointestinal bleeding.
  • Pancreatic Cancer: Typically causes insidious-onset epigastric pain radiating to the back, associated with jaundice and significant weight loss.
  • Cholangiocarcinoma: A cancer of the bile ducts presenting with jaundice, pale stools, dark urine, and upper abdominal discomfort.
  • Hepatocellular carcinoma: Can cause right upper quadrant pain, weight loss, and signs of decompensated liver disease in patients with underlying cirrhosis.
  • Ovarian cancer: May present with non-specific abdominal bloating, pain, or early satiety, sometimes referred to the upper abdomen.
  • Cholecystitis: Presents as severe, constant right upper quadrant pain, often with fever and a positive Murphy's sign on examination.
  • Cholangitis: An infection of the biliary tree, classically presenting with Charcot's triad: right upper quadrant pain, fever, and jaundice.
  • Pyelonephritis: An infection of the kidney causing flank or upper abdominal pain, fever, and systemic symptoms like rigors.
  • Hepatitis: Inflammation of the liver (viral or otherwise) causing right upper quadrant pain, jaundice, fatigue, and nausea.
  • Pneumonia: A lower-lobe pneumonia can irritate the diaphragm, causing referred pain to the upper abdomen.
  • Acute pancreatitis: Characterised by severe, constant epigastric pain often radiating through to the back, accompanied by nausea and vomiting.
  • Intestinal obstruction: Presents with colicky abdominal pain, vomiting (which may be bilious or faeculent), abdominal distension, and absolute constipation.
  • Peritonitis: Caused by perforation of a viscus, leading to generalised, severe abdominal pain, rigidity, and signs of shock.
  • Diabetic Ketoacidosis: A metabolic emergency that can present with significant abdominal pain, vomiting, and Kussmaul breathing.
Common causes
  • Gastroenteritis: A common viral or bacterial infection causing diffuse cramping abdominal pain, vomiting, and diarrhoea.
  • GORD: Causes a burning retrosternal or epigastric discomfort (heartburn), often worsened by meals or lying flat and relieved by antacids.
  • Hiatus hernia: Protrusion of the stomach into the chest, which can exacerbate symptoms of GORD.
  • Peptic Ulcer Disease: Typically causes a localised, burning epigastric pain that may be either relieved (duodenal ulcer) or worsened (gastric ulcer) by food.
  • Irritable bowel syndrome: A functional disorder causing chronic, relapsing abdominal cramps, bloating, and an altered bowel habit.
  • Gallstone disease: Presents as biliary colic—a severe, constant pain in the epigastrium or right upper quadrant, often precipitated by fatty meals.
  • Urinary tract stone: A stone passing down the ureter can cause severe flank pain that may radiate to the upper abdomen or groin.
  • Constipation: A very common cause of diffuse, colicky abdominal pain and bloating.
  • Diverticular disease: While typically causing left lower quadrant pain, inflammation can sometimes cause more generalised or upper abdominal pain.
  • Hernia: An epigastric or umbilical hernia can become trapped or strangulated, causing localised pain.
  • Functional abdominal pain syndrome: A chronic pain condition where no organic cause is identified despite thorough investigation.
  • COVID-19: Can present with a variety of gastrointestinal symptoms, including upper abdominal pain, nausea, and diarrhoea.
Easily missed
  • Chronic Pancreatitis: Often presents with recurrent episodes of upper abdominal pain, which may be less severe than acute attacks, and can lead to steatorrhoea and diabetes.
  • Crohn's disease: This inflammatory bowel disease can affect any part of the GI tract, including the stomach or duodenum, causing upper abdominal pain.
  • Ulcerative colitis: While primarily affecting the colon, severe pancolitis can cause generalised abdominal pain.
  • Coeliac disease: An autoimmune reaction to gluten that can present with dyspepsia, bloating, and abdominal pain.
  • Sickle cell crisis: Vaso-occlusion within the abdominal organs can cause severe upper abdominal pain, mimicking an acute surgical abdomen.
  • Addison's disease: Adrenal insufficiency can cause a non-specific 'Addisonian crisis' with abdominal pain, vomiting, and collapse.
  • Herpes Zoster: (Shingles) can cause a severe, burning, unilateral pain in a dermatomal distribution across the abdomen or chest days before the characteristic rash appears.
Other Differential Diagnoses to consider
  • Splenic infarction: Rare cause of acute upper abdominal pain, often associated with thromboembolic conditions or sickle cell disease.
  • Endometriosis: Can present with cyclical upper abdominal pain, especially if diaphragmatic involvement occurs.
  • Porphyria: Metabolic disorder causing intermittent abdominal pain with neurological symptoms.
  • Spinal referred pain: Pain radiating from thoracic or lumbar spine pathology mimicking visceral pain.
  • Hypercalcaemia: May present with diffuse abdominal discomfort and systemic symptoms such as confusion or lethargy.
  • Angina equivalent: Ischaemic cardiac pain presenting atypically as upper abdominal discomfort, particularly in diabetics or women.
  • Abdominal wall pain: Localised pain due to musculoskeletal causes, such as trauma or nerve entrapment.
  • Gastric volvulus: Rare condition causing acute severe epigastric pain, associated with retching or inability to vomit.
  • Diaphragmatic hernia: Herniation through the diaphragm, leading to upper abdominal pain and respiratory symptoms.
  • Liver abscess: Infection of liver tissue presenting with right upper quadrant pain, fever, and systemic signs.
History Taking
Patient’s Description: How can I help you today?
Starting with an open-ended question, such as "Can you tell me more about what brought you here today?" allows the patient to describe their symptoms in their own words. Relevance: This guides the clinician towards key areas for further questioning and avoids premature assumptions.
Can you describe the pain? (Quality and intensity)
  • Understanding whether the pain is burning, stabbing, cramping, or dull helps narrow the differential diagnosis.
  • Relevance: A burning pain may suggest GORD, whereas a cramping pain could indicate biliary colic or bowel obstruction. Sharp, sudden onset might suggest a perforated viscus or aortic aneurysm rupture.
When did the pain start? (Onset and duration)
  • Acute pain is often associated with emergent conditions like acute pancreatitis or myocardial infarction.
  • Relevance: Chronic or intermittent pain might suggest peptic ulcer disease, gallstones, or functional disorders.
What makes it better or worse? (Triggers and alleviating factors)
  • Explores the relationship between symptoms and specific factors like eating, movement, or medications.
  • Relevance: Pain relieved by antacids suggests GORD or peptic ulcer disease, while worsening with fatty meals may indicate biliary pathology.
Is the pain localised, or does it radiate elsewhere?
  • Determines if the pain stays confined to one area or spreads to others, such as the back or shoulders.
  • Relevance: Radiation to the back is common in pancreatitis, while right shoulder pain may indicate gallbladder involvement (referred pain).
Have you experienced any associated symptoms? (Nausea, vomiting, fever, jaundice, etc.)
  • Associated symptoms provide clues to the underlying cause of pain.
  • Relevance: Fever and jaundice suggest cholangitis; nausea and vomiting may indicate bowel obstruction or gastritis. Absence of these symptoms reduces the likelihood of these conditions.
Any history of similar symptoms, or related medical conditions?
  • Explores the possibility of recurrent or chronic conditions such as gallstones or peptic ulcers.
  • Relevance: A history of similar episodes may support a chronic diagnosis, whereas a new onset in a high-risk patient might signal a more serious condition.
**Ideas, Concerns, and Expectations (ICE)**
  • Understanding the patient’s thoughts and worries allows for a patient-centred consultation.
  • Relevance: Helps identify fears of conditions like cancer, address misinformation, and align management with the patient’s expectations.
Examination Strategy
General Appearance and Vital Signs
  • Assess for signs of distress, pallor, jaundice, or diaphoresis. Check vital signs, including heart rate, blood pressure, respiratory rate, and temperature.
  • Relevance: Tachycardia and hypotension may suggest shock due to haemorrhage, sepsis, or acute pancreatitis. Jaundice can indicate biliary or liver pathology.
Inspection of the Abdomen
  • Look for visible masses, distension, scars, or abnormal movements such as peristalsis.
  • Relevance: Distension may indicate obstruction or ascites. Scars may point to previous surgeries, and visible pulsations could suggest an aortic aneurysm.
Palpation of the Abdomen
  • Palpate for tenderness, guarding, rigidity, or masses. Assess for rebound tenderness and organomegaly.
  • Relevance: Tenderness in the right upper quadrant suggests biliary or liver pathology. Epigastric tenderness may indicate gastritis or pancreatitis. Guarding and rigidity are concerning for peritonitis.
Percussion of the Abdomen
  • Perform percussion to identify areas of dullness (e.g., organomegaly, ascites) or tympany (e.g., obstruction).
  • Relevance: Shifting dullness indicates ascites. Hyper-resonance may occur in bowel obstruction or perforation.
Auscultation of the Abdomen
  • Listen for bowel sounds and bruits over major arteries.
  • Relevance: Absent bowel sounds suggest ileus or peritonitis. Hyperactive bowel sounds may indicate obstruction. Bruits may point to vascular conditions like mesenteric ischaemia.
Assessment for Murphy’s Sign
  • Palpate the right upper quadrant while asking the patient to inhale deeply. Positive if pain is elicited and respiration halts.
  • Relevance: Suggests acute cholecystitis. A negative test makes this diagnosis less likely.
Examination for Cullen’s and Grey-Turner’s Signs
  • Inspect the periumbilical area (Cullen’s sign) and flanks (Grey-Turner’s sign) for discolouration.
  • Relevance: These signs indicate retroperitoneal bleeding, commonly associated with severe pancreatitis or ruptured ectopic pregnancy.
Examination for Signs of Chronic Liver Disease
  • Look for spider naevi, palmar erythema, gynaecomastia, or ascites.
  • Relevance: These findings suggest underlying liver disease, which may contribute to symptoms of upper abdominal pain.
Red Flag Synthesis
Severe, sudden-onset abdominal pain
  • Sudden and severe pain may indicate a perforated viscus, ruptured abdominal aortic aneurysm (AAA), or mesenteric ischaemia.
  • Significance: These are life-threatening conditions that require immediate emergency management.
Haemodynamic instability (e.g., hypotension, tachycardia)
  • Suggests shock, which could result from haemorrhage (e.g., ruptured AAA), severe sepsis (e.g., cholangitis), or perforation.
  • Significance: Urgent resuscitation and identification of the underlying cause are critical.
Jaundice and fever
  • These symptoms suggest ascending cholangitis, a serious biliary tract infection.
  • Significance: Ascending cholangitis requires urgent antibiotic therapy and potential biliary drainage.
Progressive weight loss and anorexia
  • May indicate malignancies such as pancreatic cancer, gastric cancer, or cholangiocarcinoma.
  • Significance: Requires urgent investigation for an underlying neoplastic process.
Persistent vomiting
  • Could suggest gastrointestinal obstruction, severe gastritis, or other structural abnormalities.
  • Significance: Requires prompt imaging and evaluation to identify the cause of obstruction or persistent symptoms.
Haematemesis or melaena
  • Indicates gastrointestinal bleeding, which may result from peptic ulcers, varices, or malignancy.
  • Significance: Needs urgent endoscopy and stabilisation.
Rebound tenderness and abdominal rigidity
  • Signs of peritonitis, which may result from perforation, ischaemia, or infection.
  • Significance: Requires urgent surgical assessment and intervention.
Severe back pain with abdominal pain
  • May indicate a ruptured or dissecting abdominal aortic aneurysm.
  • Significance: This is a surgical emergency requiring immediate vascular intervention.
Fever with hypotension
  • Suggests severe sepsis, often secondary to conditions like cholangitis, pancreatitis, or intra-abdominal abscesses.
  • Significance: Requires urgent resuscitation and broad-spectrum antibiotics.
Investigations Strategy & Interpretation
Full Blood Count (FBC)
  • Helps identify anaemia, infection, or inflammation. A raised white cell count may suggest infection or inflammation, while anaemia could point towards chronic gastrointestinal bleeding or malignancy.
  • Result Significance: A raised WBC may suggest infections such as cholecystitis or cholangitis. Negative results make systemic inflammation or infection less likely.
Liver Function Tests (LFTs)
  • Assess for liver or biliary pathology. Elevated bilirubin or alkaline phosphatase suggests biliary obstruction, while raised transaminases indicate hepatocellular injury.
  • Result Significance: Raised bilirubin and alkaline phosphatase point to cholangitis or gallstones. Normal results make biliary or hepatic pathology less likely.
Serum Amylase and Lipase
  • Used to diagnose acute pancreatitis. Elevated levels are highly specific for pancreatic inflammation.
  • Result Significance: Elevated levels confirm pancreatitis. Normal levels make pancreatitis less likely, though sensitivity is not 100%.
C-Reactive Protein (CRP)
  • An inflammatory marker used to assess the severity of infection or inflammation.
  • Result Significance: Raised CRP suggests significant inflammation, such as in pancreatitis or cholangitis. A low CRP reduces the likelihood of severe inflammatory conditions.
Abdominal Ultrasound
  • First-line imaging for suspected biliary or hepatic pathology, as well as for identifying ascites or abdominal masses.
  • Result Significance: Detects gallstones, biliary obstruction, or liver masses. Negative findings may necessitate further imaging.
CT Abdomen and Pelvis
  • Used for detailed evaluation of the abdomen, particularly for suspected malignancies, pancreatitis, or vascular emergencies.
  • Result Significance: Identifies malignancies, perforations, or ischaemia. Negative findings help rule out these serious conditions.
Upper GI Endoscopy
  • Essential for visualising the oesophagus, stomach, and duodenum in cases of suspected peptic ulcer disease, GORD, or malignancy.
  • Result Significance: Identifies ulcers, oesophagitis, or gastric cancer. Normal findings may redirect focus to other causes.
Electrocardiogram (ECG)
  • Screen for myocardial ischaemia, which can present atypically as upper abdominal pain.
  • Result Significance: Abnormalities suggest cardiac involvement. A normal ECG does not exclude ischaemia but reduces its likelihood.
Helicobacter pylori Testing
  • Used to diagnose H. pylori infection in patients with suspected peptic ulcer disease.
  • Result Significance: A positive test supports a diagnosis of H. pylori-related gastritis or ulcers. Negative findings suggest alternative causes.
Evidence-Based Diagnosis (EBDx)
Murphy’s Sign in Acute Cholecystitis
  • Murphy’s sign is elicited by pressing over the gallbladder area during inspiration, resulting in pain and the cessation of breath.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 65%, Specificity: 87%, Positive Likelihood Ratio (LR+): 5.0.
  • Reference: Strasberg et al., "Clinical Assessment of Cholecystitis," JAMA, 2018.
  • Significance: A positive Murphy’s sign strongly suggests acute cholecystitis, particularly in conjunction with fever and leukocytosis.
Amylase/Lipase Elevation in Acute Pancreatitis
  • Serum amylase and lipase are elevated in most cases of acute pancreatitis, with lipase being more specific to pancreatic pathology.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 90%, Specificity: 80%, LR+: 4.5 for lipase (values >3x normal limit).
  • Reference: Tenner et al., "Management of Acute Pancreatitis," American Journal of Gastroenterology, 2019.
  • Significance: Elevated lipase strongly supports the diagnosis of acute pancreatitis, while normal levels significantly reduce its likelihood.
Ultrasound for Gallstones and Biliary Pathology
  • Abdominal ultrasound is the preferred first-line imaging for detecting gallstones, biliary dilation, and gallbladder wall thickening.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 84%, Specificity: 99%, LR+: 84 for detecting gallstones.
  • Reference: European Association for the Study of the Liver (EASL) guidelines, 2016.
  • Significance: Positive findings confirm biliary pathology, while negative findings in a well-performed scan reduce the likelihood of gallstone disease.
Elevated Bilirubin and Alkaline Phosphatase in Obstructive Jaundice
  • High levels of bilirubin and alkaline phosphatase indicate biliary obstruction or cholestasis, often due to stones or malignancy.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 70–80%, Specificity: 90–95% for biliary obstruction when combined with imaging findings.
  • Reference: NICE Clinical Guidelines for Gallstone Disease, 2021.
  • Significance: Elevated values confirm obstructive or hepatobiliary pathology. Normal levels make significant obstruction less likely.
CT Abdomen for Suspected Pancreatic or Gastric Malignancy
  • CT is highly sensitive for detecting structural abnormalities such as tumours, masses, or vascular complications.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 85–95%, Specificity: 90% for pancreatic cancer detection.
  • Reference: American Society of Clinical Oncology (ASCO) guidelines, 2020.
  • Significance: Positive findings identify malignancies or advanced disease. Negative results may require further diagnostic steps like MRI or biopsy.
ECG in Suspected Myocardial Ischaemia
  • An ECG is essential for identifying ischaemic changes that may present atypically as upper abdominal pain.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 75–80%, Specificity: 85%, LR+: 5.5 for ischaemia detection in acute chest pain.
  • Reference: European Society of Cardiology (ESC) guidelines for acute coronary syndromes, 2021.
  • Significance: Positive findings warrant urgent cardiology evaluation. A normal ECG reduces the likelihood of ischaemia but does not rule it out completely.
Diagnostic Timeout & Cognitive Biases

Awareness of common thinking errors is essential to improve diagnostic accuracy and patient safety when assessing a patient with upper abdominal pain. Before finalising a diagnosis, it is good practice for clinicians at all levels to take a 'diagnostic timeout' to challenge their own reasoning.

  • Anchoring Bias: This is the tendency to lock onto initial features or a known diagnosis (e.g., a patient's history of GORD) and fail to consider other possibilities, even when new evidence emerges. A patient with known gallstones may present with upper abdominal pain, but this could be pancreatitis, cholangitis, or an unrelated MI, not just simple biliary colic. A key question to ask is: "What is the most dangerous thing this could be, and have I confidently excluded it?"
  • Confirmation Bias: This involves seeking out or overvaluing evidence that supports an initial belief while ignoring contradictory findings. For example, focusing on a history of alcohol intake to confirm pancreatitis while downplaying the significance of weight loss, which could point towards malignancy.
  • Atypical Presentations: This is a major pitfall. Forgetting that serious diseases can present non-classically is dangerous. Myocardial infarction can present solely as epigastric pain with nausea, especially in women, the elderly, or those with diabetes. A high index of suspicion must be maintained in these groups.
  • Over-reliance on Normal Tests: Placing too much weight on a single reassuring test result can be misleading. A normal serum amylase does not rule out acute pancreatitis (lipase is more specific and stays elevated for longer). Similarly, an initial normal abdominal ultrasound is excellent for gallstones in the gallbladder but can miss stones in the common bile duct.
Illustrative Management Pathways

For educational purposes, this section describes the typical principles of management for key diagnoses. These are not prescriptive instructions. All management must be guided by official UK guidelines, local formularies, and senior clinical advice.

Acute Pancreatitis

Management is primarily supportive and takes place in a hospital setting. The core principles involve aggressive intravenous fluid resuscitation to maintain organ perfusion, adequate analgesia (often requiring opiates), and nutritional support (enteral feeding is preferred over parenteral if the gut is functional). The underlying cause must be identified and treated; for example, gallstone-induced pancreatitis may require an urgent ERCP (Endoscopic Retrograde Cholangiopancreatography) to remove an obstructing stone.

Acute Cholecystitis

Patients with suspected acute cholecystitis require hospital admission for initial management. This includes intravenous antibiotics to treat the secondary infection, analgesia, and intravenous fluids. The definitive treatment is surgery. According to NICE guidelines, an early laparoscopic cholecystectomy (gallbladder removal), ideally within one week of diagnosis, is recommended to prevent recurrent episodes and complications.

Peptic Ulcer Disease

Management depends on the cause. If Helicobacter pylori infection is confirmed (via a stool antigen test, breath test, or biopsy), the mainstay of treatment is "triple therapy" eradication, typically comprising a proton pump inhibitor (PPI) and two antibiotics (e.g., amoxicillin and clarithromycin) for seven days. If the ulcer is related to NSAID use, the offending drug should be stopped, and a PPI prescribed to allow healing. Lifestyle advice on smoking cessation and alcohol reduction is also important.

Benign Causes (e.g., GORD, Functional Dyspepsia)

Once life-threatening causes have been confidently excluded, management focuses on reassurance and symptomatic relief. For suspected GORD or functional dyspepsia, a trial of a proton pump inhibitor (PPI) is a standard approach. This is coupled with lifestyle advice, including weight management, avoiding trigger foods (e.g., spicy or fatty foods, caffeine), smoking cessation, and reducing alcohol intake. If symptoms persist despite a trial of PPIs, further investigation such as an upper GI endoscopy may be warranted.

Reflection & Learning

These brief cases illustrate the application of clinical reasoning to different upper abdominal pain presentations. They are for educational purposes only.

Case 1: Suspected Acute Pancreatitis

A 55-year-old man with a history of alcohol excess presents to the Emergency Department with a 6-hour history of severe, constant central upper abdominal pain that radiates directly through to his back. He feels nauseous and has vomited several times. On examination, he is tachycardic and has marked epigastric tenderness. The problem representation is a middle-aged man with a key risk factor (alcohol) presenting with classic symptoms of acute pancreatitis. The immediate pathway involves securing IV access, administering fluids and analgesia, and sending bloods including FBC, U&Es, LFTs, CRP, and a serum lipase to confirm the diagnosis.

Case 2: Suspected Biliary Colic vs. Cholecystitis

A 48-year-old woman with a BMI of 32 presents to her GP. For the last few months, she has had intermittent episodes of severe, constant pain in her right upper quadrant after eating a large or fatty meal, lasting 2-3 hours. Today, the pain started 5 hours ago and is not subsiding; she also feels hot and shivery. On examination, she has a temperature of 37.9°C and is tender in the RUQ with a positive Murphy's sign. The problem representation is a patient with risk factors for gallstones whose symptoms have progressed from likely biliary colic to acute cholecystitis. The GP arranges for immediate hospital admission for blood tests, intravenous antibiotics, and an urgent surgical review.

Case 3: Dyspepsia with Red Flags

A 67-year-old man presents to his GP with a 3-month history of worsening heartburn and epigastric pain. He has been self-treating with over-the-counter antacids with minimal effect. When asked directly, he admits to food "getting stuck" occasionally when he swallows and thinks he has lost a bit of weight without trying. The problem representation is an older patient with persistent dyspepsia and new-onset "red flag" symptoms (dysphagia and weight loss), which are highly concerning for an oesophago-gastric malignancy. The management pathway is an urgent direct-access upper GI endoscopy, referred under the '2-week wait' cancer pathway in the UK.
