Dyspepsia Diagnostic Reasoning Reference
Context: Dyspepsia Assessment
Overview & Links to Key Guidelines

Dyspepsia, a syndrome of epigastric pain or discomfort, is one of the most common presentations in both primary care and gastroenterology. The symptoms, which include heartburn, bloating, early satiety, and nausea, affect a significant portion of the adult population. The core challenge for the clinician is to differentiate common, benign causes like functional dyspepsia or gastro-oesophageal reflux disease (GORD) from serious underlying pathology, most notably peptic ulcer disease and upper gastrointestinal malignancy.

A structured and systematic approach is essential to make an accurate diagnosis, provide effective relief, and ensure that 'red flag' symptoms prompting urgent investigation are not missed. This educational module provides a foundational framework for the clinical reasoning process in patients presenting with dyspepsia. For definitive, real-time management protocols, users must refer to official UK guidelines from authoritative bodies such as NICE.

  • NICE Guideline [CG184]: Gastro-oesophageal reflux disease and dyspepsia in adults: investigation and management.
  • NICE Guideline [NG12]: Suspected cancer: recognition and referral.
Pathophysiology/Basic Science

Understanding the origin of dyspeptic symptoms is key to forming a differential diagnosis. Pain and discomfort can be generated through several distinct mechanisms:

  • Mucosal Damage and Inflammation: This is the classic mechanism for conditions like GORD and Peptic Ulcer Disease. The protective mucosal layer of the oesophagus or stomach is compromised, allowing gastric acid and pepsin to irritate and erode the underlying tissue. In GORD, this is due to reflux of acidic stomach contents into the oesophagus. In Peptic Ulcer Disease, the primary drivers are often Helicobacter pylori infection, which disrupts the protective mucus layer, and Non-Steroidal Anti-Inflammatory Drugs (NSAIDs), which inhibit prostaglandin synthesis, reducing mucus and bicarbonate production.
  • Visceral Hypersensitivity: A key mechanism thought to underlie Functional Dyspepsia. In this condition, sensory nerves in the stomach and duodenum become over-sensitive to normal physiological stimuli, such as gastric distension after a meal or the presence of acid. This results in the perception of pain or discomfort from stimuli that would not be noticed by a healthy individual.
  • Gastroduodenal Motor Dysfunction: Also central to Functional Dyspepsia, this involves abnormalities in the physical movement of the stomach. This can manifest as delayed gastric emptying (gastroparesis), where food remains in the stomach for longer than normal, leading to prolonged fullness and bloating. Alternatively, it can involve impaired gastric accommodation, where the upper part of the stomach fails to relax properly to receive a meal, causing early satiety.
  • Biliary and Pancreatic Pain: Gallstone disease can cause dyspepsia-like symptoms when a stone temporarily obstructs the cystic duct or common bile duct, typically leading to severe, constant epigastric or right upper quadrant pain as the gallbladder contracts against the obstruction. Acute pancreatitis causes severe epigastric pain, often radiating to the back, due to autodigestion and inflammation of the pancreas by activated digestive enzymes.
  • Malignant Infiltration: In oesophageal, gastric, or pancreatic cancer, a growing tumour can cause symptoms through several mechanisms. It can physically obstruct the passage of food (causing dysphagia or vomiting), ulcerate and bleed (causing anaemia), or invade local nerves and structures (causing persistent, severe pain and weight loss).
Differential Diagnosis (DDx) Framework

A structured approach to the differential diagnosis of dyspepsia is essential to ensure that common benign conditions are managed appropriately while potentially life-threatening causes are identified promptly.

Must not miss!
  • Angina: Can present as epigastric discomfort, particularly on exertion, which may be mistaken for heartburn.
  • Myocardial Infarction: A life-threatening emergency which can present atypically with epigastric pain and nausea, rather than classic chest pain.
  • Oesophageal cancer: Presents with progressive difficulty swallowing (dysphagia) and often associated with weight loss and persistent dyspepsia.
  • Gastric cancer: May present with persistent epigastric pain, unexplained weight loss, anaemia, and early satiety.
  • Pancreatic Cancer: Often presents insidiously with vague upper abdominal pain radiating to the back, profound weight loss, and sometimes jaundice.
  • Acute pancreatitis: Characterised by a sudden onset of severe, constant epigastric pain often radiating to the back, associated with nausea and vomiting.
  • Cholecystitis: Inflammation of the gallbladder causing severe, constant right upper quadrant or epigastric pain, often with fever and localised tenderness.
  • Cholangitis: A potentially life-threatening infection of the bile ducts, presenting with fever, jaundice, and right upper quadrant pain (Charcot's triad).
  • Intestinal obstruction: Presents with colicky abdominal pain, vomiting (which may be bilious or faeculent), abdominal distension, and constipation.
Common causes
  • GORD: A very common cause of burning retrosternal pain (heartburn) and acid regurgitation, often worse after meals or on lying down.
  • Non-ulcer dyspepsia: Also known as functional dyspepsia, a diagnosis of exclusion characterised by dyspeptic symptoms without an identifiable organic cause.
  • Peptic Ulcer Disease: Presents with a localised, often gnawing or burning, epigastric pain that may be relieved by food or antacids.
  • Gallstone disease: Can cause severe, episodic pain in the epigastrium or right upper quadrant, typically after fatty meals (biliary colic).
  • COVID-19: Can present with a range of gastrointestinal symptoms, including loss of appetite, nausea, and dyspepsia-like discomfort.
Easily missed
  • Medication-induced dyspepsia: Commonly caused by drugs like NSAIDs, bisphosphonates, steroids, and certain antibiotics which irritate the gastric mucosa.
  • Alcohol abuse: Excessive alcohol intake is a frequent cause of gastritis and can exacerbate GORD and peptic ulcer disease.
  • Food allergy or intolerance: Conditions like lactose intolerance or other food sensitivities can cause bloating, pain, and dyspeptic symptoms.
  • Diabetic Gastroparesis: A complication of diabetes causing delayed gastric emptying, leading to early satiety, bloating, and vomiting.
Other Differential Diagnoses to consider
  • COVID-19: Can present with atypical GI symptoms, including nausea, dyspepsia, and loss of appetite
  • H. pylori infection: Chronic infection linked with dyspepsia and peptic ulcer disease
  • Functional dyspepsia: A common, idiopathic GI disorder with no underlying structural disease
  • Zollinger-Ellison syndrome: Gastrin-secreting tumour leading to excessive stomach acid production and peptic ulcers
  • Hypercalcaemia: Elevated calcium levels may cause nausea and dyspepsia symptoms
  • Chronic kidney disease: Can cause uraemic gastritis, leading to dyspepsia
  • Coeliac disease: Gluten intolerance often presents with dyspepsia among other GI symptoms
  • Anxiety or stress: Psychological factors can significantly contribute to GI distress, including dyspepsia
History Taking
**Patient’s Description**: How can I help you today?
Starting with an open-ended question allows the patient to describe their symptoms in their own words, which can guide further targeted questions. Relevance: Provides insight into the patient’s primary concerns, enabling a tailored history-taking approach.
Can you describe the sensation (e.g., burning, pressure, discomfort)?
  • Understanding the sensation type helps differentiate GORD, which is often burning, from peptic ulcer pain, which may be gnawing or dull.
  • Relevance: Burning suggests GORD, while a gnawing or dull pain may point towards peptic ulcer disease or gallstone issues.
When did it start, and has it changed over time?
  • Assessing onset and progression helps determine whether this is an acute or chronic issue. Chronicity may suggest functional dyspepsia or GORD, while sudden onset raises concerns for acute gastritis or pancreatitis.
  • Relevance: A recent onset suggests acute pathology like acute gastritis, while longstanding symptoms may indicate GORD or functional dyspepsia.
Are there any specific triggers or factors that worsen or relieve the symptoms?
  • Identifying triggers can help determine if symptoms are related to food, position, or medications. GORD symptoms often worsen after meals or with recumbency, while ulcers may be relieved by eating.
  • Relevance: Worsening after meals suggests gallbladder pathology, while relief with food suggests peptic ulcer disease.
**Associated Symptoms**
  • Asking about nausea, vomiting, weight loss, and other associated symptoms helps narrow the differential.
  • Relevance: Weight loss could indicate malignancy, while nausea and vomiting may point to gastritis or gastroparesis.
**Ideas, Concerns, and Expectations (ICE)**
  • ICE questions help ensure that the consultation addresses the patient’s perspective, any worries they may have, and their treatment expectations.
  • Relevance: Enables identification of specific concerns, such as fear of cancer, and clarifies what the patient hopes for in terms of symptom management or further testing.
Examination Strategy
General Observation and Vital Signs
  • Observe for signs of distress, pallor, jaundice, or cachexia. Check for fever, tachycardia, hypotension, and other vital abnormalities.
  • Relevance: Pallor may suggest anaemia (e.g., from GI bleeding), jaundice could indicate hepatobiliary disease, and fever may be seen in infections such as cholangitis or pancreatitis. Abnormal vitals could indicate systemic involvement or shock.
Inspection of the Abdomen
  • Look for distention, visible masses, surgical scars, or pulsations, which may provide clues to underlying pathology.
  • Relevance: Abdominal distention could suggest intestinal obstruction, while visible masses may indicate malignancy. Surgical scars may hint at prior abdominal surgeries related to the current symptoms.
Palpation of the Abdomen
  • Assess for tenderness, rebound tenderness, guarding, or palpable masses.
  • Relevance: Epigastric tenderness could suggest gastritis, peptic ulcer disease, or pancreatitis. Right upper quadrant tenderness may indicate cholecystitis, while a palpable mass could suggest a malignancy or large gastric ulcer.
Percussion of the Abdomen
  • Check for dullness, especially in the flanks and lower abdomen, which could suggest ascites or mass effect.
  • Relevance: Ascites might be due to liver disease or malignancy, and dullness over an area may suggest a mass, such as a hepatomegaly or a large tumour.
Auscultation of the Abdomen
  • Listen for bowel sounds, noting whether they are normal, hypoactive, or hyperactive.
  • Relevance: Absent or hypoactive bowel sounds could indicate ileus or obstruction, while hyperactive sounds are often seen in gastroenteritis or partial obstruction.
Examination for Signs of Anaemia
  • Check for pallor in the conjunctiva and palmar creases.
  • Relevance: Pallor may suggest anaemia, which could result from chronic bleeding (e.g., from a gastric ulcer or malignancy).
Digital Rectal Examination (DRE)
  • Perform a DRE to check for melaena or rectal masses.
  • Relevance: Melaena can indicate upper GI bleeding, such as from a gastric ulcer, while rectal masses might suggest colorectal malignancy that could present with upper GI symptoms due to referred pain.
Red Flag Synthesis

This section synthesises the key 'red flag' findings from the source document. Any of these findings in a patient with dyspepsia should prompt consideration of urgent referral for investigation, typically an upper GI endoscopy, as per NICE guidelines.

Unintentional Weight Loss
  • Unintentional weight loss is a key indicator that there may be an underlying malignancy, especially in cases of gastric, oesophageal, or pancreatic cancer.
  • Significance: This finding necessitates urgent evaluation to rule out serious conditions such as GI malignancy.
Progressive Dysphagia (Difficulty Swallowing)
  • Progressive dysphagia suggests a possible obstructive lesion in the oesophagus, which could be due to oesophageal cancer.
  • Significance: This symptom requires prompt investigation as it may indicate a significant narrowing or obstruction in the oesophagus.
Persistent Vomiting
  • Ongoing vomiting, particularly if it is bilious or bloody, suggests severe obstruction or peptic ulcer complications.
  • Significance: Persistent vomiting could indicate gastric outlet obstruction, malignancy, or advanced peptic ulcer disease, all of which may need urgent intervention.
Melaena or Haematemesis
  • The presence of melaena (black, tarry stools) or haematemesis (vomiting blood) indicates upper GI bleeding, which could result from peptic ulcers or malignancy.
  • Significance: These symptoms warrant immediate investigation for potential sources of GI bleeding, as they may be life-threatening.
Anaemia (Iron Deficiency)
  • Iron deficiency anaemia, especially in older patients, can be a marker for chronic blood loss in the GI tract, possibly from a malignancy.
  • Significance: Anaemia with dyspepsia raises suspicion for malignancy and warrants thorough GI evaluation.
Epigastric Mass
  • An epigastric mass may represent a tumour, such as gastric or pancreatic cancer, which could be a serious cause of dyspepsia.
  • Significance: The finding of an abdominal mass requires urgent imaging and further investigation for malignancy.
Investigations Strategy & Interpretation
Full Blood Count (FBC)
  • A full blood count helps identify anaemia, which may suggest chronic bleeding or malabsorption associated with GI malignancies or peptic ulcer disease.
  • Result Significance: Low haemoglobin suggests anaemia, potentially indicating chronic GI blood loss or malignancy. Normal FBC does not rule out other causes of dyspepsia but may lower suspicion for severe anaemia-linked conditions.
Urea and Electrolytes (U&Es)
  • Evaluates kidney function, which can be affected by dehydration due to vomiting or underlying conditions like chronic kidney disease, a potential cause of uraemic gastritis.
  • Result Significance: Abnormal U&Es could indicate kidney impairment, especially relevant if dyspepsia is accompanied by nausea or uraemic symptoms.
Liver Function Tests (LFTs)
  • LFTs assess liver health, which is relevant in dyspepsia cases where hepatobiliary diseases like gallstones or cholangitis are suspected.
  • Result Significance: Elevated liver enzymes may indicate hepatobiliary disease, while normal LFTs can help rule out liver-related causes of dyspepsia.
Helicobacter pylori Testing (Stool antigen, Urea breath test)
  • Helicobacter pylori infection is a major cause of peptic ulcer disease and can exacerbate dyspeptic symptoms.
  • Result Significance: A positive result indicates H. pylori infection, supporting a diagnosis of peptic ulcer disease, while a negative result lowers suspicion for H. pylori as a cause.
Upper Gastrointestinal Endoscopy (OGD)
  • An endoscopy is essential for visualising the upper GI tract, especially when dyspepsia is associated with red flags such as weight loss, anaemia, or dysphagia.
  • Result Significance: Positive findings may include ulcers, malignancy, or oesophagitis. A normal result may suggest functional dyspepsia if red flags are absent.
Abdominal Ultrasound
  • Ultrasound is useful for evaluating gallbladder and liver health, especially in patients presenting with right upper quadrant pain or suspected biliary disease.
  • Result Significance: Positive findings of gallstones or biliary obstruction suggest gallbladder disease, while a normal scan can help exclude these conditions.
CT Abdomen
  • A CT scan is indicated if malignancy or complex pathology is suspected, especially in cases of significant weight loss or unexplained abdominal pain.
  • Result Significance: Positive findings could include tumours, lymphadenopathy, or advanced disease, warranting further intervention. A negative result may reduce suspicion of malignancy.
Calcium Levels
  • Hypercalcaemia may cause dyspeptic symptoms and is associated with parathyroid disorders or malignancy.
  • Result Significance: Elevated calcium levels support hypercalcaemia as a possible cause of symptoms, while normal levels make it less likely.
Evidence-Based Diagnosis (EBDx)
Unintentional Weight Loss as a Red Flag
  • Unintentional weight loss in patients with dyspepsia has a high association with underlying malignancies, especially in patients over 55. Weight loss also increases the likelihood of organic pathology.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 53%, Specificity: 80%, Positive Likelihood Ratio (LR+): 2.65 for detecting GI malignancy.
  • Significance: Positive findings strongly suggest a need for upper GI endoscopy to evaluate for malignancy, especially when coupled with other red flag symptoms.
Helicobacter pylori Testing
  • Helicobacter pylori infection is a major cause of peptic ulcer disease and is associated with dyspeptic symptoms in a significant number of cases.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 94%, Specificity: 88% for urea breath test.
  • Significance: Positive H. pylori findings suggest a likely bacterial cause for symptoms, supporting eradication therapy. A negative result reduces the likelihood of H. pylori as an underlying cause.
Upper Gastrointestinal Endoscopy (OGD) in Persistent Dyspepsia with Red Flags
  • Endoscopy is highly effective in diagnosing structural causes of dyspepsia, especially in the presence of alarm features such as weight loss, persistent vomiting, and anaemia.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 95%, Specificity: 90% for upper GI malignancies.
  • Significance: Positive findings from OGD confirm structural pathology, while negative results in the absence of other alarming features may suggest functional dyspepsia.
Iron Deficiency Anaemia
  • Iron deficiency anaemia is often associated with GI bleeding, especially in older patients presenting with new-onset dyspepsia.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 50%, Specificity: 85% for malignancy detection.
  • Significance: The presence of iron deficiency anaemia in dyspeptic patients necessitates evaluation for GI bleeding sources, including malignancies.
Right Upper Quadrant Pain in Dyspepsia
  • Right upper quadrant pain, particularly post-prandial, is a significant indicator of gallbladder disease, such as cholelithiasis or cholecystitis, as a cause of dyspepsia.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 75%, Specificity: 92% for gallbladder pathology.
  • Significance: Positive findings suggest gallbladder disease, and patients may benefit from imaging studies like abdominal ultrasound for confirmation.
Abdominal Ultrasound for Hepatobiliary Disease
  • An abdominal ultrasound is highly specific for gallstones and biliary pathology, often causing dyspeptic symptoms in the right upper quadrant or epigastrium.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 84%, Specificity: 99% for detecting gallstones.
  • Significance: Positive findings confirm gallstone disease or biliary obstruction, while a negative scan significantly reduces the likelihood of hepatobiliary causes for dyspepsia.
Diagnostic Timeout & Cognitive Biases

Awareness of common thinking errors is essential to improve diagnostic accuracy and patient safety when assessing a patient with dyspepsia. Before finalising a diagnosis, it is good practice for clinicians at all levels to take a 'diagnostic timeout' to challenge their own reasoning.

  • Anchoring Bias: The tendency to anchor on a patient's long-standing history of GORD or "IBS" and attribute new or worsening symptoms to the known condition. This can lead to a failure to consider a new, more sinister pathology like gastric cancer, especially if the character of the pain changes or red flags develop. A key question is: "Could this be something other than their usual reflux?"
  • Confirmation Bias: Selectively focusing on features that support a benign diagnosis (e.g., patient stress, dietary indiscretion) while downplaying more concerning evidence, such as a subtle but progressive weight loss or a new iron deficiency. This can reinforce a diagnosis of functional dyspepsia while missing an underlying malignancy.
  • Atypical Presentations: A major pitfall is forgetting that other serious diseases can manifest as dyspepsia. The most critical example is Acute Coronary Syndrome (ACS), where inferior myocardial infarction can present solely as epigastric pain and nausea, without any chest pain. Always consider cardiac causes in patients with relevant risk factors.
  • Premature Closure: Deciding on a diagnosis too early in the process. For example, finding a patient is positive for H. pylori and stopping there, without considering that a patient can have both H. pylori and a separate pathology like oesophageal cancer. The presence of one finding does not exclude another, particularly if alarm symptoms persist after treatment.
Illustrative Management Pathways

For educational purposes, this section describes the typical principles of management for key diagnoses. These are not prescriptive instructions. All management must be guided by official UK guidelines (e.g., NICE CG184), local formularies, and senior clinical advice.

Uninvestigated Dyspepsia (No Red Flags)

In patients without alarm features, the initial approach in primary care is guided by NICE. This involves reviewing medications for potential causes (e.g., NSAIDs). The next step is a "test-and-treat" strategy for Helicobacter pylori using a non-invasive test (urea breath test or stool antigen test). If positive, eradication therapy is offered. If negative, or if symptoms persist after eradication, a trial of a full-dose Proton Pump Inhibitor (PPI) for four weeks is recommended. Lifestyle advice on diet, alcohol, and weight management is a cornerstone of management throughout.

Gastro-Oesophageal Reflux Disease (GORD)

Management focuses on symptom control. This begins with lifestyle modifications (weight loss, elevating the head of the bed, avoiding trigger foods). For symptom relief, alginates or antacids can be used. The mainstay of medical therapy is a four-to-eight-week course of a full-dose PPI. If symptoms recur after stopping treatment, a PPI can be used at the lowest effective dose as needed. Patients with persistent symptoms may require endoscopy to rule out complications like Barrett's oesophagus.

Peptic Ulcer Disease

Management depends on the underlying cause identified at endoscopy. If the patient is H. pylori positive, a one-week course of "triple therapy" (a PPI plus two antibiotics, e.g., clarithromycin and amoxicillin/metronidazole) is prescribed. If the ulcer is related to NSAID use, the NSAID should be stopped if possible, and a full-dose PPI is given for 4-8 weeks to allow healing. Repeat endoscopy may be required to confirm healing of gastric ulcers.

Dyspepsia with Alarm Symptoms

The presence of any red flag symptom (e.g., dysphagia, weight loss, melaena, persistent vomiting, iron deficiency anaemia, epigastric mass) mandates an urgent (two-week wait) referral for upper gastrointestinal endoscopy to exclude malignancy. Management should not be initiated in primary care that could mask symptoms (e.g., PPI trial) and delay diagnosis. The patient's subsequent management will be dictated entirely by the findings of the endoscopy and any biopsies taken.

Reflection & Learning

These brief cases illustrate the application of clinical reasoning to different dyspepsia presentations. They are for educational purposes only.

Case 1: Suspected GORD in Primary Care

A 42-year-old marketing manager presents to his GP with a 6-month history of burning central chest pain after meals, particularly spicy food and wine. It is worse at night when he lies down. He has no red flag symptoms. The problem representation is a man under 55 with classic symptoms of GORD and no alarm features. Following NICE guidance, after reviewing medications, he is offered a trial of a PPI for one month and given lifestyle advice. His symptoms resolve completely.

Case 2: New Onset Dyspepsia with Red Flags

A 68-year-old retired teacher presents with a 3-month history of new-onset epigastric pain. She feels full after eating only small amounts of food and thinks she has lost weight, although she hasn't weighed herself. A routine blood test shows a new iron deficiency anaemia. The problem representation is a patient over 55 presenting with new-onset dyspepsia and multiple alarm features (weight loss, early satiety, anaemia). This is highly concerning for an upper GI malignancy. The GP makes an urgent two-week wait referral for an OGD.

Case 3: Atypical Dyspepsia Suggesting Biliary Cause

A 50-year-old woman presents with recurrent, severe episodes of upper abdominal pain. The pain is constant, lasts for 2-3 hours, and is often triggered by eating a large or fatty meal. Between episodes, she feels well but has some mild bloating. She has tried over-the-counter antacids with no relief. The problem representation is episodic, severe, post-prandial epigastric pain, which is more characteristic of biliary colic than simple dyspepsia or GORD. The GP organises blood tests (including LFTs) and an abdominal ultrasound, which confirms the presence of gallstones.
