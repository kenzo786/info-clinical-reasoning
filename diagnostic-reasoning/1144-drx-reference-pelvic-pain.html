Pelvic Pain Diagnostic Reasoning Reference
Context: Pelvic Pain Assessment
Overview

Pelvic pain is a common and complex presentation in clinical practice, affecting a wide demographic and spanning a spectrum from benign physiological processes to life-threatening emergencies. The anatomical density of the female pelvis, housing gynaecological, urinary, and gastrointestinal organs in close proximity, creates a significant diagnostic challenge. The clinician's primary task is to systematically differentiate between these potential origins to ensure that critical diagnoses, such as Ectopic Pregnancy or Ovarian Torsion, are identified and managed urgently, while also providing appropriate care for chronic and less urgent conditions.

This educational module provides a structured framework for the clinical reasoning process in assessing a patient with pelvic pain. It is designed for educational purposes to build foundational knowledge. For definitive, real-time clinical management, users must consult official UK guidelines from authoritative bodies such as the National Institute for Health and Care Excellence (NICE) (e.g., NG122 for Endometriosis, NG108 for Ovarian Cancer) and the Royal College of Obstetricians and Gynaecologists (RCOG).

Pathophysiology/Basic Science

Understanding the underlying mechanism of pain generation in the pelvis is fundamental to formulating an accurate differential diagnosis. Pelvic pain can be classified based on its neurophysiological origin:

  • Visceral Pain: This arises from the pelvic organs themselves (uterus, ovaries, bladder, bowel). It is typically triggered by stretching of an organ's capsule (e.g., ovarian cyst, distended bladder), powerful smooth muscle contraction (e.g., uterine cramps in dysmenorrhoea, bowel colic in gastroenteritis), or ischaemia (e.g., ovarian torsion). Visceral pain is often mediated by autonomic nerve fibres, resulting in a characteristically diffuse, poorly-localised, dull, or cramping sensation, often perceived in the midline.
  • Somatic Pain: This type of pain originates from the parietal peritoneum, the lining of the abdominal and pelvic cavity. This lining is innervated by somatic nerves, which also supply the skin and muscles. When the peritoneum becomes inflamed due to infection (e.g., appendicitis, PID) or chemical irritation (e.g., blood from a ruptured ectopic pregnancy), the resulting pain is sharp, intense, and precisely localised to the area of inflammation. It is classically worsened by any movement that jostles the peritoneum, such as coughing or walking.
  • Inflammatory Pain: This is a key component in conditions like Pelvic Inflammatory Disease (PID), diverticulitis, and appendicitis. The release of inflammatory mediators (prostaglandins, bradykinin) directly stimulates nociceptors, leading to a persistent, often severe pain, frequently associated with systemic symptoms like fever.
  • Referred Pain: Pain that is perceived at a location distant from the site of pathology. A classic example in pelvic pathology is shoulder tip pain caused by diaphragmatic irritation from blood in the peritoneal cavity (e.g., from a ruptured ectopic pregnancy or ovarian cyst).
  • Neuropathic Pain: This arises from damage to or dysfunction of the nerves themselves. In the pelvic region, this can occur due to nerve entrapment (e.g., pudendal neuralgia) or chronic sensitisation from long-standing inflammatory conditions. The pain is often described as burning, shooting, or electric-shock-like.
Differential Diagnosis (DDx) Framework

A structured approach to the differential diagnosis of pelvic pain is essential to ensure life-threatening conditions are prioritised.

Must not miss!
  • Ectopic pregnancy: Typically presents in early pregnancy with abdominal pain and vaginal bleeding, a surgical and medical emergency.
  • Incarcerated hernia: A hernia that becomes trapped and cannot be reduced, potentially leading to strangulation and bowel obstruction.
  • Appendicitis: Classically causes periumbilical pain migrating to the right iliac fossa, with associated anorexia, nausea, and fever.
  • Colorectal cancer: May present with a persistent change in bowel habit, rectal bleeding, and abdominal pain, particularly in older adults.
  • Ovarian cancer: Often presents with vague, persistent symptoms like bloating, early satiety, and pelvic pain, especially in post-menopausal women.
  • Endometrial cancer: The hallmark symptom is post-menopausal bleeding, though pelvic pain can also be a feature.
  • Cervical cancer: May present with post-coital bleeding, intermenstrual bleeding, or abnormal vaginal discharge.
  • Pelvic Inflammatory Disease: An infection of the upper female genital tract, presenting with bilateral lower abdominal pain, deep dyspareunia, and abnormal discharge.
  • Pelvic abscess: A localised collection of pus in the pelvis, often a complication of PID or surgery, causing severe pain and systemic illness.
  • Diverticulitis: Inflammation of pouches in the colon, typically causing left iliac fossa pain, fever, and altered bowel habit.
  • Acute urinary retention: The sudden inability to pass urine, causing severe suprapubic pain and a palpable bladder.
  • Ovarian torsion: The twisting of an ovary on its ligamentous supports, causing sudden-onset, severe, unilateral pelvic pain, often with nausea and vomiting.
Common causes
  • UTI: A common infection causing dysuria, urinary frequency, urgency, and suprapubic pain.
  • Mittelschmerz: Mid-cycle, unilateral pelvic pain associated with ovulation, which is typically self-limiting.
  • Threatened Miscarriage: Presents with vaginal bleeding and cramping abdominal pain during early pregnancy, with a closed cervix.
  • Ovarian cyst: Can cause a dull ache, but may cause severe pain if it ruptures or bleeds.
  • Endometriosis: A chronic condition causing cyclical pelvic pain, dysmenorrhoea, dyspareunia, and potential subfertility.
  • Chronic Pelvic Pain: Persistent or recurrent pelvic pain lasting for more than six months, with a wide range of potential causes.
  • Constipation: A common cause of lower abdominal discomfort, often described as colicky and relieved by defecation.
  • Irritable bowel syndrome: A functional bowel disorder characterised by abdominal pain, bloating, and altered bowel habit.
  • MSK Sprains and strains: Musculoskeletal pain from the abdominal wall, back, or pelvic floor muscles, often related to activity.
  • Incorrectly sited IUCD: An intrauterine contraceptive device that has migrated can cause pain and abnormal bleeding.
  • Renal colic (Urinary tract stone): Severe, intermittent loin-to-groin pain caused by a stone passing down the ureter.
  • Psychogenic: Pelvic pain that is influenced by or is a manifestation of psychological factors.
  • COVID-19: Can present with a wide range of symptoms, including gastrointestinal or systemic symptoms that may manifest as pelvic pain.
Easily missed
  • Crohn's disease: A type of inflammatory bowel disease that can cause chronic abdominal pain, diarrhoea, and weight loss.
  • Ulcerative colitis: Another form of inflammatory bowel disease, primarily causing bloody diarrhoea and lower abdominal cramping.
Other Differential Diagnoses to consider
  • COVID-19: Unusual presentations may include systemic symptoms or GI manifestations.
  • Pelvic Congestion Syndrome: Chronic pelvic pain due to venous insufficiency, often exacerbated by prolonged standing.
  • Primary Dysmenorrhoea: Cyclic pelvic pain linked to menstruation, common in younger women.
  • Abdominal Aortic Aneurysm (AAA): Rare, but pelvic pain can occur due to referred pain; important in older patients.
  • Neuropathic Pain (e.g., Pudendal Neuralgia): Chronic pain from nerve entrapment or irritation.
  • Pelvic Floor Dysfunction: May present with nonspecific pelvic pain, often linked to muscle spasm or imbalance.
  • Haemorrhoids: Pelvic pain can occasionally be referred, especially with external thrombosis.
  • Prostatitis: Can cause pelvic pain, especially in male patients with genitourinary symptoms.
History Taking
**Patient’s Description**: How can I help you today?
Asking an open-ended question such as, “Can you tell me more about what brought you here today?” allows the patient to share their perspective on the presenting symptom without restriction. Relevance: This approach provides valuable insight into the patient's primary concerns and unique experience with pelvic pain, helping the clinician determine appropriate follow-up questions.
Can you describe the pain? (Quality, type)
  • Asking the patient to describe the quality of the pain (e.g., sharp, dull, cramping, throbbing) may give clues about its origin. For instance, cramping pain may suggest gynaecological causes such as dysmenorrhoea or endometriosis, while sharp, intermittent pain could suggest renal colic.
  • Relevance: Sharp or stabbing pain might suggest renal or musculoskeletal causes, whereas dull or cramping pain is often linked to GI or gynaecological origins.
When did the pain start, and has it changed over time? (Onset, duration, progression)
  • Determining the onset, duration, and progression can help narrow down acute versus chronic causes. Sudden, intense pain suggests conditions like ectopic pregnancy or ovarian torsion, while chronic, intermittent pain may point to IBS or endometriosis.
  • Relevance: Acute onset may indicate emergencies like ovarian torsion or appendicitis, while gradual onset with periodic exacerbations suggests chronic issues such as endometriosis or IBS.
Are there any specific triggers or factors that make the pain better or worse?
  • Asking about triggers or relieving factors (e.g., relation to menstrual cycle, eating, movement) may help distinguish between causes. For example, IBS pain may worsen after meals, while endometriosis pain often worsens around menstruation.
  • Relevance: Worsening pain with food intake suggests GI involvement, whereas worsening with movement may indicate musculoskeletal causes.
Have you noticed any other symptoms along with the pain? (Associated Symptoms)
  • Asking about symptoms such as nausea, fever, vaginal bleeding, urinary issues, or GI disturbances can guide the differential diagnosis. For instance, fever may suggest infection (e.g., PID), while abnormal vaginal bleeding can raise suspicion of gynaecological pathology like miscarriage or ectopic pregnancy.
  • Relevance: Positive response can point to specific diagnoses (e.g., urinary symptoms suggest UTI or renal colic, while fever and chills suggest PID or abscess).
Do you have a history of gynaecological issues or surgeries?
  • Prior gynaecological history, such as ovarian cysts, endometriosis, or surgeries, may predispose the patient to recurring or specific types of pelvic pain.
  • Relevance: Positive history may point towards a recurrence of a known gynaecological issue, while a lack of history could suggest a new pathology.
Do you have any personal or family history of GI, urinary, or gynaecological cancers?
  • Personal or family history of cancer (e.g., colorectal, ovarian, cervical) is significant as it raises the risk for malignancies, which may present as pelvic pain.
  • Relevance: A positive history could indicate higher suspicion for cancers as a cause, while a negative history decreases the likelihood.
Are you currently taking any medications, including hormonal therapies or IUD?
  • Medications, including hormonal therapies or IUD placement, can influence pelvic pain. For instance, IUDs may lead to improper positioning pain, while hormonal therapy can cause changes in menstrual patterns.
  • Relevance: Presence of an IUD or use of hormones may guide towards issues such as IUD malposition or hormonal side effects, while a lack of these factors may indicate other causes.
**Ideas, Concerns, and Expectations (ICE)**
  • Asking the patient for their ideas, concerns, and expectations allows the clinician to gauge the patient's perspective, any underlying fears (e.g., cancer, infertility), and their hopes for the outcome of the consultation.
  • Relevance: This approach ensures the patient feels heard and that the consultation aligns with their values and concerns, such as fear of serious conditions (e.g., cancer, ectopic pregnancy).
Examination Strategy
General Inspection
  • Observe the patient’s general appearance, posture, and any signs of distress, which can indicate acute pathology. Look for pallor, which may suggest anaemia or blood loss, or signs of systemic illness such as fever or cachexia, which could indicate chronic disease or malignancy.
  • Relevance: Signs of distress and pallor may suggest an urgent or serious condition such as ectopic pregnancy or malignancy, while their absence can make acute, severe conditions less likely.
Vital Signs
  • Measure temperature, heart rate, respiratory rate, and blood pressure. Fever may indicate infection (e.g., PID), while hypotension with tachycardia could suggest hypovolaemia, possibly from internal bleeding (e.g., ectopic pregnancy).
  • Relevance: Tachycardia and hypotension suggest haemodynamic instability, which could be due to severe infection or ectopic pregnancy. A normal vital sign reading may reduce the likelihood of these urgent causes.
Abdominal Examination - Inspection
  • Look for visible distension, masses, or skin changes. Distension can suggest gastrointestinal causes like bowel obstruction, while visible masses may indicate an abdominal or pelvic mass such as an ovarian cyst or tumour.
  • Relevance: Visible mass may suggest malignancy or an ovarian cyst, while absence of these signs may reduce the likelihood of abdominal masses.
Abdominal Examination - Palpation
  • Palpate the abdomen systematically to assess for tenderness, rebound tenderness, guarding, or masses. Localised tenderness may point to appendicitis (right lower quadrant), diverticulitis (left lower quadrant), or ovarian torsion. Guarding and rebound tenderness suggest peritoneal irritation, often seen in peritonitis.
  • Relevance: Localised tenderness with guarding or rebound indicates possible inflammation or infection such as appendicitis or ovarian torsion, whereas an absence of tenderness may lower suspicion for these conditions.
Abdominal Examination - Percussion
  • Assess for signs of ascites or shifting dullness, which may indicate malignancy or advanced liver disease. Tympany may be found in bowel obstruction.
  • Relevance: Dullness suggests fluid accumulation, which may indicate malignancy or inflammatory processes, while tympany may suggest bowel distension.
Abdominal Examination - Auscultation
  • Listen for bowel sounds. Hyperactive sounds may indicate bowel obstruction, while absent sounds suggest peritonitis or ileus.
  • Relevance: Hyperactive sounds support a GI cause like obstruction, whereas absent sounds may suggest severe inflammation or infection.
Pelvic Examination - External Inspection
  • Look for signs of vulval or vaginal inflammation, discharge, or lesions, which could suggest infection, abscess, or trauma. Genital lesions may suggest sexually transmitted infections contributing to pelvic pain.
  • Relevance: Signs of inflammation or lesions raise suspicion for PID or STI, while the absence of these signs makes infectious causes less likely.
Pelvic Examination - Bimanual Palpation
  • Assess the uterus and adnexa for tenderness, masses, or abnormalities. Cervical motion tenderness is indicative of PID, while adnexal tenderness and mass may indicate ectopic pregnancy, ovarian torsion, or cyst.
  • Relevance: Cervical motion tenderness suggests PID; adnexal mass with tenderness may indicate ovarian torsion or ectopic pregnancy. Absence of these findings lowers suspicion for these conditions.
Rectal Examination (if indicated)
  • In cases of significant pelvic or lower abdominal pain, a rectal examination may help to evaluate for masses, tenderness, or rectal bleeding, which could suggest GI causes such as colorectal cancer or diverticulitis.
  • Relevance: Positive findings of rectal tenderness or mass may indicate diverticulitis or colorectal malignancy, while a negative examination can reduce the likelihood of these conditions.
Red Flag Synthesis
Severe, sudden onset pelvic pain
  • Sudden, severe pelvic pain may indicate a life-threatening emergency such as ectopic pregnancy, ovarian torsion, or ruptured ovarian cyst.
  • Significance: This could indicate ectopic pregnancy, ovarian torsion, or ruptured cyst, all of which require immediate intervention to prevent complications.
Unexplained weight loss and fatigue
  • Unintentional weight loss and fatigue, especially when associated with pelvic pain, could suggest malignancies such as ovarian or colorectal cancer.
  • Significance: This may indicate underlying malignancy, including ovarian or colorectal cancer, necessitating urgent diagnostic evaluation.
Fever with pelvic pain
  • Fever alongside pelvic pain suggests infection, potentially PID, pelvic abscess, or peritonitis, which can progress quickly if untreated.
  • Significance: This may indicate severe infection such as PID, pelvic abscess, or peritonitis, which requires prompt antibiotic therapy or surgical intervention.
Heavy vaginal bleeding
  • Significant vaginal bleeding can suggest conditions like miscarriage, ectopic pregnancy, or malignancy, which are potentially life-threatening if not managed swiftly.
  • Significance: This could indicate ectopic pregnancy, miscarriage, or gynaecological cancer, all requiring urgent attention.
Persistent change in bowel or urinary habits
  • Changes such as new-onset constipation, diarrhoea, urinary frequency, or dysuria may indicate pelvic or abdominal malignancy, especially in the context of pelvic pain.
  • Significance: This could signify colorectal or bladder cancer, warranting further investigation.
Abnormal vaginal discharge
  • Purulent or malodorous discharge with pelvic pain could suggest a severe infection, such as PID, which can lead to infertility if untreated.
  • Significance: This may indicate PID or other infections, necessitating prompt treatment to prevent complications like infertility.
Rebound tenderness on abdominal examination
  • Rebound tenderness indicates peritoneal irritation, often due to acute conditions like appendicitis or peritonitis, both of which can deteriorate rapidly.
  • Significance: This could signify peritonitis or appendicitis, requiring urgent surgical intervention.
Investigations Strategy & Interpretation
Urine Pregnancy Test
  • This is a crucial first-line test in women of childbearing age presenting with pelvic pain, as it helps identify or rule out pregnancy-related causes such as ectopic pregnancy.
  • Result Significance: A positive result strongly suggests pregnancy and raises suspicion for ectopic pregnancy in the context of pelvic pain. A negative result, while it does not entirely rule out ectopic pregnancy, reduces the likelihood.
Complete Blood Count (CBC)
  • Assesses for signs of infection (e.g., elevated white cell count) or anaemia, which may indicate blood loss from causes such as ectopic pregnancy or malignancy.
  • Result Significance: Elevated white cell count suggests infection or inflammation, while a low haemoglobin level may indicate blood loss, pointing towards conditions like ectopic pregnancy or GI bleeding.
Urinalysis and Urine Culture
  • Used to assess for urinary tract infections, which are common causes of pelvic pain. Urinalysis can also help identify renal stones or signs of haematuria.
  • Result Significance: Positive findings for nitrites or leukocytes suggest UTI, while haematuria could indicate renal stones or malignancy. A negative result makes a urinary cause less likely.
Pelvic Ultrasound
  • A non-invasive imaging modality ideal for evaluating gynaecological pathology such as ovarian cysts, fibroids, or ectopic pregnancy, as well as renal pathology.
  • Result Significance: Positive findings, such as adnexal masses or free fluid, may indicate ectopic pregnancy, ovarian cysts, or torsion. A normal result can help rule out significant structural causes of pelvic pain.
STI Screening (Chlamydia and Gonorrhoea NAAT)
  • Screening for sexually transmitted infections is essential in patients at risk for PID, which can present with pelvic pain. NAAT (nucleic acid amplification tests) are highly sensitive for detecting chlamydia and gonorrhoea.
  • Result Significance: Positive results confirm an STI diagnosis, pointing towards PID as a likely cause. Negative results do not exclude PID but reduce the likelihood of STI-related causes.
CA-125 Blood Test
  • This biomarker may be elevated in ovarian cancer and other gynaecological conditions. While not diagnostic, it can guide further investigation if elevated, especially in post-menopausal patients with pelvic pain.
  • Result Significance: An elevated CA-125 level raises suspicion for ovarian cancer but is not definitive, as it may also be elevated in benign conditions. A normal level is reassuring but not definitive for ruling out malignancy.
Abdominal and Pelvic CT Scan
  • CT imaging provides detailed information on abdominal and pelvic organs, useful in cases where ultrasound findings are inconclusive or malignancy is suspected.
  • Result Significance: Findings such as a pelvic mass, thickening, or enlarged lymph nodes may suggest malignancy or advanced infection. A negative result can help rule out significant anatomical pathology.
Endometrial Biopsy
  • In post-menopausal women with unexplained pelvic pain and abnormal bleeding, endometrial biopsy can help assess for endometrial cancer.
  • Result Significance: A positive finding of abnormal or cancerous cells suggests endometrial malignancy, warranting further oncological work-up. A negative result reduces this likelihood.
Evidence-Based Diagnosis (EBDx)
Sudden onset, severe pelvic pain
  • Sudden severe pelvic pain can indicate urgent conditions such as ectopic pregnancy, ovarian torsion, or ruptured ovarian cyst. Ectopic pregnancy specifically has a high risk of haemorrhage and requires immediate intervention.
  • Evidence: Positive likelihood ratio (LR+) for ectopic pregnancy when sudden pain is present: 7.6.
  • Reference: American College of Obstetricians and Gynecologists (ACOG) 2018 guidelines on ectopic pregnancy management.
  • Significance: A sudden, severe onset of pelvic pain significantly increases the probability of a life-threatening diagnosis such as ectopic pregnancy, requiring immediate further testing.
Urine Pregnancy Test (UPT)
  • The UPT is a highly sensitive and specific test for pregnancy detection, important in women presenting with pelvic pain. Ectopic pregnancy often presents with a positive UPT and is a high-priority diagnosis to consider.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 99%, Specificity: 99% for detecting pregnancy.
  • Reference: National Institute for Health and Care Excellence (NICE) guidelines on ectopic pregnancy and miscarriage (2021).
  • Significance: A positive UPT confirms pregnancy, and in the context of pelvic pain, raises suspicion for ectopic pregnancy. A negative result reduces the likelihood but does not entirely rule it out.
Cervical Motion Tenderness (CMT) on pelvic examination
  • CMT is a key physical finding in diagnosing pelvic inflammatory disease (PID). When present with pelvic pain and vaginal discharge, it strongly suggests PID.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 94%, Specificity: 33% for PID.
  • Reference: Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC) guidelines for treatment of sexually transmitted infections (2021).
  • Significance: The presence of CMT in a patient with pelvic pain and risk factors for PID supports the diagnosis and warrants prompt treatment. However, a negative finding reduces the likelihood but does not fully rule out PID.
Elevated White Cell Count (WCC)
  • An elevated WCC indicates a systemic inflammatory response and is a common marker of infection or inflammatory processes like appendicitis, PID, or pelvic abscess.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity for PID: 58%, Specificity: 77% when WCC is elevated.
  • Reference: British Infection Association guidelines on pelvic inflammatory disease (2019).
  • Significance: Elevated WCC supports the likelihood of an infectious or inflammatory cause of pelvic pain. A normal WCC reduces the likelihood of acute infection, but does not fully exclude it.
Pelvic Ultrasound
  • Pelvic ultrasound is highly effective for visualising gynaecological structures, detecting ectopic pregnancy, ovarian cysts, and other pelvic masses.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 87%, Specificity: 94% for detecting adnexal masses such as ectopic pregnancies or ovarian cysts.
  • Reference: American College of Radiology Appropriateness Criteria for acute pelvic pain (2018).
  • Significance: A positive ultrasound finding of an adnexal mass or free fluid strongly supports diagnoses like ectopic pregnancy or ovarian cyst rupture. A negative result reduces the likelihood of these structural abnormalities.
STI Screening (Chlamydia and Gonorrhoea NAAT)
  • STI screening via NAAT is sensitive and specific for chlamydia and gonorrhoea, two common causes of PID. Positive results confirm the presence of an STI, which supports the diagnosis of PID in the context of pelvic pain.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 90-95%, Specificity: 95% for detecting chlamydia and gonorrhoea infections.
  • Reference: British Association for Sexual Health and HIV (BASHH) guidelines for PID management (2019).
  • Significance: A positive STI result is highly suggestive of PID in symptomatic patients. A negative result does not exclude PID entirely but reduces the likelihood of an STI-related cause.
CA-125 Level
  • CA-125 is often elevated in ovarian cancer and may also be raised in endometriosis or PID. Although not specific, it can support further evaluation in cases with high suspicion for malignancy.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 80% for ovarian cancer, Specificity: 75% in postmenopausal women.
  • Reference: National Comprehensive Cancer Network (NCCN) guidelines for ovarian cancer (2021).
  • Significance: Elevated CA-125 in a post-menopausal patient with pelvic pain raises suspicion for ovarian cancer, though other causes can also elevate this marker. A normal CA-125 is reassuring but does not exclude malignancy.
Diagnostic Timeout & Cognitive Biases

To improve diagnostic accuracy in cases of pelvic pain, it is vital for clinicians to be aware of common cognitive errors. Taking a 'diagnostic timeout' to reflect on one's reasoning can prevent misdiagnosis.

  • Anchoring Bias: This is the tendency to fixate on initial information. For example, prematurely diagnosing a UTI in a patient with dysuria and pelvic pain, while failing to consider PID or an ectopic pregnancy, especially if a sexual history is not taken or a pregnancy test is overlooked.
  • Confirmation Bias: This involves seeking out or giving more weight to information that supports a pre-existing belief. A clinician might focus on a history of constipation to confirm IBS, while ignoring new red flag symptoms like weight loss or rectal bleeding that might suggest colorectal cancer.
  • Atypical Presentations: A significant pitfall is failing to recognise that serious conditions can present non-classically. Ectopic pregnancy may occur with no missed period or only light spotting. Ovarian cancer often presents with vague, non-specific symptoms like bloating or early satiety, which can be easily dismissed as benign GI issues.
  • Premature Closure: This is the error of accepting a diagnosis before it has been fully verified. For instance, diagnosing endometriosis as the cause of chronic pain without considering other overlapping conditions like pelvic floor dysfunction or interstitial cystitis, leading to incomplete management. A key question to ask is: "What else could this be?" or "Have I ruled out the most dangerous possibilities?"
Illustrative Management Pathways

For educational purposes, this section describes the typical principles of management for key diagnoses. These are not prescriptive instructions. All management must be guided by official UK guidelines, local formularies, and senior clinical advice.

Ectopic Pregnancy

A suspected ectopic pregnancy is a gynaecological emergency requiring immediate admission to hospital. Management is guided by the patient's clinical stability, serum hCG levels, and ultrasound findings. Stable, asymptomatic patients with small ectopic pregnancies and low hCG levels may be managed expectantly or medically with methotrexate. Most patients, particularly those who are symptomatic or haemodynamically unstable, require surgical management, typically a laparoscopic salpingectomy (removal of the fallopian tube).

Pelvic Inflammatory Disease (PID)

Management of PID is focused on eradicating the infection with broad-spectrum antibiotics to cover common pathogens like Chlamydia trachomatis, Neisseria gonorrhoeae, and anaerobes. Treatment should be started empirically as soon as the diagnosis is suspected, without waiting for test results. Patients who are systemically unwell, pregnant, or have a suspected pelvic abscess require admission for intravenous antibiotics. Contact tracing and treatment for sexual partners are essential to prevent reinfection and further transmission.

Ovarian Torsion

Ovarian torsion is a surgical emergency requiring immediate referral and intervention to prevent ovarian necrosis and preserve fertility. The definitive treatment is surgery, almost always via laparoscopy. The goal is to detort (untwist) the ovary. If the ovary appears viable after detorsion, it is conserved. If it is clearly necrotic or if there is a high suspicion of malignancy, a salpingo-oophorectomy (removal of the ovary and fallopian tube) may be necessary.

Chronic Pelvic Pain (e.g., Endometriosis, IBS)

Once life-threatening causes have been excluded, the management of chronic pelvic pain is often complex and requires a multidisciplinary approach. For endometriosis, treatment is aimed at symptomatic relief and may include simple analgesia (paracetamol, NSAIDs), hormonal therapies (e.g., combined oral contraceptive pill, progesterone-only methods) to suppress ovulation and menstruation, or surgical ablation/excision of endometriotic deposits. For IBS, management focuses on lifestyle and dietary advice (e.g., low FODMAP diet), alongside antispasmodics or other agents to target specific symptoms. Reassurance and psychological support are key components for all forms of chronic pain.

Reflection & Learning

These brief cases illustrate the application of clinical reasoning to different pelvic pain presentations. They are for educational purposes only.

Case 1: Suspected Ectopic Pregnancy in the Emergency Department

A 24-year-old woman presents to the Emergency Department with a 6-hour history of sudden-onset, sharp, right-sided lower abdominal pain and light vaginal spotting. Her last menstrual period was 7 weeks ago. She is pale, with a heart rate of 110 bpm and blood pressure of 100/60 mmHg. The problem representation is a young woman of childbearing age with amenorrhoea presenting with acute pelvic pain and haemodynamic instability, a classic picture for a ruptured ectopic pregnancy. The immediate pathway involves securing IV access, urgent blood tests including a pregnancy test and crossmatch, and an emergency referral to the on-call gynaecology team for ultrasound and probable surgical intervention.

Case 2: Suspected PID in a Sexual Health Clinic

A 21-year-old woman attends a clinic with a 1-week history of dull, bilateral lower abdominal pain, a new, unpleasant-smelling vaginal discharge, and pain during intercourse. She reports having a new sexual partner recently and does not use condoms consistently. On examination, she is afebrile but has marked tenderness in both adnexae and exquisite cervical motion tenderness. The problem representation is a young, sexually active woman with risk factors presenting with symptoms and signs highly indicative of Pelvic Inflammatory Disease (PID). The pathway involves taking swabs for STIs, starting empirical broad-spectrum antibiotics immediately, providing analgesia, and giving advice on partner notification and future contraception.

Case 3: Suspected Ovarian Cancer in General Practice

A 58-year-old woman presents to her GP for the third time in four months with persistent pelvic bloating and a feeling of fullness after eating only small meals. She has also noticed her bowels are more sluggish than usual and feels tired all the time. Her family history is significant for an aunt who had breast cancer. The problem representation is a post-menopausal woman with persistent, new-onset pelvic and abdominal symptoms that are red flags for ovarian cancer. The GP arranges urgent blood tests, including a CA-125, and refers for a pelvic ultrasound, following the NICE urgent cancer referral pathway.
