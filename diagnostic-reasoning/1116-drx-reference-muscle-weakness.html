Muscle Weakness Diagnostic Reasoning Reference
Context: Muscle Weakness Assessment
Overview

Muscle weakness is a common and diagnostically challenging presentation in clinical practice, encompassing a vast spectrum of conditions from benign and self-limiting states to severe, life-threatening neurological or systemic emergencies. The fundamental challenge for the clinician is to differentiate between central and peripheral causes, identify the specific level of the nervous system or muscle affected, and urgently recognise red flag features that signify a need for immediate intervention.

A systematic and structured approach is essential to navigate the complex differential diagnosis. The initial assessment must focus on identifying critical conditions such as Guillain-Barré syndrome, myasthenic crisis, stroke, spinal cord compression, and rhabdomyolysis, which require immediate hospital admission and specialist management.

This educational module is designed to build a foundational understanding of the clinical reasoning process for a patient presenting with muscle weakness. For definitive, real-time management protocols, users must refer to official UK guidelines from authoritative bodies such as NICE (e.g., NG127 for Motor Neurone Disease, CG186 for Multiple Sclerosis) and guidance from specialist societies like the Association of British Neurologists (ABN).

Pathophysiology/Basic Science

Understanding the anatomical origin of muscle weakness is fundamental to localising the lesion and formulating a differential diagnosis. Weakness can arise from pathology at any point along the motor pathway, from the brain to the muscle itself:

  • Upper Motor Neuron (UMN) Lesions: Damage to the motor cortex in the brain (e.g., stroke, tumour) or the descending corticospinal tracts in the brainstem or spinal cord (e.g., multiple sclerosis, spinal cord compression) disrupts the primary signal for movement. This typically results in weakness affecting large groups of muscles (e.g., hemiparesis, paraparesis), often accompanied by signs of spasticity, hyperreflexia, and a positive Babinski sign.
  • Lower Motor Neuron (LMN) Lesions: Damage to the anterior horn cells in the spinal cord (e.g., motor neurone disease), the nerve root (radiculopathy), or the peripheral nerve (neuropathy) interrupts the final common pathway to the muscle. This leads to flaccid weakness, hypotonia, hyporeflexia, muscle atrophy, and fasciculations, often in a specific myotomal or dermatomal pattern.
  • Neuromuscular Junction (NMJ) Disorders: Pathology at the synapse between the nerve and muscle impairs signal transmission. In Myasthenia Gravis, autoantibodies block or destroy acetylcholine receptors, leading to fatiguability—weakness that worsens with repetitive use and improves with rest. In Lambert-Eaton syndrome, impaired acetylcholine release causes weakness that may transiently improve with exertion.
  • Myopathies: These are disorders intrinsic to the muscle fibre itself. Inflammation (e.g., polymyositis), metabolic dysfunction, genetic defects (e.g., muscular dystrophies), or direct toxic damage (e.g., statin-induced myopathy, rhabdomyolysis) can all lead to muscle cell injury or death. This typically causes symmetrical, proximal muscle weakness, as the large muscles of the shoulders and hips are most affected.
  • Systemic and Other Causes: Generalised weakness can also be a manifestation of a systemic illness rather than a primary neuromuscular disorder. Conditions like hypothyroidism, severe vitamin D deficiency, cancer cachexia, and chronic fatigue syndrome can cause significant weakness through various mechanisms, including metabolic slowing, impaired calcium homeostasis, or profound deconditioning.
Differential Diagnosis (DDx) Framework

A structured approach to the differential diagnosis of muscle weakness is essential to ensure life-threatening conditions are prioritised.

Must not miss!
  • Rhabdomyolysis (inc Statin related): Presents with severe muscle pain, weakness, and dark urine, indicating rapid muscle breakdown that can lead to kidney failure.
  • Polymyalgia rheumatica: An inflammatory condition in older adults causing pain and stiffness in the shoulder and pelvic girdles, often with associated weakness.
  • Cancer cachexia: Severe muscle wasting and weakness associated with an underlying malignancy, often accompanied by weight loss and fatigue.
  • Peripheral Arterial Disease: Can cause muscle weakness and cramping in the legs (claudication) due to insufficient blood flow during exertion.
  • Guillain-Barré syndrome: An acute, rapidly progressive, ascending symmetrical weakness with reduced or absent reflexes, often following an infection.
Common causes
  • Sprains and strains: Localised weakness resulting from acute injury to muscles or ligaments.
  • Viral infection: Generalised weakness and fatigue (myalgia) are common during and after systemic viral illnesses.
  • Joint pains: Perceived weakness due to pain-related inhibition of movement in arthritic or injured joints.
  • Disuse atrophy: Muscle wasting and weakness resulting from prolonged immobility or lack of physical activity.
  • Vitamin D deficiency: Can cause proximal muscle weakness and bone pain (osteomalacia).
  • Mononeuropathy: Weakness confined to the distribution of a single peripheral nerve, often due to compression (e.g., carpal tunnel syndrome).
  • Hypothyroidism: A metabolic state causing generalised fatigue, lethargy, and often proximal muscle weakness.
  • Fibromyalgia: A chronic condition characterised by widespread pain, fatigue, and subjective weakness without objective findings.
  • Chronic fatigue syndrome: Profound and persistent fatigue and a sense of weakness, exacerbated by exertion.
  • Psychogenic: Weakness that does not conform to anatomical patterns and may be inconsistent on examination.
  • COVID-19: Can cause significant weakness and fatigue both during the acute illness and as part of a post-viral syndrome.
Easily missed
  • Connective tissue diseases: Systemic conditions like Lupus (SLE) or Sjögren's can present with inflammatory myopathy and weakness.
  • Polymyositis: An autoimmune inflammatory myopathy causing progressive, symmetrical, proximal muscle weakness.
  • Peripheral neuropathy: A "glove and stocking" pattern of sensory loss and distal weakness, often caused by diabetes or alcohol.
  • Diabetic neuropathy: A common complication of diabetes leading to distal sensory loss and weakness.
  • Alcohol abuse: Can cause weakness through direct toxic myopathy, peripheral neuropathy, and poor nutrition.
  • Vitamin B12 deficiency: May cause weakness through peripheral neuropathy or, more seriously, subacute combined degeneration of the spinal cord.
  • Metabolic Syndrome: A cluster of conditions that can contribute to deconditioning and perceived weakness.
  • Heavy metal poisoning: Rare cause of peripheral neuropathy and muscle weakness (e.g., lead, arsenic).
  • Paraneoplastic syndrome: Neuromuscular syndromes (e.g., Lambert-Eaton) caused by a remote malignancy.
  • Medication induced neuropathy: Weakness as a side effect of certain drugs, such as some chemotherapy agents or antibiotics.
Other Differential Diagnoses to consider
  • Multiple sclerosis: Episodic weakness due to demyelination of central nervous system nerves.
  • Myasthenia gravis: Fatiguable weakness, often worsened with exertion and improved with rest.
  • Motor neurone disease: Progressive, asymmetrical muscle weakness and atrophy.
  • Electrolyte disturbances: Potassium, calcium, or magnesium imbalances leading to muscle weakness.
  • Cushing's syndrome: Muscle weakness, particularly in the proximal limbs, due to chronic glucocorticoid excess.
  • Stroke: Sudden onset of muscle weakness or paralysis, typically affecting one side of the body.
  • Lymes disease: Bacterial infection transmitted by ticks causing intermittent episodes of muscle weakness and fatigue.
  • Amyloidosis: Rare deposition disorder that can lead to muscle weakness and multi-system involvement.
History Taking
Patient’s Description: How can I help you today?

Starting with an open-ended question like, "Can you tell me more about what brought you here today?" allows the patient to describe their symptoms in their own words.

Relevance: This helps to establish rapport and gives insight into the patient’s main concerns, which will guide more specific questions.
Onset of Weakness: When did you first notice the weakness?
  • Identifying whether the weakness was sudden or gradual helps differentiate between acute causes (e.g., stroke, Guillain-Barré syndrome) and chronic conditions (e.g., hypothyroidism, chronic fatigue syndrome).
  • Relevance: Sudden onset may suggest vascular, inflammatory, or traumatic causes, while gradual onset points to metabolic or degenerative diseases.
Location of Weakness: Is the weakness affecting specific muscles or a particular part of your body?
  • Localised muscle weakness (e.g., in one limb) may indicate mononeuropathy, stroke, or nerve injury, while generalised weakness may suggest systemic conditions like hypothyroidism, chronic fatigue syndrome, or myasthenia gravis.
  • Relevance: Focal weakness may indicate a nerve-related problem (e.g., mononeuropathy), whereas generalised weakness suggests systemic issues (e.g., electrolyte imbalances, vitamin deficiencies).
Progression of Weakness: Has the weakness worsened, improved, or stayed the same over time?
  • Worsening weakness over time may indicate progressive neurological or autoimmune conditions (e.g., motor neurone disease, polymyositis).
  • Relevance: Progressive weakness could suggest conditions like motor neurone disease or myositis, whereas fluctuating weakness could suggest myasthenia gravis.
Exacerbating or Relieving Factors: Is there anything that makes the weakness worse or better?
  • Weakness worsening with exertion and improving with rest is characteristic of myasthenia gravis, while weakness that improves after physical activity could suggest psychogenic causes.
  • Relevance: Positive response suggesting conditions like myasthenia gravis, while lack of fluctuation may rule out neuromuscular disorders.
Associated Symptom: Pain: Are you experiencing any muscle pain or tenderness?
  • Muscle pain may suggest inflammatory conditions like polymyositis or rhabdomyolysis, while absence of pain could point towards neurological causes (e.g., motor neurone disease, peripheral neuropathy).
  • Relevance: Painful weakness may suggest myopathy or inflammatory conditions, while pain-free weakness suggests neurological causes.
Associated Symptom: Fatigue: Do you feel unusually tired or fatigued?
  • Fatigue may accompany systemic causes of muscle weakness like chronic fatigue syndrome, hypothyroidism, or vitamin D deficiency.
  • Relevance: Positive response suggests conditions like hypothyroidism or chronic fatigue syndrome, while lack of fatigue may guide away from systemic causes.
Associated Symptom: Fever or recent viral illness: Have you had any fever or viral symptoms (e.g., cough, sore throat) recently?
  • A recent viral infection may suggest post-viral fatigue, viral myositis, or Guillain-Barré syndrome.
  • Relevance: A positive response suggests post-viral myalgia, viral myositis, or Guillain-Barré syndrome, while a negative response may help rule out these conditions.
Past Medical History: Do you have any known medical conditions like diabetes, hypothyroidism, or a history of cancer?
  • Conditions like diabetes (peripheral neuropathy), hypothyroidism, or a history of cancer (cachexia or paraneoplastic syndrome) can contribute to muscle weakness.
  • Relevance: Helps identify underlying chronic conditions that could be related to the weakness.
Drug History: Are you on any medications, particularly statins or chemotherapy?
  • Statins are associated with rhabdomyolysis and myopathy, while chemotherapy drugs can cause neuropathy and muscle weakness.
  • Relevance: Medication-induced weakness (e.g., statins, chemotherapy) should be considered, especially with a positive history.
Family History: Is there any family history of neurological or muscle disorders?
  • Genetic conditions like muscular dystrophy or inherited neuropathies can present with muscle weakness.
  • Relevance: A positive family history may suggest inherited muscle or nerve diseases, while a negative history can help rule out these conditions.
Ideas, Concerns, and Expectations (ICE)
  • Ideas: "What do you think might be causing this?" — Helps to understand the patient’s perspective and any preconceived ideas they have about their symptoms.
  • Concerns: "Is there something specific you are worried about?" — Identifies any serious concerns, such as fears of cancer or neurological disease.
  • Expectations: "What were you hoping we might be able to do for you today?" — Clarifies what the patient expects from the consultation, whether it be reassurance, further tests, or a treatment plan.
  • Relevance: Helps ensure the consultation addresses the patient’s worries (e.g., fear of cancer or neurological disease) and provides a shared understanding of next steps.
Examination Strategy
General Inspection
  • Look for visible signs of muscle wasting, fasciculations, asymmetry, or abnormal movements. Assess the patient’s general appearance for signs of malnutrition, cachexia, or unintentional weight loss.
  • Relevance: Muscle wasting and fasciculations suggest neurogenic causes (e.g., motor neurone disease). Cachexia may indicate cancer or chronic illness. Symmetrical wasting may point to chronic conditions like myositis, while asymmetry suggests peripheral neuropathy or stroke.
Palpation of Muscles
  • Palpate major muscle groups for tenderness, tone, and bulk. Assess for increased or decreased muscle tone and any tenderness or swelling.
  • Relevance: Tender muscles may suggest myositis or rhabdomyolysis. Increased tone (spasticity) could indicate central nervous system lesions (e.g., stroke, multiple sclerosis), while decreased tone (flaccidity) may suggest lower motor neuron lesions or Guillain-Barré syndrome.
Assessment of Muscle Strength
  • Test muscle strength in major muscle groups bilaterally using the Medical Research Council (MRC) scale (0-5). Compare proximal and distal muscle strength on both sides of the body.
  • Relevance: Proximal muscle weakness suggests myopathies (e.g., polymyositis, muscular dystrophy), while distal weakness is more indicative of neuropathies or motor neurone disease. Symmetrical weakness may point to metabolic or systemic conditions like hypothyroidism or chronic fatigue syndrome.
Reflex Testing
  • Test deep tendon reflexes (e.g., biceps, triceps, patellar, Achilles) using a reflex hammer. Assess for hyperreflexia or hyporeflexia.
  • Relevance: Hyperreflexia suggests upper motor neuron lesions (e.g., stroke, multiple sclerosis), while hyporeflexia may indicate lower motor neuron conditions (e.g., Guillain-Barré syndrome, peripheral neuropathy). Absent reflexes with muscle weakness can point to peripheral nerve damage or acute neurological conditions.
Gait Assessment
  • Observe the patient’s gait, looking for abnormalities such as a waddling gait, foot drop, or hemiparetic gait.
  • Relevance: A waddling gait suggests proximal muscle weakness (e.g., myopathies), while foot drop indicates peripheral neuropathy. Hemiparetic gait could be seen in stroke or other upper motor neuron lesions.
Coordination Testing
  • Test for coordination using rapid alternating movements, finger-nose testing, and heel-shin testing. Assess for tremors or ataxia.
  • Relevance: Coordination issues may indicate cerebellar dysfunction or upper motor neuron lesions (e.g., stroke, multiple sclerosis). A tremor could suggest Parkinson’s disease or metabolic disturbances (e.g., hyperthyroidism).
Cranial Nerve Examination (if relevant)
  • In cases of suspected neurological causes, assess cranial nerve function, especially for facial weakness or dysarthria.
  • Relevance: Cranial nerve abnormalities, such as facial asymmetry, may point to central nervous system conditions like stroke or Guillain-Barré syndrome. Bulbar weakness may indicate motor neurone disease or myasthenia gravis.
Sensory Examination
  • Assess light touch, pinprick, vibration, and proprioception in both upper and lower limbs. Check for sensory deficits.
  • Relevance: Sensory deficits may indicate peripheral neuropathy (e.g., diabetic neuropathy, vitamin B12 deficiency) or central nervous system lesions. A sensory level could indicate spinal cord involvement.
Red Flag Synthesis
Sudden Onset of Muscle Weakness
  • Sudden muscle weakness, particularly if focal or unilateral, may suggest an acute neurological event such as a stroke or transient ischaemic attack (TIA).
  • Significance: Sudden weakness could indicate a cerebrovascular accident (stroke) or TIA, both of which are medical emergencies requiring immediate intervention.
Progressive or Rapidly Worsening Weakness
  • Weakness that worsens rapidly, particularly if ascending (starting in the legs and moving upwards), suggests Guillain-Barré syndrome, a potentially life-threatening condition affecting the peripheral nervous system.
  • Significance: Progressive, ascending weakness may indicate Guillain-Barré syndrome, which can lead to respiratory failure and requires urgent hospital admission.
Weakness with Difficulty Breathing or Swallowing
  • If muscle weakness is accompanied by breathing or swallowing difficulties, this raises concern for neuromuscular disorders such as myasthenia gravis or motor neurone disease, which can affect respiratory muscles.
  • Significance: This could indicate myasthenia gravis or motor neurone disease, both of which can cause life-threatening respiratory failure if not treated promptly.
Muscle Weakness with Severe Muscle Pain or Dark Urine
  • Severe muscle pain, swelling, and dark-coloured urine in the context of muscle weakness suggest rhabdomyolysis, a condition where muscle breakdown leads to the release of harmful proteins into the bloodstream.
  • Significance: Rhabdomyolysis can cause acute kidney injury and electrolyte imbalances, requiring immediate treatment to prevent severe complications.
Weight Loss or Systemic Symptoms (e.g. night sweats, fever)
  • Unintentional weight loss, fever, or night sweats in a patient with muscle weakness raises concerns about malignancy (e.g., cancer cachexia) or systemic inflammatory conditions such as polymyositis or paraneoplastic syndromes.
  • Significance: These symptoms may indicate an underlying malignancy or systemic condition that requires urgent investigation to rule out life-threatening causes.
New Onset Muscle Weakness in a Patient on Statins or Other Medications
  • New muscle weakness in a patient on statins or other medications known to cause myopathy suggests the possibility of medication-induced muscle damage, including rhabdomyolysis in the case of statins.
  • Significance: Statin-induced rhabdomyolysis is a medical emergency, requiring prompt discontinuation of the drug and supportive treatment to prevent complications such as renal failure.
Investigations Strategy & Interpretation
Full Blood Count (FBC)
  • A full blood count can help identify anaemia, infection, or systemic inflammation, which might be underlying causes of muscle weakness.
  • Result Significance: Elevated white blood cells suggest infection or inflammation (e.g., polymyositis), while anaemia could indicate chronic disease or nutritional deficiencies.
Creatine Kinase (CK)
  • Creatine kinase levels are elevated in conditions involving muscle damage, such as rhabdomyolysis, polymyositis, or statin-induced myopathy.
  • Result Significance: Elevated CK indicates muscle breakdown, suggesting diagnoses such as rhabdomyolysis, myositis, or muscular dystrophies. A normal CK level may help rule out these conditions.
Thyroid Function Tests (TFTs)
  • Thyroid function tests (TFTs) assess for hypothyroidism or hyperthyroidism, both of which can cause muscle weakness.
  • Result Significance: Abnormal results indicating hypothyroidism could explain generalised fatigue and weakness, while hyperthyroidism may cause myopathy. Normal results would help rule out thyroid-related causes.
Electrolyte Panel (including calcium, magnesium, and potassium)
  • Electrolyte imbalances (especially potassium, calcium, and magnesium) can cause muscle weakness or cramps.
  • Result Significance: Hypokalaemia, hypocalcaemia, or hypomagnesaemia could suggest metabolic causes of muscle weakness, such as electrolyte disturbances or metabolic myopathies. Normal levels help exclude these causes.
Nerve Conduction Studies (NCS) and Electromyography (EMG)
  • Nerve conduction studies and electromyography are used to evaluate the electrical activity in nerves and muscles, helping to distinguish between neuropathic and myopathic causes of weakness.
  • Result Significance: Abnormal nerve conduction may indicate peripheral neuropathy (e.g., diabetic neuropathy), while abnormal EMG findings suggest myopathies or motor neurone disease. Normal results may help rule out these conditions.
MRI of Brain and Spinal Cord
  • MRI is useful for evaluating central nervous system causes of weakness, such as stroke, multiple sclerosis, or spinal cord compression.
  • Result Significance: Positive findings may suggest central nervous system lesions (e.g., stroke, multiple sclerosis, or a tumour), while a normal scan can rule out structural causes of neurological weakness.
Autoimmune Panel (e.g., ANA, Anti-dsDNA)
  • An autoimmune panel is indicated if connective tissue or autoimmune diseases are suspected, such as polymyositis or systemic lupus erythematosus (SLE).
  • Result Significance: Positive results for ANA or anti-dsDNA suggest an autoimmune process (e.g., polymyositis, SLE), while negative results can help rule out autoimmune aetiologies.
Serum Vitamin B12 and Folate Levels
  • Vitamin B12 and folate deficiencies can lead to peripheral neuropathy, presenting as muscle weakness.
  • Result Significance: Low vitamin B12 or folate levels suggest a deficiency-related neuropathy, while normal levels help rule out nutritional deficiencies as a cause of the weakness.
Chest X-ray or CT Scan
  • Imaging may be warranted if there is suspicion of cancer, such as paraneoplastic syndrome, or sarcoidosis contributing to muscle weakness.
  • Result Significance: A positive finding, such as a lung mass, could suggest malignancy or sarcoidosis. Negative imaging helps rule out thoracic causes like cancer or pulmonary diseases.
Lumbar Puncture
  • A lumbar puncture may be indicated if Guillain-Barré syndrome or other neurological conditions are suspected, as it can reveal raised protein levels in the cerebrospinal fluid (CSF).
  • Result Significance: Elevated CSF protein with normal white cell count (albuminocytologic dissociation) is indicative of Guillain-Barré syndrome. A normal CSF study would make this diagnosis less likely.
Evidence-Based Diagnosis (EBDx)
Elevated Creatine Kinase (CK) in Rhabdomyolysis
  • Elevated CK is a hallmark of muscle injury and is particularly diagnostic of rhabdomyolysis, where levels often exceed five times the upper limit of normal.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 94%, Specificity: 97%, Likelihood Ratio: 31.3 for CK levels > 1000 IU/L in diagnosing rhabdomyolysis.
  • Reference: Huerta-Alardín, A.L. et al., *The New England Journal of Medicine*, 2005.
  • Significance: A markedly elevated CK strongly suggests rhabdomyolysis, while normal or mildly elevated CK levels make this diagnosis less likely.
Proximal Muscle Weakness in Polymyositis
  • Polymyositis typically presents with symmetrical, proximal muscle weakness. Electromyography and muscle biopsy provide definitive diagnosis.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 75%, Specificity: 89% for proximal muscle weakness in diagnosing polymyositis.
  • Reference: Dalakas, M.C. et al., *The Lancet Neurology*, 2015.
  • Significance: Proximal muscle weakness with elevated inflammatory markers (e.g., CK, aldolase) significantly increases the likelihood of polymyositis. Negative findings make the diagnosis less likely but not impossible.
Thyroid Function Tests in Hypothyroidism
  • Hypothyroidism can present with generalised muscle weakness, often associated with fatigue and weight gain. TFTs (TSH and free T4) are key in diagnosis.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 98%, Specificity: 96% for TSH >10 mIU/L in diagnosing hypothyroidism.
  • Reference: Vanderpump, M.P. et al., *The BMJ*, 2014.
  • Significance: Elevated TSH with low free T4 is diagnostic of hypothyroidism. Normal TFTs effectively rule out this condition as a cause of muscle weakness.
Nerve Conduction Studies in Guillain-Barré Syndrome
  • Nerve conduction studies (NCS) reveal demyelination or axonal damage in Guillain-Barré syndrome (GBS), characterised by ascending paralysis and are critical in its diagnosis.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 85%, Specificity: 90%, Likelihood Ratio: 8.5 for abnormal NCS findings in diagnosing GBS.
  • Reference: Ropper, A.H. et al., *The New England Journal of Medicine*, 2012.
  • Significance: Positive NCS findings confirm GBS, while normal studies make GBS less likely but do not completely exclude it, particularly in early disease.
Electrolyte Imbalance (Hypokalaemia) in Muscle Weakness
  • Hypokalaemia can lead to muscle weakness, cramps, and in severe cases, paralysis. Serum potassium levels are essential for diagnosing this condition.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 92%, Specificity: 90% for serum potassium < 3.0 mmol/L in diagnosing hypokalaemia-induced muscle weakness.
  • Reference: Gennari, F.J., *The New England Journal of Medicine*, 1998.
  • Significance: Low potassium levels confirm a metabolic cause for muscle weakness. Normal potassium levels exclude hypokalaemia as the cause of weakness.
CSF Analysis in Guillain-Barré Syndrome (Albuminocytologic Dissociation)
  • CSF analysis often shows elevated protein with normal cell count (albuminocytologic dissociation) in Guillain-Barré syndrome.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 50-60% in the first week, increasing to 80% after two weeks of onset, with Specificity: 90% for diagnosing GBS.
  • Reference: van Doorn, P.A. et al., *Lancet Neurology*, 2013.
  • Significance: Positive CSF findings support the diagnosis of GBS, especially in conjunction with clinical and NCS findings. Normal results early in the disease course do not exclude GBS.
Diagnostic Timeout & Cognitive Biases

Awareness of common thinking errors is essential to improve diagnostic accuracy and patient safety when assessing a patient with muscle weakness. Before finalising a diagnosis, it is good practice for clinicians at all levels to take a 'diagnostic timeout' to challenge their own reasoning.

  • Anchoring Bias: The tendency to fixate on an initial piece of information, such as the patient's age or a known comorbidity. For example, attributing progressive weakness in an elderly patient solely to "old age" or "deconditioning" while failing to consider a new diagnosis like motor neurone disease or myopathy. A key question to ask is: "Could something else be going on here?"
  • Confirmation Bias: Seeking out or giving more weight to evidence that supports an initial hypothesis while ignoring contradictory findings. For instance, focusing on normal inflammatory markers to rule out myositis while downplaying the patient's clear report of progressive proximal weakness.
  • Atypical Presentations: A significant pitfall is forgetting that serious diseases can present in non-classical ways. For example, Guillain-Barré syndrome can sometimes present with sensory or autonomic features before significant weakness develops. Myasthenia Gravis can present with isolated ocular symptoms (ptosis, diplopia) long before generalised weakness occurs.
  • Premature Closure: Accepting a diagnosis before it has been fully verified. A common example is diagnosing "post-viral fatigue" in a patient with weakness after an infection, without systematically ruling out the more serious possibility of Guillain-Barré syndrome, especially if there are any neurological signs like reflex changes.
Illustrative Management Pathways

For educational purposes, this section describes the typical principles of management for key diagnoses. These are not prescriptive instructions. All management must be guided by official UK guidelines, local formularies, and senior clinical advice.

Guillain-Barré Syndrome (GBS)

GBS is a neurological emergency requiring immediate hospital admission, often to a high-dependency or intensive care unit. Management is primarily supportive, with a critical focus on monitoring respiratory function (e.g., vital capacity measurements) due to the risk of respiratory muscle paralysis. Specific treatment involves immunotherapy with either intravenous immunoglobulin (IVIg) or plasma exchange (PE) to hasten recovery. Physiotherapy and rehabilitation are vital from an early stage to maximise functional recovery.

Rhabdomyolysis

Management requires urgent hospitalisation to prevent acute kidney injury (AKI). The cornerstone of treatment is aggressive intravenous fluid resuscitation to maintain a high urine output and flush out myoglobin. The underlying cause (e.g., crush injury, certain drugs like statins, extreme exertion) must be identified and addressed. Close monitoring of kidney function (U&Es) and serum creatine kinase (CK) is essential, and electrolyte abnormalities (especially hyperkalaemia) must be corrected promptly.

Myasthenic Crisis

This is a life-threatening exacerbation of myasthenia gravis with weakness of the respiratory muscles, requiring immediate admission to an intensive care unit. Management involves securing the airway, often with mechanical ventilation. As with GBS, treatment is with IVIg or plasma exchange. Any precipitating factors, such as infection or medications, should be identified and treated. Cholinesterase inhibitors are often temporarily withheld and then carefully reintroduced.

Chronic/Metabolic Causes (e.g., Hypothyroidism, Vitamin Deficiency)

Once life-threatening conditions have been excluded, management is directed at the underlying cause. For hypothyroidism, this involves thyroid hormone replacement with levothyroxine, guided by TSH levels. For Vitamin D or B12 deficiency, appropriate replacement therapy is initiated. Management often involves a multidisciplinary approach including physiotherapy to address deconditioning and improve strength, alongside patient education and reassurance.

Reflection & Learning

These brief cases illustrate the application of clinical reasoning to different presentations of muscle weakness. They are for educational purposes only.

Case 1: Suspected Guillain-Barré Syndrome in A&E

A 28-year-old woman presents to the Emergency Department with a 3-day history of "pins and needles" that started in her feet and has now ascended to her knees, alongside a feeling of "heaviness" in her legs. She reports having had a diarrhoeal illness about two weeks prior. On examination, she has symmetrical weakness of ankle dorsiflexion and knee extension (MRC grade 4/5), and both her ankle and knee reflexes are absent. The problem representation is a young adult with a post-infectious, rapidly ascending, areflexic weakness, highly suspicious for Guillain-Barré Syndrome. The immediate pathway involves urgent neurological referral, admission for respiratory monitoring, and arrangement for nerve conduction studies and a lumbar puncture.

Case 2: Suspected Polymyositis in a Rheumatology Clinic

A 55-year-old woman is referred by her GP with a 3-month history of progressive difficulty getting up from a chair and combing her hair. She denies any significant muscle pain but feels profoundly weak in her shoulders and hips. She has no sensory symptoms. Examination confirms symmetrical proximal weakness in both upper and lower limbs (MRC grade 3-4/5), while distal strength and all reflexes are normal. Initial blood tests show a markedly elevated creatine kinase (CK) level. The problem representation is a middle-aged woman with subacute, progressive, painless, symmetrical proximal weakness and a high CK, characteristic of an inflammatory myopathy like polymyositis. The pathway involves further immunological screening, EMG, and consideration of a muscle biopsy for definitive diagnosis and initiation of immunosuppressive therapy.

Case 3: Statin-Induced Myopathy in General Practice

A 68-year-old man, who was started on a high-dose statin for secondary prevention six weeks ago, presents to his GP complaining of new-onset aching in his thighs and feeling weaker. He struggles more on the stairs. He has no red flag symptoms like dark urine or swallowing problems. His examination is unremarkable apart from mild tenderness over his quadriceps. The problem representation is a patient who has developed new myalgia and weakness shortly after starting a statin. The immediate action is to check a creatine kinase (CK) level and to advise the patient to temporarily stop the statin. Management will depend on the CK level and the resolution of symptoms, with safety netting advice to seek urgent care if pain becomes severe or urine changes colour.
