Haemoptysis Diagnostic Reasoning Reference
Context: Haemoptysis Assessment
Overview

Haemoptysis, the coughing up of blood from the respiratory tract, is a symptom that causes significant patient anxiety and presents a diagnostic challenge for clinicians. It can range in severity from blood-streaked sputum to massive, life-threatening haemorrhage. The causes are diverse, spanning common infections like bronchitis to critical diagnoses such as lung cancer, pulmonary embolism, and tuberculosis. The fundamental clinical task is to differentiate benign, self-limiting causes from those that require urgent investigation and intervention.

A systematic and structured approach is therefore essential. The initial assessment must focus on haemodynamic stability, the volume of blood loss, and identifying red flags that point towards a serious underlying pathology. This educational module provides a foundational framework for the clinical reasoning process in a patient presenting with haemoptysis. For definitive, real-time management protocols, users must refer to official UK guidelines from authoritative bodies such as NICE (e.g., NG12 for Suspected Cancer, CG191 for Tuberculosis) and the British Thoracic Society (BTS) Guideline for the Investigation of Haemoptysis.

Pathophysiology/Basic Science

Understanding the origin of bleeding is key to forming a differential diagnosis for haemoptysis. The blood can originate from either the high-pressure bronchial circulation or the low-pressure pulmonary circulation, and the underlying mechanism dictates the likely cause:

  • Airway Inflammation and Infection: This is the most common cause. In conditions like acute bronchitis or pneumonia, inflammation of the mucosal lining leads to hyperaemia (increased blood flow) and mucosal friability. The mechanical stress of forceful coughing can cause these superficial blood vessels to rupture, resulting in blood-streaked sputum.
  • Neovascularisation and Tumour Erosion: Malignant tumours, such as bronchogenic carcinoma, induce the formation of new, fragile blood vessels (neovascularisation) to support their growth. These vessels are prone to rupture. Furthermore, as the tumour grows, it can directly invade and erode into the wall of larger, more established bronchial or pulmonary arteries, leading to more significant haemoptysis.
  • Chronic Airway Damage (Bronchiectasis): In bronchiectasis, the airways are permanently widened and damaged, often due to recurrent infections. This process leads to hypertrophy of the bronchial arteries and the formation of tortuous, fragile new vessels that run close to the damaged airway surface. Erosion of these vessels is a classic cause of recurrent and sometimes massive haemoptysis.
  • Vascular Disruption (Pulmonary Embolism): A pulmonary embolism can cause haemoptysis through the mechanism of pulmonary infarction. When a segment of the lung loses its blood supply from the pulmonary artery, it can lead to ischaemic tissue death. This results in bleeding into the alveolar spaces, which is then coughed up, often with associated pleuritic chest pain.
  • Elevated Pulmonary Venous Pressure: In conditions like left ventricular failure, pressure backs up from the failing left heart into the pulmonary venous system. This congests the pulmonary capillaries in the alveolar walls. The increased pressure can force red blood cells to extravasate into the alveoli, leading to the classic pink, frothy sputum of acute pulmonary oedema.
  • Alveolar Haemorrhage Syndromes: In rare autoimmune conditions like Goodpasture's syndrome or vasculitis (e.g., GPA), autoantibodies attack the basement membranes of the alveolar capillaries. This causes widespread capillary inflammation and rupture, leading to diffuse bleeding directly into the alveolar spaces.
Differential Diagnosis (DDx) Framework

A structured approach to the differential diagnosis of haemoptysis is essential to ensure life-threatening conditions are prioritised.

Must not miss!
  • Pneumonia: Presents with productive cough, fever, and pleuritic chest pain, with haemoptysis often mixed with purulent sputum.
  • Tuberculosis: Often associated with chronic cough, weight loss, fever, and night sweats, with haemoptysis ranging from streaks to massive bleeding from cavitating lesions.
  • Lung cancer: May present with persistent haemoptysis, often in a smoker over 40, potentially with associated weight loss, hoarseness, or chest pain.
  • Pulmonary Embolism: Characterised by a sudden onset of dyspnoea and pleuritic chest pain, where haemoptysis can be a sign of pulmonary infarction.
  • Left ventricular failure: Typically presents with breathlessness (orthopnoea, PND) and pink, frothy sputum rather than frank blood.
Common causes
  • URTI: Upper Respiratory Tract Infection can cause blood-streaked sputum from severe coughing and inflammation in the nasopharynx or trachea.
  • Bronchitis: Acute inflammation of the bronchial tubes, commonly causing a cough with blood-tinged sputum due to mucosal irritation.
  • Bronchiectasis: Chronic condition with recurrent cough and purulent sputum, where haemoptysis is common due to damaged, fragile airways.
  • COVID-19: Can present with a range of respiratory symptoms including cough and fever, with haemoptysis being a less common but recognised feature.
Easily missed
  • Anticoagulants: Use of medications like warfarin or DOACs can lead to haemoptysis even with minor airway irritation.
  • Pulmonary hypertension: Elevated pressure in the pulmonary arteries can cause haemoptysis, often with exertion and signs of right heart strain.
  • Bleeding & Bruising: Systemic coagulopathies or bleeding disorders can manifest with haemoptysis as part of a wider bleeding tendency.
Other Differential Diagnoses to consider
  • Goodpasture's Syndrome: Autoimmune condition with pulmonary haemorrhage and glomerulonephritis.
  • Wegener's Granulomatosis (GPA): Vasculitis causing granulomas and capillary rupture in the respiratory tract.
  • Aspergilloma: Fungal ball within a lung cavity causing vascular erosion.
  • Trauma: Blunt or penetrating injury to the chest or respiratory tract causing haemoptysis.
  • Arteriovenous Malformation (AVM): Congenital or acquired vascular abnormality leading to pulmonary bleeding.
  • Idiopathic Pulmonary Haemosiderosis: Rare condition characterised by repeated pulmonary haemorrhage of unknown cause.
  • Cystic Fibrosis: Recurrent infections and bronchiectasis leading to haemoptysis.
History Taking
Patient’s Description: How can I help you today?
Starting with an open-ended question such as "Can you tell me more about what brought you here today?" allows the patient to describe their symptoms in their own words. Relevance: This provides a natural flow for history-taking, ensuring the consultation focuses on the patient’s primary concerns and helps guide more targeted follow-up questions.
When did the haemoptysis first occur?
  • Identifies the onset of symptoms, which helps differentiate between acute (e.g., pneumonia, PE) and chronic conditions (e.g., TB, lung cancer).
  • Relevance: Acute onset suggests causes like PE, pneumonia, or trauma. Chronic onset favours conditions such as bronchiectasis or malignancy.
How much blood have you noticed, and what does it look like?
  • Clarifies the severity and nature of the haemoptysis (e.g., streaks of blood vs large-volume haemoptysis).
  • Relevance: Large-volume haemoptysis raises concern for serious conditions such as malignancy, bronchiectasis, or TB. Bright red blood suggests active bleeding, while darker blood may indicate older bleeding or gastric origin.
Is the haemoptysis associated with coughing or sputum production?
  • Helps assess whether haemoptysis is related to infectious or inflammatory conditions.
  • Relevance: Cough with green or yellow sputum suggests bronchitis, pneumonia, or bronchiectasis. Dry cough with haemoptysis may occur in TB or malignancy.
Have you had fever, weight loss, or night sweats?
  • Explores systemic symptoms that could indicate infection or malignancy.
  • Relevance: Fever and night sweats are common in TB or pneumonia. Weight loss raises suspicion of malignancy or chronic infections like TB.
Have you experienced chest pain or shortness of breath?
  • Assesses for associated symptoms that could indicate conditions such as PE or cardiac causes.
  • Relevance: Chest pain and acute shortness of breath strongly suggest PE, while orthopnoea or paroxysmal nocturnal dyspnoea may point to left ventricular failure.
Do you smoke, or have you had a history of significant smoking?
  • Assesses risk factors for lung cancer and chronic pulmonary conditions.
  • Relevance: Smoking history is a major risk factor for malignancy and chronic lung disease like COPD, both of which can cause haemoptysis.
Do you have a history of anticoagulant use or bleeding disorders?
  • Explores drug and past medical history contributing to haemoptysis.
  • Relevance: Anticoagulants (e.g., warfarin) and coagulopathies increase the risk of pulmonary bleeding even with minor triggers.
Have you travelled recently, particularly to areas with endemic TB?
  • Assesses risk for infectious causes like TB.
  • Relevance: Recent travel to high TB prevalence regions raises the index of suspicion for TB.
Associated Symptoms: Have you noticed leg swelling or pain?
  • Investigates for deep vein thrombosis (DVT) as a precursor to pulmonary embolism.
  • Relevance: Leg swelling or pain with haemoptysis strongly suggests a thromboembolic event (PE).
Ideas, Concerns, and Expectations (ICE)
  • Explores the patient’s perspective about their symptoms.
  • Relevance: Addresses the patient’s beliefs about possible causes (e.g., cancer, infection), fears (e.g., life-threatening conditions), and desired outcomes (e.g., symptom resolution, reassurance).
Examination Strategy
General Inspection
  • Look for signs of pallor, cyanosis, or visible respiratory distress, which can indicate significant blood loss, hypoxaemia, or severe underlying pathology.
  • Relevance: Pallor suggests anaemia secondary to chronic bleeding. Cyanosis indicates hypoxaemia, seen in pulmonary embolism or advanced lung disease. Absence of these signs may rule out severe hypoxic or anaemic states.
Vital Signs
  • Assess heart rate, blood pressure, respiratory rate, oxygen saturation, and temperature.
  • Relevance: - Tachycardia and hypotension suggest haemodynamic instability due to large-volume haemoptysis or PE. - Fever indicates infection such as pneumonia or TB. - Hypoxia supports diagnoses like PE or pneumonia. - Stable vitals may rule out life-threatening conditions.
Respiratory Examination: Inspection
  • Observe for chest wall deformities, accessory muscle use, or asymmetry in chest expansion.
  • Relevance: Accessory muscle use or asymmetry may indicate severe lung pathology (e.g., consolidation or effusion in pneumonia, malignancy). Absence of these findings suggests less severe respiratory compromise.
Palpation of the Chest
  • Assess for tracheal deviation, chest wall tenderness, and tactile vocal fremitus.
  • Relevance: - Tracheal deviation may indicate a mass or tension pneumothorax. - Tenderness suggests trauma or rib fractures. - Altered fremitus may indicate consolidation (increased fremitus) or effusion (decreased fremitus).
Percussion of the Chest
  • Assess for areas of dullness or hyperresonance.
  • Relevance: - Dullness indicates consolidation (e.g., pneumonia) or effusion. - Hyperresonance suggests pneumothorax. - Normal findings may rule out significant pleural or parenchymal pathology.
Auscultation of the Lungs
  • Listen for crackles, wheezes, or decreased breath sounds.
  • Relevance: - Crackles suggest pneumonia, bronchiectasis, or heart failure. - Wheezes are seen in bronchospasm or airway obstruction (e.g., malignancy). - Absent or reduced sounds may indicate effusion or pneumothorax.
Cardiovascular Examination
  • Assess for signs of heart failure or pulmonary hypertension, including raised JVP, displaced apex beat, and peripheral oedema.
  • Relevance: Signs of heart failure support diagnoses like left ventricular failure or pulmonary hypertension. Absence of these signs may suggest non-cardiac causes.
Examination of the Legs
  • Check for signs of deep vein thrombosis (DVT), such as unilateral leg swelling, tenderness, and erythema.
  • Relevance: Presence of DVT signs supports a diagnosis of pulmonary embolism. Negative findings do not rule out PE but reduce its likelihood.
Red Flag Synthesis
Large-volume haemoptysis (≥200 mL in 24 hours)
  • Large-volume haemoptysis can rapidly lead to airway obstruction or haemodynamic instability due to significant blood loss.
  • Significance: This could indicate life-threatening conditions such as massive pulmonary haemorrhage, advanced TB, or lung malignancy, requiring immediate intervention.
Severe dyspnoea or hypoxia
  • Severe dyspnoea or hypoxia suggests significant respiratory compromise, potentially due to pulmonary embolism, massive pneumonia, or airway obstruction from malignancy.
  • Significance: This could indicate life-threatening respiratory failure and requires urgent assessment and stabilisation.
Unintentional weight loss or night sweats
  • These systemic symptoms are often associated with malignancy or chronic infections such as TB.
  • Significance: This could indicate lung cancer or disseminated TB, warranting prompt investigation including imaging and microbiological studies.
Persistent haemoptysis (lasting ≥3 weeks)
  • Persistent haemoptysis raises suspicion of serious conditions such as lung cancer, TB, or bronchiectasis.
  • Significance: This could indicate chronic or progressive lung pathology, necessitating further evaluation with chest imaging and specialist referral.
Chest pain with haemoptysis
  • Chest pain, particularly pleuritic or associated with haemoptysis, suggests pulmonary embolism, infarction, or malignancy causing pleural involvement.
  • Significance: This could indicate pulmonary embolism or cancer with pleural invasion, both of which require urgent investigation and management.
Haemoptysis in a smoker >40 years old
  • Haemoptysis in a smoker, particularly with a significant smoking history, raises concern for lung cancer.
  • Significance: This could indicate primary lung malignancy, and urgent imaging (e.g., CT chest) is required to rule out cancer.
Signs of deep vein thrombosis (DVT)
  • Unilateral leg swelling, tenderness, or erythema associated with haemoptysis is suggestive of pulmonary embolism from a thromboembolic source.
  • Significance: This could indicate a venous thromboembolism, with pulmonary embolism potentially causing life-threatening complications.
Immunosuppressed state with haemoptysis
  • Patients who are immunosuppressed (e.g., due to chemotherapy, HIV) are at higher risk of opportunistic infections or malignancies like Kaposi sarcoma, presenting with haemoptysis.
  • Significance: This could indicate severe opportunistic lung infection (e.g., fungal infection) or malignancy, requiring urgent evaluation.
Investigations Strategy & Interpretation
Full Blood Count (FBC)
  • Helps assess for anaemia due to chronic blood loss, leukocytosis suggesting infection, or thrombocytopenia indicating a bleeding disorder.
  • Result Significance: Anaemia supports chronic haemoptysis, leukocytosis suggests pneumonia or TB, and thrombocytopenia may indicate a coagulopathy.
Coagulation Profile
  • Evaluates for coagulopathies or excessive anticoagulation in patients on warfarin or direct oral anticoagulants (DOACs).
  • Result Significance: Abnormal results suggest a bleeding disorder or therapeutic overdose of anticoagulants. Normal findings make this less likely.
Chest X-ray (CXR)
  • First-line imaging to assess for lung pathology such as infection, malignancy, or cavitating lesions.
  • Result Significance: Positive findings like consolidation suggest pneumonia, masses raise concern for malignancy, and cavitation is seen in TB or abscesses. A normal CXR does not rule out PE or early malignancy.
Computed Tomography (CT) Chest
  • Provides detailed imaging to detect masses, pulmonary embolism, or bronchiectasis not visible on CXR.
  • Result Significance: Positive findings can confirm malignancy, PE, or other structural abnormalities. Negative findings reduce suspicion of many serious causes.
Sputum Culture and Sensitivity
  • Identifies bacterial or mycobacterial pathogens in cases of suspected infection.
  • Result Significance: Positive results confirm bacterial pneumonia or TB. Negative findings do not exclude infection, particularly viral or atypical pathogens.
D-dimer
  • Useful in ruling out pulmonary embolism in patients with low pre-test probability.
  • Result Significance: A normal D-dimer makes PE unlikely, while an elevated level warrants further imaging (e.g., CT pulmonary angiogram).
CT Pulmonary Angiography (CTPA)
  • Gold standard for diagnosing pulmonary embolism in cases of haemoptysis with suspected PE.
  • Result Significance: Positive findings confirm pulmonary embolism. A negative result rules out PE with high specificity.
Bronchoscopy
  • Direct visualisation of the airways to identify endobronchial lesions, bleeding sites, or foreign bodies.
  • Result Significance: Positive findings of a mass or bleeding point confirm local pathology (e.g., malignancy, bronchiectasis). Negative findings are less common in visible haemoptysis.
Arterial Blood Gas (ABG)
  • Evaluates oxygenation and acid-base status in patients with respiratory distress or hypoxia.
  • Result Significance: Hypoxaemia supports severe conditions like PE or pneumonia. Normal oxygenation is reassuring but does not rule out all significant pathology.
Tuberculin Skin Test or IGRA (Interferon-Gamma Release Assay)
  • Identifies latent or active TB in high-risk patients.
  • Result Significance: Positive results suggest TB infection. Negative findings reduce but do not exclude the possibility of TB.
Evidence-Based Diagnosis (EBDx)
Persistent haemoptysis in a smoker (>40 years)
  • Smoking history significantly raises the pre-test probability of lung cancer in haemoptysis presentations.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 80%, Specificity: 77%, Positive Likelihood Ratio (LR+): 3.5.
  • Reference: National Institute for Health and Care Excellence (NICE) guidelines on suspected cancer referral.
  • Significance: A positive finding strongly suggests malignancy and warrants urgent referral for imaging and bronchoscopy.
Chest X-ray abnormalities (e.g., mass or cavitation)
  • Chest X-ray is often the first imaging modality to identify structural lung abnormalities in haemoptysis.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 75%, Specificity: 70% for detecting lung cancer. Sensitivity for cavitating TB lesions is 83%.
  • Reference: BTS Guidelines for Investigation of Haemoptysis.
  • Significance: A positive CXR finding supports malignancy or TB. A negative result does not exclude PE or malignancy, especially early-stage disease.
D-dimer in suspected pulmonary embolism
  • D-dimer is a highly sensitive test for thromboembolic disease, but its specificity is limited.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 95%, Specificity: 40%, LR+: 1.58, LR-: 0.13 (in low-risk patients).
  • Reference: Wells Criteria for PE and NICE Guidelines.
  • Significance: A negative D-dimer effectively rules out PE in low-risk patients, but positive results require further investigation with CTPA.
CT Pulmonary Angiography (CTPA) for pulmonary embolism
  • CTPA is the gold standard for diagnosing pulmonary embolism, providing direct visualisation of thromboemboli.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 83%, Specificity: 96%, LR+: 20.75, LR-: 0.18.
  • Reference: PIOPED II Study (Prospective Investigation of Pulmonary Embolism Diagnosis).
  • Significance: A positive result confirms PE. A negative result effectively rules out clinically significant embolism.
Positive sputum culture for Mycobacterium tuberculosis
  • Sputum cultures are highly specific for diagnosing active TB, especially in haemoptysis cases with systemic symptoms.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 70-85% (for three samples), Specificity: >98%.
  • Reference: World Health Organisation (WHO) guidelines on TB diagnostics.
  • Significance: Positive results confirm TB. Negative results require further testing (e.g., bronchoscopy) if clinical suspicion remains high.
Bronchoscopy for endobronchial lesions
  • Direct visualisation of airways via bronchoscopy is critical for diagnosing endobronchial tumours or bronchiectasis in unexplained haemoptysis.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 88%, Specificity: 96% for central bronchogenic carcinoma.
  • Reference: American College of Chest Physicians Guidelines on Lung Cancer Diagnosis.
  • Significance: A positive finding confirms local pathology such as malignancy or a bleeding source. Negative findings necessitate further imaging or systemic investigation.
Weight loss and night sweats in malignancy or TB
  • Unintentional weight loss and night sweats are key systemic symptoms associated with chronic infections and malignancy.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 60-70%, Specificity: 75% for lung cancer. Sensitivity for TB with these symptoms is 85%.
  • Reference: NICE Guidelines for TB and Lung Cancer Diagnosis.
  • Significance: Positive findings raise suspicion for malignancy or TB and guide further diagnostic work-up. Absence reduces likelihood but does not exclude either condition.
Diagnostic Timeout & Cognitive Biases

Awareness of common thinking errors is essential to improve diagnostic accuracy and patient safety when assessing a patient with haemoptysis. Before finalising a diagnosis, it is good practice for clinicians at all levels to take a 'diagnostic timeout' to challenge their own reasoning.

  • Anchoring Bias: The tendency to prematurely label haemoptysis as being due to a benign cause, like 'simple bronchitis', especially in a younger patient. This can lead to a failure to consider more sinister diagnoses, even when red flags like persistence of symptoms or a smoking history are present. A key question is: "Could this be lung cancer, and have I done enough to exclude it?"
  • Confirmation Bias: Actively seeking out or over-emphasising evidence that supports an initial belief. For example, focusing on a history of recent cough and cold to confirm an infective cause, while downplaying the significance of a 3-week duration of haemoptysis which should prompt consideration of malignancy.
  • Availability Heuristic: Overestimating the likelihood of a diagnosis because it is more recent or memorable in the clinician's experience. For example, after seeing several cases of PE, a clinician might over-investigate for PE in a low-risk patient while missing the signs of a chronic condition like bronchiectasis.
  • Over-reliance on Normal Tests: A critical pitfall is being falsely reassured by a normal initial investigation. A normal chest X-ray does not rule out lung cancer (especially central tumours) or a pulmonary embolism. It is crucial to correlate test results with the overall clinical picture and pre-test probability. Persistent, unexplained haemoptysis with a normal CXR requires further investigation, typically with a CT scan and/or bronchoscopy.
Illustrative Management Pathways

For educational purposes, this section describes the typical principles of management for key diagnoses. These are not prescriptive instructions. All management must be guided by official UK guidelines, local formularies, and senior clinical advice.

Massive Haemoptysis

This is a medical emergency. The immediate priority is not diagnosis but airway protection and haemodynamic stabilisation (ABC approach). The patient should be positioned with the bleeding side down, if known, to protect the contralateral lung. High-flow oxygen should be administered. Urgent involvement of senior respiratory physicians and critical care is mandatory. Management may involve emergency bronchoscopy to localise and tamponade the bleeding site, or interventional radiology for bronchial artery embolisation to stop the bleed.

Suspected Lung Cancer

Based on NICE guideline NG12, patients aged 40 and over with unexplained haemoptysis should be offered an urgent (two-week wait) chest X-ray. If the CXR is normal but clinical suspicion is high, or if the CXR shows suspicious findings, an urgent referral to a chest physician is required. Further investigation will typically involve a contrast-enhanced CT scan of the chest and subsequent bronchoscopy with biopsy to establish a histological diagnosis and guide treatment by the specialist multidisciplinary team (MDT).

Suspected Pulmonary Embolism (PE)

If PE is suspected as the cause of haemoptysis, especially with associated pleuritic pain or dyspnoea, risk stratification using a tool like the Wells score is performed. In patients with a 'PE likely' score, or in those with an 'unlikely' score but a positive D-dimer, a definitive imaging test is required. A CT Pulmonary Angiogram (CTPA) is the standard investigation. If confirmed, treatment involves immediate anticoagulation, with the choice of agent (e.g., DOAC or LMWH) guided by local protocols and patient factors.

Infective Causes (e.g., Bronchiectasis, Pneumonia)

When an infective cause is suspected (e.g., purulent sputum, fever, focal crackles on auscultation), management focuses on identifying and treating the underlying pathogen. Sputum samples for microscopy, culture, and sensitivity (including for acid-fast bacilli if TB is suspected) are crucial. A chest X-ray helps confirm pneumonia. Treatment involves appropriate antibiotics based on likely organisms and local guidelines. For patients with known bronchiectasis, a short course of antibiotics as part of a pre-agreed self-management plan may be appropriate for an acute exacerbation.

Reflection & Learning

These brief cases illustrate the application of clinical reasoning to different haemoptysis presentations. They are for educational purposes only.

Case 1: Suspected Malignancy in Primary Care

A 65-year-old man, a lifelong smoker, presents to his GP with a 4-week history of coughing up small flecks of bright red blood. He has also lost about 4kg in weight and feels more tired than usual. The problem representation is an older male with a major risk factor (smoking) and constitutional symptoms (weight loss) presenting with persistent haemoptysis. This is highly suspicious for lung cancer. The GP correctly arranges an urgent two-week wait chest X-ray and refers him to the respiratory clinic, as per NICE guidelines.

Case 2: Suspected Infection in the Emergency Department

A 48-year-old woman with a known history of bronchiectasis presents to the Emergency Department. For the past three days, her daily productive cough has worsened, her sputum is now green and blood-streaked, and she has a low-grade fever. On examination, coarse crackles are heard at both lung bases. The problem representation is a patient with a known chronic lung disease presenting with classic features of an acute infective exacerbation. The pathway involves obtaining sputum cultures, a chest X-ray to rule out new consolidation, and starting empirical antibiotics according to local guidelines.

Case 3: Suspected PE in an Ambulatory Care Unit

A 32-year-old woman presents with a sudden onset of sharp, right-sided chest pain and a single episode of coughing up blood-streaked sputum. She recently returned from a long-haul flight. Her observations show a heart rate of 110 bpm and oxygen saturation of 94%. The problem representation is a young woman with a recent risk factor for VTE presenting with haemoptysis, pleuritic chest pain and tachycardia, making pulmonary embolism a key differential. Her Wells score is calculated as 'PE likely', prompting an immediate referral for a CTPA to confirm or exclude the diagnosis.
