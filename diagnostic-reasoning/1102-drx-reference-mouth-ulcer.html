Mouth Ulcer Diagnostic Reasoning Reference
Context: Mouth Ulcer Assessment
Overview

Mouth ulcers are an extremely common clinical presentation in both primary care and dentistry, affecting a significant portion of the population at some point in their lives. The diagnostic challenge lies in distinguishing between common, benign causes, such as traumatic ulcers or recurrent aphthous stomatitis, and the rare but critical diagnoses that can present with oral ulceration, most notably oral squamous cell carcinoma and systemic diseases like inflammatory bowel disease or immunodeficiency.

A systematic and structured approach to history and examination is essential to identify red flag features that warrant urgent investigation or referral. This ensures that potentially life-threatening conditions are not missed, while avoiding unnecessary anxiety and investigation for the vast majority of patients with self-limiting conditions. This educational module provides a foundational framework for the clinical reasoning process behind diagnosing mouth ulcers. For definitive, real-time management protocols, users must refer to official UK guidelines from authoritative bodies such as NICE (e.g., NG12 for Head and Neck Cancer) and the British Society for Oral Medicine (BSOM).

Pathophysiology/Basic Science

Understanding the underlying mechanism of ulcer formation is key to forming a differential diagnosis. Oral ulcers represent a breach in the integrity of the mucosal epithelium, exposing the underlying connective tissue. This can occur through several distinct pathophysiological processes:

  • Traumatic Ulceration: This is the simplest mechanism, involving direct physical or chemical injury to the mucosa. Mechanical trauma (e.g., a sharp tooth, ill-fitting denture, or accidental bite) or chemical burns can cause localised cell death and epithelial sloughing, resulting in a painful ulcer. The inflammatory response is typically localised and resolves once the injurious stimulus is removed.
  • Immunologically-Mediated Ulceration: This is central to conditions like Recurrent Aphthous Stomatitis (RAS) and Lichen Planus. In RAS, it is thought that a localised, T-cell-mediated immune response targets the oral epithelium, leading to focal cell death and ulceration. Triggers can vary widely, from stress to hormonal changes or local trauma, in genetically predisposed individuals. In erosive lichen planus, a similar cell-mediated cytotoxic reaction against basal keratinocytes causes mucosal breakdown.
  • Infectious Ulceration: Pathogens can cause ulcers directly or indirectly. Viruses like Herpes Simplex (HSV) replicate within epithelial cells, causing them to rupture (lysis) and form vesicles which then break down into ulcers. Bacteria, as seen in Acute Necrotising Ulcerative Gingivitis (ANUG), can proliferate and release toxins that cause tissue necrosis and ulceration, particularly in the presence of poor oral hygiene. Fungal infections like candidiasis can also cause erosions and ulceration, especially in immunocompromised individuals.
  • Neoplastic Ulceration: In oral squamous cell carcinoma, malignant transformation of epithelial cells leads to uncontrolled proliferation. As the tumour grows, it can outstrip its blood supply, leading to central necrosis and ulceration. The ulcer is often characterised by induration (hardness) and fixation to underlying tissues due to invasive growth.
  • Systemic Disease Manifestation: Oral ulcers can be a sign of systemic pathology. In Crohn's disease, extra-intestinal inflammation can manifest as deep, linear ulcers in the mouth. In haematological deficiencies (e.g., iron, B12, folate), impaired mucosal cell turnover can lead to epithelial breakdown and ulceration. In agranulocytosis (e.g., drug-induced), the lack of neutrophils severely impairs the immune defence, allowing minor mucosal breaks to become large, necrotic ulcers.
Differential Diagnosis (DDx) Framework

A structured approach to the differential diagnosis of mouth ulcers is essential to ensure life-threatening conditions are prioritised.

Must not miss!
  • Oral cancer: A persistent, non-healing ulcer, often with rolled edges and induration, commonly seen in smokers and heavy drinkers.
  • Inflammatory Bowel Disease: Recurrent aphthous-like ulcers which can be an extra-intestinal manifestation of Crohn's disease or ulcerative colitis, often associated with GI symptoms.
  • HIV: May present with various oral lesions including large, persistent, or atypical ulcers due to immunodeficiency.
  • Stevens-Johnson syndrome: An acute, severe mucocutaneous reaction, often drug-induced, causing widespread blistering and ulceration of the mouth and other mucous membranes.
Common causes
  • Traumatic ulcer: A painful ulcer with a history of mechanical or chemical injury, which typically heals once the cause is removed.
  • Aphthous ulceration: Recurrent, painful, round or oval ulcers with a yellowish-grey base and an erythematous halo, occurring on non-keratinised mucosa.
  • Herpes stomatitis: Multiple small vesicles that rupture to form painful ulcers, often preceded by a prodrome of tingling or burning, caused by the herpes simplex virus.
  • Oral candidiasis: Presents as white plaques that can be wiped off to reveal an erythematous base, or as red, atrophic patches (erythematous candidiasis), often in immunocompromised patients or denture wearers.
  • Acute ulcerative gingivitis: A painful infection of the gums causing ulceration and necrosis of the interdental papillae, often associated with poor oral hygiene, stress, and smoking.
  • Hand, foot, and mouth disease: A viral illness, typically in children, causing ulcers in the mouth along with a characteristic rash on the hands and feet.
  • Lichen planus: A chronic inflammatory condition that can present as white, lacy streaks (Wickham's striae), red patches, or erosive ulcers on the buccal mucosa.
  • COVID-19: May be associated with various oral manifestations including ulcers, though the link is still being established.
Easily missed
  • Agranulocytosis (DMARDs): Severe oral ulceration and infection resulting from a critically low white blood cell count, often a side effect of certain medications like methotrexate or carbimazole.
  • Coeliac disease: Recurrent aphthous-like stomatitis can be the only manifestation of underlying coeliac disease due to malabsorption and nutritional deficiencies.
Other Differential Diagnoses to consider
  • COVID-19: Can present with oral lesions, including ulcers, though less commonly. Consider if associated with other systemic symptoms.
  • Behçet's disease: Recurrent oral and genital ulcers, often with ocular involvement.
  • Lupus erythematosus: Oral ulcers are common in systemic lupus, often painless.
  • Syphilis: May present with painless ulcers in the mouth (primary chancre).
  • Erythema multiforme: Oral ulcers associated with target lesions on the skin.
  • Leukaemia: Can cause oral ulcers due to immunosuppression or chemotherapeutic agents.
  • Iron, folate, or vitamin B12 deficiency: Nutritional deficiencies are common causes of oral ulcers.
History Taking
**Patient’s Description**: How can I help you today?

Asking the patient to describe their symptoms in their own words is crucial. A question like, "Can you tell me more about what brought you here today?" is a good starting point. This open-ended question allows the patient to provide unfiltered details about their condition, including any concerns they feel are important.

Relevance: Allows the patient to describe the issue without constraints, which helps guide the consultation and elicits crucial details that could lead to a focused diagnostic approach.
When did the ulcer start, and has it changed over time?
  • Asking about the onset of the ulcer and its progression over time can help differentiate between acute and chronic causes. For example, traumatic ulcers often heal quickly, while ulcers associated with oral cancer or autoimmune conditions persist or worsen over time.
  • Relevance: A positive response suggesting chronicity raises concern for causes such as oral cancer, Behçet's disease, or HIV-related ulcers. A short duration is more typical of traumatic or viral causes.
Have you noticed any triggers, such as stress, food, or trauma?
  • Ulcers triggered by stress or minor trauma may suggest aphthous ulcers, while trauma from dental appliances or accidental biting could suggest traumatic ulcers.
  • Relevance: A positive response suggests aphthous ulceration or traumatic ulcer. A negative response points towards other systemic causes like viral infections or autoimmune conditions.
Are there any associated symptoms like fever, fatigue, skin rashes, or joint pain?
  • Fever and systemic symptoms suggest an underlying infectious cause (e.g., herpes stomatitis) or systemic disease like inflammatory bowel disease or Behçet's disease.
  • Relevance: A positive response suggests viral infections, autoimmune diseases, or systemic conditions like Crohn's disease or HIV. A negative response may point toward more localised conditions such as traumatic ulcers.
Do you have a history of gastrointestinal problems, skin conditions, or any other recurrent ulcers?
  • Gastrointestinal symptoms (e.g., diarrhoea, abdominal pain) may point to Crohn’s disease or coeliac disease, while recurrent ulcers may suggest Behçet’s disease or aphthous stomatitis.
  • Relevance: Positive findings suggest conditions like coeliac disease, Crohn’s disease, or Behçet’s disease. A negative response may lean towards isolated or benign causes.
Do you take any medications or have any recent changes in your prescriptions?
  • Medications, particularly DMARDs (Disease-modifying antirheumatic drugs), immunosuppressants, and chemotherapeutic agents, can lead to agranulocytosis and result in oral ulcers.
  • Relevance: A positive response points toward drug-induced ulcers or agranulocytosis. A negative response suggests causes other than drug-related ones.
**Ideas, Concerns, and Expectations (ICE)**
  • Asking the patient, "What do you think might be causing this?" can help clarify their ideas about their condition. For example, they may suspect stress or worry about cancer.
  • Follow up with, "Is there something specific you are worried about?" to explore their concerns. This might include fear of oral cancer or a chronic illness.
  • "What were you hoping we might be able to do for you today?" helps clarify their expectations. Some patients may expect reassurance, while others may be hoping for specific investigations or treatments.
  • Relevance: Understanding the patient's perspective helps address their concerns and manage expectations, ensuring they feel heard and valued. It also helps guide your consultation by addressing what they find most distressing or important.
Examination Strategy
Inspection of the oral cavity and lips
  • Begin by inspecting the lips, buccal mucosa, tongue, palate, and gingivae for any ulcers or lesions. Note the size, location, number, and appearance (e.g., indurated, necrotic, or erythematous).
  • Relevance: - Positive findings of single, persistent ulcers may suggest oral cancer. - Multiple, painful ulcers may indicate aphthous ulcers, viral infections (e.g., herpes), or autoimmune conditions like Behçet’s disease. - White patches may suggest oral candidiasis or lichen planus. - A negative finding of ulcers or lesions suggests causes outside the oral cavity, such as gastrointestinal issues like coeliac disease.
Palpation of the ulcer(s) and surrounding tissue
  • Gently palpate the ulcer and surrounding tissue to assess for tenderness, induration, or fixation to underlying tissues, which can differentiate between benign and malignant causes.
  • Relevance: - Tender ulcers are common in aphthous ulcers and traumatic ulcers. - Indurated or fixed ulcers raise suspicion of malignancy (e.g., oral cancer). - Non-tender ulcers may suggest syphilis or other painless ulcers like those in lupus erythematosus.
Inspection of the oropharynx and throat
  • Examine the oropharynx and throat for signs of infection or inflammation, such as erythema, exudates, or additional lesions. Check the tonsils and posterior pharynx for ulcers or white patches.
  • Relevance: - Positive findings such as exudates or vesicles in the throat may suggest viral infections (e.g., herpes stomatitis or hand, foot, and mouth disease). - Pharyngeal erythema and ulceration could indicate Stevens-Johnson syndrome or another systemic condition. - A normal oropharynx may suggest causes unrelated to systemic infections.
Examination of cervical lymph nodes
  • Palpate the cervical lymph nodes (submandibular, submental, and anterior/posterior cervical chains) for tenderness or enlargement.
  • Relevance: - Positive findings of enlarged, tender lymph nodes may suggest infections such as herpes stomatitis, hand, foot, and mouth disease, or oral candidiasis. - Firm, non-tender lymphadenopathy raises concern for malignancy (e.g., oral cancer or lymphoma). - Absence of lymphadenopathy may be seen in non-infectious causes such as aphthous ulcers.
Inspection of the skin and mucous membranes
  • Examine the skin and other mucous membranes (e.g., eyes, genitals) for any associated lesions, rashes, or target lesions. Check for conjunctival involvement.
  • Relevance: - Positive findings of target lesions suggest erythema multiforme, often linked to infections or drugs like Stevens-Johnson syndrome. - Genital ulcers, alongside oral ulcers, may indicate Behçet's disease. - Normal skin examination suggests a localised oral condition, such as traumatic ulcers or aphthous stomatitis.
Examination for systemic signs (temperature, pallor, weight loss)
  • Check for fever, pallor, and weight loss, as these systemic signs may point towards more serious underlying conditions.
  • Relevance: - Fever may suggest an infectious cause such as herpes stomatitis or acute ulcerative gingivitis. - Pallor and weight loss may indicate more systemic or malignant processes such as cancer, HIV, or inflammatory bowel disease. - Absence of systemic signs may favour benign or local causes, such as traumatic ulcers.
Red Flag Synthesis
Persistent ulcer lasting more than 3 weeks
  • A mouth ulcer that persists for more than 3 weeks is a significant red flag, especially if it is non-healing, indurated, or has irregular borders.
  • Significance: This could indicate oral cancer, particularly squamous cell carcinoma, and requires urgent referral for biopsy and further evaluation.
Unexplained weight loss
  • Unintended weight loss can suggest a systemic disease process, including malignancy or chronic infection.
  • Significance: This could be a sign of underlying oral cancer, HIV, or gastrointestinal malignancy and requires prompt investigation.
Non-tender, persistent cervical lymphadenopathy
  • Firm, non-tender lymph nodes in the cervical region, especially if they persist, may indicate metastatic spread from oral cancers.
  • Significance: This could signify regional metastasis from head and neck cancers, necessitating urgent imaging and referral to an ENT specialist.
Multiple ulcers with systemic symptoms (fever, malaise)
  • Multiple ulcers occurring alongside systemic symptoms such as fever or malaise may point to a more serious underlying infection or systemic disease.
  • Significance: This could suggest conditions such as herpes stomatitis, acute ulcerative gingivitis, or HIV. In severe cases, it could indicate Stevens-Johnson syndrome, which is a medical emergency.
Oral ulceration with eye or genital involvement
  • The presence of oral ulcers alongside ocular or genital ulcers should raise suspicion for multisystem diseases.
  • Significance: This could suggest Behçet's disease or erythema multiforme, which require specialist referral and multidisciplinary management due to potential systemic involvement.
Severe pain not responsive to typical analgesics
  • Oral ulcers causing severe pain that is disproportionate or unresponsive to standard analgesia may indicate a more serious underlying cause.
  • Significance: This could be a sign of malignancy, aggressive infections (e.g., necrotising ulcerative gingivitis), or immunocompromised states like HIV, all of which require urgent intervention.
Investigations Strategy & Interpretation
Swab of the ulcer for culture and sensitivity
  • This investigation involves taking a swab of the ulcer to detect bacterial, viral, or fungal infections. It is particularly useful in cases where infection (e.g., herpes stomatitis, oral candidiasis) is suspected.
  • Result Significance: - Positive results may indicate viral infections such as herpes simplex virus or fungal infections like Candida. - Negative results help rule out infective causes and direct the focus toward other non-infectious aetiologies like aphthous ulcers or oral cancer.
Blood tests: Full blood count (FBC)
  • A full blood count is essential to check for anaemia, leukopenia, or signs of infection, which may point to underlying causes such as agranulocytosis, leukaemia, or infection-related ulcers.
  • Result Significance: - A low white cell count may suggest agranulocytosis (drug-induced or related to systemic conditions). - Anaemia, particularly iron deficiency, folate deficiency, or vitamin B12 deficiency, may suggest nutritional causes for recurrent ulcers. - A normal FBC may help rule out haematological causes.
Serological testing for HIV
  • HIV serology is indicated in cases of persistent or multiple oral ulcers, particularly if associated with other systemic symptoms (e.g., weight loss, recurrent infections). Oral ulcers can be an early sign of HIV.
  • Result Significance: - A positive result confirms HIV, requiring prompt referral for further management. - A negative result helps rule out HIV as a cause of oral ulcers, narrowing the differential to other systemic or local conditions.
Biopsy of the ulcer
  • A biopsy is essential for persistent, indurated ulcers to rule out malignancy, particularly squamous cell carcinoma, which may present with a non-healing oral ulcer.
  • Result Significance: - A positive result for malignancy confirms oral cancer, requiring urgent referral for oncological management. - A negative result rules out malignancy and helps to focus the investigation on other potential causes such as autoimmune conditions or chronic infections.
Coeliac serology (anti-tTG, anti-EMA)
  • Testing for coeliac disease should be considered in patients with recurrent oral ulcers, particularly if they have gastrointestinal symptoms or a history of malabsorption.
  • Result Significance: - Positive serology (anti-tissue transglutaminase, anti-endomysial antibodies) suggests coeliac disease, requiring further management including a gluten-free diet. - Negative serology helps rule out coeliac disease as a cause of the oral ulcers, allowing the clinician to focus on other potential causes.
Endoscopy (in patients with suspected inflammatory bowel disease)
  • Endoscopy is indicated in patients with recurrent oral ulcers who also present with gastrointestinal symptoms, as it helps assess for inflammatory bowel disease (IBD), such as Crohn’s disease, which may have extra-intestinal manifestations.
  • Result Significance: - Positive findings such as mucosal inflammation, ulceration, or strictures would support a diagnosis of IBD. - A negative result would make IBD less likely, helping to exclude this as a cause of recurrent oral ulcers.
Evidence-Based Diagnosis (EBDx)
Persistent oral ulcer lasting >3 weeks (suggesting oral cancer)
  • Persistent oral ulcers are a key sign of oral squamous cell carcinoma. Their significance increases if they are non-healing, indurated, and accompanied by other signs such as lymphadenopathy.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 85%, Specificity: 70%, Positive Predictive Value (PPV): 1.4% (due to the low prevalence of oral cancer in the general population).
  • Reference: British Society for Oral Medicine guidelines, 2019.
  • Significance: Positive findings increase the likelihood of oral cancer and warrant urgent biopsy. Negative findings decrease the likelihood of malignancy but do not fully rule it out without a biopsy.
Swab for herpes simplex virus in patients with painful ulcers
  • Herpes simplex virus (HSV) can cause painful oral ulcers. A viral swab with PCR testing is the gold standard for diagnosis.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 92%, Specificity: 99% for PCR-based HSV testing.
  • Reference: Cochrane Review on viral diagnostics, 2020.
  • Significance: Positive results confirm HSV infection, while negative results rule out viral aetiology, leading to consideration of other causes like aphthous stomatitis or drug reactions.
Cervical lymphadenopathy in suspected malignancy
  • Firm, non-tender lymphadenopathy in the cervical region is highly concerning for malignancy, particularly in head and neck cancers.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 75%, Specificity: 90%, Likelihood Ratio (positive): 7.5.
  • Reference: NICE Head and Neck Cancer Guidelines, 2021.
  • Significance: Positive findings significantly increase the suspicion of metastatic cancer, while negative findings reduce but do not eliminate the possibility of malignancy.
Positive coeliac serology (anti-tTG, anti-EMA) in recurrent oral ulcers
  • Recurrent oral ulcers may be a manifestation of coeliac disease. Serological testing for anti-tTG and anti-EMA antibodies is highly sensitive and specific for the diagnosis of coeliac disease.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 90-95%, Specificity: 95-99% for anti-tTG. Similar values for anti-EMA.
  • Reference: ESPGHAN Guidelines for Coeliac Disease, 2020.
  • Significance: Positive serology strongly suggests coeliac disease as the underlying cause, and a gluten-free diet is typically initiated. Negative serology rules out coeliac disease with high confidence.
Biopsy of persistent ulcer for oral cancer
  • For ulcers that persist despite treatment, a biopsy is the gold standard to confirm or rule out malignancy. Histopathological examination will reveal dysplastic or malignant cells.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 95%, Specificity: 100% for diagnosing oral squamous cell carcinoma via biopsy.
  • Reference: Oral Oncology journal, 2018.
  • Significance: A positive biopsy confirms malignancy, requiring urgent oncological management. A negative biopsy excludes cancer and directs attention towards benign causes like chronic ulcers or autoimmune conditions.
Diagnostic Timeout & Cognitive Biases

Awareness of common thinking errors is essential to improve diagnostic accuracy when assessing a patient with mouth ulcers. Before finalising a diagnosis, it is good practice to take a 'diagnostic timeout' to challenge your own reasoning.

  • Anchoring Bias: The tendency to lock onto an early piece of information. For example, prematurely diagnosing recurrent aphthous stomatitis in a patient who reports stress as a trigger, while failing to adequately consider that a new, persistent ulcer in a high-risk site (e.g., lateral border of the tongue) could be malignant, irrespective of stress levels. A key question is: "What are the features that don't fit with a simple aphthous ulcer?"
  • Confirmation Bias: Selectively seeking or over-weighting evidence that supports an initial hypothesis. For instance, focusing on a history of accidental biting to confirm a traumatic ulcer, while ignoring the fact the ulcer feels hard (indurated) on palpation—a finding that contradicts a simple traumatic cause.
  • Availability Heuristic: Diagnosing a condition simply because it comes to mind easily, often because it is common or was recently seen. The vast majority of ulcers are aphthous, leading to a risk of reflexively diagnosing this without considering less common but more serious alternatives in patients with atypical features.
  • Premature Closure: Accepting a diagnosis before it has been fully verified. For example, diagnosing a traumatic ulcer from a sharp cusp on a tooth without questioning why that trauma is happening now. A new underlying lesion (e.g., a tumour) could be causing the mucosa to bulge and be caught more easily. Always ask: "Have I considered an alternative, even if it's less likely?"
Illustrative Management Pathways

For educational purposes, this section describes the typical principles of management for key diagnoses. These are not prescriptive instructions. All management must be guided by official UK guidelines, local formularies, and senior clinical advice.

Suspected Oral Cancer

Any ulcer that is suspicious of malignancy (persistent for >3 weeks, indurated, non-healing, associated with firm lymphadenopathy) requires an urgent two-week wait referral to a secondary care specialist, typically a Maxillofacial or Head and Neck surgeon. The patient will undergo a thorough examination and a biopsy of the lesion for histopathological diagnosis. Subsequent management is determined by staging and will be coordinated by a multidisciplinary team (MDT).

Recurrent Aphthous Stomatitis (RAS)

Once more serious causes are excluded, management is focused on symptomatic relief and reducing recurrence. First-line treatment includes topical antimicrobial mouthwash (e.g., chlorhexidine) to prevent secondary infection, and topical analgesics. For more severe or frequent episodes, topical corticosteroids (e.g., hydrocortisone buccal tablets) are used. In primary care, it is crucial to screen for underlying causes such as haematological deficiencies (FBC, ferritin, B12, folate) or coeliac disease, especially if ulcers are severe or recurrent.

Primary Herpetic Gingivostomatitis

Management is primarily supportive, focusing on maintaining hydration (especially in children), providing adequate analgesia (e.g., paracetamol or ibuprofen), and advising a soft diet. Topical anaesthetic gels may provide temporary relief. Oral antiviral therapy (e.g., aciclovir) is not routinely recommended for immunocompetent individuals unless they present within 72 hours of onset and are systemically unwell or at risk of complications. Hospital admission should be considered for patients who are unable to maintain hydration or who are significantly immunocompromised.

Systemic Causes (e.g., IBD, Coeliac Disease)

If oral ulceration is suspected to be a manifestation of an underlying systemic disease, management involves treating the underlying condition. This requires referral to the appropriate specialist, such as a gastroenterologist. While the systemic disease is being managed, symptomatic relief for the oral ulcers can be provided as for RAS. The oral lesions typically improve as the systemic disease is brought under control.

Reflection & Learning

These brief cases illustrate the application of clinical reasoning to different mouth ulcer presentations. They are for educational purposes only.

Case 1: Suspected Oral Cancer in Primary Care

A 68-year-old male, a lifelong smoker with significant alcohol intake, presents to his GP with a sore area on his tongue that has been present for about two months. On examination, there is a 2cm ulcer on the right lateral border of the tongue with raised, hard edges. It is largely painless to palpation. There is a palpable, firm, non-tender submandibular lymph node on the right side. The problem representation is an elderly man with major risk factors presenting with a chronic, indurated ulcer and associated lymphadenopathy. This is highly suspicious for oral squamous cell carcinoma with regional metastasis. The immediate action is an urgent two-week wait referral to the local head and neck cancer service.

Case 2: Recurrent Ulcers with Systemic Features

A 24-year-old female presents with a history of recurrent, painful mouth ulcers occurring 3-4 times a year since she was a teenager. She also mentions feeling tired all the time and experiencing intermittent abdominal bloating and loose stools. Examination reveals two small, round ulcers on her buccal mucosa with red halos. The rest of the examination is normal. The problem representation is a young woman with long-standing recurrent aphthous stomatitis associated with constitutional and gastrointestinal symptoms. This raises suspicion for an underlying systemic cause like coeliac disease or inflammatory bowel disease. The appropriate next step is to perform blood tests, including a full blood count, haematinics (ferritin, B12, folate), and coeliac serology (anti-tTG).

Case 3: Acute Viral Illness in a Child

A mother brings her 3-year-old son to the urgent care centre. He developed a fever 24 hours ago, has been refusing to eat or drink, and is drooling more than usual. On examination, the child is febrile (38.5°C) and miserable. His gums are visibly swollen and red, and there are multiple small, ruptured ulcers on his palate and gums. Tender anterior cervical lymph nodes are palpable. The problem representation is a young child with an acute febrile illness, oral pain, and characteristic signs of diffuse gingival inflammation and ulceration. The clinical picture is classic for primary herpetic gingivostomatitis. Management focuses on supportive care: advising the mother on analgesia (paracetamol/ibuprofen) and strategies to maintain hydration, with safety netting advice to return if he shows signs of dehydration.
