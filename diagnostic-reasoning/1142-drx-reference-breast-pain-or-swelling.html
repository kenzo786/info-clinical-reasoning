Breast Pain or Swelling Diagnostic Reasoning Reference
Context: Breast Pain or Swelling Assessment
Overview

Breast pain or swelling (mastalgia) is an extremely common presentation in both primary care and specialist clinics, affecting up to 70% of women at some point in their lives. While the vast majority of cases are due to benign physiological or fibrocystic changes, the primary challenge for the clinician is to provide reassurance while maintaining a high index of suspicion for breast cancer, which can also present with these symptoms. A systematic and sensitive approach is essential to differentiate benign from potentially malignant causes, address patient anxiety, and ensure timely referral to specialist services when necessary.

This educational module is designed to provide a foundational understanding of the clinical reasoning process for breast pain and swelling. For definitive, real-time management protocols, users must refer to official UK guidelines from authoritative bodies such as NICE (e.g., NG101 for Familial Breast Cancer and NG12 for Breast Cancer) and local breast clinic referral pathways.

Pathophysiology/Basic Science

Understanding the origin of breast pain and swelling is key to diagnosis. Symptoms can be generated through several distinct mechanisms, each pointing towards a different set of differential diagnoses:

  • Hormonal Influence: The glandular tissue of the breast is highly sensitive to fluctuations in oestrogen and progesterone. The cyclical hormonal changes of the menstrual cycle cause proliferation and subsequent involution of breast tissue, leading to fluid retention, oedema, and tenderness. This is the underlying cause of benign cyclical mastalgia, the most common type of breast pain.
  • Inflammation and Infection: In conditions like mastitis or a breast abscess, bacterial invasion (commonly Staphylococcus aureus) leads to an acute inflammatory response. This causes localised vasodilation, increased capillary permeability, and neutrophil infiltration, resulting in the classic signs of infection: pain, swelling, redness (erythema), and heat.
  • Ductal Ectasia and Obstruction: Benign conditions like ductal ectasia (widening of the milk ducts) or blockage can lead to the accumulation of secretions, causing distension and localised inflammation. In lactational mastitis, a blocked milk duct is often the initiating event.
  • Neoplastic Growth: Malignant tumours can cause pain and swelling through several mechanisms. Rapidly growing tumours can stretch surrounding tissues and nerves. Invasion into the pectoral muscles or chest wall causes fixed, persistent pain. Obstruction of dermal lymphatics by cancer cells leads to lymphoedema, causing the characteristic skin thickening and pitting known as 'peau d'orange', a hallmark of inflammatory breast cancer.
  • Musculoskeletal Origin: Pain can be referred to the breast area from the underlying chest wall. Inflammation of the costochondral joints (costochondritis), muscle strain of the pectoralis major, or rib pathology can produce pain that is perceived by the patient as originating in the breast. This pain is typically well-localised and reproducible on palpation of the chest wall.
  • Nerve-Related Pain: Irritation or compression of the intercostal nerves (thoracic radiculopathy) can cause a burning or shooting pain that radiates in a dermatomal pattern across the chest wall, which can be mistaken for breast pain. A classic example is the pre-eruptive phase of Herpes Zoster (shingles).
Differential Diagnosis (DDx) Framework

A structured approach to the differential diagnosis of breast pain or swelling is essential to ensure life-threatening or serious conditions are prioritised.

Must not miss!
  • Breast cancer: Often presents as a painless lump, but can cause pain, swelling, skin changes, or nipple abnormalities.
  • Metastatic cancer (chest wall): Secondary deposits from other primary cancers (e.g., lung) causing localised, persistent chest wall pain.
  • Pulmonary Embolism: Can present with chest pain that may be perceived in the breast area, typically pleuritic and associated with breathlessness.
  • Myocardial Infarction: Can present with atypical chest pain radiating to the breast area, especially in women.
Common causes
  • Cyclical breast pain: Diffuse, heavy, or aching pain, often in the upper outer quadrants, that correlates with the menstrual cycle.
  • Pregnancy: Hormonally-driven breast tenderness and swelling, particularly common in the first trimester.
  • Contraceptives: Hormonal contraceptives can induce breast pain and tenderness as a side effect.
  • Fibrocystic disease: Benign, lumpy or rope-like breast tissue texture that can be associated with pain and tenderness.
  • Mastitis: Infection of the breast tissue causing localised pain, swelling, redness, and warmth, often with systemic fever.
  • Breast abscess: A localised collection of pus within the breast, presenting as a painful, fluctuant, tender mass.
  • Musculoskeletal pain: Pain originating from the chest wall muscles or ribs that is reproducible on palpation or with movement.
  • Trauma-induced Breast Pain or Swelling: Pain or bruising resulting from a direct injury to the breast.
  • COVID-19: Can cause myalgia and chest pain as part of the systemic viral illness.
Easily missed
  • Medication-induced Breast Pain or Swelling: Caused by drugs such as spironolactone, antidepressants, or antipsychotics.
  • Herpes Zoster: A unilateral, burning, dermatomal pain that precedes the characteristic vesicular rash.
  • Thoracic Radiculopathy: Nerve root pain from the thoracic spine causing a band-like pain across the chest.
  • Psychogenic: Breast pain related to anxiety or other psychological distress, a diagnosis of exclusion.
Other Differential Diagnoses to consider
  • COVID-19: Can cause myalgias, chest pain, and other systemic symptoms
  • Costochondritis: Inflammation of costal cartilage, presenting with chest wall pain, sometimes mistaken for breast pain
  • Abscess from non-breast origin: Infection from adjacent skin structures causing local swelling or pain
  • Inflammatory breast cancer: A rare but aggressive form of breast cancer presenting with breast swelling, redness, and pain
  • Heart failure: Occasionally leads to chest wall oedema or tenderness in severe cases
  • Paget’s disease of the breast: A rare cancer presenting with nipple or areolar pain, itching, and changes
History Taking
**Patient’s Description**: How can I help you today?
Starting with an open-ended question allows the patient to describe their symptoms in their own words, providing valuable insight into their concerns and perspective. Relevance: This approach guides the clinician in formulating more specific, targeted questions based on the patient’s narrative.
Can you describe the nature of the breast pain or swelling?
  • This question helps differentiate between types of pain (sharp, dull, throbbing) and the presence of swelling or tenderness.
  • Relevance: Cyclical, dull pain may suggest fibrocystic disease, whereas sharp or burning pain could indicate infection (e.g., mastitis or abscess) or nerve involvement (e.g., thoracic radiculopathy).
When did you first notice these symptoms?
  • Onset can provide clues about the underlying cause, distinguishing acute from chronic conditions.
  • Relevance: Sudden onset with severe pain may suggest an acute inflammatory or infective process, whereas a gradual onset could indicate fibrocystic changes or a musculoskeletal cause.
Is there anything that makes the pain worse or better?
  • Understanding triggers or relieving factors can point towards specific diagnoses.
  • Relevance: Pain worsened by movement or posture may suggest musculoskeletal causes, while hormonal fluctuations indicating cyclical pain can suggest benign breast disease.
Have you experienced any additional symptoms, such as fever, redness, or a change in the breast skin?
  • Associated symptoms can help distinguish between differentials like infection and malignancy.
  • Relevance: Fever and redness indicate infection (mastitis, abscess), whereas skin changes (dimpling or redness) raise suspicion for inflammatory breast cancer.
Do you have a personal or family history of breast disease or cancer?
  • Personal or family history can significantly impact the assessment of breast pain and cancer risk.
  • Relevance: A positive history may raise suspicion for breast cancer, while no family history may suggest a benign cause.
Have you started or changed any medications recently?
  • Some medications can lead to breast pain or swelling, such as hormonal therapies or certain antipsychotics.
  • Relevance: Positive response could indicate medication-induced symptoms, while negative response reduces this likelihood.
**Ideas, Concerns, and Expectations (ICE)**
  • Understanding the patient’s ideas, concerns, and expectations (ICE) provides insight into their mental state, potential anxieties (e.g., fear of cancer), and desired outcomes.
  • Relevance: Addresses the patient’s perspective, addressing possible concerns (e.g., breast cancer or heart issues) and expectations, enhancing trust and patient-centred care.
Examination Strategy
Inspection of the breasts
  • Inspect both breasts with the patient sitting upright, looking for symmetry, skin changes (redness, dimpling, peau d'orange), and visible lumps or asymmetry.
  • Relevance: Skin changes like redness or swelling suggest mastitis or breast abscess, while dimpling or peau d'orange appearance may indicate inflammatory breast cancer. Absence of these changes may favour benign causes like fibrocystic changes.
Palpation of the breasts
  • Palpate both breasts systematically using the flat of the fingers to detect lumps, tenderness, or induration.
  • Relevance: Tenderness with warmth and swelling suggests infection (mastitis or abscess), while a fixed, hard lump may indicate malignancy. Diffuse lumpiness without focal tenderness could indicate fibrocystic changes.
Examination of the nipples
  • Inspect for nipple discharge, retraction, scaling, or ulceration, as these can be signs of underlying pathology.
  • Relevance: Nipple retraction or discharge (especially if bloody) may raise suspicion for malignancy, while scaling or ulceration can suggest Paget’s disease of the breast. Normal nipples reduce the likelihood of these diagnoses.
Axillary and supraclavicular lymph node examination
  • Palpate the axillary and supraclavicular lymph nodes for any enlargement, tenderness, or firmness.
  • Relevance: Enlarged, non-tender nodes raise suspicion for malignancy, while tender, mobile nodes may suggest infection or inflammation. Absence of lymphadenopathy can help reduce suspicion of malignancy or systemic infection.
Assessment of the chest wall
  • Palpate along the chest wall and ribs to identify any tenderness or palpable masses that might indicate musculoskeletal causes of breast pain.
  • Relevance: Localised tenderness along the chest wall may indicate costochondritis or a musculoskeletal origin. A lack of chest wall tenderness may suggest the breast tissue itself as the source of pain.
Examination for signs of systemic illness
  • Assess for fever, generalised lymphadenopathy, or other systemic signs that might suggest an infectious or inflammatory aetiology.
  • Relevance: Fever or generalised lymphadenopathy can support diagnoses such as mastitis or systemic infections. The absence of systemic symptoms may reduce suspicion for infections and help focus on localised or benign breast conditions.
Red Flag Synthesis
Unilateral, hard, fixed lump in the breast
  • A hard, immobile lump suggests a concerning mass, potentially indicating malignancy, especially if unilateral and non-tender.
  • Significance: This finding could indicate breast cancer, requiring urgent imaging and biopsy to determine diagnosis.
Nipple discharge, particularly if bloody or clear and unilateral
  • Bloody or clear discharge from one nipple may suggest an underlying malignancy or intraductal pathology, rather than benign ductal ectasia.
  • Significance: This finding raises suspicion for breast cancer or ductal carcinoma in situ, warranting prompt evaluation with imaging and possibly cytology.
Skin changes such as dimpling, thickening, or peau d'orange
  • Skin dimpling or an “orange-peel” texture suggests potential lymphatic obstruction, often associated with inflammatory breast cancer.
  • Significance: These skin changes may indicate inflammatory breast cancer, an aggressive malignancy that requires urgent specialist referral and imaging.
Unexplained fever with breast redness or warmth
  • Systemic symptoms, along with localised breast warmth or redness, suggest infection, such as mastitis or breast abscess, which can progress rapidly if untreated.
  • Significance: May indicate mastitis or abscess formation; untreated, these can lead to sepsis, especially in immunocompromised or postpartum patients, necessitating urgent antibiotic therapy.
Axillary or supraclavicular lymphadenopathy
  • Enlarged, non-tender lymph nodes, particularly supraclavicular, raise concerns for metastatic spread or systemic malignancy.
  • Significance: Persistent lymphadenopathy in these areas can be a sign of breast cancer or other systemic cancers, necessitating further imaging and biopsy.
Sudden onset, severe chest pain or difficulty breathing
  • Acute chest pain or breathlessness in patients with breast pain/swelling warrants investigation for possible pulmonary embolism or myocardial infarction, especially in patients with risk factors.
  • Significance: May indicate life-threatening conditions like pulmonary embolism or myocardial infarction, requiring immediate emergency management.
Rapidly enlarging breast mass
  • A mass that grows quickly within days to weeks is concerning for aggressive pathologies such as inflammatory breast cancer.
  • Significance: Suggests the need for urgent imaging and potential biopsy, as rapid growth is characteristic of certain aggressive malignancies.
Investigations Strategy & Interpretation
Mammography
  • Mammography is an imaging study that provides a detailed view of breast tissue, helping to identify masses, calcifications, and other abnormal changes.
  • Result Significance: Positive findings, such as suspicious masses or calcifications, raise suspicion for malignancy and may require further biopsy. Negative results in younger patients may be followed by ultrasound, as mammography can be less sensitive in dense breast tissue.
Breast Ultrasound
  • Ultrasound is useful for differentiating between solid and cystic masses and is particularly helpful in younger patients with dense breast tissue or when assessing palpable masses.
  • Result Significance: Positive findings such as solid, irregular masses increase suspicion for malignancy. Cystic findings may suggest benign conditions like fibrocystic changes. A negative ultrasound may help rule out cysts or solid lesions in the area of concern.
Fine Needle Aspiration (FNA) or Core Needle Biopsy
  • FNA or core biopsy allows for sampling of cells or tissue from suspicious areas to confirm the presence of malignancy or determine benign pathology.
  • Result Significance: Positive findings of atypical cells or malignancy confirm the diagnosis of cancer, while benign pathology may suggest conditions such as fibroadenoma or cysts. Negative results or inadequate samples may require repeat biopsy if clinical suspicion remains high.
Complete Blood Count (CBC)
  • A CBC can help identify systemic infection or inflammation, which may be associated with mastitis or breast abscess.
  • Result Significance: Elevated white cell count may indicate infection (e.g., mastitis or abscess), while a normal count might lower suspicion for infection but does not rule out localised breast pathology.
C-Reactive Protein (CRP)
  • CRP is an inflammatory marker that can help gauge the presence and severity of an inflammatory or infectious process.
  • Result Significance: Elevated CRP may support the diagnosis of an active infection or inflammation (e.g., mastitis or abscess), while a low CRP suggests that inflammation is less likely to be a significant component.
Electrocardiogram (ECG)
  • An ECG is essential if there is concern that breast pain may be referred pain from cardiac causes, such as myocardial infarction, especially in patients with cardiac risk factors.
  • Result Significance: Abnormalities on ECG may indicate myocardial infarction or ischemia, requiring immediate cardiac evaluation. A normal ECG does not entirely exclude cardiac causes but makes them less likely.
Chest X-Ray
  • A chest X-ray is useful if there is suspicion of metastatic disease to the chest wall or concern for other thoracic pathology, such as pulmonary embolism.
  • Result Significance: Findings suggestive of chest wall masses may indicate metastatic cancer. Clear lung fields decrease the likelihood of pulmonary causes of chest/breast pain.
Evidence-Based Diagnosis (EBDx)
Mammography for detecting breast cancer
  • Mammography is a primary imaging modality for detecting breast cancer, particularly in patients over 40 years or those with high-risk factors.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 87%, Specificity: 92%, Positive Likelihood Ratio: 10.9, Negative Likelihood Ratio: 0.14.
  • Reference: American College of Radiology (ACR) Guidelines, 2017.
  • Significance: Positive findings suggest breast cancer or ductal carcinoma in situ (DCIS). A negative mammogram significantly lowers the likelihood of breast cancer but does not completely exclude it, particularly in younger patients with dense breast tissue.
Breast Ultrasound for differentiating solid from cystic lesions
  • Ultrasound helps distinguish between solid and cystic masses and is often used alongside mammography, especially in dense breast tissue.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 95% for cystic lesions, Specificity: 85%.
  • Reference: Breast Cancer Surveillance Consortium (BCSC) data, 2019.
  • Significance: Positive findings of a solid mass raise suspicion for malignancy, while cystic lesions are generally benign. Ultrasound is useful in assessing palpable lumps and can guide fine-needle aspiration if needed.
Nipple discharge characteristics for breast cancer
  • Unilateral, bloody, or clear discharge from one nipple is associated with a higher likelihood of malignancy, unlike bilateral or milky discharge, which is often benign.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 56% for malignancy with bloody discharge, Specificity: 95%.
  • Reference: National Comprehensive Cancer Network (NCCN) Breast Cancer Guidelines, 2021.
  • Significance: A positive finding of unilateral or bloody discharge suggests possible malignancy (e.g., ductal carcinoma in situ), while negative findings reduce this likelihood.
Fine Needle Aspiration (FNA) or Core Biopsy for confirming breast cancer
  • Biopsy confirms malignancy by sampling cells from suspicious masses and is often considered the gold standard when a solid lesion is identified on imaging.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 97%, Specificity: 98% for detecting malignancy.
  • Reference: ACR Practice Parameters for Breast Imaging, 2020.
  • Significance: Positive findings on biopsy confirm malignancy, such as invasive ductal carcinoma, while negative findings can confirm benign pathology, providing reassurance and often avoiding unnecessary surgical interventions.
CRP levels for infection-related breast pain (e.g., mastitis)
  • Elevated CRP levels support an inflammatory or infectious process, useful in differentiating mastitis or breast abscess from non-infectious causes of breast pain.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 88%, Specificity: 70% for bacterial infections.
  • Reference: Cochrane Review on Biomarkers for Infection, 2019.
  • Significance: A raised CRP with localised breast pain supports a diagnosis of mastitis or abscess, while a normal CRP makes infection less likely.
Presence of axillary or supraclavicular lymphadenopathy in suspected breast cancer
  • Persistent lymphadenopathy, particularly in the supraclavicular region, can indicate breast cancer metastasis.
  • Evidence: Positive predictive value (PPV) for malignancy in palpable axillary lymph nodes: 70%.
  • Reference: American Society of Clinical Oncology (ASCO) Guidelines, 2018.
  • Significance: Positive findings of lymphadenopathy are highly suggestive of malignancy, prompting staging and additional imaging to assess for metastatic disease. Negative findings reduce suspicion of metastasis, allowing focus on primary breast pathology.
Diagnostic Timeout & Cognitive Biases

Awareness of common thinking errors is essential to improve diagnostic accuracy and patient safety when assessing a patient with breast pain or swelling. Before finalising a diagnosis, it is good practice for clinicians at all levels to take a 'diagnostic timeout' to challenge their own reasoning.

  • Anchoring Bias: The tendency to anchor on the patient's age or a history of benign cyclical pain, leading to premature closure and failure to consider cancer. A young patient can still get breast cancer, and a patient with known fibrocystic change can develop a new, separate malignancy. A key question to ask is: "Could this be cancer, and what features would make me more or less concerned?"
  • Confirmation Bias: Seeking or overemphasising features that support a benign diagnosis (e.g., cyclical nature of the pain) while downplaying red flags (e.g., a new skin change or a subtle palpable asymmetry). It's crucial to actively seek out and document the absence of red flags.
  • Atypical Presentations: A major pitfall is failing to recognise that inflammatory breast cancer can present without a discrete lump, mimicking mastitis with pain, redness, and swelling. A history of non-response to antibiotics in a suspected 'mastitis' case, particularly in a non-lactating woman, should immediately raise suspicion for inflammatory cancer.
  • Minimisation/Reassurance Bias: The strong desire to reassure an anxious patient can lead to an incomplete assessment. It is easy to label pain as 'musculoskeletal' or 'hormonal' without performing a thorough breast and lymph node examination. A complete examination is mandatory for every patient presenting with a new breast symptom.
Illustrative Management Pathways

For educational purposes, this section describes the typical principles of management for key diagnoses. These are not prescriptive instructions. All management must be guided by official UK guidelines, local formularies, and senior clinical advice.

Suspected Breast Cancer

Any patient with clinical findings suspicious for breast cancer (e.g., a discrete lump, concerning skin changes, suspicious lymphadenopathy) requires an urgent two-week-wait (2WW) referral to a specialist breast clinic. The cornerstone of diagnosis is 'Triple Assessment', which includes: 1) Clinical history and examination, 2) Breast imaging (mammography and/or ultrasound), and 3) Tissue sampling (core biopsy or fine-needle aspiration). This combined approach provides a highly accurate diagnosis to guide further management by the multidisciplinary team (MDT).

Mastitis and Breast Abscess

Management of mastitis focuses on antibiotic therapy (e.g., flucloxacillin, targeting Staph. aureus), effective milk removal (in lactating women), and analgesia. If a breast abscess is suspected (a tender, fluctuant mass), an ultrasound is required to confirm the diagnosis and guide drainage, which is typically performed via needle aspiration. Patients who are systemically unwell or fail to respond to oral antibiotics may require hospital admission for intravenous therapy.

Benign Breast Pain (e.g., Cyclical Mastalgia)

Once serious pathology has been excluded through a thorough history and examination (and imaging, if indicated), management focuses on explanation, reassurance, and symptomatic relief. First-line advice includes wearing a well-fitting, supportive bra. Simple analgesia (e.g., paracetamol, topical NSAIDs) can be effective. For severe, persistent symptoms, hormonal treatments may be considered in a specialist setting, but the mainstay of management is reassurance and lifestyle advice.

Reflection & Learning

These brief cases illustrate the application of clinical reasoning to different presentations of breast pain and swelling. They are for educational purposes only.

Case 1: Benign Cyclical Pain in Primary Care

A 28-year-old woman presents to her GP with a 6-month history of bilateral breast tenderness and a feeling of 'heaviness'. The symptoms are worse in the week leading up to her period and resolve completely once menstruation begins. On examination, her breasts feel diffusely nodular, but there are no discrete lumps, skin changes, or lymphadenopathy. The problem representation is a young, pre-menopausal woman with classic cyclical mastalgia. After a reassuring examination, the management plan involves explaining the hormonal cause of her symptoms, advising on a supportive bra, and suggesting simple analgesia as needed.

Case 2: Suspected Malignancy

A 65-year-old post-menopausal woman presents with a new, painless lump in her right breast that she noticed 3 weeks ago. She has no other symptoms. Her family history is significant for a sister diagnosed with breast cancer at age 55. On examination, there is a hard, irregular, 2cm mass in the upper outer quadrant of the right breast. It feels fixed to the underlying tissue. No axillary nodes are palpable. The problem representation is a post-menopausal woman with a new, hard, fixed breast lump and a family history of breast cancer, which is highly suspicious for malignancy. An urgent 2WW referral to the breast clinic for triple assessment is the mandatory next step.

Case 3: Lactational Mastitis

A 32-year-old woman, 6 weeks postpartum and breastfeeding, presents with a 2-day history of a painful, red, swollen area on her left breast. She also feels generally unwell with a fever and body aches. On examination, she has a temperature of 38.5°C. There is a tender, warm, erythematous, wedge-shaped area on the lateral aspect of her left breast. There is no fluctuant mass. The problem representation is a lactating woman with acute, localised breast inflammation and systemic signs of infection, consistent with lactational mastitis. Immediate management involves prescribing oral antibiotics (e.g., flucloxacillin), providing analgesia, and giving crucial advice on continuing to breastfeed or express milk from the affected breast to prevent engorgement and abscess formation.
