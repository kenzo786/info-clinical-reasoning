Limping Child Diagnostic Reasoning Reference
Context: Limping Child Assessment
Overview

A child presenting with a limp is a common and diagnostically challenging scenario in both primary and emergency care. The potential causes are extensive, ranging from trivial, self-limiting injuries to severe, limb- or life-threatening conditions such as septic arthritis or malignancy. The primary challenge for the clinician is to systematically and efficiently differentiate between these possibilities, ensuring that urgent diagnoses are identified promptly while avoiding unnecessary investigation for benign causes.

A meticulous approach, beginning with a detailed history and thorough physical examination, is essential. The assessment must be guided by an awareness of age-specific epidemiology (e.g., transient synovitis in younger children, slipped capital femoral epiphysis in adolescents) and a high index of suspicion for 'red flag' features that point towards serious underlying pathology. This educational module is designed to provide a foundational framework for the clinical reasoning process for a limping child. For definitive, real-time management protocols, clinicians must refer to official UK guidelines from authoritative bodies such as NICE (e.g., CKS for Limping Child in under 16s) and relevant Royal College publications.

Pathophysiology/Basic Science

Understanding the underlying mechanism of the limp is fundamental to forming a differential diagnosis. A limp is an abnormal gait pattern caused by pain, weakness, or deformity. The causative pathologies can be grouped by their mechanism:

  • Infective/Inflammatory: This is a critical category. In septic arthritis, bacteria directly seed the synovial space, leading to a rapid and destructive inflammatory cascade, purulent effusion, and severe pain on any joint movement. In osteomyelitis, infection of the bone itself causes localised inflammation, periosteal reaction, and bone destruction. In contrast, transient synovitis is thought to be a self-limiting, post-viral inflammatory response within the synovium, causing an effusion and pain but without the destructive potential of infection.
  • Mechanical/Developmental: In Slipped Capital Femoral Epiphysis (SCFE), weakness in the proximal femoral growth plate (physis) allows the femoral head to slip posteriorly and inferiorly, typically occurring in overweight adolescents during a growth spurt. Developmental Dysplasia of the Hip (DDH) involves abnormal formation of the hip joint, leading to instability and, if uncorrected, a painless "waddling" gait.
  • Avascular Necrosis: This involves the death of bone tissue due to a loss of blood supply. The classic example is Legg-Calvé-Perthes disease, where the blood supply to the femoral head is interrupted. This leads to collapse, fragmentation, and subsequent abnormal regrowth of the femoral head, causing pain and a limp.
  • Traumatic: Direct trauma can cause an obvious fracture, leading to pain and inability to bear weight. Less obvious are toddler's fractures (subtle spiral fractures of the tibia) or stress fractures from overuse. Soft tissue injuries like sprains and strains cause pain through ligamentous or musculotendinous injury.
  • Neoplastic: Malignancies such as leukaemia can cause bone pain and a limp through the infiltration of bone marrow by blast cells, which increases intra-medullary pressure. Primary bone tumours (e.g., osteosarcoma, Ewing's sarcoma) cause pain by destroying local bone and irritating the periosteum.
Differential Diagnosis (DDx) Framework

A structured approach to the differential diagnosis of a limping child is essential to ensure life-threatening or limb-threatening conditions are prioritised.

Must not miss!
  • Bone fracture: Presents with acute pain, swelling, and inability to bear weight, often following a specific injury.
  • Septic arthritis: A severe bacterial joint infection causing fever, intense pain, swelling, and refusal to move or bear weight on the affected limb.
  • Osteomyelitis: An infection of the bone itself, presenting with localised bone pain, tenderness, fever, and often a limp or refusal to use the limb.
  • Slipped femoral epiphysis: Occurs in adolescents, presenting as hip, groin, or knee pain and a limp, where the head of the femur slips off the neck.
  • Perthes’ disease: Avascular necrosis of the femoral head in younger children, causing an insidious onset of a limp and hip or knee pain.
  • Developmental Dysplasia of the Hip: A congenital or developmental abnormality of the hip joint, often presenting with a painless limp or waddling gait once the child starts walking.
  • Bone tumours: Malignancies like osteosarcoma or Ewing's sarcoma can cause persistent, localised bone pain, often worse at night, and a limp.
  • Sarcoma: A cancer of connective tissues (including bone or muscle) that can present with a painful lump, swelling, and a limp.
  • Leukaemia: This cancer of the blood cells can present with bone pain, limping, fatigue, bruising, and fever due to marrow infiltration.
  • Child abuse: A limp may result from non-accidental injury, with inconsistent histories or multiple injuries being key red flags.
Common causes
  • Sprains and strains: Common minor injuries to ligaments or muscles from twists or falls, causing localised pain and a temporary limp.
  • Transient synovitis: The most common cause of hip pain in children, presenting as an acute, self-limiting inflammation of the hip joint, often following a viral illness.
  • Osgood-Schlatter's disease: A common cause of knee pain in active adolescents, caused by inflammation of the tibial tuberosity at the point of the patellar tendon insertion.
  • Osteochondritis dissecans: A condition where a small segment of bone separates from its surrounding region due to a lack of blood supply, typically affecting the knee.
  • Acute viral myositis: Muscle inflammation caused by a viral infection (e.g., influenza, COVID-19), leading to muscle pain, weakness, and difficulty walking.
  • COVID-19: Can be associated with various musculoskeletal symptoms, including myalgia, arthralgia, or inflammatory syndromes that may cause a limp.
Easily missed
  • Abdominal pain presentation: Conditions like appendicitis or a psoas abscess can irritate the hip flexor muscles, causing a limp as a referred symptom.
  • Osteomalacia: Softening of the bones, typically due to vitamin D deficiency, which can cause bone pain, muscle weakness, and a waddling gait.
Other Differential Diagnoses to consider
  • Juvenile idiopathic arthritis (JIA): Chronic inflammatory arthritis causing joint pain and swelling.
  • Reactive arthritis: Joint pain post-infection, often following GI or GU infections.
  • Rheumatic fever: Post-streptococcal condition causing migratory joint pain, fever, and systemic symptoms.
  • Neurological disorders (e.g., cerebral palsy): Conditions with spasticity or muscle weakness, affecting gait.
  • Legg-Calvé-Perthes disease: Avascular necrosis of the femoral head, causing hip pain and limping.
  • Hypermobile joint syndrome: Joint pain and instability due to hypermobility, potentially leading to joint strain.
  • Sickle cell disease: Can cause bone pain (dactylitis) and avascular necrosis due to sickling episodes.
  • Lyme disease: Bacterial infection from tick bites, occasionally presenting with joint pain or swelling.
History Taking
Patient’s Description: Can you tell me more about what brought you here today?
Starting with open-ended questions allows the patient (or parent) to share their concerns in their own words, helping uncover the primary symptom and any initial observations. Relevance: This opening helps guide further focused questions on symptoms such as pain, stiffness, or recent injuries, and directs attention to potential causes like infection, trauma, or developmental conditions.
Can you describe when the limp began?
  • Understanding the onset (acute, gradual, or recurrent) helps narrow down causes; acute onset might suggest trauma or infection, whereas gradual onset may indicate developmental or degenerative conditions.
  • Relevance: A sudden onset could point to fracture, septic arthritis, or slipped femoral epiphysis, while a gradual onset may suggest conditions like Perthes’ disease or juvenile idiopathic arthritis (JIA).
Is there pain? If so, where is it located, and how would you describe it?
  • Determining the location and character of pain helps differentiate between localised versus systemic or referred causes.
  • Relevance: Localised hip pain could suggest Perthes’ disease or slipped femoral epiphysis; diffuse or non-specific pain might indicate leukaemia or viral myositis. Absence of pain might suggest a neurological or functional cause.
Have there been any recent injuries or falls?
  • This question assesses for trauma as a potential cause, which is particularly relevant in active children or toddlers learning to walk.
  • Relevance: A positive history may indicate fractures, sprains, or strains, whereas a negative history might shift focus to inflammatory or systemic causes.
Do any particular activities make the limp better or worse?
  • Assessing for activity-related changes helps in evaluating mechanical versus inflammatory causes.
  • Relevance: Increased pain with activity may indicate conditions like Osgood-Schlatter’s disease or osteochondritis dissecans, whereas rest pain might suggest a more concerning diagnosis such as malignancy.
Has there been any fever, recent infections, or other systemic symptoms (e.g., weight loss, fatigue)?
  • Systemic symptoms can indicate an infectious or inflammatory cause or raise concern for malignancy.
  • Relevance: Fever or recent infection may suggest septic arthritis or reactive arthritis, while systemic signs like weight loss or fatigue could suggest leukaemia or other malignancies.
Has the child or family recently had COVID-19?
  • This assesses for potential post-viral complications, such as myositis or reactive arthritis.
  • Relevance: A positive history of recent COVID-19 may indicate post-viral myositis or joint pain secondary to immune responses.
Does the child have any other joint issues, rashes, or swelling?
  • This question explores signs of systemic inflammatory or autoimmune conditions.
  • Relevance: Additional joint pain or rashes could indicate juvenile idiopathic arthritis, reactive arthritis, or systemic inflammatory conditions like rheumatic fever.
Any previous health issues, family history of bone/joint disorders, or use of medications?
  • This assesses for underlying health issues or genetic predispositions.
  • Relevance: Family history of developmental hip dysplasia, autoimmune conditions, or osteoporosis may guide the differential diagnosis. Medications like steroids may contribute to bone issues.
Ideas, Concerns, and Expectations (ICE)
  • ICE questions are essential in understanding the patient's or parent's perspective, worries, and hopes regarding the diagnosis and treatment.
  • Relevance: This allows the clinician to address fears, such as cancer or permanent disability, and to manage expectations around the diagnostic process and follow-up.
Examination Strategy
General Observation and Gait Assessment
  • Begin by observing the child’s general appearance, gait, and any obvious deformities, asymmetry, or discomfort.
  • Relevance: A limp favouring one side may suggest trauma or joint pathology, while a waddling gait may indicate developmental dysplasia of the hip. Guarded or painful movements may point to infections like septic arthritis.
Inspection of Lower Limbs
  • Inspect the hips, knees, and ankles for swelling, redness, bruising, or deformities, and observe muscle bulk and symmetry.
  • Relevance: Swelling or redness over a joint may indicate infection or inflammation (e.g., septic arthritis or juvenile idiopathic arthritis), while bruising could indicate trauma. Atrophy of thigh or calf muscles may suggest chronic joint or hip pathology.
Palpation of Lower Limbs
  • Palpate the joints, bones, and soft tissues for tenderness, warmth, and crepitus.
  • Relevance: Tenderness in specific areas, like over the hip or knee, may suggest transient synovitis, Perthes' disease, or trauma. Warmth and pain on palpation suggest infection or inflammatory processes, such as osteomyelitis or septic arthritis.
Range of Motion (ROM) Testing
  • Assess active and passive ROM in the hip, knee, and ankle joints, looking for limitations or pain.
  • Relevance: Pain on passive movement, particularly at the hip, could indicate septic arthritis or transient synovitis, while a limited ROM in multiple joints might point to juvenile idiopathic arthritis. A normal ROM with pain only on active movement might indicate soft tissue injury.
Galeazzi Test (for leg length discrepancy)
  • With the child lying supine, flex both hips and knees to 90° and observe the knee height differences.
  • Relevance: A positive Galeazzi test, where one knee is lower than the other, may suggest hip dislocation or developmental dysplasia of the hip (DDH), especially in younger children.
Trendelenburg Test
  • Ask the child to stand on one leg while lifting the opposite knee. Observe for any pelvic drop on the non-weight-bearing side.
  • Relevance: A positive Trendelenburg sign, where the pelvis drops on the unsupported side, may indicate hip instability or weakness of the gluteal muscles, commonly seen in conditions like Perthes’ disease or slipped femoral epiphysis.
Spinal Examination
  • Inspect and palpate the spine to assess for tenderness or deformities, and perform a ROM test.
  • Relevance: Spinal abnormalities or tenderness might suggest referred pain from the spine or neurological conditions that can affect gait, such as scoliosis or spinal infections.
Neurological Examination of the Lower Limbs
  • Evaluate motor strength, reflexes, and sensation in the lower limbs, and perform tests for tone and coordination.
  • Relevance: Weakness, hypertonia, or altered reflexes may point to a neurological cause for the limp, such as cerebral palsy or muscular dystrophy. Normal findings help exclude neurogenic causes.
Abdominal Examination
  • Palpate for any tenderness, masses, or organomegaly, which may suggest referred pain contributing to the limp.
  • Relevance: Right lower quadrant pain, for instance, could indicate appendicitis presenting with a limp, while abdominal masses could suggest leukaemia.
Red Flag Synthesis
Fever and Limp with Joint Pain or Swelling
  • Fever in conjunction with joint pain or swelling suggests a possible infectious or inflammatory cause, which may be life-threatening if not promptly treated.
  • Significance: This could indicate septic arthritis or osteomyelitis, both of which are medical emergencies requiring urgent treatment to prevent joint destruction and systemic infection.
Night Pain or Persistent Pain at Rest
  • Pain that disrupts sleep or is persistent even at rest is concerning and may indicate more serious underlying pathology, as opposed to benign or mechanical causes of limp.
  • Significance: This could signify a malignancy, such as leukaemia or bone tumours, necessitating immediate further investigation.
Weight Loss, Fatigue, or Generalised Malaise
  • Systemic symptoms like weight loss, fatigue, or malaise in a child with a limp are red flags that raise suspicion for serious systemic illnesses, particularly malignancies.
  • Significance: These symptoms could indicate leukaemia or other cancers, warranting urgent investigation for haematologic or metastatic diseases.
Severe Localised Bone Pain and Swelling
  • Severe, localised pain with or without swelling that is disproportionate to any reported injury may indicate a serious pathology beyond a simple musculoskeletal injury.
  • Significance: This could suggest bone tumours such as osteosarcoma, Ewing sarcoma, or other malignancies, necessitating urgent imaging and referral.
Altered Mental State or New-Onset Neurological Symptoms
  • Changes in mental state or the appearance of neurological deficits are worrying signs that can indicate central nervous system involvement, such as spinal cord or intracranial pathologies.
  • Significance: This may indicate neurological conditions or spinal cord compression, requiring urgent neurological assessment.
Asymmetry in Leg Length or Waddling Gait
  • Noticeable asymmetry or a waddling gait in children is suggestive of underlying hip pathology, which may be progressive and lead to significant functional impairment if untreated.
  • Significance: This could indicate developmental dysplasia of the hip, slipped femoral epiphysis, or Perthes’ disease, all of which require prompt orthopaedic referral.
History or Signs of Non-Accidental Injury
  • Unexplained or inconsistent injury history, bruising in unusual areas, or other signs of abuse are red flags for potential non-accidental injury.
  • Significance: Non-accidental injury is a safeguarding emergency, necessitating immediate involvement of child protection services and further assessment.
Investigations Strategy & Interpretation
Full Blood Count (FBC)
  • FBC can help identify signs of infection, inflammation, or haematologic abnormalities, which may be linked to conditions such as infection or malignancy.
  • Result Significance: Elevated white cell count may indicate infection (e.g., osteomyelitis or septic arthritis), while anaemia or abnormal leukocytes could suggest leukaemia.
Erythrocyte Sedimentation Rate (ESR) and C-Reactive Protein (CRP)
  • ESR and CRP are inflammatory markers that can indicate the presence of an inflammatory or infectious process.
  • Result Significance: Elevated ESR and CRP levels support diagnoses such as septic arthritis, osteomyelitis, or juvenile idiopathic arthritis, while normal levels make infection or significant inflammation less likely.
Blood Culture
  • Blood cultures help to identify bloodstream infections, which can also provide insight into potential joint or bone infections.
  • Result Significance: Positive cultures suggest bacteraemia, which may support a diagnosis of septic arthritis or osteomyelitis, warranting urgent treatment.
X-ray of the Affected Limb
  • X-rays are useful for visualising bone integrity, joint spaces, and any obvious fractures or bone lesions.
  • Result Significance: Positive findings (e.g., fracture, bone tumour, or Perthes’ disease) help confirm specific diagnoses, while negative findings may help exclude structural bone issues.
MRI of the Affected Limb
  • MRI provides detailed imaging of soft tissues, bone marrow, and joints, and can detect pathology not visible on X-ray.
  • Result Significance: MRI can identify early osteomyelitis, septic arthritis, or soft tissue abnormalities, which are crucial for conditions where early treatment is essential.
Ultrasound of the Hip
  • Ultrasound is especially useful for evaluating effusions or inflammation in the hip joint, and is non-invasive.
  • Result Significance: Presence of joint effusion may suggest transient synovitis or septic arthritis. A negative result can help rule out effusion-related causes.
Joint Aspiration and Fluid Analysis
  • Joint aspiration allows for analysis of synovial fluid to assess for infection, crystals, or inflammatory markers.
  • Result Significance: Positive findings for bacteria confirm septic arthritis, while inflammatory markers support an inflammatory condition like juvenile idiopathic arthritis. Clear fluid without pathogens may support transient synovitis.
Bone Scan
  • Bone scans are used to detect areas of increased bone metabolism, helpful in identifying infections, fractures, or tumours.
  • Result Significance: Positive findings support diagnoses such as osteomyelitis or bone tumours, while a normal scan may rule out significant bony pathology.
Evidence-Based Diagnosis (EBDx)
Fever and Elevated Inflammatory Markers in Suspected Septic Arthritis
  • Fever combined with raised inflammatory markers (CRP and ESR) significantly increases the likelihood of septic arthritis in a child with a limp.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 90%, Specificity: 85% for CRP >20 mg/L; Likelihood Ratio: CRP >20 mg/L has a positive LR of 8.0 for septic arthritis.
  • Reference: Caird et al., "The Role of Laboratory Studies in Differentiating Between Septic Arthritis and Transient Synovitis of the Hip," Journal of Bone and Joint Surgery, 2006.
  • Significance: Positive findings strongly suggest septic arthritis, a medical emergency requiring urgent intervention. Negative findings reduce the likelihood of septic arthritis but do not fully exclude it.
Positive Blood Culture in Suspected Osteomyelitis or Septic Arthritis
  • Positive blood cultures provide a definitive diagnosis of bacteraemia, which is highly suggestive of either septic arthritis or osteomyelitis in the context of fever and a limp.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 50-60% in osteomyelitis; Specificity: >95% for bacterial aetiology if positive.
  • Reference: Peltola et al., "Blood Culture Findings in Children with Acute Haematogenous Osteomyelitis," Clinical Infectious Diseases, 2014.
  • Significance: Positive cultures indicate a high likelihood of bacterial osteomyelitis or septic arthritis, requiring antibiotic therapy. Negative results do not exclude infection, especially if CRP and ESR are raised.
Joint Effusion on Ultrasound in Suspected Transient Synovitis
  • The presence of joint effusion on ultrasound is supportive of transient synovitis, especially if fever and inflammatory markers are absent.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 100% for detection of effusion; however, the specificity for transient synovitis vs. septic arthritis requires clinical correlation.
  • Reference: Kocher et al., "Differentiating Between Septic Arthritis and Transient Synovitis of the Hip in Children: An Evidence-Based Clinical Prediction Algorithm," Journal of Bone and Joint Surgery, 1999.
  • Significance: Positive findings (joint effusion without fever or high CRP) support a diagnosis of transient synovitis. Effusion with raised inflammatory markers may instead indicate septic arthritis.
X-ray Findings in Suspected Slipped Capital Femoral Epiphysis (SCFE)
  • X-rays showing widening of the physis or displacement of the femoral head confirm SCFE, especially in adolescents presenting with hip, groin, or knee pain.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 95% for SCFE on lateral X-rays; Specificity: 100% in symptomatic cases.
  • Reference: Loder et al., "Slipped Capital Femoral Epiphysis," Journal of Pediatric Orthopaedics, 1993.
  • Significance: Positive X-ray findings confirm SCFE, a serious orthopaedic emergency requiring prompt surgical management to prevent further slippage and avascular necrosis. A negative X-ray does not rule out early or mild cases, where MRI may be warranted.
MRI Findings in Suspected Osteomyelitis
  • MRI is highly sensitive for detecting early changes in bone marrow associated with osteomyelitis, even when X-rays are normal.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 98%, Specificity: 92% for early osteomyelitis on MRI.
  • Reference: Connolly et al., "MRI for Early Detection of Acute Osteomyelitis in Children," Radiology, 2002.
  • Significance: Positive MRI findings confirm osteomyelitis and guide treatment with antibiotics or surgical debridement. A normal MRI can effectively rule out osteomyelitis in most cases.
Pain on Passive Movement in Suspected Septic Arthritis
  • Pain on passive joint movement is highly indicative of septic arthritis, especially in children with fever and hip involvement.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 85%, Specificity: 80% for septic arthritis.
  • Reference: Kocher et al., "Evidence-Based Algorithm for Septic Arthritis in Children," Journal of Bone and Joint Surgery, 1999.
  • Significance: Positive findings strongly suggest septic arthritis, especially when paired with fever and raised inflammatory markers. A negative result makes septic arthritis less likely but warrants further evaluation if other signs are present.
Diagnostic Timeout & Cognitive Biases

Awareness of common thinking errors is critical to improve diagnostic accuracy and patient safety when assessing a limping child. Before finalising a diagnosis, it is good practice for clinicians to take a 'diagnostic timeout' to challenge their own reasoning.

  • Anchoring Bias: The tendency to lock onto an initial piece of information, such as a recent minor fall, and attribute the limp to simple trauma. This can lead to prematurely closing the inquiry and missing a more serious underlying diagnosis like septic arthritis or leukaemia, where a history of minor trauma is common and coincidental. A key question to ask is: "If it weren't for the fall, what else could this be?"
  • Availability Heuristic: Overestimating the likelihood of common diagnoses like transient synovitis because they are frequently encountered, while under-considering rarer but more severe conditions. While transient synovitis is common, it is a diagnosis of exclusion; septic arthritis must be actively ruled out first.
  • Confirmation Bias: Selectively seeking or over-emphasising findings that support a preferred diagnosis while ignoring contradictory evidence. For example, focusing on a normal temperature to rule out infection while downplaying a significantly raised CRP or a child's refusal to bear weight.
  • Attribution Error (Safeguarding): A major pitfall is failing to consider non-accidental injury (NAI). This can occur when a clinician accepts an implausible or inconsistent history from caregivers without appropriate professional curiosity. Any discrepancy between the reported mechanism and the clinical findings must trigger safeguarding considerations.
Illustrative Management Pathways

For educational purposes, this section describes the typical principles of management for key diagnoses. These are not prescriptive instructions. All management must be guided by official UK guidelines, local formularies, and senior clinical advice.

Septic Arthritis

This is a surgical emergency requiring immediate hospital admission under the care of an orthopaedic team. Management is focused on urgent administration of intravenous antibiotics (following local microbiology guidelines) and surgical intervention. A surgical washout (arthrotomy or arthroscopy) is typically required to remove purulent material from the joint, reduce pressure, and obtain samples for culture to guide antibiotic therapy. This prevents cartilage destruction and long-term joint damage.

Slipped Capital Femoral Epiphysis (SCFE)

Once suspected, SCFE is an orthopaedic emergency. The child must be made strictly non-weight-bearing immediately to prevent further slippage. Urgent referral and admission to an orthopaedic unit is mandatory. The definitive treatment is surgical, involving in-situ fixation with a screw to stabilise the epiphysis and prevent further displacement and the risk of avascular necrosis of the femoral head.

Transient Synovitis

This is a diagnosis of exclusion. Once serious causes like septic arthritis have been confidently ruled out (e.g., the child is afebrile, well-appearing, has normal or near-normal inflammatory markers, and can tolerate some weight-bearing), management is supportive. This involves simple analgesia (e.g., paracetamol, ibuprofen), rest, and reassurance for the parents. Crucially, clear safety netting advice must be given to return for review if the child develops a fever, becomes systemically unwell, or the pain worsens or fails to improve within 24-48 hours.

Benign Causes (e.g., Minor Sprain, Myositis)

After a thorough assessment has excluded red flag conditions, management for minor traumatic or post-viral causes focuses on reassurance and symptomatic relief. This includes advice on relative rest, appropriate weight-bearing as tolerated, and simple analgesia. Safety netting is also vital, with instructions to seek re-evaluation if the limp persists beyond a few days or if new, concerning symptoms develop.

Reflection & Learning

These brief cases illustrate the application of clinical reasoning to different presentations of a limping child. They are for educational purposes only.

Case 1: Suspected Septic Arthritis in the Emergency Department

A 4-year-old boy presents to the Emergency Department with a 24-hour history of refusing to walk and crying with any movement of his right leg. He has a temperature of 38.8°C. On examination, he is tearful, holds his right hip flexed and externally rotated, and screams on any attempt at passive movement. The problem representation is a febrile, systemically unwell child with an acute, non-weight-bearing limp and exquisite pain on passive hip movement. This is highly suspicious for septic arthritis, an orthopaedic emergency. The immediate pathway involves blood tests (FBC, CRP, blood culture), urgent orthopaedic referral, and preparation for IV antibiotics and surgical washout.

Case 2: Suspected SCFE in General Practice

A 13-year-old boy, who is above the 98th centile for weight, presents to his GP with a 4-week history of a worsening limp and vague left knee pain. The pain is worse with activity. On examination, there is reduced internal rotation of the left hip. The problem representation is an overweight adolescent male with insidious onset of a limp and referred knee pain, with clinical signs of limited hip movement. This presentation is classic for a Slipped Capital Femoral Epiphysis (SCFE). The GP makes the child non-weight-bearing immediately and arranges for urgent hospital admission for pelvic X-rays and orthopaedic review.

Case 3: Transient Synovitis in Urgent Care

A 5-year-old girl presents to an urgent care centre with a limp that started this morning. Her mother reports the child had a cold last week. She is afebrile (37.1°C) and appears well. She is limping but can bear weight for a few steps. There is some pain and limitation on the extremes of hip rotation, but the joint is not exquisitely tender. The problem representation is a well, afebrile child with a post-viral, weight-bearing limp. After blood tests show a normal FBC and only a minimally raised CRP, a diagnosis of probable transient synovitis is made. Management involves analgesia, reassurance, and clear safety netting advice to return if she develops a fever or is unable to bear weight.
