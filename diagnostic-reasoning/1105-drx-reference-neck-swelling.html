Neck Swelling Diagnostic Reasoning Reference
Context: Neck Swelling Assessment
Overview

Neck swelling is a very common clinical presentation in both primary and secondary care, encompassing a wide spectrum of pathologies from benign and self-limiting to urgent and life-threatening. The anatomical complexity of the neck, which houses critical vascular, respiratory, endocrine, and lymphatic structures, makes the diagnostic challenge particularly acute. The primary task for the clinician is to systematically differentiate between inflammatory, congenital, and neoplastic causes, paying close attention to red flag features that might indicate malignancy or airway compromise.

A structured and logical approach is paramount to ensure that serious conditions, such as metastatic carcinoma, lymphoma, or deep neck abscesses, are identified promptly, while simultaneously avoiding excessive investigation for more common, benign causes like reactive lymphadenopathy. This educational module provides a foundational framework for the clinical reasoning process when faced with a patient with a neck swelling. For definitive, real-time management protocols, users must refer to official UK guidelines from authoritative bodies such as NICE (e.g., NG12 for Suspected Cancer) and relevant specialty associations like the British Thyroid Association.

Pathophysiology/Basic Science

Understanding the underlying mechanism of a neck swelling is fundamental to formulating an accurate differential diagnosis. The origin of the swelling can be categorised into several distinct pathophysiological processes:

  • Inflammatory/Infective: This is the most common cause of neck lumps, particularly in younger patients. Pathogens (viral or bacterial) trigger an immune response in the cervical lymph nodes, causing them to enlarge and become tender (reactive lymphadenopathy). If the infection is not contained, it can progress to form a collection of pus, known as an abscess, which presents as a painful, fluctuant swelling.
  • Neoplastic: This involves abnormal, uncontrolled cell growth. It can be primary, originating from tissues within the neck (e.g., lymphoma from lymphocytes, thyroid cancer from thyroid follicular cells), or secondary (metastatic), where cancer cells from a distant primary site (e.g., lung, stomach, or oropharynx) have spread to the cervical lymph nodes. Malignant lumps are typically hard, irregular, and may become fixed to underlying tissues as they invade local structures.
  • Congenital: These swellings arise from the failure of embryonic structures to regress during foetal development. A thyroglossal duct cyst, for example, forms from remnants of the tract along which the thyroid gland descends, typically presenting as a midline cyst that moves upwards on tongue protrusion. A branchial cleft cyst arises from incomplete closure of the branchial arches and usually presents as a smooth, non-tender mass on the lateral side of the neck.
  • Glandular Enlargement: This involves the swelling of the major glands located in the neck. The thyroid gland can enlarge diffusely (goitre) due to iodine deficiency, autoimmune disease (Graves' or Hashimoto's), or develop discrete nodules. The salivary glands (parotid, submandibular) can swell due to infection (sialadenitis), duct blockage by a stone (sialolithiasis), or tumours.
  • Vascular: Though rare, a swelling can originate from blood vessels. A carotid artery aneurysm, for example, is a localised bulge in the wall of the carotid artery, which would present as a pulsatile mass. A carotid body tumour (paraganglioma) is a rare, highly vascular tumour arising from the chemoreceptor cells at the carotid bifurcation.
Differential Diagnosis (DDx) Framework

A structured approach to the differential diagnosis of neck swelling is essential to ensure life-threatening conditions are prioritised.

Must not miss!
  • Carotid aneurysm: A rare, pulsatile lateral neck mass that requires urgent vascular assessment to prevent rupture.
  • Lymphoma: Often presents with painless, firm, 'rubbery' lymph nodes, which may be associated with systemic 'B' symptoms (fever, night sweats, weight loss).
  • Metastatic Disease: Typically presents as a hard, painless, and fixed lymph node, often from a primary cancer in the head, neck, lung, or GI tract.
  • Thyroid cancer: Can present as a solitary thyroid nodule or a rapidly enlarging goitre, sometimes with associated hoarseness or dysphagia.
  • Tuberculosis (Lymphadenopathy): Commonly presents as 'scrofula', a chronic, painless mass of matted lymph nodes which may form a 'cold' abscess without prominent signs of inflammation.
Common causes
  • Lymphadenopathy: A general term for enlarged lymph nodes, most commonly due to benign reactive causes (e.g., viral or bacterial infection).
  • Goitre: A diffuse or nodular enlargement of the thyroid gland, which is often asymptomatic but can cause compressive symptoms.
  • Thyroid lump: A discrete nodule within the thyroid gland, which is common and usually benign but requires investigation to exclude malignancy.
  • Thyroglossal cyst: A painless, mobile, midline cyst that characteristically moves upwards when the tongue is protruded.
  • Sebaceous cyst: A common skin lesion caused by a blocked sebaceous gland, containing keratin and typically having a central punctum.
  • Lipoma: A benign, slow-growing tumour of fat cells, presenting as a soft, mobile, non-tender subcutaneous mass.
  • Branchial cyst: A smooth, non-tender congenital cyst typically found on the lateral aspect of the neck, anterior to the sternocleidomastoid muscle.
  • COVID-19: Can cause reactive cervical lymphadenopathy as part of the systemic viral immune response.
Easily missed
  • Cervical rib: An extra rib arising from the seventh cervical vertebra which can present as a hard, bony lump in the supraclavicular fossa and may cause thoracic outlet syndrome.
  • Pharyngeal pouch: A posteromedial diverticulum of the pharynx which can present as a gurgling neck mass, dysphagia, and regurgitation of undigested food.
  • Sarcoidosis: A multi-system granulomatous disease that can cause painless, firm cervical lymphadenopathy, often alongside bilateral hilar lymphadenopathy on chest X-ray.
Other Differential Diagnoses to consider
  • Sialadenitis: Inflammation or infection of the salivary glands, often presenting with painful swelling.
  • Submandibular gland tumour: Can present as a painless mass in the neck.
  • Paraganglioma: A rare tumour that can arise along the carotid body, presenting as a neck mass.
  • Congenital neck masses: Such as dermoid cysts, presenting in children and young adults.
  • Hodgkin's lymphoma: Often presents with painless cervical lymphadenopathy.
  • COVID-19-related lymphadenopathy: Swelling of lymph nodes secondary to viral infection.
  • Deep neck abscess: Usually associated with pain, fever, and sometimes airway compromise.
  • Riedel's thyroiditis: A rare form of chronic thyroiditis causing a hard, fixed thyroid mass.
History Taking
Patient’s Description: How can I help you today?
Starting with an open-ended question such as, “Can you tell me more about what brought you here today?” allows the patient to provide a broad and subjective account of their symptoms. Relevance: Allows the patient to describe the issue in their own words, guiding further targeted questions about the neck swelling, its nature, and associated symptoms.
When did the swelling first start?
  • This helps to establish whether the swelling is acute or chronic.
  • Relevance: An acute onset may suggest infection (e.g., lymphadenitis), trauma, or an inflammatory process, while a more gradual onset could indicate malignancy (e.g., lymphoma, thyroid cancer), or a benign cause (e.g., goitre).
Has the swelling changed in size?
  • This question assesses progression or regression of the swelling.
  • Relevance: Rapid enlargement may indicate an abscess, infection, or malignancy, while stable size over a long period is more suggestive of a benign condition, such as a lipoma or congenital cyst.
Is the swelling painful or tender?
  • Pain or tenderness can help differentiate between inflammatory/infective causes and neoplastic ones.
  • Relevance: A painful swelling is more likely to be inflammatory or infective (e.g., lymphadenitis, sialadenitis), while a painless mass may raise concern for malignancy (e.g., lymphoma, thyroid cancer) or congenital causes (e.g., thyroglossal or branchial cyst).
Have you noticed any difficulty swallowing or breathing?
  • This explores potential compressive symptoms from the swelling.
  • Relevance: Difficulty swallowing (dysphagia) or breathing (dyspnoea) could suggest a large goitre, thyroid malignancy, or other mass causing compression of the oesophagus or trachea. Absence of these symptoms can help rule out significant compressive pathology.
Have you had any recent infections, fevers, or weight loss?
  • These are important to assess for systemic involvement or underlying malignancy.
  • Relevance: Recent infections or fevers suggest an infective cause (e.g., bacterial or viral lymphadenitis, tuberculosis), while weight loss and night sweats may raise suspicion of malignancy (e.g., lymphoma, metastatic disease).
Do you have any history of thyroid disease or cancer?
  • Personal and family history is essential for identifying risk factors for thyroid-related pathology.
  • Relevance: A history of thyroid disease raises suspicion for conditions like goitre or thyroid nodules, while a personal or family history of cancer increases the likelihood of metastatic disease or primary thyroid malignancy.
Have you noticed any lumps or swelling in other parts of your body?
  • This explores for lymphadenopathy in other regions, which may suggest systemic disease.
  • Relevance: Generalised lymphadenopathy can be associated with systemic conditions like lymphoma, sarcoidosis, or tuberculosis. The absence of other lumps makes localised causes (e.g., benign neck mass) more likely.
Ideas, Concerns, and Expectations (ICE)
  • Ideas: “What do you think might be causing this?” Helps to identify whether the patient has any specific beliefs about the cause, such as cancer or infection.
  • Concerns: “Is there something specific you are worried about?” Allows exploration of any significant fears, such as malignancy or the need for surgery.
  • Expectations: “What were you hoping we might be able to do for you today?” Helps clarify whether the patient is seeking reassurance, further investigation, or treatment, and ensures alignment of treatment plans.
  • Relevance: Addressing ICE ensures that the consultation covers the patient’s perspective, worries (e.g., fear of cancer), and expectations, facilitating a more patient-centred approach.
Examination Strategy
Inspection of the neck
  • Begin by inspecting the neck for any visible lumps, asymmetry, skin changes (e.g., redness, scarring), or swelling. Note the location, size, and number of any visible lumps.
  • Relevance: Visible, well-defined masses suggest benign conditions like a thyroid goitre, lipoma, or cyst, while asymmetry, fixed masses, or skin changes (e.g., erythema) may raise suspicion for malignancy or an inflammatory process such as infection or an abscess.
Palpation of the neck
  • Palpate the neck to assess the location, size, consistency (hard, soft, firm), and mobility of any lumps. Check for tenderness and whether the lump is fixed to underlying structures.
  • Relevance: A firm, non-tender, mobile mass may indicate a benign lesion (e.g., lipoma, cyst), whereas a hard, fixed mass could suggest malignancy (e.g., thyroid cancer, metastatic disease). Tender, mobile nodes may suggest lymphadenitis or an acute infective cause.
Palpation of lymph nodes
  • Palpate the cervical lymph nodes in sequence: submental, submandibular, pre-auricular, post-auricular, occipital, anterior and posterior cervical chains, and supraclavicular nodes.
  • Relevance: Enlarged, tender nodes suggest infection (e.g., lymphadenitis, tuberculosis), while non-tender, firm nodes may indicate malignancy (e.g., lymphoma, metastatic disease). Supraclavicular node involvement is particularly concerning for malignancy (e.g., metastatic cancer).
Examination of the thyroid gland
  • Inspect and palpate the thyroid gland, asking the patient to swallow to check for movement of the thyroid. Feel for size, consistency, nodularity, and tenderness.
  • Relevance: A diffusely enlarged thyroid suggests a goitre (e.g., Graves’ disease, Hashimoto’s thyroiditis). Nodularity may indicate a thyroid nodule or malignancy. A thyroid mass that moves with swallowing suggests a thyroid origin, while a fixed mass may indicate malignancy.
Assessment of neck movement
  • Assess the range of motion of the neck by asking the patient to turn their head and look up and down. Note any discomfort, limitation of movement, or pain.
  • Relevance: Restriction of neck movement could indicate deep-seated infections (e.g., deep neck abscess) or mass effect from a large lesion. Normal movement suggests a less invasive pathology such as a superficial cyst or benign mass.
Auscultation for carotid bruits
  • Auscultate over the carotid arteries to check for bruits, which are abnormal sounds caused by turbulent blood flow, often associated with stenosis or aneurysms.
  • Relevance: A positive carotid bruit may suggest a carotid artery aneurysm or stenosis, which could be the cause of a pulsatile mass in the neck. The absence of a bruit helps to rule out vascular causes of neck swelling.
Examination of the oral cavity and oropharynx
  • Inspect the oral cavity and oropharynx for any masses, asymmetry, or signs of infection (e.g., tonsillar exudate, swelling). Pay particular attention to the tonsils, floor of the mouth, and base of the tongue.
  • Relevance: Intraoral masses, asymmetry, or signs of infection could suggest pharyngeal causes of neck swelling, such as a pharyngeal pouch, tonsillar abscess, or malignancy.
Examination of skin overlying the swelling
  • Inspect the skin overlying the swelling for any erythema, warmth, or ulceration. Palpate for tenderness and fluctuance, indicating possible infection or abscess.
  • Relevance: Skin changes such as erythema or warmth may suggest an underlying infection (e.g., cellulitis, abscess), while ulceration or skin invasion could indicate malignancy.
Red Flag Synthesis
Severe difficulty swallowing (dysphagia) or breathing (dyspnoea)
  • These symptoms suggest compression of the oesophagus or trachea, potentially caused by a large mass, goitre, or malignancy.
  • Significance: This could indicate a compressive thyroid mass or malignancy, requiring urgent evaluation for airway compromise or surgical intervention.
Rapidly enlarging neck mass
  • A mass that enlarges quickly raises suspicion for an aggressive malignancy, such as lymphoma, or an infectious process like an abscess.
  • Significance: This could indicate lymphoma, thyroid cancer, or a deep neck abscess, necessitating urgent biopsy or imaging and potential surgical intervention.
Painless, firm, and fixed lymph nodes
  • Non-tender, firm, and fixed lymph nodes are concerning for malignancy, particularly metastatic disease or lymphoma.
  • Significance: This could indicate metastatic cancer, lymphoma, or other malignant processes, requiring urgent referral for biopsy and oncological assessment.
Supraclavicular lymphadenopathy
  • Enlarged supraclavicular nodes, particularly on the left (Virchow’s node), are highly suspicious for abdominal or thoracic malignancies.
  • Significance: This could indicate metastatic disease from cancers of the stomach, lung, or other organs, requiring immediate imaging and further investigation.
Unexplained weight loss, night sweats, or fever
  • These systemic symptoms raise concern for malignancy, such as lymphoma, or systemic infection like tuberculosis.
  • Significance: This could indicate lymphoma, metastatic cancer, or tuberculosis, necessitating prompt referral for further diagnostic tests such as blood tests, imaging, and possibly biopsy.
Hoarseness or voice changes
  • Hoarseness or voice changes may indicate involvement of the recurrent laryngeal nerve, which can be compressed by a thyroid mass or other neck tumour.
  • Significance: This could indicate a thyroid malignancy or other neck tumour compressing the recurrent laryngeal nerve, requiring urgent referral to ENT for laryngoscopy and imaging.
History of prior malignancy
  • A history of prior cancer increases the risk of metastatic disease, presenting as a new neck mass.
  • Significance: This could indicate metastasis from a previous malignancy (e.g., breast, lung, or head and neck cancer), warranting urgent imaging and oncological referral.
Investigations Strategy & Interpretation
Full Blood Count (FBC)
  • A full blood count is useful for identifying signs of infection, anaemia, or malignancy. It helps assess for systemic involvement such as raised white cell counts in infection or haematological abnormalities in cases of lymphoma.
  • Result Significance: A raised white cell count suggests infection or inflammation, while anaemia and abnormal lymphocyte counts could indicate malignancy, such as lymphoma. A normal FBC may rule out systemic infection or haematological causes of the swelling.
Thyroid Function Tests (TFTs)
  • Thyroid function tests are relevant in assessing thyroid-related causes of neck swelling, such as goitre or thyroid nodules. Abnormal thyroid hormone levels (T3, T4, and TSH) can help identify underlying thyroid dysfunction.
  • Result Significance: Abnormal TFTs (e.g., elevated TSH, low T3/T4) suggest thyroid disease (e.g., hypothyroidism, hyperthyroidism). Normal results reduce the likelihood of thyroid-related causes of neck swelling.
Ultrasound of the neck
  • Ultrasound is the first-line imaging modality for evaluating neck masses. It helps assess the size, consistency, and vascularity of neck lumps, and differentiate between solid and cystic lesions.
  • Result Significance: A solid mass could suggest malignancy (e.g., thyroid cancer, lymphoma), while a cystic mass may indicate a benign lesion (e.g., thyroglossal or branchial cyst). Ultrasound may also help guide fine-needle aspiration for further investigation.
Fine-Needle Aspiration (FNA) and Cytology
  • FNA is essential for obtaining cells from a neck mass for cytological examination. It is particularly useful for diagnosing malignancies (e.g., thyroid cancer, lymphoma) or inflammatory conditions.
  • Result Significance: Positive results indicating malignancy (e.g., atypical cells, malignancy) confirm diagnoses such as thyroid cancer or lymphoma. Negative results suggest benign causes, though repeat sampling may be necessary for confirmation.
Chest X-ray (CXR)
  • A chest X-ray can help detect mediastinal masses, lung lesions, or signs of tuberculosis, which may present with cervical lymphadenopathy.
  • Result Significance: A positive result showing a mediastinal mass or lung pathology raises suspicion for lymphoma or metastatic disease. A normal CXR makes these diagnoses less likely but does not completely rule them out.
CT Scan (Neck and Chest)
  • A CT scan provides detailed imaging of the neck and surrounding structures. It is especially helpful in assessing deeper neck masses, potential malignancy, or metastatic disease.
  • Result Significance: A positive CT scan showing a solid mass with irregular borders or signs of local invasion suggests malignancy, while a negative result may suggest benign or inflammatory causes of neck swelling.
Erythrocyte Sedimentation Rate (ESR) / C-Reactive Protein (CRP)
  • ESR and CRP are markers of inflammation. Elevated levels can indicate an inflammatory or infective process contributing to neck swelling.
  • Result Significance: Elevated ESR/CRP suggests an inflammatory or infective cause, such as tuberculosis, sarcoidosis, or a deep neck abscess. Normal levels make significant inflammation or infection less likely.
HIV Testing
  • HIV testing is relevant in cases where generalised lymphadenopathy is present, as HIV infection can lead to widespread lymph node enlargement.
  • Result Significance: A positive result indicates HIV infection, which may explain the neck swelling due to HIV-associated lymphadenopathy. A negative result rules out HIV as a cause.
Evidence-Based Diagnosis (EBDx)
Painless, firm, fixed lymph nodes (suggestive of malignancy)
  • Painless, firm, and fixed lymphadenopathy is highly suggestive of malignancy, particularly lymphoma or metastatic disease. A detailed physical examination can help differentiate between benign and malignant causes.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 90%, Specificity: 75%, Likelihood Ratio (LR) for malignancy: 3.6 (for firm, non-tender nodes).
  • Significance: Positive findings increase suspicion for lymphoma or metastatic cancer, prompting urgent biopsy. Negative findings make malignancy less likely but not ruled out, especially if other concerning features are present.
Rapidly enlarging neck mass
  • A rapidly growing neck mass raises suspicion for an aggressive malignancy such as lymphoma or a deep neck abscess, both of which require urgent intervention.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity for malignancy: 85%, Specificity: 80%, LR for malignancy: 4.2 (in the context of rapid growth).
  • Significance: Positive findings increase the likelihood of aggressive malignancies or infections. Rapid evaluation with imaging and biopsy is critical for prompt diagnosis.
Thyroid ultrasound for thyroid nodules
  • Thyroid ultrasound helps in characterising thyroid nodules, differentiating between benign and malignant features based on size, calcification, vascularity, and margin irregularity.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity for malignancy: 85%, Specificity: 92%, LR: 10.6 (for nodules with irregular borders and microcalcifications).
  • Significance: Ultrasound findings suggestive of malignancy (e.g., irregular borders, microcalcifications) warrant further evaluation with FNA. Negative findings decrease the likelihood of malignancy but follow-up may be needed.
Fine-Needle Aspiration (FNA) for thyroid or lymph node masses
  • FNA is essential for cytological evaluation of neck masses, particularly in differentiating between benign and malignant causes. It is a highly specific and sensitive test for thyroid and lymph node pathology.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity for malignancy: 90%, Specificity: 96%, LR: 22.5 for detecting malignant cells.
  • Significance: A positive FNA result confirms malignancy, allowing for rapid referral to oncology. Negative results should be interpreted cautiously and may require repeat sampling if suspicion remains high.
Elevated ESR/CRP in tuberculosis or sarcoidosis
  • Raised ESR or CRP suggests an inflammatory or infective process. These markers are commonly elevated in cases of tuberculosis or sarcoidosis, which may present with neck lymphadenopathy.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 75% for tuberculosis, Specificity: 60%, LR: 2.0 (for elevated ESR in tuberculosis).
  • Significance: Positive findings suggest an underlying inflammatory or infective cause, particularly tuberculosis or sarcoidosis. Negative findings make these diagnoses less likely but do not completely exclude them.
Supraclavicular lymphadenopathy
  • Supraclavicular lymphadenopathy, especially on the left side (Virchow’s node), is highly suspicious for malignancy, particularly gastrointestinal or thoracic cancers.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity for malignancy: 60%, Specificity: 90%, LR: 9.5 for metastatic disease in supraclavicular nodes.
  • Significance: Positive findings indicate a high likelihood of metastatic malignancy and necessitate urgent imaging and further diagnostic work-up.
Diagnostic Timeout & Cognitive Biases

Awareness of common thinking errors is essential to improve diagnostic accuracy and patient safety when assessing a patient with neck swelling. Before finalising a diagnosis, it is good practice for clinicians at all levels to take a 'diagnostic timeout' to challenge their own reasoning.

  • Anchoring Bias: This is the tendency to fixate on initial information. For example, a young patient reports a recent sore throat, leading the clinician to anchor on the diagnosis of reactive lymphadenopathy, potentially overlooking sinister features like a very hard, fixed node that would suggest malignancy. A key question to ask is: "If it wasn't a reactive node, what is the most serious thing it could be?"
  • Confirmation Bias: This involves seeking out or giving more weight to evidence that supports an initial hypothesis. For instance, a clinician suspecting a simple sebaceous cyst might focus on its smooth surface while downplaying the patient's report of rapid growth, which is a red flag for malignancy.
  • Sutton's Law (Going where the money is): This bias involves favouring a single, obvious diagnosis while neglecting others. In a patient with known hypothyroidism, a new neck lump might be immediately attributed to the goitre, causing a delay in investigating a potentially new, separate pathology like a lymphoma.
  • Ageism Bias: A common pitfall is dismissing a persistent neck lump in a young person as benign or infective without due consideration. While malignancy is less common in the young, cancers like lymphoma and thyroid cancer can and do occur, and a thorough assessment is always required regardless of age.
Illustrative Management Pathways

For educational purposes, this section describes the typical principles of management for key diagnoses. These are not prescriptive instructions. All management must be guided by official UK guidelines (e.g., NICE guidance NG12), local formularies, and senior clinical advice.

Suspected Malignancy (e.g., Metastatic node, Lymphoma)

Patients with a neck lump that is suspicious for cancer (e.g., hard, fixed, painless, associated with red flag symptoms) should be referred urgently on a two-week wait pathway to a relevant specialist team (usually ENT or haematology). The initial investigation is typically an ultrasound scan, which can characterise the lump and any associated lymph nodes. This is often followed by a biopsy, which may be a fine-needle aspiration (FNA) for cytology or a core biopsy for histology, to establish a definitive diagnosis and guide further management by the multidisciplinary team (MDT).

Suspected Infective Cause (e.g., Abscess)

A tender, warm, and fluctuant swelling suggests a bacterial abscess. Management involves a course of empirical antibiotics covering common skin and oral pathogens. If the abscess is large, fluctuant, or not responding to antibiotics, it will likely require surgical incision and drainage to remove the pus collection. A sample of the pus should be sent for microscopy, culture, and sensitivity to guide targeted antibiotic therapy.

Thyroid Nodule

The investigation of a thyroid lump begins with blood tests for Thyroid Function (TFTs). The key imaging modality is a thyroid ultrasound, which scores the nodule's features (e.g., size, composition, echogenicity, margins) to stratify the risk of malignancy (U1-U5 score). Based on this risk score and the nodule size, a fine-needle aspiration (FNA) may be performed. The results of the FNA (Thy1-Thy5) will determine the need for further management, which can range from reassurance and discharge to specialist endocrinology/ENT follow-up or diagnostic hemithyroidectomy.

Benign Causes (e.g., Lipoma, Sebaceous Cyst)

Once a confident clinical diagnosis of a benign lesion like a lipoma or sebaceous cyst is made, and sinister pathology has been excluded, the primary management is reassurance. These lesions are harmless, and no treatment is required. If the lump becomes symptomatic (e.g., infected, painful) or the patient desires removal for cosmetic reasons, they can be referred for elective minor surgery, often performed in a primary care or community setting.

Reflection & Learning

These brief cases illustrate the application of clinical reasoning to different neck swelling presentations. They are for educational purposes only.

Case 1: The Worrisome Lump in an Older Man

A 72-year-old male smoker presents with a 6-week history of a painless, hard lump in his right neck. On examination, a 3cm, non-tender, fixed mass is felt in the right supraclavicular fossa. He also mentions losing some weight unintentionally. The problem representation is an older man with significant risk factors (age, smoking) and red flag features (hard, fixed, supraclavicular mass, weight loss). This presentation is highly suspicious for metastatic malignancy. The immediate pathway is an urgent two-week wait cancer referral to ENT for investigation, likely starting with a CT scan of the neck and chest and a biopsy of the mass.

Case 2: The Midline Lump in a Young Woman

A 24-year-old woman presents to her GP with a lump in the middle of her neck that she first noticed a few months ago. She reports it is painless and mobile. On examination, there is a 1cm, smooth, non-tender cystic-feeling lump in the midline below the hyoid bone. Crucially, the lump moves upwards when the patient is asked to stick out her tongue. The problem representation is a young woman with a mobile midline neck mass that elevates on tongue protrusion. This is the classic presentation of a thyroglossal duct cyst. Management involves referral to ENT for assessment and consideration of surgical excision (Sistrunk procedure) to prevent future infection and provide a definitive histological diagnosis.

Case 3: The Tender Swelling after a Cold

A 19-year-old university student presents with a painful swelling on the side of his neck that appeared 4 days ago. He has just recovered from a significant sore throat and cold. On examination, he has several tender, mobile lymph nodes, each about 1-1.5cm in diameter, in the left anterior cervical chain. The overlying skin is normal. The problem representation is a young, otherwise healthy man with acute-onset, tender, mobile cervical lymph nodes immediately following an upper respiratory tract infection. This is most consistent with benign reactive lymphadenopathy. After a full history and examination exclude any red flags, management involves reassurance, advice on simple analgesia, and safety netting instructions to return if the lumps persist for more than a few weeks or grow larger.
