Bleeding and Bruising Diagnostic Reasoning Reference
Context: Bleeding and Bruising Assessment
Overview

Bleeding and bruising are common clinical presentations that range from trivial, benign findings to the first sign of a life-threatening systemic disease. The core challenge for the clinician is to differentiate between minor, localised issues (like trauma or age-related skin changes) and significant underlying pathology, such as haematological malignancy, severe liver disease, or critical coagulation disorders. A systematic and structured approach is essential to identify red flags, risk stratify the patient, and guide appropriate investigation and management.

This educational module is designed to provide a foundational framework for the clinical reasoning process when faced with a patient presenting with bleeding or bruising. For definitive, real-time management protocols, users must refer to official UK guidelines from authoritative bodies such as the British Society for Haematology (BSH), NICE (e.g., NG151 for Sepsis, NG59 for Cirrhosis), and the UK Health Security Agency.

Pathophysiology/Basic Science

Understanding the pathophysiology of haemostasis is fundamental to diagnosing the cause of bleeding and bruising. Haemostasis can be divided into primary and secondary processes, and defects in any component can lead to clinical signs.

  • Vascular Integrity: The blood vessel wall is the first line of defence. Damage to the endothelium exposes collagen, initiating clotting. Conditions that weaken the vessel wall or its supporting connective tissue, such as age-related changes (senile purpura), Vitamin C deficiency (scurvy), or vasculitis, can lead to bruising and bleeding with minimal or no trauma.
  • Primary Haemostasis (The Platelet Plug): This involves platelet adhesion, activation, and aggregation to form an initial, unstable plug at the site of injury. Disorders of primary haemostasis typically cause mucocutaneous bleeding (petechiae, purpura, epistaxis, gingival bleeding). They are caused by either a low number of platelets (thrombocytopenia), as seen in leukaemia or Immune Thrombocytopenia (ITP), or poorly functioning platelets (thrombocytopathy), which can be inherited (e.g., von Willebrand disease) or acquired (e.g., due to drugs like aspirin or clopidogrel).
  • Secondary Haemostasis (The Fibrin Clot): This involves the coagulation cascade, a series of enzymatic reactions involving clotting factors (most produced by the liver) that culminates in the formation of a stable fibrin mesh. Defects in this system typically cause deep tissue bleeding, such as haemarthrosis (joint bleeds) or large haematomas. Causes include inherited disorders (e.g., Haemophilia A/B) or acquired deficiencies due to liver disease, Vitamin K deficiency, or anticoagulant medications (e.g., warfarin, DOACs).
  • Disseminated Intravascular Coagulation (DIC): This is a complex and life-threatening condition, often triggered by sepsis or major trauma, where widespread activation of the clotting cascade leads to the consumption of platelets and clotting factors. This paradoxically results in both microvascular thrombosis and severe, uncontrolled bleeding.
Differential Diagnosis (DDx) Framework

A structured approach to the differential diagnosis of bleeding and bruising is essential to ensure life-threatening conditions are prioritised.

Must not miss!
  • Sepsis: A life-threatening organ dysfunction caused by a dysregulated host response to infection, which can lead to DIC and widespread bleeding.
  • Liver cirrhosis: End-stage liver disease leading to impaired synthesis of clotting factors and thrombocytopenia due to portal hypertension and splenomegaly.
  • Lymphoma: A cancer of the lymphatic system that can infiltrate the bone marrow, leading to pancytopenia and subsequent bleeding.
  • Leukaemia: Malignancy of blood-forming cells in the bone marrow, causing a failure to produce normal platelets and leading to severe bleeding and bruising.
  • Multiple myeloma: A cancer of plasma cells that can suppress normal haematopoiesis and produce abnormal proteins that interfere with platelet function and coagulation.
  • Aplastic anaemia: A rare condition where the bone marrow fails to produce enough blood cells, including platelets, leading to a high risk of bleeding.
  • Thrombocytopenic purpura: Refers to purpura associated with a low platelet count, which can be caused by disorders like ITP or Thrombotic Thrombocytopenic Purpura (TTP).
  • Coagulation disorders: Inherited or acquired defects in the clotting cascade, such as haemophilia or von Willebrand disease, leading to significant bleeding.
Common causes
  • Trauma-induced Bleeding and Bruising: The most frequent cause, resulting from direct physical injury to blood vessels.
  • Age related purpura: Also known as senile purpura, caused by increased skin fragility and loss of subcutaneous tissue in older adults, leading to easy bruising on exposed areas.
  • Anticoagulants and Antiplatelets: Therapeutic use of medications like warfarin, DOACs, aspirin, or clopidogrel which intentionally inhibit clotting.
  • COVID-19: The viral infection and the associated inflammatory response can be linked to coagulopathy, causing both thrombosis and bleeding complications.
Easily missed
  • Medication-induced Bleeding and Bruising: Bleeding caused by drugs other than anticoagulants, such as SSRIs, corticosteroids, or certain antibiotics that can affect platelet function.
  • HSP: Henoch-Schönlein purpura is a systemic vasculitis of small vessels, typically presenting with a classic triad of palpable purpura, arthritis, and abdominal pain.
  • Vitamin K deficiency: Leads to impaired synthesis of vitamin K-dependent clotting factors (II, VII, IX, X), often seen in newborns, malabsorption, or poor nutrition.
  • Vitamin C deficiency: Scurvy causes impaired collagen synthesis, leading to weakened capillary walls and resulting in perifollicular haemorrhages and bleeding gums.
  • Connective tissue diseases: Conditions like Ehlers-Danlos syndrome can cause hyperflexible joints and fragile blood vessels, leading to easy bruising.
Other Differential Diagnoses to consider
  • COVID-19: Associated with increased coagulopathy, potentially causing bleeding or bruising in some cases.
  • Disseminated Intravascular Coagulation (DIC): Secondary to conditions like trauma, sepsis, or malignancy, causing widespread bleeding and bruising.
  • Cushing's syndrome: Causes skin thinning and easy bruising due to long-term corticosteroid exposure or endogenous hypercortisolism.
  • Immune thrombocytopenia (ITP): An autoimmune disorder causing low platelets, leading to petechiae, bruising, and mucosal bleeding.
  • Von Willebrand disease: A common inherited bleeding disorder affecting blood clotting, often resulting in mild to moderate bleeding.
History Taking
**Patient’s Description**: How can I help you today?
Beginning with an open-ended question, such as “Can you tell me more about what brought you here today?”, encourages the patient to share their concerns in their own words. Relevance: Allows the patient to describe the issue with context, helping guide further specific questions and offering insights into severity and timeline.
Onset, Duration, and Progression
  • “When did you first notice the bleeding/bruising?”
  • “Has it worsened, improved, or stayed the same?”
  • Relevance: A sudden onset may suggest trauma, sepsis, or a coagulation disorder, while a more gradual progression could indicate a chronic condition like liver disease or leukaemia.
Description/Quality of Bleeding and Bruising
  • “Can you describe the type and location of bruises or bleeding?”
  • “Do the bruises appear without any apparent trauma?”
  • Relevance: Spontaneous bruising or bleeding without trauma raises suspicion for conditions like ITP, leukaemia, or coagulation disorders. Location-specific patterns (e.g., on the extremities) can indicate vasculitis or connective tissue disorders.
Triggers, Exacerbating, and Relieving Factors
  • “Have you noticed anything that makes the bruising/bleeding worse or better?”
  • Relevance: If bruising worsens with minor trauma, this could indicate medication effects, especially anticoagulants, or an underlying bleeding disorder. No identifiable trigger could imply idiopathic causes like ITP.
Associated Symptoms
  • “Have you had any fevers, weight loss, or night sweats?”
  • “Have you noticed bleeding gums, nosebleeds, or blood in urine or stools?”
  • Relevance: Constitutional symptoms like fever, weight loss, and night sweats can suggest malignancy (e.g., lymphoma) or sepsis. Mucosal bleeding may point towards thrombocytopenia or coagulation disorders.
Past Medical History
  • “Have you had any prior issues with bleeding or bruising?”
  • “Do you have any history of liver disease or anaemia?”
  • Relevance: Previous bleeding issues may indicate an underlying bleeding disorder. Liver disease is relevant as it impairs clotting factor production.
Drug History
  • “Are you taking any blood thinners, like warfarin or aspirin?”
  • “Have you recently started any new medications?”
  • Relevance: Anticoagulants and antiplatelet drugs are major contributors to easy bruising. Certain other medications, including SSRIs and corticosteroids, may also increase bruising tendency.
Family History
  • “Does anyone in your family have a history of bleeding disorders?”
  • Relevance: A family history of bleeding disorders can suggest inherited conditions like haemophilia or von Willebrand disease.
**Ideas, Concerns, and Expectations (ICE)**
  • Ask: “What do you think might be causing this?”
  • Ask: “Is there something specific you are worried about?”
  • Ask: “What were you hoping we might be able to do for you today?”
  • Relevance: Helps address the patient’s perspective and specific concerns, such as worries about cancer or clotting disorders, as well as their expectations for diagnosis or treatment.
Examination Strategy
General Inspection
  • Observe the patient’s general appearance for pallor, jaundice, or signs of chronic illness.
  • Relevance: Pallor may indicate anaemia, possibly due to underlying haematological conditions like leukaemia. Jaundice may suggest liver dysfunction, which can impair clotting factor production, contributing to bleeding and bruising.
Inspection of Skin
  • Examine the skin for petechiae, ecchymoses, and purpura, noting their distribution, size, and colour.
  • Relevance: Petechiae and purpura suggest platelet disorders (e.g., ITP or leukaemia), while ecchymoses in exposed areas could point to senile purpura. The presence of purpura on the lower extremities, especially palpable, can indicate vasculitis such as Henoch-Schönlein purpura (HSP).
Palpation for Hepatosplenomegaly
  • Palpate the abdomen to check for liver and spleen enlargement, which may indicate underlying haematological or hepatic conditions.
  • Relevance: Hepatosplenomegaly could suggest leukaemia, lymphoma, or chronic liver disease, all of which can be associated with bleeding tendencies. A negative finding may help rule out these systemic causes.
Assessment of Lymph Nodes
  • Palpate lymph nodes in cervical, axillary, and inguinal regions for enlargement.
  • Relevance: Lymphadenopathy may suggest lymphoma or leukaemia, both of which can present with bleeding or bruising due to bone marrow suppression or abnormal blood counts. A lack of lymphadenopathy may help rule out haematological malignancies.
Examination of the Oral Cavity and Mucosa
  • Inspect the oral cavity for bleeding gums, petechiae on the mucosa, or any ulcerations.
  • Relevance: Mucosal bleeding is indicative of thrombocytopenia or coagulation disorders. Healthy mucosa without petechiae may help rule out severe thrombocytopenia.
Vital Signs
  • Check for fever, tachycardia, and hypotension.
  • Relevance: Fever could suggest an infectious or systemic inflammatory cause (e.g., sepsis). Hypotension and tachycardia may indicate acute blood loss or sepsis, both of which are critical to address urgently.
Joint Examination
  • Assess for joint swelling or tenderness, particularly in weight-bearing joints.
  • Relevance: Joint bleeding (haemarthrosis) may suggest coagulation disorders such as haemophilia, while joint swelling with purpura can be seen in HSP or other vasculitic processes. A lack of joint involvement may help rule out these conditions.
Red Flag Synthesis
Fever with bleeding or bruising
  • Fever accompanied by bleeding or bruising can indicate systemic infection or sepsis, which may progress to disseminated intravascular coagulation (DIC), a life-threatening condition.
  • Significance: This could indicate sepsis or DIC, both of which require urgent intervention to prevent organ damage and mortality.
Severe fatigue, unintentional weight loss, or night sweats
  • These systemic symptoms can indicate underlying haematological malignancies, such as lymphoma or leukaemia, which may present with bleeding and bruising due to bone marrow suppression.
  • Significance: Such symptoms suggest possible malignancy and necessitate thorough evaluation, including blood tests and imaging, to rule out cancer.
Unexplained or spontaneous mucosal bleeding (e.g., gums, nosebleeds)
  • Spontaneous mucosal bleeding suggests a significant underlying coagulopathy, such as thrombocytopenia or a coagulation disorder, especially if there is no history of trauma or injury.
  • Significance: Could indicate thrombocytopenia or a severe coagulopathy, requiring prompt assessment of platelet count and clotting function.
Severe abdominal pain with bleeding or bruising
  • Abdominal pain associated with bruising or bleeding raises concern for underlying liver disease, splenic rupture, or vasculitis (e.g., Henoch-Schönlein purpura).
  • Significance: This may signify hepatic pathology, splenic injury, or vasculitis and could be an emergency, particularly if haemodynamic instability is present.
Rapid onset of extensive bruising
  • A sudden spread of bruising without a history of trauma suggests an acute coagulopathy, potentially due to DIC, immune thrombocytopenia, or severe liver dysfunction.
  • Significance: This pattern of bruising may indicate a life-threatening bleeding disorder, requiring urgent evaluation and intervention.
Neurological symptoms with bleeding/bruising (e.g., headache, confusion, vision changes)
  • Neurological symptoms in the context of bleeding or bruising may indicate intracranial haemorrhage, especially if there is a history of anticoagulant use or underlying coagulopathy.
  • Significance: These symptoms may suggest intracranial bleeding, requiring immediate neuroimaging and specialist referral to prevent lasting neurological damage or death.
Investigations Strategy & Interpretation
Full Blood Count (FBC)
  • A full blood count helps assess haemoglobin levels, white blood cell count, and platelet count, which are essential for diagnosing anaemia, infection, and thrombocytopenia.
  • Result Significance: Low platelets may suggest immune thrombocytopenia (ITP) or bone marrow suppression (e.g., leukaemia). A raised white cell count can indicate infection or malignancy. Anaemia may indicate chronic disease, malignancy, or acute blood loss.
Coagulation Profile (PT, aPTT, INR)
  • Assessing prothrombin time (PT), activated partial thromboplastin time (aPTT), and international normalised ratio (INR) helps evaluate coagulation pathways, which are important in bleeding disorders.
  • Result Significance: Prolonged PT or aPTT may suggest liver disease, vitamin K deficiency, or coagulation disorders such as haemophilia. A raised INR in the absence of anticoagulation therapy suggests an increased bleeding risk.
Liver Function Tests (LFTs)
  • Liver function tests assess the production of clotting factors and general liver health, as liver dysfunction can lead to coagulopathy.
  • Result Significance: Elevated liver enzymes and bilirubin may indicate liver disease, while low albumin and abnormal clotting factors are consistent with advanced hepatic impairment, contributing to bleeding risk.
Peripheral Blood Smear
  • Examining the blood smear provides details about cell morphology, which is useful in diagnosing haematological conditions.
  • Result Significance: Abnormal cell shapes or the presence of blasts may indicate leukaemia or other blood dyscrasias. A normal smear would make haematological malignancy less likely.
Bone Marrow Biopsy
  • This investigation is indicated if a haematological malignancy or bone marrow failure (e.g., aplastic anaemia) is suspected based on initial findings.
  • Result Significance: Findings of abnormal cell populations or marrow infiltration are diagnostic for conditions such as leukaemia or myeloma, while a lack of abnormalities can help rule out these conditions.
Immunoglobulins and Serum Protein Electrophoresis
  • Helps identify monoclonal protein spikes, which are suggestive of multiple myeloma, a condition that can present with bleeding and bruising.
  • Result Significance: A monoclonal spike would support a diagnosis of multiple myeloma, while a normal result makes this diagnosis less likely.
Urinalysis
  • Urinalysis helps identify haematuria, which may occur in bleeding disorders and some vasculitic conditions like Henoch-Schönlein purpura (HSP).
  • Result Significance: The presence of blood or protein in the urine can indicate renal involvement in systemic conditions like vasculitis, while a negative result may help rule out these causes.
Autoimmune Screen (e.g., ANA, ANCA)
  • Autoimmune testing is useful if a connective tissue disorder or vasculitis is suspected, as these conditions can present with bruising or bleeding.
  • Result Significance: Positive ANA or ANCA may suggest autoimmune diseases such as systemic lupus erythematosus or vasculitis, which would warrant further evaluation and management.
Evidence-Based Diagnosis (EBDx)
Thrombocytopenia (Low Platelet Count)
  • Thrombocytopenia is commonly associated with bleeding and bruising, particularly spontaneous mucosal bleeding, and petechiae. Diagnosing thrombocytopenia as a cause of bleeding requires identifying platelet counts below 100 x 10^9/L.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 90% for bleeding symptoms in patients with platelet counts below 50 x 10^9/L. Specificity for distinguishing thrombocytopenia as a bleeding cause over other causes of bruising is high at 95% when platelets are <30 x 10^9/L.
  • Reference: Harrison's Principles of Internal Medicine, 20th Edition; NICE Guidelines on Immune Thrombocytopenic Purpura.
  • Significance: Positive findings strongly indicate thrombocytopenia as a cause, while normal platelet levels can rule out thrombocytopenic causes.
Prolonged PT/INR in Liver Disease
  • In liver disease, the production of clotting factors is impaired, which can be detected by prolonged PT or INR values, reflecting increased bleeding risk. This is particularly sensitive for detecting liver-induced coagulopathy.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity of 85% for bleeding in chronic liver disease with an INR >1.5, while specificity for identifying liver disease versus other causes is around 90%.
  • Reference: British Society of Gastroenterology (BSG) guidelines on the management of liver disease.
  • Significance: An elevated INR in the absence of anticoagulant use strongly suggests liver-related bleeding, while normal levels argue against significant liver dysfunction as the bleeding cause.
Positive Blood Smear for Blast Cells in Leukaemia
  • The presence of blast cells on a peripheral blood smear is a key diagnostic indicator of leukaemia. This finding helps identify malignant haematological conditions that impair normal platelet and white cell function, contributing to bleeding and bruising.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity of 92% for leukaemia with the presence of blasts, specificity of 98% in distinguishing leukaemia from other haematological disorders.
  • Reference: "Blood Smears in Diagnosing Leukaemia" - Journal of Hematology (2020).
  • Significance: A positive result supports a diagnosis of leukaemia, while absence of blasts reduces the likelihood of leukaemia but warrants further investigation if symptoms persist.
Raised CRP/ESR in Vasculitis
  • An elevated C-reactive protein (CRP) or erythrocyte sedimentation rate (ESR) can indicate inflammation associated with vasculitic processes such as Henoch-Schönlein purpura (HSP), which presents with bruising, purpura, and sometimes renal involvement.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity: 80%, Specificity: 75% for systemic vasculitis with elevated CRP and ESR values.
  • Reference: "Assessment of CRP and ESR in Diagnosing Vasculitis" - BMJ Rheumatology, 2019.
  • Significance: Elevated markers support the diagnosis of vasculitis as a cause of bruising; normal levels may make vasculitis less likely but are not definitive.
Presence of Monoclonal Spike in Serum Protein Electrophoresis
  • A monoclonal spike detected on serum protein electrophoresis is a hallmark of multiple myeloma, a plasma cell disorder that can lead to anaemia and bleeding tendencies due to bone marrow infiltration.
  • Evidence: Sensitivity of 90% for multiple myeloma in symptomatic patients, with a specificity of 98% for diagnosing plasma cell dyscrasias when a monoclonal spike is present.
  • Reference: NICE Guidelines on Diagnosis and Management of Multiple Myeloma (2018).
  • Significance: A positive result supports the diagnosis of multiple myeloma, while a negative result reduces the likelihood of this as the bleeding cause.
Diagnostic Timeout & Cognitive Biases

Before finalising a diagnosis for bleeding and bruising, it is crucial to take a 'diagnostic timeout' to reflect on potential cognitive errors that can lead to misdiagnosis.

  • Anchoring Bias: The tendency to lock onto the most obvious cause, such as attributing an elderly patient's bruising solely to 'age related purpura' or their anticoagulant medication, without fully considering new red flag symptoms like weight loss or fatigue that might suggest an underlying malignancy. Ask yourself: "Could there be a second diagnosis?"
  • Confirmation Bias: Seeking out or giving more weight to evidence that confirms an initial hypothesis while ignoring contradictory findings. For example, focusing on a history of minor trauma to explain bruising while downplaying the significance of new-onset mucosal bleeding or petechiae, which point towards a platelet disorder.
  • Availability Heuristic: Overestimating the likelihood of a diagnosis because it is more recent or memorable in a clinician's experience. For instance, after seeing several cases of ITP, a clinician might be more inclined to diagnose it again, potentially overlooking signs of a different, more serious condition like aplastic anaemia.
  • Premature Closure: Accepting a diagnosis before it has been fully verified. For example, concluding bruising is due to warfarin use based on a high INR, without performing a full blood count that might reveal a co-existing and critical thrombocytopenia from an unrelated cause like leukaemia. A key question is: "What doesn't fit?"
Illustrative Management Pathways

This section outlines educational principles for managing key causes of bleeding and bruising. These are not prescriptive instructions. All management must be guided by official UK guidelines, local formularies, and senior clinical advice.

Suspected Haematological Malignancy (e.g., Leukaemia)

If the history, examination (e.g., splenomegaly, lymphadenopathy), or initial blood tests (e.g., very low or high white cell count, low platelets, blast cells on film) suggest leukaemia, this constitutes a medical emergency. The patient requires immediate referral and admission to hospital under the care of a haematology team. Further management involves urgent bone marrow biopsy to confirm the diagnosis, supportive care (e.g., blood and platelet transfusions, prophylactic antibiotics), and initiation of specific chemotherapy protocols.

Immune Thrombocytopenia (ITP)

In a patient with isolated, significant thrombocytopenia and mucocutaneous bleeding, without other red flags, ITP is a likely diagnosis. Management depends on the platelet count and bleeding severity. If there is no active, significant bleeding and the platelet count is not critically low (e.g., >20-30 x 10^9/L), a 'watch and wait' approach may be appropriate. For severe thrombocytopenia or active bleeding, first-line treatment in secondary care typically involves corticosteroids (e.g., prednisolone) to suppress the autoimmune response. Intravenous immunoglobulin (IVIg) may be used for rapid, temporary increases in platelet count in emergency situations.

Anticoagulant-Associated Bleeding

For patients on anticoagulants (e.g., warfarin, DOACs) who present with significant bleeding, urgent intervention is required. This involves stopping the offending drug and assessing the severity of the bleed. Specific reversal agents may be required (e.g., Vitamin K for warfarin, andexanet alfa for certain DOACs), guided by local protocols and haematology advice. The decision to restart anticoagulation later depends on balancing the risk of further bleeding against the risk of thrombosis.

Benign Causes (e.g., Age-Related Purpura)

Once serious pathology has been confidently excluded through a thorough history, examination, and normal baseline blood tests (FBC, coagulation screen), management is focused on education and reassurance. For age-related purpura, explaining the benign nature of the condition, which is due to skin and blood vessel fragility, is key. There is no specific treatment, and advice may include moisturising the skin and taking care to avoid trauma.

Reflection & Learning

These brief cases illustrate the application of clinical reasoning to different presentations of bleeding and bruising. They are for educational purposes only.

Case 1: Suspected Leukaemia in Primary Care

A 28-year-old man presents to his GP with a 3-week history of feeling exhausted, noticing bruises on his legs without trauma, and bleeding gums when brushing his teeth. On examination, he is pale and has several small, non-palpable bruises. The problem representation is a young adult with constitutional symptoms (fatigue) and signs of mucocutaneous bleeding, raising high suspicion for a haematological malignancy. The GP arranges urgent blood tests (FBC, blood film) which show pancytopenia and blast cells, leading to an immediate emergency referral to haematology.

Case 2: Anticoagulant-related Bruising in A&E

An 82-year-old woman with atrial fibrillation, treated with apixaban, presents to the Emergency Department after a minor fall. She has a very large, dark purple ecchymosis covering her entire left forearm. She denies head injury, headache, or other bleeding. Vital signs are stable. The problem representation is an elderly patient on a DOAC with extensive bruising disproportionate to the mechanism of injury. Key management steps include excluding more serious injury, checking a FBC and coagulation profile (though DOACs don't reliably affect standard tests), and making a risk-benefit decision with senior input regarding temporarily withholding the apixaban.

Case 3: Vasculitis in a Paediatric Clinic

A 6-year-old boy is brought to a paediatric clinic with a 2-day history of a rash on his legs, sore ankles, and tummy pain. The rash consists of raised, purplish, non-blanching spots (palpable purpura) predominantly over his buttocks and lower limbs. The problem representation is a child with the classic triad of palpable purpura, arthritis, and abdominal pain, highly suggestive of Henoch-Schönlein purpura (HSP). Initial investigations would include urinalysis to check for renal involvement (proteinuria/haematuria), blood pressure measurement, and a FBC/coagulation screen to exclude thrombocytopenia. Management is primarily supportive with analgesia.
