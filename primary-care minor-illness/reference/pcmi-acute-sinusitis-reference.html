Acute Sinusitis in Primary Care Minor Illness
Context: Upper Respiratory and Sinus Symptoms First-Contact Assessment
Overview and key guidance

Acute sinusitis, or acute rhinosinusitis, is a common first-contact presentation after a cold. Most cases are self-limiting and viral at the start, so the key clinical task is to separate uncomplicated illness from the small number of patients with orbital spread, intracranial spread, or another diagnosis that needs urgent review.

Typical symptoms are nasal blockage, nasal discharge, facial pain or pressure, and a reduced sense of smell. The condition commonly lasts two to three weeks, even when the initial viral upper respiratory tract infection has already started to settle.

Pathophysiology and why this matters
  • Viral trigger: most episodes begin with mucosal inflammation after a common cold.
  • Ostial obstruction: swollen mucosa blocks sinus drainage, causing pressure and mucus trapping.
  • Secondary bacterial infection: can occur, but usually after the illness has already become more persistent or severe.
  • Why it matters: the first job is usually symptom relief and safety-netting, not immediate antibiotics.
Differential diagnosis framework
Common and likely
  • Acute viral URTI / common cold
  • Allergic rhinitis
  • Simple acute rhinosinusitis
Often confused with sinusitis
  • Dental abscess: tooth-centred pain or tenderness.
  • Migraine or facial pain syndromes: pain without nasal features.
  • Upper airway allergy: itch, sneezing, watery discharge.
Must not miss
  • Orbital cellulitis / abscess
  • Intracranial infection: meningitis, abscess, cavernous sinus thrombosis.
  • Nasal tumour / other structural lesion: persistent unilateral symptoms or blood-stained discharge.
Focused history taking
Symptom pattern: congestion, discharge, facial pain or pressure, smell loss

Why it matters: The classic pattern is nasal blockage plus facial pressure, often worse on bending forward.

Positive answer: Facial pain with a blocked nose supports rhinosinusitis; prominent itch and sneezing point more toward allergy.

Action: Decide whether this is likely uncomplicated sinusitis or whether another diagnosis should be prioritised.

Duration and course: How long have symptoms lasted, and are they improving, stable, or worsening?

Why it matters: Duration is one of the most important triage clues in sinusitis.

Positive answer: Less than 10 days usually supports self-limiting viral disease; symptoms beyond 10 days that are not improving raise the case for GP review or prescription treatment.

Action: Use the timeline to guide self-care, delayed treatment, or referral rather than treating every case the same way.

Red-flag symptom screen: eye symptoms, severe headache, neck stiffness, altered consciousness, unilateral blood-stained discharge

Why it matters: These features shift the diagnosis away from routine sinusitis and toward complications or a structural cause.

Positive answer: Visual change, severe frontal headache, meningism, or persistent unilateral symptoms are not routine community-management findings.

Action: Arrange urgent referral if any orbital, intracranial, or unilateral red flag is present.

Background risk and treatment history: asthma, allergy, immunosuppression, pregnancy, previous episodes, and what has already been tried

Why it matters: Comorbidity changes both the safety threshold and the treatment choices.

Positive answer: Immunosuppression, pregnancy, or repeated episodes lowers the threshold for escalation and careful follow-up.

Action: Tailor advice, check medicine safety, and avoid over-reliance on repeated OTC treatment.

Focused examination strategy
When examination is needed

Why it matters: Many straightforward cases can be managed from the history, but uncertain or severe presentations should be examined.

Positive trigger: Eye symptoms, high fever, unilateral symptoms, severe pain, or poor response to initial self-care.

Action: Arrange face-to-face examination rather than assuming routine sinusitis.

Nasal and facial examination: inspect for mucosal inflammation, purulent discharge, facial tenderness, and swelling

Why it matters: It helps confirm the likely diagnosis and look for more serious spread.

Positive finding: Inflamed nasal mucosa and facial tenderness fit sinusitis; periorbital swelling, redness, or pain with eye movement is more concerning.

Action: Treat conservatively if uncomplicated, or escalate if red flags are seen.

General observations and vital signs

Why it matters: Fever, systemic illness, or altered appearance may be the clue that the illness is not routine.

Positive finding: High temperature or a very unwell appearance raises concern for severe infection or complication.

Action: Lower the threshold for urgent referral or secondary-care assessment.

Red flag synthesis and escalation triggers
  • Emergency: periorbital swelling, painful or restricted eye movements, visual loss, proptosis, severe frontal headache, meningism, altered consciousness, or new focal neurology.
  • Urgent GP / ENT review: persistent unilateral symptoms, blood-stained discharge, recurrent disease, or symptoms not improving after about 10 days.
  • Same-day escalation: high fever, signs of sepsis, or a patient who looks systemically very unwell.

Diagnostic timeout and common pitfalls
  • Do not equate green discharge with bacterial infection.
  • Do not miss dental pain or migraine just because the nose is blocked.
  • Do not keep advising repeated decongestant use beyond the safe window.
  • Do not miss eye symptoms, severe headache, or unilateral blood-stained discharge.
Management, follow-up, and safety-netting
Uncomplicated disease

offer reassurance, rest, hydration, simple analgesia, and saline nasal sprays or irrigation.

Symptomatic products

topical decongestants may help short-term, but they should not be used for more than 7 days because of rebound congestion risk.

Prescription options

intranasal corticosteroids can be considered when symptoms persist beyond 10 days; antibiotics are reserved for severe, worsening, or high-risk cases.

Safety-netting

seek urgent review for eye swelling, visual change, severe headache, meningism, confusion, or rapid deterioration.

Special populations and role-aware notes
  • Children: keep a lower threshold for referral if there is eye swelling or a very unwell appearance.
  • Older adults: atypical presentation and comorbidity make careful review more important.
  • Pregnancy and breastfeeding: favour self-care and simple analgesia; avoid routine decongestant use.
  • Immunosuppression: lowers the threshold for investigation, antibiotics, and hospital assessment.
  • Role-aware note: pharmacy or minor-illness management is only appropriate when the picture is uncomplicated and there are no red flags.
Illustrative cases
  • Case 1: 34-year-old with 6 days of blocked nose and facial pressure after a cold, afebrile, no red flags: uncomplicated acute sinusitis.
  • Case 2: 52-year-old with 12 days of persistent facial pressure and purulent discharge, not improving: review for intranasal corticosteroid or delayed treatment.
  • Case 3: child with swollen red eyelid and fever after a recent cold: urgent referral for orbital complication until proven otherwise.
