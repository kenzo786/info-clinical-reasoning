Acute Diarrhoea in Primary Care
Context: Loose Stools First-Contact Assessment
Overview and key guidance

Acute diarrhoea is usually a short-lived infectious illness, but the immediate task is to separate routine self-limiting gastroenteritis from dehydration, dysentery, sepsis, Clostridioides difficile, inflammatory bowel disease, or another serious cause. The consultation should start with hydration status and red flags rather than assuming the problem is "just a bug".

UK primary care guidance is conservative: oral rehydration and safety-netting are the foundation, antibiotics are not used for uncomplicated viral illness, and loperamide is only for selected uncomplicated watery diarrhoea in adults. Children, frail adults, and anyone with blood in stool or significant systemic upset need a much lower threshold for escalation.

Pathophysiology and why this matters
  • Mechanism: diarrhoea reflects disturbed water and electrolyte handling in the intestine.
  • Common pattern: viral gastroenteritis produces watery stool, cramps, nausea, and usually settles spontaneously.
  • Important complication: fluid loss can quickly lead to dehydration, especially in children and older adults.
  • Why it matters: the main management task is rehydration and identifying which patients are too unwell or too high risk for simple OTC advice.
Differential diagnosis framework
Common and likely
  • Viral gastroenteritis
  • Food-related gastroenteritis
  • Self-limiting acute infective diarrhoea
Often confused with acute diarrhoea
  • Traveller's diarrhoea
  • Medication-related diarrhoea
  • Post-antibiotic diarrhoea
  • Irritable bowel syndrome flare
Must not miss
  • Dehydration or shock
  • Dysentery
  • Clostridioides difficile infection
  • Inflammatory bowel disease flare
  • Sepsis or significant abdominal pathology
Focused history taking
Stool pattern: frequency, watery or bloody, mucus, nocturnal symptoms, duration

Why it matters: watery diarrhoea is usually less concerning than bloody or persistent diarrhoea.

Positive answer: blood, mucus, or symptoms beyond 14 days should shift the diagnosis away from simple viral illness.

Action: consider urgent GP review, stool testing, or referral depending on severity.

Hydration and systemic upset: thirst, reduced urine, dizziness, lethargy, vomiting, fever, confusion

Why it matters: dehydration is the main immediate risk in acute diarrhoea.

Positive answer: dry mouth, low urine output, dizziness, or confusion raises concern for clinically important dehydration.

Action: escalate earlier, and do not rely on simple self-care alone if oral intake is poor.

Red flag screen: blood, high fever, severe pain, weight loss, recent antibiotics, recent hospitalisation, immunocompromise

Why it matters: these features raise concern for invasive infection, C. difficile, IBD, or another serious diagnosis.

Positive answer: blood in stool, severe abdominal pain, or recent antibiotic exposure should change the whole pathway.

Action: refer or investigate rather than advising routine OTC treatment.

Exposure and context: travel, sick contacts, outbreaks, dietary trigger, medication review

Why it matters: context helps separate viral gastroenteritis from food poisoning, traveller's diarrhoea, or medication-related symptoms.

Positive answer: recent travel or outbreak exposure supports an infective cause, while new medicines may explain non-infective diarrhoea.

Action: tailor advice and decide whether further investigation is needed.

Focused examination strategy
General appearance and observations

Why it matters: the patient may look well despite frequent stools, or may show significant dehydration or sepsis.

Positive finding: tachycardia, hypotension, fever, or confusion suggests higher risk.

Action: escalate if the patient looks unwell rather than relying on symptom count alone.

Hydration assessment: mucous membranes, capillary refill, urine output, tears, orthostatic symptoms

Why it matters: hydration status is the key decision point in most cases.

Positive finding: dry mucosa, prolonged capillary refill, poor urine output, or orthostatic dizziness indicates clinically important fluid loss.

Action: prioritise oral rehydration or urgent review depending on severity.

Targeted abdominal and systemic exam

Why it matters: severe pain, guarding, focal tenderness, or fever can suggest pathology beyond routine gastroenteritis.

Positive finding: significant abdominal tenderness or distension should not be treated as simple OTC diarrhoea.

Action: arrange same-day assessment or urgent referral if concerning.

Red flag synthesis and escalation triggers
  • Urgent / same-day: blood in stool, severe dehydration, shock, severe abdominal pain, high fever, or systemic illness.
  • Prompt GP review: recent antibiotics or hospitalisation, suspected C. difficile, IBD flare, symptoms lasting more than 14 days, or immunocompromise.
  • Routine self-care only: short-lived watery diarrhoea in a well patient with no red flags.
Investigations and decision points
  • No routine stool test: classic mild viral diarrhoea usually does not need investigation.
  • Stool culture: consider if there is blood, prolonged symptoms, severe illness, outbreak concern, or immunocompromise.
  • Observation: assess hydration and severity before deciding on home care.
  • Medication review: check for recent antibiotics, which raises the possibility of C. difficile.
Evidence and guideline pearls
  • Oral rehydration is the cornerstone of treatment.
  • Antibiotics are not used for uncomplicated viral diarrhoea.
  • Loperamide is only for selected watery diarrhoea in adults and should be avoided when there is blood, fever, or suspected inflammatory disease.
  • Children and frail adults need a lower threshold for review because dehydration develops faster.
Diagnostic timeout and common pitfalls
  • Do not dismiss blood in stool as routine gastroenteritis.
  • Do not recommend loperamide when there is fever, blood, or suspected C. difficile / IBD.
  • Do not miss dehydration because the patient is still drinking some fluids.
  • Do not forget recent antibiotics or hospital admission.
Management, follow-up, and safety-netting

Uncomplicated illness: encourage oral rehydration, rest, and a normal diet as tolerated.

Adult symptom control: loperamide may be used only when diarrhoea is watery and red flags are absent.

Safety-netting: seek urgent help for blood in stool, worsening abdominal pain, fever, dehydration, confusion, or diarrhoea that does not settle.

Special populations and role-aware notes
  • Children: dehydration risk is higher and ORS should be used early.
  • Older adults: lower threshold for urgent review because dehydration and sepsis can evolve quickly.
  • Recent antibiotics or IBD: avoid loperamide and seek medical review.
  • Role-aware note: community pharmacy can support ORS advice only if the patient is clearly low risk and well enough for home care.
Illustrative cases
  • Case 1: a well adult with two days of watery diarrhoea, no blood, and good oral intake can usually be managed with ORS and safety-netting.
  • Case 2: diarrhoea after recent antibiotics with fever and abdominal pain should raise concern for C. difficile.
  • Case 3: blood in stool with severe pain or dehydration needs urgent GP or hospital assessment.
