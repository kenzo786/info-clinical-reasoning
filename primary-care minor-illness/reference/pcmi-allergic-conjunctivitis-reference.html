Allergic Conjunctivitis in Primary Care Minor Illness
Context: Itchy Red Eye First-Contact Assessment
Overview and key guidance

Allergic conjunctivitis is a common, usually benign cause of bilateral itchy red eyes, often linked with hay fever or other atopic disease. The hallmark symptom is itch; this is the clue that keeps the diagnosis on track.

The first-contact task is to distinguish a typical itchy allergic eye from a painful or unilateral red eye that could represent keratitis, uveitis, glaucoma, or other pathology. Management is usually stepwise, starting with trigger reduction and lubricants, then escalating to topical or oral anti-allergy treatment when needed.

Pathophysiology and why this matters
  • Type I hypersensitivity: allergen exposure cross-links IgE on conjunctival mast cells, causing histamine release.
  • Why patients itch: histamine stimulation of conjunctival nerves makes itch the dominant symptom.
  • Why eyes water and swell: vasodilation and vascular permeability produce redness, tearing, and chemosis.
  • Why it matters: knowing itch is central helps separate allergy from the painful red-eye causes that must not be missed.
Differential diagnosis framework
Common and likely
  • Seasonal allergic conjunctivitis
  • Perennial allergic conjunctivitis
  • Allergic conjunctivitis with rhinitis
Often confused with allergy
  • Viral conjunctivitis: watery but more infective, often with recent URTI and pre-auricular nodes.
  • Bacterial conjunctivitis: more sticky or purulent, less itch-dominant.
  • Dry eye: irritation and burning, usually less itchy and more fluctuating.
Must not miss
  • Keratitis: pain, photophobia, reduced vision, contact lens risk.
  • Anterior uveitis: unilateral pain, photophobia, ciliary flush.
  • Acute angle-closure glaucoma: severe pain, reduced vision, headache, haloes.
Focused history taking
Core symptom pattern: itch, watering, bilaterality, seasonality, trigger exposure

Why it matters: itch is the single most useful symptom for allergic conjunctivitis.

Positive answer: bilateral itch with watery discharge and seasonal or exposure-related pattern strongly supports allergy.

Action: keep the patient on an allergy pathway only if the history stays itch-dominant and non-red-flag.

Associated atopy and upper-airway symptoms: rhinitis, sneezing, asthma, eczema

Why it matters: associated atopy increases diagnostic confidence and guides combined eye-plus-nasal treatment.

Positive answer: hay fever pattern or other atopic history makes allergy more likely.

Action: consider whether oral antihistamines or broader allergy advice are needed, not just eye drops.

Red-flag screen: pain, photophobia, reduced vision, unilateral symptoms, contact lens wear, trauma

Why it matters: these answers are how the novice avoids missing serious ocular disease.

Positive answer: a painful or unilateral red eye, especially in a contact lens wearer, is not routine allergic conjunctivitis.

Action: refer urgently rather than trying escalating OTC anti-allergy treatment.

Focused examination strategy
Visual acuity and laterality check

Why it matters: allergic conjunctivitis should not meaningfully reduce acuity, and it is usually bilateral.

Positive finding: persistent reduced vision or marked unilateral disease is a red flag.

Action: stop and escalate rather than assuming allergy.

Conjunctiva, lids, discharge, and pupil

Why it matters: typical findings are diffuse redness, watery discharge, and mild oedema; the pupil and cornea should stay normal.

Positive finding: chemosis and lid swelling support allergy; corneal opacity or pupil abnormality do not.

Action: treat allergy only if deeper eye pathology has been reasonably excluded.

Extra caution in contact lens wearers or severe symptoms

Why it matters: contact lenses lower the threshold for urgent evaluation of any painful red eye.

Positive finding: pain, focal corneal concern, or lens-related symptoms make keratitis more important than allergy.

Action: lens removal and urgent same-day optometry or ophthalmology review.

Red flag synthesis and escalation triggers
  • Urgent / same-day: pain, photophobia, reduced vision, unilateral marked redness, corneal haze, abnormal pupil, or trauma history.
  • Urgent / same-day: contact lens wearer with painful red eye or severe symptoms not fitting straightforward allergy.
  • Prompt review: symptoms that do not improve as expected, uncertainty about the diagnosis, or concern about more severe allergic eye disease.
Investigations and decision points
Usually a clinical diagnosis

based on bilateral itch, watery symptoms, and atopic pattern.

Main decision point

whether this is truly a benign allergic eye or whether the history/exam contains red flags.

No role for routine antibiotics

allergy is not an infective process.

Specialist assessment

needed if the clinician cannot confidently exclude corneal or deeper ocular disease.

Diagnostic timeout and common pitfalls
  • Do not call a painful red eye “allergy”.
  • Do not forget that allergic conjunctivitis is usually bilateral and itch-dominant.
  • Do not miss contact lens-related keratitis because the eye is watery.
  • Do not reach for antibiotics when the pattern is clearly allergic.
Management, follow-up, and safety-netting
First-line management

allergen avoidance, cold compresses, avoiding eye rubbing, and lubricants for comfort.

Escalation of treatment

consider topical anti-allergy drops and oral antihistamines depending on symptom burden and associated rhinitis.

Safety-netting

seek urgent review for pain, photophobia, visual change, unilateral worsening, or failure to improve with a reasonable treatment trial.

Special populations and role-aware notes
  • Children: check age licensing and make sure infective conjunctivitis is not being mistaken for allergy.
  • Older adults: think about dry eye overlap and avoid sedating oral antihistamines when possible.
  • Pregnancy: start conservatively with non-drug measures and simple lubricants.
  • Role-aware note: first-contact clinicians can manage typical allergic conjunctivitis safely only if the red-eye exclusion step has been done properly.
Illustrative cases
  • Case 1: 25-year-old with bilateral itchy watery eyes every spring and associated sneezing: seasonal allergic conjunctivitis.
  • Case 2: contact lens wearer with unilateral painful photophobic red eye: urgent keratitis exclusion, not routine allergy.
  • Case 3: child with itchy eyes and eczema background but no pain or photophobia: likely allergic conjunctivitis if bilateral and watery.
