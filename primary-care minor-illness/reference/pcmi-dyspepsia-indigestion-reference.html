Indigestion / Dyspepsia in Primary Care Minor Illness
Context: Upper Gastrointestinal Discomfort First-Contact Assessment
Overview and key guidance

Dyspepsia describes upper gastrointestinal symptoms such as epigastric pain or discomfort, heartburn, bloating, early satiety, belching, and nausea. In primary care the key task is to separate likely functional dyspepsia or reflux from peptic ulcer disease, medication-related symptoms, biliary or pancreatic disease, cardiac pain, or upper GI cancer.

Most uncomplicated dyspepsia can be managed with conservative advice and short courses of treatment, but the consultation must actively look for alarm features. NICE guidance supports a cautious, stepwise approach, with testing or referral when red flags, age-related risk, or persistence make simple self-management unsafe.

Pathophysiology and why this matters
  • Acid and mucosal defence: symptoms can arise when stomach acid overwhelms protective mechanisms.
  • Functional dyspepsia: common, with no structural cause identified, and often linked to gut-brain interaction.
  • Peptic ulcer disease: commonly linked to H. pylori or NSAID exposure.
  • Why it matters: symptoms can be benign, but the same symptom cluster can also hide serious disease, so the red-flag screen is essential.
Differential diagnosis framework
Common and likely
  • Functional dyspepsia
  • GORD
  • Medication-related dyspepsia
Often confused with dyspepsia
  • Peptic ulcer disease: often NSAID or H. pylori related.
  • Biliary colic / biliary tract disease: often related to fatty food.
  • Pancreatitis: severe epigastric pain radiating to the back.
Must not miss
  • Upper GI cancer: dysphagia, weight loss, anaemia, persistent vomiting, mass, bleeding.
  • Cardiac pain: epigastric discomfort can be an atypical MI presentation.
  • Upper GI bleeding: haematemesis or melaena.
Focused history taking
Symptom pattern: meal relation, posture relation, heartburn, epigastric pain, nausea, bloating

Why it matters: the symptom pattern helps separate reflux-dominant disease from ulcer-like or non-specific dyspepsia.

Positive answer: burning after meals or when lying down suggests GORD; gnawing epigastric pain may suggest ulcer disease.

Action: use the pattern to decide whether self-care, acid suppression, or referral is most appropriate.

Red flag screen: dysphagia, weight loss, persistent vomiting, bleeding, anaemia, severe pain

Why it matters: alarm features are the main reason dyspepsia needs urgent investigation.

Positive answer: any swallowing difficulty, weight loss, or bleeding raises concern for cancer or upper GI bleeding.

Action: refer urgently rather than starting routine OTC management.

Medication history: NSAIDs, aspirin, bisphosphonates, steroids, calcium channel blockers, iron

Why it matters: medication is a common and modifiable cause of dyspepsia.

Positive answer: NSAID use is especially important because it can trigger or worsen ulcer disease.

Action: review whether the medicine can be stopped, swapped, or gastroprotected by the prescriber.

Risk context: age, smoking, alcohol, previous ulcer or GORD, cardiac risk, previous H. pylori testing

Why it matters: age and vascular risk lower the threshold for referral or cardiac consideration.

Positive answer: new symptoms in an older adult, or exertional discomfort with cardiac risk factors, deserves more caution.

Action: keep the differential broad and avoid assuming all epigastric pain is simple indigestion.

Focused examination strategy
When examination is needed

Why it matters: many mild dyspepsia presentations are assessed from history, but red flags or systemic illness need examination.

Positive trigger: weight loss, vomiting, anaemia concern, severe pain, or uncertainty about the diagnosis.

Action: examine rather than treating as simple indigestion by default.

General observation: pallor, cachexia, distress, dehydration

Why it matters: the overall appearance can reveal anaemia, malignancy, or systemic illness.

Positive finding: pallor or weight loss should not be ignored.

Action: escalate if the patient looks unwell or cachectic.

Abdominal examination: epigastric tenderness, mass, guarding, distension

Why it matters: examination can point to ulcer disease, biliary disease, pancreatitis, or obstruction.

Positive finding: a palpable mass or severe tenderness is not routine dyspepsia.

Action: arrange urgent review if the abdominal exam is concerning.

Cardiac consideration: exertional features, breathlessness, sweating, radiation, cardiovascular risk

Why it matters: inferior MI can present with epigastric pain and nausea.

Positive finding: exertional or anginal features should lower the threshold for emergency referral.

Action: do not treat the symptom as simple indigestion if cardiac pain is plausible.

Red flag synthesis and escalation triggers
  • Emergency same-day: haematemesis, melaena, severe pain, or possible cardiac pain.
  • Urgent 2WW / GP referral: dysphagia, weight loss, persistent vomiting, anaemia, mass, or new dyspepsia in an older adult.
  • Prompt review: symptoms that recur quickly or fail to improve after a sensible treatment trial.
  • Do not manage as simple OTC: if the story is progressive, unexplained, or mixed with systemic illness.

Diagnostic timeout and common pitfalls
  • Do not assume all epigastric pain is benign.
  • Do not miss cardiac pain because the pain is “indigestion-like”.
  • Do not keep supplying acid suppression without checking for alarm features.
  • Do not overlook NSAID-related symptoms.
Management, follow-up, and safety-netting
Core management

start with lifestyle and trigger reduction, then use antacids / alginates for quick relief or a short PPI trial if reflux is likely.

Medication review

stop or review likely offending drugs where appropriate.

H. pylori

test and treat if indicated, or refer for GP investigation if alarm features are present.

Safety-netting

urgent help for dysphagia, bleeding, persistent vomiting, severe pain, or worsening symptoms.

Special populations and role-aware notes
  • Children: persistent symptoms deserve GP review rather than repeat OTC treatment.
  • Older adults: lower threshold for referral and investigation.
  • Pregnancy / breastfeeding: keep treatment conservative and use self-care / antacids or alginates first.
  • Role-aware note: simple reflux-like dyspepsia can be managed in pharmacy, but alarm features or cardiac concern need GP or emergency assessment.
