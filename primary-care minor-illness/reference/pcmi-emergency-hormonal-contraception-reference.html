Emergency Hormonal Contraception in Primary Care Minor Illness
Context: Emergency Contraception First-Contact Assessment
Overview and key guidance

A request for emergency contraception is not a symptom diagnosis but a time-critical risk-management consultation. The clinician must clarify the timing and nature of unprotected intercourse or contraceptive failure, offer the most effective available option, and identify safeguarding concerns, existing pregnancy concerns, ectopic risk, and key medication interactions.

The copper IUD is the most effective emergency contraception overall and should be discussed whenever feasible. Ulipristal acetate is generally the most effective oral option, while levonorgestrel remains useful in the appropriate time window and interacts differently with ongoing contraception. Current UK practice is guided primarily by FSRH emergency contraception guidance.

Pathophysiology and why this matters
  • Levonorgestrel: mainly delays or inhibits ovulation if given before the LH surge has progressed too far.
  • Ulipristal acetate: delays ovulation later in the cycle than levonorgestrel can.
  • Copper IUD: is the most effective option and is not undermined by body weight or enzyme-inducing medicines in the way oral methods can be.
  • Clinical implication: the consultation is highly time- and context-dependent; the “best” option changes with timing, cycle stage, medicines, and access.
Differential diagnosis framework
Primary problem to solve
  • Risk of pregnancy after UPSI or contraceptive failure
Important concurrent issues
  • Existing pregnancy already established
  • STI exposure
  • Need for ongoing contraception
Must not miss
  • Sexual assault or coercion
  • Safeguarding concern in a child or vulnerable adult
  • Ectopic pregnancy concern: unilateral abdominal pain or pregnancy concern after treatment failure
Focused history taking
Timing and nature of the event: exact time since UPSI, repeated episodes, method failure details

Why it matters: Timing determines which emergency contraception options are still suitable.

Positive answer: Multiple episodes or presentation outside oral windows changes the recommendation toward Cu-IUD referral.

Action: Select the best available option based on hours since intercourse and whether more than one risk event occurred.

Cycle and pregnancy history: LMP, cycle regularity, overdue bleed, pregnancy symptoms

Why it matters: This helps estimate risk, possible ovulation timing, and whether pregnancy may already exist.

Positive answer: Overdue bleeding or pregnancy symptoms should prompt pregnancy testing and a more careful assessment of expectations.

Action: Test or refer as appropriate; do not pretend EHC will solve an already-established pregnancy.

Drug history and medical factors: enzyme inducers, gastric pH agents, severe asthma on oral steroids, malabsorption

Why it matters: Drug interactions and comorbidity can materially reduce oral EHC effectiveness or suitability.

Positive answer: Enzyme-inducing medication pushes the recommendation strongly toward Cu-IUD; severe asthma on oral steroids may make ulipristal unsuitable.

Action: Use FSRH-style logic and avoid casual supply of a compromised oral option.

Safeguarding and consent context

Why it matters: A technically correct contraceptive choice is not enough if the consultation misses exploitation or coercion.

Positive answer: Non-consensual sex, concern about control, or age-related safeguarding concerns trigger specialist pathways.

Action: Follow safeguarding procedures and involve appropriate services urgently.

Focused examination strategy
General principle: oral EHC usually does not require examination

Why it matters: This is primarily a history-led consultation.

Positive trigger for exam: abdominal pain, bleeding, ectopic concern, STI symptoms, or pelvic pathology concern.

Action: move out of a simple supply model and into clinical assessment or referral.

BMI / weight and practical suitability discussion

Why it matters: Oral EHC effectiveness may be reduced in higher BMI or body weight, though thresholds should be handled cautiously and in line with current guidance.

Positive finding: Higher BMI strengthens the case for Cu-IUD counselling rather than overconfident oral reassurance.

Action: explain uncertainty honestly and offer the more reliable option where possible.

Red flag synthesis and escalation triggers
  • Urgent safeguarding action: sexual assault, coercion, or child protection concern.
  • Urgent same-day clinical review: unilateral abdominal pain, fever, significant bleeding, or likely ectopic / PID concern.
  • Urgent specialist contraception pathway: outside oral window, enzyme-inducing medication, or Cu-IUD is clearly the best option and needs arranging quickly.
Investigations and decision points
  • Pregnancy testing: if existing pregnancy is possible or if subsequent follow-up is needed after EHC use.
  • No routine pelvic examination: for a straightforward oral EHC consultation without concerning symptoms.
  • Decision point: Cu-IUD versus oral method should be explicit, not implied.
  • STI screening signposting: should be built into the consultation when relevant.
Evidence and guideline pearls
  • Cu-IUD is the most effective emergency contraception and should be discussed whenever feasible.
  • Ulipristal is generally the preferred oral option when suitable and within the relevant time window.
  • After ulipristal, hormonal contraception should be delayed for 5 days and barrier precautions used in the interim.
  • Enzyme-inducing medicines materially reduce oral EHC effectiveness; do not hide this uncertainty from the patient.
Diagnostic timeout and common pitfalls
  • Do not frame oral EHC as equally effective to Cu-IUD.
  • Do not forget repeated UPSI in the same cycle.
  • Do not miss safeguarding because the consultation feels transactional.
  • Do not give false reassurance when interacting medicines are present.
Management, follow-up, and safety-netting

Preferred pathway: discuss Cu-IUD, then select the best suitable oral option if that is the chosen or available route.

Counselling essentials: what to do if vomiting occurs within 3 hours, when to start or restart regular contraception, when to use barrier methods, and when to do a pregnancy test.

Method-specific follow-up: after ulipristal, delay hormonal contraception for 5 days and use barrier protection in the interim; after levonorgestrel, ongoing hormonal contraception can usually be restarted immediately with the appropriate additional precautions.

Safety-netting: urgent review for severe abdominal pain, late or unusual period, or concern about pregnancy.

Special populations and role-aware notes
  • Under 16s: Fraser competence and safeguarding are central.
  • Breastfeeding: treatment choice and counselling differ between oral methods; check current guidance before advising.
  • Higher BMI / enzyme-inducing drugs: push the conversation toward Cu-IUD rather than overselling oral efficacy.
  • Role-aware note: pharmacy clinicians can deliver excellent first-contact EHC care, but complex safeguarding, pelvic pain, or Cu-IUD access needs prompt GP or sexual-health linkage rather than a narrow supply model.
Illustrative cases
  • Case 1: 26-year-old, condom failure 30 hours ago, no interactions: ulipristal after Cu-IUD discussion.
  • Case 2: patient on carbamazepine with UPSI 48 hours ago: urgent Cu-IUD pathway rather than false reassurance with oral EHC.
  • Case 3: 15-year-old requesting EHC: Fraser assessment plus safeguarding and STI discussion built into the consultation.
