Haemorrhoids in Primary Care
Context: Rectal Bleeding and Anorectal Symptoms First-Contact Assessment
Overview and key guidance

Haemorrhoids are a common cause of painless bright red rectal bleeding, anal discomfort, pruritus, and prolapse, but the first task in primary care is to avoid assuming that every bleeding anorectal symptom is benign. The consultation must actively exclude colorectal cancer, inflammatory bowel disease, anal fissure, and other causes of rectal bleeding before settling on haemorrhoids as the explanation.

UK practice is conservative: confirm a typical symptom pattern, check red flags, examine when appropriate, and prioritise fibre, fluid, and toileting advice. Topical treatments may help symptoms, but they should not distract from bowel habit optimisation and cancer-safe referral when the pattern is atypical.

Pathophysiology and why this matters
  • Normal anatomy: anal vascular cushions are a normal part of continence, not a disease.
  • Mechanism: chronic straining, constipation, pregnancy, prolonged sitting, or raised intra-abdominal pressure can engorge and displace these cushions.
  • Typical symptoms: painless bright red bleeding, lumping or prolapse, soreness, itch, and soiling.
  • Why it matters: the same symptom pattern can also be caused by fissure, IBD, cancer, or rectal polyps, so the diagnosis must be made carefully rather than by assumption.
Differential diagnosis framework
Common and likely
  • Internal or external haemorrhoids: bright red bleeding, prolapse, or irritation linked to straining.
  • Anal fissure: sharp pain and bright bleeding on defecation rather than prolapsing swelling.
  • Pruritus ani: isolated anal itch without true haemorrhoidal swelling or bleeding.
Often confused with haemorrhoids
  • Constipation-related straining: bright red blood on wiping may reflect straining rather than primary haemorrhoidal pain.
  • Perianal skin tags: lumps or prolapse can be mistaken for active haemorrhoidal swelling.
  • Rectal polyps: consider in younger patients with persistent bleeding not fitting simple piles.
Must not miss
  • Colorectal cancer: rectal bleeding with altered bowel habit, anaemia, weight loss, or persistent change from baseline.
  • Inflammatory bowel disease: rectal bleeding, diarrhoea, weight loss, or perianal disease beyond simple piles.
  • Anal cancer: persistent anal pain, bleeding, ulceration, or a new mass rather than simple benign anorectal symptoms.
  • Significant GI blood loss / iron deficiency anaemia: ongoing bleeding symptoms or anaemia point away from minor anorectal disease.
Focused history taking
Bleeding character: bright red, painless, on paper, in bowl, or mixed with stool?

Why it matters: typical haemorrhoidal bleeding is bright red and usually not mixed through the stool.

Positive answer: blood mixed with stool, dark blood, or bleeding with a change in bowel habit is less typical.

Action: if the pattern is atypical, broaden the differential and consider cancer referral criteria.

Pain and prolapse pattern: discomfort, lump, itch, soiling, or severe pain

Why it matters: severe sharp pain is more suggestive of fissure or thrombosis than uncomplicated internal haemorrhoids.

Positive answer: prolapse, itch, and mild soreness fit haemorrhoids; severe pain shifts the diagnosis away from simple internal haemorrhoids.

Action: examine for fissure, thrombosis, or other anorectal pathology.

Red flag screen: change in bowel habit, weight loss, abdominal pain, anaemia, family history, age over 50, persistent symptoms

Why it matters: haemorrhoids do not explain red-flag cancer features.

Positive answer: any of these features raises concern for colorectal cancer or inflammatory bowel disease.

Action: refer using the appropriate urgent pathway rather than treating as routine haemorrhoids.

Contributing factors: constipation, straining, sitting on the toilet, pregnancy, anticoagulants, diarrhoea, heavy lifting

Why it matters: these factors drive symptoms and recurrence.

Positive answer: constipation and straining strongly support haemorrhoids, while anticoagulation makes bleeding more consequential.

Action: correct the bowel habit problem and lower the threshold for review if bleeding is heavy or drug-related.

Focused examination strategy
Perianal inspection: fissure, external haemorrhoid, thrombosis, skin tags, dermatitis

Why it matters: external pathology can often be identified by inspection and may explain pain better than haemorrhoids.

Positive finding: a fissure or thrombosed lump changes management away from simple reassurance alone.

Action: treat or refer according to the visible pathology.

Digital rectal examination: if tolerated and clinically appropriate

Why it matters: DRE helps detect masses, tenderness, blood, and gross abnormalities.

Positive finding: a mass, marked tenderness, or blood on the glove makes routine haemorrhoids less secure as the diagnosis.

Action: escalate investigation rather than relying on symptomatic treatment.

Proctoscopy / visual confirmation when indicated

Why it matters: internal haemorrhoids may be seen directly when the diagnosis remains uncertain.

Positive finding: prolapsing internal cushions support haemorrhoids.

Action: use the finding to guide conservative management or referral if symptoms are significant.

Red flag synthesis and escalation triggers
  • Urgent cancer pathway / GP review: rectal bleeding with age over 50, change in bowel habit, weight loss, anaemia, abdominal mass, or persistent unexplained bleeding.
  • Same-day review: heavy bleeding, significant pain, thrombosed haemorrhoid with severe symptoms, or a patient who looks unwell.
  • Routine haemorrhoid management: classic painless bright red bleeding with an obvious constipation/straining history and no red flags.
Investigations and decision points
No routine tests

typical mild haemorrhoids with a clear pattern usually do not need investigation in pharmacy.

Blood tests

consider if bleeding is recurrent or significant enough to suggest anaemia.

Endoscopy / urgent referral

use red flags, age, and persistent symptoms to decide if cancer work-up is needed.

Examination decision

inspect and examine if the diagnosis is uncertain or pain is prominent.

Evidence and guideline pearls
Conservative management with fibre

Conservative management with fibre and fluid is the foundation of treatment.

Topical steroids or local anaesthetics can help

Topical steroids or local anaesthetics can help short-term symptoms but should not be used for long periods.

Bulk-forming laxatives are useful when diet alone

Bulk-forming laxatives are useful when diet alone is not enough.

Bleeding always needs cancer-safe thinking

Bleeding always needs cancer-safe thinking, especially in older adults.

Diagnostic timeout and common pitfalls
  • Do not label every rectal bleed as haemorrhoids.
  • Do not miss fissure when pain is the dominant symptom.
  • Do not ignore change in bowel habit, anaemia, or weight loss.
  • Do not rely on topical products without fixing constipation and straining.
Management, follow-up, and safety-netting
Uncomplicated haemorrhoids

give fibre, fluids, toileting advice, and short-term symptom relief if needed.

Persistent or recurrent symptoms

reassess the diagnosis, check for anaemia or other red flags, and consider referral.

Safety-netting

return urgently for heavy bleeding, worsening pain, black stool, weight loss, change in bowel habit, or symptoms that do not improve.

Special populations and role-aware notes
  • Pregnancy: haemorrhoids are common, but conservative management remains the mainstay.
  • Older adults: maintain a lower threshold for cancer assessment.
  • Anticoagulated patients: bleeding is more clinically important and should be reviewed carefully.
  • Role-aware note: community pharmacy can support conservative care only when the pattern is classic and low risk.
Illustrative cases
  • Case 1: a 32-year-old with bright red blood on wiping, constipation, and no red flags can usually be managed conservatively.
  • Case 2: a 58-year-old with rectal bleeding and a change in bowel habit needs urgent cancer-pathway review.
  • Case 3: severe pain with a tender external lump suggests thrombosed haemorrhoid or fissure rather than simple internal haemorrhoids.
