Acute Cough in Primary Care Minor Illness
Context: Acute Cough First-Contact Assessment
Overview and key guidance

Acute cough is usually defined as a cough lasting less than 3 weeks. In first-contact primary care, most cases reflect a self-limiting viral upper respiratory tract infection or acute bronchitis, but the consultation must reliably separate this large low-risk group from the smaller group with pneumonia, acute asthma or COPD exacerbation, pulmonary embolism, pneumothorax, heart failure, or sepsis.

The immediate tasks for a novice clinician are to assess severity, identify red flags, decide whether the patient can be managed with self-care and safety-netting, and avoid unnecessary antibiotics. Current UK practice is anchored by NICE NG120 for acute cough antimicrobial prescribing, with CRB-65 used in primary care when pneumonia is suspected.

Pathophysiology and why this matters

Cough is a protective reflex triggered by airway inflammation, secretions, mechanical irritation, or parenchymal disease. Viral infection causes epithelial injury and transient bronchial hyper-responsiveness, which explains why cough may outlast the initial coryzal illness.

  • Viral URTI / acute bronchitis: airway irritation and inflammation drive cough with modest systemic upset.
  • Pneumonia: alveolar infection and consolidation produce fever, focal chest signs, pleuritic pain, and sometimes hypoxia.
  • Asthma / COPD exacerbation: bronchospasm and airway narrowing produce wheeze, breathlessness, and reduced air entry.
  • PE / pneumothorax / heart failure: cough may be secondary, so new cough should not distract from a more dangerous cardiopulmonary process.
Differential diagnosis framework
Must not miss
  • Pneumonia: fever, pleuritic pain, focal crackles, systemic illness, raised CRB-65.
  • Sepsis: acute infection with confusion, hypotension, cyanosis, marked tachypnoea, or clinical deterioration.
  • Acute asthma or COPD exacerbation: wheeze, respiratory distress, poor air entry, inability to speak full sentences.
  • Pulmonary embolism: pleuritic pain, haemoptysis, unexplained dyspnoea, VTE risk factors.
  • Pneumothorax: sudden pleuritic pain with reduced unilateral air entry and acute breathlessness.
  • Heart failure: orthopnoea, oedema, basal crackles, frothy sputum, or new exertional breathlessness.
Common causes
  • Viral URTI
  • Acute bronchitis
  • Post-viral cough
  • COVID-19 or influenza-like illness
  • Upper airway cough syndrome
Easily missed or context-dependent
  • ACE inhibitor cough
  • Pertussis
  • Foreign body aspiration
  • Lung cancer when cough persists, especially with haemoptysis or weight loss
Focused history taking
Duration and trajectory: When did the cough start, and is it improving, static, or worsening?

Why it matters: Distinguishes acute viral illness from persistent or progressive disease.

What a positive answer means: A worsening pattern after several days, or lack of any improvement by 3-4 weeks, raises concern for pneumonia, unresolved inflammation, or another diagnosis.

What it should trigger: Consider examination, CRB-65 if pneumonia is suspected, and follow-up or referral if the course is atypical.

Breathlessness and exercise tolerance: Are they short of breath at rest, on exertion, or only during coughing episodes?

Why it matters: Helps separate a simple cough from significant respiratory compromise.

What a positive answer means: Dyspnoea at rest, inability to complete sentences, or rapidly worsening breathlessness raises asthma, COPD exacerbation, PE, pneumothorax, heart failure, or severe pneumonia.

What it should trigger: Immediate vital signs, oxygen saturation, chest examination, and same-day escalation if unstable.

Chest pain and haemoptysis: Is there pleuritic pain, central chest pain, or any blood in the sputum?

Why it matters: These features move the consultation out of “simple cough” territory.

What a positive answer means: Pleuritic pain suggests pneumonia, PE, or pneumothorax; central crushing pain needs urgent cardiac assessment; haemoptysis needs urgent explanation.

What it should trigger: Same-day medical review or emergency escalation depending on severity and overall stability.

Systemic features and comorbidity: Ask about fever, rigors, confusion, asthma/COPD, heart failure, cancer, immunosuppression, smoking, and VTE risk factors.

Why it matters: Risk status changes the threshold for antibiotics, investigations, or admission.

What a positive answer means: Confusion, rigors, major comorbidity, or immunosuppression increases the chance of serious infection or poor outcome.

What it should trigger: Lower threshold for face-to-face examination, CRB-65, chest X-ray, urgent treatment, or hospital assessment.

Focused examination strategy
General impression and respiratory effort

Why it matters: Ill appearance, exhaustion, cyanosis, or obvious respiratory distress may trump the rest of the history.

Positive finding: Inability to speak full sentences, drowsiness, use of accessory muscles, or cyanosis suggests urgent instability.

Action: Same-day urgent escalation or emergency transfer.

Vital signs: temperature, pulse, respiratory rate, BP, SpO2

Why it matters: Objective physiology is central to distinguishing uncomplicated illness from pneumonia or sepsis.

Positive finding: Tachypnoea, hypoxia, hypotension, or fever substantially raise concern for serious infection or respiratory disease.

Action: Use findings to guide CRB-65, need for antibiotics, and need for hospital assessment.

Chest examination: air entry, wheeze, crackles, focal dullness

Why it matters: Focal chest signs increase the likelihood of pneumonia; widespread wheeze suggests bronchospasm.

Positive finding: Focal crackles or bronchial breathing suggest consolidation; unilateral absent air entry suggests pneumothorax; basal crackles with oedema suggest heart failure.

Action: Decide on CRB-65, chest X-ray, acute treatment, or emergency referral.

Red flag synthesis and escalation triggers
  • Emergency / same-day escalation: severe breathlessness, SpO2 reduction, confusion, hypotension, cyanosis, haemoptysis, severe pleuritic pain, or suspected sepsis.
  • Urgent community assessment: suspected pneumonia, deterioration in a frail or immunocompromised patient, or acute worsening of asthma/COPD/heart failure.
  • Routine follow-up rather than immediate antibiotics: stable viral cough with normal observations and no focal chest signs.
Investigations and decision points
  • CRB-65: use if pneumonia is suspected in primary care.
  • Chest X-ray: not routine for simple cough, but appropriate when pneumonia, malignancy, or another structural pathology is suspected.
  • Pulse oximetry: high-value point-of-care test in breathless, older, or comorbid patients.
  • No routine antibiotics or routine bloods: in otherwise well patients with uncomplicated acute cough / bronchitis.
Evidence and guideline pearls
  • NICE NG120 advises not to routinely offer antibiotics for acute cough in people who are not systemically very unwell and are not at higher risk of complications.
  • Explain early that cough often lasts 3-4 weeks; expectation management is part of good treatment.
  • Oral or inhaled corticosteroids are not recommended for uncomplicated acute bronchitis unless treating a separate condition such as asthma.
  • Pholcodine products were withdrawn from the UK market in 2023, and codeine linctus is now POM-only.
Diagnostic timeout and common pitfalls
  • Do not anchor on sputum colour: yellow or green sputum alone does not prove bacterial infection.
  • Do not miss physiology: respiratory rate, SpO2, and blood pressure can reveal the sick patient quickly.
  • Do not forget alternatives: pleuritic pain plus cough is not automatically pneumonia; think PE or pneumothorax.
  • Do not overprescribe antibiotics: stewardship matters, but not at the expense of missing pneumonia in a higher-risk patient.
Management, follow-up, and safety-netting

Low-risk viral cough: reassure, advise fluids, rest, smoking cessation, simple symptomatic relief, and explain likely duration.

If pneumonia is suspected: assess CRB-65, follow local antimicrobial guidance, and decide on home care versus same-day hospital assessment.

Safety-netting: return urgently for worsening breathlessness, chest pain, haemoptysis, confusion, inability to maintain oral intake, or failure to improve over the expected course.

Special populations and role-aware notes
  • Children: have a lower threshold for escalation if there is stridor, grunting, poor feeding, lethargy, or suspected foreign body.
  • Older adults: may present with confusion, falls, or decline rather than obvious fever; use a lower threshold for investigation.
  • Pregnancy: prioritise assessment of severity and safe symptomatic treatment; avoid unnecessary antibiotics but do not undertreat pneumonia.
  • Role-aware note: pharmacist prescribers and Pharmacy First clinicians can manage low-risk viral cough conservatively, but abnormal observations, focal chest signs, or systemic deterioration should move the patient into GP / same-day / ED pathways.
Illustrative cases
  • Case 1: 28-year-old with 5 days of cough, coryza, mild myalgia, normal observations, clear chest: manage as viral cough with self-care and safety-netting.
  • Case 2: 77-year-old with confusion, fever, RR 32, focal crackles: treat as high-risk pneumonia and arrange urgent hospital assessment.
  • Case 3: 44-year-old with cough, pleuritic pain, haemoptysis, recent long-haul flight, normal chest exam: do not anchor on bronchitis; escalate for PE assessment.
