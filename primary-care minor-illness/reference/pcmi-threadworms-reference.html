Threadworms (Enterobiasis) in Primary Care Minor Illness
Context: Perianal itch and household transmission first-contact assessment
Overview and key guidance

Threadworm infection is common in children and household contacts. The classic symptom is nocturnal perianal itching, sometimes with visible small white worms around the anus or on stool.

The key task is to confirm a likely threadworm pattern, exclude red flags, and give clear advice that treats the whole household with mebendazole plus six weeks of hygiene measures to reduce reinfection.

Pathophysiology and why this matters
  • Enterobius vermicularis: microscopic eggs are swallowed and mature in the gut.
  • Nocturnal egg-laying: female worms migrate to the perianal skin at night, causing itch.
  • Household spread: eggs contaminate hands, bedding, towels, clothing, and surfaces.
  • Why it matters: medicine alone is often not enough unless the whole household and hygiene cycle are addressed.
Differential diagnosis framework
Common and likely
  • Threadworms
  • Perianal dermatitis or eczema
  • Haemorrhoids
Often confused with threadworms
  • Anal fissure
  • Candidiasis or irritant rash
  • Scabies
Must not miss
  • Severe abdominal pathology
  • Blood in stool or unexplained weight loss
  • Secondary skin infection from scratching
Focused history taking
Symptom pattern: nocturnal itch, disturbed sleep, visible worms, vulval irritation

Why it matters: night-time perianal itch is the most useful clue to the diagnosis.

Positive answer: visible worms or classic night itching makes threadworms likely.

Action: proceed to household treatment advice if the rest of the history is typical and there are no red flags.

Household pattern: school, nursery, siblings, or other family members affected

Why it matters: threadworms spread easily within households and close contacts.

Positive answer: more than one person with itch supports the diagnosis and the need to treat everyone together.

Action: advise simultaneous treatment for the household, not just the symptomatic person.

Red-flag screen: abdominal pain, blood, weight loss, fever, sore infected skin

Why it matters: these features are not typical of uncomplicated threadworms.

Positive answer: severe pain, bleeding, or a spreading rash lowers confidence in simple enterobiasis.

Action: refer to GP rather than supplying OTC treatment alone.

Focused examination strategy
When examination is needed

Why it matters: a typical history often makes examination unnecessary, but uncertainty or red flags change this.

Positive trigger: persistent symptoms, blood, severe pain, or visible skin infection.

Action: arrange an appropriate perianal or general examination or GP review.

Perianal inspection

Why it matters: excoriation or a visible worm can support the diagnosis.

Positive finding: scratch marks, irritated skin, or rarely a visible thread-like worm.

Action: continue with treatment advice if the rest of the picture fits; otherwise reconsider the diagnosis.

Targeted tests

Why it matters: the tape test can confirm eggs if the story is unclear.

Positive finding: eggs on the tape support threadworms.

Action: use testing only if needed, not as routine for a classic presentation.

Red flag synthesis and escalation triggers
  • Urgent / same-day: severe or persistent abdominal pain, fever with an unwell child, or secondary skin infection.
  • Prompt GP review: blood in stool, weight loss, persistent symptoms after treatment, or diagnostic uncertainty.
  • Routine threadworm management: nocturnal itch with a typical household pattern and no red flags.
Investigations and decision points
  • No routine tests: a classic history is usually enough.
  • Consider tape test: when the diagnosis is uncertain.
  • Consider GP review: if symptoms are atypical or persist despite correct treatment.
  • Check age and pregnancy status: before suggesting OTC mebendazole.
Evidence and guideline pearls
  • Mebendazole is usually first-line for adults and children over 2 years.
  • A repeat dose after 2 weeks helps clear newly hatched worms.
  • All household members should be treated at the same time.
  • Hygiene measures should continue for 6 weeks to reduce reinfection.
Diagnostic timeout and common pitfalls
  • Do not assume every itchy bottom is threadworms.
  • Do not miss eczema, fissure, haemorrhoids, or scabies.
  • Do not treat a whole household without checking age and pregnancy status.
  • Do not forget that symptoms can recur if hygiene is incomplete.
Management, follow-up, and safety-netting

First-line care: mebendazole plus strict hand hygiene, morning washing, short nails, clean underwear, and hot-washing bedding where practical.

Household advice: treat everyone at the same time, even if they have no symptoms.

Safety-netting: seek review if symptoms persist after the second dose, if pain or bleeding develops, or if the skin becomes infected.

Special populations and role-aware notes
  • Children: common in this group; keep the explanation simple and practical.
  • Pregnancy: avoid casual OTC supply and refer for advice.
  • Breastfeeding: use caution and keep advice conservative.
  • Community pharmacy: suitable for typical uncomplicated cases, but refer if red flags are present.
Illustrative cases
  • Case 1: child with night-time anal itch and siblings also itchy: treat the household.
  • Case 2: adult with persistent itch and a painful red rash: consider secondary infection or another diagnosis.
  • Case 3: child with severe abdominal pain and blood in stool: not routine threadworms, needs GP review.
