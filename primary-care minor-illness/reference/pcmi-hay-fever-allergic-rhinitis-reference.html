Hay Fever (Allergic Rhinitis) in Primary Care Minor Illness
Context: Seasonal or perennial nasal allergy first-contact assessment
Overview and key guidance

Allergic rhinitis is an IgE-mediated inflammatory response of the nasal mucosa. Typical symptoms are sneezing, itching, clear rhinorrhoea, and nasal congestion, often with itchy watery eyes.

The key task is to distinguish a likely allergic pattern from infective rhinitis, rhinitis medicamentosa, nasal polyps, or a more serious unilateral or structural problem that needs medical review.

Pathophysiology and why this matters
  • Allergen exposure: pollen, dust mite, pet dander, or mould can trigger symptoms.
  • Mucosal inflammation: leads to itch, sneeze, watery discharge, and congestion.
  • Seasonal or perennial pattern: the history is often more useful than a single examination finding.
  • Why it matters: the right treatment improves quality of life, sleep, and school or work attendance.
Differential diagnosis framework
Common and likely
  • Seasonal allergic rhinitis: sneezing, itch, and watery rhinorrhoea during pollen exposure.
  • Perennial allergic rhinitis: persistent nasal itch and rhinorrhoea from year-round allergen exposure.
  • Viral rhinitis / common cold: congestion and rhinorrhoea with self-limiting coryzal symptoms.
Often confused with hay fever
  • Non-allergic rhinitis: chronic congestion or rhinorrhoea without itch, seasonality, or clear allergen triggers.
  • Rhinitis medicamentosa: rebound congestion after frequent decongestant spray use.
  • Nasal polyps: persistent blockage and hyposmia, especially if bilateral and non-itchy.
Must not miss
  • Sinonasal tumour: unilateral blockage, bleeding, facial pain, or mass-like features.
  • GPA or other destructive inflammatory disease: crusting, epistaxis, deformity, or wider inflammatory features beyond simple rhinitis.
  • CSF leak: persistent unilateral watery rhinorrhoea, especially if positional or post-traumatic.
  • Acute bacterial sinusitis with orbital features: facial pain with periorbital swelling, diplopia, or reduced eye movement rather than uncomplicated rhinitis.
Focused history taking
Symptom pattern: sneeze, itch, clear rhinorrhoea, congestion, eye symptoms

Why it matters: itch and recurrent watery symptoms strongly support allergic rhinitis over infection.

Positive answer: seasonal recurrence, itchy eyes, or clear triggers makes hay fever more likely.

Action: treat as allergic rhinitis if the pattern is classic; do not default to antibiotics or repeated cold remedies.

Trigger history: pollen, animals, dust, mould, home or work exposure

Why it matters: identifying a trigger supports the diagnosis and gives practical advice.

Positive answer: symptoms that reliably worsen in certain settings or seasons fit an allergic pattern.

Action: discuss avoidance where practical and plan symptom control around predictable exposure.

Red-flag pattern: unilateral symptoms, blood-stained discharge, anosmia, severe facial pain

Why it matters: allergic rhinitis is usually bilateral and itchy; unilateral or destructive features need more caution.

Positive answer: one-sided obstruction, crusting, bleeding, or loss of smell without blockage is not routine hay fever.

Action: arrange GP review or ENT assessment depending on the pattern and severity.

Focused examination strategy
When examination is needed

Why it matters: many classic hay fever cases can be managed on history alone, but atypical features need examination.

Positive trigger: unilateral blockage, blood-stained discharge, facial pain, periorbital swelling, or uncertain diagnosis.

Action: examine nose, eyes, and face or refer if the setting cannot safely assess the problem.

Nasal and eye findings

Why it matters: pale boggy mucosa and watery discharge support allergic rhinitis.

Positive finding: nasal polyps, crusting, ulceration, or marked swelling suggest a different diagnosis.

Action: treat as allergic rhinitis only when the picture is otherwise typical; otherwise arrange review.

Eye and facial check

Why it matters: orbital or facial complications change urgency.

Positive finding: periorbital swelling, visual disturbance, or severe facial pain suggests more than simple hay fever.

Action: seek same-day medical review if these features are present.

Red flag synthesis and escalation triggers
  • Urgent / same-day: periorbital swelling, visual disturbance, severe facial pain, or signs of orbital spread.
  • Prompt GP / ENT review: unilateral symptoms, blood-stained discharge, persistent anosmia, crusting, or suspected structural disease.
  • Routine allergic rhinitis management: classic bilateral itchy sneezing and watery symptoms with a clear trigger pattern.
Investigations and decision points
No routine tests

classic hay fever is usually a clinical diagnosis.

Consider GP review

if diagnosis is uncertain, symptoms are severe, or first-line treatment fails.

Consider allergy testing

when a confirmed trigger history would change management.

Consider ENT / urgent review

for unilateral, bleeding, or destructive features.

Intranasal corticosteroids are usually the most effective option for persistent congestion.

Oral non-sedating antihistamines can help itch, sneeze, and rhinorrhoea.

Oral decongestants

Oral decongestants and repeated topical decongestant use should be approached cautiously because of adverse effects and rebound congestion.

Diagnostic timeout and common pitfalls
  • Do not call every blocked nose “hay fever” without checking the pattern.
  • Do not miss rhinitis medicamentosa in people overusing nasal sprays.
  • Do not ignore unilateral symptoms or bleeding.
  • Do not overpromise OTC treatment if symptoms are severe or persistent.
Management, follow-up, and safety-netting
First-line care

allergen avoidance where practical, saline rinses, and an oral antihistamine or intranasal corticosteroid according to symptom pattern.

Eye symptoms

Safety-netting

seek review if symptoms are unilateral, bloody, severe, or not improving despite appropriate first-line treatment.

Special populations and role-aware notes
  • Children: use age-appropriate advice and check for comorbid asthma or sleep disturbance.
  • Pregnancy: keep treatment choices cautious and conservative.
  • Asthma: allergic rhinitis can worsen lower airway control, so symptoms should not be dismissed.
  • Community pharmacy: suitable for classic mild-to-moderate hay fever, but refer if severe, unilateral, or atypical.
Illustrative cases
  • Case 1: springtime sneezing, itchy eyes, and clear rhinorrhoea every year: classic seasonal allergic rhinitis.
  • Case 2: blocked nose worsened by repeated decongestant spray use: consider rhinitis medicamentosa.
  • Case 3: unilateral blockage with blood-stained discharge and facial pain: not routine hay fever, needs review.
