Insect Bites and Stings in Primary Care Minor Illness
Context: Bite, Sting, and Allergic Reaction First-Contact Assessment
Overview and key guidance

Most insect bites and stings cause a short-lived local reaction with itch, redness, and mild swelling. The key job in first-contact care is to distinguish a simple local reaction from anaphylaxis, spreading cellulitis, a severe local reaction, or a tick bite that may need follow-up for Lyme disease.

Self-care, reassurance, and symptom relief are enough for most people. A careful history and a quick look at the reaction should make it possible to identify the uncommon cases that need emergency help or GP review.

Pathophysiology and why this matters
  • Simple bite or sting: local histamine release produces itch and redness.
  • Large local reaction: a bigger allergic inflammatory response can look dramatic but is not the same as infection.
  • Anaphylaxis: a systemic allergic reaction with airway or circulatory risk.
  • Why it matters: treatment depends on whether the problem is local, allergic, infectious, or a tick-bite follow-up issue.
Differential diagnosis framework
Common and likely
  • Simple local reaction
  • Large local reaction
  • Papular urticaria, especially in children
Often confused with bites and stings
  • Contact dermatitis
  • Urticaria
  • Scabies when itch is widespread or recurrent
Must not miss
  • Anaphylaxis
  • Spreading cellulitis
  • Stings in the mouth, throat, or eye
  • Tick bite with possible Lyme disease
Focused history taking
Event details: what bit or stung the patient, and when did it happen?

Why it matters: the timing and trigger help separate a short-lived local reaction from a more concerning course.

Positive answer: a rapid reaction after a sting is common; delayed expansion over hours to days can still be allergic or infectious.

Action: decide whether simple self-care is enough or whether the reaction needs escalation.

Systemic and airway symptoms: wheeze, stridor, lip or tongue swelling, dizziness, collapse

Why it matters: these are the key clues to anaphylaxis.

Positive answer: breathing difficulty or circulatory symptoms make this an emergency rather than a simple bite.

Action: treat as anaphylaxis and arrange emergency help immediately.

Infection screen: spreading redness, warmth, tenderness, fever, pus

Why it matters: scratching can lead to secondary cellulitis, which needs a different pathway from a large local allergic reaction.

Positive answer: tender spreading erythema and systemic upset point more toward infection than allergy.

Action: arrange GP assessment for possible antibiotics if cellulitis is suspected.

Tick-bite and risk history: tick exposure, fever, headache, neck stiffness, pregnancy, previous anaphylaxis

Why it matters: tick bites may need follow-up for Lyme disease, and previous systemic allergy changes the safety threshold.

Positive answer: a tick bite with later rash or systemic symptoms is not a routine bite; a history of anaphylaxis raises urgency for any new sting.

Action: give specific safety-netting and arrange review when needed.

Focused examination strategy
When examination is needed

Why it matters: the rash and the patient’s overall appearance usually clarify the diagnosis quickly.

Positive trigger: facial swelling, respiratory distress, a very large reaction, or signs of infection.

Action: escalate promptly if the reaction is not clearly simple and local.

Reaction inspection: size, itch, tenderness, blistering, and stinger presence

Why it matters: large local allergic reactions are usually itchy and swollen, while cellulitis is more tender and progressively spreading.

Positive finding: a stinger still in place supports bee sting management; blistering or extensive swelling makes the reaction more significant.

Action: remove the stinger if present, then choose self-care, treatment, or referral.

Observation and vital signs: breathing, circulation, and fever if infection is suspected

Why it matters: objective physiology is the quickest way to spot an emergency.

Positive finding: wheeze, stridor, hypotension, or an unwell appearance supports urgent escalation; fever makes cellulitis more likely.

Action: act on instability immediately rather than continuing routine advice.

Red flag synthesis and escalation triggers
  • Emergency: any signs of anaphylaxis, including wheeze, stridor, angioedema, hypotension, or collapse.
  • Urgent GP review: spreading cellulitis, high-risk sting location, severe local swelling, or a tick bite with later systemic symptoms.
  • Prompt review: reactions that are unusually painful, blistering, recurrent, or not improving as expected.
Investigations and decision points
  • No routine tests: needed for a simple bite or sting.
  • Cellulitis assessment: if redness is spreading or the area is tender and warm.
  • Tick-bite follow-up: if a migrating rash or systemic symptoms develop later.
  • Specialist allergy review: if a true systemic reaction has occurred.
Evidence and guideline pearls
  • Most insect bites and stings are self-limiting and can be treated symptomatically.
  • Large local reactions are allergic, not infectious, so antibiotics are usually not needed.
  • Antibiotics are reserved for clear secondary cellulitis.
  • Any systemic allergic reaction needs emergency treatment, not oral antihistamines alone.
Diagnostic timeout and common pitfalls
  • Do not confuse large local allergy with cellulitis.
  • Do not miss anaphylaxis because the skin reaction looks modest.
  • Do not give antibiotics for uncomplicated bites.
  • Do not forget tick bites and later Lyme disease symptoms.
Management, follow-up, and safety-netting

Simple local reaction: wash the area, use a cold compress, elevate if possible, and avoid scratching.

Symptom relief: oral antihistamines and mild topical hydrocortisone can help itch.

Escalation: cellulitis needs GP review, and any anaphylaxis symptoms need emergency help immediately.

Safety-netting: seek review if swelling spreads, fever develops, symptoms fail to improve, or a later target-like rash appears after a tick bite.

Special populations and role-aware notes
  • Children: papular urticaria is common and scratching can lead to secondary infection.
  • Older adults: sedating antihistamines can increase falls risk.
  • Pregnancy and breastfeeding: conservative symptom relief is preferred; choose antihistamines cautiously if needed.
  • History of anaphylaxis: treat any new sting as higher risk.
Illustrative cases
  • Case 1: itchy swollen bite after a picnic, no fever or breathing problems: simple local reaction.
  • Case 2: rapidly spreading tender redness and fever after a scratch: possible cellulitis needing GP review.
  • Case 3: wheeze and lip swelling minutes after a wasp sting: anaphylaxis and emergency escalation.
