Vaginal Thrush in Primary Care Minor Illness
Context: Vulvovaginal Symptoms First-Contact Assessment
Overview and key guidance

Vaginal thrush, or vulvovaginal candidiasis, is common and often straightforward, but first-contact assessment must still distinguish classic candidiasis from bacterial vaginosis, STI-related discharge, vulval dermatoses, pelvic inflammatory disease, and recurrent or complicated disease.

The immediate clinical task is to decide whether this is a typical uncomplicated episode that can be treated empirically, or whether the pattern is atypical enough to need examination, swabs, pregnancy-specific advice, or STI-focused assessment. NICE CKS and BASHH-style principles support short-course azole treatment for uncomplicated disease and a more structured plan for recurrence or treatment failure.

Pathophysiology and why this matters
  • Candida overgrowth: usually reflects overgrowth of a commensal yeast rather than a classic STI.
  • Inflammatory response: causes itch, soreness, erythema, fissuring, and thick white discharge.
  • Predisposing factors: antibiotics, pregnancy, diabetes, immunosuppression, and some hormonal states.
  • Why it matters: if the story does not fit candidiasis, treating every vaginal itch as “thrush” creates missed diagnoses and repeated reattendance.
Differential diagnosis framework
Common and likely
  • Uncomplicated vulvovaginal candidiasis
  • Recurrent candidiasis
  • Irritant or allergic vulvitis
Often confused with thrush
  • Bacterial vaginosis: malodourous discharge, usually less itchy.
  • Trichomonas / STI-related vaginitis: discharge plus STI risk, soreness, or frothy discharge.
  • Atrophic vaginitis / GSM: especially post-menopause.
Must not miss
  • PID: pelvic pain, fever, dyspareunia, abnormal bleeding.
  • Herpes or vulval dermatoses: ulcers, blisters, severe skin change.
  • VIN / malignancy pathway concern: persistent lesion, unexplained lump, ulcer, or white plaque.
Focused history taking
Symptom pattern: itch, soreness, discharge character, dysuria, dyspareunia

Why it matters: Classic thrush is itchy and sore with thick non-offensive discharge; other patterns point elsewhere.

Positive answer: Fishy smell suggests BV; pelvic pain or bleeding suggests STI / PID; ulcers or blisters suggest herpes or dermatosis.

Action: Decide whether empirical antifungal treatment is reasonable or whether examination / STI pathway is needed.

Episode frequency and previous response: first episode, recurrent episode, treatment failure

Why it matters: Recurrent disease changes both diagnosis confidence and management.

Positive answer: Frequent episodes or recent treatment failure raise resistant species, poor adherence, or wrong diagnosis.

Action: Move toward examination, swabs, and structured longer-term management rather than repeated one-off OTC treatment.

Predisposing factors and medication history: antibiotics, diabetes, pregnancy, immunosuppression, interacting drugs

Why it matters: These factors increase candidiasis risk and influence treatment safety.

Positive answer: Pregnancy changes treatment choice; warfarin or statin use makes oral fluconazole less straightforward; poor glycaemic control supports recurrence risk.

Action: Tailor treatment route and decide whether GP review or swabs are needed.

Focused examination strategy
When examination is needed

Why it matters: Not every classic recurrent episode needs examination, but atypical or complicated presentations do.

Positive trigger: First episode with uncertainty, recurrent disease, treatment failure, pelvic pain, or skin change.

Action: Arrange appropriate pelvic or vulval examination and testing.

Vulval inspection and speculum findings when examined

Why it matters: Exam helps confirm candidiasis and identify alternatives.

Positive finding: Erythema, fissuring, and adherent white discharge support thrush; ulceration, white plaques, or marked atrophy suggest other disease.

Action: Treat, swab, or refer based on the visual pattern.

Swabs and pH testing when indicated

Why it matters: Recurrent or atypical symptoms should not rely on guesswork.

Positive finding: Culture confirms candida species; elevated vaginal pH points away from uncomplicated thrush.

Action: Use results to guide longer-course or alternative therapy and to avoid repeated mislabelling.

Red flag synthesis and escalation triggers
  • Urgent / same-day: pelvic pain, fever, severe abdominal pain, post-menopausal bleeding, or severe vulval lesions.
  • Prompt GP / sexual health review: recurrent episodes, treatment failure, STI concern, age outside OTC range, or persistent vulval skin change.
  • Routine empirical treatment: classic uncomplicated episode in a patient with a known pattern and no red flags.
Investigations and decision points
  • No routine swab: needed for every classic uncomplicated case.
  • High vaginal swab: for recurrent, persistent, or atypical symptoms.
  • Pregnancy testing: if history suggests possible pregnancy and treatment choices need clarifying.
  • Consider HbA1c or diabetes review: in recurrent disease or symptoms suggesting poor glycaemic control.
Evidence and guideline pearls
  • Short-course topical azoles and single-dose oral fluconazole are both effective for uncomplicated thrush in non-pregnant adults.
  • Oral azoles should generally be avoided in pregnancy; topical treatment is preferred.
  • Recurrent thrush should be confirmed and managed as a planned problem, not as endless repeat “acute” episodes.
  • Partner treatment is not needed unless the partner has symptoms.
Diagnostic timeout and common pitfalls
  • Do not call every vaginal itch “thrush”.
  • Do not miss STI, BV, or dermatoses because the patient has had thrush before.
  • Do not keep re-treating recurrent symptoms without confirming the diagnosis.
  • Do not forget fluconazole interactions and pregnancy status.
Management, follow-up, and safety-netting

Uncomplicated disease: offer topical azole or oral fluconazole when appropriate, plus vulval skin-care advice and realistic time to improvement.

Recurrent or complicated disease: arrange examination, swabs, and a structured longer-term plan rather than repeated self-treatment.

Safety-netting: seek review if symptoms fail to improve, recur quickly, or new pelvic pain, fever, bleeding, or malodour develops.

Special populations and role-aware notes
  • Children: candidiasis is less common and the differential is broader.
  • Older / post-menopausal adults: think atrophic vaginitis and vulval dermatoses early.
  • Pregnancy: use topical therapy and a lower threshold for confirming the diagnosis.
  • Role-aware note: community pharmacy can help with clearly uncomplicated recurrent patterns in the licensed range, but atypical symptoms, recurrence, pregnancy complexity, or STI concern should move the patient into GP or sexual-health assessment.
Illustrative cases
  • Case 1: 27-year-old with itch, soreness, thick white non-offensive discharge after antibiotics: uncomplicated thrush.
  • Case 2: 41-year-old with five episodes this year and partial response to fluconazole: recurrent disease needing swab and plan.
  • Case 3: 58-year-old with vulval soreness, white patch, and post-menopausal bleeding: not routine thrush, urgent gynae pathway concern.
