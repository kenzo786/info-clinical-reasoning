Earache (Otalgia) in Primary Care Minor Illness
Context: Ear, nose and throat first-contact assessment
Overview and key guidance

Earache is common in first-contact primary care and in community pharmacy. The key task is to decide whether the pain is coming from the ear itself or is referred from the jaw, teeth, throat, or neck.

The common primary causes are acute otitis media and otitis externa. Most uncomplicated cases are self-limiting, but mastoiditis, facial nerve palsy, necrotising otitis externa, and sudden sensorineural hearing loss are red flags that need urgent escalation.

Pathophysiology and why this matters
  • Acute otitis media: middle-ear inflammation often follows a viral URTI and Eustachian tube dysfunction.
  • Otitis externa: inflammation of the external canal usually follows loss of the protective wax barrier from water, trauma, or skin disease.
  • Referred pain: shared nerve supply means a normal ear exam does not exclude clinically important pathology.
  • Why it matters: the consultation must separate benign self-limiting pain from complications or non-ear sources.
Differential diagnosis framework
Common and likely
  • Acute otitis media: ear pain with recent URTI and an inflamed or bulging tympanic membrane.
  • Otitis externa: tragal tenderness and canal inflammation rather than a bulging eardrum.
  • Earwax / canal irritation: blocked or irritated canal without middle-ear inflammatory signs.
Often referred pain
  • Dental disease: referred otalgia with tooth pain, chewing pain, or gum inflammation.
  • Temporomandibular joint dysfunction: jaw pain, clicking, or tenderness causing referred otalgia.
  • Pharyngitis or tonsillitis: referred ear pain with sore throat and inflamed pharynx or tonsils.
Must not miss
  • Mastoiditis: post-auricular swelling, pinna displacement, or systemic unwellness after ear infection.
  • Necrotising otitis externa: severe otalgia with granulation tissue or cranial nerve features, especially in diabetes or immunocompromise.
  • Temporal arteritis in older adults: new unilateral head or face pain with scalp tenderness or jaw claudication.
  • Head and neck malignancy in persistent unilateral pain: refractory unilateral otalgia with weight loss, dysphagia, or a neck mass.
Focused history taking
Pain pattern: onset, severity, triggers, ear movement pain

Why it matters: pain on tragal pressure or pinna movement points toward otitis externa.

Positive answer: rapid onset after a cold supports otitis media; pain after swimming or trauma supports otitis externa.

Action: decide whether this is likely primary ear disease or needs referral for examination.

Associated symptoms: discharge, hearing loss, fever, vertigo, coryza, sore throat

Why it matters: discharge and canal itch suggest otitis externa, while fever and URTI symptoms fit otitis media.

Positive answer: hearing loss, vertigo, or systemic upset raises concern for complication or alternative diagnosis.

Action: lower the threshold for GP review if symptoms are severe, persistent, or atypical.

Risk history: diabetes, immunosuppression, trauma, swimming, age over 50, cancer symptoms

Why it matters: these features shift the differential toward necrotising otitis externa, temporal arteritis, or malignancy.

Positive answer: diabetes or immunosuppression makes severe infection more concerning; persistent unilateral pain in an older adult is not routine earache.

Action: refer urgently when risk factors align with red flags.

Focused examination strategy
When examination is needed

Why it matters: earache cannot be safely triaged without looking at the ear when symptoms are atypical or severe.

Positive trigger: visible discharge, swelling, severe tenderness, or inability to visualise the tympanic membrane.

Action: arrange same-day GP or urgent assessment if the exam cannot be completed safely in the current setting.

Otoscopy findings: canal vs tympanic membrane

Why it matters: canal inflammation suggests otitis externa; a bulging red tympanic membrane suggests otitis media.

Positive finding: tragal tenderness, swollen canal, or debris supports otitis externa; bulging membrane supports otitis media.

Action: treat supportively when mild and uncomplicated, or refer if severe, uncertain, or complicated.

Head, neck, and cranial nerve check

Why it matters: mastoid tenderness, facial weakness, or scalp tenderness changes the urgency completely.

Positive finding: mastoid swelling, facial asymmetry, or temporal artery tenderness requires urgent escalation.

Action: refer the patient rather than offering routine self-care advice.

Red flag synthesis and escalation triggers
  • Urgent / same-day: mastoid swelling, facial nerve palsy, sudden sensorineural hearing loss, severe systemic illness, or severe pain in a diabetic or immunocompromised patient.
  • Urgent / same-day: suspected necrotising otitis externa, perichondritis, or foreign body including a battery.
  • Prompt GP review: persistent unilateral pain, older age with scalp tenderness or jaw claudication, or normal ear exam with ongoing pain.
Investigations and decision points
Otoscopy

the key decision point when available.

Temperature and general appearance

help identify systemic illness.

Consider examination of teeth, TMJ, throat, and neck

if otoscopy is normal.

Low threshold for specialist assessment

if the diagnosis is uncertain or red flags are present.

Evidence and guideline pearls
Self-limiting illness still needs analgesia

Acute otitis media is often self-limiting and analgesia is the main treatment.

Antibiotics should not be treated as routine for

Antibiotics should not be treated as routine for uncomplicated AOM.

Topical treatment is first-line

Otitis externa is usually managed with topical treatment when it is uncomplicated and the eardrum is intact or the product is appropriate for uncertain tympanic membrane status.

Perforation changes drop choice

Quinolone ear drops are preferred when tympanic membrane perforation cannot be excluded.

Diagnostic timeout and common pitfalls
  • Do not assume all ear pain is otitis media.
  • Do not miss referred dental or TMJ pain when otoscopy is normal.
  • Do not keep treating severe ear pain as simple OE if the patient is diabetic or systemically unwell.
  • Do not miss persistent unilateral pain in an older adult.
Management, follow-up, and safety-netting
Uncomplicated AOM

provide reassurance, regular analgesia, and clear expectations about spontaneous improvement.

Uncomplicated OE

keep the ear dry, use topical therapy when appropriate, and avoid cotton buds or swimming.

Safety-netting

seek urgent review if pain worsens, fever or confusion develops, discharge persists, facial weakness appears, or mastoid swelling develops.

Special populations and role-aware notes
  • Children: AOM is common; focus on pain control and safety netting.
  • Older adults: persistent unilateral pain deserves a lower threshold for investigation.
  • Diabetes / immunosuppression: necrotising otitis externa must be considered early.
  • Community pharmacy: triage, analgesia advice, ear-care counselling, and referral when red flags or diagnostic uncertainty are present.
  • Primary care: confirm the diagnosis, examine the ear, and treat or refer according to severity and findings.
Illustrative cases
  • Case 1: child with cold symptoms, fever, and bulging red tympanic membrane. Treat as acute otitis media with analgesia and safety netting.
  • Case 2: swimmer with pain on tragus pressure and swollen canal. Treat as otitis externa if uncomplicated and the eardrum status is suitable for topical therapy.
  • Case 3: older smoker with persistent unilateral earache and normal otoscopy. Do not treat as routine earache; refer for urgent assessment of referred pain or malignancy.
