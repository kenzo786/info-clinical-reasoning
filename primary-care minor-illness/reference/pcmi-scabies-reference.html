Scabies in Primary Care Minor Illness
Context: Pruritic Rash and Contact Infestation First-Contact Assessment
Overview and key guidance

Scabies is a common pruritic skin infestation caused by Sarcoptes scabiei var. hominis. The consultation needs to recognise the typical story of nocturnal itch, contact spread, and classic distribution, while also excluding crusted scabies, secondary infection, and non-scabies itchy dermatoses.

Most uncomplicated cases can be managed in primary care if the diagnosis is sound and the patient can follow treatment instructions carefully. The most important practical step is to treat the patient and all close contacts together, because missing contacts is a common reason for apparent treatment failure.

Pathophysiology and why this matters
  • Mite infestation: the female mite burrows in the stratum corneum and lays eggs.
  • Symptoms are immunological: itch is a delayed type IV hypersensitivity reaction to mite proteins, eggs, and faeces.
  • Incubation matters: symptoms may take weeks to appear in a first infestation, but recur much faster with reinfestation.
  • Why it matters: the itch can continue after treatment even when the mites are gone, so counselling must separate normal post-treatment itch from treatment failure.
Differential diagnosis framework
Common and likely
  • Scabies: nocturnal itch, classic distribution, itchy contacts.
  • Eczema: can itch intensely but usually lacks contact spread.
  • Insect bites: often more exposed-site and less household clustering.
Often confused with scabies
  • Atopic or pompholyx eczema
  • Dermatitis herpetiformis
  • Folliculitis / urticaria
Must not miss
  • Crusted scabies: hyperinfestation, often in elderly or immunosuppressed patients.
  • Secondary bacterial infection: cellulitis, impetiginisation, fever, or widespread pain.
  • Outbreak setting: care home or institutional spread needs coordinated action.
Focused history taking
Itch pattern: severe itch, worse at night, sleep disturbance

Why it matters: nocturnal itch is one of the strongest clues for scabies.

Positive answer: night-time worsening and disrupted sleep make scabies more likely.

Action: move toward a scabies pathway rather than treating as nonspecific itch.

Contact history: household members, sexual partners, close skin-to-skin contacts, outbreaks

Why it matters: scabies is spread by close contact, so itchy contacts are highly informative.

Positive answer: someone else in the household itching strongly supports the diagnosis.

Action: plan simultaneous treatment for all close contacts if scabies is likely.

Distribution and lesions: finger webs, wrists, axillae, waistline, genitalia, excoriations, burrows

Why it matters: the distribution is often more diagnostic than the itch alone.

Positive answer: burrows or classic site involvement makes scabies much more likely.

Action: confirm on exam where possible and begin treatment planning.

Risk context and prior treatment: immunosuppression, age, care home exposure, previous scabicide use, application technique

Why it matters: crusted scabies, treatment failure, and reinfestation are often explained by risk context or incorrect use.

Positive answer: steroid treatment, chemotherapy, or care home exposure lowers the threshold for referral.

Action: do not simply repeat the same treatment without checking how it was used and whether contacts were treated.

Focused examination strategy
When examination is needed

Why it matters: many cases are diagnosed clinically, but exam helps confirm the distribution and look for red flags.

Positive trigger: diagnostic uncertainty, severe itch, contact spread, or concern about crusted scabies or infection.

Action: inspect the skin carefully before deciding on management.

Inspect classic sites: finger webs, wrists, elbows, axillae, waistline, buttocks, genitalia

Why it matters: typical site involvement strongly supports scabies.

Positive finding: papules, excoriations, or burrows in these sites make scabies more likely.

Action: treat as scabies unless another diagnosis is more convincing.

Look for burden or complications: crusting, thick scale, secondary infection, widespread excoriation

Why it matters: crusted scabies and infection need a lower threshold for urgent review.

Positive finding: thick crusts, widespread plaques, or hot painful skin suggest escalation.

Action: refer urgently if crusted scabies or infection is suspected.

Use simple diagnostic aids when appropriate: dermoscopy or burrow ink test

Why it matters: these aids can support diagnosis when burrows are not obvious.

Positive finding: visualising a burrow or mite increases diagnostic confidence.

Action: use the result to support treatment and avoid uncertainty-driven delay.

Red flag synthesis and escalation triggers
  • Urgent same-day: crusted scabies, widespread crusting or warty scale, or suspected outbreak in a care setting.
  • Urgent review: secondary bacterial infection, fever, pain, pus, or spreading redness.
  • Prompt referral: diagnostic uncertainty or failure after correctly applied treatment to all contacts.
  • Lower threshold: immunosuppression, frailty, or inability to manage treatment correctly at home.

Diagnostic timeout and common pitfalls
  • Do not miss scabies because the rash looks like eczema.
  • Do not forget to ask about itchy contacts.
  • Do not treat contacts separately on different days.
  • Do not mistake post-treatment itch for treatment failure without checking for new burrows.
Management, follow-up, and safety-netting
Core management

treat the patient and all close contacts at the same time, using permethrin first line and malathion if needed.

Environmental measures

hot wash bedding, towels, and clothes used recently, and bag non-washable items.

Counselling

explain correct application, timing of the repeat dose, and the possibility of post-treatment itch.

Safety-netting

seek review if new burrows appear, itch is not improving over time, or infection develops.

Special populations and role-aware notes
  • Children: check age-appropriate licensing and product guidance.
  • Older adults: think crusted scabies early and ask for help with application.
  • Pregnancy / breastfeeding: permethrin is generally preferred first line.
  • Role-aware note: uncomplicated cases can be managed in pharmacy, but crusting, immunosuppression, outbreaks, or failed treatment need GP / specialist care.
