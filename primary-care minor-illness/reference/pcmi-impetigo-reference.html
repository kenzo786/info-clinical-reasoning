Impetigo in Primary Care Minor Illness
Context: Superficial Skin Infection First-Contact Assessment
Overview and key guidance

Impetigo is a common, highly contagious superficial bacterial skin infection. In first-contact primary care, the immediate task is to decide whether the presentation is localised non-bullous disease that can be managed conservatively, or whether it is bullous, widespread, or complicated enough to need GP review or urgent escalation.

Non-bullous impetigo typically causes honey-coloured crusts, most often around the nose and mouth. Bullous impetigo is less common, but it is more concerning because it is more likely to need oral antibiotic treatment and can be associated with more severe disease in infants.

Pathophysiology and why this matters
  • Cause: usually Staphylococcus aureus, sometimes Streptococcus pyogenes.
  • Non-bullous disease: superficial infection causes vesicles that rupture and leave crusting.
  • Bullous disease: toxin-producing staphylococci cause flaccid blisters.
  • Why it matters: the type and extent of the rash determines whether the patient can stay in primary care or needs oral treatment or urgent review.
Differential diagnosis framework
Common and likely
  • Non-bullous impetigo: honey-coloured crusted lesions, often around the nose or mouth.
  • Bullous impetigo: flaccid blisters and superficial erosions, often in younger children.
  • Infected eczema / impetiginised dermatitis: underlying eczema with crusting, oozing, and secondary bacterial infection.
Often confused with impetigo
  • Herpes simplex: grouped vesicles and possible prodrome.
  • Tinea: annular lesion with scale and central clearing.
  • Contact dermatitis: exposure-linked rash with itch and inflammation.
Must not miss
  • Spreading cellulitis: progressive erythema, warmth, swelling, or systemic symptoms beyond a localized lesion.
  • Systemic illness / sepsis: fever with tachycardia, lethargy, or rapidly worsening widespread disease.
  • Staphylococcal scalded skin syndrome: widespread tender erythema or skin peeling, especially in infants.
  • Periorbital infection: consider when facial lesions are near the eye or swelling extends beyond the skin lesion.
Focused history taking
Lesion evolution: how did it start and spread?

Why it matters: The time course helps distinguish impetigo from other vesicular or eczematous rashes.

Positive answer: Starting as small spots or blisters that rupture into crusts supports impetigo; chronic itchy eczema points elsewhere.

Action: Decide whether the picture is localised, widespread, or uncertain.

Distribution and contacts: where are the lesions and is anyone else affected?

Why it matters: The face and exposed areas are common sites, and spread within households or nurseries is common.

Positive answer: Multiple family members, school contacts, or lesions around the nose and mouth support impetigo as a contagious diagnosis.

Action: Reinforce hygiene advice and exclusion guidance, and consider broader treatment if widespread.

Red-flag screen: fever, malaise, spreading redness, facial swelling, infant age, immunosuppression

Why it matters: These features raise the risk of cellulitis, systemic illness, or severe toxin-mediated disease.

Positive answer: Fever, rapidly expanding erythema, or a very young/unwell child is not routine community management.

Action: Escalate to GP or urgent assessment rather than offering only OTC treatment.

Skin background and treatment history: eczema, scratching, previous impetigo, antibiotics tried, allergies

Why it matters: Impetigo often develops on broken skin, and prior treatment failure may imply resistant infection or wrong diagnosis.

Positive answer: Background eczema or repeated episodes increase the need to treat the underlying skin barrier as well as the infection.

Action: Tailor treatment and avoid repeated ineffective OTC-only management.

Focused examination strategy
When examination is needed

Why it matters: Visual diagnosis is central, but the child or adult should be examined if the diagnosis is uncertain or the rash is extensive.

Positive trigger: Bullae, spreading erythema, facial involvement, or lesions around an eczematous background.

Action: Confirm the morphology and decide whether topical or oral treatment is appropriate.

Rash inspection: crusts, bullae, surrounding erythema, and distribution

Why it matters: Honey-coloured crusts point toward non-bullous impetigo; flaccid blisters point toward bullous disease.

Positive finding: Localised crusted lesions support community treatment; widespread bullae suggest a need for oral antibiotics and review.

Action: Match the extent of the rash to the treatment pathway.

Check for complications: cellulitis, periorbital involvement, systemic upset, dehydration in younger children

Why it matters: Complications change the urgency of care.

Positive finding: Warmth, tenderness, swelling, or a sick appearance makes simple impetigo less likely.

Action: Escalate for GP or urgent hospital assessment as needed.

Red flag synthesis and escalation triggers
  • Urgent / same-day: systemic illness, spreading cellulitis, facial or periorbital infection, or a very unwell infant.
  • Urgent / same-day: widespread bullous impetigo, especially if there is concern for staphylococcal scalded skin syndrome.
  • Routine but prompt GP review: localised impetigo that is not improving, recurrent disease, or diagnostic uncertainty.

Diagnostic timeout and common pitfalls
  • Do not call every crusted rash impetigo.
  • Do not miss infected eczema, herpes, or tinea when the morphology is atypical.
  • Do not treat widespread bullous disease as a routine OTC problem.
  • Do not forget to think about cellulitis or systemic illness when the child looks unwell.
Management, follow-up, and safety-netting
Uncomplicated localised disease

hygiene advice, keep lesions clean, avoid scratching, and use a topical antiseptic if suitable.

Widespread or bullous disease

arrange GP review for oral treatment rather than persisting with self-care alone.

Safety-netting

seek urgent review if redness spreads, fever develops, the child becomes unwell, or the rash worsens despite treatment.

Special populations and role-aware notes
  • Infants: lower threshold for urgent review, especially with bullous disease or systemic symptoms.
  • Children: hygiene, school exclusion advice, and spread prevention matter as much as the medicine choice.
  • Immunosuppression or eczema: lower the threshold for GP review because complications are more likely.
Illustrative cases
  • Case 1: few crusted lesions around the nose, otherwise well: localised non-bullous impetigo.
  • Case 2: widespread crusts and new fever: GP review rather than simple OTC care.
  • Case 3: infant with flaccid blisters and a sick appearance: urgent assessment for bullous impetigo or SSSS.
