Fever in Primary Care
Context: First-contact assessment of fever in adults and children
Overview and key guidance

Fever is a symptom, not a diagnosis. In primary care the first task is to decide whether the patient has a likely self-limiting viral illness that can be managed safely with advice and symptom relief, or whether the pattern suggests sepsis, meningitis, pneumonia, pyelonephritis, Kawasaki disease, or another serious cause that needs urgent escalation.

UK guidance is conservative: treat distress, not the number on the thermometer, and use age-appropriate risk tools when the patient is a child. In under-5s, the NICE traffic light system helps structure the consultation; in adults, physiological observations and NEWS2 can help identify deterioration. A fever without a source should always trigger a deliberate search for a focus and red flags rather than a quick assumption of "viral".

Pathophysiology and why this matters
  • Set-point change: fever is a regulated rise in the hypothalamic temperature set-point, not uncontrolled overheating.
  • Pyrogens: infection or inflammation releases mediators that raise prostaglandin activity and reset the set-point upwards.
  • Clinical effect: the patient feels cold, shivers, and vasoconstricts until the new set-point is reached; later sweating occurs when the set-point falls.
  • Why it matters: this explains why antipyretics improve comfort but do not "cure" the cause, and why the main job is to find the source and identify any dangerous illness early.
Differential diagnosis framework
Common and likely
  • Viral upper respiratory infection: fever with coryza, sore throat, or cough and a reassuring overall picture.
  • Viral gastroenteritis: vomiting and diarrhoea with sick contacts and spontaneous improvement.
  • Self-limiting febrile illness without a clear focus: brief fever in an otherwise stable patient with a reassuring examination.
  • Otitis media, tonsillitis, or other local infection: fever with a focal ENT or chest source on history or examination.
Likely when there are localising clues
  • Urinary tract infection or pyelonephritis: urinary symptoms, loin pain, vomiting, rigors.
  • [sc name="jitl-term-link" Q="Pneumonia assessment and management in primary care" text="Pneumonia"][/sc]: cough, breathlessness, chest pain, tachypnoea, low oxygen saturation.
  • Skin and soft tissue infection: focal redness, swelling, warmth, tenderness.
  • Inflammatory illness: persistent fever with joint, rash, or mucosal features.
Must not miss
  • [sc name="jitl-term-link" Q="Sepsis recognition and same-day management" text="Sepsis"][/sc]: systemic toxicity, tachycardia, lethargy, or rapidly worsening illness out of keeping with a simple local infection.
  • [sc name="jitl-term-link" Q="Meningitis recognition and emergency assessment" text="Meningitis"][/sc] or encephalitis: fever, neck stiffness, photophobia, confusion, or reduced consciousness rather than a routine febrile illness.
  • Non-blanching rash / meningococcal disease: fever with petechiae, purpura, or rapidly progressive systemic illness.
  • Kawasaki disease in children: prolonged fever with rash, conjunctivitis, mucosal change, or extremity findings.
  • Malaria or other travel-related infection: fever after travel, especially with rigors, headache, or GI symptoms.
Focused history taking
Age and vulnerability: infant, young child, older adult, pregnancy, immunocompromise

Why it matters: age and host factors change the risk threshold and the likely serious diagnoses.

Positive answer: fever in a baby under 3 months, an immunocompromised adult, or an older adult who seems "off" should lower the threshold for urgent review.

Action: treat the case as higher risk until proven otherwise, not as routine self-care.

Duration and pattern: how long, continuous or intermittent, improving or worsening, recurrent

Why it matters: persistent or recurrent fever raises the chance of bacterial infection, inflammatory disease, or a missed focus.

Positive answer: fever lasting more than 5 days in a child, or a fever that is worsening rather than settling, should prompt reconsideration of the diagnosis.

Action: look for a source, consider blood or urine testing when indicated, and escalate if the overall picture is not reassuring.

Localising symptoms: cough, sore throat, ear pain, urinary symptoms, vomiting, diarrhoea, abdominal pain, rash, headache, neck stiffness

Why it matters: fever with a focus is easier to triage than fever without a focus.

Positive answer: cough and breathlessness point toward chest infection; urinary symptoms point toward UTI or pyelonephritis; headache and neck stiffness raise concern for meningitis; rash or mucosal changes may indicate meningococcal disease or Kawasaki disease.

Action: use the focus to choose the next exam target and whether the patient needs same-day referral.

Severity and systemic upset: hydration, urine output, lethargy, confusion, rigors, breathing difficulty, reduced intake

Why it matters: systemically unwell patients can deteriorate quickly even if the temperature itself is modest.

Positive answer: poor intake, reduced urine output, confusion, or severe rigors increases concern for dehydration or sepsis.

Action: move away from routine advice and towards urgent assessment or hospital referral.

Exposure and medicines: sick contacts, travel, bites, animal exposure, immunosuppressants, recent antibiotics, [sc name="jitl-drug-link" drug="Paracetamol" text="paracetamol"][/sc]/[sc name="jitl-drug-link" drug="Ibuprofen" text="ibuprofen"][/sc] use

Why it matters: exposure history can reveal travel-related infection or medication-related risk, and medicines can hide complications or overdose.

Positive answer: recent travel, malaria exposure, or immunosuppressant use should not be treated as routine fever; repeated antipyretic use can also mask progression.

Action: broaden the differential, ask about prevention and timing, and lower the threshold for urgent review when the risk profile is unclear.

Focused examination strategy
Overall appearance and observations: alertness, work of breathing, pulse, respiratory rate, oxygen saturation, temperature

Why it matters: the general picture is often more informative than the number on the thermometer.

Positive finding: tachycardia, tachypnoea, low oxygen saturation, mottled skin, or an obviously toxic appearance suggests severe illness or sepsis.

Action: escalate urgently and do not anchor on a presumed viral illness.

Hydration and circulation: capillary refill, mucous membranes, tears, urine output, skin turgor

Why it matters: dehydration can coexist with infection and may itself drive clinical deterioration.

Positive finding: dry mucosa, prolonged capillary refill, poor tears, or low urine output raises the need for urgent fluid assessment.

Action: advise oral rehydration if safe, but refer if hydration is poor or the child or adult looks significantly unwell.

Targeted systems exam: chest, ears/throat, abdomen, urine symptoms, skin, neurology

Why it matters: a fever without source needs a deliberate search for the focus.

Positive finding: focal chest crepitations, otitis media, tonsillar exudate, abdominal tenderness, flank pain, rash, neck stiffness, or photophobia each shifts the differential.

Action: use the result to guide testing, treatment, or same-day escalation rather than giving generic reassurance.

Child-specific risk assessment: NICE traffic light features in under-5s

Why it matters: the traffic light system helps separate low-risk from higher-risk febrile children.

Positive finding: red or multiple amber features increase the likelihood of serious illness.

Action: admit, urgently refer, or arrange senior review depending on the overall risk picture.

Red flag synthesis and escalation triggers
  • Immediate emergency referral: non-blanching rash, meningism, confusion, seizure, severe respiratory distress, shock, or a very unwell child or adult.
  • Same-day urgent review: fever in a baby under 3 months, suspected sepsis, immunocompromise, suspected pneumonia with hypoxia, pyelonephritis, or a child with red traffic light features.
  • Prompt GP or paediatric review: persistent fever beyond 5 days in a child, recurrent fever, or fever with a concerning focal symptom even if the patient is currently stable.
Investigations and decision points
No routine tests

a straightforward viral febrile illness in a well patient generally needs no testing in pharmacy or uncomplicated primary care triage.

Urinalysis or urine culture

if urinary symptoms, abdominal pain, or unexplained fever suggest UTI or pyelonephritis.

Respiratory assessment

oxygen saturation, chest exam, and possible urgent chest imaging or same-day review if lower respiratory tract infection is suspected.

Bloods / NEWS2 / senior review

if the patient is systemically unwell, deteriorating, or has suspected sepsis.

Travel-related tests

consider malaria or other imported infection if there is fever after travel to an endemic area.

Evidence and guideline pearls
NICE guidance for young children emphasises

NICE guidance for young children emphasises looking for serious illness rather than treating fever itself as the problem.

Antipyretics are for comfort

Antipyretics are for comfort and distress, not for normalising the temperature.

Do not alternate antipyretics routinely

Routine alternating [sc name="jitl-drug-link" drug="Paracetamol" text="paracetamol"][/sc] and [sc name="jitl-drug-link" drug="Ibuprofen" text="ibuprofen"][/sc] is not recommended for ordinary fever management.

Antibiotics do not treat fever itself

Antibiotics do not treat fever itself; they are only appropriate when there is a bacterial source or a confirmed indication.

Diagnostic timeout and common pitfalls
  • Do not equate fever with infection severity. A modest fever can coexist with serious illness, and a high fever can be viral.
  • Do not anchor on "viral" before checking for sepsis or meningitis red flags.
  • Do not miss urinary, chest, or CNS sources when the symptom is non-specific.
  • Do not overuse antipyretics as a substitute for reassessment if the patient is worsening.
Management, follow-up, and safety-netting
Well patient with likely viral illness

give hydration advice, rest, and [sc name="jitl-drug-link" drug="Paracetamol" text="paracetamol"][/sc] or [sc name="jitl-drug-link" drug="Ibuprofen" text="ibuprofen"][/sc] for distress if not contraindicated.

Possible bacterial or focal infection

arrange appropriate same-day review, urine testing, chest assessment, or other investigations based on the source.

Safety-netting

seek urgent help for breathing difficulty, non-blanching rash, confusion, seizure, worsening drowsiness, dehydration, persistent vomiting, or a child who becomes more unwell.

Special populations and role-aware notes
  • Infants under 3 months: fever is high risk and needs urgent same-day assessment.
  • Older adults: severe illness can present without a dramatic fever, so a low threshold for review is appropriate.
  • Pregnancy: fever needs a broader differential, including urinary and respiratory infection.
  • Immunocompromised patients: low threshold for urgent review because infection may progress rapidly and the temperature response can be atypical.
  • Role-aware note: community pharmacy can support self-care advice only when the patient is clearly low risk and well enough for home management; any uncertainty, red flag, or high-risk group should be escalated.
Illustrative cases
  • Case 1: a well 7-year-old with fever, coryza, and cough, normal breathing, and no red flags can usually be managed with fluids, antipyretics for distress, and safety-netting.
  • Case 2: a 2-year-old with fever, dysuria, and vomiting should be assessed for UTI or pyelonephritis rather than treated as a generic viral illness.
  • Case 3: fever, headache, neck stiffness, and a non-blanching rash is a medical emergency and needs immediate escalation.
  • Case 4: a child with fever for more than 5 days, conjunctivitis, rash, and mucosal changes should raise concern for Kawasaki disease.
