Contact Dermatitis in Primary Care Minor Illness
Context: Localised Eczematous Rash First-Contact Assessment
Overview and key guidance

Contact dermatitis is a common localised eczematous rash caused by either direct irritation or an allergic delayed hypersensitivity reaction. In first-contact primary care, the immediate task is to decide whether this is a straightforward irritant or allergic dermatitis that can be managed with trigger avoidance, barrier repair, and topical anti-inflammatory treatment, or whether the pattern suggests infection, scabies, psoriasis, tinea, or another diagnosis.

Most cases are suitable for conservative management in primary care if the rash is localised, non-infected, and the trigger is plausible. NICE-style guidance supports emollients and short courses of topical corticosteroids when appropriate, but the safest consultation is the one that actively checks for infection, diagnostic uncertainty, and high-risk sites before recommending treatment.

Pathophysiology and why this matters
  • Irritant dermatitis: repeated exposure to water, detergents, solvents, or other irritants damages the skin barrier directly.
  • Allergic dermatitis: a delayed type IV hypersensitivity reaction develops after sensitisation to an allergen such as nickel, fragrances, or preservatives.
  • Barrier failure: dryness and cracking make the skin more vulnerable to further irritation and secondary infection.
  • Why it matters: the consultation should not stop at treating the rash; identifying the trigger is what prevents recurrence.
Differential diagnosis framework
Common and likely
  • Irritant contact dermatitis: often on hands with wet work or frequent washing.
  • Allergic contact dermatitis: delayed rash after a trigger such as jewellery, cosmetics, or plants.
  • Atopic eczema: especially if there is a background of dry, relapsing eczema or atopy.
Often confused with dermatitis
  • Psoriasis: well-demarcated plaques with thicker scale.
  • Tinea: annular rash with an active scaly edge.
  • Scabies: intense itch, often nocturnal, with itchy contacts.
Must not miss
  • [sc name="jitl-term-link" Q="Cellulitis diagnosis and management" text="Cellulitis"][/sc]: hot, painful, spreading erythema with systemic illness.
  • Eczema herpeticum: painful vesicular eruption with punched-out erosions.
  • Cutaneous malignancy or bullous disease: persistent, atypical, or treatment-resistant rash.
Focused history taking
Symptoms and distribution: itch, soreness, burning, site, shape, and whether it matches contact

Why it matters: contact dermatitis usually maps to an exposure site, such as the hands, wrists, earlobes, face, or a clothing line.

Positive answer: a clear contact pattern supports irritant or allergic dermatitis; widespread or atypical disease makes the diagnosis less secure.

Action: if the rash is localised and the exposure pattern is convincing, conservative primary-care management is reasonable.

Trigger history: new soaps, detergents, gloves, cosmetics, jewellery, plants, hobbies, occupational wet work

Why it matters: identifying the trigger is the most important long-term treatment.

Positive answer: frequent handwashing, cleaning work, nickel jewellery, or a new cosmetic makes the cause more likely and helps separate irritant from allergic disease.

Action: advise avoidance or substitution of the trigger and document likely culprit(s) for future prevention.

Complication screen: pain, weeping, crusting, fever, rapid spread, blisters, facial swelling

Why it matters: these features suggest infection, eczema herpeticum, or a severe reaction rather than uncomplicated dermatitis.

Positive answer: pain, pus, fever, or rapidly worsening redness makes secondary infection more likely; painful blisters raise concern for herpes-related disease.

Action: escalate rather than treating as routine self-care.

Background and treatment history: atopy, occupation, prior treatment, pregnancy, comorbidity, immunosuppression

Why it matters: background eczema and fragile skin increase susceptibility, while immunosuppression changes the safety threshold.

Positive answer: repeated self-treatment failure, immunosuppression, or pregnancy complexity lowers the threshold for review or referral.

Action: tailor the treatment plan and avoid simply repeating OTC products without reassessment.

Focused examination strategy
When examination is needed

Why it matters: simple localised dermatitis may be managed from history alone, but infection or diagnostic uncertainty needs examination.

Positive trigger: uncertain diagnosis, extensive rash, facial involvement, or any sign of infection or blistering.

Action: inspect the rash before deciding on treatment or referral.

Inspect morphology: erythema, scaling, vesicles, fissures, lichenification, crusting

Why it matters: the rash pattern helps separate dermatitis from psoriasis, tinea, scabies, and infection.

Positive finding: dryness, fissuring, and contact-pattern erythema support dermatitis; golden crusts or pus suggest infection.

Action: treat simple dermatitis, but seek another pathway if the appearance is atypical.

Check site and spread: hands, wrists, face, eyelids, neck, feet, flexures, and any rapidly spreading area

Why it matters: the body site influences potency choice and whether the rash is likely to be irritant, allergic, or something else.

Positive finding: hand dermatitis fits wet work; eyelid or facial involvement needs more caution; widespread spread pushes toward referral.

Action: adapt the treatment and avoid over-treating thin or sensitive skin.

Look for infection or other disease: warmth, tenderness, discharge, burrows, asymmetric plaques

Why it matters: infection and scabies are common mimics and common reasons for treatment failure.

Positive finding: warmth and pain suggest cellulitis; burrows and itchy contacts suggest scabies.

Action: refer or treat the alternative diagnosis rather than giving routine dermatitis treatment alone.

Red flag synthesis and escalation triggers
  • Urgent same-day: fever, spreading erythema, marked pain, pus, facial swelling, or widespread blistering.
  • Same-day dermatology / emergency concern: eczema herpeticum, severe extensive reaction, or diagnostic concern for bullous disease or malignancy.
  • Prompt GP review: persistent rash despite correct treatment, recurrent flare, or occupational dermatitis affecting work.
  • Do not manage as routine OTC: contact urticaria with breathlessness, or any suggestion of anaphylaxis.

Diagnostic timeout and common pitfalls
  • Do not call every itchy rash “contact dermatitis”.
  • Do not miss infection because the rash is itchy rather than painful.
  • Do not treat tinea or scabies with steroid alone.
  • Do not miss occupational triggers when the rash keeps recurring.
Management, follow-up, and safety-netting
Core management

identify and avoid the trigger, then restore the skin barrier with emollients and reduce inflammation if needed with a short course of topical corticosteroid.

Counselling

emollients are the foundation; hydrocortisone is for inflamed skin only and should not be used on infected or broken skin.

Follow-up

review if the rash is not improving after around 1 to 2 weeks of correct treatment or if it comes back quickly after stopping treatment.

Safety-netting

urgent review for fever, spreading redness, pus, widespread blisters, or facial swelling.

Special populations and role-aware notes
  • Children: use lower-potency treatment cautiously and consider eczema or infection early.
  • Older adults: fragile skin needs gentler care and a lower threshold for infection review.
  • Pregnancy and breastfeeding: emollients are first line; mild topical steroids are usually acceptable when used briefly and appropriately.
  • Role-aware note: pharmacy is suitable for localised uncomplicated cases, but diagnostic uncertainty, infection, and occupational impairment should move the patient into GP or dermatology assessment.
