Constipation in Primary Care Minor Illness
Context: Reduced Bowel Frequency and Difficult Stool Passage First-Contact Assessment
Overview and key guidance

Constipation is a symptom rather than a diagnosis, and in primary care the job is to decide whether this is likely to be functional constipation, drug-related constipation, faecal impaction, or another condition that needs referral. Typical features include fewer bowel movements, hard or lumpy stool, straining, bloating, and a sense of incomplete evacuation.

Most uncomplicated cases can be managed conservatively with lifestyle advice and stepwise laxatives. The key safety task is to exclude red flags such as bleeding, weight loss, new change in bowel habit in an older adult, obstruction, or palpable mass before treating it as routine constipation.

Pathophysiology and why this matters
  • Slow transit: stool moves too slowly through the colon, so too much water is absorbed and stools become hard.
  • Defecatory disorder: the pelvic floor or anal sphincter does not coordinate properly, making evacuation difficult.
  • Secondary causes: medicines, immobility, low fibre intake, dehydration, hypothyroidism, neurological disease, and faecal loading can all contribute.
  • Why it matters: asking about cause early avoids over-treating a medication problem or missing something serious.
Differential diagnosis framework
Common and likely
  • Functional constipation: hard infrequent stools without red flags or a clear secondary cause.
  • Drug-induced constipation: temporal link with opioids, anticholinergics, iron, or other constipating medicines.
  • IBS-C: chronic constipation with abdominal pain related to defecation and a fluctuating pattern.
Often confused with constipation
  • Faecal impaction: overflow diarrhoea, confusion, rectal discomfort.
  • Hypothyroidism: constipation with fatigue, weight gain, dry skin.
  • Medication side effect: especially opioids and anticholinergic drugs.
Must not miss
  • Colorectal cancer: bleeding, weight loss, anaemia, new change in bowel habit.
  • Bowel obstruction: pain, vomiting, distension, absolute constipation.
  • Rectal or abdominal mass: needs urgent assessment.
Focused history taking
Symptom pattern: frequency, straining, hard stools, bloating, incomplete evacuation

Why it matters: these features help separate simple constipation from IBS-C, impaction, or another bowel disorder.

Positive answer: hard, infrequent stools with straining support constipation; pain with bowel habit change suggests IBS-C.

Action: use the pattern to decide whether conservative treatment is reasonable or whether a different pathway is needed.

Red flag screen: bleeding, weight loss, anaemia, vomiting, pain, distension, older age with new change

Why it matters: red flags are the main reason constipation should not be treated as routine self-care.

Positive answer: any bleeding, weight loss, or new persistent change in bowel habit raises concern for malignancy.

Action: refer rather than simply supplying laxatives.

Medication history: opioids, anticholinergics, iron, calcium channel blockers, antidepressants

Why it matters: medicines are a common and reversible cause of constipation.

Positive answer: new or dose-increased opioids or anticholinergic drugs make a drug cause more likely.

Action: advise review of the causative medicine with the prescriber and plan laxative support if appropriate.

Functional context: fibre, fluids, mobility, toileting routine, pregnancy, neurological disease

Why it matters: lifestyle factors and comorbidity often explain ongoing symptoms and guide treatment choice.

Positive answer: low fibre, poor hydration, immobility, or previous impaction lowers the threshold for treatment and review.

Action: start with practical self-care advice while keeping the plan simple and achievable.

Focused examination strategy
When examination is needed

Why it matters: many uncomplicated cases do not need examination, but red flags or suspected impaction do.

Positive trigger: abdominal pain, vomiting, distension, suspected impaction, or concern about malignancy.

Action: examine rather than treating blindly as simple constipation.

Abdominal examination: distension, tenderness, mass, bowel sounds

Why it matters: helps identify obstruction, severe faecal loading, or another abdominal cause.

Positive finding: distension or a mass is a referral trigger; generalised tenderness raises concern for acute pathology.

Action: escalate if the abdomen is concerning rather than assuming uncomplicated constipation.

Digital rectal examination when indicated: impaction, tone, mass, rectal loading

Why it matters: DRE is important if impaction or rectal pathology is suspected.

Positive finding: hard stool in the rectum supports impaction and changes management.

Action: do not keep escalating oral laxatives alone if impaction is present.

General observation: pallor, weight loss, frailty, confusion, dehydration

Why it matters: the overall look of the patient can reveal malignancy, dehydration, or frailty-related impaction.

Positive finding: cachexia or pallor supports urgent investigation.

Action: move to referral if the patient looks unwell or there is significant weight loss.

Red flag synthesis and escalation triggers
  • Urgent same-day: severe abdominal pain, vomiting, distension, or suspected bowel obstruction.
  • Prompt GP / 2WW concern: rectal bleeding, weight loss, anaemia, new persistent change in bowel habit in an older adult, or a mass.
  • Prompt review: faecal impaction, overflow diarrhoea, confusion, or failure of sensible OTC treatment.
  • Do not treat as simple constipation: if the history is atypical or the patient appears systemically unwell.
Investigations and decision points
No routine test

typical uncomplicated constipation may be managed clinically.

Blood tests

if anaemia, hypothyroidism, metabolic disease, or another systemic cause is suspected.

DRE / imaging

if impaction or obstruction is suspected.

Urgent cancer pathway

if red flags suggest colorectal cancer.

Diagnostic timeout and common pitfalls
  • Do not ignore red flags because constipation is common.
  • Do not miss impaction in older or immobile patients.
  • Do not keep repeating stimulant laxatives without review.
  • Do not overlook a drug cause when symptoms started after a new medicine.
Management, follow-up, and safety-netting
Core management

start with diet, fluids, exercise, and toileting routine, then step through bulk-forming, osmotic, and short-term stimulant laxatives as needed.

Impaction

use a separate disimpaction plan rather than routine dose escalation.

Follow-up

review if symptoms do not settle after a sensible trial, or sooner if there is pain, vomiting, or bleeding.

Safety-netting

urgent help for bleeding, weight loss, severe pain, vomiting, or abdominal swelling.

Special populations and role-aware notes
  • Children: osmotic laxatives are often preferred and persistent symptoms deserve careful review.
  • Older adults: impaction and drug causes are common, and confusion may be a clue.
  • Pregnancy / breastfeeding: conservative measures first; bulk-forming and osmotic laxatives are usually acceptable.
  • Role-aware note: simple constipation can be managed in pharmacy, but red flags, impaction, or persistence need GP assessment.
Illustrative cases
  • Case 1: hard stools, bloating, low fibre intake: functional constipation.
  • Case 2: overflow diarrhoea and confusion: faecal impaction until proven otherwise.
  • Case 3: constipation with weight loss and bleeding: urgent colorectal cancer referral.
