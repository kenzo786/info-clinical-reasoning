Athlete's Foot in Primary Care
Context: Itchy Scaly Foot Rash First-Contact Assessment
Overview and key guidance

Athlete's foot, or tinea pedis, is a common superficial fungal infection of the feet. In first-contact primary care, the main task is to distinguish a straightforward fungal rash that can be managed with topical antifungal treatment and foot-care advice from bacterial infection, diabetic foot risk, inflammatory dermatoses, or a more extensive fungal pattern that may need oral treatment or review.

The typical interdigital pattern causes itch, maceration, scaling, and fissuring between the toes, especially between the fourth and fifth toes. Other patterns such as moccasin-type scaling across the soles or a vesicular presentation are still consistent with tinea pedis, but they need a more careful diagnostic and management decision.

Pathophysiology and why this matters
  • Cause: dermatophyte fungi such as Trichophyton rubrum infect keratinised skin.
  • Favouring environment: warm, moist, occlusive footwear and poor drying between the toes encourage fungal growth.
  • Barrier damage: maceration and fissuring increase discomfort and the risk of secondary bacterial infection.
  • Why it matters: a fungal rash may be simple, but missed cellulitis, diabetic foot breakdown, or a mimic such as eczema or psoriasis can lead to the wrong pathway.
Differential diagnosis framework
Common and likely
  • Interdigital tinea pedis
  • Moccasin-type tinea pedis
  • Vesicular tinea pedis
Often confused with athlete's foot
  • Contact dermatitis / eczema: often more inflamed or exposure-linked.
  • Psoriasis: well-demarcated plaques with more persistent scale.
  • Erythrasma: toe-web scaling with less itch and a different treatment pathway.
  • Pitted keratolysis: malodorous pits on the soles rather than classic fungal scale.
Must not miss
  • Cellulitis: hot, painful, spreading erythema with systemic upset.
  • Diabetic foot breakdown: ulceration, tissue loss, or infection.
  • Extensive onychomycosis or refractory fungal disease: may explain recurrence and change treatment.
Focused history taking
Symptom pattern and site: itch, soreness, burning, fissures, blisters, interdigital vs sole involvement

Why it matters: the pattern helps separate routine interdigital tinea from moccasin disease, vesicular disease, or a non-fungal mimic.

Positive answer: itchy toe-web maceration supports tinea; marked pain, rapid spread, or ulceration points away from simple fungal disease.

Action: decide whether the patient is suitable for topical treatment only or needs examination and referral.

Time course and previous treatment: first episode, recurrent disease, failed OTCs, adherence

Why it matters: repeated recurrence or poor response may reflect misdiagnosis, poor adherence, or nail involvement acting as a reservoir.

Positive answer: persistence despite correct topical use raises the threshold for rethinking the diagnosis or considering oral treatment.

Action: check how treatment was used and whether further review is needed.

Risk factors and context: occlusive footwear, sport, communal showers, hyperhidrosis, nail disease

Why it matters: a plausible fungal exposure pattern increases diagnostic confidence and shapes prevention advice.

Positive answer: trainers, boots, swimming-pool use, or sweaty feet all support tinea; fungal toenails support chronic recurrence.

Action: build footwear and hygiene counselling into the treatment plan.

Complication and comorbidity screen: diabetes, peripheral vascular disease, immunosuppression, spreading redness, fever, skin breakdown

Why it matters: these features turn a minor rash into a higher-risk foot problem.

Positive answer: diabetes with fissuring or ulceration, or any spreading painful erythema, lowers the threshold for urgent medical review.

Action: do not manage complicated or high-risk foot disease as simple self-care.

Focused examination strategy
Inspect the distribution: toe webs, soles, heels, sides of feet, bilateral vs unilateral pattern

Why it matters: interdigital and moccasin patterns are common fungal presentations, while very symmetrical inflammatory rashes may suggest a mimic.

Positive finding: macerated toe webs or diffuse sole scaling support tinea; atypical widespread inflammation reduces certainty.

Action: treat straightforward patterns and rethink unclear ones.

Look at morphology: scale, maceration, fissures, vesicles, crusting, tenderness, warmth

Why it matters: morphology separates fungal disease from eczema, psoriasis, and secondary infection.

Positive finding: dry scale and maceration support tinea; warmth, tenderness, pus, or rapidly spreading erythema suggest bacterial infection.

Action: escalate if the foot looks infected rather than simply fungal.

Inspect the nails and skin integrity: onychomycosis, breaks, ulcers, interdigital breakdown

Why it matters: nail involvement explains recurrence and ulceration changes the urgency, especially in diabetes.

Positive finding: thickened discoloured nails support fungal recurrence; ulcers or deep cracks increase infection risk.

Action: consider broader management or referral instead of simple topical advice.

Red flag synthesis and escalation triggers
  • Urgent / same-day: painful spreading erythema, fever, significant swelling, suspected cellulitis, or acute diabetic foot concern.
  • Prompt GP review: ulceration, immunocompromise, poorly controlled diabetes, extensive vesicular or moccasin disease, or diagnostic uncertainty.
  • Routine topical management: classic uncomplicated interdigital or limited plantar tinea with no red flags.
Investigations and decision points
  • No routine testing: needed for straightforward clinical diagnoses.
  • Mycology / skin scrapings: consider if the diagnosis is uncertain or the rash has not responded to correct treatment.
  • Check for onychomycosis: because concurrent nail disease often maintains recurrence.
  • Assess diabetic foot risk: if there is skin breakdown, neuropathy, or vascular disease.
Evidence and guideline pearls
  • Topical antifungals are first-line for uncomplicated tinea pedis.
  • Topical terbinafine often works with a shorter course than imidazoles, but licensed age ranges vary by product.
  • Moccasin-type or treatment-resistant disease is less likely to settle with simple topical therapy alone.
  • Do not use a plain topical steroid on suspected fungal infection.
Diagnostic timeout and common pitfalls
  • Do not call every scaly foot rash tinea.
  • Do not miss cellulitis because the patient started with itch.
  • Do not overlook nail disease when the rash keeps recurring.
  • Do not manage diabetic foot skin breakdown as a routine OTC problem.
Management, follow-up, and safety-netting

Uncomplicated tinea: combine foot hygiene with a suitable topical antifungal and enough treatment duration.

Counselling: wash and dry thoroughly, especially between the toes, change socks daily, avoid occlusive footwear where possible, and use communal-area footwear such as flip-flops.

Safety-netting: seek review if the rash becomes painful, hot, swollen, ulcerated, or fails to improve after correct treatment.

Special populations and role-aware notes
  • Children: athlete's foot is less common and product licences vary, so check the age range carefully.
  • Pregnancy / breastfeeding: topical imidazoles are usually the most straightforward option; avoid assuming oral therapy is appropriate.
  • Older adults: think about self-care ability, polypharmacy, and foot-risk comorbidity.
  • Diabetes / immunocompromise: use a lower threshold for medical review because infection and tissue damage matter more.
  • Role-aware note: pharmacy can manage classic uncomplicated cases, but extensive, recurrent, infected, or high-risk foot presentations need GP or specialist assessment.
Illustrative cases
  • Case 1: 22-year-old swimmer with itchy maceration between the fourth and fifth toes: uncomplicated interdigital tinea.
  • Case 2: 67-year-old with diabetes and deep sole fissures plus redness: higher-risk foot problem needing medical review.
  • Case 3: persistent diffuse sole scaling plus thickened toenails after multiple OTC courses: reconsider diagnosis and nail involvement.
