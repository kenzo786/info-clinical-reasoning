Oral Thrush in Adults in Primary Care
Context: White Oral Lesions First-Contact Assessment
Overview and key guidance

Oral thrush in adults is usually oral candidiasis caused by Candida overgrowth, but first-contact assessment must still distinguish uncomplicated thrush from leukoplakia, oral lichen planus, oral hairy leukoplakia, squamous cell carcinoma, and oesophageal spread. The key question is whether this is a simple, treatable mouth infection or a marker of immunosuppression, poor inhaler technique, denture-related disease, or a potentially malignant lesion.

UK primary care practice is conservative: use a clear oral examination, treat straightforward candidiasis, correct the predisposing factor, and refer promptly if the lesion is non-scrapable, the patient is systemically unwell, or there are features of immunocompromise or oesophageal involvement. The most important novice habit is to check whether the white patch wipes away and whether the patient has a reason to get thrush in the first place.

Pathophysiology and why this matters
  • Candida overgrowth: oral thrush reflects overgrowth of a commensal yeast rather than a classic invasive infection.
  • Predisposing factors: inhaled corticosteroids, recent antibiotics, dentures, poor oral hygiene, diabetes, and immunosuppression all make candidal overgrowth more likely.
  • Why it matters: identifying the underlying driver reduces recurrence and avoids repeated empiric treatment that misses a different diagnosis.
  • Clinical pattern: scrapable creamy-white plaques, soreness, altered taste, or a cottony mouth are typical; a fixed lesion is not.
Differential diagnosis framework
Common and likely
  • Oral candidiasis (thrush)
  • Food debris or coating
  • Denture stomatitis
Often confused with thrush
  • Leukoplakia: persistent non-scrapable white patch.
  • Oral lichen planus: bilateral white striae or erosive lesions.
  • Oral hairy leukoplakia: usually linked with immunosuppression.
Must not miss
  • Squamous cell carcinoma
  • Oesophageal candidiasis
  • Serious immunosuppression such as uncontrolled diabetes or undiagnosed HIV
Focused history taking
Lesion behaviour: can it be wiped off, how long has it been present, has it recurred?

Why it matters: scrapable plaques support thrush, while persistent non-scrapable lesions need a different pathway.

Positive answer: a patch that cannot be removed, persists for more than a few weeks, or recurs repeatedly raises concern for leukoplakia, lichen planus, or malignancy.

Action: refer for assessment rather than repeating empiric treatment if the lesion is fixed or atypical.

Predisposing factors: inhaled steroids, recent antibiotics, dentures, diabetes, dry mouth, immunosuppression

Why it matters: these factors make candidiasis more likely and identify the cause that should be addressed.

Positive answer: inhaled corticosteroid use, denture wear, or known diabetes makes thrush more plausible; unexplained immunosuppression makes it more concerning.

Action: correct the trigger, review inhaler technique or denture hygiene, and lower the threshold for further assessment if the trigger is absent.

Symptom screen: soreness, altered taste, burning mouth, odynophagia, dysphagia, weight loss, fever

Why it matters: uncomplicated oral thrush usually causes local discomfort, not systemic illness or oesophageal symptoms.

Positive answer: pain on swallowing, difficulty swallowing, weight loss, or fever moves the case away from simple oral thrush.

Action: escalate to GP assessment for possible oesophageal spread or alternative pathology.

Risk and cancer screen: smoking, alcohol, unilateral lesion, ulcer, bleeding, induration, neck lump

Why it matters: fixed or ulcerated lesions and head-and-neck risk factors increase concern for malignancy.

Positive answer: induration, bleeding, a neck lump, or a lesion that does not scrape off should not be labelled as thrush.

Action: arrange urgent dental, GP, or specialist referral depending on the local pathway.

Focused examination strategy
Oral inspection: plaques, distribution, and whether they wipe off

Why it matters: the diagnosis of thrush is usually clinical and depends on the oral appearance.

Positive finding: scrapable creamy-white plaques on the tongue, buccal mucosa, or palate support oral candidiasis.

Action: treat as thrush if the pattern is classic; if not scrapable, do not manage as routine thrush.

Denture and inhaler check: fit, overnight removal, cleaning, inhaler technique, mouth rinsing

Why it matters: dentures and inhaled steroids are common reversible causes of recurrence.

Positive finding: worn dentures, poor cleaning, or not rinsing after inhaled corticosteroids makes recurrence more likely.

Action: give specific hygiene advice and correct inhaler technique rather than relying on antifungal treatment alone.

General exam if indicated: weight, hydration, systemic illness, lymph nodes

Why it matters: systemic upset or lymphadenopathy can suggest something more than simple thrush.

Positive finding: weight loss, fever, or a neck lump should raise concern for immunosuppression or malignancy.

Action: do not stop at topical treatment if the broader picture is concerning.

Red flag synthesis and escalation triggers
  • Urgent GP / dental / specialist referral: non-scrapable patch, ulcer, induration, bleeding, neck lump, weight loss, or recurrent unexplained thrush.
  • Same-day review: odynophagia, dysphagia, fever, or significant immunosuppression with oral lesions.
  • Routine primary-care treatment: classic scrapable thrush with an obvious trigger and no red flags.
Investigations and decision points
No routine tests

classic uncomplicated thrush usually does not need investigations.

Swab or further assessment

consider when the appearance is atypical, treatment fails, or the patient is immunocompromised.

Blood tests

consider if recurrent thrush suggests diabetes, haematological disease, or other immunosuppression.

Referral decision

use the oral exam to decide whether this is candidiasis or a lesion needing urgent review.

Diagnostic timeout and common pitfalls
  • Do not call every white patch thrush.
  • Do not treat a fixed lesion as candidiasis if it does not scrape off.
  • Do not miss immunosuppression when thrush is recurrent or extensive.
  • Do not forget inhaled steroid technique and denture hygiene.
Management, follow-up, and safety-netting
Uncomplicated thrush

treat with appropriate topical antifungal therapy and correct the underlying trigger.

Recurrent or severe disease

review for diabetes, inhaled steroid use, denture-related disease, or immunosuppression; consider escalation if the picture is not straightforward.

Safety-netting

return if symptoms fail to improve, recur quickly, or if pain on swallowing, weight loss, bleeding, or a persistent lesion develops.

Special populations and role-aware notes
  • Immunosuppressed adults: lower threshold for medical review and for considering systemic treatment or an underlying cause.
  • Denture wearers: remove dentures overnight and clean them carefully to reduce recurrence.
  • Inhaled corticosteroid users: rinse and spit after inhaler use and check technique.
  • Role-aware note: community pharmacy can support hygiene and triage when the presentation is clearly typical, but non-scrapable lesions or oesophageal symptoms need referral.
Illustrative cases
  • Case 1: a denture wearer with scrapable plaques and sore mouth can usually be managed as uncomplicated thrush with hygiene advice and topical treatment.
  • Case 2: a patient on inhaled steroids with recurrent thrush needs inhaler technique review and consideration of an underlying trigger such as diabetes.
  • Case 3: a non-scrapable white patch with weight loss or a neck lump is not routine thrush and needs urgent assessment.
