Headache in Primary Care Minor Illness
Context: Headache First-Contact Assessment
Overview and key guidance

Headache is common in first-contact primary care, but the consultation is high stakes because the clinician must quickly identify the minority with serious secondary causes while still managing common migraine, tension-type headache, cluster headache, and medication-overuse headache effectively.

The first question is not “which painkiller should I use?” but “is this a dangerous headache, a primary headache, or a headache secondary to another condition?” NICE CG150 remains the main UK headache guideline for diagnosis and management in people over 12, with additional same-day pathways for thunderclap headache, meningitis, stroke, and giant cell arteritis.

Pathophysiology and why this matters
  • Migraine: a neurovascular disorder involving trigeminovascular activation and CGRP-mediated neuroinflammation.
  • Tension-type headache: often relates to myofascial pain and central sensitisation rather than structural intracranial pathology.
  • Cluster headache: involves hypothalamic and trigeminal-autonomic activation; the phenotype is distinctive and severe.
  • Secondary headache: may reflect bleeding, infection, raised intracranial pressure, vasculitis, or medication overuse, so the history must actively search for danger signs.
Differential diagnosis framework
Must not miss
  • Subarachnoid haemorrhage: thunderclap onset, maximal intensity within minutes.
  • [sc name="jitl-term-link" Q="Meningitis recognition and emergency assessment" text="Meningitis"][/sc] / encephalitis: fever, neck stiffness, photophobia, altered mental state, rash.
  • Stroke / intracranial bleed: focal deficit, seizure, reduced consciousness.
  • [sc name="jitl-term-link" Q="Giant cell arteritis assessment and management" text="Giant cell arteritis"][/sc]: new headache in older adult with jaw claudication, scalp tenderness, or visual symptoms.
  • Raised intracranial pressure / space-occupying lesion: progressive headache, vomiting, papilloedema, seizures, worse on waking or Valsalva.
Common primary headaches
  • Migraine with or without aura
  • Tension-type headache
  • Cluster headache
  • Medication-overuse headache
Other causes to keep in play
  • Sinus disease
  • Temporomandibular / cervicogenic headache
  • Hypertensive emergency when severe BP elevation accompanies headache plus end-organ features
Focused history taking
Onset and time course: sudden, gradual, recurrent, or progressively worsening?

Why it matters: Time course often separates benign recurrent headaches from neurology emergencies.

Positive answer: Thunderclap onset points to [sc name="jitl-term-link" Q="Subarachnoid haemorrhage assessment in headache" text="SAH"][/sc] until proven otherwise; progressive worsening over weeks raises raised ICP or mass lesion.

Action: [sc name="jitl-term-link" Q="Thunderclap headache and subarachnoid haemorrhage assessment" text="Thunderclap headache"][/sc] needs emergency assessment; progressive or changing pattern needs further evaluation rather than routine analgesia alone.

Phenotype and associated symptoms: site, character, severity, nausea, photophobia, aura, autonomic features

Why it matters: Recognisable patterns increase confidence in diagnosing primary headache.

Positive answer: Unilateral pulsatile headache with nausea and photophobia supports migraine; strictly unilateral orbital pain with tearing and nasal symptoms supports cluster headache.

Action: Match treatment to phenotype and avoid inappropriate escalation when the story is classic and the examination is reassuring.

Red flag screen: fever, neck stiffness, focal neurology, visual change, jaw claudication, trauma, immunosuppression, cancer history

Why it matters: These questions actively search for secondary causes.

Positive answer: Visual symptoms or jaw claudication in someone over 50 raise [sc name="jitl-term-link" Q="Giant cell arteritis assessment and management" text="GCA"][/sc]; fever and neck stiffness raise meningitis; cancer or immunosuppression raises intracranial secondary disease.

Action: Escalate the patient to same-day or emergency review depending on the syndrome.

Medication use and frequency: what is being taken, how often, and on how many days each month?

Why it matters: Medication-overuse headache is common, missed, and management-changing.

Positive answer: Near-daily headache with frequent analgesic, triptan, or opioid use strongly suggests MOH.

Action: Shift the plan toward withdrawal support, prevention, and follow-up instead of escalating acute analgesia.

Context and patient meaning: pregnancy, recent procedure, infection context, and what feels different from usual

Why it matters: Context often converts a vague headache into a recognisable high-risk pattern.

Positive answer: Post-partum headache, immunosuppression, recent lumbar puncture, or a patient insisting “this is not my usual migraine” should lower the threshold for escalation.

Action: Reframe the consultation as a possible secondary headache until proved otherwise.

Focused examination strategy
General assessment and vital signs

Why it matters: Fever, marked hypertension with end-organ concern, or obvious distress can immediately reframe the consultation.

Positive finding: Fever supports infection; severe BP elevation with neurology or visual change raises hypertensive emergency.

Action: Escalate urgently when physiology is abnormal.

Neurological examination and fundoscopy

Why it matters: Primary headache should have a normal focused neurological examination.

Positive finding: Focal deficit, altered coordination, abnormal visual fields, or papilloedema point away from benign primary headache.

Action: Same-day or emergency neuroimaging pathway depending on severity.

Head and neck examination: meningism, temporal arteries, cranial tenderness, TMJ / neck

Why it matters: Examination can strengthen suspicion for meningitis, [sc name="jitl-term-link" Q="Giant cell arteritis assessment and management" text="GCA"][/sc], or musculoskeletal contributors.

Positive finding: Neck stiffness raises CNS infection; temporal artery tenderness in older adult raises [sc name="jitl-term-link" Q="Giant cell arteritis assessment and management" text="GCA"][/sc].

Action: Urgent referral, ESR/CRP pathway, and do not delay steroids if [sc name="jitl-term-link" Q="Giant cell arteritis assessment and management" text="GCA"][/sc] is strongly suspected.

Teaching rule for novices: a normal exam supports primary headache but does not rescue a dangerous history

Why it matters: Serious headache syndromes can present early before obvious examination findings appear.

Positive finding: A thunderclap history or clear [sc name="jitl-term-link" Q="Giant cell arteritis assessment and management" text="GCA"][/sc] story still needs escalation even if the examination is initially non-dramatic.

Action: Let the time course and red-flag history drive escalation, not just the bedside examination.

Red flag synthesis and escalation triggers
  • Emergency: thunderclap headache, seizure, reduced consciousness, focal neurology, suspected meningitis, or post-traumatic concerning features.
  • Same-day urgent assessment: suspected [sc name="jitl-term-link" Q="Giant cell arteritis assessment and management" text="GCA"][/sc], papilloedema, rapidly progressive new headache, new headache in an immunocompromised / cancer patient, or a first presentation strongly suggestive of cluster headache needing diagnosis confirmation and acute treatment planning.
  • Routine primary-care management: stable recurrent migraine or TTH with normal examination and no red flags.
Investigations and decision points
No routine neuroimaging for classic primary headache

Why it matters: Typical primary headache with a normal examination and no red flags is mainly a clinical diagnosis.

Positive decision: Stable recurrent migraine or tension-type headache does not need imaging as reassurance.

Action: Explain the reasoning clearly and focus on treatment and safety-netting.

ESR / CRP pathway when [sc name="jitl-term-link" Q="Giant cell arteritis assessment and management" text="GCA"][/sc] is suspected

Why it matters: In an older adult with new headache, jaw claudication, or visual symptoms, inflammatory markers are high-value but not definitive.

Positive decision: Strong [sc name="jitl-term-link" Q="Giant cell arteritis assessment and management" text="GCA"][/sc] suspicion should still prompt same-day action even before results return.

Action: Do not delay steroids or urgent specialist advice if vision is at risk.

Targeted bedside checks: BP, fundoscopy, pregnancy testing, and focused neurological assessment

Why it matters: These targeted checks usually add more than indiscriminate blood panels in first-contact headache assessment.

Positive decision: Severe BP elevation, papilloedema, or pregnancy-related context can redirect the whole pathway.

Action: Use bedside information to choose between routine treatment, same-day review, and emergency escalation.

Urgent neuroimaging pathway

Why it matters: Imaging is for clinically suspected secondary headache, not general reassurance.

Positive decision: Focal neurology, papilloedema, reduced consciousness, or a concerning progressive pattern strengthen the case for urgent imaging.

Action: Move into same-day or emergency pathways rather than arranging delayed routine imaging.

Evidence and guideline pearls
Primary headache is mainly diagnosed by history

NICE CG150 emphasises careful clinical diagnosis and discourages routine imaging for typical primary headache.

Medication-overuse headache should be taught early

Medication-overuse headache should be explained proactively to anyone relying on frequent acute treatment.

Acute migraine drugs work best early in the attack

[sc name="jitl-drug-link" drug="Triptans" text="Triptans"][/sc]: work best early in migraine but are contraindicated in ischaemic heart disease, previous stroke or TIA, and uncontrolled hypertension.

[sc name="jitl-drug-link" drug="Ibuprofen" text="Ibuprofen"][/sc] and [sc name="jitl-drug-link" drug="Paracetamol" text="paracetamol"][/sc]: remain common first-line options depending on the patient context.

Prevention and referral decisions

Prophylaxis is for frequent or disabling headache, and cluster headache should prompt a more urgent specialist treatment conversation than routine primary headache care.

Diagnostic timeout and common pitfalls
  • Do not normalise a new headache in older adults: always think [sc name="jitl-term-link" Q="Giant cell arteritis assessment and management" text="GCA"][/sc] and secondary causes.
  • Do not confuse chronic analgesic use with treatment failure alone: think MOH.
  • Do not let a migraine history blind you: patients with known migraine can still have a new dangerous headache.
  • Do not skip fundoscopy when raised ICP is in play.
Management, follow-up, and safety-netting
Migraine pathway

Use early acute therapy, consider a [sc name="jitl-drug-link" drug="Triptans" text="triptan"][/sc] plus simple analgesia where appropriate, and discuss triggers and headache diary use.

Tension-type headache pathway

Use simple analgesia, sleep, hydration, and review of stress or musculoskeletal contributors rather than escalating into high-risk acute medication use.

Cluster headache and MOH pathways

Do not treat cluster headache as a routine “bad headache”; confirm the diagnosis urgently and link into early specialist treatment pathways where oxygen and triptan-based strategies are available.

For medication-overuse headache, explain the diagnosis, plan withdrawal, and arrange review rather than escalating acute analgesia.

Safety-netting and urgent return advice

Seek urgent help for thunderclap onset, fever with neck stiffness, new weakness, seizure, visual loss, or a major change in headache pattern.

Special populations and role-aware notes
  • Children and adolescents: migraine is common, but persistent or unusual headache deserves a lower threshold for escalation.
  • Older adults: new headache is secondary until assessed otherwise; [sc name="jitl-term-link" Q="Giant cell arteritis assessment and management" text="GCA"][/sc] is a recurring must-not-miss.
  • Pregnancy: paracetamol is first-line; medication choice must account for gestation and comorbidity.
  • Role-aware note: pharmacist prescribers can manage clear primary headache presentations within competence, but abnormal neurology, [sc name="jitl-term-link" Q="Giant cell arteritis assessment and management" text="GCA"][/sc] features, or diagnostic uncertainty should move the patient into same-day GP or ED assessment.
