Head Lice in Primary Care
Context: Itchy Scalp and Household Infestation First-Contact Assessment
Overview and key guidance

Head lice infestation is common in school-age children and household contacts. The crucial diagnostic point is that treatment is only recommended if a live louse is found; nits alone do not prove an active infestation.

The first-contact task is to confirm active infestation, identify secondary bacterial infection or significant treatment failure, and then give clear advice on physical treatments or wet combing plus contact-checking. Much of the value of the consultation lies in de-stigmatising the condition and avoiding unnecessary treatment.

Pathophysiology and why this matters
  • Organism: Pediculus humanus capitis lives on the scalp and feeds on blood.
  • Transmission: spread is mainly by direct head-to-head contact, not by jumping or flying.
  • Itch mechanism: itching reflects sensitivity to louse saliva and may be absent early on.
  • Why it matters: correct diagnosis avoids treating dandruff, old nits, or product residue as active infestation.
Differential diagnosis framework
Common and likely
  • Active head lice infestation
  • Past infestation with residual nits
  • Asymptomatic infestation found by screening
Often confused with head lice
  • Dandruff / seborrhoeic scaling: flakes move easily and are not fixed to the hair shaft.
  • Hair casts or product residue: may mimic nits but do not indicate active lice.
  • Scalp eczema / psoriasis: can cause itch and visible scale.
Must not miss
  • Secondary bacterial infection: sore, crusted, weeping scalp.
  • Persistent diagnostic uncertainty: severe itch with no live lice found.
  • Significant psychological or family distress: may need broader support.
Focused history taking
Diagnostic history: was a live louse actually seen, or only eggs / itch?

Why it matters: treatment should be based on active infestation, not nits alone.

Positive answer: a live louse found on wet-combing or direct inspection confirms the diagnosis.

Action: proceed to treatment and contact-check advice if the rest of the story is uncomplicated.

Household and school pattern: siblings, classmates, repeated reinfestation

Why it matters: recurrent infestation often reflects untreated contacts rather than drug failure.

Positive answer: multiple affected contacts makes household checking essential.

Action: advise contact-finding and simultaneous treatment where active lice are confirmed.

Treatment history: wet combing, dimeticone, malathion, missed second dose, technique

Why it matters: apparent treatment failure is often due to incomplete application or failure to repeat treatment at the right interval.

Positive answer: missed second application or no confirmation of live lice changes the interpretation completely.

Action: correct technique before assuming resistance or escalation.

Complication screen: sore scalp, crusting, pus, fever, diagnostic uncertainty

Why it matters: secondary infection or persistent uncertainty moves the case out of routine self-care.

Positive answer: sore weeping skin or significant distress needs medical review.

Action: refer rather than continuing repeated OTC treatments.

Focused examination strategy
Confirm active infestation: wet combing and looking for live lice

Why it matters: a live louse is the diagnostic threshold for treatment.

Positive finding: a moving louse on scalp or comb confirms active infestation.

Action: give treatment advice and contact-check guidance.

Differentiate nits from scale: fixed egg cases vs loose flakes

Why it matters: many families are alarmed by nits or dandruff without active lice.

Positive finding: eggs are fixed to the hair shaft; dandruff is easily moved.

Action: avoid unnecessary insecticide use if active lice are not found.

Check scalp integrity: excoriation, crusting, secondary infection

Why it matters: a sore or infected scalp changes the pathway.

Positive finding: weeping, crusting, tenderness, or pustules suggest secondary infection.

Action: refer for medical review rather than relying on lice treatment alone.

Red flag synthesis and escalation triggers
  • Prompt GP review: secondary bacterial infection, very sore scalp, or repeated treatment failure after correct use.
  • Reassurance / no treatment: nits alone with no live lice found.
  • Routine treatment: active infestation confirmed by a live louse and no complications.
Investigations and decision points
  • No laboratory testing: needed for typical cases.
  • Wet-combing confirmation: is the key decision tool in routine practice.
  • Review technique carefully: before labelling a treatment as failed.
Evidence and guideline pearls
  • Treat only active infestation confirmed by a live louse.
  • Physical treatments such as dimeticone are often preferred because resistance is less relevant.
  • A second treatment at the correct interval is usually essential unless the chosen product states otherwise.
  • Hot-washing the whole house or bagging soft furnishings is not the core management strategy.
Diagnostic timeout and common pitfalls
  • Do not treat nits alone as active infestation.
  • Do not assume resistance before checking technique and the second dose.
  • Do not miss bacterial infection on an excoriated scalp.
Management, follow-up, and safety-netting

Routine management: wet combing or a suitable physical treatment, with repetition at the correct interval where required.

Counselling: check close contacts, confirm correct application, and emphasise that head lice are common and not caused by poor hygiene.

Safety-netting: seek review if the scalp becomes sore, crusted, or if live lice persist despite correct treatment.

Special populations and role-aware notes
  • Infants: wet combing is often the simplest first option; check product licences carefully.
  • Pregnancy / breastfeeding: physical methods and product choice should stay conservative.
  • Sensitive scalp / eczema: consider irritation risk when choosing treatment.
  • Role-aware note: pharmacy can manage uncomplicated active infestation, but infection or repeated failure needs GP review.
Illustrative cases
  • Case 1: live lice found on wet-combing in siblings: treat confirmed cases and check all contacts.
  • Case 2: itch plus nits only, no live lice: do not automatically treat.
  • Case 3: sore crusted scalp after repeated scratching: secondary infection needs medical review.
