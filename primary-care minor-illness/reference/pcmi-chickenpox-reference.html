Chickenpox in Primary Care
Context: Vesicular Rash First-Contact Assessment
Overview and key guidance

Chickenpox is usually a self-limiting viral illness in children, but first-contact assessment must separate uncomplicated disease from severe infection, secondary bacterial infection, neurological complications, respiratory involvement, and high-risk situations such as pregnancy or immunocompromise.

UK guidance is cautious and practical: use supportive care for healthy children, avoid ibuprofen, and refer promptly when the history or examination suggests complications or the patient belongs to a higher-risk group. The key consultation question is not simply whether the rash "looks like chickenpox", but whether this patient can safely recover at home.

Pathophysiology and why this matters
  • Cause: primary infection with varicella-zoster virus (VZV).
  • Spread: respiratory droplets and direct contact with vesicle fluid make it highly contagious.
  • Rash pattern: viraemia seeds the skin in crops, so lesions appear in different stages at the same time.
  • Why it matters: the diagnosis is usually clinical, infectivity is prolonged until all lesions crust, and the same virus later remains latent and can reactivate as shingles.
Differential diagnosis framework
Common and likely
  • Chickenpox
  • Hand, foot and mouth disease
  • Impetigo
  • Insect bites or scabies
Often confused with chickenpox
  • Hand, foot and mouth disease: mouth lesions plus hands and feet are more typical than trunk-predominant cropping.
  • Impetigo: localised honey-coloured crusts, often with no prodromal fever.
  • Disseminated HSV: consider if lesions are atypical, severe, or in a neonate.
Must not miss
  • Secondary bacterial infection / cellulitis
  • Encephalitis or cerebellitis
  • Varicella pneumonitis
  • Severe drug reaction such as SJS
Focused history taking
Rash pattern and timing: onset, crops, itch, distribution, crusting

Why it matters: the classic pattern is a pruritic vesicular rash with lesions in multiple stages.

Positive answer: trunk-predominant crops with fresh and crusted lesions support chickenpox; a localised crusting rash points more toward impetigo.

Action: if the rash pattern is not classic, broaden the differential rather than assuming simple varicella.

Systemic symptoms: fever, malaise, reduced intake, dehydration

Why it matters: mild fever and malaise are expected, but severe systemic upset is not routine.

Positive answer: persistent high fever, poor intake, vomiting, or reduced urine output raises concern for complications or dehydration.

Action: increase the urgency of review and safety-net more strongly.

Red flag screen: drowsiness, confusion, ataxia, headache, neck stiffness, cough, breathlessness, chest pain, severe skin pain

Why it matters: neurological, respiratory, or severe skin symptoms suggest serious complications.

Positive answer: these features point away from routine community management.

Action: arrange urgent same-day assessment or emergency referral.

High-risk groups: pregnancy, neonates, immunocompromise, smokers, adults

Why it matters: adults and high-risk groups are more likely to develop severe disease, especially pneumonitis.

Positive answer: pregnancy, immunosuppression, or neonatal exposure lowers the threshold for specialist advice.

Action: escalate early rather than managing as routine self-care.

Focused examination strategy
General observation and observations

Why it matters: the child or adult’s overall appearance often separates uncomplicated infection from serious illness.

Positive finding: lethargy, respiratory distress, dehydration, or toxic appearance is not routine chickenpox.

Action: lower the threshold for urgent referral.

Skin examination: lesion stage, distribution, and secondary infection

Why it matters: cropping supports the diagnosis and secondary infection changes management.

Positive finding: vesicles, crusts, and fresh lesions together support chickenpox; spreading erythema, swelling, or pus suggests bacterial superinfection.

Action: use the pattern to decide between supportive care, antibiotic treatment, or referral.

Targeted systems exam: chest and neurology when concerned

Why it matters: pneumonitis and encephalitis are the complications most likely to be missed without deliberate checking.

Positive finding: cough, hypoxia, focal chest signs, drowsiness, ataxia, or neck stiffness is concerning.

Action: arrange urgent hospital assessment.

Red flag synthesis and escalation triggers
  • Urgent / same-day: drowsiness, confusion, ataxia, seizures, breathlessness, chest pain, severe cough, or rapidly spreading cellulitis.
  • Immediate specialist advice: pregnancy, immunocompromise, neonatal exposure, or an adult with suspected varicella pneumonitis.
  • Routine management: a well child with classic rash, mild fever, and no red flags.
Investigations and decision points
No routine tests

classic chickenpox is usually a clinical diagnosis.

Swab or review

consider if the rash is atypical, severe, or diagnostically unclear.

Antibiotic decision

only if there is clear secondary bacterial infection.

Antiviral decision

specialist assessment is needed for adults and higher-risk groups when treatment may help.

Diagnostic timeout and common pitfalls
  • Do not miss secondary bacterial infection because the rash is familiar.
  • Do not advise ibuprofen as routine fever treatment.
  • Do not treat every vesicular rash as chickenpox without checking the distribution.
  • Do not underestimate adult, pregnant, neonatal, or immunocompromised disease.
Management, follow-up, and safety-netting
Uncomplicated disease

give supportive care, fluids, itch relief, and paracetamol for pain or fever.

Escalation pathway

urgent review is needed for respiratory, neurological, or severe skin features, and for higher-risk groups.

Safety-netting

return if the child or adult becomes drowsy, breathless, dehydrated, more unwell, or develops spreading redness, pus, or severe pain.

Special populations and role-aware notes
  • Pregnancy: needs urgent specialist advice because maternal disease can be severe.
  • Neonates and immunocompromised patients: low threshold for urgent referral and possible antiviral therapy.
  • Adults: higher risk of pneumonitis, especially if smoking or pregnant.
  • Role-aware note: community pharmacy can advise on supportive care only when the case is clearly uncomplicated; otherwise refer.
Illustrative cases
  • Case 1: a healthy child with classic cropping, mild fever, and no red flags can usually be managed with supportive care.
  • Case 2: an adult with chickenpox and cough, breathlessness, or chest pain needs urgent review for pneumonitis.
  • Case 3: a child with a localised crusting rash and no fever is more likely to have impetigo than chickenpox.
