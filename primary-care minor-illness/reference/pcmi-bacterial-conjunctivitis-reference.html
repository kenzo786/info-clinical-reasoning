Bacterial Conjunctivitis in Primary Care Minor Illness
Context: Red Eye First-Contact Assessment
Overview and key guidance

Bacterial conjunctivitis is a common cause of a sticky red eye, usually with mucopurulent discharge and gritty irritation rather than true pain. The first-contact task is to confirm that this really is a superficial conjunctival problem and not a sight-threatening red-eye presentation.

Most uncomplicated cases are self-limiting, and antimicrobial stewardship matters because antibiotics are often unnecessary. The key safety step is to look for pain, photophobia, visual loss, corneal abnormality, or contact lens-related risk, because those features move the patient out of a routine conjunctivitis pathway.

Pathophysiology and why this matters
  • Conjunctival surface infection: bacterial inoculation triggers conjunctival inflammation, vasodilation, and neutrophil-rich discharge.
  • Mucopurulent discharge: this is the classic “sticky eye” pattern and often causes lids to stick together on waking.
  • Why it matters: simple conjunctivitis affects the conjunctiva, not the cornea or deeper ocular structures.
  • Clinical consequence: true pain, photophobia, or meaningful visual loss should make the clinician reconsider the diagnosis immediately.
Differential diagnosis framework
Common and likely
  • Bacterial conjunctivitis
  • Viral conjunctivitis
  • Blepharoconjunctivitis
Often confused with bacterial conjunctivitis
  • Allergic conjunctivitis: itch-dominant, bilateral, watery, often recurrent or seasonal.
  • Dry eye: irritation and redness, usually less sticky and more chronic or fluctuating.
  • Foreign body / abrasion: more unilateral and more painful, often with clear trauma history.
Must not miss
  • Microbial keratitis: especially in contact lens wearers.
  • Anterior uveitis: pain, photophobia, and ciliary flush.
  • Acute angle-closure glaucoma: severe pain, reduced vision, haloes, nausea.
Focused history taking
Symptom profile: stickiness, discharge type, grittiness, itch, unilateral or bilateral spread

Why it matters: bacterial conjunctivitis is usually sticky and gritty; itch points toward allergy and severe pain points away from simple conjunctivitis.

Positive answer: mucopurulent discharge and lids stuck together support bacterial disease; watery itch-dominant symptoms suggest allergy or viral disease.

Action: decide whether a self-limiting conjunctivitis pathway is still reasonable.

Red-flag screen: true pain, photophobia, reduced vision, trauma, corneal foreign-body sensation

Why it matters: these are the questions that separate a minor red eye from a potentially sight-threatening one.

Positive answer: pain, light sensitivity, or reduced acuity are not typical features of routine bacterial conjunctivitis.

Action: arrange urgent optometry, ophthalmology, or emergency assessment rather than routine treatment.

Contact lens and host factors: contact lens wear, immunosuppression, age under 2, neonate, recurrent disease

Why it matters: contact lens wear changes the safety threshold because microbial keratitis can present like “simple conjunctivitis” early on.

Positive answer: a painful red eye in a contact lens wearer is keratitis until proven otherwise; neonates need urgent specialist assessment.

Action: stop lens wear immediately and refer appropriately.

Focused examination strategy
Visual acuity and general appearance of the eye

Why it matters: acuity is the simplest high-yield check for serious eye disease.

Positive finding: reduced vision not explained by discharge is a red flag.

Action: stop treating this as routine conjunctivitis and escalate.

Conjunctiva, discharge, cornea, and pupil

Why it matters: bacterial conjunctivitis gives diffuse conjunctival injection and discharge, but the cornea and pupil should stay normal.

Positive finding: corneal opacity, focal whitening, ciliary flush, or abnormal pupil are not routine conjunctivitis.

Action: urgent specialist or optometry review.

Contact lens and eyelid review when relevant

Why it matters: lids may show blepharitis, but contact lens risk pushes the threshold for concern much lower.

Positive finding: lid-margin disease may explain recurrent irritation; contact lens wear with pain remains a red flag whatever the external appearance.

Action: treat lens-related painful red eye as urgent until keratitis is excluded.

Red flag synthesis and escalation triggers
  • Urgent / same-day: pain, photophobia, reduced vision, corneal opacity, ciliary flush, abnormal pupil, or proptosis.
  • Urgent / same-day: contact lens wearer with a painful red eye, neonate with purulent discharge, or recent trauma / surgery.
  • Prompt review: symptoms not following the expected benign course, recurrent episodes, or uncertainty about diagnosis.
Investigations and decision points
Usually a clinical diagnosis

no routine swab is needed for straightforward uncomplicated disease.

Decision point

the main question is whether red flags or contact lens risk are present, not whether immediate microbiology is needed.

Consider microbiology or specialist input

recurrent, severe, neonatal, or treatment-resistant cases.

Practical threshold

if the clinician cannot confidently say the cornea and vision are safe, escalate rather than guess.

Diagnostic timeout and common pitfalls
  • Do not call every sticky red eye “bacterial conjunctivitis”.
  • Do not miss keratitis because the discharge looks infective.
  • Do not forget to ask about contact lenses explicitly.
  • Do not over-prescribe antibiotics when the case is mild and self-limiting.
Management, follow-up, and safety-netting
Uncomplicated disease

advise hand hygiene, lid cleansing, separate towels, and realistic expectations about spontaneous improvement.

Medication strategy

use lubricants for comfort and consider targeted antibiotic use only where the risk-benefit balance is reasonable.

Safety-netting

urgent review for pain, photophobia, worsening redness, visual change, or failure to improve along the expected course.

Special populations and role-aware notes
  • Children under 2 and neonates: lower threshold for medical review, and neonates need urgent specialist assessment.
  • Older adults: check whether drop administration is practical and think about dry eye as a mimic.
  • Pregnancy: hygiene and symptom control are still important, but medication choices should stay conservative.
  • Role-aware note: pharmacists and first-contact clinicians can manage simple conjunctivitis safely only if the serious red-eye features have been deliberately excluded.
Illustrative cases
  • Case 1: 32-year-old with sticky lids on waking, gritty bilateral spread, and no pain or photophobia: likely uncomplicated conjunctivitis.
  • Case 2: 21-year-old contact lens wearer with red painful eye and blurred vision: urgent keratitis exclusion.
  • Case 3: 10-day-old infant with purulent eye discharge: urgent neonatal ophthalmic assessment.
