Back Pain in Primary Care Minor Illness
Context: Back Pain First-Contact Assessment
Overview and key guidance

Acute low back pain is common in primary care and most cases are non-specific or mechanical. The job of the first-contact clinician is to identify the small group with cauda equina syndrome, fracture, malignancy, infection, inflammatory disease, or referred visceral pain, while supporting the much larger group to stay active and recover safely.

The consultation should be structured around red flags, neurological assessment, activity-focused management, and realistic recovery framing. NICE NG59 remains the key UK guidance for low back pain and sciatica.

Pathophysiology and why this matters
  • Mechanical low back pain: often arises from muscles, ligaments, facet joints, or discs without a single dangerous lesion.
  • Sciatica: neuropathic pain from nerve root irritation, often due to disc prolapse or foraminal narrowing.
  • Inflammatory or malignant pain: often behaves differently from mechanical pain, with night pain, stiffness, systemic features, or persistent progression.
  • Cauda equina syndrome: a time-critical compressive syndrome where delay can lead to permanent bladder, bowel, or sexual dysfunction.
Differential diagnosis framework
Must not miss
  • Cauda equina syndrome
  • Vertebral fracture
  • Spinal malignancy / metastasis
  • Spinal infection
  • Abdominal aortic aneurysm / visceral referred pain
Common causes
  • Non-specific low back pain
  • Sciatica / lumbar radiculopathy
  • Muscle strain
Important alternative patterns
  • Inflammatory back pain
  • Renal colic or pyelonephritis
  • Pancreatic or pelvic referred pain
Focused history taking
Pain behaviour: onset, trigger, site, radiation, aggravating and easing factors

Why it matters: Mechanical pain varies with movement; non-mechanical pain behaves differently.

Positive answer: Pain radiating below the knee with dermatomal symptoms supports radiculopathy; constant night pain raises malignancy or infection.

Action: Match the syndrome and decide whether this is routine back pain care or a red-flag pathway.

Cauda equina screen: saddle numbness, urinary retention, incontinence, bilateral leg symptoms

Why it matters: Missing cauda equina can cause permanent harm.

Positive answer: Saddle anaesthesia, new bladder dysfunction, or progressive bilateral leg symptoms require emergency imaging assessment.

Action: Immediate same-day emergency referral; do not manage as routine sciatica.

Red flags for fracture, cancer, infection: trauma, osteoporosis, steroids, fever, cancer history, weight loss, IV drug use

Why it matters: These features raise the pre-test probability of serious spinal pathology.

Positive answer: Minor trauma in someone osteoporotic raises fracture; fever and constant pain raise infection; cancer history with night pain raises metastasis.

Action: Lower threshold for imaging, blood tests, and same-day specialty review.

Function and psychosocial impact: mobility, work, fear, sleep, mood

Why it matters: Yellow flags help predict chronicity and help shape support.

Positive answer: Severe fear-avoidance or work disability may prolong recovery even when structural risk is low.

Action: Use clearer education, follow-up, and allied-health input rather than escalating medication alone.

Focused examination strategy
Observation and movement: posture, gait, ability to stand, sit, and mobilise

Why it matters: Gives a quick functional baseline and reveals major distress or neurological compromise.

Positive finding: Antalgic gait is common; profound weakness, collapse, or inability to mobilise is more concerning.

Action: Escalate if functional deficit is disproportionate or rapidly progressive.

Spinal and neurological examination: tenderness, range of movement, power, reflexes, sensation

Why it matters: Helps separate simple mechanical pain from radiculopathy or cord / root compromise.

Positive finding: Objective weakness, reflex change, or dermatomal sensory loss supports radiculopathy; bony tenderness raises fracture.

Action: Decide on same-day referral, imaging, or structured community management.

Straight leg raise and focused abdominal / systemic check when indicated

Why it matters: Supports nerve root irritation and helps avoid tunnel vision.

Positive finding: Positive straight leg raise supports sciatica; pulsatile abdominal mass or abdominal tenderness suggests non-spinal pathology.

Action: Modify the pathway quickly if the pain is not behaving like uncomplicated back pain.

Red flag synthesis and escalation triggers
  • Emergency: suspected cauda equina syndrome, rupturing AAA, or severe progressive neurology.
  • Same-day urgent review: suspected fracture, infection, malignancy, or rapidly worsening radiculopathy.
  • Routine management: neurologically intact mechanical pain without red flags.
Investigations and decision points
  • No routine imaging: for uncomplicated mechanical back pain.
  • MRI: where cauda equina, infection, malignancy, or significant radiculopathy is suspected.
  • Blood tests: consider inflammatory markers when infection or malignancy is possible.
  • STarT Back or equivalent stratification: can help plan support intensity in non-red-flag cases.
Evidence and guideline pearls
  • NICE NG59 advises against routine imaging in non-specialist settings when there are no red flags.
  • Staying active is a treatment, not an optional extra.
  • Paracetamol alone is not recommended for low back pain.
  • Gabapentinoids, benzodiazepines, and routine strong opioids are poor fits for simple back pain management.
Diagnostic timeout and common pitfalls
  • Do not under-ask about bladder and saddle symptoms.
  • Do not promise quick imaging when it is unlikely to help.
  • Do not confuse pain severity with structural danger.
  • Do not miss non-spinal causes of back pain.
Management, follow-up, and safety-netting

Low-risk mechanical pain: encourage activity, explain recovery, consider NSAID if appropriate, and give practical return-to-function advice.

Sciatica: assess severity, give safety-netting for neurological progression, and arrange follow-up if function is significantly affected.

Analgesia principles: use the lowest-risk, shortest-course option that enables movement. Avoid drifting into routine opioid use for uncomplicated low back pain, and avoid bed rest as a management plan.

Safety-netting: seek urgent help for saddle numbness, bladder or bowel change, progressive weakness, fever, or worsening night pain.

Special populations and role-aware notes
  • Children: back pain is less common and deserves a lower threshold for medical assessment.
  • Older adults: think fracture, malignancy, and AAA more readily; oral NSAIDs often carry more risk.
  • Pregnancy: prioritise non-pharmacological management and obstetric-safe analgesia choices.
  • Role-aware note: community pharmacy or prescribing-pharmacist management is appropriate only for clearly low-risk mechanical pain; red flags or objective neurology need GP / same-day / ED escalation.
Illustrative cases
  • Case 1: 39-year-old after lifting, local lumbar pain, no red flags, normal neurology: mechanical back pain.
  • Case 2: 58-year-old with unilateral leg pain below knee and mild sensory change, no cauda equina features: sciatica with close follow-up.
  • Case 3: 64-year-old with bilateral leg pain, urinary difficulty, and saddle numbness: cauda equina until proven otherwise.
