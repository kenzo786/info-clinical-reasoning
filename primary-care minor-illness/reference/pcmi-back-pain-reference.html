Back Pain in Primary Care Minor Illness
Context: Back Pain First-Contact Assessment
Overview and key guidance

Acute low back pain is common in primary care and most cases are non-specific or mechanical. The job of the first-contact clinician is to identify the small group with cauda equina syndrome, fracture, malignancy, infection, inflammatory disease, or referred visceral pain, while supporting the much larger group to stay active and recover safely.

The consultation should be structured around red flags, neurological assessment, activity-focused management, and realistic recovery framing. NICE NG59 remains the key UK guidance for low back pain and sciatica.

Pathophysiology and why this matters
  • Mechanical low back pain: often arises from muscles, ligaments, facet joints, or discs without a single dangerous lesion.
  • Sciatica: neuropathic pain from nerve root irritation, often due to disc prolapse or foraminal narrowing.
  • Inflammatory or malignant pain: often behaves differently from mechanical pain, with night pain, stiffness, systemic features, or persistent progression.
  • Cauda equina syndrome: a time-critical compressive syndrome where delay can lead to permanent bladder, bowel, or sexual dysfunction.
Differential diagnosis framework
Must not miss
  • Cauda equina syndrome
  • Vertebral fracture
  • Spinal malignancy / metastasis
  • Spinal infection
  • Abdominal aortic aneurysm / visceral referred pain
Common causes
  • Non-specific low back pain
  • Sciatica / lumbar radiculopathy
  • Muscle strain
Important alternative patterns
  • Inflammatory back pain
  • Renal colic or pyelonephritis
  • Pancreatic or pelvic referred pain
Focused history taking
Pain behaviour: onset, trigger, site, movement relationship, aggravating and easing factors

Why it matters: Mechanical pain is usually linked to posture or movement; inflammatory, infective, malignant, or visceral pain often behaves differently.

Positive answer: Pain worse with bending or lifting supports a mechanical process; constant night pain or pain unrelated to movement lowers confidence in a simple mechanical diagnosis.

Action: Decide whether this is likely routine back pain, radiculopathy, or a non-mechanical red-flag pathway.

Radiation and neurological symptoms: pain below the knee, dermatomal tingling, numbness, weakness

Why it matters: Neuropathic leg symptoms increase the chance of radiculopathy and help identify who needs closer neurological assessment.

Positive answer: Pain radiating below the knee with dermatomal numbness, paraesthesia, or weakness supports lumbar nerve-root irritation more than simple muscular strain.

Action: Perform focused neurological examination, document function clearly, and escalate if weakness is objective or progressing.

Cauda equina screen: saddle numbness, urinary retention, incontinence, altered perineal sensation, bilateral leg symptoms

Why it matters: Missing cauda equina syndrome can cause permanent bladder, bowel, and sexual dysfunction.

Positive answer: Saddle anaesthesia, new urinary retention, overflow incontinence, or bilateral progressive leg symptoms are emergency features rather than “bad sciatica”.

Action: Arrange immediate same-day emergency assessment and likely MRI pathway; do not manage as routine community back pain.

Red flags for fracture, cancer, and infection: trauma, osteoporosis, steroids, fever, cancer history, weight loss, IV drug use

Why it matters: These features materially raise the probability of serious spinal pathology.

Positive answer: Minor trauma in someone osteoporotic raises fracture; fever with constant pain raises infection; cancer history with night pain raises spinal metastasis.

Action: Lower the threshold for imaging, inflammatory markers, blood cultures when relevant, and same-day specialty review.

Function and psychosocial impact: mobility, sleep, work, fear-avoidance, mood, and patient goals

Why it matters: Yellow flags influence recovery, adherence, and the likelihood of chronic disability.

Positive answer: Severe fear-avoidance, work disability, major sleep disruption, or catastrophising may prolong symptoms even when structural risk is low.

Action: Use clearer recovery framing, paced activity advice, follow-up, and allied-health support rather than escalating medication alone.

Focused examination strategy
Observation and movement: posture, gait, ability to stand, sit, turn, and mobilise

Why it matters: Gives a quick functional baseline and reveals major distress or neurological compromise.

Positive finding: Antalgic gait is common; profound weakness, collapse, or inability to mobilise is more concerning.

Action: Escalate if functional deficit is disproportionate or rapidly progressive.

Lumbar palpation and range of movement: tenderness, deformity, and pattern of movement restriction

Why it matters: Bony tenderness, deformity, or severely restricted movement can shift the likelihood away from a simple soft-tissue diagnosis.

Positive finding: Midline bony tenderness raises fracture concern; globally reduced movement with no neurology may still fit severe mechanical pain.

Action: Combine findings with history rather than over-calling serious disease from restriction alone.

Neurological examination and straight leg raise: power, reflexes, sensation, root tension signs

Why it matters: Objective weakness, reflex change, or dermatomal sensory loss separates radiculopathy from uncomplicated back pain.

Positive finding: Positive straight leg raise supports nerve-root irritation; objective weakness or progressive neurology changes the urgency.

Action: Decide whether this is structured community management, urgent review, or emergency escalation.

Focused abdominal and systemic examination when indicated

Why it matters: It helps avoid tunnel vision when the pain may not be spinal at all.

Positive finding: Pulsatile abdominal mass, abdominal tenderness, fever, or costovertebral angle tenderness points away from routine mechanical back pain.

Action: Modify the pathway rapidly if the pain is behaving like AAA, renal, infective, or other visceral pathology.

Red flag synthesis and escalation triggers
  • Emergency: suspected cauda equina syndrome, rupturing AAA, or severe progressive neurology.
  • Same-day urgent review: suspected fracture, infection, malignancy, or rapidly worsening radiculopathy.
  • Routine management: neurologically intact mechanical pain without red flags.
Investigations and decision points
No routine imaging for uncomplicated mechanical pain

Why it matters: Imaging in non-red-flag back pain rarely changes management and can medicalise normal degenerative findings.

Positive decision: Normal neurology, no red flags, and a mechanical story support conservative management without imaging.

Action: Explain the reasoning clearly so imaging is not used as a reassurance substitute.

MRI pathway for serious or progressive disease

Why it matters: MRI is the key test when cauda equina, infection, malignancy, or significant nerve-root compromise is suspected.

Positive decision: Cauda equina features, objective progressive neurology, systemic red flags, or cancer concern strengthen the need for urgent MRI access.

Action: Move to same-day or emergency specialty pathways rather than routine community follow-up.

Blood tests when infection or malignancy is possible

Why it matters: ESR, CRP, FBC, renal function, or other tests can support the seriousness assessment when the diagnosis is not straightforward mechanical pain.

Positive decision: Fever, immunosuppression, malignancy history, or constant night pain raise the value of inflammatory markers and broader testing.

Action: Use bloods to support escalation, not to delay it.

Risk stratification and follow-up planning

Why it matters: Tools such as STarT Back can help decide how much support and follow-up low-risk versus persistent cases need.

Positive decision: High psychosocial burden or functional impact supports more structured follow-up even when red flags are absent.

Action: Match follow-up intensity to risk rather than treating every uncomplicated back pain case the same way.

Evidence and guideline pearls
Imaging is not reassurance treatment

NICE NG59 advises against routine imaging in non-specialist settings when there are no red flags because it rarely improves outcomes.

Activity is part of treatment

Staying active, maintaining function, and avoiding prolonged bed rest are core management principles rather than optional extras.

Analgesic cautions

[sc name="jitl-drug-link" drug="Ibuprofen" text="NSAIDs"][/sc]: can help selected patients but need routine GI, renal, cardiovascular, and pregnancy-risk thinking.

[sc name="jitl-drug-link" drug="Gabapentinoids" text="Gabapentinoids"][/sc], [sc name="jitl-drug-link" drug="Benzodiazepines" text="benzodiazepines"][/sc], and strong opioids: are poor fits for uncomplicated back pain management.

Reasoning pearl for novices

Back pain improves when clinicians explain recovery and support movement; it worsens when clinicians over-medicalise routine cases or under-recognise red flags.

Diagnostic timeout and common pitfalls
  • Do not under-ask about bladder and saddle symptoms.
  • Do not promise quick imaging when it is unlikely to help.
  • Do not confuse pain severity with structural danger.
  • Do not miss non-spinal causes of back pain.
Management, follow-up, and safety-netting
Low-risk mechanical pain pathway

Encourage activity, explain the expected course, and give practical return-to-function advice rather than promising cure by rest or imaging.

Analgesia strategy

Use the lowest-risk, shortest-course option that enables movement. [sc name="jitl-drug-link" drug="Ibuprofen" text="NSAIDs"][/sc] may help where appropriate, but routine opioid drift should be actively avoided.

Sciatica and follow-up planning

Assess severity and function, give safety-netting for neurological progression, and arrange follow-up if disability is meaningful or symptoms are not settling.

Safety-netting and urgent return advice

Seek urgent help for saddle numbness, bladder or bowel change, progressive weakness, fever, or worsening night pain.

Special populations and role-aware notes
  • Children: back pain is less common and deserves a lower threshold for medical assessment.
  • Older adults: think fracture, malignancy, and AAA more readily; oral NSAIDs often carry more risk.
  • Pregnancy: prioritise non-pharmacological management and obstetric-safe analgesia choices.
  • Role-aware note: community pharmacy or prescribing-pharmacist management is appropriate only for clearly low-risk mechanical pain; red flags or objective neurology need GP / same-day / ED escalation.
Illustrative cases
  • Case 1: 39-year-old after lifting, local lumbar pain, no red flags, normal neurology: mechanical back pain.
  • Case 2: 58-year-old with unilateral leg pain below knee and mild sensory change, no cauda equina features: sciatica with close follow-up.
  • Case 3: 64-year-old with bilateral leg pain, urinary difficulty, and saddle numbness: cauda equina until proven otherwise.
