Otitis Externa in Primary Care Minor Illness
Context: Ear Pain and Ear Discharge First-Contact Assessment
Overview and key guidance

Otitis externa is inflammation of the external auditory canal, usually presenting with rapid-onset ear pain, itching, a blocked feeling, tenderness of the tragus or pinna, and sometimes otorrhoea or conductive hearing loss. Most cases are localised and respond to topical treatment plus self-care, but first-contact assessment must distinguish uncomplicated otitis externa from acute otitis media with perforation, foreign body, Ramsay Hunt syndrome, mastoiditis, and necrotising otitis externa.

The immediate clinical task is to decide whether this is uncomplicated canal inflammation suitable for local treatment and safety-netting, or whether severe pain, marked swelling, spreading infection, cranial nerve involvement, diabetes, or immunosuppression makes same-day escalation necessary. NICE CKS-style practice supports analgesia, keeping the ear dry, topical treatment for confirmed uncomplicated disease, and early escalation when more serious pathology is possible.

Pathophysiology and why this matters
  • Barrier failure: moisture, trauma from cotton buds or scratching, hearing-aid irritation, or underlying eczema can disrupt the protective acidic ear-canal environment.
  • Inflammation and infection: the swollen canal becomes painful, narrowed, and prone to secondary bacterial infection, often involving Pseudomonas aeruginosa or Staphylococcus aureus.
  • Why it matters: severe oedema can prevent drops reaching the canal, and severe or disproportionate pain in a diabetic or immunocompromised patient raises concern for necrotising otitis externa.
  • Clinical consequence: pain on moving the tragus or pinna strongly supports an external canal process rather than middle-ear infection.
Differential diagnosis framework
Common and likely
  • Acute diffuse otitis externa
  • Localised otitis externa / furunculosis
  • Contact or eczematous otitis externa
Often confused with otitis externa
  • Acute otitis media with perforation: otorrhoea with a middle-ear source, often less tragal tenderness.
  • Impacted wax or debris: blockage and hearing loss without the same inflammatory pain profile.
  • Foreign body: especially in children or with sudden unilateral symptoms.
Must not miss
  • Necrotising otitis externa: severe deep pain, diabetes or immunosuppression, granulation tissue, cranial neuropathy.
  • Mastoiditis: swelling, tenderness, or erythema behind the ear with systemic illness or otitis media features.
  • Ramsay Hunt syndrome: severe otalgia with vesicles and facial weakness.
Focused history taking
Symptom pattern: pain, itch, discharge, blocked sensation, hearing change, pain on pinna or tragus movement

Why it matters: the combination of canal pain and tenderness on moving the outer ear is highly suggestive of otitis externa.

Positive answer: marked tragal tenderness supports an external canal process; sudden hearing loss, vertigo, or severe systemic symptoms point away from a simple case.

Action: decide whether the presentation is clinically straightforward or whether otoscopy, urgent review, or alternative diagnoses need priority.

Triggers and local risk factors: swimming, water exposure, cotton buds, scratching, hearing aids, eczema, psoriasis

Why it matters: these are common predisposing factors and help explain recurrence or chronic irritation.

Positive answer: water exposure or trauma strengthens the diagnosis; hearing aids or dermatoses may need prevention-focused advice.

Action: include trigger removal and ear-care advice in management, and consider eczematous disease if itch dominates over pain.

Comorbidity and red-flag screen: diabetes, immunosuppression, fever, facial weakness, spreading redness, severe nocturnal pain

Why it matters: host factors and severe features change the threshold for same-day referral.

Positive answer: diabetes, immunosuppression, disproportionate pain, or cranial nerve symptoms raise concern for invasive infection.

Action: move from routine topical management to same-day GP, ENT, or emergency assessment depending on severity.

Focused examination strategy
Outer ear and peri-auricular inspection

Why it matters: inspection identifies cellulitis, Ramsay Hunt vesicles, mastoid swelling, and the severity of local inflammation.

Positive finding: erythema extending beyond the canal, pinna swelling, or post-auricular tenderness suggests more than simple local canal inflammation.

Action: escalate if infection appears to be spreading or if mastoiditis or zoster is possible.

Tragal / pinna tenderness and canal otoscopy

Why it matters: this is the key bedside combination for diagnosing otitis externa and judging severity.

Positive finding: pain with tragal pressure plus an erythematous, oedematous canal with debris strongly supports otitis externa.

Action: if the canal is too swollen to visualise adequately or deliver treatment, consider wick placement or same-day review depending on setting and expertise.

Cranial nerve and systemic assessment when severe

Why it matters: severe or atypical disease may already be extending beyond the canal.

Positive finding: facial weakness, systemic illness, or deep disproportionate pain are red flags for serious infection.

Action: arrange same-day urgent assessment rather than continuing with routine community management.

Red flag synthesis and escalation triggers
  • Urgent / same-day: severe deep pain, diabetes or immunosuppression, facial weakness, spreading cellulitis, complete canal obstruction, mastoid swelling, or systemic illness.
  • Prompt GP / ENT review: recurrent or treatment-resistant disease, uncertain diagnosis, inability to visualise the canal safely, or suspected fungal / dermatological disease needing targeted management.
  • Routine uncomplicated case: localised canal inflammation without systemic features or high-risk host factors.
Investigations and decision points
No routine swab

for every typical uncomplicated case.

Microscopy / culture

consider if recurrent, chronic, fungal-appearing, or not responding to appropriate topical treatment.

No routine oral antibiotics

unless there is cellulitis beyond the canal, systemic illness, or high-risk complications.

Decision point

the practical question is often whether the canal is open enough for topical therapy and whether there are red flags, rather than whether blood tests are needed.

Diagnostic timeout and common pitfalls
  • Do not label every painful ear as otitis media.
  • Do not miss necrotising otitis externa in a diabetic or immunocompromised patient with severe pain.
  • Do not keep adding drops if the canal is too swollen for them to reach the target.
  • Do not overlook underlying eczema, hearing-aid irritation, or canal trauma in recurrent disease.
Management, follow-up, and safety-netting
Uncomplicated disease

provide analgesia, advise strict ear-drying and avoidance of trauma, and use appropriate topical treatment in line with local or national guidance.

More severe disease

consider wick placement, reassessment, or escalation if swelling prevents treatment delivery or if the diagnosis is not secure.

Safety-netting

seek urgent review for worsening pain, facial weakness, fever, spreading redness, marked hearing change, or lack of improvement after an appropriate treatment window.

Special populations and role-aware notes
  • Children: think about foreign body and acute otitis media as common mimics.
  • Older adults: severe pain deserves extra caution, especially if diabetes is present.
  • Pregnancy: topical management is usually still the main approach, but choice of product should follow current guidance and the clinical context.
  • Role-aware note: pharmacists and first-contact clinicians can recognise likely uncomplicated disease and reinforce self-care, but severe pain, host-risk factors, or diagnostic uncertainty should move the patient into same-day medical assessment.
Illustrative cases
  • Case 1: 24-year-old swimmer with itch, otalgia, and tenderness of the tragus after pool use: uncomplicated diffuse otitis externa.
  • Case 2: 68-year-old hearing-aid user with severe swelling and blocked canal preventing visualisation: otitis externa needing escalation for effective topical delivery and review.
  • Case 3: 76-year-old with diabetes and severe deep nocturnal ear pain with facial weakness: urgent same-day assessment for possible necrotising otitis externa.
