Acute Otitis Media in Primary Care
Context: Ear Pain First-Contact Assessment
Overview and key guidance

Acute otitis media is a common childhood presentation in primary care. The core task is to decide whether this is a typical self-limiting infection that can be managed with analgesia and safety-netting, or whether red flags, complications, or an unusual age/pattern mean the child needs same-day review or escalation.

NICE NG91 supports analgesia-first management for most children, with antibiotics reserved for systemic illness, higher-risk groups, or children with otorrhoea or bilateral disease under 2 years. A useful consultation starts with age, symptom pattern, and a deliberate search for complications before assuming this is routine earache.

Pathophysiology and why this matters
  • Trigger: often follows a viral upper respiratory tract infection.
  • Mechanism: Eustachian tube dysfunction causes middle-ear fluid, pressure, and inflammation.
  • Typical effect: ear pain, fever, irritability, disturbed sleep, and sometimes transient hearing loss.
  • Why it matters: understanding the pressure-driven pain explains why analgesia is usually the main treatment and why a bulging tympanic membrane matters more than a sore ear alone.
Differential diagnosis framework
Common and likely
  • Acute otitis media
  • Otitis media with effusion
  • Otitis externa
  • Referred pain from throat, teeth, or jaw
Must not miss
  • Mastoiditis
  • Facial nerve palsy
  • Intracranial complications such as meningitis
  • Sepsis or severe systemic illness
Context clues
  • Otitis externa: pain on pinna or tragus movement, itchy or swollen canal.
  • OME: hearing loss without acute pain or fever.
  • Foreign body: unilateral symptoms in a young child.
  • Cholesteatoma: chronic discharge or recurrent hearing loss rather than a simple acute infection.
Focused history taking
Symptom pattern: ear pain, fever, irritability, feeding or sleep disturbance

Why it matters: classic AOM is painful, febrile, and often follows a cold.

Positive answer: ear discharge, sudden improvement after discharge, or hearing loss changes the differential and may suggest perforation or OME.

Action: decide whether this is straightforward AOM, a likely alternative diagnosis, or a child needing same-day review.

Age, recurrence, and risk factors: under 2, bilateral disease, recurrent episodes, immunosuppression, cleft palate, Down syndrome

Why it matters: age and comorbidity alter the threshold for antibiotics and referral.

Positive answer: under-2 bilateral disease or recurrent episodes raises the chance that a back-up or immediate prescription is appropriate; anatomical or immune risk lowers the threshold further.

Action: use these details to decide between watchful waiting, GP review, or antibiotic consideration.

Red flag screen: swelling behind the ear, facial weakness, severe headache, neck stiffness, photophobia, confusion, vertigo, toxic appearance

Why it matters: complications are uncommon but clinically important.

Positive answer: any of these features moves the consultation away from routine primary-care management.

Action: escalate urgently rather than relying on OTC advice alone.

Focused examination strategy
General observation and observations

Why it matters: the child’s overall state helps separate routine infection from systemic illness.

Positive finding: drowsiness, marked lethargy, high fever, or an obviously unwell child raises concern for complication or sepsis.

Action: lower the threshold for same-day GP or urgent hospital assessment.

Otoscopy: tympanic membrane appearance

Why it matters: A bulging, red, opaque tympanic membrane supports AOM; a normal membrane or a swollen canal suggests another cause.

Positive finding: bulging TM with loss of light reflex strengthens the diagnosis.

Action: use the finding to support analgesia-first management or, if perforation/discharge is present, to consider antibiotics or referral.

Post-auricular and cranial nerve check

Why it matters: mastoiditis and facial nerve involvement are red flags.

Positive finding: post-auricular swelling/tenderness or facial asymmetry is not routine earache.

Action: urgent escalation to GP or hospital assessment.

Red flag synthesis and escalation triggers
  • Urgent / same-day: mastoid swelling, facial weakness, severe vertigo, toxic appearance, or intracranial symptoms.
  • Prompt GP review: under 2 with bilateral AOM, otorrhoea, recurrent episodes, or high-risk comorbidity.
  • Routine management: typical unilateral AOM in a well child with no red flags.
Investigations and decision points
  • No routine tests: classic uncomplicated AOM usually does not need swabs or blood tests.
  • Otoscopy: is the key decision point when the exam is available.
  • Consider antibiotic threshold: if bilateral disease in a child under 2, otorrhoea, or significant risk factors are present.
  • Consider alternative diagnosis: if the ear exam does not match the story.
Evidence and guideline pearls
  • NICE NG91 recommends analgesia first for most children with AOM.
  • Antibiotics have limited benefit in uncomplicated cases and do not replace symptom control.
  • Delayed or immediate antibiotics are reserved for the children most likely to benefit, especially those under 2 with bilateral disease or any child with otorrhoea.
  • Decongestants and antihistamines should not be used as routine AOM treatment.
Diagnostic timeout and common pitfalls
  • Do not assume every earache is AOM.
  • Do not miss otitis externa when the canal is swollen or pain is triggered by pinna movement.
  • Do not ignore mastoid swelling or facial weakness because the child is otherwise “just a bit unwell”.
  • Do not over-focus on antibiotics when the real intervention is accurate analgesia and safety-netting.
Management, follow-up, and safety-netting

Uncomplicated disease: give regular paracetamol or ibuprofen, explain that pain should begin to settle over 3 days, and provide clear advice about when to seek review.

Antibiotic pathway: if the child is in a group likely to benefit, a GP may use an immediate or back-up antibiotic strategy rather than OTC-only care.

Safety-netting: return urgently for mastoid swelling, facial weakness, stiff neck, photophobia, severe headache, worsening systemic illness, or failure to improve.

Special populations and role-aware notes
  • Children under 2: lower threshold for GP review and antibiotics if bilateral or complicated.
  • Immunosuppressed or anatomically high-risk children: same-day GP assessment is more appropriate.
  • Pregnancy / breastfeeding in older patients: routine analgesic principles still apply, but adult ear pain should prompt a broader differential.
  • Role-aware note: community pharmacy can support analgesia and triage, but it should not try to manage red-flag ear disease as routine self-care.
Illustrative cases
  • Case 1: a well 3-year-old with ear pain after a cold and a bulging tympanic membrane can usually be managed with analgesia and safety-netting.
  • Case 2: a 4-year-old with otorrhoea and reduced pain may need GP review because perforation changes the antibiotic threshold.
  • Case 3: ear pain with mastoid swelling is not simple AOM and needs urgent escalation.
