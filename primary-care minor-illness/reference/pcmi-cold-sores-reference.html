Cold Sores in Primary Care
Context: Recurrent Perioral Vesicles and Lip Lesions
Overview and key guidance

Cold sores are usually recurrent herpes simplex virus lesions on or around the lips, often preceded by tingling or burning. Most uncomplicated episodes can be managed with self-care and early topical treatment, but the assessment must still identify ocular involvement, eczema herpeticum, immunosuppression, or a non-healing lesion that could represent skin cancer.

UK primary care advice is conservative: recognise the prodrome, treat early if using topical antivirals, and refer promptly for eye symptoms, widespread disease, neonatal exposure, severe immunosuppression, or a lesion that does not follow the expected 7 to 10 day course. The key question is not just "is this a cold sore?" but "is this a routine episode or something more serious?".

Pathophysiology and why this matters
  • Cause: herpes simplex virus reactivates from latency in the trigeminal ganglion.
  • Triggering factors: ultraviolet light, stress, febrile illness, trauma, and intercurrent illness can precipitate recurrence.
  • Typical pattern: a prodrome of tingling or burning, followed by grouped vesicles and crusting on the lip or perioral skin.
  • Why it matters: recognising the prodrome allows early treatment, while an atypical or non-healing lesion needs a broader differential.
Differential diagnosis framework
Common and likely
  • Cold sores / recurrent HSV
  • Impetigo
  • Aphthous ulcers if lesions are intra-oral
Often confused with cold sores
  • Impetigo: honey-coloured crusts, often without prodrome.
  • Traumatic ulcer or lip bite: local injury without recurrent pattern.
  • Angular cheilitis: soreness and cracking at the mouth corners.
Must not miss
  • Eczema herpeticum
  • Ocular HSV / keratitis
  • Non-healing lip lesion / squamous cell carcinoma
  • Severe disseminated infection in immunocompromise
Focused history taking
Prodrome and lesion pattern: tingling, burning, vesicles, crusting, duration

Why it matters: a classic cold sore has a short prodrome followed by grouped vesicles on the lip.

Positive answer: tingling before a recurring lip lesion strongly supports HSV reactivation.

Action: if the lesion is not following the expected pattern or duration, think about a mimic or a non-healing lesion.

Trigger and recurrence history: UV exposure, stress, febrile illness, trauma, frequency of outbreaks

Why it matters: recurrent triggers support HSV and help prevention advice.

Positive answer: repeated attacks, especially in the same site, are typical of cold sores.

Action: give trigger advice and consider escalation if episodes are frequent or severe.

Red flag screen: eye pain, visual change, widespread rash, fever, confusion, eczema, immunosuppression, neonatal exposure

Why it matters: eye disease, eczema herpeticum, and disseminated HSV need urgent action.

Positive answer: any eye symptom or widespread vesicular eruption changes the pathway completely.

Action: arrange urgent same-day or emergency referral depending on severity.

Cancer-safe screen: persistent lesion, non-healing ulcer, smoking, alcohol, induration, bleeding

Why it matters: a non-healing lip lesion is not routine cold sores.

Positive answer: persistence beyond the expected healing time or a fixed ulcer raises concern for SCC.

Action: refer rather than repeating self-care treatment.

Focused examination strategy
Lesion inspection: location, stage, crusting, and whether it is grouped

Why it matters: the appearance should match a short HSV episode on or around the lip.

Positive finding: grouped vesicles or crusted lesions at the lip margin fit cold sores.

Action: if the lesion is unusual, ulcerated, or persistent, do not assume routine cold sores.

Eye and skin check: conjunctiva, eyelids, facial rash, eczema

Why it matters: eye involvement and eczema herpeticum are urgent complications.

Positive finding: eye redness, pain, photophobia, or widespread painful vesicles changes management immediately.

Action: refer urgently for ophthalmology or medical assessment.

General exam if indicated: fever, systemic illness, immunocompromise, hydration

Why it matters: systemic illness makes a simple self-managed episode less likely.

Positive finding: fever or a patient who looks unwell should prompt broader assessment.

Action: lower the threshold for GP review or escalation.

Red flag synthesis and escalation triggers
  • Immediate referral: eye pain, visual change, photophobia, or any suspected ocular HSV.
  • Urgent same-day review: eczema herpeticum, neonatal lesion, widespread disease, severe immunosuppression, confusion, or fever with systemic illness.
  • Routine self-care or OTC treatment: typical recurrent lip cold sore with prodrome and no red flags.
Investigations and decision points
  • No routine tests: typical recurrent cold sores are usually a clinical diagnosis.
  • Urgent review: if the lesion is non-healing, atypical, or suspicious for malignancy.
  • Specialist care: if ocular involvement, eczema herpeticum, or severe disseminated infection is suspected.
  • Medication decision: check renal function and drug history if oral antivirals are being considered by the GP.
Evidence and guideline pearls
  • Topical aciclovir has modest benefit and works best if started at the prodrome.
  • Oral antivirals are more effective but are reserved for severe, frequent, or complicated disease.
  • Hydrocolloid patches can protect lesions but do not contain antiviral medicine.
  • Recurrent cold sores are often self-limiting in 7 to 10 days.
Diagnostic timeout and common pitfalls
  • Do not miss eye symptoms.
  • Do not keep treating a non-healing lip lesion as a cold sore.
  • Do not forget eczema herpeticum in a patient with atopic eczema.
  • Do not expect topical antivirals to help much if started late.
Management, follow-up, and safety-netting

Uncomplicated cold sore: give self-care advice, infection-control advice, and early topical treatment if appropriate.

More severe disease: refer for oral antivirals, ocular review, or broader assessment if the pattern is frequent, severe, or atypical.

Safety-netting: seek urgent help for eye symptoms, widespread rash, fever, confusion, or lesions that are not healing.

Special populations and role-aware notes
  • Immunosuppressed adults: lower threshold for oral antivirals and referral.
  • Pregnancy and breastfeeding: topical treatment is usually acceptable, but severe or atypical disease needs medical review.
  • Atopic eczema: be alert for eczema herpeticum.
  • Role-aware note: pharmacy can support routine cold sore self-care only when the lesion is typical and localised.
Illustrative cases
  • Case 1: recurrent tingling followed by a small lip vesicle can usually be managed with early topical treatment and advice.
  • Case 2: eye pain or photophobia with a facial lesion needs urgent ophthalmology referral.
  • Case 3: a non-healing ulcerated lip lesion in a smoker needs cancer-pathway assessment, not repeat cold sore treatment.
