Acute Sore Throat in Primary Care Minor Illness
Context: Pharyngitis and Tonsillitis First-Contact Assessment
Overview and key guidance

Acute sore throat is usually a self-limiting upper respiratory infection, most often viral, but the consultation still needs to separate low-risk pharyngitis from bacterial tonsillitis, scarlet fever, glandular fever, peritonsillar abscess, epiglottitis, or systemic illness.

The practical task in primary care is to decide whether the patient can be managed safely with self-care and symptomatic treatment, or whether red flags, marked systemic features, or a high likelihood of streptococcal infection mean urgent review, testing, or antibiotics. NICE NG84 and NHS Pharmacy First principles support conservative, score-guided prescribing rather than routine antibiotics.

Pathophysiology and why this matters
  • Most cases are viral: pharyngeal inflammation is usually part of an upper respiratory tract infection and settles without specific antimicrobial treatment.
  • Bacterial cases still matter: group A streptococcal infection can cause more severe symptoms and specific complications such as scarlet fever or quinsy.
  • Inflammation drives symptoms: mucosal oedema and local immune activation cause pain on swallowing, fever, and tonsillar exudate.
  • Why it matters: the key risk is not the sore throat itself but missing airway compromise, sepsis, or another serious diagnosis in a patient who looks “like a simple throat infection”.
Differential diagnosis framework
Common and likely
  • Viral pharyngitis or tonsillitis
  • Uncomplicated streptococcal tonsillitis
  • Post-nasal drip / URTI-related throat irritation
Often confused with sore throat
  • Infectious mononucleosis: severe fatigue, lymphadenopathy, prolonged course.
  • Scarlet fever: sore throat with a characteristic rash.
  • Glandular or viral illness with oral ulceration: may sound similar but often has broader systemic clues.
Must not miss
  • Peritonsillar abscess (quinsy): unilateral pain, trismus, muffled voice, uvular deviation.
  • Epiglottitis / airway compromise: drooling, stridor, difficulty swallowing saliva, respiratory distress.
  • Sepsis: marked systemic illness, hypotension, confusion, or rigors.
  • Immunocompromise with sore throat: consider neutropenic sepsis or drug-related agranulocytosis.
Focused history taking
Symptom pattern: sore throat severity, onset, fever, cough, coryza, swallowing difficulty

Why it matters: a short, self-limiting URTI pattern with cough and coryza is more suggestive of viral disease, while severe throat pain with fever and rapid onset raises the likelihood of streptococcal infection.

Positive answer: no cough, fever, and a rapid onset support a higher FeverPAIN score; cough and coryza lower the likelihood of bacterial tonsillitis.

Action: use the overall pattern to decide whether simple self-care is enough or whether scoring, testing, or antibiotics should be considered.

Airway and dehydration screen: drooling, stridor, muffled voice, inability to swallow fluids, shortness of breath

Why it matters: these features suggest airway compromise or inability to maintain hydration, which are not appropriate for routine community management.

Positive answer: drooling, stridor, or inability to swallow saliva is a red flag for urgent referral.

Action: arrange same-day emergency assessment rather than advice-only management.

Quinsy and bacterial complication screen: unilateral pain, ear pain, trismus, voice change, uvular deviation, neck swelling

Why it matters: peritonsillar abscess often starts as an apparently routine sore throat, so the history needs to look for asymmetry and progression.

Positive answer: unilateral worsening pain, trismus, or muffled voice makes quinsy much more likely.

Action: urgent same-day ENT / emergency assessment is more appropriate than routine primary-care treatment.

Risk factors and context: immunocompromise, pregnancy, child age, medication history, exposure history, previous episodes

Why it matters: age, immune status, and medication history change both risk and treatment options.

Positive answer: chemotherapy, DMARDs, carbimazole, or other immunosuppressive treatment lowers the threshold for urgent review; recurrent or prolonged symptoms suggest another diagnosis such as glandular fever.

Action: tailor the pathway, avoid casual antibiotic use, and escalate earlier if the patient is higher risk.

Focused examination strategy
When examination is needed

Why it matters: many uncomplicated sore throats need only a brief assessment, but red flags or diagnostic uncertainty should prompt examination.

Positive trigger: severe pain, fever, unilateral symptoms, inability to swallow, immunocompromise, or concern about dehydration.

Action: inspect the throat and assess the patient rather than relying on history alone.

Observe general appearance and vital signs: temperature, pulse, respiratory rate, hydration, voice quality

Why it matters: the sick patient may have little to do with a simple viral sore throat and much more to do with sepsis, airway compromise, or dehydration.

Positive finding: tachycardia, high fever, stridor, muffled voice, or obvious distress increases urgency.

Action: escalate to same-day urgent or emergency care if the patient looks systemically unwell.

Inspect the oropharynx: tonsillar swelling, exudate, asymmetry, uvula position, palate petechiae

Why it matters: exam can support bacterial tonsillitis, scarlet fever, glandular fever, or quinsy, but it is not definitive on its own.

Positive finding: marked unilateral swelling, uvular deviation, or pus with trismus is concerning for abscess; exudate alone does not prove bacterial disease.

Action: use the exam to refine the differential and decide whether scoring, antibiotics, or referral is needed.

Neck and lymph node assessment: cervical lymphadenopathy, tenderness, neck stiffness

Why it matters: posterior or generalized lymphadenopathy can support glandular fever, while neck stiffness or marked tenderness raises concern for more serious pathology.

Positive finding: prominent nodes plus fatigue should prompt consideration of infectious mononucleosis.

Action: avoid overcalling bacterial tonsillitis and give safer, more cautious advice.

Red flag synthesis and escalation triggers
  • Emergency same-day: stridor, drooling, respiratory distress, inability to swallow saliva, or rapidly worsening airway symptoms.
  • Urgent same-day: unilateral throat pain, trismus, muffled voice, uvular deviation, severe systemic illness, or suspected quinsy.
  • Prompt clinical review: symptoms lasting more than about a week, recurrent episodes, marked fatigue, or concern for glandular fever.
  • Lower threshold for escalation: pregnancy, immunocompromise, medication-related neutropenia risk, or diagnostic uncertainty.
Investigations and decision points
  • No routine test: many uncomplicated cases can be managed clinically without swabs or blood tests.
  • FeverPAIN score: use it to support antibiotic decisions when bacterial tonsillitis is being considered.
  • Throat swab / further testing: consider only when the result will change management or local pathways require it.
  • FBC / urgent bloods: if immunocompromised, systemically unwell, or agranulocytosis is a concern.
  • Further referral: if glandular fever, scarlet fever, quinsy, or epiglottitis is suspected.
Evidence and guideline pearls
  • NICE NG84 supports conservative, score-guided antibiotic use rather than routine antibiotics for all sore throats.
  • Most sore throats improve within about a week with self-care and simple analgesia.
  • Antibiotics are mainly for patients at higher likelihood of bacterial infection or higher risk of complications.
  • Local pharmacy and GP pathways should stay aligned so that referral thresholds are clear and consistent.
Diagnostic timeout and common pitfalls
  • Do not assume every sore throat needs antibiotics.
  • Do not miss quinsy because the throat “just looks inflamed”.
  • Do not miss airway compromise if the patient cannot swallow saliva or is drooling.
  • Do not forget immunocompromise or drug-related agranulocytosis risk.
Management, follow-up, and safety-netting

Low-risk illness: advise rest, fluids, reassurance, and regular simple analgesia such as paracetamol or ibuprofen if suitable.

Symptom relief: lozenges or sprays may help short-term, but they are adjuncts rather than core treatment.

Antibiotics: consider only if FeverPAIN / clinical picture supports bacterial infection or if scarlet fever is suspected.

Safety-netting: seek urgent help if swallowing worsens, breathing becomes difficult, unilateral pain develops, the patient deteriorates, or symptoms fail to improve after about a week.

Special populations and role-aware notes
  • Children: assess carefully for hydration, drooling, and the possibility of airway compromise or scarlet fever.
  • Pregnancy: keep medicines conservative and seek senior advice if systemic illness is present.
  • Immunocompromise: lower threshold for urgent assessment and blood tests.
  • Role-aware note: community pharmacy can manage many low-risk cases, but red flags, marked systemic illness, and higher-risk patients should move into GP or urgent care assessment.
Illustrative cases
  • Case 1: 19-year-old with 3 days of sore throat, cough, coryza, and mild fever: likely viral and suitable for self-care.
  • Case 2: 26-year-old with fever, no cough, tonsillar exudate, and rapid onset: consider higher FeverPAIN score and possible antibiotics.
  • Case 3: 34-year-old with unilateral throat pain, trismus, and muffled voice: suspect quinsy and arrange urgent same-day assessment.
