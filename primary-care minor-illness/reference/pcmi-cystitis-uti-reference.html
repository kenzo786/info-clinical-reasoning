Cystitis (UTI in Women) in Primary Care Minor Illness
Context: Lower Urinary Tract Infection First-Contact Assessment
Overview and key guidance

Cystitis is an acute lower urinary tract infection that is common in primary care and usually presents with dysuria, urinary frequency, and urgency. The first task is to decide whether this is a straightforward lower UTI that can be managed in the community, or whether the story suggests pyelonephritis, sepsis, pregnancy-related infection, recurrent disease, or a non-UTI diagnosis.

In a novice consultation, the diagnosis is usually clinical. NICE NG109 supports symptom-based diagnosis in non-pregnant women under 65 with typical symptoms, with urine testing reserved for selected cases. The consultation should stay focused on safety, antimicrobial stewardship, and clear advice on when to escalate.

Pathophysiology and why this matters
  • Ascending infection: most uncomplicated UTIs are caused by gut flora, usually E. coli, ascending from the periurethral area into the bladder.
  • Why women are affected more often: the female urethra is short and close to the anus, so bacterial ascent is easier.
  • Inflammatory symptoms: infection irritates the bladder and urethra, producing dysuria, urgency, frequency, and suprapubic discomfort.
  • Why it matters: the main danger is not the symptom cluster itself, but missing a more serious upper UTI, sepsis, pregnancy-related infection, or a different cause of urinary symptoms.
Differential diagnosis framework
Common and likely
  • Uncomplicated cystitis
  • Recurrent cystitis
  • Early or partially treated lower UTI
Often confused with UTI
  • Vaginitis: discharge, itch, odour, external soreness.
  • Urethritis / STI: dysuria with sexual risk, discharge, or new partner.
  • Overactive bladder: urgency/frequency without acute infection features.
Must not miss
  • Pyelonephritis: fever, loin pain, vomiting, rigors.
  • Sepsis: confusion, hypotension, tachycardia, marked systemic illness.
  • Pregnancy-related UTI
  • Renal stone or obstruction: colicky flank pain, haematuria, severe pain
  • Malignancy concern: persistent haematuria, especially in older adults
Focused history taking
Core urinary symptom pattern: dysuria, frequency, urgency, suprapubic pain, haematuria

Why it matters: Two or more classic symptoms make uncomplicated cystitis much more likely in non-pregnant women under 65.

Positive answer: a typical acute symptom cluster supports clinical diagnosis.

Action: move toward uncomplicated UTI management unless a red flag or alternative diagnosis is present.

Red flag screen: fever, loin pain, rigors, vomiting, confusion, breathlessness, severe unwellness

Why it matters: these features suggest upper UTI or sepsis rather than simple cystitis.

Positive answer: systemic symptoms or flank pain raise the risk of pyelonephritis or sepsis.

Action: same-day escalation, and emergency assessment if sepsis is a concern.

Vaginal and sexual history: discharge, itch, odour, dyspareunia, new partner, STI risk

Why it matters: vaginal symptoms point away from cystitis and toward vaginitis or STI-related disease.

Positive answer: discharge, itch, or odour makes pure UTI less likely; STI risk changes the pathway.

Action: consider examination, vaginal swabs, STI testing, or referral depending on the pattern.

Pregnancy, recurrence, and risk factors: pregnancy, recurrent episodes, diabetes, renal disease, immunosuppression, recent antibiotics

Why it matters: these factors increase complexity or change investigation and antibiotic choice.

Positive answer: pregnancy, recurrent UTI, or major comorbidity lowers the threshold for urine culture and senior review.

Action: adjust the management plan and avoid treating the case as a simple one-off episode.

Focused examination strategy
When examination is needed

Why it matters: simple cystitis often does not need examination, but uncertainty or red flags do.

Positive trigger: fever, flank pain, abdominal tenderness, vaginal symptoms, pregnancy, or diagnostic uncertainty.

Action: examine rather than relying purely on the symptom label.

Core observations: temperature, pulse, blood pressure, general appearance

Why it matters: vital signs reveal the sick patient early and help identify sepsis.

Positive finding: fever, tachycardia, hypotension, or toxic appearance suggests escalation.

Action: move to urgent same-day or emergency care pathways.

Abdominal and flank examination: suprapubic tenderness, loin tenderness, guarding

Why it matters: helps separate lower UTI from pyelonephritis or other abdominal pathology.

Positive finding: suprapubic tenderness supports cystitis; loin tenderness or peritonism pushes away from simple lower UTI.

Action: escalate if flank pain or systemic signs are present.

Pelvic examination only if indicated

Why it matters: a pelvic exam is not routine for clear cystitis, but may be needed if vaginal symptoms, STI concern, or alternative pathology is suspected.

Positive finding: discharge, cervical motion tenderness, or vulval inflammation points away from simple cystitis.

Action: use swabs / STI pathways or specialist review rather than treating as routine UTI.

Red flag synthesis and escalation triggers
  • Emergency same-day: suspected sepsis, hypotension, confusion, severe systemic illness, or inability to keep fluids down.
  • Urgent same-day: fever with loin pain, vomiting, rigors, pregnancy with systemic illness, or suspected pyelonephritis.
  • Prompt primary care review: recurrent UTIs, treatment failure, atypical symptoms, or persistent haematuria.
  • Alternative pathway: vaginal discharge, itch, or STI risk should move the patient away from a simple UTI pathway.
Investigations and decision points
  • No routine urine dipstick: in non-pregnant women under 65 with a typical symptom cluster.
  • Urine culture: in pregnancy, recurrent infection, treatment failure, or suspected pyelonephritis.
  • Pregnancy testing: if pregnancy is possible and it would change management.
  • Consider STI testing or vaginal swabs: if vaginal symptoms or sexual risk are present.
  • Blood tests / imaging: only if the presentation is complicated or the diagnosis is not straightforward.
Evidence and guideline pearls
  • NICE NG109 supports a symptom-led diagnosis in non-pregnant women under 65 with typical features.
  • Antimicrobial stewardship matters, but delaying treatment is not appropriate when the clinical picture is clear or the patient is higher risk.
  • Antibiotic choice should follow local resistance patterns and current UK guidance.
  • Back-up or delayed prescribing can be reasonable in selected mild cases when the patient is safe and well informed.
Diagnostic timeout and common pitfalls
  • Do not over-rely on dipsticks: they are not needed for every typical case.
  • Do not miss vaginitis or STI: urinary burning with discharge is not automatically cystitis.
  • Do not ignore recurrence: repeated “simple UTIs” need a better explanation.
  • Do not undercall systemic illness: fever and loin pain are not simple cystitis.
Management, follow-up, and safety-netting

Typical uncomplicated cystitis: offer appropriate first-line antibiotics, explain expected symptom improvement, and keep the plan short and clear.

Supportive care: simple analgesia and hydration advice can help, but they do not replace antibiotics when treatment is indicated.

Follow-up: seek review if symptoms are not improving within 48 hours, or sooner if they worsen.

Safety-netting: urgent review for fever, flank pain, vomiting, confusion, persistent haematuria, or any deterioration.

Special populations and role-aware notes
  • Pregnancy: lower threshold for urine culture and prompt treatment.
  • Older adults: presentation may be atypical, but asymptomatic bacteriuria should not be treated.
  • Children and men: cystitis is not a simple routine pathway and needs different assessment.
  • Role-aware note: community pharmacy can identify red flags and provide advice, but ongoing management and antibiotics belong in GP / primary-care prescribing pathways.
Illustrative cases
  • Case 1: 24-year-old with dysuria, frequency, no vaginal symptoms, no fever: uncomplicated cystitis.
  • Case 2: 33-year-old with dysuria, discharge, and new partner: consider vaginitis or STI rather than simple UTI.
  • Case 3: 71-year-old with confusion and fever: treat as possible complicated UTI / sepsis, not routine cystitis.
