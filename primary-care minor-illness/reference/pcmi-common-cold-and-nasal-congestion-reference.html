Common Cold and Nasal Congestion in Primary Care Minor Illness
Context: Acute viral upper respiratory symptoms first-contact assessment
Overview and key guidance

The common cold is an acute, self-limiting viral upper respiratory tract infection. Typical symptoms include blocked or runny nose, sneezing, sore throat, mild cough, and general malaise.

The first task is to decide whether this is straightforward viral rhinitis that can be managed supportively, or whether the pattern suggests influenza, COVID-19, bacterial sinusitis, pneumonia, a foreign body, or another complication that needs review.

Pathophysiology and why this matters
  • Viral mucosal inflammation: causes oedema, mucus production, and the sensation of blockage.
  • Self-limited course: symptoms usually improve without antibiotics or specific antiviral treatment.
  • Symptom overlap: fever, cough, and congestion can also appear in influenza or COVID-19.
  • Why it matters: over-treating uncomplicated colds adds side effects and does not shorten the illness meaningfully.
Differential diagnosis framework
Common and likely
  • Common cold / viral URTI: nasal blockage, sore throat, cough, and coryzal symptoms settling spontaneously.
  • Allergic rhinitis: sneezing, itch, and watery rhinorrhoea without infective systemic upset.
  • Non-specific viral pharyngitis: sore throat within a broader coryzal illness and no strong bacterial pointers.
Often confused with a cold
  • Influenza: higher fever, prominent systemic symptoms, myalgia.
  • COVID-19: variable features, exposure history, loss of taste or smell may occur.
  • Acute sinusitis: facial pain, purulent discharge, prolonged or worsening course.
Must not miss
  • [sc name="jitl-term-link" Q="Pneumonia assessment and management in primary care" text="Pneumonia"][/sc]: breathlessness, pleuritic pain, persistent fever, focal chest signs.
  • [sc name="jitl-term-link" Q="Meningitis recognition and emergency assessment" text="Meningitis"][/sc] or sepsis: severe headache, neck stiffness, photophobia, reduced responsiveness.
  • Nasal foreign body: especially unilateral foul discharge in a child.
Focused history taking
Symptom pattern: runny or blocked nose, sore throat, cough, fever, duration

Why it matters: the usual cold pattern is gradual onset with nasal and throat symptoms predominate over systemic illness.

Positive answer: prominent fever, rigors, or marked myalgia points more toward influenza or another systemic infection.

Action: support self-care for a typical cold; review or refer if the illness is severe, prolonged, or atypical.

Exposure and context: contacts, seasonal pattern, allergy history, COVID risk

Why it matters: exposure history and recurrence pattern help separate viral infection from allergic rhinitis.

Positive answer: itchy eyes, recurrent seasonal symptoms, or predictable triggers make allergic rhinitis more likely.

Action: choose self-care advice for viral illness, or allergic rhinitis treatment if the history fits that pattern better.

Risk factors and vulnerability: frailty, pregnancy, chronic disease, immunosuppression

Why it matters: complications are more concerning in people with significant comorbidity or impaired immunity.

Positive answer: breathlessness, dehydration, or reduced oral intake in a vulnerable person lowers the threshold for review.

Action: arrange a lower threshold for GP assessment or urgent review if the patient is at risk of deterioration.

Focused examination strategy
When examination is needed

Why it matters: many colds need no examination, but red flags or diagnostic uncertainty do.

Positive trigger: severe illness, breathlessness, neck stiffness, unilateral facial pain, or a child who appears unwell.

Action: examine the chest, throat, ears, and nose or arrange review if current setting cannot do this safely.

Upper airway and chest findings

Why it matters: localising signs help separate simple URTI from sinusitis, otitis, or pneumonia.

Positive finding: focal chest crackles, marked tonsillar exudate, or unilateral facial tenderness changes the working diagnosis.

Action: target management to the observed source rather than treating everything as a routine cold.

Hydration and general appearance

Why it matters: the common cold should not cause severe toxicity or dehydration.

Positive finding: tachypnoea, poor perfusion, or inability to drink suggests a more serious illness.

Action: escalate urgently if the patient looks systemically unwell.

Red flag synthesis and escalation triggers
  • Urgent / same-day: breathlessness, chest pain, confusion, dehydration, stridor, drooling, neck stiffness, or reduced consciousness.
  • Prompt GP review: prolonged illness, unilateral facial pain, severe ear pain, high fever, or worsening after initial improvement.
  • Routine self-care: mild viral symptoms with no red flags and a typical improving cold pattern.
Investigations and decision points
No routine tests

uncomplicated colds usually need no investigations.

Consider COVID or flu testing

when local advice, vulnerability, or public health guidance makes it relevant.

Consider sinusitis review

if symptoms are prolonged, severe, or facial pain becomes prominent.

Consider chest assessment

if cough is persistent or breathlessness develops.

Evidence and guideline pearls
Antibiotics do not help uncomplicated common cold

Antibiotics do not help uncomplicated common cold symptoms.

Analgesia, fluids, rest

Analgesia, fluids, rest, and simple nasal saline measures are usually the most helpful options.

Decongestants may provide short-term relief for

Decongestants may provide short-term relief for some adults, but should be used cautiously because of adverse effects and contraindications.

Children need age-appropriate symptomatic advice

Children need age-appropriate symptomatic advice and careful safety-netting rather than routine medication escalation.

Diagnostic timeout and common pitfalls
  • Do not label allergic rhinitis as a cold without checking for itch and recurrence.
  • Do not miss influenza, COVID-19, or bacterial sinusitis when fever or facial pain is prominent.
  • Do not recommend antibiotics for an uncomplicated viral cold.
  • Do not overuse oral decongestants in patients where they may be unsuitable.
Management, follow-up, and safety-netting
Supportive care

offer reassurance, rest, fluids, salt-water or saline nasal measures, and simple analgesia.

Nasal congestion

short-term symptomatic decongestant advice may be reasonable for some adults, but keep advice conservative and time-limited.

Safety-netting

seek review if symptoms worsen, fever becomes high, breathing becomes difficult, or pain localises to the face or chest.

Special populations and role-aware notes
  • Children: use simple supportive advice and a lower threshold for review if they look unwell.
  • Pregnancy: keep medicines cautious and prefer non-drug measures where possible.
  • Older adults or frail patients: have a lower threshold for assessing dehydration, chest infection, or deterioration.
  • Community pharmacy: appropriate for simple self-limiting symptoms, but persistent, severe, or atypical illness should be moved into GP assessment.
Illustrative cases
  • Case 1: adult with 3 days of runny nose, mild sore throat, and cough, afebrile and improving: routine common cold.
  • Case 2: child with unilateral foul nasal discharge and blocked nose: consider nasal foreign body rather than a simple cold.
  • Case 3: adult with fever, facial pain, and symptoms worsening after a week: review for sinusitis or another complication.
