Warts and Verrucas in Primary Care
Context: Benign HPV Skin Lesions First-Contact Assessment
Overview and key guidance

Common warts and verrucas are benign epidermal lesions caused by human papillomavirus. The first-contact task is to confirm a likely wart, distinguish it from corns, callus, or a suspicious skin lesion, and then decide whether no treatment, salicylic acid, or referral is the safest approach.

Many warts resolve spontaneously, especially in children. Because treatment can be slow and irritating, the safest consultation includes a clear decision about whether treatment is actually needed, where the lesion is located, and whether the patient has any reason why destructive treatment would be unsafe.

Pathophysiology and why this matters
  • Cause: HPV infects the epidermis and stimulates keratinocyte proliferation.
  • Appearance: rough papules or plaques may show thrombosed capillaries as black dots.
  • Natural history: many resolve gradually via cell-mediated immune clearance.
  • Why it matters: not every lesion needs treatment, but some lesions should not be treated as routine warts at all.
Differential diagnosis framework
Common and likely
  • Common wart
  • Verruca
  • Flat wart
Often confused with warts or verrucas
  • Corn or callus: pressure lesion, no thrombosed capillaries.
  • Seborrhoeic keratosis: especially in older adults.
  • Molluscum contagiosum: central umbilication rather than a rough hyperkeratotic surface.
Must not miss
  • Suspicious skin lesion / malignancy: bleeding, ulceration, rapid change.
  • Anogenital warts: different pathway and no OTC destructive treatment.
  • Diabetic or vascular-risk foot lesion: do not treat as routine verruca.
Focused history taking
Lesion pattern and site: hand or foot, pain on pressure, rough surface, black dots, duration

Why it matters: the site and appearance help distinguish a verruca from a corn or another lesion.

Positive answer: rough hyperkeratotic lesion with thrombosed capillaries supports a wart; pure pressure pain without wart features suggests a corn.

Action: decide whether the lesion is suitable for routine treatment or needs a different pathway.

Change and red flags: bleeding, rapid growth, ulceration, colour change, uncertainty

Why it matters: suspicious lesions should not be treated destructively before the diagnosis is secure.

Positive answer: any unexplained change or bleeding lowers confidence in a benign wart diagnosis.

Action: refer rather than recommending OTC wart treatment.

Risk factors and contraindications: diabetes, peripheral vascular disease, neuropathy, immunosuppression, anogenital location

Why it matters: these factors can make routine salicylic acid or freezing unsafe or inappropriate.

Positive answer: diabetic foot risk or immunocompromise makes simple OTC destruction less safe.

Action: refer to GP or podiatry rather than supplying routine treatment.

Focused examination strategy
Inspect morphology: rough keratotic surface, interruption of skin lines, thrombosed capillaries

Why it matters: morphology increases confidence that the lesion is a wart rather than a pressure lesion.

Positive finding: black dots and interrupted dermatoglyphics support verruca.

Action: proceed with conservative or topical management if the rest of the assessment is low risk.

Check site-specific risk: plantar lesion, facial lesion, periungual lesion, anogenital lesion

Why it matters: treatment safety depends heavily on lesion location.

Positive finding: plantar lesions in low-risk patients may be suitable for salicylic acid; facial or anogenital lesions are not.

Action: choose no treatment, topical treatment, or referral appropriately.

Look for reasons not to treat routinely: ulceration, infection, vascular compromise, uncertain diagnosis

Why it matters: destructive treatment can do harm if the lesion or patient is high risk.

Positive finding: ulceration, erythema, or poor foot health moves the case away from OTC management.

Action: refer instead of continuing with home destruction.

Red flag synthesis and escalation triggers
  • Prompt referral: suspicious change, bleeding, ulceration, or diagnostic uncertainty.
  • Do not manage as routine verruca: anogenital lesion, diabetic / vascular-risk foot lesion, or immunocompromised patient.
  • Routine conservative / topical care: typical low-risk common wart or verruca.

Diagnostic timeout and common pitfalls
  • Do not freeze or cauterise an uncertain lesion at home.
  • Do not treat diabetic or vascular-risk foot lesions as routine verrucas.
  • Do not forget that no treatment is sometimes the safest choice.
Management, follow-up, and safety-netting
Low-risk lesions

consider watchful waiting or salicylic acid with clear instructions and persistence.

Counselling

protect surrounding skin, use treatment regularly, and expect a slow response.

Safety-netting

seek review if the lesion changes, bleeds, becomes infected, or does not behave like a simple wart.

Special populations and role-aware notes
  • Children: spontaneous resolution is common and treatment may be more burdensome than helpful.
  • Older adults: keep a higher index of suspicion for non-wart lesions.
  • Pregnancy / breastfeeding: stay conservative and check product safety if treatment is chosen.
  • Role-aware note: pharmacy can manage typical low-risk warts, but risky sites or risky feet need referral.
