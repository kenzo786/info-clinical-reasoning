[sc name="map-steps-and-links" id="1292" anchor="110" title="Management" color="blue" step="10" info-modal="" pathway="display-none" youtube="display-none"][/sc]<details class="kb-accordion kb-parent d-blue" open> <summary id="s-is.110" role="button">Management and safety-netting</summary> <ul> <li><strong>Uncomplicated illness:</strong> oral rehydration, rest, and normal diet as tolerated.</li> <li><strong>Adult symptom control:</strong> loperamide may be used only when diarrhoea is watery and red flags are absent.</li> <li><strong>Safety-net:</strong> seek urgent help for blood in stool, worsening abdominal pain, fever, dehydration, confusion, or diarrhoea that does not settle.</li> </ul> </details> [sc name="jitl-link-10" Q="Acute Diarrhoea"][/sc]
[sc name="map-steps-and-links" id="1292" anchor="111" title="Special Populations" color="blue" step="11" info-modal="" pathway="display-none" youtube="display-none"][/sc]<details class="kb-accordion kb-parent d-blue" open> <summary id="s-is.111" role="button">Special populations and role-aware notes</summary> <ul> <li><strong>Children:</strong> dehydration risk is higher and ORS should be used early.</li> <li><strong>Older adults:</strong> lower threshold for urgent review because dehydration and sepsis can evolve quickly.</li> <li><strong>Recent antibiotics or IBD:</strong> avoid loperamide and seek medical review.</li> <li><strong>Role-aware note:</strong> community pharmacy can support ORS advice only if the patient is clearly low risk and well enough for home care.</li> </ul> </details>
