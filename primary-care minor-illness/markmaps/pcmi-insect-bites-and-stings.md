[sc name="map-steps-and-links" id="1290" anchor="101" title="Overview" color="green" step="1" info-modal="" pathway="display-none" youtube="display-none"][/sc]<details class="kb-accordion kb-parent d-green" open> <summary id="s-is.101" role="button">Overview</summary> <ul> <li><strong>Clinical challenge:</strong> most reactions are simple and short-lived, but anaphylaxis, cellulitis, severe local reactions, and tick bites need active exclusion.</li> <li><strong>Core stance:</strong> start with airway, circulation, and infection risk before treating itch.</li> <li><strong>Expected course:</strong> simple reactions usually settle over hours to a few days.</li> </ul> </details>
[sc name="map-steps-and-links" id="1290" anchor="102" title="Pathophysiology" color="green" step="2" info-modal="" pathway="display-none" youtube="display-none"][/sc]<details class="kb-accordion kb-parent d-green" open> <summary id="s-is.102" role="button">Pathophysiology</summary> <ul> <li><strong>Simple reaction:</strong> local histamine release causes itch, redness, and swelling.</li> <li><strong>Large local reaction:</strong> allergic inflammation can look dramatic without being infection.</li> <li><strong>Anaphylaxis:</strong> a systemic allergic reaction with airway and circulatory risk.</li> </ul></details>
[sc name="map-steps-and-links" id="1290" anchor="103" title="Differential Diagnosis" color="purple" step="3" info-modal="" pathway="display-none" youtube="display-none"][/sc]<details class="kb-accordion kb-parent d-purple" open> <summary id="s-is.103" role="button">Differential diagnosis framework</summary> <ul class="no-bullet"> <li>[sc name="link-websheet" id="1290" anchor="103.1"][/sc]<strong>Common and likely:</strong> simple local reaction, large local reaction, papular urticaria.</li> <li>[sc name="link-websheet" id="1290" anchor="103.2"][/sc]<strong>Often confused with bites:</strong> contact dermatitis, urticaria, scabies.</li> <li>[sc name="link-websheet" id="1290" anchor="103.3"][/sc]<strong>Must not miss:</strong> anaphylaxis, cellulitis, mouth or throat stings, tick bite with Lyme follow-up.</li> </ul> </details>
[sc name="map-steps-and-links" id="1290" anchor="104" title="History" color="yellow" step="4" info-modal="" pathway="display-none" youtube="display-none"][/sc]<details class="kb-accordion kb-parent d-yellow" open> <summary id="s-is.104" role="button">Focused history taking</summary> <ul class="no-bullet"> <li>[sc name="link-websheet" id="1290" anchor="104.1"][/sc]<strong>Event details:</strong> what bit or stung the patient, and when?</li> <li>[sc name="link-websheet" id="1290" anchor="104.2"][/sc]<strong>Airway and systemic symptoms:</strong> wheeze, stridor, swelling, dizziness, collapse.</li> <li>[sc name="link-websheet" id="1290" anchor="104.3"][/sc]<strong>Infection screen:</strong> spreading redness, warmth, tenderness, fever, pus.</li> <li>[sc name="link-websheet" id="1290" anchor="104.4"][/sc]<strong>Tick-bite and risk history:</strong> tick exposure, fever, headache, neck stiffness, pregnancy, previous anaphylaxis.</li> </ul> </details>
[sc name="map-steps-and-links" id="1290" anchor="105" title="Examination" color="yellow" step="5" info-modal="" pathway="display-none" youtube="display-none"][/sc]<details class="kb-accordion kb-parent d-yellow" open> <summary id="s-is.105" role="button">Focused examination strategy</summary> <ul class="no-bullet"> <li>[sc name="link-websheet" id="1290" anchor="105.1"][/sc]<strong>When to examine:</strong> facial swelling, respiratory distress, large reaction, or infection signs.</li> <li>[sc name="link-websheet" id="1290" anchor="105.2"][/sc]<strong>Reaction inspection:</strong> size, itch, tenderness, blistering, and whether a stinger remains.</li> <li>[sc name="link-websheet" id="1290" anchor="105.3"][/sc]<strong>Observation and vital signs:</strong> breathing, circulation, and fever when infection is suspected.</li> </ul> </details>
[sc name="map-steps-and-links" id="1290" anchor="106" title="Red Flags" color="pink" step="6" info-modal="" pathway="display-none" youtube="display-none"][/sc]<details class="kb-accordion kb-parent d-pink" open> <summary id="s-is.106" role="button">Red flag synthesis</summary> <ul class="no-bullet"> <li><strong>Emergency:</strong> any signs of anaphylaxis, including wheeze, stridor, angioedema, hypotension, or collapse.</li> <li><strong>Urgent GP review:</strong> spreading cellulitis, high-risk sting location, severe local swelling, or a tick bite with later systemic symptoms.</li> <li><strong>Prompt review:</strong> unusually painful, blistering, recurrent, or not-improving reactions.</li> </ul> </details>
[sc name="map-steps-and-links" id="1290" anchor="107" title="Investigations" color="yellow" step="7" info-modal="" pathway="display-none" youtube="display-none"][/sc]<details class="kb-accordion kb-parent d-yellow" open> <summary id="s-is.107" role="button">Investigations and decision points</summary> <ul class="no-bullet"> <li>[sc name="link-websheet" id="1290" anchor="107"][/sc]<strong>No routine tests</strong> for a simple bite or sting.</li> <li>[sc name="link-websheet" id="1290" anchor="107"][/sc]<strong>Assess for cellulitis</strong> if redness is spreading or the area is tender and warm.</li> <li>[sc name="link-websheet" id="1290" anchor="107"][/sc]<strong>Tick-bite follow-up</strong> if a migrating rash or systemic symptoms develop later.</li> <li>[sc name="link-websheet" id="1290" anchor="107"][/sc]<strong>Specialist allergy review</strong> if a true systemic reaction has occurred.</li> </ul> </details>
[sc name="map-steps-and-links" id="1290" anchor="108" title="Evidence & Guidance" color="purple" step="8" info-modal="" pathway="display-none" youtube="display-none"][/sc]<details class="kb-accordion kb-parent d-purple" open> <summary id="s-is.108" role="button">Evidence and guideline pearls</summary> <ul class="no-bullet"> <li>[sc name="link-websheet" id="1290" anchor="108"][/sc]Most bites and stings are self-limiting and can be treated symptomatically.</li> <li>[sc name="link-websheet" id="1290" anchor="108"][/sc]Large local reactions are allergic, not infectious, so antibiotics are usually not needed.</li> <li>[sc name="link-websheet" id="1290" anchor="108"][/sc]Antibiotics are reserved for clear secondary cellulitis.</li> <li>[sc name="link-websheet" id="1290" anchor="108"][/sc]A systemic allergic reaction needs emergency treatment, not antihistamines alone.</li> </ul> </details>
[sc name="map-steps-and-links" id="1290" anchor="109" title="Diagnostic Timeout" color="pink" step="9" info-modal="" pathway="display-none" youtube="display-none"][/sc]<details class="kb-accordion kb-parent d-pink" open> <summary id="s-is.109" role="button">Diagnostic timeout &amp; cognitive biases</summary> <ul> <li><strong>Do not confuse large local allergy with cellulitis.</strong></li> <li><strong>Do not miss anaphylaxis because the skin reaction looks modest.</strong></li> <li><strong>Do not give antibiotics for uncomplicated bites.</strong></li> <li><strong>Do not forget tick bites and later Lyme disease symptoms.</strong></li> </ul> </details>
[sc name="map-steps-and-links" id="1290" anchor="110" title="Management" color="blue" step="10" info-modal="" pathway="display-none" youtube="display-none"][/sc]<details class="kb-accordion kb-parent d-blue" open> <summary id="s-is.110" role="button">Management and safety-netting</summary> <ul class="no-bullet"> <li>[sc name="link-websheet" id="1290" anchor="110"][/sc]<strong>Simple local reaction:</strong> wash the area, use a cold compress, elevate if possible, and avoid scratching.</li> <li>[sc name="link-websheet" id="1290" anchor="110"][/sc]<strong>Symptom relief:</strong> oral antihistamines and mild topical hydrocortisone can help itch.</li> <li>[sc name="link-websheet" id="1290" anchor="110"][/sc]<strong>Escalation:</strong> cellulitis needs GP review, and any anaphylaxis symptoms need emergency help immediately.</li> <li>[sc name="link-websheet" id="1290" anchor="110"][/sc]<strong>Safety-netting:</strong> seek review if swelling spreads, fever develops, or a later target-like rash appears after a tick bite.</li> </ul> </details>
[sc name="map-steps-and-links" id="1290" anchor="111" title="Special Populations" color="blue" step="11" info-modal="" pathway="display-none" youtube="display-none"][/sc]<details class="kb-accordion kb-parent d-blue" open> <summary id="s-is.111" role="button">Special populations and role-aware notes</summary> <ul class="no-bullet"> <li>[sc name="link-websheet" id="1290" anchor="111"][/sc]<strong>Children:</strong> papular urticaria is common and scratching can lead to secondary infection.</li> <li>[sc name="link-websheet" id="1290" anchor="111"][/sc]<strong>Older adults:</strong> sedating antihistamines can increase falls risk.</li> <li>[sc name="link-websheet" id="1290" anchor="111"][/sc]<strong>Pregnancy and breastfeeding:</strong> conservative symptom relief is preferred; choose antihistamines cautiously if needed.</li> <li>[sc name="link-websheet" id="1290" anchor="111"][/sc]<strong>History of anaphylaxis:</strong> treat any new sting as higher risk.</li> </ul> </details>
[sc name="map-steps-and-links" id="1290" anchor="112" title="Illustrative Cases" color="grey" step="12" info-modal="" pathway="display-none" youtube="display-none"][/sc]<details class="kb-accordion kb-parent d-grey" open> <summary id="s-is.112" role="button">Illustrative cases</summary> <ul class="no-bullet"> <li>[sc name="link-websheet" id="1290" anchor="112"][/sc]<strong>Case 1:</strong> itchy swollen bite after a picnic, no fever or breathing problems: simple local reaction.</li> <li>[sc name="link-websheet" id="1290" anchor="112"][/sc]<strong>Case 2:</strong> rapidly spreading tender redness and fever after a scratch: possible cellulitis needing GP review.</li> <li>[sc name="link-websheet" id="1290" anchor="112"][/sc]<strong>Case 3:</strong> wheeze and lip swelling minutes after a wasp sting: anaphylaxis and emergency escalation.</li> </ul> </details>
