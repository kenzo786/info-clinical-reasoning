[sc name="map-steps-and-links" id="1276" anchor="101" title="Overview" color="green" step="1" info-modal="" pathway="display-none" youtube="display-none"][/sc]<details class="kb-accordion kb-parent d-green" open> <summary id="s-is.101" role="button">Overview</summary> <ul> <li><strong>Clinical challenge:</strong> distinguish classic candidiasis from BV, STI-related discharge, vulval dermatoses, PID, and recurrent complicated disease.</li> <li><strong>Guideline frame:</strong> uncomplicated episodes can be treated empirically, but atypical patterns need examination, swabs, pregnancy-specific advice, or STI-focused assessment.</li> <li><strong>Core stance:</strong> not every itchy discharge is thrush, and repeated empirical treatment can hide a different diagnosis.</li> </ul> </details>
[sc name="map-steps-and-links" id="1276" anchor="102" title="Pathophysiology" color="green" step="2" info-modal="" pathway="display-none" youtube="display-none"][/sc]<details class="kb-accordion kb-parent d-green" open> <summary id="s-is.102" role="button">Pathophysiology/basic science</summary> <ul> <li><strong>Candida overgrowth:</strong> usually reflects overgrowth of a commensal yeast rather than an STI.</li> <li><strong>Inflammatory response:</strong> drives itch, soreness, erythema, fissuring, and thick white discharge.</li> <li><strong>Predisposing factors:</strong> antibiotics, pregnancy, diabetes, immunosuppression, and some hormonal states.</li> <li><strong>Why it matters:</strong> symptom pattern and recurrence history help separate candidiasis from mimics.</li> </ul></details>
[sc name="map-steps-and-links" id="1276" anchor="103" title="Differential Diagnosis" color="purple" step="3" info-modal="" pathway="display-none" youtube="display-none"][/sc]<details class="kb-accordion kb-parent d-purple" open> <summary id="s-is.103" role="button">Differential diagnosis (ddx) framework</summary> <ul class="no-bullet"> <li>[sc name="link-websheet" id="1276" anchor="103.1"][/sc]<strong>Common and likely:</strong> uncomplicated candidiasis, recurrent candidiasis, irritant or allergic vulvitis.</li> <li>[sc name="link-websheet" id="1276" anchor="103.2"][/sc]<strong>Often confused with thrush:</strong> BV, trichomonas / STI-related vaginitis, atrophic vaginitis.</li> <li>[sc name="link-websheet" id="1276" anchor="103.3"][/sc]<strong>Must not miss:</strong> PID, herpes or dermatoses, VIN or cancer concern.</li> </ul> </details>
[sc name="map-steps-and-links" id="1276" anchor="104" title="History" color="yellow" step="4" info-modal="" pathway="display-none" youtube="display-none"][/sc]<details class="kb-accordion kb-parent d-yellow" open> <summary id="s-is.104" role="button">History taking</summary> <ul class="no-bullet"> <li>[sc name="link-websheet" id="1276" anchor="104.1"][/sc]<strong>Symptom pattern</strong>: itch, soreness, discharge, dysuria, dyspareunia.</li> <li>[sc name="link-websheet" id="1276" anchor="104.2"][/sc]<strong>Episode frequency and previous response</strong>: first episode, recurrence, or treatment failure?</li> <li>[sc name="link-websheet" id="1276" anchor="104.3"][/sc]<strong>Predisposing factors and medication history</strong>: antibiotics, diabetes, pregnancy, immunosuppression, interacting drugs.</li> </ul> </details>

[sc name="map-steps-and-links" id="1276" anchor="106" title="Red Flags" color="pink" step="6" info-modal="" pathway="display-none" youtube="display-none"][/sc]<details class="kb-accordion kb-parent d-pink" open> <summary id="s-is.106" role="button">Red flag synthesis</summary> <ul class="no-bullet"> <li><strong>Urgent / same-day:</strong> pelvic pain, fever, severe abdominal pain, post-menopausal bleeding, or severe vulval lesions.</li> <li><strong>Prompt review:</strong> recurrent episodes, treatment failure, STI concern, age outside OTC range, or persistent vulval skin change.</li> <li><strong>Routine empirical treatment:</strong> classic uncomplicated episode in a patient with a known pattern and no red flags.</li> </ul> </details>
[sc name="map-steps-and-links" id="1276" anchor="107" title="Investigations" color="yellow" step="7" info-modal="" pathway="display-none" youtube="display-none"][/sc]<details class="kb-accordion kb-parent d-yellow" open> <summary id="s-is.107" role="button">Investigations strategy &amp; interpretation</summary> <ul class="no-bullet"> <li>[sc name="link-websheet" id="1276" anchor="107"][/sc]<strong>No routine swab</strong> for every classic uncomplicated case.</li> <li>[sc name="link-websheet" id="1276" anchor="107"][/sc]<strong>High vaginal swab</strong> for recurrent, persistent, or atypical symptoms.</li> <li>[sc name="link-websheet" id="1276" anchor="107"][/sc]<strong>Pregnancy testing</strong> if pregnancy is possible and treatment choices need clarifying.</li> <li>[sc name="link-websheet" id="1276" anchor="107"][/sc]<strong>HbA1c or diabetes review</strong> if recurrent symptoms or poor glycaemic control are suspected.</li> </ul> </details>
[sc name="map-steps-and-links" id="1276" anchor="108" title="Evidence & Guidance" color="purple" step="8" info-modal="" pathway="display-none" youtube="display-none"][/sc]<details class="kb-accordion kb-parent d-purple" open> <summary id="s-is.108" role="button">Evidence and guideline pearls</summary> <ul class="no-bullet"> <li>[sc name="link-websheet" id="1276" anchor="108"][/sc]Short-course topical azoles and single-dose oral fluconazole both work for uncomplicated thrush in non-pregnant adults.</li> <li>[sc name="link-websheet" id="1276" anchor="108"][/sc]Oral azoles should generally be avoided in pregnancy; topical treatment is preferred.</li> <li>[sc name="link-websheet" id="1276" anchor="108"][/sc]Partner treatment is not needed unless the partner has symptoms.</li> <li>[sc name="link-websheet" id="1276" anchor="108"][/sc]Recurrent disease should be confirmed and planned, not endlessly re-labelled as another acute episode.</li> </ul> </details>
[sc name="map-steps-and-links" id="1276" anchor="109" title="Diagnostic Timeout" color="pink" step="9" info-modal="" pathway="display-none" youtube="display-none"][/sc]<details class="kb-accordion kb-parent d-pink" open> <summary id="s-is.109" role="button">Diagnostic timeout &amp; cognitive biases</summary> <ul> <li><strong>Do not call every vaginal itch thrush.</strong></li> <li><strong>Do not miss STI, BV, or dermatoses because the patient has had thrush before.</strong></li> <li><strong>Do not keep re-treating recurrent symptoms without confirming the diagnosis.</strong></li> <li><strong>Do not forget fluconazole interactions and pregnancy status.</strong></li> </ul> </details>
[sc name="map-steps-and-links" id="1276" anchor="110" title="Management" color="blue" step="10" info-modal="" pathway="display-none" youtube="display-none"][/sc]<details class="kb-accordion kb-parent d-blue" open> <summary id="s-is.110" role="button">Management and safety-netting</summary> <ul class="no-bullet"> <li>[sc name="link-websheet" id="1276" anchor="110"][/sc]<strong>Uncomplicated disease:</strong> azole treatment plus vulval skin-care advice and realistic time to improvement.</li> <li>[sc name="link-websheet" id="1276" anchor="110"][/sc]<strong>Recurrent or complicated disease:</strong> examine, swab, and make a structured plan rather than repeated self-treatment.</li> <li>[sc name="link-websheet" id="1276" anchor="110"][/sc]<strong>Urgent return:</strong> symptoms that fail to improve, recur quickly, or are joined by pelvic pain, fever, bleeding, or malodour.</li> </ul> </details>
[sc name="map-steps-and-links" id="1276" anchor="111" title="Special Populations" color="blue" step="11" info-modal="" pathway="display-none" youtube="display-none"][/sc]<details class="kb-accordion kb-parent d-blue" open> <summary id="s-is.111" role="button">Special populations and role-aware notes</summary> <ul class="no-bullet"> <li>[sc name="link-websheet" id="1276" anchor="111"][/sc]<strong>Children:</strong> candidiasis is less common and the differential is broader.</li> <li>[sc name="link-websheet" id="1276" anchor="111"][/sc]<strong>Older / post-menopausal adults:</strong> think atrophic vaginitis and vulval dermatoses early.</li> <li>[sc name="link-websheet" id="1276" anchor="111"][/sc]<strong>Pregnancy:</strong> use topical therapy and lower the threshold for confirming the diagnosis.</li> <li>[sc name="link-websheet" id="1276" anchor="111"][/sc]<strong>Role-aware note:</strong> community pharmacy can help with clearly uncomplicated patterns in the licensed range, but atypical symptoms, recurrence, pregnancy complexity, or STI concern should move to GP or sexual-health assessment.</li> </ul> </details>
