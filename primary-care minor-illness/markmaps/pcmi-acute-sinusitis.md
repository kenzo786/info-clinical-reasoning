[sc name="map-steps-and-links" id="1283" anchor="101" title="Overview" color="green" step="1" info-modal="" pathway="display-none" youtube="display-none"][/sc]<details class="kb-accordion kb-parent d-green" open> <summary id="s-is.101" role="button">Overview</summary> <ul> <li><strong>Clinical challenge:</strong> Most acute sinusitis is self-limiting after a viral URTI, but you must exclude orbital spread, intracranial spread, and structural causes.</li> <li><strong>Core stance:</strong> start with symptoms, duration, and red flags before reaching for antibiotics.</li> <li><strong>Expected course:</strong> symptoms commonly last 2 to 3 weeks.</li> </ul> </details>
[sc name="map-steps-and-links" id="1283" anchor="102" title="Pathophysiology" color="green" step="2" info-modal="" pathway="display-none" youtube="display-none"][/sc]<details class="kb-accordion kb-parent d-green" open> <summary id="s-is.102" role="button">Pathophysiology</summary> <ul> <li><strong>Trigger:</strong> viral URTI causes mucosal inflammation.</li> <li><strong>Mechanism:</strong> swollen mucosa blocks sinus drainage and traps mucus.</li> <li><strong>Why it matters:</strong> the first-line response is usually self-care, not immediate antibiotics.</li> </ul></details>
[sc name="map-steps-and-links" id="1283" anchor="103" title="Differential Diagnosis" color="purple" step="3" info-modal="" pathway="display-none" youtube="display-none"][/sc]<details class="kb-accordion kb-parent d-purple" open> <summary id="s-is.103" role="button">Differential diagnosis framework</summary> <ul class="no-bullet"> <li>[sc name="link-websheet" id="1283" anchor="103.1"][/sc]<strong>Common and likely:</strong> viral URTI, allergic rhinitis, uncomplicated rhinosinusitis.</li> <li>[sc name="link-websheet" id="1283" anchor="103.2"][/sc]<strong>Often confused with sinusitis:</strong> dental abscess, migraine or facial pain syndromes.</li> <li>[sc name="link-websheet" id="1283" anchor="103.3"][/sc]<strong>Must not miss:</strong> orbital cellulitis, intracranial infection, nasal tumour.</li> </ul> </details>
[sc name="map-steps-and-links" id="1283" anchor="104" title="History" color="yellow" step="4" info-modal="" pathway="display-none" youtube="display-none"][/sc]<details class="kb-accordion kb-parent d-yellow" open> <summary id="s-is.104" role="button">Focused history taking</summary> <ul class="no-bullet"> <li>[sc name="link-websheet" id="1283" anchor="104.1"][/sc]<strong>Symptom pattern:</strong> congestion, discharge, facial pressure, smell loss.</li> <li>[sc name="link-websheet" id="1283" anchor="104.2"][/sc]<strong>Duration and course:</strong> less than 10 days is usually viral; persistent symptoms need review.</li> <li>[sc name="link-websheet" id="1283" anchor="104.3"][/sc]<strong>Red-flag screen:</strong> eye symptoms, severe headache, neck stiffness, altered consciousness, unilateral blood-stained discharge.</li> <li>[sc name="link-websheet" id="1283" anchor="104.4"][/sc]<strong>Background risk:</strong> immunosuppression, pregnancy, asthma/allergy, previous episodes, and what has already been tried.</li> </ul> </details>
[sc name="map-steps-and-links" id="1283" anchor="105" title="Examination" color="yellow" step="5" info-modal="" pathway="display-none" youtube="display-none"][/sc]<details class="kb-accordion kb-parent d-yellow" open> <summary id="s-is.105" role="button">Focused examination strategy</summary> <ul class="no-bullet"> <li>[sc name="link-websheet" id="1283" anchor="105.1"][/sc]<strong>When to examine:</strong> severe, uncertain, unilateral, or red-flag presentations.</li> <li>[sc name="link-websheet" id="1283" anchor="105.2"][/sc]<strong>Nasal and facial exam:</strong> inflamed mucosa or tenderness supports sinusitis; eye swelling or painful eye movements are concerning.</li> <li>[sc name="link-websheet" id="1283" anchor="105.3"][/sc]<strong>General observations:</strong> fever or a very unwell appearance raises the urgency.</li> </ul> </details>
[sc name="map-steps-and-links" id="1283" anchor="106" title="Red Flags" color="pink" step="6" info-modal="" pathway="display-none" youtube="display-none"][/sc]<details class="kb-accordion kb-parent d-pink" open> <summary id="s-is.106" role="button">Red flag synthesis</summary> <ul class="no-bullet"> <li><strong>Emergency:</strong> periorbital swelling, painful or restricted eye movements, visual loss, proptosis, severe frontal headache, meningism, altered consciousness, or focal neurology.</li> <li><strong>Urgent GP / ENT:</strong> persistent unilateral symptoms, blood-stained discharge, recurrent disease, or symptoms not improving after about 10 days.</li> <li><strong>Same-day escalation:</strong> high fever, sepsis features, or a patient who looks systemically very unwell.</li> </ul> </details>
[sc name="map-steps-and-links" id="1283" anchor="107" title="Investigations" color="yellow" step="7" info-modal="" pathway="display-none" youtube="display-none"][/sc]<details class="kb-accordion kb-parent d-yellow" open> <summary id="s-is.107" role="button">Investigations and decision points</summary> <ul class="no-bullet"> <li>[sc name="link-websheet" id="1283" anchor="107"][/sc]<strong>No routine tests</strong> for most uncomplicated cases.</li> <li>[sc name="link-websheet" id="1283" anchor="107"][/sc]<strong>Consider review or referral</strong> if the diagnosis is uncertain or red flags are present.</li> <li>[sc name="link-websheet" id="1283" anchor="107"][/sc]<strong>Persistent symptoms beyond 10 days</strong> support GP review and possible prescription treatment.</li> <li>[sc name="link-websheet" id="1283" anchor="107"][/sc]<strong>Use local pharmacy pathway criteria</strong> only if the case stays uncomplicated.</li> </ul> </details>
[sc name="map-steps-and-links" id="1283" anchor="108" title="Evidence & Guidance" color="purple" step="8" info-modal="" pathway="display-none" youtube="display-none"][/sc]<details class="kb-accordion kb-parent d-purple" open> <summary id="s-is.108" role="button">Evidence and guideline pearls</summary> <ul class="no-bullet"> <li>[sc name="link-websheet" id="1283" anchor="108"][/sc]Most cases begin with viral URTI and resolve without antibiotics.</li> <li>[sc name="link-websheet" id="1283" anchor="108"][/sc]Intranasal corticosteroids are a reasonable option when symptoms persist beyond 10 days.</li> <li>[sc name="link-websheet" id="1283" anchor="108"][/sc]Antibiotics are not needed for symptoms under 10 days unless the patient is systemically very unwell or high risk.</li> <li>[sc name="link-websheet" id="1283" anchor="108"][/sc]Delayed antibiotics are generally safer than immediate antibiotics for many persistent but uncomplicated cases.</li> </ul> </details>
[sc name="map-steps-and-links" id="1283" anchor="109" title="Diagnostic Timeout" color="pink" step="9" info-modal="" pathway="display-none" youtube="display-none"][/sc]<details class="kb-accordion kb-parent d-pink" open> <summary id="s-is.109" role="button">Diagnostic timeout &amp; cognitive biases</summary> <ul> <li><strong>Do not equate green discharge with bacterial infection.</strong></li> <li><strong>Do not miss dental pain, migraine, or allergy when the nose is blocked.</strong></li> <li><strong>Do not keep recommending decongestants beyond 7 days.</strong></li> <li><strong>Do not miss eye symptoms, severe headache, or unilateral blood-stained discharge.</strong></li> </ul> </details>
[sc name="map-steps-and-links" id="1283" anchor="110" title="Management" color="blue" step="10" info-modal="" pathway="display-none" youtube="display-none"][/sc]<details class="kb-accordion kb-parent d-blue" open> <summary id="s-is.110" role="button">Management and safety-netting</summary> <ul class="no-bullet"> <li>[sc name="link-websheet" id="1283" anchor="110"][/sc]<strong>Uncomplicated disease:</strong> reassurance, rest, hydration, analgesia, and saline sprays or irrigation.</li> <li>[sc name="link-websheet" id="1283" anchor="110"][/sc]<strong>Topical decongestants:</strong> short-term relief only, with a maximum of 7 days.</li> <li>[sc name="link-websheet" id="1283" anchor="110"][/sc]<strong>Prescription options:</strong> intranasal corticosteroids if symptoms persist; antibiotics only for severe, worsening, or high-risk cases.</li> <li>[sc name="link-websheet" id="1283" anchor="110"][/sc]<strong>Urgent return:</strong> eye swelling, vision change, severe headache, meningism, confusion, or rapid deterioration.</li> </ul> </details>
[sc name="map-steps-and-links" id="1283" anchor="111" title="Special Populations" color="blue" step="11" info-modal="" pathway="display-none" youtube="display-none"][/sc]<details class="kb-accordion kb-parent d-blue" open> <summary id="s-is.111" role="button">Special populations and role-aware notes</summary> <ul class="no-bullet"> <li>[sc name="link-websheet" id="1283" anchor="111"][/sc]<strong>Children:</strong> lower threshold for referral if eye swelling or marked illness is present.</li> <li>[sc name="link-websheet" id="1283" anchor="111"][/sc]<strong>Older adults and immunosuppressed patients:</strong> keep a lower threshold for investigation and escalation.</li> <li>[sc name="link-websheet" id="1283" anchor="111"][/sc]<strong>Pregnancy and breastfeeding:</strong> favour self-care and avoid casual decongestant use.</li> <li>[sc name="link-websheet" id="1283" anchor="111"][/sc]<strong>Role-aware note:</strong> primary care or pharmacy management is only suitable when the picture remains uncomplicated.</li> </ul> </details>
[sc name="map-steps-and-links" id="1283" anchor="112" title="Illustrative Cases" color="grey" step="12" info-modal="" pathway="display-none" youtube="display-none"][/sc]<details class="kb-accordion kb-parent d-grey" open> <summary id="s-is.112" role="button">Illustrative cases</summary> <ul class="no-bullet"> <li>[sc name="link-websheet" id="1283" anchor="112"][/sc]<strong>Case 1:</strong> 34-year-old with 6 days of blocked nose and facial pressure after a cold, afebrile, no red flags: uncomplicated acute sinusitis.</li> <li>[sc name="link-websheet" id="1283" anchor="112"][/sc]<strong>Case 2:</strong> 52-year-old with 12 days of persistent facial pressure and purulent discharge: review for intranasal steroid or delayed treatment.</li> <li>[sc name="link-websheet" id="1283" anchor="112"][/sc]<strong>Case 3:</strong> child with swollen red eyelid and fever after a recent cold: urgent referral for orbital complication until proven otherwise.</li> </ul> </details>
