[sc name="map-steps-and-links" id="1274" anchor="101" title="Overview" color="green" step="1" info-modal="" pathway="display-none" youtube="display-none"][/sc]<details class="kb-accordion kb-parent d-green" open> <summary id="s-is.101" role="button">Overview</summary> <ul> <li><strong>Clinical challenge:</strong> this is a time-critical risk-management consultation, not a routine supply transaction.</li> <li><strong>Guideline frame:</strong> FSRH emergency contraception guidance, with Cu-IUD discussed whenever feasible.</li> <li><strong>Core stance:</strong> offer the most effective appropriate option, not just the most convenient tablet.</li> </ul> </details>
[sc name="map-steps-and-links" id="1274" anchor="102" title="Pathophysiology" color="green" step="2" info-modal="" pathway="display-none" youtube="display-none"][/sc]<details class="kb-accordion kb-parent d-green" open> <summary id="s-is.102" role="button">Pathophysiology/basic science</summary> <ul> <li><strong>Levonorgestrel:</strong> mainly delays or inhibits ovulation earlier in the cycle window.</li> <li><strong>Ulipristal:</strong> delays ovulation later in the cycle than levonorgestrel can.</li> <li><strong>Cu-IUD:</strong> most effective overall and not undermined in the same way by higher body weight or enzyme-inducing medicines.</li> <li><strong>Clinical implication:</strong> the best option changes with timing, cycle stage, medicines, and access.</li> </ul></details>
[sc name="map-steps-and-links" id="1274" anchor="103" title="Problem Framework" color="purple" step="3" info-modal="" pathway="display-none" youtube="display-none"][/sc]<details class="kb-accordion kb-parent d-purple" open> <summary id="s-is.103" role="button">Problem framework</summary> <ul class="no-bullet"> <li>[sc name="link-websheet" id="1274" anchor="103.1"][/sc]<strong>Primary problem:</strong> risk of pregnancy after UPSI or contraceptive failure.</li> <li>[sc name="link-websheet" id="1274" anchor="103.2"][/sc]<strong>Concurrent issues:</strong> existing pregnancy, STI exposure, ongoing contraception needs.</li> <li>[sc name="link-websheet" id="1274" anchor="103.3"][/sc]<strong>Must not miss:</strong> sexual assault, safeguarding concern, ectopic risk.</li> </ul> </details>
[sc name="map-steps-and-links" id="1274" anchor="104" title="History" color="yellow" step="4" info-modal="" pathway="display-none" youtube="display-none"][/sc]<details class="kb-accordion kb-parent d-yellow" open> <summary id="s-is.104" role="button">History taking</summary> <ul class="no-bullet"> <li>[sc name="link-websheet" id="1274" anchor="104.1"][/sc]<strong>Timing and nature of the event</strong>: exact time since UPSI, repeated episodes, method failure details.</li> <li>[sc name="link-websheet" id="1274" anchor="104.2"][/sc]<strong>Cycle and pregnancy history</strong>: LMP, cycle regularity, overdue bleed, pregnancy symptoms.</li> <li>[sc name="link-websheet" id="1274" anchor="104.3"][/sc]<strong>Drug history and medical factors</strong>: enzyme inducers, gastric pH agents, severe asthma on oral steroids, malabsorption.</li> <li>[sc name="link-websheet" id="1274" anchor="104.4"][/sc]<strong>Safeguarding and consent context</strong></li> </ul> </details>
[sc name="map-steps-and-links" id="1274" anchor="105" title="Examination" color="yellow" step="5" info-modal="" pathway="display-none" youtube="display-none"][/sc]<details class="kb-accordion kb-parent d-yellow" open> <summary id="s-is.105" role="button">Examination strategy</summary> <ul class="no-bullet"> <li>[sc name="link-websheet" id="1274" anchor="105.1"][/sc]<strong>General principle</strong>: oral EHC is usually history-led and does not need routine examination.</li> <li>[sc name="link-websheet" id="1274" anchor="105.2"][/sc]<strong>BMI / weight and practical suitability discussion</strong></li> <li><strong>Examination threshold:</strong> lower threshold for abdominal or pelvic assessment if pain, bleeding, STI symptoms, or ectopic concern are present.</li> </ul> </details>
[sc name="map-steps-and-links" id="1274" anchor="106" title="Red Flags" color="pink" step="6" info-modal="" pathway="display-none" youtube="display-none"][/sc]<details class="kb-accordion kb-parent d-pink" open> <summary id="s-is.106" role="button">Red flag synthesis</summary> <ul class="no-bullet"> <li><strong>Urgent safeguarding action:</strong> assault, coercion, or child protection concern.</li> <li><strong>Urgent same-day clinical review:</strong> unilateral abdominal pain, fever, significant bleeding, ectopic or PID concern.</li> <li><strong>Urgent specialist pathway:</strong> outside oral window, enzyme-inducing medication, or Cu-IUD clearly preferred and needing rapid access.</li> </ul> </details>
[sc name="map-steps-and-links" id="1274" anchor="107" title="Investigations" color="yellow" step="7" info-modal="" pathway="display-none" youtube="display-none"][/sc]<details class="kb-accordion kb-parent d-yellow" open> <summary id="s-is.107" role="button">Investigations strategy &amp; interpretation</summary> <ul class="no-bullet"> <li>[sc name="link-websheet" id="1274" anchor="107"][/sc]<strong>Pregnancy testing</strong> if existing pregnancy is possible or later follow-up is needed.</li> <li>[sc name="link-websheet" id="1274" anchor="107"][/sc]<strong>No routine pelvic examination</strong> for a straightforward oral EHC consultation without concerning symptoms.</li> <li>[sc name="link-websheet" id="1274" anchor="107"][/sc]<strong>Decision point:</strong> make Cu-IUD versus oral choice explicit.</li> <li>[sc name="link-websheet" id="1274" anchor="107"][/sc]<strong>STI screening signposting</strong> should be built into the consultation when relevant.</li> </ul> </details>
[sc name="map-steps-and-links" id="1274" anchor="108" title="Evidence & Guidance" color="purple" step="8" info-modal="" pathway="display-none" youtube="display-none"][/sc]<details class="kb-accordion kb-parent d-purple" open> <summary id="s-is.108" role="button">Evidence and guideline pearls</summary> <ul class="no-bullet"> <li>[sc name="link-websheet" id="1274" anchor="108"][/sc]Cu-IUD is the most effective emergency contraception.</li> <li>[sc name="link-websheet" id="1274" anchor="108"][/sc]Ulipristal is generally the preferred oral option when suitable.</li> <li>[sc name="link-websheet" id="1274" anchor="108"][/sc]After ulipristal, delay hormonal contraception for 5 days and use barrier precautions.</li> <li>[sc name="link-websheet" id="1274" anchor="108"][/sc]Do not hide oral-method uncertainty when enzyme-inducing medicines are present.</li> </ul> </details>
[sc name="map-steps-and-links" id="1274" anchor="109" title="Diagnostic Timeout" color="pink" step="9" info-modal="" pathway="display-none" youtube="display-none"][/sc]<details class="kb-accordion kb-parent d-pink" open> <summary id="s-is.109" role="button">Diagnostic timeout &amp; cognitive biases</summary> <ul> <li><strong>Do not frame oral EHC as equally effective to Cu-IUD.</strong></li> <li><strong>Do not forget repeated UPSI in the same cycle.</strong></li> <li><strong>Do not miss safeguarding because the consultation feels transactional.</strong></li> <li><strong>Do not give false reassurance when interacting medicines are present.</strong></li> </ul> </details>
[sc name="map-steps-and-links" id="1274" anchor="110" title="Management" color="blue" step="10" info-modal="" pathway="display-none" youtube="display-none"][/sc]<details class="kb-accordion kb-parent d-blue" open> <summary id="s-is.110" role="button">Management and safety-netting</summary> <ul class="no-bullet"> <li>[sc name="link-websheet" id="1274" anchor="110"][/sc]<strong>Preferred pathway:</strong> discuss Cu-IUD, then select the best suitable oral option if that is the chosen or available route.</li> <li>[sc name="link-websheet" id="1274" anchor="110"][/sc]<strong>Counselling essentials:</strong> vomiting within 3 hours, restart timing for contraception, barrier use, and pregnancy test timing.</li> <li>[sc name="link-websheet" id="1274" anchor="110"][/sc]<strong>Method-specific follow-up:</strong> after ulipristal, delay hormonal contraception for 5 days; after levonorgestrel, ongoing hormonal contraception can usually restart immediately with the appropriate precautions.</li> <li>[sc name="link-websheet" id="1274" anchor="110"][/sc]<strong>Urgent return:</strong> severe abdominal pain, late or unusual period, or concern about pregnancy.</li> </ul> </details>
[sc name="map-steps-and-links" id="1274" anchor="111" title="Special Populations" color="blue" step="11" info-modal="" pathway="display-none" youtube="display-none"][/sc]<details class="kb-accordion kb-parent d-blue" open> <summary id="s-is.111" role="button">Special populations and role-aware notes</summary> <ul class="no-bullet"> <li>[sc name="link-websheet" id="1274" anchor="111"][/sc]<strong>Under 16s:</strong> Fraser competence and safeguarding are central.</li> <li>[sc name="link-websheet" id="1274" anchor="111"][/sc]<strong>Breastfeeding:</strong> counselling differs between oral methods; check current guidance before advising.</li> <li>[sc name="link-websheet" id="1274" anchor="111"][/sc]<strong>Higher BMI / enzyme-inducing drugs:</strong> push the conversation toward Cu-IUD rather than overselling oral efficacy.</li> <li>[sc name="link-websheet" id="1274" anchor="111"][/sc]<strong>Role-aware note:</strong> pharmacy clinicians can deliver excellent first-contact EHC care, but complex safeguarding, pelvic pain, or Cu-IUD access needs prompt GP or sexual-health linkage rather than a narrow supply model.</li> </ul> </details>
