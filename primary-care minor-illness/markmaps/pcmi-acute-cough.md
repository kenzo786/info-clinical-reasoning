[sc name="map-steps-and-links" id="1272" anchor="101" title="Overview" color="green" step="1" info-modal="" pathway="display-none" youtube="display-none"][/sc]<details class="kb-accordion kb-parent d-green" open> <summary id="s-is.101" role="button">Overview</summary> <ul> <li><strong>Clinical challenge:</strong> Acute cough is usually less than 3 weeks and is often viral, but the consultation must actively exclude pneumonia, sepsis, PE, pneumothorax, acute asthma/COPD, and heart failure.</li> <li><strong>Guideline frame:</strong> NICE NG120 plus CRB-65 when pneumonia is suspected.</li> <li><strong>Core stance:</strong> Start with physiology and red flags before deciding this is uncomplicated infection.</li> </ul> </details>
[sc name="map-steps-and-links" id="1272" anchor="102" title="Pathophysiology" color="green" step="2" info-modal="" pathway="display-none" youtube="display-none"][/sc]<details class="kb-accordion kb-parent d-green" open> <summary id="s-is.102" role="button">Pathophysiology/basic science</summary> <ul> <li><strong>Viral URTI / bronchitis:</strong> airway inflammation and hyper-responsiveness drive cough.</li> <li><strong>Pneumonia:</strong> alveolar infection produces fever, pleuritic pain, focal chest signs, and sometimes hypoxia.</li> <li><strong>Asthma / COPD exacerbation:</strong> bronchospasm and airway narrowing produce wheeze, breathlessness, and poor air entry.</li> <li><strong>Why it matters:</strong> cough may be secondary to a more dangerous cardiopulmonary process.</li> </ul></details>
[sc name="map-steps-and-links" id="1272" anchor="103" title="Differential Diagnosis" color="purple" step="3" info-modal="" pathway="display-none" youtube="display-none"][/sc]<details class="kb-accordion kb-parent d-purple" open> <summary id="s-is.103" role="button">Differential diagnosis (ddx) framework</summary> <ul class="no-bullet"> <li>[sc name="link-websheet" id="1272" anchor="103.1"][/sc]<strong>Must not miss:</strong> pneumonia, sepsis, PE, pneumothorax, acute asthma/COPD, heart failure.</li> <li>[sc name="link-websheet" id="1272" anchor="103.2"][/sc]<strong>Common causes:</strong> viral URTI, acute bronchitis, post-viral cough, COVID-19 / influenza-like illness, upper airway cough syndrome.</li> <li>[sc name="link-websheet" id="1272" anchor="103.3"][/sc]<strong>Easily missed:</strong> ACE inhibitor cough, pertussis, foreign body, lung cancer context.</li> </ul> </details>
[sc name="map-steps-and-links" id="1272" anchor="104" title="History" color="yellow" step="4" info-modal="" pathway="display-none" youtube="display-none"][/sc]<details class="kb-accordion kb-parent d-yellow" open> <summary id="s-is.104" role="button">History taking</summary> <ul class="no-bullet"> <li>[sc name="link-websheet" id="1272" anchor="104.1"][/sc]<strong>Duration and trajectory</strong>: improving, static, or worsening?</li> <li>[sc name="link-websheet" id="1272" anchor="104.2"][/sc]<strong>Breathlessness and exercise tolerance</strong>: rest breathlessness changes the risk picture.</li> <li>[sc name="link-websheet" id="1272" anchor="104.3"][/sc]<strong>Chest pain and haemoptysis</strong>: pleuritic pain or blood in sputum needs escalation thinking.</li> <li>[sc name="link-websheet" id="1272" anchor="104.4"][/sc]<strong>Systemic features and comorbidity</strong>: fever, confusion, COPD/asthma, heart failure, cancer, immunosuppression, smoking, VTE risk.</li> </ul> </details>
[sc name="map-steps-and-links" id="1272" anchor="105" title="Examination" color="yellow" step="5" info-modal="" pathway="display-none" youtube="display-none"][/sc]<details class="kb-accordion kb-parent d-yellow" open> <summary id="s-is.105" role="button">Examination strategy</summary> <ul class="no-bullet"> <li>[sc name="link-websheet" id="1272" anchor="105.1"][/sc]<strong>General impression and respiratory effort</strong></li> <li>[sc name="link-websheet" id="1272" anchor="105.2"][/sc]<strong>Vital signs</strong>: temperature, pulse, respiratory rate, BP, SpO2.</li> <li>[sc name="link-websheet" id="1272" anchor="105.3"][/sc]<strong>Chest examination</strong>: air entry, wheeze, crackles, focal dullness.</li> </ul> </details>
[sc name="map-steps-and-links" id="1272" anchor="106" title="Red Flags" color="pink" step="6" info-modal="" pathway="display-none" youtube="display-none"][/sc]<details class="kb-accordion kb-parent d-pink" open> <summary id="s-is.106" role="button">Red flag synthesis</summary> <ul class="no-bullet"> <li><strong>Emergency / same-day:</strong> severe breathlessness, hypoxia, confusion, hypotension, cyanosis, haemoptysis, severe pleuritic pain, or suspected sepsis.</li> <li><strong>Urgent community review:</strong> suspected pneumonia, frailty or immunocompromise with deterioration, or acute worsening asthma/COPD/heart failure.</li> <li><strong>Routine follow-up:</strong> stable viral cough with normal observations and no focal chest signs.</li> </ul> </details>
[sc name="map-steps-and-links" id="1272" anchor="107" title="Investigations" color="yellow" step="7" info-modal="" pathway="display-none" youtube="display-none"][/sc]<details class="kb-accordion kb-parent d-yellow" open> <summary id="s-is.107" role="button">Investigations strategy &amp; interpretation</summary> <ul class="no-bullet"> <li>[sc name="link-websheet" id="1272" anchor="107"][/sc]<strong>CRB-65</strong> when pneumonia is suspected.</li> <li>[sc name="link-websheet" id="1272" anchor="107"][/sc]<strong>Pulse oximetry</strong> is high-value in breathless, older, or comorbid patients.</li> <li>[sc name="link-websheet" id="1272" anchor="107"][/sc]<strong>Chest X-ray</strong> when pneumonia, malignancy, or structural disease is suspected.</li> <li>[sc name="link-websheet" id="1272" anchor="107"][/sc]<strong>No routine antibiotics or routine bloods</strong> for uncomplicated acute cough / bronchitis.</li> </ul> </details>
[sc name="map-steps-and-links" id="1272" anchor="108" title="Evidence & Guidance" color="purple" step="8" info-modal="" pathway="display-none" youtube="display-none"][/sc]<details class="kb-accordion kb-parent d-purple" open> <summary id="s-is.108" role="button">Evidence and guideline pearls</summary> <ul class="no-bullet"> <li>[sc name="link-websheet" id="1272" anchor="108"][/sc]NICE NG120: do not routinely offer antibiotics for uncomplicated acute cough.</li> <li>[sc name="link-websheet" id="1272" anchor="108"][/sc]Explain early that cough often lasts 3 to 4 weeks.</li> <li>[sc name="link-websheet" id="1272" anchor="108"][/sc]Oral or inhaled corticosteroids are not recommended for uncomplicated acute bronchitis unless treating another condition such as asthma.</li> <li>[sc name="link-websheet" id="1272" anchor="108"][/sc]Pholcodine is withdrawn; codeine linctus is POM-only.</li> </ul> </details>
[sc name="map-steps-and-links" id="1272" anchor="109" title="Diagnostic Timeout" color="pink" step="9" info-modal="" pathway="display-none" youtube="display-none"][/sc]<details class="kb-accordion kb-parent d-pink" open> <summary id="s-is.109" role="button">Diagnostic timeout &amp; cognitive biases</summary> <ul> <li><strong>Do not anchor on sputum colour:</strong> yellow or green sputum alone does not prove bacterial infection.</li> <li><strong>Do not miss physiology:</strong> respiratory rate, SpO2, and blood pressure can reveal the sick patient quickly.</li> <li><strong>Do not forget alternatives:</strong> pleuritic pain plus cough is not automatically pneumonia; think PE or pneumothorax.</li> <li><strong>Do not overprescribe antibiotics:</strong> stewardship matters, but not at the expense of missing pneumonia in a higher-risk patient.</li> </ul> </details>
[sc name="map-steps-and-links" id="1272" anchor="110" title="Management" color="blue" step="10" info-modal="" pathway="display-none" youtube="display-none"][/sc]<details class="kb-accordion kb-parent d-blue" open> <summary id="s-is.110" role="button">Management and safety-netting</summary> <ul class="no-bullet"> <li>[sc name="link-websheet" id="1272" anchor="110"][/sc]<strong>Low-risk viral cough:</strong> reassure, advise fluids, rest, smoking cessation, simple symptomatic relief, and explain likely duration.</li> <li>[sc name="link-websheet" id="1272" anchor="110"][/sc]<strong>Suspected pneumonia:</strong> assess CRB-65, follow local antimicrobial guidance, and decide on home care versus same-day hospital assessment.</li> <li>[sc name="link-websheet" id="1272" anchor="110"][/sc]<strong>Urgent return:</strong> worsening breathlessness, chest pain, haemoptysis, confusion, inability to maintain oral intake, or failure to improve as expected.</li> </ul> </details>
[sc name="map-steps-and-links" id="1272" anchor="111" title="Special Populations" color="blue" step="11" info-modal="" pathway="display-none" youtube="display-none"][/sc]<details class="kb-accordion kb-parent d-blue" open> <summary id="s-is.111" role="button">Special populations and role-aware notes</summary> <ul class="no-bullet"> <li>[sc name="link-websheet" id="1272" anchor="111"][/sc]<strong>Children:</strong> escalate sooner if there is poor feeding, work of breathing concern, lethargy, or possible foreign body.</li> <li>[sc name="link-websheet" id="1272" anchor="111"][/sc]<strong>Older adults:</strong> confusion, falls, or functional decline may be the clue to pneumonia.</li> <li>[sc name="link-websheet" id="1272" anchor="111"][/sc]<strong>Pregnancy:</strong> use pregnancy-safe symptomatic advice and do not undertreat suspected pneumonia.</li> <li>[sc name="link-websheet" id="1272" anchor="111"][/sc]<strong>Role-aware note:</strong> low-risk viral cough may stay in pharmacy or primary care pathways, but abnormal observations, focal chest signs, or systemic deterioration should move to GP / same-day / ED assessment.</li> </ul> </details>
[sc name="map-steps-and-links" id="1272" anchor="112" title="Illustrative Cases" color="grey" step="12" info-modal="" pathway="display-none" youtube="display-none"][/sc]<details class="kb-accordion kb-parent d-grey" open> <summary id="s-is.112" role="button">Illustrative cases</summary> <ul class="no-bullet"> <li>[sc name="link-websheet" id="1272" anchor="112"][/sc]<strong>Case 1:</strong> 28-year-old with 5 days of cough, coryza, mild myalgia, normal observations, clear chest: viral cough with self-care and safety-netting.</li> <li>[sc name="link-websheet" id="1272" anchor="112"][/sc]<strong>Case 2:</strong> 77-year-old with confusion, fever, RR 32, focal crackles: high-risk pneumonia needing urgent hospital assessment.</li> <li>[sc name="link-websheet" id="1272" anchor="112"][/sc]<strong>Case 3:</strong> 44-year-old with cough, pleuritic pain, haemoptysis, recent long-haul flight, normal chest exam: do not anchor on bronchitis; escalate for PE assessment.</li> </ul> </details>
