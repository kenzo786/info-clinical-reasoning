Space-Occupying Lesion (SOL)
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Space-Occupying Lesion (SOL)"][/sc]
  • A space-occupying lesion (SOL) refers to any abnormal mass in the brain that occupies space and causes compression, displacement, or increased pressure within the skull. These masses may be neoplastic (primary or metastatic tumours), vascular (e.g. haemorrhage), infectious (e.g. abscess), or cystic (e.g. arachnoid cyst).
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Space-Occupying Lesion (SOL)"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Space-Occupying Lesion (SOL)"][/sc]
  • Prevalence in UK GP Population: Space-occupying lesions are rare in primary care. The most common SOL, brain tumours, have an incidence of approximately 10-15 per 100,000 annually in the UK.
  • UK Hospital Prevalence: Hospital cases are more frequent, particularly in neurology or oncology departments. Brain metastases are found in up to 25% of cancer patients.
  • Worldwide Prevalence: Globally, the incidence of primary brain tumours varies between 5-10 per 100,000 people annually.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Space-Occupying Lesion (SOL)"][/sc]
  • SOLs create mass effects by increasing intracranial pressure (ICP) due to the rigid confines of the skull. Depending on the lesion’s size, growth rate, and location, this leads to brain tissue displacement, compression of structures, and potential disruption of cerebral blood flow. The pathophysiology may differ based on the nature of the lesion (e.g., tumour vs. abscess vs. haemorrhage), but the final common pathway includes herniation risks, ischaemia, and hydrocephalus.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Space-Occupying Lesion (SOL)"][/sc]
  • Acute: Sudden symptoms, often from haemorrhage or infection, causing a rapid increase in ICP (hours to days).
  • Subacute: Gradual symptom progression due to a slower-growing tumour, infection, or cyst (weeks to months).
  • Chronic: Long-standing lesions such as low-grade tumours or cysts with insidious onset (months to years).
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Space-Occupying Lesion (SOL)"][/sc]
  • Headache: The most common symptom, caused by raised ICP, often worse in the morning or when lying down due to impaired CSF drainage.
  • Nausea and vomiting: Due to increased ICP stimulating the brainstem's vomiting centre.
  • Seizures: SOLs, especially tumours, irritate cortical neurons, leading to focal or generalised seizures.
  • Focal neurological deficits: Depend on the lesion’s location; e.g., weakness or aphasia if in motor or language areas.
  • Visual disturbances: Caused by compression of optic pathways or raised ICP affecting vision (e.g., papilloedema).
  • Changes in consciousness: Caused by extensive mass effect or brain herniation, reducing cortical function.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Space-Occupying Lesion (SOL)"][/sc]
  • Papilloedema: Swelling of the optic disc seen on fundoscopy due to increased ICP.
  • Focal motor deficits: Weakness, hemiparesis, or reflex asymmetry, indicating a lesion affecting motor pathways.
  • Altered mental status: Confusion, drowsiness, or lethargy from raised ICP or brainstem compression.
  • Cranial nerve palsies: Compression of cranial nerves, especially in posterior fossa lesions (e.g., CN VI or CN VII palsy).
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Space-Occupying Lesion (SOL)"][/sc]
  • Rapid onset of symptoms: Suggests haemorrhage or aggressive tumour, leading to urgent raised ICP and herniation risks.
  • Altered consciousness: Indicates significant mass effect or early signs of brain herniation.
  • Severe, worsening headache with vomiting: Suggests marked increase in ICP, often seen with posterior fossa lesions compressing the brainstem.
  • New-onset seizures: Particularly concerning in adults without prior seizure history, often indicating an irritative lesion such as a tumour.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Space-Occupying Lesion (SOL)"][/sc]
  • Subtle personality changes or cognitive decline: In slowly growing lesions like low-grade gliomas, cognitive symptoms can develop insidiously over months, leading to delays in diagnosis.
  • Asymptomatic presentation: Some slow-growing lesions, such as meningiomas, may be found incidentally on imaging done for other reasons.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Space-Occupying Lesion (SOL)"][/sc]
  • CT Head: Often the first-line imaging, identifies large masses, midline shifts, and haemorrhages. May show hypodense or hyperdense regions depending on the lesion.
  • MRI Brain: Preferred imaging modality, provides superior detail in identifying lesion type, location, and extent. Contrast enhancement helps differentiate tumour types.
  • Fundoscopy: Checks for papilloedema, indicating raised ICP.
  • EEG: Used if seizures are present, can show focal epileptic activity linked to the lesion.
  • Biopsy: If a tumour is suspected, tissue sampling via biopsy or resection is essential for histological diagnosis.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Space-Occupying Lesion (SOL)"][/sc]
  • A 55-year-old male presents with progressively worsening headaches, occasional vomiting, and new-onset seizures over the past 3 months. Neurological exam reveals right-sided hemiparesis and papilloedema on fundoscopy.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Space-Occupying Lesion (SOL)"][/sc]
  • Stroke: Rapid onset, often focal deficits without progressive headache or seizures. CT/MRI shows acute ischaemia or haemorrhage, not mass effect.
  • Subdural haematoma: Can present with similar mass effect and raised ICP, but often history of trauma with crescent-shaped bleed on CT.
  • Brain abscess: May present with fever, focal deficits, and mass effect on imaging but shows ring enhancement with central necrosis.
  • Encephalitis: Typically presents with fever and altered mental status, with imaging showing diffuse brain inflammation rather than a focal mass.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Space-Occupying Lesion (SOL)"][/sc]
  • Surgical resection: Indicated for accessible tumours or lesions causing significant mass effect, with the goal of reducing ICP and obtaining a histological diagnosis.
  • Radiotherapy: Used for malignant tumours like glioblastoma or metastatic lesions, either as an adjunct to surgery or as the main treatment if inoperable.
  • Corticosteroids: Dexamethasone is often given to reduce cerebral oedema and alleviate symptoms of raised ICP.
  • Antiepileptic drugs: Used to manage or prevent seizures, which are common in SOLs, especially tumours.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Space-Occupying Lesion (SOL)"][/sc]
  • Brain herniation: Caused by raised ICP, this is a life-threatening emergency where brain structures are displaced, leading to brainstem compression and cardiorespiratory arrest.
  • Hydrocephalus: Obstruction of CSF flow, particularly with posterior fossa lesions, may result in increased ventricular pressure.
  • Cognitive and neurological impairment: Depending on the lesion’s location and extent, long-term deficits can include memory loss, motor dysfunction, and speech disturbances.
