Stress or Anxiety
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Stress or Anxiety"][/sc]
  • Stress refers to the physiological and psychological responses to external demands or stressors, which may be physical, emotional, or environmental. Anxiety is a persistent state of excessive worry, fear, or nervousness that may or may not be linked to specific stressors. Anxiety can manifest as generalised anxiety disorder (GAD), panic disorder, or social anxiety, among others. Both conditions can significantly impact daily functioning and well-being.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Stress or Anxiety"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Stress or Anxiety"][/sc]
  • UK GP population: Anxiety disorders are seen in 15-20% of GP consultations annually, with a higher incidence in women.
  • UK hospital prevalence: Anxiety-related conditions account for about 10-12% of hospital admissions, often in cases of severe anxiety or panic attacks.
  • Worldwide prevalence: Globally, anxiety disorders have a lifetime prevalence of approximately 15%, with higher rates in high-income countries.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Stress or Anxiety"][/sc]
  • Stress activates the hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal (HPA) axis, leading to the release of cortisol and adrenaline, which prepare the body for a "fight or flight" response. In chronic stress, prolonged activation of the HPA axis can lead to dysregulation of cortisol production, resulting in fatigue, immune suppression, and emotional instability.
  • Anxiety is driven by dysregulation in the brain's limbic system, particularly in areas such as the amygdala and prefrontal cortex. Neurotransmitters like serotonin, GABA, and norepinephrine are often imbalanced, leading to heightened arousal and worry. Overactivity of the sympathetic nervous system can contribute to physical symptoms like tachycardia and sweating.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Stress or Anxiety"][/sc]
  • Acute stress or anxiety: Sudden onset following an identifiable stressor, resolving once the stressor is removed or managed.
  • Chronic stress or generalised anxiety disorder: Lasts for months or years, with a slow build-up of symptoms, often waxing and waning in severity.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Stress or Anxiety"][/sc]
  • Excessive worry - Persistent, disproportionate worry about various aspects of life. Related to the overactivity of the amygdala and prefrontal cortex.
  • Fatigue - A common symptom due to prolonged HPA axis activation leading to exhaustion of the body's energy reserves.
  • Difficulty concentrating - Impaired focus due to heightened arousal and cognitive interference from anxious thoughts.
  • Restlessness - A feeling of being "on edge", linked to overactivation of the sympathetic nervous system.
  • Insomnia - Difficulty falling or staying asleep due to an overactive stress response and excessive rumination.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Stress or Anxiety"][/sc]
  • Tachycardia - Increased heart rate, often associated with sympathetic overdrive.
  • Trembling or shaking - Physical manifestations of heightened autonomic arousal.
  • Hyperventilation - Rapid breathing, often in response to perceived threats, leading to dizziness or tingling.
  • Cold, clammy hands - A physiological response to sympathetic nervous system activation, as blood flow is redirected away from the skin.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Stress or Anxiety"][/sc]
  • Chest pain - May suggest a cardiovascular cause, such as acute coronary syndrome, rather than anxiety alone.
  • Suicidal ideation - Indicates severe anxiety or co-existing depression, requiring urgent intervention to prevent self-harm.
  • Unexplained weight loss - Could point to an underlying medical condition, such as hyperthyroidism or malignancy, rather than anxiety alone.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Stress or Anxiety"][/sc]
  • Somatisation - Presenting with multiple vague physical symptoms, such as gastrointestinal discomfort or headaches, without clear psychological distress.
  • Anger or irritability - In some individuals, anxiety may manifest as irritability or outbursts rather than traditional worry or fear.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Stress or Anxiety"][/sc]
  • General Health Questionnaire (GHQ-12) - A 12-item screening tool to assess psychological distress and likelihood of anxiety or depression.
  • Blood tests - Full blood count (FBC), thyroid function tests, and electrolyte panels to exclude organic causes like anaemia or hyperthyroidism.
  • ECG - Performed to rule out cardiac causes in patients presenting with chest pain or palpitations.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Stress or Anxiety"][/sc]
  • A 32-year-old female presents with a 6-month history of excessive worry, restlessness, and difficulty sleeping. Symptoms have worsened after a recent job change, and she reports occasional heart palpitations but no chest pain or shortness of breath.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Stress or Anxiety"][/sc]
  • Hyperthyroidism - Differentiated by physical signs such as tremor, heat intolerance, weight loss, and confirmed by abnormal thyroid function tests.
  • Depression - Often presents with more low mood, anhedonia, and lethargy, while anxiety is marked by excessive worry and arousal.
  • Panic disorder - Characterised by episodic, sudden attacks of intense fear, as opposed to the more chronic, generalised nature of anxiety.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Stress or Anxiety"][/sc]
  • Cognitive behavioural therapy (CBT) - First-line intervention for anxiety disorders, aiming to alter negative thought patterns.
  • Selective serotonin reuptake inhibitors (SSRIs) - Medications like sertraline are often prescribed for generalised anxiety disorder.
  • Benzodiazepines - Used for short-term relief in acute anxiety episodes, though not recommended for long-term use due to dependence risk.
  • Lifestyle modifications - Including regular exercise, relaxation techniques, and sleep hygiene.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Stress or Anxiety"][/sc]
  • Cardiovascular disease - Chronic anxiety has been linked to an increased risk of hypertension and coronary artery disease.
  • Substance abuse - Patients may resort to alcohol or drugs to manage symptoms of anxiety.
  • Depression - Untreated anxiety can progress to depression, compounding the impact on daily life.
