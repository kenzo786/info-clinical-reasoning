Hypoxia
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Hypoxia"][/sc]
  • Hypoxia is a medical condition characterised by insufficient oxygen supply at the tissue level despite adequate blood flow. It can result from various underlying causes such as respiratory, cardiovascular, or haematological disorders and may lead to cellular dysfunction or death if not treated promptly. Hypoxia can be classified into different types based on its cause: hypoxic, anaemic, circulatory, and histotoxic.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Hypoxia"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Hypoxia"][/sc]
  • In UK general practice, hypoxia is a relatively common finding, often associated with chronic conditions such as COPD or heart failure. It is present in approximately 8-10% of hospital admissions, particularly in emergency settings.
  • Worldwide, the prevalence of hypoxia is significant, especially in low-resource settings with high rates of respiratory diseases like pneumonia or in high-altitude regions.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Hypoxia"][/sc]
  • Hypoxia occurs when there is a failure in oxygen delivery to tissues, which can happen due to:
    • Reduced oxygen availability (e.g., high altitude, lung diseases reducing gas exchange).
    • Impaired oxygen transport (e.g., anaemia, carbon monoxide poisoning).
    • Impaired perfusion (e.g., shock, heart failure).
    • Cellular utilisation defects (e.g., cyanide poisoning).
    This leads to anaerobic metabolism and lactic acid accumulation, causing acidosis and eventual cellular damage or death.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Hypoxia"][/sc]
  • Hypoxia can develop acutely (e.g., from a pulmonary embolism or acute respiratory distress) or chronically (e.g., in COPD or chronic heart failure). Acute hypoxia typically presents rapidly and can progress within minutes to hours, while chronic hypoxia may develop insidiously over weeks to months.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Hypoxia"][/sc]
  • Dyspnoea - The most common symptom, caused by insufficient oxygen in the bloodstream stimulating respiratory centres.
  • Fatigue - Results from reduced oxygen delivery to tissues, impairing cellular energy production.
  • Confusion - Due to hypoxia affecting brain function, leading to altered mental status or confusion, especially in acute or severe cases.
  • Cyanosis - Bluish discolouration of the skin, typically in the lips or extremities, due to reduced oxygenation of haemoglobin.
  • Chest pain - Often seen in cases of hypoxia related to cardiac or respiratory causes, such as pulmonary embolism.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Hypoxia"][/sc]
  • Tachypnoea - Increased respiratory rate as the body tries to compensate for low oxygen levels.
  • Tachycardia - Increased heart rate to improve oxygen delivery to tissues.
  • Central cyanosis - Bluish discolouration of the central parts of the body (e.g., lips, tongue) indicating severe hypoxia.
  • Altered mental state - Seen in more severe cases, with confusion or agitation indicating cerebral hypoxia.
  • Use of accessory muscles - In respiratory distress, patients may use additional muscles (e.g., neck muscles) to breathe, indicating severe respiratory compromise.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Hypoxia"][/sc]
  • Altered consciousness - Suggests severe hypoxia affecting brain function, indicating an urgent need for intervention.
  • Severe cyanosis - Indicates critically low oxygen levels, requiring immediate oxygenation support.
  • Chest pain with hypoxia - May indicate myocardial ischaemia, pulmonary embolism, or another life-threatening condition.
  • Sudden onset of dyspnoea - Can suggest acute life-threatening causes like a pulmonary embolism or pneumothorax.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Hypoxia"][/sc]
  • Silent hypoxia - Common in COVID-19 pneumonia, where patients may have dangerously low oxygen levels but report minimal respiratory discomfort, possibly due to altered central regulation of breathing.
  • Chronic hypoxia - May present with vague symptoms like fatigue, weight loss, and reduced exercise tolerance, which could be mistaken for other chronic conditions.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Hypoxia"][/sc]
  • Pulse oximetry - Measures peripheral oxygen saturation (SpO2). Values < 94% suggest hypoxia. Hypoxia is due to impaired oxygen transport or lung dysfunction.
  • Arterial blood gas (ABG) - Provides direct measurement of PaO2, pH, and PaCO2. PaO2 < 8 kPa (60 mmHg) confirms hypoxaemia, with or without hypercapnia depending on the underlying cause.
  • Chest X-ray - To assess for lung pathologies such as pneumonia, pulmonary oedema, or pneumothorax that could cause hypoxia.
  • CT pulmonary angiogram (CTPA) - To rule out pulmonary embolism in cases of sudden onset hypoxia with risk factors for thromboembolism.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Hypoxia"][/sc]
  • A 65-year-old male with a history of COPD presents with acute worsening dyspnoea, cyanosis, and confusion. Pulse oximetry shows SpO2 of 85% on room air, and arterial blood gas demonstrates a PaO2 of 7.5 kPa (56 mmHg).
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Hypoxia"][/sc]
  • Pulmonary embolism - Presents with acute hypoxia, tachycardia, and pleuritic chest pain. Differentiated by presence of risk factors (e.g., immobility), sudden onset, and diagnostic imaging (CTPA).
  • Pneumonia - Fever, cough, and localised chest signs (crackles) alongside hypoxia. Confirmed with chest X-ray showing consolidation.
  • Heart failure - Orthopnoea, peripheral oedema, and pulmonary oedema on imaging, usually with chronic symptoms preceding hypoxia.
  • Anaemia - Hypoxia in the context of pallor, fatigue, and low haemoglobin on blood tests. Hypoxia is due to reduced oxygen-carrying capacity.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Hypoxia"][/sc]
  • Oxygen therapy - Administer oxygen (target SpO2 94-98% or 88-92% in COPD) via nasal cannula or face mask.
  • Treat the underlying cause - E.g., antibiotics for pneumonia, anticoagulation for pulmonary embolism, diuretics for heart failure.
  • Ventilatory support - Non-invasive ventilation (CPAP/BiPAP) or mechanical ventilation in severe cases where oxygen therapy alone is insufficient.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Hypoxia"][/sc]
  • Respiratory failure - Progression of hypoxia to inadequate gas exchange, necessitating ventilatory support.
  • Cardiac arrest - Due to severe hypoxia impairing myocardial oxygenation, potentially leading to arrhythmias or arrest.
  • Organ dysfunction - Prolonged hypoxia can result in multi-organ failure, especially affecting the brain, kidneys, and heart.
