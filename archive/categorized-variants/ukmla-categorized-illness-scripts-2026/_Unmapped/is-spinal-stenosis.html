Spinal Stenosis
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Spinal Stenosis"][/sc]
  • Spinal stenosis is a condition characterised by narrowing of the spinal canal, typically leading to compression of the spinal cord and/or nerve roots. This compression can cause neurogenic claudication, pain, and neurological deficits in affected patients. Spinal stenosis can occur at any level of the spine but is most commonly seen in the lumbar and cervical regions.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Spinal Stenosis"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Spinal Stenosis"][/sc]
  • Prevalence in the UK GP population: Spinal stenosis is relatively common in adults over 50 years, affecting about 5–10% of the general population.
  • UK hospital prevalence: The condition accounts for a significant proportion of spinal surgeries in older adults, with lumbar stenosis being a leading cause of spinal surgery in the UK.
  • Worldwide prevalence: It is estimated that approximately 250 million people worldwide experience spinal stenosis, with a higher prevalence in older populations due to degenerative changes.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Spinal Stenosis"][/sc]
  • Spinal stenosis usually arises from degenerative changes in the spine, including disc degeneration, facet joint osteoarthritis, and ligamentum flavum hypertrophy. These changes lead to a reduced diameter of the spinal canal.
  • The narrowed spinal canal or intervertebral foramina compress the spinal cord and/or nerve roots, leading to ischemia and nerve root inflammation.
  • Neurogenic claudication and radicular pain occur due to reduced blood flow, inflammation, and compression of neural structures, particularly during activities that further reduce the canal diameter, such as walking or standing.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Spinal Stenosis"][/sc]
  • Early stage: Initial symptoms may be mild, including intermittent pain or discomfort, particularly with prolonged standing or walking.
  • Progressive stage: Symptoms worsen over months to years, with increasing limitations on physical activity due to pain, weakness, and claudication.
  • Late stage: Severe neurogenic claudication and neurological deficits, including bowel or bladder dysfunction in severe cases, may be present.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Spinal Stenosis"][/sc]
  • Low back pain - Often due to degenerative changes in the spine and inflammation around the compressed nerve roots.
  • Neurogenic claudication - Pathophysiologically due to vascular insufficiency of the nerve roots, typically exacerbated by standing and relieved by sitting or lumbar flexion.
  • Paresthesia or numbness in the legs - Caused by nerve root compression, leading to sensory disturbances in the dermatomal distribution.
  • Leg weakness - Results from prolonged nerve compression, affecting motor pathways and potentially limiting mobility.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Spinal Stenosis"][/sc]
  • Altered gait (wide-based or antalgic gait) - Occurs as patients attempt to minimise pain and pressure on affected nerve roots.
  • Positive Romberg's test - Due to proprioceptive deficits secondary to spinal cord or nerve root compression.
  • Reduced lumbar spine extension - The narrowing of the spinal canal with extension exacerbates symptoms, leading to limited range of motion.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Spinal Stenosis"][/sc]
  • Bowel or bladder dysfunction - May indicate cauda equina syndrome, an emergency condition due to severe nerve root compression.
  • Saddle anaesthesia - Loss of sensation around the perineal area, suggesting possible cauda equina involvement.
  • Severe motor weakness in the lower limbs - Significant weakness may suggest advanced compression or ischaemia of nerve roots.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Spinal Stenosis"][/sc]
  • Isolated sensory changes - Some patients may present with isolated numbness or paraesthesia without significant pain or weakness.
  • Non-localised back pain - Diffuse pain without typical neurogenic claudication, possibly leading to missed diagnosis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Spinal Stenosis"][/sc]
  • MRI scan of the spine - Preferred imaging modality; shows narrowing of the spinal canal and nerve root compression.
  • CT myelogram - Used if MRI is contraindicated; provides detailed imaging of the bony structures and spinal canal.
  • Nerve conduction studies - Helpful in differentiating peripheral neuropathy from radicular causes of leg symptoms.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Spinal Stenosis"][/sc]
  • A 65-year-old man with progressive low back pain and leg weakness worsened by standing and relieved by sitting, consistent with neurogenic claudication, worsening over a year.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Spinal Stenosis"][/sc]
  • Peripheral vascular disease (PVD) - Typically causes exertional leg pain but is not relieved by sitting or lumbar flexion.
  • Herniated disc - Often acute onset with radicular pain, but less likely to cause neurogenic claudication.
  • Diabetic neuropathy - Sensory loss and paraesthesia may be similar but generally lack a positional component.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Spinal Stenosis"][/sc]
  • Non-surgical management - Includes physiotherapy, NSAIDs, and activity modification.
  • Epidural steroid injections - For short-term symptom relief in patients unresponsive to conservative therapy.
  • Surgical decompression - Recommended for patients with significant functional impairment or refractory symptoms; procedures may include laminectomy or spinal fusion.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Spinal Stenosis"][/sc]
  • Cauda equina syndrome - Rare but serious complication with potential for irreversible neurological damage.
  • Chronic pain and disability - May develop if untreated, especially in patients with significant spinal degeneration.
