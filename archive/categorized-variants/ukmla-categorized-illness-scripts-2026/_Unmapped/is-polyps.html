Polyps
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Polyps"][/sc]
  • Polyps are abnormal growths of tissue that project from a mucous membrane. They can occur in various parts of the body, such as the colon, stomach, nasal cavity, and uterus. The majority of polyps are benign, but some, particularly adenomatous polyps in the colon, can undergo dysplastic changes and become malignant over time. They are classified based on their histological type (e.g., adenomatous, hyperplastic) and morphology (e.g., sessile, pedunculated).
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Polyps"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Polyps"][/sc]
  • UK GP population: Approximately 20-25% of people over 50 years old have colonic polyps.
  • UK hospital prevalence: Polyps are found in up to 40% of individuals undergoing colonoscopy for screening purposes.
  • Worldwide prevalence: Colon polyps are more common in Western countries, with a prevalence of 30-40% in adults aged 50 and older. Nasal and uterine polyps are less well-studied but occur less frequently.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Polyps"][/sc]
  • Polyps develop due to abnormal proliferation of the epithelial lining of mucous membranes. The exact mechanisms depend on the type of polyp:
    • Adenomatous polyps: Result from mutations in tumour suppressor genes like APC, leading to uncontrolled cell growth. Dysplasia can progress to carcinoma over time (adenoma-carcinoma sequence).
    • Hyperplastic polyps: These arise from benign overgrowth of normal epithelial cells without significant malignant potential.
    • Inflammatory polyps: Typically seen in conditions like inflammatory bowel disease (IBD), they result from chronic mucosal injury and regeneration.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Polyps"][/sc]
  • Polyps typically grow slowly over many years. Adenomatous polyps can take 10-15 years to progress to cancer if left untreated.
  • Some polyps may remain stable and asymptomatic for long periods, while others may increase in size or number, especially in hereditary syndromes like familial adenomatous polyposis (FAP).
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Polyps"][/sc]
  • Asymptomatic: Most polyps, particularly in the colon, are asymptomatic and discovered incidentally during screening colonoscopies.
  • Rectal bleeding: Bleeding occurs when polyps erode the mucosal lining, causing intermittent blood in stools.
  • Change in bowel habits: Larger polyps, especially in the colon, can cause constipation or diarrhoea by partially obstructing the bowel lumen.
  • Abdominal pain: Occasional with larger polyps, likely due to partial obstruction or inflammatory changes.
  • Postnasal drip and nasal congestion: Common with nasal polyps, caused by the obstruction of normal sinus drainage.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Polyps"][/sc]
  • Rectal mass: On digital rectal examination, large rectal polyps can occasionally be palpated.
  • Visible polyps: In cases of nasal polyps, a direct nasal examination may reveal soft, pale masses.
  • Abdominal tenderness: Rare but may occur if there is partial bowel obstruction or inflammation.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Polyps"][/sc]
  • Unexplained weight loss: This may indicate malignancy in a polyp, especially if accompanied by anaemia or bleeding.
  • Iron-deficiency anaemia: Indicates chronic blood loss from a polyp, suggesting potential malignancy or significant size.
  • Persistent abdominal pain with bowel obstruction: Large polyps can cause partial bowel obstruction, which requires prompt intervention.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Polyps"][/sc]
  • Small polyps: These may present with very subtle or no symptoms and are often missed unless screening tests like colonoscopy are performed.
  • Nasal polyps with no sinus symptoms: Some nasal polyps may not cause obstruction or sinus issues, only being discovered during routine ENT evaluations.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Polyps"][/sc]
  • Colonoscopy: Direct visualisation of colonic polyps; polyps appear as raised mucosal lesions. Biopsy confirms histology.
  • Flexible sigmoidoscopy: Useful for visualising polyps in the rectum and sigmoid colon.
  • CT colonography: A non-invasive imaging alternative to colonoscopy that can detect large polyps.
  • Nasal endoscopy: Identifies nasal polyps directly, often used by ENT specialists.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Polyps"][/sc]
  • A 55-year-old male with no significant medical history presents for a routine screening colonoscopy. A 1.5 cm sessile polyp is found in the sigmoid colon, asymptomatic. Biopsy confirms an adenomatous polyp with low-grade dysplasia.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Polyps"][/sc]
  • Diverticulosis: Unlike polyps, diverticula are outpouchings of the colonic wall. They typically present with left-sided abdominal pain and changes in bowel habits but lack the mucosal overgrowth seen in polyps.
  • Colorectal cancer: Large, malignant polyps can be confused with colorectal cancer. Cancer tends to present with more advanced symptoms like persistent bleeding, obstruction, and constitutional symptoms.
  • Inflammatory bowel disease (IBD): Inflammatory polyps may be present, but IBD is characterised by chronic diarrhoea, abdominal pain, and systemic symptoms like weight loss or fever.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Polyps"][/sc]
  • Endoscopic polypectomy: Polyps are typically removed during colonoscopy to prevent progression to colorectal cancer.
  • Surveillance: Patients with adenomatous polyps require follow-up colonoscopies every 3-5 years to monitor for new polyps or cancerous changes.
  • Medical treatment (nasal polyps): Nasal polyps are often treated with topical corticosteroids or oral steroids for inflammation control. Surgery is indicated if medical management fails.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Polyps"][/sc]
  • Colorectal cancer: Malignant transformation of adenomatous polyps is the most significant complication, particularly with larger polyps (>1 cm) or those with high-grade dysplasia.
  • Intestinal obstruction: Large polyps can obstruct the bowel, leading to significant morbidity.
  • Post-polypectomy bleeding: A common complication following endoscopic removal of larger polyps, typically managed with endoscopic interventions.
