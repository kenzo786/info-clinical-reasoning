Scleroderma
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Scleroderma"][/sc]
  • Scleroderma, also known as systemic sclerosis, is an autoimmune disease characterised by fibrosis (thickening and hardening of connective tissues), vascular abnormalities, and immune dysregulation. It typically affects the skin, but can also involve internal organs, leading to significant morbidity and potential mortality.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Scleroderma"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Scleroderma"][/sc]
  • Prevalence in UK GP population: Approximately 8-10 cases per 100,000 individuals.
  • Prevalence in UK hospital settings: Scleroderma accounts for around 1% of autoimmune connective tissue disease presentations.
  • Worldwide prevalence: Estimated to be 50-300 cases per million people, varying by region and ethnicity. More common in women (4:1 ratio) and often presents between ages 30-50.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Scleroderma"][/sc]
  • Scleroderma pathogenesis involves three primary mechanisms: autoimmunity, vasculopathy, and fibrosis.
  • Autoimmunity: Dysregulation of the immune system leads to activation of B and T cells, resulting in the production of autoantibodies, which contribute to inflammation and tissue damage.
  • Vasculopathy: Endothelial cell injury triggers vasoconstriction and promotes fibrosis, reducing blood flow. This leads to tissue ischemia and exacerbates damage in affected areas, especially in the skin, lungs, and gastrointestinal tract.
  • Fibrosis: Fibroblast activation results in excessive extracellular matrix deposition, thickening tissues and leading to organ dysfunction. Transforming growth factor-beta (TGF-β) plays a key role in fibrosis development.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Scleroderma"][/sc]
  • Early Phase (months to 1-2 years): Raynaud’s phenomenon, puffy fingers, and mild joint pain typically develop first.
  • Progressive Phase (1-5 years): Skin thickening, multisystem involvement (GI, respiratory, cardiac). Severity depends on subtype (limited or diffuse).
  • Late Phase (5+ years): Chronic organ involvement, particularly with the lungs, heart, and kidneys, can lead to severe complications and impact prognosis.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Scleroderma"][/sc]
  • Raynaud's Phenomenon - Vasospasm of small vessels in response to cold or stress, leading to white-blue-red colour changes in fingers and toes due to reduced blood flow.
  • Skin Thickening - Gradual thickening and hardening of skin, particularly on hands, face, and forearms, due to excess collagen deposition.
  • Joint Pain - Arthralgia due to fibrosis in connective tissues surrounding joints.
  • Gastro-oesophageal Reflux - Oesophageal dysmotility leads to acid reflux and dysphagia, resulting from smooth muscle fibrosis.
  • Dyspnoea - Shortness of breath, often due to lung involvement (interstitial lung disease or pulmonary hypertension).
  • Fatigue - Generalised fatigue related to chronic inflammation and organ involvement.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Scleroderma"][/sc]
  • Skin Changes - Taut, thickened skin, often with a shiny or leathery appearance, especially on the hands and face.
  • Sclerodactyly - Tightening of the skin on the fingers, causing them to appear thin and tapering, leading to restricted finger mobility.
  • Telangiectasia - Small, dilated blood vessels visible on the skin, due to vascular abnormalities.
  • Digital Ulcers - Non-healing sores on the fingertips resulting from poor blood flow and skin integrity.
  • Calcinosis - Calcium deposits under the skin, particularly on fingers, due to abnormal calcium metabolism.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Scleroderma"][/sc]
  • Severe Raynaud’s with Ulceration - May indicate critical ischemia and risk of gangrene.
  • Dyspnoea with Rapid Progression - Suggests severe interstitial lung disease or pulmonary hypertension, which are associated with poor prognosis.
  • Hypertensive Crisis - May signify scleroderma renal crisis, a life-threatening complication characterised by sudden-onset renal failure and hypertension.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Scleroderma"][/sc]
  • Isolated Pulmonary Hypertension - Rare, but can occur without classic skin changes, making diagnosis challenging.
  • Juvenile Scleroderma - Scleroderma presenting in children may lack typical skin findings and more often involve internal organs.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Scleroderma"][/sc]
  • ANA and Scl-70 Antibodies - Positive in most cases, particularly Scl-70 in diffuse cutaneous scleroderma, reflecting autoimmune involvement.
  • Nailfold Capillaroscopy - Shows capillary dropout and dilation, indicating microvascular damage typical of scleroderma.
  • High-Resolution CT (HRCT) of Lungs - Detects interstitial lung disease with characteristic fibrosis patterns.
  • Pulmonary Function Tests (PFTs) - May show reduced forced vital capacity (FVC) in lung involvement and decreased diffusion capacity for carbon monoxide (DLCO) in pulmonary hypertension.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Scleroderma"][/sc]
  • A 42-year-old woman presents with progressive skin thickening over 3 years, Raynaud’s phenomenon, and dyspnoea on exertion, with recent digital ulcers. Blood tests reveal positive ANA and Scl-70 antibodies.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Scleroderma"][/sc]
  • Rheumatoid Arthritis (RA) - RA affects joints with inflammatory pain and swelling; lacks skin fibrosis but may have vasculitis.
  • Mixed Connective Tissue Disease (MCTD) - Overlaps with scleroderma but typically has anti-RNP antibodies, milder skin involvement, and overlapping features of lupus and myositis.
  • Systemic Lupus Erythematosus (SLE) - Can present with Raynaud’s and joint pain, but often has a malar rash and renal disease without skin thickening.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Scleroderma"][/sc]
  • Raynaud’s Phenomenon - Calcium channel blockers (e.g., nifedipine) as first-line therapy to improve blood flow.
  • Skin Fibrosis - Methotrexate or mycophenolate mofetil for immune suppression; low-dose corticosteroids may be used cautiously.
  • Interstitial Lung Disease - Immunosuppressants such as cyclophosphamide or mycophenolate; antifibrotic agents (e.g., nintedanib) in progressive cases.
  • Pulmonary Hypertension - Phosphodiesterase-5 inhibitors or endothelin receptor antagonists; severe cases may require prostacyclin analogues.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Scleroderma"][/sc]
  • Interstitial Lung Disease - Progressive fibrosis leads to respiratory failure; more common in diffuse cutaneous scleroderma.
  • Scleroderma Renal Crisis - Acute renal failure and malignant hypertension, primarily in diffuse scleroderma; may cause end-stage renal disease if not treated.
  • Gastrointestinal Dysmotility - Chronic dysmotility can lead to malnutrition and small intestine bacterial overgrowth (SIBO).
