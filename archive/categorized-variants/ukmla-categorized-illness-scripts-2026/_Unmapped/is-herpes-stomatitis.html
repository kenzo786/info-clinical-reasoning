Herpes Stomatitis illness script
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Herpes Stomatitis"][/sc]
  • Herpes stomatitis is an oral infection caused by the herpes simplex virus (HSV), primarily HSV-1. It is characterised by the sudden onset of fever, irritability, and multiple painful oral and perioral vesicles that ulcerate, typically affecting the lips, tongue, gingiva, and palate. It is most commonly seen in children but can also affect adults.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Herpes Stomatitis"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Herpes Stomatitis"][/sc]
  • In the UK, herpes stomatitis is most frequently seen in children under the age of 5. It is estimated that around 90% of adults worldwide have been exposed to HSV-1 by adulthood, but only a fraction develop clinical disease. In primary care, HSV stomatitis is relatively rare, but oral herpes infections are a common outpatient presentation. Worldwide, HSV-1 is highly prevalent, with significant differences in symptomatic presentation based on geographic location.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Herpes Stomatitis"][/sc]
  • Herpes stomatitis is caused by the herpes simplex virus (HSV-1), which is typically transmitted via direct contact with infected saliva or vesicular fluid. Upon entering the body, the virus invades the mucosal or skin surfaces, replicating within epithelial cells. It then ascends sensory nerves and establishes latency in the trigeminal ganglia. Upon reactivation, the virus travels back down the sensory nerves, leading to recurrent mucocutaneous lesions.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Herpes Stomatitis"][/sc]
  • The typical time course of primary herpes stomatitis involves an incubation period of 2-12 days. It presents acutely with fever and malaise, followed by the appearance of oral vesicles that ulcerate. The active disease typically lasts 10-14 days. After the initial infection, HSV remains dormant in sensory ganglia and may reactivate in the form of recurrent herpes labialis ("cold sores"), often triggered by stress, illness, or immunosuppression.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Herpes Stomatitis"][/sc]
  • Fever - High fever is often the first symptom and occurs due to systemic immune response to viral infection.
  • Malaise and irritability - General feeling of being unwell due to the viral invasion and systemic inflammatory response.
  • Painful oral ulcers - These are caused by the rupture of HSV-induced vesicles, leading to erosion of mucosal surfaces and ulceration.
  • Dysphagia - Painful swallowing occurs due to the oral ulcers affecting the mucosal lining of the pharynx and oral cavity.
  • Halitosis - Bad breath results from bacterial overgrowth and necrotic tissue within the ulcers.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Herpes Stomatitis"][/sc]
  • Vesicular lesions - Small, fluid-filled vesicles on the lips, gingiva, tongue, and hard palate, reflecting viral replication in epithelial cells.
  • Ulcerated lesions - Vesicles rapidly rupture, leading to shallow ulcers with an erythematous border, common in primary infection.
  • Gingivitis - Inflammation of the gums is a hallmark of primary herpes stomatitis, caused by viral damage to the oral mucosa.
  • Lymphadenopathy - Enlarged, tender cervical lymph nodes due to localised immune response.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Herpes Stomatitis"][/sc]
  • Dehydration - Severe dysphagia and painful oral lesions can result in decreased oral intake and dehydration, particularly in young children.
  • Encephalitis - Altered mental status, seizures, and neurological symptoms may indicate HSV encephalitis, a rare but serious complication due to viral spread to the central nervous system.
  • Severe systemic illness - Immunocompromised patients may develop disseminated herpes infection, which can affect multiple organs.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Herpes Stomatitis"][/sc]
  • Mild or asymptomatic cases - Many primary HSV infections, especially in adults, may be asymptomatic or present with mild, non-specific symptoms, making the diagnosis difficult.
  • Recurrent herpes labialis - Reactivation of HSV often presents as cold sores, without systemic symptoms, which is a less severe manifestation than primary stomatitis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Herpes Stomatitis"][/sc]
  • Tzanck smear - Detects multinucleated giant cells in samples from lesions, indicating HSV infection.
  • Polymerase Chain Reaction (PCR) - Highly sensitive test to detect HSV DNA, particularly useful in cases of atypical presentations or complications like encephalitis.
  • HSV culture - Viral culture from lesions can confirm the presence of HSV, although PCR is more commonly used.
  • Serology - May show evidence of past HSV-1 infection, but is not typically helpful in acute management.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Herpes Stomatitis"][/sc]
  • A 3-year-old child presents with a 3-day history of fever, irritability, painful oral ulcers, and refusal to eat. On examination, multiple vesicular lesions are seen on the tongue and lips, along with swollen cervical lymph nodes.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Herpes Stomatitis"][/sc]
  • Hand, foot, and mouth disease - Presents with vesicles on hands and feet in addition to oral ulcers, usually caused by Coxsackie virus.
  • Aphthous stomatitis - Painful oral ulcers but no associated systemic symptoms like fever or lymphadenopathy.
  • Herpangina - Caused by enteroviruses, involves vesicles in the posterior oral cavity, typically without gingivitis.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Herpes Stomatitis"][/sc]
  • Supportive care - Hydration, pain control with paracetamol or ibuprofen, and topical anaesthetic agents like lidocaine gel for oral pain relief.
  • Antiviral therapy - Aciclovir may be used in severe cases, particularly in immunocompromised patients or those presenting early in the disease course.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Herpes Stomatitis"][/sc]
  • Dehydration - Common in children due to difficulty swallowing.
  • HSV encephalitis - A potentially life-threatening complication, especially in immunocompromised patients, requiring urgent antiviral treatment.
  • Ocular involvement - HSV can spread to the eyes, leading to keratitis, which may result in vision loss if untreated.
