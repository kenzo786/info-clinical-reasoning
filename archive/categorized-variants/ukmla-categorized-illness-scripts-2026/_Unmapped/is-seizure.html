Seizure
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Seizure"][/sc]
  • A seizure is a transient, uncontrolled electrical disturbance in the brain that causes changes in behaviour, movements, sensations, or levels of consciousness. It can be provoked (due to an acute cause like metabolic imbalance) or unprovoked (without an immediate cause), often linked to epilepsy when recurrent.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Seizure"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Seizure"][/sc]
  • UK General Practice population: ~1% prevalence of epilepsy (recurrent seizures).
  • UK hospital prevalence: 4-10% of emergency department attendances for neurological complaints.
  • Worldwide: ~50 million people live with epilepsy, with higher prevalence in low-income countries.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Seizure"][/sc]
  • Seizures occur when there is excessive, synchronous neuronal activity in the brain, disrupting normal electrical signalling. This can result from imbalances between excitatory and inhibitory neurotransmitters (e.g., glutamate and GABA), structural abnormalities (e.g., stroke, tumours), or genetic predispositions affecting ion channel functioning.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Seizure"][/sc]
  • Seizures may last from a few seconds to a few minutes. Postictal confusion may follow, lasting minutes to hours. Chronic seizure disorders, such as epilepsy, may persist lifelong, requiring long-term management.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Seizure"][/sc]
  • Loss of consciousness - Sudden loss of awareness due to abnormal cortical activity, common in generalised seizures.
  • Involuntary jerking movements - These occur due to abnormal motor cortex activation, common in tonic-clonic seizures.
  • Aura - Sensory disturbances (e.g., visual or auditory changes) prior to the seizure, often seen in focal seizures, caused by initial abnormal electrical activity.
  • Confusion post-seizure (postictal state) - Due to the brain recovering from widespread neuronal activity, leading to temporary cognitive dysfunction.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Seizure"][/sc]
  • Tonic-clonic movements - Generalised stiffening and rhythmic jerking, indicating widespread neuronal involvement.
  • Tongue biting - Results from involuntary contractions of the jaw muscles during a seizure.
  • Incontinence - Loss of bladder or bowel control due to involuntary muscle relaxation during a seizure.
  • Postictal drowsiness or confusion - Seen after the seizure due to temporary disruption of normal brain activity.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Seizure"][/sc]
  • Prolonged seizure (>5 minutes) - May indicate status epilepticus, which is a medical emergency and can lead to brain damage.
  • Seizures in a febrile patient - Suggests possible CNS infection, such as meningitis or encephalitis.
  • Focal neurological deficits post-seizure - May indicate a structural brain lesion (e.g., tumour or stroke).
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Seizure"][/sc]
  • Absence seizures - Brief episodes of staring and unresponsiveness, often mistaken for daydreaming, due to altered electrical activity in the thalamocortical circuits.
  • Non-convulsive seizures - These present without visible motor symptoms, manifesting instead with confusion or altered consciousness, which can be missed if subtle.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Seizure"][/sc]
  • Electroencephalogram (EEG) - Abnormal electrical activity patterns, such as spikes or sharp waves, help localise the seizure focus.
  • CT or MRI brain - Structural abnormalities like tumours, infarcts, or malformations can be identified as causes of seizures.
  • Blood tests - Assess metabolic disturbances (e.g., hypoglycaemia, electrolyte imbalances) or infections that can provoke seizures.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Seizure"][/sc]
  • A 23-year-old female presents with sudden loss of consciousness, tonic-clonic movements lasting 2 minutes, followed by postictal confusion and headache. No prior history of seizures. She recalls experiencing a brief visual aura beforehand.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Seizure"][/sc]
  • Syncope - Sudden loss of consciousness due to transient cerebral hypoperfusion, often lacking the postictal confusion seen in seizures.
  • Panic attacks - Episodes of intense fear and hyperventilation, without true loss of consciousness or postictal confusion.
  • Transient ischaemic attack (TIA) - Brief neurological deficits, usually without loss of consciousness or postictal state, often in elderly patients.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Seizure"][/sc]
  • Acute management: Benzodiazepines (e.g., lorazepam IV) for seizure termination, followed by phenytoin if prolonged.
  • Long-term management: Anti-epileptic drugs (e.g., sodium valproate, carbamazepine) tailored based on seizure type and patient profile.
  • Lifestyle modifications: Avoidance of triggers (e.g., sleep deprivation, alcohol) and education on seizure precautions.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Seizure"][/sc]
  • Status epilepticus - Prolonged seizure activity (>5 minutes), leading to potential brain damage or death if untreated.
  • Sudden unexpected death in epilepsy (SUDEP) - Rare, but serious complication of poorly controlled epilepsy, linked to respiratory or cardiac dysfunction during or after seizures.
  • Injuries - Falls or trauma during seizures can lead to fractures, head injuries, or burns.
