Medication-Induced Bleeding and Bruising
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Medication-Induced Bleeding and Bruising"][/sc]
  • Medication-induced bleeding and bruising is characterised by spontaneous or exaggerated bleeding due to pharmacological agents that interfere with haemostasis. It can result in mucosal, cutaneous, or internal bleeding, often due to anticoagulants, antiplatelet drugs, or medications affecting blood vessel integrity.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Medication-Induced Bleeding and Bruising"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Medication-Induced Bleeding and Bruising"][/sc]
  • In the UK GP population, medication-induced bleeding is a significant cause of emergency visits, particularly in patients on anticoagulants or antiplatelet therapy. Incidence in the hospital setting is around 2-5% of admissions, with higher rates among elderly patients or those with polypharmacy.
  • Worldwide, medication-induced bleeding is common, especially with increased use of anticoagulants, and contributes to morbidity and mortality in developed countries.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Medication-Induced Bleeding and Bruising"][/sc]
  • Medication-induced bleeding occurs when drugs interfere with haemostatic processes:
  • Anticoagulants (e.g., warfarin, DOACs) inhibit clot formation by affecting clotting factors.
  • Antiplatelet drugs (e.g., aspirin, clopidogrel) impair platelet aggregation, leading to reduced ability to form initial platelet plugs.
  • Medications that impact vessel wall integrity or cause vascular fragility (e.g., corticosteroids) can exacerbate bleeding.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Medication-Induced Bleeding and Bruising"][/sc]
  • Onset: Typically within days to weeks of initiating medication, though it may be delayed depending on drug accumulation.
  • Progression: Symptoms may progressively worsen if medications are continued or drug interactions increase bleeding risk.
  • Resolution: Bleeding symptoms generally resolve within days to weeks of stopping the offending agent, depending on the drug’s half-life and patient’s haemostatic recovery.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Medication-Induced Bleeding and Bruising"][/sc]
  • Easy bruising - Patients often report bruises appearing with minimal or no trauma due to impaired coagulation.
  • Nosebleeds (epistaxis) - Common in those on antiplatelet or anticoagulant therapy as nasal mucosa is prone to trauma and bleeding.
  • Gastrointestinal bleeding - Can manifest as melaena or haematemesis, often exacerbated by NSAIDs or anticoagulants.
  • Haematuria - Occurs when blood in the urine results from anticoagulation or reduced platelet function.
  • Asymptomatic in some cases - Patients may not notice symptoms until significant bleeding occurs.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Medication-Induced Bleeding and Bruising"][/sc]
  • Bruising - Ecchymoses, typically non-tender, can appear on extremities and trunk due to reduced clot formation.
  • Bleeding gums - Often noted with brushing, related to impaired platelet aggregation or coagulopathy.
  • Conjunctival haemorrhage - Small blood vessel rupture in the eye, seen in patients on anticoagulants or antiplatelet drugs.
  • Hepatosplenomegaly - Rare but may indicate underlying liver disease contributing to coagulopathy.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Medication-Induced Bleeding and Bruising"][/sc]
  • Intracranial haemorrhage - Severe headache, altered mental state; a complication of anticoagulation therapy requiring immediate intervention.
  • Severe gastrointestinal bleeding - Presenting with haematochezia or hypotension, indicating substantial blood loss and risk of shock.
  • Haemoptysis - Can signify major bleeding risk if on anticoagulants, especially with concurrent lung pathology.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Medication-Induced Bleeding and Bruising"][/sc]
  • Isolated haematoma - Localised swelling may appear without generalised bleeding signs, often misinterpreted as soft tissue injury.
  • Occult bleeding - Blood loss in internal organs without overt symptoms; common in elderly patients on anticoagulants.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Medication-Induced Bleeding and Bruising"][/sc]
  • Complete Blood Count (CBC) - Low haemoglobin in anaemia; thrombocytopenia if drug-induced platelet suppression is present.
  • Prothrombin Time (PT) and International Normalised Ratio (INR) - Prolonged in warfarin users; indicates anticoagulant effect.
  • Activated Partial Thromboplastin Time (aPTT) - Prolonged in heparin users or those on direct thrombin inhibitors.
  • Liver Function Tests (LFTs) - Elevated in liver disease, which can worsen coagulopathy.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Medication-Induced Bleeding and Bruising"][/sc]
  • A 75-year-old woman presents with spontaneous bruising and epistaxis over two weeks following initiation of warfarin therapy. Notable bleeding risk factors include hypertension and polypharmacy.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Medication-Induced Bleeding and Bruising"][/sc]
  • Primary bleeding disorders (e.g., haemophilia) - Tend to present from a young age and often familial, unlike medication-induced cases.
  • Thrombocytopenia - May present similarly but is characterised by low platelet count not associated with anticoagulant use.
  • Vitamin K deficiency - Causes bleeding with prolonged PT but often related to malabsorption rather than medication.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Medication-Induced Bleeding and Bruising"][/sc]
  • Immediate cessation of offending drug - Discontinuation of anticoagulants or adjustment of dose.
  • Vitamin K or FFP administration - For warfarin-induced bleeding, Vitamin K or fresh frozen plasma (FFP) can help reverse anticoagulation.
  • Platelet transfusion - If platelet function is impaired or platelet count is low due to medication.
  • Tranexamic acid - For minor bleeding; helps reduce fibrinolysis.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Medication-Induced Bleeding and Bruising"][/sc]
  • Severe haemorrhage - Including life-threatening internal bleeding; common in older adults.
  • Anaemia - Chronic blood loss may result in iron-deficiency anaemia, especially if bleeding is occult.
  • Intracranial haemorrhage - Particularly high-risk in patients with fall risk or head trauma.
