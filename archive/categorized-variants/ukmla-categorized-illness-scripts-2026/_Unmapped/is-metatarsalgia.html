Metatarsalgia
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Metatarsalgia"][/sc]
  • Metatarsalgia refers to pain and inflammation localised to the forefoot, specifically around the metatarsal heads. It is typically a symptom rather than a specific diagnosis and can arise from mechanical overload, repetitive stress, or underlying conditions affecting the metatarsophalangeal joints.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Metatarsalgia"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Metatarsalgia"][/sc]
  • UK GP Population: Metatarsalgia accounts for approximately 5-10% of foot-related complaints.
  • UK Hospital Prevalence: Often seen in outpatient orthopaedic and podiatry clinics, particularly in athletes and older adults.
  • Worldwide Prevalence: Prevalence ranges from 10-25% depending on the population, with a higher incidence in individuals involved in high-impact sports or with foot deformities.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Metatarsalgia"][/sc]
  • Metatarsalgia results from increased pressure on the metatarsal heads, leading to inflammation of surrounding soft tissues.
  • Excessive or abnormal loading may stem from biomechanical factors (e.g., high arches, hammer toes), footwear issues, or systemic conditions (e.g., rheumatoid arthritis).
  • Repetitive microtrauma may result in synovitis, capsulitis, or stress fractures in severe cases.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Metatarsalgia"][/sc]
  • Acute Phase: Pain develops rapidly with high-impact activities or new footwear.
  • Chronic Phase: Pain becomes persistent with exacerbations during weight-bearing activities.
  • Resolution Phase: With appropriate management, symptoms improve over weeks to months, though recurrence is possible without addressing underlying factors.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Metatarsalgia"][/sc]
  • Forefoot Pain: Localised to the metatarsal heads, often described as sharp or burning, due to inflammation and tissue irritation.
  • Worsening with Activity: Pain increases with prolonged standing or walking, attributed to repetitive mechanical stress.
  • Relief with Rest: Indicates a mechanical aetiology as weight-bearing exacerbates symptoms.
  • Possible Numbness or Tingling: May indicate nerve compression or Morton’s neuroma.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Metatarsalgia"][/sc]
  • Palpable Tenderness: Over the metatarsal heads, caused by localised inflammation.
  • Callus Formation: At pressure points, indicating chronic overload.
  • Deformities: Such as claw toes or high arches, contributing to abnormal foot mechanics.
  • Positive Mulder's Sign: Clicking or pain when squeezing the forefoot, suggestive of Morton’s neuroma.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Metatarsalgia"][/sc]
  • Severe Swelling or Erythema: May suggest septic arthritis or cellulitis.
  • Persistent Pain Despite Rest: Could indicate a stress fracture or tumour.
  • Systemic Symptoms: Fever or malaise may point to an inflammatory or infectious cause.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Metatarsalgia"][/sc]
  • Pain Radiating Up the Leg: May lead to misdiagnosis of sciatica.
  • No Pain but Numbness: Could reflect neuropathy or Morton’s neuroma.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Metatarsalgia"][/sc]
  • X-Ray: To exclude fractures, arthritis, or deformities (e.g., hallux valgus).
  • Ultrasound: Useful for diagnosing Morton’s neuroma or soft tissue inflammation.
  • MRI: Reserved for cases where stress fractures or soft tissue pathology (e.g., tumours) are suspected.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Metatarsalgia"][/sc]
  • A 45-year-old female presents with a 3-month history of forefoot pain, worsened by walking and alleviated by rest. No systemic symptoms reported. Examination reveals tenderness over the second metatarsal head and callus formation.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Metatarsalgia"][/sc]
  • Morton’s Neuroma: Differs by presence of numbness and positive Mulder’s sign.
  • Stress Fracture: Pain persists at rest, confirmed on imaging.
  • Rheumatoid Arthritis: Bilateral symptoms, systemic involvement, elevated inflammatory markers.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Metatarsalgia"][/sc]
  • Conservative: Footwear modification, orthotic insoles, activity modification.
  • Medications: Analgesics (paracetamol, NSAIDs).
  • Rehabilitation: Physiotherapy to address biomechanical issues.
  • Surgical: Reserved for refractory cases, e.g., osteotomy or neurectomy.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Metatarsalgia"][/sc]
  • Chronic Pain: Due to untreated biomechanical abnormalities.
  • Stress Fractures: From ongoing overload.
  • Deformities: Progression of claw toes or bunions.
