Medication Overuse Headache (MOH)
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Medication Overuse Headache (MOH)"][/sc]
  • Medication Overuse Headache (MOH) is a secondary headache disorder caused by the excessive or inappropriate use of acute headache medications. MOH develops when patients use pain-relief medications frequently (≥10–15 days per month) for more than three months, leading to the transformation of episodic headaches (commonly migraine or tension-type) into chronic, persistent headaches.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Medication Overuse Headache (MOH)"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Medication Overuse Headache (MOH)"][/sc]
  • In the UK, MOH affects approximately 1-2% of the general population.
  • It accounts for 30-50% of chronic daily headaches seen in neurology clinics.
  • Globally, the prevalence of MOH is estimated to be 1-3% of the population, with a higher prevalence in females (3-4 times more common).
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Medication Overuse Headache (MOH)"][/sc]
  • MOH occurs due to the chronic overuse of acute pain medications, which alters pain modulation mechanisms in the brain. Overuse of medications such as triptans, opioids, or simple analgesics can lead to central sensitisation, reduced pain thresholds, and increased headache frequency.
  • Repeated use of these medications may also cause neurochemical changes, particularly in serotonin and dopamine pathways, further exacerbating headache patterns.
  • This leads to a 'rebound' phenomenon, where the cessation of medication worsens the headache, prompting further use, creating a vicious cycle.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Medication Overuse Headache (MOH)"][/sc]
  • Early Phase: Episodic headaches initially respond to acute medication.
  • Chronic Phase: With repeated use, headaches become more frequent, eventually occurring daily or almost daily.
  • Post-treatment Phase: Following medication withdrawal, headaches may worsen temporarily but improve within weeks to months.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Medication Overuse Headache (MOH)"][/sc]
  • Daily or near-daily headaches – Often persistent and of varying intensity. This occurs due to central sensitisation and neurochemical changes in the brain.
  • Headache upon waking – Common as medication wears off overnight, leading to 'rebound' headaches.
  • Headache worsening with medication use – The very medication intended to relieve headaches triggers and perpetuates them.
  • Restlessness, nausea, or irritability – Due to fluctuating levels of medication in the system and withdrawal effects.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Medication Overuse Headache (MOH)"][/sc]
  • No specific neurological signs – Neurological examination is usually normal, helping differentiate MOH from other causes of secondary headaches.
  • Signs of medication overuse – Patients may carry medications or report frequent use of over-the-counter drugs.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Medication Overuse Headache (MOH)"][/sc]
  • Sudden-onset, severe headache – May indicate a subarachnoid haemorrhage or other life-threatening causes.
  • Neurological deficits – Such as weakness or vision changes, which suggest alternative diagnoses like stroke, brain tumour, or giant cell arteritis.
  • Fever and stiff neck – Suggests meningitis or encephalitis.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Medication Overuse Headache (MOH)"][/sc]
  • Patients denying overuse – Some patients may underreport medication use due to unawareness of its overuse.
  • Headache without 'overuse' amounts – In rare cases, headaches may occur even when medications are not used in typical overuse quantities.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Medication Overuse Headache (MOH)"][/sc]
  • No definitive diagnostic tests – MOH is diagnosed clinically based on patient history and the pattern of medication use.
  • Brain imaging – MRI or CT may be done to exclude other causes of chronic headaches but is usually normal in MOH.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Medication Overuse Headache (MOH)"][/sc]
  • A 42-year-old female with a history of episodic migraines presents with daily headaches for the past 6 months. She takes triptans 5 days per week and paracetamol almost daily, with worsening symptoms over time. She experiences headaches upon waking, worsened by medication use.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Medication Overuse Headache (MOH)"][/sc]
  • Chronic Migraine – Less frequent medication use, with episodic history of migraines still evident.
  • Tension-type headache – No worsening with medication use, typically less intense than MOH.
  • Cluster headache – Severe, unilateral headaches with autonomic symptoms, and no clear relationship with medication overuse.
  • Sinus headache – Accompanied by nasal symptoms or sinusitis; does not respond to typical headache treatments.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Medication Overuse Headache (MOH)"][/sc]
  • Medication withdrawal – Gradual or abrupt withdrawal of overused medications under medical supervision.
  • Preventive therapy – Introduction of headache preventive treatments such as topiramate, amitriptyline, or propranolol to reduce headache frequency.
  • Education and behavioural therapy – Education on avoiding future overuse and lifestyle changes to manage headache triggers.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Medication Overuse Headache (MOH)"][/sc]
  • Chronic daily headache – Persistent, intractable headaches without proper treatment.
  • Medication dependence – Psychological or physiological dependence on analgesics or triptans.
  • Reduced quality of life – Significant impact on personal and professional life due to chronic headache and medication overuse.
