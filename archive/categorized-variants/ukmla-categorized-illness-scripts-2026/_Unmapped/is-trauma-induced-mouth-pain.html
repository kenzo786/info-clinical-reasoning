Trauma-Induced Mouth Pain
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Trauma-Induced Mouth Pain"][/sc]
  • Trauma-induced mouth pain refers to acute or chronic pain in the oral cavity resulting from direct injury to the tissues, teeth, or bones of the mouth. Common causes include accidental biting, dental procedures, blunt force trauma, or burns from hot foods or chemicals. The condition is characterised by localised pain, swelling, and potential complications like infection or bleeding.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Trauma-Induced Mouth Pain"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Trauma-Induced Mouth Pain"][/sc]
  • In the UK GP population, trauma-induced mouth pain is a common presentation, particularly in children and adults engaging in contact sports. Dental trauma occurs in approximately 5% of all injuries treated in A&E. Worldwide, oral injuries are common, with prevalence higher in areas where safety measures in sports and transportation are less robust.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Trauma-Induced Mouth Pain"][/sc]
  • The injury leads to direct damage to the mucosal lining, teeth, or bones, causing inflammation, pain, and sometimes disruption of nerve endings. This inflammatory response triggers swelling, redness, and potential disruption of oral function. In cases of severe trauma, bones may fracture or teeth may dislodge, leading to further complications like infection or tissue necrosis.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Trauma-Induced Mouth Pain"][/sc]
  • Immediately post-trauma: Acute pain, swelling, and possible bleeding. The severity of symptoms depends on the extent of injury.
  • 1-3 days post-trauma: Swelling and inflammation peak. If the injury is minor, healing begins, with pain subsiding after this period. Severe injuries may show signs of infection.
  • 7-10 days post-trauma: Most soft tissue injuries begin healing with pain and inflammation gradually reducing.
  • Chronic phase: In cases of severe or untreated trauma, chronic pain, non-healing wounds, or complications like abscesses may develop.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Trauma-Induced Mouth Pain"][/sc]
  • Pain - Acute, localised pain due to nerve irritation and inflammation.
  • Swelling - Inflammatory response to tissue damage leading to oedema in the affected area.
  • Bleeding - Immediate after trauma due to ruptured capillaries in soft tissues.
  • Difficulty chewing - Pain and swelling may impair normal oral function.
  • Tooth sensitivity or fracture - Dental trauma can lead to exposed dentin or fractures, resulting in heightened sensitivity or sharp pain.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Trauma-Induced Mouth Pain"][/sc]
  • Swelling of oral mucosa - Visible oedema as a result of inflammation from trauma.
  • Ecchymosis or bruising - Submucosal bleeding manifesting as purple or blue discolouration of oral tissues.
  • Loose or fractured teeth - Physical signs of dental injury often visible or detected upon examination.
  • Bleeding - Fresh blood or dried clots may be seen, depending on the timing of the trauma.
  • Restricted jaw movement - In cases of mandibular trauma or severe swelling, jaw movement may be impaired.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Trauma-Induced Mouth Pain"][/sc]
  • Persistent or worsening pain - May indicate infection, abscess formation, or deeper tissue injury like a fracture.
  • Systemic signs of infection - Fever, malaise, or lymphadenopathy suggest a secondary infection, which can progress to cellulitis or sepsis.
  • Numbness or paraesthesia - Could suggest nerve damage, particularly the inferior alveolar nerve in mandibular trauma.
  • Difficulty breathing or swallowing - May indicate airway compromise or retropharyngeal haematoma, which is a medical emergency.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Trauma-Induced Mouth Pain"][/sc]
  • Chronic, dull pain - Instead of acute pain, some individuals may present with persistent, dull pain from undetected fractures or chronic inflammation.
  • Delayed swelling or infection - In some cases, swelling or signs of infection may occur several days after the initial trauma due to delayed bacterial colonisation.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Trauma-Induced Mouth Pain"][/sc]
  • Dental X-ray - Used to identify fractures, dislocations, or tooth root damage. A panoramic radiograph may show mandibular fractures.
  • CT scan - For severe trauma, especially involving the jaw or facial bones, to assess for fractures or deep tissue damage.
  • Oral examination - Key for identifying soft tissue injuries, loose teeth, and overall structural damage.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Trauma-Induced Mouth Pain"][/sc]
  • A 25-year-old male presents with sharp mouth pain, swelling, and difficulty chewing after a fall while playing football. Examination reveals ecchymosis and a loose upper incisor. No systemic symptoms are present.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Trauma-Induced Mouth Pain"][/sc]
  • Dental caries - Unlike trauma-induced pain, caries pain is usually progressive and linked to sensitivity rather than acute trauma.
  • Temporomandibular joint dysfunction (TMD) - TMD typically presents with jaw pain and clicking, rather than direct oral trauma.
  • Oral infections (e.g., abscess) - Presents with systemic signs like fever and severe, localised pain, often in the absence of trauma.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Trauma-Induced Mouth Pain"][/sc]
  • Analgesia - First-line management involves pain relief using paracetamol or ibuprofen.
  • Antibiotics - If infection is suspected or present, prescribe antibiotics like amoxicillin or metronidazole.
  • Referral to a dentist or maxillofacial surgeon - Urgent if there is dental injury, fractures, or persistent bleeding.
  • Soft diet and avoidance of trauma - Recommended to reduce pressure on the injury during healing.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Trauma-Induced Mouth Pain"][/sc]
  • Infection - Trauma can lead to bacterial invasion and abscess formation.
  • Chronic pain - Due to nerve damage or persistent inflammation.
  • Tooth loss - Severe trauma may result in avulsed or non-recoverable teeth.
  • Malocclusion - Resulting from untreated dental or jaw fractures.
