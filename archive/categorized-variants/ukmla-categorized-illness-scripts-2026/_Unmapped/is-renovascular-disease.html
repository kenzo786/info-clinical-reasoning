Renovascular Disease
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Renovascular Disease"][/sc]
  • Renovascular disease (RVD) refers to the narrowing or blockage of one or both renal arteries, often due to atherosclerosis or fibromuscular dysplasia. This condition reduces blood flow to the kidneys, leading to ischaemia and secondary hypertension. RVD can lead to progressive renal impairment if untreated.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Renovascular Disease"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Renovascular Disease"][/sc]
  • Prevalence in UK GP population: ~2-5% of hypertension cases, higher in older adults and individuals with atherosclerotic disease.
  • UK Hospital Prevalence: Seen in 5-15% of patients undergoing renal angiography for resistant hypertension.
  • Worldwide Prevalence: Similar rates globally, with increased prevalence in populations with high atherosclerosis incidence.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Renovascular Disease"][/sc]
  • Renovascular disease usually results from either atherosclerosis (more common in older patients) or fibromuscular dysplasia (more common in younger, especially female, patients).
  • In atherosclerosis, plaque narrows the renal arteries, causing reduced perfusion, which activates the renin-angiotensin-aldosterone system (RAAS) and raises blood pressure.
  • Fibromuscular dysplasia leads to stenotic lesions in renal arteries, reducing blood flow similarly, activating RAAS, and leading to hypertension.
  • Chronic renal hypoperfusion can lead to irreversible kidney damage, contributing to progressive chronic kidney disease (CKD).
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Renovascular Disease"][/sc]
  • Early Stage: Often asymptomatic; may present with new or worsening hypertension.
  • Intermediate Stage: Hypertension becomes resistant to treatment; some patients may experience intermittent renal function decline.
  • Late Stage: Significant renal impairment may develop, leading to end-stage renal disease in severe cases if untreated.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Renovascular Disease"][/sc]
  • Hypertension (often resistant to treatment) - Due to RAAS activation, hypertension is often the first and most prominent symptom.
  • Fatigue - Due to decreased renal function and hypertension, resulting in reduced overall organ perfusion.
  • Headache - Often related to severe or resistant hypertension.
  • Signs of kidney impairment (e.g., oedema, uraemic symptoms) - Reduced renal perfusion over time can lead to CKD, manifesting as fluid overload and uraemia.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Renovascular Disease"][/sc]
  • Resistant hypertension - Hypertension that does not respond to three or more antihypertensive agents.
  • Abdominal bruit - A sign of turbulent blood flow due to narrowed renal arteries; detected on auscultation.
  • Renal impairment (elevated creatinine) - Indicative of chronic kidney disease if renovascular disease persists.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Renovascular Disease"][/sc]
  • Sudden onset or worsening of hypertension in patients under 30 or over 55 - May indicate underlying fibromuscular dysplasia or advanced atherosclerosis.
  • Acute kidney injury after starting ACE inhibitors or ARBs - Suggests renal artery stenosis, as reduced glomerular perfusion worsens renal function in these patients.
  • Flash pulmonary oedema - Due to sudden volume overload in cases of bilateral renovascular disease.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Renovascular Disease"][/sc]
  • Non-resistant hypertension with renal impairment - Can present without resistant hypertension, especially in advanced CKD cases.
  • Normal renal function in early fibromuscular dysplasia - Renal impairment may not be evident if stenosis is mild.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Renovascular Disease"][/sc]
  • Renal Doppler ultrasound - Can detect abnormal blood flow in the renal arteries, useful in initial assessment.
  • MR or CT angiography - Used to visualise stenosis directly; MR angiography is preferred to avoid nephrotoxic contrast in CKD patients.
  • Serum creatinine and eGFR - To assess renal function; elevated creatinine and reduced eGFR are indicative of kidney impairment.
  • Plasma renin activity (PRA) - Elevated in renovascular disease due to RAAS activation.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Renovascular Disease"][/sc]
  • A 68-year-old male with a history of poorly controlled hypertension presents with worsening renal function and an abdominal bruit on examination.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Renovascular Disease"][/sc]
  • Essential hypertension - Lacks secondary features like bruits or rapid onset.
  • Chronic kidney disease - May present similarly but often without hypertension or abdominal bruit unless due to renovascular cause.
  • Fibromuscular dysplasia - Typically affects younger females and involves non-atherosclerotic stenosis.
  • Phaeochromocytoma - Presents with episodic hypertension, often with headache, palpitations, and sweating.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Renovascular Disease"][/sc]
  • Medical therapy - Includes antihypertensive agents, with ACE inhibitors/ARBs (cautiously in unilateral disease), calcium channel blockers, and beta-blockers.
  • Revascularisation - Angioplasty or surgical revascularisation may be indicated in severe cases, especially in flash pulmonary oedema or worsening renal function.
  • Manage atherosclerosis risk factors - Lipid-lowering therapy, antiplatelet agents, and lifestyle modifications.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Renovascular Disease"][/sc]
  • Chronic kidney disease (CKD) - Due to prolonged renal ischaemia.
  • Cardiovascular complications - Uncontrolled hypertension leads to left ventricular hypertrophy, heart failure, and increased risk of stroke.
  • End-stage renal disease (ESRD) - Occurs if renal function declines irreversibly.
