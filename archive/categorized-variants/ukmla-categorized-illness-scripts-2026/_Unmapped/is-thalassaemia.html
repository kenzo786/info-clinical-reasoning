Thalassaemia
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Thalassaemia"][/sc]
  • Thalassaemia is a group of inherited blood disorders caused by defects in the synthesis of haemoglobin chains, leading to ineffective erythropoiesis and varying degrees of anaemia. It is classified into alpha and beta thalassaemia depending on the defective globin chain. The condition ranges from asymptomatic carriers to severe anaemia requiring lifelong transfusions.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Thalassaemia"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Thalassaemia"][/sc]
  • Prevalence in the UK: Thalassaemia is relatively uncommon in the general population, with an estimated prevalence of 0.1% for beta thalassaemia. However, carrier status can be more prevalent in communities of Mediterranean, South Asian, and African descent.
  • Worldwide prevalence: Higher in regions like the Mediterranean, Middle East, South Asia, and Southeast Asia. Beta thalassaemia is particularly common in Cyprus, where up to 14% of the population are carriers.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Thalassaemia"][/sc]
  • Thalassaemia results from mutations in the genes responsible for alpha or beta globin chains. In alpha thalassaemia, there is decreased production of alpha chains, while beta thalassaemia is characterised by reduced or absent beta-globin synthesis. The imbalance between globin chains leads to ineffective erythropoiesis, haemolysis, and anaemia. The excess chains precipitate in red blood cells (RBCs), leading to destruction of developing erythroblasts in the bone marrow and shortened lifespan of mature RBCs in circulation.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Thalassaemia"][/sc]
  • Neonatal period: Most patients are asymptomatic at birth because of the predominant production of fetal haemoglobin (HbF), which does not require beta globin.
  • Infancy to early childhood: Symptoms such as anaemia, growth retardation, and failure to thrive develop as fetal haemoglobin declines and adult haemoglobin production begins.
  • Adolescence and adulthood: Without appropriate treatment, chronic anaemia, organ damage from iron overload (secondary to transfusions), and skeletal deformities can develop.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Thalassaemia"][/sc]
  • Fatigue and weakness - Due to chronic anaemia from ineffective erythropoiesis, patients often present with these symptoms.
  • Pallor - Reduced haemoglobin levels cause decreased oxygen delivery to tissues, resulting in pale skin.
  • Jaundice - Excessive haemolysis leads to increased bilirubin production, causing yellowing of the skin and sclera.
  • Shortness of breath - Anaemia leads to decreased oxygen-carrying capacity, manifesting as dyspnoea, especially during exertion.
  • Bone pain and deformities - The body attempts to compensate for the anaemia by expanding the bone marrow, leading to skeletal changes, particularly in the face and skull.
  • Abdominal distension - Due to splenomegaly, as the spleen works harder to filter out abnormal RBCs.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Thalassaemia"][/sc]
  • Pallor - Indicates anaemia due to low haemoglobin levels.
  • Splenomegaly - The spleen is enlarged as it filters out abnormal erythrocytes and extramedullary haematopoiesis occurs.
  • Hepatomegaly - Due to extramedullary haematopoiesis and iron overload from frequent blood transfusions.
  • Growth retardation - Chronic anaemia and ineffective erythropoiesis impair normal growth and development.
  • Skeletal deformities - "Chipmunk facies" and frontal bossing result from expanded bone marrow to increase RBC production.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Thalassaemia"][/sc]
  • Severe anaemia - Sudden worsening of anaemia may indicate an aplastic crisis, commonly triggered by parvovirus B19 infection.
  • Iron overload - Occurs due to frequent transfusions, leading to endocrine complications (e.g., diabetes), liver cirrhosis, and cardiac failure.
  • Heart failure - A result of chronic severe anaemia or iron overload, requiring urgent intervention.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Thalassaemia"][/sc]
  • Patients with thalassaemia minor or trait may remain asymptomatic or present with mild anaemia that can be mistaken for iron-deficiency anaemia. They typically present with microcytosis without significant clinical symptoms.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Thalassaemia"][/sc]
  • Full blood count (FBC) - Microcytic, hypochromic anaemia with low mean corpuscular volume (MCV) and mean corpuscular haemoglobin (MCH).
  • Blood film - Target cells and nucleated RBCs indicate thalassaemia. Basophilic stippling may also be seen.
  • Haemoglobin electrophoresis - Identifies abnormal haemoglobin variants (e.g., HbA2 elevation in beta thalassaemia).
  • Genetic testing - Confirms mutations in alpha or beta globin genes.
  • Iron studies - To differentiate from iron deficiency, as iron levels are typically normal or elevated in thalassaemia.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Thalassaemia"][/sc]
  • A 2-year-old child of Mediterranean descent presents with pallor, fatigue, and failure to thrive. Full blood count shows microcytic anaemia, and haemoglobin electrophoresis reveals increased HbA2, consistent with beta thalassaemia major.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Thalassaemia"][/sc]
  • Iron-deficiency anaemia - Also presents with microcytic anaemia, but iron studies will show low serum iron and ferritin, differentiating it from thalassaemia.
  • Sideroblastic anaemia - Microcytic anaemia with ringed sideroblasts on bone marrow biopsy; iron overload is common, unlike in thalassaemia where iron overload is transfusion-related.
  • Lead poisoning - Causes microcytic anaemia with basophilic stippling, but blood lead levels help differentiate this from thalassaemia.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Thalassaemia"][/sc]
  • Regular blood transfusions - Lifelong transfusions to maintain haemoglobin levels and prevent complications like bone marrow expansion and skeletal deformities.
  • Iron chelation therapy - To manage iron overload from repeated transfusions (e.g., deferoxamine, deferasirox).
  • Folic acid supplementation - Supports erythropoiesis in patients with chronic anaemia.
  • Bone marrow transplant - The only curative option for thalassaemia major, usually performed in childhood.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Thalassaemia"][/sc]
  • Iron overload - A common complication due to transfusions, leading to cardiac, hepatic, and endocrine dysfunction.
  • Cardiac failure - Due to chronic anaemia or iron deposition in cardiac tissue.
  • Bone deformities - Untreated or inadequately transfused patients may develop skeletal abnormalities due to bone marrow expansion.
  • Infections - Especially in post-splenectomy patients or those on iron chelation therapy.
