Addison's Disease
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Addison's Disease"][/sc]
  • Addison’s disease, or primary adrenal insufficiency, is a rare endocrine disorder where the adrenal glands fail to produce sufficient amounts of cortisol and aldosterone. This condition leads to a variety of systemic symptoms, including fatigue, hypotension, and electrolyte imbalances, due to the loss of glucocorticoid and mineralocorticoid function.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Addison's Disease"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Addison's Disease"][/sc]
  • In the UK, the prevalence of Addison’s disease in the general population is approximately 1 in 10,000, making it a rare condition.
  • Worldwide, it is estimated to affect between 4-6 per 100,000 people.
  • Hospital admissions in the UK related to adrenal insufficiency are also rare, but Addisonian crisis can lead to emergency hospitalisation.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Addison's Disease"][/sc]
  • Addison's disease is primarily caused by autoimmune destruction of the adrenal cortex, resulting in decreased production of glucocorticoids (cortisol) and mineralocorticoids (aldosterone).
  • The loss of cortisol leads to impaired glucose metabolism, stress response, and inflammation regulation.
  • The absence of aldosterone causes dysregulation of sodium, potassium, and water balance, leading to hyponatraemia, hyperkalaemia, and hypotension.
  • The adrenal medulla is typically unaffected, so adrenaline and noradrenaline production remains intact.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Addison's Disease"][/sc]
  • Addison's disease often has an insidious onset, with symptoms gradually worsening over months to years.
  • Early symptoms such as fatigue and hyperpigmentation may be subtle, but if untreated, the condition can progress to an Addisonian crisis, which is a life-threatening emergency.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Addison's Disease"][/sc]
  • Fatigue - Common and progressive, resulting from decreased cortisol levels and impaired energy metabolism.
  • Weight loss - A result of anorexia and dehydration due to electrolyte imbalances and cortisol deficiency.
  • Hyperpigmentation - Increased ACTH stimulates melanocytes, leading to darker skin, especially in sun-exposed areas.
  • Salt craving - Due to aldosterone deficiency causing sodium loss through urine.
  • Dizziness and hypotension - Secondary to aldosterone deficiency, leading to reduced blood volume and vascular tone.
  • Muscle weakness - Caused by electrolyte disturbances (hyponatraemia, hyperkalaemia) and glucocorticoid deficiency.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Addison's Disease"][/sc]
  • Postural hypotension - Reflects hypovolaemia due to aldosterone deficiency, leading to a drop in blood pressure upon standing.
  • Hyperpigmentation - Particularly noticeable in skin folds, scars, and mucous membranes, linked to elevated ACTH levels.
  • Dehydration - Due to fluid loss from sodium wasting and aldosterone deficiency.
  • Weight loss - Visible due to chronic dehydration, anorexia, and muscle wasting from cortisol deficiency.
  • Vitiligo - Sometimes present due to the autoimmune nature of the disease.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Addison's Disease"][/sc]
  • Addisonian Crisis - Acute onset of severe hypotension, shock, and confusion, often triggered by infection, surgery, or trauma. It signifies critical cortisol deficiency and can lead to death if untreated.
  • Severe hyponatraemia - May cause confusion, seizures, and coma due to cerebral oedema.
  • Hyperkalaemia - Significant potassium elevation can result in cardiac arrhythmias, which may be life-threatening.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Addison's Disease"][/sc]
  • Isolated fatigue and weight loss - Without hyperpigmentation or electrolyte abnormalities, Addison's disease may be mistaken for chronic fatigue syndrome or depression.
  • Intermittent gastrointestinal symptoms - Such as nausea, vomiting, and diarrhoea, which may lead to misdiagnosis as a gastrointestinal disorder.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Addison's Disease"][/sc]
  • Serum cortisol - Low morning cortisol (<100 nmol/L) is suggestive of adrenal insufficiency.
  • ACTH stimulation test (Synacthen test) - A failure to raise cortisol levels after administration of synthetic ACTH confirms primary adrenal insufficiency.
  • Serum electrolytes - Hyponatraemia and hyperkalaemia due to aldosterone deficiency.
  • Adrenal antibodies - Presence of 21-hydroxylase antibodies indicates autoimmune adrenalitis, the most common cause of Addison's disease.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Addison's Disease"][/sc]
  • A 45-year-old woman presents with a 6-month history of increasing fatigue, weight loss, and hyperpigmentation. She reports dizziness on standing and salt cravings. Blood tests reveal hyponatraemia and hyperkalaemia, with a low morning cortisol.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Addison's Disease"][/sc]
  • Secondary adrenal insufficiency - Caused by pituitary dysfunction; differs as aldosterone levels are typically normal, so electrolyte imbalances are absent.
  • Chronic fatigue syndrome - Overlaps with fatigue and weight loss, but lacks hyperpigmentation and electrolyte disturbances.
  • Hyperthyroidism - May cause weight loss and fatigue but is usually associated with hyperactivity and tachycardia rather than hypotension.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Addison's Disease"][/sc]
  • Hydrocortisone - Glucocorticoid replacement therapy to mimic normal cortisol levels. Doses vary, but patients typically take 15-30 mg/day in divided doses.
  • Fludrocortisone - Mineralocorticoid replacement to manage aldosterone deficiency, usually 50-200 mcg/day.
  • Crisis prevention - Increased doses of hydrocortisone during stress, illness, or surgery to prevent Addisonian crisis.
  • Education - Patients should be trained to recognise the symptoms of an impending crisis and carry emergency hydrocortisone injections.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Addison's Disease"][/sc]
  • Addisonian Crisis - Most serious complication, with risk of hypovolaemic shock and death without treatment.
  • Osteoporosis - Long-term steroid therapy increases the risk of osteoporosis due to effects on bone metabolism.
  • Infections - Patients are at increased risk of infections due to immunosuppressive effects of corticosteroid therapy.
