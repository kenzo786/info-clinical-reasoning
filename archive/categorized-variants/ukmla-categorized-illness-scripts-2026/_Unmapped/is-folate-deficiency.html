Folate Deficiency
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Folate Deficiency"][/sc]
  • Folate deficiency, also known as folic acid deficiency, refers to a decreased level of folate in the body, leading to impaired DNA synthesis, particularly affecting rapidly dividing cells such as those in the bone marrow. This deficiency can result in megaloblastic anaemia, a condition characterised by large, immature red blood cells. Folate is a water-soluble B vitamin (vitamin B9) crucial for the production of nucleotides, amino acids, and the metabolism of homocysteine.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Folate Deficiency"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Folate Deficiency"][/sc]
  • In the UK GP population, folate deficiency is relatively rare but may affect certain at-risk groups such as pregnant women, the elderly, and those with alcohol dependence. Prevalence in the UK is estimated at approximately 2-5% of the general population. Hospital prevalence is higher due to associated conditions such as malabsorption syndromes and chronic illness. Worldwide, folate deficiency remains a significant public health concern, particularly in low-income countries where folate-rich foods may be less available.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Folate Deficiency"][/sc]
  • Folate is necessary for the synthesis of purines and pyrimidines, which are the building blocks of DNA. In folate deficiency, this process is disrupted, particularly affecting cells with high turnover rates like erythroid precursors in the bone marrow. This results in defective DNA synthesis and the development of megaloblastic anaemia, characterised by large, immature red blood cells. Inadequate folate also leads to the accumulation of homocysteine, which has been associated with cardiovascular risks.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Folate Deficiency"][/sc]
  • Folate deficiency typically develops over weeks to months, depending on dietary intake and body stores. In mild cases, symptoms may be minimal or absent. Over time, if untreated, anaemia and its associated symptoms worsen. Neurological manifestations, such as cognitive changes, can develop if deficiency persists.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Folate Deficiency"][/sc]
  • Fatigue - Due to decreased oxygen-carrying capacity from anaemia, tissues receive less oxygen, leading to fatigue and weakness.
  • Pallor - Reduced haemoglobin levels result in pale skin and mucous membranes.
  • Shortness of breath - As anaemia progresses, reduced oxygen delivery triggers compensatory mechanisms, leading to dyspnoea on exertion.
  • Glossitis - Folate deficiency can cause inflammation of the tongue, leading to a smooth, sore tongue.
  • Neurological symptoms - In severe cases, cognitive dysfunction, irritability, and depression may occur due to altered neurotransmitter synthesis and homocysteine accumulation.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Folate Deficiency"][/sc]
  • Pallor - Visible paleness of the skin and conjunctiva due to anaemia.
  • Smooth, swollen tongue (Glossitis) - Inflammation of the tongue is common in severe folate deficiency.
  • Tachycardia - Increased heart rate as a compensatory response to anaemia to enhance oxygen delivery.
  • Angular stomatitis - Cracks or sores at the corners of the mouth can occur due to mucosal atrophy.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Folate Deficiency"][/sc]
  • Neurological symptoms - Persistent cognitive changes, paraesthesia, or ataxia may suggest long-standing folate deficiency and potential irreversible nerve damage.
  • Severe anaemia - Marked pallor, weakness, and cardiovascular strain (e.g., heart failure) indicate profound deficiency.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Folate Deficiency"][/sc]
  • Isolated neurological symptoms - Patients may present with cognitive or mood disturbances before overt haematological signs develop, particularly in elderly individuals.
  • Macrocytosis without anaemia - In some cases, the only laboratory finding may be enlarged red blood cells, without symptoms of anaemia.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Folate Deficiency"][/sc]
  • Full blood count (FBC) - Shows macrocytic anaemia with a mean corpuscular volume (MCV) > 100 fL. Reticulocyte count may be low.
  • Serum folate levels - Low serum folate (<3 ng/mL or <7 nmol/L) confirms the diagnosis.
  • Serum homocysteine - Elevated homocysteine levels (>15 μmol/L) due to impaired methylation reactions.
  • Bone marrow biopsy (rarely needed) - Shows megaloblastic changes if performed.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Folate Deficiency"][/sc]
  • A 55-year-old woman presents with a 3-month history of increasing fatigue, shortness of breath on exertion, and occasional palpitations. She reports a vegetarian diet and a recent period of poor appetite. Examination reveals pallor and a smooth, swollen tongue.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Folate Deficiency"][/sc]
  • Vitamin B12 deficiency - Also causes macrocytic anaemia but with neurological signs such as numbness and balance problems. Serum B12 levels will be low, distinguishing it from folate deficiency.
  • Iron deficiency anaemia - Results in microcytic anaemia, often with low ferritin and iron levels, differing from the macrocytosis seen in folate deficiency.
  • Myelodysplastic syndromes - Can present with macrocytosis and pancytopenia but usually have additional cytopenias and abnormal bone marrow findings.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Folate Deficiency"][/sc]
  • Oral folic acid - 5 mg daily for 4 months is typically recommended. Continue supplementation for longer in cases of chronic malabsorption.
  • Dietary advice - Increase intake of folate-rich foods such as leafy green vegetables, legumes, and fortified cereals.
  • Address underlying causes - If malabsorption or other conditions are contributing, these must be treated alongside folate replacement.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Folate Deficiency"][/sc]
  • Severe anaemia - Can lead to heart failure, particularly in elderly patients.
  • Neurocognitive deficits - Persistent folate deficiency can cause irreversible cognitive decline and psychiatric disturbances if untreated.
  • Hyperhomocysteinaemia - Elevated homocysteine levels increase the risk of cardiovascular disease and venous thromboembolism.
