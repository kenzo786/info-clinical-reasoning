Keratitis (Bacterial)
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Keratitis (Bacterial)"][/sc]
  • Bacterial keratitis is an infection of the cornea caused by bacterial pathogens, typically associated with corneal epithelial disruption. It is characterised by inflammation, pain, and visual impairment, and can lead to permanent vision loss if untreated.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Keratitis (Bacterial)"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Keratitis (Bacterial)"][/sc]
  • UK GP prevalence: Rare, but higher in contact lens wearers (approximately 2–5 cases per 10,000 annually).
  • UK hospital prevalence: Seen more commonly in emergency ophthalmology, accounting for up to 65% of infectious keratitis cases.
  • Worldwide prevalence: Varies, with higher rates in tropical regions due to environmental factors.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Keratitis (Bacterial)"][/sc]
  • Disruption of the corneal epithelium (e.g., from trauma or contact lens use) allows bacterial pathogens to invade the corneal stroma.
  • Bacterial toxins and host inflammatory responses cause keratocyte death, stromal degradation, and tissue necrosis.
  • Common pathogens include *Pseudomonas aeruginosa*, *Staphylococcus aureus*, and *Streptococcus pneumoniae*.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Keratitis (Bacterial)"][/sc]
  • Initial stage: Rapid onset within 24–48 hours of symptoms.
  • Progressive stage: Untreated, may lead to corneal ulceration and stromal necrosis within days.
  • Resolution stage: With appropriate treatment, symptoms improve within 1–2 weeks, but scarring may remain.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Keratitis (Bacterial)"][/sc]
  • Severe ocular pain - Due to nerve stimulation in the corneal stroma by inflammatory mediators.
  • Photophobia - Caused by inflammation affecting the iris and ciliary body.
  • Blurred vision - Results from corneal oedema and irregular surface causing light scattering.
  • Redness - Due to dilation of conjunctival and episcleral vessels.
  • Tearing - Reflex tearing stimulated by corneal irritation.
  • Discharge - Typically purulent or mucopurulent in bacterial infections.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Keratitis (Bacterial)"][/sc]
  • Corneal ulcer - Localised defect seen on slit-lamp examination, representing stromal necrosis.
  • Hypopyon - Layer of inflammatory cells in the anterior chamber, indicating severe inflammation.
  • Conjunctival injection - Prominent redness due to vascular dilation.
  • Decreased corneal sensation - Observed in advanced cases, indicating extensive damage.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Keratitis (Bacterial)"][/sc]
  • Progressive visual loss - Suggests impending corneal perforation or endophthalmitis.
  • Severe pain unresponsive to treatment - May indicate deeper ocular involvement.
  • Hypopyon - Signifies severe intraocular inflammation and risk of complications.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Keratitis (Bacterial)"][/sc]
  • Minimal redness or discomfort in immunocompromised patients due to blunted inflammatory response.
  • Chronic indolent course in cases with suboptimal contact lens hygiene or mild trauma.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Keratitis (Bacterial)"][/sc]
  • Corneal scrape for microscopy and culture - Identifies causative bacteria and guides antibiotic therapy.
  • Fluorescein staining - Highlights epithelial defects visible under blue light.
  • Slit-lamp examination - Confirms corneal ulceration, infiltrates, and anterior chamber inflammation.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Keratitis (Bacterial)"][/sc]
  • A 35-year-old contact lens wearer presents with a 24-hour history of severe left eye pain, redness, blurred vision, and purulent discharge, worsening despite over-the-counter lubricants.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Keratitis (Bacterial)"][/sc]
  • Herpetic keratitis - Dendritic ulcer seen on fluorescein staining, minimal discharge.
  • Fungal keratitis - Gradual onset, satellite lesions, associated with plant trauma.
  • Acanthamoeba keratitis - Severe pain out of proportion to findings, ring infiltrate.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Keratitis (Bacterial)"][/sc]
  • Empirical antibiotic therapy - Fortified topical antibiotics (e.g., cefuroxime and gentamicin) every hour initially.
  • Stop contact lens use - Prevents further irritation and contamination.
  • Adjunctive therapy - Cycloplegics to reduce pain and photophobia.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Keratitis (Bacterial)"][/sc]
  • Corneal scarring - Leads to permanent visual impairment.
  • Corneal perforation - May result in secondary endophthalmitis and loss of the eye.
  • Glaucoma - Secondary to intraocular inflammation or steroid use.
