Alcohol Abuse
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Alcohol Abuse"][/sc]
  • Alcohol abuse, also termed alcohol use disorder (AUD), refers to the harmful or hazardous consumption of alcohol, characterised by an inability to control drinking, continued use despite negative consequences, and an increased tolerance or withdrawal symptoms. It ranges in severity from mild to severe, with severe AUD having significant impacts on both physical and mental health.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Alcohol Abuse"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Alcohol Abuse"][/sc]
  • In the UK, alcohol abuse is estimated to affect around 1.4 million people, though only a fraction seek treatment. Among the GP population, around 3-4% present with alcohol-related issues. In UK hospitals, alcohol-related admissions represent about 6% of all admissions. Globally, the World Health Organisation (WHO) estimates that 283 million people suffer from alcohol use disorder.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Alcohol Abuse"][/sc]
  • Alcohol abuse affects the brain's reward and stress systems, particularly altering dopamine and gamma-aminobutyric acid (GABA) signalling. Chronic exposure leads to neuroadaptation, where increased alcohol tolerance and withdrawal symptoms develop due to desensitisation of GABA receptors and upregulation of excitatory neurotransmitters. Over time, this disrupts executive control and increases the compulsive need for alcohol, reinforcing addiction. Chronic alcohol use also impacts other organs, contributing to liver disease (cirrhosis), cardiovascular problems, and neuropsychiatric complications.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Alcohol Abuse"][/sc]
  • Early Phase: Initial occasional binge drinking or regular use with few consequences. Tolerance begins to build.
  • Middle Phase: Drinking becomes more frequent, and withdrawal symptoms (e.g., tremors) may occur. Social, occupational, and health consequences emerge.
  • Late Phase: Severe physical dependence, chronic health complications, and severe social or occupational impairment. Withdrawal symptoms become more pronounced, requiring medical intervention.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Alcohol Abuse"][/sc]
  • Cravings for alcohol - A powerful desire to drink driven by dopamine dysregulation in the brain's reward pathways.
  • Inability to control drinking - Loss of executive function due to prefrontal cortex damage, making it difficult to stop drinking despite a desire to do so.
  • Withdrawal symptoms - Including tremors, sweating, and nausea, resulting from neuroadaptation and imbalance between inhibitory and excitatory neurotransmission.
  • Increased tolerance - The need to consume more alcohol to achieve the same effect due to downregulation of GABA receptors.
  • Gastrointestinal symptoms - Such as nausea, vomiting, and diarrhoea, due to alcohol's irritant effect on the gastric mucosa.
  • Depression and anxiety - Mood disturbances result from chronic alcohol-induced neurochemical imbalances, particularly affecting serotonin and dopamine pathways.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Alcohol Abuse"][/sc]
  • Tremors - Particularly in the hands, due to withdrawal-induced autonomic hyperactivity.
  • Jaundice - Yellowing of the skin and eyes, indicating advanced liver damage and reduced liver function (bilirubin build-up).
  • Alcohol odour on breath - A common sign during or after a recent drinking episode.
  • Palmar erythema - Reddening of the palms due to altered oestrogen metabolism in chronic liver disease.
  • Ascites - Fluid accumulation in the abdomen, associated with cirrhosis and portal hypertension in chronic alcohol abuse.
  • Spider naevi - Small, spider-like blood vessels on the skin, due to elevated oestrogen levels from liver dysfunction.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Alcohol Abuse"][/sc]
  • Severe withdrawal (delirium tremens) - Includes confusion, agitation, hallucinations, and seizures, potentially life-threatening due to autonomic hyperactivity.
  • Upper gastrointestinal bleeding - Haematemesis or melena may indicate oesophageal varices, which can rupture in cirrhosis.
  • Altered mental status - Suggestive of hepatic encephalopathy or Wernicke-Korsakoff syndrome, both of which can progress to coma if untreated.
  • Jaundice with ascites - Indicates advanced liver failure and portal hypertension.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Alcohol Abuse"][/sc]
  • High-functioning alcoholics - Individuals who maintain employment and social relationships but have heavy drinking patterns. They may present late with advanced liver disease.
  • Isolated symptoms of depression or anxiety - Alcohol use can mask itself as psychiatric symptoms, especially in younger patients.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Alcohol Abuse"][/sc]
  • Full blood count (FBC) - May show macrocytic anaemia due to folate deficiency, elevated mean corpuscular volume (MCV), and thrombocytopenia (low platelets) associated with liver dysfunction.
  • Liver function tests (LFTs) - Elevated liver enzymes (ALT, AST), particularly an AST:ALT ratio >2, suggestive of alcoholic liver disease. Elevated bilirubin may indicate advanced liver damage.
  • Gamma-glutamyl transferase (GGT) - Often elevated in chronic alcohol use, even before other liver enzymes rise.
  • Carbohydrate-deficient transferrin (CDT) - A specific marker of heavy alcohol use over the past 2 weeks.
  • Abdominal ultrasound - May show liver steatosis, hepatomegaly, or signs of cirrhosis such as a nodular liver or ascites.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Alcohol Abuse"][/sc]
  • A 45-year-old male with a history of daily alcohol consumption presents with tremors, nausea, and jaundice. He reports difficulty controlling his drinking and experiences withdrawal symptoms when attempting to stop. LFTs show elevated liver enzymes, and ultrasound reveals hepatic steatosis.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Alcohol Abuse"][/sc]
  • Non-alcoholic fatty liver disease (NAFLD) - Lacks the history of alcohol use, and AST:ALT ratio is usually <1.
  • Viral hepatitis - Presents with jaundice and liver enzyme elevation, but serological markers for hepatitis A, B, or C will be positive.
  • Benzodiazepine or barbiturate withdrawal - Similar withdrawal symptoms, but often without the same pattern of liver enzyme derangement.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Alcohol Abuse"][/sc]
  • Detoxification - Medically supervised withdrawal using benzodiazepines (e.g., chlordiazepoxide) to prevent seizures and delirium tremens.
  • Psychosocial support - Referral to alcohol support services such as Alcoholics Anonymous, and psychological therapy (e.g., cognitive-behavioural therapy).
  • Medication - Naltrexone, acamprosate, or disulfiram can help prevent relapse by reducing cravings or making alcohol consumption unpleasant.
  • Liver disease management - If liver damage is present, manage with lifestyle modification, nutritional support, and specific treatments (e.g., lactulose for hepatic encephalopathy).
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Alcohol Abuse"][/sc]
  • Cirrhosis - Progressive scarring of the liver due to chronic alcohol-induced liver damage. Complications include portal hypertension, ascites, and oesophageal varices.
  • Hepatic encephalopathy - Mental status changes due to the build-up of ammonia and other toxins in liver failure.
  • Wernicke-Korsakoff syndrome - A neurological condition due to thiamine deficiency, presenting with confusion, ataxia, and ophthalmoplegia (Wernicke's) or memory impairment (Korsakoff’s).
  • Cardiomyopathy - Alcohol-induced weakening of the heart muscle, leading to heart failure.
