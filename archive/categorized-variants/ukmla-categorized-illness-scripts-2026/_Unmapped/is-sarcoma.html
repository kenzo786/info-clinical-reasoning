Sarcoma
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Sarcoma"][/sc]
  • Sarcoma is a group of rare malignant tumours originating in mesenchymal tissues, which include bone, muscle, fat, cartilage, and connective tissues. Sarcomas are broadly classified into soft tissue sarcomas (STS) and bone sarcomas, with subtypes based on the specific tissue affected, such as osteosarcoma, rhabdomyosarcoma, and liposarcoma.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Sarcoma"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Sarcoma"][/sc]
  • In the UK, sarcomas represent approximately 1% of all adult cancers and 15% of paediatric cancers, with soft tissue sarcomas more common than bone sarcomas in adults. The UK incidence is about 3-4 cases per 100,000 per year.
  • Hospital prevalence is higher in tertiary centres due to specialist care needs. Globally, incidence rates vary but remain rare, with similar rates in Western populations.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Sarcoma"][/sc]
  • Sarcomas arise from mutations within mesenchymal stem cells, leading to unregulated cell proliferation and tumour formation. Oncogenic mutations affect tumour suppressor genes (e.g., p53) or proto-oncogenes, resulting in altered cellular functions like apoptosis, adhesion, and differentiation. Genetic syndromes (e.g., Li-Fraumeni syndrome) increase risk.
  • Soft tissue sarcomas grow by local extension, whereas some sarcomas metastasise haematogenously, often to the lungs, due to proximity to blood vessels.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Sarcoma"][/sc]
  • Typically, sarcomas have an insidious onset, progressing slowly over months to years. Soft tissue sarcomas often present as a painless, growing mass, while bone sarcomas may cause pain, particularly at night.
  • Advanced stages may lead to metastasis, primarily affecting the lungs or other bones.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Sarcoma"][/sc]
  • Swelling or mass - Frequently, sarcomas present as a progressively enlarging, painless mass. This occurs due to unchecked tumour growth within soft tissues or bone.
  • Localized pain (bone sarcomas) - Bone sarcomas often present with pain, typically worse at night, due to the expansion of the tumour within the bone, causing periosteal stretching.
  • Restricted movement - Soft tissue sarcomas near joints may limit range of motion as they expand and compress adjacent structures.
  • Systemic symptoms (rare) - Fever, weight loss, and fatigue may occur in advanced or metastatic disease.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Sarcoma"][/sc]
  • Palpable mass - On examination, a firm or hard, non-tender mass may be present. Soft tissue sarcomas are often deep-seated, while bone sarcomas may involve the surface of long bones.
  • Reduced range of motion - Mass effect from tumours near joints may lead to observable limitations in mobility.
  • Soft tissue swelling - Overlying tissue may appear swollen or show dilated veins, especially in larger tumours, due to compression of local structures.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Sarcoma"][/sc]
  • Rapidly enlarging mass - Suggests aggressive malignancy, potentially indicating a high-grade sarcoma or metastasis.
  • Night pain - Commonly associated with bone sarcomas and malignancies, night pain indicates tumour growth within confined spaces.
  • Systemic symptoms - Such as fever or weight loss, may indicate advanced disease or metastatic spread.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Sarcoma"][/sc]
  • Small or superficial masses - Smaller, superficial sarcomas may be mistaken for benign lipomas or cysts, delaying diagnosis.
  • Absence of pain - Especially in soft tissue sarcomas, which can lead to late presentation and advanced disease upon diagnosis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Sarcoma"][/sc]
  • Imaging (MRI, CT, X-ray) - MRI is preferred for soft tissue sarcomas, showing size and depth; CT is used for staging, especially to detect lung metastasis. Bone sarcomas are often seen on X-ray, with lytic or sclerotic lesions.
  • Biopsy - Core needle or excisional biopsy is required for definitive diagnosis. Histopathology reveals sarcoma subtype and grade.
  • Staging scans - PET scans or CT for staging and metastasis detection.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Sarcoma"][/sc]
  • A 45-year-old man presents with a gradually enlarging, painless thigh mass over six months, with recent limitation of knee movement and occasional night pain.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Sarcoma"][/sc]
  • Benign soft tissue tumours - Typically well-circumscribed, slow-growing, and lack invasion, whereas sarcomas are infiltrative.
  • Osteomyelitis - May present with bone pain and systemic symptoms but is often associated with infection markers.
  • Lymphoma - Can cause lymphadenopathy and masses but typically shows constitutional symptoms and lymphatic spread.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Sarcoma"][/sc]
  • Surgery - Wide local excision or limb-sparing surgery where possible. Amputation may be necessary for extensive bone sarcomas.
  • Radiotherapy - Adjuvant radiotherapy is used for certain soft tissue sarcomas to decrease recurrence risk.
  • Chemotherapy - Neoadjuvant or adjuvant chemotherapy may be indicated, especially in high-grade sarcomas (e.g., osteosarcoma).
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Sarcoma"][/sc]
  • Metastasis - Primarily to lungs, particularly in high-grade sarcomas; significantly impacts prognosis.
  • Local recurrence - Common in high-grade or inadequately resected sarcomas, requiring ongoing surveillance.
  • Functional impairment - Due to tumour location or surgical resection, impacting quality of life.
