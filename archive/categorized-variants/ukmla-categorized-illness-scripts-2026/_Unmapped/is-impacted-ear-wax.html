Impacted Ear Wax (Cerumen Impaction)
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Impacted Ear Wax (Cerumen Impaction)"][/sc]
  • Impacted ear wax, also known as cerumen impaction, is the accumulation of ear wax (cerumen) in the ear canal, which leads to obstruction, discomfort, or symptoms such as hearing loss. Normally produced to protect the ear canal, ear wax can become impacted due to excessive production, anatomical factors, or improper cleaning methods. This condition is common but easily treatable with ear irrigation, cerumenolytic agents, or manual removal.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Impacted Ear Wax (Cerumen Impaction)"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Impacted Ear Wax (Cerumen Impaction)"][/sc]
  • UK GP Population: Cerumen impaction accounts for approximately 2-6% of all GP consultations in the UK.
  • UK Hospital Prevalence: It is less common in hospitals, but may still present in elderly or paediatric patients requiring hospitalisation for complications.
  • Worldwide Prevalence: Around 10% of children, 5-15% of adults, and over 30% of elderly populations experience impacted ear wax.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Impacted Ear Wax (Cerumen Impaction)"][/sc]
  • Cerumen is produced by glands in the outer third of the ear canal and serves as a protective barrier, trapping dust, debris, and microorganisms. The wax naturally migrates outward due to jaw movements and epithelial turnover. However, factors like narrow or tortuous ear canals, overproduction of cerumen, or excessive ear cleaning can lead to accumulation and impaction. Impacted cerumen can then block sound transmission to the tympanic membrane, causing conductive hearing loss, discomfort, and even infections in severe cases.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Impacted Ear Wax (Cerumen Impaction)"][/sc]
  • Acute phase: Ear wax can become impacted quickly after improper cleaning or water exposure, leading to sudden blockage.
  • Chronic phase: Slow, gradual accumulation over weeks to months, often asymptomatic until the canal is fully blocked.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Impacted Ear Wax (Cerumen Impaction)"][/sc]
  • Hearing loss - Conductive hearing loss due to the blockage of the external auditory canal by cerumen.
  • Ear discomfort/fullness - Pressure sensation or fullness due to impacted wax pressing on the canal wall.
  • Tinnitus - Ringing or buzzing in the ear, which can occur due to pressure on the tympanic membrane.
  • Itching - Irritation of the ear canal skin caused by impacted cerumen.
  • Otalgia (ear pain) - Rarely, impacted wax can cause pain if it presses on the canal wall or becomes infected.
  • Dizziness - Vertigo-like symptoms can occur if cerumen impacts the ear canal near the eardrum, affecting balance.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Impacted Ear Wax (Cerumen Impaction)"][/sc]
  • Visible cerumen occluding the ear canal - Direct otoscopic visualisation of ear wax blocking the canal.
  • Decreased mobility of the tympanic membrane - Observed during pneumatic otoscopy, indicating blockage.
  • Conductive hearing loss on tuning fork tests - Weber test lateralises to the affected ear, and Rinne test shows bone conduction > air conduction.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Impacted Ear Wax (Cerumen Impaction)"][/sc]
  • Severe pain or drainage from the ear - Suggests secondary infection, such as otitis externa or perforated tympanic membrane.
  • Vertigo or significant hearing loss - Could indicate more serious pathology, such as a vestibular issue or underlying tumour.
  • Fever or systemic signs - May signify spreading infection, like cellulitis or mastoiditis.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Impacted Ear Wax (Cerumen Impaction)"][/sc]
  • Asymptomatic presentation - Some patients may present without symptoms despite significant blockage, only diagnosed incidentally during an ear examination.
  • Non-specific dizziness - Elderly patients may attribute balance issues or dizziness to other causes, overlooking ear wax as a contributor.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Impacted Ear Wax (Cerumen Impaction)"][/sc]
  • Otoscopy - Direct visualisation of cerumen blocking the ear canal is diagnostic. It helps differentiate wax from other causes of obstruction.
  • Tuning fork tests - The Weber and Rinne tests confirm conductive hearing loss, as bone conduction is better than air conduction in the affected ear.
  • Audiometry - In cases of uncertainty, formal hearing tests can confirm the degree and type of hearing loss.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Impacted Ear Wax (Cerumen Impaction)"][/sc]
  • A 65-year-old male presents with gradual hearing loss in the left ear over several months, associated with a sensation of fullness and occasional tinnitus. No pain, vertigo, or drainage.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Impacted Ear Wax (Cerumen Impaction)"][/sc]
  • Otitis Externa - Differs by presenting with pain, discharge, and erythema of the ear canal, whereas impacted ear wax is painless unless infected.
  • Foreign body in the ear - Common in children, may present with similar hearing loss or discomfort but usually a history of insertion exists.
  • Cholesteatoma - Characterised by foul-smelling discharge, hearing loss, and retraction pockets on otoscopy, unlike ear wax impaction.
  • Otitis Media with Effusion - Fluid behind the tympanic membrane causes conductive hearing loss but is visualised as a fluid line or bubbles on otoscopy.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Impacted Ear Wax (Cerumen Impaction)"][/sc]
  • Cerumenolytic agents: Softening agents like sodium bicarbonate drops or olive oil drops to break down wax before removal.
  • Irrigation: Ear syringing with warm water is the most common method in GP practices, though contraindicated with tympanic membrane perforation.
  • Manual removal: In cases of resistant or very hard wax, instruments like a Jobson-Horne probe or microsuction can be used.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Impacted Ear Wax (Cerumen Impaction)"][/sc]
  • Otitis externa: Impacted cerumen can predispose to external ear infections if wax traps moisture or debris.
  • Tympanic membrane perforation: Aggressive irrigation or improper cleaning can cause perforation.
  • Conductive hearing loss: Persistent wax can lead to long-term hearing difficulties if not addressed.
