Nasal Injury/Septal Deviation
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Nasal Injury/Septal Deviation"][/sc]
  • A nasal injury with septal deviation refers to trauma or force applied to the nose that causes displacement of the nasal septum, the structure dividing the nasal cavity into two sides. This can lead to nasal obstruction, altered airflow, and cosmetic deformity. Deviations may occur due to trauma, such as a direct blow to the nose, or may be congenital.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Nasal Injury/Septal Deviation"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Nasal Injury/Septal Deviation"][/sc]
  • Prevalence in the UK GP population is not specifically documented, but nasal trauma is common and may lead to septal deviation. Approximately 30% of nasal fractures are associated with septal deviation.
  • UK hospital prevalence of nasal trauma leading to septal deviation is higher in males, particularly between 15-30 years of age.
  • Globally, nasal septal deviation has been observed in approximately 80% of individuals, though many are asymptomatic. Trauma-related septal deviation is common in sports, accidents, and violent encounters.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Nasal Injury/Septal Deviation"][/sc]
  • Nasal injury leading to septal deviation occurs when force is applied to the nasal bridge, causing displacement or fracture of the nasal bones and the underlying septum. The nasal septum is made of both bone and cartilage, and a fracture or dislocation can disrupt its normal position.
  • Trauma may cause bleeding (haematoma) between the septal cartilage and its perichondrium, which can lead to cartilage necrosis if untreated, worsening the deviation.
  • In the long term, septal deviation can result in nasal obstruction, impaired mucociliary function, and increased susceptibility to sinus infections.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Nasal Injury/Septal Deviation"][/sc]
  • Acute phase: Immediately following trauma, there may be pain, swelling, epistaxis (nosebleed), and difficulty breathing through the affected nostril(s).
  • Subacute phase: Over days to weeks, the swelling may reduce, but obstruction due to the septal deviation may persist. If untreated, haematoma formation or necrosis of cartilage may develop.
  • Chronic phase: Long-standing untreated septal deviation can result in chronic nasal obstruction, breathing difficulties, and recurrent sinus infections.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Nasal Injury/Septal Deviation"][/sc]
  • Nasal obstruction - The most common symptom, often worse on one side due to deviation blocking airflow. Results from narrowing of the nasal passage.
  • Difficulty breathing through the nose - Common due to compromised airflow through the narrowed nasal passage. Related to impaired airflow caused by septal deviation.
  • Frequent sinus infections - Chronic nasal obstruction can impede sinus drainage, leading to recurrent infections.
  • Snoring - Common due to turbulent airflow and compromised nasal patency, often associated with mouth breathing during sleep.
  • Nosebleeds (epistaxis) - Trauma or dryness in the nasal cavity due to altered airflow can predispose the mucosa to bleeding.
  • Facial pain or pressure - May occur with associated sinusitis or due to trauma affecting nearby facial structures.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Nasal Injury/Septal Deviation"][/sc]
  • Visible nasal asymmetry - Deviation of the nasal septum may cause the external nose to appear crooked or asymmetrical due to displacement of the underlying structure.
  • Septal deviation on examination - Anterior rhinoscopy or endoscopy reveals a visible displacement of the septum, often bulging into one of the nasal passages.
  • Nasal mucosal swelling - Swelling and erythema of the nasal mucosa may be present, especially following trauma or if there is ongoing inflammation.
  • Decreased airflow - Noted on airflow testing or by blocking one nostril at a time; there is a noticeable reduction in airflow through the affected side.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Nasal Injury/Septal Deviation"][/sc]
  • Septal haematoma - A medical emergency, where blood collects between the septal cartilage and mucosa, causing pressure necrosis if untreated. It can lead to permanent cartilage damage and a saddle nose deformity.
  • Uncontrolled epistaxis - Persistent or severe nosebleeds may indicate significant vascular injury and could lead to hypovolaemia if untreated.
  • Orbital or neurological symptoms - Signs such as visual changes or altered consciousness may suggest associated facial fractures or intracranial injury, requiring urgent intervention.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Nasal Injury/Septal Deviation"][/sc]
  • Asymptomatic deviation - Many individuals with a deviated septum are asymptomatic, discovered incidentally during unrelated examinations. This occurs due to minimal airway obstruction or compensatory airflow through the unaffected side.
  • Chronic sinusitis without obvious trauma - Long-standing septal deviation may present with recurrent sinus infections without a clear history of trauma, as obstruction of sinus drainage can occur gradually.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Nasal Injury/Septal Deviation"][/sc]
  • Anterior rhinoscopy - Direct visualisation of the nasal passages using a speculum can identify septal deviation or septal haematomas. Displacement of the septum is visible on inspection.
  • Nasal endoscopy - A flexible endoscope is inserted into the nasal cavity for detailed assessment of septal deviation, mucosal condition, and possible sinus involvement.
  • CT scan of the sinuses - May be required if there is suspicion of more complex facial fractures or in cases of chronic sinusitis. Shows bony deformities, sinus opacification, and soft tissue changes.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Nasal Injury/Septal Deviation"][/sc]
  • A 25-year-old male presents with nasal obstruction, recurrent sinus infections, and snoring following a sports-related nasal trauma 6 months ago. Examination reveals nasal septal deviation with visible asymmetry and reduced airflow on the right side.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Nasal Injury/Septal Deviation"][/sc]
  • Chronic rhinosinusitis - Differs in that nasal polyps and mucopurulent discharge are common, and there is often a history of allergy. Septal deviation can be a contributing factor.
  • Nasal polyps - Soft, painless growths in the nasal cavity causing obstruction, often seen in patients with asthma or chronic rhinosinusitis. They are bilateral, unlike a septal deviation.
  • Nasal fracture - Presents similarly with nasal asymmetry but may be associated with bruising, crepitus, and more acute pain immediately following trauma. Septal deviation is a possible outcome.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Nasal Injury/Septal Deviation"][/sc]
  • Conservative management - In mild cases without significant symptoms, no treatment may be required. Nasal saline sprays and decongestants can be used for symptom relief.
  • Septoplasty - The surgical procedure to correct septal deviation, indicated in patients with significant nasal obstruction or recurrent sinusitis.
  • Management of complications - Septal haematomas require urgent drainage. Antibiotics may be needed if there is associated infection.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Nasal Injury/Septal Deviation"][/sc]
  • Septal perforation - A hole in the septum may develop post-surgery or due to untreated haematomas, leading to crusting, bleeding, and whistling sounds when breathing.
  • Saddle nose deformity - Collapse of the nasal bridge can occur due to septal cartilage necrosis, leading to a sunken nasal appearance.
  • Recurrent sinusitis - Persistent nasal obstruction can lead to chronic sinus drainage issues and recurrent infections.
