Laryngitis
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Laryngitis"][/sc]
  • Laryngitis refers to inflammation of the larynx, commonly due to viral infection, overuse of the vocal cords, or irritation from substances such as smoke. It is characterised by hoarseness, voice loss, and throat discomfort. The condition is typically self-limiting, lasting from a few days to up to two weeks.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Laryngitis"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Laryngitis"][/sc]
  • Prevalence in UK GP population: Laryngitis is a common condition, with about 1-2% of GP consultations annually attributed to acute laryngitis.
  • Hospital prevalence in the UK: Rarely requires hospitalisation unless complications arise (e.g., in immunocompromised patients).
  • Worldwide prevalence: Similar globally, but risk factors such as smoking and air pollution may affect incidence rates.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Laryngitis"][/sc]
  • Step 1: Infection or irritation causes inflammation of the laryngeal mucosa.
  • Step 2: Inflammatory mediators such as cytokines and prostaglandins increase blood flow to the larynx, leading to oedema.
  • Step 3: Oedema affects vocal fold vibration, resulting in hoarseness or aphonia (loss of voice).
  • Step 4: Persistent irritation may lead to further damage of the laryngeal tissue, particularly with chronic exposure to irritants.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Laryngitis"][/sc]
  • Acute: Lasts less than 3 weeks, typically resolves within 7-14 days. Most common form.
  • Chronic: Persists for more than 3 weeks, often due to prolonged exposure to irritants or chronic vocal strain.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Laryngitis"][/sc]
  • Hoarseness - Due to inflammation and oedema of the vocal cords, affecting their ability to vibrate properly.
  • Loss of voice (aphonia) - Severe oedema or inflammation can completely obstruct the vocal folds, resulting in temporary loss of voice.
  • Sore throat - Inflammation in the laryngeal mucosa causes discomfort and pain.
  • Dry cough - The irritated laryngeal mucosa triggers a reflexive, non-productive cough.
  • Fever - If laryngitis is caused by infection, low-grade fever may occur as part of the systemic inflammatory response.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Laryngitis"][/sc]
  • Hoarse voice - The most recognisable sign, resulting from vocal cord oedema and inflammation.
  • Red or swollen larynx - Direct visualisation via laryngoscopy may show inflammation and swelling of the laryngeal mucosa.
  • Stridor (in severe cases) - Indicates significant laryngeal oedema or obstruction, typically seen in more severe or complicated cases.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Laryngitis"][/sc]
  • Stridor - Suggests significant upper airway obstruction, which may progress to respiratory distress. It may be a sign of epiglottitis, an emergency condition.
  • Difficulty breathing - May indicate impending airway compromise, particularly in cases of bacterial infection or in immunocompromised patients.
  • High fever - Can be a sign of bacterial laryngitis, abscess formation, or epiglottitis, which require urgent intervention.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Laryngitis"][/sc]
  • Chronic laryngitis without hoarseness - In some cases, patients may present with chronic cough or throat clearing, with little change in voice quality, particularly in smokers or individuals with chronic reflux.
  • Asymptomatic presentation - Laryngitis may occasionally present asymptomatically, particularly in cases related to gastro-oesophageal reflux or mild viral infections.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Laryngitis"][/sc]
  • Laryngoscopy - Visualisation of the larynx reveals oedema, erythema, or vocal cord abnormalities. This is used for persistent cases lasting >2-3 weeks.
  • Viral swabs - If a viral aetiology is suspected, nasopharyngeal swabs may confirm common viruses (e.g., rhinovirus, influenza).
  • Throat swabs - To rule out bacterial infections such as group A streptococcus, particularly if signs of bacterial involvement (e.g., fever, purulent discharge) are present.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Laryngitis"][/sc]
  • A 34-year-old female presents with a 5-day history of progressive hoarseness, sore throat, and dry cough. No fever or significant breathing difficulty. Symptoms started after a viral upper respiratory tract infection.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Laryngitis"][/sc]
  • Pharyngitis - While both conditions may present with sore throat, pharyngitis typically lacks the hoarseness seen in laryngitis and may present with fever and odynophagia.
  • Epiglottitis - A more severe condition that presents with fever, drooling, stridor, and rapid-onset respiratory distress. Hoarseness may be absent.
  • Vocal cord nodules - Chronic hoarseness without associated cough or throat discomfort, often in individuals with voice overuse.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Laryngitis"][/sc]
  • Supportive care - Resting the voice, staying hydrated, and using humidified air.
  • Analgesics - Paracetamol or ibuprofen for pain relief and fever management.
  • Antibiotics - Only indicated if bacterial infection is suspected (e.g., in bacterial laryngitis or strep throat).
  • Proton pump inhibitors (PPIs) - For cases related to gastro-oesophageal reflux, addressing the underlying cause of laryngitis.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Laryngitis"][/sc]
  • Chronic laryngitis - May develop if acute laryngitis is recurrent or prolonged, particularly with continued exposure to irritants like smoking or reflux.
  • Vocal cord nodules - Prolonged vocal strain and inflammation may lead to the development of nodules, causing persistent hoarseness.
  • Airway obstruction - Severe bacterial infections, such as epiglottitis, may lead to airway compromise and require emergency intervention.
