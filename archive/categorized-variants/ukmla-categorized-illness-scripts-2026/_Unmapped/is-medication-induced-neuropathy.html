Medication-Induced Neuropathy
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Medication-Induced Neuropathy"][/sc]
  • Medication-induced neuropathy refers to peripheral nerve damage caused by the use of certain drugs. It typically presents with sensory, motor, or autonomic dysfunction. Commonly implicated medications include chemotherapeutic agents, antiretrovirals, and certain antibiotics.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Medication-Induced Neuropathy"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Medication-Induced Neuropathy"][/sc]
  • In the UK, the prevalence of medication-induced neuropathy is uncertain, but chemotherapeutic drugs are responsible for up to 40% of cases in patients undergoing cancer treatment.
  • In hospital settings, incidence can be higher due to widespread use of neurotoxic medications, especially in oncology and infectious disease departments.
  • Globally, medication-induced neuropathy is increasing due to rising chemotherapy use, with estimates showing up to 68% of cancer patients may develop neuropathy during treatment.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Medication-Induced Neuropathy"][/sc]
  • Medications can cause neuropathy through several mechanisms:
    • Axonal degeneration: Most drugs, particularly chemotherapeutics, impair microtubule formation, disrupting axonal transport, leading to axonal injury.
    • Myelin damage: Some agents, like certain antibiotics, cause direct myelin sheath damage, leading to demyelination and nerve dysfunction.
    • Vascular toxicity: Drugs like platinum-based chemotherapies induce endothelial damage, reducing blood flow and causing ischaemic neuropathy.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Medication-Induced Neuropathy"][/sc]
  • Neuropathy onset is typically gradual:
    • Acute phase: Symptoms can emerge within days to weeks of starting the offending medication (e.g., vincristine-induced neuropathy).
    • Subacute phase: Symptoms often build progressively, worsening over weeks to months during ongoing treatment (e.g., platinum-based chemotherapies).
    • Chronic phase: Neuropathy can persist or worsen after discontinuation of the drug, with recovery taking months to years.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Medication-Induced Neuropathy"][/sc]
  • Distal paraesthesia: Patients typically report numbness and tingling, beginning in the feet and hands due to the length-dependent nature of axonal damage.
  • Burning pain: Burning or shooting pain may result from damage to small sensory fibres, disrupting pain perception.
  • Muscle weakness: Particularly in cases involving motor neuropathy, as seen with drugs like vincristine, where motor axons are affected.
  • Loss of balance: Due to sensory loss in the feet, patients may struggle with proprioception, especially in the dark.
  • Autonomic dysfunction: Less common but may include constipation, orthostatic hypotension, or urinary retention, especially with medications affecting autonomic nerves.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Medication-Induced Neuropathy"][/sc]
  • Reduced reflexes: Diminished or absent ankle reflexes are common due to peripheral nerve dysfunction, primarily affecting large myelinated fibres.
  • Distal muscle atrophy: Seen in more severe or chronic cases, particularly with motor involvement, indicating long-standing denervation.
  • Altered sensation: Patients may have decreased vibration or pinprick sensation distally, reflecting sensory fibre involvement.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Medication-Induced Neuropathy"][/sc]
  • Rapid progression of weakness: This suggests more severe axonal damage or Guillain-Barré-like syndromes caused by drugs such as checkpoint inhibitors.
  • Severe autonomic dysfunction: Could indicate involvement of autonomic nerves, requiring immediate attention, as seen in cases with antiretroviral therapy.
  • Respiratory muscle weakness: Rare but serious, especially with vincristine, where involvement of phrenic nerves may lead to respiratory failure.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Medication-Induced Neuropathy"][/sc]
  • Mononeuropathy multiplex: Some medications may cause isolated nerve involvement, resulting in atypical focal neuropathies rather than a length-dependent polyneuropathy.
  • Pain without sensory loss: Certain drugs, particularly antiretrovirals, may cause painful neuropathy without objective sensory findings.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Medication-Induced Neuropathy"][/sc]
  • Nerve conduction studies (NCS): Show reduced amplitude of sensory and motor responses in axonal neuropathies, with or without prolonged latencies depending on the drug.
  • Electromyography (EMG): Used to assess muscle denervation and reinnervation, revealing chronic denervation in motor neuropathies.
  • Serum B12 and folate: Rule out nutritional causes of neuropathy that may mimic or worsen drug-induced neuropathy.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Medication-Induced Neuropathy"][/sc]
  • A 55-year-old male, receiving cisplatin for lung cancer, presents with a 3-month history of numbness and tingling in the feet, progressing to involve the hands. On examination, absent ankle reflexes and reduced vibration sensation in the toes are noted.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Medication-Induced Neuropathy"][/sc]
  • Diabetic neuropathy: Presents with similar distal paraesthesia and numbness, but typically associated with a history of diabetes and high blood glucose levels.
  • Chronic inflammatory demyelinating polyneuropathy (CIDP): More likely to have progressive motor weakness and responds to immunotherapy, unlike most drug-induced neuropathies.
  • Alcohol-related neuropathy: History of chronic alcohol use, with similar distal sensory symptoms, but usually slower onset compared to medication-induced neuropathy.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Medication-Induced Neuropathy"][/sc]
  • Discontinuation of offending medication: If possible, the first step is stopping or reducing the neurotoxic drug.
  • Symptom management: Gabapentin or amitriptyline are often used for neuropathic pain management.
  • Physical therapy: To address muscle weakness and balance issues, particularly in more severe cases.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Medication-Induced Neuropathy"][/sc]
  • Persistent neuropathy: Even after stopping the offending drug, symptoms may persist for months or years, particularly in the case of chemotherapy-induced neuropathy.
  • Chronic pain: Neuropathic pain may become chronic and require long-term management.
  • Disability: Severe motor neuropathy can lead to long-term disability, particularly with ambulation and fine motor skills.
