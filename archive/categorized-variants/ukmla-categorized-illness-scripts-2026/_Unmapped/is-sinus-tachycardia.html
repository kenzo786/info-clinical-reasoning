Sinus Tachycardia
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Sinus Tachycardia"][/sc]
  • Sinus tachycardia is defined as an elevated heart rate of over 100 beats per minute (bpm) originating from the sinoatrial (SA) node. It occurs in response to a physiological stimulus such as fever, exercise, or anxiety, or as part of a pathological condition like infection or hypovolaemia. It is characterised by a regular rhythm with a normal P wave preceding each QRS complex.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Sinus Tachycardia"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Sinus Tachycardia"][/sc]
  • Sinus tachycardia is common across all age groups and can be seen in both primary care and hospital settings. In the UK, it is commonly encountered in patients with conditions like infection, dehydration, or anxiety. Exact prevalence is hard to determine because sinus tachycardia is often a transient, physiological response. Worldwide, it is a common presenting sign in emergency departments and clinics, particularly in younger individuals and those under stress.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Sinus Tachycardia"][/sc]
  • Sinus tachycardia results from increased sympathetic stimulation or reduced parasympathetic tone, leading to a faster firing rate of the SA node. Common triggers include stress, hypovolaemia, hypoxia, fever, or drugs such as stimulants. The heart rate increases to maintain cardiac output in response to increased metabolic demands or reduced blood volume.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Sinus Tachycardia"][/sc]
  • Sinus tachycardia often presents acutely, resolving once the underlying cause is addressed. It can be transient, such as with exercise or fever, or persistent in cases of chronic stress, pain, or hyperthyroidism. If untreated in pathological cases, it may persist over weeks to months, depending on the causative factor.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Sinus Tachycardia"][/sc]
  • Palpitations - Patients often feel their heart racing due to the increased heart rate. This is a direct result of the elevated firing of the SA node, increasing cardiac output.
  • Shortness of breath - Due to increased metabolic demands, especially during exertion. The body demands more oxygen as heart rate rises, leading to perceived breathlessness.
  • Chest discomfort - Increased myocardial oxygen demand in sinus tachycardia can cause discomfort, especially if there is underlying coronary artery disease.
  • Anxiety - The sensation of a racing heart can lead to feelings of unease or anxiety, which may further exacerbate the tachycardia in a feedback loop.
  • Dizziness - Inadequate perfusion of the brain due to insufficient cardiac output in some cases may result in light-headedness.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Sinus Tachycardia"][/sc]
  • Increased heart rate (over 100 bpm) - The hallmark of sinus tachycardia, typically regular with a normal rhythm.
  • Normal P waves on ECG - Reflects a normal origin of electrical impulses from the SA node.
  • Bounding pulse - With an elevated heart rate, the pulse may feel more forceful on palpation due to increased cardiac output.
  • Normal blood pressure - Despite the increased heart rate, blood pressure may remain normal if the cardiac output is adequate to compensate.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Sinus Tachycardia"][/sc]
  • Chest pain with tachycardia - May signify myocardial ischaemia, especially in patients with coronary artery disease, as increased heart rate exacerbates oxygen demand.
  • Syncope - Could suggest underlying arrhythmias or inadequate cerebral perfusion due to the tachycardia.
  • Hypotension - Sinus tachycardia with hypotension may indicate shock or significant hypovolaemia, requiring immediate intervention.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Sinus Tachycardia"][/sc]
  • Some patients, particularly the elderly, may present with vague symptoms like generalised fatigue or weakness instead of classic symptoms like palpitations. These atypical presentations can delay diagnosis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Sinus Tachycardia"][/sc]
  • ECG - Sinus rhythm with heart rate >100 bpm, normal P waves preceding each QRS complex.
  • Thyroid function tests - To exclude hyperthyroidism as a cause of persistent sinus tachycardia.
  • Full blood count - To identify anaemia or infection, which can cause compensatory tachycardia.
  • Serum electrolytes - Hypokalaemia or hypomagnesaemia can contribute to arrhythmias.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Sinus Tachycardia"][/sc]
  • A 28-year-old female presents with palpitations, shortness of breath, and dizziness following recent viral illness. Her ECG shows sinus tachycardia at 110 bpm, and her thyroid function is normal.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Sinus Tachycardia"][/sc]
  • Atrial fibrillation - Irregularly irregular heart rate, absence of P waves on ECG.
  • Supraventricular tachycardia (SVT) - Abrupt onset, very rapid heart rate (often >150 bpm), absent P waves, narrow QRS complexes.
  • Hyperthyroidism - Persistent tachycardia with signs of hypermetabolism (weight loss, tremor, heat intolerance); confirmed by elevated T3 and T4 levels.
  • Panic disorder - Episodic tachycardia associated with intense fear, hyperventilation, and no abnormal ECG findings.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Sinus Tachycardia"][/sc]
  • Treat underlying cause - Correction of triggers such as dehydration, fever, infection, or pain is the mainstay of treatment.
  • Beta-blockers (e.g., propranolol, bisoprolol) - Can be used to control heart rate in cases of persistent or symptomatic sinus tachycardia, especially if associated with anxiety or hyperthyroidism.
  • Ivabradine - May be considered in cases where beta-blockers are contraindicated, as it directly lowers heart rate without affecting blood pressure.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Sinus Tachycardia"][/sc]
  • Heart failure - Prolonged sinus tachycardia increases myocardial oxygen demand, which may exacerbate heart failure in susceptible individuals.
  • Ischaemia - In patients with underlying coronary artery disease, sinus tachycardia can reduce myocardial perfusion, leading to ischaemia.
