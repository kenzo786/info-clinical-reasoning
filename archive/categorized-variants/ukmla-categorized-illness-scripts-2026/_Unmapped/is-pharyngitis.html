Pharyngitis illness script
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Pharyngitis"][/sc]
  • Pharyngitis is the inflammation of the pharynx, often extending to the tonsils, causing a sore throat. It is commonly caused by viral infections (e.g., rhinovirus, adenovirus) but may also result from bacterial pathogens, most notably Group A Streptococcus (GAS). Symptoms can range from mild irritation to severe pain with systemic signs of infection.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Pharyngitis"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Pharyngitis"][/sc]
  • Pharyngitis accounts for 2-4% of GP consultations in the UK. Approximately 5-15% of adult cases and 20-30% of paediatric cases are due to Group A Streptococcus. Worldwide, viral pharyngitis is the most common, affecting millions annually, while bacterial causes vary by region and season.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Pharyngitis"][/sc]
  • Pharyngitis occurs when infectious agents (viruses or bacteria) invade the mucosa of the pharynx and tonsils. In viral cases, the infection causes cellular damage, leading to inflammation, erythema, and swelling. Bacterial pharyngitis, particularly from GAS, induces a stronger immune response with the release of toxins, leading to purulent exudates, lymphoid hyperplasia, and local tissue destruction.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Pharyngitis"][/sc]
  • Pharyngitis typically begins acutely within 1-2 days after exposure to the infectious agent. Viral pharyngitis resolves spontaneously within 5-7 days, while untreated bacterial pharyngitis can last longer, up to 10 days. With antibiotic therapy, bacterial cases improve within 2-3 days. Without treatment, complications like rheumatic fever or abscess formation may arise.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Pharyngitis"][/sc]
  • Sore throat - The most common symptom, caused by the irritation of pharyngeal mucosa due to inflammation.
  • Fever - Arises due to the systemic immune response triggered by pyrogens from the infectious organism.
  • Odynophagia - Painful swallowing, often due to localised oedema and inflammation in the pharyngeal and tonsillar regions.
  • Fatigue - A common symptom in both viral and bacterial cases due to the body's immune response.
  • Headache - Frequently accompanies fever and is part of the systemic reaction to infection.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Pharyngitis"][/sc]
  • Erythematous pharynx - The pharyngeal mucosa appears red due to dilated blood vessels as a result of inflammation.
  • Tonsillar exudate - White or yellow patches on the tonsils are common in bacterial infections, particularly Group A Streptococcus.
  • Cervical lymphadenopathy - Swollen and tender lymph nodes in the neck are due to the local immune response to infection.
  • Tonsillar hypertrophy - Enlarged tonsils are often seen due to lymphoid tissue proliferation in response to infection.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Pharyngitis"][/sc]
  • Stridor - May indicate upper airway obstruction, a medical emergency requiring urgent evaluation.
  • Drooling - Suggests severe difficulty swallowing, potentially indicating epiglottitis or peritonsillar abscess.
  • Respiratory distress - May signify airway compromise, often related to deep neck infections or severe pharyngeal swelling.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Pharyngitis"][/sc]
  • In older adults or immunocompromised patients, pharyngitis may present with mild sore throat, fatigue, or even be asymptomatic. Atypical symptoms like earache or gastrointestinal upset may also occur in some cases.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Pharyngitis"][/sc]
  • Rapid antigen detection test (RADT) - Used to detect Group A Streptococcus in patients with suspected bacterial pharyngitis. A positive result confirms the diagnosis, with a sensitivity of around 85-90%.
  • Throat culture - The gold standard for diagnosing bacterial pharyngitis, it is more sensitive than RADT but takes 24-48 hours to yield results.
  • Full blood count (FBC) - May show elevated white blood cell counts, particularly in bacterial infections or mononucleosis (with atypical lymphocytes).
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Pharyngitis"][/sc]
  • A 20-year-old university student presents with a 2-day history of sore throat, fever, and difficulty swallowing. Examination reveals tender anterior cervical lymphadenopathy and tonsillar exudate. No cough is reported.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Pharyngitis"][/sc]
  • Infectious mononucleosis - Often presents with pharyngitis, but with posterior cervical lymphadenopathy and splenomegaly. Blood tests show atypical lymphocytes.
  • Epiglottitis - Severe sore throat, drooling, and stridor. Patients are typically toxic-appearing, and this is a medical emergency.
  • Hand, foot, and mouth disease - Presents with vesicular lesions on the pharynx, hands, and feet, and is caused by coxsackievirus.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Pharyngitis"][/sc]
  • In the UK, the first-line treatment for bacterial pharyngitis (GAS) is a 10-day course of penicillin V or amoxicillin. Macrolides (e.g., clarithromycin) are used in penicillin-allergic patients. Supportive care, including analgesics (e.g., paracetamol, ibuprofen) and hydration, is recommended for viral pharyngitis, as antibiotics are ineffective.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Pharyngitis"][/sc]
  • Rheumatic fever - A delayed, immune-mediated complication of untreated GAS pharyngitis, leading to damage to heart valves.
  • Peritonsillar abscess - A collection of pus near the tonsils causing severe pain, difficulty swallowing, and potential airway obstruction.
  • Post-streptococcal glomerulonephritis - A renal complication marked by haematuria, hypertension, and proteinuria, arising after GAS infection.
