Exercise-induced Haematuria
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Exercise-induced Haematuria"][/sc]
  • Exercise-induced haematuria is a benign condition characterised by the presence of blood in the urine following vigorous physical activity. It is typically transient, resolving within 24-72 hours post-exercise, and is not associated with systemic symptoms or underlying renal pathology in most cases.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Exercise-induced Haematuria"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Exercise-induced Haematuria"][/sc]
  • Prevalence in UK GP Population: Incidence data is limited; however, it is considered uncommon and often underreported due to its benign nature.
  • Prevalence in UK Hospitals: Rarely necessitates hospital presentation unless haematuria persists or is associated with other symptoms.
  • Worldwide Prevalence: Affects approximately 18% of marathon runners, with higher rates observed in intense sports such as long-distance running and contact sports.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Exercise-induced Haematuria"][/sc]
  • Exercise-induced haematuria is thought to arise due to physical trauma, especially in high-impact sports, or intense exertion, leading to disruption of the glomerular basement membrane. This allows erythrocytes to enter the urine, manifesting as visible or microscopic haematuria. Hypoperfusion during prolonged exercise, particularly in the kidneys, may also contribute.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Exercise-induced Haematuria"][/sc]
  • Acute Onset: Haematuria usually presents shortly after exercise and is self-limited, resolving spontaneously within 24-72 hours.
  • Resolution Phase: Blood in the urine diminishes as renal perfusion normalises, and the glomerular membrane re-establishes integrity.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Exercise-induced Haematuria"][/sc]
  • Asymptomatic presentation - Most cases are asymptomatic other than haematuria, with patients unaware until testing reveals microscopic blood in the urine.
  • Visible Haematuria - Some may notice red-tinged or dark urine post-exercise, caused by erythrocytes in the urine due to glomerular membrane disruption.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Exercise-induced Haematuria"][/sc]
  • Microscopic Haematuria - Detected on urine dipstick or microscopy, often found incidentally. Occurs due to glomerular erythrocyte leakage post-exercise.
  • Macroscopic Haematuria - If present, this is visible blood in the urine; usually mild and transient.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Exercise-induced Haematuria"][/sc]
  • Persistent Haematuria beyond 72 hours - May indicate renal or urinary tract pathology, such as kidney stones or malignancy.
  • Associated Flank Pain, Fever, or Dysuria - Raises concern for infection, trauma, or more serious renal pathology.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Exercise-induced Haematuria"][/sc]
  • Haematuria lasting several days post-exercise - Could be mistaken for exercise-induced haematuria but may indicate renal disease if prolonged.
  • Exercise-induced haematuria without visible blood - Microscopic haematuria without macroscopic evidence can lead to a missed diagnosis if no urinalysis is performed.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Exercise-induced Haematuria"][/sc]
  • Urinalysis - Dipstick test shows positive for blood, with microscopic examination confirming erythrocytes. No significant proteinuria or leukocytes should be present in isolated exercise-induced haematuria.
  • Renal Function Tests - Typically normal. Abnormal results may suggest underlying renal disease.
  • Urine Cytology - Indicated if haematuria persists to rule out malignancy.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Exercise-induced Haematuria"][/sc]
  • A 25-year-old marathon runner reports red-tinged urine immediately after a long run, which resolves spontaneously after 48 hours with no associated pain or systemic symptoms.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Exercise-induced Haematuria"][/sc]
  • Urinary Tract Infection (UTI) - Differentiated by symptoms such as dysuria, frequency, and positive leukocytes on urinalysis.
  • Renal Calculi - Often presents with severe flank pain and haematuria. Imaging such as CT may reveal stones.
  • Glomerulonephritis - Persistent haematuria with proteinuria and potential renal dysfunction. Further autoimmune or renal testing is warranted.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Exercise-induced Haematuria"][/sc]
  • Reassurance and Monitoring - Educate on the benign nature of exercise-induced haematuria and advise re-evaluation if haematuria persists beyond 72 hours.
  • Avoidance of High-impact Exercise - Reducing the intensity or impact may prevent recurrence.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Exercise-induced Haematuria"][/sc]
  • None in isolated cases - Prognosis is excellent with no known long-term complications if transient haematuria resolves.
