Diabetic Gastroparesis
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Diabetic Gastroparesis"][/sc]
  • Diabetic gastroparesis is a chronic, delayed gastric emptying condition that occurs in individuals with diabetes due to autonomic neuropathy affecting the vagus nerve. It leads to ineffective peristalsis, slowing or halting stomach content passage into the small intestine.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Diabetic Gastroparesis"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Diabetic Gastroparesis"][/sc]
  • Prevalence in UK GP population: ~0.2–0.4% among diabetic patients.
  • UK hospital prevalence: Higher rates in hospitalised patients with long-term diabetes, especially in those with poor glycaemic control.
  • Worldwide prevalence: Estimated at 5% in patients with type 1 diabetes and 1% in those with type 2 diabetes.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Diabetic Gastroparesis"][/sc]
  • Prolonged hyperglycaemia leads to autonomic neuropathy, particularly affecting the vagus nerve.
  • Damage to the vagus nerve impairs gastric motility and disrupts the coordinated movement of stomach muscles, slowing gastric emptying.
  • Increased sensitivity of stomach smooth muscles to hormones such as gastrin and motilin may worsen symptoms.
  • Hyperglycaemia directly affects gastric motor function, further delaying gastric emptying.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Diabetic Gastroparesis"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Can be asymptomatic or with mild, intermittent symptoms in early diabetes.
  • Progressive phase: Gradual worsening of symptoms correlating with longer duration and poorer control of diabetes.
  • Chronic phase: Persistent, debilitating symptoms in advanced diabetes, often requiring medical intervention.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Diabetic Gastroparesis"][/sc]
  • Nausea and vomiting - Result from impaired gastric emptying, with undigested food lingering in the stomach.
  • Early satiety - Due to delayed emptying, the stomach remains full, causing a sensation of fullness soon after starting a meal.
  • Bloating and abdominal discomfort - Caused by food and gas buildup in the stomach, secondary to delayed transit.
  • Weight loss - Chronic symptoms interfere with regular eating habits and nutrient absorption, leading to unintended weight loss.
  • Reflux and regurgitation - The retained gastric contents can lead to reflux, exacerbated by delayed emptying.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Diabetic Gastroparesis"][/sc]
  • Abdominal distension - Visible bloating from gas and delayed food passage in the stomach.
  • Succussion splash - Audible sound upon shaking the abdomen, indicating retained stomach contents several hours post-meal.
  • Malnutrition indicators - Signs such as muscle wasting, dry skin, or hair loss due to poor nutritional intake.
  • Halitosis - Foul breath from retained, fermenting gastric contents, particularly noticeable in severe cases.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Diabetic Gastroparesis"][/sc]
  • Severe, persistent vomiting - May indicate risk of electrolyte imbalance and dehydration.
  • Significant weight loss and malnutrition - Suggests severe disease affecting overall nutritional status.
  • Frequent hypoglycaemia - Gastroparesis disrupts food absorption, complicating blood glucose control and increasing hypoglycaemia risk.
  • Acute abdominal pain - Could signify gastric bezoars or, rarely, perforation or bowel obstruction.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Diabetic Gastroparesis"][/sc]
  • Intermittent symptoms - Some patients may experience symptom-free periods, complicating diagnosis.
  • Mild bloating or dyspepsia - Often misattributed to general dyspeptic symptoms rather than gastroparesis.
  • Only postprandial hypoglycaemia - Some may present with erratic glucose levels without typical GI symptoms, leading to delayed diagnosis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Diabetic Gastroparesis"][/sc]
  • Gastric emptying scintigraphy - The gold standard test, showing delayed gastric emptying of a radiolabelled meal.
  • 13C-octanoic acid breath test - Non-invasive and useful to assess gastric emptying rate based on breath samples.
  • Upper endoscopy - Rules out mechanical obstruction, with findings sometimes showing retained food or bezoars.
  • Glycaemic monitoring - Essential for identifying and managing blood glucose fluctuations related to delayed digestion.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Diabetic Gastroparesis"][/sc]
  • 56-year-old woman with a 15-year history of type 2 diabetes, presents with 6 months of nausea, early satiety, and significant bloating, worsened by meals, leading to poor blood glucose control.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Diabetic Gastroparesis"][/sc]
  • Peptic ulcer disease - More likely to cause epigastric pain and melena, unlike gastroparesis.
  • Functional dyspepsia - Overlaps with gastroparesis symptoms but lacks objective delay in gastric emptying.
  • Chronic pancreatitis - May also have nausea and bloating, but typically presents with chronic epigastric pain and steatorrhoea.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Diabetic Gastroparesis"][/sc]
  • Dietary modification - Frequent small meals, low in fat and fibre, to ease gastric emptying.
  • Prokinetic agents - Metoclopramide or domperidone to promote gastric motility.
  • Optimisation of glycaemic control - Essential to reduce symptoms and prevent further autonomic nerve damage.
  • Antiemetics - Ondansetron or similar agents for symptomatic relief of nausea and vomiting.
  • Gastric electrical stimulation - For severe, refractory cases, though not universally available or recommended.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Diabetic Gastroparesis"][/sc]
  • Gastric bezoars - Indigestible masses in the stomach from delayed emptying, potentially causing obstruction.
  • Severe malnutrition - Risk of long-term nutritional deficiencies and weight loss.
  • Frequent hypoglycaemic episodes - Gastric emptying inconsistency complicates glucose management.
  • Reduced quality of life - Due to chronic symptoms and complex dietary and glycaemic management.
