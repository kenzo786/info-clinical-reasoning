Plantar Fasciitis
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Plantar Fasciitis"][/sc]
  • Plantar fasciitis is a common cause of heel pain resulting from inflammation or degeneration of the plantar fascia, a thick band of connective tissue supporting the arch of the foot. It typically presents with pain at the plantar aspect of the heel, particularly during weight-bearing activities, especially after rest.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Plantar Fasciitis"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Plantar Fasciitis"][/sc]
  • Plantar fasciitis accounts for approximately 11-15% of foot symptoms presenting in UK primary care.
  • The prevalence in UK hospital outpatient settings is lower but still significant, especially in orthopaedic and sports medicine clinics.
  • Globally, plantar fasciitis affects approximately 1 in 10 individuals during their lifetime, with higher prevalence in middle-aged and physically active populations.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Plantar Fasciitis"][/sc]
  • Repetitive microtrauma and mechanical overload lead to microtears and inflammation in the plantar fascia.
  • Over time, the condition can progress to a degenerative process, with fibroblast proliferation and matrix remodelling, termed "plantar fasciosis".
  • Risk factors include prolonged standing, high body mass index (BMI), improper footwear, and excessive physical activity.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Plantar Fasciitis"][/sc]
  • Acute Phase: Initial onset of pain, often associated with increased activity or a change in footwear.
  • Chronic Phase: Pain persists for months if untreated, becoming more pronounced with long periods of standing or walking.
  • Episodes may fluctuate, with symptoms worsening after inactivity or excessive activity.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Plantar Fasciitis"][/sc]
  • Heel Pain: Sharp or stabbing pain at the heel's underside, often worse in the morning or after rest due to stiffness of the plantar fascia.
  • Pain with Weight-Bearing: Discomfort exacerbated by prolonged standing, walking, or running, linked to mechanical stress on the plantar fascia.
  • Improvement with Activity: Pain may decrease after warming up but often returns with prolonged activity, indicating tissue adaptability but recurring strain.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Plantar Fasciitis"][/sc]
  • Localised Tenderness: Pain upon palpation at the medial calcaneal tubercle, correlating with the inflamed region of the plantar fascia.
  • Decreased Ankle Dorsiflexion: Tightness in the Achilles tendon or gastrocnemius muscle, contributing to increased tension on the plantar fascia.
  • Antalgic Gait: Altered walking pattern to reduce weight on the affected heel.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Plantar Fasciitis"][/sc]
  • Neurological Symptoms: Tingling, numbness, or weakness may indicate tarsal tunnel syndrome or nerve entrapment.
  • Systemic Symptoms: Fever, night sweats, or unexplained weight loss raise concern for infection or malignancy.
  • Sudden Severe Pain: Possible rupture of the plantar fascia.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Plantar Fasciitis"][/sc]
  • Bilateral Symptoms: Unusual in isolated plantar fasciitis; consider systemic conditions like rheumatoid arthritis.
  • Lateral Heel Pain: May point to other causes such as calcaneal stress fracture.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Plantar Fasciitis"][/sc]
  • Clinical Examination: Diagnosis is often clinical, with localisation of tenderness at the medial heel.
  • Ultrasound: Thickened plantar fascia (>4 mm) with hypoechoic areas and possible calcifications.
  • X-ray: May show calcaneal spurs, though not diagnostic of plantar fasciitis.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Plantar Fasciitis"][/sc]
  • A 45-year-old female presents with 3 months of sharp, morning heel pain, worse after rest but improving slightly with activity. She reports no systemic symptoms.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Plantar Fasciitis"][/sc]
  • Achilles Tendinopathy: Pain posteriorly, not plantar.
  • Calcaneal Stress Fracture: Persistent pain, tender to palpation diffusely.
  • Tarsal Tunnel Syndrome: Neuropathic symptoms and medial ankle pain.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Plantar Fasciitis"][/sc]
  • Conservative Management: Stretching exercises, orthotics, and NSAIDs are first-line treatments.
  • Physical Therapy: Focus on improving flexibility and strength in the foot and calf muscles.
  • Procedures: Corticosteroid injections or extracorporeal shockwave therapy in refractory cases.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Plantar Fasciitis"][/sc]
  • Chronic Pain: Long-term heel pain affecting quality of life.
  • Plantar Fascia Rupture: Rare, often due to aggressive treatments or corticosteroid injections.
  • Altered Gait: Leading to secondary musculoskeletal problems.
