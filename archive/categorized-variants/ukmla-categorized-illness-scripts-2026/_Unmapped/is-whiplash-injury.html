Whiplash Injury
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Whiplash Injury"][/sc]
  • Whiplash injury is a soft tissue neck injury resulting from a sudden and forceful hyperextension followed by hyperflexion of the cervical spine, typically due to rear-end vehicular collisions. It primarily affects the ligaments, intervertebral discs, and muscles of the neck.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Whiplash Injury"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Whiplash Injury"][/sc]
  • In the UK GP population, whiplash accounts for about 300,000 new cases annually, with 3–4% of these progressing to chronic symptoms.
  • Hospital prevalence is lower as most cases are managed in primary care, with emergency departments reporting a minority of severe cases involving associated fractures or dislocations.
  • Worldwide prevalence varies, with the incidence highest in regions with significant motor vehicle usage and road traffic accidents.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Whiplash Injury"][/sc]
  • The injury begins with rapid hyperextension of the cervical spine, causing strain on the anterior longitudinal ligament and intervertebral discs.
  • Subsequent hyperflexion places strain on the posterior elements, including ligaments and facet joints, leading to soft tissue microtears and inflammation.
  • This biomechanical insult results in pain, stiffness, and occasionally altered nerve function when structures such as the cervical nerve roots are involved.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Whiplash Injury"][/sc]
  • Acute Phase: Symptoms such as pain and stiffness peak within 24–48 hours post-injury.
  • Subacute Phase: Symptoms may begin to resolve within 1–2 weeks, but some cases show persistence.
  • Chronic Phase: Defined as symptoms persisting beyond 6 months, often associated with central sensitisation.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Whiplash Injury"][/sc]
  • Neck pain - Resulting from soft tissue inflammation and muscle strain.
  • Neck stiffness - Due to muscular spasm and swelling.
  • Headache - Often cervicogenic, linked to tension in the upper cervical muscles and ligaments.
  • Shoulder/arm pain - Caused by referred pain or nerve root irritation.
  • Dizziness - Related to disturbances in proprioception or vestibular function.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Whiplash Injury"][/sc]
  • Reduced neck range of motion - Due to pain and muscle guarding.
  • Tenderness over cervical spine - Indicative of soft tissue injury.
  • Paraspinal muscle spasm - Palpable tightness and pain upon examination.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Whiplash Injury"][/sc]
  • Neurological deficits - Indicate possible cervical nerve root or spinal cord compression.
  • Severe, progressive pain - Suggests fractures or underlying pathology like malignancy.
  • Unexplained weight loss or fever - May point to infection or malignancy.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Whiplash Injury"][/sc]
  • Minimal initial pain - Can delay diagnosis; inflammation develops later.
  • Symptoms without trauma history - Suggests alternative diagnoses like fibromyalgia.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Whiplash Injury"][/sc]
  • Cervical spine X-ray - Excludes fractures or dislocations.
  • MRI - Identifies soft tissue injuries or nerve compression.
  • CT scan - Used in high-energy trauma to evaluate complex fractures.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Whiplash Injury"][/sc]
  • A 35-year-old female presents with neck pain, stiffness, and headaches two days after a rear-end motor vehicle collision, with no neurological deficits on examination.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Whiplash Injury"][/sc]
  • Cervical fracture - Presents with severe pain and limited movement; X-ray/CT confirms diagnosis.
  • Cervical radiculopathy - Neurological deficits present, confirmed by MRI.
  • Fibromyalgia - Chronic pain with systemic symptoms such as fatigue.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Whiplash Injury"][/sc]
  • Analgesics - Paracetamol or NSAIDs for pain relief.
  • Physiotherapy - Early mobilisation and strengthening exercises.
  • Psychological support - Address chronic pain or PTSD risks.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Whiplash Injury"][/sc]
  • Chronic pain - Often related to central sensitisation.
  • Post-traumatic stress disorder - Psychological sequelae of the injury.
  • Work disability - Due to prolonged symptoms impacting daily function.
