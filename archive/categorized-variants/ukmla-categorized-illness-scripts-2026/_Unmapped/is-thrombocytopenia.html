Thrombocytopenia
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Thrombocytopenia"][/sc]
  • Thrombocytopenia is defined as a platelet count of less than 150 x 109/L in peripheral blood. It can be due to decreased production, increased destruction, or sequestration of platelets. Clinically, thrombocytopenia may lead to bleeding tendencies, though it is often asymptomatic, particularly when platelet counts remain above 50 x 109/L.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Thrombocytopenia"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Thrombocytopenia"][/sc]
  • Prevalence in UK GP population: Thrombocytopenia is relatively rare in primary care settings, but it may be seen in patients with underlying chronic diseases (e.g., liver disease, HIV).
  • Prevalence in UK hospitalised patients: The incidence increases in hospital settings, especially in critically ill patients, where it can affect up to 20-40% of intensive care patients.
  • Worldwide prevalence: Variable, depending on causes. For example, immune thrombocytopenia (ITP) affects approximately 2-5 per 100,000 adults annually.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Thrombocytopenia"][/sc]
  • Thrombocytopenia occurs due to one or more of the following mechanisms:
    • Decreased platelet production – Resulting from bone marrow suppression (e.g., leukaemia, aplastic anaemia, chemotherapy, infections like HIV).
    • Increased platelet destruction – As seen in immune-mediated conditions (e.g., ITP, systemic lupus erythematosus) or non-immune causes like disseminated intravascular coagulation (DIC).
    • Sequestration – Occurs when platelets are trapped in the spleen, often seen in patients with splenomegaly, as in liver cirrhosis.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Thrombocytopenia"][/sc]
  • Acute: May occur within hours to days in cases of acute infections, drug-induced thrombocytopenia, or DIC.
  • Subacute/Chronic: Conditions like ITP or bone marrow failure syndromes often develop more gradually over weeks to months, while chronic liver disease can cause long-term thrombocytopenia due to splenic sequestration.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Thrombocytopenia"][/sc]
  • Asymptomatic – Many patients remain asymptomatic, especially if platelet counts are above 50 x 109/L, as coagulation remains relatively preserved.
  • Easy bruising (Ecchymoses) – Caused by capillary fragility due to insufficient platelet-mediated haemostasis.
  • Prolonged bleeding from minor cuts – Impaired clot formation leads to increased bleeding duration from even superficial wounds.
  • Menorrhagia – Common in women due to the inability to maintain adequate platelet plug formation during menstruation.
  • Spontaneous mucosal bleeding – Particularly nasal or oral bleeding; this indicates a severe reduction in platelet count, as mucosal membranes are prone to minor trauma.
  • Fatigue – Generalised fatigue may occur, especially in chronic conditions associated with thrombocytopenia, possibly related to underlying anaemia or systemic disease.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Thrombocytopenia"][/sc]
  • Purpura – Non-blanching red or purple lesions caused by extravasation of blood due to impaired platelet function.
  • Petechiae – Pinpoint haemorrhages in the skin and mucous membranes, a common finding in severe thrombocytopenia (<30 x 109/L).
  • Haematoma – Large areas of bruising may develop after minor trauma due to insufficient haemostasis.
  • Splenomegaly – Seen in conditions such as liver cirrhosis, where platelet sequestration in an enlarged spleen leads to thrombocytopenia.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Thrombocytopenia"][/sc]
  • Spontaneous intracranial haemorrhage – A life-threatening complication in severe thrombocytopenia (<10 x 109/L) due to the inability to maintain cerebral microvascular integrity.
  • Gastrointestinal bleeding – Suggests a critical reduction in platelet count and increased risk for haemorrhage, potentially requiring urgent transfusion and endoscopic intervention.
  • Haematuria – May indicate severe thrombocytopenia or underlying systemic disease (e.g., DIC, vasculitis).
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Thrombocytopenia"][/sc]
  • Painless, severe purpura without any bruising – May occur in elderly patients where thrombocytopenia is due to bone marrow suppression rather than immune-mediated destruction.
  • Thrombocytopenia without bleeding – Found incidentally during routine blood tests, particularly in patients with chronic liver disease or splenomegaly.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Thrombocytopenia"][/sc]
  • Full Blood Count (FBC) – Confirms low platelet count (<150 x 109/L) and may reveal concurrent anaemia or leukopenia in conditions like aplastic anaemia or DIC.
  • Peripheral Blood Smear – Assesses platelet morphology; large platelets suggest ITP, while fragmented RBCs (schistocytes) may indicate DIC or thrombotic thrombocytopenic purpura (TTP).
  • Bone Marrow Biopsy – Used when primary bone marrow failure is suspected, revealing hypocellularity in aplastic anaemia or megakaryocytic hyperplasia in ITP.
  • Liver Function Tests (LFTs) – Abnormal results suggest liver disease, a common cause of thrombocytopenia due to splenic sequestration and decreased thrombopoietin production.
  • Coagulation Profile – Prolonged PT/INR and aPTT may indicate DIC, a condition associated with both bleeding and thrombocytopenia.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Thrombocytopenia"][/sc]
  • A 45-year-old female presents with easy bruising and heavy menstrual bleeding over the last few months. On examination, she has petechiae and mild splenomegaly. Platelet count is 30 x 109/L, and further investigation reveals immune thrombocytopenia.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Thrombocytopenia"][/sc]
  • Immune Thrombocytopenia (ITP) – Isolated thrombocytopenia, often with a history of viral illness or autoimmune disease.
  • Aplastic Anaemia – Associated with pancytopenia (anaemia, leukopenia, and thrombocytopenia), and bone marrow biopsy shows hypocellularity.
  • Disseminated Intravascular Coagulation (DIC) – Associated with bleeding, thrombocytopenia, and prolonged clotting times (raised PT/INR, aPTT).
  • Thrombotic Thrombocytopenic Purpura (TTP) – Characterised by thrombocytopenia, microangiopathic haemolytic anaemia, neurological symptoms, and renal dysfunction.
  • Hypersplenism – Associated with splenomegaly and pancytopenia, particularly in the context of liver disease.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Thrombocytopenia"][/sc]
  • Immune Thrombocytopenia (ITP) – First-line treatment includes corticosteroids (e.g., prednisolone) and intravenous immunoglobulin (IVIg) for severe cases. Splenectomy or thrombopoietin receptor agonists (e.g., eltrombopag) are options for refractory cases.
  • Thrombotic Thrombocytopenic Purpura (TTP) – Plasma exchange and corticosteroids are the mainstays of treatment.
  • DIC – Management involves treating the underlying cause, with supportive care including blood product replacement (platelets, fresh frozen plasma).
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Thrombocytopenia"][/sc]
  • Severe bleeding – Includes gastrointestinal and intracranial haemorrhage, which can be fatal, particularly when platelet counts drop below 10 x 109/L.
  • Chronic fatigue – Seen in patients with long-term conditions like ITP, particularly when associated with anaemia or secondary infections.
  • Secondary infections – Result from immunosuppression, particularly in patients treated with corticosteroids or immunosuppressive agents.
