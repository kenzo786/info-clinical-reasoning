March Fracture
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="March Fracture"][/sc]
  • March fracture is a type of stress fracture involving the metatarsal bones, most commonly the second or third metatarsal. It results from repetitive stress or overuse, particularly in activities involving prolonged walking, running, or marching, hence the name. Unlike acute fractures, it occurs without a significant traumatic event.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="March Fracture"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="March Fracture"][/sc]
  • Prevalence in the UK GP population: Commonly seen in athletes, military recruits, and individuals engaged in repetitive high-impact activities. Exact prevalence data in primary care is sparse.
  • UK hospital prevalence: Accounts for a significant proportion of stress fractures seen in sports medicine and orthopaedic clinics.
  • Worldwide prevalence: Approximately 20% of all stress fractures, with higher rates in physically active populations.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="March Fracture"][/sc]
  • Repetitive mechanical loading overwhelms the ability of bone remodelling to repair microdamage.
  • Increased osteoclastic activity weakens the bone before osteoblastic activity can lay down new bone, creating a microfracture.
  • In the metatarsals, these forces are exacerbated by their role in weight-bearing and propulsion during walking and running.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="March Fracture"][/sc]
  • Initial phase: Subtle onset of pain during activity.
  • Progressive phase: Pain becomes more intense and may persist at rest.
  • Chronic phase: If untreated, may result in complete fracture or deformity.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="March Fracture"][/sc]
  • Gradual onset of foot pain - Typically localised to the forefoot; due to repetitive microtrauma leading to bone stress.
  • Worsening pain with activity - Pain intensifies with weight-bearing activities as mechanical stress exacerbates microdamage.
  • Relief of pain with rest - Pain often subsides with cessation of activity, reflecting the non-traumatic nature of the injury.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="March Fracture"][/sc]
  • Localised tenderness - Palpable pain over the affected metatarsal, reflecting inflammation and periosteal irritation.
  • Swelling - Mild soft tissue swelling over the area due to localised inflammatory response.
  • Antalgic gait - Altered walking pattern to offload weight from the painful region.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="March Fracture"][/sc]
  • Persistent pain despite rest - Suggests progression to a complete fracture or delayed healing.
  • Severe swelling and erythema - May indicate infection or complex regional pain syndrome (CRPS).
  • Numbness or paraesthesia - Could signal nerve entrapment or compromise.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="March Fracture"][/sc]
  • No significant pain - Some patients may only report discomfort or notice swelling without classic pain.
  • Non-localised pain - Pain diffusely in the midfoot, leading to delayed diagnosis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="March Fracture"][/sc]
  • X-ray - Often normal initially; later may show periosteal reaction or fracture line.
  • Bone scan - Increased uptake in the affected area indicates bone remodelling.
  • MRI - Highly sensitive for early stress fractures, showing oedema and microfractures.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="March Fracture"][/sc]
  • A 22-year-old female military recruit presents with insidious onset forefoot pain, worsened by prolonged marching, with tenderness over the second metatarsal.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="March Fracture"][/sc]
  • Morton's neuroma - Presents with radiating pain and paraesthesia, typically between the third and fourth toes.
  • Metatarsalgia - Generalised forefoot pain, often associated with biomechanical abnormalities.
  • Plantar fasciitis - Pain localised to the heel and plantar fascia, not the metatarsals.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="March Fracture"][/sc]
  • Activity modification - Rest and cessation of weight-bearing activities to allow healing.
  • Immobilisation - Use of a stiff-soled shoe or walking boot.
  • Physiotherapy - Gradual return to activity with strengthening and conditioning exercises.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="March Fracture"][/sc]
  • Complete fracture - May occur if stress fracture is untreated or ignored.
  • Chronic pain - Prolonged recovery due to delayed diagnosis or inadequate treatment.
