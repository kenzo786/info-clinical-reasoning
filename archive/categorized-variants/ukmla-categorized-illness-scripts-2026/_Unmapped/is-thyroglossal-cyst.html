Thyroglossal Cyst
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Thyroglossal Cyst"][/sc]
  • A thyroglossal cyst is a congenital neck mass arising from persistent remnants of the thyroglossal duct, the tract along which the thyroid gland descends during embryonic development. It typically presents as a painless, midline cystic swelling in the neck, often near the hyoid bone.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Thyroglossal Cyst"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Thyroglossal Cyst"][/sc]
  • Prevalence in the UK GP population: Thyroglossal cysts are the most common congenital cervical abnormality, accounting for around 70% of congenital neck masses. They are present in 7% of the general population but often remain asymptomatic.
  • UK hospital prevalence: In the hospital setting, they are commonly diagnosed in paediatric and young adult populations, especially in the first two decades of life.
  • Worldwide prevalence: There is no significant geographical variance in the prevalence of thyroglossal cysts, and they are observed globally.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Thyroglossal Cyst"][/sc]
  • During embryogenesis, the thyroid gland forms at the base of the tongue and descends along the thyroglossal duct to its final position in the neck. Normally, the duct obliterates after this descent. A thyroglossal cyst forms when the duct fails to fully regress, leaving a residual epithelial-lined tract that can fill with fluid or mucus, forming a cyst.
  • Infection of the cyst is common, particularly if the cyst becomes obstructed, leading to inflammation and abscess formation.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Thyroglossal Cyst"][/sc]
  • Congenital in origin, thyroglossal cysts are often present at birth but may not become noticeable until later in childhood or early adulthood.
  • They can remain asymptomatic for years and only become symptomatic if they enlarge or become infected. In some cases, rapid enlargement may occur due to infection or trauma.
  • If untreated, recurrent infections may develop, and there is a small risk (approximately 1%) of malignant transformation, typically into papillary thyroid carcinoma.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Thyroglossal Cyst"][/sc]
  • Painless, midline neck mass - The cyst usually presents as a smooth, soft, and non-tender swelling. It moves upwards when the patient swallows or protrudes their tongue due to its attachment to the foramen cecum via the thyroglossal tract.
  • Dysphagia - Occurs if the cyst enlarges significantly, compressing adjacent structures like the oesophagus.
  • Infection - Pain, erythema, and fever may occur if the cyst becomes infected, leading to abscess formation and inflammation.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Thyroglossal Cyst"][/sc]
  • Midline neck mass - A palpable, mobile cyst located in the midline of the neck, typically near the hyoid bone, that moves with swallowing or tongue protrusion.
  • Inflammation - Signs of infection, such as redness, tenderness, and warmth over the cyst, may indicate secondary infection.
  • Fistula formation - Chronic infection can result in the development of a sinus tract or fistula, which may drain mucus or pus.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Thyroglossal Cyst"][/sc]
  • Rapid growth - Suggests possible malignant transformation, particularly into papillary thyroid carcinoma, and warrants further investigation.
  • Persistent dysphagia or dyspnoea - May indicate compression of the oesophagus or trachea due to an enlarging cyst, requiring urgent evaluation.
  • Recurrent infections - Multiple episodes of infection may indicate chronic inflammation, increasing the risk of complications like fistula formation or abscesses.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Thyroglossal Cyst"][/sc]
  • Lateral neck mass - Though thyroglossal cysts are typically midline, occasionally they can present laterally, leading to confusion with branchial cleft cysts or lymphadenopathy.
  • Thyroid tissue involvement - Rarely, the cyst may contain ectopic thyroid tissue, which could lead to hypothyroidism if the ectopic tissue is removed surgically.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Thyroglossal Cyst"][/sc]
  • Ultrasound scan (USS) - Confirms the cystic nature of the mass, shows its relation to surrounding structures, and helps rule out solid or malignant masses.
  • Fine-needle aspiration (FNA) - May be performed if there is suspicion of malignancy or to confirm infection by aspirating fluid for cytology or microbiology.
  • CT or MRI scan - These imaging modalities are reserved for cases with unclear anatomy or suspicion of malignancy, providing detailed structural information.
  • Thyroid function tests (TFTs) - Assesses thyroid hormone levels to rule out associated thyroid dysfunction, especially if the cyst contains ectopic thyroid tissue.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Thyroglossal Cyst"][/sc]
  • A 15-year-old male presents with a painless, midline neck mass that moves with swallowing. The swelling has gradually increased in size over the past year. There is no history of pain, dysphagia, or recent infection.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Thyroglossal Cyst"][/sc]
  • Branchial cleft cyst - Typically presents as a lateral neck mass, usually in the anterior triangle, without midline movement on swallowing.
  • Thyroid nodule - May also present as a neck mass, but thyroid nodules are often more fixed and may be associated with abnormal TFTs or a palpable thyroid gland.
  • Lymphadenopathy - Often presents as a firm, mobile mass but is usually associated with infection, malignancy, or systemic symptoms.
  • Dermoid cyst - A congenital cyst that may present as a neck mass but typically does not move with swallowing or tongue protrusion.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Thyroglossal Cyst"][/sc]
  • Surgical excision (Sistrunk procedure) - The definitive treatment involves removing the cyst along with part of the hyoid bone to minimise recurrence. This is the standard of care in the UK.
  • Antibiotics - If the cyst is infected, empirical antibiotics are started, followed by drainage of any abscess. Surgery is delayed until the infection resolves.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Thyroglossal Cyst"][/sc]
  • Recurrent infections - Chronic infection can lead to abscess formation and fistulae, significantly increasing morbidity.
  • Malignant transformation - Although rare (around 1% of cases), thyroglossal cysts may undergo malignant change, usually into papillary thyroid carcinoma.
  • Recurrence - Recurrence after surgery is rare but can occur, particularly if the Sistrunk procedure is not properly performed.
