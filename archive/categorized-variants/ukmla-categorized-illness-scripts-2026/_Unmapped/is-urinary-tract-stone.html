Urinary Tract Stone (Urolithiasis)
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Urinary Tract Stone (Urolithiasis)"][/sc]
  • Urinary tract stones, or urolithiasis, are solid masses formed from crystals in the urinary tract, commonly composed of calcium oxalate, uric acid, struvite, or cystine. These stones can develop in the kidneys, ureters, bladder, or urethra, causing pain, obstruction, and potential kidney damage if left untreated.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Urinary Tract Stone (Urolithiasis)"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Urinary Tract Stone (Urolithiasis)"][/sc]
  • Prevalence in UK GP population: Approximately 1-2% of adults present with symptomatic urinary stones.
  • Hospital prevalence in the UK: Stones account for 5-10% of hospital urology admissions.
  • Worldwide prevalence: Varies by region, 1-15%, with higher rates in hotter climates due to dehydration risk.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Urinary Tract Stone (Urolithiasis)"][/sc]
  • Stones form when urine becomes supersaturated with stone-forming compounds (e.g., calcium, oxalate, uric acid). This supersaturation leads to crystallisation, followed by stone growth in susceptible individuals.
  • Key risk factors include dehydration (reduces urine volume, increasing concentration), dietary factors (high oxalate, low fluid intake), metabolic abnormalities, and urinary tract infections (UTIs) which promote struvite stone formation.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Urinary Tract Stone (Urolithiasis)"][/sc]
  • Acute: Onset of severe pain, often renal colic, when the stone obstructs urinary flow.
  • Subacute/Chronic: Smaller stones may remain asymptomatic or cause mild discomfort, developing over months or years.
  • Recurrent: High recurrence rate, particularly in those with metabolic risk factors, within 5 years.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Urinary Tract Stone (Urolithiasis)"][/sc]
  • Flank pain - Severe, colicky pain in the flank due to ureteral spasm from obstruction.
  • Haematuria - Microscopic or visible blood in urine as stones damage the ureter or bladder lining.
  • Nausea and vomiting - Result from severe pain and vagal stimulation due to the stone obstruction.
  • Urgency/frequency - Particularly if the stone is located near the bladder, causing irritation.
  • Asymptomatic - Small or non-obstructive stones may produce no symptoms and be incidentally found on imaging.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Urinary Tract Stone (Urolithiasis)"][/sc]
  • Costovertebral angle (CVA) tenderness - Indicates inflammation or irritation around the kidney from the obstructed stone.
  • Gross haematuria - Visible blood in urine in severe cases due to mucosal damage by the stone.
  • Abdominal guarding - Voluntary or involuntary muscle contraction in response to pain in the abdomen or flank.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Urinary Tract Stone (Urolithiasis)"][/sc]
  • Fever and chills - Signify a potential urinary infection, indicating pyelonephritis or urosepsis.
  • Severe, unrelenting pain - May indicate worsening obstruction or possible kidney damage.
  • Oliguria or anuria - Reduced or absent urine output may signal complete obstruction, a medical emergency.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Urinary Tract Stone (Urolithiasis)"][/sc]
  • Painless haematuria - Common in bladder stones or incidental stones, without ureteral obstruction.
  • Recurrent UTIs - May occur with struvite stones as a nidus for infection.
  • Lower abdominal pain - Particularly with bladder or distal ureteral stones, potentially mimicking pelvic pathology.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Urinary Tract Stone (Urolithiasis)"][/sc]
  • Urinalysis - Detects haematuria and crystals; possible pyuria if infection coexists.
  • Non-contrast CT scan (KUB) - Highly sensitive and specific for stones; shows location, size, and density.
  • Ultrasound - Useful for detecting hydronephrosis; may miss smaller stones.
  • Serum urea and electrolytes - Assess renal function and electrolyte imbalances.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Urinary Tract Stone (Urolithiasis)"][/sc]
  • A 45-year-old male presents with sudden onset severe right flank pain, radiating to the groin, with associated nausea and haematuria, suggestive of urinary tract stone.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Urinary Tract Stone (Urolithiasis)"][/sc]
  • Pyelonephritis - Presents with fever, systemic symptoms; CVA tenderness but lacks colicky pain.
  • Acute appendicitis - Right lower quadrant pain, often with fever; does not radiate to the groin.
  • Abdominal aortic aneurysm - Back pain, hypotension if ruptured, and pulsatile mass in abdomen.
  • Biliary colic - Epigastric pain radiating to the back; nausea and vomiting but no haematuria.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Urinary Tract Stone (Urolithiasis)"][/sc]
  • Analgesia - NSAIDs (e.g., ibuprofen) or opioids for severe pain; NSAIDs reduce ureteral inflammation.
  • Hydration - Encourage oral fluids; IV fluids if necessary to maintain adequate urine flow.
  • Medical expulsive therapy - Alpha-blockers (e.g., tamsulosin) may help in passing stones <10 mm.
  • Definitive management - Extracorporeal shock wave lithotripsy (ESWL), ureteroscopy, or percutaneous nephrolithotomy for larger stones.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Urinary Tract Stone (Urolithiasis)"][/sc]
  • Obstructive nephropathy - Chronic obstruction leading to kidney damage or renal failure.
  • Hydronephrosis - Swelling of the kidney due to urine build-up behind an obstructed stone.
  • Urinary tract infections (UTIs) - Stones act as a nidus for infection, increasing risk of UTIs and pyelonephritis.
  • Urosepsis - Severe infection spreading to the bloodstream; requires prompt treatment.
