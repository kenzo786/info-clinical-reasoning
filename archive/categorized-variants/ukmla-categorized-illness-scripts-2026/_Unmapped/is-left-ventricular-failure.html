Left Ventricular Failure
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Left Ventricular Failure"][/sc]
  • Left ventricular failure (LVF) is a condition characterised by the inability of the left ventricle to pump blood effectively, leading to inadequate systemic perfusion and congestion in the pulmonary circulation. It may occur as a result of systolic dysfunction (impaired contraction) or diastolic dysfunction (impaired relaxation).
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Left Ventricular Failure"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Left Ventricular Failure"][/sc]
  • In the UK, approximately 900,000 people live with heart failure, with LVF contributing significantly.
  • Hospital prevalence: Around 70% of heart failure hospital admissions involve LVF.
  • Worldwide prevalence: Estimated to affect 1-2% of the population, increasing to over 10% in those aged >70 years.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Left Ventricular Failure"][/sc]
  • Reduced left ventricular contractility or compliance leads to impaired ejection of blood into the systemic circulation.
  • Increased left ventricular end-diastolic pressure (LVEDP) results in backward transmission of pressure to the pulmonary veins and capillaries, causing pulmonary congestion and oedema.
  • Decreased cardiac output triggers compensatory mechanisms, such as activation of the renin-angiotensin-aldosterone system (RAAS), leading to fluid retention and further worsening of congestion.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Left Ventricular Failure"][/sc]
  • Acute: Rapid onset, often due to myocardial infarction, arrhythmia, or acute valvular disease.
  • Chronic: Gradual progression, typically associated with hypertension or chronic ischaemic heart disease.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Left Ventricular Failure"][/sc]
  • Dyspnoea: Pulmonary congestion leads to reduced gas exchange and increased work of breathing.
  • Orthopnoea: Redistribution of fluid into the pulmonary circulation when lying flat increases congestion.
  • Paroxysmal nocturnal dyspnoea: Episodic nocturnal pulmonary oedema due to supine positioning.
  • Fatigue: Decreased cardiac output leads to poor systemic perfusion.
  • Cough with frothy sputum: Pulmonary oedema may manifest as productive cough.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Left Ventricular Failure"][/sc]
  • Basal crackles: Indicative of pulmonary oedema due to fluid in the alveoli.
  • Tachycardia: Compensatory mechanism to maintain cardiac output.
  • Peripheral cyanosis: Reduced systemic perfusion leads to desaturation of haemoglobin in extremities.
  • Displaced apex beat: Suggests left ventricular hypertrophy or dilation.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Left Ventricular Failure"][/sc]
  • Severe dyspnoea or orthopnoea: May indicate acute pulmonary oedema.
  • Hypotension: Suggests cardiogenic shock or advanced heart failure.
  • Altered mental state: Can signify poor perfusion and hypoxia.
  • Chest pain: May indicate myocardial ischaemia or infarction.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Left Ventricular Failure"][/sc]
  • Unexplained fatigue: May be the sole symptom in elderly patients.
  • Chronic cough: Sometimes misdiagnosed as a respiratory condition.
  • Nocturia: Fluid redistribution and increased renal perfusion at night.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Left Ventricular Failure"][/sc]
  • Chest X-ray: May show pulmonary oedema, cardiomegaly, or pleural effusions.
  • ECG: Detects arrhythmias, ischaemia, or left ventricular hypertrophy.
  • Echocardiogram: Confirms reduced ejection fraction or diastolic dysfunction.
  • BNP/NT-proBNP: Elevated levels indicate myocardial strain.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Left Ventricular Failure"][/sc]
  • A 65-year-old male with a history of hypertension presents with worsening dyspnoea over 2 weeks, orthopnoea, and fatigue. Examination reveals basal crackles and a displaced apex beat.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Left Ventricular Failure"][/sc]
  • Chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD): Usually associated with a history of smoking and airflow limitation on spirometry.
  • Pulmonary embolism: Acute onset with pleuritic chest pain and haemoptysis; confirmed by CT pulmonary angiography.
  • Mitral valve disease: Murmur on auscultation, confirmed by echocardiography.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Left Ventricular Failure"][/sc]
  • Lifestyle modifications: Salt restriction, fluid monitoring, and regular exercise.
  • Pharmacological therapy: ACE inhibitors, beta-blockers, diuretics, and mineralocorticoid receptor antagonists.
  • Device therapy: Implantable cardioverter defibrillators or biventricular pacemakers in severe cases.
  • Acute management: Oxygen, intravenous diuretics, and vasodilators in cases of pulmonary oedema.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Left Ventricular Failure"][/sc]
  • Cardiogenic shock: Severe reduction in cardiac output causing multiorgan failure.
  • Arrhythmias: Atrial fibrillation or ventricular tachycardia due to myocardial strain.
  • Thromboembolism: Due to stasis of blood in a dilated left ventricle.
