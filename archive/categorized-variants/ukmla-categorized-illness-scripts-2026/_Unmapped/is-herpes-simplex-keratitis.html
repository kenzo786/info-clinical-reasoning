Herpes Simplex Keratitis
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Herpes Simplex Keratitis"][/sc]
  • Herpes simplex keratitis (HSK) is a viral infection of the cornea caused predominantly by the herpes simplex virus type 1 (HSV-1), characterised by epithelial or stromal involvement leading to inflammation, ulceration, and potential visual impairment. It is the most common cause of corneal blindness in developed countries.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Herpes Simplex Keratitis"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Herpes Simplex Keratitis"][/sc]
  • Prevalence in UK GP population: Approximately 6 per 100,000 annually.
  • UK hospital prevalence: Accounts for 10-15% of corneal ulcer presentations.
  • Worldwide prevalence: Estimated incidence of 1.5 million cases per year, with 40,000 new cases of corneal blindness annually.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Herpes Simplex Keratitis"][/sc]
  • Primary infection with HSV-1 often occurs in childhood and remains latent in the trigeminal ganglion.
  • Reactivation occurs due to triggers such as stress, UV light, immunosuppression, or trauma.
  • The virus replicates, causing corneal epithelial cell death, inflammation, and immune-mediated stromal damage.
  • Chronic inflammation and scarring may lead to reduced corneal transparency and neovascularisation.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Herpes Simplex Keratitis"][/sc]
  • Acute phase: Epithelial keratitis with dendritic ulcers typically resolves in 1-2 weeks with treatment.
  • Chronic phase: May progress to stromal keratitis, leading to recurrent episodes over months to years.
  • Long-term: Risk of corneal scarring, reduced vision, or blindness in untreated or severe cases.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Herpes Simplex Keratitis"][/sc]
  • Eye pain - Due to corneal nerve inflammation and damage.
  • Photophobia - Resulting from light sensitivity due to corneal inflammation.
  • Blurred vision - Caused by corneal oedema or epithelial disruption.
  • Tearing - Reflex lacrimation in response to corneal irritation.
  • Foreign body sensation - From corneal ulceration and inflammation.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Herpes Simplex Keratitis"][/sc]
  • Dendritic corneal ulcers - Pathognomonic branching lesions observed on fluorescein staining, due to viral replication and epithelial damage.
  • Reduced corneal sensation - HSV affects corneal nerves, leading to hypoesthesia or anaesthesia.
  • Conjunctival injection - Reflects localised inflammation around the cornea.
  • Corneal oedema - Due to inflammatory cell infiltration and endothelial dysfunction.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Herpes Simplex Keratitis"][/sc]
  • Deep stromal involvement - Indicates stromal keratitis, which has a high risk of scarring and vision loss.
  • Corneal perforation - A rare but severe complication signalling ulcer progression.
  • Secondary bacterial infection - Worsens corneal damage and can lead to endophthalmitis.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Herpes Simplex Keratitis"][/sc]
  • Disciform keratitis - Stromal oedema without epithelial ulceration, often immune-mediated.
  • Chronic epithelial defects - May mimic neurotrophic keratopathy.
  • Bilateral involvement - Rare and typically occurs in immunocompromised patients.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Herpes Simplex Keratitis"][/sc]
  • Fluorescein staining - Reveals dendritic ulcers with terminal bulbs; confirms epithelial keratitis.
  • Slit lamp examination - Detects stromal oedema, keratic precipitates, and reduced corneal sensation.
  • Viral PCR - Confirms HSV DNA, especially in atypical or severe cases.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Herpes Simplex Keratitis"][/sc]
  • A 35-year-old male presents with a 3-day history of unilateral eye pain, photophobia, and blurred vision. Examination reveals dendritic corneal ulcers on fluorescein staining and reduced corneal sensation.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Herpes Simplex Keratitis"][/sc]
  • Bacterial keratitis - Typically more purulent discharge, no dendritic ulcers, positive bacterial cultures.
  • Acanthamoeba keratitis - Severe pain disproportionate to signs, history of contact lens use, ring infiltrate on examination.
  • Fungal keratitis - Satellite lesions, slower onset, positive fungal cultures.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Herpes Simplex Keratitis"][/sc]
  • Topical antivirals - Aciclovir 3% ointment 5 times daily for epithelial keratitis.
  • Oral antivirals - Aciclovir 400 mg 5 times daily for stromal or severe epithelial keratitis.
  • Avoid topical steroids - Contraindicated in epithelial keratitis but used cautiously in stromal keratitis under specialist guidance.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Herpes Simplex Keratitis"][/sc]
  • Corneal scarring - Leading to permanent vision loss.
  • Neovascularisation - Compromising corneal transparency.
  • Glaucoma - Secondary to chronic inflammation or steroid use.
