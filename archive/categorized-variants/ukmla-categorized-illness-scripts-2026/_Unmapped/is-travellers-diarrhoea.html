Traveller’s Diarrhoea
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Traveller’s Diarrhoea"][/sc]
  • Traveller’s diarrhoea is defined as the passage of three or more unformed stools per day accompanied by symptoms such as abdominal cramps, nausea, or vomiting in a person who has recently travelled to a resource-limited area. It is usually caused by ingesting contaminated food or water, with bacteria such as *Escherichia coli* being the most common pathogen.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Traveller’s Diarrhoea"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Traveller’s Diarrhoea"][/sc]
  • Prevalence in the UK GP population: Very low, as cases are usually contracted abroad.
  • UK hospital prevalence: Accounts for some hospitalisations post-travel, especially if symptoms are severe or persistent.
  • Worldwide prevalence: Affects 20-50% of international travellers, particularly those visiting regions like South Asia, Sub-Saharan Africa, Latin America, and Southeast Asia.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Traveller’s Diarrhoea"][/sc]
  • Traveller’s diarrhoea primarily results from the ingestion of contaminated food or water containing pathogenic microorganisms, most commonly enterotoxigenic *E. coli* (ETEC). These bacteria produce toxins that stimulate intestinal cells to secrete electrolytes and water into the gut, leading to diarrhoea. Other organisms such as *Campylobacter*, *Salmonella*, and viruses like norovirus can also cause this condition. The immune system attempts to clear the infection, resulting in systemic symptoms like fever and malaise.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Traveller’s Diarrhoea"][/sc]
  • Incubation period: Typically 1–3 days after ingestion of contaminated food or water.
  • Acute phase: Symptoms develop rapidly and usually resolve within 3–7 days, although some cases can last up to 10 days.
  • Chronic phase: Rarely, complications such as post-infectious irritable bowel syndrome (IBS) can result in prolonged symptoms lasting weeks to months.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Traveller’s Diarrhoea"][/sc]
  • Diarrhoea – Frequent passage of loose stools occurs due to increased intestinal secretion of fluids and electrolytes in response to bacterial toxins.
  • Abdominal cramping – Spasms of the intestinal muscles caused by inflammation and irritation of the gut lining.
  • Nausea and vomiting – Likely due to toxins or irritation of the gastrointestinal tract, causing activation of the vomiting centre in the brain.
  • Fever – A systemic response to infection, caused by immune activation against pathogens.
  • Fatigue and malaise – Generalised symptoms resulting from the immune response and dehydration.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Traveller’s Diarrhoea"][/sc]
  • Dehydration – Signs include dry mucous membranes, decreased skin turgor, and reduced urine output, resulting from fluid losses due to diarrhoea.
  • Tachycardia – Increased heart rate as a compensatory response to fluid loss and dehydration.
  • Hypotension – Low blood pressure in severe cases of dehydration, resulting from decreased circulating blood volume.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Traveller’s Diarrhoea"][/sc]
  • Blood in stools – May indicate invasive bacterial infection (e.g., *Shigella*, *Campylobacter*) or amoebic dysentery, requiring urgent treatment.
  • Persistent diarrhoea (>10 days) – Could suggest parasitic infections (e.g., *Giardia lamblia*) or post-infectious irritable bowel syndrome.
  • High fever (>38.5°C) – Suggests a more severe or systemic infection that may require hospitalisation and intravenous treatment.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Traveller’s Diarrhoea"][/sc]
  • Some cases may present with only mild diarrhoea or without abdominal pain, leading to underreporting or delayed diagnosis.
  • Chronic symptoms such as fatigue and bloating, especially after return from travel, may be the only signs of persistent infection or post-infectious IBS.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Traveller’s Diarrhoea"][/sc]
  • Stool culture – Identifies bacterial pathogens. Positive findings indicate infection with organisms such as *E. coli*, *Salmonella*, or *Shigella*.
  • Ova, cysts, and parasites (OCP) test – Used to detect parasitic infections such as *Giardia* or *Entamoeba histolytica*.
  • FBC (Full Blood Count) – May show raised white cell count, indicating an immune response to infection.
  • Urea and electrolytes (U&E) – Assesses dehydration and electrolyte imbalances due to diarrhoea.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Traveller’s Diarrhoea"][/sc]
  • A 30-year-old male presents with a 3-day history of watery diarrhoea, abdominal cramps, and low-grade fever, 2 days after returning from a trip to South East Asia. He reports eating street food during his travels.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Traveller’s Diarrhoea"][/sc]
  • Viral gastroenteritis – More likely to cause vomiting and resolves faster (within 2–3 days), often associated with outbreaks.
  • Food poisoning – Usually has a more abrupt onset within hours after ingestion, with more pronounced vomiting.
  • Giardiasis – Chronic, watery diarrhoea lasting more than 10 days, often associated with bloating and weight loss.
  • Inflammatory bowel disease (IBD) – Chronic diarrhoea with blood and systemic symptoms, often with a history of previous episodes.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Traveller’s Diarrhoea"][/sc]
  • Hydration – Oral rehydration solutions (ORS) to replace lost fluids and electrolytes.
  • Antimotility agents (e.g., loperamide) – Can be used to reduce diarrhoea but should be avoided if blood is present in stools.
  • Antibiotics – In moderate to severe cases, empirical antibiotics such as azithromycin are recommended, especially if bacterial infection is suspected.
  • Probiotics – May help in reducing the duration of diarrhoea by restoring gut flora.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Traveller’s Diarrhoea"][/sc]
  • Dehydration – Most common complication, especially in vulnerable populations like children and the elderly.
  • Post-infectious IBS – Chronic abdominal pain and altered bowel habits following resolution of the acute infection.
  • Reactive arthritis – Inflammation of the joints following infection with certain pathogens, including *Campylobacter* and *Salmonella*.
