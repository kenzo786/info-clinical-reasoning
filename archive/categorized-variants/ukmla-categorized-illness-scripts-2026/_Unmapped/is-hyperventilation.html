Hyperventilation
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Hyperventilation"][/sc]
  • Hyperventilation is a condition characterised by abnormally rapid or deep breathing, leading to a decrease in arterial carbon dioxide levels (hypocapnia). This results in respiratory alkalosis, which can cause a range of physiological disturbances. It is often triggered by anxiety, stress, or medical conditions such as lung disease or metabolic disturbances.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Hyperventilation"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Hyperventilation"][/sc]
  • Prevalence in the UK GP population: Around 6-11% of patients may experience hyperventilation episodes, commonly associated with anxiety disorders.
  • UK hospital prevalence: Hyperventilation is often encountered in emergency departments due to panic attacks or metabolic disturbances.
  • Worldwide prevalence: It varies based on underlying causes, with panic disorder affecting 2-3% of the global population.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Hyperventilation"][/sc]
  • Hyperventilation leads to excessive elimination of CO2, resulting in a decrease in arterial carbon dioxide (PaCO2). This reduction causes respiratory alkalosis, characterised by an increase in blood pH.
  • The higher pH causes vasoconstriction, particularly in cerebral vessels, reducing blood flow to the brain, which can lead to dizziness, lightheadedness, and other neurological symptoms.
  • Alkalosis also shifts the oxygen-haemoglobin dissociation curve to the left, impairing oxygen delivery to tissues.
  • Other systemic effects include altered calcium and potassium levels, leading to muscle twitching and paraesthesia.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Hyperventilation"][/sc]
  • Acute phase: Onset is usually sudden and often associated with emotional triggers (e.g., stress, anxiety) or metabolic causes (e.g., acidosis).
  • Chronic phase: Hyperventilation syndrome may occur, where episodic hyperventilation recurs over a long period, often without clear triggers.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Hyperventilation"][/sc]
  • Dyspnoea (shortness of breath) - Results from the feeling of air hunger despite rapid breathing; pathophysiologically linked to the body's urge to normalise CO2 levels.
  • Dizziness or lightheadedness - Caused by cerebral vasoconstriction due to hypocapnia and reduced blood flow to the brain.
  • Chest pain - Often non-cardiac and related to muscle tension or anxiety during hyperventilation.
  • Paraesthesia (numbness and tingling) - Caused by changes in calcium levels due to alkalosis, affecting nerve function, commonly seen in the fingers, toes, and around the mouth.
  • Palpitations - Triggered by sympathetic activation during anxiety or stress.
  • Syncope (fainting) - In rare cases, severe cerebral vasoconstriction can reduce cerebral perfusion enough to cause syncope.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Hyperventilation"][/sc]
  • Tachypnoea (rapid breathing) - The hallmark of hyperventilation, visible as rapid, shallow breathing.
  • Carpopedal spasm - A classic sign, caused by hypocalcaemia secondary to respiratory alkalosis, leading to involuntary muscle contractions in the hands and feet.
  • Perioral paraesthesia - Numbness or tingling around the mouth, due to altered calcium and potassium levels.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Hyperventilation"][/sc]
  • Chest pain with radiation - Could indicate a cardiac cause (e.g., myocardial infarction) rather than hyperventilation alone.
  • Severe dyspnoea unresponsive to breathing techniques - May suggest an underlying pulmonary condition, such as a pulmonary embolism or pneumothorax.
  • Altered mental status or confusion - Suggests severe hypocapnia or cerebral hypoxia and requires urgent investigation.
  • Syncope with exertion - May indicate a more serious cardiovascular condition such as aortic stenosis.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Hyperventilation"][/sc]
  • Asymptomatic hyperventilation - Some patients may have recurrent hyperventilation without significant symptoms, particularly those with chronic anxiety.
  • Chronic fatigue or sleep disturbances - In patients with hyperventilation syndrome, these symptoms may dominate the clinical picture rather than acute respiratory distress.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Hyperventilation"][/sc]
  • Arterial Blood Gas (ABG) - Typically shows low PaCO2 (7.45), confirming respiratory alkalosis.
  • ECG - Done to rule out cardiac causes, typically normal but may show non-specific ST-T changes due to alkalosis.
  • Chest X-ray - Usually normal, but used to exclude structural lung disease or pneumothorax.
  • Serum electrolytes - Hypocalcaemia and hypokalaemia may be seen as a result of alkalosis.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Hyperventilation"][/sc]
  • A 28-year-old woman presents to A&E with sudden onset of dyspnoea, dizziness, and tingling in her fingers after an argument. She reports feeling anxious and is breathing rapidly but has no history of lung or heart disease. Physical exam reveals tachypnoea and carpopedal spasm.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Hyperventilation"][/sc]
  • Panic disorder - May present similarly with hyperventilation but often includes overwhelming fear and recurrent episodes of panic attacks.
  • Asthma - Hyperventilation may occur, but asthma will also present with wheezing and reversible airway obstruction on spirometry.
  • Pulmonary embolism - Acute dyspnoea and hyperventilation, but usually with pleuritic chest pain and signs of right heart strain on ECG or imaging.
  • Metabolic acidosis - Hyperventilation compensates for acidosis, but ABG will show low pH and bicarbonate levels.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Hyperventilation"][/sc]
  • Reassurance and breathing exercises - Encouraging slow, controlled breathing (e.g., pursed-lip breathing) to reduce respiratory rate and correct CO2 levels.
  • Psychological support - Referral for cognitive behavioural therapy (CBT) if anxiety is a trigger, as per UK guidelines for managing anxiety disorders.
  • Benzodiazepines - May be used in the acute setting to relieve severe anxiety and break the cycle of hyperventilation, though not recommended long-term.
  • Treat underlying cause - If metabolic or respiratory causes are identified, they should be managed accordingly (e.g., correcting metabolic acidosis).
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Hyperventilation"][/sc]
  • Syncope - Cerebral vasoconstriction from severe hypocapnia can reduce brain perfusion, leading to fainting.
  • Severe hypocalcaemia - Prolonged alkalosis can significantly lower ionised calcium levels, causing muscle spasms and tetany.
  • Chronic hyperventilation syndrome - May result in ongoing symptoms like fatigue, muscle tension, and dyspnoea even after acute episodes resolve.
