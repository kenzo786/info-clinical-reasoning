Salivary Gland Tumour
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Salivary Gland Tumour"][/sc]
  • A salivary gland tumour refers to an abnormal growth within one of the salivary glands. The major salivary glands include the parotid, submandibular, and sublingual glands, with tumours most commonly arising in the parotid gland (about 80%). These tumours can be benign or malignant, with benign tumours such as pleomorphic adenoma being more common. Malignant forms include mucoepidermoid carcinoma and adenoid cystic carcinoma.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Salivary Gland Tumour"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Salivary Gland Tumour"][/sc]
  • Prevalence in UK GP population: Rare, estimated at around 1-2 per 100,000 people annually.
  • UK hospital prevalence: Salivary gland tumours represent approximately 2-3% of all head and neck cancers.
  • Worldwide prevalence: Comparable to the UK, with an annual incidence of about 0.4-2.5 cases per 100,000 globally.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Salivary Gland Tumour"][/sc]
  • Salivary gland tumours arise from the epithelial or myoepithelial cells of the glandular tissue. Benign tumours, such as pleomorphic adenoma, result from slow, localised cellular proliferation, whereas malignant tumours (e.g. adenoid cystic carcinoma) involve genetic mutations, leading to uncontrolled cellular growth and invasion into surrounding tissues.
  • Malignant tumours can spread via the lymphatic system, leading to regional lymph node involvement, or metastasise via haematogenous routes, particularly affecting the lungs.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Salivary Gland Tumour"][/sc]
  • Benign tumours: Often slow-growing over months to years and can remain asymptomatic for a long time.
  • Malignant tumours: May exhibit more rapid growth, with potential for invasion into adjacent tissues and distant metastasis over weeks to months.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Salivary Gland Tumour"][/sc]
  • Painless swelling: The most common symptom, particularly in benign tumours. This occurs due to the slow enlargement of the mass within the gland, which doesn't initially affect surrounding structures.
  • Pain: More common in malignant tumours, where invasion of nearby nerves can cause discomfort or frank pain.
  • Facial nerve weakness: Suggestive of malignant involvement of the facial nerve, particularly in parotid gland tumours.
  • Difficulty swallowing or chewing: Tumours involving the submandibular or sublingual glands can compress adjacent structures like the jaw or tongue.
  • Trismus: Difficulty opening the mouth, often due to tumour invasion into the masseter or pterygoid muscles.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Salivary Gland Tumour"][/sc]
  • Palpable mass: A firm, non-tender, and sometimes mobile mass is typically felt, especially in the parotid region.
  • Facial asymmetry: May occur with larger tumours or those affecting the facial nerve, resulting in muscle weakness.
  • Facial nerve palsy: Indicates malignancy, especially if there is loss of facial movement, drooping of the mouth, or inability to close the eye.
  • Lymphadenopathy: Suggests metastatic spread or inflammation if lymph nodes are enlarged.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Salivary Gland Tumour"][/sc]
  • Rapid growth: Suggests malignant transformation or a high-grade malignancy.
  • Facial nerve paralysis: Strong indicator of malignancy, particularly in parotid tumours, signifying local invasion.
  • Lymphadenopathy: May indicate metastatic disease, particularly in malignant salivary tumours.
  • Ulceration or fixation of the tumour: Suggests malignancy with local tissue invasion.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Salivary Gland Tumour"][/sc]
  • Small, mobile painless mass: Often seen in benign tumours like pleomorphic adenomas. These can be missed or mistaken for lymph nodes.
  • Non-specific neck discomfort: Sometimes the only presenting complaint in early-stage malignant tumours, particularly in submandibular gland involvement.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Salivary Gland Tumour"][/sc]
  • Ultrasound: First-line imaging in the UK. A well-circumscribed hypoechoic lesion is indicative of a benign tumour, while malignant tumours may appear irregular with poorly defined margins.
  • Fine Needle Aspiration Cytology (FNAC): Useful for initial diagnosis, especially in distinguishing benign from malignant tumours. FNAC showing pleomorphic adenoma suggests a benign tumour, whereas high cellularity with atypia suggests malignancy.
  • CT/MRI: Used for staging or when malignancy is suspected. Malignant tumours show invasive features or regional lymph node involvement.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Salivary Gland Tumour"][/sc]
  • A 55-year-old male presents with a 6-month history of painless swelling in the right parotid region. Recently, he has developed facial nerve weakness. Examination reveals a firm, immobile mass in the parotid region with facial asymmetry.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Salivary Gland Tumour"][/sc]
  • Lymphoma: Presents with lymphadenopathy, and systemic symptoms like fever or weight loss, unlike salivary gland tumours which are typically painless masses.
  • Sialadenitis: Typically presents with pain and signs of infection such as fever, with swelling fluctuating in size. No facial nerve involvement unless complicated.
  • Benign cyst: These are usually fluctuant, well-demarcated, and asymptomatic masses without facial nerve involvement.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Salivary Gland Tumour"][/sc]
  • Benign tumours: Surgical excision, usually superficial parotidectomy, is the mainstay treatment. Regular follow-up is necessary due to recurrence risk.
  • Malignant tumours: Treatment involves surgery with wide excision, often combined with radiotherapy. Lymph node dissection may be required if metastasis is present.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Salivary Gland Tumour"][/sc]
  • Facial nerve damage: This can occur during surgery, especially in parotidectomy, leading to facial weakness or paralysis.
  • Recurrence: Benign tumours such as pleomorphic adenoma have a 5-10% recurrence rate after surgery.
  • Metastasis: Malignant tumours, particularly adenoid cystic carcinoma, can metastasise to the lungs, leading to poor prognosis.
