Referred Neck Pain
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Referred Neck Pain"][/sc]
  • Referred neck pain refers to pain originating from another anatomical region, such as the shoulders, upper back, or even visceral organs, that is perceived in the neck. It is typically due to shared neural pathways or nerve irritation. Common sources of referred pain include cervical radiculopathy, shoulder pathologies, or visceral conditions such as myocardial infarction or gallbladder disease.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Referred Neck Pain"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Referred Neck Pain"][/sc]
  • UK GP Prevalence: Neck pain is one of the most common musculoskeletal complaints, with a lifetime prevalence of up to 70%. Referred pain contributes to a smaller but significant portion of these cases.
  • UK Hospital Prevalence: Approximately 15-20% of hospital visits for neck pain may involve referred pain from non-neck origins.
  • Worldwide Prevalence: Neck pain prevalence is similar globally, with referred pain as a less common but important differential diagnosis, particularly in settings with higher cardiovascular disease prevalence.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Referred Neck Pain"][/sc]
  • Referred neck pain occurs due to the convergence of sensory input from different tissues (somatic or visceral) in the dorsal horn of the spinal cord, which leads to misinterpretation of the pain's true source. For example, pain from myocardial ischaemia may be referred to the neck due to the overlap in nerve supply (C3-C5 dermatomes). Other common mechanisms include nerve root irritation, inflammation, or muscle tension that transmits pain signals to areas distant from the original source.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Referred Neck Pain"][/sc]
  • Acute phase: Immediate pain referral following injury, inflammation, or acute visceral events like myocardial infarction. Pain is sudden and sharp, often without trauma to the neck.
  • Subacute phase: Persistent pain over weeks to months, often following resolution of the primary cause, like a shoulder injury, with ongoing referred pain due to continued neural sensitisation.
  • Chronic phase: Referred pain persisting for more than 3 months, often associated with chronic conditions like cervical spondylosis or gallbladder disease. Pain may become less localised, and neurological symptoms like paraesthesia may emerge.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Referred Neck Pain"][/sc]
  • Neck pain without trauma - Pain felt in the neck despite the primary pathology being elsewhere, such as the shoulder or heart. This occurs due to shared nerve pathways.
  • Radiating arm or shoulder pain - Due to cervical radiculopathy, where nerve compression leads to referred pain down the arm.
  • Chest pain or discomfort - When neck pain is referred from myocardial ischaemia, it may be accompanied by chest pressure or tightness.
  • Headaches - Particularly occipital headaches can occur when upper cervical nerves (C1-C2) are involved.
  • Pain exacerbated by certain movements - Movements that compress nerve roots or put pressure on muscles, like looking upwards or sideways, may worsen the referred pain.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Referred Neck Pain"][/sc]
  • Tenderness in the neck muscles - Muscle spasm or tension may occur as a secondary response to the referred pain source, causing palpable tenderness.
  • Reduced range of motion - Limited movement in the neck, especially with flexion and extension, may suggest nerve involvement or muscular compensation.
  • Positive Spurling's test - If the pain is referred from cervical radiculopathy, this test (neck extension and lateral rotation with axial compression) may reproduce the symptoms.
  • Neurological deficits - In cervical radiculopathy, weakness, paraesthesia, or reflex changes may occur in the corresponding dermatome or myotome.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Referred Neck Pain"][/sc]
  • Chest pain with exertion - This may indicate referred pain from myocardial ischaemia, a medical emergency.
  • Severe unrelenting pain at night - May suggest a malignancy (e.g., Pancoast tumour) referring pain to the neck.
  • Neurological deficits - Progressive motor weakness or sensory loss may indicate severe cervical radiculopathy, cord compression, or spinal tumour.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Referred Neck Pain"][/sc]
  • Right-sided neck pain and shoulder pain without overt chest pain - Referred pain from a silent myocardial infarction, more common in diabetics or elderly patients, may present without typical chest symptoms.
  • Unilateral headache and neck pain - Can occur in cervical facet syndrome or C2-C3 referred pain, which may be misdiagnosed as migraine or tension-type headache.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Referred Neck Pain"][/sc]
  • Cervical spine X-ray - May show degenerative changes if cervical spondylosis is the cause of referred pain.
  • MRI of the cervical spine - Preferred to detect nerve root compression or disc herniation causing referred pain.
  • ECG and cardiac biomarkers - If cardiac referred pain is suspected, these tests can help rule out myocardial infarction.
  • Nerve conduction studies - Useful in diagnosing radiculopathy or nerve injury contributing to referred pain.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Referred Neck Pain"][/sc]
  • A 55-year-old male presents with intermittent right-sided neck pain radiating to the shoulder, associated with exertional chest discomfort. No history of neck trauma.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Referred Neck Pain"][/sc]
  • Cervical spondylosis - Differentiated by chronic neck pain, stiffness, and radiographic evidence of degenerative changes.
  • Shoulder impingement - Presents with shoulder-specific pain and limitation of movement, improving with shoulder injections.
  • Myocardial ischaemia - Typically presents with exertional chest pain, dyspnoea, and ECG changes, with neck pain as a referred symptom.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Referred Neck Pain"][/sc]
  • Physiotherapy - For musculoskeletal causes like cervical spondylosis or shoulder pathology, exercises and postural correction are key.
  • Analgesia - NSAIDs and paracetamol can help manage pain in non-cardiac referred pain cases.
  • Management of the underlying condition - For cardiac causes, angina management (e.g., nitrates, beta-blockers) or revascularisation might be required.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Referred Neck Pain"][/sc]
  • Chronic pain - May develop due to persistent nerve sensitisation or untreated underlying conditions.
  • Myelopathy - If cervical compression is not treated, it can progress to spinal cord damage.
  • Cardiac complications - Untreated myocardial ischaemia can lead to myocardial infarction or heart failure.
