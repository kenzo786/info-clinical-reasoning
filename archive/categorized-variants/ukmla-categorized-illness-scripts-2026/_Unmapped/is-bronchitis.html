Bronchitis
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Bronchitis"][/sc]
  • Bronchitis is inflammation of the bronchi in the lungs, characterised by increased mucus production and swelling of the bronchial walls. It can be classified into acute bronchitis, typically caused by viral infections, and chronic bronchitis, a form of chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD) resulting from prolonged irritation such as smoking.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Bronchitis"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Bronchitis"][/sc]
  • Acute bronchitis is common, with a prevalence of 5% in the UK GP population annually. It accounts for a significant proportion of outpatient visits worldwide.
  • Chronic bronchitis affects approximately 3% of the UK population, with higher prevalence in smokers and industrial workers.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Bronchitis"][/sc]
  • Acute bronchitis involves infection-induced inflammation of bronchial epithelial cells, leading to oedema, increased mucus production, and impaired ciliary function.
  • Chronic bronchitis is driven by chronic exposure to irritants, resulting in goblet cell hyperplasia, mucus hypersecretion, and persistent airway inflammation.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Bronchitis"][/sc]
  • Acute bronchitis typically lasts less than three weeks, with resolution of symptoms by this time.
  • Chronic bronchitis is persistent, with symptoms lasting for three months or more in two consecutive years.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Bronchitis"][/sc]
  • Cough - Often productive in chronic bronchitis and dry initially in acute bronchitis. Pathophysiology involves irritation and inflammation of the bronchial mucosa.
  • Sputum production - May be clear or purulent in acute bronchitis; typically mucoid in chronic bronchitis due to mucus hypersecretion.
  • Shortness of breath - More prominent in chronic bronchitis due to airflow obstruction and reduced gas exchange.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Bronchitis"][/sc]
  • Wheezing - Results from narrowed airways due to inflammation and mucus plugging.
  • Rhonchi - Caused by turbulent airflow through mucus-filled bronchi.
  • Reduced air entry - May be noted in chronic cases due to airway obstruction.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Bronchitis"][/sc]
  • Haemoptysis - May indicate malignancy or tuberculosis.
  • Persistent fever - Could suggest pneumonia or other complications.
  • Weight loss - Raises suspicion for underlying malignancy.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Bronchitis"][/sc]
  • Chronic bronchitis presenting with mild dyspnoea without significant cough may be missed.
  • Acute bronchitis in elderly patients may present primarily with confusion or lethargy due to systemic effects of infection.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Bronchitis"][/sc]
  • Chest X-ray - Usually normal in bronchitis but performed to rule out pneumonia (consolidation).
  • Sputum culture - May identify causative pathogens, particularly in chronic bronchitis exacerbations.
  • Spirometry - Reveals airflow limitation in chronic bronchitis.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Bronchitis"][/sc]
  • A 45-year-old male smoker presenting with a 2-week history of productive cough, low-grade fever, and wheezing, resolving spontaneously without intervention.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Bronchitis"][/sc]
  • Pneumonia - Features high fever and focal chest signs.
  • Asthma - Reversible airflow obstruction with history of episodic wheezing.
  • Chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD) - Persistent airflow limitation with significant smoking history.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Bronchitis"][/sc]
  • Acute bronchitis - Supportive care with hydration and analgesics. Antibiotics are not typically indicated.
  • Chronic bronchitis - Smoking cessation, bronchodilators, inhaled corticosteroids, and pulmonary rehabilitation.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Bronchitis"][/sc]
  • Acute bronchitis: Progression to pneumonia (rare).
  • Chronic bronchitis: Pulmonary hypertension, cor pulmonale, and recurrent respiratory infections.
