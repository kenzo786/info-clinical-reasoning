Acute Coronary Syndrome (ACS)
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Acute Coronary Syndrome (ACS)"][/sc]
  • Acute Coronary Syndrome (ACS) refers to a spectrum of conditions caused by sudden, reduced blood flow to the heart muscle. This includes unstable angina, non-ST elevation myocardial infarction (NSTEMI), and ST elevation myocardial infarction (STEMI). ACS typically results from the rupture of atherosclerotic plaques in coronary arteries, leading to partial or complete occlusion of the vessel.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Acute Coronary Syndrome (ACS)"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Acute Coronary Syndrome (ACS)"][/sc]
  • In the UK, approximately 114,000 people per year experience a heart attack (myocardial infarction), a form of ACS.
  • ACS accounts for around 5% of emergency medical admissions in the UK.
  • Globally, coronary artery disease (including ACS) is a leading cause of death, accounting for around 9 million deaths annually.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Acute Coronary Syndrome (ACS)"][/sc]
  • ACS occurs when an atherosclerotic plaque in the coronary artery ruptures, exposing its contents to circulating blood.
  • This triggers platelet activation and aggregation, leading to thrombus formation.
  • The thrombus can partially or completely obstruct the coronary artery, reducing or halting oxygen supply to the myocardium (heart muscle).
  • The resulting ischaemia leads to myocardial injury or necrosis, depending on the duration and extent of the obstruction.
  • Unstable angina involves ischaemia without myocardial necrosis, while NSTEMI and STEMI involve necrosis (with STEMI being more severe due to complete occlusion).
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Acute Coronary Syndrome (ACS)"][/sc]
  • Onset: Symptoms can develop suddenly over minutes to hours.
  • Initial phase: Myocardial ischaemia can cause chest pain and other symptoms immediately.
  • Progression: Without intervention, ischaemia can lead to myocardial infarction and necrosis, occurring within 30 minutes to several hours after onset.
  • Recovery phase: If treated, the myocardial tissue may recover, but necrosis leads to permanent damage and scarring.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Acute Coronary Syndrome (ACS)"][/sc]
  • Chest pain - The most common symptom, described as a central, crushing, or squeezing pain. This occurs due to myocardial ischaemia and hypoxia.
  • Radiation to the left arm, jaw, or neck - Referred pain from the heart is transmitted via nerve pathways, leading to discomfort in these areas.
  • Dyspnoea - Shortness of breath due to impaired myocardial function and pulmonary congestion.
  • Nausea and vomiting - These can be due to vagal stimulation during acute ischaemia.
  • Diaphoresis - Sweating is triggered by sympathetic nervous system activation in response to pain and stress.
  • Fatigue - Generalised weakness due to reduced cardiac output and perfusion.
  • Asymptomatic - Atypical silent myocardial infarctions can occur, especially in elderly and diabetic patients.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Acute Coronary Syndrome (ACS)"][/sc]
  • Tachycardia - A rapid heart rate results from sympathetic activation in response to pain and hypoxia.
  • Hypotension - Low blood pressure can result from reduced myocardial function or cardiogenic shock.
  • New heart murmur - A mitral regurgitation murmur may develop if papillary muscles are affected by ischaemia.
  • Cold, clammy skin - Sympathetic activation causes vasoconstriction, leading to cold, sweaty skin.
  • Crackles on lung auscultation - Pulmonary oedema can result from left ventricular dysfunction.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Acute Coronary Syndrome (ACS)"][/sc]
  • Severe, unrelenting chest pain - May indicate ongoing ischaemia or infarction, requiring urgent intervention.
  • Haemodynamic instability (e.g., hypotension) - Could signify cardiogenic shock, a life-threatening complication.
  • Syncope or loss of consciousness - Suggests a critical reduction in cardiac output, possibly due to arrhythmia or heart failure.
  • Sudden shortness of breath with crackles - May indicate acute pulmonary oedema secondary to heart failure.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Acute Coronary Syndrome (ACS)"][/sc]
  • Epigastric pain - Rather than chest pain, some patients, especially the elderly or those with diabetes, may present with indigestion-like discomfort.
  • Silent myocardial infarction - Asymptomatic or with minimal symptoms such as fatigue or dyspnoea, often seen in elderly patients.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Acute Coronary Syndrome (ACS)"][/sc]
  • ECG - ST elevation (STEMI) or ST depression/T-wave inversion (NSTEMI/unstable angina) indicates myocardial ischaemia. Occurs due to depolarisation abnormalities in the ischaemic myocardium.
  • Troponin I/T - Elevated troponin levels indicate myocardial injury or necrosis in NSTEMI and STEMI.
  • Coronary angiography - Shows areas of coronary artery occlusion, used to confirm the diagnosis and guide treatment.
  • Chest X-ray - Can identify pulmonary oedema or other causes of chest pain.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Acute Coronary Syndrome (ACS)"][/sc]
  • A 65-year-old male presents with sudden-onset, crushing central chest pain radiating to the left arm, associated with dyspnoea and diaphoresis, lasting 30 minutes. ECG shows ST elevation.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Acute Coronary Syndrome (ACS)"][/sc]
  • Pulmonary Embolism - Often presents with pleuritic chest pain and dyspnoea rather than the crushing pain of ACS. ECG shows sinus tachycardia or right heart strain.
  • Aortic Dissection - Sudden, tearing chest pain radiating to the back. Blood pressure discrepancy between arms, widened mediastinum on chest X-ray.
  • Pericarditis - Sharp chest pain relieved by sitting forward. Diffuse ST elevation without reciprocal changes on ECG.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Acute Coronary Syndrome (ACS)"][/sc]
  • Initial management - Aspirin, nitrates, oxygen (if hypoxic), and morphine for pain relief. Dual antiplatelet therapy (e.g., aspirin + clopidogrel) is standard.
  • Reperfusion therapy - For STEMI, immediate reperfusion with primary percutaneous coronary intervention (PCI) or fibrinolysis is indicated.
  • Beta-blockers and ACE inhibitors - Started in stable patients to reduce myocardial oxygen demand and prevent further events.
  • Long-term management - Statins, lifestyle modification (e.g., smoking cessation, dietary changes), and cardiac rehabilitation.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Acute Coronary Syndrome (ACS)"][/sc]
  • Heart failure - Resulting from significant myocardial necrosis reducing left ventricular function.
  • Arrhythmias - Including ventricular tachycardia and fibrillation, which can be life-threatening.
  • Cardiogenic shock - Severe reduction in cardiac output due to extensive myocardial damage.
  • Myocardial rupture - Rare but fatal, occurring when necrotic myocardial tissue tears.
