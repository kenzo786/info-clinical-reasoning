Reiter's Syndrome (Reactive Arthritis)
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Reiter's Syndrome (Reactive Arthritis)"][/sc]
  • Reiter's Syndrome, or Reactive Arthritis, is a type of seronegative spondyloarthropathy that typically follows infection (often urogenital or gastrointestinal) and is characterised by the classic triad of arthritis, urethritis, and conjunctivitis. It is an autoimmune response, triggered by infection, but not caused by direct infection of the joint.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Reiter's Syndrome (Reactive Arthritis)"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Reiter's Syndrome (Reactive Arthritis)"][/sc]
  • Prevalence in the UK GP population: Approximately 30 cases per 100,000 population.
  • Prevalence in UK hospitals: Hospitalised patients with prior infection may present with reactive arthritis at higher rates, though data is variable.
  • Worldwide prevalence: More common in younger adults, especially males aged 20-40, and in populations with the HLA-B27 gene.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Reiter's Syndrome (Reactive Arthritis)"][/sc]
  • Following a gastrointestinal or urogenital infection, an immune-mediated response occurs, resulting in inflammation in joints, eyes, and the urinary tract.
  • The condition is strongly associated with the HLA-B27 gene, which contributes to an abnormal immune response, causing joint inflammation and other features of reactive arthritis.
  • Molecular mimicry may play a role, where immune cells targeting the initial infection cross-react with host cells, causing autoimmune inflammation.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Reiter's Syndrome (Reactive Arthritis)"][/sc]
  • Acute phase: Symptoms typically arise 1-4 weeks post-infection, with joint pain, eye involvement, and urinary symptoms developing over days to weeks.
  • Subacute phase: Symptoms can persist for several weeks or months, with waxing and waning patterns common.
  • Chronic phase: A subset may develop chronic arthritis, lasting beyond 6 months, particularly in those positive for HLA-B27.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Reiter's Syndrome (Reactive Arthritis)"][/sc]
  • Arthritis - Pain and swelling in large joints, especially the knees and ankles; caused by autoimmune inflammation targeting synovial tissue.
  • Urethritis - Dysuria and urethral discharge are common; inflammation is often due to prior urogenital infection.
  • Conjunctivitis - Eye redness, irritation, or discharge; inflammatory cells target ocular mucosa in response to infection-triggered immune response.
  • Enthesitis - Pain and inflammation at tendon or ligament insertions, e.g., Achilles tendon, related to autoimmune inflammation.
  • Skin lesions - Keratoderma blennorrhagica, a rash on palms and soles, due to abnormal keratinocyte response in HLA-B27 individuals.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Reiter's Syndrome (Reactive Arthritis)"][/sc]
  • Joint swelling and warmth - Synovial inflammation leads to increased blood flow and joint effusion.
  • Conjunctival injection - Redness in the eyes due to conjunctivitis caused by inflammatory cell infiltration.
  • Plantar fasciitis - Tenderness at the heel due to entheseal involvement in inflammation.
  • Skin lesions - Vesicular or scaly lesions (keratoderma blennorrhagica) on palms and soles, associated with abnormal immune response.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Reiter's Syndrome (Reactive Arthritis)"][/sc]
  • Severe eye pain or vision changes - May indicate uveitis, a complication requiring urgent ophthalmologic evaluation to prevent vision loss.
  • Persistent cardiac symptoms - Reactive arthritis may cause aortitis or valvular disease; monitor for potential cardiovascular complications.
  • Systemic symptoms - Fever, weight loss, or malaise could signify systemic inflammation or complications, such as spondylitis.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Reiter's Syndrome (Reactive Arthritis)"][/sc]
  • Isolated arthritis - Some may present only with joint pain without the classic triad, especially in chronic or recurrent cases.
  • Isolated eye involvement - Conjunctivitis or uveitis may be the sole manifestation, especially in early or mild cases.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Reiter's Syndrome (Reactive Arthritis)"][/sc]
  • HLA-B27 testing - Positive in up to 80% of cases; a genetic marker associated with reactive arthritis.
  • ESR and CRP - Elevated in active disease due to systemic inflammation.
  • Joint aspiration - Typically reveals sterile synovial fluid with increased white cells, indicating inflammatory arthritis without infection.
  • Urine and stool cultures - May identify the initial infective organism, though cultures are often negative by the time arthritis develops.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Reiter's Syndrome (Reactive Arthritis)"][/sc]
  • A 28-year-old male presents with knee pain, urethral discharge, and red eyes following a recent episode of gastroenteritis. Symptoms have persisted for two weeks and are worsening, with mild fever.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Reiter's Syndrome (Reactive Arthritis)"][/sc]
  • Septic arthritis - Differentiated by joint aspiration revealing infection and often absence of urethritis and conjunctivitis.
  • Ankylosing spondylitis - Chronic back pain and limited spinal mobility; often lacks urogenital and ocular symptoms.
  • Psoriatic arthritis - May involve skin and nails but typically lacks conjunctivitis and prior infectious trigger.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Reiter's Syndrome (Reactive Arthritis)"][/sc]
  • NSAIDs - First-line for pain and inflammation, e.g., ibuprofen 400-600 mg TID.
  • Corticosteroids - Intra-articular steroids for severe joint involvement; oral corticosteroids for persistent inflammation.
  • DMARDs - For chronic cases, methotrexate may be considered if NSAIDs are inadequate.
  • Antibiotics - Reserved for ongoing or recurrent infections but not for arthritis directly.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Reiter's Syndrome (Reactive Arthritis)"][/sc]
  • Chronic arthritis - Occurs in 15-20% of cases, especially in HLA-B27 positive individuals, causing ongoing joint pain and potential disability.
  • Ocular complications - Uveitis can lead to vision impairment if untreated.
  • Cardiac involvement - Aortitis or heart valve disease is rare but serious, requiring long-term monitoring.
