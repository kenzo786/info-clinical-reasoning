Shoulder Pain
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Shoulder Pain"][/sc]
  • Shoulder pain refers to discomfort or aching in the shoulder joint or surrounding structures, including muscles, tendons, ligaments, and bursae. It is often classified as acute (12 weeks).
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Shoulder Pain"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Shoulder Pain"][/sc]
  • Prevalence in UK GP population: Approximately 1-2% of consultations annually.
  • UK hospital prevalence: Common in musculoskeletal outpatient clinics, accounting for up to 16% of patients referred for shoulder complaints.
  • Worldwide prevalence: Lifetime prevalence is estimated at 7-34%, with higher incidence in manual labourers and older adults.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Shoulder Pain"][/sc]
  • Shoulder pain arises from local inflammation, mechanical stress, degenerative changes, or referred pain from other structures (e.g., the neck).
  • Common mechanisms include rotator cuff tendinopathy (overuse leading to microtrauma and inflammation), impingement syndrome (compression of tendons in the subacromial space), and adhesive capsulitis (fibrotic changes in the joint capsule).
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Shoulder Pain"][/sc]
  • Acute phase: Sudden onset of pain, often following trauma or overuse.
  • Subacute phase: Gradual reduction in acute inflammation but persistent discomfort or stiffness.
  • Chronic phase: Lasting pain and functional limitation, potentially accompanied by compensatory biomechanical changes.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Shoulder Pain"][/sc]
  • Pain with movement - Indicates inflammation or mechanical impingement; commonly due to rotator cuff injuries.
  • Stiffness - Often linked to adhesive capsulitis, caused by fibrosis limiting range of motion.
  • Night pain - Suggests conditions like rotator cuff tears or bursitis due to positional exacerbation.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Shoulder Pain"][/sc]
  • Reduced range of motion - Indicates structural abnormalities such as adhesive capsulitis or impingement.
  • Tenderness on palpation - Suggests localised inflammation or trauma, e.g., in rotator cuff tendinopathy.
  • <b+Positive impingement tests - Tests like Neer’s or Hawkins-Kennedy confirm subacromial impingement syndrome.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Shoulder Pain"][/sc]
  • Severe pain at rest - May indicate infection or malignancy.
  • Neurological deficits - Suggests nerve root compression or brachial plexopathy.
  • Systemic symptoms (fever, weight loss) - May indicate septic arthritis or metastatic disease.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Shoulder Pain"][/sc]
  • Referred pain from cervical spine disorders or myocardial ischaemia may present as isolated shoulder pain without clear musculoskeletal findings.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Shoulder Pain"][/sc]
  • X-ray - May show osteoarthritis, fractures, or calcific tendinopathy.
  • Ultrasound - Useful for detecting rotator cuff tears or bursitis.
  • MRI - Gold standard for soft tissue injuries and labral tears.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Shoulder Pain"][/sc]
  • A 52-year-old manual labourer presents with progressive right shoulder pain for three months, exacerbated by overhead activities, with night pain and reduced range of motion.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Shoulder Pain"][/sc]
  • Rotator cuff tendinopathy - Pain localised to lateral shoulder, aggravated by lifting.
  • Adhesive capsulitis - Global stiffness, gradual onset.
  • Glenohumeral arthritis - Crepitus, constant deep pain.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Shoulder Pain"][/sc]
  • Conservative management - Includes NSAIDs, physiotherapy, and activity modification.
  • Invasive management - Steroid injections for severe cases; surgery (e.g., rotator cuff repair) for refractory conditions.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Shoulder Pain"][/sc]
  • Chronic pain and functional limitation.
  • Secondary osteoarthritis due to altered biomechanics.
