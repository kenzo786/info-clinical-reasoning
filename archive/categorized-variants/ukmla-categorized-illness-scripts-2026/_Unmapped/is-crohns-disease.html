Crohn's Disease
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Crohn's Disease"][/sc]
  • Crohn's disease is a chronic, relapsing inflammatory bowel disease (IBD) that can affect any part of the gastrointestinal tract, from mouth to anus. It is characterised by transmural inflammation, leading to complications such as strictures, fistulas, and abscesses. Unlike ulcerative colitis, which is limited to the colon, Crohn’s can affect multiple discontinuous areas (skip lesions).
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Crohn's Disease"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Crohn's Disease"][/sc]
  • In the UK, the prevalence of Crohn’s disease is estimated at 157 per 100,000 population in general practice settings.
  • Hospital admissions for Crohn's are around 30 per 100,000 annually in the UK.
  • Worldwide, the prevalence varies, with higher rates in Europe and North America, approximately 100-300 per 100,000.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Crohn's Disease"][/sc]
  • Crohn’s disease arises from a complex interaction between genetic predisposition, environmental triggers, and an abnormal immune response. A dysregulated immune system in the gut leads to inflammation, predominantly affecting the terminal ileum but potentially involving any part of the GI tract.
  • The transmural nature of inflammation can lead to complications such as strictures (due to fibrosis), fistulas (due to penetrating inflammation), and malabsorption (from mucosal damage).
  • Involvement of mesenteric lymph nodes and granulomas are hallmark features, contributing to the chronicity of inflammation and fibrosis.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Crohn's Disease"][/sc]
  • Crohn’s disease typically has an insidious onset, with symptoms developing gradually over weeks to months.
  • It follows a relapsing-remitting course, with periods of flare-ups and remission. During a flare, inflammation can worsen leading to severe symptoms and complications.
  • Progression of disease can lead to chronic complications such as strictures, fistulas, and intestinal obstruction.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Crohn's Disease"][/sc]
  • Diarrhoea - Chronic, often non-bloody, diarrhoea occurs due to inflammation and malabsorption in the small intestine.
  • Abdominal pain - Typically cramping, located in the right lower quadrant, results from inflammation, strictures, or bowel obstruction.
  • Weight loss - Caused by malabsorption of nutrients, reduced appetite, and increased metabolic demand due to chronic inflammation.
  • Fatigue - A common non-specific symptom linked to chronic inflammation and anaemia.
  • Fever - Reflects active inflammation and may be associated with abscess formation.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Crohn's Disease"][/sc]
  • Abdominal tenderness - Most commonly in the right lower quadrant, due to ileocaecal involvement.
  • Pallor - Reflects anaemia, which can be due to chronic inflammation or malabsorption of iron and vitamin B12.
  • Perianal disease - Skin tags, fistulas, and abscesses may occur due to the transmural inflammation.
  • Oral ulcers - A less common manifestation reflecting gastrointestinal tract involvement extending to the mouth.
  • Weight loss - Clinically noticeable in severe or chronic cases due to malabsorption and nutritional deficiencies.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Crohn's Disease"][/sc]
  • Severe abdominal pain and vomiting - May indicate bowel obstruction due to strictures.
  • Unintentional weight loss - Can signify malabsorption and a more aggressive disease course.
  • Perianal fistulas - Reflects advanced disease and may lead to perirectal abscesses.
  • Severe bleeding - Suggests complications such as intestinal perforation or severe inflammation.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Crohn's Disease"][/sc]
  • Isolated perianal disease - In some patients, Crohn's can present solely with perianal disease without significant GI symptoms.
  • Extraintestinal manifestations - Arthritis, uveitis, and erythema nodosum may be the first signs before gastrointestinal symptoms appear.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Crohn's Disease"][/sc]
  • Colonoscopy with biopsy - The gold standard for diagnosis, typically revealing skip lesions, cobblestone mucosa, and granulomas.
  • Faecal calprotectin - Elevated levels indicate intestinal inflammation and are used for monitoring disease activity.
  • CRP and ESR - Markers of systemic inflammation that are commonly elevated during flares.
  • Small bowel imaging (MR enterography) - Useful for assessing small bowel involvement, strictures, and fistulas.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Crohn's Disease"][/sc]
  • A 25-year-old woman presents with 6 months of diarrhoea, abdominal pain, weight loss, and fatigue. She also reports occasional episodes of fever. On examination, there is tenderness in the right lower quadrant and perianal skin tags.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Crohn's Disease"][/sc]
  • Ulcerative colitis - Differs as it is limited to the colon, presents with bloody diarrhoea, and lacks the transmural involvement of Crohn’s.
  • Infectious colitis - Acute onset of diarrhoea and fever, often resolving with treatment, unlike the chronic course of Crohn’s.
  • Coeliac disease - Presents with malabsorption but lacks the inflammatory findings on endoscopy typical of Crohn's.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Crohn's Disease"][/sc]
  • Induction of remission - Corticosteroids are commonly used to induce remission during flares.
  • Maintenance therapy - Immunosuppressants like azathioprine or biologics (e.g. infliximab) are used to maintain remission.
  • Nutritional support - Patients with malabsorption may require dietary modification, enteral feeding, or supplementation.
  • Surgical management - Reserved for complications like strictures, fistulas, or failure of medical therapy.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Crohn's Disease"][/sc]
  • Bowel obstruction - Caused by strictures from chronic inflammation.
  • Fistulas and abscesses - Due to transmural inflammation, commonly in the perianal region.
  • Malnutrition - From chronic malabsorption of nutrients, particularly in extensive small bowel involvement.
  • Colorectal cancer - Increased risk due to chronic inflammation, particularly if colonic involvement is present.
