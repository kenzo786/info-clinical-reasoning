Torsades de Pointes
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Torsades de Pointes"][/sc]
  • Torsades de Pointes (TdP) is a specific type of polymorphic ventricular tachycardia characterised by rapid, irregular QRS complexes that appear to twist around the isoelectric line on the ECG. It is typically associated with prolonged QT intervals and can lead to sudden cardiac death if not treated promptly. The condition often arises in the context of electrolyte disturbances, drug-induced QT prolongation, or congenital long QT syndromes.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Torsades de Pointes"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Torsades de Pointes"][/sc]
  • UK GP population prevalence: Rare, with most cases identified in hospital settings following drug exposure or electrolyte imbalance.
  • UK hospital prevalence: More common in critical care settings, especially among patients receiving medications that prolong the QT interval or those with electrolyte imbalances. Prevalence varies but is estimated around 2-5% of patients with prolonged QT.
  • Worldwide prevalence: Globally, TdP remains rare, though more prevalent in populations with a higher burden of cardiovascular disease and in those exposed to QT-prolonging drugs.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Torsades de Pointes"][/sc]
  • TdP occurs due to prolonged repolarisation of the myocardium, reflected as QT interval prolongation on an ECG. This prolonged repolarisation creates an environment where early afterdepolarisations (EADs) can trigger abnormal electrical activity. The twisting morphology of the QRS complexes arises from this chaotic electrical activity, which can degenerate into ventricular fibrillation, leading to sudden cardiac arrest.
  • Key factors contributing to TdP include:
    • Prolonged QT interval (due to medications, electrolyte disturbances, or genetic mutations)
    • Hypokalaemia, hypomagnesaemia
    • Bradycardia, which further prolongs the QT interval
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Torsades de Pointes"][/sc]
  • Acute onset: TdP can occur abruptly, particularly in the setting of acute QT prolongation due to drug administration or electrolyte imbalance.
  • Self-limiting or degenerative: TdP episodes may spontaneously resolve but can also degenerate into ventricular fibrillation, resulting in sudden cardiac arrest if not promptly treated.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Torsades de Pointes"][/sc]
  • Palpitations - Sudden awareness of an abnormal, rapid heartbeat; caused by chaotic electrical activity within the ventricles.
  • Dizziness - Transient loss of perfusion to the brain due to decreased cardiac output during episodes of rapid ventricular tachycardia.
  • Syncope - A temporary loss of consciousness resulting from inadequate cerebral perfusion during prolonged tachycardia episodes.
  • Shortness of breath - Caused by reduced cardiac output, leading to inadequate oxygen delivery to tissues.
  • Chest pain - May occur due to myocardial ischaemia from decreased coronary perfusion during episodes of rapid tachycardia.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Torsades de Pointes"][/sc]
  • ECG: Polymorphic ventricular tachycardia - Characteristic twisting morphology of the QRS complexes.
  • Prolonged QT interval - Seen on prior ECGs, indicating delayed ventricular repolarisation.
  • Hypotension - Resulting from the reduced cardiac output during periods of rapid ventricular contraction.
  • Pallor - Due to poor peripheral perfusion during prolonged tachycardia episodes.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Torsades de Pointes"][/sc]
  • Recurrent syncope - Suggests sustained or frequent TdP episodes, indicating the potential for progression to sudden cardiac death.
  • Severe bradycardia - Exacerbates QT prolongation and increases the risk of TdP onset.
  • Sudden cardiac arrest - Represents the end-stage complication of untreated or severe TdP, where the tachyarrhythmia degenerates into ventricular fibrillation.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Torsades de Pointes"][/sc]
  • Asymptomatic Prolonged QT Interval - Some patients may have a prolonged QT interval without symptoms, especially if on chronic medications or with mild electrolyte disturbances. This puts them at higher risk of sudden, unexpected TdP episodes.
  • Isolated Palpitations - TdP can occasionally present only with palpitations, without syncope or dizziness, which can delay recognition and treatment.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Torsades de Pointes"][/sc]
  • ECG - Shows the classic polymorphic ventricular tachycardia with a twisting QRS complex. A prolonged QT interval (>450 ms in men, >460 ms in women) is commonly observed on prior ECGs.
  • Serum electrolytes - Low potassium (<3.5 mmol/L) and magnesium (<0.7 mmol/L) are common precipitating factors for TdP.
  • Holter monitoring - May be used for patients with recurrent or intermittent symptoms to capture transient episodes of QT prolongation or arrhythmias.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Torsades de Pointes"][/sc]
  • A 55-year-old female presents with sudden episodes of palpitations, dizziness, and two syncopal episodes over the past 24 hours. ECG shows a prolonged QT interval and polymorphic ventricular tachycardia consistent with Torsades de Pointes.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Torsades de Pointes"][/sc]
  • Ventricular Tachycardia (VT) - Monomorphic VT does not exhibit the twisting QRS morphology seen in TdP and typically occurs without QT prolongation.
  • Atrial Fibrillation - While irregular, atrial fibrillation has narrow QRS complexes unless there is underlying bundle branch block.
  • Brugada Syndrome - Another cause of polymorphic VT, but distinguished by characteristic ECG findings (coved ST-segment elevation in leads V1-V3) without QT prolongation.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Torsades de Pointes"][/sc]
  • Immediate management:
    • Intravenous magnesium sulphate (2 g IV bolus), regardless of serum magnesium levels, as it stabilises the cardiac membrane and terminates TdP.
    • Correction of electrolyte imbalances (especially potassium and magnesium) to normal or slightly elevated levels.
    • Temporary transvenous pacing or isoprenaline infusion for bradycardia-induced TdP.
    • Cardioversion or defibrillation in haemodynamically unstable patients or when TdP degenerates into ventricular fibrillation.
  • Long-term management:
    • Avoidance of QT-prolonging medications.
    • Beta-blockers for patients with congenital long QT syndrome to reduce adrenergic triggers for TdP.
    • Implantable cardioverter-defibrillator (ICD) for patients with recurrent episodes or high-risk congenital syndromes.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Torsades de Pointes"][/sc]
  • Sudden Cardiac Death - Due to the progression of TdP to ventricular fibrillation, a life-threatening complication.
  • Recurrent Syncope - Can lead to trauma or injury, especially if occurring without warning.
  • Heart Failure - Chronic TdP episodes or sustained ventricular tachyarrhythmias can reduce cardiac function over time, leading to heart failure.
