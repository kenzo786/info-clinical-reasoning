Vestibular Migraine
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Vestibular Migraine"][/sc]
  • Vestibular migraine is a neurological disorder characterised by episodic vertigo in individuals with a history of migraine. It involves recurrent attacks of dizziness or vertigo, often accompanied by migraine headache symptoms (such as photophobia, phonophobia, or nausea). Unlike typical migraine, the primary symptom is vertigo or imbalance, rather than headache.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Vestibular Migraine"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Vestibular Migraine"][/sc]
  • UK GP population: Estimated prevalence is about 1% of the general population, but higher in patients with migraines (about 10% of those with migraines).
  • UK hospital prevalence: Vestibular migraine accounts for 7-9% of patients presenting with vertigo in specialist clinics.
  • Worldwide prevalence: Globally, vestibular migraine is thought to affect 1-3% of the population, with a higher prevalence in females.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Vestibular Migraine"][/sc]
  • Vestibular migraine is believed to result from dysfunction in the brainstem and the vestibular pathways. Migraines are associated with altered sensory processing, and vestibular structures are particularly vulnerable to these disturbances. The trigeminal-vestibular pathways may be involved, leading to abnormal vestibular signals and triggering vertigo. Central sensitisation, cortical spreading depression, and neuroinflammatory changes contribute to the condition’s symptoms.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Vestibular Migraine"][/sc]
  • Prodrome: Symptoms such as mood changes, fatigue, or neck stiffness may occur hours to days before an episode.
  • Attack phase: Vertigo or dizziness lasting from minutes to hours, often accompanied by other migraine symptoms such as light and sound sensitivity.
  • Postdrome: After the episode, patients may experience fatigue, difficulty concentrating, or residual dizziness for hours to days.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Vestibular Migraine"][/sc]
  • Vertigo (episodic) - The most common symptom, caused by disturbances in the brainstem and vestibular nuclei, leading to an abnormal perception of movement.
  • Nausea - Often accompanying vertigo, likely due to autonomic dysfunction affecting the gastrointestinal system.
  • Photophobia and phonophobia - These symptoms mirror those of typical migraines, resulting from cortical hyperexcitability.
  • Headache - Migraine headache may or may not accompany the vertigo, caused by neuroinflammation and abnormal pain processing.
  • Visual aura - Some patients may experience visual disturbances, such as flashing lights or zigzag patterns, due to cortical spreading depression.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Vestibular Migraine"][/sc]
  • Gait instability - Patients may show unsteady walking during an attack due to altered vestibular input.
  • Nystagmus - Rapid, involuntary eye movements may occur due to vestibular dysfunction.
  • Normal neurological examination between episodes - Unlike other vestibular disorders, patients often have no abnormal signs between attacks, suggesting a paroxysmal disorder.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Vestibular Migraine"][/sc]
  • Sudden onset, continuous vertigo lasting days - This may indicate vestibular neuritis or a cerebrovascular event rather than vestibular migraine.
  • Severe unilateral headache with neurological deficits - This could signify a stroke or intracranial haemorrhage, particularly if associated with hemiparesis or dysphasia.
  • Persistent hearing loss or tinnitus - Suggests Ménière’s disease or acoustic neuroma, requiring further investigation.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Vestibular Migraine"][/sc]
  • Some patients may experience isolated vertigo without typical migraine features (headache, aura). In these cases, diagnosis may be delayed, especially if the patient has no prior history of migraine.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Vestibular Migraine"][/sc]
  • Clinical diagnosis - Based on the International Classification of Headache Disorders (ICHD-3) criteria, which include recurrent episodes of vertigo associated with migraine symptoms.
  • Vestibular testing - Electronystagmography (ENG) or video head impulse test (vHIT) may show normal findings, as vestibular migraine often has no permanent vestibular damage.
  • Brain MRI - Performed to rule out other causes of vertigo, especially if neurological deficits are present. Imaging is typically normal in vestibular migraine.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Vestibular Migraine"][/sc]
  • A 35-year-old female with a history of migraines presents with recurrent episodes of spinning vertigo lasting several hours, associated with nausea and photophobia but without a significant headache. Neurological examination is normal between episodes.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Vestibular Migraine"][/sc]
  • Ménière’s disease - Associated with hearing loss and tinnitus, which are absent in vestibular migraine.
  • Benign paroxysmal positional vertigo (BPPV) - Shorter episodes of vertigo triggered by head movement, usually lasting seconds to minutes.
  • Vestibular neuritis - Continuous vertigo lasting days, without the episodic nature or migraine features of vestibular migraine.
  • Transient ischaemic attack (TIA) - Sudden onset of neurological symptoms, often with focal deficits like weakness or speech disturbances, distinguishing it from vestibular migraine.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Vestibular Migraine"][/sc]
  • Acute management - Vestibular suppressants (e.g., prochlorperazine or cinnarizine) for acute vertigo. Triptans may be used if migraine headache accompanies the vertigo.
  • Preventative treatment - Beta-blockers (propranolol), calcium channel blockers (flunarizine), or anti-epileptics (topiramate) are used for prophylaxis. Lifestyle changes, including avoiding migraine triggers, are essential.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Vestibular Migraine"][/sc]
  • Chronic vestibular dysfunction - Repeated episodes may lead to long-term balance problems and difficulty with daily activities.
  • Anxiety and depression - Frequent, unpredictable episodes of vertigo can cause significant psychological distress.
  • Medication overuse headache - Overuse of acute medications like triptans can lead to rebound headaches and chronic migraine.
