Hand, Foot, and Mouth Disease (HFMD)
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Hand, Foot, and Mouth Disease (HFMD)"][/sc]
  • Hand, Foot, and Mouth Disease (HFMD) is a common viral illness primarily affecting children under the age of 10, characterised by fever, mouth ulcers, and a rash on the hands, feet, and sometimes buttocks. It is most frequently caused by coxsackievirus A16 and enterovirus 71, with transmission occurring via direct contact with bodily fluids, respiratory droplets, or contaminated surfaces.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Hand, Foot, and Mouth Disease (HFMD)"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Hand, Foot, and Mouth Disease (HFMD)"][/sc]
  • UK GP population: HFMD is a common paediatric illness with seasonal outbreaks, particularly in late summer and early autumn. It is most common in children under 5.
  • UK hospital prevalence: Most cases are self-limiting, but hospitalisation may occur in rare cases due to complications such as dehydration or encephalitis, particularly with enterovirus 71.
  • Worldwide prevalence: HFMD is endemic in many countries, particularly in the Asia-Pacific region, where large outbreaks occur.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Hand, Foot, and Mouth Disease (HFMD)"][/sc]
  • HFMD is caused by enteroviruses, primarily coxsackievirus A16 and enterovirus 71. The virus enters through the oropharyngeal mucosa, infecting epithelial cells and lymphoid tissues. It then disseminates via the bloodstream (viraemia) to various sites, including the skin and oral mucosa, leading to the characteristic vesicular rash and ulcers. In some cases, particularly with enterovirus 71, the virus can invade the central nervous system, leading to severe neurological complications.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Hand, Foot, and Mouth Disease (HFMD)"][/sc]
  • Incubation period: 3-6 days after exposure to the virus.
  • Prodromal phase: Mild fever, malaise, and sore throat lasting 1-2 days.
  • Rash and ulcer phase: 3-7 days with vesicular rash on hands, feet, and buttocks, and oral ulcers.
  • Recovery phase: Symptoms resolve in 7-10 days with supportive care, although peeling of the skin on hands and feet may occur during healing.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Hand, Foot, and Mouth Disease (HFMD)"][/sc]
  • Fever - Often the first symptom, linked to the systemic immune response to viral infection.
  • Mouth sores - Painful ulcers on the oral mucosa caused by viral invasion and localised destruction of epithelial cells.
  • Vesicular rash - Appears on the hands, feet, and sometimes buttocks due to viral replication in epithelial cells, forming vesicles filled with fluid.
  • Sore throat - Linked to inflammation of the oropharyngeal mucosa caused by viral replication.
  • Irritability and fatigue - Resulting from systemic inflammation and the body's response to the viral infection.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Hand, Foot, and Mouth Disease (HFMD)"][/sc]
  • Vesicular rash - Small fluid-filled vesicles typically found on hands, feet, and buttocks, related to viral replication in skin epithelial cells.
  • Oral ulcers - Painful sores on the tongue, gums, and inner cheeks, resulting from localised epithelial destruction by the virus.
  • Low-grade fever - Often a mild fever (up to 38°C), indicating the systemic response to the viral infection.
  • Dehydration - Due to painful oral ulcers causing difficulty in eating and drinking.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Hand, Foot, and Mouth Disease (HFMD)"][/sc]
  • Neurological signs (e.g., seizures, altered consciousness) - May indicate encephalitis, particularly with enterovirus 71 infection. This results from viral invasion of the central nervous system.
  • Severe dehydration - Due to poor oral intake from painful ulcers, this may lead to hospitalisation for intravenous fluids.
  • Persistent high fever (>39°C for more than 3 days) - Could signify a secondary bacterial infection or more severe viral involvement.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Hand, Foot, and Mouth Disease (HFMD)"][/sc]
  • Adults with mild or asymptomatic infection - In some adults, HFMD may present with few or no symptoms, which can delay diagnosis and increase the risk of spreading the virus.
  • Older children with severe systemic illness - Enterovirus 71 infections in older children may present with neurological symptoms or severe systemic involvement, which are not typical in younger children.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Hand, Foot, and Mouth Disease (HFMD)"][/sc]
  • Clinical diagnosis - Based on characteristic symptoms (rash and oral ulcers) and patient history. No routine tests are necessary.
  • Viral PCR from throat swab, vesicle fluid, or stool - Used in severe or atypical cases to identify the viral strain (especially enterovirus 71) to guide prognosis and management.
  • Full blood count (FBC) - May show mild lymphocytosis, but generally non-specific.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Hand, Foot, and Mouth Disease (HFMD)"][/sc]
  • A 3-year-old male presents with a 2-day history of fever, painful oral ulcers, and a vesicular rash on his hands, feet, and buttocks. He has reduced oral intake due to mouth pain and mild irritability.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Hand, Foot, and Mouth Disease (HFMD)"][/sc]
  • Varicella (chickenpox) - Vesicular rash involves the trunk and face rather than hands and feet, and lesions are more widespread and in various stages of healing.
  • Herpes simplex virus (HSV) stomatitis - Presents with painful oral ulcers but typically without the vesicular rash on the extremities.
  • Kawasaki disease - Features include fever lasting more than 5 days, conjunctivitis, and mucosal changes, but lacks vesicular rash on hands and feet.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Hand, Foot, and Mouth Disease (HFMD)"][/sc]
  • Supportive care - Encourage fluid intake to prevent dehydration. Analgesia (paracetamol or ibuprofen) to manage fever and pain.
  • Hospital admission - In cases of severe dehydration or neurological involvement (e.g., encephalitis), intravenous fluids or other supportive measures are needed.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Hand, Foot, and Mouth Disease (HFMD)"][/sc]
  • Dehydration - Common in children with severe oral ulcers, requiring hospitalisation for IV fluids.
  • Encephalitis - Particularly associated with enterovirus 71, this can lead to long-term neurological deficits or death if untreated.
  • Nail shedding (onychomadesis) - Temporary nail loss may occur a few weeks after recovery, but typically resolves without intervention.
