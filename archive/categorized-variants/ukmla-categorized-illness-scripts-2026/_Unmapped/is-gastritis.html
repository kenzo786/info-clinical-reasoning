Gastritis
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Gastritis"][/sc]
  • Gastritis is the inflammation of the gastric mucosa, often caused by infections (e.g., Helicobacter pylori), alcohol, NSAID use, or stress. It can be acute or chronic, resulting in varying degrees of gastric lining erosion and, potentially, ulcer formation.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Gastritis"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Gastritis"][/sc]
  • Prevalence in UK GP population: Approximately 5–10% of patients presenting with dyspepsia have gastritis.
  • UK hospital prevalence: Gastritis accounts for about 1–2% of GI-related admissions.
  • Worldwide prevalence: Helicobacter pylori-associated gastritis has a global prevalence of ~50%, though symptomatic cases are much lower.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Gastritis"][/sc]
  • 1. **Acute gastritis**: Often due to factors like alcohol, NSAIDs, or infections that damage gastric mucosal cells, reducing protective mucus and bicarbonate, exposing the stomach lining to acidic damage.
  • 2. **Chronic gastritis**: Typically from prolonged H. pylori infection or autoimmune mechanisms. H. pylori infection induces an inflammatory response, damaging gastric cells, potentially causing ulcers and atrophic changes.
  • 3. **Autoimmune gastritis**: Antibodies attack parietal cells, decreasing intrinsic factor production, leading to B12 malabsorption and pernicious anaemia.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Gastritis"][/sc]
  • Acute Gastritis: Rapid onset, often resolving within days to weeks once the causative factor is removed.
  • Chronic Gastritis: Gradual progression over months to years; can lead to atrophic changes and increased risk of gastric carcinoma.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Gastritis"][/sc]
  • Epigastric pain - Common and typically worsened by food; occurs due to mucosal inflammation and acid exposure.
  • Heartburn - Often due to acid reflux or increased gastric acidity irritating the oesophagus.
  • Nausea and vomiting - Caused by gastric irritation and mucosal damage.
  • Loss of appetite - Common in chronic cases; may result from chronic discomfort and nausea.
  • Bloating - Caused by altered gastric motility and gas retention in inflamed stomach lining.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Gastritis"][/sc]
  • Epigastric tenderness - Indicates localised inflammation in the gastric region.
  • Positive stool guaiac test - Occurs if there is bleeding from erosions in the stomach lining, indicating erosive gastritis.
  • Pallor - Seen in chronic cases due to blood loss or B12 deficiency, indicating anaemia.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Gastritis"][/sc]
  • Haematemesis - Sign of potential gastric erosion or ulceration with bleeding; requires urgent evaluation.
  • Melena - Suggests gastrointestinal bleeding, typically from the upper GI tract, indicating severe mucosal damage.
  • Unintentional weight loss - May indicate gastric cancer, especially in chronic cases.
  • Severe persistent pain - Could indicate a peptic ulcer or even gastric perforation.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Gastritis"][/sc]
  • Silent gastritis: Common in elderly patients who may not feel pain due to altered pain perception.
  • Non-specific dyspepsia: Mild or vague upper abdominal discomfort that may not be linked directly to food intake.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Gastritis"][/sc]
  • Upper GI endoscopy - Reveals mucosal erythema, erosions, or ulcerations; biopsy can confirm inflammation or H. pylori infection.
  • H. pylori testing (urea breath test or stool antigen test) - Detects presence of H. pylori, a common cause of chronic gastritis.
  • Full blood count (FBC) - May show anaemia due to chronic blood loss or vitamin B12 deficiency in autoimmune gastritis.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Gastritis"][/sc]
  • 54-year-old male with a three-month history of epigastric pain, occasional nausea, and weight loss, with a history of NSAID use.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Gastritis"][/sc]
  • Peptic ulcer disease (PUD) - Similar epigastric pain, but often relieved by eating, and may present with more severe bleeding.
  • Gastro-oesophageal reflux disease (GORD) - Often has heartburn as the predominant symptom, with pain worsening when lying down.
  • Functional dyspepsia - Presents similarly but lacks endoscopic evidence of inflammation or structural abnormalities.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Gastritis"][/sc]
  • Proton pump inhibitors (PPIs) - First-line treatment for symptom relief and mucosal healing, e.g., omeprazole 20 mg daily.
  • H. pylori eradication therapy - Combination of PPI and antibiotics (e.g., amoxicillin and clarithromycin) for confirmed H. pylori infection.
  • Avoidance of irritants - Discontinuing NSAIDs and reducing alcohol consumption.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Gastritis"][/sc]
  • Peptic ulcer disease - Chronic inflammation can lead to ulceration with risk of bleeding or perforation.
  • Gastric carcinoma - Chronic H. pylori infection or autoimmune gastritis can progress to gastric atrophy and malignancy.
  • Pernicious anaemia - Associated with autoimmune gastritis and B12 deficiency, leading to neurological complications if untreated.
