Peripheral Arterial Disease (Buttock Claudication)
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Peripheral Arterial Disease (Buttock Claudication)"][/sc]
  • Peripheral Arterial Disease (PAD) is a chronic vascular condition characterised by progressive atherosclerotic narrowing or occlusion of the peripheral arteries. Buttock claudication refers specifically to ischaemic pain in the buttock region caused by arterial insufficiency, often due to stenosis or occlusion in the aortoiliac arteries.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Peripheral Arterial Disease (Buttock Claudication)"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Peripheral Arterial Disease (Buttock Claudication)"][/sc]
  • In the UK, the prevalence of PAD in the general population is approximately 3-10%, increasing to 15-20% in individuals aged over 70 years.
  • Globally, PAD affects over 200 million people, with higher prevalence in low- and middle-income countries.
  • Buttock claudication is less common than calf claudication, associated with significant atherosclerosis in the aortoiliac region, occurring in approximately 5-10% of individuals with PAD.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Peripheral Arterial Disease (Buttock Claudication)"][/sc]
  • Atherosclerosis causes plaque formation within the arterial wall, leading to stenosis or occlusion of affected arteries.
  • The aortoiliac segment is affected, reducing blood flow to the buttocks and thighs during exertion, causing ischaemia and pain (claudication).
  • Exercise increases muscle demand for oxygen and nutrients, which cannot be met due to restricted arterial blood flow, leading to lactic acid build-up and ischaemic pain.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Peripheral Arterial Disease (Buttock Claudication)"][/sc]
  • PAD progresses chronically over years, often beginning with asymptomatic atherosclerosis.
  • Intermittent claudication develops as arterial stenosis worsens, with symptoms emerging during exercise and subsiding at rest.
  • Critical limb ischaemia may develop in severe cases, characterised by rest pain, ulceration, or gangrene.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Peripheral Arterial Disease (Buttock Claudication)"][/sc]
  • Buttock Pain with Walking - Caused by ischaemia in gluteal muscles due to reduced arterial blood flow during exercise.
  • Thigh or Leg Fatigue - Muscle fatigue occurs due to insufficient oxygen delivery to the tissues.
  • Symptoms Relieved by Rest - Intermittent claudication improves with rest as oxygen demand decreases.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Peripheral Arterial Disease (Buttock Claudication)"][/sc]
  • Diminished Femoral Pulses - Indicates reduced blood flow through the aortoiliac arteries.
  • Cold or Pale Lower Extremities - Caused by reduced perfusion to the distal tissues.
  • Bruits Over Aortoiliac Region - Turbulent blood flow due to arterial stenosis.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Peripheral Arterial Disease (Buttock Claudication)"][/sc]
  • Rest Pain - Suggests critical limb ischaemia and impending tissue damage.
  • Non-Healing Ulcers - Indicates severe arterial insufficiency and risk of gangrene.
  • Gangrene - Signifies advanced PAD with significant tissue necrosis.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Peripheral Arterial Disease (Buttock Claudication)"][/sc]
  • Isolated lower back pain mimicking spinal stenosis without typical claudication symptoms.
  • Asymptomatic PAD detected incidentally through abnormal ankle-brachial pressure index (ABPI).
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Peripheral Arterial Disease (Buttock Claudication)"][/sc]
  • Ankle-Brachial Pressure Index (ABPI) - An ABPI <0.9 confirms PAD; low readings reflect reduced arterial perfusion.
  • Duplex Ultrasonography - Visualises arterial stenosis or occlusion in the aortoiliac region.
  • Magnetic Resonance Angiography (MRA) - Provides detailed imaging of arterial anatomy to localise stenosis.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Peripheral Arterial Disease (Buttock Claudication)"][/sc]
  • A 62-year-old male presents with exertional buttock pain relieved by rest, a history of smoking, and diminished femoral pulses, with an ABPI of 0.7.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Peripheral Arterial Disease (Buttock Claudication)"][/sc]
  • Spinal Stenosis - Features neurogenic claudication, often bilateral, worsens with extension, and improves with flexion.
  • Venous Insufficiency - Presents with oedema and varicosities, symptoms worsen with prolonged standing.
  • Hip Osteoarthritis - Pain localised to the hip, worsened by joint movement, not relieved by rest.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Peripheral Arterial Disease (Buttock Claudication)"][/sc]
  • Lifestyle Modifications - Smoking cessation, structured exercise programmes, and dietary changes.
  • Medical Therapy - Antiplatelet agents (e.g., clopidogrel) and statins to reduce cardiovascular risk.
  • Surgical Intervention - Angioplasty or bypass surgery for severe, disabling claudication or critical limb ischaemia.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Peripheral Arterial Disease (Buttock Claudication)"][/sc]
  • Critical Limb Ischaemia - Leads to rest pain, ulcers, and gangrene; occurs in 1-3% annually.
  • Cardiovascular Events - Increased risk of myocardial infarction and stroke due to systemic atherosclerosis.
  • Limb Loss - Severe ischaemia and tissue necrosis may necessitate amputation.
