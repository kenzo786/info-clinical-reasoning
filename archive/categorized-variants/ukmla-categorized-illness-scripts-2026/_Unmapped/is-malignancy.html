Malignancy
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Malignancy"][/sc]
  • Malignancy refers to the presence of cancerous cells that have the ability to invade surrounding tissues and spread to distant sites in the body. Malignancies arise due to genetic mutations that lead to uncontrolled cell growth, loss of normal tissue architecture, and the potential for metastasis.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Malignancy"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Malignancy"][/sc]
  • UK GP population prevalence: In the UK, cancer diagnoses are common, with approximately 1 in 2 people developing some form of cancer in their lifetime.
  • UK hospital prevalence: Cancer accounts for a significant proportion of hospital admissions, with over 300,000 new diagnoses annually.
  • Worldwide prevalence: Globally, there are an estimated 19.3 million new cancer cases per year, making it a leading cause of morbidity and mortality.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Malignancy"][/sc]
  • 1. Genetic mutations: Malignancies begin with mutations in proto-oncogenes, tumour suppressor genes, or DNA repair genes.
  • 2. Uncontrolled cell proliferation: These mutations disrupt normal cell cycle regulation, leading to uncontrolled growth.
  • 3. Invasion and metastasis: Cancerous cells invade surrounding tissues and enter the bloodstream or lymphatic system, spreading to distant organs.
  • 4. Angiogenesis: Tumours stimulate new blood vessel formation to supply their growth, further promoting their survival and spread.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Malignancy"][/sc]
  • Initial Phase: Slow growth of a small primary tumour, often asymptomatic.
  • Progressive Phase: Growth becomes more rapid as genetic mutations accumulate, leading to local invasion and symptoms.
  • Advanced Phase: Tumour metastasis to distant sites, resulting in systemic symptoms such as weight loss, fatigue, and organ-specific dysfunction.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Malignancy"][/sc]
  • Unexplained weight loss - Results from the high metabolic activity of cancer cells and cytokine release leading to a catabolic state.
  • Fatigue - Caused by anaemia, chronic inflammation, and the systemic effects of malignancy.
  • Pain - Due to tumour invasion of nearby tissues, nerves, or bones.
  • Persistent cough or hoarseness - Can occur in lung cancer or malignancies affecting the throat or larynx due to local invasion or pressure.
  • Palpable lump - Caused by the growth of solid tumours near the skin or within organs such as the breast or lymph nodes.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Malignancy"][/sc]
  • Pallor - Due to anaemia, common in malignancies causing bone marrow suppression or gastrointestinal blood loss.
  • Palpable mass - A physical finding in solid tumours, often irregular in shape, hard, and non-mobile.
  • Lymphadenopathy - Enlarged lymph nodes result from metastasis or local immune response.
  • Jaundice - Can indicate liver metastasis or obstruction of the bile ducts.
  • Cachexia - Profound weight loss and muscle wasting due to systemic inflammation and metabolic changes.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Malignancy"][/sc]
  • Unexplained weight loss - Suggests significant systemic disease, commonly linked to cancer cachexia.
  • Night sweats - Can indicate lymphoma or advanced malignancy, often associated with systemic inflammation.
  • Haemoptysis - Coughing up blood may signal lung cancer or an airway-invading malignancy.
  • Dysphagia - Difficulty swallowing suggests oesophageal or head and neck cancer due to local obstruction or tumour invasion.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Malignancy"][/sc]
  • Paraneoplastic syndromes - Presenting with systemic effects unrelated to tumour size or location, such as hypercalcaemia in lung cancer.
  • Silent tumours - Tumours of the pancreas or ovary may remain asymptomatic until they are advanced and have metastasised.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Malignancy"][/sc]
  • CT scan - Detailed imaging that can reveal the size, location, and extent of primary tumours and metastases.
  • Biopsy - Histological examination confirms the diagnosis and identifies the type of cancer.
  • Blood tests (e.g., tumour markers) - May show elevated levels of substances like CA-125, PSA, or CEA, helping to diagnose specific cancers.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Malignancy"][/sc]
  • A 65-year-old male presents with a three-month history of unexplained weight loss, fatigue, and a persistent cough. A CT scan reveals a lung mass with evidence of metastases in the liver.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Malignancy"][/sc]
  • Chronic infection (e.g., tuberculosis) - Presents with weight loss, cough, and systemic symptoms but typically shows granulomatous inflammation on biopsy rather than malignancy.
  • Autoimmune disease (e.g., lupus) - Can cause weight loss and fatigue, but usually includes signs such as rash, joint pain, or specific serological markers.
  • Benign tumours - May present with a palpable lump or mass but lack the systemic symptoms and progressive nature of malignancies.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Malignancy"][/sc]
  • Surgery - Primary treatment for solid tumours when resectable, aiming for complete removal.
  • Chemotherapy - Systemic treatment targeting rapidly dividing cells, used in both localised and metastatic disease.
  • Radiotherapy - Localised treatment to shrink tumours or manage symptoms, such as pain in bone metastases.
  • Immunotherapy - Novel treatments targeting immune checkpoints (e.g., PD-1 inhibitors) to enhance the body’s immune response against cancer cells.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Malignancy"][/sc]
  • Metastasis - Spread of cancer to distant organs such as the liver, lungs, or bones, leading to organ failure.
  • Cachexia - Severe weight loss and muscle wasting, which can be fatal in advanced cancer.
  • Thrombosis - Cancer increases the risk of venous thromboembolism due to hypercoagulability.
