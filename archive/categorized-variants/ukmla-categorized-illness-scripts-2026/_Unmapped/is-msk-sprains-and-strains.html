MSK Sprains and Strains
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="MSK Sprains and Strains"][/sc]
  • Musculoskeletal (MSK) sprains and strains are injuries to soft tissues—specifically ligaments in sprains and muscles or tendons in strains. These injuries often result from sudden stretching or tearing of the tissue due to trauma or excessive force. Sprains typically affect ligaments (e.g., ankle sprains), while strains involve muscles or tendons (e.g., hamstring strain).
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="MSK Sprains and Strains"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="MSK Sprains and Strains"][/sc]
  • UK GP prevalence: MSK injuries are common, with 1-2% of GP consultations involving sprains and strains.
  • UK hospital prevalence: They are frequently seen in A&E, particularly in sports-related contexts, with sprains and strains constituting a large portion of MSK injuries.
  • Worldwide prevalence: Similar to the UK, these injuries are globally prevalent, particularly in active populations.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="MSK Sprains and Strains"][/sc]
  • Sprains: Involves damage to ligaments due to overstretching or tearing. This damage disrupts ligament integrity, causing pain, inflammation, and sometimes instability in the affected joint.
  • Strains: Occur when muscle or tendon fibres are overstretched or torn. Microtears in the muscle or tendon fibres initiate an inflammatory response, leading to swelling, pain, and reduced mobility.
  • Inflammatory response: Cellular damage activates inflammatory mediators (e.g., prostaglandins), causing vasodilation, increased permeability, and fluid accumulation, resulting in swelling and pain.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="MSK Sprains and Strains"][/sc]
  • Acute Phase (0-72 hours): Initial pain, swelling, and limited mobility due to inflammation.
  • Subacute Phase (3 days–2 weeks): Pain and swelling decrease, but stiffness and bruising may be present. Tissue repair begins.
  • Recovery Phase (2 weeks–3 months): Healing progresses, with gradual improvement in strength and function. Physiotherapy may aid recovery.
  • Chronic Phase (>3 months): Occurs in some cases, with persistent pain or weakness. Common in severe injuries or without proper rehabilitation.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="MSK Sprains and Strains"][/sc]
  • Pain - Localised pain at the injury site, usually immediate in onset. Caused by tissue damage and activation of pain receptors.
  • Swelling - Due to inflammation and fluid leakage from blood vessels at the injury site.
  • Bruising - Resulting from bleeding into surrounding tissues due to rupture of small blood vessels.
  • Limited Mobility - Reduced range of motion due to pain, swelling, or tissue damage, particularly in weight-bearing joints.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="MSK Sprains and Strains"][/sc]
  • Swelling - Visible and palpable enlargement at the injury site, caused by local fluid accumulation due to inflammation.
  • Tenderness - Local tenderness on palpation indicates inflammation and sensitivity at the injury site.
  • Reduced Range of Motion (ROM) - Joint or muscle restriction on movement due to pain or structural damage.
  • Ecchymosis - Bruising in the area of injury due to blood vessel rupture and local bleeding.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="MSK Sprains and Strains"][/sc]
  • Severe pain with weight-bearing - May indicate a fracture or more serious injury, especially in the lower limb.
  • Numbness or tingling - Suggests possible nerve injury or compression.
  • Joint instability - May indicate significant ligamentous injury, requiring assessment for potential surgery.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="MSK Sprains and Strains"][/sc]
  • Minimal pain or swelling - In some mild cases, symptoms may be absent or very limited, potentially leading to underestimation of injury severity.
  • Delayed onset of pain - Pain that appears hours after the injury, potentially due to delayed inflammatory response.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="MSK Sprains and Strains"][/sc]
  • Clinical Assessment - Based on history and physical examination; may be sufficient for mild sprains and strains.
  • X-ray - Indicated if fracture is suspected; X-ray findings will typically be normal in simple sprains and strains.
  • MRI - Used to evaluate ligament, tendon, or muscle damage in severe or persistent cases. T2-weighted MRI may show high signal intensity in the injured tissue due to fluid and inflammation.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="MSK Sprains and Strains"][/sc]
  • A 30-year-old male presents with a swollen, painful right ankle following a sports injury, unable to bear weight and with visible bruising.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="MSK Sprains and Strains"][/sc]
  • Fracture - Typically involves more severe pain, bony tenderness, and potentially deformity; confirmed with X-ray.
  • Tendon rupture - Sudden loss of movement or strength in a specific muscle; may show a palpable gap on examination.
  • Dislocation - Usually presents with visible joint deformity and inability to move the joint; confirmed with imaging.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="MSK Sprains and Strains"][/sc]
  • Rest, Ice, Compression, Elevation (RICE) - Initial management for reducing swelling and pain.
  • Analgesics - NSAIDs or paracetamol for pain relief.
  • Physiotherapy - To restore strength, flexibility, and mobility as recovery progresses.
  • Gradual Return to Activity - Recommended to avoid re-injury and promote full recovery.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="MSK Sprains and Strains"][/sc]
  • Chronic Pain - Persistent pain if healing is incomplete or due to poor rehabilitation.
  • Instability - Recurrent sprains may lead to joint instability, particularly in the ankle.
  • Arthritis - Increased risk of degenerative changes in joints if ligaments or tendons are chronically damaged.
