Spinal Sprains and Strains
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Spinal Sprains and Strains"][/sc]
  • A spinal sprain or strain refers to damage or overstretching of the muscles, ligaments, or tendons that support the spine. Sprains involve ligamentous damage, while strains affect muscles or tendons. They commonly result from abrupt, forceful movements or poor biomechanics, leading to acute or subacute back pain.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Spinal Sprains and Strains"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Spinal Sprains and Strains"][/sc]
  • UK GP population: Approximately 5-10% of all general practice visits are related to back pain, with strains/sprains as a common cause.
  • UK hospital prevalence: Low, as most cases are managed in primary care; however, back pain is a frequent reason for A&E visits.
  • Worldwide prevalence: Back pain affects up to 80% of adults at some point; spinal sprains and strains are significant contributors.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Spinal Sprains and Strains"][/sc]
  • 1. Muscle/Tendon Strain: Excessive or sudden tension causes microscopic tears within the muscle fibres or tendons, leading to inflammation and muscle spasm.
  • 2. Ligament Sprain: Ligaments overstretch or tear due to an excessive force, resulting in swelling, localised pain, and reduced joint stability.
  • 3. Inflammation: Damaged tissue releases inflammatory mediators, which stimulate pain receptors and result in localised swelling and tenderness.
  • 4. Muscle Spasm: Surrounding muscles contract involuntarily to protect the affected area, which can exacerbate pain and restrict movement.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Spinal Sprains and Strains"][/sc]
  • Acute Phase: Pain typically peaks within the first 48 hours, with inflammation and muscle spasm prominent.
  • Subacute Phase: Pain and mobility improve progressively over 1-3 weeks with rest and appropriate management.
  • Chronic Phase: In a minority of cases, pain persists beyond 3 months, often due to incomplete healing or poor biomechanical adaptation.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Spinal Sprains and Strains"][/sc]
  • Localised Back Pain: Sharp or aching pain near the affected area due to inflammation and tissue damage.
  • Muscle Stiffness: Reduced range of motion as a protective response to prevent further strain on injured tissues.
  • Radiating Pain (buttocks or thighs): Muscle spasm and inflammation can lead to referred pain along peripheral nerve pathways.
  • Worsened Pain with Movement: Movement stretches the affected ligaments or muscles, aggravating pain.
  • Difficulty Maintaining Upright Posture: Muscle weakness and pain can impact posture, leading to compensatory adjustments.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Spinal Sprains and Strains"][/sc]
  • Localised Tenderness: Palpation reveals tenderness over injured muscles or ligaments due to inflammatory mediators.
  • Muscle Spasm: Muscles near the injured site contract involuntarily to stabilise the area and prevent further injury.
  • Restricted Range of Motion: Patients may be unable to perform movements involving the injured muscles or ligaments without significant discomfort.
  • Normal Neurological Examination: Unlike nerve compression, spinal sprains and strains do not typically result in neurological deficits.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Spinal Sprains and Strains"][/sc]
  • Severe or Progressive Neurological Deficits: Indicates possible nerve root or spinal cord involvement, warranting imaging.
  • Unexplained Weight Loss: Could suggest malignancy or infection if concurrent with back pain.
  • Fever: Raises concern for infection, such as osteomyelitis or discitis.
  • History of Trauma: High-impact trauma may indicate fractures or other serious injuries.
  • Age over 50 or under 20: Atypical for simple strain, suggesting alternative diagnoses (e.g., malignancy or congenital spine issues).
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Spinal Sprains and Strains"][/sc]
  • Minimal Pain but Significant Stiffness: Some patients may not experience intense pain but report marked stiffness, often due to chronic strains or partial healing.
  • Bilateral Symptoms: Rare in simple sprains and may suggest systemic or inflammatory conditions (e.g., ankylosing spondylitis).
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Spinal Sprains and Strains"][/sc]
  • X-rays: Usually normal in sprains/strains but may be indicated to exclude fractures, especially if trauma or red flags are present.
  • MRI: Not routinely required; reserved for cases with red flags or persistent symptoms to evaluate soft tissue injury and rule out serious pathology.
  • Blood Tests: Generally normal, except in cases where infection, inflammatory conditions, or malignancy are suspected based on red flags.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Spinal Sprains and Strains"][/sc]
  • A 35-year-old male presents with acute lower back pain after lifting a heavy object, reporting worsened pain on movement and difficulty maintaining an upright posture. No neurological symptoms reported.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Spinal Sprains and Strains"][/sc]
  • Herniated Disc: Typically presents with radicular pain, sensory deficits, or motor weakness, which are absent in simple strains or sprains.
  • Vertebral Fracture: Associated with trauma, focal tenderness, and possible neurological impairment, especially in elderly or osteoporotic patients.
  • Ankylosing Spondylitis: Chronic, bilateral back pain in younger patients with morning stiffness, relieved by exercise, unlike acute injury pain.
  • Spinal Infection: Fever, weight loss, and severe pain with local tenderness, unlike typical strain/sprain presentations.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Spinal Sprains and Strains"][/sc]
  • Rest and Activity Modification: Short-term rest, avoiding aggravating activities, while maintaining gentle movements as tolerated.
  • Analgesia: Paracetamol and NSAIDs as first-line pain management per UK guidelines; muscle relaxants or stronger analgesia for more severe cases.
  • Physical Therapy: Core strengthening, flexibility exercises, and manual therapy as symptoms allow to restore function and prevent recurrence.
  • Patient Education: Emphasise correct lifting techniques, posture, and ergonomics to prevent recurrence.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Spinal Sprains and Strains"][/sc]
  • Chronic Pain: Occurs in a minority; associated with prolonged inflammation and maladaptive pain processing.
  • Reduced Mobility: Chronic strains may result in decreased range of motion, impacting daily function.
  • Recurrence: High risk in cases of inadequate rehabilitation or poor body mechanics.
