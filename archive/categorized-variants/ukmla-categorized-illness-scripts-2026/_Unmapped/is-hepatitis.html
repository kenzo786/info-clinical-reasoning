Hepatitis
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Hepatitis"][/sc]
  • Hepatitis is an inflammatory condition of the liver characterised by hepatocellular injury, often leading to liver dysfunction. It can result from viral infections (e.g., hepatitis A, B, C), toxic substances (such as alcohol or medications), autoimmune processes, or metabolic disorders. Chronic hepatitis can progress to cirrhosis, liver failure, and hepatocellular carcinoma.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Hepatitis"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Hepatitis"][/sc]
  • UK GP Prevalence: Chronic hepatitis C prevalence is approximately 0.4% in the UK adult population.
  • UK Hospital Prevalence: Hepatitis is a common cause of liver-related admissions, with hepatitis C and B accounting for most cases.
  • Worldwide Prevalence: Chronic hepatitis B and C affect 350 million and 70 million people globally, respectively. Hepatitis A is endemic in areas with poor sanitation.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Hepatitis"][/sc]
  • Hepatitis involves inflammation of the liver tissue due to direct injury (e.g., toxins) or immune-mediated response (e.g., viral infection).
  • Viral hepatitis (A, B, C, D, E) primarily damages hepatocytes through immune-mediated mechanisms, especially cytotoxic T cells targeting infected hepatocytes.
  • In autoimmune hepatitis, autoantibodies target hepatocytes, leading to chronic inflammation and fibrosis.
  • Chronic liver inflammation may progress to fibrosis, cirrhosis, and hepatocellular carcinoma, particularly in hepatitis B and C.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Hepatitis"][/sc]
  • Acute phase: Hepatitis A and E cause self-limiting acute hepatitis over weeks.
  • Chronic phase: Hepatitis B, C, and autoimmune hepatitis can persist over months to years, often asymptomatically before progressing to fibrosis and cirrhosis.
  • Fulminant hepatitis: Rapid progression to liver failure can occur, particularly in hepatitis B and autoimmune hepatitis.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Hepatitis"][/sc]
  • Fatigue - Common in chronic hepatitis due to ongoing inflammation and impaired liver function, affecting energy metabolism.
  • Jaundice - Yellowing of skin and eyes from elevated bilirubin due to hepatocellular dysfunction.
  • Abdominal pain - Right upper quadrant pain from liver inflammation and stretching of the liver capsule.
  • Nausea and vomiting - Result from hepatic dysfunction and toxins in the bloodstream affecting the chemoreceptor trigger zone.
  • Pruritus - Generalised itching due to bile salts accumulating in the skin, especially in cholestatic hepatitis.
  • Anorexia - Loss of appetite related to metabolic disturbances and systemic inflammation.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Hepatitis"][/sc]
  • Jaundice - Visible yellowing of skin/eyes from bilirubin accumulation.
  • Hepatomegaly - Enlarged liver from inflammation or fibrosis.
  • Splenomegaly - Enlarged spleen, often due to portal hypertension in chronic hepatitis cases.
  • Spider naevi - Capillary lesions seen in chronic liver disease due to elevated oestrogen.
  • Ascites - Abdominal fluid accumulation due to portal hypertension and reduced albumin.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Hepatitis"][/sc]
  • Hepatic encephalopathy - Confusion and cognitive impairment due to ammonia build-up, indicating liver failure.
  • Rapidly worsening jaundice - Suggests acute liver failure or rapid disease progression, often in hepatitis B.
  • Ascites with abdominal pain - May indicate spontaneous bacterial peritonitis in cirrhotic patients.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Hepatitis"][/sc]
  • Asymptomatic presentation - Particularly in chronic hepatitis B and C, often detected incidentally during routine blood tests.
  • Extrahepatic symptoms - Such as arthralgia, glomerulonephritis, or skin vasculitis in hepatitis B or C, due to immune complex deposition.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Hepatitis"][/sc]
  • Liver function tests (LFTs) - Elevated ALT and AST indicate hepatocellular injury; high ALP and bilirubin suggest cholestatic disease.
  • Viral serologies - Anti-HAV IgM, HBsAg, HCV RNA to confirm the type of viral hepatitis.
  • Liver ultrasound - Detects hepatomegaly, fibrosis, or cirrhotic changes.
  • Autoantibodies - ANA, SMA, or anti-LKM1 in suspected autoimmune hepatitis.
  • Liver biopsy - Confirms hepatitis type and stage, especially in chronic or autoimmune hepatitis.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Hepatitis"][/sc]
  • A 35-year-old male with a 2-week history of fatigue, jaundice, and mild abdominal pain, presenting with elevated liver enzymes and positive HBsAg.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Hepatitis"][/sc]
  • Alcoholic hepatitis - History of alcohol use; AST:ALT ratio often >2.
  • Non-alcoholic fatty liver disease - Associated with obesity and metabolic syndrome; elevated ALT.
  • Drug-induced liver injury - Recent medication use; variable LFT patterns.
  • Primary biliary cholangitis - High ALP with AMA positivity; more common in females.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Hepatitis"][/sc]
  • Acute viral hepatitis - Supportive care, avoid hepatotoxic drugs; hepatitis A and B immunoglobulin in contacts if needed.
  • Chronic hepatitis B - Antiviral therapy (e.g., tenofovir or entecavir) for patients with high viral load or liver damage.
  • Chronic hepatitis C - Direct-acting antivirals (e.g., sofosbuvir-based regimens) for 8–12 weeks.
  • Autoimmune hepatitis - Immunosuppressants (e.g., prednisolone and azathioprine).
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Hepatitis"][/sc]
  • Cirrhosis - Fibrosis due to chronic inflammation; leads to portal hypertension and liver failure.
  • Hepatocellular carcinoma - Increased risk with chronic hepatitis B and C; regular screening recommended.
  • Fulminant hepatic failure - Rapid liver failure in severe cases, especially in acute hepatitis B.
