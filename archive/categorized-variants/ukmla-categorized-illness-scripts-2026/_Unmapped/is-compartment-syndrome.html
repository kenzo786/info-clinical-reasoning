Compartment Syndrome
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Compartment Syndrome"][/sc]
  • Compartment syndrome is a condition where increased pressure within a closed fascial compartment compromises circulation and tissue function, potentially leading to irreversible damage if untreated. It can occur acutely, typically after trauma, or as a chronic exertional condition in athletes.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Compartment Syndrome"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Compartment Syndrome"][/sc]
  • In the UK, acute compartment syndrome (ACS) is estimated to occur in 3.1 cases per 100,000 annually.
  • Higher prevalence in trauma centres, with approximately 7% of tibial fractures developing ACS.
  • Worldwide, the prevalence reflects similar trauma and surgical trends, with chronic exertional compartment syndrome (CECS) more commonly reported in athletes.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Compartment Syndrome"][/sc]
  • Trauma or repetitive exertion causes oedema or bleeding within a compartment bound by fascia.
  • The increase in intracompartmental pressure exceeds venous pressure, impairing venous outflow while arterial inflow continues.
  • This leads to tissue ischaemia, cellular dysfunction, and necrosis if untreated.
  • End-organ effects occur due to hypoxia and release of inflammatory mediators.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Compartment Syndrome"][/sc]
  • Acute compartment syndrome typically develops within hours of trauma or surgery.
  • Chronic exertional compartment syndrome develops gradually with physical activity, resolving with rest.
  • Without intervention in ACS, ischaemic damage progresses rapidly, with irreversible tissue damage occurring within 6-12 hours.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Compartment Syndrome"][/sc]
  • Pain out of proportion to injury - Caused by ischaemia and nerve irritation; hallmark of early ACS.
  • Paresthesia - Due to nerve compression and hypoxia, typically in the distribution of the affected compartment.
  • Weakness - Early sign of nerve or muscle dysfunction.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Compartment Syndrome"][/sc]
  • Tense, swollen compartment - Due to oedema or haemorrhage; palpable firmness indicates elevated pressure.
  • Pain with passive stretch - Caused by increased pressure and sensitivity of ischaemic muscles.
  • Absent or reduced distal pulses - Late sign indicating severe vascular compromise.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Compartment Syndrome"][/sc]
  • Severe pain unresponsive to analgesia - Indicates worsening ischaemia and pressure.
  • Loss of sensation - Signifies significant nerve involvement.
  • Paralysis - Indicates advanced ischaemic or nerve injury; associated with poor outcomes.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Compartment Syndrome"][/sc]
  • Minimal pain - In cases of altered pain perception (e.g., in diabetics with neuropathy).
  • Subacute progression - May occur in chronic exertional cases or atypical trauma patterns.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Compartment Syndrome"][/sc]
  • Intracompartmental pressure measurement - Pressure >30 mmHg or delta pressure (diastolic BP - compartment pressure) <30 mmHg confirms diagnosis.
  • Imaging (e.g., MRI, ultrasound) - Rarely used but can show oedema in chronic cases.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Compartment Syndrome"][/sc]
  • A 25-year-old male presents with severe, escalating leg pain following a tibial fracture 6 hours earlier. Examination reveals a tense calf with pain on passive stretch and paraesthesia over the foot.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Compartment Syndrome"][/sc]
  • Deep vein thrombosis (DVT) - Lacks acute pain on passive movement and has different risk factors (e.g., immobility).
  • Cellulitis - Presents with erythema and systemic signs of infection.
  • Peripheral arterial occlusion - Associated with cold, pulseless limb and clear arterial history.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Compartment Syndrome"][/sc]
  • Fasciotomy - Immediate surgical decompression is the definitive treatment.
  • Supportive measures - Elevation, analgesia, and monitoring while awaiting surgery.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Compartment Syndrome"][/sc]
  • Volkmann's contracture - Ischaemic contracture due to prolonged muscle ischaemia.
  • Chronic pain - Resulting from nerve or tissue damage.
  • Amputation - Required in severe cases with necrosis.
