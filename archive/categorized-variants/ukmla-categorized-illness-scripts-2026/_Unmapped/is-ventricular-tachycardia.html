Ventricular Tachycardia (VT)
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Ventricular Tachycardia (VT)"][/sc]
  • Ventricular tachycardia (VT) is a type of tachyarrhythmia originating from the ventricles, characterised by a rapid heart rate, usually over 100 beats per minute. It occurs due to abnormal electrical activity in the ventricles and can be sustained (lasting more than 30 seconds) or non-sustained (lasting less than 30 seconds). VT can compromise cardiac output and potentially progress to ventricular fibrillation, leading to sudden cardiac arrest.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Ventricular Tachycardia (VT)"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Ventricular Tachycardia (VT)"][/sc]
  • In the UK, VT accounts for approximately 5% of cases of arrhythmias diagnosed in general practice.
  • Hospital prevalence of VT is higher, especially in patients with structural heart disease, including coronary artery disease (CAD) and heart failure, with an incidence rate of 2-4% in post-myocardial infarction patients.
  • Worldwide, VT occurs in 10-30% of cases of cardiac arrhythmias and is a leading cause of sudden cardiac death globally.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Ventricular Tachycardia (VT)"][/sc]
  • VT results from abnormal automaticity, triggered activity, or re-entrant circuits in the ventricular myocardium. The most common mechanism is re-entry, where an electrical impulse continues to cycle within the ventricles due to regions of slow conduction, often secondary to ischaemic or scarred myocardial tissue.
  • This aberrant electrical activity leads to rapid ventricular contraction, reducing the time for ventricular filling and subsequently decreasing cardiac output.
  • In patients with structural heart disease, scar tissue disrupts normal conduction, increasing the risk of re-entrant pathways. Myocardial infarction, cardiomyopathy, and electrolyte imbalances are common underlying conditions predisposing to VT.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Ventricular Tachycardia (VT)"][/sc]
  • Acute onset: VT can occur suddenly, often triggered by stress, exercise, or ischaemia. Non-sustained VT episodes are brief and self-limiting, whereas sustained VT can persist and lead to hemodynamic instability.
  • Chronic or recurrent episodes: Some patients experience recurrent VT episodes, especially in those with underlying structural heart disease. This can progress to sustained VT or ventricular fibrillation.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Ventricular Tachycardia (VT)"][/sc]
  • Palpitations: Patients frequently report sudden awareness of rapid, irregular heartbeats due to abnormal electrical activity in the ventricles.
  • Dizziness or lightheadedness: Decreased cardiac output leads to cerebral hypoperfusion, causing these symptoms.
  • Syncope: A result of severe cerebral hypoperfusion, often seen in sustained VT, especially if it progresses to ventricular fibrillation.
  • Chest pain: May occur due to ischaemia from decreased coronary perfusion during the rapid heart rate.
  • Dyspnoea: Decreased cardiac output and pulmonary congestion can cause shortness of breath.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Ventricular Tachycardia (VT)"][/sc]
  • Tachycardia: Heart rate is typically over 100 bpm, often 150-200 bpm, due to rapid ventricular depolarisation.
  • Hypotension: Caused by reduced cardiac output, especially in sustained VT.
  • Jugular venous distension: Results from increased right atrial pressure due to impaired ventricular function.
  • Cool, clammy skin: Peripheral vasoconstriction occurs in response to reduced perfusion.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Ventricular Tachycardia (VT)"][/sc]
  • Syncope: Signifies significant reduction in cerebral blood flow, often indicating progression to ventricular fibrillation or severe hemodynamic compromise.
  • Sudden onset of chest pain and dyspnoea: May indicate acute coronary syndrome or imminent cardiac arrest due to worsening VT.
  • Hypotension: Marked drop in blood pressure suggests poor perfusion, which may rapidly deteriorate into cardiogenic shock.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Ventricular Tachycardia (VT)"][/sc]
  • Asymptomatic VT: Non-sustained VT can occasionally be asymptomatic, especially in patients with well-compensated cardiac output. This may be detected incidentally on routine ECG monitoring.
  • Atypical chest pain: Some patients may experience vague, non-anginal chest discomfort instead of classic ischaemic pain.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Ventricular Tachycardia (VT)"][/sc]
  • ECG: Shows wide-complex tachycardia with a regular rhythm. QRS complexes are typically greater than 120 ms, indicating ventricular origin.
  • Serum electrolytes: Hypokalaemia and hypomagnesaemia may predispose to VT.
  • Echocardiogram: Evaluates structural heart disease, such as left ventricular dysfunction or scar tissue from a previous myocardial infarction.
  • Coronary angiography: Performed in suspected ischaemic VT to assess for coronary artery disease.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Ventricular Tachycardia (VT)"][/sc]
  • A 55-year-old male with a history of myocardial infarction presents with sudden onset palpitations, dizziness, and syncope lasting several minutes. ECG reveals a wide-complex tachycardia with a heart rate of 180 bpm, consistent with sustained ventricular tachycardia.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Ventricular Tachycardia (VT)"][/sc]
  • Supraventricular tachycardia (SVT) with aberrancy: Differentiated by narrow QRS complexes unless there is pre-existing bundle branch block. SVT usually has a faster onset and termination compared to VT.
  • Atrial fibrillation with rapid ventricular response: Irregularly irregular rhythm on ECG, with variable R-R intervals, which helps distinguish it from regular VT.
  • Torsades de pointes: A form of polymorphic VT associated with prolonged QT interval, which is not seen in typical VT cases.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Ventricular Tachycardia (VT)"][/sc]
  • Immediate management: If haemodynamically unstable, urgent synchronised cardioversion is indicated. If stable, intravenous antiarrhythmics such as amiodarone can be used.
  • Long-term management: Includes implantable cardioverter-defibrillator (ICD) placement for patients at high risk of recurrent VT or sudden cardiac death. Beta-blockers and antiarrhythmics may be used to reduce recurrence.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Ventricular Tachycardia (VT)"][/sc]
  • Ventricular fibrillation: VT can degenerate into ventricular fibrillation, a life-threatening arrhythmia requiring immediate defibrillation.
  • Sudden cardiac death: Sustained VT, especially in those with structural heart disease, is a major cause of sudden cardiac arrest.
  • Heart failure: Chronic VT can lead to or worsen existing heart failure due to prolonged decreased cardiac output and ventricular dysfunction.
