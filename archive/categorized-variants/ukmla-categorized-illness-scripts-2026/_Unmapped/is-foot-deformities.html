Foot Deformities
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Foot Deformities"][/sc]
  • Foot deformities are structural abnormalities of the foot that result from congenital, developmental, or acquired conditions. These deformities may involve the bones, joints, muscles, or soft tissues, leading to altered foot shape, biomechanics, and functionality. Common types include pes planus (flatfoot), pes cavus (high-arched foot), hallux valgus (bunions), and claw toes.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Foot Deformities"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Foot Deformities"][/sc]
  • Prevalence in the UK GP population: Approximately 5–10% of adults report symptomatic foot deformities, with higher prevalence in older populations.
  • Hospital prevalence: Foot deformities account for 15–20% of podiatry outpatient visits.
  • Worldwide prevalence: Hallux valgus affects up to 23% of adults aged 18–65 years and 36% of those over 65. Pes planus is more common in children and adolescents, while pes cavus affects around 10% of the general population.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Foot Deformities"][/sc]
  • Congenital deformities: Result from genetic mutations or developmental abnormalities during fetal growth (e.g., clubfoot).
  • Neuromuscular causes: Conditions like cerebral palsy or Charcot-Marie-Tooth disease lead to imbalanced muscle forces, causing deformities such as pes cavus or claw toes.
  • Acquired deformities: Overuse, trauma, or arthritis (e.g., rheumatoid arthritis) disrupt the biomechanical alignment of the foot, leading to conditions like hallux valgus.
  • Soft tissue dysfunction: Weakening of ligaments and tendons, such as posterior tibial tendon dysfunction, contributes to deformities like flatfoot.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Foot Deformities"][/sc]
  • Congenital: Present at birth and may progress or stabilise without intervention.
  • Progressive deformities: Worsen over years due to degenerative changes, neuromuscular imbalances, or mechanical stresses.
  • Acute onset: Trauma-related deformities can occur suddenly following injury.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Foot Deformities"][/sc]
  • Pain: Most common symptom, often due to joint strain, nerve compression, or altered load distribution.
  • Difficulty walking: Arises from altered foot mechanics or discomfort.
  • Shoe-fitting issues: Result from abnormal foot shapes like bunions or high arches.
  • Swelling: Associated with inflammation or overuse in areas of deformity.
  • Asymptomatic: Many deformities are identified incidentally during routine examination.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Foot Deformities"][/sc]
  • Visible deformity: Hallux valgus presents with lateral deviation of the big toe; pes planus shows loss of the medial arch.
  • Abnormal gait: Compensatory mechanisms lead to altered walking patterns.
  • Pressure sores: Develop due to uneven weight distribution or poorly fitting footwear.
  • Calluses: Result from chronic pressure on specific areas of the foot.
  • Joint stiffness: May be due to associated arthritis or structural abnormalities.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Foot Deformities"][/sc]
  • Severe pain: May indicate a stress fracture, infection, or advanced arthritis.
  • Skin breakdown: Suggests risk of infection, especially in diabetic patients.
  • Neurological deficits: Loss of sensation or motor weakness may indicate underlying neurological disease.
  • Acute deformity: Could result from trauma or a Charcot joint.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Foot Deformities"][/sc]
  • Mild symptoms in severe deformities: Advanced deformities, like severe flatfoot, may present with minimal pain due to compensatory mechanisms.
  • Bilateral symmetrical deformities: Suggest systemic conditions like rheumatoid arthritis or neuromuscular disease.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Foot Deformities"][/sc]
  • X-ray: Reveals bone alignment, joint space narrowing, or fractures. Typical findings include metatarsophalangeal joint deviation in hallux valgus.
  • MRI: Useful for soft tissue assessment, such as tendon tears or inflammation.
  • Clinical examination: Gait analysis and arch observation aid in diagnosing flatfoot or pes cavus.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Foot Deformities"][/sc]
  • A 45-year-old woman presents with progressive bunion deformity over 3 years, accompanied by pain while walking and difficulty finding comfortable shoes.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Foot Deformities"][/sc]
  • Plantar fasciitis: Presents with heel pain rather than structural deformity.
  • Morton’s neuroma: Neuropathic pain between toes, without visible deformity.
  • Arthritis: May cause pain and stiffness but typically lacks prominent deformity initially.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Foot Deformities"][/sc]
  • Conservative: Orthotics, physiotherapy, and pain management.
  • Surgical: Corrective surgery, such as osteotomy for hallux valgus, in severe cases.
  • Specialist referral: To podiatry or orthopaedics for complex cases.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Foot Deformities"][/sc]
  • Chronic pain: From joint damage or persistent misalignment.
  • Ulceration: Particularly in diabetics, due to pressure points.
  • Functional impairment: Reduced mobility and quality of life.
