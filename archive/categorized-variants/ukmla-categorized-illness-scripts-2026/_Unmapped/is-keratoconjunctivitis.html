Keratoconjunctivitis
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Keratoconjunctivitis"][/sc]
  • Keratoconjunctivitis refers to inflammation of both the cornea (keratitis) and the conjunctiva. It can have infectious, allergic, or non-infectious causes. Common forms include adenoviral keratoconjunctivitis, dry eye-related keratoconjunctivitis sicca, and vernal/atopic keratoconjunctivitis.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Keratoconjunctivitis"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Keratoconjunctivitis"][/sc]
  • Prevalence in the UK GP population: Adenoviral keratoconjunctivitis is a frequent presentation, particularly during outbreaks, with sporadic cases throughout the year.
  • Hospital prevalence in the UK: Accounts for a significant proportion of ophthalmology outpatient visits, especially in cases of severe or recurrent forms.
  • Worldwide prevalence: Varies by aetiology; dry eye syndrome affects 5-30% globally, and adenoviral outbreaks are common in densely populated areas.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Keratoconjunctivitis"][/sc]
  • Adenoviral keratoconjunctivitis: Viral replication damages epithelial cells, triggering an immune response that results in inflammation.
  • Keratoconjunctivitis sicca: Tear film instability leads to desiccation of the corneal and conjunctival epithelium, provoking chronic inflammation.
  • Allergic keratoconjunctivitis: Hypersensitivity reactions mediated by IgE lead to mast cell degranulation, releasing histamines and cytokines that inflame ocular tissues.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Keratoconjunctivitis"][/sc]
  • Acute phase: Symptoms such as redness, tearing, and photophobia typically develop rapidly in infectious or allergic cases.
  • Chronic phase: Dry eye or allergic keratoconjunctivitis can persist for weeks to months, often with recurrent exacerbations.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Keratoconjunctivitis"][/sc]
  • Redness: Caused by vasodilation of conjunctival blood vessels due to inflammation.
  • Foreign body sensation: Resulting from corneal epithelial disruption or tear film instability.
  • Photophobia: Due to irritation of the cornea and its high density of nerve endings.
  • Tearing: Reflex lacrimation triggered by corneal irritation or inflammation.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Keratoconjunctivitis"][/sc]
  • Conjunctival injection: Indicative of inflammation and increased vascular permeability.
  • Corneal epithelial defects: Seen in fluorescein staining, suggestive of keratitis.
  • Palpebral conjunctival papillae or follicles: Indicative of allergic or viral aetiologies.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Keratoconjunctivitis"][/sc]
  • Severe pain: May indicate corneal ulceration or secondary bacterial infection.
  • Marked visual impairment: Suggestive of keratitis or deeper corneal involvement.
  • Hypopyon: Presence of pus in the anterior chamber may signal endophthalmitis.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Keratoconjunctivitis"][/sc]
  • Mild or absent redness: Seen in chronic dry eye or mild allergic forms.
  • Unilateral symptoms: Can occur in initial stages of adenoviral keratoconjunctivitis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Keratoconjunctivitis"][/sc]
  • Fluorescein staining: Reveals corneal epithelial defects characteristic of keratitis.
  • Tear break-up time: Prolonged in dry eye syndrome.
  • Conjunctival swab: Viral culture or PCR for adenovirus.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Keratoconjunctivitis"][/sc]
  • A 32-year-old female presents with a 5-day history of bilateral eye redness, tearing, and photophobia. Symptoms began after an upper respiratory infection.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Keratoconjunctivitis"][/sc]
  • Conjunctivitis: More isolated conjunctival involvement without corneal signs.
  • Uveitis: Associated with photophobia and anterior chamber inflammation.
  • Blepharitis: Eyelid margin involvement, often with crusting.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Keratoconjunctivitis"][/sc]
  • Supportive care: Cool compresses and artificial tears for symptomatic relief.
  • Topical antihistamines: For allergic keratoconjunctivitis.
  • Topical antivirals: Reserved for severe adenoviral keratoconjunctivitis.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Keratoconjunctivitis"][/sc]
  • Corneal scarring: Leading to visual impairment in severe or recurrent cases.
  • Secondary infections: Bacterial superinfection of the cornea.
