Immunodeficiency
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Immunodeficiency"][/sc]
  • Immunodeficiency refers to a state in which the immune system’s ability to fight infections and malignancies is compromised or entirely absent. It can be primary (congenital, due to genetic mutations) or secondary (acquired, due to external factors such as infections, malignancies, or immunosuppressive therapies).
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Immunodeficiency"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Immunodeficiency"][/sc]
  • Primary immunodeficiency: Prevalence in the UK GP population is estimated at 1 in 1,200, with milder undiagnosed forms likely more common.
  • Secondary immunodeficiency is more prevalent, associated with conditions like HIV/AIDS, chemotherapy, or organ transplantation.
  • Worldwide prevalence varies; primary immunodeficiency prevalence ranges from 1 in 1,200 to 1 in 25,000 depending on the disorder, while secondary immunodeficiency (e.g., HIV) is significantly higher in certain regions.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Immunodeficiency"][/sc]
  • Immunodeficiency arises due to a failure in one or more components of the immune system:
    • Primary immunodeficiency: Genetic defects impair immune cells or molecules, e.g., T-cell or B-cell defects, or complement protein abnormalities.
    • Secondary immunodeficiency: Causes include viral destruction of immune cells (e.g., HIV depleting CD4+ T-cells), iatrogenic suppression (e.g., corticosteroids inhibiting cytokine production), or systemic disease (e.g., cancer).
  • This results in increased susceptibility to infections, autoimmune disorders, and malignancies.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Immunodeficiency"][/sc]
  • Primary immunodeficiency: Symptoms often manifest in infancy or early childhood but may be delayed depending on severity (e.g., milder IgA deficiency).
  • Secondary immunodeficiency: Time course depends on cause; e.g., HIV progresses over years, while immunosuppression from chemotherapy resolves within weeks to months after treatment cessation.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Immunodeficiency"][/sc]
  • Recurrent infections: Frequent bacterial, viral, or fungal infections due to impaired immune defence.
  • Failure to thrive: In children with primary immunodeficiency, malnutrition and infections impair growth.
  • Chronic diarrhoea: Seen in conditions like HIV or SCID, due to opportunistic infections or malabsorption.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Immunodeficiency"][/sc]
  • Lymphadenopathy: Seen in HIV and some primary immunodeficiencies; reflects immune activation or infection.
  • Poor wound healing: Associated with secondary immunodeficiency, e.g., diabetes or chronic steroid use.
  • Unusual infection sites: Disseminated infections or abscesses in rare locations suggest immunodeficiency.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Immunodeficiency"][/sc]
  • Opportunistic infections: Indicates profound immune suppression (e.g., Pneumocystis jirovecii pneumonia in HIV).
  • Severe or recurrent infections: Suggests underlying immunodeficiency.
  • Failure to respond to standard treatment: Indicates possible underlying immune dysfunction.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Immunodeficiency"][/sc]
  • Mild infections: In early or mild immunodeficiencies, common infections may occur but respond normally to treatment.
  • Autoimmune diseases: Paradoxically seen in some immunodeficiencies due to immune dysregulation (e.g., CVID).
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Immunodeficiency"][/sc]
  • Full blood count (FBC): May show lymphopenia or neutropenia depending on the type of immunodeficiency.
  • Serum immunoglobulins: Low IgG, IgA, or IgM suggest primary immunodeficiency.
  • HIV testing: To rule out secondary immunodeficiency.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Immunodeficiency"][/sc]
  • A 4-year-old male with a history of recurrent bacterial pneumonias, chronic diarrhoea, and failure to thrive, presenting with a new episode of otitis media.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Immunodeficiency"][/sc]
  • Cystic fibrosis: Distinguished by pulmonary and pancreatic insufficiency signs.
  • Leukaemia: Considered if pancytopenia or blast cells are present on FBC.
  • Allergies or asthma: Generally lack recurrent systemic infections.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Immunodeficiency"][/sc]
  • Antibiotics: Empirical or targeted therapy for infections.
  • Immunoglobulin replacement therapy: For conditions like CVID or XLA.
  • Bone marrow transplant: Curative for severe conditions like SCID.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Immunodeficiency"][/sc]
  • Chronic infections: May lead to organ damage (e.g., bronchiectasis).
  • Autoimmune disease: Occurs in conditions like CVID due to immune dysregulation.
  • Malignancies: Increased risk of lymphoma or leukaemia in immunodeficient states.
