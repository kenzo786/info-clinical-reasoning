Post-Concussion Headache
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Post-Concussion Headache"][/sc]
  • Post-concussion headache is a headache that occurs following a mild traumatic brain injury (TBI), typically within 7 days of the initial injury. It is part of the post-concussion syndrome and may persist for weeks to months. The headache may resemble a tension-type or migraine-type headache.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Post-Concussion Headache"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Post-Concussion Headache"][/sc]
  • UK GP population: Approximately 10-20% of individuals with mild TBI experience persistent post-concussion headache.
  • UK hospital prevalence: Among hospitalised patients with TBI, about 30-50% report headaches within 1 year.
  • Worldwide prevalence: Studies show that 15-30% of patients develop persistent post-concussion headaches globally.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Post-Concussion Headache"][/sc]
  • 1. Initial injury: Trauma causes biomechanical forces on the brain, leading to neuronal and axonal damage.
  • 2. Inflammation: Inflammatory cascades and neurotransmitter changes occur, sensitising pain pathways.
  • 3. Central sensitisation: Persistent alterations in pain processing pathways, including the trigeminovascular system, result in headache persistence.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Post-Concussion Headache"][/sc]
  • Acute phase: Headaches typically begin within 7 days of the injury and may initially resolve within weeks.
  • Subacute phase: In some individuals, headaches persist for 3 months.
  • Chronic phase: Headaches lasting longer than 3 months are considered chronic, potentially persisting for years.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Post-Concussion Headache"][/sc]
  • Headache (tension-type or migraine-like) - Often the most prominent symptom due to central sensitisation and activation of pain pathways.
  • Nausea - Often associated with migraine-type headaches, linked to brainstem activation.
  • Photophobia - Sensitivity to light due to heightened sensory processing.
  • Phonophobia - Sensitivity to sound due to central nervous system hyperexcitability.
  • Dizziness - Likely related to vestibular system involvement post-injury.
  • Fatigue - Likely due to neuroinflammatory changes and cognitive strain.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Post-Concussion Headache"][/sc]
  • Tenderness in the neck or head muscles - Reflects muscle tension and spasm.
  • Difficulty concentrating (cognitive signs) - May indicate underlying neuronal damage and inflammation.
  • Sleep disturbances - Often secondary to autonomic nervous system dysfunction and pain.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Post-Concussion Headache"][/sc]
  • Worsening headache - May indicate intracranial bleeding or increased intracranial pressure (ICP).
  • Vomiting - Suggests raised ICP or more severe brain injury.
  • Seizures - Can indicate post-traumatic epilepsy or significant brain injury.
  • Focal neurological deficits - May suggest more serious brain pathology such as a subdural haematoma.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Post-Concussion Headache"][/sc]
  • Late-onset headaches - Headaches that begin weeks or months after the injury may be missed as related to concussion.
  • Headaches without typical trauma history - Some patients may not recall a significant trauma, complicating diagnosis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Post-Concussion Headache"][/sc]
  • CT or MRI of the brain - Usually normal but indicated if red flag symptoms are present (e.g., worsening headaches, vomiting) to rule out structural lesions.
  • Neuropsychological testing - May be helpful in assessing cognitive deficits associated with post-concussion syndrome.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Post-Concussion Headache"][/sc]
  • A 35-year-old male presents 2 weeks post-head injury with persistent daily headaches, photophobia, and dizziness, following a fall during a football game.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Post-Concussion Headache"][/sc]
  • Migraine - Typically more episodic, often with a prior history of migraines.
  • Medication-overuse headache - Related to excessive analgesic use, usually presents with a more chronic pattern.
  • Subdural haematoma - Headaches may be associated with focal neurological signs and require urgent imaging.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Post-Concussion Headache"][/sc]
  • Analgesia - Paracetamol or NSAIDs are first-line for headache relief.
  • Amitriptyline or propranolol - May be used for chronic headache management, particularly if migraine-like features are present.
  • Physiotherapy - Recommended for associated neck pain or dizziness.
  • Cognitive behavioural therapy (CBT) - Can be useful in patients with psychological components to their symptoms.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Post-Concussion Headache"][/sc]
  • Chronic daily headaches - Persistence beyond 3 months is common, linked to central sensitisation.
  • Psychological issues - Anxiety, depression, and PTSD can be triggered by persistent symptoms.
  • Reduced quality of life - Ongoing pain and cognitive difficulties can severely impair daily functioning.
