Pancreatitis [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Pancreatitis"][/sc]
Problem representation [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Pancreatitis"][/sc]
  • A 45-year-old male with a history of alcohol use presents with acute, severe epigastric pain radiating to the back, nausea, and vomiting for 24 hours. He is febrile and has an elevated heart rate.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Pancreatitis"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Pancreatitis"][/sc]
  • Pancreatitis refers to the inflammation of the pancreas, commonly caused by gallstones or chronic alcohol use. It can be acute or chronic, with acute pancreatitis characterised by sudden onset of symptoms, and chronic pancreatitis resulting in long-term damage and loss of pancreatic function.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Pancreatitis"][/sc]
  • In the UK, acute pancreatitis has an incidence of around 30 cases per 100,000 per year. Hospital admissions for acute pancreatitis in the UK are increasing, with alcohol and gallstones being the most common causes. Worldwide, incidence rates vary between 13-45 cases per 100,000 annually.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Pancreatitis"][/sc]
  • In acute pancreatitis, digestive enzymes become activated within the pancreas instead of the small intestine, leading to autodigestion and inflammation. The inflammatory cascade leads to tissue damage, necrosis, and systemic inflammatory response. Gallstones cause pancreatic duct obstruction, while alcohol increases pancreatic secretions, triggering enzyme activation.
Time course [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Pancreatitis"][/sc]
  • Acute pancreatitis presents suddenly, usually within hours to days of triggering events (e.g., alcohol consumption, gallstone passage). Mild cases resolve in a few days, but severe cases can result in prolonged hospitalisation with complications like necrosis and infection. Chronic pancreatitis develops over months to years, with episodes of acute exacerbation.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Pancreatitis"][/sc]
Epigastric pain
  • This is the most common symptom (90%). The pain is often severe, constant, and radiates to the back. It results from the inflammation and autodigestion of pancreatic tissue.
Nausea and vomiting
  • Occurs in about 70% of patients. This is due to the proximity of the pancreas to the stomach and its involvement in the systemic inflammatory response.
Fever
  • Fever (50-60%) results from the inflammatory response and may indicate systemic infection or necrosis in severe cases.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Pancreatitis"][/sc]
Tachycardia
  • Common in acute cases due to pain, fever, or hypovolemia.
Abdominal tenderness
  • Palpation reveals tenderness in the epigastric region due to pancreatic inflammation.
Guarding or rigidity
  • Present in severe cases, these findings may suggest peritonitis.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Pancreatitis"][/sc]
  • In older adults or immunocompromised individuals, pancreatitis may present with only mild abdominal discomfort or systemic signs like confusion or sepsis, due to blunted inflammatory responses.
Red flag features [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Pancreatitis"][/sc]
  • Hypotension: May indicate severe dehydration, necrosis, or sepsis.
  • Jaundice: Suggests gallstone pancreatitis with biliary obstruction.
  • Confusion: May be a sign of severe systemic involvement or multi-organ failure.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Pancreatitis"][/sc]
Serum amylase and lipase
  • Both enzymes are elevated in acute pancreatitis. Lipase is more specific and remains elevated for longer. Elevated levels indicate pancreatic inflammation and enzyme leakage into the bloodstream.
CT abdomen
  • CT scan helps assess the severity of pancreatitis, identifying necrosis, fluid collections, or abscesses.
Ultrasound
  • Abdominal ultrasound is essential to evaluate for gallstones, which are a common cause of pancreatitis.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Pancreatitis"][/sc]
  • Biliary colic: Absence of elevated pancreatic enzymes and self-limited pain.
  • Peptic ulcer disease: Pain often related to meals, and lacks pancreatic enzyme elevation.
  • Acute cholecystitis: Pain is more localised to the right upper quadrant, often with a positive Murphy's sign.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Pancreatitis"][/sc]
  • Initial management involves supportive care: fluid resuscitation, pain control (usually with opioids), and bowel rest. In severe cases, ICU care may be needed. Treat underlying causes (e.g., ERCP for gallstone removal). Antibiotics are not routinely used unless infection is present.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Pancreatitis"][/sc]
  • Pancreatic necrosis: Occurs in about 20% of cases and can lead to infection, sepsis, and multi-organ failure.
  • Pseudocyst formation: Occurs in 10-20% of cases and may require drainage if symptomatic or infected.
  • Chronic pancreatitis: Long-term complication from recurrent episodes leading to pancreatic insufficiency.
