Lymphoedema
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Lymphoedema"][/sc]
  • Lymphoedema is a chronic condition characterised by abnormal accumulation of protein-rich interstitial fluid, usually in the limbs, due to impaired lymphatic drainage. It can be primary (due to congenital lymphatic abnormalities) or secondary (resulting from damage to the lymphatic system, such as after surgery, infection, or radiation therapy). This leads to swelling, tissue fibrosis, and an increased risk of infection.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Lymphoedema"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Lymphoedema"][/sc]
  • UK GP Population: Lymphoedema affects about 1.33 per 1,000 patients, often seen in those with a history of cancer treatment or chronic venous insufficiency.
  • UK Hospital Prevalence: Hospital-based studies suggest a prevalence of 1.5–2% among admitted patients, with higher rates in oncology and post-surgical cases.
  • Worldwide Prevalence: Globally, lymphoedema affects up to 250 million people, with the highest incidence in areas endemic to filariasis.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Lymphoedema"][/sc]
  • Lymphoedema develops due to a failure of the lymphatic system to drain interstitial fluid, leading to fluid accumulation in the tissues.
  • Step 1: Impaired lymphatic drainage leads to accumulation of protein-rich fluid in the interstitial space.
  • Step 2: Chronic stasis of this fluid triggers inflammation and activation of fibroblasts, resulting in tissue fibrosis and scarring.
  • Step 3: Long-term lymphatic stasis can lead to adipose tissue deposition and further structural changes in the skin and subcutaneous tissues, including hyperkeratosis and papillomatosis.
  • Step 4: The protein-rich fluid creates a fertile environment for bacterial growth, increasing susceptibility to infections such as cellulitis.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Lymphoedema"][/sc]
  • Early stage: Intermittent swelling, often resolving with rest and elevation. Skin and tissues remain soft.
  • Intermediate stage: Persistent swelling, tissue fibrosis begins, pitting oedema may reduce.
  • Late stage: Severe fibrosis and skin changes, such as hyperkeratosis and papillomatosis. Swelling becomes non-pitting and irreversible.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Lymphoedema"][/sc]
  • Swelling of the affected limb(s) - Typically starts intermittently but progresses to persistent swelling. Pathophysiology: Impaired lymphatic drainage leads to fluid accumulation.
  • Heaviness or discomfort - The limb feels heavy or tight due to excess fluid accumulation and tissue fibrosis.
  • Reduced mobility or flexibility - Swelling and fibrosis may limit joint movement, particularly in severe or chronic cases.
  • Skin tightness - Stretching of the skin due to oedema leads to a sensation of tightness.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Lymphoedema"][/sc]
  • Non-pitting oedema - Swelling that does not indent when pressure is applied, due to chronic tissue fibrosis.
  • Skin thickening (hyperkeratosis) - Prolonged lymphoedema leads to fibrosis and structural skin changes.
  • Stemmer’s sign - Inability to pinch the skin on the dorsal surface of the toes or fingers, indicating lymphoedema.
  • Recurrent cellulitis - Frequent skin infections due to stagnant protein-rich fluid, which promotes bacterial growth.
  • Papillomatosis - Wart-like skin projections due to chronic inflammation and lymphatic stasis.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Lymphoedema"][/sc]
  • Sudden onset of swelling - May indicate a deep vein thrombosis (DVT) or malignancy. Requires urgent investigation.
  • Recurrent cellulitis - Can lead to severe systemic infection or chronic lymphatic damage.
  • Rapid worsening of oedema - Could signify tumour recurrence in cancer patients or a secondary obstruction.
  • Pain - Lymphoedema is typically painless; new or worsening pain suggests infection or malignancy.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Lymphoedema"][/sc]
  • Swelling in unusual locations - Lymphoedema typically affects the limbs but may present in the face, genitalia, or abdomen, particularly in congenital cases.
  • Asymmetrical swelling - In primary lymphoedema, one limb may be affected years before the other, leading to diagnostic delays.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Lymphoedema"][/sc]
  • Doppler ultrasound - To exclude venous thrombosis and assess venous insufficiency. Findings: Absence of DVT or venous obstruction.
  • Lymphoscintigraphy - Nuclear medicine imaging that evaluates lymphatic drainage. Findings: Delayed or absent lymphatic uptake and transport.
  • Bioimpedance spectroscopy - Measures extracellular fluid to detect early lymphoedema. Findings: Increased impedance indicates fluid accumulation.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Lymphoedema"][/sc]
  • A 55-year-old woman with a history of breast cancer presents with progressive swelling in her left arm, heaviness, and reduced mobility. Examination reveals non-pitting oedema and skin thickening, consistent with secondary lymphoedema post-axillary lymph node dissection.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Lymphoedema"][/sc]
  • Chronic venous insufficiency - Swelling is typically pitting, often improves with elevation, and associated with varicose veins. Lymphoedema causes non-pitting oedema and does not improve with elevation.
  • Deep vein thrombosis (DVT) - Usually causes acute swelling with pain and erythema. Doppler ultrasound can confirm the presence of DVT, whereas lymphoedema progresses slowly and is painless.
  • Lipodema - Affects primarily the lower limbs, with symmetric fat distribution. Unlike lymphoedema, it spares the feet and does not cause non-pitting oedema.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Lymphoedema"][/sc]
  • Compression therapy - The cornerstone of treatment involves using compression bandages or garments to promote lymphatic drainage.
  • Manual lymphatic drainage (MLD) - A specialised form of massage that encourages lymph flow.
  • Exercise - Regular physical activity helps to improve lymphatic flow and reduce oedema.
  • Skin care - To prevent infections and maintain skin integrity, particularly in those at risk of cellulitis.
  • Antibiotics - For treating recurrent cellulitis or infections.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Lymphoedema"][/sc]
  • Recurrent cellulitis - Inflammation and infection of the skin due to stagnant protein-rich lymph fluid, leading to potential sepsis.
  • Lymphangiosarcoma - Rare but aggressive cancer associated with long-standing lymphoedema. Presents as purplish skin lesions and has a poor prognosis.
  • Fibrosis and immobility - Advanced lymphoedema can cause tissue fibrosis, resulting in significant functional impairment.
