Post-Operative Infection
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Post-Operative Infection"][/sc]
  • Post-operative infection refers to an infection occurring at or near a surgical site within 30 days of surgery (or up to 90 days if an implant is placed). These infections can involve the incision site (superficial or deep) or an organ/space associated with the procedure.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Post-Operative Infection"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Post-Operative Infection"][/sc]
  • In the UK, surgical site infections (SSIs) affect approximately 5% of patients undergoing surgery, with variations by type of surgery.
  • Hospital prevalence studies indicate SSIs account for 15-20% of all healthcare-associated infections.
  • Globally, rates range from 2-25% depending on surgical and healthcare settings, particularly higher in low-resource settings.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Post-Operative Infection"][/sc]
  • Surgical trauma disrupts the skin barrier, providing an entry point for pathogens.
  • Contamination of the wound during surgery, inadequate sterilisation, or pre-existing colonisation can lead to infection.
  • Host factors like impaired immunity, diabetes, and poor nutritional status increase susceptibility.
  • Pathogens multiply locally, causing an inflammatory response, tissue necrosis, and potential abscess formation.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Post-Operative Infection"][/sc]
  • Immediate (within hours): Often due to contaminated surgical tools or severe immunosuppression.
  • Early (1-7 days): Usually bacterial infection from Staphylococcus aureus or streptococci.
  • Late (7-30 days): May involve slower-growing organisms like coagulase-negative staphylococci.
  • Chronic (>30 days): Often related to implants, biofilms, or atypical organisms.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Post-Operative Infection"][/sc]
  • Fever - Resulting from systemic inflammatory response to bacterial toxins and cytokine release.
  • Localised pain - Indicative of tissue inflammation and infection.
  • Redness and swelling - Due to vasodilation and increased vascular permeability in the affected area.
  • Purulent discharge - Caused by accumulation of neutrophils and bacterial debris.
  • Malaise - Systemic manifestation of infection-induced inflammatory mediators.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Post-Operative Infection"][/sc]
  • Erythema - Reflects local inflammatory response with increased blood flow.
  • Swelling - Resulting from oedema caused by capillary leakage.
  • Wound dehiscence - May signify underlying infection weakening the tissue.
  • Tenderness - Indicative of inflammation and nerve irritation.
  • Systemic signs (e.g., tachycardia, hypotension) - Suggest systemic spread or sepsis.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Post-Operative Infection"][/sc]
  • Sepsis - Tachycardia, hypotension, or altered mental state indicating systemic infection.
  • Necrotising fasciitis - Rapidly spreading erythema with disproportionate pain, suggesting a life-threatening soft tissue infection.
  • Persistent fever - May indicate deep or systemic infection requiring urgent evaluation.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Post-Operative Infection"][/sc]
  • Chronic discharge without overt erythema - Common in implant-associated infections.
  • Low-grade fever or fatigue - May be the only manifestation in immunosuppressed patients.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Post-Operative Infection"][/sc]
  • Wound culture - Identifies causative organisms, guiding antibiotic therapy.
  • Full blood count - Raised white cell count indicative of systemic inflammation.
  • CRP/ESR - Elevated in response to acute or chronic inflammation.
  • Imaging (e.g., ultrasound, CT) - Useful for detecting abscesses or deep infections.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Post-Operative Infection"][/sc]
  • A 65-year-old male, day 7 post-abdominal surgery, presents with fever, localised pain, erythema, and purulent wound discharge.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Post-Operative Infection"][/sc]
  • Cellulitis - Diffuse erythema without purulent discharge.
  • Deep vein thrombosis - Swelling and pain without erythema or fever.
  • Seroma - Clear fluid accumulation without signs of infection.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Post-Operative Infection"][/sc]
  • Antibiotics - Based on local guidelines and culture results; empirical therapy often includes flucloxacillin or co-amoxiclav.
  • Surgical debridement - Essential for removing infected or necrotic tissue in severe cases.
  • Supportive care - Fluid resuscitation and analgesia as needed.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Post-Operative Infection"][/sc]
  • Sepsis - May lead to multi-organ failure and death.
  • Chronic wound infection - Results in delayed healing and functional impairment.
  • Scarring - Long-term cosmetic or functional issues.
