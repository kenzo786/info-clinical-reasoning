Iritis (Uveitis)
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Iritis (Uveitis)"][/sc]
  • Iritis, a subtype of anterior uveitis, is the inflammation of the iris, which forms the anterior portion of the uvea. It is characterised by redness, pain, photophobia, and potentially reduced vision. Iritis can occur in isolation or as part of systemic inflammatory diseases.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Iritis (Uveitis)"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Iritis (Uveitis)"][/sc]
  • Prevalence in UK GP population: Approximately 1 case per 1,000 patients annually.
  • Prevalence in UK hospitals: Accounts for around 10–20% of uveitis cases.
  • Worldwide prevalence: Estimated at 17–52 cases per 100,000 population annually, varying by region and population demographics.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Iritis (Uveitis)"][/sc]
  • Disruption of the blood-aqueous barrier causes immune-mediated inflammation within the anterior chamber.
  • Release of cytokines (e.g., TNF-α, IL-6) promotes leukocyte recruitment and iris stromal oedema.
  • Breakdown of ocular tissues leads to protein leakage, manifesting as aqueous flare, and aggregation of inflammatory cells into keratic precipitates.
  • Chronic or recurrent inflammation can result in complications like synechiae, cataracts, or glaucoma.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Iritis (Uveitis)"][/sc]
  • Acute phase: Onset within hours to days, often resolving within weeks with treatment.
  • Chronic or recurrent phase: May persist or recur over months to years, requiring long-term management.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Iritis (Uveitis)"][/sc]
  • Eye pain - Due to ciliary muscle spasm and inflammation of adjacent structures.
  • Redness - Caused by dilation of conjunctival and episcleral vessels.
  • Photophobia - Reflex irritation of the iris and ciliary body triggered by light.
  • Blurred vision - Result of inflammatory cells and proteins in the aqueous humour.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Iritis (Uveitis)"][/sc]
  • Conjunctival injection - Prominent around the limbus (ciliary flush) due to vascular dilation.
  • Anterior chamber cells - Inflammatory cells seen on slit-lamp examination, indicative of active inflammation.
  • Hypopyon - Layering of white blood cells in the anterior chamber in severe cases.
  • Keratic precipitates - Aggregates of inflammatory cells on the corneal endothelium.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Iritis (Uveitis)"][/sc]
  • Severe vision loss - May indicate retinal involvement or optic nerve complications.
  • Persistent inflammation - Suggests underlying systemic disease or inadequate treatment.
  • Ocular hypertension - Potential progression to secondary glaucoma.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Iritis (Uveitis)"][/sc]
  • Insidious onset with minimal symptoms, common in chronic or low-grade inflammation.
  • Painless presentation, occasionally seen in immunocompromised individuals.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Iritis (Uveitis)"][/sc]
  • Slit-lamp examination - Detects anterior chamber cells and flare.
  • Intraocular pressure measurement - Elevated in secondary glaucoma or decreased in acute iritis.
  • Fundus examination - Excludes posterior involvement.
  • Laboratory tests - HLA-B27 typing, syphilis serology, and tuberculosis screening in systemic cases.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Iritis (Uveitis)"][/sc]
  • A 35-year-old male presents with a 3-day history of unilateral eye pain, photophobia, and blurred vision. No significant systemic symptoms reported.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Iritis (Uveitis)"][/sc]
  • Conjunctivitis - Lacks photophobia and anterior chamber cells.
  • Scleritis - More severe pain, tenderness, and episcleral thickening.
  • Acute angle-closure glaucoma - Markedly elevated intraocular pressure, nausea, and headache.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Iritis (Uveitis)"][/sc]
  • Topical corticosteroids (e.g., prednisolone acetate) to reduce inflammation.
  • Cycloplegics (e.g., atropine) to relieve pain and prevent synechiae.
  • Systemic therapy - Immunosuppressive agents in refractory or systemic cases.
  • Monitoring - Regular intraocular pressure checks during corticosteroid use.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Iritis (Uveitis)"][/sc]
  • Synechiae - Adhesions of the iris to the lens or cornea, causing irregular pupil shape.
  • Cataracts - Due to chronic inflammation or prolonged corticosteroid use.
  • Glaucoma - Secondary to inflammation or steroid-induced pressure elevation.
