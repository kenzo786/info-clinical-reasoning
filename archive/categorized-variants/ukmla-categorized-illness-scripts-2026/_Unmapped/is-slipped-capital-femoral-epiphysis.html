Slipped Capital Femoral Epiphysis (SCFE)
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Slipped Capital Femoral Epiphysis (SCFE)"][/sc]
  • Slipped capital femoral epiphysis (SCFE) is a condition in which the proximal femoral epiphysis (growth plate) slips posteriorly and inferiorly relative to the femoral neck. It typically occurs during adolescence, most often in overweight children or those undergoing rapid growth. It is considered an orthopaedic emergency due to the risk of avascular necrosis of the femoral head.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Slipped Capital Femoral Epiphysis (SCFE)"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Slipped Capital Femoral Epiphysis (SCFE)"][/sc]
  • UK GP prevalence: SCFE is relatively rare but more common among adolescents, particularly those with obesity. Exact GP prevalence is estimated at 2-3 per 100,000.
  • UK hospital prevalence: SCFE is one of the most common hip disorders in adolescents, with higher incidence rates in secondary care settings.
  • Worldwide prevalence: Approximately 10.8 per 100,000 in the United States, with similar rates in other developed countries.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Slipped Capital Femoral Epiphysis (SCFE)"][/sc]
  • The primary mechanism involves weakness in the proximal femoral growth plate, which is less stable during periods of rapid growth or hormonal changes, making it susceptible to mechanical stress and shear forces.
  • Endocrine factors, such as hypothyroidism, obesity, or growth hormone abnormalities, may also contribute to growth plate instability.
  • The epiphysis "slips" off the femoral neck due to these forces, resulting in malalignment and impaired blood supply to the femoral head, risking avascular necrosis.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Slipped Capital Femoral Epiphysis (SCFE)"][/sc]
  • Acute: Sudden onset of hip or knee pain with an inability to bear weight, typically following minor trauma.
  • Chronic: More gradual onset of pain over weeks or months, often presenting with a limp and intermittent discomfort.
  • Acute-on-chronic: Symptoms gradually worsen until a specific incident causes a significant increase in pain and functional impairment.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Slipped Capital Femoral Epiphysis (SCFE)"][/sc]
  • Hip pain - The most common presenting symptom, often due to inflammation and abnormal pressure on the joint surfaces.
  • Knee pain - Referred pain to the knee is frequent, as the obturator nerve can transmit hip joint pain to the knee.
  • Limping or difficulty walking - Affected children may adopt an antalgic gait due to pain and instability of the femoral epiphysis.
  • Stiffness and reduced range of motion - Results from synovial irritation and mechanical restriction due to the slipped epiphysis.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Slipped Capital Femoral Epiphysis (SCFE)"][/sc]
  • Restricted internal rotation of the hip - Due to mechanical displacement and inflammation limiting joint mobility.
  • Obligate external rotation - The leg often externally rotates when flexed, as this position minimises strain on the femoral epiphysis.
  • Positive Trendelenburg sign - Indicative of weakness in hip abductors or joint instability caused by the slipped epiphysis.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Slipped Capital Femoral Epiphysis (SCFE)"][/sc]
  • Inability to bear weight - Signifies possible severe slippage or avascular necrosis, requiring urgent orthopaedic intervention.
  • Sudden worsening of pain - May indicate an acute slip in a chronic SCFE, increasing the risk of complications like avascular necrosis.
  • Leg length discrepancy - Suggestive of progressive slippage, leading to deformity and functional impairment.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Slipped Capital Femoral Epiphysis (SCFE)"][/sc]
  • Painless limp - Some children may present with minimal or no pain but have significant mechanical symptoms.
  • Bilateral hip involvement - Although SCFE is typically unilateral, bilateral cases may be seen, particularly in children with underlying endocrine disorders.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Slipped Capital Femoral Epiphysis (SCFE)"][/sc]
  • X-ray (AP and frog-leg lateral views) - Diagnostic imaging showing displacement of the femoral head relative to the femoral neck; characteristic “ice cream slipping off a cone” appearance.
  • MRI (if X-ray inconclusive) - May show early changes such as oedema in the growth plate, useful in atypical or early presentations.
  • Endocrine evaluation - Recommended in cases with atypical presentations or bilateral SCFE to rule out hormonal abnormalities.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Slipped Capital Femoral Epiphysis (SCFE)"][/sc]
  • An overweight 13-year-old boy with a 2-month history of progressive left hip and knee pain, worsened by activity and accompanied by a noticeable limp.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Slipped Capital Femoral Epiphysis (SCFE)"][/sc]
  • Hip fracture - Often has a history of acute trauma with more abrupt onset of pain.
  • Legg-Calvé-Perthes disease - Typically presents in younger children with gradual onset of hip pain and limp.
  • Juvenile idiopathic arthritis - May present with hip pain but is typically associated with systemic symptoms and inflammation in other joints.
  • Transient synovitis - Presents acutely with hip pain but usually resolves within 1-2 weeks.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Slipped Capital Femoral Epiphysis (SCFE)"][/sc]
  • Immediate referral to orthopaedics - SCFE is managed as an orthopaedic emergency to prevent further slippage and avascular necrosis.
  • Surgical fixation - In situ pinning is the standard treatment, stabilising the femoral head to prevent further displacement.
  • Weight-bearing restrictions - Patients are typically advised to limit weight-bearing post-operatively to allow healing and reduce strain on the growth plate.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Slipped Capital Femoral Epiphysis (SCFE)"][/sc]
  • Avascular necrosis of the femoral head - Occurs if blood supply is compromised, leading to long-term disability if untreated.
  • Chondrolysis - Loss of articular cartilage can lead to chronic pain and stiffness, sometimes requiring further surgical intervention.
  • Leg length discrepancy - Progressive slippage may result in leg length differences, impacting gait and function.
