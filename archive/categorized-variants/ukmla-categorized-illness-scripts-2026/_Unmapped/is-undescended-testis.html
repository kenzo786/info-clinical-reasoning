Undescended Testis
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Undescended Testis"][/sc]
  • An undescended testis, also known as cryptorchidism, is a condition in which one or both testes fail to descend into the scrotum, remaining in the abdomen, inguinal canal, or upper scrotum. It is most commonly diagnosed in newborns and typically resolves spontaneously within the first few months of life, although some cases may persist.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Undescended Testis"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Undescended Testis"][/sc]
  • UK GP prevalence: Cryptorchidism affects approximately 2-5% of full-term male infants and up to 30% of premature male infants.
  • UK hospital prevalence: Of boys born with undescended testes, around 1-2% still have the condition at one year of age.
  • Worldwide prevalence: Global rates are similar, though prevalence may vary based on prematurity rates and access to early paediatric care.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Undescended Testis"][/sc]
  • Testicular descent is a complex process influenced by hormonal, mechanical, and genetic factors. The descent is divided into two main phases: the transabdominal phase, guided by the gubernaculum, and the inguinoscrotal phase, largely driven by androgens and the genitofemoral nerve.
  • Failure of descent can occur due to disruptions in hormonal pathways (e.g., testosterone deficiency), anatomical abnormalities (e.g., patent processus vaginalis), or genetic factors, leading to retention of the testis in the abdominal or inguinal region.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Undescended Testis"][/sc]
  • Neonatal Period: Undescended testes are identified during newborn examination, with many cases descending spontaneously by 3-6 months.
  • Persistent Cases: Testes that remain undescended beyond 6-12 months are less likely to descend without intervention and may require surgical correction.
  • Long-term: If untreated, cryptorchidism can lead to complications such as infertility and an increased risk of testicular malignancy.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Undescended Testis"][/sc]
  • Asymptomatic - Undescended testes are often detected incidentally during routine examination without causing noticeable symptoms, as the primary concern is the location rather than any immediate discomfort or pain.
  • Fertility Concerns in Adolescents/Adults - In longstanding cases, undescended testes may be associated with reduced fertility due to prolonged exposure to higher intra-abdominal temperatures.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Undescended Testis"][/sc]
  • Non-palpable or Ectopic Testis - Absence of the testis in the scrotum during physical examination, which may be located higher in the inguinal canal or within the abdomen.
  • Inguinal Fullness or Mass - In some cases, an undescended testis may be felt as a small mass in the inguinal area, particularly in the inguinal phase.
  • Retractile Testis - Some testes may move up temporarily but can be manipulated down into the scrotum; this should be differentiated from truly undescended testes.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Undescended Testis"][/sc]
  • Bilateral Non-palpable Testes - This raises concern for an underlying endocrine disorder, such as congenital adrenal hyperplasia, requiring immediate endocrinological evaluation.
  • Acute Pain or Tenderness - Sudden onset of pain may indicate testicular torsion, which is a urological emergency.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Undescended Testis"][/sc]
  • Retractile Testis - This is often confused with undescended testis as the testis is not consistently present in the scrotum but can be manually positioned; it usually resolves with growth and does not require surgery.
  • Cryptorchidism in Older Children or Adolescents - If diagnosed later in life, it may be associated with hormonal disorders or acquired undescended testis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Undescended Testis"][/sc]
  • Physical Examination - Palpation of the scrotum and inguinal regions to locate the testis, often the primary diagnostic method.
  • Ultrasound - Used to locate non-palpable testes, though not always necessary unless physical exam is inconclusive.
  • Laparoscopy - Direct visualisation in cases of non-palpable testes to identify intra-abdominal testes, often both diagnostic and therapeutic.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Undescended Testis"][/sc]
  • A 3-month-old male infant is brought in for a routine check-up; examination reveals a non-palpable right testis, suggesting an undescended testis likely requiring further observation or intervention if persistence continues.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Undescended Testis"][/sc]
  • Retractile Testis - Testis can be moved into the scrotum; unlike cryptorchidism, it does not require surgery.
  • Inguinal Hernia - May present as an inguinal mass; however, a hernia is usually reducible, and transillumination may reveal fluid.
  • Testicular Agenesis - Absence of the testis, confirmed through imaging or surgical exploration; requires no surgical repositioning but may need endocrine follow-up.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Undescended Testis"][/sc]
  • Observation - Recommended for infants under 6 months as spontaneous descent may occur.
  • Orchiopexy - Surgical repositioning of the testis, ideally performed between 6 and 12 months if descent has not occurred, to reduce risks of infertility and malignancy.
  • Hormone Therapy - Rarely used in the UK, though sometimes considered for stimulation in select cases.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Undescended Testis"][/sc]
  • Infertility - Higher temperatures in undescended testis impair spermatogenesis; occurs in untreated bilateral cases more frequently.
  • Testicular Cancer - Increased risk of germ cell tumours in undescended testis, even after orchiopexy.
  • Torsion of Undescended Testis - Increased risk of torsion in undescended testes, potentially leading to loss of testicular tissue.
