Trauma-Induced Bleeding and Bruising
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Trauma-Induced Bleeding and Bruising"][/sc]
  • Trauma-induced bleeding and bruising is characterised by the leakage of blood into tissues or body cavities following an external force or trauma. This condition results from the rupture of small blood vessels, such as capillaries, veins, or arteries, which may occur at different levels of severity depending on the force applied and individual factors affecting bleeding risk (e.g., clotting disorders).
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Trauma-Induced Bleeding and Bruising"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Trauma-Induced Bleeding and Bruising"][/sc]
  • In the UK general practice population, minor trauma-induced bruising is common, while more severe cases requiring hospitalisation are less frequent.
  • Approximately 1 in 1,000 UK hospital admissions involve trauma-associated bleeding, often from falls, sports injuries, or road traffic accidents.
  • Worldwide, trauma-induced bleeding accounts for significant morbidity and is a leading cause of preventable death in trauma patients.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Trauma-Induced Bleeding and Bruising"][/sc]
  • Upon trauma, mechanical force damages small blood vessels, causing blood to leak into surrounding tissues, resulting in haematomas or bruising.
  • The release of blood components into tissues triggers an inflammatory response, leading to swelling, warmth, and pain.
  • If clotting is impaired or delayed (e.g., in patients with clotting disorders or on anticoagulants), bleeding may persist or recur, increasing risk of haematoma expansion.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Trauma-Induced Bleeding and Bruising"][/sc]
  • Acute Phase: Bruising or bleeding appears immediately following trauma, evolving in colour as haemoglobin breaks down in tissues.
  • Subacute Phase: Swelling and pain may persist over days to a week, with bruising changing from blue/purple to green/yellow.
  • Resolution Phase: Bruising gradually fades over 2–3 weeks as haematomas are absorbed by the body.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Trauma-Induced Bleeding and Bruising"][/sc]
  • Pain - Caused by tissue injury and inflammatory response, which stimulates local nerve endings.
  • Swelling - Due to accumulation of blood and inflammatory fluid at the site of injury.
  • Visible Bruising - Discolouration occurs as blood leaks into tissues and undergoes breakdown; this is commonly the earliest and most visible sign.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Trauma-Induced Bleeding and Bruising"][/sc]
  • Ecchymosis - Large, discoloured patches often indicating deeper bleeding into subcutaneous tissue.
  • Haematoma - Localised swelling with a palpable mass, suggesting a collection of clotted blood in tissue.
  • Warmth at the Site - Reflects inflammatory response to the injury, which increases blood flow to the affected area.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Trauma-Induced Bleeding and Bruising"][/sc]
  • Uncontrolled or Persistent Bleeding - May indicate clotting disorder or vascular injury.
  • Large, Expanding Haematomas - Suggests possible vascular injury that may lead to compartment syndrome.
  • Bleeding in Atypical Sites - Such as spontaneous bruising or bleeding in the absence of trauma, which may signify an underlying haematological disorder.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Trauma-Induced Bleeding and Bruising"][/sc]
  • Bruising without Trauma - Often seen in patients on anticoagulants or with haematological disorders.
  • Delayed Bruising - Common in elderly or those with connective tissue disorders where vessel fragility leads to delayed symptom onset.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Trauma-Induced Bleeding and Bruising"][/sc]
  • Full Blood Count (FBC) - Checks for anaemia or low platelets, which could indicate bleeding severity or haematological conditions.
  • Coagulation Profile - Includes PT, aPTT, and INR to assess clotting function and rule out bleeding disorders.
  • Imaging (Ultrasound or CT) - Used in cases of suspected internal bleeding to locate and assess haematoma or vascular injury.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Trauma-Induced Bleeding and Bruising"][/sc]
  • A 45-year-old male presents with a 2-day history of painful swelling and bruising on the left thigh following a football injury, worsening over time with difficulty weight-bearing.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Trauma-Induced Bleeding and Bruising"][/sc]
  • Spontaneous Bruising - Lacks history of trauma and often associated with underlying conditions such as liver disease or clotting disorders.
  • Muscle Strain - Typically presents without significant ecchymosis but with focal muscle tenderness and weakness.
  • Deep Vein Thrombosis (DVT) - Presents with unilateral limb swelling and warmth but typically lacks external bruising unless complicated by haematoma.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Trauma-Induced Bleeding and Bruising"][/sc]
  • First-line: RICE Protocol (Rest, Ice, Compression, Elevation) - Recommended to reduce swelling and pain for minor bruising.
  • Analgesia (Paracetamol or NSAIDs) - For pain management, with caution if bleeding risk is increased.
  • Surgical Intervention - May be required for large, expanding haematomas or in cases of vascular injury.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Trauma-Induced Bleeding and Bruising"][/sc]
  • Compartment Syndrome - Risk in large haematomas, where pressure may compromise blood flow and tissue viability.
  • Infection - Haematomas can occasionally become infected, especially if adjacent to skin abrasions.
  • Permanent Scarring or Skin Discolouration - May result from large bruises or recurrent trauma in the same area.
