Nasopharyngeal Cancer illness script
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Nasopharyngeal Cancer"][/sc]
  • Nasopharyngeal cancer (NPC) is a malignant tumour that arises from the epithelial lining of the nasopharynx, the uppermost part of the throat behind the nose. It is a distinct subtype of head and neck cancers, with squamous cell carcinoma being the most common histological type.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Nasopharyngeal Cancer"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Nasopharyngeal Cancer"][/sc]
  • UK GP population prevalence: NPC is rare, with an incidence rate of around 0.5 per 100,000 people.
  • UK hospital prevalence: Higher incidence in certain subpopulations (e.g., those of Southeast Asian descent) with a focus in tertiary cancer centres.
  • Worldwide prevalence: NPC is much more common in certain regions, particularly Southern China, Southeast Asia, and parts of North Africa, with an incidence as high as 25-50 per 100,000 people.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Nasopharyngeal Cancer"][/sc]
  • NPC typically originates in the epithelial cells of the nasopharynx. Its development is linked to a combination of genetic susceptibility, environmental factors, and Epstein-Barr Virus (EBV) infection. EBV infection leads to the transformation of infected nasopharyngeal epithelial cells into malignant cells. Genetic predisposition and consumption of preserved foods (high in nitrosamines) also play a role in tumour development.
  • The tumour invades local structures such as the nasal cavity, oropharynx, and skull base, and can metastasise to cervical lymph nodes and distant organs.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Nasopharyngeal Cancer"][/sc]
  • Early stage: NPC often presents asymptomatically or with mild, non-specific symptoms such as nasal obstruction or a painless neck mass (due to lymphadenopathy).
  • Advanced stage: Tumour growth leads to symptoms related to local invasion (e.g., nasal bleeding, hearing loss, cranial nerve palsies) and distant metastases (e.g., bone pain, respiratory symptoms).
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Nasopharyngeal Cancer"][/sc]
  • Painless neck mass - Often the initial presentation due to lymphatic spread; occurs in approximately 75% of cases. The nasopharynx has rich lymphatic drainage, leading to cervical lymphadenopathy.
  • Nasal obstruction - Caused by tumour expansion into the nasal cavity.
  • Nasal bleeding (epistaxis) - Tumour erosion of blood vessels leads to spontaneous bleeding from the nose.
  • Tinnitus or hearing loss - Eustachian tube blockage from tumour invasion may cause conductive hearing loss and tinnitus, especially in unilateral cases.
  • Headaches - Skull base involvement and intracranial invasion can result in headaches due to increased pressure or nerve irritation.
  • Cranial nerve palsies - Involvement of cranial nerves (III, IV, V, VI) leads to facial pain, numbness, diplopia, or dysphagia, depending on the nerve affected.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Nasopharyngeal Cancer"][/sc]
  • Cervical lymphadenopathy - Enlarged, firm, non-tender lymph nodes, often unilateral, due to early regional metastasis.
  • Nasal mass or discharge - Visible mass on nasoendoscopy or mucopurulent discharge from tumour involvement.
  • Hearing loss (conductive) - Secondary to Eustachian tube dysfunction caused by tumour invasion, leading to fluid build-up in the middle ear.
  • Facial numbness - Involvement of the trigeminal nerve (CN V) from skull base invasion.
  • Ocular signs - Diplopia or ptosis due to cranial nerve III, IV, or VI involvement.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Nasopharyngeal Cancer"][/sc]
  • Cranial nerve palsies - Indicates advanced local invasion into the skull base and nerves; may signal inoperability or the need for aggressive therapy.
  • Persistent unilateral hearing loss - Suggests Eustachian tube blockage and possible tumour extension into the ear canal.
  • Epistaxis with a neck mass - Strong indicator of nasopharyngeal malignancy and should prompt urgent investigation.
  • Bone pain - May signify distant metastases, particularly to the bones, and indicates advanced disease.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Nasopharyngeal Cancer"][/sc]
  • Asymptomatic - Some patients, particularly in early stages, may only present with cervical lymphadenopathy, which can delay diagnosis.
  • Unilateral ear symptoms without other nasopharyngeal symptoms - Isolated ear pain or hearing loss without nasal or throat symptoms can lead to misdiagnosis as an ear infection or otitis media.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Nasopharyngeal Cancer"][/sc]
  • Nasoendoscopy - Direct visualisation of the nasopharynx to identify any tumour or suspicious lesions.
  • Biopsy - Histopathological confirmation of malignancy; typically shows non-keratinising squamous cell carcinoma.
  • EBV serology - EBV DNA or antibody titres (e.g., EBV viral capsid antigen) may support the diagnosis as EBV is associated with NPC.
  • MRI of the head and neck - Used to assess the extent of the tumour, including local invasion into the skull base and nerves.
  • CT of chest, abdomen, pelvis - Staging investigation to detect distant metastases.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Nasopharyngeal Cancer"][/sc]
  • A 50-year-old male of Southeast Asian descent presents with a 3-month history of progressive unilateral neck swelling, nasal obstruction, and intermittent epistaxis. He also reports hearing loss in the left ear and headaches over the past month.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Nasopharyngeal Cancer"][/sc]
  • Lymphoma - Lymphadenopathy without significant nasopharyngeal symptoms; lacks the EBV association and local nasopharyngeal tumour mass.
  • Chronic rhinosinusitis - Causes nasal obstruction and discharge, but typically lacks cervical lymphadenopathy and has a more inflammatory rather than malignant presentation.
  • Adenoid hypertrophy - Common in children and may present similarly to NPC, but is benign and not associated with the same systemic symptoms or lymphadenopathy.
  • Wegener's granulomatosis (GPA) - Can cause nasal involvement and systemic symptoms, but typically shows vasculitis and granulomas on biopsy, and there may be renal involvement.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Nasopharyngeal Cancer"][/sc]
  • Radiotherapy - Mainstay of treatment for NPC, especially in early-stage disease. Intensity-modulated radiotherapy (IMRT) is preferred for precise targeting.
  • Chemoradiotherapy - Used in locally advanced disease, combining radiation with cisplatin-based chemotherapy to enhance tumour control.
  • Adjuvant chemotherapy - May be used in more advanced stages to reduce the risk of metastasis.
  • Follow-up - Regular imaging (e.g., MRI, PET-CT) and EBV DNA monitoring are used post-treatment to detect recurrence.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Nasopharyngeal Cancer"][/sc]
  • Metastasis - NPC commonly metastasises to cervical lymph nodes, bones, lungs, and liver. Metastasis is present in up to 10% of newly diagnosed cases.
  • Cranial nerve damage - Tumour invasion into the skull base can cause permanent nerve damage, leading to facial paralysis or diplopia.
  • Radiation-induced damage - Xerostomia, hearing loss, and damage to nearby tissues are common side effects of radiotherapy.
  • Recurrence - Recurrence rates are significant, particularly in locally advanced disease, and require ongoing surveillance.
