Mesenteric Ischaemia
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Mesenteric Ischaemia"][/sc]
  • Mesenteric ischaemia refers to a reduction in blood flow to the small intestine or colon, resulting in tissue hypoxia and potential necrosis. It can be acute or chronic, with acute mesenteric ischaemia being a surgical emergency. Causes include arterial embolism, thrombosis, and non-occlusive mechanisms (e.g., hypotension). Chronic mesenteric ischaemia typically results from progressive atherosclerosis of the mesenteric arteries.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Mesenteric Ischaemia"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Mesenteric Ischaemia"][/sc]
  • Prevalence in the UK GP population: Rare, with chronic cases often underdiagnosed.
  • UK hospital prevalence: Acute mesenteric ischaemia accounts for around 1 in 1000 hospital admissions. Chronic cases are more frequent in older adults with cardiovascular risk factors.
  • Worldwide prevalence: Similar trends worldwide, more common in developed countries with an aging population and high rates of atherosclerotic disease.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Mesenteric Ischaemia"][/sc]
  • Mesenteric ischaemia occurs when blood supply to the intestines is insufficient to meet metabolic demands. The causes include:
    • Arterial embolism: Commonly originates from the heart in patients with atrial fibrillation, leading to sudden occlusion of the superior mesenteric artery.
    • Arterial thrombosis: Often secondary to atherosclerosis, reducing lumen diameter in the mesenteric arteries.
    • Non-occlusive ischaemia: Hypoperfusion due to hypotension or vasospasm, particularly in critically ill patients.
    • Venous thrombosis: Thrombosis in the mesenteric veins can also reduce venous drainage, leading to ischaemia.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Mesenteric Ischaemia"][/sc]
  • Acute mesenteric ischaemia: Sudden onset of severe abdominal pain, often associated with cardiovascular collapse if untreated, and can lead to bowel necrosis within hours.
  • Chronic mesenteric ischaemia: Insidious onset with episodic postprandial abdominal pain over months or years, leading to progressive weight loss and fear of eating (food aversion).
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Mesenteric Ischaemia"][/sc]
  • Severe abdominal pain - Disproportionate to examination findings, due to ischaemia-induced necrosis of bowel tissue.
  • Nausea and vomiting - Due to intestinal irritation and reduced motility caused by hypoxia of the bowel wall.
  • Diarrhoea - Often occurs as the bowel becomes damaged and inflamed, leading to exudation into the lumen.
  • Weight loss (chronic cases) - Results from malabsorption and food aversion due to postprandial pain.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Mesenteric Ischaemia"][/sc]
  • Tender abdomen - Diffuse tenderness due to ischaemic bowel injury, though tenderness may be less pronounced early on.
  • Distended abdomen - May occur due to bowel oedema or infarction causing paralytic ileus.
  • Absent bowel sounds - Indicates bowel stasis or paralytic ileus as a result of ischaemia.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Mesenteric Ischaemia"][/sc]
  • Severe, sudden abdominal pain - Signifies acute onset of ischaemia, requiring urgent intervention to prevent bowel necrosis.
  • Hypotension and shock - Indicates systemic involvement and possible progression to bowel necrosis and sepsis.
  • Blood in stool - Suggests mucosal infarction and bowel necrosis.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Mesenteric Ischaemia"][/sc]
  • Minimal pain with rapid deterioration - Seen in elderly or immunocompromised patients who may not mount an appropriate pain response to ischaemia.
  • Chronic mesenteric ischaemia mimicking IBS - Intermittent postprandial pain may be mistaken for irritable bowel syndrome.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Mesenteric Ischaemia"][/sc]
  • CT angiography - First-line test showing vascular occlusion or stenosis of the mesenteric vessels. Reveals areas of bowel ischaemia.
  • Blood tests - Elevated lactate suggests bowel necrosis, while leukocytosis may indicate infection or inflammation.
  • Arterial blood gases - Metabolic acidosis may indicate systemic hypoperfusion due to severe ischaemia.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Mesenteric Ischaemia"][/sc]
  • A 65-year-old man with a history of atrial fibrillation presents with sudden-onset severe abdominal pain, nausea, and vomiting. Examination reveals a soft but mildly tender abdomen with no peritoneal signs. Blood tests show elevated lactate.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Mesenteric Ischaemia"][/sc]
  • Acute pancreatitis - Differs by severe epigastric pain radiating to the back, elevated serum amylase/lipase.
  • Bowel perforation - Features include acute abdomen with rigid abdomen and pneumoperitoneum on X-ray.
  • Intestinal obstruction - Presents with colicky pain, vomiting, and visible peristalsis, with distended abdomen.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Mesenteric Ischaemia"][/sc]
  • Acute mesenteric ischaemia - Immediate surgical intervention to remove necrotic bowel, revascularisation procedures (e.g., embolectomy), anticoagulation for embolic causes.
  • Chronic mesenteric ischaemia - Angioplasty and stenting of stenosed mesenteric arteries, alongside risk factor modification (e.g., smoking cessation, statins).
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Mesenteric Ischaemia"][/sc]
  • Bowel necrosis - Results from prolonged ischaemia and requires surgical resection, with high mortality.
  • Sepsis - Bowel perforation due to necrosis can lead to peritonitis and sepsis.
  • Short bowel syndrome - Occurs after extensive resection of necrotic bowel, leading to malabsorption and diarrhoea.
