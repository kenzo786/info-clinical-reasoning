Medication-induced Calf Pain
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Medication-induced Calf Pain"][/sc]
  • Medication-induced calf pain refers to muscular pain or discomfort localised to the calf region caused by pharmacological agents. Commonly implicated medications include statins, fluoroquinolones, and corticosteroids. The condition can present with muscle tenderness, cramping, or stiffness, and may range from mild discomfort to severe pain associated with rhabdomyolysis.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Medication-induced Calf Pain"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Medication-induced Calf Pain"][/sc]
  • Prevalence in UK GP population: Statin-associated muscle pain occurs in approximately 7-29% of patients prescribed statins.
  • UK hospital prevalence: Cases requiring hospitalisation (e.g., due to rhabdomyolysis) are rare, estimated at 0.1% of those on implicated medications.
  • Worldwide prevalence: Similar prevalence rates globally, with variation depending on drug prescribing practices and population susceptibilities.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Medication-induced Calf Pain"][/sc]
  • Statins: Disruption of mitochondrial function and increased oxidative stress lead to muscle damage.
  • Fluoroquinolones: Direct cytotoxic effects on muscle and tendons, mediated by oxidative stress and inhibition of tenocyte activity.
  • Corticosteroids: Protein catabolism and muscle atrophy may contribute to localised muscle discomfort.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Medication-induced Calf Pain"][/sc]
  • Onset: Symptoms typically develop within days to weeks after initiation of the causative medication.
  • Progression: May worsen with continued use; cessation often leads to gradual symptom resolution.
  • Chronic phase: Rare cases may lead to persistent symptoms if severe damage (e.g., rhabdomyolysis) occurs.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Medication-induced Calf Pain"][/sc]
  • Calf pain - Typically described as aching or cramping due to local muscle inflammation and mitochondrial dysfunction.
  • Muscle stiffness - Results from impaired calcium homeostasis within muscle cells.
  • Fatigue or weakness - Linked to muscle fibre damage and reduced energy production.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Medication-induced Calf Pain"][/sc]
  • Localised muscle tenderness - Indicates inflammation and damage to the muscle fibres.
  • Swelling (rare) - May occur in severe cases, suggesting significant inflammation or fluid accumulation.
  • Dark urine - Indicates myoglobinuria in cases of rhabdomyolysis.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Medication-induced Calf Pain"][/sc]
  • Severe pain unresponsive to withdrawal of medication - Suggests complications like rhabdomyolysis.
  • Dark-coloured urine - Indicates potential myoglobinuria, a marker for rhabdomyolysis and kidney damage.
  • Generalised muscle weakness - May reflect widespread muscle involvement or systemic toxicity.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Medication-induced Calf Pain"][/sc]
  • Isolated fatigue without pain - Can occur due to subtle mitochondrial dysfunction.
  • Delayed onset after cessation of medication - Reflects prolonged pharmacological effects.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Medication-induced Calf Pain"][/sc]
  • Creatine kinase (CK) levels: Elevated in rhabdomyolysis; mild elevations may be seen with muscle damage.
  • Urinalysis: Presence of myoglobin indicates myoglobinuria, suggestive of severe muscle breakdown.
  • Liver function tests: Elevated transaminases may occur due to shared muscle-liver enzymatic pathways.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Medication-induced Calf Pain"][/sc]
  • A 55-year-old male presenting with progressive bilateral calf pain and stiffness 3 weeks after initiating statin therapy, with elevated CK and no other systemic symptoms.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Medication-induced Calf Pain"][/sc]
  • Deep vein thrombosis: Differentiated by unilateral swelling, redness, and positive Doppler ultrasound findings.
  • Peripheral arterial disease: Features intermittent claudication and abnormal ankle-brachial index.
  • Idiopathic myositis: Associated with systemic features like fever and autoantibody positivity.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Medication-induced Calf Pain"][/sc]
  • Immediate cessation of causative medication: Primary intervention.
  • Supportive care: Includes hydration to prevent kidney damage in rhabdomyolysis.
  • Alternative medications: Replace offending agents (e.g., use non-statin lipid-lowering therapies if statins are the cause).
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Medication-induced Calf Pain"][/sc]
  • Rhabdomyolysis: Severe muscle breakdown leading to kidney injury.
  • Chronic muscle pain: Rarely persists even after drug cessation.
