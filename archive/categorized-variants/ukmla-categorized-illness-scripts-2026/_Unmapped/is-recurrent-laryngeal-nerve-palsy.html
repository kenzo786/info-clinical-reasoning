Recurrent Laryngeal Nerve (RLN) Palsy
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Recurrent Laryngeal Nerve (RLN) Palsy"][/sc]
  • Recurrent Laryngeal Nerve (RLN) palsy refers to dysfunction of the recurrent branch of the vagus nerve (cranial nerve X), which innervates the intrinsic muscles of the larynx (except the cricothyroid). This results in vocal cord paralysis or paresis, affecting voice, breathing, and sometimes swallowing. Unilateral palsy leads to hoarseness, while bilateral involvement can cause airway obstruction.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Recurrent Laryngeal Nerve (RLN) Palsy"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Recurrent Laryngeal Nerve (RLN) Palsy"][/sc]
  • RLN palsy affects approximately 1 in 100,000 GP patients per year in the UK. Hospital-based prevalence is higher, particularly after thyroid and cardiothoracic surgeries, with rates as high as 4-10%. Worldwide, RLN palsy is most common in regions with high thyroid surgery rates.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Recurrent Laryngeal Nerve (RLN) Palsy"][/sc]
  • The recurrent laryngeal nerve arises from the vagus nerve and loops around the aortic arch (left side) or subclavian artery (right side), ascending to innervate the larynx. Damage to this nerve disrupts motor signals to the laryngeal muscles, resulting in vocal cord paralysis. Causes include trauma during surgery (thyroid, parathyroid, or oesophageal), malignancy, or prolonged intubation. Compression by a tumour, aneurysm, or lymph node enlargement can also affect the nerve, particularly on the left side due to its longer course.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Recurrent Laryngeal Nerve (RLN) Palsy"][/sc]
  • Acute: Symptoms such as hoarseness or breathing difficulties may appear immediately after surgical trauma or nerve compression.
  • Subacute: Gradual onset of symptoms over weeks, often due to neoplastic or inflammatory processes.
  • Chronic: Long-standing RLN palsy can lead to permanent vocal cord dysfunction and possible airway compromise.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Recurrent Laryngeal Nerve (RLN) Palsy"][/sc]
  • Hoarseness - Caused by unilateral vocal cord paralysis, preventing proper adduction during phonation.
  • Breathiness - Results from incomplete closure of the glottis, allowing air to escape during speech.
  • Dyspnoea - In bilateral RLN palsy, both vocal cords may be paralysed in a partially closed position, leading to airway obstruction.
  • Dysphagia - Dysfunction of the laryngeal muscles can impair the swallowing mechanism, leading to aspiration risk.
  • Cough - A weak or ineffective cough may occur due to reduced glottic closure during forced expiration.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Recurrent Laryngeal Nerve (RLN) Palsy"][/sc]
  • Unilateral vocal cord paralysis - Observed during laryngoscopy, with one vocal cord remaining immobile during breathing and phonation.
  • Bilateral vocal cord paralysis - Both vocal cords remain immobile, often in a midline or near-midline position, resulting in stridor and breathing difficulties.
  • Stridor - High-pitched sound during inspiration, more common in bilateral palsy due to partial airway obstruction.
  • Voice changes - Weak, breathy, or hoarse voice, indicating incomplete vocal cord closure.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Recurrent Laryngeal Nerve (RLN) Palsy"][/sc]
  • Severe dyspnoea/stridor - Indicates potential bilateral vocal cord paralysis, requiring urgent airway management.
  • Progressive dysphagia or weight loss - May suggest malignancy compressing the RLN, particularly in the context of neoplastic causes.
  • Pain associated with voice changes - Uncommon in RLN palsy; suggests malignancy or invasive pathology such as an apical lung tumour (Pancoast tumour).
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Recurrent Laryngeal Nerve (RLN) Palsy"][/sc]
  • Intermittent hoarseness - May occur in RLN palsy secondary to recurrent compression, as in cases of positional or vascular anomalies.
  • Isolated cough without voice changes - Rare, but can happen in early stages or in cases where the nerve's motor function is more affected than sensory input.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Recurrent Laryngeal Nerve (RLN) Palsy"][/sc]
  • Flexible laryngoscopy - Direct visualisation of vocal cord paralysis or paresis. One or both cords may appear immobile.
  • CT/MRI of the neck and thorax - Used to identify causes such as tumours or aortic arch abnormalities compressing the nerve.
  • Electromyography (EMG) - Measures electrical activity of the laryngeal muscles to assess the degree of nerve injury.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Recurrent Laryngeal Nerve (RLN) Palsy"][/sc]
  • A 55-year-old male presents with a 3-month history of hoarseness following thyroid surgery, along with intermittent dyspnoea, worse on exertion. Laryngoscopy confirms unilateral vocal cord paralysis.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Recurrent Laryngeal Nerve (RLN) Palsy"][/sc]
  • Vocal cord nodules or polyps - Typically present with hoarseness but without dyspnoea or stridor. No vocal cord paralysis on laryngoscopy.
  • Myasthenia gravis - May cause hoarseness, but often associated with fluctuating muscle weakness, including the eyes and limbs.
  • Thyroid carcinoma - Can invade or compress the RLN, but often accompanied by other symptoms like a neck mass or dysphagia.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Recurrent Laryngeal Nerve (RLN) Palsy"][/sc]
  • Voice therapy - First-line for unilateral RLN palsy, aimed at strengthening vocal cord function.
  • Surgical options - Medialisation thyroplasty for unilateral paralysis; tracheostomy may be required in bilateral palsy with airway compromise.
  • Treat underlying cause - For neoplastic or compressive lesions, surgical resection or radiation therapy may be necessary.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Recurrent Laryngeal Nerve (RLN) Palsy"][/sc]
  • Chronic aspiration - May lead to recurrent chest infections and pneumonia due to poor glottic closure.
  • Airway obstruction - In bilateral palsy, airway obstruction can result in stridor and respiratory distress, requiring urgent intervention.
