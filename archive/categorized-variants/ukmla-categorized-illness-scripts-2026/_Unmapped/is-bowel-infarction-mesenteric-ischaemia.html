Bowel Infarction (Mesenteric Ischaemia)
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Bowel Infarction (Mesenteric Ischaemia)"][/sc]
  • Bowel infarction, or mesenteric ischaemia, is a condition characterised by the compromised blood supply to the intestines, leading to bowel necrosis if untreated. It can be acute or chronic and is typically caused by arterial occlusion, venous thrombosis, or non-occlusive mechanisms.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Bowel Infarction (Mesenteric Ischaemia)"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Bowel Infarction (Mesenteric Ischaemia)"][/sc]
  • Prevalence in the UK GP population: Rare, accounting for <0.1% of primary care consultations.
  • Hospital prevalence in the UK: Approximately 1 in 1,000 hospital admissions.
  • Worldwide prevalence: Varies widely but accounts for 1%–2% of acute abdomen cases requiring surgery.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Bowel Infarction (Mesenteric Ischaemia)"][/sc]
  • Mesenteric ischaemia occurs due to insufficient blood flow to the intestines.
  • Acute arterial occlusion (most common): Emboli from the heart (e.g., atrial fibrillation) or thrombotic occlusion in atherosclerotic vessels.
  • Venous thrombosis: Leads to venous congestion, reducing arterial flow and oxygenation.
  • Non-occlusive causes: Hypotension or vasospasm results in critical ischaemia in the setting of low perfusion states.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Bowel Infarction (Mesenteric Ischaemia)"][/sc]
  • Hyperacute: Minutes to hours in embolic arterial occlusion.
  • Acute: Over hours to a day in thrombotic arterial occlusion or venous thrombosis.
  • Chronic: Gradual over months with episodic abdominal pain due to progressive atherosclerotic narrowing.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Bowel Infarction (Mesenteric Ischaemia)"][/sc]
  • Severe abdominal pain - Out of proportion to clinical findings due to ischaemic damage to the bowel.
  • Nausea and vomiting - Resulting from bowel irritation and early ischaemia.
  • Diarrhoea or bloody stool - Indicating mucosal injury and bowel wall necrosis.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Bowel Infarction (Mesenteric Ischaemia)"][/sc]
  • Diffuse abdominal tenderness - Reflects ischaemic or necrotic bowel.
  • Hypotension - Indicative of systemic inflammatory response or hypovolaemia.
  • Absent bowel sounds - Suggesting advanced bowel necrosis.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Bowel Infarction (Mesenteric Ischaemia)"][/sc]
  • Peritonitis - Indicates perforation or severe necrosis, necessitating urgent surgery.
  • Sepsis - Signifies systemic inflammation due to bowel necrosis or perforation.
  • Acidosis on blood gas - Suggestive of tissue ischaemia and impending shock.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Bowel Infarction (Mesenteric Ischaemia)"][/sc]
  • Intermittent postprandial pain in chronic ischaemia - Mimics other gastrointestinal disorders like peptic ulcers.
  • Painless presentations in diabetic or elderly patients due to impaired pain sensation.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Bowel Infarction (Mesenteric Ischaemia)"][/sc]
  • CT angiography - Gold standard showing occlusion or reduced blood flow in mesenteric vessels.
  • Lactate levels - Elevated due to anaerobic metabolism in ischaemic tissue.
  • Plain abdominal X-ray - May show pneumatosis intestinalis or free air if perforation occurs.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Bowel Infarction (Mesenteric Ischaemia)"][/sc]
  • A 70-year-old man with a history of atrial fibrillation presents with sudden-onset severe abdominal pain out of proportion to examination findings, accompanied by nausea and vomiting.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Bowel Infarction (Mesenteric Ischaemia)"][/sc]
  • Acute pancreatitis - More localised pain and elevated amylase/lipase levels.
  • Small bowel obstruction - Colicky pain and dilated bowel loops on imaging.
  • Peptic ulcer perforation - Sudden pain with free air under the diaphragm on imaging.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Bowel Infarction (Mesenteric Ischaemia)"][/sc]
  • Resuscitation with fluids and correction of acidosis.
  • Broad-spectrum antibiotics to prevent sepsis.
  • Urgent surgical intervention for revascularisation or bowel resection if indicated.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Bowel Infarction (Mesenteric Ischaemia)"][/sc]
  • Sepsis and multiorgan failure - Common in delayed diagnosis.
  • Short bowel syndrome - Following extensive resection.
  • Chronic mesenteric ischaemia - If collateral circulation develops insufficiently.
