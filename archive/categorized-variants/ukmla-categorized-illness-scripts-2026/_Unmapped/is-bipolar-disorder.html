Bipolar Disorder
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Bipolar Disorder"][/sc]
  • Bipolar disorder is a chronic mental health condition characterised by recurrent episodes of mood disturbances, including manic, hypomanic, and depressive phases. Manic episodes are periods of elevated or irritable mood, increased energy, and hyperactivity, while depressive episodes involve low mood, fatigue, and a lack of interest in activities.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Bipolar Disorder"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Bipolar Disorder"][/sc]
  • UK GP Population: Prevalence is approximately 1%.
  • UK Hospital Prevalence: Bipolar disorder accounts for around 5% of psychiatric admissions.
  • Worldwide Prevalence: Bipolar disorder affects approximately 1-2% of the global population.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Bipolar Disorder"][/sc]
  • Genetic Factors: Bipolar disorder has a strong genetic component, with polygenic risk influencing neurotransmitter pathways. Genes involved in dopamine, serotonin, and glutamate regulation are implicated.
  • Neurotransmitter Imbalance: Dopaminergic and serotonergic dysregulation are associated with mood instability. Elevated dopamine during mania and reduced serotonin during depressive episodes are key factors.
  • Structural Brain Changes: Imaging studies show changes in the prefrontal cortex, amygdala, and hippocampus, regions that control mood regulation, which may contribute to symptoms.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Bipolar Disorder"][/sc]
  • Manic Phase: Symptoms develop rapidly, often over days to weeks, and last for at least one week unless interrupted by treatment.
  • Depressive Phase: Typically lasts several weeks to months, with a slower onset than mania. Episodes can recur with varied frequency.
  • Hypomanic Phase: Similar to mania but shorter and less severe, lasting at least four days without severe functional impairment.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Bipolar Disorder"][/sc]
  • Elevated Mood (Mania/Hypomania): Persistent euphoria or irritability, related to increased dopamine activity.
  • Increased Energy and Activity: Hyperactivity and reduced need for sleep, due to heightened sympathetic nervous system activity.
  • Depressed Mood (Depressive Phase): Persistent sadness and loss of interest, often associated with reduced serotonin levels.
  • Fatigue and Sleep Disturbance: In depressive episodes, due to decreased energy and circadian rhythm dysregulation.
  • Grandiosity: Inflated self-esteem and unrealistic confidence, common in mania due to reduced inhibitory control.
  • Racing Thoughts: Rapid thought flow, often leading to distractibility, due to increased neural excitability.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Bipolar Disorder"][/sc]
  • Pressured Speech: Rapid, excessive talking, often with frequent interruptions, due to heightened mental excitability.
  • Agitation: Restlessness or fidgeting, particularly in manic episodes, due to increased autonomic arousal.
  • Psychomotor Retardation (Depression): Slowed physical movements and speech, commonly seen in depressive episodes.
  • Impulsivity: Risk-taking behaviours, often observed during manic phases due to impaired judgement and increased dopamine activity.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Bipolar Disorder"][/sc]
  • Psychosis (Hallucinations or Delusions): Indicates severe mania or depression, suggesting an increased risk of harm to self or others.
  • Suicidal Ideation: Common in depressive phases, associated with hopelessness and despair. Indicates high risk for self-harm and requires urgent intervention.
  • Severe Functional Impairment: Inability to maintain daily responsibilities, reflecting disease severity and the need for more intensive treatment.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Bipolar Disorder"][/sc]
  • Mixed Episodes: Concurrent depressive and manic symptoms, causing diagnostic challenges due to overlapping features.
  • Rapid Cycling: Four or more mood episodes within a year, often leading to a more severe and treatment-resistant form of the disorder.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Bipolar Disorder"][/sc]
  • Mood Disorder Questionnaire (MDQ): Screening tool for bipolar disorder, indicating symptom patterns suggestive of bipolar disorder if positive.
  • Blood Tests: Thyroid function tests to rule out thyroid dysfunction, which can mimic mood symptoms.
  • Psychiatric Assessment: Comprehensive interview to evaluate mood history, symptom severity, and patterns.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Bipolar Disorder"][/sc]
  • A 30-year-old woman presents with recurrent episodes of elevated mood, decreased need for sleep, and risk-taking behaviours, alternating with periods of low mood, fatigue, and social withdrawal, persisting for years.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Bipolar Disorder"][/sc]
  • Unipolar Depression: Lacks episodes of mania; mood disturbances are consistently low.
  • Borderline Personality Disorder: Similar mood swings, but more reactive to interpersonal events.
  • ADHD: Features hyperactivity, but without cyclical mood changes typical of bipolar disorder.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Bipolar Disorder"][/sc]
  • Mood Stabilizers: Lithium or valproate for long-term mood stabilisation.
  • Atypical Antipsychotics: E.g., olanzapine, for acute manic episodes.
  • Psychotherapy: Cognitive Behavioural Therapy (CBT) to support mood regulation and coping strategies.
  • Supportive Measures: Family therapy and lifestyle modification to support long-term management.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Bipolar Disorder"][/sc]
  • Suicidal Behaviour: Increased risk in depressive phases, potentially life-threatening.
  • Substance Abuse: High rates due to impulsivity and mood instability, complicating management.
  • Social and Occupational Impairment: Difficulty maintaining relationships and employment, impacting quality of life.
