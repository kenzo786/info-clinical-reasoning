Medication-Induced Jaundice
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Medication-Induced Jaundice"][/sc]
  • Medication-induced jaundice is a form of liver injury where medications lead to an accumulation of bilirubin in the body, resulting in yellowing of the skin and eyes. This condition often arises due to hepatocellular damage or cholestasis (bile flow obstruction) caused by drugs. Medications commonly associated with jaundice include acetaminophen, antibiotics (e.g., amoxicillin-clavulanate), and certain anti-tubercular drugs.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Medication-Induced Jaundice"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Medication-Induced Jaundice"][/sc]
  • Prevalence in the UK GP population: Rare, with drug-induced liver injury estimated at 1 in 10,000 to 100,000 prescriptions.
  • Hospital prevalence (UK): Drug-induced jaundice accounts for approximately 10% of cases of jaundice seen in hospitalised patients with liver injury.
  • Worldwide prevalence: Similar to UK, though prevalence varies depending on prescription habits; around 2-3 per 100,000 person-years globally for severe cases.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Medication-Induced Jaundice"][/sc]
  • Drug-induced hepatocellular injury: Certain medications can directly damage liver cells, leading to impaired bilirubin conjugation and excretion, thus increasing unconjugated bilirubin in the blood.
  • Cholestatic effects: Some drugs cause cholestasis by impairing bile secretion or causing bile duct obstruction. This leads to conjugated bilirubin buildup.
  • Immunoallergic reactions: Drugs can trigger immune-mediated liver damage, where immune cells attack liver tissue, resulting in inflammation and jaundice.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Medication-Induced Jaundice"][/sc]
  • Acute phase: Typically occurs within days to weeks of drug exposure, particularly with high-dose or prolonged use.
  • Subacute phase: Some medications may cause jaundice after weeks to months of use, especially with cumulative hepatotoxicity.
  • Resolution phase: On drug discontinuation, symptoms may improve within days, though full resolution may take weeks to months.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Medication-Induced Jaundice"][/sc]
  • Yellowing of skin and eyes (jaundice) - Due to bilirubin accumulation from impaired liver processing.
  • Fatigue - Common due to systemic inflammation and liver dysfunction reducing energy metabolism.
  • Nausea and vomiting - Often caused by liver toxicity and impaired bile excretion leading to gastrointestinal discomfort.
  • Dark urine - Caused by high levels of conjugated bilirubin being excreted through the kidneys.
  • Itching (pruritus) - Resulting from bile acids accumulating in the skin, often in cholestatic forms of jaundice.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Medication-Induced Jaundice"][/sc]
  • Jaundice (yellow skin and sclera) - Visible yellowing due to bilirubin deposition in skin and mucous membranes.
  • Hepatomegaly - Enlarged liver, possibly palpable, indicating inflammation or congestion due to liver injury.
  • Dark urine - Visible change in urine colour from high bilirubin excretion.
  • Clay-coloured stools - Lack of bile reaching the intestines in cases of severe cholestasis.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Medication-Induced Jaundice"][/sc]
  • Severe abdominal pain - May suggest acute liver failure or biliary obstruction; needs urgent assessment.
  • Rapidly worsening jaundice - Can indicate progressing liver failure and requires immediate intervention.
  • Confusion or altered mental status - Could signify hepatic encephalopathy from severe liver dysfunction.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Medication-Induced Jaundice"][/sc]
  • Asymptomatic jaundice: Some patients may not exhibit symptoms until advanced liver injury has occurred, especially with slow-developing hepatotoxicity.
  • Non-specific symptoms: Mild fatigue or malaise can occur without overt jaundice, particularly in early stages.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Medication-Induced Jaundice"][/sc]
  • Liver function tests (LFTs) - Elevated ALT and AST in hepatocellular injury, elevated ALP and GGT in cholestasis; bilirubin levels may be raised.
  • Serum bilirubin - Elevated total and direct bilirubin, indicating impaired liver processing or excretion.
  • Imaging (Ultrasound, MRI) - To rule out structural causes of jaundice, e.g., bile duct obstruction.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Medication-Induced Jaundice"][/sc]
  • A 45-year-old woman presents with 2 weeks of yellowing of the eyes, dark urine, and fatigue after starting a new medication (e.g., amoxicillin-clavulanate). Liver function tests reveal elevated ALT, AST, and bilirubin levels.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Medication-Induced Jaundice"][/sc]
  • Viral hepatitis - Presents with fever and systemic symptoms; confirmed by hepatitis serology.
  • Gallstones with biliary obstruction - Often causes severe pain, confirmed by imaging (e.g., ultrasound).
  • Autoimmune hepatitis - Chronic liver inflammation with autoantibodies and requires immunosuppressive treatment.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Medication-Induced Jaundice"][/sc]
  • Immediate discontinuation of the offending drug is critical.
  • Supportive care: Hydration, antiemetics for nausea.
  • Consider referral to hepatology if liver function does not improve.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Medication-Induced Jaundice"][/sc]
  • Acute liver failure - Due to severe hepatocellular injury; requires urgent treatment and possible liver transplantation.
  • Chronic liver disease - Can develop if hepatotoxicity is not resolved, leading to cirrhosis.
