Warts and Verrucae
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Warts and Verrucae"][/sc]
  • Warts and verrucae are benign proliferative lesions of the skin and mucosa caused by human papillomavirus (HPV). They are characterised by hyperkeratotic, exophytic growths. Verrucae specifically refer to warts located on the soles of the feet.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Warts and Verrucae"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Warts and Verrucae"][/sc]
  • Prevalence in UK GP population: Estimated at 3-5% for common warts, higher in school-age children.
  • UK hospital prevalence: Less commonly seen in secondary care except for recalcitrant or atypical cases.
  • Worldwide prevalence: Approximately 10%, with higher rates in children and immunosuppressed individuals.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Warts and Verrucae"][/sc]
  • HPV infects basal keratinocytes through microabrasions in the skin.
  • Viral replication occurs within keratinocytes, promoting hyperproliferation and disrupting normal differentiation.
  • Accumulation of keratin leads to the characteristic thickened, rough appearance of warts.
  • The immune response varies; warts often persist in individuals with suboptimal cell-mediated immunity.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Warts and Verrucae"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Initial HPV infection and local proliferation, often unnoticed.
  • Established phase: Visible wart formation; can persist for months to years if untreated.
  • Resolution phase: Can resolve spontaneously within 1-2 years due to immune response, particularly in children.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Warts and Verrucae"][/sc]
  • Asymptomatic presentation - Most warts are painless unless in weight-bearing areas (verrucae).
  • Pain - Verrucae can cause discomfort when walking due to pressure on plantar lesions.
  • Pruritus - Occasional itching due to irritation or mild inflammation.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Warts and Verrucae"][/sc]
  • Hyperkeratotic papules - Rough, raised lesions with a cauliflower-like surface.
  • Black dots - Thrombosed capillaries visible on paring the lesion.
  • Interruption of skin lines - Normal skin lines are disrupted by wart growth.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Warts and Verrucae"][/sc]
  • Rapid growth or change in appearance - May indicate malignancy (e.g., squamous cell carcinoma).
  • Ulceration or bleeding - Suggests trauma, secondary infection, or rare malignancy.
  • Persistence despite treatment - Possible immune deficiency or misdiagnosis.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Warts and Verrucae"][/sc]
  • Flat warts - Smooth, flat-topped lesions seen on the face or hands.
  • Recalcitrant warts - Resistant to standard treatments, often in immunosuppressed patients.
  • Giant warts - Large lesions, rare, sometimes linked to immunosuppression or specific HPV strains.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Warts and Verrucae"][/sc]
  • Clinical diagnosis - Based on characteristic appearance.
  • Dermatoscopy - Shows thrombosed capillaries and disrupted skin lines.
  • Histopathology (rarely needed) - Reveals hyperkeratosis, papillomatosis, and viral inclusion bodies.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Warts and Verrucae"][/sc]
  • A 12-year-old child presents with multiple rough, hyperkeratotic papules on the hands and feet for the past six months, causing mild pain on walking but no other symptoms.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Warts and Verrucae"][/sc]
  • Corn/callus - Differentiated by retained skin lines and lack of thrombosed capillaries.
  • Squamous cell carcinoma - Rapid growth, ulceration, and atypical location suggest malignancy.
  • Molluscum contagiosum - Pearly, umbilicated papules rather than rough lesions.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Warts and Verrucae"][/sc]
  • Topical salicylic acid - First-line treatment; applied daily to soften and remove keratin.
  • Cryotherapy - Liquid nitrogen used to induce local destruction; repeated every 2-3 weeks.
  • Immunotherapy - Topical agents (e.g., imiquimod) or intralesional injections for refractory cases.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Warts and Verrucae"][/sc]
  • Secondary bacterial infection - Due to trauma or over-treatment.
  • Scarring - Potentially from treatment modalities like cryotherapy.
  • Psychological impact - Social stigma, especially in children.
