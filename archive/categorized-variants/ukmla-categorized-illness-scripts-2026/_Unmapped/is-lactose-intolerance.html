Lactose Intolerance
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Lactose Intolerance"][/sc]
  • Lactose intolerance is a digestive disorder caused by the inability to digest lactose, a sugar found in milk and dairy products. This condition occurs due to a deficiency of lactase, an enzyme produced in the small intestine, which is required to break down lactose into glucose and galactose for absorption. It leads to gastrointestinal symptoms after consuming lactose-containing foods.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Lactose Intolerance"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Lactose Intolerance"][/sc]
  • Prevalence in the UK GP population: Around 5% of the UK population has lactose intolerance, with variation in ethnic groups (higher in those of Asian, African, and Middle Eastern descent).
  • UK hospital prevalence: Rarely presents in hospitals except when it leads to severe malnutrition or misdiagnosed as other gastrointestinal disorders.
  • Worldwide prevalence: It affects around 68% of the global population, with higher prevalence in East Asian, African, and Mediterranean populations.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Lactose Intolerance"][/sc]
  • In lactose intolerance, the deficiency of the enzyme lactase in the small intestine impairs the breakdown of lactose into glucose and galactose. As a result, undigested lactose passes into the colon, where it is fermented by gut bacteria. This fermentation produces gas (hydrogen, methane, and carbon dioxide) and osmotic effects, drawing water into the colon, which leads to bloating, diarrhoea, and flatulence.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Lactose Intolerance"][/sc]
  • Acute Phase: Symptoms typically begin 30 minutes to 2 hours after consuming lactose-containing foods.
  • Chronic Phase: If lactose consumption continues, symptoms persist with varying intensity depending on the amount of lactose ingested.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Lactose Intolerance"][/sc]
  • Bloating - Caused by gas produced during the fermentation of undigested lactose in the colon.
  • Diarrhoea - Osmotic effect of lactose draws water into the bowel, leading to loose stools.
  • Flatulence - Excess gas from bacterial fermentation of lactose results in increased flatulence.
  • Abdominal pain - Cramping or discomfort due to gas and increased bowel motility.
  • Nausea - Sometimes occurs, especially in severe cases or when large amounts of lactose are consumed.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Lactose Intolerance"][/sc]
  • Abdominal distension - Visible bloating due to gas accumulation.
  • Borborygmi (stomach rumbling) - Audible bowel sounds due to gas and fluid movement in the intestines.
  • Watery stools - Seen during diarrhoeal episodes from the osmotic effect of unabsorbed lactose.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Lactose Intolerance"][/sc]
  • Weight loss - May indicate malabsorption or another underlying gastrointestinal condition.
  • Blood in stools - Suggests an alternative diagnosis, such as inflammatory bowel disease (IBD) or colorectal cancer.
  • Persistent vomiting - May indicate a more serious gastrointestinal obstruction or disorder.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Lactose Intolerance"][/sc]
  • Chronic fatigue - Lactose intolerance may rarely present with nonspecific symptoms such as fatigue due to malabsorption of nutrients.
  • Minimal or no gastrointestinal symptoms - Some individuals may have only mild bloating or intermittent diarrhoea, which can be mistaken for irritable bowel syndrome (IBS).
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Lactose Intolerance"][/sc]
  • Hydrogen breath test - Elevated hydrogen levels after ingesting lactose indicate bacterial fermentation of undigested lactose in the colon. Hydrogen levels above 20 ppm (parts per million) within 90 minutes are considered diagnostic.
  • Lactose tolerance test - Blood glucose levels are measured after ingesting lactose. A lack of significant rise in glucose (<1.1 mmol/L) suggests lactose malabsorption.
  • Stool acidity test (in infants) - Increased stool acidity (pH <5.5) due to lactic acid production from bacterial fermentation of lactose.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Lactose Intolerance"][/sc]
  • A 30-year-old male of Asian descent presents with abdominal bloating, diarrhoea, and flatulence after consuming dairy products for the past 6 months. Symptoms resolve on avoiding milk products.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Lactose Intolerance"][/sc]
  • Irritable bowel syndrome (IBS) - IBS presents with bloating and diarrhoea but may also include constipation and does not necessarily correlate with lactose intake.
  • Inflammatory bowel disease (IBD) - IBD can cause chronic diarrhoea and abdominal pain, often with blood in stools and systemic symptoms like fever or weight loss.
  • Coeliac disease - Coeliac disease causes malabsorption and presents with diarrhoea, weight loss, and fatigue. It improves on a gluten-free diet, not a lactose-free diet.
  • Small intestinal bacterial overgrowth (SIBO) - SIBO may cause bloating, diarrhoea, and flatulence, but hydrogen breath test shows abnormal patterns distinct from lactose intolerance.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Lactose Intolerance"][/sc]
  • Lactose-free diet - The primary management involves reducing or eliminating lactose-containing foods from the diet.
  • Lactase enzyme supplements - Available over-the-counter, these can be taken before consuming lactose-containing foods to aid digestion.
  • Calcium and vitamin D supplements - For individuals avoiding dairy, supplementation may be necessary to prevent deficiencies.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Lactose Intolerance"][/sc]
  • Nutritional deficiencies - Long-term avoidance of dairy can lead to deficiencies in calcium and vitamin D, increasing the risk of osteoporosis.
  • Secondary malnutrition - In severe cases, chronic diarrhoea may contribute to broader malnutrition if lactose intolerance is misdiagnosed or untreated.
