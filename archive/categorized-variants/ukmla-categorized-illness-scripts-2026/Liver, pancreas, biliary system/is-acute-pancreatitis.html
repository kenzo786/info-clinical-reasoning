Acute Pancreatitis
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Acute Pancreatitis"][/sc]
  • Acute pancreatitis is an inflammatory condition of the pancreas characterized by the sudden onset of severe abdominal pain, often associated with elevated pancreatic enzymes in the blood, and can range from mild to life-threatening. The condition is generally self-limiting but may progress to necrotising pancreatitis in severe cases, leading to systemic complications.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Acute Pancreatitis"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Acute Pancreatitis"][/sc]
  • UK General Population: Acute pancreatitis affects around 20–30 per 100,000 individuals annually, with slightly higher rates in males.
  • UK Hospital Prevalence: It accounts for approximately 3% of hospital admissions due to abdominal pain.
  • Worldwide: Global incidence varies but is generally similar, with higher rates in regions with high alcohol consumption.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Acute Pancreatitis"][/sc]
  • Initial insult, commonly due to gallstones or alcohol, triggers acinar cell injury.
  • This results in the activation of pancreatic enzymes (trypsinogen to trypsin) within the pancreas, leading to autodigestion and inflammation.
  • Inflammation recruits neutrophils, macrophages, and cytokines (IL-1, IL-6, TNF-α), exacerbating local injury and increasing vascular permeability.
  • Severe cases progress to necrosis and systemic inflammatory response, which can lead to multi-organ dysfunction.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Acute Pancreatitis"][/sc]
  • Early Phase (First 24–48 hours): Acute inflammation, abdominal pain, systemic inflammatory response.
  • Late Phase (>1 week): Potential for complications, including necrosis, infection, and pseudocyst formation if severe.
  • Recovery Phase: Resolution of inflammation and gradual improvement in symptoms, lasting days to weeks.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Acute Pancreatitis"][/sc]
  • Severe Epigastric Pain - Often radiating to the back; due to inflammation and oedema of pancreatic tissue.
  • Nausea and Vomiting - Stimulation of the autonomic nervous system by inflammatory cytokines and increased intra-abdominal pressure.
  • Fever - Systemic inflammatory response, especially in severe cases.
  • Jaundice - Can occur if gallstones are present, obstructing the bile duct.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Acute Pancreatitis"][/sc]
  • Epigastric Tenderness - Due to localised inflammation in the upper abdomen.
  • Guarding or Rigidity - May indicate peritonitis in severe cases with pancreatic necrosis.
  • Hypotension - Systemic inflammatory response leading to vasodilation and fluid loss.
  • Grey-Turner Sign - Ecchymosis of the flanks, associated with retroperitoneal haemorrhage.
  • Cullen’s Sign - Periumbilical ecchymosis, indicative of severe haemorrhagic pancreatitis.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Acute Pancreatitis"][/sc]
  • Severe Hypotension - Indicates shock and potential multi-organ dysfunction.
  • Persistent Fever or Sepsis - Suggestive of infected pancreatic necrosis, a life-threatening complication.
  • Altered Mental Status - May signal sepsis or encephalopathy from severe systemic inflammation.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Acute Pancreatitis"][/sc]
  • Painless Jaundice - Seen in patients with obstructive pancreatitis secondary to gallstones, may be mistaken for other hepatobiliary diseases.
  • Predominant Respiratory Symptoms - Due to acute respiratory distress syndrome (ARDS) in severe cases, leading to dyspnoea without notable abdominal pain.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Acute Pancreatitis"][/sc]
  • Serum Amylase and Lipase - Elevated (>3x upper limit of normal); lipase is more specific for pancreatic inflammation.
  • CT Abdomen - Imaging modality of choice to assess severity, detect necrosis, pseudocysts, and complications.
  • Ultrasound - Useful for identifying gallstones as an underlying cause.
  • CRP and WCC - Elevated in cases with systemic inflammation; CRP >150 mg/L may indicate severe pancreatitis.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Acute Pancreatitis"][/sc]
  • A 45-year-old male presents with acute onset of severe, radiating epigastric pain, nausea, and vomiting, with elevated serum lipase levels and history of chronic alcohol use.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Acute Pancreatitis"][/sc]
  • Cholecystitis - RUQ pain, often with fever and Murphy’s sign, but without elevated lipase.
  • Perforated Peptic Ulcer - Sudden severe pain with signs of peritonitis, air under the diaphragm on imaging.
  • Mesenteric Ischaemia - Diffuse abdominal pain, especially in elderly patients, with high lactate levels.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Acute Pancreatitis"][/sc]
  • Supportive Care - IV fluids, pain management, nil by mouth initially to rest the pancreas.
  • Antibiotics - Only in cases of confirmed or suspected infected necrosis.
  • Management of Underlying Cause - For example, ERCP in biliary pancreatitis, alcohol cessation support in alcoholic pancreatitis.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Acute Pancreatitis"][/sc]
  • Necrotising Pancreatitis - Leads to extensive tissue damage, associated with high mortality.
  • Pseudocyst Formation - Occurs in 10–20% of cases, can lead to infection or rupture.
  • Multi-Organ Dysfunction Syndrome (MODS) - In severe cases with systemic inflammatory response, leading to ARDS, renal failure, and sepsis.
