Macular Degeneration
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Macular Degeneration"][/sc]
  • Macular degeneration is a progressive degenerative condition of the macula, primarily affecting central vision. It is classified into two forms: dry (atrophic) macular degeneration, characterised by drusen and retinal atrophy, and wet (neovascular) macular degeneration, involving abnormal blood vessel growth under the retina. It is the leading cause of vision loss in individuals over 50 years in developed countries.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Macular Degeneration"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Macular Degeneration"][/sc]
  • Prevalence in UK GP population: Approximately 1.4% for late-stage disease in those aged ≥50 years.
  • UK hospital prevalence: Higher in ophthalmology clinics, especially for wet macular degeneration.
  • Worldwide prevalence: Around 8.7% for all stages of macular degeneration, with the highest burden in developed nations.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Macular Degeneration"][/sc]
  • Initial accumulation of lipid and protein deposits (drusen) in Bruch's membrane leads to dysfunction of retinal pigment epithelium (RPE).
  • In dry macular degeneration, chronic inflammation and oxidative stress cause progressive atrophy of the RPE and photoreceptors.
  • In wet macular degeneration, hypoxia induces vascular endothelial growth factor (VEGF), leading to neovascularisation and subsequent haemorrhage, fluid leakage, and fibrosis.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Macular Degeneration"][/sc]
  • Dry macular degeneration progresses over years to decades, often asymptomatically in early stages.
  • Wet macular degeneration can develop rapidly, leading to significant vision loss within weeks to months.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Macular Degeneration"][/sc]
  • Blurred central vision - Due to loss of photoreceptors in the macula.
  • Metamorphopsia (distorted vision) - Caused by macular swelling or neovascular changes.
  • Central scotoma - From photoreceptor death or haemorrhage in advanced disease.
  • Difficulty reading or recognising faces - Reflects impaired central vision.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Macular Degeneration"][/sc]
  • Drusen - Yellow deposits visible on fundoscopy in dry macular degeneration.
  • Retinal atrophy - Thinning and loss of the macular region in advanced dry disease.
  • Subretinal haemorrhages - Indicative of neovascularisation in wet macular degeneration.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Macular Degeneration"][/sc]
  • Sudden vision loss - May indicate retinal detachment or advanced wet macular degeneration.
  • Metamorphopsia worsening rapidly - Suggestive of wet macular degeneration.
  • Photopsia (flashes of light) - May signal retinal complications or significant neovascular changes.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Macular Degeneration"][/sc]
  • Peripheral vision loss without significant central changes (uncommon).
  • Asymptomatic drusen detected on routine screening.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Macular Degeneration"][/sc]
  • Fundoscopy - Drusen, atrophy, or haemorrhages visible.
  • Optical Coherence Tomography (OCT) - Detects macular thickness, atrophy, or fluid.
  • Fluorescein angiography - Identifies neovascularisation in wet macular degeneration.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Macular Degeneration"][/sc]
  • A 75-year-old woman with gradual central vision loss over 5 years, presenting with difficulty reading and recognising faces, worsened by sudden distortion of vision over the past month.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Macular Degeneration"][/sc]
  • Diabetic retinopathy - Differs by presence of diabetic history and widespread retinal haemorrhages.
  • Retinal vein occlusion - Sudden onset with "blood and thunder" appearance on fundoscopy.
  • Central serous retinopathy - Common in younger patients, with fluid accumulation and less drusen.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Macular Degeneration"][/sc]
  • Dry macular degeneration - Lifestyle modifications, vitamin supplementation (AREDS formula), and low-vision aids.
  • Wet macular degeneration - Intravitreal anti-VEGF injections (e.g., ranibizumab), laser therapy, or photodynamic therapy.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Macular Degeneration"][/sc]
  • Severe vision loss - Leading to disability and reduced quality of life.
  • Choroidal neovascularisation - Associated with rapid progression in wet macular degeneration.
  • Geographic atrophy - End-stage dry macular degeneration with profound vision loss.
