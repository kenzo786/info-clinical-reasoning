Chronic Kidney Disease (CKD)
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Chronic Kidney Disease (CKD)"][/sc]
  • Chronic Kidney Disease (CKD) is a long-term condition characterised by progressive, irreversible damage to the kidneys, leading to a decline in kidney function. It is defined by the presence of kidney damage (e.g., proteinuria or structural abnormalities) or a reduced glomerular filtration rate (GFR) below 60 mL/min/1.73 m² for three months or longer, irrespective of the underlying cause.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Chronic Kidney Disease (CKD)"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Chronic Kidney Disease (CKD)"][/sc]
  • Prevalence in the UK GP population: Approximately 10-15% of the adult population.
  • UK hospital prevalence: CKD is more prevalent in hospital settings, particularly among those with comorbidities such as diabetes or hypertension.
  • Worldwide prevalence: CKD affects around 10-12% of the global population, with higher rates in elderly populations and those with cardiovascular disease.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Chronic Kidney Disease (CKD)"][/sc]
  • CKD involves progressive nephron loss due to various underlying causes (e.g., diabetes, hypertension, glomerulonephritis). As nephrons are lost, the remaining nephrons undergo hyperfiltration to maintain overall kidney function. Over time, this hyperfiltration leads to further nephron injury, promoting a cycle of renal damage. Inflammation, fibrosis, and scarring contribute to structural damage, which results in decreased glomerular filtration rate (GFR) and eventual kidney failure.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Chronic Kidney Disease (CKD)"][/sc]
  • CKD progresses slowly over years, often in stages. In the early stages (1-2), the patient may be asymptomatic with minor reductions in GFR. In stages 3-4, symptoms such as fatigue, oedema, and anaemia develop due to further decline in kidney function. End-stage renal disease (stage 5) occurs when GFR drops below 15 mL/min/1.73 m², necessitating dialysis or kidney transplantation.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Chronic Kidney Disease (CKD)"][/sc]
  • Fatigue - Due to anaemia (reduced erythropoietin production by the kidneys), electrolyte imbalances, and uraemia.
  • Oedema - Caused by reduced excretion of sodium and water, leading to fluid retention.
  • Dyspnoea - Related to fluid overload, pulmonary oedema, and anaemia.
  • Nocturia - Impaired kidney function reduces the ability to concentrate urine, leading to increased urination at night.
  • Pruritus - Uraemia causes the accumulation of waste products, which can deposit in the skin, leading to itching.
  • Muscle cramps - Result from electrolyte imbalances, especially low calcium and high phosphate levels.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Chronic Kidney Disease (CKD)"][/sc]
  • Hypertension - Due to fluid overload and activation of the renin-angiotensin-aldosterone system (RAAS).
  • Pallor - Resulting from anaemia secondary to decreased erythropoietin production.
  • Pitting oedema - Occurs due to fluid retention, most commonly seen in the legs and ankles.
  • Pericardial rub - A sign of uraemic pericarditis, which occurs in advanced CKD due to the accumulation of uraemic toxins.
  • Cachexia - Wasting syndrome observed in advanced CKD due to poor appetite, malnutrition, and uraemia.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Chronic Kidney Disease (CKD)"][/sc]
  • Oliguria or anuria - Indicates rapid decline in kidney function or obstruction, a potential emergency requiring immediate intervention.
  • Severe hyperkalaemia - Can cause life-threatening arrhythmias; usually indicates advanced CKD or acute kidney injury (AKI) superimposed on CKD.
  • Severe dyspnoea - Suggestive of pulmonary oedema or heart failure due to volume overload, requiring urgent management.
  • Pericarditis - A sign of uraemic pericarditis, which may lead to tamponade if untreated.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Chronic Kidney Disease (CKD)"][/sc]
  • Isolated hypertension - In some patients, CKD may initially present with poorly controlled hypertension without other significant symptoms.
  • Asymptomatic proteinuria - CKD may be identified incidentally through routine blood or urine tests in asymptomatic patients.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Chronic Kidney Disease (CKD)"][/sc]
  • Serum creatinine and eGFR - Elevated creatinine and reduced estimated GFR (eGFR) indicate decreased kidney function.
  • Urine dipstick and ACR (albumin:creatinine ratio) - Presence of proteinuria (often microalbuminuria) is an early marker of kidney damage.
  • Ultrasound - May show small, shrunken kidneys, suggesting chronicity, or structural abnormalities such as cysts.
  • Serum electrolytes - Hyperkalaemia and metabolic acidosis may indicate worsening renal function and impaired excretion.
  • Full blood count - Anaemia is common due to reduced erythropoietin production.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Chronic Kidney Disease (CKD)"][/sc]
  • A 65-year-old male presents with progressive fatigue, ankle swelling, and nocturia over the last 6 months. He has a history of hypertension and poorly controlled type 2 diabetes. Blood tests reveal an eGFR of 35 mL/min/1.73 m² and microalbuminuria.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Chronic Kidney Disease (CKD)"][/sc]
  • Acute Kidney Injury (AKI) - Sudden decline in kidney function, often reversible, compared to the gradual and irreversible decline in CKD.
  • Nephrotic Syndrome - Characterised by significant proteinuria, oedema, and hypoalbuminaemia, but with normal or near-normal GFR in early stages.
  • Polycystic Kidney Disease - May present similarly, but ultrasound shows large, cystic kidneys, and there is often a family history of the condition.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Chronic Kidney Disease (CKD)"][/sc]
  • Blood pressure control - Target <140/90 mmHg (or <130/80 mmHg in those with proteinuria). First-line medications include ACE inhibitors or angiotensin receptor blockers (ARBs).
  • Glycaemic control - Aim for HbA1c <53 mmol/mol (7%) to slow the progression of CKD in diabetic patients.
  • Dietary management - Salt restriction (<6 g/day), protein restriction in advanced CKD, and managing potassium and phosphate levels.
  • Erythropoietin-stimulating agents (ESA) - For CKD-related anaemia.
  • Renal replacement therapy - Dialysis or kidney transplantation for end-stage renal disease (GFR <15 mL/min/1.73 m²).
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Chronic Kidney Disease (CKD)"][/sc]
  • Cardiovascular disease - Leading cause of death in CKD patients due to hypertension, hyperlipidaemia, and vascular calcification.
  • Bone disease - CKD-mineral and bone disorder (CKD-MBD) occurs due to impaired phosphate excretion and altered calcium metabolism, leading to bone fragility.
  • Hyperkalaemia - Can cause life-threatening cardiac arrhythmias, especially in advanced CKD.
  • Metabolic acidosis - Due to the kidneys' inability to excrete acid, leading to bone resorption and muscle wasting.
