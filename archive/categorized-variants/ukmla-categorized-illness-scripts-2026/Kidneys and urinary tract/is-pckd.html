Polycystic Kidney Disease (PCKD)
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Polycystic Kidney Disease (PCKD)"][/sc]
  • Polycystic Kidney Disease (PCKD) is a genetic disorder characterised by the development of numerous fluid-filled cysts in the kidneys. It can lead to progressive kidney enlargement, hypertension, and eventual kidney failure. The two main forms are autosomal dominant polycystic kidney disease (ADPKD), which typically presents in adulthood, and autosomal recessive polycystic kidney disease (ARPKD), which manifests in infancy or early childhood.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Polycystic Kidney Disease (PCKD)"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Polycystic Kidney Disease (PCKD)"][/sc]
  • UK GP Population Prevalence: ADPKD affects approximately 1 in 1,000 people, making it one of the most common genetic disorders.
  • UK Hospital Prevalence: Around 10% of people with end-stage renal disease (ESRD) requiring dialysis have ADPKD.
  • Worldwide Prevalence: ADPKD has a global prevalence of approximately 4-6 million people. ARPKD is rarer, occurring in 1 in 20,000 live births worldwide.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Polycystic Kidney Disease (PCKD)"][/sc]
  • PCKD is caused by mutations in genes responsible for the production of proteins involved in kidney tubule function. In ADPKD, mutations in the PKD1 or PKD2 genes lead to the formation of numerous cysts. These cysts gradually expand, distorting renal architecture and impairing kidney function. The cysts also trigger a local inflammatory response, resulting in interstitial fibrosis. Over time, this fibrosis contributes to chronic kidney disease (CKD) and eventual ESRD.
  • In ARPKD, mutations in the PKHD1 gene result in cystic dilation of the collecting ducts, with more severe and earlier-onset renal failure.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Polycystic Kidney Disease (PCKD)"][/sc]
  • Early Stage (Childhood to Early Adulthood): In ADPKD, patients are typically asymptomatic or have mild symptoms, with preserved kidney function.
  • Middle Stage (20-50 years): Cysts enlarge progressively, and patients may develop hypertension, haematuria, and flank pain. Renal function begins to decline.
  • Late Stage (Over 50 years): Many patients progress to CKD and ESRD, requiring dialysis or kidney transplantation.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Polycystic Kidney Disease (PCKD)"][/sc]
  • Asymptomatic (early stages): PCKD often presents asymptomatically in its early stages, especially ADPKD. This is due to the slow growth of cysts with no immediate impact on kidney function.
  • Flank Pain: Pain results from the expansion of renal cysts, causing capsular stretching or cyst rupture.
  • Hypertension: Due to the cysts compressing blood vessels, leading to renal ischaemia and increased renin production.
  • Haematuria: Cyst rupture can lead to bleeding into the urinary system, resulting in visible or microscopic blood in the urine.
  • Recurrent UTIs: The cysts can become infected, leading to recurrent urinary tract infections.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Polycystic Kidney Disease (PCKD)"][/sc]
  • Hypertension: Elevated blood pressure due to activation of the renin-angiotensin system as a result of cyst-induced renal ischaemia.
  • Palpable Enlarged Kidneys: On physical examination, the kidneys may be palpable due to cystic enlargement.
  • Abdominal Tenderness: Localised tenderness may occur in the flanks due to expanding cysts or cyst rupture.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Polycystic Kidney Disease (PCKD)"][/sc]
  • Severe Flank Pain with Fever: This may indicate cyst infection or rupture, a serious complication requiring urgent treatment.
  • Gross Haematuria: Significant haematuria could signal a ruptured cyst or bleeding from an aneurysm, requiring prompt investigation to prevent further complications.
  • Sudden Onset Severe Headache: In patients with PCKD, this could be due to a ruptured intracranial aneurysm, which is a life-threatening complication.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Polycystic Kidney Disease (PCKD)"][/sc]
  • Minimal Renal Symptoms with Neurological Complications: In some patients, the first symptom may be a cerebral aneurysm rupture, rather than kidney-related issues.
  • Isolated Hypertension: Some patients may present only with hypertension, without overt kidney-related symptoms, which can delay the diagnosis of PCKD.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Polycystic Kidney Disease (PCKD)"][/sc]
  • Ultrasound: The initial imaging modality of choice, showing multiple renal cysts. In ADPKD, two or more cysts in one kidney in a patient younger than 30 years are diagnostic.
  • MRI or CT Scan: These can provide more detailed information on cyst size, number, and potential complications, such as stones or infection.
  • Genetic Testing: Confirms mutations in PKD1 or PKD2 genes for ADPKD and PKHD1 for ARPKD. This is particularly useful in cases with a family history or unclear diagnosis.
  • Renal Function Tests: eGFR (estimated Glomerular Filtration Rate) to assess the degree of renal impairment.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Polycystic Kidney Disease (PCKD)"][/sc]
  • A 35-year-old male presents with a history of hypertension, dull flank pain, and haematuria. He reports a family history of kidney disease. On examination, he has palpable enlarged kidneys and elevated blood pressure. Ultrasound reveals multiple cysts in both kidneys.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Polycystic Kidney Disease (PCKD)"][/sc]
  • Medullary Sponge Kidney: This condition also involves renal cysts but presents with recurrent kidney stones and urinary tract infections rather than progressive renal failure.
  • Renal Cell Carcinoma (RCC): Unlike PCKD, RCC often presents with a single mass and may cause systemic symptoms like weight loss and fever.
  • Hydronephrosis: Caused by obstruction of urinary flow, leading to kidney enlargement, but without the multiple cysts characteristic of PCKD.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Polycystic Kidney Disease (PCKD)"][/sc]
  • Blood Pressure Control: ACE inhibitors or ARBs are first-line to control hypertension and slow the progression of kidney damage.
  • Tolvaptan: A vasopressin receptor antagonist that can slow the growth of cysts and preserve kidney function in certain patients with ADPKD.
  • Management of Complications: Pain control, treatment of UTIs, and monitoring for intracranial aneurysms are crucial.
  • End-stage Renal Disease: Dialysis or kidney transplantation is required when ESRD develops.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Polycystic Kidney Disease (PCKD)"][/sc]
  • Chronic Kidney Disease (CKD) and End-Stage Renal Disease (ESRD): Occurs in the majority of ADPKD patients by the age of 60, due to progressive loss of kidney function.
  • Intracranial Aneurysms: Occur in approximately 8% of patients with ADPKD, with a risk of rupture and subarachnoid haemorrhage.
  • Liver Cysts: Hepatic cysts are common, particularly in older patients, but they rarely cause liver dysfunction.
