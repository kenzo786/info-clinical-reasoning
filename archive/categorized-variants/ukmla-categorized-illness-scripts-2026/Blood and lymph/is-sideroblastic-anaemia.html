Sideroblastic Anaemia
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Sideroblastic Anaemia"][/sc]
  • Sideroblastic anaemia is a group of heterogeneous disorders characterised by the presence of ringed sideroblasts in the bone marrow and impaired haemoglobin synthesis. It occurs due to defects in mitochondrial iron metabolism, leading to iron being trapped in the mitochondria of developing erythrocytes, which causes ineffective erythropoiesis and microcytic or normocytic anaemia.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Sideroblastic Anaemia"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Sideroblastic Anaemia"][/sc]
  • Prevalence in the UK GP population is rare, estimated to be <0.1% of anaemias.
  • It is more frequently diagnosed in hospitalised patients with chronic anaemia, particularly in haematology clinics, though exact hospital prevalence is unclear.
  • Globally, the condition is rare, but it can be associated with certain regions where specific genetic mutations or environmental toxins are prevalent (e.g. chronic alcoholism or lead exposure).
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Sideroblastic Anaemia"][/sc]
  • The core defect in sideroblastic anaemia is the failure to incorporate iron into haem, which occurs due to defective haemoglobin synthesis. This defect results from abnormalities in the enzymes responsible for mitochondrial iron metabolism (e.g., ALA synthase).
  • As iron is not properly used, it accumulates in the mitochondria of erythroblasts, forming ringed sideroblasts visible in bone marrow biopsies.
  • There are two main types: congenital (e.g., X-linked sideroblastic anaemia) and acquired (e.g., related to myelodysplastic syndromes, alcohol, drugs, or toxins like lead).
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Sideroblastic Anaemia"][/sc]
  • In congenital cases, symptoms may appear in childhood, but the condition can remain undiagnosed until adulthood in milder forms.
  • In acquired sideroblastic anaemia, symptoms typically develop gradually, over months to years, often in association with underlying causes like alcoholism, drug use, or chronic disease.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Sideroblastic Anaemia"][/sc]
  • Fatigue - A common symptom due to decreased oxygen delivery from anaemia.
  • Dyspnoea - Often occurs on exertion due to reduced haemoglobin and oxygen transport.
  • Pallor - Caused by decreased haemoglobin levels in the blood.
  • Dizziness - Due to decreased cerebral oxygenation, common in anaemias.
  • Palpitations - The heart compensates for reduced oxygen-carrying capacity by increasing heart rate.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Sideroblastic Anaemia"][/sc]
  • Pallor - Indicative of anaemia, due to reduced haemoglobin in circulating blood.
  • Tachycardia - A compensatory response to increase oxygen delivery in the context of anaemia.
  • Splenomegaly - Can occur due to increased destruction of defective erythrocytes in congenital or acquired forms.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Sideroblastic Anaemia"][/sc]
  • Severe dyspnoea or chest pain - May indicate cardiac strain or heart failure secondary to chronic severe anaemia.
  • Unexplained neurological symptoms - Can occur in sideroblastic anaemia secondary to lead poisoning, requiring urgent intervention.
  • Marked splenomegaly - May suggest an associated haematological malignancy like myelodysplastic syndrome.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Sideroblastic Anaemia"][/sc]
  • Some patients may present asymptomatically and be diagnosed incidentally with microcytic anaemia on a routine blood test. The absence of symptoms may occur in milder forms, especially in chronic disease or alcoholism-associated cases.
  • Patients with lead poisoning may present with non-specific gastrointestinal symptoms and neurological findings like peripheral neuropathy.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Sideroblastic Anaemia"][/sc]
  • Full Blood Count (FBC) - Typically shows microcytic anaemia (low MCV) but can be normocytic in some cases.
  • Peripheral Blood Smear - Shows hypochromic, microcytic red cells with increased anisocytosis and poikilocytosis.
  • Bone Marrow Biopsy - Shows ringed sideroblasts in erythroblasts (iron-laden mitochondria seen with Prussian blue staining).
  • Serum Ferritin and Iron Studies - Increased ferritin and serum iron with low transferrin saturation, indicating ineffective erythropoiesis.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Sideroblastic Anaemia"][/sc]
  • A 45-year-old male with a history of chronic alcohol use presents with 6 months of progressive fatigue, dyspnoea on exertion, and pallor. FBC shows microcytic anaemia, and bone marrow biopsy reveals ringed sideroblasts.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Sideroblastic Anaemia"][/sc]
  • Iron-deficiency anaemia - Presents with similar microcytic anaemia, but iron studies will show low serum iron and low ferritin, in contrast to sideroblastic anaemia where iron stores are elevated.
  • Thalassaemia - Also causes microcytic anaemia, but electrophoresis will show abnormal haemoglobin patterns, and there will be no ringed sideroblasts.
  • Lead poisoning - Can cause sideroblastic anaemia, but associated findings include basophilic stippling on a blood smear and a history of exposure to lead.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Sideroblastic Anaemia"][/sc]
  • Acquired cases - Management is targeted at the underlying cause (e.g., alcohol cessation, removal of lead exposure). Vitamin B6 (pyridoxine) supplementation can be helpful, especially in congenital cases or those due to certain medications.
  • Congenital cases - Pyridoxine supplementation is the mainstay of treatment. In more severe cases, blood transfusions may be required.
  • Myelodysplastic syndromes - More complex treatment options include erythropoiesis-stimulating agents, chemotherapy, or bone marrow transplant in severe cases.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Sideroblastic Anaemia"][/sc]
  • Heart failure - From chronic severe anaemia leading to high-output cardiac failure.
  • Secondary haemochromatosis - Iron overload due to repeated blood transfusions or ineffective erythropoiesis can cause liver cirrhosis, diabetes, and cardiomyopathy.
