Anaemia (Shortness of Breath - SOB)
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Anaemia (Shortness of Breath - SOB)"][/sc]
  • Anaemia is a condition characterised by a reduction in the haemoglobin concentration in the blood, leading to impaired oxygen transport to tissues. The World Health Organisation defines anaemia as haemoglobin levels below 130 g/L in men, 120 g/L in non-pregnant women, and 110 g/L in pregnant women.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Anaemia (Shortness of Breath - SOB)"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Anaemia (Shortness of Breath - SOB)"][/sc]
  • Prevalence in the UK GP population: Anaemia is present in approximately 3-5% of the general population and more common in women and the elderly.
  • UK hospital prevalence: Anaemia is found in up to 25% of hospitalised patients.
  • Worldwide prevalence: Anaemia affects about 24.8% of the global population, with iron-deficiency anaemia being the most common subtype.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Anaemia (Shortness of Breath - SOB)"][/sc]
  • Reduced haemoglobin production: Causes include iron deficiency, vitamin B12/folate deficiency, and chronic diseases impairing erythropoiesis.
  • Increased haemoglobin loss: Due to bleeding (e.g., gastrointestinal, menstrual, trauma).
  • Increased haemolysis: Conditions such as haemolytic anaemias result in accelerated red blood cell destruction.
  • Impaired oxygen transport: Reduced haemoglobin leads to hypoxia, triggering compensatory mechanisms like increased cardiac output and respiratory rate.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Anaemia (Shortness of Breath - SOB)"][/sc]
  • Acute: Rapid onset, often due to bleeding or haemolysis, leading to symptoms like sudden fatigue and dyspnoea.
  • Chronic: Gradual onset over weeks to months, commonly seen in nutritional deficiencies or chronic diseases, with slower compensatory adjustments by the body.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Anaemia (Shortness of Breath - SOB)"][/sc]
  • Fatigue: Caused by tissue hypoxia reducing energy production.
  • Shortness of breath (SOB): Exertional dyspnoea occurs due to insufficient oxygen delivery during physical activity.
  • Palpitations: Compensatory tachycardia increases cardiac output to maintain oxygen delivery.
  • Pallor: Reduced haemoglobin levels result in pale skin and mucous membranes.
  • Dizziness: Hypoperfusion to the brain due to anaemia-induced hypoxia.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Anaemia (Shortness of Breath - SOB)"][/sc]
  • Pallor: Observed in skin and conjunctiva due to decreased haemoglobin concentration.
  • Increased heart rate: A compensatory mechanism to improve oxygen delivery to tissues.
  • Systolic flow murmur: High-output state from decreased blood viscosity in severe anaemia.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Anaemia (Shortness of Breath - SOB)"][/sc]
  • Haematemesis or melena: Suggests significant gastrointestinal bleeding.
  • Unexplained weight loss: May indicate malignancy-related anaemia.
  • Neurological symptoms (e.g., paraesthesia, ataxia): Seen in vitamin B12 deficiency anaemia.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Anaemia (Shortness of Breath - SOB)"][/sc]
  • Asymptomatic: Common in mild anaemia, especially in chronic cases where compensatory mechanisms develop gradually.
  • Heart failure exacerbation: In patients with comorbidities, anaemia may worsen pre-existing conditions.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Anaemia (Shortness of Breath - SOB)"][/sc]
  • Full blood count (FBC): Low haemoglobin, mean corpuscular volume (MCV), and red cell distribution width (RDW) help differentiate anaemia subtypes.
  • Iron studies: Low ferritin indicates iron deficiency; high ferritin suggests inflammation or chronic disease.
  • Vitamin B12 and folate levels: Deficiencies confirm megaloblastic anaemia.
  • Peripheral blood smear: Identifies morphological changes in red blood cells.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Anaemia (Shortness of Breath - SOB)"][/sc]
  • A 65-year-old woman presents with progressive fatigue, shortness of breath on exertion, and pallor over three months. Investigations reveal microcytic hypochromic anaemia.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Anaemia (Shortness of Breath - SOB)"][/sc]
  • Iron-deficiency anaemia: Low ferritin, microcytosis, and hypochromia differentiate it.
  • Anaemia of chronic disease: Normocytic or mildly microcytic with raised ferritin.
  • Vitamin B12 deficiency: Macrocytosis and neurological symptoms.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Anaemia (Shortness of Breath - SOB)"][/sc]
  • Iron deficiency: Oral ferrous sulphate; intravenous iron for intolerance or severe deficiency.
  • Vitamin B12 deficiency: Intramuscular hydroxocobalamin injections.
  • Chronic disease-related anaemia: Treat the underlying condition; erythropoiesis-stimulating agents if severe.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Anaemia (Shortness of Breath - SOB)"][/sc]
  • Heart failure: Chronic anaemia increases cardiac workload.
  • Impaired quality of life: Persistent fatigue and reduced functional capacity.
