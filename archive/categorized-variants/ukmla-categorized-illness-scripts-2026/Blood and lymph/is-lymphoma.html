Lymphoma
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Lymphoma"][/sc]
  • Lymphoma is a type of cancer that originates in the lymphatic system, particularly affecting lymphocytes, a subset of white blood cells. The two main categories are Hodgkin lymphoma (HL) and non-Hodgkin lymphoma (NHL), distinguished by the presence of Reed-Sternberg cells in HL. Lymphoma can manifest in lymph nodes or extranodal sites, leading to systemic and local symptoms.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Lymphoma"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Lymphoma"][/sc]
  • Lymphoma accounts for approximately 5% of all cancers globally. In the UK, it has an annual incidence rate of about 14 per 100,000 for NHL and 2.7 per 100,000 for HL. NHL is more prevalent in older adults, whereas HL has a bimodal distribution, peaking in young adults (ages 20-30) and again in those over 55.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Lymphoma"][/sc]
  • Lymphoma arises from genetic mutations in lymphocytes, causing uncontrolled proliferation and survival. In HL, the characteristic Reed-Sternberg cells release cytokines, leading to inflammatory responses and systemic symptoms. NHL has diverse subtypes, each with different mutations affecting B-cells or T-cells. As the disease progresses, malignant cells accumulate in lymph nodes or spread to other organs, disrupting immune function and causing tissue infiltration.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Lymphoma"][/sc]
  • Lymphoma can present acutely or insidiously over months to years. Hodgkin lymphoma tends to have a more predictable course with early-stage curability, whereas NHL can range from indolent (slow-growing) to aggressive. Without treatment, indolent forms may progress over years, while aggressive forms can lead to rapid deterioration over weeks to months.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Lymphoma"][/sc]
  • Painless lymphadenopathy - Swelling of lymph nodes without tenderness, commonly in the neck, armpit, or groin. This occurs due to lymphocyte accumulation.
  • B symptoms (fever, night sweats, weight loss) - These systemic symptoms reflect cytokine release and systemic inflammation, more common in advanced disease.
  • Fatigue - Generalised fatigue results from systemic inflammatory responses and, in advanced stages, from anaemia or bone marrow involvement.
  • Pruritus - Generalised itching, often severe, possibly due to cytokine release, especially in Hodgkin lymphoma.
  • Cough or chest discomfort - Mediastinal lymphadenopathy can compress airways or adjacent structures, leading to respiratory symptoms.
  • Abdominal discomfort or fullness - Enlarged abdominal lymph nodes or splenomegaly can cause feelings of bloating or distension.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Lymphoma"][/sc]
  • Painless, enlarged lymph nodes - Typically >1 cm, firm, non-tender, mobile nodes, commonly seen in cervical, axillary, or inguinal regions.
  • Hepatosplenomegaly - Enlarged liver and spleen due to lymphoid cell infiltration, often seen in advanced disease.
  • Pallor - Anaemia from bone marrow infiltration or chronic inflammation can manifest as pale skin and mucous membranes.
  • Skin rash or nodules - Occasionally, lymphoma can infiltrate the skin (cutaneous lymphoma) presenting with nodules or rashes.
  • Superior vena cava syndrome - Facial swelling and cyanosis due to compression of the superior vena cava by mediastinal lymphadenopathy.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Lymphoma"][/sc]
  • B symptoms - These indicate advanced disease and a worse prognosis due to systemic inflammation and organ involvement.
  • Rapidly enlarging lymph nodes - Suggests aggressive lymphoma or disease progression, requiring urgent investigation.
  • Superior vena cava syndrome - Obstruction of venous drainage, leading to facial swelling, cyanosis, and dyspnoea, indicating significant mediastinal lymphadenopathy.
  • Neurological symptoms - Spinal cord compression from lymphomatous involvement of the spine, or CNS involvement, can lead to paralysis or cognitive changes, requiring emergency intervention.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Lymphoma"][/sc]
  • Extranodal involvement without lymphadenopathy - Particularly in NHL, where lymphoma may present in the stomach, intestines, skin, or CNS without significant peripheral lymph node enlargement.
  • Isolated cytopenias - Involvement of the bone marrow may manifest solely as anaemia, leukopenia, or thrombocytopenia, especially in indolent forms.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Lymphoma"][/sc]
  • Excisional lymph node biopsy - Gold standard for diagnosis. Reed-Sternberg cells are diagnostic of Hodgkin lymphoma, whereas NHL has varied histological features depending on the subtype.
  • Full blood count (FBC) - May reveal anaemia, leukopenia, or thrombocytopenia due to bone marrow infiltration.
  • Lactate dehydrogenase (LDH) - Elevated in aggressive lymphomas due to high cell turnover.
  • Positron emission tomography (PET) scan - Useful for staging and assessing metabolic activity of lymph nodes and extranodal sites.
  • CT scan - Used for staging by identifying the size and distribution of lymphadenopathy.
  • Bone marrow biopsy - Performed in advanced stages to assess for marrow involvement.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Lymphoma"][/sc]
  • A 25-year-old male presents with a 2-month history of painless cervical lymphadenopathy, intermittent fever, and night sweats. Physical examination reveals enlarged, non-tender cervical nodes, and hepatosplenomegaly.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Lymphoma"][/sc]
  • Infectious mononucleosis - Characterised by fever, pharyngitis, and lymphadenopathy. Lymph node enlargement in lymphoma is typically more persistent and non-tender compared to the tender nodes seen in infection.
  • Leukaemia - Both conditions can cause lymphadenopathy, but leukaemia is more likely to present with significant cytopenias and circulating blasts on a blood smear.
  • Sarcoidosis - Can present with lymphadenopathy but usually has associated pulmonary involvement and a non-caseating granulomatous histology.
  • Systemic lupus erythematosus (SLE) - May cause lymphadenopathy, but is associated with autoimmune features, such as a malar rash and multi-organ involvement.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Lymphoma"][/sc]
  • Hodgkin lymphoma - Treated with combination chemotherapy (e.g., ABVD regimen: Adriamycin, Bleomycin, Vinblastine, Dacarbazine) with or without radiotherapy, depending on the stage.
  • Non-Hodgkin lymphoma - Treatment varies based on subtype and stage. Indolent forms may be managed with watchful waiting or rituximab-based regimens. Aggressive forms are treated with combination chemotherapy (e.g., R-CHOP: Rituximab, Cyclophosphamide, Hydroxydaunomycin, Oncovin, Prednisolone).
  • Supportive care - Involves treating complications such as infections, anaemia, or tumour lysis syndrome, and providing palliative care if necessary.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Lymphoma"][/sc]
  • Tumour lysis syndrome - Rapid destruction of lymphoma cells, especially following treatment, leading to hyperkalaemia, hyperuricaemia, and acute kidney injury.
  • Bone marrow failure - Advanced lymphoma may infiltrate the bone marrow, leading to pancytopenia and increased susceptibility to infections and bleeding.
  • Second malignancies - Chemotherapy and radiotherapy increase the risk of secondary cancers, such as leukaemia or solid tumours, in survivors.
