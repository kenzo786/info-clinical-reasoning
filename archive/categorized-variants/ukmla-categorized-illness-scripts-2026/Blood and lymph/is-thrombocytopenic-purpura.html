Thrombocytopenic Purpura
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Thrombocytopenic Purpura"][/sc]
  • Thrombocytopenic purpura is a condition characterised by a low platelet count (thrombocytopenia) that leads to purpuric (purple discolouration) lesions on the skin and mucous membranes. It results primarily from immune-mediated platelet destruction, often manifesting as bruising, petechiae, and bleeding tendencies.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Thrombocytopenic Purpura"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Thrombocytopenic Purpura"][/sc]
  • Prevalence (UK GP population): Roughly 1 in 10,000 individuals per year.
  • Prevalence (UK hospitals): Common haematologic disorder, accounting for a significant proportion of referrals for unexplained bleeding and bruising.
  • Global prevalence: Incidence varies but is estimated at approximately 1-6 cases per 100,000 people annually.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Thrombocytopenic Purpura"][/sc]
  • Thrombocytopenic purpura (especially immune thrombocytopenic purpura) arises when autoantibodies target platelet antigens, marking platelets for destruction. This immune response triggers splenic macrophages to clear platelets prematurely, leading to a reduced platelet count.
  • The absence of platelets compromises primary haemostasis, which disrupts clot formation at sites of vascular injury, resulting in bleeding.
  • In secondary causes (e.g., thrombotic thrombocytopenic purpura, TTP), excessive consumption of platelets occurs due to microthrombi formation in small vessels.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Thrombocytopenic Purpura"][/sc]
  • Acute (days-weeks): Sudden onset of bruising, petechiae, and mucosal bleeding. Typical in idiopathic thrombocytopenic purpura (ITP) in children post-viral infection.
  • Chronic (months-years): Slow progression of symptoms; more common in adults with ITP, often relapsing and remitting.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Thrombocytopenic Purpura"][/sc]
  • Bruising - Caused by inadequate clot formation due to reduced platelets; presents as spontaneous or minimal-trauma bruising.
  • Petechiae - Small, red-purple spots due to subcutaneous bleeding, often in dependent areas; result from capillary bleeding.
  • Mucosal bleeding - Includes epistaxis, gum bleeding, and menorrhagia, occurring due to fragile vascular structures lacking adequate platelet protection.
  • Haematuria and gastrointestinal bleeding - Rare but severe, these indicate significant thrombocytopenia with vascular compromise.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Thrombocytopenic Purpura"][/sc]
  • Petechiae - Non-blanching spots on the skin, signalling capillary bleeding; a primary sign of platelet dysfunction.
  • Ecchymoses (bruises) - Widespread bruising disproportionate to trauma; indicative of inadequate platelet levels.
  • Gingival bleeding - Spot bleeding from gums without provocation, reflecting mucosal fragility.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Thrombocytopenic Purpura"][/sc]
  • Severe gastrointestinal bleeding - Signifies major vascular bleeding; requires urgent intervention.
  • Severe, unprovoked haemorrhages - Indicates profound thrombocytopenia; often warrants immediate treatment.
  • Neurological symptoms (TTP) - Suggests thrombotic thrombocytopenic purpura with potential central nervous system involvement; requires urgent plasma exchange.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Thrombocytopenic Purpura"][/sc]
  • Some patients may present with minimal to no bleeding symptoms despite low platelets; this is often seen in chronic ITP with compensatory mechanisms limiting bleeding.
  • Non-purpuric rash resembling allergic reaction may occur, occasionally misattributed to another cause in early TTP cases.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Thrombocytopenic Purpura"][/sc]
  • Full blood count (FBC): Confirms low platelet count, typically below 100,000/mcL in ITP, with otherwise normal white cells and haemoglobin.
  • Peripheral blood smear: Examines for large, immature platelets in ITP or fragmented cells in TTP, aiding differential diagnosis.
  • Bone marrow biopsy (if indicated): Reserved for atypical cases to exclude marrow pathology; normal or increased megakaryocytes suggest ITP.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Thrombocytopenic Purpura"][/sc]
  • A 35-year-old female presents with progressive bruising, petechiae, and mild gingival bleeding over three weeks, with no significant past history, suggestive of immune thrombocytopenic purpura.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Thrombocytopenic Purpura"][/sc]
  • Thrombotic thrombocytopenic purpura (TTP): Presents with fever, neurological symptoms, and haemolysis in addition to thrombocytopenia, distinguishing it from ITP.
  • Leukaemia: Thrombocytopenia accompanied by anaemia, leukocytosis, or blasts on blood smear, differentiating it from isolated platelet deficiency in ITP.
  • Aplastic anaemia: Pancytopenia with no megakaryocyte production in bone marrow, in contrast to isolated thrombocytopenia in ITP.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Thrombocytopenic Purpura"][/sc]
  • First-line therapy: Corticosteroids (e.g., prednisolone), aimed at reducing immune-mediated platelet destruction.
  • Second-line therapies: Rituximab or thrombopoietin receptor agonists for refractory cases or those with steroid dependency.
  • Splenectomy: Considered in severe or chronic cases where other treatments fail.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Thrombocytopenic Purpura"][/sc]
  • Severe haemorrhage: Occurs in less than 5% of ITP patients but can be life-threatening, especially in the central nervous system.
  • Infection (post-splenectomy): Higher risk due to asplenia, necessitating vaccination and prophylactic antibiotics.
