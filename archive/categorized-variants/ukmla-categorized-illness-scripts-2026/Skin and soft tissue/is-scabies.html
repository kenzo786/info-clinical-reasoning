Scabies
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Scabies"][/sc]
  • Scabies is a contagious skin infestation caused by the Sarcoptes scabiei mite, leading to intense itching and a characteristic rash due to an allergic reaction to the mites, eggs, and faeces within the skin. Transmission occurs primarily through prolonged skin-to-skin contact, and it is prevalent in crowded living conditions.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Scabies"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Scabies"][/sc]
  • UK General Practice: Estimated prevalence of scabies in GP consultations is 2.8 per 1,000 patients annually, with higher rates in nursing homes and institutional settings.
  • UK Hospital Setting: Scabies outbreaks are seen sporadically in hospitals, often in care facilities for the elderly or vulnerable populations.
  • Worldwide Prevalence: Scabies affects over 200 million people globally, with the highest incidence in tropical and resource-limited regions.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Scabies"][/sc]
  • 1. The scabies mite burrows into the epidermis to lay eggs, leading to a localized immune response.
  • 2. The host immune system reacts to mite antigens, causing hypersensitivity and intense pruritus.
  • 3. Burrowing creates serpiginous (wavy) burrows, especially in intertriginous areas (e.g., web spaces of fingers, wrists).
  • 4. Persistent scratching can lead to secondary bacterial infection, commonly by Staphylococcus or Streptococcus species.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Scabies"][/sc]
  • Incubation Period: 2-6 weeks in first-time infestations; 1-4 days in recurrent infestations due to sensitisation.
  • Symptom Progression: Intense itching typically begins 2-6 weeks after exposure and worsens, often intensifying at night.
  • Resolution with Treatment: Symptoms typically resolve within 2 weeks after effective treatment, but itching may persist for several weeks due to immune response.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Scabies"][/sc]
  • Intense itching - Often worse at night; due to hypersensitivity to mite antigens in the skin.
  • Rash - Erythematous papules, vesicles, and pustules due to inflammation from mite burrows and immune response.
  • Burrows - Fine, wavy lines in the skin, typically found in web spaces, wrists, and genital area; caused by the mites' movement within the epidermis.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Scabies"][/sc]
  • Scabies burrows - Short, wavy, slightly raised lines, usually found in the web spaces of the fingers and wrists.
  • Erythematous papules - Small, red bumps due to inflammatory response.
  • Excoriations - Linear scratch marks from itching, increasing the risk of secondary bacterial infection.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Scabies"][/sc]
  • Crusted (Norwegian) scabies - Extensive, thick crusting with minimal pruritus; indicates severe infestation, often seen in immunocompromised patients. Can lead to widespread outbreaks due to high mite load.
  • Secondary bacterial infection - Signs include oozing, yellow crusts (impetigo), and localised cellulitis; can lead to cellulitis or sepsis if untreated.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Scabies"][/sc]
  • Crusted scabies - May present without typical pruritus or burrows but with extensive scaling; often missed, especially in elderly or immunocompromised patients.
  • Scalp or face involvement - Rare in classic scabies but more common in infants, elderly, and those with compromised immune systems.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Scabies"][/sc]
  • Skin scraping - Identification of mites, eggs, or faeces under microscopy confirms diagnosis; burrows or papules are scraped and examined.
  • Dermatoscopy - Can reveal characteristic “jet with contrail” appearance of burrows with mite at the leading end.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Scabies"][/sc]
  • A 24-year-old male presents with intense itching, primarily on his hands and wrists, worsening over 4 weeks, and unresponsive to moisturisers. Physical exam shows erythematous papules and linear burrows in the interdigital spaces.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Scabies"][/sc]
  • Contact dermatitis - Often has a known allergen trigger and lacks burrows; distribution may be limited to contact areas.
  • Atopic dermatitis - Chronic eczematous patches, often with a family history; typically involves flexural areas.
  • Insect bites - Often more isolated and do not follow a serpiginous pattern; usually self-limiting.
  • Psoriasis - Plaques with silvery scales, commonly found on extensor surfaces rather than interdigital spaces.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Scabies"][/sc]
  • Permethrin cream 5% - Applied to entire body from neck down and left on for 8-12 hours; repeat application in 7 days as per UK guidelines.
  • Oral ivermectin - Used in crusted scabies or if topical treatment fails; requires two doses, 1 week apart.
  • Hygiene measures - Wash bedding, clothing, and towels at 60°C; household contacts should be treated concurrently.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Scabies"][/sc]
  • Secondary bacterial infections - Common due to excoriation; can lead to cellulitis or impetigo if untreated.
  • Crusted scabies - Highly infectious form, leading to outbreaks in close-contact settings; difficult to treat and more prone to recurrence.
  • Post-scabies pruritus - Persistent itching due to prolonged immune response; may last several weeks post-treatment.
