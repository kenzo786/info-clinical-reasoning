Cow’s Milk Allergy (CMA)
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Cow’s Milk Allergy (CMA)"][/sc]
  • Cow’s milk allergy (CMA) is an adverse immune-mediated reaction to proteins found in cow’s milk. It is classified into IgE-mediated, non-IgE-mediated, or mixed types, depending on the underlying immunological mechanism. It is the most common food allergy in infants and young children.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Cow’s Milk Allergy (CMA)"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Cow’s Milk Allergy (CMA)"][/sc]
  • Prevalence in UK GP population: Approximately 2-3% of children under 3 years.
  • Hospital prevalence: Higher due to more severe cases, including failure to thrive and anaphylaxis.
  • Worldwide prevalence: Similar estimates of 2-7% depending on region and diagnostic criteria.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Cow’s Milk Allergy (CMA)"][/sc]
  • IgE-mediated CMA: Rapid hypersensitivity reaction due to IgE antibodies binding milk proteins, leading to histamine release from mast cells and basophils.
  • Non-IgE-mediated CMA: T-cell-mediated response resulting in chronic inflammation, often involving the gastrointestinal tract.
  • Mixed type: Features of both IgE and non-IgE mechanisms.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Cow’s Milk Allergy (CMA)"][/sc]
  • Acute reactions (IgE-mediated): Within minutes to 2 hours of milk ingestion.
  • Delayed reactions (non-IgE-mediated): Several hours to days post-exposure, presenting with chronic symptoms.
  • Resolution: Most children outgrow CMA by age 5, but severe cases may persist into adulthood.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Cow’s Milk Allergy (CMA)"][/sc]
  • Gastrointestinal symptoms - Diarrhoea, vomiting, and abdominal pain due to inflammation of the gastrointestinal lining (non-IgE-mediated).
  • Skin symptoms - Urticaria and angioedema occur due to histamine-mediated capillary leakage (IgE-mediated).
  • Respiratory symptoms - Wheezing or stridor, often linked to severe IgE-mediated reactions affecting the airway.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Cow’s Milk Allergy (CMA)"][/sc]
  • Failure to thrive - Poor weight gain and growth from chronic malabsorption in non-IgE-mediated cases.
  • Eczema - Exacerbation due to non-IgE immune activation leading to skin barrier dysfunction.
  • Oedema - Seen in severe cases, potentially from protein-losing enteropathy.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Cow’s Milk Allergy (CMA)"][/sc]
  • Anaphylaxis - Generalised urticaria, hypotension, and respiratory distress; requires immediate treatment with intramuscular adrenaline.
  • Severe dehydration - Due to persistent vomiting and diarrhoea, signifying significant gastrointestinal involvement.
  • Growth faltering - Chronic malabsorption or inadequate dietary intake from strict elimination diets.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Cow’s Milk Allergy (CMA)"][/sc]
  • Chronic constipation with soft stools – Seen in non-IgE-mediated CMA with colonic inflammation.
  • Recurrent infections – May result from compromised nutritional status.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Cow’s Milk Allergy (CMA)"][/sc]
  • Skin prick test - Positive wheal for cow’s milk proteins in IgE-mediated CMA.
  • Serum-specific IgE levels - Elevated IgE antibodies to cow’s milk proteins.
  • Food elimination and challenge - Resolution of symptoms with milk elimination and recurrence on reintroduction, used in non-IgE cases.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Cow’s Milk Allergy (CMA)"][/sc]
  • A 6-month-old infant presents with recurrent vomiting, diarrhoea, and eczema, with onset shortly after introduction of formula containing cow’s milk.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Cow’s Milk Allergy (CMA)"][/sc]
  • Lactose intolerance - Lacks immune involvement, presents with bloating and diarrhoea.
  • Gastroesophageal reflux disease - Chronic vomiting without immune-mediated symptoms like eczema or anaphylaxis.
  • Coeliac disease - Triggered by gluten, associated with specific antibodies and villous atrophy.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Cow’s Milk Allergy (CMA)"][/sc]
  • Strict avoidance of cow’s milk protein.
  • Use of extensively hydrolysed or amino acid-based formulas for infants.
  • Reintroduction trials under medical supervision, typically after age 1 year.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Cow’s Milk Allergy (CMA)"][/sc]
  • Anaphylaxis - Life-threatening; managed with adrenaline and supportive care.
  • Growth delay - Due to nutritional deficiencies from elimination diets.
  • Psychological impact - Increased anxiety in caregivers over dietary restrictions and fear of reactions.
