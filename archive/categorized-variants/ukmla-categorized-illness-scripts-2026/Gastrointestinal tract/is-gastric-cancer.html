Gastric Cancer
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Gastric Cancer"][/sc]
  • Gastric cancer refers to the malignant growth of cells in the lining of the stomach. The majority of cases are adenocarcinomas, arising from the glandular cells of the stomach lining. Other histological subtypes include lymphoma, gastrointestinal stromal tumours (GISTs), and neuroendocrine tumours. Gastric cancer often progresses silently, only becoming symptomatic in advanced stages.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Gastric Cancer"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Gastric Cancer"][/sc]
  • Worldwide Prevalence: Gastric cancer is the fifth most common cancer worldwide, with over 1 million new cases annually. It is more prevalent in East Asia (especially Japan, Korea, and China).
  • UK Prevalence: Gastric cancer is less common in the UK, accounting for about 6,500 new cases per year. The incidence is declining, but it remains more common in older adults, particularly over the age of 60.
  • Risk Factors: Helicobacter pylori infection, chronic gastritis, dietary factors (high salt, smoked foods), smoking, and a family history of gastric cancer.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Gastric Cancer"][/sc]
  • Gastric cancer begins with chronic inflammation and mucosal damage, often due to Helicobacter pylori infection. This leads to atrophic gastritis and intestinal metaplasia, a precancerous state. Over time, genetic mutations (e.g., in CDH1, TP53) accumulate, resulting in dysplastic cells that progress to invasive adenocarcinoma. Tumour growth leads to local invasion, lymphatic spread, and distant metastasis, most commonly to the liver, peritoneum, and lungs.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Gastric Cancer"][/sc]
  • Early Stage: Often asymptomatic, may progress over months or years. Precancerous changes develop slowly.
  • Advanced Stage: Symptoms such as weight loss, dysphagia, and persistent epigastric pain develop as the tumour grows. The disease can metastasise within months of symptom onset if untreated.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Gastric Cancer"][/sc]
  • Weight loss - Common due to tumour-induced anorexia and altered metabolism. Tumour cytokines like TNF-α and IL-6 contribute to cachexia.
  • Persistent epigastric pain - Often caused by the invasion of the tumour into surrounding tissues, leading to nerve irritation.
  • Nausea and vomiting - Results from gastric outlet obstruction or peritoneal involvement causing delayed gastric emptying.
  • Dysphagia - In cases where the tumour is located near the gastroesophageal junction, leading to obstruction.
  • Haematemesis or melena - Tumour ulceration can cause bleeding into the gastrointestinal tract.
  • Early satiety - A result of the tumour reducing the stomach's functional capacity.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Gastric Cancer"][/sc]
  • Pallor - Anaemia from chronic blood loss due to tumour bleeding.
  • Palpable abdominal mass - In advanced cases, the tumour may be felt in the epigastrium.
  • Virchow’s node (left supraclavicular lymphadenopathy) - Indicative of metastatic spread via the thoracic duct.
  • Sister Mary Joseph nodule - A palpable nodule at the umbilicus, indicating peritoneal metastasis.
  • Ascites - Associated with peritoneal metastases or liver involvement, leading to fluid accumulation.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Gastric Cancer"][/sc]
  • Unexplained weight loss - Signifies systemic effects of advanced malignancy or metastasis.
  • Progressive dysphagia - Suggests an obstructive tumour at the gastroesophageal junction.
  • Iron-deficiency anaemia - A result of chronic gastrointestinal bleeding.
  • Persistent vomiting - May indicate gastric outlet obstruction by the tumour.
  • Haematemesis or melena - Suggests tumour ulceration and gastrointestinal bleeding.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Gastric Cancer"][/sc]
  • Some patients may present with only vague symptoms such as dyspepsia or intermittent epigastric discomfort, which can be mistaken for benign peptic ulcer disease or functional dyspepsia.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Gastric Cancer"][/sc]
  • Upper GI endoscopy - The gold standard for diagnosis, allows visualisation and biopsy of gastric lesions. Histological examination confirms adenocarcinoma.
  • CT scan - Used for staging to assess local invasion, lymph node involvement, and distant metastasis.
  • FBC - May show microcytic anaemia due to chronic bleeding.
  • Liver function tests - Elevated ALP or GGT may suggest liver metastases.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Gastric Cancer"][/sc]
  • A 68-year-old male with a 3-month history of progressive weight loss, early satiety, and intermittent epigastric pain. Endoscopy revealed a gastric mass, confirmed to be adenocarcinoma on biopsy.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Gastric Cancer"][/sc]
  • Peptic ulcer disease: Similar epigastric pain but often relieved by food or antacids; no weight loss or progressive symptoms.
  • Gastroesophageal reflux disease (GORD): Typically presents with heartburn and regurgitation, but lacks weight loss or anaemia.
  • Gastric lymphoma: Presents similarly but may show different histological findings (e.g., lymphoid tissue involvement).
  • Pancreatic cancer: Can cause epigastric pain and weight loss, but often presents with jaundice and back pain due to tumour location.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Gastric Cancer"][/sc]
  • Early-stage disease: Surgical resection (gastrectomy) is the primary treatment. Endoscopic submucosal dissection (ESD) may be used for very early cancers.
  • Advanced-stage disease: Chemotherapy (e.g., FLOT regimen: 5-FU, leucovorin, oxaliplatin, docetaxel) is used for unresectable or metastatic disease. Targeted therapies (e.g., trastuzumab for HER2-positive tumours) may also be considered.
  • Palliative care: Symptom management, including relief of pain, obstruction, and nutritional support, is essential in advanced cases.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Gastric Cancer"][/sc]
  • Metastasis: Most commonly to the liver, peritoneum, and lungs, leading to multi-organ dysfunction and poor prognosis.
  • Gastric outlet obstruction: Tumour growth near the pylorus can block gastric emptying, causing persistent vomiting and nutritional deficiencies.
  • Perforation: Tumour ulceration can result in gastric perforation, leading to peritonitis, a surgical emergency.
