Short Bowel Syndrome (SBS)
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Short Bowel Syndrome (SBS)"][/sc]
  • Short Bowel Syndrome (SBS) is a malabsorptive condition resulting from extensive surgical resection of the small intestine. It occurs when there is insufficient intestinal surface area for adequate absorption of nutrients, leading to malnutrition, diarrhoea, dehydration, and electrolyte imbalances.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Short Bowel Syndrome (SBS)"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Short Bowel Syndrome (SBS)"][/sc]
  • In the UK, SBS is relatively rare, with an incidence of about 2 per million people per year.
  • Worldwide, prevalence is difficult to estimate due to variations in surgical practices and healthcare access. However, it is more common in areas with higher rates of bowel resection surgeries for conditions such as Crohn’s disease, mesenteric ischemia, and trauma.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Short Bowel Syndrome (SBS)"][/sc]
  • The small intestine is responsible for nutrient absorption. In SBS, a large portion of this organ is removed, which drastically reduces the absorptive surface area.
  • This results in inadequate absorption of macronutrients, electrolytes, and water, leading to malnutrition, dehydration, and diarrhoea.
  • The ileum is especially important for absorbing bile salts and vitamin B12. When the ileum is resected, bile salts are lost, leading to fat malabsorption and steatorrhoea.
  • In severe cases, intestinal adaptation may not fully compensate, necessitating lifelong parenteral nutrition.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Short Bowel Syndrome (SBS)"][/sc]
  • Acute Phase (first 4-6 weeks post-surgery): Patients experience significant diarrhoea, dehydration, and malnutrition due to the immediate loss of intestinal function.
  • Adaptation Phase (6-12 months): The remaining bowel undergoes structural and functional adaptation, increasing absorption efficiency.
  • Maintenance Phase: The patient reaches a stable state of intestinal absorption, but may still require dietary modifications and possibly parenteral nutrition.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Short Bowel Syndrome (SBS)"][/sc]
  • Diarrhoea - The most common symptom due to the loss of absorptive capacity of the bowel, leading to excess water in the stool.
  • Weight loss - Due to malabsorption of nutrients, especially in the acute phase.
  • Dehydration - Caused by excessive water loss in stools, worsened by reduced electrolyte absorption.
  • Steatorrhoea - Fatty stools due to malabsorption of fats, especially if the ileum is resected.
  • Fatigue - A result of malnutrition and electrolyte imbalances, especially low potassium and magnesium.
  • Vitamin deficiencies - Particularly vitamin B12 (if the terminal ileum is resected), fat-soluble vitamins (A, D, E, K), and minerals (iron, calcium, zinc).
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Short Bowel Syndrome (SBS)"][/sc]
  • Muscle wasting - Due to chronic malnutrition and weight loss.
  • Dry mucous membranes - A sign of dehydration, often noted on examination.
  • Abdominal distension - May be present due to bowel gas or fluid accumulation, particularly in cases of bacterial overgrowth.
  • Pallor - Due to anaemia, often caused by iron or vitamin B12 deficiency.
  • Oedema - Secondary to protein malnutrition, which leads to hypoalbuminaemia and subsequent fluid retention.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Short Bowel Syndrome (SBS)"][/sc]
  • Severe dehydration - May indicate the need for hospitalisation and parenteral fluid therapy.
  • Refractory diarrhoea - Suggests inadequate adaptation or underlying infection requiring further investigation.
  • Sudden weight loss - Could signal severe malnutrition or complications like intestinal obstruction.
  • Sepsis or fever - Could indicate bacterial translocation or an intra-abdominal abscess, which is a potential complication of SBS.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Short Bowel Syndrome (SBS)"][/sc]
  • Mild symptoms - In some cases, patients with less extensive resections may present with subtle symptoms like mild diarrhoea or fatigue, which could be mistaken for irritable bowel syndrome (IBS) or other benign conditions.
  • Intermittent symptoms - Periodic flare-ups of diarrhoea and malabsorption, sometimes associated with dietary changes, may delay diagnosis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Short Bowel Syndrome (SBS)"][/sc]
  • Full Blood Count (FBC) - May show anaemia due to vitamin B12 or iron deficiency.
  • Electrolytes (U&Es) - May reveal low potassium, sodium, and magnesium, reflecting malabsorption and dehydration.
  • Stool fat analysis - Confirmatory for steatorrhoea, indicating fat malabsorption.
  • Serum vitamin levels - Low levels of fat-soluble vitamins (A, D, E, K) and vitamin B12, which indicate specific nutrient malabsorption.
  • Abdominal imaging (CT/MRI) - May be used to assess for complications like obstruction, abscess, or small bowel dilation.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Short Bowel Syndrome (SBS)"][/sc]
  • A 45-year-old male, post-resection of 70% of his small intestine following mesenteric ischemia, presents with chronic diarrhoea, weight loss, and fatigue. He has a history of multiple hospital admissions for dehydration and electrolyte imbalances.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Short Bowel Syndrome (SBS)"][/sc]
  • Crohn’s disease - Differentiated by inflammatory markers and endoscopic findings of transmural inflammation.
  • Coeliac disease - Diagnosed through serological testing (anti-tTG) and duodenal biopsy showing villous atrophy.
  • Chronic pancreatitis - Associated with fat malabsorption, but differentiated by imaging showing pancreatic calcifications or enzyme levels.
  • Small intestinal bacterial overgrowth (SIBO) - Overlaps with SBS but typically responds to antibiotic therapy and may show positive breath tests for hydrogen/methane.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Short Bowel Syndrome (SBS)"][/sc]
  • Dietary modification - High-protein, low-fat diet with supplementation of electrolytes and vitamins.
  • Antidiarrhoeal agents - Loperamide or codeine phosphate to reduce stool frequency.
  • Parenteral nutrition - Required for patients with severe malabsorption who cannot meet their nutritional needs enterally.
  • Probiotics/Antibiotics - Used in cases of bacterial overgrowth to reduce fermentation and diarrhoea.
  • GLP-2 analogues (teduglutide) - Promotes intestinal adaptation and improves nutrient absorption.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Short Bowel Syndrome (SBS)"][/sc]
  • Chronic kidney disease (CKD) - Due to recurrent dehydration and electrolyte imbalances.
  • Liver disease - Parenteral nutrition-associated liver disease is a significant long-term complication.
  • Intestinal failure - In severe cases, the remaining bowel is insufficient to sustain life without parenteral nutrition.
  • Small bowel bacterial overgrowth (SIBO) - Due to altered gut motility and stasis in the remaining bowel, leading to bloating, diarrhoea, and malabsorption.
