Anal Fissure
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Anal Fissure"][/sc]
  • An anal fissure is a small tear in the lining of the anal canal. It typically occurs in the posterior midline and is associated with pain and bleeding during defaecation. Fissures can be classified as acute (lasting less than 6 weeks) or chronic (lasting more than 6 weeks, often associated with fibrosis or skin tags).
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Anal Fissure"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Anal Fissure"][/sc]
  • Anal fissures are common, affecting 1 in 350 adults annually in the UK primary care population.
  • In hospital settings, they are less frequent but still significant, particularly in patients with chronic constipation or inflammatory bowel disease (IBD).
  • Worldwide, they are prevalent across all populations, with no significant gender difference, though they tend to occur more frequently in young to middle-aged adults.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Anal Fissure"][/sc]
  • The pathophysiology of anal fissures involves trauma to the anal mucosa, typically due to passing hard or large stools (common in constipation) or repeated episodes of diarrhoea. The tear exposes the underlying muscle (internal anal sphincter), leading to pain. This can result in a cycle of sphincter spasm, reduced blood flow to the area, and delayed healing. Chronic fissures may develop as a result of repeated trauma and poor perfusion to the wound site.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Anal Fissure"][/sc]
  • Acute phase: Lasts less than 6 weeks and involves sharp pain during defaecation, sometimes with bright red blood.
  • Chronic phase: Persists beyond 6 weeks, often characterised by a less intense but more constant pain, skin tag formation (sentinel pile), and fibrosis.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Anal Fissure"][/sc]
  • Pain during defaecation - A sharp, burning pain felt during bowel movements due to the mucosal tear, worsened by the passage of hard stools.
  • Rectal bleeding - Bright red blood on toilet paper or in the stool, a result of the tear in the highly vascularised anal canal.
  • Chronic pain - With chronic fissures, pain may persist between bowel movements, due to ongoing inflammation and muscle spasm.
  • Constipation - Often a consequence of pain avoidance behaviours leading to delayed defaecation, which exacerbates the condition.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Anal Fissure"][/sc]
  • Visible tear in the anal canal - Typically in the posterior midline upon visual inspection.
  • Sentinel skin tag - Chronic fissures often lead to fibrotic tissue at the distal end of the fissure, forming a skin tag.
  • Increased anal sphincter tone - Palpable during a digital rectal exam, due to spasm of the internal anal sphincter.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Anal Fissure"][/sc]
  • Painless bleeding - Suggests a diagnosis other than anal fissure, such as colorectal cancer or haemorrhoids.
  • Persistent or recurrent symptoms - May indicate underlying pathology, such as Crohn's disease, which often presents with atypical fissures off the midline.
  • Systemic symptoms (fever, weight loss) - Suggests systemic infection or malignancy, which requires urgent evaluation.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Anal Fissure"][/sc]
  • Fissures off the midline - These are uncommon and often associated with conditions like Crohn's disease or syphilis.
  • Multiple fissures - Can occur in conditions like HIV, tuberculosis, or leukaemia, where immune compromise leads to poor wound healing.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Anal Fissure"][/sc]
  • Clinical examination - Direct visual inspection of the anal area, confirming the presence of a fissure.
  • Proctoscopy - Typically not necessary unless malignancy is suspected or there is failure to respond to treatment.
  • Blood tests - In cases where systemic causes are suspected, a full blood count and inflammatory markers may be useful to rule out conditions like IBD or infection.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Anal Fissure"][/sc]
  • A 35-year-old male presents with sharp rectal pain and bright red bleeding on defaecation for 3 weeks, associated with constipation. Examination reveals a posterior midline anal fissure.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Anal Fissure"][/sc]
  • Haemorrhoids - Typically painless or cause dull ache, with bleeding occurring more with straining. No visible tear on examination.
  • Perianal abscess - Associated with constant pain, often throbbing, with systemic symptoms such as fever. Examination may reveal swelling or induration.
  • Inflammatory Bowel Disease (IBD) - Crohn’s disease can cause fissures, particularly off the midline, and often presents with diarrhoea, abdominal pain, and systemic symptoms.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Anal Fissure"][/sc]
  • Conservative treatment: Dietary modification to soften stools (high-fibre diet), adequate hydration, and sitz baths to promote healing.
  • Topical treatments: Nitroglycerin ointment or calcium channel blockers (e.g. diltiazem) to reduce sphincter spasm and promote healing.
  • Surgical treatment: Lateral internal sphincterotomy for chronic fissures or fissures unresponsive to medical treatment, aiming to reduce sphincter tone.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Anal Fissure"][/sc]
  • Chronic fissure formation - Leads to persistent pain and discomfort, and can result in fibrosis or the development of sentinel piles.
  • Infection - Rarely, fissures can become infected, leading to abscess formation or fistula development.
  • Faecal incontinence - A potential complication of surgical treatment, particularly sphincterotomy, due to the weakening of the anal sphincter.
