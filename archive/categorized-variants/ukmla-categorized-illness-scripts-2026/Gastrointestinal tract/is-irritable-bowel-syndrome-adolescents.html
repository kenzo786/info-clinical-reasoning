Irritable Bowel Syndrome (Adolescents)
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Irritable Bowel Syndrome (Adolescents)"][/sc]
  • Irritable Bowel Syndrome (IBS) is a chronic functional gastrointestinal disorder characterised by abdominal pain associated with altered bowel habits (diarrhoea, constipation, or both) in the absence of structural or biochemical abnormalities. It often involves a multifactorial interplay between gut motility, visceral hypersensitivity, gut-brain axis dysfunction, and altered microbiota.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Irritable Bowel Syndrome (Adolescents)"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Irritable Bowel Syndrome (Adolescents)"][/sc]
  • In the UK, IBS affects approximately 10-20% of adolescents seen in GP practices.
  • Globally, the prevalence of IBS is estimated at 11%, with variations depending on diagnostic criteria used (e.g., Rome IV).
  • It is more common in females than males, with a higher prevalence during adolescence and young adulthood.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Irritable Bowel Syndrome (Adolescents)"][/sc]
  • Visceral hypersensitivity: Enhanced sensitivity of intestinal nerves leads to increased pain perception.
  • Gut motility dysregulation: Abnormalities in bowel muscle contractions contribute to diarrhoea and constipation.
  • Gut-brain axis disruption: Psychological stress exacerbates symptoms via altered signalling between the brain and gut.
  • Altered gut microbiota: Imbalances in gut bacteria may influence inflammation and motility.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Irritable Bowel Syndrome (Adolescents)"][/sc]
  • Symptoms often develop in adolescence and persist chronically, with intermittent exacerbations triggered by stress, diet, or infection.
  • Some patients experience remission over time, while others continue to have fluctuating symptoms into adulthood.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Irritable Bowel Syndrome (Adolescents)"][/sc]
  • Abdominal pain - Often crampy, episodic, and located in the lower abdomen; worsened by stress or specific foods due to visceral hypersensitivity.
  • Altered bowel habits - Includes diarrhoea, constipation, or alternating patterns due to dysregulated gut motility.
  • Bloating - Sensation of abdominal fullness linked to altered gas handling and gut microbiota imbalance.
  • Mucus in stool - Indicates abnormal intestinal secretions; common in IBS-D (diarrhoea-predominant IBS).
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Irritable Bowel Syndrome (Adolescents)"][/sc]
  • Abdominal tenderness - Mild and diffuse, often without rebound tenderness or guarding, due to visceral hypersensitivity.
  • Absence of alarm features - Lack of signs such as weight loss, anaemia, or fever supports a functional diagnosis.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Irritable Bowel Syndrome (Adolescents)"][/sc]
  • Weight loss - Suggests organic disease like inflammatory bowel disease (IBD).
  • Rectal bleeding - Indicative of structural or inflammatory conditions (e.g., IBD or malignancy).
  • Nocturnal symptoms - Suggests organic pathology rather than functional.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Irritable Bowel Syndrome (Adolescents)"][/sc]
  • Predominant bloating - May overshadow pain or bowel habit changes.
  • Minimal pain - Presentations without significant abdominal discomfort may delay diagnosis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Irritable Bowel Syndrome (Adolescents)"][/sc]
  • Full blood count (FBC) - To rule out anaemia and inflammation; should be normal in IBS.
  • Faecal calprotectin - Low levels exclude IBD; elevated levels necessitate further investigation.
  • Coeliac serology - Negative tests exclude coeliac disease as a differential.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Irritable Bowel Syndrome (Adolescents)"][/sc]
  • A 15-year-old female with a 6-month history of intermittent lower abdominal pain, alternating diarrhoea and constipation, and bloating, exacerbated by stress and certain foods.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Irritable Bowel Syndrome (Adolescents)"][/sc]
  • Inflammatory bowel disease (IBD) - Presence of rectal bleeding, weight loss, and elevated faecal calprotectin.
  • Coeliac disease - Positive coeliac serology and symptoms exacerbated by gluten.
  • Functional constipation - Absence of pain and predominant hard stools.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Irritable Bowel Syndrome (Adolescents)"][/sc]
  • Dietary modification - Low FODMAP diet under dietitian supervision.
  • Pharmacological treatment - Laxatives for constipation-predominant IBS; loperamide for diarrhoea-predominant IBS.
  • Psychological therapies - Cognitive behavioural therapy or gut-directed hypnotherapy for refractory cases.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Irritable Bowel Syndrome (Adolescents)"][/sc]
  • Impact on quality of life - Chronic symptoms lead to school absenteeism and social isolation.
  • Psychological comorbidities - Higher prevalence of anxiety and depression.
