Malabsorption
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Malabsorption"][/sc]
  • Malabsorption is a condition where the small intestine cannot properly absorb nutrients, including fats, proteins, carbohydrates, vitamins, or minerals, leading to nutritional deficiencies and a variety of symptoms.
  • It can result from disorders affecting digestion, mucosal absorption, or transport of nutrients.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Malabsorption"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Malabsorption"][/sc]
  • In the UK, malabsorption syndromes are relatively uncommon in general practice, with coeliac disease affecting around 1% of the population, while other causes are less prevalent.
  • In hospital settings, malabsorption is seen more frequently due to conditions like chronic pancreatitis, small bowel disease, or following surgeries such as gastric bypass.
  • Worldwide, prevalence varies depending on the cause, with higher rates in regions where infections such as tropical sprue or parasitic infestations are common.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Malabsorption"][/sc]
  • Malabsorption results from defects in one or more stages of nutrient processing: digestion, mucosal absorption, or transport of nutrients.
  • 1. **Intraluminal digestion**: Problems such as pancreatic insufficiency or bile acid deficiency can impair the breakdown of macronutrients.
  • 2. **Mucosal absorption**: Damage to the small bowel mucosa (e.g., in coeliac disease) can reduce the surface area available for nutrient absorption.
  • 3. **Nutrient transport**: Conditions like lymphatic obstruction can affect the transport of absorbed fats into the lymphatic system.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Malabsorption"][/sc]
  • Malabsorption can develop gradually over weeks to months, with chronic forms often presenting with progressive symptoms like weight loss and diarrhoea.
  • In acute cases, such as after gastrointestinal infections or surgeries, symptoms can present more abruptly.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Malabsorption"][/sc]
  • Diarrhoea – Results from unabsorbed nutrients in the gut lumen, drawing water into the intestine through osmotic effects.
  • Steatorrhoea – Fatty, oily stools are common in fat malabsorption due to unabsorbed lipids passing through the intestines.
  • Weight loss – Caused by chronic nutrient deficiencies, particularly of calories and proteins.
  • Abdominal pain or bloating – Can occur due to fermentation of unabsorbed carbohydrates in the colon.
  • Fatigue – Results from calorie deprivation and deficiencies in essential vitamins (e.g., B12, iron).
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Malabsorption"][/sc]
  • Muscle wasting – From chronic protein-calorie malnutrition.
  • Pallor – Indicative of anaemia due to iron or vitamin B12 deficiency.
  • Peripheral oedema – Due to low albumin levels from protein malabsorption.
  • Glossitis – Inflammation of the tongue, often related to vitamin B12 or folate deficiency.
  • Bruising – May be due to vitamin K deficiency, which impairs blood clotting.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Malabsorption"][/sc]
  • Unexplained weight loss – Could indicate severe malabsorption or an underlying malignancy.
  • Rectal bleeding or black stools – Suggestive of gastrointestinal bleeding, which may indicate a serious underlying condition like cancer or severe inflammatory bowel disease.
  • Severe anaemia – Can be a sign of significant chronic malnutrition or gastrointestinal blood loss.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Malabsorption"][/sc]
  • Subclinical malabsorption can occur in mild coeliac disease, presenting only with iron deficiency anaemia without diarrhoea or other GI symptoms.
  • Fatigue without significant gastrointestinal symptoms may be the primary complaint in patients with isolated micronutrient deficiencies.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Malabsorption"][/sc]
  • Faecal fat test – Elevated levels of fat in stool indicate fat malabsorption. Normally, faecal fat should be <7g/day.
  • Coeliac serology – Anti-tissue transglutaminase (tTG) antibodies and endomysial antibodies are elevated in coeliac disease.
  • Small bowel biopsy – May show villous atrophy in coeliac disease or other mucosal changes in tropical sprue.
  • Breath tests (e.g., hydrogen breath test) – Positive in carbohydrate malabsorption (e.g., lactose intolerance).
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Malabsorption"][/sc]
  • A 35-year-old female presents with a 6-month history of diarrhoea, unintentional weight loss, and fatigue. She has noticed oily stools and has a family history of coeliac disease.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Malabsorption"][/sc]
  • Coeliac disease – Typically presents with diarrhoea, steatorrhoea, and weight loss but can also have extra-intestinal symptoms like anaemia.
  • Chronic pancreatitis – Causes fat malabsorption due to insufficient enzyme production. Abdominal pain is a key distinguishing feature.
  • Inflammatory bowel disease (IBD) – Can cause malabsorption, but bloody diarrhoea and abdominal pain are more prominent in IBD.
  • Tropical sprue – A malabsorption syndrome that occurs in tropical regions, often with a history of travel.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Malabsorption"][/sc]
  • Coeliac disease – Managed with a lifelong gluten-free diet and monitoring for complications like osteoporosis.
  • Pancreatic insufficiency – Treated with pancreatic enzyme replacement therapy.
  • General management includes addressing nutrient deficiencies (e.g., iron, calcium, fat-soluble vitamins) and managing any underlying cause of malabsorption.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Malabsorption"][/sc]
  • Osteoporosis – Due to long-term calcium and vitamin D malabsorption.
  • Anaemia – Can result from iron, folate, or vitamin B12 deficiency.
  • Neuropathy – Secondary to vitamin B12 deficiency in severe cases of malabsorption.
