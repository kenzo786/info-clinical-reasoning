Endometriosis
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Endometriosis"][/sc]
  • Endometriosis is a chronic gynaecological condition characterised by the presence of endometrial-like tissue outside the uterus, leading to inflammatory responses, fibrosis, and adhesion formation. This ectopic endometrial tissue commonly affects the ovaries, fallopian tubes, peritoneum, and, less frequently, distant organs.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Endometriosis"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Endometriosis"][/sc]
  • UK GP population: Approximately 1-2% of women presenting to GPs may have endometriosis.
  • UK hospital prevalence: In women undergoing diagnostic laparoscopy for pelvic pain or infertility, up to 40-50% may have endometriosis.
  • Worldwide prevalence: Affects 5-10% of women of reproductive age globally; however, prevalence is challenging to estimate due to delayed diagnosis and subclinical cases.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Endometriosis"][/sc]
  • Retrograde menstruation: The most widely accepted theory suggests that menstrual blood flows backwards through the fallopian tubes, implanting endometrial cells onto pelvic structures.
  • Metaplasia theory: Proposes that peritoneal cells can transform into endometrial-like cells under certain conditions.
  • Immune dysfunction: Ineffective immune clearance of ectopic endometrial cells may contribute to their persistence and growth outside the uterus.
  • Inflammatory response: Ectopic endometrial cells elicit a localised inflammatory response, causing scarring, fibrosis, and adhesions which lead to symptoms.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Endometriosis"][/sc]
  • Onset: Symptoms often begin in adolescence or early adulthood and may worsen over time.
  • Progression: Endometriosis is typically progressive, with increasing lesion size and associated symptoms; however, it may remain static in some cases.
  • Chronicity: Symptoms often persist unless treated, and may return after discontinuing treatment, particularly after hormonal therapy cessation.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Endometriosis"][/sc]
  • Chronic pelvic pain: Commonest symptom, often cyclical and linked to menstrual bleeding due to inflammatory and fibrotic changes.
  • Dysmenorrhoea: Severe menstrual pain due to inflammation and bleeding of ectopic endometrial tissue during menstruation.
  • Dyspareunia: Pain during intercourse, particularly with deep penetration, resulting from lesions and adhesions in the pelvic cavity.
  • Infertility: Approximately 30-50% of women with endometriosis experience infertility, possibly due to adhesions, tubal obstruction, or altered ovarian function.
  • Dyschezia: Pain during defaecation due to rectosigmoid endometriosis, especially around menstruation.
  • Dysuria: Painful urination may occur if endometriosis affects the bladder or urinary tract.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Endometriosis"][/sc]
  • Pelvic tenderness: Tenderness on bimanual examination due to inflammation and adhesions in the pelvis.
  • Fixed or retroverted uterus: Adhesions may fix the uterus in a retroverted position.
  • Adnexal masses: Tender masses on palpation may indicate endometriomas (ovarian cysts filled with old blood).
  • Tender nodularity in the posterior fornix: Palpable tender nodules suggest deep infiltrating endometriosis, often found in the uterosacral ligaments.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Endometriosis"][/sc]
  • Severe, acute pelvic pain: May indicate ruptured endometrioma or other complications; urgent evaluation needed to exclude acute abdomen.
  • Haematuria or rectal bleeding: In cases of bladder or bowel involvement; significant as it may indicate deep infiltrative endometriosis affecting these organs.
  • Progressive worsening of pain: Could signify increasing lesion load or fibrosis, warranting further investigation and possible treatment adjustment.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Endometriosis"][/sc]
  • Asymptomatic or minimal symptoms: Occurs in some women, often only discovered during investigations for infertility or unrelated pelvic issues.
  • Extragenital symptoms: Rarely, endometriosis can affect distant sites (e.g., thoracic cavity), causing symptoms such as chest pain or haemoptysis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Endometriosis"][/sc]
  • Pelvic ultrasound: May show endometriomas, though small lesions are often not visible.
  • Diagnostic laparoscopy: Gold standard for diagnosis, allowing direct visualisation and biopsy of lesions.
  • MRI pelvis: Useful for deep infiltrating endometriosis, particularly for rectovaginal or bladder involvement.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Endometriosis"][/sc]
  • A 30-year-old woman presents with chronic cyclical pelvic pain, dysmenorrhoea, and difficulty conceiving for the past 2 years, consistent with suspected endometriosis.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Endometriosis"][/sc]
  • Pelvic inflammatory disease: Pain may be similar but usually associated with fever, abnormal discharge, and elevated inflammatory markers.
  • Irritable bowel syndrome: Overlapping symptoms of pelvic pain and bloating, but no dysmenorrhoea or infertility.
  • Adenomyosis: Similar presentation with dysmenorrhoea, but often presents with a diffusely enlarged, tender uterus.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Endometriosis"][/sc]
  • Analgesia: NSAIDs for pain management.
  • Hormonal therapy: Combined oral contraceptives, progestogens, or GnRH analogues to suppress ovulation and reduce endometrial activity.
  • Surgical options: Laparoscopic excision or ablation of lesions for symptom relief, especially in cases resistant to medical therapy.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Endometriosis"][/sc]
  • Infertility: Common complication due to tubal adhesions and ovarian dysfunction.
  • Chronic pain syndrome: Due to persistent inflammation and nerve sensitisation.
  • Adhesion formation: Leads to chronic pelvic pain and organ dysfunction.
