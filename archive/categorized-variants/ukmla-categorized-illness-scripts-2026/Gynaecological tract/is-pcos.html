Polycystic Ovary Syndrome (PCOS)
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Polycystic Ovary Syndrome (PCOS)"][/sc]
  • Polycystic ovary syndrome (PCOS) is a common endocrine disorder characterised by a combination of hyperandrogenism, oligo/anovulation, and polycystic ovarian morphology. It is associated with metabolic dysfunction, including insulin resistance and an increased risk of type 2 diabetes. The condition is diagnosed based on the Rotterdam criteria, which requires two out of three of the following: clinical or biochemical hyperandrogenism, oligo/anovulation, and polycystic ovaries on ultrasound.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Polycystic Ovary Syndrome (PCOS)"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Polycystic Ovary Syndrome (PCOS)"][/sc]
  • PCOS affects approximately 6-10% of women of reproductive age globally. In the UK, the prevalence in general practice settings is around 5-10%, while hospital prevalence tends to be slightly higher due to referral bias in specialist care.
  • It is one of the most common causes of infertility in women.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Polycystic Ovary Syndrome (PCOS)"][/sc]
  • PCOS is thought to arise from a combination of genetic, environmental, and hormonal factors. The key mechanisms include:
  • 1. Hyperandrogenism: Excess androgen production from the ovaries and, to a lesser extent, the adrenal glands. This leads to symptoms like hirsutism and acne.
  • 2. Insulin resistance: Present in 50-70% of women with PCOS, contributing to hyperinsulinaemia. This amplifies ovarian androgen production and inhibits normal follicular development, leading to anovulation.
  • 3. Follicular arrest: In PCOS, ovarian follicles are arrested in their development, which is reflected as "polycystic" ovaries on ultrasound.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Polycystic Ovary Syndrome (PCOS)"][/sc]
  • PCOS typically manifests during adolescence or early adulthood, coinciding with the onset of menstruation. It is often a chronic condition with symptoms that may fluctuate depending on weight, lifestyle, and hormonal changes. Symptoms can persist throughout reproductive years and may evolve with age, particularly if metabolic complications like diabetes or cardiovascular disease develop.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Polycystic Ovary Syndrome (PCOS)"][/sc]
  • Oligomenorrhoea/Amenorrhoea - Irregular or absent menstrual periods due to anovulation. Hyperinsulinaemia and increased androgens disrupt the hypothalamic-pituitary-ovarian axis, preventing normal ovulation.
  • Hirsutism - Excessive hair growth in a male pattern (face, chest) caused by increased androgen levels.
  • Acne - Due to hyperandrogenism, stimulating sebaceous gland activity.
  • Weight gain - Common, likely linked to insulin resistance, which promotes fat storage.
  • Infertility - Due to chronic anovulation, ovulation does not occur regularly, leading to difficulty conceiving.
  • Asymptomatic - Some women may present with no overt symptoms and only be diagnosed during fertility investigations or incidental ultrasound findings.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Polycystic Ovary Syndrome (PCOS)"][/sc]
  • Hirsutism - Male-pattern hair growth on the face, chest, and abdomen, due to hyperandrogenism.
  • Obesity - Weight gain, especially central adiposity, is often seen due to insulin resistance.
  • Acanthosis nigricans - Darkened, velvety skin patches, typically in body folds, resulting from insulin resistance.
  • Polycystic ovaries on ultrasound - Multiple small cysts (arrested follicles) seen on transvaginal or pelvic ultrasound.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Polycystic Ovary Syndrome (PCOS)"][/sc]
  • Rapid onset of hirsutism or virilisation - May suggest an androgen-secreting tumour rather than PCOS.
  • Severe oligomenorrhoea/amenorrhoea with significant weight loss - Could indicate a hypothalamic-pituitary disorder, such as functional hypothalamic amenorrhoea.
  • Signs of Cushing's syndrome - Consider if there is rapid weight gain, purple striae, or a buffalo hump.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Polycystic Ovary Syndrome (PCOS)"][/sc]
  • Lean PCOS - Women who present with typical PCOS symptoms but without obesity. These patients may still exhibit hyperandrogenism and ovulatory dysfunction despite a normal body mass index (BMI).
  • Postmenopausal women - PCOS may persist post-menopause but can present with metabolic sequelae such as type 2 diabetes, cardiovascular disease, and endometrial hyperplasia.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Polycystic Ovary Syndrome (PCOS)"][/sc]
  • Blood tests - Elevated androgens (total testosterone, free testosterone), raised luteinising hormone (LH) with normal or low follicle-stimulating hormone (FSH). Insulin resistance can be assessed by fasting glucose and insulin levels, and HbA1c.
  • Pelvic ultrasound - Shows "string of pearls" appearance of multiple small follicles (polycystic ovaries). Criteria include >12 follicles measuring 2-9mm or increased ovarian volume >10 cm³.
  • Exclusion tests - 17-hydroxyprogesterone to exclude congenital adrenal hyperplasia; cortisol for Cushing’s syndrome; prolactin and thyroid function tests to exclude other causes of amenorrhoea.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Polycystic Ovary Syndrome (PCOS)"][/sc]
  • A 26-year-old woman presents with irregular periods, weight gain, and hirsutism over the past 2 years. She is concerned about infertility after 12 months of unprotected intercourse without conceiving. Examination reveals central obesity and facial hirsutism. Pelvic ultrasound confirms polycystic ovaries.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Polycystic Ovary Syndrome (PCOS)"][/sc]
  • Hypothyroidism - Can present with irregular periods and weight gain, but often includes fatigue and cold intolerance; thyroid function tests differentiate.
  • Cushing’s syndrome - Causes weight gain, hirsutism, and menstrual irregularities, but with additional signs like purple striae, moon facies, and elevated cortisol levels.
  • Androgen-secreting tumour - More rapid onset of virilisation and elevated androgen levels; imaging is required to detect adrenal or ovarian tumours.
  • Congenital adrenal hyperplasia (late-onset) - Similar hyperandrogenic symptoms but with elevated 17-hydroxyprogesterone levels.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Polycystic Ovary Syndrome (PCOS)"][/sc]
  • Lifestyle modifications - Weight loss, exercise, and a low glycaemic index diet are first-line therapies to improve insulin sensitivity and restore ovulatory cycles.
  • Oral contraceptive pills (OCP) - Combination OCPs help regulate menstrual cycles and reduce androgen levels.
  • Metformin - Used to improve insulin sensitivity, particularly in women with metabolic syndrome or insulin resistance.
  • Clomifene citrate - A first-line treatment for infertility in women with PCOS to stimulate ovulation.
  • Anti-androgens - Spironolactone may be used to reduce hirsutism, though not recommended for use during pregnancy.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Polycystic Ovary Syndrome (PCOS)"][/sc]
  • Infertility - Due to chronic anovulation, making conception difficult without intervention.
  • Type 2 diabetes mellitus - Higher risk due to insulin resistance; may develop later in life if metabolic syndrome is untreated.
  • Endometrial hyperplasia - Prolonged anovulation leads to unopposed oestrogen stimulation of the endometrium, increasing the risk of endometrial cancer.
  • Cardiovascular disease - Associated with long-term metabolic abnormalities, including dyslipidaemia, hypertension, and insulin resistance.
