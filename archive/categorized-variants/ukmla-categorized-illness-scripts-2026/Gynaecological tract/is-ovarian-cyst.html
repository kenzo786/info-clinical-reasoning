Ovarian Cyst
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Ovarian Cyst"][/sc]
  • An ovarian cyst is a fluid-filled sac that develops on or within an ovary. They are common, often asymptomatic, and can vary in size. Most ovarian cysts are benign and occur as part of the normal menstrual cycle (functional cysts), but others, such as pathological cysts, can arise from neoplastic or infectious processes.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Ovarian Cyst"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Ovarian Cyst"][/sc]
  • UK GP population: Approximately 10% of women develop an ovarian cyst that requires treatment during their lifetime.
  • UK hospital prevalence: Around 1-3% of women admitted with acute pelvic pain have ovarian cysts.
  • Worldwide prevalence: Functional ovarian cysts are present in about 20% of women of reproductive age globally.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Ovarian Cyst"][/sc]
  • Ovarian cysts form when fluid accumulates within a sac in the ovary. Functional cysts develop due to normal hormonal variations in the menstrual cycle:
    • Follicular cysts form when a follicle does not release its egg and continues to grow.
    • Corpus luteum cysts occur when the corpus luteum fails to degenerate after ovulation, accumulating fluid.
  • Pathological cysts, such as dermoid cysts or endometriomas, arise from abnormal cellular growth or other underlying conditions, including endometriosis or infection.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Ovarian Cyst"][/sc]
  • Functional cysts typically resolve within 1-3 menstrual cycles without intervention.
  • Pathological cysts may persist or grow and can require surgical intervention.
  • Cysts associated with malignancy, while rare, can progress over months to years and often present later in the disease process.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Ovarian Cyst"][/sc]
  • Asymptomatic - Many ovarian cysts, especially functional ones, do not cause symptoms and are often found incidentally during imaging.
  • Pelvic pain - May be dull or sharp and localised to the side of the cyst due to stretching or rupture. Rupture of a cyst can cause sudden severe pain.
  • Abdominal bloating or distension - Large cysts can press on surrounding organs, leading to discomfort or a feeling of fullness.
  • Changes in menstrual cycle - Hormone-secreting cysts may cause irregular periods or heavier bleeding.
  • Pain during intercourse (dyspareunia) - Occurs when a cyst causes irritation or pressure on the reproductive organs.
  • Urinary symptoms - Large cysts may exert pressure on the bladder, leading to increased frequency or difficulty emptying the bladder.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Ovarian Cyst"][/sc]
  • Pelvic mass on palpation - An ovarian cyst may be palpable if large, but smaller cysts are typically not detectable on physical examination.
  • Tenderness on abdominal or pelvic examination - Tenderness may suggest cyst rupture or torsion.
  • Signs of peritonitis - If a cyst ruptures or torsion occurs, the patient may present with guarding, rebound tenderness, and other signs of acute abdomen.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Ovarian Cyst"][/sc]
  • Severe acute abdominal pain - May indicate ovarian torsion or cyst rupture, both of which are surgical emergencies.
  • Unexplained weight loss and early satiety - These symptoms raise suspicion for malignancy, particularly ovarian cancer.
  • Postmenopausal ovarian cysts - Postmenopausal women with ovarian cysts have a higher risk of malignancy, requiring urgent evaluation.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Ovarian Cyst"][/sc]
  • Chronic pelvic pain without mass - Cysts may present as a source of chronic pelvic discomfort without any acute symptoms or palpable mass, often related to endometriomas.
  • Urinary or gastrointestinal complaints - Atypical presentations may involve symptoms such as constipation or urinary retention due to large cysts exerting pressure on surrounding structures.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Ovarian Cyst"][/sc]
  • Transvaginal ultrasound (TVUS) - First-line imaging modality to assess cyst characteristics (size, septation, solid components). Functional cysts appear simple, while complex cysts suggest neoplasms or endometriomas.
  • CA-125 blood test - Used in postmenopausal women or if malignancy is suspected. Elevated CA-125 is associated with ovarian cancer but can be raised in benign conditions like endometriosis or pelvic inflammatory disease (PID).
  • CT or MRI - Performed if TVUS findings are inconclusive or if malignancy is strongly suspected. Provides further anatomical detail and differentiation of cyst types.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Ovarian Cyst"][/sc]
  • A 30-year-old woman presents with intermittent right-sided pelvic pain over 3 months, worsened by intercourse. Transvaginal ultrasound reveals a 5 cm simple ovarian cyst.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Ovarian Cyst"][/sc]
  • Ectopic pregnancy - Presents with pelvic pain and amenorrhoea; positive pregnancy test differentiates it.
  • Endometriosis - Characterised by chronic pelvic pain and dysmenorrhoea; endometriomas can be mistaken for ovarian cysts on ultrasound.
  • Pelvic inflammatory disease (PID) - Associated with fever, purulent discharge, and pain on cervical motion tenderness, differentiating it from cysts.
  • Ovarian torsion - Presents with sudden severe pain, nausea, and vomiting. TVUS shows a twisted pedicle or reduced ovarian blood flow.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Ovarian Cyst"][/sc]
  • Watchful waiting - Functional cysts <5 cm usually resolve spontaneously and are monitored with repeat ultrasound.
  • Surgical intervention - Indicated for cysts >5 cm, persistent or symptomatic cysts, or suspected malignancy. Laparoscopy is preferred for smaller cysts, while laparotomy may be needed for large or complex cysts.
  • Hormonal therapy - Combined oral contraceptives may prevent the formation of new functional cysts.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Ovarian Cyst"][/sc]
  • Ovarian torsion - The twisting of the ovary or cyst causes acute ischaemia. Requires urgent surgery to prevent loss of the ovary.
  • Cyst rupture - Can cause internal bleeding and peritonitis, presenting as an acute abdomen.
  • Malignant transformation - Rare, but some cysts can develop into ovarian cancer, particularly in postmenopausal women.
