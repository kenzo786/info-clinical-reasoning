Bell's Palsy
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Bell's Palsy"][/sc]
  • Bell's palsy is an acute, unilateral lower motor neurone facial nerve (cranial nerve VII) paralysis of unknown aetiology. It results in weakness or complete paralysis of the muscles on one side of the face. The condition is typically self-limiting, with most cases resolving spontaneously within weeks to months.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Bell's Palsy"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Bell's Palsy"][/sc]
  • Prevalence in the UK GP population: Approximately 20-30 cases per 100,000 people annually.
  • Hospital prevalence: Often presents in A&E and neurology outpatient clinics; hospitalisation is rare unless complications arise.
  • Worldwide prevalence: Similar to UK rates, affecting 15-30 cases per 100,000 people per year globally.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Bell's Palsy"][/sc]
  • Bell's palsy involves inflammation of the facial nerve, likely triggered by viral infections (e.g., herpes simplex virus). The inflammation causes compression of the nerve as it travels through the narrow facial canal. This compression leads to ischaemia and demyelination, resulting in the loss of nerve function.
  • The exact cause remains unknown, but it is often linked to viral reactivation, autoimmune responses, or vascular ischaemia.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Bell's Palsy"][/sc]
  • Onset: Rapid, usually within 48-72 hours.
  • Acute phase: Maximum weakness is often reached within one week of onset.
  • Recovery phase: Spontaneous recovery typically begins within 3 weeks, with full recovery by 3-6 months in 85% of cases.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Bell's Palsy"][/sc]
  • Unilateral facial weakness - Sudden inability to move the muscles on one side of the face due to lower motor neurone lesion of the facial nerve.
  • Inability to close the eye - Weakness of the orbicularis oculi muscle, leading to difficulty closing the eyelid.
  • Loss of facial expressions - Paralysis of muscles involved in smiling, frowning, and raising the eyebrows due to facial nerve involvement.
  • Ear pain or discomfort - Often due to inflammation or irritation of the facial nerve near its origin close to the ear canal.
  • Altered taste - Loss of taste sensation in the anterior two-thirds of the tongue, as the chorda tympani branch of the facial nerve is affected.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Bell's Palsy"][/sc]
  • Unilateral facial droop - Classic sign of facial nerve palsy, involving sagging of the mouth and flattening of nasolabial folds.
  • Inability to raise eyebrows or close the eye - Upper and lower facial muscles are equally affected due to lower motor neurone lesion.
  • Decreased blinking - Loss of motor control over eyelid muscles leads to reduced blink reflex, increasing the risk of corneal dryness or ulceration.
  • Hyperacusis - Increased sensitivity to sound due to paralysis of the stapedius muscle (also innervated by the facial nerve).
  • Loss of taste - Due to chorda tympani nerve involvement affecting taste sensation on the anterior two-thirds of the tongue.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Bell's Palsy"][/sc]
  • Bilateral facial paralysis - This could suggest more serious conditions such as Guillain-Barré syndrome, Lyme disease, or sarcoidosis.
  • Progressive neurological deficits - Could indicate a central cause such as a brainstem lesion or stroke.
  • Severe headache, neck stiffness, or fever - May indicate an infectious process such as meningitis.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Bell's Palsy"][/sc]
  • Slow onset of symptoms - Typically, Bell's palsy has a rapid onset. Gradual progression may indicate a tumour compressing the facial nerve.
  • Bilateral weakness - More indicative of conditions like Guillain-Barré syndrome or Lyme disease.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Bell's Palsy"][/sc]
  • Clinical diagnosis: Based on history and characteristic findings. No specific laboratory or imaging tests are required unless atypical features are present.
  • Blood tests: To exclude Lyme disease, especially in endemic areas (Lyme serology).
  • Imaging: MRI may be indicated if the presentation is atypical, to exclude tumours or strokes.
  • Nerve conduction studies (NCS): Occasionally used to assess the degree of nerve damage in prolonged cases.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Bell's Palsy"][/sc]
  • A 35-year-old female presents with a 48-hour history of sudden, painless, unilateral facial weakness. She reports difficulty closing her left eye and a slight reduction in taste sensation. Examination reveals weakness in both upper and lower facial muscles on the left side, with loss of the nasolabial fold.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Bell's Palsy"][/sc]
  • Stroke (ischaemic/haemorrhagic): Sudden onset, typically spares the upper face (forehead), often associated with other neurological deficits (e.g., limb weakness).
  • Ramsay Hunt syndrome: Herpetic vesicles in the ear canal or on the tongue, associated with facial nerve palsy, ear pain, and hearing loss.
  • Guillain-Barré syndrome: Often bilateral, may be associated with limb weakness or sensory changes, and may progress over days to weeks.
  • Lyme disease: May present with bilateral facial palsy, especially in endemic areas. Look for history of tick bites or erythema migrans.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Bell's Palsy"][/sc]
  • Corticosteroids: Prednisolone 50 mg daily for 10 days is recommended as the first-line treatment within 72 hours of symptom onset.
  • Eye care: Lubricating eye drops and taping the eye closed at night to prevent corneal damage.
  • Antiviral therapy: May be considered in severe cases or if vesicles suggest herpetic involvement (e.g., aciclovir).
  • Physiotherapy: Facial exercises may aid in recovery in prolonged cases.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Bell's Palsy"][/sc]
  • Corneal ulceration: Due to incomplete eye closure and reduced blink reflex.
  • Permanent facial weakness: Seen in up to 15% of patients, especially those with complete paralysis at onset.
  • Synkinesis: Involuntary facial movements during voluntary action, caused by aberrant nerve regeneration.
