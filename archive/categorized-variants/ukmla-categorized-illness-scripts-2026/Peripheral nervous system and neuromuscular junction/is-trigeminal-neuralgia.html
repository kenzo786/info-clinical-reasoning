Trigeminal Neuralgia
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Trigeminal Neuralgia"][/sc]
  • Trigeminal neuralgia (TN) is a chronic pain disorder characterised by recurrent episodes of severe, unilateral facial pain that follows the distribution of the trigeminal nerve (cranial nerve V). It is often described as sudden, sharp, stabbing, or electric shock-like pain, which can last from a few seconds to two minutes. The pain is typically triggered by mild stimuli such as chewing, talking, or touching the face.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Trigeminal Neuralgia"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Trigeminal Neuralgia"][/sc]
  • UK GP population prevalence: Approximately 1 in 8,000 per year.
  • UK hospital prevalence: Trigeminal neuralgia accounts for about 10-12% of facial pain cases referred to neurology or pain clinics.
  • Worldwide prevalence: Estimates suggest around 0.03% of the global population is affected, with a higher incidence in older adults, particularly women over the age of 50.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Trigeminal Neuralgia"][/sc]
  • Compression of the trigeminal nerve root: The most common cause is compression by an aberrant blood vessel (usually the superior cerebellar artery), leading to demyelination of the nerve. This abnormal signalling results in hyperexcitability, leading to the intense pain characteristic of TN.
  • Secondary trigeminal neuralgia: Less commonly, TN can be secondary to conditions such as multiple sclerosis (due to demyelination within the brainstem), tumours, or vascular malformations that affect the trigeminal nerve.
  • Nerve sensitisation: In the long term, repeated episodes of pain can lead to central sensitisation, where the brain becomes hypersensitive to pain stimuli.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Trigeminal Neuralgia"][/sc]
  • Acute phase: Sudden onset of paroxysmal pain lasting seconds to minutes, often triggered by light touch or movement. May occur multiple times per day.
  • Chronic phase: Without treatment, episodes may increase in frequency and severity over time, leading to significant impairment in quality of life. Pain-free intervals may shorten, and the condition may become more persistent.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Trigeminal Neuralgia"][/sc]
  • Unilateral facial pain: The hallmark symptom, characterised by sudden, intense, stabbing pain in the distribution of the trigeminal nerve. This occurs most commonly in the maxillary (V2) or mandibular (V3) branches.
  • Pain triggered by everyday activities: Triggers include chewing, talking, touching the face, or exposure to wind. These activities lead to mechanical stimulation of the trigeminal nerve, which is abnormally excitable due to demyelination.
  • Episodic nature: Pain typically lasts seconds to minutes but recurs frequently throughout the day. The episodic nature is thought to be related to transient changes in nerve excitability and firing patterns.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Trigeminal Neuralgia"][/sc]
  • No objective sensory deficits: Neurological examination is typically normal between attacks, as trigeminal neuralgia affects nerve signalling but does not cause permanent damage to the sensory nerves in early stages.
  • Facial wincing or grimacing during attacks: During painful episodes, patients may exhibit involuntary facial movements due to the intensity of the pain.
  • Absence of other neurological signs: In classical TN, no other neurological deficits (e.g., weakness or loss of sensation) are present. If such signs are observed, secondary causes like multiple sclerosis or tumour must be considered.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Trigeminal Neuralgia"][/sc]
  • Bilateral symptoms: Trigeminal neuralgia is typically unilateral. Bilateral symptoms may suggest multiple sclerosis or other systemic neurological conditions.
  • Ophthalmic branch involvement (V1): Isolated pain in the V1 branch (forehead and eye region) may indicate an alternative diagnosis, such as cluster headache or a central lesion like a brainstem tumour.
  • Neurological deficits: Weakness, sensory loss, or ataxia should raise suspicion of secondary causes such as tumours, multiple sclerosis, or vascular malformations.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Trigeminal Neuralgia"][/sc]
  • Atypical facial pain: Some patients may experience constant, dull, burning pain rather than the characteristic sharp, stabbing pain. This may be due to chronic sensitisation of the trigeminal nerve.
  • Pain without clear triggers: In some cases, patients report pain that is not linked to typical triggers, possibly reflecting a central sensitisation process.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Trigeminal Neuralgia"][/sc]
  • MRI brain scan: The primary imaging study used to exclude secondary causes like multiple sclerosis, tumours, or vascular compression. Findings of vascular compression or demyelination near the trigeminal nerve root may be seen.
  • Neurological exam: While the exam is typically normal, it helps to rule out secondary causes. If any neurological deficits are present, further investigation is required.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Trigeminal Neuralgia"][/sc]
  • A 58-year-old woman presents with recurrent episodes of sudden, sharp, unilateral facial pain affecting the lower face and jaw (V3 distribution), triggered by chewing and speaking, lasting several seconds per episode over the past 3 months.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Trigeminal Neuralgia"][/sc]
  • Cluster headache: Unlike TN, cluster headaches involve deep, throbbing pain around the eye (V1 distribution), are often associated with autonomic symptoms (tearing, rhinorrhoea), and last 15-180 minutes.
  • Postherpetic neuralgia: This occurs after shingles and presents as persistent burning pain in the same area. A history of rash helps differentiate it from TN.
  • Multiple sclerosis: MS may cause secondary TN, especially in younger patients or those with bilateral pain, along with other neurological symptoms such as visual disturbances or limb weakness.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Trigeminal Neuralgia"][/sc]
  • First-line medication: Carbamazepine is the first-line treatment, as per UK guidelines, due to its efficacy in reducing nerve hyperexcitability. Starting dose: 100 mg twice daily, titrated up as needed.
  • Alternative medications: Gabapentin or pregabalin may be used if carbamazepine is not tolerated.
  • Surgical options: For refractory cases, microvascular decompression or gamma knife surgery may be considered to relieve vascular compression of the nerve.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Trigeminal Neuralgia"][/sc]
  • Depression and anxiety: Chronic pain and the fear of triggering an attack can lead to significant psychological distress.
  • Medication side effects: Long-term use of carbamazepine can cause sedation, dizziness, and potential bone marrow suppression (requiring periodic monitoring).
  • Central sensitisation: Recurrent episodes of pain may lead to chronic sensitisation of pain pathways, making the condition harder to treat over time.
