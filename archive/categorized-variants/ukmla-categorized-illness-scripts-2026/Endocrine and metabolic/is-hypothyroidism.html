Hypothyroidism
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Hypothyroidism"][/sc]
  • Hypothyroidism is a clinical condition characterised by insufficient production of thyroid hormones (thyroxine [T4] and triiodothyronine [T3]) by the thyroid gland. It leads to a reduced basal metabolic rate and systemic effects on multiple organ systems. Primary hypothyroidism, resulting from thyroid dysfunction, is the most common form, but secondary hypothyroidism can result from pituitary or hypothalamic disorders.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Hypothyroidism"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Hypothyroidism"][/sc]
  • In the UK, hypothyroidism affects approximately 2% of the population, with a higher prevalence in women (estimated around 5-8%).
  • Globally, the prevalence is around 1-5%, though this varies by iodine sufficiency in the diet.
  • Hospital prevalence in the UK is lower than primary care due to the chronic nature of the disease and treatment in outpatient settings.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Hypothyroidism"][/sc]
  • The thyroid gland produces T4 and T3 under the control of thyroid-stimulating hormone (TSH) from the pituitary gland. In primary hypothyroidism, the thyroid fails to produce sufficient hormones due to autoimmune destruction (Hashimoto's thyroiditis), iodine deficiency, or iatrogenic causes (thyroidectomy, radiation).
  • In secondary hypothyroidism, there is insufficient TSH production from the pituitary, leading to reduced thyroid hormone levels. This can result from pituitary tumours, surgery, or trauma.
  • The lack of thyroid hormones leads to decreased metabolic rate, impaired thermoregulation, and reduced protein synthesis, affecting the cardiovascular, gastrointestinal, and nervous systems.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Hypothyroidism"][/sc]
  • Hypothyroidism often develops insidiously over months to years. Symptoms may be subtle early on and worsen gradually as hormone levels decline.
  • Acute hypothyroidism (rare) can occur following thyroidectomy or radiation therapy. In some cases, post-partum thyroiditis can lead to a temporary hypothyroid phase.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Hypothyroidism"][/sc]
  • Fatigue - Decreased cellular energy production and low metabolic rate due to lack of thyroid hormones.
  • Weight gain - Slowed metabolism and reduced lipolysis lead to gradual weight increase.
  • Cold intolerance - Reduced thermogenesis makes it difficult to maintain body heat.
  • Dry skin - Impaired protein synthesis and reduced sebaceous gland function lead to dry, flaky skin.
  • Constipation - Slowed gastrointestinal motility due to the effect of hypothyroidism on smooth muscle activity.
  • Depression - Thyroid hormone deficiency impacts neurotransmitter synthesis, particularly serotonin.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Hypothyroidism"][/sc]
  • Bradycardia - Reduced stimulation of the heart by thyroid hormones leads to a slower heart rate.
  • Non-pitting oedema (myxoedema) - Accumulation of glycosaminoglycans in the dermis causes fluid retention.
  • Goitre - Thyroid enlargement due to increased TSH stimulation in primary hypothyroidism.
  • Coarse hair and skin - Poor keratin synthesis leads to changes in hair texture and skin thickness.
  • Delayed reflexes - Slowed neuromuscular transmission due to reduced thyroid hormone action.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Hypothyroidism"][/sc]
  • Myxoedema coma - Life-threatening condition marked by hypothermia, bradycardia, hypotension, and altered consciousness. It signifies severe hypothyroidism and requires urgent intervention.
  • Cardiac failure - Hypothyroidism can lead to heart failure due to bradycardia and impaired cardiac contractility.
  • Hyponatraemia - Due to inappropriate secretion of antidiuretic hormone (ADH) or reduced free water clearance, signifying severe hypothyroidism.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Hypothyroidism"][/sc]
  • Subclinical hypothyroidism - Elevated TSH with normal T4 levels. Often asymptomatic but may present with mild symptoms like fatigue or cold intolerance.
  • Elderly patients - Symptoms may be subtle or masked by coexisting conditions; cognitive impairment or depression may be the only signs.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Hypothyroidism"][/sc]
  • Serum TSH - Elevated in primary hypothyroidism, normal or low in secondary hypothyroidism. High TSH indicates reduced feedback from low thyroid hormone levels.
  • Free T4 - Low in both primary and secondary hypothyroidism. Reflects reduced thyroid hormone production.
  • Anti-thyroid peroxidase (TPO) antibodies - Positive in Hashimoto’s thyroiditis, indicating autoimmune thyroid destruction.
  • Full blood count - May reveal normocytic anaemia due to reduced erythropoiesis.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Hypothyroidism"][/sc]
  • A 45-year-old woman presents with progressive fatigue, weight gain, and cold intolerance over the past 6 months. Examination reveals bradycardia, dry skin, and delayed reflexes.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Hypothyroidism"][/sc]
  • Depression - Overlaps with fatigue and low mood but lacks cold intolerance, bradycardia, and abnormal thyroid function tests.
  • Chronic fatigue syndrome - Presents with fatigue but lacks the metabolic and hormonal disturbances seen in hypothyroidism.
  • Polycystic ovary syndrome (PCOS) - Can present with weight gain and fatigue, but is associated with hyperandrogenism and normal thyroid function.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Hypothyroidism"][/sc]
  • Levothyroxine - Synthetic T4 is the first-line treatment. Doses are adjusted based on serum TSH and clinical response. Regular monitoring is recommended, starting every 6-8 weeks until stable.
  • Iodine supplementation - For patients with iodine deficiency, although uncommon in the UK.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Hypothyroidism"][/sc]
  • Myxoedema coma - A rare but life-threatening complication of severe untreated hypothyroidism, characterised by hypothermia, altered mental state, and multi-organ failure.
  • Cardiovascular disease - Hypothyroidism is associated with hyperlipidaemia, leading to an increased risk of atherosclerosis and cardiovascular events.
  • Infertility - Untreated hypothyroidism can impair ovulation, contributing to infertility in women.
