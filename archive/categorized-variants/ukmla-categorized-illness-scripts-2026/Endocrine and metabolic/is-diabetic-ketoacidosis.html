Diabetic Ketoacidosis (DKA)
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Diabetic Ketoacidosis (DKA)"][/sc]
  • Diabetic ketoacidosis (DKA) is a life-threatening metabolic complication of diabetes mellitus, characterised by hyperglycaemia, ketosis, and metabolic acidosis. It results from insulin deficiency or resistance combined with increased counterregulatory hormones, leading to uncontrolled lipolysis and ketogenesis. It is most commonly associated with type 1 diabetes but can also occur in type 2 diabetes, especially under conditions of stress such as infection.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Diabetic Ketoacidosis (DKA)"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Diabetic Ketoacidosis (DKA)"][/sc]
  • In the UK, DKA is estimated to occur in 4-8% of patients with type 1 diabetes each year.
  • Hospital admissions for DKA in the UK are approximately 8-10 per 1000 people with diabetes annually.
  • Globally, the incidence of DKA is rising, with higher rates in countries with poor diabetes management infrastructure.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Diabetic Ketoacidosis (DKA)"][/sc]
  • Insulin Deficiency: In DKA, the lack of insulin or its inability to function causes an increase in lipolysis. This releases free fatty acids, which are converted into ketone bodies (acetoacetate and β-hydroxybutyrate) in the liver.
  • Ketogenesis: The overproduction of ketones leads to an accumulation in the bloodstream, causing metabolic acidosis.
  • Hyperglycaemia: Due to insulin deficiency, glucose cannot enter cells, leading to high blood glucose levels, which results in osmotic diuresis and dehydration.
  • Electrolyte Imbalance: The osmotic diuresis causes loss of water and electrolytes, particularly sodium, potassium, and chloride, which further exacerbates the acidosis.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Diabetic Ketoacidosis (DKA)"][/sc]
  • DKA typically evolves over a period of hours to days, often triggered by infection, illness, or lack of insulin. Early symptoms like polyuria and polydipsia may progress to more severe symptoms such as vomiting and altered mental state within 24-48 hours if untreated.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Diabetic Ketoacidosis (DKA)"][/sc]
  • Polyuria - Excess glucose in the blood leads to osmotic diuresis, resulting in frequent urination.
  • Polydipsia - The excessive loss of water through urine causes dehydration, leading to intense thirst.
  • Nausea and Vomiting - Accumulation of ketones leads to gastrointestinal irritation, resulting in nausea and vomiting.
  • Abdominal Pain - Commonly seen in DKA due to metabolic acidosis irritating the gut and peritoneum.
  • Fatigue - Dehydration, electrolyte imbalance, and hyperglycaemia lead to extreme weakness and fatigue.
  • Altered Mental Status - Progressive dehydration, acidosis, and electrolyte imbalance can lead to confusion, drowsiness, or even coma.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Diabetic Ketoacidosis (DKA)"][/sc]
  • Dehydration - Signs include dry mucous membranes, reduced skin turgor, and tachycardia, all resulting from fluid loss.
  • Kussmaul Breathing - Deep, laboured breathing is a compensatory response to metabolic acidosis as the body attempts to expel CO2 to reduce acidity.
  • Fruity Breath Odour - The breakdown of ketones, particularly acetone, leads to a distinctive sweet, fruity smell on the breath.
  • Hypotension - Severe dehydration and vasodilation secondary to acidosis can result in low blood pressure.
  • Confusion or Coma - Indicates severe DKA with electrolyte disturbances and acidosis affecting brain function.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Diabetic Ketoacidosis (DKA)"][/sc]
  • Altered Mental State - Suggests severe acidosis and electrolyte imbalance, increasing the risk of cerebral oedema, particularly in children.
  • Severe Hypotension - Indicative of shock due to profound dehydration and possible cardiovascular collapse.
  • Severe Hypokalaemia - May cause life-threatening arrhythmias due to potassium depletion, exacerbated by insulin therapy.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Diabetic Ketoacidosis (DKA)"][/sc]
  • Normoglycaemic DKA - Seen in patients on SGLT2 inhibitors (e.g., dapagliflozin), where ketosis occurs with normal glucose levels. This can delay diagnosis.
  • Elderly Patients - May present primarily with confusion or lethargy, without significant polyuria or polydipsia, making DKA harder to detect.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Diabetic Ketoacidosis (DKA)"][/sc]
  • Blood Glucose: Typically ≥ 11 mmol/L but can be lower in normoglycaemic DKA.
  • Ketones (β-hydroxybutyrate): Elevated (>3 mmol/L). Ketones are produced due to fat metabolism in the absence of insulin.
  • Arterial Blood Gas: pH < 7.3 indicates acidosis. Low bicarbonate (<15 mmol/L) supports metabolic acidosis.
  • Electrolytes: Hyperkalaemia initially due to acidosis, but total body potassium is often depleted. Sodium is usually low due to osmotic diuresis.
  • Urine Dipstick: Positive for ketones and glucose.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Diabetic Ketoacidosis (DKA)"][/sc]
  • A 24-year-old female with type 1 diabetes presents with a 24-hour history of nausea, vomiting, and abdominal pain. She reports polyuria and polydipsia for the last 2 days, and has been lethargic. Examination shows dehydration, Kussmaul breathing, and a fruity breath odour.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Diabetic Ketoacidosis (DKA)"][/sc]
  • Hyperosmolar Hyperglycaemic State (HHS): Distinguished by minimal or absent ketones, higher blood glucose (>30 mmol/L), and more profound dehydration, but without significant acidosis.
  • Lactic Acidosis: May occur in severe sepsis or metformin use. Acidosis with elevated lactate but without ketosis or significant hyperglycaemia.
  • Alcoholic Ketoacidosis: Seen in chronic alcoholics, characterised by elevated ketones and acidosis but usually with normal or low glucose levels.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Diabetic Ketoacidosis (DKA)"][/sc]
  • Fluid Resuscitation: Start with 0.9% sodium chloride IV. Rapid replacement of fluids corrects dehydration and helps reduce blood glucose and ketone levels.
  • Insulin Therapy: IV insulin infusion (0.1 units/kg/hour) to reduce blood glucose and suppress ketogenesis. Transition to subcutaneous insulin when ketones are cleared, and glucose is controlled.
  • Potassium Replacement: Monitor and correct potassium levels. Potassium may initially be high, but total body potassium is usually low and will drop with insulin therapy.
  • Address Underlying Cause: Treat infections or other precipitating factors (e.g., missed insulin doses).
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Diabetic Ketoacidosis (DKA)"][/sc]
  • Cerebral Oedema: More common in children, this life-threatening complication is due to rapid shifts in fluid balance during treatment.
  • Hypokalaemia: Develops during insulin therapy as potassium moves into cells, leading to muscle weakness and cardiac arrhythmias if not managed.
  • Acute Kidney Injury: Results from severe dehydration and hypovolaemia, which can impair renal perfusion.
