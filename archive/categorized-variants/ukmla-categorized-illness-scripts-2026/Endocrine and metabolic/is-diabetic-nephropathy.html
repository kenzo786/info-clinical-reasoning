Diabetic Nephropathy
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Diabetic Nephropathy"][/sc]
  • Diabetic nephropathy is a progressive kidney disease caused by damage to the capillaries in the kidneys' glomeruli due to chronic hyperglycaemia in diabetes mellitus. It is characterised by persistent proteinuria, a decline in glomerular filtration rate (GFR), and can lead to end-stage renal disease (ESRD).
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Diabetic Nephropathy"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Diabetic Nephropathy"][/sc]
  • Prevalence in the UK GP population: Approximately 20-40% of people with diabetes will develop diabetic nephropathy.
  • Prevalence in UK hospital settings: Diabetic nephropathy accounts for 30-40% of cases of chronic kidney disease (CKD) in hospitals.
  • Worldwide prevalence: Diabetic nephropathy affects 30-50% of patients with type 1 and type 2 diabetes globally.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Diabetic Nephropathy"][/sc]
  • Chronic hyperglycaemia in diabetes leads to damage of the glomerular capillaries through several mechanisms including advanced glycation end-product (AGE) formation, oxidative stress, and inflammation.
  • Glomerular basement membrane thickening, mesangial expansion, and podocyte loss occur, resulting in increased glomerular permeability and subsequent proteinuria.
  • Over time, progressive sclerosis of the glomeruli leads to a reduction in GFR, eventually causing chronic kidney disease and, in some cases, end-stage renal disease (ESRD).
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Diabetic Nephropathy"][/sc]
  • Early stage: Asymptomatic microalbuminuria develops in the initial years of poorly controlled diabetes.
  • Intermediate stage: Overt proteinuria (macroalbuminuria) appears after 10-15 years, along with a declining GFR.
  • Late stage: Progressive renal insufficiency and ESRD typically develop 20-30 years after the onset of diabetes without adequate control.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Diabetic Nephropathy"][/sc]
  • Proteinuria (often asymptomatic) - In early stages, diabetic nephropathy may present with microalbuminuria detected on screening, without overt symptoms.
  • Oedema - Due to the loss of albumin in the urine, hypoalbuminaemia develops, leading to fluid retention, commonly causing peripheral oedema.
  • Fatigue - As kidney function declines, anaemia and uraemia contribute to generalised fatigue.
  • Nocturia - Reduced concentrating ability of the kidneys in chronic kidney disease leads to frequent urination at night.
  • Nausea and vomiting - Uraemia in advanced stages causes gastrointestinal symptoms like nausea and vomiting.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Diabetic Nephropathy"][/sc]
  • Hypertension - Glomerular damage increases sodium retention and fluid overload, raising blood pressure.
  • Oedema - Visible swelling of the lower extremities, periorbital oedema, or ascites may be present due to hypoalbuminaemia.
  • Pallor - Anaemia of chronic disease and decreased erythropoietin production from the damaged kidneys can cause pallor.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Diabetic Nephropathy"][/sc]
  • Sudden worsening of oedema or hypertension - Indicates rapid deterioration in kidney function or nephrotic syndrome.
  • Gross haematuria - May indicate glomerulonephritis or malignancy superimposed on diabetic nephropathy.
  • Severe anaemia - Suggests advanced renal disease or complications like gastrointestinal bleeding due to uraemia.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Diabetic Nephropathy"][/sc]
  • Rapid progression without significant proteinuria - Some patients may develop rapidly progressive CKD with only mild proteinuria, possibly due to concurrent ischaemic kidney disease.
  • Normotensive diabetic nephropathy - While hypertension is common, some patients may present with normal blood pressure despite significant kidney damage.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Diabetic Nephropathy"][/sc]
  • Urine albumin-creatinine ratio (ACR) - Microalbuminuria is the earliest marker of diabetic nephropathy (ACR ≥3 mg/mmol).
  • Serum creatinine and estimated GFR (eGFR) - Used to assess kidney function. A declining eGFR indicates worsening kidney damage (normal eGFR is ≥90 mL/min/1.73 m²).
  • Blood pressure monitoring - Persistent hypertension is a common finding in diabetic nephropathy.
  • Renal ultrasound - May show reduced kidney size in advanced disease, though imaging is often normal in early stages.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Diabetic Nephropathy"][/sc]
  • A 56-year-old man with a 15-year history of type 2 diabetes presents with progressive lower limb oedema, hypertension, and fatigue. His urine ACR is 45 mg/mmol, and eGFR has declined to 40 mL/min/1.73 m² over the past year.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Diabetic Nephropathy"][/sc]
  • Hypertensive nephropathy - Hypertension is more prominent, with less significant proteinuria compared to diabetic nephropathy.
  • Chronic glomerulonephritis - Often presents with haematuria and a more rapid progression to ESRD.
  • Ischaemic nephropathy - Typically occurs in older patients with atherosclerotic disease; progression may be rapid without significant proteinuria.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Diabetic Nephropathy"][/sc]
  • Glycaemic control - Optimisation of blood glucose levels with metformin or insulin to slow progression.
  • Blood pressure control - Target BP <130/80 mmHg using ACE inhibitors or ARBs, which also reduce proteinuria.
  • Management of proteinuria - ACE inhibitors or ARBs are first-line treatments to reduce glomerular hypertension and proteinuria.
  • Lifestyle changes - Low-protein diet and smoking cessation to preserve renal function.
  • Dialysis and transplantation - Indicated for ESRD when eGFR falls below 15 mL/min/1.73 m².
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Diabetic Nephropathy"][/sc]
  • End-stage renal disease (ESRD) - Progressive loss of kidney function requiring dialysis or transplantation in advanced stages.
  • Cardiovascular disease - Due to hypertension, dyslipidaemia, and hyperglycaemia, diabetic nephropathy increases the risk of heart disease.
  • Chronic anaemia - Reduced erythropoietin production leads to anaemia of chronic disease in later stages.
