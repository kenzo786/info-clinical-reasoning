Metabolic Syndrome
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Metabolic Syndrome"][/sc]
  • Metabolic Syndrome is a cluster of interrelated cardiovascular and metabolic risk factors, including central obesity, insulin resistance, dyslipidaemia (elevated triglycerides and reduced HDL), and hypertension. It increases the risk of type 2 diabetes mellitus (T2DM) and cardiovascular diseases (CVD). In the UK, the diagnosis is typically based on criteria such as those from the International Diabetes Federation (IDF) or the National Cholesterol Education Program Adult Treatment Panel III (NCEP ATP III).
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Metabolic Syndrome"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Metabolic Syndrome"][/sc]
  • UK GP population prevalence: Around 25-30% of adults.
  • UK hospital prevalence: Often diagnosed in up to 40% of patients with cardiovascular disease and/or type 2 diabetes.
  • Worldwide prevalence: Estimated at 20-25% of the global adult population, but varies based on population characteristics and diagnostic criteria used.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Metabolic Syndrome"][/sc]
  • Metabolic Syndrome is primarily driven by central obesity and insulin resistance. Adipose tissue, especially visceral fat, secretes pro-inflammatory cytokines like TNF-α and IL-6, leading to chronic low-grade inflammation. Insulin resistance develops as a result, reducing glucose uptake in peripheral tissues, causing hyperglycaemia. The liver responds by increasing lipogenesis, resulting in hypertriglyceridaemia. Insulin resistance also contributes to hypertension by activating the sympathetic nervous system and increasing renal sodium retention.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Metabolic Syndrome"][/sc]
  • Early stage: Insulin resistance and central obesity develop gradually, often asymptomatic.
  • Progression: Over time, dyslipidaemia, hypertension, and impaired glucose tolerance may manifest.
  • Late stage: Uncontrolled progression can lead to type 2 diabetes, cardiovascular events (e.g., myocardial infarction, stroke), and non-alcoholic fatty liver disease (NAFLD).
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Metabolic Syndrome"][/sc]
  • Asymptomatic (common) - Many patients present with no symptoms until complications arise.
  • Fatigue - Results from insulin resistance leading to impaired glucose utilisation in tissues.
  • Polyuria and Polydipsia - Indicate advancing insulin resistance or type 2 diabetes, due to osmotic diuresis from hyperglycaemia.
  • Weight gain (central obesity) - Caused by increased visceral adiposity and dysregulated lipid metabolism.
  • Shortness of breath on exertion - May suggest underlying cardiovascular disease related to metabolic syndrome.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Metabolic Syndrome"][/sc]
  • Central obesity (waist circumference > 94 cm in men, > 80 cm in women) - Indicates visceral fat accumulation, which is a key driver of the syndrome.
  • Hypertension (blood pressure > 130/85 mmHg) - Common due to insulin resistance, which increases vascular tone.
  • Acanthosis nigricans - Hyperpigmented, velvety skin often found in skin folds, associated with insulin resistance.
  • Hypertriglyceridaemia (> 1.7 mmol/L) - Reflects dyslipidaemia caused by insulin resistance and increased liver lipogenesis.
  • Reduced HDL cholesterol (< 1.0 mmol/L in men, < 1.3 mmol/L in women) - Another component of dyslipidaemia that increases cardiovascular risk.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Metabolic Syndrome"][/sc]
  • Uncontrolled hypertension (> 180/110 mmHg) - Signifies a high risk of stroke or other cardiovascular events.
  • Chest pain or angina - May indicate underlying ischaemic heart disease, requiring urgent investigation.
  • Sudden vision loss or weakness - Suggests potential cerebrovascular events (stroke) associated with uncontrolled metabolic syndrome.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Metabolic Syndrome"][/sc]
  • Normal weight but metabolically unhealthy ("TOFI" - Thin Outside, Fat Inside) - Some patients may have a normal BMI but exhibit central obesity and metabolic disturbances.
  • Peripheral neuropathy - Can present without frank diabetes, due to long-standing insulin resistance affecting nerve function.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Metabolic Syndrome"][/sc]
  • Fasting plasma glucose > 5.6 mmol/L - Indicates impaired glucose tolerance or diabetes.
  • Triglycerides > 1.7 mmol/L - High levels are a marker of dyslipidaemia.
  • HDL cholesterol < 1.0 mmol/L (men), < 1.3 mmol/L (women) - Low levels increase cardiovascular risk.
  • Oral glucose tolerance test (OGTT) - Can assess for impaired glucose tolerance if fasting glucose is borderline.
  • Waist circumference measurement - Central obesity is a key diagnostic criterion for metabolic syndrome.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Metabolic Syndrome"][/sc]
  • A 45-year-old male with central obesity, hypertension, and elevated triglycerides, complaining of fatigue and intermittent chest discomfort. His fasting plasma glucose is 6.2 mmol/L, with a waist circumference of 102 cm.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Metabolic Syndrome"][/sc]
  • Type 2 Diabetes Mellitus - May be differentiated by more prominent hyperglycaemia and a confirmed diagnosis of diabetes.
  • Polycystic Ovary Syndrome (PCOS) - In women, can present similarly but with menstrual irregularities and androgen excess.
  • Hypothyroidism - May cause similar weight gain and lipid abnormalities but usually presents with fatigue, cold intolerance, and bradycardia.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Metabolic Syndrome"][/sc]
  • Lifestyle modification - Weight loss, dietary changes (reduce refined sugars, saturated fats), and increased physical activity are first-line recommendations per UK guidelines.
  • Medications - Statins for dyslipidaemia, antihypertensives (e.g., ACE inhibitors or ARBs), and metformin for insulin resistance.
  • Management of individual components - Treat hypertension, dyslipidaemia, and hyperglycaemia according to their respective guidelines.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Metabolic Syndrome"][/sc]
  • Type 2 Diabetes Mellitus - A major complication, affecting 10-20% of patients with metabolic syndrome over time.
  • Cardiovascular disease - Leading cause of morbidity and mortality; patients have a significantly increased risk of myocardial infarction and stroke.
  • Non-alcoholic fatty liver disease (NAFLD) - Common in patients with metabolic syndrome, with the potential to progress to cirrhosis.
