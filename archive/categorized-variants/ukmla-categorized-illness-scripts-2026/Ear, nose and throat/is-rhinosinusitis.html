Rhinosinusitis Illness Script
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Rhinosinusitis"][/sc]
  • Rhinosinusitis refers to the inflammation of the nasal mucosa and the paranasal sinuses. It can be classified into acute (less than 4 weeks), subacute (4–12 weeks), and chronic (more than 12 weeks). Rhinosinusitis is typically caused by viral infections but can also be bacterial, allergic, or fungal in nature.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Rhinosinusitis"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Rhinosinusitis"][/sc]
  • UK General Practice Population: Approximately 2% of GP visits in the UK are related to acute rhinosinusitis. Chronic rhinosinusitis affects around 11% of the UK population.
  • UK Hospital Prevalence: Rhinosinusitis is one of the most common reasons for outpatient ENT appointments.
  • Worldwide Prevalence: Acute rhinosinusitis affects about 6–15% of the global population annually, while chronic rhinosinusitis has a prevalence of around 12% worldwide.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Rhinosinusitis"][/sc]
  • Viral Infection: Typically starts with a viral upper respiratory infection (e.g. rhinovirus) causing mucosal inflammation and swelling of the sinus ostia, leading to impaired drainage.
  • Bacterial Superinfection: In some cases, secondary bacterial infection occurs due to the accumulation of mucus, most commonly involving organisms such as Streptococcus pneumoniae and Haemophilus influenzae.
  • Chronic Inflammation: Persistent inflammation in chronic rhinosinusitis may be driven by environmental allergens, impaired mucociliary clearance, or structural abnormalities (e.g., deviated septum, polyps).
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Rhinosinusitis"][/sc]
  • Acute Rhinosinusitis: Symptoms typically resolve within 7-10 days for viral causes. Bacterial rhinosinusitis can take longer, lasting up to 4 weeks.
  • Subacute Rhinosinusitis: Symptoms persist for 4–12 weeks but with less severity than acute episodes.
  • Chronic Rhinosinusitis: Symptoms persist beyond 12 weeks, often with periods of exacerbation and remission.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Rhinosinusitis"][/sc]
  • Nasal Congestion/Obstruction - Swelling of the nasal and sinus mucosa due to inflammation obstructs airflow, causing a sensation of blockage.
  • Purulent Nasal Discharge - Mucopurulent discharge often indicates bacterial infection or secondary bacterial involvement in viral cases.
  • Facial Pain/Pressure - Inflammation of the sinuses, particularly the maxillary and ethmoid sinuses, leads to pressure and pain in the face, often exacerbated by leaning forward.
  • Hyposmia/Anosmia - Inflammation of the olfactory mucosa results in decreased or absent sense of smell.
  • Postnasal Drip - Mucus from the inflamed sinuses drains into the pharynx, leading to throat irritation and frequent coughing, particularly at night.
  • Fever - Often seen in acute bacterial rhinosinusitis due to systemic inflammatory response.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Rhinosinusitis"][/sc]
  • Tenderness over Sinuses - Palpation of the maxillary, ethmoid, or frontal sinuses may elicit tenderness due to inflammation and pressure buildup.
  • Purulent Nasal Secretions - Direct observation of thick, discoloured nasal discharge is a sign of infection, often seen with bacterial rhinosinusitis.
  • Oedematous Nasal Mucosa - Swollen, inflamed mucosa observed during nasal examination (rhinoscopy) is characteristic of both viral and bacterial rhinosinusitis.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Rhinosinusitis"][/sc]
  • Orbital Swelling or Erythema - May indicate the spread of infection to the orbit, which can lead to orbital cellulitis, a serious complication requiring urgent treatment.
  • Severe Frontal Headache - May suggest intracranial extension, such as a subdural abscess or meningitis.
  • High Fever (>39°C) - A high, persistent fever may indicate a more severe bacterial infection, often requiring antibiotic therapy or further investigation for complications.
  • Altered Mental Status - Suggests possible intracranial involvement, including meningitis or brain abscess, and requires urgent intervention.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Rhinosinusitis"][/sc]
  • Chronic Rhinosinusitis without Nasal Polyps - May present with prolonged nasal obstruction and anosmia but without significant purulent discharge or facial pain.
  • Allergic Fungal Rhinosinusitis - Chronic presentation with eosinophilic mucin and nasal polyps, but often without typical infectious symptoms like fever or acute pain.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Rhinosinusitis"][/sc]
  • Anterior Rhinoscopy - Allows direct visualisation of the nasal mucosa, revealing oedema, purulent discharge, or polyps.
  • Nasal Endoscopy - More detailed examination for cases of chronic rhinosinusitis, can reveal obstructed drainage pathways or mucosal thickening.
  • CT Scan of Sinuses - Used in complicated or chronic cases to evaluate for mucosal thickening, air-fluid levels, or sinus opacification. Findings suggestive of rhinosinusitis include maxillary sinus opacification and air-fluid levels.
  • C-reactive Protein (CRP) - Elevated in acute bacterial infections but may be normal in viral or chronic rhinosinusitis.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Rhinosinusitis"][/sc]
  • A 35-year-old female presents with a 10-day history of nasal congestion, purulent nasal discharge, and facial pain, worsened by leaning forward. She reports anosmia and mild fever (38.1°C).
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Rhinosinusitis"][/sc]
  • Allergic Rhinitis - Often presents with clear nasal discharge, sneezing, and itching but lacks purulent discharge and fever seen in rhinosinusitis.
  • Dental Abscess - Can cause maxillary sinus pain but typically associated with localized dental symptoms such as toothache and gum swelling.
  • Migraine Headache - Can mimic sinusitis with facial pain but usually lacks nasal symptoms like congestion or discharge.
  • Nasal Polyposis - May present with nasal obstruction and anosmia but typically lacks acute purulent discharge or fever.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Rhinosinusitis"][/sc]
  • Acute Viral Rhinosinusitis: Supportive care with saline nasal irrigation, analgesics (e.g., paracetamol), and decongestants if required. Antibiotics are not indicated.
  • Bacterial Rhinosinusitis: First-line antibiotic is amoxicillin 500mg TDS for 5–7 days. If penicillin allergic, doxycycline 200mg on day 1, then 100mg daily for 5 days.
  • Chronic Rhinosinusitis: Intranasal corticosteroids (e.g., mometasone furoate 50 micrograms) and saline irrigation are mainstays of treatment. Surgical intervention (e.g., functional endoscopic sinus surgery) may be required in refractory cases.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Rhinosinusitis"][/sc]
  • Orbital Cellulitis: Infection spreads to the orbit, causing swelling, proptosis, and visual disturbance. Requires immediate IV antibiotics.
  • Meningitis: Rarely, the infection can spread to the meninges, presenting with severe headache, fever, neck stiffness, and altered mental status.
  • Chronic Rhinosinusitis: Persistence of inflammation leads to prolonged symptoms, often with recurrent episodes and decreased quality of life.
