Mastoiditis
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Mastoiditis"][/sc]
  • Mastoiditis is a bacterial infection of the mastoid air cells, located within the temporal bone behind the ear. It typically occurs as a complication of acute otitis media (AOM) when infection spreads from the middle ear to the mastoid process. The infection can lead to destruction of the bone, abscess formation, and, if untreated, serious intracranial complications.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Mastoiditis"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Mastoiditis"][/sc]
  • In the UK, mastoiditis is rare, with an incidence of approximately 1-2 cases per 10,000 children with acute otitis media. It is more common in children under two years of age. Worldwide, mastoiditis is less common in high-income countries due to the widespread use of antibiotics but remains a significant health concern in low-income settings.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Mastoiditis"][/sc]
  • Acute otitis media leads to infection in the middle ear, causing the eustachian tube to become blocked. This allows bacteria to proliferate, leading to pressure build-up. If the infection is not treated, it can spread to the mastoid air cells, leading to inflammation, bone destruction, and pus accumulation in the mastoid process. This can result in the formation of abscesses and, if severe, extension to adjacent structures such as the brain.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Mastoiditis"][/sc]
  • Mastoiditis usually develops days to weeks after the onset of acute otitis media. Early stages involve localised mastoid inflammation, with progressive stages marked by abscess formation, and, if left untreated, further extension to the adjacent skull or intracranial spaces within weeks.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Mastoiditis"][/sc]
  • Otalgia - Ear pain, usually worsening after an episode of acute otitis media, occurs due to inflammation and pressure in the mastoid air cells.
  • Fever - Reflects the body’s systemic inflammatory response to the bacterial infection.
  • Hearing loss - Conductive hearing loss occurs due to fluid and infection affecting the middle ear and mastoid bone.
  • Otorrhoea - Discharge from the ear, often purulent, occurs due to the rupture of the tympanic membrane as pressure builds in the middle ear.
  • Malaise - General unwellness due to the systemic nature of the infection.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Mastoiditis"][/sc]
  • Postauricular erythema and swelling - Redness and swelling behind the ear are key signs of mastoid involvement and abscess formation.
  • Protrusion of the auricle - The ear may appear pushed forward due to the swelling of the underlying mastoid process.
  • Tenderness over the mastoid - Palpation reveals pain due to inflammation of the mastoid bone.
  • Fluctuant swelling - Indicative of a subperiosteal abscess, where pus collects under the periosteum of the mastoid.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Mastoiditis"][/sc]
  • Neck stiffness - May indicate meningitis, a severe intracranial complication of mastoiditis.
  • Severe headache - Can be a sign of intracranial extension, such as an epidural or brain abscess.
  • Cranial nerve palsies - Facial nerve involvement (e.g., facial droop) suggests the infection has spread to the skull base or intracranial structures.
  • Altered mental status - May indicate encephalitis or other severe intracranial infections.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Mastoiditis"][/sc]
  • Painless swelling behind the ear - Some cases may present without otalgia, particularly in immunocompromised patients.
  • Isolated fever - In early or subtle cases, fever may be the only symptom, especially in young children.
  • Chronic suppurative otitis media - In some cases, mastoiditis may develop slowly over weeks or months without acute symptoms, leading to chronic infection.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Mastoiditis"][/sc]
  • CT Scan of Temporal Bones - Expected to show destruction of the mastoid air cells and abscess formation. This is the gold standard for diagnosing mastoiditis.
  • Ear swab culture - To identify the causative organism and guide antibiotic therapy. Common pathogens include Streptococcus pneumoniae, Haemophilus influenzae, and Staphylococcus aureus.
  • Full blood count (FBC) - Raised white cell count is expected, indicating a bacterial infection.
  • C-reactive protein (CRP) - Elevated CRP levels are seen in active infection and inflammation.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Mastoiditis"][/sc]
  • A 3-year-old boy presents with fever, otalgia, and purulent ear discharge following a recent episode of acute otitis media. He has postauricular swelling and tenderness with outward displacement of the ear. Symptoms have worsened over the past 48 hours.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Mastoiditis"][/sc]
  • Acute Otitis Media - Less severe with no postauricular swelling or mastoid tenderness.
  • Chronic Suppurative Otitis Media - Typically presents with persistent ear discharge and hearing loss but without acute signs like fever or postauricular erythema.
  • Cholesteatoma - Can cause hearing loss and discharge, but the absence of fever and acute inflammation distinguishes it from mastoiditis.
  • Lymphadenopathy - Enlarged lymph nodes can mimic mastoid swelling, but tenderness and erythema are usually absent.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Mastoiditis"][/sc]
  • Antibiotics - Intravenous broad-spectrum antibiotics (e.g., ceftriaxone or co-amoxiclav) are initiated, with adjustments based on culture results. Oral antibiotics are given for several weeks after initial IV therapy.
  • Myringotomy - To drain the middle ear and relieve pressure, especially if there is significant middle ear effusion.
  • Mastoidectomy - Surgical drainage of the mastoid may be necessary in cases of abscess formation or failure to respond to antibiotics.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Mastoiditis"][/sc]
  • Meningitis - Infection can spread to the meninges, leading to life-threatening bacterial meningitis.
  • Brain abscess - Extension of the infection intracranially can result in abscess formation, requiring urgent surgical intervention.
  • Facial nerve palsy - Direct involvement of the facial nerve can result in facial weakness or paralysis.
  • Hearing loss - Permanent conductive or sensorineural hearing loss may occur due to chronic infection or surgical intervention.
