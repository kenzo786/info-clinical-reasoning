Septic Arthritis
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Septic Arthritis"][/sc]
  • Septic arthritis is a joint infection caused by bacteria, fungi, or viruses, most commonly bacterial. It involves the invasion of synovial fluid and joint tissues by pathogens, leading to inflammation, pain, and often rapid joint damage. It is an orthopaedic emergency due to the potential for permanent joint destruction if not treated promptly.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Septic Arthritis"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Septic Arthritis"][/sc]
  • UK GP Population: Approximately 5 cases per 100,000 annually, with higher rates in patients with rheumatoid arthritis or joint prosthetics.
  • UK Hospital Prevalence: 8-27% of monoarticular arthritis cases presenting to A&E or inpatient settings involve septic arthritis.
  • Worldwide Prevalence: Varies but is estimated at 2-10 cases per 100,000 in developed countries; incidence is higher in those with risk factors like immunosuppression and intravenous drug use.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Septic Arthritis"][/sc]
  • Septic arthritis typically arises from haematogenous spread, where pathogens enter the joint from the bloodstream. Direct inoculation via trauma or joint surgery and local spread from adjacent infections are also possible.
  • Bacterial invasion triggers an inflammatory response, recruiting neutrophils and releasing cytokines, causing synovitis and increased intra-articular pressure.
  • Pro-inflammatory cytokines lead to cartilage breakdown and bone damage, particularly in weight-bearing joints, resulting in rapid joint destruction without prompt treatment.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Septic Arthritis"][/sc]
  • Initial Phase (1-2 days): Acute onset of joint pain, swelling, and fever.
  • Progressive Phase (3-7 days): Worsening pain, increased swelling, restricted joint movement, and possible systemic signs such as chills and fatigue.
  • Chronic Phase (Weeks): In untreated cases, significant joint destruction, decreased mobility, and long-term disability.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Septic Arthritis"][/sc]
  • Joint Pain - Sudden, severe pain in the affected joint due to inflammatory cytokines and swelling increasing intra-articular pressure.
  • Swelling - Marked swelling from synovial fluid accumulation caused by inflammatory exudate in response to infection.
  • Fever - Common systemic response to infection, often over 38°C due to pyrogen release from the infection.
  • Reduced Joint Mobility - Pain and swelling limit range of motion due to joint effusion and synovial inflammation.
  • Systemic Symptoms - Chills, malaise, and generalised fatigue reflect the body’s systemic response to infection.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Septic Arthritis"][/sc]
  • Joint Warmth - Local inflammation causes vasodilation and increased blood flow, making the joint feel warm to the touch.
  • Redness - Erythema around the joint due to inflammatory mediators that increase vascular permeability and cause localised redness.
  • Tenderness - Extreme tenderness upon palpation from heightened sensitivity and pressure due to inflammation.
  • Effusion - Visible joint swelling, especially in superficial joints, due to fluid accumulation in the joint capsule.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Septic Arthritis"][/sc]
  • Severe Pain and Rapidly Worsening Swelling - Suggests aggressive infection, potentially leading to joint destruction if untreated.
  • Systemic Symptoms of Sepsis (Hypotension, Tachycardia) - Can indicate systemic involvement or bacteraemia and signal high risk for multi-organ failure.
  • High Fever & Rigors - May reflect bacteraemia, requiring urgent intervention to prevent metastatic infections.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Septic Arthritis"][/sc]
  • Painless Joint Swelling - Seen in immunocompromised or elderly patients, potentially delaying diagnosis as the typical pain response is diminished.
  • Low-Grade Fever or Afebrile - Some older or immunosuppressed patients may not mount a febrile response, complicating early diagnosis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Septic Arthritis"][/sc]
  • Joint Aspiration and Synovial Fluid Analysis - Shows purulent fluid with elevated white cells (>50,000/mm³), often with neutrophil predominance. Culture identifies causative organism.
  • Blood Cultures - May show bacterial growth, especially in cases of bacteraemia, confirming systemic infection.
  • CRP and ESR - Both are significantly elevated due to systemic inflammation.
  • X-ray and Ultrasound - May show joint effusion; however, plain X-rays are usually non-specific early on. Ultrasound helps identify fluid for aspiration.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Septic Arthritis"][/sc]
  • A 60-year-old male with a history of diabetes presents with acute onset of severe pain and swelling in the right knee, with fever of 38.5°C and limited joint mobility over 24 hours.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Septic Arthritis"][/sc]
  • Crystal Arthropathy (e.g., Gout) - Often presents with intense joint pain and swelling but has a characteristic negative bacterial culture and presence of crystals in synovial fluid.
  • Rheumatoid Arthritis - Typically affects multiple joints symmetrically and is associated with chronic symptoms rather than an acute presentation.
  • Reactive Arthritis - Follows infection in other body parts; synovial fluid is sterile, distinguishing it from septic arthritis.
  • Osteoarthritis - Presents with chronic pain, stiffness, and a slower course, with no systemic symptoms or joint warmth and redness.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Septic Arthritis"][/sc]
  • Empirical IV Antibiotics - Based on local resistance patterns, often flucloxacillin or cefuroxime in the UK, pending culture results.
  • Joint Drainage - Repeated aspiration or surgical drainage to reduce bacterial load and relieve pressure.
  • Analgesia and Anti-inflammatory Medication - Helps control pain and inflammation; NSAIDs are typically used unless contraindicated.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Septic Arthritis"][/sc]
  • Joint Destruction - Rapid joint erosion due to inflammatory cytokines, leading to chronic disability and requiring potential joint replacement.
  • Sepsis - Infection may spread, leading to systemic involvement and multi-organ dysfunction, especially if diagnosis and treatment are delayed.
  • Osteomyelitis - Infection may spread to nearby bone, requiring extended treatment and increasing risk for long-term disability.
