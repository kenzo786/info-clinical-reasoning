Soft Tissue Injury
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Soft Tissue Injury"][/sc]
  • A soft tissue injury (STI) refers to damage to muscles, ligaments, or tendons, typically caused by trauma, overuse, or strain. Common examples include sprains, strains, contusions, and tendinopathies. These injuries are non-bony and often lead to inflammation, pain, swelling, and functional impairment in the affected area.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Soft Tissue Injury"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Soft Tissue Injury"][/sc]
  • In the UK GP population, soft tissue injuries account for approximately 5–10% of musculoskeletal consultations annually.
  • In UK hospital settings, they are a frequent presentation in emergency departments, particularly sprains and strains, accounting for around 20% of sports-related injuries.
  • Globally, soft tissue injuries are among the most common causes of musculoskeletal complaints, with higher prevalence in active individuals, such as athletes or labour-intensive workers.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Soft Tissue Injury"][/sc]
  • Soft tissue injury results from mechanical overload or trauma, leading to microscopic or macroscopic disruption of muscle, ligament, or tendon fibres.
  • Initial trauma triggers an inflammatory response, marked by vasodilation and increased capillary permeability, causing swelling and recruitment of immune cells to clear debris and promote repair.
  • Fibroblast activation occurs in the proliferative phase, producing collagen for tissue repair, which later remodels for functional recovery. However, excessive scarring or improper collagen alignment can result in chronic dysfunction.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Soft Tissue Injury"][/sc]
  • Acute Phase (0–72 hours): Characterised by pain, swelling, and functional limitation due to inflammation.
  • Subacute Phase (3–21 days): Repair and healing begin with reduced swelling, though pain may persist with movement or pressure.
  • Chronic Phase (>21 days): Ongoing recovery with potential for scar tissue formation, which may result in stiffness or chronic pain if not properly managed.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Soft Tissue Injury"][/sc]
  • Pain - Localised to the site of injury, resulting from nociceptor activation due to inflammation and tissue damage.
  • Swelling - Caused by fluid accumulation from increased vascular permeability during the inflammatory response.
  • Bruising - Due to blood leakage from damaged capillaries into the surrounding tissues.
  • Stiffness - Reflects inflammatory effects on surrounding tissues, often exacerbated by immobilisation.
  • Weakness - Related to disruption of muscle or tendon integrity and pain-induced inhibition.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Soft Tissue Injury"][/sc]
  • Tenderness - Pain elicited on palpation due to inflammation and localised tissue injury.
  • Swelling - Visible or palpable enlargement of the area, indicating inflammatory fluid accumulation.
  • Ecchymosis - Discolouration due to subcutaneous bleeding.
  • Reduced Range of Motion - Indicates pain or mechanical obstruction due to swelling or injury.
  • Instability (for ligament injuries) - Suggests significant ligament damage and impaired joint integrity.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Soft Tissue Injury"][/sc]
  • Severe, persistent pain - May indicate underlying fracture or compartment syndrome.
  • Loss of sensation - Suggests nerve involvement, such as entrapment or injury.
  • Cold or pale extremity - Can signal compromised vascular supply or acute compartment syndrome.
  • Non-resolving symptoms after 2–3 weeks - Raises concern for chronic issues, e.g., tendinopathy or undiagnosed ligament tear.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Soft Tissue Injury"][/sc]
  • Minimal pain but significant functional loss in chronic tendinopathies, possibly due to fibrotic scarring.
  • Delayed bruising in deep injuries, where bleeding is not initially visible.
  • Chronic stiffness without acute swelling in elderly patients with degenerative changes.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Soft Tissue Injury"][/sc]
  • X-rays - Used to exclude fractures; typically normal in soft tissue injuries.
  • Ultrasound - Identifies ligament, tendon, or muscle tears; shows hypoechoic areas in partial tears.
  • MRI - Provides detailed imaging of soft tissue structures, revealing oedema, tears, or inflammation.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Soft Tissue Injury"][/sc]
  • A 25-year-old male presents with acute ankle pain and swelling following a twisting injury during football. Examination reveals tenderness over the lateral ligament, swelling, and restricted range of motion.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Soft Tissue Injury"][/sc]
  • Fracture - Presence of severe pain, inability to bear weight, and positive X-ray findings.
  • Compartment Syndrome - Severe pain disproportionate to the injury, tense swelling, and neurovascular compromise.
  • Tendon Rupture - Palpable defect and inability to perform movements specific to the affected tendon.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Soft Tissue Injury"][/sc]
  • Initial Management - RICE protocol (Rest, Ice, Compression, Elevation).
  • Pharmacological - Analgesics, e.g., paracetamol or NSAIDs, for pain relief and inflammation control.
  • Rehabilitation - Physiotherapy to restore function and prevent chronic issues.
  • Surgical - Considered for severe ligament tears or tendon ruptures not responding to conservative measures.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Soft Tissue Injury"][/sc]
  • Chronic Pain - Due to improper healing or nerve sensitisation.
  • Stiffness - Resulting from scar tissue or lack of rehabilitation.
  • Joint Instability - Following severe ligament damage without repair.
