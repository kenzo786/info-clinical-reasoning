Lumbar Disc Prolapse
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Lumbar Disc Prolapse"][/sc]
  • Lumbar disc prolapse, also known as a herniated or slipped disc, is a condition where the nucleus pulposus (the soft, gel-like centre of an intervertebral disc) protrudes through a tear in the annulus fibrosus (the tougher outer layer). This displacement can compress nearby neural structures, including spinal nerves, leading to symptoms such as pain, weakness, or sensory disturbances.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Lumbar Disc Prolapse"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Lumbar Disc Prolapse"][/sc]
  • UK GP prevalence: Approximately 5–10% of adults presenting with lower back pain.
  • UK hospital prevalence: Commonly seen in orthopaedic and neurosurgery departments, accounting for around 2–5% of spinal surgeries annually.
  • Worldwide prevalence: Estimated at 1–3% of the global population at any time, more common in individuals aged 30–50 years.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Lumbar Disc Prolapse"][/sc]
  • Degeneration of the intervertebral disc, often due to ageing, leads to weakening of the annulus fibrosus.
  • Increased intradiscal pressure (e.g., from heavy lifting or poor posture) causes the nucleus pulposus to herniate.
  • The herniated material may compress spinal nerve roots or the cauda equina, triggering inflammation and neurogenic pain.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Lumbar Disc Prolapse"][/sc]
  • Acute phase: Onset of symptoms, typically following an identifiable event like heavy lifting.
  • Subacute phase: Persistence of symptoms for 6–12 weeks, with variable severity.
  • Chronic phase: Symptoms lasting >12 weeks, often involving chronic pain or neurological deficits.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Lumbar Disc Prolapse"][/sc]
  • Lower back pain: Caused by local inflammation and mechanical irritation.
  • Radicular pain (sciatica): Sharp, shooting pain radiating down the leg, often due to nerve root compression.
  • Paresthesia: Tingling or "pins and needles" sensation along the dermatomal distribution of the affected nerve.
  • Weakness: Muscle weakness due to impaired nerve conduction.
  • Bowel/bladder dysfunction: Rare but significant; indicates possible cauda equina syndrome.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Lumbar Disc Prolapse"][/sc]
  • Positive straight leg raise test: Reproduces radicular pain by stretching the affected nerve root.
  • Reduced reflexes: Hyporeflexia in the corresponding spinal segment due to nerve compression.
  • Dermatomal sensory loss: Indicates involvement of a specific nerve root.
  • Muscle weakness: Weakness in muscles innervated by the compressed nerve.
  • Saddle anaesthesia: Sign of cauda equina syndrome; sensory loss in the perineal region.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Lumbar Disc Prolapse"][/sc]
  • Bowel or bladder dysfunction: Suggests cauda equina syndrome, a surgical emergency.
  • Saddle anaesthesia: Indicates possible compression of the cauda equina.
  • Progressive neurological deficits: May signal worsening nerve root or spinal cord compression.
  • Unexplained weight loss or night pain: Raises suspicion for malignancy or infection.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Lumbar Disc Prolapse"][/sc]
  • Predominant thigh pain without lower leg involvement, reflecting atypical nerve root impingement.
  • Chronic, low-grade symptoms mistaken for musculoskeletal strain.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Lumbar Disc Prolapse"][/sc]
  • MRI of the lumbar spine: Gold standard for visualising herniated discs and nerve compression.
  • X-ray: May show degenerative changes but not disc herniation.
  • Nerve conduction studies: Used if MRI findings are inconclusive, assessing functional nerve impairment.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Lumbar Disc Prolapse"][/sc]
  • A 40-year-old male presents with acute onset of lower back pain radiating to the left leg following heavy lifting. Symptoms include paraesthesia in the L5 dermatome and difficulty lifting the foot.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Lumbar Disc Prolapse"][/sc]
  • Lumbar spondylosis: Typically lacks radicular pain; degenerative changes on imaging.
  • Sacroiliac joint dysfunction: Pain localised to the sacroiliac region without radicular features.
  • Piriformis syndrome: Mimics sciatica but lacks back pain and neurological deficits.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Lumbar Disc Prolapse"][/sc]
  • Conservative management: Analgesia (NSAIDs, paracetamol), physiotherapy, and activity modification.
  • Epidural steroid injection: Considered for persistent radicular pain.
  • Surgery: Indicated for severe or progressive neurological deficits, or refractory pain. Options include microdiscectomy or laminectomy.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Lumbar Disc Prolapse"][/sc]
  • Chronic pain: Persistent symptoms despite treatment.
  • Cauda equina syndrome: Emergency requiring decompression surgery.
  • Neurological deficits: Permanent motor or sensory loss if untreated.
