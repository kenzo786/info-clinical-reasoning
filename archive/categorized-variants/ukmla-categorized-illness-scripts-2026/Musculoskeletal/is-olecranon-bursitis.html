Olecranon Bursitis
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Olecranon Bursitis"][/sc]
  • Olecranon bursitis is the inflammation of the olecranon bursa, a small fluid-filled sac located over the olecranon process at the posterior aspect of the elbow. It typically results from repetitive trauma, infection, or inflammatory conditions like gout or rheumatoid arthritis.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Olecranon Bursitis"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Olecranon Bursitis"][/sc]
  • Prevalence in the UK GP population: Common, especially among manual labourers or athletes.
  • UK hospital prevalence: Accounts for a small percentage of cases presenting with elbow pain and swelling in A&E settings.
  • Worldwide prevalence: Similar prevalence patterns globally, particularly in populations engaging in repetitive elbow activities or prone to infection.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Olecranon Bursitis"][/sc]
  • The olecranon bursa reduces friction during movement of the elbow. Inflammation occurs when repetitive trauma or acute injury triggers a local inflammatory response. Increased vascular permeability leads to fluid accumulation and swelling. Infective causes, such as septic bursitis, occur due to bacterial invasion, typically Staphylococcus aureus.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Olecranon Bursitis"][/sc]
  • Acute phase: Sudden onset, particularly in cases of infection or trauma.
  • Chronic phase: Gradual swelling, often associated with recurrent trauma or underlying systemic disease.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Olecranon Bursitis"][/sc]
  • Swelling over the posterior elbow: The most common presenting symptom, caused by fluid accumulation in the bursa.
  • Pain or tenderness: Often present in acute or septic cases, due to inflammation and pressure on surrounding tissues.
  • Reduced range of motion: Occurs when significant swelling impedes elbow flexion or extension.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Olecranon Bursitis"][/sc]
  • Localised swelling: Soft, fluctuant swelling over the olecranon process, typically without erythema in non-infective cases.
  • Erythema and warmth: Indicative of septic bursitis, due to localised infection and inflammation.
  • Fluctuance: Suggestive of significant fluid accumulation.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Olecranon Bursitis"][/sc]
  • Severe pain and systemic symptoms (e.g., fever): May indicate septic bursitis, which requires urgent treatment to prevent spread of infection.
  • Rapid progression of swelling and erythema: Suggestive of an abscess or severe infection.
  • Failure to improve with initial management: Could indicate an underlying inflammatory condition or resistant infection.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Olecranon Bursitis"][/sc]
  • Asymptomatic swelling: Common in chronic bursitis without inflammation.
  • Misdiagnosed soft tissue mass: Can mimic neoplastic conditions due to chronic firm swelling.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Olecranon Bursitis"][/sc]
  • Aspiration and fluid analysis: Differentiates septic bursitis (cloudy fluid, elevated WCC) from aseptic causes.
  • Blood tests: CRP, ESR, and WCC may be elevated in infection or inflammatory conditions.
  • Imaging: Ultrasound confirms fluid collection; X-ray may rule out trauma or gouty tophi.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Olecranon Bursitis"][/sc]
  • A 45-year-old male presents with a 2-week history of a swollen, tender elbow following repetitive manual work, with no systemic symptoms. Examination reveals fluctuant swelling without erythema.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Olecranon Bursitis"][/sc]
  • Septic arthritis: Diffuse joint swelling with fever and more severe pain.
  • Gout: Acute onset with history of hyperuricaemia, often with tophi or erythema.
  • Trauma-related haematoma: Bruising and history of direct injury, often firmer to palpation.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Olecranon Bursitis"][/sc]
  • Aseptic bursitis: Rest, NSAIDs, compression, and aspiration if symptomatic.
  • Septic bursitis: Antibiotics (e.g., flucloxacillin) and drainage of purulent fluid.
  • Chronic bursitis: Address underlying causes and consider surgical excision if refractory.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Olecranon Bursitis"][/sc]
  • Chronic recurrence: Ongoing inflammation due to repetitive trauma.
  • Abscess formation: Complication of untreated septic bursitis.
  • Spread of infection: Rare progression to cellulitis or osteomyelitis.
