Polymyalgia Rheumatica (PMR) and Giant Cell Arteritis (GCA)
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Polymyalgia Rheumatica (PMR) and Giant Cell Arteritis (GCA)"][/sc]
  • Polymyalgia Rheumatica (PMR): A systemic inflammatory disorder characterised by proximal muscle pain and stiffness, particularly in the shoulders, neck, and hips, without significant muscle weakness. It is associated with elevated inflammatory markers.
  • Giant Cell Arteritis (GCA): Also known as temporal arteritis, it is a vasculitis affecting large and medium-sized arteries, particularly the branches of the external carotid artery, and often associated with PMR. It can lead to severe complications such as irreversible blindness if untreated.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Polymyalgia Rheumatica (PMR) and Giant Cell Arteritis (GCA)"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Polymyalgia Rheumatica (PMR) and Giant Cell Arteritis (GCA)"][/sc]
  • Prevalence of PMR in the UK: Approximately 1 in 1,000 GP consultations annually, mostly affecting individuals over 50 years of age.
  • Prevalence of GCA in the UK: Around 20 cases per 100,000 people aged over 50 years annually.
  • Global prevalence: More common in Northern European populations; rare in individuals under 50 or those of non-European descent.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Polymyalgia Rheumatica (PMR) and Giant Cell Arteritis (GCA)"][/sc]
  • PMR: Likely immune-mediated inflammation of synovial structures, bursae, and tendons, resulting in pain and stiffness.
  • GCA: Immune system activation leads to granulomatous inflammation of arterial walls, with infiltration by T cells and macrophages. This results in intimal hyperplasia, luminal narrowing, and ischemia.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Polymyalgia Rheumatica (PMR) and Giant Cell Arteritis (GCA)"][/sc]
  • Insidious onset over weeks to months for PMR.
  • GCA may present acutely or subacutely, particularly with symptoms of headache and visual disturbances.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Polymyalgia Rheumatica (PMR) and Giant Cell Arteritis (GCA)"][/sc]
  • Proximal muscle stiffness (PMR): Worse in the morning and after rest due to synovial inflammation and effusion.
  • Headache (GCA): Often localised to the temples; caused by arterial inflammation.
  • Jaw claudication (GCA): Pain on chewing due to ischemia in the masseter muscles.
  • Systemic symptoms: Fever, fatigue, and weight loss due to systemic inflammation.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Polymyalgia Rheumatica (PMR) and Giant Cell Arteritis (GCA)"][/sc]
  • Tenderness over the temporal arteries (GCA): Indicates active inflammation of the arterial wall.
  • Reduced pulse in temporal arteries (GCA): Due to luminal narrowing from intimal hyperplasia.
  • Limited shoulder or hip mobility (PMR): Reflects pain and stiffness rather than muscle weakness.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Polymyalgia Rheumatica (PMR) and Giant Cell Arteritis (GCA)"][/sc]
  • Visual symptoms (GCA): Blurred vision or sudden vision loss may signify ischemia of the optic nerve.
  • Stroke symptoms: Rarely, ischemic events can occur due to large vessel involvement.
  • Severe unremitting headache: May indicate extensive arterial involvement or an alternative diagnosis.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Polymyalgia Rheumatica (PMR) and Giant Cell Arteritis (GCA)"][/sc]
  • Isolated constitutional symptoms: Without prominent musculoskeletal involvement.
  • Asymmetrical pain in PMR: May mimic other inflammatory conditions.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Polymyalgia Rheumatica (PMR) and Giant Cell Arteritis (GCA)"][/sc]
  • Inflammatory markers (ESR and CRP): Elevated due to systemic inflammation.
  • Temporal artery biopsy (GCA): Reveals granulomatous inflammation with multinucleated giant cells.
  • Ultrasound of temporal arteries: Halo sign indicating arterial wall oedema.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Polymyalgia Rheumatica (PMR) and Giant Cell Arteritis (GCA)"][/sc]
  • A 72-year-old woman with a 2-month history of bilateral shoulder stiffness and pain, along with weight loss and new-onset headache, found to have an elevated ESR and tenderness over the temporal artery.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Polymyalgia Rheumatica (PMR) and Giant Cell Arteritis (GCA)"][/sc]
  • Rheumatoid arthritis: Typically involves smaller joints and may present with joint erosions on imaging.
  • Fibromyalgia: Chronic pain syndrome without elevated inflammatory markers.
  • Systemic lupus erythematosus: May present with multisystem involvement and positive ANA titres.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Polymyalgia Rheumatica (PMR) and Giant Cell Arteritis (GCA)"][/sc]
  • PMR: Low-dose corticosteroids (e.g., prednisolone 15 mg daily), tapered based on symptoms and inflammatory markers.
  • GCA: High-dose corticosteroids (e.g., prednisolone 40-60 mg daily) with immediate initiation to prevent complications.
  • Adjuncts: Bone protection with calcium, vitamin D, and bisphosphonates. Consider aspirin for GCA.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Polymyalgia Rheumatica (PMR) and Giant Cell Arteritis (GCA)"][/sc]
  • GCA: Blindness, stroke, aortic aneurysm.
  • PMR: Long-term corticosteroid use complications such as osteoporosis and diabetes.
