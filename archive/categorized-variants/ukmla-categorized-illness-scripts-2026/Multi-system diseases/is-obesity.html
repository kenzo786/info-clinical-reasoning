Obesity
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Obesity"][/sc]
  • Obesity is a chronic condition characterised by an excessive accumulation of body fat, typically defined by a body mass index (BMI) ≥30 kg/m². It is associated with increased risk for a wide range of medical conditions, including cardiovascular diseases, type 2 diabetes, and certain cancers.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Obesity"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Obesity"][/sc]
  • Prevalence in the UK GP population: Approximately 28% of adults in the UK are classified as obese.
  • Hospital prevalence: A significant proportion of hospital admissions involve obese individuals, with obesity contributing to complications in management of chronic diseases.
  • Global prevalence: Worldwide, the prevalence of obesity has tripled since 1975, with 39% of adults being overweight and 13% obese.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Obesity"][/sc]
  • Obesity develops from an imbalance between caloric intake and energy expenditure, leading to excess fat storage. Multiple factors contribute, including genetic predisposition, hormonal dysregulation (e.g., leptin resistance), and environmental influences (e.g., sedentary lifestyle and high-calorie diets). The chronic inflammatory state associated with visceral fat contributes to insulin resistance, hypertension, dyslipidaemia, and atherosclerosis.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Obesity"][/sc]
  • Obesity typically develops over several years as gradual weight gain. It often begins in childhood or early adulthood and progresses if lifestyle factors and metabolic imbalances are not addressed. Complications such as type 2 diabetes and cardiovascular disease may emerge after years or decades of being overweight or obese.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Obesity"][/sc]
  • Fatigue and lethargy - Excess body weight increases the energy required for physical tasks, leading to reduced stamina and early fatigue.
  • Shortness of breath (dyspnoea) - Often due to reduced respiratory efficiency, increased chest wall weight, and potential co-existing conditions like obstructive sleep apnoea.
  • Joint pain - Increased body weight places excessive stress on weight-bearing joints, commonly causing osteoarthritis.
  • Snoring and sleep disturbances - Associated with obstructive sleep apnoea, where excess neck fat obstructs airflow during sleep.
  • Often asymptomatic - Many patients with obesity may not report symptoms until secondary complications arise.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Obesity"][/sc]
  • Increased BMI (≥30 kg/m²) - A primary indicator of obesity, calculated by weight in kg divided by height in metres squared.
  • Central (abdominal) obesity - Measured by waist circumference (≥94 cm for men, ≥80 cm for women), central obesity is more strongly associated with metabolic complications.
  • Acanthosis nigricans - Darkened, velvety patches of skin, often around the neck or armpits, associated with insulin resistance.
  • Peripheral oedema - Swelling in the lower extremities, which can result from obesity-induced venous stasis or heart failure.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Obesity"][/sc]
  • Rapid weight gain - May indicate secondary causes such as hypothyroidism or Cushing’s syndrome, requiring further investigation.
  • Severe shortness of breath - Suggests respiratory compromise, such as from obstructive sleep apnoea or cardiopulmonary issues.
  • Severe joint pain or immobility - Can indicate advanced osteoarthritis or other musculoskeletal complications.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Obesity"][/sc]
  • Normal BMI but central obesity - Patients may have a normal BMI but carry excessive abdominal fat, increasing the risk for metabolic complications without overt obesity.
  • Obesity in individuals with preserved physical fitness - Some individuals may be physically fit but still meet BMI criteria for obesity, termed 'metabolically healthy obesity'.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Obesity"][/sc]
  • BMI calculation - A simple measure to assess obesity, but may not accurately reflect body fat distribution or muscle mass.
  • Waist circumference - Central obesity is a strong predictor of metabolic risk, with increased risk at ≥94 cm in men and ≥80 cm in women.
  • Lipid profile - Obesity is associated with dyslipidaemia, particularly elevated triglycerides and low HDL cholesterol.
  • HbA1c or fasting blood glucose - Screens for type 2 diabetes, a common complication of obesity due to insulin resistance.
  • Liver function tests (LFTs) - Elevated ALT and AST levels may indicate non-alcoholic fatty liver disease (NAFLD), which is strongly linked to obesity.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Obesity"][/sc]
  • A 45-year-old female presents with a 10-year history of weight gain, BMI 35 kg/m², central obesity, fatigue, and joint pain. She has a family history of type 2 diabetes and reports recent onset of shortness of breath on exertion.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Obesity"][/sc]
  • Hypothyroidism - Presents with weight gain, fatigue, and cold intolerance. Differentiated by low thyroid hormone levels (low free T4 and elevated TSH).
  • Cushing’s syndrome - Causes central obesity, facial rounding, and striae, with elevated cortisol levels.
  • Polycystic ovary syndrome (PCOS) - Common in women with obesity, presents with menstrual irregularities, hirsutism, and insulin resistance.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Obesity"][/sc]
  • Lifestyle modification - The cornerstone of obesity management, involving dietary changes (caloric reduction, healthy eating patterns), increased physical activity, and behavioural support.
  • Pharmacotherapy - Options include orlistat (intestinal fat absorption inhibitor) or GLP-1 agonists like liraglutide for patients with BMI ≥30 or ≥27 with comorbidities.
  • Bariatric surgery - Indicated for individuals with BMI ≥40, or ≥35 with associated comorbidities, offering significant and sustained weight loss.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Obesity"][/sc]
  • Type 2 diabetes - Results from insulin resistance, with obesity as the primary risk factor.
  • Cardiovascular disease - Obesity promotes hypertension, dyslipidaemia, and atherosclerosis, increasing the risk for heart attack and stroke.
  • Non-alcoholic fatty liver disease (NAFLD) - Fat accumulation in the liver can lead to inflammation, fibrosis, and cirrhosis.
