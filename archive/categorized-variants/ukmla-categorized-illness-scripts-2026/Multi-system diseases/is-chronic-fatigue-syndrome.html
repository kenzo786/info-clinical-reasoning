Chronic Fatigue Syndrome (CFS)
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Chronic Fatigue Syndrome (CFS)"][/sc]
  • Chronic Fatigue Syndrome (CFS), also known as Myalgic Encephalomyelitis (ME), is a complex and debilitating condition characterised by persistent, unexplained fatigue lasting for at least six months. This fatigue is not alleviated by rest and is often worsened by physical or mental activity. CFS often involves a range of additional symptoms including cognitive dysfunction, sleep disturbances, and widespread musculoskeletal pain.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Chronic Fatigue Syndrome (CFS)"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Chronic Fatigue Syndrome (CFS)"][/sc]
  • Prevalence in the UK GP population is estimated to be around 0.2-0.4%.
  • Hospital prevalence in the UK is difficult to estimate but likely lower, as most patients are managed in primary care.
  • Worldwide prevalence is variable, ranging between 0.1% and 2.5% depending on diagnostic criteria and population studied.
  • CFS is more common in females than males, with a typical onset between the ages of 20 and 40.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Chronic Fatigue Syndrome (CFS)"][/sc]
  • The pathophysiology of CFS is poorly understood and likely multifactorial. Potential mechanisms include:
    • Immune dysfunction: Abnormal immune responses, such as chronic low-grade inflammation and altered cytokine profiles, are commonly reported.
    • Neuroendocrine dysregulation: Dysfunctions in the hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal (HPA) axis, leading to altered stress hormone levels.
    • Mitochondrial dysfunction: Impaired energy metabolism at the cellular level, potentially explaining the hallmark fatigue.
    • Autonomic nervous system imbalance: Some studies suggest autonomic dysfunction, particularly involving abnormal heart rate and blood pressure responses.
    • Central sensitisation: Amplified pain and fatigue perception due to abnormal sensory processing in the central nervous system.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Chronic Fatigue Syndrome (CFS)"][/sc]
  • Acute phase: Symptoms often follow an acute viral or stressful event and persist beyond normal recovery time.
  • Chronic phase: Fatigue and other symptoms last for months or years. Fluctuations in symptom severity are common, with relapses often triggered by overexertion or stress.
  • Remission/relapse: Some patients experience periods of improvement, but complete recovery is rare. Relapses are common, and the condition may worsen over time.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Chronic Fatigue Syndrome (CFS)"][/sc]
  • Fatigue - Persistent, overwhelming fatigue is the hallmark symptom. It is thought to result from mitochondrial dysfunction and impaired energy metabolism.
  • Post-exertional malaise - Worsening of symptoms following physical or mental activity, likely due to abnormal energy production and autonomic dysregulation.
  • Unrefreshing sleep - Despite adequate sleep, patients feel unrefreshed, possibly due to abnormal sleep architecture and HPA axis dysfunction.
  • Cognitive difficulties ("brain fog") - Memory impairment and concentration problems, likely due to central nervous system involvement and neuroinflammation.
  • Muscle and joint pain - Widespread pain without clear inflammatory markers, potentially due to central sensitisation and altered pain perception.
  • Headaches - Chronic headaches may occur, possibly due to vascular dysregulation or neuroinflammatory changes.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Chronic Fatigue Syndrome (CFS)"][/sc]
  • Orthostatic hypotension - Common, possibly due to autonomic dysfunction and impaired vascular regulation.
  • Tachycardia - Increased heart rate, especially upon standing (postural orthostatic tachycardia syndrome), which can be linked to autonomic nervous system imbalance.
  • Pallor - In some patients, pallor may be observed, potentially related to poor peripheral circulation.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Chronic Fatigue Syndrome (CFS)"][/sc]
  • Unintentional weight loss - May suggest malignancy or other serious conditions.
  • Neurological signs - Focal neurological deficits (e.g., limb weakness, numbness) could indicate multiple sclerosis or other neurological disorders.
  • Severe night sweats - May indicate infection or malignancy, and is not typically associated with CFS.
  • Lymphadenopathy - Generalised or significant lymph node enlargement could suggest lymphoma or chronic infection.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Chronic Fatigue Syndrome (CFS)"][/sc]
  • Some patients may present with isolated cognitive impairment ("brain fog") or unrefreshing sleep as dominant symptoms, leading to under-recognition of the condition.
  • Older patients may attribute fatigue to ageing, delaying diagnosis.
  • Some patients may have minimal post-exertional malaise but still experience significant fatigue, making differentiation from depression challenging.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Chronic Fatigue Syndrome (CFS)"][/sc]
  • Full blood count (FBC) - Typically normal. Used to exclude anaemia or infection.
  • Thyroid function tests (TFTs) - Should be normal in CFS but rule out hypothyroidism, which can cause similar fatigue.
  • Liver function tests (LFTs) - Normal. Rule out hepatic causes of fatigue.
  • Vitamin B12 and folate levels - To rule out deficiency-related fatigue.
  • ESR and CRP - Typically normal in CFS; elevated values may suggest inflammatory or infectious causes.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Chronic Fatigue Syndrome (CFS)"][/sc]
  • A 35-year-old female presents with persistent fatigue for 9 months, exacerbated by physical activity. She also complains of difficulty concentrating, unrefreshing sleep, and intermittent muscle pain. Routine blood tests are normal.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Chronic Fatigue Syndrome (CFS)"][/sc]
  • Depression: While fatigue is common in both, depression typically involves low mood and anhedonia, whereas CFS involves post-exertional malaise and unrefreshing sleep.
  • Hypothyroidism: Can cause fatigue, but usually accompanied by weight gain, cold intolerance, and bradycardia. TFTs are abnormal in hypothyroidism but normal in CFS.
  • Fibromyalgia: Significant overlap with CFS, but fibromyalgia predominantly presents with widespread pain and tender points rather than post-exertional fatigue.
  • Multiple sclerosis: Fatigue can occur, but neurological signs (e.g., optic neuritis, motor weakness) are more pronounced.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Chronic Fatigue Syndrome (CFS)"][/sc]
  • Graded exercise therapy (GET) - A gradual increase in physical activity, tailored to patient tolerance. Recently debated due to concerns of worsening symptoms in some patients.
  • Cognitive behavioural therapy (CBT) - Helps patients cope with symptoms and manage the impact on daily life.
  • Symptomatic management - For pain, sleep disturbances, and mood disorders. Medications such as amitriptyline for pain or melatonin for sleep may be used.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Chronic Fatigue Syndrome (CFS)"][/sc]
  • Depression and anxiety: Common due to the chronic and disabling nature of the illness.
  • Deconditioning: Physical inactivity can lead to muscle weakness and cardiovascular deconditioning.
  • Social isolation: The severe fatigue may result in reduced ability to engage in social and occupational activities.
