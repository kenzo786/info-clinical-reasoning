Subarachnoid Haemorrhage (SAH)
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Subarachnoid Haemorrhage (SAH)"][/sc]
  • Subarachnoid haemorrhage (SAH) refers to bleeding into the subarachnoid space, the area between the arachnoid membrane and the pia mater surrounding the brain. It is most commonly caused by a ruptured aneurysm and leads to a sudden increase in intracranial pressure, often causing severe neurological impairment and death. It accounts for approximately 5% of all strokes.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Subarachnoid Haemorrhage (SAH)"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Subarachnoid Haemorrhage (SAH)"][/sc]
  • Prevalence: Subarachnoid haemorrhage affects approximately 6-10 per 100,000 people annually in the UK, with higher rates in hospitalised populations.
  • Worldwide, the incidence varies, with Japan and Finland showing higher rates (up to 20 per 100,000), while other regions have lower rates.
  • SAH is more common in individuals aged 40-60, with a female predominance due to a higher incidence of aneurysms.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Subarachnoid Haemorrhage (SAH)"][/sc]
  • Subarachnoid haemorrhage occurs when a blood vessel within the brain, typically a cerebral aneurysm, ruptures. The rupture causes blood to rapidly leak into the subarachnoid space, leading to a sharp rise in intracranial pressure.
  • The blood itself can irritate the meninges, causing severe headaches and neck stiffness. Blood breakdown products can lead to vasospasm of nearby arteries, which is a significant cause of delayed cerebral ischaemia.
  • Increased intracranial pressure reduces cerebral perfusion, potentially leading to global or focal brain ischaemia. The haemorrhage also triggers an inflammatory response, exacerbating brain injury.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Subarachnoid Haemorrhage (SAH)"][/sc]
  • Acute phase: Sudden onset of severe symptoms, typically within seconds to minutes after rupture, often presenting with a "thunderclap headache." Neurological deterioration is rapid in cases of large haemorrhages.
  • Early complications: Vasospasm occurs in about 30% of patients between days 4-14, leading to delayed cerebral ischaemia.
  • Chronic phase: If patients survive the acute phase, they may face long-term neurological deficits and require rehabilitation for cognitive and physical impairments.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Subarachnoid Haemorrhage (SAH)"][/sc]
  • Sudden, severe headache ("thunderclap headache") - Caused by the abrupt increase in intracranial pressure and meningeal irritation.
  • Nausea and vomiting - Resulting from raised intracranial pressure and irritation of the vomiting centre in the medulla.
  • Photophobia - Meningeal irritation from the blood in the subarachnoid space can lead to sensitivity to light.
  • Neck stiffness - A sign of meningeal irritation due to blood in the subarachnoid space.
  • Seizures - Occur in about 10-20% of cases, likely due to cortical irritation.
  • Loss of consciousness - May occur at onset due to a large bleed or significant rise in intracranial pressure.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Subarachnoid Haemorrhage (SAH)"][/sc]
  • Altered level of consciousness - From increased intracranial pressure and brain injury.
  • Neck stiffness - From meningeal irritation due to blood in the subarachnoid space.
  • Focal neurological deficits - Depending on the location and size of the haemorrhage, patients may present with hemiparesis, aphasia, or cranial nerve palsies.
  • Papilloedema - A sign of raised intracranial pressure, seen on fundoscopic examination.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Subarachnoid Haemorrhage (SAH)"][/sc]
  • Thunderclap headache - A hallmark of SAH, indicating rapid intracranial bleeding and increased pressure.
  • Rapid deterioration in consciousness - Suggests significant bleeding or impending brain herniation.
  • Severe neck stiffness - Indicates meningeal irritation from the haemorrhage, particularly in larger bleeds.
  • Focal neurological signs - May indicate a more localised bleed or associated vasospasm causing ischaemia.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Subarachnoid Haemorrhage (SAH)"][/sc]
  • Mild headache - Some patients may present with a less severe headache, leading to a missed or delayed diagnosis.
  • Confusion without headache - In elderly patients, SAH may present primarily with confusion or altered mental status, without the classical headache.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Subarachnoid Haemorrhage (SAH)"][/sc]
  • CT Head - First-line test; reveals blood in the subarachnoid space in 95% of cases within the first 24 hours.
  • Lumbar puncture - If CT is negative but suspicion remains high; CSF analysis will show xanthochromia (yellowish discolouration) due to breakdown of haemoglobin.
  • CT angiography - Performed to locate the source of bleeding (aneurysm or arteriovenous malformation).
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Subarachnoid Haemorrhage (SAH)"][/sc]
  • A 52-year-old woman presents with a sudden onset "thunderclap headache," neck stiffness, and photophobia, followed by a brief loss of consciousness. Examination reveals drowsiness and mild right-sided weakness.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Subarachnoid Haemorrhage (SAH)"][/sc]
  • Migraine - Typically has a slower onset and associated with auras; lacks neck stiffness or altered consciousness.
  • Meningitis - May present with headache and neck stiffness, but fever and a more gradual onset of symptoms are characteristic.
  • Intracerebral haemorrhage - May present similarly, but the bleeding is typically confined to brain parenchyma and does not involve the subarachnoid space.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Subarachnoid Haemorrhage (SAH)"][/sc]
  • Initial management - Stabilise airway, breathing, and circulation (ABC), and manage raised intracranial pressure.
  • Surgical intervention - Aneurysm clipping or endovascular coiling to prevent re-bleeding, preferably within 72 hours.
  • Vasospasm management - Nimodipine (calcium channel blocker) is given to prevent delayed cerebral ischaemia.
  • Supportive care - Close monitoring in an intensive care setting for neurological status, blood pressure control, and seizure prevention.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Subarachnoid Haemorrhage (SAH)"][/sc]
  • Re-bleeding - Occurs in 20% of untreated cases, with a high risk of mortality.
  • Vasospasm - Causes delayed cerebral ischaemia, leading to stroke-like symptoms.
  • Hydrocephalus - Due to blockage of cerebrospinal fluid pathways by blood clots, leading to raised intracranial pressure.
