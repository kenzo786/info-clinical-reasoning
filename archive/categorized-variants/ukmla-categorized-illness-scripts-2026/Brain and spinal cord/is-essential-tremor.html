Essential Tremor
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Essential Tremor"][/sc]
  • Essential tremor (ET) is a neurological condition characterised by a postural and kinetic tremor, predominantly affecting the upper limbs. It is the most common movement disorder and typically worsens with voluntary movements such as writing or holding objects. Unlike Parkinson's disease, essential tremor does not usually include other neurological symptoms like rigidity or bradykinesia.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Essential Tremor"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Essential Tremor"][/sc]
  • Prevalence in UK GP Population: Essential tremor affects approximately 4-5% of the population over the age of 40 in primary care settings.
  • UK Hospital Prevalence: Hospitalisation due to ET is rare, but severe cases needing specialised care are seen in neurology clinics.
  • Worldwide Prevalence: Global prevalence is around 0.9-4.6%, with increasing incidence in older populations.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Essential Tremor"][/sc]
  • While the exact pathophysiology of essential tremor is not completely understood, it is thought to involve dysfunction in the central nervous system, particularly the cerebellum and its connections to the thalamus. Abnormal oscillatory activity in these circuits is believed to cause tremor. Genetic factors also play a role, with about half of cases being familial (autosomal dominant inheritance).
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Essential Tremor"][/sc]
  • Essential tremor is typically a chronic condition that develops gradually over time. Tremor severity may increase with age, but the rate of progression is variable. Most patients experience a slow progression over decades.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Essential Tremor"][/sc]
  • Tremor of the hands - The hallmark symptom is a bilateral action tremor of the hands, most noticeable when performing fine motor tasks like writing or drinking. This occurs due to abnormal firing in the cerebellar-thalamic pathway.
  • Tremor of the head - Some patients develop head tremor ("yes-yes" or "no-no" motion) without significant lower limb involvement. This reflects cerebellar involvement in head and neck motor control.
  • Voice tremor - In some cases, patients may present with a quavering or shaky voice due to involvement of the muscles of the larynx and pharynx.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Essential Tremor"][/sc]
  • Bilateral postural tremor - A key clinical finding is a tremor that is most evident when the arms are held against gravity (e.g., during the "arms outstretched" test). This is due to dysfunction in cerebellar circuits regulating postural control.
  • Kinetic tremor - Tremor that becomes worse during voluntary movements such as touching the finger to the nose or pouring water. This relates to cerebellar dysfunction affecting fine motor coordination.
  • Normal neurological examination otherwise - There are no signs of bradykinesia, rigidity, or postural instability, which helps differentiate ET from Parkinson's disease.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Essential Tremor"][/sc]
  • Asymmetric tremor - May suggest an alternative diagnosis such as Parkinson's disease, as ET is typically symmetric.
  • Resting tremor - Tremor present at rest, especially in conjunction with rigidity or bradykinesia, may indicate Parkinson's disease.
  • Rapid progression - Faster than expected progression of tremor or the appearance of other neurological signs (e.g., ataxia, cognitive decline) may indicate a neurodegenerative condition such as multiple system atrophy (MSA).
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Essential Tremor"][/sc]
  • Isolated head tremor - Some patients may present with isolated head tremor, which can be confused with dystonia or cervical tremor.
  • Voice tremor without hand tremor - Rarely, essential tremor may affect only the voice, leading to diagnostic confusion with vocal cord disorders.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Essential Tremor"][/sc]
  • Clinical diagnosis - ET is primarily diagnosed clinically. A detailed history and physical examination are crucial.
  • Exclusion of other causes - Routine blood tests such as thyroid function tests (TFTs) may be performed to exclude hyperthyroidism, which can cause tremor.
  • Response to alcohol - Tremor may transiently improve with alcohol consumption, a distinctive feature in ET. This is thought to result from alcohol's dampening effect on the cerebellum.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Essential Tremor"][/sc]
  • A 65-year-old man presents with a 10-year history of bilateral hand tremor that worsens with activity, such as holding a cup. He reports a family history of tremor and notes mild improvement after drinking alcohol.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Essential Tremor"][/sc]
  • Parkinson's disease - Distinguished by the presence of a resting tremor, rigidity, bradykinesia, and asymmetry.
  • Dystonia - Typically involves sustained muscle contractions causing abnormal postures, often focal or segmental, unlike the rhythmic tremor of ET.
  • Drug-induced tremor - Caused by medications such as beta-agonists or antipsychotics, typically resolves after discontinuation of the offending drug.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Essential Tremor"][/sc]
  • First-line treatment - Propranolol (a beta-blocker) or primidone (an anticonvulsant) are the first-line treatments. Both reduce tremor amplitude in most patients.
  • Second-line treatment - Other medications include gabapentin or topiramate.
  • Surgical treatment - In severe cases, deep brain stimulation (DBS) targeting the thalamus may be considered.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Essential Tremor"][/sc]
  • Functional impairment - Tremor can severely affect daily activities such as writing or eating, reducing quality of life.
  • Psychological impact - Patients may experience anxiety or depression due to social embarrassment from tremor.
