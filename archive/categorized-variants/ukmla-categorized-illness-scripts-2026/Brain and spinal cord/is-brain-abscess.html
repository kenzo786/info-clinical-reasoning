Brain Abscess
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Brain Abscess"][/sc]
  • A brain abscess is a focal, intracranial infection characterised by a collection of pus within the brain parenchyma. It typically results from direct extension from nearby infections (e.g., sinusitis, otitis media), haematogenous spread from distant sources (e.g., infective endocarditis), or trauma/surgery. Left untreated, it can lead to severe neurological damage or death.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Brain Abscess"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Brain Abscess"][/sc]
  • In the UK, brain abscesses are rare, with an incidence of approximately 1 per 100,000 people. The condition is more common in hospital settings, particularly among immunocompromised patients and those with chronic conditions such as congenital heart disease or HIV. Globally, prevalence varies but remains low, with higher rates in regions where bacterial infections like otitis media are more prevalent.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Brain Abscess"][/sc]
  • Brain abscess formation occurs in stages. First, a local infection (e.g., sinusitis, dental infection) spreads to brain tissue. The infection triggers an inflammatory response, leading to oedema and neutrophil infiltration. This results in the formation of a cerebritis (inflammatory area), which later encapsulates as a pus-filled abscess. If the abscess enlarges, it increases intracranial pressure (ICP) and may cause herniation or rupture, spreading infection into the ventricles or subarachnoid space.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Brain Abscess"][/sc]
  • Brain abscesses typically develop over days to weeks. Initial symptoms are often vague, with headache and fever in the early stages. As the abscess grows, symptoms related to increased ICP (e.g., vomiting, drowsiness) and focal neurological deficits may emerge. Without treatment, rapid deterioration can occur due to herniation, rupture, or sepsis.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Brain Abscess"][/sc]
  • Headache - The most common presenting symptom, caused by increased intracranial pressure (ICP) from the abscess or surrounding oedema.
  • Fever - Reflects the body's systemic inflammatory response to infection, though it may be absent in later stages.
  • Nausea and vomiting - Result from raised ICP affecting the vomiting centre in the medulla oblongata.
  • Focal neurological deficits - Depend on the location of the abscess; e.g., motor weakness if in the motor cortex, or visual disturbances if in the occipital lobe.
  • Altered consciousness - As ICP rises, cerebral perfusion decreases, leading to drowsiness, confusion, and in severe cases, coma.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Brain Abscess"][/sc]
  • Focal neurological signs - Motor weakness, aphasia, or sensory deficits are related to abscess location and surrounding tissue involvement.
  • Papilloedema - A sign of raised intracranial pressure, seen on fundoscopic examination.
  • Seizures - May occur due to irritation of cortical tissue by the abscess.
  • Signs of infection - Fever, tachycardia, and signs of the primary infection source (e.g., ear infection, dental abscess).
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Brain Abscess"][/sc]
  • Rapidly worsening headache - May indicate abscess enlargement, increased ICP, or impending herniation.
  • Sudden alteration in consciousness - Suggests herniation or rupture of the abscess, leading to acute intracranial pressure changes.
  • New-onset seizures - A sign of cortical irritation, indicating worsening of the condition or increased pressure.
  • Signs of meningism - Stiff neck and photophobia may suggest abscess rupture into the ventricles, leading to meningitis.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Brain Abscess"][/sc]
  • Chronic low-grade symptoms - Some patients, especially immunocompromised, may present with a prolonged course of mild headache and confusion without overt infection signs, making the diagnosis difficult.
  • Absence of fever - Particularly in the elderly or those with impaired immune responses, fever may be absent despite significant infection.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Brain Abscess"][/sc]
  • MRI with contrast - Preferred imaging modality; reveals a ring-enhancing lesion with surrounding oedema, due to the capsule formation and inflammation.
  • CT scan with contrast - Faster and more accessible than MRI; shows a hypodense centre with a ring-enhancing capsule.
  • Blood cultures - Used to identify the causative organism, particularly if haematogenous spread is suspected.
  • Full blood count - May show raised white cell count (leukocytosis), indicating infection.
  • C-reactive protein (CRP) and erythrocyte sedimentation rate (ESR) - Elevated due to systemic inflammation.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Brain Abscess"][/sc]
  • A 45-year-old male presents with a 10-day history of worsening headache, fever, and vomiting. He reports a recent history of chronic sinusitis. On examination, he has right-sided hemiparesis and papilloedema.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Brain Abscess"][/sc]
  • Subdural empyema - Presents similarly but typically shows crescentic fluid collection on imaging, without the well-formed capsule seen in abscesses.
  • Stroke - Can cause focal deficits, but onset is usually acute, and imaging reveals infarction without ring enhancement.
  • Primary brain tumour - Often causes progressive symptoms, but lacks systemic infection signs, and imaging shows solid, non-enhancing masses.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Brain Abscess"][/sc]
  • Antibiotics - Empirical therapy should cover anaerobes and common aerobic organisms. First-line UK therapy includes ceftriaxone with metronidazole. Treatment is modified based on culture results.
  • Surgical drainage - Needed for larger abscesses (>2.5 cm) to reduce mass effect and obtain samples for microbiological diagnosis.
  • Management of raised ICP - Dexamethasone is used to reduce perilesional oedema, and mannitol may be needed to manage raised ICP.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Brain Abscess"][/sc]
  • Intracranial rupture - The abscess may rupture into the ventricles, leading to ventriculitis and acute hydrocephalus.
  • Herniation - Rapid increase in ICP may cause brain herniation, a life-threatening complication.
  • Sepsis - Spread of infection to the bloodstream may result in systemic inflammatory response syndrome (SIRS) and septic shock.
