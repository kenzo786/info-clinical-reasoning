Congenital Heart Disease
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Congenital Heart Disease"][/sc]
  • Congenital heart disease (CHD) refers to structural abnormalities of the heart or great vessels present from birth, arising due to improper embryological development. It encompasses a spectrum of conditions, from simple lesions like atrial septal defects to complex abnormalities like tetralogy of Fallot or hypoplastic left heart syndrome.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Congenital Heart Disease"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Congenital Heart Disease"][/sc]
  • UK prevalence in the general population is approximately 1 in 100 live births, with around 0.8% of infants being affected annually.
  • CHD is the most common congenital anomaly, with worldwide prevalence estimated at 8-12 per 1,000 live births.
  • Advances in diagnosis and treatment have improved survival, leading to an increasing number of adults living with CHD.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Congenital Heart Disease"][/sc]
  • Embryological disruption during weeks 3-8 of gestation affects heart development, leading to malformations.
  • Defects may involve septal development (e.g., ventricular septal defect), valve formation (e.g., pulmonary stenosis), or abnormal vessel connections (e.g., transposition of the great arteries).
  • Pathophysiological outcomes depend on the defect, including altered blood flow patterns, pressure overload, hypoxaemia, or cyanosis in complex lesions.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Congenital Heart Disease"][/sc]
  • Neonatal period: Severe forms may present with cyanosis or heart failure soon after birth.
  • Infancy to childhood: Growth failure, feeding difficulties, or recurrent respiratory infections may predominate.
  • Adolescence to adulthood: Residual lesions, arrhythmias, or heart failure may develop due to repaired or untreated defects.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Congenital Heart Disease"][/sc]
  • Cyanosis: Common in conditions with right-to-left shunting; results from deoxygenated blood bypassing the lungs.
  • Failure to thrive: Often due to increased metabolic demands and decreased oxygen delivery.
  • Feeding difficulties: Secondary to dyspnoea or fatigue during feeding in infants.
  • Recurrent respiratory infections: Caused by pulmonary congestion or abnormal flow dynamics.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Congenital Heart Disease"][/sc]
  • Murmurs: Turbulent blood flow through abnormal heart structures, often the first clue in auscultation.
  • Cyanosis: Peripheral or central; indicates desaturation due to right-to-left shunting.
  • Tachypnoea: Reflects heart failure or pulmonary over-circulation.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Congenital Heart Disease"][/sc]
  • Severe cyanosis: Suggests duct-dependent lesions requiring urgent intervention to maintain systemic blood flow.
  • Cardiogenic shock: Indicated by hypotension, poor perfusion, and acidosis; can signify critical stenosis or closure of a ductus arteriosus.
  • Failure to wean from oxygen: May point to undiagnosed structural heart disease.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Congenital Heart Disease"][/sc]
  • Subtle growth delay: May be the only indicator in less severe cases.
  • Late cyanosis: Occurs in Eisenmenger syndrome when a long-standing left-to-right shunt reverses.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Congenital Heart Disease"][/sc]
  • Echocardiography: Key imaging modality to visualise structural abnormalities and flow dynamics.
  • Pulse oximetry: Identifies hypoxaemia or oxygen desaturation, critical in cyanotic conditions.
  • ECG: May reveal arrhythmias, chamber hypertrophy, or conduction abnormalities.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Congenital Heart Disease"][/sc]
  • An 8-week-old male presents with central cyanosis, tachypnoea, and poor feeding. On examination, a loud systolic murmur is noted, and oxygen saturations are 85%.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Congenital Heart Disease"][/sc]
  • Pulmonary atresia: Presents with severe cyanosis; differs in echocardiographic findings.
  • Sepsis: Features fever, systemic signs of infection; lacks structural defects on imaging.
  • Persistent pulmonary hypertension of the newborn: Mimics cyanosis but resolves without structural anomalies.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Congenital Heart Disease"][/sc]
  • Initial stabilisation: Prostaglandin E1 to maintain ductal patency in duct-dependent lesions.
  • Surgical intervention: Repair or palliation tailored to the specific defect.
  • Long-term management: Regular cardiology follow-up, endocarditis prophylaxis, and monitoring for complications.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Congenital Heart Disease"][/sc]
  • Heart failure: From volume or pressure overload.
  • Arrhythmias: Due to scarring or chamber enlargement.
  • Endocarditis: Risk in unrepaired or prosthetic defects.
