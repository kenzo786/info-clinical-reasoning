Ectopic Pregnancy
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Ectopic Pregnancy"][/sc]
  • Ectopic pregnancy is a condition in which a fertilised ovum implants outside the uterine cavity, most commonly in the fallopian tube. It is a potentially life-threatening emergency due to the risk of tubal rupture and haemorrhage.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Ectopic Pregnancy"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Ectopic Pregnancy"][/sc]
  • The prevalence of ectopic pregnancy in the UK is approximately 11 per 1,000 pregnancies.
  • In UK general practice, the incidence is lower, with fewer cases presenting before referral to secondary care. It represents around 1-2% of all pregnancies worldwide.
  • Ectopic pregnancy is responsible for approximately 3-10% of maternal mortality globally.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Ectopic Pregnancy"][/sc]
  • Normally, the fertilised egg travels through the fallopian tube to the uterus. In ectopic pregnancy, an obstruction or impaired tubal function causes implantation within the tube or elsewhere.
  • The most common site is the fallopian tube (95%), followed by the ovary, cervix, or abdominal cavity.
  • The growing embryo stretches the fallopian tube, causing inflammation, pain, and eventually rupture if untreated, leading to haemoperitoneum.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Ectopic Pregnancy"][/sc]
  • Ectopic pregnancy typically becomes symptomatic between 6-10 weeks of gestation. Early symptoms may be subtle but rapidly progress to more severe signs such as pain and haemodynamic instability if rupture occurs.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Ectopic Pregnancy"][/sc]
  • Pelvic pain - The most common symptom, caused by tubal distension or rupture.
  • Amenorrhoea - Missed period is a key clue to pregnancy status.
  • Vaginal bleeding - Light spotting or irregular bleeding due to hormonal dysregulation.
  • Shoulder tip pain - Referred pain from diaphragmatic irritation due to haemoperitoneum in case of rupture.
  • Dizziness or syncope - May occur due to intra-abdominal haemorrhage leading to hypovolaemia.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Ectopic Pregnancy"][/sc]
  • Adnexal tenderness - Caused by inflammation and distension of the fallopian tube.
  • Abdominal guarding or rebound tenderness - Signifies peritoneal irritation due to blood leakage.
  • Hypotension and tachycardia - Occur in cases of rupture, signalling significant blood loss and shock.
  • Pallor - Due to hypovolaemic shock following tubal rupture.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Ectopic Pregnancy"][/sc]
  • Severe abdominal pain - Signifies possible tubal rupture and requires immediate intervention.
  • Haemodynamic instability (hypotension, tachycardia) - Suggests major haemorrhage and impending shock.
  • Syncope - May indicate significant internal bleeding.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Ectopic Pregnancy"][/sc]
  • Some ectopic pregnancies may present with minimal or no pain and only mild vaginal bleeding, particularly in cases of non-tubal implantation (e.g., cervical or abdominal).
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Ectopic Pregnancy"][/sc]
  • Serum β-hCG - Levels are typically lower than expected for gestational age, with slower rise compared to a normal intrauterine pregnancy.
  • Transvaginal ultrasound - Absence of an intrauterine pregnancy combined with the presence of an adnexal mass or free fluid in the pelvis raises suspicion for ectopic pregnancy.
  • Complete blood count (FBC) - May reveal anaemia in cases of significant blood loss.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Ectopic Pregnancy"][/sc]
  • A 29-year-old woman presents with 6 weeks of amenorrhoea, increasing lower abdominal pain, light vaginal bleeding, and dizziness. Her vitals show hypotension and tachycardia.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Ectopic Pregnancy"][/sc]
  • Miscarriage - Similar symptoms but often with visible intrauterine pregnancy on ultrasound.
  • Ovarian cyst rupture - Sudden pelvic pain but typically no positive pregnancy test.
  • Pelvic inflammatory disease - Pelvic pain and discharge, but absence of pregnancy and usually gradual onset.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Ectopic Pregnancy"][/sc]
  • Medical management - Methotrexate may be used for stable, unruptured ectopic pregnancies with low β-hCG levels.
  • Surgical management - Laparoscopic salpingectomy or salpingostomy is indicated in cases of rupture or haemodynamic instability.
  • Expectant management - In select cases, if β-hCG levels are declining, and the patient is stable, conservative management may be considered.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Ectopic Pregnancy"][/sc]
  • Tubal rupture - Can lead to life-threatening haemorrhage and hypovolaemic shock.
  • Infertility - Damage to the fallopian tubes increases the risk of future infertility.
  • Recurrence - There is an increased risk of future ectopic pregnancies.
