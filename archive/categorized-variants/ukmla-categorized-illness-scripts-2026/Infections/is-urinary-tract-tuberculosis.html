Urinary Tract Tuberculosis (UTB)
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Urinary Tract Tuberculosis (UTB)"][/sc]
  • Urinary tract tuberculosis (UTB) is a form of extrapulmonary tuberculosis affecting the urinary system, primarily caused by Mycobacterium tuberculosis. It is characterised by chronic infection leading to granulomatous inflammation, fibrosis, and, potentially, urinary tract obstruction. UTB may involve the kidneys, ureters, bladder, and, less commonly, the urethra and prostate.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Urinary Tract Tuberculosis (UTB)"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Urinary Tract Tuberculosis (UTB)"][/sc]
  • UK GP Population Prevalence: Low, due to effective TB control; higher among immigrant populations from TB-endemic countries.
  • UK Hospital Prevalence: UTB cases are relatively rare in hospital admissions, but constitute about 15-20% of extrapulmonary TB cases.
  • Global Prevalence: Approximately 1-2% of all tuberculosis cases are urinary tract tuberculosis, with higher prevalence in TB-endemic regions such as Southeast Asia and sub-Saharan Africa.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Urinary Tract Tuberculosis (UTB)"][/sc]
  • Primary Infection: Mycobacterium tuberculosis primarily infects the lungs, where it forms granulomas and may enter a latent state.
  • Haematogenous Spread: In susceptible individuals, bacteria spread via the bloodstream, infecting the kidneys first due to high vascularity. The infection can later progress to other urinary structures.
  • Granuloma Formation: Granulomas form in the kidneys, leading to necrosis, fibrosis, and caseous lesions, which impair renal function and cause urinary obstruction.
  • Local Invasion: In advanced cases, infection spreads to the bladder, ureters, and, occasionally, the genital tract, causing chronic inflammation and scarring.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Urinary Tract Tuberculosis (UTB)"][/sc]
  • Latent Phase: Initial exposure and containment in granulomas, often asymptomatic and can last years.
  • Active Phase: Gradual symptom onset as granulomas in the kidneys lead to local inflammation and eventual tissue destruction, often months to years after primary infection.
  • Progressive Phase: Advanced infection may involve obstruction, hydronephrosis, and spread to bladder or genital organs if untreated.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Urinary Tract Tuberculosis (UTB)"][/sc]
  • Dysuria: Painful urination occurs due to bladder wall inflammation.
  • Haematuria: Blood in urine is common due to granulomatous lesions or mucosal ulcerations.
  • Flank Pain: Chronic kidney involvement can lead to unilateral or bilateral flank pain as granulomas cause renal obstruction.
  • Urinary Frequency: Increased urination frequency due to bladder involvement and irritation.
  • Asymptomatic Presentation: In early stages, UTB may be silent, especially in patients with latent TB.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Urinary Tract Tuberculosis (UTB)"][/sc]
  • Fever: Low-grade fever can be due to systemic inflammation associated with TB infection.
  • Weight Loss: Chronic infections like TB often lead to unintended weight loss due to increased metabolic demands and cytokine production.
  • Flank Tenderness: Palpable tenderness over affected kidneys is common with active infection and inflammation.
  • Renal Mass (rare): Palpable kidney mass may form in chronic cases due to granulomatous inflammation and scarring.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Urinary Tract Tuberculosis (UTB)"][/sc]
  • Persistent Haematuria: Indicative of mucosal ulceration or advanced disease; may signify impending obstruction.
  • Progressive Renal Dysfunction: Suggests possible hydronephrosis or renal impairment from prolonged infection and fibrosis.
  • Systemic Symptoms (Fever, Night Sweats): Indicates active TB infection with risk of dissemination.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Urinary Tract Tuberculosis (UTB)"][/sc]
  • Isolated Bladder Symptoms: Mimics common cystitis without renal involvement, leading to misdiagnosis if not tested for TB.
  • Renal Mass without Inflammatory Symptoms: Seen in chronic cases with fibrosis, mimicking malignancy.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Urinary Tract Tuberculosis (UTB)"][/sc]
  • Urine Culture: Repeated cultures (3-5 samples) detect acid-fast bacilli (AFB); positive culture confirms UTB.
  • Imaging (Ultrasound or CT KUB): Identifies renal scarring, calcifications, or hydronephrosis indicating chronic UTB.
  • Polymerase Chain Reaction (PCR): Sensitive for detecting Mycobacterium DNA, especially in culture-negative cases.
  • ESR and CRP: Elevated inflammatory markers are non-specific but support active infection.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Urinary Tract Tuberculosis (UTB)"][/sc]
  • A 45-year-old male presents with six months of haematuria, dysuria, and weight loss. Imaging reveals renal calcification, and urine cultures confirm Mycobacterium tuberculosis.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Urinary Tract Tuberculosis (UTB)"][/sc]
  • Chronic Pyelonephritis: UTB often has more insidious onset and can cause caseous necrosis, whereas chronic pyelonephritis shows persistent bacterial infection without caseous lesions.
  • Renal Cell Carcinoma: Presents with haematuria but lacks the granulomatous inflammation seen in UTB; imaging may show solid renal mass instead of calcified or cavitary lesions.
  • Bladder Cancer: Often causes painless haematuria and is common in older patients with smoking history; UTB shows inflammatory signs and infection on urine culture.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Urinary Tract Tuberculosis (UTB)"][/sc]
  • First-Line Therapy: Standard anti-TB therapy (isoniazid, rifampicin, ethambutol, and pyrazinamide) for 6-9 months.
  • Surgical Intervention: Indicated for severe obstruction or hydronephrosis; nephrectomy or urinary diversion procedures may be required in advanced cases.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Urinary Tract Tuberculosis (UTB)"][/sc]
  • Renal Failure: Chronic obstruction and fibrosis lead to progressive renal impairment.
  • Hydronephrosis: Secondary to ureteral obstruction by granulomatous tissue or strictures.
  • Bladder Contracture: Long-term inflammation and fibrosis reduce bladder capacity.
