Retropharyngeal Abscess illness script
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Retropharyngeal Abscess"][/sc]
  • Retropharyngeal abscess (RPA) is a deep neck space infection located in the retropharyngeal space, which lies behind the pharynx. It is characterised by a collection of pus that can cause airway obstruction and sepsis if not treated promptly. The condition is more common in children, typically following an upper respiratory tract infection, trauma, or spread of infection from nearby structures.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Retropharyngeal Abscess"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Retropharyngeal Abscess"][/sc]
  • Prevalence in the UK GP population: RPA is rare in general practice, with most cases referred to hospital. It is primarily seen in children under 5 years of age.
  • Hospital prevalence in the UK: RPAs account for less than 0.2% of paediatric admissions for respiratory distress but are more common in emergency ENT referrals.
  • Worldwide prevalence: Incidence is higher in low-resource settings where upper respiratory infections are more common, and access to early medical intervention may be limited.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Retropharyngeal Abscess"][/sc]
  • The retropharyngeal space is located between the buccopharyngeal fascia and the prevertebral fascia. Infection in this space usually results from the spread of a bacterial infection from adjacent structures (e.g., tonsils, adenoids, or sinuses) or from direct trauma (e.g., foreign body or intubation).
  • Bacteria, commonly Streptococcus pyogenes and Staphylococcus aureus, proliferate and cause suppuration, leading to the formation of an abscess. This abscess creates pressure within the confined retropharyngeal space, potentially leading to airway obstruction or mediastinitis if it spreads inferiorly.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Retropharyngeal Abscess"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Initial symptoms may develop 1–2 days following a preceding infection (e.g., pharyngitis or tonsillitis), with mild sore throat and fever.
  • Progression: Over 3–7 days, symptoms worsen with increased neck pain, dysphagia (difficulty swallowing), odynophagia (painful swallowing), and fever.
  • Late phase: Within a week, airway compromise, neck swelling, and systemic sepsis may develop if untreated, leading to a medical emergency.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Retropharyngeal Abscess"][/sc]
  • Fever - Due to the systemic inflammatory response to infection and pus formation in the retropharyngeal space.
  • Neck pain - Localised inflammation and pressure from the abscess cause pain, particularly in the posterior pharyngeal region.
  • Sore throat - Often one of the earliest symptoms, caused by the involvement of the pharyngeal tissue in the infectious process.
  • Dysphagia - Difficulty swallowing occurs due to swelling and inflammation in the posterior pharynx, obstructing normal passage of food.
  • Odynophagia - Painful swallowing due to pressure exerted by the abscess on the surrounding nerves and structures.
  • Stridor - A high-pitched breathing sound indicating partial airway obstruction, which occurs as the abscess increases in size.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Retropharyngeal Abscess"][/sc]
  • Neck swelling - Results from the accumulation of pus and associated inflammation, usually observed anteriorly or laterally.
  • Torticollis - Stiffness or limited movement of the neck as the child may try to position the head to minimise pain and pressure from the abscess.
  • Drooling - Indicates difficulty swallowing or fear of swallowing due to pain or partial airway obstruction.
  • Trismus - Limited mouth opening can occur due to involvement of adjacent structures like the pterygoid muscles.
  • Tachypnoea - Rapid breathing indicates respiratory distress, especially if airway obstruction is impending.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Retropharyngeal Abscess"][/sc]
  • Stridor - Indicative of airway compromise. This signifies a critical narrowing of the airway and requires immediate intervention to prevent respiratory arrest.
  • Tachypnoea with accessory muscle use - Suggests impending respiratory failure. As the abscess grows, it compresses the airway, leading to compensatory use of accessory muscles to maintain oxygenation.
  • Altered mental status - Suggests sepsis or hypoxia, both of which are life-threatening and require urgent treatment.
  • Rapidly progressing neck swelling - May indicate rapid abscess expansion or extension into the mediastinum, increasing the risk of life-threatening complications like mediastinitis or airway obstruction.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Retropharyngeal Abscess"][/sc]
  • Low-grade fever with minimal neck pain - Particularly in immunocompromised individuals or elderly patients, symptoms may be blunted or slow to progress, leading to delayed diagnosis.
  • Referred otalgia - Ear pain can sometimes occur without obvious throat symptoms due to shared innervation between the throat and ear (cranial nerve IX).
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Retropharyngeal Abscess"][/sc]
  • Neck X-ray (lateral) - Shows prevertebral soft tissue swelling, which is a hallmark of retropharyngeal abscess. Soft tissue width greater than half the vertebral body width at C2 is abnormal.
  • CT scan with contrast - Confirms diagnosis by identifying the extent of the abscess, pus collection, and any potential spread to surrounding tissues.
  • Blood cultures - Useful for identifying the causative organism, particularly in cases of systemic sepsis.
  • Full blood count - Raised white cell count (leucocytosis) indicates infection.
  • C-reactive protein (CRP) - Elevated CRP supports a diagnosis of bacterial infection and helps monitor the response to treatment.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Retropharyngeal Abscess"][/sc]
  • A 3-year-old male presents with a 3-day history of fever, sore throat, dysphagia, and worsening neck pain. On examination, he has torticollis, drooling, and mild stridor. Lateral neck X-ray shows prevertebral soft tissue swelling, and CT scan confirms a retropharyngeal abscess.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Retropharyngeal Abscess"][/sc]
  • Peritonsillar abscess (quinsy) - Differentiated by its more lateralised swelling and uvular deviation, whereas RPA is midline or posterior.
  • Epiglottitis - Presents with a muffled "hot potato" voice and drooling, but tends to have a more rapid onset and no lateral neck swelling on X-ray.
  • Lymphadenitis - Inflammation of lymph nodes, often without the systemic severity and airway risk seen in RPA.
  • Foreign body aspiration - Sudden onset of respiratory distress with a history of choking, no fever, and negative imaging for abscess.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Retropharyngeal Abscess"][/sc]
  • Intravenous antibiotics: Empirical treatment includes high-dose IV co-amoxiclav or ceftriaxone, covering both aerobic and anaerobic organisms. Adjust based on culture results.
  • Surgical drainage: If there is airway compromise or the abscess does not resolve with antibiotics alone, surgical drainage is indicated.
  • Airway management: In cases of impending airway obstruction, secure the airway early via intubation or tracheostomy.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Retropharyngeal Abscess"][/sc]
  • Mediastinitis: If the abscess extends inferiorly into the mediastinum, life-threatening mediastinitis can develop.
  • Sepsis: Systemic spread of the infection can lead to septic shock if not treated promptly.
  • Airway obstruction: Compression from the abscess can cause significant airway compromise, potentially resulting in respiratory failure and death if unrecognised.
  • Jugular vein thrombosis: Rarely, infection may spread to the jugular vein, causing thrombophlebitis (Lemierre’s syndrome).
