Obstructive Sleep Apnoea (OSA)
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Obstructive Sleep Apnoea (OSA)"][/sc]
  • Obstructive Sleep Apnoea (OSA) is a sleep-related breathing disorder characterised by recurrent episodes of complete or partial upper airway obstruction during sleep, leading to intermittent hypoxia and fragmented sleep. This results in excessive daytime sleepiness, impaired cognitive function, and cardiovascular complications.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Obstructive Sleep Apnoea (OSA)"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Obstructive Sleep Apnoea (OSA)"][/sc]
  • Prevalence in the UK GP population: Approximately 4% of middle-aged men and 2% of women are diagnosed with OSA.
  • UK hospital prevalence: OSA is more commonly diagnosed in hospital sleep clinics, especially in patients with obesity and cardiovascular risk factors.
  • Worldwide prevalence: Globally, OSA affects about 1 billion people, with a prevalence of approximately 9–38% depending on the population studied and diagnostic criteria used.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Obstructive Sleep Apnoea (OSA)"][/sc]
  • During sleep, the upper airway muscles relax, leading to a reduction in airway diameter. In individuals with OSA, this reduction is exaggerated due to anatomical factors (e.g., enlarged tonsils, obesity, or craniofacial abnormalities) or reduced neuromuscular control, causing airway collapse.
  • The airway obstruction results in reduced or absent airflow despite continued respiratory effort (apnoeas or hypopnoeas), leading to intermittent hypoxia, hypercapnia, and arousals from sleep.
  • These repeated events lead to sleep fragmentation, activation of the sympathetic nervous system, increased blood pressure, and systemic inflammation, contributing to long-term cardiovascular risks such as hypertension, atrial fibrillation, and stroke.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Obstructive Sleep Apnoea (OSA)"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Mild OSA often goes undiagnosed, with patients experiencing fatigue and poor concentration. Symptoms progress slowly over months or years.
  • Middle phase: Daytime sleepiness becomes more noticeable, and loud snoring is often reported by bed partners. Apnoeic episodes increase in frequency.
  • Late phase: If untreated, patients develop serious complications such as hypertension, heart failure, or stroke. Quality of life declines significantly due to chronic fatigue and impaired cognitive function.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Obstructive Sleep Apnoea (OSA)"][/sc]
  • Excessive daytime sleepiness - Due to fragmented sleep and frequent arousals during the night, leading to poor sleep quality.
  • Loud snoring - Caused by turbulent airflow through the narrowed airway during sleep.
  • Observed apnoeas (breathing pauses) - Often reported by bed partners, resulting from complete airway obstruction.
  • Morning headaches - Due to hypoxia and hypercapnia during apnoeic episodes, leading to vasodilation and headache.
  • Cognitive impairment - Poor concentration and memory problems arise from chronic sleep deprivation and intermittent hypoxia.
  • Nocturia - Increased frequency of urination at night is thought to be related to the effects of OSA on cardiovascular function and fluid regulation.
  • Dry mouth or sore throat upon waking - Caused by mouth breathing during sleep due to airway obstruction.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Obstructive Sleep Apnoea (OSA)"][/sc]
  • Obesity - Common in patients with OSA due to the mechanical effects of excess weight on the upper airway.
  • Hypertension - A consequence of chronic sympathetic nervous system activation during apnoeic episodes.
  • Retrognathia or crowded oropharynx - Craniofacial abnormalities may predispose individuals to airway collapse.
  • Neck circumference > 43 cm in men or > 40 cm in women - Increased neck fat deposits are associated with OSA.
  • Signs of right heart failure - Severe, long-term OSA can lead to pulmonary hypertension and right-sided heart failure.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Obstructive Sleep Apnoea (OSA)"][/sc]
  • Severe daytime sleepiness leading to accidents - Increases the risk of motor vehicle or occupational accidents due to impaired alertness.
  • Uncontrolled hypertension - Resistant to medical treatment, often indicates more severe OSA or cardiovascular complications.
  • Signs of heart failure - Suggests progression of OSA to cause serious cardiovascular complications such as right heart failure.
  • Frequent nocturnal gasping or choking - Indicative of severe apnoeas and desaturations, posing a risk of sudden cardiovascular events.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Obstructive Sleep Apnoea (OSA)"][/sc]
  • Insomnia without daytime sleepiness - Some patients may present with difficulty initiating or maintaining sleep without excessive sleepiness, especially in women and elderly individuals.
  • Depression or mood disorders - Mood changes may be more prominent than classical symptoms of sleepiness in some patients with OSA.
  • Silent apnoeas - Some patients, especially the elderly, may not have noticeable snoring or apnoeas but still experience significant sleep fragmentation and cardiovascular risk.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Obstructive Sleep Apnoea (OSA)"][/sc]
  • Polysomnography (PSG) - The gold standard test for diagnosing OSA. It measures apnoea-hypopnoea index (AHI), oxygen desaturation, and arousal events. An AHI ≥ 5 events/hour is diagnostic of OSA.
  • Home sleep apnoea testing (HSAT) - Less comprehensive than PSG but used in selected patients. Measures oxygen desaturation and airflow. Positive results correlate with moderate to severe OSA.
  • Oximetry - Overnight pulse oximetry may show intermittent drops in oxygen saturation (desaturations), often suggestive of OSA in high-risk patients.
  • Epworth Sleepiness Scale (ESS) - A subjective assessment of daytime sleepiness; scores ≥ 11 suggest excessive sleepiness and may warrant further investigation for OSA.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Obstructive Sleep Apnoea (OSA)"][/sc]
  • A 45-year-old obese male presents with loud snoring, witnessed apnoeas, excessive daytime sleepiness, and morning headaches for the past year. His wife reports frequent nocturnal gasping episodes.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Obstructive Sleep Apnoea (OSA)"][/sc]
  • Primary snoring - Differs from OSA as there are no apnoeic episodes or oxygen desaturations, and daytime sleepiness is absent.
  • Central sleep apnoea - Characterised by cessation of both airflow and respiratory effort, often associated with heart failure or opioid use, unlike OSA which has continued respiratory effort during apnoeas.
  • Chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD) - May present with similar symptoms such as fatigue and morning headaches, but pulmonary function tests would reveal obstructive lung disease and hypoxia would be more persistent.
  • Obesity hypoventilation syndrome - Characterised by chronic hypercapnia and hypoxia in obese patients, but without the intermittent apnoeic episodes seen in OSA.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Obstructive Sleep Apnoea (OSA)"][/sc]
  • Lifestyle modification - Weight loss, avoiding alcohol and sedatives before bedtime, and positional therapy to prevent supine sleeping.
  • Continuous positive airway pressure (CPAP) - The first-line treatment for moderate to severe OSA, providing positive pressure to keep the airway open during sleep.
  • Mandibular advancement devices (MAD) - Considered in mild to moderate OSA or patients intolerant to CPAP. These devices reposition the jaw to keep the airway open.
  • Surgery - Reserved for patients with structural abnormalities (e.g., tonsillar hypertrophy) or those failing conservative treatment. Procedures include uvulopalatopharyngoplasty (UPPP) or maxillomandibular advancement.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Obstructive Sleep Apnoea (OSA)"][/sc]
  • Cardiovascular disease - Increased risk of hypertension, heart failure, myocardial infarction, stroke, and atrial fibrillation due to the chronic sympathetic activation and intermittent hypoxia.
  • Diabetes mellitus - OSA is associated with insulin resistance and increased risk of type 2 diabetes.
  • Accidents - Due to excessive daytime sleepiness, patients are at higher risk of motor vehicle accidents and occupational injuries.
  • Depression and anxiety - Mood disorders are common due to chronic sleep deprivation and its effect on brain function.
