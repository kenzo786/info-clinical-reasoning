Pleural Effusion
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Pleural Effusion"][/sc]
  • Pleural effusion is the accumulation of excess fluid between the layers of the pleura, which are the thin membranes that cover the lungs and line the chest cavity. This fluid build-up can be due to various underlying conditions, and it can be classified as transudative or exudative based on the biochemical composition of the fluid.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Pleural Effusion"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Pleural Effusion"][/sc]
  • In the UK GP population, pleural effusion is relatively uncommon, with a prevalence of approximately 0.1%.
  • In UK hospital settings, pleural effusion is more frequently seen, especially in patients with cardiac, pulmonary, or neoplastic conditions, with an estimated prevalence of 4% among hospitalised patients.
  • Globally, pleural effusion affects around 320 per 100,000 people annually, with a higher prevalence in regions with endemic tuberculosis and poor access to healthcare.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Pleural Effusion"][/sc]
  • Pleural effusion develops when the balance between pleural fluid formation and absorption is disrupted. Causes include:
    • Increased hydrostatic pressure, such as in congestive heart failure, leads to transudative effusion.
    • Increased capillary permeability or inflammation, such as in infection or malignancy, leads to exudative effusion.
    • Decreased oncotic pressure, due to low serum albumin (e.g., in nephrotic syndrome), can also cause transudative effusions.
    • Blockage of lymphatic drainage due to malignancy or trauma can cause fluid accumulation.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Pleural Effusion"][/sc]
  • The time course of pleural effusion varies depending on the underlying cause:
    • Acute onset is seen in conditions like pulmonary embolism or pneumonia.
    • Chronic, slowly progressing effusion is common in malignancies or heart failure.
    • Recurrent effusions are often associated with malignancies or heart failure that is not well controlled.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Pleural Effusion"][/sc]
  • Dyspnoea (shortness of breath) – Caused by the compression of lung tissue due to fluid accumulation, limiting lung expansion.
  • Pleuritic chest pain – This sharp, localised pain worsens with inspiration and is due to irritation of the inflamed pleural surfaces.
  • Cough – Often dry and non-productive, the cough is caused by lung compression or irritation of the pleura.
  • Fatigue – General fatigue can result from underlying conditions such as malignancy or infection that cause the effusion.
  • Pleural effusion may be asymptomatic in small volumes or slowly developing cases.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Pleural Effusion"][/sc]
  • Dullness to percussion – The presence of fluid dampens the percussion note over the affected area.
  • Reduced or absent breath sounds – Fluid in the pleural space impairs sound transmission from the lungs.
  • Decreased tactile fremitus – The accumulation of fluid decreases the transmission of vibrations from the vocal cords to the chest wall.
  • Pleural rub – Inflammation of the pleura can produce a rubbing sound on auscultation in some cases.
  • Tracheal deviation (in large effusions) – Large volumes of fluid can push the trachea to the opposite side.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Pleural Effusion"][/sc]
  • Haemoptysis – Suggests underlying malignancy or pulmonary embolism.
  • Weight loss and night sweats – Can indicate tuberculosis or malignancy.
  • Sudden-onset severe dyspnoea – May suggest pulmonary embolism or large pleural effusion causing respiratory compromise.
  • Chest pain radiating to the back or arm – Can suggest thoracic malignancy or a complicated infectious process.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Pleural Effusion"][/sc]
  • Minimal or absent respiratory symptoms – In slow-developing effusions, such as those caused by heart failure, symptoms may be mild or absent.
  • Fever and chills – More common in parapneumonic effusions or empyema, which are bacterial infections of the pleural space.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Pleural Effusion"][/sc]
  • Chest X-ray – Fluid is seen as a homogenous opacity, often with a meniscus sign, in the lower zones.
  • Ultrasound – Confirms the presence of fluid and is used to guide thoracocentesis.
  • Diagnostic thoracocentesis – Analysis of pleural fluid for protein, LDH, glucose, cell count, cytology, and microbiology to distinguish between transudative and exudative effusion (Light's criteria).
  • CT scan – Helpful in identifying the underlying cause, especially in cases of malignancy or suspected pulmonary embolism.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Pleural Effusion"][/sc]
  • A 65-year-old male presents with progressive dyspnoea over two weeks, pleuritic chest pain, and a non-productive cough. His history includes congestive heart failure, and he is on diuretics.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Pleural Effusion"][/sc]
  • Congestive Heart Failure – Often causes bilateral pleural effusions. Typically associated with lower extremity oedema, raised JVP, and cardiomegaly on imaging.
  • Pulmonary Embolism – May present with sudden-onset dyspnoea and pleuritic pain. Effusion tends to be small, often associated with haemoptysis and hypoxia.
  • Pneumonia – Can cause parapneumonic effusion or empyema. Features include fever, productive cough, and infiltrates on chest X-ray.
  • Malignancy – Typically presents with a unilateral effusion, weight loss, and systemic symptoms. Fluid may be haemorrhagic, and cytology may reveal malignant cells.
  • Cirrhosis – Causes hepatic hydrothorax, often right-sided. Ascites and peripheral oedema are commonly present.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Pleural Effusion"][/sc]
  • Treat underlying cause – Manage heart failure, pneumonia, or malignancy appropriately.
  • Therapeutic thoracocentesis – Indicated for symptomatic relief, especially in large effusions causing dyspnoea.
  • Diuretics – Used in heart failure to reduce fluid overload.
  • Chest drain – May be needed for recurrent or complicated effusions, such as empyema.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Pleural Effusion"][/sc]
  • Empyema – Infection of the pleural fluid, often requiring prolonged antibiotics and drainage.
  • Fibrosis and trapped lung – Chronic effusion or infection can lead to pleural thickening, restricting lung expansion.
  • Respiratory failure – In large or rapidly accumulating effusions, respiratory compromise can occur, necessitating urgent intervention.
