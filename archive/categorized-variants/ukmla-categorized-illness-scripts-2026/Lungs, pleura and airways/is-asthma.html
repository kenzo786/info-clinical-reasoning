Asthma
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Asthma"][/sc]
  • Asthma is a chronic inflammatory disease of the airways characterised by variable airflow obstruction and hyperresponsiveness, often triggered by allergens, irritants, exercise, or infections. Symptoms are episodic, including wheezing, breathlessness, chest tightness, and cough. It can range from mild to severe and is reversible, either spontaneously or with treatment.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Asthma"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Asthma"][/sc]
  • Prevalence in UK GP population: Approximately 12% of adults and 20% of children are diagnosed with asthma.
  • UK hospital prevalence: Asthma accounts for around 60,000 hospital admissions annually in the UK.
  • Worldwide prevalence: Around 300 million people are affected globally, with prevalence varying between 1-18% depending on the country.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Asthma"][/sc]
  • Asthma pathophysiology involves chronic inflammation of the airways. Exposure to triggers such as allergens, irritants, or infections leads to the activation of immune cells (e.g., mast cells, eosinophils), causing release of inflammatory mediators like histamines and leukotrienes.
  • These mediators cause bronchoconstriction (airway narrowing), increased mucus production, and airway oedema, resulting in obstruction. The hyperresponsiveness of the airways also leads to further bronchoconstriction upon exposure to stimuli.
  • Repeated episodes can lead to airway remodelling, with thickening of the airway walls and permanent obstruction over time.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Asthma"][/sc]
  • Intermittent episodes: Often short-term exacerbations of asthma triggered by allergens, infections, or exercise.
  • Chronic phase: Over months to years, patients may develop persistent symptoms with less clear triggers, though symptoms can still fluctuate in severity.
  • Acute exacerbations: These are periods of increased severity, with sudden onset of intense symptoms, often requiring emergency treatment.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Asthma"][/sc]
  • Wheezing: Caused by narrowed airways due to bronchoconstriction and mucus plugging, leading to turbulent airflow.
  • Shortness of breath (dyspnoea): Bronchoconstriction and inflammation result in decreased airflow, increasing the work of breathing.
  • Cough: Often worse at night or early morning due to airway hyperresponsiveness and mucus production.
  • Chest tightness: Likely related to bronchospasm and the struggle to overcome resistance to airflow.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Asthma"][/sc]
  • Wheezing on auscultation: A high-pitched, musical sound heard during expiration due to turbulent airflow through constricted airways.
  • Prolonged expiration: Air trapping occurs due to obstruction, prolonging the expiratory phase of breathing.
  • Accessory muscle use: Increased effort to breathe leads to the use of neck and shoulder muscles during breathing, especially in severe cases.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Asthma"][/sc]
  • Silent chest: The absence of wheezing in a patient with severe asthma can indicate life-threatening airflow obstruction.
  • Cyanosis: Bluish discolouration of lips or skin due to hypoxaemia, suggesting impending respiratory failure.
  • Confusion or drowsiness: Signs of hypercapnia (elevated CO₂ levels) and respiratory fatigue, which may require immediate ventilatory support.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Asthma"][/sc]
  • Cough-variant asthma: Chronic cough as the only symptom, without typical wheezing or shortness of breath.
  • Exercise-induced asthma: Wheezing and breathlessness triggered primarily by physical activity, without symptoms at rest.
  • Occupational asthma: Symptoms related to workplace exposures, improving when away from work.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Asthma"][/sc]
  • Spirometry: Demonstrates an obstructive pattern (FEV1/FVC ratio < 0.7), with reversible airflow obstruction after bronchodilator administration.
  • Peak flow variability: Peak expiratory flow (PEF) monitoring shows variability of >20% over time, supporting the diagnosis.
  • FeNO (fractional exhaled nitric oxide): Elevated levels (>40 ppb) indicate eosinophilic inflammation in the airways.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Asthma"][/sc]
  • A 22-year-old woman presents with intermittent episodes of wheezing, shortness of breath, and chest tightness, often triggered by dust exposure or exercise. Symptoms have worsened over the past 6 months, particularly at night.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Asthma"][/sc]
  • Chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD): Typically occurs in older patients with a smoking history, with less variability in symptoms and less reversibility on spirometry.
  • Vocal cord dysfunction: Presents with wheezing and shortness of breath, but spirometry is often normal, and symptoms are more related to upper airway obstruction.
  • Heart failure: Dyspnoea, orthopnoea, and fluid overload signs are more prominent, with abnormal chest radiographs (pulmonary oedema) and raised BNP levels.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Asthma"][/sc]
  • Stepwise approach: Following the NICE guidelines, starting with short-acting beta-agonists (SABAs), progressing to inhaled corticosteroids (ICS) and long-acting beta-agonists (LABAs) as needed.
  • Acute exacerbations: Use of nebulised bronchodilators (salbutamol, ipratropium), oral or IV corticosteroids, and oxygen therapy in severe cases.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Asthma"][/sc]
  • Respiratory failure: Severe exacerbations may lead to hypoxaemia and hypercapnia, requiring ventilatory support.
  • Pneumothorax: Rare but possible due to air trapping and overinflation of alveoli.
  • Airway remodelling: Long-term, untreated asthma can result in permanent structural changes in the airways, leading to fixed airflow limitation.
