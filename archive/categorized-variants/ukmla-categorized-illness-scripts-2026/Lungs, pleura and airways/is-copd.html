COPD
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="COPD"][/sc]
  • Chronic Obstructive Pulmonary Disease (COPD) is a progressive lung disease characterised by persistent airflow limitation due to small airway disease (chronic bronchitis) and alveolar destruction (emphysema). COPD is largely caused by smoking and environmental pollutants and is typically irreversible.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="COPD"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="COPD"][/sc]
  • In the UK, approximately 1.2 million people are diagnosed with COPD, making up around 2% of the population and 4.5% of the GP population.
  • COPD accounts for around 10% of hospital admissions related to respiratory illness in the UK.
  • Globally, COPD affects more than 200 million people and is the third leading cause of death worldwide.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="COPD"][/sc]
  • Chronic exposure to noxious particles (e.g., cigarette smoke) leads to inflammation in the lungs.
  • This causes narrowing of the small airways (chronic bronchitis) and destruction of alveolar walls (emphysema).
  • The airflow obstruction results from both airway narrowing and loss of elastic recoil, making it difficult to exhale fully.
  • There is also impaired gas exchange, leading to chronic hypoxia and hypercapnia over time.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="COPD"][/sc]
  • COPD is a chronic condition that develops gradually over years, often following prolonged exposure to irritants such as cigarette smoke.
  • Early stages may be asymptomatic, progressing to chronic cough and breathlessness. Exacerbations (acute worsening) may occur due to infections or other triggers.
  • Late stages are marked by persistent breathlessness, frequent exacerbations, and respiratory failure.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="COPD"][/sc]
  • Chronic cough - Often the first symptom, caused by inflammation of the airways leading to mucus hypersecretion.
  • Sputum production - Excess mucus production due to chronic inflammation and airway irritation.
  • Dyspnoea (breathlessness) - The most prominent symptom, initially during exertion and eventually at rest, caused by airflow obstruction and reduced lung compliance.
  • Wheezing - Due to narrowed airways and obstruction.
  • Fatigue - Resulting from the increased work of breathing and reduced oxygen delivery to tissues.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="COPD"][/sc]
  • Prolonged expiratory phase - Due to airflow obstruction and difficulty in exhaling fully.
  • Wheezing - Audible on auscultation, due to narrowed airways.
  • Barrel chest - Due to hyperinflation of the lungs, increasing the anteroposterior diameter of the chest.
  • Pursed-lip breathing - A compensatory mechanism to increase airway pressure and prevent airway collapse during expiration.
  • Use of accessory muscles - Indicates increased work of breathing as lung function declines.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="COPD"][/sc]
  • Cyanosis - Indicates hypoxaemia and suggests advanced disease or acute exacerbation.
  • Peripheral oedema - Suggests cor pulmonale, a complication of COPD where chronic hypoxia leads to right-sided heart failure.
  • Altered mental state - Can signify hypercapnia, especially in severe exacerbations.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="COPD"][/sc]
  • Minimal cough or sputum - Some patients may not present with a chronic productive cough but primarily with dyspnoea.
  • Isolated weight loss - Severe emphysema can lead to cachexia without significant respiratory symptoms.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="COPD"][/sc]
  • Spirometry - The hallmark diagnostic test. FEV1/FVC ratio <70% confirms airflow limitation. FEV1 is often reduced, indicating severity.
  • Chest X-ray - May show hyperinflation, flattened diaphragm, and reduced vascular markings in emphysema.
  • Arterial Blood Gas (ABG) - Important in acute exacerbations. It may reveal hypoxia and hypercapnia.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="COPD"][/sc]
  • A 65-year-old male smoker with a 40-pack-year history presents with chronic productive cough, worsening dyspnoea, and occasional wheezing. He reports frequent exacerbations requiring antibiotics.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="COPD"][/sc]
  • Asthma - Reversible airflow obstruction, often with childhood onset, significant diurnal variation, and a history of atopy.
  • Bronchiectasis - Chronic productive cough with purulent sputum and recurrent infections. Often associated with a history of previous lung infections.
  • Congestive Heart Failure (CHF) - Dyspnoea and oedema, but CXR may show pulmonary congestion, and echocardiogram reveals impaired cardiac function.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="COPD"][/sc]
  • Smoking cessation - The single most effective intervention to slow disease progression.
  • Bronchodilators - Short-acting (e.g., salbutamol) or long-acting (e.g., tiotropium) to relieve airflow obstruction.
  • Inhaled corticosteroids - For patients with frequent exacerbations.
  • Oxygen therapy - Long-term oxygen therapy for patients with chronic hypoxia (PaO2 <7.3 kPa).
  • Pulmonary rehabilitation - Exercise and education programmes to improve quality of life.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="COPD"][/sc]
  • Cor pulmonale - Right heart failure due to chronic pulmonary hypertension from hypoxia.
  • Pneumothorax - Emphysematous bullae can rupture, leading to lung collapse.
  • Respiratory failure - Due to progressive hypoxia and hypercapnia, particularly in end-stage disease.
