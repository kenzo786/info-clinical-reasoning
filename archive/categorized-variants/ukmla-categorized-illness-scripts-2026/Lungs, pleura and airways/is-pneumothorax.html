Pneumothorax
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Pneumothorax"][/sc]
  • Pneumothorax is the presence of air in the pleural space, which leads to partial or complete collapse of the lung. This can be spontaneous (primary or secondary) or result from trauma, medical procedures, or lung disease. The condition disrupts the negative pressure that normally keeps the lung inflated.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Pneumothorax"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Pneumothorax"][/sc]
  • Risk Factors: Tall, thin males aged 10-30, smoking (up to 22× risk increase), family history, and subpleural blebs contribute to primary spontaneous pneumothorax. Secondary causes include COPD, cystic fibrosis, tuberculosis, lung malignancy, and connective tissue disorders. Trauma, mechanical ventilation, and invasive procedures can also induce pneumothorax.
  • Prevention: Smoking cessation significantly reduces risk. Optimising lung disease management lowers secondary pneumothorax incidence. Patients with recurrent episodes may require pleurodesis or surgical intervention. Recent pneumothorax patients should avoid high-altitude travel, air travel, and scuba diving.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Pneumothorax"][/sc]
  • Pneumothorax occurs when air enters the pleural space, either due to a breach in the visceral pleura (lining the lung) or the parietal pleura (lining the chest wall). The negative pressure that usually keeps the lungs expanded is lost, leading to partial or complete lung collapse. In primary pneumothorax, this is typically due to the rupture of small subpleural blebs, while in secondary pneumothorax, it is often related to underlying lung diseases like COPD, where the structural integrity of the lung is compromised.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Pneumothorax"][/sc]
  • Acute onset: Symptoms typically develop suddenly, often over minutes to hours.
  • Untreated course: If untreated, a pneumothorax can persist for days or even weeks, depending on the size and cause. Small pneumothoraces may resolve on their own, but larger ones can progress to life-threatening complications like tension pneumothorax.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Pneumothorax"][/sc]
  • Sudden onset of pleuritic chest pain: Caused by the stretching of the pleura as the lung collapses.
  • Acute dyspnoea: Due to reduced lung volume and impaired ventilation.
  • Cough: A non-productive, dry cough may occur due to irritation of the pleura.
  • Asymptomatic: Especially in small pneumothoraces, symptoms may be minimal or absent.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Pneumothorax"][/sc]
  • Reduced breath sounds on the affected side: Due to lack of air movement in the collapsed lung.
  • Hyper-resonance on percussion: Caused by the presence of air in the pleural space.
  • Decreased chest expansion: Especially on the side of the pneumothorax.
  • Tachypnoea: Increased respiratory rate as compensation for hypoxia.
  • Tracheal deviation (in tension pneumothorax): A late sign indicating increased intrathoracic pressure on one side, shifting the mediastinum.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Pneumothorax"][/sc]
  • Tracheal deviation away from the affected side: Indicates tension pneumothorax, a medical emergency that can cause cardiovascular collapse due to compression of the great vessels.
  • Hypotension: Suggests decreased venous return and possible tension pneumothorax.
  • Distended neck veins: A sign of elevated intrathoracic pressure in tension pneumothorax, impairing venous return to the heart.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Pneumothorax"][/sc]
  • Mild or no symptoms in small pneumothoraces: Particularly in young, healthy individuals, small pneumothoraces may not cause noticeable symptoms.
  • Misinterpreted as musculoskeletal pain: Pleuritic chest pain may be mistaken for muscle strain or costochondritis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Pneumothorax"][/sc]
  • Chest X-ray: Shows a visible rim of air with the absence of lung markings in the area of pneumothorax. The lung edge may be visible as a sharp white line. In tension pneumothorax, mediastinal shift and diaphragm depression may be seen.
  • CT scan: More sensitive for small or complex pneumothoraces, especially in secondary causes or trauma.
  • Arterial blood gases (ABG): May show hypoxaemia and respiratory alkalosis in larger pneumothoraces or those causing significant respiratory compromise.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Pneumothorax"][/sc]
  • A 25-year-old tall, thin male presents with sudden onset sharp left-sided chest pain and shortness of breath while at rest. There is no history of trauma or pre-existing lung disease.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Pneumothorax"][/sc]
  • Pulmonary embolism: More likely to present with pleuritic pain, haemoptysis, and signs of deep vein thrombosis. A CTPA would show a filling defect in the pulmonary artery.
  • Pleurisy: Typically associated with a viral infection, producing pleuritic pain without a significant history of lung collapse. A chest X-ray may be normal.
  • Myocardial infarction: Usually presents with central chest pain and may have ECG changes, troponin elevation, and a non-clear chest X-ray.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Pneumothorax"][/sc]
  • Observation: Small primary pneumothoraces (<2 cm) in asymptomatic patients may be managed conservatively with observation and follow-up chest X-rays.
  • Needle aspiration: First-line for symptomatic primary pneumothoraces or those >2 cm. If successful, the patient can be discharged with follow-up.
  • Chest drain insertion: Indicated for large pneumothoraces, secondary pneumothoraces, or if needle aspiration fails.
  • Oxygen therapy: Can help reabsorb air from the pleural space more quickly, particularly in patients with hypoxaemia.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Pneumothorax"][/sc]
  • Tension pneumothorax: Life-threatening complication due to increased intrathoracic pressure, causing cardiovascular collapse. Requires emergency decompression.
  • Recurrent pneumothorax: Up to 30% of patients may experience recurrence, particularly if no preventive treatment like pleurodesis is performed.
  • Respiratory failure: In secondary pneumothorax, patients with pre-existing lung disease may experience significant hypoxia and respiratory compromise.
