Infective Endocarditis
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Infective Endocarditis"][/sc]
  • Infective endocarditis (IE) is an infection of the endocardial surface of the heart, typically involving the heart valves. It is characterised by the formation of vegetations composed of microorganisms, fibrin, and inflammatory cells. The condition can cause valve destruction, systemic emboli, and severe systemic complications.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Infective Endocarditis"][/sc]
  • Prevalence (UK GP population): Approximately 1 in 20,000 per year.
  • Hospital prevalence (UK): 3-10 cases per 100,000 per year, increasing in at-risk populations.
  • Global prevalence: Approximately 3-10 cases per 100,000 per year, with higher incidence in populations with prosthetic valves, congenital heart disease, and history of intravenous drug use.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Infective Endocarditis"][/sc]
  • IE develops when microorganisms (often bacteria) enter the bloodstream and adhere to the endocardium, particularly at sites of endothelial injury on heart valves. Damaged endothelium allows fibrin and platelets to deposit, creating a surface for bacterial colonisation.
  • The vegetations formed on heart valves release cytokines and inflammatory cells, causing local destruction and dysfunction of the valve.
  • Embolic phenomena result from fragmentation of the vegetations, potentially leading to embolisation to the brain, kidneys, and lungs.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Infective Endocarditis"][/sc]
  • Acute IE: Develops within days to weeks, commonly from highly virulent organisms like Staphylococcus aureus, with rapid onset of symptoms and complications.
  • Subacute IE: Progresses over weeks to months, often caused by less virulent organisms like Streptococcus viridans, with insidious symptoms and lower-grade fever.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Infective Endocarditis"][/sc]
  • Fever - Most common symptom, resulting from the systemic inflammatory response to infection.
  • Fatigue - General malaise due to systemic infection and inflammatory burden.
  • Chills and night sweats - Intermittent bacteraemia causes periodic fevers and sweating.
  • Weight loss - Chronic infection can lead to catabolic states and weight loss.
  • Dyspnoea - May indicate heart failure from valve dysfunction or embolic pulmonary events.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Infective Endocarditis"][/sc]
  • New or changing heart murmur - Indicates valve involvement and damage.
  • Janeway lesions - Non-tender, erythematous macules on palms/soles, representing microemboli.
  • Osler nodes - Painful, erythematous nodules on fingers/toes due to immune complex deposition.
  • Splinter haemorrhages - Linear haemorrhages under nails, related to small vessel emboli.
  • Roth spots - Retinal haemorrhages with pale centres, due to immune complex vasculitis.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Infective Endocarditis"][/sc]
  • Neurological deficits - Suggests embolisation to the brain, a severe complication with risk of stroke.
  • Heart failure symptoms - Indicates valvular dysfunction; may lead to cardiogenic shock if untreated.
  • Septic emboli signs (lung, spleen, kidneys) - Embolic spread can cause multi-organ dysfunction.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Infective Endocarditis"][/sc]
  • Low-grade fever with non-specific symptoms - May be misdiagnosed as a flu-like illness, particularly in subacute cases.
  • Confusion or altered mental status - Can occur due to septic emboli or toxic encephalopathy in elderly patients.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Infective Endocarditis"][/sc]
  • Blood cultures - Multiple cultures to identify causative organism; commonly shows Staphylococcus, Streptococcus, or Enterococcus species.
  • Echocardiography (TTE/TOE) - To visualise vegetations and assess valvular damage; TOE is more sensitive.
  • Full blood count - Leukocytosis common; anaemia of chronic disease may be present.
  • ESR/CRP - Elevated markers indicate systemic inflammation.
  • Urinalysis - May show microscopic haematuria or proteinuria from embolic or immune complex deposition.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Infective Endocarditis"][/sc]
  • A 60-year-old male presents with a 3-week history of fever, malaise, weight loss, and new heart murmur. Echocardiogram reveals vegetations on the mitral valve.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Infective Endocarditis"][/sc]
  • Non-infective endocarditis - No pathogen on cultures; often associated with malignancy or autoimmune conditions.
  • Rheumatic fever - Fever, joint pain, and cardiac involvement post-streptococcal infection.
  • Vasculitis - Can present with multi-organ involvement and fever; autoantibodies often positive.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Infective Endocarditis"][/sc]
  • Empirical antibiotic therapy - Broad-spectrum (e.g., vancomycin and gentamicin) until cultures identify organism.
  • Definitive therapy - Tailored antibiotics based on sensitivity, typically for 4-6 weeks.
  • Surgical intervention - Required for severe valvular dysfunction, abscess formation, or resistant organisms.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Infective Endocarditis"][/sc]
  • Heart failure - Common due to valve destruction, leading to poor prognosis if untreated.
  • Systemic embolisation - Emboli to brain, kidneys, or lungs; can cause ischaemic stroke, infarcts.
  • Perivalvular abscess - Requires surgical drainage and prolonged antibiotic therapy; increases mortality risk.
