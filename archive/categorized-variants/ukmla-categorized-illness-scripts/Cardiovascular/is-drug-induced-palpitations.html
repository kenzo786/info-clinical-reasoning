Drug-Induced Palpitations
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Drug-Induced Palpitations"][/sc]
  • Drug-induced palpitations are a subjective sensation of rapid, irregular, or forceful heartbeats triggered by medication or substance use. Commonly implicated substances include stimulants (e.g., caffeine, amphetamines), certain prescription drugs (e.g., beta-agonists, anticholinergics), and recreational drugs (e.g., cocaine). The condition results from alterations in cardiac conduction or increased sympathetic activity due to these agents.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Drug-Induced Palpitations"][/sc]
  • UK GP Population: Palpitations account for 16% of GP consultations related to heart symptoms, with drug-induced cases making up a smaller proportion (5-10%).
  • UK Hospital Prevalence: Approximately 25% of hospitalised patients with palpitations have a drug-related cause, particularly in emergency or cardiology settings.
  • Worldwide Prevalence: Varies by region and substance use patterns. Drug-induced palpitations are more prevalent in countries with higher rates of stimulant use or prescription drug misuse.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Drug-Induced Palpitations"][/sc]
  • Drug-induced palpitations occur due to disruption in the autonomic regulation of heart rate or direct effects on the heart's electrical conduction system. Stimulants like caffeine, amphetamines, and cocaine enhance sympathetic nervous system activity, causing increased heart rate (tachycardia) and heightened myocardial excitability. Medications such as beta-agonists and thyroid hormone replacements can increase cardiac contractility and excitability, while anticholinergics reduce vagal tone, promoting arrhythmias.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Drug-Induced Palpitations"][/sc]
  • Drug-induced palpitations often occur within minutes to hours of drug consumption. Depending on the agent, palpitations may be acute and transient or prolonged with repetitive exposure (chronic stimulant use). Symptoms usually resolve when the drug is metabolised or discontinued.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Drug-Induced Palpitations"][/sc]
  • Palpitations - Patients feel a fluttering, pounding, or racing sensation in the chest. This is due to increased sympathetic tone or arrhythmia caused by the drug's impact on cardiac conduction.
  • Chest discomfort - Some patients report tightness or pain due to increased myocardial oxygen demand during tachycardia.
  • Anxiety or restlessness - Common with stimulant drugs, related to excess sympathetic activation.
  • Dizziness or light-headedness - Results from reduced cardiac output during arrhythmic episodes.
  • Shortness of breath - Occurs when cardiac output is compromised or there is increased oxygen demand.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Drug-Induced Palpitations"][/sc]
  • Tachycardia - Elevated heart rate due to increased sympathetic activity or direct cardiac effects of the drug.
  • Irregular heartbeat - May be observed on palpation of the pulse or ECG, indicating arrhythmia caused by drug effects on cardiac ion channels.
  • Hypertension - Common with stimulant drugs, due to sympathetic nervous system stimulation.
  • Mydriasis (pupil dilation) - Associated with sympathetic overdrive, especially in stimulant or anticholinergic drug use.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Drug-Induced Palpitations"][/sc]
  • Syncope or near-syncope - May suggest life-threatening arrhythmia (e.g., ventricular tachycardia) or severe hypotension.
  • Severe chest pain - Can indicate myocardial ischaemia due to increased cardiac workload or coronary vasospasm from stimulants.
  • Severe shortness of breath - May indicate heart failure or pulmonary oedema secondary to tachyarrhythmia or prolonged drug exposure.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Drug-Induced Palpitations"][/sc]
  • Asymptomatic or mild palpitations - Some individuals, particularly those with a high baseline heart rate, may not perceive palpitations despite significant arrhythmias. This occurs due to tolerance to the drugâ€™s effects over time.
  • Fatigue and malaise - Especially in chronic stimulant use or beta-agonist overuse, where sympathetic stimulation can lead to exhaustion over time.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Drug-Induced Palpitations"][/sc]
  • ECG (Electrocardiogram) - Often shows sinus tachycardia or arrhythmias such as atrial fibrillation or ventricular ectopy. Pathophysiology: Drug effects on ion channels (e.g., sodium or potassium channels) alter cardiac electrical conduction.
  • Blood tests - Serum electrolytes to rule out electrolyte disturbances, and toxicology screening to detect causative drugs (e.g., amphetamines, cocaine).
  • Holter monitoring - Continuous ECG monitoring for patients with intermittent palpitations. Detects episodic arrhythmias or tachycardia.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Drug-Induced Palpitations"][/sc]
  • A 32-year-old male presents with sudden-onset palpitations, anxiety, and dizziness 1 hour after consuming an energy drink and a decongestant. His ECG shows sinus tachycardia.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Drug-Induced Palpitations"][/sc]
  • Anxiety disorder - Anxiety-related palpitations are typically associated with hyperventilation or panic attacks and lack a clear temporal relationship with drug intake.
  • Atrial fibrillation - Can present similarly but is often chronic or paroxysmal, and may have underlying structural heart disease or be age-related.
  • Hyperthyroidism - Palpitations in hyperthyroidism are persistent, associated with other symptoms (e.g., weight loss, heat intolerance), and confirmed by elevated thyroid hormone levels.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Drug-Induced Palpitations"][/sc]
  • Discontinuation of the causative agent - Immediate cessation of the drug or substance causing palpitations.
  • Beta-blockers (e.g., propranolol) - Used to reduce sympathetic activity in cases of stimulant-induced tachycardia or anxiety-related palpitations.
  • IV fluids - To manage dehydration, especially in patients with stimulant use, which can exacerbate palpitations.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Drug-Induced Palpitations"][/sc]
  • Cardiac arrhythmias - Prolonged stimulant or drug use can lead to atrial or ventricular arrhythmias.
  • Myocardial ischaemia - Increased myocardial oxygen demand due to tachycardia may precipitate angina or myocardial infarction.
  • Heart failure - Chronic arrhythmias can impair cardiac function, leading to heart failure in severe cases.
