Otitis Media
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Otitis Media"][/sc]
  • Otitis Media is an infection or inflammation of the middle ear, typically occurring due to dysfunction of the Eustachian tube, leading to fluid accumulation behind the tympanic membrane. The two main subtypes are acute otitis media (AOM), which is characterised by a rapid onset of symptoms such as pain and fever, and otitis media with effusion (OME), which involves fluid in the middle ear without signs of acute infection.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Otitis Media"][/sc]
  • Otitis media is one of the most common childhood infections worldwide. In the UK, approximately 20% of children under the age of 5 experience at least one episode annually. Hospitalisation rates for severe cases of AOM are low, but recurrent infections can lead to significant morbidity. Globally, otitis media affects an estimated 700 million people annually, with a higher burden in low-income countries due to limited healthcare access.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Otitis Media"][/sc]
  • Otitis media usually begins with Eustachian tube dysfunction, often secondary to upper respiratory infections or allergic rhinitis. This dysfunction leads to negative pressure in the middle ear, pulling fluid into the space behind the tympanic membrane. In cases of AOM, this fluid becomes infected with bacteria or viruses (most commonly Streptococcus pneumoniae and Haemophilus influenzae), causing inflammation, pain, and sometimes fever. In OME, the fluid persists but is non-infectious, resulting in hearing loss or a sense of fullness in the ear without acute symptoms.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Otitis Media"][/sc]
  • AOM typically develops within 1-3 days following the onset of upper respiratory tract symptoms and resolves within 7-10 days with appropriate treatment. Otitis media with effusion may persist for weeks to months, with fluid in the ear often taking longer to clear even after the acute infection has resolved.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Otitis Media"][/sc]
  • Ear pain (otalgia) - Due to inflammation of the tympanic membrane and increased pressure in the middle ear cavity.
  • Fever - A common response to bacterial infection, typically present in AOM.
  • Hearing loss - Results from fluid accumulation in the middle ear, blocking sound transmission, especially in OME.
  • Ear fullness - A sensation of pressure due to fluid in the middle ear, more commonly associated with OME.
  • Otorrhoea - Discharge from the ear can occur if the tympanic membrane ruptures, releasing fluid.
  • Asymptomatic - In OME, some children may not exhibit noticeable symptoms, but parents may report hearing difficulties.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Otitis Media"][/sc]
  • Bulging tympanic membrane - Seen on otoscopy, indicating fluid accumulation behind the eardrum.
  • Redness of the tympanic membrane - Suggests inflammation, often present in AOM.
  • Reduced mobility of the tympanic membrane - Detected using pneumatic otoscopy; immobility indicates fluid behind the eardrum.
  • Air-fluid levels behind the tympanic membrane - Visible on otoscopy, particularly in OME.
  • Tympanic membrane perforation - May occur in severe AOM, leading to purulent discharge from the ear.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Otitis Media"][/sc]
  • Severe headache - May indicate complications such as mastoiditis or intracranial spread of infection.
  • Swelling or tenderness behind the ear - Suggests mastoiditis, which can lead to abscess formation and further complications.
  • Neurological symptoms (e.g. facial paralysis) - Can be a sign of a more serious complication like a cholesteatoma or intracranial infection.
  • Persistent high fever - Raises concern for systemic spread or inadequate treatment response.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Otitis Media"][/sc]
  • Asymptomatic otitis media with effusion - Children may present with subtle hearing loss or speech delays rather than pain or fever.
  • Recurrent AOM - Some children may have frequent episodes of AOM without typical signs of infection, requiring audiological evaluation.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Otitis Media"][/sc]
  • Otoscopy - Key diagnostic tool. Findings include bulging, redness, or immobility of the tympanic membrane, suggesting infection or effusion.
  • Tympanometry - Assesses the mobility of the tympanic membrane and can confirm the presence of middle ear fluid.
  • Hearing tests - Audiometry may be required for children with OME to assess hearing loss and plan management.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Otitis Media"][/sc]
  • A 2-year-old child presents with a 2-day history of ear pain, fever, and irritability following a recent upper respiratory infection. Otoscopy reveals a bulging, erythematous tympanic membrane.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Otitis Media"][/sc]
  • Otitis Externa - Distinguished by tenderness of the external ear and absence of middle ear effusion on otoscopy.
  • Sinusitis - Can present with facial pain and fever but typically lacks ear-specific findings like a bulging tympanic membrane.
  • Temporomandibular joint (TMJ) dysfunction - Causes ear pain without signs of infection or fluid on otoscopy.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Otitis Media"][/sc]
  • First-line - For AOM, NICE guidelines recommend analgesia (paracetamol or ibuprofen) and a wait-and-see approach for 48-72 hours. Antibiotics (e.g., amoxicillin) are prescribed if symptoms persist or worsen.
  • OME - Usually managed conservatively, but persistent cases may require hearing tests or grommet insertion.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Otitis Media"][/sc]
  • Mastoiditis - Infection can spread to the mastoid bone, leading to abscess formation. Requires prompt treatment with intravenous antibiotics or surgery.
  • Hearing loss - Chronic OME can lead to conductive hearing loss, which may impact language development in children.
  • Intracranial complications - Rare but serious, including meningitis or brain abscess.
