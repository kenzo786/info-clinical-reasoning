Benign Paroxysmal Positional Vertigo (BPPV)
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Benign Paroxysmal Positional Vertigo (BPPV)"][/sc]
  • BPPV is a common vestibular disorder caused by displaced otoliths (calcium carbonate crystals) within the semicircular canals, leading to transient episodes of vertigo. It is triggered by changes in head position relative to gravity and typically lasts less than a minute.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Benign Paroxysmal Positional Vertigo (BPPV)"][/sc]
  • UK GP prevalence: BPPV is diagnosed in 1 in 1,000 consultations.
  • UK hospital prevalence: It is the cause of approximately 20% of all vestibular disorders.
  • Worldwide prevalence: 2.4% of the general population will experience BPPV in their lifetime, with recurrence rates as high as 50% within five years.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Benign Paroxysmal Positional Vertigo (BPPV)"][/sc]
  • Normally, the otoliths are located in the utricle and saccule of the vestibular system, which help detect linear acceleration. In BPPV, these otoliths become dislodged and enter the semicircular canals, most commonly the posterior canal.
  • When the head moves, these free-floating particles cause abnormal stimulation of the hair cells in the semicircular canals, leading to the perception of vertigo.
  • The posterior canal is the most frequently affected due to its anatomical position, making it more prone to trap the dislodged otoliths.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Benign Paroxysmal Positional Vertigo (BPPV)"][/sc]
  • Acute phase: Episodes of vertigo typically last less than 60 seconds, triggered by head movements such as rolling over in bed, looking up, or bending down.
  • Recurrent phase: Symptoms tend to recur intermittently, often with prolonged symptom-free intervals between episodes.
  • Resolution phase: Symptoms may resolve spontaneously over days to weeks, though in some cases, they persist and require treatment.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Benign Paroxysmal Positional Vertigo (BPPV)"][/sc]
  • Vertigo - Sudden onset of spinning sensation when moving the head, particularly with positional changes like turning over in bed or looking up. This is due to abnormal endolymphatic flow in the semicircular canals.
  • Nausea - Often accompanies vertigo due to vestibular stimulation of the vomiting centre.
  • Imbalance - Mild disequilibrium may persist between episodes of vertigo because of the vestibular system's role in maintaining balance.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Benign Paroxysmal Positional Vertigo (BPPV)"][/sc]
  • Positional Nystagmus - Characteristic torsional nystagmus triggered by specific head movements during the Dix-Hallpike manoeuvre. This occurs due to the movement of otoliths within the affected canal.
  • No neurological deficits - Absence of focal neurological signs helps differentiate BPPV from central causes of vertigo.
  • Normal ear examination - There are no abnormal findings on otoscopic or external ear examination in BPPV.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Benign Paroxysmal Positional Vertigo (BPPV)"][/sc]
  • Persistent vertigo lasting hours or days - May indicate a central cause such as a vestibular migraine or stroke.
  • Focal neurological signs (e.g., limb weakness, dysarthria) - Suggests a central lesion, such as a cerebellar stroke, rather than peripheral causes like BPPV.
  • Sudden hearing loss or tinnitus - May signify Meniere's disease or a vestibular schwannoma, both of which require further investigation.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Benign Paroxysmal Positional Vertigo (BPPV)"][/sc]
  • Vertigo without head movement - Some patients report vertigo without clear triggers, leading to a delay in diagnosis.
  • Prolonged episodes - While BPPV episodes typically last seconds, prolonged symptoms may lead to confusion with other vestibular disorders.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Benign Paroxysmal Positional Vertigo (BPPV)"][/sc]
  • Dix-Hallpike manoeuvre - Positive if torsional nystagmus is observed after rapid head movement, which confirms posterior canal BPPV. The nystagmus is delayed and fatigues upon repetition.
  • Supine roll test - This is used to diagnose horizontal canal BPPV, where horizontal nystagmus is observed.
  • Caloric testing - Typically normal in BPPV but can be used to exclude other vestibular pathologies.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Benign Paroxysmal Positional Vertigo (BPPV)"][/sc]
  • A 60-year-old woman presents with recurrent, brief episodes of vertigo, particularly when turning in bed or looking up. Each episode lasts less than 30 seconds and is associated with nausea but no hearing loss or neurological symptoms.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Benign Paroxysmal Positional Vertigo (BPPV)"][/sc]
  • Vestibular Neuritis - Differentiated by continuous vertigo lasting hours to days, often following a viral infection, without hearing loss or tinnitus.
  • Meniere's Disease - Episodes last longer (hours), accompanied by hearing loss and tinnitus.
  • Stroke - Central causes of vertigo present with persistent symptoms and focal neurological deficits such as dysarthria or limb weakness.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Benign Paroxysmal Positional Vertigo (BPPV)"][/sc]
  • Epley manoeuvre - Canalith repositioning manoeuvre used to move the otoliths back to the utricle, providing symptom relief.
  • Brandt-Daroff exercises - Home exercises to reduce recurrence by habituating the vestibular system.
  • Medication - Vestibular suppressants (e.g., betahistine) are generally avoided unless symptoms are prolonged, as they can interfere with compensation.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Benign Paroxysmal Positional Vertigo (BPPV)"][/sc]
  • Falls - Due to sudden vertigo, increasing the risk of injury, particularly in older adults.
  • Anxiety - Recurrent episodes of vertigo may lead to fear of movement and a decline in quality of life.
