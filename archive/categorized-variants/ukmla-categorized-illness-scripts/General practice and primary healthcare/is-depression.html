Depression
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Depression"][/sc]
  • Depression is a common mental health disorder characterised by persistent low mood, loss of interest or pleasure (anhedonia), and a range of emotional, cognitive, and physical symptoms. The condition significantly affects daily functioning and quality of life. Clinical depression must last for at least two weeks to be formally diagnosed.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Depression"][/sc]
  • Prevalence in the UK GP population: Approximately 3.3% of people are diagnosed with depression annually.
  • UK hospital prevalence: Around 10-15% of hospital patients may present with depressive symptoms.
  • Worldwide prevalence: Depression affects an estimated 280 million people globally, with a lifetime prevalence of around 10% in most populations.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Depression"][/sc]
  • Depression is believed to result from a complex interplay between biological, psychological, and environmental factors. The primary biological hypothesis is the dysregulation of neurotransmitters (particularly serotonin, norepinephrine, and dopamine) in key brain areas such as the prefrontal cortex and limbic system. Chronic stress can lead to hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal (HPA) axis hyperactivity, resulting in elevated cortisol levels and hippocampal atrophy. Inflammatory processes and neuroplasticity disturbances are also implicated in the pathogenesis of depression.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Depression"][/sc]
  • Depression typically progresses through several phases. Initial symptoms may develop insidiously over weeks to months. The acute phase, lasting 6-12 weeks, is characterised by severe symptoms. Untreated, depression may become chronic, lasting for years. Periods of remission may occur, but relapse is common, particularly if underlying causes are not addressed.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Depression"][/sc]
  • Low mood - Persistent sadness or feeling "empty", resulting from dysregulation of serotonin in mood-regulating areas of the brain.
  • Anhedonia - Loss of interest or pleasure in previously enjoyable activities, associated with dopamine dysregulation in reward pathways.
  • Fatigue - Constant tiredness due to changes in sleep patterns, energy metabolism, and neuroendocrine dysfunction.
  • Sleep disturbances - Insomnia or hypersomnia, linked to circadian rhythm disruption and serotonin deficits.
  • Appetite changes - Weight loss or gain, possibly due to altered hypothalamic function affecting hunger and satiety signals.
  • Concentration difficulties - Impaired cognitive function, likely due to prefrontal cortex dysregulation and neuroplasticity issues.
  • Feelings of worthlessness or guilt - Cognitive distortions related to self-criticism and negative bias in information processing.
  • Suicidal ideation - Thoughts of death or suicide, emerging from overwhelming hopelessness and emotional distress.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Depression"][/sc]
  • Flat affect - Reduced emotional expression, often linked to serotonin dysregulation in the limbic system.
  • Psychomotor retardation or agitation - Slowed movements or restlessness due to altered basal ganglia function.
  • Poor eye contact - Indicative of social withdrawal and cognitive disengagement, common in depressed patients.
  • Slouched posture - A sign of low energy and motivation, potentially linked to physical fatigue and emotional withdrawal.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Depression"][/sc]
  • Suicidal ideation - Requires immediate intervention due to the risk of suicide, which can be driven by extreme hopelessness.
  • Psychotic features - Hallucinations or delusions, often signify severe depression with psychosis, requiring urgent care.
  • Severe self-neglect - A sign of profound depression, with the risk of physical health deterioration and increased morbidity.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Depression"][/sc]
  • Atypical depression - May include mood reactivity, hypersomnia, and weight gain rather than the typical presentation of low mood and insomnia. This variant is thought to be linked to different neurotransmitter imbalances, particularly serotonin and dopamine.
  • Masked depression - Somatic complaints (e.g., pain, gastrointestinal issues) may dominate rather than emotional symptoms, often due to the patientâ€™s inability to recognise or express mood-related issues.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Depression"][/sc]
  • PHQ-9 questionnaire - A 9-item scale used to quantify the severity of depressive symptoms, with higher scores indicating more severe depression.
  • Blood tests (e.g., TSH, FBC) - Used to rule out underlying causes such as hypothyroidism or anaemia, which may mimic depressive symptoms.
  • Mental health assessment - A structured psychiatric interview to assess the patientâ€™s mood, cognition, and risk factors for severe outcomes such as suicide.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Depression"][/sc]
  • A 40-year-old woman presents with a 3-month history of persistent low mood, fatigue, and loss of interest in her usual activities. She reports difficulty concentrating at work and has had trouble sleeping, waking up early in the morning.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Depression"][/sc]
  • Hypothyroidism - Characterised by fatigue, weight gain, and low mood, but differs from depression in that physical symptoms like cold intolerance and dry skin predominate. Confirmed by low TSH levels.
  • Generalised anxiety disorder (GAD) - Presents with excessive worry and restlessness, but mood reactivity is often more intact than in depression. Anxiety is typically more prominent than low mood.
  • Dysthymia (Persistent depressive disorder) - Similar to depression but with less severe symptoms lasting for at least 2 years.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Depression"][/sc]
  • Psychotherapy - Cognitive behavioural therapy (CBT) is the first-line psychological treatment for mild to moderate depression in the UK.
  • Antidepressants - Selective serotonin reuptake inhibitors (SSRIs) such as sertraline are commonly prescribed for moderate to severe depression.
  • Combination therapy - Severe or treatment-resistant depression often requires both medication and psychotherapy.
  • Crisis intervention - In cases of suicidal ideation or psychosis, urgent referral to mental health services is essential.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Depression"][/sc]
  • Suicide - The most severe complication, with an elevated risk in untreated depression.
  • Substance abuse - Depression increases the risk of alcohol and drug abuse as a form of self-medication.
  • Chronic physical illness - Depression has been linked to increased morbidity from chronic conditions such as cardiovascular disease, partly due to lifestyle factors and chronic inflammation.
