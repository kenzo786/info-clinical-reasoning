Cyclical Breast Pain
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Cyclical Breast Pain"][/sc]
  • Cyclical breast pain (mastalgia) is a recurring discomfort or pain in one or both breasts, often linked to the menstrual cycle. It is typically bilateral, occurs in the luteal phase of the cycle, and resolves with the onset of menstruation.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Cyclical Breast Pain"][/sc]
  • UK GP prevalence: Cyclical breast pain affects approximately 50-70% of women at some point, with around 15-20% seeking medical attention.
  • UK hospital prevalence: Estimated to account for 10-15% of breast clinic referrals.
  • Worldwide prevalence: Similar to UK data, varying slightly depending on population demographics and cultural factors.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Cyclical Breast Pain"][/sc]
  • Cyclical breast pain is thought to result from hormonal fluctuations, primarily oestrogen and progesterone, affecting breast tissue during the menstrual cycle. These hormonal changes may increase water retention, alter the ductal and stromal tissue structure, and heighten sensitivity, leading to pain or discomfort. High prolactin levels in response to stress and dietary factors may also play a role.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Cyclical Breast Pain"][/sc]
  • The pain typically begins during the luteal phase (about 1-2 weeks before menstruation) and resolves with the onset of menses. The cyclic nature is recurrent with each menstrual cycle.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Cyclical Breast Pain"][/sc]
  • Bilateral breast pain - Most common symptom; pain is usually diffuse, affecting the upper outer quadrants and may radiate to the axillae. This is due to hormonal changes causing increased sensitivity in breast tissue.
  • Swelling or fullness - Some patients report breast swelling or heaviness, linked to water retention in response to fluctuating oestrogen and progesterone levels.
  • Breast tenderness - The breasts may feel sore to touch, likely due to oedema and ductal congestion as hormonal fluctuations impact tissue sensitivity.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Cyclical Breast Pain"][/sc]
  • Diffuse breast tenderness - Palpation often reveals tenderness without discrete lumps, aligning with the hormonal sensitivity affecting the whole breast tissue.
  • Breast fullness or engorgement - Visible fullness or engorgement may be noted, especially in premenstrual phases, due to hormonal-induced water retention in the tissue.
  • Absence of masses - Typically, no palpable masses are present, distinguishing cyclical mastalgia from other causes like fibroadenomas or malignancies.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Cyclical Breast Pain"][/sc]
  • Unilateral, non-cyclical pain - Suggests a potential underlying pathology, such as infection or malignancy, as cyclical pain is generally bilateral.
  • Discrete lump - A fixed, hard, or irregular lump that does not fluctuate with the menstrual cycle could indicate breast cancer.
  • Skin changes - Dimpling, retraction, or peau d'orange appearance warrants further evaluation for malignancy.
  • Nipple discharge - Particularly if bloody or unilateral, it may indicate an intraductal papilloma or malignancy.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Cyclical Breast Pain"][/sc]
  • Some women may experience pain that does not resolve with menstruation, raising suspicion for a non-cyclical cause or coexisting pathology.
  • Occasional unilateral cyclical pain can mimic conditions like mastitis or early-stage breast cancer, leading to missed diagnosis without a thorough history.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Cyclical Breast Pain"][/sc]
  • Clinical breast examination - Typically reveals diffuse tenderness without a discrete mass, supporting a cyclical cause if no red flags are present.
  • Mammography or ultrasound - Only indicated if there are red flags; generally, cyclical breast pain does not alter imaging.
  • Hormonal level assessment - Rarely indicated, but hormonal profiles can occasionally help if other endocrine issues are suspected.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Cyclical Breast Pain"][/sc]
  • A 35-year-old woman with a history of bilateral, cyclical breast pain localised to the upper outer quadrants, worsening before menstruation, with no palpable masses or alarming features on examination.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Cyclical Breast Pain"][/sc]
  • Fibrocystic breast changes - Similar cyclical pattern, but typically associated with discrete cysts palpable on examination.
  • Non-cyclical mastalgia - Pain unrelated to the menstrual cycle, more likely due to trauma, infection, or musculoskeletal issues.
  • Mastitis - Often presents with localised pain, erythema, and systemic symptoms like fever.
  • Breast cancer - Rarely presents with pain alone, typically associated with a palpable mass, skin changes, or nipple discharge.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Cyclical Breast Pain"][/sc]
  • Reassurance and monitoring - First-line approach as cyclical breast pain is often benign and self-limiting.
  • Lifestyle modifications - Reducing caffeine and dietary fat; wearing a supportive bra may help alleviate symptoms.
  • Medications - NSAIDs for pain relief; in severe cases, hormone-modulating agents like tamoxifen may be considered under specialist guidance.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Cyclical Breast Pain"][/sc]
  • Chronic pain - Some patients may develop persistent pain that impacts quality of life, necessitating ongoing pain management or hormonal therapy.
  • Psychological impact - Anxiety related to breast cancer fears can cause emotional distress and impact daily life, particularly if pain recurs or intensifies.
