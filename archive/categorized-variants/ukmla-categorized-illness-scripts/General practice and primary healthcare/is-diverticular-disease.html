Diverticular Disease
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Diverticular Disease"][/sc]
  • Diverticular disease refers to symptomatic manifestations resulting from diverticula, which are small bulging pouches that form in the walls of the large intestine, particularly the sigmoid colon. It encompasses both diverticulosis (presence of diverticula without symptoms) and diverticulitis (inflammation of diverticula, causing pain and complications).
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Diverticular Disease"][/sc]
  • UK GP Population: Diverticular disease affects around 5-10% of individuals aged 40-49, rising to 50-60% in those over 80 years.
  • UK Hospital Prevalence: Hospital admissions for acute diverticulitis occur in 20-40% of those with diverticulosis, typically due to complications.
  • Worldwide Prevalence: Prevalence varies, being more common in Western societies due to low-fibre diets. Rates of diverticulosis in adults aged over 50 reach 40-60%, with diverticulitis occurring in about 4% of these cases.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Diverticular Disease"][/sc]
  • Diverticulum formation: Caused by increased colonic intraluminal pressure, usually due to a low-fibre diet. This leads to mucosal and submucosal herniation through the muscularis layer, forming pouches.
  • Diverticulitis: Occurs when faecal matter becomes trapped in a diverticulum, causing inflammation, microperforation, and in severe cases, abscess formation or rupture.
  • Complications: Inflammation may lead to abscesses, perforation, fistulae, or strictures due to chronic scarring.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Diverticular Disease"][/sc]
  • Diverticulosis: Often asymptomatic and slowly progressive over decades.
  • Diverticulitis: Sudden onset of symptoms, with acute episodes lasting days to weeks if uncomplicated. Complications can lead to chronic issues like fistulas or recurrent infections.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Diverticular Disease"][/sc]
  • Left lower quadrant (LLQ) pain - Results from inflammation, typically in the sigmoid colon.
  • Fever - Caused by systemic inflammation in diverticulitis.
  • Altered bowel habits (constipation or diarrhoea) - Inflammation disrupts normal bowel function.
  • Bloating - Increased gas production due to altered colonic motility.
  • Nausea and vomiting - Result from bowel obstruction or irritation of the gastrointestinal tract.
  • Painless rectal bleeding - In diverticulosis, due to erosion of blood vessels at the site of diverticula (less common in diverticulitis).
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Diverticular Disease"][/sc]
  • LLQ tenderness - Localised peritoneal irritation from inflamed diverticula.
  • Palpable mass - Indicates a possible abscess formation.
  • Rebound tenderness - Suggests peritonitis, often a sign of perforation.
  • Hypotension and tachycardia - May occur in cases of significant bleeding or sepsis.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Diverticular Disease"][/sc]
  • Generalised abdominal pain with rigidity - May indicate perforation, leading to peritonitis.
  • Severe, sudden LLQ pain with haemodynamic instability - Suggests a colonic abscess or significant haemorrhage.
  • Persistent vomiting and distension - Could indicate bowel obstruction.
  • Rectal bleeding with signs of shock - Suggestive of a diverticular bleed requiring urgent intervention.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Diverticular Disease"][/sc]
  • Right-sided diverticulitis - Although rare, can present with right-sided pain, mimicking appendicitis.
  • Asymptomatic diverticulosis - Many individuals remain completely asymptomatic, with diverticula detected incidentally on imaging.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Diverticular Disease"][/sc]
  • Full blood count (FBC): Elevated white cell count (WBC) indicative of infection or inflammation.
  • CRP: Elevated C-reactive protein reflects systemic inflammation.
  • CT scan of the abdomen and pelvis: First-line imaging, revealing diverticula, thickened colonic walls, abscesses, or free air in cases of perforation.
  • Colonoscopy: Used for evaluation post-diverticulitis episode, to rule out malignancy. Not advised in acute diverticulitis due to perforation risk.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Diverticular Disease"][/sc]
  • A 65-year-old male presents with a 3-day history of left lower quadrant pain, fever, and constipation. On examination, LLQ tenderness with mild guarding is noted, and the FBC shows leucocytosis.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Diverticular Disease"][/sc]
  • Colorectal carcinoma: Can present with similar LLQ pain and altered bowel habits but usually with weight loss, rectal bleeding, and more chronic symptoms.
  • Ulcerative colitis: Diffuse abdominal pain and diarrhoea, often with a history of bloody stools, and systemic symptoms like weight loss.
  • Appendicitis: Typically causes right-sided abdominal pain, but in rare cases of a long appendix, it can present with LLQ pain.
  • Ischaemic colitis: Sudden onset of abdominal pain and bloody diarrhoea, commonly in elderly patients with cardiovascular risk factors.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Diverticular Disease"][/sc]
  • Mild diverticulitis: Managed with oral antibiotics (e.g. co-amoxiclav), bowel rest, and a liquid diet.
  • Severe diverticulitis: Hospitalisation, IV antibiotics (e.g. cefuroxime and metronidazole), and analgesia.
  • Surgery: Indicated for complications such as abscess formation, perforation, fistulas, or recurrent diverticulitis. Hartmann's procedure may be needed in emergencies.
  • Prevention: High-fibre diet to prevent future diverticula and reduce symptoms.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Diverticular Disease"][/sc]
  • Abscess: Localised infection requiring drainage, occurring in 15% of patients with complicated diverticulitis.
  • Perforation: A life-threatening emergency due to faecal peritonitis.
  • Fistula: Abnormal connections between bowel and bladder, vagina, or skin, developing in chronic cases.
  • Stricture formation: Chronic inflammation leads to colonic narrowing, causing bowel obstruction.
