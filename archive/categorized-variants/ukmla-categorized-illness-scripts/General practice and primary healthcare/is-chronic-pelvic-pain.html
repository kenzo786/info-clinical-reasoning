Chronic Pelvic Pain
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Chronic Pelvic Pain"][/sc]
  • Chronic pelvic pain (CPP) is defined as non-cyclic pain localised to the pelvis or lower abdomen that persists for six months or longer and is severe enough to cause functional impairment or require medical intervention. CPP is often multifactorial and may be associated with urological, gynaecological, gastrointestinal, musculoskeletal, or neurological causes. The pain can be constant or intermittent and may vary in severity.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Chronic Pelvic Pain"][/sc]
  • Prevalence in UK general practice: Chronic pelvic pain affects approximately 3-15% of women in primary care settings, making it one of the most common reasons for gynaecological referrals.
  • UK hospital prevalence: Around 20-30% of all laparoscopies performed in gynaecology departments in the UK are for the investigation of CPP.
  • Worldwide prevalence: Varies, with studies estimating that 2-24% of women globally experience CPP. Prevalence is less well-studied in men but recognised in association with chronic prostatitis and other urological conditions.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Chronic Pelvic Pain"][/sc]
  • CPP is a complex condition often involving multiple overlapping mechanisms and comorbidities. Inflammatory, neuropathic, and myofascial pathways play roles, frequently compounded by psychological factors like anxiety and depression, which can both exacerbate and be exacerbated by chronic pain.
  • Neuroinflammation: Persistent inflammation in pelvic organs (e.g., bladder in interstitial cystitis, uterus in endometriosis) may sensitise nerves, leading to pain hypersensitivity.
  • Central sensitisation: Chronic pain can lead to an increased sensitivity in the central nervous system, where non-painful stimuli become painful (allodynia) and painful stimuli feel more intense (hyperalgesia).
  • Myofascial component: Pelvic muscle spasms and tension (e.g., in the levator ani or obturator internus muscles) can contribute to pain.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Chronic Pelvic Pain"][/sc]
  • Acute onset is uncommon; CPP generally develops gradually over months to years and becomes chronic once it persists for over six months. The pain may vary cyclically (in association with menstrual cycle) or remain constant.
  • Relapses and remissions are common, with symptom flares occurring due to stress, activity, hormonal changes, or infection.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Chronic Pelvic Pain"][/sc]
  • Pelvic pain - Persistent or intermittent pain, often exacerbated by activity or intercourse, resulting from neural and muscle involvement in the pelvis.
  • Dysmenorrhoea - Painful menstruation, commonly seen in endometriosis, due to inflammation and sensitisation.
  • Dyspareunia - Pain during intercourse, often linked to pelvic muscle spasms or associated gynaecological issues.
  • Bladder and bowel dysfunction - Includes symptoms such as urinary urgency, frequency, or constipation, associated with interstitial cystitis or irritable bowel syndrome (IBS).
  • Low back pain - Reflects muscle tension and may be referred pain from pelvic organs or musculature.
  • Fatigue and sleep disturbances - Secondary to chronic pain affecting overall quality of life and leading to sleep disruptions.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Chronic Pelvic Pain"][/sc]
  • Tenderness on pelvic examination - Generalised or localised pain upon palpation, often found in conditions like endometriosis or pelvic inflammatory disease.
  • Muscle tenderness or spasm - Noted in the pelvic floor muscles, particularly in myofascial pain syndromes.
  • Reduced range of motion in the lumbar spine - May occur due to tension in associated muscles and referred pain.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Chronic Pelvic Pain"][/sc]
  • Unexplained weight loss - Could indicate malignancy, especially if associated with bleeding or persistent systemic symptoms.
  • Post-menopausal bleeding - Raises suspicion of gynaecological malignancy, particularly endometrial cancer.
  • Acute, severe pain with fever - May suggest acute pelvic inflammatory disease, abscess, or ruptured ovarian cyst.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Chronic Pelvic Pain"][/sc]
  • Isolated urinary or bowel symptoms - May present with symptoms similar to bladder pain syndrome or IBS without typical pelvic pain.
  • Lower back pain without overt pelvic pain - Often misdiagnosed as musculoskeletal but may have a pelvic origin.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Chronic Pelvic Pain"][/sc]
  • Pelvic ultrasound - Often used to rule out anatomical abnormalities like ovarian cysts, fibroids. Findings may be normal in CPP.
  • Diagnostic laparoscopy - Considered gold-standard for diagnosing endometriosis, which can cause CPP.
  • Urinalysis and culture - To exclude urinary tract infection, which may mimic CPP.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Chronic Pelvic Pain"][/sc]
  • A 34-year-old woman presents with a six-month history of lower abdominal and pelvic pain, exacerbated by physical activity and intercourse, associated with menstrual irregularities and urinary urgency.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Chronic Pelvic Pain"][/sc]
  • Endometriosis - Cyclic pain, dysmenorrhoea, dyspareunia; often visible on laparoscopy.
  • Interstitial cystitis - Pain relieved by voiding, urinary urgency/frequency.
  • Irritable bowel syndrome (IBS) - Pain related to bowel movements, constipation/diarrhoea.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Chronic Pelvic Pain"][/sc]
  • Pain management - Includes NSAIDs, hormonal therapies (for endometriosis), and neuropathic pain medications like amitriptyline.
  • Physiotherapy - Pelvic floor physical therapy can alleviate myofascial pain.
  • Psychotherapy - Cognitive behavioural therapy (CBT) may help manage chronic pain and associated psychological distress.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Chronic Pelvic Pain"][/sc]
  • Quality of life impairment - Chronic pain can lead to depression, anxiety, and social withdrawal.
  • Infertility - Particularly common in cases of untreated endometriosis.
  • Chronic disability - May result from the combination of physical and psychological impacts of CPP.
