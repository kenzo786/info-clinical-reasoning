White Coat Hypertension
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="White Coat Hypertension"][/sc]
  • White coat hypertension is a condition where patients exhibit elevated blood pressure (BP) readings in clinical settings but have normal readings outside of the healthcare environment. This phenomenon is attributed to anxiety induced by the presence of medical personnel ("white coat effect"). The diagnosis is confirmed when ambulatory or home blood pressure monitoring shows consistently normal readings despite high values in the clinic.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="White Coat Hypertension"][/sc]
  • Prevalence in the UK GP population is estimated to be 10-20% of patients with suspected hypertension.
  • It is more common in individuals over 50 years of age and in females.
  • Globally, the prevalence of white coat hypertension is around 15-30% among patients with elevated office BP.
  • Approximately one-third of individuals diagnosed with hypertension based solely on clinic measurements may actually have white coat hypertension.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="White Coat Hypertension"][/sc]
  • The core mechanism is the acute stress response triggered by the clinical environment, leading to a surge in sympathetic nervous system activity.
  • This sympathetic activation results in vasoconstriction and increased heart rate, causing transient elevations in blood pressure.
  • While the homeostatic mechanisms normally maintain BP at baseline levels, repeated exposure to clinical settings may condition patients to exhibit these responses whenever in similar environments, without true underlying hypertension.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="White Coat Hypertension"][/sc]
  • White coat hypertension typically presents as an episodic phenomenon, with BP elevation occurring acutely during healthcare visits and returning to normal once the patient is in a non-clinical environment.
  • In some patients, white coat hypertension can persist for years if not identified, with potential progression to sustained hypertension due to vascular changes over time.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="White Coat Hypertension"][/sc]
  • Asymptomatic in most cases - The majority of individuals with white coat hypertension do not experience symptoms because the BP elevation is transient and does not affect end organs.
  • Occasional headache or lightheadedness - In some cases, the acute rise in BP during clinical visits may cause mild symptoms such as headaches, which are due to the increased cerebrovascular resistance.
  • Anxiety-related symptoms - Patients may report palpitations, sweating, or nervousness, which are driven by the stress and anxiety of being in a healthcare setting.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="White Coat Hypertension"][/sc]
  • Elevated blood pressure in clinic - The most significant finding, usually >140/90 mmHg in office settings, often normal when checked outside.
  • Normal cardiovascular examination - Apart from elevated BP, the rest of the examination (heart sounds, peripheral pulses) is typically normal.
  • Signs of anxiety - The patient may exhibit visible signs of nervousness, such as rapid breathing, fidgeting, or sweating during the clinic visit.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="White Coat Hypertension"][/sc]
  • Persistently elevated BP despite ambulatory/home monitoring - This may indicate sustained hypertension rather than white coat hypertension, necessitating further investigation and possible treatment.
  • Signs of end-organ damage - Symptoms such as chest pain, visual disturbances, or signs of heart failure may suggest true hypertension with target organ damage, requiring urgent intervention.
  • Severe headache or neurological deficits - These could indicate hypertensive emergency, though rare in white coat hypertension, and demand immediate attention.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="White Coat Hypertension"][/sc]
  • Masked hypertension - In this condition, the patient exhibits normal BP in the clinic but elevated readings at home, opposite to white coat hypertension.
  • Patients with known anxiety disorders - These individuals may show highly variable BP, not necessarily confined to clinic settings, complicating the diagnosis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="White Coat Hypertension"][/sc]
  • Ambulatory blood pressure monitoring (ABPM) - Considered the gold standard for diagnosis. A 24-hour ABPM will show elevated BP in the clinic with normal BP during other times, confirming white coat hypertension.
  • Home blood pressure monitoring (HBPM) - Patients can record their BP at home. If readings at home are consistently <135/85 mmHg but elevated in the clinic, this supports the diagnosis.
  • Routine blood tests (FBC, U&Es) - Usually normal, performed to exclude secondary causes of hypertension or end-organ damage in unclear cases.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="White Coat Hypertension"][/sc]
  • A 55-year-old woman with consistently elevated blood pressure readings in the GP surgery but normal blood pressure on home monitoring, with no associated symptoms or signs of hypertension-related organ damage.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="White Coat Hypertension"][/sc]
  • Sustained hypertension - Unlike white coat hypertension, sustained hypertension will show consistently elevated BP on both office and home/ambulatory readings.
  • Masked hypertension - The opposite condition, where BP is normal in clinic but elevated at home, suggesting true hypertension that is missed in office visits.
  • Anxiety disorder - Anxiety-driven BP elevation can be confused with white coat hypertension, but it often manifests with additional anxiety-related symptoms outside of the clinical setting as well.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="White Coat Hypertension"][/sc]
  • Lifestyle modifications - Since white coat hypertension is often benign, treatment focuses on lifestyle changes such as weight management, exercise, and a low-salt diet to prevent progression to sustained hypertension.
  • Monitoring - Regular follow-up with ABPM or HBPM to ensure that true hypertension does not develop.
  • Pharmacotherapy - Generally not indicated unless there is evidence of progression to sustained hypertension or target organ damage.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="White Coat Hypertension"][/sc]
  • Progression to sustained hypertension - Around 25% of individuals with white coat hypertension eventually develop true hypertension, likely due to cumulative vascular stress.
  • Increased cardiovascular risk - While generally low, individuals with white coat hypertension may have a slightly higher risk of cardiovascular events compared to normotensive individuals.
