Respiratory Failure
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Respiratory Failure"][/sc]
  • Respiratory failure is defined as the inability of the respiratory system to maintain adequate gas exchange, characterised by low oxygen levels (hypoxia) and/or high carbon dioxide levels (hypercapnia). It can be classified as type 1 (hypoxaemic) or type 2 (hypercapnic), depending on the underlying disturbance. Acute respiratory failure develops over minutes to hours, whereas chronic respiratory failure evolves over days to weeks.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Respiratory Failure"][/sc]
  • In the UK, respiratory failure is common in patients with chronic respiratory diseases, such as chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD). Hospital admissions due to respiratory failure in the UK range from 300 to 400 per 100,000 population annually, with a global prevalence higher in areas with increased pollution and smoking.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Respiratory Failure"][/sc]
  • In respiratory failure, gas exchange in the lungs is impaired. In type 1 failure, the alveoli cannot oxygenate blood due to ventilation-perfusion (V/Q) mismatch, often seen in pneumonia or pulmonary oedema. Type 2 failure involves impaired CO2 elimination due to ventilatory pump failure, commonly due to COPD, neuromuscular disorders, or chest wall abnormalities. Chronic respiratory failure involves compensatory mechanisms like bicarbonate retention to buffer excess CO2.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Respiratory Failure"][/sc]
  • Acute respiratory failure: Rapid onset, minutes to hours, common in severe asthma or trauma.
  • Chronic respiratory failure: Develops over days to weeks, often in COPD or interstitial lung disease, allowing time for metabolic compensation.
  • Acute-on-chronic respiratory failure: Acute exacerbation of chronic respiratory disease, with rapid deterioration in a previously stable patient, as in a COPD exacerbation.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Respiratory Failure"][/sc]
  • Dyspnoea (shortness of breath) - Due to hypoxia or hypercapnia, resulting from poor gas exchange.
  • Cough - A reflex triggered by airway irritation, common in obstructive lung disease.
  • Confusion or lethargy - Due to hypercapnia, which causes cerebral vasodilation and impaired brain function.
  • Cyanosis - Bluish discolouration of the skin and mucous membranes due to desaturated haemoglobin, indicating significant hypoxia.
  • Chest pain - May occur in conditions like pulmonary embolism or pneumothorax leading to respiratory failure.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Respiratory Failure"][/sc]
  • Tachypnoea - Increased respiratory rate as a compensatory response to hypoxia or hypercapnia.
  • Use of accessory muscles - Indicates respiratory distress as the patient attempts to enhance ventilation.
  • Hypoxia (SpO2 < 92%) - Low oxygen saturation detectable via pulse oximetry, often seen in type 1 respiratory failure.
  • Hypercapnia (PaCO2 > 6.0 kPa) - Indicates type 2 respiratory failure, causing a range of symptoms from headache to altered consciousness.
  • Altered mental state - Due to the effects of hypercapnia or severe hypoxia on cerebral function.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Respiratory Failure"][/sc]
  • Rapid deterioration in respiratory status - Could signify acute worsening of underlying disease, such as pneumothorax or pulmonary embolism.
  • Confusion or coma - Indicates severe hypercapnia or hypoxia, which may lead to respiratory arrest if untreated.
  • Cyanosis - Indicates severe hypoxia and the potential for tissue hypoxia and organ failure.
  • Paradoxical abdominal movement - Suggests diaphragmatic weakness, seen in neuromuscular causes of respiratory failure.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Respiratory Failure"][/sc]
  • Mild dyspnoea with minimal hypoxia - Chronic compensatory mechanisms in patients with COPD may delay recognition of decompensation.
  • Gradual cognitive decline - In chronic hypercapnia, subtle neurological deficits may precede more obvious symptoms.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Respiratory Failure"][/sc]
  • Arterial blood gas (ABG) - Key test showing hypoxaemia (PaO2 < 8 kPa), hypercapnia (PaCO2 > 6.0 kPa), and acidosis (pH < 7.35).
  • Chest X-ray - May reveal underlying causes like pneumonia, pulmonary oedema, or pneumothorax.
  • Pulmonary function tests - In chronic cases, useful to assess baseline lung function and identify obstructive or restrictive patterns.
  • Blood tests - Full blood count (FBC) to check for anaemia or infection, and urea and electrolytes (U&E) to monitor for metabolic compensation.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Respiratory Failure"][/sc]
  • A 65-year-old male with a history of COPD presents with increasing shortness of breath, confusion, and a cough over the past 24 hours. His SpO2 is 85% on room air, and ABG shows hypoxaemia and hypercapnia.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Respiratory Failure"][/sc]
  • Pulmonary embolism - Sudden onset of dyspnoea and pleuritic chest pain, usually with a normal chest X-ray but hypoxaemia on ABG.
  • Congestive heart failure - May present similarly, but with signs of fluid overload (e.g., peripheral oedema) and cardiomegaly on chest X-ray.
  • Pneumonia - Fever, productive cough, and focal infiltrates on chest X-ray, often accompanied by hypoxaemia.
  • Acute asthma exacerbation - Wheezing and tight chest, often in younger patients with hyperinflated lungs on X-ray.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Respiratory Failure"][/sc]
  • Oxygen therapy - Aim to maintain SpO2 > 88% but avoid hyperoxia in type 2 failure to prevent worsening hypercapnia.
  • Non-invasive ventilation (NIV) - Used for patients with type 2 respiratory failure (e.g., COPD exacerbation) to reduce CO2 levels.
  • Bronchodilators and steroids - For underlying obstructive diseases like COPD or asthma.
  • Antibiotics - If infection such as pneumonia is contributing to respiratory failure.
  • Ventilatory support - In severe cases, mechanical ventilation may be required to manage acute respiratory failure.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Respiratory Failure"][/sc]
  • Cardiac arrest - Due to severe hypoxia or acidosis.
  • Respiratory arrest - If untreated, especially in acute respiratory failure with inadequate ventilation.
  • Multi-organ failure - Hypoxia can lead to widespread tissue damage, affecting organs such as the kidneys and liver.
  • Prolonged mechanical ventilation - Leads to complications like ventilator-associated pneumonia or barotrauma.
