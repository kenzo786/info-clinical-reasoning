Anaemia of Chronic Disease (ACD)
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Anaemia of Chronic Disease (ACD)"][/sc]
  • Anaemia of chronic disease (ACD), also known as anaemia of inflammation, is a hypoproliferative anaemia that occurs in patients with chronic inflammatory, infectious, or neoplastic conditions. It is characterised by impaired iron utilisation despite adequate stores, reduced erythropoiesis, and shortened red blood cell survival, leading to mild-to-moderate normocytic or microcytic anaemia.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Anaemia of Chronic Disease (ACD)"][/sc]
  • Prevalence in the UK GP population: Anaemia is present in approximately 2-5% of GP consultations, with ACD accounting for a significant proportion, particularly among older adults.
  • Hospital prevalence in the UK: Anaemia is found in around 30% of hospitalised patients, with chronic disease anaemia being a common type in those with chronic inflammatory or neoplastic conditions.
  • Worldwide prevalence: ACD is common worldwide, especially in low- and middle-income countries where infectious diseases like tuberculosis and HIV are prevalent.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Anaemia of Chronic Disease (ACD)"][/sc]
  • 1. Chronic inflammation triggers an increase in cytokines, particularly interleukin-6 (IL-6).
  • 2. IL-6 stimulates the liver to produce hepcidin, a hormone that inhibits iron absorption from the gut and traps iron within macrophages.
  • 3. This results in reduced iron availability for erythropoiesis, despite normal or increased total body iron stores.
  • 4. Erythropoiesis is further impaired by reduced erythropoietin production and response, leading to fewer red blood cells.
  • 5. Red blood cell lifespan is shortened due to increased phagocytosis and oxidative stress.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Anaemia of Chronic Disease (ACD)"][/sc]
  • Initial phase: As the underlying chronic condition progresses, mild anaemia may develop insidiously over weeks to months.
  • Chronic phase: The anaemia often stabilises at a mild to moderate level, persisting as long as the underlying inflammatory condition remains active.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Anaemia of Chronic Disease (ACD)"][/sc]
  • Fatigue - Reduced oxygen delivery to tissues due to anaemia leads to fatigue. Chronic inflammation also contributes to this symptom via cytokine-mediated mechanisms.
  • Shortness of breath on exertion - Decreased haemoglobin levels reduce oxygen-carrying capacity, causing dyspnoea during physical activity.
  • Pallor - Reduced haemoglobin concentration leads to decreased oxygenation of the skin and mucous membranes, resulting in visible paleness.
  • Often asymptomatic - ACD is frequently mild and may not cause noticeable symptoms, especially in older adults or in the presence of slow-onset anaemia.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Anaemia of Chronic Disease (ACD)"][/sc]
  • Pallor - Pale skin and mucous membranes reflect reduced haemoglobin levels and decreased blood flow.
  • Tachycardia - The heart compensates for reduced oxygen delivery by increasing cardiac output.
  • Mild splenomegaly - Chronic inflammation and increased red cell destruction may cause slight enlargement of the spleen.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Anaemia of Chronic Disease (ACD)"][/sc]
  • Severe anaemia (haemoglobin <80 g/L) - May indicate a superimposed condition like iron deficiency anaemia or acute blood loss, requiring urgent investigation.
  • Sudden worsening of symptoms - Could suggest acute infection, malignancy progression, or bone marrow failure.
  • Unintentional weight loss - May signify an underlying malignancy, particularly if coupled with ACD.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Anaemia of Chronic Disease (ACD)"][/sc]
  • Mild cognitive impairment - Particularly in older adults, chronic anaemia can present as subtle mental changes or reduced concentration, due to diminished cerebral oxygenation.
  • Depression or lethargy - Inflammatory cytokines may exacerbate mood disorders or generalised lethargy.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Anaemia of Chronic Disease (ACD)"][/sc]
  • Full blood count (FBC): Normocytic or mildly microcytic anaemia, haemoglobin reduced, normal or slightly reduced mean corpuscular volume (MCV).
  • Serum ferritin: Normal or elevated, reflecting adequate or increased iron stores.
  • Serum iron and transferrin saturation: Both are low, as hepcidin blocks iron release from stores.
  • C-reactive protein (CRP) and erythrocyte sedimentation rate (ESR): Raised, indicating chronic inflammation.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Anaemia of Chronic Disease (ACD)"][/sc]
  • A 65-year-old male with a history of rheumatoid arthritis presents with 3 months of worsening fatigue and mild shortness of breath. Laboratory tests show normocytic anaemia with elevated ferritin and low transferrin saturation.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Anaemia of Chronic Disease (ACD)"][/sc]
  • Iron deficiency anaemia: Typically microcytic, with low ferritin and low iron stores.
  • Thalassaemia: Microcytic anaemia with normal or raised ferritin; usually a familial history.
  • Chronic kidney disease (CKD) anaemia: Anaemia due to decreased erythropoietin production, commonly normocytic with normal iron studies but raised urea and creatinine.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Anaemia of Chronic Disease (ACD)"][/sc]
  • Treat the underlying condition: Effective management of the chronic inflammatory or infectious disease will often improve ACD.
  • Erythropoiesis-stimulating agents (ESAs): Used in certain cases (e.g., chronic kidney disease) to stimulate red blood cell production.
  • Iron supplementation: Generally not beneficial unless iron deficiency coexists.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Anaemia of Chronic Disease (ACD)"][/sc]
  • Progressive anaemia: May occur if the underlying condition worsens or if superimposed iron deficiency develops.
  • Cardiac complications: Chronic anaemia increases the risk of heart failure and ischaemic heart disease, especially in older adults.
  • Impaired quality of life: Fatigue and reduced physical capacity can lead to reduced daily functioning and social withdrawal.
