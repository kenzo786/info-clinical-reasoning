Type 1 Diabetes Mellitus
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Type 1 Diabetes Mellitus"][/sc]
  • Type 1 Diabetes Mellitus (T1DM) is a chronic autoimmune condition characterised by the destruction of insulin-producing beta cells in the pancreas. This leads to an absolute insulin deficiency, resulting in hyperglycaemia. It typically presents in childhood or early adulthood but can develop at any age.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Type 1 Diabetes Mellitus"][/sc]
  • Prevalence in UK GP population: Approximately 0.5% of the population.
  • Hospital prevalence in the UK: T1DM accounts for about 10% of all diabetes cases seen in hospital.
  • Worldwide prevalence: Around 9 million people globally have T1DM, making up about 5-10% of all diabetes cases.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Type 1 Diabetes Mellitus"][/sc]
  • Autoimmune destruction of pancreatic beta cells, typically mediated by T-cells, leads to an absolute deficiency of insulin.
  • Without insulin, glucose cannot enter cells, leading to hyperglycaemia.
  • The liver continues to produce glucose (gluconeogenesis), worsening hyperglycaemia.
  • The kidneys filter excess glucose, leading to glycosuria and osmotic diuresis (polyuria and dehydration).
  • Lack of insulin also leads to unregulated lipolysis and ketogenesis, resulting in diabetic ketoacidosis (DKA) in severe cases.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Type 1 Diabetes Mellitus"][/sc]
  • Initial Phase: Gradual autoimmune destruction, with subclinical hyperglycaemia for months to years.
  • Onset: Acute presentation once beta cell mass falls below 20%, often with symptoms of hyperglycaemia.
  • Chronic Phase: Lifelong insulin dependency after diagnosis.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Type 1 Diabetes Mellitus"][/sc]
  • Polyuria - Excessive urination due to osmotic diuresis caused by glycosuria.
  • Polydipsia - Excessive thirst due to dehydration from osmotic diuresis.
  • Weight loss - Due to catabolism of fat and muscle in the absence of insulin.
  • Fatigue - Results from the inability of cells to use glucose for energy without insulin.
  • Blurred vision - Caused by glucose-induced osmotic changes in the lens of the eye.
  • Ketoacidosis symptoms (nausea, vomiting, abdominal pain) - Results from the accumulation of ketones in the blood due to fat metabolism.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Type 1 Diabetes Mellitus"][/sc]
  • Dehydration - Dry mucous membranes, poor skin turgor due to osmotic diuresis.
  • Ketonuria - Presence of ketones in the urine due to fat metabolism.
  • Fruity breath odour - Caused by exhaled acetone in ketoacidosis.
  • Altered mental status - Can occur in severe hyperglycaemia or ketoacidosis.
  • Tachycardia and hypotension - Signs of dehydration and possible impending shock in ketoacidosis.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Type 1 Diabetes Mellitus"][/sc]
  • Kussmaul breathing - Deep, laboured breathing due to metabolic acidosis, a hallmark of diabetic ketoacidosis (DKA).
  • Altered consciousness - Suggests severe DKA or hypoglycaemia; can lead to coma.
  • Severe dehydration - May indicate impending shock in DKA, requiring immediate fluid resuscitation.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Type 1 Diabetes Mellitus"][/sc]
  • Late onset Type 1 diabetes (LADA) - Presents in adulthood with slow progression, often initially misdiagnosed as Type 2 diabetes.
  • Silent diabetes - Rarely, T1DM may be diagnosed incidentally without the typical symptomatic presentation, especially in screening tests.

Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Type 1 Diabetes Mellitus"][/sc]
  • A 14-year-old boy presents with a 3-week history of polyuria, polydipsia, weight loss, and fatigue. He is found to have hyperglycaemia and ketonuria on investigation, consistent with new-onset type 1 diabetes.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Type 1 Diabetes Mellitus"][/sc]
  • Type 2 Diabetes - Distinguished by obesity, metabolic syndrome, and higher C-peptide levels.
  • MODY (Maturity-Onset Diabetes of the Young) - Often familial, with onset before 25 and absence of autoantibodies.
  • Diabetes secondary to pancreatic disease - Associated with pancreatic pathology (e.g., pancreatitis), lacks autoimmunity.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Type 1 Diabetes Mellitus"][/sc]
  • Insulin therapy - Basal-bolus regimen with long-acting and short-acting insulin, titrated according to blood glucose levels.
  • Continuous glucose monitoring (CGM) - Recommended to improve glucose control.
  • Patient education - On carbohydrate counting, insulin adjustment, and hypoglycaemia management.
  • Regular follow-up - Annual review to monitor for complications like retinopathy, nephropathy, and neuropathy.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Type 1 Diabetes Mellitus"][/sc]
  • Diabetic ketoacidosis (DKA) - Acute, life-threatening complication due to severe insulin deficiency.
  • Hypoglycaemia - Common side effect of insulin therapy, especially if insulin dosing is not well matched to carbohydrate intake.
  • Microvascular complications - Retinopathy, nephropathy, and neuropathy due to chronic hyperglycaemia.
  • Macrovascular complications - Increased risk of cardiovascular disease.
