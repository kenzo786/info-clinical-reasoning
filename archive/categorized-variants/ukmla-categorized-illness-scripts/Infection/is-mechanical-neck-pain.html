Mechanical Neck Pain
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Mechanical Neck Pain"][/sc]
  • Mechanical neck pain is a musculoskeletal condition characterised by localised pain in the cervical spine, often exacerbated by movement, posture, or prolonged static positions. It is non-radiating in most cases and lacks neurological deficits.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Mechanical Neck Pain"][/sc]
  • Prevalence in UK GP population: Approximately 10-20% of adult consultations involve neck pain, with mechanical neck pain being the most common type.
  • UK hospital prevalence: Less common, as patients often present in primary care unless symptoms are severe or atypical.
  • Worldwide prevalence: Around 16-75% of individuals experience neck pain during their lifetime, with a yearly prevalence of 10-20%.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Mechanical Neck Pain"][/sc]
  • Caused by strain, overuse, or minor trauma to cervical spine structures such as muscles, ligaments, intervertebral discs, and facet joints.
  • Microtrauma or poor posture can lead to local inflammation, muscle spasm, and pain receptor sensitisation in the cervical spine.
  • Altered biomechanics may further perpetuate the cycle of pain and stiffness.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Mechanical Neck Pain"][/sc]
  • Acute phase: Sudden onset, often after identifiable strain, lasting days to weeks.
  • Subacute phase: Gradual improvement over weeks to months, though symptoms may persist.
  • Chronic phase: Symptoms lasting >3 months, often linked to poor ergonomics or recurrent episodes.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Mechanical Neck Pain"][/sc]
  • Localised neck pain - Commonly worsens with movement or maintaining one position for prolonged periods, due to mechanical irritation and inflammation of cervical structures.
  • Stiffness - Frequently noted in the morning or after prolonged inactivity, caused by muscle spasm and joint restriction.
  • Reduced range of motion - A consequence of pain, stiffness, and muscle guarding.
  • Non-specific headache - Referred pain from cervical structures, often occipital or suboccipital in location.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Mechanical Neck Pain"][/sc]
  • Tenderness over cervical spine - Indicative of local inflammation or muscle strain.
  • Restricted cervical spine movements - Pain and spasm limit flexion, extension, or rotation.
  • Postural abnormalities - Forward head posture or asymmetry due to muscle imbalance.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Mechanical Neck Pain"][/sc]
  • Severe or progressive neurological symptoms - May indicate cervical radiculopathy or myelopathy.
  • Unrelenting night pain or systemic symptoms - Raises concern for malignancy or infection.
  • History of significant trauma - Suggests possible fracture or dislocation.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Mechanical Neck Pain"][/sc]
  • Chronic low-grade pain with mild neurological symptoms, which may mimic degenerative disc disease or fibromyalgia.
  • Bilateral symptoms with gait instability, suggesting spinal cord involvement rather than localised mechanical pain.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Mechanical Neck Pain"][/sc]
  • X-ray - May show normal findings or age-related degenerative changes, used to rule out significant trauma.
  • MRI - Indicated if neurological signs are present, showing disc pathology or nerve compression.
  • ESR/CRP - Elevated levels suggest inflammatory or infectious processes.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Mechanical Neck Pain"][/sc]
  • A 45-year-old office worker presents with a 2-week history of localised neck pain exacerbated by prolonged sitting and computer use, associated with morning stiffness and reduced range of motion.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Mechanical Neck Pain"][/sc]
  • Cervical radiculopathy - Distinguished by radiating arm pain and neurological deficits.
  • Cervical myelopathy - Presents with bilateral neurological symptoms and gait disturbance.
  • Fibromyalgia - Generalised pain and tenderness without localised signs.
  • Ankylosing spondylitis - Chronic inflammatory back pain with systemic symptoms.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Mechanical Neck Pain"][/sc]
  • Non-pharmacological: Education on posture, ergonomic adjustments, and exercise programmes.
  • Pharmacological: Analgesics (paracetamol, NSAIDs) and muscle relaxants as needed.
  • Physiotherapy: Tailored exercises to restore range of motion and strength.
  • Rare interventions: Injections or surgery for refractory cases.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Mechanical Neck Pain"][/sc]
  • Chronic pain and disability, linked to poor ergonomics and lack of treatment adherence.
  • Progression to secondary conditions such as cervicogenic headache or radiculopathy in severe cases.
