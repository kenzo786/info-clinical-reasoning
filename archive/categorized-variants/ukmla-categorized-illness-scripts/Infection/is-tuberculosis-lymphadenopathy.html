Tuberculosis (Lymphadenopathy)
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Tuberculosis (Lymphadenopathy)"][/sc]
  • Tuberculosis (TB) lymphadenopathy refers to the involvement of lymph nodes by Mycobacterium tuberculosis infection. It is the most common form of extrapulmonary tuberculosis and typically presents as chronic, painless swelling of the lymph nodes, often in the cervical region. TB lymphadenopathy can develop after primary TB infection or reactivation of latent TB.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Tuberculosis (Lymphadenopathy)"][/sc]
  • In the UK GP population, tuberculosis is relatively uncommon, with a prevalence of 8-10 per 100,000 individuals. TB lymphadenopathy represents about 30-40% of extrapulmonary TB cases.
  • In UK hospitals, TB remains more common in certain at-risk groups, including those with HIV, immigrants from endemic regions, and immunocompromised individuals.
  • Worldwide, TB is a significant public health concern, particularly in low-income countries, with approximately 10 million cases reported annually. TB lymphadenopathy is particularly prevalent in regions with high TB burden, such as sub-Saharan Africa and Southeast Asia.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Tuberculosis (Lymphadenopathy)"][/sc]
  • After inhalation of aerosolised droplets containing Mycobacterium tuberculosis, the bacteria reach the alveoli and initiate a local immune response.
  • In cases where the immune system contains the infection, it may remain latent. If it spreads, either from the primary infection or through reactivation of latent TB, it can involve lymph nodes.
  • TB lymphadenopathy arises when mycobacteria infect and proliferate within the lymphatic system, leading to granuloma formation. Caseating necrosis may occur in the centre of these granulomas, causing node enlargement.
  • The cervical lymph nodes are most commonly involved, but other nodal groups, such as mediastinal or mesenteric nodes, can also be affected.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Tuberculosis (Lymphadenopathy)"][/sc]
  • **Early phase:** During primary TB infection, lymph node involvement may be asymptomatic, with slow progression of nodal swelling over weeks to months.
  • **Chronic phase:** Chronic, painless swelling of affected nodes persists. Without treatment, the nodes may eventually rupture and form draining sinuses.
  • **Late phase/Complications:** Untreated cases may progress to disseminated TB or cause extensive tissue destruction in nearby areas.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Tuberculosis (Lymphadenopathy)"][/sc]
  • Painless lymph node swelling - The most common symptom, caused by granulomatous inflammation and caseation necrosis of the affected nodes.
  • Fever - A common systemic symptom due to the body's inflammatory response to the infection.
  • Night sweats - These are characteristic of TB and are caused by cytokine-mediated inflammatory processes, particularly IL-1 and TNF-alpha.
  • Weight loss - The bodyâ€™s catabolic state during chronic infection leads to cachexia.
  • Fatigue - A non-specific symptom resulting from chronic infection and systemic inflammation.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Tuberculosis (Lymphadenopathy)"][/sc]
  • Firm, non-tender lymphadenopathy - Nodes are characteristically firm and matted due to fibrosis and granulomatous changes.
  • Fluctuant nodes - As caseous necrosis progresses, nodes may become soft and fluctuant.
  • Cold abscesses - These may form in advanced stages when necrotic material breaks through the skin, creating painless draining sinuses.
  • Hepatosplenomegaly - May be present in disseminated or more advanced cases of TB infection.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Tuberculosis (Lymphadenopathy)"][/sc]
  • Rapidly enlarging lymph nodes - This may suggest malignancy or a more aggressive infection such as lymphoma or metastatic disease.
  • Breathing difficulties or dysphagia - If the mediastinal nodes are involved, compression of the airways or oesophagus can occur.
  • Cold abscess formation - Signifies progression of the disease and the risk of systemic dissemination (miliary TB).
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Tuberculosis (Lymphadenopathy)"][/sc]
  • Isolated mesenteric lymphadenopathy - Can present with abdominal pain, mimicking conditions such as appendicitis or Crohnâ€™s disease.
  • Mediastinal TB - Often presents with cough, chest pain, and difficulty swallowing, mimicking lung carcinoma or sarcoidosis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Tuberculosis (Lymphadenopathy)"][/sc]
  • Tuberculin skin test (TST/Mantoux test) - A positive result indicates TB exposure, but does not differentiate between active and latent TB.
  • Interferon-gamma release assays (IGRA) - These blood tests (e.g., Quantiferon) detect TB infection with greater specificity than TST and are preferred in BCG-vaccinated individuals.
  • Fine needle aspiration cytology (FNAC) - Aspiration of the lymph node may reveal granulomas, caseous necrosis, or acid-fast bacilli on Ziehl-Neelsen staining.
  • Cultures and PCR - Mycobacterial culture or PCR from lymph node biopsy can confirm the diagnosis, though culture results take weeks.
  • Chest X-ray - May show hilar lymphadenopathy or pulmonary involvement, supporting the diagnosis of TB.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Tuberculosis (Lymphadenopathy)"][/sc]
  • A 35-year-old male immigrant from South Asia presents with a 4-month history of progressive, painless cervical lymphadenopathy, night sweats, weight loss, and low-grade fever. Fine needle aspiration reveals granulomatous inflammation.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Tuberculosis (Lymphadenopathy)"][/sc]
  • Lymphoma - Presents with painless lymphadenopathy but lacks the systemic features (night sweats, fever, weight loss) seen in TB. Biopsy shows malignant cells rather than granulomas.
  • Sarcoidosis - Granulomatous lymphadenopathy, but lacks caseation necrosis. Serum ACE levels are often elevated, and there is no history of TB exposure.
  • Metastatic carcinoma - Rapidly enlarging nodes, often painful, and primary tumour can usually be identified. Biopsy reveals malignant epithelial cells.
  • Cat scratch disease - Regional lymphadenopathy, typically with a history of cat exposure and often self-limiting.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Tuberculosis (Lymphadenopathy)"][/sc]
  • First-line treatment (UK guidelines) - 6-month regimen of antituberculous therapy: initial 2 months of isoniazid, rifampicin, pyrazinamide, and ethambutol (HRZE), followed by 4 months of isoniazid and rifampicin (HR).
  • Adjunctive corticosteroids - Considered in cases with severe inflammation or airway compression from lymphadenopathy.
  • Surgical intervention - Rarely needed, but may be considered for cold abscess drainage or excision of ruptured lymph nodes.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Tuberculosis (Lymphadenopathy)"][/sc]
  • Sinus tract formation - Occurs when necrotic nodes rupture and drain through the skin.
  • Disseminated TB (Miliary TB) - Spread of TB from lymph nodes to other organs, including the lungs, liver, and brain.
  • Superimposed bacterial infection - Draining sinuses can become secondarily infected.
