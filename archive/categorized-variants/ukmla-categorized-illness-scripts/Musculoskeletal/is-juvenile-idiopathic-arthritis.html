Juvenile Idiopathic Arthritis (JIA)
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Juvenile Idiopathic Arthritis (JIA)"][/sc]
  • Juvenile idiopathic arthritis (JIA) is a heterogeneous group of autoimmune and autoinflammatory diseases characterised by arthritis of unknown cause lasting at least six weeks, with onset before the age of 16 years. It is the most common chronic rheumatological condition in children.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Juvenile Idiopathic Arthritis (JIA)"][/sc]
  • UK GP population prevalence: Approximately 1 in 1,000 children.
  • UK hospital prevalence: Accounts for 1 in 10,000 paediatric rheumatology consultations annually.
  • Worldwide prevalence: Varies by region, estimated between 0.07 and 4 per 1,000 children globally.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Juvenile Idiopathic Arthritis (JIA)"][/sc]
  • Genetic susceptibility, including HLA associations, predisposes to an abnormal immune response.
  • Environmental triggers, such as infections, can initiate immune dysregulation.
  • Inappropriate activation of T-cells and cytokines (e.g., TNF-Î±, IL-1, IL-6) leads to synovial inflammation.
  • Chronic inflammation results in synovial hyperplasia, angiogenesis, and joint damage due to pannus formation.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Juvenile Idiopathic Arthritis (JIA)"][/sc]
  • Initial presentation often includes joint pain, stiffness, and swelling over weeks to months.
  • Subtypes of JIA have variable courses:
    • Oligoarticular: Flares with remission possible.
    • Polyarticular: Chronic with persistent inflammation.
    • Systemic: Waxing and waning fevers with inflammatory episodes.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Juvenile Idiopathic Arthritis (JIA)"][/sc]
  • Joint Pain - Caused by synovial inflammation, leading to pressure and sensitisation of pain fibres.
  • Morning Stiffness - Due to accumulation of inflammatory mediators overnight.
  • Joint Swelling - Results from synovial hyperplasia and effusion.
  • Fatigue - Likely secondary to systemic inflammation and cytokine dysregulation.
  • Fever (Systemic JIA) - Driven by IL-6 and other pro-inflammatory cytokines.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Juvenile Idiopathic Arthritis (JIA)"][/sc]
  • Warmth over Joints - Indicates active inflammation and increased vascular flow.
  • Limited Range of Motion - Due to pain, effusion, or synovial thickening.
  • Growth Abnormalities - Overgrowth or undergrowth caused by localised inflammation or corticosteroid use.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Juvenile Idiopathic Arthritis (JIA)"][/sc]
  • Uveitis - Chronic inflammation of the eye; risk of vision loss if untreated.
  • Systemic Features - High fevers, hepatosplenomegaly, or serositis may indicate systemic JIA.
  • Failure to Thrive - Reflects chronic disease or systemic involvement.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Juvenile Idiopathic Arthritis (JIA)"][/sc]
  • Arthritis limited to small joints, which may mimic early rheumatoid arthritis.
  • Recurrent fevers without clear joint involvement, suggesting systemic onset.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Juvenile Idiopathic Arthritis (JIA)"][/sc]
  • ESR/CRP - Elevated due to systemic inflammation.
  • Rheumatoid Factor (RF) - Positive in a subset of polyarticular JIA.
  • ANA - Frequently positive in oligoarticular JIA and associated with uveitis risk.
  • Joint Imaging (Ultrasound/MRI) - Detects synovitis, effusion, and damage.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Juvenile Idiopathic Arthritis (JIA)"][/sc]
  • A 7-year-old girl presents with a 2-month history of joint pain and swelling, predominantly in the knees, associated with morning stiffness and fatigue. ESR is raised, and ANA is positive.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Juvenile Idiopathic Arthritis (JIA)"][/sc]
  • Rheumatic Fever - Differentiated by migratory arthritis and carditis.
  • Septic Arthritis - Acute onset with fever and single joint involvement.
  • Leukaemia - Associated with systemic symptoms like pallor, bruising, and hepatosplenomegaly.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Juvenile Idiopathic Arthritis (JIA)"][/sc]
  • First-line - NSAIDs for symptom control.
  • Second-line - Methotrexate or other DMARDs for disease modification.
  • Biologics - Anti-TNF agents for refractory cases.
  • Management of Uveitis - Topical corticosteroids and immunomodulators as needed.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Juvenile Idiopathic Arthritis (JIA)"][/sc]
  • Joint Destruction - From chronic uncontrolled inflammation.
  • Growth Retardation - Due to chronic disease or corticosteroid use.
  • Uveitis - Risk of blindness if untreated.
