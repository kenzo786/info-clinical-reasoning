Trauma-Induced Haematuria
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Trauma-Induced Haematuria"][/sc]
  • Trauma-induced haematuria is the presence of blood in the urine as a result of injury to the genitourinary tract. It is classified as either microscopic (detectable only on urinalysis) or macroscopic (visible to the naked eye). This condition can be caused by blunt or penetrating trauma to the kidneys, ureters, bladder, or urethra, and is often indicative of underlying tissue injury in these areas.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Trauma-Induced Haematuria"][/sc]
  • Prevalence in UK general practice is low, given that haematuria is generally more common in non-traumatic conditions.
  • In hospital settings, trauma-induced haematuria is frequently seen in A&E departments, particularly in cases involving road traffic accidents, sports injuries, or falls. It accounts for a significant proportion of genitourinary trauma presentations.
  • Globally, the incidence of trauma-induced haematuria varies by region, with higher rates in areas with increased vehicular or occupational hazards.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Trauma-Induced Haematuria"][/sc]
  • Trauma to the genitourinary organs causes mechanical disruption of blood vessels, leading to bleeding into the urinary tract. The location and extent of injury dictate whether haematuria is visible (gross) or only detectable microscopically.
  • In blunt trauma, kinetic energy transfer to the kidneys or bladder may result in contusion, laceration, or rupture. Penetrating trauma, such as from gunshots or sharp objects, directly injures blood vessels and tissue, leading to haemorrhage.
  • Other factors such as coagulation abnormalities, high blood pressure, or pre-existing kidney conditions can exacerbate bleeding.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Trauma-Induced Haematuria"][/sc]
  • Immediate phase: Haematuria may be present immediately following trauma due to initial bleeding.
  • Subacute phase: Haematuria typically reduces as tissue healing begins, generally within days to weeks depending on severity.
  • Chronic phase: Persistent haematuria beyond several weeks may indicate ongoing pathology, such as an unhealed injury or a secondary complication (e.g., infection).
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Trauma-Induced Haematuria"][/sc]
  • Visible blood in urine - Indicative of gross haematuria due to tissue injury causing bleeding into the urinary tract.
  • Pain in the flank, abdomen, or pelvis - Reflects the injury site (e.g., flank pain suggests renal involvement) and results from tissue damage and inflammation.
  • Urinary retention or dysuria - May occur if there is injury near the bladder or urethra, causing swelling or obstruction.
  • Asymptomatic (microscopic haematuria) - Trauma may only be detectable via urinalysis; common in mild or lower-grade injuries.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Trauma-Induced Haematuria"][/sc]
  • Haematuria on urinalysis - Presence of red blood cells confirms bleeding, typically from the urinary tract.
  • Flank or abdominal tenderness - Localised pain on palpation indicates potential renal or bladder injury.
  • Hypotension or tachycardia - Signs of haemorrhagic shock, suggesting significant blood loss in severe trauma cases.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Trauma-Induced Haematuria"][/sc]
  • Severe hypotension or shock - Indicates potential internal bleeding and haemorrhagic shock; requires urgent intervention.
  • Gross haematuria with flank pain - Suggests kidney injury; risk of severe renal damage or rupture.
  • Urinary obstruction or retention - May indicate severe trauma to the bladder or urethra, leading to urinary complications.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Trauma-Induced Haematuria"][/sc]
  • Delayed haematuria onset - Haematuria may present after initial trauma due to secondary bleeding or re-opening of vascular injury.
  • Isolated microscopic haematuria - Minimal bleeding without significant pain; may lead to underestimation of injury severity.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Trauma-Induced Haematuria"][/sc]
  • Urinalysis: Confirms presence of red blood cells; degree of haematuria helps estimate injury severity.
  • Imaging (CT KUB with contrast): Identifies anatomical injury location and severity; contrast highlights vascular damage.
  • Ultrasound: Used for initial assessment in haemodynamically unstable patients; identifies fluid collections or organ injury.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Trauma-Induced Haematuria"][/sc]
  • A 35-year-old male presents with gross haematuria, left flank pain, and hypotension following a road traffic accident.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Trauma-Induced Haematuria"][/sc]
  • Urinary Tract Infection (UTI): Typically presents with dysuria and pyuria; less likely post-trauma without a history of infection symptoms.
  • Nephrolithiasis: Causes haematuria and flank pain but is associated with a history of renal colic and no recent trauma.
  • Bladder cancer: Presents with painless haematuria, often in older patients; usually a chronic condition.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Trauma-Induced Haematuria"][/sc]
  • Initial stabilisation: Address shock symptoms with IV fluids or blood transfusion.
  • Surgical intervention: Required for severe injuries such as renal or bladder rupture; guidelines recommend imaging to guide intervention decisions.
  • Conservative management: Observation for minor injuries; bed rest and monitoring for signs of bleeding.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Trauma-Induced Haematuria"][/sc]
  • Haemorrhagic shock: Severe bleeding can lead to life-threatening blood pressure drops and requires urgent resuscitation.
  • Chronic renal impairment: Secondary to unrecognised or poorly managed renal trauma; may result in long-term kidney damage.
  • Urinary tract infection: Secondary infection can occur if there is damage to the bladder or urethra.
