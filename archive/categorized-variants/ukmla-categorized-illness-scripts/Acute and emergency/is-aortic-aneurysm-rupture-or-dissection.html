Aortic Aneurysm (Rupture or Dissection)
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Aortic Aneurysm (Rupture or Dissection)"][/sc]
  • Aortic aneurysm refers to an abnormal, localized dilation of the aorta, with a diameter 1.5 times the expected normal diameter, resulting from weakening of the aortic wall.
  • Rupture occurs when the aneurysm wall breaks, leading to life-threatening haemorrhage.
  • Aortic dissection involves a tear in the aortic intima, allowing blood to flow between the layers of the aortic wall, creating a false lumen.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Aortic Aneurysm (Rupture or Dissection)"][/sc]
  • Prevalence in UK GP population: Approximately 1-2% in adults over 65 years, more common in males.
  • UK hospital prevalence: Aneurysm rupture and dissection account for around 2.5 per 100,000 person-years for hospital admissions, with a higher incidence in males over 60.
  • Worldwide prevalence: Similar prevalence with aortic aneurysms affecting roughly 3-6% of the global population over 65.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Aortic Aneurysm (Rupture or Dissection)"][/sc]
  • Aortic wall weakening occurs due to chronic hypertension, genetic connective tissue disorders (e.g., Marfan syndrome), or degeneration with ageing.
  • The weakened aorta expands, creating an aneurysm prone to rupture under increased pressure.
  • In aortic dissection, a tear in the intima allows blood to penetrate the aortic wall, forming a false lumen that can impede blood flow, increase wall stress, and potentially cause rupture.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Aortic Aneurysm (Rupture or Dissection)"][/sc]
  • Asymptomatic phase: Aneurysms grow slowly and may remain asymptomatic for years.
  • Acute phase (rupture or dissection): Sudden severe symptoms develop, often life-threatening.
  • Post-rupture phase: Without intervention, fatality is high within hours due to haemorrhagic shock; with intervention, recovery can occur, though morbidity remains high.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Aortic Aneurysm (Rupture or Dissection)"][/sc]
  • Sudden severe chest or back pain - Often described as "tearing" or "ripping"; occurs due to acute wall stress and tearing in the case of dissection.
  • Abdominal pain - Common in abdominal aortic aneurysms (AAA), due to pressure on surrounding structures.
  • Syncope - Caused by hypovolemic shock from rapid blood loss in rupture or by compromised blood flow in dissection.
  • Dyspnoea - Can occur if the thoracic aneurysm compresses the airway.
  • Asymptomatic - Many aortic aneurysms are asymptomatic until they enlarge significantly or rupture.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Aortic Aneurysm (Rupture or Dissection)"][/sc]
  • Hypotension - Seen in rupture due to blood loss; hypovolaemic shock.
  • Pulsatile abdominal mass - Often found in AAA; represents the aneurysm itself.
  • Asymmetrical blood pressure - In dissection, pressure differences in limbs can result from impaired blood flow.
  • Tachycardia - A response to blood loss or pain.
  • Absent peripheral pulses - In dissection, distal blood flow can be blocked.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Aortic Aneurysm (Rupture or Dissection)"][/sc]
  • Severe, sudden tearing chest or back pain - Suggests aortic dissection; urgent assessment required.
  • Hypotension with pulsatile mass - Indicates likely aneurysm rupture, requiring immediate intervention.
  • Neurological deficits - May indicate dissection affecting aortic branches supplying the brain.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Aortic Aneurysm (Rupture or Dissection)"][/sc]
  • Gradual pain - A less typical presentation that may delay diagnosis; often in chronic dissection.
  • Respiratory symptoms - If the aneurysm compresses airway structures, respiratory distress can dominate the presentation.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Aortic Aneurysm (Rupture or Dissection)"][/sc]
  • CT Angiography - Gold standard; shows aortic dilation, dissection flaps, and haemorrhage; used to confirm diagnosis.
  • Ultrasound - Common for AAA screening; shows size and risk of rupture.
  • ECG - Rules out myocardial infarction, which can present similarly.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Aortic Aneurysm (Rupture or Dissection)"][/sc]
  • A 65-year-old male with sudden onset, severe tearing chest pain radiating to the back, hypotension, and a history of hypertension, presenting in acute distress.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Aortic Aneurysm (Rupture or Dissection)"][/sc]
  • Myocardial infarction - Pain is more constant and associated with ECG changes.
  • Pulmonary embolism - Sudden pleuritic pain and dyspnoea without tearing quality, associated with hypoxia.
  • Pericarditis - Sharp, positional pain relieved by sitting up, usually without hypotension.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Aortic Aneurysm (Rupture or Dissection)"][/sc]
  • Stabilisation - Includes blood pressure control, IV fluids for hypotension, and oxygen support.
  • Surgical repair - Urgent for rupture or dissection; options include endovascular repair or open surgery.
  • Medication - Long-term beta-blockers to reduce shear stress on the aorta; statins to lower vascular risk.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Aortic Aneurysm (Rupture or Dissection)"][/sc]
  • Haemorrhagic shock - Frequent complication of rupture; leads to multiorgan failure if untreated.
  • Organ ischaemia - Resulting from compromised blood flow in dissection; can cause myocardial, renal, or cerebral infarctions.
  • Death - High mortality rate, especially in untreated ruptures and dissections.
