Cluster Headache
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Cluster Headache"][/sc]
  • Cluster headache is a primary headache disorder characterised by recurrent, unilateral attacks of severe orbital, supraorbital, or temporal pain, often accompanied by ipsilateral autonomic symptoms (e.g., lacrimation, nasal congestion). These headaches typically occur in clusters (bouts) over a period of weeks or months, followed by remission periods. The attacks last 15 minutes to 3 hours and occur multiple times a day, often at the same time of day.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Cluster Headache"][/sc]
  • UK GP population prevalence: Approximately 1 in 1,000.
  • UK hospital prevalence: Cluster headache accounts for about 0.1% of neurology outpatient visits.
  • Worldwide prevalence: 0.1-0.2% of the global population, with a male-to-female ratio of about 4:1. Most commonly presents in males aged 20-50 years.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Cluster Headache"][/sc]
  • Cluster headaches are believed to originate from the hypothalamus, which plays a role in circadian rhythms. Dysfunctional hypothalamic activation triggers the trigeminal-autonomic reflex, leading to pain via activation of the trigeminal nerve and associated autonomic symptoms through the parasympathetic system.
  • Neurotransmitter changes, particularly involving serotonin and calcitonin gene-related peptide (CGRP), also play a role in the pathogenesis of cluster headaches.
  • Circadian periodicity is thought to arise from abnormal hypothalamic control, leading to consistent daily attacks during active bouts.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Cluster Headache"][/sc]
  • Cluster headaches typically present in episodic forms (about 85% of cases). Patients experience daily attacks for 4-12 weeks (the "cluster period") followed by months or even years of remission.
  • Each headache attack lasts from 15 minutes to 3 hours, and attacks can occur up to 8 times per day, often at similar times each day.
  • Chronic cluster headaches occur in 10-15% of patients, where remission periods are either absent or last less than 1 month in a year.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Cluster Headache"][/sc]
  • Severe unilateral orbital or temporal pain - This hallmark symptom is caused by activation of the trigeminal nerve, leading to intense pain in the distribution of the ophthalmic branch.
  • Lacrimation - Caused by parasympathetic hyperactivity, leading to overproduction of tears from the lacrimal gland.
  • Nasal congestion or rhinorrhoea - Nasal symptoms result from parasympathetic activation affecting the nasal mucosa.
  • Ptosis - Caused by dysfunction of the sympathetic nerves controlling the upper eyelid muscles.
  • Restlessness or agitation - Patients often describe a sense of unease or discomfort during attacks, attributed to the severity of pain.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Cluster Headache"][/sc]
  • Ipsilateral lacrimation - Visible tearing in the affected eye, due to parasympathetic overactivity.
  • Conjunctival injection - Reddening of the affected eye caused by vasodilation of the conjunctival vessels.
  • Horner's syndrome - Characterised by ptosis, miosis, and anhidrosis, reflecting sympathetic disruption.
  • Facial sweating - Seen on the affected side, related to parasympathetic overactivity.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Cluster Headache"][/sc]
  • New onset headache in patients over 50 - Raises concern for secondary causes such as giant cell arteritis or brain tumours.
  • Fever or signs of systemic infection - May suggest meningitis or intracranial abscess.
  • Altered mental status - May indicate a neurological emergency such as stroke or encephalitis.
  • Prolonged visual changes - Could indicate optic nerve compression or vascular occlusion.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Cluster Headache"][/sc]
  • Bilateral cluster headache - Although cluster headaches are typically unilateral, some patients experience bilateral pain, which can be mistaken for other headache syndromes.
  • Absence of autonomic features - A minority of patients may present without typical parasympathetic symptoms, making the diagnosis more challenging.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Cluster Headache"][/sc]
  • Clinical Diagnosis - Cluster headache is primarily diagnosed based on history and clinical criteria. No specific biomarker or test is available.
  • MRI Brain - Recommended to exclude structural causes such as tumours or vascular malformations in cases with atypical presentations or red flag features.
  • CT Head - Sometimes used in acute settings to rule out other causes of headache, such as subarachnoid haemorrhage.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Cluster Headache"][/sc]
  • A 35-year-old male presents with excruciating right-sided orbital pain lasting 1 hour, occurring up to 4 times per day for the past 3 weeks, accompanied by tearing and nasal congestion. The episodes occur at the same time daily and are relieved by pacing.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Cluster Headache"][/sc]
  • Migraine - Typically unilateral but associated with a longer duration (4-72 hours), throbbing pain, photophobia, and phonophobia.
  • Trigeminal neuralgia - Presents with sharp, electric shock-like pain but lacks the autonomic features seen in cluster headache and is triggered by sensory stimuli.
  • Paroxysmal hemicrania - Similar to cluster headache but shorter-lasting attacks (2-30 minutes) and responds completely to indomethacin.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Cluster Headache"][/sc]
  • Acute treatment: High-flow oxygen therapy (12-15 L/min for 15 minutes) and subcutaneous sumatriptan are first-line treatments.
  • Prophylactic treatment: Verapamil is the first-line preventive therapy in the UK. Alternatives include lithium or topiramate.
  • Invasive treatments: Occipital nerve stimulation or deep brain stimulation may be considered for refractory chronic cases.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Cluster Headache"][/sc]
  • Medication overuse headache - Frequent use of abortive medications can lead to chronic daily headaches.
  • Psychological impact - The severe and disabling nature of cluster headaches may lead to depression and anxiety in affected individuals.
  • Intractable pain - Chronic cases may become resistant to treatment, leading to a significant reduction in quality of life.
