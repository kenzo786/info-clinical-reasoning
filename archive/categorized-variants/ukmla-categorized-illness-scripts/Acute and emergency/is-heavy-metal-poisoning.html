Heavy Metal Poisoning
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Heavy Metal Poisoning"][/sc]
  • Heavy metal poisoning refers to the toxic accumulation of heavy metals such as lead, mercury, arsenic, or cadmium in the body. It can occur acutely or chronically due to occupational exposure, environmental contamination, or ingestion. The condition primarily affects organ systems such as the nervous, gastrointestinal, and renal systems.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Heavy Metal Poisoning"][/sc]
  • Prevalence in the UK GP population is relatively low, primarily affecting individuals with occupational exposures (e.g., industrial workers).
  • Hospital presentations are uncommon but can be seen in cases of acute poisoning or severe chronic toxicity.
  • Worldwide, heavy metal poisoning is more prevalent in areas with less environmental regulation, affecting millions in developing countries, particularly in mining and agricultural regions.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Heavy Metal Poisoning"][/sc]
  • Heavy metals interfere with cellular metabolism by binding to proteins and enzymes, leading to disruption of critical biological processes. They can generate oxidative stress, damage DNA, and impair mitochondrial function. Long-term exposure leads to cumulative damage, especially in high metabolic tissues like the brain and kidneys.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Heavy Metal Poisoning"][/sc]
  • Acute poisoning presents within hours to days of exposure with rapid onset of symptoms such as vomiting, abdominal pain, and confusion.
  • Chronic poisoning develops over months or years, often with insidious onset of non-specific symptoms like fatigue, irritability, and cognitive decline.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Heavy Metal Poisoning"][/sc]
  • Abdominal pain - Common due to the toxic effects of metals on the gastrointestinal tract.
  • Neurological symptoms - Tremors, confusion, and irritability are due to neurotoxicity and disrupted neurotransmitter function, particularly in mercury and lead poisoning.
  • Fatigue - Chronic poisoning can cause fatigue due to mitochondrial dysfunction and oxidative stress.
  • Headache - Likely due to direct neurotoxic effects or metabolic derangements.
  • Nausea and vomiting - Typically an acute reaction to gastrointestinal irritation by ingested metals.
  • Renal symptoms - Metals like cadmium can accumulate in kidneys, leading to nephrotoxicity.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Heavy Metal Poisoning"][/sc]
  • Tremors - Seen particularly in mercury poisoning due to disruption of central nervous system activity.
  • Blue lines on gums (lead lines) - Lead poisoning often causes this sign due to deposition of lead in tissues, including the gums.
  • Peripheral neuropathy - Characterised by weakness or paraesthesia, often seen in lead or arsenic poisoning due to axonal degeneration.
  • Hypertension - Chronic lead exposure can cause vascular damage, leading to elevated blood pressure.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Heavy Metal Poisoning"][/sc]
  • Encephalopathy - Can signify severe neurotoxicity and requires urgent intervention.
  • Renal failure - Indicates nephrotoxicity, especially in cadmium or lead poisoning, and may lead to permanent kidney damage if untreated.
  • Severe respiratory distress - Seen in acute inhalational mercury poisoning, indicating pulmonary toxicity.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Heavy Metal Poisoning"][/sc]
  • Children may present with developmental delay or behavioural issues due to chronic low-level exposure to lead.
  • Individuals with occupational exposure may have vague, non-specific symptoms such as headaches or fatigue, which may be missed or attributed to other causes.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Heavy Metal Poisoning"][/sc]
  • Blood lead levels - Elevated in cases of lead poisoning; levels above 10 Î¼g/dL are concerning.
  • Urine mercury levels - Indicates mercury exposure; elevated levels correlate with toxicity.
  • Renal function tests - Elevated creatinine or reduced glomerular filtration rate (GFR) may indicate nephrotoxicity.
  • Complete blood count (CBC) - Microcytic anaemia with basophilic stippling suggests lead poisoning.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Heavy Metal Poisoning"][/sc]
  • A 45-year-old male industrial worker presents with several months of fatigue, irritability, and abdominal pain. He also has hypertension and recently developed tremors.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Heavy Metal Poisoning"][/sc]
  • Alcohol withdrawal - May present with tremors and irritability, but lacks the history of metal exposure and would show improvement over time with abstinence.
  • Chronic kidney disease - May cause fatigue and hypertension, but lacks the neurological symptoms seen in heavy metal poisoning.
  • Thyrotoxicosis - Can cause irritability and tremors, but would present with other signs like weight loss and heat intolerance.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Heavy Metal Poisoning"][/sc]
  • Chelation therapy - Agents like dimercaprol or EDTA bind heavy metals and facilitate their excretion.
  • Supportive care - Intravenous fluids, electrolyte management, and monitoring of renal and hepatic function.
  • Elimination of exposure - Removal from the source of heavy metal exposure is crucial to prevent further toxicity.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Heavy Metal Poisoning"][/sc]
  • Chronic renal failure - Heavy metals can cause progressive renal damage, leading to permanent loss of kidney function.
  • Neurological damage - Persistent cognitive deficits or peripheral neuropathy may remain even after the removal of the heavy metal.
  • Cancer - Some heavy metals, such as arsenic, are carcinogenic, increasing the risk of lung and bladder cancer.
