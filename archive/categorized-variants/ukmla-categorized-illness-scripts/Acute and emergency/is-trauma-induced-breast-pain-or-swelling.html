Trauma-induced Breast Pain or Swelling
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Trauma-induced Breast Pain or Swelling"][/sc]
  • Trauma-induced breast pain or swelling refers to discomfort or oedema of the breast tissue following direct physical trauma. It may be accompanied by bruising, tenderness, and localised inflammation. This condition typically results from sports injuries, accidents, or blunt force impacts to the chest area and may present with or without visible tissue damage. The severity can range from mild and self-limiting to more intense and associated with internal breast haematoma or fat necrosis.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Trauma-induced Breast Pain or Swelling"][/sc]
  • Prevalence among the UK GP population: Incidence is low; minor breast trauma cases are often managed at home and may go unreported.
  • UK hospital prevalence: Minor cases are uncommon in hospital settings, while severe cases or cases requiring imaging or surgical intervention are rare but seen in emergency settings.
  • Worldwide prevalence: Similar patterns worldwide; minor trauma to the breast may be common, but hospitalised cases are relatively rare.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Trauma-induced Breast Pain or Swelling"][/sc]
  • Trauma to the breast leads to localised soft tissue injury. The impact may cause rupturing of small blood vessels, leading to haematoma formation and, in some cases, fat necrosis due to the disruption of adipose tissue.
  • Bruising and oedema develop as a result of inflammatory responses. Damaged cells release cytokines, causing localised pain, swelling, and increased blood flow to the area, leading to erythema and tenderness.
  • In severe cases, a substantial haematoma may develop, leading to more intense and prolonged pain and discomfort, and occasionally creating a palpable lump in the breast.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Trauma-induced Breast Pain or Swelling"][/sc]
  • Immediate: Acute pain and tenderness typically arise immediately following the trauma, with possible erythema.
  • Early phase (0-48 hours): Bruising and swelling become more evident as localised blood vessels continue to leak blood into the surrounding tissues.
  • Resolution (3-7 days): In minor cases, pain and swelling gradually resolve with conservative treatment. Bruising may take longer (1-2 weeks) to completely resolve.
  • Chronic phase (>7 days): In cases with haematoma or fat necrosis, a palpable lump may persist for weeks to months and require follow-up imaging.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Trauma-induced Breast Pain or Swelling"][/sc]
  • Pain - Immediate sharp or throbbing pain at the trauma site due to tissue damage and inflammation.
  • Swelling - Develops as inflammatory processes cause oedema around the injured area.
  • Bruising - Ecchymosis results from the rupture of small blood vessels, usually visible within 24-48 hours post-injury.
  • Tenderness - The injured tissue becomes painful on palpation due to localised inflammation.
  • Palpable lump - A lump may develop due to haematoma or fat necrosis, often noted if the trauma was severe.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Trauma-induced Breast Pain or Swelling"][/sc]
  • Ecchymosis - Discolouration resulting from subcutaneous bleeding, generally appearing within 24 hours post-trauma.
  • Swelling - Localised oedema due to inflammatory responses and increased vascular permeability.
  • Tenderness on palpation - The affected area is usually tender to touch due to pain from inflammation.
  • Palpable mass - A firm or tender lump may be detected if a haematoma or fat necrosis develops.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Trauma-induced Breast Pain or Swelling"][/sc]
  • Rapid increase in breast size - Could indicate an expanding haematoma, potentially requiring surgical intervention.
  • Non-resolving lump - Persisting mass after trauma may signify fat necrosis or, in rare cases, underlying pathology like breast cancer that was incidentally found.
  • Systemic symptoms (fever, erythema, warmth) - Possible signs of superimposed infection or breast abscess formation.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Trauma-induced Breast Pain or Swelling"][/sc]
  • Absence of ecchymosis - In deeper trauma cases, bruising may not be visible externally, leading to underestimation of injury.
  • Delayed lump formation - A lump may appear weeks after the trauma due to fat necrosis, potentially causing diagnostic confusion.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Trauma-induced Breast Pain or Swelling"][/sc]
  • Ultrasound - First-line imaging to assess haematoma, fat necrosis, or underlying structural abnormalities.
  • Mammogram - May be used in women over 40 to rule out concurrent malignancies if a persistent mass is present.
  • Magnetic Resonance Imaging (MRI) - Used selectively for detailed imaging, especially for complex or unresolved cases.
  • Biopsy - Performed if there is concern for malignancy, especially if imaging reveals suspicious features.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Trauma-induced Breast Pain or Swelling"][/sc]
  • A 35-year-old woman presents with acute right breast pain and swelling following a blunt injury sustained during a sports activity. Examination reveals tenderness, ecchymosis, and mild oedema.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Trauma-induced Breast Pain or Swelling"][/sc]
  • Mastitis - Typically presents with fever, erythema, warmth, and diffuse tenderness, often seen in breastfeeding women.
  • Breast abscess - Localised painful mass with signs of infection (erythema, warmth, fever), often in the context of untreated mastitis.
  • Breast cancer - Hard, immovable mass often without pain or recent trauma; may require imaging for differentiation.
  • Cyst - Palpable, tender mass but often non-tender and not trauma-related; cyst fluid on aspiration can confirm diagnosis.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Trauma-induced Breast Pain or Swelling"][/sc]
  • Conservative management - Includes rest, ice application, and analgesics such as paracetamol or ibuprofen for pain relief.
  • Monitoring - Follow-up if a palpable lump persists, ensuring no suspicion of malignancy or ongoing haematoma.
  • Surgical intervention - Required in rare cases of large, expanding haematomas or infected haematomas requiring drainage.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Trauma-induced Breast Pain or Swelling"][/sc]
  • Fat necrosis - Occurs when damaged adipose tissue undergoes necrosis; often presents as a persistent lump and may resemble malignancy on imaging.
  • Haematoma infection - Rare but possible; can lead to abscess formation requiring drainage and antibiotics.
  • Chronic pain - Can occur if tissue damage and inflammation are prolonged, especially with significant haematoma.
