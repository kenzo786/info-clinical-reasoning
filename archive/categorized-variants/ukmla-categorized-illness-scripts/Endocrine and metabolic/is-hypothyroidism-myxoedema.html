Hypothyroidism (Myxoedema)
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Hypothyroidism (Myxoedema)"][/sc]
  • Hypothyroidism is a condition characterised by insufficient production of thyroid hormones (thyroxine - T4 and triiodothyronine - T3) by the thyroid gland. It leads to a generalised slowing of metabolic processes. Severe, untreated hypothyroidism, also known as myxoedema, can be life-threatening and involves multi-organ dysfunction.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Hypothyroidism (Myxoedema)"][/sc]
  • In the UK, hypothyroidism affects approximately 2-5% of the general population, with a higher prevalence in women and older adults.
  • Worldwide, the prevalence is estimated at around 3-8% of the population.
  • Primary hypothyroidism, caused by thyroid gland dysfunction, accounts for the majority of cases in primary care, while hospitalised cases often involve secondary causes related to pituitary or hypothalamic disease.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Hypothyroidism (Myxoedema)"][/sc]
  • In hypothyroidism, there is reduced synthesis of thyroid hormones (T4 and T3). In primary hypothyroidism, this is due to intrinsic thyroid gland dysfunction (e.g., autoimmune thyroiditis, iodine deficiency). In secondary or tertiary hypothyroidism, there is reduced stimulation of the thyroid gland by thyroid-stimulating hormone (TSH) due to pituitary or hypothalamic dysfunction.
  • This hormone deficiency leads to a reduced basal metabolic rate, which in turn affects multiple systems such as cardiovascular (bradycardia), musculoskeletal (weakness), gastrointestinal (constipation), and neurological (cognitive slowing).
  • The accumulation of glycosaminoglycans in the skin and tissues results in myxoedema, causing thickened skin and non-pitting oedema.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Hypothyroidism (Myxoedema)"][/sc]
  • Hypothyroidism often develops insidiously over months to years. Initially, symptoms are mild or asymptomatic, but as hormone levels decline, systemic features become more apparent.
  • In untreated or severe cases, hypothyroidism can progress to myxoedema coma, a rare but life-threatening condition characterised by hypothermia, bradycardia, and altered mental state.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Hypothyroidism (Myxoedema)"][/sc]
  • Fatigue - Fatigue is the most common symptom, caused by a reduced metabolic rate affecting energy production and muscle function.
  • Weight gain - Due to decreased basal metabolic rate, energy expenditure is reduced, leading to weight gain despite unchanged caloric intake.
  • Cold intolerance - Reduced thermogenesis from lower thyroid hormone levels results in difficulty tolerating cold temperatures.
  • Constipation - Slowed gastrointestinal motility due to reduced smooth muscle activity leads to constipation.
  • Depression - Neuropsychiatric symptoms such as depression occur due to decreased cerebral metabolic activity and neurotransmitter imbalance.
  • Memory loss - Cognitive slowing and impaired memory arise from reduced neuronal metabolism and synaptic activity.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Hypothyroidism (Myxoedema)"][/sc]
  • Bradycardia - Slowed heart rate due to decreased sympathetic activity and reduced sensitivity of adrenergic receptors.
  • Non-pitting oedema (myxoedema) - Accumulation of glycosaminoglycans in tissues causes thickening of the skin, particularly around the face and extremities.
  • Dry, coarse skin - Reduced sebaceous and sweat gland activity leads to dry, thickened skin.
  • Alopecia - Thinning hair and loss of outer eyebrows are due to impaired keratin production and reduced hair follicle turnover.
  • Delayed reflexes - Prolonged relaxation phase of deep tendon reflexes occurs due to impaired muscle relaxation and conduction velocity.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Hypothyroidism (Myxoedema)"][/sc]
  • Altered mental status - May indicate myxoedema coma, a life-threatening condition requiring urgent intervention.
  • Hypothermia - Signifies severe hypothyroidism, often present in myxoedema coma, due to impaired thermoregulation.
  • Bradycardia and hypotension - These can indicate cardiovascular collapse in myxoedema coma.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Hypothyroidism (Myxoedema)"][/sc]
  • Older adults may present primarily with cognitive decline or depression, without the classic metabolic symptoms.
  • Some patients may exhibit subclinical hypothyroidism, with elevated TSH levels but normal free T4, leading to subtle or absent symptoms.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Hypothyroidism (Myxoedema)"][/sc]
  • Serum TSH - Elevated in primary hypothyroidism (normal range: 0.4-4.0 mU/L). TSH is the most sensitive marker of thyroid dysfunction.
  • Free T4 - Low in overt hypothyroidism (normal range: 9-25 pmol/L), confirming decreased thyroid hormone production.

Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Hypothyroidism (Myxoedema)"][/sc]
  • A 55-year-old woman presents with a 6-month history of fatigue, weight gain, cold intolerance, and constipation. Examination reveals bradycardia and non-pitting oedema. Blood tests show elevated TSH and low free T4.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Hypothyroidism (Myxoedema)"][/sc]
  • Depression - Similar fatigue and weight gain, but lacks other endocrine features like bradycardia and myxoedema.
  • Chronic fatigue syndrome - Overlaps with fatigue, but lacks specific thyroid abnormalities and typically shows post-exertional malaise.
  • Anaemia - Can cause fatigue and pallor, but would show low haemoglobin, rather than thyroid hormone abnormalities.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Hypothyroidism (Myxoedema)"][/sc]
  • Levothyroxine - First-line treatment, dosed to normalise TSH levels. Starting dose varies by age and comorbidities, typically 50-100 mcg daily, adjusted based on regular TSH monitoring.
  • For myxoedema coma, intravenous levothyroxine and supportive care (e.g., warming, fluids) are required.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Hypothyroidism (Myxoedema)"][/sc]
  • Cardiovascular disease - Untreated hypothyroidism increases the risk of atherosclerosis and coronary artery disease due to altered lipid metabolism.
  • Myxoedema coma - A severe complication characterised by altered mental status, hypothermia, and cardiovascular collapse, requiring emergency treatment.
  • Infertility - Hypothyroidism can cause menstrual irregularities and ovulatory dysfunction, leading to infertility.
