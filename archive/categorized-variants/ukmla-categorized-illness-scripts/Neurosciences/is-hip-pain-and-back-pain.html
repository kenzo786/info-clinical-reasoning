Hip Pain and Back Pain
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Hip Pain and Back Pain"][/sc]
  • Hip pain and back pain are common musculoskeletal complaints that may originate from multiple structures, including bones, muscles, ligaments, tendons, nerves, and internal organs. Hip pain often involves the hip joint or its periarticular structures, while back pain can stem from the spine, paraspinal muscles, or referred sources. Both conditions frequently overlap in clinical presentations.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Hip Pain and Back Pain"][/sc]
  • Pain localised to the hip or lower back - Results from inflammation, trauma, or degenerative changes in musculoskeletal structures. Osteoarthritis or disc herniation are common causes.
  • Referred pain - Pain may radiate to the thigh or groin due to nerve involvement, such as lumbar radiculopathy or hip joint pathology.
  • Stiffness - Common in degenerative conditions like osteoarthritis or ankylosing spondylitis, due to inflammation or joint deformity.
  • Difficulty in walking or weight-bearing - Suggests severe joint pathology, fractures, or significant inflammation.
  • Neurological symptoms (e.g., numbness, weakness) - Indicates potential nerve compression, such as from spinal stenosis or disc prolapse.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Hip Pain and Back Pain"][/sc]
  • Tenderness on palpation - Indicates localised inflammation or trauma to the hip or paraspinal regions.
  • Reduced range of motion - Suggests structural joint abnormalities or inflammation (e.g., arthritis).
  • Positive straight leg raise test - Indicates nerve root irritation, commonly seen in lumbar radiculopathy.
  • Postural abnormalities - Common in scoliosis, kyphosis, or muscle spasm due to back pain.
  • Neurological deficits - Such as decreased reflexes or muscle weakness, suggest nerve involvement.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Hip Pain and Back Pain"][/sc]
  • Unexplained weight loss - May indicate malignancy or systemic disease.
  • Fever or night sweats - Suggests infection such as septic arthritis or spinal abscess.
  • Severe, progressive neurological deficits - Points to potential cauda equina syndrome or spinal cord compression.
  • Persistent pain at night - Often associated with malignancies or serious pathologies like ankylosing spondylitis.
  • History of trauma - Raises concern for fractures, especially in osteoporotic patients.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Hip Pain and Back Pain"][/sc]
  • Hip pain without mechanical symptoms - May indicate referred pain from intra-abdominal or pelvic sources.
  • Back pain with urinary retention - Suggests cauda equina syndrome, a surgical emergency.
  • Transient or migratory pain - Can be seen in inflammatory conditions such as rheumatoid arthritis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Hip Pain and Back Pain"][/sc]
  • X-ray of hip/spine - Shows degenerative changes, fractures, or alignment abnormalities.
  • MRI - Identifies soft tissue injuries, nerve compression, or malignancies.
  • Blood tests (CRP, ESR) - Elevated levels indicate inflammation or infection.
  • Nerve conduction studies - Evaluate nerve involvement in cases of radiculopathy.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Hip Pain and Back Pain"][/sc]

A 55-year-old male presents with a 6-month history of progressive hip and back pain, stiffness worse in the morning, and occasional radiating pain to the left thigh. Examination reveals limited lumbar flexion and positive straight leg raise.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Hip Pain and Back Pain"][/sc]
  • Osteoarthritis - Predominantly affects the hip, with pain worsened by activity.
  • Disc herniation - Back pain with radiculopathy and neurological findings.
  • Ankylosing spondylitis - Morning stiffness and improvement with exercise.
  • Hip fracture - Acute pain with inability to bear weight, often post-trauma.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Hip Pain and Back Pain"][/sc]
  • Conservative management - Physiotherapy, analgesia (paracetamol, NSAIDs), and activity modification.
  • Surgical intervention - Hip replacement for severe osteoarthritis or decompression for nerve root compression.
  • Anti-inflammatory treatments - DMARDs for inflammatory conditions like ankylosing spondylitis.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Hip Pain and Back Pain"][/sc]
  • Chronic pain - Often due to untreated or advanced degenerative changes.
  • Neurological deficits - From prolonged nerve compression or spinal stenosis.
  • Impaired mobility - Results from severe joint or spinal pathologies.
