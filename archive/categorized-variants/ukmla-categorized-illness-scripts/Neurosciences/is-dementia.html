Dementia
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Dementia"][/sc]
  • Dementia is a clinical syndrome characterised by a progressive decline in cognitive function severe enough to interfere with daily life. It encompasses multiple domains such as memory, language, visuospatial skills, executive function, and emotional regulation. The condition is caused by a variety of neurodegenerative diseases, with Alzheimerâ€™s disease being the most common cause.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Dementia"][/sc]
  • Prevalence in UK GP population: Around 1.3% of patients registered with GPs have a diagnosis of dementia.
  • Prevalence in UK hospitals: Dementia affects around 25% of hospital inpatients aged 65 and older.
  • Worldwide prevalence: Approximately 50 million people globally live with dementia, and this is expected to triple by 2050.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Dementia"][/sc]
  • Dementia results from the progressive degeneration of neurons in key brain regions responsible for cognitive processes. In Alzheimer's disease, accumulation of beta-amyloid plaques and tau protein tangles disrupt synaptic communication and lead to neuronal death. In vascular dementia, chronic reduced blood flow leads to ischaemic damage. In Lewy body dementia, misfolded alpha-synuclein proteins form inclusions within neurons, impairing function. Other forms, such as frontotemporal dementia, involve selective degeneration of the frontal and temporal lobes.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Dementia"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Mild memory problems and subtle cognitive impairments (e.g. difficulty with complex tasks or planning).
  • Middle phase: Worsening memory, language deficits, impaired judgement, and functional decline (e.g. getting lost in familiar places).
  • Late phase: Severe cognitive and functional impairment, loss of independence, personality changes, and difficulty with basic self-care.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Dementia"][/sc]
  • Memory loss - Often the first symptom, especially affecting short-term memory due to hippocampal degeneration in Alzheimerâ€™s disease.
  • Disorientation - Loss of spatial and temporal awareness, associated with parietal lobe involvement.
  • Language impairment - Difficulty finding words or following conversations, linked to damage in the temporal lobe.
  • Difficulty with problem-solving and planning - Executive dysfunction arises due to frontal lobe involvement, affecting judgement and decision-making.
  • Personality changes - Irritability, apathy, or socially inappropriate behaviour due to frontal lobe atrophy, common in frontotemporal dementia.
  • Visual hallucinations - More common in Lewy body dementia, likely due to occipital lobe involvement.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Dementia"][/sc]
  • Cognitive impairment - Deficits in memory, attention, and language, assessed via tools like the MMSE (Mini-Mental State Examination).
  • Altered gait - Seen in vascular dementia and Lewy body dementia due to basal ganglia involvement, leading to parkinsonism.
  • Myoclonus - Brief, involuntary muscle jerks, often noted in late-stage Alzheimerâ€™s disease.
  • Rigidity - Common in Lewy body dementia, reflecting parkinsonian features.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Dementia"][/sc]
  • Rapid progression - Suggestive of prion disease or an underlying malignancy rather than typical neurodegenerative dementias.
  • Focal neurological deficits - May indicate vascular dementia or a mass lesion rather than Alzheimerâ€™s disease.
  • Severe behavioural disturbances - Early, severe personality changes may indicate frontotemporal dementia.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Dementia"][/sc]
  • Early-onset dementia (before 65) - Presents with greater behavioural or language symptoms and may be misdiagnosed as depression or stress.
  • Misattributed memory loss - Mild symptoms can be dismissed as "normal ageing," delaying diagnosis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Dementia"][/sc]
  • Cognitive screening tests - MMSE (score < 24/30 suggests dementia), MoCA (Montreal Cognitive Assessment), or Addenbrooke's Cognitive Examination.
  • Brain imaging (CT/MRI) - Shows cerebral atrophy in Alzheimerâ€™s, white matter lesions in vascular dementia, or frontal lobe atrophy in frontotemporal dementia.
  • Blood tests - Exclude reversible causes like vitamin B12 deficiency, thyroid dysfunction, or infections (e.g., syphilis, HIV).
  • CSF analysis - May reveal biomarkers such as tau protein in Alzheimerâ€™s disease.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Dementia"][/sc]
  • A 75-year-old female presents with a 2-year history of progressive memory loss, disorientation, and difficulty managing daily tasks. She now struggles with conversations and becomes easily lost even in familiar places.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Dementia"][/sc]
  • Delirium - Acute onset, fluctuating course, and reversible with treatment of the underlying cause (e.g., infection or metabolic disturbance), differentiates it from dementia.
  • Depression - Also called pseudodementia; presents with cognitive complaints but lacks true cognitive deficits on formal testing.
  • Normal pressure hydrocephalus - Triad of cognitive impairment, urinary incontinence, and gait disturbance, identified by enlarged ventricles on imaging.
  • Parkinsonâ€™s disease dementia - Motor symptoms predominate early, with cognitive decline occurring later in the disease course.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Dementia"][/sc]
  • Non-pharmacological - Cognitive stimulation therapy, memory aids, and caregiver support. Patient-centred care is essential.
  • Pharmacological - Acetylcholinesterase inhibitors (e.g., donepezil, rivastigmine) are first-line for Alzheimerâ€™s disease. Memantine, an NMDA receptor antagonist, may be used for moderate-to-severe cases.
  • Managing behavioural symptoms - Antipsychotics may be used short-term for agitation, though caution is advised due to increased mortality in dementia patients.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Dementia"][/sc]
  • Functional decline - Inability to perform basic activities of daily living, leading to dependence.
  • Falls - Common in advanced stages due to poor coordination, leading to fractures or head injuries.
  • Infections - Aspiration pneumonia or urinary tract infections are common due to immobility and reduced self-care.
  • Death - Dementia is a terminal condition, with the median survival being 4-8 years after diagnosis.
