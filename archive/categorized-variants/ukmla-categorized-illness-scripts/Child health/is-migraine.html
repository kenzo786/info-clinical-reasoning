Migraine
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Migraine"][/sc]
  • Migraine is a common primary headache disorder characterised by recurrent attacks of moderate to severe headache, often accompanied by associated symptoms such as nausea, vomiting, and sensitivity to light (photophobia) and sound (phonophobia). Migraines may occur with or without aura, a set of transient focal neurological symptoms typically preceding the headache phase.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Migraine"][/sc]
  • Prevalence in UK GP population: Approximately 1 in 7 people (14.7%), making it one of the most common neurological disorders.
  • UK hospital prevalence: Migraine accounts for around 30% of all outpatient neurology appointments.
  • Worldwide prevalence: Affects approximately 1 billion people globally, with a higher prevalence in women (18%) compared to men (6%).
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Migraine"][/sc]
  • Step 1: Migraine is thought to originate in the brainstem or hypothalamus, where neuronal hyperexcitability occurs.
  • Step 2: This hyperactivity leads to cortical spreading depression (CSD), a wave of depolarisation followed by inhibition across the cerebral cortex, responsible for aura.
  • Step 3: Activation of the trigeminovascular system causes the release of vasoactive neuropeptides, including calcitonin gene-related peptide (CGRP), which induces vasodilation and neurogenic inflammation.
  • Step 4: Peripheral and central sensitisation of trigeminal neurons leads to pain transmission and the characteristic headache.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Migraine"][/sc]
  • Prodrome: Occurs hours to days before the headache, characterised by mood changes, fatigue, neck stiffness, and food cravings.
  • Aura: Transient neurological symptoms that last 5-60 minutes, usually involving visual disturbances, sensory changes, or speech difficulties.
  • Headache phase: Lasts 4-72 hours, typically unilateral and throbbing, exacerbated by physical activity and accompanied by nausea, photophobia, and phonophobia.
  • Postdrome: Follows headache resolution, often involving fatigue, irritability, and difficulty concentrating.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Migraine"][/sc]
  • Headache - A unilateral, pulsating pain that worsens with physical activity. Caused by trigeminovascular activation and neurogenic inflammation.
  • Nausea and vomiting - Triggered by brainstem involvement affecting the vomiting centre in the medulla.
  • Photophobia and phonophobia - Increased sensitivity to light and sound due to cortical hyperexcitability and sensory pathway sensitisation.
  • Aura - Visual or sensory disturbances resulting from cortical spreading depression affecting specific brain regions.
  • Neck stiffness - Linked to muscle tension and involvement of the cervical nerves.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Migraine"][/sc]
  • Visual aura - Transient visual symptoms such as scintillating scotomas caused by cortical spreading depression in the occipital lobe.
  • Pallor - A non-specific sign due to autonomic dysfunction during the migraine attack.
  • Neck tenderness - Related to muscle tension and cervical nerve involvement, often found during palpation.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Migraine"][/sc]
  • Thunderclap headache - Sudden onset of severe headache, possibly indicative of subarachnoid haemorrhage or other serious vascular causes.
  • New-onset headache after age 50 - Raises concern for secondary causes such as giant cell arteritis or intracranial neoplasm.
  • Progressive worsening over time - May indicate a space-occupying lesion, such as a brain tumour.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Migraine"][/sc]
  • Vestibular migraine - Presents with vertigo and balance disturbances without significant headache. Linked to brainstem involvement affecting vestibular pathways.
  • Hemiplegic migraine - Features motor weakness resembling a stroke, caused by cortical spreading depression affecting motor areas.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Migraine"][/sc]
  • Clinical diagnosis - Primarily based on patient history and symptom patterns. No specific biomarkers exist for migraine diagnosis.
  • MRI brain - Performed if red flags are present to rule out secondary causes. Findings are typically normal in primary migraine.
  • CT head - Used in emergency settings to rule out acute intracranial pathology, such as haemorrhage, when sudden onset or atypical features are present.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Migraine"][/sc]
  • A 35-year-old woman presents with recurrent unilateral pulsating headaches lasting 1-2 days, accompanied by nausea, vomiting, and photophobia. She reports visual auras preceding the headache.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Migraine"][/sc]
  • Tension-type headache - Typically bilateral, non-pulsating, and not worsened by physical activity, unlike the unilateral, throbbing nature of migraines.
  • Cluster headache - Severe, unilateral pain localised around the eye with autonomic symptoms (e.g., tearing, rhinorrhoea). Cluster headaches have shorter duration and more frequent attacks compared to migraines.
  • Subarachnoid haemorrhage - Sudden onset "thunderclap" headache, often with neck stiffness and altered consciousness, unlike the gradual onset of migraines.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Migraine"][/sc]
  • Acute treatment - Simple analgesics (e.g., ibuprofen, paracetamol) or triptans (e.g., sumatriptan) are first-line therapies. Antiemetics (e.g., metoclopramide) can be used for nausea.
  • Prophylactic treatment - Beta-blockers (e.g., propranolol), tricyclic antidepressants (e.g., amitriptyline), or anticonvulsants (e.g., topiramate) are considered for patients with frequent or severe attacks.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Migraine"][/sc]
  • Medication overuse headache - Chronic headache resulting from frequent use of acute headache medications, particularly triptans or opioids.
  • Status migrainosus - A severe migraine attack lasting more than 72 hours, which can be debilitating and may require hospitalisation.
  • Stroke - Migraine, especially with aura, is a risk factor for ischaemic stroke, particularly in women who smoke or use oral contraceptives.
