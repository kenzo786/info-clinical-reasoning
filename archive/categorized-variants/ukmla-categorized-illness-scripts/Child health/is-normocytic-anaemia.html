Normocytic Anaemia
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Normocytic Anaemia"][/sc]
  • Normocytic anaemia is a type of anaemia where the mean corpuscular volume (MCV) of red blood cells remains within the normal range (80-100 fL), but there is a decreased overall number of red blood cells or haemoglobin. It often results from conditions like chronic disease, acute blood loss, or bone marrow disorders.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Normocytic Anaemia"][/sc]
  • In the UK, normocytic anaemia is commonly seen in general practice, particularly among the elderly and those with chronic conditions. Hospital prevalence can vary, with anaemia of chronic disease being a leading cause in medical wards. Globally, it is a significant component of anaemia in regions where chronic diseases such as kidney disease or cancer are prevalent.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Normocytic Anaemia"][/sc]
  • Normocytic anaemia typically results from three main mechanisms: decreased production of red blood cells (due to chronic disease or bone marrow disorders), increased destruction (e.g., haemolysis), or acute blood loss. In chronic disease, cytokines like interleukin-6 (IL-6) impair iron metabolism and erythropoiesis, leading to normocytic anaemia.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Normocytic Anaemia"][/sc]
  • Normocytic anaemia may develop over weeks to months, often linked to the underlying chronic disease. Acute causes like haemorrhage may present rapidly, while anaemia of chronic disease or kidney disease usually has an insidious onset. The condition can persist long-term unless the underlying cause is treated.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Normocytic Anaemia"][/sc]
  • Fatigue - The most common symptom, caused by reduced oxygen-carrying capacity of the blood, leading to decreased tissue oxygenation.
  • Dyspnoea - Shortness of breath on exertion is due to the body's compensatory mechanism to meet oxygen demand when haemoglobin levels are low.
  • Pallor - A reduction in haemoglobin makes skin and mucous membranes appear pale, especially in severe cases.
  • Dizziness or light-headedness - Related to decreased oxygen supply to the brain.
  • Asymptomatic - Many cases of normocytic anaemia, especially in chronic diseases, may be asymptomatic and only detected through routine blood tests.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Normocytic Anaemia"][/sc]
  • Pallor of conjunctivae and skin - Indicates decreased haemoglobin and reduced perfusion.
  • Tachycardia - A compensatory response to increase cardiac output in response to reduced oxygen-carrying capacity.
  • Systolic flow murmur - Caused by increased blood flow velocity due to the lower viscosity of anaemic blood.
  • Signs of underlying disease - For example, oedema in chronic kidney disease or hepatomegaly in liver disease.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Normocytic Anaemia"][/sc]
  • Severe fatigue or weakness - May indicate profound anaemia, which could be a sign of significant bone marrow failure or acute haemorrhage.
  • Dyspnoea at rest - Suggests severe anaemia requiring urgent intervention, possibly linked to heart failure or severe blood loss.
  • Chest pain or palpitations - Could indicate myocardial ischaemia due to the heart working harder to compensate for anaemia.
  • Haematuria or melena - Suggests possible gastrointestinal or genitourinary bleeding, which may be the source of blood loss leading to anaemia.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Normocytic Anaemia"][/sc]
  • Isolated fatigue in elderly patients - May be dismissed as part of ageing rather than anaemia, leading to missed diagnosis.
  • Asymptomatic anaemia in routine blood tests - Particularly in chronic disease patients who may not exhibit classic symptoms of anaemia.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Normocytic Anaemia"][/sc]
  • Full blood count (FBC) - Typically reveals low haemoglobin (<120 g/L for women, <130 g/L for men), with normal MCV (80-100 fL).
  • Reticulocyte count - Used to assess bone marrow response; a low count suggests underproduction, while a high count may indicate increased destruction.
  • Iron studies - Ferritin may be elevated in anaemia of chronic disease, while total iron-binding capacity (TIBC) is reduced.
  • Renal function tests - Elevated creatinine or urea may suggest chronic kidney disease as the cause.
  • Inflammatory markers (CRP, ESR) - Elevated in anaemia of chronic disease due to underlying inflammation.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Normocytic Anaemia"][/sc]
  • A 65-year-old male with a history of chronic kidney disease presents with 3 months of worsening fatigue and shortness of breath on exertion. Blood tests reveal normocytic anaemia with a haemoglobin level of 105 g/L.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Normocytic Anaemia"][/sc]
  • Anaemia of chronic disease - Distinguished by elevated inflammatory markers and low TIBC.
  • Acute blood loss - History of trauma or gastrointestinal bleeding, with possible haemodynamic instability.
  • Haemolytic anaemia - High reticulocyte count and evidence of haemolysis (e.g., elevated bilirubin).
  • Bone marrow failure - Pancytopenia on FBC and abnormal bone marrow biopsy findings.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Normocytic Anaemia"][/sc]
  • Treat underlying cause - Management of the primary condition, such as treating chronic kidney disease or infection, is essential.
  • Erythropoiesis-stimulating agents (ESAs) - Used in chronic kidney disease patients to stimulate red cell production.
  • Blood transfusion - Reserved for severe or symptomatic anaemia, especially in acute blood loss.
  • Iron supplementation - May be necessary if iron deficiency coexists, especially in cases of blood loss.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Normocytic Anaemia"][/sc]
  • Heart failure - Due to chronic high-output state in response to anaemia.
  • Myocardial ischaemia - Occurs due to reduced oxygen supply to the heart.
  • Infection - Increased susceptibility in patients with anaemia of chronic disease, particularly if linked to immune dysregulation.
