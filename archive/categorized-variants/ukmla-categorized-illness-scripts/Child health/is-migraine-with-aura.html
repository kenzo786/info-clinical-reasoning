Migraine with Aura
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Migraine with Aura"][/sc]
  • Migraine with aura is a primary headache disorder characterised by recurrent episodes of moderate-to-severe headache, typically unilateral, accompanied by transient neurological symptoms called an "aura". The aura commonly manifests as visual disturbances, though it may involve sensory, speech, or motor symptoms. The aura precedes or accompanies the headache, lasting from 5 to 60 minutes, followed by a headache phase that may persist for hours.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Migraine with Aura"][/sc]
  • Prevalence in the UK general population: 5â€“15% of people experience migraines, with 20â€“30% of them having migraines with aura.
  • Hospital prevalence: 1-2% of all Emergency Department visits in the UK are due to headaches, and migraines make up a significant portion of these.
  • Worldwide prevalence: Approximately 15% of the global population suffer from migraines, with 20% of these cases involving aura.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Migraine with Aura"][/sc]
  • Cortical spreading depression: A wave of neuronal depolarisation followed by suppression of brain activity propagates across the cerebral cortex, particularly the occipital lobe, leading to the aura symptoms (especially visual disturbances).
  • Activation of the trigeminovascular system: This leads to the release of vasoactive peptides such as CGRP (calcitonin gene-related peptide), which causes vasodilation of cerebral blood vessels, contributing to the headache.
  • Neurogenic inflammation: Release of inflammatory mediators around the meninges leads to further sensitisation of pain pathways, exacerbating headache severity.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Migraine with Aura"][/sc]
  • Prodrome: Occurs hours to days before the headache, includes mood changes, neck stiffness, and food cravings.
  • Aura: Develops over 5â€“20 minutes, lasting up to 60 minutes. Visual disturbances are the most common, but sensory and speech disturbances may also occur.
  • Headache: Lasts 4â€“72 hours, often unilateral, pulsating, moderate-to-severe intensity, and aggravated by physical activity.
  • Postdrome: May last 24â€“48 hours after the headache, with fatigue, difficulty concentrating, and generalised malaise.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Migraine with Aura"][/sc]
  • Visual disturbances (aura): Including zigzag lines, scotomas, and flashes of light. Caused by cortical spreading depression, which predominantly affects the occipital lobe.
  • Unilateral, throbbing headache: Caused by activation of trigeminal pain pathways and vasodilation of intracranial vessels.
  • Nausea and vomiting: Likely due to activation of the brainstem and the autonomic nervous system.
  • Photophobia and phonophobia: Heightened sensitivity to light and sound, linked to the sensitisation of central pain pathways.
  • Speech disturbances: Including dysphasia, due to cortical involvement in the temporal lobe during aura.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Migraine with Aura"][/sc]
  • No specific physical signs: Migraine with aura is typically a clinical diagnosis, and no characteristic findings are observable during the headache-free period.
  • Visual field deficits: May be detected during the aura phase in cases with visual symptoms, due to occipital cortex involvement.
  • Mild neurological deficits during aura: Such as weakness or altered sensation, which resolve within 60 minutes.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Migraine with Aura"][/sc]
  • Sudden onset "thunderclap" headache: Could indicate subarachnoid haemorrhage (SAH) or other serious pathology rather than migraine.
  • Headache with fever and neck stiffness: May suggest meningitis, a medical emergency.
  • New neurological deficits persisting beyond the aura phase: Raises concern for stroke or transient ischaemic attack (TIA).
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Migraine with Aura"][/sc]
  • Acephalgic migraine: Aura occurs without the subsequent headache, making diagnosis challenging. Caused by the same cortical spreading depression but without activating pain pathways.
  • Hemiplegic migraine: A rare subtype where the aura includes transient hemiparesis, mimicking a stroke.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Migraine with Aura"][/sc]
  • Clinical diagnosis: Based on history of recurrent headaches with aura. No specific diagnostic test, but neuroimaging (MRI brain) may be used to rule out other causes such as stroke or tumour.
  • MRI or CT brain: Normal in typical migraine but may be done to exclude structural causes, especially if red flags are present.
  • EEG: Not indicated for diagnosing migraine but may be used in cases where seizures are considered in the differential diagnosis.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Migraine with Aura"][/sc]
  • A 30-year-old woman presents with a 10-year history of episodic, unilateral, throbbing headaches preceded by visual auras. The headaches are moderate-to-severe, lasting up to 24 hours, associated with nausea, photophobia, and phonophobia, and are relieved by lying down in a dark room.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Migraine with Aura"][/sc]
  • Tension headache: Bilateral, pressing/tightening headache without aura, often related to stress. No nausea or photophobia.
  • Cluster headache: Severe unilateral headache with autonomic features (e.g., lacrimation, rhinorrhoea), but no aura.
  • Transient ischaemic attack (TIA): Sudden onset of neurological symptoms, typically no headache. Aura-like symptoms last longer than an hour.
  • Seizure: Can cause transient focal neurological symptoms, but typically associated with loss of consciousness or abnormal movements.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Migraine with Aura"][/sc]
  • Acute management: NSAIDs (e.g., ibuprofen), triptans (e.g., sumatriptan), and antiemetics (e.g., metoclopramide) are the mainstays of treatment.
  • Prophylactic therapy: Consider if migraines are frequent (â‰¥4 per month). Options include beta-blockers (propranolol), anticonvulsants (topiramate), or tricyclic antidepressants (amitriptyline).
  • Non-pharmacological strategies: Include lifestyle modification (e.g., stress management, regular sleep), avoiding known triggers (e.g., certain foods, alcohol, or hormonal changes).
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Migraine with Aura"][/sc]
  • Medication overuse headache: Frequent use of acute headache medication may lead to chronic daily headaches.
  • Migraine with brainstem aura: Involves brainstem symptoms such as vertigo, dysarthria, and ataxia, indicating more widespread cortical involvement.
  • Stroke: Migraine with aura is associated with a slightly increased risk of stroke, particularly in women using oestrogen-containing contraceptives.
