Incarcerated Hernia
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Incarcerated Hernia"][/sc]
  • An incarcerated hernia occurs when herniated tissue becomes trapped outside the abdominal wall, unable to be reduced back into the abdominal cavity. This condition can lead to vascular compromise of the herniated contents, risking strangulation, ischaemia, and necrosis.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Incarcerated Hernia"][/sc]
  • UK GP prevalence: Hernias are common, with an estimated 1-2% prevalence among adults, and 20% of inguinal hernias may become incarcerated.
  • UK hospital prevalence: Incarcerated hernias represent a significant cause of acute abdominal admissions.
  • Worldwide prevalence: Hernias affect up to 4% of the global population; incarceration complicates 10-20% of these cases, depending on hernia type and population.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Incarcerated Hernia"][/sc]
  • An incarcerated hernia occurs when herniated contents, often intestines, protrude through a weakened area in the abdominal wall and become trapped.
  • This entrapment compromises venous return, leading to congestion, oedema, and risk of strangulation. Strangulation involves arterial blood supply occlusion, causing ischaemia and necrosis of the affected tissue if not promptly treated.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Incarcerated Hernia"][/sc]
  • Initial phase: Hernias often begin as reducible and asymptomatic. Symptoms may arise upon incarceration.
  • Incarceration phase: Presents acutely with pain, swelling, and inability to reduce the hernia.
  • Progression to strangulation: Untreated incarceration may lead to strangulation within hours, causing ischaemic tissue and severe pain.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Incarcerated Hernia"][/sc]
  • Pain at the hernia site - Caused by trapped tissue and local ischaemia, typically severe and sharp.
  • Swelling and tenderness - Due to local inflammation, venous congestion, and oedema.
  • Nausea and vomiting - Result from bowel obstruction in cases of incarcerated bowel loops.
  • Constipation or absence of flatus - Due to intestinal obstruction, which may signal severe compromise.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Incarcerated Hernia"][/sc]
  • Palpable, non-reducible mass - Indicates tissue entrapment, often firm and tender to palpation.
  • Redness over hernia site - Reflects inflammation or progression to strangulation.
  • Tachycardia and fever - Suggest systemic inflammatory response, particularly if strangulation occurs.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Incarcerated Hernia"][/sc]
  • Sudden onset of severe pain - May indicate strangulation with potential for ischaemia and necrosis.
  • Systemic symptoms (fever, tachycardia) - Signs of systemic inflammatory response or sepsis from necrotic bowel tissue.
  • Persistent vomiting - Indicates bowel obstruction or strangulation requiring urgent intervention.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Incarcerated Hernia"][/sc]
  • Mild or intermittent pain - Some cases may present with less intense symptoms, leading to misdiagnosis, particularly in elderly patients.
  • Absence of visible bulge - May occur in internal hernias or in obese patients, where palpation is challenging.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Incarcerated Hernia"][/sc]
  • Ultrasound - Shows herniated content and lack of reducibility; assesses vascularity in suspected strangulation.
  • CT scan - Offers detailed imaging of the hernia sac and may show bowel obstruction or ischaemia.
  • Blood tests - Full blood count may show leukocytosis, and elevated lactate indicates possible tissue ischaemia.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Incarcerated Hernia"][/sc]
  • 45-year-old male presents with sudden severe abdominal pain and a non-reducible, tender groin mass, along with nausea and vomiting for the past 4 hours.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Incarcerated Hernia"][/sc]
  • Strangulated hernia - More severe pain and rapid progression to systemic symptoms.
  • Appendicitis - Localised pain near the right lower quadrant; no palpable groin mass.
  • Lymphadenopathy - Enlarged nodes in groin without acute symptoms of incarceration.
  • Testicular torsion - Severe scrotal pain but lacks palpable groin mass.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Incarcerated Hernia"][/sc]
  • Immediate surgery - Recommended for irreducible hernias to prevent strangulation and tissue necrosis. Laparoscopic or open repair may be performed.
  • Pain management - Includes analgesics; opioids may be needed for severe cases.
  • Antibiotics - Administered perioperatively to prevent infection, especially if necrotic tissue is present.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Incarcerated Hernia"][/sc]
  • Strangulation - Risk of bowel ischaemia and necrosis, requiring urgent surgical intervention; occurs in up to 30% of incarcerated hernias.
  • Bowel obstruction - Common complication leading to nausea, vomiting, and abdominal pain.
  • Peritonitis - Due to bowel perforation in strangulated cases, leading to sepsis if untreated.
