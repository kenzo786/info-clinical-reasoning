Reactive Arthritis
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Reactive Arthritis"][/sc]
  • Reactive arthritis (ReA) is a seronegative spondyloarthritis that develops following an infection, most commonly affecting the genitourinary or gastrointestinal tract. It is characterised by the classic triad of arthritis, urethritis, and conjunctivitis, although this is not always present. The condition is believed to result from an immune-mediated response to infection rather than direct joint infection.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Reactive Arthritis"][/sc]
  • Prevalence in the UK GP population: Estimated at 30â€“40 cases per 100,000 annually.
  • UK hospital prevalence: Rarely requiring hospitalisation; severe cases are less frequent but significant.
  • Worldwide prevalence: More common in populations with a high prevalence of HLA-B27, such as those of northern European descent, with rates up to 5% in post-infectious cases.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Reactive Arthritis"][/sc]
  • Triggering infection leads to immune activation. Common infections include Chlamydia trachomatis, Salmonella, Shigella, Yersinia, and Campylobacter.
  • Genetic predisposition: HLA-B27 positive individuals are at increased risk, suggesting a link to antigen presentation and aberrant immune responses.
  • Molecular mimicry: Pathogen antigens may resemble self-antigens, triggering an autoimmune response.
  • Synovial inflammation occurs as immune cells infiltrate joints, leading to pain, swelling, and stiffness.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Reactive Arthritis"][/sc]
  • Acute phase: Develops 1â€“4 weeks after the inciting infection.
  • Subacute phase: Symptoms may persist for weeks to months if untreated.
  • Chronic phase: In a minority of cases, arthritis becomes persistent, leading to ongoing symptoms or progression to spondyloarthritis.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Reactive Arthritis"][/sc]
  • Joint pain and swelling - Typically affects large weight-bearing joints (e.g., knees, ankles). Pathophysiology: Synovial inflammation due to immune complex deposition and immune cell infiltration.
  • Urethritis - Dysuria or discharge may occur. Pathophysiology: Immune response to prior genitourinary infection.
  • Ocular symptoms - Conjunctivitis or uveitis causing redness, pain, and photophobia. Pathophysiology: Inflammatory response in ocular tissues.
  • Back pain - Suggestive of sacroiliitis in some cases. Pathophysiology: Enthesitis and inflammation of sacroiliac joints.
  • Fatigue and malaise - Systemic inflammatory response contributing to constitutional symptoms.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Reactive Arthritis"][/sc]
  • Joint swelling and tenderness - Observable in affected joints, indicating synovitis.
  • Conjunctival injection - Redness of the conjunctiva, typical of conjunctivitis.
  • Plantar fasciitis or Achilles tendonitis - Enthesitis presenting as localised tenderness.
  • Nail changes - Hyperkeratosis or pitting, sometimes seen in associated conditions like psoriasis.
  • Skin lesions - Keratoderma blennorrhagicum or circinate balanitis, often related to the underlying infection.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Reactive Arthritis"][/sc]
  • Severe back pain with neurological signs - May indicate sacroiliitis or spinal involvement requiring urgent intervention.
  • Vision loss or severe eye pain - Suggestive of uveitis; untreated cases can lead to vision-threatening complications.
  • Unexplained weight loss or fever - Could indicate an underlying systemic infection or another autoimmune condition.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Reactive Arthritis"][/sc]
  • Isolated sacroiliitis without peripheral arthritis - More common in chronic cases.
  • Predominantly ocular or skin involvement without joint symptoms - May delay diagnosis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Reactive Arthritis"][/sc]
  • Blood tests - Raised CRP and ESR indicating inflammation.
  • HLA-B27 testing - Positive in up to 75% of cases, especially with axial involvement.
  • Synovial fluid analysis - Excludes septic arthritis; findings include elevated white cells without organisms.
  • Imaging - X-rays or MRI to detect sacroiliitis or joint damage.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Reactive Arthritis"][/sc]

A 28-year-old male presents with a 2-week history of knee pain and swelling, dysuria, and redness in one eye following a recent diarrhoeal illness.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Reactive Arthritis"][/sc]
  • Septic arthritis - Typically monoarticular with fever and positive synovial fluid culture.
  • Gout - Acute onset, monosodium urate crystals on joint aspiration.
  • Psoriatic arthritis - Joint and skin involvement, nail changes more common.
  • Ankylosing spondylitis - Chronic back pain and axial stiffness without prior infection.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Reactive Arthritis"][/sc]
  • NSAIDs - First-line for symptom relief.
  • Intra-articular corticosteroids - For persistent joint symptoms.
  • DMARDs - Methotrexate or sulfasalazine for chronic or severe cases.
  • Antibiotics - Targeting the underlying infection if still active.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Reactive Arthritis"][/sc]
  • Chronic arthritis - Persistent joint symptoms in a minority of cases.
  • Spondyloarthritis - Progression to axial involvement.
  • Vision loss - Due to untreated uveitis.
