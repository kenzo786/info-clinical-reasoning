Meningitis
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Meningitis"][/sc]
  • Meningitis is the inflammation of the meninges, the protective membranes covering the brain and spinal cord, typically caused by bacterial, viral, or fungal infections. Bacterial meningitis, which is the most serious form, can lead to rapid deterioration and is a medical emergency. Viral meningitis is generally less severe but can still cause significant morbidity.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Meningitis"][/sc]
  • In the UK, bacterial meningitis has an incidence of approximately 2 per 100,000 annually. Viral meningitis is more common, with an incidence of about 5â€“10 per 100,000 people. Worldwide, the burden varies significantly, with higher rates in regions like sub-Saharan Africa, the "meningitis belt," where seasonal epidemics are common.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Meningitis"][/sc]
  • Meningitis occurs when infectious organisms breach the blood-brain barrier and invade the cerebrospinal fluid (CSF). This triggers an inflammatory response in the meninges, leading to increased intracranial pressure (ICP), cerebral oedema, and impaired CSF flow. In bacterial meningitis, the release of endotoxins exacerbates inflammation, causing vasculitis and potentially leading to cerebral infarction.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Meningitis"][/sc]
  • Acute phase: Symptoms such as headache, fever, and neck stiffness develop rapidly over 24-48 hours.
  • Subacute phase: In viral meningitis, symptoms may progress more slowly over several days.
  • Chronic phase: Rare in typical meningitis cases, but fungal or tuberculous meningitis can have a prolonged course over weeks to months.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Meningitis"][/sc]
  • Fever - Caused by systemic inflammation and cytokine release due to infection.
  • Headache - Due to increased intracranial pressure from meningeal inflammation.
  • Neck stiffness (nuchal rigidity) - Results from irritation of the meninges, particularly the spinal meninges.
  • Photophobia - Sensitivity to light is common and may be due to irritation of the optic nerve by meningeal inflammation.
  • Altered mental status - Ranges from confusion to coma, often due to increased intracranial pressure or direct inflammatory damage to brain tissue.
  • Nausea and vomiting - Caused by increased intracranial pressure stimulating the vomiting centre in the brainstem.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Meningitis"][/sc]
  • Kernig's sign - Resistance to passive knee extension when the hip is flexed, caused by meningeal irritation.
  • Brudzinski's sign - Involuntary lifting of the legs when the neck is flexed, due to meningeal irritation.
  • Focal neurological deficits - May indicate localised brain involvement or complications such as abscesses or infarction.
  • Non-blanching rash - Seen in meningococcal meningitis, this is caused by disseminated intravascular coagulation (DIC) and sepsis.
  • Raised intracranial pressure (papilloedema) - Increased pressure from cerebral oedema may cause optic disc swelling, visible on fundoscopy.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Meningitis"][/sc]
  • Severe headache and rapid progression of symptoms - Indicates raised intracranial pressure and a potential for rapid deterioration.
  • Seizures - May suggest cerebral involvement or severe brain inflammation, warranting urgent intervention.
  • Non-blanching purpuric rash - Suggestive of meningococcal septicaemia, indicating a medical emergency.
  • Altered consciousness or coma - Signifies advanced disease and a risk of brain herniation or death.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Meningitis"][/sc]
  • Immunocompromised patients - May present with subtle symptoms like mild fever and confusion, without the classic triad of fever, headache, and neck stiffness.
  • Neonates and infants - Present with non-specific symptoms such as poor feeding, irritability, or lethargy, often without neck stiffness or photophobia.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Meningitis"][/sc]
  • Lumbar puncture (LP) - CSF analysis typically shows high white cell count, raised protein, and low glucose in bacterial meningitis. Viral meningitis may show lymphocytic pleocytosis with normal glucose.
  • Blood cultures - Essential in identifying causative organisms, particularly in bacterial meningitis.
  • CT head - Performed prior to LP if raised ICP is suspected to avoid brain herniation. Findings may include cerebral oedema or abscesses.
  • Full blood count (FBC) - Typically shows leucocytosis in bacterial meningitis and lymphocytosis in viral cases.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Meningitis"][/sc]
  • A 21-year-old male presents with a 24-hour history of high fever, severe headache, neck stiffness, and confusion. He has a non-blanching purpuric rash and photophobia. He is acutely unwell with signs of raised intracranial pressure.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Meningitis"][/sc]
  • Encephalitis - Typically presents with more pronounced neurological deficits such as seizures, behavioural changes, and motor weakness. LP shows lymphocytic pleocytosis and normal glucose.
  • Subarachnoid haemorrhage (SAH) - Acute onset "thunderclap" headache with possible focal neurological signs. CT head may show blood in the subarachnoid space.
  • Brain abscess - May present with focal neurological deficits, fever, and headache. CT or MRI will show a ring-enhancing lesion.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Meningitis"][/sc]
  • Bacterial meningitis - Immediate empirical IV antibiotics (e.g., ceftriaxone or cefotaxime) based on local microbiology guidelines, and adjunctive corticosteroids (e.g., dexamethasone) to reduce inflammation.
  • Viral meningitis - Supportive care, with antiviral therapy (e.g., aciclovir) in suspected or confirmed cases of herpes simplex virus (HSV) infection.
  • Fungal meningitis - Amphotericin B and flucytosine for cryptococcal meningitis, particularly in immunocompromised individuals.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Meningitis"][/sc]
  • Sepsis - Seen in meningococcal meningitis, this can lead to multi-organ failure and is associated with a high mortality rate.
  • Hydrocephalus - Increased CSF production or decreased absorption can lead to chronic hydrocephalus, requiring shunt placement.
  • Deafness - Inflammation of the inner ear can result in permanent hearing loss, especially in bacterial cases.
  • Seizures and long-term neurological deficits - Ongoing cerebral irritation or infarction can cause epilepsy, motor, or cognitive deficits.
