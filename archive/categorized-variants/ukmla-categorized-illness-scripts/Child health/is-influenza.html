Influenza illness script
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Influenza"][/sc]
  • Influenza is an acute viral respiratory illness caused by influenza viruses (mainly types A and B). It is characterised by the sudden onset of systemic symptoms like fever, chills, and myalgia, along with respiratory symptoms such as cough and sore throat. The disease typically occurs in seasonal epidemics and can lead to severe complications, especially in vulnerable populations like the elderly, young children, and those with chronic health conditions.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Influenza"][/sc]
  • In the UK, influenza affects 5-15% of the population annually, with GP consultations peaking during the winter months.
  • Hospitalisation rates for severe influenza are approximately 10-50 per 100,000 people annually, with increased admissions during influenza outbreaks.
  • Globally, seasonal influenza epidemics cause 3-5 million severe cases and 290,000-650,000 deaths annually, according to the World Health Organization (WHO).
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Influenza"][/sc]
  • Influenza virus is transmitted via respiratory droplets. After entry, the virus binds to and invades the epithelial cells of the upper and lower respiratory tract through haemagglutinin proteins.
  • Once inside the host cells, viral replication occurs, leading to direct cellular injury and triggering the release of pro-inflammatory cytokines. This immune response is responsible for many of the systemic symptoms like fever and malaise.
  • The virus's destruction of the respiratory epithelium predisposes patients to secondary bacterial infections, which can lead to pneumonia and other complications.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Influenza"][/sc]
  • Incubation Period: 1-4 days after exposure.
  • Acute Phase: Symptoms typically last for 3-7 days, with fever and systemic symptoms peaking within the first 2-3 days.
  • Recovery Phase: Full recovery generally occurs within 1-2 weeks, though cough and fatigue may persist for several weeks.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Influenza"][/sc]
  • Fever (often >38Â°C) - Due to cytokine release during the immune response to viral infection.
  • Chills - Associated with the body's effort to raise core temperature during fever.
  • Myalgia - Caused by the systemic inflammatory response and cytokine release.
  • Headache - Linked to inflammatory mediators and fever.
  • Cough (usually dry) - Results from irritation and inflammation of the respiratory epithelium.
  • Sore throat - Due to inflammation of the pharynx and surrounding tissues.
  • Fatigue and malaise - Systemic response to viral infection, mediated by cytokines and generalised inflammation.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Influenza"][/sc]
  • Fever (>38Â°C) - Elevated body temperature is a common sign due to the body's immune response.
  • Tachypnoea - Increased respiratory rate as a result of hypoxia or respiratory distress from lung involvement.
  • Tachycardia - Elevated heart rate, often due to fever and dehydration.
  • Rhonchi or wheezing - Indicates lower respiratory tract involvement or secondary bronchitis.
  • Pharyngeal erythema - Inflammation of the throat due to viral replication and immune response.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Influenza"][/sc]
  • Shortness of breath - May indicate viral pneumonia or secondary bacterial pneumonia.
  • Haemoptysis - Could signify severe lower respiratory tract infection or secondary complications.
  • Confusion - May reflect sepsis, hypoxia, or neurological involvement such as encephalitis.
  • Severe chest pain - Could indicate cardiovascular complications such as myocarditis or acute coronary syndromes.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Influenza"][/sc]
  • Elderly patients: May present with atypical features such as confusion or fatigue rather than fever and respiratory symptoms, due to altered immune response and comorbidities.
  • Immunocompromised patients: May have milder or absent symptoms despite significant viral replication due to blunted immune response.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Influenza"][/sc]
  • Rapid antigen tests: Detects influenza viral antigens, with moderate sensitivity and specificity.
  • RT-PCR (Reverse transcription-polymerase chain reaction): The gold standard for diagnosing influenza, highly sensitive and specific. It detects viral RNA in respiratory specimens.
  • Full blood count (FBC): May show leukopenia or lymphopenia, indicating viral infection.
  • Chest X-ray: Performed if pneumonia is suspected, showing patchy consolidation or interstitial infiltrates.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Influenza"][/sc]
  • A 35-year-old male presents with 3 days of fever, chills, myalgia, dry cough, and fatigue following contact with a co-worker who had similar symptoms. He is otherwise healthy, with no significant medical history.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Influenza"][/sc]
  • Common cold (rhinovirus): Typically milder, with predominant nasal congestion and rhinorrhoea rather than high fever and systemic symptoms.
  • Pneumonia: May present with similar respiratory symptoms but often includes pleuritic chest pain, productive cough, and more severe shortness of breath. Chest X-ray helps differentiate.
  • COVID-19: May present with fever, cough, and fatigue, but often includes anosmia (loss of smell) and a more insidious onset of symptoms. PCR testing confirms the diagnosis.
  • Bronchitis: Similar cough and fatigue but often without the systemic features of influenza like high fever and myalgia.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Influenza"][/sc]
  • Antiviral therapy (e.g., oseltamivir or zanamivir): Should be started within 48 hours of symptom onset for high-risk groups (e.g., elderly, immunocompromised).
  • Symptomatic treatment: Paracetamol or ibuprofen for fever and myalgia. Ensure adequate hydration and rest.
  • Antibiotics: Only indicated if secondary bacterial infection such as pneumonia is suspected.
  • Vaccination: Annual influenza vaccination is recommended for high-risk populations and healthcare workers to prevent infection.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Influenza"][/sc]
  • Pneumonia: Can be viral or secondary bacterial. Common pathogens include Streptococcus pneumoniae and Staphylococcus aureus.
  • Acute respiratory distress syndrome (ARDS): Severe lung inflammation leading to respiratory failure.
  • Myocarditis: Inflammation of the heart muscle, potentially leading to heart failure or arrhythmias.
  • Encephalitis: Inflammation of the brain, presenting with confusion, seizures, or altered consciousness.
