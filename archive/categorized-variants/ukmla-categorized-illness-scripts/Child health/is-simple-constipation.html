Simple Constipation
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Simple Constipation"][/sc]
  • Simple constipation is a common functional gastrointestinal disorder characterised by infrequent, difficult, or painful defaecation. It involves stool hardening and incomplete evacuation, often due to lifestyle factors or mild, non-organic dysfunctions in bowel motility or evacuation.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Simple Constipation"][/sc]
  • UK GP Population: Affects approximately 15-20% of the general population, with increased prevalence in females and older adults.
  • UK Hospital Prevalence: Less common in hospital settings unless associated with comorbidities (e.g., immobility or medication side effects).
  • Worldwide Prevalence: Estimated at 10-20%, with significant variation by country and lifestyle factors.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Simple Constipation"][/sc]
  • Dietary and Lifestyle Factors: Low dietary fibre, dehydration, and sedentary lifestyle reduce stool bulk and slow colonic transit time.
  • Neuromuscular Function: Alterations in colonic motility, potentially due to delayed colonic transit or inadequate propulsive contractions, can limit stool movement.
  • Pelvic Floor Dysfunction: Dyssynergic defaecation, where the pelvic muscles fail to relax during bowel movements, can lead to incomplete evacuation and worsened symptoms.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Simple Constipation"][/sc]
  • Acute Constipation: Short-term, often due to temporary dietary changes, stress, or medications (e.g., opioids).
  • Chronic Constipation: Symptoms persisting over three months, with episodic or continuous patterns depending on lifestyle and management consistency.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Simple Constipation"][/sc]
  • Infrequent Bowel Movements - Defined as fewer than three per week; related to slowed colonic transit and insufficient dietary fibre or fluids.
  • Straining - Due to hard, dry stool that is difficult to pass; results from low fluid intake and prolonged stool transit time.
  • Sensation of Incomplete Evacuation - Caused by retained stool and dyssynergic defaecation; may lead to discomfort and continued straining.
  • Abdominal Discomfort/Bloating - Accumulation of stool and gas in the colon can stretch the bowel wall, causing bloating and cramping sensations.
  • Hard or Lumpy Stools - Results from prolonged transit time, where more water is absorbed from the stool in the colon.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Simple Constipation"][/sc]
  • Abdominal Tenderness - Mild tenderness, particularly in the lower abdomen, may indicate retained stool or gas build-up.
  • Palpable Faecal Mass - Felt on palpation of the lower abdomen, indicating faecal loading in the rectosigmoid colon.
  • Rectal Fullness on DRE (Digital Rectal Examination) - May confirm the presence of hard stool in the rectum, supporting a diagnosis of constipation.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Simple Constipation"][/sc]
  • Weight Loss - Unintentional weight loss may suggest malignancy or metabolic disorder affecting bowel function.
  • Rectal Bleeding - Bright red blood or melena can indicate colorectal cancer, haemorrhoids, or anal fissures.
  • Severe Pain - Constant, severe pain may signal bowel obstruction or ischaemia, requiring immediate investigation.
  • Iron Deficiency Anaemia - Anaemia may indicate chronic GI blood loss, often from a malignancy in older patients.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Simple Constipation"][/sc]
  • Chronic Fatigue - Due to discomfort and possible malabsorption, some patients may present with fatigue without obvious GI symptoms.
  • Urinary Symptoms - Faecal impaction can cause bladder pressure, leading to urinary frequency or retention.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Simple Constipation"][/sc]
  • Blood Tests: Full blood count (FBC), ferritin, and thyroid function tests (TFTs) to exclude anaemia, thyroid dysfunction, or metabolic disorders.
  • Abdominal X-ray: May reveal faecal loading in the colon, supporting constipation diagnosis.
  • Colonoscopy (if red flags): Performed if there is unexplained weight loss, anaemia, or bleeding, to rule out malignancy or inflammatory bowel disease.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Simple Constipation"][/sc]
  • An otherwise healthy 45-year-old female presents with a two-month history of infrequent bowel movements, straining, and abdominal discomfort, with no red flag symptoms reported.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Simple Constipation"][/sc]
  • Irritable Bowel Syndrome (IBS): Constipation subtype presents with abdominal pain relieved by defaecation; no alarm features.
  • Colorectal Cancer: Often has red flags (e.g., weight loss, anaemia); requires colonoscopy if suspected.
  • Hypothyroidism: Causes constipation with fatigue and weight gain; diagnosed via TFTs.
  • Obstructive Lesions: Bowel obstruction may present with severe pain, vomiting, and no gas or stool passage.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Simple Constipation"][/sc]
  • Diet and Lifestyle Changes: Increase fibre intake, hydration, and exercise; recommended as first-line treatment.
  • Bulk-forming Laxatives: E.g., ispaghula husk, to increase stool bulk and soften stool.
  • Osmotic Laxatives: E.g., lactulose or macrogol, to retain water in the stool and aid passage.
  • Stimulant Laxatives (short-term): For occasional use in unresponsive cases (e.g., senna or bisacodyl).
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Simple Constipation"][/sc]
  • Faecal Impaction: Common in chronic cases, can cause rectal pain and incontinence.
  • Anal Fissures or Haemorrhoids: Caused by straining and hard stools, leading to bleeding and pain during defaecation.
  • Diverticulosis: Chronic pressure in the colon can result in the formation of diverticula, potentially leading to inflammation.
