Joint Pains
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Joint Pains"][/sc]
  • Joint pain, or arthralgia, refers to discomfort, aches, or soreness in any of the bodyâ€™s joints. It may be localised to a single joint or involve multiple joints. It can result from inflammation, injury, or degenerative processes and is often a hallmark of both systemic and localised conditions.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Joint Pains"][/sc]
  • UK GP prevalence: Joint pain is one of the most common presentations in primary care, affecting 20-30% of adults at some point in their lives.
  • UK hospital prevalence: About 15% of hospital admissions related to musculoskeletal disorders involve joint pain.
  • Worldwide prevalence: Globally, osteoarthritis (OA), the leading cause of joint pain, affects over 240 million people, with rheumatoid arthritis (RA) and other inflammatory conditions adding significantly to this burden.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Joint Pains"][/sc]
  • Degenerative (Osteoarthritis): Progressive wear and tear of cartilage leads to joint space narrowing, bone remodelling, and osteophyte formation, resulting in pain and stiffness.
  • Inflammatory (Rheumatoid Arthritis): Immune-mediated synovial inflammation causes swelling, erosion of cartilage, and bone destruction.
  • Infectious (Septic Arthritis): Bacterial invasion of a joint triggers an inflammatory response that can rapidly destroy cartilage.
  • Crystal Deposition (Gout): Deposition of urate crystals in joints triggers acute inflammation and severe pain.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Joint Pains"][/sc]
  • Acute: Sudden onset, lasting days to weeks, often related to infection, injury, or crystal-induced arthritis (gout).
  • Subacute: Gradual onset over weeks to months, common in inflammatory conditions like rheumatoid arthritis.
  • Chronic: Persists for months to years, typically seen in osteoarthritis or poorly controlled inflammatory conditions.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Joint Pains"][/sc]
  • Joint pain - Localised discomfort due to cartilage damage or inflammation; common in all joint-related conditions.
  • Joint stiffness - Often worse in the morning or after inactivity, caused by joint swelling or reduced lubrication (seen in osteoarthritis and rheumatoid arthritis).
  • Swelling - Due to synovial inflammation (inflammatory arthritis) or fluid accumulation (trauma or infection).
  • Reduced range of motion - Due to pain, swelling, or structural damage in the joint.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Joint Pains"][/sc]
  • Joint tenderness - Local pain upon palpation, indicative of synovitis or degenerative changes.
  • Effusion - Swelling within the joint capsule, commonly seen in septic arthritis or inflammatory conditions like RA.
  • Joint deformity - Chronic inflammation or joint destruction may lead to visible deformities, particularly in rheumatoid arthritis.
  • Crepitus - A grating sound or sensation caused by cartilage wear in osteoarthritis.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Joint Pains"][/sc]
  • Fever - Suggestive of septic arthritis or systemic infection.
  • Rapid onset with severe pain - May indicate acute gout or septic arthritis.
  • Weight loss, night sweats - Concerning for malignancy or systemic autoimmune diseases like lupus.
  • Neurological deficits - Could suggest spinal cord compression or peripheral nerve damage.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Joint Pains"][/sc]
  • Gout in unusual joints - While most commonly affecting the first metatarsophalangeal joint, it may present atypically in the knees, wrists, or elbows.
  • Septic arthritis in immunocompromised patients - May present with less pronounced systemic symptoms or absent fever.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Joint Pains"][/sc]
  • Full blood count (FBC) - Elevated white cells in infection or inflammation (leukocytosis).
  • Inflammatory markers (ESR, CRP) - Raised in inflammatory arthritis, infections, and systemic disease.
  • Joint aspiration and synovial fluid analysis - Assessing for crystals (gout, pseudogout), bacteria (septic arthritis), and inflammatory cells.
  • X-ray of affected joints - Joint space narrowing and osteophyte formation in osteoarthritis; erosions in rheumatoid arthritis.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Joint Pains"][/sc]
  • A 60-year-old woman presents with a 6-month history of gradual-onset knee pain, stiffness worse in the morning, and occasional swelling. Symptoms have progressively worsened, limiting her mobility.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Joint Pains"][/sc]
  • Osteoarthritis - Characterised by joint pain and stiffness with mechanical symptoms like crepitus; X-ray shows joint space narrowing.
  • Rheumatoid Arthritis - Symmetrical joint swelling, morning stiffness, systemic symptoms like fatigue; positive rheumatoid factor or anti-CCP.
  • Gout - Acute, severe pain in a single joint, typically the big toe; raised uric acid and crystals seen on joint aspiration.
  • Septic Arthritis - Acute monoarticular arthritis with systemic symptoms (fever); positive joint fluid culture.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Joint Pains"][/sc]
  • Osteoarthritis: First-line involves analgesia (paracetamol, NSAIDs), physiotherapy, and weight loss. Severe cases may require joint replacement surgery.
  • Rheumatoid Arthritis: DMARDs (e.g., methotrexate) and biologic agents (e.g., TNF inhibitors) are used to control inflammation. Corticosteroids for acute flares.
  • Gout: NSAIDs or colchicine for acute attacks; urate-lowering therapy (allopurinol) for long-term management.
  • Septic Arthritis: Urgent joint drainage and intravenous antibiotics.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Joint Pains"][/sc]
  • Joint destruction - Seen in rheumatoid arthritis and septic arthritis, leading to chronic pain and disability.
  • Chronic disability - Resulting from osteoarthritis or poorly controlled inflammatory arthritis.
  • Systemic complications - Rheumatoid arthritis can cause cardiovascular disease, lung involvement, and other systemic issues.
