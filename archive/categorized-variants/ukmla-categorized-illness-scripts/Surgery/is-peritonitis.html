Peritonitis
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Peritonitis"][/sc]
  • Peritonitis is inflammation of the peritoneum, the thin layer of tissue covering the inner abdominal wall and organs within the abdomen. This condition can be caused by infection (most commonly bacterial) or chemical irritation, often secondary to gastrointestinal perforation, trauma, or other intra-abdominal pathology. Peritonitis is a medical emergency due to the risk of rapid progression to sepsis and multi-organ failure.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Peritonitis"][/sc]
  • In the UK general practice setting, peritonitis is rare, as cases often present acutely and are promptly referred to secondary care.
  • In UK hospitals, peritonitis is more common, particularly in patients with risk factors like peptic ulcer disease, diverticulitis, or post-surgical complications.
  • Worldwide, the incidence varies by healthcare setting, with higher rates in areas where delayed presentation of abdominal diseases is more common.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Peritonitis"][/sc]
  • Peritonitis generally begins with bacterial or chemical irritation of the peritoneum.
  • Bacterial peritonitis often occurs due to a gastrointestinal perforation (e.g., from an ulcer or diverticulitis), allowing bacteria to enter the sterile peritoneal cavity.
  • The peritoneal response includes inflammation, fluid accumulation, and recruitment of immune cells to the affected area.
  • Without treatment, inflammation can spread throughout the peritoneum, leading to sepsis and potential progression to systemic inflammatory response syndrome (SIRS) and multi-organ failure.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Peritonitis"][/sc]
  • Acute phase (hours to days): Rapid onset of severe abdominal pain and signs of systemic infection (e.g., fever, tachycardia).
  • Progression phase (if untreated, hours to days): Localised inflammation extends to generalised peritonitis, increasing the risk of sepsis and multi-organ dysfunction.
  • Resolution or Complications (days to weeks): With prompt treatment, recovery occurs within days to weeks. If untreated, peritonitis can lead to abscess formation, bowel necrosis, or septic shock.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Peritonitis"][/sc]
  • Severe abdominal pain: Sudden and severe pain often starts in one area and can spread across the abdomen due to peritoneal irritation.

  • Nausea and vomiting: Common as the gastrointestinal system responds to peritoneal inflammation.
  • Loss of appetite: Anorexia frequently accompanies abdominal inflammation.
  • Bloating: Often due to paralytic ileus (intestinal paralysis) secondary to inflammation.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Peritonitis"][/sc]
  • Abdominal tenderness and guarding: Caused by peritoneal irritation, resulting in voluntary or involuntary guarding as muscles contract to protect inflamed tissues.
  • Rebound tenderness: Pain upon release of pressure, a classic sign of peritoneal irritation.
  • Decreased or absent bowel sounds: Indicates paralytic ileus due to inflammation.
  • Tachycardia and hypotension: Reflects the systemic inflammatory response and potential sepsis.
  • Fever: Resulting from infection-related immune activation.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Peritonitis"][/sc]
  • Hypotension: Can signify progressing sepsis or shock; urgently requires intervention.
  • Altered mental status: Indicative of severe sepsis or septic shock.
  • Persistent tachycardia: A marker for systemic inflammation and potential cardiovascular compromise.
  • Rigid abdomen: Suggests a severe or late-stage peritonitis and warrants immediate surgical evaluation.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Peritonitis"][/sc]
  • In elderly or immunocompromised patients: May present without typical fever or with only mild pain due to blunted immune response, leading to delayed diagnosis.
  • Paediatric presentations: Children may present with non-specific symptoms such as irritability and anorexia rather than overt abdominal pain.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Peritonitis"][/sc]
  • Full Blood Count (FBC): Often shows elevated white cell count due to infection.
  • C-reactive protein (CRP): Elevated in inflammatory responses.
  • Blood cultures: To identify sepsis pathogens if systemic infection is suspected.
  • Abdominal X-ray: May reveal free air under the diaphragm in cases of perforation.
  • Ultrasound/CT scan: CT is particularly useful to detect perforation, abscesses, or inflammation extent.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Peritonitis"][/sc]
  • 45-year-old male presenting with sudden severe abdominal pain, fever, and rebound tenderness, progressing over several hours with hypotension on arrival to A&E.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Peritonitis"][/sc]
  • Acute appendicitis: Localised pain typically begins in the periumbilical region and moves to the right lower quadrant.
  • Acute pancreatitis: Epigastric pain radiates to the back; elevated serum amylase/lipase.
  • Gastroenteritis: Diffuse pain with diarrhoea and vomiting; often lacks peritoneal signs.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Peritonitis"][/sc]
  • IV antibiotics: Broad-spectrum antibiotics targeting common abdominal pathogens (e.g., ceftriaxone and metronidazole).
  • Fluids resuscitation: Aggressive IV fluids to support cardiovascular status.
  • Surgical consultation: Required if perforation or other surgical cause is suspected, often necessitating laparotomy or laparoscopy.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Peritonitis"][/sc]
  • Sepsis and septic shock: High risk without prompt intervention, due to bacterial spread and systemic inflammatory response.
  • Abscess formation: Intra-abdominal abscesses can form as a result of localised infection containment.
  • Multi-organ failure: Resulting from septic shock, it is a leading cause of death in untreated or severe cases.
