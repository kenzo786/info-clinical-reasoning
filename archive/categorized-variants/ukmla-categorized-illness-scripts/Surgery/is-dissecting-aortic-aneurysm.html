Aortic Dissection
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Aortic Dissection"][/sc]
  • An aortic dissection is a life-threatening condition where a tear forms in the innermost layer (intima) of the aorta, allowing blood to enter the medial layer, creating a false lumen. This process weakens the vessel wall and can cause further tearing, vessel rupture, or occlusion of arterial branches. It requires urgent medical intervention to prevent fatal outcomes.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Aortic Dissection"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Aortic Dissection"][/sc]
  • Risk Factors: The primary risk factor is chronic hypertension, which contributes to over 70% of cases. Other significant risk factors include connective tissue disorders (e.g., Marfan syndrome, Ehlers-Danlos syndrome), bicuspid aortic valve, aortic aneurysm, atherosclerosis, smoking, and cocaine use. Prior cardiac surgery (e.g., aortic valve replacement) and inflammatory conditions such as giant cell arteritis also increase risk.
  • Prevention: Effective blood pressure management with antihypertensive therapy (e.g., beta-blockers, ACE inhibitors) significantly reduces risk. Lifestyle modifications such as smoking cessation, regular cardiovascular exercise, and avoidance of stimulant drugs (e.g., cocaine) are crucial. Patients with known aortic aneurysms or genetic predispositions should undergo regular surveillance with imaging (e.g., CT/MRI) to monitor aortic size and intervene before dissection occurs.
Pathophysiology
  • An aortic dissection occurs due to a tear in the aortic intima, which is often triggered by chronic hypertension or connective tissue disorders (e.g., Marfan syndrome). Blood surges through this tear, dissecting the medial layer and creating a false lumen between the layers of the aortic wall.
  • As the dissection propagates, it can obstruct branches of the aorta, reducing blood flow to vital organs, or rupture into the pericardial sac or pleural cavity, resulting in cardiac tamponade or exsanguination.
Aortic Dissection Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Aortic Dissection"][/sc]
  • Acute Phase: Within hours to days, rapid onset of severe chest or back pain, typically presenting as a medical emergency.
  • Subacute Phase: Over days to weeks, symptoms may stabilise or worsen progressively depending on the extent of the dissection.
  • Chronic Phase: Persistent dissection can lead to long-term complications like chronic heart failure or aneurysmal rupture.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Aortic Dissection"][/sc]
  • Sudden severe chest pain - Classically described as "tearing" or "ripping" in nature due to the sudden splitting of the aortic layers.
  • Back pain - Radiates to the back due to the extension of the dissection, particularly in descending aortic dissections.
  • Abdominal pain - Can occur if the dissection extends into the abdominal aorta, compromising mesenteric or renal arteries.
  • Syncope - A result of compromised blood flow to the brain or due to cardiac tamponade.
  • Dyspnoea - Occurs if there is heart failure or tamponade due to extension of the dissection into the aortic root.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Aortic Dissection"][/sc]
  • Pulse deficit - Asymmetrical pulses due to disruption of arterial flow to one arm or leg from the dissection.
  • Hypotension - May signify impending rupture, cardiac tamponade, or massive blood loss.
  • Aortic regurgitation murmur - A diastolic murmur may be present if the dissection involves the aortic root and damages the aortic valve.
  • Neurological deficits - Can occur if the dissection affects arteries supplying the brain or spinal cord.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Aortic Dissection"][/sc]
  • Sudden severe chest or back pain - Suggests acute dissection; rupture risk is high.
  • Syncope - Indicates severe compromise of blood flow to the brain or cardiac tamponade.
  • Pulse deficits or differential blood pressure in arms - Signifies possible arterial occlusion from dissection.
  • Hypotension with chest pain - Suggests rupture or tamponade, a critical complication requiring immediate intervention.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Aortic Dissection"][/sc]
  • Isolated abdominal pain - Dissections affecting the abdominal aorta may present with isolated abdominal pain, mimicking other conditions like pancreatitis.
  • Isolated neurological symptoms - Rarely, the dissection may affect cerebral circulation leading to isolated neurological symptoms such as transient ischaemic attacks or stroke.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Aortic Dissection"][/sc]
  • CT angiography - The gold standard for diagnosing aortic dissection. It shows the intimal tear, true and false lumens, and extension of the dissection. Findings: false lumen with contrast enhancement of the true lumen.
  • Echocardiography (Transoesophageal) - Useful in emergencies to detect proximal dissections and assess for aortic regurgitation or tamponade.
  • Chest X-ray - May show a widened mediastinum, though this is not always present.
  • Blood tests - Raised D-dimer can support the diagnosis but is not specific to dissection.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Aortic Dissection"][/sc]
  • A 65-year-old male with a history of hypertension presents with sudden onset severe "tearing" chest pain radiating to the back, associated with syncope. Examination reveals unequal pulses and hypotension.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Aortic Dissection"][/sc]
  • Myocardial infarction - Presents with chest pain but is more likely to cause diffuse, crushing pain rather than tearing pain. ECG changes (ST elevation) and elevated troponins support MI diagnosis.
  • Pulmonary embolism - Acute chest pain and dyspnoea, but often presents with pleuritic pain and is associated with hypoxia. CT pulmonary angiography can differentiate it.
  • Pericarditis - Presents with sharp chest pain relieved by sitting forward, often associated with viral infection. ECG shows diffuse ST elevation.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Aortic Dissection"][/sc]
  • Medical Management - Immediate blood pressure control with IV beta-blockers (e.g., labetalol) to reduce shear stress on the aortic wall. Target systolic BP <120 mmHg.
  • Surgical Management - Type A dissections (involving the ascending aorta) require emergency surgery to prevent rupture or tamponade. Type B dissections may be managed conservatively with medications unless complications arise.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Aortic Dissection"][/sc]
  • Aortic rupture - Often fatal due to massive haemorrhage.
  • Cardiac tamponade - Accumulation of blood in the pericardial sac, leading to shock and death if not promptly treated.
  • Stroke - Due to involvement of the arteries supplying the brain.
  • Organ ischaemia - Dissection may compromise blood supply to kidneys, intestines, or spinal cord, causing renal failure, mesenteric ischaemia, or paraplegia.
