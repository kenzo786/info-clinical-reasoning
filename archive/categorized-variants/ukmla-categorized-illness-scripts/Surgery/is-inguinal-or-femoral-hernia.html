Inguinal or Femoral Hernia
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Inguinal or Femoral Hernia"][/sc]
  • An inguinal or femoral hernia is the protrusion of abdominal contents, such as bowel or fat, through a weakness in the abdominal wall. - **Inguinal hernia**: Protrudes through the inguinal canal (direct or indirect). - **Femoral hernia**: Protrudes through the femoral canal, appearing below the inguinal ligament. These are more common in women.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Inguinal or Femoral Hernia"][/sc]
  • Prevalence in the UK GP population: 3-8% of men, with femoral hernias rarer (0.5%).
  • UK hospital admissions: Common for elective and emergency surgical referrals.
  • Worldwide prevalence: Estimated at 15-25% lifetime risk in men, lower in women, with a higher proportion of femoral hernias in women.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Inguinal or Femoral Hernia"][/sc]
  • Increased intra-abdominal pressure (e.g., chronic coughing, heavy lifting) weakens the abdominal wall.
  • Congenital factors (e.g., patent processus vaginalis) contribute to indirect inguinal hernias.
  • Femoral hernias occur due to a tight femoral ring, more common in women due to wider pelvic anatomy.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Inguinal or Femoral Hernia"][/sc]
  • Gradual development over weeks/months; can remain asymptomatic.
  • Sudden onset in the case of incarceration or strangulation, requiring urgent medical attention.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Inguinal or Femoral Hernia"][/sc]
  • Groin lump - Typically soft and reducible, pathophysiologically due to increased intra-abdominal contents entering the hernia sac.
  • Discomfort or pain - Exacerbated by activity or strain; caused by tissue stretch or compression of nearby structures.
  • Bowel obstruction symptoms (nausea, vomiting) - Seen in strangulated hernias due to lumen compromise.
  • Asymptomatic - Common presentation; hernia detected on physical examination or incidentally.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Inguinal or Femoral Hernia"][/sc]
  • Palpable mass in the groin - Reducible or non-reducible, indicating the presence of herniated contents.
  • Visible bulge - Increases on coughing or straining, due to intra-abdominal pressure forcing contents into the hernia sac.
  • Signs of strangulation - Tenderness, erythema, and warmth suggest ischaemia or necrosis.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Inguinal or Femoral Hernia"][/sc]
  • Irreducible lump - Indicates incarceration; requires urgent assessment.
  • Severe pain - Suggests strangulation or impending ischaemia.
  • Skin changes (erythema, necrosis) - Signify compromised blood flow and possible necrosis.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Inguinal or Femoral Hernia"][/sc]
  • Groin pain without visible bulge - May indicate a small or deep hernia.
  • Chronic intermittent discomfort - Especially in the case of a sliding or intermittent hernia.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Inguinal or Femoral Hernia"][/sc]
  • Physical examination - Primary diagnostic method, observing reducibility and cough impulse.
  • Ultrasound - Confirms diagnosis, especially in equivocal cases or suspected femoral hernias.
  • CT or MRI - Used for complex cases or when complications like strangulation are suspected.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Inguinal or Femoral Hernia"][/sc]
  • A 65-year-old man presents with a gradually enlarging, painless groin lump that becomes painful after heavy lifting, with no systemic symptoms.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Inguinal or Femoral Hernia"][/sc]
  • Lymphadenopathy - Typically firm and non-reducible, often associated with systemic infection or malignancy.
  • Femoral aneurysm - Pulsatile mass, confirmed by Doppler ultrasound.
  • Hydrocele - Transilluminates and does not change with coughing.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Inguinal or Femoral Hernia"][/sc]
  • Elective surgical repair - Recommended for symptomatic hernias, using open or laparoscopic techniques.
  • Emergency surgery - Required for incarcerated or strangulated hernias to prevent necrosis.
  • Conservative management - Considered in asymptomatic elderly patients with significant comorbidities.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Inguinal or Femoral Hernia"][/sc]
  • Strangulation - Leads to ischaemia and necrosis of herniated bowel; occurs in 2-5% of cases.
  • Obstruction - Causes bowel obstruction symptoms; seen in larger or incarcerated hernias.
  • Recurrence - Post-surgical recurrence rate is 1-5% depending on repair technique.
