Coeliac Disease
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Coeliac Disease"][/sc]
  • Coeliac disease is a chronic autoimmune disorder where ingestion of gluten (found in wheat, barley, and rye) leads to inflammation and damage of the small intestinal mucosa, specifically the villi, resulting in malabsorption. This reaction is mediated by an abnormal immune response to gliadin, a component of gluten.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Coeliac Disease"][/sc]
  • Prevalence in UK GP population: 1% (though many cases remain undiagnosed).
  • Prevalence in UK hospital settings: Higher in populations presenting with diarrhoea or malabsorption syndromes, up to 4% in some studies.
  • Worldwide prevalence: 1% of the population, though higher rates seen in Europe and North America.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Coeliac Disease"][/sc]
  • When gluten is ingested, the gliadin component interacts with tissue transglutaminase (tTG) in the small intestine. This modified gliadin is presented by antigen-presenting cells to CD4+ T cells, triggering an inflammatory response.
  • The inflammation damages the villi of the small intestine, causing villous atrophy, crypt hyperplasia, and infiltration of lymphocytes.
  • This leads to impaired absorption of nutrients, particularly fat, iron, folate, and calcium, contributing to malnutrition and related symptoms.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Coeliac Disease"][/sc]
  • Coeliac disease can develop at any age after gluten introduction, but symptoms may develop gradually over months or years.
  • Patients may present acutely with diarrhoea or abdominal pain, but more often it manifests as a chronic illness with nonspecific symptoms such as fatigue or anaemia.
  • With a gluten-free diet, improvement typically begins within a few weeks, but complete mucosal healing may take months to years.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Coeliac Disease"][/sc]
  • Diarrhoea - Due to malabsorption of fat (steatorrhoea), leading to loose, pale, and foul-smelling stools.
  • Abdominal pain - Secondary to inflammation and irritation of the intestinal lining.
  • Bloating - Caused by malabsorption and fermentation of undigested carbohydrates in the bowel.
  • Fatigue - Due to iron, folate, and other nutrient deficiencies caused by malabsorption.
  • Weight loss - Resulting from malnutrition and reduced caloric intake.
  • Dermatitis herpetiformis - A pruritic, blistering skin rash associated with coeliac disease.
  • Asymptomatic - Many individuals with coeliac disease may not have symptoms, especially those diagnosed via screening for family history or associated conditions.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Coeliac Disease"][/sc]
  • Pallor - Indicative of iron deficiency anaemia, common due to malabsorption of iron in the small intestine.
  • Angular stomatitis - A sign of B vitamin deficiencies (e.g., B12, folate) due to malabsorption.
  • Dermatitis herpetiformis - A pathognomonic rash associated with coeliac disease, found in 10-15% of patients.
  • Oedema - Can occur due to protein deficiency (hypoalbuminaemia).
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Coeliac Disease"][/sc]
  • Unexplained weight loss - Suggests severe malabsorption and risk of malnutrition or malignancy (e.g., enteropathy-associated T-cell lymphoma).
  • Persistent diarrhoea despite gluten-free diet - May indicate refractory coeliac disease or an alternative diagnosis such as small intestinal bacterial overgrowth (SIBO).
  • Iron deficiency anaemia unresponsive to iron supplements - Indicates ongoing malabsorption or refractory coeliac disease.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Coeliac Disease"][/sc]
  • Isolated iron deficiency anaemia - In some patients, iron deficiency may be the only manifestation of coeliac disease, without GI symptoms.
  • Neurological symptoms - Peripheral neuropathy or ataxia may occur, thought to be related to vitamin deficiencies or immune-mediated mechanisms.
  • Osteoporosis - Due to malabsorption of calcium and vitamin D, leading to bone demineralisation.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Coeliac Disease"][/sc]
  • Tissue transglutaminase IgA (tTG-IgA) - First-line test for coeliac disease; elevated due to the autoimmune response. Sensitivity ~98% in untreated coeliac disease.
  • Endomysial antibodies (EMA) - Another highly specific antibody test for coeliac disease.
  • Total IgA - Measured to rule out IgA deficiency, which can lead to false-negative tTG-IgA results.
  • Duodenal biopsy - Confirms diagnosis, showing villous atrophy, crypt hyperplasia, and lymphocytic infiltration.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Coeliac Disease"][/sc]
  • A 35-year-old female presents with a 6-month history of chronic diarrhoea, fatigue, and weight loss. Blood tests reveal iron deficiency anaemia, and her tissue transglutaminase IgA is positive.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Coeliac Disease"][/sc]
  • Irritable bowel syndrome (IBS) - Unlike coeliac disease, IBS does not cause malabsorption or weight loss. Diagnosis of exclusion, based on clinical symptoms.
  • Inflammatory bowel disease (IBD) - Crohnâ€™s disease and ulcerative colitis may also present with diarrhoea and weight loss but are differentiated by endoscopic findings and lack of response to a gluten-free diet.
  • Small intestinal bacterial overgrowth (SIBO) - Overlaps in symptoms like bloating and diarrhoea, but hydrogen breath testing can confirm SIBO.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Coeliac Disease"][/sc]
  • Gluten-free diet - Lifelong strict adherence to a gluten-free diet is the cornerstone of treatment. Regular dietician input is crucial for maintaining adequate nutrition.
  • Monitoring - Regular follow-up with serological tests (tTG-IgA) and clinical assessments to ensure adherence and resolution of symptoms.
  • Supplementation - Replace any deficiencies, such as iron, folate, calcium, and vitamin D.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Coeliac Disease"][/sc]
  • Osteoporosis - Due to chronic malabsorption of calcium and vitamin D.
  • Refractory coeliac disease - A rare condition where symptoms persist despite a gluten-free diet, potentially leading to enteropathy-associated T-cell lymphoma.
  • Enteropathy-associated T-cell lymphoma - A rare but severe complication, more common in long-standing, untreated coeliac disease.
