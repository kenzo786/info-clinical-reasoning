Anaemia
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Anaemia"][/sc]
  • Anaemia is defined as a reduction in the oxygen-carrying capacity of the blood, typically due to a decrease in red blood cells (RBCs) or haemoglobin concentration. In the UK, anaemia is typically diagnosed when haemoglobin levels are less than 130 g/L in men and less than 120 g/L in women. It can be caused by a variety of factors, including blood loss, decreased RBC production, or increased RBC destruction.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Anaemia"][/sc]
  • UK GP prevalence: Anaemia is common in primary care, affecting around 4-8% of adults.
  • UK hospital prevalence: It is frequently diagnosed in hospitalised patients, particularly in those with chronic conditions, with prevalence ranging from 10-20% in general wards.
  • Worldwide prevalence: Globally, anaemia affects over 1.6 billion people, with the highest prevalence in low- and middle-income countries.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Anaemia"][/sc]
  • Blood loss: Acute or chronic bleeding can lead to iron deficiency anaemia due to the loss of iron-containing RBCs.
  • Decreased RBC production: This can occur due to iron, vitamin B12, or folate deficiency, or due to chronic diseases that impair erythropoiesis (e.g., chronic kidney disease).
  • Increased RBC destruction: Conditions like haemolytic anaemias lead to premature destruction of RBCs, causing anaemia.
  • Impaired haemoglobin synthesis: Conditions such as thalassaemia result in defective haemoglobin production, leading to anaemia.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Anaemia"][/sc]
  • Acute anaemia: May develop quickly in cases of acute blood loss, presenting within hours to days.
  • Chronic anaemia: Develops slowly over weeks to months in conditions like chronic disease or nutritional deficiencies, allowing time for physiological adaptation, leading to fewer symptoms.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Anaemia"][/sc]
  • Fatigue: Reduced oxygen delivery to tissues results in decreased energy production and generalised tiredness.
  • Dyspnoea on exertion: Reduced haemoglobin levels limit oxygen transport, causing shortness of breath during physical activity.
  • Dizziness: Decreased oxygenation of the brain can cause light-headedness or dizziness.
  • Pallor: Less haemoglobin in the blood gives the skin a pale appearance, especially noticeable in areas like the conjunctivae.
  • Asymptomatic: Anaemia can be asymptomatic, particularly if it develops gradually and the body has time to compensate.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Anaemia"][/sc]
  • Pallor: Visible paleness of the skin and mucous membranes due to decreased oxyhaemoglobin.
  • Tachycardia: A compensatory increase in heart rate occurs to maintain oxygen delivery to tissues.
  • Glossitis: In conditions like iron or vitamin B12 deficiency, the tongue may become swollen, smooth, and red.
  • Spoon nails (koilonychia): Typically associated with iron deficiency anaemia, leading to thin, brittle nails with a concave shape.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Anaemia"][/sc]
  • Severe fatigue with chest pain: May indicate heart failure or myocardial ischaemia due to inadequate oxygen delivery.
  • Dark or bloody stools (melaena): Can indicate gastrointestinal bleeding as a cause of iron deficiency anaemia.
  • Jaundice: Suggests haemolytic anaemia, where RBCs are destroyed at a faster rate, causing bilirubin build-up.
  • Weight loss and night sweats: Could be indicative of malignancy or chronic infection, causing anaemia of chronic disease.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Anaemia"][/sc]
  • Mild anaemia in elderly patients: Often presents with vague symptoms like weakness or cognitive impairment, which may be mistaken for ageing.
  • Anaemia in pregnancy: May present with normal haemoglobin levels but reduced in relation to the increased plasma volume (physiological anaemia of pregnancy).
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Anaemia"][/sc]
  • Full blood count (FBC): Low haemoglobin (<130 g/L in men, <120 g/L in women) confirms anaemia. Low mean corpuscular volume (MCV) indicates microcytic anaemia, commonly from iron deficiency.
  • Ferritin levels: Low ferritin indicates iron deficiency anaemia, while high levels suggest anaemia of chronic disease.
  • Vitamin B12 and folate levels: Deficiencies lead to macrocytic anaemia with elevated MCV.
  • Reticulocyte count: A low count indicates decreased RBC production, while a high count suggests haemolysis or blood loss.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Anaemia"][/sc]
  • A 65-year-old woman presents with progressive fatigue, shortness of breath on exertion, and pallor over the past three months. Full blood count reveals a haemoglobin level of 95 g/L and low ferritin.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Anaemia"][/sc]
  • Iron deficiency anaemia: Often due to blood loss, especially gastrointestinal, presents with low ferritin and microcytic anaemia.
  • Anaemia of chronic disease: Inflammatory conditions cause normal or raised ferritin and normocytic or microcytic anaemia.
  • Vitamin B12 deficiency: Presents with macrocytic anaemia and neurological symptoms like paraesthesia and gait disturbances.
  • Thalassaemia: Genetic disorder causing microcytic anaemia with normal iron levels and target cells on blood film.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Anaemia"][/sc]
  • Iron deficiency anaemia: Oral iron supplements (ferrous sulfate) are first-line treatment. Intravenous iron is used in cases of poor absorption or intolerance.
  • Vitamin B12 deficiency: Intramuscular B12 injections, followed by oral maintenance therapy.
  • Anaemia of chronic disease: Treatment focuses on managing the underlying condition, with erythropoiesis-stimulating agents in select cases.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Anaemia"][/sc]
  • Heart failure: Chronic anaemia leads to compensatory mechanisms such as tachycardia, which may strain the heart and lead to failure.
  • Impaired cognitive function: Particularly in the elderly, chronic anaemia can contribute to cognitive decline and dementia.
  • Infections: Severe anaemia can weaken the immune system, increasing susceptibility to infections.
