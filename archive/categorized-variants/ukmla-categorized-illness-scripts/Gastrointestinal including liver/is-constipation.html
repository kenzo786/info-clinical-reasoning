Constipation
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Constipation"][/sc]
  • Constipation is defined as infrequent bowel movements (typically less than three per week) or difficulty in passing stools. It is commonly associated with hard, dry stools, straining, and the sensation of incomplete evacuation. Chronic constipation is often diagnosed when these symptoms persist for more than three months.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Constipation"][/sc]
  • UK GP prevalence: Around 12% of adults are affected by constipation at some point. More common in the elderly and women.
  • UK hospital prevalence: Constipation accounts for about 1% of hospital admissions, often seen in patients with comorbidities or on multiple medications.
  • Worldwide prevalence: Globally, about 14% of the population experiences constipation, with higher rates in Western countries.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Constipation"][/sc]
  • Constipation occurs due to slowed colonic transit, impaired defecation, or a combination of both. Slowed transit may result from inadequate water absorption, a low-fibre diet, or dysmotility. Impaired defecation could be due to pelvic floor dysfunction or weakened peristalsis. Medications like opioids or conditions such as hypothyroidism may also affect bowel movement through various mechanisms like altering neurotransmitter signalling or fluid regulation in the gut.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Constipation"][/sc]
  • Acute constipation can occur suddenly, often linked to diet changes, medication use, or stress. Chronic constipation develops over weeks to months, and symptoms can persist for longer than three months. Constipation may also alternate with periods of normal bowel function.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Constipation"][/sc]
  • Infrequent bowel movements - Defined as fewer than three bowel movements per week. This occurs due to slowed colonic transit or poor dietary fibre intake.
  • Hard, lumpy stools - Due to excessive water absorption in the colon, leading to dehydration of stool contents.
  • Straining during defecation - Associated with weakened peristalsis or pelvic floor dysfunction, making bowel movements more difficult.
  • Sensation of incomplete evacuation - May result from impaired rectal sensation or outlet obstruction.
  • Abdominal discomfort - Caused by the accumulation of hard stools or gas in the intestines, leading to bloating and cramps.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Constipation"][/sc]
  • Abdominal distension - Caused by the accumulation of gas or fecal matter in the intestines.
  • Palpable mass in the abdomen - May indicate fecal impaction, especially in the left lower quadrant (colon).
  • Reduced bowel sounds - Suggests reduced peristalsis, a sign of slow colonic transit.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Constipation"][/sc]
  • Unintentional weight loss - Could indicate malignancy or a systemic disease such as hyperthyroidism or coeliac disease.
  • Rectal bleeding - Suggests a more serious underlying condition such as colorectal cancer, diverticulitis, or haemorrhoids.
  • Family history of colorectal cancer - Increases the risk of malignancy, especially in older adults.
  • Severe, persistent abdominal pain - Could indicate bowel obstruction or an inflammatory condition such as diverticulitis.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Constipation"][/sc]
  • Alternating constipation and diarrhoea - May be a sign of irritable bowel syndrome or colorectal cancer, where bowel function becomes irregular.
  • Non-specific systemic symptoms - Fatigue and malaise could be linked to hypothyroidism or other metabolic disorders that affect gut motility.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Constipation"][/sc]
  • Full blood count (FBC) - May reveal anaemia, which could indicate colorectal cancer or gastrointestinal bleeding.
  • Thyroid function tests - Hypothyroidism is a common metabolic cause of constipation.
  • Colonoscopy - Used to rule out colorectal cancer or structural abnormalities if red flags are present, particularly in patients over 50.
  • Stool testing for occult blood (FOB test) - To detect hidden blood in stool, which can be a sign of colorectal cancer.
  • Abdominal X-ray - May show faecal loading or obstruction, especially in severe or chronic cases.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Constipation"][/sc]
  • A 65-year-old female presents with a three-month history of infrequent bowel movements, hard stools, and straining. She reports mild abdominal discomfort but no weight loss or rectal bleeding. Symptoms worsened after starting opioid analgesics for osteoarthritis.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Constipation"][/sc]
  • Irritable Bowel Syndrome (IBS) - Alternating diarrhoea and constipation, often associated with abdominal pain and bloating. Differs from chronic constipation as it presents with fluctuating bowel habits.
  • Colorectal cancer - Persistent change in bowel habits, unintentional weight loss, rectal bleeding, and anaemia may be present. Red flags differentiate it from benign constipation.
  • Hypothyroidism - Symptoms include fatigue, weight gain, dry skin, and slow heart rate in addition to constipation. TSH levels confirm diagnosis.
  • Faecal impaction - Severe constipation leading to a palpable mass and possibly overflow diarrhoea. Abdominal X-ray confirms the diagnosis.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Constipation"][/sc]
  • Lifestyle modifications: Increase dietary fibre (aim for 30g per day) and water intake. Encourage regular physical activity.
  • First-line pharmacological treatment: Bulk-forming laxatives such as ispaghula husk (Fybogel). If ineffective, consider osmotic laxatives like lactulose.
  • Second-line treatment: Stimulant laxatives such as senna or bisacodyl for short-term use in refractory cases.
  • Treat underlying causes: Address contributing factors such as hypothyroidism or medication use (e.g., reduce opioid use if possible).
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Constipation"][/sc]
  • Faecal impaction: Occurs in severe cases where hard stools accumulate in the colon, leading to obstruction.
  • Haemorrhoids: Straining during defecation can lead to swollen blood vessels in the rectum, causing pain and bleeding.
  • Rectal prolapse: Chronic straining may weaken pelvic muscles, leading to prolapse of rectal tissue through the anus.
  • Colorectal cancer: Chronic constipation has been linked to an increased risk of colorectal cancer, though the exact mechanism is unclear.
