Retinal Vein Occlusion (RVO)
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Retinal Vein Occlusion (RVO)"][/sc]
  • Retinal vein occlusion (RVO) is a vascular disorder characterised by the obstruction of the retinal venous circulation, leading to retinal ischaemia, haemorrhage, and oedema. It is classified into two main types: central retinal vein occlusion (CRVO) and branch retinal vein occlusion (BRVO), depending on whether the central retinal vein or one of its branches is affected.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Retinal Vein Occlusion (RVO)"][/sc]
  • Prevalence in the UK GP population: Approximately 1-2% of individuals over 40 years of age.
  • UK hospital prevalence: Common cause of visual loss in older adults, with CRVO being less common than BRVO.
  • Worldwide prevalence: Estimated at 16 million cases globally, with BRVO being approximately 4 times more prevalent than CRVO.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Retinal Vein Occlusion (RVO)"][/sc]
  • Obstruction of the retinal vein leads to increased venous pressure, causing stagnation of blood flow and subsequent retinal hypoxia.
  • Hypoxia triggers the release of vascular endothelial growth factor (VEGF), promoting vascular permeability and oedema.
  • Venous obstruction may result from atherosclerosis of adjacent arteries, hypercoagulability, or external compression.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Retinal Vein Occlusion (RVO)"][/sc]
  • Acute Phase: Sudden onset of visual loss or blurring due to retinal oedema and haemorrhage.
  • Chronic Phase: Over weeks to months, resolution of oedema may occur, but complications like neovascularisation and macular degeneration can develop.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Retinal Vein Occlusion (RVO)"][/sc]
  • Sudden, painless vision loss or blurring: Due to macular oedema or haemorrhage, reflecting impaired retinal perfusion.
  • Scotomas or blind spots: Localised retinal ischaemia causes areas of non-functioning retina.
  • Fluctuating vision: Oedema and haemorrhage variability can cause intermittent visual changes.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Retinal Vein Occlusion (RVO)"][/sc]
  • Retinal haemorrhages ("blood and thunder" appearance): Resulting from increased venous pressure and rupture of capillaries.
  • Macular oedema: Secondary to VEGF-induced vascular leakage.
  • Venous dilation and tortuosity: Due to increased intraluminal pressure.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Retinal Vein Occlusion (RVO)"][/sc]
  • Neovascularisation: Indicates severe ischaemia, with a risk of neovascular glaucoma and further visual loss.
  • Severe or sudden bilateral visual loss: Suggestive of systemic or bilateral retinal vascular pathology.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Retinal Vein Occlusion (RVO)"][/sc]
  • Gradual vision loss: Can occur in chronic RVO cases with slow progression of macular oedema.
  • Minimal symptoms: In cases of peripheral BRVO, visual acuity may be preserved.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Retinal Vein Occlusion (RVO)"][/sc]
  • Fundoscopy: Haemorrhages, oedema, venous dilation and tortuosity.
  • Optical coherence tomography (OCT): Shows macular oedema and retinal thickening.
  • Fluorescein angiography: Identifies areas of capillary non-perfusion and neovascularisation.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Retinal Vein Occlusion (RVO)"][/sc]
  • A 65-year-old male with a history of hypertension presents with sudden, painless vision loss in the left eye over 2 days, with no systemic symptoms.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Retinal Vein Occlusion (RVO)"][/sc]
  • Diabetic retinopathy: Differentiated by bilateral involvement and presence of microaneurysms.
  • Age-related macular degeneration: Distinguished by drusen and central geographic atrophy.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Retinal Vein Occlusion (RVO)"][/sc]
  • Intravitreal anti-VEGF therapy: First-line treatment for macular oedema.
  • Laser photocoagulation: For ischaemia and neovascularisation.
  • Management of systemic risk factors: Control of hypertension and diabetes.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Retinal Vein Occlusion (RVO)"][/sc]
  • Neovascular glaucoma: Secondary to ischaemia-induced VEGF release.
  • Chronic macular oedema: Leads to persistent visual impairment.
