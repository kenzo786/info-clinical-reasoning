Burning Mouth Syndrome (BMS)
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Burning Mouth Syndrome (BMS)"][/sc]
  • Burning Mouth Syndrome (BMS) is a chronic condition characterised by a persistent burning or tingling sensation in the oral mucosa, typically without visible clinical signs or lesions. The sensation often affects the tongue (glossodynia), lips, and other areas of the mouth. It is classified as primary (idiopathic) or secondary, the latter being associated with underlying conditions such as nutritional deficiencies or oral candidiasis.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Burning Mouth Syndrome (BMS)"][/sc]
  • UK GP population prevalence: Approximately 1.5% of the general population.
  • UK hospital prevalence: More common in specialist oral medicine clinics, though precise data is variable.
  • Worldwide prevalence: Estimated at 0.7% to 3.7%, with a higher prevalence in postmenopausal women, particularly in individuals aged 50-70 years.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Burning Mouth Syndrome (BMS)"][/sc]
  • Neurogenic dysfunction: Damage or dysfunction in peripheral or central nervous pathways involved in pain perception is hypothesised, especially dysfunction in small-diameter sensory fibres in the oral mucosa.
  • Altered taste perception: Loss of taste (hypogeusia) or dysgeusia may occur due to abnormal interactions between the gustatory and trigeminal nerves.
  • Hormonal factors: Oestrogen deficiency, particularly in postmenopausal women, is thought to contribute to mucosal sensitivity and neuropathic changes.
  • Psychogenic factors: Anxiety, depression, and chronic stress are commonly associated with BMS and may exacerbate the pain perception mechanisms in affected individuals.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Burning Mouth Syndrome (BMS)"][/sc]
  • Onset: Gradual onset, often following dental procedures, illnesses, or hormonal changes.
  • Course: Chronic, with symptoms persisting for months to years. Symptoms may fluctuate in intensity, often worsening throughout the day.
  • Duration: Persistent, often lasting over 6 months to several years if left untreated.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Burning Mouth Syndrome (BMS)"][/sc]
  • Burning sensation in the mouth - Persistent burning pain in the tongue, lips, or other oral areas. Pathophysiologically, this is due to dysfunction in nociceptive pathways or small-fibre neuropathy.
  • Xerostomia (dry mouth) - A sensation of dryness in the mouth, often related to altered salivary gland function or neuropathy affecting oral mucosal moisture perception.
  • Dysgeusia (altered taste) - Taste disturbances, such as metallic or bitter tastes, due to abnormal interactions between gustatory and pain pathways.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Burning Mouth Syndrome (BMS)"][/sc]
  • Absence of clinical lesions - Despite significant symptoms, the oral mucosa appears normal on physical examination, which helps distinguish primary BMS from other conditions like oral lichen planus.
  • Normal salivary flow rates - Despite complaints of dry mouth, objective measurements of salivary flow are typically within normal limits.
  • No localised sensory loss - Unlike other neuropathic conditions, patients typically do not have overt sensory deficits in the oral region on neurological examination.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Burning Mouth Syndrome (BMS)"][/sc]
  • Visible oral lesions - Could suggest conditions such as oral cancer, lichen planus, or candidiasis rather than BMS, requiring biopsy or further investigation.

Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Burning Mouth Syndrome (BMS)"][/sc]
  • Isolated taste disturbances without pain - In some cases, patients may present primarily with dysgeusia without significant burning sensations, which can lead to delayed diagnosis.
  • Intermittent burning pain - Some patients may report symptoms that come and go, rather than the classic persistent pain, which can be confused with other oral mucosal conditions.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Burning Mouth Syndrome (BMS)"][/sc]
  • Clinical diagnosis - Based on history and exclusion of other causes (e.g., infections, autoimmune diseases).
  • Full blood count (FBC) and ferritin - To exclude anaemia or iron deficiency as potential secondary causes.
  • Vitamin B12 and folate levels - Low levels are associated with secondary BMS and neuropathic symptoms.
  • Fasting glucose levels - To screen for diabetes mellitus, which can contribute to neuropathy and oral discomfort.
  • Salivary flow rate testing - Used to objectively rule out xerostomia despite subjective complaints of dry mouth.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Burning Mouth Syndrome (BMS)"][/sc]
  • A 55-year-old postmenopausal woman presents with a 6-month history of persistent burning sensation on the tongue and lips, worsening throughout the day, accompanied by dry mouth and metallic taste, with no visible oral lesions or significant findings on examination.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Burning Mouth Syndrome (BMS)"][/sc]
  • Oral candidiasis - Often presents with burning and pain but is accompanied by visible white plaques on the mucosa, which scrape off, unlike the normal mucosa in BMS.
  • Oral lichen planus - Painful oral condition with characteristic lacy, white, reticular patches on the buccal mucosa, absent in BMS.
  • SjÃ¶grenâ€™s syndrome - Causes dry mouth but is associated with dry eyes, systemic symptoms, and reduced salivary flow on objective testing.
  • Trigeminal neuralgia - Can present with oral burning but typically causes unilateral, sharp, lancinating pain, differing from the more diffuse, persistent pain in BMS.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Burning Mouth Syndrome (BMS)"][/sc]
  • Tricyclic antidepressants (e.g., amitriptyline) - Used at low doses for neuropathic pain management.
  • Topical capsaicin - Provides relief by desensitising pain receptors in the oral mucosa.
  • Cognitive behavioural therapy (CBT) - Effective for managing the psychological components of chronic pain in BMS patients.
  • Alpha-lipoic acid - Antioxidant therapy with some evidence of efficacy in reducing burning symptoms in idiopathic BMS.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Burning Mouth Syndrome (BMS)"][/sc]
  • Chronic pain and psychological impact - Long-standing burning pain can lead to anxiety, depression, and reduced quality of life.
  • Malnutrition - Persistent oral discomfort may lead to reduced oral intake, causing weight loss or nutritional deficiencies.
