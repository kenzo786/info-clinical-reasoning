Septal Haematoma
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Septal Haematoma"][/sc]
  • A septal haematoma is a collection of blood within the nasal septum, typically between the cartilage or bone and the perichondrium/periosteum. It usually results from trauma to the nose, causing bleeding that accumulates in this space. If untreated, the haematoma can impair blood supply to the cartilage, leading to necrosis, infection, and potential long-term deformities.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Septal Haematoma"][/sc]
  • Septal haematoma is relatively uncommon. It occurs more frequently in the context of nasal trauma, such as from sports injuries, accidents, or physical altercations.
  • It is more common in children and young adults due to the softer cartilage and less developed vascular network compared to adults.
  • Exact prevalence data for the UK population are limited, but it is encountered in hospital settings, particularly in emergency departments, following facial trauma.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Septal Haematoma"][/sc]
  • Septal haematomas form when trauma disrupts blood vessels within the septum. Blood accumulates between the septal cartilage and the perichondrium or periosteum, separating the cartilage from its blood supply.
  • The avascular cartilage of the septum relies on diffusion from the perichondrium for nutrients and oxygen. When separated, the cartilage undergoes necrosis if not revascularised promptly.
  • Untreated haematomas may become infected, leading to abscess formation, and eventually cause structural collapse of the septum, resulting in a "saddle nose" deformity.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Septal Haematoma"][/sc]
  • Immediate: Following trauma, a septal haematoma may form within minutes to hours, resulting in nasal obstruction and pain.
  • 24-48 hours: If untreated, the haematoma can enlarge, worsen the obstruction, and increase the risk of infection.
  • 48-72 hours: At this stage, necrosis of the septal cartilage may begin, potentially leading to long-term complications such as nasal deformity.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Septal Haematoma"][/sc]
  • Nasal obstruction: The haematoma causes swelling, which can block the nasal passage. This occurs as blood accumulates, compressing the airway.
  • Nasal pain: Pain occurs due to the pressure of the haematoma on surrounding tissues, particularly when the septal mucosa is stretched.
  • Nasal congestion: Patients may feel a fullness or blocked sensation due to the blood-filled septum preventing normal airflow.
  • Difficulty breathing through the nose: The obstruction from the haematoma reduces airflow, leading to difficulty breathing.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Septal Haematoma"][/sc]
  • Visible swelling of the septum: Inspection often reveals bilateral or unilateral swelling of the septum, typically bluish or reddish in colour. This indicates blood collection within the septum.
  • Fluctuant mass on palpation: On examination, a soft, fluctuant swelling can be palpated along the septum, confirming the presence of a fluid collection (blood).
  • Nasal asymmetry: As the haematoma expands, it may cause visible distortion of the external nose or asymmetry of the nostrils.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Septal Haematoma"][/sc]
  • Fever: Suggests infection, possibly progressing to a septal abscess, which could lead to more serious complications if untreated, such as cavernous sinus thrombosis.
  • Purulent nasal discharge: Indicates secondary bacterial infection. If accompanied by fever, it may signify an abscess.
  • Severe pain with erythema: May suggest the development of an abscess or impending cartilage necrosis.
  • Nasal deformity (saddle nose): A late-stage sign indicating cartilage necrosis and collapse due to untreated or infected septal haematoma.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Septal Haematoma"][/sc]
  • In some cases, particularly in children, a septal haematoma may present with minimal symptoms, such as mild nasal obstruction or discomfort, making it easy to overlook without careful examination.
  • Atypical presentations may also include delayed symptoms, where nasal obstruction becomes noticeable only after the haematoma enlarges or infection sets in.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Septal Haematoma"][/sc]
  • Anterior rhinoscopy: This is the primary diagnostic tool. It reveals a bulging, swollen septum. Fluctuance on palpation further supports the diagnosis of a haematoma.
  • Nasal endoscopy: May be used for more detailed visualisation, particularly if the haematoma is difficult to view on anterior rhinoscopy.
  • CT scan: Occasionally used if there is concern for associated fractures or deeper injury, but not routinely required for straightforward cases.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Septal Haematoma"][/sc]
  • A 12-year-old boy presents with nasal obstruction and mild pain 24 hours after sustaining a blow to the nose during a football match. Anterior rhinoscopy reveals a fluctuant swelling of the septum bilaterally.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Septal Haematoma"][/sc]
  • Nasal fracture: May also present with nasal pain and obstruction. However, fractures typically involve visible deformity, bruising, and crepitus rather than a fluctuant septal mass.
  • Septal abscess: Can be a progression from an untreated haematoma. Signs of infection, such as fever and purulent discharge, distinguish it from a simple haematoma.
  • Nasal polyps: Polyps can cause nasal obstruction but are usually not associated with trauma, pain, or fluctuant masses.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Septal Haematoma"][/sc]
  • Urgent drainage: The haematoma must be drained to prevent cartilage necrosis. This is typically done via incision and drainage under local or general anaesthesia, depending on patient age and comfort.
  • Antibiotics: Prophylactic antibiotics, such as flucloxacillin or co-amoxiclav, are often prescribed to prevent secondary infection and abscess formation.
  • Follow-up: Patients should be reviewed after 24-48 hours to ensure that the haematoma has not reaccumulated and that infection has not developed.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Septal Haematoma"][/sc]
  • Septal abscess: An untreated haematoma can become infected, forming an abscess. This can lead to cartilage destruction and necrosis.
  • Saddle nose deformity: Necrosis of the septal cartilage leads to collapse of the nasal structure, resulting in a characteristic depression of the nasal bridge.
  • Cavernous sinus thrombosis: Although rare, untreated infections can spread, leading to serious complications such as cavernous sinus thrombosis, which can be life-threatening.
