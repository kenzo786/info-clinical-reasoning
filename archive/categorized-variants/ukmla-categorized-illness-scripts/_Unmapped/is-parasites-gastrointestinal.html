Parasites (Gastrointestinal)
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Parasites (Gastrointestinal)"][/sc]
  • Gastrointestinal parasitic infections are caused by protozoa or helminths that invade and reside in the digestive tract. Common parasites include *Giardia lamblia*, *Entamoeba histolytica*, and helminths like *Ascaris lumbricoides*, *Trichuris trichiura*, and *Strongyloides stercoralis*. These parasites cause symptoms through disruption of normal gut function, inflammation, and nutrient malabsorption. They are a significant global health problem, particularly in developing countries, and can range from asymptomatic colonisation to life-threatening illness.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Parasites (Gastrointestinal)"][/sc]
  • In the UK, gastrointestinal parasitic infections are relatively uncommon, with cases mostly seen in returning travellers, immigrants, and certain immunocompromised populations. *Giardia lamblia* is the most commonly reported parasite in the UK. Prevalence is higher worldwide, especially in tropical and subtropical regions where inadequate sanitation and contaminated water are significant risk factors. Approximately 24% of the global population is estimated to be infected with soil-transmitted helminths.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Parasites (Gastrointestinal)"][/sc]
  • Gastrointestinal parasites can be ingested via contaminated food or water, where they then localise to the intestines. Protozoa like *Giardia* adhere to the small intestine's mucosal lining, disrupting absorption and leading to diarrhoea. Helminths like *Ascaris* and *Trichuris* may cause mechanical obstruction or inflammation, leading to malnutrition, abdominal pain, and growth retardation. Some parasites invade tissue and provoke immune responses, which can lead to further damage to the mucosa and systemic illness.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Parasites (Gastrointestinal)"][/sc]
  • The time course of gastrointestinal parasitic infections can vary. Infections can be acute, with symptoms appearing days to weeks after exposure, such as in giardiasis. Chronic infections, particularly helminths, may persist for months or years, leading to long-term complications like anaemia or malnutrition. Symptoms may be episodic, particularly in protozoal infections, where recurrent diarrhoea is common.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Parasites (Gastrointestinal)"][/sc]
  • Diarrhoea - Often watery and persistent, caused by damage to the intestinal mucosa, as seen in *Giardia* and *Entamoeba* infections. Malabsorption contributes to stool frequency and consistency.
  • Abdominal pain - Caused by inflammation, mechanical irritation, or obstruction, common in helminthic infections like *Ascaris*.
  • Weight loss - Resulting from malabsorption and nutrient loss, especially in chronic infections like *Giardia* or *Strongyloides*.
  • Nausea and vomiting - May occur due to irritation of the upper gastrointestinal tract.
  • Flatulence and bloating - Particularly seen in *Giardia* infection, related to disruption of normal digestion and fermentation processes in the intestines.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Parasites (Gastrointestinal)"][/sc]
  • Dehydration - Secondary to prolonged diarrhoea and vomiting, with signs such as dry mucous membranes and hypotension.
  • Abdominal tenderness - Often localised to the right lower quadrant or diffuse, depending on the extent of inflammation or parasitic burden.
  • Failure to thrive (in children) - A result of chronic malnutrition from parasitic infection, especially in *Ascaris* or *Trichuris* infections.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Parasites (Gastrointestinal)"][/sc]
  • Severe dehydration - Significant water and electrolyte loss can lead to shock, acute kidney injury, and death if untreated.
  • Rectal prolapse - Associated with heavy *Trichuris* (whipworm) infections, where straining during bowel movements causes rectal tissue to protrude.
  • Obstruction - Large *Ascaris* loads can cause bowel obstruction, which presents with severe abdominal pain, vomiting, and inability to pass stools.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Parasites (Gastrointestinal)"][/sc]
  • Asymptomatic infection - Many parasitic infections, especially helminths, may be asymptomatic, with carriers showing no signs of illness but contributing to transmission.
  • Fever and systemic symptoms - While gastrointestinal parasites typically cause localised symptoms, invasive amoebiasis can lead to liver abscesses and systemic fever.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Parasites (Gastrointestinal)"][/sc]
  • Stool microscopy and culture - Detects ova, cysts, and parasites. *Giardia*, *Entamoeba*, and helminths can be visualised under a microscope.
  • Stool antigen tests - Enzyme immunoassays (EIA) for *Giardia* and *Cryptosporidium* are more sensitive than microscopy.
  • Full blood count (FBC) - Eosinophilia is common in helminthic infections, indicating a parasitic response.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Parasites (Gastrointestinal)"][/sc]
  • A 32-year-old male returns from travel in Southeast Asia with 3 weeks of intermittent watery diarrhoea, abdominal pain, and 4 kg weight loss. Stool microscopy confirms *Giardia lamblia* infection.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Parasites (Gastrointestinal)"][/sc]
  • Inflammatory bowel disease (IBD) - Chronic diarrhoea and weight loss seen in IBD, but parasitic infections typically have travel history and eosinophilia.
  • IBS (Irritable bowel syndrome) - Can present similarly but lacks the systemic signs like weight loss or eosinophilia found in parasitic infections.
  • Bacterial gastroenteritis - Acute, more severe onset compared to chronic, and often self-limiting, unlike parasitic infections.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Parasites (Gastrointestinal)"][/sc]
  • *Giardia* infection - Treat with metronidazole 400 mg three times daily for 5 days (UK guidelines).
  • *Ascaris* infection - Albendazole 400 mg as a single dose.
  • Hydration - Oral or intravenous fluid rehydration for dehydration due to diarrhoea.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Parasites (Gastrointestinal)"][/sc]
  • Malnutrition and growth delay - Chronic parasitic infections like *Ascaris* or *Trichuris* impair nutrient absorption and lead to growth retardation, especially in children.
  • Liver abscesses - Seen in invasive *Entamoeba histolytica* infections, where the parasite migrates to the liver, causing abscess formation and fever.
  • Obstruction - Heavy worm loads in *Ascaris* infections can cause bowel obstruction, which may necessitate surgical intervention.
