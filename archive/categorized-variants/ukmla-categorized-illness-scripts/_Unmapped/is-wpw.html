Wolff-Parkinson-White (WPW) Syndrome
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Wolff-Parkinson-White (WPW) Syndrome"][/sc]
  • Wolff-Parkinson-White (WPW) syndrome is a congenital cardiac condition characterised by the presence of an accessory conduction pathway (Bundle of Kent), which leads to episodes of paroxysmal tachycardia. This accessory pathway allows for abnormal electrical conduction between the atria and ventricles, bypassing the normal atrioventricular (AV) node delay.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Wolff-Parkinson-White (WPW) Syndrome"][/sc]
  • WPW syndrome affects approximately 0.1-0.3% of the general population worldwide.
  • In the UK, it is estimated to affect 1 in 1,000 people. However, many individuals are asymptomatic, and the condition is often detected incidentally.
  • It is more commonly diagnosed in males and often presents in childhood or early adulthood.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Wolff-Parkinson-White (WPW) Syndrome"][/sc]
  • In WPW syndrome, the accessory pathway (Bundle of Kent) bypasses the normal AV node conduction, allowing rapid conduction from the atria to the ventricles.
  • This leads to a pre-excitation of the ventricles, manifesting as a shortened PR interval on an ECG.
  • Episodes of tachycardia are typically due to re-entrant circuits, where electrical impulses circle between the normal conduction system and the accessory pathway, causing paroxysmal supraventricular tachycardia (SVT).
  • In rare cases, this can result in life-threatening arrhythmias such as atrial fibrillation (AF) with rapid ventricular rates.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Wolff-Parkinson-White (WPW) Syndrome"][/sc]
  • WPW syndrome may present at any age, but symptoms often emerge during adolescence or early adulthood.
  • Some patients remain asymptomatic throughout their lives, while others may experience recurrent episodes of tachycardia, which can be paroxysmal (lasting seconds to hours).
  • In rare cases, rapid arrhythmias like AF may lead to sudden cardiac arrest.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Wolff-Parkinson-White (WPW) Syndrome"][/sc]
  • Palpitations - Most common symptom, resulting from episodes of rapid heart rate caused by re-entrant tachycardias through the accessory pathway.
  • Light-headedness or dizziness - Occurs due to decreased cardiac output during episodes of tachycardia, leading to reduced cerebral perfusion.
  • Syncope - May occur during prolonged episodes of tachycardia, especially if blood pressure falls critically due to poor ventricular filling.
  • Chest pain - Due to increased myocardial oxygen demand during tachycardia, or in the case of myocardial ischaemia.
  • Fatigue - Caused by frequent tachycardia episodes, leading to overall reduced physical endurance.
  • Asymptomatic - A significant proportion of individuals with WPW remain asymptomatic, especially in cases detected incidentally on ECG.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Wolff-Parkinson-White (WPW) Syndrome"][/sc]
  • Tachycardia - Often supraventricular in nature, with heart rates typically between 150-250 beats per minute during episodes.
  • Hypotension - Can occur during prolonged tachycardia due to reduced cardiac output.
  • Irregular pulse - If the patient develops atrial fibrillation or other irregular arrhythmias.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Wolff-Parkinson-White (WPW) Syndrome"][/sc]
  • Syncope with exertion - May indicate a life-threatening arrhythmia like ventricular fibrillation.
  • Chest pain with palpitations - Can suggest myocardial ischaemia due to rapid heart rates, and may herald an impending infarction.
  • Sudden cardiac arrest - Rare but life-threatening complication due to rapid ventricular response or ventricular fibrillation in WPW.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Wolff-Parkinson-White (WPW) Syndrome"][/sc]
  • WPW may present asymptomatically, especially if diagnosed incidentally on routine ECG, as the accessory pathway does not always result in symptomatic tachycardia.
  • Some patients may have vague symptoms such as occasional light-headedness or fatigue without clear episodes of palpitations, making the diagnosis more challenging.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Wolff-Parkinson-White (WPW) Syndrome"][/sc]
  • ECG - Classic findings include a shortened PR interval (<120 ms), presence of a delta wave (slurred upstroke of the QRS complex), and a widened QRS complex. These findings occur due to pre-excitation of the ventricles.
  • 24-hour Holter monitoring - Useful to capture intermittent episodes of tachycardia if initial ECG is inconclusive.
  • Electrophysiological study (EPS) - Definitive diagnostic test to map the accessory pathway and assess the risk of arrhythmias.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Wolff-Parkinson-White (WPW) Syndrome"][/sc]
  • A 25-year-old male presents with recurrent episodes of palpitations lasting 10-15 minutes, associated with light-headedness and occasional chest pain. ECG shows a delta wave, consistent with WPW syndrome.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Wolff-Parkinson-White (WPW) Syndrome"][/sc]
  • Atrial fibrillation - Can also cause palpitations, but lacks the classic ECG features of WPW (no delta wave, irregularly irregular rhythm).
  • AV nodal re-entrant tachycardia (AVNRT) - A type of supraventricular tachycardia (SVT) that lacks the delta wave and may not show a shortened PR interval.
  • Ventricular tachycardia - Can cause palpitations and hypotension, but typically presents with wide QRS complexes unrelated to pre-excitation pathways.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Wolff-Parkinson-White (WPW) Syndrome"][/sc]
  • Acute management - Vagal manoeuvres or IV adenosine for acute SVT episodes. In unstable patients, DC cardioversion may be required.
  • Long-term management - Catheter ablation of the accessory pathway is the definitive treatment. Anti-arrhythmic drugs like flecainide may be used if ablation is not feasible.
  • Follow-up - Regular follow-up with ECGs and monitoring for recurrence is important after treatment.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Wolff-Parkinson-White (WPW) Syndrome"][/sc]
  • Atrial fibrillation - Occurs in 10-30% of patients with WPW and can rapidly degenerate into ventricular fibrillation if untreated.
  • Sudden cardiac death - Rare but can occur due to rapid ventricular rates or degeneration into ventricular fibrillation.
  • Tachycardia-induced cardiomyopathy - Chronic untreated tachycardia can lead to heart failure.
