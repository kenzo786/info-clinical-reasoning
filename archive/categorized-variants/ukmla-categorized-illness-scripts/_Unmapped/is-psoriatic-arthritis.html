Psoriatic Arthritis
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Psoriatic Arthritis"][/sc]
  • Psoriatic arthritis (PsA) is a chronic inflammatory arthritis associated with psoriasis. It is characterised by joint pain, stiffness, and swelling, and commonly involves the peripheral joints, axial skeleton, and entheses (points where tendons and ligaments insert into bone). PsA is considered part of the broader category of spondyloarthropathies and may range from mild to severe, with potential joint destruction if untreated.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Psoriatic Arthritis"][/sc]
  • Prevalence in UK GP population: Approximately 0.1-0.3%.
  • Prevalence in UK hospital settings: Around 5-7% of all arthritis cases seen.
  • Worldwide prevalence: Varies; estimated at 0.1-1% in general populations but higher in patients with psoriasis (up to 30%).
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Psoriatic Arthritis"][/sc]
  • The exact pathophysiology of PsA is not fully understood but involves immune system dysregulation, likely triggered by genetic and environmental factors.
  • Autoimmune responses are directed against joint structures, entheses, and skin, often due to T-cell activation and subsequent cytokine release (IL-17, TNF-Î±) causing inflammation.
  • Chronic inflammation leads to synovial proliferation (pannus formation), joint erosion, and bone destruction, as well as enthesitis in areas like Achilles tendon insertions.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Psoriatic Arthritis"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Initial presentation of joint pain and stiffness, often in distal interphalangeal (DIP) joints, associated with skin lesions of psoriasis.
  • Progressive phase: Over months to years, additional joint involvement and progressive structural damage occur without treatment.
  • Chronic phase: Permanent joint deformity and functional impairment may develop in untreated cases, along with potential extra-articular manifestations.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Psoriatic Arthritis"][/sc]
  • Joint Pain - Most commonly affects hands and feet, particularly DIP joints; caused by inflammation within the joint capsule and surrounding entheses.
  • Joint Stiffness - Worse in the morning and lasting over 30 minutes, due to prolonged inflammation overnight and reduced joint fluidity.
  • Swelling - Often in fingers or toes (dactylitis), caused by inflammation and oedema in joint and surrounding soft tissues.
  • Psoriatic Skin Lesions - Plaque psoriasis commonly affects the scalp, elbows, and knees; triggered by immune-mediated epidermal hyperproliferation.
  • Fatigue - Due to chronic inflammation and immune activation, leading to systemic effects on energy metabolism.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Psoriatic Arthritis"][/sc]
  • Dactylitis ("Sausage digits") - Swelling of entire fingers or toes, resulting from inflammation of both the joint and enthesis.
  • Nail Pitting - Tiny indentations in the nails, linked to psoriatic involvement of the nail matrix.
  • Enthesitis - Tenderness at tendon insertion sites, such as Achilles or plantar fascia; inflammation of tendon insertion points.
  • Asymmetrical Arthritis - Predominantly affects a few joints on one side, often in hands and feet, due to the nature of psoriatic inflammation.
  • Skin Plaques - Raised, red, scaly patches typical of psoriasis, usually on extensor surfaces.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Psoriatic Arthritis"][/sc]
  • Spinal Involvement - Severe back pain or stiffness; may indicate axial PsA, which can cause significant functional limitation and deformity if untreated.
  • Ocular Symptoms - Eye pain or redness can signal uveitis, an inflammatory eye condition associated with PsA and other spondyloarthropathies, posing a risk for vision loss.
  • Severe Nail Changes - Marked nail destruction can signify more aggressive disease and correlates with higher risk of joint damage.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Psoriatic Arthritis"][/sc]
  • Isolated axial involvement without peripheral arthritis, which may resemble ankylosing spondylitis.
  • Arthritis without skin lesions (10-15% of cases), sometimes only with nail changes, making PsA difficult to distinguish from other arthropathies.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Psoriatic Arthritis"][/sc]
  • Inflammatory Markers (ESR, CRP) - Typically elevated due to systemic inflammation, but not specific to PsA.
  • X-ray - Shows joint erosion, periostitis, and "pencil-in-cup" deformity in advanced cases.
  • MRI - Useful for early detection of enthesitis and sacroiliitis; highlights active inflammation and structural changes.
  • Rheumatoid Factor (RF) and Anti-CCP Antibodies - Negative in PsA, helping differentiate from rheumatoid arthritis.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Psoriatic Arthritis"][/sc]
  • A 42-year-old female presents with 6 months of joint pain and swelling in her fingers and toes, morning stiffness lasting over 30 minutes, and a history of plaque psoriasis.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Psoriatic Arthritis"][/sc]
  • Rheumatoid Arthritis - Symmetrical joint involvement, typically with positive RF and anti-CCP antibodies.
  • Ankylosing Spondylitis - Mainly axial involvement; strong HLA-B27 association, less peripheral joint disease.
  • Osteoarthritis - Often affects weight-bearing joints with no systemic symptoms or inflammatory markers elevation.
  • Gout - Acute, often monoarticular arthritis, elevated serum uric acid, and crystals in joint fluid.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Psoriatic Arthritis"][/sc]
  • NSAIDs - Used for pain and inflammation control.
  • DMARDs (Methotrexate, Sulfasalazine) - First-line for disease-modifying therapy in PsA.
  • Biologics (TNF inhibitors, IL-17 inhibitors) - Used in moderate to severe cases or when DMARDs fail, following UK NICE guidelines.
  • Physiotherapy - Essential for maintaining joint function and mobility.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Psoriatic Arthritis"][/sc]
  • Joint Destruction - Progressive structural damage and deformity if untreated.
  • Cardiovascular Disease - Increased risk of atherosclerosis due to systemic inflammation.
  • Ocular Complications - Uveitis and other eye disorders associated with PsA can lead to vision impairment.
