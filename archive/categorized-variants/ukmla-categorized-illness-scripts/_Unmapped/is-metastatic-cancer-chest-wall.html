Metastatic Cancer (Chest Wall)
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Metastatic Cancer (Chest Wall)"][/sc]
  • Metastatic cancer involving the chest wall occurs when cancer from a primary site spreads to structures within the chest wall, which may include ribs, muscles, pleura, and adjacent soft tissues. This condition is characterised by local invasion and can present with pain, swelling, and potential compromise of adjacent structures. Common primary cancers that metastasise to the chest wall include breast, lung, and renal cell carcinomas.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Metastatic Cancer (Chest Wall)"][/sc]
  • Prevalence: Metastatic cancer involving the chest wall is relatively uncommon compared to other metastatic sites (e.g., liver, bones), but it represents a significant morbidity factor in cancers prone to bone metastasis.
  • UK Primary Care: Rare, as patients are generally referred to oncology centres following primary cancer diagnosis.
  • UK Hospital Prevalence: Common in oncology settings, particularly for breast cancer and lung cancer patients.
  • Worldwide: The prevalence varies depending on cancer type, with breast cancer as the most common origin of chest wall metastasis worldwide.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Metastatic Cancer (Chest Wall)"][/sc]
  • The metastatic process begins with cancer cells from the primary tumour detaching and invading the bloodstream or lymphatic system.
  • These cells then migrate to the chest wall, where they adhere to and invade local structures such as ribs, muscles, and pleura.
  • Metastasis to bone (including ribs) is often driven by factors that stimulate osteoclastic activity, leading to bone resorption, structural weakness, and pain.
  • Local invasion by tumour cells elicits an inflammatory response, leading to pain, swelling, and sometimes erythema of the overlying skin.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Metastatic Cancer (Chest Wall)"][/sc]
  • Early stage: Asymptomatic or mild pain that may be intermittent and associated with movement.
  • Progression: Increasingly persistent pain, swelling, and localised tenderness. There may be noticeable swelling or mass formation.
  • Late stage: Severe pain, functional impairment, possible neurological symptoms if adjacent nerves are involved, and skin changes such as ulceration.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Metastatic Cancer (Chest Wall)"][/sc]
  • Localized chest wall pain - Often the most common presenting symptom; occurs due to periosteal irritation and local invasion of bone or soft tissue.
  • Swelling or palpable mass - Caused by tumour growth in the chest wall tissues, particularly when metastasis involves the soft tissues.
  • Restricted movement - Pain and invasion of local muscles can limit movement, particularly in the shoulder and chest.
  • Dyspnoea (if pleura is involved) - When metastasis spreads to the pleura, it may cause pleural effusion, resulting in difficulty breathing.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Metastatic Cancer (Chest Wall)"][/sc]
  • Palpable mass on the chest wall - Indicates local tumour growth in the soft tissues or bone.
  • Tenderness over affected area - Due to periosteal involvement and inflammation, the area may be exquisitely tender.
  • Skin changes over the tumour (e.g., erythema or ulceration) - Occur with tumour growth extending to or affecting the skin; may appear red, warm, or ulcerated in advanced cases.
  • Decreased breath sounds (if pleural effusion is present) - Suggests pleural involvement, a common site for metastatic spread in chest wall cancers.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Metastatic Cancer (Chest Wall)"][/sc]
  • Severe, unrelenting pain - May indicate extensive bone involvement or nerve compression, requiring prompt management for pain control and structural support.
  • Neurological deficits (e.g., numbness or weakness in upper limb) - Suggests potential nerve or spinal cord involvement and requires urgent imaging.
  • Signs of pleural effusion (e.g., dyspnoea, orthopnoea) - Indicates pleural spread and can lead to respiratory compromise if untreated.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Metastatic Cancer (Chest Wall)"][/sc]
  • Asymptomatic chest wall mass - Some patients may have a palpable mass without pain, especially in slow-growing or indolent tumours.
  • Referred pain to shoulder or back - Metastasis to the ribs or upper chest wall may present with pain radiating to the shoulder or back rather than localised chest wall pain.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Metastatic Cancer (Chest Wall)"][/sc]
  • Chest X-ray - May show rib lytic lesions or soft tissue masses if bone is involved.
  • CT scan - Provides detailed imaging of bone and soft tissue structures; often shows masses, bone erosion, and pleural involvement.
  • MRI - Best for assessing soft tissue invasion and neural involvement; shows tumour spread into muscle, nerves, and potentially pleura.
  • Biopsy - Confirms diagnosis; histological analysis identifies cancer type, receptor status (e.g., ER, PR in breast cancer), and may guide treatment.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Metastatic Cancer (Chest Wall)"][/sc]
  • A 65-year-old female with a history of breast cancer presents with persistent chest wall pain, a palpable mass over the anterior chest, and restricted shoulder movement, progressing over 3 months.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Metastatic Cancer (Chest Wall)"][/sc]
  • Primary chest wall tumour (e.g., sarcoma) - Often presents with a firm, localised mass, but lacks the typical primary cancer history and systemic signs.
  • Osteomyelitis of the ribs - May present with localised pain and swelling, but often includes fever and systemic signs of infection.
  • Costochondritis - Causes localised chest wall pain but lacks a palpable mass and imaging evidence of bone destruction or soft tissue mass.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Metastatic Cancer (Chest Wall)"][/sc]
  • Pain management - Opioids, NSAIDs, and local radiation therapy for pain control based on UK cancer pain guidelines.
  • Systemic therapy - Chemotherapy, hormonal therapy, or targeted therapy based on primary cancer type, as per UK oncology protocols.
  • Radiation therapy - Palliative radiation for local tumour control and symptom relief.
  • Surgical intervention - Considered for structural support or if resection could alleviate pain or prevent complications.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Metastatic Cancer (Chest Wall)"][/sc]
  • Pathological fractures - Due to bone weakening from tumour invasion, commonly seen in rib metastases, leading to chronic pain and further immobility.
  • Pleural effusion - May complicate breathing and often requires drainage; can signify advanced disease.
  • Nerve compression syndromes - Chest wall tumours may compress nearby nerves, causing pain, numbness, or motor deficits in affected areas.
  • Prognosis - Generally poor, with survival heavily dependent on primary tumour type, extent of spread, and response to therapy.
