Osteochondritis Dissecans
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Osteochondritis Dissecans"][/sc]
  • Osteochondritis dissecans (OCD) is an idiopathic joint disorder where a segment of subchondral bone and its overlying cartilage separate, potentially leading to loose body formation within the joint space. It commonly affects the knee, but may also involve the elbow, ankle, and hip. The condition often arises in the paediatric and adolescent population and can progress to joint degeneration if untreated.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Osteochondritis Dissecans"][/sc]
  • In the UK, osteochondritis dissecans has a prevalence rate of approximately 15-29 per 100,000 among the general population.
  • The condition is more prevalent in adolescents and young adults, particularly those involved in sports, with males affected more often than females at a ratio of approximately 2:1.
  • Worldwide, prevalence estimates are similar, with a higher incidence among high-impact sports participants.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Osteochondritis Dissecans"][/sc]
  • The pathophysiology of osteochondritis dissecans is thought to be multifactorial, involving repetitive microtrauma or ischemia leading to focal subchondral bone necrosis. This necrosis can weaken the bone-cartilage interface, causing cartilage delamination and separation.
  • As the necrotic area fails to heal adequately, the subchondral bone may fragment, and fragments can become detached, resulting in a loose body within the joint and potential joint damage.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Osteochondritis Dissecans"][/sc]
  • Initial Phase: Early signs include intermittent pain and stiffness, typically occurring during activity and resolving with rest.
  • Progressive Phase: As the lesion advances, symptoms may increase in frequency and severity, with potential for joint locking or catching.
  • Advanced Phase: Detachment of the osteochondral fragment may cause persistent pain and joint dysfunction, eventually leading to secondary osteoarthritis if untreated.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Osteochondritis Dissecans"][/sc]
  • Joint Pain - Typically activity-related pain in the affected joint due to mechanical stress on the weakened subchondral bone and cartilage.
  • Swelling - Mild to moderate joint swelling caused by synovial irritation and inflammatory response to the unstable fragment.
  • Joint Stiffness - Common in the morning or after periods of inactivity as a result of joint effusion and inflammation.
  • Locking or Catching Sensation - Occurs due to loose body formation or instability of the osteochondral fragment within the joint.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Osteochondritis Dissecans"][/sc]
  • Tenderness Over Joint - Pain on palpation of the joint line, often corresponding to the location of the lesion.
  • Joint Effusion - Visible or palpable swelling due to inflammation and increased synovial fluid.
  • Decreased Range of Motion - Limited movement, especially in flexion and extension, due to pain or mechanical obstruction from a loose body.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Osteochondritis Dissecans"][/sc]
  • Persistent Joint Pain - May indicate progressive degeneration or failure of conservative management.
  • Recurrent Locking or Catching - Suggests loose body formation which may require surgical intervention to prevent further damage.
  • Significant Swelling or Joint Instability - Potential signs of advanced joint damage or secondary osteoarthritis.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Osteochondritis Dissecans"][/sc]
  • Presentation with only mild pain and no obvious mechanical symptoms can occur, especially in early stages, making early diagnosis challenging. This may be due to minimal necrosis that initially does not significantly affect the overlying cartilage.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Osteochondritis Dissecans"][/sc]
  • X-ray - May show subchondral lucency or fragment separation. Useful for initial assessment of bone changes.
  • MRI - Preferred modality; reveals cartilage status, subchondral bone integrity, and any loose bodies, giving a clearer indication of lesion stability.
  • CT Scan - Provides detailed information on lesion depth and stability when MRI is unavailable or inconclusive.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Osteochondritis Dissecans"][/sc]
  • A 14-year-old male presents with a six-month history of intermittent knee pain exacerbated by sports, along with occasional knee swelling and a recent locking episode.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Osteochondritis Dissecans"][/sc]
  • Patellofemoral Pain Syndrome - Differs by diffuse anterior knee pain with no mechanical symptoms.
  • Meniscal Tear - Presents with similar locking symptoms but often associated with acute trauma.
  • Juvenile Osteoarthritis - Rare but considered in persistent joint pain, with radiographic evidence of joint space narrowing.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Osteochondritis Dissecans"][/sc]
  • Non-Surgical - Rest, activity modification, and physiotherapy for stable lesions without loose bodies; recommended for early-stage OCD in adolescents.
  • Surgical - Indicated for unstable lesions or persistent symptoms, involving drilling, fixation, or fragment removal if necessary.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Osteochondritis Dissecans"][/sc]
  • Osteoarthritis - Increased risk due to joint incongruity or loose bodies leading to cartilage wear.
  • Joint Dysfunction - Persistent pain, limited movement, or recurrent effusion impacting quality of life and joint function.
