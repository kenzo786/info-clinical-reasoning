Metastatic Cancer
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Metastatic Cancer"][/sc]
  • Metastatic cancer refers to the spread of malignant cells from the primary tumour site to distant organs or tissues. This process involves invasion into surrounding tissue, entry into the bloodstream or lymphatic system, and colonisation of distant sites. Metastatic disease typically signifies an advanced stage of cancer, with significant implications for prognosis and management.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Metastatic Cancer"][/sc]
  • In the UK, metastatic cancer accounts for approximately 50% of cancer-related mortality. Around 16-18% of newly diagnosed cancer patients have metastasis at presentation.
  • Globally, about 10-30% of cancers are metastatic at diagnosis, with significant variation depending on tumour type and region.
  • Common cancers that metastasise include lung, breast, prostate, colorectal, and melanoma.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Metastatic Cancer"][/sc]
  • The process of metastasis begins with local invasion of the primary tumour into surrounding tissues.
  • Tumour cells enter the bloodstream or lymphatic system (intravasation) and travel to distant organs.
  • Upon reaching a distant site, cells exit the circulation (extravasation) and invade the new tissue environment, where they proliferate and establish secondary tumours.
  • The ability of cancer cells to survive and grow in foreign environments depends on factors such as immune evasion, angiogenesis, and resistance to apoptosis.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Metastatic Cancer"][/sc]
  • Metastasis may develop at any point during the course of cancer, though it often signifies late-stage disease. The time from initial diagnosis to metastasis can range from months to years depending on cancer type.
  • Early phase: asymptomatic or mild symptoms.
  • Mid phase: increasing symptoms related to the size and location of metastatic tumours.
  • Late phase: severe systemic symptoms (e.g., cachexia, severe pain) and organ failure if vital structures are involved.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Metastatic Cancer"][/sc]
  • Fatigue - Common due to systemic effects of cancer, such as anaemia, metabolic disturbances, and inflammatory cytokines.
  • Weight loss - Often related to cancer cachexia, where metabolic alterations lead to muscle wasting and fat loss.
  • Pain - Localised to sites of metastasis (e.g., bone, liver) due to tumour invasion of sensitive structures.
  • Cough or shortness of breath - Seen in lung metastasis due to obstruction or pleural effusion.
  • Neurological symptoms (e.g., headaches, seizures) - Occur with brain metastasis due to increased intracranial pressure or tumour invasion of brain tissue.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Metastatic Cancer"][/sc]
  • Palpable mass - May be noted in superficial organs such as the liver or soft tissue metastases.
  • Lymphadenopathy - Enlarged lymph nodes due to metastatic spread, particularly in the cervical, axillary, or inguinal regions.
  • Jaundice - A sign of liver metastasis, due to bile duct obstruction or liver dysfunction.
  • Neurological deficits - Focal deficits (e.g., weakness, aphasia) may occur in cases of brain or spinal cord metastasis.
  • Ascites - Abdominal distension due to peritoneal metastasis or liver involvement causing portal hypertension.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Metastatic Cancer"][/sc]
  • Severe, persistent pain - May indicate bone metastasis or nerve compression.
  • Sudden neurological changes - New onset seizures or focal neurological deficits can signify brain metastasis or spinal cord compression.
  • Severe dyspnoea - Could suggest large pleural effusion or lung metastasis impairing respiratory function.
  • Unexplained bleeding - May be a sign of coagulopathy or liver dysfunction due to extensive liver metastasis.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Metastatic Cancer"][/sc]
  • Some cancers metastasise in ways that are unusual for their type, such as gastrointestinal cancers presenting with brain metastasis. These may be missed unless thorough investigation is pursued.
  • Occult metastasis: Patients may present with symptoms like unexplained weight loss or fatigue without obvious primary tumour symptoms, complicating early detection.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Metastatic Cancer"][/sc]
  • Imaging (CT, MRI, PET) - Identifies metastatic deposits. CT and MRI show structural changes, while PET highlights metabolically active areas.
  • Biopsy - Tissue sampling confirms metastatic disease, revealing histology matching the primary tumour.
  • Tumour markers - Blood tests such as PSA (prostate), CEA (colorectal), and CA-125 (ovarian) can indicate metastatic spread.
  • Blood tests - Liver function tests (LFTs), calcium levels, and full blood counts may show abnormalities related to metastasis (e.g., hypercalcaemia, anaemia).
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Metastatic Cancer"][/sc]
  • A 65-year-old male presents with progressive fatigue, unintentional weight loss, and worsening right hip pain. CT scan reveals multiple lytic bone lesions, and biopsy confirms metastatic adenocarcinoma of the prostate.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Metastatic Cancer"][/sc]
  • Primary bone tumour - Typically presents with localised bone pain, but imaging and biopsy would show primary bone neoplasm rather than metastatic lesions.
  • Chronic infection (e.g., tuberculosis) - Can cause weight loss and systemic symptoms, but imaging shows granulomatous changes rather than metastatic spread.
  • Multiple myeloma - Presents with bone pain and lytic lesions, but associated with raised paraproteins and plasmacytosis in bone marrow.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Metastatic Cancer"][/sc]
  • Systemic therapy - Chemotherapy, immunotherapy, or targeted therapies depending on tumour type and receptor status (e.g., hormone therapy for prostate or breast cancer).
  • Palliative care - Symptom control, including analgesia, appetite stimulation, and management of side effects.
  • Radiotherapy - Used for local control of painful or high-risk metastases (e.g., brain, bone).
  • Surgery - In select cases, surgery may be needed for spinal cord compression or symptomatic metastasis.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Metastatic Cancer"][/sc]
  • Organ failure - Metastasis to vital organs such as the liver or lungs can lead to their failure.
  • Spinal cord compression - A life-threatening complication of vertebral metastasis.
  • Hypercalcaemia - Often seen in bone metastases, leading to confusion, polyuria, and arrhythmias.
  • Prognosis - Generally poor, with median survival varying widely based on tumour type, ranging from months to years depending on the response to treatment.
