Thoracic Radiculopathy
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Thoracic Radiculopathy"][/sc]
  • Thoracic radiculopathy is a clinical syndrome caused by compression, inflammation, or irritation of a thoracic nerve root. It typically manifests as pain radiating along the corresponding thoracic dermatome and may be associated with numbness, tingling, or weakness in the thoracic region. Less commonly, patients may experience visceral symptoms that mimic cardiac, abdominal, or pulmonary conditions due to the dermatomal distribution along the trunk.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Thoracic Radiculopathy"][/sc]
  • Thoracic radiculopathy is relatively rare compared to cervical and lumbar radiculopathies, representing <0.1% of all radiculopathy cases seen in the UK general population and approximately 0.5% in hospital settings. Globally, thoracic radiculopathy is uncommon, with exact prevalence data being limited. However, it is more common in older adults due to age-related degenerative changes.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Thoracic Radiculopathy"][/sc]
  • Thoracic radiculopathy is most often caused by degenerative disc disease, herniated thoracic discs, osteophyte formation, or spinal stenosis. These conditions compress the nerve root, leading to irritation or inflammation.
  • The thoracic nerves exit the spinal canal through the intervertebral foramen, where narrowing due to herniation, bone spurs, or disc degeneration can lead to nerve root impingement. This irritation results in the typical radicular pain along the nerveâ€™s dermatomal distribution.
  • Inflammation around the nerve root further contributes to pain, altered sensation, and potentially motor symptoms.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Thoracic Radiculopathy"][/sc]
  • Thoracic radiculopathy typically develops gradually due to chronic degenerative changes but can also have an acute onset if caused by trauma or disc herniation. Symptoms may fluctuate in intensity and duration over weeks to months. If left untreated, symptoms may become chronic and potentially debilitating.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Thoracic Radiculopathy"][/sc]
  • Radicular pain - Commonly presents as a sharp, burning, or shooting pain radiating along a thoracic dermatome; caused by nerve root irritation.
  • Numbness or tingling - Sensory deficits along the affected dermatome, resulting from compression or irritation of sensory nerve fibres.
  • Weakness - May occur in the intercostal or paraspinal muscles if motor nerve fibres are involved, though weakness is less common in thoracic radiculopathy compared to cervical or lumbar regions.
  • Visceral or referred pain - Some patients experience deep chest or abdominal pain mimicking cardiac or gastrointestinal issues due to thoracic nerve involvement in visceral sensation.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Thoracic Radiculopathy"][/sc]
  • Dermatomal tenderness - Tenderness along the affected dermatome due to inflammation of the nerve root.
  • Reduced sensation - Decreased or altered sensation (hypoaesthesia or paraesthesia) in the affected dermatome caused by sensory fibre compression.
  • Muscle spasm - Muscle stiffness or spasm in paraspinal muscles secondary to nerve irritation.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Thoracic Radiculopathy"][/sc]
  • Sudden onset of severe pain - May indicate an acute disc herniation or trauma.
  • Progressive neurological deficit - Worsening numbness, tingling, or weakness may signify worsening nerve compression or myelopathy.
  • Fever and weight loss - Concerning for infectious or neoplastic causes, such as spinal epidural abscess or metastasis.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Thoracic Radiculopathy"][/sc]
  • Isolated abdominal or chest pain - Misinterpretation as gastrointestinal or cardiac pathology due to the visceral innervation of thoracic nerves.
  • Lack of dermatome-specific symptoms - Some patients may have non-radicular, poorly localised pain, leading to delayed diagnosis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Thoracic Radiculopathy"][/sc]
  • MRI of the thoracic spine - Preferred imaging to visualise nerve root compression, disc herniation, or spinal stenosis. Shows soft tissue contrast and can reveal nerve impingement.
  • CT scan - May be used if MRI is contraindicated, especially useful for bony structures and osteophyte-related impingement.
  • Nerve conduction studies (NCS) - Can help differentiate thoracic radiculopathy from other neuropathies by assessing nerve root function.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Thoracic Radiculopathy"][/sc]
  • A 58-year-old woman presents with gradually worsening, sharp, radiating pain along the left T5 dermatome, accompanied by numbness and occasional tingling, with a recent increase in severity over 3 months.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Thoracic Radiculopathy"][/sc]
  • Intercostal neuritis - Presents with similar dermatomal pain but often without the MRI findings of nerve compression.
  • Herpes zoster (shingles) - Characterised by unilateral dermatomal pain with a vesicular rash appearing within days of pain onset.
  • Myocardial infarction - Severe chest pain without a dermatomal pattern; ECG changes and cardiac enzymes are diagnostic.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Thoracic Radiculopathy"][/sc]
  • Conservative management - First-line includes NSAIDs, physiotherapy, and activity modification to reduce inflammation and muscle tension.
  • Interventional therapy - Epidural steroid injections for unresponsive cases to reduce inflammation at the nerve root.
  • Surgical intervention - Reserved for refractory cases with progressive neurological deficits; options include decompression surgery.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Thoracic Radiculopathy"][/sc]
  • Chronic pain syndrome - Persistent pain due to chronic nerve root irritation, seen in cases where conservative management fails.
  • Permanent sensory deficits - Possible if the nerve is severely compressed for extended periods without intervention.
  • Muscle atrophy - Due to prolonged denervation in severe, untreated cases with significant motor involvement.
