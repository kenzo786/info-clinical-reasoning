Interstitial Cystitis
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Interstitial Cystitis"][/sc]
  • Interstitial cystitis (IC), also known as bladder pain syndrome (BPS), is a chronic condition characterised by bladder pain or discomfort, urinary urgency, and frequency, in the absence of infection or other identifiable causes. It is a diagnosis of exclusion.

Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Interstitial Cystitis"][/sc]
  • Disruption of the glycosaminoglycan (GAG) layer of the bladder leads to increased permeability to urine solutes.
  • Local inflammation and mast cell activation contribute to chronic pain and hypersensitivity.
  • Neurogenic upregulation results in heightened sensory input from bladder afferents, leading to pain and urgency.
  • Potential autoimmune or chronic infection triggers have been proposed but not consistently confirmed.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Interstitial Cystitis"][/sc]
  • Initial phase: Gradual onset of urinary symptoms and pain, often over months to years.
  • Chronic phase: Persistent or fluctuating symptoms lasting years, often without complete resolution.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Interstitial Cystitis"][/sc]
  • Bladder pain or discomfort - Localised pain, worsened with bladder filling; due to increased sensitivity from bladder afferents.
  • Urinary urgency - Sudden strong urge to urinate, linked to neural upregulation and detrusor hypersensitivity.
  • Increased urinary frequency - Often voiding more than 10 times daily; a compensatory response to avoid bladder discomfort.
  • Nocturia - Frequently waking at night to urinate; linked to disrupted bladder filling sensation.
  • Dyspareunia - Pain during intercourse, possibly due to pelvic floor dysfunction or referred pain.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Interstitial Cystitis"][/sc]
  • Pelvic tenderness - Detected on bimanual examination, indicating localised inflammation or pelvic floor dysfunction.
  • Absence of other pathological signs - Important in ruling out infections or structural abnormalities.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Interstitial Cystitis"][/sc]
  • Haematuria - May indicate malignancy or urolithiasis; requires further evaluation.
  • Significant weight loss - Suggests possible systemic illness, such as cancer.
  • Severe, sudden onset pain - Raises concern for acute pathology, e.g., urolithiasis or infection.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Interstitial Cystitis"][/sc]
  • Mild or intermittent symptoms - Can delay diagnosis; may still show bladder wall inflammation on cystoscopy.
  • Predominantly pelvic pain - Without overt urinary symptoms, leading to misdiagnosis as chronic pelvic pain syndrome.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Interstitial Cystitis"][/sc]
  • Urinalysis - Excludes infection (negative nitrites/leukocytes) and haematuria.
  • Urine culture - Rules out bacterial infection.
  • Cystoscopy - Visualisation of bladder wall; findings may include Hunnerâ€™s lesions or glomerulations.
  • Potassium sensitivity test - Rarely used; identifies bladder permeability defects.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Interstitial Cystitis"][/sc]
  • A 45-year-old woman with a 2-year history of pelvic pain exacerbated by bladder filling, associated with increased urinary frequency and urgency, without haematuria or infection.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Interstitial Cystitis"][/sc]
  • Recurrent urinary tract infections (UTIs) - Differentiated by positive cultures and response to antibiotics.
  • Overactive bladder - Features urgency and frequency but lacks pain as a hallmark symptom.
  • Endometriosis - May mimic IC but has cyclical symptoms linked to menstruation.
  • Bladder cancer - Distinguished by persistent haematuria and possible mass on imaging.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Interstitial Cystitis"][/sc]
  • Lifestyle modification - Includes bladder training, stress management, and dietary adjustments (e.g., avoiding irritants).
  • Pharmacological therapy - Options include oral amitriptyline or pentosan polysulphate sodium (not widely available in the UK).
  • Bladder instillation therapy - Use of agents like heparin or lidocaine directly into the bladder.
  • Pelvic floor physiotherapy - Effective for pain and functional improvement.
  • Severe cases - Cystectomy or neuromodulation (e.g., sacral nerve stimulation) as a last resort.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Interstitial Cystitis"][/sc]
  • Reduced quality of life - Chronic pain and urinary symptoms impact mental health and daily functioning.
  • Depression and anxiety - High prevalence due to chronic symptom burden.
  • Bladder fibrosis - Rare but can occur with chronic inflammation, leading to reduced bladder capacity.
