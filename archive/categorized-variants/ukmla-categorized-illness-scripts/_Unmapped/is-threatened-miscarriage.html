Threatened Miscarriage
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Threatened Miscarriage"][/sc]
  • Threatened miscarriage refers to vaginal bleeding within the first 20 weeks of gestation, accompanied by a closed cervix and an ongoing pregnancy. It suggests a risk of miscarriage but does not confirm it, as the pregnancy may still progress to term. There is no expulsion of the products of conception at this stage.

Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Threatened Miscarriage"][/sc]
  • The pathophysiology of threatened miscarriage centres on localised decidual bleeding within the uterine wall that does not disrupt the gestational sac. Possible causes include subchorionic haemorrhage or hormonal imbalances that weaken uterine support structures. The cervix remains closed, protecting the embryo. The degree of bleeding influences pregnancy outcomes, with heavy or recurrent bleeding increasing the likelihood of miscarriage.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Threatened Miscarriage"][/sc]
  • Initial phase: Vaginal bleeding occurs, typically minor to moderate, within the first 20 weeks of pregnancy.
  • Progression: Bleeding may persist, subside, or worsen. If it persists or worsens, it may lead to increased risk of complete miscarriage.
  • Resolution: Many cases resolve without progression to miscarriage, especially if bleeding is light and transitory. This phase may last a few days to a week, depending on bleeding intensity.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Threatened Miscarriage"][/sc]
  • Vaginal bleeding - Typically light to moderate, often presenting as spotting. Bleeding results from decidual bleeding without separation of the placenta.
  • Lower abdominal pain - Cramping or dull pain may accompany bleeding, related to uterine contraction or decidual irritation.
  • No significant systemic symptoms - Usually, there is no fever or systemic illness, distinguishing it from infections like chorioamnionitis.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Threatened Miscarriage"][/sc]
  • Closed cervix on examination - The cervix remains closed, a key sign that distinguishes threatened miscarriage from inevitable miscarriage.
  • Absence of expelled products of conception - Unlike incomplete or complete miscarriage, no products are passed in a threatened miscarriage.
  • Mild tenderness on palpation - Some women may have mild suprapubic tenderness due to uterine irritability.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Threatened Miscarriage"][/sc]
  • Heavy bleeding - Can signify progression towards an incomplete or inevitable miscarriage.
  • Passage of clots or tissue - Suggests a partial or complete miscarriage.
  • Severe abdominal pain - May indicate ectopic pregnancy or miscarriage progression, requiring urgent evaluation.
  • Hypotension or tachycardia - Can indicate haemorrhage and hemodynamic instability, which are urgent complications.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Threatened Miscarriage"][/sc]
  • Asymptomatic cases - Some patients with early decidual bleeding may be asymptomatic or have only slight spotting, which can delay diagnosis.
  • Mild, intermittent spotting without pain - This presentation may lead patients to overlook the possibility of miscarriage risk.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Threatened Miscarriage"][/sc]
  • Ultrasound - Transvaginal ultrasound shows an intact gestational sac with fetal cardiac activity, ruling out complete or inevitable miscarriage.
  • Serum hCG levels - Serial hCG levels should rise appropriately in a viable pregnancy; inadequate rise may indicate non-viable pregnancy.
  • Full blood count (FBC) - Assesses haemoglobin and haematocrit for blood loss assessment.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Threatened Miscarriage"][/sc]
  • A 28-year-old woman presents with mild vaginal bleeding and lower abdominal cramps at 9 weeks gestation, with a closed cervix on examination and no passage of tissue.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Threatened Miscarriage"][/sc]
  • Ectopic pregnancy - Presents with abdominal pain and vaginal bleeding, but usually without intrauterine gestation on ultrasound and possibly with signs of shock if ruptured.
  • Inevitable miscarriage - Similar symptoms, but with an open cervix, often heavier bleeding, and abdominal pain.
  • Subchorionic haemorrhage - Detected on ultrasound; often asymptomatic but may cause light bleeding without cervical changes.
  • Gestational trophoblastic disease - Presents with abnormal uterine growth, severe nausea, high hCG levels, and no fetal pole on ultrasound.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Threatened Miscarriage"][/sc]
  • Expectant management - Observation is appropriate if bleeding is mild and there is no hemodynamic instability.
  • Progesterone supplementation - Recommended in cases of threatened miscarriage with recurrent pregnancy loss history.
  • Patient education - Patients are advised to avoid heavy lifting, rest as needed, and report any escalation of symptoms.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Threatened Miscarriage"][/sc]
  • Progression to miscarriage - Occurs in approximately 30-50% of cases of threatened miscarriage.
  • Emotional distress - Common due to uncertainty in pregnancy outcome, which may require psychological support.
  • Preterm labour - Some women with threatened miscarriage may be at higher risk for preterm delivery.
