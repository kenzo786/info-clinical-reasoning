Endometritis
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Endometritis"][/sc]
  • Endometritis is an inflammation of the endometrium, the inner lining of the uterus. It is usually caused by an infection, often following childbirth (postpartum endometritis), miscarriage, abortion, or certain gynaecological procedures. The condition can be acute or chronic, with acute endometritis typically associated with infections and chronic endometritis often related to non-specific inflammation or underlying pathology like pelvic inflammatory disease (PID).
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Endometritis"][/sc]
  • Endometritis is more common in women following delivery or invasive procedures. In the UK, the prevalence of postpartum endometritis is estimated to be around 2-5% following vaginal delivery and up to 27% following caesarean section without antibiotic prophylaxis. Chronic endometritis has a prevalence of about 10% in women with recurrent pregnancy loss or infertility. Worldwide, it is more common in settings with poor obstetric care or lack of access to prophylactic antibiotics.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Endometritis"][/sc]
  • Endometritis typically results from an ascending infection from the lower genital tract. Bacteria, such as *Escherichia coli*, *Group B Streptococcus*, or *Chlamydia trachomatis*, enter the uterine cavity and trigger an inflammatory response. In postpartum cases, retained placental tissue or membranes provide a nidus for bacterial colonisation, exacerbating infection. The inflammation can disrupt the normal architecture of the endometrium, potentially leading to chronic changes or scarring if untreated.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Endometritis"][/sc]
  • Acute endometritis: Onset is typically within 2-10 days post-delivery or procedure, with rapid progression if untreated.
  • Chronic endometritis: This can develop over weeks to months, often with subtle or asymptomatic presentation, particularly in cases related to infertility or pelvic inflammatory disease (PID).
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Endometritis"][/sc]
  • Fever - Due to systemic inflammatory response and the release of pyrogens by the infective organisms.
  • Lower abdominal pain - Caused by inflammation of the uterine lining, leading to discomfort or cramping sensations.
  • Abnormal vaginal discharge - Typically foul-smelling, as bacterial infection causes necrosis of tissue and pus production.
  • Heavy or prolonged menstrual bleeding - This can occur due to endometrial disruption and inflammation in chronic cases.
  • Fatigue or malaise - A generalised response to infection, where systemic inflammation leads to lethargy and malaise.

Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Endometritis"][/sc]
  • Subclinical chronic endometritis - May present asymptomatically or with mild, non-specific symptoms such as chronic pelvic discomfort or abnormal uterine bleeding.
  • Post-menopausal women - Endometritis in this group may be missed due to the assumption of non-infective causes for postmenopausal bleeding.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Endometritis"][/sc]
  • Full blood count (FBC) - Raised white cell count (WCC) is indicative of infection and inflammation.
  • C-reactive protein (CRP) - Elevated CRP reflects acute inflammation and infection.
  • Endometrial biopsy (in chronic cases) - Histological examination shows plasma cells in the endometrial stroma, indicative of chronic endometritis.
  • Cervical swabs - Can identify the causative organism (e.g., *Chlamydia*, *Gonorrhoea*, or *E. coli*).
  • Ultrasound - May show retained products of conception or intrauterine debris in postpartum cases.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Endometritis"][/sc]
  • A 30-year-old woman, 5 days postpartum following caesarean section, presents with fever, lower abdominal pain, and foul-smelling vaginal discharge.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Endometritis"][/sc]
  • Pelvic Inflammatory Disease (PID) - PID affects the upper genital tract and can present similarly, but may have a history of sexually transmitted infection (STI) and involves broader pelvic tenderness.
  • Retained products of conception (RPOC) - Can present with ongoing bleeding and infection, often confirmed via ultrasound imaging.
  • Urinary tract infection (UTI) - Presents with dysuria and frequency, and can co-occur postpartum but without the typical uterine tenderness of endometritis.
  • Chorioamnionitis - Occurs during labour with fever and uterine tenderness, but would not present postpartum.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Endometritis"][/sc]
  • Empirical antibiotics - In the UK, NICE guidelines recommend broad-spectrum antibiotics such as IV co-amoxiclav for postpartum endometritis, or metronidazole and doxycycline in non-obstetric cases.
  • Remove retained products - If ultrasound confirms retained placental tissue, surgical evacuation may be required.
  • Supportive care - Includes antipyretics, fluids, and monitoring for signs of systemic infection or sepsis.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Endometritis"][/sc]
  • Sepsis - Occurs when infection spreads systemically, with a high mortality risk if untreated.
  • Pelvic abscess - A localised collection of pus may form, requiring drainage.
  • Infertility - Chronic inflammation and scarring of the endometrium can lead to infertility, particularly in chronic cases.
