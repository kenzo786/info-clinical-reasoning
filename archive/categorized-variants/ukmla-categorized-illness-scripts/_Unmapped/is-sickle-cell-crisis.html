Sickle Cell Crisis
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Sickle Cell Crisis"][/sc]
  • A sickle cell crisis is an acute exacerbation of symptoms in individuals with sickle cell disease (SCD), characterised primarily by episodes of severe pain due to vaso-occlusion, acute chest syndrome, or sequestration crises. The crisis occurs when sickle-shaped red blood cells obstruct blood flow, leading to tissue ischemia, pain, and potentially severe complications like acute organ damage.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Sickle Cell Crisis"][/sc]
  • UK Prevalence (GP population): Approximately 12,500 people live with SCD in the UK, primarily in Black African and Afro-Caribbean communities.
  • UK Hospital Prevalence: Sickle cell crises are a leading cause of hospital admissions for acute pain management among individuals with haemoglobinopathies.
  • Global Prevalence: Highest in Sub-Saharan Africa, the Middle East, and India. Estimated 20-25 million people worldwide have SCD.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Sickle Cell Crisis"][/sc]
  • Genetic Mutation: SCD is caused by a mutation in the HBB gene that results in haemoglobin S (HbS). Under low oxygen conditions, HbS polymerises, leading to the deformation of red blood cells into a sickle shape.
  • Vaso-Occlusion: Sickle-shaped cells become rigid and adhere to the vascular endothelium, causing blockages and reduced blood flow, leading to tissue ischemia and pain.
  • Haemolysis: Sickle cells have a shorter lifespan (10-20 days compared to the normal 120 days), causing chronic haemolysis, anaemia, and increased bilirubin levels.
  • Reperfusion Injury: After occlusion and ischemia, reperfusion can cause oxidative damage, exacerbating the pain and inflammation in tissues.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Sickle Cell Crisis"][/sc]
  • Initial Phase: Triggers (e.g., hypoxia, dehydration, infection) lead to sickling and vaso-occlusion, with pain onset in hours.
  • Acute Phase: Pain intensifies and may last hours to days, with the potential for severe complications (e.g., acute chest syndrome).
  • Resolution: Pain subsides over days as vaso-occlusion resolves; however, organ damage may remain or recur.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Sickle Cell Crisis"][/sc]
  • Pain (typically in bones, joints, or chest): Caused by ischemia from vaso-occlusion. Most common presenting symptom.
  • Fatigue and weakness: Due to chronic anaemia from haemolysis.
  • Dyspnoea or chest pain: Suggestive of acute chest syndrome or hypoxia.
  • Abdominal pain: Due to splenic or hepatic infarction.
  • Fever: May indicate infection or inflammatory response due to cell breakdown.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Sickle Cell Crisis"][/sc]
  • Pallor: Resulting from chronic anaemia and haemolysis.
  • Jaundice: Due to increased bilirubin from haemolysis.
  • Splenomegaly: Often seen in children; due to splenic sequestration.
  • Tachycardia: As a response to hypoxia and anaemia.
  • Hypoxaemia: Common in acute chest syndrome.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Sickle Cell Crisis"][/sc]
  • Severe hypoxaemia: May indicate acute chest syndrome, a life-threatening complication due to pulmonary vaso-occlusion.
  • Sudden severe anaemia: Suggestive of aplastic crisis or splenic sequestration.
  • Altered mental status: Could indicate stroke or CNS involvement from sickling in cerebral vessels.
  • Fever with chills: Increased risk of sepsis due to asplenia.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Sickle Cell Crisis"][/sc]
  • Neuropathic pain: Can be mistaken for nerve injury, especially in extremities due to repeated ischemia.
  • Silent strokes: May occur without overt neurological deficits but contribute to cognitive decline.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Sickle Cell Crisis"][/sc]
  • Full Blood Count (FBC): Shows anaemia with low haemoglobin; reticulocyte count may be high due to increased erythropoiesis.
  • Peripheral Blood Smear: Reveals sickle-shaped cells and Howell-Jolly bodies (indicative of asplenia).
  • Haemoglobin Electrophoresis: Confirms diagnosis by showing HbS as predominant haemoglobin.
  • Chest X-ray: To assess for signs of acute chest syndrome in cases with dyspnoea or chest pain.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Sickle Cell Crisis"][/sc]
  • A 25-year-old woman with known sickle cell disease presents with acute onset severe bone pain in the arms and legs, fever, and dyspnoea lasting several hours. She reports a similar episode six months prior, which resolved with analgesia and hydration.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Sickle Cell Crisis"][/sc]
  • Osteomyelitis: Similar bone pain and fever; differs in chronicity and confirmed by imaging or cultures.
  • Septic arthritis: Joint-specific pain with fever; joint aspiration confirms infection.
  • Pulmonary embolism: Similar respiratory symptoms, but absence of haemolysis or sickle cells in smear; diagnosed via imaging.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Sickle Cell Crisis"][/sc]
  • Analgesia: Paracetamol or NSAIDs for mild pain; opioids for severe pain.
  • Hydration: IV fluids to reduce blood viscosity and prevent further sickling.
  • Oxygen therapy: For hypoxaemia or acute chest syndrome.
  • Antibiotics: If infection is suspected, broad-spectrum antibiotics are recommended.
  • Exchange transfusion: For severe anaemia, acute chest syndrome, or stroke prevention.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Sickle Cell Crisis"][/sc]
  • Acute Chest Syndrome: Occurs in approximately 30% of SCD patients and is the leading cause of mortality.
  • Stroke: Increased risk due to cerebrovascular occlusion; often results in cognitive impairment.
  • Chronic kidney disease: Due to recurrent vaso-occlusion in renal circulation, leading to renal failure.
  • Splenic Sequestration and Asplenia: Increased susceptibility to infections due to functional asplenia.
