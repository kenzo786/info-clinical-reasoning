Medication-Induced Gait Abnormalities
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Medication-Induced Gait Abnormalities"][/sc]
  • Medication-induced gait abnormalities refer to alterations in walking patterns or postural stability caused by pharmacological agents. These abnormalities may result from drug effects on the central or peripheral nervous system, musculoskeletal function, or vestibular mechanisms. Commonly implicated drug classes include antipsychotics, benzodiazepines, antidepressants, and antihypertensives.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Medication-Induced Gait Abnormalities"][/sc]
  • Prevalence in UK GP population: Estimated at 1-3% of patients, with elderly patients at greater risk due to polypharmacy.
  • UK hospital prevalence: Likely higher in inpatient settings, particularly in geriatrics and psychiatry wards.
  • Worldwide prevalence: Variable, but estimated around 2-5% of patients on medications with high fall risk profiles.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Medication-Induced Gait Abnormalities"][/sc]
  • Drug-induced gait changes primarily affect the central nervous system (CNS) through effects on neurotransmitters like dopamine, GABA, and serotonin. CNS-acting drugs (e.g., antipsychotics, benzodiazepines) can cause extrapyramidal symptoms (EPS) or sedation, leading to motor instability and poor coordination.
  • Anticholinergic drugs impair proprioceptive feedback and balance, contributing to gait instability.
  • Some antihypertensives (e.g., beta-blockers) affect blood flow and vascular tone, potentially causing postural hypotension and dizziness, which increases the risk of falls and gait disturbances.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Medication-Induced Gait Abnormalities"][/sc]
  • Acute: Gait disturbances may appear within hours to days after initiating or increasing the dose of certain medications (e.g., sedatives, antipsychotics).
  • Subacute: Symptoms may develop over several days to weeks, especially in drugs that gradually accumulate or reach steady-state levels (e.g., lithium, some antidepressants).
  • Chronic: In some cases, persistent gait abnormalities can occur with prolonged exposure to drugs causing long-term neurological changes (e.g., tardive dyskinesia from chronic antipsychotic use).
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Medication-Induced Gait Abnormalities"][/sc]
  • Unsteadiness - Patients often report feeling off-balance due to sedation or postural instability, especially with CNS depressants like benzodiazepines.
  • Shuffling Gait - A characteristic gait pattern, particularly in patients on antipsychotics due to extrapyramidal side effects (EPS) impacting motor control.
  • Slowed Movements (Bradykinesia) - Common with dopamine antagonists (antipsychotics), bradykinesia results from blocked dopamine pathways affecting voluntary motor function.
  • Stiffness or Rigidity - Often seen with antipsychotics, rigidity arises from neurotransmitter dysregulation affecting motor neuron pathways.
  • Dizziness - Seen in antihypertensives causing orthostatic hypotension; reduced cerebral blood flow causes dizziness and increases fall risk.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Medication-Induced Gait Abnormalities"][/sc]
  • Ataxic Gait - Impaired coordination may be observed, especially with sedative medications affecting the cerebellum and proprioceptive feedback.
  • Postural Instability - Frequently seen in benzodiazepine or antipsychotic use, due to CNS depressant effects that impair balance mechanisms.
  • Tremor - Medications such as lithium or SSRIs can induce tremors through their effects on neurotransmitter regulation.
  • Hypotension on Standing - Often observed with antihypertensives (especially alpha-blockers) and causes dizziness, increasing fall risk during ambulation.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Medication-Induced Gait Abnormalities"][/sc]
  • Severe Extrapyramidal Symptoms (EPS) - This includes acute dystonia, which can indicate a serious drug reaction and requires immediate attention to prevent airway compromise.
  • Persistent Orthostatic Hypotension - Severe or unresponsive hypotension may indicate the need to adjust or discontinue medication to avoid falls and injury.
  • Altered Mental Status or Confusion - Significant cognitive impairment may signify overdose or toxic effects, particularly in elderly patients with polypharmacy.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Medication-Induced Gait Abnormalities"][/sc]
  • Intermittent Gait Disturbance - Periodic gait instability, particularly with drugs with variable serum levels (e.g., lithium).
  • Masked Bradykinesia - Patients on antipsychotics may develop subtle bradykinesia mistaken for age-related slowness.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Medication-Induced Gait Abnormalities"][/sc]
  • Serum Drug Levels - Used to measure drugs like lithium, where serum level monitoring can reveal toxicity contributing to gait issues.
  • Postural Blood Pressure Measurements - Drop in BP upon standing can confirm orthostatic hypotension induced by antihypertensives.
  • Neurological Examination - Assesses motor function and identifies EPS, ataxia, or tremor directly attributable to medication effects.

Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Medication-Induced Gait Abnormalities"][/sc]
  • Review and reduce polypharmacy where possible, particularly in elderly patients.
  • Adjust or discontinue offending medications if safe to do so.
  • Physiotherapy and gait training can help to improve balance and minimise fall risk.
  • For antipsychotic-induced EPS, consider adding or switching to anticholinergic agents (e.g., procyclidine), as per NICE guidelines.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Medication-Induced Gait Abnormalities"][/sc]
  • Falls and Fractures - Increased fall risk due to gait instability, particularly in older adults; fractures are a common outcome.
  • Extrapyramidal Symptoms - Chronic use of certain drugs (e.g., antipsychotics) can lead to persistent movement disorders.
  • Deconditioning - Patients may become less active due to fear of falling, worsening overall mobility.
