Presbycusis
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Presbycusis"][/sc]
  • Presbycusis, also known as age-related hearing loss, is a progressive, bilateral sensorineural hearing impairment that commonly affects individuals as they age. It primarily results from degenerative changes in the cochlea or auditory nerve pathways, leading to difficulties in hearing high-frequency sounds.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Presbycusis"][/sc]
  • Presbycusis is the most common cause of hearing loss in adults. In the UK, it affects around 40% of people over the age of 65 and up to 70% of those over the age of 75. Worldwide, it affects approximately one-third of individuals aged 65 and older. In primary care settings, hearing loss due to presbycusis is frequently reported among older adults.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Presbycusis"][/sc]
  • Presbycusis arises from cumulative damage to the cochlea and the auditory nerve due to ageing. The key pathophysiological changes include:
    • Degeneration of hair cells within the cochlea, particularly the outer hair cells, responsible for amplifying sound.
    • Loss of neurons in the auditory pathway, which affects sound signal transmission.
    • Stiffening of the basilar membrane, which impairs frequency discrimination, particularly for high-pitched sounds.
    • Decreased blood supply to the inner ear due to microvascular changes.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Presbycusis"][/sc]
  • Presbycusis progresses gradually over several years. It typically begins in the fifth or sixth decade of life and worsens with age. Initial symptoms often include difficulty hearing high-frequency sounds, and over time, this can extend to lower frequencies, affecting communication.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Presbycusis"][/sc]
  • Hearing loss - The most common symptom, particularly affecting high-pitched sounds like conversations in noisy environments. This is due to the degeneration of cochlear hair cells and stiffening of the basilar membrane.
  • Tinnitus - A persistent ringing or buzzing in the ears, often caused by damage to the cochlear hair cells, leading to abnormal neural activity.
  • Difficulty understanding speech - Especially in noisy environments, due to impaired frequency discrimination caused by the stiffened basilar membrane.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Presbycusis"][/sc]
  • Bilateral sensorineural hearing loss - Most apparent in high-frequency ranges, confirmed through audiometric testing, caused by cochlear degeneration.
  • Absent or reduced acoustic reflexes - Indicating damage to the auditory pathway or inner ear structures.
  • Normal otoscopic examination - The external ear and tympanic membrane typically appear normal as the pathology lies within the cochlea and auditory nerve.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Presbycusis"][/sc]
  • Sudden hearing loss - May indicate an acute condition such as sudden sensorineural hearing loss or vascular events and should prompt immediate referral.
  • Unilateral hearing loss - Could suggest a more serious condition such as vestibular schwannoma or other tumours.
  • Severe vertigo - May indicate a vestibular disorder like Meniere's disease or a stroke involving the posterior circulation.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Presbycusis"][/sc]
  • Mild unilateral hearing loss - This could be missed in early stages as presbycusis is typically bilateral. Unilateral loss may be due to another cause like conductive hearing loss.
  • Progressive speech difficulties without obvious hearing loss - Some patients may initially present with only difficulty in understanding speech, especially in noisy environments, without recognising the extent of hearing loss.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Presbycusis"][/sc]
  • Pure-tone audiometry - Reveals a bilateral, symmetric sensorineural hearing loss that primarily affects higher frequencies (above 2 kHz).
  • Speech audiometry - Demonstrates difficulty in speech discrimination, particularly in background noise, due to impaired frequency resolution.
  • Otoacoustic emissions (OAEs) - Often reduced or absent in presbycusis due to damage to the outer hair cells in the cochlea.
  • Tympanometry - Typically normal, as presbycusis is not associated with middle ear pathology.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Presbycusis"][/sc]
  • A 72-year-old male presents with gradually worsening hearing loss over the past 5 years, particularly struggling to hear conversations in noisy environments and experiencing occasional tinnitus. No history of ear infections or noise exposure.

Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Presbycusis"][/sc]
  • Hearing aids - Most patients benefit from amplification devices to improve hearing, particularly for high-frequency sounds.
  • Cochlear implants - In severe cases of presbycusis where hearing aids are insufficient, cochlear implants may be considered.
  • Assistive listening devices - These include phone amplifiers and TV listening systems, which can improve quality of life.
  • Auditory rehabilitation - This can include speech-reading training and communication strategies.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Presbycusis"][/sc]
  • Social isolation and depression - Due to communication difficulties, patients may withdraw from social interactions, leading to mental health issues.
  • Falls - Hearing impairment is associated with an increased risk of falls, possibly due to altered spatial awareness and imbalance.
  • Cognitive decline - There is a known association between hearing loss and an increased risk of dementia, possibly due to reduced sensory input to the brain.
