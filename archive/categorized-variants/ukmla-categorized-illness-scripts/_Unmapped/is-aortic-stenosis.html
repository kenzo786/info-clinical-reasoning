Aortic Stenosis
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Aortic Stenosis"][/sc]
  • Aortic stenosis (AS) is the narrowing of the aortic valve opening, resulting in restricted blood flow from the left ventricle to the aorta during systole. This increased afterload leads to left ventricular hypertrophy and can progress to heart failure if untreated. It is most commonly caused by age-related calcification, congenital bicuspid aortic valve, or rheumatic heart disease.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Aortic Stenosis"][/sc]
  • UK GP prevalence: 3% in those over 75 years of age.
  • UK hospital prevalence: Up to 5% in older adults, accounting for 25% of valve replacement surgeries.
  • Worldwide prevalence: Increasing due to ageing populations, with similar rates in developed countries.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Aortic Stenosis"][/sc]
  • 1. Progressive calcification or fibrosis causes narrowing of the aortic valve opening.
  • 2. The left ventricle compensates by hypertrophying to maintain cardiac output.
  • 3. Over time, increased left ventricular wall stress leads to diastolic dysfunction and myocardial ischaemia.
  • 4. This results in symptoms of heart failure, angina, and syncope.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Aortic Stenosis"][/sc]
  • Early: Asymptomatic phase lasting years to decades.
  • Progressive: Gradual development of symptoms such as exertional dyspnoea, fatigue, and syncope.
  • Late: Advanced heart failure symptoms, with a high risk of sudden cardiac death if untreated.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Aortic Stenosis"][/sc]
  • Exertional dyspnoea - Reduced cardiac output and increased left ventricular pressure lead to pulmonary congestion during exertion.
  • Chest pain (angina) - Myocardial ischaemia occurs due to increased oxygen demand from hypertrophied myocardium and reduced coronary perfusion.
  • Syncope - Decreased cerebral perfusion during exertion due to fixed cardiac output, resulting in transient loss of consciousness.
  • Fatigue - Due to reduced cardiac output, leading to decreased oxygen delivery to tissues.
  • Palpitations - Often due to arrhythmias secondary to left ventricular hypertrophy or atrial enlargement.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Aortic Stenosis"][/sc]
  • Ejection systolic murmur - A crescendo-decrescendo murmur heard best at the right upper sternal border, caused by turbulent flow through the narrowed valve.
  • Reduced or delayed carotid upstroke ("pulsus parvus et tardus") - Caused by reduced blood flow through the aortic valve.
  • Narrow pulse pressure - The difference between systolic and diastolic pressures is diminished due to reduced stroke volume.
  • Heaving apex beat - Reflects left ventricular hypertrophy as the heart works harder to pump blood through the stenotic valve.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Aortic Stenosis"][/sc]
  • Syncope - Signifies severe stenosis, often occurring during exertion. Indicates increased risk of sudden cardiac death due to fixed cardiac output.
  • Heart failure symptoms (e.g., orthopnoea, paroxysmal nocturnal dyspnoea) - Suggests advanced disease with significant left ventricular dysfunction.
  • Sudden death - Can occur due to arrhythmias or left ventricular failure in severe aortic stenosis.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Aortic Stenosis"][/sc]
  • Asymptomatic presentation - Especially in older adults, where mild stenosis may go unnoticed. The body compensates until a critical threshold is reached.
  • Atypical chest pain - Instead of typical angina, patients may present with vague or non-specific chest discomfort.
  • Stroke - Rarely, embolisation from calcified aortic valve plaques may lead to a cerebrovascular event.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Aortic Stenosis"][/sc]
  • Echocardiogram - Shows valve anatomy, degree of stenosis, and left ventricular hypertrophy. Peak gradient >40 mmHg or valve area <1 cmÂ² indicates severe stenosis.
  • ECG - Shows signs of left ventricular hypertrophy, such as large S wave in V1 and large R wave in V5 (Sokolow-Lyon criteria).
  • Chest X-ray - May show aortic valve calcification and evidence of left ventricular hypertrophy or pulmonary congestion.
  • Cardiac catheterisation - Measures the pressure gradient across the aortic valve, confirming severity.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Aortic Stenosis"][/sc]
  • An 82-year-old man presents with a 6-month history of exertional dyspnoea, chest pain, and occasional dizziness. Examination reveals a harsh ejection systolic murmur at the right upper sternal border and a slow-rising carotid pulse.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Aortic Stenosis"][/sc]
  • Hypertrophic cardiomyopathy - May present with a similar murmur but is associated with a bisferiens pulse and dynamic obstruction, often diagnosed via family history and echocardiogram showing asymmetric septal hypertrophy.
  • Aortic regurgitation - Presents with a diastolic murmur, widened pulse pressure, and bounding pulse, differentiating it from aortic stenosis.
  • Mitral stenosis - Characterised by a mid-diastolic murmur and symptoms of pulmonary hypertension, with no delayed carotid upstroke or ejection murmur.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Aortic Stenosis"][/sc]
  • Medical Management - For symptom relief only, including diuretics for heart failure symptoms. There are no medical therapies to halt stenosis progression.
  • Surgical Aortic Valve Replacement (SAVR) - Recommended for symptomatic patients with severe AS or those with evidence of left ventricular dysfunction (ejection fraction <50%).
  • Transcatheter Aortic Valve Implantation (TAVI) - Minimally invasive alternative for patients who are at high surgical risk, with good outcomes in older adults.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Aortic Stenosis"][/sc]
  • Heart failure - Common, due to chronic pressure overload and left ventricular dysfunction.
  • Arrhythmias - Both atrial fibrillation and ventricular arrhythmias can occur due to left atrial enlargement and ventricular strain.
  • Sudden cardiac death - Particularly in patients with severe stenosis or unrecognised symptoms.
