Cholestasis
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Cholestasis"][/sc]
  • Cholestasis is a condition characterised by impaired bile flow, which can be due to either intrahepatic (within the liver) or extrahepatic (outside the liver) causes. It leads to accumulation of bile acids, bilirubin, and cholesterol in the bloodstream, often resulting in jaundice, pruritus, and elevated liver enzymes. It is classified as obstructive (mechanical obstruction) or non-obstructive (metabolic and functional disorders).
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Cholestasis"][/sc]
  • UK General Practice: Cholestasis is relatively uncommon in primary care, with most cases identified in secondary or tertiary settings.
  • UK Hospital Prevalence: Approximately 1 in 1,000 hospital admissions are associated with cholestatic liver disease, with higher incidence among patients with chronic liver disease, autoimmune disorders, or biliary obstructions.

Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Cholestasis"][/sc]
  • Cholestasis arises from disrupted bile secretion, leading to an accumulation of bile constituents (bile acids, bilirubin, cholesterol) within the liver and bloodstream.
  • Intrahepatic cholestasis involves hepatocellular or bile canalicular dysfunction, often due to drug-induced liver injury, infections, or genetic diseases.
  • Extrahepatic cholestasis is typically due to obstruction in the bile ducts from stones, strictures, or tumours, causing a mechanical backup.
  • The retention of bile acids and bilirubin in tissues leads to jaundice, pruritus, and liver damage if untreated.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Cholestasis"][/sc]
  • Acute phase: Symptoms such as jaundice, pruritus, and fatigue develop over days to weeks, typically in cases with drug or obstructive causes.
  • Chronic phase: In progressive conditions like primary biliary cholangitis, symptoms evolve over months to years, often starting with asymptomatic liver enzyme elevation.
  • Progression: Without intervention, cholestasis can lead to fibrosis, cirrhosis, and hepatic failure in chronic cases.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Cholestasis"][/sc]
  • Jaundice: Yellowing of the skin and sclerae due to elevated bilirubin levels caused by impaired bile excretion.
  • Pruritus: Often severe itching due to bile acids depositing in the skin; intensified in chronic cholestasis.
  • Fatigue: Common in cholestatic patients, potentially due to systemic inflammation or liver dysfunction.
  • Dark urine: Due to increased conjugated bilirubin excretion in the kidneys.
  • Pale stools: Caused by decreased bile reaching the intestines, affecting stool pigmentation.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Cholestasis"][/sc]
  • Icterus: Yellowing of the sclera due to hyperbilirubinaemia, indicating systemic bilirubin buildup.
  • Hepatomegaly: Enlarged liver due to bile retention and inflammation, especially in chronic cholestatic conditions.
  • Excoriations: Skin scratches from severe pruritus, often seen in advanced cases.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Cholestasis"][/sc]
  • Severe abdominal pain: May indicate biliary obstruction due to stones, tumours, or pancreatitis.
  • Unintentional weight loss: Suggests possible malignancy, especially in cases of extrahepatic cholestasis.
  • Signs of hepatic encephalopathy: Confusion or altered mental status may indicate advanced liver failure in chronic cholestasis.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Cholestasis"][/sc]
  • Asymptomatic cholestasis: In conditions like primary biliary cholangitis, asymptomatic liver enzyme elevation may precede overt symptoms for years.
  • Non-specific gastrointestinal symptoms: Dyspepsia or mild abdominal discomfort may precede jaundice or pruritus in some patients.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Cholestasis"][/sc]
  • Liver function tests: Elevated ALP and GGT indicate cholestasis; bilirubin may also be raised, especially in obstructive cholestasis.
  • Imaging: Abdominal ultrasound or MRCP to visualise bile duct obstructions or strictures.
  • Autoimmune markers: AMA for primary biliary cholangitis; ANCA or IgG4 for other autoimmune or related cholestatic disorders.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Cholestasis"][/sc]
  • A 54-year-old woman presents with 6 weeks of progressive fatigue, pruritus, and jaundice. Blood tests show elevated ALP, GGT, and bilirubin, with imaging revealing bile duct strictures.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Cholestasis"][/sc]
  • Viral hepatitis: Presents with jaundice and liver enzyme elevation but typically with elevated ALT/AST ratios rather than ALP/GGT.
  • Alcoholic liver disease: May cause jaundice, hepatomegaly, and ALP elevation, though with a history of alcohol use.
  • Gallstones: Typically present with episodic abdominal pain, especially if obstructing the common bile duct, causing acute cholestasis.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Cholestasis"][/sc]
  • Ursodeoxycholic acid (UDCA): First-line for primary biliary cholangitis, reducing bile toxicity and slowing disease progression.
  • Cholestyramine: For pruritus relief; binds bile acids in the intestines to reduce systemic itching.
  • Endoscopic or surgical intervention: Necessary for obstructive cases, such as ERCP for stone removal or stenting in malignancy.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Cholestasis"][/sc]
  • Liver cirrhosis: Chronic cholestasis can lead to fibrosis and eventual cirrhosis if untreated.
  • Vitamin deficiencies: Malabsorption of fat-soluble vitamins (A, D, E, K) is common due to impaired bile flow.
  • Cholangitis: Bacterial infection in obstructed bile ducts, particularly in extrahepatic cholestasis, can lead to sepsis if not managed promptly.
