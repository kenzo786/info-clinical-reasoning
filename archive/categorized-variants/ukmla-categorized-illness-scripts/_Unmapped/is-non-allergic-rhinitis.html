Non-allergic Rhinitis
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Non-allergic Rhinitis"][/sc]
  • Non-allergic rhinitis is a chronic condition characterised by nasal symptoms such as rhinorrhoea, nasal congestion, sneezing, and postnasal drip that occur in the absence of IgE-mediated allergic triggers or an infectious cause. It is often idiopathic but may be triggered by environmental factors, medications, or underlying structural abnormalities.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Non-allergic Rhinitis"][/sc]
  • Prevalence in UK GP population: Approximately 5-10% of adults report non-allergic rhinitis symptoms.
  • UK hospital prevalence: Non-allergic rhinitis accounts for a significant portion of referrals to ENT clinics.
  • Worldwide prevalence: Estimated at 10-20%, varying by environmental and occupational exposures.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Non-allergic Rhinitis"][/sc]
  • Non-allergic rhinitis involves hyperresponsiveness of the nasal mucosa to various triggers, including environmental irritants, temperature changes, or neurogenic stimuli.
  • Neurogenic inflammation may play a role, with dysregulation of the autonomic nervous system causing overactivation of parasympathetic pathways and resultant mucus hypersecretion and vasodilation.
  • Non-IgE mediated mast cell activation may also contribute in certain subtypes.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Non-allergic Rhinitis"][/sc]
  • Chronic course: Symptoms typically persist for weeks to months, often without significant seasonal variation.
  • Exacerbations: Triggered by environmental irritants, temperature changes, or other non-allergic factors.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Non-allergic Rhinitis"][/sc]
  • Rhinorrhoea - Commonly reported as clear nasal discharge, due to increased mucus production caused by neurogenic and inflammatory mechanisms.
  • Nasal congestion - Caused by vasodilation and increased blood flow to the nasal mucosa, leading to swelling.
  • Postnasal drip - Mucus accumulation in the posterior nasal cavity and throat caused by excessive secretion.
  • Frequent sneezing - Result of nasal mucosal irritation due to non-allergic triggers such as strong odours or air pollutants.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Non-allergic Rhinitis"][/sc]
  • Swollen nasal turbinates - Observed on anterior rhinoscopy, indicative of mucosal inflammation.
  • Clear nasal discharge - Seen during examination, reflecting hypersecretion of mucus.
  • Pale or bluish nasal mucosa - Common finding due to chronic vasodilation and oedema.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Non-allergic Rhinitis"][/sc]
  • Unilateral nasal symptoms - May indicate a structural abnormality or tumour.
  • Recurrent epistaxis - Could signify a neoplasm or vasculitis.
  • Severe pain or anosmia - Suggests possible sinusitis or other complications.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Non-allergic Rhinitis"][/sc]
  • Isolated postnasal drip without rhinorrhoea - Can occur due to predominant mucus accumulation in the posterior nasal cavity.
  • Intermittent symptoms - Triggered exclusively by specific irritants such as perfumes or cleaning agents.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Non-allergic Rhinitis"][/sc]
  • Anterior rhinoscopy - Shows swollen, pale, or bluish nasal mucosa.
  • Skin prick testing - Negative for common allergens, ruling out allergic rhinitis.
  • Nasal endoscopy - May reveal structural abnormalities or exclude other causes like polyps.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Non-allergic Rhinitis"][/sc]
  • A 35-year-old non-smoker presents with a 6-month history of clear rhinorrhoea, nasal congestion, and postnasal drip, with symptoms exacerbated by strong odours and cold air.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Non-allergic Rhinitis"][/sc]
  • Allergic rhinitis - Differentiated by positive allergen testing and seasonal pattern.
  • Sinusitis - Typically presents with facial pain and purulent nasal discharge.
  • Nasal polyps - Often causes persistent nasal obstruction and may be visible on examination.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Non-allergic Rhinitis"][/sc]
  • Saline nasal irrigation - Effective for symptom relief by clearing nasal mucus and irritants.
  • Intranasal corticosteroids - Reduce inflammation and nasal hyperreactivity.
  • Anticholinergic nasal sprays - Help to control rhinorrhoea by inhibiting parasympathetic activity.
  • Avoidance of triggers - Advisable for patients sensitive to specific irritants.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Non-allergic Rhinitis"][/sc]
  • Chronic sinusitis - Occurs due to prolonged mucosal inflammation and obstruction.
  • Impaired quality of life - Significant impact on sleep, work productivity, and overall well-being.
