Branchial Cyst
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Branchial Cyst"][/sc]
  • A branchial cyst is a congenital epithelial cyst that arises from remnants of the branchial apparatus, most commonly the second branchial cleft. It typically presents as a painless, slow-growing mass on the lateral aspect of the neck, anterior to the sternocleidomastoid muscle.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Branchial Cyst"][/sc]
  • Prevalence in UK GP population: < 1% of all neck masses seen in primary care.
  • UK hospital prevalence: Branchial cysts account for 20-25% of paediatric neck masses, and less commonly in adults.
  • Worldwide prevalence: Similar rates of occurrence worldwide, though more often identified in paediatric populations.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Branchial Cyst"][/sc]
  • Branchial cysts result from the incomplete obliteration of the branchial apparatus during embryogenesis, specifically the second branchial cleft. Normally, these clefts should regress by the 7th week of gestation. Failure to do so leaves epithelial remnants that may accumulate fluid over time, forming cystic structures.
  • The cysts are lined by respiratory or squamous epithelium and may contain lymphoid tissue, which can become infected or inflamed, leading to symptoms.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Branchial Cyst"][/sc]
  • Branchial cysts are usually present at birth but may remain asymptomatic for years.
  • Most commonly, they present in late childhood or early adulthood, typically after a mild infection or trauma causes the cyst to enlarge or become inflamed.
  • In some cases, they may remain asymptomatic throughout life unless secondarily infected.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Branchial Cyst"][/sc]
  • Painless neck mass - The most common presentation, often noted anterior to the sternocleidomastoid. Caused by the gradual accumulation of fluid in the epithelial-lined cyst.
  • Swelling after upper respiratory tract infection - Infections may cause the cyst to become inflamed or secondarily infected, leading to noticeable swelling.
  • Dysphagia or difficulty swallowing - If the cyst becomes large enough, it may compress adjacent structures, including the oesophagus.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Branchial Cyst"][/sc]
  • Soft, fluctuant mass - Palpation reveals a soft and compressible mass, indicative of a cystic lesion filled with fluid.
  • No overlying skin changes - Typically, there is no erythema or warmth unless the cyst is secondarily infected.
  • Lateral neck location - The mass is located anterior to the sternocleidomastoid muscle, differentiating it from other neck swellings such as thyroid or midline cysts.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Branchial Cyst"][/sc]
  • Rapid growth or pain - May indicate secondary infection or malignancy, necessitating urgent intervention.
  • Fixed, non-mobile mass - Suggests possible malignancy, as benign branchial cysts are typically mobile and non-adherent to surrounding structures.
  • Dysphonia or hoarseness - May indicate compression of nearby structures like the recurrent laryngeal nerve.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Branchial Cyst"][/sc]
  • Asymptomatic small cyst - Many branchial cysts remain undiagnosed until incidental imaging or an upper respiratory tract infection triggers swelling.
  • Infected cyst presenting with abscess formation - Rarely, a cyst may become acutely infected, leading to abscess formation with signs of erythema, warmth, and tenderness.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Branchial Cyst"][/sc]
  • Ultrasound scan - The first-line imaging modality for a suspected branchial cyst. Typically shows a well-defined, anechoic (fluid-filled) structure.
  • Fine needle aspiration (FNA) - Used to confirm the diagnosis, showing proteinaceous fluid with cholesterol crystals. FNA is also used to rule out malignancy.
  • CT/MRI - Performed if malignancy is suspected or for surgical planning, showing a cystic mass with well-defined borders.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Branchial Cyst"][/sc]
  • A 25-year-old male presents with a 2-month history of a painless swelling in the lateral neck, which enlarged after a recent upper respiratory infection. No associated pain or dysphagia. Examination reveals a soft, fluctuant mass anterior to the sternocleidomastoid, without overlying skin changes.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Branchial Cyst"][/sc]
  • Thyroglossal duct cyst - Presents as a midline neck mass, typically moves with swallowing or tongue protrusion, unlike branchial cysts which are lateral.
  • Lymphadenopathy - Can present as a neck mass but typically associated with systemic symptoms like fever, night sweats, or weight loss, which are not seen in uncomplicated branchial cysts.
  • Neck abscess - Presents with more pronounced signs of infection (erythema, tenderness, warmth), whereas branchial cysts are usually painless and non-inflammatory unless secondarily infected.
  • Cystic hygroma - A congenital lymphatic malformation, usually presenting in infants and located posterior to the sternocleidomastoid.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Branchial Cyst"][/sc]
  • Surgical excision - The definitive treatment is complete surgical removal of the cyst, usually performed to prevent recurrent infections or for cosmetic reasons. Surgery may require careful dissection due to proximity to major neck structures.
  • Antibiotics - If the cyst is secondarily infected, empirical broad-spectrum antibiotics may be initiated (e.g., co-amoxiclav), with a switch to specific antibiotics based on culture results.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Branchial Cyst"][/sc]
  • Secondary infection - One of the most common complications, leading to abscess formation and requiring drainage and antibiotics.
  • Recurrence after incomplete excision - If the cyst is not entirely excised, there is a risk of recurrence, requiring further surgery.
  • Malignant transformation - Rare, but possible, especially in older patients or those with a history of rapid growth or fixation of the mass.
