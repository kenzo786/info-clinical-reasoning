Patellofemoral Pain Syndrome
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Patellofemoral Pain Syndrome"][/sc]
  • Patellofemoral pain syndrome (PFPS), also known as "runner's knee," is a common musculoskeletal condition characterised by pain around or behind the patella (kneecap), typically exacerbated by activities that load the knee joint, such as running, squatting, and climbing stairs.
  • It occurs due to an imbalance or malalignment in the patellofemoral joint, which causes abnormal stress on the joint and surrounding soft tissues, leading to pain, inflammation, and reduced function.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Patellofemoral Pain Syndrome"][/sc]
  • Knee pain (anterior or peripatellar) - This is the hallmark symptom, often described as a dull ache or soreness that worsens with activities like squatting, running, or ascending/descending stairs. This pain is attributed to excessive stress on the patellofemoral joint, typically from patellar maltracking or weakness of the quadriceps muscles.
  • Stiffness - A sensation of tightness in the knee, particularly after prolonged sitting or activity, is common. This is due to decreased range of motion or inflammatory responses within the joint.
  • Instability or giving way - Patients may feel like their knee is unstable or weak. This is usually due to altered biomechanics caused by muscle imbalances or poor patellar tracking.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Patellofemoral Pain Syndrome"][/sc]
  • Point tenderness around the patella - Tenderness is often noted along the patellofemoral joint line, especially when pressure is applied to the patella. This occurs due to inflammation and irritation of the soft tissues surrounding the patella.
  • Patellar maltracking - The patella may not track smoothly along the femoral groove during knee movement, especially when flexing or extending. This abnormal movement contributes to pain and discomfort.
  • Weak quadriceps - Muscle weakness, particularly in the quadriceps, is often seen in PFPS. This weakness leads to inadequate support for the patella, contributing to its misalignment and abnormal joint stresses.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Patellofemoral Pain Syndrome"][/sc]
  • Severe or sudden onset pain - This may indicate a more serious underlying condition, such as a fracture or ligament injury, rather than PFPS.
  • Locking or catching sensation - Suggests the possibility of meniscal tears or other intra-articular pathology that may mimic PFPS.
  • Night pain - Persistent pain at night may indicate an inflammatory condition or infection (e.g., septic arthritis) rather than PFPS.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Patellofemoral Pain Syndrome"][/sc]
  • Patellofemoral pain in older adults - Though PFPS is most common in younger individuals, older adults may present with similar symptoms due to age-related cartilage wear and reduced muscle strength. This may be missed if clinicians expect PFPS only in younger patients.
  • Minimal pain despite significant dysfunction - Some patients may report minimal discomfort but experience significant difficulty in performing daily activities due to functional limitations from quadriceps weakness or instability.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Patellofemoral Pain Syndrome"][/sc]
  • Clinical examination - The diagnosis of PFPS is primarily clinical, with special tests such as the patellar grind test or apprehension test helping to elicit pain or discomfort in the patellofemoral joint.
  • Plain X-ray - X-rays may be used to rule out other causes of knee pain, such as fractures, patellar subluxation, or osteoarthritis. In some cases, a lateral view of the knee may show patellar malalignment.
  • MRI - MRI may be used in cases where other pathologies (e.g., meniscal tears, cartilage damage) are suspected, but it is not typically needed for routine diagnosis of PFPS.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Patellofemoral Pain Syndrome"][/sc]
  • A 24-year-old female runner presents with a dull ache around the kneecap, worsening with activity like running and squatting. She reports a feeling of knee instability and stiffness after prolonged sitting.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Patellofemoral Pain Syndrome"][/sc]
  • Patellar tendinopathy - Unlike PFPS, patellar tendinopathy pain is typically localized to the inferior pole of the patella and is exacerbated by jumping or repetitive loading activities. Tenderness is felt at the tendon rather than the joint line.
  • Osteoarthritis - Osteoarthritis tends to affect older individuals and is associated with joint stiffness, crepitus, and pain that worsens with weight-bearing activities, unlike PFPS, which usually affects younger, active individuals.
  • Meniscal tears - Meniscal tears may cause locking or clicking sensations in the knee, which are not typical of PFPS. They also often present with a history of trauma or sudden twisting movements.
  • ITB syndrome - Iliotibial band syndrome causes lateral knee pain, whereas PFPS causes anterior or peripatellar pain. ITB syndrome is typically exacerbated by running and climbing stairs.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Patellofemoral Pain Syndrome"][/sc]
  • Conservative management - This includes physiotherapy, focusing on strengthening the quadriceps and improving patellar tracking, as well as stretching of the iliotibial band and hamstrings. Ice, rest, and non-steroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs) can help manage symptoms.
  • Orthotics - Custom insoles may be beneficial for patients with abnormal foot mechanics contributing to PFPS.
  • Surgical intervention - In rare cases, surgery may be considered for patients who do not respond to conservative measures, especially those with severe patellar malalignment or damage to the articular cartilage.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Patellofemoral Pain Syndrome"][/sc]
  • Chronic pain - If untreated, PFPS can lead to persistent knee pain and functional limitations. This may be due to continued patellar maltracking or muscle imbalances.
  • Osteoarthritis - Long-standing PFPS can contribute to the development of early knee osteoarthritis, as abnormal patellar tracking may lead to increased wear on the articular cartilage.
  • Reduced physical activity - Due to pain, patients may reduce their physical activity, which can lead to deconditioning and further muscle weakness, exacerbating the symptoms of PFPS.
