Tropical Diseases
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Tropical Diseases"][/sc]
  • Tropical diseases are a group of infectious diseases prevalent in tropical and subtropical regions. They are caused by various pathogens including bacteria, viruses, protozoa, and helminths, often transmitted via vectors such as mosquitoes, flies, or contaminated water and soil. Examples include malaria, dengue fever, schistosomiasis, and leishmaniasis.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Tropical Diseases"][/sc]
  • UK GP population: Rare, primarily seen in travellers returning from endemic areas.
  • UK hospital prevalence: Approximately 2â€“5 cases per 100,000 annually, with malaria being the most common tropical disease.
  • Worldwide prevalence: Highly variable; for example, malaria affects over 200 million people annually, and dengue fever sees 390 million infections per year globally.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Tropical Diseases"][/sc]
  • Tropical diseases arise from exposure to pathogens in warm, humid climates which favour their lifecycle.
  • Vector-borne transmission: E.g., mosquitoes transmit Plasmodium spp. in malaria, causing red blood cell destruction and severe anaemia.
  • Environmental exposure: E.g., schistosomiasis is acquired through skin contact with freshwater containing parasite larvae, leading to chronic inflammation.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Tropical Diseases"][/sc]
  • Acute phase: High fever, fatigue, and other systemic symptoms (days to weeks).
  • Chronic phase: Progressive organ damage, e.g., hepatosplenomegaly in schistosomiasis or chronic lymphoedema in lymphatic filariasis (months to years).
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Tropical Diseases"][/sc]
  • Fever: Often the first symptom; caused by immune response to infection or toxins (e.g., cytokine release in malaria).
  • Fatigue: Resulting from systemic inflammation and anaemia.
  • Gastrointestinal symptoms: E.g., diarrhoea and vomiting in amoebiasis, due to mucosal damage.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Tropical Diseases"][/sc]
  • Pallor: Indicates anaemia, often severe in malaria or hookworm infection.
  • Hepatosplenomegaly: Enlargement due to chronic immune activation (e.g., schistosomiasis or visceral leishmaniasis).
  • Skin rashes: Typical of dengue fever or chikungunya, caused by capillary leakage and immune activation.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Tropical Diseases"][/sc]
  • Severe anaemia: Indicative of high parasitic load in malaria or chronic blood loss in hookworm infection.
  • Neurological symptoms: E.g., altered mental status in cerebral malaria, signifying high mortality risk.
  • Severe dehydration: Seen in cholera, can lead to hypovolaemic shock if untreated.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Tropical Diseases"][/sc]
  • Asymptomatic infection: Common in schistosomiasis or low-level malaria.
  • Isolated organ involvement: E.g., tropical splenomegaly syndrome without systemic signs.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Tropical Diseases"][/sc]
  • Blood smear: Thick and thin smears for malaria, revealing Plasmodium spp. morphology.
  • Serology: Detect antibodies or antigens (e.g., ELISA for leishmaniasis).
  • Stool examination: Identifies helminths or protozoa in intestinal infections.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Tropical Diseases"][/sc]
  • A 35-year-old male returning from sub-Saharan Africa presents with a 5-day history of high fever, fatigue, and headache. Examination reveals pallor and splenomegaly.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Tropical Diseases"][/sc]
  • Typhoid fever: Lacks cyclical fever patterns of malaria.
  • Sepsis: Generalised infection, no specific exposure history to tropical areas.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Tropical Diseases"][/sc]
  • Malaria: Artemisinin-based combination therapy (ACT).
  • Schistosomiasis: Praziquantel as the drug of choice.
  • Dengue: Supportive care, including fluid management.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Tropical Diseases"][/sc]
  • Cerebral malaria: High mortality, caused by sequestration of infected erythrocytes in cerebral capillaries.
  • Chronic lymphoedema: Result of repeated lymphatic filariasis infections.
  • Chronic organ damage: E.g., liver fibrosis in schistosomiasis.
