Eye Trauma or Foreign Body
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Eye Trauma or Foreign Body"][/sc]
  • Eye trauma refers to physical injury to the eye or its adnexa caused by blunt, penetrating, or chemical mechanisms. A foreign body in the eye is the presence of an external object lodged on or within ocular structures, commonly involving the conjunctiva or cornea.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Eye Trauma or Foreign Body"][/sc]
  • Eye trauma accounts for approximately 3% of presentations to UK general practitioners related to eye complaints.
  • Hospital presentations: Eye injuries constitute 10-20% of ocular-related emergency department visits in the UK.
  • Globally, eye trauma is a significant cause of preventable blindness, with a higher incidence in low-income regions.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Eye Trauma or Foreign Body"][/sc]
  • Blunt trauma causes compression and rebound forces, potentially leading to anterior segment injury, vitreoretinal traction, or posterior segment damage.
  • Penetrating trauma disrupts the structural integrity of the globe, risking infection and inflammation.
  • Foreign bodies may induce mechanical irritation, inflammatory responses, and secondary infection if not promptly removed.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Eye Trauma or Foreign Body"][/sc]
  • Immediate: Acute pain, photophobia, tearing, and blurred vision.
  • Hours to days: Inflammatory responses may worsen, causing redness, swelling, and discharge.
  • Days to weeks: If untreated, complications such as infection or corneal scarring may arise.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Eye Trauma or Foreign Body"][/sc]
  • Eye pain - Often caused by irritation of corneal nerves or trauma-induced tissue damage.
  • Foreign body sensation - A feeling of something in the eye due to the physical presence of an object.
  • Photophobia - Light sensitivity due to irritation or inflammation of the cornea or anterior chamber.
  • Blurred vision - Resulting from disruption of corneal clarity or refractive surface.
  • Excessive tearing - Reflex lacrimation triggered by ocular irritation.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Eye Trauma or Foreign Body"][/sc]
  • Conjunctival injection - Hyperaemia of the conjunctival vessels due to inflammation.
  • Corneal abrasion or defect - Visualised on fluorescein staining under cobalt-blue light.
  • Eyelid swelling - Oedema from trauma or inflammation.
  • Foreign body visible on examination - Often found on the cornea, conjunctiva, or under the eyelids.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Eye Trauma or Foreign Body"][/sc]
  • Severe vision loss - May indicate globe rupture or retinal detachment.
  • Hyphema - Blood in the anterior chamber, suggesting significant trauma.
  • Protruding foreign body - Signifies penetrating injury with potential for intraocular complications.
  • Severe eye pain with nausea - May indicate acute angle-closure glaucoma or intraocular pressure rise.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Eye Trauma or Foreign Body"][/sc]
  • Minimal symptoms despite severe injury - Seen in globe rupture where symptoms may not match severity.
  • Delayed symptoms - Occurs with retained intraocular foreign bodies.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Eye Trauma or Foreign Body"][/sc]
  • Slit-lamp examination - Identifies corneal defects, foreign bodies, or anterior chamber abnormalities.
  • Fluorescein staining - Highlights epithelial defects, appearing as bright green under cobalt-blue light.
  • CT scan of the orbits - Detects radiopaque foreign bodies and evaluates for globe rupture.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Eye Trauma or Foreign Body"][/sc]
  • A 32-year-old male presents with acute left eye pain, foreign body sensation, photophobia, and blurred vision after grinding metal without eye protection.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Eye Trauma or Foreign Body"][/sc]
  • Corneal ulcer - More severe pain, discharge, and epithelial defect with stromal involvement on staining.
  • Conjunctivitis - Diffuse conjunctival injection and discharge without a focal lesion.
  • Acute angle-closure glaucoma - Severe pain, headache, and elevated intraocular pressure.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Eye Trauma or Foreign Body"][/sc]
  • Foreign body removal - Using saline irrigation or cotton-tipped applicators, or referral for specialist removal if deeper structures are involved.
  • Antibiotic drops - Chloramphenicol 0.5% to prevent secondary infection.
  • Pain management - Topical anaesthetic for examination and oral analgesics.
  • Referral - Urgent ophthalmology referral for globe rupture, penetrating injuries, or suspected intraocular foreign bodies.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Eye Trauma or Foreign Body"][/sc]
  • Infection - Including bacterial keratitis or endophthalmitis, leading to vision loss.
  • Scarring - Corneal scarring can impair vision, requiring surgical intervention.
  • Vision loss - From retinal detachment, intraocular infection, or unaddressed injuries.
