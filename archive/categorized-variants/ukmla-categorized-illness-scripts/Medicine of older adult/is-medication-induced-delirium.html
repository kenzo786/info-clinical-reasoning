Medication-Induced Delirium
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Medication-Induced Delirium"][/sc]
  • Medication-induced delirium is an acute, fluctuating disorder of attention and cognitive function, precipitated by the administration of certain drugs. It typically presents with confusion, disorientation, and altered consciousness, often triggered by medications such as sedatives, anticholinergics, or opioids. Delirium can develop quickly, within hours or days of medication changes, and is often reversible with the cessation of the offending agent.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Medication-Induced Delirium"][/sc]
  • Prevalence in UK GP population: Up to 10-15% of older adults experience delirium during medical encounters, with medications often being a significant cause.
  • UK hospital prevalence: Delirium affects 15-30% of hospitalised elderly patients, with medications implicated in up to 40% of cases.
  • Worldwide prevalence: In hospital settings, particularly among older adults, the prevalence of delirium ranges from 10-40% globally, with a substantial portion attributed to medication effects.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Medication-Induced Delirium"][/sc]
  • Medications induce delirium by disrupting neurotransmitter balance in the brain. Key mechanisms include:
    • Cholinergic deficit: Many drugs (e.g., anticholinergics) block acetylcholine, leading to impaired attention and memory.
    • Dopaminergic excess: Medications like levodopa or antipsychotics can increase dopamine, resulting in hallucinations and delusions.
    • Inflammation and oxidative stress: Certain medications, particularly in frail or elderly individuals, can exacerbate neuroinflammation and oxidative damage, contributing to cognitive decline.
    • Sleep-wake cycle disruption: Sedative drugs can alter sleep patterns, further impairing cognitive function.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Medication-Induced Delirium"][/sc]
  • Onset: Delirium often develops within hours to days following medication changes or new drug initiation.
  • Duration: The condition can persist for days to weeks, particularly if the offending agent is not discontinued.
  • Resolution: Delirium typically resolves gradually after stopping the offending drug, but recovery may be prolonged in patients with underlying vulnerabilities (e.g., dementia).
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Medication-Induced Delirium"][/sc]
  • Confusion - The most common symptom, confusion arises due to impaired acetylcholine transmission, reducing attention and memory.
  • Disorientation - Patients often lose track of time and place due to cognitive disruption affecting spatial and temporal awareness.
  • Hallucinations - Visual or auditory hallucinations can occur, particularly with dopaminergic or anticholinergic drugs, due to imbalanced neurotransmitter levels.
  • Restlessness or agitation - Hyperactive delirium presents with agitation, often due to drug-induced overactivity of dopamine or withdrawal effects.
  • Inattention - A hallmark of delirium, attention deficits result from cholinergic and dopaminergic dysregulation.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Medication-Induced Delirium"][/sc]
  • Fluctuating consciousness - Characterised by an alternation between drowsiness and hypervigilance, this occurs due to impaired neurotransmitter regulation.
  • Tachycardia - Certain medications (e.g., anticholinergics) can increase heart rate by blocking vagal tone.
  • Mydriasis - Anticholinergic medications can dilate pupils by inhibiting parasympathetic activity.
  • Tremors - Excessive dopamine or drug withdrawal can result in fine motor tremors or asterixis.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Medication-Induced Delirium"][/sc]
  • Sudden onset of severe agitation or stupor - May indicate neuroleptic malignant syndrome (NMS) or serotonin syndrome, both life-threatening conditions linked to certain medications.
  • Severe autonomic instability (e.g., tachycardia, fever, diaphoresis) - Can signify drug toxicity, such as serotonin syndrome, requiring immediate medical intervention.
  • Altered consciousness with focal neurological deficits - Suggests stroke or another acute intracranial event, which can be confused with delirium but demands urgent neuroimaging.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Medication-Induced Delirium"][/sc]
  • Hypoactive delirium - Instead of agitation, patients present with lethargy, withdrawal, or excessive somnolence. It is easily missed but can still be linked to medications (e.g., opioids, sedatives).
  • Delirium superimposed on dementia - Pre-existing cognitive decline can mask the classic symptoms of delirium, making the condition difficult to diagnose without a baseline cognitive assessment.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Medication-Induced Delirium"][/sc]
  • Blood tests - FBC, U&Es, LFTs to assess for metabolic disturbances, infection, or organ dysfunction that might exacerbate delirium.
  • Toxicology screening - To identify potential offending medications or substances contributing to delirium.
  • ECG - Especially important if cardiotoxic drugs (e.g., antipsychotics) are involved, to assess for QT prolongation.
  • CT/MRI brain - Neuroimaging to exclude structural causes (e.g., stroke, haemorrhage) in patients with focal neurological signs or refractory delirium.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Medication-Induced Delirium"][/sc]
  • An 82-year-old woman with hypertension and osteoarthritis presents with confusion, agitation, and hallucinations 48 hours after being prescribed oxycodone for pain management. Symptoms fluctuated over the past day, with episodes of disorientation and visual hallucinations.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Medication-Induced Delirium"][/sc]
  • Dementia - Chronic, progressive memory loss and cognitive decline, unlike the acute, fluctuating course of delirium.
  • Stroke - Sudden onset of neurological deficits, often with focal signs, whereas delirium presents with diffuse cognitive impairment.
  • Sepsis - Can cause delirium, but fever, leukocytosis, and signs of infection are more prominent, especially in elderly patients.
  • Substance withdrawal - Delirium tremens due to alcohol withdrawal resembles delirium but often includes autonomic hyperactivity (e.g., tremor, sweating).
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Medication-Induced Delirium"][/sc]
  • Identify and discontinue offending medications - Immediate cessation or dose adjustment is key to reversing delirium.
  • Environmental modifications - Calm, well-lit rooms with regular reorientation to reduce disorientation.
  • Antipsychotics (e.g., haloperidol) - In low doses, can be used in severe agitation but should be avoided long-term due to the risk of side effects.
  • Optimisation of hydration and nutrition - Prevents metabolic derangements that may exacerbate delirium.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Medication-Induced Delirium"][/sc]
  • Prolonged hospitalisation - Delirium significantly increases length of stay, especially in elderly patients.
  • Cognitive decline - Persistent cognitive deficits may remain after the resolution of acute delirium, particularly in those with pre-existing cognitive impairment.
  • Falls and injuries - Delirium increases the risk of falls, which can lead to fractures or head trauma.
