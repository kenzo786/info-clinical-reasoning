Frailty
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Frailty"][/sc]
  • Frailty is a clinical syndrome characterised by a reduced physiological reserve and diminished resistance to stressors, increasing vulnerability to adverse outcomes like falls, hospitalisation, and mortality. It is often marked by weakness, slowness, and decreased activity, with underlying multi-system impairment.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Frailty"][/sc]
  • UK GP Population: Frailty affects approximately 10% of those over 65, rising to 25-50% in those over 85.
  • UK Hospital Prevalence: Frailty is prevalent in 25-40% of older adults admitted to hospital.
  • Worldwide Prevalence: Globally, frailty prevalence among older adults is estimated at 10-15%, with higher rates in institutional settings.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Frailty"][/sc]
  • Frailty involves complex, multi-system pathophysiology affecting muscles, the cardiovascular system, immunity, and metabolism. Chronic inflammation (elevated IL-6, TNF-Î±) leads to sarcopenia and physical decline. Mitochondrial dysfunction impairs cellular energy production, while hormonal changes (decreased oestrogen/testosterone) contribute to muscle wasting. These systemic alterations diminish overall resilience to stressors.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Frailty"][/sc]
  • Frailty typically progresses gradually over years, though acute events like infection or injury can accelerate deterioration. Phases range from prefrailty (mild weakness) to moderate frailty (reduced independence) and severe frailty (high dependence and frequent adverse events).
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Frailty"][/sc]
  • Fatigue - Persistent tiredness is common due to reduced metabolic efficiency and inflammatory responses impairing muscle function.
  • Muscle weakness - Caused by sarcopenia and reduced muscle mass, limiting physical abilities and daily functioning.
  • Weight loss - Often seen due to decreased appetite and muscle catabolism, affecting overall body mass.
  • Mobility issues - Limited physical activity and gait changes from musculoskeletal and neurological impairments.
  • Low mood or cognitive decline - Depression and cognitive impairment are common due to neuroinflammatory changes and social isolation.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Frailty"][/sc]
  • Slowness in gait - Reduced walking speed reflects decreased muscle power and energy levels.
  • Unintentional weight loss - Indicates nutritional deficits and muscle wasting, contributing to vulnerability.
  • Reduced grip strength - A key indicator of sarcopenia and frailty-related physical weakness.
  • Postural instability - A result of weakened muscles and balance issues, increasing fall risk.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Frailty"][/sc]
  • Frequent falls - Signifies severe frailty and high fall risk, often leading to fractures and hospitalisation.
  • Severe weight loss - Indicates potential for malnutrition, immune dysfunction, and increased mortality risk.
  • Confusion or delirium - May point to cognitive decline or infection, particularly sepsis, in frail patients.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Frailty"][/sc]
  • Some individuals may present with isolated symptoms like minor cognitive changes, apathy, or social withdrawal before physical signs appear, due to subtle systemic inflammatory effects on the brain.

Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Frailty"][/sc]
  • Exercise - Strength and balance training as per NICE guidelines can reduce fall risk.
  • Nutritional support - High-protein diet and supplements to address weight loss.
  • Medication review - To reduce polypharmacy and optimise treatment for co-morbid conditions.
  • Social support - Comprehensive geriatric assessment and support for independence.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Frailty"][/sc]
  • Falls and fractures - Common and often lead to hospitalisation; related to muscle weakness and balance issues.
  • Hospitalisation and dependency - Increased risk due to recurrent illness and injury; often leads to further functional decline.
  • Infection risk - Due to immune dysfunction, leading to increased morbidity and mortality.
