Haemoptysis
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Haemoptysis"][/sc]
  • Haemoptysis is the expectoration of blood originating from the lower respiratory tract, often from pulmonary vessels or bronchial circulation. It ranges in severity from blood-streaked sputum to massive haemoptysis, which poses an acute risk to life due to airway obstruction and blood loss.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Haemoptysis"][/sc]
  • UK GP population: Haemoptysis is a rare presentation, with most cases attributed to infections, but it can occasionally indicate serious underlying disease.
  • UK hospital prevalence: Around 10-15% of cases with haemoptysis require hospitalisation; commonly seen in patients with respiratory conditions like tuberculosis or malignancy.
  • Worldwide prevalence: Varies significantly by region, higher in areas with endemic tuberculosis or poor air quality. Rates are notably higher in low-income countries due to infection prevalence.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Haemoptysis"][/sc]
  • Bronchial circulation rupture: Increased pressure in bronchial arteries (e.g., due to chronic lung inflammation or malignancy) can cause vessel rupture and bleeding.
  • Infectious erosion: Pathogens, particularly mycobacterium in tuberculosis, erode lung tissue and vasculature, leading to bleeding into airways.
  • Vascular compromise: Conditions like pulmonary embolism can cause haemorrhage into alveoli due to sudden vascular occlusion, resulting in haemoptysis.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Haemoptysis"][/sc]
  • Acute onset: Commonly seen in infections or pulmonary embolism; sudden with rapid worsening.
  • Subacute/chronic: Seen in cases like lung cancer or bronchiectasis, where haemoptysis develops slowly and may progress gradually.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Haemoptysis"][/sc]
  • Blood in sputum: Ranging from streaks to larger amounts; due to bleeding into respiratory passages.
  • Cough: Often accompanies haemoptysis, as coughing reflex is triggered by blood or irritants in airways.
  • Dyspnoea: Related to airway obstruction from blood or underlying lung disease.
  • Pleuritic chest pain: Especially in cases of pulmonary embolism or infection where pleura are irritated.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Haemoptysis"][/sc]
  • Tachypnoea: Common due to hypoxia or pain; rapid breathing compensates for compromised oxygen exchange.
  • Crackles or wheezing: Due to underlying lung disease (e.g., bronchiectasis, infection) causing turbulent airflow.
  • Cyanosis: Occurs in severe cases due to impaired gas exchange from blood in alveoli or significant underlying lung pathology.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Haemoptysis"][/sc]
  • Massive haemoptysis: Life-threatening; risk of asphyxiation due to airway obstruction or hypovolaemic shock.
  • Weight loss and night sweats: Suggest malignancy or tuberculosis; significant for identifying systemic disease.
  • Persistent or worsening haemoptysis: Concerning for malignancy or untreated infection.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Haemoptysis"][/sc]
  • Scant haemoptysis with non-specific symptoms: May mask underlying cancer or TB, presenting more subtly in elderly or immunocompromised.
  • Haemoptysis without cough: Can be seen in some cases of lung cancer or pulmonary embolism, especially with minimal airway irritation.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Haemoptysis"][/sc]
  • Chest X-ray: Initial assessment for masses, infections, or structural abnormalities.
  • CT Pulmonary Angiography: Gold standard for detecting pulmonary emboli and assessing bronchial circulation abnormalities.
  • Sputum culture and acid-fast bacilli testing: Identifies bacterial causes or TB; essential for cases with chronic or recurrent haemoptysis.
  • Bronchoscopy: Used to directly visualise and source local bleeding in persistent cases.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Haemoptysis"][/sc]
  • A 65-year-old male smoker presents with a 2-week history of cough producing blood-streaked sputum, weight loss, and occasional dyspnoea.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Haemoptysis"][/sc]
  • Bronchiectasis: Presents with chronic cough and purulent sputum; differs by having a history of recurrent infections.
  • Pulmonary embolism: Acute onset, often with pleuritic pain and dyspnoea; blood in sputum tends to be sudden and episodic.
  • TB: Slow, progressive haemoptysis with night sweats and fever; common in those with immunosuppression or exposure risk.
  • Lung cancer: Persistent haemoptysis, weight loss, and localised chest pain; may be occult in early stages.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Haemoptysis"][/sc]
  • Supportive care: Oxygen, monitoring, and IV fluids for severe cases; necessary for acute haemoptysis with hypoxia.
  • Antibiotics: For bacterial infections; use culture-guided therapy or empirical treatment in high-risk infections.
  • Antituberculosis therapy: Rifampicin, isoniazid, pyrazinamide, and ethambutol (RIPE regimen) in TB-confirmed cases.
  • Bronchial artery embolisation: Interventional treatment for significant bleeding unresponsive to conservative measures.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Haemoptysis"][/sc]
  • Asphyxiation: Massive haemoptysis obstructing airways can result in life-threatening hypoxia.
  • Respiratory failure: Chronic haemoptysis may lead to worsening pulmonary function and oxygenation deficits.
  • Shock: Severe blood loss can lead to hypovolaemic shock, particularly in massive haemoptysis cases.
