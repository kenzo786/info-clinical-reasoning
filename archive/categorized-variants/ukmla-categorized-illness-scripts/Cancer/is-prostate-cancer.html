Prostate Cancer
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Prostate Cancer"][/sc]
  • Prostate cancer is a malignancy originating in the prostate gland, commonly characterised by the uncontrolled growth of abnormal glandular cells. Prostate cancer can vary significantly in terms of aggressiveness, ranging from indolent forms that may not require treatment to highly aggressive and metastatic cancers. It often affects the peripheral zone of the prostate and may remain asymptomatic in early stages.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Prostate Cancer"][/sc]
  • UK GP Population Prevalence: Approximately 1 in 8 men in the UK will be diagnosed with prostate cancer in their lifetime, with higher rates in older populations.
  • UK Hospital Prevalence: Prostate cancer accounts for around 26% of new male cancer cases in the UK.
  • Worldwide Prevalence: Prostate cancer is the second most common cancer in men globally, with incidence highest in North America and Europe.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Prostate Cancer"][/sc]
  • Prostate cancer generally originates from glandular epithelial cells in the prostate, typically in the peripheral zone.
  • Mutations in oncogenes and tumour suppressor genes (e.g., PTEN, TP53) lead to dysregulated cell proliferation.
  • Growth is often driven by androgens, which bind to androgen receptors and stimulate tumour growth. Advanced stages may become androgen-independent.
  • As the tumour progresses, it can invade local structures, spread to pelvic lymph nodes, and metastasise to distant sites, primarily bones and lymph nodes.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Prostate Cancer"][/sc]
  • Early Stage: Often asymptomatic, slow-growing, may remain undiagnosed for years.
  • Localised Disease: Growth within the prostate gland, with potential urinary symptoms or incidental detection through screening.
  • Locally Advanced Disease: Spread to surrounding tissues, increasing urinary and systemic symptoms.
  • Advanced/Metastatic Stage: Metastasis to bones and other organs; prominent systemic symptoms and pain.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Prostate Cancer"][/sc]
  • Asymptomatic in Early Stages - Commonly asymptomatic in localised stages, detected incidentally through screening.
  • Urinary Hesitancy/Weak Stream - Due to prostate enlargement compressing the urethra.
  • Nocturia - Increased frequency of urination at night from bladder outlet obstruction.
  • Haematuria - Prostate gland invasion causing bleeding into the urinary tract.
  • Bone Pain (in metastatic disease) - Often due to metastases to the pelvis, spine, or femur causing pain and sometimes fractures.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Prostate Cancer"][/sc]
  • Prostate Enlargement on Digital Rectal Exam (DRE) - The prostate may feel asymmetrically enlarged, nodular, or firm, suggesting malignancy.
  • Cachexia in Advanced Disease - Weight loss due to advanced malignancy and systemic involvement.
  • Bone Tenderness - Painful areas upon palpation, indicating bony metastasis.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Prostate Cancer"][/sc]
  • New-Onset Bone Pain - Indicates possible bony metastasis, commonly seen in the spine, pelvis, or ribs.
  • Unintentional Weight Loss - May suggest systemic disease spread or aggressive cancer subtype.
  • Lower Limb Weakness or Paraesthesia - Could signal spinal cord compression due to vertebral metastases.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Prostate Cancer"][/sc]
  • Young Patients with Aggressive Cancer - Though rare, younger patients may present with advanced disease and rapid progression.
  • Isolated Bone Pain without Urinary Symptoms - Some patients with metastatic disease may first present with skeletal pain without urinary complaints.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Prostate Cancer"][/sc]
  • PSA Test: Elevated prostate-specific antigen levels (typically >4 ng/mL in prostate cancer); helps in screening and monitoring but may have false positives in benign conditions.
  • Digital Rectal Exam (DRE): Assessment of prostate size, nodularity, and asymmetry, aiding in initial suspicion of malignancy.
  • MRI of Prostate: Multi-parametric MRI assesses tumour location, extent, and invasion.
  • Prostate Biopsy: Core needle biopsy confirms diagnosis with histopathological grading (e.g., Gleason score).
  • Bone Scan (if metastatic disease suspected): Detects bone metastases, often positive in advanced disease.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Prostate Cancer"][/sc]
  • A 70-year-old man presents with progressive nocturia, urinary hesitancy, and new-onset lower back pain over 6 months. On DRE, his prostate is asymmetrically enlarged and firm. PSA is elevated at 20 ng/mL. Imaging suggests bone metastasis.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Prostate Cancer"][/sc]
  • Benign Prostatic Hyperplasia (BPH): Generally causes similar urinary symptoms without asymmetry on DRE or elevated PSA typical of malignancy.
  • Bladder Cancer: Can cause haematuria and dysuria, but urinary cytology and cystoscopy help distinguish it from prostate cancer.
  • Chronic Prostatitis: Often presents with pelvic pain, perineal pain, and lower urinary tract symptoms but usually lacks a firm, nodular prostate on DRE.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Prostate Cancer"][/sc]
  • Active Surveillance: For low-risk, localised disease, with regular PSA tests and biopsies.
  • Radical Prostatectomy: Surgical removal of the prostate in localised cancer, generally for younger or higher-risk patients.
  • Radiotherapy: External beam or brachytherapy, especially in localised or locally advanced stages.
  • Hormonal Therapy (Androgen Deprivation Therapy): Used for advanced or metastatic cancer to slow disease progression by lowering androgen levels.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Prostate Cancer"][/sc]
  • Metastasis to Bone: Common in advanced disease, often leads to pain and pathological fractures.
  • Spinal Cord Compression: Due to vertebral metastases, causing neurological deficits or paralysis if untreated.
  • Urinary Incontinence: May occur post-treatment (especially post-surgery or radiotherapy).
  • Outlook: Localised prostate cancer has high survival rates, while metastatic prostate cancer has a 5-year survival rate below 30%.
