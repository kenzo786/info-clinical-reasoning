Sudden Sensorineural Hearing Loss (SSNHL)
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Sudden Sensorineural Hearing Loss (SSNHL)"][/sc]
  • Sudden Sensorineural Hearing Loss (SSNHL) is defined as a rapid-onset hearing loss, typically occurring over a period of fewer than 72 hours. It is a type of hearing loss affecting the inner ear or the auditory nerve, leading to a reduction of hearing function in one or both ears, with no obvious external cause such as earwax or infection. The hearing loss is usually unilateral, and the degree of loss can vary from mild to profound.

Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Sudden Sensorineural Hearing Loss (SSNHL)"][/sc]
  • The exact pathophysiology of SSNHL is unknown, but several mechanisms have been proposed:
    • Viral infections: Viral inflammation may damage the cochlea or auditory nerve, causing hearing loss.
    • Vascular occlusion: Disruption of blood supply to the cochlea due to microvascular events can result in ischaemic damage.
    • Autoimmune response: Antibodies attacking the inner ear structures can result in acute hearing loss.
    • Trauma or perilymph fistula: Sudden changes in pressure can rupture the inner ear membranes.
    • Idiopathic: In most cases (up to 90%), no identifiable cause is found.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Sudden Sensorineural Hearing Loss (SSNHL)"][/sc]
  • Onset: SSNHL typically occurs suddenly, with patients often describing hearing loss noticed upon waking or over a few hours to days (less than 72 hours).
  • Progression: The degree of hearing loss may worsen within a short period, sometimes stabilising after the initial loss.
  • Recovery: Spontaneous recovery occurs in 32â€“65% of cases, usually within the first two weeks. Prompt treatment improves the likelihood of recovery.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Sudden Sensorineural Hearing Loss (SSNHL)"][/sc]
  • Sudden unilateral hearing loss - The hallmark symptom of SSNHL, typically experienced in one ear, with a rapid onset over a few hours or less. This occurs due to damage to the cochlear structures or auditory nerve.
  • Aural fullness - A sensation of pressure or blockage in the affected ear, often accompanying hearing loss. This may be due to fluid accumulation or inflammation.
  • Tinnitus - Ringing or buzzing in the ear is common and occurs due to abnormal cochlear nerve activity or damage.
  • Vertigo - Occurs in some patients, caused by a disruption in the vestibular system, which shares structures with the cochlea.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Sudden Sensorineural Hearing Loss (SSNHL)"][/sc]
  • Positive Rinne test (air > bone conduction) - Confirms sensorineural hearing loss; the sound is heard louder through air conduction because the auditory nerve is impaired.
  • Weber test lateralises to the unaffected ear - In SSNHL, the sound localises to the better hearing ear, indicating a sensorineural loss in the opposite ear.
  • Reduced speech discrimination - The patient may struggle to distinguish words during speech audiometry testing due to auditory nerve dysfunction.

Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Sudden Sensorineural Hearing Loss (SSNHL)"][/sc]
  • Gradual hearing loss over weeks - More characteristic of chronic conditions, such as acoustic neuroma or chronic otitis media, rather than SSNHL.
  • Bilateral involvement - Rare in SSNHL; suggests other systemic diseases like autoimmune inner ear disease or meningitis.
