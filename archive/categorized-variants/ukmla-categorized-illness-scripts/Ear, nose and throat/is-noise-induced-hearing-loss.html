Noise-Induced Hearing Loss (NIHL)
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Noise-Induced Hearing Loss (NIHL)"][/sc]
  • Noise-Induced Hearing Loss (NIHL) is a sensorineural hearing impairment caused by prolonged exposure to high-intensity noise, or a single exposure to an extremely loud sound (acoustic trauma). It typically affects high-frequency hearing initially and is usually irreversible.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Noise-Induced Hearing Loss (NIHL)"][/sc]
  • UK GP prevalence: NIHL is one of the leading causes of acquired hearing loss, with approximately 1.1 million people at risk of NIHL in the UK due to occupational exposure.
  • UK hospital prevalence: NIHL accounts for a significant proportion of cases seen in audiology clinics, especially in older adults with a history of occupational noise exposure.
  • Worldwide prevalence: Globally, NIHL affects over 1.1 billion people, primarily in industrialised nations where occupational and recreational noise exposure is high.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Noise-Induced Hearing Loss (NIHL)"][/sc]
  • Exposure to loud noise causes mechanical damage to hair cells in the cochlea, particularly the outer hair cells responsible for amplifying sound. Repeated exposure leads to loss of these cells, disrupting the transmission of sound signals to the auditory nerve.
  • Free radical formation and oxidative stress are believed to contribute to hair cell death following noise exposure, exacerbating hearing loss.
  • The basilar membrane of the cochlea is more sensitive to high frequencies, explaining why NIHL initially affects high-pitched sounds.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Noise-Induced Hearing Loss (NIHL)"][/sc]
  • Acute Phase: After a single traumatic noise exposure, individuals may experience a temporary threshold shift (TTS), characterised by transient hearing loss and tinnitus, typically lasting 24-48 hours.
  • Chronic Phase: With prolonged exposure, irreversible permanent threshold shifts (PTS) develop, leading to progressive, permanent hearing loss over months to years.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Noise-Induced Hearing Loss (NIHL)"][/sc]
  • Hearing loss - Gradual, high-frequency hearing loss is often the first symptom, resulting from damage to cochlear hair cells sensitive to high-pitched sounds.
  • Tinnitus - Ringing, buzzing, or hissing in the ears, caused by abnormal nerve activity in the auditory system, often associated with cochlear hair cell damage.
  • Difficulties in speech discrimination - Especially in noisy environments, due to the selective loss of high-frequency hearing needed for consonant differentiation.
  • Hyperacusis - Heightened sensitivity to normal environmental sounds due to altered sound processing in the damaged cochlea.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Noise-Induced Hearing Loss (NIHL)"][/sc]
  • High-frequency hearing loss on audiometry - Typically a notch at 3-6 kHz on a pure tone audiogram, reflecting early cochlear damage.
  • Normal otoscopic examination - Since NIHL is a sensorineural loss, the external ear and tympanic membrane appear normal.
  • Reduced speech discrimination scores - Due to the loss of high-frequency sounds critical for understanding speech, especially consonants.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Noise-Induced Hearing Loss (NIHL)"][/sc]
  • Sudden hearing loss - Could indicate acoustic trauma or other causes of sudden sensorineural hearing loss, such as vestibular schwannoma or autoimmune disorders.
  • Asymmetry in hearing loss - Unilateral or significantly asymmetric hearing loss raises suspicion of retrocochlear pathology, such as an acoustic neuroma.
  • Persistent vertigo - May suggest vestibular involvement, which is atypical in pure NIHL and could indicate concurrent vestibular damage or another inner ear pathology.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Noise-Induced Hearing Loss (NIHL)"][/sc]
  • Sudden hearing loss without trauma - While NIHL typically presents gradually, a sudden onset without a clear noise event could mimic conditions like idiopathic sudden sensorineural hearing loss (SSNHL) or Meniere's disease.
  • Normal hearing with tinnitus - Tinnitus may present without measurable hearing loss, often in early stages of cochlear damage or when the damage affects a narrow frequency range not detected on standard audiograms.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Noise-Induced Hearing Loss (NIHL)"][/sc]
  • Audiometry - Pure tone audiometry typically shows a characteristic "noise notch" at 3-6 kHz, representing high-frequency loss. This test is the gold standard for diagnosing NIHL.
  • Speech audiometry - Decreased speech discrimination scores, especially in noisy environments, help confirm sensorineural hearing loss affecting higher frequencies.
  • Tympanometry - Normal results, ruling out conductive causes of hearing loss.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Noise-Induced Hearing Loss (NIHL)"][/sc]
  • A 55-year-old male presents with progressive difficulty hearing in noisy environments and constant tinnitus, with a history of occupational noise exposure in construction. Audiometry shows high-frequency hearing loss, consistent with noise-induced hearing loss.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Noise-Induced Hearing Loss (NIHL)"][/sc]
  • Presbycusis - Age-related hearing loss typically presents bilaterally and gradually, affecting both high and low frequencies, unlike the high-frequency loss seen in NIHL.
  • Meniere's disease - Characterised by fluctuating hearing loss, tinnitus, and episodic vertigo, differing from the stable, progressive hearing loss of NIHL.
  • Otosclerosis - Causes conductive hearing loss due to stapes fixation, showing normal audiometry in the high frequencies but conductive loss in lower frequencies.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Noise-Induced Hearing Loss (NIHL)"][/sc]
  • Prevention - The mainstay of NIHL management is prevention, with occupational noise control, use of ear protection, and limiting exposure to loud sounds.
  • Hearing aids - For individuals with significant hearing loss, amplification through hearing aids can improve hearing and quality of life.
  • Tinnitus management - Cognitive behavioural therapy (CBT) and sound therapy may be beneficial for patients with significant tinnitus.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Noise-Induced Hearing Loss (NIHL)"][/sc]
  • Social isolation and depression - Progressive hearing loss can impair communication, leading to social withdrawal and increased risk of depression.
  • Worsening tinnitus - Persistent tinnitus can exacerbate anxiety and sleep disturbances, negatively affecting quality of life.
