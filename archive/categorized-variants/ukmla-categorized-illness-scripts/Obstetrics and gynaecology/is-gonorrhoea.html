Gonorrhoea
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Gonorrhoea"][/sc]
  • Gonorrhoea is a sexually transmitted infection caused by the bacterium Neisseria gonorrhoeae. It primarily affects the mucous membranes of the urogenital tract but can also infect the rectum, pharynx, and conjunctiva. Infections may be symptomatic or asymptomatic, particularly in women, leading to transmission without detection.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Gonorrhoea"][/sc]
  • UK prevalence (GP setting): Amongst sexually active adults, approximately 47 per 100,000 annually.
  • UK hospital prevalence: Commonly seen in sexual health clinics, with rising cases due to antibiotic resistance.
  • Global prevalence: Roughly 87 million cases annually worldwide, with high prevalence in Sub-Saharan Africa, Southeast Asia, and the Americas.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Gonorrhoea"][/sc]
  • The pathogen Neisseria gonorrhoeae attaches to epithelial cells using pili and other adhesion molecules.
  • It invades local tissue, causing inflammation and immune cell infiltration, which leads to tissue damage.
  • In men, infection primarily affects the urethra, while in women, the cervix is the primary site.
  • If untreated, the bacteria can spread, causing complications such as pelvic inflammatory disease (PID) in women and epididymitis in men.
  • Disseminated gonococcal infection (DGI) occurs when the pathogen enters the bloodstream, causing systemic complications.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Gonorrhoea"][/sc]
  • Incubation period: 2-14 days post-exposure, commonly 3-5 days.
  • Acute phase: Symptomatic in men within days; may take longer in women, or remain asymptomatic.
  • Chronic or untreated phase: Risks of systemic spread, chronic pelvic pain, or infertility in long-term untreated cases.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Gonorrhoea"][/sc]
  • Dysuria - Painful urination due to urethral inflammation caused by bacterial invasion.
  • Purulent urethral discharge - Thick yellow-green discharge from the urethra, resulting from neutrophilic response to infection.
  • In women: increased vaginal discharge - Due to cervicitis, leading to excessive mucus production.
  • In men: testicular pain - Often from epididymitis, inflammation spreads to testicular structures.
  • Rectal pain or discharge - Associated with proctitis, resulting from rectal infection in receptive anal intercourse.
  • Asymptomatic presentation - Common in women (up to 50%), contributing to transmission risks.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Gonorrhoea"][/sc]
  • Urethral discharge - Visible purulent discharge, especially in men, linked to inflammation.
  • Cervical motion tenderness - Painful during examination, suggesting pelvic inflammatory disease (PID).
  • Pharyngeal erythema - Redness and swelling in the pharynx if gonorrhoea involves the throat.
  • Conjunctival injection - If present, severe redness and purulent discharge in cases of ocular infection.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Gonorrhoea"][/sc]
  • Severe pelvic or abdominal pain - May indicate pelvic inflammatory disease (PID) or tubo-ovarian abscess.
  • Fever and joint pain - Suggestive of disseminated gonococcal infection (DGI), indicating systemic spread.
  • Visual disturbances - Can be a sign of ocular gonorrhoea, potentially leading to severe corneal damage.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Gonorrhoea"][/sc]
  • Pharyngeal gonorrhoea - Often asymptomatic, can result in mild sore throat or no symptoms at all.
  • Ocular gonorrhoea - Rare, presents with conjunctivitis and possible severe ocular damage.
  • Rectal gonorrhoea - May present asymptomatically or with mild symptoms like rectal discharge or discomfort.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Gonorrhoea"][/sc]
  • NAAT (nucleic acid amplification test) - Sensitive and specific test for detecting N. gonorrhoeae DNA or RNA from urogenital, rectal, or pharyngeal swabs.
  • Microscopy and Gram stain - Gram-negative diplococci observed within neutrophils in urethral discharge samples.
  • Culture - Required for antibiotic susceptibility testing due to resistance concerns; samples taken from urethra, cervix, or pharynx.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Gonorrhoea"][/sc]
  • A 23-year-old man presents with a 4-day history of dysuria and purulent urethral discharge. Reports recent unprotected intercourse with a new partner.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Gonorrhoea"][/sc]
  • Chlamydia trachomatis infection - Often presents with milder symptoms; NAAT can differentiate.
  • Urinary tract infection (UTI) - Less likely to cause purulent discharge; urine culture can distinguish.
  • Trichomoniasis - More common in women, diagnosed via microscopy or NAAT.
  • Herpes simplex virus (HSV) - May present with ulcers and systemic symptoms; viral culture or PCR can confirm.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Gonorrhoea"][/sc]
  • UK guidelines: Single dose of intramuscular ceftriaxone 1g, with doxycycline 100 mg twice daily for 7 days if concurrent chlamydia infection is suspected.
  • Antibiotic resistance considerations: Routine susceptibility testing due to rising resistance; alternative antibiotics used based on susceptibility profile.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Gonorrhoea"][/sc]
  • Pelvic inflammatory disease (PID) - Common in untreated women, leading to infertility, ectopic pregnancy.
  • Epididymitis - Painful inflammation in men, can cause infertility if severe.
  • Disseminated gonococcal infection (DGI) - Rare, systemic spread causing arthritis, dermatitis, or endocarditis.
