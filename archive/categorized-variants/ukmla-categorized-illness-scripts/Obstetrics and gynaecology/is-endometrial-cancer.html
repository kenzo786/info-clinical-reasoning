Endometrial Cancer
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Endometrial Cancer"][/sc]
  • Endometrial cancer is a malignancy that originates from the endometrial lining of the uterus, most commonly affecting postmenopausal women. It is generally an adenocarcinoma, with endometrioid adenocarcinoma as the predominant histological subtype. The condition is often associated with unopposed oestrogen exposure, and it can present with abnormal uterine bleeding as the most common symptom.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Endometrial Cancer"][/sc]
  • Prevalence in UK GP population: Approximately 25 per 100,000 women annually.
  • UK hospital prevalence: Endometrial cancer is the fourth most common cancer in women and the most common gynaecological cancer in the UK.
  • Worldwide prevalence: Endometrial cancer has a global incidence of around 380,000 cases annually, with increasing rates linked to rising obesity and life expectancy.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Endometrial Cancer"][/sc]
  • Endometrial cancer typically arises due to an imbalance between oestrogen and progesterone, leading to excessive endometrial proliferation without regular shedding.
  • Unopposed oestrogen, whether endogenous (as seen in obesity or polycystic ovarian syndrome) or exogenous (from hormone therapy), increases mitotic activity within endometrial cells, heightening the risk of malignant transformation.
  • Mutations in tumour suppressor genes (e.g., PTEN) and oncogenes (e.g., PIK3CA) further contribute to carcinogenesis in the endometrial lining.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Endometrial Cancer"][/sc]
  • Early stages: Often asymptomatic or mild bleeding episodes. Cancerous growth is usually confined to the endometrium.
  • Progressive stages: Symptoms intensify with frequent bleeding, pain, and possible metastasis to surrounding pelvic structures.
  • Advanced stages: May lead to significant tumour burden, invasion into adjacent organs, and distant metastases.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Endometrial Cancer"][/sc]
  • Postmenopausal bleeding - Commonest symptom; occurs as the tumour disrupts normal endometrial architecture, leading to irregular shedding.
  • Intermenstrual bleeding - In premenopausal women, abnormal cycles are common; due to disrupted endometrial cell turnover.
  • Pelvic pain - Indicates local spread or tumour pressure on surrounding tissues, often a later sign.
  • Vaginal discharge - May be bloody or serous; related to necrosis and tissue breakdown within the tumour.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Endometrial Cancer"][/sc]
  • Abnormal findings on pelvic exam - Enlarged, irregularly shaped uterus may indicate tumour growth; found during bimanual exam.
  • Visible vaginal bleeding - Often noted if the patient presents with active bleeding.
  • Cachexia and weight loss - In advanced cases, systemic signs of malignancy like weight loss may be evident.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Endometrial Cancer"][/sc]
  • Postmenopausal bleeding - Strong indicator of potential endometrial malignancy; requires urgent evaluation.
  • Persistent intermenstrual bleeding - Particularly concerning if persistent in women over 45, as it may signify endometrial cancer.
  • Unexplained weight loss - Potential sign of advanced or metastatic disease.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Endometrial Cancer"][/sc]
  • Asymptomatic presentation - Especially in early stages or incidental findings on imaging for another issue.
  • Vague abdominal discomfort - May present in advanced disease as the tumour affects surrounding structures, without clear bleeding history.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Endometrial Cancer"][/sc]
  • Transvaginal ultrasound - First-line imaging; thickened endometrial lining (>4 mm in postmenopausal women) suggests possible malignancy.
  • Endometrial biopsy - Confirms diagnosis; pathologic analysis may reveal atypical cells or adenocarcinoma.
  • Hysteroscopy - Provides direct visualisation; allows targeted biopsies.
  • CT/MRI of pelvis - Used for staging; detects local invasion or distant metastasis.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Endometrial Cancer"][/sc]
  • 65-year-old postmenopausal woman presents with a 3-month history of vaginal bleeding and occasional pelvic pain, concerning for endometrial cancer.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Endometrial Cancer"][/sc]
  • Endometrial hyperplasia - Presents similarly with bleeding but lacks malignant cells on biopsy.
  • Cervical cancer - Postmenopausal bleeding, but associated with abnormal cervical smear and visible cervical lesions.
  • Polycystic ovary syndrome (PCOS) - In younger women with irregular bleeding, not typically seen postmenopause.
  • Atrophic vaginitis - Postmenopausal bleeding but linked to vaginal dryness and low oestrogen levels, no endometrial thickening.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Endometrial Cancer"][/sc]
  • Primary surgery - Total hysterectomy with bilateral salpingo-oophorectomy is the standard for early-stage disease.
  • Adjuvant radiotherapy - For higher-risk patients or to reduce local recurrence.
  • Hormone therapy - Progesterone therapy for inoperable cases or patients with specific indications.
  • Systemic chemotherapy - Reserved for advanced or recurrent cases.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Endometrial Cancer"][/sc]
  • Metastasis - Advanced disease may spread to lymph nodes, lungs, liver, and bones.
  • Recurrence - Recurrence rates vary by stage; close follow-up is required.
  • Secondary infections - Especially in patients undergoing radiotherapy or with advanced disease, prone to infections.
  • Psychological impact - Diagnosis and treatment impact mental health; requires supportive care.
