Breast Abscess
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Breast Abscess"][/sc]
  • A breast abscess is a localised collection of pus within the breast tissue, typically resulting from bacterial infection. It usually develops following mastitis, often affecting lactating women (puerperal abscess) but may also occur in non-lactating women (non-puerperal abscess).
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Breast Abscess"][/sc]
  • UK GP population: Breast abscesses are relatively rare but are seen more frequently in lactating women. Incidence estimates range from 0.1-0.5% among lactating women.
  • UK hospital prevalence: Hospital presentations of breast abscesses are typically seen in secondary care for severe cases; incidence data are not robust, but it remains uncommon overall.
  • Worldwide: Similar to UK patterns, with higher prevalence in regions where breastfeeding is more common.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Breast Abscess"][/sc]
  • Breast abscesses often start with ductal obstruction, leading to milk stasis and subsequent bacterial infection, usually by Staphylococcus aureus. Infection triggers an inflammatory response, causing localised tissue necrosis and pus formation. In non-lactating women, abscesses can arise from periductal mastitis, often linked to smoking or nipple piercings.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Breast Abscess"][/sc]
  • Initial phase: Begins with ductal blockage and mastitis, progressing to abscess formation over several days if untreated.
  • Acute phase: Rapid development of localised pain, swelling, and erythema within days to a week.
  • Chronic phase: If untreated, may persist, with possible fibrosis and scarring. Some abscesses may resolve with antibiotic therapy alone, while others may require drainage.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Breast Abscess"][/sc]
  • Localised breast pain - Caused by inflammation and pressure from pus accumulation within the tissue.
  • Swelling - Results from localised oedema and pus collection due to the immune response and infection.
  • Warmth - Increased blood flow to the infected area leads to localised warmth.
  • Fever and malaise - Systemic symptoms result from the bodyâ€™s inflammatory response to infection.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Breast Abscess"][/sc]
  • Erythema - Redness over the affected area due to increased blood flow and inflammation.
  • Fluctuant mass - A soft, compressible mass indicates the presence of pus.
  • Fever - A systemic response to infection, indicating the presence of an active infection.
  • Axillary lymphadenopathy - Lymph node enlargement occurs as part of the immune response to infection.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Breast Abscess"][/sc]
  • Skin necrosis - Suggests severe infection with potential for tissue damage.
  • Rapidly expanding erythema - May indicate cellulitis or severe abscess formation requiring urgent intervention.
  • Persistent fever post-treatment - Suggests inadequate treatment or underlying resistant organisms.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Breast Abscess"][/sc]
  • Non-lactational breast abscess in smokers - Presents with chronic symptoms and may mimic periductal mastitis.
  • Painless abscess - Occasionally, the abscess may not cause significant pain, particularly in immunocompromised individuals.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Breast Abscess"][/sc]
  • Ultrasound - Identifies fluid collection and guides drainage; shows a hypoechoic area with irregular margins.
  • Aspiration with culture - Confirms diagnosis by identifying the organism; Staphylococcus aureus is most common.
  • FBC - Elevated WBCs indicate infection.
  • CRP - Often elevated, showing an inflammatory response.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Breast Abscess"][/sc]
  • A 30-year-old lactating woman with a 3-day history of localised, painful swelling in the breast, fever, and erythema. Examination reveals a fluctuant, erythematous mass, with a diagnosis confirmed by ultrasound-guided aspiration.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Breast Abscess"][/sc]
  • Mastitis - Often presents similarly but lacks fluctuant mass; responds to antibiotics alone without drainage.
  • Galactocele - Milk-filled cyst without signs of infection; typically not erythematous or febrile.
  • Inflammatory breast cancer - Chronic erythema and oedema without fever; peau d'orange appearance on skin.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Breast Abscess"][/sc]
  • Antibiotics - Empirical antibiotics, usually flucloxacillin or co-amoxiclav, to cover common pathogens such as Staphylococcus aureus.
  • Drainage - Ultrasound-guided aspiration or incision and drainage if necessary for larger abscesses.
  • Analgesia and supportive care - NSAIDs for pain relief and support for continued breastfeeding if feasible.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Breast Abscess"][/sc]
  • Recurrence - Common if underlying ductal issues are unresolved.
  • Sepsis - Rare but serious; spread of infection into the bloodstream may occur.
  • Scarring - Can lead to cosmetic deformity or fibrosis within the breast tissue.
