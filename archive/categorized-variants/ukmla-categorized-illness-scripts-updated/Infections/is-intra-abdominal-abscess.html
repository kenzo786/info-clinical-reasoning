Intra-abdominal Abscess
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Intra-abdominal Abscess"][/sc]
  • An intra-abdominal abscess is a localised collection of pus within the peritoneal cavity, typically caused by infection following gastrointestinal perforation, surgery, or trauma. It can occur in any compartment of the abdomen, including the subphrenic, pelvic, and paracolic spaces, as well as within solid organs.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Intra-abdominal Abscess"][/sc]
  • Prevalence in UK GP population: Intra-abdominal abscesses are relatively rare in the GP setting due to their acute presentation, estimated at less than 0.1%.
  • UK hospital prevalence: Post-surgical intra-abdominal abscesses occur in 2-3% of abdominal surgeries.
  • Worldwide prevalence: Incidence varies but is more common in regions with limited access to surgical care, post-trauma, or infectious outbreaks (e.g., typhoid).
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Intra-abdominal Abscess"][/sc]
  • An intra-abdominal abscess forms when bacteria, introduced into the sterile peritoneal cavity through perforation or surgery, trigger an inflammatory response. Neutrophils and macrophages sequester the bacteria, leading to localised pus formation. Fibrin deposition walls off the abscess, limiting spread but causing pain and systemic symptoms.
  • Common pathogens include gut flora, such as *Escherichia coli*, *Bacteroides fragilis*, and *Enterococcus* species.
  • Abscess formation is a late complication, typically following a delay in resolving the primary cause of infection (e.g., bowel perforation, post-operative leak).
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Intra-abdominal Abscess"][/sc]
  • Early phase: Within hours to days post-perforation or trauma, localised peritonitis develops.
  • Abscess formation: Over days to weeks, the abscess matures as the body attempts to wall off infection.
  • Chronic phase: If untreated, the abscess can persist for weeks to months, leading to further complications like fistula formation or sepsis.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Intra-abdominal Abscess"][/sc]
  • Fever - Commonly presents due to systemic inflammation and cytokine release (IL-1, TNF) in response to infection.
  • Abdominal pain - Results from localised inflammation and stretching of the peritoneum; typically localised over the abscess site.
  • Malaise and fatigue - General systemic response to infection, possibly due to cytokine-mediated effects on energy metabolism.
  • Loss of appetite - Common in severe infections due to systemic inflammatory response syndrome (SIRS).
  • Nausea and vomiting - Often due to irritation of the gastrointestinal tract or the involvement of nearby structures.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Intra-abdominal Abscess"][/sc]
  • Tachycardia - Due to systemic inflammation and fever, leading to increased metabolic demand.
  • Abdominal tenderness - Localised over the abscess site, indicative of peritoneal irritation.
  • Rebound tenderness - Suggests peritonitis, typically seen when the abscess irritates the surrounding peritoneal structures.
  • Pallor - Associated with systemic illness or chronic infection.
  • Guarding or rigidity - Suggestive of peritoneal irritation and involuntary contraction of abdominal muscles.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Intra-abdominal Abscess"][/sc]
  • Severe sepsis or septic shock - Indicated by hypotension, altered mental status, and multi-organ failure, suggesting systemic infection has overwhelmed local defences.
  • Worsening abdominal pain with signs of peritonitis - Suggests perforation or rupture of the abscess.
  • Jaundice - Can indicate liver involvement or biliary abscess, leading to systemic complications.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Intra-abdominal Abscess"][/sc]
  • Absence of fever in immunocompromised patients - Due to impaired immune response, these patients may not mount a febrile response despite severe infection.
  • Minimal abdominal pain in elderly patients - Altered pain perception in elderly individuals can mask classic symptoms of abdominal abscess.
  • Vague systemic symptoms in diabetics - Diabetic patients may present with only non-specific signs, such as fatigue or confusion, due to altered immune and pain responses.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Intra-abdominal Abscess"][/sc]
  • Full Blood Count (FBC) - Leukocytosis is commonly seen due to the body's immune response to infection.
  • C-reactive protein (CRP) and erythrocyte sedimentation rate (ESR) - Elevated levels indicate systemic inflammation.
  • Blood cultures - May identify the causative organism, especially if the abscess has ruptured or led to bacteraemia.
  • Ultrasound - Useful initial imaging modality to localise the abscess, particularly in superficial areas or the pelvis.
  • CT Abdomen/Pelvis with contrast - Gold standard for detecting intra-abdominal abscesses, providing detailed localisation and information on surrounding structures.
  • Drainage cultures - Culturing abscess fluid post-drainage helps guide targeted antibiotic therapy.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Intra-abdominal Abscess"][/sc]
  • A 45-year-old male presents with a 1-week history of fever, general malaise, and localised lower abdominal pain following an appendectomy 10 days ago. He has developed new-onset tachycardia and tenderness on palpation in the right iliac fossa.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Intra-abdominal Abscess"][/sc]
  • Diverticulitis - Typically presents with left lower quadrant pain, fever, and altered bowel habits; CT imaging distinguishes between diverticular abscess and intra-abdominal abscess.
  • Pancreatic abscess - Presents with epigastric pain radiating to the back, usually following pancreatitis; CT will localise the abscess to the pancreas.
  • Psoas abscess - Patients often present with back pain and difficulty extending the hip, differing from the typical abdominal pain of intra-abdominal abscess.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Intra-abdominal Abscess"][/sc]
  • Antibiotics - Empirical broad-spectrum antibiotics (e.g., piperacillin-tazobactam) should be started, then tailored based on culture results.
  • Percutaneous drainage - CT-guided drainage is often first-line for accessible abscesses.
  • Surgical intervention - Laparotomy or laparoscopy may be necessary for complex, multiloculated, or inaccessible abscesses.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Intra-abdominal Abscess"][/sc]
  • Sepsis and septic shock - Resulting from systemic spread of infection; occurs in untreated or ruptured abscesses.
  • Fistula formation - Chronic abscesses may erode into surrounding structures, forming fistulae, which can complicate management.
  • Recurrence - Abscesses may recur, particularly if the underlying cause (e.g., bowel perforation) is not adequately addressed.
