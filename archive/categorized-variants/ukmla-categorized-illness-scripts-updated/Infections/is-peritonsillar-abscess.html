Peritonsillar Abscess illness script
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Peritonsillar Abscess"][/sc]
  • Peritonsillar abscess (PTA), also known as quinsy, is a collection of pus located between the palatine tonsil and the surrounding pharyngeal muscles. It typically develops as a complication of untreated or inadequately treated acute tonsillitis and is characterised by severe unilateral throat pain, difficulty swallowing (dysphagia), and trismus (difficulty opening the mouth).
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Peritonsillar Abscess"][/sc]
  • UK GP population prevalence: Peritonsillar abscess is relatively rare in general practice, with an incidence of around 30 cases per 100,000 annually.
  • UK hospital prevalence: PTA is a common reason for ENT admissions in hospitals, especially in patients aged 15-40 years.
  • Worldwide prevalence: The incidence globally mirrors that of the UK, with variations based on healthcare access and treatment of tonsillitis. PTA is seen more commonly in countries with lower access to antibiotics.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Peritonsillar Abscess"][/sc]
  • Peritonsillar abscess typically arises as a complication of acute bacterial tonsillitis. The infection spreads from the tonsils to the peritonsillar space, leading to localised inflammation and formation of pus. Bacterial pathogens, most commonly Streptococcus pyogenes, invade the tonsillar crypts, which leads to blockage of the drainage pathways and subsequent abscess formation.
  • As the infection progresses, the abscess forms between the tonsil and the superior constrictor muscles of the pharynx. The abscess causes local tissue oedema, inflammation, and pus accumulation, resulting in significant pain and difficulty in swallowing.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Peritonsillar Abscess"][/sc]
  • Acute phase (1-2 days): The patient initially presents with sore throat and tonsillitis, which fails to improve despite antibiotic treatment.
  • Abscess formation (3-5 days): The infection localises into an abscess with worsening symptoms including fever, unilateral throat pain, and difficulty opening the mouth.
  • Resolution (1-2 weeks): With appropriate treatment (drainage and antibiotics), symptoms begin to resolve, and full recovery is typically achieved within two weeks.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Peritonsillar Abscess"][/sc]
  • Unilateral throat pain - Usually severe and localised to the affected side. This is due to pressure from the abscess on the surrounding tissues and the local inflammatory response.
  • Dysphagia (difficulty swallowing) - Caused by inflammation and swelling in the peritonsillar region, making it painful to swallow.
  • Trismus (difficulty opening the mouth) - The abscess causes irritation of the nearby pterygoid muscles, resulting in spasm and difficulty opening the jaw.
  • Fever - A systemic response to the infection and abscess formation.
  • Voice changes ("hot potato voice") - The swelling in the throat affects phonation, giving the patient a muffled voice.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Peritonsillar Abscess"][/sc]
  • Unilateral tonsillar swelling with deviation of the uvula - As the abscess grows, it displaces the tonsil and pushes the uvula towards the unaffected side.
  • Peritonsillar erythema and oedema - Localised redness and swelling are seen in the peritonsillar space due to the infection.
  • Trismus - Demonstrated by the patientâ€™s inability to open the mouth fully, caused by inflammation of the pterygoid muscles.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Peritonsillar Abscess"][/sc]
  • Airway obstruction - A significant complication where the swelling from the abscess threatens the patientâ€™s airway, which can lead to stridor and respiratory distress.
  • Neck swelling and pain - May indicate spread of the infection into deeper neck spaces, potentially leading to life-threatening complications like mediastinitis.
  • Sepsis - The spread of infection into the bloodstream, presenting with hypotension, tachycardia, and confusion, signifies a medical emergency.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Peritonsillar Abscess"][/sc]
  • Bilateral peritonsillar abscess - Rare but may occur, leading to more severe airway symptoms without the classic unilateral findings.
  • Minimal pain with significant swelling - Some patients, particularly those with altered immune responses (e.g., diabetes, immunosuppression), may present with less severe pain despite the presence of a large abscess.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Peritonsillar Abscess"][/sc]
  • Clinical diagnosis - Based on history and physical examination, including tonsillar swelling, uvular deviation, and trismus.
  • Needle aspiration - Diagnostic and therapeutic. The aspiration of pus confirms the presence of an abscess and helps relieve symptoms.
  • CT scan (neck) - Used if there is uncertainty about the diagnosis or concern for deep neck space infection. Findings include a ring-enhancing fluid collection.
  • Blood tests - Full blood count (FBC) may show leukocytosis, and C-reactive protein (CRP) is typically elevated, indicating an inflammatory process.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Peritonsillar Abscess"][/sc]
  • A 25-year-old male presents with a 3-day history of worsening unilateral sore throat, difficulty swallowing, fever, and difficulty opening his mouth. On examination, there is right-sided tonsillar swelling with uvular deviation to the left, and trismus is noted.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Peritonsillar Abscess"][/sc]
  • Acute tonsillitis - No abscess formation, bilateral tonsillar involvement, and no significant trismus or uvular deviation.
  • Epiglottitis - Rapid onset of sore throat and dysphagia, with no tonsillar involvement but a risk of airway obstruction; confirmed by lateral neck X-ray or fibre-optic laryngoscopy.
  • Ludwig's angina - Infection of the submandibular space, leading to bilateral neck swelling, difficulty swallowing, and airway compromise, with no specific tonsillar involvement.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Peritonsillar Abscess"][/sc]
  • Needle aspiration or incision and drainage - First-line treatment to evacuate the abscess and relieve pressure.
  • Antibiotics - Empirical antibiotic therapy, typically with penicillin or clindamycin in penicillin-allergic patients, is recommended. Broad-spectrum antibiotics may be required if there is concern for deep neck space infection.
  • Supportive care - Includes analgesia (e.g., paracetamol, NSAIDs), hydration, and corticosteroids in selected cases to reduce swelling.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Peritonsillar Abscess"][/sc]
  • Airway obstruction - Due to severe swelling or spread of infection to surrounding tissues, potentially life-threatening.
  • Deep neck space infection - Extension of infection into the parapharyngeal or retropharyngeal space, requiring surgical intervention.
  • Sepsis - The spread of infection into the bloodstream can lead to septic shock, a medical emergency requiring intensive care.
  • Recurrence - PTA can recur, particularly if underlying chronic tonsillitis is not treated (e.g., tonsillectomy may be indicated).
