Tuberculosis (TB)
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Tuberculosis (TB)"][/sc]
  • Tuberculosis (TB) is an infectious disease primarily affecting the lungs (pulmonary TB) but can involve other organs (extrapulmonary TB). It is caused by the bacterium *Mycobacterium tuberculosis*, and its hallmark is the formation of granulomas in infected tissues. TB can be latent or active, with the active form being symptomatic and contagious.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Tuberculosis (TB)"][/sc]
  • Prevalence in the UK GP population: 7.9 cases per 100,000 (declining in recent years).
  • Prevalence in UK hospitals: 12.3 cases per 100,000, often seen in immigrant populations and those with immunocompromised states.
  • Global prevalence: 10 million people affected annually, with the highest rates in Africa and Southeast Asia.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Tuberculosis (TB)"][/sc]
  • Inhalation of *Mycobacterium tuberculosis* leads to alveolar infection.
  • The bacilli are engulfed by alveolar macrophages, where they resist destruction and multiply intracellularly.
  • An immune response forms granulomas to contain the infection. Latent TB occurs when the immune system suppresses the bacteria but doesnâ€™t eradicate it.
  • In active TB, the granulomas break down, releasing bacilli, leading to tissue destruction and symptomatic disease, especially in the lungs.
  • Dissemination via blood or lymphatics can lead to extrapulmonary TB.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Tuberculosis (TB)"][/sc]
  • Initial infection: Often asymptomatic or mild flu-like symptoms. Most individuals enter the latent phase, which may last years or a lifetime.
  • Reactivation TB: Can occur months to years later, particularly in immunosuppressed individuals.
  • Active TB: Develops progressively, with symptoms worsening over weeks to months.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Tuberculosis (TB)"][/sc]
  • Cough (chronic) - Persistent for >3 weeks, usually dry initially, becoming productive. Caused by lung parenchyma involvement and granuloma formation.
  • Weight loss - Due to chronic inflammation and systemic effects of the infection, leading to catabolism.
  • Fever - Intermittent or low-grade fever from the immune response to the infection.
  • Night sweats - Common in TB, likely due to cytokine release and fever regulation disturbance.
  • Fatigue - General malaise caused by the chronic inflammatory state.
  • Haemoptysis - Coughing up blood due to erosion of blood vessels in the lung tissue.
  • Pleuritic chest pain - Due to pleural involvement, leading to inflammation of the pleura.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Tuberculosis (TB)"][/sc]
  • Cachexia (wasting) - Generalised wasting due to the prolonged inflammatory process and malnutrition.
  • Lymphadenopathy - Especially cervical (scrofula), caused by local immune response to TB bacilli in lymph nodes.
  • Crackles (lung auscultation) - Resulting from lung parenchymal involvement and cavitation.
  • Clubbing - Can be seen in chronic pulmonary TB due to chronic hypoxia.
  • Dullness to percussion - Indicative of pleural effusion or lung consolidation.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Tuberculosis (TB)"][/sc]
  • Haemoptysis - Can indicate advanced pulmonary disease with cavitation or erosion into blood vessels, signalling the risk of massive haemorrhage.
  • Pleuritic chest pain and dyspnoea - May indicate pleural TB or pneumothorax, both serious complications.
  • Altered mental state - Suggests CNS involvement (tuberculous meningitis), a life-threatening form.
  • Progressive weight loss - Indicates worsening disease or disseminated TB.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Tuberculosis (TB)"][/sc]
  • Extrapulmonary TB - Involves lymph nodes, bones (Pottâ€™s disease), kidneys, or CNS, often without pulmonary symptoms. Can be missed if clinicians focus solely on respiratory complaints.
  • Latent TB - Asymptomatic; detected through screening in high-risk populations.
  • Miliary TB - Disseminated form of TB that can present with nonspecific systemic symptoms (fever, weight loss) and may lack respiratory symptoms.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Tuberculosis (TB)"][/sc]
  • Sputum culture for Mycobacterium tuberculosis - Gold standard but takes weeks. Positive in active pulmonary TB due to bacterial growth.
  • Chest X-ray - Shows upper lobe consolidation, cavitation, or pleural effusion in pulmonary TB.
  • Interferon-gamma release assay (IGRA) - Blood test used to detect latent TB by measuring immune response.
  • Tuberculin skin test (Mantoux) - Measures delayed-type hypersensitivity; positive in latent or active TB.
  • CT scan - More sensitive for detecting small cavitations or lymphadenopathy.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Tuberculosis (TB)"][/sc]
  • A 35-year-old man, recent immigrant from South Asia, presents with a 3-month history of cough, weight loss, night sweats, and fatigue. Chest X-ray reveals upper lobe consolidation.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Tuberculosis (TB)"][/sc]
  • Lung cancer - Similar weight loss and haemoptysis but often associated with a smoking history; diagnosis via imaging and biopsy.
  • Pneumonia - Acute presentation with fever and productive cough, typically bacterial, resolved with antibiotics.
  • Chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD) - Productive cough and shortness of breath, history of smoking; no night sweats or weight loss.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Tuberculosis (TB)"][/sc]
  • Initial phase (2 months): Isoniazid, Rifampicin, Pyrazinamide, and Ethambutol.
  • Continuation phase (4 months): Isoniazid and Rifampicin.
  • Latent TB: 3 months of Isoniazid and Rifampicin, or 6 months of Isoniazid alone.
  • Directly observed therapy (DOT): Recommended for non-adherent patients to improve compliance.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Tuberculosis (TB)"][/sc]
  • Respiratory failure - Due to extensive lung destruction and fibrosis.
  • Haemoptysis - From erosion of bronchial arteries in cavitating lesions.
  • Pneumothorax - Due to rupture of a cavity into the pleural space.
  • Disseminated TB - Miliary TB with multi-organ involvement, especially in immunocompromised patients.
