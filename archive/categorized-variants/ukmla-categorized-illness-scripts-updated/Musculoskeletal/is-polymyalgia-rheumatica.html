Polymyalgia Rheumatica (PMR)
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Polymyalgia Rheumatica (PMR)"][/sc]
  • Polymyalgia rheumatica (PMR) is an inflammatory disorder characterised by pain and stiffness, primarily affecting the shoulder and pelvic girdles. It is strongly associated with older age and is often linked to giant cell arteritis (GCA). PMR is distinct from other musculoskeletal conditions due to its rapid response to low-dose corticosteroids.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Polymyalgia Rheumatica (PMR)"][/sc]
  • PMR primarily affects individuals over the age of 50, with peak incidence between 70-80 years. In the UK, the annual incidence is approximately 77 per 100,000 in the GP population, while hospital prevalence varies due to varying referral rates. Worldwide, incidence is higher in populations of Northern European descent and less common in non-Caucasian populations.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Polymyalgia Rheumatica (PMR)"][/sc]
  • PMR is believed to result from an immune-mediated inflammatory response, though the precise trigger remains unclear. It primarily affects the synovial tissues and bursae around large proximal joints. Elevated levels of pro-inflammatory cytokines such as interleukin-6 (IL-6) and tumour necrosis factor-alpha (TNF-Î±) contribute to systemic inflammation, leading to characteristic symptoms. PMR is closely associated with giant cell arteritis (GCA), with shared inflammatory pathways but differing clinical manifestations.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Polymyalgia Rheumatica (PMR)"][/sc]
  • PMR typically develops over weeks to months, with a subacute onset of pain and stiffness. Symptoms may fluctuate, but without treatment, the disease can last for years. Most patients experience symptom resolution within 2-3 years with appropriate corticosteroid therapy.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Polymyalgia Rheumatica (PMR)"][/sc]
  • Bilateral shoulder pain - Commonest symptom; caused by synovitis and bursitis around the shoulder joints.
  • Morning stiffness - Often lasting over 45 minutes, due to systemic inflammation affecting synovial membranes.
  • Pelvic girdle pain - Pain in the hip region due to synovitis in the large proximal joints.
  • Fatigue - Systemic inflammation leads to malaise and a general feeling of tiredness.
  • Weight loss - Chronic inflammation can cause metabolic changes and reduced appetite.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Polymyalgia Rheumatica (PMR)"][/sc]
  • Reduced range of motion in shoulders - Due to pain and synovitis affecting the joints.
  • Weakness - Often perceived as weakness due to pain, although muscle strength is usually preserved.
  • Tenderness over affected muscles - Tenderness is due to inflammation of periarticular structures rather than the muscles themselves.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Polymyalgia Rheumatica (PMR)"][/sc]
  • Headache and scalp tenderness - May indicate giant cell arteritis (GCA), a serious complication that requires urgent treatment to prevent vision loss.
  • Jaw claudication - A symptom of GCA, resulting from inflammation of the arteries supplying the jaw muscles.
  • Visual disturbances - Blurred or double vision may precede permanent vision loss in GCA.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Polymyalgia Rheumatica (PMR)"][/sc]
  • Some patients may present with isolated limb pain without morning stiffness, which can be misinterpreted as mechanical causes such as osteoarthritis. Mild cases of PMR may be mistaken for fibromyalgia, particularly when the inflammatory markers are normal.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Polymyalgia Rheumatica (PMR)"][/sc]
  • Raised erythrocyte sedimentation rate (ESR) - Typically >40 mm/hour, reflects systemic inflammation due to elevated fibrinogen levels.
  • Elevated C-reactive protein (CRP) - CRP is usually raised, often used to monitor disease activity and response to treatment.
  • Normal creatine kinase (CK) - Helps differentiate PMR from conditions like myositis, where CK is elevated.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Polymyalgia Rheumatica (PMR)"][/sc]
  • An older adult (aged 70-75) presents with several weeks of bilateral shoulder and pelvic girdle pain, morning stiffness lasting over an hour, and fatigue. There is a rapid response to low-dose corticosteroids.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Polymyalgia Rheumatica (PMR)"][/sc]
  • Rheumatoid arthritis (RA) - RA typically involves small joints of the hands and feet, whereas PMR affects proximal joints.
  • Fibromyalgia - Fibromyalgia lacks the inflammatory markers found in PMR and often presents with chronic widespread pain rather than stiffness.
  • Myositis - Elevation in creatine kinase (CK) helps differentiate myositis from PMR.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Polymyalgia Rheumatica (PMR)"][/sc]
  • Initial treatment with low-dose prednisolone (12.5â€“25 mg/day) is recommended. Most patients experience symptom improvement within 24-48 hours. Gradual tapering of corticosteroids over months to years is necessary to minimise side effects. Monitoring of inflammatory markers and symptoms is crucial during treatment. In resistant or relapsing cases, methotrexate may be used as a steroid-sparing agent.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Polymyalgia Rheumatica (PMR)"][/sc]
  • Giant cell arteritis (GCA) - Occurs in 10-15% of PMR patients and can lead to serious complications, including irreversible vision loss due to occlusive arteritis of the ophthalmic artery.
  • Corticosteroid side effects - Long-term steroid use can lead to osteoporosis, diabetes, hypertension, and increased risk of infections.
