Knee Fracture or Dislocation
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Knee Fracture or Dislocation"][/sc]
  • A knee fracture refers to a break in one of the bony structures that form the knee joint, most commonly the patella, distal femur, or proximal tibia.
  • A knee dislocation occurs when there is a complete disruption of the normal alignment between the femur and tibia, often involving ligament damage.
  • Both conditions are associated with trauma and can lead to significant functional impairment if not managed appropriately.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Knee Fracture or Dislocation"][/sc]
  • Severe Pain - Caused by disruption of bone integrity or ligamentous structures, leading to activation of nociceptors.
  • Swelling - Results from haemorrhage or synovial effusion due to injury to vascular or joint structures.
  • Inability to Bear Weight - Due to mechanical instability and pain inhibition reflex from injury to the knee joint.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Knee Fracture or Dislocation"][/sc]
  • Visible Deformity - Often seen in dislocations; reflects abnormal bone alignment or displacement.
  • Ecchymosis - Indicates soft tissue trauma and possible underlying vascular injury.
  • Restricted Range of Motion - Due to pain, swelling, and mechanical obstruction by bony fragments or disrupted ligaments.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Knee Fracture or Dislocation"][/sc]
  • Neurovascular Compromise - Indicates potential damage to the popliteal artery or peroneal nerve, which may lead to limb ischaemia or nerve palsy.
  • Open Fracture or Dislocation - Suggests high-energy trauma and an increased risk of infection and compartment syndrome.
  • Persistent Instability - May signify a major ligamentous or meniscal injury requiring urgent orthopaedic intervention.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Knee Fracture or Dislocation"][/sc]
  • Minimal Pain Despite Dislocation - Seen in chronic dislocations due to adaptive changes in soft tissues.
  • Absent Swelling - May occur in immunocompromised or elderly patients due to reduced inflammatory response.
  • Intermittent Instability - May occur in subtle ligamentous injuries without obvious dislocation.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Knee Fracture or Dislocation"][/sc]
  • X-ray - Reveals fractures or gross dislocations; typical findings include disrupted bony alignment or fracture lines.
  • MRI - Identifies ligamentous injuries or cartilage damage, often used when soft tissue injuries are suspected.
  • Doppler Ultrasound - Assesses vascular integrity, particularly in cases of suspected popliteal artery injury.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Knee Fracture or Dislocation"][/sc]
  • A 35-year-old male presents with acute severe knee pain, swelling, and inability to bear weight following a high-energy fall during sports. Examination reveals visible deformity and ecchymosis, with absent distal pulses.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Knee Fracture or Dislocation"][/sc]
  • Ligamentous Injury - Presents with instability but lacks visible bony deformity; MRI confirms diagnosis.
  • Patellar Dislocation - Typically associated with lateral displacement of the patella, often in young athletes.
  • Septic Arthritis - Features include fever and marked joint tenderness, with positive synovial fluid analysis for infection.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Knee Fracture or Dislocation"][/sc]
  • Immobilisation - Initial stabilisation with a splint or cast to prevent further injury.
  • Surgical Intervention - Required for displaced fractures, ligament repair, or vascular injury.
  • Rehabilitation - Physiotherapy to restore range of motion and strength post-treatment.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Knee Fracture or Dislocation"][/sc]
  • Post-Traumatic Arthritis - Caused by cartilage damage or altered joint mechanics.
  • Compartment Syndrome - Results from increased compartmental pressure due to swelling or haemorrhage.
  • Chronic Instability - Due to incomplete ligament repair or poor rehabilitation.
