Fibromyalgia
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Fibromyalgia"][/sc]
  • Fibromyalgia is a chronic disorder characterised by widespread musculoskeletal pain, often accompanied by fatigue, sleep disturbances, cognitive issues (referred to as "fibro fog"), and a heightened sensitivity to pressure. It is considered a central sensitisation syndrome, where altered pain processing in the central nervous system leads to amplification of pain signals.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Fibromyalgia"][/sc]
  • Prevalence in UK GP population: Approximately 2.2% of the adult population, with a higher prevalence in females (around 4.7%) compared to males (0.8%).
  • UK hospital prevalence: Fibromyalgia is less commonly seen in secondary care settings, as it is often managed in primary care. However, it accounts for around 5-10% of rheumatology referrals.
  • Worldwide prevalence: Estimated at 2-4%, with similar trends in terms of female predominance (female-to-male ratio of 3:1).
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Fibromyalgia"][/sc]
  • The primary mechanism underlying fibromyalgia is central sensitisation. Abnormalities in neurotransmitters, particularly an imbalance between excitatory (e.g., glutamate) and inhibitory (e.g., serotonin, noradrenaline) signals, lead to heightened pain perception. There is also evidence of abnormal hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal (HPA) axis function, with blunted cortisol responses to stress, contributing to fatigue and cognitive symptoms.
  • Peripheral pain signals from muscles and soft tissues may also be exaggerated, although no consistent structural abnormalities are identified in these tissues.
  • Genetic predisposition and environmental triggers (such as physical trauma, infection, or emotional stress) may contribute to the development and persistence of the condition.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Fibromyalgia"][/sc]
  • Fibromyalgia is a chronic condition, often developing insidiously over months to years. Symptoms tend to wax and wane but rarely resolve completely. Episodes of symptom exacerbation may be triggered by physical or psychological stressors.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Fibromyalgia"][/sc]
  • Widespread pain - Generalised musculoskeletal pain, typically described as aching or burning, often worse in areas of pressure. This is linked to central sensitisation and abnormal pain amplification in the central nervous system.
  • Fatigue - Persistent tiredness not relieved by rest. This may be due to disrupted sleep architecture (reduced restorative sleep) and abnormal HPA axis function.
  • Sleep disturbances - Non-refreshing sleep, frequent awakenings, and difficulty falling asleep, often associated with pain and altered neurotransmitter regulation.
  • Cognitive difficulties ("fibro fog") - Memory issues, difficulty concentrating, and slowed thought processes are common. These are thought to be related to neurotransmitter imbalances and chronic sleep deprivation.
  • Headaches - Often tension-type or migraine headaches, possibly related to increased pain sensitivity and abnormal muscle tension.
  • Depression and anxiety - Common comorbidities due to the chronic nature of the condition, persistent pain, and associated functional impairment.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Fibromyalgia"][/sc]
  • Allodynia - Pain from stimuli that are not typically painful, such as light pressure. This reflects the central sensitisation process, where non-painful stimuli are misinterpreted as painful by the nervous system.
  • Tender points - Discrete areas of tenderness, often located at specific anatomical sites (e.g., neck, shoulders, elbows, hips, and knees). These are indicative of increased pain sensitivity but are not associated with local inflammation or damage.
  • Absence of synovitis or objective joint swelling - Despite complaints of joint pain, fibromyalgia does not cause inflammatory joint changes, differentiating it from conditions like rheumatoid arthritis.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Fibromyalgia"][/sc]
  • Weight loss - May suggest malignancy or systemic disease rather than fibromyalgia.
  • Fever - Unexplained fevers are uncommon in fibromyalgia and may indicate infection or an autoimmune condition.
  • Focal neurological deficits - Fibromyalgia does not cause neurological deficits (e.g., weakness, numbness), which may point to multiple sclerosis, stroke, or other neurological conditions.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Fibromyalgia"][/sc]
  • Isolated cognitive dysfunction ("fibro fog") - In some cases, patients may primarily present with cognitive issues, leading to possible misdiagnosis as dementia or psychiatric conditions.
  • Localized pain - While fibromyalgia usually causes widespread pain, some individuals may present with pain in a limited number of body regions, potentially leading to confusion with regional pain syndromes.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Fibromyalgia"][/sc]
  • Fibromyalgia is a clinical diagnosis. No definitive laboratory or imaging test exists to confirm the diagnosis. Routine blood tests (e.g., FBC, ESR, CRP) are usually normal but may be used to rule out other conditions such as inflammatory arthritis or thyroid disease.
  • Tender point examination - Palpation of 18 specific points; pain at 11 or more supports the diagnosis. However, this criterion is no longer essential for diagnosis in many guidelines.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Fibromyalgia"][/sc]
  • A 45-year-old woman presents with a 2-year history of generalised musculoskeletal pain, fatigue, non-restorative sleep, and cognitive difficulties. The pain is widespread, and she reports tenderness over her shoulders and hips.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Fibromyalgia"][/sc]
  • Chronic fatigue syndrome (CFS) - Like fibromyalgia, CFS causes fatigue and cognitive issues, but pain is less prominent. Post-exertional malaise is a key feature of CFS.
  • Rheumatoid arthritis (RA) - RA causes joint pain and morning stiffness, but unlike fibromyalgia, it is associated with synovitis and raised inflammatory markers.
  • Hypothyroidism - Fatigue, cognitive dysfunction, and myalgia can mimic fibromyalgia, but hypothyroidism usually presents with weight gain, cold intolerance, and abnormal thyroid function tests.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Fibromyalgia"][/sc]
  • Education and self-management - Providing patients with information about the condition and encouraging physical activity and relaxation techniques.
  • Pharmacological treatments - Antidepressants (e.g., amitriptyline, duloxetine), and anticonvulsants (e.g., pregabalin) are used to manage pain and improve sleep.
  • Cognitive-behavioural therapy (CBT) - To help with coping strategies and reduce the impact of symptoms on daily functioning.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Fibromyalgia"][/sc]
  • Chronic disability - Persistent pain and fatigue can lead to significant impairment in daily functioning and employment.
  • Psychiatric comorbidities - Depression and anxiety are common due to the chronic nature of fibromyalgia and the associated social and functional limitations.
