Osteomyelitis
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Osteomyelitis"][/sc]
  • Osteomyelitis is an infection of the bone characterised by inflammation, typically caused by bacterial invasion, with Staphylococcus aureus as the most common pathogen. The infection can occur via haematogenous spread, contiguous infection from adjacent structures, or direct inoculation due to trauma or surgery. Osteomyelitis may be acute, subacute, or chronic depending on the duration and severity.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Osteomyelitis"][/sc]
  • Osteomyelitis incidence in the UK general practice population is approximately 0.1% annually, though rates are higher in hospitalised patients due to trauma and surgical exposure. Globally, osteomyelitis is a significant condition, with higher prevalence in low- and middle-income countries where healthcare resources for early intervention may be limited.
  • In children, haematogenous osteomyelitis is more common, particularly affecting long bones. In adults, chronic osteomyelitis is more common, often related to diabetes or peripheral vascular disease.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Osteomyelitis"][/sc]
  • Bacteria enter the bone via the bloodstream (haematogenous spread), direct inoculation, or contiguous spread from nearby infections. Once in the bone, they proliferate, causing an inflammatory response that leads to oedema, raised intraosseous pressure, and impaired blood supply.
  • This inflammatory response may result in necrosis of bone tissue and sequestrum formation. Chronic osteomyelitis can develop with repeated episodes of infection, often due to antibiotic-resistant bacteria or an inability to fully eradicate the infection.
  • The body may attempt to wall off the infection, creating an involucrum, or new bone formation around the sequestrum, leading to chronic infection and potential for sinus tract formation.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Osteomyelitis"][/sc]
  • Acute: Rapid onset within days to weeks, marked by severe symptoms.
  • Subacute: Onset over several weeks with less intense symptoms, typically in cases where antibiotic treatment is partially effective.
  • Chronic: Persisting for months or years, characterised by repeated flare-ups, often with low-grade symptoms, bone sclerosis, and sequestra.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Osteomyelitis"][/sc]
  • Bone pain - Often the primary symptom; due to pressure increase and inflammatory response.
  • Swelling and warmth over affected area - Secondary to localised inflammation and vascular response.
  • Fever and malaise - Common in acute cases; reflects systemic immune response to infection.
  • Reduced range of motion - Particularly in osteomyelitis near joints, due to pain and swelling.
  • Fatigue - In chronic cases; related to systemic infection and inflammatory burden.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Osteomyelitis"][/sc]
  • Local tenderness - Due to inflammation and oedema in the affected bone.
  • Swelling and erythema - Resulting from localised inflammatory response and increased vascularity.
  • Fever - Sign of systemic infection, more common in acute osteomyelitis.
  • Sinus tract formation (in chronic cases) - Persistent infection leads to skin perforation for drainage.
  • Decreased joint movement - Particularly with contiguous infections affecting nearby joints.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Osteomyelitis"][/sc]
  • High fever and chills - Indicates a systemic infection; risk of sepsis.
  • Progressive pain despite antibiotics - May suggest abscess or necrotic bone tissue (sequestra).
  • Neurological deficits - If affecting the spine, indicates risk of spinal cord compression.
  • Sinus tract with purulent discharge - A sign of chronic infection and osteonecrosis, requiring debridement.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Osteomyelitis"][/sc]
  • Absence of fever - Common in chronic or immunocompromised patients, where immune response is blunted.
  • Minimal localised symptoms - In cases with deep-seated infections or those in immunocompromised patients.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Osteomyelitis"][/sc]
  • Blood tests (CRP, ESR, WCC) - Elevated inflammatory markers are common due to the systemic response.
  • Blood cultures - To identify causative organism in haematogenous cases.
  • X-ray - May show bone destruction in chronic cases; often normal in early stages.
  • MRI - Sensitive for early bone oedema and abscess formation.
  • Bone biopsy - Gold standard for definitive diagnosis and culture in chronic or unresponsive cases.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Osteomyelitis"][/sc]
  • A 10-year-old boy presents with a 3-day history of fever, localised pain, swelling, and erythema over the left tibia, with a limited range of motion and raised inflammatory markers on blood tests.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Osteomyelitis"][/sc]
  • Septic arthritis - Joint-specific pain and swelling; rapid deterioration in joint function.
  • Cellulitis - Localised skin infection, no bone involvement; no changes on bone imaging.
  • Bone tumour - Persistent pain without fever; X-ray may reveal a mass rather than osteolysis.
  • Gout - Acute onset with joint pain; urate crystals on aspiration, typically involving a joint.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Osteomyelitis"][/sc]
  • Antibiotics - Empiric therapy with flucloxacillin or clindamycin if MRSA suspected, typically IV for 4-6 weeks.
  • Surgical debridement - Required in chronic or unresponsive cases to remove necrotic bone.
  • Supportive care - Includes pain management and, if needed, physiotherapy to restore function post-infection.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Osteomyelitis"][/sc]
  • Sepsis - Bacterial dissemination; high mortality if untreated.
  • Chronic osteomyelitis - Recurrent infections, typically requiring prolonged antibiotic therapy and surgery.
  • Pathological fractures - Weakened bone from chronic infection prone to fractures.
  • Amputation - Severe cases with extensive necrosis or non-response to treatment, especially in diabetics.
