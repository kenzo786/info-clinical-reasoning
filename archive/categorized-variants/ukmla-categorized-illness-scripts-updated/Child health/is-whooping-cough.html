Whooping Cough (Pertussis)
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Whooping Cough (Pertussis)"][/sc]
  • Whooping cough, also known as pertussis, is a highly contagious bacterial respiratory infection caused by Bordetella pertussis. It is characterised by severe paroxysmal coughing followed by a "whooping" sound during inspiration. The disease often occurs in phases and can lead to severe complications, especially in infants and unvaccinated individuals.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Whooping Cough (Pertussis)"][/sc]
  • UK General Practice: Pertussis has an annual incidence of approximately 5-10 per 100,000 population, with outbreaks occurring in cycles every 3-5 years.
  • UK Hospital Prevalence: Most hospitalised cases are in infants under six months of age.
  • Worldwide Prevalence: Approximately 24.1 million cases annually, with the highest burden in low-income countries where vaccination coverage is low.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Whooping Cough (Pertussis)"][/sc]
  • Bordetella pertussis attaches to the ciliated epithelial cells of the respiratory tract using adhesins such as filamentous haemagglutinin.
  • The bacterium releases toxins, including pertussis toxin, which impairs immune responses and causes local tissue damage.
  • Tracheal cytotoxin disrupts ciliary function, leading to accumulation of mucus and the characteristic cough.
  • Immune response generates significant inflammation, exacerbating airway obstruction.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Whooping Cough (Pertussis)"][/sc]
  • Catarrhal Phase (1-2 weeks): Non-specific symptoms including rhinorrhoea, mild cough, and low-grade fever.
  • Paroxysmal Phase (2-6 weeks): Intense bouts of coughing followed by inspiratory "whoop"; post-tussive vomiting common.
  • Convalescent Phase (weeks to months): Gradual reduction in cough frequency and severity.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Whooping Cough (Pertussis)"][/sc]
  • Paroxysmal cough: Sudden, intense coughing fits due to airway irritation and mucus accumulation.
  • Inspiratory whoop: High-pitched "whoop" sound caused by rapid air intake after coughing fits.
  • Post-tussive vomiting: Vomiting following coughing episodes due to gag reflex stimulation.
  • Rhinorrhoea: Nasal discharge in the early catarrhal phase due to mucosal irritation.
  • Fatigue: Resulting from prolonged coughing episodes and sleep disruption.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Whooping Cough (Pertussis)"][/sc]
  • Inspiratory whoop: Pathognomonic sound due to airway narrowing and forceful air intake.
  • Cyanosis: Peripheral or central cyanosis due to hypoxia during prolonged coughing episodes.
  • Apnoea (infants): Cessation of breathing triggered by severe coughing or airway inflammation.
  • Subconjunctival haemorrhage: Small haemorrhages caused by increased intrathoracic pressure during coughing.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Whooping Cough (Pertussis)"][/sc]
  • Apnoea: Suggests impending respiratory failure, especially in neonates.
  • Severe cyanosis: Indicates significant hypoxaemia, requiring urgent intervention.
  • Seizures: Can indicate hypoxic encephalopathy or pertussis-related encephalitis.
  • Persistent vomiting: May lead to dehydration and electrolyte imbalances.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Whooping Cough (Pertussis)"][/sc]
  • Adults and adolescents: Prolonged dry cough without inspiratory whoop.
  • Vaccinated individuals: Milder symptoms or only the catarrhal phase due to partial immunity.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Whooping Cough (Pertussis)"][/sc]
  • Nasopharyngeal swab for PCR: Detects B. pertussis DNA, highly sensitive in early stages.
  • Culture: Confirms diagnosis but has lower sensitivity and takes longer.
  • Serology: Useful in late disease, detecting IgG antibodies against pertussis toxin.
  • Full blood count: Leukocytosis with lymphocytosis common.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Whooping Cough (Pertussis)"][/sc]
  • A 6-month-old infant presenting with a 2-week history of paroxysmal cough, post-tussive vomiting, and cyanosis during coughing fits. No recent vaccinations.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Whooping Cough (Pertussis)"][/sc]
  • Viral bronchiolitis: Differs by wheezing, no whoop, and RSV aetiology.
  • Croup: Barking cough and inspiratory stridor instead of paroxysmal cough.
  • Atypical pneumonia: Persistent cough without inspiratory whoop.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Whooping Cough (Pertussis)"][/sc]
  • Antibiotics: Macrolides (e.g., azithromycin) are first-line treatment to reduce transmission.
  • Supportive care: Includes hydration, oxygen for hypoxia, and monitoring for complications.
  • Prevention: Routine vaccination (DTaP) and booster doses as per UK immunisation schedule.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Whooping Cough (Pertussis)"][/sc]
  • Pneumonia: Secondary bacterial infection, a leading cause of pertussis-related death.
  • Hypoxaemia: Due to severe coughing and airway obstruction.
  • Seizures: Resulting from hypoxia or pertussis toxin effects on the CNS.
  • Death: Most common in infants under six months of age.
