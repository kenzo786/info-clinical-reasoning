Urinary Tract Infection (UTI)
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Urinary Tract Infection (UTI)"][/sc]
  • A urinary tract infection (UTI) is an infection that affects part of the urinary system, which includes the kidneys, ureters, bladder, and urethra. Most commonly, UTIs involve the lower urinary tract (bladder and urethra). It is primarily caused by bacteria such as *Escherichia coli* (E. coli), but other pathogens can also be involved.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Urinary Tract Infection (UTI)"][/sc]
  • UK GP population: UTIs account for around 1-3% of GP consultations. Women are disproportionately affected, with 50-60% experiencing a UTI in their lifetime.
  • UK hospital prevalence: UTIs are a common cause of hospital admissions, particularly among elderly patients and those with comorbidities.
  • Worldwide prevalence: UTIs are a global health concern, with an annual incidence of approximately 150 million cases worldwide.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Urinary Tract Infection (UTI)"][/sc]
  • UTIs typically occur when pathogens, usually bacteria from the gastrointestinal tract (like *E. coli*), ascend the urethra into the bladder. In some cases, they may ascend further to the kidneys, causing pyelonephritis. Disruption of normal urinary flow, poor immune response, or mechanical issues (such as catheter use) can predispose individuals to infection.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Urinary Tract Infection (UTI)"][/sc]
  • Acute phase: Symptoms of UTI, such as dysuria, frequency, and urgency, usually develop within a few days.
  • Resolution: With appropriate treatment, symptoms typically resolve within 48-72 hours.
  • Chronic or recurrent phase: Some patients, particularly those with underlying conditions, may experience recurrent UTIs over months or years.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Urinary Tract Infection (UTI)"][/sc]
  • Dysuria (painful urination) - Caused by irritation of the urethral and bladder lining due to inflammation.
  • Increased urinary frequency - Inflammation of the bladder leads to heightened sensation of needing to void.
  • Urgency - A sudden, intense need to urinate due to bladder irritation.
  • Suprapubic pain - Occurs due to bladder inflammation and distension.
  • Haematuria - Blood in the urine, often due to irritation of the bladder lining.
  • Asymptomatic bacteriuria - Some patients, particularly the elderly and pregnant women, may present without any symptoms despite bacterial colonisation in the urinary tract.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Urinary Tract Infection (UTI)"][/sc]
  • Suprapubic tenderness - Bladder distension and inflammation can cause tenderness in the lower abdomen.
  • Fever - While uncommon in uncomplicated UTIs, it can occur, particularly in cases of pyelonephritis (upper urinary tract infection).
  • Costovertebral angle tenderness - Indicates pyelonephritis, due to inflammation and infection of the kidney.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Urinary Tract Infection (UTI)"][/sc]
  • Fever, rigors - May indicate upper urinary tract involvement (pyelonephritis) or sepsis.
  • Flank pain - Suggests pyelonephritis or renal involvement.
  • Hypotension, tachycardia - Signs of urosepsis, a life-threatening complication where infection spreads to the bloodstream.
  • Confusion or altered mental status - Common in elderly patients, indicating systemic involvement, often associated with urosepsis.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Urinary Tract Infection (UTI)"][/sc]
  • Elderly patients: May present with confusion, lethargy, or falls instead of classic urinary symptoms.
  • Pregnancy: Often asymptomatic, but if symptomatic, the presentation may be more severe due to anatomical and physiological changes.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Urinary Tract Infection (UTI)"][/sc]
  • Urinalysis: Presence of nitrites, leucocytes, and blood suggests infection. Nitrites indicate bacterial conversion of nitrates, typically by *E. coli*.
  • Urine culture: Confirms the causative organism, typically showing growth of *E. coli*. A colony count >10^5 CFU/mL is diagnostic of UTI.
  • Full blood count (FBC): May show raised white cell count in cases of systemic infection or pyelonephritis.
  • Blood cultures: Should be taken if sepsis is suspected to identify bacteraemia.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Urinary Tract Infection (UTI)"][/sc]
  • A 32-year-old woman presents with 2 days of dysuria, urinary frequency, and suprapubic pain. No fever or flank pain reported. No significant medical history.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Urinary Tract Infection (UTI)"][/sc]
  • Vaginitis: Often presents with vaginal discharge and irritation, but without urinary frequency or urgency.
  • Urethritis: Similar symptoms but commonly associated with sexually transmitted infections. Discharge may be present.
  • Interstitial cystitis: Presents with chronic pelvic pain and urinary frequency but lacks infectious findings in urine culture.
  • Pelvic inflammatory disease (PID): Characterised by pelvic pain, fever, and vaginal discharge, often following a sexually transmitted infection.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Urinary Tract Infection (UTI)"][/sc]
  • First-line antibiotics: Nitrofurantoin (100 mg BD for 3-5 days) or Trimethoprim (200 mg BD for 3 days) in non-pregnant women.
  • Pregnancy: Avoid Trimethoprim in the first trimester. Nitrofurantoin is usually preferred.
  • Hydration: Encourage fluid intake to help flush out bacteria.
  • Recurrent UTIs: Prophylactic antibiotics or lifestyle modifications (e.g., increased hydration, urination after intercourse).
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Urinary Tract Infection (UTI)"][/sc]
  • Pyelonephritis: Infection ascends to the kidneys, causing fever, flank pain, and systemic symptoms.
  • Urosepsis: Sepsis originating from the urinary tract can lead to multi-organ failure and is life-threatening.
  • Renal abscess: Rare but serious complication involving localised infection within the kidney tissue.
