Pancreatic Cancer
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Pancreatic Cancer"][/sc]
  • Pancreatic cancer is a malignant tumour originating from the exocrine or endocrine components of the pancreas, most commonly from the ductal cells (pancreatic adenocarcinoma). It is characterised by its aggressive nature, early local invasion, and a high potential for metastasis. The disease often presents late in its course, contributing to its high mortality rate.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Pancreatic Cancer"][/sc]
  • In the UK, pancreatic cancer accounts for around 3% of all cancers, with approximately 10,500 new cases diagnosed annually.
  • Globally, pancreatic cancer ranks as the 12th most common cancer but is the 7th leading cause of cancer death due to its poor prognosis.
  • The incidence is higher in individuals over 65, with a slight male predominance.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Pancreatic Cancer"][/sc]
  • The majority of pancreatic cancers arise from the exocrine cells, specifically the ductal epithelium. Mutations in key genes such as KRAS, p53, and SMAD4 lead to uncontrolled cell growth.
  • As the tumour grows, it invades surrounding tissues, including the bile ducts, causing obstructive jaundice. It can also invade nerves, causing pain, and the blood vessels, leading to vascular complications.
  • Late-stage disease commonly metastasises to the liver, peritoneum, and lungs.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Pancreatic Cancer"][/sc]
  • Pancreatic cancer is insidious, with patients often asymptomatic in the early stages. By the time symptoms develop, the disease is usually advanced.
  • In the early phase, subtle symptoms such as fatigue or anorexia may appear, followed by more specific signs such as jaundice, weight loss, and abdominal pain as the tumour progresses.
  • The median survival after diagnosis is typically less than 6 months for advanced cases, with a 5-year survival rate of about 5-10%.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Pancreatic Cancer"][/sc]
  • Weight loss - Due to cancer-related metabolic demands, malabsorption (from pancreatic exocrine insufficiency), and anorexia.
  • Abdominal pain - Often epigastric and radiating to the back, caused by tumour invasion of the celiac plexus or retroperitoneal structures.
  • Jaundice - Caused by obstruction of the bile duct, leading to accumulation of bilirubin. Common in tumours located in the pancreatic head.
  • Fatigue - A non-specific but common symptom related to the systemic effects of cancer, such as increased cytokine production and nutritional deficiencies.
  • Nausea and vomiting - Often related to gastrointestinal obstruction from tumour growth or bile stasis.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Pancreatic Cancer"][/sc]
  • Painless jaundice - Classically associated with tumours of the pancreatic head, as bile duct obstruction occurs without inflammation.
  • Palpable gallbladder (Courvoisierâ€™s sign) - A non-tender, enlarged gallbladder, suggesting obstructive jaundice from a malignant cause rather than gallstones.
  • Ascites - Indicative of peritoneal metastases or portal vein involvement, leading to increased intra-abdominal pressure and fluid accumulation.
  • Cachexia - Generalised wasting seen in advanced cancer due to systemic effects of the tumour and chronic malabsorption.
  • Thrombophlebitis (Trousseau's sign) - A migratory thrombophlebitis reflecting hypercoagulability often associated with pancreatic cancer.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Pancreatic Cancer"][/sc]
  • Painless jaundice - Suggests obstructive jaundice, likely from a tumour at the head of the pancreas. Indicates significant bile duct involvement.
  • Significant weight loss - Rapid and unexplained weight loss is highly suspicious for advanced pancreatic cancer due to malnutrition and tumour burden.
  • New onset diabetes in an older adult - Pancreatic cancer can cause insulin insufficiency and is often associated with diabetes diagnosis in older individuals.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Pancreatic Cancer"][/sc]
  • Acute pancreatitis - Rarely, pancreatic cancer may present as acute pancreatitis, especially in tumours obstructing the pancreatic duct.
  • Diabetes mellitus - New-onset diabetes in an older adult without obvious risk factors can be an atypical sign of underlying pancreatic malignancy.
  • Back pain without abdominal symptoms - Tumour invasion into the retroperitoneum may cause isolated back pain without typical abdominal findings.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Pancreatic Cancer"][/sc]
  • Abdominal ultrasound - Often the first imaging test performed, particularly in jaundiced patients. May reveal a dilated bile duct or a pancreatic mass.
  • CT abdomen with contrast - The most sensitive imaging for detecting pancreatic tumours and assessing their resectability. Tumour appears as a hypodense mass.
  • CA 19-9 - A tumour marker that is elevated in 70-80% of patients with pancreatic cancer, though non-specific. Levels may correlate with tumour burden.
  • Endoscopic ultrasound (EUS) - Allows for fine-needle aspiration (FNA) of the tumour for histological confirmation.
  • MRCP/ERCP - Can provide detailed imaging of the biliary and pancreatic ducts, with the potential for stenting if bile duct obstruction is confirmed.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Pancreatic Cancer"][/sc]
  • A 65-year-old male presents with a 3-month history of progressive weight loss, jaundice, and intermittent epigastric pain radiating to the back. He has recently developed pale stools and dark urine. Imaging reveals a mass in the head of the pancreas.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Pancreatic Cancer"][/sc]
  • Cholangiocarcinoma - Differentiated by lack of pancreatic mass and more central bile duct involvement on imaging.
  • Chronic pancreatitis - Often presents with a history of recurrent pancreatitis, calcifications on imaging, and less severe weight loss.
  • Gallstone disease - Associated with episodic, colicky pain and evidence of gallstones on imaging, without a pancreatic mass.
  • Hepatocellular carcinoma - May present with jaundice, but liver imaging will reveal a primary liver tumour.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Pancreatic Cancer"][/sc]
  • Surgical resection (Whipple procedure) - The only potential curative treatment, but only feasible in 15-20% of patients with localised, resectable disease.
  • Adjuvant chemotherapy (e.g., gemcitabine, FOLFIRINOX) - Recommended for patients after surgery to improve survival outcomes.
  • Palliative care - For advanced or metastatic disease, focusing on symptom management, including pain relief, bile duct stenting for jaundice, and nutritional support.
  • Radiotherapy - May be used in combination with chemotherapy for local control in non-resectable cases.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Pancreatic Cancer"][/sc]
  • Liver metastasis - The most common site of distant spread, causing liver dysfunction and worsening jaundice.
  • Obstructive jaundice - May lead to cholangitis or biliary cirrhosis if left untreated.
  • Peritoneal carcinomatosis - Spread to the peritoneum can cause ascites, bowel obstruction, and significant abdominal pain.
  • Diabetes mellitus - Tumour destruction of pancreatic tissue can lead to insulin insufficiency and secondary diabetes.
