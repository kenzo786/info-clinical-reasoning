Placenta Praevia
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Placenta Praevia"][/sc]
  • Placenta praevia is a condition in pregnancy where the placenta partially or completely covers the internal cervical os, leading to a risk of painless vaginal bleeding, especially in the second and third trimesters.
  • It is classified based on placental location in relation to the cervix: complete, partial, marginal, or low-lying placenta.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Placenta Praevia"][/sc]
  • UK general population prevalence: approximately 5 per 1,000 pregnancies.
  • UK hospital prevalence: around 0.5% of all pregnancies reaching the third trimester.
  • Global prevalence: ranges from 3 to 10 per 1,000 pregnancies, varying by region and risk factors.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Placenta Praevia"][/sc]
  • Occurs due to abnormal implantation of the placenta over the cervical os, often associated with previous uterine surgery, multiparity, or advanced maternal age.
  • As the uterus expands, the lower uterine segment stretches, potentially disrupting the placental attachment and causing painless antepartum haemorrhage.
  • Placental invasion abnormalities (placenta accreta, increta, percreta) may coexist, increasing the risk of severe postpartum haemorrhage.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Placenta Praevia"][/sc]
  • First trimester: Often detected incidentally on routine ultrasound.
  • Second trimester: May persist but can resolve as the uterus expands.
  • Third trimester: Increased risk of painless vaginal bleeding due to placental separation from the expanding lower uterine segment.
  • Peripartum: Risk of massive haemorrhage during labour if undiagnosed or improperly managed.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Placenta Praevia"][/sc]
  • Painless vaginal bleeding - Classic presentation; occurs as the lower uterine segment expands, causing placental separation and bleeding.
  • Recurrent bleeding episodes - Typically in the second or third trimester, often increasing in severity.
  • Post-coital bleeding - Due to mechanical disruption of the placenta near the cervix.
  • Asymptomatic - In some cases, placenta praevia is only detected on routine ultrasound with no bleeding episodes.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Placenta Praevia"][/sc]
  • Non-tender uterus - Unlike placental abruption, the uterus remains soft and non-tender.
  • Malpresentation (e.g., breech, transverse lie) - Due to placental obstruction of the lower uterine segment.
  • Shock (in severe cases) - If significant haemorrhage occurs, signs of hypovolaemia may develop.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Placenta Praevia"][/sc]
  • Heavy, painless vaginal bleeding - Suggests severe placental separation and risk of haemorrhagic shock.
  • Signs of hypovolaemic shock (tachycardia, hypotension, pallor) - Indicates significant blood loss requiring urgent resuscitation.
  • Placenta accreta spectrum disorder - If associated with prior caesarean sections, there is a risk of life-threatening postpartum haemorrhage.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Placenta Praevia"][/sc]
  • Minimal spotting rather than heavy bleeding, leading to a missed diagnosis.
  • Misdiagnosis as cervical ectropion or vaginal infection in early pregnancy.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Placenta Praevia"][/sc]
  • Transvaginal ultrasound scan (TVUS) - Gold standard for diagnosis, showing placental position relative to the cervix.
  • Transabdominal ultrasound - Initial screening tool, but less accurate.
  • Full blood count (FBC) - To assess for anaemia and haemorrhagic impact.
  • Coagulation profile - To exclude coagulopathy in cases of severe haemorrhage.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Placenta Praevia"][/sc]
  • Placental abruption - Painful bleeding with a tense, tender uterus.
  • Vasa praevia - Painless vaginal bleeding with foetal distress.
  • Cervical ectropion - Post-coital bleeding but no placental involvement.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Placenta Praevia"][/sc]
  • Avoid vaginal examination in suspected cases.
  • Monitor with ultrasound; planned caesarean section at 36-37 weeks if placenta remains praevia.
  • Immediate hospitalisation for significant bleeding; blood transfusion if required.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Placenta Praevia"][/sc]
  • Massive postpartum haemorrhage.
  • Preterm birth.
  • Placenta accreta spectrum.
