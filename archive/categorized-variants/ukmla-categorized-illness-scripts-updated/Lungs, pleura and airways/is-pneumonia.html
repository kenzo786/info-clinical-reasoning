Pneumonia [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Pneumonia"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Pneumonia"][/sc]
  • Pneumonia is an acute infection of the lung parenchyma, leading to inflammation and consolidation of alveolar spaces. It can be caused by bacteria, viruses, fungi, or aspiration. The infection disrupts normal gas exchange and can result in significant morbidity and mortality, particularly in vulnerable populations such as the elderly or immunocompromised individuals.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Pneumonia"][/sc]
  • [sc name="1095-jitl-epidemiology-link" Q="Pneumonia"][/sc]
  • Risk Factors: Advanced age, smoking, chronic lung disease (e.g., COPD, asthma), immunosuppression (HIV, chemotherapy, long-term corticosteroid use), neurological conditions causing aspiration (stroke, Parkinsonâ€™s disease), and alcohol misuse increase susceptibility to pneumonia. Exposure to air pollution and overcrowded living conditions also elevate risk.
  • Prevention: Vaccination against pneumococcus (e.g., Pneumococcal polysaccharide vaccine, PPV23), influenza, and COVID-19 significantly reduces the risk. Smoking cessation, good hand hygiene, and prompt treatment of respiratory infections are essential preventive measures. Aspiration precautions, such as modifying diets in dysphagic patients, can prevent aspiration pneumonia.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Pneumonia"][/sc]
  • Step 1: Pathogens enter the lower respiratory tract via inhalation, aspiration, or haematogenous spread. The immune system fails to contain the infection due to virulence factors or host weakness.
  • Step 2: Alveolar macrophages initiate an inflammatory response, releasing cytokines and recruiting neutrophils. This leads to alveolar filling with exudates, immune cells, and debris.
  • Step 3: Consolidation of lung tissue occurs, impairing gas exchange and leading to hypoxia.
  • Step 4: The body attempts to clear the infection, which can result in resolution or lead to complications like abscess formation or pleural effusion.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Pneumonia"][/sc]
  • Onset: Acute, usually over a few days.
  • Initial Phase: Symptoms of upper respiratory tract infection may precede pneumonia, such as fever, malaise, and cough.
  • Progression: Over days, cough worsens, and patients may develop pleuritic chest pain, productive cough, and dyspnoea.
  • Resolution: With treatment, clinical improvement typically occurs within 48-72 hours. Full recovery can take several weeks, especially in elderly or debilitated patients.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Pneumonia"][/sc]
  • Cough (productive) - Due to irritation of the respiratory epithelium and accumulation of secretions in the alveoli.
  • Fever - A result of the systemic inflammatory response triggered by the infection.
  • Dyspnoea - Due to impaired gas exchange caused by alveolar consolidation and hypoxia.
  • Pleuritic chest pain - Results from inflammation of the pleura, often exacerbated by deep breathing or coughing.
  • Fatigue and malaise - Common systemic symptoms associated with infection and inflammation.
  • Confusion (in elderly) - Atypical presentation caused by hypoxia, sepsis, or dehydration.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Pneumonia"][/sc]
  • Tachypnoea - Increased respiratory rate due to hypoxia and the body's need to compensate.
  • Tachycardia - Reflects the body's effort to maintain oxygen delivery to tissues in the context of hypoxia.
  • Crackles (on auscultation) - Result from air passing through fluid-filled alveoli during inspiration.
  • Decreased breath sounds - Associated with consolidation and reduced air entry in affected lung regions.
  • Dullness to percussion - Due to lung consolidation, which increases tissue density in the affected area.
  • Cyanosis (in severe cases) - Indication of significant hypoxia and impaired oxygenation.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Pneumonia"][/sc]
  • Severe hypoxia (SpO2 < 92%) - Indicates severe impairment in gas exchange, potentially requiring urgent oxygen therapy or ventilation.
  • Altered mental status - Suggests hypoxia or sepsis; may be a sign of impending respiratory failure or severe systemic infection.
  • Hypotension - A late sign of sepsis, indicating circulatory collapse and the need for aggressive fluid resuscitation or inotropic support.
  • Persistent high fever > 39Â°C - Could indicate an ongoing or unresolving infection, possibly due to resistant organisms or complications like empyema.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Pneumonia"][/sc]
  • Elderly patients - Often present with confusion, falls, or generalised weakness rather than respiratory symptoms, due to poor immune response and reduced physiological reserve.
  • Atypical pathogens (e.g. Mycoplasma) - Patients may present with a dry cough, headache, and myalgia rather than typical signs of consolidation.
  • Immunocompromised hosts - May present with minimal or no fever and a slower disease progression due to reduced immune response.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Pneumonia"][/sc]
  • Chest X-ray - Consolidation, lobar or patchy infiltrates, often in the lower lobes. Explains the airspace filling with inflammatory exudate.
  • Full blood count - Raised white blood cell count (neutrophilia in bacterial pneumonia) due to immune response to infection.
  • CRP - Elevated, indicating acute inflammation. Helps in monitoring the response to treatment.
  • Sputum culture - Identifies causative organism and guides antibiotic therapy, especially in cases of suspected resistant bacteria.
  • Blood gas analysis - May reveal hypoxaemia, hypercapnia in severe cases, and metabolic acidosis in septic patients.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Pneumonia"][/sc]
  • An 80-year-old man presents with a 5-day history of productive cough, fever, and increasing breathlessness. On examination, he has tachypnoea, crackles at the right lung base, and oxygen saturation of 90% on room air.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Pneumonia"][/sc]
  • Heart failure - Presents with dyspnoea, orthopnoea, and peripheral oedema, but lacks fever and the productive cough associated with pneumonia. CXR shows pulmonary oedema rather than consolidation.
  • Pulmonary embolism - Acute onset of dyspnoea, pleuritic chest pain, and haemoptysis, but no fever or productive cough. D-dimer and CTPA help differentiate it.
  • Bronchitis - Presents with cough and wheezing but without consolidation on chest X-ray. Symptoms tend to be less severe than pneumonia.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Pneumonia"][/sc]
  • Antibiotics - Empirical treatment with amoxicillin (500mg three times daily for 5-7 days). If penicillin allergic, doxycycline or clarithromycin can be used.
  • Oxygen therapy - Administered if oxygen saturation < 92% or in the case of significant respiratory distress.
  • IV fluids - For patients with hypotension or signs of dehydration.
  • Analgesia - For pleuritic chest pain, paracetamol or NSAIDs can be used.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Pneumonia"][/sc]
  • Sepsis - Occurs due to widespread infection and systemic inflammatory response. It increases mortality, especially in the elderly.
  • Empyema - Collection of pus in the pleural cavity; occurs in 2-5% of pneumonia cases. Requires drainage and prolonged antibiotic therapy.
  • Acute respiratory distress syndrome (ARDS) - Severe, life-threatening condition resulting from diffuse lung injury. Patients require mechanical ventilation.
  • Lung abscess - Necrosis of lung tissue forming a cavity filled with pus. Occurs in severe or untreated pneumonia.
