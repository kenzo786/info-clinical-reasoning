Sarcoidosis
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Sarcoidosis"][/sc]
  • Sarcoidosis is a multi-system granulomatous disorder of unknown aetiology, characterised by the formation of non-caseating granulomas in affected tissues. It primarily affects the lungs and lymphatic system, but it can involve virtually any organ, including the skin, eyes, heart, and nervous system.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Sarcoidosis"][/sc]
  • UK GP population: Sarcoidosis is relatively rare, with an incidence of about 3 per 100,000 people per year.
  • UK hospital prevalence: Sarcoidosis represents 0.2-1% of hospital admissions related to respiratory illness.
  • Worldwide prevalence: Highest in Northern Europe, particularly among Scandinavian populations, with an annual incidence of 5-40 per 100,000 people. It is less common in Africa and Asia but is more severe in African American populations.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Sarcoidosis"][/sc]
  • The precise cause of sarcoidosis is unknown, but it is believed to be an exaggerated immune response to an unknown antigen. The pathophysiology involves the formation of non-caseating granulomas, which are clusters of macrophages surrounded by lymphocytes. These granulomas can cause organ damage by disrupting normal tissue architecture and function. In the lungs, granuloma formation can lead to fibrosis over time, impairing respiratory function.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Sarcoidosis"][/sc]
  • Sarcoidosis typically follows one of two patterns: acute or chronic. The acute form may resolve spontaneously within 1-2 years, while chronic sarcoidosis progresses slowly, potentially leading to long-term organ damage. Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Sarcoidosis"][/sc]
  • Fatigue - Common in over 70% of patients. It may result from chronic inflammation and immune activation.
  • Dyspnoea (shortness of breath) - Caused by lung involvement due to granulomas disrupting normal lung architecture and leading to decreased gas exchange.
  • Cough - A dry cough is common due to pulmonary involvement and irritation from granulomas.
  • Chest pain - Can be pleuritic or due to mediastinal lymphadenopathy compressing surrounding structures.
  • Skin lesions - Such as erythema nodosum, particularly in acute sarcoidosis like LÃ¶fgrenâ€™s syndrome. The pathophysiology involves immune complex deposition in the subcutaneous fat.
  • Visual disturbances - Due to uveitis, with granuloma formation in the eye leading to inflammation and vision changes.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Sarcoidosis"][/sc]
  • Bilateral hilar lymphadenopathy - Often seen on chest X-ray, due to granulomas in the mediastinal lymph nodes.
  • Skin nodules (erythema nodosum) - Tender red nodules, often on the shins, caused by an inflammatory reaction in the subcutaneous fat.
  • Crackles on lung auscultation - Reflects the presence of pulmonary involvement with granulomatous inflammation or fibrosis.
  • Uveitis - Can present as eye redness, photophobia, and decreased vision due to granulomatous inflammation in the uveal tract.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Sarcoidosis"][/sc]
  • Severe dyspnoea and hypoxaemia - May indicate extensive pulmonary fibrosis or acute respiratory failure.
  • Heart block or arrhythmias - Suggest cardiac involvement, which occurs in about 5% of patients, but may lead to sudden cardiac death if untreated.
  • Neurological symptoms - Including weakness, facial nerve palsy, or seizures, indicating neurosarcoidosis, which can be a serious and potentially life-threatening complication.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Sarcoidosis"][/sc]
  • Isolated extrapulmonary involvement - Sarcoidosis can present without pulmonary symptoms, involving organs such as the skin, eyes, or heart. Lack of respiratory symptoms may delay diagnosis.
  • Chronic fatigue without other symptoms - Fatigue as a solitary symptom may be mistaken for other chronic conditions like depression or fibromyalgia.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Sarcoidosis"][/sc]
  • Chest X-ray - Bilateral hilar lymphadenopathy is a hallmark. Staging based on lung involvement (Stages 0-4).
  • Serum ACE levels - Elevated in about 60% of cases due to macrophage activation in granulomas.
  • Lung function tests - May show a restrictive pattern in advanced pulmonary sarcoidosis.
  • Biopsy - Histological examination reveals non-caseating granulomas, which confirm the diagnosis.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Sarcoidosis"][/sc]
  • A 35-year-old African woman presents with a 3-month history of progressive dyspnoea, dry cough, and fatigue. She also reports red, tender lumps on her shins and blurred vision in her left eye. Chest X-ray reveals bilateral hilar lymphadenopathy.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Sarcoidosis"][/sc]
  • Tuberculosis - Differentiated by caseating granulomas on biopsy and a history of exposure or positive Mantoux test.
  • Lymphoma - May present with similar lymphadenopathy, but systemic symptoms (fever, weight loss) are more prominent, and biopsy shows malignant cells.
  • Hypersensitivity pneumonitis - Presents with similar lung symptoms but is linked to specific environmental exposures and lacks the characteristic granulomas of sarcoidosis.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Sarcoidosis"][/sc]
  • Corticosteroids - First-line treatment for most patients with significant symptoms or organ involvement. Prednisolone is commonly used in the UK, starting at 20-40 mg/day.
  • Immunosuppressants - Methotrexate or azathioprine may be used as steroid-sparing agents in chronic disease.
  • Antimalarials - Hydroxychloroquine is used for skin or mild pulmonary disease.
  • TNF inhibitors - In refractory cases, infliximab can be considered.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Sarcoidosis"][/sc]
  • Pulmonary fibrosis - Occurs in chronic cases, leading to irreversible lung damage and respiratory failure.
  • Cardiac sarcoidosis - Can cause arrhythmias, heart block, or heart failure.
  • Neurosarcoidosis - May lead to cranial nerve palsies, seizures, or even death if untreated.
  • Blindness - Due to untreated uveitis leading to optic nerve damage.
