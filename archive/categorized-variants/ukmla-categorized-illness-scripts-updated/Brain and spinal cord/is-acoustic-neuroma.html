Acoustic Neuroma (Vestibular Schwannoma)
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Acoustic Neuroma (Vestibular Schwannoma)"][/sc]
  • Acoustic neuroma, also known as vestibular schwannoma, is a benign tumour that arises from Schwann cells, typically affecting the vestibulocochlear nerve (cranial nerve VIII). It most commonly involves the vestibular portion of the nerve and can compress surrounding structures, leading to hearing loss, imbalance, and other cranial nerve dysfunctions. It is slow-growing and non-malignant but may cause significant morbidity due to its proximity to vital structures in the cerebellopontine angle.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Acoustic Neuroma (Vestibular Schwannoma)"][/sc]
  • In the UK, the incidence of acoustic neuroma is approximately 1-2 cases per 100,000 people per year.
  • Acoustic neuroma accounts for about 6-10% of all intracranial tumours and 80-90% of cerebellopontine angle tumours worldwide.
  • The condition predominantly affects adults between 40 and 60 years old, and there is no significant gender predisposition.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Acoustic Neuroma (Vestibular Schwannoma)"][/sc]
  • Acoustic neuromas arise from Schwann cells, which provide myelin sheath insulation to the vestibulocochlear nerve. The tumour typically develops from the vestibular component of the nerve, with growth in the cerebellopontine angle.
  • As the tumour enlarges, it compresses adjacent structures such as the cochlear nerve (leading to hearing loss), the facial nerve (causing facial weakness or numbness), and brainstem structures (leading to balance issues or hydrocephalus).
  • The tumourâ€™s slow growth allows for adaptive changes in the central auditory and vestibular pathways, often delaying symptom onset.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Acoustic Neuroma (Vestibular Schwannoma)"][/sc]
  • The growth of acoustic neuromas is typically slow, often spanning several years. Many patients are asymptomatic in the early stages.
  • Initial symptoms are usually subtle, with progressive hearing loss or tinnitus being the first to present.
  • As the tumour grows, balance problems, vertigo, and cranial nerve involvement (e.g., facial numbness) become more apparent.
  • In the late stages, larger tumours may compress the brainstem, leading to life-threatening complications such as hydrocephalus or raised intracranial pressure.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Acoustic Neuroma (Vestibular Schwannoma)"][/sc]
  • Hearing loss - Progressive unilateral sensorineural hearing loss, due to cochlear nerve compression. This is the most common presenting symptom.
  • Tinnitus - Unilateral tinnitus often accompanies hearing loss due to irritation of the cochlear nerve.
  • Imbalance and dizziness - Vestibular dysfunction due to involvement of the vestibular nerve, presenting as chronic disequilibrium rather than acute vertigo.
  • Facial numbness - Larger tumours may compress the trigeminal nerve, leading to facial sensory disturbances.
  • Headache - Occurs in later stages when the tumour compresses the brainstem or causes raised intracranial pressure.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Acoustic Neuroma (Vestibular Schwannoma)"][/sc]
  • Unilateral sensorineural hearing loss - Detected on audiometry and Weberâ€™s test (lateralises to the unaffected ear).
  • Absent corneal reflex - Indicative of trigeminal nerve involvement.
  • Facial weakness or asymmetry - Compression of the facial nerve in larger tumours can lead to these findings.
  • Nystagmus - Occurs in advanced cases due to vestibular nerve dysfunction.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Acoustic Neuroma (Vestibular Schwannoma)"][/sc]
  • Severe headache with vomiting - May indicate raised intracranial pressure or hydrocephalus, requiring urgent intervention.
  • Sudden onset facial paralysis - Suggests significant tumour growth with brainstem compression or facial nerve involvement.
  • Rapidly worsening balance issues or ataxia - May be due to cerebellar compression.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Acoustic Neuroma (Vestibular Schwannoma)"][/sc]
  • Sudden hearing loss - Though rare, acoustic neuromas can present with abrupt unilateral hearing loss, often mistaken for other causes of sensorineural hearing loss like viral infections.
  • Normal hearing with balance issues - Some patients may primarily complain of disequilibrium without noticeable hearing loss, delaying the diagnosis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Acoustic Neuroma (Vestibular Schwannoma)"][/sc]
  • Audiometry - Typically shows unilateral sensorineural hearing loss.
  • Magnetic Resonance Imaging (MRI) with contrast - Gold standard for diagnosis, revealing a well-demarcated tumour in the cerebellopontine angle. Contrast enhancement confirms the lesion.
  • Brainstem Evoked Response Audiometry (BERA) - Useful in detecting retrocochlear pathology, showing delayed latency of wave V.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Acoustic Neuroma (Vestibular Schwannoma)"][/sc]
  • A 52-year-old woman presents with a 1-year history of progressive unilateral hearing loss, associated with tinnitus and intermittent dizziness. Physical examination reveals sensorineural hearing loss on the right side and absent corneal reflex. MRI confirms a 2 cm vestibular schwannoma in the cerebellopontine angle.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Acoustic Neuroma (Vestibular Schwannoma)"][/sc]
  • Meniereâ€™s disease - Presents with episodic vertigo, fluctuating hearing loss, and tinnitus, but typically shows normal MRI findings.
  • Multiple sclerosis - Can present with vertigo and hearing disturbances, but is associated with multiple demyelinating lesions on MRI and may affect both sides.
  • Sudden sensorineural hearing loss (SSNHL) - Sudden onset hearing loss often idiopathic or viral in origin, typically lacking vestibular symptoms or MRI findings of a tumour.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Acoustic Neuroma (Vestibular Schwannoma)"][/sc]
  • Watchful waiting - For small tumours with minimal symptoms, regular MRI monitoring is recommended to assess for growth.
  • Stereotactic radiosurgery - Considered for medium-sized tumours or for patients who are not surgical candidates, this method aims to halt tumour growth without open surgery.
  • Microsurgical removal - Indicated for larger tumours or symptomatic cases. Depending on tumour size, either the translabyrinthine or retrosigmoid approach may be used.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Acoustic Neuroma (Vestibular Schwannoma)"][/sc]
  • Hearing loss - Permanent, particularly with larger tumours or following surgical resection.
  • Facial nerve paralysis - A risk in both large tumours and post-surgical outcomes due to facial nerve involvement.
  • Hydrocephalus - Occurs in late-stage disease when the tumour obstructs cerebrospinal fluid flow.
  • Recurrence - Tumour recurrence can occur after incomplete resection or stereotactic radiosurgery.
