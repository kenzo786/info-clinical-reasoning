Vitamin D Deficiency
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Vitamin D Deficiency"][/sc]
  • Vitamin D deficiency is a condition where there is insufficient vitamin D in the body, leading to inadequate calcium absorption, impaired bone metabolism, and various other systemic effects. Vitamin D is essential for calcium and phosphate homeostasis and maintaining bone health. Deficiency can lead to rickets in children and osteomalacia in adults.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Vitamin D Deficiency"][/sc]
  • UK GP population: Vitamin D deficiency is highly prevalent, affecting up to 30-40% of adults during winter months.
  • UK hospital population: Hospitalised patients, especially elderly and chronically ill individuals, are at higher risk, with prevalence rates of around 60% in some groups.
  • Worldwide prevalence: Worldwide, it affects approximately 1 billion people, with a higher prevalence in regions with less sun exposure.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Vitamin D Deficiency"][/sc]
  • Vitamin D, primarily synthesised in the skin via sunlight (UVB), is converted to its active form, calcitriol, in the liver and kidneys. When deficient, there is reduced calcium absorption in the gut, leading to hypocalcaemia. This triggers an increase in parathyroid hormone (PTH), causing bone resorption to maintain calcium levels, resulting in bone demineralisation, muscle weakness, and impaired bone remodelling.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Vitamin D Deficiency"][/sc]
  • Early stage: Asymptomatic or mild non-specific symptoms like fatigue and muscle weakness over months to years.
  • Progressive phase: Development of bone pain, increased muscle weakness, and hypocalcaemic symptoms over several months.
  • Late stage: Persistent deficiency can result in osteomalacia, rickets in children, or more severe complications like fractures over years.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Vitamin D Deficiency"][/sc]
  • Fatigue - Commonly reported due to impaired calcium metabolism affecting cellular energy processes.
  • Muscle weakness - Due to reduced calcium influx into muscle cells, leading to diminished muscle contraction strength.
  • Bone pain - Especially in weight-bearing areas, as bone demineralisation occurs and leads to softer bones.
  • Hypocalcaemic symptoms (e.g., tetany, paraesthesia) - Low calcium levels cause neuromuscular irritability, manifesting in muscle spasms and tingling.
  • Depression - Low vitamin D levels have been associated with mood disturbances due to its role in brain function.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Vitamin D Deficiency"][/sc]
  • Myopathy - Proximal muscle weakness due to calcium dysregulation affecting muscle fibre function.
  • Bone tenderness - Softened bones, especially in the ribs or long bones, can be painful on palpation.
  • Gait disturbances - Difficulty walking or waddling gait due to muscle weakness and bone pain.
  • Delayed tooth eruption (in children) - Impaired bone and tooth development caused by inadequate mineralisation.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Vitamin D Deficiency"][/sc]
  • Severe hypocalcaemia - Can present with seizures, laryngospasm, or cardiac arrhythmias, indicating dangerously low calcium levels due to vitamin D deficiency.
  • Fractures - Indicates advanced osteomalacia or osteoporosis due to prolonged deficiency.
  • Rickets (in children) - Severe bowing of the legs or growth retardation, signifying significant impairment of bone formation.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Vitamin D Deficiency"][/sc]
  • Asymptomatic deficiency - Many individuals may have no symptoms and only be diagnosed through routine blood tests showing low vitamin D levels.
  • Neurological symptoms - Some individuals may present with subtle cognitive disturbances or paraesthesia due to hypocalcaemia without classical bone or muscle symptoms.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Vitamin D Deficiency"][/sc]
  • Serum 25-hydroxyvitamin D (25(OH)D) levels - Levels < 25 nmol/L indicate deficiency. 25(OH)D is the best marker of overall vitamin D status.
  • Serum calcium and phosphate - Often normal but may show hypocalcaemia or low phosphate in severe cases due to impaired absorption.
  • Parathyroid hormone (PTH) - Elevated in response to hypocalcaemia as the body tries to maintain calcium levels through bone resorption.
  • X-rays - May show osteopenia or Looserâ€™s zones (pseudofractures) in severe cases of osteomalacia.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Vitamin D Deficiency"][/sc]
  • A 65-year-old woman presents with 6 months of generalised fatigue, worsening muscle weakness, and intermittent bone pain, particularly in her hips and lower back. She is found to have a low serum 25(OH)D level of 15 nmol/L.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Vitamin D Deficiency"][/sc]
  • Hypoparathyroidism - Differentiated by low PTH levels, whereas vitamin D deficiency presents with elevated PTH.
  • Osteoporosis - Typically presents without muscle weakness or hypocalcaemia, primarily leading to fractures without biochemical abnormalities.
  • Chronic kidney disease - May present similarly with hypocalcaemia, but associated with elevated creatinine and phosphate levels due to impaired renal function.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Vitamin D Deficiency"][/sc]
  • Vitamin D supplementation: In the UK, NICE recommends 800-1,000 IU of vitamin D daily for deficiency, with higher doses of 2,000-4,000 IU for severe deficiency.
  • Calcium supplementation: May be co-administered if dietary intake is inadequate or if there is evidence of hypocalcaemia.
  • Sunlight exposure: Encouraged for natural vitamin D production, especially in mild cases.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Vitamin D Deficiency"][/sc]
  • Fractures - Due to weakened bones and increased risk of osteoporosis and osteomalacia.
  • Cardiovascular disease - There is some evidence linking vitamin D deficiency to increased cardiovascular risk, possibly through effects on blood pressure regulation and inflammation.
