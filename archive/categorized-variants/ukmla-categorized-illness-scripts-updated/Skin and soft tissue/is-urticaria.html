Urticaria
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Urticaria"][/sc]
  • Urticaria, also known as hives, is a common skin condition characterised by transient, raised, pruritic wheals (often pink or red) that vary in size and shape. Lesions appear suddenly and typically resolve within 24 hours, though new lesions can continue to form.
  • It may be classified as acute (lasting <6 weeks) or chronic (lasting â‰¥6 weeks), with or without an identifiable trigger.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Urticaria"][/sc]
  • Urticaria affects approximately 15-20% of individuals at some point in their lives globally.
  • Acute urticaria is more common, with prevalence estimates around 1% in the UK GP population.
  • Chronic urticaria has a prevalence of approximately 0.5-1% worldwide, with a higher prevalence in women and often impacting quality of life significantly.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Urticaria"][/sc]
  • Urticaria is mediated by the release of histamine and other pro-inflammatory mediators from activated mast cells and basophils in the skin.
  • Histamine release leads to vasodilation and increased vascular permeability, causing plasma leakage and dermal oedema, which forms the wheals.
  • In chronic urticaria, autoimmune mechanisms may contribute, involving autoantibodies against IgE or the IgE receptor (FcÎµRI) on mast cells, leading to their activation.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Urticaria"][/sc]
  • Acute Phase: Wheals appear suddenly and resolve within 24 hours, though new lesions may continue to appear for days to weeks.
  • Chronic Phase: If symptoms persist for more than 6 weeks, the condition is considered chronic. Symptoms may last for months to years, with spontaneous resolution or fluctuation.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Urticaria"][/sc]
  • Pruritus (itching) - Itching is caused by histamine and other mediators released by mast cells, stimulating sensory nerves in the skin.
  • Burning or stinging sensation - Commonly experienced in areas of extensive wheals, due to irritation of nerve endings by inflammatory mediators.
  • Swelling (angioedema) - May occur with urticaria, especially in deeper tissues, from histamine-induced fluid accumulation in subcutaneous layers.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Urticaria"][/sc]
  • Wheals - Raised, erythematous, and oedematous plaques with surrounding redness, due to dermal oedema from histamine release.
  • Dermatographism - Skin changes in response to pressure, often seen as red, raised lines when skin is stroked, indicating heightened skin sensitivity.
  • Angioedema - Swelling around eyes, lips, or other areas, representing deeper dermal involvement.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Urticaria"][/sc]
  • Difficulty breathing or swallowing - Could indicate laryngeal oedema or anaphylaxis, requiring urgent medical attention.
  • Severe abdominal pain - May suggest visceral angioedema, particularly in cases with associated systemic involvement.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Urticaria"][/sc]
  • Urticarial vasculitis - Presents as painful, long-lasting wheals that may leave residual hyperpigmentation; caused by immune complex deposition in vessel walls.
  • Cold urticaria - Triggered by cold exposure, causing wheals upon rewarming; due to altered mast cell response to temperature change.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Urticaria"][/sc]
  • Complete blood count (CBC) - To check for anaemia or eosinophilia, especially if other allergic conditions are suspected.
  • Thyroid function tests - Important in chronic urticaria due to possible autoimmune thyroid disease association.
  • Allergy testing - Considered in acute urticaria if a specific trigger is suspected, such as foods or drugs.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Urticaria"][/sc]
  • A 32-year-old woman presents with sudden onset of itchy, red wheals on her trunk and limbs that resolve within hours but recur daily for the past 8 weeks. She also reports lip swelling intermittently.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Urticaria"][/sc]
  • Contact dermatitis - Localised reaction at the site of contact, usually with a delayed onset, unlike urticariaâ€™s rapid appearance and disappearance.
  • Viral exanthem - More widespread, non-itchy rash, often with prodromal symptoms, differing from the intense itching of urticaria.
  • Urticarial vasculitis - Painful, long-lasting lesions with residual pigmentation, unlike typical transient urticaria wheals.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Urticaria"][/sc]
  • Antihistamines - Non-sedating antihistamines such as cetirizine or loratadine are first-line for symptom control; doses may be increased if needed.
  • Short-course corticosteroids - For severe, acute flares, oral corticosteroids may be used briefly but are not recommended for chronic management.
  • Omalizumab - Anti-IgE monoclonal antibody considered in chronic refractory cases, per NICE guidelines.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Urticaria"][/sc]
  • Anaphylaxis - Rare but severe complication, especially if urticaria is associated with angioedema or systemic symptoms.
  • Impact on quality of life - Chronic itching and sleep disturbance can lead to anxiety, depression, and reduced social interactions.
