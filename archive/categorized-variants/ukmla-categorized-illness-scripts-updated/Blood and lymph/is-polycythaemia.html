Polycythaemia
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Polycythaemia"][/sc]
  • Polycythaemia is a haematological condition characterised by an increased concentration of red blood cells (RBCs) in the blood, leading to an elevated haematocrit and haemoglobin level. It can be classified as primary (often due to genetic mutations, like in polycythaemia vera) or secondary (due to external factors such as chronic hypoxia).
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Polycythaemia"][/sc]
  • Prevalence in UK general population: Polycythaemia vera affects approximately 2 per 100,000 people.
  • Hospital prevalence in the UK: Primary polycythaemia accounts for about 95% of polycythaemia cases requiring treatment, most often polycythaemia vera.
  • Worldwide prevalence: Rates are similar globally, although secondary polycythaemia prevalence is higher in areas with low oxygen availability, such as high altitudes.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Polycythaemia"][/sc]
  • Primary polycythaemia: In polycythaemia vera, a JAK2 mutation leads to abnormal signalling in haematopoietic stem cells, causing excessive RBC production independent of erythropoietin (EPO).
  • Secondary polycythaemia: Chronic hypoxia (e.g., due to lung disease, high altitude) or elevated EPO levels (e.g., renal tumour) stimulates increased RBC production as a compensatory response to low oxygen levels.
  • Hyperviscosity: Increased RBC mass raises blood viscosity, which can impair blood flow, increasing the risk of thrombosis.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Polycythaemia"][/sc]
  • Slow progression: Polycythaemia often develops over months to years, with symptoms emerging gradually.
  • Chronic phase: If untreated, primary polycythaemia can lead to thrombotic complications or transition to a myelofibrotic phase with bone marrow fibrosis.
  • Acute progression: Acute thrombotic events can occur, leading to strokes or myocardial infarction.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Polycythaemia"][/sc]
  • Fatigue - Commonly reported due to increased blood viscosity leading to poor tissue oxygenation.
  • Headache - Occurs from hyperviscosity-related reduced cerebral perfusion.
  • Pruritus, especially after warm showers - Believed to be linked to histamine release from excess basophils in polycythaemia vera.
  • Dizziness and light-headedness - Due to poor oxygen delivery in highly viscous blood.
  • Dyspnoea - Occurs in advanced cases due to decreased pulmonary oxygen exchange efficiency.
  • Asymptomatic presentation - Common in mild cases, often detected incidentally during routine blood tests.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Polycythaemia"][/sc]
  • Plethoric complexion (ruddy appearance) - Due to increased RBC mass and blood flow to the skin.
  • Splenomegaly - Common in polycythaemia vera, resulting from increased blood cell production.
  • Hypertension - Raised blood viscosity increases peripheral resistance.
  • Thrombotic signs (e.g., deep vein thrombosis, stroke) - Caused by hyperviscosity and increased clotting.
  • Hepatomegaly - Occasionally present due to extramedullary haematopoiesis.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Polycythaemia"][/sc]
  • Severe headache with visual changes - Indicates possible cerebral thrombosis, requiring urgent assessment.
  • Chest pain or shortness of breath - May signify myocardial infarction or pulmonary embolism.
  • Sudden weakness or difficulty speaking - Suggests a thrombotic stroke.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Polycythaemia"][/sc]
  • Mild headaches or fatigue without other symptoms - Common in early or mild cases and may be misdiagnosed as general fatigue.
  • Isolated hypertension - May be mistaken for essential hypertension without recognition of underlying polycythaemia.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Polycythaemia"][/sc]
  • Full blood count - Elevated haematocrit (>52% in men, >48% in women) and haemoglobin levels indicate polycythaemia.
  • Serum erythropoietin (EPO) - Low in primary polycythaemia, raised in secondary forms.
  • JAK2 mutation analysis - Positive in ~95% of polycythaemia vera cases, helping confirm primary polycythaemia.
  • Arterial blood gases (ABG) - May show hypoxaemia in secondary polycythaemia due to chronic hypoxia.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Polycythaemia"][/sc]
  • A 65-year-old man presents with fatigue, headaches, and plethoric complexion. Blood tests reveal elevated haematocrit and positive JAK2 mutation, suggesting polycythaemia vera.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Polycythaemia"][/sc]
  • Chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD) - Distinguished by chronic hypoxia but with associated lung findings.
  • Chronic kidney disease - May cause secondary polycythaemia due to raised EPO from renal hypoxia or tumours.
  • Dehydration - Can mimic polycythaemia but shows elevated haematocrit with normal RBC mass.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Polycythaemia"][/sc]
  • Phlebotomy - Primary treatment to maintain haematocrit below 45% in polycythaemia vera.
  • Aspirin (75-100 mg daily) - Reduces thrombotic risk in primary polycythaemia.
  • Hydroxycarbamide - Used for high-risk patients, especially with thrombotic history.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Polycythaemia"][/sc]
  • Thrombosis - Common due to increased blood viscosity, leading to strokes or myocardial infarctions.
  • Myelofibrosis - Progression to fibrosis in polycythaemia vera, causing bone marrow scarring and cytopenias.
  • Acute leukaemia - Rare but increased risk in polycythaemia vera, particularly with long-term treatment.
