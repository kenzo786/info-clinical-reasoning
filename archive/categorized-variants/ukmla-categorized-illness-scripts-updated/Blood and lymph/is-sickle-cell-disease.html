Sickle Cell Disease [sc name="ask-ai-100" Q="Sickle Cell Disease"][/sc]
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Sickle Cell Disease"][/sc]
  • Sickle cell disease (SCD) is an inherited haemoglobinopathy characterised by the presence of sickle-shaped red blood cells (RBCs). This results from a mutation in the HBB gene, which leads to the production of abnormal haemoglobin S (HbS). When deoxygenated, HbS polymerises, causing RBCs to become rigid, leading to vaso-occlusion, haemolysis, and a range of systemic complications.

Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Sickle Cell Disease"][/sc]
  • Gene Mutation: SCD is caused by a single point mutation in the beta-globin gene (HBB), leading to the substitution of valine for glutamic acid at the sixth position of the beta-globin chain.
  • Haemoglobin S Polymerisation: When deoxygenated, HbS polymerises, leading to the deformation of RBCs into a sickle shape, making them rigid and sticky.
  • Vaso-occlusion: Sickled cells can obstruct small blood vessels, leading to tissue ischaemia and pain crises, one of the hallmark features of SCD.
  • Haemolysis: Sickled RBCs are fragile and prone to destruction, resulting in chronic haemolytic anaemia, which contributes to fatigue and jaundice.
  • End-organ Damage: Recurrent vaso-occlusion and haemolysis cause progressive damage to organs such as the spleen, kidneys, lungs, and brain.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Sickle Cell Disease"][/sc]
  • Neonatal Period: Newborns with SCD are typically asymptomatic due to the presence of fetal haemoglobin (HbF), which prevents sickling. Symptoms begin to emerge around 6 months of age as HbF levels decrease.
  • Childhood and Adolescence: Recurrent painful vaso-occlusive crises, frequent infections, and organ complications such as splenic infarction and acute chest syndrome may occur.
  • Adulthood: Progressive organ damage becomes more prominent, leading to complications such as pulmonary hypertension, renal failure, and strokes.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Sickle Cell Disease"][/sc]
  • Vaso-occlusive Pain Crises: Caused by sickled cells obstructing blood flow to tissues, resulting in ischaemia and severe pain, commonly affecting the bones and joints.
  • Fatigue: Due to chronic haemolysis, patients often present with anaemia, which leads to generalised fatigue and weakness.
  • Jaundice: Haemolysis releases bilirubin, leading to yellowing of the skin and sclerae.
  • Frequent Infections: Splenic dysfunction due to repeated infarctions leads to increased susceptibility to encapsulated bacteria.
  • Dyspnoea: Pulmonary complications such as acute chest syndrome and chronic lung damage can result in shortness of breath.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Sickle Cell Disease"][/sc]
  • Pallor: Reflects anaemia due to haemolysis, often observed in the conjunctiva and skin.
  • Splenomegaly: Common in children due to splenic sequestration of sickled cells, though the spleen may become fibrotic in adulthood due to repeated infarctions.
  • Leg Ulcers: Due to chronic vascular occlusion and poor healing, particularly around the ankles.
  • Hypoxaemia: Reflects acute chest syndrome or chronic lung involvement, often presenting with low oxygen saturation.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Sickle Cell Disease"][/sc]
  • Severe Chest Pain or Dyspnoea: May indicate acute chest syndrome, a life-threatening complication due to pulmonary infarction or infection.
  • Neurological Deficits: Sudden weakness, facial drooping, or slurred speech could signify a cerebrovascular event (stroke).
  • Severe Abdominal Pain: Could be a sign of splenic sequestration crisis or mesenteric ischaemia.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Sickle Cell Disease"][/sc]
  • Asymptomatic in Early Life: Due to protective levels of HbF in infancy, patients may not exhibit symptoms until later in childhood.
  • Stroke in Young Adults: Though more common in children, silent strokes can occur without classic symptoms and lead to cognitive decline.
  • Priapism: Persistent painful erection due to vaso-occlusion in the penile vasculature, sometimes occurring without other SCD symptoms.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Sickle Cell Disease"][/sc]
  • Full Blood Count (FBC): Shows normocytic anaemia with elevated reticulocyte count, reflecting haemolysis.
  • Haemoglobin Electrophoresis: Confirms the presence of HbS. This is the definitive diagnostic test for SCD.
  • Bilirubin Levels: Elevated due to increased haemolysis, contributing to jaundice.
  • Transcranial Doppler Ultrasound: Used to assess stroke risk in children by detecting abnormal cerebral blood flow velocities.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Sickle Cell Disease"][/sc]
  • A 22-year-old male with known sickle cell disease presents with severe pain in the lower back and thighs, fatigue, and shortness of breath, lasting for 24 hours. He has had similar pain crises in the past.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Sickle Cell Disease"][/sc]
  • Thalassaemia: Unlike SCD, thalassaemia presents with microcytic anaemia and is diagnosed by abnormal haemoglobin electrophoresis showing HbA2 or HbF.
  • Autoimmune Haemolytic Anaemia (AIHA): AIHA presents with similar haemolysis but lacks the recurrent vaso-occlusive crises seen in SCD.
  • G6PD Deficiency: Haemolysis is typically triggered by oxidative stress (e.g., infection or drugs) and is associated with jaundice, but vaso-occlusion does not occur.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Sickle Cell Disease"][/sc]
  • Hydroxycarbamide: The mainstay of treatment to reduce the frequency of pain crises by increasing HbF levels, which inhibits sickling.
  • Penicillin Prophylaxis: Lifelong antibiotic prophylaxis due to splenic dysfunction and increased risk of infection.
  • Blood Transfusions: Used for managing severe anaemia and preventing stroke in high-risk patients.
  • Bone Marrow Transplant: Potentially curative but reserved for severe cases with a suitable donor.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Sickle Cell Disease"][/sc]
  • Stroke: Particularly common in children and young adults due to cerebrovascular occlusion.
  • Acute Chest Syndrome: A life-threatening complication often triggered by infection or infarction, leading to respiratory failure.
  • Renal Failure: Chronic kidney damage occurs due to repeated vaso-occlusion and reduced blood flow to the kidneys.
  • Avascular Necrosis: Infarction of bone, particularly in the femoral head, leading to joint destruction and chronic pain.
