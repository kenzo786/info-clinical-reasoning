Vestibular Neuritis
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Vestibular Neuritis"][/sc]
  • Vestibular neuritis is an inflammation of the vestibular nerve, typically caused by a viral infection. It results in sudden, severe vertigo without hearing loss or tinnitus. The condition affects the nerve responsible for transmitting balance information from the inner ear to the brain, leading to vertigo, imbalance, and nausea.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Vestibular Neuritis"][/sc]
  • Prevalence in UK GP population: Vestibular neuritis accounts for 7% of patients presenting with vertigo in primary care.
  • UK hospital prevalence: It represents 3-10% of patients with vertigo presenting to hospital emergency departments.
  • Worldwide prevalence: Vestibular neuritis affects approximately 3.5 per 100,000 people globally each year.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Vestibular Neuritis"][/sc]
  • The vestibular nerve transmits balance information from the semicircular canals of the inner ear to the brain. In vestibular neuritis, inflammationâ€”often following a viral upper respiratory infectionâ€”impairs the nerve's ability to relay signals accurately, causing a mismatch between the left and right vestibular systems. This mismatch results in the characteristic vertigo and balance disturbances. The inflammation is usually limited to the vestibular nerve, sparing the cochlear nerve, which is why hearing loss and tinnitus are absent.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Vestibular Neuritis"][/sc]
  • Acute phase: Sudden onset of severe vertigo, nausea, vomiting, and imbalance lasting for hours to days.
  • Subacute phase: After the acute phase, symptoms gradually improve over several days to weeks, with vertigo subsiding but imbalance and mild dizziness persisting.
  • Chronic phase: Patients may experience residual unsteadiness or mild dizziness for several weeks or even months, though most recover fully.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Vestibular Neuritis"][/sc]
  • Vertigo - Sudden, severe spinning sensation lasting hours to days due to inflammation of the vestibular nerve, disrupting balance signals.
  • Nausea and vomiting - Caused by the intense vertigo, as the brain misinterprets signals from the inner ear.
  • Imbalance - Difficulty maintaining balance due to impaired vestibular function. Patients feel unsteady when walking, especially in the acute phase.
  • Oscillopsia - Perception of objects moving back and forth, caused by disrupted gaze stability from vestibular dysfunction.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Vestibular Neuritis"][/sc]
  • Unidirectional horizontal nystagmus - Fast phase beats away from the affected ear, indicating vestibular asymmetry.
  • Positive head impulse test - A corrective saccade when the head is turned rapidly toward the affected side, indicating reduced vestibulo-ocular reflex.
  • Ataxic gait - Wobbling or stumbling while walking, due to impaired balance from the vestibular nerve dysfunction.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Vestibular Neuritis"][/sc]
  • Severe headache or neurological deficits - Could indicate a central cause of vertigo (e.g., stroke or brainstem lesion) rather than vestibular neuritis.
  • Sudden hearing loss or tinnitus - Suggests labyrinthitis or a central lesion, as vestibular neuritis typically spares hearing.
  • Prolonged vertigo without improvement - May indicate a more serious central cause such as posterior circulation stroke.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Vestibular Neuritis"][/sc]
  • Mild vertigo with rapid recovery - Some patients may present with mild symptoms that improve within hours, which can be mistaken for benign positional vertigo (BPPV).
  • Intermittent symptoms - Rather than a continuous course, patients may experience episodic vertigo, making it challenging to distinguish from MÃ©niÃ¨re's disease.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Vestibular Neuritis"][/sc]
  • Head impulse test - Positive result indicates reduced vestibulo-ocular reflex function, specifically towards the affected side.
  • Caloric testing - Reduced or absent response on the affected side confirms unilateral vestibular dysfunction.
  • MRI brain - Performed to rule out central causes of vertigo (e.g., stroke, tumour) in patients with red flag features or atypical presentation.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Vestibular Neuritis"][/sc]
  • A 45-year-old woman presents with sudden onset of severe vertigo, nausea, and vomiting lasting for 48 hours. She reports no hearing loss or tinnitus. Examination shows unidirectional nystagmus and a positive head impulse test on the left.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Vestibular Neuritis"][/sc]
  • Benign Paroxysmal Positional Vertigo (BPPV) - Episodic vertigo triggered by head movements; no continuous vertigo and no prolonged imbalance.
  • Labyrinthitis - Similar to vestibular neuritis but includes hearing loss and tinnitus.
  • MÃ©niÃ¨re's Disease - Recurrent vertigo with fluctuating hearing loss and tinnitus.
  • Posterior Circulation Stroke - Acute vertigo with neurological deficits (e.g., dysarthria, ataxia), requires urgent neuroimaging.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Vestibular Neuritis"][/sc]
  • Acute management: Vestibular suppressants (e.g., prochlorperazine) and antiemetics for severe symptoms in the acute phase (use sparingly due to potential suppression of vestibular compensation).
  • Vestibular rehabilitation: Exercises to promote central compensation and restore balance function during recovery.
  • Short course of corticosteroids: May reduce inflammation of the vestibular nerve and accelerate recovery, though evidence is mixed.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Vestibular Neuritis"][/sc]
  • Chronic imbalance - Rare, but some patients may experience long-term unsteadiness or dizziness due to incomplete vestibular compensation.
  • Anxiety and depression - Persistent vestibular dysfunction can lead to anxiety related to fear of falling or recurring episodes.
  • Benign paroxysmal positional vertigo (BPPV) - Can develop in the months following vestibular neuritis, likely due to otoliths becoming dislodged during inflammation.
