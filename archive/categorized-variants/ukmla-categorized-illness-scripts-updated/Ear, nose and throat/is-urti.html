URTI (Common cold) illness script
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="URTI (Common cold)"][/sc]
  • The common cold, or upper respiratory tract infection (URTI), is a viral infection primarily affecting the nose and throat. It is usually caused by rhinoviruses, although other viruses such as coronaviruses and adenoviruses can also be responsible. Symptoms include rhinorrhoea, sneezing, nasal congestion, sore throat, cough, and occasionally mild fever. It is generally self-limiting and lasts about 7-10 days.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="URTI (Common cold)"][/sc]
  • In the UK, URTIs are responsible for over 27 million GP consultations annually. Worldwide, adults experience 2-4 episodes per year, while children may have 6-8. Hospital admissions are uncommon, but URTIs can exacerbate chronic conditions like asthma or COPD, especially in at-risk groups.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="URTI (Common cold)"][/sc]
  • URTIs begin when viruses enter the body through the nose or mouth, attaching to the epithelial cells of the upper respiratory tract. The viruses replicate, causing cell damage and triggering an immune response. This immune activation leads to inflammation, which causes the characteristic symptoms of nasal congestion, increased mucus production, and sore throat. Systemic immune responses, such as cytokine release, may cause malaise and fever in some cases.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="URTI (Common cold)"][/sc]
  • Incubation period: 1-3 days after viral exposure.
  • Symptom onset: Symptoms develop gradually, peaking by day 2-3.
  • Duration: Most symptoms resolve within 7-10 days, though a cough can persist for up to 2-3 weeks in some cases.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="URTI (Common cold)"][/sc]
  • Nasal congestion - Inflammation of nasal mucosa leads to vasodilation and increased mucus production, causing blockage of the nasal passages.
  • Rhinorrhoea (runny nose) - Excessive mucus production due to viral-induced inflammation and irritation of nasal tissues.
  • Sore throat - Viral infection and immune response in the pharyngeal mucosa lead to irritation and discomfort.
  • Cough - Irritation of the respiratory epithelium and postnasal drip trigger the cough reflex. The cough may be mild and non-productive in most cases.
  • Malaise - Systemic effects of cytokine release during the immune response cause generalised fatigue and a feeling of discomfort.
  • Mild fever - A slight rise in body temperature (rare in adults) may occur as part of the systemic immune response to the viral infection, though high fever is uncommon.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="URTI (Common cold)"][/sc]
  • Nasal mucosal swelling - Visible inflammation and swelling of the nasal mucosa on examination due to increased vascular permeability and fluid accumulation.
  • Pharyngeal erythema - Redness of the throat caused by inflammation of the pharyngeal tissues.
  • Clear nasal discharge - Thin, watery mucus often seen early in the course of the illness, reflecting increased secretions from inflamed nasal tissues.

Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="URTI (Common cold)"][/sc]
  • Older adults or immunocompromised patients may present with less pronounced symptoms, such as only a mild cough or sore throat. These patients may have a blunted immune response, which leads to fewer overt symptoms.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="URTI (Common cold)"][/sc]
  • No routine tests required: URTI is diagnosed clinically based on history and physical examination.
  • Viral PCR testing: May be done during outbreaks or for specific diagnoses (e.g., COVID-19). It identifies the viral pathogen in nasal or throat swabs.
  • Full blood count (FBC): Typically normal or showing mild lymphocytosis in viral infections.
Problem representation [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="URTI (Common cold)"][/sc]
  • A 30-year-old male presents with a 4-day history of sore throat, nasal congestion, sneezing, and mild cough. He has no significant fever or dyspnoea. His symptoms started after a family member developed similar symptoms.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="URTI (Common cold)"][/sc]
  • Influenza: Sudden onset with higher fever and more severe systemic symptoms like myalgia and fatigue.
  • Bacterial pharyngitis: Marked sore throat with fever and absence of cough; confirmed with throat swab or rapid antigen test.
  • Allergic rhinitis: Seasonal or perennial, with prominent itching, sneezing, and clear rhinorrhoea. No fever or systemic symptoms.
  • Pneumonia: Fever, productive cough, and dyspnoea with abnormal lung auscultation findings. Confirmed with chest X-ray.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="URTI (Common cold)"][/sc]
  • Supportive care: Paracetamol or ibuprofen for pain and fever, rest, and hydration.
  • Decongestants: Nasal sprays (e.g., oxymetazoline) or oral pseudoephedrine for nasal congestion relief.
  • Saline nasal spray: Non-medicated option to clear nasal congestion.
  • Cough suppressants: Dextromethorphan may be used for a persistent or bothersome cough.
  • Antibiotics: Not recommended for viral URTIs unless bacterial superinfection is suspected.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="URTI (Common cold)"][/sc]
  • Secondary bacterial infections: Sinusitis, otitis media, or bronchitis can occur due to impaired mucociliary clearance and immune defence.
  • Exacerbation of asthma: URTIs can trigger asthma exacerbations in patients with underlying respiratory conditions.
  • Prognosis: Excellent in most cases, with complete recovery in 7-10 days. Severe complications are rare.
