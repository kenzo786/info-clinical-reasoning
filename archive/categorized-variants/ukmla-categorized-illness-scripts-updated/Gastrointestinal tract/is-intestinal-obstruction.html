Intestinal Obstruction
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Intestinal Obstruction"][/sc]
  • Intestinal obstruction is a blockage in the small or large intestine that prevents the normal passage of digestive contents. It can be due to mechanical causes (e.g., adhesions, hernias, tumours) or functional causes (e.g., paralytic ileus). The obstruction can lead to distension, ischemia, necrosis, and perforation if untreated, making it a surgical emergency.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Intestinal Obstruction"][/sc]
  • UK GP prevalence: Rare, as patients with suspected intestinal obstruction are typically referred directly to secondary care.
  • UK hospital prevalence: Approximately 1-3% of emergency surgical admissions, with small bowel obstructions being more common than large bowel obstructions.
  • Worldwide prevalence: Varies by country and healthcare access, but mechanical obstruction is a common cause of surgical emergencies globally, particularly in the elderly.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Intestinal Obstruction"][/sc]
  • 1. Blockage: Either mechanical (e.g., adhesions or tumours) or functional (e.g., paralytic ileus) mechanisms obstruct the intestinal lumen.
  • 2. Bowel distension: Trapped fluid and gases accumulate above the obstruction, causing distension and compression of the bowel walls.
  • 3. Ischemia: Increased intraluminal pressure impairs blood flow, leading to ischemia and potential necrosis.
  • 4. Bacterial translocation: Overgrowth of bacteria in stagnant bowel contents can lead to translocation across the bowel wall and into the bloodstream, causing sepsis.
  • 5. Perforation: Untreated ischemic necrosis can lead to perforation and peritonitis, a life-threatening complication.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Intestinal Obstruction"][/sc]
  • Acute onset: Hours to days, typically in cases of volvulus or incarcerated hernias, where symptoms develop rapidly.
  • Subacute onset: Gradual symptom development over days to a week, often seen in cases of adhesions or malignant obstructions.
  • Chronic course: Slow progression, with intermittent symptoms that may be present for weeks or months, commonly associated with partial obstructions or malignancy.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Intestinal Obstruction"][/sc]
  • Abdominal pain: Often colicky in nature, reflecting peristaltic waves trying to overcome the obstruction. In ischemic or necrotic bowel, the pain becomes constant and severe.
  • Nausea and vomiting: Common, especially in proximal obstructions, where digestive contents back up. Vomitus may become faeculent in distal obstructions.
  • Distension: Results from trapped gases and fluids; more pronounced in distal obstructions.
  • Constipation or obstipation: Absence of flatus or bowel movements indicates a complete obstruction, often presenting in the later stages of distal obstructions.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Intestinal Obstruction"][/sc]
  • Abdominal tenderness: Localised or diffuse tenderness, which may indicate peritoneal irritation or impending perforation.
  • Abdominal distension: Visible and palpable bloating, more noticeable in distal obstructions.

  • Fever and tachycardia: Indicative of infection or sepsis, particularly if bowel necrosis or perforation has occurred.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Intestinal Obstruction"][/sc]
  • Constant, severe pain: Suggests bowel ischemia or necrosis, which requires immediate surgical intervention.
  • Haemodynamic instability (tachycardia, hypotension): May indicate sepsis or hypovolaemia from bowel ischemia.
  • Rebound tenderness and guarding: Signifies peritonitis, often due to bowel perforation.
  • Fever: Suggests infection, with a heightened concern for perforation or necrosis if present.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Intestinal Obstruction"][/sc]
  • Mild or intermittent pain: Seen in partial obstructions, where the blockage allows some passage of intestinal contents.
  • Minimal vomiting: Common in distal obstructions or low-grade obstructions that permit slow gastric emptying.
  • Confusion and reduced consciousness: Particularly in older adults who may not localise pain or show classic symptoms, presenting instead with altered mental status.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Intestinal Obstruction"][/sc]
  • Abdominal X-ray: Shows dilated bowel loops with air-fluid levels, indicating obstruction. "Coffee bean" sign is suggestive of sigmoid volvulus.
  • CT abdomen and pelvis: More detailed than X-ray, identifying site and cause of obstruction (e.g., tumour, hernia). Enhanced scans can reveal ischemia or necrosis.
  • Blood tests: Elevated white cell count and CRP in infection; lactate elevation suggests ischemia.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Intestinal Obstruction"][/sc]
  • An elderly woman presents with a 2-day history of colicky abdominal pain, nausea, vomiting, and progressive distension. Examination reveals a tender, distended abdomen with high-pitched bowel sounds.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Intestinal Obstruction"][/sc]
  • Gastroenteritis: Typically features diarrhoea; fever more common; minimal or absent abdominal distension.
  • Constipation/impaction: May have chronic abdominal pain and distension but lacks nausea and vomiting; bowel sounds usually normal.
  • Mesenteric ischemia: Severe, constant abdominal pain with bloody stools; rapid progression to shock in acute cases.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Intestinal Obstruction"][/sc]
  • Initial stabilisation: IV fluids for resuscitation, electrolyte correction, and nasogastric tube for decompression.
  • Analgesia and antiemetics: As needed for symptom relief while awaiting definitive treatment.
  • Surgical intervention: Required in most cases, especially for complete obstruction, ischaemia, or necrosis. Options include resection or adhesiolysis.
  • Postoperative care: Includes infection monitoring and fluid management to support recovery and prevent recurrence.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Intestinal Obstruction"][/sc]
  • Ischemia and necrosis: Bowel wall ischemia due to pressure build-up can lead to tissue death and perforation.
  • Sepsis: Bacterial translocation from the compromised gut leads to systemic infection, especially in cases of perforation.
  • Peritonitis: Rupture of necrotic bowel content into the peritoneum causes life-threatening inflammation.
  • Mortality risk: Significant if complications such as necrosis or sepsis develop, particularly in elderly or immunocompromised patients.
