Oesophageal Varices
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Oesophageal Varices"][/sc]
  • Oesophageal varices are abnormally dilated submucosal veins in the lower oesophagus, typically caused by portal hypertension, often due to liver cirrhosis. They pose a significant risk of rupture, leading to life-threatening upper gastrointestinal (GI) bleeding.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Oesophageal Varices"][/sc]
  • UK GP Prevalence: Rare, as patients with cirrhosis and portal hypertension are primarily managed in secondary care.
  • UK Hospital Prevalence: In cirrhotic patients, up to 50% develop oesophageal varices, with approximately 5-15% per year experiencing bleeding.
  • Worldwide Prevalence: Highly variable; in cirrhosis, oesophageal varices are present in up to 70%, depending on liver disease severity and geography.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Oesophageal Varices"][/sc]
  • Portal hypertension due to liver cirrhosis, thrombosis, or other causes increases pressure in the portal venous system.
  • This elevated pressure leads to blood flow diversion via collateral veins, including those in the oesophagus.
  • With prolonged high portal pressure, submucosal veins in the oesophagus dilate and form varices.
  • As varices enlarge, the vessel walls thin, increasing the risk of rupture and haemorrhage.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Oesophageal Varices"][/sc]
  • Initial Phase: Develops slowly with the progression of portal hypertension; often asymptomatic initially.
  • Progressive Phase: Varices enlarge and risk of bleeding increases, especially if portal hypertension worsens.
  • Acute Phase: Variceal rupture can occur suddenly, leading to acute GI bleeding.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Oesophageal Varices"][/sc]
  • Asymptomatic - Often no symptoms until rupture; varices develop silently.
  • Haematemesis - Vomiting blood due to variceal rupture, a hallmark of active bleeding.
  • Melena - Black, tarry stools from digestion of blood in the GI tract.
  • Fatigue - Secondary to chronic blood loss, anaemia can lead to fatigue.
  • Abdominal Pain - Uncommon but may occur if portal hypertension is severe or ascites present.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Oesophageal Varices"][/sc]
  • Hypotension - Due to blood loss from variceal bleeding.
  • Tachycardia - Compensatory response to significant blood loss.
  • Pallor - Anaemia due to acute or chronic blood loss may cause pallor.
  • Ascites - Indicates advanced liver disease and portal hypertension.
  • Spider Naevi - Common in chronic liver disease, a secondary sign of liver dysfunction.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Oesophageal Varices"][/sc]
  • Profuse Haematemesis - Suggests active variceal bleeding, a medical emergency requiring immediate intervention.
  • Hypotension and Tachycardia - Indicative of hypovolaemic shock due to severe blood loss.
  • Confusion or Altered Mental State - May suggest hepatic encephalopathy or significant blood loss, indicating severe underlying liver disease.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Oesophageal Varices"][/sc]
  • Non-haemorrhagic symptoms - Fatigue, pallor, or mild anaemia due to chronic microbleeds may occur without a major bleeding episode.
  • Silent Anaemia - Gradual blood loss may present only as anaemia without obvious GI bleeding symptoms.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Oesophageal Varices"][/sc]
  • Endoscopy - Visualisation of varices; identifies high-risk varices (e.g., large size, red wale markings).
  • Full Blood Count (FBC) - May show anaemia (blood loss) or thrombocytopenia (portal hypertension-related splenic sequestration).
  • Liver Function Tests (LFTs) - Abnormal in cirrhosis; raised bilirubin, low albumin may indicate chronic liver disease.
  • Clotting Profile (INR) - Often elevated in liver disease, indicating synthetic dysfunction.
  • Ultrasound/Doppler - Assesses portal vein flow and liver architecture, identifying portal hypertension.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Oesophageal Varices"][/sc]
  • A 55-year-old man with a history of cirrhosis presents with sudden haematemesis, hypotension, and tachycardia. Endoscopy reveals large oesophageal varices.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Oesophageal Varices"][/sc]
  • Peptic Ulcer Disease - More likely to present with epigastric pain; endoscopy reveals gastric or duodenal ulcer, not oesophageal varices.
  • Mallory-Weiss Tear - Usually occurs after forceful vomiting; endoscopy shows mucosal tear at gastro-oesophageal junction, not varices.
  • Gastric Cancer - May cause chronic blood loss and anaemia; endoscopy reveals mass rather than varices.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Oesophageal Varices"][/sc]
  • Acute Management: Immediate resuscitation with IV fluids or blood transfusion; vasoactive drugs (e.g., terlipressin) to reduce portal pressure; urgent endoscopic variceal banding.
  • Prevention: Non-selective beta-blockers (e.g., propranolol) to reduce portal pressure in patients with known varices; regular endoscopic surveillance and banding as needed.
  • Liver Transplant Evaluation: Considered for patients with decompensated cirrhosis.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Oesophageal Varices"][/sc]
  • Variceal Bleeding - Common and life-threatening; often recurrent with high mortality.
  • Hepatic Encephalopathy - Resulting from severe liver dysfunction, worsened by bleeding-induced nitrogen load.
  • Hypovolaemic Shock - Due to severe blood loss, leading to multi-organ failure if not promptly managed.
  • Death - Mortality rates can be high, particularly in cases of recurrent bleeding and advanced liver disease.
