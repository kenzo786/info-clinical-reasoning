Hernia
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Hernia"][/sc]
  • A hernia is the abnormal protrusion of an organ or tissue through the wall of the cavity that normally contains it, most commonly occurring in the abdominal region where intra-abdominal contents push through a weakness or defect in the abdominal wall.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Hernia"][/sc]
  • Prevalence in the UK GP population: Hernias are relatively common, with approximately 1.7% of adults experiencing hernias, with higher rates in men.
  • UK hospital prevalence: Hernia repair is among the most common general surgical procedures, with an estimated 80,000 cases per year.
  • Worldwide prevalence: Global prevalence rates vary, but hernias are estimated to affect 4-5% of the population, with the highest rates in men and the elderly.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Hernia"][/sc]
  • Hernias occur when a weakness or defect in the muscle or connective tissue allows internal organs, typically parts of the intestine or fatty tissue, to push through.
  • Increased intra-abdominal pressure, often due to heavy lifting, chronic cough, obesity, or constipation, can contribute to hernia formation.
  • Inguinal hernias, the most common type, are related to weakness in the inguinal canal, often due to congenital or acquired factors.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Hernia"][/sc]
  • Gradual onset in many cases, as the hernia develops slowly due to weakened structures over time.
  • Symptoms may initially be intermittent and often increase with physical exertion, coughing, or straining.
  • If the hernia becomes incarcerated or strangulated, symptoms can rapidly progress to an emergency, with intense pain and risk of ischaemia.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Hernia"][/sc]
  • Painless or mild bulge in the groin or abdominal area - Often the first sign, resulting from organ protrusion through a weakness in the abdominal wall.
  • Intermittent pain or discomfort - Especially with physical exertion or straining, due to increased intra-abdominal pressure causing more protrusion.
  • Heaviness or dragging sensation - Due to the weight of the herniated contents pulling on surrounding tissues.
  • Sudden, intense pain - A possible sign of incarceration or strangulation, where blood flow is compromised, leading to tissue ischaemia.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Hernia"][/sc]
  • Visible or palpable bulge - Most common in the groin or abdominal area, seen on inspection or felt on palpation, indicative of a hernia sac with protruding contents.
  • Increased bulge with Valsalva manoeuvre - The bulge becomes more prominent when intra-abdominal pressure increases, supporting the presence of a hernia.
  • Non-reducible, tender bulge - A possible sign of incarceration or strangulation, where the hernia cannot be pushed back in, with associated tenderness.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Hernia"][/sc]
  • Severe, sudden pain - May indicate strangulation, where compromised blood flow risks tissue necrosis and ischaemia.
  • Irreducible bulge with systemic symptoms - Fever, tachycardia, and tenderness over the hernia site may signal incarceration with potential bowel obstruction or ischaemia.
  • Signs of bowel obstruction - Vomiting, abdominal distension, and constipation may indicate an obstructed hernia, a surgical emergency.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Hernia"][/sc]
  • Chronic groin pain without a palpable bulge - Can occur with small or early-stage hernias or in obese patients where the bulge may not be apparent.
  • Intermittent gastrointestinal symptoms - Nausea or bowel irregularities can sometimes be due to a subtle, reducible hernia affecting bowel loops.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Hernia"][/sc]
  • Physical Examination - Often sufficient; the bulge may increase with Valsalva manoeuvre, particularly useful in diagnosing inguinal hernias.
  • Ultrasound - Useful in detecting occult or difficult-to-palpate hernias, showing protruding tissue through a defect.
  • CT or MRI - Used for complex cases or recurrent hernias, providing detailed images of the hernia sac and surrounding anatomy.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Hernia"][/sc]
  • A 55-year-old male presents with a progressively enlarging groin bulge, mild discomfort exacerbated by lifting, and a sensation of heaviness in the area.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Hernia"][/sc]
  • Inguinal lymphadenopathy - Unlike hernias, lymph nodes do not typically enlarge with Valsalva and may be tender with infection.
  • Femoral aneurysm - A pulsatile mass near the femoral artery that does not change with pressure or position, unlike a reducible hernia.
  • Testicular torsion - Presents with acute scrotal pain, but no visible abdominal bulge or reducibility.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Hernia"][/sc]
  • Watchful waiting - In asymptomatic cases, particularly for inguinal hernias, though risk of complications should be discussed.
  • Surgical repair - Preferred for symptomatic hernias or those at risk of incarceration, with laparoscopic or open mesh repair recommended by NHS guidelines.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Hernia"][/sc]
  • Incarceration - Occurs in 10-20% of untreated cases, where the hernia cannot be reduced, increasing the risk of obstruction and strangulation.
  • Strangulation - A medical emergency, where blood flow is compromised, leading to tissue ischaemia and necrosis if untreated.
  • Recurrent hernia - Can occur post-repair in up to 10% of cases, depending on repair type and patient factors (e.g., smoking, obesity).
