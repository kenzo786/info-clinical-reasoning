Abdominal Pain - Appendicitis
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Abdominal Pain - Appendicitis"][/sc]
  • Appendicitis is the inflammation of the vermiform appendix, a tubular structure connected to the caecum. This condition typically results from obstruction of the appendix lumen, leading to bacterial overgrowth, inflammation, and subsequent tissue damage. It is a common cause of acute abdominal pain requiring urgent medical intervention.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Abdominal Pain - Appendicitis"][/sc]
  • In the UK, appendicitis accounts for approximately 40,000 cases per year, representing around 1.5% of all emergency surgical presentations.
  • Worldwide, appendicitis has an annual incidence rate of 86-100 per 100,000 people, with the highest incidence seen in individuals aged 10-30 years.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Abdominal Pain - Appendicitis"][/sc]
  • Obstruction of the appendix lumen by faecaliths, lymphoid hyperplasia, or foreign bodies triggers bacterial growth and localised infection.
  • The trapped bacteria cause mucosal injury and inflammation, leading to increased intraluminal pressure. This compresses blood vessels, causing ischaemia and further bacterial proliferation.
  • If untreated, the pressure may lead to rupture, releasing bacteria into the peritoneal cavity, resulting in peritonitis and potentially life-threatening sepsis.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Abdominal Pain - Appendicitis"][/sc]
  • Early Phase: Mild, poorly localised central abdominal pain with nausea.
  • Progressive Phase: Pain intensifies and localises to the right lower quadrant (McBurneyâ€™s point) over hours.
  • Late Phase: Untreated, may lead to appendix rupture within 24-72 hours of symptom onset, with possible progression to peritonitis.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Abdominal Pain - Appendicitis"][/sc]
  • Abdominal Pain: Typically starts as dull periumbilical pain that later localises to the right lower quadrant, reflecting irritation of the parietal peritoneum.
  • Nausea and Vomiting: Frequently accompanies abdominal pain, likely secondary to visceral pain and localised inflammatory mediators.
  • Anorexia: Loss of appetite is common, thought to be due to autonomic nervous system activation.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Abdominal Pain - Appendicitis"][/sc]
  • Localised Tenderness in Right Lower Quadrant: Specifically at McBurneyâ€™s point, due to inflamed peritoneum.
  • Rebound Tenderness: Pain worsens upon quick release of pressure, indicating peritoneal irritation.
  • Fever: Low-grade fever commonly occurs with inflammation; higher temperatures may suggest progression to peritonitis.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Abdominal Pain - Appendicitis"][/sc]
  • High Fever & Chills: Suggests progression to sepsis or abscess formation.
  • Severe Generalised Abdominal Pain: May indicate ruptured appendix with secondary peritonitis.
  • Tachycardia & Hypotension: Signs of septic shock due to peritoneal infection spread.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Abdominal Pain - Appendicitis"][/sc]
  • Retrocaecal Appendix: Pain may be more diffuse or localise to the right flank due to retroperitoneal position.
  • Pregnancy: Displacement of the appendix may cause right upper quadrant pain.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Abdominal Pain - Appendicitis"][/sc]
  • FBC: Elevated white blood cell count, particularly neutrophils, indicating inflammation.
  • Ultrasound: First-line imaging showing non-compressible, thickened appendix; preferred in children and pregnant women.
  • CT Scan: High sensitivity for visualising an inflamed or perforated appendix; often used in adults when diagnosis is unclear.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Abdominal Pain - Appendicitis"][/sc]
  • An 18-year-old male presents with 24-hour history of central abdominal pain that has localised to the right lower quadrant, with associated nausea and fever.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Abdominal Pain - Appendicitis"][/sc]
  • Gastroenteritis: Diffuse abdominal pain, commonly with diarrhoea, lacks the localised tenderness of appendicitis.
  • Mesenteric Adenitis: Presents with right lower quadrant pain, but typically in children with upper respiratory symptoms.
  • Diverticulitis: More common in older adults, usually presents with left lower quadrant pain.
  • Ovarian Torsion (females): Right lower quadrant pain, but often with sudden onset and adnexal tenderness.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Abdominal Pain - Appendicitis"][/sc]
  • Appendectomy: Gold-standard treatment, typically performed laparoscopically, to prevent progression to perforation.
  • Antibiotics: Preoperative broad-spectrum antibiotics (e.g., co-amoxiclav) to reduce infection risk.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Abdominal Pain - Appendicitis"][/sc]
  • Perforation: Occurs in approximately 20% of cases, leading to peritonitis and potential abscess formation.
  • Abscess Formation: Localised collection of pus, often requiring drainage, occurs if inflammation spreads to nearby structures.
  • Sepsis: Generalised infection may result if untreated appendicitis progresses to peritoneal infection.
