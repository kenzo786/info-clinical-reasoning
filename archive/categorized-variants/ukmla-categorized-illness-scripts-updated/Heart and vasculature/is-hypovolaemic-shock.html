Hypovolaemic Shock
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Hypovolaemic Shock"][/sc]
  • Hypovolaemic shock is a life-threatening condition caused by a significant decrease in intravascular volume, leading to inadequate tissue perfusion and oxygenation. It commonly results from severe fluid loss due to haemorrhage, dehydration, or burns, leading to a mismatch between oxygen delivery and tissue oxygen demand. Without prompt treatment, it can lead to organ failure and death.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Hypovolaemic Shock"][/sc]
  • In the UK, hypovolaemic shock is commonly seen in emergency and trauma care, particularly in cases of major trauma, gastrointestinal bleeding, or postoperative complications. It is relatively rare in primary care. The worldwide prevalence varies, with higher rates in regions with higher incidences of trauma, malnutrition, and infectious diseases causing diarrhoea. Global estimates suggest hypovolaemic shock contributes significantly to preventable deaths, particularly in resource-limited settings.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Hypovolaemic Shock"][/sc]
  • Hypovolaemic shock occurs due to a reduction in circulating blood volume. This leads to decreased venous return to the heart, reducing cardiac output (CO). The body compensates via activation of the sympathetic nervous system (SNS), resulting in vasoconstriction and increased heart rate. The renin-angiotensin-aldosterone system (RAAS) is also activated to retain sodium and water. However, when volume loss exceeds the compensatory capacity, tissue perfusion drops, leading to anaerobic metabolism, lactic acidosis, and multi-organ dysfunction.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Hypovolaemic Shock"][/sc]
  • Hypovolaemic shock can develop rapidly, especially in cases of acute haemorrhage, or more gradually in conditions like dehydration. The course progresses through four stages:
    • Initial stage: Mild volume loss with compensatory mechanisms maintaining perfusion.
    • Compensatory stage: Moderate volume loss, SNS activation increases heart rate and vasoconstriction.
    • Progressive stage: Significant volume loss, inadequate perfusion leads to lactic acidosis and organ dysfunction.
    • Irreversible stage: Severe organ damage, even with intervention, survival is unlikely.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Hypovolaemic Shock"][/sc]
  • Tachycardia - An early compensatory response to maintain cardiac output due to decreased circulating volume.
  • Thirst - Results from the bodyâ€™s attempt to increase fluid intake in response to volume depletion.
  • Lightheadedness or dizziness - Caused by decreased cerebral perfusion as blood volume drops.
  • Cold, clammy skin - Results from vasoconstriction as part of the sympathetic response.
  • Oliguria - Decreased urine output due to RAAS activation and ADH release to conserve fluid.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Hypovolaemic Shock"][/sc]
  • Tachypnoea - Increased respiratory rate occurs as the body attempts to compensate for metabolic acidosis due to hypoperfusion and lactic acid accumulation.
  • Hypotension - A hallmark sign of progressive shock, indicating inadequate cardiac output and perfusion.
  • Delayed capillary refill - Prolonged refill time (>2 seconds) due to peripheral vasoconstriction and reduced blood flow to the skin.
  • Altered mental status - Confusion or decreased consciousness due to cerebral hypoperfusion.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Hypovolaemic Shock"][/sc]
  • Severe hypotension (systolic BP < 90 mmHg) - Suggests decompensated shock with imminent organ failure.
  • Altered mental status or coma - Indicates severe cerebral hypoperfusion, often a late sign of advanced shock.
  • Absent or weak pulses - Reflects poor perfusion and the critical need for immediate intervention.
  • Respiratory failure - Can signal end-stage shock where acidosis and hypoxia are overwhelming compensatory mechanisms.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Hypovolaemic Shock"][/sc]
  • Normotensive hypovolaemic shock - In elderly or chronically hypertensive patients, baseline higher blood pressure may mask hypotension until shock is advanced.
  • Bradycardia - In hypothermia-related hypovolaemic shock, the bodyâ€™s compensatory responses may be blunted.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Hypovolaemic Shock"][/sc]
  • Full blood count (FBC) - May reveal haemoconcentration in dehydration or anaemia in haemorrhage.
  • Serum lactate - Elevated due to anaerobic metabolism, a marker of tissue hypoxia and shock severity.
  • Arterial blood gas (ABG) - Reveals metabolic acidosis (low pH, low bicarbonate), reflecting lactic acidosis.
  • Coagulation profile - Useful in haemorrhagic shock, where coagulopathy may be present.
  • Focused Assessment with Sonography for Trauma (FAST) - Can identify free fluid in cases of traumatic haemorrhage.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Hypovolaemic Shock"][/sc]
  • A 65-year-old male presents with acute onset weakness, dizziness, and confusion following profuse vomiting and diarrhoea. Examination shows hypotension, tachycardia, and cold, clammy extremities. Blood tests reveal elevated lactate and metabolic acidosis.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Hypovolaemic Shock"][/sc]
  • Cardiogenic shock - Distinguished by evidence of myocardial dysfunction (e.g., ECG changes, raised troponins).
  • Septic shock - Often associated with fever, elevated white cell count, and a source of infection.
  • Anaphylactic shock - Characterised by a history of allergen exposure, angio-oedema, and bronchospasm.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Hypovolaemic Shock"][/sc]
  • Fluid resuscitation - IV crystalloids (e.g., 0.9% saline or Hartmannâ€™s solution) are the first-line treatment to restore volume.
  • Blood transfusion - Indicated in haemorrhagic shock with significant blood loss.
  • Inotropic support - Noradrenaline may be used in cases of refractory hypotension after adequate volume resuscitation.
  • Treat the underlying cause - For example, surgery in trauma-related haemorrhage or antibiotics for septic shock.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Hypovolaemic Shock"][/sc]
  • Acute kidney injury - Due to prolonged hypoperfusion and ischaemia.
  • Multi-organ failure - Prolonged shock can lead to failure of vital organs such as the liver, lungs, and heart.
  • Death - Without timely intervention, hypovolaemic shock has a high mortality rate, particularly in its decompensated stage.
