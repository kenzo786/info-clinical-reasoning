Deep Vein Thrombosis (DVT)
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Deep Vein Thrombosis (DVT)"][/sc]
  • DVT is the formation of a blood clot (thrombus) in a deep vein, typically in the legs. It is part of the broader spectrum of venous thromboembolism (VTE), which includes both DVT and pulmonary embolism (PE). DVT can lead to complications like PE if the clot dislodges and travels to the lungs.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Deep Vein Thrombosis (DVT)"][/sc]
  • In the UK, the annual incidence of DVT is approximately 1-2 per 1,000 people in the general population.
  • Hospital prevalence is higher, with an incidence of up to 1 in 100 patients, particularly among those who are immobilised or post-surgical.
  • Globally, DVT incidence varies but is generally estimated to affect 1-2 per 1,000 individuals per year, with higher rates in Western countries.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Deep Vein Thrombosis (DVT)"][/sc]
  • DVT is primarily driven by Virchow's triad: venous stasis, endothelial injury, and hypercoagulability.
  • Venous stasis occurs when blood flow is slowed, often due to immobility, surgery, or prolonged sitting.
  • Endothelial injury triggers the coagulation cascade, leading to clot formation. This may occur due to trauma, surgery, or vascular inflammation.
  • Hypercoagulability can be inherited (e.g., factor V Leiden mutation) or acquired (e.g., cancer, oral contraceptives).
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Deep Vein Thrombosis (DVT)"][/sc]
  • Acute phase: DVT typically develops over hours to days, especially after triggering events like surgery or prolonged immobility.
  • Subacute phase: The clot stabilises over days to weeks; symptoms may improve or worsen depending on size and location of the thrombus.
  • Chronic phase: Untreated DVT can lead to long-term complications such as post-thrombotic syndrome, characterised by chronic leg swelling and pain.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Deep Vein Thrombosis (DVT)"][/sc]
  • Unilateral leg pain: Commonly due to venous obstruction and inflammatory mediators causing localised pain.
  • Swelling in one leg: Caused by impaired venous return leading to fluid accumulation (oedema).
  • Redness or warmth: Inflammatory response in the surrounding tissues due to the thrombus.
  • Asymptomatic: Around 50% of DVT cases may be clinically silent, with symptoms only manifesting after complications such as PE.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Deep Vein Thrombosis (DVT)"][/sc]
  • Calf tenderness: Indicative of local inflammation due to the presence of a thrombus in the deep veins.
  • Unilateral leg oedema: Caused by venous outflow obstruction leading to fluid accumulation.
  • Homan's sign: Pain on dorsiflexion of the foot, though it is not specific or highly sensitive for DVT.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Deep Vein Thrombosis (DVT)"][/sc]
  • Shortness of breath and chest pain: May indicate a pulmonary embolism (PE), a potentially life-threatening complication of DVT.
  • Severe leg pain with cyanosis: Suggests a rare complication called phlegmasia cerulea dolens, which can lead to venous gangrene if untreated.
  • Tachycardia, hypotension: These signs could be associated with a massive PE causing cardiovascular compromise.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Deep Vein Thrombosis (DVT)"][/sc]
  • Bilateral leg swelling: Rare but possible, especially in pelvic vein DVT, which may cause more widespread venous obstruction.
  • Upper extremity DVT: Rare and usually associated with central venous catheters or thoracic outlet syndrome.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Deep Vein Thrombosis (DVT)"][/sc]
  • D-dimer: Elevated in DVT due to fibrin degradation products; however, non-specific and can be elevated in other conditions.
  • Compression ultrasound: The gold standard in diagnosing DVT. Lack of vein compressibility is a key finding.
  • Venography: Rarely used but considered the definitive diagnostic test, showing a filling defect in the vein.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Deep Vein Thrombosis (DVT)"][/sc]
  • A 45-year-old male presents with 2 days of progressively worsening unilateral calf pain and swelling following a long-haul flight. There is tenderness on palpation and slight erythema over the affected area.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Deep Vein Thrombosis (DVT)"][/sc]
  • Cellulitis: More likely to present with diffuse erythema and warmth, typically not confined to a vein distribution.
  • Superficial thrombophlebitis: Localised pain and tenderness over a superficial vein, not involving deep veins.
  • Chronic venous insufficiency: Typically presents with long-standing leg swelling and skin changes rather than acute pain and tenderness.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Deep Vein Thrombosis (DVT)"][/sc]
  • Anticoagulation: Initial treatment with low molecular weight heparin (LMWH) or direct oral anticoagulants (DOACs), followed by long-term anticoagulation, typically for 3-6 months.
  • Compression stockings: May be recommended to prevent post-thrombotic syndrome.
  • Thrombolysis: Considered in massive DVTs or cases complicated by phlegmasia cerulea dolens.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Deep Vein Thrombosis (DVT)"][/sc]
  • Pulmonary embolism: Occurs in up to 50% of untreated DVTs, can be life-threatening.
  • Post-thrombotic syndrome: Chronic pain and swelling due to venous insufficiency; affects up to 50% of patients.
  • Phlegmasia cerulea dolens: Severe complication with high morbidity, involving venous gangrene due to extensive clotting and venous obstruction.
