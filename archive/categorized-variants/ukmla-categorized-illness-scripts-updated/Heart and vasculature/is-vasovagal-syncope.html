Vasovagal Syncope
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Vasovagal Syncope"][/sc]
  • Vasovagal syncope, also known as neurocardiogenic syncope, is a common cause of fainting triggered by a reflex response that causes a sudden drop in heart rate (bradycardia) and blood pressure (hypotension), leading to reduced cerebral perfusion and transient loss of consciousness. It is often precipitated by emotional stress, pain, prolonged standing, or heat exposure.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Vasovagal Syncope"][/sc]
  • In the UK, vasovagal syncope is the most common cause of fainting, accounting for approximately 50-60% of syncopal episodes presenting in primary care.
  • Hospital prevalence is lower as many episodes are not reported or do not require hospital admission.
  • Worldwide prevalence is estimated at 20-35% of the population experiencing at least one syncopal episode in their lifetime, with the highest incidence in adolescents and older adults.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Vasovagal Syncope"][/sc]
  • The underlying mechanism of vasovagal syncope involves a reflex triggered by emotional or physical stress. This reflex, via the autonomic nervous system, causes excessive parasympathetic (vagal) activity and reduced sympathetic tone.
  • This leads to vasodilation (decreased vascular resistance) and/or bradycardia, reducing cardiac output and blood pressure, causing transient global cerebral hypoperfusion, and resulting in syncope.
  • Typically, this is a self-limiting condition with rapid recovery of consciousness once lying down, restoring cerebral blood flow.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Vasovagal Syncope"][/sc]
  • Prodrome: Typically lasts seconds to minutes, with symptoms such as light-headedness, sweating, or nausea.
  • Syncopal episode: Lasts a few seconds to a minute, marked by loss of consciousness.
  • Postdrome: May include brief confusion or fatigue, but full recovery typically occurs within minutes.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Vasovagal Syncope"][/sc]
  • Light-headedness - A feeling of dizziness or faintness due to decreased cerebral perfusion during the prodromal phase.
  • Nausea - Often occurs as a result of autonomic activation (particularly vagal stimulation).
  • Sweating - Diaphoresis due to autonomic activation as part of the reflex response.
  • Blurring or tunnelling of vision - Visual symptoms result from diminished cerebral blood flow, particularly to the occipital lobe.
  • Loss of consciousness - Occurs when cerebral perfusion falls below a critical threshold, usually lasting for seconds.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Vasovagal Syncope"][/sc]
  • Pallor - A common finding due to peripheral vasodilation and reduced cardiac output.
  • Bradycardia - Slow heart rate is caused by excessive parasympathetic stimulation.
  • Hypotension - Low blood pressure due to vasodilation and reduced vascular resistance.
  • Rapid recovery upon lying down - Restores cerebral perfusion, leading to prompt recovery of consciousness.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Vasovagal Syncope"][/sc]
  • Syncope during exertion - May suggest a more serious cardiac cause, such as arrhythmia or structural heart disease.
  • Family history of sudden cardiac death - Raises suspicion of genetic conditions like long QT syndrome or hypertrophic cardiomyopathy.
  • Prolonged recovery time - Longer than typical post-syncopal confusion or delay in regaining consciousness could indicate neurological conditions such as seizures.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Vasovagal Syncope"][/sc]
  • No prodromal symptoms - Some patients may faint abruptly without warning, complicating diagnosis.
  • Prolonged syncope - Although rare, prolonged episodes might raise suspicion of other causes, including seizures or hypoglycaemia.
  • Recurrent episodes in elderly patients - More difficult to attribute solely to vasovagal syncope, requiring evaluation for other causes like orthostatic hypotension or cardiac arrhythmias.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Vasovagal Syncope"][/sc]
  • Orthostatic vital signs - To rule out orthostatic hypotension, a significant drop in blood pressure on standing may suggest other causes of syncope.
  • ECG - Normal findings in vasovagal syncope; used to exclude cardiac causes (e.g., arrhythmias).
  • Head-up tilt test - Provokes a syncopal episode in a controlled environment to confirm vasovagal syncope diagnosis.
  • Echocardiogram - Normal in vasovagal syncope but indicated if there is suspicion of structural heart disease.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Vasovagal Syncope"][/sc]
  • A 22-year-old woman experiences a brief loss of consciousness after standing in a crowded room, preceded by light-headedness and sweating, with rapid recovery upon lying down. She has a history of similar episodes triggered by stress.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Vasovagal Syncope"][/sc]
  • Cardiac syncope - Sudden onset without prodrome, often during exertion, requires ECG and echocardiogram to rule out arrhythmia or structural heart disease.
  • Seizures - Typically associated with postictal confusion and tonic-clonic movements, differentiate using EEG.
  • Orthostatic hypotension - Syncope triggered by standing with sustained hypotension; differentiate using orthostatic vital signs.
  • Hypoglycaemia - May present with confusion, tremors, and sweating; confirm with blood glucose levels.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Vasovagal Syncope"][/sc]
  • Lifestyle modification - Advise avoiding triggers (e.g., prolonged standing, dehydration), adequate hydration, and recognising prodromal symptoms.
  • Physical counterpressure manoeuvres - Techniques like leg crossing and muscle tensing during prodrome can prevent progression to syncope.
  • Pharmacological treatment - In recurrent, severe cases, medications like midodrine (alpha agonist) or fludrocortisone may be used to increase blood pressure.
  • Pacemaker - Rarely indicated, only for patients with significant bradycardia and recurrent syncope unresponsive to other measures.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Vasovagal Syncope"][/sc]
  • Physical injury from falls - The most common complication, including head trauma or fractures from abrupt syncope episodes.
  • Psychosocial impact - Anxiety about recurrent episodes may lead to avoidance of social or occupational situations.
  • Rare complications - In extreme cases, prolonged asystole or cardiac arrest may occur, but these are exceedingly rare in vasovagal syncope.
