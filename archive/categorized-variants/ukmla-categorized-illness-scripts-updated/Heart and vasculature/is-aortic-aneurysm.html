Aortic Aneurysm
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Aortic Aneurysm"][/sc]
  • An aortic aneurysm is a pathological dilation of the aorta, typically greater than 1.5 times its normal diameter. Aneurysms can occur in any part of the aorta, but the most common forms are abdominal aortic aneurysm (AAA) and thoracic aortic aneurysm (TAA). Aneurysms are often asymptomatic until they rupture, leading to life-threatening haemorrhage.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Aortic Aneurysm"][/sc]
  • Prevalence of abdominal aortic aneurysms in the UK general population is around 1-2% in men over 65. It is more common in men, with a male-to-female ratio of approximately 4:1. In hospital settings, AAAs are identified in 3-5% of males aged 65-75. Globally, AAAs are less common in women and in populations from Asia and Africa.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Aortic Aneurysm"][/sc]
  • The development of an aortic aneurysm results from a combination of genetic, environmental, and mechanical factors. Chronic inflammation and degeneration of the aortic wallâ€™s elastic fibres lead to a weakened vessel wall. This is typically due to risk factors such as atherosclerosis, hypertension, and smoking, which promote mechanical stress and inflammatory changes. As the vessel wall weakens, the aorta dilates, creating an aneurysm. Over time, this dilation increases the risk of rupture due to high-pressure blood flow, especially if the aneurysm exceeds 5.5 cm in diameter.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Aortic Aneurysm"][/sc]
  • Aortic aneurysms typically develop and enlarge slowly over many years, often asymptomatically. Small aneurysms (<5.5 cm) may remain stable for years, while larger aneurysms grow more rapidly and are at higher risk of rupture. The average rate of growth for AAA is 2-3 mm/year. Rupture, when it occurs, is often sudden and catastrophic.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Aortic Aneurysm"][/sc]
  • Asymptomatic - Most aortic aneurysms are asymptomatic and detected incidentally during imaging for unrelated reasons.
  • Abdominal pain (AAA) - Diffuse or focal, often in the lower back or flank. The pain arises from the mechanical stretching of the aneurysm wall and local inflammation.
  • Chest pain (TAA) - Common with thoracic aneurysms. Pain is often described as deep, constant, and may radiate to the back. It reflects the expanding aneurysm pressing on nearby structures.
  • Pulsatile abdominal mass (AAA) - Sometimes described by patients, this may be felt as a throbbing sensation in the abdomen.
  • Hoarseness (TAA) - Pressure on the recurrent laryngeal nerve can cause a change in voice.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Aortic Aneurysm"][/sc]
  • Pulsatile abdominal mass (AAA) - Palpable pulsation in the abdomen, typically above the umbilicus. This reflects the large, dilated aorta.
  • Hypotension (ruptured AAA) - Due to massive internal haemorrhage, a drop in blood pressure is a sign of aneurysmal rupture.
  • Grey Turnerâ€™s sign - Flank ecchymosis indicating retroperitoneal haemorrhage from a ruptured AAA.
  • Distal embolisation - Signs of emboli (e.g., blue toes or sudden limb ischaemia) may arise from thrombus in the aneurysm sac breaking off and travelling downstream.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Aortic Aneurysm"][/sc]
  • Severe, sudden abdominal or back pain - Suggests impending or actual rupture of an AAA, an emergency condition requiring immediate intervention.
  • Hypotension with abdominal pain - Indicates aneurysmal rupture, leading to massive haemorrhage and shock.
  • Syncope - May indicate severe blood loss from rupture.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Aortic Aneurysm"][/sc]
  • Renal colic-like pain - A leaking AAA can irritate the retroperitoneum, mimicking symptoms of kidney stones.
  • Heart failure - A large thoracic aneurysm may compress the heart or great vessels, leading to symptoms resembling heart failure.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Aortic Aneurysm"][/sc]
  • Abdominal Ultrasound (AAA) - Initial screening test. A dilated aorta â‰¥3 cm confirms the presence of an aneurysm.
  • CT Angiography (CTA) - Provides detailed imaging of the aneurysm size, extent, and relation to surrounding structures. It is used preoperatively and in emergencies.
  • X-ray (TAA) - May show widened mediastinum or abnormal aortic contour in thoracic aneurysms.
  • Magnetic Resonance Angiography (MRA) - Useful in evaluating thoracic aneurysms in patients who cannot undergo CT angiography.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Aortic Aneurysm"][/sc]
  • A 70-year-old male presents with sudden-onset severe back pain and hypotension. He has a known 5.5 cm abdominal aortic aneurysm, and his symptoms are consistent with a ruptured aneurysm.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Aortic Aneurysm"][/sc]
  • Renal colic - May present with flank pain, but lacks the pulsatile mass or cardiovascular collapse seen with AAA rupture.
  • Acute myocardial infarction - Can cause severe chest or abdominal pain, but does not typically feature the pulsatile abdominal mass of an aneurysm.
  • Gastrointestinal perforation - Presents with acute abdomen and hypotension but no pulsatile mass or back pain radiating from the aorta.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Aortic Aneurysm"][/sc]
  • Surveillance - For small AAAs (<5.5 cm), regular monitoring with ultrasound every 6-12 months is recommended.
  • Elective repair - Indicated for AAAs â‰¥5.5 cm or rapidly enlarging aneurysms. Options include endovascular aneurysm repair (EVAR) or open surgical repair.
  • Emergency surgery - For ruptured aneurysms, emergency EVAR or open repair is required to control bleeding.
  • Blood pressure control - Tight control of hypertension with beta-blockers or other antihypertensives can slow aneurysm growth.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Aortic Aneurysm"][/sc]
  • Rupture - The most severe complication, with high mortality rates (up to 80%). It leads to massive internal haemorrhage.
  • Thromboembolism - Clots within the aneurysm can break off and lead to distal embolic events, such as stroke or limb ischaemia.
  • Infection - Rare but can occur, particularly with prosthetic grafts used in repair. Known as mycotic aneurysm.
