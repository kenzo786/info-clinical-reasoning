Corneal Ulcer
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Corneal Ulcer"][/sc]
  • A corneal ulcer is a full-thickness epithelial defect of the cornea accompanied by underlying stromal inflammation. It is often caused by infection (bacterial, viral, fungal, or protozoal), trauma, or immune-mediated processes.
  • It is a medical emergency that can lead to significant visual impairment or blindness if untreated.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Corneal Ulcer"][/sc]
  • UK GP prevalence: Relatively uncommon; seen in approximately 3-5 cases per 100,000 population annually.
  • UK hospital prevalence: More frequently managed in tertiary care or ophthalmology units, particularly in urban centres.
  • Worldwide prevalence: Higher in developing countries due to limited access to healthcare, with rates up to 800 cases per 100,000 population annually in rural regions with high rates of agricultural eye injuries.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Corneal Ulcer"][/sc]
  • Initial injury to the corneal epithelium (e.g., trauma, contact lens use) allows microorganisms to penetrate.
  • Infective agents trigger a local immune response, causing inflammation of the corneal stroma.
  • Enzymatic degradation by neutrophils and microbial toxins leads to stromal thinning and necrosis.
  • Corneal oedema, hypopyon formation, and neovascularisation can develop as part of the inflammatory response.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Corneal Ulcer"][/sc]
  • Acute onset: Symptoms typically develop within 24-48 hours of epithelial damage.
  • Progressive phase: Corneal thinning and ulceration may worsen over days without intervention.
  • Resolution phase: With treatment, symptoms improve over weeks, though scarring and visual deficits may persist.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Corneal Ulcer"][/sc]
  • Severe eye pain - Due to exposure of corneal nerves and inflammatory mediators.
  • Redness - Caused by hyperaemia of conjunctival and episcleral vessels.
  • Photophobia - Increased sensitivity to light due to inflammation affecting corneal transparency.
  • Blurred vision - Corneal oedema and ulceration disrupt light refraction.
  • Foreign body sensation - Resulting from epithelial damage.
  • Discharge - Purulent or mucopurulent discharge, particularly with bacterial infections.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Corneal Ulcer"][/sc]
  • Corneal opacity - Stromal infiltration and necrosis lead to a visible white or grey opacity.
  • Hypopyon - Layering of white cells in the anterior chamber due to severe inflammation.
  • Conjunctival injection - Engorgement of conjunctival blood vessels near the limbus.
  • Corneal ulceration on slit-lamp examination - Visible epithelial defect with fluorescein staining.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Corneal Ulcer"][/sc]
  • Hypopyon - Indicates severe infection and risk of corneal perforation.
  • Rapidly worsening symptoms - Suggest aggressive microbial pathogens (e.g., Pseudomonas aeruginosa).
  • Corneal perforation - Can lead to endophthalmitis or permanent blindness.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Corneal Ulcer"][/sc]
  • Minimal pain in neurotrophic keratitis - Reduced corneal sensation masks symptoms.
  • Slow progression in fungal infections - May delay recognition and treatment.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Corneal Ulcer"][/sc]
  • Slit-lamp examination with fluorescein staining - Reveals epithelial defects and stromal involvement.
  • Corneal scraping for microbiological culture - Identifies causative organisms and guides antimicrobial therapy.
  • Anterior chamber tap - Performed if endophthalmitis is suspected.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Corneal Ulcer"][/sc]
  • A 35-year-old contact lens wearer presents with acute onset of severe left eye pain, redness, blurred vision, and photophobia for 24 hours.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Corneal Ulcer"][/sc]
  • Conjunctivitis - Differentiated by lack of significant pain and absence of corneal involvement.
  • Herpes simplex keratitis - Dendritic ulcer pattern seen on fluorescein staining.
  • Acute anterior uveitis - Associated with photophobia and hypopyon but lacks epithelial defects.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Corneal Ulcer"][/sc]
  • Broad-spectrum topical antibiotics - Initially, e.g., ofloxacin or fortified cefuroxime and gentamicin drops.
  • Antifungal drops - If fungal keratitis is suspected.
  • Antiviral therapy - For herpes simplex keratitis.
  • Cycloplegic agents - To relieve pain from ciliary spasm.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Corneal Ulcer"][/sc]
  • Corneal scarring - Permanent visual impairment.
  • Perforation - Leads to endophthalmitis or need for emergency surgery.
  • Glaucoma - Due to increased intraocular pressure from chronic inflammation.
