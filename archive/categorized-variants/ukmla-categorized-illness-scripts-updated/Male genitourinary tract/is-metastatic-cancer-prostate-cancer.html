Metastatic Cancer (e.g., Prostate Cancer)
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Metastatic Cancer (e.g., Prostate Cancer)"][/sc]
  • Metastatic cancer refers to cancer that has spread from its primary site to distant organs or tissues. In metastatic prostate cancer, the cancer typically spreads to bones, lymph nodes, or less commonly, visceral organs like the liver or lungs. The condition is characterised by the proliferation of malignant cells in secondary locations, causing systemic symptoms and organ dysfunction.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Metastatic Cancer (e.g., Prostate Cancer)"][/sc]
  • Prevalence in the UK GP population: Prostate cancer is the most common cancer in men, with metastatic disease present at diagnosis in approximately 15-20% of cases.
  • UK hospital prevalence: Around 30-40% of prostate cancer cases progress to metastatic disease during the course of the illness.
  • Worldwide prevalence: Prostate cancer is the second most common cancer in men globally, with an estimated 10-20% presenting with metastatic disease.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Metastatic Cancer (e.g., Prostate Cancer)"][/sc]
  • Prostate cancer cells acquire mutations enabling them to detach from the primary tumour.
  • Cells invade local tissues and blood/lymphatic vessels via epithelial-to-mesenchymal transition.
  • Once in circulation, cells evade immune surveillance and adhere to endothelial cells at distant sites.
  • Prostate cancer has a tropism for bone, where metastatic cells disrupt normal bone remodelling, causing osteoblastic lesions.
  • Other mechanisms include angiogenesis and the alteration of local tissue environments to support tumour growth.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Metastatic Cancer (e.g., Prostate Cancer)"][/sc]
  • Early stage: Often asymptomatic or mild symptoms confined to local disease.
  • Progression: Symptoms of bone pain, systemic effects, or organ-specific dysfunction emerge as metastases develop.
  • Advanced stage: Debilitating symptoms, significant organ dysfunction, and complications such as pathological fractures or spinal cord compression.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Metastatic Cancer (e.g., Prostate Cancer)"][/sc]
  • Bone pain - Commonly in the back, pelvis, or hips due to osteoblastic activity in bone metastases disrupting normal bone architecture.
  • Fatigue - Often a result of anaemia or systemic inflammation caused by tumour activity.
  • Weight loss - Associated with cancer cachexia, driven by systemic metabolic derangements and tumour burden.
  • Haematuria - May occur if the urinary system is affected by local tumour invasion.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Metastatic Cancer (e.g., Prostate Cancer)"][/sc]
  • Pallor - Indicative of anaemia due to chronic disease or bone marrow infiltration.
  • Pathological fractures - Suggestive of weakened bone from metastases disrupting normal bone integrity.
  • Lymphadenopathy - Enlarged nodes often reflect metastatic spread to pelvic or retroperitoneal lymph nodes.
  • Spinal cord compression signs - Neurological deficits such as weakness or incontinence due to epidural metastases.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Metastatic Cancer (e.g., Prostate Cancer)"][/sc]
  • Severe bone pain with neurological symptoms - May signify spinal cord compression, requiring urgent intervention to prevent paralysis.
  • Hypercalcaemia - Symptoms such as confusion, polyuria, and constipation indicate significant skeletal involvement and require immediate correction.
  • Significant weight loss - Suggestive of advanced disease or systemic cachexia.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Metastatic Cancer (e.g., Prostate Cancer)"][/sc]
  • Isolated lymphadenopathy - May be misdiagnosed as lymphoma in the absence of bone symptoms.
  • Visceral metastases - Rarely, patients may present with symptoms of liver or lung involvement without bone lesions.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Metastatic Cancer (e.g., Prostate Cancer)"][/sc]
  • Prostate-specific antigen (PSA) - Elevated in metastatic prostate cancer; levels above 100 ng/mL are often suggestive of advanced disease.
  • Bone scintigraphy - Detects osteoblastic activity, identifying skeletal metastases.
  • MRI - Essential for identifying spinal cord compression or pelvic disease.
  • CT scan - Useful for detecting visceral metastases.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Metastatic Cancer (e.g., Prostate Cancer)"][/sc]
  • A 68-year-old man presents with progressive lower back pain and unintentional weight loss over six months. Examination reveals pallor and bony tenderness in the lumbar spine. PSA is elevated at 120 ng/mL, and bone scintigraphy confirms widespread skeletal metastases.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Metastatic Cancer (e.g., Prostate Cancer)"][/sc]
  • Multiple myeloma - Differentiated by lytic bone lesions, hypercalcaemia, and monoclonal protein on serum electrophoresis.
  • Benign prostatic hyperplasia - Lacks systemic symptoms and metastases; PSA levels are typically modestly elevated.
  • Renal cell carcinoma - May mimic metastatic prostate cancer but often presents with haematuria and renal masses on imaging.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Metastatic Cancer (e.g., Prostate Cancer)"][/sc]
  • Androgen deprivation therapy (ADT) - First-line treatment to reduce testosterone levels driving cancer growth.
  • Docetaxel chemotherapy - Often combined with ADT for high-volume metastatic disease.
  • Bone-targeted therapies - Bisphosphonates or denosumab to prevent skeletal-related complications.
  • Radiotherapy - Palliative for symptomatic bone metastases or spinal cord compression.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Metastatic Cancer (e.g., Prostate Cancer)"][/sc]
  • Pathological fractures - Common due to weakened bones from metastases.
  • Spinal cord compression - Can cause paralysis if untreated.
  • Renal failure - Secondary to obstructive uropathy or hypercalcaemia.
