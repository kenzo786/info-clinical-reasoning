Stye (Hordeolum)
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Stye (Hordeolum)"][/sc]
  • A stye (hordeolum) is a localised, acute bacterial infection or inflammation of the sebaceous glands of the eyelid, typically caused by Staphylococcus aureus. It may involve the external (Zeis or Moll glands) or internal (Meibomian glands) eyelid structures.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Stye (Hordeolum)"][/sc]
  • Prevalence in UK GP population: Estimated at 5â€“10% of patients presenting with eyelid complaints annually.
  • UK hospital prevalence: Rarely requires hospitalisation; most cases are managed in primary care.
  • Worldwide prevalence: Common, affecting all age groups, with no significant geographic or demographic predilection.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Stye (Hordeolum)"][/sc]
  • Blockage of the sebaceous glands creates a favourable environment for bacterial overgrowth, typically by Staphylococcus aureus.
  • Inflammation ensues, resulting in redness, swelling, and pain.
  • If untreated, infection may spread to surrounding tissue, leading to preseptal cellulitis or abscess formation.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Stye (Hordeolum)"][/sc]
  • Acute onset: Develops rapidly over 1â€“2 days.
  • Resolution: Typically resolves spontaneously within 7â€“10 days with conservative management. Larger lesions may persist longer or require intervention.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Stye (Hordeolum)"][/sc]
  • Localised eyelid pain: Due to inflammation and pressure from the swelling.
  • Swelling: Caused by accumulation of inflammatory exudates in the glandular tissue.
  • Redness: Secondary to increased blood flow from inflammation.
  • Foreign body sensation: Irritation from the swelling impinging on adjacent structures.
  • Watery eyes: Reflex lacrimation in response to irritation.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Stye (Hordeolum)"][/sc]
  • Focal swelling: Localised, tender lump on the eyelid margin (external stye) or beneath the lid surface (internal stye).
  • Erythema: Localised redness due to vascular dilation and inflammation.
  • Fluctuance: Suggests pus collection, particularly in larger lesions.
  • Crusting: May occur if the lesion drains spontaneously.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Stye (Hordeolum)"][/sc]
  • Periorbital swelling: May indicate spread to surrounding tissues, raising concern for preseptal or orbital cellulitis.
  • Fever: Suggests systemic involvement or severe infection.
  • Visual disturbance: Indicates possible compression or inflammation of ocular structures, requiring urgent evaluation.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Stye (Hordeolum)"][/sc]
  • Chronic or recurrent styes: May indicate underlying conditions like blepharitis or rosacea.
  • Painless swelling: Suggestive of a chalazion rather than a stye.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Stye (Hordeolum)"][/sc]
  • Clinical examination: Diagnosis is typically clinical, based on characteristic symptoms and signs.
  • Microbial culture: Reserved for recurrent or non-resolving cases, particularly to identify resistant organisms.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Stye (Hordeolum)"][/sc]
  • A 35-year-old woman presents with a 3-day history of localised redness, pain, and swelling on the upper eyelid. No systemic symptoms or visual changes are reported.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Stye (Hordeolum)"][/sc]
  • Chalazion: Chronic, painless swelling without acute inflammation.
  • Preseptal cellulitis: Diffuse swelling and erythema without a focal lesion; systemic symptoms may be present.
  • Orbital cellulitis: Painful proptosis, ophthalmoplegia, and systemic symptoms.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Stye (Hordeolum)"][/sc]
  • Conservative: Warm compresses 3â€“4 times daily to promote drainage.
  • Topical antibiotics: Chloramphenicol ointment may be used if there is concern for secondary bacterial infection.
  • Incision and drainage: For persistent or large styes unresponsive to conservative measures.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Stye (Hordeolum)"][/sc]
  • Preseptal cellulitis: Spread of infection to surrounding eyelid tissues, requiring systemic antibiotics.
  • Chronic chalazion: Resulting from unresolved blockage.
