Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="HirschsprungГўв‚¬в„ўs Disease (Children)"][/sc]
  • Developmental Defect: HirschsprungГўв‚¬в„ўs disease arises due to a failure of neural crest cells to migrate to the distal bowel during embryonic development, resulting in aganglionosis.
  • Absence of Ganglion Cells: The aganglionic segment cannot perform peristalsis, leading to functional bowel obstruction in the affected area.
  • Consequences: Accumulation of faecal material and dilatation of the proximal bowel (megacolon) due to an inability to move stool through the affected segment.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="HirschsprungГўв‚¬в„ўs Disease (Children)"][/sc]
  • Neonatal Period: Symptoms typically appear within 48 hours of birth, with delayed meconium passage and abdominal distention.
  • Infancy: If undiagnosed, persistent constipation, vomiting, and feeding difficulties may develop.
  • Childhood: Recurrent episodes of constipation and potential enterocolitis in severe cases.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="HirschsprungГўв‚¬в„ўs Disease (Children)"][/sc]
  • Delayed passage of meconium (>48 hours after birth) - A classic sign due to failure of peristalsis in the aganglionic bowel segment.
  • Chronic constipation - Caused by obstruction in the affected bowel area, leading to difficulty passing stool.
  • Abdominal distention - Accumulation of faecal material and gas proximal to the obstruction.
  • Vomiting - May occur due to the severity of obstruction and abdominal pressure.
  • Poor feeding and failure to thrive - Reduced nutrient intake and malabsorption in chronic cases.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="HirschsprungГўв‚¬в„ўs Disease (Children)"][/sc]
  • Abdominal distention - Swollen abdomen due to trapped faeces and gas in the bowel.
  • Palpable mass of faeces in the abdomen - Stool accumulation proximal to the aganglionic segment.
  • Explosive expulsion of stool on rectal examination - Occurs when digital stimulation relieves a temporary obstruction.
  • Poor growth - Due to malnutrition from chronic obstruction and inadequate nutrient absorption.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="HirschsprungГўв‚¬в„ўs Disease (Children)"][/sc]
  • Enterocolitis - Severe abdominal pain, diarrhoea, fever; can indicate Hirschsprung-associated enterocolitis (HAEC), a life-threatening condition due to bacterial overgrowth and bowel inflammation.
  • Sepsis - Possible in severe cases of HAEC, requiring prompt medical intervention to avoid systemic complications.
  • Failure to thrive - Signifies chronic malabsorption and possible nutritional deficiencies.

  • Late-onset HirschsprungГўв‚¬в„ўs disease - Can present with chronic constipation in older children without neonatal symptoms; pathophysiology involves less extensive aganglionosis.
  • Intermittent diarrhoea - Due to proximal overflow of faecal material, mimicking other gastrointestinal disorders.

  • Contrast enema: Shows a transition zone between the dilated proximal bowel and the narrow distal segment.

Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="HirschsprungГўв‚¬в„ўs Disease (Children)"][/sc]
  • Meconium ileus: Often associated with cystic fibrosis, characterised by thickened meconium causing obstruction.
  • Intestinal atresia: Congenital malformation where segments of the intestine are missing; no transition zone on imaging.
  • Chronic constipation due to functional causes: No aganglionosis or transition zone; responds to conservative measures.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="HirschsprungГўв‚¬в„ўs Disease (Children)"][/sc]
  • Surgical resection: Removal of the aganglionic segment is the definitive treatment, often through a pull-through procedure.
  • Preoperative management: Includes decompression, antibiotics for HAEC, and close monitoring for signs of enterocolitis.
  • Postoperative care: Nutritional support and monitoring for complications such as stricture or bowel dysfunction.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="HirschsprungГўв‚¬в„ўs Disease (Children)"][/sc]
  • Hirschsprung-associated enterocolitis (HAEC): Occurs in 30-50% of cases; can lead to bowel perforation and sepsis.
  • Strictures: Fibrotic narrowing of the bowel segment post-surgery; may require further intervention.
  • Incontinence and constipation: Persistent bowel dysfunction post-surgery in some children.
