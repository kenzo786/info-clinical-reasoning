Cervicitis
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Cervicitis"][/sc]
  • Cervicitis refers to inflammation of the cervix, most commonly caused by infection. The condition can be acute or chronic, with infectious agents such as Chlamydia trachomatis and Neisseria gonorrhoeae being typical causes. It can also occur due to non-infectious irritants, including chemicals and allergies. Key features include vaginal discharge, intermenstrual or postcoital bleeding, and often presents asymptomatically in many women.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Cervicitis"][/sc]
  • Prevalence (UK GP population): Cervicitis is commonly seen in sexually active women, particularly those aged 15-24. Chlamydia-associated cervicitis affects approximately 3-5% of sexually active women in the UK.
  • Prevalence (UK hospital): Hospital admissions due to cervicitis are uncommon, as the condition is usually managed in primary care. However, complications such as pelvic inflammatory disease (PID) may lead to hospitalisation.
  • Worldwide prevalence: Cervicitis, particularly from Chlamydia and gonorrhoea, is a major global issue, with an estimated 130 million new cases of Chlamydia and 87 million new cases of gonorrhoea annually worldwide.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Cervicitis"][/sc]
  • Cervicitis occurs when the mucosal lining of the cervix becomes inflamed. Infectious agents such as Chlamydia trachomatis or Neisseria gonorrhoeae trigger an immune response, leading to increased blood flow and immune cell infiltration. This results in the breakdown of the mucosal barrier, increased mucus production, and tissue damage. Non-infectious causes, such as irritants from spermicides, can cause a similar inflammatory response, though it tends to be less severe.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Cervicitis"][/sc]
  • Acute cervicitis: Symptoms develop within days to weeks following exposure to an infectious agent.
  • Chronic cervicitis: Can persist for months or even years if untreated, often with fewer symptoms than in the acute phase.
  • Resolution: Typically, with appropriate antibiotic treatment, symptoms resolve within 7-10 days. However, untreated cervicitis can lead to complications such as PID.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Cervicitis"][/sc]
  • Vaginal discharge - Increased or abnormal discharge is common due to the breakdown of the mucosal barrier and increased mucous production. Often yellow or purulent in infectious cases.
  • Intermenstrual or postcoital bleeding - Inflammation leads to friability of the cervical tissue, making it prone to bleeding with minor trauma, such as during intercourse.
  • Pelvic pain - In cases of chronic inflammation or ascending infection, pain may be experienced, reflecting deeper involvement of reproductive structures.
  • Dysuria - If the infection extends to the urethra, patients may experience painful urination.
  • Asymptomatic - Many women, especially with Chlamydia-associated cervicitis, present without noticeable symptoms, which makes regular screening important.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Cervicitis"][/sc]
  • Cervical discharge - Observed during a speculum examination; may appear purulent or mucopurulent in bacterial cervicitis.
  • Cervical erythema - The cervix appears inflamed and red due to increased vascularity and immune response.
  • Cervical tenderness - Palpation of the cervix during a bimanual exam may elicit pain, indicating localised inflammation.
  • Cervical friability - The cervix may bleed easily upon contact, a sign of tissue fragility due to inflammation.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Cervicitis"][/sc]
  • Severe pelvic pain - May indicate progression to pelvic inflammatory disease (PID) with involvement of the upper genital tract.
  • Fever - Suggests systemic infection or severe localised infection, potentially requiring urgent treatment.
  • Lower abdominal tenderness - Can signify the spread of infection to the uterus or fallopian tubes, leading to PID.
  • Adnexal tenderness - Suggests the involvement of the fallopian tubes and ovaries, which could indicate PID or tubo-ovarian abscess.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Cervicitis"][/sc]
  • Asymptomatic - Cervicitis, especially from Chlamydia, may show no symptoms. The infection remains undetected without regular screening.
  • Mild urinary symptoms - Some patients may only have symptoms like dysuria or urinary frequency, which could be mistaken for a urinary tract infection.
  • Isolated postcoital bleeding - In some cases, postcoital bleeding may be the only symptom, leading to a delay in recognising underlying cervicitis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Cervicitis"][/sc]
  • NAAT (Nucleic Acid Amplification Test) - A high-sensitivity test for detecting Chlamydia trachomatis and Neisseria gonorrhoeae from cervical swabs or urine. Positive findings confirm infection.
  • Microscopy - A wet mount can show increased white blood cells (WBCs) suggestive of infection.
  • Cervical swab culture - Useful for identifying Neisseria gonorrhoeae, but NAAT is preferred due to higher sensitivity.
  • pH test - The vaginal pH may be elevated in bacterial infections, helping to differentiate from other causes of vaginal discharge.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Cervicitis"][/sc]
  • A 22-year-old sexually active woman presents with a 2-week history of yellow vaginal discharge, mild pelvic discomfort, and occasional postcoital bleeding. She denies dysuria or systemic symptoms.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Cervicitis"][/sc]
  • Pelvic inflammatory disease (PID) - More severe pelvic pain, fever, and adnexal tenderness are typical. PID involves the upper genital tract, while cervicitis is more localised.
  • Bacterial vaginosis - Vaginal discharge with a fishy odour, often without significant inflammation or cervical tenderness.
  • Vaginal candidiasis - Thick, white discharge with itching, but no associated cervical inflammation or pain.
  • Urinary tract infection (UTI) - Dysuria without vaginal discharge or bleeding, and urine culture will help differentiate.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Cervicitis"][/sc]
  • Chlamydia trachomatis - Doxycycline 100mg twice daily for 7 days (first-line UK treatment).
  • Neisseria gonorrhoeae - Ceftriaxone 1g intramuscularly as a single dose, often combined with doxycycline for Chlamydia co-infection.
  • Symptom management - Analgesia for pelvic discomfort and avoidance of sexual intercourse during treatment to prevent transmission.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Cervicitis"][/sc]
  • Pelvic inflammatory disease (PID) - Untreated cervicitis can ascend, leading to PID in up to 30% of cases.
  • Infertility - Chronic infection and scarring of the fallopian tubes may lead to infertility, especially after repeated infections.
  • Chronic pelvic pain - Persistent inflammation can result in long-term pelvic pain.
  • Ectopic pregnancy - Tubal damage from chronic inflammation increases the risk of ectopic pregnancies.
