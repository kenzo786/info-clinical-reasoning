Viral Induced Wheeze
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Viral Induced Wheeze"][/sc]
  • Viral-induced wheeze is a condition characterised by episodic wheezing and airway obstruction triggered by viral respiratory infections, typically in children under five years of age. Unlike asthma, the symptoms are not persistent and are directly linked to viral triggers without significant atopy or allergic sensitisation.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Viral Induced Wheeze"][/sc]
  • Prevalence in UK general practice: Around 20% of children under five may experience viral-induced wheeze episodes annually.
  • UK hospital prevalence: Accounts for 30-50% of paediatric admissions for wheezing-related respiratory illness.
  • Worldwide prevalence: Variable, but similar rates in high-income countries; often underestimated in low-resource settings due to diagnostic limitations.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Viral Induced Wheeze"][/sc]
  • Viral infection (commonly RSV, rhinovirus, or parainfluenza) triggers airway inflammation.
  • Inflammation leads to hyper-responsiveness, mucus hypersecretion, and oedema, causing airway narrowing.
  • Small airways in young children contribute to increased airflow resistance during exhalation, resulting in wheezing.
  • Recovery is typically rapid after infection resolves, as airway structure and function return to baseline.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Viral Induced Wheeze"][/sc]
  • Acute onset: Symptoms usually appear within 24-48 hours of viral infection.
  • Peak severity: Symptoms are most severe around days 3-5 of illness.
  • Resolution: Symptoms typically resolve within 7-10 days, with complete recovery expected in uncomplicated cases.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Viral Induced Wheeze"][/sc]
  • Wheezing - Caused by turbulent airflow through narrowed airways due to inflammation and oedema.
  • Shortness of breath - Results from increased airway resistance and reduced gas exchange efficiency.
  • Cough - Often dry and triggered by airway irritation or mucus accumulation.
  • Fever - Commonly accompanies viral infections, reflecting the systemic inflammatory response.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Viral Induced Wheeze"][/sc]
  • Expiratory wheeze - Audible during auscultation, indicating airflow limitation during exhalation.
  • Prolonged expiration - Demonstrates obstructive airway disease due to reduced airflow.
  • Subcostal or intercostal recession - Reflects increased work of breathing in response to airway obstruction.
  • Tachypnoea - A compensatory mechanism to maintain oxygenation and ventilation.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Viral Induced Wheeze"][/sc]
  • Cyanosis - Indicates severe hypoxaemia and impending respiratory failure.
  • Apnoea episodes - Suggests critical airway obstruction or exhaustion.
  • Altered mental state - Can signify hypercapnia or severe hypoxia, requiring urgent intervention.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Viral Induced Wheeze"][/sc]
  • Chronic or recurrent wheezing without clear viral triggers, which may mimic asthma or structural airway anomalies.
  • Isolated cough without wheezing, potentially delaying diagnosis.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Viral Induced Wheeze"][/sc]
  • Pulse oximetry - Measures oxygen saturation; values <92% suggest hypoxaemia.
  • Chest X-ray - Often unremarkable but may exclude other causes like pneumonia or foreign body.
  • Nasal swab for viral PCR - Identifies causative viruses, guiding prognosis and public health measures.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Viral Induced Wheeze"][/sc]
  • A 3-year-old boy presents with a 2-day history of wheezing, coughing, and mild fever following a viral upper respiratory infection. Symptoms peaked on day 3 and are associated with subcostal recession and expiratory wheezing on auscultation.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Viral Induced Wheeze"][/sc]
  • Asthma - Persistent symptoms with atopic history; responds to bronchodilators.
  • Bronchiolitis - Younger age (<2 years), with crackles and diffuse wheezing on auscultation.
  • Foreign body aspiration - Sudden onset, unilateral wheezing, or asymmetric breath sounds.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Viral Induced Wheeze"][/sc]
  • Salbutamol (via spacer) - First-line for acute symptom relief.
  • Oxygen therapy - For hypoxaemia (saturations <92%).
  • Consider oral corticosteroids - Only in severe cases or recurrent wheeze with features suggestive of asthma.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Viral Induced Wheeze"][/sc]
  • Respiratory failure - From progressive hypoxaemia or hypercapnia.
  • Chronic wheeze - Persistent airway hyper-reactivity in a subset of children.
