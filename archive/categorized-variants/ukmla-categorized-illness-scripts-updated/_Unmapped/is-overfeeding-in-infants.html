Overfeeding in Infants
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Overfeeding in Infants"][/sc]
  • Overfeeding in infants refers to excessive caloric intake relative to the infant's nutritional requirements, often caused by feeding too frequently, providing excessive volumes of milk (breast or formula), or improper feeding practices. It may result in gastrointestinal discomfort, increased spit-up or vomiting, and rapid weight gain.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Overfeeding in Infants"][/sc]
  • Prevalence is difficult to estimate but is more common in formula-fed infants compared to exclusively breastfed infants due to caregiver control of feed volume.
  • Seen more often in contexts of caregiver anxiety about infant hunger or weight gain.
  • Incidence varies globally depending on cultural feeding practices, with limited specific UK data available.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Overfeeding in Infants"][/sc]
  • Excessive milk intake leads to gastrointestinal overdistension, delayed gastric emptying, and increased gastro-oesophageal reflux.
  • Unabsorbed lactose from excessive milk may cause diarrhoea due to osmotic effects in the intestine.
  • Chronic overfeeding can result in rapid weight gain, altering the trajectory of growth curves.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Overfeeding in Infants"][/sc]
  • Acute phase: Symptoms like vomiting and irritability develop shortly after overfeeding episodes.
  • Chronic phase: Sustained overfeeding results in rapid weight gain and potential developmental issues.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Overfeeding in Infants"][/sc]
  • Vomiting or increased spit-up - Due to gastric overdistension and reflux.
  • Irritability - Caused by gastrointestinal discomfort and distension.
  • Loose stools - Resulting from unabsorbed lactose in excessive feeds.
  • Frequent feeding cues - Misinterpreted signs of hunger when discomfort leads to non-nutritive sucking.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Overfeeding in Infants"][/sc]
  • Rapid weight gain - Growth charts show weight crossing centiles disproportionately to length.
  • Abdominal distension - Visible bloating due to excessive milk volume.
  • Increased regurgitation - Observed during examination or reported by caregivers.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Overfeeding in Infants"][/sc]
  • Projectile vomiting - Suggestive of pyloric stenosis rather than simple overfeeding.
  • Failure to thrive - Indicates malabsorption or another underlying condition.

Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Overfeeding in Infants"][/sc]
  • Asymptomatic rapid weight gain - Identified only during routine growth monitoring.
  • Recurrent respiratory symptoms - Due to silent aspiration from gastro-oesophageal reflux.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Overfeeding in Infants"][/sc]
  • Growth chart analysis - Identifies disproportionate weight gain.
  • Abdominal ultrasound - Rules out structural abnormalities like pyloric stenosis.
  • Stool analysis - Checks for lactose malabsorption or infection.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Overfeeding in Infants"][/sc]
  • A 2-month-old male, formula-fed, presenting with frequent vomiting, irritability, and disproportionate weight gain over 4 weeks.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Overfeeding in Infants"][/sc]
  • Pyloric stenosis - Projectile vomiting, no diarrhoea, palpable "olive" mass.
  • Gastro-oesophageal reflux disease (GORD) - Vomiting without rapid weight gain, associated feeding aversion.
  • Cowâ€™s milk protein allergy - Associated with eczema, bloody stools.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Overfeeding in Infants"][/sc]
  • Educate caregivers on appropriate feeding volumes and intervals.
  • Transition to paced feeding techniques to reduce overfeeding.
  • Monitor growth trends and adjust feeding plans as needed.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Overfeeding in Infants"][/sc]
  • Obesity - Long-term risk if overfeeding persists.
  • Increased risk of gastro-oesophageal reflux disease (GORD) - Chronic distension weakens oesophageal sphincter.
  • Behavioural feeding issues - Difficulty transitioning to solids due to caregiver feeding practices.
