Ramsay Hunt Syndrome
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Ramsay Hunt Syndrome"][/sc]
  • Ramsay Hunt syndrome (RHS), also known as herpes zoster oticus, is a neurological disorder resulting from the reactivation of the varicella-zoster virus (VZV) in the geniculate ganglion of the facial nerve (cranial nerve VII). It is characterised by the triad of ipsilateral facial paralysis, ear pain, and vesicular rash in the ear or mouth.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Ramsay Hunt Syndrome"][/sc]
  • Ramsay Hunt syndrome accounts for approximately 12% of all facial nerve palsies. The overall incidence is around 5 per 100,000 people annually in the UK. It is more common in older adults, particularly those over 60 years old. Worldwide prevalence aligns similarly to the incidence in the UK.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Ramsay Hunt Syndrome"][/sc]
  • Ramsay Hunt syndrome is caused by the reactivation of dormant varicella-zoster virus in the geniculate ganglion, which affects the facial nerve. VZV can remain latent in the sensory ganglia after the initial chickenpox infection. Reactivation may occur due to stress, immunosuppression, or ageing, leading to inflammation and damage of the facial nerve and other cranial nerves (V, IX, X) in some cases. The virus may also cause localised vasculitis and nerve oedema.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Ramsay Hunt Syndrome"][/sc]
  • The disease typically begins with non-specific symptoms such as malaise and ear pain. Within 1-3 days, facial paralysis develops, accompanied by a vesicular rash in or around the ear. Symptoms peak in about a week, and without treatment, recovery can take weeks to months, with some cases of permanent nerve damage.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Ramsay Hunt Syndrome"][/sc]
  • Facial paralysis - Due to VZV-induced inflammation of the facial nerve, resulting in unilateral facial drooping.
  • Otalgia (ear pain) - Caused by irritation of the sensory branches of the facial and trigeminal nerves.
  • Vesicular rash - Seen in the external auditory canal or palate, due to viral replication in sensory ganglia.
  • Tinnitus - VZV affects the auditory nerve, leading to abnormal auditory perceptions.
  • Hearing loss - Conductive or sensorineural, related to involvement of the cochlear nerve or middle ear structures.
  • Vertigo - Viral inflammation of the vestibular nerve leads to balance disturbances.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Ramsay Hunt Syndrome"][/sc]
  • Unilateral facial weakness - Lower motor neuron facial palsy affecting both upper and lower face, confirming involvement of the facial nerve.
  • Vesicular rash - Characteristic herpetiform vesicles in the ear canal, tympanic membrane, or oral mucosa.
  • Loss of taste on the anterior two-thirds of the tongue - Reflecting facial nerve involvement in gustatory sensation.
  • Hyperacusis - Increased sensitivity to sound due to paralysis of the stapedius muscle, controlled by the facial nerve.
  • Decreased lacrimation - Reflects parasympathetic dysfunction of the facial nerve affecting tear production.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Ramsay Hunt Syndrome"][/sc]
  • Bilateral facial weakness - May suggest a more systemic neurological disorder such as Guillain-BarrÃ© syndrome or sarcoidosis.
  • Hearing loss with vertigo - Indicates vestibulocochlear nerve involvement, which can lead to permanent auditory or balance deficits.
  • Ophthalmoplegia - Involvement of cranial nerve III, IV, or VI suggests spread of the infection and risk of intracranial complications.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Ramsay Hunt Syndrome"][/sc]
  • Absence of rash (zoster sine herpete) - Some patients may have facial palsy and ear pain without visible vesicles, making diagnosis more challenging.
  • Facial pain without paralysis - Rare cases where only sensory involvement occurs, without motor symptoms.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Ramsay Hunt Syndrome"][/sc]
  • Clinical diagnosis - Based on the classic triad of ear pain, vesicular rash, and facial paralysis.
  • Polymerase chain reaction (PCR) for VZV - Used on vesicle fluid, blood, or CSF to confirm viral presence in atypical cases.
  • Magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) - May show enhancement of the facial nerve in severe or prolonged cases.
  • Pure tone audiometry - Used to assess hearing loss in patients with suspected vestibulocochlear involvement.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Ramsay Hunt Syndrome"][/sc]
  • A 65-year-old man presents with acute onset of right-sided facial paralysis, ear pain, and vesicular rash on the external ear. He reports associated tinnitus and mild vertigo over the past two days.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Ramsay Hunt Syndrome"][/sc]
  • Bell's palsy - Facial paralysis without a rash, typically with a more abrupt onset and associated with reactivation of HSV-1.
  • Acoustic neuroma - Gradual hearing loss and balance issues without acute pain or rash, often detected on imaging.
  • Stroke - Central facial paralysis sparing the forehead, often accompanied by other neurological deficits such as hemiparesis.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Ramsay Hunt Syndrome"][/sc]
  • Antiviral therapy - Oral aciclovir (800 mg five times daily for 7-10 days) or valaciclovir (1000 mg three times daily for 7-10 days) to reduce viral replication.
  • Corticosteroids - Prednisolone (50 mg once daily for 7 days, then taper) to reduce inflammation and nerve damage.
  • Analgesics - NSAIDs or paracetamol for pain management.
  • Eye care - Lubricating eye drops or taping the eyelids at night to prevent corneal damage in patients with incomplete eyelid closure.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Ramsay Hunt Syndrome"][/sc]
  • Permanent facial weakness - Occurs in up to 30% of untreated cases due to nerve degeneration.
  • Postherpetic neuralgia - Persistent pain after the rash heals, more common in older adults.
  • Sensorineural hearing loss - Can be permanent if the cochlear nerve is involved.
