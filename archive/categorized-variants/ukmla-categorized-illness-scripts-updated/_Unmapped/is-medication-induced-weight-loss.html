Medication-Induced Weight Loss
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Medication-Induced Weight Loss"][/sc]
  • Medication-induced weight loss refers to unintentional or excessive loss of body weight due to the effects of prescribed or over-the-counter drugs. Common causative agents include medications that affect appetite, metabolism, gastrointestinal function, or nutrient absorption, such as certain antidepressants, antidiabetic drugs (e.g., GLP-1 receptor agonists), stimulants, and chemotherapy agents.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Medication-Induced Weight Loss"][/sc]
  • Prevalence in UK GP population: Precise data on medication-induced weight loss is limited, but it is recognised in clinical practice, particularly with drugs like GLP-1 receptor agonists and certain psychotropic drugs.
  • UK hospital prevalence: More common among patients receiving chemotherapy or treatments for chronic conditions like diabetes and psychiatric disorders.
  • Worldwide prevalence: Similar patterns are observed globally, especially with the rising use of weight-altering drugs such as GLP-1 analogues for diabetes and weight management.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Medication-Induced Weight Loss"][/sc]
  • Appetite suppression: Medications like stimulants and certain antidepressants (e.g., SSRIs) reduce appetite by altering neurotransmitter levels (e.g., serotonin, dopamine).
  • Increased metabolism: Some drugs (e.g., thyroid hormone replacements) increase metabolic rate, leading to higher energy expenditure and weight loss.
  • Gastrointestinal effects: Drugs like metformin or GLP-1 receptor agonists cause nausea, vomiting, or diarrhoea, reducing caloric intake and nutrient absorption.
  • Malabsorption: Chemotherapy and other agents affecting the gastrointestinal lining impair nutrient absorption, leading to weight loss.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Medication-Induced Weight Loss"][/sc]
  • Acute phase: Rapid onset of weight loss can occur with chemotherapy or high-dose stimulant medications within weeks of initiation.
  • Chronic phase: Gradual weight loss may develop over months with long-term use of appetite-suppressing or metabolism-enhancing drugs like GLP-1 receptor agonists or antidepressants.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Medication-Induced Weight Loss"][/sc]
  • Weight loss - The most prominent symptom, typically a gradual or sometimes rapid reduction in body weight. Caused by appetite suppression, increased metabolism, or malabsorption.
  • Loss of appetite - Frequently seen with drugs that affect neurotransmitter levels (e.g., SSRIs, stimulants). Reduced hunger signalling in the hypothalamus is a major factor.
  • Fatigue - Occurs due to reduced caloric intake or nutrient deficiency, especially with drugs that cause gastrointestinal upset (e.g., GLP-1 receptor agonists).
  • Gastrointestinal discomfort - Nausea, vomiting, or diarrhoea associated with drugs like metformin or chemotherapy agents, contributing to decreased food intake.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Medication-Induced Weight Loss"][/sc]
  • Visible weight loss - Loss of body fat or muscle mass, noticeable over time.
  • Muscle wasting - In chronic cases or with chemotherapy, there may be muscle atrophy due to nutrient deficiencies and catabolism.
  • Pallor or frailty - May indicate malnutrition or underlying metabolic imbalance, especially in cases involving nutrient malabsorption.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Medication-Induced Weight Loss"][/sc]
  • Unintentional rapid weight loss - Can signify severe malnutrition, malignancy, or a severe adverse reaction to medication.
  • Severe gastrointestinal symptoms - Persistent nausea, vomiting, or diarrhoea may indicate serious drug toxicity or malabsorption, leading to dehydration and electrolyte imbalance.
  • Muscle wasting - Suggests prolonged catabolism or malnutrition, raising concerns for cachexia, especially in cancer patients.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Medication-Induced Weight Loss"][/sc]
  • Weight loss without noticeable appetite reduction - Can occur with metabolism-boosting drugs like thyroid hormones.
  • Minimal gastrointestinal symptoms - Some patients on GLP-1 receptor agonists may not experience the expected nausea but still lose weight.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Medication-Induced Weight Loss"][/sc]
  • Full blood count (FBC) - To detect anaemia or infection as a cause of or consequence of weight loss.
  • Urea and electrolytes (U&E) - To assess kidney function and electrolyte balance, particularly if dehydration or gastrointestinal losses are suspected.
  • Thyroid function tests (TFTs) - To rule out hyperthyroidism as a cause of increased metabolism and weight loss.
  • Liver function tests (LFTs) - To detect hepatic causes of weight loss, especially in patients on hepatotoxic medications.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Medication-Induced Weight Loss"][/sc]
  • A 45-year-old woman with type 2 diabetes presents with gradual weight loss of 7 kg over three months, associated with decreased appetite and nausea. She has recently started on GLP-1 receptor agonist therapy.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Medication-Induced Weight Loss"][/sc]
  • Hyperthyroidism - Differentiated by symptoms such as heat intolerance, tremors, and palpitations, along with elevated thyroid hormone levels.
  • Malignancy - More likely with constitutional symptoms like night sweats, fever, and persistent fatigue.
  • Chronic infection (e.g., tuberculosis) - Weight loss accompanied by fever, night sweats, and cough.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Medication-Induced Weight Loss"][/sc]
  • Adjust or discontinue offending medication - If possible, switch to an alternative with less impact on weight.
  • Dietary counselling - To ensure adequate caloric intake and manage side effects like nausea or vomiting.
  • Supportive care - For patients on chemotherapy or GLP-1 agonists, antiemetics and small, frequent meals may help mitigate side effects.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Medication-Induced Weight Loss"][/sc]
  • Malnutrition - Prolonged weight loss may lead to deficiencies in vital nutrients, affecting overall health.
  • Dehydration - Especially in patients with significant gastrointestinal side effects.
  • Muscle wasting and weakness - Occurs due to prolonged caloric and protein deficiency.
