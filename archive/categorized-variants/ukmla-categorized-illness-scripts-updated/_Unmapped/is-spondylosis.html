Spondylosis
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Spondylosis"][/sc]
  • Spondylosis refers to degenerative changes in the spine, including osteoarthritis, disc degeneration, and bone spur formation. It often affects the cervical and lumbar regions and is characterised by chronic pain, stiffness, and sometimes radiculopathy or myelopathy if neural structures are compromised.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Spondylosis"][/sc]
  • Prevalence in UK GP population: ~27% of people over 50 have some degree of spinal degeneration.
  • Hospital prevalence: Spondylosis is a frequent finding on imaging but is often asymptomatic and incidental.
  • Worldwide prevalence: Spondylosis prevalence increases with age, affecting ~80% of people over 60, though only a subset experience symptoms.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Spondylosis"][/sc]
  • The intervertebral discs degenerate due to ageing, resulting in reduced disc height and increased stress on facet joints.
  • This stress leads to articular cartilage degeneration, subchondral sclerosis, and osteophyte formation, which can impinge on spinal nerves or the spinal cord.
  • Inflammatory processes contribute to pain, while chronic wear may lead to spinal stenosis, radiculopathy, or myelopathy.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Spondylosis"][/sc]
  • Early Phase: Often asymptomatic or with mild stiffness; changes may be incidental findings on imaging.
  • Progressive Phase: Development of chronic pain, stiffness, or radicular symptoms over months to years.
  • Advanced Phase: Complications like spinal stenosis or myelopathy, which can cause significant disability and neurological deficits.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Spondylosis"][/sc]
  • Chronic back or neck pain - Caused by disc degeneration and inflammation in facet joints; often worsens with activity.
  • Stiffness - Due to loss of disc flexibility and formation of osteophytes; typically worse in the morning or after inactivity.
  • Radicular pain - Pain radiating to limbs due to nerve root compression, most common in cervical and lumbar spondylosis.
  • Paresthesia or numbness - From nerve compression, leading to sensory deficits in the affected dermatome.
  • Weakness - Can occur in severe cases of nerve root or spinal cord compression, particularly in myelopathy.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Spondylosis"][/sc]
  • Reduced range of motion - Due to stiffness from osteophytes and loss of disc flexibility.
  • Tenderness over affected area - Common in cervical and lumbar regions; indicates local inflammation.
  • Positive Spurlingâ€™s test (cervical spondylosis) - Elicits radicular pain upon neck extension and rotation, suggesting nerve root compression.
  • Reflex changes (hyperreflexia in myelopathy) - Indicative of spinal cord compression.
  • Gait abnormalities - In severe cervical myelopathy, due to motor pathway compression in the spinal cord.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Spondylosis"][/sc]
  • Progressive neurological deficit - Worsening weakness, numbness, or bowel/bladder dysfunction; indicates spinal cord or cauda equina compression.
  • Severe, unremitting pain at night - May suggest malignancy, infection, or severe nerve root compression.
  • Incontinence - Can indicate cauda equina syndrome or severe spinal cord compression requiring urgent intervention.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Spondylosis"][/sc]
  • Asymptomatic findings - Many individuals show radiographic evidence of spondylosis without symptoms; often incidental findings on imaging.
  • Atypical radiculopathy without pain - In some cases, only sensory deficits are noted, which may lead to delayed recognition.
  • Isolated weakness - Rare, but may be the only initial presentation in advanced myelopathy.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Spondylosis"][/sc]
  • X-ray of the spine - Shows osteophytes, reduced disc height, and other degenerative changes typical in spondylosis.
  • MRI - Assesses disc pathology, nerve root compression, and spinal cord involvement; crucial for evaluating neurological symptoms.
  • CT scan - Useful for detailed assessment of bony changes if MRI is contraindicated; shows facet joint degeneration and stenosis.
  • Nerve conduction studies - Helps assess radiculopathy if nerve root compression is suspected.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Spondylosis"][/sc]
  • A 65-year-old male presents with chronic neck pain, morning stiffness, and occasional left-arm radicular pain, progressively worsening over the past year.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Spondylosis"][/sc]
  • Disc herniation - Often acute with focal radiculopathy; typically lacks widespread degenerative changes.
  • Spinal stenosis - May coexist with spondylosis but often presents with neurogenic claudication rather than isolated radicular symptoms.
  • Myofascial pain syndrome - Pain typically localised with no neurological deficits; lacks imaging evidence of spinal degeneration.
  • Rheumatoid arthritis - Inflammatory pattern with systemic signs and more symmetrical joint involvement; often involves cervical spine (e.g., atlantoaxial subluxation).
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Spondylosis"][/sc]
  • Analgesia - Stepwise use of paracetamol, NSAIDs, and muscle relaxants as per NICE guidelines; opioid use only in refractory cases.
  • Physiotherapy - Strengthening and flexibility exercises; postural training and specific neck/lumbar exercises.
  • Epidural steroid injections - For radicular pain due to nerve root compression, often in collaboration with pain management specialists.
  • Surgery - Indicated in cases with progressive neurological deficit or failure of conservative measures; options include decompression or spinal fusion.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Spondylosis"][/sc]
  • Spinal stenosis - Often occurs in advanced spondylosis, leading to neurogenic claudication or myelopathy.
  • Radiculopathy - Chronic compression of nerve roots, potentially causing persistent sensory deficits.
  • Myelopathy - Rare but serious complication of cervical spondylosis; may lead to irreversible neurological deficits if untreated.
  • Prognosis - Typically slowly progressive; outcomes vary based on severity and presence of neurological involvement. Conservative management often effective in milder cases.
