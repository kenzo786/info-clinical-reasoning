Oesophageal Strictures and Webs
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Oesophageal Strictures and Webs"][/sc]
  • Oesophageal strictures: Narrowing of the oesophagus often due to inflammation, fibrosis, or scarring of the oesophageal lining. Commonly secondary to conditions like gastro-oesophageal reflux disease (GORD), peptic ulcer disease, or trauma.
  • Oesophageal webs: Thin, membranous structures, primarily found in the upper oesophagus, partially obstructing the lumen. Typically congenital or associated with iron deficiency anaemia (e.g., Plummer-Vinson syndrome).
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Oesophageal Strictures and Webs"][/sc]
  • UK GP prevalence: Oesophageal strictures are relatively common in patients with chronic acid reflux, estimated at around 1 in 100 of GORD patients.
  • UK hospital prevalence: Often seen in gastroenterology units, especially among patients with GORD, oesophageal cancer, or a history of radiotherapy.
  • Worldwide prevalence: Strictures are a frequent complication of acid reflux worldwide; webs are less common and often associated with geographical variations, iron deficiency, or genetic factors.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Oesophageal Strictures and Webs"][/sc]
  • Strictures: Persistent irritation from acid or trauma leads to chronic inflammation, promoting fibroblast activity and collagen deposition in the oesophageal submucosa, eventually causing luminal narrowing.
  • Webs: Thin, fibrous bands develop due to inflammation or genetic predisposition, commonly in the upper oesophagus. They may also form secondary to iron deficiency, which disrupts epithelial maintenance and leads to dysphagia.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Oesophageal Strictures and Webs"][/sc]
  • Acute phase: Webs can develop slowly but may suddenly obstruct if combined with food bolus impaction.
  • Chronic phase: Strictures often progress gradually over months to years, particularly if the causative factor, such as acid reflux, persists untreated.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Oesophageal Strictures and Webs"][/sc]
  • Dysphagia (most common) - Difficulty swallowing solids progressing to liquids, due to mechanical obstruction caused by luminal narrowing.
  • Odynophagia - Painful swallowing, particularly with oesophagitis, as the inflamed lining becomes sensitive.
  • Unintentional weight loss - Due to poor nutritional intake resulting from dysphagia.
  • Regurgitation - Food or fluid coming back up, related to obstruction within the oesophageal passage.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Oesophageal Strictures and Webs"][/sc]
  • Pallor - Common in cases with iron deficiency (e.g., Plummer-Vinson syndrome), resulting from chronic blood loss or malnutrition.
  • Cachexia - In cases with prolonged malnutrition due to severe dysphagia, particularly in oesophageal strictures.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Oesophageal Strictures and Webs"][/sc]
  • Progressive dysphagia - Especially if progressing from solids to liquids; may indicate malignancy.
  • Unintentional weight loss - Could suggest malignancy or severe malnutrition.
  • Haematemesis or melaena - Possible sign of ulceration or malignancy, requiring urgent investigation.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Oesophageal Strictures and Webs"][/sc]
  • Intermittent dysphagia without progression - Seen in mild strictures or webs; may lead to delayed diagnosis.
  • Asymptomatic iron deficiency anaemia - Particularly in Plummer-Vinson syndrome, presenting without noticeable dysphagia.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Oesophageal Strictures and Webs"][/sc]
  • Barium swallow: Reveals luminal narrowing or web structures; narrowing suggests a stricture, while a shelf-like defect suggests a web.
  • Endoscopy: Allows direct visualisation and biopsy, if malignancy is suspected. Strictures may show scarred mucosa; webs appear as thin bands.
  • Oesophageal manometry: Assesses motility; may be used if dysphagia persists without clear structural abnormality.
  • Iron studies: In cases of suspected Plummer-Vinson syndrome, iron deficiency is common.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Oesophageal Strictures and Webs"][/sc]
  • 60-year-old male with a history of GORD presenting with progressive dysphagia to solids over six months, accompanied by 5 kg unintentional weight loss and intermittent regurgitation.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Oesophageal Strictures and Webs"][/sc]
  • Achalasia: Manometry shows high LES pressure with absent peristalsis, distinguishing it from strictures/webs.
  • Oesophageal cancer: More likely to have red-flag features like rapid weight loss and progressive dysphagia.
  • Peptic stricture: Related to chronic acid reflux but often distinguishable by response to PPI therapy.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Oesophageal Strictures and Webs"][/sc]
  • Strictures: Endoscopic dilatation is the first-line therapy, with PPI therapy to prevent recurrence.
  • Webs: Often treated with dilatation if symptomatic; iron supplementation for associated iron deficiency anaemia.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Oesophageal Strictures and Webs"][/sc]
  • Perforation: Risk during endoscopic dilation, requiring emergency intervention.
  • Malnutrition: Severe dysphagia can lead to significant weight loss and nutritional deficiencies.
