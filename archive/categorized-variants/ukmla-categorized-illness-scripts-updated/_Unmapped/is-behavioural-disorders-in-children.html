Behavioural Disorders in Children
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Behavioural Disorders in Children"][/sc]
  • Behavioural disorders in children encompass a range of disruptive, attention, and mood-related behaviours that impair daily functioning, learning, and socialisation. These include Attention-Deficit/Hyperactivity Disorder (ADHD), Oppositional Defiant Disorder (ODD), and Conduct Disorder (CD), which present with different patterns of inattention, hyperactivity, defiance, aggression, and rule-breaking.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Behavioural Disorders in Children"][/sc]
  • UK GP prevalence: Approximately 5% of children are diagnosed with ADHD, 3% with ODD, and 1.5% with CD annually.
  • UK hospital prevalence: Behavioural disorders account for up to 20% of paediatric mental health referrals, especially for ADHD.
  • Worldwide prevalence: ADHD affects approximately 5-7% of children, ODD 3.3%, and CD 4% globally, with varying diagnostic criteria across regions.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Behavioural Disorders in Children"][/sc]
  • Neurobiological factors: Dysregulation in the dopaminergic and noradrenergic pathways is implicated in ADHD, affecting attention and impulsivity. Dysfunctional reward processing circuits may lead to hyperactivity and impulsiveness.
  • Environmental factors: Childhood exposure to trauma, inconsistent parenting, and social adversity are linked to increased risk of ODD and CD.
  • Genetic predisposition: Behavioural disorders show strong heritability, with polymorphisms in genes related to dopamine (DRD4) and serotonin linked to ADHD and CD risk.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Behavioural Disorders in Children"][/sc]
  • Onset: Symptoms generally emerge in early childhood, often before age 7 for ADHD and later in childhood for ODD and CD.
  • Progression: ADHD symptoms can persist into adolescence and adulthood; ODD may escalate to CD, especially without intervention.
  • Long-term course: Early intervention is key, as untreated behavioural disorders can lead to social dysfunction and increased risk of substance use in adolescence.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Behavioural Disorders in Children"][/sc]
  • Inattention (ADHD) - Commonly presents as distractibility and inability to focus, due to impaired dopamine-mediated attentional networks.
  • Hyperactivity (ADHD) - Often shows as fidgeting or excessive movement, linked to dysregulated motor and reward processing pathways.
  • Impulsivity (ADHD) - Manifests in actions without forethought, related to reduced activity in the prefrontal cortex.
  • Argumentative or defiant behaviour (ODD) - Persistent disobedience towards authority, potentially arising from maladaptive socialisation or inconsistent discipline.
  • Physical aggression (CD) - Engaging in fights or harm to others, likely tied to impulsive behaviour and emotional dysregulation.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Behavioural Disorders in Children"][/sc]
  • Restlessness or constant movement - Observed in ADHD; linked to hyperactive motor pathways in the central nervous system.
  • Defiance towards adults - Typical of ODD; characterised by repeated challenging of authority figures.
  • Low frustration tolerance - Found in CD and ODD; may lead to outbursts and confrontational behaviour.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Behavioural Disorders in Children"][/sc]
  • Severe aggression or self-harm - May indicate increased risk for future antisocial behaviour or underlying trauma.
  • Substance misuse - Seen in adolescents with untreated behavioural disorders, often signalling high-risk coping mechanisms.
  • Social isolation - Reflects significant dysfunction, increasing risk of depressive or anxiety comorbidities.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Behavioural Disorders in Children"][/sc]
  • Subtle inattention without hyperactivity - May be overlooked in ADHD, especially in girls, due to social masking behaviours.
  • Excessive shyness with defiance - Can be misinterpreted as anxiety rather than ODD; linked to underlying social skill deficits.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Behavioural Disorders in Children"][/sc]
  • Conners Rating Scale (ADHD) - Scores above threshold indicate inattention and hyperactivity.
  • Strengths and Difficulties Questionnaire (SDQ) - Measures emotional and behavioural aspects; high scores suggest behavioural issues.
  • Clinical interview and behavioural observation - Necessary for diagnosing ODD and CD, as formal biomarkers are unavailable.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Behavioural Disorders in Children"][/sc]
  • A 7-year-old boy presents with difficulty focusing at school, frequent defiance towards authority, and hyperactive behaviour persisting for over 6 months.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Behavioural Disorders in Children"][/sc]
  • Autism Spectrum Disorder (ASD) - Involves social and communication difficulties without the hyperactivity seen in ADHD.
  • Depression or anxiety disorders - Emotional dysregulation may present similarly to ODD, but without the pervasive defiance and aggression.
  • Intellectual Disability (ID) - Impaired cognitive skills can lead to frustration-based behaviours but differ by limited cognitive performance.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Behavioural Disorders in Children"][/sc]
  • Behavioural therapy - Recommended as first-line for ADHD, ODD, and CD to improve coping and social skills.
  • Methylphenidate (ADHD) - A stimulant improving attention and impulse control; titrated based on response and tolerance.
  • Family therapy (ODD/CD) - Assists in establishing boundaries and positive reinforcement strategies.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Behavioural Disorders in Children"][/sc]
  • School failure - Common in untreated ADHD and CD, due to impaired learning and social difficulties.
  • Substance use disorder - Higher in adolescents with untreated CD, often as a coping mechanism.
  • Antisocial personality disorder - Rare but severe, associated with longstanding, untreated CD symptoms into adulthood.
