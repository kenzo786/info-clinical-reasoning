Skin Infection
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Skin Infection"][/sc]
  • Skin infection refers to an invasion of the skin layers by pathogens, primarily bacteria (e.g., Staphylococcus aureus and Streptococcus pyogenes), viruses, fungi, or parasites. These infections can affect superficial, dermal, or subcutaneous layers and may present as cellulitis, abscesses, impetigo, or other dermatological infections.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Skin Infection"][/sc]
  • UK GP Population: Skin infections are commonly seen, particularly cellulitis and impetigo, which represent a significant proportion of dermatological consultations.
  • UK Hospital Prevalence: Infections like cellulitis lead to a considerable number of hospital admissions, with bacterial skin infections accounting for a major share of inpatient dermatology cases.
  • Worldwide Prevalence: Skin infections are highly prevalent globally, with varying rates influenced by hygiene, climate, and healthcare access. Rates are higher in tropical regions and areas with limited access to healthcare.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Skin Infection"][/sc]
  • Pathogens enter the skin through cuts, abrasions, insect bites, or other breaches in the skin barrier.
  • The immune response triggers inflammation, with neutrophils and macrophages releasing cytokines and enzymes to combat the infection, leading to erythema and warmth.
  • If bacteria, particularly Staphylococcus aureus, proliferate and release toxins, they can cause further local tissue damage and, in severe cases, systemic symptoms like fever.
  • In deeper infections like cellulitis or abscesses, infection extends to the dermis and subcutaneous layers, where tissue necrosis and suppuration (pus formation) can occur.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Skin Infection"][/sc]
  • Acute: Symptoms often develop within hours to days post-exposure or injury, with rapid onset of redness, swelling, and warmth.
  • Subacute: If untreated, infections may progress to include purulent discharge or systemic symptoms over several days to weeks.
  • Chronic/Recurrent: Infections may recur, especially in immunocompromised individuals, or if underlying skin conditions, such as eczema, persist.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Skin Infection"][/sc]
  • Redness - Caused by inflammation and increased blood flow to the affected area as the immune system responds to infection.
  • Swelling - Due to accumulation of fluid from inflammation, leading to oedema in the skin and underlying tissues.
  • Pain - Arises from inflammatory mediators like prostaglandins sensitising local nerves.
  • Warmth - Local increase in temperature as blood flow rises in response to inflammatory cytokines.
  • Pus formation - Indicates bacterial involvement, where neutrophils create pus by lysing the infected tissue.
  • Fever and chills - Seen in more extensive infections, indicating systemic inflammatory response as cytokines enter the bloodstream.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Skin Infection"][/sc]
  • Erythema - Observable redness of the skin due to increased blood flow from inflammation.
  • Induration - A firm or hardened feeling in the skin, resulting from tissue oedema and immune cell infiltration.
  • Fluctuance - Soft area on palpation suggesting an abscess filled with pus.
  • Lymphadenopathy - Swelling of regional lymph nodes as they process immune cells fighting the infection.
  • Systemic symptoms - Signs like fever or tachycardia may appear in cases with significant spread or deep tissue involvement.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Skin Infection"][/sc]
  • Rapidly spreading erythema - Can indicate necrotising fasciitis, a serious soft tissue infection needing urgent treatment.
  • Severe pain disproportionate to visible findings - Suggests potential for deep or invasive infection, like fasciitis.
  • Systemic symptoms (fever, hypotension) - May signify sepsis, a life-threatening systemic response.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Skin Infection"][/sc]
  • Immunocompromised patients may present with minimal redness or pain due to impaired inflammatory response, leading to delayed diagnosis.
  • Elderly patients may show confusion or malaise rather than localised symptoms, potentially masking the severity of infection.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Skin Infection"][/sc]
  • Swab and culture - Identifies causative bacteria; important to determine antibiotic sensitivity, especially if MRSA is suspected.
  • Blood tests (CRP, WBC) - Elevated inflammatory markers like CRP and white blood cell count indicate active infection.
  • Imaging (ultrasound) - May be used to assess for abscess formation or to rule out deep tissue involvement.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Skin Infection"][/sc]
  • A 45-year-old male presents with a 2-day history of a painful, red, and swollen lower leg, with associated fever, indicating likely cellulitis.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Skin Infection"][/sc]
  • Deep vein thrombosis (DVT) - Often unilateral with swelling and pain, but without erythema or warmth unless superficial veins are involved.
  • Contact dermatitis - Presents with redness and itching, usually bilateral, with a history of exposure to irritants.
  • Erysipelas - Superficial infection with sharp, raised borders; often associated with fever but lacks deep tissue involvement.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Skin Infection"][/sc]
  • Oral antibiotics - First-line treatment includes flucloxacillin for mild to moderate cases, covering common bacteria like Staphylococcus aureus.
  • Intravenous antibiotics - For severe cases or if oral therapy is ineffective, IV antibiotics such as ceftriaxone are recommended.
  • Drainage - Abscesses may require incision and drainage for effective treatment and resolution.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Skin Infection"][/sc]
  • Sepsis - Due to systemic spread of infection, leading to significant morbidity and requiring urgent management.
  • Abscess formation - Can result in a walled-off collection of pus, needing drainage to resolve.
  • Chronic or recurrent infection - Possible in those with underlying risk factors (e.g., diabetes), affecting quality of life.
