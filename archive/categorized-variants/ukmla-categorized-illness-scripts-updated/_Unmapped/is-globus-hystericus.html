Globus Hystericus
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Globus Hystericus"][/sc]
  • Globus hystericus, also known as globus sensation or globus pharyngeus, is the subjective sensation of a lump or foreign body in the throat without any objective evidence of obstruction. This sensation is typically chronic and can fluctuate in intensity. It is classified as a functional disorder, often linked to psychological factors, anxiety, and stress. Despite its distressing nature, globus sensation does not typically impair swallowing.

Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Globus Hystericus"][/sc]
  • The exact pathophysiology of globus hystericus remains unclear, though several mechanisms have been proposed:
  • Psychological Factors: Strongly associated with stress, anxiety, and depression. Psychological stress may increase muscle tension in the upper oesophageal sphincter and pharyngeal musculature, leading to the sensation of a "lump".
  • Gastroesophageal Reflux Disease (GORD): Reflux can lead to laryngopharyngeal irritation, causing a persistent sensation of fullness or tightness in the throat.
  • Upper Oesophageal Sphincter Dysfunction: Dysregulated or hypertonic muscle tone in the cricopharyngeus muscle can lead to a sensation of constriction in the throat.
  • Somatic Sensitisation: Increased sensitivity or abnormal sensory processing in the throat may contribute to the feeling of a lump.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Globus Hystericus"][/sc]
  • Acute Phase: Often triggered by a stressful event or psychological distress. Patients may experience a sudden awareness of a lump in their throat, typically lasting a few days to a few weeks.
  • Chronic Phase: In the absence of intervention, globus sensation may become chronic, with intermittent flare-ups triggered by stress or emotional distress. It may persist for months or years with varying intensity.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Globus Hystericus"][/sc]
  • Sensation of a Lump in the Throat: Most common symptom, described as a feeling of constriction, fullness, or "something stuck" in the throat. Likely due to increased muscle tension and somatic sensitisation.
  • Worsening with Anxiety or Stress: The sensation often exacerbates in stressful situations, reinforcing the link between globus sensation and psychological factors.
  • No Difficulty Swallowing: Unlike structural abnormalities, patients typically do not experience true dysphagia (difficulty swallowing solid or liquid food), helping differentiate it from other oesophageal conditions.
  • Relief with Eating: Some patients report that eating temporarily alleviates the sensation, possibly due to relaxation of the upper oesophageal sphincter during swallowing.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Globus Hystericus"][/sc]
  • Absence of Physical Findings: Clinical examination typically reveals no structural abnormalities, such as neck masses or visible obstructions in the oropharynx or larynx.
  • Normal ENT Examination: A thorough ear, nose, and throat examination often shows no abnormalities, helping rule out organic causes of throat discomfort.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Globus Hystericus"][/sc]
  • Progressive Dysphagia: Progressive difficulty swallowing could indicate a structural or neurological cause, such as oesophageal cancer or a neuromuscular disorder.
  • Unintentional Weight Loss: Significant weight loss may suggest malignancy or severe systemic illness, prompting further investigation.
  • Persistent Hoarseness or Voice Changes: May indicate laryngeal pathology, such as laryngeal cancer, requiring urgent ENT referral.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Globus Hystericus"][/sc]
  • Globus with True Dysphagia: While globus is generally not associated with difficulty swallowing, concurrent dysphagia might indicate an underlying structural issue.
  • Globus in Absence of Psychological Symptoms: Though rare, some patients may present without apparent anxiety or stress, possibly due to subclinical reflux or undiagnosed oesophageal motility disorders.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Globus Hystericus"][/sc]
  • Flexible Nasendoscopy: Performed to assess for structural abnormalities in the larynx and pharynx; normal findings are common in globus sensation.
  • Barium Swallow Study: Used to rule out oesophageal motility disorders and structural obstructions; typically normal in globus but helps rule out anatomical causes.
  • 24-Hour pH Monitoring: May be considered if reflux is suspected, as it assesses for acid exposure that could contribute to throat symptoms.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Globus Hystericus"][/sc]
  • A 35-year-old female with no significant medical history presents with a six-month history of intermittent sensation of a lump in the throat, worsened by stress, without any difficulty swallowing or weight loss.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Globus Hystericus"][/sc]
  • Gastroesophageal Reflux Disease (GORD): Similar sensation due to acid reflux; confirmed by pH monitoring or improvement with antacids.
  • Oesophageal Cancer: Progressive dysphagia, weight loss, and other systemic signs; usually identified by imaging and biopsy.
  • Achalasia: True dysphagia for both solids and liquids, often with regurgitation; confirmed by oesophageal manometry.
  • Thyroid Nodules/Goitre: May cause a sensation of throat fullness but typically accompanied by a palpable mass.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Globus Hystericus"][/sc]
  • Reassurance and Explanation: Reassurance about the benign nature of the condition is essential. Education on the link between stress and symptoms may alleviate anxiety.
  • Stress Management Techniques: Cognitive-behavioural therapy (CBT) and other psychological therapies can help address underlying anxiety and stress.
  • Empirical Proton Pump Inhibitor (PPI) Therapy: For patients with suspected reflux; trial of PPIs may help if reflux is contributing.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Globus Hystericus"][/sc]
  • Psychological Distress: Chronic globus sensation can lead to heightened anxiety, social withdrawal, and depression.
  • Unnecessary Investigations: Excessive investigations may occur due to persistent symptoms, although reassurance and proper explanation can mitigate this.
