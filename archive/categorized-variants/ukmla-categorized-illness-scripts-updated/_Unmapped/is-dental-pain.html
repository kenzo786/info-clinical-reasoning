Dental Pain
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Dental Pain"][/sc]
  • Dental pain, also known as odontalgia, refers to discomfort or pain originating from the teeth or surrounding structures (periodontium). It may result from a variety of causes including dental caries, pulpitis, trauma, or infection, and can be acute or chronic. The pain can range from mild to severe and is often exacerbated by stimuli such as temperature changes, pressure, or sweet foods.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Dental Pain"][/sc]
  • Dental pain is one of the most common complaints in primary care and emergency settings. In the UK, about 20% of adults report dental pain in any given year. Globally, untreated dental caries, which is a leading cause of dental pain, affects approximately 2.5 billion people. Dental pain is more prevalent in lower socioeconomic groups due to barriers in accessing dental care.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Dental Pain"][/sc]
  • The pathophysiology of dental pain largely depends on the underlying cause:
    • Caries: Bacterial infiltration of enamel and dentin leads to demineralisation and exposure of the dental pulp, resulting in inflammatory responses and pain.
    • Pulpitis: Inflammation of the dental pulp due to infection or trauma causes increased pressure in the confined pulp chamber, leading to nerve irritation and pain.
    • Periodontitis: Inflammation of the periodontal ligament and alveolar bone due to bacterial infection results in pain, often associated with tooth mobility.
    • Trauma: Direct injury to the tooth or supporting structures can damage the nerve or blood supply, leading to pain and sensitivity.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Dental Pain"][/sc]
  • Acute: Sudden onset, often linked to infection (e.g., acute pulpitis, abscess). The pain may be sharp, severe, and exacerbated by thermal stimuli or chewing.
  • Chronic: Long-standing pain that may fluctuate in intensity, often due to underlying chronic conditions such as periodontitis or unresolved caries.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Dental Pain"][/sc]
  • Pain on chewing or biting - Due to pressure on the inflamed periodontal or pulp tissue, commonly seen in dental caries or cracked teeth.
  • Sensitivity to hot or cold - A result of exposed dentin or pulp from caries or enamel erosion, which irritates the dental nerve.
  • Constant throbbing pain - Indicative of pulpitis or dental abscess, where inflammation leads to continuous nerve irritation.
  • Swelling or tenderness of the gums - Often associated with periodontitis or abscess formation, due to the localised infection and inflammation.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Dental Pain"][/sc]
  • Localised tenderness on palpation - Suggests inflammation or infection in the affected tooth or surrounding structures.
  • Visible dental caries or fractured tooth - Common cause of pain, seen on inspection as darkened, decayed areas or cracks in the enamel.
  • Swollen, erythematous gums - Indicates gingivitis or periodontitis, often associated with localised infection or plaque buildup.
  • Fistula or draining abscess - A sign of severe infection where pus has created a sinus tract to the surface, often with associated swelling.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Dental Pain"][/sc]
  • Facial swelling - May indicate spread of infection beyond the tooth into surrounding tissues (e.g., cellulitis or Ludwigâ€™s angina), which can compromise airway patency.
  • Trismus (difficulty opening mouth) - Can signify deep space infection (e.g., peritonsillar abscess) or involvement of the muscles of mastication.
  • Fever - Suggests systemic involvement of infection, indicating a possible dental abscess or spreading odontogenic infection.
  • Severe, unremitting pain - May point to severe infection, malignancy, or non-dental causes like trigeminal neuralgia.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Dental Pain"][/sc]
  • Referred pain to the jaw or ear - Dental pain can present atypically as referred pain due to shared nerve pathways between the teeth and jaw or ear (trigeminal nerve).
  • Non-localised pain - In some cases, especially with early infection or trauma, pain may not localise to a specific tooth, making diagnosis challenging.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Dental Pain"][/sc]
  • Dental X-ray (periapical, panoramic) - Can reveal caries, fractures, and abscesses. X-rays may show periapical radiolucency in cases of abscess or pulpitis.
  • Pulp vitality testing (cold test) - Helps assess pulp health; a non-responsive tooth may indicate necrosis, whereas an exaggerated response may suggest pulpitis.
  • Probe depth measurement - Used to evaluate for periodontal disease by measuring the depth of pockets around the tooth, indicating periodontal involvement.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Dental Pain"][/sc]
  • A 35-year-old male presents with sharp pain in the lower molar region, worsened by chewing and cold drinks. The pain has been intermittent for 3 days, with associated mild swelling of the gums.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Dental Pain"][/sc]
  • Trigeminal neuralgia: Sudden, intense facial pain, often triggered by minor stimuli, differs from the more continuous or dull pain of dental causes.
  • Sinusitis: May cause pain referred to the upper teeth, typically accompanied by nasal congestion or headache, which distinguishes it from localised dental pain.
  • Temporomandibular joint (TMJ) dysfunction: Jaw pain, often related to movement or clicking, without signs of dental infection or decay.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Dental Pain"][/sc]
  • Caries: Filling or root canal treatment depending on the extent of decay. Antibiotics may be indicated if there is an associated abscess or systemic signs of infection.
  • Pulpitis: Early stages can be treated with pulp capping, but irreversible pulpitis requires root canal treatment or tooth extraction.
  • Periodontitis: Scaling and root planing to remove plaque and calculus. Advanced cases may require surgical intervention and systemic antibiotics if infection is present.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Dental Pain"][/sc]
  • Dental abscess: If untreated, dental infections can progress to an abscess, which may require drainage and antibiotics.
  • Osteomyelitis of the jaw: A rare but severe infection of the jawbone, often requiring surgical debridement and prolonged antibiotic therapy.
  • Systemic spread of infection: In cases of severe odontogenic infection, there is a risk of sepsis, cellulitis, or life-threatening conditions such as Ludwigâ€™s angina.
