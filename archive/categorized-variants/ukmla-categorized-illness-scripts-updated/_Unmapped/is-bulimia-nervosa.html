Bulimia Nervosa
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Bulimia Nervosa"][/sc]
  • Bulimia nervosa is an eating disorder characterised by recurrent episodes of binge eating, followed by inappropriate compensatory behaviours to prevent weight gain, such as self-induced vomiting, misuse of laxatives, fasting, or excessive exercise. These episodes occur at least once a week for three months. It is associated with an overvaluation of body shape and weight, leading to significant psychological distress.

Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Bulimia Nervosa"][/sc]
  • Bulimia nervosa arises from a complex interaction of genetic, neurobiological, psychological, and sociocultural factors. Dysregulation of neurotransmitters, particularly serotonin and dopamine, contributes to impaired appetite control and mood regulation. Binge episodes are triggered by a combination of emotional distress, hunger, or restriction, leading to an overwhelming urge to consume large quantities of food. Compensatory behaviours aim to alleviate guilt and prevent weight gain. The recurrent cycle of bingeing and purging can cause profound metabolic and gastrointestinal disturbances, including electrolyte imbalances, gastrointestinal damage, and cardiovascular stress.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Bulimia Nervosa"][/sc]
  • Bulimia nervosa often begins in late adolescence or early adulthood, with a chronic course if untreated. Symptoms typically fluctuate, with periods of remission followed by relapse. Without intervention, the disorder can persist for years or even decades, causing long-term psychological and physical health problems.
