Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Henoch-SchÃ¶nlein Purpura (HSP)"][/sc]
  • Henoch-SchÃ¶nlein Purpura (HSP) is an immune-mediated vasculitis affecting small vessels, predominantly in the skin, gastrointestinal tract, kidneys, and joints. It is characterised by a tetrad of non-thrombocytopenic purpura, arthritis, abdominal pain, and renal involvement. The condition is most common in children and is often self-limiting.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Henoch-SchÃ¶nlein Purpura (HSP)"][/sc]
  • In the UK, HSP has an incidence rate of approximately 20 per 100,000 children annually, with a peak incidence between ages 3-15 years.
  • It is less common in adults, where it presents with a higher rate of complications, particularly renal involvement.
  • Globally, HSP incidence varies but generally follows similar patterns, with higher prevalence among children.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Henoch-SchÃ¶nlein Purpura (HSP)"][/sc]
  • HSP is an IgA-mediated immune complex vasculitis that triggers inflammation in small vessels.
    • 1. Antigens, often following an infection (commonly streptococcal or viral), lead to an abnormal immune response.
    • 2. IgA immune complexes form and deposit in vessel walls, particularly in skin, kidneys, and GI tract.
    • 3. Immune complexes activate the complement system, causing neutrophil and macrophage infiltration, leading to vessel inflammation and tissue damage.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Henoch-SchÃ¶nlein Purpura (HSP)"][/sc]
  • Acute phase (2-6 weeks): Symptoms develop, often with purpura and joint pain initially, followed by abdominal pain and renal involvement.
  • Resolution phase: Symptoms gradually subside; however, renal symptoms can persist or recur over months, particularly in adults.
  • Chronic phase: In rare cases, chronic kidney disease may develop as a complication of prolonged renal involvement.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Henoch-SchÃ¶nlein Purpura (HSP)"][/sc]
  • Purpura - Non-blanching purpura, typically on lower limbs and buttocks, due to small vessel vasculitis causing extravasation of red blood cells.
  • Arthralgia/Arthritis - Pain and swelling in knees and ankles, related to immune complex deposition in joint tissues.
  • Abdominal Pain - Colicky pain due to intestinal involvement, often presenting with nausea or vomiting, caused by vascular inflammation in the GI tract.
  • Haematuria/Proteinuria - Renal involvement may lead to blood/protein in urine, a result of glomerular inflammation and IgA deposition.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Henoch-SchÃ¶nlein Purpura (HSP)"][/sc]
  • Palpable Purpura - Non-blanching purpuric rash on lower extremities and buttocks.
  • Joint Swelling - Swelling and tenderness in large joints, especially the knees and ankles.
  • Abdominal Tenderness - Localised tenderness, particularly in the lower abdomen.
  • Hypertension - May occur with significant renal involvement, due to impaired kidney function.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Henoch-SchÃ¶nlein Purpura (HSP)"][/sc]
  • Severe abdominal pain - May indicate intussusception, a serious complication of HSP in children.
  • Renal impairment signs - Persistent hypertension, oliguria, or rising creatinine may signal developing nephropathy.
  • Severe proteinuria or nephrotic syndrome - Indicates significant glomerular involvement and risk for long-term renal damage.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Henoch-SchÃ¶nlein Purpura (HSP)"][/sc]
  • HSP without purpura is rare but may occur; GI or renal symptoms alone can delay diagnosis.
  • Severe renal involvement without systemic symptoms may be more common in adults, leading to an insidious presentation.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Henoch-SchÃ¶nlein Purpura (HSP)"][/sc]
  • Urinalysis: Haematuria and proteinuria are common, reflecting renal involvement.
  • Renal function tests: Elevated creatinine or decreased GFR in severe cases indicate renal impairment.
  • Full blood count: Platelet count is normal, helping distinguish HSP from thrombocytopenic purpura.
  • IgA levels: May be elevated but are not specific for HSP; useful for supporting evidence.
  • Skin or renal biopsy: Rarely needed; shows IgA deposits in vessel walls if performed.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Henoch-SchÃ¶nlein Purpura (HSP)"][/sc]
  • A 6-year-old child presents with a three-day history of palpable purpura on the lower extremities, abdominal pain, and recent onset of knee swelling.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Henoch-SchÃ¶nlein Purpura (HSP)"][/sc]
  • Immune Thrombocytopenic Purpura (ITP) - Distinguished by thrombocytopenia and absence of renal involvement.
  • Kawasaki Disease - Presents with fever and lymphadenopathy, often without purpura, and usually affects younger children.
  • Systemic Lupus Erythematosus (SLE) - Can present with renal involvement and rash, but typically includes additional systemic features (e.g., ANA positivity).
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Henoch-SchÃ¶nlein Purpura (HSP)"][/sc]
  • Supportive care: Rest, hydration, and analgesia for joint pain.
  • NSAIDs: For pain control, though caution is needed if there is significant renal involvement.
  • Corticosteroids: Considered for severe abdominal pain or significant renal involvement, but evidence is mixed on long-term outcomes.
  • Referral to nephrology: Indicated for persistent renal symptoms, particularly in adults.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Henoch-SchÃ¶nlein Purpura (HSP)"][/sc]
  • Chronic kidney disease (CKD): Occurs in a minority, particularly adults with prolonged nephropathy.
  • Intussusception: Rare but life-threatening complication due to intestinal swelling and obstruction in children.
  • Hypertension: Can develop secondary to chronic kidney disease.
