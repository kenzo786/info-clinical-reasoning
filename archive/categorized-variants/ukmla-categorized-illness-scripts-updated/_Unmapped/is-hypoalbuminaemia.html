Hypoalbuminaemia
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Hypoalbuminaemia"][/sc]
  • Hypoalbuminaemia is defined as a serum albumin level below 35 g/L in adults. Albumin, a protein produced in the liver, plays a crucial role in maintaining oncotic pressure and transporting substances such as hormones, drugs, and waste products. Hypoalbuminaemia reflects either decreased production, increased loss, or redistribution of albumin, and is commonly associated with liver disease, nephrotic syndrome, malnutrition, or chronic inflammatory states.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Hypoalbuminaemia"][/sc]
  • UK GP population: Hypoalbuminaemia is relatively uncommon in primary care settings, typically seen in chronic illnesses, with a prevalence of around 0.5-1%.
  • UK hospital prevalence: Hypoalbuminaemia is more common in hospitalised patients, particularly those with chronic liver or kidney disease, with a prevalence of 20-30%.
  • Worldwide prevalence: Globally, the prevalence of hypoalbuminaemia varies based on population health status, with higher rates in low-income regions due to malnutrition and infections.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Hypoalbuminaemia"][/sc]
  • Hypoalbuminaemia results from several mechanisms:
    • Decreased synthesis: Chronic liver diseases like cirrhosis impair albumin production.
    • Increased loss: Conditions like nephrotic syndrome cause significant urinary loss of albumin.
    • Increased catabolism: Chronic inflammation increases albumin breakdown.
    • Redistribution: Albumin shifts from the vascular space to the extravascular space during sepsis or in protein-losing enteropathy.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Hypoalbuminaemia"][/sc]
  • Acute hypoalbuminaemia: Can develop rapidly in the setting of acute illness such as sepsis, trauma, or burns.
  • Chronic hypoalbuminaemia: Typically seen in patients with chronic liver disease, nephrotic syndrome, or malnutrition, developing over weeks to months.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Hypoalbuminaemia"][/sc]
  • Fatigue - Due to reduced oncotic pressure leading to fluid shift and tissue oedema, which impairs normal cellular function.
  • Peripheral oedema - Common symptom due to reduced plasma oncotic pressure, causing fluid to leak into interstitial spaces.
  • Dyspnoea - Can result from pleural effusions, which develop due to hypoalbuminaemia-induced fluid accumulation.
  • Abdominal distension - Caused by ascites, which occurs due to a shift in fluid into the peritoneal cavity, particularly in liver disease.
  • Often asymptomatic - Many cases of mild hypoalbuminaemia are found incidentally on blood tests, especially in chronic conditions.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Hypoalbuminaemia"][/sc]
  • Peripheral oedema - Seen in the ankles or sacrum, caused by fluid leakage due to decreased oncotic pressure.
  • Ascites - Fluid accumulation in the abdomen, often associated with liver disease or nephrotic syndrome.
  • Pleural effusion - Fluid in the pleural cavity, leading to decreased breath sounds and dullness to percussion.
  • Muscle wasting - Results from prolonged protein deficiency and catabolism.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Hypoalbuminaemia"][/sc]
  • Severe peripheral oedema - May indicate advanced hypoalbuminaemia, suggesting significant fluid imbalance and the risk of skin breakdown or infection.
  • Rapid onset ascites - Can be a sign of worsening liver failure or severe nephrotic syndrome, requiring urgent investigation.
  • Pulmonary oedema - Can lead to respiratory distress and signifies severe hypoalbuminaemia or a rapid worsening of the underlying disease, such as nephrotic syndrome or heart failure.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Hypoalbuminaemia"][/sc]
  • Isolated ascites - Some patients may present with isolated abdominal distension due to ascites without peripheral oedema, particularly in liver disease.
  • Generalised weakness - In patients with chronic conditions like malignancy, hypoalbuminaemia can present with non-specific symptoms like weakness or lethargy.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Hypoalbuminaemia"][/sc]
  • Serum albumin - The hallmark diagnostic test, with levels below 35 g/L indicating hypoalbuminaemia.
  • Liver function tests (LFTs) - Can show elevated transaminases or bilirubin in liver disease, which causes hypoalbuminaemia due to reduced production.
  • Urinalysis - Detects proteinuria in nephrotic syndrome, indicating urinary loss of albumin.
  • C-reactive protein (CRP) - Elevated in inflammatory conditions that increase albumin catabolism.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Hypoalbuminaemia"][/sc]
  • A 55-year-old man presents with progressive bilateral ankle swelling and abdominal distension over 3 months. He has a history of chronic liver disease and has been experiencing increasing fatigue and dyspnoea on exertion.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Hypoalbuminaemia"][/sc]
  • Nephrotic syndrome: Typically presents with proteinuria, oedema, and hypoalbuminaemia. Unlike liver disease, urinalysis reveals significant protein loss.
  • Liver cirrhosis: Presents with jaundice, ascites, and variceal bleeding. LFTs show evidence of liver dysfunction, with hypoalbuminaemia from reduced production.
  • Heart failure: Causes oedema and dyspnoea, but hypoalbuminaemia is less common. Echocardiography and raised BNP levels help distinguish it.
  • Protein-losing enteropathy: Causes hypoalbuminaemia due to gastrointestinal loss of protein, often with diarrhoea and weight loss.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Hypoalbuminaemia"][/sc]
  • Treat underlying cause: Management of hypoalbuminaemia involves addressing the root cause (e.g., liver disease, nephrotic syndrome).
  • Albumin infusion: In severe cases or acute settings (e.g., sepsis, major surgery), intravenous albumin may be administered, though its use is debated.
  • Nutritional support: Optimising protein intake, especially in cases of malnutrition, is critical.
  • Diuretics: For oedema management, particularly in nephrotic syndrome or heart failure, though they must be used cautiously.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Hypoalbuminaemia"][/sc]
  • Infections: Patients with hypoalbuminaemia are at increased risk of infections due to impaired immune response and skin breakdown from oedema.
  • Thromboembolism: Particularly in nephrotic syndrome, due to hypercoagulability associated with protein loss.
  • Severe fluid overload: Persistent hypoalbuminaemia can lead to significant ascites, pleural effusion, or even pulmonary oedema, complicating management.
