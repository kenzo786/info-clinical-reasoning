Femoral Artery Aneurysm
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Femoral Artery Aneurysm"][/sc]
  • A femoral artery aneurysm is a focal dilation of the femoral artery, commonly the common femoral artery, with a diameter exceeding 1.5 times its normal size. It is a type of peripheral artery aneurysm and may be asymptomatic or symptomatic. When symptomatic, it can present with limb-threatening complications, including thromboembolism or rupture.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Femoral Artery Aneurysm"][/sc]
  • Femoral artery aneurysms are uncommon, with an estimated prevalence of less than 0.5% in the general population.
  • More prevalent in older men, particularly those over the age of 60, with a male-to-female ratio of approximately 10:1.
  • Often associated with atherosclerotic disease, coexisting with other aneurysms, especially in the abdominal aorta and popliteal artery (present in up to 50% of cases).
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Femoral Artery Aneurysm"][/sc]
  • The pathogenesis involves weakening of the arterial wall due to atherosclerosis, which causes degradation of the structural proteins, elastin, and collagen, within the vessel wall.
  • Progressive vessel wall weakening leads to dilation under the force of blood flow. Chronic inflammation and cellular dysfunction further contribute to the degradation and thinning of the vessel wall.
  • Risk factors include advanced age, hypertension, smoking, and hyperlipidaemia, all of which exacerbate arterial wall degradation.
  • Femoral artery aneurysms carry a high risk of thromboembolism, as the turbulent blood flow within the aneurysmal sac increases the risk of thrombus formation.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Femoral Artery Aneurysm"][/sc]
  • Femoral artery aneurysms tend to develop slowly over years, often remaining asymptomatic initially.
  • Once symptomatic, rapid progression may occur due to thrombus formation or rupture, particularly in larger aneurysms.
  • Without intervention, there is a significant risk of limb-threatening ischaemia or rupture.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Femoral Artery Aneurysm"][/sc]
  • Asymptomatic - Many cases are asymptomatic and discovered incidentally on imaging. The aneurysm may be silent until it causes complications like thromboembolism or rupture.
  • Pain in the groin or thigh - A dull or aching pain in the groin or thigh, caused by pressure of the aneurysm on adjacent structures or due to aneurysmal expansion.
  • Limb ischaemia - Sudden onset of pain, pallor, or paraesthesia in the leg, which may be due to thromboembolism from the aneurysm, leading to acute limb ischaemia.
  • Swelling in the groin - A palpable mass in the groin may be noticed by the patient or on examination, due to the enlarged aneurysmal artery.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Femoral Artery Aneurysm"][/sc]
  • Pulsatile mass in the groin - A visible or palpable pulsating mass in the groin due to the dilated aneurysmal femoral artery.
  • Decreased or absent distal pulses - Due to distal embolisation or thrombosis affecting blood flow to the lower extremity.
  • Skin changes (pallor, cyanosis) - May occur with acute limb ischaemia as blood flow to the limb is compromised by thromboembolism.
  • Bruit over the aneurysm - A turbulent blood flow sound on auscultation over the femoral artery may be noted due to the aneurysm.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Femoral Artery Aneurysm"][/sc]
  • Severe limb pain or loss of sensation - Suggests acute limb ischaemia, a limb-threatening emergency due to thromboembolism.
  • Rapidly expanding mass or new pain - May indicate impending rupture, which is a surgical emergency due to the risk of haemorrhage.
  • Skin ulceration or necrosis - Indicates advanced ischaemia with possible tissue loss, requiring urgent intervention.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Femoral Artery Aneurysm"][/sc]
  • Isolated leg pain without a palpable mass - May be mistaken for musculoskeletal pain; however, underlying aneurysm with thromboembolism should be considered if pulses are diminished.
  • Painless limb ischaemia - Rarely, embolisation may occur without pain, particularly in individuals with neuropathy, leading to delayed presentation.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Femoral Artery Aneurysm"][/sc]
  • Ultrasound Doppler - First-line imaging to assess aneurysm size and blood flow characteristics, identifying thrombosis or turbulent flow in the aneurysmal sac.
  • CT Angiography - Provides detailed visualisation of the aneurysm, surrounding structures, and extent of any thrombus. Useful in planning surgical intervention.
  • Ankle-Brachial Index (ABI) - Reduced ABI may indicate impaired distal perfusion due to thromboembolism from the aneurysm.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Femoral Artery Aneurysm"][/sc]
  • A 68-year-old male presents with a pulsatile mass in the right groin and mild, aching thigh pain. Examination reveals a diminished dorsalis pedis pulse. History of smoking and hypertension.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Femoral Artery Aneurysm"][/sc]
  • Inguinal hernia - Presents with a groin mass, but typically reducible and non-pulsatile, often with a cough impulse.
  • Lymphadenopathy - Usually firm, non-pulsatile, and may be tender; often associated with systemic signs of infection or malignancy.
  • Pseudoaneurysm - Often secondary to trauma or iatrogenic causes; presents as a pulsatile groin mass but with different ultrasound characteristics (e.g., "yin-yang" sign).
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Femoral Artery Aneurysm"][/sc]
  • Surveillance - Small, asymptomatic aneurysms (<2 cm) may be monitored with regular ultrasound imaging.
  • Surgical Repair - Indicated for symptomatic or large aneurysms (>2 cm), or those with evidence of thrombus. Options include open repair with resection and grafting, or endovascular repair.
  • Antithrombotic Therapy - Aspirin or anticoagulation to reduce the risk of thromboembolism, especially in patients with large or symptomatic aneurysms.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Femoral Artery Aneurysm"][/sc]
  • Thromboembolism - Common and potentially limb-threatening; may lead to acute limb ischaemia.
  • Rupture - Rare but life-threatening complication; can cause significant haemorrhage, leading to hypotension and shock.
  • Chronic limb ischaemia - Due to recurrent thromboembolism, causing long-term limb disability or tissue loss.
