Vocal Cord Nodules
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Vocal Cord Nodules"][/sc]
  • Vocal cord nodules are benign, symmetrical lesions that develop on the vocal cords due to chronic vocal strain or misuse. They are often referred to as "singer's nodules" as they are common in individuals who frequently use their voice, such as singers, teachers, or public speakers. Nodules cause the vocal cords to vibrate abnormally, resulting in a hoarse or breathy voice.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Vocal Cord Nodules"][/sc]
  • Prevalence in the UK GP population is low, with vocal cord nodules accounting for around 1% of voice disorders presenting in primary care.
  • In the UK hospital population, the prevalence is higher among professional voice users such as singers and teachers.
  • Worldwide prevalence is difficult to establish, but vocal cord nodules are more common in females and children, particularly in boys.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Vocal Cord Nodules"][/sc]
  • Vocal cord nodules form as a result of repeated trauma or irritation to the vocal cords. This irritation leads to inflammation, which causes small areas of thickening on the edge of the vocal cords. Over time, these areas harden into fibrous nodules. Nodules typically develop bilaterally on the anterior one-third of the vocal cords, where vibration is most vigorous during speech.
  • The nodules prevent the vocal cords from closing properly, which leads to incomplete phonation and a breathy or hoarse voice.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Vocal Cord Nodules"][/sc]
  • Early Phase: Repeated vocal misuse leads to acute inflammation and vocal fatigue. Voice may become hoarse, but this stage is reversible with rest.
  • Progression: Continued misuse causes thickening of the vocal cords, and nodules begin to form. The voice becomes persistently hoarse or breathy.
  • Chronic Phase: Established fibrotic nodules lead to permanent vocal changes. Symptoms may persist until treatment is sought.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Vocal Cord Nodules"][/sc]
  • Hoarseness - The most common symptom, resulting from the disruption in the vibration and closure of the vocal cords.
  • Vocal fatigue - Prolonged speaking or singing can lead to a tired, strained feeling in the throat, due to overuse of compensatory muscles.
  • Breathy voice - Nodules prevent complete closure of the vocal cords, allowing air to escape during phonation, leading to a breathy voice.
  • Reduced vocal range - Particularly in singers, the ability to reach high notes may diminish as the vocal cords are unable to vibrate efficiently.
  • Frequent throat clearing - Individuals may feel the need to clear their throat frequently, possibly due to irritation or incomplete closure of the vocal cords.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Vocal Cord Nodules"][/sc]

  • Visible nodules - On laryngoscopy, symmetrical nodules are seen on the anterior one-third of the vocal cords.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Vocal Cord Nodules"][/sc]
  • Unilateral lesions - May suggest malignancy or other pathology, as vocal cord nodules are typically bilateral.
  • Rapid progression of symptoms - A sudden worsening of symptoms could indicate a more serious condition such as vocal cord paralysis or carcinoma.
  • Persistent pain - Vocal cord nodules are generally painless. Persistent pain, especially with referred otalgia (ear pain), may indicate malignancy.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Vocal Cord Nodules"][/sc]
  • Mild or intermittent hoarseness - Some patients, especially in early stages, may only have mild or episodic symptoms that are easily ignored.
  • Children - Boys may develop nodules due to vocal strain from shouting or loud play, often without significant symptoms other than a hoarse voice.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Vocal Cord Nodules"][/sc]
  • Laryngoscopy - Direct visualisation of the vocal cords using a flexible or rigid laryngoscope shows bilateral nodules located at the junction of the anterior and middle third of the vocal cords.
  • Stroboscopy - Allows assessment of vocal cord vibration during phonation, which may reveal irregular or asymmetric motion caused by nodules.
  • Voice analysis - Acoustic analysis may demonstrate changes in vocal parameters such as reduced pitch range or increased jitter.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Vocal Cord Nodules"][/sc]
  • A 35-year-old female teacher presents with a 6-month history of progressive hoarseness and vocal fatigue, worsened after prolonged speaking. No pain or systemic symptoms. Laryngoscopy reveals bilateral nodules on the vocal cords.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Vocal Cord Nodules"][/sc]
  • Vocal cord polyps - These are unilateral and more vascular, often associated with acute vocal trauma.
  • Vocal cord cysts - Unlike nodules, cysts are deeper within the vocal cord and may cause more significant disruption of voice.
  • Vocal cord paralysis - Typically presents with breathy voice, but on examination, one vocal cord will not move properly.
  • Laryngeal carcinoma - Hoarseness with other symptoms such as weight loss, referred pain, or haemoptysis raises suspicion for malignancy.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Vocal Cord Nodules"][/sc]
  • Voice therapy - First-line treatment, focusing on improving vocal technique, reducing strain, and rehabilitating the vocal cords.
  • Vocal rest - Particularly important in the acute phase to allow inflammation to subside.
  • Surgical removal - Indicated in cases where conservative management fails after several months. Phonomicrosurgery is used to excise the nodules.
  • Hydration and reflux management - Keeping the vocal cords hydrated and managing any underlying gastroesophageal reflux can prevent worsening.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Vocal Cord Nodules"][/sc]
  • Permanent vocal changes - Without treatment, nodules can cause lasting damage to the vocal cords, leading to chronic dysphonia.
  • Psychosocial impact - For individuals who rely on their voice professionally, vocal cord nodules can cause significant stress, anxiety, or even loss of livelihood.
