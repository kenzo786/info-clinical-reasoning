Vascular Dementia
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Vascular Dementia"][/sc]
  • Vascular dementia is a form of dementia caused by chronic, reduced blood flow to the brain, often due to small vessel disease or multiple strokes. This results in progressive cognitive decline, primarily affecting executive function, memory, and processing speed. Unlike Alzheimer's disease, which usually begins with memory impairment, vascular dementia commonly affects attention, planning, and judgment earlier in its course.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Vascular Dementia"][/sc]
  • In the UK, vascular dementia is the second most common cause of dementia, accounting for approximately 15-20% of cases in GP practices.
  • Worldwide, vascular dementia prevalence varies by population, with higher rates in individuals over 65, estimated between 1.5-4%. The prevalence increases with age and the presence of cerebrovascular risk factors.
  • Higher incidence is noted in individuals with hypertension, diabetes, and a history of stroke.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Vascular Dementia"][/sc]
  • Vascular dementia arises from cerebrovascular damage, such as small or large vessel strokes, chronic ischaemia, or haemorrhages. Key mechanisms include:
    • Ischaemic Damage: Reduced blood flow from blocked or narrowed vessels causes neuron death, leading to areas of brain infarction.
    • Small Vessel Disease: Chronic hypertension and diabetes can cause arteriosclerosis of small vessels, impacting white matter and deep brain structures.
    • Haemorrhagic Lesions: Less common, but microbleeds in the brain due to amyloid angiopathy or hypertensive bleeding can contribute to vascular dementia.
  • These processes result in cognitive deficits due to impaired communication between brain regions and neuronal loss.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Vascular Dementia"][/sc]
  • Initial Phase: May begin with mild cognitive impairment (MCI), often asymptomatic or with subtle issues in planning or concentration.
  • Progression: Vascular dementia can progress in a stepwise manner, with noticeable declines following cerebrovascular events (e.g., small strokes).
  • Late Phase: Severe cognitive and physical disability, with impaired daily functioning, memory loss, and behavioural changes.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Vascular Dementia"][/sc]
  • Executive Dysfunction: Difficulty with planning, organisation, and multitasking, due to frontal lobe and white matter involvement.
  • Slowed Thinking: Reduced processing speed due to disrupted brain networks.
  • Short-Term Memory Loss: Less common initially but worsens over time, reflecting hippocampal and cortical ischaemic damage.
  • Behavioural Changes: Apathy, irritability, or depression due to frontal-subcortical circuit disruption.
  • Gait Abnormalities: Small vessel disease often affects basal ganglia, leading to slower, shuffling gait.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Vascular Dementia"][/sc]
  • Bradyphrenia (Slowness of Thought): Often noticeable in early stages, due to frontal and white matter involvement.
  • Gait Disturbances: Unsteady, shuffling gait resulting from basal ganglia damage and white matter ischaemia.
  • Focal Neurological Deficits: Symptoms such as hemiparesis or dysarthria if past strokes affected motor pathways.

Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Vascular Dementia"][/sc]
  • Isolated Memory Loss: Atypical in vascular dementia and may delay diagnosis, resembling Alzheimerâ€™s disease more closely.
  • Sudden Onset with Stepwise Decline: Presents like acute cognitive impairment following each stroke.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Vascular Dementia"][/sc]
  • Brain MRI: Shows ischaemic changes, small vessel disease, or lacunar infarcts in affected areas.
  • CT Head: Used if MRI is unavailable; reveals cortical atrophy and white matter changes.
  • Cognitive Testing: Neuropsychological assessments focus on executive function and processing speed (e.g., MoCA).
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Vascular Dementia"][/sc]
  • A 78-year-old male with a history of hypertension and multiple strokes presents with a gradual decline in planning and organisation abilities, slowed thinking, and a shuffling gait over the last 3 years.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Vascular Dementia"][/sc]
  • Alzheimerâ€™s Disease: Progressive memory loss is more pronounced than executive dysfunction in early stages.
  • Dementia with Lewy Bodies: Features visual hallucinations, fluctuating cognition, and Parkinsonian motor symptoms.
  • Frontotemporal Dementia: Early changes in behaviour or language without significant memory loss initially.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Vascular Dementia"][/sc]
  • Risk Factor Management: Control of hypertension, diabetes, and hyperlipidaemia is essential to slow progression.
  • Antiplatelet Therapy: May reduce the risk of further ischaemic events if no contraindications exist.
  • Cognitive Stimulation Therapy: Encourages engagement and maintenance of cognitive abilities.
  • Psychological Support: For patients and caregivers to manage cognitive and behavioural changes.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Vascular Dementia"][/sc]
  • Progressive Disability: Impaired functioning in daily life, requiring caregiver support.
  • Depression and Anxiety: Frequently arise due to cognitive and physical limitations.
  • Falls and Fractures: Associated with gait and balance issues, leading to increased injury risk.
  • Prognosis: Variable; cognitive decline may progress slowly or rapidly depending on risk factors and stroke recurrence.
