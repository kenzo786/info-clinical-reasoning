Bone Tumours
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Bone Tumours"][/sc]
  • Bone tumours refer to a diverse group of neoplastic lesions originating from bone tissue. These can be benign (e.g., osteochondroma, enchondroma) or malignant (e.g., osteosarcoma, Ewing's sarcoma, chondrosarcoma). Malignant bone tumours, particularly primary bone cancers, are less common than metastases from other cancers but are highly aggressive and require prompt treatment.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Bone Tumours"][/sc]
  • In the UK, primary bone cancers are rare, with an incidence of approximately 8 cases per million people annually. Benign bone tumours are more common, though exact prevalence is underreported due to frequent asymptomatic presentations.
  • Primary malignant bone tumours are seen more often in adolescents and young adults, with osteosarcoma being most common in those aged 10-30 and chondrosarcoma predominant in those over 40.
  • Worldwide, the prevalence of primary bone cancers varies but remains low, with similar incidence patterns seen across various regions.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Bone Tumours"][/sc]
  • Bone tumours develop when there is an imbalance in bone remodelling and cellular proliferation, leading to abnormal growth of bone tissue. In malignant tumours, genetic mutations disrupt regulatory genes, resulting in unregulated cell growth, local tissue invasion, and possible metastasis.
  • Specific mutations and molecular pathways involved vary by tumour type. For example, osteosarcoma often involves mutations in p53 or RB1 genes, while Ewingâ€™s sarcoma is associated with a translocation between chromosomes 11 and 22.
  • As tumours grow, they compromise the structural integrity of bone, leading to localised pain, deformity, or fracture. In malignant cases, the tumour can also invade adjacent tissues and metastasise, commonly to the lungs.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Bone Tumours"][/sc]
  • Benign bone tumours: Often asymptomatic and slowly progressive. May remain stable or grow minimally over years.
  • Malignant bone tumours: Typically exhibit a rapid progression, with symptoms worsening over weeks to months. Tumour growth can lead to structural compromise, pain, and systemic symptoms if metastasis occurs.
  • Metastatic spread: In malignant cases, metastasis (often to the lungs) may occur early in aggressive tumours like Ewing's sarcoma and osteosarcoma, with corresponding respiratory symptoms if the lungs are involved.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Bone Tumours"][/sc]
  • Bone pain: Most common symptom, often worse at night or with activity. Pain occurs due to tumour growth exerting pressure on the periosteum or infiltrating local tissues.
  • Swelling: Visible or palpable mass may form near the affected bone as the tumour grows, particularly in osteosarcoma and chondrosarcoma.
  • Pathological fractures: Weakened bone structure from tumour involvement can lead to fractures with minimal trauma.
  • Systemic symptoms (e.g., fever, weight loss): Occurs in more advanced or metastatic disease due to the inflammatory response or metastatic burden, particularly in Ewingâ€™s sarcoma.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Bone Tumours"][/sc]
  • Palpable mass: A firm or tender mass may be felt near the affected bone, often in the femur, tibia, or humerus in primary bone tumours.
  • Tenderness to palpation: Localised tenderness over the tumour site due to periosteal irritation.
  • Limited range of motion: Especially in tumours near joints, tumour expansion restricts movement due to pain or mechanical obstruction.
  • Signs of fracture: Swelling, bruising, and deformity if a pathological fracture has occurred due to bone weakness.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Bone Tumours"][/sc]
  • Unrelenting night pain: Indicates possible malignancy or aggressive tumour behaviour. Sign of periosteal irritation or nerve compression.
  • Unexplained weight loss: Can suggest a malignancy with systemic involvement or metastasis.
  • Rapidly enlarging mass: Suggests aggressive growth, often seen in malignant bone tumours like osteosarcoma.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Bone Tumours"][/sc]
  • Asymptomatic incidental findings, especially in benign tumours like osteochondroma, which may be identified on imaging for unrelated reasons.
  • Non-specific pain or vague symptoms in early stages, leading to misdiagnosis as musculoskeletal strain or injury.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Bone Tumours"][/sc]
  • X-ray: Initial imaging showing bone lysis, sclerosis, or sunburst pattern in malignant tumours. Benign tumours may show well-defined, regular edges.
  • MRI: Provides detailed assessment of soft tissue extension and intramedullary involvement, especially in Ewingâ€™s sarcoma.
  • CT scan: Used to evaluate cortical bone involvement and detect lung metastases.
  • Biopsy: Required for definitive diagnosis, determining tumour type and grading for appropriate treatment planning.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Bone Tumours"][/sc]
  • A 15-year-old adolescent male presents with progressively worsening left leg pain, particularly at night, with visible swelling over the tibia. X-ray shows a sunburst pattern indicative of osteosarcoma.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Bone Tumours"][/sc]
  • Osteomyelitis: Typically presents with fever and systemic signs of infection, and may show bone destruction but lacks a soft tissue mass on MRI.
  • Bone metastasis: More common in older adults, with known primary malignancy elsewhere. Lesions are often multiple and more likely in axial skeleton.
  • Osteoid osteoma: Small, benign tumour that presents with night pain, but pain is relieved by NSAIDs. Radiologically shows a characteristic nidus.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Bone Tumours"][/sc]
  • Benign tumours: Often monitored with serial imaging. Surgery considered if symptomatic or at risk of fracture.
  • Malignant tumours: Multimodal treatment includes surgical resection with limb salvage, chemotherapy (e.g., methotrexate, doxorubicin for osteosarcoma), and possibly radiotherapy for non-surgical candidates.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Bone Tumours"][/sc]
  • Metastasis: Commonly to the lungs in osteosarcoma, leading to respiratory symptoms. Poor prognosis if metastatic at diagnosis.
  • Pathological fractures: Result from weakened bone structure, commonly complicating benign and malignant tumours alike.
  • Treatment-related complications: Chemotherapy can cause myelosuppression, nephrotoxicity, and cardiotoxicity; limb-salvage surgery may have associated morbidity.
