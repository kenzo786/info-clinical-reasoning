Ascites
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Ascites"][/sc]
  • Ascites is the abnormal accumulation of fluid in the peritoneal cavity, typically due to portal hypertension. It is most commonly a consequence of liver cirrhosis but can also result from malignancy, heart failure, or other systemic conditions.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Ascites"][/sc]
  • UK GP Population: Ascites is rare in primary care, usually seen in patients with known cirrhosis or heart failure.
  • UK Hospital Prevalence: Around 60% of patients with cirrhosis will develop ascites within 10 years of diagnosis.
  • Worldwide Prevalence: Approximately 85% of cases are due to liver cirrhosis; other causes include malignancy (10%) and heart failure (5%).
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Ascites"][/sc]
  • Ascites forms due to an imbalance between capillary hydrostatic pressure and oncotic pressure in the peritoneum. The most common cause, liver cirrhosis, leads to portal hypertension, increasing hydrostatic pressure in the splanchnic vessels. This promotes fluid leakage into the peritoneal cavity. Concurrent hypoalbuminaemia decreases plasma oncotic pressure, reducing fluid reabsorption. In response, the kidneys retain sodium and water, worsening fluid accumulation.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Ascites"][/sc]
  • Initial phase: Gradual accumulation of fluid, often asymptomatic.
  • Progressive phase: Increasing abdominal distension, discomfort, and weight gain.
  • Advanced phase: Difficulty breathing (due to diaphragmatic elevation), early satiety, and significant oedema.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Ascites"][/sc]
  • Abdominal distension - Gradual increase in abdominal size due to the accumulation of peritoneal fluid. Caused by increased hydrostatic pressure from portal hypertension.
  • Abdominal discomfort - Stretching of the peritoneum leads to vague discomfort or pressure in the abdomen.
  • Weight gain - Rapid weight increase is common, reflecting fluid retention rather than fat accumulation.
  • Dyspnoea - Difficulty breathing due to diaphragmatic elevation caused by fluid pressure.
  • Early satiety - Compression of the stomach by fluid-filled peritoneum reduces the ability to eat normal portions.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Ascites"][/sc]
  • Shifting dullness - Due to the movement of free fluid in the peritoneal cavity, detectable on percussion.
  • Fluid thrill - Palpation of a wave of fluid across the abdomen when percussing one side indicates significant ascites.
  • Peripheral oedema - Caused by hypoalbuminaemia and sodium retention, leading to fluid accumulation in dependent tissues.
  • Hepatomegaly - Enlarged liver may be palpable if cirrhosis is present, causing increased portal hypertension.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Ascites"][/sc]
  • Spontaneous bacterial peritonitis (SBP) - Fever, abdominal pain, or encephalopathy could suggest SBP, a life-threatening infection of ascitic fluid. Requires urgent diagnostic paracentesis.
  • Hepatorenal syndrome - Progressive renal failure in cirrhotic patients with ascites, characterised by oliguria and increasing serum creatinine levels.
  • Rapid onset ascites - Suggests malignancy or venous thrombosis (e.g., Budd-Chiari syndrome).
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Ascites"][/sc]
  • Minimal ascites - Small amounts of fluid may be asymptomatic and detectable only via imaging.
  • Pseudoascites - Obesity or gaseous distension may mimic ascites but lacks fluid signs such as shifting dullness.
  • Ascites in non-cirrhotic patients - Can occur due to malignancy, pancreatitis, or tuberculosis, and may be misattributed to other causes without full investigation.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Ascites"][/sc]
  • Abdominal ultrasound - Confirms the presence of free fluid and may show cirrhosis or malignancy. Typical finding: hypoechoic fluid collection.
  • Diagnostic paracentesis - Ascitic fluid analysis: high serum-ascites albumin gradient (SAAG) (>11 g/L) indicates portal hypertension, while low SAAG suggests malignancy or infection.
  • Full blood count (FBC) - Leukocytosis may indicate infection such as SBP. Anaemia is common in chronic liver disease.
  • Liver function tests (LFTs) - Raised transaminases, low albumin, and prolonged prothrombin time suggest liver dysfunction as the cause of ascites.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Ascites"][/sc]
  • A 55-year-old male with a history of chronic alcohol use presents with progressive abdominal distension, dyspnoea, and weight gain over the past two months. Examination reveals shifting dullness, peripheral oedema, and hepatomegaly.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Ascites"][/sc]
  • Malignancy - Malignant ascites is associated with rapid onset, significant weight loss, and low SAAG. Imaging may reveal a primary tumour or peritoneal carcinomatosis.
  • Heart failure - Ascites due to right-sided heart failure presents with elevated jugular venous pressure and peripheral oedema. Echocardiogram shows reduced cardiac function.
  • Nephrotic syndrome - Presents with generalised oedema, proteinuria, and hypoalbuminaemia. Ascites is usually part of a wider oedematous state.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Ascites"][/sc]
  • Dietary sodium restriction - Reduces sodium retention, limiting fluid accumulation.
  • Diuretics - Spironolactone (first-line) with or without furosemide to promote natriuresis and fluid removal.
  • Therapeutic paracentesis - Large-volume paracentesis for symptomatic relief, with albumin infusion to prevent hypovolaemia.
  • Liver transplantation - Definitive treatment in patients with cirrhosis and refractory ascites.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Ascites"][/sc]
  • Spontaneous bacterial peritonitis (SBP) - Occurs in 10-30% of patients with ascites; mortality is high without treatment.
  • Hepatorenal syndrome - Progresses in cirrhotic patients, with a poor prognosis without liver transplantation.
  • Refractory ascites - Ascites that no longer responds to medical management, requiring frequent paracentesis or consideration for transplantation.
