Frontal Lobe Lesions
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Frontal Lobe Lesions"][/sc]
  • Frontal lobe lesions refer to abnormalities, including tumours, traumatic injuries, strokes, or neurodegenerative diseases, that impair the function of the frontal lobe. This brain region is crucial for higher executive functions, personality regulation, motor control, and speech production. The condition often leads to cognitive, behavioural, and motor deficits depending on the lesionâ€™s exact location and extent within the frontal lobe.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Frontal Lobe Lesions"][/sc]
  • UK GP Prevalence: Rare, but presenting to primary care often as personality changes or subtle executive dysfunction.
  • UK Hospital Prevalence: Higher, especially in neurology and neurosurgery settings, where brain tumours and traumatic brain injuries are more common.
  • Worldwide Prevalence: The incidence varies, with brain trauma, tumours, and stroke causing the majority of cases. Traumatic brain injuries (TBIs) affect approximately 1.7 million people annually worldwide, often involving frontal regions.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Frontal Lobe Lesions"][/sc]
  • Frontal lobe lesions disrupt neural circuits involved in executive functioning, emotional regulation, and motor control. Damage to the prefrontal cortex may affect decision-making, social behaviour, and emotional responses due to impaired connections with the limbic system. Lesions in the motor cortex result in contralateral motor weakness. Injury to Broca's area, located in the dominant hemisphere, can cause expressive aphasia. Lesions in different regions of the frontal lobe lead to distinct but overlapping deficits due to interconnected cortical and subcortical networks.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Frontal Lobe Lesions"][/sc]
  • Acute onset: Common in cases of trauma or stroke, with symptoms emerging within hours to days.
  • Subacute onset: Tumours or slow-growing lesions often present symptoms over weeks to months as mass effect increases.
  • Chronic progression: Neurodegenerative diseases impacting the frontal lobe, like frontotemporal dementia, progress gradually over years, leading to increasing cognitive and behavioural impairments.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Frontal Lobe Lesions"][/sc]
  • Personality changes - Common in lesions affecting the prefrontal cortex, leading to impulsivity, disinhibition, and lack of empathy, as these areas modulate social behaviours.
  • Executive dysfunction - Difficulty with planning, problem-solving, and multitasking due to impaired frontal lobe processing of complex tasks.
  • Apathy or lack of motivation - A result of disrupted mesolimbic pathways involving reward and motivation centres.
  • Memory impairment - Lesions in the prefrontal cortex can impair working memory, although less commonly than temporal lobe lesions.
  • Expressive aphasia - Lesions in Brocaâ€™s area lead to difficulty forming words and constructing sentences.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Frontal Lobe Lesions"][/sc]
  • Contralateral weakness - Lesions in the primary motor cortex affect motor output, causing weakness on the opposite side of the body.
  • Positive grasp reflex - A frontal release sign often appearing when higher-order frontal inhibition is lost.
  • Reduced insight - Patients may lack awareness of their behavioural and cognitive deficits due to impaired self-monitoring.
  • Disinhibited behaviour - Loss of social restraint and impulsivity due to disrupted prefrontal control of socially appropriate behaviour.
  • Expressive aphasia - Difficulty in verbal expression without impairing comprehension, common in left frontal lesions involving Brocaâ€™s area.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Frontal Lobe Lesions"][/sc]
  • Rapidly progressive symptoms - May indicate a fast-growing tumour, haemorrhage, or abscess.
  • Severe headache - Suggests increased intracranial pressure, commonly seen in high-grade tumours or large lesions.
  • Seizures - Sudden onset or new seizures in an adult may be linked to space-occupying lesions or haemorrhage.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Frontal Lobe Lesions"][/sc]
  • Subtle executive dysfunction without motor signs - Particularly common in early stages of frontal tumours or dementia, often overlooked as normal age-related cognitive changes.
  • Isolated mood or personality changes - Occasionally, frontal lesions may mimic psychiatric conditions such as depression or mania, especially in younger patients.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Frontal Lobe Lesions"][/sc]
  • MRI brain - Gold standard; identifies structural changes, lesion size, and location. T1-weighted images can show mass lesions, while T2 and FLAIR sequences highlight oedema and gliosis.
  • CT head - Useful in acute settings, especially for trauma or haemorrhage. May be less sensitive than MRI for soft tissue differentiation.
  • Neuropsychological testing - Assesses specific deficits in executive function, memory, and language, valuable for subtle frontal lobe involvement.
  • EEG - May be used if seizures are a concern; abnormal findings can localise seizure activity to frontal regions.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Frontal Lobe Lesions"][/sc]
  • A 56-year-old male presents with progressive personality changes, apathy, and difficulty with planning tasks over six months. Neurological examination reveals contralateral weakness and grasp reflex.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Frontal Lobe Lesions"][/sc]
  • Frontotemporal dementia - Progressive cognitive and behavioural changes but without focal neurological signs.
  • Schizophrenia - Typically younger onset with auditory hallucinations, unlike neurological findings in frontal lesions.
  • Stroke - Sudden onset rather than gradual progression; often with focal weakness and language deficits.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Frontal Lobe Lesions"][/sc]
  • Surgical resection - For accessible tumours or symptomatic lesions causing significant pressure effects.
  • Radiotherapy - Used post-surgery for malignant lesions or as primary treatment for inoperable tumours.
  • Corticosteroids - Prescribed to reduce cerebral oedema and intracranial pressure associated with tumours.
  • Anticonvulsants - Used if seizures are present; standard options include levetiracetam or phenytoin.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Frontal Lobe Lesions"][/sc]
  • Seizures - Common with cortical irritation from tumours or haemorrhage, often managed with anticonvulsants.
  • Intracranial hypertension - Caused by growing lesions, leading to symptoms such as headaches, nausea, and potential herniation risks if untreated.
  • Permanent cognitive impairment - Depending on the lesionâ€™s size, location, and treatment efficacy, residual deficits may persist.
