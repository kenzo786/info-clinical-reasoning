Wilson's Disease
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Wilson's Disease"][/sc]
  • Wilson's disease is a rare autosomal recessive disorder of copper metabolism that leads to the toxic accumulation of copper in various organs, most notably the liver and brain. The condition results from mutations in the ATP7B gene, which impairs the transport of copper and causes its build-up, leading to hepatic, neurological, and psychiatric manifestations.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Wilson's Disease"][/sc]
  • Worldwide prevalence is estimated at 1 in 30,000 individuals, but this may vary between populations.
  • In the UK, it is rare, with an estimated prevalence of 1 in 30,000-50,000. It is a very uncommon condition in general practice.
  • It is often underdiagnosed due to the wide range of presenting symptoms, and many cases are not recognised until adolescence or early adulthood.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Wilson's Disease"][/sc]
  • Wilsonâ€™s disease is caused by mutations in the ATP7B gene located on chromosome 13, which is responsible for encoding a copper-transporting P-type ATPase. This enzyme is involved in the excretion of copper into bile and its incorporation into ceruloplasmin.
  • Without proper functioning of ATP7B, copper accumulates in hepatocytes. Initially, copper is stored in the liver, leading to hepatocellular damage and cirrhosis.
  • As copper storage exceeds hepatic capacity, it spills over into the bloodstream and deposits in other organs, such as the brain (primarily the basal ganglia), kidneys, and corneas, causing systemic symptoms.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Wilson's Disease"][/sc]
  • The onset of Wilson's disease usually occurs between the ages of 5 and 35, although cases can present as early as age 3 or in late adulthood.
  • The hepatic phase may begin in childhood with asymptomatic liver dysfunction, progressing to cirrhosis or acute liver failure if untreated.
  • Neurological symptoms often appear in late adolescence or adulthood, typically years after hepatic involvement, and can be insidious or rapidly progressive.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Wilson's Disease"][/sc]
  • Fatigue - Due to hepatic dysfunction and chronic inflammation affecting metabolic processes.
  • Jaundice - Resulting from liver damage causing an impaired ability to process bilirubin.
  • Tremor - Often seen in neurological involvement due to copper deposition in the basal ganglia.
  • Personality changes - Psychiatric symptoms can manifest as irritability, depression, or anxiety, linked to brain involvement, particularly in the basal ganglia and frontal lobe.
  • Abdominal pain - May occur due to hepatomegaly, cirrhosis, or gallstones.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Wilson's Disease"][/sc]
  • Kayser-Fleischer rings - Brownish-green rings around the cornea caused by copper deposition in Descemetâ€™s membrane.
  • Hepatomegaly - Enlargement of the liver due to copper accumulation and liver dysfunction.
  • Ataxia - A result of damage to the cerebellum or basal ganglia, leading to uncoordinated movements.
  • Slurred speech (dysarthria) - Occurs due to neurological damage affecting motor control of speech.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Wilson's Disease"][/sc]
  • Acute liver failure - Can be life-threatening; signifies advanced hepatic involvement.
  • Severe neuropsychiatric symptoms - Sudden onset or rapid progression of neurological signs can indicate brain damage from copper accumulation.
  • Haemolytic anaemia - A possible consequence of copper-induced red blood cell destruction.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Wilson's Disease"][/sc]
  • Asymptomatic liver disease - Some patients may present with subclinical liver disease identified only through abnormal liver function tests.
  • Isolated psychiatric symptoms - These may mimic primary psychiatric disorders such as depression or schizophrenia without evident hepatic or neurological signs.
  • Renal tubular acidosis - An uncommon presentation with features of metabolic acidosis and electrolyte imbalances.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Wilson's Disease"][/sc]
  • Serum ceruloplasmin - Low (<0.2 g/L), as ceruloplasmin synthesis is defective in Wilson's disease.
  • 24-hour urinary copper excretion - Elevated (>100 Âµg/day), reflecting the body's inability to excrete copper via the liver.
  • Liver biopsy with copper quantification - Elevated hepatic copper content (>250 Âµg/g dry weight).
  • Genetic testing - Identification of ATP7B gene mutations confirms the diagnosis.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Wilson's Disease"][/sc]
  • A 15-year-old male presents with fatigue, jaundice, and tremors. Over the past year, he has developed mood changes and difficulty with coordination. Examination reveals hepatomegaly and Kayser-Fleischer rings.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Wilson's Disease"][/sc]
  • Autoimmune hepatitis - Often presents with jaundice and elevated liver enzymes but lacks Kayser-Fleischer rings and has positive autoimmune markers (ANA, SMA).
  • Hepatitis B/C - Viral hepatitis can mimic the hepatic manifestations but is confirmed with specific viral serologies.
  • Huntington's disease - Progressive neurological disease with psychiatric symptoms but does not present with liver dysfunction or Kayser-Fleischer rings.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Wilson's Disease"][/sc]
  • Chelation therapy - Penicillamine is the first-line treatment in the UK, promoting urinary excretion of copper.
  • Zinc supplementation - Blocks copper absorption from the gastrointestinal tract.
  • Liver transplantation - Indicated in cases of fulminant liver failure or decompensated cirrhosis.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Wilson's Disease"][/sc]
  • Cirrhosis - Results from chronic liver damage, which may progress to hepatocellular carcinoma.
  • Neurological deterioration - Progressive brain damage can lead to permanent movement disorders or cognitive decline.
  • Acute liver failure - Life-threatening if not managed promptly, often requiring liver transplantation.
