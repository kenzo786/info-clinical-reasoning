Vitamin Deficiencies
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Vitamin Deficiencies"][/sc]
  • Vitamin deficiencies refer to the lack of sufficient levels of one or more vitamins essential for normal metabolic functions, resulting in clinical manifestations depending on the specific vitamin involved. These deficiencies can be caused by poor dietary intake, malabsorption, or increased physiological needs. Common deficiencies include vitamins B12, D, C, A, and folate.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Vitamin Deficiencies"][/sc]
  • UK GP Population: Vitamin D deficiency is the most common, affecting up to 30% of adults in the UK, especially during winter months. Vitamin B12 and folate deficiencies are also prevalent in the elderly and those with specific dietary restrictions (vegetarians).
  • UK Hospital Prevalence: Hospitalised patients, especially those with chronic illnesses or malabsorption syndromes, often present with deficiencies. In the UK, 10â€“20% of hospitalised elderly patients show signs of vitamin B12 deficiency.
  • Worldwide Prevalence: Globally, vitamin A deficiency is a significant public health issue in low-income countries, affecting around 250 million preschool children. Vitamin D and B12 deficiencies are also widespread in both developed and developing nations.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Vitamin Deficiencies"][/sc]
  • Step 1: Inadequate intake or absorption of the specific vitamin leads to a depletion of stored vitamins (if applicable).
  • Step 2: The body starts to show symptoms as essential biochemical processes involving the vitamin are impaired. For example, vitamin B12 deficiency affects DNA synthesis and neuronal function, while vitamin D deficiency impairs calcium absorption, leading to bone demineralisation.
  • Step 3: Continued deficiency results in characteristic clinical signs and potential irreversible damage, such as anaemia, neurological symptoms (B12 deficiency), or rickets/osteomalacia (vitamin D deficiency).
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Vitamin Deficiencies"][/sc]
  • Early Phase: Most vitamin deficiencies develop gradually over months or years due to the depletion of body stores.
  • Mid Phase: As stores deplete, subclinical symptoms like fatigue (B12, folate, D deficiencies) or mild cognitive impairment (B12) start to appear.
  • Late Phase: Overt clinical manifestations such as macrocytic anaemia (B12), peripheral neuropathy (B12), rickets (vitamin D), or scurvy (vitamin C) occur. Irreversible complications such as neurological damage may ensue if untreated.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Vitamin Deficiencies"][/sc]
  • Fatigue: Common across most deficiencies (B12, folate, D, C) due to impaired energy production or red blood cell synthesis.
  • Numbness and tingling: Seen in vitamin B12 deficiency due to demyelination of peripheral nerves.
  • Bone pain and muscle weakness: Common in vitamin D deficiency, resulting from poor calcium homeostasis and bone demineralisation.
  • Glossitis or mouth ulcers: Seen in folate or vitamin B12 deficiency due to impaired DNA synthesis in rapidly dividing cells like mucosal cells.
  • Frequent infections: Common in vitamin C deficiency (scurvy) due to impaired collagen synthesis and weakened immune response.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Vitamin Deficiencies"][/sc]
  • Pallor: Common in B12, folate, or iron deficiency, due to anaemia caused by impaired erythropoiesis.
  • Peripheral neuropathy: Seen in B12 deficiency as a result of nerve damage, often beginning with numbness and tingling in the extremities.
  • Rickets (children) / Osteomalacia (adults): Classic signs of vitamin D deficiency due to impaired calcium and phosphate metabolism, leading to bone softening and deformities.
  • Bleeding gums: A classic sign of vitamin C deficiency (scurvy) due to defective collagen formation and capillary fragility.
  • Hyperpigmentation or angular cheilitis: Seen in severe B12 or folate deficiency due to impaired cell division affecting skin and mucous membranes.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Vitamin Deficiencies"][/sc]
  • Severe neurological deficits: In vitamin B12 deficiency, these may indicate irreversible damage, such as subacute combined degeneration of the spinal cord, potentially leading to paralysis if untreated.
  • Osteomalacia/rickets: Indicates advanced vitamin D deficiency, which can lead to permanent skeletal deformities and fractures.
  • Severe bleeding or haemorrhage: Seen in advanced vitamin C deficiency (scurvy), which can lead to life-threatening haemorrhage due to capillary fragility.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Vitamin Deficiencies"][/sc]
  • Vitamin B12 deficiency without anaemia: Some patients, particularly the elderly, may present with neurological symptoms without typical macrocytic anaemia due to compensated bone marrow activity.
  • Vitamin D deficiency in non-skeletal symptoms: Patients may present with mood changes, fatigue, or muscle aches before overt bone signs, especially in subtropical regions with partial sun exposure.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Vitamin Deficiencies"][/sc]
  • Serum B12 levels: Low serum B12 (<150 pmol/L) indicates deficiency. Functional tests like methylmalonic acid (MMA) may be elevated in early deficiency due to impaired metabolism of fatty acids.
  • Serum 25(OH)D: This is the primary test for vitamin D deficiency. Levels <25 nmol/L suggest deficiency. Vitamin D regulates calcium and phosphate metabolism, and low levels impair bone mineralisation.
  • Full blood count (FBC): Macrocytic anaemia (MCV >100 fL) in B12/folate deficiency. A low haemoglobin level may be seen in other deficiencies like iron or folate.
  • Serum ferritin: Useful to check iron stores in patients with vitamin deficiency, especially those presenting with fatigue and anaemia.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Vitamin Deficiencies"][/sc]
  • A 65-year-old female presents with progressive fatigue, numbness in the feet, and occasional falls over 6 months. She has a vegan diet and mild pallor, with a macrocytic anaemia noted on her blood tests.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Vitamin Deficiencies"][/sc]
  • Iron deficiency anaemia: Unlike vitamin B12 deficiency, this presents with microcytic anaemia, typically without neurological symptoms.
  • Thyroid dysfunction: Hypothyroidism can mimic fatigue and depression seen in vitamin D deficiency, but thyroid tests (TSH, free T4) will be abnormal.
  • Multiple sclerosis: Can cause neurological symptoms similar to B12 deficiency, but often includes optic neuritis and relapsing-remitting episodes on MRI.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Vitamin Deficiencies"][/sc]
  • Vitamin B12 deficiency: Hydroxocobalamin 1 mg intramuscular injections every 2-3 months or oral cyanocobalamin in non-absorption issues.
  • Vitamin D deficiency: High-dose vitamin D (cholecalciferol 20,000-50,000 IU/week) followed by maintenance doses of 800â€“2,000 IU/day.
  • Folate deficiency: Oral folic acid 5 mg daily for 4 months or until stores are replete.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Vitamin Deficiencies"][/sc]
  • Neurological damage: Vitamin B12 deficiency can cause irreversible damage to the spinal cord and peripheral nerves if not treated early.
  • Osteoporosis and fractures: Chronic vitamin D deficiency leads to decreased bone density, increasing the risk of fractures.
  • Scurvy: Advanced vitamin C deficiency can lead to fatal bleeding disorders due to impaired collagen synthesis.
