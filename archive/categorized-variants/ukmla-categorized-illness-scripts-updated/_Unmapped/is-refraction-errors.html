Refraction Errors
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Refraction Errors"][/sc]
  • Refraction errors are optical defects of the eye that prevent light from focusing directly on the retina, leading to blurred vision. The most common types include myopia (short-sightedness), hyperopia (long-sightedness), astigmatism (irregular curvature of the cornea or lens), and presbyopia (age-related difficulty focusing on near objects).
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Refraction Errors"][/sc]
  • Prevalence in the UK GP population: Approximately 49% of adults have a significant refraction error.
  • Prevalence in UK hospitals: Commonly identified during ophthalmology consultations but not usually a primary reason for hospitalisation.
  • Worldwide prevalence: Affects around 35% of the global population; myopia alone is estimated to impact 27%.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Refraction Errors"][/sc]
  • Refraction errors occur due to mismatches between the eye's refractive power and the axial length of the eyeball:
    • Myopia: Excessive axial length or increased refractive power causes light to focus in front of the retina.
    • Hyperopia: Insufficient axial length or decreased refractive power results in light focusing behind the retina.
    • Astigmatism: Uneven corneal or lens curvature causes light to focus irregularly on the retina.
    • Presbyopia: Age-related loss of lens elasticity reduces near focusing ability.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Refraction Errors"][/sc]
  • Myopia and hyperopia: Often diagnosed in childhood; can stabilise or progress with age.
  • Astigmatism: Can be present from birth and usually remains stable throughout life.
  • Presbyopia: Gradual onset typically starts around the age of 40, progressively worsening.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Refraction Errors"][/sc]
  • Blurred vision: Caused by improper focus of light on the retina.
  • Eye strain: Results from excessive effort to focus on objects.
  • Headaches: Often due to sustained visual strain.
  • Difficulty with near or distance vision: Depending on the specific refraction error, near or distant objects may appear blurry.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Refraction Errors"][/sc]
  • Reduced visual acuity: Demonstrated on a Snellen or LogMAR chart test.
  • Squinting: Indicates an attempt to improve focus.
  • Abnormal retinoscopy findings: Reflex movements suggest a mismatch in refractive power.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Refraction Errors"][/sc]
  • Sudden visual loss: May indicate retinal detachment or acute angle-closure glaucoma.
  • Visual distortion: Could suggest macular disease.
  • Persistent headaches: Could signal increased intracranial pressure or other neurological causes.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Refraction Errors"][/sc]
  • Asymptomatic individuals: Some may not realise they have reduced vision until tested, particularly in one eye.
  • Intermittent blurriness: May occur with transient refractive shifts due to conditions like diabetes or dry eye.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Refraction Errors"][/sc]
  • Visual acuity testing: Snellen or LogMAR chart demonstrates degree of visual impairment.
  • Retinoscopy: Evaluates the refractive power of the eye using light reflexes.
  • Autorefractor measurement: Provides an objective assessment of refractive error.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Refraction Errors"][/sc]
  • A 16-year-old female presents with progressive difficulty seeing the whiteboard at school. No associated pain or redness. Symptoms have been present for one year and are worsening.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Refraction Errors"][/sc]
  • Cataracts: Presents with glare and progressive blurring, often in older adults.
  • Macular degeneration: Central vision loss, more common in the elderly.
  • Corneal scarring: History of trauma or infection with associated visual loss.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Refraction Errors"][/sc]
  • Corrective lenses: Glasses or contact lenses to adjust refractive power.
  • Refractive surgery: LASIK or PRK for permanent correction in suitable candidates.
  • Orthokeratology: Overnight contact lenses for temporary reshaping of the cornea.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Refraction Errors"][/sc]
  • Amblyopia: Lazy eye due to uncorrected refractive error in childhood.
  • Recurrent headaches: Chronic strain from uncorrected vision problems.
  • Decreased quality of life: Impaired daily functioning and education/work performance.
