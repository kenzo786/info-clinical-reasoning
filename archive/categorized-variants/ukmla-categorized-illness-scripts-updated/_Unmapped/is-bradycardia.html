Bradycardia
Definition [sc name="ask-ai-101" Q="Bradycardia"][/sc]
  • Bradycardia is defined as a resting heart rate of fewer than 60 beats per minute (bpm) in adults. It can be physiological (e.g., in well-trained athletes) or pathological, resulting from intrinsic cardiac conduction disorders or extrinsic causes such as medications or metabolic disturbances.
Epidemiology [sc name="ask-ai-102" Q="Bradycardia"][/sc]
  • UK General Practice: Approximately 0.6-1.2% of patients seen in general practice may present with bradycardia.
  • UK Hospital Prevalence: Bradycardia requiring hospitalisation or investigation is seen in around 1-5% of hospitalised patients.
  • Worldwide Prevalence: Exact prevalence varies, but rates are higher in populations with high levels of athletic activity and the elderly.
Pathophysiology [sc name="ask-ai-103" Q="Bradycardia"][/sc]
  • Bradycardia results from a disturbance in the heart's electrical conduction system. It may involve sinoatrial node dysfunction (sinus bradycardia), atrioventricular (AV) block, or suppression of normal pacemaker function due to extrinsic factors such as hypothyroidism or medications (e.g., beta-blockers).
  • Sinoatrial Node Dysfunction: The SA node fails to generate impulses at a normal rate.
  • AV Block: Impulse transmission through the AV node is delayed or interrupted.
  • Extrinsic Factors: Drugs or metabolic changes depress pacemaker activity or slow conduction.
Time Course [sc name="ask-ai-104" Q="Bradycardia"][/sc]
  • Acute Onset: Bradycardia can develop suddenly due to acute causes such as myocardial infarction, overdose of medications (e.g., beta-blockers), or electrolyte disturbances (e.g., hyperkalaemia).
  • Chronic Course: In conditions such as hypothyroidism or chronic sinoatrial node dysfunction, bradycardia may develop slowly over weeks to months.
  • Intermittent Bradycardia: Seen in conditions like sick sinus syndrome, where episodes of bradycardia alternate with tachycardia.
Symptoms [sc name="ask-ai-105" Q="Bradycardia"][/sc]
  • Fatigue - Due to reduced cardiac output, leading to diminished perfusion of tissues.
  • Dizziness - Caused by decreased cerebral perfusion as a result of low heart rate.
  • Syncope - Occurs when bradycardia results in significant hypotension, leading to transient cerebral hypoperfusion.
  • Shortness of Breath - Reduced cardiac output causes poor oxygen delivery to tissues, creating a sensation of breathlessness.
  • Chest Pain - If bradycardia leads to coronary hypoperfusion, myocardial ischaemia can occur, causing chest pain.
  • Asymptomatic - Often in athletes or individuals with high vagal tone, where bradycardia is a physiological adaptation.
Signs [sc name="ask-ai-106" Q="Bradycardia"][/sc]
  • Slow Pulse - Directly indicative of bradycardia (heart rate < 60 bpm).
  • Hypotension - Due to reduced stroke volume and cardiac output from a slower heart rate.
  • Cool Extremities - Peripheral vasoconstriction occurs as a compensatory mechanism for low cardiac output.
  • Altered Mental Status - Severe bradycardia can impair cerebral perfusion, leading to confusion or agitation.
Red Flag Features [sc name="ask-ai-107" Q="Bradycardia"][/sc]
  • Syncope with Injury - May indicate severe, prolonged bradycardia causing significant cerebral hypoperfusion and potential arrhythmias.
  • Chest Pain - Can signify myocardial ischaemia secondary to reduced coronary perfusion.
  • Severe Hypotension - Indicates dangerously low cardiac output, possibly leading to shock or multi-organ failure.
  • Altered Mental Status - Suggests cerebral hypoperfusion from inadequate cardiac output, necessitating urgent intervention.
Atypical Presentations [sc name="ask-ai-108" Q="Bradycardia"][/sc]
  • Asymptomatic Bradycardia - Often seen in fit athletes or individuals with high vagal tone; there is no clinical consequence despite a heart rate of < 60 bpm.
  • Bradycardia in Hypothyroidism - The patient may primarily present with other hypothyroid symptoms (e.g., fatigue, weight gain) rather than directly with bradycardia.
Diagnostic Tests [sc name="ask-ai-109" Q="Bradycardia"][/sc]
  • ECG - Confirms bradycardia and identifies the type (sinus bradycardia, AV block). Findings include a heart rate < 60 bpm, prolonged PR interval (in AV block), or absence of P waves in some cases of sick sinus syndrome.
  • Thyroid Function Tests - Low T3/T4 and elevated TSH may indicate hypothyroidism as a cause of bradycardia.
  • Electrolytes - Elevated potassium (hyperkalaemia) or calcium levels can affect the electrical activity of the heart, leading to bradycardia.
  • Holter Monitor - Continuous ECG monitoring to detect intermittent bradycardia, especially in suspected sick sinus syndrome.
Problem Representation [sc name="ask-ai-110" Q="Bradycardia"][/sc]
  • A 68-year-old male presents with recurrent episodes of dizziness and near-syncope over the last few weeks. ECG shows a heart rate of 45 bpm with intermittent AV block. Thyroid function tests are normal.
Differential Diagnosis [sc name="ask-ai-111" Q="Bradycardia"][/sc]
  • Atrial Fibrillation with Slow Ventricular Response - Irregularly irregular rhythm; no consistent P waves on ECG.
  • Sick Sinus Syndrome - Alternating bradycardia and tachycardia; episodes of bradycardia occur intermittently.
  • Hypothyroidism - Bradycardia accompanied by classic hypothyroid symptoms (e.g., cold intolerance, weight gain); confirmed by elevated TSH and low T3/T4.
  • Drug-Induced Bradycardia - History of beta-blocker or calcium channel blocker use with an appropriate dose-response relationship to the bradycardia.
Treatment [sc name="ask-ai-112" Q="Bradycardia"][/sc]
  • Remove Reversible Causes: Discontinue any offending drugs (e.g., beta-blockers), correct electrolyte imbalances, or treat hypothyroidism.
  • Atropine: First-line for acute symptomatic bradycardia (0.5 mg IV bolus, repeat as needed to a maximum of 3 mg).
  • Pacing: Temporary pacing or permanent pacemaker placement for chronic bradycardia due to AV block or sick sinus syndrome, according to NICE guidelines.
Complications [sc name="ask-ai-115" Q="Bradycardia"][/sc]
  • Syncope and Falls - Due to cerebral hypoperfusion; common in elderly patients.
  • Heart Failure - Longstanding bradycardia can lead to reduced cardiac output and heart failure symptoms.
  • Sudden Cardiac Death - Particularly in high-degree AV block or severe bradycardia, if left untreated.
